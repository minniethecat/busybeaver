[033-001]Though he was too late to take part in the capture of the Taku Forts, he

[033-002]was in time to witness the destruction of the Summer Palace at

[033-003]Peking--the act by which Lord Elgin, in the name of European

[033-004]civilisation, took vengeance upon the barbarism of the East.

[033-005]The war was over; but the [[british-army|British Army]] remained in the country, until

[033-006]the payment of an indemnity by the Chinese Government was completed. A

[033-007]camp was formed at Tientsin, and Gordon was occupied in setting up huts

[033-008]for the troops. While he was thus engaged, he had a slight attack of

[033-009]smallpox. 'I am glad to say,' he told his sister, 'that this disease has

[033-010]brought me back to my Saviour, and I trust in future to be a better

[033-011]Christian than I have been hitherto.'

[033-012]Curiously enough a similar circumstance had, more than twenty years

[033-013]earlier, brought about a singular succession of events which were now

[033-014]upon the point of opening the way to Gordon's first great adventure. In

[033-015]1837, a village schoolmaster near Canton had been attacked by illness;

[033-016]and, as in the case of Gordon, illness had been followed by a religious

[033-017]revulsion. Hong-Siu-Tsuen--for such was his name--saw visions, went into

[033-018]ecstasies, and entered into relations with the Deity. Shortly

[033-019]afterwards, he fell in with a Methodist missionary from America, who

[033-020]instructed him in the Christian religion. The new doctrine, working upon

[033-021]the mystical ferment already in Hong's mind, produced a remarkable

[033-022]result. He was, he declared, the prophet of God; he was more--he was the

[033-023]Son of God; he was Tien Wang, the Celestial King; he was the younger

[033-024]brother of Jesus. The times were propitious, and proselytes soon

[033-025]gathered around him. Having conceived a grudge against the Government,

[033-026]owing to his failure in an examination, Hong gave a political turn to

[033-027]his teaching, which soon developed into a propaganda of rebellion

[033-028]against the rule of the Manchus and the Mandarins. The authorities took

[033-029]fright, attempted to suppress Hong by force, and failed. The movement

[033-030]spread. By 1850 the rebels were overrunning the populous and flourishing

[033-031]delta of the Yangtse Kiang, and had become a formidable force. In 1853

[033-032]they captured Nankin, which was henceforth their capital. The Tien Wang,

[033-033]established himself in a splendid palace, and proclaimed his new

[033-034]evangel. His theogony included the wife of God, or the celestial Mother,

[033-035]the wife of Jesus, or the celestial daughter-in-law, and a sister of

[033-036]Jesus, whom he married to one of his lieutenants, who thus became the

[033-037]celestial son-in-law; the Holy Ghost, however, was eliminated.

[033-038]His mission was to root out Demons and Manchus from the face of the

[033-039]earth, and to establish Taiping, the reign of eternal peace. In the

[033-040]meantime, retiring into the depths of his palace, he left the further

[033-041]conduct of earthly operations to his lieutenants, upon whom he bestowed

[033-042]the title of 'Wangs' (kings), while he himself, surrounded by thirty

[033-043]wives and one hundred concubines, devoted his energies to the spiritual

[033-044]side of his mission. The Taiping Rebellion, as it came to be called, had

[033-045]now reached its furthest extent. The rebels were even able to occupy,

[033-046]for more than a year, the semi-European city of Shanghai. But then the

[033-047]tide turned. The latent forces of the Empire gradually asserted

[033-048]themselves. The rebels lost ground, their armies were defeated, and in

[033-049]1859 Nankin itself was besieged, and the Celestial King trembled in his

[033-050]palace. The end seemed to be at hand, when there was a sudden twist of

[033-051]Fortune's wheel. The war of 1860, the invasion of China by European

[033-052]armies, their march into the interior, and their occupation of Peking,

[033-053]not only saved the rebels from destruction, but allowed them to recover

[033-054]the greater part of what they had lost. Once more they seized upon the

[033-055]provinces of the delta, once more they menaced Shanghai. It was clear

[033-056]that the Imperial army was incompetent, and the Shanghai merchants

[033-057]determined to provide for their own safety as best they could. They

[033-058]accordingly got together a body of troops, partly Chinese and partly

[033-059]European, and under European officers, to which they entrusted the

[033-060]defence of the town. This small force, which, after a few preliminary

[033-061]successes, received from the Chinese Government the title of the 'Ever

[033-062]Victorious Army', was able to hold the rebels at bay, but it could do no

[033-063]more. For two years Shanghai was in constant danger. The Taipings,

[033-064]steadily growing in power, were spreading destruction far and wide. The

[033-065]Ever Victorious Army was the only force capable of opposing them, and

[033-066]the Ever Victorious Army was defeated more often than not. Its first

[033-067]European leader had been killed; his successor quarrelled with the

[033-068]Chinese Governor, Li Hung Chang, and was dismissed. At last it was

[033-069]determined to ask the General at the head of the British Army of

[033-070]Occupation for the loan of an officer to command the force. The English,

[033-071]who had been at first inclined to favour the Taipings, on religious

[033-072]grounds, were now convinced, on practical grounds, of the necessity of

[033-073]suppressing them. It was in these circumstances that, early in 1863, the

[033-074]command of the Ever Victorious Army was offered to Gordon. He accepted

[033-075]it, received the title of General from the Chinese authorities, and

[033-076]entered forthwith upon his new task. He was just thirty.

[033-077]In eighteen months, he told Li Hung Chang, the business would be

[033-078]finished; and he was as good as his word. The difficulties before him

[033-079]were very great. A vast tract of country was in the possession of the

[033-080]rebels--an area, at the lowest estimate, of 14,000 square miles with a

[033-081]population of 20,000,000. For centuries this low-lying plain of the

[033-082]Yangtse delta, rich in silk and tea, fertilised by elaborate irrigation,

[033-083]and covered with great walled cities, had been one of the most

[033-084]flourishing districts in China. Though it was now being rapidly ruined

[033-085]by the depredations of the Taipings, its strategic strength was

[033-086]obviously enormous. Gordon, however, with the eye of a born general,

[033-087]perceived that he could convert the very feature of the country which,

[033-088]on the face of it, most favoured an army on the defence--its complicated

[033-089]geographical system of interlacing roads and waterways, canals, lakes

[033-090]and rivers--into a means of offensive warfare. The force at his disposal

[033-091]was small, but it was mobile. He had a passion for map-making, and had

[033-092]already, in his leisure hours, made a careful survey of the country

[033-093]round Shanghai; he was thus able to execute a series of manoeuvres which

[033-094]proved fatal to the enemy. By swift marches and counter-marches, by

[033-095]sudden attacks and surprises, above all by the dispatch of armed

[033-096]steamboats up the circuitous waterways into positions from which they

[033-097]could fall upon the enemy in reverse, he was able gradually to force

[033-098]back the rebels, to cut them off piecemeal in the field, and to seize

[033-099]upon their cities. But, brilliant as these operations were, Gordon's

[033-100]military genius showed itself no less unmistakably in other directions.

[033-101]The Ever Victorious Army, recruited from the riff-raff of Shanghai, was

[033-102]an ill-disciplined, ill-organised body of about three thousand men,

[033-103]constantly on the verge of mutiny, supporting itself on plunder, and, at

[033-104]the slightest provocation, melting into thin air. Gordon, by sheer force

[033-105]of character, established over this incoherent mass of ruffians an

[033-106]extraordinary ascendancy. He drilled them with rigid severity; he put

[033-107]them into a uniform, armed them systematically, substituted pay for

[033-108]loot, and was even able, at last, to introduce regulations of a sanitary

[033-109]kind. There were some terrible scenes, in which the General, alone,

[033-110]faced the whole furious army, and quelled it: scenes of rage, desperation,

[033-111]towering courage, and summary execution. Eventually he attained an

[033-112]almost magical prestige. Walking at the head of his troops with nothing

[033-113]but a light cane in his hand, he seemed to pass through every danger

[033-114]with the scatheless equanimity of a demi-god. The Taipings themselves

[033-115]were awed into a strange reverence. More than once their leaders, in a

[033-116]frenzy of fear and admiration, ordered the sharp-shooters not to take

[033-117]aim at the advancing figure of the faintly smiling Englishman.

[033-118]It is significant that Gordon found it easier to win battles and to

[033-119]crush mutineers than to keep on good terms with the Chinese authorities.

[033-120]He had to act in cooperation with a large native force; and it was only

[033-121]natural that the general at the head of it should grow more and more

[033-122]jealous and angry as the Englishman's successes revealed more and more

[033-123]clearly his own incompetence. At first, indeed, Gordon could rely upon

[033-124]the support of the Governor. Li Flung Chang's experience of Europeans

[033-125]had been hitherto limited to low-class adventurers, and Gordon came as a

[033-126]revelation.

[033-127]'It is a direct blessing from Heaven,' he noted in his diary, 'the

[033-128]coming of this British Gordon.... He is superior in manner and bearing

[033-129]to any of the foreigners whom I have come into contact with, and does

[033-130]not show outwardly that conceit which makes most of them repugnant in my

[033-131]sight.'

[033-132]A few months later, after he had accompanied Gordon on a victorious

[033-133]expedition, the Mandarin's enthusiasm burst forth.

[033-134]'What a sight for tired eyes,' he wrote, 'what an elixir for a heavy

[033-135]heart--to see this splendid Englishman fight!... If there is anything

[033-136]that I admire nearly as much as the superb scholarship of Tseng Kuofan,

[033-137]it is the military qualities of this fine officer. He is a glorious

[033-138]fellow!' In his emotion, Li Hung Chang addressed Gordon as his brother,

[033-139]declaring that he 'considered him worthy to fill the place of the

[033-140]brother who is departed. Could I have said more in all the words of the

[033-141]world?' Then something happened which impressed and mystified the

[033-142]sensitive Chinaman.

[033-143]'The Englishman's face was first filled with a deep pleasure, and then

[033-144]he seemed to be thinking of something depressing and sad; for the

[033-145]smile went from his mouth and there were tears in his eyes when he

[033-146]thanked me for what I had said. Can it be that he has, or has had, some

[033-147]great trouble in his life, and that he fights recklessly to forget it,

[033-148]or that Death has no terrors for him?'

[033-149]But, as time went on, Li Hung Chang's attitude began to change. 'General

[033-150]Gordon,' he notes in July, 'must control his tongue, even if he lets his

[033-151]mind run loose.' The Englishman had accused him of intriguing with the

[033-152]Chinese general, and of withholding money due to the Ever Victorious

[033-153]Army. 'Why does he not accord me the honours that are due to me, as head

[033-154]of the military and civil authority in these parts?' By September, the

[033-155]Governor's earlier transports have been replaced by a more judicial

[033-156]frame of mind.

[033-157]'With his many faults, his pride, his temper, and his never-ending

[033-158]demand for money, (for one is a noble man, and in spite of all I have

[033-159]said to him or about him) I will ever think most highly of him.... He is

[033-160]an honest man, but difficult to get on with.'

[033-161]Disagreements of this kind might perhaps have been tided over until the

[033-162]end of the campaign; but an unfortunate incident suddenly led to a more

[033-163]serious quarrel. Gordon's advance had been fiercely contested, but it

[033-164]had been constant; he had captured several important towns; and in

[033-165]October he laid siege to the city of Soo-chow, once one of the most

[033-166]famous and splendid in China. In December, its fall being obviously

[033-167]imminent, the Taiping leaders agreed to surrender it on condition that

[033-168]their lives were spared. Gordon was a party to the agreement, and laid

[033-169]special stress upon his presence with the Imperial forces as a pledge of

[033-170]its fulfilment. No sooner, however, was the city surrendered than the

[033-171]rebel 'Wangs' were assassinated. In his fury, it is said that Gordon

[033-172]searched everywhere for Li Hung Chang with a loaded pistol in his hand.

[033-173]He was convinced of the complicity of the Governor, who, on his side,

[033-174]denied that he was responsible for what had happened.
