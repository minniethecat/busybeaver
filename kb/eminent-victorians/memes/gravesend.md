# Gravesend

A modest port town on the south bank of the Thames estuary, Gravesend occupies a small but pivotal place in the life of Charles George Gordon. It was here, far from the drawing-rooms and battlefields that would later define his legend, that Gordon underwent the spiritual transformation which Strachey presents as the key to his enigmatic character. Six years of solitude among the new Thames forts turned a capable Royal Engineer into "the mystic of Gravesend" [^1] — a man who would spend the rest of his life searching for the answer to a single question.

## Location

Gravesend lies on the south bank of the River Thames in Kent, approximately twenty miles east of London. In the mid-Victorian period it served both as a commercial landing point and as a strategic defensive position guarding the river approach to the capital. It was the latter function that brought Gordon there in the 1860s, charged with supervising the construction of a system of forts at the mouth of the Thames [^2].

## In the Narrative

Strachey devotes careful attention to the Gravesend years, treating them as the crucible in which Gordon's inner life was forged. Gordon arrived as a thirty-two-year-old officer with a reputation for competence at anything that did not require ease in society. "When he was appointed to Gravesend to supervise the erection of a system of forts at the mouth of the Thames," Strachey writes, "he remained there quietly for six years, and at last was almost forgotten" [^2].

The isolation suited him. Gordon had a horror of fashionable society — "the presence of ladies — especially of fashionable ladies — filled him with uneasiness," and he harboured "a deeper dread of the world's contaminations" [^3]. At Gravesend he could escape both.

### The Spiritual Turning-Point

During his retirement at Gravesend, Gordon began asking the question that would never leave him: "What was the Will of God?" Strachey treats this as the originating impulse of Gordon's whole subsequent career: "The question, which first became insistent during his retirement at Gravesend, never afterwards left him; it might almost be said that he spent the remainder of his life in searching for the answer to it" [^4].

Gordon filled notebooks with theological speculations during these years. "The published extracts from these voluminous outpourings," Strachey notes, "lay bare the inner history of Gordon's spirit, and reveal the pious visionary of Gravesend in the restless hero of three continents" [^1]. The phrase became Strachey's shorthand for Gordon's duality — the contemplative mystic who was also a man of action.

## Historical Context

The Thames fortifications that Gordon supervised were part of a mid-century programme of coastal defence, driven by fears of French naval power under Napoleon III. The forts — at Gravesend, Tilbury, and other Thames-side positions — represented the Victorian state's investment in defending the imperial capital. For Gordon, however, the engineering was secondary to the solitude it afforded.

## Key Events

- **1865–1871**: Gordon stationed at Gravesend, supervising Thames fort construction [^2]
- **Spiritual awakening**: Gordon's intense evangelical piety crystallised during these years of relative obscurity [^4]
- **1871**: Gordon's seclusion ended when he was appointed British representative on the International Commission for the Danube, a post that launched him into the wider world [^1]
