## Overview

Stratford Canning, 1st Viscount Stratford de Redcliffe (1786–1880), was Britain's long-serving ambassador to the Ottoman Empire and one of the most formidable diplomats of the Victorian era. In Lytton Strachey's *Eminent Victorians*, he appears in the [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]] narrative as the imposing figure at [[constantinople|Constantinople]] whose authority both aided and—at times—frustrated the nursing mission in the Crimea.

> When Lord Stratford de Redcliffe, our ambassador at Constantinople, was asked by Mr. Macdonald to indicate how The Times Fund could best be employed, he answered that there was indeed one essential object. [020-083]

## Ambassador at Constantinople

Stratford de Redcliffe's diplomatic career was dominated by his long tenure as ambassador to the Sublime Porte. His influence at [[constantinople|Constantinople]] was so great that he was known throughout Europe as "the Great Elchi" (the Great Ambassador). He was the man who, more than any other, shaped British policy towards the Ottoman Empire in the decades leading up to the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]].

## Involvement with Florence Nightingale's Mission

When [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]] arrived at [[scutari|Scutari]], she found herself operating in the shadow of Lord Stratford's immense diplomatic presence. The relationship was complex. Nightingale persuaded Stratford — or thought she had persuaded him — to sanction expenditures for the hospital, and a hundred and twenty-five workmen were immediately engaged [020-193].

But the ambassador's cooperation was not always reliable. When the workmen struck, Lord Stratford washed his hands of the affair [020-196]. The episode illustrates the characteristic tension between Nightingale's reforming drive and the cautious inertia of established authority — a pattern that runs throughout her story.

## Diplomatic Legacy

Stratford de Redcliffe's career spanned the long nineteenth century from the Napoleonic era to the age of [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]]. As a diplomat, he was noted for his commanding personal presence, his mastery of Ottoman affairs, and an imperious manner that at times exceeded even the authority of the Foreign Secretary in London. He was raised to the peerage as Viscount Stratford de Redcliffe in 1852.

Strachey's portrait of him, though brief, captures the essential quality: a great man of the old diplomatic school, accustomed to having his way at [[constantinople|Constantinople]], and mildly bewildered to find his authority contested by a determined woman with a lamp.
