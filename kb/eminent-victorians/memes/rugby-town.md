# Rugby

The Warwickshire market town that became, under [[dr-arnold|Dr. Arnold]], synonymous with the Victorian ideal of Christian gentlemanly education. Strachey's Rugby is less a geographical location than a moral laboratory — the place where [[Rugby School]], transformed by Arnold's theocratic vision, turned out generations of earnest, public-spirited, intellectually limited young men who went on to govern an empire. The [[public-school-education]] modelled at Rugby became one of the defining institutions of Victorian England.

## Location

Rugby is a market town in eastern Warwickshire, in the English Midlands. In Arnold's day it was a modest provincial centre, its life dominated by the school whose trustees — "the twelve trustees, noblemen and gentlemen of Warwickshire" (027-090) — had appointed the young headmaster in 1828. The town lay at a remove from the intellectual ferment of [[oxford-university|Oxford]] and the political bustle of London, a circumstance that suited Arnold's project of creating a self-contained moral community insulated from the corruptions of the wider world (027-089–104).

## In the Narrative

### Arnold's Rugby

Rugby appears in *Eminent Victorians* exclusively through the biography of Dr. Thomas Arnold, headmaster from 1828 until his sudden death in 1842. Strachey describes Arnold as "the man who, at the age of thirty-three, became headmaster of Rugby. His outward appearance was the index of his inward character; everything about him denoted energy, earnestness, and the best intentions" (027-171–174). The town itself fades into the background; what matters is the moral and spiritual regime Arnold erected within the school.

Arnold's ambition was "to make the school a place of really Christian education" (028-043), pursued by modelling Rugby's governance on Old Testament theocracy. "He would treat the boys at Rugby as Jehovah had treated the Chosen People: he would found a theocracy; and there should be judges in Israel" (028-079–081). The prefect system — whereby Sixth Form boys exercised authority over juniors, answerable only to the remote headmaster — created a hierarchy of moral surveillance in which, as Strachey wryly observes, "the Rugby schoolboy walked in a holy dread of some sudden manifestation of the sweeping gown, the majestic tone, the piercing glance, of Dr. Arnold" (028-098–102).

### The Town and Its People

Arnold's relationship with the town beyond the school gates was one of benevolent condescension. "Dr. Arnold himself occasionally visited them, in Rugby; and the condescension with which he shook hands with old men and women of the working classes was long remembered in the neighbourhood" (029-153–155). He divided the lower orders into the "good poor," who deserved Christian sympathy, and the rest — trades unionists and agitators — whom he regarded with "horror and alarm" (029-155–159). The town thus became the stage for Arnold's performance of what Strachey terms his "orthodox Liberalism" — tolerant of those with whom he agreed, suspicious of everyone else (029-131–144).

> "His great object, throughout his career at Rugby, was, as he repeatedly said, to 'make the school a place of really Christian education'. To introduce 'a religious principle into education', was his 'most earnest wish'." (028-042–048)

### Carlyle's Verdict

Thomas Carlyle, after visiting Rugby, characterised the Doctor as a man "full of good intentions but frightfully encumbered with churchwardenly self-importance" (030-145). The judgement captures the tension Strachey develops throughout — Arnold as a figure of moral seriousness whose limitations were inseparable from his virtues.

## Historical Context

Rugby School was founded in 1567 by Lawrence Sheriff, a London grocer born in the town, as a free grammar school. By the early nineteenth century it had evolved into a boarding school for the upper classes. When Arnold arrived, the public schools were "still virgin forests, untouched by the hand of reform" (028-002–003), dominated by the flogging regime of Keate at Eton.

Arnold embraced the middle-class demand for moral education but resisted utilitarian calls for a modern curriculum. "Physical science was not taught at Rugby," Strachey notes; Arnold judged it too large a subject to be studied incidentally, and rather than make it central he excluded it (029-045–049). Classical languages and the chapel remained the twin pillars of the Rugby system.

> "Rather than have physical science the principal thing in my son's mind, I would gladly have him think that the sun went around the earth, and that the stars were so many spangles set in the bright blue firmament." (029-050–053)

Rugby prospered under Arnold — "its numbers rose higher than ever before" (031-130) — and its graduates impressed Oxford with chapel-going and visits to the deserving poor (031-122–124). But the product was not scholarship: it was a type, the earnest, morally self-conscious gentleman. Even that proved unstable: Arthur Hugh Clough, Arnold's star pupil, lost his faith at Oxford and "spent the rest of his existence lamenting that loss" (031-112–115).

## Notable Figures Associated with Rugby

- **[[dr-arnold|Dr. Thomas Arnold]]** (1795–1842): Headmaster (1828–1842), the dominant figure of the town and Strachey's most sustained exercise in ironic portraiture
- **[[Arthur Hugh Clough]]** (1819–1861): Arnold's favourite pupil, sent to Rugby at ten, in the Sixth Form at sixteen — the most famous casualty of Arnold's system, who lost his faith at Oxford (031-082–115)
- **Matthew Arnold** (1822–1888): Arnold's son, poet and critic, educated at Rugby and Oxford
- **Lawrence Sheriff** (c. 1515–1567): The Rugby-born London grocer whose endowment founded the school in 1567
