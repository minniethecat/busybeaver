[015-001]Whether the effect of these affirmations upon Lord Clarendon was as

[015-002]great as Manning supposed is somewhat doubtful; but it is at any rate

[015-003]certain that [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] failed to carry the Cabinet with him; and,

[015-004]when at last a proposal was definitely made that the English Government

[015-005]should invite the Powers of Europe to intervene at the [[rome|Vatican]], it was

[015-006]rejected. Manning always believed that this was the direct result of Mr.

[015-007]Russell's dispatches, which had acted as an antidote to the poison of

[015-008]Lord Acton's letters, and thus carried the day. If that was so, the

[015-009]discretion of biographers has not yet entirely lifted the veil from

[015-010]these proceedings Manning had assuredly performed no small service for

[015-011]his cause. Yet his modesty would not allow him to assume for himself a

[015-012]credit which, after all, was due elsewhere; and when he told the story

[015-013]of those days, he would add, with more than wonted seriousness, 'It was

[015-014]by the Divine Will that the designs of His enemies were frustrated'.

[015-015]Meanwhile, in the North Transept of St. Peter's a certain amount of

[015-016]preliminary business had been carried through. Various miscellaneous

[015-017]points in Christian doctrine had been satisfactorily determined. Among

[015-018]others, the following Canons were laid down by the Fathers: 'If anyone

[015-019]does not accept for sacred and canonical the whole and every part of the

[015-020]Books of Holy Scripture, or deny that they are divinely inspired, let

[015-021]him be anathema.' 'If anyone says that miracles cannot be, and

[015-022]therefore, the accounts of them, even those in Holy Scriptures must be

[015-023]assigned a place among fables and myths, or that the divine origin of

[015-024]the Christian religion cannot rightly be proved from them, let him be

[015-025]anathema.' 'If anyone says that the doctrines of the Church can ever

[015-026]receive a sense in accordance with the progress of science, other than

[015-027]that sense which the Church has understood and still understands, let

[015-028]him be anathema.' 'If anyone says that it is not possible, by the

[015-029]natural light of human reason, to acquire a certain knowledge of the One

[015-030]and True God, let him be anathema.' In other words, it became an article

[015-031]of Faith that Faith was not necessary for a true knowledge of God.

[015-032]Having disposed of these minor matters, the Fathers found themselves at

[015-033]last approaching the great question of Infallibility.

[015-034]Two main issues, it soon appeared, were before them: the. Pope's

[015-035]infallibility was admitted, ostensibly at least, by all; what remained

[015-036]to be determined was: (1) whether the definition of the Pope's

[015-037]Infallibility was opportune, and (2) what the definition of the Pope's

[015-038]Infallibility was.

[015-039](1) It soon became clear that the sense of the Council was

[015-040]overwhelmingly in favour of a definition. The Inopportunists were a

[015-041]small minority; they were outvoted, and they were obliged to give way.

[015-042]It only remained, therefore, to come to a decision upon the second

[015-043]question--what the definition should actually be.

[015-044](2) It now became the object of the Inopportunists to limit the scope of

[015-045]the definition as much as possible, while the Infallibilists were no

[015-046]less eager to extend it. Now everyone, or nearly everyone, was ready to

[015-047]limit the [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]] to pronouncements ex cathedra--that is to

[015-048]say, to those made by the Pope in his capacity of Universal Doctor; but

[015-049]this only served to raise the ulterior, the portentous, and indeed the

[015-050]really crucial question--to WHICH of the Papal pronouncements ex

[015-051]cathedra did Infallibility adhere?

[015-052]The discussions which followed were, naturally enough, numerous,

[015-053]complicated, and embittered, and in all of them Manning played a

[015-054]conspicuous part. For two months the Fathers deliberated; through fifty

[015-055]sessions they sought the guidance of the Holy Ghost. The wooden seats,

[015-056]covered though they were with Brussels carpet, grew harder and harder;

[015-057]and still the mitred Councillors sat on. The Pope himself began to grow

[015-058]impatient; for one thing, he declared, he was being ruined by the mere

[015-059]expense of lodging and keeping the multitude of his adherents. 'Questi

[015-060]infallibilisti mi faranno fallire', said his Holiness. At length it

[015-061]appeared that the Inopportunists were dragging out the proceedings in

[015-062]the hope of obtaining an indefinite postponement. Then the authorities

[015-063]began to act; a bishop was shouted down, and the closure was brought

[015-064]into operation. At this point the French Government, after long

[015-065]hesitation, finally decided to intervene, and Cardinal Antonelli was

[015-066]informed that if the Definition was proceeded with, the French troops

[015-067]would be withdrawn from Rome. But the astute Cardinal judged that he

[015-068]could safely ignore the threat. He saw that Napoleon III was tottering

[015-069]to his fall and would never risk an open rupture with the Vatican.

[015-070]Accordingly, it was determined to bring the proceedings to a close by a

[015-071]final vote. Already the Inopportunists, seeing that the game was up, had

[015-072]shaken the dust of Rome from their feet. On July 18th, 1870, the Council

[015-073]met for the last time. As the first of the Fathers stepped forward to

[015-074]declare his vote, a storm of thunder and lightning suddenly burst over

[015-075]St. Peter's. All through the morning the voting continued, and every

[015-076]vote was accompanied by a flash and a roar from heaven. Both sides, with

[015-077]equal justice, claimed the portent as a manifestation of the Divine

[015-078]Opinion. When the votes were examined, it was found that 533 were in

[015-079]favour of the proposed definition and two against it. Next day, war was

[015-080]declared between France and Germany, and a few weeks later the French

[015-081]troops were withdrawn from Rome. Almost in the same moment, the

[015-082]successor of St. Peter had lost his Temporal Power, and gained

[015-083]Infallibility.

[015-084]What the Council had done was merely to assent to a definition of the

[015-085]dogma of the Infallibility of the Roman Pontiff which Pius IX had

[015-086]issued, proprio motu, a few days before. The definition itself was

[015-087]perhaps somewhat less extreme than might have been expected. The Pope,

[015-088]it declared, is possessed, when he speaks ex cathedra, of 'that

[015-089]infallibility with which the Redeemer willed that His Church should be

[015-090]endowed for defining doctrine regarding faith or morals'. Thus it became

[015-091]a dogma of faith that a Papal definition regarding faith or morals is

[015-092]infallible; but beyond that, both the Holy Father and the Council

[015-093]maintained a judicious reserve. Over what OTHER matters besides faith

[015-094]and morals the Papal infallibility might or might not extend still

[015-095]remained in doubt. And there were further questions, no less serious, to

[015-096]which no decisive answer was then, or ever has been since, provided.

[015-097]How was it to be determined, for instance, which particular Papal

[015-098]decisions did in fact come within the scope of the definition? Who was

[015-099]to decide what was or was not a matter of faith or morals? Or precisely

[015-100]WHEN the Roman Pontiff was speaking ex cathedra? Was the famous Syllabus

[015-101]Errorum, for example, issued ex cathedra or not? Grave theologians have

[015-102]never been able to make up their minds. Yet to admit doubts in such

[015-103]matters as these is surely dangerous. 'In duty to our supreme pastoral

[015-104]office,' proclaimed the Sovereign Pontiff, 'by the bowels of Christ we

[015-105]earnestly entreat all Christ's faithful people, and we also command them

[015-106]by the authority of God and our Saviour, that they study and labour to

[015-107]expel and eliminate errors and display the light of the purest faith.'

[015-108]Well might the faithful study and labour to such ends! For, while the

[015-109]offence remained ambiguous, there was no ambiguity about the penalty.

[015-110]One hair's-breadth from the unknown path of truth, one shadow of

[015-111]impurity in the mysterious light of faith, and there shall be anathema!

[015-112]anathema! anathema! When the framers of such edicts called upon the

[015-113]bowels of Christ to justify them, might they not have done well to have

[015-114]paused a little, and to have called to mind the counsel of another

[015-115]sovereign ruler, though a heretic--Oliver Cromwell? 'Bethink ye, bethink

[015-116]ye, in the bowels of Christ, that ye may be mistaken!'

[015-117]One of the secondary results of the Council was the excommunication of

[015-118]Dr. Dollinger, and a few more of the most uncompromising of the

[015-119]Inopportunists. Among these, however, Lord Acton was not included.

[015-120]Nobody ever discovered why. Was it because he was too important for the

[015-121]Holy See to care to interfere with him? Or was it because he was not

[015-122]important enough?

[015-123]Another ulterior consequence was the appearance of a pamphlet by Mr.

[015-124]Gladstone, entitled 'Vaticanism', in which the awful implications

[015-125]involved in the declaration of Infallibility were laid before the

[015-126]British Public. How was it possible, Mr. Gladstone asked, with all the

[015-127]fulminating accompaniments of his most agitated rhetoric, to depend

[015-128]henceforward upon the civil allegiance of Roman Catholics? To this

[015-129]question the words of Cardinal Antonelli to the Austrian Ambassador

[015-130]might have seemed a sufficient reply. 'There is a great difference,'

[015-131]said his Eminence, between theory and practice. No one will ever prevent

[015-132]the Church from proclaiming the great principles upon which its Divine

[015-133]fabric is based; but, as regards the application of those sacred laws,

[015-134]the Church, imitating the example of its Divine Founder, is inclined to

[015-135]take into consideration the natural weaknesses of mankind.' And, in any

[015-136]case, it was hard to see how the system of Faith, which had enabled Pope

[015-137]Gregory XIII to effect, by the hands of English Catholics, a whole

[015-138]series of attempts to murder Queen Elizabeth, can have been rendered a

[015-139]much more dangerous engine of disloyalty by the Definition of 1870. But

[015-140]such considerations failed to reassure Mr. Gladstone; the British Public

[015-141]was of a like mind; and 145,000 copies of the pamphlet were sold within

[015-142]two months. Various replies appeared, and Manning was not behindhand.

[015-143]His share in the controversy led to a curious personal encounter.

[015-144]His conversion had come as a great shock to Mr. Gladstone. Manning had

[015-145]breathed no word of its approach to his old and intimate friend, and

[015-146]when the news reached him, it seemed almost an act of personal injury.

[015-147]'I felt,' Mr. Gladstone said, 'as if Manning had murdered my mother by

[015-148]mistake.' For twelve years the two men did not meet, after which they

[015-149]occasionally saw each other and renewed their correspondence. This was

[015-150]the condition of affairs when Mr. Gladstone published his pamphlet. As

[015-151]soon as it appeared, Manning wrote a letter to the New York Herald,

[015-152]contradicting its conclusions and declaring that its publication was

[015-153]'the first event that has overcast a friendship of forty-five years'.

[015-154]Mr. Gladstone replied to this letter in a second pamphlet. At the close

[015-155]of his theological arguments, he added the following passage:

[015-156]'I feel it necessary, in concluding this answer, to state that

[015-157][[cardinal-manning|Archbishop Manning]] has fallen into most serious inaccuracy in his letter

[015-158]of November 10th, wherein he describes 'my Expostulation as the first

[015-159]event which has overcast a friendship of forty-five years. I allude to

[015-160]the subject with regret; and without entering into details.'

[015-161]Manning replied in a private letter:

[015-162]'My dear Gladstone,' he wrote, 'you say that I am in error in stating

[015-163]that your former pamphlet is the first act which has overcast our

[015-164]friendship.

[015-165]'If you refer to my act in 1851 in submitting to the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], by

[015-166]which we were separated for some twelve years, I can understand it.

[015-167]'If you refer to any other act either on your part or mine I am not

[015-168]conscious of it, and would desire to know what it may be.

[015-169]'My act in 1851 may have overcast your friendship for me. It did not

[015-170]overcast my friendship for you, as I think the last years have shown.

[015-171]'You will not, I hope, think me over-sensitive in asking for this

[015-172]explanation. Believe me, yours affectionately,

[015-173]'H. E. M.'

[015-174]'My dear Archbishop Manning,' Mr. Gladstone answered, 'it did, I

[015-175]confess, seem to me an astonishing error to state in public that a

[015-176]friendship had not been overcast for forty-five years until now, which

[015-177]your letter declares has been suspended as to all action for twelve ...
