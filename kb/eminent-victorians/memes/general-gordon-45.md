[045-001]One painful thought obsessed him. He believed that the two Egyptian

[045-002]officers, who had been put to death after the defeat in March, had been

[045-003]unjustly executed. He had given way to 'outside influences'; the two

[045-004]Pashas had been 'judicially murdered'. Again and again he referred to

[045-005]the incident with a haunting remorse. "The Times", perhaps, would

[045-006]consider that he had been justified; but what did that matter? 'If The

[045-007]Times saw this in print, it would say, "Why, then, did you act as you

[045-008]did?" to which I fear I have no answer.' He determined to make what

[045-009]reparation he could, and to send the families of the unfortunate Pashas

[045-010]L1,000 each.

[045-011]On a similar, but a less serious, occasion, he put the same principle

[045-012]into action. He boxed the ears of a careless telegraph clerk--'and then,

[045-013]as my conscience pricked me, I gave him $5. He said he did not mind if I

[045-014]killed him--I was his father (a chocolate-coloured youth of twenty).'

[045-015]His temper, indeed, was growing more and more uncertain, as he himself

[045-016]was well aware. He observed with horror that men trembled when they came

[045-017]into his presence--that their hands shook so that they could not hold a

[045-018]match to a cigarette.

[045-019]He trusted no one. Looking into the faces of those who surrounded him,

[045-020]he saw only the ill-dissimulated signs of treachery and dislike. Of the

[045-021]40,000 inhabitants of [[khartoum|Khartoum]] he calculated that two-thirds were

[045-022]willing--were perhaps anxious--to become the subjects of the Mahdi.

[045-023]'These people are not worth any great sacrifice,' he bitterly observed.

[045-024]The Egyptian officials were utterly incompetent; the soldiers were

[045-025]cowards. All his admiration was reserved for his enemies. The meanest of

[045-026]the Mahdi's followers was, he realised, 'a determined warrior, who could

[045-027]undergo thirst and privation, who no more cared for pain or death than

[045-028]if he were stone'. Those were the men whom, if the choice had lain with

[045-029]him, he would have wished to command. And yet, strangely enough, he

[045-030]persistently underrated the strength of the forces against him. A

[045-031]handful of Englishmen--a handful of Turks would, he believed, be enough

[045-032]to defeat the Mahdi's hosts and destroy his dominion. He knew very

[045-033]little Arabic, and he depended for his information upon a few ignorant

[045-034]English-speaking subordinates. The Mahdi himself he viewed with

[045-035]ambiguous feelings. He jibed at him as a vulgar impostor; but it is easy

[045-036]to perceive, under his scornful jocularities, the traces of an uneasy

[045-037]respect.

[045-038]He spent long hours upon the palace roof, gazing northwards; but the

[045-039]veil of mystery and silence was unbroken. In spite of the efforts of

[045-040]Major Kitchener, the officer in command of the Egyptian Intelligence

[045-041]Service, hardly any messengers ever reached Khartoum; and when they did,

[045-042]the information they brought was tormentingly scanty. Major Kitchener

[045-043]did not escape the attentions of Gordon's pen. When news came at last,

[045-044]it was terrible: Colonel Stewart and his companions had been killed. The

[045-045]Abbas, after having passed uninjured through the part of the river

[045-046]commanded by the Mahdi's troops, had struck upon a rock; Colonel Stewart

[045-047]had disembarked in safety; and, while he was waiting for camels to

[045-048]convey the detachment across the desert into Egypt, had accepted the

[045-049]hospitality of a local Sheikh. Hardly had the Europeans entered the

[045-050]Sheikh's hut when they were set upon and murdered; their native

[045-051]followers shared their fate. The treacherous Sheikh was an adherent of

[045-052]the Mahdi, and to the Mahdi all Colonel Stewart's papers, filled with

[045-053]information as to the condition of Khartoum, were immediately sent. When

[045-054]the first rumours of the disaster reached Gordon, he pictured, in a

[045-055]flash of intuition, the actual details of the catastrophe. 'I feel

[045-056]somehow convinced,' he wrote, they were captured by treachery ...

[045-057]Stewart was not a bit suspicious (I am made up of it). I can see in

[045-058]imagination the whole scene, the Sheikh inviting them to land ... then a

[045-059]rush of wild Arabs, and all is over!' 'It is very sad,' he added, 'but

[045-060]being ordained, we must not murmur.' And yet he believed that the true

[045-061]responsibility lay with him; it was the punishment of his own sins. 'I

[045-062]look on it,' was his unexpected conclusion, 'as being a Nemesis on the

[045-063]death of the two Pashas.'

[045-064]The workings of his conscience did indeed take on surprising shapes. Of

[045-065]the three ex-governors of Darfur, Bahr-el-Ghazal, and Equatoria, Emin

[045-066]Pasha had disappeared, Lupton Bey had died, and Slatin Pasha was held in

[045-067]captivity by the Mahdi. By birth an Austrian and a Catholic, Slatin, in

[045-068]the last desperate stages of his resistance, had adopted the expedient

[045-069]of announcing his conversion to Mohammedanism, in order to win the

[045-070]confidence of his native troops. On his capture, the fact of his

[045-071]conversion procured him some degree of consideration; and, though he

[045-072]occasionally suffered from the caprices of his masters, he had so far

[045-073]escaped the terrible punishment which had been meted out to some other

[045-074]of the Mahdi's European prisoners--that of close confinement in the

[045-075]common gaol. He was now kept prisoner in one of the camps in the

[045-076]neighbourhood of Khartoum. He managed to smuggle through a letter to

[045-077]Gordon, asking for assistance, in case he could make his escape. To this

[045-078]letter Gordon did not reply. Slatin wrote again and again; his piteous

[045-079]appeals, couched in no less piteous French, made no effect upon the

[045-080]heart of the Governor-General.

[045-081]'Excellence!' he wrote, 'J'ai envoye deux lettres, sans avoir recu une

[045-082]reponse de votre excellence.... Excellence! j'ai me battu 27 FOIS pour

[045-083]le gouvernement contre l'ennemi--on m'a feri deux fois, et j'ai rien

[045-084]fait contre l'honneur--rien de chose qui doit empeche votre excellence

[045-085]de m'ecrir une reponse que je sais quoi faire. JE VOUS PRIE, Excellence,

[045-086]de m'honore avec une reponse. P.S. Si votre Excellence ont peutetre

[045-087]entendu que j'ai fait quelque chose contre l'honneur d'un officier et

[045-088]cela vous empeche de m'ecrir, je vous prie de me donner l'occasion de me

[045-089]defendre, et jugez apres la verite.'

[045-090]The unfortunate Slatin understood well enough the cause of Gordon's

[045-091]silence. It was in vain that he explained the motives of his conversion,

[045-092]in vain that he pointed out that it had been made easier for him since

[045-093]he had, 'PERHAPS UNHAPPILY, not received a strict religious education at

[045-094]home'. Gordon was adamant. Slatin had 'denied his Lord', and that was

[045-095]enough. His communications with Khartoum were discovered and he was put

[045-096]in chains. When Gordon heard of it, he noted the fact grimly in his

[045-097]diary, without a comment.

[045-098]A more ghastly fate awaited another European who had fallen into the

[045-099]hands of the Mahdi. Clavier Pain, a French adventurer, who had taken

[045-100]part in the Commune, and who was now wandering, for reasons which have

[045-101]never been discovered, in the wastes of the Sudan, was seized by the

[045-102]Arabs, made prisoner, and hurried from camp to camp. He was attacked by

[045-103]fever; but mercy was not among the virtues of the savage soldiers who

[045-104]held him in their power. Hoisted upon the back of a camel, he was being

[045-105]carried across the desert, when, overcome by weakness, he lost his hold,

[045-106]and fell to the ground. Time or trouble were not to be wasted upon an

[045-107]infidel. Orders were given that he should be immediately buried; the

[045-108]orders were carried out; and in a few moments the cavalcade had left the

[045-109]little hillock far behind. But some of those who were present believed

[045-110]that Olivier Pain had been still breathing when his body was covered

[045-111]with the sand.

[045-112]Gordon, on hearing that a Frenchman had been captured by the Mahdi,

[045-113]became extremely interested. The idea occurred to him that this

[045-114]mysterious individual was none other than Ernest Renan, 'who,' he wrote,

[045-115]in his last publication 'takes leave of the world, and is said to have

[045-116]gone into Africa, not to reappear again'. He had met Renan at the rooms

[045-117]of the Royal Geographical Society, had noticed that he looked bored--the

[045-118]result, no doubt, of too much admiration--and had felt an instinct that

[045-119]he would meet him again. The instinct now seemed to be justified. There

[045-120]could hardly be any doubt that it WAS Renan; who else could it be? 'If

[045-121]he comes to the lines,' he decided, 'and it is Renan, I shall go and see

[045-122]him, for whatever one may think of his unbelief in our Lord, he

[045-123]certainly dared to say what he thought, and he has not changed his creed

[045-124]to save his life.' That the mellifluous author of the Vie de Jesus

[045-125]should have determined to end his days in the depths of Africa, and have

[045-126]come, in accordance with an intuition, to renew his acquaintance with

[045-127][[general-gordon|General Gordon]] in the lines of Khartoum, would indeed have been a

[045-128]strange occurrence; but who shall limit the strangeness of the

[045-129]possibilities that lie in wait for the sons of men? At that very moment,

[045-130]in the south-eastern corner of the Sudan, another Frenchman, of a

[045-131]peculiar eminence, was fulfilling a destiny more extraordinary than the

[045-132]wildest romance. In the town of Harrar, near the Red Sea, Arthur Rimbaud

[045-133]surveyed with splenetic impatience the tragedy of Khartoum.

[045-134]'C'est justement les Anglais,' he wrote, 'avec leur absurde politique,

[045-135]qui minent desormais le commerce de toutes ces cotes. Ils ont voulu tout

[045-136]remanier et ils sont arrives a faire pire que les Egyptiens et les

[045-137]Turcs, ruines par eux. Leur Gordon est un idiot, leur Wolseley un ane,

[045-138]et toutes leurs entreprises une suite insensee d'absurdites et de

[045-139]depredations.'

[045-140]So wrote the amazing poet of the Saison d'Enfer amid those futile

[045-141]turmoils of petty commerce, in which, with an inexplicable deliberation,

[045-142]he had forgotten the enchantments of an unparalleled adolescence,

[045-143]forgotten the fogs of London and the streets of Brussels, forgotten

[045-144]Paris, forgotten the subtleties and the frenzies of inspiration,

[045-145]forgotten the agonised embraces of Verlaine.

[045-146]When the contents of Colonel Stewart's papers had been interpreted to

[045-147]the Mahdi, he realised the serious condition of Khartoum, and decided

[045-148]that the time had come to press the siege to a final conclusion. At the

[045-149]end of October, he himself, at the head of a fresh army, appeared

[045-150]outside the town. From that moment, the investment assumed a more and

[045-151]more menacing character. The lack of provisions now for the first time

[045-152]began to make itself felt. November 30th--the date fixed by Gordon as

[045-153]the last possible moment of his resistance--came and went; the

[045-154]Expeditionary Force had made no sign. The fortunate discovery of a large

[045-155]store of grain, concealed by some merchants for purposes of speculation,

[045-156]once more postponed the catastrophe. But the attacking army grew daily

[045-157]more active; the skirmishes around the lines and on the river more

[045-158]damaging to the besieged; and the Mahdi's guns began an intermittent

[045-159]bombardment of the palace. By December 10th it was calculated that there

[045-160]was not fifteen days' food in the town; 'truly I am worn to a shadow

[045-161]with the food question', Gordon wrote; 'it is one continuous demand'. At

[045-162]the same time he received the ominous news that five of his soldiers had

[045-163]deserted to the Mahdi. His predicament was terrible; but he calculated,

[045-164]from a few dubious messages that had reached him, that the relieving

[045-165]force could not be very far away. Accordingly, on the 14th, he decided

[045-166]to send down one of his four remaining steamers, the Bordeen, to meet it

[045-167]at Metemmah, in order to deliver to the officer in command the latest

[045-168]information as to the condition of the town. The Bordeen carried down

[045-169]the last portion of the Journals, and Gordon's final messages to his

[045-170]friends. Owing to a misunderstanding, he believed that Sir Evelyn Baring

[045-171]was accompanying the expedition from Egypt, and some of his latest and

[045-172]most successful satirical fancies played around the vision of the

[045-173]distressed Consul-General perched for days upon the painful eminence of

[045-174]a camel's hump. 'There was a slight laugh when Khartoum heard Baring was

[045-175]bumping his way up here--a regular Nemesis.' But, when Sir Evelyn Baring

[045-176]actually arrived--in whatever condition--what would happen? Gordon lost

[045-177]himself in the multitude of his speculations. His own object, he

[045-178]declared, was, 'of course, to make tracks'. Then in one of his strange

[045-179]premonitory rhapsodies, he threw out, half in jest and half in earnest,

[045-180]that the best solution of all the difficulties of the future would be

[045-181]the appointment of Major Kitchener as Governor-General of the Sudan. The
