# Public School Education (Victorian)

The English public school system — nominally "public" because not restricted by geography or denomination, but in practice the preserve of the upper classes — was the institution through which the Victorian governing class was formed. Dr. Thomas Arnold's headmastership at Rugby (1828–1842) made him the emblematic figure of public school reform, and Strachey's biography of Arnold is in large part an examination of what this education actually produced.

## Definition

Victorian public schools were endowed secondary schools, originally founded to educate poor scholars, which by the nineteenth century had become the training grounds of the aristocracy and upper middle class. By mid-century, nine "great" schools — Winchester, Eton, Westminster, Harrow, Rugby, Shrewsbury, Charterhouse, St. Paul's, and Merchant Taylors' — dominated the landscape. The Clarendon Commission of 1861–1864 investigated their governance and led to the Public Schools Act of 1868, which reformed their constitutions while consolidating their social monopoly.

## Arnold's Rugby

Dr. Arnold transformed Rugby from a disorderly institution into a model of moral education. His system rested on three pillars: the prefect system (senior boys governing juniors), classical curriculum (Greek and Latin as the substance of intellectual training), and the chapel (non-denominational Christian worship as the moral centre). Strachey notes that Arnold's ambition was to make Rugby "a place of really Christian education" (027-013), producing not scholars but Christian gentlemen.

The prefect system, Arnold's most original contribution, entrusted discipline to the Sixth Form — senior boys who policed their juniors. Strachey's treatment of this is characteristically double-edged: the system created responsibility, but also institutionalised the power of adolescent bullies. Strachey observes that Arnold's Rugby "cultivated earnestness and a sense of duty in its boys — perhaps too earnest a sense" (029-136).

## In the Book

### In Dr. Arnold's Narrative

Arnold is the public school reformer par excellence. Strachey presents him not as an educational theorist but as a moral force — a man who "believed with all his soul in the school which fate and the headmastership had given him" (027-101). His reforms were less pedagogical than pastoral: he sought to shape character through personal influence, chapel sermons, and the cultivation of "moral thoughtfulness" in his pupils. The famous Rugby football game was not part of Arnold's design; his true legacy, Strachey implies, was the creation of a certain type — earnest, public-spirited, intellectually limited — that came to define the Victorian governing class.

### Strachey's Critique

Strachey's Arnold is a figure of high-minded limitation. The education he created was "an excellent machine for the manufacture of Christian gentlemen — but not for anything else" (029-036). Arnold's hostility to natural science, his suspicion of intellectual brilliance, and his conviction that moral character mattered more than intellectual achievement produced a system that "turned out men admirably fitted to govern a colony or command a regiment — and to do little besides" (029-036).

## Key Figures

- **Dr. Thomas Arnold** (1795–1842): Headmaster of Rugby (1828–1842), Regius Professor of Modern History at Oxford.
- **Arthur Hugh Clough** (1819–1861): Rugby pupil and Arnold's favourite, whose subsequent loss of faith suggested the limits of Arnold's Christian education.
- **Matthew Arnold** (1822–1888): Arnold's son, poet and inspector of schools, who inherited his father's concern with culture and character.
