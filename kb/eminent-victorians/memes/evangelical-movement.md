# Evangelical Movement

The Evangelical movement within the Church of England was the low-church, Protestant revival tradition that emphasised personal conversion, the supreme authority of Scripture, the centrality of Christ's Atonement, and an activist piety — the form of Christianity in which Henry Edward Manning was raised, and against which he would spend his life reacting.

## Definition

Evangelical Anglicanism was defined by several core commitments: the necessity of personal conversion (being "born again"), the sole authority of the Bible in matters of faith, the substitutionary Atonement of Christ on the cross, and a practical Christianity expressed in philanthropy, missionary work, and moral reform. Evangelicals were "earnest over the Atonement, confessed to a personal love of Jesus Christ, and seemed to have arranged the whole of their lives, down to the minutest details of act and speech, with reference to Eternity" (002-094). Strachey notes drily that "such extremes were the rare exceptions" in the complacent Church of the 1820s (002-098).

## In the Book

### Manning's Evangelical Childhood

The Manning household was steeped in Evangelical piety. Strachey opens the biography with the image of the young Manning's mother asking him whether he had seen a peacock in the farmyard and the nursemaid answering no — a small incident whose significance lay in the mother's moralising: "she declared that his nurse had told a falsehood" (001-135). This atmosphere of scrupulous moral vigilance, of every word and act examined for eternal significance, defined Manning's earliest formation.

When Manning became Archdeacon of Chichester and was drawn into the Oxford Movement, he "shook off his early Evangelical convictions" and began an active correspondence with Newman (005-130). Strachey treats this as the first of several decisive ideological ruptures in Manning's life — from Evangelicalism to Tractarianism, from Anglicanism to Rome, and finally to Ultramontanism — each a more extreme version of the last.

### Evangelicalism vs. Tractarianism

The Oxford Movement defined itself in opposition to Evangelicalism. Where Tractarians emphasised sacraments, priesthood, and the visible Church, Evangelicals stressed personal faith, Bible-reading, and inward conversion. The conflict was both theological and social: Tractarianism was an Oxford movement of dons and fellows; Evangelicalism was broader, more populist, and had greater purchase among the middle classes. The *Record* newspaper, "an influential Evangelical journal," took up the anti-Tractarian cause and "sniffed Popery in every direction" (004-167).

### Evangelicalism in the Nightingale Narrative

Florence Nightingale's own spiritual formation was shaped by Evangelical influences, though her mature religious outlook was more mystical and less doctrinal. Her famous "call to service" occurred within the Evangelical framework of personal vocation, even as she transcended its limitations.

## Key Figures

- **William Wilberforce** (1759–1833): The Clapham Sect leader whose blend of Evangelical piety and social reform (abolition of the slave trade) set the template for Anglican Evangelical activism. Manning's early patron, Samuel Wilberforce (the bishop), was his son.
- **Charles Simeon** (1759–1836): Cambridge clergyman who revived Evangelical preaching in the university, influencing generations of ordinands.
- **Lord Shaftesbury** (1801–1885): The great Evangelical philanthropist and factory reformer.

## Relationship to Other Movements

- **Oxford Movement / Tractarianism**: The theological antagonist. Where Tractarianism sought to recover Catholicity, Evangelicalism insisted on Protestant purity.
- **Broad Church**: The liberal Anglican middle way, equally opposed to Evangelical "enthusiasm" and Tractarian "superstition."
- **Methodism**: The evangelical revival outside the Church of England from which Anglican Evangelicalism drew much of its spiritual energy while maintaining the establishment position.
