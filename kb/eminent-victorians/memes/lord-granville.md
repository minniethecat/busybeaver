# Lord Granville (2nd Earl Granville)

Foreign Secretary in [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]]'s second administration, whose cautious handling of the Gordon mission epitomized the Government's hesitant response to the Sudan crisis in 1884–1885.

## Life

Granville George Leveson-Gower, 2nd Earl Granville, was born in 1815 into one of the great Whig families. He entered [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] as a young man and served in a series of high offices — including Foreign Secretary three times — across a political career that spanned four decades. A close ally of [[william-ewart-gladstone]], Granville was the Foreign Secretary when [[general-gordon|General Gordon]] was dispatched to the Sudan.

### The Gordon Mission

> "If you resolve to send Gordon, pray let him be under Sir Evelyn Baring's orders." [038-041]

Granville's role in the Gordon affair was central and, in Strachey's telling, deeply compromised. When the Cabinet decided to send [[general-gordon]] to the Sudan, it fell to Granville, as Foreign Secretary, to manage the diplomatic dimensions of the mission. He seemed, at first, to understand the dangers: on January 10th, 1884, he telegraphed that Gordon should be placed under [[evelyn-baring]]'s authority [038-041]. Yet within days, he had authorized Gordon's mission without adequate safeguards.

Strachey's Granville is a figure of well-meaning but fatal indecision. "Clearly, on the part of Lord Granville at any rate," Strachey writes with characteristic irony, "there was no lack of good intentions" [039-019]. Granville bought Gordon's railway tickets through France [039-109], dispatched his instructions, and then watched as events spiralled beyond his control.

### The Failure

When Gordon was besieged in [[khartoum|Khartoum]], Granville became the object of mounting frustration — from [[evelyn-baring]] in Cairo, from the press in London, and eventually from his own Cabinet colleagues. "The question now is," Baring told Granville, whether the Government understood the gravity of the position [040-163]. Granville's responses were consistently conciliatory and consistently ineffectual.

Strachey captures the Foreign Secretary's limitations in a devastating phrase: "Lord Granville was made to exclaim in another passage: 'What that Mahdi is about, Lord Granville is entirely unable to imagine'" [044-090]. The man responsible for British diplomacy in the region could not fathom the forces arrayed against him.

### Later Years

Granville continued to serve as Foreign Secretary until [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]]'s government fell in 1885. He returned briefly to the Colonial Office in 1886 before retiring from active politics. He died in 1891.

## Character and Beliefs

Granville represented the Whig tradition at its most polished — courteous, well-intentioned, fundamentally decent — and, in Strachey's view, fundamentally inadequate to the challenges of imperial governance. He was a diplomat by instinct, always seeking compromise and conciliation. But in the Sudan, there was no compromise to be had with the Mahdi, and conciliation could not rescue Gordon from [[khartoum|Khartoum]].

## Historical Significance

Lord Granville's tenure as Foreign Secretary spanned critical moments in British imperial history. Beyond the Gordon crisis, he dealt with the aftermath of the First Boer War, tensions with Russia in Central Asia, and the complexities of the European alliance system. His career illustrates the transition from mid-Victorian diplomatic methods to the harder-edged imperialism of the late-Victorian and Edwardian periods.

## In This Book

Granville appears exclusively in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47). Strachey uses him to personify the failures of Gladstonian foreign policy: good intentions without the will to act, diplomatic courtesy without strategic clarity. Granville is not vilified — Strachey's irony is too gentle for that — but he emerges as an emblem of a system that could not bring itself to do what the situation demanded.
