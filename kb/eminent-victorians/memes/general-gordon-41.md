[041-001]'Either I must believe He does all things in mercy and love, or else I

[041-002]disbelieve His existence; there is no half way in the matter. What holes

[041-003]do I not put myself into! And for what? So mixed are my ideas. I believe

[041-004]ambition put me here in this ruin.'

[041-005]Was not that the explanation of it all? 'Our Lord's promise is not for

[041-006]the fulfilment of earthly wishes; therefore, if things come to ruin here

[041-007]He is still faithful, and is carrying out His great work of divine

[041-008]wisdom.' How could he have forgotten that? But he would not transgress

[041-009]again. 'I owe all to God, and nothing to myself, for, humanly speaking,

[041-010]I have done very foolish things. However, if I am humbled, the better

[041-011]for me.'

[041-012]News of the changed circumstances at [[khartoum|Khartoum]] was not slow in reaching

[041-013]England, and a feeling of anxiety began to spread. Among the first to

[041-014]realise the gravity of the situation was Queen Victoria. 'It is

[041-015]alarming,' she telegraphed to Lord Hartington on March 25th. 'General

[041-016]Gordon is in danger; you are bound to try to save him ... You have

[041-017]incurred a fearful responsibility.' With an unerring instinct, Her

[041-018]Majesty forestalled and expressed the popular sentiment. During April,

[041-019]when it had become clear that the wire between Khartoum and Cairo had

[041-020]been severed; when, as time passed, no word came northward, save vague

[041-021]rumours of disaster; when at last a curtain of impenetrable mystery

[041-022]closed over Khartoum, the growing uneasiness manifested itself in

[041-023]letters to the newspapers, in leading articles, and in a flood of

[041-024]subscriptions towards a relief fund. At the beginning of May, the public

[041-025]alarm reached a climax. It now appeared to be certain, not only that

[041-026][[general-gordon|General Gordon]] was in imminent danger, but that no steps had yet been

[041-027]taken by the Government to save him.

[041-028]On the 5th, there was a meeting of protest and indignation at St.

[041-029]James's Hall; on the 9th there was a mass meeting in Hyde Park; on the

[041-030]11th there was a meeting at Manchester. The Baroness Burdett-Coutts

[041-031]wrote an agitated letter to "The Times" begging for further

[041-032]subscriptions. Somebody else proposed that a special fund should be

[041-033]started with which 'to bribe the tribes to secure the General's personal

[041-034]safety'. A country vicar made another suggestion. Why should not public

[041-035]prayers be offered up for General Gordon in every church in the kingdom?

[041-036]He himself had adopted that course last Sunday. 'Is not this,' he

[041-037]concluded, 'what the godly man, the true hero, himself would wish to be

[041-038]done?' It was all of no avail. General Gordon remained in peril; the

[041-039]Government remained inactive. Finally, a vote of censure was moved in

[041-040]the House of Commons; but that too proved useless. It was strange; the

[041-041]same executive which, two months before, had trimmed its sails so

[041-042]eagerly to the shifting gusts of popular opinion, now, in spite of a

[041-043]rising hurricane, held on its course. A new spirit, it was clear--a

[041-044]determined, an intractable spirit--had taken control of the Sudan

[041-045]situation. What was it? The explanation was simple, and it was ominous.

[041-046][[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] had intervened.

[041-047]The old statesman was now entering upon the penultimate period of his

[041-048]enormous career. He who had once been the rising hope of the stern and

[041-049]unbending Tories, had at length emerged, after a lifetime of

[041-050]transmutations, as the champion of militant democracy. He was at the

[041-051]apex of his power. His great rival was dead; he stood pre-eminent in the

[041-052]eye of the nation; he enjoyed the applause, the confidence, the

[041-053]admiration, the adoration, even, of multitudes. Yet--such was the

[041-054]peculiar character of the man, and such was the intensity of the

[041-055]feelings which he called forth--at this very moment, at the height of

[041-056]his popularity, he was distrusted and loathed; already an unparalleled

[041-057]animosity was gathering its forces against him. For, indeed, there was

[041-058]something in his nature which invited--which demanded--the clashing

[041-059]reactions of passionate extremes. It was easy to worship Mr. Gladstone;

[041-060]to see in him the perfect model of the upright man--the man of virtue

[041-061]and of religion--the man whose whole life had been devoted to the

[041-062]application of high principles to affairs of State; the man, too, whose

[041-063]sense of right and justice was invigorated and ennobled by an

[041-064]enthusiastic heart. It was also easy to detest him as a hypocrite, to

[041-065]despise him as a demagogue, and to dread him as a crafty manipulator of

[041-066]men and things for the purposes of his own ambition.

[041-067]It might have been supposed that one or other of these conflicting

[041-068]judgments must have been palpably absurd, that nothing short of gross

[041-069]prejudice or wilful blindness, on one side or the other, could reconcile

[041-070]such contradictory conceptions of a single human being. But it was not

[041-071]so; 'the elements' were 'so mixed' in Mr. Gladstone that his bitterest

[041-072]enemies (and his enemies were never mild) and his warmest friends (and

[041-073]his friends were never tepid) could justify, with equal plausibility,

[041-074]their denunciations or their praises. What, then, was the truth? In the

[041-075]physical universe there are no chimeras. But man is more various than

[041-076]nature; was Mr. Gladstone, perhaps, a chimera of the spirit? Did his

[041-077]very essence lie in the confusion of incompatibles? His very essence? It

[041-078]eludes the hand that seems to grasp it. One is baffled, as his political

[041-079]opponents were baffled fifty years ago. The soft serpent coils harden

[041-080]into quick strength that has vanished, leaving only emptiness and

[041-081]perplexity behind. Speech was the fibre of his being; and, when he

[041-082]spoke, the ambiguity of ambiguity was revealed. The long, winding,

[041-083]intricate sentences, with their vast burden of subtle and complicated

[041-084]qualifications, befogged the mind like clouds, and like clouds, too,

[041-085]dropped thunder bolts. Could it not then at least be said of him with

[041-086]certainty that his was a complex character? But here also there was a

[041-087]contradiction.

[041-088]In spite of the involutions of his intellect and the contortions of his

[041-089]spirit, it is impossible not to perceive a strain of naivete in Mr.

[041-090]Gladstone. He adhered to some of his principles that of the value of

[041-091]representative institutions, for instance with a faith which was

[041-092]singularly literal; his views upon religion were uncritical to

[041-093]crudeness; he had no sense of humour. Compared with Disraeli's, his

[041-094]attitude towards life strikes one as that of an ingenuous child. His

[041-095]very egoism was simple-minded; through all the labyrinth of his passions

[041-096]there ran a single thread. But the centre of the labyrinth? Ah! the

[041-097]thread might lead there, through those wandering mazes, at last. Only,

[041-098]with the last corner turned, the last step taken, the explorer might

[041-099]find that he was looking down into the gulf of a crater. The flame shot

[041-100]out on every side, scorching and brilliant; but in the midst, there was

[041-101]a darkness.

[041-102]That Mr. Gladstone's motives and ambitions were not merely those of a

[041-103]hunter after popularity was never shown more clearly than in that part

[041-104]of his career which, more than any other, has been emphasised by his

[041-105]enemies--his conduct towards General Gordon. He had been originally

[041-106]opposed to Gordon's appointment, but he had consented to it partly,

[041-107]perhaps, owing to the persuasion that its purpose did not extend beyond

[041-108]the making of a 'report'. Gordon once gone, events had taken their own

[041-109]course; the policy of the Government began to slide, automatically, down

[041-110]a slope at the bottom of which lay the conquest of the Sudan and the

[041-111]annexation of Egypt. Sir Gerald Graham's bloody victories awoke Mr.

[041-112]Gladstone to the true condition of affairs; he recognised the road he

[041-113]was on and its destination; but there was still time to turn back.

[041-114]It was he who had insisted upon the withdrawal of the English army from

[041-115]the Eastern Sudan. The imperialists were sadly disappointed. They had

[041-116]supposed that the old lion had gone to sleep, and suddenly he had come

[041-117]out of his lair, and was roaring. All their hopes now centred upon

[041-118]Khartoum. General Gordon was cut off; he was surrounded, he was in

[041-119]danger; he must be relieved. A British force must be sent to save him.

[041-120]But Mr. Gladstone was not to be caught napping a second time. When the

[041-121]agitation rose, when popular sentiment was deeply stirred, when the

[041-122]country, the Press, the Sovereign herself, declared that the national

[041-123]honour was involved with the fate of General Gordon, Mr. Gladstone

[041-124]remained immovable. Others might picture the triumphant rescue of a

[041-125]Christian hero from the clutches of heathen savages; before HIS eyes was

[041-126]the vision of battle, murder, and sudden death, the horrors of defeat

[041-127]and victory, the slaughter and the anguish of thousands, the violence of

[041-128]military domination, the enslavement of a people.

[041-129]The invasion of the Sudan, he had flashed out in the House of Commons,

[041-130]would be a war of conquest against a people struggling to be free. 'Yes,

[041-131]those people are struggling to be free, and they are rightly struggling

[041-132]to be free.' Mr. Gladstone--it was one of his old-fashioned

[041-133]simplicities--believed in liberty. If, indeed, it should turn out to be

[041-134]the fact that General Gordon was in serious danger, then, no doubt, it

[041-135]would be necessary to send a relief expedition to Khartoum. But, he

[041-136]could see no sufficient reason to believe that it was the fact.

[041-137]Communications, it was true, had been interrupted between Khartoum and

[041-138]Cairo, but no news was not necessarily bad news, and the little

[041-139]information that had come through from General Gordon seemed to indicate

[041-140]that he could hold out for months. So his agile mind worked, spinning

[041-141]its familiar web of possibilities and contingencies and fine

[041-142]distinctions. General Gordon, he was convinced, might be hemmed in, but

[041-143]he was not surrounded. Surely, it was the duty of the Government to take

[041-144]no rash step, but to consider and to inquire, and, when it acted, to act

[041-145]upon reasonable conviction. And then, there was another question. If it

[041-146]was true--and he believed it was true--that General Gordon's line of

[041-147]retreat was open, why did not General Gordon use it?

[041-148]Perhaps he might be unable to withdraw the Egyptian garrison, but it was

[041-149]not for the sake of the Egyptian garrison that the relief expedition was

[041-150]proposed; it was simply and solely to secure the personal safety of

[041-151]General Gordon. And General Gordon had it in his power to secure his

[041-152]personal safety himself; and he refused to do so; he lingered on in

[041-153]Khartoum, deliberately, wilfully, in defiance of the obvious wishes of

[041-154]his superiors. Oh! it was perfectly clear what General Gordon was doing:

[041-155]he was trying to force the hand of the English Government. He was hoping

[041-156]that if he only remained long enough at Khartoum, he would oblige the

[041-157]English Government to send an army into the Sudan which should smash up

[041-158]the Mahdi. That, then, was General Gordon's calculation! Well, General

[041-159]Gordon would learn that he had made a mistake. Who was he that he should

[041-160]dare to imagine that he could impose his will upon Mr. Gladstone? The

[041-161]old man's eyes glared. If it came to a struggle between them--well, they

[041-162]should see! As the weeks passed, the strange situation grew tenser. It

[041-163]was like some silent deadly game of bluff. And who knows what was

[041-164]passing in the obscure depths of that terrifying spirit? What mysterious

[041-165]mixture of remorse, rage, and jealousy? Who was it that was ultimately

[041-166]responsible for sending General Gordon to Khartoum? But then, what did

[041-167]that matter? Why did not the man come back? He was a Christian hero,

[041-168]wasn't he? Were there no other Christian heroes in the world? A

[041-169]Christian hero! Let him wait until the Mahdi's ring was really round

[041-170]him, until the Mahdi's spear was really about to fall! That would be the

[041-171]test of heroism! If he slipped back then, with his tail between his

[041-172]legs--! The world would judge.

[041-173]One of the last telegrams sent by Gordon before the wire was cut seemed

[041-174]to support exactly Mr. Gladstone's diagnosis of the case. He told Sir

[041-175]Evelyn Baring that, since the Government refused to send either an

[041-176]expedition or Zobeir, he would 'consider himself free to act according

[041-177]to circumstances.' 'Eventually,' he said, 'you will be forced to smash

[041-178]up the Mahdi', and he declared that if the Government persisted in its

[041-179]present line of conduct, it would be branded with an 'indelible

[041-180]disgrace'. The message was made public, and it happened that Mr.

[041-181]Gladstone saw it for the first time in a newspaper, during a country

[041-182]visit. Another of the guests, who was in the room at the moment, thus

[041-183]describes the scene: 'He took up the paper, his eye instantly fell on

[041-184]the telegram, and he read it through. As he read, his face hardened and

[041-185]whitened, the eyes burned as I have seen them once or twice in the House

[041-186]of Commons when he was angered--burned with a deep fire, as if they

[041-187]would have consumed the sheet on which Gordon's message was printed, or

[041-188]as if Gordon's words had burned into his soul, which was looking out in

[041-189]wrath and flame. He said not a word. For perhaps two or three minutes he

[041-190]sat still, his face all the while like the face you may read of in

[041-191]Milton--like none other I ever saw. Then he rose, still without a word,

[041-192]and was seen no more that morning.'
