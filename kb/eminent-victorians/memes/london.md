# London

The city through which the currents of Victorian power flowed. Strachey's four biographies trace arcs that all intersect in London — Manning arriving from the provinces to build his ecclesiastical career, Nightingale returning from the Crimea to conduct her campaigns of reform, Arnold's influence radiating outward from Rugby to the capital's newspapers, and Gordon's fatal mission beginning and ending in London offices. In the narrative of *Eminent Victorians*, London is less a place described than the steady, humming background against which all ambition is measured.

## In the Narrative

London appears in every corner of the book, but Strachey rarely describes its streets or monuments directly. Instead, it is the arena of action: the city where Cardinal Manning's funeral procession brought "vast crowds of working people" into the streets, impressed by "the magnetic vigour of the dead man's spirit" [^1]; where Cardinal Wiseman sought to reanimate English Catholicism with Manning as his instrument [^2]; where Florence Nightingale, after her return from the East, conducted her social and administrative campaigns in Mayfair drawing-rooms and government offices [^3].

### Association with the Eminent Victorians

Each of Strachey's subjects passed through London at a decisive moment. **Cardinal Manning** built his ecclesiastical career there, rising from Archdeacon of Chichester to Archbishop of Westminster, and was buried there in a funeral that seemed to the crowds "something untameable" [^1]. **Florence Nightingale** returned from Scutari in 1856 and, after a brief interval in London society, withdrew into the invalid's room from which she conducted decades of bureaucratic warfare against the Medical Department and the War Office. **Dr. Arnold** never lived in the capital, but his ideas migrated there through the press, where *The Times* printed his educational manifestos. **General Gordon**, between his African and Chinese adventures, made fleeting appearances in London that only seemed to confirm his alienation from the conventions of English life.

## Key Sites in the Narrative

- **Archbishop's House, Westminster**: Manning's Episcopal palace — the seat of Roman Catholic authority in England [^1]
- **Mayfair**: The district where Miss Nightingale's family rented rooms for the London season, providing a surreal contrast to the Crimean wards [^3]
- **The War Office**: The institution Nightingale besieged with her memoranda and statistics after the war
- **Fleet Street and *The Times***: The press that carried Russell's Crimean dispatches and Arnold's educational letters alike
