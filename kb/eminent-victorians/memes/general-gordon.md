# General Charles George Gordon

Gordon was the most peculiar of Strachey's four subjects: a soldier who hated war, an imperial governor who despised empire, a Christian whose faith was so private it verged on heresy. His death at [[khartoum|Khartoum]] in 1885, holding out against the Mahdist forces while a British relief expedition crawled up the Nile, made him the most famous Victorian martyr. Strachey's biography is an anatomy of that martyrdom — less a celebration than a diagnosis.

## Life

### Early Campaigns

Gordon served with distinction in the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]], but his first great command came in China. As commander of the "Ever-Victorious Army," he suppressed the Taiping Rebellion (1863–1864) with a combination of military efficiency and moral authority that made him famous. The "Chinese Gordon" who returned to England was already a legend.

### Governor of the Sudan

Gordon's appointment as Governor of the Equatorial Provinces (1874–1879) and then Governor-General of the Sudan placed him at the centre of Britain's informal empire in Africa. His administration was driven by two obsessions: the suppression of the slave trade and the conviction that Africans were his spiritual equals. He governed with a Bible in one hand and a cane in the other — the latter used not as a weapon but as a symbol of patriarchal authority.

### Khartoum

In 1884, the British government sent Gordon to [[khartoum|Khartoum]] to evacuate Egyptian garrisons threatened by the Mahdist uprising. Once there, Gordon decided evacuation was impossible — or perhaps he simply could not bear to leave. He fortified the city and held out for nearly a year against overwhelming forces. The British relief expedition arrived on January 28, 1885 — two days after the Mahdi's forces had breached the defences and killed Gordon on the steps of the palace.

## Strachey's Portrait

Strachey sees in Gordon a death-wish disguised as duty. The man who gave away his pay, who treated Sudanese slaves as brothers, who communed with God in the desert and recorded his mystical experiences in a journal — this was not a soldier in the conventional sense. He was, Strachey implies, a kind of saint: but a saint who needed a martyrdom to complete his sainthood, and found one at [[khartoum|Khartoum]].

The famous "too late" of the relief expedition is, in Strachey's telling, not an accident but the fulfilment of Gordon's deepest wish. He had been seeking death all his life. [[khartoum|Khartoum]] gave it to him.
