[032-001]The end of the term was approaching, and to all appearance the Doctor

[032-002]was in excellent spirits. On June 11th, after a hard day's work, he

[032-003]spent the evening with a friend in the discussion of various topics upon

[032-004]which he often touched in his conversation the comparison of the art of

[032-005]medicine in barbarous and civilised ages, the philological importance of

[032-006]provincial vocabularies, and the threatening prospect of the moral

[032-007]condition of the United States. Left alone, he turned to his diary.

[032-008]'The day after tomorrow,' he wrote, 'is my birthday, if I am permitted

[032-009]to live to see it--my forty-seventh birthday since my birth. How large a

[032-010]portion of my life on earth is already passed! And then--what is to

[032-011]follow this life? How visibly my outward work seems contracting and

[032-012]softening away into the gentler employments of old age. In one sense how

[032-013]nearly can I now say, "Vivi". And I thank God that, as far as ambition

[032-014]is concerned, it is, I trust, fully mortified; I have no desire other

[032-015]than to step back from my present place in the world, and not to rise to

[032-016]a higher. Still there are works which, with God's permission, I would do

[032-017]before the night cometh.'

[032-018]Dr. Arnold was thinking of his great work on Church and State.

[032-019]Early next morning he awoke with a sharp pain in his chest. The pain

[032-020]increasing, a physician was sent for; and in the meantime Mrs. Arnold

[032-021]read aloud to her husband the Fifty-first Psalm. Upon one of their boys

[032-022]coming into the room,

[032-023]'My son, thank God for me,' said Dr. Arnold; and as the boy did not at

[032-024]once catch his meaning, he added, 'Thank God, Tom, for giving me this

[032-025]pain; I have suffered so little pain in my life that I feel it is very

[032-026]good for me. Now God has given it to me, and I do so thank Him for it.'

[032-027]Then Mrs. Arnold read from the Prayer-book the 'Visitation of the Sick',

[032-028]her husband listening with deep attention, and assenting with an

[032-029]emphatic 'Yes' at the end of many of the sentences. When the physician

[032-030]arrived, he perceived at once the gravity of the case: it was an attack

[032-031]of angina pectoris. He began to prepare some laudanum, while Mrs. Arnold

[032-032]went out to fetch the children. All at once, as the medical man was

[032-033]bending over his glasses, there was a rattle from the bed; a convulsive

[032-034]struggle followed; and, when the unhappy woman, with the children, and

[032-035]all the servants, rushed into the room, Dr. Arnold had passed from his

[032-036]perplexities forever.

[032-037]There can be little doubt that what he had achieved justified the

[032-038]prediction of the Provost of Oriel that he would 'change the face of

[032-039]education all through the public schools of England'. It is true that,

[032-040]so far as the actual machinery of education was concerned, Dr. Arnold

[032-041]not only failed to effect a change, but deliberately adhered to the old

[032-042]system. The monastic and literary conceptions of education, which had

[032-043]their roots in the Middle Ages, and had been accepted and strengthened

[032-044]at the revival of Learning, he adopted almost without hesitation. Under

[032-045]him, the public school remained, in essentials, a conventional

[032-046]establishment, devoted to the teaching of Greek and Latin grammar. Had

[032-047]he set on foot reforms in these directions, it seems probable that he

[032-048]might have succeeded in carrying the parents of England with him. The

[032-049]moment was ripe; there was a general desire for educational changes; and

[032-050]Dr. Arnold's great reputation could hardly have been resisted. As it

[032-051]was, he threw the whole weight of his influence into the opposite scale,

[032-052]and the ancient system became more firmly established than ever.

[032-053]The changes which he did effect were of a very different nature. By

[032-054]introducing morals and religion into his scheme of education, he altered

[032-055]the whole atmosphere of public-school life. Henceforward the old

[032-056]rough-and-tumble, which was typified by the regime of Keate at Eton,

[032-057]became impossible. After Dr. Arnold, no public school could venture to

[032-058]ignore the virtues of respectability. Again, by his introduction of the

[032-059]prefectorial system, Dr. Arnold produced far-reaching effects--effects

[032-060]which he himself, perhaps, would have found perplexing. In his day, when

[032-061]the school hours were over, the boys were free to enjoy themselves as

[032-062]they liked; to bathe, to fish, to ramble for long afternoons in the

[032-063]country, collecting eggs or gathering flowers. 'The taste of the boys at

[032-064]this period,' writes an old Rugbaean who had been under Arnold, 'leaned

[032-065]strongly towards flowers'. The words have an odd look today. 'The modern

[032-066]reader of "Tom Brown's Schooldays" searches in vain for any reference to

[032-067]compulsory games, house colours, or cricket averages. In those days,

[032-068]when boys played games they played them for pleasure; but in those days

[032-069]the prefectorial system--the system which hands over the life of a

[032-070]school to an oligarchy of a dozen youths of seventeen--was still in its

[032-071]infancy, and had not yet borne its fruit.

[032-072]Teachers and prophets have strange after-histories; and that of Dr.

[032-073]Arnold has been no exception. The earnest enthusiast who strove to make

[032-074]his pupils Christian gentlemen and who governed his school according to

[032-075]the principles of the Old Testament, has proved to be the founder of the

[032-076]worship of athletics and the worship of good form. Upon those two poles

[032-077]our public schools have turned for so long that we have almost come to

[032-078]believe that such is their essential nature, and that an English public

[032-079]schoolboy who wears the wrong clothes and takes no interest in football,

[032-080]is a contradiction in terms. Yet it was not so before Dr. Arnold; will

[032-081]it always be so after him? We shall see.

[032-089]The End of [[general-gordon|General Gordon]]

[032-090]DURING the year 1883 a solitary English gentleman was to be seen,

[032-091]wandering, with a thick book under his arm, in the neighbourhood of

[032-092]Jerusalem. His unassuming figure, short and slight, with its

[032-093]half-gliding, half-tripping motion, gave him a boyish aspect, which

[032-094]contrasted, oddly, but not unpleasantly, with the touch of grey on his

[032-095]hair and whiskers. There was the same contrast--enigmatic and

[032-096]attractive--between the sunburnt brick-red complexion--the hue of the

[032-097]seasoned traveller--and the large blue eyes, with their look of almost

[032-098]childish sincerity. To the friendly inquirer, he would explain, in a

[032-099]row, soft, and very distinct voice, that he was engaged in elucidating

[032-100]four questions--the site of the Crucifixion, the line of division

[032-101]between the tribes of Benjamin and Judah, the identification of Gideon,

[032-102]and the position of the Garden of Eden. He was also, he would add, most

[032-103]anxious to discover the spot where the Ark first touched ground, after

[032-104]the subsidence of the Flood: he believed, indeed, that he had solved

[032-105]that problem, as a reference to some passages in the book which he was

[032-106]carrying would show.

[032-107]This singular person was General Gordon, and his book was the Holy

[032-108]Bible.

[032-109]In such complete retirement from the world and the ways of men, it might

[032-110]have seemed that a life of inordinate activity had found at last a

[032-111]longed-for, final peacefulness. For month after month, for an entire

[032-112]year, the General lingered by the banks of the Jordan. But then the

[032-113]enchantment was suddenly broken. Once more adventure claimed him; he

[032-114]plunged into the whirl of high affairs; his fate was mingled with the

[032-115]frenzies of Empire and the doom of peoples. And it was not in peace and

[032-116]rest, but in ruin and horror, that he reached his end.

[032-117]The circumstances of that tragic history, so famous, so bitterly

[032-118]debated, so often and so controversially described, remain full of

[032-119]suggestion for the curious examiner of the past. There emerges from

[032-120]those obscure, unhappy records an interest, not merely political and

[032-121]historical, but human and dramatic. One catches a vision of strange

[032-122]characters, moved by mysterious impulses, interacting in queer

[032-123]complication, and hurrying at last--so it almost seems--like creatures

[032-124]in a puppet show to a predestined catastrophe. The characters, too, have

[032-125]a charm of their own: they are curiously English. What other nation on

[032-126]the face of the earth could have produced [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] and Sir Evelyn

[032-127]Baring and Lord Hartington and General Gordon? Alike in their emphasis

[032-128]and their lack of emphasis, in their eccentricity and their

[032-129]conventionality, in their matter-of-factness and their romance, these

[032-130]four figures seem to embody the mingling contradictions of the English

[032-131]spirit. As for the mise-en-scene, it is perfectly appropriate. But

[032-132]first, let us glance at the earlier adventures of the hero of the piece.

[032-133]Charles George Gordon was born in 1833. His father, of Highland and

[032-134]military descent, was himself a Lieutenant-General; his mother came of a

[032-135]family of merchants, distinguished for their sea voyages into remote

[032-136]regions of the Globe. As a boy, Charlie was remarkable for his high

[032-137]spirits, pluck, and love of mischief. Destined for the Artillery, he was

[032-138]sent to the Academy at Woolwich, where some other characteristics made

[032-139]their appearance. On one occasion, when the cadets had been forbidden to

[032-140]leave the dining-room and the senior corporal stood with outstretched

[032-141]arms in the doorway to prevent their exit, Charlie Gordon put his head

[032-142]down, and, butting the officer in the pit of the stomach, projected him

[032-143]down a flight of stairs and through a glass door at the bottom. For this

[032-144]act of insubordination he was nearly dismissed--while the captain of his

[032-145]company predicted that he would never make an officer. A little later,

[032-146]when he was eighteen, it came to the knowledge of the authorities that

[032-147]bullying was rife at the Academy. The new-comers were questioned, and

[032-148]one of them said that Charlie Gordon had hit him over the head with a

[032-149]clothes-brush. He had worked well, and his record was on the whole a

[032-150]good one; but the authorities took a serious view of the case, and held

[032-151]back his commission for six months. It was owing to this delay that he

[032-152]went into the Royal Engineers, instead of the Royal Artillery.

[032-153]He was sent to Pembroke, to work at the erection of fortifications; and

[032-154]at Pembroke those religious convictions, which never afterwards left

[032-155]him, first gained a hold upon his mind. Under the influence of his

[032-156]sister Augusta and of a 'very religious captain of the name of Drew', he

[032-157]began to reflect upon his sins, look up texts, and hope for salvation.

[032-158]Though he had never been confirmed--he never was confirmed--he took the

[032-159]sacrament every Sunday; and he eagerly perused the Priceless Diamond,

[032-160]Scott's Commentaries, and The Remains of the Rev. R. McCheyne. 'No

[032-161]novels or worldly books,' he wrote to his sister, 'come up to the

[032-162]Commentaries of Scott.... I, remember well when you used to get them in

[032-163]numbers, and I used to laugh at them; but, thank God, it is different

[032-164]with me now. I feel much happier and more contented than I used to do. I

[032-165]did not like Pembroke, but now I would not wish for any prettier place.

[032-166]I have got a horse and gig, and Drew and myself drive all about the

[032-167]country. I hope my dear father and mother think of eternal things ...

[032-168]Dearest Augusta, pray for me, I beg of you.'

[032-169]He was twenty-one; the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]] broke out; and before the year was

[032-170]over, he had managed to get himself transferred to Balaclava. During the

[032-171]siege of Sebastopol he behaved with conspicuous gallantry. Upon the

[032-172]declaration of peace, he was sent to Bessarabia to assist in determining

[032-173]the frontier between Russia and Turkey, in accordance with the Treaty of

[032-174]Paris; and upon this duty he was occupied for nearly two years. Not long

[032-175]after his return home, in 1860, war was declared upon China. Captain

[032-176]Gordon was dispatched to the scene of operations, but the fighting was

[032-177]over before he arrived. Nevertheless, he was to remain for the next four

[032-178]years in China, where he was to lay the foundations of extraordinary

[032-179]renown.
