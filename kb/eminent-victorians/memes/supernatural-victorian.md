# The Supernatural in Victorian Religion

The Oxford Movement was, at its core, an attempt to re-enchant the world — to restore the conviction that "the supernatural was everywhere and at all times, a living force, floating invisible in angels, inspiring saints, and investing with miraculous properties the commonest material things" (003-073). This supernaturalism was what most sharply distinguished Tractarians from both Evangelicals (who located the supernatural exclusively in conversion and Scripture) and Broad Church liberals (who explained it away).

## Newman's Supernatural Universe

Newman's mind was "a dreamer whose secret spirit dwelt apart in delectable mountains, an artist whose subtle senses caught, like a shower in the sunshine, the impalpable rainbow of the immaterial world" (003-040). His religious imagination required a universe thick with spiritual presences — angels, saints, the efficacy of consecrated oil, the real presence in the Eucharist. Strachey ascribes this partly to temperament (Newman as Romantic artist manqué) and partly to Oxford: "the air was thick with clerical sanctity, heavy with the odours of tradition and the soft warmth of spiritual authority" (003-055).

## Miracles and the Modern Mind

Strachey himself is a rationalist who finds supernatural belief at once fascinating and preposterous. His description of Newman "dallying with visions of angelic visitations and the efficacy of the oil of St Walburga" (003-055) is not quite mockery — it acknowledges the aesthetic appeal of belief while declining to credit its premises. This is the quintessential Stracheyan posture: the ironist who understands what he cannot believe.

The Tractarian insistence on the supernatural reversed the dominant tendency of English religious thought since the Reformation. The pre-Tractarian Church of England had been "comfortable" — its divines "subscribed with a sigh or a smile to the Thirty-nine Articles" and did not trouble themselves about angelic hierarchies (002-080). Newman and Keble made the supernatural mandatory again.

## Manning and Miracles

Manning's supernaturalism was more practical than Newman's. As Archbishop of Westminster, he investigated reports of miraculous phenomena with the same administrative rigour he applied to diocesan finances. Strachey notes that Manning's belief in the supernatural was inseparable from his belief in authority — both came from the same psychological source, the craving for certainty in an uncertain world (013-136).

## In the Book

The supernatural pervades all four biographies. Gordon's mystical communion with God in the Sudan desert, Nightingale's sense of divine calling, Arnold's providential view of history, and Manning's Eucharistic piety — all testify to the Victorian conviction that the veil between natural and supernatural was thin. Strachey treats this conviction with a mixture of aesthetic appreciation and anthropological curiosity: he does not believe, but he understands why belief was so compelling.
