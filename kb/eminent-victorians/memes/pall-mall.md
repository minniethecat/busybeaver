# Pall Mall

A London thoroughfare whose name, in the pages of *Eminent Victorians*, is invoked not for its architecture but for its journalism. The *Pall Mall Gazette*, edited by the crusading W. T. Stead, became the instrument through which public opinion was mobilised to send General Gordon to the Sudan. Strachey treats the *Gazette* as a character in its own right — the voice of the new mass-circulation press, half prophet and half mob, whose editorials could make and unmake imperial policy.

## In the Narrative

The *Pall Mall Gazette* appears at the climax of the Gordon chapters as the engine of a remarkable campaign of press agitation. "It so fell out," Strachey writes with studied nonchalance, that Gordon, having retired to domestic life, was discovered by Stead — and the pages of the *Pall Mall Gazette* became the platform from which the general was propelled toward his doom [^1]. The newspaper "dilated upon" Gordon's virtues [^2], published his views on the Sudan, and created a popular demand for his appointment that the government of Mr. Gladstone found impossible to resist.

## Key Events

- **The Stead-Gordon Interviews (1884)**: W. T. Stead interviewed Gordon for the *Pall Mall Gazette*, publishing a series of articles that presented the general as the only man who could resolve the Sudan crisis [^1]
- **The Gordon Mission Campaign**: The *Gazette* ran a sustained press campaign demanding Gordon's dispatch to Khartoum — a campaign that succeeded in its immediate aim of securing his appointment and failed catastrophically in its larger purpose [^3]
- **The Aftermath**: After Gordon's death, the *Pall Mall Gazette* continued to cover the Sudan crisis, its earlier enthusiasm now transmuted into recrimination and memorial [^4]
