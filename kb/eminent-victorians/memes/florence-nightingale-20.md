[020-001]Lasciate ogni speranza, voi ch'entrate: the delusive doors bore no such

[020-002]inscription; and yet behind them Hell yawned. Want, neglect, confusion,

[020-003]misery--in every shape and in every degree of intensity--filled the

[020-004]endless corridors and the vast apartments of the gigantic barrack-house,

[020-005]which, without forethought or preparation, had been hurriedly set aside

[020-006]as the chief shelter for the victims of the war. The very building

[020-007]itself was radically defective. Huge sewers underlay it, and cesspools

[020-008]loaded with filth wafted their poison into the upper rooms. The floors

[020-009]were in so rotten a condition that many of them could not be scrubbed;

[020-010]the walls were thick with dirt; incredible multitudes of vermin swarmed

[020-011]everywhere. And, enormous as the building was, it was yet too small. It

[020-012]contained four miles of beds, crushed together so close that there was

[020-013]but just room to pass between them. Under such conditions, the most

[020-014]elaborate system of ventilation might well have been at fault; but here

[020-015]there was no ventilation. The stench was indescribable. 'I have been

[020-016]well acquainted,' said [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]], 'with the dwellings of the

[020-017]worst parts of most of the great cities in Europe, but have never been

[020-018]in any atmosphere which I could compare with that of the Barrack

[020-019]Hospital at night.' The structural defects were equalled by the

[020-020]deficiencies in the commonest objects of hospital use. There were not

[020-021]enough bedsteads; the sheets were of canvas, and so coarse that the

[020-022]wounded men recoiled from them, begging to be left in their blankets;

[020-023]there was no bedroom furniture of any kind, and empty beer bottles were

[020-024]used for candlesticks. There were no basins, no towels, no soap, no

[020-025]brooms, no mops, no trays, no plates; there were neither slippers nor

[020-026]scissors, neither shoe-brushes nor blacking; there were no knives or

[020-027]forks or spoons. The supply of fuel was constantly deficient. The

[020-028]cooking arrangements were preposterously inadequate, and the laundry was

[020-029]a farce. As for purely medical materials, the tale was no better.

[020-030]Stretchers, splints, bandages--all were lacking; and so were the most

[020-031]ordinary drugs.

[020-032]To replace such wants, to struggle against such difficulties, there was

[020-033]a handful of men overburdened by the strain of ceaseless work, bound

[020-034]down by the traditions of official routine, and enfeebled either by old

[020-035]age or inexperience or sheer incompetence. They had proved utterly

[020-036]unequal to their task. The principal doctor was lost in the imbecilities

[020-037]of a senile optimism. The wretched official whose business it was to

[020-038]provide for the wants of the hospital was tied fast hand and foot by red

[020-039]tape. A few of the younger doctors struggled valiantly, but what could

[020-040]they do? Unprepared, disorganised, with such help only as they could

[020-041]find among the miserable band of convalescent soldiers drafted off to

[020-042]tend their sick comrades, they were faced with disease, mutilation, and

[020-043]death in all their most appalling forms, crowded multitudinously about

[020-044]them in an ever-increasing mass. They were like men in a shipwreck,

[020-045]fighting, not for safety, but for the next moment's bare existence--to

[020-046]gain, by yet another frenzied effort, some brief respite from the waters

[020-047]of destruction.

[020-048]In these surroundings, those who had been long inured to scenes of human

[020-049]suffering--surgeons with a world-wide knowledge of agonies, soldiers

[020-050]familiar with fields of carnage, missionaries with remembrances of

[020-051]famine and of plague--yet found a depth of horror which they had never

[020-052]known before. There were moments, there were places, in the Barrack

[020-053]Hospital at [[scutari|Scutari]], where the strongest hand was struck with trembling,

[020-054]and the boldest eye would turn away its gaze.

[020-055]Miss Nightingale came, and she, at any rate, in that inferno, did not

[020-056]abandon hope. For one thing, she brought material succour. Before she

[020-057]left London she had consulted Dr. Andrew Smith, the head of the Army

[020-058]Medical Board, as to whether it would be useful to take out stores of

[020-059]any kind to Scutari; and Dr. Andrew Smith had told her that 'nothing was

[020-060]needed'. Even Sidney Herbert had given her similar assurances; possibly,

[020-061]owing to an oversight, there might have been some delay in the delivery

[020-062]of the medical stores, which, he said, had been sent out from England

[020-063]'in profusion', but 'four days would have remedied this'. She preferred

[020-064]to trust her own instincts, and at Marseilles purchased a large quantity

[020-065]of miscellaneous provisions, which were of the utmost use at Scutari.

[020-066]She came, too, amply provided with money--in all, during her stay in the

[020-067]East, about L7,000 reached her from private sources; and, in addition,

[020-068]she was able to avail herself of another valuable means of help. At the

[020-069]same time as herself, Mr. Macdonald, of The Times, had arrived at

[020-070]Scutari, charged with the duty of administering the large sums of money

[020-071]collected through the agency of that newspaper in aid of the sick and

[020-072]wounded; and Mr. Macdonald had the sense to see that the best use he

[020-073]could make of The Times Fund was to put it at the disposal of Miss

[020-074]Nightingale.

[020-075]'I cannot conceive,' wrote an eye-witness, 'as I now calmly look back on

[020-076]the first three weeks after the arrival of the wounded from Inkerman,

[020-077]how it could have been possible to have avoided a state of things too

[020-078]disastrous to contemplate, had not Miss Nightingale been there, with the

[020-079]means placed at her disposal by Mr. Macdonald.'

[020-080]But the official view was different. What! Was the public service to

[020-081]admit, by accepting outside charity, that it was unable to discharge its

[020-082]own duties without the assistance of private and irregular benevolence?

[020-083]Never! And accordingly when Lord Stratford de Redcliffe, our ambassador

[020-084]at Constantinople, was asked by Mr. Macdonald to indicate how The Times

[020-085]Fund could best be employed, he answered that there was indeed one

[020-086]object to which it might very well be devoted--the building of an

[020-087]English Protestant Church at Pera.

[020-088]Mr. Macdonald did not waste further time with Lord Stratford, and

[020-089]immediately joined forces with Miss Nightingale. But, with such a frame

[020-090]of mind in the highest quarters, it is easy to imagine the kind of

[020-091]disgust and alarm with which the sudden intrusion of a band of amateurs

[020-092]and females must have filled the minds of the ordinary officer and the

[020-093]ordinary military surgeon. They could not understand it--what had women

[020-094]to do with war? Honest Colonels relieved their spleen by the cracking of

[020-095]heavy jokes about 'the Bird'; while poor Dr. Hall, a rough terrier of a

[020-096]man, who had worried his way to the top of his profession, was struck

[020-097]speechless with astonishment, and at last observed that Miss

[020-098]Nightingale's appointment was extremely droll.

[020-099]Her position was, indeed, an official one, but it was hardly the easier

[020-100]for that. In the hospitals it was her duty to provide the services of

[020-101]herself and her nurses when they were asked for by the doctors, and not

[020-102]until then. At first some of the surgeons would have nothing to say to

[020-103]her, and, though she was welcomed by others, the majority were hostile

[020-104]and suspicious. But gradually she gained ground. Her good will could not

[020-105]be denied, and her capacity could not be disregarded. With consummate

[020-106]tact, with all the gentleness of supreme strength, she managed at last

[020-107]to impose her personality upon the susceptible, overwrought,

[020-108]discouraged, and helpless group of men in authority who surrounded her.

[020-109]She stood firm; she was a rock in the angry ocean; with her alone was

[020-110]safety, comfort, life. And so it was that hope dawned at Scutari. The

[020-111]reign of chaos and old night began to dwindle; order came upon the

[020-112]scene, and common sense, and forethought, and decision, radiating out

[020-113]from the little room off the great gallery in the Barrack Hospital

[020-114]where, day and night, the Lady Superintendent was at her task. Progress

[020-115]might be slow, but it was sure.

[020-116]The first sign of a great change came with the appearance of some of

[020-117]those necessary objects with which the hospitals had been unprovided for

[020-118]months. The sick men began to enjoy the use of towels and soap, knives

[020-119]and forks, combs and tooth-brushes. Dr. Hall might snort when he heard

[020-120]of it, asking, with a growl, what a soldier wanted with a tooth-brush;

[020-121]but the good work went on. Eventually the whole business of purveying to

[020-122]the hospitals was, in effect, carried out by Miss Nightingale. She

[020-123]alone, it seemed, whatever the contingency, knew where to lay her hands

[020-124]on what was wanted; she alone could dispense her stores with readiness;

[020-125]above all, she alone possessed the art of circumventing the pernicious

[020-126]influences of official etiquette. This was her greatest enemy, and

[020-127]sometimes even she was baffled by it. On one occasion 27,000 shirts,

[020-128]sent out at her instance by the Home Government, arrived, were landed,

[020-129]and were only waiting to be unpacked. But the official 'Purveyor'

[020-130]intervened; 'he could not unpack them,' he said, 'with out a Board.'

[020-131]Miss Nightingale pleaded in vain; the sick and wounded lay half-naked

[020-132]shivering for want of clothing; and three weeks elapsed before the Board

[020-133]released the shirts. A little later, however, on a similar occasion,

[020-134]Miss Nightingale felt that she could assert her own authority. She

[020-135]ordered a Government consignment to be forcibly opened while the

[020-136]miserable 'Purveyor' stood by, wringing his hands in departmental agony.

[020-137]Vast quantities of valuable stores sent from England lay, she found,

[020-138]engulfed in the bottomless abyss of the Turkish Customs House. Other

[020-139]ship-loads, buried beneath munitions of war destined for Balaclava,

[020-140]passed Scutari without a sign, and thus hospital materials were

[020-141]sometimes carried to and fro three times over the Black Sea, before they

[020-142]reached their destination. The whole system was clearly at fault, and

[020-143]Miss Nightingale suggested to the home authorities that a Government

[020-144]Store House should be instituted at Scutari for the reception and

[020-145]distribution of the consignments. Six months after her arrival this was

[020-146]done.

[020-147]In the meantime, she had reorganised the kitchens and the laundries in

[020-148]the hospitals. The ill-cooked hunks of meat, vilely served at irregular

[020-149]intervals, which had hitherto been the only diet for the sick men, were

[020-150]replaced by punctual meals, well-prepared and appetising, while

[020-151]strengthening extra foods--soups and wines and jellies ('preposterous

[020-152]luxuries', snarled Dr. Hall)--were distributed to those who needed them.

[020-153]One thing, however, she could not effect. The separation of the bones

[020-154]from the meat was no part of official cookery: the rule was that the

[020-155]food must be divided into equal portions, and if some of the portions

[020-156]were all bone--well, every man must take his chance. The rule, perhaps,

[020-157]was not a very good one; but there it was. 'It would require a new

[020-158]Regulation of the Service,' she was told, 'to bone the meat.' As for the

[020-159]washing arrangements, they were revolutionised. Up to the time of Miss

[020-160]Nightingale's arrival, the number of shirts the authorities had

[020-161]succeeded in washing was seven. The hospital bedding, she found, was

[020-162]'washed' in cold water. She took a Turkish house, had boilers installed,

[020-163]and employed soldiers' wives to do the laundry work. The expenses were

[020-164]defrayed from her own funds and that of The Times; and henceforward, the

[020-165]sick and wounded had the comfort of clean linen.

[020-166]Then she turned her attention to their clothing. Owing to military

[020-167]exigencies, the greater number of the men had abandoned their kit; their

[020-168]knapsacks were lost forever; they possessed nothing but what was on

[020-169]their persons, and that was usually only fit for speedy destruction. The

[020-170]'Purveyor', of course, pointed out that, according to the regulations,

[020-171]all soldiers should bring with them into hospital an adequate supply of

[020-172]clothing, and he declared that it was no business of his to make good

[020-173]their deficiencies. Apparently, it was the business of Miss Nightingale.

[020-174]She procured socks, boots, and shirts in enormous quantities; she had

[020-175]trousers made, she rigged up dressing-gowns. 'The fact is,' she told

[020-176]Sidney Herbert, I am now clothing the [[british-army|British Army]].'

[020-177]All at once, word came from the Crimea that a great new contingent of

[020-178]sick and wounded might shortly be expected. Where were they to go? Every

[020-179]available inch in the wards was occupied; the affair was serious and

[020-180]pressing, and the authorities stood aghast. There were some dilapidated

[020-181]rooms in the Barrack Hospital, unfit for human habitation, but Miss

[020-182]Nightingale believed that if measures were promptly taken they might be

[020-183]made capable of accommodating several hundred beds. One of the doctors

[020-184]agreed with her; the rest of the officials were irresolute--it would be

[020-185]a very expensive job, they said; it would involve building; and who

[020-186]could take the responsibility? The proper course was that a

[020-187]representation should be made to the Director-General of the Army

[020-188]Medical Department in London; then the Director-General would apply to

[020-189]the Horse Guards, the Horse Guards would move the Ordnance, the Ordnance

[020-190]would lay the matter before the Treasury, and, if the Treasury gave its

[020-191]consent, the work might be correctly carried through, several months

[020-192]after the necessity for it had disappeared. Miss Nightingale, however,

[020-193]had made up her mind, and she persuaded Lord Stratford--or thought she

[020-194]had persuaded him--to give his sanction to the required expenditure. One

[020-195]hundred and twenty-five workmen were immediately engaged, and the work

[020-196]was begun. The workmen struck; whereupon Lord Stratford washed his hands

[020-197]of the whole business. Miss Nightingale engaged 200 other workmen on her

[020-198]own authority, and paid the bill out of her own resources. The wards

[020-199]were ready by the required date; 500 sick men were received in them; and

[020-200]all the utensils, including knives, forks, spoons, cans and towels, were

[020-201]supplied by Miss Nightingale.
