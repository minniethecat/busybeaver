# Cardinal Nicholas Wiseman

The first Cardinal Archbishop of Westminster following the restoration of the Catholic hierarchy in England in 1850, and the man who presided over the English Catholic revival during its most dramatic period of growth. Wiseman was a figure of immense physical bulk, sanguine temperament, and genuine scholarly accomplishment — Strachey describes him as "a man of vast physique" with a "jovial manner" and "facile talk" [009-047]. Yet his last years were "distracted and embittered by the fury of opposing principles and the venom of personal animosities" [010-003], as the struggle between [[cardinal-manning]] and the Old Catholic party tore his household and his diocese apart.

## Life

### Early Life and Scholarship

Wiseman was born in 1802 to one of the oldest English Catholic families — a lineage that had preserved the faith uninterruptedly since the Elizabethan persecution [009-110]. Unlike the converts who would later flood into the Church from the [[oxford-movement]], Wiseman was no newcomer to [[rome|Rome]]; he spent most of his early life there, becoming thoroughly Roman in his habits and sympathies [009-110]. He distinguished himself as a scholar of wide-ranging capacity, publishing works on literature, archaeology, and theology. His momentous article on St. Augustine and the Donatists was, in Strachey's account, the piece that "had finally convinced [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] that the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] was in schism" [009-089] — making Wiseman an intellectual architect of the conversion of [[john-henry-newman]] even before he became his ecclesiastical superior.

### The Restoration of the Hierarchy

In 1850, [[pope-pius-ix]] divided the whole of England into dioceses and placed Wiseman at the head of them as Archbishop of Westminster [009-081]. Wiseman's encyclical, dated "from without the Flaminian Gate," announced the new departure and was greeted in England by a storm of indignation — the famous "Papal Aggression" controversy, culminating in Lord John Russell's furibund letter against the insolence of the Pope's territorial claims [009-082]. Strachey notes that though the particular point of contention — the English territorial titles of the new Roman bishops — was an insignificant one, "the instinct of Lord John and of the English people was in reality sound enough. Wiseman's installation did mean, in fact, a new move in the Papal game; it meant an advance, if not an aggression — a quickening in England of the long-dormant energies of the [[catholic-church|Roman Church]]" [009-089].

### The Oxford Movement and the Convert Harvest

Before his elevation to the cardinalate, Wiseman — then simply Dr. Wiseman — had watched the [[oxford-movement]] with eager anticipation. "Dr. Wiseman, at the head of them, was watching and waiting with special eagerness. His hand was held out under the ripening fruit; the delicious morsel seemed to be trembling on its stalk; and yet it did not fall" [006-080]. When [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] finally converted, Wiseman was "particularly pleased" [006-120], recognising the harvest of converts as a vindication of the Catholic cause in England.

Wiseman's final years were consumed by the relentless campaign of Manning to displace [[dr-errington|Dr. Errington]], the Cardinal's co-adjutor and lifelong friend. Strachey captures the pathos of the situation: "Left to himself, Wiseman might have compromised with the Old Catholics and [[dr-errington|Dr. Errington]]; but when Manning had once appeared upon the scene, all compromise became impossible" [010-006]. The Cardinal pleaded in vain; his friendship with Errington was "plucked up by the roots, and the harmony of his private life was utterly destroyed" [010-050].

## Character

Strachey's portrait of Wiseman is one of the gentler passages in the Manning biography — a relief from the relentless intensity of its central subject. Wiseman emerges as "a man of vast physique — 'your immense', an Irish servant used respectfully to call him — of sanguine temperament, of genial disposition, of versatile capacity" who "seemed to have engrafted upon the robustness of his English nature the facile, child-like, and expansive qualities of the South" [009-144].

> "So far from being a Bishop Blougram (as the rumour went) he was, in fact, the very antithesis of that subtle and worldly-wise ecclesiastic. He had innocently looked forward all his life to the reunion of England to the See of Peter, and eventually had come to believe that, in God's hand, he was the instrument destined to bring about this miraculous consummation." [009-144]

His character was not without its vanities. He devoted much time to ceremonial details — "his knowledge of rubric and ritual, and of the symbolical significations of vestments, has rarely been equalled, and he took a profound delight in the ordering and the performance of elaborate processions" [009-144]. Nor was he above enjoying the pleasures of the table. Some of [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s disciples were "astonished and grieved to find that he sat down to four courses of fish during Lent," prompting one to remark that "there is a lobster salad side to the Cardinal" [009-144].

His administrative style was "dilatory, unbusinesslike, and infirm" [010-085] — a characteristic Manning exploited with consummate skill. When the dispute with Errington reached its crisis, Wiseman was "ready enough to leave the conduct of affairs in his hands" [010-085], unable to match the relentless energy of his Provost.

## Relationships

Wiseman's web of relationships defined both his influence and his tragedy:

- **[[cardinal-manning]]**: Wiseman recognised Manning's energy early, placing him at the head of the Oblates of St. Charles and as Provost of Westminster [009-121]. But Manning repaid this trust by dismantling Wiseman's friendships, isolating him from his household, and ultimately engineering Errington's removal. Wiseman became dependent on Manning even as Manning was the instrument of his misery.

- **[[john-henry-newman]]**: Wiseman was instrumental in [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s conversion, having written the article on St. Augustine that finally convinced [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]'s schism [009-089]. Yet during [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s later tribulations — the failed Irish University, the collapse of the scripture translation project — Wiseman proved an unreliable patron. "He was persuaded by Cardinal Wiseman to undertake the editorship" of the English Scriptures, only to find that "it was all useless; Cardinal Wiseman began to think of other things" [012-022].

- **[[dr-errington|Dr. George Errington]]**: Wiseman's co-adjutor and intimate friend, an Old Catholic of the most rigid type who was created Archbishop of Trebizond *in partibus infidelium* [009-126]. Errington was "liberated" from his position by papal decree at Manning's urging — a rupture that devastated the Cardinal [010-229].

- **[[pope-pius-ix]]**: The Pope who raised Wiseman to the cardinalate in 1850 and who later, under Manning's influence, removed Wiseman's closest ally [009-081].

## In This Book

Wiseman is, in Strachey's design, a secondary figure — the gentle, ineffectual counterweight to Manning's ferocious will. Where Manning is disciplined, Wiseman is dilatory; where Manning is calculating, Wiseman is innocent; where Manning is ascetic, Wiseman enjoys his four courses of fish. The Cardinal's tragedy is that his very virtues — his geniality, his scholarly absorption, his childlike faith in the reunion of England to [[rome|Rome]] — made him easy prey.

Strachey's irony is unusually tender here. The portrait of Wiseman composing acrostics in Latin verse, playing battledore and shuttlecock with his little nieces, and smilingly replying to the Master of Ceremonies who had stopped a procession by "special revelation" — "You may let the procession go on... I have just obtained permission, by special revelation, to proceed with it" [009-144] — is affectionate rather than cutting. The Cardinal is a man out of his depth, a scholar thrust into the role of ecclesiastical politician, whose final years were "distracted and embittered" [010-003] by forces he could neither comprehend nor control.

## Historical Significance

Wiseman's historical importance rests on two foundations. First, as the first Cardinal Archbishop of Westminster after the restoration of the hierarchy, he embodied the new public prominence of English Catholicism after centuries of legal disability — a position made possible by [[catholic-emancipation]] in 1829. His installation, and the uproar it caused, marked the moment when English Catholics re-entered the national consciousness as a political and social force.

Second, and more poignantly, Wiseman's failure to control the succession to his see enabled Manning's ascent to absolute authority over the English Catholic community. [[monsignor-talbot|Monsignor Talbot]] would later warn Manning: "if you yield and do not fight the battle of the Holy See against the detestable spirit growing up in England, he will begin to regret Cardinal Wiseman, who knew how to keep the laity in order" [013-075]. The remark captures the retrospective judgment: Wiseman had been, for all his weaknesses, a more humane and conciliatory shepherd than the ultramontane rigorist who succeeded him.
