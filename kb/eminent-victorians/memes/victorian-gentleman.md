# The Victorian Gentleman

The gentleman ideal was the social cement of Victorian England — an amalgam of birth, education, manners, and moral character that defined the governing class. Strachey's four subjects — Manning the clerical politician, Nightingale the lady-reformer, Arnold the pedagogue, and Gordon the soldier-saint — each embodied and troubled this ideal in different ways.

## Definition

A gentleman was defined less by what he was than by what he was not: not in trade, not manual, not vulgar, not enthusiastic. Strachey's pre-Tractarian clergy were gentlemen who "rode gaily to hounds of a morning as gentlemen should, and, as gentlemen should, carried their two bottles of an evening" (002-080). The ideal combined classical education (a gentleman knew his Horace), social ease (he never tried too hard), moral seriousness (but not fanaticism), and service to the nation (the gentleman's calling).

## In the Book

### Manning: The Gentleman-Cleric

Manning was in many ways the perfect Victorian gentleman: handsome, polite, well-connected, rising effortlessly through the Church hierarchy. His brother-in-law was a bishop, his friend was Gladstone. Yet Strachey exposes the machinery beneath the surface: the careful cultivation of allies, the strategic friendships, the destruction of private correspondence after his wife's death. "He never spoke of her; every letter, every record, of his married life he destroyed" (002-058). The gentleman's composure was, in Manning's case, a form of erasure.

### Nightingale: The Lady Against the Gentlemen

Nightingale's entire career was a war against gentlemanly incompetence. The military officials who opposed her at Scutari were gentlemen first, administrators second — their amateurism was a point of class pride. Strachey notes that when Sidney Herbert suggested engaging civilian nurses, "he was at once laughed out of court" (021-068) by gentleman-officers who found the idea beneath their dignity. Nightingale's fury was directed not at individuals but at the system that made gentlemanly amateurism the governing principle of the British Army.

### Arnold: Manufacturing Gentlemen

Arnold's Rugby was a gentleman-factory. Strachey observes with irony that the product was "men admirably fitted to govern a colony or command a regiment — and to do little besides." The prefect system taught boys to rule; the classical curriculum taught them that they were heirs to Greece and Rome. The limitation, which Arnold half-perceived and Strachey exposes, was intellectual: a gentleman was trained to govern, not to think.

### Gordon: The Anti-Gentleman

General Gordon was a gentleman by birth and an ascetic saint by conviction — a combination that broke the gentleman ideal from within. He gave away his pay, read the Bible obsessively, treated colonial subjects as spiritual equals, and died at Khartoum in a manner that was either sublime or absurd, depending on the observer. Strachey's Gordon is the gentleman ideal pushed to its breaking point: service without self, duty without calculation, faith without institution.
