[016-004]'Our differences, my dear Archbishop, are indeed profound. We refer

[016-005]them, I suppose, in humble silence to a Higher Power ... You assured me

[016-006]once of your prayers at all and at the most solemn time. I received that

[016-007]assurance with gratitude, and still cherish it. As and when they move

[016-008]upwards, there is a meeting-point for those whom a chasm separates

[016-009]below. I remain always, affectionately yours,

[016-011]Speaking of this correspondence in after years, [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]] said:

[016-012]'From the way in which [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] alluded to the overcasting of our

[016-013]friendship, people might have thought that I had picked his pocket.'

[016-014]VIII

[016-015]IN 1875, Manning's labours received their final reward: he was made a

[016-016]Cardinal. His long and strange career, with its high hopes, its bitter

[016-017]disappointments, its struggles, its renunciations, had come at last to

[016-018]fruition in a Princedom of the Church.

[016-019]'Ask in faith and in perfect confidence,' he himself once wrote, and God

[016-020]will give us what we ask. You may say, "But do you mean that He will

[016-021]give us the very thing?" That, God has not said. God has said that He

[016-022]will give you whatsoever you ask; but the form in which it will come,

[016-023]and the time in which He will give it, He keeps in His own power.

[016-024]Sometimes our prayers are answered in the very things which we put from

[016-025]us; sometimes it may be a chastisement, or a loss, or a visitation

[016-026]against which our hearts rise, and we seem to see that God has not only

[016-027]forgotten us, but has begun to deal with us in severity. Those very

[016-028]things are the answers to our prayers. He knows what we desire, and He

[016-029]gives us the things for which we ask; but in the form which His own

[016-030]Divine Wisdom sees to be best.'

[016-031]There was one to whom Manning's elevation would no doubt have given a

[016-032]peculiar satisfaction--his old friend Monsignor Talbot. But this was not

[016-033]to be. That industrious worker in the cause of [[rome|Rome]] had been removed

[016-034]some years previously to a sequestered home at Passy, whose padded walls

[016-035]were impervious to the rumours of the outer world. Pius IX had been much

[016-036]afflicted by this unfortunate event; he had not been able to resign

[016-037]himself to the loss of his secretary, and he had given orders that

[016-038]Monsignor Talbot's apartment in the Vatican should be preserved

[016-039]precisely as he had left it, in case of his return. But Monsignor Talbot

[016-040]never returned. Manning's feelings upon the subject appear to have been

[016-041]less tender than the Pope's. In all his letters, in all his papers, in

[016-042]all his biographical memoranda, not a word of allusion is to be found to

[016-043]the misfortune, nor to the death, of the most loyal of his adherents.

[016-044]Monsignor Talbot's name disappears suddenly and for ever--like a stone

[016-045]cast into the waters.

[016-046]Manning was now an old man, and his outward form had assumed that

[016-047]appearance of austere asceticism which is, perhaps, the one thing

[016-048]immediately suggested by his name to the ordinary Englishman. The spare

[016-049]and stately form, the head--massive, emaciated, terrible--with the great

[016-050]nose, the glittering eyes, and the mouth drawn back and compressed into

[016-051]the grim rigidities of age, self-mortification, and authority--such is

[016-052]the vision that still lingers in the public mind--the vision which,

[016-053]actual and palpable like some embodied memory of the Middle Ages, used

[016-054]to pass and repass, less than a generation since, through the streets of

[016-055]London. For the activities of this extraordinary figure were great and

[016-056]varied. He ruled his diocese with the despotic zeal of a born

[016-057]administrator. He threw himself into social work of every kind; he

[016-058]organised charities, he lectured on temperance; he delivered innumerable

[016-059]sermons; he produced an unending series of devotional books. And he

[016-060]brooked no brother near the throne: [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] languished in Birmingham; and

[016-061]even the Jesuits trembled and obeyed.

[016-062]Nor was it only among his own community that his energy and his

[016-063]experience found scope. He gradually came to play an important part in

[016-064]public affairs, upon questions of labour, poverty, and education. He sat

[016-065]on Royal Commissions and corresponded with Cabinet Ministers. At last,

[016-066]no philanthropic meeting at the Guildhall was considered complete

[016-067]without the presence of Cardinal Manning. A special degree of precedence

[016-068]was accorded to him. Though the rank of a Cardinal-Archbishop is

[016-069]officially unknown in England, his name appeared in public documents--as

[016-070]a token, it must be supposed, of personal consideration--above the names

[016-071]of peers and bishops, and immediately below that of the Prince of Wales.

[016-072]In his private life he was secluded. The ambiguities of his social

[016-073]position, and his desire to maintain intact the peculiar eminence of his

[016-074]office, combined to hold him aloof from the ordinary gatherings of

[016-075]society, though on the rare occasions of his appearance among

[016-076]fashionable and exalted persons, he carried all before him. His

[016-077]favourite haunt was the Athenaeum Club, where he sat scanning the

[016-078]newspapers, or conversing with the old friends of former days. He was a

[016-079]member, too, of that distinguished body, the Metaphysical Society, which

[016-080]met once a month during the palmy years of the seventies to discuss, in

[016-081]strict privacy, the fundamental problems of the destiny of man.

[016-082]After a comfortable dinner at the Grosvenor Hotel, the Society, which

[016-083]included Professor Huxley and Professor Tyndall, Mr. John Morley and Sir

[016-084]James Stephen, the Duke of Argyll, Lord Tennyson, and Dean Church, would

[016-085]gather around to hear and discuss a paper read by one of the members

[016-086]upon such questions as: 'What is death?' 'Is God unknowable?' or 'The

[016-087]nature of the Moral Principle'. Sometimes, however, the speculations of

[016-088]the Society ranged in other directions.

[016-089]'I think the paper that interested me most of all that were ever read at

[016-090]our meetings,' says Sir Mountstuart Elphinstone Grant-Duff, 'was one on

[016-091]"Wherein consists the special beauty of imperfection and decay?" in

[016-092]which were propounded the questions "Are not ruins recognised and felt

[016-093]to be more beautiful than perfect structures? Why are they so? Ought

[016-094]they to be so?'

[016-095]'Unfortunately, however, the answers given to these questions by the

[016-096]Metaphysical Society have not been recorded for the instruction of

[016-097]mankind.

[016-098]Manning read several papers, and Professor Huxley and Mr. John Morley

[016-099]listened with attention while he expressed his views upon 'The Soul

[016-100]before and after Death', or explained why it is 'That legitimate

[016-101]Authority is an Evidence of Truth'. Yet, somehow or other, his Eminence

[016-102]never felt quite at ease in these assemblies; he was more at home with

[016-103]audiences of a different kind; and we must look in other directions for

[016-104]the free and full manifestation of his speculative gifts.

[016-105]In a series of lectures, for instance, delivered in 1861--it was the

[016-106]first year of the unification of Italy--upon 'The Present Crisis of the

[016-107]Holy See, tested by prophecy', we catch some glimpses of the kind of

[016-108]problems which were truly congenial to his mind.

[016-109]'In the following pages,' he said, 'I have endeavoured, but for so great

[016-110]a subject most insufficiently, to show that what is passing in our times

[016-111]is the prelude of the antichristian period of the final dethronement of

[016-112]Christendom, and of the restoration of society without God in the

[016-113]world.' 'My intention is,' he continued, 'to examine the present

[016-114]relation of the Church to the civil powers of the world by the light of

[016-115]a prophecy recorded by St Paul.'

[016-116]This prophecy (2 Thess. ii 3 to 11) is concerned with the coming of the

[016-117]Antichrist, and the greater part of the lectures is devoted to a minute

[016-118]examination of this subject. There is no passage in Scripture, Manning

[016-119]pointed out, relating to the coming of Christ more explicit and express

[016-120]than those foretelling Antichrist; it therefore behoved the faithful to

[016-121]consider the matter more fully than they are wont to do. In the first

[016-122]place, Antichrist is a person. 'To deny the personality of Antichrist is

[016-123]to deny the plain testimony of Holy Scripture.' And we must remember

[016-124]that 'it is a law of Holy Scripture that when persons are prophesied of,

[016-125]persons appear'.

[016-126]Again, there was every reason to believe that Antichrist, when he did

[016-127]appear, would turn out to be a Jew.

[016-128]'Such was the opinion of St. Irenaeus, St. Jerome, and of the author of

[016-129]the work De Consummatione Mundi, ascribed to St. Hippolytus, and of a

[016-130]writer of a Commentary on the Epistle to the Thessalonians, ascribed to

[016-131]St. Ambrose, of many others, who said that he will be of the tribe of

[016-132]Dan: as, for instance, St. Gregory the Great, Theodoret, Aretas of

[016-133]Caesarea, and many more. Such also is the opinion of Bellarmine, who

[016-134]calls it certain. Lessius affirms that the Fathers, with unanimous

[016-135]consent, teach as undoubted that Antichrist will be a Jew. Ribera

[016-136]repeats the same opinion, and adds that Aretas, St. Bede, Haymo, St.

[016-137]Anselm, and Rupert affirm that for this reason the tribe of Dan is not

[016-138]numbered among those who are sealed in the Apocalypse ... Now, I think

[016-139]no one can consider the dispersion and providential preservation of the

[016-140]Jews among all the nations of the world and the indestructible vitality

[016-141]of their race without believing that they are reserved for some future

[016-142]action of His judgment and Grace. And this is foretold again and again

[016-143]in the New Testament.'

[016-144]'Our Lord,' continued Manning, widening the sweep of his speculations,

[016-145]'has said of these latter times: "There shall arise false Christs and

[016-146]false prophets, insomuch as to deceive even the elect"; that is, they

[016-147]shall not be deceived; but those who have lost faith in the Incarnation,

[016-148]such as humanitarians, rationalists, and pantheists, may well be

[016-149]deceived by any person of great political power and success, who should

[016-150]restore the Jews to their own land, and people Jerusalem once more with

[016-151]the sons of the Patriarchs. And, there is nothing in the political

[016-152]aspect of the world which renders such a combination impossible; indeed,

[016-153]the state of Syria, and the tide of European diplomacy, which 'is

[016-154]continually moving eastward, render such an event within a reasonable

[016-155]probability.'

[016-156]Then Manning threw out a bold suggestion. 'A successful medium,' he

[016-157]said, 'might well pass himself off by his preternatural endowments as

[016-158]the promised Messiahs.'

[016-159]Manning went on to discuss the course of events which would lead to the

[016-160]final catastrophe. But this subject, he confessed,

[016-161]'deals with agencies so transcendent and mysterious, that all I shall

[016-162]venture to do will be to sketch in outline what the broad and luminous

[016-163]prophecies, especially of the Book of Daniel and the Apocalypse, set

[016-164]forth without attempting to enter into minute details, which can only be

[016-165]interpreted by the event'.

[016-166]While applauding his modesty, we need follow Manning no further in his

[016-167]commentary upon those broad and luminous works; except to observe that

[016-168]'the apostasy of the City of Rome from the Vicar of Christ and its

[016-169]destruction by the Antichrist' was, in his opinion, certain. Nor was he

[016-170]without authority for this belief. For it was held by 'Malvenda, who

[016-171]writes expressly on the subject', and who, besides, 'states as the

[016-172]opinion of Ribera, Gaspar Melus, Viegas, Suarez, Bellarmine, and Bosius

[016-173]that Rome shall apostatise from the faith'.

[016-174]IX

[016-175]THE death of Pius IX brought to Manning a last flattering testimony of

[016-176]the confidence with which he was regarded at the Court of Rome. In one

[016-177]of the private consultations preceding the Conclave, a Cardinal

[016-178]suggested that Manning should succeed to the Papacy. He replied that he

[016-179]was unfit for the position, because it was essential for the interests

[016-180]of the Holy See that the next Pope should be an Italian. The suggestion

[016-181]was pressed, but Manning held firm. Thus it happened that the Triple

[016-182]Tiara seemed to come, for a moment, within the grasp of the late

[016-183]Archdeacon of Chichester; and the cautious hand refrained.
