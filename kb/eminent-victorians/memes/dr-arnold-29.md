[029-001]In the actual sphere of teaching, Dr. Arnold's reforms were tentative

[029-002]and few. He introduced modern history, modern languages, and mathematics

[029-003]into the school curriculum; but the results were not encouraging. He

[029-004]devoted to the teaching of history one hour a week; yet, though he took

[029-005]care to inculcate in these lessons a wholesome hatred of moral evil, and

[029-006]to point out from time to time the indications of the providential

[029-007]government of the world, his pupils never seemed to make much progress

[029-008]in the subject. Could it have been that the time allotted to it was

[029-009]insufficient? Dr. Arnold had some suspicions that this might be the

[029-010]case. With modern languages there was the same difficulty. Here his

[029-011]hopes were certainly not excessive. 'I assume it,' he wrote, 'as the

[029-012]foundation of all my view of the case, that boys at a public school

[029-013]never will learn to speak or pronounce French well, under any

[029-014]circumstances.' It would be enough if they could 'learn it grammatically

[029-015]as a dead language. But even this they very seldom managed to do.

[029-016]'I know too well, [he was obliged to confess,] that most of the boys

[029-017]would pass a very poor examination even in French grammar. But so it is

[029-018]with their mathematics; and so it will be with any branch of knowledge

[029-019]that is taught but seldom, and is felt to be quite subordinate to the

[029-020]boys' main study.'

[029-021]The boys' main study remained the dead languages of Greece and [[rome|Rome]].

[029-022]That the classics should form the basis of all teaching was an axiom

[029-023]with Dr. Arnold. 'The study of language,' he said, 'seems to me as if it

[029-024]was given for the very purpose of forming the human mind in youth; and

[029-025]the Greek and Latin languages seem the very instruments by which this is

[029-026]to be effected.' Certainly, there was something providential about

[029-027]it--from the point of view of the teacher as well as of the taught. If

[029-028]Greek and Latin had not been 'given' in that convenient manner, Dr.

[029-029]Arnold, who had spent his life in acquiring those languages, might have

[029-030]discovered that he had acquired them in vain. As it was, he could set

[029-031]the noses of his pupils to the grindstone of syntax and prosody with a

[029-032]clear conscience. Latin verses and Greek prepositions divided between

[029-033]them the labours of the week.

[029-034]As time went on he became, he declared, 'increasingly convinced that it

[029-035]is not knowledge, but the means of gaining knowledge which I have to

[029-036]teach'. The reading of the school was devoted almost entirely to

[029-037]selected passages from the prose writers of antiquity. 'Boys,' he

[029-038]remarked, 'do not like poetry.' Perhaps his own poetical taste was a

[029-039]little dubious; at any rate, it is certain that he considered the Greek

[029-040]Tragedians greatly overrated, and that he ranked Propertius as 'an

[029-041]indifferent poet'. As for Aristophanes, owing to his strong moral

[029-042]disapprobation, he could not bring himself to read him until he was

[029-043]forty, when, it is true, he was much struck by the 'Clouds'. But

[029-044]Juvenal, the Doctor could never bring himself to read at all.

[029-045]Physical science was not taught at [[rugby-school|Rugby]]. Since, in Dr. Arnold's

[029-046]opinion, it was too great a subject to be studied en parergo, obviously

[029-047]only two alternatives were possible: it must either take the chief place

[029-048]in the school curriculum, or it must be left out altogether. Before such

[029-049]a choice, Dr. Arnold did not hesitate for a moment.

[029-050]'Rather than have physical science the principal thing in my son's

[029-051]mind,' he exclaimed in a letter to a friend, I would gladly have him

[029-052]think that the sun went around the earth, and that the stars were so

[029-053]many spangles set in the bright blue firmament. Surely the one thing

[029-054]needful for a Christian and an English man to study is Christian, moral,

[029-055]and political philosophy.'

[029-056]A Christian and an Englishman! After all, it was not in the classroom,

[029-057]nor in the boarding-house, that the essential elements of instruction

[029-058]could be imparted which should qualify the youthful neophyte to deserve

[029-059]those names. The final, the fundamental lesson could only be taught in

[029-060]the school chapel; in the school chapel the centre of Dr. Arnold's

[029-061]system of education was inevitably fixed. There, too, the Doctor himself

[029-062]appeared in the plenitude of his dignity and his enthusiasm. There, with

[029-063]the morning sun shining on the freshly scrubbed faces of his 300 pupils,

[029-064]or, in the dusk of evening, through a glimmer of candles, his stately

[029-065]form, rapt in devotion or vibrant with exhortation, would dominate the

[029-066]scene. Every phase of the Church service seemed to receive its supreme

[029-067]expression in his voice, his attitude, his look. During the Te Deum, his

[029-068]whole countenance would light up; and he read the Psalms with such

[029-069]conviction that boys would often declare, after hearing him, that they

[029-070]understood them now for the first time.

[029-071]It was his opinion that the creeds in public worship ought to be used as

[029-072]triumphant hymns of thanksgiving, and, in accordance with this view,

[029-073]although unfortunately he possessed no natural gift for music, he

[029-074]regularly joined in the chanting of the Nicene Creed with a visible

[029-075]animation and a peculiar fervour, which it was impossible to forget. The

[029-076]Communion service he regarded as a direct and special counterpoise to

[029-077]that false communion and false companionship, which, as he often

[029-078]observed, was a great source of mischief in the school; and he bent

[029-079]himself down with glistening eyes, and trembling voice, and looks of

[029-080]paternal solicitude, in the administration of the elements. Nor was it

[029-081]only the different sections of the liturgy, but the very divisions of

[029-082]the ecclesiastical year that reflected themselves in his demeanour; the

[029-083]most careless observer, we are told, 'could not fail to be struck by the

[029-084]triumphant exultation of his whole manner on Easter Sunday'; though it

[029-085]needed a more familiar eye to discern the subtleties in his bearing

[029-086]which were produced by the approach or Advent, and the solemn thoughts

[029-087]which it awakened of the advance of human life, the progress of the

[029-088]human race, and the condition of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]].

[029-089]At the end of the evening service, the culminating moment of the week

[029-090]had come: the Doctor delivered his sermon. It was not until then, as all

[029-091]who had known him agreed, it was not until one had heard and seen him in

[029-092]the pulpit, that one could fully realise what it was to be face to face

[029-093]with Dr. Arnold. The whole character of the man--so we are

[029-094]assured--stood at last revealed. His congregation sat in fixed attention

[029-095](with the exception of the younger boys, whose thoughts occasionally

[029-096]wandered), while he propounded the general principles both of his own

[029-097]conduct and that of the Almighty, or indicated the bearing of the

[029-098]incidents of Jewish history in the sixth century B.C. upon the conduct

[029-099]of English schoolboys in 1830. Then, more than ever, his deep

[029-100]consciousness of the invisible world became evident; then, more than

[029-101]ever, he seemed to be battling with the wicked one. For his sermons ran

[029-102]on the eternal themes of the darkness of evil, the craft of the tempter,

[029-103]the punishment of obliquity, and he justified the persistence with which

[029-104]he dwelt upon these painful subjects by an appeal to a general

[029-105]principle: 'The spirit of Elijah,' he said, 'must ever precede the

[029-106]spirit of Christ.'

[029-107]The impression produced upon the boys was remarkable. It was noticed

[029-108]that even the most careless would sometimes, during the course of the

[029-109]week, refer almost involuntarily to the sermon of the past Sunday, as a

[029-110]condemnation of what they were doing. Others were heard to wonder how it

[029-111]was that the Doctor's preaching, to which they had attended at the time

[029-112]so assiduously, seemed, after all, to have such a small effect upon what

[029-113]they did. An old gentleman, recalling those vanished hours, tried to

[029-114]recapture in words his state of mind as he sat in the darkened chapel,

[029-115]while Dr. Arnold's sermons, with their high-toned exhortations, their

[029-116]grave and sombre messages of incalculable import, clothed, like Dr.

[029-117]Arnold's body in its gown and bands, in the traditional stiffness of a

[029-118]formal phraseology, reverberated through his adolescent ears. 'I used,'

[029-119]he said, 'to listen to those sermons from first to last with a kind of

[029-120]awe.'

[029-121]His success was not limited to his pupils and immediate auditors. The

[029-122]sermons were collected into five large volumes; they were the first of

[029-123]their kind; and they were received with admiration by a wide circle of

[029-124]pious readers. Queen Victoria herself possessed a copy in which several

[029-125]passages were marked in pencil, by the Royal hand.

[029-126]Dr. Arnold's energies were by no means exhausted by his duties at Rugby.

[029-127]He became known not merely as a headmaster, but as a public man. He held

[029-128]decided opinions upon a large number of topics; and he enunciated

[029-129]them--based as they were almost invariably upon general principles--in

[029-130]pamphlets, in prefaces, and in magazine articles, with an impressive

[029-131]self-confidence. He was, as he constantly declared, a Liberal. In his

[029-132]opinion, by the very constitution of human nature, the principles of

[029-133]progress and reform had been those of wisdom and justice in every age of

[029-134]the world--except one: that which had preceded the fall of man from

[029-135]Paradise. Had he lived then, Dr. Arnold would have been a Conservative.

[029-136]As it was, his Liberalism was tempered by an 'abhorrence of the spirit

[029-137]of 1789, of the American War, of the French Economistes, and of the

[029-138]English Whigs of the latter part of the seventeenth century'; and he

[029-139]always entertained a profound respect for the hereditary peerage. It

[029-140]might almost be said, in fact, that he was an orthodox Liberal. He

[029-141]believed in toleration too, within limits; that is to say, in the

[029-142]toleration of those with whom he agreed. 'I would give James Mill as

[029-143]much opportunity for advocating his opinion,' he said, 'as is consistent

[029-144]with a voyage to Botany Bay.'

[029-145]He had become convinced of the duty of sympathising with the lower

[029-146]orders ever since he had made a serious study of the Epistle of St.

[029-147]James; but he perceived clearly that the lower orders fell into two

[029-148]classes, and that it was necessary to distinguish between them. There

[029-149]were the 'good poor'--and there were the others. 'I am glad that you

[029-150]have made acquaintance with some of the good poor,' he wrote to a

[029-151]Cambridge undergraduate. 'I quite agree with you that it is most

[029-152]instructive to visit them.' Dr. Arnold himself occasionally visited

[029-153]them, in Rugby; and the condescension with which he shook hands with old

[029-154]men and women of the working classes was long remembered in the

[029-155]neighbourhood. As for the others, he regarded them with horror and

[029-156]alarm. 'The disorders in our social state,' he wrote to the Chevalier

[029-157]Bunsen in 1834, 'appear to me to continue unabated. You have heard, I

[029-158]doubt not, of the Trades Unions; a fearful engine of mischief, ready to

[029-159]not or to assassinate; and I see no counteracting power.'

[029-160]On the whole, his view of the condition of England was a gloomy one. He

[029-161]recommended a correspondent to read

[029-162]'Isaiah iii, v, xxii; Jeremiah v, xxii, xxx; Amos iv; and Habakkuk ii',

[029-163]adding, 'you will be struck, I think, with the close resemblance of our

[029-164]own state with that of the Jews before the second destruction of

[029-165]Jerusalem'.

[029-166]When he was told that the gift of tongues had descended on the

[029-167]Irvingites at Glasgow, he was not surprised. 'I should take it,' he

[029-168]said, 'merely as a sign of the coming of the day of the Lord.' And he

[029-169]was convinced that the day of the Lord was coming--'the termination of

[029-170]one of the great [Greek: aiones] of the human race'. Of that he had no

[029-171]doubt whatever; wherever he looked he saw 'calamities, wars, tumults,

[029-172]pestilences, earthquakes, etc., all marking the time of one of God's

[029-173]peculiar seasons of visitation'. His only uncertainty was whether this

[029-174]termination of an [Greek: aion] would turn out to be the absolutely

[029-175]final one; but that he believed 'no created being knows or can know'. In

[029-176]any case, he had 'not the slightest expectation of what is commonly

[029-177]meant by the Millennium'. And his only consolation was that he preferred

[029-178]the present Ministry, inefficient as it was, to the Tories.
