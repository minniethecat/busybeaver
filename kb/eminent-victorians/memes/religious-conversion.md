# Religious Conversion in Victorian England

The act of changing ecclesiastical allegiance — most consequentially, from the Church of England to the Church of Rome — was the defining spiritual drama of the Oxford Movement era. In Strachey's narrative, conversion is never purely theological: it is psychological, political, and often unintentionally comic.

## Definition

Religious conversion in the Victorian context most often meant reception into the Roman Catholic Church from Anglicanism. The process was freighted with social as well as spiritual consequences: converts lost preferment, broke friendships, and faced the suspicion of a Protestant nation that equated Rome with foreign domination. Strachey treats conversion not as a moment of grace but as the terminal point of a long psychological trajectory — the logical conclusion of premises accepted years before.

## Context in Victorian England

The "flood of converts" from the Church of England to Rome (009-154) following Newman's submission in 1845 was one of the most remarked-upon religious phenomena of the mid-Victorian period. The Oxford Movement, begun to revitalise Anglicanism, had ended by delivering many of its finest minds to the Roman obedience. Strachey's Manning biography traces this arc with particular attention to its ironies: the Tractarians who set out to prove that the Church of England was the true Catholic Church found themselves proving the opposite.

## In the Book

### In Cardinal Manning's Narrative

Conversion structures Manning's entire life. His movement from Evangelical Anglicanism to Tractarian High Churchmanship, and then to Roman Catholicism (1851), represents three successive conversions, each more absolute than the last. Strachey treats Manning's final conversion with characteristic dryness: "On April 6th, 1851, the final step was taken: Manning was received into the Roman Catholic Church" (009-001). The decision cost him his archdeaconry, his friendships, and his position in English society — but placed him on the path to the cardinalate.

Newman's earlier conversion (1845) had been the catalyst. Manning, who had visited Newman at Littlemore only to be turned away at the door (006-033), felt Newman's secession as a personal betrayal. Yet within six years, Manning followed. Strachey notes that "after Newman's conversion, he almost convinced himself that his 'visions' about the Roman Church had been the promptings of the Holy Ghost" (009-078).

### In Arthur Hugh Clough's Narrative

Clough's experience represents the obverse of conversion: loss of faith. Unlike Newman and Manning, who moved from one certainty to another, Clough "was made so uneasy by the loss of his that he went on looking for it everywhere as long as he lived; but somehow he never could find it" (006-106). Strachey contrasts this with James Anthony Froude, for whom "the loss of his faith turned out to be rather like the loss of a heavy portmanteau, which one afterwards discovers to have been full of old rags and brickbats" (006-106).

## Psychology of Conversion

Strachey's treatment of conversion is consistently psychological rather than theological. He notes how Manning, in the years before his conversion, "began to believe that the Church of England was no true Church at all" (008-075) — but presents this conviction as the rationalisation of a decision already unconsciously made. The converts at Littlemore "had passed, by a kind of irresistible momentum, from logic to logic" (006-078). Conversion, in Strachey's hands, is the inevitable terminus of a syllogism whose premises were laid down years before — the intellect catching up with the heart.

## Key Figures

- **John Henry Newman** (1801–1890): Received into the Catholic Church in 1845. His conversion was the watershed event of the Oxford Movement.
- **Henry Edward Manning** (1808–1892): Received into the Catholic Church in 1851. His conversion was more politically calculated than Newman's but no less consequential.
- **W. G. Ward** (1812–1882): The Tractarian logician whose *Ideal of a Christian Church* (1844) advocated Roman Catholicism while still an Anglican.
- **James Anthony Froude** (1818–1894): Brother of Hurrell, who lost his Anglican faith and found the experience liberating.
