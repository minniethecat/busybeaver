## Overview

Edward Bouverie Pusey (1800–1882) was Regius Professor of Hebrew at [[oxford-university|Oxford University]] and one of the three founding leaders of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]. His adherence lent the movement academic respectability, and his name became so synonymous with it that its followers were widely known as "Puseyites."

> Among the earliest of the converts was Dr Pusey, a man of wealth and learning, a professor, a canon of Christ Church, who had, it was rumoured, been to Germany. [003-141]

## Role in the Oxford Movement

Pusey joined [[john-henry-newman|John Henry Newman]] and [[john-keble|John Keble]] in the campaign to revive Catholic doctrine and practice within the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]. When the [[tractarianism|Tracts for the Times]] were launched under [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s editorship, Pusey became a major contributor, writing learned treatises on subjects such as Baptismal Regeneration [003-152].

His wealth, academic standing, and canonical position at Christ Church gave the movement a respectability it would otherwise have lacked. Unlike [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s mercurial brilliance or Keble's poetic piety, Pusey represented solid, professorial authority — the [[oxford-university|Oxford]] establishment in revolt against itself.

## After Newman's Conversion

[[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s conversion to Roman Catholicism in 1845 was a devastating blow to the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]. Pusey and Keble, however, refused to follow. Strachey captures their predicament with characteristic wit:

> Keble and Pusey continued for the rest of their lives to dance in an exemplary manner upon the tight-rope of High Anglicanism. [006-132]

This image — of two devout men maintaining an impossible balance between Catholic theology and Anglican loyalty — is one of Strachey's most memorable. Pusey spent the remaining decades of his long life defending the Anglican via media, insisting that the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] was truly Catholic without being Roman.

## Theological Contributions

Pusey's scholarship ranged across patristics, biblical studies, and sacramental theology. His learned works on Baptismal Regeneration and the Real Presence in the Eucharist became standard texts of the Anglo-Catholic tradition. He also played a key role in the establishment of religious orders within Anglicanism, founding the first Anglican sisterhood.

## Legacy

Pusey's legacy is embedded in the very name by which the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]'s followers were known: Puseyites. Though the term was originally a slur, it reflected the degree to which Pusey had become the public face of Anglican Catholicism. [[anglo-catholicism|Anglo-Catholicism]], the enduring tradition he helped to shape, remained a significant force within the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] well into the twentieth century.
