# Lord Hartington (8th Duke of Devonshire)

Liberal statesman, Secretary of State for War during the Gordon expedition, and the slow-moving conscience of the Cabinet — Strachey's portrait of Hartington is among the most affectionate in *Eminent Victorians*.

## Life

Spencer Compton Cavendish, Marquess of Hartington, was born in 1833 as the heir to the Dukedom of Devonshire. He entered [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] in 1857 and rose through the ranks of the Liberal Party to become one of its most respected figures. By the time of the Gordon crisis in 1884–1885, he was Secretary of State for War in [[william-ewart-gladstone]]'s second administration.

### The Gordon Crisis

Hartington's role in *Eminent Victorians* is centred on the agonizing question of whether to send a relief expedition to rescue [[general-gordon]] from [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. As Secretary of State for War, Hartington was at the centre of a triangular struggle: Gordon demanding action from the Sudan, [[evelyn-baring]] in Cairo urging precision and clarity, and [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] in London resisting military intervention.

Strachey's Hartington is a study in the virtues and limitations of the English aristocratic character. "Lord Hartington's conscience was of a piece with the rest of him," Strachey writes. "It was solid, it was slow, it was enormous" [043-035]. When others wavered or prevaricated, Hartington's moral solidity provided an anchor: "In Lord Hartington they saw, embodied and glorified, the very qualities that they most admired" [043-060].

### The Decision

> "Lord Hartington was slow. He was slow in arriving at a decision; and when he had arrived at it, he was slow in carrying it out." [043-095]

The relief expedition was finally authorized, but it was a decision reached through Hartington's characteristic deliberation — and it arrived too late. Gordon died at [[khartoum|Khartoum]] in January 1885, two days before the British relief force reached the city. Hartington's slowness, which in normal times might have been a statesmanlike virtue, had become, in Strachey's telling, a tragic liability.

At one critical juncture, Hartington remarked that this was "the most painful moment of his life" [043-078] — a rare expression of feeling from a man not given to emotional display. The relief expedition's failure haunted the Government and contributed to its fall later that year.

### Later Career

Hartington broke with [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] over Irish Home Rule in 1886, becoming leader of the Liberal Unionists. He later served in [[lord-salisbury]]'s coalition government. In 1891 he succeeded his father as the 8th Duke of Devonshire. He died in 1908.

## Character and Beliefs

Strachey's portrait of Hartington is remarkably sympathetic. Where other figures in *Eminent Victorians* are subjected to ironic dissection, Hartington is presented as fundamentally decent — slow, unimaginative perhaps, but honest and courageous. "Lord Hartington was never self-seeking," Strachey writes [043-066]; he was a man who inspired trust precisely because he seemed incapable of duplicity.

The pathos of Hartington's story is that his very virtues — deliberation, caution, conscientiousness — contributed to disaster. A more decisive or reckless minister might have acted sooner; Hartington's scrupulousness became, in the circumstances of the Sudan, indistinguishable from paralysis.

## Historical Significance

Lord Hartington, as Duke of Devonshire, was one of the most consequential Liberal statesmen of the late Victorian period. His break with [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] over Irish Home Rule in 1886 split the Liberal Party and kept it out of power for most of the following two decades. His political career spans the transition from Gladstonian liberalism to the coalition politics of the Edwardian era.

## In This Book

Hartington appears primarily in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47). Strachey uses him to explore a recurring theme: the tension between English decency and imperial necessity. Hartington's virtues are real, but they are also, in the context of empire, a kind of luxury — the indulgence of a conscience that operates at a distance from the suffering it cannot prevent. The portrait is Strachey at his most nuanced: satirical, yes, but also genuinely admiring.
