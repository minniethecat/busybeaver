# Cairo

The city where the threads of General Gordon's African destiny were pulled, slackened, and finally snapped. In Strachey's narrative of *Eminent Victorians*, Cairo is the seat of the Egyptian government — a place of Pashas and palace intrigue, of "unscrupulous cunning" and reluctant responsibility. It is from Cairo that Gordon was repeatedly summoned and dispatched, and it is in Cairo that the fatal consequences of English imperial policy in the Sudan were coolly absorbed by officials who "calmly" received news of disaster [^1].

## In the Narrative

Cairo appears throughout the Gordon chapters as the fulcrum of Anglo-Egyptian administration. The Khedive Ismail, having annexed the Sudan to Egypt, required a European governor-general to manage his troubled southern provinces [^2]. Gordon was the man chosen, and Cairo became the staging-post for his African adventures: the place where he received his commission, where he clashed with the Egyptian bureaucracy, and where he returned between expeditions. Strachey depicts the Cairo of the Pashas as a world of indolence and evasion — when word of the Hicks disaster reached the capital, "the Pashas calmly" contemplated the news without urgency [^1].

### Association with General Gordon

Gordon's relationship with Cairo was one of mutual incomprehension. Summoned by the Khedive to preside over the evacuation of the Sudan, he arrived in Cairo to find "the whole official world" resistant to his mission [^3]. The Egyptian authorities, Strachey implies, preferred inaction to the discomfort of decision. Between his tours of duty, Gordon would pass through Cairo — pulling "the old strings" of influence [^4] — only to find that the strings were attached to nothing at all. Before the travellers reached Cairo on his final journey, "steps had been taken which" had already sealed the fate of his mission [^5].

## Key Events

- **Gordon's Appointment (1874)**: Gordon was summoned to Cairo by the Khedive Ismail to take up the governorship of the equatorial provinces of the Sudan, succeeding Sir Samuel Baker [^2]
- **The Hicks Disaster (1883)**: News of the annihilation of Hicks Pasha's army by the Mahdi's forces reached Cairo, where the Egyptian government received it with a fatal passivity [^1]
- **Gordon's Final Departure (1884)**: Gordon stopped in Cairo en route to Khartoum, saw the English Ambassador, and proceeded southward into the mission that would end in his death [^3]
- **The Slave Trade**: Cairo's markets were a terminus of the Sudanese slave trade, which Gordon was charged with suppressing — "the slave-markets of Cairo and Constantinople" [^6]
