[034-001]'I asked him why I should plot, and go around a mountain, when a mere

[034-002]order, written with five strokes of the quill, would have accomplished

[034-003]the same thing. He did not answer, but he insulted me, and said he would

[034-004]report my treachery, as he called it, to Shanghai and England. Let him

[034-005]do so; he cannot bring the crazy Wangs back.'

[034-006]The agitated Mandarin hoped to placate Gordon by a large gratuity and an

[034-007]Imperial medal; but the plan was not successful.

[034-008]'[[general-gordon|General Gordon]],' he writes, 'called upon me in his angriest mood. He

[034-009]repeated his former speeches about the Wangs. I did not attempt to argue

[034-010]with him ... He refused the 10,000 taels, which I had ready for him,

[034-011]and, with an oath, said that he did not want the Throne's medal. This is

[034-012]showing the greatest disrespect.'

[034-013]Gordon resigned his command; and it was only with the utmost reluctance

[034-014]that he agreed at last to resume it. An arduous and terrible series of

[034-015]operations followed; but they were successful, and by June, 1864, the

[034-016]Ever Victorious Army, having accomplished its task, was disbanded. The

[034-017]Imperial forces now closed round Nankin; the last hopes of the Tien Wang

[034-018]had vanished. In the recesses of his seraglio, the Celestial King,

[034-019]judging that the time had come for the conclusion of his mission,

[034-020]swallowed gold leaf until he ascended to Heaven. In July, Nankin was

[034-021]taken, the remaining chiefs were executed, and the rebellion was at an

[034-022]end. The Chinese Government gave Gordon the highest rank in its military

[034-023]hierarchy, and invested him with the yellow jacket and the peacock's

[034-024]feather. He rejected an enormous offer of money; but he could not refuse

[034-025]a great gold medal, specially struck in his honour by order of the

[034-026]Emperor. At the end of the year he returned to England, where the

[034-027]conqueror of the Taipings was made a Companion of the Bath.

[034-028]That the English authorities should have seen fit to recognise Gordon's

[034-029]services by the reward usually reserved for industrious clerks was

[034-030]typical of their attitude towards him until the very end of his career.

[034-031]Perhaps if he had been ready to make the most of the wave of popularity

[034-032]which greeted him on his return--if he had advertised his fame and, amid

[034-033]high circles, played the part of Chinese Gordon in a becoming

[034-034]manner--the results would have been different. But he was by nature

[034-035]farouche; his soul revolted against dinner parties and stiff shirts; and

[034-036]the presence of ladies--especially of fashionable ladies--filled him

[034-037]with uneasiness. He had, besides, a deeper dread of the world's

[034-038]contaminations. And so, when he was appointed to Gravesend to supervise

[034-039]the erection of a system of forts at the mouth of the Thames, he

[034-040]remained there quietly for six years, and at last was almost forgotten.

[034-041]The forts, which were extremely expensive and quite useless, occupied

[034-042]his working hours; his leisure he devoted to acts of charity and to

[034-043]religious contemplation. The neighbourhood was a poverty-stricken one,

[034-044]and the kind Colonel, with his tripping step and simple manner, was soon

[034-045]a familiar figure in it, chatting with the seamen, taking provisions to

[034-046]starving families, or visiting some bedridden old woman to light her

[034-047]fire. He was particularly fond of boys. Ragged street arabs and rough

[034-048]sailor-lads crowded about him. They were made free of his house and

[034-049]garden; they visited him in the evenings for lessons and advice; he

[034-050]helped them, found them employment, corresponded with them when they

[034-051]went out into the world. They were, he said, his Wangs. It was only by a

[034-052]singular austerity of living that he was able to afford such a variety

[034-053]of charitable expenses. The easy luxuries of his class and station were

[034-054]unknown to him: his clothes verged upon the shabby; and his frugal meals

[034-055]were eaten at a table with a drawer, into which the loaf and plate were

[034-056]quickly swept at the approach of his poor visitors. Special occasions

[034-057]demanded special sacrifices. When, during the Lancashire famine, a

[034-058]public subscription was opened, finding that he had no ready money, he

[034-059]remembered his Chinese medal, and, after effacing the inscription,

[034-060]dispatched it as an anonymous gift.

[034-061]Except for his boys and his paupers, he lived alone. In his solitude, he

[034-062]ruminated upon the mysteries of the universe; and those religious

[034-063]tendencies, which had already shown themselves, now became a fixed and

[034-064]dominating factor in his life. His reading was confined almost entirely

[034-065]to the Bible; but the Bible he read and re-read with an untiring,

[034-066]unending assiduity. There, he was convinced, all truth was to be found;

[034-067]and he was equally convinced that he could find it. The doubts of

[034-068]philosophers, the investigations of commentators, the smiles of men of

[034-069]the world, the dogmas of Churches--such things meant nothing to the

[034-070]Colonel. Two facts alone were evident: there was the Bible, and there

[034-071]was himself; and all that remained to be done was for him to discover

[034-072]what were the Bible's instructions, and to act accordingly. In order to

[034-073]make this discovery it was only necessary for him to read the Bible over

[034-074]and over again; and therefore, for the rest of his life, he did so.

[034-075]The faith that he evolved was mystical and fatalistic; it was also

[034-076]highly unconventional. His creed, based upon the narrow foundations of

[034-077]Jewish Scripture, eked out occasionally by some English evangelical

[034-078]manual, was yet wide enough to ignore every doctrinal difference, and

[034-079]even, at moments, to transcend the bounds of Christianity itself. The

[034-080]just man was he who submitted to the Will of God, and the Will of God,

[034-081]inscrutable and absolute, could be served aright only by those who

[034-082]turned away from earthly desires and temporal temptations, to rest

[034-083]themselves whole-heartedly upon the in-dwelling Spirit. Human beings

[034-084]were the transitory embodiments of souls who had existed through an

[034-085]infinite past, and would continue to exist through an infinite future.

[034-086]The world was vanity; the flesh was dust and ashes.

[034-087]'A man,' Gordon wrote to his sister, 'who knows not the secret, who has

[034-088]not the in-dwelling of God revealed to him, is like this--[picture of a

[034-089]circle with Body and Soul written within it]. He takes the promises and

[034-090]curses as addressed to him as one man, and will not hear of there being

[034-091]any birth before his natural birth, in any existence except with the

[034-092]body he is in. The man to whom the secret (the indwelling of God) is

[034-093]revealed is like this: [picture of a circle with soul and body enclosed

[034-094]in two separate circles].

[034-095]He applies the promises to one and the curses to the other, if

[034-096]disobedient, which he must be, except the soul is enabled by God to

[034-097]rule. He then sees he is not of this world; for when he speaks of

[034-098]himself he quite disregards the body his soul lives in, which is

[034-099]earthly.'

[034-100]Such conceptions are familiar enough in the history of religious

[034-101]thought: they are those of the hermit and the fakir; and it might have

[034-102]been expected that, when once they had taken hold upon his mind, Gordon

[034-103]would have been content to lay aside the activities of his profession,

[034-104]and would have relapsed at last into the complete retirement of holy

[034-105]meditation. But there were other elements in his nature which urged him

[034-106]towards a very different course. He was no simple quietist. He was an

[034-107]English gentleman, an officer, a man of energy and action, a lover of

[034-108]danger and the audacities that defeat danger; a passionate creature,

[034-109]flowing over with the self-assertiveness of independent judgment and the

[034-110]arbitrary temper of command.

[034-111]Whatever he might find in his pocket-Bible, it was not for such as he to

[034-112]dream out his days in devout obscurity. But, conveniently enough, he

[034-113]found nothing in his pocket-Bible indicating that he should. What he did

[034-114]find was that the Will of God was inscrutable and absolute; that it was

[034-115]man's duty to follow where God's hand led; and, if God's hand led

[034-116]towards violent excitements and extraordinary vicissitudes, that it was

[034-117]not only futile, it was impious to turn another way. Fatalism is always

[034-118]apt to be a double-edged philosophy; for while, on the one hand, it

[034-119]reveals the minutest occurrences as the immutable result of a rigid

[034-120]chain of infinitely predestined causes, on the other, it invests the

[034-121]wildest incoherences of conduct or of circumstance with the sanctity of

[034-122]eternal law. And Gordon's fatalism was no exception. The same doctrine

[034-123]that led him to dally with omens, to search for prophetic texts, and to

[034-124]append, in brackets, the apotropaic initials D.V. after every statement

[034-125]in his letters implying futurity, led him also to envisage his moods and

[034-126]his desires, his passing reckless whims and his deep unconscious

[034-127]instincts, as the mysterious manifestations of the indwelling God. That

[034-128]there was danger lurking in such a creed he was very well aware. The

[034-129]grosser temptations of the world--money and the vulgar attributes of

[034-130]power--had, indeed, no charms for him; but there were subtler and more

[034-131]insinuating allurements which it was not so easy to resist. More than

[034-132]one observer declared that ambition was, in reality, the essential

[034-133]motive in his life: ambition, neither for wealth nor titles, but for

[034-134]fame and influence, for the swaying of multitudes, and for that kind of

[034-135]enlarged and intensified existence 'where breath breathes most even in

[034-136]the mouths of men'. Was it so? In the depths of Gordon's soul there were

[034-137]intertwining contradictions--intricate recesses where egoism and

[034-138]renunciation melted into one another, where the flesh lost itself in the

[034-139]spirit, and the spirit in the flesh. What was the Will of God? The

[034-140]question, which first became insistent during his retirement at

[034-141]Gravesend, never afterwards left him; it might almost be said that he

[034-142]spent the remainder of his life in searching for the answer to it. In

[034-143]all his Odysseys, in all his strange and agitated adventures, a day

[034-144]never passed on which he neglected the voice of eternal wisdom as it

[034-145]spoke through the words of Paul or Solomon, of Jonah or Habakkuk. He

[034-146]opened his Bible, he read, and then he noted down his reflections upon

[034-147]scraps of paper, which, periodically pinned together, he dispatched to

[034-148]one or other of his religious friends, and particularly his sister

[034-149]Augusta. The published extracts from these voluminous outpourings lay

[034-150]bare the inner history of Gordon's spirit, and reveal the pious

[034-151]visionary of Gravesend in the restless hero of three continents.

[034-152]His seclusion came to an end in a distinctly providential manner. In

[034-153]accordance with a stipulation in the Treaty of Paris, an international

[034-154]commission had been appointed to improve the navigation of the Danube;

[034-155]and Gordon, who had acted on a similar body fifteen years earlier, was

[034-156]sent out to represent Great Britain. At Constantinople, he chanced to

[034-157]meet the Egyptian minister, Nubar Pasha. The Governorship of the

[034-158]Equatorial Provinces of the Sudan was about to fall vacant; and Nubar

[034-159]offered the post to Gordon, who accepted it.

[034-160]'For some wise design,' he wrote to his sister, 'God turns events one

[034-161]way or another, whether man likes it or not, as a man driving a horse

[034-162]turns it to right or left without consideration as to whether the horse

[034-163]likes that way or not. To be happy, a man must be like a well-broken,

[034-164]willing horse, ready for anything. Events will go as God likes.'

[034-165]And then followed six years of extraordinary, desperate, unceasing, and

[034-166]ungrateful labour. The unexplored and pestilential region of Equatoria,

[034-167]stretching southwards to the Great Lakes and the sources of the Nile,

[034-168]had been annexed to Egypt by the Khedive Ismail, who, while he

[034-169]squandered his millions on Parisian ballet-dancers, dreamt strange

[034-170]dreams of glory and empire. Those dim tracts of swamp and forest in

[034-171]Central Africa were--so he declared--to be 'opened up'; they were to

[034-172]receive the blessings of civilisation, they were to become a source of

[034-173]eternal honour to himself and Egypt. The slave-trade, which flourished

[034-174]there, was to be put down; the savage inhabitants were to become

[034-175]acquainted with freedom, justice, and prosperity. Incidentally, a

[034-176]government monopoly in ivory was to be established, and the place was to

[034-177]be made a paying concern. Ismail, hopelessly in debt to a horde of

[034-178]European creditors, looked to Europe to support him in his schemes.

[034-179]Europe, and, in particular, England, with her passion for extraneous

[034-180]philanthropy, was not averse. Sir Samuel Baker became the first Governor

[034-181]of Equatoria, and now Gordon was to carry on the good work. In such

[034-182]circumstances it was only natural that Gordon should consider himself a

[034-183]special instrument in God's band. To put his disinterestedness beyond

[034-184]doubt, he reduced his salary, which had been fixed at L10,000, to

[034-185]L2,000. He took over his new duties early in 1874, and it was not long

[034-186]before he had a first hint of disillusionment. On his way up the Nile,

[034-187]he was received in state at [[khartoum|Khartoum]] by the Egyptian Governor-General of

[034-188]the Sudan, his immediate official superior.
