# Sanitary Reform

The application of engineering, statistics, and administrative will to the improvement of public health — the cause that transformed Florence Nightingale from the Lady with the Lamp into what Strachey calls "a glorified sanitary engineer" (026-052). In Strachey's telling, drains matter more than devotion.

## Nightingale's Sanitary Crusade

Nightingale's discovery at Scutari was that more soldiers died from disease than from wounds — and that disease was caused not by fate but by "insanitary principles, with unventilated rooms, with defective drainage" (024-153). Her post-war career was devoted to proving, through statistical evidence and royal commissions, that sanitary reform saved lives. Strachey notes that "the hospitals were, in effect, carried out by Miss Nightingale" — not through bedside care but through administrative pressure (024-153).

## Strachey's Treatment

Strachey's famous description of Nightingale as less angel of mercy than administrative genius is anchored in her sanitary work. She "had the statistical mind" and used it to force reform on a resistant War Office. The irony is double: the woman celebrated for feminine compassion was in fact a bureaucratic warrior, and the reforms she achieved through decades of invalidism from her sofa were more consequential than anything she did in the Crimea.
