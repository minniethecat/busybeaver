# Religious Doubt and Scepticism in Victorian England

The loss of religious faith was the great unspoken crisis of Victorian intellectual life. For every Newman who found certainty in Rome, there was a Clough who lost it everywhere. Strachey treats doubt not as intellectual error but as a psychological condition — the inevitable consequence of minds too honest to accept the Tractarian synthesis and too earnest to be content without belief.

## Definition

Victorian religious doubt took many forms: uncertainty about the historical accuracy of Scripture (prompted by German Higher Criticism), doubt about the moral character of the Old Testament God, scepticism about miracles in an age of science, and existential doubt about the very possibility of religious knowledge. Strachey distinguishes two modes of doubt: the anguished searching of Arthur Hugh Clough, who "went on looking for it everywhere as long as he lived," and the relieved liberation of James Anthony Froude, for whom doubt was "like the loss of a heavy portmanteau... full of old rags and brickbats" (006-106).

## In the Book

Doubt appears in all four biographies as the counterpoint to certainty. Manning's trajectory — Evangelical to Tractarian to Roman Catholic to Ultramontane — can be read as a flight from doubt, each step a more desperate grasp at absolute authority. Nightingale's mysticism side-stepped doctrinal questions entirely. Arnold's Broad Church liberalism was designed to accommodate doubt within the Church rather than expel it. Gordon's faith was too simple, too childlike, to admit doubt at all.

### Arthur Hugh Clough

Clough is Strachey's paradigmatic doubter. A Rugby pupil and Arnold's favourite, he absorbed the moral earnestness of the public school without its doctrinal certainty. "At the time of the Oxford Movement, Clough had passed his life in a state of religious tension" (023-159). His poetry — *Dipsychus*, *The Bothie of Tober-na-Vuolich* — gave voice to the divided consciousness of the Victorian intellectual: morally serious, intellectually honest, unable to believe.

## Key Figures

- **Arthur Hugh Clough** (1819–1861): Poet, Rugby pupil, and Arnold's protégé, whose loss of faith became the subject of his finest poetry.
- **James Anthony Froude** (1818–1894): Younger brother of Hurrell Froude, historian, and biographer of Carlyle, whose break with Anglican orthodoxy was more decisive — and more cheerful — than Clough's.
- **George Eliot** (1819–1880): The great novelist of Victorian doubt, whose translation of Strauss's *Life of Jesus* and novels of provincial life explored the moral possibilities of unbelief.
