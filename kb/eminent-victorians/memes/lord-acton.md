# Lord Acton (1st Baron Acton)

The great Victorian historian, Liberal Catholic thinker, and opponent of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]], whose friendship with [[cardinal-manning]] illuminates the intellectual tensions within nineteenth-century Catholicism.

## Life

John Emerich Edward Dalberg-Acton, 1st Baron Acton, was born in 1834 into a cosmopolitan Catholic family with connections across the European aristocracy. Educated at Oscott under [[cardinal-wiseman]] and later at Munich under the great Church historian Ignaz von Döllinger, Acton became one of the most learned men of his generation.

### The Manning Friendship

Acton's role in *Eminent Victorians* centres on his unlikely friendship with [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]]. The two men could hardly have been more different: Manning the Ultramontane, the enthusiast for papal authority; Acton the Liberal Catholic, the scholar who believed that the Church's claims must be subject to historical scrutiny.

> "The outcome of his friendship with Lord Acton, a young Catholic layman of great abilities and high rank." [014-132]

Strachey depicts the friendship as revealing Manning's capacity for intellectual connection — and his ultimate inability to let friendship override ambition. Acton was, in the theological debates of the time, a Liberal Catholic: "he went to [[rome|Rome]] with a difference" [014-144], committed to the Church but suspicious of its centralizing, anti-intellectual tendencies.

### The Vatican Council

Acton's most consequential role was his opposition to the doctrine of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]] at the [[vatican-council-1870|First Vatican Council]] (1869–1870). Working through Döllinger and a network of European Liberal Catholics, Acton fought to prevent the proclamation of the dogma. Manning, by contrast, was one of the doctrine's most effective advocates.

The tension came to a head in their correspondence. Acton's letters became a conduit through which Manning received intelligence about the opposition — and, in Strachey's ironic telling, "thus carried the ammunition for his own destruction" [015-008]. Manning, who "despised Lord Acton almost as much as he disliked his opinions" [014-157], used Acton's information while keeping him at arm's length.

### Later Years

Acton was not included among the Ultramontane victors after the Council [015-119]. He retreated to scholarship, eventually becoming Regius Professor of Modern History at Cambridge in 1895. His inaugural lecture on the study of history remains a classic statement of the historian's craft. It was Acton who coined the famous maxim: "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely." He died in 1902.

## Character and Beliefs

Acton represented the Catholic intellectual tradition at its most rigorous. He believed that the Church had nothing to fear from historical truth, and that the suppression of scholarship in the name of authority was a betrayal of the faith. His tragedy was to watch the Church he loved choose authority over inquiry.

## Historical Significance

Lord Acton was one of the greatest historians of the Victorian age. His vision of universal history, his insistence on the moral dimension of historical judgment, and his editing of the Cambridge Modern History established standards of scholarship that endured for generations. As a Liberal Catholic, he represented a tradition that would eventually influence the reforms of the Second [[vatican-council-1870|Vatican Council]].

## In This Book

Acton appears in the Manning section (Chapters 1–17) as Manning's intellectual foil — the scholar whose friendship Manning valued but whose principles he could not share. Strachey's treatment is sympathetic: Acton is the one figure in the Manning drama who consistently puts truth before advantage.
