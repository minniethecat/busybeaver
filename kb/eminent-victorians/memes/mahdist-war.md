# Mahdist War

The religious-political uprising that provided the occasion for Gordon's martyrdom. Muhammad Ahmad, a Sufi religious leader, proclaimed himself the Mahdi (the prophesied redeemer of Islam) in 1881 and led a revolt against Egyptian rule in the Sudan. His forces captured Khartoum in 1885, killed Gordon, and established an independent Mahdist state that survived until Kitchener's reconquest in 1898–1899.

## Gordon's Involvement

Gordon's appointment to evacuate Egyptian garrisons was the British government's reluctant response to the Mahdist threat. The mission was supposed to be administrative; it became military. Gordon's decision to stay and fight — against instructions, against prudence, against the logic of the situation — transformed a colonial evacuation into an imperial tragedy.

## Strachey's Treatment

Strachey treats the Mahdi only as a shadow — the force that defines Gordon's martyrdom without being given an independent character. The Mahdist War is, in Strachey's narrative, not a conflict between two civilisations but the external pressure that reveals Gordon's internal contradictions.
