[025-001]Meanwhile, still ravenous for yet more and more work, her activities had

[025-002]branched out into new directions. The Army in India claimed her

[025-003]attention. A Sanitary Commission, appointed at her suggestion, and

[025-004]working under her auspices, did for our troops there what the four

[025-005]Sub-Commissions were doing for those at home. At the same time, these

[025-006]very years which saw her laying the foundations of the whole modern

[025-007]system of medical work in the Army, saw her also beginning to bring her

[025-008]knowledge, her influence, and her activity into the service of the

[025-009]country at large. Her "Notes on Hospitals" (1859) revolutionised the

[025-010]theory of hospital construction and hospital management. She was

[025-011]immediately recognised as the leading expert upon all the questions

[025-012]involved; her advice flowed unceasingly and in all directions, so that

[025-013]there is no great hospital today which does not bear upon it the impress

[025-014]of her mind. Nor was this all. With the opening of the Nightingale

[025-015]Training School for Nurses at St. Thomas's Hospital (1860), she became

[025-016]the founder of modern nursing.

[025-017]But a terrible crisis was now fast approaching. Sidney Herbert had

[025-018]consented to undertake the root and branch reform of the [[war-office|War Office]]. He

[025-019]had sallied forth into that tropical jungle of festooned

[025-020]obstructiveness, of intertwisted irresponsibilities, of crouching

[025-021]prejudices, of abuses grown stiff and rigid with antiquity, which for so

[025-022]many years to come was destined to lure reforming Ministers to their

[025-023]doom.

[025-024]'The War Office,' said [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]], 'is a very slow office, an

[025-025]enormously expensive office, and one in which the Minister's intentions

[025-026]can be entirely negated by all his sub-departments, and those of each of

[025-027]the sub-departments by every other.'

[025-028]It was true; and of course, at the, first rumour of a change, the old

[025-029]phalanx of reaction was bristling with its accustomed spears. At its

[025-030]head stood no longer Dr. Andrew Smith, who, some time since, had

[025-031]followed the Bison into outer darkness, but a yet more formidable

[025-032]figure, the Permanent Under-Secretary himself, Sir Benjamin Hawes--Ben

[025-033]Hawes the Nightingale Cabinet irreverently dubbed him 'a man remarkable

[025-034]even among civil servants for adroitness in baffling inconvenient

[025-035]inquiries, resource in raising false issues, and, in, short, a

[025-036]consummate command of all the arts of officially sticking in the mud'.

[025-037]'Our scheme will probably result in Ben Hawes's resignation,' Miss

[025-038]Nightingale said; 'and that is another of its advantages.' Ben Hawes

[025-039]himself, however, did not quite see it in that light. He set himself to

[025-040]resist the wishes of the Minister by every means in his power. The

[025-041]struggle was long, and desperate; and, as it proceeded, it gradually

[025-042]became evident to Miss Nightingale that something was the matter with

[025-043]Sidney Herbert. What was it? His health, never very strong, was, he

[025-044]said, in danger of collapsing under the strain of his work. But, after

[025-045]all, what is illness, when there is a War Office to be reorganised? Then

[025-046]he began to talk of retiring altogether from public life. The doctors

[025-047]were consulted, and declared that, above all things, what was necessary

[025-048]was rest. Rest! She grew seriously alarmed. Was it possible that, at the

[025-049]last moment, the crowning wreath of victory was to be snatched from her

[025-050]grasp? She was not to be put aside by doctors; they were talking

[025-051]nonsense; the necessary thing was not rest, but the reform of the War

[025-052]Office; and, besides, she knew very well from her own case what one

[025-053]could do even when one was on the point of death.

[025-054]She expostulated vehemently, passionately; the goal was so near, so very

[025-055]near; he could not turn back now! At any rate, he could not resist Miss

[025-056]Nightingale. A compromise was arranged. Very reluctantly, he exchanged

[025-057]the turmoil of the House of Commons for the dignity of the House of

[025-058]Lords, and he remained at the War Office. She was delighted. 'One fight

[025-059]more, the best and the last,' she said.

[025-060]For several more months the fight did indeed go on. But the strain upon

[025-061]him was greater even than she perhaps could realise. Besides the

[025-062]intestine war in his office, he had to face a constant battle in the

[025-063]Cabinet with [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]--a more redoubtable antagonist even than Ben

[025-064]Hawes--over the estimates. His health grew worse and worse. He was

[025-065]attacked by fainting-fits; and there were some days when he could only

[025-066]just keep himself going by gulps of brandy. Miss Nightingale spurred him

[025-067]forward with her encouragements and her admonitions, her zeal and her

[025-068]example. But at last his spirit began to sink as well as his body. He

[025-069]could no longer hope; he could no longer desire; it was useless, all

[025-070]useless; it was utterly impossible. He had failed. The dreadful moment

[025-071]came when the truth was forced upon him: he would never be able to

[025-072]reform the War Office. But a yet more dreadful moment lay behind; he

[025-073]must go to Miss Nightingale and tell her that he was a failure, a beaten

[025-074]man.

[025-075]'Blessed are the merciful!' What strange ironic prescience had led

[025-076]Prince Albert, in the simplicity of his heart, to choose that motto for

[025-077]the Crimean brooch? The words hold a double lesson; and, alas! when she

[025-078]brought herself to realise at length what was indeed the fact and what

[025-079]there was no helping, it was not in mercy that she turned upon her old

[025-080]friend.

[025-081]'Beaten!' she exclaimed. 'Can't you see that you've simply thrown away

[025-082]the game? And with all the winning cards in your hands! And so noble a

[025-083]game! Sidney Herbert beaten! And beaten by Ben Hawes! It is a worse

[025-084]disgrace ...' her full rage burst out at last, '... a worse disgrace

[025-085]than the hospitals at [[scutari|Scutari]].'

[025-086]He dragged himself away from her, dragged himself to Spa, hoping vainly

[025-087]for a return to health, and then, despairing, back again to England, to

[025-088]Wilton, to the majestic house standing there resplendent in the summer

[025-089]sunshine, among the great cedars which had lent their shade to Sir

[025-090]Philip Sidney, and all those familiar, darling haunts of beauty which he

[025-091]loved, each one of them, 'as if they were persons'; and at, Wilton he

[025-092]died. After having received the Eucharist, he had become perfectly calm;

[025-093]then, almost unconscious, his lips were seen to be moving. Those about

[025-094]him bent down. 'Poor Florence! Poor Florence!' they just caught.' ...

[025-095]Our joint work ... unfinished ... tried to do ...' and they could hear

[025-096]no more.

[025-097]When the onward rush of a powerful spirit sweeps a weaker one to its

[025-098]destruction, the commonplaces of the moral judgment are better left

[025-099]unmade. If Miss Nightingale had been less ruthless, Sidney Herbert would

[025-100]not have perished; but then, she would not have been Miss Nightingale.

[025-101]The force that created was the force that destroyed. It was her Demon

[025-102]that was responsible. When the fatal news reached her, she was overcome

[025-103]by agony. In the revulsion of her feelings, she made a worship of the

[025-104]dead man's memory; and the facile instrument which had broken in her

[025-105]hand she spoke of forever after as her 'Master'. Then, almost at the

[025-106]same moment, another blow fell on her. Arthur Clough, worn out by

[025-107]labours very different from those of Sidney Herbert, died too: never

[025-108]more would he tie up her parcels. And yet a third disaster followed. The

[025-109]faithful Aunt Mai did not, to be sure, die; no, she did something almost

[025-110]worse: she left Miss Nightingale. She was growing old, and she felt that

[025-111]she had closer and more imperative duties with her own family. Her niece

[025-112]could hardly forgive her. She poured out, in one of her enormous

[025-113]letters, a passionate diatribe upon the faithlessness, the lack of

[025-114]sympathy, the stupidity, the ineptitude of women. Her doctrines had

[025-115]taken no hold among them; she had never known one who had appris a

[025-116]apprendre; she could not even get a woman secretary; 'they don't know

[025-117]the names of the Cabinet Ministers--they don't know which of the

[025-118]Churches has Bishops and which not'. As for the spirit of

[025-119]self-sacrifice, well--Sidney Herbert and Arthur Clough were men, and

[025-120]they indeed had shown their devotion; but women--! She would mount three

[025-121]widow's caps 'for a sign'. The first two would be for Clough and for her

[025-122]Master; but the third--'the biggest widow's cap of all'--would be for

[025-123]Aunt Mai. She did well to be angry; she was deserted in her hour of

[025-124]need; and after all, could she be sure that even the male sex was so

[025-125]impeccable? There was Dr. Sutherland, bungling as usual. Perhaps even he

[025-126]intended to go off one of these days, too? She gave him a look, and he

[025-127]shivered in his shoes. No!--she grinned sardonically; she would always

[025-128]have Dr. Sutherland. And then she reflected that there was one thing

[025-129]more that she would always have--her work.

[025-130]IV

[025-131]SIDNEY HERBERT'S death finally put an end to Miss Nightingale's dream of

[025-132]a reformed War Office. For a moment, indeed, in the first agony of her

[025-133]disappointment, she had wildly clutched at a straw; she had written to

[025-134]M. Gladstone to beg him to take up the burden of Sidney Herbert's work.

[025-135]And Mr. Gladstone had replied with a sympathetic account of the funeral.

[025-136]Succeeding Secretaries of State managed between them to undo a good deal

[025-137]of what had been accomplished, but they could not undo it all; and for

[025-138]ten years more (1862-72) Miss Nightingale remained a potent influence at

[025-139]the War Office. After that, her direct connection with the Army came to

[025-140]an end, and her energies began to turn more and more completely towards

[025-141]more general objects. Her work upon hospital reform assumed enormous

[025-142]proportions; she was able to improve the conditions in infirmaries and

[025-143]workhouses; and one of her most remarkable papers forestalls the

[025-144]recommendations of the Poor Law Commission of 1909. Her training, school

[025-145]for nurses, with all that it involved in initiative, control,

[025-146]responsibillity, and combat, would have been enough in itself to have

[025-147]absorbed the whole efforts of at least two lives of ordinary vigour. And

[025-148]at the same time her work in connection with India, which had begun with

[025-149]the Sanitary Commission on the Indian Army, spread and ramified in a

[025-150]multitude of directions. Her tentacles reached the India Office and

[025-151]succeeded in establishing a hold even upon those slippery high places.

[025-152]For many years it was de rigueur for the newly appointed Viceroy, before

[025-153]he left England, to pay a visit to Miss Nightingale.

[025-154]After much hesitation, she had settled down in a small house in South

[025-155]Street, where she remained for the rest of her life. That life was a

[025-156]very long one; the dying woman reached her ninety-first year. Her ill

[025-157]health gradually diminished; the crises of extreme danger became less

[025-158]frequent, and at last altogether ceased; she remained an invalid, but an

[025-159]invalid of a curious character--an invalid who was too weak to walk

[025-160]downstairs and who worked far harder than most Cabinet Ministers. Her

[025-161]illness, whatever it may have been, was certainly not inconvenient. It

[025-162]involved seclusion; and an extraordinary, an unparalleled seclusion was,

[025-163]it might almost have been said, the mainspring of Miss Nightingale's

[025-164]life. Lying on her sofa in the little upper room in South Street, she

[025-165]combined the intense vitality of a dominating woman of the world with

[025-166]the mysterious and romantic quality of a myth. She was a legend in her

[025-167]lifetime, and she knew it. She tasted the joys of power, like those

[025-168]Eastern Emperors whose autocratic rule was based upon invisibility, with

[025-169]the mingled satisfactions of obscurity and fame.

[025-170]And she found the machinery of illness hardly less effective as a

[025-171]barrier against the eyes of men than the ceremonial of a palace. Great

[025-172]statesmen and renowned generals were obliged to beg for audiences;

[025-173]admiring princesses from foreign countries found that they must see her

[025-174]at her own time, or not at all; and the ordinary mortal had no hope of

[025-175]ever getting beyond the downstairs sitting-room and Dr. Sutherland. For

[025-176]that indefatigable disciple did, indeed, never desert her. He might be

[025-177]impatient, he might be restless, but he remained. His 'incurable

[025-178]looseness of thought', for so she termed it, continued at her service to

[025-179]the end. Once, it is true, he had actually ventured to take a holiday;

[025-180]but he was recalled, and he did not repeat the experiment. He was wanted

[025-181]downstairs. There he sat, transacting business answering correspondence,

[025-182]interviewing callers, and exchanging innumerable notes with the unseen

[025-183]power above. Sometimes word came down that Miss Nightingale was just

[025-184]well enough to see one of her visitors. The fortunate man was led up,

[025-185]was ushered, trembling, into the shaded chamber, and, of course, could

[025-186]never afterwards forget the interview. Very rarely, indeed, once or

[025-187]twice a year, perhaps, but nobody could be quite certain, in deadly

[025-188]secrecy, Miss Nightingale went out for a drive in the Park.

[025-189]Unrecognised, the living legend flitted for a moment before the common

[025-190]gaze. And the precaution was necessary; for there were times when, at

[025-191]some public function, the rumour of her presence was spread abroad; and

[025-192]ladies, mistaken by the crowd for Miss Nightingale, were followed,

[025-193]pressed upon, vehemently supplicated 'Let me touch your shawl'; 'Let me

[025-194]stroke your arm'; such was the strange adoration in the hearts of the

[025-195]people. That vast reserve of force lay there behind her; she could use

[025-196]it, if she could. But she preferred never to use it. On occasions, she

[025-197]might hint or threaten, she might balance the sword of Damocles over the

[025-198]head of the Bison; she might, by a word, by a glance, remind some

[025-199]refractory Minister, some unpersuadable Viceroy, sitting in audience

[025-200]with her in the little upper room, that she was something more than a

[025-201]mere sick woman, that she had only, so to speak, to go to the window and

[025-202]wave her handkerchief, for ... dreadful things to follow. But that was

[025-203]enough; they understood; the myth was there--obvious, portentous,

[025-204]impalpable; and so it remained to the last.
