[022-001]Amid all these activities, [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]] took up the further task of

[022-002]inspecting the hospitals in the Crimea itself. The labour was extreme,

[022-003]and the conditions of life were almost intolerable. She spent whole days

[022-004]in the saddle, or was driven over those bleak and rocky heights in a

[022-005]baggage cart. Sometimes she stood for hours in the heavily failing snow,

[022-006]and would only reach her hut at dead of night after walking for miles

[022-007]through perilous ravines. Her powers of resistance seemed incredible,

[022-008]but at last they were exhausted. She was attacked by fever, and for a

[022-009]moment came very near to death. Yet she worked on; if she could not

[022-010]move, she could at least write, and write she did until her mind had

[022-011]left her; and after it had left her, in what seemed the delirious trance

[022-012]of death itself, she still wrote. When, after many weeks, she was strong

[022-013]enough to travel, she was implored to return to England, but she utterly

[022-014]refused. She would not go back, she said, before the last of the

[022-015]soldiers had left [[scutari|Scutari]].

[022-016]This happy moment had almost arrived, when suddenly the smouldering

[022-017]hostilities of the medical authorities burst out into a flame. Dr.

[022-018]Hall's labours had been rewarded by a K.C.B--letters which, as Miss

[022-019]Nightingale told Sidney Herbert, she could only suppose to mean 'Knight

[022-020]of the Crimean Burial-Grounds'--and the honour had turned his head. He

[022-021]was Sir John, and he would be thwarted no longer. Disputes had lately

[022-022]arisen between Miss Nightingale and some of the nurses in the Crimean

[022-023]hospitals. The situation had been embittered by rumours of religious

[022-024]dissensions, while the Crimean nurses were Roman Catholics, many of

[022-025]those at Scutari were suspected of a regrettable propensity towards the

[022-026]tenets of Dr. Pusey. Miss Nightingale was by no means disturbed by these

[022-027]sectarian differences, but any suggestion that her supreme authority

[022-028]over all the nurses with the Army was, no doubt, enough to rouse her to

[022-029]fury; and it appeared that Mrs. Bridgeman, the Reverend Mother in the

[022-030]Crimea, had ventured to call that authority in question. Sir John Hall

[022-031]thought that his opportunity had come, and strongly supported Mrs.

[022-032]Bridgeman--or, as Miss Nightingale preferred to call her, the 'Reverend

[022-033]Brickbat'.

[022-034]There was a violent struggle; Miss Nightingale's rage was terrible. Dr.

[022-035]Hall, she declared, was doing his best to 'root her out of the Crimea'.

[022-036]She would bear it no longer; the [[war-office|War Office]] was playing her false; there

[022-037]was only one thing to be done--Sidney Herbert must move for the

[022-038]production of papers in the House of Commons, so that the public might

[022-039]be able to judge between her and her enemies. Sidney Herbert, with great

[022-040]difficulty, calmed her down. Orders were immediately dispatched putting

[022-041]her supremacy beyond doubt, and the Reverend Brickbat withdrew from the

[022-042]scene. Sir John, however, was more tenacious. A few weeks later, Miss

[022-043]Nightingale and her nurses visited the Crimea for the last time, and the

[022-044]brilliant idea occurred to him that he could crush her by a very simple

[022-045]expedient--he would starve her into submission; and he actually ordered

[022-046]that no rations of any kind should be supplied to her. He had already

[022-047]tried this plan with great effect upon an unfortunate medical man whose

[022-048]presence in the Crimea he had considered an intrusion; but he was now to

[022-049]learn that such tricks were thrown away upon Miss Nightingale. With

[022-050]extraordinary foresight, she had brought with her a great supply of

[022-051]food; she succeeded in obtaining more at her own expense and by her own

[022-052]exertions; and thus for ten days, in that inhospitable country, she was

[022-053]able to feed herself and twenty-four nurses. Eventually, the military

[022-054]authorities intervened in her favour, and Sir John had to confess that

[022-055]he was beaten.

[022-056]It was not until July, 1856--four months after the Declaration of

[022-057]Peace--that Miss Nightingale left Scutari for England. Her reputation

[022-058]was now enormous, and the enthusiasm of the public was unbounded. The

[022-059]royal approbation was expressed by the gift of a brooch, accompanied by

[022-060]a private letter.

[022-061]'You are, I know, well aware,' wrote Her Majesty, 'of the high sense I

[022-062]entertain of the Christian devotion which you have displayed during this

[022-063]great and bloody war, and I need hardly repeat to you how warm my

[022-064]admiration is for your services, which are fully equal to those of my

[022-065]dear and brave soldiers, whose sufferings you have had the privilege of

[022-066]alleviating in so merciful a manner. I am, however, anxious of marking

[022-067]my feelings in a manner which I trust will be agreeable to you, and

[022-068]therefore, send you with this letter a brooch, the form and emblems of

[022-069]which commemorate your great and blessed work, and which I hope you will

[022-070]wear as a mark of the high approbation of your Sovereign!

[022-071]'It will be a very great satisfaction to me,' Her Majesty added, 'to

[022-072]make the acquaintance of one who has set so bright an example to our

[022-073]sex.'

[022-074]The brooch, which was designed by the Prince Consort, bore a St.

[022-075]George's cross in red enamel, and the Royal cipher surmounted by

[022-076]diamonds. The whole was encircled by the inscription 'Blessed are the

[022-077]Merciful'.

[022-078]III

[022-079]THE name of Florence Nightingale lives in the memory of the world by

[022-080]virtue of the lurid and heroic adventure of the Crimea. Had she died--as

[022-081]she nearly did--upon her return to England, her reputation would hardly

[022-082]have been different; her legend would have come down to us almost as we

[022-083]know it today--that gentle vision of female virtue which first took

[022-084]shape before the adoring eyes of the sick soldiers at Scutari. Yet, as a

[022-085]matter of fact, she lived for more than half a century after the Crimean

[022-086]War; and during the greater part of that long period, all the energy and

[022-087]all the devotion of her extraordinary nature were working at their

[022-088]highest pitch. What she accomplished in those years of unknown labour

[022-089]could, indeed, hardly have been more glorious than her Crimean triumphs,

[022-090]but it was certainly more important. The true history was far stranger

[022-091]even than the myth. In Miss Nightingale's own eyes the adventure of the

[022-092]Crimea was a mere incident--scarcely more than a useful stepping-stone

[022-093]in her career. It was the fulcrum with which she hoped to move the

[022-094]world; but it was only the fulcrum. For more than a generation she was

[022-095]to sit in secret, working her lever: and her real "life" began at the

[022-096]very moment when, in the popular imagination, it had ended.

[022-097]She arrived in England in a shattered state of health. The hardships and

[022-098]the ceaseless effort of the last two years had undermined her nervous

[022-099]system; her heart was pronounced to be affected; she suffered constantly

[022-100]from fainting-fits and terrible attacks of utter physical prostration.

[022-101]The doctors declared that one thing alone would save her--a complete and

[022-102]prolonged rest. But that was also the one thing with which she would

[022-103]have nothing to do. She had never been in the habit of resting; why

[022-104]should she begin now? Now, when her opportunity had come at last; now,

[022-105]when the iron was hot, and it was time to strike? No; she had work to

[022-106]do; and, come what might, she would do it. The doctors protested in

[022-107]vain; in vain her family lamented and entreated; in vain her friends

[022-108]pointed out to her the madness of such a course. Madness?

[022-109]Mad--possessed--perhaps she was. A demoniac frenzy had seized upon her.

[022-110]As she lay upon her sofa, gasping, she devoured blue-books, dictated

[022-111]letters, and, in the intervals of her palpitations, cracked her febrile

[022-112]jokes. For months at a stretch she never left her bed. For years she was

[022-113]in daily expectation of death. But she would not rest. At this rate, the

[022-114]doctors assured her, even if she did not die, she would, become an

[022-115]invalid for life. She could not help that; there was the work to be

[022-116]done; and, as for rest, very likely she might rest ... when she had done

[022-117]it.

[022-118]Wherever she went, in London or in the country, in the hills of

[022-119]Derbyshire, or among the rhododendrons at Embley, she was haunted by a

[022-120]ghost. It was the spectre of Scutari--the hideous vision of the

[022-121]organisation of a military hospital. She would lay that phantom, or she

[022-122]would perish. The whole system of the Army Medical Department, the

[022-123]education of the Medical Officer, the regulations of hospital procedure

[022-124]... REST? How could she rest while these things were as they were,

[022-125]while, if the like necessity were to arise again, the like results would

[022-126]follow? And, even in peace and at home, what was the sanitary condition

[022-127]of the Army? The mortality in the barracks was, she found, nearly double

[022-128]the mortality in civil life. 'You might as well take 1,100 men every

[022-129]year out upon Salisbury Plain and shoot them,' she said. After

[022-130]inspecting the hospitals at Chatham, she smiled grimly. 'Yes, this is

[022-131]one more symptom of the system which, in the Crimea, put to death 16,000

[022-132]men.' Scutari had given her knowledge; and it had given her power too:

[022-133]her enormous reputation was at her back--an incalculable force. Other

[022-134]work, other duties, might lie before her; but the most urgent, the most

[022-135]obvious of all, was to look to the health of the Army.

[022-136]One of her very first steps was to take advantage of the invitation

[022-137]which Queen Victoria had sent her to the Crimea, together with the

[022-138]commemorative brooch. Within a few weeks of her return she visited

[022-139]Balmoral, and had several interviews with both the Queen and the Prince,

[022-140]Consort. 'She put before us,' wrote the Prince in his diary, 'all the

[022-141]defects of our present military hospital system, and the reforms that

[022-142]are needed.' She related 'the whole story' of her experiences in the

[022-143]East; and, in addition, she managed to have some long and confidential

[022-144]talks with His Royal Highness on metaphysics and religion. The

[022-145]impression which she created was excellent. 'Sie gefallt uns sehr,'

[022-146]noted the Prince, 'ist sehr bescheiden.' Her Majesty's comment was

[022-147]different--'Such a HEAD! I wish we had her at the War Office.'

[022-148]But Miss Nightingale was not at the War Office, and for a very simple

[022-149]reason: she was a woman. Lord Panmure, however, was (though indeed the

[022-150]reason for that was not quite so simple); and it was upon Lord Panmure

[022-151]that the issue of Miss Nightingale's efforts for reform must primarily

[022-152]depend. That burly Scottish nobleman had not, in spite of his most

[022-153]earnest endeavours, had a very easy time of it as Secretary of State for

[022-154]War. He had come into office in the middle of the Sebastopol Campaign,

[022-155]and had felt himself very well fitted for the position, since he had

[022-156]acquired in former days an inside knowledge of the Army--as a Captain of

[022-157]Hussars. It was this inside knowledge which had enabled him to inform

[022-158]Miss Nightingale with such authority that 'the British soldier is not a

[022-159]remitting animal'. And perhaps it was this same consciousness of a

[022-160]command of his subject which had impelled him to write a dispatch to

[022-161]Lord Raglan, blandly informing the Commander-in-Chief in the Field just

[022-162]how he was neglecting his duties, and pointing out to him that if he

[022-163]would only try he really might do a little better next time.

[022-164]Lord Raglan's reply, calculated as it was to make its recipient sink

[022-165]into the earth, did not quite have that effect upon Lord Panmure, who,

[022-166]whatever might have been his faults, had never been accused of being

[022-167]supersensitive. However, he allowed the matter to drop; and a little

[022-168]later Lord Raglan died--worn out, some people said, by work and anxiety.

[022-169]He was succeeded by an excellent red-nosed old gentleman, General

[022-170]Simpson, whom nobody has ever heard of, and who took Sebastopol. But

[022-171]Lord Panmure's relations with him were hardly more satisfactory than his

[022-172]relations with Lord Raglan; for, while Lord Raglan had been too

[022-173]independent, poor General Simpson erred in the opposite direction,

[022-174]perpetually asked advice, suffered from lumbago, doubted (his nose

[022-175]growing redder and redder daily) whether he was fit for his post, and, by

[022-176]alternate mails, sent in and withdrew his resignation. Then, too, both

[022-177]the General and the Minister suffered acutely from that distressingly

[022-178]useful new invention, the electric telegraph. On one occasion General

[022-179]Simpson felt obliged actually to expostulate. 'I think, my Lord,' he

[022-180]wrote, 'that some telegraphic messages reach us that cannot be sent

[022-181]under due authority, and are perhaps unknown to you, although under the

[022-182]protection of your Lordship's name.
