# British Army

The institution that defeated Napoleon and then spent forty years in peacetime decay, emerging into the Crimean War as "a gigantic machine for the prevention of effective action." Strachey's treatment of the Army runs through all four biographies: Manning's early political ambitions, Nightingale's sanitary crusade, Arnold's moralising about military virtue, and Gordon's entire career.

## The Crimean Debacle

The Army that landed in the Crimea in 1854 had purchased its officers, neglected its medical service, and organised its supply chains on principles of aristocratic patronage. The result was catastrophe: more soldiers died of disease than of wounds. Nightingale's exposure of these failures was the catalyst for administrative reform.

## Gordon's Army

Gordon was the Army's strangest product: a soldier of genius who despised military glory, an imperial agent who treated colonial subjects as equals, a Christian mystic who read the Bible more than his orders. His career — from the Crimea to China to the Sudan — was a series of triumphs that exposed the limitations of the institution that produced him.
