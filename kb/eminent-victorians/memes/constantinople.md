# Constantinople

Constantinople, the imperial capital of the [[ottoman-empire]], occupied a position of singular strategic and symbolic significance in the mid-nineteenth century. Straddling the [[bosphorus]] strait between Europe and Asia, it was the city through which the wounded of the [[crimean-war]] were evacuated, where [[florence-nightingale]] launched her revolution in military nursing, and where [[general-gordon]] received the appointment that would shape his final destiny.

## Location

Constantinople stands at the confluence of the Bosphorus Strait, the Sea of Marmara, and the Golden Horn, controlling the maritime passage between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean (PN 019-174, PN 019-175). During the Crimean War, its suburb [[scutari]] (Uskudar), located on the Asiatic shore of the Bosphorus, became the site of the British military hospitals that received thousands of wounded soldiers shipped across the Black Sea from the Crimean battlefields (PN 019-174, PN 019-183). The European quarter of [[pera]], home to the British embassy, was the seat of diplomatic power -- it was from Pera that Lord Stratford de Redcliffe, the British ambassador, conducted his famously high-handed dealings with both the Ottoman authorities and his own countrymen (PN 020-084, PN 020-087).

## In the Narrative

Constantinople appears at critical junctures in both the Nightingale and Gordon biographies within Strachey's work, serving not merely as backdrop but as a node where personal destiny and imperial history intersect.

### Association with Florence Nightingale

The city first enters the narrative when Florence Nightingale, having assembled her party of thirty-eight nurses within a week of Sidney Herbert's commission, departed England "amid a great burst of popular enthusiasm" for Constantinople (PN 019-125). She arrived at Scutari, its Asiatic suburb, on November 4th, 1854 -- ten days after the battle of Balaclava and the day before Inkerman -- to confront the administrative catastrophe of the British military hospitals (PN 019-174, PN 019-175, PN 019-176). As one of her party exclaimed upon approaching the city, "Oh, dear Miss Nightingale, when we land, let there be no delays, let us get straight to nursing the poor fellows!" -- to which Nightingale famously replied, "The strongest will be wanted at the wash-tub" (PN 021-014). Her subsequent battles with Ottoman bureaucracy were epitomised by the discovery that "vast quantities of valuable stores sent from England lay engulfed in the bottomless abyss of the Turkish Customs House" (PN 020-084).

> "Miss Nightingale arrived at Scutari -- a suburb of Constantinople, on the Asiatic side of the Bosphorus -- on November 4th, 1854; it was ten days after the battle of Balaclava, and the day before the battle of Inkerman." (PN 019-174, PN 019-175, PN 019-176)

### Association with General Gordon

Years after the Crimean War, Constantinople became the hinge-point of Charles George Gordon's career. Sent to represent Great Britain on an international Danube navigation commission, Gordon stopped at Constantinople in the early 1870s, where he "chanced to meet the Egyptian minister, Nubar Pasha" (PN 034-156). Nubar offered Gordon the Governorship of the Equatorial Provinces of the Sudan, an appointment that launched Gordon into the six years of "extraordinary, desperate, unceasing, and ungrateful labour" that would define the remainder of his life and ultimately lead to his death at Khartoum (PN 034-157, PN 034-158, PN 034-166). The city also appears later in Gordon's story as one terminus of the slave trade he fought to suppress -- Strachey notes that "the root of the malady lay in the slave-markets of Cairo and Constantinople: the supply followed the demand" (PN 035-127).

> "At Constantinople, he chanced to meet the Egyptian minister, Nubar Pasha. The Governorship of the Equatorial Provinces of the Sudan was about to fall vacant; and Nubar offered the post to Gordon, who accepted it." (PN 034-156, PN 034-157, PN 034-158)

## Strategic Significance

Constantinople's geopolitical importance during the Victorian era was immense. As the capital of the Ottoman Empire -- the "Sick Man of Europe" -- it was the focal point of the Eastern Question that dominated British foreign policy throughout the period. The Crimean War itself was fought in large part to prevent Russian expansion toward the Straits, and the logistical chain that sustained the British war effort ran through Constantinople and its port facilities (PN 019-099, PN 019-125). Strachey captures the city's dual character -- simultaneously a seat of Oriental intrigue and the essential staging-ground for Britain's imperial military operations -- through his treatment of the incompetent Turkish customs system and the labyrinthine diplomatic world of Pera (PN 020-084, PN 020-087).
