[009-001]For the moment, however, it seemed as if the Fates had at last been

[009-002]successful in their little game of shunting Manning. The splendid career

[009-003]which he had so laboriously built up from the small beginnings of his

[009-004]Sussex curacy was shattered--and shattered by the inevitable operation

[009-005]of his own essential needs. He was over forty, and he had been put back

[009-006]once more to the very bottom rung of the ladder--a middle-aged neophyte

[009-007]with, so far as could be seen, no special claim to the attention of his

[009-008]new superiors. The example of [[john-henry-newman|Newman]], a far more illustrious convert,

[009-009]was hardly reassuring: he had been relegated to a complete obscurity, in

[009-010]which he was to remain until extreme old age. Why should there be

[009-011]anything better in store for Manning? Yet it so happened that within

[009-012]fourteen years of his conversion Manning was Archbishop of Westminster

[009-013]and the supreme ruler of the Roman Catholic community in England. This

[009-014]time the Fates gave up the unequal struggle; they paid over their stakes

[009-015]in despair, and retired from the game.

[009-016]Nevertheless it is difficult to feel quite sure that Manning's plunge

[009-017]was as hazardous as it appeared. Certainly he was not a man who was

[009-018]likely to forget to look before he leaped, nor one who, if he happened

[009-019]to know that there was a mattress spread to receive him, would leap with

[009-020]less conviction. In the light of after-events, one would be glad to know

[009-021]what precisely passed at that mysterious interview of his with the Pope,

[009-022]three years before his conversion. It is at least possible that the

[009-023]authorities in [[rome|Rome]] had their eye on Manning; the may well have felt

[009-024]that the Archdeacon of Chichester would be a great catch. What did Pio

[009-025]Nono say? It is easy to imagine the persuasive innocence of his Italian

[009-026]voice. 'Ah, dear Signor Manning, why don't you come over to us? Do you

[009-027]suppose that we should not look after you?'

[009-028]At any rate, when he did go over, Manning was looked after very

[009-029]thoroughly. There was, it is true, a momentary embarrassment at the

[009-030]outset: it was only with the greatest difficulty that he could bring

[009-031]himself to abandon his faith in the validity of Anglican Orders, in

[009-032]which he believed 'with consciousness stronger than all reasoning'. He

[009-033]was convinced that he was still a priest. When the Rev. Mr. Tierney, who

[009-034]had received him into the Roman Catholic communion, assured him that

[009-035]this was not the case, he was filled with dismay and mortification.

[009-036]After a five hour discussion, he started to his feet in a rage. 'Then,

[009-037]Mr. Tierney,' he exclaimed, 'you think me insincere.'

[009-038]The bitter draught was swallowed at last, and, after that, all went

[009-039]smoothly. Manning hastened to Rome, and was immediately placed by the

[009-040]Pope in the highly select Accademia Ecclesiastica, commonly known as the

[009-041]'Nursery of Cardinals', for the purpose of completing his theological

[009-042]studies. When the course was finished, he continued, by the Pope's

[009-043]special request, to spend six months of every year in Rome, where he

[009-044]preached to the English visitors, became acquainted with the great

[009-045]personages of the Papal court, and enjoyed the privilege of constant

[009-046]interviews with the Holy Father. At the same time, he was able to make

[009-047]himself useful in London, where Cardinal Wiseman, the newly created

[009-048]Archbishop of Westminster, was seeking to reanimate the Roman Catholic

[009-049]community. Manning was not only extremely popular in the pulpit and in

[009-050]the confessional; he was not only highly efficient as a gleaner of

[009-051]souls--and of souls who moved in the best society; he also possessed a

[009-052]familiarity with official persons and official ways, which was

[009-053]invaluable. When the question arose of the appointment of Catholic

[009-054]chaplains in the Crimea during the war, it was Manning who approached

[009-055]the Minister, interviewed the Permanent Secretary, and finally succeeded

[009-056]in obtaining all that was required. When a special Reformatory for

[009-057]Catholic children was proposed, Manning carried through the negotiation

[009-058]with the Government. When an attempt was made to remove Catholic

[009-059]children from the Workhouses, Manning was again indispensable. No wonder

[009-060]Cardinal Wiseman soon determined to find some occupation of special

[009-061]importance for the energetic convert. He had long wished to establish a

[009-062]congregation of secular priests in London particularly devoted to his

[009-063]service, and the opportunity for the experiment had clearly now arisen.

[009-064]The order of the Oblates of St. Charles was founded in Bayswater, and

[009-065]Manning was put at its head. Unfortunately, no portion of the body of

[009-066]St. Charles could be obtained for the new community, but two relics of

[009-067]his blood were brought over to Bayswater from Milan. Almost at the same

[009-068]time the Pope signified his appreciation of Manning's efforts by

[009-069]appointing him Provost of the Chapter of Westminster--a position which

[009-070]placed him at the head of the Canons of the diocese.

[009-071]This double promotion was the signal for the outbreak of an

[009-072]extraordinary internal struggle, which raged without intermission for

[009-073]the next seven years, and was to end only with the accession of Manning

[009-074]to the Archbishopric. The condition of the Roman Catholic community in

[009-075]England was at that time a singular one. On the one hand the old

[009-076]repressive laws of the seventeenth century had been repealed by liberal

[009-077]legislation, and on the other a large new body of distinguished converts

[009-078]had entered the [[catholic-church|Roman Church]] as a result of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]. It was

[009-079]evident that there was a 'boom' in English Catholicism, and, in 1850,

[009-080]Pius IX recognised the fact by dividing up the whole of England into

[009-081]dioceses, and placing Wiseman at the head of them as Archbishop of

[009-082]Westminster. Wiseman's encyclical, dated 'from without the Flaminian

[009-083]Gate', in which he announced the new departure, was greeted in England

[009-084]by a storm of indignation, culminating in the famous and furibund letter

[009-085]of Lord John Russell, then Prime Minister, against the insolence of the

[009-086]'Papal Aggression'. Though the particular point against which the outcry

[009-087]was raised--the English territorial titles of the new Roman bishops--was

[009-088]an insignificant one, the instinct of Lord John and of the English

[009-089]people was in reality sound enough. Wiseman's installation did mean, in

[009-090]fact, a new move in the Papal game; it meant an advance, if not an

[009-091]aggression--a quickening in England of the long-dormant energies of the

[009-092]Roman Church. That Church has never had the reputation of being an

[009-093]institution to be trifled with; and, in those days, the Pope was still

[009-094]ruling as a temporal Prince over the fairest provinces of Italy. Surely,

[009-095]if the images of Guy Fawkes had not been garnished, on that fifth of

[009-096]November, with triple crowns, it would have been a very poor compliment

[009-097]to His Holiness.

[009-098]But it was not only the honest Protestants of England who had cause to

[009-099]dread the arrival of the new Cardinal Archbishop; there was a party

[009-100]among the Catholics themselves who viewed his installation with alarm

[009-101]and disgust. The families in which the Catholic tradition had been

[009-102]handed down uninterruptedly since the days of Elizabeth, which had known

[009-103]the pains of exile and of martyrdom, and which clung together an alien

[009-104]and isolated group in the midst of English society, now began to feel

[009-105]that they were, after all, of small moment in the counsels of Rome. They

[009-106]had laboured through the heat of the day, but now it seemed as if the

[009-107]harvest was to be gathered in by a crowd of converts who were

[009-108]proclaiming on every side as something new and wonderful the truths

[009-109]which the Old Catholics, as they came to be called, had not only known,

[009-110]but for which they had suffered for generations. Cardinal Wiseman, it is

[009-111]true, was no convert; he belonged to one of the oldest of the Catholic

[009-112]families; but he had spent most of his life in Rome, he was out of touch

[009-113]with English traditions, and his sympathy with Newman and his followers

[009-114]was only too apparent. One of his first acts as Archbishop was to

[009-115]appoint the convert W. G. Ward, who was not even in holy orders, to be

[009-116]Professor of Theology at St. Edmund's College--the chief seminary for

[009-117]young priests, in which the ancient traditions of Douay were still

[009-118]flourishing. Ward was an ardent Papalist and his appointment indicated

[009-119]clearly enough that in Wiseman's opinion there was too little of the

[009-120]Italian spirit in the English community. The uneasiness of the Old

[009-121]Catholics was becoming intense, when they were reassured by Wiseman's

[009-122]appointing as his co-adjutor and successor his intimate friend, Dr.

[009-123]Errington, who was created on the occasion Archbishop of Trebizond in

[009-124]partibus infidelium. Not only was Dr. Errington an Old Catholic of the

[009-125]most rigid type, he was a man of extreme energy, whose influence was

[009-126]certain to be great; and, in any case, Wiseman was growing old, so that

[009-127]before very long it seemed inevitable that the policy of the diocese

[009-128]would be in proper hands. Such was the position of affairs when, two

[009-129]years after Errington's appointment, Manning became head of the Oblates

[009-130]of St. Charles and Provost of the Chapter of Westminster.

[009-131]The Archbishop of Trebizond had been for some time growing more and more

[009-132]suspicious of Manning's influence, and this sudden elevation appeared to

[009-133]justify his worst fears. But his alarm was turned to fury when he

[009-134]learned that St. Edmund's College, from which he had just succeeded in

[009-135]removing the obnoxious W. G. Ward, was to be placed under the control of

[009-136]the Oblates of St. Charles. The Oblates did not attempt to conceal the

[009-137]fact that one of their principal aims was to introduce the customs of a

[009-138]Roman Seminary into England. A grim perspective of espionage and

[009-139]tale-bearing, foreign habits, and Italian devotions opened out before

[009-140]the dismayed eyes of the Old Catholics; they determined to resist to the

[009-141]utmost; and it was upon the question of the control of St. Edmund's that

[009-142]the first battle in the long campaign between Errington and Manning was

[009-143]fought.

[009-144]Cardinal Wiseman was now obviously declining towards the grave. A man of

[009-145]vast physique--'your immense', an Irish servant used respectfully to

[009-146]call him--of sanguine temperament, of genial disposition, of versatile

[009-147]capacity, he seemed to have engrafted upon the robustness of his English

[009-148]nature the facile, child-like, and expansive qualities of the South. So

[009-149]far from being a Bishop Blougram (as the rumour went) he was, in fact,

[009-150]the very antithesis of that subtle and worldly-wise ecclesiastic. He had

[009-151]innocently looked forward all his life to the reunion of England to the

[009-152]See of Peter, and eventually had come to believe that, in God's hand, he

[009-153]was the instrument destined to bring about this miraculous consummation.

[009-154]Was not the [[oxford-university|Oxford]] Movement, with its flood of converts, a clear sign of

[009-155]the Divine will? Had he not himself been the author of that momentous

[009-156]article on St. Augustine and the Donatists, which had finally convinced

[009-157]Newman that the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] was in schism? And then, had he not

[009-158]been able to set afoot a Crusade of Prayer throughout Catholic Europe

[009-159]for the conversion of England?

[009-160]He awaited the result with eager expectation, and in the meantime he set

[009-161]himself to smooth away the hostility of his countrymen by delivering

[009-162]courses of popular lectures on literature and archaeology. He devoted

[009-163]much time and attention to the ceremonial details of his princely

[009-164]office. His knowledge of rubric and ritual, and of the symbolical

[009-165]significations of vestments, has rarely been equalled, and he took a

[009-166]profound delight in the ordering and the performance of elaborate

[009-167]processions. During one of these functions, an unexpected difficulty

[009-168]arose: the Master of Ceremonies suddenly gave the word for a halt, and,

[009-169]on being asked the reason, replied that he had been instructed that

[009-170]moment by special revelation to stop the procession. The Cardinal,

[009-171]however, was not at a loss. 'You may let the procession go on,' he

[009-172]smilingly replied. 'I have just obtained permission, by special

[009-173]revelation, to proceed with it.' His leisure hours he spent in the

[009-174]writing of edifying novels, the composition of acrostics in Latin Verse,

[009-175]and in playing battledore and shuttlecock with his little nieces. There

[009-176]was, indeed, only one point in which he resembled Bishop Blougram--his

[009-177]love of a good table. Some of Newman's disciples were astonished and

[009-178]grieved to find that he sat down to four courses of fish during Lent. 'I

[009-179]am sorry to say,' remarked one of them afterwards, 'that there is a

[009-180]lobster salad side to the Cardinal.'
