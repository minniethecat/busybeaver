[043-001]But Sir Evelyn Baring was inaudible, and Gordon now cared very little

[043-002]for his opinions. Is it possible that, if only for a moment, in his

[043-003]extraordinary predicament, he may have listened to another and a very

[043-004]different voice--a voice of singular quality, a voice which--for so one

[043-005]would fain imagine--may well have wakened some familiar echoes in his

[043-006]heart? One day, he received a private letter from the Mahdi. The letter

[043-007]was accompanied by a small bundle of clothes.

[043-008]'In the name of God!' wrote the Mahdi, 'herewith a suit of clothes,

[043-009]consisting of a coat (jibbeh), an overcoat, a turban, a cap, a girdle,

[043-010]and beads. This is the clothing of those who have given up this world

[043-011]and its vanities, and who look for the world to come, for everlasting

[043-012]happiness in Paradise. If you truly desire to come to God and seek to

[043-013]live a godly life, you must at once wear this suit, and come out to

[043-014]accept your everlasting good fortune.'

[043-015]Did the words bear no meaning to the mystic of Gravesend? But he was an

[043-016]English gentleman, an English officer. He flung the clothes to the

[043-017]ground, and trampled on them in the sight of all. Then, alone, he went

[043-018]up to the roof of his high palace, and turned the telescope once more,

[043-019]almost mechanically, towards the north.

[043-020]But nothing broke the immovability of that hard horizon; and, indeed,

[043-021]how was it possible that help should come to him now? He seemed to be

[043-022]utterly abandoned. Sir Evelyn Baring had disappeared into his financial

[043-023]conference. In England, [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] had held firm, had outfaced the

[043-024]House of Commons, had ignored the Press. He appeared to have triumphed.

[043-025]Though it was clear that no preparations of any kind were being made for

[043-026]the relief of Gordon, the anxiety and agitation of the public, which had

[043-027]risen so suddenly to such a height of vehemence, had died down. The

[043-028]dangerous beast had been quelled by the stern eye of its master. Other

[043-029]questions became more interesting--the Reform Bill, the Russians, the

[043-030]House of Lords. Gordon, silent in [[khartoum|Khartoum]], had almost dropped out of

[043-031]remembrance. And yet, help did come after all. And it came from an

[043-032]unexpected quarter. Lord Hartington had been for some time convinced

[043-033]that he was responsible for Gordon's appointment; and his conscience was

[043-034]beginning to grow uncomfortable.

[043-035]Lord Hartington's conscience was of a piece with the rest of him. It was

[043-036]not, like Mr. Gladstone's, a salamander-conscience--an intangible,

[043-037]dangerous creature, that loved to live in the fire; nor was it, like

[043-038]Gordon's, a restless conscience; nor, like Sir Evelyn Baring's, a

[043-039]diplomatic conscience; it was a commonplace affair. Lord Hartington

[043-040]himself would have been disgusted by any mention of it. If he had been

[043-041]obliged, he would have alluded to it distantly; he would have muttered

[043-042]that it was a bore not to do the proper thing. He was usually bored--for

[043-043]one reason or another; but this particular form of boredom he found more

[043-044]intense than all the rest. He would take endless pains to avoid it. Of

[043-045]course, the whole thing was a nuisance--an obvious nuisance; and

[043-046]everyone else must feel just as he did about it. And yet people seemed

[043-047]to have got it into their heads that he had some kind of special faculty

[043-048]in such matters--that there was some peculiar value in his judgment on a

[043-049]question of right and wrong. He could not understand why it was; but

[043-050]whenever there was a dispute about cards in a club, it was brought to

[043-051]him to settle. It was most odd. But it was trite. In public affairs, no

[043-052]less than in private, Lord Hartington's decisions carried an

[043-053]extraordinary weight. The feeling of his idle friends in high society

[043-054]was shared by the great mass of the English people; here was a man they

[043-055]could trust. For indeed he was built upon a pattern which was very dear

[043-056]to his countrymen. It was not simply that he was honest: it was that his

[043-057]honesty was an English honesty--an honest which naturally belonged to

[043-058]one who, so it seemed to them, was the living image of what an

[043-059]Englishman should be.

[043-060]In Lord Hartington they saw, embodied and glorified, the very qualities

[043-061]which were nearest to their hearts--impartiality, solidity, common

[043-062]sense--the qualities by which they themselves longed to be

[043-063]distinguished, and by which, in their happier moments, they believed

[043-064]they were. If ever they began to have misgivings, there, at any rate,

[043-065]was the example of Lord Hartington to encourage them and guide

[043-066]them--Lord Hartington who was never self-seeking, who was never excited,

[043-067]and who had no imagination at all. Everything they knew about him fitted

[043-068]into the picture, adding to their admiration and respect. His fondness

[043-069]for field sports gave them a feeling of security; and certainly there

[043-070]could be no nonsense about a man who confessed to two ambitions--to

[043-071]become Prime Minister and to win the Derby--and who put the second above

[043-072]the first. They loved him for his casualness--for his inexactness--for

[043-073]refusing to make life a cut-and-dried business--for ramming an official

[043-074]dispatch of high importance into his coat-pocket, and finding it there,

[043-075]still unopened, at Newmarket, several days later. They loved him for his

[043-076]hatred of fine sentiments; they were delighted when they heard that at

[043-077]some function, on a florid speaker's avowing that 'this was the proudest

[043-078]moment of his life', Lord Hartington had growled in an undertone 'the

[043-079]proudest moment of my life was when MY pig won the prize at Skipton

[043-080]Fair'. Above all, they loved him for being dull. It was the greatest

[043-081]comfort--with Lord Hartington they could always be absolutely certain

[043-082]that he would never, in any circumstances, be either brilliant, or

[043-083]subtle, or surprising, or impassioned, or profound. As they sat,

[043-084]listening to his speeches, in which considerations of stolid plainness

[043-085]succeeded one another with complete flatness, they felt, involved and

[043-086]supported by the colossal tedium, that their confidence was finally

[043-087]assured. They looked up, and took their fill of the sturdy, obvious

[043-088]presence. The inheritor of a splendid dukedom might almost have passed

[043-089]for a farm hand. Almost, but not quite. For an air that was difficult to

[043-090]explain, of preponderating authority, lurked in the solid figure; and

[043-091]the lordly breeding of the House of Cavendish was visible in the large,

[043-092]long, bearded, unimpressionable face.

[043-093]One other characteristic--the necessary consequence, or, indeed, it

[043-094]might almost be said, the essential expression, of all the

[043-095]rest--completes the portrait: Lord Hartington was slow. He was slow in

[043-096]movement, slow in apprehension, slow in thought and the communication of

[043-097]thought, slow to decide, and slow to act. More than once this

[043-098]disposition exercised a profound effect upon his career. A private

[043-099]individual may, perhaps, be slow with impunity; but a statesman who is

[043-100]slow--whatever the force of his character and the strength of his

[043-101]judgment--can hardly escape unhurt from the hurrying of Time's winged

[043-102]chariot, can hardly hope to avoid some grave disaster or some

[043-103]irretrievable mistake. The fate of [[general-gordon|General Gordon]], so intricately

[043-104]interwoven with such a mass of complicated circumstance with the

[043-105]policies of England and of Egypt, with the fanaticism of the Mahdi, with

[043-106]the irreproachability of Sir Evelyn Baring, with Mr. Gladstone's

[043-107]mysterious passions--was finally determined by the fact that Lord

[043-108]Hartington was slow. If he had been even a very little quicker--if he

[043-109]had been quicker by two days ... but it could not be. The ponderous

[043-110]machinery took so long to set itself in motion; the great wheels and

[043-111]levers, once started, revolved with such a laborious, such a painful

[043-112]deliberation, that at last their work was accomplished--surely, firmly,

[043-113]completely, in the best English manner, and too late.

[043-114]Seven stages may be discerned in the history of Lord Hartington's

[043-115]influence upon the fate of General Gordon. At the end of the first

[043-116]stage, he had become convinced that he was responsible for Gordon's

[043-117]appointment to Khartoum. At the end of the second, he had perceived that

[043-118]his conscience would not allow him to remain inactive in the face of

[043-119]Gordon's danger. At the end of the third, he had made an attempt to

[043-120]induce the Cabinet to send an expedition to Gordon's relief. At the end

[043-121]of the fourth, he had realised that the Cabinet had decided to postpone

[043-122]the relief of Gordon indefinitely. At the end of the fifth, he had come

[043-123]to the conclusion that he must put pressure upon Mr. Gladstone. At the

[043-124]end of the sixth, he had attempted to put pressure upon Mr. Gladstone,

[043-125]and had not succeeded. At the end of the seventh, he had succeeded in

[043-126]putting pressure upon Mr. Gladstone; the relief expedition had been

[043-127]ordered; he could do no more.

[043-128]The turning-point in this long and extraordinary process occurred

[043-129]towards the end of April, when the Cabinet, after the receipt of Sir

[043-130]Evelyn Baring's final dispatch, decided to take no immediate measures

[043-131]for Gordon's relief. From that moment it was clear that there was only

[043-132]one course open to Lord Hartington--to tell Mr. Gladstone that he would

[043-133]resign unless a relief expedition was sent. But it took him more than

[043-134]three months to come to this conclusion. He always found the proceedings

[043-135]at Cabinet meetings particularly hard to follow. The interchange of

[043-136]question and answer, of proposal and counterproposal, the crowded

[043-137]counsellors, Mr. Gladstone's subtleties, the abrupt and complicated

[043-138]resolutions--these things invariably left him confused and perplexed.

[043-139]After the crucial Cabinet at the end of April, he came away in a state

[043-140]of uncertainty as to what had occurred; he had to write to Lord

[043-141]Granville to find out; and by that time, of course, the Government's

[043-142]decision had been telegraphed to Egypt. Three weeks later, in the middle

[043-143]of May, he had grown so uneasy that he felt himself obliged to address a

[043-144]circular letter to the Cabinet proposing that preparations for a relief

[043-145]expedition should be set on foot at once. And then he began to

[043-146]understand that nothing would ever be done until Mr. Gladstone, by some

[043-147]means or other, had been forced to give his consent. A singular combat

[043-148]followed. The slippery old man perpetually eluded the cumbrous grasp of

[043-149]his antagonist. He delayed, he postponed, he raised interminable

[043-150]difficulties, he prevaricated, he was silent, he disappeared. Lord

[043-151]Hartington was dauntless. Gradually, inch by inch, he drove the Prime

[043-152]Minister into a corner. But in the meantime many weeks had passed. On

[043-153]July 1st, Lord Hartington was still remarking that he 'really did not

[043-154]feel that he knew the mind or intention of the Government in respect of

[043-155]the relief of General Gordon'. The month was spent in a succession of

[043-156]stubborn efforts to wring from Mr. Gladstone some definite statement

[043-157]upon the question. It was useless. On July 31st, Lord Hartington did the

[043-158]deed. He stated that, unless an expedition was sent, he would resign. It

[043-159]was, he said, 'a question of personal honour and good faith, and I don't

[043-160]see how I can yield upon it'. His conscience had worked itself to rest

[043-161]at last.

[043-162]When Mr. Gladstone read the words, he realised that the game was over.

[043-163]Lord Hartington's position in the Liberal Party was second only to his

[043-164]own; he was the leader of the rich and powerful Whig aristocracy; his

[043-165]influence with the country was immense. Nor was he the man to make idle

[043-166]threats of resignation; he had said he would resign, and resign he

[043-167]would: the collapse of the Government would be the inevitable result. On

[043-168]August 5th, therefore, Parliament was asked to make a grant of L300,000,

[043-169]in order 'to enable Her Majesty's Government to undertake operations for

[043-170]the relief of General Gordon, should they become necessary'. The money

[043-171]was voted; and even then, at that last hour, Mr. Gladstone made another,

[043-172]final, desperate twist. Trying to save himself by the proviso which he

[043-173]had inserted into the resolution, he declared that he was still

[043-174]unconvinced of the necessity of any operations at all. 'I nearly,' he

[043-175]wrote to Lord Hartington, 'but not quite, adopt words received today

[043-176]from Granville. "It is clear, I think, that Gordon has our messages, and

[043-177]does not choose to answer them."' Nearly, but not quite! The

[043-178]qualification was masterly; but it was of no avail. This time, the

[043-179]sinuous creature was held by too firm a grasp. On August 26th, Lord

[043-180]Wolseley was appointed to command the relief expedition; and on

[043-181]September 9th, he arrived in Egypt.
