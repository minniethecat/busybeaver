# Siege of Khartoum

The siege that killed Gordon and haunted the British Empire. For nearly a year, Gordon held Khartoum against the Mahdi's forces while a relief expedition crawled up the Nile — arriving, famously, two days too late. Strachey's treatment of the siege is less military history than psychological drama: Gordon waiting for a death he half-willed, the British government dithering, and the nation discovering too late what it had asked of its hero.

## Gordon's Defence

Gordon fortified Khartoum with characteristic energy, organising defences, rationing supplies, and maintaining morale through personal example and religious exhortation. His journals from the siege — mystical, practical, despairing, exalted by turns — are the primary source for Strachey's portrait of a man who had found his destiny in annihilation.

## The Relief Expedition

Lord Wolseley's expedition, dispatched only after public opinion forced Gladstone's hand, faced logistical obstacles that made its progress agonisingly slow. The advance party reached Khartoum on January 28, 1885, to find the city in Mahdist hands and Gordon dead — killed, according to legend, on the steps of the governor's palace.

## Aftermath

Gordon's death provoked a political crisis in Britain. Gladstone, who had opposed the Sudan adventure from the start, was blamed for the delay; the phrase "too late" became a weapon against his government. Gordon became a martyr, his legend eclipsing the complex and contradictory figure Strachey set out to recover.
