[035-001]The function ended in a prolonged banquet, followed by a mixed ballet of

[035-002]soldiers and completely naked young women, who danced in a circle, beat

[035-003]time with their feet, and accompanied their gestures with a curious

[035-004]sound of clucking. At last the Austrian Consul, overcome by the

[035-005]exhilaration of the scene, flung himself in a frenzy among the dancers;

[035-006]the Governor-General, shouting with delight, seemed about to follow

[035-007]suit, when Gordon abruptly left the room, and the party broke up in

[035-008]confusion.

[035-009]When, 1,500 miles to the southward, Gordon reached the seat of his

[035-010]government, and the desolation of the Tropics closed over him, the

[035-011]agonising nature of his task stood fully revealed. For the next three

[035-012]years he struggled with enormous difficulties--with the confused and

[035-013]horrible country, the appalling climate, the maddening insects and the

[035-014]loathsome diseases, the indifference of subordinates and superiors, the

[035-015]savagery of the slave-traders, and the hatred of the inhabitants. One by

[035-016]one the small company of his European staff succumbed. With a few

[035-017]hundred Egyptian soldiers he had to suppress insurrections, make roads,

[035-018]establish fortified posts, and enforce the government monopoly of ivory.

[035-019]All this he accomplished; he even succeeded in sending enough money to

[035-020]Cairo to pay for the expenses of the expedition. But a deep gloom had

[035-021]fallen upon his spirit. When, after a series of incredible obstacles had

[035-022]been overcome, a steamer was launched upon the unexplored Albert Nyanza,

[035-023]he turned his back upon the lake, leaving the glory of its navigation to

[035-024]his Italian lieutenant, Gessi. 'I wish,' he wrote, 'to give a practical

[035-025]proof of what I think regarding the inordinate praise which is given to

[035-026]an explorer.' Among his distresses and self-mortifications, he loathed

[035-027]the thought of all such honours, and remembered the attentions of

[035-028]English society with a snarl.

[035-029]'When, D.V., I get home, I do not dine out. My reminiscences of these

[035-030]lands will not be more pleasant to me than the China ones. What I shall

[035-031]have done, will be what I have done. Men think giving dinners is

[035-032]conferring a favour on you ... Why not give dinners to those who need

[035-033]them?'

[035-034]No! His heart was set upon a very different object.

[035-035]'To each is allotted a distinct work, to each a destined goal; to some

[035-036]the seat at the right hand or left hand of the Saviour. (It was not His

[035-037]to give; it was already given--Matthew xx, 23. Again, Judas went to "HIS

[035-038]OWN PLACE"--Acts i, 25.) It is difficult for the flesh to accept: "Ye

[035-039]are dead, ye have naught to do with the world". How difficult for anyone

[035-040]to be circumcised from the world, to be as indifferent to its pleasures,

[035-041]its sorrows, and its comforts as a corpse is! That is to know the

[035-042]resurrection.'

[035-043]But the Holy Bible was not his only solace. For now, under the parching

[035-044]African sun, we catch glimpses, for the first time, of Gordon's hand

[035-045]stretching out towards stimulants of a more material quality. For months

[035-046]together, we are told, he would drink nothing but pure water; and then

[035-047]... water that was not so pure. In his fits of melancholy, he would shut

[035-048]himself up in his tent for days at a time, with a hatchet and a flag

[035-049]placed at the door to indicate that he was not to be disturbed for any

[035-050]reason whatever; until at last the cloud would lift, the signals would

[035-051]be removed, and the Governor would reappear, brisk and cheerful.

[035-052]During, one of these retirements, there was grave danger of a native

[035-053]attack upon the camp. Colonel Long, the Chief of Staff, ventured, after

[035-054]some hesitation, to ignore the flag and hatchet, and to enter the

[035-055]forbidden tent. He found Gordon seated at a table, upon which were an

[035-056]open Bible and an open bottle of brandy. Long explained the

[035-057]circumstances, but could obtain no answer beyond the abrupt words--'You

[035-058]are commander of the camp'--and was obliged to retire, nonplussed, to

[035-059]deal with the situation as best he could. On the following morning,

[035-060]Gordon, cleanly shaven, and in the full-dress uniform of the Royal

[035-061]Engineers, entered Long's hut with his usual tripping step, exclaiming

[035-062]'Old fellow, now don't be angry with me. I was very low last night.

[035-063]Let's have a good breakfast--a little b. and s. Do you feel up to it?'

[035-064]And, with these veering moods and dangerous restoratives, there came an

[035-065]intensification of the queer and violent elements in the temper of the

[035-066]man.

[035-067]His eccentricities grew upon him. He found it more and more

[035-068]uncomfortable to follow the ordinary course. Official routine was an

[035-069]agony to him. His caustic and satirical humour expressed itself in a

[035-070]style that astounded government departments. While he jibed at his

[035-071]superiors, his subordinates learned to dread the explosions of his

[035-072]wrath. There were moments when his passion became utterly ungovernable;

[035-073]and the gentle soldier of God, who had spent the day in quoting texts

[035-074]for the edification of his sister, would slap the face of his Arab

[035-075]aide-de-camp in a sudden access of fury, or set upon his Alsatian

[035-076]servant and kick him until he screamed.

[035-077]At the end of three years, Gordon resigned his post in Equatoria, and

[035-078]prepared to return home. But again Providence intervened: the Khedive

[035-079]offered him, as an inducement to remain in the Egyptian service, a

[035-080]position of still higher consequence--the Governor-Generalship of the

[035-081]whole Sudan; and Gordon once more took up his task. Another three years

[035-082]were passed in grappling with vast revolting provinces, with the

[035-083]ineradicable iniquities of the slave-trade, and with all the

[035-084]complications of weakness and corruption incident to an oriental

[035-085]administration extending over almost boundless tracts of savage

[035-086]territory which had never been effectively subdued. His headquarters

[035-087]were fixed in the palace at [[khartoum|Khartoum]]; but there were various interludes

[035-088]in his government. Once, when the Khedive's finances had become

[035-089]peculiarly embroiled, he summoned Gordon to Cairo to preside over a

[035-090]commission which should set matters to rights. Gordon accepted the post,

[035-091]but soon found that his situation was untenable. He was between the

[035-092]devil and the deep sea--between the unscrupulous cunning of the Egyptian

[035-093]Pashas, and the immeasurable immensity of the Khedive's debts to his

[035-094]European creditors. The Pashas were anxious to use him as a respectable

[035-095]mask for their own nefarious dealings; and the representatives of the

[035-096]European creditors, who looked upon him as an irresponsible intruder,

[035-097]were anxious simply to get rid of him as soon as they could. One of

[035-098]these representatives was Sir Evelyn Baring, whom Gordon now met for the

[035-099]first time. An immediate antagonism flashed out between the two men. But

[035-100]their hostility had no time to mature; for Gordon, baffled on all sides,

[035-101]and deserted even by the Khedive, precipitately returned to his

[035-102]Governor-Generalship. Whatever else Providence might have decreed, it

[035-103]had certainly not decided that he should be a financier.

[035-104]His tastes and his talents were indeed of a very different kind. In his

[035-105]absence, a rebellion had broken out in Darfur--one of the vast outlying

[035-106]provinces of his government--where a native chieftain, Zobeir, had

[035-107]erected, on a basis of slave-traffic, a dangerous military power. Zobeir

[035-108]himself had been lured to Cairo, where he was detained in a state of

[035-109]semi-captivity; but his son, Suleiman, ruled in his stead, and was now

[035-110]defying the Governor-General. Gordon determined upon a hazardous stroke.

[035-111]He mounted a camel, and rode, alone, in the blazing heat, across

[035-112]eighty-five miles of desert, to Suleiman's camp. His sudden apparition

[035-113]dumbfounded the rebels; his imperious bearing overawed them; he

[035-114]signified to them that in two days they must disarm and disperse; and

[035-115]the whole host obeyed. Gordon returned to Khartoum in triumph. But he

[035-116]had not heard the last of Suleiman. Flying southwards from Darfur to the

[035-117]neighbouring province of Bahr-el-Ghazal, the young man was soon once

[035-118]more at the head of a formidable force. A prolonged campaign of extreme

[035-119]difficulty and danger followed. Eventually, Gordon, summoned again to

[035-120]Cairo, was obliged to leave to Gessi the task of finally crushing the

[035-121]revolt. After a brilliant campaign, Gessi forced Suleiman to surrender,

[035-122]and then shot him as a rebel. The deed was to exercise a curious

[035-123]influence upon Gordon's fate. Though Suleiman had been killed and his

[035-124]power broken, the slave-trade still flourished in the Sudan. Gordon's

[035-125]efforts to suppress it resembled the palliatives of an empiric treating

[035-126]the superficial symptoms of some profound constitutional disease. The

[035-127]root of the malady lay in the slave-markets of Cairo and Constantinople:

[035-128]the supply followed the demand. Gordon, after years of labour, might

[035-129]here and there stop up a spring or divert a tributary, but, somehow or

[035-130]other the waters would reach the river-bed. In the end, he himself came

[035-131]to recognise this. 'When you have got the ink that has soaked into

[035-132]blotting-paper out of it,' he said, 'then slavery will cease in these

[035-133]lands.' And yet he struggled desperately on; it was not for him to

[035-134]murmur. 'I feel my own weakness, and look to Him who is Almighty, and I

[035-135]leave the issue without inordinate care to Him.'

[035-136]Relief came at last. The Khedive Ismail was deposed; and Gordon felt at

[035-137]liberty to send in his resignation. Before he left Egypt, however, he

[035-138]was to experience yet one more remarkable adventure. At his own request,

[035-139]he set out on a diplomatic mission to the Negus of Abyssinia. The

[035-140]mission was a complete failure. The Negus was intractable, and, when his

[035-141]bribes were refused, furious. Gordon was ignominiously dismissed; every

[035-142]insult was heaped on him; he was arrested, and obliged to traverse the

[035-143]Abyssinian Mountains in the depth of winter under the escort of a savage

[035-144]troop of horse. When, after great hardships and dangers, he reached

[035-145]Cairo, he found the whole official world up in arms against him. The

[035-146]Pashas had determined at last that they had no further use for this

[035-147]honest and peculiar Englishman. It was arranged that one of his

[035-148]confidential dispatches should be published in the newspapers;

[035-149]naturally, it contained indiscretions; there was a universal outcry--the

[035-150]man was insubordinate, and mad. He departed under a storm of obloquy. It

[035-151]seemed impossible that he should ever return to Egypt. On his way home

[035-152]he stopped in Paris, saw the English Ambassador, Lord Lyons, and

[035-153]speedily came into conflict with him over Egyptian affairs. There ensued

[035-154]a heated correspondence, which was finally closed by a letter from

[035-155]Gordon, ending as follows:

[035-156]'I have some comfort in thinking that in ten or fifteen years' time it

[035-157]will matter little to either of us. A black box, six feet six by three

[035-158]feet wide, will then contain all that is left of Ambassador, or Cabinet

[035-159]Minister, or of your humble and obedient servant.'

[035-160]He arrived in England early in 1880 ill and exhausted; and it might have

[035-161]been supposed that after the terrible activities of his African exile he

[035-162]would have been ready to rest. But the very opposite was the case; the

[035-163]next three years were the most momentous of his life. He hurried from

[035-164]post to post, from enterprise to enterprise, from continent to

[035-165]continent, with a vertiginous rapidity. He accepted the Private

[035-166]Secretaryship to Lord Ripon, the new Viceroy of India, and, three days

[035-167]after his arrival at Bombay, he resigned. He had suddenly realised that

[035-168]he was not cut out for a Private Secretary, when, on an address being

[035-169]sent in from some deputation, he was asked to say that the Viceroy had

[035-170]read it with interest. 'You know perfectly,' he said to Lord William

[035-171]Beresford, 'that Lord Ripon has never read it, and I can't say that sort

[035-172]of thing; so I will resign, and you take in my resignation.' He

[035-173]confessed to Lord William that the world was not big enough for him,

[035-174]that there was 'no king or country big enough'; and then he added,

[035-175]hitting him on the shoulder, 'Yes, that is flesh, that is what I hate,

[035-176]and what makes me wish to die.'
