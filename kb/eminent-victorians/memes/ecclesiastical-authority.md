# Ecclesiastical Authority

The question of who holds legitimate authority in the Church — bishops, councils, or the Pope — was the fault line that ran through Victorian religion. Strachey's Manning is a case study in the psychology of authority: a man who craved absolute certainty and found it, successively, in Anglican orders, Roman obedience, and Papal Infallibility.

## Definition

Ecclesiastical authority is the claim of the Church to govern its members in matters of faith, morals, and discipline. In the Anglican context, authority was diffused: Scripture, tradition, reason, and the Crown-in-Parliament all claimed a share. The Oxford Movement challenged this diffusion by insisting on the exclusive authority of the Apostolic Succession — bishops consecrated in an unbroken line from the Apostles — as the guarantor of Catholic truth (003-073). The logical terminus of this position, as Newman and Manning discovered, was Rome.

## Stracheyan Irony

Strachey's treatment of ecclesiastical authority is relentlessly ironic. He recounts Manning's delight on becoming a Catholic that he could now "believe everything without mental reservation" — and then shows how the very authority Manning submitted to required him to fight political battles, manage ecclesiastical intrigue, and navigate the competing claims of Jesuits, bishops, and the Curia. Submission to absolute authority, far from ending conflict, simply moved it to a different arena.
