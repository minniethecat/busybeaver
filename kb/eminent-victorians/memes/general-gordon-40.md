[040-001]In spite of this little contretemps, Gordon was in the highest spirits.

[040-002]At last his capacities had been recognised by his countrymen; at last he

[040-003]had been entrusted with a task great enough to satisfy even his desires.

[040-004]He was already famous; he would soon be glorious. Looking out once more

[040-005]over the familiar desert, he felt the searchings of his conscience

[040-006]stilled by the manifest certainty that it was for this that Providence

[040-007]had been reserving him through all these years of labour and of sorrow

[040-008]for this! What was the Mahdi to stand up against him! A thousand

[040-009]schemes, a thousand possibilities sprang to life in his pullulating

[040-010]brain. A new intoxication carried him away. 'Il faut etre toujours ivre.

[040-011]Tout est la: c'est l'unique question.' Little though he knew it, Gordon

[040-012]was a disciple of Baudelaire. 'Pour ne pas sentir l'horrible fardeau du

[040-013]Temps qui brise vos epaules et vous penche vers la terre, il faut vous

[040-014]enivrer sans treve.' Yes--but how feeble were those gross resources of

[040-015]the miserable Abdul-Shakur! Rum? Brandy? Oh, he knew all about them;

[040-016]they were nothing. He tossed off a glass. They were nothing at all. The

[040-017]true drunkenness lay elsewhere. He seized a paper and pencil, and dashed

[040-018]down a telegram to Sir Evelyn Baring. Another thought struck him, and

[040-019]another telegram followed. And another, and yet another. He had made up

[040-020]his mind; he would visit the Mahdi in person, and alone. He might do

[040-021]that; or he might retire to the Equator. He would decidedly retire to

[040-022]the Equator, and hand over the Bahr-el-Ghazal province to the King of

[040-023]the Belgians. A whole flock of telegrams flew to Cairo from every

[040-024]stopping-place. Sir Evelyn Baring was patient and discrete; he could be

[040-025]trusted with such confidences; but unfortunately Gordon's strange

[040-026]exhilaration found other outlets. At Berber, in the course of a speech

[040-027]to the assembled chiefs, he revealed the intention of the Egyptian

[040-028]Government to withdraw from the Sudan. The news was everywhere in a

[040-029]moment, and the results were disastrous. The tribesmen, whom fear and

[040-030]interest had still kept loyal, perceived that they need look no more for

[040-031]help or punishment from Egypt, and began to turn their eyes towards the

[040-032]rising sun.

[040-033]Nevertheless, for the moment, the prospect wore a favourable appearance.

[040-034]The Governor-General was welcomed at every stage of his journey, and on

[040-035]February 18th he made a triumphal entry into [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. The feeble

[040-036]garrison, the panic-stricken inhabitants, hailed him as a deliverer.

[040-037]Surely they need fear no more, now that the great English Pasha had come

[040-038]among them. His first acts seemed to show that a new and happy era had

[040-039]begun. Taxes were remitted, the bonds of the usurers were destroyed, the

[040-040]victims of Egyptian injustice were set free from the prisons; the

[040-041]immemorial instruments of torture--the stocks and the whips and the

[040-042]branding-irons were broken to pieces in the public square. A bolder

[040-043]measure had been already taken. A proclamation had been issued

[040-044]sanctioning slavery in the Sudan. Gordon, arguing that he was powerless

[040-045]to do away with the odious institution, which, as soon as the withdrawal

[040-046]was carried out, would inevitably become universal, had decided to reap

[040-047]what benefit he could from the public abandonment of an unpopular

[040-048]policy. At Khartoum the announcement was received with enthusiasm, but

[040-049]it caused considerable perturbation in England. The Christian hero, who

[040-050]had spent so many years of his life in suppressing slavery, was now

[040-051]suddenly found to be using his high powers to set it up again. The

[040-052]Anti-Slavery Society made a menacing movement, but the Government showed

[040-053]a bold front, and the popular belief in Gordon's infallibility carried

[040-054]the day.

[040-055]He himself was still radiant. Nor, amid the jubilation and the devotion

[040-056]which surrounded him, did he forget higher things. In all this turmoil,

[040-057]he told his sister, he was 'supported'. He gave injunctions that his

[040-058]Egyptian troops should have regular morning and evening prayers; 'they

[040-059]worship one God,' he said, 'Jehovah.' And he ordered an Arabic text,

[040-060]'God rules the hearts of all men', to be put up over the chair of state

[040-061]in his audience chamber. As the days went by, he began to feel at home

[040-062]again in the huge palace which he knew so well. The glare and the heat

[040-063]of that southern atmosphere, the movement of the crowded city, the

[040-064]dark-faced populace, the soldiers and the suppliants, the reawakened

[040-065]consciousness of power, the glamour and the mystery of the whole strange

[040-066]scene--these things seized upon him, engulfed him, and worked a new

[040-067]transformation on his intoxicated heart. England, with its complications

[040-068]and its policies, became an empty vision to him; Sir Evelyn Baring, with

[040-069]his cautions and sagacities, hardly more than a tiresome name. He was

[040-070]Gordon Pasha, he was the Governor-General, he was the ruler of the

[040-071]Sudan. He was among his people--his own people, and it was to them only

[040-072]that he was responsible--to them, and to God. Was he to let them fall

[040-073]without a blow into the clutches of a sanguinary impostor? Never! He was

[040-074]there to prevent that. The distant governments might mutter something

[040-075]about 'evacuation'; his thoughts were elsewhere. He poured them into his

[040-076]telegrams, and Sir Evelyn Baring sat aghast. The man who had left London

[040-077]a month before, with instructions to 'report upon the best means of

[040-078]effecting the evacuation of the Sudan', was now openly talking of

[040-079]'smashing up the Mahdi' with the aid of British and Indian troops. Sir

[040-080]Evelyn Baring counted upon his fingers the various stages of this

[040-081]extraordinary development in [[general-gordon|General Gordon]]'s opinions. But he might

[040-082]have saved himself the trouble, for, in fact, it was less a development

[040-083]than a reversion. Under the stress of the excitements and the realities

[040-084]of his situation at Khartoum, the policy which Gordon was now proposing

[040-085]to carry out had come to tally, in every particular, with the policy

[040-086]which he had originally advocated with such vigorous conviction in the

[040-087]pages of the Pall Mall Gazette.

[040-088]Nor was the adoption of that policy by the English Government by any

[040-089]means out of the question. For, in the meantime, events had been taking

[040-090]place in the Eastern Sudan, in the neighbourhood of the Red Sea port of

[040-091]Suakin, which were to have a decisive effect upon the prospects of

[040-092]Khartoum. General Baker, the brother of Sir Samuel Baker, attempting to

[040-093]relieve the beleaguered garrisons of Sinkat and Tokar, had rashly

[040-094]attacked the forces of Osman Digna, had been defeated, and obliged to

[040-095]retire. Sinkat and Tokar had then fallen into the hands of the Mahdi's

[040-096]general. There was a great outcry in England, and a wave of warlike

[040-097]feeling passed over the country. Lord Wolseley at once drew up a

[040-098]memorandum advocating the annexation of the Sudan. In the House of

[040-099]Commons even Liberals began to demand vengeance and military action,

[040-100]whereupon the Government dispatched Sir Gerald Graham with a

[040-101]considerable British force to Suakin. Sir Gerald Graham advanced, and in

[040-102]the battles of El Teb and Tamai inflicted two bloody defeats upon the

[040-103]Mahdi's forces. It almost seemed as if the Government was now committed

[040-104]to a policy of interference and conquest; as if the imperialist section

[040-105]of the Cabinet were at last to have their way. The dispatch of Sir

[040-106]Gerald Graham coincided with Gordon's sudden demand for British and

[040-107]Indian troops with which to 'smash up the Mahdi'. The business, he

[040-108]assured Sir Evelyn Baring, in a stream of telegrams, could very easily

[040-109]be done. It made him sick, he said, to see himself held in check and the

[040-110]people of the Sudan tyrannised over by 'a feeble lot of stinking

[040-111]Dervishes'. Let Zobeir at once be sent down to him, and all would be

[040-112]well.

[040-113]The original Sultans of the country had unfortunately proved

[040-114]disappointing. Their place should be taken by Zobeir. After the Mahdi

[040-115]had been smashed up, Zobeir should rule the Sudan as a subsidised vassal

[040-116]of England, on a similar footing to that of the Amir of Afghanistan. The

[040-117]plan was perhaps feasible; but it was clearly incompatible with the

[040-118]policy of evacuation, as it had been hitherto laid down by the English

[040-119]Government. Should they reverse that policy? Should they appoint Zobeir,

[040-120]reinforce Sir Gerald Graham, and smash up the Mahdi? They could not make

[040-121]up their minds. So far as Zobeir was concerned, there were two

[040-122]counterbalancing considerations; on the one hand, Evelyn Baring now

[040-123]declared that he was in favour of the appointment; but, on the other

[040-124]hand, would English public opinion consent to a man, described by Gordon

[040-125]himself as 'the greatest slave-hunter who ever existed', being given an

[040-126]English subsidy and the control of the Sudan? While the Cabinet was

[040-127]wavering, Gordon took a fatal step. The delay was intolerable, and one

[040-128]evening, in a rage, he revealed his desire for Zobeir--which had

[040-129]hitherto been kept a profound official secret--to Mr Power, the English

[040-130]Consul at Khartoum, and the special correspondent of "The Times."

[040-131]Perhaps he calculated that the public announcement of his wishes would

[040-132]oblige the Government to yield to them; if so, he was completely

[040-133]mistaken, for the result was the very reverse. The country, already

[040-134]startled by the proclamation in favour of slavery, could not swallow

[040-135]Zobeir. The Anti-Slavery Society set on foot a violent agitation,

[040-136]opinion in the House of Commons suddenly stiffened, and the Cabinet, by

[040-137]a substantial majority, decided that Zobeir should remain in Cairo. The

[040-138]imperialist wave had risen high, but it had not risen high enough; and

[040-139]now it was rapidly subsiding. The Government's next action was decisive.

[040-140]Sir Gerald Graham and his [[british-army|British Army]] were withdrawn from the Sudan.

[040-141]The critical fortnight during which these events took place was the

[040-142]first fortnight of March. By the close of it, Gordon's position had

[040-143]undergone a rapid and terrible change. Not only did he find himself

[040-144]deprived, by the decision of the Government, both of the hope of

[040-145]Zobeir's assistance and of the prospect of smashing up the Mahdi with

[040-146]the aid of British troops; the military movements in the Eastern Sudan

[040-147]produced, at the very same moment, a yet more fatal consequence. The

[040-148]adherents of the Mahdi had been maddened, they had not been crushed, by

[040-149]Sir Gerald Graham's victories. When, immediately afterwards, the English

[040-150]withdrew to Suakin, from which they never again emerged, the inference

[040-151]seemed obvious; they had been defeated, and their power was at an end.

[040-152]The warlike tribes to the north and the northeast of Khartoum had long

[040-153]been wavering. They now hesitated no longer, and joined the Mahdi. From

[040-154]that moment--it was less than a month from Gordon's arrival at

[040-155]Khartoum--the situation of the town was desperate. The line of

[040-156]communications was cut. Though it still might be possible for occasional

[040-157]native messengers, or for a few individuals on an armed steamer, to win

[040-158]their way down the river into Egypt, the removal of a large number of

[040-159]persons--the loyal inhabitants or the Egyptian garrison--was

[040-160]henceforward an impossibility. The whole scheme of the Gordon mission

[040-161]had irremediably collapsed; worse still, Gordon himself, so far from

[040-162]having effected the evacuation of the Sudan, was surrounded by the

[040-163]enemy. 'The question now is,' Sir Evelyn Baring told Lord Granville, on

[040-164]March 24th, 'how to get General Gordon and Colonel Stewart away from

[040-165]Khartoum.'

[040-166]The actual condition of the town, however, was not, from a military

[040-167]point of view, so serious as Colonel Coetlogon, in the first moments of

[040-168]panic after the Hicks disaster, had supposed. Gordon was of opinion that

[040-169]it was capable of sustaining a siege of many months. With his usual

[040-170]vigour, he had already begun to prepare an elaborate system of

[040-171]earthworks, mines, and wire entanglements. There was a five or six

[040-172]months' supply of food, there was a great quantity of ammunition, the

[040-173]garrison numbered about 8,000 men. There were, besides, nine small

[040-174]paddle-wheel steamers, hitherto used for purposes of communication along

[040-175]the Nile, which, fitted with guns and protected by metal plates, were of

[040-176]considerable military value. 'We are all right,' Gordon told his sister

[040-177]on March 15th. 'We shall, D. V., go on for months.' So far, at any rate,

[040-178]there was no cause for despair. But the effervescent happiness of three

[040-179]weeks since had vanished. Gloom, doubt, disillusionment,

[040-180]self-questioning, had swooped down again upon their victim.
