## Overview

Pope Pius IX (1792–1878) reigned as Supreme Pontiff for thirty-two years, the longest pontificate in Catholic history. He is a looming presence in Lytton Strachey's biography of [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]], where he appears as the distant but decisive authority whose personal interventions repeatedly shaped the course of English Catholicism.

> Pius IX was furious; the Chapter had committed an 'insulta al Papa', he exclaimed, striking his breast three times in his rage. [010-178]

## The Restoration of the English Hierarchy

One of Pius IX's most consequential acts for English Catholicism was the restoration of the Catholic hierarchy in 1850. He divided the whole of England into dioceses and placed [[cardinal-wiseman|Cardinal Wiseman]] at their head as Archbishop of Westminster [009-080]. This act — announced in Wiseman's encyclical "from without the Flaminian Gate" — provoked the anti-Catholic "Papal Aggression" outcry in England.

## Manning's Interview and Rise

Manning's path to ecclesiastical power in England ran directly through [[rome|Rome]]. After his conversion from Anglicanism, Manning had a long interview with Pius IX. Strachey notes, with characteristic irony, that Manning's only record of this pivotal meeting was the bald statement: "Audience today at the [[rome|Vatican]]." What precisely passed never transpired [007-139].

Years later, when the Chapter of Westminster proposed [[dr-errington|Dr. Errington]] as Manning's coadjutor, Pius IX exploded in fury at what he saw as an insult to papal authority [010-178]. Brushing aside the recommendations of Chapter and Cardinals alike, the Pope acted on what he believed was divine inspiration — the whispered words "Mettetelo li!" (Put him there!) — and made Manning Archbishop of Westminster by a direct Pontifical act [011-019].

## The Vatican Council and Infallibility

Pius IX convened the [[vatican-council-1870|First Vatican Council]] (1869–1870), which defined the dogma of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]] — the doctrine that the Pope, when speaking *ex cathedra* on matters of faith and morals, is preserved from error. This doctrine was the theological culmination of the Ultramontane movement that Manning championed in England.

## Legacy

Pius IX's pontificate transformed the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s relationship with the modern world. He lost the Papal States to Italian unification, becoming the "Prisoner of the [[rome|Vatican]]," yet he strengthened papal spiritual authority to an unprecedented degree. For Manning, Pius IX was the Vicar of Christ whose personal favour had placed him on the throne of Westminster — a debt Manning repaid with a lifetime of ultramontane loyalty.
