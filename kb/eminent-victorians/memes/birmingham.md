# Birmingham

The city of Newman's long exile. While Manning rose through the ecclesiastical ranks in London, John Henry Newman — the other great convert of the Oxford Movement — settled in Birmingham in 1849 to found a house of the Oratory of St. Philip Neri. In Strachey's narrative, Birmingham is the deliberate antithesis to Westminster: the provincial retreat of the defeated intellectual against the metropolitan citadel of the triumphant administrator.

## In the Narrative

Birmingham enters Strachey's story as Newman's chosen place of retreat after his conversion to Roman Catholicism. Having left Oxford, having abandoned the Church of England, Newman "settled down in Birmingham as the head of a small community of Oratorian fathers" [^1] — a modest congregation in a manufacturing city far from the centres of power. The Oratory became Newman's home, his sanctuary, and his intellectual workshop, where he wrote the *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* and the *Grammar of Assent*.

Strachey uses Birmingham as a spatial metaphor for the Manning-Newman rivalry. When Manning sought to have Newman made a Bishop, it was partly to honour him and partly — Strachey insinuates — to neutralise a potential rival. The Bishop of Birmingham, Dr. Ullathorne, was approached with the proposal. Newman "languished in Birmingham" while Manning received the Cardinal's hat in Rome [^2]. The geographical distance between Birmingham and Westminster — barely a hundred miles by rail — becomes, in Strachey's telling, the measure of the psychological gulf between the two men.

### Association with John Henry Newman

The Birmingham Oratory was Newman's home from 1849 until his death in 1890. Here he lived as Superior of a community that followed the rule of St. Philip Neri — a life of prayer, study, and pastoral care among the Catholic poor of the industrial city. Strachey notes that Manning "never visited Birmingham," so that "the two men" — Manning and Newman — "never met" after their estrangement [^3]. The Oratory became the symbol of what Manning could not reach and would not understand.

## Historical Context

Birmingham in the mid-nineteenth century was the heart of the Industrial Revolution — a city of iron foundries, metal workshops, and smoke-blackened brick. For Newman to choose it as the site of an Oratory was itself a statement: Catholicism was not merely for the old recusant gentry of the countryside but for the new urban working classes. As Newman himself wrote, with characteristic understatement: "Birmingham people have souls; and I have neither taste nor talent for the country" [^1].
