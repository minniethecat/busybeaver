# Rev. Mr. Barnes

Vicar of Heavitree and a friend of [[general-gordon|General Gordon]], whose quiet presence illuminates the human side of Gordon's last days in England before his mission to the Sudan.

## Life

The Reverend Mr. Barnes was the vicar of Heavitree, near Exeter, and a friend of [[general-gordon]]. His correspondence with Gordon "filling over a large number of sheets" [036-049] suggests a relationship of some intimacy — Gordon, for all his public persona, chose his private confidants carefully.

### The Last Days

Barnes appears most vividly in the account of Gordon's final days in England. After his fateful interview with [[william-stead]] and the Cabinet's decision to send him to the Sudan, Gordon went to stay with Barnes at the Heavitree Vicarage [038-046]. It was a moment of peculiar stillness before the storm.

> "Gordon was silent; but Mr. Barnes noticed that his face wore an expression of intense melancholy." [038-056]

Barnes accompanied Gordon on a drive through the Devon countryside — an image Strachey renders with characteristic pathos: the general, about to depart for his fate, silent beside the country vicar. When Barnes asked Gordon whether he expected to return, Gordon's reply was characteristic in its indirection: he asked Barnes what he thought of the doctrine of the transmigration of souls [038-061].

Strachey notes that the language Gordon would have used with Barnes was quite different from the martial rhetoric of his public statements — "it was a language that he would have used with the Rev. Mr. Barnes or his sister Augusta" [044-050], intimate and unguarded.

## Character and Beliefs

Barnes is the silent witness — the ordinary clergyman whose life briefly intersected with history. His most significant act was simply being present, offering the friendship and normalcy that Gordon, in the extraordinary circumstances of his departure, seems to have needed.

## In This Book

Barnes appears briefly in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47) as a figure of quiet domesticity against which Gordon's drama is played out. He is one of those minor characters — like "his sister Augusta" — whom Strachey uses to humanize his subjects and remind us that even heroes have friends, doubts, and melancholy country drives.
