[017-001]Leo XIII was elected, and there was a great change in the policy of the

[017-002][[rome|Vatican]]. Liberalism became the order of the day. And now at last the

[017-003]opportunity seemed ripe for an act which, in the opinion of the majority

[017-004]of English Catholics, had long been due--the bestowal of some mark of

[017-005]recognition from the Holy See upon the labours and the sanctity of

[017-006]Father [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]. It was felt that a Cardinal's hat was the one fitting

[017-007]reward for such a life, and accordingly the Duke of Norfolk,

[017-008]representing the Catholic laity of England, visited Manning, and

[017-009]suggested that he should forward the proposal to the Vatican. Manning

[017-010]agreed, and then there followed a curious series of incidents--the last

[017-011]encounter in the jarring lives of those two men. A letter was drawn up

[017-012]by Manning for the eye of the Pope, embodying the Duke of Norfolk's

[017-013]proposal; but there was an unaccountable delay in the transmission of

[017-014]this letter; months passed, and it had not reached the Holy Father. The

[017-015]whole matter would, perhaps, have dropped out of sight and been

[017-016]forgotten, in a way which had become customary when honours for Newman

[017-017]were concerned, had not the Duke of Norfolk himself, when he was next in

[017-018]Rome, ventured to recommend to Leo XIII that Dr. Newman should be made a

[017-019]Cardinal. His Holiness welcomed the proposal; but, he said, he could do

[017-020]nothing until he knew the views of [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]]. Thereupon, the Duke

[017-021]of Norfolk wrote to Manning, explaining what had occurred; shortly

[017-022]afterwards, Manning's letter of recommendation, after a delay of six

[017-023]months, reached the Pope, and the offer of a Cardinalate was immediately

[017-024]dispatched to Newman.

[017-025]But the affair was not yet over. The offer had been made; would it be

[017-026]accepted? There was one difficulty in the way. Newman was now an infirm

[017-027]old man of seventy-eight; and it is a rule that all Cardinals who are

[017-028]not also diocesan Bishops or Archbishops reside, as a matter of course,

[017-029]at Rome. The change would have been impossible for one of his years--for

[017-030]one, too, whose whole life was now bound up with the Oratory at

[017-031]Birmingham. But, of course, there was nothing to prevent His Holiness

[017-032]from making an exception in Newman's case, and allowing him to end his

[017-033]days in England. Yet how was Newman himself to suggest this? The offer

[017-034]of the Hat had come to him as an almost miraculous token of renewed

[017-035]confidence, of ultimate reconciliation. The old, long, bitter

[017-036]estrangement was ended at last. 'The cloud is lifted from me for ever!'

[017-037]he exclaimed when the news reached him. It would be melancholy indeed if

[017-038]the cup were now to be once more dashed from his lips and he was obliged

[017-039]to refuse the signal honour. In his perplexity he went to the Bishop of

[017-040]Birmingham and explained the whole situation. The Bishop assured him

[017-041]that all would be well; that he himself would communicate with the

[017-042]authorities, and put the facts of the case before them. Accordingly,

[017-043]while Newman wrote formally refusing the Hat, on the ground of his

[017-044]unwillingness to leave the Oratory, the Bishop wrote two letters to

[017-045]Manning, one official and one private, in which the following passages

[017-046]occurred:

[017-047]'Dr. Newman has far too humble and delicate a mind to dream of thinking

[017-048]or saying anything which would look like hinting at any kind of terms

[017-049]with the Sovereign Pontiff.... I think, however, that I ought to express

[017-050]my own sense of what Dr. Newman's dispositions are, and that it will be

[017-051]expected of me ... I am thoroughly confident that nothing stands in the

[017-052]way of his most grateful acceptance, except what he tells me greatly

[017-053]distresses him--namely, the having to leave the Oratory at a critical

[017-054]period of its existence, and the impossibility of his beginning a new

[017-055]life at his advanced age.'

[017-056]And in his private letter the Bishop said:

[017-057]'Dr. Newman is very much aged, and softened with age and the trials he

[017-058]has had, especially the loss of his two brethren, St. John and Caswall;

[017-059]he can never refer to these losses without weeping and becoming

[017-060]speechless for a time. He is very much affected by the Pope's kindness

[017-061]and would, I know, like to receive the great honour offered him, but

[017-062]feels the whole difficulty at his age of changing his life or having to

[017-063]leave the Oratory--which I am sure he could not do. If the Holy Father

[017-064]thinks well to confer on him the dignity, leaving him where he is, I

[017-065]know how immensely he would be gratified, and you will know how

[017-066]generally the conferring on him the Cardinalate will be applauded.'

[017-067]These two letters, together with Newman's refusal, reached Manning as he

[017-068]was on the point of starting for Rome. After he had left England, the

[017-069]following statement appeared in "The Times":

[017-070]'Pope Leo XIII has intimated his desire to raise Dr. Newman to the rank

[017-071]of Cardinal, but with expressions of deep respect for the Holy See, Dr.

[017-072]Newman has excused himself from accepting the Purple.'

[017-073]When Newman's eyes fell upon the announcement, he realised at once that

[017-074]a secret and powerful force was working against him. He trembled, as he

[017-075]had so often trembled before; and certainly the danger was not

[017-076]imaginary. In the ordinary course of things, how could such a paragraph

[017-077]have been inserted without his authority? And consequently, did it not

[017-078]convey to the world, not only an absolute refusal which he had never

[017-079]intended, but a wish on his part to emphasise publicly his rejection of

[017-080]the proffered honour? Did it not imply that he had lightly declined a

[017-081]proposal for which in reality he was deeply thankful? And when the fatal

[017-082]paragraph was read in Rome, might it not actually lead to the offer of

[017-083]the Cardinalate being finally withheld?

[017-084]In great agitation, Newman appealed to the Duke of Norfolk.

[017-085]'As to the statement,' he wrote, 'of my refusing a Cardinal's Hat, which

[017-086]is in the papers, you must not believe it, for this reason:

[017-087]'Of course, it implies that an offer has been made me, and I have sent

[017-088]an answer to it. Now I have ever understood that it is a point of

[017-089]propriety and honour to consider such communications sacred. This

[017-090]statement, therefore, cannot come from me. Nor could it come from Rome,

[017-091]for it was made public before my answer got to Rome.

[017-092]'It could only come, then, from someone who not only read my letter,

[017-093]but, instead of leaving to the Pope to interpret it, took upon himself

[017-094]to put an interpretation upon it, and published that interpretation to

[017-095]the world.

[017-096]'A private letter, addressed to Roman Authorities, is interpreted on its

[017-097]way and published in the English papers. How is it possible that anyone

[017-098]can have done this?'

[017-099]The crushing indictment pointed straight at Manning. And it was true.

[017-100]Manning had done the impossible deed. Knowing what he did, with the

[017-101]Bishop of Birmingham's two letters in his pocket, he had put it about

[017-102]that Newman had refused the Hat. But a change had come over the spirit

[017-103]of the Holy See. Things were not as they had once been: Monsignor Talbot

[017-104]was at Passy, and Pio Nono was--where? The Duke of Norfolk intervened

[017-105]once again; Manning was profuse in his apologies for having

[017-106]misunderstood Newman's intentions, and hurried to the Pope to rectify

[017-107]the error. Without hesitation, the Sovereign Pontiff relaxed the rule of

[017-108]Roman residence, and Newman became a Cardinal.

[017-109]He lived to enjoy his glory for more than ten years. Since he rarely

[017-110]left the Oratory, and since Manning never visited Birmingham, the two

[017-111]Cardinals met only once or twice. After one of these occasions, on

[017-112]returning to the Oratory, Cardinal Newman said, 'What do you think

[017-113]Cardinal Manning did to me? He kissed me!'

[017-114]On Newman's death, Manning delivered a funeral oration, which opened

[017-115]thus:

[017-116]'We have lost our greatest witness for the Faith, and we are all poorer

[017-117]and lower by the loss.

[017-118]'When these tidings came to me, my first thought was this, in what way

[017-119]can I, once more, show my love and veneration for my brother and friend

[017-120]of more than sixty years?'

[017-121]In private, however, the surviving Cardinal's tone was apt to be more

[017-122]... direct. 'Poor Newman!' he once exclaimed in a moment of genial

[017-123]expansion. 'Poor Newman! He was a great hater!'

[017-124]X

[017-125]IN that gaunt and gloomy building--more like a barracks than an

[017-126]Episcopal palace--Archbishop's House, Westminster, Manning's existence

[017-127]stretched itself out into an extreme old age. As his years increased,

[017-128]his activities, if that were possible, increased too. Meetings,

[017-129]missions, lectures, sermons, articles, interviews, letters--such things

[017-130]came upon him in redoubled multitudes, and were dispatched with an

[017-131]unrelenting zeal. But this was not all; with age, he seemed to acquire

[017-132]what was almost a new fervour, an unaccustomed, unexpected, freeing of

[017-133]the spirit, filling him with preoccupations which he had hardly felt

[017-134]before. 'They say I am ambitious,' he noted in his Diary, 'but do I rest

[017-135]in my ambition?'

[017-136]No, assuredly he did not rest; but he worked now with no arriere pensee

[017-137]for the greater glory of God. A kind of frenzy fell upon him. Poverty,

[017-138]drunkenness, vice, all the horrors and terrors of our civilisation

[017-139]seized upon his mind, and urged him forward to new fields of action and

[017-140]new fields of thought. The temper of his soul assumed almost a

[017-141]revolutionary cast. 'I am a Mosaic Radical,' he exclaimed; and, indeed,

[017-142]in the exaltation of his energies, the incoherence of his conceptions,

[017-143]the democratic urgency of his desires, combined with his awe-inspiring

[017-144]aspect and his venerable age, it was easy enough to trace the mingled

[017-145]qualities of the patriarch, the prophet, and the demagogue. As, in his

[017-146]soiled and shabby garments, the old man harangued the crowds of

[017-147]Bermondsey or Peckham upon the virtues of Temperance, assuring them,

[017-148]with all the passion of conviction, as a final argument, that the

[017-149]majority of the Apostles were total abstainers, this Prince of the

[017-150]Church might have passed as a leader of the Salvation Army. His

[017-151]popularity was immense, reaching its height during the great Dock

[017-152]Strikes of 1889, when, after the victory of the men was assured, Manning

[017-153]was able, by his persuasive eloquence and the weight of his character,

[017-154]to prevent its being carried to excess. After other conciliators--among

[017-155]whom was the Bishop of London--had given up the task in disgust, the

[017-156]octogenarian Cardinal worked on with indefatigable resolution. At last,

[017-157]late at night, in the schools in Kirby Street, Bermondsey, he rose to

[017-158]address the strikers. An enthusiastic eye-witness has described the

[017-159]scene:

[017-160]'Unaccustomed tears glistened in the eyes of his rough and work-stained

[017-161]hearers as the Cardinal raised his hand and solemnly urged them not to

[017-162]prolong one moment more than they could help the perilous uncertainty

[017-163]and the sufferings of their wives and children. Just above his uplifted

[017-164]hand was a figure of the Madonna and Child; and some among the men tell

[017-165]how a sudden light seemed to swim around it as the speaker pleaded for

[017-166]the women and children. When he sat down all in the room knew that he

[017-167]had won the day, and that, so far as the Strike Committee was concerned,

[017-168]the matter was at an end.'

[017-169]In those days, there were strange visitors at the Archbishop's House.

[017-170]Careful priests and conscientious secretaries wondered what the world

[017-171]was coming to when they saw labour leaders like Mr. John Burns and Mr.

[017-172]Ben Tillett, and land-reformers like Mr. Henry George, being ushered

[017-173]into the presence of his Eminence. Even the notorious Mr. Stead

[017-174]appeared, and his scandalous paper with its unspeakable revelations lay

[017-175]upon the Cardinal's table. This proved too much for one of the faithful

[017-176]tonsured dependents of the place, and he ventured to expostulate with

[017-177]his master. But he never did so again.

[017-178]When the guests were gone, and the great room was empty, the old man

[017-179]would draw himself nearer to the enormous fire, and review once more,

[017-180]for the thousandth time, the long adventure of his life. He would bring

[017-181]out his diaries and his memoranda, he would rearrange his notes, he

[017-182]would turn over again the yellow leaves of faded correspondences;

[017-183]seizing his pen, he would pour out his comments and reflections, and

[017-184]fill, with an extraordinary solicitude, page after page with

[017-185]elucidations, explanations, justifications, of the vanished incidents of

[017-186]a remote past. He would snip with scissors the pages of ancient

[017-187]journals, and with delicate ecclesiastical fingers, drop unknown

[017-188]mysteries into the flames.
