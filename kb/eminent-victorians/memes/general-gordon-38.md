[038-001]The decision to withdraw from the Sudan had no sooner been taken than it

[038-002]had become evident that the operation would be a difficult and hazardous

[038-003]one, and that it would be necessary to send to [[khartoum|Khartoum]] an emissary

[038-004]armed with special powers and possessed of special ability, to carry it

[038-005]out. Towards the end of November, somebody at the [[war-office|War Office]]--it is not

[038-006]clear who--had suggested that this emissary should be [[general-gordon|General Gordon]].

[038-007]Lord Granville, the Foreign Secretary, had thereupon telegraphed to Sir

[038-008]Evelyn Baring asking whether, in his opinion, the presence of General

[038-009]Gordon would be useful in Egypt; Sir Evelyn Baring had replied that the

[038-010]Egyptian Government was averse to this proposal, and the matter had

[038-011]dropped.

[038-012]There was no further reference to Gordon in the official dispatches

[038-013]until after his return to England. Nor, before that date, was any

[038-014]allusion made to him as a possible unraveller of the Sudan difficulty,

[038-015]in the Press. In all the discussions which followed the news of the

[038-016]Hicks disaster, his name is only to be found in occasional and

[038-017]incidental references to his work "In the Sudan". The "Pall Mall

[038-018]Gazette", which, more than any other newspaper, interested itself in

[038-019]Egyptian affairs, alluded to Gordon once or twice as a geographical

[038-020]expert; but, in an enumeration of the leading authorities on the Sudan,

[038-021]left him out of account altogether. Yet it was from the "Pall Mall

[038-022]Gazette" that the impulsion which projected him into a blaze of

[038-023]publicity finally came. Mr. Stead, its enterprising editor, went down to

[038-024]Southampton the day after Gordon's arrival there, and obtained an

[038-025]interview. Now when he was in the mood--after a little b. and s.,

[038-026]especially--no one was more capable than Gordon, with his facile speech

[038-027]and his free-and-easy manners, of furnishing good copy for a journalist;

[038-028]and Mr. Stead made the most of his opportunity. The interview, copious

[038-029]and pointed, was published next day in the most prominent part of the

[038-030]paper, together with a leading article, demanding that the General

[038-031]should be immediately dispatched to Khartoum with the widest powers. The

[038-032]rest of the Press, both in London and in the provinces, at once took up

[038-033]the cry: General Gordon was a capable and energetic officer, he was a

[038-034]noble and God-fearing man, he was a national asset, he was a statesman

[038-035]in the highest sense of the word; the occasion was pressing and

[038-036]perilous; General Gordon had been for years Governor-General of the

[038-037]Sudan; General Gordon alone had the knowledge, the courage, the virtue,

[038-038]which would save the situation; General Gordon must go to Khartoum. So,

[038-039]for a week, the papers sang in chorus. But already those in high places

[038-040]had taken a step. Mr. Stead's interview appeared on the afternoon of

[038-041]January 9th, and on the morning of January 10th Lord Granville

[038-042]telegraphed to Sir Evelyn Baring, proposing, for a second time, that

[038-043]Gordon's services should be utilised in Egypt. But Sir Evelyn Baring,

[038-044]for the second time, rejected the proposal.

[038-045]While these messages were flashing to and fro, Gordon himself was paying

[038-046]a visit to the Rev. Mr. Barnes at the Vicarage of Heavitree, near

[038-047]Exeter. The conversation ran chiefly on Biblical and spiritual

[038-048]matters--on the light thrown by the Old Testament upon the geography of

[038-049]Palestine, and on the relations between man and his Maker; but, there

[038-050]were moments when topics of a more worldly interest arose. It happened

[038-051]that Sir Samuel Baker, Gordon's predecessor in Equatoria, lived in the

[038-052]neighbourhood. A meeting was arranged, and the two ex-Governors, with

[038-053]Mr. Barnes in attendance, went for a drive together. In the carriage,

[038-054]Sir Samuel Baker, taking up the tale of the "Pall Mall Gazette", dilated

[038-055]upon the necessity of his friend's returning to the Sudan as

[038-056]Governor-General. Gordon was silent; but Mr. Barnes noticed that his

[038-057]blue eyes flashed, while an eager expression passed over his face. Late

[038-058]that night, after the Vicar had retired to bed, he was surprised by the

[038-059]door suddenly opening, and by the appearance of his guest swiftly

[038-060]tripping into the room. 'You saw me today?' the low voice abruptly

[038-061]questioned. 'You mean in the carriage?' replied the startled Mr. Barnes.

[038-062]'Yes,' came the reply; 'you saw ME--that was MYSELF--the self I want to

[038-063]get rid of.' There was a sliding movement, the door swung to, and the

[038-064]Vicar found himself alone again.

[038-065]It was clear that a disturbing influence had found its way into Gordon's

[038-066]mind. His thoughts, wandering through Africa, flitted to the Sudan; they

[038-067]did not linger at the Congo. During the same visit, he took the

[038-068]opportunity of calling upon Dr. Temple, the Bishop of Exeter, and asking

[038-069]him, merely as a hypothetical question, whether, in his opinion,

[038-070]Sudanese converts to Christianity might be permitted to keep three

[038-071]wives. His Lordship answered that this would be uncanonical.

[038-072]A few days later, it appeared that the conversation in the carriage at

[038-073]Heavitree had borne fruit. Gordon wrote a letter to Sir Samuel Baker,

[038-074]further elaborating the opinions on the Sudan which he had already

[038-075]expressed in his interview with Mr. Stead; the letter was clearly

[038-076]intended for publication, and published it was in "The Times" of January

[038-077]14th. On the same day, Gordon's name began once more to buzz along the

[038-078]wires in secret questions and answers to and from the highest quarters.

[038-079]'Might it not be advisable,' telegraphed Lord Granville to Mr.

[038-080][[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]], to put a little pressure on Baring, to induce him to accept

[038-081]the assistance of General Gordon?' Mr. Gladstone replied, also by a

[038-082]telegram, in the affirmative; and on the 15th, Lord Wolseley telegraphed

[038-083]to Gordon begging him to come to London immediately. Lord Wolseley, who

[038-084]was one of Gordon's oldest friends, was at that time Adjutant-General of

[038-085]the Forces; there was a long interview; and, though the details of the

[038-086]conversation have never transpired, it is known that, in the course of

[038-087]it, Lord Wolseley asked Gordon if he would be willing to go to the

[038-088]Sudan, to which Gordon replied that there was only one objection--his

[038-089]prior engagement to the King of the Belgians. Before nightfall, Lord

[038-090]Granville, by private telegram, had 'put a little pressure on Baring'.

[038-091]'He had,' he said, 'heard indirectly that Gordon was ready to go at once

[038-092]to the Sudan on the following rather vague terms: His mission to be to

[038-093]report to Her Majesty's Government on the military situation, and to

[038-094]return without any further engagement. He would be under you for

[038-095]instructions and will send letters through you under flying seal ... He

[038-096]might be of use,' Lord Granville added, in informing you and us of the

[038-097]situation. It would be popular at home, but there may be countervailing

[038-098]objections. Tell me,' such was Lord Granville's concluding injunction,

[038-099]'your real opinion.' It was the third time of asking, and Sir Evelyn

[038-100]Baring resisted no longer.

[038-101]'Gordon,' he telegraphed on the 16th, 'would be the best man if he will

[038-102]pledge himself to carry out the policy of withdrawing from the Sudan as

[038-103]quickly as is possible, consistently with saving life. He must also

[038-104]understand that he must take his instructions from the British

[038-105]representative in Egypt ... I would rather have him than anyone else,

[038-106]provided there is a perfectly clear understanding with him as to what

[038-107]his position is to be and what line of policy he is to carry out.

[038-108]Otherwise, not ... Whoever goes should be distinctly warned that he will

[038-109]undertake a service of great difficulty and danger.'

[038-110]In the meantime, Gordon, with the Sudan upon his lips, with the Sudan in

[038-111]his imagination, had hurried to Brussels, to obtain from the King of the

[038-112]Belgians a reluctant consent to the postponement of his Congo mission.

[038-113]On the 17th he was recalled to London by a telegram from Lord Wolseley.

[038-114]On the 18th the final decision was made. 'At noon,' Gordon told the Rev.

[038-115]Mr. Barnes, Wolseley came to me and took me to the Ministers. He went in

[038-116]and talked to the Ministers, and came back and said: "Her Majesty's

[038-117]Government wants you to undertake this. Government is determined to

[038-118]evacuate the Sudan, for they will not guarantee future government. Will

[038-119]you go and do it?" I said: "Yes." He said: "Go in." I went in and saw

[038-120]them. They said: "Did Wolseley tell you your orders?" I said: "Yes." I

[038-121]said: "You will not guarantee future government of the Sudan, and you

[038-122]wish me to go up and evacuate now." They said: "Yes", and it was over.'

[038-123]Such was the sequence of events which ended in General Gordon's last

[038-124]appointment. The precise motives of those responsible for these

[038-125]transactions are less easy to discern. It is difficult to understand

[038-126]what the reasons could have been which induced the Government, not only

[038-127]to override the hesitations of Sir Evelyn Baring, but to overlook the

[038-128]grave and obvious dangers involved in sending such a man as Gordon to

[038-129]the Sudan. The whole history of his life, the whole bent of his

[038-130]character, seemed to disqualify him for the task for which he had been

[038-131]chosen. He was before all things a fighter, an enthusiast, a bold

[038-132]adventurer; and he was now to be entrusted with the conduct of an

[038-133]inglorious retreat. He was alien to the subtleties of civilised

[038-134]statesmanship, he was unamenable to official control, he was incapable

[038-135]of the skilful management of delicate situations; and he was now to be

[038-136]placed in a position of great complexity, requiring at once a cool

[038-137]judgment, a clear perception of fact, and a fixed determination to carry

[038-138]out a line of policy laid down from above. He had, it is true, been

[038-139]Governor-General of the Sudan; but he was now to return to the scene of

[038-140]his greatness as the emissary of a defeated and humbled power; he was to

[038-141]be a fugitive where he had once been a ruler; the very success of his

[038-142]mission was to consist in establishing the triumph of those forces which

[038-143]he had spent years in trampling underfoot. All this should have been

[038-144]clear to those in authority, after a very little reflection. It was

[038-145]clear enough to Sir Evelyn Baring, though, with characteristic

[038-146]reticence, he had abstained from giving expression to his thoughts. But,

[038-147]even if a general acquaintance with Gordon's life and character were not

[038-148]sufficient to lead to these conclusions, he himself had taken care to

[038-149]put their validity beyond reasonable doubt. Both in his interview with

[038-150]Mr. Stead and in his letter to Sir Samuel Baker, he had indicated

[038-151]unmistakably his own attitude towards the Sudan situation. The policy

[038-152]which he advocated, the state of feeling in which he showed himself to

[038-153]be, was diametrically opposed to the declared intentions of the

[038-154]Government. He was by no means in favour of withdrawing from the Sudan;

[038-155]he was in favour, as might have been supposed, of vigorous military

[038-156]action. It might be necessary to abandon, for the time being, the more

[038-157]remote garrisons in Darfur and Equatoria; but Khartoum must be held at

[038-158]all costs. To allow the Mahdi to enter Khartoum would not merely mean

[038-159]the return of the whole of the Sudan to barbarism; it would be a menace

[038-160]to the safety of Egypt herself. To attempt to protect Egypt against the

[038-161]Mahdi by fortifying her southern frontier was preposterous. 'You might

[038-162]as well fortify against a fever.' Arabia, Syria, the whole Mohammedan

[038-163]world, would be shaken by the Mahdi's advance. 'In self-defence,' Gordon

[038-164]declared to Mr. Stead, the policy of evacuation cannot possibly be

[038-165]justified.' The true policy was obvious. A strong man--Sir Samuel Baker,

[038-166]perhaps--must be sent to Khartoum, with a large contingent of Indian and

[038-167]Turkish troops and with two millions of money. He would very soon

[038-168]overpower the Mahdi, whose forces would 'fall to pieces of themselves'.

[038-169]For in Gordon's opinion it was 'an entire mistake to regard the Mahdi as

[038-170]in any sense a religious leader'; he would collapse as soon as he was

[038-171]face to face with an English general. Then the distant regions of Darfur

[038-172]and Equatoria could once more be occupied; their original Sultans could

[038-173]be reinstated; the whole country would be placed under civilised rule;

[038-174]and the slave-trade would be finally abolished. These were the views

[038-175]which Gordon publicly expressed on January 9th and on January 14th; and

[038-176]it certainly seems strange that on January 10th and on January 14th,

[038-177]Lord Granville should have proposed, without a word of consultation with

[038-178]Gordon himself, to send him on a mission which involved, not the

[038-179]reconquest, but the abandonment of the Sudan; Gordon, indeed, when he

[038-180]was actually approached by Lord Wolseley, had apparently agreed to

[038-181]become the agent of a policy which was exactly the reverse of his own.

[038-182]No doubt, too, it is possible for a subordinate to suppress his private

[038-183]convictions and to carry out loyally, in spite of them, the orders of

[038-184]his superiors. But how rare are the qualities of self-control and wisdom

[038-185]which such a subordinate must possess! And how little reason there was

[038-186]to think that General Gordon possessed them!
