[006-001]The same entrainment which carried Ward away when he sat down to a piano

[006-002]possessed him whenever he embarked on a religious discussion. 'The thing

[006-003]that was utterly abhorrent to him,' said one of his friends, 'was to

[006-004]stop short.' Given the premises, he would follow out their implications

[006-005]with the mercilessness of a medieval monk, and when he had reached the

[006-006]last limits of argument, be ready to maintain whatever propositions he

[006-007]might find there with his dying breath. He had the extreme innocence of

[006-008]a child and a mathematician. Captivated by the glittering eye of [[john-henry-newman|Newman]],

[006-009]he swallowed whole the supernatural conception of the universe which

[006-010]Newman had evolved, accepted it as a fundamental premise, and 'began at

[006-011]once to deduce from it whatsoever there might be to be deduced.' His

[006-012]very first deductions included irrefutable proofs of (I) God's

[006-013]particular providence for individuals; (2) the real efficacy of

[006-014]intercessory prayer; (3) the reality of our communion with the saints

[006-015]departed; (4) the constant presence and assistance of the angels of God.

[006-016]Later on he explained mathematically the importance of the Ember Days:

[006-017]'Who can tell,' he added, 'the degree of blessing lost to us in this

[006-018]land by neglecting, as we alone of Christian Churches do neglect, these

[006-019]holy days?' He then proceeded to convict the Reformers, not only of

[006-020]rebellion, but'--for my own part I see not how we can avoid adding--of

[006-021]perjury.' Every day his arguments became more extreme, more rigorously

[006-022]exact, and more distressing to his master. Newman was in the position of

[006-023]a cautious commander-in-chief being hurried into an engagement against

[006-024]his will by a dashing cavalry officer. Ward forced him forward step by

[006-025]step towards-no! he could not bear it; he shuddered and drew back. But

[006-026]it was of no avail. In vain did Keble and Pusey wring their hands and

[006-027]stretch forth their pleading arms to their now vanishing brother. The

[006-028]fatal moment was fast approaching. Ward at last published a devastating

[006-029]book in which he proved conclusively, by a series of syllogisms, that

[006-030]the only proper course for the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] was to repent in

[006-031]sackcloth and ashes her separation from the Communion of [[rome|Rome]]. The

[006-032]reckless author was deprived of his degree by an outraged University,

[006-033]and a few weeks later was received into the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]].

[006-034]Newman, in a kind of despair, had flung himself into the labours of

[006-035]historical compilation. His views of history had changed since the days

[006-036]when, as an undergraduate, he had feasted on the worldly pages of

[006-037]Gibbon.

[006-038]'Revealed religion,' he now thought, 'furnishes facts to other sciences,

[006-039]which those sciences, left to themselves, would never reach. Thus, in

[006-040]the science of history, the preservation of our race in Noah's Ark is an

[006-041]historical fact, which history never would arrive at without

[006-042]revelation.'

[006-043]With these principles to guide him, he plunged with his disciples into a

[006-044]prolonged study of the English Saints. Biographies soon appeared of St.

[006-045]Bega, St. Adamnan, St. Gundleus, St. Guthlake, Brother Drithelm, St.

[006-046]Amphibalus, St. Wuistan, St. Ebba, St. Neot, St. Ninian, and Cunibert

[006-047]the Hermit. Their austerities, their virginity, and their miraculous

[006-048]powers were described in detail. The public learned with astonishment

[006-049]that St Ninian had turned a staff into a tree; that St. German had

[006-050]stopped a cock from crowing, and that a child had been raised from the

[006-051]dead to convert St. Helier. The series has subsequently been continued

[006-052]by a more modern writer whose relation of the history of the blessed St.

[006-053]Mael contains, perhaps, even more matter for edification than Newman's

[006-054]biographies.

[006-055]At the time, indeed, those works caused considerable scandal. Clergymen

[006-056]denounced them in pamphlets. St. Cuthbert was described by his

[006-057]biographer as having 'carried the jealousy of women, characteristic of

[006-058]all the saints, to an extraordinary pitch'. An example was given,

[006-059]whenever he held a spiritual conversation with St Ebba, he was careful

[006-060]to spend the ensuing ours of darkness 'in prayer, up to his neck in

[006-061]water'. 'Persons who invent such tales,' wrote one indignant

[006-062]commentator, 'cast very grave and just suspicions on the purity of their

[006-063]own minds. And young persons, who talk and think in this way, are in

[006-064]extreme danger of falling into sinful habits. As to the volumes before

[006-065]us, the authors have, in their fanatical panegyrics of virginity, made

[006-066]use of language downright profane.'

[006-067]One of the disciples at [[littlemore|Littlemore]] was James Anthony Froude, the younger

[006-068]brother of Hurrell, and it fell to his lot to be responsible for the

[006-069]biography of St. Neot. While he was composing it, he began to feel some

[006-070]qualms. Saints who lighted fires with icicles, changed bandits into

[006-071]wolves, and floated across the Irish Channel on altar-stones, produced a

[006-072]disturbing effect on his historical conscience. But he had promised his

[006-073]services to Newman, and he determined to carry through the work in the

[006-074]spirit in which he had begun it. He did so; but he thought it proper to

[006-075]add the following sentence by way of conclusion: 'This is all, and

[006-076]indeed rather more than all, that is known to men of the blessed St.

[006-077]Neot; but not more than is known to the angels in heaven.'

[006-078]Meanwhile, the English Roman Catholics were growing impatient; was the

[006-079]great conversion never coming, for which they had prayed so fervently

[006-080]and so long? Dr. Wiseman, at the head of them, was watching and waiting

[006-081]with special eagerness. His hand was held out under the ripening fruit;

[006-082]the delicious morsel seemed to be trembling on its stalk; and yet it did

[006-083]not fall. At last, unable to bear the suspense any longer, he dispatched

[006-084]to Littlemore Father Smith, an old pupil of Newman's, who had lately

[006-085]joined the Roman communion, with instructions that he should do his

[006-086]best, under cover of a simple visit of friendship, to discover how the

[006-087]land lay. Father Smith was received somewhat coldly, and the

[006-088]conversation ran entirely on topics which had nothing to do with

[006-089]religion. When the company separated before dinner, he was beginning to

[006-090]think that his errand had been useless; but, on their reassembling, he

[006-091]suddenly noticed that Newman had changed his trousers, and that the

[006-092]colour of the pair which he was now wearing was grey. At the earliest

[006-093]moment, the emissary rushed back post-haste to Dr. Wiseman. 'All is

[006-094]well,' he exclaimed; 'Newman no longer considers that he is in Anglican

[006-095]orders." Praise be to God!' answered Dr Wiseman. 'But how do you know?'

[006-096]Father Smith described what he had seen. 'Oh, is that all? My dear

[006-097]father, how can you be so foolish?' But Father Smith was not to be

[006-098]shaken. 'I know the man,' he said, and I know what it means. 'Newman will

[006-099]come, and he will come soon.'

[006-100]And Father Smith was right. A few weeks later, Newman suddenly slipped

[006-101]off to a priest, and all was over. Perhaps he would have hesitated

[006-102]longer still, if he could have foreseen how he was to pass the next

[006-103]thirty years of his unfortunate existence; but the future was hidden,

[006-104]and all that was certain was that the past had gone forever, and that

[006-105]his eyes would rest no more upon the snapdragons of Trinity.

[006-106]The [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] was now ended. The University breathed such a sigh

[006-107]of relief as usually follows the difficult expulsion of a hard piece of

[006-108]matter from a living organism, and actually began to attend to

[006-109]education. As for the Church of England, she had tasted blood, and it

[006-110]was clear that she would never again be content with a vegetable diet.

[006-111]Her clergy, however, maintained their reputation for judicious

[006-112]compromise, for they followed Newman up to the very point beyond which

[006-113]his conclusions were logical, and, while they intoned, confessed, swung

[006-114]incense, and burned candles with the exhilaration of converts, they yet

[006-115]managed to do so with a subtle nuance which showed that they had nothing

[006-116]to do with Rome. Various individuals underwent more violent changes.

[006-117]Several had preceded Newman into the Roman fold; among others an unhappy

[006-118]Mr. Sibthorpe, who subsequently changed his mind, and returned to the

[006-119]Church of his fathers, and then--perhaps it was only natural--changed

[006-120]his mind again. Many more followed Newman, and Dr. Wiseman was

[006-121]particularly pleased by the conversion of a Mr. Morris, who, as he said,

[006-122]was 'the author of the essay, which won the prize on the best method of

[006-123]proving Christianity to the Hindus'. Hurrell Froude had died before

[006-124]Newman had read the fatal article on St. Augustine; but his brother,

[006-125]James Anthony, together with Arthur Clough, the poet, went through an

[006-126]experience which was more distressing in those days than it has since

[006-127]become; they lost their faith. With this difference, however, that while

[006-128]in Froude's case the loss of his faith turned out to be rather like the

[006-129]loss of a heavy portmanteau, which one afterwards discovers to have been

[006-130]full of old rags and brickbats, Clough was made so uneasy by the loss of

[006-131]his that he went on looking for it everywhere as long as he lived; but

[006-132]somehow he never could find it. On the other hand, Keble and Pusey

[006-133]continued for the rest of their lives to dance in an exemplary manner

[006-134]upon the tight-rope of High Anglicanism; in such an exemplary manner,

[006-135]indeed, that the tight-rope has its dancers still.

[006-136]IV

[006-137]MANNING was now thirty-eight, and it was clear that he was the rising

[006-138]man in the Church of England. He had many powerful connections: he was

[006-139]the brother-in-law of Samuel Wilberforce, who had been lately made a

[006-140]bishop; he was a close friend of [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]], who was a Cabinet

[006-141]Minister; and he was becoming well known in the influential circles of

[006-142]society in London. His talent for affairs was recognised not only in the

[006-143]Church, but in the world at large, and he busied himself with matters of

[006-144]such varied scope as National Education, the administration of the Poor

[006-145]Law, and the Employment of Women. Mr. Gladstone kept up an intimate

[006-146]correspondence with him on these and on other subjects, mingling in his

[006-147]letters the details of practical statesmanship with the speculations of

[006-148]a religious thinker. 'Sir James Graham,' he wrote, in a discussion of

[006-149]the bastardy clauses of the Poor Law, 'is much pleased with the tone of

[006-150]your two communications. He is disposed, without putting an end to the

[006-151]application of the workhouse test against the mother, to make the remedy

[006-152]against the putative father "real and effective" for expenses incurred

[006-153]in the workhouse. I am not enough acquainted to know whether it would be

[006-154]advisable to go further. You have not proposed it; and I am disposed to

[006-155]believe that only with a revived and improved discipline in the Church

[006-156]can we hope for any generally effective check upon lawless lust.' 'I

[006-157]agree with you EMINENTLY,' he writes, in a later letter, 'in your

[006-158]doctrine of FILTRATION. But it sometimes occurs to me, though the

[006-159]question may seem a strange one, how far was the Reformation, but

[006-160]especially the Continental Reformation, designed by God, in the region

[006-161]of final causes, for that purification of the Roman Church which it has

[006-162]actually realised?'

[006-163]In his archdeaconry, Manning lived to the full the active life of a

[006-164]country clergyman. His slim, athletic figure was seen everywhere in the

[006-165]streets of Chichester, or on the lawns of the neighbouring rectories, or

[006-166]galloping over the downs in breeches and gaiters, or cutting brilliant

[006-167]figures on the ice. He was an excellent judge of horse-flesh, and the

[006-168]pair of greys which drew his hooded phaeton so swiftly through the lanes

[006-169]were the admiration of the county. His features were already beginning

[006-170]to assume their ascetic cast, but the spirit of youth had not yet fled

[006-171]from them, so that he seemed to combine the attractions of dignity and

[006-172]grace. He was a good talker, a sympathetic listener, a man who

[006-173]understood the difficult art of preserving all the vigour of a manly

[006-174]character and yet never giving offence. No wonder that his sermons drew

[006-175]crowds, no wonder that his spiritual advice was sought for eagerly by an

[006-176]ever-growing group of penitents; no wonder that men would say, when his

[006-177]name was mentioned, 'Oh, Manning! No power on earth can keep HIM from a

[006-178]bishopric!'
