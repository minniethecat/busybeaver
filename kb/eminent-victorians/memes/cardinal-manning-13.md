[013-001]'Dear Monsignor Talbot,' he wrote in reply, 'I have received your

[013-002]letter, inviting me to preach in your Church at [[rome|Rome]] to an audience of

[013-003]Protestants more educated than could ever be the case in England.

[013-004]'However, Birmingham people have souls; and I have neither taste nor

[013-005]talent for the sort of work which you cut out for me. And I beg to

[013-006]decline your offer.

[013-007]I am, yours truly,

[013-008]JOHN H. NEWMAN.'

[013-009]Such words were not the words of wisdom. It is easy to imagine the

[013-010]feelings of Monsignor Talbot. '[[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s work none here can understand,'

[013-011]he burst out to his friend. 'Poor man, by living almost ever since he

[013-012]has been a Catholic, surrounded by a set of inferior men who idolise

[013-013]him, I do not think he has ever acquired the Catholic instincts.' As for

[013-014]his views on the Temporal Power--'well, people said that he had actually

[013-015]sent a subscription to Garibaldi. Yes, the man was incomprehensible,

[013-016]heretical, dangerous; he was "uncatholic and unchristian."' Monsignor

[013-017]Talbot even trembled for the position of Manning in England.

[013-018]'I am afraid that the old school of Catholics will rally round Newman in

[013-019]opposition to you and Rome. Stand firm, do not yield a bit in the line

[013-020]you have taken. As I have promised, I shall stand by you. You will have

[013-021]battles to fight because every Englishman is naturally anti-Roman. To be

[013-022]Roman is an effort to an Englishman an effort. Dr. Newman is more English than

[013-023]the English. His spirit must be crushed.'

[013-024]His spirit must be crushed! Certainly there could be no doubt of that.

[013-025]'What you write about Dr Newman,' Manning replied, 'is true. Whether he

[013-026]knows it or not, he has become the centre of those who hold low views

[013-027]about the Holy See, are anti-Roman, cold and silent, to say no more,

[013-028]about the Temporal Power; national, English, critical of Catholic

[013-029]devotions, and always on the lower side.... You will take care,' he

[013-030]concluded, 'that things are correctly known and understood where you

[013-031]are.'

[013-032]The confederates matured their plans. While Newman was making his

[013-033]arrangements for the [[oxford-university|Oxford]] Oratory, Cardinal Reisach visited London.

[013-034]'Cardinal Reisach has just left,' wrote Manning to Monsignor Talbot: 'he

[013-035]has seen and understands all that is going on in England.' But Newman

[013-036]had no suspicions. It was true that persistent rumours of his

[013-037]unorthodoxy and his anti-Roman leanings had begun to float about, and

[013-038]these rumours had been traced to Rome. But what were rumours? Then, too,

[013-039]Newman found out that Cardinal Reisach had been to Oxford without his

[013-040]knowledge, and had inspected the land for the Oratory. That seemed odd;

[013-041]but all doubts were set at rest by the arrival from Propaganda of an

[013-042]official ratification of his scheme. There would be nothing but plain

[013-043]sailing now. Newman was almost happy; radiant visions came into his mind

[013-044]of a wonderful future in Oxford, the gradual growth of Catholic

[013-045]principles, the decay of liberalism, the inauguration of a second Oxford

[013-046]Movement, the conversion--who knows?--of Mark Pattison, the triumph of

[013-047]the Church.... 'Earlier failures do not matter now,' he exclaimed to a

[013-048]friend. 'I see that I have been reserved by God for this.'

[013-049]Just then a long blue envelope was brought into the room. Newman opened

[013-050]it. 'All is over,' he said, 'I am not allowed to go.' The envelope

[013-051]contained a letter from the Bishop announcing that, together with the

[013-052]formal permission for an Oratory at Oxford, Propaganda had issued a

[013-053]secret instruction to the effect that Newman himself was by no means to

[013-054]reside there. If he showed signs of doing so, he was blandly and suavely

[013-055]('blande suaviterque' were the words of the Latin instrument) to be

[013-056]prevented. And now the secret instruction had come into

[013-057]operation--blande suaviterque: Dr. Newman's spirit had been crushed.

[013-058]His friends made some gallant efforts to retrieve the situation; but, it

[013-059]was in vain. Father St. John hurried to Rome and the indignant laity of

[013-060]England, headed by Lord Edward Howard, the guardian of the young Duke of

[013-061]Norfolk, seized the opportunity of a particularly virulent anonymous

[013-062]attack upon Newman, to send him an address in which they expressed their

[013-063]feeling that 'every blow that touches you inflicts a wound upon the

[013-064][[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] in this country'. The only result was an outburst of

[013-065]redoubled fury upon the part of Monsignor Talbot. The address, he

[013-066]declared, was an insult to the Holy See. 'What is the province of the

[013-067]laity?' he interjected. 'To hunt, to shoot, to entertain. These matters

[013-068]they understand, but to meddle with ecclesiastical matters they have no

[013-069]right at all.' Once more he warned Manning to be careful.

[013-070]'Dr. Newman is the most dangerous man in England, and you will see that

[013-071]he will make use of the laity against your Grace. You must not be afraid

[013-072]of him. It will require much prudence, but you must be firm. The Holy

[013-073]Father still places his confidence in you; but if you yield and do not

[013-074]fight the battle of the Holy See against the detestable spirit growing

[013-075]up in England, he will begin to regret Cardinal Wiseman, who knew how to

[013-076]keep the laity in order.' Manning had no thought of 'yielding'; but, he

[013-077]pointed out to his agitated friend that an open conflict between himself

[013-078]and Newman would be 'as great a scandal to the Church in England, and as

[013-079]great a victory to the Anglicans, as could be'. He would act quietly,

[013-080]and there would be no more difficulty. The Bishops were united, and the

[013-081]Church was sound.

[013-082]On this, Monsignor Talbot hurried to Father St. John's lodgings in Rome

[013-083]to express his regret at the misunderstanding that had arisen, to wonder

[013-084]how it could possibly have occurred, and to hope that Dr. Newman might

[013-085]consent to be made a Protonotary Apostolic. That was all the

[013-086]satisfaction that Father St. John was to obtain from his visit to Rome.

[013-087]A few weeks later, the scheme of the Oxford Oratory was finally quashed.

[013-088]When all was over, Manning thought that the time had come for a

[013-089]reconciliation. He made advances through a common friend; what had he

[013-090]done, he asked, to offend Dr. Newman? Letters passed, and, naturally

[013-091]enough, they only widened the breach. Newman was not the man to be

[013-092]polite.

[013-093]'I can only repeat,' he wrote at last, 'what I said when you last heard

[013-094]from me. I do not know whether I am on my head or my heels when I have

[013-095]active relations with you. In spite of my friendly feelings, this is the

[013-096]judgment of my intellect.' 'Meanwhile,' he concluded, 'I propose to say

[013-097]seven masses for your intention amid the difficulties and anxieties of

[013-098]your ecclesiastical duties.'

[013-099]And Manning could only return the compliment.

[013-100]At about this time, the Curate of [[littlemore|Littlemore]] had a singular experience.

[013-101]As he was passing by the Church he noticed an old man, very poorly

[013-102]dressed in an old grey coat with the collar turned up, leaning over the

[013-103]lych gate, in floods of tears. He was apparently in great trouble, and

[013-104]his hat was pulled down over his eyes as if he wished to hide his

[013-105]features. For a moment, however, he turned towards the Curate, who was

[013-106]suddenly struck by something familiar in the face. Could it be--? A

[013-107]photograph hung over the Curate's mantelpiece of the man who had made

[013-108]Littlemore famous by his sojourn there more than twenty years ago--he

[013-109]had never seen the original; but now, was it possible--? He looked

[013-110]again, and he could doubt no longer. It was Dr. Newman. He sprang

[013-111]forward, with proffers of assistance. Could he be of any use? 'Oh no,

[013-112]no!' was the reply. 'Oh no, no!' But the Curate felt that he could not

[013-113]run away and leave so eminent a character in such distress. 'Was it not

[013-114]Dr. Newman he had the honour of addressing?' he asked, with all the

[013-115]respect and sympathy at his command. 'Was there nothing that could be

[013-116]done?' But the old man hardly seemed to understand what was being said

[013-117]to him. 'Oh no, no!' he repeated, with the tears streaming down his

[013-118]face, 'Oh no, no!'

[013-119]VII

[013-120]MEANWHILE, a remarkable problem was absorbing the attention of the

[013-121]Catholic Church. Once more, for a moment, the eyes of all Christendom

[013-122]were fixed upon Rome. The temporal Power of the Pope had now almost

[013-123]vanished; but, as his worldly dominions steadily diminished, the

[013-124]spiritual pretensions of the Holy Father no less steadily increased. For

[013-125]seven centuries the immaculate conception of the Virgin had been highly

[013-126]problematical; Pio Nono spoke, and the doctrine became an article of

[013-127]faith. A few years later, the Court of Rome took another step: a

[013-128]Syllabus Errorum was issued, in which all the favourite beliefs of the

[013-129]modern world--the rights of democracies, the claims of science, the

[013-130]sanctity of free speech, the principles of toleration--were

[013-131]categorically denounced, and their supporters abandoned to the Divine

[013-132]wrath.

[013-133]Yet it was observed that the modern world proceeded as before. Something

[013-134]more drastic appeared to be necessary--some bold and striking measure

[013-135]which should concentrate the forces of the faithful, and confound their

[013-136]enemies. The tremendous doctrine of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]], beloved of all

[013-137]good Catholics, seemed to offer just the opening that was required. Let

[013-138]that doctrine be proclaimed, with the assent of the whole Church, an

[013-139]article of faith, and, in the face of such an affirmation, let the

[013-140]modern world do its worst! Accordingly, a General Council--the first to

[013-141]be held since the Council of Trent more than 300 years before--was

[013-142]summoned to the Vatican, for the purpose, so it was announced, of

[013-143]providing 'an adequate remedy to the disorders, intellectual and moral,

[013-144]of Christendom'. The programme might seem a large one, even for a

[013-145]General Council; but everyone knew what it meant.

[013-146]Everyone, however, was not quite of one mind. There were those to whom

[013-147]even the mysteries of infallibility caused some searchings of heart. It

[013-148]was true, no doubt, that Our Lord, by saying to Peter, 'Thou art Cephas,

[013-149]which is by interpretation a stone', thereby endowed that Apostle with

[013-150]the supreme and full primacy and principality over the Universal

[013-151]Catholic Church; it was equally certain that Peter afterwards became the

[013-152]Bishop of Rome; nor could it be doubted that the Roman Pontiff was his

[013-153]successor. Thus it followed directly that the Roman Pontiff was the

[013-154]head, heart, mind, and tongue of the Catholic Church; and moreover, it

[013-155]was plain that when Our Lord prayed for Peter that his faith should not

[013-156]fail, that prayer implied the doctrine of Papal Infallibility. All these

[013-157]things were obvious, and yet--and yet--might not the formal declaration

[013-158]of such truths in the year of his grace 1870 be, to say the least of it,

[013-159]inopportune? Might it not come as an offence, as a scandal even, to

[013-160]those unacquainted with the niceties of Catholic dogma? Such were the

[013-161]uneasy reflections of grave and learned ecclesiastics and theologians in

[013-162]England, France, and Germany. Newman was more than usually upset;

[013-163]Monseigneur Dupanloup was disgusted; and Dr. Dollinger prepared himself

[013-164]for resistance. It was clear that there would be a disaffected minority

[013-165]at the Council.

[013-166]Catholic apologists have often argued that the Pope's claim to

[013-167]infallibility implies no more than the necessary claim of every ruler,

[013-168]of every government, to the right of supreme command. In England, for

[013-169]instance, the Estates of the Realm exercise an absolute authority in

[013-170]secular matters; no one questions this authority, no one suggests that

[013-171]it is absurd or exorbitant; in other words, by general consent the

[013-172]Estates of the Realm are, within their sphere, infallible. Why,

[013-173]therefore, should the Pope, within his sphere--the sphere of the

[013-174]Catholic Church--be denied a similar infallibility? If there is nothing

[013-175]monstrous in an Act of Parliament laying down what all men shall do, why

[013-176]should there be anything monstrous in a Papal Encyclical laying down

[013-177]what all men shall believe? The argument is simple; in fact, it is too

[013-178]simple; for it takes for granted the very question which is in dispute.

[013-179]Is there indeed no radical and essential distinction between supremacy

[013-180]and infallibility? Between the right of a Borough Council to regulate

[013-181]the traffic and the right of the Vicar of Christ to decide upon the

[013-182]qualifications for Everlasting Bliss?
