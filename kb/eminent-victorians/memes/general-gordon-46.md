[046-001]'Now MARK THIS, if the Expeditionary Force, and I ask for no more than

[046-002]200 men, does not come in ten days, the town may fall; and I have done

[046-003]my best for the honour of our country. Good-bye.--C. G. GORDON.

[046-004]'You send me no information, though you have lots of money. C. G. G.'

[046-005]To his sister Augusta he was more explicit.

[046-006]'I decline to agree,' he told her, 'that the expedition comes for my

[046-007]relief; it comes for the relief of the garrisons, which I failed to

[046-008]accomplish. I expect Her Majesty's Government are in a precious rage

[046-009]with me for holding out and forcing their hand.'

[046-010]The admission is significant. And then came the final adieux.

[046-011]'This may be the last letter you will receive from me, for we are on our

[046-012]last legs, owing to the delay of the expedition. However, God rules all,

[046-013]and, as He will rule to His glory and our welfare, His will be done. I

[046-014]fear, owing to circumstances, that my affairs are pecuniarily not over

[046-015]bright ... your affectionate brother, C. G. GORDON.

[046-016]'P.S. I am quite happy, thank God, and, like Lawrence, I have TRIED to

[046-017]do my duty.'

[046-018]The delay of the expedition was even more serious than Gordon had

[046-019]supposed. Lord Wolseley had made the most elaborate preparations. He had

[046-020]collected together a picked army of 10,000 of the finest British troops;

[046-021]he had arranged a system of river transports with infinite care. For it

[046-022]was his intention to take no risks; he would advance in force up the

[046-023]Nile; he had determined that the fate of Gordon should not depend upon

[046-024]the dangerous hazards of a small and hasty exploit. There is no

[046-025]doubt--in view of the opposition which the relieving force actually met

[046-026]with--that his decision was a wise one; but unfortunately, he had

[046-027]miscalculated some of the essential elements in the situation. When his

[046-028]preparations were at last complete, it was found that the Nile had sunk

[046-029]so low that the flotillas, over which so much care had been lavished,

[046-030]and upon which depended the whole success of the campaign, would be

[046-031]unable to surmount the cataracts. At the same time--it was by then the

[046-032]middle of November--a message arrived from Gordon indicating that

[046-033][[khartoum|Khartoum]] was in serious straits. It was clear that an immediate advance

[046-034]was necessary; the river route was out of the question; a swift dash

[046-035]across the desert was the only possible expedient after all. But no

[046-036]preparations for land transport had been made; weeks elapsed before a

[046-037]sufficient number of camels could be collected; and more weeks before

[046-038]those collected were trained for military march. It was not until

[046-039]December 30th--more than a fortnight after the last entry in Gordon's

[046-040]Journal--that Sir Herbert Stewart, at the head of 1,100 British troops,

[046-041]was able to leave Korti on his march towards Metemmah, 170 miles across

[046-042]the desert. His advance was slow, and it was tenaciously disputed by,

[046-043]the Mahdi's forces. There was a desperate engagement on January 17th at

[046-044]the wells of Abu Klea; the British square was broken; for a moment

[046-045]victory hung in the balance; but the Arabs were repulsed. On the 19th

[046-046]there was another furiously contested fight, in which Sir Herbert

[046-047]Stewart was killed. On the 21st, the force, now diminished by over 250

[046-048]casualties, reached Metemmah. Three days elapsed in reconnoitering the

[046-049]country, and strengthening the position of the camp. On the 24th, Sir

[046-050]Charles Wilson, who had succeeded to the command, embarked on the

[046-051]Bordeen, and started up the river for Khartoum. On the following

[046-052]evening, the vessel struck on a rock, causing a further delay of

[046-053]twenty-four hours. It was not until January 28th that Sir Charles

[046-054]Wilson, arriving under a heavy fire within sight of Khartoum, saw that

[046-055]the Egyptian flag was not flying from the roof of the palace. The signs

[046-056]of ruin and destruction on every hand showed clearly enough that the

[046-057]town had fallen. The relief expedition was two days late.

[046-058]The details of what passed within Khartoum during the last weeks of the

[046-059]siege are unknown to us. In the diary of Bordeini Bey, a Levantine

[046-060]merchant, we catch a few glimpses of the final stages of the

[046-061]catastrophe--of the starving populace, the exhausted garrison, the

[046-062]fluctuations of despair and hope, the dauntless energy of the

[046-063]Governor-General. Still he worked on, indefatigably, apportioning

[046-064]provisions, collecting ammunition, consulting with the townspeople,

[046-065]encouraging the soldiers. His hair had suddenly turned quite white. Late

[046-066]one evening, Bordeini Bey went to visit him in the palace, which was

[046-067]being bombarded by the Mahdi's cannon. The high building, brilliantly

[046-068]lighted up, afforded an excellent mark. As the shot came whistling

[046-069]around the windows, the merchant suggested that it would be advisable to

[046-070]stop them up with boxes full of sand. Upon this, Gordon Pasha became

[046-071]enraged.

[046-072]'He called up the guard, and gave them orders to shoot me if I moved; he

[046-073]then brought a very large lantern which would hold twenty-four candles.

[046-074]He and I then put the candles into the sockets, placed the lantern on

[046-075]the table in front of the window, lit the candles, and then we sat down

[046-076]at the table. The Pasha then said, "When God was portioning out fear to

[046-077]all the people in the world, at last it came to my turn, and there was

[046-078]no fear left to give me. Go, tell all the people in Khartoum that Gordon

[046-079]fears nothing, for God has created him without fear."'

[046-080]On January 5th, Omdurman, a village on the opposite bank of the Nile,

[046-081]which had hitherto been occupied by the besieged, was taken by the

[046-082]Arabs. The town was now closely surrounded, and every chance of

[046-083]obtaining fresh supplies was cut off. The famine became terrible; dogs,

[046-084]donkeys, skins, gum, palm fibre, were devoured by the desperate

[046-085]inhabitants. The soldiers stood on the fortifications like pieces of

[046-086]wood. Hundreds died of hunger daily: their corpses filled the streets;

[046-087]and the survivors had not the strength to bury the dead. On the 20th,

[046-088]the news of the battle of Abu Klea reached Khartoum. The English were

[046-089]coming at last. Hope rose; every morning the Governor-General assured

[046-090]the townspeople that one day more would see the end of their sufferings;

[046-091]and night after night his words were proved untrue.

[046-092]On the 23rd, a rumour spread that a spy had arrived with letters, and

[046-093]that the English army was at hand. A merchant found a piece of newspaper

[046-094]lying in the road, in which it was stated that the strength of the

[046-095]relieving forces was 15,000 men. For a moment, hope flickered up again,

[046-096]only to relapse once more. The rumour, the letters, the printed paper,

[046-097]all had been contrivances of Gordon to inspire the garrison with the

[046-098]courage to hold out. On the 25th, it was obvious that the Arabs were

[046-099]preparing an attack, and a deputation of the principal inhabitants

[046-100]waited upon the Governor-General. But he refused to see them; Bordeini

[046-101]Bey was alone admitted to his presence. He was sitting on a divan, and,

[046-102]as Bordeini Bey came into the room, he snatched the fez from his head

[046-103]and flung it from him.

[046-104]'What more can I say?' he exclaimed, in a voice such as the merchant had

[046-105]never heard before. 'The people will no longer believe me. I have told

[046-106]them over and over again that help would be here, but it has never come,

[046-107]and now they must see I tell them lies. I can do nothing more. Go, and

[046-108]collect all the people you can on the lines, and make a good stand. Now

[046-109]leave me to smoke these cigarettes.'

[046-110]Bordeini Bey knew then, he tells us, that Gordon Pasha was in despair.

[046-111]He left the room, having looked upon the Governor-General for the last

[046-112]time.

[046-113]When the English force reached Metemmah, the Mahdi, who had originally

[046-114]intended to reduce Khartoum to surrender through starvation, decided to

[046-115]attempt its capture by assault. The receding Nile had left one portion

[046-116]of the town's circumference undefended; as the river withdrew, the

[046-117]rampart had crumbled; a broad expanse of mud was left between the wall

[046-118]and the water, and the soldiers, overcome by hunger and the lassitude of

[046-119]hopelessness, had trusted to the morass to protect them, and neglected

[046-120]to repair the breach. Early on the morning of the 26th, the Arabs

[046-121]crossed the river at this point. The mud, partially dried up, presented

[046-122]no obstacle; nor did the ruined fortification, feebly manned by some

[046-123]half-dying troops. Resistance was futile, and it was scarcely offered:

[046-124]the Mahdi's army swarmed into Khartoum. Gordon had long debated with

[046-125]himself what his action should be at the supreme moment. 'I shall never

[046-126](D.V.),' he had told Sir Evelyn Baring, 'be taken alive.' He had had

[046-127]gunpowder put into the cellars of the palace, so that the whole building

[046-128]might, at a moment's notice, be blown into the air. But then misgivings

[046-129]had come upon him; was it not his duty 'to maintain the faith, and, if

[046-130]necessary, to suffer for it'?--to remain a tortured and humiliated

[046-131]witness of his Lord in the Mahdi's chains? The blowing up of the palace

[046-132]would have, he thought, 'more or less the taint of suicide', would be,

[046-133]in a way, taking things out of God's hands'. He remained undecided; and

[046-134]meanwhile, to be ready for every contingency, he kept one of his little

[046-135]armoured vessels close at hand on the river, with steam up, day and

[046-136]night, to transport him, if so he should decide, southward, through the

[046-137]enemy, to the recesses of Equatoria. The sudden appearance of the Arabs,

[046-138]the complete collapse of the defence, saved him the necessity of making

[046-139]up his mind. He had been on the roof, in his dressing-gown, when the

[046-140]attack began; and he had only time to hurry to his bedroom, to slip on a

[046-141]white uniform, and to seize up a sword and a revolver, before the

[046-142]foremost of the assailants were in the palace. The crowd was led by four

[046-143]of the fiercest of the Mahdi's followers--tall and swarthy Dervishes,

[046-144]splendid in their many-coloured jibbehs, their great swords drawn from

[046-145]their scabbards of brass and velvet, their spears flourishing above

[046-146]their heads. Gordon met them at the top of the staircase. For a moment,

[046-147]there was a deathly pause, while he stood in silence, surveying his

[046-148]antagonists. Then it is said that Taha Shahin, the Dongolawi, cried in a

[046-149]loud voice, 'Mala' oun el yom yomek!' (O cursed one, your time is come),

[046-150]and plunged his spear into the Englishman's body. His only reply was a

[046-151]gesture of contempt. Another spear transfixed him; he fell, and the

[046-152]swords of the three other Dervishes instantly hacked him to death. Thus,

[046-153]if we are to believe the official chroniclers, in the dignity of

[046-154]unresisting disdain, [[general-gordon|General Gordon]] met his end. But it is only fitting

[046-155]that the last moments of one whose whole life was passed in

[046-156]contradiction should be involved in mystery and doubt. Other witnesses

[046-157]told a very different story. The man whom they saw die was not a saint

[046-158]but a warrior. With intrepidity, with skill, with desperation, he flew

[046-159]at his enemies. When his pistol was exhausted, he fought on with his

[046-160]sword; he forced his way almost to the bottom of the staircase; and,

[046-161]among, a heap of corpses, only succumbed at length to the sheer weight

[046-162]of the multitudes against him.

[046-163]That morning, while Slatin Pasha was sitting in his chains in the camp

[046-164]at Omdurman, he saw a group of Arabs approaching, one of whom was

[046-165]carrying something wrapped up in a cloth. As the group passed him, they

[046-166]stopped for a moment, and railed at him in savage mockery. Then the

[046-167]cloth was lifted, and he saw before him Gordon's head. The trophy was

[046-168]taken to the Mahdi: at last the two fanatics had indeed met face to

[046-169]face. The Mahdi ordered the head to be fixed between the branches of a

[046-170]tree in the public highway, and all who passed threw stones at it. The

[046-171]hawks of the desert swept and circled about it--those very hawks which

[046-172]the blue eyes had so often watched.
