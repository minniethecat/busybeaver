## Overview

Sidney Herbert (1810–1861) was a British statesman who served as Secretary at War during the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]] and later as Secretary of State for War. He was the most important political ally and personal friend of [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]], through whom she exercised her influence on British military and sanitary reform.

> Of Miss Nightingale's friends, the most important was Sidney Herbert. He was a man upon whom the good fairies seemed to have showered, as he lay in his cradle, all their most agreeable gifts. [023-067]

## Political Career and the War Office

Herbert was at the [[war-office|War Office]] and in the Cabinet during the critical early phase of the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]]. His position enabled him to act as the vital conduit between [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]]'s ambitions for hospital reform and the machinery of government [019-113].

Strachey portrays Herbert as a figure of natural charm and ability, yet one who lacked the final measure of forceful decision. Comparing him to his successor [[lord-panmure|Lord Panmure]], Strachey wrote:

> If Lord Panmure was a bison, Sidney Herbert, no doubt, was a stag — a comely, gallant creature springing through the forest; but the alarm was given, the pack was in full cry: he was off. [023-125]

## Partnership with Florence Nightingale

Herbert's relationship with Nightingale was the defining collaboration of his public life. He was an intimate friend, convinced by personal experience in charitable work of her supreme capacity [019-114]. It was Herbert's letter requesting her services that crossed with her own letter volunteering to go to the East [019-117].

For years, Herbert saw Nightingale almost daily, for long hours together, corresponding with her incessantly when they were apart [023-103]. Nightingale sent him confidential reports from [[scutari|Scutari]] — communications that were by no means official in character [021-084].

Their partnership was not without friction. When Herbert misinterpreted her wishes, Nightingale took the extraordinary step of noting their conversation and insisting he sign the notes [021-121]. Yet this quarrel was exceptional; she continued to work alike through Herbert and his successor [[lord-panmure|Lord Panmure]] [021-124].

## The Royal Commission and Sanitary Reform

After the war, Nightingale pressed for a Royal Commission into army sanitary conditions. When it was established, she was victorious: Herbert was appointed its Chairman [024-018]. He served as her essential link, consulting her in private at every important juncture [024-071].

## Later Career and Death

Herbert later rose to Secretary of State for War [024-159], but the burdens of office and his intense involvement in Nightingale's reform agenda exhausted him. Strachey describes Nightingale oscillating between writing him a "last letter" and offering to nurse sufferers of the Indian Mutiny [024-096].

Herbert died in 1861 at the age of fifty, worn down by political life.

## Legacy

Herbert's historical significance rests on his role as the parliamentary instrument through which [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]] achieved her reforms. Strachey's portrait depicts a man of unusual personal grace who found his life's meaning in service to a woman of more formidable will.
