[014-001]There is one distinction, at any rate, which is palpable: the decisions

[014-002]of a supreme authority can be altered; those of an infallible authority

[014-003]cannot. A Borough Council may change its traffic regulations at the next

[014-004]meeting; but the Vicar of Christ, when in certain circumstances and with

[014-005]certain precautions, he has once spoken, has expressed, for all the

[014-006]ages, a part of the immutable, absolute, and eternal Truth. It is this

[014-007]that makes the papal pretensions so extraordinary and so enormous. It is

[014-008]also this that gives them their charm. Catholic apologists, when they

[014-009]try to tone down those pretensions and to explain them away, forget that

[014-010]it is in their very exorbitance that their fascination lies. If the Pope

[014-011]were indeed nothing more than a magnified Borough Councillor, we should

[014-012]hardly have heard so much of him. It is not because he satisfies the

[014-013]reason, but because he astounds it, that men abase themselves before the

[014-014]Vicar of Christ.

[014-015]And certainly the doctrine of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]] presents to the reason

[014-016]a sufficiency of stumbling-blocks. In the fourteenth century, for

[014-017]instance, the following case arose. John XXII asserted in his bull 'Cum

[014-018]inter nonnullos' that the doctrine of the poverty of Christ was

[014-019]heretical. Now, according to the light of reason, one of two things must

[014-020]follow from this--either John XXII was himself a heretic, or he was no

[014-021]Pope. For his predecessor, Nicholas III, had asserted in his bull 'Exiit

[014-022]qui seminat' that the doctrine of the poverty of Christ was the true

[014-023]doctrine, the denial of which was heresy. Thus if John XXII was right,

[014-024]Nicholas III was a heretic, and in that case Nicholas's nominations of

[014-025]Cardinals were void, and the conclave which elected John was illegal--so

[014-026]that John was no Pope, his nominations of Cardinals were void, and the

[014-027]whole Papal succession vitiated. On the other hand, if John was

[014-028]wrong--well, he was a heretic; and the same inconvenient results

[014-029]followed. And, in either case, what becomes of Papal Infallibility?

[014-030]But such crude and fundamental questions as these were not likely to

[014-031]trouble the Council. The discordant minority took another line.

[014-032]Infallibility they admitted readily enough, the infallibility, that is

[014-033]to say, of the Church; what they shrank from was the pronouncement that

[014-034]this infallibility was concentrated in the Bishop of [[rome|Rome]]. They would

[014-035]not actually deny that, as a matter of fact, it was so concentrated; but

[014-036]to declare that it was, to make the belief that it was an article of

[014-037]faith--what could be more--it was their favourite expression--more

[014-038]inopportune? In truth, the Gallican spirit still lingered among them. At

[014-039]heart, they hated the autocracy of Rome--the domination of the

[014-040]centralised Italian organisation over the whole vast body of the Church.

[014-041]They secretly hankered, even at this late hour, after some form of

[014-042]constitutional government, and they knew that the last faint vestige of

[014-043]such a dream would vanish utterly with the declaration of the

[014-044]infallibility of the Pope. It did not occur to them, apparently, that a

[014-045]constitutional Catholicism might be a contradiction in terms, and that

[014-046]the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], without the absolute dominion of the Pope, might

[014-047]resemble the play of Hamlet without the Prince of Denmark.

[014-048]Pius IX himself was troubled by doubts. 'Before I was Pope,' he

[014-049]observed, 'I believed in Papal Infallibility, now I feel it.' As for

[014-050]Manning, his certainty was no less complete than his master's. Apart

[014-051]from the Holy Ghost, his appointment to the See of Westminster had been

[014-052]due to Pio Nono's shrewd appreciation of the fact that he was the one

[014-053]man in England upon whose fidelity the Roman Government could absolutely

[014-054]rely. The voice which kept repeating 'Mettetelo li, mettetelo li' in his

[014-055]Holiness's ear, whether or not it was inspired by God, was certainly

[014-056]inspired by political sagacity. For now Manning was to show that he was

[014-057]not unworthy of the trust which had been reposed in him. He flew to Rome

[014-058]in a whirlwind of Papal enthusiasm. On the way, in Paris, he stopped for

[014-059]a moment to interview those two great props of French respectability, M.

[014-060]Guizot and M. Thiers. Both were careful not to commit themselves, but

[014-061]both were exceedingly polite. 'I am awaiting your Council,' said M.

[014-062]Guizot, 'with great anxiety. It is the last great moral power and may

[014-063]restore the peace of Europe.' M. Thiers delivered a brief harangue in

[014-064]favour of the principles of the Revolution, which, he declared, were the

[014-065]very marrow of all Frenchmen; yet, he added, he had always supported the

[014-066]Temporal Power of the Pope. 'Mais, M. Thiers,' said Manning, 'vous etes

[014-067]effectivement croyant.' 'En Dieu,' replied M. Thiers.

[014-068]The Rome which Manning reached towards the close of 1869 was still the

[014-069]Rome which, for so many centuries, had been the proud and visible apex,

[014-070]the palpitating heart, the sacred sanctuary, of the most extraordinary

[014-071]mingling of spiritual and earthly powers that the world has ever known.

[014-072]The Pope now, it is true, ruled over little more than the City

[014-073]itself--the Patrimony of St. Peter--and he ruled there less by the Grace

[014-074]of God than by the goodwill of Napoleon III; yet he was still a

[014-075]sovereign Prince, and Rome was still the capital of the Papal State; she

[014-076]was not yet the capital of Italy. The last hour of this strange dominion

[014-077]had almost struck. As if she knew that her doom was upon her, the

[014-078]Eternal City arrayed herself to meet it in all her glory.

[014-079]The whole world seemed to be gathered together within her walls. Her

[014-080]streets were filled with crowned heads and Princes of the Church, great

[014-081]ladies and great theologians, artists and friars, diplomats and

[014-082]newspaper reporters. Seven hundred bishops were there from all the

[014-083]corners of Christendom, and in all the varieties of ecclesiastical

[014-084]magnificence in falling lace and sweeping purple and flowing violet

[014-085]veils. Zouaves stood in the colonnade of St Peter's, and Papal troops

[014-086]were on the Quirinal. Cardinals passed, hatted and robed, in their

[014-087]enormous carriage of state, like mysterious painted idols. Then there

[014-088]was a sudden hush: the crowd grew thicker and expectation filled, the

[014-089]air. Yes! it was he! He was coming! The Holy Father! But first there

[014-090]appeared, mounted on a white mule and clothed in a magenta mantle, a

[014-091]grave dignitary bearing aloft a silver cross. The golden coach followed,

[014-092]drawn by six horses gorgeously caparisoned, and within, the smiling

[014-093]white-haired Pio Nono, scattering his benedictions, while the multitude

[014-094]fell upon its knees as one man. Such were the daily spectacles of

[014-095]coloured pomp and of antique solemnity, which so long as the sun was

[014-096]shining, at any rate--dazzled the onlooker into a happy forgetfulness of

[014-097]the reverse side of the Papal dispensation--the nauseating filth of the

[014-098]highways, the cattle stabled in the palaces of the great, and the fever

[014-099]flitting through the ghastly tenements of the poor.

[014-100]In St. Peter's, the North Transept had been screened off; rows of wooden

[014-101]seats had been erected covered with Brussels carpet; and upon these

[014-102]seats sat each crowned with a white mitre, the 700 Bishops in Council.

[014-103]Here all day long rolled forth, in sonorous Latin, the interminable

[014-104]periods of episcopal oratory; but it was not here that the issue of the

[014-105]Council was determined. The assembled Fathers might talk till the

[014-106]marbles of St. Peter's themselves grew weary of the reverberations; the

[014-107]fate of the Church was decided in a very different manner--by little

[014-108]knots of influential persons meeting quietly of a morning in the back

[014-109]room of some inconspicuous lodging-house, by a sunset rendezvous in the

[014-110]Borghese Gardens between a Cardinal and a Diplomatist by a whispered

[014-111]conference in an alcove at a Princess's evening party, with the gay

[014-112]world chattering all about. And, of course, on such momentous occasions

[014-113]as these, Manning was in his element. None knew those difficult ropes

[014-114]better than he; none used them with a more serviceable and yet discreet

[014-115]alacrity. In every juncture he had the right word, or the right silence;

[014-116]his influence ramified in all directions, from the Pope's audience

[014-117]chamber to the English Cabinet. 'Il Diavolo del Concilio' his enemies

[014-118]called him; and he gloried in the name.

[014-119]The real crux of the position was less ecclesiastical than diplomatic.

[014-120]The Papal Court, with its huge majority of Italian Bishops, could make

[014-121]sure enough, when it came to the point, of carrying its wishes through

[014-122]the Council; what was far more dubious was the attitude of the foreign

[014-123]Governments--especially those of France and England. The French

[014-124]Government dreaded a schism among its Catholic subjects; it disliked the

[014-125]prospect of an extension of the influence of the Pope over the mass of

[014-126]the population of France; and, since the very existence of the last

[014-127]remnant of the Pope's Temporal Power depended upon the French army, it

[014-128]was able to apply considerable pressure upon the Vatican. The interests

[014-129]of England were less directly involved, but it happened that at this

[014-130]moment [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] was Prime Minister, and Mr. Gladstone entertained

[014-131]strong views upon the Infallibility of the Pope. His opinions upon the

[014-132]subject were in part the outcome of his friendship with Lord Acton, a

[014-133]historian to whom learning and judgment had not been granted in equal

[014-134]proportions, and who, after years of incredible and indeed well-nigh

[014-135]mythical research, had come to the conclusion that the Pope could err.

[014-136]In this Mr. Gladstone entirely concurred, though he did not share the

[014-137]rest of his friend's theological opinions; for Lord Acton, while

[014-138]straining at the gnat of Infallibility, had swallowed the camel of the

[014-139]Roman Catholic Faith. 'Que diable allait-il faire dans cette galere?'

[014-140]one cannot help asking, as one watched that laborious and scrupulous

[014-141]scholar, that lifelong enthusiast for liberty, that almost hysterical

[014-142]reviler of priesthood and persecution, trailing his learning so

[014-143]discrepantly along the dusty Roman way. But, there are some who know how

[014-144]to wear their Rome with a difference; and Lord Acton was one of these.

[014-145]He was now engaged in fluttering like a moth round the Council and in

[014-146]writing long letters to Mr. Gladstone, impressing upon him the gravity

[014-147]of the situation, and urging him to bring his influence to bear. If the

[014-148]Dogma were carried--he declared, no man who accepted it could remain a

[014-149]loyal subject and Catholics would everywhere become 'irredeemable

[014-150]enemies of civil and religious liberty'. In these circumstances, was it

[014-151]not plainly incumbent upon the English Government, involved as it was

[014-152]with the powerful Roman Catholic forces in Ireland, to intervene? Mr.

[014-153]Gladstone allowed himself to become convinced, and Lord Acton began to

[014-154]hope that his efforts would be successful. But, he had forgotten one

[014-155]element in the situation; he had reckoned without the Archbishop of

[014-156]Westminster. The sharp nose of Manning sniffed out the whole intrigue.

[014-157]Though he despised Lord Acton almost as much as he disliked him--'such

[014-158]men,' he said, 'are all vanity: they have the inflation of German

[014-159]professors, and the ruthless talk of undergraduates'--yet he realised

[014-160]clearly enough the danger of his correspondence with the Prime Minister,

[014-161]and immediately took steps to counteract it. There was a semi-official

[014-162]agent of the English Government in Rome, Mr. Odo Russell, and around him

[014-163]Manning set to work to spin his spider's web of delicate and clinging

[014-164]diplomacy. Preliminary politenesses were followed by long walks upon the

[014-165]Pincio, and the gradual interchange of more and more important and

[014-166]confidential communications. Soon poor Mr. Russell was little better

[014-167]than a fly buzzing in gossamer. And Manning was careful to see that he

[014-168]buzzed on the right note. In his dispatches to the Foreign Secretary,

[014-169]Lord Clarendon, Mr. Russell explained in detail the true nature of the

[014-170]Council, that it was merely a meeting of a few Roman Catholic prelates

[014-171]to discuss some internal matters of Church discipline, that it had no

[014-172]political significance whatever, that the question of Infallibility,

[014-173]about which there had been so much random talk, was a purely theological

[014-174]question, and that, whatever decision might be come to on the subject,

[014-175]the position of Roman Catholics throughout the world would remain

[014-176]unchanged.
