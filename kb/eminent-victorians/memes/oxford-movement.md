# Oxford Movement

A religious revival movement within the Church of England, originating at Oxford University in 1833 and lasting until John Henry Newman's conversion to Roman Catholicism in 1845. The Movement sought to reclaim the Catholic heritage of Anglicanism — emphasizing apostolic succession, sacramental theology, and the authority of the Early Church Fathers — against what its leaders saw as the spiritual complacency of the established Church and the encroachments of liberal rationalism.

## Definition

The Oxford Movement began when John Keble, Hurrell Froude, and John Henry Newman, convinced that the Church of England had lost sight of its Catholic foundations, took the Christian Religion *au pied de la lettre* — a thing "not been done in England for centuries" (003-073). They meant every word of the Creeds literally, believed in the real presence of the supernatural in daily life, and saw the Church not as a department of state but as a divine institution stretching back through Apostolic Succession to Christ himself (003-073). The Movement's public face was the *Tracts for the Times*, a series of pamphlets published from 1833 to 1841 under Newman's editorship, addressing doctrines from Baptismal Regeneration to the Mysticism of the Early Fathers (003-161).

## Context in Victorian England

The Movement emerged against a background of spiritual torpor in the established Church. Strachey describes the pre-Tractarian clergy as gentlemen who "rode gaily to hounds of a morning" and "carried their two bottles of an evening" (002-080). This complacency was shaken by the liberal aftershocks of the French Revolution: the Reform Bill passed, Bentham and the Mills propounded Utilitarianism, and even Churchmen like Dr. Whately began to suggest that "different opinions might be permitted upon matters of doubt" (002-111). It was Keble, looking out from his Gloucestershire rectory at a world where "Infidelity was stalking through the land" and "the hideous doctrines of Democracy were being openly preached" (002-131), who provided the initial spark.

## Key Figures

The Movement was driven by three men of distinct temperaments:

- **John Keble** (1792–1866): The pious country clergyman whose *Christian Year* carried his name "into the remotest lodging-houses of England" (002-127). It was his National Apostasy sermon of 1833 that is conventionally dated as the Movement's beginning. Keble provided the moral fervour; he wished England "vastly more superstitious, more bigoted, more gloomy, more fierce in its religion" (003-100).

- **John Henry Newman** (1801–1890): The genius without whom "the Oxford Movement would have flickered out its little flame unobserved in the Common Room of Oriel" (003-038). Strachey portrays him as "a child of the Romantic Revival, a creature of emotion and of memory, a dreamer" (003-040). It was Newman's intellect, his editorial stewardship of the *Tracts*, and ultimately his conversion to Rome in 1845 that defined the Movement's arc.

- **Edward Bouverie Pusey** (1800–1882): Regius Professor of Hebrew at Oxford, a man of wealth and learning — "it was rumoured, had been to Germany" (003-109). His adherence to the Movement lent it academic respectability, and his name became so synonymous with it that its followers were called "Puseyites." After Newman's conversion, Pusey and Keble "continued for the rest of their lives to dance in an exemplary manner upon the tight-rope of High Anglicanism" (006-106).

- **Hurrell Froude** (1803–1836): Keble's pupil, whose romantic devotion to sanctity and intolerance of moderation supplied the Movement's combative edge. It was Froude who remarked of Cranmer that "he burned well" (003-105). He died young, before the Movement reached its crisis.

- **W. G. Ward** (1812–1882): Newman's disciple whose logical relentlessness pushed Tractarian principles to their extreme — he "had the extreme innocence of a child and a mathematician" (006-036). His *Ideal of a Christian Church* (1844) explicitly advocated Roman Catholicism while still an Anglican, precipitating the final crisis.

## In the Book

Strachey treats the Oxford Movement primarily through its impact on Cardinal Manning's career and, secondarily, as the intellectual crucible that shaped figures in other biographies such as Arthur Hugh Clough (in the Florence Nightingale and Dr. Arnold narratives).

### In Cardinal Manning's Narrative

The Movement is the dominant force in Manning's early ecclesiastical life. As a young Archdeacon of Chichester, Manning was "one of the most noted and determined of the Tractarians" (005-136). Strachey traces his careful navigation between Newman's increasingly Roman direction and the suspicions of Low Church bishops — a political tightrope that Manning walked with characteristic deftness, befriending both Newman and the evangelical Archdeacon Hare simultaneously (004-167). When Newman finally converted in 1845, Manning felt betrayed; the Movement that had given his Anglicanism its theological substance had now delivered its leader to Rome. Manning's own conversion followed six years later.

Strachey describes the moment of the Movement's end with characteristic irony: "The University breathed such a sigh of relief as usually follows the difficult expulsion of a hard piece of matter from a living organism, and actually began to attend to education" (006-106). The Church of England, he adds, "had tasted blood, and it was clear that she would never again be content with a vegetable diet" (006-106).

### In Dr. Arnold's Narrative

The Oxford Movement appears as the antithesis of Thomas Arnold's Broad Church liberalism. Arnold's scheme for admitting Dissenters into the Church was precisely the kind of latitudinarianism that Keble and Newman set out to combat (002-112). The Rugby School ethos of "muscular Christianity" — practical, moral, anti-dogmatic — was fundamentally at odds with Tractarian sacramentalism.

## Historical Significance

The Movement permanently altered the character of the Church of England. Its ritualist legacy — vestments, incense, candles, confessionals — became entrenched in Anglo-Catholic parishes despite the departure of Newman and many followers to Rome. Strachey notes that the clergy "maintained their reputation for judicious compromise, for they followed Newman up to the very point beyond which his conclusions were logical" (006-106). The "flood of converts" to Rome became, for Manning, evidence of the Movement's inherent trajectory toward Catholicism (009-154).

The movement also produced what Strachey regards as Newman's true legacy: not theological system-building but literary art. Newman "lived to write his Apologia, and to reach immortality, neither as a thinker nor as a theologian, but as an artist who has embalmed the poignant history of an intensely human spirit in the magical spices of words" (003-062).

## Related Movements

- **Anglo-Catholicism**: The ritualist and theological tradition that survived Newman's departure within the Church of England
- **Ultramontanism**: The movement toward papal supremacy within Roman Catholicism, into which Manning threw himself after his conversion
- **Broad Church / Liberal Anglicanism**: The opposing tendency, represented by Thomas Arnold, which sought to minimize doctrinal barriers
- **Evangelical Revival**: The low-church movement emphasizing personal conversion and biblical authority, equally opposed to Tractarian ritualism
