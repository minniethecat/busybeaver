[007-001]Such was the fair outward seeming of the Archdeacon's life; but, the

[007-002]inward reality was different. The more active, the more fortunate, the

[007-003]more full of happy promise his existence became, the more persistently

[007-004]was his secret imagination haunted by a dreadful vision--the lake that

[007-005]burneth forever with brimstone and fire. The temptations of the Evil One

[007-006]are many, Manning knew; and he knew also that, for him at least, the

[007-007]most subtle and terrible of all temptations was the temptation of

[007-008]worldly success. He tried to reassure himself, but it was in vain. He

[007-009]committed his thoughts to a diary, weighing scrupulously his every

[007-010]motive, examining with relentless searchings into the depths of his

[007-011]heart. Perhaps, after all, his longings for preferment were merely

[007-012]legitimatehopes for 'an elevation into a sphere of higher usefulness'.

[007-013]But no, there was something more than that. 'I do feel pleasure,' he

[007-014]noted, 'in honour, precedence, elevation, the society of great people,

[007-015]and all this is very shameful and mean.'

[007-016]After [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s conversion, he almost convinced himself that his 'visions

[007-017]of an ecclesiastical future' were justified by the role that he would

[007-018]play as a 'healer of the breach in the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]'. [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]

[007-019]agreed with him; but there was One higher than Mr. Gladstone, and did He

[007-020]agree?

[007-021]'I am pierced by anxious thoughts. God knows what my desires have been

[007-022]and are, and why they are crossed.... I am flattering myself with a

[007-023]fancy about depth and reality.... The great question is: Is God enough

[007-024]for you now? And if you are as now even to the end of life, will it

[007-025]suffice you?... Certainly I would rather choose to be stayed on God,

[007-026]than to be in the thrones of the world and the Church. Nothing else will

[007-027]go into Eternity.'

[007-028]In a moment of ambition, he had applied for the Readership of Lincoln's

[007-029]Inn, but, owing chiefly to the hostile influence of the Record, the

[007-030]appointment had gone elsewhere. A little later, a more important

[007-031]position was offered to him--the office of sub-almoner to the Queen,

[007-032]which had just been vacated by the Archbishop of York, and was almost

[007-033]certain to lead to a mitre. The offer threw Manning into an agony of

[007-034]self-examination. He drew up elaborate tables, after the manner of

[007-035]Robinson Crusoe, with the reasons for and against his acceptance of the

[007-036]post:

[007-037]FOR AGAINST

[007-038]1. That it comes unsought. 1. Not therefore to be accepted. Such

[007-039]things are trials as well as leadings.

[007-040]2. That it is honourable. 2. Being what I am, ought I

[007-041]not therefore to decline it--

[007-042](1) as humiliation;

[007-043](2) as revenge on myself

[007-044]for Lincoln's Inn;

[007-045](3) as a testimony?

[007-046]And so on. He found in the end ten 'negative reasons', with no

[007-047]affirmative ones to balance them, and, after a week's deliberation, he

[007-048]rejected the offer.

[007-049]But peace of mind was as far off from him as ever. First the bitter

[007-050]thought came to him that 'in all this Satan tells me I am doing it to be

[007-051]thought mortified and holy'; and then he was obsessed by the still

[007-052]bitterer feelings of ineradicable disappointment and regret. He had lost

[007-053]a great opportunity, and it brought him small comfort to consider that

[007-054]'in the region of counsels, self-chastisement, humiliation,

[007-055]self-discipline, penance, and of the Cross', he had perhaps done right.

[007-056]The crisis passed, but it was succeeded by a fiercer one. Manning was

[007-057]taken seriously ill, and became convinced that he might die at any

[007-058]moment. The entries in his Diary grew more elaborate than ever; his

[007-059]remorse for the past, his resolutions for the future, his protestations

[007-060]of submission to the will of God, filled page after page of parallel

[007-061]columns, headings and sub-headings, numbered clauses, and analytical

[007-062]tables. 'How do I feel about Death?' he wrote.

[007-063]'Certainly great fear:

[007-064]1. Because of the uncertainty of our state before God.

[007-065]2. Because of the consciousness--

[007-066](1) of great sins past,

[007-067](2) of great sinfulness,

[007-068](3) of most shallow repentance.

[007-069]What shall I do?'

[007-070]He decided to mortify himself, to read St Thomas Aquinas, and to make

[007-071]his 'night prayers forty instead of thirty minutes'. He determined

[007-072]during Lent 'to use no pleasant bread (except on Sundays and feasts)

[007-073]such as cake and sweetmeat'; but he added the proviso 'I do not include

[007-074]plain biscuits'. Opposite this entry appears the word 'KEPT'. And yet

[007-075]his back-slidings were many. Looking back over a single week, he was

[007-076]obliged to register 'petulance twice' and 'complacent visions'. He heard

[007-077]his curate being commended for bringing so many souls to God during

[007-078]Lent, and he 'could not bear it'; but the remorse was terrible: 'I

[007-079]abhorred myself on the spot, and looked upward for help.' He made out

[007-080]list upon list of the Almighty's special mercies towards him, and they

[007-081]included his creation, his regeneration, and (No. 5) 'the preservation

[007-082]of my life six times to my knowledge:

[007-083](1) In illness at the age of nine.

[007-084](2) In the water.

[007-085](3) By a runaway horse at [[oxford-university|Oxford]].

[007-086](4) By the same.

[007-087](5) By falling nearly through the ceiling of a church.

[007-088](6) Again by a fall of a horse. And I know not

[007-089]how often in shooting, riding, etc.'

[007-090]At last he became convalescent; but the spiritual experiences of those

[007-091]agitated weeks left an indelible mark upon his mind, and prepared the

[007-092]way for the great change which was to follow.

[007-093]For he had other doubts besides those which held him in torment as to

[007-094]his own salvation; he was in doubt about the whole framework of his

[007-095]faith. Newman's conversion, he found, had meant something more to him

[007-096]than he had first realised. It had seemed to come as a call to the

[007-097]redoubling of his Anglican activities; but supposing, in reality, it

[007-098]were a call towards something very different--towards an abandonment of

[007-099]those activities altogether? It might be 'a trial', or again it might be

[007-100]a 'leading'; how was he to judge? Already, before his illness, these

[007-101]doubts had begun to take possession of his mind.

[007-102]'I am conscious to myself,' he wrote in his Diary, 'of an extensively

[007-103]changed feeling towards the [[catholic-church|Church of Rome]] ... The Church of England

[007-104]seems to me to be diseased: 1. ORGANICALLY (six sub-headings). 2.

[007-105]FUNCTIONALLY (seven sub-headings) ... Wherever it seems healthy, it

[007-106]approximates the system of [[rome|Rome]].'

[007-107]Then thoughts of the Virgin Mary suddenly began to assail him:

[007-108](1) If John the Baptist were sanctified from the womb,

[007-109]how much more the B.V.!

[007-110](2) If Enoch and Elijah were exempted from death,

[007-111]why not the B.V. from sin?

[007-112](3) It is a strange way of loving the Son to slight

[007-113]the mother!'

[007-114]The arguments seemed irresistible, and a few weeks later the following

[007-115]entry occurs--'Strange thoughts have visited me:

[007-116](1) I have felt that the Episcopate of the Church of England is

[007-117]secularised and bound down beyond hope....

[007-118](2) I feel as if a light had fallen upon me. My feeling about the

[007-119]Roman Church is not intellectual. I have intellectual

[007-120]difficulties, but the great moral difficulties seem melting.

[007-121](3) Something keeps rising and saying, "You will end in the Roman

[007-122]Church".

[007-123]He noted altogether twenty-five of these 'strange thoughts'. His mind

[007-124]hovered anxiously round--

[007-125](1) The Incarnation,

[007-126](2) The Real Presence,

[007-127]i. Regeneration,

[007-128]ii. Eucharist, and

[007-129](3) The Exaltation of S. M. and Saints.

[007-130]His twenty-second strange thought was as follows: 'How do I know where I

[007-131]may be two years hence? Where was Newman five years ago?'

[007-132]It was significant, but hardly surprising, that, after his illness,

[007-133]Manning should have chosen to recuperate in Rome. He spent several

[007-134]months there, and his Diary during the whole of that period is concerned

[007-135]entirely with detailed descriptions of churches, ceremonies, and relics,

[007-136]and with minute accounts of conversations with priests and nuns. There

[007-137]is not a single reference either to the objects of art or to the

[007-138]antiquities of the place; but another omission was still more

[007-139]remarkable. Manning had a long interview with Pius IX, and his only

[007-140]record of it is contained in the bald statement: 'Audience today at the

[007-141]Vatican'. Precisely what passed on that occasion never transpired; all

[007-142]that is known is that His Holiness expressed considerable surprise on

[007-143]learning from the Archdeacon that the chalice was used in the Anglican

[007-144]Church in the administration of Communion. 'What!' he exclaimed, is the

[007-145]same chalice made use of by everyone?' 'I remember the pain I felt,'

[007-146]said Manning, long afterwards, 'at seeing how unknown we were to the

[007-147]Vicar of Jesus Christ. It made me feel our isolation.'

[007-148]On his return to England, he took up once more the work in his

[007-149]Archdeaconry with what appetite he might. Ravaged by doubt, distracted

[007-150]by speculation, he yet managed to maintain an outward presence of

[007-151]unshaken calm. His only confidant was Robert Wilberforce, to whom, for

[007-152]the next two years, he poured forth in a series of letters, headed

[007-153]'UNDER THE SEAL' to indicate that they contained the secrets of the

[007-154]confessional--the whole history of his spiritual perturbations. The

[007-155]irony of his position was singular; for, during the whole of this time,

[007-156]Manning was himself holding back from the Church of Rome a host of

[007-157]hesitating penitents by means of arguments which he was at the very

[007-158]moment denouncing as fallacious to his own confessor. But what else

[007-159]could he do? When he received, for instance, a letter such as the

[007-160]following from an agitated lady, what was he to say?

[007-161]'MY DEAR FATHER IN CHRIST,

[007-162]' ... I am sure you would pity me and like to help me, if you knew the

[007-163]unhappy, unsettled state my mind is in, and the misery of being

[007-164]ENTIRELY, WHEREVER I AM, with those who look upon joining the Church of

[007-165]Rome as the most awful "fall" conceivable to any one, and are devoid of

[007-166]the smallest comprehension of how any enlightened person can do it....

[007-167]My old Evangelical friends, with all my deep, deep love for them, do not

[007-168]succeed in shaking me in the least....

[007-169]'My brother has just published a book called "Regeneration", which all

[007-170]my friends are reading and highly extolling; it has a very contrary

[007-171]effect to what he would desire on my mind. I can read and understand it

[007-172]all in an altogether different sense, and the facts which he quotes

[007-173]about the articles as drawn up in 1536, and again in 1552, and of the

[007-174]Irish articles of 1615 and 1634, STARTLE and SHAKE me about the Reformed

[007-175]Church in England far more than anything else, and have done so ever

[007-176]since I first saw them in Mr. Maskell's pamphlet (as quoted from Mr

[007-177]Dodsworth's).

[007-178]'I do hope you have some time and thought to pray for me still. Mr.

[007-179]Galton's letters long ago grew into short formal notes, which hurt me

[007-180]and annoyed me particularly, and I never answered his last, so,

[007-181]literally, I have no one to say things to and get help from, which in

[007-182]one sense is a comfort when my convictions seem to be leading me on and

[007-183]on, and gaining strength in spite of all the dreariness of my lot.

[007-184]'Do you know I can't help being very anxious and unhappy about poor

[007-185]Sister Harriet. I am afraid of her GOING OUT OF HER MIND. She comforts

[007-186]herself by an occasional outpouring of everything to me, and I had a

[007-187]letter this morning.... She says Sister May has promised the Vicar never

[007-188]to talk to her or allow her to talk on the subject with her, and I doubt

[007-189]whether this can be good for her, because though she has lost her faith,

[007-190]she says, in the Church of England, yet she never thinks of what she

[007-191]could have faith in, and resolutely without inquiring into the question

[007-192]determines not to be a Roman Catholic, so that really, you see, she is

[007-193]allowing her mind to run adrift and yet perfectly powerless.
