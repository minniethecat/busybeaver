[018-001]Sometimes he would turn to the four red folio scrapbooks with their

[018-002]collection of newspaper cuttings, concerning himself, over a period of

[018-003]thirty years. Then the pale cheeks would flush and the close-drawn lips

[018-004]would grow even more menacing than before. 'Stupid, mulish malice,' he

[018-005]would note. 'Pure lying--conscious, deliberate and designed.'

[018-006]'Suggestive lying. Personal animosity is at the bottom of this.'

[018-007]And then he would suddenly begin to doubt. After all, where was he? What

[018-008]had he accomplished? Had any of it been worthwhile? Had he not been out

[018-009]of the world all his life! Out of the world!

[018-010]'Croker's "Life and Letters", and Hayward's "Letters",' he notes, 'are

[018-011]so full of politics, literature, action, events, collision of mind with

[018-012]mind, and that with such a multitude of men in every state of life, that

[018-013]when I look back, it seems as if I had been simply useless.'

[018-014]And again, 'The complete isolation and exclusion from the official life

[018-015]of England in which I have lived, makes me feel as if I had done

[018-016]nothing'. He struggled to console himself with the reflexion that all

[018-017]this was only 'the natural order'. 'If the natural order is moved by the

[018-018]supernatural order, then I may not have done nothing. Fifty years of

[018-019]witness for God and His Truth, I hope, has not been in vain.' But the

[018-020]same thoughts recurred. 'In reading Macaulay's life I had a haunting

[018-021]feeling that his had been a life of public utility and mine a vita

[018-022]umbratilis, a life in the shade.' Ah! it was God's will. 'Mine has been

[018-023]a life of fifty years out of the world as [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]]'s has been in it.

[018-024]The work of his life in this world is manifest. I hope mine may be in

[018-025]the next. I suppose our Lord called me out of the world because He saw

[018-026]that I should lose my soul in it.' Clearly, that was the explanation.

[018-027]And yet he remained sufficiently in the world to discharge with absolute

[018-028]efficiency the complex government of his diocese almost up to the last

[018-029]moment of his existence. Though his bodily strength gradually ebbed, the

[018-030]vigour of his mind was undismayed. At last, supported by cushions, he

[018-031]continued, by means of a dictated correspondence, to exert his

[018-032]accustomed rule. Only occasionally would he lay aside his work to plunge

[018-033]into the yet more necessary duties of devotion. Never again would he

[018-034]preach; never again would he put into practice those three salutary

[018-035]rules of his in choosing a subject for a sermon: '(1) asking God to

[018-036]guide the choice; (2) applying the matter to myself; (3) making the sign

[018-037]of the cross on my head and heart and lips in honour of the Sacred

[018-038]Mouth;' but he could still pray; he could turn especially to the Holy

[018-039]Ghost.

[018-040]'A very simple but devout person,' he wrote in one of his latest

[018-041]memoranda, 'asked me why in my first volume of sermons I said so little

[018-042]about the Holy Ghost. I was not aware of it; but I found it to be true.

[018-043]I at once resolved that I would make a reparation every day of my life

[018-044]to the Holy Ghost. This I have never failed to do to this day. To this I

[018-045]owe the light and faith which brought me into the truefold. I bought all

[018-046]the books I could about the Holy Ghost. I worked out the truths about

[018-047]His personality, His presence, and His office. This made me understand

[018-048]the last paragraph in the Apostles' Creed, and made me a Catholic

[018-049]Christian.'

[018-050]So, though Death came slowly, struggling step by step with that bold and

[018-051]tenacious spirit, when he did come at last the Cardinal was ready. Robed

[018-052]in his archiepiscopal vestments, his rochet, his girdle, and his

[018-053]mozzetta, with the scarlet biretta on his head, and the pectoral cross

[018-054]upon his breast, he made his solemn Profession of Faith in the Holy

[018-055][[catholic-church|Roman Church]]. A crowd of lesser dignitaries, each in the garments of his

[018-056]office, attended the ceremonial. The Bishop of Salford held up the

[018-057]Pontificale and the Bishop of Amycla bore the wax taper. The provost of

[018-058]Westminster, on his knees, read aloud the Profession of Faith,

[018-059]surrounded by the Canons of the Diocese. Towards those who gathered

[018-060]about him, the dying man was still able to show some signs of

[018-061]recognition, and even, perhaps, of affection; yet it seemed that his

[018-062]chief preoccupation, up to the very end, was with his obedience to the

[018-063]rules prescribed by the Divine Authority. 'I am glad to have been able

[018-064]to do everything in due order', were among his last words. 'Si fort

[018-065]qu'on soit,' says one of the profoundest of the observers of the human

[018-066]heart, 'on peut eprouver le besoin de s'incliner devant quelqu'un ou

[018-067]quelque chose. S'incliner devant Dieu, c'est toujours le moins

[018-068]humiliant.'

[018-069]Manning died on January 14th, 1892, in the eighty-fifth year of his age.

[018-070]A few days later Mr. Gladstone took occasion, in a letter to a friend,

[018-071]to refer to his relations with the late Cardinal. Manning's conversion

[018-072]was, he said,

[018-073]'altogether the severest blow that ever befell me. In a late letter the

[018-074]Cardinal termed it a quarrel, but in my reply I told him it was not a

[018-075]quarrel, but a death; and that was the truth. Since then there have been

[018-076]vicissitudes. But I am quite certain that to the last his personal

[018-077]feelings never changed; and I believe also that he kept a promise made

[018-078]in 1851, to remember me before God at the most solemn moments; a promise

[018-079]which I greatly valued. The whole subject is to me at once of extreme

[018-080]interest and of considerable restraint.'

[018-081]'His reluctance to die,' concluded Mr. Gladstone, 'may be explained by

[018-082]an intense anxiety to complete unfulfilled service.'

[018-083]The funeral was the occasion of a popular demonstration such as has

[018-084]rarely been witnessed in the streets of London. The route of the

[018-085]procession was lined by vast crowds of working people, whose

[018-086]imaginations, in some instinctive manner, had been touched. Many who had

[018-087]hardly seen him declared that in [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]] they had lost their

[018-088]best friend. Was it the magnetic vigour of the dead man's spirit that

[018-089]moved them? Or was it his valiant disregard of common custom and those

[018-090]conventional reserves and poor punctilios which are wont to hem about

[018-091]the great? Or was it something untameable in his glances and in his

[018-092]gestures? Or was it, perhaps, the mysterious glamour lingering about

[018-093]him, of the antique organisation of [[rome|Rome]]? For whatever cause, the mind

[018-094]of the people had been impressed; and yet, after all, the impression was

[018-095]more acute than lasting. The Cardinal's memory is a dim thing today. And

[018-096]he who descends into the crypt of that Cathedral which Manning never

[018-097]lived to see, will observe, in the quiet niche with the sepulchral

[018-098]monument, that the dust lies thick on the strange, the incongruous, the

[018-099]almost impossible object which, with its elaborations of dependent

[018-100]tassels, hangs down from the dim vault like some forlorn and forgotten

[018-101]trophy--the Hat.

[018-127]EVERY one knows the popular conception of Florence Nightingale. The

[018-128]saintly, self-sacrificing woman, the delicate maiden of high degree who

[018-129]threw aside the pleasures of a life of ease to succour the afflicted;

[018-130]the Lady with the Lamp, gliding through the horrors of the hospital at

[018-131][[scutari|Scutari]], and consecrating with the radiance of her goodness the dying

[018-132]soldier's couch. The vision is familiar to all--but the truth was

[018-133]different. The Miss Nightingale of fact was not as facile as fancy

[018-134]painted her. She worked in another fashion and towards another end; she

[018-135]moved under the stress of an impetus which finds no place in the popular

[018-136]imagination. A Demon possessed her. Now demons, whatever else they may

[018-137]be, are full of interest. And so it happens that in the real Miss

[018-138]Nightingale there was more that was interesting than in the legendary

[018-139]one; there was also less that was agreeable.

[018-140]Her family was extremely well-to-do, and connected by marriage with a

[018-141]spreading circle of other well-to-do families. There was a large country

[018-142]house in Derbyshire; there was another in the New Forest; there were

[018-143]Mayfair rooms for the London season and all its finest parties; there

[018-144]were tours on the Continent with even more than the usual number of

[018-145]Italian operas and of glimpses at the celebrities of Paris. Brought up

[018-146]among such advantages, it was only natural to suppose that Florence

[018-147]would show a proper appreciation of them by doing her duty in that state

[018-148]of life unto which it had pleased God to call her--in other words, by

[018-149]marrying, after a fitting number of dances and dinner-parties, an

[018-150]eligible gentleman, and living happily ever afterwards. Her sister, her

[018-151]cousins, all the young ladies of her acquaintance, were either getting

[018-152]ready to do this or had already done it.

[018-153]It was inconceivable that Florence should dream of anything else; yet

[018-154]dream she did. Ah! To do her duty in that state of life unto which it

[018-155]had pleased God to call her! Assuredly, she would not be behindhand in

[018-156]doing her duty; but unto what state of life HAD it pleased God to call

[018-157]her? That was the question. God's calls are many, and they are strange.

[018-158]Unto what state of life had it pleased Him to call Charlotte Corday, or

[018-159]Elizabeth of Hungary? What was that secret voice in her ear, if it was

[018-160]not a call? Why had she felt, from her earliest years, those mysterious

[018-161]promptings towards ... she hardly knew what, but certainly towards

[018-162]something very different from anything around her? Why, as a child in

[018-163]the nursery, when her sister had shown a healthy pleasure in tearing her

[018-164]dolls to pieces, had SHE shown an almost morbid one in sewing them up

[018-165]again? Why was she driven now to minister to the poor in their cottages,

[018-166]to watch by sick-beds, to put her dog's wounded paw into elaborate

[018-167]splints as if it was a human being? Why was her head filled with queer

[018-168]imaginations of the country house at Embley turned, by some enchantment,

[018-169]into a hospital, with herself as matron moving about among the beds? Why

[018-170]was even her vision of heaven itself filled with suffering patients to

[018-171]whom she was being useful? So she dreamed and wondered, and, taking out

[018-172]her diary, she poured into it the agitations of her soul. And then the

[018-173]bell rang, and it was time to go and dress for dinner.

[018-174]As the years passed, a restlessness began to grow upon her. She was

[018-175]unhappy, and at last she knew it. Mrs. Nightingale, too, began to notice

[018-176]that there was something wrong. It was very odd--what could be the

[018-177]matter with dear Flo? Mr. Nightingale suggested that a husband might be

[018-178]advisable; but the curious thing was that she seemed to take no interest

[018-179]in husbands. And with her attractions, and her accomplishments, too!

[018-180]There was nothing in the world to prevent her making a really brilliant

[018-181]match. But no! She would think of nothing but how to satisfy that

[018-182]singular craving of hers to be DOING something. As if there was not

[018-183]plenty to do in any case, in the ordinary way, at home. There was the

[018-184]china to look after, and there was her father to be read to after

[018-185]dinner. Mrs. Nightingale could not understand it; and then one day her

[018-186]perplexity was changed to consternation and alarm. Florence announced an

[018-187]extreme desire to go to Salisbury Hospital for several months as a

[018-188]nurse; and she confessed to some visionary plan of eventually setting up

[018-189]in a house of her own in a neighbouring village, and there founding

[018-190]'something like a Protestant Sisterhood, without vows, for women of

[018-191]educated feelings'. The whole scheme was summarily brushed aside as

[018-192]preposterous; and Mrs. Nightingale, after the first shock of terror, was

[018-193]able to settle down again more or less comfortably to her embroidery.

[018-194]But Florence, who was now twenty-five and felt that the dream of her

[018-195]life had been shattered, came near to desperation.
