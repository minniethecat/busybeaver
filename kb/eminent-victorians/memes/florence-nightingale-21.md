[021-001]This remarkable woman was in truth performing the function of an

[021-002]administrative chief. How had this come about? Was she not in reality

[021-003]merely a nurse? Was it not her duty simply to tend the sick? And indeed,

[021-004]was it not as a ministering angel, a gentle 'lady with a lamp', that she

[021-005]actually impressed the minds of her contemporaries? No doubt that was

[021-006]so; and yet it is no less certain that, as she herself said, the

[021-007]specific business of nursing was 'the least important of the functions

[021-008]into which she had been forced'. It was clear that in the state of

[021-009]disorganisation into which the hospitals at [[scutari|Scutari]] had fallen, the most

[021-010]pressing, the really vital, need was for something more than nursing; it

[021-011]was for the necessary elements of civilised life--the commonest material

[021-012]objects, the most ordinary cleanliness, the rudimentary habits of order

[021-013]and authority. 'Oh, dear [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]],' said one of her party as

[021-014]they were approaching Constantinople, 'when we land, let there be no

[021-015]delays, let us get straight to nursing the poor fellows!' 'The strongest

[021-016]will be wanted at the wash-tub,' was Miss Nightingale's answer. And it

[021-017]was upon the wash-tub, and all that the wash-tub stood for, that she

[021-018]expended her greatest energies. Yet to say that, is perhaps to say too

[021-019]much. For to those who watched her at work among the sick, moving day

[021-020]and night from bed to bed, with that unflinching courage, with that

[021-021]indefatigable vigilance, it seemed as if the concentrated force of an

[021-022]undivided and unparalleled devotion could hardly suffice for that

[021-023]portion of her task alone.

[021-024]Wherever, in those vast wards, suffering was at its worst and the need

[021-025]for help was greatest, there, as if by magic, was Miss Nightingale. Her

[021-026]superhuman equanimity would, at the moment of some ghastly operation,

[021-027]nerve the victim to endure, and almost to hope. Her sympathy would

[021-028]assuage the pangs of dying and bring back to those still living

[021-029]something of the forgotten charm of life. Over and over again her

[021-030]untiring efforts rescued those whom the surgeons had abandoned as beyond

[021-031]the possibility of cure. Her mere presence brought with it a strange

[021-032]influence. A passionate idolatry spread among the men--they kissed her

[021-033]shadow as it passed. They did more. 'Before she came,' said a soldier,

[021-034]'there was cussin' and swearin' but after that it was as 'oly as a

[021-035]church.' The most cherished privilege of the fighting man was abandoned

[021-036]for the sake of Miss Nightingale. In those 'lowest sinks of human

[021-037]misery', as she herself put it, she never heard the use of one

[021-038]expression 'which could distress a gentlewoman'.

[021-039]She was heroic; and these were the humble tributes paid by those of

[021-040]grosser mould to that high quality. Certainly, she was heroic. Yet her

[021-041]heroism was not of that simple sort so dear to the readers of novels and

[021-042]the compilers of hagiologies--the romantic sentimental heroism with

[021-043]which mankind loves to invest its chosen darlings: it was made of

[021-044]sterner stuff. To the wounded soldier on his couch of agony, she might

[021-045]well appear in the guise of a gracious angel of mercy; but the military

[021-046]surgeons, and the orderlies, and her own nurses, and the 'Purveyor', and

[021-047]Dr. Hall, and, even Lord Stratford himself, could tell a different

[021-048]story. It was not by gentle sweetness and womanly self-abnegation that

[021-049]she had brought order out of chaos in the Scutari hospitals, that, from

[021-050]her own resources, she had clothed the [[british-army|British Army]], that she had spread

[021-051]her dominion over the serried and reluctant powers of the official

[021-052]world; it was by strict method, by stern discipline, by rigid attention

[021-053]to detail, by ceaseless labour, and by the fixed determination of an

[021-054]indomitable will.

[021-055]Beneath her cool and calm demeanour lurked fierce and passionate fires.

[021-056]As she passed through the wards in her plain dress, so quiet, so

[021-057]unassuming, she struck the casual observer simply as the pattern of a

[021-058]perfect lady; but the keener eye perceived something more than that--the

[021-059]serenity of high deliberation in the scope of the capacious brow, the

[021-060]sign of power in the dominating curve of the thin nose, and the traces

[021-061]of a harsh and dangerous temper--something peevish, something mocking,

[021-062]and yet something precise--in the small and delicate mouth. There was

[021-063]humour in the face; but the curious watcher might wonder whether it was

[021-064]humour of a very pleasant kind; might ask himself, even as he heard the

[021-065]laughter and marked the jokes with which she cheered the spirits of her

[021-066]patients, what sort of sardonic merriment this same lady might not give

[021-067]vent to, in the privacy of her chamber. As for her voice, it was true of

[021-068]it, even more than of her countenance, that it 'had that in it one must

[021-069]fain call master'. Those clear tones were in no need of emphasis: 'I

[021-070]never heard her raise her voice', said one of her companions. 'Only when

[021-071]she had spoken, it seemed as if nothing could follow but obedience.'

[021-072]Once, when she had given some direction, a doctor ventured to remark

[021-073]that the thing could not be done. 'But it must be done,' said Miss

[021-074]Nightingale. A chance bystander, who heard the words, never forgot

[021-075]through all his life the irresistible authority of them. And they were

[021-076]spoken quietly--very quietly indeed.

[021-077]Late at night, when the long miles of beds lay wrapped in darkness, Miss

[021-078]Nightingale would sit at work in her little room, over her

[021-079]correspondence. It was one of the most formidable of all her duties.

[021-080]There were hundreds of letters to be written to the friends and

[021-081]relations of soldiers; there was the enormous mass of official documents

[021-082]to be dealt with; there were her own private letters to be answered;

[021-083]and, most important of all, there was the composition of her long and

[021-084]confidential reports to Sidney Herbert. These were by no means official

[021-085]communications. Her soul, pent up all day in the restraint and reserve

[021-086]of a vast responsibility, now at last poured itself out in these letters

[021-087]with all its natural vehemence, like a swollen torrent through an open

[021-088]sluice. Here, at least, she did not mince matters. Here she painted in

[021-089]her darkest colours the hideous scenes which surrounded her; here she

[021-090]tore away remorselessly the last veils still shrouding the abominable

[021-091]truth. Then she would fill pages with recommendations and suggestions,

[021-092]with criticisms of the minutest details of organisation, with elaborate

[021-093]calculations of contingencies, with exhaustive analyses and statistical

[021-094]statements piled up in breathless eagerness one on the top of the other.

[021-095]And then her pen, in the virulence of its volubility, would rush on to

[021-096]the discussion of individuals, to the denunciation of an incompetent

[021-097]surgeon or the ridicule of a self-sufficient nurse. Her sarcasm searched

[021-098]the ranks of the officials with the deadly and unsparing precision of a

[021-099]machine-gun. Her nicknames were terrible. She respected no one: Lord

[021-100]Stratford, Lord Raglan, Lady Stratford, Dr. Andrew Smith, Dr. Hall, the

[021-101]Commissary-General, the Purveyor--she fulminated against them all. The

[021-102]intolerable futility of mankind obsessed her like a nightmare, and she

[021-103]gnashed her teeth against it. 'I do well to be angry,' was the burden of

[021-104]her cry. 'How many just men were there at Scutari? How many who cared at

[021-105]all for the sick, or had done anything for their relief? Were there ten?

[021-106]Were there five? Was there even one?' She could not be sure.

[021-107]At one time, during several weeks, her vituperations descended upon the

[021-108]head of Sidney Herbert himself. He had misinterpreted her wishes, he had

[021-109]traversed her positive instructions, and it was not until he had

[021-110]admitted his error and apologised in abject terms that he was allowed

[021-111]again into favour. While this misunderstanding was at its height, an

[021-112]aristocratic young gentleman arrived at Scutari with a recommendation

[021-113]from the Minister. He had come out from England filled with a romantic

[021-114]desire to render homage to the angelic heroine of his dreams. He had, he

[021-115]said, cast aside his life of ease and luxury; he would devote his days

[021-116]and nights to the service of that gentle lady; he would perform the most

[021-117]menial offices, he would 'fag' for her, he would be her footman--and

[021-118]feel requited by a single smile. A single smile, indeed, he had, but it

[021-119]was of an unexpected kind. Miss Nightingale at first refused to see him,

[021-120]and then, when she consented, believing that he was an emissary sent by

[021-121]Sidney Herbert to put her in the wrong over their dispute, she took

[021-122]notes of her conversation with him, and insisted on his signing them at

[021-123]the end of it. The young gentleman returned to England by the next ship.

[021-124]This quarrel with Sidney Herbert was, however, an exceptional incident.

[021-125]Alike by him, and by Lord Panmure, his successor at the [[war-office|War Office]], she

[021-126]was firmly supported; and the fact that during the whole of her stay at

[021-127]Scutari she had the Home Government at her back, was her trump card in

[021-128]her dealings with the hospital authorities. Nor was it only the

[021-129]Government that was behind her: public opinion in England early

[021-130]recognised the high importance of her mission, and its enthusiastic

[021-131]appreciation of her work soon reached an extraordinary height. The Queen

[021-132]herself was deeply moved. She made repeated inquiries as to the welfare

[021-133]of Miss Nightingale; she asked to see her accounts of the wounded, and

[021-134]made her the intermediary between the throne and the troops.

[021-135]'Let Mrs. Herbert know,' she wrote to the War Minister, 'that I wish

[021-136]Miss Nightingale and the ladies would tell these poor noble, wounded,

[021-137]and sick men that NO ONE takes a warmer interest or feels MORE for their

[021-138]sufferings or admires their courage and heroism MORE than their Queen.

[021-139]Day and night she thinks of her beloved troops. So does the Prince. Beg

[021-140]Mrs. Herbert to communicate these my words to those ladies, as I know

[021-141]that our sympathy is much valued by these noble fellows.'

[021-142]The letter was read aloud in the wards by the Chaplain. 'It is a very

[021-143]feeling letter,' said the men.

[021-144]And so the months passed, and that fell winter which had begun with

[021-145]Inkerman and had dragged itself out through the long agony of the

[021-146]investment of Sebastopol, at last was over. In May, 1855, after six

[021-147]months of labour, Miss Nightingale could look with something like

[021-148]satisfaction at the condition of the Scutari hospitals. Had they done

[021-149]nothing more than survive the terrible strain which had been put upon

[021-150]them, it would have been a matter for congratulation; but they had done

[021-151]much more than that--they had marvellously improved. The confusion and

[021-152]the pressure in the wards had come to an end; order reigned in them, and

[021-153]cleanliness; the supplies were bountiful and prompt; important sanitary

[021-154]works had been carried out. One simple comparison of figures was enough

[021-155]to reveal the extraordinary change: the rate of mortality among the

[021-156]cases treated had fallen from forty-two percent to twenty-two per 1,000.

[021-157]But still, the indefatigable lady was not satisfied. The main problem

[021-158]had been solved--the physical needs of the men had been provided for;

[021-159]their mental and spiritual needs remained. She set up and furnished

[021-160]reading-rooms and recreation rooms. She started classes and lectures.

[021-161]Officers were amazed to see her treating their men as if they were human

[021-162]beings, and assured her that she would only end by 'spoiling the

[021-163]brutes'. But that was not Miss Nightingale's opinion, and she was

[021-164]justified. The private soldier began to drink less and even--though that

[021-165]seemed impossible--to save his pay. Miss Nightingale became a banker for

[021-166]the Army, receiving and sending home large sums of money every month. At

[021-167]last, reluctantly, the Government followed suit, and established

[021-168]machinery of its own for the remission of money. Lord Panmure, however,

[021-169]remained sceptical; 'it will do no good,' he pronounced; 'the British

[021-170]soldier is not a remitting animal.' But, in fact during the next six

[021-171]months L71,000 was sent home.
