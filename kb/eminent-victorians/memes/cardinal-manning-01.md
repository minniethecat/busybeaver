[001-001]EMINENT VICTORIANS

[001-002]by Lytton Strachey

[001-003]Preface

[001-004]THE history of the Victorian Age will never be written; we know too much

[001-005]about it. For ignorance is the first requisite of the historian--ignorance,

[001-006]which simplifies and clarifies, which selects and omits, with a placid

[001-007]perfection unattainable by the highest art. Concerning the Age which has

[001-008]just passed, our fathers and our grandfathers have poured forth and

[001-009]accumulated so vast a quantity of information that the industry of a

[001-010]Ranke would be submerged by it, and the perspicacity of a Gibbon would

[001-011]quail before it. It is not by the direct method of a scrupulous

[001-012]narration that the explorer of the past can hope to depict that singular

[001-013]epoch. If he is wise, he will adopt a subtler strategy. He will attack

[001-014]his subject in unexpected places; he will fall upon the flank, or the

[001-015]rear; he will shoot a sudden, revealing searchlight into obscure

[001-016]recesses, hitherto undivined. He will row out over that great ocean of

[001-017]material, and lower down into it, here and there, a little bucket, which

[001-018]will bring up to the light of day some characteristic specimen, from

[001-019]those far depths, to be examined with a careful curiosity. Guided by

[001-020]these considerations, I have written the ensuing studies. I have

[001-021]attempted, through the medium of biography, to present some Victorian

[001-022]visions to the modern eye. They are, in one sense, haphazard

[001-023]visions--that is to say, my choice of subjects has been determined by no

[001-024]desire to construct a system or to prove a theory, but by simple motives

[001-025]of convenience and of art. It has been my purpose to illustrate rather

[001-026]than to explain. It would have been futile to hope to tell even a precis

[001-027]of the truth about the Victorian age, for the shortest precis must fill

[001-028]innumerable volumes. But, in the lives of an ecclesiastic, an

[001-029]educational authority, a woman of action, and a man of adventure, I have

[001-030]sought to examine and elucidate certain fragments of the truth which

[001-031]took my fancy and lay to my hand.

[001-032]I hope, however, that the following pages may prove to be of interest

[001-033]from the strictly biographical, no less than from the historical point

[001-034]of view. Human beings are too important to be treated as mere symptoms

[001-035]of the past. They have a value which is independent of any temporal

[001-036]processes--which is eternal, and must be felt for its own sake. The art

[001-037]of biography seems to have fallen on evil times in England. We have had,

[001-038]it is true, a few masterpieces, but we have never had, like the French,

[001-039]a great biographical tradition; we have had no Fontenelles and

[001-040]Condorcets, with their incomparable eloges, compressing into a few

[001-041]shining pages the manifold existences of men. With us, the most delicate

[001-042]and humane of all the branches of the art of writing has been relegated

[001-043]to the journeymen of letters; we do not reflect that it is perhaps as

[001-044]difficult to write a good life as to live one. Those two fat volumes,

[001-045]with which it is our custom to commemorate the dead--who does not know

[001-046]them, with their ill-digested masses of material, their slipshod style,

[001-047]their tone of tedious panegyric, their lamentable lack of selection, of

[001-048]detachment, of design? They are as familiar as the cortege of the

[001-049]undertaker, and wear the same air of slow, funereal barbarism. One is

[001-050]tempted to suppose, of some of them, that they were composed by that

[001-051]functionary as the final item of his job. The studies in this book are

[001-052]indebted, in more ways than one, to such works--works which certainly

[001-053]deserve the name of Standard Biographies. For they have provided me not

[001-054]only with much indispensable information, but with something even more

[001-055]precious--an example. How many lessons are to be learned from them! But

[001-056]it is hardly necessary to particularise. To preserve, for instance, a

[001-057]becoming brevity--a brevity which excludes everything that is redundant

[001-058]and nothing that is significant--that, surely, is the first duty of the

[001-059]biographer. The second, no less surely, is to maintain his own freedom

[001-060]of spirit. It is not his business to be complimentary; it is his

[001-061]business to lay bare the facts of the case, as he understands them. That

[001-062]is what I have aimed at in this book--to lay bare the facts of some

[001-063]cases, as I understand them, dispassionately, impartially, and without

[001-064]ulterior intentions. To quote the words of a Master--'Je n'impose rien;

[001-065]je ne propose rien: j'expose.'

[001-066]L.S.

[001-067]A list of the principal sources from which I have drawn is appended to

[001-068]each Biography. I would indicate, as an honourable exception to the

[001-069]current commodity, Sir Edward Cook's excellent Life of Florence

[001-070]Nightingale, without which my own study, though composed on a very

[001-071]different scale and from a decidedly different angle, could not have

[001-072]been written.

[001-073][[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]]

[001-074]HENRY EDWARD MANNING was born in 1807 and died in 1892. His life was

[001-075]extraordinary in many ways, but its interest for the modern inquirer

[001-076]depends mainly upon two considerations--the light which his career

[001-077]throws upon the spirit of his age, and the psychological problems

[001-078]suggested by his inner history. He belonged to that class of eminent

[001-079]ecclesiastics--and it is by no means a small class--who have been

[001-080]distinguished less for saintliness and learning than for practical

[001-081]ability. Had he lived in the Middle Ages he would certainly have been

[001-082]neither a Francis nor an Aquinas, but he might have been an Innocent. As

[001-083]it was, born in the England of the nineteenth century, growing up in the

[001-084]very seed-time of modern progress, coming to maturity with the first

[001-085]onrush of Liberalism, and living long enough to witness the victories of

[001-086]Science and Democracy, he yet, by a strange concatenation of

[001-087]circumstances, seemed almost to revive in his own person that long line

[001-088]of diplomatic and administrative clerics which, one would have thought,

[001-089]had come to an end for ever with Cardinal Wolsey.

[001-090]In Manning, so it appeared, the Middle Ages lived again. The tall gaunt

[001-091]figure, with the face of smiling asceticism, the robes, and the biretta,

[001-092]as it passed in triumph from High Mass at the Oratory to philanthropic

[001-093]gatherings at Exeter Hall, from Strike Committees at the Docks to

[001-094]Mayfair drawing-rooms where fashionable ladies knelt to the Prince of

[001-095]the Church, certainly bore witness to a singular condition of affairs.

[001-096]What had happened? Had a dominating character imposed itself upon a

[001-097]hostile environment? Or was the nineteenth century, after all, not so

[001-098]hostile? Was there something in it, scientific and progressive as it

[001-099]was, which went out to welcome the representative of ancient tradition

[001-100]and uncompromising faith? Had it, perhaps, a place in its heart for such

[001-101]as Manning--a soft place, one might almost say? Or, on the other hand,

[001-102]was it he who had been supple and yielding? He who had won by art what

[001-103]he would never have won by force, and who had managed, so to speak, to

[001-104]be one of the leaders of the procession less through merit than through

[001-105]a superior faculty for gliding adroitly to the front rank? And, in any

[001-106]case, by what odd chances, what shifts and struggles, what combinations

[001-107]of circumstance and character, had this old man come to be where he was?

[001-108]Such questions are easier to ask than to answer; but it may be

[001-109]instructive, and even amusing, to look a little more closely into the

[001-110]complexities of so curious a story.

[001-111]I

[001-112]UNDOUBTEDLY, what is most obviously striking in the history of Manning's

[001-113]career is the persistent strength of his innate characteristics. Through

[001-114]all the changes of his fortunes the powerful spirit of the man worked on

[001-115]undismayed. It was as if the Fates had laid a wager that they would

[001-116]daunt him; and in the end they lost their bet.

[001-117]His father was a rich West Indian merchant, a governor of the Bank of

[001-118]England, a Member of Parliament, who drove into town every day from his

[001-119]country seat in a coach and four, and was content with nothing short of

[001-120]a bishop for the christening of his children. Little Henry, like the

[001-121]rest, had his bishop; but he was obliged to wait for him--for as long as

[001-122]eighteen months. In those days, and even a generation later, as Keble

[001-123]bears witness, there was great laxity in regard to the early baptism of

[001-124]children. The delay has been noted by Manning's biographer as the first

[001-125]stumbling-block in the spiritual life of the future Cardinal; but he

[001-126]surmounted it with success.

[001-127]His father was more careful in other ways.

[001-128]'His refinement and delicacy of mind were such,' wrote Manning long

[001-129]afterwards, 'that I never heard out of his mouth a word which might not

[001-130]have been spoken in the presence of the most pure and sensitive--except,'

[001-131]he adds, 'on one occasion. He was then forced by others to repeat a

[001-132]negro story which, though free from all evil de sexu, was indelicate. He

[001-133]did it with great resistance. His example gave me a hatred of all such

[001-134]talk.'

[001-135]The family lived in an atmosphere of Evangelical piety. One day the

[001-136]little boy came in from the farmyard, and his mother asked him whether

[001-137]he had seen the peacock. 'I said yes, and the nurse said no, and my

[001-138]mother made me kneel down and beg God to forgive me for not speaking the

[001-139]truth.' At the age of four the child was told by a cousin of the age of

[001-140]six that 'God had a book in which He wrote down everything we did wrong.

[001-141]This so terrified me for days that I remember being found by my mother

[001-142]sitting under a kind of writing-table in great fear. I never forgot this

[001-143]at any time in my life,' the Cardinal tells us, 'and it has been a great

[001-144]grace to me.' When he was nine years old he 'devoured the Apocalypse;

[001-145]and I never all through my life forgot the "lake that burneth with fire

[001-146]and brimstone". That verse has kept me like an audible voice through all

[001-147]my life, and through worlds of danger in my youth.'

[001-148]At Harrow the worlds of danger were already around him; but yet he

[001-149]listened to the audible voice. 'At school and college I never failed to

[001-150]say my prayers, so far as memory serves me, even for a day.' And he

[001-151]underwent another religious experience: he read Paley's Evidences. 'I

[001-152]took in the whole argument,' wrote Manning, when he was over seventy,

[001-153]'and I thank God that nothing has ever shaken it.' Yet on the whole he

[001-154]led the unspiritual life of an ordinary schoolboy. We have glimpses of

[001-155]him as a handsome lad, playing cricket, or strutting about in tasselled

[001-156]Hessian top-boots. And on one occasion at least he gave proof of a

[001-157]certain dexterity of conduct which deserved to be remembered. He went

[001-158]out of bounds, and a master, riding by and seeing him on the other side

[001-159]of a field, tied his horse to a gate, and ran after him. The astute

[001-160]youth outran the master, fetched a circle, reached the gate, jumped on

[001-161]to the horse's back and rode off. For this he was very properly

[001-162]chastised; but, of what use was chastisement? No whipping, however

[001-163]severe, could have eradicated from little Henry's mind a quality at

[001-164]least as firmly planted in it as his fear of Hell and his belief in the

[001-165]arguments of Paley.

[001-166]It had been his father's wish that Manning should go into the Church;

[001-167]but the thought disgusted him; and when he reached [[oxford-university|Oxford]], his tastes,

[001-168]his ambitions, his successes at the Union, all seemed to mark him out

[001-169]for a political career. He was a year junior to Samuel Wilberforce, and

[001-170]a year senior to [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]]. In those days the Union was the

[001-171]recruiting-ground for young politicians; Ministers came down from London

[001-172]to listen to the debates; and a few years later the Duke of Newcastle

[001-173]gave Gladstone a pocket borough on the strength of his speech at the

[001-174]Union against the Reform Bill. To those three young men, indeed, the

[001-175]whole world lay open. Were they not rich, well-connected, and endowed

[001-176]with an infinite capacity for making speeches? The event justified the

[001-177]highest expectations of their friends; for the least distinguished of

[001-178]the three died a bishop. The only danger lay in another direction.
