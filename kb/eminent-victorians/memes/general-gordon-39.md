[039-001]In fact, the conduct of the Government wears so singular an appearance

[039-002]that it has seemed necessary to account for it by some ulterior

[039-003]explanation. It has often been asserted that the true cause of Gordon's

[039-004]appointment was the clamour in the Press. It is said--among others, by

[039-005]Sir Evelyn Baring himself, who has given something like an official

[039-006]sanction to this view of the case--that the Government could not resist

[039-007]the pressure of the newspapers and the feeling in the country which it

[039-008]indicated; that Ministers, carried off their feet by a wave of 'Gordon

[039-009]cultus', were obliged to give way to the inevitable. But this suggestion

[039-010]is hardly supported by an examination of the facts. Already, early in

[039-011]December, and many weeks before Gordon's name had begun to figure in the

[039-012]newspapers, Lord Granville had made his first effort to induce Sir

[039-013]Evelyn Baring to accept Gordon's services. The first newspaper demand

[039-014]for a Gordon mission appeared in the "Pall Mall Gazette" on the

[039-015]afternoon of January 9th; and the very next morning, Lord Granville was

[039-016]making his second telegraphic attack upon Sir Evelyn Baring. The feeling

[039-017]in the Press did not become general until the 11th, and on the 14th Lord

[039-018]Granville, in his telegram to [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]], for the third time proposed

[039-019]the appointment of Gordon. Clearly, on the part of Lord Granville at any

[039-020]rate, there was no extreme desire to resist the wishes of the Press. Nor

[039-021]was the Government as a whole by any means incapable of ignoring public

[039-022]opinion; a few months were to show that, plainly enough. It is difficult

[039-023]to avoid the conclusion that if Ministers had been opposed to the

[039-024]appointment of Gordon, he would never have been appointed. As it was,

[039-025]the newspapers were in fact forestalled, rather than followed, by the

[039-026]Government.

[039-027]How, then, are we to explain the Government's action? Are we to suppose

[039-028]that its members, like the members of the public at large, were

[039-029]themselves carried away by a sudden enthusiasm, a sudden conviction that

[039-030]they had found their saviour; that [[general-gordon|General Gordon]] was the man--they did

[039-031]not quite know why, but that was of no consequence--the one man to get

[039-032]them out of the whole Sudan difficulty--they did not quite know how, but

[039-033]that was of no consequence either if only he were sent to [[khartoum|Khartoum]]?

[039-034]Doubtless even Cabinet Ministers are liable to such impulses; doubtless

[039-035]it is possible that the Cabinet of that day allowed itself to drift, out

[039-036]of mere lack of consideration, and judgment, and foresight, along the

[039-037]rapid stream of popular feeling towards the inevitable cataract. That

[039-038]may be so; yet there are indications that a more definite influence was

[039-039]at work. There was a section of the Government which had never become

[039-040]quite reconciled to the policy of withdrawing from the Sudan. To this

[039-041]section--we may call it the imperialist section--which was led, inside

[039-042]the Cabinet, by Lord Hartington, and outside by Lord Wolseley, the

[039-043]policy which really commended itself was the very policy which had been

[039-044]outlined by General Gordon in his interview with Mr. Stead and his

[039-045]letter to Sir Samuel Baker. They saw that it might be necessary to

[039-046]abandon some of the outlying parts of the Sudan to the Mahdi; but the

[039-047]prospect of leaving the whole province in his hands was highly

[039-048]distasteful to them; above all, they dreaded the loss of Khartoum. Now,

[039-049]supposing that General Gordon, in response to a popular agitation in the

[039-050]Press, were sent to Khartoum, what would follow? Was it not at least

[039-051]possible that, once there, with his views and his character, he would,

[039-052]for some reason or other, refrain from carrying out a policy of pacific

[039-053]retreat? Was it not possible that in that case he might so involve the

[039-054]English Government that it would find itself obliged, almost

[039-055]imperceptibly perhaps, to substitute for its policy of withdrawal a

[039-056]policy of advance? Was it not possible that General Gordon might get

[039-057]into difficulties, that he might be surrounded and cut off from Egypt'?

[039-058]If that were to happen, how could the English Government avoid the

[039-059]necessity of sending an expedition to rescue him? And, if an English

[039-060]expedition went to the Sudan, was it conceivable that it would leave the

[039-061]Mahdi as it found him? In short, would not the dispatch of General

[039-062]Gordon to Khartoum involve, almost inevitably, the conquest of the Sudan

[039-063]by British troops, followed by a British occupation? And, behind all

[039-064]these questions, a still larger question loomed. The position of the

[039-065]English in Egypt itself was still ambiguous; the future was obscure; how

[039-066]long, in reality, would an English army remain in Egypt? Was not one

[039-067]thing, at least, obvious--that if the English were to conquer and occupy

[039-068]the Sudan, their evacuation of Egypt would become impossible?

[039-069]With our present information, it would be rash to affirm that all, or

[039-070]any, of these considerations were present to the minds of the

[039-071]imperialist section of the Government. Yet it is difficult to believe

[039-072]that a man such as Lord Wolseley, for instance, with his knowledge of

[039-073]affairs and his knowledge of Gordon, could have altogether overlooked

[039-074]them. Lord Hartington, indeed, may well have failed to realise at once

[039-075]the implications of General Gordon's appointment--for it took Lord

[039-076]Hartington some time to realise the implications of anything; but Lord

[039-077]Hartington was very far from being a fool; and we may well suppose that

[039-078]he instinctively, perhaps subconsciously, apprehended the elements of a

[039-079]situation which he never formulated to himself. However that may be,

[039-080]certain circumstances are significant. It is significant that the

[039-081]go-between who acted as the Government's agent in its negotiations with

[039-082]Gordon was an imperialist--Lord Wolseley. It is significant that the

[039-083]'Ministers' whom Gordon finally interviewed, and who actually determined

[039-084]his appointment were by no means the whole of the Cabinet, but a small

[039-085]section of it, presided over by Lord Hartington. It is significant, too,

[039-086]that Gordon's mission was represented both to Sir Evelyn Baring, who was

[039-087]opposed to his appointment, and to Mr. Gladstone, who was opposed to an

[039-088]active policy in the Sudan, as a mission merely 'to report'; while, no

[039-089]sooner was the mission actually decided upon, than it began to assume a

[039-090]very different complexion. In his final interview with the 'Ministers',

[039-091]Gordon we know (though he said nothing about it to the Rev. Mr Barnes)

[039-092]threw out the suggestion that it might be as well to make him the

[039-093]Governor-General of the Sudan. The suggestion, for the moment, was not

[039-094]taken up; but it is obvious that a man does not propose to become a

[039-095]Governor-General in order to make a report.

[039-096]We are in the region of speculations; one other presents itself. Was the

[039-097]movement in the Press during that second week of January a genuine

[039-098]movement, expressing a spontaneous wave of popular feeling? Or was it a

[039-099]cause of that feeling, rather than an effect? The engineering of a

[039-100]newspaper agitation may not have been an impossibility--even so long ago

[039-101]as 1884. One would like to know more than one is ever likely to know of

[039-102]the relations of the imperialist section of the Government with Mr.

[039-103]Stead.

[039-104]But it is time to return to the solidity of fact. Within a few hours of

[039-105]his interview with the Ministers, Gordon had left England forever. At

[039-106]eight o'clock in the evening, there was a little gathering of elderly

[039-107]gentlemen at Victoria Station. Gordon, accompanied by Colonel Stewart,

[039-108]who was to act as his second-in-command, tripped on to the platform.

[039-109]Lord Granville bought the necessary tickets; the Duke of Cambridge

[039-110]opened the railway-carriage door. The General jumped into the train; and

[039-111]then Lord Wolseley appeared, carrying a leather bag, in which was L200

[039-112]in gold, collected from friends at the last moment for the contingencies

[039-113]of the journey. The bag was handed through the window. The train

[039-114]started. As it did so, Gordon leaned out and addressed a last whispered

[039-115]question to Lord Wolseley. Yes, it had been done. Lord Wolseley had seen

[039-116]to it himself; next morning, every member of the Cabinet would receive a

[039-117]copy of Dr. Samuel Clarke's Scripture Promises. That was all. The train

[039-118]rolled out of the station.

[039-119]Before the travellers reached Cairo, steps had been taken which finally

[039-120]put an end to the theory--if it had ever been seriously held--that the

[039-121]purpose of the mission was simply the making of a report. On the very

[039-122]day of Gordon's departure, Lord Granville telegraphed to Sir Evelyn

[039-123]Baring as follows: 'Gordon suggests that it may be announced in Egypt

[039-124]that he is on his way to Khartoum to arrange for the future settlement

[039-125]of the Sudan for the best advantage of the people.' Nothing was said of

[039-126]reporting. A few days later, Gordon himself telegraphed to Lord

[039-127]Granville suggesting that he should be made Governor-General of the

[039-128]Sudan, in order to 'accomplish the evacuation', and to 'restore to the

[039-129]various Sultans of the Sudan their independence'. Lord Granville at once

[039-130]authorised Sir Evelyn Baring to issue, if he thought fit, a proclamation

[039-131]to this effect in the name of the Khedive. Thus the mission 'to report'

[039-132]had already swollen into a Governor-Generalship, with the object, not

[039-133]merely of effecting the evacuation of the Sudan, but also of setting up

[039-134]'various Sultans' to take the place of the Egyptian Government.

[039-135]In Cairo, in spite of the hostilities of the past, Gordon was received

[039-136]with every politeness. He was at once proclaimed Governor-General of the

[039-137]Sudan, with the widest powers. He was on the point of starting off again

[039-138]on his journey southwards, when a singular and important incident

[039-139]occurred. Zobeir, the rebel chieftain of Darfur, against whose forces

[039-140]Gordon had struggled for years, and whose son, Suleiman, had been

[039-141]captured and executed by Gessi, Gordon's lieutenant, was still detained

[039-142]at Cairo. It so fell out that he went to pay a visit to one of the

[039-143]Ministers at the same time as the new Governor-General. The two men met

[039-144]face to face, and, as he looked into the savage countenance of his old

[039-145]enemy, an extraordinary shock of inspiration ran through Gordon's brain.

[039-146]He was seized, as he explained in a State paper, which he drew up

[039-147]immediately after the meeting, with a 'mystic feeling' that he could

[039-148]trust Zobeir. It was true that Zobeir was 'the greatest slave-hunter who

[039-149]ever existed'; it was true that he had a personal hatred of Gordon,

[039-150]owing to the execution of Suleiman--'and one cannot wonder at it, if one

[039-151]is a father'; it was true that, only a few days previously, on his way

[039-152]to Egypt, Gordon himself had been so convinced of the dangerous

[039-153]character of Zobeir that he had recommended by telegram his removal to

[039-154]Cyprus. But such considerations were utterly obliterated by that one

[039-155]moment of electric impact of personal vision; henceforward, there was a

[039-156]rooted conviction in Gordon's mind that Zobeir was to be trusted, that

[039-157]Zobeir must join him at Khartoum, that Zobeir's presence would paralyse

[039-158]the Mahdi, that Zobeir must succeed him in the government of the country

[039-159]after the evacuation. Did not Sir Evelyn Baring, too, have the mystic

[039-160]feeling? Sir Evelyn Baring confessed that he had not. He distrusted

[039-161]mystic feelings. Zobeir, no doubt, might possibly be useful; but, before

[039-162]deciding upon so important a matter, it was necessary to reflect and to

[039-163]consult.

[039-164]In the meantime, failing Zobeir, something might perhaps be done with

[039-165]the Emir Abdul Shakur, the heir of the Darfur Sultans. The Emir, who had

[039-166]been living in domestic retirement in Cairo, was with some difficulty

[039-167]discovered, given L2,000, an embroidered uniform, together with the

[039-168]largest decoration that could be found, and informed that he was to

[039-169]start at once with General Gordon for the Sudan, where it would be his

[039-170]duty to occupy the province of Darfur, after driving out the forces of

[039-171]the Mahdi. The poor man begged for a little delay; but no delay could be

[039-172]granted. He hurried to the railway station in his frockcoat and fez, and

[039-173]rather the worse for liquor. Several extra carriages for his

[039-174]twenty-three wives and a large quantity of luggage had then to be

[039-175]hitched on to the Governor-General's train; and at the last moment some

[039-176]commotion was caused by the unaccountable disappearance of his

[039-177]embroidered uniform. It was found, but his troubles were not over. On

[039-178]the steamer, General Gordon was very rude to him, and he drowned his

[039-179]chagrin in hot rum and water. At Assuan he disembarked, declaring that

[039-180]he would go no farther. Eventually, however, he got as far as Dongola,

[039-181]whence, after a stay of a few months, he returned with his family to

[039-182]Cairo.
