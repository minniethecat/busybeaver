# Crimean War

The war that made Florence Nightingale a national figure and exposed the rot at the heart of the British military establishment. Strachey's treatment of the Crimean campaign is relentlessly focused not on battles and strategy but on hospitals, drains, and supply chains — the unglamorous infrastructure of war that determined who lived and who died.

## Overview

The war began from a dispute over the protection of Christian holy places in Ottoman Palestine and escalated into a Great Power confrontation over the declining Ottoman Empire. The main theatre was the Crimean Peninsula, where British, French, and Turkish forces besieged the Russian naval base at Sebastopol. The siege lasted nearly a year (1854–1855) and exposed the catastrophic inadequacy of British military logistics.

## Nightingale's War

Nightingale arrived at the British base hospital at Scutari (across the Bosphorus from Constantinople) in November 1854. What she found was a medical system in collapse: wounded men lying on bare floors, no bedding, no adequate food, no medical supplies, and a mortality rate driven not by enemy action but by disease — cholera, dysentery, typhus. Her reforms, resisted at every step by the military medical establishment, transformed the Scutari hospitals and established her reputation.

## Strachey's Treatment

Strachey is almost entirely uninterested in the military conduct of the war. The Charge of the Light Brigade, the siege of Sebastopol, the diplomacy of the peace — all are subordinated to a single story: Florence Nightingale versus the British Army's administration. The war is the setting; the real drama is bureaucratic.
