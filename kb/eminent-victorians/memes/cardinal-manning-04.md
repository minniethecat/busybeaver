[004-001]They arrived at this idea by considering the number five, the number of

[004-002]the senses, as the mystical opponent of the visible and sensible

[004-003]universe--ta aistheta, as distinguished from ta noita. Origen lays down

[004-004]the rule in express terms. '"The number five,"' he says, '"frequently,

[004-005]nay almost always, is taken for the five senses."' In another passage,

[004-006]Keble deals with an even more recondite question. He quotes the teaching

[004-007]of St. Barnabas that 'Abraham, who first gave men circumcision, did

[004-008]thereby perform a spiritual and typical action, looking forward to the

[004-009]Son'. St. Barnabas's argument is as follows: Abraham circumcised of his

[004-010]house men to the number of 318. Why 318? Observe first the 18, then the

[004-011]300. Of the two letters which stand for 18, 10 is represented by 1, 8 by

[004-012]H. 'Thou hast here,' says St. Barnabas, 'the word of Jesus.' As for the

[004-013]300, 'the Cross is represented by Tau, and the letter Tau represents

[004-014]that number'.

[004-015]Unfortunately, however, St. Barnabas's premise was of doubtful validity,

[004-016]as the Rev. Mr. Maitland pointed out, in a pamphlet impugning the

[004-017]conclusions of the Tract. 'The simple fact is,' he wrote, 'that when

[004-018]Abraham pursued Chedorlaomer "he armed his trained servants, BORN IN HIS

[004-019]OWN HOUSE, three hundred and eighteen". When, more than thirteen

[004-020](according to the common chronology, fifteen) years after, he

[004-021]circumcised "all the men of his house, BORN IN THE HOUSE, AND BOUGHT

[004-022]WITH MONEY OF THE STRANGER", and, in fact, every male who was as much as

[004-023]eight days old, we are not told what the number amounted to. Shall we

[004-024]suppose (just for the sake of the interpretation) that Abraham's family

[004-025]had so dwindled in the interval as that now all the males of his

[004-026]household, trained men, slaves, and children, equalled only and exactly

[004-027]the number of his warriors fifteen years before?'

[004-028]The question seems difficult to answer, but Keble had, as a matter of

[004-029]fact, forestalled the argument in the following passage, which had

[004-030]apparently escaped the notice of the Rev. Mr. Maitland:

[004-031]'Now whether the facts were really so or not (if it were, it was surely

[004-032]by special providence), that Abraham's household at the time of the

[004-033]circumcision was exactly the same number as before; still the argument

[004-034]of St. Barnabas will stand. As thus: circumcision had from the

[004-035]beginning, a reference to our SAVIOUR, as in other respects, so in this;

[004-036]that the mystical number, which is the cipher of Jesus crucified, was

[004-037]the number of the first circumcised household in the strength of which

[004-038]Abraham prevailed against the powers of the world. So St. Clement of

[004-039]Alexandria, as cited by Fell.'

[004-040]And Keble supports his contention through ten pages of close print, with

[004-041]references to Aristeas, St. Augustine, St. Jerome, and Dr. Whitby.

[004-042]Writings of this kind could not fail in their effect. Pious youths in

[004-043][[oxford-university|Oxford]] were carried away by them, and began to flock around the standard

[004-044]of [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]. Newman himself became a party chief--encouraging, organising,

[004-045]persuading. His long black figure, swiftly passing through the streets,

[004-046]was pointed at with awe; crowds flocked to his sermons; his words were

[004-047]repeated from mouth to mouth; 'Credo in Newmannum' became a common

[004-048]catchword. Jokes were made about the [[church-of-england|Church of England]], and practices,

[004-049]unknown for centuries, began to be revived. Young men fasted and did

[004-050]penance, recited the hours of the Roman Breviary, and confessed their

[004-051]sins to Dr. Pusey. Nor was the movement confined to Oxford; it spread in

[004-052]widening circles through the parishes of England; the dormant devotion

[004-053]of the country was suddenly aroused. The new strange notion of taking

[004-054]Christianity literally was delightful to earnest minds; but it was also

[004-055]alarming. Really to mean every word you said, when you repeated the

[004-056]Athanasian Creed! How wonderful! And what enticing and mysterious vistas

[004-057]burst upon the view! But then, those vistas, where were they leading?

[004-058]Supposing--oh heavens!--supposing after all they were to lead to--!

[004-059]III

[004-060]IN due course, the Tracts made their appearance at the remote rectory in

[004-061]Sussex. Manning was some years younger than Newman, and the two men had

[004-062]only met occasionally at the University; but now, through common

[004-063]friends, a closer relationship began to grow up between them. It was

[004-064]only to be expected that Newman should be anxious to enroll the rising

[004-065]young Rector among his followers; and, on Manning's side, there were

[004-066]many causes which impelled him to accept the overtures from Oxford.

[004-067]He was a man of a serious and vigorous temperament, to whom it was

[004-068]inevitable that the bold high principles of the Movement should strongly

[004-069]appeal. There was also an element in his mind that element which had

[004-070]terrified him in his childhood with Apocalyptic visions, and urged him

[004-071]in his youth to Bible readings after breakfast--which now brought him

[004-072]under the spell of the Oxford theories of sacramental mysticism. And

[004-073]besides, the Movement offered another attraction: it imputed an

[004-074]extraordinary, transcendent merit to the profession which Manning

[004-075]himself pursued. The cleric was not as his lay brethren; he was a

[004-076]creature apart, chosen by Divine will and sanctified by Divine

[004-077]mysteries. It was a relief to find, when one had supposed that one was

[004-078]nothing but a clergyman, that one might, after all, be something

[004-079]else--one might be a priest.

[004-080]Accordingly, Manning shook off his early Evangelical convictions,

[004-081]started an active correspondence with Newman, and was soon working for

[004-082]the new cause. He collected quotations, and began to translate the works

[004-083]of Optatus for Dr. Pusey. He wrote an article on Justin for the British

[004-084]Critic, "Newman's Magazine". He published a sermon on Faith, with notes

[004-085]and appendices, which was condemned by an evangelical bishop, and

[004-086]fiercely attacked by no less a person than the celebrated Mr. Bowdler.

[004-087]'The sermon,' said Mr Bowdler, in a book which he devoted to the

[004-088]subject, 'was bad enough, but the appendix was abominable.' At the same

[004-089]time he was busy asserting the independence of the Church of England,

[004-090]opposing secular education, and bringing out pamphlets against the

[004-091]Ecclesiastical Commission, which had been appointed by Parliament to

[004-092]report on Church Property. Then we find him in the role of a spiritual

[004-093]director of souls. Ladies met him by stealth in his church, and made

[004-094]their confessions. Over one case--that of a lady, who found herself

[004-095]drifting towards [[rome|Rome]]--he consulted Newman. Newman advised him to

[004-096]'enlarge upon the doctrine of I Cor. vii';

[004-097]'also, I think you must press on her the prospect of benefiting the poor

[004-098]Church, through which she has her baptism, by stopping in it. Does she

[004-099]not care for the souls of all around her, steeped and stifled in

[004-100]Protestantism? How will she best care for them by indulging her own

[004-101]feelings in the communion of Rome, or in denying herself, and staying in

[004-102]sackcloth and ashes to do them good?'

[004-103]Whether these arguments were successful does not appear.

[004-104]For several years after his wife's death, Manning was occupied with

[004-105]these new activities, while his relations with Newman developed into

[004-106]what was apparently a warm friendship. 'And now vive valeque, my dear

[004-107]Manning', we find Newman writing in a letter dated 'in festo S. Car.

[004-108]1838', 'as wishes and prays yours affectionately, John H. Newman'. But,

[004-109]as time went on, the situation became more complicated. [[oxford-movement|Tractarianism]]

[004-110]began to arouse the hostility, not only of the evangelical, but of the

[004-111]moderate churchmen, who could not help perceiving in the ever-deepening,

[004-112]'catholicism' of the Oxford party, the dread approaches of Rome. The

[004-113]"Record" newspaper an influential Evangelical journal--took up the

[004-114]matter and sniffed Popery in every direction; it spoke of certain

[004-115]clergymen as 'tainted'; and after that, preferment seemed to pass those

[004-116]clergymen by. The fact that Manning found it wise to conduct his

[004-117]confessional ministrations in secret was in itself highly significant.

[004-118]It was necessary to be careful, and Manning was very careful indeed. The

[004-119]neighbouring Archdeacon, Mr. Hare, was a low churchman; Manning made

[004-120]friends with him, as warmly, it seemed, as he had made friends with

[004-121]Newman. He corresponded with him, asked his advice about the books he

[004-122]should read, and discussed questions of Theology--'As to Gal. vi 15, we

[004-123]cannot differ.... With a man who reads and reasons I can have no

[004-124]controversy; and you do both.' Archdeacon Hare was pleased, but soon a

[004-125]rumour reached him, which was, to say the least of it, upsetting.

[004-126]Manning had been removing the high pews from a church in Brighton, and

[004-127]putting in open benches in their place. Everyone knew what that meant;

[004-128]everyone knew that a high pew was one of the bulwarks of Protestantism,

[004-129]and that an open bench had upon it the taint of Rome. But Manning

[004-130]hastened to explain:

[004-131]'My dear friend,' he wrote, 'I did not exchange pews for open benches,

[004-132]but got the pews (the same in number) moved from the nave of the church

[004-133]to the walls of the side aisles, so that the whole church has a regular

[004-134]arrangement of open benches, which (irregularly) existed before ... I am

[004-135]not today quite well, so farewell, with much regard--Yours ever, H. E.

[004-136]M.'

[004-137]Archdeacon Hare was reassured.

[004-138]It was important that he should be, for the Archdeacon of Chichester was

[004-139]growing very old, and Hare's influence might be exceedingly useful when

[004-140]a vacancy occurred. So, indeed, it fell out. A new bishop, Dr.

[004-141]Shuttleworth, was appointed to the See, and the old Archdeacon took the

[004-142]opportunity of retiring. Manning was obviously marked out as his

[004-143]successor, but the new bishop happened to be a low churchman, an

[004-144]aggressive low churchman, who went so far as to parody the Tractarian

[004-145]fashion of using Saints' days for the dating of letters by writing 'The

[004-146]Palace, washing-day', at the beginning of his. And--what was equally

[004-147]serious--his views were shared by Mrs. Shuttleworth, who had already

[004-148]decided that the pushing young Rector was 'tainted'. But at the critical

[004-149]moment Archdeacon Hare came to the rescue; he persuaded the Bishop that

[004-150]Manning was safe; and the appointment was accordingly made--behind Mrs.

[004-151]Shuttleworth's back. She was furious, but it was too late; Manning was

[004-152]an Archdeacon. All the lady could do, to indicate her disapprobation,

[004-153]was to put a copy of Mr. Bowdler's book in a conspicuous position on the

[004-154]drawing-room table, when he came to pay his respects at the Palace.

[004-155]Among the letters of congratulation which Manning received, was one from

[004-156]Mr [[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]], with whom he had remained on terms of close friendship

[004-157]since their days together at Oxford.

[004-158]'I rejoice,' Mr Gladstone wrote, 'on your account personally; but more

[004-159]for the sake of the Church. All my brothers-in-law are here and scarcely

[004-160]less delighted than I am. With great glee am I about to write your new

[004-161]address; but, the occasion really calls for higher sentiments; and sure

[004-162]am I that you are one of the men to whom it is specially given to

[004-163]develop the solution of that great problem--how all our minor

[004-164]distractions are to be either abandoned, absorbed, or harmonised through

[004-165]the might of the great principle of communion in the body of Christ.'

[004-166]Manning was an Archdeacon; but he was not yet out of the woods. His

[004-167]relations with the Tractarians had leaked out, and the Record was

[004-168]beginning to be suspicious. If Mrs. Shuttleworth's opinion of him were

[004-169]to become general, it would certainly be a grave matter. Nobody could

[004-170]wish to live and die a mere Archdeacon. And then, at that very moment,

[004-171]an event occurred which made it imperative to take a definite step, one

[004-172]way or the other. That event was the publication of Tract No. 90.

[004-173]For some time it had been obvious to every impartial onlooker that

[004-174]Newman was slipping down an inclined plane at the bottom of which lay

[004-175]one thing, and one thing only--the [[catholic-church|Roman Catholic Church]]. What was

[004-176]surprising was the length of time which he was taking to reach the

[004-177]inevitable destination. Years passed before he came to realise that his

[004-178]grandiose edifice of a Church Universal would crumble to pieces if one

[004-179]of its foundation stones was to be an amatory intrigue of Henry VIII.

[004-180]But, at last he began to see that terrible monarch glowering at him

[004-181]wherever he turned his eyes. First he tried to exorcise the spectre with

[004-182]the rolling periods of the Caroline divines; but it only strutted the

[004-183]more truculently. Then in despair he plunged into the writings of the

[004-184]early Fathers, and sought to discover some way out of his difficulties

[004-185]in the complicated labyrinth of ecclesiastical history. After months

[004-186]spent in the study of the Monophysite heresy, the alarming conclusion

[004-187]began to force itself upon him that the Church of England was perhaps in

[004-188]schism. Eventually he read an article by a Roman Catholic on St.

[004-189]Augustine and the Donatists, which seemed to put the matter beyond

[004-190]doubt. St. Augustine, in the fifth century, had pointed out that the

[004-191]Donatists were heretics because the Bishop of Rome had said so. The

[004-192]argument was crushing; it rang in Newman's ears for days and nights;

[004-193]and, though he continued to linger on in agony for six years more, he

[004-194]never could discover any reply to it. All he could hope to do was to

[004-195]persuade himself and anyone else who liked to listen to him that the

[004-196]holding of Anglican orders was not inconsistent with a belief in the

[004-197]whole cycle of Roman doctrine as laid down at the Council of Trent. In

[004-198]this way he supposed that he could at once avoid the deadly sin of

[004-199]heresy and conscientiously remain a clergyman in the Church of England;

[004-200]and with this end in view, he composed Tract No. 90.
