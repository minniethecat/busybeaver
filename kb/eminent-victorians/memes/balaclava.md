# Balaclava

The name Balaclava is fixed in British memory by the charge of the Light Brigade, but in *Eminent Victorians* it serves a different function: it is the place where two of Strachey's subjects — Florence Nightingale and Charles Gordon — first encountered the realities of war. For Nightingale it was the battle whose wounded flooded the hospitals of Scutari; for Gordon it was the theatre in which a restless young officer found his métier.

## Location

Balaclava is a small port on the south-western coast of the Crimean peninsula, on the Black Sea. During the Crimean War (1853–1856), it served as the British Army's principal supply base, connected to the siege lines before Sevastopol by a single muddy track that became notorious as the source of the army's logistical collapse.

## In the Narrative

### Florence Nightingale and the Balaclava Aftermath

Nightingale arrived at Scutari, on the Asiatic shore of the Bosphorus, on 4 November 1854 — "ten days after the battle of Balaclava, and the day before the battle of Inkerman" [^1]. The timing was catastrophic. The hospitals at Scutari, which had already broken down under the casualties of the Alma, were about to receive the shattered survivors of two more engagements. "The organisation of the hospitals," Strachey writes, "had already given way under the stress of the battle of the Alma, was now to be subjected to the further pressure of two more battles" [^1].

The supply line to Balaclava became a central grievance in Nightingale's subsequent campaign. Vast quantities of stores — "buried beneath munitions of war destined for Balaclava" — passed Scutari without stopping, "and thus hospital materials were sometimes carried to and fro three times over the Black Sea, before they were landed" [^2]. The absurdity of this arrangement, in which the needs of the wounded were subordinated to the inertia of military logistics, became one of her most powerful arguments for reform.

### Gordon at Balaclava

Gordon was twenty-one when the Crimean War broke out. "Before the year was over," Strachey records, "he had managed to get himself transferred to Balaclava" [^3]. The phrase "managed to get himself transferred" is characteristic of Strachey's Gordon — the restless spirit who could never stay where he was put. During the siege of Sevastopol, Gordon "behaved with conspicuous gallantry" [^3], and it was here that his reputation as a soldier who sought danger rather than avoided it was first established.

## Key Events

- **25 October 1854**: Battle of Balaclava — the Thin Red Line, Charge of the Heavy Brigade, and Charge of the Light Brigade [^1]
- **4 November 1854**: Nightingale arrives at Scutari to receive the wounded from Balaclava [^1]
- **1854–1855**: Siege of Sevastopol — Gordon serves with the Royal Engineers at Balaclava [^3]
