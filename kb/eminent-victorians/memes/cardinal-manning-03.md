[003-001]Such were the preoccupations of this young man. Perhaps they would have

[003-002]been different, if he had had a little less of what [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] describes as

[003-003]his 'high severe idea of the intrinsic excellence of Virginity'; but it

[003-004]is useless to speculate.

[003-005]Naturally enough the fierce and burning zeal of Keble had a profound

[003-006]effect upon his mind. The two became intimate friends, and Froude,

[003-007]eagerly seizing upon the doctrines of the elder man, saw to it that they

[003-008]had as full a measure of controversial notoriety as an [[oxford-university|Oxford]] common

[003-009]room could afford. He plunged the metaphysical mysteries of the Holy

[003-010][[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] into the atmosphere of party politics. Surprised Doctors

[003-011]of Divinity found themselves suddenly faced with strange questions which

[003-012]had never entered their heads before. Was the [[church-of-england|Church of England]], or was

[003-013]it not, a part of the Church Catholic? If it was, were not the Reformers

[003-014]of the sixteenth century renegades? Was not the participation of the

[003-015]Body and Blood of Christ essential to the maintenance of Christian life

[003-016]and hope in each individual? Were Timothy and Titus Bishops? Or were

[003-017]they not? If they were, did it not follow that the power of

[003-018]administering the Holy Eucharist was the attribute of a sacred order

[003-019]founded by Christ Himself? Did not the Fathers refer to the tradition of

[003-020]the Church as to something independent of the written word, and

[003-021]sufficient to refute heresy, even alone? Was it not, therefore, God's

[003-022]unwritten word? And did it not demand the same reverence from us as the

[003-023]Scriptures, and for exactly the same reason--BECAUSE IT WAS HIS WORD?

[003-024]The Doctors of Divinity were aghast at such questions, which seemed to

[003-025]lead they hardly knew whither; and they found it difficult to think of

[003-026]very apposite answers. But Hurrell Froude supplied the answers himself

[003-027]readily enough. All Oxford, all England, should know the truth. The time

[003-028]was out of joint, and he was only too delighted to have been born to set

[003-029]it right.

[003-030]But, after all, something more was needed than even the excitement of

[003-031]Froude combined with the conviction of Keble to ruffle seriously the

[003-032]vast calm waters of Christian thought; and it so happened that that

[003-033]thing was not wanting: it was the genius of John Henry Newman. If Newman

[003-034]had never lived, or if his father, when the gig came round on the fatal

[003-035]morning, still undecided between the two Universities, had chanced to

[003-036]turn the horse's head in the direction of Cambridge, who can doubt that

[003-037]the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] would have flickered out its little flame unobserved

[003-038]in the Common Room of Oriel? And how different, too, would have been the

[003-039]fate of Newman himself! He was a child of the Romantic Revival, a

[003-040]creature of emotion and of memory, a dreamer whose secret spirit dwelt

[003-041]apart in delectable mountains, an artist whose subtle senses caught,

[003-042]like a shower in the sunshine, the impalpable rainbow of the immaterial

[003-043]world. In other times, under other skies, his days would have been more

[003-044]fortunate. He might have helped to weave the garland of Meleager, or to

[003-045]mix the lapis lazuli of Fra Angelico, or to chase the delicate truth in

[003-046]the shade of an Athenian palaestra, or his hands might have fashioned

[003-047]those ethereal faces that smile in the niches of Chartres. Even in his

[003-048]own age he might, at Cambridge, whose cloisters have ever been

[003-049]consecrated to poetry and common sense, have followed quietly in Gray's

[003-050]footsteps and brought into flower those seeds of inspiration which now

[003-051]lie embedded amid the faded devotion of the Lyra Apostolica.

[003-052]At Oxford, he was doomed. He could not withstand the last enchantment of

[003-053]the Middle Age. It was in vain that he plunged into the pages of Gibbon

[003-054]or communed for long hours with Beethoven over his beloved violin. The

[003-055]air was thick with clerical sanctity, heavy with the odours of tradition

[003-056]and the soft warmth of spiritual authority; his friendship with Hurrell

[003-057]Froude did the rest. All that was weakest in him hurried him onward, and

[003-058]all that was strongest in him too. His curious and vaulting imagination

[003-059]began to construct vast philosophical fabrics out of the writings of

[003-060]ancient monks, and to dally with visions of angelic visitations and the

[003-061]efficacy of the oil of St Walburga; his emotional nature became absorbed

[003-062]in the partisan passions of a University clique; and his subtle

[003-063]intellect concerned itself more and more exclusively with the

[003-064]dialectical splitting of dogmatical hairs. His future course was marked

[003-065]out for him all too clearly; and yet by a singular chance the true

[003-066]nature of the man was to emerge triumphant in the end. If Newman had

[003-067]died at the age of sixty, today he would have been already forgotten,

[003-068]save by a few ecclesiastical historians; but he lived to write his

[003-069]Apologia, and to reach immortality, neither as a thinker nor as a

[003-070]theologian, but as an artist who has embalmed the poignant history of an

[003-071]intensely human spirit in the magical spices of words.

[003-072]When Froude succeeded in impregnating Newman with the ideas of Keble,

[003-073]the Oxford Movement began. The original and remarkable characteristic of

[003-074]these three men was that they took the Christian Religion au pied de la

[003-075]lettre. This had not been done in England for centuries. When they

[003-076]declared every Sunday that they believed in the Holy Catholic Church,

[003-077]they meant it. When they repeated the Athanasian Creed, they meant it.

[003-078]Even, when they subscribed to the Thirty-nine Articles, they meant it-or

[003-079]at least they thought they did. Now such a state of mind was

[003-080]dangerous--more dangerous indeed--than they at first realised. They had

[003-081]started with the innocent assumption that the Christian Religion was

[003-082]contained in the doctrines of the Church of England; but, the more they

[003-083]examined this matter, the more difficult and dubious it became. The

[003-084]Church of England bore everywhere upon it the signs of human

[003-085]imperfection; it was the outcome of revolution and of compromise, of the

[003-086]exigencies of politicians and the caprices of princes, of the prejudices

[003-087]of theologians and the necessities of the State. How had it happened

[003-088]that this piece of patchwork had become the receptacle for the august

[003-089]and infinite mysteries of the Christian Faith? This was the problem with

[003-090]which Newman and his friends found themselves confronted. Other men

[003-091]might, and apparently did, see nothing very strange in such a situation;

[003-092]but other men saw in Christianity itself scarcely more than a convenient

[003-093]and respectable appendage to existence, by which a sound system of

[003-094]morals was inculcated, and through which one might hope to attain to

[003-095]everlasting bliss.

[003-096]To Newman and Keble it was otherwise. They saw a transcendent

[003-097]manifestation of Divine power flowing down elaborate and immense through

[003-098]the ages; a consecrated priesthood, stretching back, through the mystic

[003-099]symbol of the laying on of hands, to the very Godhead; a whole universe

[003-100]of spiritual beings brought into communion with the Eternal by means of

[003-101]wafers; a great mass of metaphysical doctrines, at once incomprehensible

[003-102]and of incalculable import, laid down with infinite certitude; they saw

[003-103]the supernatural everywhere and at all times, a living force, floating

[003-104]invisible in angels, inspiring saints, and investing with miraculous

[003-105]properties the commonest material things. No wonder that they found such

[003-106]a spectacle hard to bring into line with the institution which had been

[003-107]evolved from the divorce of Henry VIII, the intrigues of Elizabethan

[003-108]parliaments, and the Revolution of 1688. They did, no doubt, soon

[003-109]satisfy themselves that they had succeeded in this apparently hopeless

[003-110]task; but, the conclusions which they came to in order to do so were

[003-111]decidedly startling.

[003-112]The Church of England, they declared, was indeed the one true Church,

[003-113]but she had been under an eclipse since the Reformation; in fact, since

[003-114]she had begun to exist. She had, it is true, escaped the corruptions of

[003-115][[rome|Rome]]; but she had become enslaved by the secular power, and degraded by

[003-116]the false doctrines of Protestantism. The Christian Religion was still

[003-117]preserved intact by the English priesthood, but it was preserved, as it

[003-118]were, unconsciously--a priceless deposit, handed down blindly from

[003-119]generation to generation, and subsisting less by the will of man than

[003-120]through the ordinance of God as expressed in the mysterious virtue of

[003-121]the Sacraments. Christianity, in short, had become entangled in a series

[003-122]of unfortunate circumstances from which it was the plain duty of Newman

[003-123]and his friends to rescue it forthwith. What was curious was that this

[003-124]task had been reserved, in so marked a manner, for them. Some of the

[003-125]divines of the seventeenth century had, perhaps, been vouchsafed

[003-126]glimpses of the truth; but they were glimpses and nothing more. No, the

[003-127]waters of the true Faith had dived underground at the Reformation, and

[003-128]they were waiting for the wand of Newman to strike the rock before they

[003-129]should burst forth once more into the light of day. The whole matter, no

[003-130]doubt, was Providential--what other explanation could there be?

[003-131]The first step, it was clear, was to purge the Church of her shames and

[003-132]her errors. The Reformers must be exposed; the yoke of the secular power

[003-133]must be thrown off; dogma must be reinstated in its old pre-eminence;

[003-134]and Christians must be reminded of what they had apparently

[003-135]forgotten--the presence of the supernatural in daily life. 'It would be

[003-136]a gain to this country,' Keble observed, 'were it vastly more

[003-137]superstitious, more bigoted, more gloomy, more fierce in its religion,

[003-138]than at present it shows itself to be.' 'The only good I know of

[003-139]Cranmer,' said Hurrell Froude, 'was that he burned well.' Newman

[003-140]preached, and soon the new views began to spread. Among the earliest of

[003-141]the converts was Dr Pusey, a man of wealth and learning, a professor, a

[003-142]canon of Christ Church, who had, it was rumoured, been to Germany. Then

[003-143]the Tracts for the Times were started under Newman's editorship, and the

[003-144]Movement was launched upon the world.

[003-145]The Tracts were written 'with the hope of rousing members of our Church

[003-146]to comprehend her alarming position ... as a man might give notice of a

[003-147]fire or inundation, to startle all who heard him'. They may be said to

[003-148]have succeeded in their objective, for the sensation which they caused

[003-149]among clergymen throughout the country was extreme. They dealt with a

[003-150]great variety of questions, but the underlying intention of all of them

[003-151]was to attack the accepted doctrines and practices of the Church of

[003-152]England. Dr. Pusey wrote learnedly on Baptismal Regeneration; he also

[003-153]wrote on Fasting. His treatment of the latter subject met with

[003-154]considerable disapproval, which surprised the Doctor. 'I was not

[003-155]prepared,' he said, 'for people questioning, even in the abstract, the

[003-156]duty of fasting; I thought serious-minded persons at least supposed they

[003-157]practised fasting in some way or other. I assumed the duty to be

[003-158]acknowledged and thought it only undervalued.' We live and learn, even

[003-159]though we have been to Germany.

[003-160]Other tracts discussed the Holy Catholic Church, the Clergy, and the

[003-161]Liturgy. One treated of the question 'whether a clergyman of the Church

[003-162]of England be now bound to have morning and evening prayers daily in his

[003-163]parish church?' Another pointed out the 'Indications of a superintending

[003-164]Providence in the preservation of the Prayer-book and in the changes

[003-165]which it has undergone'. Another consisted of a collection of 'Advent

[003-166]Sermons on Antichrist'. Keble wrote a long and elaborate tract 'On the

[003-167]Mysticism attributed to the Early Fathers of the Church', in which he

[003-168]expressed his opinions upon a large number of curious matters.

[003-169]'According to men's usual way of talking,' he wrote, 'it would be called

[003-170]an accidental circumstance that there were five loaves, not more nor

[003-171]less, in the store of Our Lord and His disciples wherewith to provide

[003-172]the miraculous feast. But the ancient interpreters treat it as designed

[003-173]and providential, in this surely not erring: and their conjecture is

[003-174]that it represents the sacrifice of the whole world of sense, and

[003-175]especially of the Old Dispensation, which, being outward and visible,

[003-176]might be called the dispensation of the senses, to the FATHER of our

[003-177]LORD JESUS CHRIST, to be a pledge and means of communion with Him

[003-178]according to the terms of the new or evangelical law.
