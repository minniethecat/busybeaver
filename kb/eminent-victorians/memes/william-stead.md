# William Thomas Stead

Pioneering editor of the Pall Mall Gazette whose sensational interview with [[general-gordon|General Gordon]] in January 1884 launched the public campaign that sent Gordon to the Sudan — and ultimately to his death at [[khartoum|Khartoum]].

## Life

William Thomas Stead was born in 1849, the son of a Congregationalist minister. He entered journalism as a young man and rose rapidly through sheer energy, conviction, and a flair for the dramatic. By 1883, he was the editor of the Pall Mall Gazette, transforming it into the most sensational and influential newspaper in London.

### The Gordon Interview

> "Mr. Stead, its enterprising editor, went down to Southampton and interviewed the General." [038-023]

Stead's defining role in *Eminent Victorians* came in January 1884, when [[general-gordon]] was about to depart for the Congo. Stead, sensing a story, travelled to Southampton to interview Gordon. During that interview, Gordon laid out his views on the Sudan crisis with characteristic frankness — calling for the appointment of a strong man to manage the evacuation, and effectively nominating himself for the role.

"Mr. Stead made the most of his opportunity" [038-028]. The interview, published in the Pall Mall Gazette on January 9, 1884, created an immediate sensation. It was the interview that made Gordon's mission inevitable — the Government, under intense public pressure, could no longer ignore the popular hero's offer. Within days, Gordon was on his way to [[khartoum|Khartoum]].

### The Campaign

The relationship between Stead and Gordon did not end with the interview. As the crisis in the Sudan deepened, Stead became one of Gordon's most vocal champions in the press, using the Pall Mall Gazette to demand action from a reluctant Government. When Gordon was besieged in [[khartoum|Khartoum]], Stead's paper was at the forefront of the campaign for a relief expedition.

Strachey notes Gordon's strategic use of Stead: "Gordon wrote a letter to [[sir-samuel-baker|Sir Samuel Baker]]" and "declared to Mr. Stead" his views on the Sudan [038-150] [038-164] — the general understood the power of the press and used it to pressure his political masters.

### Later Career

Stead's later career was as dramatic as his rise. In 1885, he was imprisoned for his role in the "Maiden Tribute of Modern Babylon" exposé of child prostitution — a case that made him a martyr to press freedom. He continued to edit influential publications and became an advocate for causes ranging from peace to spiritualism. He died in 1912, aboard the RMS Titanic.

## Character and Beliefs

Stead embodied the "New Journalism" — sensational, crusading, unashamedly personal. His combination of moral fervour and tabloid techniques made him one of the most controversial figures in Victorian journalism. Strachey presents him as a force of nature: the journalist whose interview changed history, for good or ill.

## Historical Significance

Stead was one of the most influential journalists of the Victorian era. His innovations in format, style, and campaigning transformed British journalism. His role in the Gordon affair illustrates the growing power of the press in shaping foreign policy — a power that would only increase in the decades that followed.

## In This Book

Stead appears in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47) as the catalyst: the journalist whose interview turned Gordon's private musings into a public cause. Strachey's treatment is characteristically ironic — Stead's enterprise and Gordon's candour combine to set in motion a chain of events that neither could control.
