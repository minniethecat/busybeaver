# Liberalism (Victorian Era)

The dominant political and intellectual tendency of nineteenth-century Britain, encompassing commitments to individual liberty, parliamentary reform, free trade, religious toleration, and the limitation of ecclesiastical authority in civil affairs. In Strachey's *Eminent Victorians*, Liberalism is the secular tide against which his subjects — Manning, Nightingale, Arnold, and Gordon — all struggled, each in their own way.

## Definition

Liberalism in the Victorian context was not a single doctrine but a cluster of related commitments: the extension of the franchise (Reform Acts of 1832 and 1867), the removal of religious tests for public office (Catholic Emancipation 1829), free trade (repeal of the Corn Laws 1846), and the assertion of parliamentary sovereignty over ecclesiastical privilege. Its intellectual roots lay in the Lockean tradition of natural rights, the utilitarian rationalism of Bentham and the Mills, and the French Revolutionary principles that "began to make their way into England" after the Napoleonic wars (002-106).

## In the Book

### Manning and the Reaction Against Liberalism

Cardinal Manning's entire career can be read as a flight from Liberalism. The young Manning dreamed of a political career in the House of Commons; his father's bankruptcy foreclosed that path and drove him into the Church (002-011). But even there, Liberalism pursued him: the Reform Bill passed, Bentham and the Mills propounded Utilitarianism, and "rumours were abroad of disestablishment" (002-108). Manning responded by moving ever further from the liberal Anglicanism of his youth — through Tractarianism, to Roman Catholicism, and finally to the ultramontane absolutism of Vatican I. Strachey presents this trajectory as a psychological logic: the more the world embraced liberty, the more Manning craved authority.

Liberalism became "the order of the day" in the Vatican itself after Pius IX's death, frustrating Manning's ultramontane programme (017-002). Strachey's irony is palpable: the Cardinal who had spent his life fighting Liberalism found himself outmanoeuvred by it even within the Church he had joined to escape it.

### Other Biographies

Liberalism took different forms across the four biographies. For Dr. Arnold, it meant Broad Church reform — a liberalised Anglicanism that could encompass Dissenters. For Florence Nightingale, it was the bureaucratic rationalism by which she sought to reform the War Office and public health. For General Gordon, Liberalism's anti-imperial and anti-militarist currents complicated his own sense of imperial duty. Strachey's Dr. Arnold "drew up a disquieting scheme for allowing Dissenters into the Church, though it is true that he did not go quite so far as to contemplate the admission of Unitarians" (002-112) — a telling qualification that exposes the limits of Victorian Liberalism even among its advocates.

## Key Figures

- **William Ewart Gladstone** (1809–1898): Four-time Liberal Prime Minister and Manning's intimate friend before their religious divergence. Gladstone embodied the Liberal synthesis of High Church Anglicanism with progressive politics — the very combination Manning found impossible. His long correspondence with Manning on subjects from Poor Law reform to theology is one of Strachey's recurring motifs (006-206).

- **John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873): The philosopher of Utilitarianism and *On Liberty*, whose rationalist Liberalism was precisely the intellectual force the Oxford Movement was founded to combat.

## Related Concepts

- **Gallicanism**: The French Catholic analogue of political Liberalism within the Church — the desire for national ecclesiastical autonomy against Roman centralisation.
- **Ultramontanism**: Liberalism's theological antithesis — the doctrine of absolute papal authority that Manning championed.
- **Broad Church**: The liberal Anglican party that sought to minimise doctrinal requirements and maximise comprehensiveness.
