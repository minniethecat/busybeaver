Louka, the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] family's maidservant, is *Arms and the Man*'s most subversive character. Beneath her servant's uniform, she harbours an ambition that explicitly challenges the class hierarchy of late-19th-century [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]. Her pursuit of [[sergius-saranoff]] -- not as a romantic ideal but as a strategic career move -- makes her the character who most clearly sees through the aristocracy's pretensions while ruthlessly exploiting them. Where [[raina-petkoff]] must learn to abandon romantic illusions, Louka has never had the luxury of holding any.

## 角色身份

- **剧中定位**：Servant（女仆）turned social climber -- the character who most explicitly embodies Shaw's class critique
- **出场幕次**：ACT I / ACT II / ACT III
- **社会阶层**：Servant class（仆从阶层），主动向贵族阶层攀升
- **关联人物**：[[raina-petkoff]]（女主人，阶级对手）· [[sergius-saranoff]]（追求目标）· [[nicola]]（同为仆人，前追求者）· [[catherine-petkoff]]（女主人）· [[major-petkoff]]（男主人）

Louka is introduced "smoking a cigaret" on the rain-swept balcony（002-015） -- a small act of defiance that immediately signals her refusal to accept servant decorum. Shaw uses her throughout the play to expose the hypocrisy that underpins the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] family's genteel self-image.

## 性格特征

Louka is defined by the friction between her low social position and her high ambition. Her character traits are weapons in a class war:

- **主导特质**：Fierce ambition. Louka refuses to accept that birth determines destiny. She tells [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] bluntly: "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola"（002-079） -- and in doing so, reveals that she considers her own soul anything but servile. Her ambition is pragmatic, not romantic: she pursues [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] not because she loves him but because he offers a path out of the servant class.

- **性格矛盾**：Pride vs. dependence. Louka's pride is genuine -- she genuinely considers herself superior to the aristocrats she serves -- but her only route to independence runs through seducing a man who embodies the class system she despises. She must flatter [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s vanity even as she privately sees through it.

- **作者塑造意图**：Shaw uses Louka to argue that class hierarchy corrupts everyone it touches. Louka's ambition is not presented as a moral failing but as a rational response to an irrational system. When she tells [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] "If you were in love with me, would you be ashamed to own me before your friends?"（002-535）, she exposes the gap between his "higher love" rhetoric and his class snobbery.

## 关键情节

### ACT I: The Knowing Servant

Louka's brief appearance in ACT I establishes her as an observer who sees more than she tells. She enters [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s room while [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is hidden behind the curtain and almost certainly suspects something -- her "grimace" at [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s order（001-164） signals both her awareness and her contempt. She is the first character to recognise that the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] is built on secrets and lies.

### ACT II: The Seduction of Sergius

The central Louka-[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] scene in ACT II is the play's most explicit class confrontation. Louka deliberately baits [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], first by insulting [[nicola|Nicola]] -- "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola"（002-079） -- then by challenging [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s courage:

> LOUKA. I do defy her. I will defy her. What do I care for her?
>
> （002-030）

Louka's opening declaration to [[nicola|Nicola]] establishes the terms of her character: defiance is not a tactic but an identity. She follows this with an explicit threat:

> LOUKA. Yes; and you like me the better for it, don't you? But I know some family secrets they wouldn't care to have told, young as I am. Let them quarrel with me if they dare!
>
> （002-047 -- 002-049）

Knowledge is Louka's only weapon, and she wields it openly. Unlike [[nicola|Nicola]], who hoards secrets as insurance, Louka threatens to deploy them as artillery. This willingness to risk everything distinguishes her from every other servant in Shaw's comic universe.

> SERGIUS. (_coming clear of the table and drawing her with him_). I am surprised at myself, Louka. What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, say if he saw me now? What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here? (_Letting go her hand and slipping his arm dexterously round her waist._) Do you consider my figure handsome, Louka?
>
> （002-508 -- 002-513）

Sergius's self-dramatising confession -- rehearsing his own multiple identities while simultaneously seducing the maid -- is the play's most economically ironic passage. He asks philosophical questions ("Which of the six is the real man?") while doing exactly what his "higher love" condemns. Louka, who sees through every layer of his performance, plays along -- and wins.

### ACT III: The Engagement

Louka's triumph comes when [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], having discovered [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s feelings for [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], defiantly announces his engagement to Louka. The scene is richly ambiguous: Louka has achieved her goal, but her victory speech reveals that she sees marriage as a transaction rather than a romance.

> LOUKA. (_suddenly breaking out at Sergius_). I have been insulted by everyone here. You set them the example. You owe me an apology. ... LOUKA. Not to you, his equal and his enemy. To me, his poor servant, he will not refuse to apologize. SERGIUS. (_approvingly_). You are right. (_He bends his knee in his grandest manner._) Forgive me! LOUKA. I forgive you. (_She timidly gives him her hand, which he kisses._) That touch makes me your affianced wife.
>
> （003-1012 -- 003-1028）

The exchange is Louka's masterpiece of manipulation. She demands an apology not as a humiliation but as a test -- and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], predictably, theatricalises his submission into a grand romantic gesture. "That touch makes me your affianced wife" is simultaneously romantic and contractual; Louka has closed the deal. Shaw leaves the audience uncertain whether this represents liberation or merely another form of entrapment.

## 人物弧光

- **起点**（ACT I）：A servant quietly observing and judging her employers, concealing her ambition behind professional deference.
- **转折点**：The confrontation with [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] in ACT II, where she drops the servant mask and speaks to him as an equal -- or rather, as a superior who sees through his pretensions.
- **终点**（ACT III）：Engaged to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and on the verge of entering the class she once served. Her trajectory is the play's most vertical social climb.
- **未变之处**：Her clear-eyed pragmatism. Louka never romanticises her ambition -- she wants security and status, not poetry. Even her "victory" is assessed in practical terms.

## 主题关联

- [[social-class]] -- Louka is Shaw's primary vehicle for class critique. Her ambition reveals the injustice of a system where birth trumps ability, and her success suggests that the old order is permeable -- though perhaps only for those willing to manipulate its hypocrisies.
- [[realism]] -- Louka is the most consistently realistic character in the play. She never indulges in romantic self-deception; she knows what she wants and how to get it.
- [[romanticism]] -- Louka serves as a foil to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s [[romanticism|romantic idealism]]. Both women are intelligent and dissatisfied, but Louka channels her intelligence into strategy rather than fantasy.
- [[servant-agency]] -- Louka's determination to escape servitude through her own efforts challenges the Victorian assumption that servants should be grateful and passive.

## 经典台词

> "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola."（002-079）

Louka's insult to [[nicola|Nicola]] is really a declaration of her own identity: whatever her job title says, she refuses to think of herself as subordinate.

> "What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, do if he found himself in my place?"（002-508）

The question that flips the power dynamic -- Louka demands that [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] imagine himself in *her* social position, a thought exercise the aristocracy rarely performs.

> "I know some family secrets they wouldn't care to have told, young as I am. Let them quarrel with me if they dare!"（002-047 -- 002-049）

This line distills Louka's tactical philosophy: secrets are capital, and the servant who threatens to spend them wields a power the aristocracy cannot match with rank alone.

> "That touch makes me your affianced wife."（003-1027 -- 003-1028）

The line that seals Louka's social ascent -- at once romantic and transactional, it captures the ambiguity of her triumph.

## 批评解读

Critics have debated whether Louka represents genuine feminist agency or merely a cynical individualism. Her ambition is admirable, but her method -- seducing a man who is, by her own analysis, a fraud -- suggests the limited options available to ambitious women in 19th-century [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]].

Shaw gives Louka some of the play's sharpest lines and most penetrating observations, yet her victory is qualified: she exchanges one form of dependence (servitude) for another (marriage to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]). Whether this represents progress or merely lateral movement within a patriarchal system remains a live critical question.

> **Critical Note -- Louka and the Servant-as-Revolutionary**: Louka's dramatic power derives from her refusal to perform the gratitude expected of her class. Unlike [[nicola|Nicola]], whose servility is calculated and strategic, Louka's defiance is visceral and immediate -- she interrupts the play's romantic comedy with the language of class war. When she tells [[nicola|Nicola]] "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola"（002-079）, and he replies complacently "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-081）, Shaw stages in two lines a complete philosophical argument about accommodation versus resistance. Louka's trajectory -- from servant to officer's fiancee -- appears to validate her strategy of defiance, but critics have noted that her success depends on exploiting the same aristocratic hypocrisy she condemns. She rises not by dismantling the class system but by becoming its beneficiary -- a resolution that may be more Fabian (gradual reform from within) than revolutionary.
