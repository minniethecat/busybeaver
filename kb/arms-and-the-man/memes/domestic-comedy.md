# Domestic Comedy

*Arms and the Man* belongs to the genre of domestic comedy — the theatrical tradition that takes the household as its setting and the family's romantic and social complications as its material. But Shaw transforms the genre from within, using its familiar furniture (the drawing room, the love triangle, the servant intrigue) to house arguments that challenge the social order the genre traditionally affirmed.

## The Domestic Setting

The play's settings are resolutely domestic: a [[bedchamber]], a [[library]], a household with its servants, its visitors, its electric bell. The war is outside, glimpsed through windows and recounted in stories. Shaw's innovation is to make the domestic sphere the battleground: the real conflicts are not on the battlefield but in the drawing room, where romantic ideals, class ambitions, and sexual politics clash beneath the surface of polite conversation.

## Genre Subversion

Traditional domestic comedy — the comedy of Sheridan and Wilde that Shaw inherited — typically restored the social order at the end. The right couples married, the impostors were exposed, the household returned to equilibrium. Shaw borrows the form but reverses the function. His play ends not with the restoration of order but with its rearrangement — a new and more rational configuration that rewards honesty over performance and competence over display.
