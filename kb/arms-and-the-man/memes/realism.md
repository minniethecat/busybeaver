# Realism

The philosophical counterweight to romanticism in Shaw's play — a matter-of-fact
approach to soldiering, courtship, and social relations rooted in professional
competence rather than literary ideals. Embodied primarily in [[captain-bluntschli]],
realism functions as both a dramatic foil (exposing the absurdity of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s
heroic posturing and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s operatic fantasies) and a positive philosophy: the
argument that honesty, competence, and clear-eyed self-assessment produce better
outcomes — in war and in love — than the romantic pursuit of glory.

## Definition

Realism in *Arms and the Man* is embodied primarily in the [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]]:
one who fights for a living, carries chocolate instead of cartridges (001-396),
and regards cavalry charges as unprofessional folly (001-448). Shaw's realists —
[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] and [[nicola|Nicola]] — are clear-eyed about their own motives, class position,
and the practical demands of survival.

## In the Play

### ACT I

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] introduces realism through his account of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge.
As a professional, he judges the charge not by its heroism but by its tactical
absurdity:

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it. (001-448)

The [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] — pragmatic sustenance over
romantic ammunition — become the play's central symbol of the real over the
ideal:

> MAN. …I made up my mind to keep the chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that chocolate yesterday. (001-396)

> MAN. He did it like an operatic tenor—a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We did laugh. (001-489)

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s shock registers the gap between her romantic
expectations and [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s "unprofessional" honesty:

> RAINA (_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_). Chocolate! Do you mean to tell me that you carry chocolate in your cartridge case instead of cartridges? (001-398)

> RAINA (_him almost maternally: he shakes his head_). Oh, you are a very poor soldier—a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture—remember that. (001-566)

The epithet "chocolate cream soldier" — coined by Raina in this moment of comic disillusionment — will echo through the entire play, transforming from insult to pet name to proof of the romantic self-deception that realism exists to correct.

### ACT II

[[nicola|Nicola]], the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] family servant, extends realism into the domestic sphere.
His calculating servility shocks [[louka|Louka]] but
demonstrates Shaw's point: realism without moral content can become mere
self-interest:

> NICOLA. …and I don't ask the reason now that you cry off. I'm a professional. I know my duty. You have a great future before you if you are careful. (003-764)

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], by contrast, integrates professional competence
with personal integrity, offering a more complete model of the realist ethos.

### ACT III

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] returns as the successful realist: he has inherited his father's
hotels, knows his exact age and assets, and wins [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] through honesty rather
than heroic posturing. His famous line is
Shaw's comic verdict on romanticism, delivered by realism's most charming
representative:

> BLUNTSCHLI. (_promptly_). An incurably romantic disposition. I ran away from home twice when I was a boy. I went into the army instead of into my father's business. I climbed the balcony of this house when a man of sense would have dived into the nearest cellar. (003-1082)

> RAINA (_in uncontrollable vexation_). I quite agree with your account of yourself. You are a romantic idiot. (_Bluntschli is unspeakably taken aback._) Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three. (003-1109)

The chocolate cream soldier's final vindication comes when the truth outs:

> BLUNTSCHLI (_rising_). It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier. (_Petkoff and Sergius are equally astonished._) The gracious young lady saved my life by giving me chocolate creams when I was starving—shall I ever forget their flavour! (003-963)

## Key Scene

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns…? (001-448)

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s professional critique exposes the cavalry charge — [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s great
heroic deed — as tactical stupidity. Shaw uses the [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]]'s
perspective to debunk military romanticism. This single question, asked by a fleeing mercenary to his captor, is the philosophical fulcrum on which the entire play turns.

> RAINA (_promptly_). An incurably romantic disposition. I ran away from home twice before I was twenty-one. (003-1082)

Raina's confession — extracted by Bluntschli's relentless honesty — is realism's diagnostic triumph. She names her condition, and in naming it, begins to overcome it.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw presents realism not as cynicism but as **professional integrity**.
The [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] fights efficiently and survives; the amateur hero
charges machine guns and gets his men killed. For Shaw, the realist's
awareness of practical constraints — money, class, mortality — is a form
of honesty that romanticism lacks. Bluntschli's Swiss neutrality, his
mercenary professionalism, and his hotel inheritance are not merely
comic details — they constitute an argument: that the world would be
better run by competent professionals than by romantic amateurs.

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's realism in this play prefigures his later, more explicitly
philosophical works. The chocolate cream soldier is an early sketch of
the Shavian hero — the practical man who cuts through convention with
candor and competence. Critics have noted that Bluntschli's realism is
gendered: his professional ethos is coded masculine, while Raina's
romanticism is coded feminine, and the play's resolution — Raina
accepting Bluntschli's unromantic proposal — can be read either as
her liberation from feminine delusion or her absorption into masculine
rationality. The ambiguity is characteristically Shavian.

> Shaw's realism is never merely documentary; it is always comic, always argumentative, and always in service of the idea that the world can be improved if we would only see it clearly. — Michael Holroyd, *Bernard Shaw* (1988)
