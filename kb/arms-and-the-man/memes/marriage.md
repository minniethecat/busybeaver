# Marriage

Marriage in *Arms and the Man* is treated not as the culmination of romance but as a social institution subject to the same forces of [[social-class|class]], money, and pragmatism that govern every other aspect of the characters' lives. The play features two engagements — one broken, one formed — and a marriage proposal that is more business negotiation than love declaration.

## In the Play

### ACT I

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s engagement to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] is the play's starting emotional reality. She has just learned of his glorious cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]], and her opening rhapsody establishes the engagement as a product of romantic idealism. The photograph on her chest of drawers — Sergius "in the uniform of a Bulgarian officer, extremely handsome"（001-024）— is an icon of an imaginary man. Raina is engaged to a portrait, a uniform, an idea.

### ACT II

The engagement begins to unravel under the pressure of reality. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s flirtation with the servant [[louka|Louka]] reveals that his romantic posturing is not exclusive to his fiancee. Raina's own emotional shift toward [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] — whose chocolate creams have displaced Sergius's cavalry charge in her imagination — makes her "longing to do or say something dreadful" to shock Sergius（002-716）. The engagement, kept alive only by social convention and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]'s management, is a hollow shell by the midpoint of the act.

### ACT III

The play's matrimonial resolution is characteristically Shawian in its practicality. Bluntschli's proposal to Raina is precipitated not by a romantic declaration but by the revelation of his inheritance. When [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] asks about Bluntschli's prospects, the Swiss captain answers with a hotel inventory rather than a love poem. Raina's acceptance — and her father's — is sealed by Bluntschli's net worth, not his charm.

The parallel courtship of [[louka|Louka]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] follows a different logic. Louka, the servant girl, has maneuvered Sergius into a position where honor demands he marry her. The Byronic cavalry officer — who spent three acts denouncing the servitude of social convention — is ensnared by the conventions he despises. Louka's triumph is the victory of practical ambition over romantic posturing.

## Key Scene

> BLUNTSCHLI. I have about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks — but I haven't a single reason for asking you to marry me.（003-1294–1296）

The hotel inventory-as-proposal is Shaw's most economical demolition of the romantic marriage plot. Bluntschli offers not love but linen — and Raina accepts. The gesture is comic but the point is serious: marriage, in Shaw's world, is a material arrangement that may or may not include love, not a love story that happens to include material arrangements.

## Shaw's Position

As a critic of Victorian marriage conventions, Shaw uses the play to advance several arguments: that arranged marriages based on class compatibility are no more absurd than romantic marriages based on cavalry charges; that money is a legitimate, even honest, factor in matrimonial decisions; and that the servant class has as much right to pursue advantageous marriages as their masters.

- **Target of satire**: The romantic marriage plot that disguises material calculations as love at first sight
- **Alternative proposed**: Marriage as an honest contract between adults who understand what they are trading
