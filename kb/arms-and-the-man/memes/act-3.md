[003-001]In [[library|the library]] after lunch. It is not much of a library, its literary

[003-002]equipment consisting of a single fixed shelf stocked with old

[003-003]paper-covered novels, broken backed, coffee stained, torn and thumbed,

[003-004]and a couple of little hanging shelves with a few gift books on them,

[003-005]the rest of the wall space being occupied by trophies of war and the

[003-006]chase. But it is a most comfortable sitting-room. A row of three large

[003-007]windows in the front of the house shew a mountain panorama, which is

[003-008]just now seen in one of its softest aspects in the mellowing afternoon

[003-009]light. In the left hand corner, a square earthenware stove, a perfect

[003-010]tower of colored pottery, rises nearly to the ceiling and guarantees

[003-011]plenty of warmth. The ottoman in the middle is a circular bank of

[003-012]decorated cushions, and the window seats are well upholstered divans.

[003-013]Little Turkish tables, one of them with an elaborate hookah on it, and

[003-014]a screen to match them, complete the handsome effect of the furnishing.

[003-015]There is one object, however, which is hopelessly out of keeping with

[003-016]its surroundings. This is a small kitchen table, much the worse for

[003-017]wear, fitted as a writing table with an old canister full of pens, an

[003-018]eggcup filled with ink, and a deplorable scrap of severely used pink

[003-019]blotting paper.

[003-020]At the side of this table, which stands on the right, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is

[003-021]hard at work, with a couple of maps before him, writing orders. At the

[003-022]head of it sits [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who is also supposed to be at work, but who is

[003-023]actually gnawing the feather of a pen, and contemplating Bluntschli’s

[003-024]quick, sure, businesslike progress with a mixture of envious irritation

[003-025]at his own incapacity, and awestruck wonder at an ability which seems

[003-026]to him almost miraculous, though its prosaic character forbids him to

[003-027]esteem it. The major is comfortably established on the ottoman, with a

[003-028]newspaper in his hand and the tube of the hookah within his reach.

[003-029][[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] sits at the stove, with her back to them, embroidering.

[003-030][[raina-petkoff|Raina]], reclining on the divan under the left hand window, is gazing in

[003-031]a daydream out at the Balkan landscape, with a neglected novel in her

[003-032]lap.

[003-033]The door is on the left. The button of the electric bell is between the

[003-034]door and the fireplace.

[003-035]PETKOFF.

[003-036](_looking up from his paper to watch how they are getting on at the

[003-037]table_). Are you sure I can’t help you in any way, Bluntschli?

[003-038]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-039](_without interrupting his writing or looking up_). Quite sure, thank

[003-040]you. Saranoff and I will manage it.

[003-041]SERGIUS.

[003-042](_grimly_). Yes: we’ll manage it. He finds out what to do; draws up the

[003-043]orders; and I sign ’em. Division of labour, Major. (_Bluntschli passes

[003-044]him a paper._) Another one? Thank you. (_He plants the papers squarely

[003-045]before him; sets his chair carefully parallel to them; and signs with

[003-046]the air of a man resolutely performing a difficult and dangerous

[003-047]feat._) This hand is more accustomed to the sword than to the pen.

[003-048]PETKOFF.

[003-049]It’s very good of you, Bluntschli, it is indeed, to let yourself be put

[003-050]upon in this way. Now are you quite sure I can do nothing?

[003-051]CATHERINE.

[003-052](_in a low, warning tone_). You can stop interrupting, Paul.

[003-053]PETKOFF.

[003-054](_starting and looking round at her_). Eh? Oh! Quite right, my love,

[003-055]quite right. (_He takes his newspaper up, but lets it drop again._) Ah,

[003-056]you haven’t been campaigning, Catherine: you don’t know how pleasant it

[003-057]is for us to sit here, after a good lunch, with nothing to do but enjoy

[003-058]ourselves. There’s only one thing I want to make me thoroughly

[003-059]comfortable.

[003-060]CATHERINE.

[003-061]What is that?

[003-062]PETKOFF.

[003-063]My old coat. I’m not at home in this one: I feel as if I were on

[003-064]parade.

[003-065]CATHERINE.

[003-066]My dear Paul, how absurd you are about that old coat! It must be

[003-067]hanging in the blue closet where you left it.

[003-068]PETKOFF.

[003-069]My dear Catherine, I tell you I’ve looked there. Am I to believe my own

[003-070]eyes or not? (_Catherine quietly rises and presses the button of the

[003-071]electric bell by the fireplace._) What are you shewing off that bell

[003-072]for? (_She looks at him majestically, and silently resumes her chair

[003-073]and her needlework._) My dear: if you think the obstinacy of your sex

[003-074]can make a coat out of two old dressing gowns of Raina’s, your

[003-075]waterproof, and my mackintosh, you’re mistaken. That’s exactly what the

[003-076]blue closet contains at present. (_[[nicola|Nicola]] presents himself._)

[003-077]CATHERINE.

[003-078](_unmoved by [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]’s sally_). Nicola: go to the blue closet and bring

[003-079]your master’s old coat here—the braided one he usually wears in the

[003-080]house.

[003-081]NICOLA.

[003-082]Yes, madam. (_Nicola goes out._)

[003-083]PETKOFF.

[003-084]Catherine.

[003-085]CATHERINE.

[003-086]Yes, Paul?

[003-087]PETKOFF.

[003-088]I bet you any piece of jewellery you like to order from Sofia against a

[003-089]week’s housekeeping money, that the coat isn’t there.

[003-090]CATHERINE.

[003-091]Done, Paul.

[003-092]PETKOFF.

[003-093](_excited by the prospect of a gamble_). Come: here’s an opportunity

[003-094]for some sport. Who’ll bet on it? Bluntschli: I’ll give you six to one.

[003-095]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-096](_imperturbably_). It would be robbing you, Major. Madame is sure to be

[003-097]right. (_Without looking up, he passes another batch of papers to

[003-098]Sergius._)

[003-099]SERGIUS.

[003-100](_also excited_). Bravo, [[switzerland|Switzerland]]! Major: I bet my best charger

[003-101]against an Arab mare for Raina that Nicola finds the coat in the blue

[003-102]closet.

[003-103]PETKOFF.

[003-104](_eagerly_). Your best char—

[003-105]CATHERINE.

[003-106](_hastily interrupting him_). Don’t be foolish, Paul. An Arabian mare

[003-107]will cost you 50,000 levas.

[003-108]RAINA.

[003-109](_suddenly coming out of her picturesque revery_). Really, mother, if

[003-110]you are going to take the jewellery, I don’t see why you should grudge

[003-111]me my Arab.

[003-112](_Nicola comes back with the coat and brings it to Petkoff, who can

[003-113]hardly believe his eyes._)

[003-114]CATHERINE.

[003-115]Where was it, Nicola?

[003-116]NICOLA.

[003-117]Hanging in the blue closet, madam.

[003-118]PETKOFF.

[003-119]Well, I am d—

[003-120]CATHERINE.

[003-121](_stopping him_). Paul!

[003-122]PETKOFF.

[003-123]I could have sworn it wasn’t there. Age is beginning to tell on me. I’m

[003-124]getting hallucinations. (_To Nicola._) Here: help me to change. Excuse

[003-125]me, Bluntschli. (_He begins changing coats, Nicola acting as valet._)

[003-126]Remember: I didn’t take that bet of yours, Sergius. You’d better give

[003-127]Raina that Arab steed yourself, since you’ve roused her expectations.

[003-128]Eh, Raina? (_He looks round at her; but she is again rapt in the

[003-129]landscape. With a little gush of paternal affection and pride, he

[003-130]points her out to them and says_) She’s dreaming, as usual.

[003-131]SERGIUS.

[003-132]Assuredly she shall not be the loser.

[003-133]PETKOFF.

[003-134]So much the better for her. I shan’t come off so cheap, I expect. (_The

[003-135]change is now complete. Nicola goes out with the discarded coat._) Ah,

[003-136]now I feel at home at last. (_He sits down and takes his newspaper with

[003-137]a grunt of relief._)

[003-138]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-139](_to Sergius, handing a paper_). That’s the last order.

[003-140]PETKOFF.

[003-141](_jumping up_). What! finished?

[003-142]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-143]Finished. (_Petkoff goes beside Sergius; looks curiously over his left

[003-144]shoulder as he signs; and says with childlike envy_) Haven’t you

[003-145]anything for me to sign?

[003-146]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-147]Not necessary. His signature will do.

[003-148]PETKOFF.

[003-149]Ah, well, I think we’ve done a thundering good day’s work. (_He goes

[003-150]away from the table._) Can I do anything more?

[003-151]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-152]You had better both see the fellows that are to take these. (_To

[003-153]Sergius._) Pack them off at once; and shew them that I’ve marked on the

[003-154]orders the time they should hand them in by. Tell them that if they

[003-155]stop to drink or tell stories—if they’re five minutes late, they’ll

[003-156]have the skin taken off their backs.

[003-157]SERGIUS.

[003-158](_rising indignantly_). I’ll say so. And if one of them is man enough

[003-159]to spit in my face for insulting him, I’ll buy his discharge and give

[003-160]him a pension. (_He strides out, his humanity deeply outraged._)

[003-161]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-162](_confidentially_). Just see that he talks to them properly, Major,

[003-163]will you?

[003-164]PETKOFF.

[003-165](_officiously_). Quite right, Bluntschli, quite right. I’ll see to it.

[003-166](_He goes to the door importantly, but hesitates on the threshold._) By

[003-167]the bye, Catherine, you may as well come, too. They’ll be far more

[003-168]frightened of you than of me.

[003-169]CATHERINE.

[003-170](_putting down her embroidery_). I daresay I had better. You will only

[003-171]splutter at them. (_She goes out, Petkoff holding the door for her and

[003-172]following her._)

[003-173]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-174]What a country! They make cannons out of cherry trees; and the officers

[003-175]send for their wives to keep discipline! (_He begins to fold and docket

[003-176]the papers. Raina, who has risen from the divan, strolls down the room

[003-177]with her hands clasped behind her, and looks mischievously at him._)

[003-178]RAINA.

[003-179]You look ever so much nicer than when we last met. (_He looks up,

[003-180]surprised._) What have you done to yourself?

[003-181]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-182]Washed; brushed; good night’s sleep and breakfast. That’s all.

[003-183]RAINA.

[003-184]Did you get back safely that morning?

[003-185]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-186]Quite, thanks.

[003-187]RAINA.

[003-188]Were they angry with you for running away from Sergius’s charge?

[003-189]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-190]No, they were glad; because they’d all just run away themselves.

[003-191]RAINA.

[003-192](_going to the table, and leaning over it towards him_). It must have

[003-193]made a lovely story for them—all that about me and my room.

[003-194]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-195]Capital story. But I only told it to one of them—a particular friend.

[003-196]RAINA.

[003-197]On whose discretion you could absolutely rely?

[003-198]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-199]Absolutely.

[003-200]RAINA.

[003-201]Hm! He told it all to my father and Sergius the day you exchanged the

[003-202]prisoners. (_She turns away and strolls carelessly across to the other

[003-203]side of the room._)

[003-204]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-205](_deeply concerned and half incredulous_). No! you don’t mean that, do

[003-206]you?

[003-207]RAINA.

[003-208](_turning, with sudden earnestness_). I do indeed. But they don’t know

[003-209]that it was in this house that you hid. If Sergius knew, he would

[003-210]challenge you and kill you in a duel.

[003-211]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-212]Bless me! then don’t tell him.

[003-213]RAINA.

[003-214](_full of reproach for his levity_). Can you realize what it is to me

[003-215]to deceive him? I want to be quite perfect with Sergius—no meanness, no

[003-216]smallness, no deceit. My relation to him is the one really beautiful

[003-217]and noble part of my life. I hope you can understand that.

[003-218]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-219](_sceptically_). You mean that you wouldn’t like him to find out that

[003-220]the story about the ice pudding was a—a—a—You know.

[003-221]RAINA.

[003-222](_wincing_). Ah, don’t talk of it in that flippant way. I lied: I know

[003-223]it. But I did it to save your life. He would have killed you. That was

[003-224]the second time I ever uttered a falsehood. (_Bluntschli rises quickly

[003-225]and looks doubtfully and somewhat severely at her._) Do you remember

[003-226]the first time?

[003-227]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-228]I! No. Was I present?

[003-229]RAINA.

[003-230]Yes; and I told the officer who was searching for you that you were not

[003-231]present.

[003-232]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-233]True. I should have remembered it.

[003-234]RAINA.

[003-235](_greatly encouraged_). Ah, it is natural that you should forget it

[003-236]first. It cost you nothing: it cost me a lie!—a lie!! (_She sits down

[003-237]on the ottoman, looking straight before her with her hands clasped on

[003-238]her knee. Bluntschli, quite touched, goes to the ottoman with a

[003-239]particularly reassuring and considerate air, and sits down beside

[003-240]her._)

[003-241]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-242]My dear young lady, don’t let this worry you. Remember: I’m a soldier.

[003-243]Now what are the two things that happen to a soldier so often that he

[003-244]comes to think nothing of them? One is hearing people tell lies (_Raina

[003-245]recoils_): the other is getting his life saved in all sorts of ways by

[003-246]all sorts of people.

[003-247]RAINA.

[003-248](_rising in indignant protest_). And so he becomes a creature incapable

[003-249]of faith and of gratitude.

[003-250]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-251](_making a wry face_). Do you like gratitude? I don’t. If pity is akin

[003-252]to love, gratitude is akin to the other thing.

[003-253]RAINA.

[003-254]Gratitude! (_Turning on him._) If you are incapable of gratitude you

[003-255]are incapable of any noble sentiment. Even animals are grateful. Oh, I

[003-256]see now exactly what you think of me! You were not surprised to hear me

[003-257]lie. To you it was something I probably did every day—every hour. That

[003-258]is how men think of women. (_She walks up the room melodramatically._)

[003-259]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-260](_dubiously_). There’s reason in everything. You said you’d told only

[003-261]two lies in your whole life. Dear young lady: isn’t that rather a short

[003-262]allowance? I’m quite a straightforward man myself; but it wouldn’t last

[003-263]me a whole morning.

[003-264]RAINA.

[003-265](_staring haughtily at him_). Do you know, sir, that you are insulting

[003-266]me?

[003-267]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-268]I can’t help it. When you get into that noble attitude and speak in

[003-269]that thrilling voice, I admire you; but I find it impossible to believe

[003-270]a single word you say.

[003-271]RAINA.

[003-272](_superbly_). Captain Bluntschli!

[003-273]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-274](_unmoved_). Yes?

[003-275]RAINA.

[003-276](_coming a little towards him, as if she could not believe her

[003-277]senses_). Do you mean what you said just now? Do you know what you said

[003-278]just now?

[003-279]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-280]I do.

[003-281]RAINA.

[003-282](_gasping_). I! I!!! (_She points to herself incredulously, meaning “I,

[003-283]Raina Petkoff, tell lies!” He meets her gaze unflinchingly. She

[003-284]suddenly sits down beside him, and adds, with a complete change of

[003-285]manner from the heroic to the familiar_) How did you find me out?

[003-286]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-287](_promptly_). Instinct, dear young lady. Instinct, and experience of

[003-288]the world.

[003-289]RAINA.

[003-290](_wonderingly_). Do you know, you are the first man I ever met who did

[003-291]not take me seriously?

[003-292]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-293]You mean, don’t you, that I am the first man that has ever taken you

[003-294]quite seriously?

[003-295]RAINA.

[003-296]Yes, I suppose I do mean that. (_Cosily, quite at her ease with him._)

[003-297]How strange it is to be talked to in such a way! You know, I’ve always

[003-298]gone on like that—I mean the noble attitude and the thrilling voice. I

[003-299]did it when I was a tiny child to my nurse. She believed in it. I do it

[003-300]before my parents. They believe in it. I do it before Sergius. He

[003-301]believes in it.

[003-302]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-303]Yes: he’s a little in that line himself, isn’t he?

[003-304]RAINA.

[003-305](_startled_). Do you think so?

[003-306]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-307]You know him better than I do.

[003-308]RAINA.

[003-309]I wonder—I wonder is he? If I thought that—! (_Discouraged._) Ah, well,

[003-310]what does it matter? I suppose, now that you’ve found me out, you

[003-311]despise me.

[003-312]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-313](_warmly, rising_). No, my dear young lady, no, no, no a thousand

[003-314]times. It’s part of your youth—part of your charm. I’m like all the

[003-315]rest of them—the nurse—your parents—Sergius: I’m your infatuated

[003-316]admirer.

[003-317]RAINA.

[003-318](_pleased_). Really?

[003-319]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-320](_slapping his breast smartly with his hand, German fashion_). Hand

[003-321]aufs Herz! Really and truly.

[003-322]RAINA.

[003-323](_very happy_). But what did you think of me for giving you my

[003-324]portrait?

[003-325]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-326](_astonished_). Your portrait! You never gave me your portrait.

[003-327]RAINA.

[003-328](_quickly_). Do you mean to say you never got it?

[003-329]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-330]No. (_He sits down beside her, with renewed interest, and says, with

[003-331]some complacency._) When did you send it to me?

[003-332]RAINA.

[003-333](_indignantly_). I did not send it to you. (_She turns her head away,

[003-334]and adds, reluctantly._) It was in the pocket of that coat.

[003-335]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-336](_pursing his lips and rounding his eyes_). Oh-o-oh! I never found it.

[003-337]It must be there still.

[003-338]RAINA.

[003-339](_springing up_). There still!—for my father to find the first time he

[003-340]puts his hand in his pocket! Oh, how could you be so stupid?

[003-341]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-342](_rising also_). It doesn’t matter: it’s only a photograph: how can he

[003-343]tell who it was intended for? Tell him he put it there himself.

[003-344]RAINA.

[003-345](_impatiently_). Yes, that is so clever—so clever! What shall I do?

[003-346]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-347]Ah, I see. You wrote something on it. That was rash!

[003-348]RAINA.

[003-349](_annoyed almost to tears_). Oh, to have done such a thing for you, who

[003-350]care no more—except to laugh at me—oh! Are you sure nobody has touched

[003-351]it?

[003-352]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-353]Well, I can’t be quite sure. You see I couldn’t carry it about with me

[003-354]all the time: one can’t take much luggage on active service.

[003-355]RAINA.

[003-356]What did you do with it?

[003-357]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-358]When I got through to Peerot I had to put it in safe keeping somehow. I

[003-359]thought of the railway cloak room; but that’s the surest place to get

[003-360]looted in modern warfare. So I pawned it.

[003-361]RAINA.

[003-362]Pawned it!!!

[003-363]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-364]I know it doesn’t sound nice; but it was much the safest plan. I

[003-365]redeemed it the day before yesterday. Heaven only knows whether the

[003-366]pawnbroker cleared out the pockets or not.

[003-367]RAINA.

[003-368](_furious—throwing the words right into his face_). You have a low,

[003-369]shopkeeping mind. You think of things that would never come into a

[003-370]gentleman’s head.

[003-371]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-372](_phlegmatically_). That’s the Swiss national character, dear lady.

[003-373]RAINA.

[003-374]Oh, I wish I had never met you. (_She flounces away and sits at the

[003-375]window fuming._)

[003-376](_[[louka|Louka]] comes in with a heap of letters and telegrams on her salver,

[003-377]and crosses, with her bold, free gait, to the table. Her left sleeve is

[003-378]looped up to the shoulder with a brooch, shewing her naked arm, with a

[003-379]broad gilt bracelet covering the bruise._)

[003-380]LOUKA.

[003-381](_to Bluntschli_). For you. (_She empties the salver recklessly on the

[003-382]table._) The messenger is waiting. (_She is determined not to be civil

[003-383]to a Servian, even if she must bring him his letters._)

[003-384]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-385](_to Raina_). Will you excuse me: the last postal delivery that reached

[003-386]me was three weeks ago. These are the subsequent accumulations. Four

[003-387]telegrams—a week old. (_He opens one._) Oho! Bad news!

[003-388]RAINA.

[003-389](_rising and advancing a little remorsefully_). Bad news?

[003-390]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-391]My father’s dead. (_He looks at the telegram with his lips pursed,

[003-392]musing on the unexpected change in his arrangements._)

[003-393]RAINA.

[003-394]Oh, how very sad!

[003-395]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-396]Yes: I shall have to start for home in an hour. He has left a lot of

[003-397]big hotels behind him to be looked after. (_Takes up a heavy letter in

[003-398]a long blue envelope._) Here’s a whacking letter from the family

[003-399]solicitor. (_He pulls out the enclosures and glances over them._) Great

[003-400]Heavens! Seventy! Two hundred! (_In a crescendo of dismay._) Four

[003-401]hundred! Four thousand!! Nine thousand six hundred!!! What on earth

[003-402]shall I do with them all?

[003-403]RAINA.

[003-404](_timidly_). Nine thousand hotels?

[003-405]BLUNTSCHLI.

[003-406]Hotels! Nonsense. If you only knew!—oh, it’s too ridiculous! Excuse me:

[003-407]I must give my fellow orders about starting. (_He leaves the room

[003-408]hastily, with the documents in his hand._)

[003-409]LOUKA.

[003-410](_tauntingly_). He has not much heart, that Swiss, though he is so fond

[003-411]of the Servians. He has not a word of grief for his poor father.

[003-412]RAINA.

[003-413](_bitterly_). Grief!—a man who has been doing nothing but killing

[003-414]people for years! What does he care? What does any soldier care? (_She

[003-415]goes to the door, evidently restraining her tears with difficulty._)

[003-416]LOUKA.

[003-417]Major Saranoff has been fighting, too; and he has plenty of heart left.

[003-418](_Raina, at the door, looks haughtily at her and goes out._) Aha! I

[003-419]thought you wouldn’t get much feeling out of your soldier. (_She is

[003-420]following Raina when Nicola enters with an armful of logs for the

[003-421]fire._)

[003-422]NICOLA.

[003-423](_grinning amorously at her_). I’ve been trying all the afternoon to

[003-424]get a minute alone with you, my girl. (_His countenance changes as he

[003-425]notices her arm._) Why, what fashion is that of wearing your sleeve,
