# Romantic Education

"Romantic education" is the phrase that best describes the cultural formation Shaw's play anatomizes. [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] has been raised on a diet of [[alexander-pushkin|Pushkin]]'s poetry, [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s heroes, and [[verdi|Verdi]]'s operas — a curriculum that has taught her to expect life to follow the patterns of romantic art. The play is the record of that education's failure and Raina's gradual reconstruction of a more adequate worldview.

## The Curriculum

Raina's romantic education has three components, each targeted by a different aspect of Shaw's critique:

1. **Literature (Pushkin, Byron):** Teaches that love is transcendent passion and that men are noble heroes. Shaw's counter: the chocolate cream soldier and the hotel inventory.

2. **Opera (Verdi):** Teaches that life is a drama of grand emotions and noble sacrifices. Shaw's counter: the [[anti-climax]] and the practical arrangement.

3. **Popular culture (novels, photographs):** Teaches that appearance is substance and that the [[uniform]] is the man. Shaw's counter: the coat that replaces the uniform, the competence that replaces the cavalry charge.

## The Education's Failure

The play is structured as Raina's education in reverse — the systematic dismantling of everything her romantic education taught her. The hero turns out to be a fool, the enemy turns out to be the man she should marry, the love that should be transcendent turns out to be a practical arrangement, and the "noble attitude" she was taught to perform turns out to be a costume she can finally discard.

## Shaw's Pedagogical Project

Shaw, the lifelong educator and pamphleteer, understood his plays as instruments of re-education. *Arms and the Man* is a pedagogical intervention — an attempt to cure its audience of the romantic education it has absorbed from literature, opera, and popular culture. The laughter the play provokes is the laughter of recognition: we laugh at Raina's romantic illusions because we recognize them as our own.
