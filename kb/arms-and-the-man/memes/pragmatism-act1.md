# Pragmatism in Act I

Pragmatism enters the play through [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s exhausted, unromantic presence. While [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] are intoxicated by stories of heroic cavalry charges, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] climbs a drainpipe to escape death and asks for food. His first self-description disclaims all national and ideological allegiance:

> MAN. Austrian! not I. Don't hate me, dear young lady. I am only a Swiss, fighting merely as a professional soldier. I joined Servia because it was nearest to me. Be generous: you've beaten us hollow.（001-361）

This is pragmatism's founding declaration: war is a trade, not a crusade; nationality is geography, not identity; survival is the goal, not glory. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s Swiss neutrality — neither Austrian nor Servian, neither patriot nor ideologue — is the geopolitical expression of his pragmatic philosophy.

His critique of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge follows the same logic:

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it.（001-448）

What [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] celebrates as "our patriotism—our heroic ideals" (001-085), [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] diagnoses as tactical incompetence. The same event, viewed through romantic and pragmatic lenses, yields opposite judgments: heroism or stupidity. Shaw's point is not that one perspective is more true than the other, but that the pragmatic perspective has survival value — it keeps men alive — while the romantic perspective gets them killed.

The Act closes with [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s instinctive coinage, which names the philosophy before she understands it:

> RAINA. Oh, you are a very poor soldier—a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture—remember that.（001-566）

The "chocolate cream soldier" is pragmatism domesticated — sweet, practical, and faintly ridiculous, yet ultimately the only kind of soldier worth being. Raina's coinage is a moment of intuitive insight: she instinctively reaches for the domestic and the edible to describe a man who defies every military convention she has been taught to revere.
