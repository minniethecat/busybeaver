# Romantic Hero

The romantic hero — the gallant officer, the Byronic lover, the man of action and passion — is the archetype Shaw's play dismantles. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] is the romantic hero in full costume: the handsome cavalry officer, the hero of Slivnitza, the practitioner of [[higher-love|higher love]], the man whose photograph inspires adoration. And he is, as Shaw systematically demonstrates, a fraud.

## The Archetype

The romantic hero, as codified by Romantic literature and opera, is defined by a cluster of traits: physical beauty, martial courage, emotional intensity, scorn for convention, and a capacity for transcendent passion. Every trait is present in Sergius — and every trait is exposed, over the course of the play, as performance rather than substance.

## The Deconstruction

Shaw's deconstruction of the romantic hero proceeds through comic juxtaposition. Sergius's cavalry charge, the supreme expression of martial heroism, is revealed as a logistical accident. His "higher love," the supreme expression of romantic passion, is revealed as a rationalization of infidelity. His challenge to a duel, the supreme expression of honor, is defused by Bluntschli's professional competence. Each revelation strips away a layer of the heroic mystique.

## The Replacement

The romantic hero is replaced not by an anti-hero but by a different kind of hero: the competent professional. Bluntschli — unglamorous, undemonstrative, interested in chocolate and cartridges and hotel inventories — is Shaw's alternative. He does what the romantic hero claims to do (protect, provide, be honest) without the theatrical apparatus that makes the romantic hero dangerous.
