# Swiss Mercenary

Bluntschli's Swiss nationality is not incidental character detail but the foundational element
of his professional identity. Switzerland, a neutral nation historically associated with
mercenary service, supplies soldiers who have no national stake in the conflicts they fight.
"I am only a Swiss," Bluntschli tells Raina, "fighting merely as a professional soldier. I
joined Servia because it came first on the road from Switzerland"（001-362）.

## The Neutrality Concept

Swiss neutrality transforms soldiering from patriotic duty into commercial service. Bluntschli
can fight for Servia without being Serbian — he fights because Servia hired him, just as he
could have fought for Bulgaria had the road gone the other way. This is what Shaw presents as
a virtue: the Swiss mercenary is immune to the nationalist rhetoric that inflames both sides
of a conflict. He sees war as it is — a trade — rather than as his culture's narratives
require him to see it.

The neutrality is doubled: Switzerland is a neutral nation, and Bluntschli is a neutral observer
of Balkan passions. He can assess Sergius's cavalry charge with professional detachment because
nothing in his identity depends on believing in Bulgarian heroism. His critique — that the charge
was "the maddest" tactical folly ever let loose on a field of battle（001-501）— is not treason
but professional analysis, and his Swissness is what makes that analysis possible.

## Mercenary as Honest Man

Shaw's Fabian socialism saw patriotic rhetoric as a dangerous ideology that enables nations to
send young men to die. The Swiss mercenary, by contrast, is a figure of integrity precisely
because he admits his mercenary motives. Bluntschli fights for pay and says so. His father's
hotels（001-690）extend the professional logic beyond the battlefield: soldiering is one trade
among many, and the competent professional may move between them — from artillery to hospitality —
without loss of identity.

Shaw's professional soldier is a businessman in uniform, and his victory over the aristocratic
amateur is Shaw's comic prophecy of the twentieth century.
