# Class Bedroom Scene

The bedroom in Act I of *Arms and the Man* is not merely a domestic interior but a stage on which class identity is performed, challenged, and momentarily inverted. The encounter between the fugitive mercenary [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] and the lady [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] dramatizes Shaw's central class argument: that rank is a costume as thin as the curtains through which Bluntschli enters.

## The Bedroom as Class Stage

The class structure is established through [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic idealism, which is inseparable from her position as a wealthy officer's daughter. Her engagement to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] — "the hero of the hour, the idol of the regiment" — is as much a class alliance as a romantic one. Raina's mother [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], a woman who "is determined to be a Viennese lady"（001-038）, models the aspirational anxiety that defines the provincial elite: neither fully European nor contentedly Bulgarian, the Petkoffs inhabit a precarious class perch.

When [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] enters her bedroom, the class distance between a fugitive mercenary and a lady is immediately and comically evident. Raina asserts her rank with the declaration: "I am a Petkoff... the Petkoffs, the richest and best known in our country"（001-616 to 001-621）. Bluntschli's deflating reply — "Oh, yes, of course. I beg your pardon. The Petkoffs, to be sure. How stupid of me!"（001-623 to 001-624） — is deliberately unconvincing: a Swiss professional soldier, he assesses social claims with the same pragmatism he applies to ammunition.

## Chocolate Creams and Class Expectations

His dietary preference for chocolate over bullets outrages Raina's "most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）. The chocolate creams embody Shaw's argument that class-based expectations of masculine heroism are a sentimental fiction — a real soldier carries food, not romance. Bluntschli's class identity as a Swiss mercenary is itself a category disruption: he is neither aristocrat nor peasant, neither Bulgarian patriot nor Servian enemy, but a professional who sells competence to the highest bidder. His famous observation — "nine soldiers out of ten are born fools"（001-293） — is as much a class statement as a military one.

## The Library as Class Symbol

Raina's announcement — "Do you know what a library is? ... Yes, we have one, the only one in Bulgaria"（001-647 to 001-651） — reveals the library as the Petkoffs' premier status token. It is not a place of reading but a prop in the performance of civilization. Bluntschli's response — "Actually a real library! I should like to see that"（001-653） — is poised between genuine curiosity and gentle mockery. Raina presses the point: "I tell you these things to shew you that you are not in the house of ignorant country folk who would kill you the moment they saw your Servian uniform, but among civilized people"（001-655 to 001-658）. The irony is dense: the Petkoffs' claim to enlightened civility depends on a roomful of books they evidently do not read, in a house where they "go to Bucharest every year for the opera season"（001-658） — yet cannot recognize the practical competence of a Swiss mercenary as a form of intelligence superior to their own.

## The Bedroom as Threshold

The bedroom is, in Shaw's dramatic architecture, a threshold space where the normal rules of class deference are suspended. Raina cannot call for servants without exposing Bluntschli; Bluntschli, a fugitive, cannot assert the privileges of his professional rank. The scene's power derives from this mutual vulnerability — lady and mercenary, each stripped of their usual social protections, are forced to negotiate as near-equals. The bedroom becomes a laboratory in which Shaw tests the claim that class is a natural hierarchy by removing the props — servants, uniforms, public audiences — on which that hierarchy depends.
