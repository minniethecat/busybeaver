# Sofia

## Basic Information

**Sofia**, the capital of Bulgaria, is referenced throughout *Arms and the Man* as the offstage centre of political and military authority. While the play's action takes place in the Petkoff house in a provincial Bulgarian town, Sofia represents the world of official power — the place where peace treaties are signed, military careers are made, reputations are calibrated, and social status is measured against metropolitan standards. The distance between the provincial Petkoff house and Sofia is both geographical and cultural: the Petkoffs are provincials navigating their relationship to the capital's authority.

## Stage Directions Description

Sofia is not depicted through stage directions but functions as a symbolic location in dialogue. It is the destination of Bluntschli's return journey, the source of military orders, and the reference point for the Petkoff family's social standing. The play's spatial imagination is structured around the opposition between the domestic (Petkoff house, library, garden) and the metropolitan (Sofia, the court, the army headquarters). The Petkoff house is where people live; Sofia is where they are judged.

The stage directions for Act III set this provincial-metropolitan tension in spatial terms:

> "In the library after lunch. It is not much of a library, its literary equipment consisting of a single fixed shelf stocked with old paper-covered novels, broken-backed, coffee-stained, torn and thumbed; and a couple of little hanging shelves with a few gift books on them, the rest of the wall space being occupied by trophies of war and the chase."（003-001）

The Petkoffs' library — proudly cited by Major Petkoff as a cultural credential（"I took care to let them know that we had a library" [002-186]）— is a provincial imitation of metropolitan sophistication. Sofia, by implication, would have real libraries.

## Dramatic Function

Sofia serves as the unseen arbiter of social and military status. Sergius's reputation was made on the battlefield at Slivnitza but is maintained in Sofia's drawing rooms and officers' clubs. His anxiety about how he is perceived in the capital drives much of his behaviour: the heroic cavalry charge was as much a performance for Sofia's opinion as a military action.

Louka's ambition explicitly maps the geography of aspiration:

> "When I leave their service and start a shop in Sofia, their custom will be half my capital: their bad word would ruin me."（002-038–002-039）

Sofia here is not merely a city but a social economy — a network of reputation and custom that determines economic survival. Louka's calculation, spoken in Act II, reveals a character who grasps the material basis of social relations more precisely than any of her social superiors.

Major Petkoff's discussion of military logistics casually invokes Sofia as a strategic reference point:

> "I happened to ride back the quicker:—there's no forage for them on the Sofia road."（002-423）

The matter-of-fact mention of the "Sofia road" — as if it were as ordinary as the garden path — normalises the capital as a presence in daily life while simultaneously marking Petkoff as someone whose professional world extends beyond the provincial town.

The coat-return scene uses Sofia as a pivot between public and private worlds. Bluntschli arrives from the world of military operations and peace treaties (centred in Sofia), enters the Petkoff house, and must be smuggled out before the husband and future son-in-law — both figures of military authority connected to Sofia — discover him:

> "Oh, Heavens, he's come to return the coat! Send him away — say we're not at home."（002-754）

The library door — symbol of the Petkoffs' metropolitan pretensions — becomes a physical barrier against exposure:

> "Is the library door shut?"（002-772）

Bluntschli's return journey to Sofia — and his detour to the Petkoff house — links the intimate domestic drama to the larger political world. His practical errand (returning Major Petkoff's coat, which he borrowed during his escape [002-810]) becomes the vehicle for the play's romantic resolution. Shaw's structural point is characteristic: the personal (a borrowed coat) and the political (the peace treaty) are inseparable, and both find their resolution in the same domestic space.

Sergius's bet with Bluntschli invokes Sofia as the measure of luxury and status:

> "I bet you any piece of jewellery you like to order from Sofia against a week's hotel bill."（003-088）

The phrase "order from Sofia" — not "buy from the village" or "import from Vienna" — positions the capital as the source of desirable goods and the benchmark of value. Even a bet between a Bulgarian major and a Swiss captain is denominated in Sofia's commercial reach.

Bluntschli's final vision of the future extends Louka's earlier ambition into marital partnership:

> "She'll set up a shop later on in Sofia; and I look forward to her custom and recommendation."（003-1001）

Sofia here is the horizon of economic independence — the place where a former servant can become a shopkeeper and where a professional soldier can look forward not to further battles but to "custom and recommendation." The play ends, in effect, with the promise of a Sofia that is commercial, peaceful, and indifferent to the military glories that opened Act I.

The peace treaty that ends the Serbo-Bulgarian War is a Sofia affair, negotiated by diplomats far from the battlefield:

> "The war's over. The treaty was signed three days ago at Bucharest."（002-136–002-137）

Although the treaty was actually signed at Bucharest (not Sofia), the demobilisation decree and the bureaucratic machinery of peace radiate from the capital. Catherine's patriotic fury — "Peace!"（002-147）— is the cry of a provincial patriot who imagines the capital's diplomats have betrayed the battlefield's glory. The contrast between the passionate war rhetoric of Act I and the bureaucratic peace of Act II and III is partly a contrast between the provincial experience of war and the metropolitan management of peace. Sofia, as the seat of government, transforms the glorious Bulgarian victory into an administrative matter — a deflation that mirrors the play's larger anti-romantic project.

## Symbolic Meaning

Sofia functions symbolically as the site of modernity, ambition, and social fluidity in a society still negotiating its identity. It represents several overlapping ideas:

- **Modernity vs. tradition**: The Petkoffs' electric bell（"What's an electric bell?" [002-191]）, proudly installed by Catherine as a badge of civilisation, is a Sofia import — a technology whose value is entirely social. Major Petkoff's bafflement — "Why not shout for him?"（002-196）— captures the gulf between provincial pragmatism and metropolitan pretension.

- **Aspiration as identity**: The Petkoffs' library, their electric bell, their anxiety about how they are perceived by Russian officers — all are calibrations against an imagined Sofia standard. They are provincials performing metropolitan identity for an audience they rarely see.

- **Social mobility and class**: Louka's ambition to open a shop in Sofia represents the possibility of class transformation that the provincial household cannot offer. In the Petkoff house, Louka is a servant; in Sofia, she can be a proprietor. The capital dissolves fixed social roles.

- **The bureaucratic-democratic alternative to military glory**: The play's movement from Slivnitza (battlefield, Act I) to the Petkoff garden (domestic space, Act II) to the library (bureaucratic space, Act III) traces a geography of demystification. Sofia, as the seat of government, is the endpoint of this trajectory — a place where soldiers discuss hotel management rather than cavalry charges.

## Historical-Geographical Background

Sofia became the capital of the newly autonomous Principality of Bulgaria in 1879, following the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–78 and the subsequent Congress of Berlin. By 1885, when the play is set, Sofia was a young capital of a young nation — barely six years old in its role. The city was rapidly transforming from an Ottoman provincial centre into a European-style capital, with new boulevards, government buildings, and a self-consciously modern elite.

This historical context informs the play's satirical treatment of Bulgarian social pretension. The Petkoffs' obsession with their library, their electric bell, and their social standing are the anxieties of a nation defining itself against European expectations. Sofia, as the symbol of this modernising impulse, represents both aspiration and pretension — the desire to be taken seriously by the great powers that is both comic and poignant.

Sofia's rapid growth in the 1880s — its population roughly doubled in the decade after becoming capital — created a fluid social environment in which status was newly negotiable. This context illuminates Louka's ambition and Sergius's status anxiety: in a society as young as 1880s Bulgaria, the boundaries between servant and gentlewoman, between hero and fraud, are more porous than they appear.

The Serbo-Bulgarian War of 1885, though fought near the western border (Slivnitza is about 30 km from Sofia), was directed from the capital. The Bulgarian victory at Slivnitza, which Catherine celebrates so extravagantly（001-056）, was a genuine military achievement for a young army, but it was also a triumph managed and memorialised by Sofia's political and military establishment. Sergius's promotion anxiety — "He bores my life out about our not promoting him"（002-224）— reflects the capital's role as the gatekeeper of military careers.

Shaw's choice to set the play in a provincial house rather than in Sofia itself is strategically important: it allows him to show Bulgarians for whom the capital is an object of aspiration rather than a site of inhabitation. The Petkoffs relate to Sofia much as Bulgarians related to Vienna or St. Petersburg — as a source of standards they struggle to meet.

## Critical Perspectives

**Nicholas Grene** (1984) argued that the spatial structure of *Arms and the Man* — provincial house versus metropolitan capital — replicates a pattern found throughout Shaw's early plays. The provincial setting allows Shaw to satirise social pretension without becoming entangled in the specific politics of the capital; Sofia hovers as an "offstage judge" whose standards the characters internalise but never directly encounter.

**Margery M. Morgan** (1972) situated the play within the context of Shaw's critique of Victorian domestic ideology. The Petkoff house, she argued, is a "theatre of status" in which every object — from the library to the electric bell — is a prop in the family's performance of metropolitan civility. Sofia is the imagined audience for this performance.

**Michael Holroyd** (1988) noted that Shaw's experience of Dublin — a provincial capital aspiring to metropolitan status — informed his treatment of Sofia. The Petkoffs' anxious modernity, their oscillation between boasting and self-deprecation, and their sensitivity to external judgment all echo the Anglo-Irish provincial experience that Shaw knew intimately.

**A. M. Gibbs** (2001) observed that the play's geography traces what he called a "trajectory of deromanticisation": from the starlit Balkan balcony of Act I, through the sunlit garden of Act II, to the shabby library of Act III. Sofia, as the unseen endpoint of this trajectory, represents the bureaucratic-democratic resolution that Shaw preferred to both romantic heroism (Slivnitza) and domestic pretension (the Petkoff house). Bluntschli's final speech about hotel management — the most prosaic of occupations — is Shaw's Sofia manifesto: the capital's future lies not in cavalry charges but in commerce.
