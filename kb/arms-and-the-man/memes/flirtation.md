# Flirtation

Flirtation is the play's most active romantic mode. Despite — or because of — all the rhetoric about [[higher-love|higher love]] and [[noble-attitude|noble attitudes]], the characters spend much of the play engaged in sexual pursuit: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] pursuing [[louka|Louka]], [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] testing her power over [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], Louka maneuvering Sergius toward a proposal. The play's honesty about flirtation is one of its most subversive features — an acknowledgment that the language of love is often the language of strategy.

## Sergius and Louka

The flirtation between Sergius and Louka is the play's most explicit treatment of sexual pursuit. Sergius, the Byronic cavalry officer, pursues Louka with the same theatrical intensity he brings to everything. Louka, the ambitious servant, encourages his pursuit while calculating its strategic value. Their encounters are duets of mutual manipulation — each using the other for purposes the language of romance disguises.

## Raina's Flirtation

Raina's flirtation with Bluntschli is more subtle but equally strategic. Her famous comment — "I always feel a longing to do or say something dreadful to him"（002-716）— is the confession of a woman who has discovered the pleasures of romantic power. The "dreadful" things she wants to say to Sergius are weapons in a campaign of erotic repositioning: she is preparing to leave one man for another, and flirtation is the instrument of transition.

## Shaw's Honesty

Shaw's treatment of flirtation is characteristic of his larger project: the replacement of romantic illusion with realistic analysis. Victorian theater would typically present flirtation either as innocent play or as wicked seduction. Shaw presents it as what it is: the negotiation of desire between people with different resources, different vulnerabilities, and different goals. The result is less romantic than convention would demand — and more honest.
