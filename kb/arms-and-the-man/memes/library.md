# Library

The library of the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff House]] is the central dramatic space of *Arms and the Man*, serving as the setting for Acts I and III. Shaw describes it as a "half rich Bulgarian, quarter Viennese, quarter Swiss" room [from stage directions], a space that functions simultaneously as [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber and the family's cultural showpiece — a duality that mirrors the play's tension between private truth and public performance.

## A Roomful of Books

The library's cultural significance is established through the Petkoffs' self-conscious pride in it. When Bluntschli's presence forces the family to maintain appearances, the library becomes a running joke:

> Do you know what a library is? — A library? A roomful of books. [001-647, 001-649]

The exchange between Petkoff and Bluntschli exposes the library as a status symbol rather than a site of learning. The Bulgarian officer class values the appearance of cultivation — "they wanted to let them know that we had a library" [002-186] — without engaging in actual intellectual activity.

## The Stage of Intrigue

The library's dual function as bedchamber and reception room makes it the perfect setting for the play's central deceptions. In Act I, Raina hides Bluntschli behind the curtains of this very room. In Act III, the same library becomes the stage for Bluntschli's return and the unravelling of all pretences. The library door [002-772, 002-834] is repeatedly referenced as a threshold between private secrets and public exposure — Petkoff cannot bring himself to speak its name when Bluntschli's incriminating coat is discovered there.

## Dramatic Function

Shaw uses the library as more than scenery. Its [[social-class|cultural hybridity]] — Bulgarian, Viennese, Swiss — reflects the play's thematic concern with national identity and European modernity. The room's transformation from Raina's intimate refuge (Act I) to a space of social reckoning (Act III) tracks her own journey from [[romanticism|romantic]] fantasy to [[pragmatism|pragmatic]] self-knowledge.
