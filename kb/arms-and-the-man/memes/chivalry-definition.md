# Chivalry (Definition)

Chivalry in *Arms and the Man* is the master-illusion from which the play's other romantic delusions descend. The medieval code of knightly honour — courage in battle, courtesy to ladies, loyalty to one's word — supplies [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] with the script he performs, [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] with the ideal she worships, and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine Petkoff]] with the social vocabulary she deploys to manage inconvenient truths. Shaw's play subjects each of these performances to comic deflation, revealing chivalry not as a moral code but as an aesthetic costume that licenses the very behaviour it pretends to prohibit.

## What Chivalry Means in the Play

In its historical sense, chivalry (from Old French *chevalerie*, "horsemanship") was the ethical system governing the medieval knightly class: a fusion of martial prowess, feudal loyalty, and courtly love. By the late nineteenth century, when Shaw wrote *Arms and the Man* (1894), chivalry had undergone a Victorian revival — repackaged through Tennyson's *Idylls of the King*, the Pre-Raphaelite rediscovery of Arthurian legend, and the "muscular Christianity" of Charles Kingsley and Thomas Hughes. This revival, Shaw believed, was intellectually bankrupt: it aestheticised violence, sanctified class hierarchy, and provided a rhetorical alibi for male predation, all under the banner of moral refinement.

Shaw's chivalry is thus a double target. It is, first, a military anachronism — a pre-industrial code of personal gallantry rendered suicidal by machine guns and artillery. Second, it is a social performance — a set of gestures (kneeling, hand-kissing, elevated diction) that the play's aristocrats deploy to distinguish themselves from servants and tradesmen while concealing their ordinary selfishness.

## The Chivalric Tradition and Its Victorian Afterlife

To understand Shaw's critique, one must recognise that the chivalric revival was not a fringe enthusiasm but a dominant current in late-Victorian culture. The Medievalism of Ruskin and Morris, the Eglinton Tournament of 1839 (a full-scale re-enactment of medieval jousting attended by 100,000 spectators), the founding of the Boy Scouts with its chivalric oath — all attest to a widespread conviction that the knightly virtues could redeem an age of industrial ugliness and imperial anxiety.

Shaw was having none of it. As a Fabian socialist and a disciple of Ibsen, he regarded the chivalric revival as reactionary nostalgia dressed in moral costume. The knight, in Shaw's analysis, was an aristocratic thug whose violence was retrospectively sanitised by poets. *Arms and the Man* translates this critique into drama: every invocation of chivalry in the play is either self-serving, hypocritical, or strategically deployed to avoid an uncomfortable truth.

### Key Elements of the Victorian Chivalric Revival

| Element | Cultural Expression | Shaw's Objection |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| Medievalism | Ruskin, Morris, Pre-Raphaelites | Aestheticises a brutal past |
| Muscular Christianity | Kingsley, Hughes, Boy Scouts | Sanctifies violence as moral fitness |
| Courtly Love | Tennyson's *Idylls*, Arthurian romance | Infantilises women on a pedestal |
| Aristocratic Honour | Eglinton Tournament, duelling codes | Naturalises class hierarchy |
| Imperial Chivalry | "White man's burden," colonial adventure | Provides moral cover for conquest |

## The Double Critique

Shaw's analysis of chivalry operates on two levels simultaneously. Militarily, chivalry is a death sentence: the cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] — the play's central chivalric episode — is exposed by [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] as suicidal amateurism dressed in operatic costume. Socially, chivalry is a class marker: it provides the gentry with a vocabulary of moral superiority that distinguishes them from servants and tradesmen while permitting behaviour — Sergius's pursuit of [[louka|Louka]], Catherine's strategic deceptions — that the code nominally forbids.

This double critique is what makes Shaw's treatment of chivalry more sophisticated than simple mockery. He is not merely pointing out that chivalry is hypocritical; he is showing that hypocrisy is built into chivalry's structure. The code is designed to be violated — that is, in effect, its social function. It provides the rhetoric of honour that allows dishonourable behaviour to pass unexamined.
