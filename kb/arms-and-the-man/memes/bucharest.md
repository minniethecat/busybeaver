# Bucharest

Bucharest, the capital of Romania, was known in the late nineteenth century as "the Paris of the East" for its Belle Epoque architecture, vibrant cultural life, and cosmopolitan aspirations. By the 1880s, the city boasted an opera house, theatres, and literary societies that made it a cultural beacon for the Balkan elite.

Raina Petkoff's reference to the Bucharest opera is significant. For a young Bulgarian woman of her class, the Bucharest opera represented access to European high culture — the world of romantic stories, heroic arias, and idealized passions that shaped her expectations of love and war. Bucharest, like Vienna, was a node in the cultural network that transmitted Romantic ideals into the Balkans.

This cultural geography matters to Shaw's argument. The romanticism Raina absorbs is not native Bulgarian tradition but imported European fantasy, filtered through opera houses in cities like Bucharest and Vienna. Shaw uses these references to show that Raina's idealism is a cultural import — as foreign to Bulgarian reality as Bluntschli's Swiss pragmatism.
