# Reversal

Reversal — the dramatic overturning of expectations — is the play's governing structural principle. Every major expectation the play establishes is systematically reversed: the hero is a chocolate-eating mercenary, the glorious cavalry charge is a logistical accident, the love story is a business negotiation, and the happy ending is an inventory of hotel assets.

## Types of Reversal

The play operates through several distinct kinds of reversal:

1. **Character reversal:** The "hero" (Sergius) becomes the fool; the "coward" (Bluntschli) becomes the admirable figure; the "romantic heroine" (Raina) becomes the pragmatic realist.

2. **Event reversal:** The cavalry charge — received as glorious victory — is reinterpreted as professional incompetence.

3. **Value reversal:** [[courage]] becomes recklessness, [[cowardice]] becomes rationality, [[honor]] becomes hypocrisy, and commerce becomes the foundation of virtue.

4. **Structural reversal:** The romantic comedy builds toward a romantic climax and delivers an anti-climax; the expectations the play creates in its audience are the expectations it systematically disappoints.

## Dramatic Function

Reversal is not merely a formal device in Shaw's hands but an argumentative strategy. By reversing expectations, the play forces the audience to re-examine the assumptions that generated those expectations. The audience that laughs at Bluntschli's chocolate creams is laughing at its own romantic conditioning — and the laughter is the vehicle for the argument.
