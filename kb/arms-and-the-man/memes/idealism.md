# Idealism

The philosophical disposition to value ideas over realities — the intellectual root of every romantic illusion in *Arms and the Man*. Idealism, for Shaw, is not a harmless aesthetic preference but a cognitive error with real-world consequences: it makes soldiers charge windmills, lovers invent "higher" affections to escape honest desire, and entire cultures congratulate themselves on virtues they do not practice. Shaw's play is, at its deepest level, an argument against idealism and for the practical, the professional, and the real.

## Definition

Idealism in the play operates on two levels.

First, as a **personal disposition**: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s tendency to interpret events through the lens of how they "should" be rather than how they are — a habit Shaw diagnoses as literary poisoning. Raina has read too much [[lord-byron|Byron]] and [[alexander-pushkin|Pushkin]]; Sergius has absorbed the chivalric code so thoroughly that he can no longer distinguish between genuine courage and operatic posturing.

Second, as a **cultural force**: the literary and philosophical tradition (Byron, Pushkin, the chivalric code, Victorian morality) that supplies the scripts these characters perform. Shaw's critique targets both levels — idealism is not just wrong, it is dangerous, because it prevents people from dealing effectively with the actual world. An idealist soldier gets his regiment slaughtered; an idealist lover cannot sustain an honest relationship; an idealist society congratulates itself on noble sentiments while perpetuating injustice.

The term has a specific Shavian valence. Shaw was writing in the tradition of philosophical idealism's critics — from Samuel Johnson kicking the stone to refute Berkeley, to the Fabian insistence on measurable social reform over utopian rhetoric. For Shaw, "idealism" names the habit of preferring a beautiful fiction to an inconvenient fact, and the play's comic structure is designed to expose the cost of that preference.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Idealism is established as [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s defining characteristic — and as a problem the play means to solve. The opening stage direction places her on a balcony, "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it"（001-030~031）. She is not merely experiencing beauty; she is performing the role of a romantic heroine, watching herself be watched. Her bedchamber is decorated with a portrait of Sergius, "a box of chocolate creams, and a miniature easel"（001-023） — the props of a consciously constructed romantic identity.

The key exposition comes in her conversation with [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]. Raina's rhapsody about [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge — "Our ideas of what Sergius would do — our patriotism — our heroic ideals"（001-085） — is immediately followed by confession. She has doubted: "perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that — indeed never, as far as I knew it then"（001-095~098）. This is a remarkable moment of dramatic self-awareness. Raina knows — or half-knows — that her ideals are literary constructs. But Shaw shows that knowing is not enough; the idealist's attachment to the beautiful fiction overrides the evidence.

The arrival of [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] — exhausted, hungry, carrying chocolate instead of cartridges — is the play's first direct collision between ideal and real. Raina's response is not mere surprise but outrage: Shaw's stage direction reads, "*outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood*. Chocolate! Do you stuff your pockets with sweets — like a schoolboy — even in the field?"（001-398~399）. The parenthetical is crucial: it is not her safety that is threatened but her *ideals of manhood*. A soldier who eats chocolate is not the Byronic hero she has been trained to expect, and the dissonance registers as moral offense.

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] then delivers the play's most devastating anti-idealist testimony. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s celebrated cavalry charge was, in his account, an act of suicidal foolishness: "He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We nearly burst with laughter at him"（001-489~491）. Worse, the charge only succeeded because the Servian artillery had been sent the wrong ammunition — the idealist is saved from the consequences of his folly by a lucky accident. Bluntschli's verdict is unambiguous: "Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all"（001-500~502）.

Raina's response is not to reconsider her ideals but to defend them: the stage direction reads "*deeply wounded, but steadfastly loyal to her ideals*"（001-504）. This is Shaw's diagnosis in miniature: the idealist, confronted with disconfirming evidence, does not revise the ideal but doubles down on it. Idealism is self-immunizing.

### ACT II

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s entrance transforms the play's treatment of idealism from comic exposure to psychological tragedy. Shaw's stage direction is a miniature character essay:

> By his brooding on the perpetual failure, not only of others, but of himself, to live up to his imaginative ideals, his consequent cynical scorn for humanity, the jejune credulity as to the absolute validity of his ideals and the unworthiness of the world in disregarding them, his wincings and mockeries under the sting of the petty disillusions which every hour spent among men brings to his infallibly quick observation, he has acquired the half tragic, half ironic air. (002-249~256)

This passage, dense with Shavian psychology, anatomizes the idealist's trap. Sergius is not a fool who believes in ideals; he is an intelligent man who *knows* the ideals are unattainable but cannot relinquish them. The result is not liberation but "cynical scorn for humanity" — a bitterness directed outward at the world for failing the ideals, rather than inward at the ideals for failing the world. The key phrase is "the jejune credulity as to the absolute validity of his ideals"（002-251~252）: Sergius never questions whether the ideals themselves might be the problem.

His confession about the cavalry charge deepens the diagnosis. He "won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way"（002-288~289）. The word "wrong" is devastating — he has internalized the professional military's judgment of his own heroism. His reward is not promotion but punishment by the very system his ideals taught him to revere. He is a hero who feels like a fraud, and his response is not to abandon the ideal of heroism but to invent a new ideal — the "higher love" — that allows him to maintain his self-conception.

The "higher love" scene is the play's most concentrated satire of idealism's self-deceptions. Raina and Sergius, alone together, construct an elaborate fiction of spiritualized affection:

> RAINA. (*Very solemnly.*) Sergius: I think we two have found the higher love. When I think of you, I feel that I could never do a base deed, or think an ignoble thought. (002-462~464)

The stage direction — *Very solemnly* — marks the gap between the characters' self-perception and Shaw's ironic framing. They are not lying to each other; they are performing a script they have both internalized, and the solemnity is the tell. The "higher love" is idealism's answer to the problem of actual desire: by spiritualizing love, the idealist can avoid confronting the inconvenient fact that [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is attracted to [[louka|Louka]] and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is drawn to [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]].

Moments later, Sergius is flirting with [[louka|Louka]] and asking her, "Louka: do you know what the higher love is?"（002-491）. The repetition of the phrase in this new context — seduction rather than spiritual communion — exposes it as a rhetorical instrument rather than a genuine conviction. When [[louka|Louka]] challenges him, Sergius threatens: "I may be worthless enough to betray the higher love; but do not you insult it"（002-528~529）. The ideal survives its own betrayal; it is too useful to abandon.

### ACT III

The play's resolution is a systematic demolition of the idealist edifice, and it proceeds on multiple fronts simultaneously.

First, the intellectual demolition. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], having witnessed a fellow soldier burned alive in a timber fire, delivers the play's most famous speech — and its most complete renunciation of idealism:

> Oh, war! war! the dream of patriots and heroes! A fraud, Bluntschli, a hollow sham, like love. (003-731~732)

When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] protests "Like love! You say that before me"（003-734）, Sergius doubles down: "A hollow sham, I say"（003-738）. The triple intensification — fraud, hollow sham, and the explicit comparison to love — links the play's two great idealist domains. War and love, the traditional proving grounds of heroic character, are both revealed as theaters of self-deception. Sergius's tragedy is that he can see the fraudulence but cannot find his way to an alternative. He is a disillusioned idealist — a man who has lost his faith but not his need for faith.

Second, the romantic demolition. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s attempt to minimize his connection with Raina — "She never cared two straws for me. Why, bless my heart and soul, look at the young lady and look at me. She, rich, young, beautiful, with her imagination full of fairy princes and noble natures and cavalry charges and goodness knows what!"（003-1069~1073） — provokes an unexpected reaction. He describes himself as "a man who has spoiled all his chances in life through an incurably romantic disposition"（003-1075~1076）, and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], recognizing the phrase, demands it be repeated. Bluntschli obliges: "An incurably romantic disposition. I ran away from home twice when I was a boy. I went into the army instead of into my father's business. I climbed the balcony of this house when a man of sense would have dived into the nearest cellar"（003-1082~1085）.

The irony is structural. The "incurably romantic disposition" belongs not to the Byronic Sergius but to the pragmatic Bluntschli — and Bluntschli's romanticism, unlike Sergius's, is a matter of *action* rather than *self-conception*. He does romantic things (climbing balconies, running away to join the army) without constructing a romantic identity around them. This distinction — between being romantic and thinking of oneself as a romantic hero — is the play's most sophisticated contribution to the critique of idealism.

The final exposure comes from [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] herself. When Bluntschli, with characteristic professional efficiency, presents her photograph as evidence in the resolution of the chocolate cream soldier mystery, she snaps:

> I quite agree with your account of yourself. You are a romantic idiot. (*Bluntschli is unspeakably taken aback.*) Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three. (003-1108~1111)

This is not merely a comic rebuke. It is Raina's declaration of independence from her own idealist past. She claims the authority of "a woman of twenty-three" over "a schoolgirl of seventeen" — a temporal self-distinction that marks her growth out of idealism. The girl who read Byron and imagined herself a heroine has become a woman who can call romanticism by its proper name: idiocy.

The play's final image confirms the inversion. Raina does not marry the Byronic hero; she marries the Swiss professional soldier who carries chocolate instead of cartridges. Her last line — "To my chocolate cream soldier!"（003-1202） — reclaims the epithet she once used with outrage as a term of endearment. Idealism has been not merely abandoned but transformed: the chocolate cream soldier, once an offense against her ideals of manhood, has become the object of her genuine affection. The idealist has become a realist, and the play celebrates the conversion.

## Idealism as Tragic Flaw: The Case of Sergius

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] is the play's most psychologically complex character, and his complexity derives entirely from his relationship to idealism. Unlike [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], whose idealism is naive and unexamined, Sergius is an idealist who has seen through his own ideals — and this knowledge, far from liberating him, becomes the source of his torment.

Shaw's stage direction describing Sergius's "Byronism" is one of the most concentrated pieces of psychological analysis in the Shavian canon. The diagnosis turns on a paradox: Sergius maintains "the jejune credulity as to the absolute validity of his ideals"（002-251~252） *even while experiencing* "the perpetual failure, not only of others, but of himself, to live up to his imaginative ideals"（002-249~250）. A rational person, confronted with persistent failure, would revise the standard. Sergius instead revises his judgment of the world: if reality cannot match the ideal, reality is at fault. The result is "cynical scorn for humanity" — misanthropy as the idealist's consolation.

This is the structure Shaw identifies as idealism's tragic flaw. It is not that ideals are bad — it is that the idealist's relationship to ideals precludes learning. The ideal functions as an unchallengeable axiom; all contrary evidence is dismissed as the world's failure, never the ideal's inadequacy. Sergius cannot ask "Is the chivalric code a good standard for a modern soldier?" because the question itself would be treason to the ideal. He can only ask "Why does the world fail the chivalric code?" — a question whose answer is always the same (the world is unworthy) and whose emotional consequence is always the same (despair).

His confession — "I won the battle the wrong way"（002-288） — captures the bind. The word "wrong" reveals that Sergius has internalized the professional military's standards even as he performs the romantic hero's gestures. He is divided against himself: the Byronic hero and the competent officer cannot coexist, and Sergius has tried to be both. The "higher love" is his attempt to resolve this division — a new ideal, higher than the one that failed, that might redeem the failure. But it is only a more elaborate version of the same structure, and it collapses under the weight of his attraction to [[louka|Louka]].

Sergius's tragedy is that he comes to see the truth — "A fraud, Bluntschli, a hollow sham"（003-731~732） — but cannot act on it. He renounces idealism verbally while remaining in its grip emotionally. His final pairing with [[louka|Louka]] is the play's suggestion of a way out: Louka refuses to participate in his idealist performances and demands that he meet her on the level of actual desire. Whether Sergius is capable of this is left ambiguous.

## Raina's Romantic Delusion: The Education of an Idealist

If [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is the tragic case, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is the comic one — and her comedy is pedagogical. Shaw uses Raina to demonstrate that idealism can be unlearned, and that the process, while uncomfortable, is ultimately liberating.

Raina's idealism is presented from the first as a matter of cultural conditioning. She is not naturally disposed to idealize; she has been *trained* to idealize, by Byron and Pushkin, by the Bucharest opera, by her mother's patriotic rhetoric. Her confession in Act I — "perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin"（001-095~096） — is remarkable for its self-awareness. Raina, even at her most idealistic, suspects that her ideals are literary constructs. But this suspicion does not yet amount to liberation, because she has no alternative framework. She can doubt the ideal only in private, to her mother; in public, and especially in the presence of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], she performs it flawlessly.

The process of her education is the play's central plot. It proceeds through three stages:

1. **Shock** — The encounter with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], a soldier who carries chocolate instead of cartridges and climbs balconies to escape death rather than to pursue romance, confronts Raina with a reality that her idealist framework cannot process. Her response — outrage, then fascination — is the beginning of cognitive dissonance.

2. **Performance** — Through Act II and much of Act III, Raina continues to perform the idealist role while secretly harboring the reality of the chocolate cream soldier. This double life is unsustainable, and the strain shows in her increasingly brittle performances of the "higher love."

3. **Renunciation** — The climax of Raina's arc is not her engagement to [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] but her self-description as "a woman of twenty-three" who is no longer "a schoolgirl of seventeen"（003-1108~1111）. This temporal self-distinction is a declaration of growth: the idealist self is not a different person but a younger, less experienced version of the same person. Growth means outgrowing idealism.

The play's title — *Arms and the Man* — takes on its full meaning here. The Virgilian echo (*Arma virumque cano*) is deliberately ironic: Shaw's epic is not of arms and the hero but of arms and the *man*, the actual human being beneath the heroic ideal. Raina's education is the process of learning to see the man rather than the hero — a process the play invites its audience to replicate.

## Shaw's Perspective

Shaw's anti-idealism is rooted in his Fabian socialism — the belief that social progress comes not from idealistic visions but from practical, incremental reform. But the critique in *Arms and the Man* is broader than politics. Shaw is diagnosing a cognitive habit that pervades Victorian culture: the preference for beautiful abstractions over inconvenient facts.

The targets are specific and carefully chosen:

- **Literary idealism**: The Byronic hero, the operatic tenor, the chivalric knight — all cultural templates that provide scripts for self-deception. Shaw's anti-romantic subtitle announces this target explicitly.
- **Patriotic idealism**: The rhetoric of "patriotism" and "heroic ideals" that [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] deploys unreflectively and that sends young men to die in cavalry charges against machine guns.
- **Romantic idealism**: The doctrine of "higher love" that allows Victorians to maintain spiritual pretensions while harboring ordinary sexual desires.
- **Moral idealism**: The belief that having noble sentiments is equivalent to doing noble deeds — a belief the play exposes in every character who congratulates themselves on their ideals while behaving selfishly.

Shaw's alternative is not cynicism — [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is the least cynical character in the play — but **professionalism**: the habit of seeing things as they are and acting competently in response. Bluntschli's chocolate is not a symbol of cowardice but of practical wisdom: a soldier who carries food instead of useless cartridges is a soldier who understands what war actually requires. His "incurably romantic disposition"（003-1075~1076） is romanticism of a different order — the willingness to take risks for genuine feeling rather than for the performance of feeling.

The play's comic structure enacts this philosophy: the characters who cling to ideals suffer ([[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s torment, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s confusion), while those who see clearly thrive ([[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], [[louka|Louka]], [[nicola|Nicola]]). Idealism, for Shaw, is not a harmless error — it is an obstacle to living well, and the play's laughter is the sound of that obstacle being removed.

- **Satirical Target**: The elevation of abstract ideals over concrete realities — in love, in war, in politics, and in self-understanding
- **Alternative**: The practical wisdom of the professional — knowing what is, rather than dreaming of what should be; acting on evidence rather than on sentiment

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's treatment of idealism in *Arms and the Man* has attracted sustained critical attention, much of it focused on the question of whether Shaw's anti-idealism is itself a kind of idealism — a faith in practicality that is no less absolute than the romantic faith it replaces.

**Eric Bentley** (1947), in *The Playwright as Thinker*, argued that Shaw's critique of idealism is the unifying thread of his entire dramatic project. For Bentley, Shaw is a "realist" in the philosophical sense — committed to the proposition that the world exists independently of our ideas about it and that the first duty of the intelligent person is to see it clearly. *Arms and the Man* is, in this reading, Shaw's most concentrated statement of the realist position: every scene is structured as a confrontation between ideal and real, and every such confrontation ends with the ideal exposed and the real vindicated.

**Margery M. Morgan** (1972), in *The Shavian Playground*, offered a more nuanced reading. Morgan noted that Bluntschli — Shaw's supposed spokesman for anti-idealism — is himself described as having an "incurably romantic disposition." The Swiss mercenary who lectures everyone on practicality climbed a balcony to escape death and carried a photograph of a woman he met for one night through a war. Morgan's point is not that Shaw contradicts himself but that Shaw's anti-idealism is more sophisticated than a simple preference for facts over fictions. What Shaw opposes is not romantic feeling but romantic *self-conception* — the habit of narrating one's life in heroic terms. Bluntschli does romantic things without thinking of himself as a romantic hero, and this is precisely what distinguishes him from Sergius.

**Michael Holroyd** (1988–1992), in his monumental biography of Shaw, situated *Arms and the Man* within Shaw's own romantic history — his early infatuations, his long celibacy, his late marriage to Charlotte Payne-Townshend. Holroyd reads the play as, in part, Shaw's working-through of his own ambivalence about romantic love: the idealist Sergius and the pragmatist Bluntschli are not antagonists but two aspects of a single psychological complex, and the play's resolution — Raina choosing the chocolate cream soldier — represents Shaw's hope that romantic feeling might survive the death of romantic ideology.

**Nicholas Grene** (1984), in *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View*, emphasized the political dimension of Shaw's anti-idealism. For Grene, the play's critique of military idealism — the Don Quixote comparison, the exposure of the cavalry charge as suicidal folly — is not merely comic but polemical. Shaw was writing in the long shadow of Tennyson's "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854), and *Arms and the Man* is, among other things, a rebuttal: the cavalry charge is not sublime, it is idiotic, and a culture that celebrates it as sublime is a culture that will keep sending young men to die in avoidable disasters.

More recent criticism has focused on the gender dimension of Shaw's critique. **J. Ellen Gainor** (2013) argued that Raina's education — from idealist schoolgirl to realist woman — is Shaw's model for a feminist epistemology: the rejection of romantic idealism is also a rejection of the patriarchal scripts that define women as objects of heroic devotion. Raina's final line ("To my chocolate cream soldier!") is an assertion of erotic agency as well as a declaration of anti-idealist allegiance.
