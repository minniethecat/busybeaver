# Petkoff Library

The [[petkoff-house|Petkoff]] library — "a single room, which was all the library the Petkoffs could afford"（002-001）— is the setting for Acts II and III, and Shaw's most economically deployed symbol of bourgeois cultural aspiration. It is a library that no one reads in, a reception room masquerading as a seat of learning, a signifier of cultivation without the cultivated substance.

## The Stage Direction

Shaw's opening description of the library is a masterpiece of comic notation. "A single room, which was all the library the Petkoffs could afford" — the qualifier "could afford" is devastating. The Petkoffs want a library — want the cultural status it confers — but can only manage the minimum. The room contains "a few books" but functions primarily as a space to receive visitors. Its most prominent feature is not a bookcase but a broken [[electric-bell|electric bell]].

## Dramatic Function

The library is the perfect setting for the play's second and third acts because it is a space of public performance. Where the [[bedchamber]] was intimate — a space of secrets and revelations — the library is social: a space where visitors are received, engagements are discussed, and identities are performed. The shift from bedchamber to library is the shift from private truth to public pretense, from the world of chocolate creams to the world of [[uniform|uniforms]].

## Symbolic Reading

The library encapsulates the Petkoffs' relationship to European culture: they have the form (a room called a library) without the substance (books that are read). The library is the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] [[uniform]] — a costume of cultivation worn by people whose real interests are practical rather than intellectual.
