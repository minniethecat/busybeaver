# Major Paul Petkoff

Major Paul Petkoff, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s father, is the play's ranking military authority — and its most thoroughly domesticated. A senior officer in the Bulgarian army who has just returned from winning the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]], Major Petkoff is preoccupied not with strategy or glory but with a missing coat and a broken [[electric-bell|electric bell]].

## Character

Major Petkoff is Shaw's portrait of military authority as domestic comedy. He cannot control his household: his wife [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] manages everything, his daughter deceives him, and his servants outmaneuver him. His military rank — the rank that commands soldiers in battle — is irrelevant to the domestic sphere, where authority flows from competence rather than title.

The Major's defining trait is his combination of official importance and personal ineffectuality. He has just won a war, but he cannot locate his own coat. He is a senior officer, but he cannot make the electric bell work. He is the head of the household, but he is the last to know what is happening in it.

## Comic Function

Major Petkoff's primary dramatic function is to provide the comic counterpoint to the romantic intensities of the younger characters. While [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] agonize over [[higher-love|higher love]] and [[honor]], the Major agonizes over his missing coat. His complaints about the war cabinet's indecision — "they will have it their own way"（002-621）— are a domestic grumble that functions as devastating institutional critique.

## Key Scene

> PETKOFF. It's not the coat I mind; it's the embarrassment.（002-613）

The distinction between the coat and the embarrassment is Major Petkoff in a sentence. The material object (the coat) matters less than the social reality (the embarrassment). The Major is a man for whom appearances are everything and substance is elusive — and Shaw's comedy thrives in the gap.
