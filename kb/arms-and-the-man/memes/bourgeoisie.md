# Bourgeoisie

The Petkoff family represents the emerging Bulgarian bourgeoisie — a class that has achieved material comfort but not yet cultural confidence. Their house, their library (never used), their electric bell (always broken), and their elaborate social ceremonies (the library visits, the calls of etiquette) are all performances of aristocratic status by a family whose wealth is grounded in land and whose manners are borrowed from abroad.

## The Petkoffs as Class Representatives

The Petkoffs embody the contradictions of the Balkan bourgeoisie. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] is a landowner and a senior military officer, but his authority is comic rather than genuine — he cannot control his household, he cannot keep track of his coat, and he is outmaneuvered by his wife and daughter at every turn. [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] is the household's effective manager — practical, decisive, unsentimental — but her authority must be exercised indirectly, through manipulation rather than command. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] has been educated into a romantic sensibility that ill prepares her for the practical life she will need to lead.

## The Library as Symbol

The Petkoff library — "a single room, which was all the library the Petkoffs could afford"（002-001）— is the play's most economical class symbol. A library is an aristocratic marker — a sign of cultivation, leisure, and inherited culture. The Petkoffs have a library, but it is the smallest possible library, and no one in the family reads. The library functions as a reception room, a place to receive visitors; its contents are irrelevant. The Petkoffs have the form of aristocracy without the substance — and Shaw's comedy thrives in the gap.

## Shaw's Class Analysis

Shaw's portrayal of the Petkoffs is satirical but not contemptuous. As a Fabian socialist, he was interested in the bourgeoisie as a class in transition — no longer feudal, not yet modern, caught between aristocratic pretension and commercial reality. The Petkoffs' domestic comedy is, in Shaw's hands, an anatomy of an entire class at a historical turning point.
