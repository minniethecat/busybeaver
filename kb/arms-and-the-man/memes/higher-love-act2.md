# Higher Love (Act II: The Invention)

Act II contains the play's definitive treatment of the "higher love." [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] has returned from war, and the gap between his legendary heroism and his mundane reality is already proving unbridgeable. Shaw's stage directions diagnose him with clinical precision: his "cynical scorn for humanity" and "secret self-contempt" (002-250) are the psychological wages of trying to live up to an impossible ideal. The "higher love" is the solution he reaches for — not as a conscious deception but as a psychological necessity.

## Three Movements of Invention

The crucial scene unfolds in three movements.

### First Movement: The Compact with Raina

Alone with [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] plays the Byronic worshipper, and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] supplies the cue:

> RAINA. (*very solemnly.*) Sergius: I think we two have found the higher love. When I think of you, I feel that I could never do a base deed, or think an ignoble thought. (002-462)

The irony is multiple. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] speaks "very solemnly" — the stage direction signals that she believes what she is saying, or at least believes that she believes it. Yet the audience has just watched her conceal a fugitive in her bedroom and lie to a Russian officer about it. The "higher love" is not a state of moral elevation; it is a state of heightened self-deception, and its function is to enable precisely the "base deeds" it claims to prohibit.

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] responds with the vocabulary of religious adoration: "My lady, and my saint!" (002-466), and when Raina begins to return the compliment — "My lord and my g—" — he cuts her off: "Sh—sh! Let me be the worshipper, dear. You little know how unworthy even the best man is of a girl's pure passion!" (002-470). The interruption is telling. Raina was about to call him "my god," completing the symmetrical deification that the "higher love" requires. Sergius silences her because he cannot bear to hear the word applied to himself — he knows, at some level, that it is false.

### Second Movement: The Test with Louka

Moments after the sacred compact with Raina, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] approaches [[louka|Louka]], the maid, and attempts to recruit her into the same fantasy:

> SERGIUS. Louka: do you know what the higher love is? (002-491)

[[louka|Louka]]'s reply — "No, sir" (002-494) — is both honest and devastating. As a servant, she has no use for concepts that separate love from its material and physical conditions. Her refusal to participate in the fiction exposes its class-bound nature: the "higher love" is a luxury good, available only to those who can afford to treat passion as a spiritual exercise rather than a practical negotiation.

### Third Movement: Self-Exposure

When [[louka|Louka]] resists his advances, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] delivers the most self-revealing speech in the play:

> SERGIUS. I am surprised at myself, Louka. What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, say if he saw me now? What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here? (002-508)

The "half dozen Sergiuses" is a confession of radical self-division. Romantic idealism, Shaw suggests, is not merely a false belief about the world; it is a false belief about the self — the multiplication of a single person into incompatible public roles, none of which corresponds to any authentic interior. The "higher love" is the glue that holds these selves together, or rather the rhetorical formula that allows them to coexist without acknowledging their contradictions.

When [[louka|Louka]] resists him further, he warns:

> SERGIUS. I may be worthless enough to betray the higher love; but do not you insult it. (002-529)

The logic is exquisitely Shavian: Sergius grants himself permission to violate the doctrine ("betray") while reserving to himself the sole right to criticise it ("do not you insult it"). The "higher love" is, at bottom, a structure of privilege — it allows the gentleman to act as he pleases while the servant is expected to maintain the fiction.

## The Dramatic Irony of the Scene

The Act II invention scene is a masterclass in dramatic irony. The audience watches a private compact being made — a declaration of spiritualised love — and then watches, in the very next moment, the compact's architect proposition the maid. Shaw does not need to comment; the structure does the work. The solemnity of the "higher love" is not hypocritical in the simple sense (Sergius may genuinely believe he means it); it is instead a piece of psychological machinery that allows him to continue believing in his own nobility while acting on impulses that contradict it.

The scene also establishes the pattern that Act III will complete: the "higher love" is not a relationship but a performance. Raina and Sergius play their parts — lady and worshipper, saint and sinner — with the self-consciousness of actors who have rehearsed their lines. Louka's "No, sir" is the one moment of unrehearsed honesty in the scene, and it is devastating precisely because it refuses to join the cast.
