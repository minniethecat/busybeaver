# Self-Interest

Rational self-interest is the play's moral foundation — the principle that replaces [[honor]], [[duty]], and [[patriotism]] in Shaw's ethical universe. The play argues, through the accumulation of comic examples, that the character who acts in his or her own rational self-interest is more honest, more effective, and ultimately more moral than the character who acts for abstract ideals.

## Self-Interest as Virtue

Shaw presents self-interest not as a vice to be overcome but as a motive to be acknowledged and directed. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s self-interest — his desire to survive, to eat, to return to his hotels — makes him honest where [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is hypocritical and effective where Sergius is incompetent. [[louka|Louka]]'s self-interest — her ambition to escape the servant class — makes her more admirable than the masters she outmaneuvers. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s self-interest — his strategic deference, his patient accumulation — is treated with respect even when it fails.

## The Critique of Self-Sacrifice

The play's corollary argument is against self-sacrifice — the romantic ideal that one should die for one's country, one's honor, or one's love. Shaw treats self-sacrifice as a form of waste: the soldier who dies for glory is as irrational as the lover who pines for an ideal. The living soldier and the pragmatic lover are, in Shaw's calculus, more useful to themselves and to others.

## Key Scene

Bluntschli's entire career in the play is an argument for self-interest. He runs from battle because survival is in his interest; he carries chocolate because eating is in his interest; he proposes marriage because a competent wife is in his interest. Each of these self-interested acts produces more benefit — to himself and to others — than the self-sacrificing alternatives would have. The man who acts in his own interest is, in Shaw's world, the man who makes other people's lives better.

## Shaw's Philosophical Context

Shaw's celebration of self-interest draws on multiple sources: the Fabian conviction that social progress requires rational self-interest rather than altruistic sentiment; the Nietzschean critique of slave morality that Shaw absorbed (ambivalently) in the 1890s; and the comic tradition stretching back to Molière, for whom the honest self-interested man was always more sympathetic than the hypocritical moralist.

- **Target of satire**: The ethic of self-sacrifice that serves the interests of those who demand sacrifices from others
- **Alternative proposed**: An ethic of rational self-interest that treats individual flourishing as the measure of morality
