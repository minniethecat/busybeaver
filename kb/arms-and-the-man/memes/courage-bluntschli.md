# Courage: Bluntschli's Professional Courage

Captain Bluntschli embodies professional courage — competence, clear-eyed self-preservation, and the refusal to be shamed into foolish risks. His courage is invisible to the romantic eye, which is precisely Shaw's point: true battlefield courage looks nothing like the operatic gallantry of patriotic legend.

## The Chocolate Cream Soldier

Bluntschli's entrance in Act I immediately destabilizes the romantic frame of courage established by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]'s narration of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge. A professional Swiss mercenary fighting for the Servian army, Bluntschli climbs a drainpipe to escape Bulgarian pursuers and, when discovered by [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], reveals that his cartridge boxes contain chocolate, not ammunition.

When accused of cowardice, he responds with the play's first redefinition of the term:

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. （001-236 to 001-238）

The chocolate — which gives him his nickname, the "chocolate cream soldier" — is not a sign of effeminacy or cowardice but of professional competence. A soldier who stays alive is more useful than a dead hero. The chocolate is, in its way, more practical than ammunition: a hungry soldier cannot fight.

## Courage as Physiology

Bluntschli brings a clinical, almost scientific understanding of courage to the play. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] challenges him — "I think I am at heart as brave as you" （001-421） — he does not dispute her. Instead, he explains the bodily reality of courage under fire:

> MAN. I can stand two days without shewing it much; but no man can stand three days: I'm as nervous as a mouse. （001-424 to 001-425）

This is a radical reframing. Courage, in Bluntschli's account, is not a moral quality or a fixed character trait — it is a depletable physiological resource. Anyone can be brave for a while; no one can be brave forever. The romantic hero who is always brave does not exist. He is a fiction, and a dangerous one, because believing in him leads real soldiers to die of exhaustion or to break under the impossible demand of perpetual courage.

## The Professional's Code

Bluntschli's professional philosophy is stated plainly: "It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can" （001-236 to 001-238）. This is not cynicism but doctrine. The professional soldier's first duty is survival, because a dead soldier is a useless soldier. Courage serves competence, not the other way around.

This professional code extends to Bluntschli's refusal of the duel in Act III. When [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] challenges him, Bluntschli refuses:

> BLUNTSCHLI. I refuse to fight you. （003-761）

Refusing to fight takes more courage than accepting, when the refusal invites the charge of cowardice. But Bluntschli is a professional; he does not let romantic notions of honor dictate his actions. A duel is an absurd risk, and he will not take it. His courage is the courage to be thought a coward.

## Deconstructing the Cavalry Charge

Bluntschli's most devastating contribution to the play's argument is his eyewitness account of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s celebrated cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]]. Where [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] sees epic heroism, Bluntschli sees tactical incompetence and dumb luck:

> MAN. Well, it's a funny sight. It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane: first one comes; then two or three close behind him; and then all the rest in a lump. （001-461 to 001-463）

The "first One — the bravest of the brave!" （001-466） of [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s imagination is, in Bluntschli's prosaic account, "the poor devil pulling at his horse" because "it's running away with him, of course: do you suppose the fellow wants to get there before the others and be killed?" （001-468 to 001-475）.

Bluntschli then identifies the rider: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who "did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills" （001-489 to 001-491）. The [[don-quixote|Cervantes]] allusion is precise: Sergius, like Quixote, is not a coward but a man whose courage is misdirected at phantoms.

The charge succeeded only because the Servian machine guns had the wrong ammunition:

> MAN. Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all. （001-500 to 001-502）

Shaw's point, delivered through Bluntschli, is not that Sergius is a coward; it is that the very category of "battlefield courage" is incoherent when examined closely. What looks like bravery is usually luck, animal panic, or institutional incompetence dressed in epaulettes.

## The Professional vs. the Amateur

Bluntschli's professional courage stands in direct contrast to the amateur gallantry celebrated by Bulgarian society. The professional:
- Values survival over glory
- Admits fear rather than performing fearlessness
- Judges actions by outcomes, not appearances
- Refuses to be shamed into foolish risks

The amateur romantic, by contrast:
- Values glory over survival
- Performs fearlessness even when afraid
- Judges actions by how they look, not what they achieve
- Accepts foolish risks to avoid the shame of appearing cowardly

The play's argument is clear: the professional's courage is the real courage, and the amateur's gallantry is a dangerous performance that gets people killed.

## You Can Always Do a Thing

Bluntschli's philosophy of action is captured in one of the play's most quoted lines: "You can always do a thing when you know it must be done" （001-585 to 001-586）. This is courage as practical necessity rather than moral grandstanding — the courage of the competent person who does what the situation requires, without fuss or fanfare.
