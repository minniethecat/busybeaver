# Prince Alexander of Battenberg

Prince Alexander Joseph of Battenberg (1857–1893) was the first reigning prince (knyaz) of modern [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], serving from 1879 until his abdication in 1886. He is the unnamed sovereign behind the military events of *Arms and the Man* — the prince under whose authority the Bulgarian army fought and won the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]].

## Historical Role

Alexander was a German prince, nephew of Tsar Alexander II of Russia, selected by the Great Powers to rule the autonomous Bulgarian principality created by the [[treaty-of-berlin|Treaty of Berlin]]. His reign was marked by persistent tension with Russia, which expected Bulgaria to function as a client state. When Alexander supported the [[bulgarian-unification|Bulgarian unification of 1885]] — against Russia's wishes — the Tsar withdrew Russian officers from the Bulgarian army.

The withdrawal was meant to cripple Bulgaria. It had the opposite effect. Deprived of Russian officers, the Bulgarian army promoted its own — and, under Bulgarian command, defeated [[servia|Servia]] at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]]. The victory established Alexander as a national hero and Bulgaria as a genuinely independent state.

## In the Play

Prince Alexander is never mentioned by name, but his presence is felt in the military structures the play depicts. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] serves in an army whose Russian officers have been recalled; the Bulgarian victory at Slivnitza — the victory that occasions Raina's opening rhapsody — was achieved under Alexander's leadership. Shaw, characteristically, ignores the prince in favor of the soldiers: the cavalry officer whose charge succeeded by accident, the Swiss mercenary who fled for his life, the major who can't keep track of his coat.
