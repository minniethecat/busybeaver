# Courage: Definition

In *Arms and the Man*, courage is a contested concept — perhaps the most thoroughly contested idea in the play. The romantic view, courage as gallantry in battle, the cavalry charge against impossible odds, is embodied by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]]. Against this, Shaw juxtaposes three alternative courages:

1. **Professional courage**: [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s competence and clear-eyed self-preservation. He carries chocolate instead of cartridges because he is "a [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]]" who understands that staying alive is a soldier's first duty: "It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can" （001-236 to 001-238）.

2. **Social courage**: [[louka|Louka]]'s refusal to accept the servile philosophy of [[nicola|Nicola]], her insistence on her own dignity and ambition against a class system that punishes such aspirations. Her courage is the courage to want more — and to act on that wanting.

3. **Strategic courage**: [[nicola|Nicola]]'s patience, his willingness to endure contempt and bide his time, saving money and secrets for a future shop in Sofia. It is a compromised, unglamorous courage — but the play does not dismiss it.

The question the play poses, through each of these characters, is not "Who is brave?" but "What *kind* of bravery matters?"

## The Romantic View vs. Shaw's Alternatives

The romantic conception of courage is established through the absent [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], whose cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] is narrated by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] as the supreme act of bravery: "He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns" （001-069 to 001-072）.

But [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s entrance immediately destabilizes this frame. A professional soldier who prefers food to ammunition, he climbs a drainpipe to save his life and, when accused of cowardice, responds with the play's first redefinition of the term:

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. （001-236 to 001-238）

This is not cowardice but professional doctrine: the soldier's job is to survive and be effective. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] challenges him — "I think I am at heart as brave as you" （001-421） — he does not dispute it. Instead, he explains the physiological reality of courage under fire: "I can stand two days without shewing it much; but no man can stand three days: I'm as nervous as a mouse" （001-424 to 001-425）. Courage, in Bluntschli's account, is not a fixed character trait but a depletable resource — a radically modern, almost clinical view that the play endorses.

## Courage as Depletable Resource

Bluntschli's account of courage as something that runs out after two or three days under fire （001-424 to 001-425） represents a fundamental challenge to the romantic ideal of courage as a permanent character trait. The romantic hero is *always* brave — it is what defines him. Shaw's professional soldier knows better: courage is a physiological capacity, not a moral quality, and it is finite.

This redefinition has profound implications. If courage is not a fixed trait but a resource that can be exhausted, then:
- No one is intrinsically "brave" or "cowardly"
- The question becomes not "Are you brave?" but "How long can you sustain courage under pressure?"
- The romantic hero who is always brave is revealed as a fictional construct

## The Ultimate Definition

By Act III, the play arrives at its mature definition of courage through [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s speech, which the play endorses even as it shows Sergius failing to live by it:

> SERGIUS. Oh, give me the man who will defy to the death any power on earth or in heaven that sets itself up against his own will and conscience: he alone is the brave man. （003-558 to 003-560）

The definition is magnificent, and it would be the play's last word on courage — except that [[louka|Louka]] immediately turns it back on him. If courage means defying power, then she — the servant who refuses to be a servant — is the truly brave one, not the major who can resign his commission with his class status intact:

> LOUKA. How easy it is to talk! Men never seem to me to grow up: they all have schoolboy's ideas. You don't know what true courage is. （003-562 to 003-563）

## Shaw's Class-Conscious Framework

Shaw's treatment of courage is class-conscious and radically democratic. The play argues that what society calls "courage" — the willingness to kill and die in battle — is actually the cheapest, most animal form of bravery, available to "an English bull terrier" （003-551） as much as to any man. The word "cheap" in Sergius's speech is precise: battlefield courage is a low-value currency, inflated by patriotic rhetoric but worth very little when examined.

Far more demanding — and far more rare — is the courage to resist social pressure. The truly brave acts in the play are performed by women (Raina hiding Bluntschli, Louka defying her class position) or by a man who rejects masculine codes of honor (Bluntschli refusing the duel).
