# Operatic Tenor

Bluntschli's description of Sergius's cavalry charge — "He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We did laugh" (001-489) — is one of the play's most effective comic deflations. The comparison reduces military heroism to theatrical performance: the cavalry charge is not a feat of arms but an aria, and the celebrated hero is not a warrior but a singer in costume.

## The Tenor as Type

The operatic tenor of the late nineteenth century occupied a specific cultural position. He was the romantic lead — the passionate lover, the noble hero, the figure around whom the drama of transcendent love and tragic sacrifice revolved. Verdi's tenors (Manrico in *Il Trovatore*, the Duke in *Rigoletto*, Alfredo in *La Traviata*) and the verismo heroes of the 1890s established a type: handsome, ardent, vocally brilliant, and emotionally excessive. The tenor was, in effect, the Byronic hero set to music — a figure designed to be consumed from a distance, in a darkened theater, by an audience that identifies with his passion without experiencing its consequences.

## Bluntschli's Diagnosis

Bluntschli's comparison is devastating because it is literally accurate. Sergius's cavalry charge, as Bluntschli has just described it, was precisely a theatrical gesture: a suicidal rush at machine guns accomplished with "flashing eyes and lovely moustache" and accompanied by a "war-cry" — the operatic tenor's high note, delivered at the climax of the act. The professional soldiers who witness it do not admire; they laugh. "We did laugh" (001-489) — the verb is blunt, unglamorous, and final. The operatic tenor, seen from the wings rather than the stalls, is comic rather than sublime.

## Performance vs. Competence

The operatic tenor metaphor encapsulates the play's central distinction between performance and competence. The tenor's job is to produce an effect — to move the audience, to seem heroic, to fill the hall with sound. The soldier's job is to achieve an objective — to survive, to protect his men, to win the battle. Sergius, who charges machine guns "like an operatic tenor," has confused these categories: he has performed heroism rather than practiced soldiering. The wrong cartridges at Slivnitza (001-493) are the material correlative of this confusion — the ammunition that should have killed him was as theatrical as his performance, blanks in a drama that mistook itself for war.

## Shaw's Theatrical Self-Consciousness

There is a layer of meta-theatrical irony in the operatic tenor comparison. Shaw, a playwright, is using one performing art (opera) to critique another (romantic drama) through the medium of a third (his own play). Bluntschli's "we did laugh" implicates the audience: they are being asked to recognize that the heroic narratives they have consumed in operas and plays are as ridiculous as Sergius's charge. The operatic tenor is, in Shaw's hands, a mirror held up to his own audience — a reminder that the romantic heroism they have paid to see is, from any practical standpoint, absurd.
