# Dragoman Pass

The Dragoman Pass is mentioned in the play's opening stage direction — "a small town near the Dragoman Pass"（001-002）— and serves as the geographical anchor for the entire drama. Located in western [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], near the border with [[servia|Servia]], the pass was the site of the final engagements of the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] in November 1885.

## Strategic Significance

The Dragoman Pass is a natural corridor through the Balkan Mountains, one of the traditional invasion routes between Servia and Bulgaria. After the Bulgarian victory at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] (November 17–19, 1885), retreating Servian forces were pursued through the pass into Servian territory. The town near the pass where the Petkoffs live would have witnessed the passage of armies — the victorious Bulgarians returning home, the defeated Servians fleeing, and isolated stragglers like [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] seeking shelter.

## In the Play

Shaw's decision to locate the play "near the Dragoman Pass" rather than in [[sofia|Sofia]] or another urban center is dramatically significant. The proximity to the battlefield — the war is literally at the Petkoffs' doorstep — makes Bluntschli's intrusion plausible. More importantly, it collapses the distance between the domestic sphere and the military sphere. The war is not happening somewhere else; it is happening down the road, and its consequences climb through bedroom windows.

## Key Scene

The opening stage direction's mention of the Dragoman Pass does the work of a historical footnote in a novel. It tells the audience — particularly a British audience in 1894, which might not have followed Balkan affairs — that this is a real place, a real war, and that the comedy about to unfold has a documentary foundation beneath its theatrical surface.
