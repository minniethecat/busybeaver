# Chocolate Cream Soldier

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s nickname for [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] — "my chocolate cream soldier"（001-566）— supplies the play's most enduring image and its most concentrated metaphor. The chocolate creams in [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s cartridge case function as Shaw's master symbol for the collision between romantic expectation and prosaic reality, between the operatic and the practical, and ultimately between two competing visions of what a man should be.

## Definition

The chocolate cream soldier is Shaw's anti-hero: a fighting man who carries confectionery instead of ammunition. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] discovers that her "enemy" soldier's revolver holds "not bullets but chocolate instead"（001-396）, her entire romantic edifice — built on Pushkin, Byron, and Verdi — collapses into comedy. The image works by inversion: where the romantic hero wields a weapon, the Shawian realist carries food; where the Byronic soldier seeks glory, the professional soldier seeks supper. The chocolate cream is thus the play's definitive counter-symbol to [[chivalry]], [[romanticism]], and the whole apparatus of military hero-worship.

The symbol operates at three interlocking levels:

1. **Material**: the physical object — a box of chocolate creams on Raina's chest of drawers（001-023）, emptied into Bluntschli's cartridge case — which grounds the metaphor in the tangible and the domestic.
2. **Verbal**: the nickname "chocolate cream soldier"（001-566）, which circulates through the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] as a private joke, a guilty secret, and finally a public confession.
3. **Thematic**: the larger idea that sustenance matters more than glory, that a man who knows the value of provisions is worthier than one who knows only how to charge — the argument Shaw makes against every convention of military romance.

## In the Play

### ACT I: Discovery and Naming

The play's opening stage direction establishes the symbolic field before a word of dialogue is spoken. On the chest of drawers in Raina's bedchamber sits "a pile of paper backed novels, a box of chocolate creams, and a miniature easel" bearing a photograph of the "extremely handsome officer" Sergius（001-023–024）. The three objects — novels, chocolates, photograph — are Raina's romantic equipment, the props of the imaginative life she has constructed from literary models. The chocolate creams belong, from the first, to the world of fantasy.

The chocolate revelation scene（001-395–414）is the play's comic centerpiece. Bluntschli, a fugitive enemy soldier, has climbed into Raina's bedroom. She questions him about the cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]], and he describes it not as a glorious victory but as a professional disaster — the regiment was sent the wrong cartridges（001-493）. When Raina demands to see his revolver, the truth emerges:

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.
>
> RAINA（_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_）. Chocolate! Do you stuff your pockets with sweets — like a schoolboy — even in the field?
>
> MAN. Yes. Isn't it contemptible?
>
> （_Raina stares at him, unable to utter her feelings. Then she sails away scornfully to the chest of drawers, and returns with the box of confectionery in her hand._）
>
> RAINA. Allow me. I am sorry I have eaten them all except these.（_She offers him the box._）
>
> MAN（_ravenously_）. You're an angel!（_He gobbles the comfits._）Creams! Delicious!（001-395–414）

This is the play's definitive exchange. Raina's outrage — "Chocolate! Do you stuff your pockets with sweets — like a schoolboy — even in the field?"（001-398–399）— measures the distance between her literary expectations and soldierly reality. She has been raised on stories of gallant officers and heroic cavalry charges; she encounters instead a hungry Swiss professional who thinks battle is a matter of logistics and chocolate is a campaigning necessity. The stage direction "outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）makes Shaw's target explicit: it is not Bluntschli but Raina's ideals that are on trial.

Yet the scene also initiates Raina's conversion. She feeds him. The maternal gesture — offering the remaining creams from her own box — reverses the power dynamic: the fearsome enemy becomes a hungry man to be nourished. Bluntschli's ravenous "Creams! Delicious!"（001-409）is the voice of simple bodily appetite cutting through romantic posturing. In Shaw's world, appetite is truth.

The naming scene follows at the act's close. After Bluntschli has described Sergius's charge as the act of a "Don Quixote flourishing like a drum major" who "ought to be courtmartialled"（001-498–500）, after he has seen Sergius's photograph and recognized him as the fool who led the suicidal charge, Raina must acknowledge the collapse of her romantic framework. Her response is not to reject Bluntschli but to rename him:

> MAN. Ah, true, dear young lady: you're always right. I know how good you have been to me: to my last hour I shall remember those three chocolate creams. It was unsoldierly; but it was angelic.
>
> RAINA. （_disarmed by pity_）. Come, don't be disheartened.（_She stoops over him almost maternally: he shakes his head._）Oh, you are a very poor soldier — a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture — remember that.（001-549–567）

The nickname is born in tenderness. Raina's "you are a very poor soldier — a chocolate cream soldier"（001-565–566）is an act of affectionate diminishment: she domesticates the enemy by naming him after the sweets she first offered him. The epithet transforms the professional soldier into something manageable, almost pet-like. But the tenderness is real, and it marks the beginning of Raina's emotional reorientation. The chocolate creams have become, as Bluntschli says, "unsoldierly; but angelic"（001-551）— a phrase that collapses the distinction between the martial and the domestic, the masculine and the maternal.

Bluntschli himself adopts the nickname moments later, as he steels himself to climb down the water pipe: "that pipe must be got down — Do you hear that, you chocolate cream soldier?"（001-587–588）. The self-address is half encouragement, half ironic self-mockery. The professional soldier has accepted Raina's diminutive; the chocolate cream soldier has been christened from both sides.

### ACT II: Secret and Circulation

Between Act I and Act II, the chocolate cream soldier has become a guilty secret — a private language shared by [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]. The women have sent Bluntschli away in Major Petkoff's old coat, and now the Major's return threatens to expose everything. Catherine's fear and Raina's perversity converge on the nickname:

> RAINA（_gazing thoughtfully at the gravel as she walks_）. The little beast!
>
> CATHERINE. Little beast! What little beast?
>
> RAINA. To go and tell! Oh, if I had him here, I'd stuff him with chocolate creams till he couldn't ever speak again!（002-689–694）

Raina's threat — to stuff Bluntschli with the very substance that defines him — is comic but revealing. The chocolate creams have become both weapon and punishment: the symbol of intimacy can, in Raina's fantasy, become an instrument of silencing. The line "I'd stuff him with chocolate creams till he couldn't ever speak again"（002-693–694）is the precise inversion of the Act I feeding scene: what was then an act of mercy would now be an act of aggression.

Catherine, the practical realist in the Petkoff household, sees the danger more clearly. She warns Raina about Sergius's jealousy, but Raina's response is a declaration of emotional independence:

> RAINA（_capriciously — half to herself_）. I always feel a longing to do or say something dreadful to him — to shock his propriety — to scandalize the five senses out of him!（_To Catherine perversely._）I don't care whether he finds out about the chocolate cream soldier or not. I half hope he may.（002-716–720）

This is a crucial psychological moment. Raina's perverse wish — "I half hope he may"（002-719）— reveals that she is already beginning to prefer the chocolate cream soldier's world of honesty to Sergius's world of posturing. The secret she should protect has become, in her mind, a weapon against the engagement she can no longer fully inhabit. The chocolate cream soldier is no longer merely a fugitive she sheltered; he has become an alternative to the life mapped out for her.

The secret surfaces despite her efforts. When [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] returns unexpectedly to the Petkoff house to return the borrowed coat, Raina's reaction is instantaneous and unguarded:

> RAINA（_completely losing her presence of mind_）. Oh, the chocolate cream soldier!（002-865–866）

The stage direction "completely losing her presence of mind"（002-865）is the play's diagnosis. The chocolate cream soldier is what Raina thinks of — what she has been thinking of — beneath the surface of her social performance. Her mother's quick cover story about "an ice pudding" and Raina's winning recovery — "I hope you didn't think that you were the chocolate cream soldier, Captain Bluntschli"（002-879–880）— establish a pattern that will govern Act II: the chocolate cream soldier is public property now, a joke shared between Raina, her mother, and Bluntschli himself, who replies with a "whimsical glance"（002-882）that he did indeed think so.

Major Petkoff, perpetually behind the narrative, provides the act's comic coda when he threatens to discipline Nicola for having "broken Raina's chocolate soldier"（002-893）— unwittingly substituting the nickname for the ice-pudding ornament. The linguistic substitution is Shaw at his most economical: the chocolate soldier has so thoroughly colonized the household's discourse that even its most obtuse member uses the phrase.

### ACT III: Confession and Acceptance

Act III brings the chocolate cream soldier fully into the open. The comic climax is a series of confrontations in which every character must reckon with what the nickname means.

The first confrontation comes through [[louka|Louka]], the servant, who deploys the term as a class weapon. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] reproaches Louka for eavesdropping, Louka retorts:

> LOUKA（_facing her and retorting her contempt with interest_）. My love, stronger than anything you can feel, even for your chocolate cream soldier.（003-869–871）

Louka, who has learned from [[nicola|Nicola]] to read the household's secrets, recognizes the chocolate cream soldier for what he is: the name of Raina's real attachment. The taunt is effective precisely because it names the truth that Raina cannot yet acknowledge — that her feeling for [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is "love," and that the pet name is the index of that love. Louka's deployment of the term as a weapon against Raina marks the moment when the private joke becomes public knowledge.

The central confrontation belongs to the men. Major Petkoff, having discovered Bluntschli's photograph with its damning inscription, solves the mystery in his own blundering way:

> PETKOFF（_turning to the others_）. Come: do you think I don't see it all?（_Goes to Sergius, and slaps him on the shoulder._）Sergius: you're the chocolate cream soldier, aren't you?
>
> SERGIUS（_starting up_）. I! a chocolate cream soldier! Certainly not.
>
> PETKOFF. Not!（_He looks at them. They are all very serious and very conscious._）Do you mean to tell me that Raina sends photographic souvenirs to other men?
>
> SERGIUS（_enigmatically_）. The world is not such an innocent place as we used to think, Petkoff.
>
> BLUNTSCHLI（_rising_）. It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier.（_Petkoff and Sergius are equally astonished._）The gracious young lady saved my life by giving me chocolate creams when I was starving — shall I ever forget their flavour!（003-950–966）

The comedy of the scene depends on the audience's understanding that the chocolate cream soldier is the last identity Sergius — the heroic Don Quixote of the cavalry charge — could possibly inhabit. Sergius's outraged "I! a chocolate cream soldier! Certainly not."（003-954）is the reflex of a man whose entire self-conception depends on being the opposite of everything the nickname represents. Bluntschli's calm "It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier."（003-963）is the opposite reflex: the professional soldier's willingness to accept any name, any classification, so long as it is accurate.

But Bluntschli's understanding of the chocolate cream soldier is incomplete. When he attempts to "put everything right" by explaining the affair away as a schoolgirl's game, he reveals his own romantic blind spot:

> BLUNTSCHLI. All that adventure which was life or death to me, was only a schoolgirl's game to her — chocolate creams and hide and seek. Here's the proof!（_He takes the photograph from the table._）Now, I ask you, would a woman who took the affair seriously have sent me this and written on it: "Raina, to her chocolate cream soldier — a souvenir"?（003-1095–1101）
>
> RAINA（_in uncontrollable vexation_）. I quite agree with your account of yourself. You are a romantic idiot.（_Bluntschli is unspeakably taken aback._）Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three.（003-1107–1111）

This exchange — "You are a romantic idiot."（003-1109）— is the play's deepest reversal. Bluntschli, the supposed realist, has failed to see that the chocolate cream soldier epithet was not a schoolgirl's joke but a declaration of feeling. He has been undone by his own literalism: he read the photograph inscription at face value, mistaking Raina's romantic irony for childish indifference. Raina, the supposed romantic, exposes him: she knows the difference between a game and a serious attachment, between "a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three"（003-1111）. The chocolate cream soldier metaphor has come full circle: the realist who carried chocolate instead of cartridges has himself been guilty of a romantic misreading.

The play's final invocation of the chocolate cream soldier is its resolution. After the truth has been acknowledged, after Bluntschli has proposed and been accepted, he demands one more confession:

> BLUNTSCHLI. That's just what I say.（_He catches her hand quickly and looks her straight in the face as he adds, with confident mastery_）Now tell us who you did give them to.
>
> RAINA（_succumbing with a shy smile_）. To my chocolate cream soldier!（003-1198–1202）

The possessive pronoun — "my" — completes the transformation. What began in Act I as a mocking diminutive has become, in Act III, a declaration of love. The chocolate cream soldier is no longer a stranger to be named but a partner to be claimed. Shaw's structural genius is to have the play's central symbol travel this arc: from contempt to affection, from private joke to public confession, from nickname to identity.

## Key Scenes

The three pivotal scenes below trace the chocolate cream soldier's transformation from comic discovery through guilty secret to public identity.

### The Revelation（Act I）

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.
>
> RAINA（_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_）. Chocolate! Do you stuff your pockets with sweets — like a schoolboy — even in the field?
>
> MAN. Yes. Isn't it contemptible?（001-395–401）

The moment that gives the play its nickname. Raina's outrage measures the collapse of her romantic education. The stage direction "outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）specifies the target: it is not Bluntschli who is on trial here but Raina's ideals, and through them, the entire apparatus of military hero-worship.

### The Naming（Act I）

> MAN. I know how good you have been to me: to my last hour I shall remember those three chocolate creams. It was unsoldierly; but it was angelic.
>
> RAINA（_disarmed by pity_）. Oh, you are a very poor soldier — a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture — remember that.（001-549–567）

The christening of the chocolate cream soldier. Bluntschli's "unsoldierly; but angelic"（001-551）is the phrase that best captures Shaw's thesis: the practical, the domestic, the nourishing — all that military romance despises — is, in the end, what saves lives. Raina's maternal tenderness transforms the enemy into a creature of confectionery.

### The Confession（Act III）

> PETKOFF. Sergius: you're the chocolate cream soldier, aren't you?
>
> SERGIUS（_starting up_）. I! a chocolate cream soldier! Certainly not.
>
> BLUNTSCHLI（_rising_）. It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier. The gracious young lady saved my life by giving me chocolate creams when I was starving — shall I ever forget their flavour!（003-952–966）

The public confession. Sergius's horrified recoil — "I! a chocolate cream soldier! Certainly not."（003-954）— and Bluntschli's calm acceptance — "I'm the chocolate cream soldier."（003-963）— constitute the play's central moral opposition in miniature. The man who cannot abide the name is the man who cannot see himself; the man who accepts it is the man who knows what he is.

### The Final Surrender（Act III）

> BLUNTSCHLI. Now tell us who you did give them to.
>
> RAINA（_succumbing with a shy smile_）. To my chocolate cream soldier!（003-1200–1202）

The play's last invocation of the nickname. The possessive "my" and the tender stage direction — "succumbing with a shy smile"（003-1202）— mark the completion of Raina's arc. The chocolate cream soldier has been reclaimed from mockery and restored to love.

## Shaw's Position

The chocolate cream is Shaw's answer to the sword. Through this single comic device, Shaw prosecutes his case against [[romanticism]] in all its forms and advances his argument for [[realism]] as the only viable philosophy of life.

- **Against military glory**: The orthodox hero carries a weapon and seeks fame; the Shawian realist carries food and seeks survival. Bluntschli's chocolate is more useful in battle than cartridges because a man who is fed can fight another day — but more importantly, a man who carries chocolate instead of ammunition has no intention of fighting at all. The symbol deflates the entire apparatus of military heroism by revealing that what soldiers actually need is not glory but provisions.

- **Against romantic love**: Raina's romantic education has taught her to expect a Byronic hero — "a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills"（001-489–491）. What she gets is a hungry Swiss who gobbles chocolate creams and is afraid of heights. The chocolate cream soldier is the anti-Sergius: where Sergius performs, Bluntschli eats; where Sergius postures, Bluntschli admits he is tired; where Sergius is Byronic, Bluntschli is Swiss. Shaw's thesis is that a man who knows his own appetite is worthier than one who knows only his own image.

- **Against class pretension**: The chocolate creams in Raina's room are a bourgeois luxury — "a box of chocolate creams"（001-023）on the chest of drawers, the confectionery of a young lady with leisure to read novels and cultivate romantic fantasies. Louka's sarcastic deployment of "your chocolate cream soldier"（003-870）exposes the class dimension: the nickname that seems affectionate from Raina is, from Louka's perspective, a mark of privilege — the indulgence of a mistress who can afford to turn soldiers into sweets.

- **For professional competence**: Bluntschli's chocolate is, above all, practical. "What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead"（001-395–396）. The old soldier, he explains, carries "grub" rather than ammunition — "The young ones carry pistols and cartridges; the old ones, grub"（001-413–414）. The distinction between young and old, romantic and practical, is also the distinction between the amateur and the professional. Shaw's ideal soldier — and by extension his ideal man — is the professional who knows what sustains him.

The chocolate cream soldier thus embodies what might be called Shaw's philosophy of the practical: the belief that truth, honesty, and common sense will always outlast illusion, pretense, and heroic posturing. That the symbol is also comic — that Shaw makes his argument through laughter rather than lecture — is the measure of his dramatic skill.

## Critical Perspectives

The chocolate cream soldier has attracted sustained critical attention as one of Shaw's most effective dramatic symbols.

**Eric Bentley**, in *Bernard Shaw*（1947）, identifies the chocolate creams as the play's "master metaphor" — a symbol that works simultaneously as comic device, character revelation, and philosophical argument. For Bentley, the chocolate creams' power lies in their domesticity: they bring the battlefield into the boudoir and the boudoir onto the battlefield, collapsing the distinction between the two spheres that romantic ideology insists on keeping separate.

**Martin Meisel**, in *Shaw and the Nineteenth-Century Theater*（1963）, reads the chocolate cream soldier as Shaw's intervention in the tradition of stage realism. The symbol, Meisel argues, performs the same function as Ibsen's pistols or Chekhov's cherry orchard — an apparently trivial object that accumulates meaning through repetition until it carries the play's entire thematic weight. What distinguishes Shaw's use is the comic register: the chocolate creams accumulate meaning not through tragedy but through variations of tone — outrage, affection, embarrassment, confession, love.

**Michael Holroyd**, in his biography *Bernard Shaw*（1988–1992）, notes that the chocolate cream soldier reflects Shaw's own experience as a vegetarian and teetotaler in a society that equated masculinity with carnivorous excess. The chocolate cream soldier is, in Holroyd's reading, a self-portrait — the Shawian man who refuses to conform to masculine conventions and is therefore dismissed as less than a man, only to triumph through superior intelligence and honesty.

**Margery M. Morgan**, in *The Shavian Playground*（1972）, emphasizes the childlike quality of the chocolate creams. The sweets belong to the nursery, and Raina's progression from "schoolgirl's game"（003-1096）to "woman of twenty-three"（003-1111）is registered through her changing relationship to the symbol. Morgan reads the chocolate creams as transitional objects — the toys of Raina's romantic childhood that must be discarded（literally torn up, in the photograph's case）before adult love can begin.

**Nicholas Grene**, in *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View*（1984）, draws attention to the pun embedded in the symbol. "Creams" suggests both confectionery and the sentimental — the "creamy" excess of romantic rhetoric that Shaw is satirizing. When Bluntschli gobbles the comfits with a ravenous "Creams! Delicious!"（001-409）, he is consuming not only Raina's chocolates but the whole apparatus of romantic fantasy she has built around them. The chocolate cream soldier is thus the figure who devours romance and replaces it with appetite.
