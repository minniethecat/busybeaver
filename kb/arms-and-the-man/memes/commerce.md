# Commerce

Commerce — the world of hotels, horses, sheets, and knives and forks — is the play's hidden moral foundation. Where [[war]] produces death and [[romanticism]] produces illusion, commerce produces value: beds to sleep in, food to eat, a living for those who work. Shaw, the Fabian socialist who was also a shrewd businessman, presents commerce not as a grubby alternative to higher callings but as a higher calling in itself — more honest than soldiering, more useful than poetry.

## Bluntschli as Commercial Hero

[[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] is the play's commercial hero. His Swiss identity is a commercial identity — Switzerland being, in Shaw's imaginative geography, the nation of hotels and banking and rational self-interest. His inheritance — "about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks"（003-1294–1296）— is a commercial inventory. His proposal to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is a commercial negotiation. His competence as a soldier is an extension of his competence as a businessman: both require the accurate assessment of resources, risks, and returns.

## Commerce vs. War

The play's deepest argument may be the argument it makes through Bluntschli's dual identity: commerce is the alternative to war. The Swiss hotel-keeper who fights as a mercenary represents the subordination of war to commerce — war as a temporary employment, not a permanent identity. When the war ends, Bluntschli returns to the hotels; his real life is commercial, and soldiering is merely an interlude.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's celebration of commerce is characteristically qualified. He does not celebrate commerce as an end in itself — the Fabian in him knew that commerce could be as exploitative as war — but he celebrates the commercial mentality as an alternative to the military mentality. The businessman who calculates profit and loss is, in Shaw's view, less dangerous than the cavalry officer who calculates glory and honor.

- **Target of satire**: The aristocratic disdain for commerce that elevates war above trade
- **Alternative proposed**: The commercial ethos — rational, peaceful, wealth-creating — as a superior alternative to the military ethos
