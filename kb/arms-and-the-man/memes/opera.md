# Opera

Opera — specifically the grand operas of [[verdi|Verdi]] and the Italian tradition — is the musical component of the romantic education Shaw's play dismantles. Alongside [[alexander-pushkin|Pushkin]]'s poetry and [[lord-byron|Byron]]'s heroes, opera supplies the emotional vocabulary through which [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] interpret their lives.

## Opera as False Consciousness

For Shaw, opera was simultaneously a beloved art form and a dangerous pedagogy. Operas taught audiences — particularly young women — that love was a transcendent passion, that soldiers were noble heroes, that death for honor was glorious, and that the world was a stage for the performance of grand emotions. Every one of these teachings is false, in Shaw's analysis, and *Arms and the Man* is a campaign to replace the operatic imagination with the realistic one.

## The Anti-Operatic Play

The play is structured as an anti-opera. Where an opera would end with the lovers united in a triumphant duet, Shaw ends with a hotel inventory. Where Verdi's tenor would die for love, Shaw's tenor (Sergius) is outmaneuvered by a servant and married off to avoid scandal. Where the operatic heroine would be transfigured by suffering, Shaw's heroine sheds her romantic costume and admits she is "only a woman."

## Key Scene

Raina's opening rhapsodies are operatic in form and content: the heroine alone in her chamber, addressing the absent hero, expressing adoration and awe. The entrance of Bluntschli — hungry, exhausted, unarmed — is the entrance of reality into the opera, and the rest of the play is the opera's gradual dismantling.
