# Nationalism

Nationalism is the political passion that drives the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] and animates the characters' public identities. But Shaw treats nationalism as he treats every other romantic ideal: with comic skepticism and an eye to its material foundations. The play's most devastating political argument may be its quiet demonstration that national feeling is a luxury of those who do not have to fight.

## Nationalism and War

The [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] was fundamentally a nationalist conflict — Servian and Bulgarian nationalisms colliding over territorial claims. The characters invoke [[patriotism]] freely: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s opening declaration that the Bulgarian victory is "splendid"（001-038）is patriotic sentiment in its most reflexive form. But the play systematically undermines the nationalist framework. [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]], a Swiss mercenary fighting for Servia, has no national stake in the conflict. He fights for pay, not for country — and this makes him, in Shaw's presentation, more honest, not less.

## The Cosmopolitan Alternative

Bluntschli's Swiss identity is Shaw's deliberate counterweight to the nationalist passions of the Bulgarian characters. Switzerland — neutral, commercial, multilingual — represents a world beyond nationalism, in which identity is defined by profession rather than nation. When Bluntschli reveals his hotel inheritance, the romantic plot dissolves into commercial calculation — and the nationalist plot, the play implies, should dissolve similarly.

## Key Scene

> BLUNTSCHLI. I am a Swiss, fighting as a professional officer in the Servian army. I have no interest in the politics of the war.（001-488–489）

Bluntschli's declaration of national disinterest is the play's quietest and most radical political statement. In a world of patriotic fervor, the man who says "I have no interest in the politics of the war" is either a traitor or a prophet. Shaw leaves the question open.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw, an Irishman writing in London about a Balkan war, had a complex relationship with nationalism. He supported Irish Home Rule, but he was suspicious of the nationalist passions that fueled militarism. *Arms and the Man* argues, implicitly, that the nation-state is a machine for converting young men into corpses — and that the professional mercenary who fights for pay may be the more moral figure, because he does not pretend his killing serves a higher cause.

- **Target of satire**: The nationalist rhetoric that transforms territorial aggression into patriotic duty
- **Alternative proposed**: A cosmopolitan professionalism that evaluates wars by their costs rather than their causes
