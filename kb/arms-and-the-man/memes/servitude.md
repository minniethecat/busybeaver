# Servitude

Servitude in *Arms and the Man* is treated not as a natural condition but as a social institution — an arrangement that can be accepted, manipulated, or rejected. Through the contrasting strategies of [[nicola|Nicola]] and [[louka|Louka]], Shaw examines the inner life of domestic service and the possibilities for agency within it.

## In the Play

### ACT II

Nicola's defense of servitude is one of the play's most philosophically rich passages. When Louka mocks him for his obsequiousness, he replies with a credo:

> NICOLA. I know my place. I put my pride in my pocket and ride it out. I can be hurt by nothing.（002-867–870）

The statement is simultaneously humiliating and defiant. "I put my pride in my pocket" is an admission of self-abasement; "I can be hurt by nothing" is a claim of invulnerability. Nicola's argument — that the servant who accepts his position becomes, paradoxically, free — is Shaw's exploration of a real psychological strategy: the servant who no longer resents his servitude is no longer wounded by it.

### ACT III

Louka's rebellion against servitude provides the counter-argument. Unlike Nicola, she refuses to "put her pride in her pocket." Her pursuit of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is not a love story but a career move — a servant's gambit to escape the servant class through marriage. Louka's famous confrontation with Sergius — "You were born to a position. I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something"（003-1091–1092）— is the play's most explicit statement of class ambition.

## Key Scene

> LOUKA. You were born to a position. I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something.（003-1091–1092）

The line is a declaration of class war couched as romantic passion. Louka is not in love with Sergius; she is in pursuit of upward mobility. Shaw's point is that the distinction barely matters — the romantic plot and the economic plot are the same plot, viewed from different angles.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of servitude reflects his Fabian socialism: the institution is unjust, but the strategies available to those within it are limited and morally complex. Nicola's strategic submission is not condemned; Louka's strategic rebellion is not celebrated. Both characters are navigating an unjust system with the tools available to them, and Shaw's sympathy extends to both.

- **Target of satire**: The class system that makes servitude an inherited condition
- **Alternative proposed**: Individual agency within an unjust system — not revolution, but strategic self-advancement
