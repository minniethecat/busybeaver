Bulgaria is more than the geographical setting of *Arms and the Man* — it is the symbolic stage on which Shaw dramatizes the collision of [[romanticism|romantic idealism]] and modern realism. Freshly liberated from Ottoman rule and eager to prove itself on the European stage, the young Bulgarian nation provides Shaw with a perfect laboratory: a society simultaneously intoxicated by romantic nationalism and unprepared for the mechanized realities of 19th-century warfare. The play's Bulgarian setting is historically specific (1885–1886, immediately after the [[serbo-bulgarian-war]]) yet thematically universal — Shaw uses the Balkans as a mirror for Victorian Britain's own [[romanticism|romantic delusion]]s about war, class, and love.

## 基本信息

- **地理位置**：Southeast Europe, Balkan Peninsula. The play is set in a small town near the Dragoman Pass, on the route between Sofia and the Serbian border.
- **剧中场景**：The entire play unfolds within a Bulgarian household and its garden, but the nation's political and military context permeates every scene.
- **类型**：Country（国家——全剧宏观设定）

## 舞台指示描述

Shaw's opening stage direction situates the play with ethnographic precision:

> Night. A lady's bedchamber in Bulgaria, in a small town near the Dragoman Pass. It is late in November, 1885. （001-001–002）

The description blends the local and the cosmopolitan: the room is "half rich Bulgarian, half cheap Viennese" （001-006）— a phrase that captures a society caught between tradition and European modernity. The Act II setting further anchors the play in Bulgarian geography:

> The sixth of March, 1886. In the garden of major Petkoff's house. It is a fine spring morning; and the garden looks fresh and pretty. Beyond the paling the tops of a couple of minarets can be seen, shewing that there is a valley there, with the little town in it. A few miles further the Balkan mountains rise and shut in the view. （002-001–005）

The Act III library stage direction underscores Bulgaria's aspirational but unconvincing modernity:

> In the library after lunch. It is not much of a library, its literary equipment consisting of a single fixed shelf stocked with old paper-covered novels, broken backed, coffee stained, torn and thumbed, and a couple of little hanging shelves with a few gift books on them, the rest of the wall space being occupied by trophies of war and the chase. But it is a most comfortable sitting-room. （003-001–010）

This cultural hybridity is essential to Shaw's critique — Bulgaria's elite imitate European romantic fashions (opera, Byron, chivalric ideals) while living on the frontier of real military conflict. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] reflects on this tension explicitly:

> RAINA. Well, it came into my head just as he was holding me in his arms and looking into my eyes, that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that—indeed never, as far as I knew it then. （001-094–098）

Raina's confession — that her heroic ideals may be imported fictions — is the intellectual core of Shaw's critique of Bulgarian romantic nationalism: the nation's self-image is borrowed from European literature, not grounded in Balkan reality.

## 剧情功能

- **承载事件**：The entire [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]], the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]], and the subsequent peace settlement occur within or adjacent to Bulgarian territory. The [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] becomes a microcosm of the nation, with Serbian fugitives, Russian officers, and Bulgarian aristocrats all crossing its threshold.

- **人物互动**：Bulgarian national identity shapes every character's position. [[raina-petkoff]] and [[sergius-saranoff]] are Bulgarian aristocrats performing European romanticism. [[captain-bluntschli]] is a Swiss mercenary fighting for Serbia — his neutrality highlights the absurdity of nationalist warfare. [[major-petkoff]] represents the provincial Bulgarian military establishment, proud of his library and electric bell as symbols of modernization.

- **信息揭示**：The gap between Bulgaria's romantic self-image and its military reality is the play's central dramatic irony. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge — celebrated as a triumph of Bulgarian spirit — was actually a tactical blunder that succeeded only by accident.

## 象征意义

- **Bulgaria as Romantic Innocence**：The young nation, like the young [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], is intoxicated by heroic narratives. Its recent liberation from Ottoman rule (1878) fuels a national mythology of martial glory that Shaw systematically deflates. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s claim that "the world is really a glorious world for women who can see its glory and men who can act its romance" （001-111–112）expresses the naive patriotic idealism the play dismantles.

- **The Library as Modernity's Facade**：[[major-petkoff]]'s prized library symbolizes Bulgaria's aspirational modernity. He boasts about it to visitors but never uses it; the books are "coffee stained, torn and thumbed" paper novels, not a working collection （003-001–005）. When [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] tells his Russian colleagues about the library — "I took care to let them know that we had a library" （002-186）— the gesture is purely performative. Shaw indicts not just Bulgaria but all societies that adopt the forms of civilization without its substance.

- **The Garden as Class Battleground**：The [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] garden in ACT II and III is where class boundaries are challenged — [[louka]] flirts with [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], [[nicola|Nicola]] schemes, and social hierarchies reveal themselves as performance rather than nature.

## 历史地理背景

- **历史事实**：The [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] (November 1885 – March 1886) was fought between the Kingdom of Serbia and the Principality of Bulgaria. Despite being newly formed and outnumbered, Bulgaria won decisively at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] (November 17–19, 1885). The war established Bulgaria as a serious Balkan power.

- **剧中改编**：Shaw compresses the war's timeline for dramatic effect. The [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] (November 1885) and the peace treaty (March 1886) serve as bookends for the play's action. Shaw's interest is not in military history per se but in its cultural interpretation — how a battle becomes a myth, and how that myth shapes the people who believe it.

- **Dragoman Pass**：The play's setting near the Dragoman Pass is geographically significant — it was the main invasion route from Serbia into Bulgaria, making the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] a plausible place for a fleeing Serbian officer to seek refuge.
