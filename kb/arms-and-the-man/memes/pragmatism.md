# Pragmatism

The practical, unsentimental worldview that Shaw offers as the antidote to romantic illusion. Embodied in [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] — the "chocolate cream soldier" — pragmatism is the philosophical engine of the play's comic resolution. Where [[romanticism]] builds castles in the air, pragmatism checks the foundations; where [[honor]] demands a duel, pragmatism asks whether the duel achieves anything. It is, in Shaw's design, not merely a personality trait but a moral and intellectual program: the argument that the world's problems are caused not by insufficient passion but by insufficient clear thinking.

## Definition

Pragmatism in *Arms and the Man* is the disposition to address situations as they are rather than as romantic convention dictates they should be. It values professional competence over amateur gallantry, rational calculation over passionate impulse, and honest self-assessment over theatrical posturing. The chocolate that [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] carries instead of ammunition is its perfect emblem:

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.（001-395）

A [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] knows that food is more urgently needed than bullets — and that survival, not glory, is the soldier's first duty. This single reversal — chocolate for cartridges — establishes the pragmatic scale of values that will measure every character in the play.

Pragmatism in Shaw's sense is distinct from cynicism. Where cynicism disbelieves in all values, pragmatism tests values against consequences. Bluntschli does not mock [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s ideals; he simply notes that they do not match the facts. His famous taxonomy of soldiers makes the same point:

> MAN. There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before. Why, how is it that you've just beaten us? Sheer ignorance of the art of war, nothing else. I never saw anything so unprofessional.（001-440）

The pragmatist sees patterns — old soldiers carry grub, young ones carry cartridges — and acts on them. He is not braver or more virtuous than the romantic; he is simply better informed, and therefore more effective.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Pragmatism enters the play through [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s exhausted, unromantic presence. While [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] are intoxicated by stories of heroic cavalry charges, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] climbs a drainpipe to escape death and asks for food. His first self-description disclaims all national and ideological allegiance:

> MAN. Austrian! not I. Don't hate me, dear young lady. I am only a Swiss, fighting merely as a professional soldier. I joined Servia because it was nearest to me. Be generous: you've beaten us hollow.（001-361）

This is pragmatism's founding declaration: war is a trade, not a crusade; nationality is geography, not identity; survival is the goal, not glory. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s Swiss neutrality — neither Austrian nor Servian, neither patriot nor ideologue — is the geopolitical expression of his pragmatic philosophy.

His critique of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge follows the same logic:

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it.（001-448）

What [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] celebrates as "our patriotism—our heroic ideals" (001-085), [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] diagnoses as tactical incompetence. The same event, viewed through romantic and pragmatic lenses, yields opposite judgments: heroism or stupidity. Shaw's point is not that one perspective is more true than the other, but that the pragmatic perspective has survival value — it keeps men alive — while the romantic perspective gets them killed.

The Act closes with [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s instinctive coinage, which names the philosophy before she understands it:

> RAINA. Oh, you are a very poor soldier—a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture—remember that.（001-566）

The "chocolate cream soldier" is pragmatism domesticated — sweet, practical, and faintly ridiculous, yet ultimately the only kind of soldier worth being.

### ACT II

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is physically absent from Act II, but pragmatism persists and deepens. The act presents three variations on the theme.

First, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] arrives at a bitter, pragmatic assessment of soldiering that echoes [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s Act I critique, though he frames it as disillusionment rather than professionalism:

> SERGIUS. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak. That is the whole secret of successful fighting. Get your enemy at a disadvantage; and never, on any account, fight him on equal terms.（002-339）

The irony is acute: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] speaks pragmatic truth but cannot live with it. Where [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] accepts the logic of soldiering and makes a profession of it, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] resigns in disgust — "I have no ambition to succeed as a tradesman" (002-346). Pragmatism without professional detachment produces only cynicism; it is the combination of clear sight and practical acceptance that makes Bluntschli's philosophy workable.

Second, [[nicola|Nicola]] articulates a servant's version of pragmatism: patient, calculating, and oriented toward long-term reward. His willingness to endure humiliation for future gain — saving money, planning a shop — mirrors [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s professional detachment. Both men understand that survival and success depend on seeing clearly, not feeling deeply. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s pragmatism is ethically thinner than [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s — it lacks the honesty and good humor — but structurally it is the same: rational self-interest as a guide to action.

Third, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] begins her pragmatic turn. Her anxiety about the "chocolate cream soldier" (002-719) being discovered is not just fear of exposure but the first stirring of honest self-assessment. She "half hopes he may" find out (002-719) — the pragmatist's instinct that concealment is more exhausting than truth.

### ACT III

Pragmatism triumphs. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] returns and resolves every romantic entanglement through clear-eyed practicality.

His refusal of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s duel is the play's definitive statement of pragmatic ethics:

> BLUNTSCHLI. I'm a professional soldier. I fight when I have to, and am very glad to get out of it when I haven't to. You're only an amateur: you think fighting's an amusement.（003-764）

The professional/amateur distinction is pragmatism's master category. The professional does what is necessary; the amateur does what feels noble. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s willingness to be called a coward — "I refuse to fight you" (003-760) — is the pragmatist's ultimate courage: the courage to reject a destructive code even at the cost of social dishonor.

When [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] insists on a duel nonetheless, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] applies pragmatic logic to the ritual itself:

> BLUNTSCHLI. Oh, thank you: that's a cavalry man's proposal. I'm in the artillery; and I have the choice of weapons. If I go, I shall take a machine gun. And there shall be no mistake about the cartridges this time.（003-649）

The reference to cartridges — echoing the "wrong cartridges" of the Slivnitza debacle (001-493) — ties pragmatism's Act I debut to its Act III vindication. The pragmatist learns from experience; the romantic repeats the same mistakes.

The play's comic resolution extends pragmatism from war to love. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] wins [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] not through courtship but through honesty — the pragmatic equivalent of romance. His self-description, offered as a deterrent, becomes instead the proposal that succeeds:

> BLUNTSCHLI. And I, a common-place Swiss soldier who hardly knows what a decent life is after fifteen years of barracks and battles—a vagabond—a man who has spoiled all his chances in life through an incurably romantic disposition—（003-1073）

The confession of "an incurably romantic disposition" is the play's deepest pragmatic insight: the pragmatist knows his own irrationality. Self-knowledge, not the absence of passion, is what distinguishes [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] from [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]. Both men are romantics; only one admits it.

The final pragmatic judgment belongs to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who, in his moment of crisis over [[nicola|Nicola]]'s behavior, appeals to pragmatism's authority:

> SERGIUS. This is either the finest heroism or the most crawling baseness. Which is it, Bluntschli?
> BLUNTSCHLI. Never mind whether it's heroism or baseness. Nicola's the ablest man I've met in Bulgaria. I'll make him manager of a hotel if he can speak French and German.（003-1007）

"Never mind whether it's heroism or baseness" — this is pragmatism in its purest form. The categories of romantic morality are irrelevant; what matters is competence. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s ability to manage a hotel is more valuable than [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s ability to feel noble. Shaw's Fabian socialism speaks through this judgment: the world needs capable administrators, not heroic posers.

## Key Scenes

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.（001-395）

The巧克力/弹药宣言。The "chocolate cream soldier" is born, and the play's central philosophical opposition — sustenance over symbol, life over glory — is established in a single exchange.

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire?（001-448）

Pragmatism's critique of romantic heroism, delivered by a fleeing mercenary to his aristocratic captor. The question is unanswerable on romantic terms, which is Shaw's point.

> MAN. There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones... The young ones carry pistols and cartridges; the old ones, grub.（001-440, 001-412-414）

The pragmatist's epistemology: observation yields pattern, pattern yields principle. Soldiers are not heroes or cowards; they are experienced or inexperienced.

> SERGIUS. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak.（002-339）

Pragmatism's dark twin: clear sight without professional acceptance produces only disillusionment. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] sees what [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] sees but cannot bear to live with it.

> BLUNTSCHLI. (_phlegmatically_). That's the Swiss national character, dear lady.（003-372）

The play's running joke: pragmatism as national identity. Swiss neutrality, Swiss professionalism, Swiss chocolate — a coherent philosophical package disguised as comic stereotype.

> BLUNTSCHLI. I'm a professional soldier. I fight when I have to, and am very glad to get out of it when I haven't to. You're only an amateur: you think fighting's an amusement.（003-764）

The professional/amateur distinction that structures the entire play. Pragmatism is not pacifism; it is discrimination — knowing when to fight and when to walk away.

> BLUNTSCHLI. Never mind whether it's heroism or baseness. Nicola's the ablest man I've met in Bulgaria. I'll make him manager of a hotel if he can speak French and German.（003-1010）

The ultimate pragmatic verdict: competence over character, results over motives. The hotel — the family business, the practical inheritance — is pragmatism's kingdom.

> BLUNTSCHLI. (_much pleased by this piece of common sense_). Saranoff: your hand. My congratulations. These heroics of yours have their practical side after all.（003-1049）

Pragmatism's blessing on [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s engagement to [[louka|Louka]] — the romantic's one genuinely pragmatic act. "Common sense" is the highest compliment in the pragmatist's lexicon.

## Shaw's Position

Pragmatism is Shaw's intellectual weapon against the entire edifice of [[romanticism|romantic idealism]] — in love, in war, in class relations. The play argues that the world's problems are caused not by insufficient passion but by insufficient clear thinking. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s pragmatism is not cynicism; it is a form of honesty that romanticism actively suppresses. When he observes that his father's hotels have "the highest opinion of his soldiers" as customers, he is not mocking the military — he is stating an economic fact that romanticism would prefer to ignore.

Pragmatism, for Shaw, is not merely a useful disposition — it is a moral imperative. The play's comic structure enacts a philosophical argument: romanticism causes suffering ([[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s misery, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s self-deception), while pragmatism produces happiness ([[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s contentment, the resolution of the romantic quadrangle). Shaw's modern sensibility — his Fabian socialism, his skepticism toward military glory, his belief in rational reform — finds its dramatic voice in the chocolate-cream soldier.

Three dimensions of Shaw's pragmatic philosophy deserve emphasis:

**The epistemological dimension**: Pragmatism is a way of knowing. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] judges the cavalry charge by its tactical outcome, not its emotional effect. He sees "old soldiers" and "young soldiers" where others see heroes and cowards. Pragmatic knowledge is empirical, probabilistic ("nine soldiers out of ten are born fools," 001-293), and always open to revision — unlike romantic conviction, which is final and absolute.

**The ethical dimension**: Pragmatism is a way of acting. It asks not "is this noble?" but "does this work?" The chocolate in the cartridge box is ethics by results: feeding a hungry soldier saves more lives than firing a bullet. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s refusal to duel is ethics by refusal: the honor code is not honored by obeying it when obedience would be destructive.

**The political dimension**: Pragmatism is Shaw's Fabian socialism in miniature. The play's resolution — a Swiss hotelier's son managing a Bulgarian household, a servant promoted to manager, a cavalry hero reduced to honest husband — is a pragmatic redistribution of roles based on competence rather than birth. The world the play leaves us with is not egalitarian, but it is functional; and for Shaw the Fabian, function is the first step toward justice.

The chocolate-cream soldier is Shaw's answer to the Byronic hero: a man who carries sweets instead of weapons, admits fear, counts his assets honestly, and wins — not through glamour but through the simple, radical act of telling the truth.

- **Satirical Target**: The belief that passionate conviction is superior to practical knowledge; the honor culture that demands duels and produces corpses; the class system that values noble gestures over competent administration
- **Alternative**: Rational self-interest, professional competence, truthful speech, and the courage to be called a coward

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's use of pragmatism as a dramatic and philosophical device has attracted sustained critical attention, particularly regarding its relationship to gender, class, and the limits of rationalism.

The gendered dimension of pragmatism has been a focus of feminist criticism. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s professionalism is coded masculine — the "strong-minded baby" with "prosaic nose" (001-216) — while [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romanticism is coded feminine. The play's resolution — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] accepting [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s unromantic proposal — has been read both as her liberation from feminine delusion and as her absorption into masculine rationality. J. Ellen Gainor notes in *Shaw's Daughters* (1991) that Raina's final choice is her own — "To my chocolate cream soldier!" (003-1202) — and that her agency in embracing pragmatism is as significant as the philosophy she embraces.

The class dimension is equally complex. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s pragmatism is bourgeois: hotels, commerce, professional fees. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s pragmatism is servile: saving tips, enduring insult, planning a shop. Both are pragmatic, but one has dignity and the other does not — and the difference, critics have argued, is class. Shaw, despite his socialism, cannot quite imagine a servant's pragmatism as heroic. As Nicholas Grene observes in *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View* (1984), "Nicola is Bluntschli without the Swiss hotels behind him — and without the hotels, pragmatism looks merely like crawling."

The philosophical limits of pragmatism have also been debated. Eric Bentley, in *The Playwright as Thinker* (1946), argues that Shaw's pragmatism is "comic pragmatism" — it works within the closed world of the play because Shaw has rigged the game. In the real world, Bentley suggests, the machine guns might fire, the cavalry might win, and the chocolate cream soldier might be shot as a spy. Pragmatism, in this reading, is a theatrical argument rather than a practical philosophy — convincing on stage, less so off it.

> "The chocolate cream soldier is Shaw's first sketch of the hero as rationalist — the man who thinks his way out of problems that feeling created. He will reappear, in various uniforms, throughout Shaw's work: as Tanner, as Undershaft, as Caesar. Each time, Shaw insists that the rational man is also the good man — a proposition that the twentieth century would test severely." — Michael Holroyd, *Bernard Shaw* (1988)

The tension between pragmatism and morality — can the efficient also be the good? — runs through all of Shaw's subsequent work. *Arms and the Man* answers optimistically, in the comic mode; *Major Barbara* and *Heartbreak House* will answer more darkly. The chocolate cream soldier is pragmatism at its most charming, but he is also pragmatism at its least tested.

## Further Reading

- Bernard Shaw, *The Quintessence of Ibsenism* (1891) — Shaw's early manifesto for theatrical realism and the "discussion play," written contemporaneously with *Arms and the Man*
- Eric Bentley, *The Playwright as Thinker* (1946) — the seminal study of Shaw as a dramatist of ideas; extensive treatment of pragmatism and anti-romanticism in the early plays
- Michael Holroyd, *Bernard Shaw* (1988–1992) — the definitive multi-volume biography; Volume 1 covers the genesis of *Arms and the Man* and Shaw's evolving philosophical commitments
- Nicholas Grene, *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View* (1984) — penetrating analysis of the limits and contradictions in Shaw's pragmatic ideal
- J. Ellen Gainor, *Shaw's Daughters: Dramatic and Narrative Constructions of Gender* (1991) — feminist reading of the gendered dynamics of pragmatism and romanticism in Shaw's work
- Margery M. Morgan, *The Shavian Playground* (1972) — explores the comic techniques Shaw uses to advance pragmatic arguments; detailed reading of *Arms and the Man*
- J. L. Wisenthal, *The Marriage of Contraries: Bernard Shaw's Middle Plays* (1974) — traces the pragmatism/romanticism dialectic from the early plays through the major middle-period works
- William Irvine, *The Universe of G.B.S.* (1949) — places Shaw's pragmatism in the context of nineteenth-century philosophical currents, particularly utilitarianism and Fabian socialism
