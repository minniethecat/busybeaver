# Inheritance

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s inheritance — the chain of hotels left to him by his father — is the play's final and most decisive material revelation. It transforms the penniless Swiss mercenary into a wealthy hotelier, validates [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic preference in the language of [[commerce]], and makes Shaw's argument about the economic foundations of [[marriage]].

## Dramatic Function

The inheritance revelation is the play's most significant structural event. Bluntschli has been presented throughout as a penniless professional soldier — competent, honest, but materially unimpressive. The revelation that he is in fact wealthy recontextualizes everything: his professional detachment was the confidence of a rich man's son, his calm in the face of danger was the equanimity of someone who always had a fallback.

## The Inventory as Proposal

Bluntschli's hotel inventory — "about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks"（003-1294–1296）— is the play's most concentrated comic scene and its most serious argument. The inventory replaces the love poem; the enumeration of assets replaces the declaration of passion. Raina accepts — not because she is mercenary but because the inventory is honest in a way that love poetry is not. The sheets and silverware are real in a way that "higher love" is not.

## Shaw's Argument

The inheritance makes explicit what the play has implied throughout: that marriage is an economic arrangement, that love operates within material constraints, and that acknowledging these facts is more honest — and ultimately more romantic — than pretending otherwise. The inheritance does not cheapen Bluntschli and Raina's relationship; it enables it.
