# Pragmatism (Definition)

Pragmatism in *Arms and the Man* is the disposition to address situations as they are rather than as romantic convention dictates they should be. It values professional competence over amateur gallantry, rational calculation over passionate impulse, and honest self-assessment over theatrical posturing. The chocolate that [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] carries instead of ammunition is its perfect emblem:

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.（001-395）

A [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] knows that food is more urgently needed than bullets — and that survival, not glory, is the soldier's first duty. This single reversal — chocolate for cartridges — establishes the pragmatic scale of values that will measure every character in the play.

Pragmatism in Shaw's sense is distinct from cynicism. Where cynicism disbelieves in all values, pragmatism tests values against consequences. Bluntschli does not mock [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s ideals; he simply notes that they do not match the facts. His famous taxonomy of soldiers makes the same point:

> MAN. There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before. Why, how is it that you've just beaten us? Sheer ignorance of the art of war, nothing else. I never saw anything so unprofessional.（001-440）

The pragmatist sees patterns — old soldiers carry grub, young ones carry cartridges — and acts on them. He is not braver or more virtuous than the romantic; he is simply better informed, and therefore more effective.

The pragmatic worldview operates on three levels: epistemological (a way of knowing based on empirical observation), ethical (a way of acting judged by results), and political (a redistribution of roles based on competence rather than birth). Each dimension finds its dramatic expression through [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s encounters with the Petkoff household.
