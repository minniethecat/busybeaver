# Raina & Sergius — The Higher Love

The romantic love between [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] is the play's central case study in romantic self-deception. Their engagement, celebrated by both families, is a relationship conducted at a distance — through photographs, letters, and heroic reputations — rather than through genuine knowledge of each other.

## The Photograph Romance

In Act I, Raina's love for Sergius is explicitly framed as idol-worship. She has only seen him in photographs (001-061). The stage direction indicates that "she takes it in her hands and elevates it like a priestess" (001-170 to 001-171), making the religious analogy explicit. Her affection is directed at the *idea* of the hero — the Byronic cavalry officer, the handsome patriot — not the actual man.

## The Higher Love Exposed

In Act II, Sergius declares the philosophy that defines their relationship:

> I think we two have found the higher love. When I think of you, I feel that I could never do a base deed, or think an ignoble thought. (002-462 to 002-464)

The speech is delivered with solemn self-regard, and Shaw immediately undercuts it through dramatic irony. Moments after this declaration, Sergius is flirting with [[louka|Louka]], the servant. The "higher love" is exposed as a rhetorical device — love as alibi, allowing Sergius to feel noble while acting selfishly.

## Louka's Devastating Critique

[[louka|Louka]] exposes the performative nature of Sergius's idealism:

> What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here? (002-509 to 002-511)

Her challenge reveals that romantic identity depends on an audience and collapses under scrutiny. The "half dozen Sergiuses" is a devastating admission: romantic selfhood is a role, not a unified self. Louka understands what Raina is only beginning to suspect — that "higher love" is a luxury available only to those whose material position allows them to ignore practical realities.

## The Collapse

By Act III, both Raina and Sergius have abandoned the fiction. Raina chooses the chocolate-cream soldier over the Byronic hero. Sergius, after his "higher love" collapses, turns to Louka with a grim pragmatism: the romantic ideal is replaced by a strategic match across class lines that neither partner romanticises.
