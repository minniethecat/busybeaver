# Photograph as Symbol

The photograph of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] on [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s chest of drawers — "a large photograph of an extremely handsome officer, whose lofty bearing and magnetic glance can be felt even through the portrait"（001-024）— is the play's most concentrated symbol of romantic idealism. It is an image of a man who does not exist — a Sergius composed of lighting, pose, and Raina's projections — and its systematic dismantling over the course of the play is Shaw's argument about the gap between image and reality.

## The Photograph as Icon

The photograph functions as an icon in the religious sense: an image through which the worshipper accesses the divine. Raina's opening rhapsody — "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）— is addressed to the photograph, not to Sergius. She is in love with an image, and the play's work is to replace the image with a person — first Bluntschli, then the real Sergius, who turns out to be far less impressive than his photograph.

## The Photograph and the Real

Shaw stages a series of encounters between the photograph and the real. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] recognizes the photograph immediately — "Why, that's the man who led the charge"（001-470）— and delivers his devastating professional assessment of the "hero." The photograph cannot answer; the image is silent before the professional's critique. Later, when the real Sergius appears, the audience has already been primed to see the gap between the image and the man.

## Symbolic Reading

The photograph is the visual equivalent of Raina's romantic education: a representation that replaces reality, an ideal that obscures the actual. The play's movement is from the photograph (Act I, worship of the image) to the hotel inventory (Act III, enumeration of material realities). The photograph, like the [[noble-attitude|noble attitude]], must be discarded for the real to emerge.
