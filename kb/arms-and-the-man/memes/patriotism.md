# Patriotism

The fervent national pride invoked by the characters of *Arms and the Man* — and systematically exposed by Shaw as a dangerous romantic illusion that enables war and masks self-interest.

## Definition

Patriotism in *Arms and the Man* is the belief in the moral superiority of one's nation and the glory of its military exploits. Shaw presents [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n patriotism as a discursive construction — a story the characters tell themselves to give meaning to violence. The play traces how patriotic rhetoric, initially thrilling, collapses under scrutiny once the reality of war intrudes.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The play opens in a fever of patriotism. [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] describes the cavalry charge with "surging enthusiasm": "our gallant splendid [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]ns with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche" (001-068 to 001-073). [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] echoes: "Our ideas of what [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] would do — our patriotism — our heroic ideals" (001-085). Yet even here, Shaw plants doubt: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] wonders whether "we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin" (001-095). Patriotism is already revealed as a literary construct.

### ACT II

[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] embodies the banality of patriotism in peacetime. He boasts of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n prowess but cannot manage his own household. The "fever of plucky patriotism which the Servian attack roused in all the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]ns" (002-099) is described retrospectively, already faded. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s return as a hero is undercut by his own admission that the cavalry charge succeeded only because the opposing side had the wrong ammunition.

### ACT III

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] delivers the play's most devastating anti-patriotic speech: "Oh, war! war! the dream of patriots and heroes! A fraud, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], a hollow sham, like love" (003-731 to 003-732). The conflation of patriotic and romantic illusion is complete. War is not glorious — it is ridiculous, a man burning alive in a timber fire because "our fellows' shells set the timber on fire and burnt him, with half a dozen other poor devils" (003-726 to 003-727).

## Shaw's Perspective

Shaw's critique of patriotism operates on two levels. First, he shows that patriotic fervor depends on ignorance of war's actual conditions — the cavalry charge succeeds through accident, not heroism. Second, he reveals that patriotic rhetoric serves class interests: officers invoke patriotism to justify their status while common soldiers do the dying. The play's ultimate verdict is delivered not by a [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n patriot but by [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], [[captain-bluntschli|the Swiss mercenary]] whose professionalism renders patriotic sentiment irrelevant.

- **Satirical Target**: The use of patriotic sentiment to glorify violence and maintain class hierarchy
- **Alternative**: Professional competence and rational self-interest over nationalistic passion
