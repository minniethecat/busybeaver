# Retreat

## Definition

**Retreat** in *Arms and the Man* is transformed by Shaw from a mark of military disgrace into a sign of practical intelligence. The play systematically inverts the conventional valuation of advance (heroic, glorious) and retreat (cowardly, shameful), arguing that the ability to retreat — to reassess, to change course, to survive — is a higher form of wisdom than the willingness to charge blindly forward [001-191].

## In the Play

Bluntschli's entire presence in the play is a consequence of retreat. He fled the battlefield after the cavalry charge, climbed a water pipe, and ended up in Raina's bedroom. His account of this retreat is delivered without shame: for a professional soldier, retreat is not cowardice but tactics. "The first duty of a soldier is to save his own skin," he might say — though Shaw gives him wittier formulations.

The cavalry charge that made Sergius famous is, in Bluntschli's account, the mirror image of retreat — an advance so reckless that it should have failed. That it succeeded makes it no less foolish; that retreat seems shameful makes it no less sensible. Shaw's ethical inversion is complete: the celebrated advance is folly, the despised retreat is wisdom.

Raina's emotional arc in the play is itself a retreat — from romantic idealism to pragmatic realism. She retreats from her engagement to Sergius, from her self-image as a romantic heroine, from the photograph on her wall. Each of these retreats is presented by Shaw as a step toward maturity, not a failure of character.

Sergius, by contrast, cannot retreat. His entire identity depends on the forward momentum of the romantic hero. When he is forced to confront the gap between his self-image and reality, he does not retreat and reassess — he doubles down, declaring a "higher love" that allows him to continue his romantic posturing while betraying its substance.

## Key Characters

- **Captain Bluntschli** — the play's philosopher of retreat; his professional acceptance of tactical withdrawal is the model of pragmatic intelligence [001-191]
- **Raina Petkoff** — her emotional retreat from romantic idealism is the play's central character arc
- **Sergius Saranoff** — incapable of retreat; his rigidity is both comic and pathetic

## Key Scenes

Act I: Bluntschli's account of his retreat from the battlefield establishes the play's counter-intuitive valuation of withdrawal [001-191].

Act III: Raina's retreat from her engagement to Sergius and her acceptance of Bluntschli's practical proposal complete her character arc.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's rehabilitation of retreat is consistent with his broader philosophical pragmatism. The ability to change course in the face of new evidence — to retreat from a position that has become untenable — is, for Shaw, the mark of the rational mind. The play's comic resolution depends on characters who can retreat and learn, while those who cannot (Sergius) are left stranded in their own rhetoric.
