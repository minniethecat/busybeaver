# Self-Deception Definition

Self-deception in the play is the gap between a character's self-conception and their actual behavior -- a gap maintained through performance, rationalization, and selective attention. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] believes she is a romantic heroine; in fact she shelters an enemy soldier, falls for his pragmatism, and has lied to everyone about it. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] believes he is a man of transcendent honor; in fact he flirts with a servant and admits his celebrated cavalry charge was suicidal folly. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] believes he is the master of his household; in fact he cannot locate his own coat. Even [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], the household's formidable matriarch, sustains the pretense of Viennese gentility while managing domestic crises with Balkan pragmatism（001-038）.

Shaw's insight is that self-deception is never merely personal. It is socially produced: Byron and Pushkin supply the scripts; opera at Bucharest provides the soundtrack; military tradition supplies the costumes. The characters are not liars in any simple sense -- they are performers who have forgotten they are on stage.
