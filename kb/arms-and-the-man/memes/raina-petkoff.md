Raina Petkoff, the 23-year-old daughter of the wealthiest family in a small [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n town, is the protagonist of *Arms and the Man*. She begins the play as a self-conscious romantic, worshipping her fiancé [[sergius-saranoff]] as an ideal hero and adoring opera-inspired notions of love and war. Her encounter with the pragmatic Swiss mercenary [[captain-bluntschli]] — the "chocolate cream soldier" — forces her to confront the gap between romantic fantasy and human reality, a transformation that drives the play's central critique of [[idealism]] and [[romanticism]].

- **剧中定位**：Protagonist（女主角），全剧围绕她的情感与认知转变展开
- **出场幕次**：ACT I / ACT II / ACT III（全程出场）
- **社会阶层**：[[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n aristocracy（保加利亚上层贵族）
- **关联人物**：[[sergius-saranoff]]（未婚夫）· [[captain-bluntschli]]（命运相遇者）· [[catherine-petkoff]]（母亲）· [[major-petkoff]]（父亲）· [[louka]]（女仆与对手）

Raina is introduced in the opening scene of ACT I, where the stage directions describe her bedchamber as decorated with "a little gallery of pictures and trophies" that reveals her romantic sensibility. Her relationship to each family member and servant maps the class structure of late-19th-century [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n society.

## 性格特征

Raina embodies the tension between [[romanticism|romantic idealism]] and [[realism|practical realism]] that Shaw uses to anatomize Victorian culture. Her character is defined by three competing impulses:

- **主导特质**：Romantic idealism — she lives through the lens of opera and poetry, staging her own emotions as theatrical performances (001-030).

> RAINA (_intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty are part of it_). (001-030)

- **性格矛盾**：Surface courage vs. inner vulnerability. She faces [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s pistol with composure, yet hides him behind her curtain in panic. She preaches noble ideals to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], yet lies effortlessly about the [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate cream soldier]] (001-566).

- **作者塑造意图**：Shaw uses Raina to demonstrate that romanticism is not innate but *learned* — a performance conditioned by literature, class privilege, and the safety of distance from real violence (001-096). Her transformation proves that honesty and pragmatism are harder but more sustainable virtues.

As the [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] encounter unfolds, Raina's self-awareness grows. Her declaration — "I ran away from home twice before I was twenty-one" (003-1082) — reframes her romanticism as restless energy rather than mere foolishness, marking a turning point in how both Bluntschli and the audience evaluate her.

## 关键情节

### ACT I: The Intruder and the Chocolate Cream Soldier

Raina's romantic world is shattered in the first act when [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]], a fleeing Serbian mercenary, climbs into her bedroom window (001-237). Initially terrified, she quickly regains her poise and hides him from [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n pursuers. When he reveals he carries chocolates instead of cartridges (001-396), she feeds him the last of her chocolate creams — the origin of the play's title metaphor:

> RAINA (_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_). Chocolate! Do you mean to tell me that you carry chocolate in your cartridge case instead of cartridges? (001-398)

> RAINA (_him almost maternally: he shakes his head_). Oh, you are a very poor soldier—a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture—remember that. (001-566)

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. (001-237)

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s frank admission punctures Raina's idealized image of soldiers. She lets him sleep in her bed while she takes her mother's room, keeping the secret from everyone except [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]].

### ACT II: The Return and the Photograph

Three months later, Raina has had time to reflect. When [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] returns to return [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s coat, Raina's reaction is a mixture of old romantic posturing and new self-awareness. She has inscribed a photograph of herself "To my chocolate cream soldier" (003-1099) — a gesture that is both mockingly affectionate and genuinely revealing. The scene exposes that her feelings for [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] have cooled while her fascination with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s honesty has grown.

### ACT III: The Unmasking and Resolution

The climax comes when [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] reveals Raina's photograph to the assembled company, exposing her private feelings (003-1099). Rather than collapse in romantic shame, Raina meets the moment with newfound directness. She acknowledges her affection for [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] and accepts his proposal — not because he is a romantic hero, but precisely because he is *not* one:

> RAINA (_succumbing with a shy smile_). To my chocolate cream soldier! (003-1202)

Her final line affirms that she has chosen reality over fantasy.

## 人物弧光

- **起点**（ACT I）：A romantic dreamer who views life through the lens of opera plots and Byron's poetry (001-096). She adores [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] as an abstract ideal and has never confronted the ugliness of real war.

- **转折点**：The midnight encounter with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]. His unheroic professionalism — carrying chocolate instead of ammunition, fleeing rather than fighting (001-237) — forces Raina to reconcile her ideals with human reality.

- **终点**（ACT III）：A young woman who has traded romantic illusion for honest affection. She chooses [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] — practical, blunt, utterly unromantic — over the dashing [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] (003-1202).

- **未变之处**：Her pride and theatrical flair remain intact. Even in her "realistic" final choice, she performs it with dramatic satisfaction. Shaw suggests that complete escape from romanticism may be impossible — the best we can do is recognize it as performance.

## 主题关联

- [[romanticism]] — Raina is the play's primary embodiment of romantic idealism. Her bedchamber decor, her worship of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], and her opera-inspired self-dramatization all mark her as Shaw's portrait of romantic culture (001-030).

- [[realism]] — Raina's journey from romantic to realist mirrors the play's argument. Her final embrace of [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] — a man who calculates survival odds, carries food instead of ammunition (001-396), and treats war as a profession — signals her conversion to Shaw's pragmatic philosophy.

- [[social-class]] — Raina's privilege enables her romanticism; only someone protected from war's real consequences can afford to aestheticize it. [[louka|Louka]], the servant, has no such luxury.

- [[higher-love]] — Raina's initial relationship with [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is the play's prime example of "higher love" — a spiritualized, desexualized romance that Shaw saw as a symptom of Victorian repression.

## 经典台词

> RAINA. I sent her away. I wanted to be alone. The stars are so beautiful! What is the matter? (001-097)

Raina's first line establishes her as a creature of aesthetic sensibility — even news of battle cannot compete with the beauty of the night sky.

> RAINA. Some soldiers, I know, are afraid of death. (001-470)

Delivered with "disdain" to the fugitive [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], this line captures Raina's pre-enlightenment romantic ignorance about the realities of combat.

> RAINA (_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_). Chocolate! Do you mean to tell me that you carry chocolate in your cartridge case instead of cartridges? (001-398)

The moment Raina's romantic fantasy of the noble enemy officer collides with the reality of a hungry, practical man who prefers sweets to ammunition.

> RAINA (_succumbing with a shy smile_). To my chocolate cream soldier! (003-1202)

Raina's final line — once a mocking epithet, now a term of genuine affection — marks her completed transformation.

## 批评解读

Raina is widely considered Shaw's most successful young female character — a portrait that balances satire with genuine sympathy. Unlike the purely satirical figures in Shaw's earlier plays, Raina's transformation feels earned and emotionally grounded.

Critics have noted that Raina's trajectory prefigures the "New Woman" of the 1890s: educated, self-aware, and capable of choosing a partner based on compatibility rather than social convention. Her final choice of the pragmatic Swiss mercenary over the aristocratic [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n officer is a quiet feminist victory disguised as romantic comedy.

Shaw himself described Raina as "a young lady who has been reading Byron and Pushkin, and who has got her ideas of life from them" — a diagnosis of how literature shapes (and distorts) romantic expectations.

> Raina is the first of Shaw's heroines who is allowed to grow rather than merely be exposed. Her transformation from romantic posturing to mature honesty gives *Arms and the Man* an emotional weight unusual in Shaw's early work. — Margery M. Morgan, *The Shavian Playground* (1972)
