# Uniform

## Definition

The **uniform** in *Arms and the Man* is a visual marker that Shaw repeatedly uses to question the relationship between appearance and identity. A uniform declares allegiance, rank, nationality, and class — all categories that the play systematically destabilises. Bluntschli, the most competent soldier in the play, is a Swiss mercenary in a Servian uniform; the Russian officer who searches the Petkoff house wears a Bulgarian uniform [001-633]. The uniform, Shaw suggests, tells you nothing reliable about the man inside it. It is the costume of war, and Shaw's project is to strip away the costume to reveal the unglamorous reality beneath.

## In the Play

Raina's first recognition of Bluntschli is through his uniform: "You recognise my uniform — Servian" [001-223]. The uniform is simultaneously a threat (Servian = enemy) and a protection (it identifies him as a soldier, not a brigand). The ambiguity is Shaw's point: a uniform is a social signal, not a moral fact. Bluntschli himself treats his uniform with characteristic pragmatism — it is a practical garment with useful pockets, not a symbol of honour.

The Russian officer who arrives searching for Bluntschli wears a Bulgarian uniform: "a young Russian officer, in Bulgarian uniform, enters, sword in hand" [001-633]. This doubling of national identities — a Russian wearing Bulgarian colours — further erodes the uniform's function as a reliable signifier. In Shaw's Balkan world, uniforms conceal as much as they reveal. The officer's uniform declares allegiance to Bulgaria, but his nationality is Russian; the uniform is, in effect, a lie made of fabric.

Raina's instinctive reaction to the Servian uniform — fear followed by fascinated sympathy — traces the play's larger movement from national prejudice to human recognition. When she sees past the uniform to the tired, hungry man, she performs the play's central ethical gesture. Shaw structures this as a progressive revelation: first the uniform (enemy), then the man (tired, professional), then the individual (Bluntschli, with his chocolates and his pragmatism).

Sergius's relationship to his uniform is theatrical rather than practical. Where Bluntschli treats his uniform as work-clothes, Sergius wears his as a costume — the outward sign of the heroic identity he performs. The contrast between the two men's relationship to their uniforms mirrors the play's larger contrast between professional competence and romantic posturing.

## Uniform and Social Class

The uniform is also a class marker. Officers' uniforms signify gentility as much as military rank — Sergius's uniform declares him a gentleman before it declares him a soldier. Bluntschli's uniform, by contrast, marks him as a mercenary — a professional rather than a patriot. The class distinction embedded in military uniform is one of Shaw's subtler critiques: the "gentleman officer" tradition that produced Sergius is contrasted unfavourably with the professional competence represented by Bluntschli.

Louka's social ambition is partly expressed through her relationship to military uniforms. Her attraction to Sergius is inseparable from what his uniform represents — social status, masculine authority, the world beyond servitude. When Louka declares "I have a feeling about you, sir" [003-2613], she is addressing the man as much as the uniform, but Shaw implies that the distinction is not one she can or wishes to make.

## Key Characters

- **Captain Bluntschli** — wears a Servian artillery officer's uniform; his identity as a Swiss professional mercenary complicates any simple reading of the uniform as a national marker. His pragmatic attitude toward uniform — pockets for chocolates, identification for safe passage — contrasts with Sergius's theatrical relationship to military regalia [001-223]
- **Sergius Saranoff** — wears his Bulgarian officer's uniform as a costume of heroism; his military identity is performative rather than professional, and the uniform is an essential prop in that performance
- **Raina Petkoff** — her reaction to Bluntschli's uniform moves from fear (Servian = enemy) to curiosity (man behind the uniform), tracing the play's central movement from romantic stereotype to human recognition [001-223]
- **The Russian Officer** — wears a Bulgarian uniform, demonstrating the fluidity — and arbitrariness — of military identity in the Balkan context [001-633]

## Key Scenes

Act I: Raina recognises Bluntschli's Servian uniform and must decide whether "the enemy" is a monster or a man. The scene is structured as a progressive stripping-away of the uniform's symbolic power — from threat to irrelevance [001-223].

Act I: The Russian officer in Bulgarian uniform searches the Petkoff house, a moment that highlights the arbitrary relationship between uniform and allegiance. The officer's uniform declares "Bulgarian ally" while his nationality is Russian — the uniform is literally a disguise [001-633].

Act II–III: Bluntschli's return in civilian clothes (or at least out of his Servian uniform) completes the symbolic arc. Once he is no longer visibly "the enemy," his pragmatic virtues — competence, honesty, practicality — become legible to the Bulgarian characters. The man without the uniform is the man who can be seen clearly.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw treats the uniform as a case study in the gap between appearance and reality — the same gap that structures his treatment of love, war, and class throughout the play. The uniform promises heroism and delivers a tired man who prefers chocolate to ammunition. It declares national identity but is worn by a Swiss mercenary. It signifies gentleman status but is valued by its wearer primarily for its pocket capacity.

The play's anti-romantic thesis is compressed into the uniform's dramatic function: strip away the costume, and you find not a monster or a hero but a professional doing a job. For Shaw, this demystification is not cynical but liberating — only by seeing past the uniform can the play's characters (and its audience) see the person.
