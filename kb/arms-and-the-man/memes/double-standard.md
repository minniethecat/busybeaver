# Double Standard

The sexual double standard — the Victorian code that permitted male infidelity while demanding female purity — is one of Shaw's most persistent targets in the play. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] embodies the double standard in its most refined form: his "higher love" philosophy allows him to be spiritually faithful to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] while physically pursuing [[louka|Louka]]. The philosophy is transparent self-justification, and Shaw exposes it as such.

## Sergius's Rationalization

Sergius's "higher love" theory — that his spiritual devotion to Raina is untouched by his physical attraction to Louka — is a philosophical version of the double standard. It permits him to have both the respectable fiancee and the servant mistress, the public virtue and the private pleasure. The theory is absurd, but its absurdity is Shaw's point: the double standard is inherently absurd, and the philosophies that justify it are rationalizations of appetite.

## Louka's Revenge

Louka's triumph — her insistence on marriage rather than seduction — is the double standard turned against its practitioner. The servant who refuses to be a mistress and demands to be a wife is the double standard's nemesis. Sergius, who believed he could have Louka on his terms, ends by having her on hers — and the Byronic seducer becomes the domesticated husband.

## Shaw's Feminist Argument

Shaw's exposure of the double standard is part of his broader feminist argument. The play treats male and female desire symmetrically: Raina's shift from Sergius to Bluntschli is no more condemned than Sergius's shift from Raina to Louka. Both characters are navigating romantic and sexual choices; both end the play with the partner they want. The double standard that would judge Raina more harshly than Sergius is, in Shaw's treatment, simply irrelevant.
