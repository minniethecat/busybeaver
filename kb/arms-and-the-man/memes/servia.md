# Servia

## Basic Information

**Servia** (modern Serbia) is the opposing nation in the Serbo-Bulgarian War of 1885, the historical conflict that forms the backdrop of *Arms and the Man*. Though never represented on stage by any Servian character other than Bluntschli (a Swiss mercenary fighting for Servia), Servia functions as a crucial dramatic offstage presence — the enemy against whom Bulgarian national identity and military honour are defined, and the entity whose soldiers turn out to be tired men who carry chocolates instead of ammunition.

## Stage Directions Description

Servia is not depicted through stage directions but constructed entirely through dialogue. The Bulgarian characters refer to the Servians with a mixture of contempt and anxiety that reveals more about Bulgarian insecurity than Servian reality.

> "A man of about 35, in a deplorable plight, bespattered with mud and blood and snow, his belt and the strap of his revolver case keeping together the torn ruins of the blue coat of a Servian artillery officer."（001-208–001-211）

This stage direction — the only visual "Servian" presence on stage — presents the enemy not as a monster but as a bedraggled, exhausted man in a ruined uniform. Shaw's descriptive choice is a rhetorical argument in itself: the Servian officer is defined by his suffering, not his menace.

Catherine Petkoff's opening news establishes the official Bulgarian view of the Servians as contemptible adversaries:

> "Can't you see it, Raina; our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched Servian dandies like chaff."（001-072–001-074）

The phrase "Servian dandies" — with its implication of effeminacy and triviality — is the first caricature the play constructs, and the one it most systematically dismantles. Within minutes of stage time, a real Servian officer enters and proves to be neither wretched nor a dandy, but a competent professional who regards the Bulgarian "heroes" with weary amusement.

## Dramatic Function

Servia serves multiple dramatic functions. It is the enemy that defines Bulgarian patriotism and military pride. Catherine's opening lines celebrate the Bulgarian victory as a proof of national superiority:

> "A great battle at Slivnitza! A victory! And it was won by Sergius."（001-056）

Yet the play immediately complicates this view — the most competent and likeable soldier in the play, Bluntschli, fought for Servia. The enemy turns out to be more admirable than the ally.

The Servian artillery officer's arrival in Raina's bedroom is the moment where the abstract enemy becomes a specific, tired, hungry human being:

> "Excuse my disturbing you; but you recognise my uniform — Servian. If I'm caught I shall be killed."（001-223–001-224）

Raina's shock is not at the danger but at the incongruity: "the enemy" is supposed to be a monster, not a man who carries chocolates instead of cartridges. This confrontation between stereotype and reality is the play's originating dramatic gesture.

Bluntschli later confesses his professional mercenary status:

> "I am not a native Servian. I am a Swiss, fighting merely as a professional soldier. I joined Servia because it was nearest to me."（001-355, 001-362）

This double displacement (Swiss man, Servian uniform) radicalises the anti-nationalist argument: the man inside the enemy uniform is not even of the enemy nation. Nationality itself is a costume.

Catherine's reaction to the peace treaty captures the Bulgarian jingoism that Shaw satirises:

> "You could have annexed Servia and made Prince Alexander Emperor of the Balkans. That's what I would have done."（002-153–002-154）

Major Petkoff's deflating reply — that such an action would require subduing "the whole Austrian Empire first" — is Shaw's anti-imperialist punchline. The private, chastened admission that follows dissolves the category of "enemy" into domestic routine:

> "The war's over. The treaty was signed three days ago at Bucharest; and the decree for our army to demobilize was issued yesterday."（002-136–002-138）

Catherine's reflexive outrage when Bluntschli is announced betrays the persistence of wartime sentiment even after peace:

> "A Servian! How dare he — Oh, I forgot. We are at peace now. I suppose we shall have them calling every day to pay their compliments."（002-735–002-736）

And yet the peace does not vanquish the old hatred:

> "We still hate the Servians: the only effect of the peace on my husband is to make him feel like a lion baulked of his prey."（002-803–002-804）

Shaw structures Servia's dramatic presence as a progressive demystification: Act I presents the Servian as monster; Act I's middle section reveals the monster as a man; Act II sees that man return as a friend; and Act III dissolves the very category of "enemy" into peaceful bureaucratic routine — with Bluntschli's inheritance distracting him from military affairs entirely.

## The Servian Soldier as Dramatic Device

Bluntschli is not Servian — he is Swiss. This is a crucial dramatic choice. Shaw could have made Bluntschli a Servian officer, which would have made a simpler point about seeing past national prejudice. Instead, he makes Bluntschli a mercenary — a professional who fights for Servia not out of patriotism but out of professional obligation:

> "There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before."（001-440–001-441）

The Swiss identity compounds the anti-nationalist argument. When Petkoff asks "A Swiss? What was he doing in the Servian army?"（002-361）, the answer — that nationality is irrelevant to professional soldiering — undermines the entire premise of patriotic war. Bluntschli fights for whoever is nearest; the Bulgarian officers fight for Bulgaria. Both are equally professional, but only one admits it.

Even Louka, the servant, registers the distance between Servian identity and personal feeling: when Bluntschli receives news of his father's death, she is "determined not to be civil to a Servian, even if she must bring him his letters"（003-383）, yet immediately concedes that "He has not much heart, that Swiss, though he is so fond of the Servians"（003-410–003-411）. The conflation of national and personal attributes is exposed as a category error.

## Historical-Geographical Background

The Serbo-Bulgarian War of 1885 was a brief conflict triggered by Bulgaria's unification with Eastern Rumelia. Serbia, fearing Bulgarian expansion and backed by Austria-Hungary, invaded Bulgarian territory in November 1885. The Serbian army, though larger, was decisively defeated at the Battle of Slivnitza (17–19 November 1885). The war lasted only two weeks before Austrian intervention forced a ceasefire and status quo peace.

Shaw uses this almost farcically brief conflict — a war that was over before the participants fully understood it had begun — to satirise the pretensions of military glory. Sergius's great cavalry charge, the foundation of his heroic reputation, occurred in a two-week war that changed nothing:

> "It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane: first one comes; then two or three close behind him; and then all the rest in a lump."（001-461–001-463）

Servia in 1885 was a kingdom recently independent from Ottoman suzerainty (formally recognised in 1878 at the Congress of Berlin). Like Bulgaria, it was a young nation aggressively asserting its place among European powers. The rivalry between the two states was partly genuine, partly a function of great-power manipulation — a complexity that Shaw captures in the play's treatment of the Russian officer in Bulgarian uniform and the Austrian influence on Servian military policy:

> "No, you are one of the Austrians who set the Servians on to rob us of our national liberty."（001-357）

Catherine's accusation (wrong in Bluntschli's case but accurate about Austrian involvement) points to the realpolitik that Shaw saw beneath nationalist rhetoric. The great powers — Russia, Austria-Hungary — used Balkan client states as proxies, and the "national hatred" between Bulgarians and Servians was partly manufactured by imperial patrons.

## Critical Perspectives

**Bernard F. Dukore** (1973) argued that Shaw's treatment of the "enemy" in *Arms and the Man* constitutes a systematically anti-jingoistic dramaturgy. By making Bluntschli a Swiss mercenary rather than a Servian patriot, Shaw denies the audience even the sentimental satisfaction of seeing a "good enemy" — the category of enemy itself is drained of meaning, replaced by the category of professional. The dramatic arc from Catherine's "wretched Servian dandies"（001-074）to Petkoff's matter-of-fact "our new Servian friends"（002-871）is Shaw's argument that national enmity is a temporary emotional state, not a permanent condition.

**Michael Holroyd** (1988) observed that Shaw's choice of the Serbo-Bulgarian War as a setting was pointed: it was a minor conflict almost comically disproportionate to the rhetoric it generated, and one in which the "victorious" Bulgarians were largely equipped and officered by Russia, while the "defeated" Servians were backed by Austria-Hungary. The war was a proxy conflict between empires, and Shaw's play treats it as such — Sergius's heroism is as much a Russian production as his uniform.

**Katherine E. Kelly** (1998) situated Shaw's treatment of Balkan nationalism within the context of late-Victorian British attitudes toward Eastern Europe. Shaw was unusual among British playwrights in presenting a Balkan conflict without orientalist condescension — the Bulgarian characters are satirised for their pretensions, but they are satirised as human beings, not as exotic others. Servia, as an offstage presence, escapes even this satire: it remains a blank screen onto which Bulgarian characters project their anxieties, much as the British public projected onto the Balkans.

**Nicholas Grene** (1984) noted that the play's treatment of Servia exemplifies Shaw's technique of "inversion": the enemy nation that the Bulgarian characters despise turns out to possess the qualities they lack (professional competence, realism, freedom from romantic illusion). The structure is emblematic of Shavian argument in general — the thesis (Servia as inferior enemy) is stated, then tested against evidence (Bluntschli, the peace treaty, the coat), and found wanting.
