# Betrayal

Betrayal is the play's most pervasive interpersonal dynamic, operating at multiple levels from the romantic to the professional to the national. Shaw's treatment of betrayal is characteristically revisionist: the play asks not whether betrayal occurs but whether the betrayed commitment was worth keeping in the first place.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The first act establishes the conditions for betrayal without depicting it. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s passionate declarations of loyalty to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] — "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）— are made moments before a more interesting man climbs through her window. The act closes with Raina having concealed an enemy soldier from her own countrymen, an act that is simultaneously treason (she aids the enemy), betrayal (of her engagement ideals), and compassion (she feeds a hungry man). Shaw refuses to let any single moral frame dominate.

### ACT II

Betrayal blossoms into open comedy. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], the supposed embodiment of higher love, is discovered making advances to [[louka|Louka]], the servant. His self-justification — that his "higher love" for Raina is a spiritual ideal untouched by his physical attraction to Louka — is Shaw's parody of the double standard that allowed Victorian gentlemen to maintain spotless public reputations while pursuing private conquests.

Simultaneously, Raina's emotional betrayal of Sergius deepens. Her perverse wish that Sergius "find out about the chocolate cream soldier"（002-719）is a desire to destroy the engagement through indirection, to be released from her commitment without having to formally break it.

### ACT III

The betrayals are resolved through reconfiguration rather than punishment. Sergius's pursuit of Louka is consummated in an engagement — a betrayal of Raina that turns out to be a solution for everyone. Raina's shift to Bluntschli is similarly reframed: what would in a conventional comedy be female inconstancy becomes, in Shaw's hands, the triumph of honest feeling over romantic pretense.

## Key Scene

> SERGIUS. I have been false to you in thought and deed — sincerely false — unfaithfully yours, Sergius.（002-837–838）

Sergius's confession — "sincerely false" — captures the play's paradoxical treatment of betrayal. He is simultaneously guilty (he has been false) and honest (he admits it). The oxymoron "sincerely false" dissolves the binary between fidelity and betrayal, replacing it with a more complex spectrum of human behavior.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of betrayal reflects his broader argument against the absolutes of romantic convention. Fidelity, in the Victorian marriage plot, is an unqualified good; infidelity, an unqualified evil. Shaw substitutes a more pragmatic calculus: some commitments deserve to be broken, and the honesty with which they are broken may be more admirable than the hypocrisy with which they are maintained.

- **Target of satire**: The Victorian ideal of absolute romantic fidelity, enforced by social pressure and maintained by hypocrisy
- **Alternative proposed**: A more flexible, honest approach to romantic commitments, in which ending a relationship that no longer serves both parties is not a moral failure but a practical necessity
