# Disillusion

Disillusion is the primary dramatic engine of *Arms and the Man*. Each major character undergoes a confrontation between their [[romanticism|romantic]] self-image and a more complex, often disappointing reality. Shaw uses disillusion not as tragedy but as liberation — the shedding of [[self-deception|false consciousness]] is the precondition for authentic life.

## Raina's Awakening

Raina begins the play immersed in romantic fantasies of war and [[honor|heroism]]. Her first moment of doubt comes early, when she acknowledges the possibility that heroic ideals might not survive contact with reality:

> [She feared] perhaps disillusion or humiliation or failure [001-089]

The arrival of [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] — exhausted, hungry, carrying chocolates instead of cartridges — initiates Raina's gradual disillusion. By the play's end, she has exchanged her romantic [[idealism]] for a clear-eyed acceptance of Bluntschli's pragmatic worldview.

## Sergius and "Petty Disillusions"

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] experiences disillusion not as a single revelation but as an accumulation of indignities. His [[chivalry|chivalric]] self-image is eroded by:

> the petty disillusions which every hour spent among the lower orders of the army brings [002-254]

Unlike Raina, Sergius resists full disillusion. He trades one illusion (military glory) for another (romantic love with Louka), suggesting that the capacity for [[self-deception]] outlasts any single disappointment.

## The Anti-Illusionist

Bluntschli is described as "an accurate calculator who has no illusions" [002-020]. He represents the endpoint of disillusion — a [[professional-soldiering|professional]] who sees the world as it is rather than as he wishes it to be. His immunity to illusion makes him the play's moral centre and the catalyst for Raina's transformation.
