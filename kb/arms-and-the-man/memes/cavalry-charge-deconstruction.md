# The Cavalry Charge: Deconstructing Battlefield Courage

Shaw's dissection of the cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] is the most concentrated attack on martial heroism in English-language drama. The charge is described three times, from three perspectives, each revealing a different layer of the illusion.

## Catherine's Version: The Romance

[[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]'s narration in Act I is pure epic romance （001-067 to 001-074）:

> CATHERINE. A cavalry charge — think of that! He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns. （001-069 to 001-072）

She describes "our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched Servian dandies like chaff." It is the language of epic poetry — Homer translated into Victorian patriotic rhetoric — and it is entirely false.

This version establishes the romantic template that the play will spend three acts dismantling: individual gallantry against impossible odds, the hero's will triumphing over tactical reality, the charge as proof that the romantic worldview is "real after all" （001-080）.

## Bluntschli's Version: The Clinical Deconstruction

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s eyewitness account （001-448 to 001-502） is the demolition. It operates on four levels:

**Tactical**: The charge was professional suicide. "Is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire?" （001-448 to 001-451）. The question is rhetorical; the answer, for any competent soldier, is no.

**Visual**: The charge looked ridiculous, not heroic. "It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane: first one comes; then two or three close behind him; and then all the rest in a lump" （001-461 to 001-463）. The simile is deliberately bathetic — peas against glass — reducing epic cavalry to a domestic accident.

**Mechanical**: The leader was not charging but being carried. "The poor devil pulling at his horse" because "it's running away with him, of course: do you suppose the fellow wants to get there before the others and be killed?" （001-468 to 001-475）. The supreme image of military courage — the leader of the charge — is revealed as a man whose horse has bolted. The "bravest of the brave" （001-466） is an accident of equine panic.

**Causal**: The victory was luck, not heroism. "He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all" （001-501 to 001-502）. The charge "succeeded" only because the Servian defenders had been sent the wrong cartridges — a supply-chain error, not a triumph of will. The entire heroic narrative rests on a logistics mistake.

Bluntschli then identifies the rider: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who "did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills" （001-489 to 001-491）.

## The Don Quixote Allusion

The [[don-quixote|Cervantes]] comparison is the interpretative key to Shaw's method. Don Quixote is not a coward — he is a man of genuine courage whose courage happens to be directed at windmills he mistakes for giants. Similarly, Sergius is not a fraud — his bravery in the charge was physiologically real and subjectively authentic. The problem is not the authenticity of the courage but its object: what Sergius was brave *about* was an illusion, a romantic fantasy with no relationship to tactical reality.

The play asks a devastating question: is courage still courage when it is directed at a phantom? And its answer is layered: yes, subjectively it is real — "in the charge I found that I was brave. Yes: that at least is real about me" （003-543 to 003-544） — but objectively it is indistinguishable from stupidity. A society that celebrates this courage without distinguishing its object is a society that will get its young men killed.

## Sergius's Version: The Bitter Self-Knowledge

Sergius's own account （002-283 to 002-289; 003-542 to 003-558） is the most complex of the three because it contains both self-affirmation and self-condemnation.

He knows the charge was strategically absurd: "I won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way" （002-288 to 002-289）. The line is rich with irony: "the right way" to lose, "the wrong way" to win — Shaw collapses the distinction between success and failure, showing that the military categories themselves are incoherent.

Yet Sergius also knows that, in the moment, he felt genuinely brave: "in the charge I found that I was brave. Yes: that at least is real about me" （003-543 to 003-544）. The authenticity of the felt experience coexists with the absurdity of the act. This is Shaw's most subtle point: battlefield courage *is* real as a subjective experience, but it is worthless — worse than worthless, it is dangerous — as a social value. The private truth does not justify the public lie.

By Act II, Sergius has resigned his commission and now articulates a cynical view of soldiering that matches Bluntschli's professional doctrine but reaches it through moral revulsion rather than pragmatic acceptance: "Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak" （002-339 to 002-341）.

## The Method: Comic Deconstruction

Shaw's technique is distinctive and strategically effective. He does not denounce military courage so much as make it look ridiculous. The chocolate cream soldier is a funnier, and therefore more effective, critique than a sermon could ever be. The deconstruction works through comic reversals:

- The cartridge box that should contain ammunition contains chocolate
- The "bravest of the brave" is being carried by a runaway horse
- The heroic charge succeeds because of a supply-chain error
- The victor won "the wrong way"
- The celebrated hero resigns in disgust

Each reversal takes a romantic expectation and flips it to comic effect. The cumulative impact is devastating: by the end of Act I, the audience can no longer hear the word "charge" without seeing peas hitting a window pane.

## Broader Implications

The deconstruction of the cavalry charge is not merely a local joke about one battle. It is Shaw's assault on the entire edifice of martial heroism — the ideology that valorizes battlefield courage as the highest human virtue and that was a cornerstone of Victorian patriotic culture. If the supreme image of military courage — the officer leading the charge — can be reduced to a man on a runaway horse facing machine guns that mercifully jam, then the entire vocabulary of martial honor collapses.

Shaw's argument, delivered through this deconstruction, is that the very category of "battlefield courage" is incoherent when examined closely. What looks like bravery is usually a combination of luck, animal panic, and institutional incompetence dressed in epaulettes. Celebrating it is not just dishonest — it is lethal.
