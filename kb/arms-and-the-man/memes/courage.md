# Courage

Shaw's radical interrogation of what it means to be brave. *Arms and the Man* proposes that true courage has little to do with the battlefield — and everything to do with defying social convention. Across three acts, Shaw dismantles every conventional notion of bravery: the cavalry charge is exposed as suicidal folly, the "hero" discovers that his celebrated courage is indistinguishable from animal aggression, and the characters who prove truly brave are not soldiers but servants — [[louka|Louka]] who refuses her assigned class position and [[nicola|Nicola]] who endures contempt in pursuit of a long-term strategy. By the final curtain, the play has relocated courage from the battlefield to the soul, from the officer's epaulettes to the servant's quiet defiance of a system that would keep her servile.

## Definition

In *Arms and the Man*, courage is a contested concept — perhaps the most thoroughly contested idea in the play. The romantic view, courage as gallantry in battle, the cavalry charge against impossible odds, is embodied by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]]. Against this, Shaw juxtaposes three alternative courages:

1. **Professional courage**: [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s competence and clear-eyed self-preservation. He carries chocolate instead of cartridges because he is "a [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]]" who understands that staying alive is a soldier's first duty: "It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can" （001-236 to 001-238）.

2. **Social courage**: [[louka|Louka]]'s refusal to accept the servile philosophy of [[nicola|Nicola]], her insistence on her own dignity and ambition against a class system that punishes such aspirations. Her courage is the courage to want more — and to act on that wanting.

3. **Strategic courage**: [[nicola|Nicola]]'s patience, his willingness to endure contempt and bide his time, saving money and secrets for a future shop in Sofia. It is a compromised, unglamorous courage — but the play does not dismiss it.

The question the play poses, through each of these characters, is not "Who is brave?" but "What *kind* of bravery matters?"

## In the Play

### ACT I

The romantic conception of courage is established through the absent [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], whose cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] is narrated by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] as the supreme act of bravery: "He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns" （001-069 to 001-072）. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s ecstatic response — "It proves that all our ideas were real after all" （001-080） — establishes courage as the linchpin of the romantic worldview: if men can be heroes, then Byron and Pushkin and Verdi have not lied.

But [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s entrance immediately destabilizes this frame. A professional soldier who prefers food to ammunition, he climbs a drainpipe to save his life and, when accused of cowardice, responds with the play's first redefinition of the term:

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. （001-236 to 001-238）

This is not cowardice but professional doctrine: the soldier's job is to survive and be effective. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] challenges him — "I think I am at heart as brave as you" （001-421） — he does not dispute it. Instead, he explains the physiological reality of courage under fire: "I can stand two days without shewing it much; but no man can stand three days: I'm as nervous as a mouse" （001-424 to 001-425）. Courage, in Bluntschli's account, is not a fixed character trait but a depletable resource — a radically modern, almost clinical view that the play endorses.

The Act culminates in [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s deconstruction of the cavalry charge itself. Asked to describe it, he delivers what is effectively the play's thesis statement on battlefield courage:

> MAN. Well, it's a funny sight. It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane: first one comes; then two or three close behind him; and then all the rest in a lump. （001-461 to 001-463）

The "first One — the bravest of the brave!" （001-466） of [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s imagination is, in Bluntschli's prosaic account, "the poor devil pulling at his horse" because "it's running away with him, of course: do you suppose the fellow wants to get there before the others and be killed?" （001-468 to 001-475）. This is the play's most devastating comic reversal: the supreme image of military courage — the leader of the charge — is revealed as a man whose horse has bolted.

Bluntschli then identifies the rider: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who "did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills" （001-489 to 001-491）. The Cervantes allusion is precise: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], like Quixote, is not a coward but a man whose courage is misdirected at phantoms. The charge succeeded only because the Servian machine guns had the wrong ammunition — "the pistol missed fire, that's all" （001-502）. Shaw's point is not that Sergius is a coward; it is that the very category of "battlefield courage" is incoherent when examined closely. What looks like bravery is usually luck, animal panic, or institutional incompetence dressed in epaulettes.

> MAN. Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all. （001-500 to 001-502）

### ACT II

The contrast between battlefield courage and social courage develops. Four months after the war, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] has resigned his commission, having discovered that his celebrated heroism was strategically worthless: "I won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way" （002-288 to 002-289）. His verdict on the military profession is now more cynical than Bluntschli's:

> SERGIUS. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak. That is the whole secret of successful fighting. Get your enemy at a disadvantage; and never, on any account, fight him on equal terms. （002-339 to 002-343）

This is, ironically, the same professional doctrine Bluntschli articulated in Act I — but where Bluntschli accepted it as a neutral fact of his trade, Sergius finds it morally repugnant. His romantic soul cannot reconcile itself to soldiering-as-business; he would rather resign than be "a tradesman" （002-348）. The irony is double: Sergius's resignation is itself a form of moral courage, a refusal to participate in a system he now sees as fraudulent. Yet Shaw does not let him keep this dignity for long.

Meanwhile, the Act introduces the play's second theatre of courage: the servants' quarters. [[louka|Louka]] defies [[nicola|Nicola]]'s philosophy of servile prudence — "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola" （002-079） — insisting on her right to self-determination. Her willingness to keep family secrets as leverage, to talk back to her social superiors, and to risk her position for her ambition demonstrates a courage that has nothing to do with weapons. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s reply — "Yes: that's the secret of success in service" （002-081） — represents the alternative: courage as endurance, as the long game, as the willingness to be thought a coward while you accumulate power.

The Act thus sets up the two competing models of social courage — Louka's defiance and Nicola's patience — that will collide in Act III.

### ACT III

The play's most sustained and explicit analysis of courage occurs in the [[louka|Louka]]-[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] exchange in the library of the [[petkoff-house]]. [[louka|Louka]] asks the question the play has been circling since Act I: "I wonder are you really a brave man?" （003-540）. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s answer is the longest speech on courage in the play, and it moves through three distinct positions:

First, he affirms his battlefield bravery as fact: "Yes: I am a brave man. My heart jumped like a woman's at the first shot; but in the charge I found that I was brave. Yes: that at least is real about me" （003-542 to 003-544）. But [[louka|Louka]] immediately undercuts this by introducing class: "Did you find in the charge that the men whose fathers are poor like mine were any less brave than the men who are rich like you?" （003-546 to 003-547）.

This question transforms the speech. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] admits that all the soldiers — rich and poor — "slashed and cursed and yelled like heroes" （003-549）. But then he pivots to the play's key distinction:

> SERGIUS. Psha! the courage to rage and kill is cheap. I have an English bull terrier who has as much of that sort of courage as the whole Bulgarian nation, and the whole Russian nation at its back. But he lets my groom thrash him, all the same. That's your soldier all over! No, Louka, your poor men can cut throats; but they are afraid of their officers; they put up with insults and blows; they stand by and see one another punished like children — aye, and help to do it when they are ordered. And the officers! — well, I am an officer. （003-550 to 003-558）

This is the turning point. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] has arrived, through his own bitter experience, at Shaw's position: the courage that society celebrates — the willingness to kill and die — is the cheapest form, available even to animals. True courage lies elsewhere:

> SERGIUS. Oh, give me the man who will defy to the death any power on earth or in heaven that sets itself up against his own will and conscience: he alone is the brave man. （003-558 to 003-560）

The definition is magnificent, and it would be the play's last word on courage — except that [[louka|Louka]] immediately turns it back on him. If courage means defying power, then she — the servant who refuses to be a servant — is the truly brave one, not the major who can resign his commission with his class status intact:

> LOUKA. How easy it is to talk! Men never seem to me to grow up: they all have schoolboy's ideas. You don't know what true courage is. （003-562 to 003-563）

She then delivers her own definition by example: "if I were Empress of Russia, above everyone in the world, then — ah, then, though according to you I could shew no courage at all; you should see, you should see" （003-570 to 003-572）. And she names the specific act of courage she has in mind: "I would marry the man I loved, which no other queen in Europe has the courage to do" （003-576 to 003-577）. The personal is the political: the courage to follow individual desire against social constraint is, in Shaw's accounting, the highest courage of all.

Earlier in the Act, [[louka|Louka]] had told [[nicola|Nicola]]: "You take all the courage out of me with your cold-blooded wisdom" （003-498）. The line is revealing: [[nicola|Nicola]]'s patient, calculating approach to social mobility is, from Louka's perspective, anti-courageous — it drains the defiance out of rebellion and replaces it with bookkeeping. Yet Shaw does not simply endorse Louka over Nicola. Nicola's philosophy — endure now, prosper later — is a legitimate strategy for the powerless, and when he tells Louka "I'll stand by you: one servant should always stand by another" （003-494）, he reveals a solidarity that is its own kind of moral courage.

The Act resolves with the triumph of Louka's approach: she wins [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] by sheer force of will, defying every convention of class and gender. And in the final scene, when [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] challenges [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] to a duel and Bluntschli refuses — "I refuse to fight you" （003-761） — it is Bluntschli's professional pragmatism, not Sergius's romantic honor, that the play endorses as the truly courageous position. Refusing to fight takes more courage than accepting, when the refusal invites the charge of cowardice.

## The Cavalry Charge: Deconstructing Battlefield Courage

Shaw's dissection of the cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] is the most concentrated attack on martial heroism in English-language drama. The charge is described three times, from three perspectives, each revealing a different layer of the illusion:

**Catherine's version** （001-067 to 001-074） is pure romance: "our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched Servian dandies like chaff." It is the language of epic poetry, and it is entirely false.

**Bluntschli's version** （001-448 to 001-502） is clinical deconstruction. The charge was tactically idiotic: "is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire?" （001-448 to 001-451）. The leader was not charging but being carried by a runaway horse: "do you suppose the fellow wants to get there before the others and be killed?" （001-474 to 001-475）. The "heroism" was indistinguishable from suicide — "he and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all" （001-501 to 001-502）. And the whole event succeeded only because of a logistical error: the Servians had been sent the wrong cartridges.

**Sergius's version** （002-283 to 002-289; 003-542 to 003-558） is the most complex. He knows the charge was strategically absurd — "I won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way" （002-288 to 002-289） — yet he also knows that, in the moment, he felt genuinely brave: "in the charge I found that I was brave. Yes: that at least is real about me" （003-543 to 003-544）. The authenticity of the felt experience coexists with the absurdity of the act. Shaw's point is subtle: battlefield courage *is* real as a subjective experience, but it is worthless — worse than worthless, it is dangerous — as a social value. Celebrating it leads young men to charge machine guns.

The Don Quixote comparison is the interpretative key. Cervantes's hero is not a coward; he is a man of genuine courage who happens to be fighting windmills. Similarly, Sergius is not a fraud — his bravery in the charge was physiologically real — but the object of his bravery was an illusion, a romantic construct with no relationship to strategic reality. The play asks: is courage still courage when it is directed at a phantom? And answers: yes, but it is also stupidity, and a society that confuses the two will get its young men killed.

## Shaw's Perspective

Shaw's treatment of courage is class-conscious and radically democratic. The play argues that what society calls "courage" — the willingness to kill and die in battle — is actually the cheapest, most animal form of bravery, available to "an English bull terrier" （003-551） as much as to any man. The word "cheap" in Sergius's speech is precise: battlefield courage is a low-value currency, inflated by patriotic rhetoric but worth very little when examined.

Far more demanding — and far more rare — is the courage to resist social pressure:

- [[louka|Louka]]'s courage to refuse servitude, to insist on her own desire and ambition against a class system designed to crush both.
- [[nicola|Nicola]]'s courage to pursue a long-term plan despite being thought a coward and a toady by those who do not understand his strategy.
- [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s courage to be professionally honest rather than romantically gallant — to admit fear, to carry chocolate, to refuse a duel.
- Even [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s courage to lie to the Russian officer while hiding a fugitive behind her curtain — "she winks and gasps, but stands her ground" （001-327） — is a form of bravery that the play quietly honors.

**Satirical Target**: The glorification of military courage as the highest human virtue — a cornerstone of Victorian and patriotic ideology that Shaw saw as lethal nonsense.

**Alternative**: Moral courage — the willingness to defy authority, convention, and social expectation in the service of one's own will and conscience. This is Sergius's own definition (003-558 to 003-560), but it is Louka who lives it.

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's redefinition of courage in *Arms and the Man* participates in a broader late-Victorian critique of martial values that includes Hardy's *The Dynasts* and Kipling's later, more ambivalent war poetry. But Shaw's treatment is distinctive for its comic method: he does not denounce military courage so much as make it look ridiculous. The chocolate cream soldier is a funnier, and therefore more effective, critique than a sermon could ever be.

Feminist critics have noted that the play's relocation of courage from the battlefield to the domestic sphere — from Sergius's cavalry charge to Louka's romantic defiance — effectively feminizes the virtue. The truly brave acts in the play are performed by women (Raina hiding Bluntschli, Louka defying her class position) or by a man who rejects masculine codes of honor (Bluntschli refusing the duel). The "hero" — Sergius — is exposed as a fraud not because he lacks physical courage but because he lacks the moral courage to live by his own stated principles.

The play also anticipates Shaw's later, more explicit engagement with the Nietzschean critique of slave morality. Sergius's speech about the bull terrier — creatures who will fight to the death but submit to the groom's thrashing — is effectively a Shavian parable about the difference between the courage of the predator and the courage of the rebel. The soldier has the first; only Louka has the second.

> In *Arms and the Man*, Shaw performs a kind of ethical judo: he takes the nineteenth century's most cherished virtue — martial courage — and flips it, revealing it as the virtue of the slave, not the master. The bravest person in the play is the servant girl who wants a better life and refuses to apologize for wanting it. — Adapted from Eric Bentley, *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration* (1957)
