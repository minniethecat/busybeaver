# Treaty of Berlin (1878)

The Treaty of Berlin, signed on July 13, 1878, was the diplomatic settlement that created the political [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] in which *Arms and the Man* takes place. Without the Treaty — and the complex of Great Power rivalries it represented — there would have been no Bulgarian state, no Bulgarian army, no cavalry charge at Slivnitza, and no Raina Petkoff to romanticize it.

## Historical Background

The treaty was the outcome of the Congress of Berlin, convened by the Great Powers (Britain, France, Germany, Austria-Hungary, Russia, Italy, and the [[ottoman-empire|Ottoman Empire]]) to revise the earlier Treaty of San Stefano, which had concluded the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878. The San Stefano treaty had created a "Greater Bulgaria" that would have dominated the [[balkans|Balkans]] — a prospect alarming to Austria-Hungary and Britain, who feared Russian expansion.

The Berlin treaty dismantled Greater Bulgaria. The territory was divided into three parts: an autonomous Bulgarian principality (roughly the northern half of modern Bulgaria), the autonomous province of Eastern Rumelia (south of the Balkan Mountains, nominally under Ottoman sovereignty), and Macedonia (returned to direct Ottoman rule). This partition was deeply resented by Bulgarian nationalists and set the stage for the [[bulgarian-unification|Bulgarian unification of 1885]].

## Significance for the Play

The play's immediate context — the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War of 1885]] — was a direct consequence of the Berlin settlement. When Bulgaria annexed Eastern Rumelia in September 1885, it was undoing the Treaty of Berlin. [[servia|Servia]]'s declaration of war was, in part, a response to this violation of the treaty order. The war that brings [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] to Raina's bedchamber was, at its deepest level, caused by a treaty signed seven years earlier in a German capital.

## Shaw's Use of History

Shaw's choice of 1885 Bulgaria as his setting was historically literate. The Serbo-Bulgarian War was a perfect vehicle for his anti-romantic thesis about [[war]]: a brief, bloody conflict triggered by treaty violations and Great Power rivalries, celebrated by its participants in the language of [[patriotism]] and [[honor]], and understood by Shaw as a case study in the machinery of romantic militarism.

## Key Scene

Shaw does not mention the Treaty of Berlin in the dialogue, but its effects are everywhere. The Bulgarian army [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] serves in, the Russian cultural influence visible in [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s reading, the very existence of the Petkoff household as a site of Bulgarian bourgeois life — all were shaped by the treaty settlement.
