# Bluntschli's Pragmatic Philosophy

[[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] embodies pragmatism not merely as a personality trait but as a coherent philosophical outlook with three interlocking components.

## Swiss Neutrality as Philosophical Identity

Bluntschli's Swiss identity is not merely biographical detail — it is the geopolitical expression of his entire worldview. His declaration to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], "I am only a Swiss, fighting merely as a professional soldier. I joined Servia because it was nearest to me" (001-361), disclaims nationality, ideology, and crusading sentiment in a single sentence. Switzerland, for Shaw, represents a kind of philosophical neutrality: the ability to observe without partisan distortion, to judge by results rather than loyalties. The play's running joke — "That's the Swiss national character, dear lady" (003-372) — packages pragmatism as national identity: Swiss neutrality, Swiss professionalism, Swiss chocolate.

## Professional Competence as Moral Program

Bluntschli's professionalism is the practical expression of his philosophy. He judges the cavalry charge not by its courage but by its tactical outcome: "is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire?" (001-448). The professional does what works; the amateur does what feels noble. Bluntschli's fourteen years of service have taught him that competence — knowing when to fight and when to run — is the soldier's true virtue, and that the honor code that demands suicidal charges is not honorable but incompetent.

His soldier taxonomy — "There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones" (001-440) — is pragmatic epistemology in action: observation yields pattern, pattern yields principle. Soldiers are not heroes or cowards; they are experienced or inexperienced. The professional learns from experience; the amateur repeats the same mistakes.

## The Incurably Romantic Disposition

The deepest layer of Bluntschli's philosophy is his self-knowledge. In the climactic scene, when he attempts to dissuade [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] from marrying him, he confesses:

> BLUNTSCHLI. And I, a common-place Swiss soldier who hardly knows what a decent life is after fifteen years of barracks and battles—a vagabond—a man who has spoiled all his chances in life through an incurably romantic disposition—（003-1073）

The confession of "an incurably romantic disposition" is the play's deepest pragmatic insight: the pragmatist knows his own irrationality. Self-knowledge, not the absence of passion, is what distinguishes [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] from [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]. Both men are romantics; only one admits it. Bluntschli carries chocolate not because he lacks passion but because he has learned — through fifteen years of barracks and battles — to manage it.

This paradox — the pragmatist who is secretly a romantic — is Shaw's most subtle philosophical move. It rescues pragmatism from the charge of soullessness and aligns it with honesty rather than coldness. The chocolate cream soldier is pragmatic not because he feels nothing but because he tells the truth about what he feels.
