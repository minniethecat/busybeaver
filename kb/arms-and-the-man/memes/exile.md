# Exile

Exile, displacement, and foreignness are conditions that shape the play's most important character. [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] is a Swiss fighting in a Balkan war — doubly foreign, belonging to neither the nation he serves nor the nation he invades. His exile is simultaneously literal (he is far from home, hunted through a foreign town) and metaphysical (he is detached from the nationalist passions that drive those around him).

## The Exile's Perspective

Bluntschli's exile gives him the play's most valuable cognitive asset: distance. He sees the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] not as a patriotic struggle but as a professional engagement. He sees [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge not as a glorious victory but as a logistical accident. He sees [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic posturing not as feminine virtue but as a cultural performance. His foreignness is not a deficiency but an advantage — the condition of his ability to see clearly.

## The Cosmopolitan Argument

Shaw's celebration of the exile's perspective is consistent with his cosmopolitan politics. The exile — the person who belongs nowhere in particular — is, in Shaw's universe, the person who sees everywhere with clarity. The patriot is blinded by sentiment; the exile is liberated by detachment. Bluntschli's Swiss identity, his mercenary profession, his inheritance in foreign hotels — all are expressions of a cosmopolitan identity that Shaw presents as the alternative to nationalist passion.
