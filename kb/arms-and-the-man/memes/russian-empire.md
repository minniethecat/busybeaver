# Russian Empire

The Russian Empire was the dominant external power in Bulgarian affairs during the period of *Arms and the Man*. As the self-appointed protector of Orthodox Slavs in the [[balkans|Balkans]], Russia had been instrumental in [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]'s liberation from [[ottoman-empire|Ottoman]] rule and continued to exercise significant influence over Bulgarian politics and military affairs in 1885.

## Historical Role

Russia's relationship with Bulgaria was complex. The [[treaty-of-berlin|Treaty of Berlin]] (1878) had created an autonomous Bulgarian principality under nominal Ottoman sovereignty, with Russia as its primary patron. Russian officers helped organize the Bulgarian army; Russian advisors shaped Bulgarian administration; Russian literature — Pushkin, Turgenev, Tolstoy — formed the cultural education of the Bulgarian elite. [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]]'s familiarity with Pushkin and [[lord-byron|Byron]] reflects this Russian cultural influence.

However, the relationship was also fraught. When Bulgaria annexed Eastern Rumelia in 1885 — the event that triggered the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] — Russia was displeased. Tsar Alexander III, who preferred a compliant Bulgarian client state, recalled Russian officers from the Bulgarian army, leaving Bulgaria to face [[servia|Servia]] largely on its own. The Bulgarian victory at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] was thus achieved without Russian support — a fact that enhanced Bulgarian national pride but strained Russo-Bulgarian relations.

## In the Play

Russian influence is felt rather than depicted. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s Bulgarian army uniform, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s literary culture, the very existence of a Bulgarian state capable of fighting a European war — all bear the impress of Russian patronage. Shaw takes this geopolitical context for granted, using it as the invisible infrastructure of his comedy.

## Shaw's Perspective

Shaw's interest in the Russian Empire was primarily as a variable in his anti-romantic equation. Nationalist sentiment, in Shaw's analysis, was as suspect as military romanticism — another ideal that persuaded ordinary people to die for the benefit of their rulers. The shifting allegiances of Great Power politics — Russia supporting Bulgaria one year, abandoning it the next — provided an ironic backdrop to the characters' earnest declarations of [[patriotism]].
