# King Milan I of Servia

King Milan I (Milan Obrenović, 1854–1901) was the ruler of [[servia|Servia]] during the events of *Arms and the Man*. His decision to declare war on [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] in November 1885 — motivated by territorial ambition and fear of Bulgarian expansion — set in motion the conflict that brings [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber.

## The Decision for War

Milan's decision to attack Bulgaria was driven by multiple calculations. The [[bulgarian-unification|Bulgarian unification]] of September 1885 had created a state larger and potentially more powerful than Servia — a direct threat to Servian ambitions in the [[balkans|Balkans]]. Milan also faced domestic pressure: his unpopularity at home (due to an unhappy marriage to Queen Natalija and general dissatisfaction with his rule) made a short, victorious war politically attractive. He expected an easy victory over a Bulgarian army whose Russian officers had been withdrawn.

The expectation was catastrophically wrong. The Bulgarian army, under Bulgarian command, routed the Servian forces at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]] and pursued them across the border. Austria-Hungary intervened to save Servia from total defeat, and the war ended after two weeks with Servia's borders unchanged but its prestige shattered.

## In the Play

Like [[prince-alexander|Prince Alexander of Battenberg]], King Milan is never mentioned in the dialogue. But the Servian soldiers who flee through the Dragoman Pass — including Bluntschli, a Swiss mercenary in Servian service — are the products of Milan's war. Shaw's silence about the monarchs who start wars is consistent with his democratic instincts: the play is about the people who fight wars, not the kings who declare them.
