# Regiment

The regiment in *Arms and the Man* functions as more than a military unit — it is the institutional setting where Shaw's critique of [[social-class|aristocratic]] military leadership plays out. The Bulgarian army's regiments are commanded by gentlemen-amateurs like [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]], whose [[romanticism|romantic]] conception of soldiering contrasts sharply with the [[professional-soldiering|professional]] competence of the Swiss mercenary [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]].

## Idol of the Regiment

Sergius is introduced as the "idol of the regiment" [001-062], a phrase that captures both his personal charisma and the cult of personality that substitutes for professional military competence. His celebrated [[cavalry]] charge — tactically reckless but romantically glorious — epitomises the regimental culture that Shaw satirises.

## The Professional's Verdict

Bluntschli, evaluating Sergius's charge from a professional standpoint, is blunt:

> Is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns? [001-448]

The question is rhetorical. Bluntschli's [[pragmatism]] exposes the regimental ethos as dangerously unserious — a form of play-acting that costs lives. His account of the charge succeeding only because the enemy lacked the right ammunition [001-501] reinforces the point: the regiment's glory is a product of luck, not [[honor|military virtue]].

## Regiments and Domestic Distraction

In Act II, the practical business of regimental command — "those three regiments" that must be sent to Phillipopolis [002-666, 002-678] — is treated by Petkoff as an annoying distraction from domestic comfort. The regiments become a running joke, symbolising the gap between the aristocratic officer's priorities (lunch, coats, gossip) and the demands of military administration.
