# Shavian Heroine

[[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] is an early and important example of the Shavian heroine — a recurring character type in Shaw's plays who begins trapped in romantic convention and grows into authentic selfhood. Unlike the passive heroines of Victorian melodrama, the Shavian heroine is active, intelligent, and capable of the self-examination that leads to genuine transformation.

## Characteristics

The Shavian heroine, as developed across Shaw's career (Raina in *Arms and the Man*, Ann Whitefield in *Man and Superman*, Barbara Undershaft in *Major Barbara*, Joan in *Saint Joan*), exhibits a cluster of traits that distinguish her from her Victorian predecessors:

1. **Intelligence:** She is smarter than the men around her, or at least quicker to see through romantic pretenses.
2. **Agency:** She acts rather than being acted upon. Her choices drive the plot.
3. **Honesty:** She achieves — or moves toward — an honesty about herself and her desires that the men around her lack.
4. **Pragmatism:** She values material realities over romantic abstractions, even if she begins the play trapped in the latter.

## Raina as Shavian Heroine

Raina embodies the arc of the Shavian heroine in compressed form. She enters the play as a romantic fantasist — a woman whose imagination has been colonized by Pushkin, Byron, and Verdi. She exits as a realist — "I may not be good, but I am real"（003-1376）. The transformation is not a moral conversion (she was never bad) but an awakening: she learns to see herself and the world without the filter of romantic convention.

## The New Woman Context

Shaw was writing in the context of the "New Woman" debates of the 1890s — a cultural conversation about women's education, employment, sexual freedom, and political rights. Raina is not a New Woman in the activist sense (she does not demand the vote or a career), but she is a New Woman in her capacity for growth, her refusal to be defined by romantic convention, and her final embrace of honesty over performance.
