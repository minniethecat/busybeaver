# Cavalry Charge at Slivnitza

Sergius Saranoff's cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] is the play's central military event — the action that establishes Sergius as a hero, inspires [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic adoration, and provides Shaw with his most concentrated case study in the gap between military romance and military reality.

## The Romantic Account

The charge, as received by [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and her mother, is a glorious victory. Sergius led a cavalry attack against Servian machine-gun positions and won — an act of such reckless bravery that "everyone" is talking about it. Raina's opening rhapsody — "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）— is the charge as romantic narrative: the gallant officer, the noble steed, the triumph of [[courage]] over technology, of spirit over machinery.

## The Professional Account

[[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]]'s debunking of the charge is the play's most devastating comic sequence. The charge, he explains, succeeded only because the Servian machine guns had the wrong cartridges — "the Serbs had machine guns but they hadn't the right cartridges"（001-493）. With the correct ammunition, the machine guns would have cut the cavalry to pieces. Sergius's glorious victory was a logistical accident; his bravery was enabled by enemy incompetence. The professional assessment: Sergius "ought to be courtmartialled" for leading a charge that should have been suicide（001-499）.

## Dramatic Function

The cavalry charge functions as the play's thesis statement — the specific military event that Shaw subjects to his general critique of romantic militarism. The charge is the romantic ideal of war made concrete: the lone hero, the noble steed, the impossible odds, the glorious victory. Shaw's demolition of the charge — its reduction to mismatched ammunition and professional incompetence — is his demolition of the entire apparatus of military romance.

## Key Scene

> BLUNTSCHLI. He ought to be courtmartialled. A cavalry charge against a battery of machine guns! Why, it's Don Quixote flourishing like a drum major.（001-498–500）

The comparison to [[don-quixote|Don Quixote]] is the killing blow. Sergius is not a hero but a comic figure — the knight of La Mancha tilting at windmills, mistaking machine guns for an opportunity for glory. The Don Quixote allusion elevates the critique from the military to the literary: Sergius is not merely an incompetent officer but a man who has mistaken the world of romance for the world of fact.
