# Peace

Peace in *Arms and the Man* is not a resolution but a different kind of disorder. The [[serbo-bulgarian-war]] has ended and the peace treaty has been signed, yet the social and domestic complications that emerge in peacetime prove as disruptive as [[cavalry|battle]].

## The Official Peace

The peace treaty is announced in Act II with a mixture of relief and anti-climax. Petkoff explains:

> It declares peace — an honorable one [002-145]

But the honor is dubious. Bluntschli, the [[professional-soldiering|professional]], notes that "we are only waiting until we are quite sure that the peace will be a lasting one" [002-231] before demobilising. The Bulgarian officers, by contrast, treat peace as an opportunity to return to domestic ease — Petkoff's primary concern is lunch, not logistics.

## Peacetime Idleness

The peace reveals the [[social-class|aristocratic]] officer class at its most absurd. Without war to occupy them, Petkoff and Sergius fuss over coats, libraries, and the logistics of sending regiments to Phillipopolis. The peace has made Petkoff "worse than ever" [002-803] — his domestic tyranny flourishes in the absence of military purpose.

For [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], peace means liberation. He has "only waited for the peace to send in my resignation" [002-298], exchanging military service for hotel management. The contrast between Bluntschli's productive peacetime ambition and the Bulgarian officers' aimless leisure reinforces Shaw's thesis: competence flourishes regardless of context.

## The Return of the Soldier

The peace also brings Bluntschli back to the Petkoff household, triggering the play's climactic revelations. The return of Raina's "[[chocolate-cream-soldier]]" — now safe in peacetime — forces the confrontation between [[romanticism|romantic]] illusion and [[pragmatism|practical]] truth that the war had deferred.
