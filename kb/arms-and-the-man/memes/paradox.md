# Paradox

Paradox — the statement that appears self-contradictory but reveals a deeper truth — is Shaw's signature rhetorical device and the intellectual engine of *Arms and the Man*. The play's most memorable lines are paradoxes: "sincerely false"（002-838）, "the coward's art"（002-979）, "unsoldierly; but angelic"（001-551）. Each paradox collapses a conventional opposition and forces the audience to construct a new understanding.

## The Paradoxical Method

Shaw's paradoxes operate through a consistent logic: they take a conventional value (sincerity, courage, soldierly conduct) and join it, through unexpected juxtaposition, with its conventional opposite (falseness, cowardice, angelic tenderness). The result is not nonsense but illumination: the paradox reveals that the conventional opposition was false, that sincerity and falseness can coexist, that the coward's art is the art of survival, that the unsoldierly act of eating chocolate can be the angelic act of needing mercy.

## Paradox as Argument

The paradox is not merely decorative in Shaw's hands but argumentative. Each paradox is a miniature version of the play's larger argument: that the conventional oppositions structuring Victorian morality (courage/cowardice, honor/pragmatism, love/commerce) are false oppositions, and that reality is more interesting and more moral than the categories that seek to contain it.
