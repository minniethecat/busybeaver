# Scandal

The spectre of scandal haunts every character in *Arms and the Man*. In the closed world of Bulgarian high society, where [[social-class|reputation]] is currency, the gap between private conduct and public appearance must be carefully managed. Shaw uses the threat of scandal to expose the hypocrisy at the heart of [[honor|aristocratic morality]].

## The Bedroom Intruder

The play's inciting scandal is literal: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] has hidden an enemy soldier in her bedroom. When her mother [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] discovers Bluntschli, she is "scandalized" [001-756] — not by the danger but by the impropriety. The scene establishes the play's moral economy: private virtue matters less than the appearance of virtue, and the greatest threat is not a Serbian soldier but a social [[scandal]].

## Louka's Weapon

[[louka|Louka]], the Petkoffs' servant, understands scandal as a tool of [[servant-agency|social leverage]]. Her willingness to "scandalize the five senses" [002-717] gives her power over Sergius — she knows his secrets and is not afraid to use them. Louka's strategic deployment of scandal-in-waiting inverts the class hierarchy: the servant controls the master through the threat of exposure.

## The Coat and the Photograph

Act III builds its [[irony|dramatic tension]] around the threat of scandal. The incriminating photograph of Raina, inscribed to her "chocolate cream soldier," sits in Bluntschli's coat — which Petkoff and Sergius casually handle without recognising. Shaw constructs an elaborate machinery of near-exposure, demonstrating that scandal in this society is both catastrophic (if discovered) and absurdly fragile (resting on a mislaid coat and an unsigned photograph).
