# Gentleman

The concept of the "gentleman" serves as one of Shaw's sharpest vehicles for [[social-class|class]] critique in *Arms and the Man*. The play systematically contrasts the aristocratic performance of gentility — embodied by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] — with the substantive decency of [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], who never claims the title but consistently behaves with greater moral integrity.

## The Gentleman's Weapon

The category of "gentleman" is introduced through its material accessories. When Raina discovers Bluntschli's pistol, she recoils:

> It is not the weapon of a gentleman! [001-261]

The line encodes an entire class ideology: gentlemen use [[chivalry|swords]], not firearms. But the play immediately subverts this — Bluntschli, the pistol-carrying professional, proves more honourable than the sabre-wielding Sergius.

## Sergius's Failed Gentility

Sergius performs the gentleman with theatrical conviction but repeatedly fails its moral demands. He flirts with Louka while engaged to Raina, then rationalises his behaviour with the very code he is violating:

> A gentleman has no right to hurt a woman under any circumstances [002-627]

Louka, undeceived, delivers the play's verdict on performative gentility: "It's so hard to know what a gentleman considers right" [002-555]. Her [[servant-agency|servant's perspective]] exposes gentility as a flexible alibi rather than a fixed moral code.

## Nicola: The Servant Gentleman

[[nicola|Nicola]], the Petkoffs' head servant, provides a third model of gentlemanly conduct. Though excluded from the category by birth, Nicola operates by a consistent code of self-interested restraint — he treats Louka with forbearance, serves his employers with discretion, and calculates his interests with the same cool rationality that Bluntschli applies to soldiering. His philosophy is [[pragmatism|pragmatic]] rather than moral: discretion brings material reward. Nicola's presence completes Shaw's taxonomy — the aristocrat who performs gentility (Sergius), the professional who embodies it without claiming it (Bluntschli), and the servant who cannot claim it at all (Nicola).

## The True Gentleman

Raina recognises Bluntschli's quality beneath his ungentlemanly exterior: "I see that you are a gentleman" [001-700]. The play's [[irony]] is that the Swiss mercenary — practical, unromantic, carrying chocolate instead of cartridges — behaves more like a gentleman than any of the Bulgarian aristocrats. Shaw, writing from his Fabian socialist perspective, suggests that genuine decency belongs to the [[professional-soldiering|professional]], not the [[romanticism|romantic]] aristocrat — a meritocratic argument that anticipates the twentieth century's gradual displacement of hereditary status by professional competence.
