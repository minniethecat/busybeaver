# Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809–1892)

Alfred Tennyson, first Baron Tennyson, served as Poet Laureate of Great Britain from 1850 until his death. He was the preeminent voice of Victorian poetry, and his works — "In Memoriam," "Idylls of the King," "Ulysses" — defined the moral and aesthetic sensibilities of the age. His 1854 poem "The Charge of the Light Brigade" became one of the most famous war poems in the English language.

"The Charge of the Light Brigade" memorialized a disastrous British cavalry charge during the Crimean War in which miscommunication sent six hundred horsemen directly into Russian artillery. Tennyson transformed this military blunder into a monument of heroic sacrifice: "Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die." The poem's martial rhythm and reverent tone made it a staple of Victorian education and patriotic sentiment.

This is precisely the cultural legacy Shaw targets in Arms and the Man. When Sergius Saranoff leads his own cavalry charge into enemy fire and survives only because the Serbians had the wrong ammunition, Shaw is rewriting Tennyson's narrative. The "heroism" of the charge is exposed as accident; the "glory" as misrepresentation. Shaw replaces Tennyson's "noble six hundred" with Bluntschli's chocolate creams.

Shaw's critique extends beyond one poem to the entire cultural apparatus that romanticizes war. Tennyson, as the institutional voice of Victorian poetry, represents the literary establishment's complicity in glorifying military folly. Arms and the Man is in part a rebuttal to the Tennysonian tradition — an insistence that soldiers would rather carry chocolate than cartridges, and that the "reason why" matters more than the doing and dying.
