# Courage: Raina's Evolving Courage

Raina Petkoff begins the play as the consumer of romantic courage — the audience for [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge, the believer in Byron and Pushkin — and ends it as a woman who has learned that courage means seeing clearly. Her arc traces the movement from romantic to real courage, and it is, in its quiet way, one of the play's most radical transformations.

## Act I: The Romantic Consumer

Raina enters the play in a state of romantic ecstasy, having just learned of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]]. Her response is the play's purest expression of the romantic worldview:

> RAINA. It proves that all our ideas were real after all. （001-080）

For Raina, the charge is not merely a military event — it is an existential validation. If men can be heroes, then the romantic literature she has consumed (Byron, Pushkin, Verdi) has not lied to her. Courage — specifically, the theatrical battlefield courage of the cavalry charge — is the evidence she needs that the world is as beautiful as art promised it would be.

Her courage at this stage is of a particular kind: the courage to believe. It takes a certain bravery to maintain romantic ideals in a world that constantly undermines them. But it is a passive courage, a consumer's courage — she is brave enough to believe in heroes, not to be one.

## Hiding Bluntschli: The First Real Courage

Raina's first moment of active courage comes when she hides [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], a fugitive Servian officer, behind her curtain while a Russian officer searches her room. The stage direction captures the moment:

> She winks and gasps, but stands her ground. （001-327）

This is real courage — not the theatrical courage of the cavalry charge but the practical courage of protecting a human being at personal risk. She lies to a military officer, she conceals an enemy combatant, and she does it while terrified. The play quietly honors this moment: Raina is braver than she knows.

Her courage here is also moral courage of a specific kind: she has promised to protect Bluntschli, and she keeps that promise even when keeping it is dangerous. The romantic who believed in heroic ideals is now, without quite realizing it, acting on them.

## The Challenge from Bluntschli

Bluntschli's presence forces Raina to confront the gap between her romantic ideals and reality. When she challenges him — "I think I am at heart as brave as you" （001-421） — he does not dispute her. Instead, he explains the physiological nature of courage: "I can stand two days without shewing it much; but no man can stand three days: I'm as nervous as a mouse" （001-424 to 001-425）.

This is Raina's first exposure to a non-romantic account of courage. Bluntschli does not tell her she is wrong — he simply reframes the entire question. Courage is not a moral quality but a physiological resource. Anyone can be brave for a while. The question is not *whether* you are brave but *how long* you can sustain it.

## Act II: The Disillusionment Begins

By Act II, four months after the war, Raina's romantic certainties are crumbling. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] has returned, and his presence forces her to confront the gap between the hero of her imagination and the man before her. His courtship of [[louka|Louka]] — discovered by Raina when she catches them in an intimate moment — accelerates her disillusionment.

But Raina's courage in Act II is not merely negative (the courage to be disillusioned). She also demonstrates the courage to maintain her secret: she has not told Sergius about the "chocolate cream soldier" she harbored. This is not cowardice but a kind of strategic silence — the courage to withhold information that would cause pain, even when honesty would be easier.

## Act III: The Courage to See Clearly

Raina's climactic moment of courage comes in Act III, when she must confront the full truth about her romantic ideals. The revelation that [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is the Swiss officer [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and [[petkoff-major-paul|Major Petkoff]] have been searching for — and that Bluntschli has returned — forces an ultimate reckoning.

Raina must now choose: cling to the romantic fiction or accept the reality. Her choice — to accept Bluntschli, the "chocolate cream soldier," the man who is professionally honest rather than romantically gallant — is the play's final endorsement of real courage over theatrical courage. She chooses the man who admits fear and carries chocolate over the man who charges machine guns.

This choice takes moral courage of the highest order. Raina must abandon the entire worldview she was raised in — the world of Byron and Pushkin, of operatic heroism and romantic love — and accept a world where courage looks like competence, and love looks like pragmatic partnership. The courage to see clearly, to relinquish comfortable illusions for uncomfortable truths, is the form of courage the play ultimately honors most.

## The Quiet Arc

Raina's courage is easy to overlook because it is quiet. She does not charge machine guns or defy class hierarchy or refuse a duel. Her courage is the courage to grow — to move from the romantic consumer of courage to a woman capable of her own acts of bravery. Her arc traces the play's central argument: that true courage has nothing to do with the battlefield and everything to do with the willingness to face reality without the consolations of romance.

By the final curtain, Raina has transformed from a girl who needed heroes to believe in into a woman who can accept a man who refuses to be one. That acceptance is, in Shaw's accounting, the bravest act in the play.
