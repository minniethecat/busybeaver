# Class Key Figures

Every major character in *Arms and the Man* embodies a distinct class position, and Shaw uses these figures to construct a systematic anatomy of class in late 19th-century Bulgarian society. No character escapes the interrogation of whether their rank reflects anything more than the accident of birth.

## Character Class Positions

| Figure | Role | Analysis |
|--------|------|----------|
| [[louka|Louka]] | Ambitious servant | Refuses class destiny; uses intelligence, secrets, and sexual attraction to ascend. Represents *defiance* — the demand that competence, not birth, determine social position. Her ascent from servant to fiancée of a cavalry officer is the play's only genuine class mobility. |
| [[nicola|Nicola]] | Pragmatic servant | Accepts class hierarchy as a system to be worked, not overturned. His philosophy — "the secret of success in service"（002-081） — represents *accommodation*. Shaw refuses to condemn him: his long-term investment in Louka's rise is arguably the play's most intelligent social strategy. |
| [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] | Upper-class romantic | Her class privilege enables her romantic fantasies; her library, her opera seasons, and her Petkoff name are props in a performance of civilization. Her eventual engagement to Bluntschli — a professional mercenary — represents a class compromise rather than a revolutionary break. |
| [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] | Aristocratic officer | Embodies the hypocrisy of upper-class honour. His class arrogance coexists with genuine self-knowledge about the emptiness of military heroism. His eventual engagement to Louka is both a class transgression and a surrender to social reality. |
| [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine Petkoff]] | Aspirational matriarch | A "farmer's wife" who "is determined to be a Viennese lady"（001-038）. Her class anxiety is the engine of the Petkoffs' social performance. |
| [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] | Provincial gentry | His military rank and his library are acquired status markers he does not fully inhabit. His inability to adapt to "civilized" customs — electric bells, not shouting for servants — marks him as a transitional figure in Bulgaria's class formation. |
| [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] | Professional outsider | A Swiss mercenary who belongs to no Bulgarian class category. His efficiency, competence, and refusal of romantic pretence make him the play's implicit standard of merit. His final triumph as a hotelier — a business owner — positions him in the rising commercial class that Nicola aspires to join. |

## Two Servants, Two Philosophies

The play's most important class contrast is between Louka and Nicola. They share the same class position — servants in the Petkoff household — but diverge completely in their response to that position:

**Louka** represents *defiance*. Her famous declaration — "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola"（002-079） and "You'll never put the soul of a servant into me"（002-093） — is a refusal of class consciousness. She distinguishes between *class as position* (servant in the household) and *class as identity* (internalized subordination). Her strategy is direct: exploit the masters' secrets, leverage sexual attraction, and demand entry to the elite on her own terms.

**Nicola** represents *accommodation*. His reply — "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-081） — acknowledges that the system rewards those who accept its terms. His strategy is indirect: feign loyalty, accumulate savings, and eventually exit servitude for shopkeeping. Nicola's final revelation — that he protected Louka as a long-term investment, positioning himself to benefit from her social ascent — reveals accommodation as a sophisticated, patient strategy rather than mere servility.

## The Elite: Three Varieties of Class Performance

The Petkoff household displays three distinct modes of elite class identity:

**Catherine Petkoff** embodies *aspiration*. A farmer's wife determined to be a Viennese lady, she is the engine of the family's social performance. Her class anxiety drives the household's accumulation of status markers — the library, the opera seasons, the "half rich Bulgarian, half cheap Viennese"（001-005 to 001-006） interior.

**Major Petkoff** embodies *arrival without mastery*. His class position has outpaced his class habitus. He possesses a library without reading, a military rank without tactical competence, and domestic customs (shouting for servants instead of ringing) that betray his provincial origins.

**Raina Petkoff** embodies *privilege as performance*. Her romantic idealism is inseparable from her class position; her "higher love" with Sergius is as much a class alliance as an emotional one. Her ultimate engagement to Bluntschli — a pragmatic Swiss professional rather than a romantic Bulgarian officer — represents a quiet class compromise, not a revolution.

**Sergius Saranoff** embodies *hypocrisy with self-knowledge*. He is the most complex of the elite characters: fully aware of the emptiness of military heroism and aristocratic honour, yet unable to relinquish the privileges they confer. His engagement to Louka is simultaneously a class transgression and an admission that the servant possesses more genuine courage than the master.

## The Outsider

**Captain Bluntschli** occupies a unique position: he belongs to no Bulgarian class category. As a Swiss mercenary, he is a professional who sells competence to the highest bidder — neither aristocrat nor peasant, neither patriot nor enemy. His famous observation — "nine soldiers out of ten are born fools"（001-293） — is a class statement as much as a military one: it asserts that professional knowledge trumps social rank. His eventual role as a hotelier positions him in the rising commercial class, the bourgeoisie that Nicola aspires to join and that Shaw, a Fabian socialist, viewed as the engine of gradual social transformation.
