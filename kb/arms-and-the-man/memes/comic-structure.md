# Comic Structure

*Arms and the Man* follows a classical three-act structure that Shaw inherited from the well-made play tradition and transformed for his own purposes. Each act has a distinct dramatic function, setting, and emotional register, and the progression from act to act traces the play's argument in structural form.

## Act I: Intrusion

The first act is set in [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s [[bedchamber]] and establishes the play's central collision: romantic idealism meets professional realism. The act has the tightest unity of time (one night), place (one room), and action (the encounter between Raina and Bluntschli). It ends with the romantic order destabilized — Raina has sheltered an enemy, Bluntschli has escaped, and the chocolate cream soldier has been named.

## Act II: Complication

The second act shifts to the [[library]] and the daylight world of social performance. The war is over; the characters must now deal with its domestic consequences. The act multiplies complications: the missing coat, Sergius's flirtation with Louka, Raina's growing ambivalence, the return of Bluntschli. Where Act I was intimate, Act II is social; where Act I was revelation, Act II is concealment.

## Act III: Resolution

The third act resolves the complications through a series of comic revelations: Bluntschli's wealth, Raina's true feelings, Sergius's entanglement with Louka. The resolution is characteristically Shavian — not a restoration of the initial order but a rearrangement into more rational configurations. The final image is not a romantic embrace but a practical understanding.

## Shaw's Adaptation

Shaw's three acts follow the well-made play formula in outline while subverting its conventions in execution. The well-made play typically restores the social order; Shaw's play rearranges it. The well-made play typically punishes transgression; Shaw's play rewards honesty. The well-made play typically ends with a moral lesson; Shaw's play ends with a hotel inventory.
