# War Cabinet

The war cabinet — the military high command that directs the war effort — is never depicted on stage but is invoked through [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s comic accounts of its deliberations. The war cabinet functions in the play as the institutional face of the military romanticism Shaw attacks: a body of senior officers whose decisions are driven by vanity, status anxiety, and sheer incompetence.

## In the Play

### ACT II

Major Petkoff's account of the war cabinet's deliberations is one of the play's most sustained satirical passages. The cabinet, he tells us, can never agree on anything — "they will have it their own way" is his refrain（002-621）. The commanding officers who should be directing military strategy are instead engaged in petty disputes about protocol, precedence, and personal dignity. The war, in Petkoff's telling, is conducted not by strategic genius but by bureaucratic wrangling.

### The Comic Gap

The war cabinet embodies the gap between military reality and military self-presentation that is the play's central concern. The same officers who preside over the cabinet's comic chaos are the officers who, after the war, will tell stories of strategic brilliance and heroic leadership. The war cabinet is where the myths that sustain military romanticism are manufactured — and where Shaw exposes the machinery of their manufacture.

## Key Scene

> PETKOFF. They will have it their own way. They can never agree about anything. That's the worst of these councils of war.（002-620–621）

Petkoff's complaint — "they can never agree about anything" — is delivered as a domestic grumble but functions as a devastating institutional critique. The men directing the war cannot agree; the men dying in the war are not consulted.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's war cabinet is of a piece with his larger critique of institutional authority. Just as Sergius's cavalry charge is revealed as incompetence dressed as heroism, the war cabinet is revealed as vanity dressed as command. The military hierarchy, in Shaw's analysis, elevates men who look the part over men who can do the job — and the consequence is death.

- **Target of satire**: The military bureaucracy that substitutes rank for competence
- **Alternative proposed**: Professional leadership based on expertise rather than social standing
