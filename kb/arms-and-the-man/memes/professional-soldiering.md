# Professional Soldiering

The ethical and practical core of Shaw's anti-romantic argument in *Arms and the Man* — the
proposition that soldiering is a trade like any other, to be judged by competence rather
than glamour. [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]], a Swiss mercenary fighting for Servia "because it came first
on the road from Switzerland"（001-362）, embodies the professional soldier's ethos: efficiency
over heroism, survival over sacrifice, and an honest accounting of costs over patriotic rhetoric.
His foil is [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]], the Bulgarian cavalry officer whose heroic charge — the very
act that defines him as a romantic hero — is exposed by Bluntschli's professional analysis as "the
maddest" tactical folly ever let loose on a field of battle（001-501）.

## Definition

Professional soldiering means fighting as a trade, not a vocation. The professional soldier
serves whoever pays, evaluates tactics by their probability of success, and regards war as
logistics rather than poetry. Bluntschli's Swiss nationality is central: Switzerland, a neutral
nation famously associated with mercenary service, supplies soldiers who have no national stake
in Balkan conflicts. "I am only a Swiss," he tells Raina, "fighting merely as a professional
soldier. I joined Servia because it came first on the road from Switzerland"（001-362）. This
is not cynicism — it is clarity. The professional admits what the romantic conceals: that war
is work, not worship.

The concept is defined by three interlocking commitments. First, **competence** — the professional
knows his trade. Bluntschli's fourteen years of service（001-441）give him authority to distinguish
old soldiers from young fools. Second, **efficiency** — the professional conserves resources.
Chocolates sustain a soldier; empty cartridges do not（001-396）. Third, **honesty** — the
professional does not dress self-interest in patriotic costume. He fights for pay, and says so.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The concept of professional soldiering is introduced through Bluntschli's critique of
[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge. Fleeing Bulgarian pursuit, Bluntschli takes refuge in
[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedroom. When she challenges his professionalism, he counters by indicting
hers — or rather, her fiance's:

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it.（001-448）

This is the play's pivotal analytical moment. Bluntschli applies a professional standard —
probability of success, cost-benefit ratio — to an act that Bulgarian culture celebrates as
heroic. The charge succeeded only by accident: the Servian artillery had been sent the wrong
cartridges and could not fire（001-493）. Professional soldiering thus decomposes the heroic
narrative into its constituent facts: luck, incompetence, and the wrong ammunition.

Bluntschli deepens his professional critique with a veteran's taxonomy of soldier types:

> MAN. Oh, yes, they are. There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before. Why, how is it that you've just beaten us? Sheer ignorance of the art of war, nothing else. (*Indignantly.*) I never saw anything so unprofessional.（001-440）

The "old soldier" — the professional — carries food, not ammunition. The "young soldier" —
the amateur — carries pistols and cartridges. This taxonomy is practical anthropology: it
classifies soldiers by their survival habits, not their ideals. When Bluntschli declares
"I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead"
（001-395）, he is not confessing cowardice but asserting professional wisdom. The chocolate
cream — later to become his pet name — is the professional soldier's ration: portable energy,
instantly consumable, sustaining where gunpowder would be useless.

Raina, whose "most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）are outraged, cannot process this
information within her romantic framework. She coins the epithet "chocolate cream soldier"
（001-566）as mockery, but the play will progressively validate the professional ethos it
represents.

### ACT II

The second act develops professional soldiering through reversal and ironic confirmation.
[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], the romantic hero, has resigned his commission. His reason mirrors Bluntschli's
professional critique — but spoke with bitter cynicism rather than cheerful candor:

> SERGIUS. I am no longer a soldier. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak. That is the whole secret of successful fighting. Get your enemy at a disadvantage; and never, on any account, fight him on equal terms.（002-339）

This is professional soldiering stripped of its ethical content — a mirror held up to
Bluntschli's ethos. Where Bluntschli's professionalism is rooted in competence and survival,
Sergius's disillusionment reduces soldiering to mere predation. The distinction matters:
Bluntschli fights when paid to fight and is "very glad to get out of it when I haven't to"
（003-765）; Sergius now sees all soldiering as cowardice dressed in uniforms.

[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]], the amiable and unreflective career officer, supplies the pragmatic
middle ground: "I suppose soldiering has to be a trade like any other trade"（002-346）.
He is not a professional in Bluntschli's sense — his competence is questionable — but he
accepts the trade-status of soldiering without Sergius's wounded idealism.

Sergius's admission that he "so unprofessionally charged" the battery（002-389）validates
Bluntschli's professional critique from the amateur's own mouth. The romantic hero concedes
the professional's argument — and, in doing so, destroys the foundation of his own identity.
He can no longer be both a hero and honest.

### ACT III

The third act completes professional soldiering's elevation from tactical doctrine to
life philosophy. Bluntschli returns — no longer a penniless fugitive but a man who has
inherited his father's hotels. His professional competence now extends to business: he
efficiently resolves the three regiments' transport problem that has baffled Petkoff and
Sergius（002-849）. Professional soldiering, it emerges, is transferable: the same
pragmatic clarity that assesses cavalry charges also assesses forage logistics.

The act's climactic confrontation between Bluntschli and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] crystallizes the
professional-amateur dichotomy:

> BLUNTSCHLI. I'm a professional soldier. I fight when I have to, and am very glad to get out of it when I haven't to. You're only an amateur: you think fighting's an amusement.（003-764）

This single speech contains the play's definitive statement on professional soldiering.
The professional fights from necessity, not desire; the amateur fights for glory, for
excitement, for self-definition. Bluntschli's refusal of Sergius's duel challenge is not
cowardice but the logical extension of professional reasoning: why fight when no strategic
objective is served?

Sergius's reply — "I could no more fight with you than I could make love to an ugly woman.
You've no magnetism: you're not a man, you're a machine"（003-770）— is the romantic's
last defense. To call the professional a "machine" is to concede his effectiveness while
denying his humanity. But Bluntschli cheerfully accepts the charge: "Quite true, quite true.
I always was that sort of chap"（003-775）. The professional has no need of magnetism; he
has results.

Bluntschli's final confession — that he possesses "an incurably romantic disposition"
（003-1082）— complicates the professional/amateur binary without dissolving it. He ran
away from home, joined the army instead of his father's business, and climbed a balcony
when a sensible man would have dived into a cellar. Professional soldiering, it turns out,
is not the absence of romance but its channeling into competent forms. The chocolate cream
soldier is a romantic — but his romance is directed at a woman, not at war.

## Key Scene

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire?（001-448）

This single question — asked by a fleeing mercenary to the fiancee of the man whose heroism
he is debunking — is the play's philosophical fulcrum. Bluntschli applies a professional
standard to an act of romantic heroism and finds it absurd. The charge succeeded, but
professional soldiering judges actions by their design, not their outcome. A cavalry charge
against machine guns remains unprofessional even if, by accident of wrong cartridges, it
carries the day.

> MAN. There are only two sorts of soldiers: old ones and young ones. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before.（001-440）

Bluntschli's veteran taxonomy reduces soldiering to a matter of experience and survival
habits. The professional difference between old and young soldiers is not courage but
what they carry: cartridges for the young, food for the old. This is professional soldiering
as practical wisdom — knowledge purchased by fourteen years of barracks and battles.

> BLUNTSCHLI. I'm a professional soldier. I fight when I have to, and am very glad to get out of it when I haven't to. You're only an amateur: you think fighting's an amusement.（003-764）

The play's definitive articulation of professional soldiering as an ethos — and its
clearest contrast with the amateur heroism it replaces. The professional fights from
necessity; the amateur fights for sport. Shaw leaves no doubt which is the more morally
defensible position.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw presents professional soldiering as **honest work**, stripping warfare of the
romantic glamour that, in his view, makes war possible. The Fabian socialist saw
military heroism as a dangerous ideology: nations go to war because young men are taught
to value glory over survival, and populations are fed narratives of cavalry charges
rather than accounts of broken knees from falling off horses（001-478）.

The professional soldier, by contrast, is a figure of integrity precisely because he
admits his mercenary motives. Bluntschli's Swiss nationality — neutral Switzerland as
the historical source of Europe's mercenaries — is not incidental. It marks him as an
outsider to Balkan nationalism, immune to the patriotic rhetoric that inflames both
Bulgarians and Serbs. His father's hotels（001-690）extend the professional logic beyond
the battlefield: soldiering is one trade among many, and the competent professional
may move between them — from artillery to hospitality — without loss of identity.

- **Satiric target**: The romantic cult of the heroic soldier — cavalry charges, flashing
  swords, patriotic self-sacrifice — which Shaw regarded as a recruitment poster for
  mass death.
- **Alternative offered**: The professional soldier — competent, honest about his motives,
  and interested in survival rather than glory. Shaw does not celebrate war; he celebrates
  the man who treats it as a job and therefore has no incentive to prolong it.

## Critical Perspectives

Professional soldiering in *Arms and the Man* has attracted varied critical attention.
Most readers recognize Bluntschli's professionalism as Shaw's vehicle for an anti-war
argument, but the nature of that argument is contested.

Eric Bentley, in *The Playwright as Thinker* (1946), identifies Bluntschli as an early
instance of the Shavian hero — the practical man whose anti-romantic frankness makes him
morally superior to the idealists he exposes. For Bentley, professional soldiering is
not merely a military doctrine but a philosophical position: the claim that clear-eyed
self-interest produces better outcomes than high-minded self-deception.

Margery M. Morgan (*The Shavian Playground*, 1972) reads the professional soldier as a
comic type — the "wise fool" whose apparent cynicism conceals genuine decency. Bluntschli's
profession of romantic disposition（003-1082）is, for Morgan, the key: Shaw's professional
is a romantic who has learned from experience, not a born anti-romantic. His professionalism
is earned, not innate.

More recent critics have noted the class dimension of professional soldiering. Bluntschli's
hotel-keeping father makes him bourgeois, not aristocratic; his professionalism is the
ethos of the commercial middle class invading the aristocratic preserve of military command.
Sergius, by contrast, is landed gentry — the class that traditionally supplied officers
who fought for honor, not pay. The play's resolution — the bourgeois professional winning
the aristocratic heroine — is a Shavian class allegory.

> Shaw's professional soldier is a businessman in uniform, and his victory over the aristocratic amateur is Shaw's comic prophecy of the twentieth century. — Nicholas Grene, *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View* (1984)
