# Soldier

The soldier is the play's central human type, but Shaw presents two incompatible versions of the figure. The romantic soldier — the cavalry officer in a splendid [[uniform]], charging the enemy with drawn sabre — is a creature of opera and epic poetry. The professional soldier — the Swiss mercenary who measures cartridges and knows when to retreat — is a tradesman in arms. The play's dramatic work is the replacement of the first by the second in the audience's estimation and in [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s affections.

## Definition

Shaw distinguishes between the soldier-as-symbol and the soldier-as-worker. The symbolic soldier exists to be admired: his [[uniform]], his bearing, his courage, his willingness to die. The worker-soldier exists to perform a function: to fight when advantageous, to retreat when necessary, to survive to fight another day. The play sides decisively with the worker. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s definition — "Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak"（002-979–981）— strips the soldier of every romantic attribute and reconstitutes him as a rational calculator of advantage.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The soldier is introduced through two radically different prisms. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s opening rhapsody over [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s portrait — "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）— establishes the romantic model. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is the soldier as operatic lead: handsome, daring, the hero of a cavalry charge that "everyone" is talking about. Moments later, [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] climbs through the window and the real soldier arrives: exhausted, hungry, unarmed (his revolver carries chocolate, not cartridges（001-396）), and thoroughly unheroic. The contrast could not be sharper or more deliberate.

### ACT II

The soldier in peacetime reveals further dimensions of Shaw's critique. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]], the play's ranking Bulgarian officer, is preoccupied not with strategy but with the lost old coat in which his wife and daughter smuggled Bluntschli to safety. His military authority is a comic mask; the real decisions in the Petkoff household are made by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], the hero of Slivnitza, spends the act maneuvering between his public engagement to Raina and his private flirtation with [[louka|Louka]] — the soldier as sexual adventurer rather than military hero.

### ACT III

Bluntschli's revelation that he has inherited a chain of hotels completes the soldier's demystification. The professional fighting man is also a businessman; war is not a calling but a temporary occupation. When [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] challenges Bluntschli to a duel, Bluntschli's calm acceptance and practical arrangements ("I'm a good shot with a pistol, if that's what you want") defuses the Byronic gesture with professional competence. The soldier as romantic duelist meets the soldier as efficient technician, and the latter wins without firing a shot.

## Key Scene

> MAN. I've no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.（001-395–396）

The chocolate revelation is the play's most concentrated dismantling of the soldier-myth. A soldier without ammunition is by romantic standards a failure; but a soldier who knows that cartridges are useless in battle (because they won't fit the gun) is a professional. The chocolate — a childish sweet — becomes the sign of adult rationality.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw, a lifelong opponent of militarism and a Fabian socialist, uses the soldier figure to attack the cultural apparatus that makes war possible. The romantic soldier exists because poets and composers have made him exist; Shaw aims to unmake him through comedy. By replacing the Byronic cavalryman with the practical Swiss mercenary, Shaw argues that soldiering should be understood as a profession like any other — dangerous, necessary in a flawed world, but undeserving of the halo of heroism that enables the next war.

- **Target of satire**: The cult of the military hero in art, literature, and popular sentiment
- **Alternative proposed**: The soldier as skilled professional, performing a difficult job without the apparatus of romantic glorification
