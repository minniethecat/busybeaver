The Petkoff House is the sole physical setting of *Arms and the Man*, but it functions as far more than backdrop. Over three acts, Shaw moves the action through three distinct spaces within the property — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber (ACT I), the garden (ACT II), and the library (ACT III) — each selected for its symbolic resonance. The house is a microcosm of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n aristocratic society: rich enough to imitate European fashion ("half rich [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n, half cheap Viennese"), aspirational enough to boast a library no one reads, and porous enough to admit fugitive soldiers, ambitious servants, and inconvenient truths through every door and window.

## 基本信息

- **地理位置**：A small town near the Dragoman Pass, on the invasion route between Serbia and [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]. The house is described as belonging to "the richest and best known family in our country" （001-621）.
- **剧中场景**：ACT I — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber; ACT II — the garden; ACT III — the library. Each space stages a different aspect of the social order under pressure.
- **类型**：Interior（室内——贵族家庭住宅）

## 舞台指示描述

Shaw's stage directions provide meticulous descriptions of each room, each carrying thematic weight.

### Raina's Bedchamber (Act I)

> Night. A lady's bedchamber in Bulgaria, in a small town near the Dragoman Pass. It is late in November, 1885. The room is decorated in the worst possible taste — half rich Bulgarian, half cheap Viennese. （001-001–006）

The "half rich [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n, half cheap Viennese" aesthetic is Shaw's shorthand for colonial mimicry — the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]s imitate European culture without understanding it. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedroom is decorated with a portrait of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] — "an extremely handsome officer, whose lofty bearing and magnetic glance can be felt even from the portrait" （001-024–025）— and opera-programme souvenirs, marking it as a shrine to romantic fantasy. The room's furnishings are catalogued in ethnographic detail: "oriental and gorgeous" textiles versus "occidental and paltry" wallpaper （001-009）, a painted wooden shrine, an ottoman, Austrian bent-wood furniture, and "a pile of paper backed novels, a box of chocolate creams" （001-023）.

### The Garden (Act II)

> The sixth of March, 1886. In the garden of major Petkoff's house. It is a fine spring morning; and the garden looks fresh and pretty. Beyond the paling the tops of a couple of minarets can be seen, shewing that there is a valley there, with the little town in it. A few miles further the Balkan mountains rise and shut in the view. （002-001–005）

The garden is a transitional space — neither fully interior (private, aristocratic) nor exterior (public, democratic). Shaw notes practical details: "fruit bushes along the paling and house, covered with washing hung out to dry" （002-008–009）— the laundry visible to visitors becomes a running joke about the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]s' incomplete gentility. A "wooden garden seat against the wall on the left" （002-014）provides a stage for the servant-class confrontations that define Act II.

### The Library (Act III)

> In the library after lunch. It is not much of a library, its literary equipment consisting of a single fixed shelf stocked with old paper-covered novels, broken backed, coffee stained, torn and thumbed, and a couple of little hanging shelves with a few gift books on them, the rest of the wall space being occupied by trophies of war and the chase. But it is a most comfortable sitting-room. （003-001–006）

The library is [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s pride — "the only one in Bulgaria" （001-651）— but it reveals everything Shaw wants to say about aspirational provincialism. The books are decorative, not functional; the wall space devoted to hunting trophies exceeds the shelf space for reading. Even the electric bell — "the button of the electric bell is between the door and the fireplace" （003-033–034）— is a status symbol that provokes confusion:

> PETKOFF. What's an electric bell? （002-191）

> CATHERINE. You touch a button; something tinkles in the kitchen; and then Nicola comes up. （002-193–194）

> PETKOFF. Why not shout for him? （002-196）

This exchange encapsulates the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]s' relationship to modernity: they purchase its symbols without understanding its logic.

## 剧情功能

- **承载事件**：Every significant encounter in the play occurs within the Petkoff House. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s escape and refuge (ACT I, bedchamber), [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s return and flirtation with [[louka|Louka]] (ACT II, garden), the photograph revelation and [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s proposal (ACT III, library) — the house witnesses the dismantling of every romantic illusion its occupants hold.

- **人物互动**：The house's spatial hierarchy enforces social boundaries that the play's action systematically transgresses. Servants enter through the garden gate; officers through the front door; fugitives through the bedroom window. Each entrance and exit maps a social position. [[louka|Louka]] knows the house's secrets — "I know some family secrets they wouldn't care to have told" （002-048–049）— and her knowledge of the architecture is a form of power.

- **信息揭示**：The house contains secrets at every level — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] hides [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] behind her curtain, [[louka|Louka]] overhears private conversations, the photograph moves from [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s private possession to public evidence. The architecture itself becomes an agent of revelation. As [[louka|Louka]] demonstrates to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], the shutters have a broken bolt — "One of them ought to be bolted at the bottom; but the bolt's gone" （001-160–161）— a physical vulnerability that enables the entire plot.

## 象征意义

- **The Bedchamber as Romantic Sanctuary**：[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedroom, with its opera souvenirs and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s portrait, is a shrine to romantic fantasy. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s intrusion through the window is a violation of both physical space and ideological sanctuary — realism breaking into the temple of romance. Raina's adoration of the portrait — "Oh, I shall never be unworthy of you any more, my hero—never, never, never" （001-173–174）— is the purest expression of the romantic illusion the house is designed to protect.

- **The Garden as Social Battleground**：Between the private house and the public road, the garden is where class hierarchies are most visibly challenged. [[louka|Louka]] uses it to intercept [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]; [[nicola|Nicola]] uses it to manage information. It is a liminal space where the old order is negotiated but not yet overthrown. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s philosophy of service — "that's the secret of success in service" （002-081）— is delivered in the garden, the one space where servants can speak freely.

- **The Library as Hollow Civilization**：[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s library — a roomful of unread books, with an electric bell he doesn't understand — is Shaw's satire of aspirational modernity. The [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]s possess the symbols of European civilization without engaging with its substance. The library scene in ACT III, where [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s competence exposes everyone's pretensions, is the play's definitive judgment on this hollow gentility.

- **Windows and Doors as Moral Thresholds**：[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] enters through a window, not a door — a spatial metaphor for his disruption of social norms. Throughout the play, characters enter and exit through different portals depending on their social status and moral trajectory. The broken bolt that allows Bluntschli's entry symbolizes the permeability of the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]]s' social defenses.

## 历史地理背景

- **历史事实**：Wealthy [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n families in the 1880s typically lived in large houses combining Ottoman architectural influences with European furnishings. The proximity to the Dragoman Pass — the main invasion route from Serbia — made such houses strategically significant during the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]].

- **剧中改编**：Shaw compresses the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] property into a single-stage set that shifts between acts. The real-world geography of the Dragoman Pass (about 40 km from Sofia, near the Serbian border) makes the Petkoff household a plausible place for a Serbian fugitive to seek shelter after the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]].

- [[captain-bluntschli]] — Intruder whose competence exposes the house's pretensions
