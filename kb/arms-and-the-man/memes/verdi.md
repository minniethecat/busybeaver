# Giuseppe Verdi

Giuseppe Verdi (1813–1901), the greatest Italian opera composer of the nineteenth century, is referenced in *Arms and the Man* as a constituent of [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]]'s romantic education. Alongside [[alexander-pushkin|Pushkin]] and [[lord-byron|Byron]], Verdi's operas supply the musical and emotional vocabulary through which Raina has learned to interpret love, war, and heroism.

## Verdi and Military Romance

Verdi's operas — particularly the early works that established his reputation (*Nabucco*, *Ernani*, *Il Trovatore*) — are saturated with the romantic military imagery Shaw's play dismantles: the heroic tenor who leads armies into battle, the noble baritone who dies for honor, the cavalry charge as the supreme expression of masculine virtue. Raina, intoxicated by Verdi's music, expects life to follow the operatic script. [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] is her comic comeuppance — the man who refuses to sing the aria she has rehearsed for him.

## Shaw and Opera

Shaw was one of the greatest music critics of his generation, and his relationship with Verdi was complex. He admired Verdi's late works — *Otello* and *Falstaff* — for their psychological depth and dramatic sophistication. But the early Verdi, the Verdi of martial choruses and heroic tenors, was for Shaw a prime example of the romantic machinery that filled young women's heads with dangerous fantasies. *Arms and the Man* is, in part, a critique of Verdi's librettists — the Scribes and Piaves who turned war into opera and love into a duet.

## Key Scene

The play itself is structured like a comic opera in reverse. Where an opera would end with the lovers united in a triumphant duet, Shaw ends with a hotel inventory. Where Verdi's hero would die for honor, Shaw's hero survives through pragmatism. The play is not an anti-opera — Shaw loved opera too much for that — but an opera rewritten by a critic who understood exactly how the form worked on its audience.
