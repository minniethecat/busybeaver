# Identity

Identity in *Arms and the Man* is not a fixed essence but a discovery — something characters must excavate from beneath layers of literary convention, social expectation, and self-performance. Shaw traces three distinct arcs of identity: Sergius's crisis of self-recognition as a "fraud," Raina's growth from romantic girl to realist woman, and Bluntschli's steady inhabitation of an identity he has never doubted. The play's comic structure is, at bottom, a drama of identity formation and recovery.

## Sergius: The Fraud's Self-Discovery

Sergius Saranoff's identity is built on a contradiction. He is celebrated as "the hero of Slivnitza" for a cavalry charge he privately knows was suicidal folly — an act that succeeded only because the enemy had the wrong cartridges (001-493). His self-description in Act II is a confession of radical fragmentation: "What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, say if he saw me now? What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here?" (002-508). The "half dozen Sergiuses" is identity as repertory company — a set of performed selves with no authentic core. His eventual crisis — "everything I think is mocked by everything I do" (003-612) — is the collapse of performed identity into acknowledged self-knowledge.

## Raina: From Romantic Girl to Realist Woman

Raina's identity transformation is the play's central developmental arc. She enters as a young woman who has constructed her self-image from Byron, Pushkin, and the opera at Bucharest — "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it" (001-030). The photograph of Sergius that she worships is a symbol of identity-before-knowledge: she loves an image, not a man. Bluntschli's entrance forces the first crack in this romantic identity, and the play traces her gradual reconstruction. Her confession — "I've always gone on like that — the noble attitude and the thrilling voice" (003-298) — is identity as self-diagnosis. Her final declaration — "To my chocolate cream soldier!" (003-1202) — announces her arrival at an identity she has chosen rather than inherited.

## Bluntschli: Identity as Self-Possession

Bluntschli stands outside the play's identity crises because he has never been in doubt about who he is. "I am a Swiss, fighting as a professional officer in the Servian army" (001-488) — the statement is factual, unapologetic, and complete. His identity does not depend on the recognition of others: he knows his age, his assets, his limitations, and his desires, and he acts on them. When Raina demands "Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three" (003-1111), she is demanding that he recognize her achieved identity — and his acknowledgment is the play's final act of mutual recognition.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw presents identity as an achievement rather than a given. The romantic culture that shapes Raina and Sergius offers ready-made identities — the hero, the heroine, the worshipper — that prove unlivable. The pragmatic alternative, embodied by Bluntschli, is identity through honest self-assessment: knowing what one is, admitting what one wants, and refusing to perform for an audience. The play's resolution suggests that genuine relationship is only possible between people who have achieved this self-knowledge — that identity must precede intimacy, not follow from it.
