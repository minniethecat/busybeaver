# Bulgarian National Revival

The Bulgarian National Revival (Vazrazhdane) was the long nineteenth-century process by which Bulgarians reasserted a distinct national identity after nearly five centuries of Ottoman rule. Beginning with the writing of Paisius of Hilendar's "Slavonic-Bulgarian History" in 1762, the Revival encompassed language standardization, educational reform, religious independence from the Greek Patriarchate, and ultimately revolutionary struggle for political autonomy.

By the 1880s, when Arms and the Man takes place, the Revival had achieved its political objectives: an autonomous Bulgarian principality existed, the Bulgarian Exarchate had been recognized, and a national consciousness had been forged. But the cultural momentum of the Revival continued. Bulgarian families like the Petkoffs were acutely aware of their national identity as something precious and recently won — which explains the patriotic intensity with which they discuss the war and Bulgarian military achievements.

This context illuminates the play's class dynamics. The Petkoffs' embrace of European culture — the Bucharest opera, Byron, Pushkin — is not a rejection of Bulgarian identity but an expression of it. The Revival had defined Bulgarian nationhood partly through cultural Europeanization, distancing the new nation from its Ottoman past. Raina's romantic idealism is thus not merely personal but national: it is the cultural program of a people defining themselves as modern Europeans.

Shaw treats this national pride with characteristic ambivalence. The Bulgarians' patriotism is genuine, but the romantic European culture they have imported to express it is exactly what Shaw's play sets out to dismantle. The collision between Bulgarian national feeling and Shaw's anti-romantic argument creates much of the play's comic tension.
