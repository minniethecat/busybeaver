# Courage: Sergius's Theatrical Courage

Major Sergius Saranoff is the play's romantic hero — and its most thoroughly dismantled figure. His celebrated cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] is the supreme image of martial courage in the play, narrated by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] as an epic of individual gallantry. But across three acts, Shaw systematically exposes this courage as theatrical performance, animal aggression, and — most damningly — a form of cowardice dressed in epaulettes.

## The Charge as Celebrated

The play's romantic conception of courage is established through the absent Sergius in Act I. His mother, [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], narrates the charge in the language of epic poetry:

> CATHERINE. He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns. （001-069 to 001-072）

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s ecstatic response — "It proves that all our ideas were real after all" （001-080） — establishes courage as the linchpin of the romantic worldview: if men can be heroes, then Byron and Pushkin and Verdi have not lied. Sergius's charge carries the entire weight of romantic idealism on its back.

## The Charge as Deconstructed

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s eyewitness account demolishes this image. The charge was tactically idiotic:

> BLUNTSCHLI. Is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? （001-448 to 001-451）

And the leader — the "bravest of the brave" — was not charging but being carried by a runaway horse:

> BLUNTSCHLI. Do you suppose the fellow wants to get there before the others and be killed? （001-474 to 001-475）

The [[don-quixote|Don Quixote]] comparison is Shaw's interpretative key. Bluntschli describes Sergius charging "like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills" （001-489 to 001-491）. Cervantes's hero is not a coward; he is a man of genuine courage who happens to be fighting windmills. Similarly, Sergius is not a fraud — his bravery in the charge was physiologically real — but the object of his bravery was an illusion, a romantic construct with no relationship to strategic reality.

The charge succeeded only because of a logistical error: "he and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all" （001-501 to 001-502）. The Servians had been sent the wrong cartridges.

## Sergius's Self-Knowledge

What makes Sergius a complex figure rather than a simple target is that he knows — or comes to know — the truth about his own courage. By Act II, four months after the war, he has resigned his commission, having discovered that his celebrated heroism was strategically worthless:

> SERGIUS. I won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way. （002-288 to 002-289）

His verdict on the military profession is now more cynical than Bluntschli's:

> SERGIUS. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak. That is the whole secret of successful fighting. Get your enemy at a disadvantage; and never, on any account, fight him on equal terms. （002-339 to 002-343）

This is, ironically, the same professional doctrine Bluntschli articulated in Act I — but where Bluntschli accepted it as a neutral fact of his trade, Sergius finds it morally repugnant. His romantic soul cannot reconcile itself to soldiering-as-business; he would rather resign than be "a tradesman" （002-348）.

The irony is double: Sergius's resignation is itself a form of moral courage, a refusal to participate in a system he now sees as fraudulent. Yet Shaw does not let him keep this dignity for long.

## The Great Speech on Courage

In Act III, [[louka|Louka]] asks Sergius the question the play has been circling since Act I: "I wonder are you really a brave man?" （003-540）. His answer is the longest speech on courage in the play, moving through three distinct positions:

First, he affirms his battlefield bravery as fact: "Yes: I am a brave man. My heart jumped like a woman's at the first shot; but in the charge I found that I was brave. Yes: that at least is real about me" （003-542 to 003-544）.

But [[louka|Louka]] immediately undercuts this by introducing class: "Did you find in the charge that the men whose fathers are poor like mine were any less brave than the men who are rich like you?" （003-546 to 003-547）.

This question transforms the speech. Sergius admits that all the soldiers — rich and poor — "slashed and cursed and yelled like heroes" （003-549）. But then he pivots to the play's key distinction:

> SERGIUS. Psha! the courage to rage and kill is cheap. I have an English bull terrier who has as much of that sort of courage as the whole Bulgarian nation, and the whole Russian nation at its back. But he lets my groom thrash him, all the same. That's your soldier all over! No, Louka, your poor men can cut throats; but they are afraid of their officers; they put up with insults and blows; they stand by and see one another punished like children — aye, and help to do it when they are ordered. And the officers! — well, I am an officer. （003-550 to 003-558）

## The Higher Definition and Its Failure

Sergius arrives, through his own bitter experience, at Shaw's position — a nobler definition of courage:

> SERGIUS. Oh, give me the man who will defy to the death any power on earth or in heaven that sets itself up against his own will and conscience: he alone is the brave man. （003-558 to 003-560）

This is magnificent, and it would be the play's last word on courage — except that [[louka|Louka]] immediately turns it back on him. If courage means defying power, then she — the servant who refuses to be a servant — is the truly brave one, not the major who can resign his commission with his class status intact:

> LOUKA. How easy it is to talk! Men never seem to me to grow up: they all have schoolboy's ideas. You don't know what true courage is. （003-562 to 003-563）

Sergius can articulate the definition of true courage but cannot live it. His class position protects him from the consequences of defiance that Louka risks every day. His courage is a theory; hers is a practice. The play's final judgment on Sergius is that he is a man who knows what courage is but lacks the moral courage to embody it.

## The Paradox of Sergius

Sergius's tragedy — and Shaw's sophistication — lies in the paradox: he is genuinely brave on the battlefield, genuinely insightful about the nature of courage, and genuinely incapable of the moral courage his own definition demands. He can charge machine guns but cannot defy social convention. His celebrated courage is revealed as the cheaper form, while the courage he lacks — the courage to follow conscience against power — is the form the play values most.
