# The Realist as Hero in Arms and the Man

## Thesis

*Arms and the Man* does not merely satirise the romantic hero — it proposes a replacement. Captain Bluntschli is Shaw's first fully realised "realist hero," a figure who would recur throughout his dramatic career (Caesar in *Caesar and Cleopatra*, Undershaft in *Major Barbara*, Tanner in *Man and Superman*). The realist hero is defined not by courage, honour, or passion — the traditional heroic virtues — but by competence, honesty, and an unillusioned understanding of how the world actually works. Shaw's innovation is not simply anti-romantic (many playwrights had mocked romantic conventions before him) but constructive: he builds a protagonist who is genuinely admirable without being in any traditional sense heroic.

## The Problem of the Romantic Hero

Shaw's critique of the romantic hero is concentrated in the figure of Sergius Saranoff. Sergius possesses every quality of the traditional stage hero: physical beauty, military glory, aristocratic bearing, romantic passion. His celebrated cavalry charge — "the first man in Europe" — is the kind of exploit around which heroic narratives are constructed. And yet, as the play systematically demonstrates, Sergius is incompetent, hypocritical, and self-deceived.

The cavalry charge that made his reputation was a professional blunder: "He ought to be court-martialled for it" [001-141]. The enemy had the wrong ammunition and could not fire; Sergius's reckless charge succeeded by accident, not by design. The heroic narrative — the dashing cavalry officer leading his men to victory — is a fiction that the facts cannot sustain. But the fiction persists because Sergius, and the society that celebrates him, needs it to persist.

Sergius's romantic passion is equally hollow. His "higher love" for Raina is a rhetorical performance that collapses under scrutiny. He pursues Louka not out of genuine feeling but out of the same impulse that drove his cavalry charge: the need to perform heroism before an audience. Sergius is not a villain; he is a man trapped inside a heroic narrative that his own experience constantly contradicts.

## The Construction of the Realist Hero

Bluntschli is introduced as the anti-Sergius: unwashed, unheroic, middle-aged, and professionally competent. His entrance is a systematic negation of heroic convention. Where the stage hero should be clean, Bluntschli is dirty. Where he should be young and beautiful, he is "a man of about thirty-five" and unremarkable. Where he should be driven by passion or honour, he is driven by professional survival — he climbed a water-pipe to escape pursuit, not out of cowardice but because his professional judgment told him the battle was lost.

But Shaw does not stop at negation. Bluntschli possesses positive virtues that the play presents as genuinely admirable:

**Competence.** Bluntschli knows his profession. He can identify the calibre of enemy guns by listening to the battle; he can estimate troop movements from the sound of artillery. His competence is not theatrical — it produces no glorious cavalry charges — but it is real. When Bluntschli speaks about soldiering, he speaks with the authority of someone who understands his trade.

**Honesty.** Bluntschli does not deceive himself. He knows he is a mercenary, not a patriot. He knows he carries chocolates instead of cartridges. He knows he climbed a water-pipe to save his skin. His self-knowledge is not flattering — it is accurate. In a play full of characters performing identities they cannot sustain, Bluntschli's refusal to perform is his defining virtue.

**Practicality.** Bluntschli's famous chocolates are a synecdoche for his worldview. He carries what sustains him, not what destroys his enemies. His proposal to Raina enumerates his professional assets — a hotel business, a modest inheritance — rather than declaring eternal passion. The practical is, for Shaw, the ethical: the man who accurately assesses his resources and acts accordingly is more trustworthy than the man who makes grand promises he cannot keep.

**Equanimity.** Bluntschli does not panic. When Sergius challenges him to a duel, his response is practical rather than theatrical: he knows Sergius is a better swordsman, and he declines on rational grounds. His refusal to be drawn into heroic postures — even when social convention demands them — is a form of moral courage that the play presents as superior to physical bravery.

## The Realist Hero and the Social Order

Bluntschli's outsider status is essential to his function. He is Swiss, not Bulgarian; a mercenary, not a patriot; a hotelier's son, not an aristocrat. His nationality, profession, and class all position him outside the Bulgarian social order that produces Sergiuses. The realist hero, in Shaw's construction, cannot emerge from within the system he critiques — he must arrive from outside, carrying chocolates and speaking uncomfortable truths.

This outsider status has a political dimension. Shaw's Fabian socialism was committed to the proposition that institutions could be reformed by people who understood how they actually worked. The realist hero is the Fabian ideal translated into dramatic form: a figure who sees through ideological mystification to the material reality beneath, and who acts on that understanding with professional competence.

Bluntschli's commercial background — his father owns hotels, and Bluntschli himself is a businessman as much as a soldier — is not incidental. The realist hero is also, in Shaw's construction, the bourgeois hero. He represents the values of the commercial middle class — thrift, competence, honesty, practicality — against the values of the aristocratic Sergius — honour, glory, passion, display. Shaw's preference is unequivocal: the bourgeois virtues are the better foundation for a decent life.

## The Limits of the Realist Hero

Shaw is too honest a dramatist to present Bluntschli as an unqualified ideal. The realist hero has limitations:

Bluntschli's practicality borders on emotional poverty. His proposal to Raina — enumerating his assets like a business prospectus — is comically unromantic. Shaw intends the comedy, but he also intends the critique: a world of pure practicality would be efficient but arid. Bluntschli needs Raina's warmth — her residual romanticism, her capacity for genuine feeling — as much as she needs his clarity.

Bluntschli's professionalism is ethically ambiguous. He fights for whoever pays him; his competence has no moral content beyond its own exercise. The mercenary is, by definition, a professional without a cause. Shaw does not resolve this ambiguity — he presents it as a fact about the modern world. Professional competence is preferable to romantic incompetence, but it is not a complete moral philosophy.

The play's ending — Bluntschli and Raina engaged, Sergius and Louka paired — is a compromise formation. The realist hero gets the girl, but the girl is herself a recovering romantic. The romantic hero gets the servant, but the servant is in many ways his superior. No one gets exactly what they wanted, and everyone gets something they can live with. The realist hero, like realism itself, is not a utopia — it is a workable arrangement.

## The Legacy

Bluntschli is Shaw's first realist hero, and the type would evolve throughout his career. Caesar (1898) is the realist hero as world-historical figure — competent, unsentimental, and surrounded by romantics who cannot understand him. Undershaft (1905) is the realist hero as arms manufacturer — morally troubling, professionally brilliant, and as honest about his trade as Bluntschli is about his. Tanner (1903) is the realist hero as philosopher — a man whose clarity of thought is both his gift and his limitation.

What unites these figures is Shaw's conviction that the most radical thing a man can be is honest — honest about his profession, his motives, his limitations, and his desserts. The realist hero does not save the world; he navigates it competently, tells the truth about what he sees, and declines to participate in the fictions that sustain everyone else's self-regard. For Shaw, writing at the end of the Victorian era, this was heroism enough.
