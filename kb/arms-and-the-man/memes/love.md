# Love

## Definition

**Love** in *Arms and the Man* is subjected to the same anti-romantic scrutiny that Shaw applies to war. The play presents a taxonomy of love: romantic infatuation (Raina's initial feeling for Sergius), the idealised "higher love" (Sergius's self-serving philosophy), practical partnership (Bluntschli's proposal), and strategic alliance (Louka's pursuit of Sergius). Each variety is exposed not as a pure emotion but as a social performance shaped by class position, gender expectation, and self-deception. Shaw's argument is that love, like soldiering, is better served by honesty and practicality than by poetic posturing.

## In the Play

Raina's opening scene establishes romantic love as a theatrical performance. She is described as "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it"（001-030—001-032）, framing her affection for Sergius as an aesthetic experience rather than a genuine emotional connection. Her love is directed at the *idea* of the hero, not the man himself — a point the play systematically unravels. The stage direction indicates that "she takes it in her hands and elevates it like a priestess"（001-170—001-171）, making the religious analogy explicit: Raina worships an image.

Sergius's philosophy of the "higher love" is Shaw's most explicit satire of romantic ideology. In Act II, Sergius declares:

> "I think we two have found the higher love. When I think of you, I feel that I could never do a base deed, or think an ignoble thought."（002-462—002-464）

The speech is delivered with solemn self-regard, and Shaw immediately undercuts it through Louka's knowing response. The "higher love" is exposed as a rhetorical device that allows Sergius to feel noble while acting selfishly — it is love as alibi.

Louka's counter-argument is devastating:

> "What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now?"（002-509）

Her challenge exposes the performative nature of Sergius's idealism — it depends on an audience and collapses under scrutiny. Louka understands something Sergius does not: that "higher love" is a luxury available only to those who can afford to ignore practical realities.

Bluntschli's proposal to Raina represents the play's alternative model of love: practical, honest, and devoid of romantic pretence. He reminds Raina of their first encounter in terms that strip away all romance:

> "I appealed to you as a fugitive, a beggar, and a starving man. You accepted me. You gave me your hand to kiss, your bed to sleep in, and your roof to shelter me"（003-1191—003-1193）

When she protests that she "did not give them to the Emperor of Switzerland," he seizes the moment with confident mastery, demanding: "Now tell us who you did give them to"（003-1198—003-1200）. Her answer — "To my chocolate cream soldier!"（003-1201—003-1202）— is the play's affirmative vision of love stripped of romantic pretence. Bluntschli does not declare love; he negotiates it, with the same professional competence he brings to soldiering. His nationality (Swiss), profession (mercenary), and pragmatic outlook all mark him as an outsider to the Bulgarian romantic conventions that entrap the other characters.

## The Economics of Love

Shaw embeds a class analysis in his treatment of love. For the Petkoffs, love is a matter of sentiment precisely because their material position is secure. For Louka, love cannot be separated from ambition — she pursues Sergius not despite but because of what he represents socially. Her pursuit is clear-eyed about both her ambition and his weakness. The distinction between attraction and ambition is deliberately ambiguous in Shaw's treatment — in a class-bound society, he implies, the distinction is artificial.

Nicola articulates the servant's view of love most explicitly. His professional philosophy is encapsulated in his response to Louka's accusation: "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola," to which he replies with perfect satisfaction: "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-079—002-081）. Shaw presents this not as cynical but as realistic: servants cannot afford the luxury of disinterested love. Nicola's engagement to Louka is an economic arrangement that either party can exit when a better prospect appears — love as employment contract.

## Key Characters

- **Raina Petkoff** — moves from romantic infatuation to mature affection; her arc is a love story told in reverse, beginning with worship of a portrait（001-170—001-171）and ending with honest, amused acceptance of her "chocolate cream soldier"（003-1201—003-1202）
- **Sergius Saranoff** — the self-proclaimed apostle of the "higher love"（002-462—002-464）; his romantic philosophy is a cover for self-interest, deployed differently with Raina (idealised) and Louka (physical)
- **Captain Bluntschli** — offers practical partnership instead of romance; his matter-of-fact proposal is the play's model of honest love, built on mutual respect rather than mutual illusion. His profession is explicit: "I am only a Swiss, fighting merely as a professional soldier"（001-361—001-362）
- **Louka** — treats love as strategic alliance; her pursuit of Sergius is clear-eyed about both her ambition and his weakness, representing love as social mobility. She challenges the "half dozen Sergiuses" who admire her figure（002-510—002-511）
- **Nicola** — the servant who explicitly theorises love as a professional transaction; his response to Louka's scorn defines his philosophy: "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-081）

## Key Scenes

Raina's worship of Sergius's portrait in Act I（001-170—001-171）establishes romantic love as idol-worship — directed at an image rather than a person. The stage direction comparing her to a priestess makes the critique explicit.

Sergius and Louka's scene in Act II（002-462—002-511）is the play's most concentrated examination of love's varieties: Sergius's "higher love" rhetoric collides with Louka's pragmatic understanding of attraction and ambition. The scene exposes the class dimension of romantic ideology — Sergius can afford to be idealistic because his material position is secure.

Bluntschli's proposal scene（003-1121—003-1202）inverts every romantic convention. He enumerates his assets (two hundred horses, carriages, tablecloths), reminds Raina of their first encounter in bluntly unromantic terms, and treats marriage as a sensible arrangement between equals. Raina's acceptance — amused, exasperated, and finally sincere — is the play's affirmative vision of love stripped of romantic pretence.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw rejects romantic love as a dangerous fiction that prevents people from seeing each other clearly. But his critique is not merely negative — the play offers an alternative model of love based on honesty, practicality, and mutual respect. Bluntschli and Raina's engagement is presented as a sensible partnership between two people who have finally stopped performing for each other.

Crucially, Shaw does not sentimentalise this alternative. Bluntschli's proposal is comically unromantic, and Raina's acceptance involves a certain deflation of her romantic expectations. The play's happy ending is muted, qualified, and deliberately anti-climactic — a marriage of convenience that is also, paradoxically, a marriage of genuine affection. Shaw's point is that genuine affection is only possible once the performance of romance has been abandoned.

## Critical Perspective

The treatment of love in *Arms and the Man* has attracted sustained critical attention since the play's premiere in 1894. Early reviewers noted Shaw's debt to Ibsen in subjecting romantic conventions to rational scrutiny, though Shaw himself distinguished his comic method from Ibsen's tragic mode. The play's taxonomy of love — romantic, idealised, pragmatic, strategic — anticipates Shaw's later, more explicit treatments of the "life force" in *Man and Superman*, where the relation between sexual attraction and rational partnership receives fuller philosophical elaboration.

Eric Bentley, in *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration* (1947), identified the play's anti-romantic treatment of love as central to Shaw's broader project of replacing the nineteenth-century "well-made play" with a drama of ideas. Bentley argued that Shaw's comic deflation of romantic love serves a serious philosophical purpose: to demonstrate that genuine human connection depends on clear-sightedness rather than illusion. The famous chocolate-cream soldier motif, in Bentley's reading, functions as an emblem of the substitution of practical nourishment for romantic starvation.

Later feminist criticism has complicated this reading. Scholars including Sally Peters and J. Ellen Gainor have examined the play's gender politics, noting that while Shaw subjects romantic ideology to critique, his alternative — Bluntschli's practical partnership — remains embedded in patriarchal structures. Raina's agency is restored not through her own initiative but through Bluntschli's confident mastery in the proposal scene. Louka's strategic use of sexuality and secrets, meanwhile, represents a more radical challenge to the romantic order, though one that Shaw ultimately contains within a conventional marriage plot.

The class dimension of Shaw's love taxonomy has received particular attention from Marxist and materialist critics. Alick West argued that Shaw's treatment of love in the play is an allegory of class relations: the Petkoffs can afford romantic sentiment because their material position is secure, while the servants' relationships are necessarily transactional. This reading locates Shaw's Fabian socialism in the play's structure rather than its explicit argument, seeing the marriage of Raina (the aristocratic idealist) to Bluntschli (the bourgeois professional) as a symbolic resolution of class contradiction through the institution of marriage — a resolution that Shaw's later plays would increasingly question.
