# The Economics of Love

Shaw embeds a sharp class analysis in his treatment of love. For the Petkoffs, love is a matter of sentiment precisely because their material position is secure. For Louka and Nicola, love cannot be separated from ambition and survival — the distinction between attraction and ambition is, in a class-bound society, artificial.

## The Servant's View

Nicola articulates the servant's philosophy most explicitly. When Louka accuses him, "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola," he replies with perfect satisfaction: "Yes: that's the secret of success in service" (002-079 to 002-081). Shaw presents this not as cynical but as realistic: servants cannot afford the luxury of disinterested love.

Nicola's engagement to Louka is an economic arrangement that either party can exit when a better prospect appears — love as employment contract. He tells Louka explicitly: "I don't ask the reason now that you cry off. I'm a professional. I know my duty. You have a great future before you if you are careful" (003-764).

## Louka's Strategic Alliance

[[louka|Louka]] treats love as strategic mobility. Her pursuit of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is clear-eyed about both her ambition and his weakness. She understands that "higher love" is a luxury available only to those who can afford to ignore practical realities. Her devastating question — "What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now?" (002-509) — exposes the class dimension of romantic ideology.

## The Petkoff Privilege

For [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], love can be a matter of ideals precisely because they never face material precarity. Raina's romantic worship of a photograph (001-170), Sergius's "higher love" rhetoric (002-462), and the operatic conventions they inhabit are all funded by the security of the [[petkoff-house]] and Major Petkoff's military rank. Their romanticism is, in part, a class performance.

## Shaw's Point

Shaw's Fabian socialism informs the argument: in a society where material security is unequally distributed, love itself is unequally available as a "pure" emotion. The servants' relationships are necessarily transactional; the masters' relationships are only apparently disinterested. By exposing this asymmetry, Shaw links his critique of romantic love to a broader critique of class society.
