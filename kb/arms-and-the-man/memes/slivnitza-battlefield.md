# Slivnitza Battlefield

The battlefield of Slivnitza, located in western [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] approximately 30 kilometers northwest of [[sofia|Sofia]], was the site of the decisive engagement of the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] (November 17–19, 1885). It is the place where [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] led his famous [[cavalry-charge|cavalry charge]] and where [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] served as a Servian artillery officer — the shared experience that links the play's two male protagonists before either has met [[raina-petkoff|Raina]].

## The Battle

The [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] was a three-day engagement in which Bulgarian forces, despite being outnumbered and abandoned by their Russian officers, defeated the invading Servian army. The battle was the turning point of the war: after Slivnitza, the Bulgarians pursued the retreating Servians across the border, and the war ended within two weeks.

## The Terrain

The battlefield's terrain — rolling hills, scattered villages, the Slivnitza ridge — was suited to the kind of engagement the play dissects. Cavalry charges across open ground against prepared positions, artillery positioned on the heights, infantry holding the line: the battle was a textbook nineteenth-century engagement, the kind that military textbooks would analyze and military romanticists would celebrate.

## The Battlefield as Memory

In the play, Slivnitza exists only in memory — in [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romanticized account, in Bluntschli's professional debunking, in Sergius's self-justifying narrative. The physical battlefield is never depicted; it is reconstructed, contested, and finally dissolved by the play's comic analysis. Shaw's point is that battlefields exist primarily in the stories told about them — and that those stories are almost always false.
