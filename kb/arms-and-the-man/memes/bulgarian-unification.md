# Bulgarian Unification (1885)

The unification of the Principality of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] and the autonomous province of Eastern Rumelia on September 6, 1885, was the event that precipitated the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]] — and thus, indirectly, the entire action of *Arms and the Man*. A bloodless coup by Bulgarian nationalists in Plovdiv, the capital of Eastern Rumelia, proclaimed the union of the two Bulgarian territories under Prince Alexander of Battenberg. [[servia|Servia]], fearing a powerful Bulgaria on its border, declared war two months later.

## The Treaty Legacy

The [[treaty-of-berlin|Treaty of Berlin]] (1878) had divided Bulgarian territory into three parts to prevent the emergence of a large Bulgarian state that would be, in effect, a Russian satellite. Bulgarian nationalists never accepted this partition. The unification of 1885 was an act of defiance against the Great Power settlement — a declaration that Bulgaria would determine its own borders.

The Great Powers' response was mixed. Russia, which preferred a compliant Bulgarian client state, was hostile. Britain, which feared Russian expansion more than Bulgarian unification, was sympathetic. Austria-Hungary, which had its own Balkan ambitions, was wary. The diplomatic confusion that followed the unification enabled Bulgaria to consolidate its position despite the Servian attack.

## The War

The Serbo-Bulgarian War lasted only two weeks (November 14–28, 1885) but established Bulgaria as the dominant power in the [[balkans|Balkans]]. The Bulgarian victory at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] — the battle in which [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] performed his famous cavalry charge — was the decisive engagement. Servia, which had expected an easy victory over a Bulgarian army stripped of its Russian officers, was routed.

## In the Play

The unification is the play's invisible catalyst. Without it, there would be no war; without the war, no cavalry charge; without the charge, no heroic Sergius to inspire [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic imagination. Shaw takes the unification as given — the characters discuss the war as a recent event, its causes already receding into the background. This historical casualness is itself a Shawian point: wars are always about something, but what they're about is quickly forgotten by those who fight them.
