# Garden

The garden of [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s house is the setting for Act II of *Arms and the Man*. Shaw's stage directions describe "a fine spring morning; and the garden looks fresh and pretty" [002-001-002], establishing an exterior space of natural renewal that ironically frames the act's revelations of [[self-deception|social hypocrisy]] and class tension.

## A Stage for Class Collision

The garden is where the play's [[social-class|class]] boundaries are most visibly transgressed. It is in the garden that [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] flirts with [[louka|Louka]], the family's servant — a scene whose impropriety is heightened by its semi-public setting. Louka enters from the garden gateway [002-559], and the spatial arrangement — servants' gate encroaching on the family's garden — materialises the class mobility that drives the subplot.

## Spring and Irony

The garden's springtime beauty — "fruit bushes along the walls" [002-008], "a wooden garden seat" [002-014] — creates [[irony|dramatic irony]] with the interpersonal conflicts staged within it. While nature renews itself, the characters remain trapped in patterns of deception and self-delusion. Sergius paces "up and down about the garden in a brown study" [002-683], physically restless but psychologically stagnant.

## Contrast with the Library

The garden serves as the exterior counterpart to the [[library]], which houses Acts I and III. Where the library is a space of concealment — curtains, closed doors, hidden soldiers — the garden is a space of exposure. It is here, in the open air, that the characters' true desires and class tensions become visible. The movement from interior (Act I) to exterior (Act II) and back to interior (Act III) structures the play's rhythm of concealment and revelation.
