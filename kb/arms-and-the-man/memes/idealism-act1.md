# Idealism in Act I

Idealism is established as [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s defining characteristic — and as a problem the play means to solve. The opening stage direction places her on a balcony, "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it" (001-030~031). She is not merely experiencing beauty; she is performing the role of a romantic heroine, watching herself be watched. Her bedchamber is decorated with a portrait of Sergius, "a box of chocolate creams, and a miniature easel" (001-023) — the props of a consciously constructed romantic identity.

## Doubt Beneath the Surface

The key exposition comes in her conversation with [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]. Raina's rhapsody about [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge — "Our ideas of what Sergius would do — our patriotism — our heroic ideals" (001-085) — is immediately followed by confession. She has doubted: "perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that — indeed never, as far as I knew it then" (001-095~098). This is a remarkable moment of dramatic self-awareness. Raina knows — or half-knows — that her ideals are literary constructs. But Shaw shows that knowing is not enough; the idealist's attachment to the beautiful fiction overrides the evidence.

## The Collision with Reality

The arrival of [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] — exhausted, hungry, carrying chocolate instead of cartridges — is the play's first direct collision between ideal and real. Raina's response is not mere surprise but outrage: Shaw's stage direction reads, "*outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood*. Chocolate! Do you stuff your pockets with sweets — like a schoolboy — even in the field?" (001-398~399). The parenthetical is crucial: it is not her safety that is threatened but her *ideals of manhood*. A soldier who eats chocolate is not the Byronic hero she has been trained to expect, and the dissonance registers as moral offense.

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] then delivers the play's most devastating anti-idealist testimony. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s celebrated cavalry charge was, in his account, an act of suicidal foolishness: "He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We nearly burst with laughter at him" (001-489~491). Worse, the charge only succeeded because the Servian artillery had been sent the wrong ammunition — the idealist is saved from the consequences of his folly by a lucky accident. Bluntschli's verdict is unambiguous: "Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all" (001-500~502).

Raina's response is not to reconsider her ideals but to defend them: the stage direction reads "*deeply wounded, but steadfastly loyal to her ideals*" (001-504). This is Shaw's diagnosis in miniature: the idealist, confronted with disconfirming evidence, does not revise the ideal but doubles down on it. Idealism is self-immunizing.
