# Vienna Opera

The opera culture of Vienna and Bucharest provides the soundtrack to Raina Petkoff's romantic education. In her Act I confession to her mother, Raina identifies the source of her "heroic ideals" with precision: "perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we were so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest" (001-096). The opera, for Shaw, is not innocent entertainment but a factory of romantic ideology — a cultural institution that teaches young women to expect tenors in uniform and young men to perform as heroes.

## The Cultural Geography of Opera

Late nineteenth-century Eastern European opera culture was centered on Vienna and radiated outward to cities like Bucharest, Budapest, and Sofia. Vienna's Court Opera (the Wiener Hofoper) was one of Europe's leading houses, producing works by Verdi, Wagner, and the verismo school. Bucharest, as the capital of newly independent Romania, cultivated an operatic season as a marker of cosmopolitan sophistication. For a Bulgarian family like the Petkoffs, attendance at the Bucharest opera was both a cultural education and a class performance — a declaration that they belonged to European civilization, not merely Balkan provincialism.

## Opera as Ideological Machinery

Shaw, who was himself a distinguished music critic before he became a playwright, understood opera as ideological machinery. The standard repertoire of the period — Verdi's *Ernani*, *Il Trovatore*, *La Traviata*; Donizetti's *Lucia di Lammermoor* — presented a world of noble heroes, tragic love, and transcendent passion. Raina has absorbed this world so thoroughly that she cannot distinguish between operatic convention and human reality. When she exclaims "the world is really a glorious world for women who can see its glory and men who can act its romance" (001-110), she is quoting the libretto of her own imagination. The opera at Bucharest has taught her a script that real life cannot follow.

## Bluntschli's Operatic Critique

Bluntschli's description of Sergius's cavalry charge uses operatic language as critique: "He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We did laugh" (001-489). The comparison — "like an operatic tenor" — is devastating because it collapses the distinction between heroic action and theatrical performance. The cavalry charge that made Sergius a national hero is, in Bluntschli's professional assessment, indistinguishable from a Verdi aria — impressive to watch, absurd to analyze. Shaw's argument is that opera culture has taught an entire generation to mistake performance for substance.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of opera culture is ambiguous. As a music critic, he admired Verdi and championed Wagner; he was not anti-opera but anti-operatic — opposed to the habits of mind that opera, in its popular reception, encouraged. The "heroic ideals" that Raina absorbs from the Bucharest opera are not the opera's fault but the audience's: she has consumed tragic passion as a consumer good and mistaken it for life instruction. The play's resolution — Raina choosing a Swiss hotelier's son over a Byronic cavalryman — is her liberation from the operatic script. Shaw, the Fabian reformer, replaces the opera house with the hotel, spectacle with commerce, aria with conversation.
