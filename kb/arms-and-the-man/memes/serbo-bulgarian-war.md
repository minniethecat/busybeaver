The Serbo-Bulgarian War (November 14, 1885 – March 3, 1886) was a short but consequential Balkan conflict that pitted the Kingdom of Serbia against the recently unified Principality of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]. Serbia, backed by Austria-Hungary, invaded to prevent [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n territorial expansion after the unification of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] and Eastern Rumelia. The war ended in a decisive and unexpected [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n victory, symbolized by the [[battle-of-slivnitza]], and established [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] as a serious Balkan power. For Shaw, this war was ideal dramatic material: a real conflict, fresh in European memory, in which the "romantic" side (the plucky, outnumbered [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]ns) won — making it the perfect vehicle for an argument that even "glorious" victories are accidents of logistics, not triumphs of national spirit.

## 历史事实

- **时间**：November 14, 1885 – March 3, 1886 (armistice December 1885; formal peace March 1886)
- **地点**：Western [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], along the Serbian-[[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n border, with the decisive engagement at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]]
- **参战方/参与者**：Kingdom of Serbia (invaders, backed by Austria-Hungary) vs. Principality of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] (defenders). Serbian King Milan I initiated the war to check [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n expansion after the unification of [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] and Eastern Rumelia (September 1885), which violated the Treaty of Berlin (1878).
- **结果**：Decisive [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n victory. Serbian forces were defeated at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]] (November 17–19) and pushed back across the border. The Treaty of Bucharest (March 3, 1886) restored the pre-war status quo. [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]'s unexpected success established its reputation as "the Prussia of the Balkans."

The war lasted only two weeks of active combat. Serbia invaded with approximately 40,000 troops, expecting a quick victory over the newly formed [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n state. Instead, [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n forces — many of them hastily mobilized militia — held defensive positions at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]] and counterattacked successfully. International pressure, particularly from Austria-Hungary, prevented [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] from pressing its advantage into Serbian territory.

## 在剧中的呈现

### 直接提及

Shaw integrates the war into nearly every scene through casual reference rather than exposition. The characters speak of "the war" as a shared experience that has transformed [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n society. The first sign of the war's end comes from the household servants:

> LOUKA. Master! back from the war! （002-087）

[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] then announces the formal conclusion:

> PETKOFF. Yes, Louka's been looking after me. The war's over. The treaty was signed three days ago at Bucharest; and the decree for our army to demobilize was issued yesterday. （002-136–138）

The peace is received not with relief but with indignation by [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], whose martial ambition far outstrips her husband's:

> CATHERINE. The war over! Paul: have you let the Austrians force you to make peace? （002-140–141）

> PETKOFF. My dear: they didn't consult me. What could I do? （002-143）

> CATHERINE. You could have annexed Servia and made Prince Alexander Emperor of the Balkans. That's what I would have done. （002-153–154）

This exchange epitomizes Shaw's satire: the civilians who experience war secondhand are more bellicose than the soldiers who fought it. [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s perspective is the opposite — war as exhaustion:

> MAN. I've not had two hours' undisturbed sleep since the war began. I'm on my way to get some sleep now. （001-577）

> MAN. I've served fourteen years: half of your fellows never smelt powder before. Why, how is it that you've just beaten us? Sheer ignorance of the art of war, nothing else. I never saw anything so unprofessional. （001-441–444）

For the [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]], war is not glory but fatigue — a job that interferes with basic human needs. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], having seen the reality behind his own legend, reaches a similar conclusion by Act II:

> SERGIUS. Soldiering, my dear madam, is the coward's art of attacking mercilessly when you are strong, and keeping out of harm's way when you are weak. That is the whole secret of successful fighting. （002-339–342）

The war's international character — fought by proxy officers — is noted with casual cynicism by [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]:

> PETKOFF. We shouldn't have been able to begin fighting if these foreigners hadn't shewn us how to do it: we knew nothing about it; and neither did the Servians. Egad, there'd have been no war without them. （002-364–366）

### 间接影响

The war's social consequences are everywhere in the play: [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] returns with enhanced status and a new wardrobe; the household's servants have managed the estate during his absence; [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n patriotism has been inflamed to the point where a Serbian uniform is a death warrant. The war has accelerated social changes — servant agency, class mobility, the questioning of aristocratic authority — that the characters are still processing.

Shaw's most pointed war reference comes through [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s professional assessment: for the professional soldier, war is not glory but exhaustion — a job that interferes with basic human needs. The contrast between [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]'s hunger for annexation and Bluntschli's hunger for sleep encapsulates the play's anti-war argument: those who romanticize war are those farthest from its reality.

## 戏剧功能

- **情节推进**：The war is the engine of the entire plot. It creates [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s fugitive status — he "escaped after Slivnitza" （002-385）, establishes [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s heroic legend, brings the characters together in the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] for the aftermath, and provides the peace treaty that enables [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s return as a respectable visitor rather than a hunted enemy.

- **人物塑造**：The war reveals the gap between each character's public performance and private reality. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] plays the hero; [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] does his job; [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] enjoys the social perks; [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] consumes the spectacle. Only the servants — [[louka|Louka]] and [[nicola|Nicola]] — are unaffected by war fever, because social ambition is more immediate to them than national glory.

- **主题服务**：Shaw uses the Serbo-Bulgarian War to prosecute his case against romantic militarism. By choosing a war that [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]] *won* — a war that produced real heroes and real victories — Shaw denies his critics the argument that he is simply mocking failure. His point is more radical: even successful wars are stupid, and even genuine victories are accidents dressed up as destiny.

## 延伸阅读

- Crampton, R. J. *A Concise History of Bulgaria*. 2nd ed., Cambridge University Press, 2005. — Chapters 5–6 provide the definitive English-language account of the Serbo-Bulgarian War, including the diplomatic context of Bulgarian unification and the Great Power response.
- Jelavich, Barbara. *History of the Balkans: Eighteenth and Nineteenth Centuries*. Cambridge University Press, 1983. — Situates the 1885 war within the broader pattern of Balkan nation-state formation and Great Power intervention.
- Holroyd, Michael. *Bernard Shaw: The Search for Love, 1856–1898*. Chatto & Windus, 1988. — Contextualizes Shaw's choice of the Serbo-Bulgarian War as a deliberate anti-jingoist gesture during the late-Victorian cult of military heroism.
- Morgan, Margery M. *The Shavian Playground: An Exploration of the Art of George Bernard Shaw*. Methuen, 1972. — Analyzes the war's function as backdrop in *Arms and the Man* and Shaw's technique of using recent history to deflate romantic nationalism.
