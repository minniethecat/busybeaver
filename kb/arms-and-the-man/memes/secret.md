# Secret

## Definition

In *Arms and the Man*, **secrets** are the structural spine of the plot and a thematic device through which Shaw explores the distance between public performance and private truth. Every major character harbours a secret, and the play's comic resolution depends on secrets being revealed, confessed, or strategically deployed. Secrecy is the mechanism by which Shaw demonstrates that the romantic ideals his characters profess are unsustainable fictions. But secrecy also has a class dimension: who gets to keep secrets, who must know them, and who can weaponise them are questions that map directly onto the play's social hierarchy.

## In the Play

Raina's central secret — that she harboured a fugitive enemy soldier in her bedroom — drives the entire plot. The secret is multiply layered: it is a military secret (harbouring an enemy), a sexual secret (a man in her bedroom), and a class secret (a gentlewoman sheltering a mercenary). The physical act of concealment is the play's originating comic image:

> "I'll help you. Hide yourself, oh, hide yourself, quick, behind the curtain."（001-288—001-290）

Louka, the servant, knows this secret and weaponises it. Her knowing aside to the audience — "secretively, laughs to herself, and goes out"（001-348）— establishes her as the play's custodian of hidden truths. The knowledge asymmetry between servant and mistress is one of Shaw's most pointed social critiques — the servant class knows the family's secrets because servants are invisible presences in the household.

Bluntschli's return is itself a secret visit: "I had better not stay here secretly longer than is necessary"（001-711—001-712）. His pragmatic caution contrasts with Raina's more theatrical approach to concealment. Where Raina treats secrets as romantic drama, Bluntschli treats them as tactical necessities — a professional soldier's understanding of operational security applied to a domestic situation.

## Secrets as Social Currency

Shaw embeds a class analysis in the play's treatment of secrecy. Louka explicitly invokes the power of hidden knowledge:

> "I know some family secrets they wouldn't care to have told, young as I am. Let them quarrel with me if they dare!"（002-048—002-049）

Nicola raises the stakes further, articulating the servant's comprehensive knowledge of the family's hidden lives:

> "I know things about the mistress that she wouldn't have the master know for a thousand levas. I know things about him that she wouldn't let him hear the last of for six months if I blabbed them to her. I know things about Raina that would break off her match with Sergius if—"（002-064—002-067）

This passage is Shaw's most explicit statement of the servant class's power through knowledge. Nicola's knowing silence is a form of professional discretion that Shaw portrays as both pragmatic and morally ambiguous — he keeps secrets not out of loyalty but out of calculation. Silence is an investment in future advantage.

Louka's trajectory demonstrates that in a hierarchical society, knowledge is a form of capital that can be converted into social mobility. Her gradual revelation of secrets drives the power shift from the Petkoffs to the servants. When she tells Sergius that Nicola knows about his flirtation — "Nicola knows your secret—and mine" — she deploys the secret at the moment when it will maximally destabilise Sergius's self-image and advance her own interests.

Nicola, by contrast, theorises the servant's relationship to secrecy explicitly. When Louka accuses him: "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola," he replies with perfect self-awareness:

> "Yes: that's the secret of success in service."（002-079—002-081）

Shaw presents this exchange without judgment. Nicola's philosophy of strategic secrecy is shown to be effective; Louka's philosophy of weaponised secrecy is shown to be socially transformative. Neither is sentimentalised, and neither is condemned.

## Secrets as Farce Mechanism

Shaw draws on the conventions of door-farce to structure the play's secret-driven plot. Raina hides Bluntschli behind a curtain; later, Bluntschli's unexpected return forces a cascade of revelations. The physical act of concealment — bodies behind curtains, whispers at doors — is the play's central comic image. But unlike pure farce, Shaw uses these mechanisms to make a serious argument: the architecture of the Petkoff house, with its curtains and doors and servant passages, is the architecture of a society built on concealment.

The play's climactic scene in Act III is a virtuoso unravelling of multiple secrets simultaneously. The key revelation comes when Bluntschli produces the incriminating photograph:

> "Your photograph, with the inscription: 'Raina, to her Chocolate Cream Soldier—a souvenir.' Now you know there's something more in this than meets the eye; and I'm going to find it out."（003-935—003-937）

The photograph is a material secret — a physical object that circulates among characters, gaining destructive power as it passes from hand to hand. Raina's secret is literally inscribed on its surface: the dedication to "her Chocolate Cream Soldier" is a confession of intimacy that contradicts her public persona. The comic energy in Act III derives from the sheer density of revelation — the social fabric cannot sustain this many secrets indefinitely.

## Key Characters

- **Raina Petkoff** — keeps the secret of harbouring Bluntschli; her romantic self-image depends on concealment, and the revelation of her secret is simultaneously a crisis and a liberation. Her instinctive act of concealment — "Hide yourself, oh, hide yourself, quick, behind the curtain"（001-288—001-289）— is the play's founding secret
- **Captain Bluntschli** — his secret return visit sets up the Act III revelations; his professional discretion treats secrecy as a practical matter rather than a moral one: "I had better not stay here secretly longer than is necessary"（001-711—001-712）
- **Louka** — the knowing servant who traffics in secrets; her upward mobility depends on what she knows and when she chooses to reveal it. Her secretive exit（001-348）opens a knowledge asymmetry that she exploits throughout the play. She converts the Petkoffs' secrets into her own social advancement
- **Nicola** — the servant who understands that keeping secrets is a professional asset: "I know things about the mistress that she wouldn't have the master know"（002-064—002-065）. His philosophy is summarised in the exchange: "You have the soul of a servant" / "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-079—002-081）
- **Sergius Saranoff** — his secret flirtation with Louka contradicts his public posture as the apostle of higher love; his secrets are the most hypocritical because his public persona is the most idealised

## Key Scenes

Raina hides Bluntschli behind the curtain in Act I（001-288—001-290）. This moment of physical concealment is the play's originating secret and its central comic image — the hidden man in the lady's bedroom, the soldier behind the drapery.

The Russian officer's search of the Petkoff house（001-300—001-344）is a scene of dramatic irony built entirely on secrets: the audience knows Bluntschli is behind the curtain, Raina knows, and the officer does not. Shaw milks the physical proximity of discovery — the officer stands inches from the hidden man — for maximum comic tension. Raina's performance of innocence, standing "with her back to the curtain where the man is hidden"（001-325）, transforms her into an active participant in the play's economy of secrets.

The photograph revelation in Act III（003-935—003-937）is the play's climactic secret-breach. Bluntschli produces the photograph with its damning inscription, and the cascade of revelations that follows — Raina's night with Bluntschli, Louka's knowledge of it, Sergius's secret courtship of Louka — unravels the entire fabric of Petkoff respectability in a single scene.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw treats secrets not as moral failings but as social mechanisms — inevitable byproducts of a society that demands public performances of virtue. The play's resolution does not punish secrecy; instead, it rewards those who can move from concealment to honest self-presentation (Bluntschli, eventually Raina) and exposes those whose public identities are entirely constructed on concealment (Sergius).

The servant characters' relationship to secrecy is particularly complex. Shaw acknowledges that for Louka and Nicola, the ability to keep and deploy secrets is one of the few forms of power available to them. The play neither condemns their strategic secrecy nor sentimentalises it. Secrecy, like soldiering, is a profession — and like all professions in Shaw's world, it is best practiced with competence rather than sentiment.

## Critical Perspective

The centrality of secrecy to *Arms and the Man* has been read through several critical lenses. Eric Bentley, in *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration* (1947), identified the play's farcical machinery of concealment and revelation as Shaw's reworking of the door-farce tradition — not as an end in itself but as a vehicle for social critique. Where traditional farce uses secrets for comic confusion, Shaw uses them to expose the contradictions between public ideology and private behaviour.

Martin Meisel, in *Shaw and the Nineteenth-Century Theater* (1963), situated the play's secret-driven plot within Shaw's broader project of subverting theatrical convention. Meisel argued that Shaw's use of farce mechanics — bodies behind curtains, whispered asides, incriminating objects — was a deliberate strategy for smuggling serious social argument into commercially viable comic form. The photograph that circulates through Act III, in Meisel's reading, is a material emblem of the way bourgeois respectability depends on the suppression of inconvenient truths.

More recent criticism has focused on the class dynamics of secrecy. The servant characters' relationship to family secrets has been read as an allegory of class consciousness: Louka and Nicola know the truth about their employers precisely because their labour makes them invisible presences in the household. This reading, developed by scholars including Sally Peters, positions secrecy as a form of class epistemology — what the servants know is structurally determined by their social position. The play's resolution, in which Louka converts her knowledge into social advancement through marriage to Sergius, has been read both as a validation of strategic secrecy and as a conservative containment of class conflict within the institution of marriage.

Margery Morgan, in *The Shavian Playground* (1972), read the play's treatment of secrecy alongside Shaw's Fabian politics. Morgan argued that Shaw's critique of the Petkoff household as an "architecture of concealment" extends to the broader social structure: the family, like the state, depends on secrets to maintain its legitimacy. Bluntschli, as the foreign professional who exposes the family's secrets without moral judgment, represents Shaw's ideal of the rational expert whose competence transcends the romantic ideologies that secrecy sustains.
