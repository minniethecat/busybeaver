# Bluntschli's Proposal

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s marriage proposal to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] in Act III is the play's climactic comic scene and Shaw's most concentrated argument about love, [[wealth]], and honesty. What should be, by romantic convention, a declaration of passion becomes, in Shaw's hands, a hotel inventory — and the substitution is the play's definitive statement.

## The Scene

The proposal scene unfolds in the [[library]] with [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] present. When asked about his prospects, Bluntschli does not praise Raina's beauty or declare his devotion. Instead, he enumerates his assets: "about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks"（003-1294–1296）. The inventory is comic — the audience laughs — but it is also, in Shaw's moral universe, more honest than a love declaration would be.

Raina's acceptance is immediate and equally practical. She has already chosen Bluntschli; the inheritance merely confirms what she already knows. The proposal is not a seduction but a negotiation, and both parties understand what they are trading: his wealth and competence for her status and vitality.

## Comic Structure

The proposal scene follows the structure of an anti-climax. The audience has been waiting three acts for Bluntschli to declare his love; when he finally does, he does it through sheets and silverware. The disappointment is the point: love, Shaw argues, is not a transcendent passion but a practical arrangement, and acknowledging this is more honest — and more comic — than pretending otherwise.

## Key Dialogue

> BLUNTSCHLI. I have about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks — but I haven't a single reason for asking you to marry me.（003-1294–1296）

The line's last clause — "I haven't a single reason" — is the Shavian punchline. Having enumerated his material assets, Bluntschli admits he has no romantic justification. The admission is simultaneously humble (he doesn't claim to deserve her) and subversive (romantic justifications are, in Shaw's analysis, justifications — after-the-fact rationalizations of a choice made on other grounds).
