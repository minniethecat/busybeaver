# Leo Tolstoy

Count Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910), author of *War and Peace* and *Anna Karenina*, was the Russian novelist whose thinking about war most closely paralleled Shaw's own. Though Tolstoy is not directly referenced in *Arms and the Man*, his presence is felt in the play's anti-romantic treatment of military life — a treatment that owes much to Tolstoy's example.

## Tolstoy and Shaw

Shaw admired Tolstoy intensely and was influenced by his philosophical writings, particularly *What Is Art?* and Tolstoy's Christian anarchist critique of the state and its military apparatus. Shaw's Fabian socialism and Tolstoy's Christian anarchism were different philosophies, but they converged on a shared target: the romantic idealization of war and the state that demanded it. *Arms and the Man* can be read as Shaw's contribution to a conversation Tolstoy had begun.

## War and Peace

The comparison between *Arms and the Man* and Tolstoy's *War and Peace* (1869) is instructive. Both works treat war as a chaotic, unheroic business managed by incompetent officers and survived by practical men. Both debunk the myth of the great commander. Both locate moral truth in the quotidian — the chocolate cream, the mundane detail that reveals more than the grand gesture. Shaw's play is, in effect, a comic distillation of Tolstoy's insights into military life.

## Key Parallel

Tolstoy's Prince Andrei, lying wounded on the field of Austerlitz and gazing at the infinite sky, experiences the dissolution of military glory into cosmic insignificance. Shaw's Captain Bluntschli, climbing a water pipe with chocolate creams in his cartridge case, experiences the same dissolution through comedy. The insight is the same; only the register differs.
