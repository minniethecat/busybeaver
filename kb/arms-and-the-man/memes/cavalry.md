# Cavalry

The cavalry charge is the pivotal military event of *Arms and the Man*, representing the clash between aristocratic [[idealism|military romanticism]] and the grim reality of modern warfare. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] leads a charge that is celebrated as heroic — yet [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]], a professional soldier, reveals it succeeded only because the enemy had the wrong ammunition. Shaw uses the charge to dismantle the entire edifice of heroic military mythology, anticipating the catastrophic cavalry charges of the First World War by two decades.

## The "Magnificent" Charge

The play opens in the aftermath of Sergius's celebrated cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]]. Catherine Petkoff, bursting with news, delivers the charge in language borrowed from epic poetry and military dispatches:

> You can't guess how splendid it is. A cavalry charge — think of that! He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns. Can't you see it, Raina; our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched Servian dandies like chaff. （001-069 — 001-074）

This is the romantic template in its purest form: individual initiative against bureaucratic caution, the lone hero at the head of his men, natural metaphors of irresistible force ("avalanche"), and the enemy reduced to contemptible objects ("dandies," "chaff"). Every element of Catherine's account belongs to the vocabulary of nineteenth-century military romanticism, familiar from Tennyson's "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854) and the illustrated war journalism that shaped Victorian civilian understanding of battle.

The passage also establishes the structure of ignorance that sustains the myth: Catherine was not there, Raina was not there, and the only witness who will speak honestly — Bluntschli — has not yet entered the house.

## Don Quixote at the Windmills

The play's central deconstruction of military heroism occurs when Raina, hearing that Bluntschli witnessed the charge, demands he describe it. Her expectation of epic grandeur collides with his unromantic reportage:

> It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane: first one comes; then two or three close behind him; and then all the rest in a lump. （001-461 — 001-463）

When Raina insists the first man must be a hero, Bluntschli delivers the definitive verdict:

> He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We nearly burst with laughter at him; but when the sergeant ran up as white as a sheet, and told us they'd sent us the wrong cartridges, and that we couldn't fire a shot for the next ten minutes, we laughed at the other side of our mouths. […] And there was Don Quixote flourishing like a drum major, thinking he'd done the cleverest thing ever known, whereas he ought to be courtmartialled for it. Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide — only the pistol missed fire, that's all. （001-489 — 001-502）

The Don Quixote comparison is devastatingly precise. Like Cervantes's knight, Sergius mistakes reality for a romance: he charges a battery of machine guns as though they were Saracen hordes, performing a gesture of chivalric warfare that modern technology has rendered suicidal. The three similes Shaw stacks in succession — "operatic tenor," "Don Quixote at the windmills," "flourishing like a drum major" — all describe *performance*, not combat. Sergius, on this account, is not fighting a battle but staging one, with the Serbian line as his unwitting audience.

Yet Shaw's irony cuts deeper than simple mockery. The charge *succeeded*. Bluntschli's "pistol missed fire" — the Serbian ammunition error, the sheer contingency of the outcome — is the point. Heroism in modern war is a narrative imposed after the fact on chaos, luck, and mutual incompetence. The cavalry charge is heroic folly not because it failed but because its success was indistinguishable from failure: the same reckless act, against a properly equipped enemy, would have been a massacre.

## The "Cradle and Grave" of Reputation

Sergius himself is not unaware of the charge's true character. When Catherine praises "that magnificent cavalry charge," his reply is layered with irony:

> Madam: it was the cradle and the grave of my military reputation. （002-281 — 002-284）

He explains: "I won the battle the wrong way when our worthy Russian generals were losing it the right way. That upset their plans, and wounded their self-esteem" （002-288 — 002-290）. Sergius understands perfectly that his heroism is a professional embarrassment. He is trapped between knowing the truth and performing the role that society demands of him. This self-awareness distinguishes Shaw's treatment from simple satire: Sergius is not merely a fool but a man who has glimpsed his own absurdity and chosen, for reasons of pride and social compulsion, to continue the performance.

The "cavalry swagger" that Shaw's stage directions attribute to Sergius （002-490） captures this doubleness — it is a physical gait that is both genuine military bearing and theatrical self-presentation, the walk of a man who knows he is being watched.

## Cavalry in the Age of Machine Guns

Shaw wrote *Arms and the Man* in 1894, when the military obsolescence of cavalry was becoming undeniable. The machine gun (the Maxim gun had been adopted by European armies in the 1880s), rapid-fire artillery, and breech-loading rifles had rendered mounted charges — the signature tactic of Napoleonic warfare — suicidal against a properly equipped enemy.

Bluntschli's professional judgment crystallizes this historical moment:

> Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it. （001-448 — 001-451）

This debate was not theatrical invention. European militaries argued fiercely about the cavalry's role through the 1890s and 1900s. The British army, haunted by the Charge of the Light Brigade (1854), was split between traditionalists who insisted on the *arme blanche* — cold steel, the sabre charge — and reformers who argued that mounted troops should function as mobile infantry, dismounting to fight with rifles. Shaw's choice to make his professional soldier Swiss, a citizen of a nation known for its militia rather than its aristocratic cavalry, sharpens the critique: military competence belongs to the [[professional-soldiering|modern professional]], not the aristocratic amateur who treats war as an extension of the hunting field.

The play's prescience is remarkable. Two decades after its premiere, the First World War would demonstrate the utter impossibility of cavalry charges against entrenched machine-gun positions, with the British cavalry suffering catastrophic losses in 1914-1915 before being largely dismounted.

## The Professional's Verdict

Bluntschli dismantles the romantic narrative with clinical precision at every turn:

> Why, a lot of your cavalry — the greatest blackguards in your army — got the better of the enemy simply because they charged before they got the order （001-244）

The comic revelation — that indiscipline, not discipline, produced the victory — inverts the military value system. The charge's true agents were not heroic officers but "blackguards" who ignored the chain of command. Even Bluntschli's own survival depends on a reversal of martial virtues: he filled his cartridge belt with chocolate instead of ammunition （001-497）, valuing sustenance over firepower, and he climbed Raina's balcony "when a man of sense would have dived into the nearest cellar" （003-1084 — 003-1085）.

The deeper point is epistemological: civilians, especially women confined to the domestic sphere, have no access to the reality of war. Raina's question — "Did you see the great cavalry charge? Oh, tell me about it. Describe it to me" （001-454 — 001-455） — is a demand for narrative, not truth. And Bluntschli's reply — "You never saw a cavalry charge, did you?" （001-457） — is less a question than a diagnosis. The romantic myth of war survives because those who consume it never witness its production.

## Dramatic Function

The cavalry charge serves as the play's central dramatic irony, operating across all three acts. In Act I, it is the foundation of Raina's romantic world-picture; Bluntschli's account shatters that picture, but incompletely — Raina protects her ideals even as she shelters the man who undermines them. In Act II, it becomes the burden Sergius carries, the performance he can neither fully inhabit nor escape, leading him into the self-division that makes him susceptible to Louka's clearer-sighted critique. In Act III, the charge is demoted to a comic item in Raina's inventory of girlish fantasies — "her imagination full of fairy princes and noble natures and cavalry charges and goodness knows what" （003-1072） — as she discards romanticism for pragmatic self-knowledge.

This three-act arc parallels the play's broader movement: the cavalry charge begins as public myth, becomes private torment, and ends as embarrassed memory.

## Critical Perspective

Shaw's treatment of the cavalry charge has been read by scholars as part of a wider late-Victorian critique of military heroism that runs from Thackeray through Hardy and into the war poets of 1914-18. Eric Bentley, in *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration* (1947), identifies the charge as Shaw's "anti-heroic fable," noting that the play "does for the military hero what *Don Quixote* did for the chivalric romance — it confronts the genre with the material reality it has suppressed."

The Charge of the Light Brigade (1854) is the unavoidable intertext. Tennyson's poem — "Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die" — had enshrined obedient self-destruction as the highest military virtue for two generations of British readers. Shaw's cavalry charge systematically reverses every term of Tennyson's myth: Sergius *does* reason why (badly), his men *don't* obey orders (they charge prematurely), and the result is survival rather than glorious death — but survival through contingency, not skill. Where Tennyson's "noble six hundred" ride into the "valley of Death" with tragic dignity, Shaw's cavalrymen "cannon together" with "mostly broken knees" （001-478 — 001-479）.

A more recent critical line, associated with feminist Shaw scholarship (see, e.g., J. Ellen Gainor's *Shaw's Daughters*, 1991), reads the cavalry charge as a specifically gendered myth: the all-male space of the battlefield is the ultimate site of patriarchal fantasy, and Shaw's deconstruction of it opens the way for the play's larger argument that women, precisely because they are excluded from martial performance, may be better positioned to see through it. Raina's gradual liberation from the cavalry myth is thus inseparable from her liberation from romantic femininity.
