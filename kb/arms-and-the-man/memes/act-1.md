[001-001]Night. A lady’s bedchamber in [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]], in a small town near the

[001-002]Dragoman Pass. It is late in November in the year 1885, and through an

[001-003]open window with a little balcony on the left can be seen a peak of the

[001-004]Balkans, wonderfully white and beautiful in the starlit snow. The

[001-005]interior of the room is not like anything to be seen in the east of

[001-006]Europe. It is half rich Bulgarian, half cheap Viennese. The counterpane

[001-007]and hangings of the bed, the window curtains, the little carpet, and

[001-008]all the ornamental textile fabrics in the room are oriental and

[001-009]gorgeous: the paper on the walls is occidental and paltry. Above the

[001-010]head of the bed, which stands against a little wall cutting off the

[001-011]right hand corner of the room diagonally, is a painted wooden shrine,

[001-012]blue and gold, with an ivory image of Christ, and a light hanging

[001-013]before it in a pierced metal ball suspended by three chains. On the

[001-014]left, further forward, is an ottoman. The washstand, against the wall

[001-015]on the left, consists of an enamelled iron basin with a pail beneath it

[001-016]in a painted metal frame, and a single towel on the rail at the side. A

[001-017]chair near it is Austrian bent wood, with cane seat. The dressing

[001-018]table, between the bed and the window, is an ordinary pine table,

[001-019]covered with a cloth of many colors, but with an expensive toilet

[001-020]mirror on it. The door is on the right; and there is a chest of drawers

[001-021]between the door and the bed. This chest of drawers is also covered by

[001-022]a variegated native cloth, and on it there is a pile of paper backed

[001-023]novels, a box of [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]], and a miniature easel, on which is a

[001-024]large photograph of an extremely handsome officer, whose lofty bearing

[001-025]and magnetic glance can be felt even from the portrait. The room is

[001-026]lighted by a candle on the chest of drawers, and another on the

[001-027]dressing table, with a box of matches beside it.

[001-028]The window is hinged doorwise and stands wide open, folding back to the

[001-029]left. Outside a pair of wooden shutters, opening outwards, also stand

[001-030]open. On the balcony, a young lady, intensely conscious of the romantic

[001-031]beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a

[001-032]part of it, is on the balcony, gazing at the snowy Balkans. She is

[001-033]covered by a long mantle of furs, worth, on a moderate estimate, about

[001-034]three times the furniture of her room.

[001-035]Her reverie is interrupted by her mother, [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine Petkoff]], a woman

[001-036]over forty, imperiously energetic, with magnificent black hair and

[001-037]eyes, who might be a very splendid specimen of the wife of a mountain

[001-038]farmer, but is determined to be a Viennese lady, and to that end wears

[001-039]a fashionable tea gown on all occasions.

[001-040]CATHERINE.

[001-041](_entering hastily, full of good news_). [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]—(_she pronounces it

[001-042]Rah-eena, with the stress on the ee_) Raina—(_she goes to the bed,

[001-043]expecting to find Raina there._) Why, where—(_Raina looks into the

[001-044]room._) Heavens! child, are you out in the night air instead of in your

[001-045]bed? You’ll catch your death. [[louka|Louka]] told me you were asleep.

[001-046]RAINA.

[001-047](_coming in_). I sent her away. I wanted to be alone. The stars are so

[001-048]beautiful! What is the matter?

[001-049]CATHERINE.

[001-050]Such news. There has been a battle!

[001-051]RAINA.

[001-052](_her eyes dilating_). Ah! (_She throws the cloak on the ottoman, and

[001-053]comes eagerly to Catherine in her nightgown, a pretty garment, but

[001-054]evidently the only one she has on._)

[001-055]CATHERINE.

[001-056]A great battle at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]]! A victory! And it was won by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]].

[001-057]RAINA.

[001-058](_with a cry of delight_). Ah! (_Rapturously._) Oh, mother! (_Then,

[001-059]with sudden anxiety_) Is father safe?

[001-060]CATHERINE.

[001-061]Of course: he sent me the news. Sergius is the hero of the hour, the

[001-062]idol of the regiment.

[001-063]RAINA.

[001-064]Tell me, tell me. How was it! (_Ecstatically_) Oh, mother, mother,

[001-065]mother! (_Raina pulls her mother down on the ottoman; and they kiss one

[001-066]another frantically._)

[001-067]CATHERINE.

[001-068](_with surging enthusiasm_). You can’t guess how splendid it is. A

[001-069][[cavalry|cavalry charge]]—think of that! He defied our Russian commanders—acted

[001-070]without orders—led a charge on his own responsibility—headed it

[001-071]himself—was the first man to sweep through their guns. Can’t you see

[001-072]it, Raina; our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes

[001-073]flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched

[001-074]Servian dandies like chaff. And you—you kept Sergius waiting a year

[001-075]before you would be betrothed to him. Oh, if you have a drop of

[001-076]Bulgarian blood in your veins, you will worship him when he comes back.

[001-077]RAINA.

[001-078]What will he care for my poor little worship after the acclamations of

[001-079]a whole army of heroes? But no matter: I am so happy—so proud! (_She

[001-080]rises and walks about excitedly._) It proves that all our ideas were

[001-081]real after all.

[001-082]CATHERINE.

[001-083](_indignantly_). Our ideas real! What do you mean?

[001-084]RAINA.

[001-085]Our ideas of what Sergius would do—our patriotism—our heroic ideals.

[001-086]Oh, what faithless little creatures girls are!—I sometimes used to

[001-087]doubt whether they were anything but dreams. When I buckled on

[001-088]Sergius’s sword he looked so noble: it was treason to think of

[001-089]disillusion or humiliation or failure. And yet—and yet—(_Quickly._)

[001-090]Promise me you’ll never tell him.

[001-091]CATHERINE.

[001-092]Don’t ask me for promises until I know what I am promising.

[001-093]RAINA.

[001-094]Well, it came into my head just as he was holding me in his arms and

[001-095]looking into my eyes, that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because

[001-096]we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so

[001-097]delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so

[001-098]seldom like that—indeed never, as far as I knew it then.

[001-099](_Remorsefully._) Only think, mother, I doubted him: I wondered whether

[001-100]all his heroic qualities and his soldiership might not prove mere

[001-101]imagination when he went into a real battle. I had an uneasy fear that

[001-102]he might cut a poor figure there beside all those clever Russian

[001-103]officers.

[001-104]CATHERINE.

[001-105]A poor figure! Shame on you! The Servians have Austrian officers who

[001-106]are just as clever as our Russians; but we have beaten them in every

[001-107]battle for all that.

[001-108]RAINA.

[001-109](_laughing and sitting down again_). Yes, I was only a prosaic little

[001-110]coward. Oh, to think that it was all true—that Sergius is just as

[001-111]splendid and noble as he looks—that the world is really a glorious

[001-112]world for women who can see its glory and men who can act its romance!

[001-113]What happiness! what unspeakable fulfilment! Ah! (_She throws herself

[001-114]on her knees beside her mother and flings her arms passionately round

[001-115]her. They are interrupted by the entry of Louka, a handsome, proud girl

[001-116]in a pretty Bulgarian peasant’s dress with double apron, so defiant

[001-117]that her servility to Raina is almost insolent. She is afraid of

[001-118]Catherine, but even with her goes as far as she dares. She is just now

[001-119]excited like the others; but she has no sympathy for Raina’s raptures

[001-120]and looks contemptuously at the ecstasies of the two before she

[001-121]addresses them._)

[001-122]LOUKA.

[001-123]If you please, madam, all the windows are to be closed and the shutters

[001-124]made fast. They say there may be shooting in the streets. (_Raina and

[001-125]Catherine rise together, alarmed._) The Servians are being chased right

[001-126]back through the pass; and they say they may run into the town. Our

[001-127]cavalry will be after them; and our people will be ready for them you

[001-128]may be sure, now that they are running away. (_She goes out on the

[001-129]balcony and pulls the outside shutters to; then steps back into the

[001-130]room._)

[001-131]RAINA.

[001-132]I wish our people were not so cruel. What glory is there in killing

[001-133]wretched fugitives?

[001-134]CATHERINE.

[001-135](_business-like, her housekeeping instincts aroused_). I must see that

[001-136]everything is made safe downstairs.

[001-137]RAINA.

[001-138](_to Louka_). Leave the shutters so that I can just close them if I

[001-139]hear any noise.

[001-140]CATHERINE.

[001-141](_authoritatively, turning on her way to the door_). Oh, no, dear, you

[001-142]must keep them fastened. You would be sure to drop off to sleep and

[001-143]leave them open. Make them fast, Louka.

[001-144]LOUKA.

[001-145]Yes, madam. (_She fastens them._)

[001-146]RAINA.

[001-147]Don’t be anxious about me. The moment I hear a shot, I shall blow out

[001-148]the candles and roll myself up in bed with my ears well covered.

[001-149]CATHERINE.

[001-150]Quite the wisest thing you can do, my love. Good-night.

[001-151]RAINA.

[001-152]Good-night. (_They kiss one another, and Raina’s emotion comes back for

[001-153]a moment._) Wish me joy of the happiest night of my life—if only there

[001-154]are no fugitives.

[001-155]CATHERINE.

[001-156]Go to bed, dear; and don’t think of them. (_She goes out._)

[001-157]LOUKA.

[001-158](_secretly, to Raina_). If you would like the shutters open, just give

[001-159]them a push like this. (_She pushes them: they open: she pulls them to

[001-160]again._) One of them ought to be bolted at the bottom; but the bolt’s

[001-161]gone.

[001-162]RAINA.

[001-163](_with dignity, reproving her_). Thanks, Louka; but we must do what we

[001-164]are told. (_Louka makes a grimace._) Good-night.

[001-165]LOUKA.

[001-166](_carelessly_). Good-night. (_She goes out, swaggering._)

[001-167](_Raina, left alone, goes to the chest of drawers, and adores the

[001-168]portrait there with feelings that are beyond all expression. She does

[001-169]not kiss it or press it to her breast, or shew it any mark of bodily

[001-170]affection; but she takes it in her hands and elevates it like a

[001-171]priestess._)

[001-172]RAINA.

[001-173](_looking up at the picture with worship._) Oh, I shall never be

[001-174]unworthy of you any more, my hero—never, never, never.

[001-175](_She replaces it reverently, and selects a novel from the little pile

[001-176]of books. She turns over the leaves dreamily; finds her page; turns the

[001-177]book inside out at it; and then, with a happy sigh, gets into bed and

[001-178]prepares to read herself to sleep. But before abandoning herself to

[001-179]fiction, she raises her eyes once more, thinking of the blessed reality

[001-180]and murmurs_)

[001-181]My hero! my hero!

[001-182](_A distant shot breaks the quiet of the night outside. She starts,

[001-183]listening; and two more shots, much nearer, follow, startling her so

[001-184]that she scrambles out of bed, and hastily blows out the candle on the

[001-185]chest of drawers. Then, putting her fingers in her ears, she runs to

[001-186]the dressing-table and blows out the light there, and hurries back to

[001-187]bed. The room is now in darkness: nothing is visible but the glimmer of

[001-188]the light in the pierced ball before the image, and the starlight seen

[001-189]through the slits at the top of the shutters. The firing breaks out

[001-190]again: there is a startling fusillade quite close at hand. Whilst it is

[001-191]still echoing, the shutters disappear, pulled open from without, and

[001-192]for an instant the rectangle of snowy starlight flashes out with the

[001-193]figure of a man in black upon it. The shutters close immediately and

[001-194]the room is dark again. But the silence is now broken by the sound of

[001-195]panting. Then there is a scrape; and the flame of a match is seen in

[001-196]the middle of the room._)

[001-197]RAINA.

[001-198](_crouching on the bed_). Who’s there? (_The match is out instantly._)

[001-199]Who’s there? Who is that?

[001-200]A MAN’S VOICE.

[001-201](_in the darkness, subduedly, but threateningly_). Sh—sh! Don’t call

[001-202]out or you’ll be shot. Be good; and no harm will happen to you. (_She

[001-203]is heard leaving her bed, and making for the door._) Take care, there’s

[001-204]no use in trying to run away. Remember, if you raise your voice my

[001-205]pistol will go off. (_Commandingly._) Strike a light and let me see

[001-206]you. Do you hear? (_Another moment of silence and darkness. Then she is

[001-207]heard retreating to the dressing-table. She lights a candle, and the

[001-208]mystery is at an end. A man of about 35, in a deplorable plight,

[001-209]bespattered with mud and blood and snow, his belt and the strap of his

[001-210]revolver case keeping together the torn ruins of the blue coat of a

[001-211]Servian artillery officer. As far as the candlelight and his unwashed,

[001-212]unkempt condition make it possible to judge, he is a man of middling

[001-213]stature and undistinguished appearance, with strong neck and shoulders,

[001-214]a roundish, obstinate looking head covered with short crisp bronze

[001-215]curls, clear quick blue eyes and good brows and mouth, a hopelessly

[001-216]prosaic nose like that of a strong-minded baby, trim soldierlike

[001-217]carriage and energetic manner, and with all his wits about him in spite

[001-218]of his desperate predicament—even with a sense of humor of it, without,

[001-219]however, the least intention of trifling with it or throwing away a

[001-220]chance. He reckons up what he can guess about Raina—her age, her social

[001-221]position, her character, the extent to which she is frightened—at a

[001-222]glance, and continues, more politely but still most determinedly_)

[001-223]Excuse my disturbing you; but you recognise my uniform—Servian. If I’m

[001-224]caught I shall be killed. (_Determinedly._) Do you understand that?

[001-225]RAINA.

[001-226]Yes.

[001-227]MAN.

[001-228]Well, I don’t intend to get killed if I can help it. (_Still more

[001-229]determinedly._) Do you understand that? (_He locks the door with a

[001-230]snap._)

[001-231]RAINA.

[001-232](_disdainfully_). I suppose not. (_She draws herself up superbly, and

[001-233]looks him straight in the face, saying with emphasis_) Some soldiers, I

[001-234]know, are afraid of death.

[001-235]MAN.

[001-236](_with grim goodhumor_). All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe

[001-237]me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the

[001-238]enemy as we can. Now if you raise an alarm—

[001-239]RAINA.

[001-240](_cutting him short_). You will shoot me. How do you know that I am

[001-241]afraid to die?

[001-242]MAN.

[001-243](_cunningly_). Ah; but suppose I don’t shoot you, what will happen

[001-244]then? Why, a lot of your cavalry—the greatest blackguards in your

[001-245]army—will burst into this pretty room of yours and slaughter me here

[001-246]like a pig; for I’ll fight like a demon: they shan’t get me into the

[001-247]street to amuse themselves with: I know what they are. Are you prepared

[001-248]to receive that sort of company in your present undress? (_Raina,

[001-249]suddenly conscious of her nightgown, instinctively shrinks and gathers

[001-250]it more closely about her. He watches her, and adds, pitilessly_) It’s

[001-251]rather scanty, eh? (_She turns to the ottoman. He raises his pistol

[001-252]instantly, and cries_) Stop! (_She stops._) Where are you going?

[001-253]RAINA.

[001-254](_with dignified patience_). Only to get my cloak.

[001-255]MAN.

[001-256](_darting to the ottoman and snatching the cloak_). A good idea. No:

[001-257]I’ll keep the cloak: and you will take care that nobody comes in and

[001-258]sees you without it. This is a better weapon than the pistol. (_He

[001-259]throws the pistol down on the ottoman._)

[001-260]RAINA.

[001-261](_revolted_). It is not the weapon of a gentleman!

[001-262]MAN.

[001-263]It’s good enough for a man with only you to stand between him and

[001-264]death. (_As they look at one another for a moment, Raina hardly able to

[001-265]believe that even a Servian officer can be so cynically and selfishly

[001-266]unchivalrous, they are startled by a sharp fusillade in the street. The

[001-267]chill of imminent death hushes the man’s voice as he adds_) Do you

[001-268]hear? If you are going to bring those scoundrels in on me you shall

[001-269]receive them as you are. (_Raina meets his eye with unflinching scorn.

[001-270]Suddenly he starts, listening. There is a step outside. Someone tries

[001-271]the door, and then knocks hurriedly and urgently at it. Raina looks at

[001-272]the man, breathless. He throws up his head with the gesture of a man

[001-273]who sees that it is all over with him, and, dropping the manner which

[001-274]he has been assuming to intimidate her, flings the cloak to her,

[001-275]exclaiming, sincerely and kindly_) No use: I’m done for. Quick! wrap

[001-276]yourself up: they’re coming!

[001-277]RAINA.

[001-278](_catching the cloak eagerly_). Oh, thank you. (_She wraps herself up

[001-279]with great relief. He draws his sabre and turns to the door, waiting._)

[001-280]LOUKA.

[001-281](_outside, knocking_). My lady, my lady! Get up, quick, and open the

[001-282]door.

[001-283]RAINA.

[001-284](_anxiously_). What will you do?

[001-285]MAN.

[001-286](_grimly_). Never mind. Keep out of the way. It will not last long.

[001-287]RAINA.

[001-288](_impulsively_). I’ll help you. Hide yourself, oh, hide yourself,

[001-289]quick, behind the curtain. (_She seizes him by a torn strip of his

[001-290]sleeve, and pulls him towards the window._)

[001-291]MAN.

[001-292](_yielding to her_). There is just half a chance, if you keep your

[001-293]head. Remember: nine soldiers out of ten are born fools. (_He hides

[001-294]behind the curtain, looking out for a moment to say, finally_) If they

[001-295]find me, I promise you a fight—a devil of a fight! (_He disappears.

[001-296]Raina takes off the cloak and throws it across the foot of the bed.

[001-297]Then with a sleepy, disturbed air, she opens the door. Louka enters

[001-298]excitedly._)

[001-299]LOUKA.

[001-300]A man has been seen climbing up the water-pipe to your balcony—a

[001-301]Servian. The soldiers want to search for him; and they are so wild and

[001-302]drunk and furious. My lady says you are to dress at once.

[001-303]RAINA.

[001-304](_as if annoyed at being disturbed_). They shall not search here. Why

[001-305]have they been let in?

[001-306]CATHERINE.

[001-307](_coming in hastily_). Raina, darling, are you safe? Have you seen

[001-308]anyone or heard anything?

[001-309]RAINA.

[001-310]I heard the shooting. Surely the soldiers will not dare come in here?

[001-311]CATHERINE.

[001-312]I have found a Russian officer, thank Heaven: he knows Sergius.

[001-313](_Speaking through the door to someone outside._) Sir, will you come in

[001-314]now! My daughter is ready.

[001-315](_A young Russian officer, in Bulgarian uniform, enters, sword in

[001-316]hand._)

[001-317]THE OFFICER.

[001-318](_with soft, feline politeness and stiff military carriage_). Good

[001-319]evening, gracious lady; I am sorry to intrude, but there is a fugitive

[001-320]hiding on the balcony. Will you and the gracious lady your mother

[001-321]please to withdraw whilst we search?

[001-322]RAINA.

[001-323](_petulantly_). Nonsense, sir, you can see that there is no one on the

[001-324]balcony. (_She throws the shutters wide open and stands with her back

[001-325]to the curtain where the man is hidden, pointing to the moonlit

[001-326]balcony. A couple of shots are fired right under the window, and a

[001-327]bullet shatters the glass opposite Raina, who winks and gasps, but

[001-328]stands her ground, whilst Catherine screams, and the officer rushes to

[001-329]the balcony._)

[001-330]THE OFFICER.

[001-331](_on the balcony, shouting savagely down to the street_). Cease firing

[001-332]there, you fools: do you hear? Cease firing, damn you. (_He glares down

[001-333]for a moment; then turns to Raina, trying to resume his polite

[001-334]manner._) Could anyone have got in without your knowledge? Were you

[001-335]asleep?

[001-336]RAINA.

[001-337]No, I have not been to bed.

[001-338]THE OFFICER.

[001-339](_impatiently, coming back into the room_). Your neighbours have their

[001-340]heads so full of runaway Servians that they see them everywhere.

[001-341](_Politely._) Gracious lady, a thousand pardons. Good-night. (_Military

[001-342]bow, which Raina returns coldly. Another to Catherine, who follows him

[001-343]out. Raina closes the shutters. She turns and sees Louka, who has been

[001-344]watching the scene curiously._)

[001-345]RAINA.

[001-346]Don’t leave my mother, Louka, whilst the soldiers are here. (_Louka

[001-347]glances at Raina, at the ottoman, at the curtain; then purses her lips

[001-348]secretively, laughs to herself, and goes out. Raina follows her to the

[001-349]door, shuts it behind her with a slam, and locks it violently. The man

[001-350]immediately steps out from behind the curtain, sheathing his sabre, and

[001-351]dismissing the danger from his mind in a businesslike way._)

[001-352]MAN.

[001-353]A narrow shave; but a miss is as good as a mile. Dear young lady, your

[001-354]servant until death. I wish for your sake I had joined the Bulgarian

[001-355]army instead of the Servian. I am not a native Servian.

[001-356]RAINA.

[001-357](_haughtily_). No, you are one of the Austrians who set the Servians on

[001-358]to rob us of our national liberty, and who officer their army for them.

[001-359]We hate them!

[001-360]MAN.

[001-361]Austrian! not I. Don’t hate me, dear young lady. I am only a Swiss,

[001-362]fighting merely as a [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]]. I joined Servia because it

[001-363]was nearest to me. Be generous: you’ve beaten us hollow.

[001-364]RAINA.

[001-365]Have I not been generous?

[001-366]MAN.

[001-367]Noble!—heroic! But I’m not saved yet. This particular rush will soon

[001-368]pass through; but the pursuit will go on all night by fits and starts.

[001-369]I must take my chance to get off during a quiet interval. You don’t

[001-370]mind my waiting just a minute or two, do you?

[001-371]RAINA.

[001-372]Oh, no: I am sorry you will have to go into danger again. (_Motioning

[001-373]towards ottoman._) Won’t you sit—(_She breaks off with an irrepressible

[001-374]cry of alarm as she catches sight of the pistol. The man, all nerves,

[001-375]shies like a frightened horse._)

[001-376]MAN.

[001-377](_irritably_). Don’t frighten me like that. What is it?

[001-378]RAINA.

[001-379]Your pistol! It was staring that officer in the face all the time. What

[001-380]an escape!

[001-381]MAN.

[001-382](_vexed at being unnecessarily terrified_). Oh, is that all?

[001-383]RAINA.

[001-384](_staring at him rather superciliously, conceiving a poorer and poorer

[001-385]opinion of him, and feeling proportionately more and more at her ease

[001-386]with him_). I am sorry I frightened you. (_She takes up the pistol and

[001-387]hands it to him._) Pray take it to protect yourself against me.

[001-388]MAN.

[001-389](_grinning wearily at the sarcasm as he takes the pistol_). No use,

[001-390]dear young lady: there’s nothing in it. It’s not loaded. (_He makes a

[001-391]grimace at it, and drops it disparagingly into his revolver case._)

[001-392]RAINA.

[001-393]Load it by all means.

[001-394]MAN.

[001-395]I’ve no ammunition. What use are cartridges in battle? I always carry

[001-396]chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that yesterday.

[001-397]RAINA.
