# Fabian Society

The Fabian Society, founded in London in 1884, was the socialist organization to which George Bernard Shaw belonged and for which he wrote, lectured, and campaigned throughout his career. Its political philosophy — gradual, rational reform through expert administration rather than revolutionary upheaval — provides the intellectual substructure of *Arms and the Man*. The play's critique of class, its preference for professional competence over inherited status, and its vision of a world run by capable administrators rather than heroic amateurs are all expressions of Fabian principles in dramatic form.

## Fabian Principles

The Fabian Society took its name from the Roman general Fabius Maximus, who defeated Hannibal through patience and attrition rather than decisive battle. This strategic gradualism defined Fabian socialism: reform would come not through revolution but through the steady penetration of institutions by competent, rational administrators. The Fabians believed in expertise, efficiency, and the gradual extension of public ownership. They were, in Shaw's own description, "constitutionalists and opportunists" — working within the existing political system to reshape it through municipal reform, public education, and the professionalization of government. The Fabian Essays (1889), to which Shaw contributed, laid out this vision in practical detail.

## Fabianism in the Play

Fabian principles structure the play at multiple levels. Bluntschli's professional soldiering — competent, rational, unromantic — is a Fabian ideal applied to military life. His elevation of Nicola to hotel manager based on ability rather than birth ("Nicola's the ablest man I've met in Bulgaria," 003-1010) is Fabian meritocracy in action. The play's resolution — a Swiss hotelier's son winning the aristocratic Raina, a servant (Louka) rising through marriage to the gentry, a cavalry hero reduced to honest husband — is a Fabian redistribution of roles based on competence rather than class.

Shaw's critique of militarism in the play is also Fabian. The Fabian Society opposed the arms race, imperial adventure, and the romanticization of war. Sergius's cavalry charge — heroic in appearance, suicidal in fact — is a Fabian case study in the cost of placing amateurs in command. The professional soldier who carries chocolate instead of cartridges is Shaw's Fabian hero: a man who values sustenance over glory and competence over display.

## The Limits of Fabianism in the Play

Shaw's Fabianism in *Arms and the Man* has limits that the play itself acknowledges. Nicola, the servant who practices patient, calculating careerism — "you've got to know your place; that's the secret of it" (003-492) — is a dark mirror of Fabian gradualism. His willingness to endure humiliation for future gain is structurally identical to the Fabian strategy but ethically thinner. The play seems to recognize that Fabianism without the dignity of independence — without Bluntschli's Swiss hotels behind him — can look merely like servility. This self-critical dimension distinguishes Shaw's dramatic intelligence from his political advocacy.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw joined the Fabian Society in September 1884, shortly after its founding, and remained a member for the rest of his life. He wrote *Arms and the Man* in 1893-1894, at the height of his Fabian activism, and the play can be read as a Fabian tract in comic form. Its argument — that the world would be better run by competent professionals than by romantic amateurs, that class origins should not determine social function, that honest self-assessment is the foundation of social progress — is the argument of the Fabian Essays set to laughter. The play's subtitle, "An Anti-Romantic Comedy," is also a political position: anti-romanticism, for Shaw, was the precondition of rational reform.
