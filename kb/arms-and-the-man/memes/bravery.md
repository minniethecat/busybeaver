# Bravery

Shaw subjects bravery — one of the cardinal virtues of the Victorian moral code — to sustained comic examination. *Arms and the Man* asks: what is bravery, exactly? Is it charging enemy machine guns on horseback, as [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] does? Or is it climbing down a water pipe in the dark while exhausted and hungry, as [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] does? The play's quiet answer is that the second may be as demanding as the first — and considerably more useful.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The play opens with news of Sergius's "brilliant cavalry charge" — the kind of bravery that wins medals and feeds romantic imaginations. But within moments, Bluntschli provides the professional debunking: the charge succeeded only because the enemy's machine guns had the wrong ammunition, and Sergius "ought to be courtmartialled" for leading it（001-499）. The brave act, examined professionally, was reckless; the heroic gesture, analyzed rationally, was incompetence.

The play then introduces its alternative model of bravery. Bluntschli's admission that he fled for his life — "The last man left alive in my regiment fled for his life, and here I am"（001-370）— is a confession that requires its own kind of courage. To admit cowardice in a culture that worships martial valor is to risk contempt. Bluntschli risks it, and in doing so demonstrates a bravery of a different order: the courage to be honest.

### ACT II

Bravery in peacetime takes less dramatic forms. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s philosophy of servitude — "I can be hurt by nothing"（002-867）— is presented as a kind of emotional bravery, the servant's courage to absorb humiliation without flinching. Nicola is not brave in the military sense, but Shaw suggests that his endurance, his strategic patience, his refusal to be provoked into rash action, constitutes its own form of valor.

### ACT III

The question of bravery is resolved in the confrontation over the duel. When [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] challenges Bluntschli to single combat, both men demonstrate courage: Sergius, the courage to risk death for honor; Bluntschli, the courage to accept a challenge and then defuse it through rational means. But Shaw's sympathies are clear. Bluntschli's calm professionalism — "I'm a good shot with a pistol" — is the mature bravery of competence; Sergius's theatrical challenge is the adolescent bravery of display.

## Key Scene

> MAN. It takes less courage to climb down than to face capture — remember that.（001-567）

Bluntschli's advice to himself — muttered as he steels himself to descend the water pipe — unites physical and moral courage in a single sentence. Climbing down requires physical bravery (the pipe is dangerous) and moral bravery (the act of flight is shameful by conventional standards). Shaw's point is that they are the same thing: all courage is the courage to do what is necessary despite fear.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw was not interested in abolishing bravery as a virtue but in redefining it. The bravery he celebrates is not the martial courage of the battlefield but the intellectual courage of the honest man, the professional competence of the skilled worker, and the emotional bravery of those — like [[nicola|Nicola]] — who endure what they cannot change.

- **Target of satire**: The equation of bravery with physical risk-taking in battle, which serves the interests of military establishments
- **Alternative proposed**: Bravery as the capacity to act rationally and honestly under pressure, whether on the battlefield, in the drawing room, or in the servant's quarters
