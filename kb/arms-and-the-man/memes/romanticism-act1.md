# Raina's Byronic Romanticism (Act I)

[[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] embodies romantic idealism in its purest form in Act I. Shaw's opening stage direction establishes her as "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty are part of it" (001-030). She adores her fiance [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] from a distance, having only seen him in photographs (001-061), treating the portrait as an icon: she "takes it in her hands and elevates it like a priestess" (001-170).

When she receives news of Sergius's cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza]], she exults in "our patriotism — our heroic ideals" (001-085). Yet Shaw immediately plants the seed of disillusionment:

> Oh, what faithless little creatures girls are! — I sometimes used to doubt whether they were anything but dreams. When I buckled on Sergius's sword he looked so noble: it was treason to think of disillusion or humiliation or failure. And yet — and yet — (001-085)

The stage direction notes that Raina's mother [[catherine-petkoff]] "would have been, only that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we were so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin" (001-095). Raina herself admits the artifice: "perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest" (001-096).

[[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]]'s entrance shatters this romantic frame. A Swiss [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] fleeing for his life, he describes Sergius's "heroic" charge as the act of a fool: "nine soldiers out of ten are born fools" (001-293). He carries chocolates instead of cartridges — the practical soldier's emblem. Raina's shock when this "chocolate cream soldier" (001-398) fails to match her "most cherished ideals of manhood" (001-398) is the first major collision between romanticism and reality in the play.

Bluntschli's professional verdict on the charge is devastating: "He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We did laugh" (001-489).
