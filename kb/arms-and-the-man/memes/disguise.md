# Disguise

Disguise is the play's governing social metaphor. Every major character practices some form of concealment: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] hides an enemy soldier and then hides the fact that she hid him; [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] hides his attraction to a servant behind a philosophy of "higher love"; [[louka|Louka]] hides her ambitions behind the mask of servitude; even [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], the play's supposed avatar of honesty, keeps his wealth a secret until the final act.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The most literal disguise is Bluntschli's: a Servian officer in a Bulgarian town, dependent on Raina's goodwill for survival. But the more significant disguises are psychological. Raina's romantic posturing — her operatic declarations of patriotism, her worship of Sergius's photograph — is revealed as a costume she has been wearing since childhood, a part she learned from Pushkin and Byron. When Bluntschli says "I'll take off my boots: my feet are blistered from the retreat"（001-328）, he is also inviting Raina to take off the costume of romantic idealism.

### ACT II

The second act multiplies the disguises. [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], the practical manager of the household, must pretend ignorance of the soldier's visit while managing the fallout. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] disguises his sexual interest in Louka as a philosophical disquisition on the difference between "higher love" and physical attraction. [[nicola|Nicola]], the most self-aware character in the play, wears his servitude as a deliberate disguise — "I put my pride in my pocket and ride it out"（002-870）.

### ACT III

The disguises fall away. Bluntschli's wealth is revealed, and with it the practical basis of his professional detachment — he was never a penniless mercenary but a rich man's son learning the family business of hotel management. Raina sheds her romantic persona and admits she is "only a woman." Louka reveals her ambitions openly. The play ends not with characters transformed but with characters revealed — the disguises were always temporary, the selves beneath them always present.

## Key Scene

> RAINA. I am only a woman. I may not be good, but I am real.（003-1376）

Raina's final self-definition — "I may not be good, but I am real" — is the play's most concentrated statement on disguise and authenticity. She has spent three acts performing romantic idealism; she ends by claiming, not virtue, but reality. The admission that she "may not be good" is simultaneously a confession (she has lied, deceived, manipulated) and a liberation (she no longer needs to pretend to be good).

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's analysis of disguise is inseparable from his critique of Victorian society. A society that demands impossible standards of purity, honor, and patriotism from its members is a society that manufactures disguises. The characters of *Arms and the Man* do not deceive because they are wicked but because the social roles available to them — romantic heroine, Byronic hero, obedient servant — cannot accommodate the full range of human desire. Disguise, in this analysis, is not a moral failing but a survival strategy.

- **Target of satire**: The social conventions that force people to pretend to be what they are not
- **Alternative proposed**: A society — and a theater — in which people can admit to being "not good, but real"
