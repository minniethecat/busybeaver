# Cowardice

Cowardice is the accusation the play most systematically subverts. Every character who might be called a coward — [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], who fled for his life; [[nicola|Nicola]], who grovels before his masters — is presented as more admirable than the "brave" characters whose courage consists of theatrical gestures. Shaw's point is not that cowardice is a virtue but that the word "cowardice" is a weapon wielded by the powerful to compel self-destructive behavior from the weak.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Bluntschli's admission — "The last man left alive in my regiment fled for his life, and here I am"（001-370）— is the play's foundational challenge to the cult of military courage. By conventional standards, Bluntschli is a coward: he ran. By Shaw's standards, Bluntschli is a rational actor: he survived. The play's moral universe is built on the difference between these two judgments.

### ACT II

Nicola's servitude is framed as a form of moral cowardice by Louka, but Nicola reframes it as strategic patience: "I can be hurt by nothing"（002-870）. His refusal to fight, his willingness to absorb humiliation, his strategic deference — these would be called cowardice in a gentleman. In a servant, they are called competence. Shaw's point is that the vocabulary of courage and cowardice is always class-inflected.

### ACT III

The question of cowardice is resolved through inversion. Bluntschli, the supposed coward, is revealed as the play's most successful character — wealthy, competent, and about to marry the heroine. Sergius, the supposed hero, is revealed as a fool whose "bravery" was incompetence dressed in a cavalry uniform.

## Key Scene

> MAN. It takes less courage to climb down than to face capture — remember that.（001-567）

Bluntschli's self-exhortation as he descends the water pipe is Shaw's definition of real courage: not the absence of fear but the willingness to act despite it. The line also implicitly redefines cowardice: the man who flees is not necessarily a coward; the man who faces capture out of fear of being thought a coward is.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of cowardice is part of his larger project of demystifying the language of military virtue. Words like "cowardice," "courage," "honor," and "glory" are, in Shaw's analysis, instruments of social control — they persuade young men to die for causes that benefit their rulers. By calling Bluntschli's flight "rational" rather than "cowardly," Shaw attempts to disarm one of those instruments.

- **Target of satire**: The vocabulary of courage and cowardice that sustains military establishments
- **Alternative proposed**: A language of rational self-interest that evaluates actions by their outcomes rather than their conformity to romantic codes
