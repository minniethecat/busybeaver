# Secrets as Social Currency

Shaw embeds a class analysis in the play's treatment of secrecy. Knowledge of family secrets
functions as a form of capital that the servant class can accumulate and spend — one of the
few forms of power available to those with no economic or social standing.

## Knowledge as Capital

Louka explicitly invokes the power of hidden knowledge:

> "I know some family secrets they wouldn't care to have told, young as I am. Let them
> quarrel with me if they dare!"（002-048 — 002-049）

Nicola raises the stakes further, articulating the servant's comprehensive knowledge of the
family's hidden lives:

> "I know things about the mistress that she wouldn't have the master know for a thousand
> levas. I know things about him that she wouldn't let him hear the last of for six months
> if I blabbed them to her. I know things about Raina that would break off her match with
> Sergius if—"（002-064 — 002-067）

This passage is Shaw's most explicit statement of the servant class's power through knowledge.
Nicola's knowing silence is a form of professional discretion that Shaw portrays as both
pragmatic and morally ambiguous — he keeps secrets not out of loyalty but out of calculation.
Silence is an investment in future advantage.

## Two Strategies

The play presents two models for how servants manage the secrets they possess. Nicola
theorizes the servant's relationship to secrecy explicitly. When Louka accuses him — "You have
the soul of a servant, Nicola" — he replies with perfect self-awareness: "Yes: that's the
secret of success in service"（002-079 — 002-081）. His strategy is silence as investment:
keep the secrets, build trust, collect rewards over time. It is effective, conservative, and
ultimately self-limiting.

Louka's trajectory demonstrates the alternative: knowledge as a weapon to be deployed at
strategic moments. Her gradual revelation of secrets drives the power shift from the Petkoffs
to the servants. When she tells Sergius that Nicola knows about his flirtation — "Nicola knows
your secret — and mine" — she deploys the secret at the moment when it will maximally
destabilize Sergius's self-image and advance her own interests. In a hierarchical society,
knowledge is a form of capital that can be converted into social mobility.

Shaw presents this exchange without judgment. Nicola's philosophy of strategic secrecy is
shown to be effective; Louka's philosophy of weaponized secrecy is shown to be socially
transformative. Neither is sentimentalized, and neither is condemned.
