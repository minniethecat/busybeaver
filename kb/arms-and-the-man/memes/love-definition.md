# Love — Definition

**Love** in *Arms and the Man* is subjected to the same anti-romantic scrutiny that Shaw applies to war. The play presents a taxonomy of love:

- **Romantic infatuation**: Raina's initial feeling for Sergius — directed at the *idea* of the hero, not the man himself (001-030 to 001-170).
- **The "higher love"**: Sergius's self-serving philosophy that allows him to feel noble while acting selfishly — love as alibi (002-462 to 002-464).
- **Practical partnership**: Bluntschli's proposal — honest, unromantic, built on mutual respect rather than mutual illusion (003-1191 to 003-1202).
- **Strategic alliance**: Louka's pursuit of Sergius — love as social mobility, clear-eyed about both her ambition and his weakness.

Each variety is exposed not as a pure emotion but as a social performance shaped by class position, gender expectation, and self-deception. Shaw's argument is that love, like soldiering, is better served by honesty and practicality than by poetic posturing.

Raina's opening scene establishes romantic love as theatrical performance. She is "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it" (001-030 to 001-032), framing her affection for Sergius as an aesthetic experience rather than genuine emotional connection. Her love is directed at a photograph she "elevates like a priestess" (001-170 to 001-171) — religious worship of an image, not connection with a person.
