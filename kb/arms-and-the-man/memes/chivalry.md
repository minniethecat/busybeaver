# Chivalry

Chivalry in *Arms and the Man* is the master-illusion from which the play's other romantic delusions descend. The medieval code of knightly honour — courage in battle, courtesy to ladies, loyalty to one's word — supplies [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] with the script he performs, [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] with the ideal she worships, and [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine Petkoff]] with the social vocabulary she deploys to manage inconvenient truths. Shaw's play subjects each of these performances to comic deflation, revealing chivalry not as a moral code but as an aesthetic costume that licenses the very behaviour it pretends to prohibit.

## Definition

In its historical sense, chivalry (from Old French *chevalerie*, "horsemanship") was the ethical system governing the medieval knightly class: a fusion of martial prowess, feudal loyalty, and courtly love. By the late nineteenth century, when Shaw wrote *Arms and the Man* (1894), chivalry had undergone a Victorian revival — repackaged through Tennyson's *Idylls of the King*, the Pre-Raphaelite rediscovery of Arthurian legend, and the "muscular Christianity" of Charles Kingsley and Thomas Hughes. This revival, Shaw believed, was intellectually bankrupt: it aestheticised violence, sanctified class hierarchy, and provided a rhetorical alibi for male predation, all under the banner of moral refinement.

Shaw's chivalry is thus a double target. It is, first, a military anachronism — a pre-industrial code of personal gallantry rendered suicidal by machine guns and artillery. Second, it is a social performance — a set of gestures (kneeling, hand-kissing, elevated diction) that the play's aristocrats deploy to distinguish themselves from servants and tradesmen while concealing their ordinary selfishness.

## The Chivalric Tradition and Its Victorian Afterlife

To understand Shaw's critique, one must recognise that the chivalric revival was not a fringe enthusiasm but a dominant current in late-Victorian culture. The Medievalism of Ruskin and Morris, the Eglinton Tournament of 1839 (a full-scale re-enactment of medieval jousting attended by 100,000 spectators), the founding of the Boy Scouts with its chivalric oath — all attest to a widespread conviction that the knightly virtues could redeem an age of industrial ugliness and imperial anxiety.

Shaw was having none of it. As a Fabian socialist and a disciple of Ibsen, he regarded the chivalric revival as reactionary nostalgia dressed in moral costume. The knight, in Shaw's analysis, was an aristocratic thug whose violence was retrospectively sanitised by poets. *Arms and the Man* translates this critique into drama: every invocation of chivalry in the play is either self-serving, hypocritical, or strategically deployed to avoid an uncomfortable truth.

## Sergius as Don Quixote: Chivalry on the Battlefield

The play's definitive judgement on chivalric warfare is delivered not by a philosopher but by a Swiss mercenary who has just witnessed it. [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]]'s description of Sergius's cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] is the play's most concentrated passage of anti-chivalric satire:

> He did it like an operatic tenor — a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We nearly burst with laughter at him; but when the sergeant ran up as white as a sheet, and told us they'd sent us the wrong cartridges, and that we couldn't fire a shot for the next ten minutes, we laughed at the other side of our mouths. （001-489–001-497）

The comparison to Don Quixote is precise and devastating. Cervantes's knight errant, like Sergius, has read too many romances and mistakes the prosaic world for the enchanted one. But where Cervantes's irony is affectionate, Shaw's is forensic. Sergius's charge is not merely foolish — it is professionally criminal. Bluntschli's assessment is unambiguous: "Of all the fools ever let loose on a field of battle, that man must be the very maddest. He and his regiment simply committed suicide" （001-500–001-502）. The chivalric performance, in Shaw's accounting, gets men killed.

Raina's initial reception of the news — recounted by her mother in Act I — perfectly captures the civilian complicity in this lethal fantasy:

> He defied our Russian commanders — acted without orders — led a charge on his own responsibility — headed it himself — was the first man to sweep through their guns. Can't you see it, Raina; our gallant splendid Bulgarians with their swords and eyes flashing, thundering down like an avalanche and scattering the wretched Servian dandies like chaff. （001-069–001-074）

Catherine's language — "gallant," "splendid," "flashing," "thundering" — is the diction of chivalric romance, and it is precisely this vocabulary that Bluntschli's matter-of-fact account ("It's like slinging a handful of peas against a window pane," 001-461–001-462） dismantles. The play's method is to juxtapose the chivalric description with the professional one, and let the audience measure the lethal distance between them.

## Chivalry as Social Performance

If Act I exposes chivalry's military bankruptcy, Act II exposes its social function. Sergius, returned from the war, immediately restages his cavalry exploit as a courtly ritual, casting Raina as the lady whose favour he sought:

> Dearest, all my deeds have been yours. You inspired me. I have gone through the war like a knight in a tournament with his lady looking on at him! （002-457–002-459）

The line is a confession dressed as a compliment. Sergius has not fought a war; he has "gone through the war *like* a knight in a tournament" — the simile itself acknowledges the gap between the chivalric model and the modern reality. He has been performing, not soldiering. Raina's reply — "My hero! My king" （002-447）— supplies the audience that the performance requires. They are co-conspirators in the fiction.

Shaw's stage directions for Sergius's entrance are themselves a critical essay on the chivalric personality:

> By his brooding on the perpetual failure, not only of others, but of himself, to live up to his imaginative ideals, his consequent cynical scorn for humanity, the jejune credulity as to the absolute validity of his ideals and the unworthiness of the world in disregarding them … he has acquired the half tragic, half ironic air … by which Childe Harold fascinated the grandmothers of his English contemporaries. （002-249–002-259）

The diagnosis is clinical: the chivalric idealist, unable to reconcile his self-image with his actual behaviour, oscillates between grandiosity and self-loathing. This oscillation structures every scene Sergius shares with [[louka|Louka]], the servant who sees through him.

## Louka and the Cracks in the Armour

The decisive test of chivalry in the play is not the battlefield but the back garden — specifically, Sergius's interactions with Louka. His chivalric code demands that a gentleman treat a lady with deference, but Louka is not a lady; she is a servant. The play dramatises, through their scenes together, the precise mechanism by which chivalry excludes those it claims to protect.

When Louka rejects Sergius's condescending apology for having made a pass at her, he cannot process the challenge on its own terms:

> SERGIUS. (*thus rudely crossed in his chivalry, throws it off with a bitter laugh and says slightingly*) Oh, you wish to be paid for the hurt? （002-634–002-635）

The stage direction — "throws it off" — is Shaw's metaphor in miniature. Chivalry is a garment Sergius can don and discard at will. When the performance fails to secure the desired response, the costume comes off, and what remains is the aristocrat reaching for his wallet. The equation of chivalry with payment — "you wish to be paid for the hurt?" — is Shaw at his most savage, collapsing the moral pretension of knightly courtesy into the economic reality of class power.

Louka's reply three lines later completes the analysis: "No, I want my hurt made well" （002-638）. Chivalry offers money; Louka demands recognition. The gap between those two currencies is the play's indictment of the entire chivalric tradition.

## The Undoing of Chivalry

Act III stages chivalry's terminal collapse on multiple fronts simultaneously. Raina, having witnessed Sergius flirt with Louka and having herself fallen for the un-chivalrous Bluntschli, deploys the language of chivalry as pure irony:

> Really, Sergius, you cannot stand by and allow such a thing. It would be unworthy of your chivalry. （003-792–003-793）

Raina's tone — the stage direction specifies "sarcastically" — marks her definitive break with the chivalric worldview. She now uses "chivalry" not as an ideal to invoke but as a weapon to wound. The word has been hollowed out and turned against its chief practitioner.

The play's most structurally revealing deployment of chivalric language occurs earlier in Act II, when Catherine attempts to expel Bluntschli from the Petkoff house:

> Will you, like the chivalrous gentleman and soldier you are, leave at once before he finds you here? （002-806–002-807）

Catherine invokes chivalry here not as a moral standard but as a compliance mechanism. The "chivalrous gentleman" is, in her usage, the man who will cooperate in his own erasure — who will silently exit so that the Petkoff family's secrets remain intact. Chivalry becomes a tool for managing inconvenient truths, precisely the opposite of the truth-telling honour it purports to represent.

## Chivalry and Gender

One of Shaw's most radical insights in *Arms and the Man* is that chivalry, far from protecting women, infantalises them. The chivalric code positions women on a pedestal — as "lady," "queen," "saint" — in order to deny them agency. Raina's journey across the three acts is a journey out of this pedestal and into self-knowledge.

Her earliest lines are saturated with the chivalric vocabulary she has absorbed from Byron and Pushkin: "My hero! my hero!" （001-181）. The [[photograph]] of Sergius, displayed on her dressing table "in a miniature easel" (001-024), literalises the worshipful gaze that chivalry demands of its female audience. But even in Act I, Raina entertains doubts:

> Perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that — indeed never, as far as I knew it then. （001-095–001-098）

The admission is remarkable: Raina grasps, at some level, that her ideals are literary constructs, not moral truths. The play's action is the process by which this intellectual suspicion becomes lived conviction.

The chivalric construction of femininity also illuminates the play's treatment of Louka. Louka is not a "lady" and therefore falls outside chivalry's protective scope — which means she can see it for what it is. Her sharpest line to Sergius — "It's so hard to know what a gentleman considers right" （002-555）— is an epistemological challenge disguised as a complaint. She is pointing out that "gentlemanly" conduct has no fixed content; it is whatever behaviour the gentleman can retrospectively justify.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's critique of chivalry is not merely satirical but philosophical. Chivalry, in his analysis, belongs to the class of what he later called "ideals" — abstractions that people invoke to avoid confronting material realities. The alternative is not cynicism but professionalism: the code of the competent specialist who does his job efficiently and honestly, represented by Bluntschli.

This is most clearly articulated in the quiet exchange between Bluntschli and Raina at the end of Act III, where Bluntschli diagnoses her condition:

> She, rich, young, beautiful, with her imagination full of fairy princes and noble natures and cavalry charges and goodness knows what! （003-1071–003-1073）

"Fairy princes and noble natures and cavalry charges" — the trinity of chivalric romance — is here named as a pathology of the imagination, something to be outgrown rather than achieved. Bluntschli's own "incurably romantic disposition" （003-1076） is the play's final irony: the professional soldier, the pragmatist, the anti-chivalric voice, turns out to be a romantic too — but a romantic who has learned to laugh at his own romanticism. That capacity for self-irony is what the chivalric characters lack and what Shaw's comic form supplies.

- **Satirical target**: The late-Victorian chivalric revival — Tennysonian medievalism, muscular Christianity, and the aristocratic code of honour — and its real-world consequence: the aestheticisation of military violence.
- **Alternative offered**: Professional competence, honest self-assessment, and the courage to face reality without the consolations of romance.

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's attack on chivalry has been interpreted along several distinct critical lines:

**The Fabian Reading.** Eric Bentley, in *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration* (1947), situates the play's anti-chivalric stance within Shaw's broader socialist critique of aristocratic institutions. Chivalry, for Bentley's Shaw, is ideology in the Marxist sense: a set of ideas that naturalise class hierarchy by making it appear noble rather than exploitative.

**The Gender Reading.** J. Ellen Gainor, in *Shaw's Daughters: Dramatic and Narrative Constructions of Gender* (1991), reads the play's treatment of chivalry as an early contribution to feminist critique. Chivalry's idealisation of women, Gainor argues, is the velvet glove on the iron fist of patriarchal control. Louka's resistance to Sergius's chivalric overtures is the play's most politically significant action because it exposes the economic dependency concealed by romantic rhetoric.

**The Performance Reading.** Martin Meisel, in *Shaw and the Nineteenth-Century Theater* (1963), emphasises the meta-theatrical dimension. Sergius is a man who has read too many plays and novels and now lives his life as though he were their protagonist. His chivalry is not a moral conviction but a role — and Shaw's play is the theatre in which that role is exposed as theatre.

**The Historical Reading.** More recently, scholars of Balkan history have noted that Shaw's depiction of the Serbo-Bulgarian War — and Sergius's charge in particular — draws on actual incidents of amateurish officer conduct during the conflict. The play is not merely a philosophical fable; it is a document of real military incompetence enabled by aristocratic codes of honour.

A representative critical assessment from Nicholas Grene's *Bernard Shaw: A Critical View* (1984) captures the consensus:

> The comedy of *Arms and the Man* depends on the constant discrepancy between the high chivalric style and the low material facts. Chocolate, not bullets; exhausted fugitives, not fearless knights; a professional tradesman, not an aristocratic lover — these substitutions constitute the play's argument before a word of polemic is spoken. Shaw understood that the most effective way to destroy an ideal is not to denounce it but to show it being out-performed by reality.
