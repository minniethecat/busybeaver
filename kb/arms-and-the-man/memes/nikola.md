# Nikola

Nikola, the previous servant in the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] whose name [[nicola|Nicola]] shares, is mentioned only once in the play — but the reference illuminates Nicola's entire philosophy of servitude. According to Nicola, the original Nikola was discharged for being "too impudent to bear"（002-876）— a warning that shapes Nicola's strategic deference.

## The Cautionary Tale

Nicola invokes his predecessor to explain his own approach to service. The original Nikola, Nicola implies, made the mistake of asserting himself — of speaking back, of demanding respect, of forgetting his place. The consequence was dismissal. Nicola's famous servility — "I put my pride in my pocket and ride it out"（002-870）— is a direct response to this cautionary example. He has learned from Nikola's fate that a servant who values his pride over his position will lose both.

## The Doubling of Names

The doubling of the name — the current servant and the former servant share "Nikola" — suggests a structural point. In the Petkoffs' world, servants are interchangeable; the name persists, even as the individual changes. Nicola's ambition to escape servitude — to open his own shop, to become his own master — is an attempt to become more than a name, to achieve the individuality that his social position denies him.
