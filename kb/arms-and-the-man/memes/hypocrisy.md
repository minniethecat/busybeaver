# Hypocrisy

Hypocrisy — the gap between professed ideals and actual behavior — is the engine of much of the play's comedy and the target of its most sustained moral critique. Every major character except [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] practices some form of hypocrisy: [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] preaches "higher love" while pursuing a servant, [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] performs romantic idealism while deceiving her father, [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] manages everyone's deceptions with matriarchal efficiency.

## Sergius's Hypocrisy

Sergius is the play's most thoroughgoing hypocrite, and Shaw presents his hypocrisy with characteristic ambivalence. Sergius's "higher love" philosophy — the idea that his spiritual devotion to Raina is untouched by his physical attraction to Louka — is transparent self-justification. But Sergius is not merely a liar; he is a man who has internalized ideals so thoroughly that he experiences his own violations of them as metaphysical crises. His confession — "I have been false to you in thought and deed — sincerely false"（002-837–838）— is simultaneously self-serving and genuine. The oxymoron "sincerely false" is Shaw's diagnosis of a particular kind of hypocrisy: the hypocrisy of a man who believes his own rationalizations.

## Raina's Hypocrisy

Raina's hypocrisy is of a different order. Where Sergius deceives others with philosophy, Raina deceives herself with aesthetics. Her romantic posturing — the "noble attitude," the heroic rhapsodies — is a performance she has practiced so long she no longer knows she is performing. Her hypocrisy is not malicious but structural: she has been raised to be a romantic heroine, and reality is the thing she cannot accommodate.

## Shaw's Treatment

Shaw's treatment of hypocrisy is characteristically unsentimental. He exposes hypocrisy without condemning the hypocrites — because he understands hypocrisy as a social product, not a personal failing. A society that demands impossible standards of purity, honor, and patriotism from its members is a society that manufactures hypocrites. The cure is not moral denunciation but social reform.

- **Target of satire**: The Victorian moral code that produces the hypocrisy it claims to condemn
- **Alternative proposed**: An ethic of honesty that accepts human imperfection rather than denying it
