# Self-Deception

The psychological engine of Shaw's *Arms and the Man* — the mechanism by which every major character (and most minor ones) sustains a self-image at odds with observable reality. Shaw treats self-deception not as a private moral failing but as the structural consequence of a romantic culture that teaches people to expect life to follow literary conventions. The play's subtitle, "An Anti-Romantic Comedy," announces its therapeutic project: to expose and, through laughter, to cure the self-deceptions that make people ridiculous, unhappy, and dangerous.

## Definition

Self-deception in the play is the gap between a character's self-conception and their actual behavior — a gap maintained through performance, rationalization, and selective attention. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] believes she is a romantic heroine; in fact she shelters an enemy soldier, falls for his pragmatism, and has lied to everyone about it. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] believes he is a man of transcendent honor; in fact he flirts with a servant and admits his celebrated cavalry charge was suicidal folly. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] believes he is the master of his household; in fact he cannot locate his own coat. Even [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], the household's formidable matriarch, sustains the pretense of Viennese gentility while managing domestic crises with Balkan pragmatism（001-038）.

Shaw's insight is that self-deception is never merely personal. It is socially produced: Byron and Pushkin supply the scripts; opera at Bucharest provides the soundtrack; military tradition supplies the costumes. The characters are not liars in any simple sense — they are performers who have forgotten they are on stage.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Self-deception is established as Raina's defining condition before she speaks a word. Shaw's opening stage direction places her on the balcony, "intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it"（001-030）. The phrase "intensely conscious" is diagnostic: Raina is not merely feeling romantic — she is watching herself feel romantic, performing the role of romantic heroine for an audience that includes herself. The photograph of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] on her chest of drawers, the box of chocolate creams, the pile of novels — Shaw's set design identifies the props of Raina's self-deception before the drama begins（001-023）.

Her confession to [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] makes the mechanism explicit. Even as she exults in [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s "heroic" cavalry charge, she reveals a suppressed doubt:

> RAINA. Our ideas of what Sergius would do — our patriotism — our heroic ideals. Oh, what faithless little creatures girls are! — I sometimes used to doubt whether they were anything but dreams. When I buckled on Sergius's sword he looked so noble: it was treason to think of disillusion or humiliation or failure. And yet — and yet — （001-089）

The hesitation — "And yet — and yet —" — is the voice of reality pressing against the romantic script. Raina immediately supplies the diagnosis herself:

> RAINA. Well, it came into my head just as he was holding me in his arms and looking into my eyes, that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that — indeed never, as far as I knew it then. （001-096）

This is the play's thesis statement. Romantic idealism is not an authentic emotion but a cultural product — literature, opera, and class aspiration have manufactured Raina's "heroic ideals." She knows this, yet she cannot act on the knowledge. The self-deception consists precisely in knowing and not-knowing simultaneously.

[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s entrance shatters the romantic frame. His revelation that he carries chocolate instead of cartridges — practical nourishment over symbolic ammunition — outrages Raina "in her most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）. His professional verdict on the cavalry charge — "nine soldiers out of ten are born fools"（001-293）— reframes heroism as stupidity. And his description of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] charging "like an operatic tenor... like Don Quixote at the windmills"（001-489）exposes the theatricality at the heart of romantic self-deception. Raina's epithet — "chocolate cream soldier"（001-566）— is her first attempt to name the gap between ideal and real.

### ACT II

Four months later, the structure of self-deception has shifted from [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]. Shaw's stage direction provides a clinical portrait of the romantic self-deceiver in crisis:

> By his brooding on the perpetual failure, not only of others, but of himself, to live up to his imaginative ideals, his consequent cynical scorn for humanity, the jejune credulity as to the absolute validity of his ideals and the unworthiness of the world in disregarding them, his wincings and mockeries under the sting of the petty disillusions which every hour spent among men brings to his infallibly quick observation... （002-250）

This is one of the most psychologically precise passages in Shaw's dramatic prose. Sergius's self-deception operates through a double movement: he clings to his ideals as "absolutely valid" while simultaneously despising the world (and himself) for failing to realize them. The result is the "half tragic, half ironic air"（002-255）of the Byronic pose — a posture that converts failure into style.

Sergius's confession to [[louka|Louka]] is the act's psychological climax. Immediately after declaring "the higher love"（002-462）to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], he corners the servant:

> SERGIUS. What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, say if he saw me now? What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here? （002-509）

The third-person self-reference is the linguistic signature of self-deception. Sergius speaks of himself as a collection of personae — "the hero," "the apostle," "the half dozen Sergiuses" — each a role sustained for a different audience. The self has become a repertory company, and the actor has lost track of which performance is real. When he adds, "do you know what the higher love is? Very fatiguing thing to keep up for any length of time, Louka. One feels the need of some relief after it"（002-496）, he admits that even his most cherished ideals are performances that exhaust him.

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s self-deception, meanwhile, has developed a new layer. She now maintains two contradictory performances: the faithful fiancee for [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and the romantic heroine for herself. Her admission — "I don't care whether he finds out about the chocolate cream soldier or not. I half hope he may"（002-719）— reveals a self that is beginning to tire of its own deceptions.

### ACT III

The third act is the play's therapeutic climax: self-deceptions are exposed and, in Shaw's comic universe, largely cured.

The pivotal scene is Raina's confession to [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]. When he tells her that her "noble attitude" and "thrilling voice" make it "impossible to believe a single word you say"（003-269）, Raina does something unprecedented: she drops the performance:

> RAINA. How strange it is to be talked to in such a way! You know, I've always gone on like that — I mean the noble attitude and the thrilling voice. I did it when I was a tiny child to my nurse. She believed in it. I do it before my parents. They believe in it. I do it before Sergius. He believes in it. （003-298）

This is the play's most profound analysis of self-deception. Raina reveals that her romantic performance is not a recent affectation but a lifelong survival strategy — a script she learned in childhood and has never been able to set down. The repetition of "they believe in it" underscores the collusive nature of self-deception: it requires an audience. Bluntschli is the first person who refuses to play along, and in doing so he liberates her.

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s self-deception collapses more violently. Cornered by [[louka|Louka]], he explodes:

> SERGIUS. Mockery, mockery everywhere: everything I think is mocked by everything I do. Coward, liar, fool! Shall I kill myself like a man, or live and pretend to laugh at myself? （003-612）

"Everything I think is mocked by everything I do" — this is self-deception's terminal crisis, the moment when the gap between self-image and behavior becomes unbridgeable. The question "live and pretend to laugh" exposes the tragic dimension beneath the comedy: even Sergius's recovery might be just another performance.

The play's resolution — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] rejecting the Byronic hero for the "chocolate cream soldier"（003-1202）, [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] choosing [[louka|Louka]] over the "higher love" — is Shaw's argument that self-deception can be overcome, not through moral exhortation but through the clarifying shock of honest speech. When Bluntschli tells Raina "You are a romantic idiot"（003-1109）and she retorts "Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three"（003-1111）, mutual disillusionment becomes the foundation of a genuine relationship.

## Self-Deception as the Play's Central Theme

Self-deception is not one theme among many in *Arms and the Man* — it is the master theme that organizes all others. [[romanticism]] is the *content* of the characters' self-deceptions (what they believe about themselves); self-deception is the *mechanism* (how they sustain those beliefs against contrary evidence). [[realism]] is the proposed cure.

Shaw structures the play as a diagnostic clinic. Each act examines a different stage of the disease:

- **ACT I: Inoculation.** The romantic self-deception is established (Raina's pose) and challenged (Bluntschli's realism). The disease is identified but not yet treated.
- **ACT II: Metastasis.** The self-deception spreads. Sergius reveals that the Byronic hero is a hollow performance. Raina's maintenance of two contradictory self-images becomes unsustainable. Even the servants ([[nicola|Nicola]] and [[louka|Louka]]) are implicated in the economy of performance and concealment.
- **ACT III: Crisis and Cure.** Self-deceptions collapse under pressure of truth-telling. Characters who can acknowledge their self-deceptions (Raina, Sergius) find happiness; those who cannot are left to their comic irrelevance (Major Petkoff, who ends the play as clueless as he began).

What distinguishes Shaw's treatment from mere satire is its psychological generosity. He does not condemn the self-deceivers — he diagnoses their condition as the predictable consequence of a culture that makes impossible demands. Raina and Sergius are victims of Byron and Pushkin, of a literary tradition that teaches young people to expect life to follow operatic conventions. Their awakening is not a moral conversion but an intellectual one: they learn to see clearly.

The play's therapeutic optimism is characteristically Shavian. Self-deception can be cured — not by introspection alone, but by encountering someone who refuses to be deceived. Bluntschli's function in the play is not merely to represent realism but to *enact* it: his honest speech creates the conditions in which others can stop performing. This is why the play remains comic rather than tragic: the truth, once spoken, is not devastating but liberating.

## Critical Perspectives

The play's anatomy of self-deception has drawn sustained critical attention. Eric Bentley, in *Bernard Shaw* (1957), identifies self-deception as Shaw's great subject across the entire canon, arguing that *Arms and the Man* is "the first play in which Shaw fully masters the comic technique of exposing self-deception not through sermon but through situation."

Later critics have read the play's treatment of self-deception through psychological and feminist lenses. Margery M. Morgan, in *The Shavian Playground* (1972), argues that Raina's confession — "I've always gone on like that" — is a proto-psychoanalytic insight: Shaw anticipates the twentieth-century understanding of personality as performance learned in childhood. The "noble attitude" and "thrilling voice" are what a later age would call a defense mechanism.

Feminist critics have noted that self-deception in the play is gendered. Raina's romanticism and Catherine's Viennese pretensions are forms of self-deception imposed by a patriarchal culture that offers women only a limited set of social scripts — the romantic heroine, the grand dame, the servant. Raina's liberation consists not in abandoning femininity but in choosing her own script: she will be a "chocolate cream soldier's" wife rather than a Byronic hero's muse.

> Shaw's genius in this play is to make us laugh at self-deception without despising the self-deceivers. Raina and Sergius are fools, but they are not villains; their follies are the follies of their culture, and their awakening is the awakening Shaw wishes for his audience. — Michael Holroyd, *Bernard Shaw* (1988)

> The play reverses the usual romantic comedy formula: instead of the lovers overcoming obstacles to find each other, they overcome their own illusions to find themselves. — Eric Bentley, *Bernard Shaw* (1957)

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of self-deception reflects his Fabian socialism and his Ibsenite conviction that social progress begins with clear sight. He distinguishes between:

- **The disease**: Self-deception sustained by cultural scripts — romantic literature, military glory, aristocratic honor, operatic passion.
- **The mechanism**: Performance maintained by collusive audiences (parents, servants, the culture at large).
- **The cure**: Not cynicism (Sergius's first, failed response) but honest self-examination fostered by encounters with honest others (Bluntschli's therapeutic candor).

**Satirical targets**: Byronism and the romantic literary tradition; operatic conventions (Verdi's *Ernani*); military hagiography; Victorian gender ideals; Bulgarian national romanticism; the class system that requires servants to sustain their masters' self-deceptions.

**Alternative**: The Shavian ideal of clear-sighted professionalism — embodied by [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] — in which one knows one's age, assets, limitations, and actual desires, and acts accordingly.
