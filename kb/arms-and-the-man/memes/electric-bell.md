# Electric Bell

The electric bell in the Petkoff library is one of Shaw's most economical comic devices — a piece of modern technology that, like the Petkoffs themselves, aspires to sophistication and achieves only dysfunction. The bell is supposed to summon servants; in practice, it never works, and the characters resort to shouting or sending messages on foot.

## Comic Function

The bell's dysfunction drives a recurring comic motif. Throughout Act II, characters attempt to use the bell — to summon [[nicola|Nicola]], to announce visitors, to assert their status — and the bell consistently fails them. [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] complains: "That bell never rings"（002-668）. [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] observes dryly that the bell has been broken "ever since we had it"（002-670）. The joke is cumulative: the Petkoffs have installed a modern convenience that has never worked, and they have never fixed it, and they continue to try to use it as though one day it might suddenly function.

## Symbolic Significance

The electric bell is a class symbol in miniature. The Petkoffs want to be modern — they want the conveniences, the technologies, the social forms of the European bourgeoisie. But their modernity is purchased, not made; they acquire the objects without developing the infrastructure to support them. The perpetually broken bell is the [[library]] that no one reads, the [[uniform]] that conceals incompetence, the "higher love" that rationalizes infidelity — an object whose form promises function but whose reality delivers only frustration.

## Shaw's Critique

The electric bell is Shaw's quietest critique of the class he depicts. The Petkoffs are not bad people; they are simply people who have acquired the surface of European civilization without its substance. The bell, in its silent mockery, makes the argument the characters cannot make about themselves.
