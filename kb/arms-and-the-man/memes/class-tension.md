# Class Tension

Class tension is the play's most persistent social dynamic, operating beneath and through the romantic plot. The Petkoffs' comfortable bourgeois existence is sustained by the labor of servants whose ambitions and resentments constantly threaten the household's equilibrium. [[louka|Louka]]'s climb from servant to lady is the play's most dramatic class event, but class tension pervades every interaction between masters and servants.

## The Servant Plot

The servant plot — Nicola and Louka's negotiation of their positions within the Petkoff household — runs parallel to the romantic plot and intersects with it decisively in Act III. Where the romantic plot involves the redistribution of romantic partners (Raina from Sergius to Bluntschli, Sergius from Raina to Louka), the servant plot involves the redistribution of class positions: Louka's escape from servitude through marriage to a gentleman.

## Class and Marriage

The play's treatment of marriage as a mechanism of class mobility is one of its most subversive features. Louka's pursuit of Sergius is explicitly a class strategy, not a romantic attachment. When she tells Sergius "You were born to a position. I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something"（003-1091–1092）, she is stating the logic of class mobility through marriage as clearly as Shaw ever does. The play does not condemn this logic; it acknowledges it as a reality of the social world.

## Key Scene

> LOUKA. You were born to a position. I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something.（003-1091–1092）

The scene between Louka and Sergius is the play's most concentrated treatment of class tension. Louka's refusal to be flattered or seduced — her insistence on marriage as the price of her availability — transforms the flirtation between gentleman and servant into a negotiation between equals. Sergius, the Byronic hero who despises social convention, is trapped by the conventions he thought he could exploit.
