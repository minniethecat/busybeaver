# Ambition

Ambition, in *Arms and the Man*, is the prerogative of servants. The Petkoffs, born to wealth and status, have no need to be ambitious; their positions are secure, their futures determined. It is [[louka|Louka]] and [[nicola|Nicola]] who must scheme, calculate, and maneuver — who must want what they do not have and plan how to get it. Shaw treats their ambition with the respect he accords to all forms of rational self-interest.

## Two Ambitions

Louka and Nicola embody two strategies of servant ambition. Nicola's is the long game: patient accumulation, strategic deference, the shop that will one day be his own. Louka's is the bold stroke: marriage to a gentleman, the servant who becomes a lady by catching a Byronic cavalry officer on the rebound. Nicola's strategy fails — Louka leaves him for Sergius — but his ambition is not condemned; it is simply outmaneuvered by a bolder competitor.

## The Morality of Ambition

Shaw refuses to moralize Louka's ambition. She is not presented as a villainess or a gold-digger; she is presented as a woman who wants more than her birth entitles her to and who uses the tools available to her — beauty, intelligence, audacity — to get it. "You were born to a position," she tells Sergius. "I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something"（003-1091–1092）. The line is a declaration of ambition as a moral right, and Shaw does not dispute it.

## Key Scene

> LOUKA. You were born to a position. I was born to nothing. And I mean to be something.（003-1091–1092）

The line is the play's most concentrated statement on class ambition. It is not a plea — Louka does not ask for permission — but a declaration. She means to be something, and Shaw treats her meaning as legitimate.
