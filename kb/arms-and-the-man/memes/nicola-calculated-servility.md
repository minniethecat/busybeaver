# Nicola's Calculated Servility

Nicola's agency is *positive* -- it operates through accumulation. He saves money, cultivates knowledge, builds his shop fund piece by piece. His servility is a conscious performance, and his awareness of the performance is what preserves his dignity: "I get tired of being a servant occasionally"（003-446）. The word "occasionally" is the key -- Nicola's identity is distinct from his role, and the fatigue of performing that role is evidence of the distinction.

## The Secret of Success

Nicola's philosophy is stated concisely in the servants' opening exchange in Act II:

> LOUKA. "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola."（002-079）
> NICOLA. "Yes: that's the secret of success in service."（002-081）

Nicola's reply reframes the entire question of servitude. Servility, in his account, is not identity but technique: a role one plays to accumulate capital. His complacency in the face of Louka's accusation is itself a demonstration of his method -- he is too self-possessed to be wounded by an insult he has already metabolized into strategy.

## Knowledge as Leverage

Nicola understands that the servant's real power is access to the family's secrets:

> "Look at me, ten years in their service. Do you think I know no secrets? I know things about the mistress that she wouldn't have the master know for a thousand levas."（002-063 to 002-065）

Knowledge is the servant's only leverage, and Nicola hoards secrets as insurance rather than deploying them as weapons. The [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] depends on servants to maintain its public fictions, and this dependence -- not kindness or loyalty -- is what creates the space for agency.

## Training Louka

Nicola's advice to Louka reveals both the sophistication and the moral cost of his strategy:

> "Be respectful; and make the mistress feel that no matter what you know or don't know, they can depend on you to hold your tongue"（002-074 to 002-076）

This amounts to training her in the performance of deference. The servant who acts too well becomes indistinguishable from the servant who *is* servile, and Nicola's final "you may depend on me to know my place if you get promoted"（003-493）suggests that the performance may have eaten into the self.

## The Shop and Its Limits

Nicola's vision of self-mastery is domestic and economic:

> "We shall have our evenings to ourselves; and I shall be master in my own house, I promise you."（003-454 to 003-455）

The shop, the private evenings -- this vision is not contemptible. It represents genuine, if modest, independence. Yet Louka's critique cuts deep: "When you set up your shop you will only be everybody's servant instead of somebody's servant"（003-450 to 003-451）. Nicola's strategy achieves freedom from one master only to submit to many -- the market replaces the manor.

## The Final Self-Abnegation

When Nicola later counsels Louka on how to "get on as a lady," his advice collapses the distinction between servitude and gentility:

> "The way to get on as a lady is the same as the way to get on as a servant: you've got to know your place; that's the secret of it. And you may depend on me to know my place if you get promoted."（003-491 to 003-493）

The final sentence is breathtaking in its self-abnegation: Nicola offers to accept a subordinate role within Louka's future marriage, knowing his place even as her place changes. Whether this is loyalty, self-interest, or the soul of a servant finally revealing its political meaning is left deliberately ambiguous.

## The Play's Verdict

The play's final verdict on Nicola is delivered by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], who has been outmanoeuvred by both servants:

> "Never mind whether it's heroism or baseness. Nicola's the ablest man I've met in Bulgaria."（003-1010）

Shaw's brilliance is to make Nicola's strategy rational while making it unheroic. Nicola is not a villain -- nor is he a hero. He is a product of a system that rewards servility and punishes honesty, and his response illuminates the system's structural cruelty rather than his individual morality.
