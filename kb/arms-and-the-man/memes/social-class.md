# Social Class

The rigid yet permeable class structure of late 19th-century [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n society, dramatized through the relationships between masters and servants in the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]]. Shaw uses class to expose the gap between romantic pretension and material reality — and to argue, with unsparing clarity, that the servant class possesses the intelligence and moral realism their masters conspicuously lack.

## Definition

Social class in *Arms and the Man* refers to the hierarchical ordering of Bulgarian society along lines of birth, wealth, occupation, and military rank. The Petkoff household — with its officers, its servants, and its aspirational bourgeoisie — functions as a laboratory in which Shaw tests the claims of heredity, breeding, and "natural" superiority against the evidence of actual human competence. The play's opening stage direction encapsulates the hybrid, unstable class identity of the Bulgarian elite: "half rich Bulgarian, half cheap Viennese"（001-005 to 001-006）. From the outset, class is presented not as a stable inheritance but as a performance — something bought, stitched together, and anxiously displayed.

Shaw distinguishes between *class as position* (the rank one happens to occupy) and *class as consciousness* (the habit of deference or defiance one internalizes). The play's central class argument turns on this distinction: Louka refuses the consciousness of a servant while remaining, for most of the action, a servant in position; Nicola embraces the servant's consciousness as a strategy for material advancement. No character, high or low, escapes the play's interrogation of whether rank reflects anything more than the accident of birth.

## In the Play

### ACT I: The Bedroom as Class Stage

The class structure is established through [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic idealism, which is inseparable from her position as a wealthy officer's daughter. Her engagement to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] — "the hero of the hour, the idol of the regiment" — is as much a class alliance as a romantic one. Raina's mother [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]], a woman who "is determined to be a Viennese lady"（001-038）, models the aspirational anxiety that defines the provincial elite: neither fully European nor contentedly Bulgarian, the Petkoffs inhabit a precarious class perch.

When [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] enters her bedroom, the class distance between a fugitive mercenary and a lady is immediately and comically evident. Raina asserts her rank with the declaration: "I am a Petkoff... the Petkoffs, the richest and best known in our country"（001-616 to 001-621）. Bluntschli's deflating reply — "Oh, yes, of course. I beg your pardon. The Petkoffs, to be sure. How stupid of me!"（001-623 to 001-624） — is deliberately unconvincing: a Swiss professional soldier, he assesses social claims with the same pragmatism he applies to ammunition.

His dietary preference for chocolate over bullets outrages Raina's "most cherished ideals of manhood"（001-398）. The chocolate creams embody Shaw's argument that class-based expectations of masculine heroism are a sentimental fiction — a real soldier carriers food, not romance. Bluntschli's class identity as a Swiss mercenary is itself a category disruption: he is neither aristocrat nor peasant, neither Bulgarian patriot nor Servian enemy, but a professional who sells competence to the highest bidder. His famous observation — "nine soldiers out of ten are born fools"（001-293） — is as much a class statement as a military one.

**The Library as Class Symbol.** Raina's announcement — "Do you know what a library is? ... Yes, we have one, the only one in Bulgaria"（001-647 to 001-651） — reveals the library as the Petkoffs' premier status token. It is not a place of reading but a prop in the performance of civilization. Bluntschli's response — "Actually a real library! I should like to see that"（001-653） — is poised between genuine curiosity and gentle mockery. Raina presses the point: "I tell you these things to shew you that you are not in the house of ignorant country folk who would kill you the moment they saw your Servian uniform, but among civilized people"（001-655 to 001-658）. The irony is dense: the Petkoffs' claim to enlightened civility depends on a roomfull of books they evidently do not read, in a house where they "go to Bucharest every year for the opera season"（001-658） — yet cannot recognize the practical competence of a Swiss mercenary as a form of intelligence superior to their own.

### ACT II: The Servant War

The second act shifts the play's class lens from the bedroom to the garden — and from the pretensions of the elite to the strategies of their servants. The opening stage direction introduces [[nicola|Nicola]] with precise class diagnosis: he possesses "the complacency of the servant who values himself on his rank in servility, and the imperturbability of the accurate calculator who has no illusions"（002-018 to 002-020）. In Nicola, Shaw creates a servant who has internalized the class system so completely that he treats servility as a professional credential.

**Nicola's Pragmatism.** Nicola's advice to [[louka|Louka]] constitutes the play's most sustained defence of class accommodation: "Be warned in time, Louka: mend your manners. I know the mistress. She is so grand that she never dreams that any servant could dare to be disrespectful to her; but if she once suspects that you are defying her, out you go"（002-025 to 002-028）. His calculus is transparently economic: "When I leave their service and start a shop in Sofia, their custom will be half my capital: their bad word would ruin me"（002-038 to 002-039）. Nicola's vision of social mobility is patiently accumulative — climb the ladder, keep the secrets, and eventually trade servitude for shopkeeping. His famous counsel — "Child, you don't know the power such high people have over the like of you and me when we try to rise out of our poverty against them"（002-061 to 002-062） — is realistic rather than craven: he has spent ten years in service and has learned that power, not merit, structures social relations.

**Louka's Defiance.** Louka's response to Nicola is the play's revolutionary class manifesto in miniature:

> "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola."（002-079）

Nicola's reply — "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-081） — could stand as the motto of a class system that rewards internalized subordination. Louka's parting shot, delivered as she carries the coffee tray into the house, echoes across the rest of the play:

> "You'll never put the soul of a servant into me."（002-093）

The contrast between Nicola and Louka structures Shaw's class argument. Nicola represents *accommodation*: he accepts the class system and works it to his long-term advantage. Louka represents *defiance*: she refuses the servant's consciousness and demands entry into the elite on her own terms. Shaw refuses to sentimentalize either strategy. Nicola's pragmatism is unheroic but materially rational; Louka's defiance is admirable but powered by secrets, manipulation, and sexual attraction as much as by principle.

**Petkoff's Class Anxiety.** [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s concern with appearances is the comic underbelly of elite class identity. His proudest achievement in the war is informational rather than military: "I did my best. I took care to let them know that we had a library"（002-186）. The line is devastating. Petkoff believes that possession of a library confers status equivalent to — or greater than — military competence. His struggle to adapt to "civilized" customs — the electric bell, the refusal to shout for servants — reveals a man whose class position has outpaced his class habitus. Shaw's satire here is affectionate but sharp: the Bulgarian elite, he implies, is a generation removed from the farm and still learning the manners it claims as its birthright.

**Louka and Sergius: The Erotics of Class.** The encounters between [[louka|Louka]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] in Act II inject sexual tension into the class dynamic. Louka's accusation — "Gentlefolk are all alike — you making love to me behind Miss Raina's back, and she doing the same behind yours"（002-542 to 002-544） — collapses the distance between upstairs and downstairs through the shared language of infidelity. Sergius's response — retreating into "freezing politeness"（002-550） and invoking the gentleman's code — reveals how class decorum functions as a shield against uncomfortable truths. When he attempts chivalric apology after hurting Louka, her reply is one of the play's sharpest class retorts:

> "That sort of apology may satisfy a lady. Of what use is it to a servant?"（002-631 to 002-632）

The line cuts through the fiction that politeness transcends class. Chivalry, Louka understands, is a code written by and for the gentlefolk; a servant who accepts its terms only confirms her subordination.

### ACT III: The Library as Contested Territory

The library — introduced in Act I as a prestige object and in Act II as a comic refrain — becomes, in Act III, the literal stage for the play's class resolution. The stage direction is devastating:

> "It is not much of a library, its literary equipment consisting of a single fixed shelf stocked with old paper-covered novels, broken backed, coffee stained, torn and thumbed, and a couple of little hanging shelves with a few gift books on them, the rest of the wall space being occupied by trophies of war and the chase."（003-001 to 003-006）

The great Petkoff library is revealed to be a shabby sitting room decorated with hunting trophies. The books are cheap editions, damaged and unread. The room's "handsome effect"（003-014） comes from Turkish cushions and divans, not from literature. Shaw's irony is architectural: the library, the Petkoffs' premier claim to civilized status, is a space organized around comfort and display, not reading. The "small kitchen table, much the worse for wear, fitted as a writing table"（003-016 to 003-017） at which Bluntschli works — efficiently, professionally, without fuss — silently rebukes the ornamental pretensions that surround it.

**Louka and Nicola: The Class Settlement.** The extended confrontation between Louka and Nicola in Act III brings the servant conflict to its climax. Nicola, humiliated by the Petkoffs' treatment of him, confesses a moment of class fatigue:

> "I get tired of being a servant occasionally."（003-446）

This admission of exhaustion is the closest Nicola comes to Louka's position — and Louka responds not with solidarity but with contempt:

> "Yes: sell your manhood for thirty levas, and buy me for ten! Keep your money. You were born to be a servant. I was not. When you set up your shop you will only be everybody's servant instead of somebody's servant."（003-448 to 003-451）

The passage distills the play's class argument into a single exchange. Nicola views money — the thirty levas, the future shop — as the instrument of class mobility. Louka views it as the price of continued servitude. "You were born to be a servant. I was not" is Louka's essentialist class reversal: she appropriates the language of birthright (normally the elite's rhetorical weapon) and turns it against Nicola. But Louka's self-identification is itself a kind of performance — she was, in fact, born a servant; her claim to inherent superiority is no more demonstrable than the Petkoffs'.

Nicola's final revelation — that his engagement to Louka was a fiction, a "protection" for a woman with "a soul above her station"（003-999） — and his declaration that he looks forward to "her custom and recommendation should she marry into the nobility"（003-1001 to 003-1002） reframes his entire strategy. Nicola is not merely a complacent servant; he is a long-term investor in Louka's social ascent, positioning himself to benefit from her success. Bluntschli's assessment — "Nicola's the ablest man I've met in Bulgaria"（003-1010 to 003-1011） — endorses Nicola's class pragmatism as the most effective intelligence in the play.

**Louka and Sergius: The Courage Debate.** The Louka-Sergius exchange about courage is the play's most systematic dismantling of class-based assumptions about character:

> "Did you find in the charge that the men whose fathers are poor like mine were any less brave than the men who are rich like you?"（003-546 to 003-547）

Sergius's honest answer — "Not a bit. They all slashed and cursed and yelled like heroes"（003-549 to 003-550） — concedes the empirical equality of courage across class lines. But his analysis goes further, exposing the structural cowardice that class hierarchy imposes:

> "Psha! the courage to rage and kill is cheap... your poor men can cut throats; but they are afraid of their officers; they put up with insults and blows; they stand by and see one another punished like children — aye, and help to do it when they are ordered."（003-550 to 003-557）

The brave poor soldier is, in Sergius's account, simultaneously a killer and a coward — courageous in the charge, craven before his social superiors. The class system does not merely rank people; it deforms their character, training the poor in deference to the point where they "help to do it when they are ordered"（003-557）.

Louka's response shifts the argument from battlefield courage to the courage of social defiance:

> "Look at me! how much am I allowed to have my own will? I have to get your room ready for you — to sweep and dust, to fetch and carry. How could that degrade me if it did not degrade you to have it done for you?"（003-567 to 003-570）

This is Shaw at his most radical. Louka argues that service relationships degrade the master as much as the servant — that the Petkoffs' dependence on Louka's labor is as morally compromising as Louka's subordination is materially constraining. Her climactic vision of empowerment — "if I were Empress of Russia, above everyone in the world, then — ah, then, though according to you I could shew no courage at all; you should see, you should see"（003-570 to 003-572） — and its specific application: "I would marry the man I loved, which no other queen in Europe has the courage to do"（003-576 to 003-577）, transforms class defiance into a personal manifesto. Her final challenge to Sergius — "If I loved you, though you would be as far beneath me as I am beneath you, I would dare to be the equal of my inferior. Would you dare as much if you loved me?"（003-577 to 003-579） — inverts the class hierarchy and tests Sergius's protestations of bravery against the one challenge that matters: the courage to marry beneath one's station.

## Shaw's Perspective

Shaw uses the servant characters — [[louka|Louka]] and [[nicola|Nicola]] — as the play's most clear-eyed social critics. While the upper-class characters are trapped in [[romanticism|romantic delusion]]s about war and love, the servants understand power as it actually operates. Shaw's socialism is evident in three structural arguments the play makes about class:

**1. Class hierarchy reflects power, not merit.** The Petkoffs' status rests on wealth, land, and military rank — none of which correlates with intelligence, honesty, or practical competence. Raina's library is unread; Sergius's heroism is theatrics; Petkoff's military contribution is informational vanity. By contrast, the servants possess actual knowledge — Louka's understanding of the family's secrets, Nicola's institutional intelligence — that the masters lack. Shaw's inversion is systematic: the lower one's class position, the more clearly one sees.

**2. The class system deforms everyone it touches.** Louka's argument that service degrades the master no less than the servant is Shaw's most penetrating class insight. Sergius's dependence on Louka's labour and his simultaneous contempt for her person is a contradiction that damages his character. Raina's performance of Petkoff superiority prevents her from recognizing Bluntschli's actual superiority — his competence, his honesty, his freedom from illusion.

**3. Social mobility requires the weapons of the weak.** Louka ascends not through meritocratic fairness but through the exploitation of secrets, sexual attraction, and psychological insight — the only tools available to a servant. Nicola's long-game investment strategy — feigning engagement to Louka, banking on her ascent — is morally ambiguous but structurally rational. Shaw, a lifelong socialist, refuses to romanticize the servant class: Louka is ruthless, Nicola is calculating. But their methods are consequences of the system, not indictments of their characters.

- **Satirical Target**: The pretence that class hierarchy reflects moral or intellectual superiority; the sentimental idealization of aristocratic honour; the self-deception of an elite that confuses ownership of a library with possession of culture.
- **Alternative**: A meritocratic order where competence and honesty matter more than birth — embodied provisionally in Bluntschli's professional ethos and, more radically, in Louka's demand that she be judged by her capacities rather than her station.

## Critical Perspectives

Shaw's treatment of class in *Arms and the Man* has been read through multiple critical lenses:

**Fabian Socialist Context.** Shaw joined the Fabian Society in 1884, a decade before *Arms and the Man* (1894). The Fabian commitment to gradualism — social transformation through permeation of existing institutions rather than revolution — is reflected in the play's class settlement. Louka does not overturn the class system; she marries into it. Nicola does not rebel; he saves and plans a shop. The play's resolution is reformist, not revolutionary: the elite opens to admit talent (Louka, Bluntschli) while the structure of property and rank remains intact.

**The Ibsenite Inheritance.** Contemporary critics noted Shaw's debt to Ibsen's *A Doll's House* (1879) and *The Wild Duck* (1884). Louka's frankness about class and gender recalls Nora's awakening; the library that symbolizes false consciousness echoes the Ekdal household's attic. But Shaw's treatment of class is more systemic than Ibsen's — the servants, not just the masters, are fully realized characters with competing class strategies.

**Servant Literature and the "New Woman."** The 1890s saw a surge of literary interest in servant characters and class mobility (Gissing's *The Nether World*, 1889; Moore's *Esther Waters*, 1894). Louka belongs to a recognizable type — the servant who refuses her station — but Shaw gives her a psychological density unusual for the period. Her class defiance is intertwined with her sexual agency in ways that anticipate the "New Woman" fiction of the fin de siècle. Eric Bentley observed that Louka is "the first Shaw heroine to combine class consciousness with erotic power" (*Bernard Shaw*, 1957).

**Postcolonial Readings.** More recent criticism has examined the play's class dynamics in the context of Bulgarian national identity. Bulgaria had achieved autonomy from the Ottoman Empire only in 1878, seven years before the play's 1885 setting. The Petkoffs' anxious performance of European refinement — the Viennese furniture, the Bucharest opera, the pretentious library — can be read as the class behaviour of a recently liberated nation performing "civilization" for external audiences. Raina's declaration that they possess "the only [library] in Bulgaria"（001-651） marks not merely class distinction but national distinction: the Petkoffs are performing Bulgaria's modernity as well as their own status.

- Bentley, Eric. *Bernard Shaw: A Reconsideration*. New Directions, 1957. — The foundational critical study; chapters on the early plays situate Shaw's class critique within Fabian gradualism.
- Dukore, Bernard F. *Bernard Shaw, Playwright: Aspects of Shavian Drama*. University of Missouri Press, 1973. — Detailed structural analysis of Shaw's dramatic method, including his handling of servant characters and class inversion.
- Holroyd, Michael. *Bernard Shaw: The Search for Love, 1856–1898*. Chatto & Windus, 1988. — The definitive biography; volume one covers Shaw's Fabian years and the composition of *Arms and the Man*.
- Innes, Christopher, ed. *The Cambridge Companion to George Bernard Shaw*. Cambridge University Press, 1998. — Essays by multiple scholars on Shaw's politics, including his treatment of class, gender, and empire.
- Shaw, Bernard. *The Intelligent Woman's Guide to Socialism and Capitalism*. Constable, 1928. — Shaw's own sustained argument for socialist reorganization, useful for understanding the political philosophy behind the play's class critique.
- Wisenthal, J.L. *The Marriage of Contraries: Bernard Shaw's Middle Plays*. Harvard University Press, 1974. — Reading of the "unpleasant" and "pleasant" plays as a unified body of work; includes analysis of class mobility across Shaw's dramatic oeuvre.
- Gahan, Peter. *Shaw Shadows: Rereading the Texts of Bernard Shaw*. University Press of Florida, 2004. — Deconstructionist readings of Shaw, including the class/gender nexus in Louka's characterization.
