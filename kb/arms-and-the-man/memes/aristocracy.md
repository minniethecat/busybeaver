# Aristocracy

Aristocracy — the hereditary ruling class whose values dominate Victorian society — is an absent presence in the play, invoked through [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s pretensions and the Petkoffs' aspirations. Shaw's comic treatment of aristocratic values is part of his larger democratic argument, and *Arms and the Man* is, among other things, a campaign to replace aristocratic ideals with professional competence.

## Aristocratic Values

The play identifies and satirizes a cluster of aristocratic values:

1. **Honor:** The code that demands duels over slights and death over dishonor. Shaw's response: the duel avoided, the hotel inventory substituted.

2. **Display:** The aristocratic emphasis on appearance, uniform, and performance. Shaw's response: the coat that replaces the uniform, the competence that makes display unnecessary.

3. **Blood:** The assumption that worth is inherited rather than achieved. Shaw's response: the professional soldier and the ambitious servant, whose worth is demonstrated rather than claimed.

## The Petkoffs' Aspirations

The Petkoffs are not aristocrats — their wealth comes from land, not titles — but they aspire to aristocratic status. Their [[library]] (a room of books no one reads), their [[uniform]] (a sign of military rank that confers social prestige), their elaborate social ceremonies — all are borrowings from an aristocratic culture they admire without fully understanding. Shaw's treatment of this aspiration is tender rather than contemptuous: the Petkoffs are comic but not villainous.

## Shaw's Democratic Argument

Shaw's critique of aristocracy is part of his broader Fabian argument for a society organized around competence rather than inheritance. The professional class that Bluntschli represents — the class that rises by ability rather than birth — is, in Shaw's analysis, the class that should govern. The play's comic treatment of aristocratic values is the sugar coating on a serious political argument.
