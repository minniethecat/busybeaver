# Heroism

Heroism is the value the play most thoroughly dismantles. The word "hero" appears in Raina's opening line — "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）— and is systematically emptied of meaning over the three acts that follow. By the final curtain, the only "heroic" action left standing is Bluntschli's quiet competence — and Shaw refuses to call it heroism at all.

## Two Models of Heroism

The play presents two competing models of the heroic. The romantic model — exemplified by [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge — defines heroism as spectacular risk-taking in pursuit of glory. The professional model — exemplified by [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s survival — defines heroism as the effective performance of one's duties under adverse conditions. The play's argument is that the first model is not only false but dangerous, and the second model is not "heroic" at all but merely competent.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Raina's opening rhapsody establishes the romantic model at its most intense. Her worship of Sergius's photograph — "He is just as splendid in real life as he is in the photograph"（001-057）— is hero-worship in its purest form: the hero as image, as icon, as object of adoration. The rest of the act systematically demolishes this idol. Bluntschli, the anti-hero, enters not in glory but in exhaustion; he describes the heroic charge not as triumph but as folly; he reveals that the hero, far from being "splendid," is a fool who "ought to be courtmartialled"（001-499）.

### ACT II

The hero in peacetime is even less impressive. Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, spends Act II engaged in a tawdry flirtation with a servant and delivering grandiose speeches about "higher love" that transparently rationalize his infidelity. Heroism, in this context, is revealed as a costume — something a man wears in public while pursuing private satisfactions.

### ACT III

The hero is finally displaced by the professional. Bluntschli — without a cavalry charge, without a photograph, without a single heroic line — emerges as the play's most admirable figure. His heroism, if it can be called that, consists of competence, honesty, and a refusal to pretend to be anything other than what he is.

## Key Scene

> RAINA. My hero! My hero!（001-064）

The play's opening line, delivered as Raina gazes at Sergius's photograph, is the thesis the play will spend three acts refuting. "Hero" is the word Shaw most wants to retire from the English language — or at least to redefine, stripping it of its martial glamour and restoring it to its root meaning: a person who acts effectively.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's critique of heroism is inseparable from his critique of war. The heroic ideal, in his analysis, is a recruitment tool — a fantasy that persuades young men to risk their lives and young women to admire them for it. By replacing the hero with the professional, Shaw attempts to starve the war machine of its cultural fuel.

- **Target of satire**: The cult of the military hero that sustains the appetite for war
- **Alternative proposed**: The competent professional — skilled, honest, and indifferent to glory — as a more admirable and less dangerous ideal
