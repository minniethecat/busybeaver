# Confession

Confession is the play's principal dramatic mechanism. Scene after scene is structured around a character admitting something previously concealed: Bluntschli confesses his flight, Raina confesses her change of heart, Sergius confesses his infidelity. Each confession advances the plot and deepens the play's argument about [[honesty]] and [[hypocrisy]].

## The Structure of Confession

Shavian confession has a distinctive structure. A character who has been maintaining a romantic fiction is brought, by pressure of circumstance, to admit the truth. The confession is comic because the truth is always more mundane than the fiction it replaces (Bluntschli's flight is not cowardice but common sense; Raina's inconstancy is not tragedy but growth). And it is liberating because the truth, once admitted, frees the character from the burden of performance.

## Confession as Liberation

The play's most significant confession is Raina's final admission: "I may not be good, but I am real"（003-1376）. The line is a confession (she is not the idealized heroine she has performed) and a liberation (she no longer needs to be). The confession functions not as an admission of guilt but as a declaration of authenticity — a shedding of the romantic costume in favor of the real self beneath.

## Shaw's Theology of Confession

Shaw, an atheist and a rationalist, treats confession not as a religious sacrament but as a psychological necessity. The characters who confess are not saved by divine grace but liberated by their own honesty. The play's argument is that truth-telling — about oneself, about one's desires, about the world — is its own reward, and that the alternative (the maintenance of romantic fictions) is a form of imprisonment.
