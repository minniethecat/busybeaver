# Age

## Definition

In *Arms and the Man*, **age** functions not merely as a biological fact but as a dramatic axis along which Shaw organizes his critique of romantic idealism. The young characters — Raina and Sergius — embody the naivety of untested ideals, while the older, more experienced characters — Bluntschli, Catherine, Petkoff — represent various accommodations with reality. Shaw uses age to dramatise the process by which romantic illusions are worn down by experience [002-063].

## In the Play

Bluntschli explicitly reckons with Raina's age as part of his pragmatic assessment of her character: "He reckons up what he can guess about Raina — her age, her social position, her character" [002-443].

This calculation, delivered with professional detachment, underscores the play's broader argument that experience — not abstract ideals — is the true measure of a person.

Raina's youth is foregrounded from the opening scene, which describes "a young lady, intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty is a part of it" [001-063]. The stage direction frames youthful self-consciousness as both a dramatic asset and a target of Shaw's irony. Raina's belief in her own romantic exceptionalism is inseparable from her age.

The play's resolution hinges on the generational contrast between Raina's romantic posturing and Bluntschli's seasoned pragmatism. By Act III, Raina has begun to shed her youthful illusions, a maturation that parallels the play's thematic movement from romanticism to realism.

## Key Characters

- **Raina Petkoff** — embodies youthful idealism, self-consciously performing the role of the romantic heroine [001-065]
- **Captain Bluntschli** — represents mature pragmatism; his professional experience as a soldier has stripped away any illusions about war or love [002-437]
- **Sergius Saranoff** — despite being older than Raina, shares her romantic immaturity; his posing and self-dramatisation are adolescent in quality
- **Catherine Petkoff** — occupies a middle ground; old enough to know better but still invested in romantic social conventions

## Key Scenes

Raina's bedroom encounter with Bluntschli (Act I) establishes the youth-experience polarity. Bluntschli's unromantic practicality — asking for food, carrying chocolates instead of ammunition — directly punctures Raina's youthful fantasy of the heroic cavalry charge [001-191].

In Act III, the age-experience theme culminates when Bluntschli reveals his professional competence and mature understanding of both warfare and human nature, in contrast to Sergius's adolescent romantic posturing [003-360].

## Shaw's Position

Shaw uses the age theme to advance his Fabian argument that progress comes through experience and practical wisdom, not through romantic heroics. Youth, in Shaw's dramatic economy, is a temporary affliction cured by exposure to reality — a position consistent with his broader critique of Victorian idealism.
