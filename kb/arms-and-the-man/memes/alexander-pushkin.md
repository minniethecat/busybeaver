# Alexander Pushkin

Alexander Sergeyevich Pushkin (1799–1837), Russia's greatest poet and the founder of modern Russian literature, is referenced in *Arms and the Man* as a formative influence on [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]]'s romantic imagination. Alongside [[lord-byron|Byron]] and Verdi, Pushkin supplies the literary models through which Raina has learned to interpret — and misrecognize — the world.

## Pushkin in the Play

Pushkin is not mentioned by name in the dialogue, but his presence is felt in Raina's literary sensibility. The Romantic tradition Pushkin embodied — the poetry of passionate love, heroic death, and national destiny — is precisely the tradition Shaw's play dismantles. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] calls [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] "My hero! My hero!"（001-064）, she is speaking the language Pushkin taught her; when she later admits "I may not be good, but I am real"（003-1376）, she is leaving Pushkin behind.

## Pushkin and Balkan Literary Culture

Pushkin's influence on Bulgarian literary culture in the late nineteenth century was substantial. As Russian influence in the Balkans expanded after the [[treaty-of-berlin|Treaty of Berlin]], Russian literature — particularly Pushkin, Turgenev, and Tolstoy — became a marker of cultivation among the Bulgarian elite. Raina's familiarity with Pushkin is thus historically accurate: the daughter of a wealthy Bulgarian family in 1885 would have encountered Russian literature as part of her education.

## Shaw's Target

Shaw's implicit critique of Pushkin is part of his larger argument against Romantic idealism. By aligning Raina's romantic sensibility with Pushkin — a poet whose work is saturated with duels, passion, honor, and tragic death — Shaw suggests that literature itself is complicit in the production of false consciousness. Raina does not naturally see the world through romantic categories; she has been taught to, by Pushkin and Byron and the whole apparatus of nineteenth-century literary culture.
