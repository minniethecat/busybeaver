# Treaty of Berlin (1878)

The Treaty of Berlin, signed on July 13, 1878, was the culmination of the Congress of Berlin convened by the Great Powers to revise the Treaty of San Stefano, which had ended the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878. Dominated by Otto von Bismarck as "honest broker," the Congress represented the interests of Britain, Austria-Hungary, Russia, Germany, France, Italy, and the Ottoman Empire.

The treaty's most consequential provision for the world of Arms and the Man was the creation of an autonomous Principality of Bulgaria — significantly smaller than the "Greater Bulgaria" Russia had envisioned at San Stefano. Eastern Rumelia was separated from Bulgaria and placed under nominal Ottoman suzerainty, setting the stage for the 1885 unification that triggered the Serbo-Bulgarian War.

Serbia emerged from the Berlin settlement with grievances. King Milan Obrenovic had hoped for territorial expansion, but the treaty recognized Serbian independence without the gains he sought. Austria-Hungary's occupation of Bosnia-Herzegovina also frustrated Serbian ambitions. These resentments, combined with Bulgaria's 1885 annexation of Eastern Rumelia, provided the spark for Serbia's disastrous 1885 invasion — the war that frames Arms and the Man.

The Berlin Congress thus shaped the entire geopolitical architecture within which Shaw's characters live. The "civilized" diplomacy of the Great Powers, conducted in a distant capital, created the conditions for Balkan conflict — a irony that aligns with Shaw's critique of the romance surrounding both war and politics.
