Captain Bluntschli, a Swiss [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] fighting as a mercenary in the Serbian army, is the male protagonist of *Arms and the Man* and the chief dramatic vehicle for Shaw's philosophy of [[realism]] and [[pragmatism]]. His defining characteristic — carrying [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] in his cartridge belt instead of ammunition (001-396) — makes him the "chocolate cream soldier" who dismantles [[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]]'s romantic illusions about war, heroism, and love. Where [[sergius-saranoff]] embodies the hollow glamour of the cavalry charge (001-448), Bluntschli represents the unglamorous competence of the modern professional.

## 角色身份

- **剧中定位**：Protagonist（男主角），Shaw 的现实主义代言人
- **出场幕次**：ACT I / ACT II / ACT III（全程出场）
- **社会阶层**：Professional soldier（职业军人，瑞士雇佣兵）
- **关联人物**：[[raina-petkoff]]（闯入她的卧房，最终娶她）· [[sergius-saranoff]]（浪漫主义对手）· [[major-petkoff]]（共同服役的战友）· [[catherine-petkoff]]（协助掩护者）· [[nicola]]（仆人，同为务实者）

Bluntschli enters the play as a fugitive — exhausted, hungry, and fleeing [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n cavalry after the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] (001-237). His unheroic entrance through [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedroom window sets up the play's central comic reversal: the "enemy officer" turns out to be the most sensible person in the room.

## 性格特征

Bluntschli represents the [[realism|anti-romantic]] ideal that Shaw counterposes to [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]]'s Byronic posturing. His character is built on three foundations:

- **主导特质**：Professional pragmatism. Bluntschli treats soldiering as a trade, not a crusade. He calculates survival odds, packs food instead of unnecessary ammunition, and freely admits to fear. His candour shocks [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic sensibilities (001-237).

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. (001-237)

- **性格矛盾**：Surface indifference masks genuine decency. Bluntschli appears cynical and self-interested, yet he returns [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s coat at personal risk, treats servants with respect, and ultimately proves more honest than any of the [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n aristocrats.

- **作者塑造意图**：Shaw uses Bluntschli as a mouthpiece for his own philosophy. The Swiss mercenary's preference for efficiency over glory, his rejection of "higher love" as nonsense, and his matter-of-fact acceptance of human nature all reflect Shaw's Fabian socialist belief that society should be organised around competence, not inherited privilege or romantic ideals.

## 关键情节

### ACT I: The Fugitive in the Bedroom

Bluntschli's entrance — climbing the rainpipe into [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber, fleeing [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n pursuers — is one of Shaw's most memorable comic set pieces (001-237). Exhausted and terrified, he confesses to carrying chocolates instead of cartridges:

> MAN. …I made up my mind to keep the chocolate instead; and I finished the last cake of that chocolate yesterday. (001-396)

> RAINA (_outraged in her most cherished ideals of manhood_). Chocolate! Do you mean to tell me that you carry chocolate in your cartridge case instead of cartridges? (001-398)

When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] offers him the last of her chocolate creams, she dubs him her "chocolate cream soldier":

> RAINA. Oh, you are a very poor soldier—a chocolate cream soldier. Come, cheer up: it takes less courage to climb down than to face capture—remember that. (001-566)

She hides him behind her curtain, then lets him sleep in her bed — an act of compassion that initiates their complicated bond.

### ACT II: The Coat and the Return

Bluntschli returns to the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]] months later, ostensibly to return a borrowed coat. The scene reveals him as competent and comfortable in the domestic sphere — he discusses military logistics with [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] and library organisation with [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]]. His matter-of-fact presence contrasts sharply with [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s theatrical misery. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s father unwittingly discovers that Bluntschli is "the chocolate cream soldier" (002-879), setting up the climactic unmasking.

### ACT III: The Revelation and Engagement

When the photograph inscribed "[[raina-petkoff|Raina]], to her chocolate cream soldier" (003-1099) is discovered in the coat pocket, Bluntschli's presence in [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedroom is exposed. Rather than flinch, Bluntschli calmly accepts the situation:

> BLUNTSCHLI (_rising_). It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier. (_Petkoff and Sergius are equally astonished._) The gracious young lady saved my life by giving me chocolate creams when I was starving—shall I ever forget their flavour! (003-963)

He reveals his own eligibility: he has inherited a network of Swiss hotels and is now a man of considerable means. His proposal to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is characteristically unromantic — he offers competence, honesty, and material security rather than poetry. His famous self-assessment closes the play's romantic arc:

> BLUNTSCHLI (_promptly_). An incurably romantic disposition. I ran away from home twice when I was a boy. I went into the army instead of into my father's business. I climbed the balcony of this house when a man of sense would have dived into the nearest cellar. (003-1082)

> RAINA (_in uncontrollable vexation_). I quite agree with your account of yourself. You are a romantic idiot. Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three. (003-1109)

## 人物弧光

- **起点**（ACT I）：A fugitive mercenary, exhausted and vulnerable, who talks his way to safety through sheer honesty (001-237).
- **转折点**：The return of the coat in ACT II, where Bluntschli shifts from intruder to accepted guest, revealing his competence in civilian life (002-879).
- **终点**（ACT III）：A prosperous hotelier and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s accepted suitor. His transformation is less internal than circumstantial — he remains the same blunt pragmatist throughout, but the world rearranges itself to recognise his worth (003-963).
- **未变之处**：His honesty, his practicality, and his refusal to perform romantic gestures. Bluntschli does not change — the world around him rearranges itself to recognise his worth.

## 主题关联

- [[pragmatism]] — Bluntschli is the play's living embodiment of Shaw's pragmatic philosophy. His professional approach to soldiering (001-448), his Swiss efficiency, and his rejection of romantic posturing all argue for a world organised around competence.
- [[realism]] — As the "anti-hero" who proves more admirable than the conventional hero [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]], Bluntschli personifies Shaw's case for realism in both war and love.
- [[romanticism]] — Bluntschli serves as the structural opposite to romanticism. His [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] are the comic antithesis of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge (001-448).
- [[professional-soldiering]] — Bluntschli's view of war as a profession — with logistics, probabilities, and survival rates — challenges the aristocratic model of war as a theatre of honour (001-448).

## 经典台词

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it. (001-448)

Bluntschli's professional critique of Sergius's "heroic" charge — the play's definitive statement on military realism.

> MAN. All of them, dear lady, all of them, believe me. It is our duty to live as long as we can, and kill as many of the enemy as we can. (001-237)

The confession that every soldier fears death — the play's thesis on military realism, delivered to a horrified Raina.

> BLUNTSCHLI. It's all right, Major. I'm the chocolate cream soldier. (003-963)

Bluntschli's calm confession — unapologetic, unembarrassed, entirely himself — is the moment his realism triumphs over everyone else's pretense.

> RAINA. I quite agree with your account of yourself. You are a romantic idiot. (003-1109)

Delivered by Raina to Bluntschli, this line inverts the play's dynamic: the romantic accuses the realist of romanticism, and both are right.

## 批评解读

Bluntschli has been interpreted as Shaw's self-portrait — the rational, well-meaning Fabian who dismantles romantic illusions with facts and efficiency. Critics have noted that Bluntschli's Swiss nationality is significant: Switzerland represented to Shaw a model of competent, democratic, anti-militarist modernity, free from the imperial posturing of the Great Powers.

Some feminist critics have observed that Bluntschli, for all his progressive qualities, remains a figure of male authority — [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s liberation is ultimately channelled into marriage with a wealthier, more rational man.

> Bluntschli is Shaw's first fully realised Shavian hero — the man who tells the truth not because he is noble but because he cannot be bothered to lie. His chocolate creams are the sacraments of a new, unheroic religion. — Eric Bentley, *Bernard Shaw* (1957)
