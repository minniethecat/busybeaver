# Wealth

Wealth is the play's final truth-teller — the material reality that cuts through three acts of romantic posturing, military [[honor]], and [[higher-love|higher love]]. When [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] reveals his inheritance in the final scene, Shaw's argument becomes explicit: money matters, and it matters more honestly than the cavalries and love sonnets that pretend money doesn't exist.

## In the Play

### ACT I

Wealth is present from the opening scene without being named. The [[petkoff-house|Petkoff house]] — "a lady's bedchamber in Bulgaria" with a balcony, a chest of drawers, a pile of novels, a box of chocolate creams（001-001–023）— is a display of bourgeois prosperity. The Petkoffs are wealthy by Bulgarian standards, and their wealth underwrites Raina's romantic leisure: she can afford to read Pushkin and dream of cavalry charges because she has never had to work.

### ACT II

The class dimension of wealth becomes explicit. [[nicola|Nicola]]'s philosophy of servitude — "I know my place"（002-889）— is a philosophy of wealth distribution. He serves because he has no money; he will marry [[louka|Louka]] because together they might accumulate enough to open a shop. His famous defense of servitude — "I put my pride in my pocket"（002-870）— is a statement about the economics of dignity: pride is a luxury the poor cannot afford.

### ACT III

The wealth revelation transforms the play. Bluntschli, the penniless Swiss mercenary, is revealed as the heir to "about two hundred horses, nine thousand six hundred pair of sheets and pillow cases, twelve thousand knives and forks"（003-1294–1296）. The inventory is comic, but its effect is serious: Raina's romantic attraction to Bluntschli is now validated by material security. Shaw's point is not that Raina is mercenary but that romantic attraction always operates within material constraints, and pretending otherwise is hypocritical.

## Key Scene

The hotel inventory-as-proposal is Shaw's most concentrated statement on wealth and romance. Bluntschli offers sheets and silverware instead of love poems; Raina accepts. The comic disproportion between the romantic convention (the proposal should be a declaration of love) and the Shawian reality (the proposal is a declaration of assets) makes the argument: money is the honest language of marriage, and love is the romantic disguise.

## Shaw's Position

As a Fabian socialist, Shaw was deeply interested in the economic foundations of social life. But his treatment of wealth in the play is characteristically nuanced: he does not condemn the Petkoffs for being rich or Bluntschli for inheriting hotels. Instead, he condemns the pretense that wealth doesn't matter — the romantic convention that love transcends economics, that honor is more important than survival, that ideals are independent of the material conditions that make them possible.

- **Target of satire**: The romantic pretense that love and honor exist outside the economic order
- **Alternative proposed**: An honest acknowledgment that marriage, like soldiering, is a material arrangement — and that honesty about money is more moral than romantic hypocrisy about it
