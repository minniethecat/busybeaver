# Coat

Major Petkoff's old coat is the play's most underrated symbol. While the [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] carry the thematic weight and the [[photograph]] carries the romantic weight, the coat carries the plot: it is the object that connects the three acts, the evidence that threatens exposure, and the mundane reality beneath the military drama.

## Dramatic Function

The coat's dramatic career is remarkable for an object so ordinary. In Act I, [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] give it to [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] to disguise his escape — a servant's coat to hide an enemy officer. In Act II, [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s obsession with the missing coat ("It's not the coat I mind; it's the embarrassment"（002-613）) drives the comedy of domestic cover-up. In Act III, Bluntschli's return of the coat precipitates the final revelations.

## Symbolic Meaning

The coat is the anti-[[uniform]]. Where the [[uniform]] represents the public, the ceremonial, the performed identity, the coat represents the private, the practical, the domestic self. Major Petkoff without his coat is a man embarrassed; Major Petkoff in his uniform is an officer with authority. The coat's journey — from Petkoff's wardrobe to Bluntschli's back to Petkoff's house — traces the path of the play's argument: from military display to domestic reality and back again, transformed by the journey.

## Key Scene

> PETKOFF. It's not the coat I mind; it's the embarrassment.（002-613）

Petkoff's distinction — between the coat and the embarrassment — is the play's comic philosophy in miniature. The coat is a physical object; the embarrassment is a social reality. The play's method is to attend to the physical object (the coat, the chocolate, the photograph) while exposing the social reality it represents.
