# Noble Attitude

The phrase "noble attitude" is Raina's term for the idealized mode of being she has been taught to aspire to — a way of conducting oneself that is simultaneously ethical and aesthetic, morally elevated and dramatically satisfying. Shaw subjects this phrase to the same treatment he gives every other romantic ideal: comic deflation through contact with reality.

## Raina's Ideal

For [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], the "noble attitude" is a comprehensive ethical posture. It means behaving as a heroine in a Pushkin poem would behave — with courage, generosity, and emotional elevation. When she shelters [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], she is not merely being kind; she is performing the noble attitude, casting herself as the heroine of a romantic drama in which an enemy soldier is transformed by her mercy.

The phrase recurs throughout the play as Raina's measure of proper conduct. But the play systematically demonstrates that the noble attitude is unsustainable — that real ethical behavior requires not performance but honesty, not elevation but pragmatism.

## Shaw's Critique

Shaw's critique of the "noble attitude" is that it aestheticizes ethics — that it makes moral behavior a matter of style rather than substance. Raina is genuinely kind to Bluntschli, but her kindness is mediated through a romantic self-image that prevents her from seeing either herself or him clearly. The noble attitude, in Shaw's analysis, is a form of ethical narcissism: it is less concerned with doing good than with looking good while doing it.

## The Break from Idealism

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s final admission — "I may not be good, but I am real"（003-1376）— is her break from the noble attitude. She exchanges the aspiration to "goodness" (which was always more performance than substance) for the acceptance of "reality" (which is less flattering but more honest). The noble attitude is abandoned; the real woman remains.

## Key Scene

> RAINA. I always feel a longing to do or say something dreadful to him — to shock his propriety — to scandalize the five senses out of him!（002-716–718）

Raina's perverse longing to "shock his propriety" is the noble attitude turning against itself. The desire to scandalize is the desire to escape the performance of goodness — to do something real, even if the real thing is dreadful. The line marks the beginning of Raina's liberation from the ideal she has been performing her whole life.
