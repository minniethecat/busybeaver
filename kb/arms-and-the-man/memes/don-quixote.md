# Don Quixote

Don Quixote, the titular hero of Miguel de Cervantes's 1605 novel, appears in *Arms and the Man* as a pointed literary allusion. When [[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]] describes [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]] as "a Don Quixote flourishing like a drum major" who led a cavalry charge "he ought to have been courtmartialled for"（001-498–500）, Shaw activates a centuries-old literary archetype: the man who sees the world not as it is but as his reading has taught him it should be.

## The Quixotic Parallel

The parallel between Sergius and Don Quixote is both structural and thematic. Like Quixote, Sergius has consumed too much romantic literature — Pushkin, Byron, Verdi — and now sees the world through its lens. His cavalry charge at the [[battle-of-slivnitza|Battle of Slivnitza]] is a Quixotic gesture: magnificent, theatrical, and tactically insane. Like Quixote tilting at windmills he mistakes for giants, Sergius charges machine guns he mistakes for an opportunity for glory.

## Shaw's Use of the Allusion

Shaw's use of Don Quixote is characteristically layered. On the surface, the allusion mocks Sergius — the romantic cavalier exposed as a comic figure. But the allusion also elevates Sergius, linking him to one of Western literature's most beloved characters. Quixote is ridiculous, but he is also, in some irreducible way, noble. Shaw's Sergius is similarly ambivalent: a fool, yes, but a fool whose folly contains a critique of the world that cannot accommodate him.

## Critical Perspective

The Quixote allusion places *Arms and the Man* in a tradition of anti-romantic comedy that stretches from Cervantes through Fielding to Shaw himself. Each generation produces its Quixotes and its Sancho Panzas; Shaw's innovation was to make the Quixote figure (Sergius) explicitly a cavalry officer and the Sancho figure (Bluntschli) a Swiss hotelier. The chivalric romance is replaced by the military romance; the practical peasant is replaced by the professional soldier.
