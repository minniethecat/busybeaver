# Servant War

The second act of *Arms and the Man* shifts the play's class lens from the bedroom to the garden — and from the pretensions of the elite to the strategies of their servants. The conflict between [[louka|Louka]] and [[nicola|Nicola]] is the play's most sustained argument about whether the class system should be accommodated or defied.

## Two Servants, Two Strategies

The opening stage direction introduces [[nicola|Nicola]] with precise class diagnosis: he possesses "the complacency of the servant who values himself on his rank in servility, and the imperturbability of the accurate calculator who has no illusions"（002-018 to 002-020）. In Nicola, Shaw creates a servant who has internalized the class system so completely that he treats servility as a professional credential.

### Nicola's Pragmatism

Nicola's advice to [[louka|Louka]] constitutes the play's most sustained defence of class accommodation: "Be warned in time, Louka: mend your manners. I know the mistress. She is so grand that she never dreams that any servant could dare to be disrespectful to her; but if she once suspects that you are defying her, out you go"（002-025 to 002-028）. His calculus is transparently economic: "When I leave their service and start a shop in Sofia, their custom will be half my capital: their bad word would ruin me"（002-038 to 002-039）. Nicola's vision of social mobility is patiently accumulative — climb the ladder, keep the secrets, and eventually trade servitude for shopkeeping. His famous counsel — "Child, you don't know the power such high people have over the like of you and me when we try to rise out of our poverty against them"（002-061 to 002-062） — is realistic rather than craven: he has spent ten years in service and has learned that power, not merit, structures social relations.

### Louka's Defiance

Louka's response to Nicola is the play's revolutionary class manifesto in miniature:

> "You have the soul of a servant, Nicola."（002-079）

Nicola's reply — "Yes: that's the secret of success in service"（002-081） — could stand as the motto of a class system that rewards internalized subordination. Louka's parting shot, delivered as she carries the coffee tray into the house, echoes across the rest of the play:

> "You'll never put the soul of a servant into me."（002-093）

The contrast between Nicola and Louka structures Shaw's class argument. Nicola represents *accommodation*: he accepts the class system and works it to his long-term advantage. Louka represents *defiance*: she refuses the servant's consciousness and demands entry into the elite on her own terms. Shaw refuses to sentimentalize either strategy. Nicola's pragmatism is unheroic but materially rational; Louka's defiance is admirable but powered by secrets, manipulation, and sexual attraction as much as by principle.

## Petkoff's Class Anxiety

[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s concern with appearances is the comic underbelly of elite class identity. His proudest achievement in the war is informational rather than military: "I did my best. I took care to let them know that we had a library"（002-186）. The line is devastating. Petkoff believes that possession of a library confers status equivalent to — or greater than — military competence. His struggle to adapt to "civilized" customs — the electric bell, the refusal to shout for servants — reveals a man whose class position has outpaced his class habitus. Shaw's satire here is affectionate but sharp: the Bulgarian elite, he implies, is a generation removed from the farm and still learning the manners it claims as its birthright.

## Louka and Sergius: The Erotics of Class

The encounters between [[louka|Louka]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] in Act II inject sexual tension into the class dynamic. Louka's accusation — "Gentlefolk are all alike — you making love to me behind Miss Raina's back, and she doing the same behind yours"（002-542 to 002-544） — collapses the distance between upstairs and downstairs through the shared language of infidelity. Sergius's response — retreating into "freezing politeness"（002-550） and invoking the gentleman's code — reveals how class decorum functions as a shield against uncomfortable truths. When he attempts chivalric apology after hurting Louka, her reply is one of the play's sharpest class retorts:

> "That sort of apology may satisfy a lady. Of what use is it to a servant?"（002-631 to 002-632）

The line cuts through the fiction that politeness transcends class. Chivalry, Louka understands, is a code written by and for the gentlefolk; a servant who accepts its terms only confirms her subordination.
