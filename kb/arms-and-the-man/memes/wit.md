# Wit

Wit — the capacity for surprising, intellectually pleasing verbal combinations — is Shaw's primary dramatic instrument. The dialogue of *Arms and the Man* crackles with aphorisms, paradoxes, and inversions that simultaneously entertain and argue. The wit is not decorative but structural: it is the means by which the play's ideas are delivered, its reversals accomplished, and its audience converted.

## The Function of Wit

Shavian wit operates at multiple levels:

1. **As argument:** Every witty line carries an intellectual payload. When Bluntschli says "I always carry chocolate instead"（001-396）, the laugh delivers the argument that chocolate is more useful than ammunition.

2. **As characterization:** Wit distinguishes the characters who think clearly (Bluntschli, Louka) from those who don't (Sergius, Raina in her romantic phase). The ability to speak wittily is, in Shaw's universe, the ability to see clearly.

3. **As critique:** Wit exposes the absurdity of social conventions by simply stating them in unexpected ways. Major Petkoff's "That bell never rings"（002-668）is funnier than any satirical essay could be — and more devastating.

## The Paradox as Instrument

Shaw's favorite rhetorical figure is the paradox — the statement that appears self-contradictory but reveals a deeper truth. "Sincerely false"（002-838）, "the coward's art"（002-979）, "unsoldierly; but angelic"（001-551）— each paradox collapses a conventional opposition (sincere/false, coward/artist, soldier/angel) and forces the audience to construct a new understanding that accommodates both terms.
