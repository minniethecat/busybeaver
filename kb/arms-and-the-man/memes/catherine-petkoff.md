Catherine Petkoff, the matriarch of the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]], appears at first glance to be a conventional Victorian mother -- enthusiastic about her daughter's engagement to the dashing [[sergius-saranoff]], attentive to domestic decorum, and content to let her husband [[major-petkoff]] play the role of family patriarch. But Shaw complicates this portrait with deft economy: Catherine is the household's real manager, the keeper of its secrets, and a woman whose practical intelligence far exceeds what her society permits her to acknowledge. Her eager complicity in hiding [[captain-bluntschli]] -- and the photograph of [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] inscribed to "her chocolate cream soldier" -- reveals a pragmatic streak that complicates her romantic surface.

## 角色身份

- **剧中定位**：Mother（母亲），[[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] 家族的实际管理者
- **出场幕次**：ACT I / ACT II / ACT III
- **社会阶层**：[[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n aristocracy（保加利亚上层贵族）
- **关联人物**：[[raina-petkoff]]（女儿，浪漫主义的共鸣者）· [[major-petkoff]]（丈夫，名义上的家长）· [[sergius-saranoff]]（女儿的未婚夫，崇拜对象）· [[captain-bluntschli]]（秘密的共谋者）· [[louka]]（女仆）

Catherine enters the play in ACT I, interrupting [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s nocturnal reverie with news of the battle at [[battle-of-slivnitza|Slivnitza]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s heroic cavalry charge. Her stage directions describe her as "a woman over forty, imperiously energetic, with magnificent black hair and eyes"（001-035） -- a physical description that suggests vitality and authority, qualities her society allows her to express only through domestic channels.

## 性格特征

Catherine's character operates on two levels -- the public performance of wifely deference and the private exercise of real authority:

- **主导特质**：Enthusiastic romanticism channeled through maternal pride. Catherine's admiration for [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] and her delight in the "romantic beauty" of [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s situation are genuine, but they also serve as a socially acceptable outlet for an energy and intelligence that Victorian domesticity would otherwise stifle.

- **性格矛盾**：Public deference vs. private competence. Catherine routinely defers to [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] in company -- "My fault, madam! I hope you will overlook it"（002-912） -- yet makes the crucial decisions (hiding [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]], managing the photograph crisis) without consulting him. She performs the role of subordinate wife while exercising the authority of household commander.

- **作者塑造意图**：Shaw uses Catherine to demonstrate that the romantic worldview is not merely a youthful folly but a cultural inheritance passed from mother to daughter. Catherine has taught [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] to adore heroes and stage her emotions -- and when reality intrudes, Catherine is the first to help hide the evidence.

## 关键情节

### ACT I: The Secret-Keeper

Catherine's first major action is her decision to help [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] hide [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] reveals the fugitive in her bedroom, Catherine does not scream for her husband or demand the intruder's removal -- she assesses the situation with "imperiously energetic" practicality（001-035） and becomes [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s co-conspirator. This scene establishes the play's pattern: the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] women manage reality while the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] men perform authority.

> CATHERINE. (_scandalized_). Well! (_She strides to the left side of the bed, Raina following and standing opposite her on the right._) He's fast asleep. The brute! ... CATHERINE. (_letting him go and turning amazed to Raina_). The poor dear! Raina!!! (_She looks sternly at her daughter. The man sleeps profoundly._)
>
> （001-756 -- 001-768）

Catherine's transformation from scandalized outrage to bewildered complicity happens in the space of a dozen lines. When [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] calls the fugitive "the poor dear," Catherine's repetition -- "The poor dear! Raina!!!" -- captures the moment when maternal indulgence overrides social propriety. Shaw gives Catherine no soliloquy of decision; her complicity is instantaneous, instinctive, and entirely characteristic.

### ACT II: The Coat Crisis

When [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] returns to return [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s borrowed coat, Catherine works frantically behind the scenes to prevent her husband from discovering the truth.

> CATHERINE. Yes, here, as soon as possible. (_Vehemently._) Be quick! (_Louka runs into the house. Catherine snatches her apron off and throws it behind a bush. She then takes up the salver and uses it as a mirror, with the result that the handkerchief tied round her head follows the apron. A touch to her hair and a shake to her dressing gown makes her presentable._) Oh, how—how—how can a man be such a fool! Such a moment to select!
>
> （002-785 -- 002-791）

This stage direction is a miniature comedy of female competence: in the seconds before [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] enters, Catherine transforms herself from dishevelled housewife to dignified hostess, all while managing a crisis her husband cannot perceive. Her exasperated "Oh, how—how—how can a man be such a fool!" encompasses both [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s inconvenient timing and the entire male gender's obliviousness.

Her whispered consultation with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] is a masterpiece of comic tension:

> CATHERINE. (_The moment Louka's back is turned, Catherine swoops on him with hurried, urgent, coaxing appeal._) Captain Bluntschli, I am very glad to see you; but you must leave this house at once. (_He raises his eyebrows._) My husband has just returned, with my future son-in-law; and they know nothing. If they did, the consequences would be terrible. You are a foreigner: you do not feel our national animosities as we do.
>
> （002-797 -- 002-801）

The mistress of the house colluding with an enemy mercenary to preserve domestic appearances -- Shaw's comic irony is at its sharpest here.

### ACT III: Mother of the Bride

Catherine's final function is to preside over the resolution. When the photograph is discovered and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s secret exposed, Catherine accepts the new arrangement with characteristic practicality. Her blessing of [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s engagement to [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] -- [[captain-bluntschli|the Swiss mercenary]] she once hid in her daughter's bedroom -- completes the play's arc from romantic secrecy to realistic acceptance.

## 人物弧光

- **起点**（ACT I）：The enthusiastic mother, sharing [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic worldview and colluding in its maintenance.
- **转折点**：The coat crisis in ACT II, where Catherine's practical management of the secret reveals how much of the [[petkoff-house|Petkoff household]]'s smooth functioning depends on her unacknowledged labour.
- **终点**（ACT III）：Still the household manager, now blessing a marriage that openly acknowledges the [[realism|practical realism]] she has always practised in private.
- **未变之处**：Her competence and her maternal devotion. Catherine does not undergo a dramatic transformation -- rather, the play's events reveal the pragmatism that was always beneath her romantic surface.

## 主题关联

- [[romanticism]] -- Catherine embodies the generational transmission of romantic ideology. Her enthusiasm for [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] is not a personal quirk but a cultural inheritance she has passed to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]].
- [[realism]] -- Catherine's practical management of the [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] crisis reveals that her romanticism is, in part, a performance -- she is more capable of realistic action than she admits.
- [[gender-dynamics]] -- Catherine's position -- wielding real authority while performing deference -- is Shaw's critique of how Victorian gender ideology constrained intelligent women.
- [[social-class]] -- Catherine's class privilege enables her romanticism; her practical skills are exercised in the service of maintaining aristocratic appearances.

## 经典台词

> "My fault, madam! I hope you will overlook it."（002-912）

Catherine's reflexive apology -- even when the fault is not hers -- captures the performative deference expected of Victorian wives. Shaw gives the line to Catherine in a moment when she is, in fact, managing a crisis her husband cannot handle.

> "Now, Catherine, it's of you that he's afraid. Press him and he'll stay."（002-933）

[[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s address to his wife -- formal, slightly patronising -- encapsulates the marital dynamic Shaw critiques. Catherine, who has been running the entire situation, must wait for her husband to grant her the floor.

> "You could have annexed Servia and made Prince Alexander Emperor of the Balkans. That's what I would have done."（002-153 -- 002-154）

This line, delivered with "flashing eyes" -- "springing erect, with flashing eyes"（002-140） -- reveals the imperial ambition concealed beneath Catherine's domestic exterior. Even [[major-petkoff|Paul]], her husband, can only reply with submissive deflection: "I don't doubt it in the least, my dear." Shaw gives Catherine, not any of the military men, the play's most aggressively expansionist sentiment -- a feminist joke wrapped in a political one.

## 批评解读

Catherine has often been read as a "stock mother" figure, but feminist critics have reassessed her as a study in the unacknowledged labour of Victorian wives. Her management of the household -- including the management of its secrets -- is the invisible work that sustains the [[major-petkoff|Petkoff]] family's social position. Shaw, who was married to a woman who managed his own household and correspondence with similar efficiency, may have drawn Catherine with more sympathy than is immediately apparent.

> **Critical Note -- Catherine as Dramatic Foil**: Catherine's function in the play is not merely maternal. Structurally, she serves as Raina's mirror and future self: a woman whose romantic education (Byron, Pushkin, the Bucharest opera) has produced the same sensibility in the next generation. When Catherine tells Raina with "surging enthusiasm" that "you can't guess how splendid it is. A cavalry charge -- think of that!"（001-068 -- 001-069）, she is performing the same romanticism she has taught her daughter -- and which the play's realist argument will progressively dismantle. By the final act, Catherine has accepted a Swiss hotelier as her son-in-law without visible distress, suggesting that her romanticism, like her deference, was always partly a performance. The critical question is whether Shaw intends this as liberation (she is free to be practical) or limitation (her romanticism was never entirely her own).
