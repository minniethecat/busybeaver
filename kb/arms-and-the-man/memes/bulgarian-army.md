# Bulgarian Army

The Bulgarian Army of 1885 was a young institution — barely seven years old — that had just achieved an improbable victory over [[servia|Servia]] in the [[serbo-bulgarian-war|Serbo-Bulgarian War]]. Its officers, including [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]] and [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius Saranoff]], were the products of a military establishment that had been trained by [[russian-empire|Russian]] advisors and was now proving itself under Bulgarian command.

## The Russian Withdrawal

The most remarkable feature of the Bulgarian victory was that it was achieved without Russian assistance. After the [[bulgarian-unification|Bulgarian unification]] of September 1885, Tsar Alexander III — who opposed the unification — withdrew Russian officers from the Bulgarian army. The withdrawal was intended to cripple Bulgaria; instead, it forced the promotion of Bulgarian officers who proved more capable than their Russian predecessors.

## The Army in the Play

The Bulgarian army appears in the play only through its officers — Major Petkoff and Captain (later Major) Sergius Saranoff. But the army's culture — its hierarchies, its uniforms, its war councils, its self-importance — pervades the domestic comedy. Shaw's treatment of the army is characteristically double-edged: the institution that won the war is also the institution that produces comic incompetents like Petkoff and romantic posturers like Sergius.

## Key Scene

Major Petkoff's complaints about the [[war-cabinet|war cabinet]] — "they will have it their own way. They can never agree about anything"（002-620–621）— is the Bulgarian army at its most characteristic: a hierarchy of men who cannot agree, making decisions that affect the lives of soldiers who are not consulted.
