# Catherine: The Conventional Woman

Catherine Petkoff is often overlooked in critical discussion, dismissed as comic relief. But
her structural position in the play's gender argument is significant. She represents the
complicity of women in maintaining the gender system — not through malice but through
enthusiastic participation. Her worship of Sergius's cavalry charge, her determination to
perform Viennese aristocratic femininity, and her horror at Louka's social ascent ("Marry
Louka! Sergius: you are bound by your word to us!" [003-1045]) all mark her as a guardian
of the old order.

## Performance as Identity

Catherine is described as "determined to be a Viennese lady, and to that end wears a
fashionable tea gown on all occasions"（001-037 — 001-039）. Her femininity is a deliberate
performance of metropolitan sophistication, an assertion of class status through costume and
manner. But where Raina's performance of romantic femininity masks a growing self-awareness,
Catherine's performance is her identity — there is nothing behind it. She is what she performs.

Her worldview is gently mocked but never condemned. Shaw refuses to punish any of his female
characters for the accommodations they make to patriarchy. Catherine's Viennese aspirations
are presented as a survival strategy, not a moral failing. In a world where women's power is
limited, performing aristocratic femininity is a rational choice — one Catherine has made with
enthusiasm and consistency.

## Structural Irrelevance

The play's resolution does not punish Catherine; it simply bypasses her. The younger generation —
Raina, Louka, Sergius, Bluntschli — negotiate their own arrangements while Catherine is still
processing the news. Her maternal authority, like the patriarchal military authority she
admires, is rendered irrelevant by the play's movement toward personal autonomy. This is
perhaps the most pointed critique the play offers: the conventional woman is not villainous,
merely obsolete. Her worldview survives her, but it does not survive the play.
