# Romanticism Definition

**Romanticism** in *Arms and the Man* refers to the worldview shaped by literary and operatic conventions: war is glorious, soldiers are noble heroes, and love is an exalted spiritual union. Shaw presents this not as a personal flaw but as a cultural inheritance — the characters have absorbed these ideals from Byron, Pushkin, and Verdi operas (001-030).

The play's subtitle, "An Anti-Romantic Comedy", announces Shaw's satirical target explicitly. The comedic engine driving all three acts is the gap between romantic ideals and the unglamorous facts of soldiering, courtship, and domestic life. Every major character is entangled in this conflict: [[raina-petkoff]] worships a photographic image rather than a real man (001-170), [[sergius-saranoff]] performs heroism he cannot sustain, and [[catherine-petkoff]] reinforces the romantic worldview through her own Byronic education.

Raina herself admits the artifice in Act I:

> Well, it came into my head just as he was holding me in his arms and looking into my eyes, that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that — indeed never, as far as I knew it then. (001-096)

Shaw distinguishes between romanticism as a cultural inheritance and romanticism as self-deception. The former is an understandable product of upper-class Victorian education; the latter is the play's satirical target — believing in glorious cavalry charges and operatic love affairs while ignoring the blood, mud, and [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] of actual experience.
