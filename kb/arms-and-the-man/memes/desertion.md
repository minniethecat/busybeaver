# Desertion

Desertion, in the romantic military code, is the ultimate disgrace — the soldier who abandons his post. Shaw systematically inverts this valuation throughout *Arms and the Man*, transforming desertion from a mark of [[cowardice]] into a sign of professional intelligence.

## Definition

The play presents three forms of flight, each reframed as a rational response rather than a moral failure: Bluntschli's tactical retreat into [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s bedchamber; his deliberate refusal to carry ammunition ("I always carry chocolate instead"（001-396）); and his escape down the water pipe in [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s old coat. In each case, Shaw invites the audience to see the action not through the lens of the court-martial but through the lens of common sense.

## In the Play

### ACT I

The play opens with a scene of flight. Bluntschli, a Servian artillery officer, has been separated from his unit and is being hunted through the streets of a Bulgarian town. He climbs the water pipe into Raina's bedroom not as an act of heroism but as an act of desperation — "The last man left alive in my regiment fled for his life"（001-370）. His confession is unadorned by shame: he ran because running was the rational choice, and he would do it again.

This is the play's foundational provocation. The audience, conditioned by a century of military romance, expects a soldier to prefer death to flight. Bluntschli prefers chocolate. The judgment Shaw invites is not on Bluntschli but on an ethos that demands death over survival.

### ACT II

The theme of flight persists in peacetime. [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] and [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s elaborate cover-up of Bluntschli's visit — the borrowed coat, the invented story, the nervous management of [[major-petkoff|Major Petkoff]]'s inquiries — is a domestic echo of military desertion. The women are fugitives in their own house, fleeing not enemy soldiers but the consequences of their own compassion.

### ACT III

The play's final inversion comes when Bluntschli, far from being shamed by his flight, is revealed as the most successful figure in the play — a wealthy hotelier's heir whose professional competence has brought him through the war intact. Desertion, Shaw implies, is simply good risk management. The soldier who survives is the soldier who can fight another day; the soldier who dies for honor is simply dead.

## Key Scene

> MAN. The last man left alive in my regiment fled for his life, and here I am. That's all.（001-370）

Bluntschli's unapologetic account of his desertion is the moment at which the play's moral universe pivots. "That's all" — no excuse, no shame, no appeal to higher duty. The statement is a fact, and the fact is sufficient.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of desertion is consistent with his Fabian commitment to rational self-interest as a moral principle. A dead soldier contributes nothing; a living soldier, even one who has fled, can return to fight another day, or — better — to build hotels and raise families. The glorification of death in battle is, for Shaw, the mechanism by which societies persuade young men to throw away their lives for the benefit of their rulers.

- **Target of satire**: The military code that equates flight with dishonor and death with glory
- **Alternative proposed**: The survival instinct as a legitimate — indeed superior — moral guide in matters of war
