# Romanticism

The dominant psychological orientation of the play's upper-class characters — a set of
cherished ideals about love, war, and nobility that Shaw systematically dismantles
across three acts through comic confrontation with reality. As the play's subtitle
("An Anti-Romantic Comedy") announces, romanticism is both Shaw's primary satirical
target and the emotional engine of every character's journey — from [[raina-petkoff]]'s
Byron-infused bedchamber worship to [[sergius-saranoff]]'s exhausted performance of
heroism, from the operatic celebration of the [[battle-of-slivnitza]] to the quiet
triumph of pragmatism in the [[petkoff-house]] library.

## Definition

Romanticism in *Arms and the Man* refers to the worldview shaped by literary and
operatic conventions: war is glorious, soldiers are noble heroes, and love is an
exalted spiritual union. Shaw presents this not as a personal flaw but as a
cultural inheritance — the characters have absorbed these ideals from Byron,
Pushkin, and Verdi operas (001-030). The play's comedic engine is the gap
between these ideals and the unglamorous facts of soldiering, courtship,
and domestic life.

## In the Play

### ACT I

[[raina-petkoff|Raina Petkoff]] embodies romantic idealism in its purest form. She is,
in Shaw's stage direction, "intensely conscious of the romantic
beauty of the night" (001-030) and adores her fiancé [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] from a
distance, having only seen him in photographs (001-061). When she
receives news of [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s cavalry charge, she exults in
"our patriotism — our heroic ideals" (001-085). Yet Shaw immediately
plants the seed of disillusionment:

> RAINA. Our ideas of what Sergius would do—our patriotism—our heroic ideals. Oh, what faithless little creatures girls are!—I sometimes used to doubt whether they were anything but dreams. When I buckled on Sergius's sword he looked so noble: it was treason to think of disillusion or humiliation or failure. And yet—and yet—Promise me you'll never tell him. (001-085)

The stage direction notes that [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s mother [[catherine-petkoff|Catherine]] "would have been, only that perhaps we
only had our heroic ideas because we were so fond of reading Byron
and Pushkin" (001-095). Raina herself admits the artifice:

> RAINA. Well, it came into my head just as he was holding me in his arms and looking into my eyes, that perhaps we only had our heroic ideas because we are so fond of reading Byron and Pushkin, and because we were so delighted with the opera that season at Bucharest. Real life is so seldom like that—indeed never, as far as I knew it then. (001-096)

[[captain-bluntschli|Captain Bluntschli]]'s entrance shatters this romantic frame. A Swiss
[[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] fleeing for his life, he describes [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s
"heroic" charge as the act of a fool: "nine soldiers out of ten are
born fools" (001-293). He carries chocolates instead of
cartridges — the practical soldier's emblem that gives the play its
nickname:

> MAN. Well, come, is it professional to throw a regiment of cavalry on a battery of machine guns, with the dead certainty that if the guns go off not a horse or man will ever get within fifty yards of the fire? I couldn't believe my eyes when I saw it. (001-448)

> MAN. He did it like an operatic tenor—a regular handsome fellow, with flashing eyes and lovely moustache, shouting a war-cry and charging like Don Quixote at the windmills. We did laugh. (001-489)

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s shock when this "chocolate cream soldier" (001-398)
fails to match her "most cherished ideals of manhood" (001-398)
is the first major collision between romanticism and reality.

### ACT II

Four months later, the romantic ideal has not recovered. [[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]
Saranoff, the "hero of the hour" (001-061), returns from war
disillusioned. Shaw's stage directions diagnose him precisely:
"extremely handsome, with the physical hardihood and vigor which
the Serbs and Bulgars conceal under their ordinary civilian dress"
(002-237). His problem — "he finds it impossible to live up to his
imaginative ideals, his consequent cynical scorn for the whole
human race, and the secret self-contempt which possesses him"
(002-250) — is Shaw's clinical description of romanticism's
psychological cost:

> SERGIUS. …he finds it impossible to live up to his imaginative ideals, his consequent cynical scorn for the whole human race, and the secret self-contempt which possesses him. (002-250)

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]] now sees "the validity of his ideals
and the unworthiness of the world to realize them" (002-252).

[[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s romantic exhaustion drives his pursuit of [[louka|Louka]], the
servant — a rebellion against the ideals he can no longer sustain.
His declaration of "higher love" (002-260) to [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is exposed as
a hollow rationalization: "do you know what the higher love is?"
(002-491). Meanwhile [[raina-petkoff|Raina]], having met the realist [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]],
begins the slow turn away from her romantic ideals, though
"steadfastly loyal to her ideals" (001-504) in public performance.

### ACT III

The resolution arrives with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s return. [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] is forced
to confront her "incurably romantic disposition" (003-1082).
[[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]] delivers Shaw's verdict: "You are a romantic idiot"
(003-1109). But Shaw does not condemn [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] — her romanticism,
however foolish, is also what makes her capable of the brave act
of sheltering an enemy soldier in Act I. The play's [[realism|anti-romantic]]
comedy ends not with the destruction of romance but with its
domestication: [[raina-petkoff|Raina]] chooses the chocolate-cream soldier over the
Byronic hero, and the practical Swiss over the operatic [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n.

## Key Scene

> RAINA (_intensely conscious of the romantic beauty of the night, and of the fact that her own youth and beauty are part of it_). (001-030)

This opening stage direction establishes [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s romantic consciousness
as the play's framing device. The entire first act unfolds within this
aestheticized perspective, which [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s entrance will demolish.

> SERGIUS. (_very solemnly._) I think we two have found the higher love. When I think of you, I feel that I could never do a base deed, or think an ignoble thought. (002-462)

The "higher love" speech — delivered with solemn self-regard — is romanticism's manifesto, and Shaw immediately undercuts it through dramatic irony and Louka's knowing response. It is love as alibi: a rhetorical device that allows Sergius to feel noble while acting selfishly.

> SERGIUS. What would Sergius, the hero of Slivnitza, say if he saw me now? What would Sergius, the apostle of the higher love, say if he saw me now? What would the half dozen Sergiuses who keep popping in and out of this handsome figure of mine say if they caught us here? (002-509)

Sergius's confession to Louka exposes the performative nature of romantic idealism — it depends on an audience and collapses under scrutiny. The "half dozen Sergiuses" is a devastating admission: romantic identity is a role, not a self.

> RAINA (_promptly_). An incurably romantic disposition. I ran away from home twice before I was twenty-one. (003-1082)

[[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s confession reframes her romanticism not as weakness but as
a restless energy that — when paired with [[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s practicality — might
become something useful. Shaw, the Fabian socialist, suggests that
idealism needs only to be directed at real objects, not abandoned.

> BLUNTSCHLI. You are a romantic idiot. (_Bluntschli is unspeakably taken aback._) Next time I hope you will know the difference between a schoolgirl of seventeen and a woman of twenty-three. (003-1109)

Shaw's comic verdict on romanticism, delivered by realism's most charming representative — and the moment Raina finally acknowledges the gap between her self-image and reality.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw uses romanticism as his primary satirical target, but his critique
is nuanced. He distinguishes between:

- **The disease**: Romanticism as self-deception — believing in
  glorious cavalry charges and operatic love affairs while ignoring
  the blood, mud, and [[chocolate-cream-soldier|chocolate creams]] of actual experience.
- **The cure**: Not cynicism ([[sergius-saranoff|Sergius]]'s fate) but professionalism and
  honesty ([[captain-bluntschli|Bluntschli]]'s ethos). The [[professional-soldiering|professional soldier]] fights
  efficiently; the honest lover admits his age, his possessions,
  and his limitations.

**Satirical targets**: Byronism, operatic conventions (Verdi's *Ernani*
is referenced throughout), Victorian gender ideals, [[bulgaria|Bulgaria]]n
national romanticism.

**Alternative**: Professional competence, candid self-knowledge,
and the "higher love" that admits its own selfish motives —
what Shaw called "the Life Force" in his later plays.

## Critical Perspectives

The play's subtitle — "An Anti-Romantic Comedy" — announces Shaw's
project: to use the comic form against the romantic conventions
that nineteenth-century theatre had inherited. Later critics have
read [[raina-petkoff|Raina]]'s journey from romanticism to realism as a feminist
narrative: a young woman learning to see men as they are rather
than as literary types. Shaw's Fabian politics inform the critique:
romanticism, like capitalism, is a system of beautiful lies that
benefits those in power — Sergius's "higher love" is as much a
class privilege as Major Petkoff's leisurely inefficiency.

> The play reverses the usual romantic comedy formula: instead of the lovers overcoming obstacles to find each other, they overcome their own illusions to find themselves. — Eric Bentley, *Bernard Shaw* (1957)
