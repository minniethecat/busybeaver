# Volga

## Overview

The Volga, the greatest of Russia's rivers, appears twice in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — never as a setting the narrator reaches, but as a point of reference. In [[yermolai-and-the-millers-wife|Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]] it is the yardstick against which the small local river is measured, and in [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]] it is "our mother Volga," a maternal presence in the wandering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[Kassyan]]'s litany of holy Russian places.

## Description

### A yardstick for the Russian landscape

Describing the winding course of the [[Ista River]], the narrator generalises from the great river to the small: "Many Russian rivers, like the Volga, have one bank rugged and precipitous, the other bounded by level meadows; and so it is with [[ista-river|the Ista]]" (002-141). Here the Volga is invoked as the type of the Russian river — the vast, familiar model against which the modest Ista is understood.

### "Our mother Volga"

In [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]], the river returns transfigured. Dreaming of a blessed land where "apples grow of gold, on silver branches" (009-493), [[kassyan|Kassyan]] recites the places he has journeyed to, and among them names "our mother Volga" (009-497) — set beside [[Simbirsk]] "the fair city" (009-495), [[Moscow]] "of the golden domes" (009-496), and [[Oka]] "the good nurse" (009-496). The river is no longer a geographical fact but a member of a sacred family of Russian places, its maternal epithet expressing [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s mystical reverence for the land.

## Role in the Stories

The Volga's two appearances mark the two registers in which Turgenev's characters speak of distant places: the measured, observing voice of the narrator, for whom the Volga is the standard great river, and the lyrical, worshipful voice of [[kassyan|Kassyan]], for whom it is a mother. Never seen, the Volga nonetheless anchors the imagined geography of the *Sketches*, standing for the immensity of Russia that lies beyond the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman's small circuit of woods and fields.
