## Overview

Stepan Niktopolionitch Komov was a neighboring [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] described by [[Ovsyanikov]] in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." He is remembered as a drunken eccentric who "settled amongst us in those days" and almost drove [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s father into his grave (006-125). His portrait serves as a satirical illustration of the capricious and destructive petty [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] of the pre-Reform era.

## Appearance and Character

Komov was a "drunken fellow" who described himself in French as "Say bon" and ordered servants to "take away the holy images" when intoxicated (006-128). In his sober moments he was truthful, but when drunk he would spin elaborate falsehoods — claiming to own three houses in [[petersburg|Petersburg]] (one red with one chimney, another yellow with two, and a third blue with none) and to have three sons despite never having been married (006-133). He would declare that admirals visited his eldest son, generals the second, and "only Englishmen" the third (006-138):

> 'And he was such a strange fellow! In his sober times he was not a liar; but when he was drunk he would begin to relate how he had three houses in Petersburg--one red, with one chimney; another yellow, with two chimneys; and a third blue, with no chimneys; and three sons (though he had never even been married), one in the infantry, another in the cavalry, and the third was his own master.' (006-132)

## Role in the Story

Komov appears solely through [[Ovsyanikov]]'s reminiscences in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" as an example of the brutish landlord class of the old days. He was "fond of treating others" and kept horses always ready to pursue reluctant guests (006-130). When hosting, he forced everyone to drink toasts to his imaginary sons on pain of being shot and denied burial (006-140). His entertainments included demanding that all present, including his serf-girls, dance through the night:

> 'Then he would jump up and scream, "Dance, God's people, for your pleasure and my diversion!" Well, then, you must dance; if you had to die for it, you must dance. He thoroughly worried his serf-girls to death.' (006-143)

Komov "took a liking" to [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s father — but his affection was oppressive. He "almost drove my father into his grave" (006-151), and only the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s own death prevented worse. Komov died in character: "in one of his drunken fits he fell off the pigeon-house" (006-153).
