# Stony Ridge

## Overview

Stony Ridge is a small locality in the countryside of central Russia, named once in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — in [[byezhin-prairie|Byezhin Prairie]], where it appears in one of the ghost stories the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys tell around their night fire.

It is mentioned only as a point on a walk, but that walk becomes the setting for one of the uncanny episodes that give the sketch its eerie atmosphere.

## Description

The name is spoken by the boy [[Pavlusha]] as he recounts something strange he heard. "I was going from Stony Ridge to [[Shashkino]]," he begins (008-538).

He describes how he "went first through our walnut wood, and then passed by a little pool" (008-539) near "a sharp turn down to the ravine," where "there is a water-pit... quite overgrown with reeds" (008-541).

It is beside this pit that "suddenly... came a sound of some one groaning, and piteously, so piteously" (008-542). Stony Ridge thus marks the beginning of a route through a haunted-seeming stretch of woodland and water.

## Role in the Stories

The locality has no importance beyond its place in [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]]'s tale, but that is precisely its function: like the other named spots in the boys' stories, it lends the folklore of [[byezhin-prairie|Byezhin Prairie]] a concrete, local geography.

By fixing the frightening groan to a real path between Stony Ridge and [[shashkino|Shashkino]], the boy makes the supernatural feel near at hand — one of the ways Turgenev renders the nighttime imagination of the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] children.
