# Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai

## Overview

Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai is a celebrated steppes horse-breeder from Tambov who appears in Turgenev's sketch "[[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]]" (Chapter XIV). He represents an older, patriarchal mode of doing business — honest, dignified, and deliberately old-fashioned — in deliberate contrast to the theatrical commercialism of horse-dealers like [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]. The narrator encounters him through a handwritten notice posted on a gate, advertising horses "of excellent sort; broken in to perfection, and free from vice" (014-325).

## Appearance & Character

Tchornobai is an old man of medium height with white hair, a friendly smile, and "fine blue eyes" (014-369). He wears a long-skirted blue coat and speaks with deliberation, in a "broad Doric" — the plain, unhurried speech of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] (014-356). His manner is warmly patriarchal: when a customer arrives, he first offers tea before discussing business: "But won't you step in and drink just a cup of tea with me first?" (014-369).

Tchornobai's defining trait is his principled rejection of the tricks and deceptions common among horse-dealers. "You won't find things with me, my good sir," he tells the narrator, "as they are with the horse-dealers; confound their tricks! There are drugs of all sorts go in there, salt and malted grains; God forgive them! But with me, you will see, sir, everything's above-board; no underhandedness" (014-424). He prides himself on the plain, honest quality of his animals and the straightforwardness of his dealings.

## Role in the Story

The narrator discovers Tchornobai almost by accident. Walking away from the bargaining at [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]'s — where [[prince-n|Prince N----]] and Hlopakov are haggling over the five-thousand-rouble Peacock — he notices a sheet of paper fixed to the gate of a little grey house, illustrated with a pen-and-ink sketch of a horse and an advertisement written in "an old-fashioned hand" (014-325). Intrigued by the promise of horses from "the celebrated steppes stud of Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai, [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] of Tambov," he knocks at the locked gate.

A [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]-woman admits him, and Tchornobai himself soon appears. He shows the narrator a series of horses, brought out by his aged coachman [[nazar-kubishkin|Nazar Kubishkin]], a wrinkled old man with a hawk nose and a wedge-shaped beard (014-356). The horses are brought out "by their halters just as they are" — no theatrical rearing or showmanship. The narrator eventually selects a modest, inexpensive horse, and when they haggle, Tchornobai "did not get excited; he spoke so reasonably, with such dignity, that I could not help 'honouring' the old man" (014-419).

At the conclusion of the sale, Tchornobai performs an old-fashioned ritual. He crosses himself, lays the skirt of his coat over his hand, takes the halter, and hands the horse to the narrator, saying: "You're his master now, with God's blessing" (014-424). The gesture is deliberately archaic — a relic of a pre-commercial world in which a horse was not merely a commodity but a living creature passed from one master to another with solemnity and blessing.

## Historical/Social Context

Tchornobai embodies the values of the old Russian provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] — patriarchal, religious, and bound by personal honour rather than commercial calculation. His appearance in "[[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]]" immediately after the scenes of [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]'s slick salesmanship and Hlopakov's parasitic clowning creates a striking moral contrast. Where [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]] performs and deceives, Tchornobai speaks plainly and delivers what he promises. Turgenev presents him not as a nostalgic idealisation, but as a living representative of a fading world — honoured, respected, but increasingly marginalised by the commercial energies of the fair.
