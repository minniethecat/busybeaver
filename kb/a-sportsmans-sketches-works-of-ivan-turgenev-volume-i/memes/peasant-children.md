# Peasant Children and Childhood

Childhood is not, in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]], merely a frame for folklore or a background detail of estate life. In [[byezhin-prairie|Byezhin Prairie]] especially, Turgenev turns his full attention on peasant children as people — their labour, their talk, their fears and their vividly distinct faces — and looks at them with the same compassionate curiosity he brings to grown men.

Where the theme of [[Folk Belief and Superstition]] treats the boys chiefly as tellers of the uncanny, this is childhood itself: the vulnerable, working, wondering life of the young among the [[Russian Peasantry]], caught between play and the hard uses of the countryside.

## The Boys of Byezhin Prairie

Lost on the darkening steppe, the narrator comes upon a group of boys minding a drove of horses at night — a familiar summer task that the children treat as an outing:

> (008-139) They were simply peasant boys from a neighbouring village, who were in charge of a drove of horses.

The night-grazing is described as a small liberation from the daytime heat and flies:

> (008-144) ...it's a great treat for the peasant boys. Bare-headed, in old fur-capes, they bestride the most spirited nags, and scurry along with merry cries and hooting and ringing laughter.

Five of them sit around the fire, and the narrator sets out to tell them apart:

> (008-185) ...boys: Fedya, Pavlusha, Ilyusha, Kostya and Vanya.

## Individual Portraits

Turgenev refuses to let the children blur into a type. [[Fedya]], the eldest, is marked at once as a boy of a better-off household, out for pleasure rather than need:

> (008-190) He belonged, by all appearances, to a well-to-do family, and had ridden out to the prairie, not through necessity, but for amusement.

[[Pavlusha]], plain-faced and poorly dressed, wins the narrator's liking for his plain good sense:

> (008-200) He was not a good-looking boy--there's no denying it!--and yet I liked him; he looked very sensible and straightforward, and there was a vigorous ring in his voice.

Little [[Kostya]] is singled out for the sorrow in his small face:

> (008-213) The fourth, Kostya, a boy of ten, aroused my curiosity by his thoughtful and sorrowful look.

## The Death of Pavel

The tenderness of the portrait is sharpened by the narrator's brief, unsparing postscript. The bold, likeable Pavlusha does not live long past that night:

> (008-708) Sad to say, I must add that in that year Pavel met his end. He was not drowned; he was killed by a fall from his horse. Pity!

Childhood in the *Sketches* is never sentimental: it is exposed to the same hard chances as every other peasant life.

## Annushka in the Woods

The theme returns, gentler, in [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]], where a small figure the narrator takes for a child of seven proves older:

> (009-529) She was not a child of seven, as I had fancied at first, from her diminutive stature, but a girl of thirteen or fourteen.

[[Annushka]], shy and mushroom-gathering, is called a "relative" of [[Kassyan]] (009-549) whose face is strikingly like his own (009-532); her wordless timidity — blushing "like a poppy" and clutching her basket (009-554) — is caught with the same close, careful gaze given to the boys.

## Hor's Children

Even the prosperous peasant household frames childhood by work and want of schooling. In [[khor-and-kalinich|Hor and Kalinitch]], [[Khor]] has reared a large family, "obedient and united" (001-272), yet only one of his sons has learned his letters:

> (001-364) 'Fedya can read.' 'And the others?' 'The others can't.'

Reading is a rare accomplishment, granted to one boy and withheld from the rest without explanation — a quiet mark of how little the wider world reached the peasant child.

## Social and Historical Context

The children of the *Sketches* work from an early age, herding and gathering, and pass their nights in the open with the horses. Schooling is exceptional; the world they inherit is one of labour, folk tale and the nearness of [[Nature and Landscape|nature]]. By granting them individual faces, names and fates, Turgenev extends to the youngest serfs the same recognition of full humanity that the book presses, chapter by chapter, against the assumptions of [[Serfdom|serfdom]].
