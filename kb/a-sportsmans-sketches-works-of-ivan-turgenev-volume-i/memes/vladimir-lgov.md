# Vladimir

## Overview

Vladimir is a freed house-serf who appears in [[Lgov]] as a self-styled amateur [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman. He attaches himself to the narrator's duck-hunting expedition alongside [[Yermolaï]], offering his services with elaborate formality and a theatrical air. Vladimir is one of Turgenev's most finely drawn comic characters: a man who has absorbed the external manners of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] through his service as a valet, yet whose pretensions to refinement and competence collapse entirely when tested by practical circumstances. He is neither malicious nor venal — only absurd, and Turgenev's satire of him is gentle rather than cruel, encompassing both his vanity and the social system that produces such hollow imitations of gentlemanly culture.

## Appearance and Manner

Vladimir makes his entrance in the story with studied self-presentation. He is a man of about twenty-five, dressed in a blue and much-worn greatcoat, a yellow waistcoat, and grey pantaloons hastily tucked into boots full of holes (007-029–007-032). His long dark hair, saturated with _kvas_, stands up in stiff tufts; his small brown eyes twinkle genially. Most strikingly, his face is bound up in a black handkerchief — the result, as he later explains with elaborate self-deprecation, of a shooting accident in which a friend's gun went off and "I lost half my chin, and the first finger of my right hand" (007-080–007-081).

His manner is the centerpiece of his character. He introduces himself in a "soft and insinuating voice" with the words, "Allow me to introduce myself; I am a [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman of these parts — Vladimir" (007-042–007-043). Turgenev immediately draws the comparison that defines him:

> The sportsman, Vladimir, uttered those words for all the world like a young provincial actor in the role of leading lover. (007-047–007-048)

Everything about Vladimir is performance. He expresses himself with "extraordinary elegance" and "obviously plumed himself on his manners" (007-055–007-056). His smile is described as "masterly and exceedingly varied," with a particular gift for the "modest, contained smile" that plays on his lips while listening to others — suggesting agreement while preserving his own sense of dignity (007-061–007-064). He was attentive, he agreed with you completely, "but still he did not lose sight of his own dignity, and seemed to wish to give you to understand that he could, if occasion arose, express convictions of his own" (007-064–007-066).

## The Valet's Education

Vladimir's biography is the key to his character. Turgenev provides it in a compact paragraph that traces the arc of the house-serf's cultural half-education:

> He was a freed house-serf; in his tender youth had been taught music, then served as valet, could read and write, had read — so much I could discover — some few trashy books, and existed now, as many do exist in Russia, without a farthing of ready money; without any regular occupation; fed by manna from heaven, or something hardly less precarious. (007-050–007-055)

This passage is a miniature of the predicament that serfdom creates when it educates without emancipating. Vladimir has been taught music, literacy, and the manners of his masters, but these acquirements are entirely surface-level — he has read "trashy books," not serious literature; he has learned the gestures of politeness without the substance of cultivation. His education has fitted him for nothing in particular. Freed but unmoored, he drifts between neighboring [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s and town acquaintances, playing preference and trading on the goodwill of people who tolerate his fine talk.

## Role in the Lgov Expedition

Vladimir functions in the story as both enabler and comic foil. He knows of [[Sutchok]] and his punt, and together with [[yermolai|Yermolaï]] he goes to secure it (007-082–007-088). His knowledge of the local terrain and people is genuine, and his social connections — the visiting of neighboring [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s and town friends — make him a useful intermediary in the countryside.

But his pretensions are systematically undercut by his practical failures. His shooting is lamentable:

> Vladimir, to Yermolaï's great satisfaction, did not shoot at all well; he seemed surprised after each unsuccessful shot, looked at his gun and blew down it, seemed puzzled, and at last explained to us the reason why he had missed his aim. (007-275–007-278)

When put to the menial task of baling water from the leaking punt with a stolen ladle, he performs adequately — until the excitement of the shooting distracts him, and he neglects his duty, contributing to the boat's eventual sinking (007-290–007-296). The irony is precise: he is capable only when performing the servile role he affects to have transcended.

The catastrophe of the sinking boat reveals Vladimir at his most exposed. Shaking like a leaf, his teeth chattering, his once-masterly smile now "utterly meaningless," Turgenev asks:

> What had become of his fine language, his feeling of fine distinctions, and of his own dignity? (007-318–007-319)

All his acquired gentility evaporates in cold water. He subsides into a state of learned helplessness — when [[yermolai|Yermolaï]] departs to find the ford, Vladimir can only add "piteously" that "he will perish without doing any good" (007-340), and when told to carry [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s gun he "obeyed submissively" (007-328). The valet re-emerges beneath the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman.

## The Distance Between Vladimir and Sutchok

One of Vladimir's most revealing moments occurs during the narrator's long conversation with [[sutchok|Sutchok]] about the fisherman's life of serial degradation. Throughout the entire exchange, Vladimir stares at the poor old man "with a contemptuous smile" (007-247–007-248). When [[sutchok|Sutchok]] departs, Vladimir's judgment is swift:

> "A stupid fellow," was his comment, when the latter had gone off; "an absolutely uneducated fellow; a peasant, nothing more. One cannot even call him a house-serf, and he was boasting all the time. How could he be an actor, be pleased to judge for yourself! You were pleased to trouble yourself for no good in talking to him." (007-249–007-253)

This is Vladimir at his least sympathetic — and most revealing. He cannot recognize that his own pretensions differ from [[sutchok|Sutchok]]'s "boasting" only in degree, not in kind. Both men are products of serfdom: [[sutchok|Sutchok]] the serf who has been reduced to nothing, Vladimir the freed serf who has built a self on borrowed manners. Vladimir's contempt is the contempt of the man who has risen just far enough to despise those who remind him of his origins.

Turgenev's handling of Vladimir is characteristic of his method throughout the *Sketches*: he makes us laugh at Vladimir's absurdities — the fine language, the theatrical bow, the puzzled examination of the gun after every missed shot — but the laughter is never cruel. Vladimir is a victim of his position, not of his character. His education has given him aspirations without the means to realize them; his freedom is nominal, his livelihood precarious. The "fine irony with which Vladimir used 'Sir' in his reply" to [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s coarse familiarity (007-069) is both genuinely comic and genuinely sad: it is the only weapon available to a man whose dignity rests entirely on manner, not substance.
