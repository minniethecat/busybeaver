# Orleans

## Overview

Orleans is the French city that appears in Turgenev's *[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]* as the birthplace of [[Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune]], one of the most colorful characters in the *[[A Sportsman's Sketches, Volume I|Sketches]]*. A city on the Loire River, historically associated with the Napoleonic era, Orleans serves as the starting point for Lejeune's improbable journey — from French drummer-boy in Napoleon's invading army to Russian gentleman and [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]. Unlike the Russian provinces and villages that dominate the geography of the *Sketches*, Orleans represents the wider European world that occasionally, and dramatically, intrudes into the narrator's provincial Russia.

## Description

Orleans is introduced in the remarkable backstory of Lejeune, whose path to respectability was "not quite ordinary." The narrator explains: "He was born in Orleans of French parents, and had gone with Napoleon, on the invasion of Russia, in the capacity of a drummer" (006-561). The economy of this sentence is characteristic of Turgenev: in two clauses, it establishes both a distant French origin and the mechanism — Napoleon's Grande Armée — that tran[[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]ed Lejeune from the Loire to the Russian interior.

The city returns in the story of Lejeune's capture by [[smolensk|Smolensk]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s during the French retreat. Taken prisoner and facing summary execution, Lejeune attempted to plead for his life in French, begging the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s to let him return "to Orleans. 'There, messieurs,' he said, 'my mother is living, une tendre mère'" (006-572). The pathos of the plea — the invocation of a mother waiting in a distant French city — is humorously undercut by the narrator's observation that "the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, doubtless through their ignorance of the geographical position of Orleans, continued to offer him a journey under water along the course of the meandering river [[gniloterka|Gniloterka]]" (006-574). The Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' incomprehension of "the geographical position of Orleans" encapsulates the vast cultural and linguistic distance between the world Lejeune came from and the one he was now trapped in.

## Role in the Stories

Orleans serves in [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] as a symbol of origins and distance. It is the place Lejeune longs to return to, the home where his mother waits, the city whose name means nothing to the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s who hold his life in their hands. The comic gap between Lejeune's desperate "Orleans" and the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' pragmatic plan to drown him in the [[gniloterka|Gniloterka]] is one of the story's most memorable moments — a clash of two worlds that cannot speak to each other. Yet Orleans is also the starting point of Lejeune's transformation: without Napoleon's invasion, without the retreat from [[moscow|Moscow]], without the capture at [[smolensk|Smolensk]], the French drummer-boy from Orleans would never have become a Russian [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] with an estate and a respectful place in provincial society. The city thus frames one of the *Sketches'* central themes — the improbable paths by which people arrive at their final identities.
