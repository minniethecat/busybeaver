# Malinina

## Overview

Malinina is a village in [[orel-province|Orel Province]] that appears in Turgenev's sketch "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." It is paired with [[Tchepligin]] in a single sentence uttered by [[Ovsyanikov]], who describes a strip of land "that runs from [[tchepligin|Tchepligin]] to Malinina" (006-100) — land that was seized by the narrator's grandfather from [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s family. Like [[tchepligin|Tchepligin]], Malinina exists in the narrative only as a topographic waypoint, yet the two village names together frame a story of dispossession that echoes across the *Sketches*.

## Description

[[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s account is geographically exact. He locates the disputed parcel as stretching "from [[tchepligin|Tchepligin]] to Malinina" and notes with bitter irony that the narrator has it "under oats now" (006-100). [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s father — "a just man" but also "a hot-tempered man" — laid a petition before the court, but "he was alone: the others did not appear — they were afraid" (006-100). The mention of Malinina thus anchors a legal drama: two village names bracket a contested strip of land, and the failure of collective action among the neighbouring [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s allows the seizure to stand.

## Role in the Stories

The [[tchepligin|Tchepligin]]–Malinina road is one of many geographic details in the *Sketches* that carry the weight of social history. Like [[Shutolomovsky]] and the [[Gniloterka]] River, Malinina is not described visually; its significance is entirely relational — it exists as the far end of a boundary dispute. The paired mention with [[tchepligin|Tchepligin]] reflects the dense inter-village geography of [[orel-province|Orel Province]], where roads, fields, and boundaries between neighbouring settlements were the sites of constant negotiation — and frequent injustice — under the old order.
