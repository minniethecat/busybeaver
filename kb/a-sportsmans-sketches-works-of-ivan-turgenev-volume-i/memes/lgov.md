# LGOV

[007-001]'Let us go to Lgov,' Yermolaï, whom the reader knows already, said to

[007-002]me one day; 'there we can shoot ducks to our heart's content.'

[007-003]Although wild duck offers no special attraction for a genuine

[007-004]sportsman, still, through lack of other game at the time (it was the

[007-005]beginning of September; snipe were not on the wing yet, and I was tired

[007-006]of running across the fields after partridges), I listened to my

[007-007]huntsman's suggestion, and we went to Lgov.

[007-008]Lgov is a large village of the steppes, with a very old stone church

[007-009]with a single cupola, and two mills on the swampy little river Rossota.

[007-010]Five miles from Lgov, this river becomes a wide swampy pond, overgrown

[007-011]at the edges, and in places also in the centre, with thick reeds. Here,

[007-012]in the creeks or rather pools between the reeds, live and breed a

[007-013]countless multitude of ducks of all possible kinds--quackers,

[007-014]half-quackers, pintails, teals, divers, etc. Small flocks are for ever

[007-015]flitting about and swimming on the water, and at a gunshot, they rise

[007-016]in such clouds that the sportsman involuntarily clutches his hat with

[007-017]one hand and utters a prolonged Pshaw! I walked with Yermolaï along

[007-018]beside the pond; but, in the first place, the duck is a wary bird, and

[007-019]is not to be met quite close to the bank; and secondly, even when some

[007-020]straggling and inexperienced teal exposed itself to our shots and lost

[007-021]its life, our dogs were not able to get it out of the thick reeds; in

[007-022]spite of their most devoted efforts they could neither swim nor tread

[007-023]on the bottom, and only cut their precious noses on the sharp reeds for

[007-024]nothing.

[007-025]'No,' was Yermolaï's comment at last, 'it won't do; we must get a

[007-026]boat.... Let us go back to Lgov.'

[007-027]We went back. We had only gone a few paces when a rather

[007-028]wretched-looking setter-dog ran out from behind a bushy willow to meet

[007-029]us, and behind him appeared a man of middle height, in a blue and

[007-030]much-worn greatcoat, a yellow waistcoat, and pantaloons of a

[007-031]nondescript grey colour, hastily tucked into high boots full of holes,

[007-032]with a red handkerchief round his neck, and a single-barrelled gun on

[007-033]his shoulder. While our dogs, with the ordinary Chinese ceremonies

[007-034]peculiar to their species, were sniffing at their new acquaintance, who

[007-035]was obviously ill at ease, held his tail between his legs, dropped his

[007-036]ears back, and kept turning round and round showing his teeth--the

[007-037]stranger approached us, and bowed with extreme civility. He appeared to

[007-038]be about twenty-five; his long dark hair, perfectly saturated with

[007-039]kvas, stood up in stiff tufts, his small brown eyes twinkled genially;

[007-040]his face was bound up in a black handkerchief, as though for toothache;

[007-041]his countenance was all smiles and amiability.

[007-042]'Allow me to introduce myself,' he began in a soft and insinuating

[007-043]voice; 'I am a sportsman of these parts--Vladimir.... Having heard of

[007-044]your presence, and having learnt that you proposed to visit the shores

[007-045]of our pond, I resolved, if it were not displeasing to you, to offer

[007-046]you my services.'

[007-047]The sportsman, Vladimir, uttered those words for all the world like a

[007-048]young provincial actor in the \_rôle\_ of leading lover. I agreed to his

[007-049]proposition, and before we had reached Lgov I had succeeded in learning

[007-050]his whole history. He was a freed house-serf; in his tender youth had

[007-051]been taught music, then served as valet, could read and write, had

[007-052]read--so much I could discover--some few trashy books, and existed now,

[007-053]as many do exist in Russia, without a farthing of ready money; without

[007-054]any regular occupation; fed by manna from heaven, or something hardly

[007-055]less precarious. He expressed himself with extraordinary elegance, and

[007-056]obviously plumed himself on his manners; he must have been devoted to

[007-057]the fair sex too, and in all probability popular with them: Russian

[007-058]girls love fine talking. Among other things, he gave me to understand

[007-059]that he sometimes visited the neighbouring landowners, and went to stay

[007-060]with friends in the town, where he played preference, and that he was

[007-061]acquainted with people in the metropolis. His smile was masterly and

[007-062]exceedingly varied; what specially suited him was a modest, contained

[007-063]smile which played on his lips as he listened to any other man's

[007-064]conversation. He was attentive to you; he agreed with you completely,

[007-065]but still he did not lose sight of his own dignity, and seemed to wish

[007-066]to give you to understand that he could, if occasion arose, express

[007-067]convictions of his own. Yermolaï, not being very refined, and quite

[007-068]devoid of 'subtlety,' began to address him with coarse familiarity. The

[007-069]fine irony with which Vladimir used 'Sir' in his reply was worth seeing.

[007-070]'Why is your face tied up? 'I inquired; 'have you toothache?'

[007-071]'No,' he answered; 'it was a most disastrous consequence of

[007-072]carelessness. I had a friend, a good fellow, but not a bit of a

[007-073]sportsman, as sometimes occurs. Well, one day he said to me, "My dear

[007-074]friend, take me out shooting; I am curious to learn what this diversion

[007-075]consists in." I did not like, of course, to refuse a comrade; I got him

[007-076]a gun and took him out shooting. Well, we shot a little in the ordinary

[007-077]way; at last we thought we would rest I sat down under a tree; but he

[007-078]began instead to play with his gun, pointing it at me meantime. I asked

[007-079]him to leave off, but in his inexperience he did not attend to my

[007-080]words, the gun went off, and I lost half my chin, and the first finger

[007-081]of my right hand.'

[007-082]We reached Lgov. Vladimir and Yermolaï had both decided that we could

[007-083]not shoot without a boat.

[007-084]'Sutchok (\_i.e.\_ the twig) has a punt,' observed Vladimir, 'but I don't

[007-085]know where he has hidden it. We must go to him.'

[007-086]'To whom?' I asked.

[007-087]'The man lives here; Sutchok is his nickname.'

[007-088]Vladimir went with Yermolaï to Sutchok's. I told them I would wait for

[007-089]them at the church. While I was looking at the tombstones in the

[007-090]churchyard, I stumbled upon a blackened, four-cornered urn with the

[007-091]following inscription, on one side in French: 'Ci-git Théophile-Henri,

[007-092]Vicomte de Blangy'; on the next; 'Under this stone is laid the body of

[007-093]a French subject, Count Blangy; born 1737, died 1799, in the 62nd year

[007-094]of his age': on the third, 'Peace to his ashes': and on the fourth:--

[007-095]'Under this stone there lies from France an emigrant.

[007-096]Of high descent was he, and also of talent.

[007-097]A wife and kindred murdered he bewailed,

[007-098]And left his land by tyrants cruel assailed;

[007-099]The friendly shores of Russia he attained,

[007-100]And hospitable shelter here he gained;

[007-101]Children he taught; their parents' cares allayed:

[007-102]Here, by God's will, in peace he has been laid.'

[007-103]The approach of Yermolaï with Vladimir and the man with the strange

[007-104]nickname, Sutchok, broke in on my meditations.

[007-105]Barelegged, ragged and dishevelled, Sutchok looked like a discharged

[007-106]stray house-serf of sixty years old.

[007-107]'Have you a boat?' I asked him.

[007-108]'I have a boat,' he answered in a hoarse, cracked voice; 'but it's a

[007-109]very poor one.'

[007-110]'How so?'

[007-111]'Its boards are split apart, and the rivets have come off the cracks.'

[007-112]'That's no great disaster!' interposed Yermolaï; 'we can stuff them up

[007-113]with tow.'

[007-114]'Of course you can,' Sutchok assented.

[007-115]'And who are you?'

[007-116]'I am the fisherman of the manor.'

[007-117]'How is it, when you're a fisherman, your boat is in such bad

[007-118]condition?'

[007-119]'There are no fish in our river.'

[007-120]'Fish don't like slimy marshes,' observed my huntsman, with the air of

[007-121]an authority.

[007-122]'Come,' I said to Yermolaï, 'go and get some tow, and make the boat

[007-123]right for us as soon as you can.'

[007-124]Yermolaï went off.

[007-125]'Well, in this way we may very likely go to the bottom,' I said to

[007-126]Vladimir. 'God is merciful,' he answered. 'Anyway, we must suppose that

[007-127]the pond is not deep.'

[007-128]'No, it is not deep,' observed Sutchok, who spoke in a strange,

[007-129]far-away voice, as though he were in a dream, 'and there's sedge and

[007-130]mud at the bottom, and it's all overgrown with sedge. But there are

[007-131]deep holes too.'

[007-132]'But if the sedge is so thick,' said Vladimir, 'it will be impossible

[007-133]to row.'

[007-134]'Who thinks of rowing in a punt? One has to punt it. I will go with

[007-135]you; my pole is there--or else one can use a wooden spade.'

[007-136]'With a spade it won't be easy; you won't touch the bottom perhaps in

[007-137]some places,' said Vladimir.

[007-138]'It's true; it won't be easy.'

[007-142]clasped behind his back, according to the old habit of house-serfs.

[007-164]'To Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev. We came to him by inheritance. But he

[007-173]Lgov was bought by him, by Afanasy Nefeditch, but it came to Sergaï

[007-182]'Then were you cook to her?'

[007-184]'What were you?'

[007-185]'Coffee-bearer.'

[007-186]'What sort of duty is that?'

[007-187]'I don't know, your honour. I stood at the sideboard, and was called

[007-191]'Yes.'

[007-192]'And were you coffee-bearer all the time?'

[007-193]'No, not all the time; I was an actor too.'

[007-194]'Really?'

[007-254]A quarter of an hour later we were sitting in Sutchok's punt. The dogs

[007-255]we left in a hut in charge of my coachman. We were not very

[007-256]comfortable, but sportsmen are not a fastidious race. At the rear end,

[007-257]which was flattened and straight, stood Sutchok, punting; I sat with

[007-258]Vladimir on the planks laid across the boat, and Yermolaï ensconced

[007-259]himself in front, in the very beak. In spite of the tow, the water soon

[007-260]made its appearance under our feet. Fortunately, the weather was calm

[007-261]and the pond seemed slumbering.

[007-262]We floated along rather slowly. The old man had difficulty in drawing

[007-263]his long pole out of the sticky mud; it came up all tangled in green

[007-264]threads of water-sedge; the flat round leaves of the water-lily also

[007-265]hindered the progress of our boat last we got up to the reeds, and then

[007-266]the fun began. Ducks flew up noisily from the pond, scared by our

[007-267]unexpected appearance in their domains, shots sounded at once after

[007-268]them; it was a pleasant sight to see these short-tailed game turning

[007-269]somersaults in the air, splashing heavily into the water. We could not,

[007-270]of course, get at all the ducks that were shot; those who were slightly

[007-271]wounded swam away; some which had been quite killed fell into such

[007-272]thick reeds that even Yermolaï's little lynx eyes could not discover

[007-273]them, yet our boat was nevertheless filled to the brim with game for
