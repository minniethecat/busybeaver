## Overview

Madame Radilov is the elderly mother of [[Radilov]] in the sketch "[[My Neighbour Radilov]]." She is a quiet, timid presence throughout the chapter, speaking little at dinner and eating almost nothing. At the chapter's close, her son abandons her to elope with [[Olga]], his sister-in-law—and when the [[Narrator]] visits her afterward, he finds her weeping over cards with [[Fyodor Miheitch]].

## Appearance and Character

Turgenev introduces Madame Radilov with economical precision:

> [005-074] In the drawing-room, in the sofa in the centre of the room, was sitting an old lady of medium height, in a cinnamon-coloured dress and a white cap, with a thinnish, kind old face, and a timid, mournful expression. (005-076)

Her physical frailty is emphasized by her "withered hands" holding "a fat worsted reticule that looked like a sack" (005-079). She speaks in a "weak and gentle voice, blinking her eyes" (005-081), and after exchanging brief pleasantries with the narrator, she says no more (005-085).

During the dinner that follows, her withdrawal from the social world is almost complete: "The old lady did not utter a word during the whole of dinner; she ate scarcely anything herself, and did not press me to partake" (005-139). Turgenev's narrator observes that "her features had an air of timorous and hopeless expectation, that melancholy of old age which it pierces one's heart to look upon" (005-141).

## Role in the Story

Madame Radilov's role is that of a witness and, ultimately, a victim. During [[radilov|Radilov]]'s harrowing account of his wife's death, she is quietly present: "the old lady had laid the stocking down on her knees, and taken a handkerchief out of her reticule; she was stealthily wiping away her tears" (005-220).

The chapter's final blow lands on her. When [[radilov|Radilov]] disappears with [[olga|Olga]], he leaves his mother behind. The narrator calls on her before quitting the countryside:

> [005-270] Before leaving the country I called on old Madame Radilov. I found her in the drawing-room; she was playing cards with Fyodor Miheitch. (005-271)

When asked if she has news of her son, "the old lady began to weep" (005-273). The narrator asks no more. The image—an abandoned mother playing cards with a ruined old man in an empty house—is one of the most quietly devastating in the entire collection.
