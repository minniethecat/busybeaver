## Overview

Porfiry Ovtchinnikov is a minor provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] mentioned in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" as the most obstreperous participant in a boundary settlement dispute. Acting not on his own behalf but as representative for his brother, he nonetheless "was the first to make an uproar" (006-267) — shouting accusations at the surveyor and disrupting the mediation. His cameo is one of several satirical portraits in the chapter of the petty [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]'s litigiousness and self-importance, rendered with the dry observational humour characteristic of [[Ovsyanikov]]'s narrative voice.

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Porfiry Ovtchinnikov appears in the text, but his behaviour at the boundary meeting paints a vivid character sketch. He "bawls" (006-269) accusations without provocation, demands the surveyor's plans while calling them "the Judas's plans" (006-271), and refuses to state his own claim when challenged. The narrator's incredulous question — "And what had the fellow to make an uproar about?" (006-268) — and the immediate answer — "He hasn't an acre of ground; he is acting as representative of his brother" (006-269) — frame his behaviour as absurd: the loudest voice in the room belongs to the man with the least at stake.

The epithet "the Judas's plans" (006-271) is revealing: Porfiry Ovtchinnikov reaches instinctively for the language of betrayal and conspiracy, treating a routine land survey as a plot against his family's honour. His belligerence is that of a man who substitutes volume for substance, theatrical outrage for legitimate grievance.

## Role in the Story

The boundary dispute scene occupies a substantial portion of [[Ovsyanikov]]'s conversation with the narrator, serving as an extended illustration of the petty conflicts that consume the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]'s energy. The mediator [[Nikifor Ilitch]] has summoned the district's [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s to [[beryozovka|Beryozovka]], urging them to "settle the boundaries" because "our district is behind all the others" (006-263). The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s begin discussions and disputes "as usual" (006-265), and the attorney makes the expected objections.

Then Porfiry Ovtchinnikov erupts. His role in the narrative is to embody the irrational obstructionism that makes rational reform impossible. He has no land, no claim, and no coherent argument — yet he dominates the proceedings through sheer noise. The scene functions as political allegory: in a society where the loudest voice prevails over the most reasonable, progress stalls. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s dry recounting of the episode stands as quiet condemnation of a class that cannot govern itself.

> But the first to make an uproar was Porfiry Ovtchinnikov.... And what had the fellow to make an uproar about?... He hasn't an acre of ground; he is acting as representative of his brother. He bawls: "No, you shall not impose on me! no, you shan't drive me to that! give the plans here! give me the surveyor's plans, the Judas's plans here!" (006-267)
