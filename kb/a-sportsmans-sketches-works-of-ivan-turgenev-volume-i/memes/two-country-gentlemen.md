# TWO COUNTRY GENTLEMEN

[013-001]I have already had the honour, kind readers, of introducing to you

[013-002]several of my neighbours; let me now seize a favourable opportunity (it

[013-003]is always a favourable opportunity with us writers) to make known to

[013-004]you two more gentlemen, on whose lands I often used to go

[013-005]shooting--very worthy, well-intentioned persons, who enjoy universal

[013-006]esteem in several districts.

[013-007]First I will describe to you the retired General-major Vyatcheslav

[013-008]Ilarionovitch Hvalinsky. Picture to yourselves a tall and once slender

[013-009]man, now inclined to corpulence, but not in the least decrepit or even

[013-010]elderly, a man of ripe age; in his very prime, as they say. It is true

[013-011]the once regular and even now rather pleasing features of his face have

[013-012]undergone some change; his cheeks are flabby; there are close wrinkles

[013-013]like rays about his eyes; a few teeth are not, as Saadi, according to

[013-014]Pushkin, used to say; his light brown hair--at least, all that is left

[013-015]of it--has assumed a purplish hue, thanks to a composition bought at

[013-016]the Romyon horse-fair of a Jew who gave himself out as an Armenian; but

[013-017]Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch has a smart walk and a ringing laugh, jingles

[013-018]his spurs and curls his moustaches, and finally speaks of himself as an

[013-019]old cavalry man, whereas we all know that really old men never talk of

[013-020]being old. He usually wears a frock-coat buttoned up to the top, a high

[013-021]cravat, starched collars, and grey sprigged trousers of a military cut;

[013-022]he wears his hat tilted over his forehead, leaving all the back of his

[013-023]head exposed. He is a good-natured man, but of rather curious notions

[013-024]and principles. For instance, he can never treat noblemen of no wealth

[013-025]or standing as equals. When he talks to them, he usually looks sideways

[013-026]at them, his cheek pressed hard against his stiff white collar, and

[013-027]suddenly he turns and silently fixes them with a clear stony stare,

[013-028]while he moves the whole skin of his head under his hair; he even has a

[013-029]way of his own in pronouncing many words; he never says, for instance:

[013-030]'Thank you, Pavel Vasilyitch,' or 'This way, if you please, Mihalo

[013-031]Ivanitch,' but always 'Fanks, Pa'l 'Asilitch,' or ''Is wy, please, Mil'

[013-032]'Vanitch.' With persons of the lower grades of society, his behaviour

[013-033]is still more quaint; he never looks at them at all, and before making

[013-034]known his desires to them, or giving an order, he repeats several times

[013-035]in succession, with a puzzled, far-away air: 'What's your name?...

[013-036]what, what's your name?' with extraordinary sharp emphasis on the first

[013-037]word, which gives the phrase a rather close resemblance to the call of

[013-038]a quail. He is very fussy and terribly close-fisted, but manages his

[013-039]land badly; he had chosen as overseer on his estate a retired

[013-040]quartermaster, a Little Russian, and a man of really exceptional

[013-041]stupidity. None of us, though, in the management of land, has ever

[013-042]surpassed a certain great Petersburg dignitary, who, having perceived

[013-043]from the reports of his steward that the cornkilns in which the corn

[013-044]was dried on his estate were often liable to catch fire, whereby he

[013-045]lost a great deal of grain, gave the strictest orders that for the

[013-046]future they should not put the sheaves in till the fire had been

[013-047]completely put out! This same great personage conceived the brilliant

[013-048]idea of sowing his fields with poppies, as the result of an apparently

[013-049]simple calculation; poppy being dearer than rye, he argued, it is

[013-050]consequently more profitable to sow poppy. He it was, too, who ordered

[013-051]his women serfs to wear tiaras after a pattern bespoken from Moscow;

[013-052]and to this day the peasant women on his lands do actually wear the

[013-053]tiaras, only they wear them over their skull-caps.... But let us return

[013-054]to Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch. Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch is a devoted

[013-055]admirer of the fair sex, and directly he catches sight of a pretty

[013-056]woman in the promenade of his district town, he is promptly off in

[013-057]pursuit, but falls at once into a sort of limping gait--that is the

[013-058]remarkable feature of the case. He is fond of playing cards, but only

[013-059]with people of a lower standing; they toady him with 'Your Excellency'

[013-060]in every sentence, while he can scold them and find fault to his

[013-061]heart's content. When he chances to play with the governor or any

[013-062]official personage, a marvellous change comes over him; he is all nods

[013-063]and smiles; he looks them in the face; he seems positively flowing with

[013-064]honey.... He even loses without grumbling. Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch

[013-065]does not read much; when he is reading he incessantly works his

[013-066]moustaches and eyebrows up and down, as if a wave were passing from

[013-067]below upwards over his face. This undulatory motion in Vyatcheslav

[013-068]Ilarionovitch's face is especially marked when (before company, of

[013-069]course) he happens to be reading the columns of the \_Journal des

[013-070]Débats\_. In the assemblies of nobility he plays a rather important

[013-071]part, but on grounds of economy he declines the honourable dignity of

[013-072]marshal. 'Gentlemen,' he usually says to the noblemen who press that

[013-073]office upon him, and he speaks in a voice filled with condescension and

[013-074]self-sufficiency: 'much indebted for the honour; but I have made up my

[013-075]mind to consecrate my leisure to solitude.' And, as he utters these

[013-076]words, he turns his head several times to right and to left, and then,

[013-077]with a dignified air, adjusts his chin and his cheek over his cravat.

[013-078]In his young days he served as adjutant to some very important person,

[013-079]whom he never speaks of except by his Christian name and patronymic;

[013-080]they do say he fulfilled other functions than those of an adjutant;

[013-081]that, for instance, in full parade get-up, buttoned up to the chin, he

[013-082]had to lather his chief in his bath--but one can't believe everything

[013-083]one hears. General Hvalinsky is not, however, fond of talking himself

[013-084]about his career in the army, which is certainly rather curious; it

[013-085]seems that he had never seen active service. General Hvalinsky lives in

[013-086]a small house alone; he has never known the joys of married life, and

[013-087]consequently he still regards himself as a possible match, and indeed a

[013-088]very eligible one. But he has a house-keeper, a dark-eyed, dark-browed,

[013-089]plump, fresh-looking woman of five-and-thirty with a moustache; she

[013-090]wears starched dresses even on week-days, and on Sundays puts on muslin

[013-091]sleeves as well. Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch is at his best at the large

[013-092]invitation dinners given by gentlemen of the neighbourhood in honour of

[013-093]the governor and other dignitaries: then he is, one may say, in his

[013-094]natural element. On these occasions he usually sits, if not on the

[013-095]governor's right hand, at least at no great distance from him; at the

[013-096]beginning of dinner he is more disposed to nurse his sense of personal

[013-097]dignity, and, sitting back in his chair, he loftily scans the necks and

[013-098]stand-up collars of the guests, without turning his head, but towards

[013-099]the end of the meal he unbends, begins smiling in all directions (he

[013-100]had been all smiles for the governor from the first), and sometimes

[013-101]even proposes the toast in honour of the fair sex, the ornament of our

[013-102]planet, as he says. General Hvalinsky shows to advantage too at all

[013-103]solemn public functions, inspections, assemblies, and exhibitions; no

[013-104]one in church goes up for the benediction with such style. Vyatcheslav

[013-105]Ilarionovitch's servants are never noisy and clamorous on the breaking

[013-106]up of assemblies or in crowded thoroughfares; as they make a way for

[013-107]him through the crowd or call his carriage, they say in an agreeable

[013-108]guttural baritone: 'By your leave, by your leave allow General

[013-109]Hvalinsky to pass,' or 'Call for General Hvalinsky's carriage.' ...

[013-110]Hvalinsky's carriage is, it must be admitted, of a rather queer design,

[013-111]and the footmen's liveries are rather threadbare (that they are grey,

[013-112]with red facings, it is hardly necessary to remark); his horses too

[013-113]have seen a good deal of hard service in their time; but Vyatcheslav

[013-114]Ilarionovitch has no pretensions to splendour, and goes so far as to

[013-115]think it beneath his rank to make an ostentation of wealth. Hvalinsky

[013-116]has no special gift of eloquence, or possibly has no opportunity of

[013-117]displaying his rhetorical powers, as he has a particular aversion, not

[013-118]only for disputing, but for discussion in general, and assiduously

[013-119]avoids long conversation of all sorts, especially with young people.

[013-120]This was certainly judicious on his part; the worst of having to do

[013-121]with the younger generation is that they are so ready to forget the

[013-122]proper respect and submission due to their superiors. In the presence

[013-123]of persons of high rank Hvalinsky is for the most part silent, while

[013-124]with persons of a lower rank, whom to judge by appearances he despises,

[013-125]though he constantly associates with them, his remarks are sharp and

[013-126]abrupt, expressions such as the following occurring incessantly:

[013-127]'That's a piece of folly, what you're saying now,' or 'I feel myself

[013-128]compelled, sir, to remind you,' or 'You ought to realise with whom you

[013-129]are dealing,' and so on. He is peculiarly dreaded by post-masters,

[013-130]officers of the local boards, and superintendents of posting stations.

[013-131]He never entertains any one in his house, and lives, as the rumour

[013-132]goes, like a screw. For all that, he's an excellent country gentleman,

[013-133]'An old soldier, a disinterested fellow, a man of principle, \_vieux

[013-134]grognard\_,' his neighbours say of him. The provincial prosecutor alone

[013-135]permits himself to smile when General Hvalinsky's excellent and solid

[013-136]qualities are referred to before him--but what will not envy drive men

[013-137]to!...

[013-138]However, we will pass now to another landed proprietor.

[013-139]Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov has no sort of resemblance to Hvalinsky; I

[013-140]hardly think he has ever served under government in any capacity, and

[013-141]he has never been reckoned handsome. Mardary Apollonitch is a little,

[013-142]fattish, bald old man of a respectable corpulence, with a double chin

[013-143]and little soft hands. He is very hospitable and jovial; lives, as the

[013-144]saying is, for his comfort; summer and winter alike, he wears a striped

[013-145]wadded dressing-gown. There's only one thing in which he is like

[013-146]General Hvalinsky; he too is a bachelor. He owns five hundred souls.

[013-147]Mardary Apollonitch's interest in his estate is of a rather superficial

[013-148]description; not to be behind the age, he ordered a threshing-machine

[013-149]from Butenop's in Moscow, locked it up in a barn, and then felt his

[013-150]mind at rest on the subject. Sometimes on a fine summer day he would

[013-151]have out his racing droshky, and drive off to his fields, to look at

[013-152]the crops and gather corn-flowers. Mardary Apollonitch's existence is

[013-153]carried on in quite the old style. His house is of an old-fashioned

[013-154]construction; in the hall there is, of course, a smell of kvas, tallow

[013-155]candles, and leather; close at hand, on the right, there is a sideboard

[013-156]with pipes and towels; in the dining-room, family portraits, flies, a

[013-157]great pot of geraniums, and a squeaky piano; in the drawing-room, three

[013-158]sofas, three tables, two looking-glasses, and a wheezy clock of

[013-159]tarnished enamel with engraved bronze hands; in the study, a table

[013-160]piled up with papers, and a bluish-coloured screen covered with

[013-161]pictures cut out of various works of last century; a bookcase full of

[013-162]musty books, spiders, and black dust; a puffy armchair; an Italian

[013-163]window; a sealed-up door into the garden.... Everything, in short, just

[013-164]as it always is. Mardary Apollonitch has a multitude of servants, all

[013-165]dressed in the old-fashioned style; in long blue full coats, with high

[013-166]collars, shortish pantaloons of a muddy hue, and yellow waistcoats.

[013-167]They address visitors as 'father.' His estate is under the

[013-168]superintendence of an agent, a peasant with a beard that covers the

[013-169]whole of his sheepskin; his household is managed by a stingy, wrinkled

[013-170]old woman, whose face is always tied up in a cinnamon-coloured

[013-171]handkerchief. In Mardary Apollonitch's stable there are thirty horses

[013-172]of various kinds; he drives out in a coach built on the estate, that

[013-173]weighs four tons. He receives visitors very cordially, and entertains

[013-174]them sumptuously; in other words, thanks to the stupefying powers of

[013-175]our national cookery, he deprives them of all capacity for doing

[013-176]anything but playing preference. For his part, he never does anything,

[013-177]and has even given up reading the \_Dream-book\_. But there are a good

[013-178]many of our landed gentry in Russia exactly like this. It will be

[013-179]asked: 'What is my object in talking about him?...' Well, by way of

[013-180]answering that question, let me describe to you one of my visits at

[013-181]Mardary Apollonitch's.

[013-182]I arrived one summer evening at seven o'clock. An evening service was

[013-183]only just over; the priest, a young man, apparently very timid, and

[013-184]only lately come from the seminary, was sitting in the drawing-room

[013-185]near the door, on the extreme edge of a chair. Mardary Apollonitch

[013-186]received me as usual, very cordially; he was genuinely delighted to see

[013-187]any visitor, and indeed he was the most good-natured of men altogether.

[013-188]The priest got up and took his hat.

[013-189]'Wait a bit, wait a bit, father,' said Mardary Apollonitch, not yet

[013-190]leaving go of my hand; 'don't go ... I have sent for some vodka for

[013-191]you.'

[013-192]'I never drink it, sir,' the priest muttered in confusion, blushing up

[013-193]to his ears.

[013-194]'What nonsense!' answered Mardary Apollonitch; 'Mishka! Yushka! vodka

[013-195]for the father!'

[013-196]Yushka, a tall, thin old man of eighty, came in with a glass of vodka

[013-197]on a dark-coloured tray, with a few patches of flesh-colour on it, all

[013-198]that was left of the original enamel.

[013-199]The priest began to decline.

[013-200]'Come, drink it up, father, no ceremony; it's too bad of you,' observed

[013-201]the landowner reproachfully.

[013-202]The poor young man had to obey.

[013-203]'There, now, father, you may go.'

[013-204]The priest took leave.

[013-205]'There, there, that'll do, get along with you....'

[013-206]'A capital fellow,' pursued Mardary Apollonitch, looking after him, 'I

[013-207]like him very much; there's only one thing--he's young yet. But how are

[013-208]you, my dear sir?... What have you been doing? How are you? Let's come

[013-209]out on to the balcony--such a lovely evening.'

[013-210]We went out on the balcony, sat down, and began to talk. Mardary

[013-211]Apollonitch glanced below, and suddenly fell into a state of tremendous

[013-212]excitement.

[013-213]'Whose hens are those? whose hens are those?' he shouted: 'Whose are

[013-214]those hens roaming about in the garden?... Whose are those hens? How

[013-215]many times I've forbidden it! How many times I've spoken about it!'

[013-216]Yushka ran out.

[013-217]'What disorder!' protested Mardary Apollonitch; 'it's horrible!'

[013-218]The unlucky hens, two speckled and one white with a topknot, as I still

[013-219]remember, went on stalking tranquilly about under the apple-trees,

[013-220]occasionally giving vent to their feelings in a prolonged clucking,

[013-221]when suddenly Yushka, bareheaded and stick in hand, with three other

[013-222]house-serfs of mature years, flew at them simultaneously. Then the fun

[013-223]began. The hens clucked, flapped their wings, hopped, raised a

[013-224]deafening cackle; the house-serfs ran, tripping up and tumbling over;

[013-225]their master shouted from the balcony like one possessed: 'Catch 'em,

[013-226]catch 'em, catch 'em, catch 'em, catch 'em, catch 'em, catch 'em!'

[013-227]At last one servant succeeded in catching the hen with the topknot,

[013-228]tumbling upon her, and at the very same moment a little girl of eleven,

[013-229]with dishevelled hair, and a dry branch in her hand, jumped over the

[013-230]garden-fence from the village street.

[013-231]'Ah, we see now whose hens!' cried the landowner in triumph. 'They're

[013-232]Yermil, the coachman's, hens! he's sent his Natalka to chase them

[013-233]out.... He didn't send his Parasha, no fear!' the landowner added in a

[013-234]low voice with a significant snigger. 'Hey, Yushka! let the hens alone;

[013-235]catch Natalka for me.'

[013-236]But before the panting Yushka had time to reach the terrified little

[013-237]girl the house-keeper suddenly appeared, snatched her by the arm, and

[013-238]slapped her several times on the back....

[013-239]'That's it! that's it!' cried the master, 'tut-tut-tut!... And carry

[013-240]off the hens, Avdotya,' he added in a loud voice, and he turned with a

[013-241]beaming face to me; 'that was a fine chase, my dear sir, hey?--I'm in a

[013-242]regular perspiration: look.'

[013-243]And Mardary Apollonitch went off into a series of chuckles.

[013-244]We remained on the balcony. The evening was really exceptionally fine.

[013-245]Tea was served us.

[013-246]'Tell me,' I began, 'Mardary Apollonitch: are those your peasants'

[013-247]huts, out there on the highroad, above the ravine?'

[013-248]'Yes ... why do you ask?'

[013-249]'I wonder at you, Mardary Apollonitch? It's really sinful. The huts

[013-250]allotted to the peasants there are wretched cramped little hovels;

[013-251]there isn't a tree to be seen near them; there's not a pond even;

[013-252]there's only one well, and that's no good. Could you really find no

[013-253]other place to settle them?... And they say you're taking away the old

[013-254]hemp-grounds, too?'

[013-255]'And what is one to do with this new division of the lands?' Mardary

[013-256]Apollonitch made answer. 'Do you know I've this re-division quite on my

[013-257]mind, and I foresee no sort of good from it. And as for my having taken

[013-258]away the hemp-ground, and their not having dug any ponds, or what

[013-259]not--as to that, my dear sir, I know my own business. I'm a plain

[013-260]man--I go on the old system. To my ideas, when a man's master--he's

[013-261]master; and when he's peasant--he's peasant. ... That's what I think

[013-262]about it.'

[013-263]To an argument so clear and convincing there was of course no answer.

[013-264]'And besides,' he went on, 'those peasants are a wretched lot; they're

[013-265]in disgrace. Particularly two families there; why, my late father--God

[013-266]rest his soul--couldn't bear them; positively couldn't bear them. And

[013-267]you know my precept is: where the father's a thief, the son's a thief;

[013-268]say what you like.... Blood, blood--oh, that's the great thing!'

[013-269]Meanwhile there was a perfect stillness in the air. Only rarely there

[013-270]came a gust of wind, which, as it sank for the last time near the

[013-271]house, brought to our ears the sound of rhythmically repeated blows,

[013-272]seeming to come from the stable. Mardary Apollonitch was in the act of

[013-273]lifting a saucer full of tea to his lips, and was just inflating his

[013-274]nostrils to sniff its fragrance--no true-born Russian, as we all know,

[013-275]can drink his tea without this preliminary--but he stopped short,

[013-276]listened, nodded his head, sipped his tea, and laying the saucer on the

[013-277]table, with the most good-natured smile imaginable, he murmured as

[013-278]though involuntarily accompanying the blows: 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk!

[013-279]Tchuki-tchuk!'

[013-280]'What is it?' I asked puzzled. 'Oh, by my order, they're punishing a

[013-281]scamp of a fellow.... Do you happen to remember Vasya, who waits at the

[013-282]sideboard?'

[013-283]'Which Vasya?'

[013-284]'Why, that waited on us at dinner just now. He with the long whiskers.'

[013-285]The fiercest indignation could not have stood against the clear mild

[013-286]gaze of Mardary Apollonitch.

[013-287]'What are you after, young man? what is it?' he said, shaking his head.

[013-288]'Am I a criminal or something, that you stare at me like that? "Whom he

[013-289]loveth he chasteneth"; you know that.'

[013-290]A quarter of an hour later I had taken leave of Mardary Apollonitch. As

[013-291]I was driving through the village I caught sight of Vasya. He was

[013-292]walking down the village street, cracking nuts. I told the coachman to

[013-293]stop the horses and called him up.

[013-294]'Well, my boy, so they've been punishing you to-day?' I said to him.

[013-295]'How did you know?' answered Vasya.

[013-296]'Your master told me.'

[013-297]'The master himself?'

[013-298]'What did he order you to be punished for?'

[013-299]'Oh, I deserved it, father; I deserved it. They don't punish for

[013-300]trifles among us; that's not the way with us--no, no. Our master's not

[013-301]like that; our master ... you won't find another master like him in all

[013-302]the province.'

[013-303]'Drive on!' I said to the coachman.' There you have it, old Russia!' I

[013-304]mused on my homeward way.
