# Petchora

## Overview

The Petchora (Pechora) is a great river of the Russian far north, named a single time in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — in the opening digression of the fourteenth sketch, [[lebedyan|Lebedyan]].

It appears not as a setting but as the outer limit of a [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman's wandering: the "blessed banks" (014-029) a hunter might reach by following his game ever farther from home.

## Description

The reference comes as the [[The Narrator|narrator]] explains, half-humorously, how a day's shooting can carry a man to the ends of Russia.

A [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman, he observes, "may often set off on a fine morning" (014-026) from his "ancestral roof, in the full intention of returning there the following evening" (014-028), and yet, "little by little, still in pursuit of snipe, may get at last to the blessed banks of Petchora" (014-029).

The river stands at the extreme edge of this imagined drift — a byword for how far a passion for the gun and the dog can lead.

## Role in the Stories

Petchora has no part in the events of the story; it is a rhetorical marker of the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman's boundless, aimless roving.

The same digression that names it is what accounts for the narrator's turning up, almost by chance, at the horse-fair of [[Lebedyan]] — so the distant northern river and the busy southern market-town are set side by side as the two poles of a hunter's wanderings.
