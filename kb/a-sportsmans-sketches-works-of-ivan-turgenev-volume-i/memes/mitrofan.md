# Mitrofan

## Overview

Mitrofan is the gardener at [[Raspberry Spring]], introduced early in Turgenev's sketch of the same name as part of the narrator's portrait of the ruined manor's surviving inhabitants. After the fire that destroyed the manor house, Mitrofan and his family were installed in a hastily assembled hut roofed with timber that had been bought ten years earlier for the construction of a Gothic pavilion (003-047–003-048). The detail is quintessential Turgenev: grand architectural ambitions reduced to patching a serf's roof. Mitrofan himself is a peripheral figure — hardworking, dutiful, scarcely heard from — but the circumstances of his domestic life encapsulate the sketch's broader meditation on decay, obligation, and the gap between the master's pretensions and the serf's reality.

## Appearance and Character

Turgenev gives no physical portrait of Mitrofan; the gardener is defined by his situation rather than his features. He is a man of quiet endurance: ordered to send greens and garden-stuff for the master's table a hundred and fifty miles away, he complies without protest (003-050–003-051). His wife [[Axinya]] is put in charge of a Tyrolese cow bought at high price in [[moscow|Moscow]] that has never yielded a drop of milk (003-051–003-053). The juxtaposition — expensive imported cow, useless; cheap serf labour, indispensable — is left for the reader to absorb.

Mitrofan's most telling trait is his quiet, unspoken charity. After the fire, the forsaken creature [[Styopushka]] "sought a refuge at the gardener Mitrofan's. The gardener left him alone; he did not say 'Live with me,' but he did not drive him away" (003-100–003-101). This minimal gesture — neither invitation nor rejection — captures the precariousness of generosity among serfs themselves. Mitrofan has nothing to spare, but he will not turn out a creature more destitute than himself.

## Role in the Story

Mitrofan appears in "[[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]]" in three linked capacities. First, he is part of the sketch's opening panorama of the ruined estate: the Gothic-pavilion timber, the Tyrolese cow, the seven children for whom "in consideration of their tender age, no special duties had been provided, a fact, however, which had not hindered them from growing up" (003-055–003-056). The irony is gentle but precise — the estate that cannot feed its serfs' children still demands greens for the master's distant table.

Second, Mitrofan serves as a silent foil to [[styopushka|Styopushka]]. When Turgenev introduces the mysterious, barely human figure of [[styopushka|Styopushka]] — who "seems never to have opened his lips from the time of his birth" (003-098–003-099) — it is Mitrofan's hut that shelters him and Mitrofan's tacit tolerance that sustains him. The gardener represents the thin layer of communal obligation that persists even at the bottom of the social order.

Third, Mitrofan is invoked at the sketch's close, when the narrator, fishing with [[Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)|Tuman]] and [[styopushka|Styopushka]], asks the stammering Styopka: "'And is Mitrofan well?'" (003-162). The casual inquiry is the only speech Mitrofan's name ever attracts, and the answer — "Well — yes, yes — your honour" (003-163) — is perfunctory. Yet the question itself is significant: in a sketch populated by the spectral and the ruined, Mitrofan is one of the few figures who is still alive, still working, still there.
