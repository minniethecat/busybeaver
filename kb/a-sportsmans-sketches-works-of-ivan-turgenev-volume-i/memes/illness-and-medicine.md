# Illness and Medicine

Sickness is never far from the world of [[A Sportsman's Sketches]], and the remedies for it range from the trained physician's powders to the wandering peasant's herbs. Two figures anchor the theme: the [[the-district-doctor|district doctor]], and the folk-healer [[Kassyan]].

## The District Doctor

The sketch opens with the narrator's own ordinary illness and the doctor's brisk, faintly mercenary bedside manner:

> (004-005) He prescribed me the usual sudorific, ordered a mustard-plaster to be put on, very deftly slid a five-rouble note up his sleeve, coughing drily and looking away as he did so.

But the tale the doctor then tells — of a young gentlewoman he attended and could not save — turns on the helplessness of medicine before a mortal disease:

> (004-161) ...be a remedy, you think, for this disease, if one could find it.

## Folk Healing

Against the town doctor stands Kassyan, whom the peasants credit with healing powers. His coachman taunts him with a death he could not prevent:

> (009-217) Why didn't you cure him, eh? You know they say you cure folks; you're a doctor.

Kassyan's medicine is the medicine of the fields — gathered on foot, in silence, from the roadside:

> (009-270) ...he was for ever stooping down to pick herbs of some kind, thrusting them into his bosom, muttering to himself.

## Social and Historical Context

The two healers measure the distance between official and folk Russia. The district doctor has learning, powders and a fee to pocket, yet is powerless at the deathbed; Kassyan has only herbs and a reputation, yet the peasants bring him their sick. Turgenev sets neither above the other: both stand, finally, before the same [[Death and Mortality|mortality]] they cannot master.
