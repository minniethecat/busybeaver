# Ilyusha

## Overview

Ilyusha is one of the five [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys in [[BYEZHIN PRAIRIE]], about twelve years old. He is the principal storyteller of the group, regaling the others with tales of the domovoy (house-spirit), the ghost of a departed master, the legendary Trishka, and other supernatural beings.

His knowledge of folk belief is extensive, and he delivers his stories with a warmth and conviction that make him the emotional centre of the fireside gathering. More than any other boy, Ilyusha embodies the oral culture of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]], in which the boundary between the natural and the supernatural remains porous.


## Appearance and Character

Ilyusha's face was "rather uninteresting" with a long, short-sighted, hook-nosed profile (008-204). Yet Turgenev notes that it expressed a "dull, sickly anxiety," and when he spoke, his features became animated with feeling. He was dressed poorly, like most of the boys, in bast shoes and old clothes.

Ilyusha sat beside [[kostya|Kostya]], "still kept blinking constrainedly" (008-228), and tended the fire, poking splinters into the boiling water (008-227). His defining traits are his intense emotional engagement with the stories he tells and his sincere, unquestioning belief in the supernatural world he describes.


## Role in the Story

Ilyusha is the wellspring of supernatural narrative in the chapter. He tells the story of the domovoy he heard in the old paper-mill (008-236--008-244), describes the ghost of a departed master seeking the magic grass that opens locks (008-353--008-412), and heatedly explains the legend of Trishka, the apocalyptic figure (008-488).

When [[kostya|Kostya]] asks about Akulina and [[vasya|Vasya]], Ilyusha supplies the details: Akulina's lover deceived her, and [[vasya|Vasya]] was his mother's favourite son, whose drowning was foretold by a supernatural voice (008-607--008-625).

Ilyusha is also the voice of superstitious warning: he cautions [[fedya|Fedya]] against speaking ill of the wood-spirit (008-574), whispers "Christ's aid be with us" at a moment of dread (008-346), and pronounces the water-spirit's calling of [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]]'s name "a bad omen" (008-656).

His parting warning to [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]] — "Take care you don't fall into the river!" (008-598) — carries a chilling prescience given [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]]'s later death.
