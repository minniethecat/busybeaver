# Vassily Nikolaitch Lubozvonov

## Overview

Vassily Nikolaitch Lubozvonov is a young [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] whose bizarre behaviour becomes the subject of puzzled discussion between [[Ovsyanikov]] and the narrator in "The Peasant Proprietor [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]." Recently come into his inheritance from his mother, Lubozvonov baffles his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s by treating them with a formality and deference utterly alien to the conventions of Russian serfdom. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], unable to make sense of the reports he hears, turns to the narrator for an explanation, making Lubozvonov's story a study in the collision between new-fangled ideas and the ancient realities of the Russian countryside.

## Appearance & Character

Lubozvonov is a young man, "not long since he received his heritage from his mother" (006-338–006-339). [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] offers a brief physical sketch: "He's as broad as he's long, and a face like this — God bless him! — and bearded, though he is so young" (006-382–006-383). The description suggests a man in robust health, which makes his puzzling behaviour harder to dismiss as mere illness — a point the narrator tries to suggest and [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] dismisses: "Ill, indeed!"

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' description of their master paints a picture of radical eccentricity: "he is a young man … like a girl, always reading books" (006-338, 006-343). When Lubozvonov first arrived at his estate and came out to meet the assembled [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, he did something unprecedented: instead of the master's customary commanding tone, he spoke to them softly. The narrator reports [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s account:

> "Vassily Nikolaitch came out to them. The peasants looked at him — and he was dressed in a velvet coat, and didn't look at them, and kept his eyes on the ground — 'I am very glad, children,' says he, 'of your devotion; only I beg of you, for God's sake, be contented and don't drink!'" (006-340–006-349)

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, accustomed to being addressed with the familiar "thou" (ty) by their masters, are thrown into confusion when Lubozvonov uses the formal "you" (vy) — "he calls us 'you,' just like a gentleman to another gentleman" (006-355). This breach of the established social grammar is more disturbing to them than any cruelty would be: it violates the unwritten code by which the world has always operated.

## Role in the Story

Lubozvonov appears in "The Peasant Proprietor [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]" as part of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s survey of the contemporary [[landowning-gentry|nobility]], contrasted with the old-fashioned [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s of the Catherinian age. After recounting the brute excesses of men like [[Stepan Niktopolionitch Komov]] and the narrator's grandfather, [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] turns to Lubozvonov as an example of a different kind of strangeness — not the old brutality, but a new, incomprehensible softness.

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' confusion is existential. One [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] tells [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]: "He is not a master at all … in the old days masters never behaved like this. They say, 'There is no making anything of him.' It is queer!" (006-376–006-378). The word "queer" here — *chudno* in the Russian — captures the uncanny quality of Lubozvonov's behaviour: it is not kindness as the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s understand it, but something alien and unsettling. When [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] presses for details, the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] can only repeat variations on the same theme: the master won't give orders, won't address them properly, won't act like a master.

The narrator's suggestion that Lubozvonov "must certainly be ill" (006-381) is a rationalist's attempt to pathologize what is essentially a cultural anomaly — a man trying to import liberal, Western-influenced ideas of human dignity into a social system built on categorical inequality. Lubozvonov's velvet coat, his downcast eyes, his formal address, his pleas for temperance — all of it reads to the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s not as benevolence but as a failure of the masterly role itself. Turgenev leaves the question open: is Lubozvonov a reformer ahead of his time, or simply a man unfitted for the responsibilities of landowning?

## Historical/Social Context

Lubozvonov represents the generation of young Russian noblemen influenced by Western liberal ideas — the same current that would produce the Decembrists and, later, the reformers of the 1860s. His velvet coat and book-reading mark him as a product of the universities, possibly educated abroad. The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' bafflement at his formal address reflects the deep grammar of serfdom: the master's "thou" to the serf was not merely a pronoun but a ritual of subordination, and Lubozvonov's refusal to participate in that ritual destabilizes the entire social order. Turgenev, writing in the 1840s–1850s, captures a transitional moment in Russian rural life — the old brutality was dying, but its replacement was not yet comprehensible to those who had been brutalized.
