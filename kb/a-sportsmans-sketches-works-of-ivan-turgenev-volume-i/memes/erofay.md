# Erofay

## Overview

Erofay is a coachman employed by the narrator in Turgenev's sketch "[[kassyan|Kassyan of Fair Springs]]" (Chapter IX). He drives the narrator's carriage and serves as a practical, plain-spoken contrast to the mysterious and otherworldly [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[kassyan|Kassyan]]. His cheerful contempt for [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s eccentricity — coupled with his earthy, no-nonsense manner — establishes the narrative's central tension between the rational and the mystical, the practical and the spiritual.

## Appearance & Character

Erofay is characterised primarily through his speech and manner rather than through physical description. He is direct, unpretentious, and occasionally sardonic. When he encounters [[kassyan|Kassyan]], he greets him with a grin: "Hallo! [[kassyan|Kassyanushka]]! good health to you!" — to which [[kassyan|Kassyan]] replies, "Good health to you, Erofay, upright man!" — a reply that carries an edge of melancholy irony (009-200). Erofay's teasing of [[kassyan|Kassyan]] reveals his own blunt practicality. When he learns that Martin the carpenter has died, he immediately asks [[kassyan|Kassyan]]: "Why didn't you cure him, eh? You know they say you cure folks; you're a doctor" — a remark delivered with evident mockery (009-208). [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s nickname, "the [[kassyan|Flea]]," is disclosed by Erofay, who whispers to the narrator that the old man is "cracked" and "such a queer fellow" (009-229).

Erofay's practical sensibility is most sharply expressed through his evaluation of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s horse and cart. He taps [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s nag disparagingly on the back with his third finger and says contemptuously: "See, it's asleep, the scare-crow!" (009-229). He almost overturns [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s little cart with one hand, remarking: "A trap!... But what will you drive in it to the clearing?... You can't harness our horses in these shafts; our horses are all too big" (009-229).

## Role in the Story

Erofay drives the narrator to the settlement where they encounter [[kassyan|Kassyan]]. When [[kassyan|Kassyan]] suggests acting as a guide to the grouse-haunted clearings, Erofay approves the plan and unharnesses the horses. While he works, he engages [[kassyan|Kassyan]] in barbed conversation — asking about Martin's death, mocking [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s reputation as a healer, and deriding the old man's horse and cart. Before they depart, Erofay whispers a warning to the narrator: "You did well, your honour, to drive with him. He is such a queer fellow; he's cracked, you know, and his nickname is the [[kassyan|Flea]]. I don't know how you managed to make him out.... But you keep a look-out where he is driving you to" (009-232).

Later in the story, when an axle breaks, it is Erofay who fits the new one, demonstrating his practical competence (009-end). Throughout the sketch, Erofay functions as the voice of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] common sense — earthy, sceptical, and grounded — against which [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s visionary strangeness is measured.

## Relationship to Kassyan

Erofay's relationship with [[kassyan|Kassyan]] is one of affectionate contempt. He treats the old man with the familiar condescension of the practical man toward the dreamer, yet there is no real malice in his mockery. His warning to the narrator — "keep a look-out where he is driving you to" — is both a joke at [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s expense and a genuine expression of the coachman's protective instinct. In the world of Turgenev's sketches, figures like Erofay represent the unreflective, instinctive life of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] — a life close to the earth, governed by practical needs, and untroubled by the metaphysical questions that torment souls like [[kassyan|Kassyan]].
