# Mitya

## Overview

Mitya (a diminutive of Dmitri) is the nephew of [[ovsyanikov|the peasant proprietor]] [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], appearing in Turgenev's sketch "The Peasant Proprietor [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]" (Chapter VI). A tall, well-made young man of twenty-eight with curly hair, Mitya represents the restless younger generation — educated but without steady occupation, caught between the old world of his uncle and the uncertain opportunities of the new. His story unfolds largely through [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s exasperated account and a brief but vivid appearance in the [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] household.

## Appearance & Character

Mitya is described as "a young man of twenty-eight, tall, well-made, and curly-headed" (006-425). He wears German-style clothing, but Turgenev notes that "the unnatural size of the puffs on his shoulders was enough alone to prove convincingly that the tailor who had cut it was a Russian of the Russians" (006-425) — a telling detail that captures Mitya's awkward position between Western aspiration and provincial Russian reality.

[[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] describes his nephew with a mixture of pride and frustration: "He's a fellow with brains — a smart fellow — I don't dispute that; he has had a good education, but I don't expect much good to come of him" (006-396). Mitya left a government post because he "didn't get on fast enough," believing — in his uncle's sarcastic paraphrase — that he should rise to general immediately (006-396). Since then, he has lived without occupation, "taken to litigation," and begun writing petitions for [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, instructing village delegates, and frequenting taverns with "city tradesmen and inn-keepers" (006-396).

When Mitya appears in person, his initial manner is bashful — he stops short in the doorway upon seeing the narrator — but he quickly becomes spirited and self-justifying. Challenged by his uncle to explain a recent complaint against him, Mitya tosses his head and declares: "I have nothing to be ashamed of" (006-437). He then launches into a detailed account of defending [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] proprietors against a corrupt government inspector, explaining how he helped them navigate the courts rather than simply bribing the official — a course of action that his uncle views as reckless and likely to bring trouble (006-437).

## Role in the Story

Mitya's role is primarily thematic: he embodies the unsettled energy of the post-Napoleonic generation in rural Russia. His story is told mostly through [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s monologue, but his brief appearance brings the conflict between the generations into sharp focus. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], a man of gravity, tradition, and patient accommodation with the system, cannot understand his nephew's restless rebellion. Mitya, for his part, is clearly intelligent and principled but lacks direction — his "spirited" self-defence cannot quite mask the aimlessness of his life.

The sketch's final note on Mitya is ambiguous. His aunt [[tatyana-ilyinitchna|Tatyana Ilyinitchna]], soft-hearted and forgiving, has interceded on his behalf, and [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] grudgingly agrees to forgive him. The last exchange between uncle and nephew carries a warning: "Only take care for the future, or else, Mitya, upon my word..." — a threat left incomplete, hanging over the young man's uncertain prospects (006-463).

## Historical/Social Context

Mitya belongs to a transitional generation in Russian history — educated enough to feel stifled by the old order but lacking the clear path that would open to later generations. His petition-writing places him in the ambiguous position of defending [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s against official corruption while remaining vulnerable to official retaliation. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s fear that the constables and police-captains "will boil his kettle for him some day" is not idle — it reflects the real dangers faced by those who challenged the administrative system in Nicholas I's Russia.
