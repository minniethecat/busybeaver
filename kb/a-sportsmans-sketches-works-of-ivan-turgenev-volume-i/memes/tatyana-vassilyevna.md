## Overview

Tatyana Vassilyevna was a provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who possessed the estate and village of [[Lgov]] for approximately twenty years before selling it to [[Afanasy Nefeditch]]. She died the year before the events of [[Lgov]], an old maid who never married and, according to the serf [[Sutchok]], extended her own single life into a blanket prohibition: she did not permit any of her serfs to marry (007-236). She inherited her serfs from her father [[Vassily Semenitch]] and ran a household that included a private serf theatre, coffee-bearers with assigned stage names, and a rotating cast of servants performing whatever duties her whims dictated.

## Appearance and Character

Tatyana Vassilyevna is never seen directly by the narrator; her character is reconstructed entirely through [[Sutchok]]'s rambling autobiography, delivered while the two men sit stranded in the overgrown pond at [[Lgov]]. She emerges as an eccentric, mildly theatrical figure of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]—the kind of [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who renamed servants on a whim, turning Kuzma into Anton because "the mistress ordered that I should be called so" (007-188). She maintained a private theatre in which serfs were "dressed up" and told "See, this is what you must say" (007-200). Her most revealing pronouncement, recalled verbatim by [[Sutchok]] decades later, was: "God forbid! here am I living single: what indulgence! What are they thinking of!" (007-238). The logic is stark: because she never married, no serf under her authority would be permitted to do so either.

## Role in the Story

Tatyana Vassilyevna occupies a pivotal position in the genealogy of [[lgov-place|Lgov]]'s ownership—a chain that the narrator and [[Yermolaï]] reconstruct during their abortive duck-shooting expedition. She inherited the serfs from her father, [[Vassily Semenitch]], and "owned us a goodish while ... twenty years" (007-180). During her tenure [[Sutchok]] served her successively as cook, coffee-bearer, and actor in the private theatre. She eventually sold [[Lgov]] to [[Afanasy Nefeditch]], from whom it passed by inheritance to [[Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev]].

Her prohibition on serf marriage is one of the Sketches' most economical illustrations of the arbitrary power wielded by the serf-owning [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] over the most intimate aspects of their serfs' lives—a single woman's personal choice becomes law for everyone beneath her, extending even to the denial of family formation. In the broader texture of [[Lgov]], her memory coexists with the chapter's melancholic comedy: a once-bustling estate is now a deserted pond where an old fisherman recounts the masters who have passed through his life like seasons.
