# MY NEIGHBOUR RADILOV

[005-001]For the autumn, woodcocks often take refuge in old gardens of

[005-002]lime-trees. There are a good many such gardens among us, in the

[005-003]province of Orel. Our forefathers, when they selected a place for

[005-004]habitation, invariably marked out two acres of good ground for a

[005-005]fruit-garden, with avenues of lime-trees. Within the last fifty, or

[005-006]seventy years at most, these mansions--'noblemen's nests,' as they call

[005-007]them--have gradually disappeared off the face of the earth; the houses

[005-008]are falling to pieces, or have been sold for the building materials;

[005-009]the stone outhouses have become piles of rubbish; the apple-trees are

[005-010]dead and turned into firewood, the hedges and fences are pulled up.

[005-011]Only the lime-trees grow in all their glory as before, and with

[005-012]ploughed fields all round them, tell a tale to this light-hearted

[005-013]generation of 'our fathers and brothers who have lived before us.'

[005-014]A magnificent tree is such an old lime-tree.... Even the merciless axe

[005-015]of the Russian peasant spares it. Its leaves are small, its powerful

[005-016]limbs spread wide in all directions; there is perpetual shade under

[005-017]them.

[005-018]Once, as I was wandering about the fields after partridges with

[005-019]Yermolaï, I saw some way off a deserted garden, and turned into it. I

[005-020]had hardly crossed its borders when a snipe rose up out of a bush with

[005-021]a clatter. I fired my gun, and at the same instant, a few paces from

[005-022]me, I heard a shriek; the frightened face of a young girl peeped out

[005-023]for a second from behind the trees, and instantly disappeared. Yermolaï

[005-024]ran up to me: 'Why are you shooting here? there is a landowner living

[005-025]here.'

[005-026]Before I had time to answer him, before my dog had had time to bring

[005-027]me, with dignified importance, the bird I had shot, swift footsteps

[005-028]were heard, and a tall man with moustaches came out of the thicket and

[005-029]stopped, with an air of displeasure, before me. I made my apologies as

[005-030]best I could, gave him my name, and offered him the bird that had been

[005-031]killed on his domains.

[005-032]'Very well,' he said to me with a smile; 'I will take your game, but

[005-033]only on one condition: that you will stay and dine with us.'

[005-034]I must confess I was not greatly delighted at his proposition, but it

[005-035]was impossible to refuse.

[005-036]'I am a landowner here, and your neighbour, Radilov; perhaps you have

[005-037]heard of me?' continued my new acquaintance; 'to-day is Sunday, and we

[005-038]shall be sure to have a decent dinner, otherwise I would not have

[005-039]invited you.'

[005-040]I made such a reply as one does make in such circumstances, and turned

[005-041]to follow him. A little path that had lately been cleared soon led us

[005-042]out of the grove of lime-trees; we came into the kitchen-garden.

[005-043]Between the old apple-trees and gooseberry bushes were rows of curly

[005-044]whitish-green cabbages; the hop twined its tendrils round high poles;

[005-045]there were thick ranks of brown twigs tangled over with dried peas;

[005-046]large flat pumpkins seemed rolling on the ground; cucumbers showed

[005-047]yellow under their dusty angular leaves; tall nettles were waving along

[005-048]the hedge; in two or three places grew clumps of tartar honeysuckle,

[005-049]elder, and wild rose--the remnants of former flower-beds. Near a small

[005-050]fish-pond, full of reddish and slimy water, we saw the well, surrounded

[005-051]by puddles. Ducks were busily splashing and waddling about these

[005-052]puddles; a dog blinking and twitching in every limb was gnawing a bone

[005-053]in the meadow, where a piebald cow was lazily chewing the grass, from

[005-054]time to time flicking its tail over its lean back. The little path

[005-055]turned to one side; from behind thick willows and birches we caught

[005-056]sight of a little grey old house, with a boarded roof and a winding

[005-057]flight of steps. Radilov stopped short.

[005-058]'But,' he said, with a good-humoured and direct look in my face,' on

[005-059]second thoughts ... perhaps you don't care to come and see me, after

[005-060]all.... In that case--'

[005-061]I did not allow him to finish, but assured him that, on the contrary,

[005-062]it would be a great pleasure to me to dine with him.

[005-063]'Well, you know best.'

[005-064]We went into the house. A young man in a long coat of stout blue cloth

[005-065]met us on the steps. Radilov at once told him to bring Yermolaï some

[005-066]vodka; my huntsman made a respectful bow to the back of the munificent

[005-067]host. From the hall, which was decorated with various parti-coloured

[005-068]pictures and check curtains, we went into a small room--Radilov's

[005-069]study. I took off my hunting accoutrements, and put my gun in a corner;

[005-070]the young man in the long-skirted coat busily brushed me down.

[005-071]'Well, now, let us go into the drawing-room.' said Radilov cordially.

[005-072]'I will make you acquainted with my mother.'

[005-073]I walked after him. In the drawing-room, in the sofa in the centre of

[005-074]the room, was sitting an old lady of medium height, in a

[005-075]cinnamon-coloured dress and a white cap, with a thinnish, kind old

[005-076]face, and a timid, mournful expression.

[005-077]'Here, mother, let me introduce to you our neighbour....'

[005-078]The old lady got up and made me a bow, not letting go out of her

[005-079]withered hands a fat worsted reticule that looked like a sack.

[005-080]'Have you been long in our neighbourhood?' she asked, in a weak and

[005-081]gentle voice, blinking her eyes.

[005-082]'No, not long.'

[005-083]'Do you intend to remain here long?'

[005-084]'Till the winter, I think.'

[005-085]The old lady said no more.

[005-086]'And here,' interposed Radilov, indicating to me a tall and thin man,

[005-087]whom I had not noticed on entering the drawing-room, 'is Fyodor

[005-088]Miheitch. ... Come, Fedya, give the visitor a specimen of your art. Why

[005-089]have you hidden yourself away in that corner?'

[005-090]Fyodor Miheitch got up at once from his chair, fetched a wretched

[005-091]little fiddle from the window, took the bow--not by the end, as is

[005-092]usual, but by the middle--put the fiddle to his chest, shut his eyes,

[005-093]and fell to dancing, singing a song, and scraping on the strings. He

[005-094]looked about seventy; a thin nankin overcoat flapped pathetically about

[005-095]his dry and bony limbs. He danced, at times skipping boldly, and then

[005-096]dropping his little bald head with his scraggy neck stretched out as if

[005-097]he were dying, stamping his feet on the ground, and sometimes bending

[005-098]his knees with obvious difficulty. A voice cracked with age came from

[005-099]his toothless mouth.

[005-100]Radilov must have guessed from the expression of my face that Fedya's

[005-101]'art' did not give me much pleasure.

[005-102]'Very good, old man, that's enough,' he said. 'You can go and refresh

[005-103]yourself.'

[005-104]Fyodor Miheitch at once laid down the fiddle on the window-sill, bowed

[005-105]first to me as the guest, then to the old lady, then to Radilov, and

[005-106]went away.

[005-107]'He too was a landowner,' my new friend continued, 'and a rich one too,

[005-108]but he ruined himself--so he lives now with me.... But in his day he

[005-109]was considered the most dashing fellow in the province; he eloped with

[005-110]two married ladies; he used to keep singers, and sang himself, and

[005-111]danced like a master.... But won't you take some vodka? dinner is just

[005-112]ready.'

[005-113]A young girl, the same that I had caught a glimpse of in the garden,

[005-114]came into the room.

[005-115]'And here is Olga!' observed Radilov, slightly turning his head; 'let

[005-116]me present you.... Well, let us go into dinner.'

[005-117]We went in and sat down to the table. While we were coming out of the

[005-118]drawing-room and taking our seats, Fyodor Miheitch, whose eyes were

[005-119]bright and his nose rather red after his 'refreshment,' sang 'Raise the

[005-120]cry of Victory.' They laid a separate cover for him in a corner on a

[005-121]little table without a table-napkin. The poor old man could not boast

[005-122]of very nice habits, and so they always kept him at some distance from

[005-123]society. He crossed himself, sighed, and began to eat like a shark. The

[005-124]dinner was in reality not bad, and in honour of Sunday was accompanied,

[005-125]of course, with shaking jelly and Spanish puffs of pastry. At the table

[005-126]Radilov, who had served ten years in an infantry regiment and had been

[005-127]in Turkey, fell to telling anecdotes; I listened to him with attention,

[005-128]and secretly watched Olga. She was not very pretty; but the tranquil

[005-129]and resolute expression of her face, her broad, white brow, her thick

[005-130]hair, and especially her brown eyes--not large, but clear, sensible and

[005-131]lively--would have made an impression on anyone in my place. She seemed

[005-132]to be following every word Radilov uttered--not so much sympathy as

[005-133]passionate attention was expressed on her face. Radilov in years might

[005-134]have been her father; he called her by her Christian name, but I

[005-135]guessed at once that she was not his daughter. In the course of

[005-136]conversation he referred to his deceased wife--'her sister,' he added,

[005-137]indicating Olga. She blushed quickly and dropped her eyes. Radilov

[005-138]paused a moment and then changed the subject. The old lady did not

[005-139]utter a word during the whole of dinner; she ate scarcely anything

[005-140]herself, and did not press me to partake. Her features had an air of

[005-141]timorous and hopeless expectation, that melancholy of old age which it

[005-142]pierces one's heart to look upon. At the end of dinner Fyodor Miheitch

[005-143]was beginning to 'celebrate' the hosts and guests, but Radilov looked

[005-144]at me and asked him to be quiet; the old man passed his hand over his

[005-145]lips, began to blink, bowed, and sat down again, but only on the very

[005-146]edge of his chair. After dinner I returned with Radilov to his study.

[005-147]In people who are constantly and intensely preoccupied with one idea,

[005-148]or one emotion, there is something in common, a kind of external

[005-149]resemblance in manner, however different may be their qualities, their

[005-150]abilities, their position in society, and their education. The more I

[005-151]watched Radilov, the more I felt that he belonged to the class of such

[005-152]people. He talked of husbandry, of the crops, of the war, of the gossip

[005-153]of the district and the approaching elections; he talked without

[005-154]constraint, and even with interest; but suddenly he would sigh and drop

[005-155]into a chair, and pass his hand over his face, like a man wearied out

[005-156]by a tedious task. His whole nature--a good and warm-hearted one

[005-157]too--seemed saturated through, steeped in some one feeling. I was

[005-158]amazed by the fact that I could not discover in him either a passion

[005-159]for eating, nor for wine, nor for sport, nor for Kursk nightingales,

[005-160]nor for epileptic pigeons, nor for Russian literature, nor for

[005-161]trotting-hacks, nor for Hungarian coats, nor for cards, nor billiards,

[005-162]nor for dances, nor trips to the provincial town or the capital, nor

[005-163]for paper-factories and beet-sugar refineries, nor for painted

[005-164]pavilions, nor for tea, nor for trace-horses trained to hold their

[005-165]heads askew, nor even for fat coachmen belted under their very

[005-166]armpits--those magnificent coachmen whose eyes, for some mysterious

[005-167]reason, seem rolling and starting out of their heads at every

[005-168]movement.... 'What sort of landowner is this, then?' I thought. At the

[005-169]same time he did not in the least pose as a gloomy man discontented

[005-170]with his destiny; on the contrary, he seemed full of indiscrimating

[005-171]good-will, cordial and even offensive readiness to become intimate with

[005-172]every one he came across. In reality you felt at the same time that he

[005-173]could not be friends, nor be really intimate with anyone, and that he

[005-174]could not be so, not because in general he was independent of other

[005-175]people, but because his whole being was for a time turned inwards upon

[005-176]himself. Looking at Radilov, I could never imagine him happy either now

[005-177]or at any time. He, too, was not handsome; but in his eyes, his smile,

[005-178]his whole being, there was a something, mysterious and extremely

[005-179]attractive--yes, mysterious is just what it was. So that you felt you

[005-180]would like to know him better, to get to love him. Of course, at times

[005-181]the landowner and the man of the steppes peeped out in him; but all the

[005-182]same he was a capital fellow.

[005-183]We were beginning to talk about the new marshal of the district, when

[005-184]suddenly we heard Olga's voice at the door: 'Tea is ready.' We went

[005-185]into the drawing-room. Fyodor Miheitch was sitting as before in his

[005-186]corner between the little window and the door, his legs curled up under

[005-187]him. Radilov's mother was knitting a stocking. From the opened windows

[005-188]came a breath of autumn freshness and the scent of apples. Olga was

[005-189]busy pouring out tea. I looked at her now with more attention than at

[005-190]dinner. Like provincial girls as a rule, she spoke very little, but at

[005-191]any rate I did not notice in her any of their anxiety to say something

[005-192]fine, together with their painful consciousness of stupidity and

[005-193]helplessness; she did not sigh as though from the burden of unutterable

[005-194]emotions, nor cast up her eyes, nor smile vaguely and dreamily. Her

[005-195]look expressed tranquil self-possession, like a man who is taking

[005-196]breath after great happiness or great excitement. Her carriage and her

[005-197]movements were resolute and free. I liked her very much.

[005-198]I fell again into conversation with Radilov. I don't recollect what

[005-199]brought us to the familiar observation that often the most

[005-200]insignificant things produce more effect on people than the most

[005-201]important.

[005-202]'Yes,' Radilov agreed, 'I have experienced that in my own case. I, as

[005-203]you know, have been married. It was not for long--three years; my wife

[005-204]died in child-birth. I thought that I should not survive her; I was

[005-205]fearfully miserable, broken down, but I could not weep--I wandered

[005-206]about like one possessed. They decked her out, as they always do, and

[005-207]laid her on a table--in this very room. The priest came, the deacons

[005-208]came, began to sing, to pray, and to burn incense; I bowed to the

[005-209]ground, and hardly shed a tear. My heart seemed turned to stone--and my

[005-210]head too--I was heavy all over. So passed my first day. Would you

[005-211]believe it? I even slept in the night. The next morning I went in to

[005-212]look at my wife: it was summer-time, the sunshine fell upon her from

[005-213]head to foot, and it was so bright. Suddenly I saw ...' (here Radilov

[005-214]gave an involuntary shudder) 'what do you think? One of her eyes was

[005-215]not quite shut, and on this eye a fly was moving.... I fell down in a

[005-216]heap, and when I came to myself, I began to weep and weep ... I could

[005-217]not stop myself....'

[005-218]Radilov was silent. I looked at him, then at Olga.... I can never

[005-219]forget the expression of her face. The old lady had laid the stocking

[005-220]down on her knees, and taken a handkerchief out of her reticule; she

[005-221]was stealthily wiping away her tears. Fyodor Miheitch suddenly got up,

[005-222]seized his fiddle, and in a wild and hoarse voice began to sing a song.

[005-223]He wanted doubtless to restore our spirits; but we all shuddered at his

[005-224]first note, and Radilov asked him to be quiet.

[005-225]'Still what is past, is past,' he continued; 'we cannot recall the

[005-226]past, and in the end ... all is for the best in this world below, as I

[005-227]think Voltaire said,' he added hurriedly.

[005-228]'Yes,' I replied, 'of course. Besides, every trouble can be endured,

[005-229]and there is no position so terrible that there is no escape from it.'

[005-230]'Do you think so?' said Radilov. 'Well, perhaps you are right. I

[005-231]recollect I lay once in the hospital in Turkey half dead; I had typhus

[005-232]fever. Well, our quarters were nothing to boast of--of course, in time

[005-233]of war--and we had to thank God for what we had! Suddenly they bring in

[005-234]more sick--where are they to put them? The doctor goes here and

[005-235]there--there is no room left. So he comes up to me and asks the

[005-236]attendant, "Is he alive?" He answers, "He was alive this morning." The

[005-237]doctor bends down, listens; I am breathing. The good man could not help

[005-238]saying, "Well, what an absurd constitution; the man's dying; he's

[005-239]certain to die, and he keeps hanging on, lingering, taking up space for

[005-240]nothing, and keeping out others." Well, I thought to myself, "So you

[005-241]are in a bad way, Mihal Mihalitch...." And, after all, I got well, and

[005-242]am alive till now, as you may see for yourself. You are right, to be

[005-243]sure.'

[005-244]'In any case I am right,' I replied; 'even if you had died, you would

[005-245]just the same have escaped from your horrible position.'

[005-246]'Of course, of course,' he added, with a violent blow of his fist on

[005-247]the table. 'One has only to come to a decision.... What is the use of

[005-248]being in a horrible position?... What is the good of delaying,

[005-249]lingering.'

[005-250]Olga rose quickly and went out into the garden.

[005-251]'Well, Fedya, a dance!' cried Radilov.

[005-252]Fedya jumped up and walked about the room with that artificial and

[005-253]peculiar motion which is affected by the man who plays the part of a

[005-254]goat with a tame bear. He sang meanwhile, 'While at our Gates....'

[005-255]The rattle of a racing droshky sounded in the drive, and in a few

[005-256]minutes a tall, broad-shouldered and stoutly made man, the peasant

[005-257]proprietor, Ovsyanikov, came into the room.

[005-258]But Ovsyanikov is such a remarkable and original personage that, with

[005-259]the reader's permission, we will put off speaking about him till the

[005-260]next sketch. And now I will only add for myself that the next day I

[005-261]started off hunting at earliest dawn with Yermolaï, and returned home

[005-262]after the day's sport was over ... that a week later I went again to

[005-263]Radilov's, but did not find him or Olga at home, and within a fortnight

[005-264]I learned that he had suddenly disappeared, left his mother, and gone

[005-265]away somewhere with his sister-in-law. The whole province was excited,

[005-266]and talked about this event, and I only then completely understood the

[005-267]expression of Olga's face while Radilov was telling us his story. It

[005-268]was breathing, not with sympathetic suffering only: it was burning with

[005-269]jealousy.

[005-270]Before leaving the country I called on old Madame Radilov. I found her

[005-271]in the drawing-room; she was playing cards with Fyodor Miheitch.

[005-272]'Have you news of your son?' I asked her at last.

[005-273]The old lady began to weep. I made no more inquiries about Radilov.
