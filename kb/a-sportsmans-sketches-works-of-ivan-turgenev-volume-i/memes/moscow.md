# Moscow

## Overview

Moscow is the ancient Russian capital and the single most-frequently mentioned external city in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — it recurs across seven of the collection's stories. It never appears as a place the narrator visits; instead it exists on the horizon of the provincial world of Orel and Kaluga, a distant metropolis toward which serfs are sent to work, from which fashions and machinery are ordered, and in which grandees, tradesmen, and cabmen make their fortunes. In the wandering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[Kassyan]]'s lyrical litany of Russian places it is "Moscow of the golden domes" (009-496), the crowning city of a sacred geography; in the mouths of [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s and serfs it is a practical destination bound up with money, labour, and rent.

## Description

Moscow enters the *Sketches* chiefly through what characters do there, or what they send for from there — the city is defined less by description than by the traffic of people and goods flowing to and from it.

### A destination for serf labour

The most persistent association is with serfs sent away to earn money for their masters. In [[raspberry-spring|Raspberry Spring]], the old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[vlass|Vlas]] tells the fisherman [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] plainly, "I have been to Moscow, to my master" (003-266), and explains that his dead son "lived in Moscow as a cabman; he paid, I must confess, rent for me" (003-275). [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] draws the economic moral: paying rent "would be very well near Moscow, but it's a different matter to pay rent here" (003-319) — the city offering wages that the countryside cannot. In [[lgov|Lgov]], the fowler Suchok recounts how a severe master, after a hunting accident, "ordered them to flog me, and to send me to learn a trade to Moscow, to a shoemaker" (007-214). And in [[the-counting-house|The Counting-House]], a serf tailor boasts that he "learnt my trade in the best house in Moscow, and worked for generals" (011-408), Moscow standing as the measure of skilled craftsmanship.

### A source of goods and fashion

Moscow is equally the source of imported goods that mark a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s status. Livestock could be "bought for a high price in Moscow" (003-052); on [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]]'s model estate in [[the-agent|The Agent]], the narrator inspects a winnowing machine "recently ordered from Moscow" (010-347). In [[two-country-gentlemen|Two Country Gentlemen]], [[Mardary Apollonitch]] "ordered a threshing-machine from Butenop's in Moscow, locked it up in a barn, and then felt his mind at rest on the subject" (013-149), while another gentleman had "his women serfs to wear tiaras after a pattern bespoken from Moscow" (013-051) — Moscow supplying both agricultural machinery and imposed fashion.

### The city of grandees and memory

For the old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] proprietor [[Ovsyanikov]], Moscow is the theatre of a vanished aristocratic grandeur. "I used to see them in Moscow," he says of the real grandees of the past (006-159); asked whether he has been there, he answers, "I went to Moscow when I was sixteen" (006-163). His recollections dwell on a count who at a banquet "made all Moscow drunk" (006-191), and on the serf Fedosya, who "lived in Moscow as a lady's maid" (006-525) before being put up for auction. The city's history also surfaces in the story of the captured Frenchman Lejeune, whom a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] taunts for having "came with twenty nations into Russia, burnt Moscow" (006-587) — the burning of 1812 fixed in [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] memory.

## Role in the Stories

Moscow functions as a structural counterweight to the immediate provincial setting. Where the narrator's hunting grounds are intimate and knowable, Moscow is the far metropolis that absorbs the region's labour and returns its fashions, machinery, and news. It is spoken of by characters of every class — serfs sent there to work, [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s ordering from its firms, an old proprietor remembering its banquets, a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] recalling its burning — so that the city becomes a shared point of reference binding the separate stories to a wider Russia. In [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s litany "Moscow of the golden domes" (009-496) is transfigured into something almost holy, the summit of the pilgrim's imagined map; elsewhere it is thoroughly worldly, a place of rent, trade, and craft. This double character — sacred capital and economic magnet — is typical of Turgenev's method of sketching a credible provincial world while gesturing toward the vast country that lies beyond the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman's route.

## Historical Context

Moscow, capital of Russia until Peter the Great moved the court to [[Petersburg]] in the early eighteenth century, remained the country's spiritual and commercial heart. [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s phrase "of the golden domes" (009-496) evokes the gilded cupolas of the Kremlin cathedrals. The reference to the city being burnt (006-587) recalls the fire of September 1812 during Napoleon's occupation — the episode that frames Lejeune's capture by [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s. "Butenop's" (013-149) was a real Moscow firm of agricultural-machinery makers, a detail that dates the estate improvements Turgenev satirises to the 1840s.
