# KASSYAN OF FAIR SPRINGS

[009-001]I was returning from hunting in a jolting little trap, and overcome by

[009-002]the stifling heat of a cloudy summer day (it is well known that the

[009-003]heat is often more insupportable on such days than in bright days,

[009-004]especially when there is no wind), I dozed and was shaken about,

[009-005]resigning myself with sullen fortitude to being persecuted by the fine

[009-006]white dust which was incessantly raised from the beaten road by the

[009-007]warped and creaking wheels, when suddenly my attention was aroused by

[009-008]the extraordinary uneasiness and agitated movements of my coachman, who

[009-009]had till that instant been more soundly dozing than I. He began tugging

[009-010]at the reins, moved uneasily on the box, and started shouting to the

[009-011]horses, staring all the while in one direction. I looked round. We were

[009-012]driving through a wide ploughed plain; low hills, also ploughed over,

[009-013]ran in gently sloping, swelling waves over it; the eye took in some

[009-014]five miles of deserted country; in the distance the round-scolloped

[009-015]tree-tops of some small birch-copses were the only objects to break the

[009-016]almost straight line of the horizon. Narrow paths ran over the fields,

[009-017]disappeared into the hollows, and wound round the hillocks. On one of

[009-018]these paths, which happened to run into our road five hundred paces

[009-019]ahead of us, I made out a kind of procession. At this my coachman was

[009-020]looking.

[009-021]It was a funeral. In front, in a little cart harnessed with one horse,

[009-022]and advancing at a walking pace, came the priest; beside him sat the

[009-023]deacon driving; behind the cart four peasants, bareheaded, carried the

[009-024]coffin, covered with a white cloth; two women followed the coffin. The

[009-025]shrill wailing voice of one of them suddenly reached my ears; I

[009-026]listened; she was intoning a dirge. Very dismal sounded this chanted,

[009-027]monotonous, hopelessly-sorrowful lament among the empty fields. The

[009-028]coachman whipped up the horses; he wanted to get in front of this

[009-029]procession. To meet a corpse on the road is a bad omen. And he did

[009-030]succeed in galloping ahead beyond this path before the funeral had had

[009-031]time to turn out of it into the high-road; but we had hardly got a

[009-032]hundred paces beyond this point, when suddenly our trap jolted

[009-033]violently, heeled on one side, and all but overturned. The coachman

[009-034]pulled up the galloping horses, and spat with a gesture of his hand.

[009-035]'What is it?' I asked.

[009-036]My coachman got down without speaking or hurrying himself.

[009-037]'But what is it?'

[009-038]'The axle is broken ... it caught fire,' he replied gloomily, and he

[009-039]suddenly arranged the collar on the off-side horse with such

[009-040]indignation that it was almost pushed over, but it stood its ground,

[009-041]snorted, shook itself, and tranquilly began to scratch its foreleg

[009-042]below the knee with its teeth.

[009-043]I got out and stood for some time on the road, a prey to a vague and

[009-044]unpleasant feeling of helplessness. The right wheel was almost

[009-045]completely bent in under the trap, and it seemed to turn its

[009-046]centre-piece upwards in dumb despair.

[009-047]'What are we to do now?' I said at last.

[009-048]'That's what's the cause of it!' said my coachman, pointing with his

[009-049]whip to the funeral procession, which had just turned into the highroad

[009-050]and was approaching us. 'I have always noticed that,' he went on; 'it's

[009-051]a true saying--"Meet a corpse"--yes, indeed.'

[009-052]And again he began worrying the off-side horse, who, seeing his

[009-053]ill-humour, resolved to remain perfectly quiet, and contented itself

[009-054]with discreetly switching its tail now and then. I walked up and down a

[009-055]little while, and then stopped again before the wheel.

[009-056]Meanwhile the funeral had come up to us. Quietly turning off the road

[009-057]on to the grass, the mournful procession moved slowly past us. My

[009-058]coachman and I took off our caps, saluted the priest, and exchanged

[009-059]glances with the bearers. They moved with difficulty under their

[009-060]burden, their broad chests standing out under the strain. Of the two

[009-061]women who followed the coffin, one was very old and pale; her set face,

[009-062]terribly distorted as it was by grief, still kept an expression of

[009-063]grave and severe dignity. She walked in silence, from time to time

[009-064]lifting her wasted hand to her thin drawn lips. The other, a young

[009-065]woman of five-and-twenty, had her eyes red and moist and her whole face

[009-066]swollen with weeping; as she passed us she ceased wailing, and hid her

[009-067]face in her sleeve.... But when the funeral had got round us and turned
