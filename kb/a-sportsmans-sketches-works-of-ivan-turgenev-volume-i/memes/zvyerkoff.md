## Overview

Alexandr Selitch Zvyerkoff is a [[petersburg|Petersburg]] gentleman of some rank who appears in the backstory of [[Arina Timofeyevna]] in "[[Yermolai-and-the-Millers-Wife]]." (002-267)

He is the former master of [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]], whom he had taken from her village to serve as his wife's lady's maid. When [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] fell in love with the footman [[petrushka|Petrushka]] and became pregnant, Zvyerkoff had her hair cut off, dressed in sackcloth, and banished to the country. He recounts this cruel act in a self-serving monologue on the ingratitude of the lower classes. (002-321)

## Appearance and Character

Zvyerkoff's physical appearance is memorably unappealing:

> His little mouse-like eyes peeped slyly out of a broad, almost square, face; he had a large, prominent nose, with distended nostrils; his close-cropped grey hair stood up like a brush above his scowling brow; his thin lips were for ever twitching and smiling mawkishly. (002-274)

> Mr. Zvyerkoff's favourite position was standing with his legs wide apart and his fat hands in his trouser pockets. (002-278)

Zvyerkoff presents himself as a man of judgment, fond of lecturing "young people" about their ignorance of Russia. (002-282) His wife is "fat, sentimental, lachrymose and spiteful," and their son "the very type of the young swell of to-day, pampered and stupid." (002-270)

## Role in the Story

### The Arina Anecdote

Zvyerkoff's defining function is the story he tells about [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] — which he recounts as an example of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] ingratitude, but which reads as a damning self-indictment.

Fifteen years earlier, Zvyerkoff and his wife took the bailiff's pretty young daughter to [[petersburg|Petersburg]] as a servant. (002-302) [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] served faithfully for ten years. (002-316) Then she begged to marry; he refused. Six months later she repeated the request; later still, she was pregnant by [[petrushka|Petrushka]], the footman. (002-337)

Zvyerkoff's response:

> I gave orders, of course, that her hair should be cut off, she should be dressed in sackcloth, and sent into the country. My wife was deprived of an excellent lady's maid; but there was no help for it: immorality cannot be tolerated in a household in any case. Better to cut off the infected member at once. (002-345)

[[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] was eventually freed when [[the-miller|Savely Alexyevitch]] purchased her freedom, and she married the Miller of [[byelev|Byelev]]. (002-367)

### Yermolaï's Connection

[[Yermolaï]] knew Zvyerkoff personally and recognized [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] immediately at the mill, prompting the narrator to draw out her story. (002-263)

## Significance

Zvyerkoff embodies the self-satisfied serf-owner who couches brutality in moral principle. His anecdote is a piece of dramatic irony — he tells it to illustrate the incorrigibility of serfs, but it reveals his own cruelty. The scene is one of the sharpest critiques of serfdom in the collection.
