# Folk Belief and Superstition

**Folk belief and superstition** form a distinct layer of peasant experience in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]. Alongside the official [[Religion and Faith|religion]] of the church runs an older, unofficial world of wood-spirits and water-spirits, omens, and restless dead — a world the peasants inhabit as naturally as they inhabit the [[Nature and Landscape|landscape]] itself.

## The Boys of Byezhin Prairie

The great set-piece of peasant folklore is [[Byezhin Prairie]], in which the narrator, lost at night, comes upon a group of peasant boys guarding horses by a fire and listens as they trade stories of the uncanny. Their talk maps a whole supernatural geography. When a strange cry sounds in the dark, they debate what walks the woods:

> (008-556) "...it is just like a wood-spirit shrieking." "The wood-spirit does not shriek; it is dumb," put in [[Ilyusha]]; "it only claps its hands and rattles."

The water is more dangerous still. The boy Pavlusha tells how, sent to the river, he heard his name called from beneath the surface in the voice of a boy who had drowned — and the others read the sign at once:

> (008-650) "...It was the water-spirit calling you, Pavel," said [[Fedya]]; "we were just talking of Vasya."

The boys cross themselves as they listen, half in fear and half in the pleasure of the telling. Turgenev neither mocks their beliefs nor endorses them; he records them as a living system of meaning.

## Omens and the Sacred Calendar

Peasant superstition is bound to the church calendar and to signs read in the natural world. The boys recall which days are dangerous, when the dead are abroad, and what may be glimpsed by those who keep vigil:

> (008-437) ...I judged he knew the village superstitions better than the others.... "But on All Hallows' day you can see the living too; those, that is, whose turn it is to die that year. You need only sit in the church porch, and keep looking at the road."

Such beliefs weave death, the seasons, and the sacred into a single fabric — the same fabric out of which the mystic [[Kassyan]] speaks in the following sketch.

## Social and Historical Context

For Turgenev's educated narrator, the boys' folklore is a window into a mind he can observe but not share. In presenting it without condescension, Turgenev honours the imaginative life of the [[Russian Peasantry|peasantry]] as fully as he honours their dignity elsewhere.

The superstition is not ignorance to be corrected but a culture to be understood — one more way the *Sketches* insist on the peasant's full humanity.
