# Khor

## Overview

Khor (also spelled Hor), whose full name is Khorkov, is one of the two central [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] figures in Turgenev's sketch "Khor and Kalinich," the opening story of *A Sportsman's Sketches*. A serf belonging to the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[polutikin|Mr. Polutikin]] in the [[kaluga-province|Kaluga Province]], Khor embodies the pragmatic, rational Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] — a man of keen intelligence, commercial instinct, and quiet self-possession who has built a prosperous life within the constraints of serfdom. His nickname "Khor" means "wild cat" in the local dialect, a moniker that captures his independent spirit and his decision to live apart from the rest of [[polutikin|Polutikin]]'s [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s on a bog in the forest (001-117). As the narrator observes, Khor's face recalled Socrates in its high, knobby forehead, small eyes, and snub nose — an extraordinary comparison that signals Turgenev's reverence for the wisdom and dignity of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] (001-173–001-174).

## Appearance & Character

Khor is an old man, bald and short but broad-shouldered and stout, with a curly beard that he strokes quietly as he converses (001-172). His movements and speech are deliberate — he "seemed to know his own value" and "spoke and moved slowly," punctuating his words with chuckles that emerged from between his long moustaches (001-177–001-179). His face, as noted, is remarkably Socratic in its features: a high, knobby forehead, small piercing eyes, and a snub nose, giving him an air of ancient wisdom.

Temperamentally, Khor is "a positive, practical man, with a head for management, a rationalist" (001-266–001-267). He has a keen grasp of actuality — he saves money, looks ahead, and keeps on good terms with his master and the local authorities (001-270–001-271). He speaks little, chuckles often, and thinks for himself, having arrived at "an ironical point of view of life" through his extensive experience of the world (001-285–001-287). Unlike his friend [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]], who is drawn to nature and believes in everything blindly, Khor is oriented toward men and society, and questions received wisdom with a skeptical turn of mind (001-286). When the narrator describes foreign lands, Khor asks methodical questions about government and administration — the practical concerns of a man who understands how power works — while [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] marvels at descriptions of mountains and waterfalls (001-336–001-339).

Despite his ironical detachment, Khor is capable of deep feeling. He is never weary of listening to [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] sing, dropping his head to one side and joining in with a lugubrious voice, particularly moved by the song "Ah, my fate, my fate!" (001-397–001-403). He is also tirelessly industrious: "there could not be a more active man; he was always busy over something — mending the cart, patching up the fence, looking after the harness" (001-404–001-406). He does not insist on cleanliness, however, remarking pragmatically that "a cottage ought to smell as if it were lived in" (001-407–001-408).

## Role in the Story

Khor's role in "Khor and [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]]" is to embody the rational, enterprising, and self-reliant side of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] character. His backstory is revealing: twenty-five years before the story's events, Khor's cottage burned down. Rather than rebuild among the other [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, he approached his late master, [[nikolai-kouzmitch|Nikolai Kouzmitch]], and negotiated the right to settle on a bog in the forest in exchange for a fixed annual rent of fifty roubles — with no labour obligations (001-109–001-115). This was a remarkable act of self-assertion within a system designed to deny [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s all agency. By the time of the story, Khor has grown rich, paying a hundred roubles in rent, and has reared a large family of sons — all obedient, united, and living together under his roof (001-118–001-119, 001-272–001-273). His homestead consists of several well-built pine-wood buildings enclosed by plank fences, a solitary and prosperous island in the forest clearing (001-060–001-061).

A central tension in Khor's characterization is his refusal to buy his freedom from [[polutikin|Polutikin]], despite clearly being able to afford it. When the narrator presses him on this, Khor demurs with characteristic indirection: "If Khor were thrown among free men, everyone without a beard would be a better man than Khor" (001-194–001-196). He explains that as a freed man he would have to become a merchant — which, he notes, is not undesirable, since "merchants have a fine life, and they have beards" (001-200–001-201). The irony is multilayered: Khor is already trading in butter and tar on the side (001-204), and his supposed modesty masks a shrewd calculation: freedom within the system — with a known master, a fixed rent, and no corvee labour — may be more materially comfortable than the precarious liberty of a freed [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] in 1850s Russia.

Khor's friendship with [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] is the story's emotional core. The two men are described as profoundly opposite types — the rationalist and the dreamer, the man of society and the man of nature — yet "Hor loved [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]], and took protecting care of him; [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] loved and respected Hor" (001-276–001-277). Their relationship illustrates Turgenev's belief that the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] contained within itself both hard-headed pragmatism and deep spiritual sensitivity. Khor cannot read — though his son [[fedya|Fedya]] can — and he notes with a mixture of fondness and exasperation that "that ne'er-do-weel" [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] can read, "and his bees never die in the winter" (001-354). In one of the story's most memorable scenes, Khor teases [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] for wearing bast shoes while Khor himself wears sturdy boots, lifting his leg to display "a boot which looked as if it had been cut out of a mammoth's hide" — yet this teasing is clearly an expression of protective concern, since Khor is outraged that [[polutikin|Polutikin]] does not properly provide for [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] (001-387–001-390).

Through Khor, Turgenev makes the case for the innate intelligence, dignity, and complexity of the Russian serf. The narrator concludes from his conversations with Khor that "Peter the Great was pre-eminently a Russian — Russian, above all, in his reforms," because the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] shares with the reforming Tsar a willingness to take what is good and sensible from abroad while remaining deeply rooted in native soil (001-347–001-348). It is a startlingly bold claim: that a serf living on a forest bog embodies the same national genius as the founder of modern Russia.
