# Tchaplino

## Overview

Tchaplino is a location mentioned in Turgenev's sketch "[[Yermolai and the Miller's Wife]]." It appears in a brief but characteristic exchange that illuminates the restless temperament of [[Yermolai]], the narrator's eccentric hunting companion. Tchaplino is not described directly; it exists in the narrative solely as the destination of [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s nocturnal wanderings.

## Description

Tchaplino enters the narrative through a dialogue that captures [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s peculiar habits. The narrator observes that [[yermolai|Yermolai]] "liked a little chat with a good companion, especially over a glass, but he would not stop long; he would get up and go" (002-104). When asked where he is heading in the dark, [[yermolai|Yermolai]] answers: "'To Tchaplino'" (002-106). The narrator presses him: "'But what's taking you to Tchaplino, ten miles away?'" (002-107). [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s reply is matter-of-fact: "'I am going to stay the night at Sophron's there'" (002-108). When offered a bed where he is, he refuses with characteristic brevity: "'No, I can't'" (002-109). The narrator wryly notes that "the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] Sophron very likely did not let him into his place, and even, I am afraid, gave him a blow to teach him 'not to disturb honest folks'" (002-110).

## Role in the Stories

Tchaplino serves as a narrative marker of [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s irreducible independence. The ten-mile distance, undertaken in darkness "through woods and water-courses" (002-110), is absurd from any practical standpoint — yet it is entirely consistent with [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s character. He is a man who cannot stay put, who prefers an uncertain welcome in a distant hut to a comfortable bed at hand. Tchaplino, in this sense, is less a geographical location than a symbol of the huntsman's compulsion to keep moving.
