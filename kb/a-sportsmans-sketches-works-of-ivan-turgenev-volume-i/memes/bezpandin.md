# Bezpandin

## Overview

Bezpandin is a neighbouring [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who appears in [[Ovsyanikov]]'s recollections in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." He is a vivid example of the aggressive land-grabbing [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] of the pre-Reform era: he ploughs over four acres of the [[shutolomovsky|Shutolomovsky]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' land, insists "the land's mine" (006-470), and threatens to break every bone in [[Mitya]]'s body when [[mitya|Mitya]] writes a petition on the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' behalf. Bezpandin never appears onstage; he is known solely through [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s indignant testimony, which uses his case to illustrate the lawless arrogance of provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s toward serfs whose masters are absent.

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Bezpandin is given in the text. His character emerges entirely through his actions: he seizes land that, by [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s account, belongs beyond dispute to the [[shutolomovsky|Shutolomovsky]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, who "have been bound to it for ages and ages" (006-473). When challenged — not by force but by a simple written petition — Bezpandin's response is instant and violent, threatening to "break every bone in that [[mitya|Mitya]]'s body, and knock his head off his shoulders" (006-475–006-476). The threat reveals a man accustomed to ruling by intimidation, confident that no higher authority will restrain him. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s dry retort — "We shall see how he will knock it off; it's still on, so far" (006-477) — underscores the impunity that Bezpandin, like many [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s of his type, counts on.

## Role in the Story

Bezpandin is introduced by [[Ovsyanikov]] in the course of a debate with his nephew [[Mitya]] about justice and self-interest. [[mitya|Mitya]] has accused [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] of only defending [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s when it costs him nothing; [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] retorts by citing the Bezpandin case, in which he did risk reprisal:

> "A neighbouring landowner, Bezpandin, has ploughed over four acres of the Shutolomovsky peasants' land. 'The land's mine,' he says. The Shutolomovsky people are on the rent-system; their landowner has gone abroad — who is to stand up for them?" (006-468–006-472)

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, left without a resident master, come to [[mitya|Mitya]] with their plea, and [[mitya|Mitya]] writes them a petition. Bezpandin's threat against [[mitya|Mitya]] — delivered, as [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] recounts it, with predictable bluster — serves as the pivot of the argument: [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s willingness to face such threats proves, to his own satisfaction, that his interventions are principled rather than self-serving.

In the larger thematic architecture of the sketch, Bezpandin represents one pole of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] spectrum that [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] anatomises: the lawless predator, contrasted with the cultivated liberal hypocrite ([[Alexandr Vladimirovitch Korolyov]]) and the responsible old-regime [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] ([[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] himself). His role is small but emblematic.
