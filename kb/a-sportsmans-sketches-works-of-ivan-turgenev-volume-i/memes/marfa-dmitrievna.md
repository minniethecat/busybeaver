# Marfa Dmitrievna

## Overview

Marfa Dmitrievna is mentioned in a single anecdote told in [[Ovsyanikov]]'s house during "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." She is the target of a casual insult by an unnamed [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], who, when she objects that he has "aspersed her reputation," retorts that he would not let his chestnut mare have her reputation (006-275–006-277). The exchange, recounted by [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] as an instance of [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] boorishness, is brief but revealing: it exposes the routine contempt with which provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s treated the women of their own class when they could do so with impunity.

## Appearance and Character

Turgenev provides no physical description of Marfa Dmitrievna, nor any biographical detail beyond her name and patronymic. She exists in the text only as the recipient of an insult. Yet her response — "How dare you asperse my reputation?" (006-276) — suggests a woman who possesses a sense of honour and the courage to demand its recognition. The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s reply, equating her reputation to that of his mare, is designed to strip her of that courage in public. The anecdote is one of several in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s monologue that illustrate the casual cruelty of the pre-Reform [[landowning-gentry|gentry]], particularly toward those who lack the power to retaliate.

## Role in the Story

Marfa Dmitrievna appears in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" within a digression: [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], recounting the misdeeds of various [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s he has known, describes an unnamed gentleman who "mortally offended Marfa Dmitrievna" (006-275). The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] has been haranguing his guests and banging his fist on the table when Marfa Dmitrievna protests his treatment of her. His retort — "I shouldn't like my chestnut mare to have your reputation" (006-277) — provokes the guests to pour him Madeira and quiet him down, after which the gathering moves on to other disturbances and fresh grievances.

The anecdote functions as a minor exhibit in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s gallery of [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] abuses. It is positioned between the story of [[Bezpandin]]'s land-grabbing and the account of [[Alexandr Vladimirovitch Korolyov]]'s liberal hypocrisy, forming part of a cumulative portrait of a class that treats people — [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and gentlewomen alike — as property to be valued or devalued at will. Marfa Dmitrievna's significance lies not in her individuality but in her typicality: she is one of countless women whose dignity was expendable in the drawing rooms of provincial Russia.
