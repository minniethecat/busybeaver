# Orel Province

## Overview

Orel Province (also spelled Oryol) is a region in western Russia that serves as the primary setting for the opening of Ivan Turgenev's *A Sportsman's Sketches*. The province lies to the east of Kaluga, and the Bolhovsky (Bolkhov) district forms part of it. The narrator uses it as the benchmark for rural poverty, describing a landscape shaped by serfdom — treeless, exhausted, and grim (001-003).

## Description

Turgenev paints a bleak picture of the Orel [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] and countryside in the opening paragraphs of Chapter I. The Orel [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] is "not tall, is bent in figure, sullen and suspicious in his looks" (001-004).

He lives in "wretched little hovels of aspen-wood" (001-006), labors as a serf, does not engage in trade, is "miserably fed, and wears slippers of bast" (001-007).

The villages themselves are equally dismal. They are "usually situated in the midst of ploughed fields, near a water-course which has been converted into a filthy pool" (001-012). Aside from a few willows and gaunt birches, "you do not see a tree for a mile round" (001-015); the huts huddle together under "roofs covered with rotting thatch" (001-017).

The narrator adds that in the Orel province "the last of the woods and copses will have disappeared five years hence, and there is no trace of moorland left" (001-023), reinforcing that deforestation and agricultural exhaustion were far advanced by the 1850s.

## Role in the Stories

Orel Province is the narrator's point of departure. He crosses from the Bolhovsky district into the Zhizdrinsky district of Kaluga at the start of "[[khor|Khor]] and Kalinich" (001-001).

The stark contrast between the two provinces frames the entire sketch: Orel represents the degraded condition of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] under serfdom, while Kaluga shows what even a marginally freer, forested economy can produce.

This geographic and economic opposition — east vs. west, field vs. forest, serf vs. rent-paying [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] — sets the thematic foundation for the entire collection.
