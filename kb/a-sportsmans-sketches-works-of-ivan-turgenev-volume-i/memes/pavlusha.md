# Pavlusha

## Overview

Pavlusha ([[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]]) is the most vividly drawn of the five [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys whom the narrator encounters around the campfire in Turgenev's "[[Byezhin Prairie]]." He is about twelve years old, physically unprepossessing, but possessed of a natural courage and practical competence that set him apart from his companions. While the other boys trade supernatural tales and shiver at every unexplained sound, Pavlusha alone questions the stories, tends the cooking pot, and ultimately charges alone into the darkness to face what might be a wolf — earning the narrator's silent admiration. Turgenev closes the sketch with one of the most devastating sentences in all of Russian literature: a brief notation that Pavlusha died that same year, killed by a fall from his horse.

## Appearance

Turgenev's physical description of Pavlusha is remarkable for its honesty — he does not prettify the boy, yet the plainness of the portrait only deepens the reader's sympathy:

> The second boy, Pavlusha, had tangled black hair, grey eyes, broad cheek-bones, a pale face pitted with small-pox, a large but well-cut mouth; his head altogether was large—'a beer-barrel head,' as they say—and his figure was square and clumsy. He was not a good-looking boy—there's no denying it!—and yet I liked him; he looked very sensible and straightforward, and there was a vigorous ring in his voice. He had nothing to boast of in his attire; it consisted simply of a homespun shirt and patched trousers. (008-036–008-045)

The frank admission — "He was not a good-looking boy—there's no denying it!" — followed immediately by "and yet I liked him" — is characteristic of Turgenev's narrative voice: unsentimental, honest, and instinct with a humane warmth that needs no idealization to justify itself. The patched trousers mark Pavlusha's poverty, distinguishing him from [[Fedya]], the well-to-do boy whose boots "were certainly his own—not his father's" (008-031–008-032).

## Courage

Pavlusha's defining quality is courage, demonstrated in two key episodes. The first is quiet and domestic: while the other boys lie about the fire, "Pavlusha was looking after them, and on his knees he was trying them by poking a splinter of wood into the boiling water" (008-050–008-052). This small act of practical competence — tending the potatoes while others talk — establishes his role as the group's stabilizer.

The second episode is dramatic. When the dogs suddenly rush barking into the darkness, and all the boys are terrified, Pavlusha alone acts:

> Pavlusha ran shouting after the dogs. Their barking quickly grew fainter in the distance.... There was the noise of the uneasy tramp of the frightened drove of horses. Pavlusha shouted aloud: 'Hey Grey! Beetle!' ... In a few minutes the barking ceased; Pavel's voice sounded still in the distance. (008-138–008-145)

He returns having driven off whatever alarmed the dogs — likely a wolf — and his calmness afterward is as impressive as his bravery during the crisis:

> 'Nothing,' answered Pavel, waving his hand to his horse; 'I suppose the dogs scented something. I thought it was a wolf,' he added, calmly drawing deep breaths into his chest. (008-148–008-151)

The narrator's silent response registers the power of the moment:

> I could not help admiring Pavel. He was very fine at that moment. His ugly face, animated by his swift ride, glowed with hardihood and determination. Without even a switch in his hand, he had, without the slightest hesitation, rushed out into the night alone to face a wolf.... 'What a splendid fellow!' I thought, looking at him. (008-152–008-158)

## The Skeptic

Pavlusha's courage has an intellectual dimension. When the other boys react to the spooky tales with exclamations of fear, Pavlusha responds with skeptical curiosity:

> 'I say!' murmured Pavel, 'what did he cough for?'
> 'I don't know; perhaps it was the damp.' (008-098–008-100)

His question — "what did he cough for?" — refuses the supernatural explanation that the other boys accept without question, seeking instead a natural cause. When the boys are terrified by the story of the drowned man's ghost, Pavlusha again demystifies:

> 'Ah, you craven crows!' cried Pavel, 'what are you frightened of? Look, the potatoes are done.' (008-146–008-148)

The juxtaposition is characteristic: Pavlusha punctures the supernatural atmosphere by drawing attention back to the material world — the potatoes are done; eat. His skepticism is not intellectual arrogance but the instinctive realism of a boy who lives close to the physical facts of existence.

## The Omen and Death

In the sketch's most unsettling moment, Pavlusha goes to fetch water from the river and hears his own name called from beneath the surface — the voice of [[vasya|Vasya]], a boy who drowned in that very spot:

> 'I heard Vasya's voice.'
> They all seemed to shudder.
> 'What do you mean? what do you mean?' stammered Kostya.
> 'I don't know. Only I went to stoop down to the water; suddenly I hear my name called in Vasya's voice, as though it came from below water: "Pavlusha, Pavlusha, come here." I came away. But I fetched the water, though.' (008-311–008-317)

The other boys interpret this as a fatal omen — "It was the water-spirit calling you, [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]]" (008-319) — and Pavlusha, for all his skepticism, seems momentarily shaken. But he recovers quickly: "'Well, never mind, don't bother about it,' [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] declared stoutly" (008-322).

Then, with devastating brevity, Turgenev closes the sketch:

> Sad to say, I must add that in that year Pavel met his end. He was not drowned; he was killed by a fall from his horse. Pity! he was a splendid fellow! (008-363–008-366)

The death is entirely mundane — not a supernatural fulfillment but a random accident. The water-spirit did not kill Pavlusha; a horse did. The two-sentence epitaph, with its simple "Pity!" and the word "splendid," is among the most powerful passages Turgenev ever wrote: an acknowledgment that courage, competence, and vitality offer no immunity against the arbitrary cruelty of the world.
