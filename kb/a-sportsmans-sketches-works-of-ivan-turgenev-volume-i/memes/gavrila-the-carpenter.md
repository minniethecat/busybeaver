## Overview

Gavrila the Carpenter is a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] from the "big village" whose encounter with a russalka (water-nymph) is told by the boy [[kostya|Kostya]] around the campfire in "[[Byezhin-Prairie]]." (008-287) Since the encounter, Gavrila has been perpetually sorrowful and never speaks — a figure who embodies the folk belief that the supernatural world can mark a person for life. (008-289)

## Appearance and Character

Gavrila does not appear directly in the story; his character is conveyed entirely through [[kostya|Kostya]]'s tale. He is known to all the boys: [[fedya|Fedya]] says "Yes, we know him," confirming he is a familiar village figure. (008-288) [[kostya|Kostya]] introduces the story by asking, "And do you know why he is so sorrowful always, never speaks?" — establishing Gavrila's defining trait before the supernatural explanation is offered. (008-289)

## Role in the Story

### The Russalka Encounter

[[kostya|Kostya]] relates the story as told by his father. Gavrila went nutting in the forest, lost his way, and sat down under a tree to wait for morning. (008-291) As he began to fall asleep:

> Suddenly he heard some one call him. He looked up; there was no one. He fell asleep again; again he was called. He looked and looked again; and in front of him there sat a russalka on a branch, swinging herself and calling him to her, and simply dying with laughing. (008-297)

The russalka was "as bright and as white sitting on the branch as some dace or a roach, or like some little carp so white and silvery." (008-303) She beckoned to him, and Gavrila was on the point of yielding — but then crossed himself:

> The Lord put it into his heart, doubtless — he crossed himself like this.… And it was so hard for him to make that cross, brothers; he said, "My hand was simply like a stone; it would not move." (008-307)

### The Curse

When Gavrila made the sign of the cross, the russalka stopped laughing and began to weep. She wiped her eyes with hair "green as any hemp" and spoke:

> "If you had not crossed yourself, man," she says, "you should have lived with me in gladness of heart to the end of your days; and I weep, I am grieved at heart because you crossed yourself; but I will not grieve alone; you too shall grieve at heart to the end of your days." (008-316)

She vanished, and Gavrila immediately saw the way out of the forest. But ever since, he has been sorrowful and silent. (008-321)

### The Boys' Reactions

The story provokes debate among the boys. [[fedya|Fedya]] wonders how "an evil thing of the woods" can ruin a Christian soul who did not listen to her. (008-323) [[kostya|Kostya]] reports that Gavrila said her voice "was as shrill and plaintive as a toad's." (008-325) [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]] insists the russalka "wanted to tickle him to death — that's what they do, those russalkas." (008-331)

## Significance

The tale of Gavrila the Carpenter is one of the richest folk narratives in "[[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]." It belongs to the chapter's larger structure of supernatural stories told by [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys around a night fire. Gavrila's fate — a living man marked by a single encounter with the other world — embodies the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] belief that the boundary between the natural and supernatural is fragile, and that crossing it, even briefly, carries permanent consequence. The story also serves as an orthodox Christian parable: the sign of the cross saves Gavrila's soul but costs him his earthly happiness.
