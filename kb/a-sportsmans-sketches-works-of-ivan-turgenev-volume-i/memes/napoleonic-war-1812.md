# The Napoleonic War of 1812

Napoleon's invasion of Russia is never the subject of a sketch, yet it lingers in the provinces as a remembered event that ordinary people can still name and judge. In [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] the war returns as a long comic digression, the story of how a French drummer became a Russian gentleman.

The tale belongs to [[Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune]], the narrator's neighbour, and it lets Turgenev fold the great national reckoning of 1812 into the same domestic, peasant-and-gentry world that fills the rest of the book — a place where a captured enemy is first a thing to be drowned and then a teacher of music for the master's daughters.

## The Drummer of the Grande Armée

Lejeune reaches his "respectable position of a Russian nobleman" by a route the narrator calls anything but ordinary:

> (006-560) ...had arrived at the respectable position of a Russian nobleman in a not quite ordinary way.

Born at Orléans, he had marched east in Napoleon's ranks:

> (006-562) ...had gone with Napoleon, on the invasion of Russia, in the capacity of a drummer.

The advance went smoothly as far as Moscow, but the retreat undid him, and near Smolensk he fell captive to the peasants:

> (006-565) ...poor Monsieur Lejeune, half-frozen and without his drum, fell into the hands of some peasants of Smolensk.

## "You Came with Twenty Nations into Russia"

For the Smolensk peasants the half-frozen Frenchman is simply the enemy of 1812, to be led to an ice-hole and made to "swim under the ice." When a passing landowner asks what they are about, the answer is matter-of-fact:

> (006-583) 'We are drowning a Frenchman, your honour.'

The landowner's reproach turns the private cruelty into the whole memory of the war — the twenty nations, the burning of Moscow, the sacrilege at the Kremlin, all charged to this one shivering drummer:

> (006-586) ...you came with twenty nations into Russia, burnt Moscow, tore down, you damned heathen! the cross from Ivan the Great, and now--mossoo, mossoo, indeed! now you turn tail! You are paying the penalty of your sins!

In the mouth of a country squire, 1812 is not a campaign of armies but a debt owed by every Frenchman.

## Saved by Music

The reckoning turns on a single, absurd question. Half-relenting, the landowner asks:

> (006-592) 'Eh? you mossoo, do you know anything of music?'

Lejeune, who has never touched a keyboard, is set before a wretched little piano to prove himself:

> (006-619) Lejeune, with a sinking heart, sat down on the music-stool; he had never touched a piano in his life.

He saves his life by doing the only thing he knows — drumming:

> (006-622) In desperation, the unhappy man beat on the keys as though on a drum, and played at hazard.

## From Enemy to Orel Gentleman

The improvisation is judged a success, and within a fortnight the prisoner has begun his rise. He passes to a richer household, wins friendship, and is absorbed wholly into the order he had come to invade:

> (006-632) ...was married to a ward of his, went into a government office, rose to the nobility, married his daughter to Lobizanyev, a landowner of Orel...

By the time the narrator meets him he is Frantz Ivanitch, an Orel landowner on friendly terms with [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] — the war's defeated enemy quietly seated among his former enemies.

## Social and Historical Context

For Turgenev's generation the war of 1812 — the "Patriotic War" — was the founding national memory, the moment the peasant and the gentleman were said to have defended Russia together. The *Sketches* honour that memory obliquely: not with battles but with the fate of one captured drummer, remembered and retold thirty years on by the very provincials who once held his life in their hands. The episode carries the book's recurring theme of [[Russia and the West]] — the foreigner drawn into Russia and made her own — and shows how deeply the events of the invasion had settled into the ordinary life of [[Landowning Gentry|the gentry]] and the [[Russian Peasantry|peasants]] alike.
