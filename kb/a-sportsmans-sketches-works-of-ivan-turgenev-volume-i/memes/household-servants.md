# Household Servants and Retainers

Beside the field peasants of the [[Russian Peasantry]] stands a distinct order of serf: the *dvorovye*, or "yard people" — the coachmen, huntsmen, footmen, cooks and stewards who serve the gentry in person. They are bound by the same [[Serfdom|serfdom]], yet their lives are shaped less by the land than by the whims of the household they attend.

It is an ambiguous middle stratum. Closer to the master than the ploughman, the house-serf is often more comfortable and more corrupted, and just as often more degraded — a man whose whole worth is the office he happens to fill, and who can be lifted or cast down at a word.

## The Huntsman Yermolaï

[[Yermolaï]], the narrator's constant hunting companion, is such a retainer. He is another man's property, kept for a single narrow service:

> (002-077) Yermolaï belonged to one of my neighbours, a landowner of the old style.

His master, indifferent to game, asks only a fixed tribute of birds and otherwise lets him drift:

> (002-086) Yermolaï was under orders to provide his master's kitchen with two brace of grouse and partridges once a month. But he might live where and how he pleased.

Freedom of movement without freedom itself: he goes where he likes only because he has been "given up as a man of no use for work of any kind" (002-088).

## Styopushka, Below the Genus "Man"

In [[raspberry-spring|Raspberry Spring]] the house-serf appears at his most abject. [[Styopushka]] is scarcely counted among the servants at all:

> (003-066) ...special order of house-serfs, and hardly under the genus 'man' at all.

Where every house-serf is at least entitled to some "ration," Styopushka has nothing, sleeping in the hayloft and living on what he can find:

> (003-069) Styopushka had absolutely no means of subsistence.

He marks the lowest reach of the domestic order — a retainer belonging to a household that gives him no place in it.

## Sutchok, Shuffled Through a Dozen Posts

The comedy and the cruelty of the servant's dependence come together in [[Sutchok]], the manor "fisherman" of [[lgov|Lgov]], whose life has been a series of arbitrary reassignments. He recites the offices he has held:

> (007-183) At first, to be sure, I was cook, and then I was coffee-bearer.

Cook, coffee-bearer, cook again, coachman — and finally, on a mistress's whim, a fisherman who can barely manage a boat:

> (007-159) You're not fit for a coachman, but be my fisherman, and shave your...

A house-serf is whatever his owner decides to call him; his trade, like his name, is not his own.

## Vasya at the Sideboard

The retainer's exposure to casual violence is drawn most chillingly in [[two-country-gentlemen|Two Country Gentlemen]]. As [[Mardary Apollonitch]] sips his tea, the sound of blows drifts in from the stable, and he keeps good-humoured time with them:

> (013-278) 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk! Tchuki-tchuk!'

The victim is his own sideboard-waiter, punished by his order:

> (013-280) Oh, by my order, they're punishing a scamp of a fellow.... Do you happen to remember Vasya, who waits at the sideboard?

Most terrible is the servant's own assent. Met in the street soon after, [[Vasya]] cracks nuts and takes his master's part:

> (013-299) Oh, I deserved it, father; I deserved it. They don't punish for trifles among us.

## Social and Historical Context

The *dvorovye* were the serfs detached from agriculture to run the gentry household — and so the most personally dependent of all. Turgenev returns to them again and again: [[Baush]] the grandfather's whipper-in, [[Petrushka]] the footman, the coachman [[Erofay]] who drives the narrator to [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan]], [[Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)|old Tuman]] recalling the masters he has outlived. Their nearness to power buys them neither safety nor dignity; it only binds them the more tightly to another's will, and shows, in miniature, how [[Serfdom|serfdom]] reached into the smallest offices of daily life. The bodily side of that power is the theme of [[Corporal Punishment and Cruelty]].
