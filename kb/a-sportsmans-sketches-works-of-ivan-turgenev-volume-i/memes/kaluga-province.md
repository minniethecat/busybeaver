# Kaluga Province

## Overview

Kaluga Province is a region in western Russia, immediately west of [[orel-province|Orel Province]], whose Zhizdrinsky district is the main hunting ground in the opening sketch of *A Sportsman's Sketches*.

Turgenev presents Kaluga as the favorable contrast to Orel in every respect — its [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s are independent traders, its villages are well-built and surrounded by forest, and its moors and woods make it excellent country for the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman (001-022).

## Description

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] of Kaluga is the inverse of his Orel counterpart. He is "tall, bold, and cheerful in his looks, neat and clean of countenance" (001-009). He lives in "roomy cottages of pine-wood" (001-008), carries on a trade in butter and tar, and "on holidays he wears boots" (001-011).

Critically, he is described as a rent-paying (*obrok*) [[russian-peasantry|peasant]], not a serf laborer (*barshchina*), which explains much of the economic divergence.

The Kaluga villages reflect this prosperity: they are "generally surrounded by forest" (001-018), the huts "stand more freely, are more upright, and have boarded roofs" (001-019); "the gates fasten closely, the hedge is not broken down" (001-020).

For the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman, Kaluga is paradise: "the moors extend over tens, the forest over hundreds of miles, and a splendid bird, the grouse, is still extant there" (001-025). The Zhizdrinsky district is the specific hunting destination where the narrator meets [[polutikin|Polutikin]] and begins the story (001-030).

## Role in the Stories

Kaluga Province is the literal and symbolic gateway into the narrative world of *A Sportsman's Sketches*. The narrator's crossing from the Bolhovsky district of Orel into the Zhizdrinsky district of Kaluga triggers the famous comparative passage that opens "[[khor|Khor]] and Kalinich" (001-001).

It is in Kaluga's fields that the narrator meets the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[polutikin|Polutikin]], who leads him to the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s [[khor|Khor]] and Kalinich — the two central figures of the first sketch.

Kaluga thus serves as the geographic foundation for Turgenev's exploration of Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] character: it provides the environment of relative freedom and natural abundance against which the human types of the collection first come into focus.
