## Overview

Anpadist is a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] from [[ryabovo|Ryabovo]], described as "a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] I knew well" (010-444), who accompanies the narrator on a shooting expedition at the end of "[[The Agent]]." Through his blunt and vivid dialogue, the full truth about the bailiff [[Sofron]] emerges — a truth that [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]], [[sofron|Sofron]]'s owner, either cannot see or chooses to ignore.

## Appearance and Character

Anpadist is a plain-speaking [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] whose direct language cuts through the pretense surrounding [[sofron|Sofron]]'s management of [[shipilovka|Shipilovka]]. When asked what sort of man [[sofron|Sofron]] is, he replies without hesitation: "He's not a man; he's a dog; you couldn't find another brute like him between here and [[kursk|Kursk]]" (010-450). His assessment is grounded in specific knowledge of [[sofron|Sofron]]'s operations — the land he has accumulated, the trades he runs, and the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s he has ruined. He is not merely venting anger but providing an informed and detailed indictment.

## Role in the Story

Anpadist's role is to supply the unvarnished [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] perspective that completes the narrator's investigation of [[shipilovka|Shipilovka]]. After the narrator has witnessed [[sofron|Sofron]]'s smoothness and the misery of the suppliants at [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]]'s house, Anpadist fills in the economic picture. He reveals that [[sofron|Sofron]] is the true master: "[[shipilovka|Shipilovka]]'s hardly reckoned as Mr. [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]]'s at all; he's not the master there; [[sofron|Sofron]]'s the master" (010-453). He itemises [[sofron|Sofron]]'s holdings — over "three hundred and seventy-five acres" of rented land, plus trade in "horses and stock, and pitch, and butter, and hemp" (010-460).

When the narrator tells Anpadist about the old man [[Antip]], Anpadist's response is grimly fatalistic: "he will eat him up now; he'll simply eat the man up. The bailiff will beat him now" (010-474). He explains that [[sofron|Sofron]] has already sent [[antip|Antip]]'s sons "for recruits out of turn" (010-483), and notes [[sofron|Sofron]]'s method: he only preys on the vulnerable — "the old men that are a bit richer, or've more children, he doesn't touch" (010-481). When the narrator asks why the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s don't complain, Anpadist's reply is bitter: "I dare say, the master'd be pleased! There's no arrears; so what does he care?" (010-469).

> 'He's a brute, not a man; a dog, I tell you; a cur, a regular cur; that's what he is!' (010-466)
