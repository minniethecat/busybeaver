# Shabolovka

## Overview

Shabolovka is a village near [[kaluga-gate|the Kaluga Gate]], mentioned in Turgenev's "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" as the residence of [[Count Alexey Grigoryevitch Orlov-Tchesmensky]], one of the most celebrated grandees of the Catherinian age. [[Ovsyanikov]], recalling a trip to [[moscow|Moscow]] in his youth, tells the narrator that "the count was pleased to live in Shabolovka, near [[kaluga-gate|the Kaluga Gate]]" (006-170), and describes the impression Orlov-Tchesmensky made on all who saw him. The village thus serves as the setting for [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s vivid portrait of an aristocratic world now vanished.

## Description

[[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s recollection of Shabolovka is inseparable from his description of its most illustrious resident. [[alexey-grigoryevitch-orlov-tchesmensky|Count Orlov-Tchesmensky]], hero of the Battle of Chesma and one of the towering figures of Catherine the Great's reign, kept open house at Shabolovka "for the wonder and admiration of all" (006-170). [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] — whose uncle served as a steward in the count's household — saw him there and was awed: "Such stateliness, such gracious condescension you can't imagine! and it's impossible to describe it. His figure alone was worth something, and his strength, and the look in his eyes!" (006-170). The count was "devoted to every kind of [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]," drove himself in races and "out-stripped every one," and yet "allowed every man access to him in person" (006-170) — a combination of magnificence and accessibility that left a lasting mark on the young [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]].

The [[kaluga-gate|Kaluga Gate]] landmark places Shabolovka on the outskirts of [[moscow|Moscow]], near one of the city's historic entrance points. The name "Shabolovka" itself — shared with a well-known [[moscow|Moscow]] street — suggests a settlement along the old Kaluga road, at the boundary between the city and the countryside.

## Role in the Stories

Shabolovka appears in the *Sketches* as a site of memory — glimpsed once in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s youth and preserved in his storytelling decades later. Its significance is entirely bound up with the figure of Orlov-Tchesmensky, whom [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] presents as a counter-example to the petty provincial landlords that dominate the rest of the chapter. Where the narrator's grandfather seized land, and where [[Komov]] drank and tyrannized, Orlov-Tchesmensky at Shabolovka represented a bygone ideal of aristocratic grandeur tempered by approachability. The village thus functions as a nostalgic touchstone in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s implicit critique of contemporary [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] decline.
