## Overview

Mihal Mihalitch is the full given name and patronymic of [[Radilov]], the enigmatic [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who is the subject of "[[My Neighbour Radilov]]." The name is revealed in a single, psychologically revealing moment: [[radilov|Radilov]] recalls lying gravely ill with typhus in a Turkish military hospital, overhearing a doctor pronounce him certain to die, and thinking to himself: "So you are in a bad way, Mihal Mihalitch..." (005-241). This solitary self-address — the only instance in the *Sketches* where a character's full name emerges through interior monologue — offers a rare glimpse into [[radilov|Radilov]]'s inner life.

## Appearance and Character

No independent physical description is attached to the name Mihal Mihalitch, as it is [[radilov|Radilov]] himself who speaks it in remembered self-address. The moment of naming occurs within a narrative of extreme physical debility: [[radilov|Radilov]] recalls being "half dead" with typhus fever (005-231), lying in an overcrowded wartime hospital where the doctor, finding no space for new patients, asks his attendant whether [[radilov|Radilov]] is still alive. The doctor's professional exasperation — "Well, what an absurd constitution; the man's dying; he's certain to die, and he keeps hanging on, lingering, taking up space for nothing, and keeping out others" (005-238) — becomes, in [[radilov|Radilov]]'s memory, the spur for a moment of existential self-recognition.

The act of addressing himself by his full name and patronymic suggests a man capable of ironic detachment even at the threshold of death. The ellipsis after "Mihal Mihalitch..." (005-241) leaves unspoken what he thought would follow — perhaps resignation, perhaps defiance. That he survived, and "am alive till now, as you may see for yourself" (005-242), gives the remembered moment a quality of quiet triumph.

## Role in the Story

The name Mihal Mihalitch surfaces during [[radilov|Radilov]]'s philosophical conversation with the narrator about whether every position offers an escape. To prove the point, [[radilov|Radilov]] tells the story of his illness in Turkey. The anecdote serves as illustration of his stated belief that "there is no position so terrible that there is no escape from it" (005-229). The narrator had just agreed with this sentiment, and [[radilov|Radilov]] offers the hospital story as personal evidence.

The self-address "So you are in a bad way, Mihal Mihalitch..." carries narrative weight beyond its brevity. In a story built around [[radilov|Radilov]]'s mysterious grief for his dead wife and his eventual elopement with his sister-in-law [[Olga]], this remembered moment of staring into the abyss casts retrospective light on his later decisions. The man who once calmly contemplated his own death — and survived — is the same man who, years later, will abruptly abandon his elderly mother and his estate to flee with a forbidden love. The name Mihal Mihalitch, spoken only to himself, marks the private core of a character who otherwise reveals little.

> 'Well, I thought to myself, "So you are in a bad way, Mihal Mihalitch...." And, after all, I got well, and am alive till now, as you may see for yourself.' (005-241)
