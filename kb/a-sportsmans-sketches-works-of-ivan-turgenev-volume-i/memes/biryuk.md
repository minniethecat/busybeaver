# BIRYUK

[012-001]I was coming back from hunting one evening alone in a racing droshky. I

[012-002]was six miles from home; my good trotting mare galloped bravely along

[012-003]the dusty road, pricking up her ears with an occasional snort; my weary

[012-004]dog stuck close to the hind-wheels, as though he were fastened there. A

[012-005]tempest was coming on. In front, a huge, purplish storm-cloud slowly

[012-006]rose from behind the forest; long grey rain-clouds flew over my head

[012-007]and to meet me; the willows stirred and whispered restlessly. The

[012-008]suffocating heat changed suddenly to a damp chilliness; the darkness

[012-009]rapidly thickened. I gave the horse a lash with the reins, descended a

[012-010]steep slope, pushed across a dry water-course overgrown with brushwood,

[012-011]mounted the hill, and drove into the forest. The road ran before me,

[012-012]bending between thick hazel bushes, now enveloped in darkness; I

[012-013]advanced with difficulty. The droshky jumped up and down over the hard

[012-014]roots of the ancient oaks and limes, which were continually intersected

[012-015]by deep ruts--the tracks of cart wheels; my horse began to stumble. A

[012-016]violent wind suddenly began to roar overhead; the trees blustered; big

[012-017]drops of rain fell with slow tap and splash on the leaves; there came a

[012-018]flash of lightning and a clap of thunder. The rain fell in torrents. I

[012-019]went on a step or so, and soon was forced to stop; my horse foundered;

[012-020]I could not see an inch before me. I managed to take refuge somehow in

[012-021]a spreading bush. Crouching down and covering my face, I waited

[012-022]patiently for the storm to blow over, when suddenly, in a flash of

[012-023]lightning, I saw a tall figure on the road. I began to stare intently

[012-024]in that direction--the figure seemed to have sprung out of the ground

[012-025]near my droshky.

[012-026]'Who's that?' inquired a ringing voice.

[012-027]'Why, who are you?'

[012-028]'I'm the forester here.'

[012-029]I mentioned my name.

[012-030]'Oh, I know! Are you on your way home?'

[012-031]'Yes. But, you see, in such a storm....'

[012-032]'Yes, there is a storm,' replied the voice.

[012-033]A pale flash of lightning lit up the forester from head to foot; a

[012-034]brief crashing clap of thunder followed at once upon it. The rain

[012-035]lashed with redoubled force.

[012-036]'It won't be over just directly,' the forester went on.

[012-037]'What's to be done?'

[012-038]'I'll take you to my hut, if you like,' he said abruptly.

[012-039]'That would be a service.'

[012-040]'Please to take your seat'

[012-041]He went up to the mare's head, took her by the bit, and pulled her up.

[012-042]We set off. I held on to the cushion of the droshky, which rocked 'like

[012-043]a boat on the sea,' and called my dog. My poor mare splashed with

[012-044]difficulty through the mud, slipped and stumbled; the forester hovered

[012-045]before the shafts to right and to left like a ghost. We drove rather a

[012-046]long while; at last my guide stopped. 'Here we are home, sir,' he

[012-047]observed in a quiet voice. The gate creaked; some puppies barked a

[012-048]welcome. I raised my head, and in a flash of lightning I made out a

[012-049]small hut in the middle of a large yard, fenced in with hurdles. From

[012-050]the one little window there was a dim light. The forester led his horse

[012-051]up to the steps and knocked at the door. 'Coming, coming!' we heard in

[012-052]a little shrill voice; there was the patter of bare feet, the bolt

[012-053]creaked, and a girl of twelve, in a little old smock tied round the

[012-054]waist with list, appeared in the doorway with a lantern in her hand.

[012-055]'Show the gentleman a light,' he said to her 'and I will put your

[012-056]droshky in the shed.'

[012-057]The little girl glanced at me, and went into the hut. I followed her.

[012-058]The forester's hut consisted of one room, smoky, low-pitched, and

[012-059]empty, without curtains or partition. A tattered sheepskin hung on the

[012-060]wall. On the bench lay a single-barrelled gun; in the corner lay a heap

[012-061]of rags; two great pots stood near the oven. A pine splinter was

[012-062]burning on the table flickering up and dying down mournfully. In the

[012-063]very middle of the hut hung a cradle, suspended from the end of a long

[012-064]horizontal pole. The little girl put out the lantern, sat down on a

[012-065]tiny stool, and with her right hand began swinging the cradle, while

[012-066]with her left she attended to the smouldering pine splinter. I looked

[012-067]round--my heart sank within me: it's not cheering to go into a

[012-068]peasant's hut at night. The baby in the cradle breathed hard and fast.

[012-069]'Are you all alone here?' I asked the little girl.

[012-070]'Yes,' she uttered, hardly audibly.

[012-071]'You're the forester's daughter?'

[012-072]'Yes,' she whispered.

[012-073]The door creaked, and the forester, bending his head, stepped across

[012-074]the threshold. He lifted the lantern from the floor, went up to the

[012-075]table, and lighted a candle.

[012-076]'I dare say you're not used to the splinter light?' said he, and he

[012-077]shook back his curls.

[012-078]I looked at him. Rarely has it been my fortune to behold such a comely

[012-079]creature. He was tall, broad-shouldered, and in marvellous proportion.

[012-080]His powerful muscles stood out in strong relief under his wet homespun

[012-081]shirt. A curly, black beard hid half of his stern and manly face; small

[012-082]brown eyes looked out boldly from under broad eyebrows which met in the

[012-083]middle. He stood before me, his arms held lightly akimbo.

[012-084]I thanked him, and asked his name.

[012-085]'My name's Foma,' he answered, 'and my nickname's Biryuk' (\_i.e.\_

[012-086]wolf). [Footnote: The name Biryuk is used in the Orel province to

[012-087]denote a solitary, misanthropic man.--\_Author's Note\_.]

[012-088]'Oh, you're Biryuk.'

[012-089]I looked with redoubled curiosity at him. From my Yermolaï and others I

[012-090]had often heard stories about the forester Biryuk, whom all the

[012-091]peasants of the surrounding districts feared as they feared fire.

[012-092]According to them there had never been such a master of his business in

[012-093]the world before. 'He won't let you carry off a handful of brushwood;

[012-094]he'll drop upon you like a fall of snow, whatever time it may be, even

[012-095]in the middle of the night, and you needn't think of resisting

[012-096]him--he's strong, and cunning as the devil.... And there's no getting

[012-097]at him anyhow; neither by brandy nor by money; there's no snare he'll

[012-098]walk into. More than once good folks have planned to put him out of the

[012-099]world, but no--it's never come off.'

[012-100]That was how the neighbouring peasants spoke of Biryuk.

[012-101]'So you're Biryuk,' I repeated; 'I've heard talk of you, brother. They

[012-102]say you show no mercy to anyone.'

[012-103]'I do my duty,' he answered grimly; 'it's not right to eat the master's

[012-104]bread for nothing.'

[012-105]He took an axe from his girdle and began splitting splinters.

[012-106]'Have you no wife?' I asked him.

[012-107]'No,' he answered, with a vigorous sweep of the axe.

[012-108]'She's dead, I suppose?'

[012-109]'No ... yes ... she's dead,' he added, and turned away. I was silent;

[012-110]he raised his eyes and looked at me.

[012-111]'She ran away with a travelling pedlar,' he brought out with a bitter

[012-112]smile. The little girl hung her head; the baby waked up and began

[012-113]crying; the little girl went to the cradle. 'There, give it him,' said

[012-114]Biryuk, thrusting a dirty feeding-bottle into her hand. 'Him, too, she

[012-115]abandoned,' he went on in an undertone, pointing to the baby. He went

[012-116]up to the door, stopped, and turned round.

[012-117]'A gentleman like you,' he began, 'wouldn't care for our bread, I dare

[012-118]say, and except bread, I've--'

[012-119]'I'm not hungry.'

[012-120]'Well, that's for you to say. I would have heated the samovar, but I've

[012-121]no tea.... I'll go and see how your horse is getting on.'

[012-122]He went out and slammed the door. I looked round again, the hut struck

[012-123]me as more melancholy than ever. The bitter smell of stale smoke choked

[012-124]my breathing unpleasantly. The little girl did not stir from her place,

[012-125]and did not raise her eyes; from time to time she jogged the cradle,

[012-126]and timidly pulled her slipping smock up on to shoulder; her bare legs

[012-127]hung motionless.

[012-128]'What's your name?' I asked her.

[012-129]'Ulita,' she said, her mournful little face drooping more than ever.

[012-130]The forester came in and sat down on the bench.

[012-131]'The storm 's passing over,' he observed, after a brief silence; 'if

[012-132]you wish it, I will guide you out of the forest.'

[012-133]I got up; Biryuk took his gun and examined the firepan.

[012-134]'What's that for?' I inquired.

[012-135]'There's mischief in the forest.... They're cutting a tree down on

[012-136]Mares' Ravine,' he added, in reply to my look of inquiry.

[012-137]'Could you hear it from here?'

[012-138]'I can hear it outside.'

[012-139]We went out together. The rain had ceased. Heavy masses of storm-cloud

[012-140]were still huddled in the distance; from time to time there were long

[012-141]flashes of lightning; but here and there overhead the dark blue sky was

[012-142]already visible; stars twinkled through the swiftly flying clouds. The

[012-143]outline of the trees, drenched with rain, and stirred by the wind,

[012-144]began to stand out in the darkness. We listened. The forester took off

[012-145]his cap and bent his head.... 'Th ... there!' he said suddenly, and he

[012-146]stretched out his hand: 'see what a night he's pitched on.' I had heard

[012-147]nothing but the rustle of the leaves. Biryuk led the mare out of the

[012-148]shed. 'But, perhaps,' he added aloud, 'this way I shall miss him.'

[012-149]'I'll go with you ... if you like?' 'Certainly,' he answered, and he

[012-150]backed the horse in again; 'we'll catch him in a trice, and then I'll

[012-151]take you. Let's be off.' We started, Biryuk in front, I following him.

[012-152]Heaven only knows how he found out his way, but he only stopped once or

[012-153]twice, and then merely to listen to the strokes of the axe. 'There,' he

[012-154]muttered, 'do you hear? do you hear?' 'Why, where?' Biryuk shrugged his

[012-155]shoulders. We went down into the ravine; the wind was still for an

[012-156]instant; the rhythmical strokes reached my hearing distinctly. Biryuk

[012-157]glanced at me and shook his head. We went farther through the wet

[012-158]bracken and nettles. A slow muffled crash was heard....

[012-159]'He's felled it,' muttered Biryuk. Meantime the sky had grown clearer

[012-160]and clearer; there was a faint light in the forest. We clambered at

[012-161]last out of the ravine.

[012-162]'Wait here a little,' the forester whispered to me. He bent down, and

[012-163]raising his gun above his head, vanished among the bushes. I began

[012-164]listening with strained attention. Across the continual roar of the

[012-165]wind faint sounds from close by reached me; there was a cautious blow

[012-166]of an axe on the brushwood, the crash of wheels, the snort of a

[012-167]horse....

[012-168]'Where are you off to? Stop!' the iron voice of Biryuk thundered

[012-169]suddenly. Another voice was heard in a pitiful shriek, like a trapped

[012-170]hare.... \_A struggle was beginning.\_

[012-171]'No, no, you've made a mistake,' Biryuk declared panting; 'you're not

[012-172]going to get off....' I rushed in the direction of the noise, and ran

[012-173]up to the scene of the conflict, stumbling at every step. A felled tree

[012-174]lay on the ground, and near it Biryuk was busily engaged holding the

[012-175]thief down and binding his hands behind his back with a kerchief. I

[012-176]came closer. Biryuk got up and set him on his feet. I saw a peasant

[012-177]drenched with rain, in tatters, and with a long dishevelled beard. A

[012-178]sorry little nag, half covered with a stiff mat, was standing by,

[012-179]together with a rough cart. The forester did not utter a word; the

[012-180]peasant too was silent; his head was shaking.

[012-181]'Let him go,' I whispered in Biryuk's ears; 'I'll pay for the tree.'

[012-182]Without a word Biryuk took the horse by the mane with his left hand; in

[012-183]his right he held the thief by the belt. 'Now turn round, you rat!' he

[012-184]said grimly.

[012-185]'The bit of an axe there, take it,' muttered the peasant.

[012-186]'No reason to lose it, certainly,' said the forester, and he picked up

[012-187]the axe. We started. I walked behind.... The rain began sprinkling

[012-188]again, and soon fell in torrents. With difficulty we made our way to

[012-189]the hut. Biryuk pushed the captured horse into the middle of the yard,

[012-190]led the peasant into the room, loosened the knot in the kerchief, and

[012-191]made him sit down in a corner. The little girl, who had fallen asleep

[012-192]near the oven, jumped up and began staring at us in silent terror. I

[012-197]'I would have shut him in the store loft, on your honour's account,' he

[012-199]'Leave him here; don't touch him,' I interrupted.

[012-200]The peasant stole a glance at me from under his brows. I vowed inwardly

[012-208]'Foma Kuzmitch,' said the peasant suddenly in a thick, broken voice;

[012-211]'Let me go.'

[012-212]Biryuk made no answer.

[012-213]'Let me go ... hunger drove me to it; let me go.'

[012-214]'I know you,' retorted the forester severely; 'your set's all

[012-216]'Let me go,' repeated the peasant. 'Our manager ... we 're ruined,

[012-218]'Ruined, indeed!... Nobody need steal.'

[012-219]'Let me go, Foma Kuzmitch.... Don't destroy me. Your manager, you know

[012-224]'Let me go,' he repeated with mournful desperation. 'Let me go; by God,
