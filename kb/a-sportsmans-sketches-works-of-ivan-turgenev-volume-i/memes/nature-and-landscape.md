# Nature and Landscape

**Nature and landscape** are the constant background — and often the true subject — of [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]. The narrator moves through the countryside of [[Oryol Province|Oryol]] and neighbouring provinces as a [[Hunting in the Sketches|hunter]], and the pretext of the hunt gives Turgenev license to record the natural world with the exactness of a naturalist and the feeling of a lyric poet.

Weather, season, and terrain are never mere scenery: they set the emotional key of each story and measure the human figures who pass through them.

## The Naturalist's Eye

Turgenev observes with precision. He distinguishes kinds of dawn, kinds of cloud, kinds of forest quiet, and he trusts the reader to feel the difference. The most celebrated example is the opening of [[Byezhin Prairie]], a passage that many Russian readers know by heart:

> (008-001) It was a glorious July day, one of those days which only come after many days of fine weather. From earliest morning the sky is clear; the sunrise does not glow with fire; it is suffused with a soft roseate flush. The sun, not fiery, not red-hot as in time of stifling drought, not dull purple as before a storm, but with a bright and genial radiance, rises peacefully behind a long and narrow cloud, shines out freshly, and plunges again into its lilac mist.

The same attentiveness records the sportsman's tense wait at nightfall, when the ear alone measures the world:

> (002-025) ...an oriole's melancholy cry sounds somewhere in the distance; then the nightingale's first note. Your heart is weary with suspense, when suddenly--but only sportsmen can understand me--suddenly in the deep hush there is a peculiar croaking and whirring sound.

## Weather as Drama

Where a story turns on danger or foreboding, the landscape turns with it. In [[Biryuk]] the narrator is overtaken in the forest by a storm that gathers with almost theatrical menace:

> (012-001) I was coming back from hunting one evening alone in a racing droshky... A tempest was coming on. In front, a huge, purplish storm-cloud slowly rose from behind the forest; long grey rain-clouds flew over my head.

The storm drives the narrator to the hut of the forester [[Biryuk]] and sets the whole encounter in motion. Throughout the *Sketches*, weather is not decoration but the engine that brings the wandering hunter into the company of peasants he would never otherwise meet.

## The Moral Landscape

For Turgenev's peasants the natural world is also a moral and spiritual order. The wandering holy man [[Kassyan]] rebukes the narrator for hunting, treating the killing of birds as a violation of a sacred whole:

> (009-160) You shoot the fowls of heaven, I suppose?... the wild things of the woods?... And is it not a sin to kill God's birds, to shed the innocent blood?

In this way nature links to the book's other great themes: it is the ground of [[Folk Belief and Superstition|folk belief]], the setting of the peasant's [[Religion and Faith|faith]], and the shared world in which narrator and [[Russian Peasantry|peasant]] meet as equals, whatever the law of [[Serfdom|serfdom]] may say.

## Social and Historical Context

By rooting his social observation in a lovingly exact landscape, Turgenev gave the anti-serfdom argument of the *Sketches* an unpolemical power.

The reader who is moved by the July dawn over the prairie, or by the storm in the forest, has already entered a world in which the peasants of that landscape must be seen as fully human. The natural descriptions, admired in their own right, thus do quiet political work.
