# Pavel Andreitch

## Overview

Pavel Andreitch is a former apothecary's assistant on the Losnyakova estate, appearing in the chapter [[THE COUNTING-HOUSE]]. He arrives at the counting-house in a fury to confront the head-clerk [[nikolai-eremyev|Nikolai Eremyev]], whom he accuses of persecuting him and blocking his marriage to a maid named [[tatyana|Tatyana]].

Their confrontation -- one of the most dramatic scenes in the collection -- pits righteous personal grievance against entrenched bureaucratic corruption. Pavel's direct, passionate challenge to Nikolai's authority reveals the web of intrigue, tale-bearing, and petty tyranny that governs life under an absentee [[landowning-gentry|landowner]].


## Appearance and Character

Pavel is described as "a tall man, apparently angry, with an irregular but bold and expressive face, and rather clean in his dress" (011-496--011-497). He is hot-tempered and direct: he flings himself into a chair (011-506), strikes the table with passion (011-516), and ultimately dashes forward with uplifted fists (011-600).

Yet beneath his fury lies a genuine sense of injustice. He appeals to Nikolai repeatedly to "tell me in a friendly way" why he is being persecuted (011-528), and his anger is rooted in love for [[tatyana|Tatyana]] and a sense that he is acting "straightforwardly" in seeking marriage (011-549--011-550).

His professional identity as an apothecary's assistant -- a position that required some education -- sets him apart from the ordinary [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and gives him a degree of independence ("I can make my bread everywhere," 011-566).


## Role in the Story

Pavel enters the counting-house seeking to settle scores with [[nikolai-eremyev|Nikolai Eremyev]]. He accuses the head-clerk of poisoning the mistress against him, colluding with the housekeeper to degrade [[tatyana|Tatyana]] from laundrymaid to scullery worker, and blocking their marriage out of petty vengeance (011-546--011-558).

The confrontation escalates as Nikolai dismisses Pavel as "an apothecary's assistant, a wretched leech" (011-570--011-571), to which Pavel retorts that he once saved Nikolai's life with his medicines -- an act he now bitterly regrets: "It was some devil drove me to cure him" (011-574--011-575).

Nikolai counters by accusing Pavel of attempting to poison him with aloes (011-576--011-581). The scene reaches its climax when Nikolai invokes Pavel's father -- "I got the better of your father; I broke his horns -- a warning to you; take care!" (011-590--011-591) -- revealing that the clerk's persecution of Pavel extends a multi-generational feud.

Pavel strikes Nikolai to the ground, and the chapter cuts away with the narrator refusing to describe the end of the scene (011-600--011-604). A week later, the narrator learns that both Pavel and Nikolai remain in the lady's service (011-605--011-606).


## Historical and Social Context

Pavel's position as an apothecary's assistant places him in an ambiguous social stratum: neither serf nor [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], he possesses a skill that grants him a measure of independence but not enough to challenge the manorial hierarchy openly.

His confrontation with Nikolai dramatises the powerlessness of the individual before a system in which the head-clerk, by controlling access to the proprietor, can ruin anyone who falls out of favour. The intergenerational dimension -- Nikolai "broke" Pavel's father as well -- underscores the systemic, enduring nature of this exploitation.
