# THE PEASANT PROPRIETOR OVSYANIKOV

[006-001]Picture to yourselves, gentle readers, a stout, tall man of seventy,

[006-002]with a face reminding one somewhat of the face of Kriloff, clear and

[006-003]intelligent eyes under overhanging brows, dignified in bearing, slow in

[006-004]speech, and deliberate in movement: there you have Ovsyanikov. He wore

[006-005]an ample blue overcoat with long sleeves, buttoned all the way up, a

[006-006]lilac silk-handkerchief round his neck, brightly polished boots with

[006-007]tassels, and altogether resembled in appearance a well-to-do merchant.

[006-008]His hands were handsome, soft, and white; he often fumbled with the

[006-009]buttons of his coat as he talked. With his dignity and his composure,

[006-010]his good sense and his indolence, his uprightness and his obstinacy,

[006-011]Ovsyanikov reminded me of the Russian boyars of the times before Peter

[006-012]the Great.... The national holiday dress would have suited him well. He

[006-013]was one of the last men left of the old time. All his neighbours had a

[006-014]great respect for him, and considered it an honour to be acquainted

[006-015]with him. His fellow peasant-proprietors almost worshipped him, and

[006-016]took off their hats to him from a distance: they were proud of him.

[006-017]Generally speaking, in these days, it is difficult to tell a

[006-018]peasant-proprietor from a peasant; his husbandry is almost worse than

[006-019]the peasant's; his calves are wretchedly small; his horses are only

[006-020]half alive; his harness is made of rope. Ovsyanikov was an exception to

[006-021]the general rule, though he did not pass for a wealthy man. He lived

[006-022]alone with his wife in a clean and comfortable little house, kept a few

[006-023]servants, whom he dressed in the Russian style and called his

[006-024]'workmen.' They were employed also in ploughing his land. He did not

[006-025]attempt to pass for a nobleman, did not affect to be a landowner;

[006-026]never, as they say, forgot himself; he did not take a seat at the first

[006-027]invitation to do so, and he never failed to rise from his seat on the

[006-028]entrance of a new guest, but with such dignity, with such stately

[006-029]courtesy, that the guest involuntarily made him a more deferential bow.

[006-030]Ovsyanikov adhered to the antique usages, not from superstition (he was

[006-031]naturally rather independent in mind), but from habit. He did not, for

[006-032]instance, like carriages with springs, because he did not find them

[006-033]comfortable, and preferred to drive in a racing droshky, or in a pretty

[006-034]little trap with leather cushions, and he always drove his good bay

[006-035]himself (he kept none but bay horses). His coachman, a young,

[006-036]rosy-cheeked fellow, his hair cut round like a basin, in a dark blue

[006-037]coat with a strap round the waist, sat respectfully beside him.

[006-038]Ovsyanikov always had a nap after dinner and visited the bath-house on

[006-039]Saturdays; he read none but religious books and used gravely to fix his

[006-040]round silver spectacles on his nose when he did so; he got up, and went

[006-041]to bed early. He shaved his beard, however, and wore his hair in the

[006-042]German style. He always received visitors cordially and affably, but he

[006-043]did not bow down to the ground, nor fuss over them and press them to

[006-044]partake of every kind of dried and salted delicacy. 'Wife!' he would

[006-045]say deliberately, not getting up from his seat, but only turning his

[006-046]head a little in her direction, 'bring the gentleman a little of

[006-047]something to eat.' He regarded it as a sin to sell wheat: it was the

[006-048]gift of God. In the year '40, at the time of the general famine and

[006-049]terrible scarcity, he shared all his store with the surrounding

[006-050]landowners and peasants; the following year they gratefully repaid

[006-051]their debt to him in kind. The neighbours often had recourse to

[006-052]Ovsyanikov as arbitrator and mediator between them, and they almost

[006-053]always acquiesced in his decision, and listened to his advice. Thanks

[006-054]to his intervention, many had conclusively settled their boundaries....

[006-055]But after two or three tussles with lady-landowners, he announced that

[006-056]he declined all mediation between persons of the feminine gender. He

[006-057]could not bear the flurry and excitement, the chatter of women and the

[006-058]'fuss.' Once his house had somehow got on fire. A workman ran to him in

[006-059]headlong haste shrieking, 'Fire, fire!' 'Well, what are you screaming

[006-060]about?' said Ovsyanikov tranquilly, 'give me my cap and my stick.' He

[006-061]liked to break in his horses himself. Once a spirited horse he was

[006-062]training bolted with him down a hillside and over a precipice. 'Come,

[006-063]there, there, you young colt, you'll kill yourself!' said Ovsyanikov

[006-064]soothingly to him, and an instant later he flew over the precipice

[006-065]together with the racing droshky, the boy who was sitting behind, and

[006-066]the horse. Fortunately, the bottom of the ravine was covered with heaps

[006-067]of sand. No one was injured; only the horse sprained a leg. 'Well, you

[006-068]see,' continued Ovsyanikov in a calm voice as he got up from the

[006-069]ground, 'I told you so.' He had found a wife to match him. Tatyana

[006-070]Ilyinitchna Ovsyanikov was a tall woman, dignified and taciturn, always

[006-071]dressed in a cinnamon-coloured silk dress. She had a cold air, though

[006-072]none complained of her severity, but, on the contrary, many poor

[006-073]creatures called her their little mother and benefactress. Her regular

[006-074]features, her large dark eyes, and her delicately cut lips, bore

[006-075]witness even now to her once celebrated beauty. Ovsyanikov had no

[006-076]children.

[006-077]I made his acquaintance, as the reader is already aware, at Radilov's,

[006-078]and two days later I went to see him. I found him at home. He was

[006-079]reading the lives of the Saints. A grey cat was purring on his

[006-080]shoulder. He received me, according to his habit, with stately

[006-081]cordiality. We fell into conversation.

[006-082]'But tell me the truth, Luka Petrovitch,' I said to him, among other

[006-083]things; 'weren't things better of old, in your time?'

[006-084]'In some ways, certainly, things were better, I should say,' replied

[006-085]Ovsyanikov; 'we lived more easily; there was a greater abundance of

[006-086]everything. ... All the same, things are better now, and they will be

[006-087]better still for your children, please God.'

[006-088]'I had expected you, Luka Petrovitch, to praise the old times.'

[006-089]'No, I have no special reason to praise old times. Here, for instance,

[006-090]though you are a landowner now, and just as much a landowner as your

[006-091]grandfather was, you have not the same power--and, indeed, you are not

[006-092]yourself the same kind of man. Even now, some noblemen oppress us; but,

[006-093]of course, it is impossible to help that altogether. Where there are

[006-094]mills grinding there will be flour. No; I don't see now what I have

[006-095]experienced myself in my youth.'

[006-096]'What, for instance?'

[006-097]'Well, for instance, I will tell you about your grandfather. He was an

[006-098]overbearing man; he oppressed us poorer folks. You know,

[006-099]perhaps--indeed, you surely know your own estates--that bit of land

[006-100]that runs from Tchepligin to Malinina--you have it under oats now....

[006-101]Well, you know, it is ours--it is all ours. Your grandfather took it

[006-102]away from us; he rode by on his horse, pointed to it with his hand, and

[006-103]said, "It's my property," and took possession of it. My father (God

[006-104]rest his soul!) was a just man; he was a hot-tempered man, too; he

[006-105]would not put up with it--indeed, who does like to lose his

[006-106]property?--and he laid a petition before the court. But he was alone:

[006-107]the others did not appear--they were afraid. So they reported to your

[006-108]grandfather that "Piotr Ovsyanikov is making a complaint against you

[006-109]that you were pleased to take away his land." Your grandfather at once

[006-110]sent his huntsman Baush with a detachment of men.... Well, they seized

[006-111]my father, and carried him to your estate. I was a little boy at that

[006-112]time; I ran after him barefoot. What happened? They brought him to your

[006-113]house, and flogged him right under your windows. And your grandfather

[006-114]stands on the balcony and looks on; and your grandmother sits at the

[006-115]window and looks on too. My father cries out, "Gracious lady, Marya

[006-116]Vasilyevna, intercede for me! have mercy on me!" But her only answer

[006-117]was to keep getting up to have a look at him. So they exacted a promise

[006-118]from my father to give up the land, and bade him be thankful they let

[006-119]him go alive. So it has remained with you. Go and ask your

[006-120]peasants--what do they call the land, indeed? It's called "The

[006-121]Cudgelled Land," because it was gained by the cudgel. So you see from

[006-122]that, we poor folks can't bewail the old order very much.'

[006-123]I did not know what answer to make Ovsyanikov, and I had not the

[006-124]courage to look him in the face.

[006-125]'We had another neighbour who settled amongst us in those days, Komov,

[006-126]Stepan Niktopolionitch. He used to worry my father out of his life;

[006-127]when it wasn't one thing, it was another. He was a drunken fellow, and

[006-128]fond of treating others; and when he was drunk he would say in French,

[006-129]"\_Say bon\_," and "Take away the holy images!" He would go to all the

[006-130]neighbours to ask them to come to him. His horses stood always in

[006-131]readiness, and if you wouldn't go he would come after you himself at

[006-132]once!... And he was such a strange fellow! In his sober times he was

[006-133]not a liar; but when he was drunk he would begin to relate how he had

[006-134]three houses in Petersburg--one red, with one chimney; another yellow,

[006-135]with two chimneys; and a third blue, with no chimneys; and three sons

[006-136](though he had never even been married), one in the infantry, another

[006-137]in the cavalry, and the third was his own master.... And he would say

[006-138]that in each house lived one of his sons; that admirals visited the

[006-139]eldest, and generals the second, and the third only Englishmen! Then he

[006-140]would get up and say, "To the health of my eldest son; he is the most

[006-141]dutiful!" and he would begin to weep. Woe to anyone who refused to

[006-142]drink the toast! "I will shoot him!" he would say; "and I won't let him

[006-143]be buried!" ... Then he would jump up and scream, "Dance, God's people,

[006-144]for your pleasure and my diversion!" Well, then, you must dance; if you

[006-145]had to die for it, you must dance. He thoroughly worried his serf-girls

[006-146]to death. Sometimes all night long till morning they would be singing

[006-147]in chorus, and the one who made the most noise would have a prize. If

[006-148]they began to be tired, he would lay his head down in his hands, and

[006-149]begins moaning: "Ah, poor forsaken orphan that I am! They abandon me,

[006-150]poor little dove!" And the stable-boys would wake the girls up at once.

[006-151]He took a liking to my father; what was he to do? He almost drove my

[006-152]father into his grave, and would actually have driven him into it, but

[006-153](thank Heaven!) he died himself; in one of his drunken fits he fell off

[006-154]the pigeon-house. ... There, that's what our sweet little neighbours

[006-155]were like!'

[006-156]'How the times have changed!' I observed.

[006-157]'Yes, yes,' Ovsyanikov assented. 'And there is this to be said--in the

[006-158]old days the nobility lived more sumptuously. I'm not speaking of the

[006-159]real grandees now. I used to see them in Moscow. They say such people

[006-160]are scarce nowadays.'

[006-161]'Have you been in Moscow?'

[006-162]'I used to stay there long, very long ago. I am now in my seventy-third

[006-163]year; and I went to Moscow when I was sixteen.'

[006-164]Ovsyanikov sighed.

[006-165]'Whom did you see there?'

[006-166]'I saw a great many grandees--and every one saw them; they kept open

[006-167]house for the wonder and admiration of all! Only no one came up to

[006-168]Count Alexey Grigoryevitch Orlov-Tchesmensky. I often saw Alexey

[006-169]Grigoryevitch; my uncle was a steward in his service. The count was

[006-170]pleased to live in Shabolovka, near the Kaluga Gate. He was a grand

[006-171]gentleman! Such stateliness, such gracious condescension you can't

[006-172]imagine! and it's impossible to describe it. His figure alone was worth

[006-173]something, and his strength, and the look in his eyes! Till you knew

[006-174]him, you did not dare come near him--you were afraid, overawed indeed;

[006-175]but directly you came near him he was like sunshine warming you up and

[006-176]making you quite cheerful. He allowed every man access to him in

[006-177]person, and he was devoted to every kind of sport. He drove himself in

[006-178]races and out-stripped every one, and he would never get in front at

[006-179]the start, so as not to offend his adversary; he would not cut it

[006-180]short, but would pass him at the finish; and he was so pleasant--he

[006-181]would soothe his adversary, praising his horse. He kept tumbler-pigeons

[006-182]of a first-rate kind. He would come out into the court, sit down in an

[006-183]arm-chair, and order them to let loose the pigeons; and his men would

[006-184]stand all round on the roofs with guns to keep off the hawks. A large

[006-185]silver basin of water used to be placed at the count's feet, and he

[006-186]looked at the pigeons reflected in the water. Beggars and poor people

[006-187]were fed in hundreds at his expense; and what a lot of money he used to

[006-188]give away!... When he got angry, it was like a clap of thunder.

[006-189]Everyone was in a great fright, but there was nothing to weep over;

[006-190]look round a minute after, and he was all smiles again! When he gave a

[006-191]banquet he made all Moscow drunk!--and see what a clever man he was!

[006-195]threw him, he perfectly loaded him with presents, and kissed him on the

[006-196]lips.... And once, during my stay at Moscow, he arranged a hunting

[006-197]party such as had never been in Russia before; he sent invitations to

[006-198]all the sportsmen in the whole empire, and fixed a day for it, and gave

[006-199]them three months' notice. They brought with them dogs and grooms:

[006-200]well, it was an army of people--a regular army!

[006-201]'First they had a banquet in the usual way, and then they set off into

[006-202]the open country. The people flocked there in thousands! And what do

[006-203]you think?... Your father's dog outran them all.'

[006-204]'Wasn't that Milovidka?' I inquired.

[006-205]'Milovidka, Milovidka!... So the count began to ask him, "Give me your

[006-213]a stone with an inscription on it over the dog.'

[006-215]'Yes, it is always like that; those who can only just keep themselves

[006-233]'You seem to be fond of hunting too, Luka Petrovitch?'

[006-234]'I should have been--certainly, not now; now my time is over--but in my

[006-235]young days.... But you know it was not an easy matter in my position.

[006-236]It's not suitable for people like us to go trailing after noblemen.
