## Overview

Count Blangy (Théophile-Henri, Vicomte de Blangy) is a French émigré of noble birth whose tombstone the narrator discovers while waiting in the churchyard of [[Lgov]]. He fled Revolutionary France, found refuge in the Russian provinces, and spent his remaining years teaching children—a representative figure of the wave of French aristocrats who sought asylum in Russia after 1789. His epitaph, engraved in both French and Russian, is one of the most elaborate inscriptions in the entire [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] (007-092).

## Appearance and Character

Count Blangy never appears as a living character; he is known solely through his funerary monument, a "blackened, four-cornered urn" (007-090) in the [[lgov-place|Lgov]] churchyard. The epitaph, however, paints a vivid portrait. The French inscription identifies him as Théophile-Henri, Vicomte de Blangy, while the Russian version—"Under this stone is laid the body of a French subject, Count Blangy; born 1737, died 1799, in the 62nd year of his age" (007-093)—supplies the bare biographical facts. The fourth side preserves a versified elegy that sketches his life's arc:

> "Of high descent was he, and also of talent.
> A wife and kindred murdered he bewailed,
> And left his land by tyrants cruel assailed;
> The friendly shores of Russia he attained,
> And hospitable shelter here he gained;
> Children he taught; their parents' cares allayed:
> Here, by God's will, in peace he has been laid." (007-095)

The verse constructs a complete narrative: aristocratic birth, the trauma of Revolutionary violence (a murdered wife and kindred), flight from "tyrants cruel," asylum in Russia, and a useful second life as a teacher before a peaceful death.

## Role in the Story

The Count's tombstone appears as a narrative pause in the chapter [[Lgov]]—a moment of historical meditation before the action resumes. The narrator, left alone in the churchyard while [[Yermolaï]] and [[Vladimir]] go to fetch [[Sutchok]], stumbles upon the monument while idly examining the graves:

> "While I was looking at the tombstones in the churchyard, I stumbled upon a blackened, four-cornered urn with the following inscription, on one side in French: 'Ci-git Théophile-Henri, Vicomte de Blangy'..." (007-090)

This brief encounter with the material trace of a foreign life, ended in a remote Russian village, is characteristic of Turgenev's method throughout the Sketches. The narrator's attention to the epitaph—he transcribes all four sides—elevates what might have been a passing detail into a miniature elegy. The Count's story mirrors, in compressed form, the larger history of Franco-Russian aristocratic exchange in the eighteenth century, and his peaceful interment in [[lgov-place|Lgov]]'s churchyard stands as a quiet counterpoint to the brutal realities of serfdom that dominate the chapter.

The tombstone also connects [[Lgov]] to the wider world beyond the Russian provinces. In a chapter otherwise concerned with a duck-shooting expedition and the rambling autobiography of a serf fisherman, the elaborate French epitaph reminds the reader that even the most remote corners of the empire were touched by the currents of European history.
