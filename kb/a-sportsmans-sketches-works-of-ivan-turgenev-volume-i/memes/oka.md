# Oka

## Overview

The Oka, one of the great tributaries of the [[Volga]], is named a single time in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — in the litany of Russian places recited by the wandering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[Kassyan]] in [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]]. Though it appears only in passing, its epithet, "the good nurse," places it among the maternal, life-giving waters of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s sacred geography.

## Description

[[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s litany rises out of his dream of a blessed country where "apples grow of gold, on silver branches, and every man lives in uprightness and content" (009-493). Recalling his own wanderings, he asks whether he has "journeyed so little already" (009-494) and lists the places he has seen: "I have been to [[romyon|Romyon]] and to [[simbirsk|Simbirsk the fair city]], and even to [[moscow|Moscow of the golden domes]]; I have been to Oka the good nurse, and to [[tsna|Tsna the dove]], and to our mother [[volga|Volga]]" (009-495 to 009-497). Within this catalogue the Oka — "the good nurse" (009-496) — is imagined not as a mere waterway but as a nourishing, almost living presence, paired with "our mother [[volga|Volga]]" and "[[tsna|Tsna the dove]]."

## Role in the Stories

Like the other rivers and cities of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s litany, the Oka has no part in the events of the *Sketches*; it exists purely as a name in the pilgrim-[[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s imagined map of Russia. Its function is to widen the geographical and spiritual horizon of [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]] beyond the narrow woodland where the story takes place, and to characterise [[kassyan|Kassyan]] himself — a rootless wanderer for whom the far rivers of Russia are objects of tender, half-religious devotion.
