# Borka Perohodov

## Overview

Borka Perohodov is a minor but pointedly debated figure in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]," invoked by [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s uncle during a quarrel with the young clerk [[Mitya]] about the scope of moral obligation. Perohodov "appropriated the public moneys" (006-498) — an act of embezzlement — and the argument that erupts around his name turns on whether poverty and family justify shielding a wrongdoer from the consequences of his crime. He never appears in the narrative; he is a case study in moral reasoning, deployed to test the boundary between charity and complicity.

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Perohodov is given. His character is sketched in a handful of hostile adjectives: according to [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s uncle, he is "a drunkard, a quarrelsome fellow" (006-500) whose poverty does not excuse his crime. [[mitya|Mitya]], who apparently "looked after him" and "took him under his protection" (006-495–006-496), offers a more sympathetic view, though [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] counters that "Perohodov suffered through his own fault, certainly" (006-497). The dispute captures a recurring tension in the sketch: how far should personal loyalty extend toward someone who has demonstrably done wrong?

## Role in the Story

Perohodov enters the dialogue as ammunition in the older [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s attack on [[mitya|Mitya]]'s moral consistency:

> "'Have you forgotten Borka Perohodov? Who was it looked after him? Who took him under his protection — eh?'" (006-495–006-496)

The accusation is that [[mitya|Mitya]], who now defends the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and claims to act "against his conscience" in nothing (006-493), once protected an embezzler. [[mitya|Mitya]]'s defence — "Perohodov suffered through his own fault, certainly" (006-497) — concedes the factual guilt while preserving the right to compassion. But the older man presses harder: "He appropriated the public moneys.... That was all!" (006-498), refusing to let the crime be softened. When [[mitya|Mitya]] pleads poverty and family as mitigating circumstances — "But, consider, uncle: his poverty, his family" (006-499) — the reply is unsparing: "Poverty, poverty.... He's a drunkard, a quarrelsome fellow; that's..." (006-500).

The exchange is brief but structurally important. Perohodov serves as the darker counterpart to the [[Bezpandin]] case: whereas [[bezpandin|Bezpandin]] is a predator whom [[mitya|Mitya]] righteously opposes, Perohodov is a wrongdoer whom [[mitya|Mitya]] — inconsistently, in his uncle's eyes — chose to protect. The pairing of the two names forces the reader to consider whether [[mitya|Mitya]]'s moral compass is as steady as he claims.
