# Sitnikov

## Overview

Sitnikov is a well-known horse-dealer at the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] fair, encountered by the narrator in Turgenev's sketch "[[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]]" (Chapter XIV). A tall, stout man no longer young, Sitnikov represents the commercial face of provincial Russia's horse trade — outwardly deferential and ceremonious, but fundamentally driven by profit and skilled in the arts of salesmanship and misrepresentation (014-212).

## Appearance & Character

Sitnikov cuts an imposing figure. He is tall and stout, dressed in a hareskin coat with a raised turnover collar, and moves "slowly" with a measured, deliberate bearing (014-212). When greeting a potential customer, he holds his cap in both hands above his head and speaks in a "sing-song voice," deploying elaborate formalities: "Ah, our respects to you. You'd like to have a look at the horses, may be?" (014-212). Every word is drawn out, as if each syllable carries the weight of commercial sincerity.

Beneath this ceremonious exterior, however, Sitnikov is a shrewd and calculating businessman. When the narrator shows genuine interest in a dapple-grey horse, Sitnikov "at once feigned absolute indifference" — a classic bargaining tactic (014-286). He asks "an impossible price," and the two begin haggling in the street, a scene that captures the ritualised nature of horse-trading in provincial Russia (014-317).

Sitnikov is also a showman. He has a stable of nearly thirty horses "groomed to perfection," displayed in horse-boxes raised above the ground, with trained stable-boys who make the animals rear, circle the yard, and pull up at precisely the right spot (014-212, 014-249). When [[prince-n|Prince N----]] arrives, Sitnikov's manner shifts instantly to obsequious deference — "Your excellency, please to come in!" — and he brings out his finest stock, including the magnificent horse Peacock (014-325).

## Role in the Story

The narrator visits Sitnikov's establishment as his first stop on the morning after his arrival at the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] fair. Sitnikov shows him a series of horses: Ermine, a well-shaped grey stallion with questionable hind legs; Falcon, a gaunt black beast of Dutch extraction that "minces and dances" without making headway — popular among middle-aged merchants for its showy but impractical gait (014-249, 014-268). The narrator declines both.

Finally, a dapple-grey of Voyakov breed catches the narrator's eye. Sitnikov immediately feigns indifference, but agrees to a test drive. When the bargaining begins, the scene is interrupted by the arrival of [[prince-n|Prince N----]], Hlopakov, and the groom [[baklaga|Baklaga]] in a splendid three-horse trap. Sitnikov abandons his negotiation with the narrator to attend to this more distinguished customer, bringing out his prize horse Peacock — a powerful dark sorrel — and quoting a price of five thousand roubles (014-325, 014-356).

## Historical/Social Context

Sitnikov embodies the commercial class of provincial Russia, whose prosperity depended on the fairs that brought together [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s, officers, and merchants. Turgenev's portrait is notably ambivalent: Sitnikov is neither condemned as a villain nor celebrated as an honest trader. His theatricality and price-gouging are presented as part of the accepted ritual of the horse fair — a world where both buyer and seller understand that appearances are performed and prices are negotiated through elaborate games of feigned indifference and exaggerated admiration.

The contrast between Sitnikov and [[anastasei-ivanitch-tchornobai|Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai]], the old-fashioned steppes breeder who appears later in the same sketch, is deliberate. Sitnikov represents the commercialised, performance-driven horse trade; Tchornobai represents an older, patriarchal model of honour and simplicity.
