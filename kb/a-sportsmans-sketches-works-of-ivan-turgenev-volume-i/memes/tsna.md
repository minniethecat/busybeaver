# Tsna

## Overview

The Tsna is a Russian river — a real tributary of the Moksha — named a single time in *A Sportsman's Sketches*, in the litany of far places recited by the wandering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[Kassyan]] in [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]].

Like the other waters of that litany it appears only as a name, but its tender epithet, "the dove," places it among the maternal, holy rivers of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s imagined map of Russia.

## Description

The name rises out of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s dream of a blessed land where "apples grow of gold, on silver branches, and every man lives in uprightness and content" (009-493).

Wondering whether he has "journeyed so little already" (009-494), he recites the places he has seen — "[[Romyon]] and to [[Simbirsk]] the fair city, and even to [[Moscow]] of the golden domes" (009-495) — before naming the rivers: "[[Oka]] the good nurse, and to Tsna the dove, and to our mother [[Volga]]" (009-497).

Within this catalogue the Tsna — "the dove" — is imagined less as a waterway than as a gentle, almost living companion, set between "[[oka|Oka the good nurse]]" and "our mother [[volga|Volga]]."

## Role in the Stories

The Tsna plays no part in the events of the sketch; it exists only as a word in the pilgrim-[[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s recitation.

Its function is to widen the horizon of [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|Kassyan of Fair Springs]] far beyond the little woodland clearing where the story takes place, and to characterise [[kassyan|Kassyan]] himself — a rootless wanderer for whom the distant rivers of Russia are objects of half-religious tenderness.
