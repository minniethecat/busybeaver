# Harkoff

## Overview

Harkoff — the city of Kharkov — appears in [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] as one entry in a catalogue of provincial cities that supplied challengers to a famous wrestling grandee of the old days. It is a name on the mental map of the *[[A Sportsman's Sketches, Volume I|Sketches]]*, invoked to convey distance, reputation, and the reach of a great lord's fame.

## Description

Harkoff is never described in itself; it exists in the story only as a point of origin. Recalling the strength and splendour of the old count — most likely [[Alexey Grigoryevitch Orlov-Tchesmensky]] — [[Ovsyanikov]] notes that "he was fond of wrestling too; strong men used to come from Tula, from Harkoff, from [[tamboff|Tamboff]], and from everywhere to him" (006-192).

The listing is formulaic and almost Homeric, placing Harkoff between [[Tula Province|Tula]] and [[Tamboff]] as one of the far cities whose champions travelled to test themselves against the count.

## Role in the Stories

Harkoff's function is to measure the count's renown by geography. Men came from as far as Harkoff because "if he threw any one he would pay him a reward" (006-194), and even a man who threw the count "he perfectly loaded with presents, and kissed on the lips" (006-195).

The city's name, folded into this catalogue of contenders, helps Turgenev evoke a vanished world of aristocratic hospitality and physical prowess. Harkoff is real, distant, and connected — a node in the informal network of the provinces through which strength and reputation once travelled.
