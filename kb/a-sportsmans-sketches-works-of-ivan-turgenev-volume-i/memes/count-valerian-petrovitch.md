## Overview

Count Valerian Petrovitch is a young [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] mentioned in "[[Raspberry Spring]]," the son of [[Count Piotr Ilitch]]. He inherited the serf [[Vlass]] and [[vlass|Vlass]]'s village from his father, becoming [[vlass|Vlass]]'s master after Piotr Ilitch's death. His appearance and character are described briefly by [[vlass|Vlass]] during a conversation at the spring, offering a snapshot of the younger generation of the Russian landed [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. (003-311)

## Appearance and Character

[[vlass|Vlass]] provides a brief but vivid description of Count Valerian Petrovitch's physical appearance. When the narrator asks if the count is well, [[vlass|Vlass]] replies that "he is well, thank God," adding that "he has grown so red, and his face looks as though it were padded" (003-316). This description — a red, padded-looking face — suggests a well-fed, prosperous young nobleman, typical of the landowning class depicted throughout [[A Sportsman's Sketches]].

## Role in the Story

Count Valerian Petrovitch appears only through the report of others. The former serf [[Mihailo Savelyev Tuman]], who had served under [[count-piotr-ilitch|Count Piotr Ilitch]], explains the chain of inheritance: "[[count-piotr-ilitch|Piotr Hitch]] gave him [[vlass|Vlass]]'s village in his lifetime" (003-313). This transfer placed [[vlass|Vlass]] under Count Valerian Petrovitch's ownership. The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[vlass|Vlass]], now in arrears and unable to pay his rent after his son's death, speaks of his current master with a mixture of deference and the resigned humor of a man who has nothing left to lose. [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] turns to the narrator and remarks on the difficulty of paying rent "here" — in the countryside far from [[moscow|Moscow]] — compared to estates near the capital (003-318), implying that Count Valerian Petrovitch, like many absentee landlords, managed his rural properties from a distance.
