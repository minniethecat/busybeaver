# Romyon

## Overview

Romyon is a Russian town that appears in two chapters of Turgenev's *[[A Sportsman's Sketches, Volume I|Sketches]]*, principally known for its horse-fair — a seasonal market where horses and equestrian goods were traded. Mentioned by both the wandering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[Kassyan]] and the narrator of [[Two Country Gentlemen]], Romyon represents one of the commercial nodes in the rural economy of provincial Russia, a place that drew traders and buyers from across the region.

## Description

Romyon is first introduced in [[Kassyan of Fair Springs]], where [[kassyan|Kassyan]] lists it among the many places he has visited in his ceaseless wanderings. Reflecting on the breadth of his travels, [[kassyan|Kassyan]] declares: "I have been to Romyon and to [[simbirsk|Simbirsk the fair city]], and even to [[moscow|Moscow of the golden domes]]; I have been to [[oka|Oka]] the good river, and to the [[volga|Volga]] the mother of waters" (009-495). The juxtaposition is telling: Romyon is mentioned in the same breath as [[simbirsk|Simbirsk]] and [[moscow|Moscow]], yet its appeal is not grandeur but commerce. It is the horse-fair that defines the town — a fact confirmed in [[Two Country Gentlemen]], where the narrator describes a coat that "has assumed a purplish hue, thanks to a composition bought at the Romyon horse-fair of a Jew who gave himself out as an Armenian" (013-016). The detail is mundane — a dye for a garment — but it captures the character of the horse-fair as a marketplace where goods of all kinds, not just horses, changed hands.

## Role in the Stories

Romyon never serves as a setting in the *Sketches*; it is invoked purely as a reference. In [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s speech, it testifies to the breadth of his wandering life — he has been to Romyon, [[simbirsk|Simbirsk]], [[moscow|Moscow]], and beyond, a man who knows the roads and fairs of half of Russia. In [[Two Country Gentlemen]], the reference to the Romyon horse-fair adds a touch of comic realism: the dye that has discolored Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch's coat came from a Jewish trader at the fair, a detail that evokes the colorful, sometimes dubious commerce of provincial market towns. Together, these brief references sketch Romyon as a place of exchange — practical, unromantic, and woven into the fabric of rural life.
