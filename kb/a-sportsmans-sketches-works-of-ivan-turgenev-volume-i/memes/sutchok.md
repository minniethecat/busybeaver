# Sutchok

## Overview

Sutchok — whose nickname means "the twig" or "the bough" — is the serf fisherman of [[lgov-place|Lgov]], and one of the most memorable minor characters in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. When the narrator needs a boat to hunt ducks on the overgrown pond at [[lgov-place|Lgov]], he is directed to Sutchok, who owns the only punt. The ensuing conversation — in which Sutchok recounts his life history of being passed from one master to another and reassigned to one task after another — becomes a quietly devastating portrait of the serf as disposable object. Sutchok does not exist as a subject; he exists as a function to which masters assign roles, and the accumulation of his roles — coachman, cook, fisherman, and half a dozen others — adds up to the life of a man who has never been permitted to become anything.

## Appearance and Manner

Turgenev's description of Sutchok is compact and unsparing:

> Barelegged, ragged and dishevelled, Sutchok looked like a discharged stray house-serf of sixty years old. (007-105–007-106)

His boat is in keeping with his person — "Its boards are split apart, and the rivets have come off the cracks" (007-111) — a physical correlative for a life that has never been mended. His voice is "hoarse, cracked" (007-108), and he speaks "in a strange, far-away voice, as though he were in a dream" (007-128–007-129). The dreamlike quality is the symptom of a consciousness that has learned to survive by not fully inhabiting its own circumstances.

Sutchok stands before the narrator and [[vladimir-lgov|Vladimir]] "his head bent and his hands clasped behind his back, according to the old habit of house-serfs" (007-141–007-142). The posture is hereditary: it is the bodily form of a lifetime of waiting for orders, of never initiating action, of being always the object of someone else's will.

## A Life in Roles

The interrogation that follows is one of Turgenev's most economical set-pieces. The narrator asks Sutchok what his occupation was before becoming a fisherman, and the answer opens a vertiginous descent into the serf's accumulated past:

> "I was coachman before."
> "Who dismissed you from being coachman?"
> "The new mistress."
> "What mistress?"
> "Oh, that bought us. Your honour does not know her; Alyona Timofyevna; she is so fat ... not young."
> "Why did she decide to make you a fisherman?"
> "God knows... She came to us from her estate in Tamboff, gave orders for all the household to come together, and came out to us... Then she began to question us in order... She came to me in my turn; so she asked: 'What have you been?' I say, 'Coachman.' 'Coachman? Well, a fine coachman you are; only look at you! You're not fit for a coachman, but be my fisherman, and shave your beard.'" (007-146–007-161)

The absurdity is self-evident: a serf's occupation is determined not by aptitude, training, or inclination, but by the whim of a new mistress who has never seen him before and whose judgment is based on a single glance. The command to "shave your beard" is a further violation — an erasure of what remains of personal identity — and the final directive, "On the occasions of my visits provide fish for the table," is followed by the impossibility: "But how is one to keep it in order?" (007-161–007-162). There are no fish in the river; Sutchok's new role is a form of punishment disguised as an appointment.

The questioning continues backward through layers of ownership:

> "Whom did you belong to before?"
> "To Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev. We came to him by inheritance. But he did not own us long; only six years altogether. I was his coachman ... but not in town, he had others there — only in the country."
> "And were you always a coachman from your youth up?"
> "Always a coachman? Oh, no! I became a coachman in Sergaï Sergiitch's time, but before that I was a cook — but not town-cook; only a cook in the country." (007-163–007-170)

The litany continues backward — Sutchok has been cook, coachman, fisherman, and at least one other role — and the masters multiply: [[afanasy-nefeditch|Afanasy Nefeditch]], [[tatyana-vassilyevna|Tatyana Vassilyevna]], and still others, each buying and selling him like a piece of furniture, each assigning him a new function according to their momentary need.

## The Question of Identity

The narrator asks Sutchok a question that the serf literally cannot answer:

> "And how old are you?"
> "I don't know." (007-203–007-204)

He cannot know because he has never been the subject of his own life. His age — the most elementary datum of identity — has never mattered to anyone, least of all to himself. His mother is a shadow he barely remembers: "I had a mother ... but I don't remember her" (007-209–007-210). He has never been married because "the late mistress, the Kingdom of Heaven be hers, didn't allow anyone to marry" (007-218–007-219). His existence has been so entirely determined by the will of others that even his own memory has become unreliable — he recalls his mother, then corrects himself, as though his past belongs to someone else and he is merely repeating what he has been told.

This exchange is among the most radical in *A Sportsman's Sketches*: it demonstrates, without ever stating, that serfdom is not only an economic institution but an ontological one. It produces people who do not know their own age.

## Laughter and the Abyss

Sutchok is a comic figure, and Turgenev intends him to be funny — the absurd succession of roles, the cracked punt, the dreamy voice. But the comedy is of a particular Russian kind: laughter that opens onto the abyss. Sutchok's life is a catalogue of everything that can be taken from a person without quite killing him: home, trade, family, memory, age. What remains is the bare capacity to punt a boat and provide fish to a table that will never eat them. In his dreamlike acceptance, Sutchok is the serf reduced to his most elemental form.
