# Kalinitch

## Overview

Kalinitch (also spelled Kalinych) is one of the two central [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] figures in Turgenev's sketch "[[khor|Khor]] and Kalinich," the opening story of *A Sportsman's Sketches*. A serf belonging to the Kaluga [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[polutikin|Mr. Polutikin]], Kalinitch represents the romantic, nature-attuned, and spiritually gifted side of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] character. Where his best friend [[khor|Khor]] is a hard-headed rationalist who has built material prosperity, Kalinitch is a dreamer who lives from hand to mouth, wearing bast shoes and dwelling in a humble cottage — yet he is endowed with gifts that even the skeptical [[khor|Khor]] acknowledges: the ability to charm away illness, to keep bees that never die in winter, and to see the world with a sweet and luminous simplicity. Turgenev paints him as a figure of almost saint-like gentleness, as close to the rhythms of the forest and the seasons as any character in the *Sketches*.

## Appearance & Character

Kalinitch is described as a man of about forty, tall and thin, with a small, erect head and a good-humoured, swarthy face somewhat pitted with smallpox — a face that pleases the narrator "from the first glance" (001-130–001-131). He has a laughing twinkle in his light blue eyes and a habit of plucking at his scanty, wedge-shaped beard with his hand (001-138–001-139). He walks not rapidly but with long, easy strides, leaning lightly on a long thin staff — a gait that suggests a man at home in the open air (001-140–001-141). When observed at rest, carving a spoon on the threshold of his beehouse, his face strikes the narrator "as sweet and clear as an evening sky" (001-154–001-156).

Kalinitch's temperament "belonged to the order of idealists and dreamers, of romantic and enthusiastic spirits" (001-268–001-269). He is "a man of the merriest and gentlest disposition," constantly singing to himself in a low voice and looking carelessly about him with untroubled serenity (001-136–001-137). He has "no liking for argument, and believed in everything blindly" — a credulousness that marks his radical difference from the ironical [[khor|Khor]] (001-286). He expresses himself with warmth, though his speech lacks the polished fluency of a factory hand (001-278). When the narrator describes foreign wonders, Kalinitch exclaims "Ah, Lord, thy will be done!" — a response of wonder and piety rather than one of analytical curiosity, which is [[khor|Khor]]'s domain (001-342). Kalinitch is drawn to descriptions of nature — mountains, waterfalls, extraordinary buildings — while [[khor|Khor]] methodically inquires about systems of government (001-336–001-339).

Unlike the prosperous and patriarchal [[khor|Khor]], Kalinitch lives with striking simplicity. He wears shoes of bast, lives from hand to mouth, and "had once had a wife, whom he had been afraid of, and he had had no children" (001-272, 001-274). He can read — a fact that the illiterate [[khor|Khor]] notes with grudging admiration, observing that Kalinitch "has school-learning, and his bees never die in the winter" (001-354). This literacy sets Kalinitch apart from most [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s of his era and hints at an inner life beyond the material concerns that dominate [[khor|Khor]]'s world.

## Role in the Story

Kalinitch serves his master [[polutikin|Polutikin]] as a hunting companion, going out every day to carry his bag, sometimes his gun, to note where game is to be found, to fetch water, build hunting shanties, and gather wild strawberries (001-132–001-135). [[polutikin|Polutikin]] cannot stir a step without him, yet Kalinitch's devotion to his master comes at a cost: because he is constantly taken away from his land by the daily hunts, "he can't farm his land properly," as [[polutikin|Polutikin]] himself admits with casual indifference (001-163–001-165). This arrangement illuminates one of serfdom's cruelties — a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] with talents and a gentle nature is effectively prevented from building any independent livelihood, his labour commandeered for a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s leisure.

Yet Kalinitch is far from merely a victim. He is endowed with powers that command respect even from the rationalist [[khor|Khor]]: he can charm away haemorrhages, fits, madness, and worms; his bees always thrive; and "he had a light hand" — a folk expression for a touch that brings good fortune (001-280–001-282). [[khor|Khor]], ever the skeptic, asks him before the narrator to perform the ritual of introducing a newly bought horse to its stable, and "with scrupulous gravity Kalinitch carried out the old sceptic's request" (001-283–001-284). These folk-healing and apicultural gifts place Kalinitch in the ancient tradition of the Russian *znakhar* — the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] healer whose knowledge is rooted in an intuitive, pre-Christian communion with the natural world.

Kalinitch's beehouse, located in the very heart of the forest, is his sanctuary. It is a small hut hung round with bunches of dry scented herbs, where he makes guests comfortable on dry hay and cuts them fresh honeycomb from the hive (001-145–001-150). It is so clean that even the narrator remarks on it, to which [[khor|Khor]] replies with a sigh that "the bees would not live there else" (001-409–001-410). This beehouse — fragrant, orderly, hidden in the deep woods — is Kalinitch's kingdom, the space where his natural gifts can flourish outside the social hierarchies that constrain him.

His friendship with [[khor|Khor]] forms the emotional centre of the sketch. The relationship is founded on a profound complementarity: "[[khor|Hor]] loved Kalinitch, and took protecting care of him; Kalinitch loved and respected [[khor|Hor]]" (001-276–001-277). Kalinitch brings wild strawberries to [[khor|Khor]] as a gift — a "delicate attention" that surprises the narrator, who "had not expected such a delicacy on the part of a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]" (001-258, 001-261). In their good-natured arguments, Kalinitch defends their master [[polutikin|Polutikin]] while [[khor|Khor]] criticizes him — Kalinitch reveres authority where [[khor|Khor]] views it with ironic distance (001-275). When [[khor|Khor]] teases him about his bast shoes, noting [[polutikin|Polutikin]]'s meager two-copper payment, "Kalinitch turned away in vexation, but [[khor|Hor]] went off into a chuckle, during which his little eyes completely disappeared" (001-395–001-396). The scene captures the deep affection beneath their banter.

Kalinitch is also celebrated for his musical gifts: he "sang rather sweetly and played a little on the balalaeca" (001-397). As the story closes and the narrator departs with Kalinitch driving the trap, it is Kalinitch who reads the natural signs — the splashing ducks and the strong scent of the grass — and correctly predicts rain (001-423–001-424). As they drive into the copse, he begins "singing in an undertone as he was jolted up and down on the driver's seat," gazing at the sunset — a final image of the dreamer at peace in his element, one with the landscape he loves (001-425–001-426).

Through Kalinitch, Turgenev argues that the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] possesses not only practical intelligence but a deep spiritual and aesthetic sensibility. Kalinitch's inability to prosper under serfdom is not a sign of personal failure but an indictment of a system that can find no proper place for a man whose gifts are gentleness, song, and communion with the bees.
