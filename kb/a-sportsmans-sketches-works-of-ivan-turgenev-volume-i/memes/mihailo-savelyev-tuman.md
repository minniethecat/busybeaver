# Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)

## Overview

Mihailo Savelyev, universally known by his nickname Tuman ("Fog"), appears as one of the two old men fishing at [[ista-river|the Ista]] River in Turgenev's sketch "[[Raspberry Spring]]." He is a freed serf, now about seventy, who had served as steward to the late [[count-piotr-ilitch|Count Piotr Ilitch]] — a grandee of the Catherinian age whose lavish hospitality was legendary throughout the province. Tuman lives in the afterglow of that vanished world, embodying a type that Turgenev sketches with characteristic ambivalence: the old retainer whose identity is so bound to the master he served that freedom, when it comes, leaves him suspended between two eras, belonging fully to neither.

## Appearance and Manner

Tuman's physical presentation and habitual demeanor are described with affectionate precision:

> He was a man of about seventy, with a regular and pleasant face. He was almost continually smiling, as only men of the time of Catherine ever do smile—a smile at once stately and indulgent; in speaking, he slowly opened and closed his lips, winked genially with his eyes, and spoke slightly through his nose. He blew his nose and took snuff too in a leisurely fashion, as though he were doing something serious. (003-285–003-293)

Every detail — the stately smile, the nasal delivery, the ceremonial taking of snuff — marks Tuman as a man of the previous century. He has internalized the decorum of the great house so thoroughly that it has become indistinguishable from his own personality. Even in his reduced circumstances — living "with a consumptive Bolhovsky man, who kept an inn" (003-267–003-268) — he preserves the bearing of the count's steward.

## The Count's Steward

Tuman's life narrative is inseparable from that of [[count-piotr-ilitch|Count Piotr Ilitch]], whose ruined mansion — "an immense two-storied wooden house, completely deserted, with its roof falling in and its windows closely stuffed up" (003-271–003-273) — still stands near the highroad as a melancholy monument to a vanished world:

> Here there once lived Count Piotr Ilitch, a rich grandee of the olden time, renowned for his hospitality. At one time the whole province used to meet at his house, to dance and make merry to their heart's content to the deafening sound of a home-trained orchestra, and the popping of rockets and Roman candles. (003-274–003-279)

The count "long continued to give balls, and to walk about with an affable smile among the crowd of fawning guests; but his property, unluckily, was not enough to last his whole life" (003-279–003-282). Ruined, he went to [[petersburg|Petersburg]] seeking a post and "died in a room at a hotel, without having gained anything by his efforts" (003-283–003-286). Tuman's own freedom — "he had received his freedom already in the count's lifetime" (003-286–003-287) — is a detail of considerable significance: it suggests that the count, for all his extravagance, recognized some obligation to his loyal steward.

Tuman's recollections of the count's hospitality are vivid and tinged with both pride and gentle self-mockery:

> The late count—God's grace be with him!—was never, I must tell you, a huntsman himself, but he kept dogs, and twice a year he was pleased to go out with them. The huntsmen assembled in the courtyard in red caftans trimmed with galloon, and blew their horns; his excellency would be pleased to come out, and his horse would be led up; his excellency would mount, and the chief huntsman puts his feet in the stirrups, takes his hat off, and puts the reins in his hat to offer them to his excellency. (003-310–003-318)

The accumulation of ceremonial detail — the red caftans, the horns, the proffered reins in the hat — captures the elaborate theater of the Catherinian noble household. Tuman recounts it not with irony but with a connoisseur's appreciation for form, as though the aesthetics of the ritual mattered more than its substance.

## The Irony of Freedom

Tuman's present circumstances stand in pointed contrast to the grandeur he remembers. He is a freed man, but his freedom has left him economically marginal, dependent on the hospitality of an innkeeper. His conversation with [[Vlass]] — the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] whose son has died and who faces ruin — highlights the gulf between Tuman's sentimental attachment to the old order and the brutal reality of serfdom as [[vlass|Vlass]] experiences it. When [[vlass|Vlass]] recounts the master's cruelty, Tuman can only murmur: "Oh! things are in a sad way, brother [[vlass|Vlass]]" (003-259–003-260). It is the limit of his comprehension: he sees the sadness but not the system that produces it.

Tuman's nostalgia is not reactionary — he does not advocate for the return of serfdom — but it is politically inert. His freedom, granted as a personal favor, has given him no tools to understand collective oppression. He is a relic, a living fossil of a social order that has already crumbled, and Turgenev's portrait of him is suffused with the pathos of obsolescence.
