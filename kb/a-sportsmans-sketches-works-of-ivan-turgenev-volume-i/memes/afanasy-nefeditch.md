## Overview

Afanasy Nefeditch was a provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who purchased the estate of [[Lgov]] from [[Tatyana Vassilyevna]]. The serfs of [[lgov-place|Lgov]] subsequently passed from him by inheritance to his nephew [[Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev]] (007-173).

He appears exclusively through [[Sutchok]]'s autobiographical recollections. He is one link in a chain of masters reconstructed during the abortive duck-shooting expedition to the overgrown pond at [[Lgov]].

## Appearance and Character

No direct description of Afanasy Nefeditch survives in the text. He is a ghost in the genealogy of [[Lgov]]'s ownership, one name among several that emerge from [[Sutchok]]'s memory as the old fisherman traces his passage through half a dozen masters.

The absence of any characterising detail is itself significant. Turgenev does not grant him the eccentricities or vivid traits that distinguish other minor [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s in the Sketches. He exists purely as a legal entity—a name in a chain of transactions that treated people as chattel, remembered only because an elderly serf was asked the right question.

## Role in the Story

Afanasy Nefeditch enters the narrative during the narrator's conversation with [[Sutchok]] at the pond. The narrator asks whose cook the old fisherman was before his service to [[Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev]] (007-171).

[[Sutchok]] replies:

> "Oh, my former master's, Afanasy Nefeditch, Sergaï Sergiitch's uncle. Lgov was bought by him, by Afanasy Nefeditch, but it came to Sergaï Sergiitch by inheritance from him." (007-172)

The narrator then traces the chain further back, asking: "Whom did he buy it from?" The answer—"From [[tatyana-vassilyevna|Tatyana Vassilyevna]]" (007-176)—completes a three-generation ownership genealogy in five lines of dialogue.

The sequence—[[Tatyana Vassilyevna]] → Afanasy Nefeditch → [[Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev]]—forms a miniature history of provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] property transactions, in which human beings were transferred along with the land as assets.

[[Sutchok]] himself had previously belonged to [[Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev]], who owned the serfs only briefly: "he did not own us long; only six years altogether" (007-164).

His role in the broader architecture of [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] is characteristic of Turgenev's documentary method. A name surfaces from a serf's memory, and behind it stands an entire system of inheritance, purchase, and the passage of human beings from one master to the next.
