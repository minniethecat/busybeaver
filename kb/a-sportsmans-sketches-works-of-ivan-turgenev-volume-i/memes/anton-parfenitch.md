## Overview

Anton Parfenitch, known by the surname Funtikov, is a minor [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] in the district of [[Ovsyanikov]], mentioned in a single passage of "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." He extends a dinner invitation to the narrator through the intermediary [[Mitya]], but [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] dismisses him with characteristic bluntness as "that old tub" who serves costly fish with rancid butter. His cameo illustrates the dense social network of provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] — and [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s sharp, unforgiving judgment of his neighbours.

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Anton Parfenitch appears in the text; he is known only through his invitation and [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s dismissive epithet. The double naming — "Funtikov, Anton Parfenitch" (006-518) — suggests a man known familiarly by his surname but formally addressed by his Christian name and patronymic, a pattern typical of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s refusal to dine with him, delivered without hesitation, reveals both [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s independence of character and his disdain for those who combine pretension with meanness: "He gives you costly fish and puts rancid butter on it. God bless him!" (006-520).

## Role in the Story

Anton Parfenitch appears near the end of the long conversation between [[Ovsyanikov]] and the narrator. The young [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[mitya|Mitya]], who has been teasing [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] about his billiard-playing and petition-drafting, suddenly remembers: "I had almost forgotten — Funtikov, Anton Parfenitch asks you to dine with him next Sunday" (006-518). The invitation is delivered through [[mitya|Mitya]] as a social proxy, indicating that Anton Parfenitch moves in the same [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] circles.

[[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s immediate refusal — "I shan't go to see that old tub" (006-520) — is one of several moments in the chapter where the old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] proprietor distinguishes himself from the surrounding landowning class. Unlike the flattering, manoeuvring [[mitya|Mitya]] or the stingy Funtikov, [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] judges men by their character, not their station. The brief exchange also demonstrates the constant social activity of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]: dinners, visits, and invitations structure their existence even as [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] himself stands partly outside this world.

> 'I had almost forgotten — Funtikov, Anton Parfenitch asks you to dine with him next Sunday.' 'I shan't go to see that old tub. He gives you costly fish and puts rancid butter on it. God bless him!' (006-518)
