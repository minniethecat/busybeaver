## Overview

Annushka is a young girl of thirteen or fourteen who appears in "[[Kassyan of Fair Springs]]" gathering mushrooms in the forest. She is the ward of [[Kassyan]], who calls her a "relative," though her striking resemblance to him and the coachman [[Erofay]]'s hints strongly suggest she is his illegitimate daughter. [[kassyan|Kassyan]] has taught her to read—an extraordinary detail that illuminates his character as much as hers.

## Appearance and Character

Annushka is first glimpsed hiding behind a tree, peering at the narrator with black eyes before diving out of sight (009-521). When [[kassyan|Kassyan]] calls her out, her physical description is rendered with tenderness:

> [009-527] Annushka left her hiding place in silence, walked softly round—her little childish feet scarcely sounded on the thick grass—and came out of the bushes near the old man. She was not a child of seven, as I had fancied at first, from her diminutive stature, but a girl of thirteen or fourteen. Her whole person was small and thin, but very neat and graceful, and her pretty little face was strikingly like Kassyan's own, though he was certainly not handsome. (009-534)

The resemblance extends beyond features to manner: "There were the same thin features, and the same strange expression, shy and confiding, melancholy and shrewd, and her gestures were the same" (009-534). She speaks little—answering [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s questions about mushrooms with a shy smile and monosyllables—and blushes easily.

## Role in the Story

Annushka's brief appearance serves to deepen the portrait of [[kassyan|Kassyan]]. When she shows him her basket of mushrooms and he exclaims "Well done, Annushka!" (009-546), the narrator asks directly if she is his daughter. Annushka's face flushes faintly (009-548), and [[kassyan|Kassyan]] replies with "affected indifference" that she is "a relative" (009-549).

The tenderness [[kassyan|Kassyan]] displays toward her is unmistakable:

> [009-558] Kassyan looked after her, then looked down and smiled to himself. In this prolonged smile, in the few words he had spoken to Annushka, and in the very sound of his voice when he spoke to her, there was an intense, indescribable love and tenderness. (009-562)

Later, the coachman [[erofay|Erofay]] confirms the implication: Annushka "is an orphan; she has no mother, and it's not even known who her mother was. But she must be a relation; she's too much like him" (009-641). He adds that [[kassyan|Kassyan]] has taught Annushka to read (009-646)—a rare accomplishment for a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] girl, testifying to [[kassyan|Kassyan]]'s unusual character.

## Historical and Social Context

Annushka's situation—an orphan girl of uncertain parentage, living with a man who is probably her father but cannot or will not acknowledge her—reflects the precarious position of illegitimate children in rural Russian society. That [[kassyan|Kassyan]] has taught her to read despite their poverty marks him as exceptional among the [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] and aligns with his portrayal throughout the sketch as a man set apart from ordinary village life.
