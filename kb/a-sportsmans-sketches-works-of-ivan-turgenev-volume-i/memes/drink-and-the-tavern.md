# Drink and the Tavern

Drink runs through [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] as steadily as tea, but to a darker end. The tavern is where debts are settled and bargains struck, and vodka is the comfort and the undoing of huntsman and landowner alike.

Turgenev treats it without sermon. Drunkenness is simply part of the weather of Russian country life, sometimes comic, often wasteful, occasionally fatal.

## Yermolaï's Thirst

The huntsman [[Yermolaï]], for all his skill in the field, is ruled by his appetite for spirits:

> (002-093) ...he was excessively fond of drink, and never could sit still long.

## The Drunken Landowner Komov

Drink is no peasant vice alone. In [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]], the neighbour [[Stepan Niktopolionitch Komov|Komov]] is a menace precisely because he is a gentleman who drinks:

> (006-127) He was a drunken fellow, and fond of treating others.

His riotous life ends as absurdly as it was lived, in a fall taken while drunk:

> (006-153) ...in one of his drunken fits he fell off the pigeon-house.

## The Tavern as Fixture

The tavern is woven into the ordinary business of the countryside. A debt between peasant and merchant is closed over a drink:

> (001-296) ...he goes with the merchant to the tavern, and there the debt is settled.

And at the great horse-fair of [[lebedyan|Lebedyan]], the tavern sorts the crowd as neatly as rank itself:

> (014-085) ...hurrying to hotel or to tavern, according to their class.

## Social and Historical Context

The government's tax farm on spirits made the tavern a fixture of every settlement and drink a pillar of rural finance. For the peasant it was refuge and snare; for the gentry, as with Komov, a slower kind of self-destruction dressed as sociability.

Turgenev sets drink beside [[Food, Tea and Hospitality|tea and hospitality]] as its harsher twin: the same impulse to gather and treat, turned toward ruin instead of welcome.
