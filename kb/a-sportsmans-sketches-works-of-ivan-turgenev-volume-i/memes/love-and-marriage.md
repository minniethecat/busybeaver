# Love and Marriage

Love in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] rarely runs free. It is hemmed in by rank and by [[Serfdom|serfdom]], so that the wish to marry can become an act of rebellion and a passion long concealed can end in sudden flight.

Turgenev returns to the theme in several sketches, always with the same quiet attention to what love costs.

## Forbidden Love: Arina's Petition

In [[yermolai-and-the-millers-wife|Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]], the lady's maid Arina throws herself at her master's feet with a single request:

> (002-325) 'Your honour, Alexandr Selitch, I beseech a favour of you.' 'What favour?' 'Let me be married.'

The refusal is casual and absolute — her marriage would inconvenience her mistress:

> (002-328) 'Nonsense! nonsense! your mistress can't endure married lady's maids.'

## Radilov and Olga

In [[my-neighbour-radilov|My Neighbour Radilov]], a widower's grief slowly gives way to a hidden love for his dead wife's sister, [[Olga]]. The narrator learns the outcome only after the fact:

> (005-264) ...he had suddenly disappeared, left his mother, and gone away somewhere with his sister-in-law.

Looking back, he reads the passion he had missed in Olga's silent face:

> (005-268) ...it was breathing, not with sympathetic suffering only: it was burning with jealousy.

## Social and Historical Context

Whether it is a serf denied leave to marry or a gentleman fleeing with his sister-in-law, love in the *Sketches* collides with the rules that govern rank and property.

For the peasant, marriage needs a master's permission; for the gentry, desire must reckon with scandal and family. Turgenev treats both with the same unsentimental sympathy, showing love less as fulfilment than as one more pressure the social order strains against.
