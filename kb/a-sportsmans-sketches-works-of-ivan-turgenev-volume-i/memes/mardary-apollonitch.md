## Overview

[[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov]] is a country gentleman introduced in "[[Two Country Gentlemen]]," where he is paired in contrast with [[General Hvalinsky]] as one of two representative types of the Russian provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] (013-001, 013-139). A bachelor and owner of five hundred souls, he lives on his estate [[shipilovka|Shipilovka]] in a manner that embodies the old patriarchal style of serf-ownership — hospitable and good-natured on the surface, yet capable of casual cruelty that the narrator presents with pointed irony (013-146, 013-153).

## Appearance and Character

Mardary Apollonitch is a little, fattish, bald old man of respectable corpulence, with a double chin and little soft hands (013-141, 013-142). He has never been reckoned handsome, and bears no resemblance to [[general-viyatcheslav-ilarionovitch-hvalinsky|General Hvalinsky]] in either appearance or temperament (013-139, 013-141).

He is described as very hospitable and jovial, living, as the saying goes, for his comfort; summer and winter alike, he wears a striped wadded dressing-gown (013-143, 013-145). His manner of life is emphatically old-fashioned: his house smells of kvas, tallow candles, and leather, with a sideboard close at hand on the right (013-153, 013-155). He receives visitors very cordially and entertains them sumptuously — the narrator wryly notes that, thanks to the stupefying powers of Russian cookery, he deprives guests of all capacity for doing anything but playing preference after dinner (013-173, 013-174).

His temperament is that of the most good-natured of men — he is genuinely delighted to see any visitor (013-186, 013-187). Yet this geniality masks a deep complacency. When the narrator questions the miserable condition of his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' huts, Mardary Apollonitch deflects with vague complaints about land re-division, showing no sense of personal responsibility (013-249, 013-255).

## Role in the Story

Mardary Apollonitch appears in "[[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Two Country Gentlemen]]" as one half of the chapter's paired portrait of provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] types, contrasted with the pompous and official-minded [[general-viyatcheslav-ilarionovitch-hvalinsky|General Hvalinsky]] (013-001, 013-139). The narrator arrives at his estate one summer evening and is received with characteristic hospitality — a priest is visiting, vodka is summoned, and the narrator's hand is warmly clasped while Mardary Apollonitch insists the priest stay for a drink (013-182, 013-185, 013-189).

The chapter's most famous scene unfolds on the balcony after tea. Mardary Apollonitch suddenly flies into excitement over hens roaming in his garden:

> 'Whose hens are those? whose hens are those?' he shouted: 'Whose are those hens roaming about in the garden?... Whose are those hens? How many times I've forbidden it! How many times I've spoken about it!' (013-213, 013-215)

Immediately following this comic outburst, the sound of measured blows comes from the stable where the servant [[Vasya]] is being flogged. In the passage that has become one of the most quoted in the entire *Sketches*, Mardary Apollonitch pauses in the act of sipping tea:

> he stopped short, listened, nodded his head, sipped his tea, and laying the saucer on the table, with the most good-natured smile imaginable, he murmured as though involuntarily accompanying the blows: 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk! Tchuki-tchuk!' (013-272, 013-276, 013-278)

When the narrator stares at him, Mardary Apollonitch responds with serene Biblical authority: "Whom he loveth he chasteneth" (013-288, 013-289). This scene — the genial master murmuring nonsense syllables in rhythm with the beating of his serf — encapsulates the moral numbness that serfdom produced in otherwise amiable men.

## Historical/Social Context

Mardary Apollonitch represents the old-school Russian [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] of the pre-Reform era — [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s who managed their estates in traditional patriarchal fashion. His estate is under the superintendence of an agent, a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] with a beard that "covers the whole of his sheepskin coat" (013-167, 013-168). His interest in agricultural improvement is purely performative: he ordered a threshing-machine from [[moscow|Moscow]] "not to be behind the age," but keeps it locked in a shed, letting it sit idle while the old methods continue (013-147, 013-148). He drives out in a home-built coach weighing four tons, keeps thirty horses of various kinds, and maintains a multitude of servants dressed in old-fashioned blue full coats with high collars, shortish pantaloons, and yellow waistcoats, who address visitors as "father" (013-164, 013-166, 013-171, 013-172).

When confronted about the wretched condition of his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' hovels — cramped, treeless, with only one bad well — he blames the land re-division rather than his own neglect, and the narrator notes pointedly that the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' allotment had in fact been reduced (013-249, 013-252, 013-255, 013-257).
