## Overview

Alexy Romanitch is a deceased brigadier and former master of the [[shumihino|Shumihino]] estate, mentioned in [[Raspberry Spring]] in connection with the enigmatic figure of [[Styopushka]]. His sole distinction in the narrative is that he "brought home from a campaign" a Turkish woman who became part of the household—an act that places him among the generation of eighteenth-century Russian officers whose military service on the empire's southern frontiers brought exotic captives, customs, and bloodlines into the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]'s world (003-082).

## Appearance and Character

Alexy Romanitch does not appear directly in the narrative; he belongs to the remembered past, invoked by the old house-serf [[Trofimitch|grandfather Trofimitch]], who "knew all the pedigrees of all the house-serfs in the direct line to the fourth generation" (003-079). The narrator reports [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]]'s recollection—that [[Styopushka]] "was related to a Turkish woman whom the late master, the brigadier Alexy Romanitch had been pleased to bring home from a campaign in the baggage waggon" (003-082)—with the dry neutrality of a genealogist recording a footnote. The detail of the "baggage waggon" is telling: the Turkish woman was tran[[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]ed as spoils of war, an acquisition rather than a person.

The epithet "the late master" and the deferential phrase "had been pleased to" belong to [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]]'s idiom, not the narrator's, preserving the house-serf's instinctive reverence for authority even when reporting an act that modern readers recognise as trafficking.

## Role in the Story

Alexy Romanitch's role in [[Raspberry Spring]] is genealogical: his Turkish captive becomes [[Styopushka]]'s ancestor—or so [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]] claims—and this distant non-Russian lineage helps explain [[Styopushka]]'s utter marginality in the [[shumihino|Shumihino]] household. The narrator observes that [[Styopushka]] lives outside every social structure of the estate:

> "Even on holidays, days of general money-giving and of feasting on buckwheat dumplings and vodka, after the old Russian fashion—even on such days Styopushka did not put in an appearance at the trestle-tables nor at the barrels; he did not make his bow nor kiss the master's hand" (003-085)

The Turkish connection, however tenuous, marks [[Styopushka]] as doubly alien: neither fully Russian nor fully serf, belonging to no recognised category. Alexy Romanitch's casual act of bringing home a captive woman thus has consequences that ripple across generations, producing a human being who fits nowhere.

The brigadier himself exemplifies a type that recurs throughout [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]: the eighteenth-century military [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] whose service to the empire blurred the boundaries between warfare, slave-taking, and estate management. Like [[Count Piotr Ilitch]], whose ruined mansion and home-trained orchestra are recalled in the same chapter, Alexy Romanitch belongs to a vanished epoch of Catherinian grandeur, remembered only in the fading recollections of aged house-serfs.
