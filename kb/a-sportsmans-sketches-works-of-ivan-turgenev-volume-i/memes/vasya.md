## Overview

Vasya is a young servant who waits at sideboard in the household of [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov]] in "[[Two-Country-Gentlemen]]." (013-281) He is the subject of one of the most memorably chilling scenes in the collection: flogged on his master's orders while Stegunov peacefully drinks tea, Vasya afterward walks through the village cracking nuts and insists he deserved his punishment — a portrait of the moral deformation serfdom inflicts on both master and serf.

## Appearance and Character

Vasya has no detailed physical description beyond being identified as "he with the long whiskers" by his master. (013-284) His character emerges through two contrasting images: the unseen figure being beaten in the stable, and the cheerful young man cracking nuts in the village street. This juxtaposition is the scene's devastating effect.

## Role in the Story

### The Flogging Scene

The narrator is taking tea with [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] when a gust of wind brings the sound of rhythmically repeated blows from the stable. Stegunov, in the act of lifting his saucer to smell the tea, pauses, listens, nods his head, and murmurs in rhythm with the blows:

> 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk! Tchuki-tchuk!' (013-278)

When the narrator asks what is happening, Stegunov replies with the most good-natured smile:

> 'Oh, by my order, they're punishing a scamp of a fellow.… Do you happen to remember Vasya, who waits at the sideboard?' (013-281)

When the narrator stares at him in indignation, Stegunov quotes Scripture in self-justification: "'Whom he loveth he chasteneth'; you know that." (013-289)

### The Aftermath

A quarter of an hour later, driving through the village, the narrator encounters Vasya himself:

> I caught sight of Vasya. He was walking down the village street, cracking nuts. I told the coachman to stop the horses and called him up. (013-291)

The narrator asks whether he has been punished:

> 'How did you know?' answered Vasya. (013-295)

When asked what the punishment was for, Vasya replies:

> 'Oh, I deserved it, father; I deserved it. They don't punish for trifles among us; that's not the way with us — no, no. Our master's not like that; our master … you won't find another master like him in all the province.' (013-299)

The narrator drives on with the bitter observation: "'There you have it, old Russia!'" (013-303)

## Significance

The Vasya episode is one of Turgenev's most concentrated critiques of serfdom. In two pages, it illustrates: the master's casual cruelty (whipping ordered as routinely as tea is poured); the master's self-exculpating piety (Scripture twisted to justify violence); and most devastatingly, the serf's internalized degradation — Vasya not only accepts his punishment but praises the master who ordered it. The scene distills an entire moral universe into a teacup, a flogging, and a handful of nuts.
