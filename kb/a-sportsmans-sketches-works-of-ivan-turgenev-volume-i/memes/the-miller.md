# The Miller

## Overview

The Miller — Savely Alexyevitch — is the husband of [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina Timofeyevna]] and the proprietor of the mill at [[zheltonhiny|Zheltonhiny]] in Turgenev's sketch "[[yermolai|Yermolai]] and the Miller's Wife" (Chapter II). He appears briefly but decisively: a corpulent, practical man who grants the narrator and [[yermolai|Yermolai]] permission to sleep in an outhouse by the river, runs to wake his wife to provide provisions, and later calls her back from her conversation with the narrator. His role is framed entirely through his marriage to [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] — a woman whose freedom he purchased from her former master — and through the commercial logic that governs his world.

## Appearance and Character

The Miller is described with blunt physicality: "a tall, fat-faced man with a bull-neck, round-bellied and corpulent" (002-185). He is a man of substance in every sense — physically imposing, economically established. When [[yermolai|Yermolai]] and the narrator knock at the mill gate, the Miller appears only after being consulted by his workman; he does not rush. He agrees to the narrator's proposal to sleep in the open air, provides straw and hay, and sends his wife with milk, eggs, potatoes, and bread (002-193). These provisions are matter-of-fact hospitality, neither warm nor grudging — the hospitality of a man who knows the value of everything.

The Miller is characterised chiefly by what [[yermolai|Yermolai]] says of him: when asked why the Miller did not let them into the cottage, [[yermolai|Yermolai]] answers simply, "He's afraid" — and [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s rebuke, "Afraid! the fat old tub!" (002-222), captures the Miller's temperament concisely: cautious, proprietary, suspicious of strangers. He is the keeper of a boundary, and he guards it.

## The Purchase of Arina

The most significant fact about the Miller is not directly narrated; it emerges from the conversation between the narrator and [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]. When the narrator asks how she came to marry the Miller, [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] replies:

> 'They bought my freedom.'
> 'Who?'
> 'Savely Alexyevitch.'
> 'Who is that?'
> 'My husband.' (002-265)

The purchase of a serf woman's freedom for the purpose of marriage is a transaction that sits uneasily between liberation and acquisition. The Miller bought [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]], and in doing so freed her from her master — but the transaction also made her his wife, and the freedom granted is immediately enclosed within another form of dependence. The Miller's motives are not explored; [[yermolai|Yermolai]]'s laconic explanation — "She can read and write; in their business it's of use. I suppose he liked her" (002-268–002-269) — suggests a mixture of practical calculation and genuine affection, the proportions of which remain opaque.

## Role in the Story

The Miller's role in "[[yermolai|Yermolai]] and the Miller's Wife" is essentially structural: he is the gatekeeper who permits the encounter between the narrator and [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]], and the husband who terminates it. When the narrator, moved by [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]'s story, attempts to ask her more, the Miller's voice cuts through the darkness: "'[[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]!' cried the miller from a distance. She got up and walked away" (002-264). The conversation is over; the husband's authority reasserts itself without drama or hesitation. The Miller's world is one of clear lines — guests sleep in the outhouse, wives obey when called, and business continues in the morning.

When [[yermolai|Yermolai]] is asked whether the Miller is "a good fellow," his answer — "So-so" (002-266) — is the final word on the subject. The Miller is neither a villain nor a hero; he is a man who operates the mill, buys what he needs, and expects the world to run on the same practical principles.

## Historical/Social Context

The Miller represents the small but growing class of commercial [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s in pre-Reform Russia — men who, through trade or industry, accumulated sufficient capital to interact with the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] economy on their own terms. The purchase of a serf's freedom was a legal possibility, but expensive and rare; that the Miller could afford to buy [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]'s freedom testifies to his relative prosperity. At the same time, the transaction underscores the commodification of human beings under serfdom: [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] passes from one form of ownership to another, and while marriage is in theory a free contract, her entrance into it was mediated entirely by purchase.
