# Arina Timofeyevna

## Overview

Arina Timofeyevna appears briefly in "[[Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]]," yet her story forms the emotional centre of the sketch. She is the [[the-miller|miller]]'s wife — a pale, sad woman who emerges from the darkness to serve [[yermolai|Yermolaï]] a glass of spirits and then sits silently on a tub, her head drooping, while the conversation unfolds around her. Through a long digression, the narrator reveals her history: she was once a serf-maid in the household of Mr. [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]], a [[petersburg|Petersburg]] gentleman who stole her from her village, and whose sentimental, spiteful wife brutalized her for the crime of falling in love. Now she is a freed woman married to a [[byelev|Byelev]] townsman, but the past continues to haunt her body — she coughs at night, and her dejected posture testifies to a lifetime of having no right to her own happiness. Arina's tragedy is not that she suffered, but that she suffered for nothing more than being young, beautiful, and capable of love — and that her suffering was inflicted by a woman who was, by her husband's account, "simply devoted to her."

## First Appearance

The narrator first encounters Arina at night, on the stoop of a mill after a long day's hunting. She appears as a figure of stillness and silence:

> Arina returned with a small flask and a glass. Yermolaï got up, crossed himself, and drank it off at a draught. "Good!" was his comment. (002-228–002-229)

Her responses to [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s questions are monosyllabic. When he asks if she is still ill, she answers simply "Yes" (002-232). When he advises her not to see a doctor — "it will be worse if you do" (002-236) — she replies "Well, I am not going" (002-237). The exchange is almost ceremonial in its flatness, the ritual of a woman who has nothing left to give language.

When the narrator himself addresses her, asking how long she has kept the mill, her answer is precise and impersonal: "It's two years since I came on Trinity day" (002-251). But when asked who she belonged to, her reply — "I'm a serf; I was a serf" (002-257) — contains the entire shape of her past: the present tense before the revision, as though she still cannot quite believe she is free.

## The Zvyerkoff Story

The sketch's long central digression is the narrator's recollection of Arina's former master, Mr. [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]], a man who "had a rather influential position, and was reputed a man of sense and education" (002-269–002-270). [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]] once told the narrator the story himself, entirely without shame, as an illustration of what fine people [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s could be if properly trained:

> "We were driving once through our village, it must be — let me be correct — yes, fifteen years ago. We saw, at the bailiff's, a young girl, his daughter, very pretty indeed... And my wife said to me: 'Kokó — you understand, of course, that is her pet name for me — let us take this girl to Petersburg; I like her, Kokó.'" (002-301–002-306)

[[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]] recounts the story with the self-satisfaction of a man performing a kindness. Arina was taken from her family, her village, everything she knew, and trained as a waiting-maid. "And what do you think? The girl made wonderful progress; my wife became simply devoted to her" (002-313–002-314). The language of benevolence masks the reality of abduction.

The catastrophe comes when Arina commits the crime of love. She asks [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]]'s wife for permission to marry the footman [[petrushka|Petrushka]]:

> "Picture to yourself, Arina — her name was Arina — rushes unannounced into my wife's room... My wife was indignant. 'Arina,' she said, 'you have shamelessly forgotten yourself... Here am I, having clothed and fed you since you were a child, letting you take your place in my own household, looking after you as a mother does her own daughter — what do you say to that?'" (002-321–002-331)

The refusal is absolute, and the punishment — Arina is sent back to the village in disgrace, her hair cropped, dressed in [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] clothes — reduces her to an object lesson in the cost of female desire under serfdom.

> "Well, I got rid of Arina. I sent her home to the village... Of course, I confess, I was a good deal upset. Well, well, well! what are you to do, though? The worst of it was, I had no peace for long after in my own house." (002-334–002-335)

[[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]]'s final concern is not for Arina — married off to a [[the-miller|miller]], her health broken — but for the inconvenience her suffering caused him.

## The Body as Record

Arina's body carries the history of her abuse. "My cough troubles me at night" (002-234), she tells [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]. She is pale, her head hangs "dejectedly" (002-239), and Turgenev specifies that her face still retains traces of former beauty — "a face that must once have been handsome" — making the visible marks of time, illness, and suffering readable as a palimpsest of what was taken from her. When the narrator tells her he knows her former master, "she replied in a low voice, and her head drooped" (002-266). The gesture is the same one she will repeat throughout the scene: the body's language of submission that has become permanent.

## Historical Significance

Arina's story illuminates a dimension of serfdom that Turgenev's male-centered sketches often understate: the particular vulnerability of serf-women to the whims of [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] women. Madame [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]]'s cruelty is not the impersonal brutality of the whip but the intimate violence of the household — the mistress who demands gratitude for having "clothed and fed you since you were a child" and who interprets a maid's desire for marriage as personal betrayal. The domestic space of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] house, celebrated in the literature of the period as a sanctuary of feminine virtue, is revealed in Arina's story as a theatre of absolute power, where the mistress's moods have the force of law and a serf-woman's body is property twice over — the master's, and the mistress's.
