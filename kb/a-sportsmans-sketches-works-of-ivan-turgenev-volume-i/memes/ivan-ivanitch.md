## Overview

Ivan Ivanitch is a deceased [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] whose restless spirit haunts the ravine of [[varnavitsi|Varnavitsi]] in [[Byezhin Prairie]]. He appears only within the embedded folk narrative told by the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boy [[Ilyusha]] around the night campfire—a ghost story within a story that exemplifies the rich oral culture of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]]. His spectral search for a magical herb reveals the persistence of pre-Christian folk belief beneath the surface of rural Orthodoxy (008-419).

## Appearance and Character

Ivan Ivanitch never appears in person; he is encountered only as a ghost. [[Ilyusha]] describes him through the testimony of [[Trofimitch|grandfather Trofimitch]], who once met the apparition at [[varnavitsi|Varnavitsi]]:

> "He wears, they say, a long skirted coat, and keeps groaning like this, and looking for something on the ground." (008-421)

When [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]] boldly addresses him, the ghost speaks "so thickly, so thickly" (008-430), his voice muffled as if by the weight of the earth. The detail of the long-skirted coat—the characteristic dress of an eighteenth-century [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]—anchors the supernatural in a recognisable social type, making the apparition at once eerie and familiar.

## Role in the Story

Ivan Ivanitch figures in one of the ghost stories that the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys trade through the night on [[Byezhin Prairie]]. After [[Fedya]] remarks that he has heard [[varnavitsi|Varnavitsi]] is haunted, [[Ilyusha]] eagerly confirms it, recounting how old [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]] encountered the late master there:

> "What," says he, "your honour, Ivan Ivanitch, are you pleased to look for on the ground?" (008-423)

[[fedya|Fedya]] interrupts in amazement—"He asked him?" (008-425)—underscoring the audacity of addressing a ghost directly. [[Ilyusha]] continues: the ghost replies that he is looking for "the herb that cleaves all things" (008-429), a folkloric plant believed to unlock any barrier. When [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]] presses further, Ivan Ivanitch answers with the story's most haunting line:

> "The tomb weighs on me; it weighs on me, Trofimitch: I want to get away—away." (008-431)

This lament—a dead man crushed by his own grave, desperate for release—is one of the most striking images in the entire cycle of campfire tales. The ghost's quest for the cleaving herb may be read as a metaphor for the unquiet conscience of the serf-owning class, whose sins bind them to the earth even after death. [[Fedya]]'s curt response—"he didn't enjoy his life enough, I suppose" (008-433)—brings the story back to the pragmatic world of the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, but the spectral image lingers.

Ivan Ivanitch's ghost story also serves a structural role in [[Byezhin Prairie]]: it follows the terrifying tale of the drowned man and precedes the discussion of All Hallows' Eve portents, contributing to the chapter's cumulative atmosphere of nocturnal dread.
