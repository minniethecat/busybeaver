# THE COUNTING-HOUSE

[011-001]It was autumn. For some hours I had been strolling across country with

[011-002]my gun, and should probably not have returned till evening to the

[011-003]tavern on the Kursk high-road where my three-horse trap was awaiting

[011-004]me, had not an exceedingly fine and persistent rain, which had worried

[011-005]me all day with the obstinacy and ruthlessness of some old maiden lady,

[011-006]driven me at last to seek at least a temporary shelter somewhere in the

[011-007]neighbourhood. While I was still deliberating in which direction to go,

[011-008]my eye suddenly fell on a low shanty near a field sown with peas. I

[011-009]went up to the shanty, glanced under the thatched roof, and saw an old

[011-010]man so infirm that he reminded me at once of the dying goat Robinson

[011-011]Crusoe found in some cave on his island. The old man was squatting on

[011-012]his heels, his little dim eyes half-closed, while hurriedly, but

[011-013]carefully, like a hare (the poor fellow had not a single tooth), he

[011-014]munched a dry, hard pea, incessantly rolling it from side to side. He

[011-015]was so absorbed in this occupation that he did not notice my entrance.

[011-016]'Grandfather! hey, grandfather!' said I. He ceased munching, lifted his

[011-017]eyebrows high, and with an effort opened his eyes.

[011-018]'What?' he mumbled in a broken voice.

[011-019]'Where is there a village near?' I asked.

[011-020]The old man fell to munching again. He had not heard me. I repeated my

[011-021]question louder than before.

[011-022]'A village?... But what do you want?'

[011-023]'Why, shelter from the rain.'

[011-024]'What?'

[011-025]'Shelter from the rain.'

[011-026]'Ah!' (He scratched his sunburnt neck.) 'Well, now, you go,' he said

[011-027]suddenly, waving his hands indefinitely, 'so ... as you go by the

[011-028]copse--see, as you go--there'll be a road; you pass it by, and keep

[011-029]right on to the right; keep right on, keep right on, keep right on....

[011-030]Well, there will be Ananyevo. Or else you'd go to Sitovka.'

[011-031]I followed the old man with difficulty. His moustaches muffled his

[011-032]voice, and his tongue too did not obey him readily.

[011-033]'Where are you from?' I asked him.

[011-034]'What?'

[011-035]'Where are you from?'

[011-036]'Ananyevo.'

[011-037]'What are you doing here?'

[011-038]'I'm watchman.'

[011-039]'Why, what are you watching?'

[011-040]'The peas.'

[011-041]I could not help smiling.

[011-042]'Really!--how old are you?'

[011-043]'God knows.'

[011-044]'Your sight's failing, I expect.'

[011-045]'What?'

[011-046]'Your sight's failing, I daresay?'

[011-047]'Yes, it's failing. At times I can hear nothing.'

[011-048]'Then how can you be a watchman, eh?'

[011-049]'Oh, my elders know about that.'

[011-050]'Elders!' I thought, and I gazed not without compassion at the poor old

[011-051]man. He fumbled about, pulled out of his bosom a bit of coarse bread,

[011-052]and began sucking it like a child, with difficulty moving his sunken

[011-053]cheeks.

[011-054]I walked in the direction of the copse, turned to the right, kept on,

[011-055]kept right on as the old man had advised me, and at last got to a large

[011-056]village with a stone church in the new style, \_i.e.\_ with columns, and

[011-057]a spacious manor-house, also with columns. While still some way off I

[011-058]noticed through the fine network of falling rain a cottage with a deal

[011-059]roof, and two chimneys, higher than the others, in all probability the

[011-060]dwelling of the village elder; and towards it I bent my steps in the

[011-061]hope of finding, in this cottage, a samovar, tea, sugar, and some not

[011-062]absolutely sour cream. Escorted by my half-frozen dog, I went up the

[011-063]steps into the outer room, opened the door, and instead of the usual

[011-064]appurtenances of a cottage, I saw several tables, heaped up with

[011-065]papers, two red cupboards, bespattered inkstands, pewter boxes of

[011-066]blotting sand weighing half a hundred-weight, long penholders, and so

[011-067]on. At one of the tables was sitting a young man of twenty with a

[011-068]swollen, sickly face, diminutive eyes, a greasy-looking forehead, and

[011-069]long straggling locks of hair. He was dressed, as one would expect, in

[011-070]a grey nankin coat, shiny with wear at the waist and the collar.
