## Overview

Count Alexey Grigoryevitch Orlov-Tchesmensky (1737–1808) was a real historical figure — a Russian military commander, statesman, and brother of Catherine the Great's favourite Grigory Orlov — who appears in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" through the nostalgic recollections of the elderly [[Ovsyanikov]]. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], who saw him in [[moscow|Moscow]] as a boy of sixteen, remembers him as the greatest of the grandees he witnessed: "no one came up to" him (006-168). His portrait serves as a foil to the petty, self-serving [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s own day.

## Appearance and Character

Orlov-Tchesmensky possessed an overwhelming personal presence. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] recalls: "His figure alone was worth something, and his strength, and the look in his eyes!" (006-173). Before meeting him, one was "afraid, overawed indeed; but directly you came near him he was like sunshine warming you up and making you quite cheerful" (006-175). He allowed "every man access to him in person" (006-176) — a striking contrast to the distant young noblemen of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s present.

His temper could be terrifying: "When he got angry, it was like a clap of thunder. Everyone was in a great fright, but there was nothing to weep over; look round a minute after, and he was all smiles again!" (006-188). This combination of fearsome power and swift forgiveness is central to the idealized portrait [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] constructs.

## Role in the Story

Orlov-Tchesmensky appears as a remembered ideal in the long conversation between [[Ovsyanikov]] and the narrator. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s uncle served as a steward in the count's service, giving the old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] proprietor intimate knowledge of the grandee's character. He describes the count's passion for [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]: driving races where he "would never get in front at the start, so as not to offend his adversary" (006-179), his prized tumbler-pigeons watched through their reflection in a silver basin of water (006-182), and his lavish generosity — "beggars and poor people were fed in hundreds at his expense" (006-186).

The story culminates in the account of a legendary hunting party the count organized for "all the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]smen in the whole empire" (006-198). [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s grandfather brought his famous hound Milovidka, who outran every other dog. When the count offered to buy her, the grandfather refused — "I am not a tradesman; I don't sell anything for filthy lucre; for your sake I am ready to part with my wife even, but not with Milovidka" (006-206). The count's response was to praise him: "I like you for it" (006-209).

> 'He was a grand gentleman! Such stateliness, such gracious condescension you can't imagine! and it's impossible to describe it.' (006-170)

## Historical and Social Context

Orlov-Tchesmensky is the only named historical figure of national importance in the *Sketches*. His inclusion serves a deliberate contrast: unlike the [[Stepan Niktopolionitch Komov|Komovs]], [[Sofron|Sofrons]], and other petty tyrants of the contemporary countryside, the grandees of Catherine's era — in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s memory — understood that power required magnanimity. The narrator draws the moral explicitly: "Then Alexey Grigoryevitch did not oppress anyone" (006-214). [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s reply is elegiac: "Yes, it is always like that; those who can only just keep themselves afloat are the ones to drag others under" (006-215).
