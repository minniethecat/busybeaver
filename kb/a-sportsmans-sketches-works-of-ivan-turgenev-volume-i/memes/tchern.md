# Tchern

## Overview

The Tchern district, located in the [[tula-province|province of Tula]], is the geographical starting point of Turgenev's celebrated sketch "[[Byezhin Prairie]]." The chapter opens on a day of perfect summer weather: "On just such a day I was once out grouse-shooting in the Tchern district of the [[tula-province|province of Tula]]" (008-036). From this hunting expedition in the Tchern, the narrator becomes lost at dusk, wanders into unfamiliar terrain, and eventually stumbles upon the group of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys around their night fire on the [[Byezhin Prairie]] — setting the stage for one of Russian literature's most famous evocations of childhood, folklore, and the uncanny.

## Description

The Tchern district is introduced through Turgenev's atmospheric prelude to "[[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]," in which he describes the ideal July day. The air is "pure and dry," scented with "wormwood, rye in blossom, and buckwheat," and "even an hour before nightfall there is no moisture in the air" (008-036). It is, the narrator notes, "for such weather that the farmer longs, for harvesting his wheat" (008-036). Into this pastoral perfection the narrator sets out to shoot grouse — and soon fills his game-bag so full that it "cut my shoulder mercilessly" (008-036).

The narrator's departure from the Tchern hunting grounds, however, leads him astray. As twilight thickens, he misses his way and instead of the familiar plain with "the oakwood on the right and the little white church in the distance," he finds himself confronted by "a scene completely different, and quite new to me" — a narrow valley with a "dense wood of aspen-trees" rising like a wall (008-036). This disorientation, beginning in the known landscape of the Tchern and ending in the eerie unfamiliarity of the [[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]] at night, is the hinge on which the entire sketch turns.

## Role in the Stories

The Tchern district functions as the narrative's point of departure — the last familiar landmark before the narrator's descent into the twilight world of the [[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]. Its brief mention establishes a concrete geography: [[tula-province|Tula Province]], near its border with Orel, in countryside of cultivated fields interspersed with woods. The narrator's misdirection — he "kept too much to the right" — transforms a routine grouse-shooting expedition into one of the most atmospheric narratives in Russian literature. The Tchern thus represents the daylight world of purposeful activity (hunting, farming, orientation by known landmarks) that the narrator leaves behind as he enters the night-world of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] children, ghost stories, and the vast uncultivated prairie.
