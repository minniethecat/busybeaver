# Ovsyanikov

## Overview

Luka Petrovitch Ovsyanikov is the title character of Turgenev's sketch "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" and appears briefly at the close of "[[My Neighbour Radilov]]." Unlike the serfs who dominate the *Sketches*' [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] gallery — [[Khor]], [[Kalinitch]], [[Kassyan]] — Ovsyanikov is a free [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] proprietor, a man who owns his land and owes no master. At seventy years old, he embodies the old Russian patriarchal virtues: dignity, deliberation, moral authority, and a rootedness in tradition that makes him a living link to the world before Peter the Great. Turgenev presents him as a kind of moral baseline against which the degeneracy of the modern [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] can be measured.

## Appearance and Manner

Ovsyanikov's physical description opens his sketch as a portrait in the grand manner:

> "Picture to yourselves, gentle readers, a stout, tall man of seventy, with a face reminding one somewhat of the face of Kriloff, clear and intelligent eyes under overhanging brows, dignified in bearing, slow in speech, and deliberate in movement: there you have Ovsyanikov." (006-001–006-004)

He wears "an ample blue overcoat with long sleeves, buttoned all the way up, a lilac silk-handkerchief round his neck, brightly polished boots with tassels, and altogether resembled in appearance a well-to-do merchant" (006-005–006-007). His hands are "handsome, soft, and white" — the hands of a man who has risen above manual labour while remaining a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]].

Turgenev's most striking comparison is historical: "Ovsyanikov reminded me of the Russian boyars of the times before Peter the Great... The national holiday dress would have suited him well" (006-011–006-012). To compare a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] to a boyar was a bold gesture in 1850s Russia, and it signals Turgenev's belief that the old [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] preserved virtues the modern [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] had lost.

## Way of Life

Ovsyanikov "adhered to the antique usages, not from superstition (he was naturally rather independent in mind), but from habit" (006-030–006-031). He napped after dinner, visited the bath-house on Saturdays, read only religious books, and addressed his wife with old-fashioned formality. His household is ordered by rhythms that seem to belong to a vanished century.

Yet he is no reactionary fossil. He "did not pass for a wealthy man" but lives comfortably on his own land (006-021), an exception to the general poverty of the free [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]]. He breaks in his own horses, and when a spirited animal throws him over a precipice, he rises with the calm observation: "I told you so" (006-068–006-069). His physical courage matches his moral composure.

His wife, [[tatyana-ilyinitchna|Tatyana Ilyinitchna]], is described as "a tall woman, dignified and taciturn, always dressed in a cinnamon-coloured silk dress. She had a cold air" (006-070–006-071). They have no children, and the marriage, like everything about Ovsyanikov, is characterized by a certain austere completeness.

## The Arbitrator

Ovsyanikov's social function is what sets him apart from other [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] figures in the *Sketches*. "Thanks to his skill in winning respect, many of his neighbours had recourse to Ovsyanikov as arbitrator and mediator between them, and they almost always acquiesced in his decision, and listened to his advice" (006-052–006-054). In a society where [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s have no formal legal standing, Ovsyanikov has carved out an informal jurisdiction grounded in personal authority rather than law. The [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] come to him because they recognize a probity they themselves lack.

This authority is expressed in his speech, which is slow, measured, and punctuated by silences that carry weight. When asked about the changes in Russia, he observes with characteristic balance: "we lived more easily; there was a greater abundance of everything... All the same, things are better now, and they will be better still" (006-085–006-086). He is neither a nostalgic nor a utopian — merely a man who has lived long enough to see both loss and gain.

## The Link Between Sketches

Ovsyanikov's brief appearance at the end of "My Neighbour [[radilov|Radilov]]" connects the two sketches: "a tall, broad-shouldered and stoutly made man, the peasant proprietor, Ovsyanikov, came into the room" (005-256–005-257). The narrator breaks off to promise that "Ovsyanikov is such a remarkable and original personage that, with the reader's permission, we will put off speaking about him till the next sketch" (005-258–005-260). This narrative gesture — the promise of a fuller portrait to come — suggests Turgenev's awareness that Ovsyanikov requires a sketch of his own.
