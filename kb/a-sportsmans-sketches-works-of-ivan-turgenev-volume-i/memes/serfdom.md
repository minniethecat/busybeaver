## Overview

Serfdom is the defining social institution depicted throughout Turgenev's "A Sportsman's Sketches." Under this system, which persisted in Russia until its abolition in 1861, [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s were legally bound to the land and to their masters. They owed labour (barshchina) or rent (obrok) to their landlords, and their lives, livelihoods, and even their bodies were subject to the master's will. Turgenev, writing in the late 1840s and early 1850s, used the hunter-narrator's travels to expose the human realities of serfdom -- not through polemic, but through vivid portraiture of individual [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and the relationships that bound them to their owners. The sketches are widely credited with helping to shift public opinion against the institution, and Tsar Alexander II is said to have acknowledged the book's influence on his decision to emancipate the serfs.

## In Turgenev's Stories

The opening sketch, "[[khor|Hor]] and [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]]," immediately establishes serfdom as the backdrop for every character interaction. The Orel [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] is described as one who "labours as a serf in the fields" (001-006), contrasting sharply with the rent-paying Kaluga [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] who engages in trade and wears boots on holidays (001-008). This distinction between serf-labour and rent-paying arrangements runs through the entire chapter. The landlord [[polutikin|Polutikin]] owns [[khor|Hor]], a shrewd [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] who negotiated a special arrangement: after his cottage burned down, [[khor|Hor]] asked to settle in the forest and pay rent rather than perform compulsory labour (001-114). [[khor|Hor]] has grown prosperous under this arrangement, yet when [[polutikin|Polutikin]] urges him to buy his freedom, [[khor|Hor]] demurs, saying he has no money (001-121). Later, in private conversation, [[khor|Hor]] gives a more nuanced explanation: he knows his master and his rent, and within the system as it stands, he has carved out a bearable existence (001-187). The narrator himself presses the question -- "It's always better to be free" -- and [[khor|Hor]]'s equivocal reply reveals the deep entanglement of dependency, pragmatism, and fear that serfdom produced in even its most capable victims (001-189). Elsewhere in the same sketch, the casual brutality of the system surfaces in an aside: [[polutikin|Polutikin]] is called away because his neighbour Pitchukoff has "flogged a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman" over a land dispute (001-169), a reminder that the master's authority extended to corporal punishment without legal recourse.

## Social/Historical Context

Serfdom in Russia was formalised in the Ulozhenie of 1649, which bound [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s to the land. By the early 19th century, roughly half of the Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]] were serfs owned by the [[landowning-gentry|nobility]]. The "obrok" (quit-rent) and "barshchina" (labour obligation) were the two principal forms of exploitation. Reformist sentiment grew after the Napoleonic Wars and the Decembrist uprising of 1825, but it was the moral pressure exerted by literary works -- above all Turgenev's "Sketches" and Gogol's "Dead Souls" -- that brought the human cost of serfdom into educated public consciousness. Turgenev himself, born into a landowning family, drew on firsthand observation; his mother was known for her harsh treatment of serfs. The emancipation of 1861 formally freed 23 million serfs, but the economic and social legacies of the system persisted for generations.
