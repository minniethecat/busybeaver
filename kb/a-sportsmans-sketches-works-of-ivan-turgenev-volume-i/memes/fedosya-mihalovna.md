## Overview

Fedosya Mihalovna is a serf woman from the village of [[mikulino|Mikulino]] who appears in [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] through a conversation between the narrator's companion and [[Luka Petrovitch Ovsyanikov]]. A [[moscow|Moscow]]-trained dress-maker whose [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] Garpentchenko recalled her from the city without providing her any work, she is left in enforced idleness while being refused the chance to buy her freedom. Her case is a compact illustration of the arbitrary power of serf-owners over the economic lives of their human property (006-522).

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Fedosya Mihalovna appears in the text; she is known entirely through the report of the narrator's companion. What emerges is the portrait of a capable, industrious, and economically self-sufficient woman. She "lived in [[moscow|Moscow]] as a dress-maker, paying her service in money, and she paid her service-money accurately—a hundred and eighty two-roubles and a half a year" (006-525). The precise figure—182.5 roubles—underscores the documentary exactness of the account: this is a woman who fulfilled every financial obligation imposed by her servitude. The narrator's interlocutor further vouches for her professional competence, noting "she knows her business; she got good orders in [[moscow|Moscow]]" (006-528).

## Role in the Story

Fedosya Mihalovna never appears on stage. She is introduced when the narrator's companion, while visiting [[Luka Petrovitch Ovsyanikov]], drops her name into the conversation: "And I met Fedosya Mihalovna" (006-522). What follows is a rapid-fire exchange that lays out her situation with the economy of a legal brief: Garpentchenko, the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who bought [[mikulino|Mikulino]] at auction, has "written for her back, and he retains her here, but does not provide any duties for her" (006-529). She wishes to buy her freedom and "has spoken to the master, but he will not give any decisive answer" (006-531). The narrator's companion asks [[Luka Petrovitch Ovsyanikov]]—who happens to be acquainted with Garpentchenko—to intercede, noting that "Fedosya would give a good price for her freedom" (006-533).

The vignette is one of several in [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] that anatomise the dysfunctions of serfdom from multiple angles. In Fedosya's case, the absurdity is stark: a skilled woman who paid her dues in full is forcibly removed from her livelihood, held in idleness, and denied the exit she is willing to purchase. Her story is the obverse of the more famous narratives of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] misery—it is the story of a serf who has done everything right and is still blocked by the caprice of a single proprietor.
