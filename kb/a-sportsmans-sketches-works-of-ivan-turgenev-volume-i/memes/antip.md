# Antip

## Overview

Antip is an old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] on the estate of [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]] who appears in Turgenev's sketch "The Agent" (Chapter X). His brief but devastating appearance — he and his young son waylay the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s carriage to beg for protection from the bailiff [[sofron|Sofron]] — is one of the most direct depictions of estate oppression in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. Antip's complaint is simple and total: [[sofron|Sofron]] has ruined him, taken his sons for recruits out of turn, seized his last cow, and beaten his wife. His appeal to the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] is met with indifference and blame, and the narrator, witnessing the scene, is left with the knowledge that Antip will be "eaten up" entirely.

## The Complaint

The encounter occurs as Arkady Pavlitch, accompanied by the narrator, is driving through his estate. An old man and a boy appear on the road and bow to the ground. The old man speaks with difficulty:

> 'Who worries you?'
> 'Sofron Yakovlitch, your honour.'
> ...
> 'Well! and how has he tormented you?'
> 'Your honour, he has ruined us utterly. Two sons, your honour, he's sent for recruits out of turn, and now he is taking the third also. Yesterday, your honour, our last cow was taken from the yard, and my old wife was beaten by his worship here: that is all the pity he has for us!' (010-380–010-386)

The catalogue of abuses is precise and cumulative: conscription (the most feared penalty of serf life, removing young men from their families for twenty-five years of military service), theft of livestock (the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s only source of subsistence), and physical violence against the elderly. Antip's plea — "Let him not destroy us to the end, gracious protector!" (010-390) — is the language of a supplicant addressing his sovereign. The irony is that the "gracious protector" is himself the owner of the system that makes such destruction possible.

## The Landowner's Response

Arkady Pavlitch's reaction is chilling. He scowls, questions the bailiff, and then, accepting [[sofron|Sofron]]'s explanation that Antip is "a drunken fellow... and lazy too... never been out of arrears this five years" (010-397), turns on the victim:

> 'And why did you get into arrears?' Mr. Pyenotchkin asked threateningly. (The old man's head sank.) 'You're fond of drinking, hanging about the taverns, I dare say.' (The old man opened his mouth to speak.) 'I know you,' Arkady Pavlitch went on emphatically; 'you think you've nothing to do but drink, and lie on the stove, and let steady peasants answer for you.' (010-410–010-415)

The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] does not investigate; he already "knows" — and what he knows is the stereotype that justifies the system. Antip, who has lost nearly everything, is accused of laziness and drinking. The bailiff's word is accepted without question; the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s word is dismissed before it is spoken.

Antip's final attempt at self-defence is cut short: "Your honour, Arkady Pavlitch, have pity, protect us; when have I been impudent? Before God Almighty, I swear it was beyond my strength. [[sofron|Sofron]] Yakovlitch has taken a dislike to me..." (010-428–010-430). The desperate religious oath, the admission of powerlessness ("beyond my strength"), and the accurate diagnosis ("taken a dislike to me") are all useless against the closed circuit of [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] and bailiff.

## Aftermath

Later, the narrator discusses Antip with [[anpadist|Anpadist]], a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] from a neighbouring village. [[anpadist|Anpadist]]'s analysis is blunt and fatalistic:

> 'There,' commented Anpadist, 'he will eat him up now; he'll simply eat the man up. The bailiff will beat him now. Such a poor, unlucky chap, come to think of it! And what's his offence?... He had some wrangle in meeting with him, the agent, and he lost all patience, I suppose, and of course he wouldn't stand it.... So he began pecking at him, Antip. Now he'll eat him up altogether.' (010-473–010-479)

The metaphor of consumption — "eat him up" — recurs three times in [[anpadist|Anpadist]]'s speech. It is the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s own analysis of how the system works: a slow, methodical devouring of the weak by the powerful. [[anpadist|Anpadist]] adds a crucial structural observation: [[sofron|Sofron]] "knows whom to fall upon. The old men that are a bit richer, or've more children, he doesn't touch, the red-headed devil! but there's all the difference here! Why he's sent Antip's sons for recruits out of turn" (010-479–010-483). The bailiff's predation is selective — he targets the most vulnerable, those without wealth or sons to protect them.

## Historical/Social Context

Antip's story is a case study in the structural violence of Russian serfdom at the estate level. The triangle of [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]–bailiff–[[russian-peasantry|peasant]] was the fundamental unit of rural power, and the bailiff occupied the crucial mediating position: he controlled information that reached the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], distributed labour and punishment, and decided which [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s prospered and which were destroyed. [[sofron|Sofron]]'s exploitation of Antip — taking his sons as recruits, stealing his cow, beating his wife — was not aberrant; it was the logic of a system in which the bailiff's power was nearly absolute and the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s recourse almost nonexistent. The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s indifference completed the circuit. [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]], the cultivated Europeanised gentleman who "punishes his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s 'from a sense of duty'" (010-033), is precisely the type of enlightened proprietor who is most dangerous: his refinement makes him impervious to the reality of his own estate.
