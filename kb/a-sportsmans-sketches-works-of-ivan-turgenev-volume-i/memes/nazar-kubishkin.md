## Overview

Nazar Kubishkin is the coachman of the horse-dealer [[Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai]], mentioned during the [[Lebedyan]] horse-fair. In Tchornobai's sales pitch — delivered through a town-crier style announcement — potential buyers are directed to ask for Kubishkin if Tchornobai himself is unavailable. (014-329) He appears as a minor figure in the bustling commercial world of the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] fair, one of many attendants and assistants who kept the horse-trading economy running.

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Nazar Kubishkin is given in the text. His role as coachman places him among the skilled serf laborers who worked closely with their masters in commercial enterprises. The fact that he is named in Tchornobai's public announcement — "should Anastasei Ivanitch be absent, then ask for Nazar Kubishkin, the coachman" (014-329) — indicates a position of some trust and responsibility within Tchornobai's horse-dealing operation.

## Role in the Story

Nazar Kubishkin appears only in the context of the horse-fair announcement. The horse-dealer [[Anastasei Ivanitch Tchornobai]], whose horses are described as being of "the highest quality," "free from vice," and "brought to perfection," addresses potential buyers through a formal-sounding declaration (014-327). The announcement ends with an instruction that purchasers should ask for Tchornobai himself, but "should Anastasei Ivanitch be absent, then ask for Nazar Kubishkin, the coachman" (014-328–014-329). The narrator, intrigued by this salesmanship, stops and decides to examine the horses, marking Kubishkin as a point of contact in the commercial machinery of the fair.
