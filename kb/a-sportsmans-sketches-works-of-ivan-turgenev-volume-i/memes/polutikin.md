# Polutikin

## Overview

Mr. Polutikin is the Kaluga [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] introduced in the opening sketch of *A Sportsman's Sketches*, "[[Khor and Kalinich]]." A "petty proprietor" (001-031) who embodies the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman, Polutikin serves as the narrative bridge through which Turgenev's hunter-narrator meets the two [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s who become the sketch's true subjects. He is a figure of genial mediocrity — a "splendid fellow" (001-036) whose enthusiasm for hunting, French cuisine, and literary pretension is matched only by his thoughtless exploitation of his serfs. Through Polutikin, Turgenev sketches the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] type with a light but unforgiving hand: the master who is "an excellent fellow" by his own class's standards, yet whose daily habits — taking [[Kalinitch]] away from his farm for hunting, paying a pittance for constant service, failing to provide boots for a man who walks miles behind his carriage — amount to a quiet, unremarkable cruelty.

## Appearance & Character

Turgenev introduces Polutikin through a series of contradictory qualifications that define his type. The narrator first meets him "on a visit to the Zhizdrinsky district in search of [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]" (001-030) and immediately categorizes him: "He was an enthusiastic [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman; it follows, therefore, that he was a splendid fellow" (001-032–001-033). This equating of [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]ing enthusiasm with moral worth — presented without irony by the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman-narrator — establishes the code by which the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] judge one another.

Polutikin's pretensions extend beyond the field. Turgenev notes that he "had, from his own account, been twice jilted by the woman he loved, and was nursing a wounded heart" (001-042–001-044) — a romantic self-image that furnishes him with a repertoire of "little songs" he performs with feeling, though "in spite of Mr. Polutikin's appreciation of its merits, had certainly never amused anyone" (001-040–001-041). He admired the works of the now-forgotten novelist [[akim-nahimov|Akim Nahimov]] and the sentimental pulp *Yury Miloslavsky* — literary tastes that mark him as a man of pretension rather than cultivation. Yet Turgenev's verdict is ultimately forgiving in its condescension: "with these insignificant failings, Mr. Polutikin was, as has been said already, an excellent fellow" (001-050–001-051).

His hospitality follows the conventions of his class: "On the first day of my acquaintance with Mr. Polutikin, he invited me to stay the night at his house" (001-052–001-053). He offers his guest "splendid spring water" (001-101) and promises "supper in the French style" (001-088) — the hallmark of a provincial gentleman who measures gentility by proximity to metropolitan fashion.

## Role in the Story

Polutikin's structural role in "[[khor|Khor]] and Kalinich" is to be the enabler who vanishes. He introduces the narrator to his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, provides the setting, then conveniently departs on business, leaving the narrator alone to form independent relationships with [[Khor]] and [[Kalinitch]]. "The next day Mr. Polutikin was obliged to go to town about some business with his neighbour Pitchukoff, who had ploughed over some land of Polutikin's, and had flogged a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman of Polutikin's on that land" (001-167–001-170). This casually reported detail — a land dispute that culminates in the flogging of a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman — passes without commentary from the narrator, yet it exposes the raw violence underpinning the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]'s everyday affairs.

Polutikin's absence is the precondition for the sketch's real work: "I went out hunting alone, and before evening I turned into [[khor|Hor]]'s house" (001-170–001-172). Without Polutikin's mediating presence, the narrator can meet [[khor|Khor]] on equal terms, and the Socratic [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] emerges in full dignity. The sketch's deep structure is thus a kind of liberation — Polutikin must leave for the serfs to speak.

When he returns on the fourth day, the narrator notes: "I was sorry to part from the old man" (001-418). The reader shares the regret; Polutikin's reappearance signals the re-imposition of the social order that the story has so beautifully suspended.

## As a Landowner

Polutikin's relationship with his serfs is the sketch's central indictment of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]], conducted through detail rather than diatribe. His treatment of [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] is revealing:

> "Kalinitch is a good peasant," Mr. Polutikin told me; "he is a clever peasant; he does everything he undertakes readily, but he can't farm his land properly; I'm always taking him away from it. He goes out hunting every day with me... How can he farm his land properly, if you think of it?" (001-162–001-166)

The reasoning is a perfect circle of exploitation: Polutikin prevents [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] from farming, then observes that [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] cannot farm well, without ever questioning whether the first fact causes the second.

The economic dimensions of the relationship are equally damning. When [[khor|Khor]] teases [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] about his bast shoes — "where are your boots? you keep going to the forest with your master" — the implication is clear: Polutikin does not pay [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] enough even for proper footwear (001-387–001-388). "The master gave me a present of them!" [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] protests of his bast shoes, his "vexation" suggesting he knows the gift is insultingly inadequate.

His arrangement with [[khor|Khor]] is more complex but no less revealing. "Twenty-five years ago his cottage was burnt down," Polutikin explains; "so he came to my late father, [[nikolai-kouzmitch|Nikolai Kouzmitch]], and said: 'Allow me to settle in your forest on the bog. I will pay you a rent, and undertake no labour of any kind.'" (001-109–001-114). The story is offered as a curiosity, but it exposes the fundamental dynamic: the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s absolute power of permission over where a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] may live. When Polutikin urges [[khor|Khor]] to buy his freedom, it is with the casualness of a man who cannot imagine why anyone would remain a serf — yet [[khor|Khor]]'s refusal exposes a reality Polutikin is too comfortable to see: that freedom in 1850s Russia was not the unambiguous good the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] imagined it to be.

The sketch's brief mention of Pitchukoff — the neighbor who "had flogged a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman" (001-169) over a land dispute — places Polutikin on a spectrum of [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] behavior. He is not the worst of his class; he does not personally wield the whip. But he belongs to a system in which whipping is a routine instrument of neighborly relations, and the narrator reports it as a footnote to Polutikin's schedule.

## Historical Context

The Polutikin type — the small provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] living among his serfs — was the backbone of the Russian [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] in the early nineteenth century. Unlike the great magnates who inhabited [[petersburg|Petersburg]] and [[moscow|Moscow]], men like Polutikin lived in daily proximity to their [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, knowing them by name, sharing their landscape, and depending on their labour for the income that sustained a precarious gentility. This proximity could produce genuine bonds — Polutikin describes [[kalinitch|Kalinitch]] with real, if patronizing, warmth — but it also normalized the master's absolute authority. Turgenev's achievement in the character is to capture exactly this ambiguity: Polutikin is neither villain nor reformer, but a man so wholly formed by his class that he cannot see his own cruelty.
