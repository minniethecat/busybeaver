# Sofron

## Overview

Sofron is the title figure of Turgenev's sketch "[[The Agent]]" and the most fully realized portrait of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]-on-[[russian-peasantry|peasant]] exploitation in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. He is a serf who has risen to the position of estate agent (or bailiff) under the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]], wielding delegated authority over his fellow [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s with a combination of theatrical obsequiousness toward his master and ruthless self-enrichment at the expense of the village. Turgenev constructs Sofron as a study in institutional corruption — not the corruption of an individual moral failure, but the inevitable product of a system in which absolute power is delegated downward without accountability. Sofron's genius lies in his mastery of the performance of loyalty: he weeps with gratitude at [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]]'s arrival, kneels for his hand, and sustains a continuous litany of flattery, while simultaneously reducing the [[shipilovka|Shipilovka]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s to destitution and pocketing the surplus.

## The Performance of Loyalty

Sofron's first appearance in the sketch is a theatrical set-piece that Turgenev stages with merciless precision. When [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]] arrives at the village, Sofron has been "making merry at [[perov|Perov]]: his face was unmistakably flushed, and there was a smell of spirits about him" (010-210–010-211). Yet the moment he appears before his master, he transforms into a virtuoso of deference:

> "Ah, our father, our gracious benefactor!" he began in a sing-song voice, and with a face of such deep feeling that it seemed every minute as if he would burst into tears; "at last you have graciously deigned to come to us ... your hand, your honour's hand," he added, his lips protruded in anticipation. (010-212–010-216)

The performance continues at escalating intensity:

> "Ah, you, our father!" cried Sofron; "how should they go ill? how should things go ill, now that you, our father, our benefactor, graciously deign to lighten our poor village with your presence, to make us happy till the day of our death?" (010-219–010-224)

Turgenev's irony operates through the narrator's deadpan observation: [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]] turns to him and asks, in French, "*N'est-ce pas que c'est touchant?*" (010-231–010-232). The switch to French — the language of the cultivated [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] — marks the distance between the master's sentimental appreciation of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] loyalty and the actual conditions Sofron's performance conceals.

## The Mechanism of Exploitation

Sofron's power derives entirely from his position as intermediary between the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] and the serfs. The land has been "settled" — "Day before yesterday the list was made out" (010-249) — and Sofron describes the process in language that mingles self-congratulation with strategic complaint:

> "We've settled the land shares, your honour; all by your gracious favour... The Hlinovsky folks made themselves disagreeable about it at first ... they were disagreeable about it, certainly... but they're a set of fools, your honour! — an ignorant lot. But we, your honour, graciously please you, gave an earnest of our gratitude, and satisfied Nikolai Nikolaitch, the mediator; we acted in everything according to your orders, your honour; as you graciously ordered, so we did." (010-248–010-257)

The speech is a masterpiece of bureaucratic trimming: every action is attributed to the master's orders, every decision framed as obedience. Yet the subtext — the satisfaction of the mediator, the resistance of the [[hlinovsky|Hlinovsky]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, the "earnest of gratitude" — suggests a different story: Sofron has used his position to enrich himself at the expense of both the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and, indirectly, his master.

When Sofron mentions "There's too little land, of course" (010-264–010-265), [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]] cuts him short, confident in his agent's zeal. It is the sketch's darkest irony: the master who prides himself on his enlightened management is being systematically deceived by the very man he trusts.

## The Village Under Sofron

The narrator's observations during his stay reveal the human cost of Sofron's administration. The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s he encounters move "with dejected faces" (010-316), and when [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]] departs, Sofron's son — "the seven-foot bailiff" (010-307) — maintains order through intimidation. The earlier scene of the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s falling silent at the approach of the master's carriage (010-162–010-164) registers the atmosphere of fear that Sofron has cultivated: it is not love of the master but terror of the agent that silences the village.

Sofron's household itself testifies to his prosperity. His cottage "stood apart from the rest in the middle of a thick green patch of hemp" (010-177–010-178), and inside, the agent's wife is observed "surreptitiously belabouring some other [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman" (010-199–010-200). The violence of the system is not confined to male authority; it reproduces itself through every level of the hierarchy Sofron has constructed.

## Historical Significance

Sofron represents a figure that Russian liberal opinion found more disturbing than the cruel [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]: the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] who internalizes and reproduces the system's cruelty against his own class. The estate agent was a common figure in serf-era Russia, a serf himself but empowered to allocate land, extract labour, and punish resistance. Because his own freedom depended entirely on the master's favour — and because his wealth came not from legal ownership but from systematic skimming — the agent had every incentive to maximize exploitation while performing perfect loyalty. Sofron is Turgenev's definitive portrait of this type, and his sketch "The Agent" was cited by contemporaries as evidence that serfdom corrupted not only the masters but the entire social fabric of rural Russia.
