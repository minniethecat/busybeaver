# Petersburg

## Overview

Petersburg — St. Petersburg, the imperial capital founded by Peter the Great — appears across six of the stories in *A Sportsman's Sketches* as the pole of rank, ambition, and modern power, opposite to the older sacredness of [[Moscow]]. Like [[moscow|Moscow]] it is never a setting the narrator visits; it exists offstage as the place from which grandees, dignitaries, and fashions come, and toward which ruined [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] and displaced serfs are drawn. Where [[moscow|Moscow]] absorbs the region's labour, Petersburg represents its social summit — the court, the bureaucracy, and the leisured wealth that reaches down into the provinces.

## Description

### The capital of rank and wealth

Petersburg is above all a badge of status. In [[raspberry-spring|Raspberry Spring]], the old count whom [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] served was so eminent that "the persons of the first rank, one may say, at Petersburg, used to visit him" (003-203); yet when he was "entirely ruined, he set off to Petersburg to try for a post for himself, and died in a room at a hotel" (003-145) — the capital being both the height of grandeur and the last resort of the bankrupt. The drunken neighbour [[stepan-niktopolionitch-komov|Komov]], in [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]], boasts in his cups of owning "three houses in Petersburg" (006-134). The polished [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]] of [[the-agent|The Agent]] belongs to this world too: "the winters he spends in Petersburg" (010-040). In [[lebedyan|Lebedyan]], the sponger [[viktor-hlopakov|Hlopakov]] makes his living by knowing "how to gain the favour of rich scapegraces from Petersburg" (014-112), and in [[two-country-gentlemen|Two Country Gentlemen]] the narrator's satire reaches its height in "a certain great Petersburg dignitary" whose absurd estate management none could surpass (013-042).

### A capital that reaches into the countryside

Petersburg also exerts a direct pull on the serfs of the province. In [[yermolai-and-the-millers-wife|Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]], the narrator recalls that "during my stay at Petersburg I had become by chance acquainted with Mr. [[Zvyerkoff]]" (002-268), whose household story reveals the capital's casual cruelty: [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]]'s wife had decided, "let us take this girl to Petersburg; I like her" (002-306), carrying the young serf [[Arina Timofeyevna|Arina]] away from her home to serve as a lady's maid — a favour that ended in [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]'s ruin.

## Role in the Stories

Petersburg is the imperial counterweight to [[Moscow]] in the geography of the *Sketches*. If [[moscow|Moscow]] is the old capital of golden domes, banquets, and craft, Petersburg is the modern capital of rank, office, and fashionable leisure. It signifies the top of the social order — the dignitaries visited by persons of the first rank, the houses owned by boasting [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s, the winters of the idle rich — and, at the same time, the remote authority that can pluck a serf-girl from her village on a whim. Always offstage, it defines by contrast the narrow provincial world the [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman actually moves through, and marks the distance between that world and the seat of power that governs it.

## Historical Context

St. Petersburg, founded by Peter the Great in 1703 and made the capital in 1712, was the seat of the Russian court and bureaucracy throughout the period of the *Sketches*. This administrative centrality explains the recurring associations in the stories: the ruined count who goes there "to try for a post" (003-145), the "great Petersburg dignitary" of the civil service (013-042), and the fashionable families who "spend the winters" there (010-040) among the social season of the capital.
