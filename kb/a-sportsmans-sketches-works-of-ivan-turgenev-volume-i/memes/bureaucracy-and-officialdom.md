# Bureaucracy and Officialdom

Beneath the [[Landowning Gentry|landowners]] and above the serfs stands a layer of clerks, cashiers, bailiffs and surveyors who administer the daily business of estate life.

Turgenev gives this world of paperwork and petty power its own sketch in [[the-counting-house|The Counting-House]], and returns to it in the boundary disputes of [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]].

## The Estate Counting-House

Sheltering from the rain, the narrator blunders into what turns out to be the administrative heart of the estate, guarded by its resident functionary:

> (011-074) 'This is the principal office of the manor,' he interrupted. 'I'm the clerk on duty.... Didn't you see the sign-board? That's what it was put up for.'

The office has its own small ladder of promotion, its titles shuffled with bureaucratic gravity:

> (011-140) 'No, sir; he was the chief cashier, but now he has been promoted to be head-clerk.'

## Petitions, Surveyors and the Law

Estate business runs on courts, complaints and plans. Ovsyanikov recalls how a wronged man's appeal to the law came to nothing when his neighbours dared not stand with him:

> (006-106) ...and he laid a petition before the court. But he was alone: the others did not appear--they were afraid.

The paperwork itself becomes an obsession: one half-mad litigant can think of nothing but the surveyor's documents that will prove his claim:

> (006-271) give me the surveyor's plans, the Judas's plans here!

## Social and Historical Context

Turgenev shows that serfdom was administered as much as it was owned. The counting-house, the petition and the surveyor's plan are the machinery through which a distant mistress's will becomes a serf's fate — and through which clerks and cashiers carve out their own small tyrannies.

The bland language of office and promotion masks a system in which the pen decides who is flogged and who is spared.
