# Baush

## Overview

Baush was the chief huntsman and whipper-in of the narrator's grandfather, remembered decades later by [[Ovsyanikov]] as one of the most remarkable figures of the old provincial world. "He was a desperate fellow," [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] recalls, "and whatever order your grandfather gave him, he would carry it out in a minute — he'd have run on to a sword at his bidding" (006-220–006-222). Baush belongs to the gallery of formidable old-style retainers in Turgenev's sketches — men of unquestioning loyalty, physical courage, and a capacity for both tremendous energy and sudden collapse. His halloo "was something like a tally-ho in the forest" (006-223), and his name, like that of [[Milovidka]] the mare, was part of the folklore of the grandfather's estate.

## Appearance & Character

No physical portrait of Baush is given, but his character is drawn in vivid strokes through [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s recollections. He is defined by three traits: absolute obedience, ferocious energy, and a surprising capacity for collapse. When the grandfather gave an order, Baush executed it without hesitation — even violent orders, such as the seizure of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s father, when "your grandfather at once sent his huntsman Baush with a detachment of men" and they "carried my father to your estate" where he was flogged under the windows (006-110–006-113). Baush was the instrument of the grandfather's will, as reliable as a weapon.

Yet he was not a simple brute. His halloo was legendary — "it was something like a tally-ho in the forest" (006-223) — suggesting a man of tremendous vocal power and presence in the field. And then there was his peculiar weakness:

> "he would suddenly turn nasty, get off his horse, and lie down on the ground … and directly the dogs ceased to hear his voice, it was all over! They lost the scent, and wouldn't go on for anything!" (006-224–006-226)

The huntsman who could "run on to a sword" could also, without warning, be struck down by some mysterious despondency, paralyzing the entire hunt. The cure was simple and telling: "Usually, on such occasions, Baush asked for some vodka, drank it up, got on his horse, and began to halloo as lustily as ever again" (006-231–006-232). The rhythm — collapse, vodka, renewal — suggests a man whose ferocity was fueled by something unstable, a characteristic pattern of the hard-living huntsman type.

## Role in the Story

Baush appears in "The Peasant Proprietor [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]" as part of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s extended reminiscence about the narrator's grandfather — a man who, [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] notes, "kept up a huge establishment and had a famous pack of hounds" (006-216). When the narrator asks, "And what sort of a man was this Baush?", [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] responds with surprise: "Why, how comes it you have heard about Milovidka, and not about Baush?" (006-217–006-218), indicating that in the grandfather's world, Baush the huntsman was as famous as Milovidka the celebrated mare.

[[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s portrait of Baush is complex, reflecting the old man's ambivalent relationship with the narrator's family. He remembers Baush as the agent of his father's humiliation — the man who physically seized his father and carried him to be flogged. Yet he speaks of Baush with the relish of a storyteller who appreciates a vivid character. The huntsman is both a perpetrator of serfdom's violence and one of its victims, bound to execute his master's cruelties and then collapse in the middle of the hunt, needing vodka to rise again. Through Baush, Turgenev sketches the psychology of the serf-retainer: utterly dependent on the master's will, capable of terrible deeds and yet deeply vulnerable, held together by loyalty and liquor.
