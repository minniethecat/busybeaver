# Ryaby

## Overview

Ryaby is a village named in the ninth sketch, [[kassyan-of-fair-springs|KASSYAN OF FAIR SPRINGS]]. It is identified only as the home of Martin the carpenter, a man the travellers never see alive — they meet his coffin on the road.

## Description

The name is spoken by the [[The Narrator|narrator]]'s coachman [[Erofay]] as a funeral procession passes them. Watching the coffin go by in silence, [[erofay|Erofay]] turns and explains: "It's Martin, the carpenter, they're burying... Martin of Ryaby" (009-072).

The dead man's village becomes a small point of recognition later, when [[erofay|Erofay]] questions [[Kassyan]] on the road. "You know Martin the carpenter.... Of course, you know Martin of Ryaby?" he asks, and when [[kassyan|Kassyan]] assents, tells him bluntly, "Well, he is dead. We have just met his coffin" (009-212) — news at which "[[kassyan|Kassyan]] shuddered."

Ryaby itself is never described; it serves only to fix the identity of the carpenter whose death shadows the encounter.

## Role in the Stories

The village anchors one of the sketch's recurring motifs — the nearness of death in the quiet countryside. By attaching Martin to a named place, [[Erofay]] makes the dead carpenter a real neighbour rather than an anonymous corpse, and it is this concrete news that draws the strange, death-haunted response from [[Kassyan]], the holy wanderer of [[fair-springs|Fair Springs]].

Ryaby is one of several small settlements that give the roads of the sketch their lived-in geography.
