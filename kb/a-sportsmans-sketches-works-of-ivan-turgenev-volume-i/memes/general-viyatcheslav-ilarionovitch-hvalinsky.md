# General Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch Hvalinsky

## Overview

General Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch Hvalinsky is a retired Major-General introduced at the opening of [[Two Country Gentlemen]] as a representative specimen of the provincial Russian [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. Turgenev devotes an extended character sketch to him before introducing the sketch's nominal subject, [[Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov]]. [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] is a bachelor who owns five hundred souls, moves in the best society of his district, and embodies a particular type: the military gentleman past his prime, clinging to the rituals of rank and gallantry in a world that no longer quite takes him at his own valuation. The portrait is satirical but not cruel — Turgenev catalogues [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]]'s vanities with the attentiveness of a naturalist pinning a specimen.

## Appearance and Character

Turgenev's physical description of [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] is exact and devastating: "Picture to yourselves a tall and once slender man, now inclined to corpulence, but not in the least decrepit or even elderly, a man of ripe age; in his very prime, as they say" (013-008–013-010). The qualifier "once" does much of the work. [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]]'s cheeks are flabby, his eyes have gathered "close wrinkles like rays," a few teeth are gone, and his remaining hair has been dyed a "purplish hue" by a dubious composition bought from a Jew who passed himself off as an Armenian at the [[romyon|Romyon]] horse-fair (013-013–013-016).

Despite these indignities, [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] maintains the bearing of an old cavalry man. He has "a smart walk and a ringing laugh, jingles his spurs and curls his moustaches" (013-017–013-019). He wears a frock-coat buttoned to the top and speaks of himself as a man of action, though Turgenev notes that "really old men never talk of being old" (013-020).

His character is governed by two incompatible impulses: the desire to display himself and the fear of his betters. At public functions, balls, and assemblies, "General Hvalinsky shows to advantage" (013-102). He is a devotee of ladies' society and an enthusiastic card-player, particularly of preference. Yet before persons of high rank, "[[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] is for the most part silent" (013-123). Turgenev captures this social self-suppression with a single devastating sentence: "He even, if he is with persons of a rank only a little below his own, treats them with a peculiar, and also complex, offhandedness, at once gracious and repulsive" (013-124–013-127). The man who jingles his spurs among the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] somehow contrives to make himself invisible when a real grandee enters the room.

## Role in the Story

[[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] is introduced in "[[Two Country Gentlemen]]" as a foil to [[Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov]], the sketch's primary subject. Turgenev's purpose in placing the two portraits side by side is taxonomic: he is cataloguing the varieties of the provincial landowning gentleman. Where Stegunov embodies the old-regime patriarchal despot — flogging serfs while sipping tea, convinced of his own benevolence — [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] represents the military variant: equally self-satisfied, equally useless, but polished by years of balls and regimental dinners into a more presentable form.

His function is thematic rather than narrative. [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]] does nothing in the sketch except exist as an instance of a type. He is "a bachelor. He owns five hundred souls" (013-146). He has "no sort of resemblance to [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]]; he too is a bachelor" (013-139) — the sentence that bridges the two portraits makes Turgenev's method explicit: collect specimens, set them side by side, and let the comparison speak.

[[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]]'s significance lies in what Turgenev leaves unstated. The General's five hundred souls, his frock-coats, his card games, and his preference parties are supported by serf labour, yet nowhere in the portrait does Turgenev mention a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]. The silence is the point: for a man of [[mardary-and-hvalinsky|Hvalinsky]]'s type, the serfs are part of the landscape, invisible except as the source of the income that funds his existence. The satire is gentler than in the Stegunov portrait, but the implication is the same: this world, for all its surface charm, is built on a foundation it refuses to acknowledge.
