# Food, Tea and Hospitality

Almost every meeting in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] is sealed over food. A samovar is set out, tea is poured, bread and eggs appear, and the ritual of reception frames the sportsman's wanderings from cottage to manor house.

Turgenev notes these offerings closely, for they map the whole social order: what is served, and by whom, tells the reader as much as any speech.

## The Samovar and Morning Tea

The hunt itself is bracketed by tea. A day in [[khor-and-kalinich|Hor and Kalinitch]] opens with it:

> (001-124) The next day, directly after our morning tea, we started out hunting again.

And a host's welcome is the same samovar, offered with a smile:

> (001-226) 'The samovar is ready,' he told me with a smile; 'let us come and have tea.'

## Peasant Fare

At the humbler end of the scale, hospitality means whatever the cottage can spare. In [[yermolai-and-the-millers-wife|Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]], the fare is modest but freely given:

> (002-193) The miller's wife brought us milk, eggs, potatoes and bread.

## Hospitality's Chilling Underside

The same ritual can shade into something colder. In [[two-country-gentlemen|Two Country Gentlemen]], tea is served in comfort while, next door, a serf is being flogged by the host's order:

> (013-245) Tea was served us.

[[Mardary Apollonitch]] sips on with perfect ease, half-consciously beating time to the blows he has commanded:

> (013-277) ...with the most good-natured smile imaginable, he murmured as though involuntarily accompanying the blows: 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk!'

## Social and Historical Context

Russian rural hospitality was a genuine virtue and a rigid code at once. The samovar was obligatory, the welcome warm, the table shared according to means. Turgenev honours the custom yet exposes its limits: the same gentleman who presses tea on a guest can order a beating between cups, and the warmth of the table never quite reaches the serf who carries it in.
