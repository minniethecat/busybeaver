# Death and Mortality

**Death** runs quietly through nearly every sketch in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]. It appears at the deathbed and in the graveyard, in the offhand report of a lost child and in the folk memory attached to a nameless mound.

Turgenev treats it without melodrama: death is simply one of the conditions of life in the countryside, borne by the [[Russian Peasantry|peasants]] with a resignation the narrator finds both moving and disquieting.

## The Deathbed

The book's most sustained treatment of dying is [[The District Doctor|The District Doctor]], in which a country physician recounts the illness and death of a young gentlewoman he could not save — and with whom, in her last days, he fell into a strange, doomed intimacy. Turgenev gives the dying girl a startling composure:

> (004-234) "Don't be afraid, don't be afraid! I am not frightened of death at all." She suddenly sat up and leaned on her elbow. "Now ... yes, now I can tell you that I thank you with my whole heart ... that you are kind and good--that I love you!"

Yet the same scene records the ordinary terror beneath that composure, in the question every patient asks:

> (004-219) Alexandra Andreevna was gazing with intent eyes at me ... her lips parted, her cheeks seemed burning. "What is it?" "Doctor, shall I die?" "Merciful Heavens!" "No, doctor, no; please don't tell me I shall live."

## Death and the Peasant

For the peasant, death is bound up with survival. In [[Raspberry Spring]], a ruined old serf explains his poverty by the loss of his son with a flatness that is more affecting than any lament:

> (003-270) "My son is dead--so I can't manage it now alone." "Your son is dead?" "He is dead. My son," added the peasant, after a pause, "lived in Moscow as a cabman; he paid, I must confess, rent for me."

The grief and the economic ruin are inseparable; under [[Serfdom|serfdom]] a death in the family is also the loss of a wage-earner, and the survivor must reckon the two together.

## The Dead in the Landscape

Death also settles into the land itself as story and superstition. Around the fire in [[Byezhin Prairie]], the peasant boys tell of a drowned man whose grave still marks the riverbank, a place where the uncanny is felt to linger:

> (008-360) ...there a drowned man was buried; he was drowned long, long ago, when the water was still deep; only his grave can still be seen, though it can only just be seen ... like this--a little mound.

Here death crosses into [[Folk Belief and Superstition|folk belief]]: the dead are not gone but present, watching from beneath the water and the earth.

## Social and Historical Context

Turgenev's refusal to sentimentalize death is part of the *Sketches*' larger realism. By showing peasants meeting loss with unshowy endurance — and gentry and serfs alike subject to the same mortality — he quietly levels the social distinctions the book elsewhere exposes. Death is the one estate that owns everyone.
