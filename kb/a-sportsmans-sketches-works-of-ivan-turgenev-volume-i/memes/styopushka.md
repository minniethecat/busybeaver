# Styopushka

## Overview

Styopushka is a character who barely exists — and that is precisely his significance in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. He appears at the beginning of "[[Raspberry Spring]]," sitting on the bank of the river Ista in the August heat, and the narrator's recognition of him triggers one of the most extraordinary descriptive passages in the entire collection: a catalogue of everything Styopushka is not. He has no position, no ties of kinship, no past to speak of, no place in any social order. He lives in a shed behind the chicken-house in summer and in the bathhouse anteroom in winter. He speaks so rarely that "he seems never to have opened his lips from the time of his birth" (003-098–003-099). He is, in Turgenev's devastating formulation, a man who "could not be classified under the special order of house-serfs, and hardly under the genus 'man' at all" (003-064–003-066). Through Styopushka, Turgenev pushes the logic of serfdom to its limit case: the human being who has been stripped of everything except the biological fact of continuing to live.

## The State of Bare Existence

The narrator's introduction of Styopushka is a systematic negation:

> "Every man has some kind of position in society, and at least some ties of some sort; every house-serf receives, if not wages, at least some so-called 'ration.' Styopushka had absolutely no means of subsistence of any kind; had no relationship to anyone; no one knew of his existence. This man had not even a past; there was no story told of him; he had probably never been enrolled on a census-revision." (003-067–003-072)

The negation accumulates until it becomes ontological: Styopushka is not merely poor or obscure, but unregistered — a man who may not officially exist. The census-revision (*reviziya*) was the basis of serf ownership, and the suggestion that Styopushka may never have been enrolled means he belongs to no one, which in the logic of serfdom means he belongs nowhere.

The only trace of his origin is a rumour, relayed by "grandfather [[trofimitch|Trofimitch]], who knew all the pedigrees of all the house-serfs in the direct line to the fourth generation" (003-079–003-080): Styopushka "was related to a Turkish woman whom the late master, the brigadier [[alexy-romanitch|Alexy Romanitch]] had been pleased to bring home from a campaign in the baggage waggon" (003-081–003-083). The detail is grotesque and precise — a Turkish woman seized in war, brought home like luggage, and vanished into the serf population leaving only this trace: a man who is barely a man, the last vanishing ripple of imperial violence.

## The Economy of Nothing

Styopushka's material existence is reduced to its physiological minimum:

> "He lived in summer in a little shed behind the chicken-house, and in winter in the ante-room of the bathhouse; in the bitter frosts he spent the night in the hayloft... He moved and walked about quite noiselessly; he sneezed and coughed behind his hand, not without apprehension; he was for ever busy and going stealthily to and fro like an ant; and all to get food — simply food to eat." (003-094–003-106)

The simile of the ant is exact: Styopushka is an organism devoted entirely to subsistence, his movements purposeful but barely visible, his existence a continuous low-grade emergency. He is never idle, but his labour produces nothing beyond his own continued existence. On holidays, when the other serfs receive "buckwheat dumplings and vodka, after the old Russian fashion," he does not appear — "he did not make his bow nor kiss the master's hand, nor toss off to the master's health" (003-087–003-089). He is outside the ritual economy of serfdom as thoroughly as he is outside its material economy.

> "And indeed, if he had not toiled from morning till night for his living, our poor friend would certainly have died of hunger." (003-106–003-107)

The sentence is a judgment on the system: a serf who receives no ration, no wages, no protection, whose survival is entirely his own achievement.

## In the Scene

When Styopushka appears in the story's action, his role is minimal but revealing. He is fishing with the narrator and the freedman [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)]] at [[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]]. When the narrator asks for a worm, "Styopushka stretched out the basket to me" (003-158) — a silent gesture of service that is all the more moving for being entirely voluntary. Later, when the distressed [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[vlass|Vlass]] tells his story of fruitless petition to his master, Styopushka offers the only suggestion he can:

> "'You should — then,' Styopushka said suddenly. He grew confused, was silent, and fell to digging in the basket." (003-296–003-297)

The broken sentence is Styopushka's only line of dialogue in the sketch. He cannot complete the thought because he has no social language for advice — he has never been in a position where his words could affect anyone's fate. He falls silent and returns to his basket, to the ant-like labour that is his only mode of being.

## Philosophy of the Invisible

Styopushka is Turgenev's answer to the question that haunts *A Sportsman's Sketches*: what is the minimum of a human life? He is not a victim in the conventional sense — no one has beaten him, stolen from him, or separated him from his family, because he has nothing to steal and no family to lose. He is instead the product of a system that can produce people who simply fall through every category, belonging to no one, recorded nowhere, receiving nothing. That he survives at all is a miracle of persistence; that he survives so completely erased is an indictment. In a collection devoted to the richness and variety of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] life under serfdom, Styopushka is the limit case — the blank at the centre of the picture that throws every other portrait into sharper relief.
