# Dogs and Hunting Companions

A sportsman is never alone, and in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] his dog is often drawn with as much care as the people around him. Turgenev gives certain dogs names, tempers and small histories, so that they read less as equipment than as fellow creatures of the same hard world.

This is distinct from [[Hunting|hunting]] as a narrative frame: here the animals themselves hold the page, from a half-starved setter to a squire's absurdly christened pet.

## Valetka, Yermolaï's Setter

The clearest portrait belongs to [[Yermolaï]]'s dog in [[yermolai-and-the-millers-wife|Yermolaï and the Miller's Wife]]:

> (002-052) He had too a setter-dog, by name Valetka.

Underfed and mocked by the house-serfs, Valetka meets every cruelty with the same stoic calm, a patience the narrator lingers over:

> (002-067) ...but all these jeers, and even blows, Valetka bore with astonishing indifference.

## Astronomer and a Master's Whims

In [[khor-and-kalinich|Hor and Kalinitch]], the landowner Polutikin's eccentricity extends to his dog, whose grand name the narrator notes with dry amusement:

> (001-042) ...he called his dog Astronomer.

The same Astronomer turns up again, hoisted bodily into a boat at his master's command, a small comic reminder that the dog shares in every outing:

> (001-090) 'Lift Astronomer in!'

## Social and Historical Context

For the country sportsman a dog was working partner, status symbol and companion at once. Turgenev uses these dogs as quiet mirrors of their owners: Valetka's beaten endurance echoes the serfs who feed him, while Astronomer's pompous name marks the harmless vanity of a minor squire.

By granting the animals a temperament of their own, the sketches let the reader read character sideways, through the creatures that trot at the edge of every scene.
