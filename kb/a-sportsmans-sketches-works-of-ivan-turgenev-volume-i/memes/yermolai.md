# Yermolaï

**Yermolaï** is one of the most frequently recurring characters in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]], appearing as the narrator's faithful [[Hunting|hunting]] companion across multiple stories. A serf belonging to a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] of the [[Orel Province]], Yermolaï embodies the paradoxical condition of the Russian [[peasant]] — outwardly subservient yet inwardly free, possessing an intimate knowledge of the natural world that surpasses that of his social superiors.

## Appearance and Character

Yermolaï is described as a man of about forty-five, tall and thin, with a long nose, narrow forehead, and small grey eyes. His lips are constantly curved in a mocking smile. Turgenev presents him as a man utterly indifferent to his own comfort:

> (002-009) His whole life was based upon the conviction that there was no necessity for him to be disturbed about anything. He existed, as it were, in a state of chronic indifference to all the troubles and vexations of life.

Despite being a serf, Yermolaï carries himself with a peculiar dignity. He cannot bear to be treated with familiarity or condescension, and his knowledge of the natural world far exceeds that of his social superiors.

## Role in the Stories

Yermolaï appears in four of the fourteen sketches: [[Yermolai-and-the-Millers-Wife]], [[Lgov]], [[My-Neighbour-Radilov]], and briefly in [[Biryuk]]. In each, he serves as the narrator's indispensable guide to the hidden corners of rural Russia.

> (007-001) "Let us go to Lgov," Yermolaï, whom the reader knows already, said to me one day. "There we can shoot ducks to our hearts' content."

His knowledge extends beyond hunting. Yermolaï can secure a boat in the middle of nowhere, negotiate with suspicious villagers, and read the character of every person he encounters with startling accuracy.

## Hunting Companion

As a hunting partner, Yermolaï is indispensable. He possesses a dog named Valetka that is as peculiar as its master. Together, they form a team that the narrator comes to rely upon completely. His sharp eyes, tireless legs, and unerring instinct for the habits of game make him, in spite of his eccentricities, an invaluable companion in the field.

## Social Significance

Turgenev uses Yermolaï to illustrate the contradictions at the heart of [[Serfdom|serfdom]]. Here is a man who is legally property yet who lives entirely on his own terms — married but rarely visiting his wife, belonging to a master but spending his days roaming freely through forest and marsh. His existence exposes the absurdity of a system that can reduce such a capable and self-possessed individual to the status of chattel.
