# Tyutyurov

## Overview

Tyutyurov is a small village in the estate country, given only a single passing mention in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — in the penultimate sketch, [[the-counting-house|THE COUNTING-HOUSE]].

It comes up not as a setting but as a destination: a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] names the place where he means to wait for the estate steward [[Vassily Nikolaevitch]] to discuss some business.

## Description

The name is spoken by the clerk on duty in the counting-house office, reporting to a sleeping visitor. A [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] named Yagushkin, asking after [[vassily-nikolaevitch|Vassily Nikolaevitch]], says "he'd go to Tyutyurov this evening and would wait for you" (011-490). The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] adds, elliptically, that he wants "to discuss some business with [[vassily-nikolaevitch|Vassily Nikolaevitch]]," though "what the business was he didn't say; '[[vassily-nikolaevitch|Vassily Nikolaevitch]] will know'" (011-490).

The village itself is never described; it is mentioned only as a fixed point on the clerk's mental map, the place a provincial [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] chooses for a private conference with the all-important steward.

## Role in the Stories

Tyutyurov serves a single function — it reminds the reader how widely [[Vassily Nikolaevitch]]'s business radiates across the countryside. That a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] must go to some village to wait for the steward, rather than the steward coming to him, subtly underlines the power relationship between the bureaucratic chief and the local gentlemen.

It is also one of several modest place-names ([[Mihyevska]], [[Goloplek]]) that Turgenev scatters through the sketches to give the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] world an ordinary, concrete geography.
