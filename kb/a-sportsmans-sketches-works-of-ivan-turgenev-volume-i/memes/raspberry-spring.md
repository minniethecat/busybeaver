# RASPBERRY SPRING

[003-001]At the beginning of August the heat often becomes insupportable. At

[003-002]that season, from twelve to three o'clock, the most determined and

[003-003]ardent sportsman is not able to hunt, and the most devoted dog begins

[003-004]to 'clean his master's spurs,' that is, to follow at his heels, his

[003-005]eyes painfully blinking, and his tongue hanging out to an exaggerated

[003-006]length; and in response to his master's reproaches he humbly wags his

[003-007]tail and shows his confusion in his face; but he does not run forward.

[003-008]I happened to be out hunting on exactly such a day. I had long been

[003-009]fighting against the temptation to lie down somewhere in the shade, at

[003-010]least for a moment; for a long time my indefatigable dog went on

[003-011]running about in the bushes, though he clearly did not himself expect

[003-012]much good from his feverish activity. The stifling heat compelled me at

[003-013]last to begin to think of husbanding our energies and strength. I

[003-014]managed to reach the little river Ista, which is already known to my

[003-015]indulgent readers, descended the steep bank, and walked along the damp,

[003-016]yellow sand in the direction of the spring, known to the whole

[003-017]neighbourhood as Raspberry Spring. This spring gushes out of a cleft in

[003-018]the bank, which widens out by degrees into a small but deep creek, and,

[003-019]twenty paces beyond it, falls with a merry babbling sound into the

[003-020]river; the short velvety grass is green about the source: the sun's

[003-021]rays scarcely ever reach its cold, silvery water. I came as far as the

[003-022]spring; a cup of birch-wood lay on the grass, left by a passing peasant

[003-023]for the public benefit. I quenched my thirst, lay down in the shade,

[003-024]and looked round. In the cave, which had been formed by the flowing of

[003-025]the stream into the river, and hence marked for ever with the trace of

[003-026]ripples, two old men were sitting with their backs to me. One, a rather

[003-027]stout and tall man in a neat dark-green coat and lined cap, was

[003-028]fishing; the other was thin and little; he wore a patched fustian coat

[003-029]and no cap; he held a little pot full of worms on his knees, and

[003-030]sometimes lifted his hand up to his grizzled little head, as though he

[003-031]wanted to protect it from the sun. I looked at him more attentively,

[003-032]and recognised in him Styopushka of Shumihino. I must ask the reader's

[003-033]leave to present this man to him.

[003-034]A few miles from my place there is a large village called Shumihino,

[003-035]with a stone church, erected in the name of St. Kosmo and St. Damian.

[003-036]Facing this church there had once stood a large and stately

[003-037]manor-house, surrounded by various outhouses, offices, workshops,

[003-038]stables and coach-houses, baths and temporary kitchens, wings for

[003-039]visitors and for bailiffs, conservatories, swings for the people, and

[003-040]other more or less useful edifices. A family of rich landowners lived

[003-041]in this manor-house, and all went well with them, till suddenly one

[003-042]morning all this prosperity was burnt to ashes. The owners removed to

[003-043]another home; the place was deserted. The blackened site of the immense

[003-044]house was transformed into a kitchen-garden, cumbered up in parts by

[003-045]piles of bricks, the remains of the old foundations. A little hut had

[003-046]been hurriedly put together out of the beams that had escaped the fire;

[003-047]it was roofed with timber bought ten years before for the construction

[003-048]of a pavilion in the Gothic style; and the gardener, Mitrofan, with his

[003-049]wife Axinya and their seven children, was installed in it. Mitrofan

[003-050]received orders to send greens and garden-stuff for the master's table,

[003-051]a hundred and fifty miles away; Axinya was put in charge of a Tyrolese

[003-052]cow, which had been bought for a high price in Moscow, but had not

[003-053]given a drop of milk since its acquisition; a crested smoke-coloured

[003-054]drake too had been left in her hands, the solitary 'seignorial' bird;

[003-055]for the children, in consideration of their tender age, no special

[003-056]duties had been provided, a fact, however, which had not hindered them

[003-057]from growing up utterly lazy. It happened to me on two occasions to

[003-058]stay the night at this gardener's, and when I passed by I used to get

[003-059]cucumbers from him, which, for some unknown reason, were even in summer

[003-060]peculiar for their size, their poor, watery flavour, and their thick

[003-061]yellow skin. It was there I first saw Styopushka. Except Mitrofan and

[003-062]his family, and the old deaf churchwarden Gerasim, kept out of charity

[003-063]in a little room at the one-eyed soldier's widow's, not one man among

[003-064]the house-serfs had remained at Shumihino; for Styopushka, whom I

[003-065]intend to introduce to the reader, could not be classified under the

[003-066]special order of house-serfs, and hardly under the genus 'man' at all.

[003-067]Every man has some kind of position in society, and at least some ties

[003-068]of some sort; every house-serf receives, if not wages, at least some

[003-069]so-called 'ration.' Styopushka had absolutely no means of subsistence

[003-070]of any kind; had no relationship to anyone; no one knew of his

[003-071]existence. This man had not even a past; there was no story told of

[003-072]him; he had probably never been enrolled on a census-revision. There

[003-073]were vague rumours that he had once belonged to someone as a valet; but

[003-074]who he was, where he came from, who was his father, and how he had come

[003-075]to be one of the Shumihino people; in what way he had come by the

[003-076]fustian coat he had worn from immemorial times; where he lived and what

[003-077]he lived on--on all these questions no one had the least idea; and, to

[003-078]tell the truth, no one took any interest in the subject. Grandfather

[003-079]Trofimitch, who knew all the pedigrees of all the house-serfs in the

[003-080]direct line to the fourth generation, had once indeed been known to say

[003-081]that he remembered that Styopushka was related to a Turkish woman whom

[003-082]the late master, the brigadier Alexy Romanitch had been pleased to

[003-083]bring home from a campaign in the baggage waggon. Even on holidays,

[003-084]days of general money-giving and of feasting on buckwheat dumplings and

[003-085]vodka, after the old Russian fashion--even on such days Styopushka did

[003-086]not put in an appearance at the trestle-tables nor at the barrels; he

[003-087]did not make his bow nor kiss the master's hand, nor toss off to the

[003-088]master's health and under the master's eye a glass filled by the fat

[003-089]hands of the bailiff. Some kind soul who passed by him might share an

[003-090]unfinished bit of dumpling with the poor beggar, perhaps. At Easter

[003-091]they said 'Christ is risen!' to him; but he did not pull up his greasy

[003-092]sleeve, and bring out of the depths of his pocket a coloured egg, to

[003-093]offer it, panting and blinking, to his young masters or to the mistress

[003-094]herself. He lived in summer in a little shed behind the chicken-house,

[003-095]and in winter in the ante-room of the bathhouse; in the bitter frosts

[003-096]he spent the night in the hayloft. The house-serfs had grown used to

[003-097]seeing him; sometimes they gave him a kick, but no one ever addressed a

[003-098]remark to him; as for him, he seems never to have opened his lips from

[003-099]the time of his birth. After the conflagration, this forsaken creature

[003-100]sought a refuge at the gardener Mitrofan's. The gardener left him

[003-101]alone; he did not say 'Live with me,' but he did not drive him away.

[003-102]And Styopushka did not live at the gardener's; his abode was the

[003-103]garden. He moved and walked about quite noiselessly; he sneezed and

[003-104]coughed behind his hand, not without apprehension; he was for ever busy

[003-105]and going stealthily to and fro like an ant; and all to get

[003-106]food--simply food to eat. And indeed, if he had not toiled from morning

[003-107]till night for his living, our poor friend would certainly have died of

[003-108]hunger. It's a sad lot not to know in the morning what you will find to

[003-109]eat before night! Sometimes Styopushka sits under the hedge and gnaws a

[003-110]radish or sucks a carrot, or shreds up some dirty cabbage-stalks; or he

[003-111]drags a bucket of water along, for some object or other, groaning as he

[003-112]goes; or he lights a fire under a small pot, and throws in some little

[003-113]black scraps which he takes from out of the bosom of his coat; or he is

[003-114]hammering in his little wooden den--driving in a nail, putting up a

[003-115]shelf for bread. And all this he does silently, as though on the sly:

[003-116]before you can look round, he's in hiding again. Sometimes he suddenly

[003-117]disappears for a couple of days; but of course no one notices his

[003-118]absence.... Then, lo and behold! he is there again, somewhere under the

[003-119]hedge, stealthily kindling a fire of sticks under a kettle. He had a

[003-120]small face, yellowish eyes, hair coming down to his eyebrows, a sharp

[003-121]nose, large transparent ears, like a bat's, and a beard that looked as

[003-122]if it were a fortnight's growth, and never grew more nor less. This,

[003-123]then, was Styopushka, whom I met on the bank of the Ista in company

[003-124]with another old man.

[003-125]I went up to him, wished him good-day, and sat down beside him.

[003-126]Styopushka's companion too I recognised as an acquaintance; he was a

[003-127]freed serf of Count Piotr Ilitch's, one Mihal Savelitch, nicknamed

[003-128]Tuman (\_i.e.\_ fog). He lived with a consumptive Bolhovsky man, who kept

[003-129]an inn, where I had several times stayed. Young officials and other

[003-130]persons of leisure travelling on the Orel highroad (merchants, buried

[003-131]in their striped rugs, have other things to do) may still see at no

[003-132]great distance from the large village of Troitska, and almost on the

[003-133]highroad, an immense two-storied wooden house, completely deserted,

[003-134]with its roof falling in and its windows closely stuffed up. At mid-day

[003-135]in bright, sunny weather nothing can be imagined more melancholy than

[003-136]this ruin. Here there once lived Count Piotr Ilitch, a rich grandee of

[003-137]the olden time, renowned for his hospitality. At one time the whole

[003-138]province used to meet at his house, to dance and make merry to their

[003-139]heart's content to the deafening sound of a home-trained orchestra, and

[003-140]the popping of rockets and Roman candles; and doubtless more than one

[003-141]aged lady sighs as she drives by the deserted palace of the boyar and

[003-142]recalls the old days and her vanished youth. The count long continued

[003-143]to give balls, and to walk about with an affable smile among the crowd

[003-144]of fawning guests; but his property, unluckily, was not enough to last

[003-145]his whole life. When he was entirely ruined, he set off to Petersburg

[003-146]to try for a post for himself, and died in a room at a hotel, without

[003-147]having gained anything by his efforts. Tuman had been a steward of his,

[003-148]and had received his freedom already in the count's lifetime. He was a

[003-149]man of about seventy, with a regular and pleasant face. He was almost

[003-150]continually smiling, as only men of the time of Catherine ever do

[003-151]smile--a smile at once stately and indulgent; in speaking, he slowly

[003-152]opened and closed his lips, winked genially with his eyes, and spoke

[003-153]slightly through his nose. He blew his nose and took snuff too in a

[003-154]leisurely fashion, as though he were doing something serious.
