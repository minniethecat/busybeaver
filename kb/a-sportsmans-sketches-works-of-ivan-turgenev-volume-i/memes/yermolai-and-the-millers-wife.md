# YERMOLAÏ AND THE MILLER'S WIFE

[002-001]One evening I went with the huntsman Yermolaï 'stand-shooting.' ... But

[002-002]perhaps all my readers may not know what 'stand-shooting' is. I will

[002-003]tell you.

[002-004]A quarter of an hour before sunset in spring-time you go out into the

[002-005]woods with your gun, but without your dog. You seek out a spot for

[002-006]yourself on the outskirts of the forest, take a look round, examine

[002-007]your caps, and glance at your companion. A quarter of an hour passes;

[002-008]the sun has set, but it is still light in the forest; the sky is clear

[002-009]and transparent; the birds are chattering and twittering; the young

[002-010]grass shines with the brilliance of emerald.... You wait. Gradually the

[002-011]recesses of the forest grow dark; the blood-red glow of the evening sky

[002-012]creeps slowly on to the roots and the trunks of the trees, and keeps

[002-013]rising higher and higher, passes from the lower, still almost leafless

[002-014]branches, to the motionless, slumbering tree-tops.... And now even the

[002-015]topmost branches are darkened; the purple sky fades to dark-blue. The

[002-016]forest fragrance grows stronger; there is a scent of warmth and damp

[002-017]earth; the fluttering breeze dies away at your side. The birds go to

[002-018]sleep--not all at once--but after their kinds; first the finches are

[002-019]hushed, a few minutes later the warblers, and after them the yellow

[002-020]buntings. In the forest it grows darker and darker. The trees melt

[002-021]together into great masses of blackness; in the dark-blue sky the first

[002-022]stars come timidly out. All the birds are asleep. Only the redstarts

[002-023]and the nuthatches are still chirping drowsily.... And now they too are

[002-024]still. The last echoing call of the pee-wit rings over our heads; the

[002-025]oriole's melancholy cry sounds somewhere in the distance; then the

[002-026]nightingale's first note. Your heart is weary with suspense, when

[002-027]suddenly--but only sportsmen can understand me--suddenly in the deep

[002-028]hush there is a peculiar croaking and whirring sound, the measured

[002-029]sweep of swift wings is heard, and the snipe, gracefully bending its

[002-030]long beak, sails smoothly down behind a dark bush to meet your shot.

[002-031]That is the meaning of 'stand-shooting.' And so I had gone out

[002-032]stand-shooting with Yermolaï; but excuse me, reader: I must first

[002-033]introduce you to Yermolaï.

[002-034]Picture to yourself a tall gaunt man of forty-five, with a long thin

[002-035]nose, a narrow forehead, little grey eyes, a bristling head of hair,

[002-036]and thick sarcastic lips. This man wore, winter and summer alike, a

[002-037]yellow nankin coat of German cut, but with a sash round the waist; he

[002-038]wore blue pantaloons and a cap of astrakhan, presented to him in a

[002-039]merry hour by a spendthrift landowner. Two bags were fastened on to his

[002-040]sash, one in front, skilfully tied into two halves, for powder and for

[002-041]shot; the other behind for game: wadding Yermolaï used to produce out

[002-042]of his peculiar, seemingly inexhaustible cap. With the money he gained

[002-043]by the game he sold, he might easily have bought himself a

[002-044]cartridge-box and powder-flask; but he never once even contemplated

[002-045]such a purchase, and continued to load his gun after his old fashion,

[002-046]exciting the admiration of all beholders by the skill with which he

[002-047]avoided the risks of spilling or mixing his powder and shot. His gun

[002-048]was a single-barrelled flint-lock, endowed, moreover, with a villainous

[002-049]habit of 'kicking.' It was due to this that Yermolaï's right cheek was

[002-050]permanently swollen to a larger size than the left. How he ever

[002-051]succeeded in hitting anything with this gun, it would take a shrewd man

[002-052]to discover--but he did. He had too a setter-dog, by name Valetka, a

[002-053]most extraordinary creature. Yermolaï never fed him. 'Me feed a dog!'

[002-054]he reasoned; 'why, a dog's a clever beast; he finds a living for

[002-055]himself.' And certainly, though Valetka's extreme thinness was a shock

[002-056]even to an indifferent observer, he still lived and had a long life;

[002-057]and in spite of his pitiable position he was not even once lost, and

[002-058]never showed an inclination to desert his master. Once indeed, in his

[002-059]youth, he had absented himself for two days, on courting bent, but this

[002-060]folly was soon over with him. Valetka's most noticeable peculiarity was

[002-061]his impenetrable indifference to everything in the world.... If it were

[002-062]not a dog I was speaking of, I should have called him 'disillusioned.'

[002-063]He usually sat with his cropped tail curled up under him, scowling and

[002-064]twitching at times, and he never smiled. (It is well known that dogs

[002-065]can smile, and smile very sweetly.) He was exceedingly ugly; and the

[002-066]idle house-serfs never lost an opportunity of jeering cruelly at his

[002-067]appearance; but all these jeers, and even blows, Valetka bore with

[002-068]astonishing indifference. He was a source of special delight to the

[002-069]cooks, who would all leave their work at once and give him chase with

[002-070]shouts and abuse, whenever, through a weakness not confined to dogs, he

[002-071]thrust his hungry nose through the half-open door of the kitchen,

[002-072]tempting with its warmth and appetising smells. He distinguished

[002-073]himself by untiring energy in the chase, and had a good scent; but if

[002-074]he chanced to overtake a slightly wounded hare, he devoured it with

[002-075]relish to the last bone, somewhere in the cool shade under the green

[002-076]bushes, at a respectful distance from Yermolaï, who was abusing him in

[002-077]every known and unknown dialect. Yermolaï belonged to one of my

[002-078]neighbours, a landowner of the old style. Landowners of the old style

[002-079]don't care for game, and prefer the domestic fowl. Only on

[002-080]extraordinary occasions, such as birthdays, namedays, and elections,

[002-081]the cooks of the old-fashioned landowners set to work to prepare some

[002-082]long-beaked birds, and, falling into the state of frenzy peculiar to

[002-083]Russians when they don't quite know what to do, they concoct such

[002-084]marvellous sauces for them that the guests examine the proffered dishes

[002-085]curiously and attentively, but rarely make up their minds to try them.

[002-086]Yermolaï was under orders to provide his master's kitchen with two

[002-087]brace of grouse and partridges once a month. But he might live where

[002-088]and how he pleased. They had given him up as a man of no use for work

[002-089]of any kind--'bone lazy,' as the expression is among us in Orel. Powder

[002-090]and shot, of course, they did not provide him, following precisely the

[002-091]same principle in virtue of which he did not feed his dog. Yermolaï was

[002-092]a very strange kind of man; heedless as a bird, rather fond of talking,

[002-093]awkward and vacant-looking; he was excessively fond of drink, and never

[002-094]could sit still long; in walking he shambled along, and rolled from

[002-095]side to side; and yet he got over fifty miles in the day with his

[002-096]rolling, shambling gait. He exposed himself to the most varied

[002-097]adventures: spent the night in the marshes, in trees, on roofs, or

[002-098]under bridges; more than once he had got shut up in lofts, cellars, or

[002-099]barns; he sometimes lost his gun, his dog, his most indispensable

[002-100]garments; got long and severe thrashings; but he always returned home,

[002-101]after a little while, in his clothes, and with his gun and his dog. One

[002-102]could not call him a cheerful man, though one almost always found him

[002-103]in an even frame of mind; he was looked on generally as an eccentric.

[002-104]Yermolaï liked a little chat with a good companion, especially over a

[002-105]glass, but he would not stop long; he would get up and go. 'But where

[002-106]the devil are you going? It's dark out of doors.' 'To Tchaplino.' 'But

[002-107]what's taking you to Tchaplino, ten miles away?' 'I am going to stay

[002-108]the night at Sophron's there.' 'But stay the night here.' 'No, I

[002-109]can't.' And Yermolaï, with his Valetka, would go off into the dark

[002-110]night, through woods and water-courses, and the peasant Sophron very

[002-111]likely did not let him into his place, and even, I am afraid, gave him

[002-112]a blow to teach him 'not to disturb honest folks.' But none could

[002-113]compare with Yermolaï in skill in deep-water fishing in spring-time, in

[002-114]catching crayfish with his hands, in tracking game by scent, in snaring

[002-115]quails, in training hawks, in capturing the nightingales who had the

[002-116]greatest variety of notes. ... One thing he could not do, train a dog;

[002-117]he had not patience enough. He had a wife too. He went to see her once

[002-118]a week. She lived in a wretched, tumble-down little hut, and led a

[002-119]hand-to-mouth existence, never knowing overnight whether she would have

[002-120]food to eat on the morrow; and in every way her lot was a pitiful one.

[002-121]Yermolaï, who seemed such a careless and easy-going fellow, treated his

[002-122]wife with cruel harshness; in his own house he assumed a stern, and

[002-123]menacing manner; and his poor wife did everything she could to please

[002-124]him, trembled when he looked at her, and spent her last farthing to buy

[002-125]him vodka; and when he stretched himself majestically on the stove and

[002-126]fell into an heroic sleep, she obsequiously covered him with a

[002-127]sheepskin. I happened myself more than once to catch an involuntary

[002-128]look in him of a kind of savage ferocity; I did not like the expression

[002-129]of his face when he finished off a wounded bird with his teeth. But

[002-130]Yermolaï never remained more than a day at home, and away from home he

[002-131]was once more the same 'Yermolka' (i.e. the shooting-cap), as he was

[002-132]called for a hundred miles round, and as he sometimes called himself.

[002-133]The lowest house-serf was conscious of being superior to this

[002-134]vagabond--and perhaps this was precisely why they treated him with

[002-135]friendliness; the peasants at first amused themselves by chasing him

[002-136]and driving him like a hare over the open country, but afterwards they

[002-137]left him in God's hands, and when once they recognised him as 'queer,'

[002-138]they no longer tormented him, and even gave him bread and entered into

[002-139]talk with him.... This was the man I took as my huntsman, and with him

[002-140]I went stand-shooting to a great birch-wood on the banks of the Ista.

[002-141]Many Russian rivers, like the Volga, have one bank rugged and

[002-142]precipitous, the other bounded by level meadows; and so it is with the

[002-143]Ista. This small river winds extremely capriciously, coils like a

[002-144]snake, and does not keep a straight course for half-a-mile together; in

[002-145]some places, from the top of a sharp declivity, one can see the river

[002-146]for ten miles, with its dykes, its pools and mills, and the gardens on

[002-147]its banks, shut in with willows and thick flower-gardens. There are

[002-148]fish in the Ista in endless numbers, especially roaches (the peasants

[002-149]take them in hot weather from under the bushes with their hands);

[002-150]little sand-pipers flutter whistling along the stony banks, which are

[002-151]streaked with cold clear streams; wild ducks dive in the middle of the

[002-152]pools, and look round warily; in the coves under the overhanging cliffs

[002-153]herons stand out in the shade.... We stood in ambush nearly an hour,

[002-154]killed two brace of wood snipe, and, as we wanted to try our luck again

[002-155]at sunrise (stand-shooting can be done as well in the early morning),

[002-156]we resolved to spend the night at the nearest mill. We came out of the

[002-157]wood, and went down the slope. The dark-blue waters of the river ran

[002-158]below; the air was thick with the mists of night. We knocked at the

[002-159]gate. The dogs began barking in the yard.

[002-160]'Who is there?' asked a hoarse and sleepy voice.

[002-161]'We are sportsmen; let us stay the night.' There was no reply. 'We will

[002-162]pay.'

[002-163]'I will go and tell the master--Sh! Curse the dogs! Go to the devil

[002-164]with you!'

[002-165]We listened as the workman went into the cottage; he soon came back to

[002-166]the gate. 'No,' he said; 'the master tells me not to let you in.'

[002-167]'Why not?'

[002-168]'He is afraid; you are sportsmen; you might set the mill on fire;

[002-169]you've firearms with you, to be sure.'

[002-170]'But what nonsense!'

[002-171]'We had our mill on fire like that last year; some fish-dealers stayed

[002-172]the night, and they managed to set it on fire somehow.'

[002-173]'But, my good friend, we can't sleep in the open air!'

[002-174]'That's your business.' He went away, his boots clacking as he walked.

[002-175]Yermolaï promised him various unpleasant things in the future. 'Let us

[002-176]go to the village,' he brought out at last, with a sigh. But it was two

[002-177]miles to the village.

[002-178]'Let us stay the night here,' I said, 'in the open air--the night is

[002-179]warm; the miller will let us have some straw if we pay for it.'

[002-180]Yermolaï agreed without discussion. We began again to knock.

[002-181]'Well, what do you want?' the workman's voice was heard again; 'I've

[002-182]told you we can't.'

[002-183]We explained to him what we wanted. He went to consult the master of

[002-184]the house, and returned with him. The little side gate creaked. The

[002-185]miller appeared, a tall, fat-faced man with a bull-neck, round-bellied

[002-186]and corpulent. He agreed to my proposal. A hundred paces from the mill

[002-187]there was a little outhouse open to the air on all sides. They carried

[002-188]straw and hay there for us; the workman set a samovar down on the grass

[002-189]near the river, and, squatting on his heels, began to blow vigorously

[002-190]into the pipe of it. The embers glowed, and threw a bright light on his

[002-191]young face. The miller ran to wake his wife, and suggested at last that

[002-192]I myself should sleep in the cottage; but I preferred to remain in the

[002-193]open air. The miller's wife brought us milk, eggs, potatoes and bread.

[002-194]Soon the samovar boiled, and we began drinking tea. A mist had risen

[002-195]from the river; there was no wind; from all round came the cry of the

[002-196]corn-crake, and faint sounds from the mill-wheels of drops that dripped

[002-197]from the paddles and of water gurgling through the bars of the lock. We

[002-198]built a small fire on the ground. While Yermolaï was baking the

[002-199]potatoes in the embers, I had time to fall into a doze. I was waked by

[002-200]a discreetly-subdued whispering near me. I lifted my head; before the

[002-201]fire, on a tub turned upside down, the miller's wife sat talking to my

[002-202]huntsman. By her dress, her movements, and her manner of speaking, I

[002-203]had already recognised that she had been in domestic service, and was

[002-204]neither peasant nor city-bred; but now for the first time I got a clear

[002-205]view of her features. She looked about thirty; her thin, pale face

[002-206]still showed the traces of remarkable beauty; what particularly charmed

[002-207]me was her eyes, large and mournful in expression. She was leaning her

[002-208]elbows on her knees, and had her face in her hands. Yermolaï was

[002-209]sitting with his back to me, and thrusting sticks into the fire.
