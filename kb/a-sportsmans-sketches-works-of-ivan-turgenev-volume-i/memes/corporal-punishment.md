# Corporal Punishment and Cruelty

If [[Serfdom]] is the book's great institution, corporal punishment is how that institution is felt on the body. Turgenev rarely stages the beating itself; instead he catches the casual ease with which masters order it and the terrible calm with which serfs accept it.

## The Flogging and the Tea

In [[two-country-gentlemen|Two Country Gentlemen]], the narrator sits at tea with the amiable landowner [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] when the sound of blows drifts in from the stable. His host does not even set down his saucer:

> (013-277) ...with the most good-natured smile imaginable, he murmured as though involuntarily accompanying the blows: 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk! Tchuki-tchuk!'

He explains it as the most ordinary of household matters:

> (013-280) 'Oh, by my order, they're punishing a scamp of a fellow.... Do you happen to remember Vasya, who waits at the sideboard?'

## "I Deserved It"

The cruelty is complete only because the victim has learned to justify it. Meeting the flogged Vasya on the road afterwards, the narrator hears not complaint but assent:

> (013-299) 'Oh, I deserved it, father; I deserved it. They don't punish for trifles among us; that's not the way with us--no, no.'

## Beating in the Counting-House

The same violence runs through [[the-counting-house|The Counting-House]], where a clerk's spite falls on the serf-girl Tatyana:

> (011-557) And it's not your doing that she's beaten and dressed in sackcloth?

## Social and Historical Context

Turgenev's method is understatement: the horror lies in the good-natured smile, the rhythm tapped out over tea, the victim's cheerful "I deserved it." By refusing melodrama he shows how bondage deforms master and serf alike — the one made careless, the other made grateful for his own beating. It is the quiet centre of the book's case against [[Serfdom|serfdom]].
