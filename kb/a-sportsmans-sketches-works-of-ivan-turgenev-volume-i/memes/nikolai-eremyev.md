# Nikolai Eremyev

## Overview

Nikolai Eremyev — universally addressed as Nikolai Eremyitch — is the head-clerk and de facto steward of the [[ananyevo|Ananyevo]] estate belonging to Madame [[Elena Nikolaevna Losnyakov]] in Turgenev's sketch "[[The Counting-House]]." Though formally a serf himself, Eremyev wields enormous delegated authority over the estate's affairs: he negotiates grain sales with merchants, receives petitions from [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, and controls access to the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]. He is, in the words of a merchant suing for his favour, "one may say, the first person here" (011-238). Turgenev's portrait of Eremyev exposes the internal corruption of the [[Serfdom|serf]] bureaucracy — a man who, having once known disgrace himself, has become the most zealous enforcer of the very system that ground him down. Unlike the cultivated cruelty of a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] like [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]], Eremyev's tyranny is coarse, direct, and entirely unadorned by European manners.

## Appearance and Position

Eremyev's physical presence announces his character before he speaks a word. When he first emerges from a back room of the counting-house, Turgenev renders him with unsparing precision:

> [011-087–011-090] a stout, short man of fifty, with a bull neck, goggle-eyes, extraordinarily round cheeks, and his whole face positively shining with sleekness.

The sleekness is not merely physical; it is the outward sign of a life spent feeding well at the estate's expense. Eremyev was formerly the chief cashier but has been promoted to head-clerk (011-140–011-141), a position that makes him, in practice, the most powerful figure on the estate below the mistress herself. A signboard outside the office proclaims in bold letters: "Head-Clerk, NIKOLAI HVOSTOV" (011-179). The surname Hvostov — derived from the Russian word for "tail" — carries a suggestion of the servile, fawning quality that Eremyev deploys upward while lashing downward. The office itself, with its tables heaped with papers, pewter inkstands, and heraldic seals, is the stage on which Eremyev performs his authority through the elaborate rituals of bureaucratic procedure.

## The Grain Negotiation: Economic Power and Corruption

Eremyev's real power is most nakedly revealed in the scene the narrator overhears through a crack in the partition — a grain negotiation with the merchant [[gavrila-antonitch|Gavrila Antonitch]]. The merchant has already agreed to pay six and a half roubles per measure into the estate's official accounts; what remains to be settled is Eremyev's personal commission:

> [011-247] "Eight roubles a measure," said the fat man emphatically.

The bargaining that follows is a masterclass in bureaucratic extraction. Eremyev invokes God Almighty to justify his price, alternates between feigned indifference and hard insistence, and makes the merchant dance through every pose of supplication:

> [011-233] "Quite so, quite so, Nikolai Eremyitch," one voice was saying; "quite so. One can't but take that into account; yes, certainly!... Hm!"

When the merchant finally capitulates — three and a half grey notes for Eremyev's pocket, six and a half officially to the house — Eremyev mutters to himself: "I ought to have got four, but I was in too great a hurry — like an ass!" (011-291–011-292). The deal is then sealed with a handshake, and the merchant is instructed to tell the mistress that "Nikolai Eremyitch has made a bargain with me for six and a half" (011-300–011-301). The entire transaction is lubricated by the shared understanding that the official price is a fiction and the real negotiation concerns what the clerk will skim.

## The Bullying of Inferiors

If Eremyev is servile to those above him — the mistress, the rhythms of her favour — he is a tyrant to those below. The scene with the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] Sidor, who comes from the village of [[goloplek|Goloplek]] to petition on behalf of his fellow carpenters, reveals the full machinery of Eremyev's intimidation. Sidor explains that the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s are being called away during the busy season, that the pay is inadequate, and that a week's work stretches into a month of hanging about:

> [011-352–011-354] "Busy time! I dare say, you're so eager to work for outsiders, and don't care to work for your mistress.... It's all the same!"

When Sidor perseveres — "Our ... [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s ... Nikolai Eremyitch ... sent word to your honour ... there is ... see here..." — and begins pulling a folded kerchief (presumably containing a bribe) from his coat, Eremyev panics at the indiscretion. He shoves Sidor out of the room, directing him to his wife's cottage for tea, where the bribe can be discreetly received: "For goodness' sake, I tell you, go on" (011-376). After Sidor departs, Eremyev mutters "Ugh!... what a bear!" (011-378), contemptuous of the very [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s he fleeces.

His treatment of the degraded tailor [[kuprya|Kuprya]], who has been turned into a stoker, shows Eremyev at his most sadistic. He taunts [[kuprya|Kuprya]] complacently: "is it bad being stoker? Is it an easy job, eh?" (011-448). When [[kuprya|Kuprya]] dares to remind him that he too was once in disgrace — "you know you have been in disgrace yourself, and you too have lived in a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s hut" (011-450–011-451) — Eremyev's geniality evaporates instantly:

> [011-452–011-455] "You'd better look out and not forget yourself in my place," the fat man interrupted emphatically; "people joke with a fool like you; you ought, you fool, to have sense, and be grateful to them for taking notice of a fool like you."

The eruption reveals the fragility of Eremyev's authority: it rests entirely on his current position, and any reminder of his own past humiliation threatens the performance of power he sustains.

## The Tatyana Affair

Eremyev's most vicious exercise of power concerns the servant girl [[tatyana|Tatyana]] and her suitor [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel Andreitch]], an apothecary's assistant attached to the estate. [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] accuses Eremyev of a sustained campaign of persecution: he has conspired with the housekeeper to spread slander, engineered [[tatyana|Tatyana]]'s degradation from laundrymaid to scullery drudge, and ensured she is beaten and dressed in sackcloth — all to prevent her marriage to [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]]:

> [011-553–011-558] "Why, haven't you been plotting with that old hag, the housekeeper, eh? Haven't you been telling tales, eh? Tell me, aren't you bringing all sorts of stories up against the defenceless girl? I suppose it's not your doing that she's been degraded from laundrymaid to washing dishes in the scullery? And it's not your doing that she's beaten and dressed in sackcloth?"

Eremyev's motive is never explicitly stated — perhaps it is revenge, perhaps desire, perhaps simply the reflex of a man who must crush whatever he cannot control. When [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] demands an explanation, Eremyev retreats behind the mistress's authority: "The mistress won't permit you to marry; it's her seignorial will! What have I to do with it?" (011-551–011-552). It is the same evasion deployed by every intermediary in the serf system: the agent claims to be merely executing orders, while the mistress remains insulated from knowledge of what her orders produce.

The confrontation escalates into violence. When Eremyev boasts that he "got the better of your father; I broke his horns — a warning to you" (011-590–011-591), [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] strikes him, and the fat clerk rolls heavily to the floor, groaning "Handcuff him, handcuff him" (011-602). The scene is the sketch's one moment of physical resistance against bureaucratic tyranny — but its aftermath is bleak. A week later the narrator learns that [[elena-nikolaevna-losnyakov|Madame Losnyakov]] has kept both Eremyev and [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] in her service, but has sent away [[tatyana|Tatyana]]: "it appeared she was not wanted" (011-607). The victim is discarded; the oppressor remains.

## Historical Significance

Nikolai Eremyev belongs to a gallery of petty bureaucratic figures through whom Turgenev anatomizes the serf system from the inside. He is the counterpart to [[Sofron]] in "[[The Agent]]" — both are serfs risen to power who exploit their fellow [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, but where [[sofron|Sofron]] is a virtuoso of flattery who weeps and kneels before his master, Eremyev is a cruder operator whose corruption is conducted in plain sight. Together they demonstrate that serfdom corrupts not only the masters but the entire chain of delegated authority: the system creates Eremyevs because it requires intermediaries, and it protects them because replacing them would require the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] to know what they do.

Eremyev's previous disgrace — his time in a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s hut — is the biographical key to his character. He is a man who has tasted humiliation and resolved never to taste it again. His cruelty is the cruelty of the survivor who has identified entirely with the power that once crushed him.
