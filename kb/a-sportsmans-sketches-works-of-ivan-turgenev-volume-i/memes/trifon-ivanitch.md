# Trifon Ivanitch

## Overview

The District Doctor — who gives his name as Trifon Ivanitch in the narrative — is the narrator and protagonist of "[[The District Doctor]]," one of the most formally unusual sketches in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. The outer frame is set by Turgenev's hunter-narrator, who falls ill in a provincial town and summons a doctor. The doctor, "a sensible fellow" who "expressed himself with vigour and some humour" (004-011), stays to talk, and the conversation turns into a confession: the story of how he fell in love with a dying patient twenty years ago. The result is a narrative within a narrative — the doctor's story takes over the sketch entirely, told in his own voice, with the hunter-narrator almost invisible as audience. Trifon Ivanitch is thus both character and storyteller, and his self-presentation as a man who cannot quite believe what happened to him is one of Turgenev's subtlest studies in the gap between professional competence and emotional exposure.

## The Frame

The doctor first appears as a type — "a thin, dark-haired man of middle height" who "prescribed me the usual sudorific, ordered a mustard-plaster to be put on, very deftly slid a five-rouble note up his sleeve, coughing drily and looking away as he did so" (004-004–004-007). The detail of the five-rouble note is essential: Trifon Ivanitch is a poor man who depends on fees, and his slight embarrassment at taking money establishes him not as greedy but as a man for whom every rouble matters. Turgenev's narrator notes the smallness of the fee without comment: the doctor's world is one of constant economic calculation, and this will color every aspect of the story he tells.

When asked to stay for tea, the doctor "began to converse freely" (004-010), and the narrator observes a truth about human intimacy: "Queer things happen in the world: you may live a long while with some people, and be on friendly terms with them, and never once speak openly with them from your soul; with others you have scarcely time to get acquainted, and all at once you are pouring out to him" (004-012–004-015). The observation licenses the confession that follows — an intimate story told to a stranger because a stranger has no stake in the telling.

## The Summons

The doctor's story begins with a call in the night. He is playing preference at the judge's house when a servant brings a note: a widow's daughter is dying, and the horses have been sent. His response is immediately professional and immediately economic:

> "She was twenty miles from the town, and it was midnight out of doors, and the roads in such a state, my word! And as she was poor herself, one could not expect more than two silver roubles, and even that problematic; and perhaps it might only be a matter of a roll of linen and a sack of oatmeal in payment. However, duty, you know, before everything: a fellow-creature may be dying." (004-036–004-041)

The sentences move from calculation to conscience in a single breath — this is the doctor's characteristic rhythm throughout the story. He is never unaware of what things cost, but he is never defined by that awareness either.

The journey is rendered with a vividness that makes it the story's first test: "a wretched little trap" with "[[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s horses, fat — too fat — and their coat as shaggy as felt; and the coachman sitting with his cap off out of respect" (004-043–004-045). The doctor reads the signs professionally: "If the coachman sits like a prince, and doesn't touch his cap, and even sneers at you behind his beard, and flicks his whip — then you may bet on six roubles. But this case, I saw, it was very different" (004-048–004-050). The economic semiotics of medical practice — reading poverty and wealth from the coachman's posture — is absorbed into the doctor's way of seeing the world.

## The Patient

The patient is [[alexandra-andreyevna|Alexandra Andreyevna]], a girl of twenty, burning with fever. The doctor's first sight of her stops him:

> "I had never seen such a face! — she was a beauty, in a word! I felt quite shaken with pity. Such lovely features; such eyes!" (004-068–004-069)

The professional becomes personal almost instantly. After bleeding her and prescribing a mixture, the doctor cannot sleep: "I could not get my patient out of my head" (004-086). He rises in the night and goes to her room, and what follows is the story's turning point:

> "The patient lay with her face towards me, and her arms flung wide apart, poor girl! I went up to her ... when suddenly she opened her eyes and fixed them full upon me!... 'Who is it?' she murmured. I was in confusion. 'Don't be alarmed, madam,' I say; 'I am the doctor; I have come to see how you feel.'" (004-091–004-095)

The confusion is not medical — he knows exactly what he is doing — but existential. In the darkness, beside a beautiful dying girl, the "doctor" is a role that suddenly feels like a costume.

## The Love That Cannot Be Named

The story that unfolds over the following days is one of the strangest love affairs in Russian literature. [[alexandra-andreyevna|Alexandra Andreyevna]], fully aware that she is dying, falls in love with her doctor with an intensity that her fever only partly explains. She confesses her love to her mother, extracts a promise from the doctor that he will marry no one else, and dies holding his hand:

> "Give me your hand," she says... I gave her my hand. "Well, forgive me," she cried suddenly; "forgive me; I was wrong.... Give me your hand — I love you." (004-162–004-170, paraphrased)

The doctor's narration at this point becomes increasingly broken, interrupted by snuff-taking and long pauses. "At this point the doctor again took snuff with exasperated energy, and for an instant became as it were frozen" (004-112). The gestures of narration — the snuff, the pauses, the sudden starts — are the only way the doctor can manage the emotion his story releases. Twenty years later, he is still not over it.

The story ends with death and a kind of marriage. [[alexandra-andreyevna|Alexandra Andreyevna]] dies, and the doctor marries a merchant's daughter — "a good sort of woman, though she is a little hasty" — but the marriage is an afterthought, described in a single sentence. The real love, the one that shaped him, is the one that lasted three weeks and ended with a dead girl's hand in his.

## The Art of the Telling

What distinguishes Trifon Ivanitch's story from a simple tale of doomed love is the quality of his narration. He is a "sensible fellow" who tells his story with ironic self-awareness, never asking for pity, never inflating his experience into tragedy. His frequent use of the word "suddenly" (the narrator notes this as a verbal tic) is the trace of a man for whom life changed without warning and who has never quite regained his footing. The story's power comes from the gap between the doctor's plain, almost clinical language and the extremity of what he describes — a gap that Turgenev, through the frame narrator, leaves the reader to fill.
