# Viktor Hlopakov

## Overview

Viktor Hlopakov is an ex-lieutenant of about thirty who frequents elections and horse-fairs, attaching himself to wealthy young rakes from [[petersburg|Petersburg]] as a kind of professional hanger-on. He appears in Turgenev's sketch "[[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]]" (Chapter XIV), where the narrator encounters him playing billiards with [[prince-n|Prince N----]] at the local inn. Hlopakov is a figure of comic pathos — a man who has squandered his own property and now survives entirely on the favour of rich acquaintances, sustained by nothing more than a single absurd catchphrase that inexplicably makes his patrons laugh (014-119).

## Appearance & Character

Hlopakov is described as a little, thinnish, dark man with black hair, brown eyes, and a thick snub nose (014-106). His manner is jovial and swaggering: he walks with a skip and a hop, waves his fat hands expansively, cocks his cap on one side, and tucks up the sleeves of his military coat to reveal its blue-black cotton lining (014-111). Turgenev's portrait is cuttingly precise. Hlopakov is not clever, not amusing, not even a buffoon — and yet wealthy young men treat him with "friendly casualness, as a good-natured fellow, but rather a fool," keeping him around for two or three weeks before abruptly ceasing to recognise him in the street (014-119).

The one secret of Hlopakov's survival is his catchphrase. For a year or two at a time, he uses a single expression in season and out of season — and for some inexplicable reason, it makes everyone laugh. Eight years before the story, his phrase was "'Umble respecks and duty"; later it became "No, that's too, too k'essk'say"; and at the time of the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] fair, it is the nonsense word "Rrrrrakaliooon" (014-119). These "not overwhelmingly witty phrases" keep him "in food and drink and clothes," even though he has run through his own property ages ago (014-119). Turgenev notes that Hlopakov is at least discreet — he has "no taste for washing dirty linen away from home, never speaks a word against anyone" (014-119).

## Role in the Story

The narrator first observes Hlopakov at the billiard-table in the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] inn, playing with [[prince-n|Prince N----]] (014-105). Whenever the prince makes a good shot or Hlopakov misses, the ex-lieutenant deploys his catchphrase: "Rrrrrakaliooon, old boy," accompanied by a sly wink of his left eye (014-171). Each time, the prince roars with laughter, and the phrase echoes through the smoke-filled room. The narrator watches this performance with a mixture of curiosity and mild disgust, wondering what the current catchword might be, before retreating to his room (014-141).

The following day, Hlopakov reappears during the horse-dealing scene at [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]'s yard. He arrives in [[prince-n|Prince N----]]'s smart trap, driven by the dissolute groom [[baklaga|Baklaga]], and descends slowly while the prince leaps out (014-295). Throughout the bargaining for the splendid horse Peacock, Hlopakov punctuates the negotiations with his catchphrase — "Oh, rrakalion! Zhaymsah!" and "I tell you three, rrakalion" — serving as the prince's comic echo (014-306, 014-317). The narrator leaves without witnessing the conclusion of the sale.

By the end of the sketch, a striking reversal has occurred. The narrator briefly encounters Hlopakov once more, and "the fortunes of Mr. Hlopakov had taken place in this interval" — he appears "downcast," shrunken into a corner, "furtively" glancing about, suggesting that his luck with patrons has turned once again (014-419, 014-424).

## Historical/Social Context

Hlopakov represents a recognisable social type in provincial Russia — the declasse gentleman who has lost his estate and now survives by attaching himself to wealthier men, providing flattery and comic relief in exchange for food, drink, and lodging. Turgenev sketches this figure with characteristic economy: Hlopakov is at once ridiculous and pitiable, a man whose entire existence depends on the whims of rich young rakes who will discard him without a second thought. His anonymity — he has no first name or patronymic in the story, only a surname and a former rank — underscores his rootlessness and the precariousness of his position.
