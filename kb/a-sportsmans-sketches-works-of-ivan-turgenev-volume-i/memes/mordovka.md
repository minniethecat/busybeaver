# Mordovka

## Overview

Mordovka is a village referenced in Turgenev's sketch "[[Lebedyan]]," the final chapter of the first volume. It appears in the opening passage of the chapter, where the narrator muses on the pleasures and discomforts of constant travel — Mordovka stands as an example of the small, obscure settlements through which the hunter must navigate his way.

## Description

The narrator introduces Mordovka in a tone of comic exasperation. He describes the experience of being "forced to be constantly moving from place to place" (014-002), and the particular indignity of having "to stop every [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] you meet with the question, 'Hey! my good man! how are we to get to Mordovka?'" (014-006). The village's obscurity is its defining feature — it is not a destination but an obstacle, a waypoint so insignificant that even the local [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s must be consulted for directions. Turgenev immediately compounds this sense of disorientation: "at Mordovka to try to extract from a half-witted [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] woman (the working population are all in the fields) whether it is far to an inn on the high-road" (014-007). The village, emptied of its able-bodied inhabitants, offers no reliable guidance.

## Role in the Stories

Mordovka serves as an opening emblem for "[[Lebedyan]]," a chapter concerned with the fair, commerce, and the bustling world of horse-dealing. By beginning with the disoriented search for an insignificant village, Turgenev establishes the contrast between the empty countryside and the lively town of [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] that awaits. Mordovka also exemplifies the narrator's self-deprecating humor — he is a gentleman hunter perpetually lost among [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s who know the land better than he does.
