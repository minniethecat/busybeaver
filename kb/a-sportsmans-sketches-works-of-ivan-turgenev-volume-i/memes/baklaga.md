## Overview

Baklaga is [[prince-n|Prince N----]]'s young groom who appears briefly in "[[Lebedyan]]." (014-199) He is described as handsome, excessively depraved, and the object of the prince's intense favour. His most striking appearance is at the reins of the prince's magnificent troika, driving through [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] with spectacular skill. (014-286)

## Appearance and Character

Baklaga's name is introduced incidentally — a waiter announces that "the groom Baklaga was asking for him," and the prince immediately orders vodka sent to him. (014-196) The narrator then provides the character's full description:

> Baklaga, as I was told afterwards, was the name of a youthful, handsome, and excessively depraved groom; the prince loved him, made him presents of horses, went out hunting with him, spent whole nights with him. (014-199)

The narrator notes the irony of this relationship by contrasting it with the prince's present-day reputation: "Now you would not know this same prince, who was once a rake and a scapegrace.… In what good odour he is now; how straight-laced, how supercilious!" (014-202)

## Role in the Story

### The Troika Drive

Baklaga's dramatic entrance occurs during the narrator's horse-trading with the dealer [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]. A splendid team of three posting-horses draws up sharply at the gate, carrying [[prince-n|Prince N----]] with Hlopakov:

> In the smart little sportsman's trap sat Prince N----; beside him Hlopakov. Baklaga was driving … and how he drove! He could have driven them through an earring, the rascal! (014-286)

The narrator lingers on the magnificence of the horses — bay trace-horses, "little, keen, black-eyed, black-legged," and a dark-bay shaft-horse with "its neck arched like a swan's, its breast forward, its legs like arrows." (014-288, 014-291) Baklaga's skill at the reins completes this image of aristocratic ostentation.

### Prince N----'s Favourite

Baklaga belongs to a recognizable Turgenev type: the handsome [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] favourite of a wealthy master. The prince's gifts — horses, hunting expeditions, "whole nights" together — suggest a relationship that exceeds the ordinary master-servant bond. The narrator's word "depraved" registers moral judgment, but his focus remains on the social observation: the same prince who once kept this reckless favourite now moves in the highest circles of respectability.

## Significance

Baklaga is a minor but vivid figure whose brief appearance illuminates [[prince-n|Prince N----]]'s character and the hidden lives of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. The contrast between the prince's rakish past and his present "straight-laced" respectability is a small but pointed example of Turgenev's irony toward the Russian landowning class.
