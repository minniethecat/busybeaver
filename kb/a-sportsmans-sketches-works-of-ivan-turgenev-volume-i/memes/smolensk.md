# Smolensk

## Overview

Smolensk is a Russian region that appears in [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]] as the setting of a comic and cruel episode from the War of 1812. It is the place where the French drummer [[Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune]] — later a respectable Russian [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] of [[orel-province|Orel Province]] — first fell into Russian hands during Napoleon's retreat.

Smolensk thus serves as a hinge between two worlds in the *[[A Sportsman's Sketches, Volume I|Sketches]]*: the historical drama of the Napoleonic invasion, and the settled provincial society in which its survivors ended their days.

## Description

Turgenev never describes the town of Smolensk itself; it exists in the story only through its [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and its frozen landscape. Retreating from [[moscow|Moscow]], "poor [[frantz-ivanitch-lejeune|Monsieur Lejeune]], half-frozen and without his drum, fell into the hands of some [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s of Smolensk" (006-565).

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s "shut him up for the night in an empty cloth factory" (006-566), fixing the scene in the industrial fringe of the province, and "the next morning brought him to an ice-hole near the dyke" (006-567), intending to make the drummer swim under the ice. Ordinary provincial life — cloth factories, dykes, rivers — has been turned to the grim business of dealing with a captured enemy.

## Role in the Stories

Smolensk is the stage for the turning point of Lejeune's improbable life. Unable to accept the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' proposition, he "began to try to persuade the Smolensk [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, in the dialect of France, to let him go to [[Orleans]]" (006-571), pleading that "my mother is living, une tendre mère" (006-572).

The [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, ignorant of the geography of France, instead offered him "a journey under water along the course of the meandering river [[Gniloterka]]" (006-573). It is only the chance intervention of a passing [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] — who asks whether the Frenchman knows music — that saves him and sets him on the road to becoming a Russian nobleman.

Smolensk is therefore the place where Lejeune's transformation begins: the point at which a soldier of Napoleon's army is nearly drowned by Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and instead absorbed, permanently, into Russian provincial life.

## Historical Context

The episode reflects the real fate of thousands of stragglers from the Grande Armée during the winter retreat of 1812, when French soldiers who escaped the fighting often fell to [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] reprisals or the cold. Smolensk, on the western road between [[moscow|Moscow]] and the frontier, was a natural point of capture.

In [[Ovsyanikov]]'s telling the historical catastrophe is refracted through provincial memory and softened into anecdote, but the menace of the ice-hole keeps the danger real.
