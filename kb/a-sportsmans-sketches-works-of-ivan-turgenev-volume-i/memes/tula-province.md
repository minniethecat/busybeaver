# Tula Province

## Overview

Tula Province is a central Russian province referenced in multiple sketches of "[[A Sportsman's Sketches]]." It serves as the setting for "[[Byezhin Prairie]]," one of the collection's most celebrated chapters, and is mentioned in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]" as a source of merchants traveling to regional fairs. Along with [[Orel Province]] and [[Kaluga Province]], Tula forms part of the geographic triangulation that anchors Turgenev's narratives in a real, named landscape.

## Description

The province is described as a hunting ground in the opening of "[[Byezhin Prairie]]": the narrator finds himself in "the district of the province of Tula" (008-037) after a day's shooting. The passage continues: "I started and shot a fair amount of game; my full game-bag cut my shoulder mercilessly; but already the evening glow had faded, and the cool shades of twilight were beginning" (008-037–039). This description establishes Tula as a landscape of rich hunting — forests, meadows, and prairie — where a [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman can fill his bag in a single day.

In "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]," Tula is mentioned in the context of commerce: merchants "used to come from Tula, from [[harkoff|Harkoff]], from [[tamboff|Tamboff]], and from everywhere" (006-193) to the regional fairs, recalling a time when [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] estates were self-sufficient economic worlds. The reference frames Tula as one node in a wider commercial network.

## Role in the Stories

Tula Province anchors the geography of the collection. While [[Orel Province]] and [[Kaluga Province]] provide the opening contrast between serf types, Tula provides the setting for the collection's most famous nocturnal scene — the boys' ghost stories on [[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]. The province thus carries both the practical function of a hunting ground and the symbolic weight of a place where [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] folklore and supernatural belief survive.
