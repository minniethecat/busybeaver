# Byezhin Prairie

**Byezhin Prairie** (sometimes translated as Bezhin Meadow or Bezhin Lea) is the setting for one of the most celebrated sketches in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] — a remote stretch of grassland above the river Snyeged where, on a summer night, a group of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys gather to watch over a herd of horses and tell each other ghost stories. Through its luminous descriptions of the Russian night landscape and its unflinching portrait of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] childhood, the sketch transforms a simple pasture into a space of mingled beauty and terror.

## The Landscape

The prairie lies in a hollow surrounded by wooded hills, accessible only by a steep and difficult path. Turgenev's description of the approach — the narrator loses his way while returning from a day's hunting — establishes the prairie as a place apart, a threshold space between the familiar and the unknown:

> (008-127) This meadow is known in our parts under the name of Byezhin Prairie.... But there was no getting home that day; the surroundings were completely unknown to me.

The darkness that envelops the prairie is rendered with extraordinary precision. Turgenev distinguishes between degrees of darkness — the "white, vague darkness" of the summer night, the deeper black of the forest edge, the silver glint of the river below. The night sky, filled with countless stars, becomes almost a character in its own right, presiding over the human drama below.

## The Night Watch

The sketch's narrative center is the circle of five [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys — [[fedya|Fedya]], [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]], [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]], [[kostya|Kostya]], and [[vanya|Vanya]] — who sit around a campfire through the night, guarding the horses and telling tales. Each boy is sharply individualized:

| Boy | Age | Character | Role in the Group |
|-----|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| **[[fedya|Fedya]]** | ~14 | Well-dressed, handsome, from a richer family | Natural leader, slightly aloof |
| **[[pavlusha|Pavlusha]]** | ~12 | Plain-featured but brave, practical | The skeptic who confronts danger |
| **[[ilyusha|Ilyusha]]** | ~12 | Earnest, anxious, from a working family | The principal storyteller |
| **[[kostya|Kostya]]** | ~10 | Dreamy, melancholic, large dark eyes | Sensitive listener |
| **[[vanya|Vanya]]** | ~7 | The youngest, barely visible under his mat | Silent presence |

## Stories in the Night

The stories the boys tell — of the домовой (house spirit), of drowned men, of the ghost of a murdered forester, of the mysterious Trishka who will come at the end of the world — are drawn from the deep well of Russian [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] folklore. Turgenev presents these tales without condescension, understanding that they express real fears: of death, of the unknown, of the vast and indifferent natural world that surrounds the tiny circle of firelight.

> (008-191) The boys fell silent. Suddenly, somewhere in the distance, a long, resonant, almost moaning sound rang out — one of those inexplicable sounds of the night which rise up sometimes out of the profound stillness... It was as though someone had shouted long and loud beneath the very horizon.

## Pavlusha's Fate

The sketch ends with a brief, devastating coda. The narrator learns that [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]] — the brave, plain-faced boy who alone dared to ride after the wolves, who alone remained calm when the others trembled — died the following year, killed by a fall from a horse. This final paragraph transforms everything that came before: the night, the stories, the circle of firelight become, in retrospect, an elegy for a childhood already touched by the shadow of mortality.
