# The Hunting Expedition to Lgov

The **[[hunting-in-the-sketches|Hunting]] Expedition to [[lgov-place|Lgov]]** is one of the most vividly realized episodes in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]], combining the practical comedy of a hunting trip gone awry with a devastating portrait of human displacement under [[Serfdom|serfdom]]. What begins as [[Yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s casual suggestion — "Let us go to [[lgov-place|Lgov]]; there we can shoot ducks to our hearts' content" — becomes, through a series of encounters and improvisations, a miniature of Russian provincial life itself.

## The Proposal

The expedition originates in [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s characteristic confidence. [[lgov-place|Lgov]], he assures the narrator, offers duck-shooting of unparalleled abundance. The large pond five miles from the village, overgrown with thick reeds, harbors waterfowl in numbers that no other local spot can match. As always with [[yermolai|Yermolaï]], practical details — how to reach the birds through the reeds, where to obtain a boat — are treated as afterthoughts, problems to be solved on arrival rather than obstacles to planning.

> (007-001) "Let us go to Lgov," Yermolaï, whom the reader knows already, said to me one day. "There we can shoot ducks to our hearts' content."

## Improvisation and Discovery

Arriving at [[lgov-place|Lgov]], the party discovers that no serviceable boat exists. What follows is an exercise in [[yermolai|Yermolaï]]'s peculiar genius for improvisation. He locates a leaky old punt, secures tow to caulk the seams, and recruits a local man named [[vladimir-lgov|Vladimir]] — a retired valet with gentlemanly pretensions — to assist. The boat, when finally launched, inspires little confidence:

> (007-112) "That's no great disaster!" interposed Yermolaï; "we can stuff them up with tow." And he proceeded to caulk the boat's leaking seams with the cheerful indifference to risk that characterized all his enterprises.

## The Encounter with Sutchok

The expedition's true center of gravity is not the hunting but the encounter with **[[sutchok|Sutchok]]** ("The [[sutchok|Bough]]"), a man whose life story encapsulates the arbitrary cruelty of the serf system. [[sutchok|Sutchok]] — whose real name is Kuzma — has been, in succession, a Cossack, a cook, a coachman, an actor, a shoemaker, and finally a fisherman, each change dictated not by his own choice but by the whims of successive owners:

> (007-086) Sutchok had been passed from hand to hand like a worn-out garment, his occupations changing with each new master's caprice. He spoke of his transformations without resentment, as though describing the weather.

[[sutchok|Sutchok]]'s passive acceptance of his fate — he answers every question with the same mild, uncomplaining simplicity — is more disturbing than any protest could be. He has been so thoroughly shaped by servitude that he no longer perceives its injustice as remarkable.

## The Hunting

The hunting itself, when the party finally sets out, is modestly successful. [[yermolai|Yermolaï]] navigates through the reeds with his lynx-like eyesight, the narrator fires at ducks that rise in sudden flurries, and [[vladimir-lgov|Vladimir]] — terrified of the leaking boat — offers a running commentary of genteel complaint. The party returns to shore with a modest bag and a far richer harvest of human observation.
