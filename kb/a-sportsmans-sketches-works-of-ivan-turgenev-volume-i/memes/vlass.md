# Vlass

## Overview

Vlass appears in a single, brief scene in Turgenev's sketch "[[Raspberry Spring]]," yet his quiet distress leaves one of the most haunting impressions in *A Sportsman's Sketches*. He is a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] of about fifty who has walked to [[moscow|Moscow]] to petition his master, [[count-valerian-petrovitch|Count Valerian Petrovitch]], for a reduction of his obrok (quit-rent) after the death of his son, who had been the family's sole support. The master drives him away without hearing his case, and Vlass returns to his village with nothing — his face breaking into a smile that cannot conceal the tears starting into his "small, screwed-up eyes." Turgenev presents Vlass's story with an almost unbearable restraint: the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] relates his disaster "with a smile, as though he were speaking of someone else," while the narrator and the reader perceive the full weight of his despair.

## Appearance and Character

Vlass enters the scene on a sweltering summer afternoon, appearing at the spring where the narrator sits with [[Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)]] and [[Styopushka]]:

> I saw a peasant of about fifty, covered with dust, in a smock, and wearing bast slippers; he carried a wickerwork pannier and a cloak on his shoulders. He went down to the spring, drank thirstily, and got up. (003-218–003-223)

His physical exhaustion — the dust, the bast slippers, the meagre belongings — tells the story of a long journey on foot before he speaks a single word. When [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] greets him, Vlass's reply is laconic to the point of evasiveness: "from a long way off" (003-228). Throughout the conversation that follows, Vlass says remarkably little, and what he does say is marked by a disconcerting emotional dissonance — he smiles while describing his son's death, he laughs when contemplating his own financial ruin. The smile is not indifference but a protective reflex; it is the expression of a man who has been so systematically ground down that open grief is a luxury he cannot afford.

## The Journey to Moscow

Vlass's son had lived in [[moscow|Moscow]] as a cabman and had paid the obrok for his father. When the son fell ill and died, Vlass — unable to manage the rent alone — undertook the arduous journey to petition his master in person:

> 'I have been to Moscow, to my master.'
> 'What for?'
> 'I went to ask him a favour.'
> 'What about?'
> 'Oh, to lessen my rent, or to let me work it out in labour, or to put me on another piece of land, or something.... My son is dead—so I can't manage it now alone.' (003-228–003-237)

The tentative, trailing quality of Vlass's request — "or something" — reveals a man who cannot even formulate what he is asking for with precision, so distant is the master's world from his own. His son, who "paid, I must confess, rent for me" (003-240), had been the economic linchpin of the family; without him, the entire fragile structure collapses.

## The Master's Refusal

[[count-valerian-petrovitch|Count Valerian Petrovitch]] does not receive Vlass as a human being with a grievance but as an administrative inconvenience:

> 'What did the master say! He drove me away! Says he, "How dare you come straight to me; there is a bailiff for such things. You ought first," says he, "to apply to the bailiff ... and where am I to put you on other land? You first," says he, "bring the debt you owe." He was angry altogether.' (003-244–003-247)

The master's response is a perfect specimen of bureaucratic cruelty dressed as procedure. Vlass has violated the chain of command by approaching the count directly — an offense more serious, in the master's eyes, than the destitution that drove him to make the journey. The demand that Vlass first pay the debt he already cannot pay before any relief can be considered completes the circular logic of a system designed to extract the maximum from the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] while offering nothing in return.

## The Smile

The defining moment of Vlass's scene — and one of the most quietly devastating passages in all of *A Sportsman's Sketches* — is Turgenev's description of how Vlass tells his story:

> The peasant related all this with a smile, as though he were speaking of someone else; but tears were starting into his small, screwed-up eyes, and his lips were quivering. (003-248–003-251)

The smile is not mirth but a form of dissociation — the only defense available to a man who has been so thoroughly dehumanized that he can no longer claim his own suffering as his own. When [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] observes that "things are in a sad way, brother Vlass," the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s voice breaks on his reply: "'Sad! No!' (Vlass's voice broke.) 'How hot it is!' he went on, wiping his face with his sleeve" (003-260–003-262). The deflection to the weather is not incidental; it is the gesture of a man who has learned that his grief has no audience.

Vlass's journey ends in complete futility. He could not even recover his son's belongings: "I say to his employer, 'I am Philip's father'; and he says, 'What do I know about that? And your son,' says he, 'left nothing; he was even in debt to me'" (003-249–003-251). At every turn — master, employer, bailiff — Vlass encounters the same answer: nothing for you.

## Historical Significance

Vlass's story dramatizes the obrok system in its most destructive form. Under obrok, the serf paid an annual quit-rent to the master rather than performing labour service (barshchina); the serf was then free to seek wage work elsewhere to earn the rent. The system presupposed that the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] had access to income-generating labour — a presupposition that collapsed the moment the family's wage-earner died or was incapacitated. Vlass's predicament — unable to work the land alone, unable to pay the rent, unable to obtain relief — was not exceptional but typical. His ninety-five roubles of rent for "the least bit of land" (003-283–003-284) was a crushing sum that left a one-man household with no margin for catastrophe.

The master's invocation of the bailiff as the proper channel for petitions reflects another structural feature of serfdom: the dispersal of responsibility. The master claims to be bound by procedure; the bailiff claims to be bound by the master's orders; and between them the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] falls into an accountability gap where no one is responsible for his welfare.
