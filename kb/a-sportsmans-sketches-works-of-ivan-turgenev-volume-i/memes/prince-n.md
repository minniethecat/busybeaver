# Prince N----

## Overview

Prince N---- is a young aristocrat of twenty-two who appears in Turgenev's sketch "[[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]]" (Chapter XIV). He is encountered at the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] horse-fair, where he stays at the same inn as the narrator, plays billiards, and purchases horses from the dealer [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]. The prince is a representative type: the wealthy young [[petersburg|Petersburg]] rake on a provincial spending spree, surrounded by hangers-on, flattered by dealers, and ultimately indifferent to everything except his own amusement. He is the gravitational centre of the fair's social world, and his presence illuminates the economy of patronage, flattery, and display that governs provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] life.

## Appearance and Manner

The prince is introduced in the billiard-room of the [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] inn, where the air is thick with tobacco smoke and the clatter of balls:

> At the billiard-table was Prince N---- a young man of two-and-twenty, with a lively and rather contemptuous face, in a coat hanging open, a red silk shirt, and loose velvet pantaloons; he was playing with the ex-lieutenant, Viktor Hlopakov. (014-102)

The details are precise and eloquent: the "lively" face that is also "rather contemptuous," the open coat and the red silk shirt that announce wealth and insouciance. The prince does not dress to impress — he dresses as though no one's opinion matters. His companion is the professional hanger-on Hlopakov, whose presence immediately signals the prince's role as patron.

The prince's manner is one of effortless superiority. When a "fair-haired young officer" attempts to join the conversation with a remark about the billiard-shot, "the prince did not even vouchsafe him a look" (014-191). When a "thin gentleman in the dress of a civilian" offers an opinion, the prince "did not answer; he flung down his cue with a disdainful air" (014-200). His conversation consists of short commands, dismissive questions, and laughter at jokes that only he fully understands. He is not cruel so much as incurious: people who are not useful or amusing simply cease to exist for him.

## The Horse-Fair

The prince's role in the horse-fair is that of the ideal customer: wealthy, easily flattered, and surrounded by men who will validate his every choice. When he arrives at [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]]'s yard in a "splendidly-matched team of three posting-horses" driven by the dissolute groom [[baklaga|Baklaga]], the dealer abandons his bargaining with the narrator to attend to the prince (014-286). The deference is absolute: "Your excellency, please to come in!" cries [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]] (014-295). A bench is brought; the finest horse, Peacock, is led out from a special stable.

The prince plays his part with practiced ease. He inspects the horse, listens to the dealer's patter, and is accompanied throughout by Hlopakov's running commentary of nonsense catchphrases — "Oh, rrakalion! Zhaymsah!" (014-306) — at which the prince laughs on cue. The scene is a perfect miniature of the provincial economy: the dealer's flattery, the aristocrat's money, and the hanger-on's buffoonery, all revolving around the beautiful animal that is the occasion for their convergence.

## Fickleness

The prince's defining trait is his fickleness. The narrator, returning to [[lebedyan-place|Lebedyan]] a week later, finds the same scene with one crucial change:

> But the usual change in the fortunes of Mr. Hlopakov had taken place in this interval: the fair-haired young officer had supplanted him in the prince's favours. The poor ex-lieutenant once more tried letting off his catchword in my presence, on the chance it might succeed as before; but, far from smiling, the prince positively scowled and shrugged his shoulders. (014-419–014-424)

The prince's favour is absolute while it lasts and absolute in its withdrawal. Hlopakov, who a week earlier had ridden in the prince's own trap, is now ignored — his catchphrase, the magical formula that sustained him, has lost its power. The prince has moved on to a new companion, and Hlopakov shrinks "into a corner" and begins "furtively filling himself a pipe" (014-424). The prince himself is unchanged; it is the nature of his attention to alight and depart without explanation.

## Historical/Social Context

Prince N---- belongs to the highest stratum of the provincial social world depicted in the *Sketches*. His anonymity — the dash that replaces his name — is a convention of the period, used to suggest that the character is drawn from life and that discretion requires the elision. The prince is not a developed character; he is a social function. His role in the narrative is to embody the capriciousness of aristocratic patronage and the money that drives the provincial horse trade. He exists to be flattered by [[sitnikov|Sitnikov]], echoed by Hlopakov, and observed by the narrator — a fixed point in the social constellation around which lesser figures orbit and from which they are periodically ejected.
