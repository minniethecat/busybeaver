# Voronezh

## Overview

Voronezh is a southern Russian city named in [[The Counting-House]] as the distant edge of a traveller's journey through a countryside of promising crops. Like the other city-names of the *[[A Sportsman's Sketches, Volume I|Sketches]]*, it is never described directly; it serves to mark the breadth of the provincial world and to lend a concrete horizon to an idle conversation in the estate office.

## Description

Voronezh appears in a small exchange about the state of the harvest. A lean, pale man of about forty — [[Gavrila Antonitch]] — praises the season, remarking, "Wonderful the young green crops this year, one may say" (011-257), and adds that he has "been going about everywhere admiring them" (011-258).

His verdict stretches across a great distance: "All the way from Voronezh they've come up wonderfully, first-class, one may say" (011-259). The city thus fixes the southern limit of his travels and of the fine crops he has observed on the road.

## Role in the Stories

The mention of Voronezh grounds a moment of provincial small talk in real geography. The head-clerk answers [[gavrila-antonitch|Gavrila Antonitch]]'s optimism with a cautious proverb — "autumn bids fair, but spring may be foul" (011-261) — and the whole exchange, anchored by the road from Voronezh, sketches the rhythms of estate conversation.

Voronezh here is less a place than a measure of distance and of the reach of a good season: to say the crops are fine "all the way from Voronezh" is to claim that the promise extends across the whole southern country.
