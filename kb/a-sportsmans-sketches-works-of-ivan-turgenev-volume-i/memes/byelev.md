# Byelev

## Overview

Byelev is a town referenced in Turgenev's sketch "[[Yermolai and the Miller's Wife]]." It appears in a brief but revealing dialogue between two [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s whom the narrator and [[Yermolai]] encounter during their night journey. The exchange revolves around legal status — one man identifies himself as a Byelev townsman, free rather than a serf — making the town a touchstone for the distinction between bonded and free persons in rural Russia.

## Description

Byelev enters the narrative obliquely, through a roadside conversation. The narrator and [[yermolai|Yermolai]], traveling at night, pass two men by the roadside. [[yermolai|Yermolai]] calls out "'Hey! Good evening'" (002-253). The conversation that follows establishes one traveler's identity: "'From Byelev. He's a Byelev townsman'" (002-255). When the other traveler is asked "'And are you too from Byelev?'" (002-256), he answers "'No, I'm a serf; I was a serf'" (002-257). This brief exchange — three lines of dialogue — draws a sharp legal line: Byelev represents the world of free townsmen, standing in pointed contrast to the bonded [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]].

## Role in the Stories

The Byelev exchange is brief but thematically significant. The serf identifies his former master as "'[[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]]'" (002-259), and the narrator quietly notes: "I exchanged glances with [[yermolai|Yermolai]]" (002-261). The juxtaposition of the free Byelev townsman and the former serf embodies the divided legal landscape of pre-reform Russia — a division that runs through the entire collection. The Byelev townsman serves as living evidence that freedom was a legal possibility, not a universal condition.
