# BYEZHIN PRAIRIE

[008-001]It was a glorious July day, one of those days which only come after

[008-002]many days of fine weather. From earliest morning the sky is clear; the

[008-003]sunrise does not glow with fire; it is suffused with a soft roseate

[008-004]flush. The sun, not fiery, not red-hot as in time of stifling drought,

[008-005]not dull purple as before a storm, but with a bright and genial

[008-006]radiance, rises peacefully behind a long and narrow cloud, shines out

[008-007]freshly, and plunges again into its lilac mist. The delicate upper edge

[008-008]of the strip of cloud flashes in little gleaming snakes; their

[008-009]brilliance is like polished silver. But, lo! the dancing rays flash

[008-010]forth again, and in solemn joy, as though flying upward, rises the

[008-011]mighty orb. About mid-day there is wont to be, high up in the sky, a

[008-012]multitude of rounded clouds, golden-grey, with soft white edges. Like

[008-013]islands scattered over an overflowing river, that bathes them in its

[008-014]unbroken reaches of deep transparent blue, they scarcely stir; farther

[008-015]down the heavens they are in movement, packing closer; now there is no

[008-016]blue to be seen between them, but they are themselves almost as blue as

[008-017]the sky, filled full with light and heat. The colour of the horizon, a

[008-018]faint pale lilac, does not change all day, and is the same all round;

[008-019]nowhere is there storm gathering and darkening; only somewhere rays of

[008-020]bluish colour stretch down from the sky; it is a sprinkling of

[008-021]scarce-perceptible rain. In the evening these clouds disappear; the

[008-022]last of them, blackish and undefined as smoke, lie streaked with pink,

[008-023]facing the setting sun; in the place where it has gone down, as calmly

[008-024]as it rose, a crimson glow lingers long over the darkening earth, and,

[008-025]softly flashing like a candle carried carelessly, the evening star

[008-026]flickers in the sky. On such days all the colours are softened, bright

[008-027]but not glaring; everything is suffused with a kind of touching

[008-028]tenderness. On such days the heat is sometimes very great; often it is

[008-029]even 'steaming' on the slopes of the fields, but a wind dispels this

[008-030]growing sultriness, and whirling eddies of dust--sure sign of settled,

[008-031]fine weather--move along the roads and across the fields in high white

[008-032]columns. In the pure dry air there is a scent of wormwood, rye in

[008-033]blossom, and buckwheat; even an hour before nightfall there is no

[008-034]moisture in the air. It is for such weather that the farmer longs, for

[008-035]harvesting his wheat....

[008-036]On just such a day I was once out grouse-shooting in the Tchern

[008-037]district of the province of Tula. I started and shot a fair amount of

[008-038]game; my full game-bag cut my shoulder mercilessly; but already the

[008-039]evening glow had faded, and the cool shades of twilight were beginning

[008-040]to grow thicker, and to spread across the sky, which was still bright,

[008-041]though no longer lighted up by the rays of the setting sun, when I at

[008-042]last decided to turn back homewards. With swift steps I passed through

[008-043]the long 'square' of underwoods, clambered up a hill, and instead of

[008-044]the familiar plain I expected to see, with the oakwood on the right and

[008-045]the little white church in the distance, I saw before me a scene

[008-046]completely different, and quite new to me. A narrow valley lay at my

[008-047]feet, and directly facing me a dense wood of aspen-trees rose up like a

[008-048]thick wall. I stood still in perplexity, looked round me.... 'Aha!' I

[008-049]thought, 'I have somehow come wrong; I kept too much to the right,' and

[008-050]surprised at my own mistake, I rapidly descended the hill. I was at

[008-051]once plunged into a disagreeable clinging mist, exactly as though I had

[008-052]gone down into a cellar; the thick high grass at the bottom of the

[008-053]valley, all drenched with dew, was white like a smooth tablecloth; one

[008-054]felt afraid somehow to walk on it. I made haste to get on the other

[008-055]side, and walked along beside the aspenwood, bearing to the left. Bats

[008-056]were already hovering over its slumbering tree-tops, mysteriously

[008-057]flitting and quivering across the clear obscure of the sky; a young

[008-058]belated hawk flew in swift, straight course upwards, hastening to its

[008-059]nest. 'Here, directly I get to this corner,' I thought to myself, 'I

[008-060]shall find the road at once; but I have come a mile out of my way!'

[008-061]I did at last reach the end of the wood, but there was no road of any

[008-062]sort there; some kind of low bushes overgrown with long grass extended

[008-065]began ransacking my brain to recall how and where I had been walking

[008-066]during the day.... 'Ah! but these are the bushes at Parahin,' I cried

[008-067]at last; 'of course! then this must be Sindyev wood. But how did I get

[008-068]here? So far?... Strange! Now I must bear to the right again.'
