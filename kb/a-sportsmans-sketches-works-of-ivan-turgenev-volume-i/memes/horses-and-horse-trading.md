# Horses and the Horse-Fair

**Horses** are everywhere in [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]: they draw the narrator's trap from one hunt to the next, they are the peasant's chief possession, and once a year they become the centre of a great provincial spectacle. Turgenev's most concentrated treatment of them is the sketch [[lebedyan|Lebedyan]], built entirely around a horse-fair.

## The Horse-Fair at Lebedyan

The narrator explains how he came to be caught up in the fair almost by accident, in the middle of a hunting trip:

> (014-025) ...how I happened five years ago to be at Lebedyan just in the very thick of the horse-fair.

The fair is a crowd of every rank, presided over by the sharp-eyed dealers:

> (014-053) The avenues formed by the rows of carts were thronged with people of every class, age, and appearance; horse-dealers in long blue coats and high caps, with sly faces, were on the look-out for purchasers; gypsies, with staring eyes and curly heads, strolled up and down.

## Bargaining and the Trade

Buying a horse is a contest of endurance. With a gypsy go-between, buyer and seller wear each other down while the animal itself stands indifferent:

> (014-066) ...a wily gypsy, they were bargaining till they were exhausted, clasping each other's hands a hundred times over, each still sticking to his price, while the subject of their dispute, a wretched little jade covered with a shrunken mat, was blinking quite unmoved.

The narrator has a practical errand of his own among all this:

> (014-088) I wanted to buy a set of three horses for my covered trap; mine had begun to show signs of breaking down.

At the stables of the famous dealer Sitnikov the haggling turns comic, and is interrupted only by the grand arrival of a nobleman's troika:

> (014-282) Sitnikov asked an impossible price. We began bargaining on the spot in the street, when suddenly a splendidly-matched team of three posting-horses flew noisily round the corner and drew up sharply at the gates before Sitnikov's house.

## Social and Historical Context

The horse is the meeting-point of the book's social worlds. For the peasant it is capital and livelihood; for the [[Landowning Gentry|gentry]] it is sport, vanity and display — the prince's flying troika set against the peasant's "wretched little jade."

At the fair, dealers, gypsies, hussars and grandees all bargain in the same mud, and Turgenev lets the animals quietly measure the men who trade in them.
