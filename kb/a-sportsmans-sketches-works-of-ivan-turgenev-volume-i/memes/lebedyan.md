# LEBEDYAN

[014-001]One of the principal advantages of hunting, my dear readers, consists

[014-002]in its forcing you to be constantly moving from place to place, which

[014-003]is highly agreeable for a man of no occupation. It is true that

[014-004]sometimes, especially in wet weather, it's not over pleasant to roam

[014-005]over by-roads, to cut 'across country,' to stop every peasant you meet

[014-006]with the question, 'Hey! my good man! how are we to get to Mordovka?'

[014-007]and at Mordovka to try to extract from a half-witted peasant woman (the

[014-008]working population are all in the fields) whether it is far to an inn

[014-009]on the high-road, and how to get to it--and then when you have gone on

[014-010]eight miles farther, instead of an inn, to come upon the deserted

[014-011]village of Hudobubnova, to the great amazement of a whole herd of pigs,

[014-012]who have been wallowing up to their ears in the black mud in the middle

[014-013]of the village street, without the slightest anticipation of ever being

[014-014]disturbed. There is no great joy either in having to cross planks that

[014-015]dance under your feet; to drop down into ravines; to wade across boggy

[014-016]streams: it is not over-pleasant to tramp twenty-four hours on end

[014-017]through the sea of green that covers the highroads or (which God

[014-018]forbid!) stay for hours stuck in the mud before a striped milestone

[014-019]with the figures 22 on one side and 23 on the other; it is not wholly

[014-020]pleasant to live for weeks together on eggs, milk, and the rye-bread

[014-021]patriots affect to be so fond of.... But there is ample compensation

[014-022]for all these inconveniences and discomforts in pleasures and

[014-023]advantages of another sort. Let us come, though, to our story.

[014-024]After all I have said above, there is no need to explain to the reader

[014-025]how I happened five years ago to be at Lebedyan just in the very thick

[014-026]of the horse-fair. We sportsmen may often set off on a fine morning

[014-027]from our more or less ancestral roof, in the full intention of

[014-028]returning there the following evening, and little by little, still in

[014-029]pursuit of snipe, may get at last to the blessed banks of Petchora.

[014-030]Besides, every lover of the gun and the dog is a passionate admirer of

[014-031]the noblest animal in the world, the horse. And so I turned up at

[014-032]Lebedyan, stopped at the hotel, changed my clothes, and went out to the

[014-033]fair. (The waiter, a thin lanky youth of twenty, had already informed

[014-034]me in a sweet nasal tenor that his Excellency Prince N----, who

[014-035]purchases the chargers of the--regiment, was staying at their house;

[014-036]that many other gentlemen had arrived; that some gypsies were to sing

[014-037]in the evenings, and there was to be a performance of \_Pan Tvardovsky\_

[014-038]at the theatre; that the horses were fetching good prices; and that

[014-039]there was a fine show of them.)

[014-040]In the market square there were endless rows of carts drawn up, and

[014-041]behind the carts, horses of every possible kind: racers, stud-horses,

[014-042]dray horses, cart-horses, posting-hacks, and simple peasants' nags.

[014-043]Some fat and sleek, assorted by colours, covered with striped

[014-044]horse-cloths, and tied up short to high racks, turned furtive glances

[014-045]backward at the too familiar whips of their owners, the horse-dealers;

[014-046]private owners' horses, sent by noblemen of the steppes a hundred or

[014-047]two hundred miles away, in charge of some decrepit old coachman and two

[014-048]or three headstrong stable-boys, shook their long necks, stamped with

[014-049]ennui, and gnawed at the fences; roan horses, from Vyatka, huddled

[014-050]close to one another; race-horses, dapple-grey, raven, and sorrel, with

[014-051]large hindquarters, flowing tails, and shaggy legs, stood in majestic

[014-052]immobility like lions. Connoisseurs stopped respectfully before them.

[014-053]The avenues formed by the rows of carts were thronged with people of

[014-054]every class, age, and appearance; horse-dealers in long blue coats and

[014-055]high caps, with sly faces, were on the look-out for purchasers;

[014-056]gypsies, with staring eyes and curly heads, strolled up and down, like

[014-057]uneasy spirits, looking into the horses' mouths, lifting up a hoof or a

[014-058]tail, shouting, swearing, acting as go-betweens, casting lots, or

[014-059]hanging about some army horse-contracter in a foraging-cap and military

[014-060]cloak, with beaver collar. A stalwart Cossack rode up and down on a

[014-061]lanky gelding with the neck of a stag, offering it for sale 'in one

[014-062]lot,' that is, saddle, bridle, and all. Peasants, in sheepskins torn at

[014-063]the arm-pits, were forcing their way despairingly through the crowd, or

[014-064]packing themselves by dozens into a cart harnessed to a horse, which

[014-065]was to be 'put to the test,' or somewhere on one side, with the aid of

[014-066]a wily gypsy, they were bargaining till they were exhausted, clasping

[014-067]each other's hands a hundred times over, each still sticking to his

[014-068]price, while the subject of their dispute, a wretched little jade

[014-069]covered with a shrunken mat, was blinking quite unmoved, as though it

[014-070]was no concern of hers.... And, after all, what difference did it make

[014-071]to her who was to have the beating of her? Broad-browed landowners,

[014-072]with dyed moustaches and an expression of dignity on their faces, in

[014-073]Polish hats and cotton overcoats pulled half-on, were talking

[014-074]condescendingly with fat merchants in felt hats and green gloves.

[014-075]Officers of different regiments were crowding everywhere; an

[014-076]extraordinarily lanky cuirassier of German extraction was languidly

[014-077]inquiring of a lame horse-dealer 'what he expected to get for that

[014-078]chestnut.' A fair-haired young hussar, a boy of nineteen, was choosing

[014-079]a trace-horse to match a lean carriage-horse; a post-boy in a

[014-080]low-crowned hat, with a peacock's feather twisted round it, in a brown

[014-081]coat and long leather gloves tied round the arm with narrow, greenish

[014-082]bands, was looking for a shaft-horse. Coachmen were plaiting the

[014-083]horses' tails, wetting their manes, and giving respectful advice to

[014-084]their masters. Those who had completed a stroke of business were

[014-085]hurrying to hotel or to tavern, according to their class.... And all

[014-086]the crowd were moving, shouting, bustling, quarrelling and making it up

[014-087]again, swearing and laughing, all up to their knees in the mud. I

[014-088]wanted to buy a set of three horses for my covered trap; mine had begun

[014-089]to show signs of breaking down. I had found two, but had not yet

[014-090]succeeded in picking up a third. After a hotel dinner, which I cannot

[014-091]bring myself to describe (even Aeneas had discovered how painful it is

[014-092]to dwell on sorrows past), I repaired to a \_café\_ so-called, which was

[014-093]the evening resort of the purchasers of cavalry mounts, horse-breeders,

[014-094]and other persons. In the billiard-room, which was plunged in grey

[014-095]floods of tobacco smoke, there were about twenty men. Here were

[014-096]free-and-easy young landowners in embroidered jackets and grey

[014-097]trousers, with long curling hair and little waxed moustaches, staring

[014-098]about them with gentlemanly insolence; other noblemen in Cossack dress,

[014-099]with extraordinarily short necks, and eyes lost in layers of fat, were

[014-100]snorting with distressing distinctness; merchants sat a little apart on

[014-101]the \_qui-vive\_, as it is called; officers were chatting freely among

[014-102]themselves. At the billiard-table was Prince N---- a young man of

[014-103]two-and-twenty, with a lively and rather contemptuous face, in a coat

[014-104]hanging open, a red silk shirt, and loose velvet pantaloons; he was

[014-105]playing with the ex-lieutenant, Viktor Hlopakov.

[014-106]The ex-lieutenant, Viktor Hlopakov, a little, thinnish, dark man of

[014-107]thirty, with black hair, brown eyes, and a thick snub nose, is a

[014-108]diligent frequenter of elections and horse-fairs. He walks with a skip

[014-109]and a hop, waves his fat hands with a jovial swagger, cocks his cap on

[014-110]one side, and tucks up the sleeves of his military coat, showing the

[014-111]blue-black cotton lining. Mr. Hlopakov knows how to gain the favour of

[014-112]rich scapegraces from Petersburg; smokes, drinks, and plays cards with

[014-113]them; calls them by their Christian names. What they find to like in

[014-114]him it is rather hard to comprehend. He is not clever; he is not

[014-115]amusing; he is not even a buffoon. It is true they treat him with

[014-116]friendly casualness, as a good-natured fellow, but rather a fool; they

[014-117]chum with him for two or three weeks, and then all of a sudden do not

[014-118]recognise him in the street, and he on his side, too, does not

[014-119]recognise them. The chief peculiarity of Lieutenant Hlopakov consists

[014-120]in his continually for a year, sometimes two at a time, using in season

[014-121]and out of season one expression, which, though not in the least

[014-122]humorous, for some reason or other makes everyone laugh. Eight years

[014-123]ago he used on every occasion to say, "'Umble respecks and duty," and

[014-124]his patrons of that date used always to fall into fits of laughter and

[014-125]make him repeat ''Umble respecks and duty'; then he began to adopt a

[014-126]more complicated expression: 'No, that's too, too k'essk'say,' and with

[014-127]the same brilliant success; two years later he had invented a fresh

[014-128]saying: '\_Ne voo\_ excite \_voo\_self \_pa\_, man of sin, sewn in a

[014-129]sheepskin,' and so on. And strange to say! these, as you see, not

[014-130]overwhelmingly witty phrases, keep him in food and drink and clothes.

[014-131](He has run through his property ages ago, and lives solely upon his

[014-132]friends.) There is, observe, absolutely no other attraction about him;

[014-133]he can, it is true, smoke a hundred pipes of Zhukov tobacco in a day,

[014-134]and when he plays billiards, throws his right leg higher than his head,

[014-135]and while taking aim shakes his cue affectedly; but, after all, not

[014-136]everyone has a fancy for these accomplishments. He can drink, too ...

[014-137]but in Russia it is hard to gain distinction as a drinker. In short,

[014-138]his success is a complete riddle to me.... There is one thing, perhaps;

[014-139]he is discreet; he has no taste for washing dirty linen away from home,

[014-140]never speaks a word against anyone.
