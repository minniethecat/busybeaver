# THE DISTRICT DOCTOR

[004-001]One day in autumn on my way back from a remote part of the country I

[004-002]caught cold and fell ill. Fortunately the fever attacked me in the

[004-003]district town at the inn; I sent for the doctor. In half-an-hour the

[004-004]district doctor appeared, a thin, dark-haired man of middle height. He

[004-005]prescribed me the usual sudorific, ordered a mustard-plaster to be put

[004-006]on, very deftly slid a five-rouble note up his sleeve, coughing drily

[004-007]and looking away as he did so, and then was getting up to go home, but

[004-008]somehow fell into talk and remained. I was exhausted with feverishness;

[004-009]I foresaw a sleepless night, and was glad of a little chat with a

[004-010]pleasant companion. Tea was served. My doctor began to converse freely.

[004-011]He was a sensible fellow, and expressed himself with vigour and some

[004-012]humour. Queer things happen in the world: you may live a long while

[004-013]with some people, and be on friendly terms with them, and never once

[004-014]speak openly with them from your soul; with others you have scarcely

[004-015]time to get acquainted, and all at once you are pouring out to him--or

[004-016]he to you--all your secrets, as though you were at confession. I don't

[004-017]know how I gained the confidence of my new friend--any way, with

[004-018]nothing to lead up to it, he told me a rather curious incident; and

[004-019]here I will report his tale for the information of the indulgent

[004-020]reader. I will try to tell it in the doctor's own words.
