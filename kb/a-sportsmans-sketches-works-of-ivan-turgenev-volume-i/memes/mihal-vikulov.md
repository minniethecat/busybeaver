## Overview

Mihal Vikulov is the agent (burmistr) of the [[ananyevo|Ananyevo]] manor in [[The Counting-House]]. He occupies a middle tier in the estate's administrative hierarchy, serving below the head-clerk [[Nikolai Hvostov]] and [[karlo-karlitch|the German]] manager [[Karlo Karlitch Lindamandol]], but above the ordinary serfs and household staff. His role is that of an intermediary—the recipient of written orders from the Chief Office and the executor of the absentee mistress's commands on the ground (011-143).

## Appearance and Character

Mihal Vikulov never appears in person; he is known solely through the administrative apparatus that invokes his name. The young clerk on duty explains the estate's hierarchy to the narrator in a businesslike tone: "There's an agent, Mihal Vikulov, but no steward" (011-143). The absence of a steward—a more senior, typically free-hired manager—is significant. In its place, the estate operates through layers of serf-officials: a head-clerk who drafts orders, an agent who executes them, and a German manager who "does not manage the estate" (011-145). Vikulov's position is that of the operational enforcer in a system where actual authority flows from the mistress, [[Elena Nikolaevna Losnyakov]], through a paper bureaucracy.

## Role in the Story

Vikulov's role is disclosed not through action but through the documentary evidence of the estate's bureaucratic machinery. When the young clerk proudly displays his penmanship, the document he produces is an official order addressed to Vikulov—Order No. 209—complete with a heraldic seal:

> "Whereas some person unknown entered the garden at Ananyevo last night in an intoxicated condition, and with unseemly songs waked the French governess, Madame Engêne, and disturbed her; and whether the watchmen saw anything, and who were on watch in the garden and permitted such disorderliness: as regards all the above-written matters, your orders are to investigate in detail, and report immediately to the Office." (011-170)

The document is a masterpiece of bureaucratic parody: an elaborate investigation launched over a drunk singing in the garden, complete with numbered orders, official seals, and the demand for a formal report. Vikulov, as the designated recipient, embodies the layer of serf-officials who carried out the will of absentee [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s—a system in which every incident generated documents and investigations, while the deeper injustices of the mistress's rule (the floggings, the arbitrary banishments, the destruction of [[Tatyana]]'s life) went unexamined. His place in the story is that of a cog in a machine whose outputs are orders, and whose inputs are human suffering.
