# Biryuk

## Overview

Biryuk — whose real name is Foma Kuzmitch — is the central figure of Turgenev's sketch "[[Biryuk]]." A towering, solitary forester charged with protecting his master's woodland, Biryuk is feared by the local [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s as an incorruptible enforcer of property law. His nickname means "wolf" in the Orel dialect, and the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s speak of him as a superhuman force: "There isn't another such master of his business anywhere. Bundles of brushwood won't be carried off by anyone... he'll pop up, like a clod out of the snow" (012-095–012-099). Yet the story's dramatic climax — in which Biryuk, after a prolonged struggle with a starving [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] caught stealing timber, suddenly releases his captive — reveals a man whose sense of justice ultimately overrides the law he is bound to enforce.

## Physical Presence

Biryuk is introduced as a disembodied voice in the storm, then revealed in a flash of lightning: "A pale flash of lightning lit up the forester from head to foot; a brief crashing clap of thunder followed at once upon it" (012-033–012-034). He is "tall, broad-shouldered, and splendidly proportioned," with "stern and manly" features, a curly black beard, and small brown eyes that peer out with "masculine force" from under thick brows (012-070–012-081). His physical power is matched by an almost supernatural competence in the forest — he moves through the darkness of the storm without hesitation, catching the thief by tracking axe-strokes through the wind (012-152–012-157).

## The Hut: A Portrait of Poverty

Biryuk's living conditions are among the bleakest in the *Sketches*: "The forester's hut consisted of one room, smoky, low-pitched, and empty, without curtains or partitions" (012-058–012-059). His wife has abandoned him, leaving him with two children — a twelve-year-old girl "in a little old smock" (012-053–012-054) and an infant in a hanging cradle. There is no food. The girl's face is "mournful" and "timid"; she has been effectively orphaned by her mother's desertion and her father's duty. When the narrator asks about the mother, Biryuk's reply is blunt: "She ran away with a travelling tradesman" (012-118). This domestic desolation is the hidden cost of the forester's fearsome reputation — the man who guards the master's trees cannot guard his own hearth.

## The Arrest

The story's central sequence is a masterpiece of dramatic tension. Biryuk announces casually: "There's mischief in the forest... They're cutting a tree down on Kobyly Verh" (012-135), and leads the narrator into the storm-swept woods. The tracking is conducted with eerie precision — "Heaven only knows how he found out his way" (012-152). When the thief is caught, Biryuk's response is grim efficiency: "the iron voice of Biryuk thundered suddenly" (012-168), and a struggle ensues. The captured [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] is "drenched with rain, in tatters, and with a long dishevelled beard" (012-177) — a figure of abject desperation.

Back in the hut, the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s plea begins with simple desperation — "Let me go... hunger drove me to it" (012-213) — and escalates into a verbal assault of terrifying intensity:

> "Come, then, here; strike yourself, here... come, cursed destroyer of men's souls! drink Christian blood, drink." (012-243–012-245)

The accusation — "destroyer of men's souls" — transforms the scene from a simple arrest into a moral crisis. The thief names Biryuk as an agent of the same system that starves him; the forester's duty is, from this perspective, complicity in murder.

## The Release

What follows is one of the most startling reversals in all of Turgenev. Just as the narrator prepares to intervene physically, "to my intense amazement, with one pull he tugged the kerchief off the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s elbows, took him by the scruff of the neck, thrust his cap over his eyes, opened the door, and shoved him out" (012-270–012-272). The release is not gentle — it is rough, abrupt, almost violent in its refusal of sentiment. Biryuk does not grant mercy; he enacts it as a physical impulse that bypasses argument.

"Go to the devil with your horse!" he shouts after him, "but mind, next time..." (012-273–012-274). The threat is perfunctory; what matters is that the law has been suspended. When the narrator, astonished, says "Well, Biryuk... you've astonished me; I see you're a splendid fellow," the forester cuts him off with vexation: "Oh, stop that, master" (012-276–012-278). He does not want gratitude, he does not want praise, and he certainly does not want to be seen as a "splendid fellow." He has violated his duty, and the violation costs him something the narrator cannot name.
