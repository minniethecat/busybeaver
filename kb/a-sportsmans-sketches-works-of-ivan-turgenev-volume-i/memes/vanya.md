# Vanya

## Overview

Vanya is the youngest of the five [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys whom the narrator encounters around the campfire in [[BYEZHIN PRAIRIE]]. At about seven years old, he is a quiet, barely-visible presence through much of the chapter, huddled under his rough mat and offering only occasional, childlike observations.

His few spoken lines — including an innocent, wondering remark about the stars — provide a gentle counterpoint to the intense supernatural tales told by the older boys. Vanya represents the unselfconscious, prelapsarian child whose view of nature is wonder rather than fear.


## Appearance and Character

Vanya is the only boy the narrator does not notice at first: "The remaining boy, Vanya, I had not observed at first; he was lying on the ground, peacefully doubled up under a square rug, from under which he only rarely thrust out his curly, light-brown head" (008-219). He was the youngest of the group, no more than seven. He was dressed weakly and rather poorly (008-219).

His voice had "a slight lisp" (008-582), marking his youth. Throughout the night, Vanya scarcely stirred under his rug (008-230), a figure of childlike stillness contrasting with the restless storytelling of the older boys.


## Role in the Story

Vanya contributes only one significant moment to the narrative, but it is among the most memorable: he suddenly calls out, "Look, look, brothers, look at God's little stars — they're swarming like bees!" (008-576). This innocent observation, coming immediately after the older boys' grim tales of ghosts and water-spirits, returns the scene to the beauty of the natural world.

When [[fedya|Fedya]] gently asks after his sister, Vanya replies with his slight lisp and then sighs (008-581--008-590). When the horses bolt, Vanya jumps up from under his rug but soon lies down again (008-386, 008-413).

He is a minor character structurally, but his presence serves an important tonal function: his innocence and silence remind the reader of the young age of all the boys who are speaking so knowingly of death and the supernatural.
