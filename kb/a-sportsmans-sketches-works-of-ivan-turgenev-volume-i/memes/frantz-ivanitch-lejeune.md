# Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune

## Overview

Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune is one of the most extraordinary characters in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — a French drummer in Napoleon's invading Grande Armée who was captured, nearly executed by drowning, rescued by a chance encounter with a Russian [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], and ultimately became a Russian nobleman and [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] of Orel. His story, recounted by [[Ovsyanikov]] in the sketch "The Peasant Proprietor [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]," spans the Napoleonic invasion of 1812 and the subsequent decades, offering a comic and humane counterpoint to the historical trauma of the French invasion. Lejeune's improbable survival and social ascent illustrate the capriciousness of fate that runs through many of Turgenev's sketches.

## Appearance & Character

Lejeune first appears in the narrative "half-frozen and without his drum," having fallen into the hands of [[smolensk|Smolensk]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s on Napoleon's disastrous retreat from [[moscow|Moscow]] (006-565). He wears "a green jacket with pink ribbons" — the remnants of his military drummer's uniform — on a day of bitter frost (006-607). The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who rescues him sees "his blue and benumbed shoulders" and wraps him in his own wolfskin pelisse (006-609). Lejeune is characterized by his desperate will to survive and by a certain comic helplessness: he shrieks "Monsieur! Monsieur!" (006-585) until he is noticed, and when rescued, he is "gasping with delight, weeping, shivering, bowing, thanking the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], the coachman, the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s" all at once (006-605–006-606).

His key talent, revealed under duress, is music: when the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] — a passionate lover of music — asks him whether he can play the piano, Lejeune, grasping at any chance of survival, nods persistently and declares, "Oui, monsieur, oui, oui, je suis musicien; je joue tous les instruments possibles!" (006-600–006-601). Whether this was entirely true or a strategic exaggeration is left ambiguous, but it saves his life. Later, he is described as "a man of about forty, with a long nose" who arrives at [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s house a little bit drunk and speaking broken Russian (006-553).

## Role in the Story

Lejeune's story is told by [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] to the narrator as a digression on the strange ways of the [[landowning-gentry|nobility]]. Born in [[orleans|Orleans]] of French parents, Lejeune marched into Russia with Napoleon as a drummer (006-561–006-562). All went well until the retreat from [[moscow|Moscow]], when the half-frozen drummer was captured by [[smolensk|Smolensk]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, shut up overnight in an empty cloth factory, and brought the next morning to an ice-hole near a dyke "to oblige them; in other words, to swim under the ice" (006-565–006-569). Lejeune refused and begged, in French, to be let go to [[orleans|Orleans]], but the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s — "doubtless through their ignorance of the geographical position of [[orleans|Orleans]]" — continued to urge him toward the water, "encouraging him with slight blows on the vertebrae of the neck and back" (006-573–006-577).

At this critical moment, the sound of sleigh bells heralds the arrival of a stout, red-faced [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] in a wolfskin pelisse, who asks the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s what they are doing (006-578–006-581). "We are drowning a Frenchman, your honour," they reply, to which the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] responds with magnificent indifference — "Ah!" — and turns away (006-583–006-584). Only when Lejeune shrieks and the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s daughters, overhearing the French cries, urge their father to intervene — and when the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] learns that Lejeune claims to be a musician — does he relent. The [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], it turns out, has just bought a piano from [[moscow|Moscow]] and has no one to play it (006-612–006-615).

Saved from the ice, Lejeune is taken to the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s house, thawed, fed, and clothed, and within a fortnight has moved on to stay with another rich [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], "with whom, in return for his accomplishments on the piano, he lived in clover for twenty years" (006-630–006-633). He eventually marries the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s ward, settles in Orel, and becomes himself a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] — "or rather, as he is called now, Frantz Ivanitch, Monsieur Lejeune" — and a neighbour of [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] (006-636). In the present day of the story, he visits [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] and, "between you and me," says [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], "he might ask me for a glass of vodka," a comic testament to his thoroughly Russian assimilation (006-637).

## Historical/Social Context

Lejeune's story is a small domestic footnote to the great historical catastrophe of 1812. Through this comic episode, Turgenev quietly complicates the nationalist narrative of the Patriotic War: a French invader, saved from [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] justice by a Russian nobleman's love of music, ends his days as a perfectly assimilated Russian [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]. The story also illustrates the arbitrary power that [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s wielded over life and death — the rescuing [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s initial indifference ("Ah!") is the same casualness with which a serf might be flogged or pardoned.
