# Kassyan

## Overview

Kassyan, also called Kassyan of Fair Springs, is the central figure of Turgenev's sketch "[[Kassyan of Fair Springs]]." A tiny, fifty-year-old [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] with the face of an ancient woodland spirit, Kassyan is one of the most enigmatic characters in *A Sportsman's Sketches* — a wandering mystic who questions the morality of hunting, speaks of a far-off warm land where "there is no winter, and man lives in peace and plenty," and communes with birds in their own language. Unlike the practical [[Khor]] or the gentle dreamer [[Kalinitch]], Kassyan belongs to an altogether different type: the *strannik* (wanderer) and folk healer, a figure on the margins of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] society whose otherworldly detachment from ordinary life makes him both a source of wisdom and an object of suspicion to his more conventional neighbors.

## Appearance

Kassyan's physical description is one of Turgenev's most vivid:

> "Picture to yourself a little creature of fifty years old, with a little round wrinkled face, a sharp nose, little, scarcely visible, brown eyes, and thick curly black hair, which stood out on his tiny head like the cap on the top of a mushroom. His whole person was excessively thin and weakly, and it is absolutely impossible to translate into words the extraordinary strangeness of his expression." (009-148–009-153)

The mushroom-cap hair, the tiny frame, the elusive strangeness — the description evokes a woodland gnome or *leshy* (forest spirit) as much as a human [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]. His voice compounds the impression: "The sound of his voice also astonished me. There was none of the weakness of age to be heard in it; it was marvellously sweet, young and almost feminine in its softness" (009-163–009-166). This androgynous sweetness sets him apart from the gruff, masculine speech of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s like [[erofay|Erofay]] the coachman.

His nickname among the local [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s is "Blochak" (Flea): "He had been aptly named Blochak," the narrator notes (009-265), a moniker that captures both his diminutive size and his restless, ungraspable nature.

## Philosophy: The Sin of Killing

Kassyan's first words to the narrator-hunter are a challenge that cuts to the moral heart of the *Sketches*:

> "You shoot the fowls of heaven, I suppose?... the wild things of the woods?... And is it not a sin to kill God's birds, to shed the innocent blood?" (009-160–009-162)

This is no idle question. It is a radical ethical critique, delivered by a serf to a gentleman, that questions the very premise of the narrator's identity as a [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman. The narrator responds with conventional arguments — that birds are "divinely allotted for the use of man" — but Kassyan's reply is unanswerable on its own terms: "Man must live by the labour of his hands. Only the idle, the worthless, live by the destruction of living things" (009-283–009-284). For Kassyan, hunting is not [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]] but sin; the blood of birds is innocent blood, and the man who sheds it places himself outside the natural order that Kassyan reveres.

## The Wandering Mystic

Kassyan is not a settled [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] but a wanderer. "I go to many places," he tells the narrator vaguely when asked where he lives (009-553). He speaks of having traveled "to the [[romyon|Romyon]], to [[simbirsk|Simbirsk the fair city]], and to [[moscow|Moscow]] with its golden cupolas, and to [[oka|Oka]] the nurse, and to Tzna the dove" (009-543–009-545) — a litany of Russian places named with the tenderness of a pilgrim naming shrines. Yet his deepest longing is for a place that may not exist:

> "They say there are warm lands where there is no winter, and the sweet-voiced bird of paradise lives in the forests... There justice and good-will prevail on earth. Yes, that's the land to live in!" (009-546–009-548)

This utopian geography — half folk-memory of a Golden Age, half eschatological hope — places Kassyan in the tradition of the Russian *strannik*, the perpetual pilgrim who has renounced fixed abode in search of spiritual truth.

In the forest, Kassyan is entirely at home: "He had been aptly named Blochak. He skipped from stump to stump, chirruped vigorously, never ceased looking about him, and constantly looking at me and my dog with such a strange searching gaze" (009-265–009-271). He mimics birds, answers their calls, and "joined in" the song of a bird singing melodiously (009-277). "He did not talk to me about hunting," the narrator notes with some discomfort; when a bird is killed, Kassyan murmurs "It's a sin... Ah, it's a sin!" (009-279) — not a protest, but a lament.

## His Mysterious Companion

During the forest walk, the narrator glimpses a young [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] girl, [[annushka|Annushka]], who seems connected to Kassyan. She is "a young girl of about sixteen, with a round, pleasing face" (009-560–009-561). When she departs, "Kassyan looked after her, and his face assumed a softening, tender expression" (009-558–009-559). The coachman [[erofay|Erofay]] later claims she is "his daughter, to be sure," though "he's not married, and never has been" (009-615–009-616). The relationship is ambiguous — possibly adoptive, possibly something more — but it rounds out Kassyan's portrait: the solitary mystic is also a guardian.

## The Coachman's Verdict

[[erofay|Erofay]] the coachman provides the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] community's view of Kassyan, and it is a mixture of respect and exasperation: "He's a queer fellow; a regular madman... such a changeable, unaccountable fellow; a regular flea" (009-610–009-614). He notes that Kassyan "can read and write, and he's good at all kinds of healing" — a grudging acknowledgment of the *znakhar*'s (folk healer's) power — but concludes: "There's no making him out" (009-621). The ordinary [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] cannot categorize a man who refuses to farm, wanders without purpose, and talks to birds. Kassyan is, in the deepest sense, *unproductive* — and in a serf economy that measures human worth by labour output, this makes him both a reproach and a mystery.
