# Axinya

## Overview

Axinya is the wife of [[Mitrofan]] the gardener and the mother of seven children, introduced in Turgenev's sketch "[[Raspberry Spring]]" as part of the ruined estate's surviving household. She appears in only two brief mentions, yet the arc of her situation — from the crowded makeshift hut to her final posting a hundred and fifty miles away — traces in miniature the vulnerability of the serf family. When the estate's decay demanded it, Axinya was separated from her husband and children and sent to manage a useless Tyrolese cow on a distant farm. She never speaks in the text; her fate is recorded in the passive voice, as a decision made by others.

## Appearance and Character

Turgenev provides no physical description of Axinya. She is known only through her circumstances: the wife of the gardener [[Mitrofan]], the mother of seven children, a woman who, "in consideration of their tender age, no special duties had been provided, a fact, however, which had not hindered them from growing up" (003-055–003-056). This single sentence, given to the children rather than to Axinya herself, is the closest the text comes to acknowledging her. The ironic understatement is characteristically Turgenevan: children left without assigned labour are permitted to grow, as if growth were a concession rather than a natural process.

What can be inferred about Axinya is shaped entirely by absence. She does not protest, does not weep, does not plead. Whether this silence reflects stoicism, despair, or simply the impossibility of resistance under serfdom is left for the reader to decide. Her installation in a hut roofed with timber meant for a Gothic pavilion (003-047–003-048) places her and her family inside the sketch's governing irony: the estate pursues architectural vanity while its serfs improvise shelter from the leftovers of abandoned projects.

## Role in the Story

Axinya is mentioned twice in "[[Raspberry Spring]]," once in each of the sketch's two movements. Her first appearance is in the opening panorama: the gardener [[Mitrofan]], "with his wife Axinya and their seven children, was installed" in the hut built from the Gothic-pavilion timber (003-049). She is listed as an item in an inventory — husband, wife, seven children, Tyrolese cow — the domestic contents of a ruined estate.

Her second mention is more pointed. The narrator reveals that Axinya was subsequently "put in charge of a Tyrolese cow, bought at a high price in [[moscow|Moscow]], which had never yielded any profit" (003-051–003-053). The posting is to a distant farm, a hundred and fifty miles away. The conjunction is brutal in its offhandedness: a woman is removed from her family and sent across the province to tend an animal that the master's whimsy imported and that has proven useless. The cow has never given milk; the woman, by implication, has never stopped giving labour.

Axinya does not reappear after this disclosure. The sketch moves on to [[Styopushka]], to [[Mihailo Savelyev (Tuman)|Tuman]], to the fishing at the spring. But the image of the gardener's wife, exiled to a remote farm with a barren cow, lingers as one of the sketch's quietest and most damning details.
