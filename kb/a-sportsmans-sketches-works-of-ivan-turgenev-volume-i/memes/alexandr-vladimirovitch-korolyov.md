## Overview

Alexandr Vladimirovitch Korolyov is a wealthy young nobleman described by [[Ovsyanikov]] in "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." He is "handsome, rich, has studied at the 'versities, and travelled abroad" (006-259) — a representative of the educated [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] who speaks movingly of [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] welfare yet fails to act on his fine words. His portrait is one of the sharpest satirical treatments of liberal hypocrisy in the *Sketches*.

## Appearance and Character

Korolyov is "every inch a nobleman" who "speaks simply and easily, and shakes hands with us all" (006-260). At the boundary meeting in [[beryozovka|Beryozovka]], he sits quietly in a corner, "sucking the knob of his cane, and only shook his head" while other [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s brawl (006-280). This studied composure sets him apart — until the moment he rises to speak. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s narration paints him as a man of polish, education, and apparent goodwill, but whose actions reveal a different reality.

## Role in the Story

Korolyov appears at a boundary meeting convened by the mediator [[Nikifor Ilitch]] in [[beryozovka|Beryozovka]]. While [[porfiry-ovtchinnikov|Porfiry Ovtchinnikov]] — a landless representative — rages and insults [[marfa-dmitrievna|Marfa Dmitrievna]], Korolyov remains silent. When he finally rises, the room falls silent: "they were all silent at once" (006-286). He delivers an eloquent speech arguing that boundary settlement is for the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]'s benefit, that [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] and [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] interests are "inseparable," and that it is "disgraceful in a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] not to interest himself in the well-being of his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s" (006-293):

> 'He seemed to go right to your heart.... All the gentry hung their heads; I myself, faith, it nearly brought me to tears. To tell the truth, you would not find sayings like that in the old books even.' (006-299)

But the conclusion is devastating. Korolyov "himself would not give up four acres of peat marsh, and wasn't willing to sell it" (006-303). He claimed he would drain the marsh and build "a cloth-factory on it, with all the latest improvements" (006-304) — but the real reason, [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] reveals, is that "Anton Karasikov had refused to buy over Korolyov's bailiff for a hundred roubles" (006-308). The meeting ends with nothing accomplished, and Korolyov "considers to this day that he is right" (006-311).

## Significance

Korolyov embodies a recurring Turgenev theme: the gulf between liberal rhetoric and self-interested action among the landed [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. His speech "went right to your heart," yet he would not part with four acres of marsh — a pointed illustration that the post-Reform [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]'s enlightenment was often skin-deep. [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s final note is ambivalent: "Alexandr [[vladimir-lgov|Vladimir]]ovitch is doing right" (006-316), but his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s "don't speak well of him" (006-315).
