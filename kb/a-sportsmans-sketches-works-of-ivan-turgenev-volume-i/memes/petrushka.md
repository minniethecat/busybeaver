## Overview

Petrushka is the footman whose love affair with [[Arina Timofeyevna]] forms the tragic backstory of "[[Yermolai and the Miller's Wife]]." When their relationship is discovered by [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]'s master, [[Zvyerkoff]], the consequences are brutal and asymmetrical: [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]]'s hair is cut off, she is dressed in sackcloth and banished to the country, while Petrushka is sent for a soldier. [[Yermolaï]] knew him personally and testifies to his character.

## Appearance and Character

Petrushka never appears directly in the narrative—his story is told entirely through the recollections of [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]], who narrates the affair to the [[Narrator]] with self-serving indignation, and through [[yermolai|Yermolaï]], who knew him. [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]] describes him dismissively as simply "Petrushka, the footman" (002-341), but his own account inadvertently reveals Petrushka's human dimension.

When recounting the discovery of the affair, [[zvyerkoff|Zvyerkoff]] makes a striking admission: "Petrushka was not to blame. We might flog him, but in my opinion he was not to blame" (002-343). Yet despite this acknowledgment, the punishment he orders falls entirely on [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]], while Petrushka faces the lesser consequence of military conscription—a sentence that, while harsh, left him alive and free of the daily humiliation [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] endured.

## Role in the Story

Petrushka's role is that of the absent catalyst. His affair with [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] is the pivot on which her entire life turns—from trusted lady's maid to disgraced serf, from the manor house to the mill. The asymmetry of the punishment reveals the gendered dimension of serfdom's cruelty: [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]], the woman, is physically mutilated and permanently degraded; Petrushka, the man, is sent away.

[[yermolai|Yermolaï]] provides the story's coda. When the narrator asks if he knew the footman, [[yermolai|Yermolaï]] replies: "Piotr Vassilyevitch? Of course, I knew him" (002-386)—using Petrushka's formal patronymic rather than the diminutive, a mark of respect. When asked where Petrushka is now, the answer is stark: "He was sent for a soldier" (002-388).

The parallel between [[arina-timofeyevna|Arina]] and Petrushka's fates is a study in the serf system's comprehensive reach: she was sold to a [[the-miller|miller]], he was conscripted into the army; neither had any say. Both were property to be disposed of.
