# THE AGENT

[010-001]Twelve miles from my place lives an acquaintance of mine, a landowner

[010-002]and a retired officer in the Guards--Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin. He

[010-003]has a great deal of game on his estate, a house built after the design

[010-004]of a French architect, and servants dressed after the English fashion;

[010-005]he gives capital dinners, and a cordial reception to visitors, and,

[010-006]with all that, one goes to see him reluctantly. He is a sensible and

[010-007]practical man, has received the excellent education now usual, has been

[010-008]in the service, mixed in the highest society, and is now devoting

[010-009]himself to his estate with great success. Arkady Pavlitch is, to judge

[010-010]by his own words, severe but just; he looks after the good of the

[010-011]peasants under his control and punishes them--for their good. 'One has

[010-012]to treat them like children,' he says on such occasions; 'their

[010-013]ignorance, \_mon cher; il faut prendre cela en considération\_.' When

[010-014]this so-called painful necessity arises, he eschews all sharp or

[010-015]violent gestures, and prefers not to raise his voice, but with a

[010-016]straight blow in the culprit's face, says calmly, 'I believe I asked

[010-017]you to do something, my friend?' or 'What is the matter, my boy? what

[010-018]are you thinking about?' while he sets his teeth a little, and the

[010-019]corners of his mouth are drawn. He is not tall, but has an elegant

[010-020]figure, and is very good-looking; his hands and nails are kept

[010-021]perfectly exquisite; his rosy cheeks and lips are simply the picture of

[010-022]health. He has a ringing, light-hearted laugh, and there is sometimes a

[010-023]very genial twinkle in his clear brown eyes. He dresses in excellent

[010-024]taste; he orders French books, prints, and papers, though he's no great

[010-025]lover of reading himself: he has hardly as much as waded through the

[010-026]\_Wandering Jew\_. He plays cards in masterly style. Altogether, Arkady

[010-027]Pavlitch is reckoned one of the most cultivated gentlemen and most

[010-028]eligible matches in our province; the ladies are perfectly wild over

[010-029]him, and especially admire his manners. He is wonderfully well

[010-030]conducted, wary as a cat, and has never from his cradle been mixed up

[010-031]in any scandal, though he is fond of making his power felt,

[010-032]intimidating or snubbing a nervous man, when he gets a chance. He has a

[010-033]positive distaste for doubtful society--he is afraid of compromising

[010-034]himself; in his lighter moments, however, he will avow himself a

[010-035]follower of Epicurus, though as a rule he speaks slightingly of

[010-036]philosophy, calling it the foggy food fit for German brains, or at

[010-037]times, simply, rot. He is fond of music too; at the card-table he is

[010-038]given to humming through his teeth, but with feeling; he knows by heart

[010-039]some snatches from \_Lucia\_ and \_Somnambula\_, but he is always apt to

[010-040]sing everything a little sharp. The winters he spends in Petersburg.

[010-041]His house is kept in extraordinarily good order; the very grooms feel

[010-042]his influence, and every day not only rub the harness and brush their

[010-043]coats, but even wash their faces. Arkady Pavlitch's house-serfs have,

[010-044]it is true, something of a hang-dog look; but among us Russians there's

[010-045]no knowing what is sullenness and what is sleepiness. Arkady Pavlitch

[010-046]speaks in a soft, agreeable voice, with emphasis and, as it were, with

[010-047]satisfaction; he brings out each word through his handsome perfumed

[010-048]moustaches; he uses a good many French expressions too, such as: \_Mais

[010-049]c'est impayable! Mais comment donc\_? and so so. For all that, I, for

[010-050]one, am never over-eager to visit him, and if it were not for the

[010-051]grouse and the partridges, I should probably have dropped his

[010-052]acquaintance altogether. One is possessed by a strange sort of

[010-053]uneasiness in his house; the very comfort is distasteful to one, and

[010-054]every evening when a befrizzed valet makes his appearance in a blue

[010-055]livery with heraldic buttons, and begins, with cringing servility,

[010-056]drawing off one's boots, one feels that if his pale, lean figure could

[010-057]suddenly be replaced by the amazingly broad cheeks and incredibly thick

[010-058]nose of a stalwart young labourer fresh from the plough, who has yet

[010-059]had time in his ten months of service to tear his new nankin coat open

[010-060]at every seam, one would be unutterably overjoyed, and would gladly run

[010-061]the risk of having one's whole leg pulled off with the boot....

[010-062]In spite of my aversion for Arkady Pavlitch, I once happened to pass a

[010-063]night in his house. The next day I ordered my carriage to be ready

[010-064]early in the morning, but he would not let me start without a regular

[010-065]breakfast in the English style, and conducted me into his study. With

[010-066]our tea they served us cutlets, boiled eggs, butter, honey, cheese, and

[010-067]so on. Two footmen in clean white gloves swiftly and silently

[010-068]anticipated our faintest desires. We sat on a Persian divan. Arkady

[010-069]Pavlitch was arrayed in loose silk trousers, a black velvet smoking

[010-070]jacket, a red fez with a blue tassel, and yellow Chinese slippers

[010-071]without heels. He drank his tea, laughed, scrutinised his finger-nails,

[010-072]propped himself up with cushions, and was altogether in an excellent

[010-073]humour. After making a hearty breakfast with obvious satisfaction,

[010-074]Arkady Pavlitch poured himself out a glass of red wine, lifted it to

[010-075]his lips, and suddenly frowned.

[010-076]'Why was not the wine warmed?' he asked rather sharply of one of the

[010-077]footmen.

[010-078]The footman stood stock-still in confusion, and turned white.

[010-079]'Didn't I ask you a question, my friend?' Arkady Pavlitch resumed

[010-080]tranquilly, never taking his eyes off the man.

[010-081]The luckless footman fidgeted in his place, twisted the napkin, and

[010-082]uttered not a word.

[010-083]Arkady Pavlitch dropped his head and looked up at him thoughtfully from

[010-084]under his eyelids.

[010-085]'\_Pardon, mon cher\_', he observed, patting my knee amicably, and again

[010-086]he stared at the footman. 'You can go,' he added, after a short

[010-087]silence, raising his eyebrows, and he rang the bell.

[010-088]A stout, swarthy, black-haired man, with a low forehead, and eyes

[010-089]positively lost in fat, came into the room.

[010-090]'About Fyodor ... make the necessary arrangements,' said Arkady

[010-091]Pavlitch in an undertone, and with complete composure.

[010-092]'Yes, sir,' answered the fat man, and he went out.

[010-093]'\_Voilà, mon cher, les désagréments de la campagne\_,' Arkady Pavlitch

[010-094]remarked gaily. 'But where are you off to? Stop, you must stay a

[010-095]little.'

[010-096]'No,' I answered; 'it's time I was off.'

[010-097]'Nothing but sport! Oh, you sportsmen! And where are you going to shoot

[010-098]just now?'

[010-099]'Thirty-five miles from here, at Ryabovo.'

[010-100]'Ryabovo? By Jove! now in that case I will come with you. Ryabovo's

[010-101]only four miles from my village Shipilovka, and it's a long while since

[010-102]I've been over to Shipilovka; I've never been able to get the time.

[010-103]Well, this is a piece of luck; you can spend the day shooting in

[010-104]Ryabovo and come on in the evening to me. We'll have supper

[010-105]together--we'll take the cook with us, and you'll stay the night with

[010-106]me. Capital! capital!' he added without waiting for my answer.

[010-107]'\_C'est arrangé\_.... Hey, you there! Have the carriage brought out, and

[010-108]look sharp. You have never been in Shipilovka? I should be ashamed to

[010-109]suggest your putting up for the night in my agent's cottage, but you're

[010-110]not particular, I know, and at Ryabovo you'd have slept in some

[010-111]hayloft.... We will go, we will go!'

[010-112]And Arkady Pavlitch hummed some French song.

[010-113]'You don't know, I dare say,' he pursued, swaying from side to side;

[010-114]'I've some peasants there who pay rent. It's the custom of the

[010-115]place--what was I to do? They pay their rent very punctually, though. I

[010-116]should, I'll own, have put them back to payment in labour, but there's

[010-117]so little land. I really wonder how they manage to make both ends meet.

[010-118]However, \_c'est leur affaire\_. My agent there's a fine fellow, \_une

[010-119]forte tête\_, a man of real administrative power! You shall see....

[010-120]Really, how luckily things have turned out!'

[010-121]There was no help for it. Instead of nine o'clock in the morning, we

[010-122]started at two in the afternoon. Sportsmen will sympathise with my

[010-123]impatience. Arkady Pavlitch liked, as he expressed it, to be

[010-124]comfortable when he had the chance, and he took with him such a supply

[010-125]of linen, dainties, wearing apparel, perfumes, pillows, and

[010-126]dressing-cases of all sorts, that a careful and self-denying German

[010-127]would have found enough to last him for a year. Every time we went down

[010-128]a steep hill, Arkady Pavlitch addressed some brief but powerful remarks

[010-129]to the coachman, from which I was able to deduce that my worthy friend

[010-130]was a thorough coward. The journey was, however, performed in safety,

[010-131]except that, in crossing a lately-repaired bridge, the trap with the

[010-132]cook in it broke down, and he got squeezed in the stomach against the

[010-133]hind-wheel.

[010-134]Arkady Pavlitch was alarmed in earnest at the sight of the fall of

[010-135]Karem, his home-made professor of the culinary art, and he sent at once

[010-136]to inquire whether his hands were injured. On receiving a reassuring

[010-137]reply to this query, his mind was set at rest immediately. With all

[010-138]this, we were rather a long time on the road; I was in the same

[010-139]carriage as Arkady Pavlitch, and towards the end of the journey I was a

[010-140]prey to deadly boredom, especially as in a few hours my companion ran

[010-141]perfectly dry of subjects of conversation, and even fell to expressing

[010-142]his liberal views on politics. At last we did arrive--not at Ryabovo,

[010-143]but at Shipilovka; it happened so somehow. I could have got no shooting

[010-144]now that day in any case, and so, raging inwardly, I submitted to my

[010-145]fate.

[010-146]The cook had arrived a few minutes before us, and apparently had had

[010-147]time to arrange things and prepare those whom it concerned, for on our

[010-148]very entrance within the village boundaries we were met by the village

[010-149]bailiff (the agent's son), a stalwart, red-haired peasant of seven

[010-150]feet; he was on horseback, bareheaded, and wearing a new overcoat, not

[010-151]buttoned up. 'And where's Sofron?' Arkady Pavlitch asked him. The

[010-152]bailiff first jumped nimbly off his horse, bowed to his master till he

[010-153]was bent double, and said: 'Good health to you, Arkady Pavlitch, sir!'

[010-154]then raised his head, shook himself, and announced that Sofron had gone

[010-155]to Perov, but they had sent after him.
