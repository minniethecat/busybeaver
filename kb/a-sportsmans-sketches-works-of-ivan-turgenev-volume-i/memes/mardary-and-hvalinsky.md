# Two Country Gentlemen

## Overview

"Two Country Gentlemen" is a satirical diptych in which Turgenev contrasts two types of the Russian provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]: **[[general-viyatcheslav-ilarionovitch-hvalinsky|Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch Hvalinsky]]**, a retired major-general whose life is a continuous performance of borrowed dignity, and **Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov**, a seemingly amiable [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] whose "old-system" management conceals a casual brutality toward his serfs. The sketch is one of Turgenev's most biting critiques of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] class — not because either man is a villain in the melodramatic sense, but because their modes of cruelty are so thoroughly normalized that neither they nor their neighbors perceive them as cruelty at all.

## Vyatcheslav Ilarionovitch Hvalinsky

Hvalinsky is a figure of elaborate self-importance, a man whose entire existence is organized around the maintenance of an imaginary rank. Turgenev's portrait is affectionate in its mockery but unsparing in its social observation:

> Picture to yourselves a tall and once slender man, now inclined to corpulence, but not in the least decrepit or even elderly, a man of ripe age; in his very prime, as they say. It is true the once regular and even now rather pleasing features of his face have undergone some change; his cheeks are flabby; there are close wrinkles like rays about his eyes; a few teeth are not, as Saadi, according to Pushkin, used to say. (013-002–013-008)

Hvalinsky's vanity is his most conspicuous trait. His hair has "assumed a purplish hue, thanks to a composition bought at the [[romyon|Romyon]] horse-fair of a Jew who gave himself out as an Armenian" (013-008–013-010), and his speech is a study in condescension. He has a "way of his own in pronouncing many words; he never says, for instance: 'Thank you, [[pavel-andreitch|Pavel]] Vasilyitch,' or 'This way, if you please, Mihalo Ivanitch,' but always 'Fanks, Pa'l 'Asilitch,' or ''Is wy, please, Mil' 'Vanitch'" (013-028–013-031). The verbal tic is a class marker disguised as an idiosyncrasy: it signals a man who has never needed to make himself clearly understood, because his rank has always done the speaking for him.

Hvalinsky's behavior is entirely governed by hierarchy:

> With persons of the lower grades of society, his behaviour is still more quaint; he never looks at them at all, and before making known his desires to them, or giving an order, he repeats several times in succession, with a puzzled, far-away air: 'What's your name?... what, what's your name?' (013-032–013-036)

Yet when in the presence of his own superiors, "a marvellous change comes over him; he is all nods and smiles; he looks them in the face; he seems positively flowing with honey" (013-056–013-058). The discrepancy reveals the essential emptiness of Hvalinsky's self-presentation: his dignity is entirely borrowed, a reflection of whatever rank he happens to be facing.

His military career is a carefully maintained fiction. "It seems that he had never seen active service," Turgenev notes with deadpan precision (013-075–013-076), and the rumor that "in full parade get-up, buttoned up to the chin, he had to lather his chief in his bath" (013-073–013-075) — followed by the disclaimer "but one can't believe everything one hears" — is an exquisite specimen of Turgenev's art of insinuation by denial.

## Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov

If Hvalinsky is a figure of comedy, [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] is a figure of horror dressed as geniality. Turgenev introduces him with deceptive mildness:

> Mardary Apollonitch Stegunov has no sort of resemblance to Hvalinsky; I hardly think he has ever served under government in any capacity, and he has never been reckoned handsome. Mardary Apollonitch is a little, fattish, bald old man of a respectable corpulence, with a double chin and little soft hands. He is very hospitable and jovial; lives, as the saying is, for his comfort; summer and winter alike, he wears a striped wadded dressing-gown. (013-120–013-126)

He owns five hundred souls and manages his estate with genial negligence, having "ordered a threshing-machine from Butenop's in [[moscow|Moscow]], locked it up in a barn, and then felt his mind at rest on the subject" (013-128–013-130). His life is indolent, comfortable, and — on the surface — harmless.

### The Philosophy of Mastery

When the narrator questions [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] about the wretched condition of his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s' huts, the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]'s reply is disarmingly frank:

> 'To my ideas, when a man's master—he's master; and when he's peasant—he's peasant. ... That's what I think about it.' (013-181–013-183)

This is not the language of cruelty but of ontology: the distinction between master and [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] is, for [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]], as natural and immutable as the difference between species. He goes on to explain that certain [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] families are "in disgrace" because his father couldn't bear them, and he has inherited the grievance: "Blood, blood—oh, that's the great thing!" (013-189). Guilt by heredity — a principle that no court of law would sanction — is for him simple common sense.

### The Beating

The sketch's most famous and disturbing scene occurs on the balcony after tea. The narrator hears "the sound of rhythmically repeated blows, seeming to come from the stable" (013-195–013-196). When he asks what it is, [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] responds with a smile of contentment:

> Mardary Apollonitch was in the act of lifting a saucer full of tea to his lips ... but he stopped short, listened, nodded his head, sipped his tea, and laying the saucer on the table, with the most good-natured smile imaginable, he murmured as though involuntarily accompanying the blows: 'Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk! Tchuki-tchuk!' (013-196–013-203)

The onomatopoeic "Tchuki-tchuki-tchuk" — [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]]'s unconscious musical accompaniment to the beating of his serf — is one of the most chilling moments in all of Turgenev. It is not sadism but something arguably worse: the complete absence of any recognition that what is happening in the stable involves a human being in pain. The beating is, for him, background noise, like the chirping of crickets.

When the narrator stares at him in disbelief, [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]]'s response is one of genuine bewilderment:

> The fiercest indignation could not have stood against the clear mild gaze of Mardary Apollonitch.
> 'What are you after, young man? what is it?' he said, shaking his head. 'Am I a criminal or something, that you stare at me like that? "Whom he loveth he chasteneth"; you know that.' (013-215–013-219)

The biblical quotation — "Whom he loveth he chasteneth" — is the final touch. [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] genuinely believes he is exercising a benevolent, paternal discipline. The serf [[vasya|Vasya]], when encountered later on the village street, is "cracking nuts" — not broken, not weeping, but going about his life, having absorbed the beating as an ordinary incident. This is the sketch's true horror: not the cruelty itself, but its total normalization.

## Significance

"Two Country Gentlemen" is Turgenev's most concentrated satire of the provincial [[landowning-gentry|gentry]], and it operates through juxtaposition. Hvalinsky's cruelty is social — he humiliates through condescension and the calculated withholding of recognition. [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]]'s cruelty is physical — he orders beatings with the same casualness with which he orders tea. Neither man would recognize himself in the word "cruel." Hvalinsky would protest that he is merely maintaining the dignity of his rank; [[mardary-apollonitch|Mardary Apollonitch]] would protest that he is merely maintaining order on his estate, as his father did before him. It is precisely this incapacity for self-recognition that Turgenev exposes as the moral rot at the heart of the [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] class.

The sketch was widely discussed upon publication and contributed to the growing public consensus that serfdom was not merely an economic institution but a moral catastrophe — one that deformed the character of the master as thoroughly as it impoverished the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]].
