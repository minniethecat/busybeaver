# Russia and the West

Turgenev, who wrote much of the *Sketches* while living abroad, measures his country against Europe on nearly every page. Native shrewdness is set beside foreign theory, and the plain Russian peasant beside the squire who apes French and English fashions.

The contrast is rarely a simple boast for either side. Russian practicality can look the wiser, yet the book also honours what Russia might still learn from the West.

## Hor, a Russian Socrates

In [[khor-and-kalinich|Hor and Kalinitch]], the shrewd peasant [[Khor]] strikes the narrator as a homegrown philosopher:

> (001-174) ...recalled Socrates; there was the same high, knobby forehead.

Hor trusts experience over abstraction, mocking foreign cleverness while quietly keeping what is useful in it:

> (001-353) ...is fond of ridiculing the thin theorising of the German; but, in Hor's words, 'The Germans are curious folk,' and he was ready to learn from them a little.

## The Europeanised Squire

Against Hor stands the polished landowner of [[the-agent|The Agent]], whose estate is a museum of borrowed taste:

> (010-004) ...of a French architect, and servants dressed after the English fashion.

Such gentlemen wear their culture as ornament. One sneers at German philosophy as

> (010-036) ...the foggy food fit for German brains

while, in [[my-neighbour-radilov|another sketch]], a flustered squire reaches for a borrowed maxim he cannot quite place:

> (005-227) ...as I think Voltaire said,' he added hurriedly.

## The Frenchman Made Russian

The theme turns gentle in the story of [[Frantz Ivanitch Lejeune]], a French drummer of 1812 pulled from under the ice and taken in by a Russian landowner:

> (006-611) ...fed him, and clothed him. The landowner conducted him to his daughters.

The captured enemy becomes an Orel gentleman and music teacher, absorbed into Russia rather than opposed to it.

## Social and Historical Context

The place of Europe in Russian life was the great argument of Turgenev's generation, dividing Slavophiles from Westernisers. The *Sketches* take no manifesto position, but their sympathies are plain: they prize the rooted good sense of Hor over the imported airs of the gentry, while conceding that West and East, in a Lejeune, can meet and merge.
