# Zhizdrinsky District

## Overview

Zhizdrinsky District is the western [[kaluga-province|Kaluga Province]] district where the narrator of "[[A Sportsman's Sketches]]" goes hunting at the collection's very opening. In the first lines of "[[Khor and Kalinich]]," the narrator announces: "On a visit to the Zhizdrinsky district in search of [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]], I met in the fields a small landed proprietor of Kaluga, [[polutikin|Polutikin]] by name" (001-030). This encounter launches the entire collection — it is in Zhizdrinsky that the narrator meets [[Polutikin]], who introduces him to [[Khor]] and [[Kalinich]], the two [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s whose contrasted characters give the opening sketch its name.

## Description

Zhizdrinsky District is introduced as the positive term in the collection's opening geographic contrast. "Anyone who has chanced to pass from the Bolhovsky district into the Zhizdrinsky district, must have been impressed by the striking difference between the race of people in the [[orel-province|province of Orel]] and the Kaluga [[russian-peasantry|peasantry]]" (001-001–003). Where the [[Bolhovsky District]] of [[orel-province|Orel Province]] is characterized by impoverished serfs living in treeless, worn-down land, Zhizdrinsky belongs to the richer, more forested [[kaluga-province|Kaluga Province]]. Its [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s are described as "tall, with bold, cheerful faces, living in spacious aspen huts, trading in timber and oil and tar, wearing boots on Sundays" (001-005–006).

The district's landscape is equally distinctive: forested, well-watered, with richer hunting grounds. It is for this very abundance of game that the narrator — a [[hunting-in-the-sketches|sport]]sman — has traveled here, and the landscape's generosity sets the tone for the social generosity he will find among its inhabitants.

## Role in the Stories

Zhizdrinsky District is the collection's narrative birthplace. Every subsequent sketch flows, directly or indirectly, from the narrator's arrival here. The district embodies the Kaluga promise: a world where [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s trade, prosper, and retain a measure of dignity impossible across the border in Orel. The contrast between Zhizdrinsky and Bolhovsky is not merely geographic but political — it is the difference between rent-paying state [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s and bonded serfs, between forest and steppe, between hope and resignation.
