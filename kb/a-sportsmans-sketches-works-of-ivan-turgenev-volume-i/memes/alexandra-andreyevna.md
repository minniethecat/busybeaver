# Alexandra Andreyevna

## Overview

Alexandra Andreyevna is the central figure — though never seen directly by the narrator — of Turgenev's sketch "[[The District Doctor]]." She is a young woman of about twenty, the daughter of a poor widow, who falls gravely ill with fever while the narrator's acquaintance, the doctor [[Trifon Ivanitch]], attends her. Over the course of her illness, Alexandra develops an intense, lucid love for the doctor — a love that she herself understands as inseparable from her approaching death. Turgenev frames her story as a story-within-a-story: the doctor recounts it to the narrator one autumn evening, still shaken by an experience that has marked him for life. Alexandra's voice reaches the reader only through the doctor's memory, yet it emerges with extraordinary vividness — passionate, clear-sighted, and devoid of self-pity.

## The Patient

Alexandra enters the narrative already ill with a "feverish" condition that the doctor, with his limited resources, cannot arrest. Her family — her mother and sisters — place "blind faith" in the doctor's abilities, a faith that torments him because he knows it is unfounded (004-043–004-048). Alexandra herself, however, sees through the medical pretense. When the doctor assures her that she will recover, she responds not with relief but with a demand for truth:

> Alexandra Andreyevna was gazing with intent eyes at me ... her lips parted, her cheeks seemed burning. "What is it?" "Doctor, shall I die?" "Merciful Heavens!" "No, doctor, no; please don't tell me I shall live ... don't say so.... If you knew.... Listen! for God's sake don't conceal my real position." (004-103–004-108)

Her insistence on knowing the truth — "If I can know for certain that I must die ... then I will tell you all—all!" (004-108–004-110) — sets her apart from the conventional dying patient of nineteenth-century fiction. She does not want comfort; she wants clarity, because clarity alone can give her the courage to speak what she has been withholding.

## The Confession

When the doctor finally admits that she is in danger, Alexandra's response is startling:

> "I shall die, I shall die." And it seemed as though she were pleased; her face grew so bright; I was alarmed. "Don't be afraid, don't be afraid! I am not frightened of death at all." (004-120–004-124)

The brightness that suffuses her face is not resignation but liberation. The certainty of death releases her from the constraints of maidenly modesty and social propriety that have governed her life. She tells the doctor:

> "Now ... yes, now I can tell you that I thank you with my whole heart ... that you are kind and good—that I love you!" (004-127–004-130)

The declaration is not sentimental but desperate — the act of a woman "who did not consider herself on the point of death" and therefore "would never have thought of me" (004-155–004-157). Alexandra's love is inextricable from her mortality; it is, in Turgenev's rendering, the last assertion of a life that has never been permitted to declare itself.

> "Do you hear, I love you!" "Alexandra Andreyevna, how have I deserved—" "No, no, you don't—you don't understand me." (004-130–004-136)

The doctor's inability to understand her — his reflexive self-deprecation, his fear — underscores the asymmetry of their situation. She is navigating the ultimate existential crisis; he is worrying about professional decorum.

## The Ring and the Final Night

In the story's most delicate detail, Alexandra gives the doctor her ring as a pledge — a gesture that later becomes the source of painful comedy when her mother enters the room and Alexandra, still lucid, declares: "It's very well you have come; look at us, we love one another—we have given each other our word" (004-177–004-179). The doctor, terrified of scandal, claims she is delirious: "She is wandering; the fever" (004-180). But Alexandra's response cuts through his evasion: "Hush, hush; you told me something quite different just now, and have taken my ring. Why do you pretend?" (004-181–004-183).

The ring — a token that would normally signify betrothal — becomes instead a memento mori, a souvenir of a love that could only exist because it had no future. After Alexandra's death, the doctor keeps it, and the memory of those three nights haunts him for the rest of his life.

## Significance

Alexandra Andreyevna is a remarkable creation in the context of 1850s Russian literature. Turgenev grants her a psychological interiority and moral agency that were rarely accorded to female characters, particularly to women who are not aristocrats. Her lucidity about her own death — her refusal to be comforted with lies — constitutes a quiet rebellion against the patriarchal structures that governed both medicine and courtship. She appropriates the language of romantic love not as fantasy but as truth-telling: she loves the doctor because his presence has made her dying meaningful, and she insists on naming that meaning even as her mother and the doctor himself try to translate it back into the safer discourse of fever and delirium.

Alexandra's story also exemplifies Turgenev's characteristic narrative indirection. We never meet her directly; we know her only through the doctor's remembered words, filtered through the narrator's report. Yet this double remove, far from diminishing her presence, intensifies it — she exists as a voice that persists in memory, a claim on the living that death cannot extinguish.
