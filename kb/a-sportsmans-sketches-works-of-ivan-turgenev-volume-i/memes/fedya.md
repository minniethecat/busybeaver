# Fedya

## Overview

Fedya is the eldest of the five [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys gathered around the campfire in [[BYEZHIN PRAIRIE]]. At fourteen, he is the natural leader of the group, distinguished by his handsome features, fine clothes, and the easy, half-careless confidence that comes from belonging to a rich [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] family.

Unlike the other boys, who are watching the horses out of necessity, Fedya has joined the night-watch "for company's sake" (008-187). His manner toward the younger boys is patronising, and he steers the conversation with the air of one accustomed to being listened to.


## Appearance and Character

Turgenev gives Fedya the most flattering physical description of any of the five boys. He was "a handsome boy, with fine, small features, curly fair hair, bright eyes, and a perpetual half-merry, half-careless smile" (008-187). He wore a print shirt with a yellow border, a new coat, and his own boots — signs of relative wealth.

He lay leaning on his elbow, smoothing out the skirts of his coat, a gesture that suggests self-consciousness about his appearance (008-226).

His tone when addressing the other boys is "patronising" (008-286): he asks questions as if granting the younger boys permission to speak, and his occasional exclamations — "Ugh!" (008-323, 008-570) — convey a mixture of curiosity and social superiority.


## Role in the Story

Fedya functions as the group's moderator and, at times, its sceptical audience. He initiates the storytelling by turning to [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]] and asking about the domovoy (house-spirit) (008-233). He asks the practical question about whether the potatoes are done (008-279), a reminder of the mundane world's claims on the supernatural conversation.

When [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]] tells his story of a heavenly portent, Fedya is the one who asks for clarification: "But tell us, please, [[pavlusha|Pavlusha]], what was seen in your parts at Shalamovo?" (008-471). His gentle teasing of [[vanya|Vanya]] — "Well, [[vanya|Vanya]], is your sister Anyutka well?" (008-581) — shows his position as the benevolent elder.

His scepticism, however, is not impervious: when [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]] warns against speaking ill of the wood-spirit, Fedya shivers slightly and shrugs (008-570).
