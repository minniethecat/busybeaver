## Overview

Nikolai Kouzmitch was a [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] in the Zhizdrinsk District of [[kaluga-province|Kaluga Province]] and the late father of [[Polutikin]]. He appears only through reported speech in "[[Khor and Kalinich]]," but his decision to grant [[Khor]] permission to settle apart on the bog is the foundational act that shaped [[khor|Khor]]'s independent life. His name is preserved in the deferential address [[khor|Khor]] used when making his petition.

## Appearance and Character

Nikolai Kouzmitch is known only through his reported words and actions. He appears to have been a practical [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who granted an unusual request — allowing a serf to live apart on marginal land in exchange for a cash rent, without the customary labour obligations. When [[khor|Khor]]'s cottage burned down, [[khor|Khor]] approached him with the request to "settle in your forest, on the bog" (001-111). Kouzmitch's response was businesslike and direct — he questioned the choice of location, set the rent at fifty roubles a year, and insisted on no arrears (001-115).

## Role in the Story

Kouzmitch's role is to provide the legal framework within which [[khor|Khor]]'s distinctive character could flourish. By accepting a cash-rent arrangement in lieu of labour service (*barshchina*), he enabled [[khor|Khor]] to develop an independent household and business — an unusual degree of autonomy for a serf. His son [[polutikin|Polutikin]] recalls the exchange in dialogue with the narrator:

> '"Allow me, Nikolai Kouzmitch," says he, "to settle in your forest, on the bog. I will pay you a good rent." "But what do you want to settle on the bog for?" "Oh, I want to; only, your honour, Nikolai Kouzmitch, be so good as not to claim any labour from me, but fix a rent as you think best." "Fifty roubles a year!" "Very well." "But I'll have no arrears, mind!" "Of course, no arrears"; and so he settled on the bog.' (001-111)

Kouzmitch is deceased by the time of the story's events, and his son [[polutikin|Polutikin]] continues to collect [[khor|Khor]]'s rent — which has since been raised to a hundred roubles a year (001-119).

## Historical and Social Context

Kouzmitch's arrangement with [[khor|Khor]] illustrates the variety of economic relationships possible within the serf system. While serfdom is often associated with uniform exploitation, [[khor|Khor]]'s *obrok* (quit-rent) arrangement allowed him to prosper independently — though he remained legally bound. The fact that [[polutikin|Polutikin]] later raised the rent and pressed [[khor|Khor]] to buy his freedom (which [[khor|Khor]] refused, claiming poverty) shows the tension inherent in such half-liberated arrangements.
