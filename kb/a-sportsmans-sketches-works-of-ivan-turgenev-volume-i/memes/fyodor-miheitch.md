# Fyodor Miheitch

## Overview

Fyodor Miheitch (called [[fedya|Fedya]] by his host) is a ruined [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who lives on the charity of [[radilov|Radilov]] in Turgenev's sketch "My Neighbour [[radilov|Radilov]]" (Chapter V). Once a wealthy man and "the most dashing fellow in the province," he has lost everything and now occupies the ambiguous position of permanent guest — part entertainer, part dependent, part family fixture (005-102). His fiddle-playing, dancing, and singing provide a melancholy counterpoint to the main action of the sketch: the tragedy of [[radilov|Radilov]]'s own domestic situation.

## Appearance and Manner

Fyodor Miheitch is about seventy years old, and Turgenev's physical description is unsparing:

> a tall and thin man... looked about seventy; a thin nankin overcoat flapped pathetically about his dry and bony limbs. He danced, at times skipping boldly, and then dropping his little bald head with his scraggy neck stretched out as if he were dying, stamping his feet on the ground, and sometimes bending his knees with obvious difficulty. A voice cracked with age came from his toothless mouth. (005-087–005-090)

The fiddle is "wretched," and his technique is deliberately wrong — he holds the bow "not by the end, as is usual, but by the middle" and puts the fiddle to his chest rather than his shoulder (005-090). Everything about his performance speaks of decay, amateurism, and a talent that was never quite real even in its prime.

Yet Fyodor Miheitch is not without dignity of a kind. After his performance, he bows formally — "first to me as the guest, then to the old lady, then to [[radilov|Radilov]]" — preserving the rituals of the drawing-room even as he occupies its lowest seat (005-104). At dinner, he is seated apart at a little table without a napkin; the family keeps him "at some distance from society" because "the poor old man could not boast of very nice habits" (005-118). He eats "like a shark" (005-118). After his "refreshment" — a euphemism for drink — his eyes are bright and his nose red, and he sings "Raise the Cry of Victory" (005-118).

## A Ruined Past

[[radilov|Radilov]]'s introduction of Fyodor Miheitch provides the essential biographical frame:

> 'He too was a landowner,' my new friend continued, 'and a rich one too, but he ruined himself — so he lives now with me.... But in his day he was considered the most dashing fellow in the province; he eloped with two married ladies; he used to keep singers, and sang himself, and danced like a master....' (005-102)

This capsule biography sketches a life of aristocratic dissipation: wealth, women, music, and ruin. The verbs are in the past tense — he *was* rich, he *eloped*, he *kept* singers, he *danced* — and the single surviving trace of this former identity is the fiddle that he can no longer play properly. Fyodor Miheitch is a living ruin, the human equivalent of the derelict manors that dot the provincial landscape.

## The Evening Scene

In the evening after dinner, the household gathers in the drawing-room. Fyodor Miheitch sits in his corner "as before" — a permanent piece of furniture (005-185). When [[radilov|Radilov]]'s sister-in-law [[olga|Olga]] sits at the piano, her face "slightly paled" and her chin "almost imperceptibly trembled" (005-221). After she finishes playing, [[radilov|Radilov]]'s mother is stealthily wiping away tears. It is at this charged moment that Fyodor Miheitch suddenly acts:

> Fyodor Miheitch suddenly got up, seized his fiddle, and in a wild and hoarse voice began to sing a song. (005-221–005-222)

The gesture is ambiguous: it may be an attempt to break the unbearable tension in the room, or the reflex of a former entertainer who knows that silence must be filled. Either way, it fails. [[radilov|Radilov]]'s mother continues to weep, and the emotional pressure in the room does not dissipate until [[radilov|Radilov]] himself, late in the evening, tells the narrator the story of his dead wife — the confession that gives the sketch its devastating final turn. Fyodor Miheitch, who has been present throughout, is excluded from this intimacy. He remains in the drawing-room, playing cards with the old lady (005-271).

## Historical/Social Context

Fyodor Miheitch belongs to a distinct Russian type: the ruined provincial nobleman whose fortune has been consumed by a combination of dissipation, mismanagement, and the structural decline of the landed [[landowning-gentry|gentry]]. In the decades before the Emancipation, many small and medium [[landowning-gentry|landowner]]s found themselves unable to sustain their estates; some, like Fyodor Miheitch, ended as dependents of more fortunate neighbours. His presence at [[radilov|Radilov]]'s table — seated apart, tolerated but not respected — is a living reminder of the precariousness of [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] identity. He is what [[radilov|Radilov]] himself might become, and what many already were.
