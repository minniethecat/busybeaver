# Religion and Faith

**Religion and faith** shape the moral world of [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] in ways both formal and intimate. Orthodox Christianity is the assumed frame of peasant life — its icons, feast-days, and language of providence run through every sketch.

Turgenev, however, is less interested in doctrine than in how faith is actually lived, from the icon-lamp in the corner of a hut to the ecstatic wanderings of a peasant mystic.

## Kassyan the Holy Wanderer

The book's fullest study of religious feeling is [[Kassyan]], the strange dwarfish peasant of the ninth sketch, a wanderer, healer, and reader of holy books who treats all killing as sacrilege. When the narrator's gun brings down a bird, Kassyan reproaches him in the accents of a preacher:

> (009-161) ...And is it not a sin to kill God's birds, to shed the innocent blood?

His faith fuses Orthodox piety with something older and more personal — a reverence for the whole living creation that shades into the world of [[Folk Belief and Superstition|folk belief]] and [[Nature and Landscape|nature]].

## Domestic and Everyday Piety

More often faith appears simply as the furniture of daily life. In the very first sketch, the narrator notes the icon and its burning lamp as a matter of course in the peasant's clean cottage:

> (001-070) ...a holy picture in its silver setting, a lamp was burning; the table of linden-wood had been lately planed and scrubbed.

The old peasant proprietor [[Ovsyanikov]] embodies a graver, bookish devotion, ordering his week around observance:

> (006-039) ...he read none but religious books and used gravely to fix his round silver spectacles on his nose when he did so; he got up, and went to bed early.

## Faith and Resignation

For the [[Russian Peasantry|peasantry]], faith is above all a way of enduring. The phrase "God's will" recurs as the natural response to death and misfortune, an acceptance in which grief and belief are indistinguishable:

> (007-102) Here, by God's will, in peace he has been laid.

## Social and Historical Context

Turgenev, himself sceptical, records peasant faith with sympathetic exactness rather than either piety or irony. In doing so he shows religion as one of the deep structures of the peasant's inner life — a source of dignity, consolation, and meaning that the institution of [[Serfdom|serfdom]] could constrain but never abolish.
