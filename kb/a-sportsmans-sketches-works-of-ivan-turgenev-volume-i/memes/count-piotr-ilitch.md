## Overview

Count Piotr Ilitch is a deceased nobleman of the Catherinian age whose ruined mansion stands as a melancholy landmark near [[ista-river|the Ista]] River. (003-136) He is remembered through the recollections of his former steward, [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Mihal Savelitch]], nicknamed [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]], who describes the count's extravagant hospitality, his home-trained orchestra, and his eventual ruin. (003-127) The count never appears directly in the narrative but serves as an emblem of a vanished era of Russian aristocratic excess.

## Appearance and Character

Count Piotr Ilitch has no living presence in the story; he is evoked entirely through [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]]'s reminiscences and the physical ruin of his mansion. The narrator describes the derelict estate in stark terms:

> At mid-day in bright, sunny weather nothing can be imagined more melancholy than this ruin. Here there once lived Count Piotr Ilitch, a rich grandee of the olden time, renowned for his hospitality. (003-135)

[[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] describes him as "a great gentleman" and "all a master should be," with a kind heart but one fatal weakness: mistresses. (003-202, 003-220)

## Role in the Story

### The Ruined Mansion

The count's abandoned two-storied wooden house, with its roof falling in and windows stuffed up, is visible from the Orel highroad near the large village of [[troitska|Troitska]]. (003-132) It stands as a physical monument to a disappeared way of life — the narrator notes that "more than one aged lady sighs as she drives by the deserted palace of the boyar and recalls the old days and her vanished youth." (003-141)

### Recollections by Tuman

[[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]], the count's former steward now a freed serf fishing on [[ista-river|the Ista]] River, provides the most detailed portrait of his old master. The count maintained a home-trained orchestra of forty musicians and fired a German conductor who "gave himself dreadful airs" by wanting to eat at the masters' table. (003-212) When he gave a banquet, "there were fireworks, and carriages driving up! They even fired off the cannon." (003-210)

The count ordered luxuries from Paris: "Embroidered coats, wigs, canes, perfumes, eau de Cologne of the best sort, snuff-boxes, huge pictures." (003-208)

### The Mistresses and Ruin

[[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] identifies the count's mistresses as the cause of his downfall:

> There was only one thing: he kept mistresses. Ugh, those mistresses! God forgive them! They were the ruin of him too. (003-223)

One in particular, Akulina, the daughter of a watchman, "simply bewitched him. She would slap the count's face sometimes." (003-230) When his property was exhausted, he went to [[petersburg|Petersburg]] to seek a government post and died in a hotel room, "without having gained anything by his efforts." (003-146)

### The Legacy

The count's son, [[count-valerian-petrovitch|Valerian Petrovitch]], inherited the estate. When a [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] named [[vlass|Vlass]] appears at the spring seeking relief from his rent after his son's death, the reader learns that Piotr Ilitch gave away one of his villages in his lifetime: "Piotr Hitch gave him [[vlass|Vlass]]'s village in his lifetime." (003-313) The younger count offers [[vlass|Vlass]] no relief, and [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]] can only murmur, "Oh! things are in a sad way, brother [[vlass|Vlass]]." (003-306)

## Significance

Count Piotr Ilitch represents the grandees of the Catherinian age — lavish, hospitable, and ultimately self-destructive. Turgenev places him in counterpoint to the suffering [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] [[vlass|Vlass]] in the same chapter: one man's reckless generosity and ruin are set against another man's helpless poverty. The ruined mansion overlooking [[ista-river|the Ista]] is one of Turgenev's most evocative images of a bygone Russia.
