# Shumihino

## Overview

Shumihino is a large village located "a few miles from my place" (003-034), as the narrator describes it in Turgenev's sketch "[[Raspberry Spring]]." Its defining feature is a stone church "erected in the name of St. Kosmo and St. Damian" (003-034). The village enters the collection primarily as the nominal home of [[Styopushka]], one of Turgenev's most haunting minor characters — a man so marginal that he exists outside every recognized social category, yet is technically "one of the Shumihino people" (003-075).

## Description

Turgenev introduces Shumihino in a paragraph of precise geographical placement: "A few miles from my place there is a large village called Shumihino, with a stone church, erected in the name of St. Kosmo and St. Damian" (003-034). The stone church marks it as a settlement of some substance in the rural landscape — most villages in the sketches have only wooden churches or none at all.

The village appears in the narrative as part of the constellation of places around the [[Ista River]], near [[Raspberry Spring]] and the village of [[Troitska]]. When the narrator encounters [[styopushka|Styopushka]] on the bank of [[ista-river|the Ista]], he immediately identifies him: "I looked at him more attentively, and recognised in him [[styopushka|Styopushka]] of Shumihino" (003-032).

## Role in the Stories

Shumihino's significance in the collection is paradoxical: it is the official residence of a man who has no actual residence. [[styopushka|Styopushka]] "could not be classified under the heading of house-serf" (003-064) — he belongs neither to the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] commune, nor to the manor-house, nor to any family. The narrator notes that no one knew "in what way he had come to be one of the Shumihino people" (003-075), where he lived, or what he lived on. In this way, Shumihino becomes more than a place-name: it is a bureaucratic tag attached to a human being who has fallen through every crack in the social order. The village's solid, named reality — its stone church, its communal identity — throws into sharper relief the utter invisibility of the man who is nominally part of it.
