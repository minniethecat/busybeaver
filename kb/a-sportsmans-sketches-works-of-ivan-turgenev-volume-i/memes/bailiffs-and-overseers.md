# Bailiffs, Agents and Overseers

Between the [[Landowning Gentry|landowner]] and the [[Russian Peasantry|field peasant]] stands the *burmister* — the bailiff, agent or overseer to whom the master hands the day-to-day rule of an estate. Drawn from the peasantry or hired from outside, he exercises the owner's authority over his fellow serfs, and the sketches return again and again to what that delegated power becomes in the using.

He is neither the clerk of [[Bureaucracy and Officialdom|estate officialdom]] nor the coachman or footman of the [[Household Servants and Retainers|household]]. His defining mark is executive power *over* the peasants — and, in Turgenev's telling, the steady abuse of it. Where an estate keeps no resident steward, it is the agent who effectively becomes the master:

> (011-143) There's an agent, Mihal Vikulov, but no steward.

## Sofron, Master of Shipilovka

The fullest portrait is Sofron Yakovlitch, the agent of [[Shipilovka]] in [[the-agent|The Agent]], whom his master [[Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]] visits with the narrator. To the peasant [[Anpadist]], the true ruler of the village is not the gentleman but his agent:

> (010-454) ...he's not the master there; Sofron's the master.

Sofron has grown rich on the estate he manages, renting land in others' names and trading on his own account:

> (010-460) He rents two hundred acres from the Hlinovsky peasants alone, and two hundred and eighty from our folks...

> (010-463) ...he does a trade in horses and stock, and pitch, and butter, and hemp...

The peasants pay for his prosperity: they "work for him like slaves" (010-457), and "He harries them out of their lives" (010-458).

## "He's Not a Man; He's a Dog"

Asked plainly what kind of man the agent is, Anpadist gives the judgment that fixes the type:

> (010-450) He's not a man; he's a dog; you couldn't find another brute like him...

The cruelty is deliberate, not indiscriminate — he measures his victims and leaves alone the ones who can resist:

> (010-482) The old men that are a bit richer, or've more children, he doesn't touch...

His singled-out victim is [[Antip]], who quarrelled with him at the village meeting; the reprisal reaches the man's whole family:

> (010-474) ...he will eat him up now; he'll simply eat...

> (010-483) Why he's sent Antip's sons for recruits out of turn, the heartless ruffian...

## Nikolai Eremyitch, the Head-Clerk

A quieter form of the same power governs the estate of Ananyevo in [[the-counting-house|The Counting-House]]. It keeps no steward; the fat [[Nikolai Eremyev|Nikolai Eremyitch]], once chief cashier, has risen to run the office:

> (011-140) ...he was the chief cashier, but now he has been promoted to be head-clerk.

Those who deal with him treat him as the estate's real authority — "you're, one may say, the first person here" (011-238) — and it is he who names the price when a merchant comes to buy the estate's grain:

> (011-247) 'Eight roubles a measure,' said the fat man emphatically.

## "Change Your Bailiff Pretty Often"

Even where no single agent dominates a chapter, the bailiff hovers as a figure to be distrusted. In [[khor-and-kalinich|Khor and Kalinich]], the shrewd peasant [[Khor]], learning that the narrator keeps an estate of his own, offers a dry piece of counsel — to shoot grouse to his heart's content, and:

> (001-416) ...change your bailiff pretty often.

The joke carries a settled peasant assumption: a bailiff is a man to be watched and replaced, never quite trusted.

## Social and Historical Context

The burmister was the hinge of the serf estate. An absent or negligent [[Landowning Gentry|landowner]] left the government of village and peasants to an agent who ruled with delegated, largely unchecked power; so long as the estate showed no arrears, the master had no complaint:

> (010-469) ...There's no arrears; so what does he care?

Turgenev's agents show what such power tended toward: Sofron enriching himself while the peasants "work for him like slaves" (010-457), Nikolai Eremyitch conducting Ananyevo's trade from behind an office partition. This is the executive face of [[Serfdom|serfdom]] — distinct from the paperwork of [[Bureaucracy and Officialdom]] and from the personal service of [[Household Servants and Retainers]], and backed, when it came to it, by the [[Corporal Punishment and Cruelty|cruelty]] that made Anpadist call the agent "a dog" (010-450).
