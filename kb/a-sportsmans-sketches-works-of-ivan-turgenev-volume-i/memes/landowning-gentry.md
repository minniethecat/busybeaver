# Landowning Gentry

The **landowning gentry** (помещики, *pomeshchiki*) are, alongside the [[Russian Peasantry|peasantry]], the other great subject of [[A Sportsman's Sketches]]. Turgenev surveys this class with an anthropologist's eye and a satirist's wit, cataloguing its types — the enlightened European, the decaying patriarch, the petty tyrant, the improvident spendthrift — and drawing from each a variation on the central theme: a class that has lost its connection to the land and the people it claims to govern.

Turgenev presents the landowning class not as a monolithic bloc but as a spectrum of human possibility, from the merely absurd to the genuinely sinister:

## The Enlightened Landowner

[[arkady-pyenotchkin|Arkady Pavlitch Pyenotchkin]] is Turgenev's most devastating portrait of the modernizing landowner. He prides himself on his European cultivation — he speaks French, reads the latest journals, insists that his house-serfs wash their faces. Yet this veneer of civilization conceals a complete indifference to the suffering his system inflicts:

> (010-026) Altogether, Arkady Pavlitch is reckoned one of the most highly educated noblemen and most eligible matches in the province. The ladies are crazy about him, and especially admire his manners.

The irony is savage: [[arkady-pyenotchkin|Pyenotchkin]]'s refinement is not a mitigation of serfdom's cruelty but its most effective mask. He never strikes anyone; he simply delegates the striking to [[sofron|Sofron]], his bailiff, and then declines to hear about it.

## The Decayed and the Absurd

Not all of Turgenev's landowners are villains; some are simply ridiculous. The retired General Khvalynsky, with his ludicrous vanity and his habit of speaking to subordinates in the third person, is a figure of pure comedy. Stegunov, the old-style landowner who sits on his balcony drinking tea while his servants are beaten in the stables, is more troubling — not because he is cruel but because cruelty has become, for him, merely habitual, a background condition of existence as natural as the weather.

There is a tragic dimension to some of these portraits. [[radilov|Radilov]], the melancholy widower who cannot recover from the death of his wife, represents a gentry that has lost its vitality. His estate is well-managed but lifeless, his hospitality correct but hollow. He is a man going through the motions of a role that no longer means anything.

## The Uncomfortable Middle

Perhaps the most interesting figure in Turgenev's gallery is [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]], the "one-household" nobleman — a man who is technically a landowner but who lives more like a prosperous [[russian-peasantry|peasant]], bluntly criticizing the abuses of his fellow nobles while maintaining a prickly pride in his own anomalous status:

> (011-067) Ovsyanikov spoke of the old days without nostalgia and of the new without enthusiasm. His judgments were sharp, his manner unyielding. He belonged to that uncomfortable middle ground where class identity blurs into something harder to name.

## Historical Context

The world Turgenev describes is a world in transition. The old patriarchal model — the landowner as father to his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, living among them, sharing their festivals and their sorrows — is dying. In its place emerges the absentee landlord, the bureaucratic estate manager, the "enlightened" owner who speaks of his [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s in French and never sets foot in their huts. Turgenev does not sentimentalize the old order, but he makes clear that the new one is, in many ways, worse.
