# Mikulino

## Overview

Mikulino is a village mentioned in Turgenev's sketch "[[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]]." It appears in a conversation between the narrator, [[Ovsyanikov]], and [[Mitya]] concerning a serf woman named [[Fedosya Mihalovna]]. Mikulino's significance lies in its role as the anchor of Fedosya's legal identity — she belongs to the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who bought the village, and her fate is tied to that transaction.

## Description

Mikulino enters the narrative through a brief exchange. When [[mitya|Mitya]] tells [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] that he "met [[fedosya-mihalovna|Fedosya Mihalovna]]" (006-522), [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]] asks for clarification. [[mitya|Mitya]] explains: "'She belongs to Garpentchenko, the [[landowning-gentry|landowner]], who bought Mikulino by auction. Fedosya is from Mikulino'" (006-524). This terse identification — a person defined by the village she comes from, and the village defined by the auction that transferred its ownership — distills the legal architecture of serfdom. Fedosya "lived in [[moscow|Moscow]] as a dress-maker, paying her service in money, and she paid her service-money accurately — a hundred and eighty two-roubles and a half a year" (006-526). Despite her economic self-sufficiency, Garpentchenko "has written for her back, and he retains her here, but does not provide any duties for her" (006-529). She "would be prepared to buy her freedom" (006-531), but the master refuses.

## Role in the Stories

Mikulino serves as a case study in the arbitrary power that land ownership conferred over human beings. The village was bought at auction, and with it came the right to summon a skilled dressmaker back from [[moscow|Moscow]] — not because her labour was needed, but simply because the master could. Fedosya's willingness to purchase her freedom means nothing against the master's refusal to sell. Mikulino thus exemplifies the legal logic that the *Sketches* repeatedly exposes: human beings treated as appurtenances to parcels of land.
