## Overview

Troitska is a large village in [[orel-province|Orel Province]], referenced in Ivan Turgenev's "[[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]]" (Chapter 3 of *A Sportsman's Sketches*). It lies near the Orel highroad, and at no great distance from the village stands an immense, completely deserted two-storied wooden house — a melancholy landmark of decaying [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] fortunes.

## Description

Turgenev locates Troitska in relation to the Orel highroad, the main route through the province. The village itself is described simply as "large," but its most striking feature is the derelict mansion that stands nearby. This "immense two-storied wooden house, completely deserted, with its roof falling in and its windows closely stuffed up" (003-132) is visible from the highroad to any traveler with leisure to notice it — though Turgenev dryly notes that merchants buried in their striped rugs "have other things to do."

The deserted house near Troitska embodies the decay of the old landowning class. Its size speaks to former prosperity; its abandonment speaks to the economic and social changes sweeping through the Russian countryside in the mid-nineteenth century.

## Role in the Stories

Troitska appears in "[[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]]" as a geographical waypoint. The narrator, on his way to the spring where he will encounter [[styopushka|Styopushka]] and the old servant [[mihailo-savelyev-tuman|Tuman]], passes near the village. The sight of the abandoned mansion triggers a reflection on the transience of wealth and status — a theme that resonates throughout the sketches.

The village also provides a geographical anchor for the chapter. [[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]] itself is located near Troitska, and the narrator's excursion from his own estate takes him through this landscape of large villages, deserted houses, and neglected roads that characterizes [[orel-province|Orel Province]] in Turgenev's portrayal.
