## Overview

Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev is a minor [[landowning-gentry|landowner]] who appears only as a name in the rambling autobiographical account of [[Sutchok]], the elderly fisherman of [[Lgov]]. He inherited the [[lgov-place|Lgov]] serfs from his uncle [[Afanasy Nefeditch]] and held them for a mere six years before they passed to [[Alyona Timofyevna]]. Though he never appears in person, his brief tenure forms one link in the intricate chain of ownership that [[Sutchok]] traces—a chain that Turgenev uses to illustrate the serf's existence as a commodity passed from hand to hand (007-164).

## Appearance and Character

No physical description of Pehterev exists in the text; he is known only through [[Sutchok]]'s terse, matter-of-fact recollection. [[Sutchok]] notes two things about him: that he inherited the serfs ("We came to him by inheritance") and that "he did not own us long; only six years altogether" (007-165). During that brief period, [[Sutchok]] served as his country coachman—a modest post, as Pehterev kept other coachmen in town. The brevity of his ownership and the absence of any anecdote about his character render him a ghostly figure in the genealogy, notable chiefly for his transience.

## Role in the Story

The name of Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev emerges during the narrator's conversation with [[Sutchok]] on the shore of the [[lgov-place|Lgov]] pond. When the narrator asks [[Sutchok]] whom he belonged to before [[Alyona Timofyevna]], the old man replies:

> "To Sergaï Sergiitch Pehterev. We came to him by inheritance. But he did not own us long; only six years altogether. I was his coachman ... but not in town, he had others there—only in the country." (007-164)

The passage is part of a longer genealogy that [[Sutchok]] recites with the detachment of a man who has been reclassified so many times that ownership has become an abstract category. Pehterev inherited the serfs from his uncle [[Afanasy Nefeditch]], who had purchased [[lgov-place|Lgov]] from [[Tatyana Vassilyevna]] (007-172). After Pehterev's brief ownership, the serfs passed to [[Alyona Timofyevna]], who demoted [[Sutchok]] from coachman to fisherman.

Pehterev's role in the narrative is characteristic of Turgenev's genealogical method in [[Lgov]]: each master in the chain is mentioned only in passing, yet their collective effect is to dramatise the serf's condition as inherited property. That Pehterev owned human beings for "only six years"—as if human ownership were a temporary lease—is presented without explicit comment, the horror of the fact left to speak for itself.
