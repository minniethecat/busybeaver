# Kaluga Gate

## Overview

The Kaluga Gate was one of the old toll-gates on the southern edge of [[Moscow]]. It is named once in *A Sportsman's Sketches*, in [[the-peasant-proprietor-ovsyanikov|The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]], as the district of the city in which a great eighteenth-century nobleman kept his house.

The mention is brief, but it fixes a real [[moscow|Moscow]] landmark within the old man's affectionate recollection of vanished grandees.

## Description

The gate surfaces in [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s account of the magnates he had seen in his youth. Among them, none could compare with "Count [[Alexey Grigoryevitch Orlov-Tchesmensky]]" (006-168), whose steward was [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s own uncle.

Recalling the count's establishment, the old man notes that "the count was pleased to live in [[Shabolovka]], near the Kaluga Gate" (006-170). The gate thus locates the count's household on the map of old [[moscow|Moscow]], in the same quarter as [[shabolovka|Shabolovka]].

## Role in the Stories

The Kaluga Gate has no action attached to it; it serves as a marker of place and social memory.

It anchors [[ovsyanikov|Ovsyanikov]]'s nostalgic portrait of a grandee "who kept open house for the wonder and admiration of all" (006-166) — the kind of lordly figure the old proprietor contrasts, wistfully, with the diminished [[landowning-gentry|gentry]] of his own later day.

The detail helps ground the reminiscence in a concrete [[moscow|Moscow]] the listener can picture.
