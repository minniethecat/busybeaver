# Trofimitch

## Overview

"Grandfather Trofimitch" is a legendary figure in the lore of the [[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]] [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s, invoked in one of [[Ilyusha]]'s ghost stories as a model of courage in the face of the supernatural. When a local ghost — the restless spirit of the deceased master [[ivan-ivanitch|Ivan Ivanitch]] — was seen wandering the countryside, it was Trofimitch who had the nerve to address him directly, asking what he sought. His bravery earns the spontaneous admiration of [[Fedya]]: "Well, I call Trofimitch a brave fellow after that" (008-427). Though Trofimitch never appears in person, his reputation as a man who faced a ghost and lived to tell of it makes him a significant figure in the oral culture of the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] community.

## Appearance & Character

No physical description of Trofimitch is given in the text; he is evoked only through the stories that circulate about him. The epithet "grandfather" suggests an elder of the village, a man whose experience and standing command respect. His defining trait is fearlessness: where supernatural apparitions would send most [[russian-peasantry|peasant]]s running, Trofimitch stops and confronts the ghost. The directness of his question — "What, your honour, [[ivan-ivanitch|Ivan Ivanitch]], are you pleased to look for on the ground?" (008-423–008-424) — reveals a practical, no-nonsense temperament. He addresses the ghost with the proper honorific ("your honour") yet presses his inquiry like a man demanding an explanation, not a supplicant cowering before the unknown.

## Role in the Story

Trofimitch appears in [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]]'s tale of [[ivan-ivanitch|Ivan Ivanitch]], a deceased master whose restless spirit was seen walking the earth. The ghost, wearing "a long skirted coat" and groaning, was "looking for something on the ground" (008-421–008-422). While others fled, Trofimitch confronted the apparition directly. When he asked what the ghost sought, the dead man replied, speaking thickly: "I am looking for the herb that cleaves all things" (008-429). Pressed further — "And what, your honour, [[ivan-ivanitch|Ivan Ivanitch]], do you want with the herb that cleaves all things?" — the ghost gave its terrible answer: "The tomb weighs on me; it weighs on me, Trofimitch: I want to get away — away" (008-431–008-432).

The exchange is remarkable for what it reveals about the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] understanding of the afterlife: the dead are not at peace; they are held down by the weight of the tomb, and they long for escape through a magical herb. Trofimitch's role is that of the witness — the one who bridged the gap between the living and the dead long enough to receive this revelation. [[fedya|Fedya]]'s immediate reaction — "Well, I call Trofimitch a brave fellow after that" — underscores how exceptional such courage was considered, while Kosyta's interjection — "I thought one could only see the departed on All Hallows' day" — shows the story serving its function as a lesson in [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] superstition (008-427, 008-434).

In a brief earlier reference in "[[raspberry-spring-place|Raspberry Spring]]," a different Trofimitch is mentioned — one "who knew all the pedigrees of all the dogs" (003-079), suggesting the name may have belonged to multiple village figures across Turgenev's rural landscape.
