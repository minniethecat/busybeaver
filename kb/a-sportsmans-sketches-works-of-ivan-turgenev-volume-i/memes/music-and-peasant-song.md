# Music and Peasant Song

**Music and song** sound through [[A Sportsman's Sketches]] as the natural language of peasant feeling. A man sings at his work, over his grief, and in his cups; a balalaika answers a long evening; a mournful air drifts across a river at dusk.

For Turgenev's [[Russian Peasantry|peasants]], song is less an art performed than an emotion overheard — and it becomes one of his surest means of revealing the inner life he elsewhere insists upon.

## The Voice of Kalinitch

The theme is present from the first sketch. [[Kalinitch]], the poetic and tender-hearted peasant, sings while his practical friend [[Khor]] listens and joins in, and the moment fixes the difference between the two men more sharply than any description could:

> (001-397) [[Kalinitch]] sang rather sweetly and played a little on the balalaëca. Hor was never weary of listening to him: all at once he would let his head drop on one side and begin to chime in, in a lugubrious voice. He was particularly fond of the song, 'Ah, my fate, my fate!'

## Song as Feeling

Again and again song carries a weight of sorrow the characters cannot otherwise express. In [[Raspberry Spring]] a single voice across the water darkens the whole scene:

> (003-328) On the other bank someone was singing a song--but such a mournful one. Our poor Vlass grew deeply dejected.

In [[My Neighbour Radilov]], the broken old retainer Fyodor Miheitch seizes his fiddle in a burst of feeling, and the attempt at cheer only deepens the general gloom:

> (005-221) Fyodor Miheitch suddenly got up, seized his fiddle, and in a wild and hoarse voice began to sing a song. He wanted doubtless to restore our spirits; but we all shuddered at his...

## Song Under Command

Song can also be commanded, and in that it registers the pressure of [[Serfdom|serfdom]]. In [[The Peasant Proprietor Ovsyanikov]], an old man recalls a landowner who ordered his peasants to sing on cue — and the terror the order produced:

> (006-352) ...suddenly gives the order: "Come, lads, sing a Russian national song!" The peasants' legs shook under them with fright; they were utterly stupefied.

What should be spontaneous is here extracted by force, and the contrast lays bare the whole distance between the peasant's own music and the uses a master might make of it.

## Social and Historical Context

By listening so closely to peasant song, Turgenev grants his subjects an inner richness the social order denied them. The singing voice — sweet, mournful, or coerced — becomes a measure of the humanity the *Sketches* were written to make visible, and a quiet rebuke to the system that could compel even a song.
