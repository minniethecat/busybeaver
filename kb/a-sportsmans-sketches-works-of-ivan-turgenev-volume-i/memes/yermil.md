# Yermil

## Overview

Yermil is a huntsman and post-rider who appears only through the ghost stories told by the [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] boys in Turgenev's "[[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]." He never speaks directly in the narrative; instead, he is the subject of one of [[Ilyusha]]'s supernatural tales, recounted around the campfire. Yermil is "a good huntsman, and everyone liked him" (008-367), yet he has an inexplicable misfortune: no dog ever lives with him. His tale of the bewitched lamb on the dam blends [[russian-peasantry|peasant]] superstition with psychological horror and is one of the most memorable passages in the sketch.

## Appearance & Character

The narrator offers no physical description of Yermil, for he never appears onstage; what we know of him comes entirely through [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]]'s testimony. He serves as both huntsman and post-rider — "Yermil always goes to the post for us" (008-365) — and is competent and well-liked. The strangeness that dogs will not live with him ("he has let all his dogs die; they never will live with him, for some reason" (008-365–008-366)) casts a shadow over his otherwise ordinary character, marking him as subtly uncanny within the story's folkloric atmosphere. Yermil emerges from the story as an ordinary man who encountered something that defies ordinary understanding: a lamb that met his gaze and spoke back.

## Role in the Story

Yermil's tale is told by [[ilyusha|Ilyusha]] as the second ghost story of the night in "[[byezhin-prairie-place|Byezhin Prairie]]." The bailiff sends Yermil to the post, and on his return at night, slightly tipsy, he rides across the dam (008-368–008-370). There, on the drowned man's grave, he sees "a little lamb, so white and curly and pretty, running about" (008-371–008-372). He dismounts, picks up the unresisting lamb, and remounts — but his horse "stares at him, and snorts and shakes his head," sensing what Yermil does not (008-374–008-375).

As he rides on with the lamb in his arms, the creature locks eyes with him: "the lamb looks him straight in the face, like this" (008-377–008-378). Unsettled, Yermil tries to treat it like an ordinary animal, stroking its wool and calling "Chucky! chucky!" And then:

> "the lamb suddenly showed its teeth and said too, 'Chucky! chucky!'" (008-381–008-382)

The moment is one of Turgenev's most effective horror effects: the lamb's mimicry of human speech transforms an innocent image into something demonic. No explanation is offered. The narrator notes dryly that the boy "had hardly uttered this last word, when suddenly both dogs got up at once, and, barking convulsively, rushed away from the fire and disappeared in the darkness" (008-383–008-385) — as though the mere telling of Yermil's tale had conjured something into the summer night.

In a later sketch, "[[Two Country Gentlemen]]," Yermil is briefly mentioned as "Yermil, the coachman's" whose hens have been causing trouble (013-232), confirming that the huntsman is a recurring background figure in the narrator's rural world.
