# HOR AND KALINITCH

[001-001]Anyone who has chanced to pass from the Bolhovsky district into the

[001-002]Zhizdrinsky district, must have been impressed by the striking

[001-003]difference between the race of people in the province of Orel and the

[001-004]population of the province of Kaluga. The peasant of Orel is not tall,

[001-005]is bent in figure, sullen and suspicious in his looks; he lives in

[001-006]wretched little hovels of aspen-wood, labours as a serf in the fields,

[001-007]and engages in no kind of trading, is miserably fed, and wears slippers

[001-008]of bast: the rent-paying peasant of Kaluga lives in roomy cottages of

[001-009]pine-wood; he is tall, bold, and cheerful in his looks, neat and clean

[001-010]of countenance; he carries on a trade in butter and tar, and on

[001-011]holidays he wears boots. The village of the Orel province (we are

[001-012]speaking now of the eastern part of the province) is usually situated

[001-013]in the midst of ploughed fields, near a water-course which has been

[001-014]converted into a filthy pool. Except for a few of the

[001-015]ever-accommodating willows, and two or three gaunt birch-trees, you do

[001-016]not see a tree for a mile round; hut is huddled up against hut, their

[001-017]roofs covered with rotting thatch.... The villages of Kaluga, on the

[001-018]contrary, are generally surrounded by forest; the huts stand more

[001-019]freely, are more upright, and have boarded roofs; the gates fasten

[001-020]closely, the hedge is not broken down nor trailing about; there are no

[001-021]gaps to invite the visits of the passing pig.... And things are much

[001-022]better in the Kaluga province for the sportsman. In the Orel province

[001-023]the last of the woods and copses will have disappeared five years

[001-024]hence, and there is no trace of moorland left; in Kaluga, on the

[001-025]contrary, the moors extend over tens, the forest over hundreds of

[001-026]miles, and a splendid bird, the grouse, is still extant there; there

[001-027]are abundance of the friendly larger snipe, and the loud-clapping

[001-028]partridge cheers and startles the sportsman and his dog by its abrupt

[001-029]upward flight.

[001-030]On a visit to the Zhizdrinsky district in search of sport, I met in the

[001-031]fields a petty proprietor of the Kaluga province called Polutikin, and

[001-032]made his acquaintance. He was an enthusiastic sportsman; it follows,

[001-033]therefore, that he was an excellent fellow. He was liable, indeed, to a

[001-034]few weaknesses; he used, for instance, to pay his addresses to every

[001-035]unmarried heiress in the province, and when he had been refused her

[001-036]hand and house, broken-hearted he confided his sorrows to all his

[001-037]friends and acquaintances, and continued to shower offerings of sour

[001-038]peaches and other raw produce from his garden upon the young lady's

[001-039]relatives; he was fond of repeating one and the same anecdote, which,

[001-040]in spite of Mr. Polutikin's appreciation of its merits, had certainly

[001-041]never amused anyone; he admired the works of Akim Nahimov and the novel

[001-042]\_Pinna\_; he stammered; he called his dog Astronomer; instead of

[001-043]'however' said 'howsomever'; and had established in his household a

[001-044]French system of cookery, the secret of which consisted, according to

[001-045]his cook's interpretation, in a complete transformation of the natural

[001-046]taste of each dish; in this \_artiste's\_ hands meat assumed the flavour

[001-047]of fish, fish of mushrooms, macaroni of gunpowder; to make up for this,

[001-048]not a single carrot went into the soup without taking the shape of a

[001-049]rhombus or a trapeze. But, with the exception of these few and

[001-050]insignificant failings, Mr. Polutikin was, as has been said already, an

[001-051]excellent fellow.

[001-052]On the first day of my acquaintance with Mr. Polutikin, he invited me

[001-053]to stay the night at his house.

[001-054]'It will be five miles farther to my house,' he added; 'it's a long way

[001-055]to walk; let us first go to Hor's.' (The reader must excuse my omitting

[001-056]his stammer.)

[001-057]'Who is Hor?'

[001-058]'A peasant of mine. He is quite close by here.'

[001-059]We went in that direction. In a well-cultivated clearing in the middle

[001-060]of the forest rose Hor's solitary homestead. It consisted of several

[001-061]pine-wood buildings, enclosed by plank fences; a porch ran along the

[001-062]front of the principal building, supported on slender posts. We went

[001-063]in. We were met by a young lad of twenty, tall and good-looking.

[001-064]'Ah, Fedya! is Hor at home?' Mr. Polutikin asked him.

[001-065]'No. Hor has gone into town,' answered the lad, smiling and showing a

[001-066]row of snow-white teeth. 'You would like the little cart brought out?'

[001-067]'Yes, my boy, the little cart. And bring us some kvas.'

[001-068]We went into the cottage. Not a single cheap glaring print was pasted

[001-069]up on the clean boards of the walls; in the corner, before the heavy,

[001-070]holy picture in its silver setting, a lamp was burning; the table of

[001-071]linden-wood had been lately planed and scrubbed; between the joists and

[001-072]in the cracks of the window-frames there were no lively Prussian

[001-073]beetles running about, nor gloomy cockroaches in hiding. The young lad

[001-074]soon reappeared with a great white pitcher filled with excellent kvas,

[001-075]a huge hunch of wheaten bread, and a dozen salted cucumbers in a wooden

[001-076]bowl. He put all these provisions on the table, and then, leaning with

[001-077]his back against the door, began to gaze with a smiling face at us. We

[001-078]had not had time to finish eating our lunch when the cart was already

[001-079]rattling before the doorstep. We went out. A curly-headed, rosy-cheeked

[001-080]boy of fifteen was sitting in the cart as driver, and with difficulty

[001-081]holding in the well-fed piebald horse. Round the cart stood six young

[001-082]giants, very like one another, and Fedya.

[001-083]'All of these Hor's sons!' said Polutikin.

[001-084]'These are all Horkies' (\_i.e.\_ wild cats), put in Fedya, who had come

[001-085]after us on to the step; 'but that's not all of them: Potap is in the

[001-086]wood, and Sidor has gone with old Hor to the town. Look out, Vasya,' he

[001-087]went on, turning to the coachman; 'drive like the wind; you are driving

[001-088]the master. Only mind what you're about over the ruts, and easy a

[001-089]little; don't tip the cart over, and upset the master's stomach!'

[001-090]The other Horkies smiled at Fedya's sally. 'Lift Astronomer in!' Mr.

[001-091]Polutikin called majestically. Fedya, not without amusement, lifted the

[001-092]dog, who wore a forced smile, into the air, and laid her at the bottom

[001-093]of the cart. Vasya let the horse go. We rolled away. 'And here is my

[001-094]counting-house,' said Mr. Polutikin suddenly to me, pointing to a

[001-095]little low-pitched house. 'Shall we go in?' 'By all means.' 'It is no

[001-096]longer used,' he observed, going in; 'still, it is worth looking at.'

[001-097]The counting-house consisted of two empty rooms. The caretaker, a

[001-098]one-eyed old man, ran out of the yard. 'Good day, Minyaitch,' said Mr.

[001-099]Polutikin; 'bring us some water.' The one-eyed old man disappeared, and

[001-100]at once returned with a bottle of water and two glasses. 'Taste it,'

[001-101]Polutikin said to me; 'it is splendid spring water.' We drank off a

[001-102]glass each, while the old man bowed low. 'Come, now, I think we can go

[001-103]on,' said my new Friend. 'In that counting-house I sold the merchant

[001-104]Alliluev four acres of forest-land for a good price.' We took our seats

[001-105]in the cart, and in half-an-hour we had reached the court of the

[001-106]manor-house.

[001-107]'Tell me, please,' I asked Polutikin at supper; 'why does Hor live

[001-108]apart from your other peasants?'

[001-109]'Well, this is why; he is a clever peasant. Twenty-five years ago his

[001-110]cottage was burnt down; so he came up to my late father and said:

[001-111]"Allow me, Nikolai Kouzmitch," says he, "to settle in your forest, on

[001-112]the bog. I will pay you a good rent." "But what do you want to settle

[001-113]on the bog for?" "Oh, I want to; only, your honour, Nikolai Kouzmitch,

[001-114]be so good as not to claim any labour from me, but fix a rent as you

[001-115]think best." "Fifty roubles a year!" "Very well." "But I'll have no

[001-116]arrears, mind!" "Of course, no arrears"; and so he settled on the bog.

[001-117]Since then they have called him Hor' (\_i.e.\_ wild cat).

[001-118]'Well, and has he grown rich?' I inquired.

[001-119]'Yes, he has grown rich. Now he pays me a round hundred for rent, and I

[001-120]shall raise it again, I dare say. I have said to him more than once,

[001-121]"Buy your freedom, Hor; come, buy your freedom." ... But he declares,

[001-122]the rogue, that he can't; has no money, he says.... As though that were

[001-123]likely....'

[001-124]The next day, directly after our morning tea, we started out hunting

[001-125]again. As we were driving through the village, Mr. Polutikin ordered

[001-126]the coachman to stop at a low-pitched cottage and called loudly,

[001-127]'Kalinitch!' 'Coming, your honour, coming' sounded a voice from the

[001-128]yard; 'I am tying on my shoes.' We went on at a walk; outside the

[001-129]village a man of about forty over-took us. He was tall and thin, with a

[001-130]small and erect head. It was Kalinitch. His good-humoured; swarthy

[001-131]face, somewhat pitted with small-pox, pleased me from the first glance.

[001-132]Kalinitch (as I learnt afterwards) went hunting every day with his

[001-133]master, carried his bag, and sometimes also his gun, noted where game

[001-134]was to be found, fetched water, built shanties, and gathered

[001-135]strawberries, and ran behind the droshky; Mr. Polutikin could not stir

[001-136]a step without him. Kalinitch was a man of the merriest and gentlest

[001-137]disposition; he was constantly singing to himself in a low voice, and

[001-138]looking carelessly about him. He spoke a little through his nose, with

[001-139]a laughing twinkle in his light blue eyes, and he had a habit of

[001-140]plucking at his scanty, wedge-shaped beard with his hand. He walked not

[001-141]rapidly, but with long strides, leaning lightly on a long thin staff.

[001-142]He addressed me more than once during the day, and he waited on me

[001-143]without, obsequiousness, but he looked after his master as if he were a

[001-144]child. When the unbearable heat drove us at mid-day to seek shelter, he

[001-145]took us to his beehouse in the very heart of the forest. There

[001-146]Kalinitch opened the little hut for us, which was hung round with

[001-147]bunches of dry scented herbs. He made us comfortable on some dry hay,

[001-148]and then put a kind of bag of network over his head, took a knife, a

[001-149]little pot, and a smouldering stick, and went to the hive to cut us out

[001-150]some honey-comb. We had a draught of spring water after the warm

[001-151]transparent honey, and then dropped asleep to the sound of the

[001-152]monotonous humming of the bees and the rustling chatter of the leaves.

[001-153]A slight gust of wind awakened me.... I opened my eyes and saw

[001-154]Kalinitch: he was sitting on the threshold of the half-opened door,

[001-155]carving a spoon with his knife. I gazed a long time admiring his face,

[001-156]as sweet and clear as an evening sky. Mr. Polutikin too woke up. We did

[001-157]not get up at once. After our long walk and our deep sleep it was

[001-158]pleasant to lie without moving in the hay; we felt weary and languid in

[001-159]body, our faces were in a slight glow of warmth, our eyes were closed

[001-160]in delicious laziness. At last we got up, and set off on our wanderings

[001-161]again till evening. At supper I began again to talk of Hor and

[001-162]Kalinitch. 'Kalinitch is a good peasant,' Mr. Polutikin told me; 'he is

[001-163]a willing and useful peasant; he can't farm his land properly; I am

[001-164]always taking him away from it. He goes out hunting every day with

[001-165]me.... You can judge for yourself how his farming must fare.'

[001-166]I agreed with him, and we went to bed.

[001-167]The next day Mr. Polutikin was obliged to go to town about some

[001-168]business with his neighbour Pitchukoff. This neighbour Pitchukoff had

[001-169]ploughed over some land of Polutikin's, and had flogged a peasant woman

[001-170]of his on this same piece of land. I went out hunting alone, and before

[001-171]evening I turned into Hor's house. On the threshold of the cottage I

[001-172]was met by an old man--bald, short, broad-shouldered, and stout--Hor

[001-173]himself. I looked with curiosity at the man. The cut of his face

[001-174]recalled Socrates; there was the same high, knobby forehead, the same

[001-175]little eyes, the same snub nose. We went into the cottage together. The

[001-176]same Fedya brought me some milk and black bread. Hor sat down on a

[001-177]bench, and, quietly stroking his curly beard, entered into conversation

[001-178]with me. He seemed to know his own value; he spoke and moved slowly;

[001-179]from time to time a chuckle came from between his long moustaches.

[001-180]We discussed the sowing, the crops, the peasant's life.... He always

[001-181]seemed to agree with me; only afterwards I had a sense of awkwardness

[001-182]and felt I was talking foolishly.... In this way our conversation was

[001-183]rather curious. Hor, doubtless through caution, expressed himself very

[001-184]obscurely at times.... Here is a specimen of our talk.

[001-185]"Tell me, Hor," I said to him, "why don't you buy your freedom from

[001-186]your master?"

[001-187]"And what would I buy my freedom for? Now I know my master, and I know

[001-188]my rent.... We have a good master."

[001-189]'It's always better to be free,' I remarked. Hor gave me a dubious look.

[001-190]'Surely,' he said.

[001-191]'Well, then, why don't you buy your freedom?' Hor shook his head.

[001-192]'What would you have me buy it with, your honour?'

[001-193]'Oh, come, now, old man!'

[001-194]'If Hor were thrown among free men,' he continued in an undertone, as

[001-195]though to himself, 'everyone without a beard would be a better man than

[001-196]Hor.'

[001-197]'Then shave your beard.'

[001-198]'What is a beard? a beard is grass: one can cut it.'

[001-199]'Well, then?'

[001-200]'But Hor will be a merchant straight away; and merchants have a fine

[001-201]life, and they have beards.'

[001-202]'Why, do you do a little trading too?' I asked him.

[001-203]'We trade a little in a little butter and a little tar.... Would your

[001-204]honour like the cart put to?'

[001-205]'You're a close man and keep a tight rein on your tongue,' I thought to

[001-206]myself. 'No,' I said aloud, 'I don't want the cart; I shall want to be

[001-207]near your homestead to-morrow, and if you will let me, I will stay the

[001-208]night in your hay-barn.'

[001-209]'You are very welcome. But will you be comfortable in the barn? I will

[001-210]tell the women to lay a sheet and put you a pillow.... Hey, girls!' he

[001-211]cried, getting up from his place; 'here, girls!... And you, Fedya, go

[001-212]with them. Women, you know, are foolish folk.'

[001-213]A quarter of an hour later Fedya conducted me with a lantern to the

[001-214]barn. I threw myself down on the fragrant hay; my dog curled himself up

[001-215]at my feet; Fedya wished me good-night; the door creaked and slammed

[001-216]to. For rather a long time I could not get to sleep. A cow came up to

[001-217]the door, and breathed heavily twice; the dog growled at her with

[001-218]dignity; a pig passed by, grunting pensively; a horse somewhere near

[001-219]began to munch the hay and snort.... At last I fell asleep.

[001-220]At sunrise Fedya awakened me. This brisk, lively young man pleased me;

[001-221]and, from what I could see, he was old Hor's favourite too. They used

[001-222]to banter one another in a very friendly way. The old man came to meet

[001-223]me. Whether because I had spent the night under his roof, or for some

[001-224]other reason, Hor certainly treated me far more cordially than the day

[001-225]before.

[001-226]'The samovar is ready,' he told me with a smile; 'let us come and have

[001-227]tea.'

[001-228]We took our seats at the table. A robust-looking peasant woman, one of

[001-229]his daughters-in-law, brought in a jug of milk. All his sons came one

[001-230]after another into the cottage.

[001-231]'What a fine set of fellows you have!' I remarked to the old man.

[001-232]'Yes,' he said, breaking off a tiny piece of sugar with his teeth; 'me

[001-233]and my old woman have nothing to complain of, seemingly.'

[001-234]'And do they all live with you?'

[001-235]'Yes; they choose to, themselves, and so they live here.'

[001-236]'And are they all married?'

[001-237]'Here's one not married, the scamp!' he answered, pointing to Fedya,

[001-238]who was leaning as before against the door. 'Vaska, he's still too

[001-239]young; he can wait.'

[001-240]'And why should I get married?' retorted Fedya; 'I'm very well off as I

[001-241]am. What do I want a wife for? To squabble with, eh?'

[001-242]'Now then, you ... ah, I know you! you wear a silver ring.... You'd

[001-243]always be after the girls up at the manor house.... "Have done, do, for

[001-244]shame!"' the old man went on, mimicking the servant girls. 'Ah, I know

[001-245]you, you white-handed rascal!'

[001-246]'But what's the good of a peasant woman?'

[001-247]'A peasant woman--is a labourer,' said Hor seriously; 'she is the

[001-248]peasant's servant.'

[001-249]'And what do I want with a labourer?'

[001-250]'I dare say; you'd like to play with the fire and let others burn their

[001-251]fingers: we know the sort of chap you are.'

[001-252]'Well, marry me, then. Well, why don't you answer?'

[001-253]'There, that's enough, that's enough, giddy pate! You see we're

[001-254]disturbing the gentleman. I'll marry you, depend on it.... And you,

[001-255]your honour, don't be vexed with him; you see, he's only a baby; he's

[001-256]not had time to get much sense.'

[001-257]Fedya shook his head.

[001-258]'Is Hor at home?' sounded a well-known voice; and Kalinitch came into

[001-259]the cottage with a bunch of wild strawberries in his hands, which he

[001-260]had gathered for his friend Hor. The old man gave him a warm welcome. I

[001-261]looked with surprise at Kalinitch. I confess I had not expected such a

[001-262]delicate attention on the part of a peasant.

[001-263]That day I started out to hunt four hours later than usual, and the

[001-264]following three days I spent at Hor's. My new friends interested me. I

[001-265]don't know how I had gained their confidence, but they began to talk to

[001-266]me without constraint. The two friends were not at all alike. Hor was a

[001-267]positive, practical man, with a head for management, a rationalist;

[001-268]Kalinitch, on the other hand, belonged to the order of idealists and

[001-269]dreamers, of romantic and enthusiastic spirits. Hor had a grasp of

[001-270]actuality--that is to say, he looked ahead, was saving a little money,

[001-271]kept on good terms with his master and the other authorities; Kalinitch

[001-272]wore shoes of bast, and lived from hand to mouth. Hor had reared a

[001-273]large family, who were obedient and united; Kalinitch had once had a

[001-274]wife, whom he had been afraid of, and he had had no children. Hor took

[001-275]a very critical view of Mr. Polutikin; Kalinitch revered his master.

[001-276]Hor loved Kalinitch, and took protecting care of him; Kalinitch loved

[001-277]and respected Hor. Hor spoke little, chuckled, and thought for himself;

[001-278]Kalinitch expressed himself with warmth, though he had not the flow of

[001-279]fine language of a smart factory hand. But Kalinitch was endowed with

[001-280]powers which even Hor recognised; he could charm away haemorrhages,

[001-281]fits, madness, and worms; his bees always did well; he had a light

[001-282]hand. Hor asked him before me to introduce a newly bought horse to his

[001-283]stable, and with scrupulous gravity Kalinitch carried out the old

[001-284]sceptic's request. Kalinitch was in closer contact with nature; Hor

[001-285]with men and society. Kalinitch had no liking for argument, and

[001-286]believed in everything blindly; Hor had reached even an ironical point

[001-287]of view of life. He had seen and experienced much, and I learnt a good

[001-288]deal from him. For instance, from his account I learnt that every year

[001-289]before mowing-time a small, peculiar-looking cart makes its appearance

[001-290]in the villages. In this cart sits a man in a long coat, who sells

[001-291]scythes. He charges one rouble twenty-five copecks--a rouble and a half

[001-292]in notes--for ready money; four roubles if he gives credit. All the

[001-293]peasants, of course, take the scythes from him on credit. In two or

[001-294]three weeks he reappears and asks for the money. As the peasant has

[001-295]only just cut his oats, he is able to pay him; he goes with the

[001-296]merchant to the tavern, and there the debt is settled. Some landowners

[001-297]conceived the idea of buying the scythes themselves for ready money and

[001-298]letting the peasants have them on credit for the same price; but the

[001-299]peasants seemed dissatisfied, even dejected; they had deprived them of

[001-300]the pleasure of tapping the scythe and listening to the ring of the

[001-301]metal, turning it over and over in their hands, and telling the

[001-302]scoundrelly city-trader twenty times over, 'Eh, my friend, you won't

[001-303]take me in with your scythe!' The same tricks are played over the sale

[001-304]of sickles, only with this difference, that the women have a hand in

[001-305]the business then, and they sometimes drive the trader himself to the

[001-306]necessity--for their good, of course--of beating them. But the women

[001-307]suffer most ill-treatment through the following circumstances.

[001-308]Contractors for the supply of stuff for paper factories employ for the

[001-309]purchase of rags a special class of men, who in some districts are

[001-310]called eagles. Such an 'eagle' receives two hundred roubles in

[001-311]bank-notes from the merchant, and starts off in search of his prey.

[001-312]But, unlike the noble bird from whom he has derived his name, he does

[001-313]not swoop down openly and boldly upon it; quite the contrary; the

[001-314]'eagle' has recourse to deceit and cunning. He leaves his cart

[001-315]somewhere in a thicket near the village, and goes himself to the

[001-316]back-yards and back-doors, like someone casually passing, or simply a

[001-317]tramp. The women scent out his proximity and steal out to meet him. The

[001-318]bargain is hurriedly concluded. For a few copper half-pence a woman

[001-319]gives the 'eagle' not only every useless rag she has, but often even

[001-320]her husband's shirt and her own petticoat. Of late the women have

[001-321]thought it profitable to steal even from themselves, and to sell hemp

[001-322]in the same way--a great extension and improvement of the business for

[001-323]the 'eagles'! To meet this, however, the peasants have grown more

[001-324]cunning in their turn, and on the slightest suspicion, on the most

[001-325]distant rumors of the approach of an 'eagle,' they have prompt and

[001-326]sharp recourse to corrective and preventive measures. And, after all,

[001-327]wasn't it disgraceful? To sell the hemp was the men's business--and

[001-328]they certainly do sell it--not in the town (they would have to drag it

[001-329]there themselves), but to traders who come for it, who, for want of

[001-330]scales, reckon forty handfuls to the pood--and you know what a

[001-331]Russian's hand is and what it can hold, especially when he 'tries his

[001-332]best'! As I had had no experience and was not 'country-bred' (as they

[001-333]say in Orel) I heard plenty of such descriptions. But Hor was not

[001-334]always the narrator; he questioned me too about many things. He learned

[001-335]that I had been in foreign parts, and his curiosity was aroused....

[001-336]Kalinitch was not behind him in curiosity; but he was more attracted by

[001-337]descriptions of nature, of mountains and waterfalls, extraordinary

[001-338]buildings and great towns; Hor was interested in questions of

[001-339]government and administration. He went through everything in order.

[001-340]'Well, is that with them as it is with us, or different?... Come, tell

[001-341]us, your honour, how is it?' 'Ah, Lord, thy will be done!' Kalinitch

[001-342]would exclaim while I told my story; Hor did not speak, but frowned

[001-343]with his bushy eyebrows, only observing at times, 'That wouldn't do for

[001-344]us; still, it's a good thing--it's right.' All his inquiries, I cannot

[001-345]recount, and it is unnecessary; but from our conversations I carried

[001-346]away one conviction, which my readers will certainly not anticipate ...

[001-347]the conviction that Peter the Great was pre-eminently a

[001-348]Russian--Russian, above all, in his reforms. The Russian is so

[001-349]convinced of his own strength and powers that he is not afraid of

[001-350]putting himself to severe strain; he takes little interest in his past,

[001-351]and looks boldly forward. What is good he likes, what is sensible he

[001-352]will have, and where it comes from he does not care. His vigorous sense
