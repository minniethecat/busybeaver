# Lawrence Redding's Studio

Lawrence Redding's studio is a cottage on the grounds of the vicarage, where the young artist lives and works. It is the setting for one of the novel's most crucial scenes: the vicar's discovery of Redding and Anne Protheroe together, confirming their secret affair.

## Location

The studio is situated in the garden of the vicarage, within sight of the main house. Its proximity to the vicarage is narratively significant: the vicar can observe comings and goings at the studio, and the studio's location places the lovers within easy reach of the crime scene.

## Description

The studio is described primarily through its function as a workspace and rendezvous point. Redding paints there — Lettice Protheroe "in a bathing dress" and Anne Protheroe in more conventional poses — and the studio's artistic purpose provides cover for private meetings. The back window features in the anonymous letter's accusation against Griselda, who was allegedly "seen leaving Lawrence Redding's cottage by the back window at twenty past six on the fatal day" (030-031).

## Story Significance

The studio is where the love triangle becomes visible. The vicar's accidental discovery of Redding and Anne together — "You've surprised our secret, sir" (004-013) — is the moment when the novel's central motive is revealed to the narrator (though not yet to the police). The studio also functions as a rendezvous point after the murder, when Anne goes there to provide Redding with an alibi.

## Key Scenes

- **The Discovery**: The vicar finds Redding and Anne together, confirming their affair (004-013)
- **The Alibi**: Anne goes to the studio after the murder to establish Redding's alibi
- **The Back Window**: An anonymous letter claims Griselda was seen leaving by the back window at the time of the murder (030-031)

## Residents / Frequent Visitors

Resident: Lawrence Redding. Frequent visitors: Anne Protheroe, Lettice Protheroe (as model), and — according to the anonymous letter — Griselda Clement.
