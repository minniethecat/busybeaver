# Mary

Mary is the housemaid at the Vicarage, in service to Mr. and Mrs. Clement. It is Mary who first discovers Colonel Protheroe's body, making her the unwitting catalyst of the entire investigation. Though a minor character, she provides critical testimony and embodies the domestic world of St. Mary Mead against which the murder unfolds.

## Role in the Story

Mary serves as the story's first witness — it is her discovery of the body that transforms the vicarage from a home into a crime scene. Her testimony about the household's movements on the day of the murder provides the factual baseline for the investigation. As a servant, she is both invisible and omniscient: she observes everything but is rarely consulted, a position that mirrors Miss Marple's own relationship to the official investigation.

## Background

Mary "is in service at the Vicarage" (001-005). She is competent in her duties but, like all servants in detective fiction, knows more than her employers suspect. The narrator notes her tendency to take "a perverse pleasure" in announcing unwelcome visitors (004-003), suggesting a personality more complex than her station implies.

## Key Actions

### The Discovery

Mary is the first to find the body: a "boy is found bathed in blood. Mary will give evidence, won't you, Mary?" (001-004). This discovery — and Mary's reaction to it — sets the novel's events in motion.

### Announcing Colonel Melchett

Mary announces the arrival of the Chief Constable: "Colonel Melchett's here. Wants to see the master" (006-129). Her role as gatekeeper to the vicarage places her at the threshold between domestic life and official investigation.

### The Testimony

During the investigation, Mary is interviewed by Colonel Melchett about the events of the previous day, including when Mr. Redding was last at the vicarage and whether the quarrel between Redding and Protheroe was overheard (008-048–058). Her answers are factual and unrevealing — she reports what she saw without interpretation.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Clement**: Mary's employer. Their relationship is one of proper domestic hierarchy, though Clement relies on her for household management more than he acknowledges.

- **Griselda Clement**: Mary's mistress. Griselda's informal manner likely makes for a less rigid household than the traditional vicarage.

- **Colonel Melchett**: Mary is interviewed by the Chief Constable, who treats her with the formal politeness due a witness rather than the familiarity of a servant.

## Character Analysis

Mary represents the domestic infrastructure of St. Mary Mead — the servants, tradesmen, and villagers whose labor makes the gentry's lives possible and whose observations, if anyone thought to ask, might solve crimes more quickly than official investigation. Her discovery of the body is the novel's originating event, yet she remains peripheral to its resolution, a commentary on who is noticed and who is not in the world of the story.

## Key Quotes

> "Colonel Melchett's here. Wants to see the master." (006-129)

## Appearances

Mary appears primarily in the early chapters (1, 4, 6, 8), centered around the vicarage household and the immediate aftermath of the murder.
