# Sermon

A sermon is a religious discourse delivered as part of a church service. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, the vicar's struggle to write his Sunday sermon bookends the narrative and provides a running motif for the tension between his spiritual duties and the worldly distractions of murder investigation.

## Definition

In the Anglican tradition, the vicar delivers a weekly sermon — a prepared address expounding on scripture and its application to daily life. Mr. Clement's sermons are a professional obligation that he approaches with varying degrees of inspiration. His wife Griselda jokes that if he ate a rice pudding every day until Sunday, "you'd preach the most marvellous sermon" (001-030) — suggesting that his preaching is more affected by domestic comfort than divine inspiration.

## Context in the Novel

The sermon motif frames the novel at both ends. In the opening chapter, the vicar is "writing a sermon" (001-046) when Griselda interrupts him with news about the artist Lawrence Redding and the tensions surrounding Colonel Protheroe. This domestic interruption foreshadows the larger interruption to come — a murder in his own study.

On the afternoon before the murder, the vicar sits down "with the intention of sketching the outline of my Sunday sermon" but is interrupted by Lawrence Redding's visit (004-083). After Redding leaves, he tries again "to settle down to my sermon, but with very poor success. I kept thinking of Lawrence and Anne Protheroe" (004-095). The unwritten sermon symbolizes the vicar's divided attention between his spiritual calling and the human drama unfolding around him.

## Examples from the Text

### The Missing Book

Griselda's search for *The Stain on the Stairs* — a detective novel she was reading — while the vicar was "writing a sermon" creates a comic juxtaposition (001-046). The vicar writes about eternal truths while his wife reads about fictional crimes — a parallel that becomes ironic when a real crime intrudes.

### Hawes's Sermons

The curate Hawes's sermons are described as "dull and dogmatic" (026-002), a judgment that reinforces his characterization as a man of rigid and troubled faith. The contrast between Hawes's dogmatic preaching and his later psychological collapse deepens the novel's theme of hidden interior lives.

## Significance

The sermon motif serves as a gentle reminder of the vicar's proper role — spiritual guide to his flock — and of how thoroughly the murder disrupts that role. The vicar never does finish his sermon that week; the investigation consumes his attention, and the pulpit becomes a place not for preaching but for observing which parishioners have come to exchange gossip. The unwritten sermon is the novel's quiet emblem of a world turned upside down.
