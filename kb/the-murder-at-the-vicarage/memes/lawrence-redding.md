# Lawrence Redding

Lawrence Redding is a young artist living in St. Mary Mead, whose love affair with Anne Protheroe places him at the center of the murder investigation. His dramatic confession — walking into the police station, throwing down the pistol, and declaring "I did it" — seems to close the case immediately, but the truth is far more complicated.

## Role in the Story

Redding is the novel's most visible suspect. His confession is the first major plot turn, and his relationship with Anne Protheroe provides the emotional core of the mystery. The question of whether he is guilty — and whether his confession is genuine or protective — drives much of the investigation. His character embodies the novel's theme that the most obvious answer is rarely the correct one.

## Background

Redding is a painter who has taken a studio cottage in St. Mary Mead, where he paints portraits of the local gentry — including Lettice Protheroe ("in a bathing dress," to the colonel's fury) and Anne Protheroe. He is described as a decent young man, liked by the vicar and by Dr. Haydock, who says "he always struck me as a decent young chap" (007-013). His affair with Anne is born of genuine love and mutual desperation rather than casual infidelity.

## Key Actions

### The Confession

Redding's confession is the novel's defining dramatic moment. He walks into the police station at ten o'clock at night, "throws down the pistol on the table, and 'I did it,' he says. Just like that" (006-038). The confession is false — he is protecting Anne — but its theatricality and apparent sincerity convince Inspector Slack.

### The Pistol

Redding owns a Mauser pistol, which he mentioned casually at a tennis party before the murder: "I've got a Mauser pistol" (004-037). This casual remark becomes sinister in retrospect, and the pistol becomes the murder weapon — though not in Redding's hands.

### The Studio Meeting

On the night before the murder, the vicar observes Redding and Anne Protheroe together in the studio, confirming their affair. Redding's response — "You've surprised our secret, sir. What are you going to do about it?" (004-013) — is a mix of defiance and vulnerability.

## Relationships

- **Anne Protheroe**: His lover. Their relationship is the emotional engine of the plot. Each is willing to die — or go to prison — to protect the other.

- **Colonel Protheroe**: His rival and victim. Redding's quarrel with Protheroe provides the most obvious motive for murder.

- **Mr. Clement**: His confidant. Redding trusts the vicar and turns to him for advice after his confession, calling him "a good sort, padre" (004-093).

- **Lettice Protheroe**: The colonel's daughter, whom Redding paints — a commission that causes friction with Protheroe and fuels village gossip about a second romantic entanglement.
