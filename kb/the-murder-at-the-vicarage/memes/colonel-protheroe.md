# Colonel Protheroe

Colonel Lucius Protheroe is the murder victim in *The Murder at the Vicarage*. A retired army officer, churchwarden of [[the-parish|St. Mary Mead parish]], and the most disliked man in the village, his death at [[the-vicarage|the Vicarage]] — announced in the novel's opening pages — triggers an investigation in which the central problem is not finding a suspect, but narrowing the field. As [[Mr. Clement]], the vicar and narrator, observes even before the murder: "anyone who murdered Colonel Protheroe would be doing the world at large a service."

## Role in the Story

Colonel Protheroe is the novel's absent center. Though he dies early in the narrative, his personality — authoritarian, moralistic, and insufferably self-righteous — shapes every aspect of the investigation. He is the churchwarden who quarrels with the vicar over parish accounts; the husband whose coldness drives his young wife Anne into the arms of [[Lawrence Redding]]; the father whose disapproval poisons his relationship with his daughter [[Lettice Protheroe]]; and the magistrate whose rigid sense of justice makes him enemies throughout the village.

His function in the plot is paradoxical: by being so universally disliked, he becomes almost impossible to avenge. The investigation must determine not who hated him — nearly everyone did — but who hated him enough, and in the right way, to pull the trigger.

As Clement reflects in the novel's most quoted line:

> "Anyone who murdered Colonel Protheroe," I said, "would be doing the world at large a service." (001-002)

## Background

Colonel Protheroe is a retired army officer who has brought military discipline to village administration. His background in colonial service (implied by his rank and bearing) has given him habits of command ill-suited to the gentle politics of parish life. He is "a great opposer of ritual in any form" (001-020), making him an uncomfortable churchwarden under a vicar of moderate High Church sympathies.

His domestic arrangements compound his difficulties. Five years before the novel's events, the widowed Colonel married Anne, a woman considerably younger and more spirited than himself. Their marriage, as Clement observes, was unlikely to be happy: "I could imagine that the state of things at [[Old Hall]] might not be too happy" (002-040). His daughter Lettice from his first marriage adds another layer of domestic tension.

## Key Actions

### The Parish Dispute

Before his death, Colonel Protheroe is engaged in a running conflict with [[Mr. Clement]] over parish finances. The specific issue — an allegedly improper payment — is trivial, but the clash reveals Protheroe's character: he is incapable of compromise, suspicious of authority he does not himself wield, and utterly certain of his own rectitude. This dispute provides Clement with both a motive (however slight) and an alibi problem — the vicar was expected at the very meeting Protheroe was attending when he died.

### The Confrontation with Lawrence Redding

Protheroe's most consequential action before his death is his conflict with [[Lawrence Redding]], the young artist who has been painting [[Griselda Clement]]'s portrait at the vicarage studio. The precise nature of their quarrel is initially unclear, but [[Miss Marple]] provides the essential clue: "Rather a violent temper, I'm afraid. He had quite a serious quarrel with Colonel Protheroe the other day" (002-073). Redding's hostility to Protheroe, rooted in his love for [[Anne Protheroe]], provides the investigation's first viable [[motive]].

### As Magistrate

Colonel Protheroe's role as a local magistrate extends his authority beyond the parish and into the legal sphere. His decisions from the bench have created enemies beyond the circle of his immediate acquaintance. The investigation must consider whether any of those enemies — poachers, petitioners, those he sentenced — might have sought revenge.

## Character Analysis

Colonel Protheroe belongs to a recognizable Christie type: the military man whose peacetime life is a series of petty tyrannies masking deeper inadequacy. His tragedy is not that he is evil — by all accounts he is merely unpleasant — but that his personality creates the conditions in which murder becomes thinkable. The novel's title is itself a kind of joke: the murder at the vicarage was almost inevitable, given who Protheroe was and where he chose to make his stand.

## Appearances

Colonel Protheroe appears directly only in the novel's opening chapters, but his presence — through the reactions of survivors, the evidence of his character, and the mystery of his death — dominates every page that follows.
