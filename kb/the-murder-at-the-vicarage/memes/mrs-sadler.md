# Mrs. Sadler

## Role

Mrs. Sadler is a resident of St. Mary Mead who serves as landlady to the curate, Hawes, during his posting at the parish. Her most significant moment in the narrative is discovering Hawes after his suicide attempt, a key turning point in the investigation.

## Key Scenes

### Discovery of Hawes (Chapter 28)

Mrs. Sadler is present at the critical moment when Hawes attempts suicide. Chapter 28 describes how Hawes's "landlady, Mrs. Sadler, laboriously unfastened two b—" [028-002], suggesting she was the one who found him and attempted to intervene or summon help. The scene is partially cut off in the available narrative fragments, but her role as first responder is clear.

### Mention in the Investigation (Chapter 31)

Mrs. Sadler is referenced again in Chapter 31, where it is suggested that "he were to hint that somebody — Mrs. Sadler — or one of her children — had ac—" [031-007]. This connects her — or her household — to the investigative threads surrounding Hawes and potentially his state of mind leading up to the incident.

## Relationships

- **Hawes**: Her tenant, the curate whose suicide attempt she discovered [028-002]
- **Mr. Clement**: The vicar who employs Hawes and would have been involved in his welfare

## Significance

Mrs. Sadler is a minor character whose narrative function is that of a witness: she provides the link between Hawes's private life and his public role as curate. Her presence at the scene of the suicide attempt makes her an incidental but important figure in the chain of events that constitute the investigation's broader context.
