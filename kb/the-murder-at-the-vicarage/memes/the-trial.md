# The Trial

The trial of Lawrence Redding and Anne Protheroe is the public culmination of the Protheroe murder case. Conducted at the Assize Court, it results in the acquittal of both defendants — a legally correct outcome achieved through a process that never identifies the real killer.

## Timeline

The trial takes place some months after the murder, following the completion of the police investigation and the inquest. It is described only briefly at the novel's conclusion, as "a matter of public knowledge" (032-003).

## Participants

- **Lawrence Redding**: Defendant, charged with murder
- **Anne Protheroe**: Defendant, charged as accessory
- **The Judge**: Presiding over the proceedings
- **The Jury**: Delivering the verdict of acquittal
- **Inspector Slack**: The investigating officer, whose "zeal and intelligence" are publicly credited

## Narrative

The trial is presented as a public performance that resolves nothing. The defendants are innocent of murder (though guilty of adultery and, in Anne's case, of retrieving the pistol), and the legal system correctly acquits them. But the real killer is never identified in court, and Inspector Slack — whose "zeal and intelligence had resulted in the criminals being brought to justice" (032-003) — receives public credit for an outcome he did not achieve. The irony is that "justice" in the legal sense has been served (the innocent are acquitted) while justice in the deeper sense (the guilty are identified) remains incomplete.

## Significance to the Investigation

The trial's significance is primarily thematic. It demonstrates the gap between legal process and moral truth — a gap that the novel has been exploring throughout. The legal system can acquit the innocent, but it cannot identify the guilty when the guilty person has not been charged. The real resolution occurs privately, through Miss Marple's quiet identification of the murderer, not through the public spectacle of the courtroom.
