# Telephone

## Definition

In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, the telephone functions as more than a communication device — it is a central plot mechanism that triggers key events, establishes alibis, and drives the investigation forward. The novel, set in 1930, captures the telephone as a relatively modern technology that shapes both domestic life and detective work in the village of St. Mary Mead.

## Context

### As Plot Trigger

The telephone call sets the narrative in motion. At half-past five, "the telephone rang" [004-096] with the news that Mr. Abbott of Lower Farm was dying. This call draws Mr. Clement away from the Vicarage, creating the window during which Colonel Protheroe is murdered. This initial call is later revealed to be part of the murderer's plan.

### As Investigative Tool

Inspector Slack immediately identifies the telephone call as a priority line of inquiry: "Trace the telephone call. You can't tell me more definite?" [013-052]. The telephone becomes central to the investigation as Slack works to identify who made the anonymous call that lured the vicar away: "I've traced that telephone call that you received" [017-002].

### As Alibi Mechanism

The telephone calls serve a dual purpose in the narrative: they both create the murder opportunity (by summoning the vicar away) and establish alibis. Slack notes: "That's two queer telephone calls in one day. Makes you think" [017-022]. The timing of the telephone call is later incorporated into the timeline of events: "Shot heard. (Accepting telephone call time as correct.)" [026-080].

### The Price Ridley Call

Mrs. Price Ridley's telephone call — which she received while allegedly witnessing something suspicious — is a key thread. Miss Marple recounts "the history of Mrs. Price Ridley's telephone call" [014-058] and analyzes it with characteristic precision. The call provides one of the crucial timeline anchors of the case.

## Examples

- **The Abbott call**: The telephone rings at half-past five, summoning Mr. Clement to Lower Farm [004-096]
- **Mrs. Price Ridley's call**: A mysterious anonymous call received at a critical time, becoming an alibi puzzle [014-058]
- **Redding's telephone alibi**: Another call that forms part of the alibi network, traced by Slack [017-002]
- **Miss Marple's analysis**: She cross-references all the telephone calls to reconstruct the true timeline

## Significance

The telephone in *The Murder at the Vicarage* represents the intersection of modern technology and traditional village life. It enables the murderer to create a false alibi through remote communication while also providing the detective with a traceable trail. The novel, published in 1930, reflects the era's fascination with and suspicion of telecommunications as a tool that could be used for both crime and detection.
