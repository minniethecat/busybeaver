# Much Benham Police

The Much Benham Police are the local law enforcement authority responsible for investigating Colonel Protheroe's murder. Led by Inspector Slack under the supervision of Colonel Melchett, they represent official investigative procedure — competent, thorough, and fundamentally incapable of solving a crime that requires understanding of human nature rather than collection of evidence.

## Description

Much Benham is the nearest town to St. Mary Mead, "two miles away" (005-069), and its police station serves as the base for the murder investigation. The force includes uniformed constables, plainclothes detectives, and administrative staff. Inspector Slack is the senior investigating officer, reporting to Colonel Melchett, the chief constable.

## Role in the Novel

The police are the novel's official investigators, and their limitations are essential to the plot. Inspector Slack is energetic and methodical: he arrives at the crime scene promptly, collects evidence, interviews witnesses, and constructs theories. But his theories are consistently wrong — Lawrence Redding as the obvious killer, Mrs. Lestrange as the blackmailer, Archer as the alternative suspect — because he cannot see beyond the surface logic of evidence to the deeper patterns of human behavior that Miss Marple recognizes.

The police's public success at the trial — where Slack receives "great credit" for his "zeal and intelligence" (032-003) — is ironic. The legal system acquits the innocent, but the police never identify the guilty.

## Key Members

- **Inspector Slack**: Senior investigating officer — energetic, unimaginative, wrong about everything
- **Colonel Melchett**: Chief Constable — Slack's superior, who occasionally restrains his enthusiasm
- **Constables**: The uniformed officers who collect evidence and take statements

## Story Significance

The Much Benham Police embody the novel's critique of professional investigation. They represent the official, procedural approach to crime-solving — an approach that works for ordinary crimes but fails when confronted with the complex web of relationships, secrets, and misdirections that characterize the Protheroe case. Their failure is not incompetence but a category error: they are looking for the wrong kind of truth in the wrong way.
