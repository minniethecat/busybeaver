# Colonel Melchett

Colonel Melchett is the Chief Constable of the county and the senior police authority overseeing the investigation into Colonel Protheroe's murder. Courteous, well-meaning, and thoroughly conventional, he represents the upper tier of official authority — competent within established frameworks but unable to perceive truths that lie outside them.

## Role in the Story

Melchett is the novel's embodiment of official authority at its highest local level. As Chief Constable, he outranks Inspector Slack and appears at key moments to direct or review the investigation. His manner is invariably polite — "Sorry, Clement" he says after calling Redding a "damned young fool" (007-033) — but his thinking never transcends the obvious. He and Slack form a complementary pair: Slack is energetic and wrong; Melchett is measured and wrong. Together they demonstrate that rank and procedure cannot substitute for insight.

## Background

Colonel Melchett is "a dapper little man with a keen eye and a neatly clipped military moustache" (007-001). His title suggests a military background, and his conduct reflects it — he is efficient, orderly, and accustomed to deference. He arrives at the vicarage promptly upon learning of the murder and takes charge with the ease of long practice.

## Key Actions

### Arrival at the Vicarage

Melchett arrives at the vicarage shortly after the murder is discovered. Mary announces him: "Colonel Melchett's here. Wants to see the master" (006-129). He immediately begins directing the investigation, interviewing witnesses and coordinating with Slack.

### The Interrogation

Melchett conducts or oversees key interviews, including that of Mary the maid (008-048–058). His questioning is methodical — he asks when Redding was last at the vicarage, whether the quarrel was overheard — but he never probes beneath the surface of the answers he receives. When Anne Protheroe confesses to the murder, Melchett is present, responding "gently" (008-085) — a reaction that reveals his fundamental decency but also his inability to see the confession as anything other than what it purports to be.

### The Inquest

Melchett participates in the inquest, finding the proceedings "a bit crowded" (009-009) but accepting its conclusions without question. Throughout the investigation, he remains cordial to Clement, never suspecting that the vicar knows more than he reveals.

## Relationships

- **Inspector Slack**: Melchett's subordinate. Melchett occasionally restrains Slack's enthusiasm but generally endorses his conclusions. Their professional relationship is correct but unilluminating.

- **Mr. Clement**: Melchett treats the vicar with the deference due a clergyman, never imagining that Clement's reticence conceals knowledge rather than ignorance.

- **Colonel Protheroe**: The victim. Though no direct relationship is detailed, the two colonels — one Chief Constable, one magistrate — represent the same class and institutional world.

## Character Analysis

Melchett is not incompetent — he is limited by the very qualities that make him a good Chief Constable: propriety, respect for procedure, and faith in the obvious. He cannot imagine that the vicar's wife might be concealing evidence, that the confession he received was performative, or that an elderly spinster might see what he cannot. His failure is institutional: the police system he represents is designed to process ordinary crimes, not the intricate deceptions of St. Mary Mead.

## Key Quotes

> "Damned young fool. Sorry, Clement. But he really is." (007-033)

> Colone Melchett said gently: (008-085)

## Appearances

Melchett appears primarily in Chapters 6–9, during the initial investigation, the confession, and the inquest.
