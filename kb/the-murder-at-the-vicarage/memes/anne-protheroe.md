# Anne Protheroe

Anne Protheroe is the young, beautiful wife of Colonel Protheroe and the lover of the artist Lawrence Redding. Trapped in a marriage to a much older, domineering man, she becomes one of the two primary suspects in his murder — a case complicated by the fact that both she and her lover confess, each trying to protect the other.

## Role in the Story

Anne Protheroe is at the emotional center of the novel's love triangle. Her affair with Lawrence Redding provides the most visible motive for the murder: together, they are "the only two people who had a motive for making away with Protheroe" (009-085). Her actions after the murder — including fabricating an alibi for Lawrence and retrieving the pistol from her husband's dressing-table — make her look guilty. But her guilt, like so much else in the novel, is more complex than it appears.

## Background

Anne is Colonel Protheroe's second wife, considerably younger than her husband and visibly unhappy in the marriage. She endures Protheroe's authoritarianism, his interference in parish affairs, and the hostility of her stepdaughter Lettice, who treats her with barely concealed contempt. Her affair with Lawrence Redding is presented not as a casual liaison but as genuine love — a desperate escape from a suffocating marriage.

## Key Actions

### The Alibi

After the murder, Anne goes to Lawrence's studio and provides him with an alibi, claiming they were together at the time of the shooting. This act of loyalty simultaneously protects and incriminates both of them, creating the central paradox of the investigation.

### Retrieving the Pistol

Anne admits to retrieving her husband's Mauser pistol from his dressing-table drawer (008-103). This admission places the murder weapon in her hands shortly before the crime — a detail that the police find highly significant but that has an innocent explanation.

### The Final Truth

Anne's confession, like Lawrence's, is motivated by love rather than guilt. Each believes the other is the murderer and tries to take the blame. The eventual revelation that neither is guilty transforms Anne from suspect to victim — a woman whose unhappy marriage made her look like a killer.

## Relationships

- **Colonel Protheroe**: Her husband. Their marriage is described as a prison from which she sees no escape except through his death or her own departure.

- **Lawrence Redding**: Her lover. Their relationship is presented sympathetically — two people who found love in impossible circumstances and whose attempts to protect each other nearly lead to their mutual destruction.

- **Lettice Protheroe**: Her stepdaughter. Their relationship is openly hostile, with Lettice treating Anne as an interloper.

- **Mr. Clement**: Her vicar, who provides pastoral care while privately suspecting her of murder.

## Key Quotes

> "I hated him. I'm glad he's dead." (Anne's admission to the vicar)

> "The pistol! Oh, it was my husband's. I got it out of the drawer of his dressing-table." (008-103)
