# The Vicarage

The Vicarage is the residence of [[Mr. Clement]], his wife [[Griselda Clement]], and their household — and the site of [[Colonel Protheroe]]'s [[the-murder|murder]]. It is the novel's primary setting and organizing metaphor: a place of spiritual and domestic order breached by violence, where the study becomes a crime scene and the garden a nexus of illicit relationships. As Clement himself notes, the story begins "at luncheon at the Vicarage" (001-001) — and the Vicarage remains the gravitational center of events throughout.

## Location

The vicarage sits at the heart of [[St. Mary Mead]], adjacent to [[the-parish|the church]] and connected by footpaths to both [[Old Hall]] (via a garden gate and wooded path) and [[Miss Marple]]'s cottage (via the vicarage garden). This geography is crucial to the [[the-murder|crime]]: the murderer's access to the study — whether through the front door, the garden window, or some less obvious route — is one of the central puzzles of the investigation.

The narrator's own sketch of the vicarage and its grounds (003-060) provides a visual key to the geography of the crime:

> "There is a path from Old Hall through the woods which comes out by our garden gate, so that most people coming from there come in at that gate and up to the study window instead of going a long way round by the road and coming to the front door." (002-005)

## Description

The vicarage is a working household as well as a residence. [[mary|Mary]], the maid, serves meals and announces visitors; the rhythms of domestic life — meals, tea, sermon-writing — continue around and through the investigation. This juxtaposition of the mundane and the murderous is one of Christie's characteristic effects, and the vicarage setting enables it perfectly.

The key spaces within the vicarage and their roles in the narrative are:

| Space | Function in Daily Life | Role in the Crime |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| The Study | Clement's writing room, facing the garden | The murder scene — Protheroe is shot here while waiting for Clement |
| The Garden | Domestic outdoor space, includes the studio | Contains Lawrence Redding's painting studio; provides access routes to and from the study |
| The Dining Room | Family meals | Setting of the novel's opening conversation, where Protheroe's murder is first contemplated in jest |
| The Front Door | Main entrance | "Always left open" (005-008) — a detail with profound implications for who could have entered |

## Story Significance

The vicarage is not merely a backdrop but an active participant in the narrative. Its open front door ("always left open," 005-008) symbolizes the permeability of the domestic sphere to violence. Its study — "my own particular room" — is the vicar's private space violated by public crime. And its garden, containing the studio where [[Lawrence Redding]] paints, is the site of the emotional drama (the Redding-Anne Protheroe affair) that provides the murder's most visible [[motive]].

The vicarage also functions as a liminal space between the official and unofficial investigations. [[Inspector Slack]] uses it as a base of operations; [[Miss Marple]] observes its comings and goings from her neighboring garden; Clement himself moves between the roles of suspect, investigator, and confidant — all within the walls of his own home.

## Key Scenes

| Scene | Location | Significance |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| The opening luncheon | Dining Room | Protheroe's murder is first mentioned — as a joke — before it happens |
| The discovery of the body | The Study | Clement finds Protheroe shot, setting the investigation in motion |
| Redding's departure | Vicarage Gate | Clement encounters a distraught Lawrence Redding leaving the vicarage grounds (005-001) |
| The police investigation | Throughout | Slack and his team treat the vicarage as both crime scene and command post |
| Miss Marple's observations | From her garden | The physical proximity of Miss Marple's house to the vicarage enables her surveillance |
