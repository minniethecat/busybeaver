# Hawes

Hawes is the curate of St. Mary Mead, newly arrived and already a source of friction with Colonel Protheroe over his "Romish tendencies" (002-041). His psychological unraveling in the novel's later chapters — marked by anonymous letters, a suspicious overdose, and a garbled confession — makes him one of the investigation's most ambiguous figures.

## Role in the Story

Hawes appears initially as a minor character — the High Church curate whose ritualistic practices annoy Colonel Protheroe and whose sermons are "dull and dogmatic" (026-002). But in the novel's final act, he emerges as a figure of genuine pathos. The anonymous letters are traced to his handwriting; he is found with an empty cachet box; he takes an overdose; and he makes a confession that seems to close the case — until Miss Marple reveals that Hawes's confession is not what it appears.

## Background

Hawes has been at St. Mary Mead for just over three weeks when the murder occurs. He "has High Church views and fasts on Fridays" (001-020), practices that antagonize the Low Church Protheroe. His manner is nervous, his health fragile, and his faith apparently more troubled than his dogmatic sermons suggest.

## Key Actions

### The Anonymous Letters

Hawes is the author of the anonymous letters that arrive during the investigation. But his motive is not malice — the letters are a cry for help from a man drowning in guilt and confusion. Miss Marple recognizes this when others see only evidence of criminal intent.

### The Overdose

When confronted with the evidence against him, Hawes takes an overdose from a cachet box labeled "Cherubim" (028-027). The vicar faces "the strongest temptation of my life" — to let Hawes die and close the case — but intervenes to save him.

### The Confession

Hawes's confession, extracted in a state of physical and mental collapse, seems to implicate him in the murder. But Miss Marple understands that Hawes is confessing not to murder but to a different sin entirely — and that his confession is designed to protect someone else.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Clement**: His vicar and superior. Clement's attitude toward Hawes shifts from mild annoyance to genuine concern to horrified suspicion and finally to compassion.

- **Colonel Protheroe**: His antagonist in church politics. Protheroe's opposition to Hawes's "Romish tendencies" creates pre-murder tension.

- **Miss Marple**: The only character who correctly interprets Hawes's actions as evidence of guilt for something other than murder.

## Character Analysis

Hawes represents the novel's exploration of guilt that is real but misdirected. He is guilty — of writing anonymous letters, of weakness, of some undisclosed sin — but not of murder. His willingness to accept blame for a crime he did not commit makes him a mirror image of Lawrence Redding, who also falsely confesses, but for love rather than guilt.
