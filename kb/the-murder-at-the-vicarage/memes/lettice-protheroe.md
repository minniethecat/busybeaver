# Lettice Protheroe

Lettice Protheroe is Colonel Protheroe's daughter from his first marriage. Described by the vicar as "something of a minx" (002-038), she provides one of the novel's secondary tensions: her barely concealed hostility toward her stepmother Anne, her provocative behavior with Lawrence Redding, and her conspicuous lack of grief after her father's death.

## Role in the Story

Lettice is a minor but significant figure in the novel's ecology of suspicion. Her presence as a model for Lawrence Redding fuels village gossip — "I wonder if there is anything between young Lawrence Redding and Lettice Protheroe" (002-110) — and provides one possible motive for her father's quarrel with the artist. Her relationship with her stepmother is openly hostile, and her reaction to her father's murder is notably cool.

## Background

Lettice is the daughter of Colonel Protheroe's first marriage and resents her stepmother Anne, who is closer to her own age than to her father's. She is described as attractive, headstrong, and indifferent to village opinion. Her father's authoritarian control has not produced obedience but rebellion.

## Key Actions

### The Bathing Dress Portrait

Lettice sits for Lawrence Redding in a bathing dress, a scandalous choice that provokes her father's fury and becomes the ostensible reason for Protheroe's quarrel with Redding. The portrait is, in fact, a red herring — the real quarrel was about Anne, not Lettice.

### After the Murder

Lettice's response to her father's death is notably unsentimental. She shows no grief and makes no pretense of mourning, a reaction that shocks the village but is consistent with her character — and, the novel suggests, with the reality that Colonel Protheroe was not a man who inspired love in his family.

## Relationships

- **Colonel Protheroe**: Her father. Their relationship is one of mutual frustration: his authoritarianism failing to control her independence.

- **Anne Protheroe**: Her stepmother. Lettice treats Anne with contempt, creating a household dynamic that contributes to Anne's desperation.

- **Lawrence Redding**: Her portrait artist. Village gossip suggests a romantic connection, but this is another of the novel's false leads.

## Character Analysis

Lettice represents the younger generation's rejection of Protheroe's rigid values. Where her father demands order and deference, Lettice offers defiance and indifference. Her lack of grief is not evidence of guilt but of the emotional poverty of her relationship with her father — a poverty that, the novel suggests, Protheroe himself created.
