[024-001]I returned to the [[the-vicarage|Vicarage]] to find [[hawes|Hawes]] waiting for me in my study. He was pacing up and down nervously, and when I entered the room he started as though he had been shot.

[024-002]“You must excuse me,” he said, wiping his forehead. “My nerves are all to pieces lately.”

[024-003]“My dear fellow,” I said, “you positively must get away for a change. We shall have you breaking down altogether, and that [[the-will|will]] never do.”

[024-004]“I can’t desert my post. No, that is a thing I will never do.”

[024-005]“It’s not a case of desertion. You are ill. I’m sure [[dr-haydock|Haydock]] would agree with me.”

[024-006]“Haydock—Haydock. What kind of a doctor is he? An ignorant country practitioner.”

[024-007]“I think you’re unfair to him. He has always been considered a very able man in his profession.”

[024-008]“Oh! Perhaps. Yes, I dare say. But I don’t like him. However, that’s not what I came to say. I came to ask you if you would be kind enough to preach tonight instead of me. I—I really do not feel equal to it.”

[024-009]“Why, certainly. I will take the service for you.”

[024-010]“No, no. I wish to take the service. I am perfectly fit. It is only the idea of getting up in the pulpit, of all those eyes staring at me. …”

[024-011]He shut his eyes and swallowed convulsively.

[024-012]It is clear to me that there is something very wrong indeed with Hawes. He seemed aware of my thoughts, for he opened his eyes and said quickly:

[024-013]“There is nothing really wrong with me. It is just these headaches—these awful racking headaches. I wonder if you could let me have a glass of water.”

[024-014]“Certainly,” I said.

[024-015]I went and fetched it myself from the tap. Ringing bells is a profitless form of exercise in our house.

[024-016]I brought the water to him and he thanked me. He took from his pocket a small cardboard box, and opening it, extracted a rice paper capsule, which he swallowed with the aid of the water.

[024-017]“A headache powder,” he explained.

[024-018]I suddenly wondered whether Hawes might have become addicted to drugs. It would explain a great many of his peculiarities.

[024-019]“You don’t take too many, I hope,” I said.

[024-020]“No—oh, no. Dr. Haydock warned me against that. But it is really wonderful. They bring instant relief.”

[024-021]Indeed he already seemed calmer and more composed.

[024-022]He stood up.

[024-023]“Then you will preach tonight? It’s very good of you, sir.”

[024-024]“Not at all. And I insist on taking the service too. Get along home and rest. No, I won’t have any argument. Not another word.”

[024-025]He thanked me again. Then he said, his eyes sliding past me to the window:

[024-026]“You—have been up at [[old-hall|Old Hall]] today, haven’t you, sir?”

[024-027]“Yes.”

[024-028]“Excuse me—but were you sent for?”

[024-029]I looked at him in surprise, and he flushed.

[024-030]“I’m sorry, sir. I—I just thought some new development might have arisen and that was why Mrs. [[colonel-protheroe|Protheroe]] had sent for you.”

[024-031]I had not the faintest intention of satisfying Hawes’s curiosity.

[024-032]“She wanted to discuss the [[funeral|funeral arrangements]] and one or two other small matters with me,” I said.

[024-033]“Oh! That was all. I see.”

[024-034]I did not speak. He fidgeted from foot to foot, and finally said:

[024-035]“Mr. [[lawrence-redding|Redding]] came to see me last night. I—I can’t imagine why.”

[024-036]“Didn’t he tell you?”

[024-037]“He—he just said he thought he’d look me up. Said it was a bit lonely in the evenings. He’s never done such a thing before.”

[024-038]“Well, he’s supposed to be pleasant company,” I said, smiling.

[024-039]“What does he want to come and see me for? I don’t like it.” His voice rose shrilly. “He spoke of dropping in again. What does it all mean? What idea do you think he has got into his head?”

[024-040]“Why should you suppose he has any ulterior motive?” I asked.

[024-041]“I don’t like it,” repeated Hawes obstinately. “I’ve never gone against *him* in any way. I never suggested that *he* was guilty—even when he accused himself I said it seemed most incomprehensible. If I’ve had [[suspicion|suspicions]] of anybody it’s been of Archer—never of him. Archer is a totally different proposition—a godless irreligious ruffian. A drunken blackguard.”

[024-042]“Don’t you think you’re being a little harsh?” I said. “After all, we really know very little about the man.”

[024-043]“A poacher, in and out of prison, capable of anything.”

[024-044]“Do you really think he shot [[colonel-melchett|Colonel]] Protheroe?” I asked curiously.

[024-045]Hawes has an inveterate dislike of answering yes or no. I have noticed it several times lately.

[024-046]“Don’t you think yourself, sir, that it’s the only possible solution?”

[024-047]“As far as we know,” I said, “there’s no evidence of any kind against him.”

[024-048]“His threats,” said Hawes eagerly. “You forget about his threats.”

[024-049]“I am sick and tired of hearing about Archer’s threats. As far as I can make out, there is no direct evidence that he ever made any.”

[024-050]“He was determined to be revenged on Colonel Protheroe. He primed himself with drink and then shot him.”

[024-051]“That’s pure supposition.”

[024-052]“But you will admit that it’s perfectly probable?”

[024-053]“No, I don’t.”

[024-054]“Possible, then?”

[024-055]“Possible, yes.”

[024-056]Hawes glanced at me sideways.

[024-057]“Why don’t you think it’s probable?”

[024-058]“Because,” I said, “a man like Archer wouldn’t think of shooting a man with a pistol. It’s the wrong weapon.”

[024-059]Hawes seemed taken aback by my argument. Evidently it wasn’t the objection he had expected.

[024-060]“Do you really think the objection is feasible?” he asked doubtingly.

[024-061]“To my mind it is a complete stumbling block to Archer’s having committed the crime,” I said.

[024-062]In face of my positive assertion, Hawes said no more. He thanked me again and left.

[024-063]I had gone as far as the front door with him, and on the hall table I saw four notes. They had certain characteristics in common. The handwriting was almost unmistakably feminine, they all bore the words, “By hand, Urgent,” and the only difference I could see was that one was noticeably dirtier than the rest.

[024-064]Their similarity gave me a curious feeling of seeing—not double but quadruple.

[024-065][[mary|Mary]] came out of the kitchen and caught me staring at them.

[024-066]“Come by hand since lunch time,” she volunteered. “All but one. I found that in the box.”

[024-067]I nodded, gathered them up and took them into the study.

[024-068]The first one ran thus:

[024-069]> “Dear Mr. Clement—Something has come to my knowledge which I feel you ought to know. It concerns the death of poor Colonel Protheroe. I should much appreciate your advice on the matter—whether to go to [[much-benham-police|the police]] or not. Since my dear husband’s death, I have such a shrinking from every kind of publicity. Perhaps you could run in and see me for a few minutes this afternoon.

[024-070]> Yours sincerely,

[024-071]> **Martha [[mrs-price-ridley|Price Ridley]]**.”

[024-072]I opened the second:

[024-073]> “Dear Mr. Clement—I am so troubled—so *exercised* in my mind—to know what I ought to do. Something has come to my ears that I feel may be important. I have such a *horror* of being mixed up with [[police|the police]] in any way. I am so disturbed and distressed. Would it be asking too much of you, dear vicar, to drop in for a few minutes and solve my doubts and perplexities for me in the wonderful way you always do?

[024-074]> “Forgive my troubling you,

[024-075]> “Yours very sincerely,

[024-076]> **Caroline [[miss-wetherby|Wetherby]]**.”

[024-077]The third, I felt, I could almost have recited beforehand.

[024-078]> “Dear Mr. Clement—Something most important has come to my ears. I feel you should be the first to know about it. Will you call in and see me this afternoon some time? I will wait in for you.”

[024-079]This militant epistle was signed “**Amanda [[miss-hartnell|Hartnell]]**.”

[024-080]I opened the fourth missive. It has been my good fortune to be troubled with very few [[anonymous-letter|anonymous letters]]. An [[letter|anonymous letter]] is, I think, the meanest and cruellest weapon there is. This one was no exception. It purported to be written by an illiterate person, but several things inclined me to disbelieve that assumption.

[024-081]> “Dear [[mr-clement|Vicar]]—I think you ought to know what is Going On. Your lady has been seen coming out of Mr. Redding’s cottage in a surreptitious manner. You know wot i mean. The two are Carrying On together. i think you ought to know.

[024-082]> “A Friend.”

[024-083]I made a faint exclamation of disgust and crumpling up the paper tossed it into the open grate just as [[griselda-clement|Griselda]] entered the room.

[024-084]“What’s that you’re throwing down so contemptuously?” she asked.

[024-085]“Filth,” I said.

[024-086]Taking a match from my pocket, I struck it and bent down. Griselda, however, was too quick for me. She had stooped down and caught up the crumpled ball of paper and smoothed it out before I could stop her.

[024-087]She read it, gave a little exclamation of disgust, and tossed it back to me, turning away as she did so. I lighted it and watched it burn.

[024-088]Griselda had moved away. She was standing by the window looking out into the garden.

[024-089]“Len,” she said, without turning round.

[024-090]“Yes, my dear.”

[024-091]“I’d like to tell you something. Yes, don’t stop me. I want to, please. When—when Lawrence Redding came here, I let you think that I had only known him slightly before. That wasn’t true. I—had known him rather well. In fact, before I met you, I had been rather in love with him. I think most people are with Lawrence. I was—well, absolutely silly about him at one time. I don’t mean I wrote him compromising letters or anything idiotic like they do in books. But I was rather keen on him once.”

[024-092]“Why didn’t you tell me?” I asked.

[024-093]“Oh! because! I don’t know exactly except that—well, you’re foolish in some ways. Just because you’re so much older than I am, you think that I—well, that I’m likely to like other people. I thought you’d be tiresome, perhaps, about me and Lawrence being friends.”

[024-094]“You’re very clever at concealing things,” I said, remembering what she had told me in that room less than a week ago, and the ingenuous way she had talked.

[024-095]“Yes, I’ve always been able to hide things. In a way, I like doing it.”

[024-096]Her voice held a childlike ring of pleasure to it.

[024-097]“But it’s quite true what I said. I didn’t know about [[anne-protheroe|Anne]], and I wondered why Lawrence was so different, not—well, really not noticing me. I’m not used to it.”

[024-098]There was a pause.

[024-099]“You do understand, Len?” said Griselda anxiously.

[024-100]“Yes,” I said, “I understand.”

[024-101]But did I?
