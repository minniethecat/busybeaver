# The Barrow

The barrow is an archaeological excavation site on Colonel Protheroe's land, conducted by Dr. Stone, a visiting archaeologist. It serves as a running subplot throughout *The Murder at the Vicarage*, providing cover for suspicious activities, a platform for minor characters, and ultimately one of the novel's many red herrings.

## Definition

A barrow is an ancient burial mound, typically from the Neolithic or Bronze Age. Dr. Stone has obtained permission to excavate a barrow on Protheroe's property, and his assistant Miss Cram, a "healthy young woman of twenty-five, noisy in manner" (002-037), accompanies him. The excavation provides a pretext for characters to be in certain places at certain times — and for the reader to wonder whether archaeology is all that Dr. Stone is digging for.

## Context in the Novel

The barrow subplot runs parallel to the murder investigation. Dennis, the vicar's nephew, dismissively remarks "I can't think what people want to grub about in barrows for, anyway" (004-042), voicing the village's bemused attitude toward the academic enterprise. Dr. Stone is frequently "at the barrow" (010-032) when investigators want to question him, and Miss Cram claims to have been at the barrow during the critical time window of the murder — an alibi that cannot be confirmed or disproven (026-085).

## Examples from the Text

### The Quarrel

Griselda mentions that Protheroe and Dr. Stone "quarrelled about the barrow" (004-041), a detail that briefly casts suspicion on the archaeologist. The nature of the quarrel is never fully explained, adding to the general atmosphere of hidden tensions in St. Mary Mead.

### Miss Cram's Alibi

Miss Cram's claim to have been at the barrow at the time of the murder is a classic Christie touch: the alibi is perfectly plausible for an archaeologist's assistant, yet completely unverifiable. The barrow, isolated on Protheroe's land, has no other witnesses — making it the perfect place for someone to claim to have been, and equally the perfect place for someone to have been doing something other than archaeology.

## Significance

The barrow functions as a minor but effective red herring. It introduces Dr. Stone and Miss Cram as plausible suspects — the outsider with a mysterious purpose, the assistant with the unconfirmed alibi — and keeps them in the reader's mind throughout the investigation. It also provides comic relief through the contrast between academic archaeology and village life, and reinforces the novel's theme that St. Mary Mead, for all its apparent dullness, harbors layers of hidden activity.
