# Mrs. Price Ridley

Mrs. Price Ridley is a member of Mr. Clement's congregation and a quintessential St. Mary Mead resident — devout, inquisitive, and incapable of distinguishing between moral concern and idle gossip. Her "wretched pound note" (001-021) provides the catalyst for the conversation that opens the novel, establishing the atmosphere of village surveillance in which the murder will occur.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Price Ridley embodies the social fabric of St. Mary Mead — its prying curiosity, its moral certainties, and its relentless surveillance of everyone's business. She appears primarily in group scenes at the vicarage or church, where her comments reveal the village's collective judgments about its members. Her role is atmospheric rather than investigatory: she represents the audience to which the crime is played and the social world that must be restored to order by its resolution.

## Background

Mrs. Price Ridley is "a devout member of my congregation" (001-022). Her piety does not prevent her from being one of the village's most energetic gossips. The incident of her lost pound note — which she suspects was stolen — triggers a heated discussion at the vicarage about crime, detection, and human nature, setting the thematic stage for the murder that follows.

## Key Actions

### The Pound Note Incident

The "whole trouble arose out of Mrs. Price Ridley's wretched pound note" (001-021). Her accusation that someone stole the note from the collection plate prompts a discussion about crime and detection that introduces the novel's central themes and, in retrospect, anticipates the murder.

### The Vicarage Gathering

At a vicarage gathering, Mrs. Price Ridley participates in the village's collective dissection of local scandals. She comments on Anne Protheroe's choice of hotel — "at the same hotel?" (002-059) — and on Lawrence Redding's behaviour with young women: "girls are so sly" (002-084). Her observations are invariably uncharitable and occasionally accurate.

### The Bathing Dress Comment

Mrs. Price Ridley's comment that Lettice Protheroe wears "her bathing dress" in a manner that is "not quite nice" (002-087) exemplifies her role as guardian of village propriety — a role that makes her both comic and, in the context of a murder investigation, potentially dangerous.

## Relationships

- **Mr. Clement**: Her vicar. Mrs. Price Ridley treats him with the deference due his office but does not hesitate to share her opinions about his parishioners.

- **Miss Wetherby**: A fellow parishioner and gossip. The two often appear together in scenes of village commentary.

- **Miss Hartnell**: Another member of the village gossip network, completing the triad of St. Mary Mead's moral observers.

- **Miss Marple**: Though both are elderly St. Mary Mead residents, their relationship is one of contrast — Miss Marple observes to understand; Mrs. Price Ridley observes to judge.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Price Ridley is a minor character who serves a major thematic function. She represents the village's collective eye — the surveillance that makes St. Mary Mead both a community and a prison. Her gossip is the informal counterpart to the police investigation: both seek to know, but where Slack and Melchett seek facts, Mrs. Price Ridley seeks moral certainties. Her presence reminds the reader that the murder does not occur in isolation but within a dense network of watching eyes and judging voices.

## Key Quotes

> "Girls are so sly." (002-084)

> "At the same hotel?" said Mrs. Price Ridley in a severe voice. (002-059)

## Appearances

Mrs. Price Ridley appears in the early chapters at the vicarage, in church-related scenes, and wherever St. Mary Mead residents gather to discuss the crime.
