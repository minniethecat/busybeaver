# Affair

The extramarital relationship between Anne Protheroe and Lawrence Redding — an open secret in St. Mary Mead that becomes the central scandal and the most apparent motive when Colonel Protheroe is found murdered in the vicar's study.

## Definition

An "affair" in the context of the novel is a romantic relationship outside marriage, conducted in defiance of social convention and village propriety. In St. Mary Mead, where "everyone knows your most intimate affairs," such a relationship is both impossible to hide and impossible to ignore. [004-025]

## Context in the Novel

The affair is established early and colors every subsequent development. Griselda jokes prophetically: "I'll have an affair with the artist," to which the vicar responds with characteristic discomfort. [001-061] The village consensus, articulated through gossip, holds that "Anne will have an affair with someone soon. Anne hates being bossed about." [002-028] When Anne herself confronts the vicar — "Do you really suspect me of having an affair with Lawrence Redding?" [003-003] — the reader understands that the question itself confirms the relationship. The affair provides what Inspector Slack considers the obvious solution: the jealous husband confronts the lover, and murder results.

## Examples from the Text

### Griselda's Prophecy

Griselda, with her characteristic blend of frivolity and insight, declares to her husband: "I'll have an affair with the artist. I will — really." [001-061] The vicar, a man of the cloth, finds the jest uncomfortable. But Griselda's joke proves prescient — though it is Anne Protheroe, not Griselda, who becomes involved with Lawrence Redding, the artist.

### Anne's Confrontation

When Anne Protheroe visits the vicarage and asks the vicar directly whether he suspects her of an affair, the question hangs heavily in the air. [003-003] She does not deny the relationship; instead, she seeks to understand how it is perceived. Her honesty about her feelings for Lawrence — "We love each other" [003-072] — recasts the affair from sordid scandal to tragic inevitability.

### The Village as Confidant

Miss Marple observes that in St. Mary Mead, privacy is an illusion. The affair is known, discussed, and interpreted by every resident. This collective awareness transforms the private relationship into public evidence, making Anne and Lawrence the obvious suspects before any investigation begins.

## Significance

The affair is the engine of the plot. Without it, there is no obvious motive, no false confession, and no misdirection for the real murderer to hide behind. Yet the novel's resolution complicates the simple narrative of jealous husband and guilty lovers. The affair is real, the emotions genuine — but they are not the cause of the murder. The affair thus serves as both a genuine emotional drama and a narrative decoy, demonstrating how the most obvious explanation can also be the most misleading.
