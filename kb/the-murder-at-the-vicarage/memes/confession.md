# Confession

A confession is a formal admission of guilt for a crime. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, confessions are central to the plot: Lawrence Redding confesses immediately, yet the truth behind his confession — and a second, more troubled confession — drives the novel's resolution.

## Definition

In criminal law and detective fiction, a confession is a statement by a suspect acknowledging responsibility for a crime. The novel explores two kinds of confession: the dramatic, public confession of Lawrence Redding, and the tortured, psychological confession of the curate Hawes. Each raises the question: is a confession always the truth?

## Context in the Novel

Lawrence Redding's confession is the novel's first major plot turn. He walks into the police station, throws down the pistol, and says "Here I am. I did it" (006-038). The confession seems to close the case immediately, but the vicar and others sense something wrong — the confession is too neat, too theatrical, and the clock's manipulation doesn't fit Redding's story.

A second confession emerges late in the investigation: the curate Hawes, found with an empty cachet box and a crumpled anonymous letter, is described as having made a "confession" (028-025). But Miss Marple sees through this too: Hawes's confession, she realizes, is not an admission of murder but an attempt to protect someone else — "that's what he wants you to think! That you know the truth — and that it's best for everyone as it is" (029-076).

## Examples from the Text

### Redding's False Confession

Redding confesses to protect Anne Protheroe, whom he loves. His confession is self-sacrificing but false — and the investigation gradually picks it apart. The manipulated clock, the telephone call, and the silencer theory all suggest a different killer.

### Hawes's Troubled Confession

Hawes's confession is more ambiguous. The vicar confronts him with the anonymous letter evidence, and Hawes, already in a fragile mental state, takes an overdose. His "confession" is entangled with guilt, fear, and a misplaced sense of duty — complicating the question of whether he is confessing to murder or to something else entirely.

## Significance

The dual confessions in the novel subvert the detective genre's conventional treatment of confession as resolution. Instead of ending the mystery, each confession deepens it. Christie uses false and ambiguous confessions to show that truth is not simply a matter of someone admitting guilt — it requires the careful sifting of evidence, motive, and character that only Miss Marple can perform.
