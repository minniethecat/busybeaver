# Pistol

The murder weapon in *The Murder at the Vicarage* is a Mauser .25 pistol, owned by Colonel Protheroe and used to shoot him in the vicarage study. The pistol is central to the investigation — its ownership, handling, and the circumstances of its discharge determine the course of the entire case.

## Definition

A Mauser .25 is a small-calibre semi-automatic pistol. Dr. Haydock, examining the wound, confirms "in all probability the bullet was fired from a pistol of small calibre — say a Mauser .25" (005-059). The weapon's characteristics — its noise, its ownership trail, and who had access to it — become critical evidence points.

## Context in the Novel

The pistol first enters the narrative well before the murder. At a tennis party, Lawrence Redding casually mentions "I've got a Mauser pistol" (004-037) — a detail that gains sinister significance after Protheroe's death. When Redding confesses, he walks into the police station and "throws down the pistol on the table" (007-008), creating an apparently straightforward case.

But the pistol's provenance is more complex. Anne Protheroe reveals she obtained it: "It was my husband's. I got it out of the drawer of his dressing-table" (008-103). This revelation — that Colonel Protheroe was killed with his own weapon, retrieved by his wife — complicates the question of premeditation and access.

## Examples from the Text

### The Pistol as Confession

Lawrence Redding's dramatic confession, throwing the pistol on the police station table with the words "Here I am. I did it. Just like that" (006-038), seems to close the case. But the vicar and others are uneasy — Redding's confession feels almost too theatrical, and the circumstances don't fully explain the murder.

### The Silencer Question

A late, crucial detail emerges: the possibility that a silencer was used. The vicar muses: "There is, I believe, an invention called a Maxim silencer. So I gather from detective stories. I wonder if, possibly, the sneeze that the maid, Clara, heard might have actually been the shot?" (030-042). A silenced pistol shot mistaken for a sneeze would explain why no gunshot was heard in the vicarage — and would point to a different kind of murderer: one who came prepared.

## Significance

The pistol functions as both the literal instrument of death and a narrative device for misdirection. Redding's possession of the weapon points to him as the obvious killer, but the silencer theory — and the discovery that a second person handled the pistol — redirects suspicion. The fact that it was Protheroe's own weapon, taken from his own dressing-table, reinforces the novel's theme that danger comes from within the familiar — from one's own household, one's own village, one's own study.
