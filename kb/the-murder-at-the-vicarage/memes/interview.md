# Interview

The police interview is the procedural backbone of the murder investigation in *The Murder at the Vicarage*. Inspector Slack's methodical round of interviews — with the vicar, the Protheroe household, Mrs. Lestrange, the parlourmaid Mary, and others — becomes the narrative engine through which alibis are tested, contradictions exposed, and the truth slowly pieced together. Each interview is a miniature drama of concealment and revelation.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, an interview is a formal, recorded conversation conducted by the police (Inspector Slack or Colonel Melchett) with anyone who may possess information about Colonel Protheroe's murder. These interviews serve a dual purpose: gathering factual evidence about times, places, and actions; and applying psychological pressure to force witnesses into admissions or contradictions. The vicar himself is interviewed early on, and he records that Slack "wanted to know the details of my interview with Protheroe" from the morning of the murder [PN=002-041].

## Context in the Novel

The investigation is structured around a series of interviews that Slack schedules with methodical precision. After the initial examination of the scene, Slack announces his plan: "I shall return on the morrow for a fuller interview" [PN=006-014]. The interviews then ripple outward from the vicarage — Slack interviews at Old Hall, then seeks "an interview with Mrs. Lestrange" whose behaviour he finds "queer" [PN=013-102], then arranges "a short interview with Mrs. Protheroe" [PN=016-027], and even interviews the chauffeur Manning with the vicar present [PN=016-032]. The parlourmaid Mary has her own "interview with the police" [PN=013-104], and Slack also conducts "an interview with the parlourmaid" at Dr. Stone's [PN=019-035]. This systematic pattern of questioning transforms the village into a network of informants and suspects.

## Examples from the Text

### The Crucial Mrs. Lestrange Interview

The most significant interview in the investigation concerns Mrs. Lestrange, whose mysterious behaviour draws Slack's attention. The police learn that "Mrs. Lestrange had had an interview with Colonel Protheroe the night before his death" [PN=015-058], a meeting she is desperate to keep secret. When pressed, she insists that "nothing which was said at that interview could possibly have any bearing upon the crime" [PN=015-117], but Slack is unconvinced. The vicar too puzzles over it: "I wonder very much what her interview with Colonel Protheroe was about" [PN=019-068]. The eventual revelation — that Mrs. Lestrange is the colonel's first wife, and that their interview concerned their estranged daughter — transforms the investigation's understanding of motive and identity [PN=019-111].

### The Vicar as Amateur Interviewer

The vicar conducts his own parallel investigation through informal interviews — conversations that are less official than Slack's but often more revealing. He "recited my interviews with Miss Hartnell and Miss Wetherby" to Miss Marple [PN=025-155], sharing what he has learned from the village women. These amateur interviews, conducted over tea rather than in the police station, uncover the social truths that the official investigation misses. The contrast between professional and amateur interviewing mirrors the novel's broader theme: that wisdom often resides in unexpected places.

## Significance

The interview is the mechanism through which the novel dramatises its central mystery: the gap between what people say and what they know. Each interview is a performance — the witness chooses what to reveal and what to conceal — and Slack's skill lies in detecting the discrepancies. But the novel also suggests that formal interviews have limits. The most important truths emerge not in the police station but in the vicarage drawing room, where Miss Marple's quiet questions elicit confidences that official procedure cannot reach. The interview, in Christie's hands, becomes a study in the art of listening and the difficulty of knowing another person's mind.
