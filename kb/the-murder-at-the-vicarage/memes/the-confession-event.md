# Lawrence Redding's Confession

Lawrence Redding's confession is one of the most dramatic moments in *The Murder at the Vicarage*. Walking into the Much Benham police station the night after the murder, throwing down his pistol, and declaring "I did it," Redding seems to close the case before it has properly begun. But his confession is false — an act of love designed to protect Anne Protheroe.

## Timeline

The confession occurs on the night of the murder, "about ten o'clock" (007-008), hours after Protheroe's body has been discovered. Redding has had time to consider his situation and has chosen to take the blame.

## Participants

- **Lawrence Redding**: The confessor, making a false admission of guilt to protect his lover
- **Inspector Slack**: The receiving officer, who accepts the confession at face value
- **Police Officers**: The witnesses to the confession

## Narrative

Redding enters the police station, throws down the Mauser pistol on the table, and says: "Here I am. I did it" (006-038). The confession is theatrical, decisive, and apparently conclusive. Inspector Slack, always inclined to accept the obvious explanation, treats the case as solved. But the vicar is uneasy — the confession does not account for the manipulated clock, and Redding's motive, while emotionally plausible (love for Anne), does not fully explain the circumstances of the shooting.

## Significance to the Investigation

Redding's confession shapes the entire early investigation. It makes him the primary suspect, diverts attention from the real killer, and creates the central dramatic irony of the novel's middle section: the reader knows Redding has confessed, but the vicar (and eventually the reader) suspects he is innocent. The confession is the novel's first major demonstration that the obvious answer is wrong — a lesson that Slack never learns but that Miss Marple understands instinctively.
