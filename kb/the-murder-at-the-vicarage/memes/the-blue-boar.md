# The Blue Boar

The Blue Boar is the inn of St. Mary Mead, serving as the village's public house and informal meeting place. In the context of the investigation, it provides accommodation for visitors and serves as a node in the village's information network.

## Location

The Blue Boar is centrally located in St. Mary Mead, accessible to villagers and visitors alike. As the only inn in the village, it is the natural lodging place for anyone without local connections — a fact that makes it relevant to the investigation's tracking of strangers' movements.

## Description

The inn is a typical English village public house, providing drink, food, and lodging. It is mentioned primarily in connection with visitors to the village and as a place where information circulates informally. Inspector Slack and his men likely use it as a base during the investigation, and village gossip flows freely across its bar.

## Story Significance

The Blue Boar represents the public, communal aspect of village life — the counterpart to the private dramas unfolding at the vicarage and Old Hall. It is where outsiders are observed, where rumors are exchanged, and where the village's collective judgment of the murder takes shape. In a novel where so much happens behind closed doors, the Blue Boar is one of the few genuinely public spaces.

## Key Scenes

- **Visitors' Lodgings**: Any stranger arriving in St. Mary Mead — including, potentially, associates of Mrs. Lestrange or Dr. Stone — would likely stay at the Blue Boar
- **Informal Investigation**: The inn serves as a venue for the informal information-gathering that supplements the official police inquiry

## Residents / Frequent Visitors

No permanent residents beyond the innkeeper and staff. Frequent visitors include most male villagers and the police officers investigating the murder.
