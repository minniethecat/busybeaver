# Alibi

An alibi is a claim that a person was elsewhere when a crime was committed. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, the battle over alibis — who has one, who lacks one, and whether the clock was manipulated to create a false one — forms the core of the investigation.

## Definition

In detective fiction and criminal law, an alibi (Latin for "elsewhere") is evidence that the accused was in a different place at the time the crime was committed, making their involvement impossible. In the novel, alibis are not simply statements of whereabouts but are woven into the central puzzle of *when* the murder actually occurred (011-084).

## Context in the Novel

The alibi question becomes critical because of the manipulated clock. Colonel Protheroe was believed to have been killed at 6:20 PM, but the vicar realizes that the murderer altered the clock to create a false timeframe. Lawrence Redding's action of returning the clock to 6:20 was an attempt to create "an alibi for himself" (006-053). Inspector Slack is drawn into this temporal trap, and the investigation revolves around finding who had an alibi for 6:20 — the fake time — but *not* for the real time of the murder (011-084).

## Examples from the Text

### The Clock as Alibi Mechanism

The vicar deduces that the murderer set the clock to 6:22 and wrote "6:20" on the note found with the body. This gave the murderer a perfect alibi for the perceived time of death, while leaving them free during the actual time window (011-083). The telephone call on the day of the murder is also understood as "a kind of alibi," designed to anchor events to a specific time and deceive investigators (017-057).

### Cast-Iron Alibis and Their Limits

Inspector Slack dismisses a suspect named Archer because "he's got an alibi" that is "awkward to get over" (025-027). But as the narrator's notes reveal, only two people lack an alibi for the critical window of 6:30–6:35: Miss Cram and Mrs. Lestrange (026-085). Yet even Mrs. Lestrange's lack of alibi proves deceptive — her true reason for being in St. Mary Mead had nothing to do with the murder.

## Significance

The alibi puzzle is the structural backbone of the novel's mystery. The false time-of-death created by the manipulated clock means that every character's alibi must be evaluated against *two* timeframes. This temporal misdirection is what ultimately allows the real murderer to escape early suspicion, and it is Miss Marple — not the police — who first grasps the full implications of the clock's significance. The novel thus demonstrates that alibis are not merely factual statements but constructs that can be manufactured, dismantled, and reinterpreted.
