# The Will

A last will and testament is a legal document that directs the disposition of a person's property after death. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, Colonel Protheroe's will is a significant but understated element of the case — a potential source of motive that, like so much else in the novel, turns out to be less important than it first appears.

## Definition

In English law, a will determines how a deceased person's estate is distributed. Changes to a will — particularly those made shortly before death — can provide powerful motives for murder. The fact that Protheroe's death "will make no difference" to Dr. Stone's archaeological arrangements (018-048) is a rare explicit mention, but the will's implications run throughout the investigation.

## Context in the Novel

The will is one of the standard motives in detective fiction: who stands to gain financially from the death? Anne Protheroe, as the wife, is the obvious beneficiary, which adds weight to the suspicion against her and Lawrence Redding. The possibility that Protheroe might have changed his will — perhaps to disinherit Anne upon discovering her affair, or to provide for his daughter Lettice, or to leave money to someone with a hold over him — hovers in the background of the investigation.

Yet Christie, characteristically, subverts the expectation. The financial motive, while present, is not the key to the crime. The real murderer did not kill for money, and the will — unlike in many detective stories — does not provide the solution.

## Examples from the Text

### A Subdued Motive

Unlike many detective novels where the will is read aloud in a dramatic scene, *The Murder at the Vicarage* treats Protheroe's estate with restraint. The financial implications of his death are real but understated — Anne Protheroe does benefit, but her motive is presented as love rather than greed, and the real killer's motive has nothing to do with inheritance.

### The Barrow and Business

Dr. Stone's casual remark that Protheroe's death "will make no difference to our arrangements" (018-048) is a telling detail. It tells us that Stone had business dealings with Protheroe, that these dealings involved the barrow excavation, and that Stone is practical enough to think about the implications of the murder for his own work — but not guilty enough to panic.

## Significance

The will's subdued role in the novel reflects Christie's mature approach to the detective genre. The financial motive, a staple of crime fiction, is present but not determinative. The real crime springs from more complex human emotions — love, duty, fear, shame — rather than from simple greed. In this, the novel anticipates the psychological sophistication of Christie's later works.
