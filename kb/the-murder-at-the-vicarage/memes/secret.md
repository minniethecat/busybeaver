# Secret

A secret is information deliberately withheld from others. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, nearly every character harbors a secret — from clandestine love affairs to hidden identities — and the investigation is, at its core, the progressive uncovering of these concealed truths.

## Definition

Secrets in the novel range from benign concealments to deadly deceptions. The vicar tries "hard to visualize Colonel Protheroe in the role of secret sinner and failed" (011-048), yet secrets prove to be everywhere in St. Mary Mead. The novel's detective work is essentially the methodical exposure of what each character is hiding.

## Context in the Novel

The plot is structured around layers of secrets being peeled away. Lawrence Redding and Anne Protheroe conduct a secret affair that becomes impossible to hide after the murder. Dr. Stone is discovered to be searching the barrow not for archaeology but for a hidden object. Mrs. Lestrange arrives in the village under an assumed name, concealing her true identity. Even the vicar's wife, Griselda, keeps secrets — and the anonymous letter accusing her of being seen at Redding's cottage "by the back window at twenty past six" (030-031) threatens to expose one.

The most dangerous secret, of course, is the murderer's identity — concealed behind a manipulated clock, a forged note, and a carefully constructed alibi.

## Examples from the Text

### The Secret Affair

Lawrence Redding and Anne Protheroe's relationship is the first and most visible secret uncovered. When the vicar stumbles upon them in the studio, Redding says: "You've surprised our secret, sir. What are you going to do about it?" (004-013). This secret, once exposed, catalyzes all the events that follow.

### Mrs. Lestrange's Identity

The most carefully guarded secret belongs to Mrs. Lestrange, whose true identity — as the mother of the real murderer — is revealed only in the final chapter. Her secret is not criminal but protective: she came to St. Mary Mead to see her dying child, not to blackmail anyone.

## Significance

Secrets in the novel function as both plot mechanism and thematic device. They create suspense, redirect suspicion, and ultimately reveal character. Christie's genius is in making the most seemingly sinister secrets — Mrs. Lestrange's assumed identity, the vicar's wife's visit to Redding's cottage — turn out to be benign, while the most dangerous secret is hidden in plain sight: the identity of a killer who looks like everyone else.
