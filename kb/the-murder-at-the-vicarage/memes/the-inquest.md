# The Inquest

The coroner's inquest is the formal legal inquiry into [[Colonel Protheroe]]'s [[the-murder|death]]. Conducted shortly after the murder at Much Benham, the nearest town with appropriate facilities, it serves as the procedural bridge between [[Inspector Slack]]'s police investigation and the formal trial — gathering sworn testimony, establishing the medical cause of death, and creating the official record on which subsequent legal proceedings will depend.

## Timeline

The inquest takes place in the days following the murder, once the [[police]] have gathered sufficient evidence to present to the coroner. Its timing is shaped by the pace of the investigation: Slack must have enough to present a coherent case, but the inquest cannot be delayed indefinitely without public scrutiny.

## Narrative

The inquest is one of the novel's set-piece scenes — a formal gathering in which the scattered facts of the investigation are assembled into a coherent narrative for the first time. For the villagers of [[St. Mary Mead]], the inquest is both a civic duty and a social occasion: the public airing of private secrets that [[gossip]] has already circulated in less official forms.

The inquest's most dramatic moment comes with Lawrence Redding's testimony. His account — truthful in its details, misleading in its implications — shapes the official understanding of the case for much of the novel. Only [[Miss Marple]], observing from the margins, recognizes what the coroner and [[police]] do not: that Redding's [[truth|truthfulness]] is partial, and that the full truth lies elsewhere.

## Significance

The inquest is more than a procedural formality. In Christie's narrative architecture, it serves three functions:

1. **Information consolidation**: The scattered clues and witness statements of the early investigation are assembled into a coherent — if incomplete — picture
2. **Public reckoning**: The village's private knowledge of the Protheroe household's troubles becomes a matter of official record
3. **Misdirection**: The inquest's formal conclusions point the investigation in the wrong direction, creating the puzzle that only [[Miss Marple]] can solve

## Outcome

The coroner returns a finding of unlawful killing and refers the case for trial. But the inquest's real outcome — unintended by the coroner — is to cement the official theory of the case that [[Miss Marple]] will ultimately overturn.
