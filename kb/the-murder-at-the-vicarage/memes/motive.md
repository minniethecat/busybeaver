# Motive

A motive is the psychological reason that drives a person to commit a crime. In *The Murder at the Vicarage*, the search for a sufficiently strong [[motive]] to kill [[Colonel Protheroe]] — a man widely disliked but seemingly not hated enough to be murdered — drives much of the early investigation. The novel's distinctive contribution to the concept is its demonstration that the strongest motives are often the least visible: [[gossip|spite]], [[secret|secrecy]], and the desperate desire to escape an intolerable situation.

## Definition

In criminal investigation, motive refers to the internal reason or incentive that leads a person to act. Unlike evidence of means or opportunity, motive addresses *why* someone would commit a crime. As [[Mr. Clement]] observes, "for murder — one would need a very strong motive" — acknowledging that the threshold for homicide is higher than for lesser offenses.

## Context in the Novel

The question of motive surfaces immediately after [[the-murder]]. Griselda voices the community's bewilderment: "there's not a shadow of a motive" for [[Lawrence Redding]] to have killed Protheroe. The answer, when it emerges, is complicated: Redding's motive was love for [[Anne Protheroe]], and the pair had decided "there was only one way out of the tangle."

But the novel complicates the concept of motive by showing that nearly everyone in [[St. Mary Mead]] had *some* reason to wish Protheroe dead:

| Suspect | Apparent Motive | Strength | Reality |
|---------|----------------|----------|---------|
| [[Lawrence Redding]] | Love for Anne Protheroe | Strong | Real — but his confession is strategically incomplete |
| [[Anne Protheroe]] | Escape from a loveless marriage | Strong | Real — she is the co-conspirator |
| [[Mr. Clement]] | Quarrel over parish accounts | Weak | Specious — the quarrel is genuine but not murderous |
| [[Lettice Protheroe]] | Resentment of her father, hatred of Anne | Moderate | Real as emotion, but not acted upon |
| [[Dr. Stone]] | Unknown — his archaeological activities are suspicious | Uncertain | Red herring |
| [[Mrs. Lestrange]] | Mysterious past connection to Protheroe | Uncertain | Red herring |

[[Miss Marple]] expands the investigation beyond obvious motives like greed or passion. Her story of "the laundry business" — a woman who hid a stolen pin to frame another "not because she wanted the pin, but because she wanted to get the other woman into trouble" (011-049) — demonstrates that [[gossip|spite]] can be as powerful a motive as love or money. For Miss Marple, the human capacity for malice is itself a sufficient explanation.

## Examples from the Text

| Example | Type of Motive | How Discovered |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Redding's love for Anne | Romantic passion | Obvious — Redding confesses his feelings |
| Anne's desire to escape | Self-preservation | Discovered through village observation of her unhappiness |
| The parish dispute | Institutional conflict | Clement's own testimony about his quarrel with Protheroe |
| "The laundry business" (011-049) | Spite/malice | Miss Marple's village parallel — pure spite as motive |
| The pistol in the dressing-table | Domestic desperation | The weapon's location reveals the intimate nature of the threat |

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[Truth]]**: Motive and truth are interdependent — you cannot know who killed Protheroe without understanding why
- **[[Confession]]**: Redding's confession provides a motive (love) that is true but incomplete
- **[[Gossip]]**: Village gossip speculates endlessly about motive; Miss Marple sorts the plausible from the fantastic
- **[[Justice]]**: Legal justice requires establishing motive beyond reasonable doubt
