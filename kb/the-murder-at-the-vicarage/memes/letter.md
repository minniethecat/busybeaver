# Letter

Written correspondence serving as a central plot mechanism in the investigation of Colonel Protheroe's murder — from the anonymous accusatory letter to the forged unfinished letter that frames the crime scene.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, a "letter" is written communication that functions as evidence, accusation, and manipulation. Letters in St. Mary Mead are not merely personal correspondence; they are instruments of intrigue that drive the investigation forward and reveal the hidden dynamics of village life. [028-013]

## Context in the Novel

Letters appear at multiple critical junctures in the story. The most significant is the anonymous letter sent to Colonel Protheroe, which precipitates events and becomes a key piece of evidence. The vicarage setting — with its letter box and postal routines — provides the mechanism through which these written communications enter the narrative. [025-064] Inspector Slack and Colonel Melchett treat letters as official evidence, while the villagers understand them as weapons in the subtle warfare of village gossip. [024-080]

## Examples from the Text

### The Anonymous Letter

An anonymous letter is received at the vicarage, accusing Colonel Protheroe. Miss Marple observes that St. Mary Mead is "troubled with very few anonymous letters," noting that "an anonymous letter is, I think, rather a cowardly thing." [024-080] The letter's existence reveals the undercurrents of resentment and malice beneath the village's placid surface. The vicar later discovers another anonymous communication, this time in the letter box, written in "the same handwriting as the anonymous letter that had come that afternoon." [028-013]

### The Forged Unfinished Letter

Colonel Protheroe is found dead beside an unfinished letter, dated 6:20. This letter becomes a cornerstone of the investigation — the time on it establishes an apparent timeline of the murder. [018-013] However, the letter is eventually exposed as a forgery: "That letter was never written by Protheroe." [027-028] The ink differs, and "Protheroe never wrote that letter." [027-030] The forger cleverly manipulated both the clock and the letter's timestamp to construct a false alibi. [011-083]

### Letters as Police Evidence

The police receive "numerous letters from people accusing themselves of the crime," a phenomenon Inspector Slack notes with professional weariness. [010-046] Anne Protheroe's letter to the vicar — handed to Inspector Slack — becomes official evidence, read and "pursed up his lips" over. [008-041]

## Significance

Letters in the novel serve a dual function: they are both clues for the detective and tools for the criminal. The forged letter demonstrates the murderer's calculated cunning, while the anonymous letters expose the village's capacity for quiet malice. The epistolary evidence drives the plot from suspicion to resolution, embodying the novel's central theme that appearances — like handwriting — can be deceptive. [029-004]
