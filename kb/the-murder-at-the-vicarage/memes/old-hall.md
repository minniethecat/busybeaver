# Old Hall

Old Hall is the residence of the Protheroe family in [[St. Mary Mead]]. More than a house, it is the physical expression of [[Colonel Protheroe]]'s social position — prominent but joyless, imposing but hollow — and the site of the domestic unhappiness that provides the novel's most visible [[motive]] for [[the-murder|murder]].

## Location

Old Hall is situated on the outskirts of [[St. Mary Mead]], connected to [[the-vicarage|the Vicarage]] by a wooded footpath that emerges at the vicarage garden gate (002-005). This path — traversed by nearly every character in the novel — makes Old Hall physically proximate to the crime scene while socially distant from the village's daily life. The grounds include the archaeological barrow that [[Dr. Stone]] is excavating, adding a layer of scholarly activity that proves relevant to the investigation.

## Description

The house was formerly owned by an Anglo-Indian colonel — a detail that resonates with Protheroe's own military background — and Clement notes his relief at the removal of "the brass tables and Burmese idols" when Anne Protheroe redecorated (003-031). Under Anne's influence, the house is "furnished now very simply, but in exquisite taste. There was a sense of harmony and rest about it" (003-031) — an aesthetic achievement that only underscores the emotional discord within.

The house contains the locked drawer from which [[Anne Protheroe]] retrieves her husband's pistol — an intimate detail that collapses the distance between domestic routine and lethal violence. That a murder weapon should come from a dressing-table drawer, kept among personal effects, makes the crime feel less like an external intrusion than a family implosion.

## Story Significance

Old Hall is the novel's primary site of domestic tension. Within its walls, three incompatible lives intersect: the Colonel's cold authoritarianism, Anne's desperate unhappiness, and [[Lettice Protheroe]]'s simmering resentment of her stepmother. The house's size — large enough to accommodate them all — only emphasizes the emotional distances between its inhabitants.

The house is also economically significant. Griselda notes "some marvellous old silver at Old Hall. Trencher salts and a Charles II Tazza — all kinds of things like that. Worth thousands of pounds" (004-034). Though [[motive|financial gain]] is not the primary driver of the crime, the Protheroe family's wealth adds a dimension to the investigation.
