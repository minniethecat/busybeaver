# Blackmail

Blackmail is a central investigative theory in *The Murder at the Vicarage*, proposed by Inspector Slack as the motive behind Colonel Protheroe's murder, and the reason for Mrs. Lestrange's suspicious presence in St. Mary Mead.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, blackmail is the act of threatening to expose a person's guilty secret or disgraceful past in exchange for money or compliance. Inspector Slack develops his theory that Colonel Protheroe was being blackmailed — and that the blackmailer, cornered by changed laws, resorted to murder (017-047).

## Context in the Novel

The blackmail theory emerges in the early aftermath of the murder investigation. Inspector Slack, the police detective assigned to the case, becomes suspicious of Mrs. Lestrange — a smart, well-dressed stranger who arrived in St. Mary Mead shortly before the murder. Slack notes that she visited Colonel Protheroe at an odd time of day and deliberately avoided Mrs. and Miss Protheroe (016-018). He constructs a theory in which Mrs. Lestrange is the blackmailer: she had successfully blackmailed Protheroe years ago, and when she tried again, he threatened legal action — leaving murder as her only escape (017-047).

The vicar, Mr. Clement, the novel's narrator, consistently rejects this theory. He finds Mrs. Lestrange to be "a lady" incapable of blackmail, though curiously he admits he could more easily believe her guilty of murder (017-054). By the end of the investigation, Clement's own notes dismiss the blackmail theory outright: the inspector's blackmail hypothesis "I cannot accept. Mrs. Lestrange is not that kind of woman" (026-085).

## Examples from the Text

### Slack's Blackmail Theory

Inspector Slack lays out his theory to the vicar with considerable confidence. He connects Mrs. Lestrange's unexplained presence in the village, her secretive visit to Protheroe, and her avoidance of Protheroe's family into a coherent narrative. Slack argues that the changed legal landscape — where blackmail prosecutions now protect victims' identities — would have put Mrs. Lestrange "in a nasty position" when Protheroe threatened to bring charges. In Slack's view, "the only thing to do to save herself is to put him out good and quick" (017-047).

### Alternative Theories

The blackmail theory is not the only investigative avenue. Miss Marple earlier remarks that a churchwarden could lead "a double life," foreshadowing Slack's suspicions (016-015). Slack himself later admits frustration with the lack of evidence: "If I could only get definite proof of blackmail — but you can't get definite proof of anything in this crime! It's theory, theory, theory" (025-036).

## Significance

The blackmail subplot serves multiple narrative functions in the novel. It provides a plausible red herring that diverts attention from the actual murderer, deepens the characterization of Mrs. Lestrange as a figure of mystery, and illustrates the gap between Inspector Slack's methodical but unimaginative police work and Miss Marple's more insightful understanding of human nature. The vicar's rejection of the blackmail theory, grounded in his reading of Mrs. Lestrange's character, reinforces the novel's theme that true understanding of crime requires understanding of people — not merely the construction of logical theories from circumstantial evidence.
