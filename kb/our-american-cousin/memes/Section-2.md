(002-001) [[lord-dundreary|Dun]] Ha! ha! ha!

(002-002) [[florence-trenchard|Flo]] What's the matter?

(002-003) Dun That wath a joke, that wath.

(002-004) Flo Where's the joke? [Dundreary screams and turns to Mrs. M.]

(002-005) [[mrs-mountchessington|Mrs M]] No.

(002-006) Dun She don't see it. Don't you see--a game of drafts--pieces of wound

(002-007) wood on square pieces of leather. That's the idea. Now, I want to put

(002-008) your brains to the test. I want to ask you a whime.

(002-009) Flo A whime, what's that?

(002-010) Dun A whime is a widdle, you know.

(002-011) Flo A widdle!

(002-012) Dun Yeth; one of those things, like--why is so and so or somebody like

(002-013) somebody else.

(002-014) Flo Oh, I see, you mean a conundrum.

(002-015) Dun Yeth, a drum, that's the idea. What is it gives a cold in the head,

(002-016) cures a cold, pays the doctor's bill and makes the home-guard look for

(002-017) substitutes? [Florence repeats it.] Yeth, do you give it up?

(002-018) Flo Yes.

(002-019) Dun Well, I'll tell you--a draught. Now I've got a better one that that:

(002-020) When is a dog's tail not a dog's tail? [Florence repeats. During this

(002-021) Florence, Mrs. M. and Dundreary are down stage.]

(002-022) Flo Yes, and willingly.

(002-023) Dun When it's a cart. [They look at him enquiringly.]

(002-024) Flo Why, what in earth has a dog's tail to do with a cart?

(002-025) Dun When it moves about, you know. A horse makes a cart move, so does a

(002-026) dog make his tail move.

(002-027) Flo Oh, I see what you mean--when it's a wagon. [Wags the letter in her

(002-028) hand.]

(002-029) Dun Well, a wagon and a cart are the same thing, ain't they! That's the

(002-030) idea--it's the same thing.

(002-031) Flo They are not the same. In the case of your conundrum there's a very

(002-032) great difference.

(002-033) Dun Now I've got another. Why does a dog waggle his tail?

(002-034) Flo Upon my word, I never inquired.

(002-035) Dun Because the tail can't waggle the dog. Ha! Ha!

(002-036) Flo Ha! ha! Is that your own, Dundreary?

(002-037) Dun Now I've got one, and this one is original.

(002-038) Flo No, no, don't spoil the last one.

(002-039) Dun Yeth; but this is extremely interesting.

(002-040) Mrs M Do you think so, Lord Dundreary?

(002-041) Dun Yeth. Miss Georgina likes me to tell her my jokes. Bye-the-bye,

(002-042) talking of that lonely sufferer, isn't she an interesting invalid? They

(002-043) do say that's what's the matter with me. I'm an interesting invalid.

(002-044) Flo Oh, that accounts for what I have heard so many young ladies

(002-045) say--Florence, dear, don't you think Lord Dundreary's extremely

(002-046) interesting? I never knew what they meant before.

(002-047) Dun Yeth, the doctor recommends me to drink donkey's milk.

(002-048) Flo [Hiding laugh.] Oh, what a clever man he must be. He knows we

(002-049) generally thrive best on our native food. [Goes up.]

(002-050) Dun [Looking first at Florence and then at Mrs M.] I'm so weak, and that

(002-051) is so strong. Yes, I'm naturally very weak, and I want strengthening.

(002-052) Yes, I guess I'll try it.

(002-053) Enter [[augusta|Augusta]]. Bus. with Dundreary, who finally exits and brings on

(002-054) Georgina, L. 1 E.

(002-055) Dun Look at this lonely sufferer. [Bringing on Georgina, seats her on

(002-056) sofa, L.] There, repothe yourself.

(002-057) Geo [Fanning herself] Thank you, my lord. Everybody is kind to me, and I

(002-058) am so delicate.

(002-059) [[augustus-trenchard|Aug]] [At table.] [[capt-de-boots|Capt. De Boots]], do help to unravel these wools for me,

(002-060) you have such an eye for color.

(002-061) Flo An eye for color! Yes, especially green.

(002-062) Dun [Screams.] Ha! ha! ha!

(002-063) All What's the matter?

(002-064) Dun Why, that wath a joke, that wath.

(002-065) Flo Where was the joke?

(002-066) Dun Especially, ha! ha!

(002-067) [[sir-edward-trenchard|Sir E]] Florence, dear, I must leave you to represent me to my guests.

(002-068) These letters will give me a great deal of business to-day.

(002-069) Flo Well, papa, remember I am your little clerk and person of all [[labor-and-worth|work]].

(002-070) Sir E No, no; this is private business--[[fortune-and-wealth|money]] matters, my love, which

(002-071) women know nothing about. [Aside.] Luckily for them, I expect Mr. [[richard-coyle|Coyle]]

(002-072) to-day.

(002-073) Flo Dear papa, how I wish you would get another agent.

(002-074) Sir E Nonsense, Florence, impossible. He knows my affairs. His father

(002-075) was agent for the late Baronet. He's one of the family, almost.

(002-076) Flo Papa, I have implicit [[religion-and-faith|faith]] in my own judgement of faces. Depend

(002-077) upon it, that man is not to be trusted.

(002-078) Sir E Florence, you are ridiculous. I could not get on a week without

(002-079) him. [Aside.] Curse him, I wish I could! [[mr-coyle|Coyle]] is a most intelligent

(002-080) agent, and a most faithful servant of the family.

(002-081) Enter [[binny|Binny]], L. 3 E.

(002-082) [[mr-binny|Binny]] Mr. Coyle and hagent with [[letters-and-documents|papers]].

(002-083) Sir E Show him into the library. I will be with him presently. [Exit

(002-084) Binny.]

(002-085) Flo Remember the archery meeting, papa. It is at three.

(002-086) Sir E Yes, yes, I'll remember. [Aside.] Pretty time for such levity when

(002-087) ruin stares me in the face. Florence, I leave you as my representative.

(002-088) [Aside.] Now to prepare myself to meet my Shylock. [Exit, R. 1 E.]

(002-089) Flo Why will papa not trust me? [Vernon comes down, R.] Oh, [[harry-vernon|Harry]]! I

(002-090) wish he would find out what a lot of pluck and common sense there is in

(002-091) this feather head of mine.

(002-092) Dun Miss Florence, will you be kind enough to tell Miss Georgina all

(002-093) about that American relative of yours.

(002-094) Flo Oh, about my [[asa-trenchard|American cousin]]; certainly. [Aside to Harry.] Let's

(002-095) have some fun. Well, he's about 17 feet high!

(002-096) Dun Good gracious! 17 feet high!

(002-097) Flo They are all 17 feet high in America, ain't they, Mr. Vernon?

(002-098) Ver Yes, that's about the average height.

(002-099) Flo And they have long black hair that reaches down to their heels; they

(002-100) have dark copper-colored skin, and they fight with--What do they fight

(002-101) with, Mr. Vernon?

(002-102) Ver Tomahawks and scalping knives.

(002-103) Flo Yes; and you'd better take care, Miss Georgina, or he'll take his

(002-104) tomahawk and scalping knife and scalp you immediately. [Georgina screams

(002-105) and faints.]

(002-106) Dun Here, somebody get something and throw over her; a pail of water;

(002-107) no, not that, she's pale enough already. [Fans her with handkerchief.]

(002-108) Georgina, don't be afraid. Dundreary's by your side, he will protect

(002-109) you.

(002-110) Flo Don't be frightened, Georgina. He will never harm you while

(002-111) Dundreary is about. Why, he could get three scalps here. [Pulls

(002-112) Dundreary's whiskers. Georgina screams.]

(002-113) Dun Don't scream, I won't lose my whiskers. I know what I'll do for my

(002-114) own safety. I will take this handkerchief and tie the roof of my head

(002-115) on. [Ties it on.]

(002-116) Flo [Pretending to cry.] Good bye, Dundreary. I'll never see you again

(002-117) in all your glory.

(002-118) Dun Don't cry, Miss Florence, I'm ready for Mr. Tommy Hawk.

(002-119) Enter Binny.

(002-120) Binny If you please, Miss, 'ere's a gent what says he's hexpected.

(002-121) Flo What's his name? Where's his card?

(002-122) Binny He didn't tell me his name, Miss, and when I haxed him for his

(002-123) card 'e said 'e had a whole pack in his valise, and if I 'ad a mine

(002-124) 'e'd play me a game of seven hup. He says he has come to stay, and he

(002-125) certainly [[beauty-and-appearance|looks]] as if he didn't mean to go.

(002-126) Flo That's him. Show him in, Mr. Binny. [Exit Binny, L. 3 E.] That's my

(002-127) American cousin, I know.

(002-128) Aug [Romantically.] Your American cousin. Oh, how delightfully romantic,

(002-129) isn't it, Capt. De Boots? [Comes down.] I can imagine the wild young

(002-130) hunter, with the free step and majestic mien of the hunter of the

(002-131) forest.

(002-132) Asa [Outside, L. 3 E.] Consarn your picture, didn't I tell you I was

(002-133) expected? You are as obstinate as Deacon Stumps' forelock, that wouldn't

(002-134) lie down and couldn't stand up. Would't pint forward and couldn't go

(002-135) backward.

(002-136) Enter Asa, L. 3 E., carrying a valise.

(002-137) Asa Where's the Squire?

(002-138) Flo Do you mean Sir Edward Trenchard, sir?

(002-139) Asa Yes.

(002-140) Flo He is not present, but I am his daughter.

(002-141) Asa Well, I guess that'll fit about as well if you tell this darned old

(002-142) shoat to take me to my room.

(002-143) Flo What does he mean by shoat?

(002-144) Binny [Taking valise.] He means me, mum; but what he wants--

(002-145) Asa Hurry up, old hoss!

(002-146) Binny He calls me a 'oss, Miss, I suppose I shall be a hox next, or

(002-147) perhaps an 'ogg.

(002-148) Asa Wal, darn me if you ain't the consarnedest old shoat I ever did see

(002-149) since I was baptized Asa Trenchard.

(002-150) Flo Ah! then it is our American cousin. Glad to see you--my brother told

(002-151) us to expect you.

(002-152) Asa Wal, yes, I guess you do b'long to my family. I'm Asa Trenchard,

(002-153) born in [[vermont|Vermont]], suckled on the banks of Muddy Creek, about the tallest

(002-154) gunner, the slickest dancer, and generally the loudest critter in the

(002-155) state. You're my cousin, be you? Wal, I ain't got no objections to kiss

(002-156) you, as one cousin ought to kiss another.

(002-157) Ver Sir, how dare you?

(002-158) Asa Are you one of the family? Cause if you ain't, you've got no right

(002-159) to interfere, and if you be, you needn't be alarmed, I ain't going to

(002-160) kiss you. Here's your young man's letter. [Gives letter and attempts to

(002-161) kiss her.]

(002-162) Flo In the old country, Mr. Trenchard, cousins content themselves with

(002-163) hands, but our hearts are with them. You are welcome, there is mine.

(002-164) [Gives her hand, which he shakes heartily.]

(002-165) Asa That'll do about as well. I won't kiss you if you don't want me to;

(002-166) but if you did, I wouldn't stop on account of that sailor man. [Business

(002-167) of Vernon threatening Asa.] Oh! now you needn't get your back up. What

(002-168) an all-fired chap you are. Now if you'll have me shown to my room, I

(002-169) should like to fix up a bit and put on a clean buzzom. [All start.] Why,

(002-170) what on earth is the matter with you all? I only spoke because you're so

(002-171) all-fired go-to-meeting like.

(002-172) Flo Show Mr. Trenchard to the red room, Mr. Binny, that is if you are

(002-173) done with it, Mr. Dundreary.

(002-174) Dun Yeth, Miss Florence. The room and I have got through with each

(002-175) other, yeth.

(002-176) [Asa and Dundreary see each other for the first time. Business of

(002-177) recognition, ad. lib.]

(002-178) Asa Concentrated essence of baboons, what on earth is that?

(002-179) Dun He's mad. Yes, Miss Florence, I've done with that room. The rooks

(002-180) crowed so that they racked my brain.

(002-181) Asa You don't mean to say that you've got any brains.

(002-182) Dun No, sir, such a thing never entered my head. The wed indians want to

(002-183) scalp me. [Holding hands to his head.]

(002-184) Flo The red room, then, Mr. Binny.

(002-185) Asa [To Binny.] Hold on! [Examines him.] Wal, darn me, but you keep your

(002-186) help in all-fired good order here. [Feels of him.] This old shoat is fat

(002-187) enough to kill. [Hits Binny in stomach. Binny runs off, L. 2 E.] Mind

(002-188) how you go up stairs, old hoss, or you'll bust your biler. [Exit, L. 3

(002-189) E.]

(002-190) Dun Now he thinks Binny's an engine and has got a boiler.

(002-191) Flo Oh, what fun!

(002-192) Mrs M Old Mark Trenchard died very rich, did he not, Florence?

(002-193) Flo Very rich, I believe.

(002-194) Aug He's not at all romantic, is he, mamma?

(002-195) Mrs M [Aside to her] My dear, I have no doubt he has solid good

(002-196) qualities, and I don't want you to laugh at him like Florence Trenchard.

(002-197) Aug No, mamma, I won't.

(002-198) Flo But what are we to do with him?

(002-199) Dun Ha! Ha! ha!

(002-200) All What is the matter?

(002-201) Dun I've got an idea.

(002-202) Flo Oh! let's hear Dundreary's idea.

(002-203) Dun It's so seldom I get an idea that when I do get one it startles me.

(002-204) Let us get a pickle bottle.

(002-205) Flo Pickle bottle! [All come down.]

(002-206) Dun Yeth; one of those things with glass sides.

(002-207) Enter Asa, L. 2 E.

(002-208) Flo Oh! you mean a glass case.

(002-209) Dun Yeth, a glass case, that's the idea, and let us put this Mr. Thomas

(002-210) Hawk in it, and have him on exhibition. That's the idea.

(002-211) Asa [Down L. of Florence, overhearing.] Oh! that's your idea, is it?

(002-212) Wal, stranger, I don't know what they're going to do with me, but

(002-213) wherever they do put me, I hope it will be out of the reach of a

(002-214) jackass. I'm a real hoss, I am, and I get kinder riley with those

(002-215) critters.
