# The Fortune Revelation

The fortune revelation is not a single moment but a chain reaction. It begins with Asa quietly informing Mary that Mark Trenchard's property is hers, not his — a disclosure made without fanfare or witnesses. It accelerates when Florence finds the half-burned will and pieces together what Asa has done. And it detonates when Mrs. Mountchessington, who has been coaching her daughter Augusta to secure the "rich American," learns that Asa has no fortune — and drops him with a speed that is the play's most damning social critique. The scene is *Our American Cousin* at its moral and comic peak: a test that everyone fails except the two people who already know what they are worth.

## In the Play

### Context

By Act III, the play's intersecting plots have converged. The Ravensdale mortgage threatens to ruin Sir Edward. Coyle has proposed to Florence as the price of forbearance. Mrs. Mountchessington, scenting money, has ordered Augusta to pursue Asa: "pray be attentive to this American savage, as I rather think he will prove the better match of the two, if what I hear of Mark Trenchard's property be correct" (004-155–157). And Asa, having met Mary Meredith, has made a private decision that will upend every calculation.

### What Happens

**The Quiet Revelation.** Asa tells Mary, in private, that her grandfather's property is hers. Mary's response — "I can hardly believe the startling news — his heiress — I, the penniless orphan of an hour ago, no longer penniless" (008-008–010) — registers the transformation in economic terms. The word "heiress" has social meaning she has never before been able to claim. Asa, by contrast, says nothing about his own sacrifice and leaves the room before Mary can thank him.

**The Burnt Will.** Florence enters and finds Mary in a state of joyous confusion. When Mary declares "That all grandfather's property is mine, mine, Florence" (008-018), Florence objects: "Why how can Mark Trenchard's property be yours, unless you marry the legatee" (008-024). The confusion is resolved when Florence spots a half-burned sheet of paper on the floor — the same paper Asa used to light his cigar moments earlier. She reads the surviving fragments: "Here is old Mark Trenchard's signature... And here are the words 'Asa Trenchard, in consideration of sole heir'" (008-034–036). The truth assembles itself: Asa, named heir, burned the will that would have secured his fortune — because the dying Mark Trenchard had changed his mind and named Mary the true beneficiary.

**The Mercenary Test.** The scene's most theatrically satisfying sequence involves Mrs. Mountchessington and Augusta. Mrs. Mountchessington, unaware of the revelation, coaches her daughter: "remember, your happiness for life will depend upon the choice you make... This American is rich, and on the whole I think a well regulated affection ought to incline to Asa Trenchard" (008-050–055). But she adds a hedge: "you must be cautious, or in grasping at Asa Trenchard's solid good qualities, you may miss them, and De Boots expectations into the bargain" (008-057–058). Augusta's reply — "I will take care not to give up my hold on poor De Boots 'till I am quite sure of the American" (008-060) — is the play's most candid admission of mercenary calculation.

Asa enters and, perceiving their game, sets the trap. He asks what they would do if a "rough spun fellow" were to propose — and then drops the news: "I wasn't heir to the fortune" (008-091). The reaction is instantaneous:

> **Mrs. Mountchessington:** "Not heir to the fortune, Mr. Trenchard?"
> **Augusta:** "What, no fortune?" (008-092–094)

Asa explains: "Nary red, it all comes to their barkin up the wrong tree about the old man's property... he thought better on it, and left it to his granddaughter Miss Mary Meredith" (008-095–100). Mrs. Mountchessington's response — "Oh, I'm delighted" (008-101) — is transparently false, and Asa knows it.

**The Sockdologizing.** Asa twists the knife: "Now, some gals, and mothers would go away from a fellow when they found that out, but you don't valley fortune, Miss Gusty?" (008-106). Mrs. Mountchessington, caught, retreats into propriety: "Mr. Trenchard, you will please recollect you are addressing my daughter, and in my presence" (008-111). Augusta: "Yes, ma, the nasty beast" (008-114). Mrs. Mountchessington's exit line — "I am aware, Mr. Trenchard, you are not used to the manners of good society" (008-115–116) — is the play's definition of hypocrisy: manners as a weapon deployed when money fails.

Asa's reply — "Don't know the manners of good society, eh? Well, I guess I know enough to turn you inside out, old gal — you sockdologizing old man-trap" (008-122–123) — was, in 1858, a coinage. "Sockdologizing" — a word Taylor invented for the play — means something like "manipulative," "scheming," "two-faced." It entered the American vernacular through this scene, and its target is not Mrs. Mountchessington alone but the entire system of marriage-as-market that she represents.

**Florence's Discovery.** Florence confronts Asa with the burnt will: "And you're determined to give up this fortune to Mary Meredith?" (008-136). Asa's explanation — "when I saw that everlasting angel of a gal movin around among them doing fixins like a sunbeam in a shady place; and when I pictured her without a dollar in the world — I — well my old Adam riz right up, and I said, 'Asa do it' — and I did it" (008-140–142) — transforms his sacrifice from legal technicality into moral action. Florence's response is to kiss him: "you unselfish man! you! you, you!" (008-148). Asa's closing line — "Well, if that ain't worth four hundred thousand dollars, I don't know what is" (008-149) — prices the kiss at exactly the value of the fortune he surrendered, and finds it a fair exchange.

### Consequences

The fortune revelation reorders every relationship in the play. The Mountchessingtons are exposed as fortune-hunters; Mary is elevated from dependent to heiress; Asa is revealed not as a fortune-seeker but as the play's moral center; and the path is cleared for the finale's pairings — Florence with Vernon, Mary with Asa, and Augusta with the long-suffering De Boots. More importantly, the scene argues that character is revealed not by what people claim to value but by what they do when the fortune disappears.

## Key References

> "I can hardly believe the startling news — his heiress — I, the penniless orphan of an hour ago, no longer penniless."
>
> — Mary Meredith, Act III (008-008–010)

Mary's language is precise: her identity has changed because her economic status has changed. "Penniless" and "heiress" are not descriptions of character but of position — and Taylor makes sure the audience understands that Mary's worth was never in question, only her legal claim.

> "Not heir to the fortune, Mr. Trenchard?"
>
> — Mrs. Mountchessington, Act III (008-092)

Five words, and Mrs. Mountchessington's entire architecture of interest collapses. The question mark at the end is doing enormous work: it is the sound of a plan failing.

> "Don't know the manners of good society, eh? Well, I guess I know enough to turn you inside out, old gal — you sockdologizing old man-trap."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act III (008-122–123)

"Sockdologizing" was Taylor's coinage and became the play's most famous word. It means, approximately, "manipulating through flattery and deceit" — and it names a type that the play has spent three acts anatomizing. The "old man-trap" makes explicit what the manners have been disguising: marriage as predation.

> "Well, if that ain't worth four hundred thousand dollars, I don't know what is."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act III (008-149)

The line prices Florence's kiss at exactly the fortune Asa surrendered. It is a joke — but it is also a genuine economic argument. Asa has traded money for love, and he considers the exchange profitable.

## Historical Note

The fortune-revelation scene draws on a long tradition of theatrical "anagnorisis" — the Aristotelian recognition that reshuffles a play's moral deck. Taylor's innovation is to stage the revelation as a series of private disclosures rather than a single public announcement: Mary learns from Asa, Florence learns from the burnt paper, the Mountchessingtons learn from Asa's deliberate trap. Each revelation tests a different relationship, and the variety of responses — joy, admiration, mercenary recoil — composes a moral spectrum. The "sockdologizing" coinage, which became a minor sensation in 1858, testifies to the scene's effectiveness as satire: audiences recognized Mrs. Mountchessington as a type worth naming.
