(008-001) [[mary-meredith|Mary]] Poor Grandfather. [Buries her face and sobs.]

(008-002) Asa [After bus.] Wal, I guess I'd better leave her alone. [Sees half

(008-003) burned will.] There lies four hundred thousand dollars, if there's a

(008-004) cent. Asa, boy, you're a hoss. [Starts off, R. 1 C.]

(008-005) Mary To me, all to me. Oh [[asa-trenchard|Mr. Trenchard]], how we have all wronged poor

(008-006) grandfather. What, gone? He felt after such tidings, he felt I should be

(008-007) left alone--who would suspect there was such delicacy under that rough

(008-008) husk, but I can hardly believe the startling news--his heiress--I, the

(008-009) penniless orphan of an hour ago, no longer penniless, but, alas, an

(008-010) orphan still, [Enter Florence.] with none to share my [[fortune-and-wealth|wealth]], none to

(008-011) love me.

(008-012) [[florence-trenchard|Flo]] [Throwing arms around Mary's neck.] What treason is this, Mary, no

(008-013) one to love you, eh, what's the matter? You've been weeping, and I met

(008-014) that American Savage coming from here; he has not been rude to you?

(008-015) Mary On no, he's the gentlest of human beings, but he has just told me

(008-016) news that has moved me strangely.

(008-017) Flo What is it, love?

(008-018) Mary That all grandfather's property is mine, mine, Florence, do you

(008-019) understand?

(008-020) Flo What! he has popped, has he? I thought he would.

(008-021) Mary Who do you mean?

(008-022) Flo Who? Asa Trenchard, to be sure.

(008-023) Mary Asa Trenchard, why, what put that in your head?

(008-024) Flo Why how can Mark Trenchard's property be yours, unless you marry the

(008-025) legatee.

(008-026) Mary The legatee? Who?

(008-027) Flo Why, you know Mark Trenchard left everything to Asa.

(008-028) Mary No, no, you have been misinformed.

(008-029) Flo Nonsence, he showed it to me, not an hour ago on a half sheet of

(008-030) rough paper just like this. [Sees will.] Like this. [Picks it up.] Why

(008-031) this is part of it, I believe.

(008-032) Mary That's the paper he lighted his cigar with.

(008-033) Flo Then he lighted his cigar with 80,000 pounds. Here is old Mark

(008-034) Trenchard's signature.

(008-035) Mary Yes, I recognize the hand.

(008-036) Flo And here are the words ``Asa Trenchard, in consideration of sole

(008-037) heir''--etc.--etc.--etc.

(008-038) Mary Oh Florence, what does this mean?

(008-039) Flo It means that he is a true hero, and he loves you, you little rogue.

(008-040) [Embraces her.]

(008-041) Mary Generous man. [Hides face in Florence's bosom.]

(008-042) Flo Oh, won't I convict him, now. I'll find him at once.

(008-043) Runs off, R. 3 E., Mary after her calling Florence!!! Florence!!! as

(008-044) scene closes.

(008-045) Change

(008-046) Scene 2.--Chamber as before.

(008-047) Enter Mrs. Montchessington, and [[augusta|Augusta]], L. 1 E.

(008-048) [[mrs-mountchessington|Mrs M]] Yes, my child, while Mr. [[capt-de-boots|De Boots]] and Mr. Trenchard are both here,

(008-049) you must ask yourself seriously, as to the state of your affections,

(008-050) remember, your happiness for life will depend upon the choice you make.

(008-051) [[augustus-trenchard|Aug]] What would you advise, mamma? You know I am always advised by you.

(008-052) Mrs M Dear, obedient child. De Boots has excellent expectations, but

(008-053) then they are only expectations after all. This American is rich, and

(008-054) on the whole I think a well regulated affection ought to incline to Asa

(008-055) Trenchard.

(008-056) Aug Very well, mamma.

(008-057) Mrs M At the same time, you must be cautious, or in grasping at Asa

(008-058) Trenchard's solid good qualities, you may miss them, and De Boots

(008-059) expectations into the bargain.

(008-060) Aug Oh, I will take care not to give up my hold on poor De Boots 'till I

(008-061) am quite sure of the American.

(008-062) Mrs M That's my own girl. [Enter Asa L.] Ah, Mr. Trenchard, we were just

(008-063) talking of your archery powers.

(008-064) Asa Wal, I guess shooting with bows and arrows is just about like most

(008-065) things in life, all you've got to do is keep the sun out of your eyes,

(008-066) look straight--pull strong--calculate the distance, and you're sure to

(008-067) hit the mark in most things as well as shooting.

(008-068) Aug But not in [[england|England]], Mr. Trenchard. There are disinterested hearts

(008-069) that only ask an opportunity of showing how they despise that gold,

(008-070) which others set such store by.

(008-071) Asa Wal, I suppose there are, Miss Gusty.

(008-072) Aug All I crave is affection.

(008-073) Asa [Crosses to C.] Do you, now? I wish I could make sure of that, for

(008-074) I've been cruelly disappointed in that particular.

(008-075) Mrs M Yes, but we are old friends, Mr. Trenchard, and you needn't be

(008-076) afraid of us.

(008-077) Asa Oh, I ain't afraid of you--both on you together.

(008-078) Mrs M People sometimes look a great way off, for that which is near at

(008-079) hand. [Glancing at Augusta and Asa alternatively.]

(008-080) Asa You don't mean, Miss Gusta. [Augusta casts sheeps eyes at him.] Now,

(008-081) don't look at me in that way. I can't stand it, if you do, I'll bust.

(008-082) Mrs M Oh, if you only knew how refreshing this ingenuousness of yours is

(008-083) to an old woman of the world like me.

(008-084) Asa Be you an old woman of the world?

(008-085) Mrs M Yes, sir.

(008-086) Aug Oh yes.

(008-087) Asa Well I don't doubt it in the least. [Aside.] This gal and the old

(008-088) woman are trying to get me on a string. [Aloud.] Wal, then, if a rough

(008-089) spun fellow like me was to come forward as a suitor for your daughter's

(008-090) hand, you wouldn't treat me as some folks do, when they find out I

(008-091) wasn't heir to the [[inheritance|fortune]].

(008-092) Mrs M Not heir to the fortune, Mr. Trenchard?

(008-093) Asa Oh, no.

(008-094) Aug What, no fortune?

(008-095) Asa Nary red, it all comes to their barkin up the wrong tree about the

(008-096) old man's property.

(008-097) Mrs M Which he left to you.

(008-098) Asa Oh, no.

(008-099) Aug Not to you?

(008-100) Asa No, which he meant to leave to me, but he thought better on it, and

(008-101) left it to his granddaughter Miss Mary Meredith.

(008-102) Mrs M Miss Mary Meredith! Oh, I'm delighted.

(008-103) Aug Delighted?

(008-104) Asa Yes, you both look tickled to death. Now, some gals, and mothers

(008-105) would go away from a fellow when they found that out, but you don't

(008-106) valley fortune, Miss Gusty?

(008-107) Mrs M [Aside, crosses to Aug.] My love, you had better go.

(008-108) Asa You crave affection, _you_ do. Now I've no fortune, but I'm filling

(008-109) over with affections which I'm ready to pour out all over you like apple

(008-110) sass, over roast pork.

(008-111) Mrs M Mr. Trenchard, you will please recollect you are addressing my

(008-112) daughter, and in my presence.

(008-113) Asa Yes, I'm offering her my heart and hand just as she wants them with

(008-114) nothing in 'em.

(008-115) Mrs M Augusta, dear, to your room.

(008-116) Aug Yes, ma, the nasty beast. [Exit R.]

(008-117) Mrs M I am aware, Mr. Trenchard, you are not used to the [[class-and-manners|manners]] of good

(008-118) society, and that, alone, will excuse the impertinence of which you have

(008-119) been guilty.

(008-120) Asa Don't know the manners of good society, eh? Well, I guess I know

(008-121) enough to turn you inside out, old gal--you sockdologizing old man-trap.

(008-122) Wal, now, when I think what I've thrown away in hard cash to-day I'm apt

(008-123) to call myself some awful hard names, 400,000 dollars is a big pile for

(008-124) a man to light his cigar with. If that gal had only given me herself in

(008-125) exchange, it wouldn't have been a bad bargain. But I dare no more ask

(008-126) that gal to be my wife, than I dare ask Queen Victoria to dance a Cape

(008-127) Cod reel.

(008-128) Enter Florence, L. 1 E.

(008-129) Flo What do you mean by doing all these dreadful things?

(008-130) Asa Which things.

(008-131) Flo Come here sir. [He does so.]

(008-132) Asa What's the matter?

(008-133) Flo Do you know this piece of paper? [Showing burnt paper.]

(008-134) Asa Well I think I have seen it before. [Aside.] Its old Mark

(008-135) Trenchard's will that I left half burned up like a landhead, that I am.

(008-136) Flo And you're determined to give up this fortune to Mary Meredith?

(008-137) Asa Well, I couldn't help it if I tried.

(008-138) Flo Oh, don't say that.

(008-139) Asa I didn't mean to do it when I first came here--hadn't the least idea

(008-140) in the world of it, but when I saw that everlasting angel of a gal movin

(008-141) around among them doing fixins like a sunbeam in a shady place; and when

(008-142) I pictured her without a dollar in the world--I--well my old Adam riz

(008-143) right up, and I said, ``Asa do it''--and I did it.

(008-144) Flo Well, I don't know who your old Adam may be, but whoever it is, he's

(008-145) a very honest man to consult you to do so good an action. But how dare

(008-146) you do such an outrageous thing? you impudent--you unceremonious, oh!

(008-147) you unselfish man! you! you, you! [Smothers him with kisses, and runs

(008-148) off, R. 1 E.]

(008-149) Asa Well, if that ain't worth four hundred thousand dollars, I don't

(008-150) know what is, it was sweeter than sweet cider right out of the bung

(008-151) hole. Let me see how things stand round here. Thanks to old whiskers

(008-152) I've got that ship for the sailor man, and that makes him and Miss

(008-153) Florence all hunk. Then there's that darned old [[richard-coyle|Coyle]]. Well I guess me

(008-154) and old [[murcott|Murcott]] can fix his flint for him. Then there's--[Looks off, L.]

(008-155) Christopher Columbus, here comes Mary.

(008-156) Enter Mary, L. 1 E.

(008-157) Mary Mr. Trenchard, what can I say to you but offer you my lifelong

(008-158) [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|gratitude]].

(008-159) Asa Don't now, Miss, don't--

(008-160) Mary If I knew what else to offer. Heaven knows there is nothing that is

(008-161) mine to give that I would keep back.

(008-162) Asa Give me yourself. [Bus.] I know what a rude, ill-mannered block I

(008-163) am; but there's a heart inside me worth something, if it's only for the

(008-164) sake of your dear little image, that's planted right plump in the middle

(008-165) of it.

(008-166) Mary Asa Trenchard, there is my hand, and my heart is in it.

(008-167) Asa [Seizes here hand, then drops it suddenly.] Miss Mary, I made what

(008-168) folks call a big sacrifice for you, this morning. Oh! I know it, I ain't

(008-169) so modest, but that I know it. Now what's this you're doing? Is this

(008-170) sacrifice you are making out of gratitude for me? Cause if it is, I

(008-171) wouldn't have it, though not to have it would nigh break my heart, tough

(008-172) as it is.

(008-173) Mary No, no, I give myself freely to you--as freely as you, this

(008-174) morning, gave my grandfather's property to me.

(008-175) Asa Say it again, last of hope and blessed promise. [Clasps her in his

(008-176) arms.] Mary, there's something tells me that you'll not repent it. I'm

(008-177) rough, Mary, awful rough, but you needn't fear that I'll ever be rough

(008-178) to you. I've camped out in the woods, Mary, often and often, and seen

(008-179) the bears at play with their cubs in the moonlight, the glistening

(008-180) teeth, that would tear the hunter, was harmless to them; the big strong

(008-181) claws that would peel a man's head, as a knife would a pumpkin, was as

(008-182) soft for them as velvet cushions, and that's what I'll be with you, my

(008-183) own little wife; and if ever harm does come to you, it must come over

(008-184) the dead body of Asa Trenchard.

(008-185) Mary I know it Asa; and if I do not prove a true and loving wife to you;

(008-186) may my mother's bright spirit never look down to bless her child.

(008-187) Asa Wal, if I don't get out in the air, I'll bust. [Exit hastily R. 1 E.

(008-188) pulling Mary after him.]

(008-189) Enter [[binny|Binny]], L. 1 E. Drunk.

(008-190) [[mr-binny|Binny]] [Calling.] Mr. H'Asa, Mr. H'Asa! Oh he's gone; well, I suppose

(008-191) he'll come back to keep his happointment. [[mr-coyle|Mr. Coyle]]'s quite impatient.

(008-192) It isn't hoften that han hamerican has the run of the wine cellars of

(008-193) [[trenchard-manor|Trenchard Manor]], and in such company, too. There's me and Mr. Coyle,

(008-194) which is a good judge of old port wine, and he knows it when he drinks;

(008-195) and his clerk, Mr. Murcott, which I don't hexactly like sitting

(008-196) down with clerks. But Mr. H'Asa wished it and Mr. Coyle hadn't any

(008-197) objections, so in course I put my feelings in my pocket, besides,

(008-198) Murcott is a man of hedication, though unfortunately taken to drink.

(008-199) Well, what of that, it's been many a man's misfortune, though I say it,

(008-200) what shouldn't say it, being a butler. But now to join my distinguished

(008-201) party. [Exit, R. 1 E.]
