# Language and Dialect

Language in Tom Taylor's *Our American Cousin* (1858) functions as an audible marker of national identity and social class. American characters speak with direct, colloquial energy; English aristocrats employ formal diction that masks intellectual vacancy; and the collision of these registers generates much of the play's comedy.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The linguistic divide between American and English characters structures the play's central thematic contrast. Asa Trenchard's Vermont vernacular—"Wal, darn me if you ain't the conc…" [002-148], "Wal, yes, I guess you do b'long t…" [002-152]—immediately marks him as an outsider in the English drawing room, but the play consistently rewards his plain speech with moral authority. Meanwhile, Lord Dundreary's mangled aristocratic diction reduces language to comic sound: his "Dundrearyisms" are not wit but its absence, delivered with unearned confidence.

The play's treatment of dialect is notably asymmetrical: English characters do not comment on American speech as "incorrect," while American characters repeatedly puncture English verbal pretension. Florence's teasing of Dundreary ("Ha! ha! Is that your own?" [002-036]) and Asa's directness with the Mountchessingtons both serve as linguistic leveling—American clarity cutting through British form.

### Associated Characters

**Asa Trenchard** is the primary vehicle of American dialect, his "Wal" and "darn me" and "I reckon" marking rural Vermont origins that the play treats as a source of integrity rather than embarrassment. **Lord Dundreary** embodies the opposite extreme: aristocratic English that signifies nothing, his speech a series of borrowed phrases and failed aphorisms. **Mrs. Mountchessington** practices a more functional version of formal English, using polite diction as a weapon of social maneuvering, while **Florence Trenchard** moves between registers, speaking both the English of her upbringing and the more direct language she adopts under Asa's influence.

### Key References

> "Asa Wal, darn me if you ain't the con…" [002-148]

Asa's first extended speech establishes his Vermont dialect as a marker of plain-dealing American identity.

> "Asa Wal, yes, I guess you do b'long t…" [002-152]

The colloquial "I guess" and dropped final consonants mark a speech pattern the play presents as natural rather than uncouth.

> "Flo Ha! ha! Is that your own, Dundreary?" [002-036]

Florence's question exposes the borrowed, secondhand quality of aristocratic wit.

> "Flo Oh! let's hear Dundreary's idea." [002-202]

The ironic prompt—Florence knows Dundreary has no real ideas—exposes the gap between linguistic form and intellectual content.

## Cultural/Historical Context

Taylor's play premiered at a moment when Anglo-American cultural relations were freighted with post-colonial tension. American English had developed distinct vocabulary, pronunciation, and rhythm by the 1850s, and British audiences were primed to receive American speech as comic—or as evidence of cultural inferiority. Taylor's innovation was to reverse the expected hierarchy: the American dialect speaker is the play's moral center, while the aristocrat who speaks "proper" English is a figure of fun. This linguistic nationalism parallels the play's broader celebration of American sincerity over British formality, and it helps explain the play's popularity with American audiences, who heard their own speech patterns dignified on the London stage.
