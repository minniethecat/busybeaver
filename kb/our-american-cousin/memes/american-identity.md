# American Identity

The self-conscious opposition between [[asa-trenchard|American democratic vigor]] and [[class-and-manners|English aristocratic convention]] that drives both the comic tension and the moral resolution of the play. Asa Trenchard, the "American cousin," serves as the living embodiment of this contrast — his plain speech, practical skills, and egalitarian instincts repeatedly clash with the rigid social codes of the English gentry at [[trenchard-manor]].

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The American identity of the protagonist is not merely a character trait but the engine of the plot itself. The Trenchard family's financial crisis stems from mortgages on their English estates, and their salvation hinges on the arrival of the American heir (003-171)(003-174). Asa's American sensibility — his refusal to cling to aristocratic pretension when honesty would serve better — ultimately resolves the inheritance question in favor of genuine merit over birthright. The English characters' assumptions about American "savagery" (004-155) are systematically subverted as Asa proves more honorable than the scheming Englishmen who surround him.

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — the "American cousin" whose plain Vermont manner disconcerts the English gentry. He embodies American practicality: he knows dairying (006-040), uses tools rather than polish (009-113), and treats social pretension as a foreign language he refuses to learn.

- **Florence Trenchard** — the English cousin whose playful curiosity about Americans masks a genuine openness. Her teasing questions — "They are all 17 feet high in America, ain't they?" (002-097) — satirize English ignorance while her growing respect for Asa models the possibility of mutual understanding.

- **Lord Dundreary** — the caricature of English aristocracy whose ineffectual drawl and self-absorption serve as the foil to Asa's directness. His "One on that Yankee" (007-024) encodes the dismissiveness the play ultimately dismantles.

- **Mrs. Mountchessington** — the social-climbing aristocrat whose condescension toward "Americans" as "so clever, and so acute" (004-183) is exposed as calculated flattery, revealing the mercenary motives beneath polished manners.

- **Mary Meredith** — the milkmaid whose genuine curiosity about American life — "I've listened to your stories until I'm half a backwoodsman's wife already" (007-139) — represents the sincere cross-cultural interest that the titled characters lack.

### American vs. English Traits in the Play

| Trait | American (Asa) | English (Gentry) |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Speech | Plain, direct, vernacular | Polished, evasive, coded |
| Labor | Skilled in dairying, manual work | Disdain for practical work |
| Honor | Personal integrity, actions over words | Reputation, appearances over substance |
| Social code | Egalitarian, judges by merit | Hierarchical, judges by birth |
| Money | Earned through work | Inherited or married into |
| Curiosity | Genuine, exploratory | Instrumental, calculating |

The table distills a pattern that runs through every act: American traits are consistently aligned with moral virtue, English traits with moral compromise. Taylor's comedy is not neutral anthropology but an argument with a clear normative conclusion.

### Key References

> "Wal, now I am alone, I can look about me and indulge the enquiring spirit of an American citizen. What an everlasting lot of things and fixins there is to be sure."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act I (004-088)(004-089)

Asa's first soliloquy in the English manor establishes the American gaze as fundamentally curious rather than reverent — he inspects the aristocratic interior not with awe but with the cataloguing instinct of a citizen, not a subject.

> "Florence: Will you be kind enough to translate that for me, for I don't understand American yet.
> Asa: Yes, I'll put it in French for you, 'petite pommes des terres.'"
>
> — Act I, the dairy scene (006-053)(006-054)(006-055)

The joke operates on multiple levels: Florence treats "American" as a foreign language, Asa's effortless code-switch to French demonstrates that his vernacular is a choice, not a limitation, and the translation of "small potatoes" into elegant French deflates the very class distinction it's meant to mark.

> "Asa: Have ye, now? Well, if a Yankee lawyer had done such a thing he would have Judge Lynch after him in no time."
>
> — Act I (005-148)(005-149)

Asa's invocation of rough American frontier justice positions him outside the English legal system that is being manipulated to ruin his relatives. The "Yankee" perspective here is the moral one — swift accountability rather than legalistic evasion.

## Cultural and Historical Context

"Our American Cousin" premiered in 1858, when transatlantic cultural tension was a staple of popular entertainment. The play draws on a well-established stage tradition of the "stage Yankee" — the plain-spoken, shrewd American whose homespun wisdom confounds European sophistication. Taylor's innovation was to make this figure not merely comic relief but the moral center of the play: Asa's American identity is the mechanism by which English corruption is exposed and English decency (in the person of Mary Meredith) is rewarded.

The play's treatment of American identity reflects the mid-19th-century moment — after the Jacksonian era's democratization but before the Civil War's rupture. Asa's Vermont plainness is presented as a national type: practical, unpretentious, physically capable, and fundamentally honest. This is American identity as self-conscious antithesis to European aristocracy, a posture that would soon give way to more anxious formulations of national character.
