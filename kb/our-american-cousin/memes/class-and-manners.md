# Class and Manners

The elaborate code of deference, address, and comportment by which English society organizes itself into ranks — and which Tom Taylor's comedy systematically exposes as a performance masking greed, incompetence, and moral vacancy. The play's central satiric project is to demonstrate that good manners and good character are not merely unrelated but often inversely correlated. Against this system, [[american-identity|Asa Trenchard's American directness]] serves as both critique and alternative.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

Class hierarchy is the invisible architecture of every scene at [[trenchard-manor]]. Sir Edward's desperate maintenance of aristocratic appearances conceals financial ruin (003-155). Mrs. Mountchessington's elaborate politeness toward Asa masks her assessment of his material worth — "Oh, you Americans are so clever, and so acute" (004-183) is a gambler's flattery, not a host's welcome. Lord Dundreary's entire persona is an extended demonstration that the highest rank can coexist with the feeblest intellect.

The American characters are positioned outside this hierarchy, and their inability or refusal to play by its rules is what enables the plot's moral resolution. Asa does not defer to title; he judges by action. This makes him socially awkward and morally indispensable in equal measure.

### Associated Characters

- **Sir Edward Trenchard** — the landed gentleman whose financial distress reveals the gap between aristocratic appearance and economic reality. His "estates" are mortgaged, his creditors clamorous — yet he maintains the manner of a man of substance.

- **Mrs. Mountchessington** — the most acute practitioner of class performance in the play. Her every word is calibrated to advance her son's social position, and her condescension toward Asa — "pray be attentive to this American savage" (004-155) — is the voice of class anxiety disguised as class confidence.

- **Augustus Trenchard** — the class performer as empty vessel. His romantic posturing, his instant recoil from financial disappointment, and his mother's transparent stage-management make him the play's exhibit of aristocratic education without substance.

- **Lord Dundreary** — the caricature of aristocratic inanity whose verbal tics and logical non sequiturs reduce class distinction to absurdity. His "One on that Yankee" (007-024) is class solidarity reduced to a punchline.

- **Asa Trenchard** — the outsider whose refusal to perform class deference exposes the system's arbitrariness. His "Yankee" directness is not rudeness but an alternative code of manners grounded in honesty rather than hierarchy.

### Class Markers in the Play

| Marker | Aristocratic Expression | Asa's Alternative |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Address | Elaborate titles, formal courtesy | First names, direct address |
| Flattery | Instrumental ("so clever, and so acute") | Absent — says what he means |
| Labor | Hidden or disdained | Visible skill (dairying, tool use) |
| Money | Discussed indirectly, through honor | Discussed plainly as fact |
| Wit | Dandified wordplay (Dundrearyisms) | Vernacular humor grounded in experience |
| Honor | Reputation among peers | Personal integrity, regardless of audience |

The table maps Taylor's systematic method: for every aristocratic norm, the play supplies an American counter-norm, and the counter-norm is always the morally superior option.

### Key References

> "Mrs M: Oh, you Americans are so clever, and so acute."
>
> — Act I (004-183)

The line is double-voiced: on its surface a compliment, in its function an assessment of potential advantage. Mrs. Mountchessington's class performance is never more polished than when she is calculating.

> "Mrs M: pray be attentive to this American savage, as I rather think he will prove the better match of the two."
>
> — Act I (004-155)(004-156)

The word "savage" — applied to a fellow English-speaking Christian from [[vermont|Vermont]] — measures the distance class ideology can create. And the pivot from insult to calculation ("the better match") reveals that class manners are entirely instrumental.

> "Dun: For you. Ha! ha! One on that Yankee."
>
> — Act II (007-024)

Dundreary's response to Asa's verbal victory reduces class solidarity to a scorecard. "One on that Yankee" treats social interaction as a competition between national types — and implicitly acknowledges that the Yankee is winning.

## Cultural and Historical Context

"Our American Cousin" premiered at a moment when the English class system was under pressure from multiple directions. The Reform Act of 1832 had expanded the franchise; the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 had shifted economic power toward industrial and commercial interests; and the Chartist movement had placed working-class political demands on the national agenda. The aristocracy depicted in Taylor's play — financially hollow, intellectually vacuous, morally compromised — reflects a widespread perception that the traditional ruling class was no longer fit for purpose.

At the same time, the play's American setting (mediated through Asa) offered a contrast that was increasingly familiar to British audiences. By the 1850s, accounts of American democratic manners — from Frances Trollope's "Domestic Manners of the Americans" (1832) to Charles Dickens's "American Notes" (1842) — had established a genre of transatlantic social commentary on which Taylor could draw.
