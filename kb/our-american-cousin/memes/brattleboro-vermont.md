## Dramatic Role

Brattleboro, Vermont, is never seen on stage but is repeatedly referenced as the hometown of Asa Trenchard, the play's American protagonist. It functions as a symbolic counterpoint to Trenchard Manor—representing American rustic simplicity, democratic values, and the "backwoods" identity that the English characters alternately mock and underestimate.

The first mention of Brattleboro occurs before Asa even appears on stage. In Act I, a letter arrives addressed to "Miss Florence Trenchard, via Brattleboro', Vermont" [001-163]. The servant John Wickens, himself a rural Englishman, responds to Binny's query "Where's that?" with the observation that Vermont is "hin the United States of North Hamerica, and a main good place for poor folks" [001-177]. This exchange immediately establishes the geographic distance and class associations that will animate the transatlantic comedy.

Asa's own letters home are written from "Brattleboro' Vt." [001-138], and his self-introduction proudly declares he was "born in Vermont, suckled on the banks of Muddy Creek, about the tallest gunner, the slickest dancer, and generally the loudest critter in the state" [002-153]. This exuberant self-description conflates person and place: Asa is Vermont, and Vermont is Asa—plain-speaking, energetic, and defiantly un-English.

The Vermont identity becomes a running motif. When Sir Edward asks if Asa hunts buffalo, Asa mutters aside: "Buffaloes down in Vermont" [004-178], parodying English ignorance of American geography. Later, Mary Meredith imagines the Trenchard homestead in romantic terms: "I can shut my eyes and almost fancy I see your home in the backwoods. There are your two sisters running about in their sunbonnets" . The character's plan to return to "the roaring old state of Vermont, and live 'long with mother" [007-162] after resolving the inheritance plot reinforces the thematic arc: the American visitor enriches English society but ultimately returns to his native soil.

## Symbolic Function

Brattleboro operates in the play's symbolic geography as the anti-Trenchard Manor. Where the manor represents hereditary hierarchy, mortgage debt, and social artifice, Brattleboro represents democratic self-making, honest labor, and natural sincerity. This binary is not simply nationalistic—Taylor complicates it by showing that Asa's Vermont values are precisely what enable him to resolve the English family's crisis, suggesting that American energy and English tradition can be complementary rather than antagonistic.

The name "Brattleboro" itself carries comic weight in performance. The double "t" and "b" sounds, combined with the apostrophe in the colloquial "Brattleboro'" pronunciation, mark the word as distinctly American—a sound foreign to English drawing-rooms. When the English characters struggle to place it geographically ("Where's that, Mr. Binny" [001-175]), the audience is reminded of the vast Atlantic gulf separating the two cultures.

## Historical Basis

Brattleboro, Vermont, was a real and thriving town in the 1850s. Located in Windham County along the Connecticut River, it was a center of commerce, publishing, and the water-cure (hydropathy) movement. By the time Tom Taylor wrote *Our American Cousin* in 1858, Brattleboro was well-known enough to serve as a plausible—and slightly exotic to English audiences—American hometown.

The town's selection as Asa's origin is likely intentional. Vermont held a particular place in the 19th-century English imagination: associated with maple sugar, independent yeoman farmers, and the idealized "pure" American character uncorrupted by urban vice. Asa's repeated emphasis on his Vermont roots plays into the transatlantic stereotype of the plain-speaking, honest American—a figure English audiences would recognize from travel literature and earlier stage Yankees on the London stage.

The specificity of "Muddy Creek" in Asa's origin story [002-153] adds verisimilitude while also functioning comedically: the name literalizes the "muddy" rustic origins that English society finds so uncouth, and which Asa himself weaponizes as a badge of democratic honor.
