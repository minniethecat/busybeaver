(005-001) Enter [[lord-dundreary|Dundreary]] and [[buddicombe|Buddicombe]], L. 1 E.

(005-002) Bud My lord--

(005-003) Dun [Business.]

(005-004) Bud My lord!

(005-005) Dun [Business.]

(005-006) Bud Your lordship!! [Louder.]

(005-007) Dun There, now you've spoiled it.

(005-008) Bud Spoiled what, my lord?

(005-009) Dun Spoiled what, my lord; why, a most magnificent sneeze!

(005-010) Bud I am very sorry, my lord.

(005-011) Dun Now that I can speak alone with you, tell me about that hair dye.

(005-012) Have you found it?

(005-013) Bud Not a trace of it, my lord.

(005-014) Dun If you don't find it, I'll discharge you.

(005-015) Bud Very well, my lord. [Bows and exits, L. 1 E.]

(005-016) Dun Very well, my lord! He's gone and lost my hair dye, and my hair

(005-017) turns red to-morrow, and when I ask him to find it for me or I'll

(005-018) discharge him, he says, ``Very well, my lord.'' He's positively idiotic,

(005-019) he is-- Ah! here comes Miss Georgina, that gorgeous creature--that

(005-020) lovely sufferer. [Exit, L. 1 E.]

(005-021) Asa [Looking out.] What's the price of hair dye? Hallo! he's coming

(005-022) again with that sick girl.

(005-023) Re-enter Dundreary and Georgina, L. 1 E.

(005-024) Dun Will you try and strengthen your limbs with a gentle walk in the

(005-025) garden?

(005-026) Georgina No, thank you, my lord. I'm so delicate. Oh, my lord, it is so

(005-027) painful to walk languidly through life, to be unable, at times, to bear

(005-028) the perfumes of one's favorite flowers. Even those violets you sent me

(005-029) yesterday I was compelled to have removed from my room, the perfume was

(005-030) too strong for me. I'm _so_ delicate.

(005-031) Dun Yes, Miss Georgina; but they're very strengthening flowers, you

(005-032) know.

(005-033) Geo Yes, my lord, you are always right.

(005-034) Dun Do you know I'm getting to be very robust?

(005-035) Geo Would I could share that fault with you; but I am so delicate.

(005-036) Dun If you were robust I should not love you as I do. It would deprive

(005-037) you of that charm which enchains me to your lovely side, which--which--

(005-038) Geo Oh, my lord, my lord! I'm going to faint.

(005-039) Dun And I'm going to sneeze, you faint while I sneeze.

(005-040) Geo [Taking his arm.] Oh! my lord.

(005-041) Dun Do you know what a sneeze is?

(005-042) Geo No, my lord.

(005-043) Dun She never sneezed. I'll tell you what a sneeze is. Imagine a very

(005-044) large spider.

(005-045) Geo [Screams.] Where, my lord?

(005-046) Dun No, no, I don't mean a real spider, only an imaginary one, a large

(005-047) spider getting up your nose, and all of a sudden, much to his disgust,

(005-048) he discovers he has put his foot in it and can't get it out again.

(005-049) Geo That must be very distressing.

(005-050) Dun For the spider, yes, and not very pleasant for the nose.

(005-051) Geo Oh! my lord, do take me to mamma.

(005-052) Dun No, you lovely sufferer, let's walk a little more.

(005-053) Geo I can't my lord, I'm _so_ delicate.

(005-054) Dun Well, then, exercise, imitate that little hop of mine. [Hops.] It

(005-055) isn't a run, it's a--

(005-056) Geo What is it?

(005-057) Dun No, it isn't a what is it. Well, let me suppose I get you an oyster.

(005-058) [Georgina shakes her head.] Oh! then suppose I get you an oyster.

(005-059) Geo No, my lord, I'm too delicate.

(005-060) Dun How would you like the left wing of a canary bird?

(005-061) Geo No, my lord, it's too strong for me.

(005-062) Dun Let me ask you a widdle--why does a duck go under water? for divers

(005-063) reasons. Now I'll give you another--why does a duck come out of the

(005-064) water? for sundry reasons. No! No! see, you live on suction, you're

(005-065) like that bird with a long bill, they call doctor, no, that's not it,

(005-066) I thought it was a doctor, because it has a long bill--I mean a

(005-067) snipe--yes, you're a lovely snipe. [Exeunt, R.]

(005-068) Asa [Looking after them.] There goes a load of wooden nutmegs. Hello,

(005-069) here comes somebody else.

(005-070) Enter Florence, R., with paper.

(005-071) [[florence-trenchard|Flo]]. [Reads.] ``One who still remembers what he ought long since to have

(005-072) forgotten, wishes to speak with Miss Trenchard.'' Florence scratched

(005-073) out, ``on matters of life and death, near the orel, in the west

(005-074) gallery,'' Written upon a dirty sheet of paper, in a hardly legible

(005-075) hand. What does this mean; it opens like one of Mrs. Radcliffe's

(005-076) romances. Well, here I am, and now for my correspondent.

(005-077) Enter [[murcott|Murcott]], L.

(005-078) Mur Oh! for one minute's clear head, Miss Florence.

(005-079) Flo I presume you are the writer of this?

(005-080) Mur Yes, I am.

(005-081) Flo You address me as an old acquaintance, but I do not recognize you.

(005-082) Mur So much the better. So much the better.

(005-083) Flo I hate mystery, sir; but you see I have come to rendezvous. I must

(005-084) know to whom I am speaking.

(005-085) Mur As frank as ever. I am [[abel-murcott|Abel Murcott]].

(005-086) Flo Starting back! You?

(005-087) Mur Do not be ashamed, I have not the strength to injure you, if I had

(005-088) the evil. In this shabby, broken down drunkard you need not fear the

(005-089) madman, who years ago forgot in his frantic passion the gulf that lay

(005-090) between your station and his own. I am harmless except to my self.

(005-091) Flo Speak on, sir; I hear you.

(005-092) Mur I need not tell you by what steps I came to this, you don't know,

(005-093) maybe you never knew, what a maddening thing a passion is when it turns

(005-094) against itself. After being expelled from my tutorship in this house, I

(005-095) lost my employment, self respect, hope. I sought to drown recollection

(005-096) and draw courage from drink. It only embittered remembrances, and

(005-097) destroyed the little courage I had left. That I have bread to eat, I

(005-098) owe to Mr. [[richard-coyle|Coyle]]; he employed me as his clerk. You know he has been with

(005-099) your father this morning. I have come to tell you my errand; are you as

(005-100) brave as you used to be when I knew--

(005-101) Flo I fear nothing.

(005-102) Mur I come to tell you of your father's ruin, his utter ruin.

(005-103) Flo My father's ruin? What? What?

(005-104) Mur His estates are mortgaged, his creditors clamorous. The Bailiffs

(005-105) will be in [[trenchard-manor|Trenchard Manor]] to-day, disguised as your own [[servants-and-service|servants]]. This

(005-106) much [[mr-coyle|Mr. Coyle]] has conceded to your father's respect for appearances.

(005-107) Flo Then beggary stares him in the face. Poor father, what a sad blow

(005-108) for him. Is that all, sir?

(005-109) Mur No; the worst remains.

(005-110) Flo Go on, sir.

(005-111) Mur Coyle knows your father's weakness and as a means of escape from

(005-112) ruin to the verge of which he has brought him, he has this day proposed

(005-113) for your hand.

(005-114) Flo Mine!

(005-115) Mur On consideration of settling on you the [[ravensdale-estate|Ravensdale Estate]].

(005-116) Flo And my father, how did he listen to such insolence?

(005-117) Mur You know as well as I do how he would hear such a proposal, at first

(005-118) a torrent of rage, then the strong ebb of selfishness set in, and

(005-119) he consented to listen to the terms, to view them as something to be

(005-120) considered, to consider them.

(005-121) Flo Good Heavens, can this be true? No, I will not believe it of my

(005-122) father, and from such lips.

(005-123) Mur You have full right to think this and to say it, but mark your

(005-124) father and Coyle to-day. You will then see if I speak truth or not.

(005-125) Flo Forgive my distrust, Mr. Murcott.

(005-126) Mur I am past taking offence or feeling scorn, I have carried more than

(005-127) can be heaped upon me, but I did not come only to give you warning of

(005-128) your danger.

(005-129) Flo Can you avert it?

(005-130) Asa (Coming down between them). Wal, stranger that's just the question I

(005-131) was going to ask.

(005-132) Flo You here, sir, and listening.

(005-133) Asa Wal, it wasn't purpose, I went in there to take a snooze, I heard

(005-134) you talking and I thought it wouldn't be polite of me not to listen to

(005-135) what you had to say. I'm a rough sort of a customer, and don't know much

(005-136) about the ways of great folks. But I've got a cool head, a stout arm,

(005-137) and a willing heart, and I think I can help you, just as one cousin

(005-138) ought to help another.

(005-139) Flo Well, I do think you are honest.

(005-140) Mur Shall I go on?

(005-141) Flo Yes, we will trust him, go on.

(005-142) Mur I found the Ravensdale [[debt-and-credit|mortgage]] while rumaging in an old deed box of

(005-143) Coyle's father's, there was a folded paper inside the deed. I took both

(005-144) to Coyle unopened, like a besotted fool that I was. My belief is strong

(005-145) that the paper was the release of the mortgage that the [[fortune-and-wealth|money]] had been

(005-146) paid off, and the release executed without the seals having been cut

(005-147) from the original mortgage. I have known such things happen.

(005-148) Asa Have ye, now? Well, if a [[asa-trenchard|Yankee]] lawyer had done such a thing he

(005-149) would have Judge Lynch after him in no time.

(005-150) Mur You can but find that release, we may unmask this diabolical fiend

(005-151) and save you.

(005-152) Flo But, surely, a villain of Coyle's stability would have destroyed the

(005-153) paper, the very key-stone of his fraud.

(005-154) Mur I fear so.

(005-155) Asa Do you, now, wal, you're wrong, you're both wrong. I guess you ain't

(005-156) either on you done much cyphering human nature. The key stone of their

(005-157) fraud is just the point your mighty cute rascals always leave unsecured.

(005-158) Come along with me, stranger, and we'll just [[labor-and-worth|work]] up this sum a little,

(005-159) two heads are better than one. Yours is a little muddled, but mine's

(005-160) pretty clear, and if I don't circumvent that old sarpint, Coyle--

(005-161) Flo Well?

(005-162) Asa Say I am a skunk, that's all, and that's the meanest kind of an

(005-163) animal. [Exit L. 1st E.]

(005-164) Flo I owe you much, Mr. Murcott, more than I can ever repay.

(005-165) Mur No, no, no, if you did but know the hope of seeing you has roused

(005-166) all the manhood that drink and misery has left me. God bless you, Miss

(005-167) Florence.

(005-168) Flo No, you don't call me Florence as you did when I was the truant

(005-169) pupil and you the indulgent tutor. [Offers her hand.]

(005-170) Mur No, no; for heaven's sake do not call back that time or I shall go

(005-171) mad! mad! mad. [Rushes off, L. 1 E., followed by Florence.]

(005-172) Scene 2--Park in 4. Rural cottage, L. 1 E., adjoining which, and

(005-173) projecting on stage an inside view of a [[dairy-farming|dairy]] with sloping roof,

(005-174) painting backing to look like milk pans. The whole scene should have a

(005-175) picturesque appearance. Garden fence run across back, ornamental gate or

(005-176) archway, R. 3 E. Pigeon house on pole near dairy, L. C. Spinning wheel

(005-177) inside cottage door, one or two rustic benches, R. and L.

(005-178) Enter John, R. 3 E., with two milk pails on a yoke, puts them down near

(005-179) dairy, then [[beauty-and-appearance|looks]] off, R. 3 E.

(005-180) John There they go, that's a bull's eye, I warrant. Dang me though, if

(005-181) I wouldn't rather see Miss [[mary-meredith|Mary]] than this cock robin sports yonder, here

(005-182) she comes. Good morning, Miss Mary. [Enter Mary from cottage L.]

(005-183) Mary Oh, Wickens, you are there. How kind of you to help me with the

(005-184) milk pails to-day, when all the lads and lasses have given themselves a

(005-185) holiday to see the shooting.

(005-186) John Ah, Miss Mary, you ought to be among then, with a green hat and

(005-187) feather, if all had their rights.

(005-188) Mary [Laughing.] Nay, ladies without a farthing in the world, ought to

(005-189) put aside their ladyships and make themselves: besides I'm proud of my

(005-190) dairy here, just help me with this troublesome fellow, steady, don't

(005-191) shake it, the cream is foaming so beautifully. There. [John carries pan

(005-192) into cottage and returns down, R.]

(005-193) John Now, Miss Mary, what can I do for you?

(005-194) Mary Let me see; well, really, I do believe, Wickens, I've nothing to do

(005-195) but amuse myself.

(005-196) John Dang it, Miss, that's a pity, cos I can't help you at that, you

(005-197) see.

(005-198) Mary Oh! Yes, bring me out dear old Welsh nurse's spinning wheel [Exit

(005-199) John into cottage, L. 2 E.] by the side of which I have stood so often,

(005-200) a round eyed baby wondering at its whirring wheel. [Reenter John with

(005-201) wheel, places it near cottage, L. 2 E.] There, that will do famously. I

(005-202) can catch the full scent of the jessamines.
