# Vermont

Asa Trenchard's home state — never seen on stage but present in every scene through his speech, his values, and the contrast he embodies. [[vermont|Vermont]] in Taylor's play is not a geographic place but a moral argument: the democratic alternative to [[trenchard-manor|English aristocratic decay]], the source of the practical competence and personal integrity that will ultimately rescue the Trenchard family from its financial and moral crisis.

## In the Play

### Significance

Vermont functions as the play's offstage counter-world to [[class-and-manners|English class society]]. Every time Asa invokes his home — his dairy knowledge (006-040), his tool skills (009-113), his "Yankee" common sense — he is importing Vermont values into the English manor. The geographic distance between Brattleboro and Trenchard Manor maps onto the cultural distance between democratic competence and aristocratic pretense.

Taylor's choice of Vermont is significant. In the 1850s, Vermont was associated with the idealized yeoman farmer — independent, hardworking, egalitarian — that Jeffersonian democracy celebrated as the backbone of the republic. It was not the industrializing Northeast nor the slaveholding South but the virtuous middle: a landscape of small farms and self-governing towns.

### Vermont vs. England: The Implicit Comparison

| Dimension | Vermont (Asa's World) | England (Trenchard World) |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Economy | Earned through labor (dairying, farming) | Inherited and mortgaged |
| Social Structure | Egalitarian, merit-based | Hierarchical, birth-based |
| Speech | Plain, direct, vernacular | Coded, polite, evasive |
| Honor | Personal integrity | Family reputation |
| Relationship to Land | Worked with hands | Owned as status symbol |
| Future Orientation | Forward-looking, practical | Backward-looking, nostalgic |

This comparison is never stated explicitly — Vermont never appears on stage — but it is the implied alternative in every scene. When Asa succeeds where English characters fail, the play credits his Vermont formation, not his Trenchard blood.

### Key References

> "Asa: I know something about dairy work, and I can tell you that your method of setting milk is about as wasteful as a Vermont farmer's idea of a London dinner."
>
> — Act I, the dairy scene (006-040)

Asa's dairy knowledge — practical, technical, earned through experience — is the first demonstration that [[american-identity|American competence]] is not a comic affectation but a genuine skill set. The "Vermont farmer" is invoked as the standard against which English practice is found wanting.

> "Mary: I've listened to your stories until I'm half a backwoodsman's wife already."
>
> — Act I (007-139)

Mary's line reveals the persuasive power of Asa's Vermont identity. She has never been to America, yet his stories have made the "backwoods" feel like a real alternative to the manor she has always known. The line also carries romantic implication: becoming a "backwoodsman's wife" means choosing Asa's world over the English one.

> "Asa: Well, if a Yankee lawyer had done such a thing he would have Judge Lynch after him in no time."
>
> — Act I (005-148)(005-149)

The invocation of "Judge Lynch" — rough frontier justice — positions Vermont (and America more broadly) as a place where accountability is swift and personal, in contrast to the legal manipulations being practiced in the English manor.

## Historical Context

Vermont in the 1850s was a state of small farms, town meetings, and strong abolitionist sentiment. It had been the first state to abolish slavery in its constitution (1777), and its political culture emphasized local governance and individual independence. Taylor's audience would have recognized "Vermont" as shorthand for a particular kind of American — the yeoman democrat, self-sufficient and unimpressed by titles.

The choice of Vermont rather than, say, New York or Boston is telling. A New York Asa would have suggested urban sophistication; a Boston Asa would have suggested intellectual aspiration. A Vermont Asa suggests something more elemental: connection to land, competence with tools, indifference to fashion. These are the traits Taylor needs his American cousin to possess if he is to serve as a moral alternative to English corruption.
