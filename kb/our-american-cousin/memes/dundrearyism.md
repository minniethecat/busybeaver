# Dundrearyism

A "Dundrearyism" is a comic verbal mannerism characteristic of Lord Dundreary in Tom Taylor's *Our American Cousin* (1858): a mangled proverb, a confused word, or an absurd non-sequitur delivered with aristocratic self-assurance. The term entered the popular lexicon after the play's success, referring to any comically garbled aphorism of the "birds of a feather" variety.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

Dundreary's verbal blunders serve as the play's primary vehicle of character comedy. His malapropisms and logical non-sequiturs mark him as endearingly foolish despite his aristocratic station, creating an inversion of expected social hierarchies that aligns with the play's broader theme of American sincerity trumping British formality. The print edition's introduction acknowledges this directly: it notes that the play "contains very few 'Dundrearyisms' such as 'birds of a feat…'" [001-011]—a meta-commentary suggesting that the character's stage business, rather than scripted wordplay, was the primary engine of the comedy.

Dundreary's speech patterns also serve a structural function, providing comic relief that punctuates the play's melodramatic plot. His entrance lines often defuse tension: when Florence asks "Where's the joke?" Dundreary "screams and turns to Mrs. M." [002-004], converting verbal exchange into physical comedy.

### Associated Characters

Lord Dundreary's speech is most frequently received and commented upon by **Florence Trenchard**, who functions as his primary audience and provocateur—she prompts him ("Oh! let's hear Dundreary's idea" [002-202]) and judges his wit ("Ha! ha! Is that your own, Dundreary?" [002-036]). **Mrs. Mountchessington** treats his verbal mannerisms with aristocratic condescension ("Do you think so, Lord Dundreary?" [002-040]), while **Asa Trenchard** encounters Dundreary's speech as one more absurd feature of English society that his American plainness can puncture.

## Cultural/Historical Context

The term "Dundrearyism" represents a rare instance of a theatrical character's speech pattern entering the general language. Edward Askew Sothern's portrayal of Lord Dundreary—with his distinctive whiskers, languid physicality, and mangled proverbs—became a cultural phenomenon in late 1850s London and New York. The character's verbal tics were imitated in drawing rooms and music halls, and "Dundrearyism" joined "malapropism" (from Sheridan's Mrs. Malaprop, 1775) in the lexicon of comic speech disorders. Unlike Mrs. Malaprop's purely verbal blunders, Dundreary's comedy was inseparable from Sothern's physical performance—the sneezing, the whisker-twirling, the baffled pauses—making the printed text a partial record of a fundamentally theatrical phenomenon.
