# The Foreclosure Threat

The financial crisis of *Our American Cousin* arrives not as a dramatic revelation but as a business memorandum. Coyle, Sir Edward's agent, delivers the news with the flat affect of a functionary: "the mortgagees in fact, write here to announce their intentions to foreclose" (003-132)(003-133). The foreclosure threat is the play's economic engine — every subsequent courtship, scheme, and negotiation is conducted in its shadow.

## In the Play

### Context

The Trenchard family's financial position, never robust, has deteriorated to the point of crisis. Sir Edward's mortgages on the Ravensdale estate have fallen into arrears (003-101)(003-155). The creditors, no longer willing to wait, have issued formal notice of their intention to foreclose. The estate — the physical and social ground of every character's identity — is about to be taken.

Into this crisis walks Asa Trenchard, the American cousin whose inheritance claim represents the family's only hope — or, depending on one's calculations, an additional complication.

### What Happens

Coyle presents Sir Edward with the mortgages and the London creditors' letters. The language is legal and precise — "Get out those mortgages and the letters from my London" (003-101) — and the news is unambiguous: the estate will be foreclosed unless funds are found.

Coyle offers a solution that is also a threat: "By marrying your daughter to the mortgagee" (003-181). The suggestion makes explicit what the play has implied from the beginning — that in the Trenchard family's world, marriage is a financial instrument, and daughters are collateral.

### The Structural Function

The foreclosure threat operates as the play's dramatic clock. Every scene that follows is shadowed by the knowledge that time is running out. The Mountchessingtons' campaign to capture Asa's fortune, Florence's maneuvering between duty and desire, and Sir Edward's reluctant negotiations all take their urgency from the ticking clock that Coyle's memorandum has set.

The foreclosure also establishes Coyle's role as antagonist — not a villain in the melodramatic sense (he is, after all, simply doing his job), but the embodiment of a system in which debt governs relationships and marriage clears balance sheets.

### Consequences

The threat is ultimately neutralized not by Asa's inheritance but by his renunciation of it — a move that separates the American cousin from the financial calculus that has governed every other character's behavior. The foreclosure threat, then, is the negative space that defines Asa's eventual moral choice: to refuse to be the mortgagee's proxy.

## Key References

> "Get out those mortgages and the letters from my London"
>
> — Sir Edward Trenchard, Act I (003-101)

Sir Edward's instruction to Coyle opens the scene that will define the play's stakes. The mortgages are not abstract — they are physical documents, and producing them makes the crisis material.

> "the mortgagees in fact, write here to announce their intentions to foreclose."
>
> — Coyle, Act I (003-132)(003-133)

The sentence's bureaucratic rhythm — "in fact," "write here," "announce their intentions" — captures Coyle's method: he delivers catastrophe in the register of routine business correspondence.

> "By marrying your daughter to the mortgagee."
>
> — Coyle, Act I (003-181)

The play's most brutal line, and its clearest statement of theme. Marriage is a financial transaction; daughters are assets; the mortgagee can be paid in either currency.

## Historical Note

The 1850s saw a series of financial crises in Britain, including the panic of 1857 — the year before Taylor's play premiered. The foreclosure threat resonated with audiences who had witnessed or experienced the fragility of land-based wealth. Taylor's decision to make the crisis a matter of mortgages rather than gambling debts (the more conventional theatrical choice) grounds the play in the specific economic anxieties of its moment.
