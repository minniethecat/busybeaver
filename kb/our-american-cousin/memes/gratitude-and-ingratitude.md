# Gratitude and Ingratitude

## Overview

Gratitude functions as the moral glue of *Our American Cousin*'s social world—an expected return on generosity that binds giver and receiver in reciprocal obligation. When characters fail to express proper gratitude, the failure registers as a moral defect; when gratitude overflows, it sanctifies the transaction that called it forth. Taylor's handling of gratitude transforms a social convention into a dramatic principle, structuring the play's movement from financial crisis through generous renunciation to grateful resolution.

## Asa's Generosity and the Demand for Gratitude

Asa Trenchard's renunciation of his financial claim—tearing up the mortgage that would make him master of Trenchard Manor—is the play's supreme act of generosity. But generosity, in Taylor's moral universe, expects a return: gratitude. Sir Edward's eventual acknowledgment—"may Heaven bless you" [^1]—fulfills this expectation, transforming a financial transaction into a cycle of gift and thanks. Asa's generosity would be incomplete without the gratitude it elicits; the play's happy ending depends as much on the recipients' proper response as on the giver's initial act.

## Florence's Gratitude and Its Limits

Florence Trenchard's position as beneficiary of Asa's generosity is complicated by her romantic feelings. Her gratitude to Asa is genuine—"God bless you, Miss Florence" [^2] he tells her, and her response, though unspoken, registers the weight of what she owes him. Yet gratitude has limits: Florence cannot repay generosity with love, and the play's resolution requires that gratitude be directed toward Harry Vernon (the young man she actually loves) rather than Asa (the benefactor she merely thanks). The triangulation of gratitude, obligation, and romantic feeling is one of the play's subtler dramatic achievements.

## Mrs. Mountchessington's Ingratitude

Mrs. Mountchessington embodies the failure of gratitude. Her dismissal of Asa as a suitable match for her daughter—despite his evident generosity and good character—represents a refusal to recognize worth that does not come packaged in aristocratic form. Her "old woman of the world" [^3] persona treats social relations as transactions that require no thanks because they are merely exchanges of value. Taylor uses Mrs. Mountchessington's ingratitude to mark the moral poverty of worldly calculation.

## Mary Meredith's Thankful Heart

Mary Meredith, the poor relation, models proper gratitude. Her acknowledgment of her dependent position—"Poor Wickens is not the only one who thinks I am a very ill-used young body" [^4]—is tempered by an awareness of what she does have rather than what she lacks. When good fortune comes to her through the play's resolution, her gratitude reads as genuine rather than calculated, the natural response of a character who has learned to value what she receives.

## The Cascade of Blessings

The play's final act features a remarkable concentration of blessing-language: "God bless you" [^2], "bless you, my children—bless you" [^5], "may Heaven bless you" [^1], "think of your poor mamma, bless you" [^6]. These blessings function as gratitude made performative—words that do not merely express thanks but actively confer benefit on their recipients. The cascade of blessings transforms the play's financial logic into a spiritual economy in which gratitude is the currency and blessing the transaction.

## Ingratitude as Moral Failure

Taylor presents ingratitude not as a minor social failing but as a symptom of deeper moral corruption. Characters who cannot feel or express gratitude—Mrs. Mountchessington, the calculating Coyle in his more mercenary moments—reveal through this incapacity their failure to recognize the bonds of obligation that constitute a moral community. Gratitude, in Taylor's vision, is not optional; it is the minimum requirement for membership in the social world the play affirms.
