# Mr. Binny

Mr. Binny is the butler at Trenchard Manor, a comic servant character in *Our American Cousin* whose self-importance and distinctive speech patterns provide running commentary on the household's social pretensions.

## In the Play

Binny enters early in Act I carrying a "green baize bag" [001-063] — the traditional butler's accessory for delivering bills and correspondence — and immediately establishes his character: "Mind your hown business instead of mindin' mine" [001-053], he tells a fellow servant, asserting a status he believes self-evident. His response to a bill delivery — "Hah! bill, of course, Miss Augusta" [001-065] — reveals his familiarity with the family's financial embarrassments, the butler's privileged position of knowing everyone's secrets.

Binny serves as Lord Dundreary's "hown man" [004-099] and introduces Buddicombe with the theatrical summons "Turkey cock number one" [004-100] — a comic flourish that suggests Binny sees himself as stage-manager of the household's entertainments. The actor Peters, who originated the role, played the part with the comic combination of dignity and absurdity that Victorian audiences expected from stage butlers.

## Character Analysis

Binny's most distinctive trait is his dialect — "hown" for "own," "mindin'" for "minding" — which marks him as Cockney or a related London working-class accent. In the Victorian theater's rigid class-coding, a servant's accent immediately signaled their position in the social hierarchy. Binny's pretensions to authority, combined with his unmistakably working-class speech, create the comic tension that defines his scenes. He is a type — the pompous upper servant — but Taylor gives him enough stage business (the green baize bag, the turkey-cock summons) that he registers as an individual comic presence rather than a mere plot function.
