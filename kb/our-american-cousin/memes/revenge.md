# Revenge

The destructive passion that drives the play's antagonist and provides the moral counterpoint to Asa's generosity. Richard Coyle's schemes are not merely mercenary — they are fueled by a cold desire to settle scores, and the play's resolution depends on breaking the cycle of retribution that revenge perpetuates.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

Coyle's vendetta against the Trenchard family predates the events of the play. His father's role in the original mortgage transaction — and the convenient absence of the release document — hints at a generational grievance (003-157)(003-158). Coyle's determination to foreclose, to force Florence into marriage, and to humiliate Sir Edward is driven by something beyond mere profit: "there, you dog, will not that be a rare revenge" (004-037).

The play resolves this thread not by satisfying Coyle's revenge but by rendering it irrelevant. Asa's renunciation of the fortune removes the object of contention, and Coyle's schemes collapse not because they are punished but because they are outflanked by generosity.

### Associated Characters

- **Richard Coyle** — revenge personified as legal procedure. His schemes use the forms of law — mortgages, foreclosures, marriage settlements — but their emotional fuel is personal animus. The gap between his legal precision and his vengeful motivation is the character's defining tension.

- **Sir Edward Trenchard** — the object of Coyle's revenge. Whatever the original injury (hinted at but never fully specified), Sir Edward's financial vulnerability makes him Coyle's target, and his aristocratic pride prevents him from seeking help until it is almost too late.

- **Asa Trenchard** — the antidote to revenge. His refusal to retaliate against those who have slighted him — Mrs. Mountchessington's condescension, Augustus's opportunism — demonstrates that revenge is a choice, not a necessity.

### Key References

> "There, you dog, will not that be a rare revenge."
>
> — Act I (004-037)

Coyle's soliloquy is the play's most explicit statement of vengeful motivation. The epithet "you dog" — addressed to the absent Sir Edward — strips away the legal propriety and reveals the raw animus beneath.

> "You can but find that release, we may unmask this diabolical fiend."
>
> — Act I (005-150)

The word "diabolical" elevates Coyle from creditor to villain, and the language of "unmasking" frames the resolution as a exposure of true character — not merely a financial settlement.

## Cultural and Historical Context

Revenge was a contested moral category in Victorian England. The Christian ethic of forgiveness (turn the other cheek) coexisted uneasily with a legal system that was itself retributive in character and a popular culture that celebrated avengers in melodrama and sensation fiction. Taylor's play leans toward the Christian-comic resolution: revenge is not defeated by counter-revenge but rendered obsolete by generosity.

The American dimension is again significant. Asa's frontier ethos — rough justice, plain dealing — presents an alternative to the European cycle of grievance and retribution. The "Yankee" who refuses to hold a grudge is Taylor's model of moral modernity.
