# The Matchmaking Proposal

The matchmaking scene is *Our American Cousin* at its most acid. Mrs. Mountchessington, having assessed Asa Trenchard as "the better match of the two" (004-156) compared to Lieutenant De Boots, deploys her daughter Augusta as bait in an archery-themed seduction that fails on every level — strategic, performative, and linguistic. The scene is a masterclass in dramatic irony: the audience watches Mrs. Mountchessington scheme, watches Asa perceive the scheme, and watches Augusta suffer through the scheme, all while the three characters occupy entirely different understandings of what is happening.

## In the Play

### Context

The Mountchessington family has attached itself to the Trenchard household on the strength of Asa's presumed inheritance. Mrs. Mountchessington, a widow with two marriageable daughters and no visible income, treats the marriage market as a professional arena. The arrival of the American heir — whose fortune, whatever its exact dimensions, is certainly larger than De Boots's "excellent" expectations (004-155) — represents an opportunity that must be pursued with strategic precision.

The scene takes place before an archery meeting, a setting that literalizes the metaphor: Mrs. Mountchessington is taking aim at a target, and she has dressed her daughter for the occasion.

### Mrs. Mountchessington's Strategy

Mrs. Mountchessington's method is research-based matchmaking. She has prepared: "I think if you read up Sam Slick a little, it might be useful, and just dip into Bancroft's History of the United States, or some of Russell's Letters; you should know something of George Washington, of whom the Americans are justly proud" (004-165)(004-167). The reading list — a comic period-piece of mid-19th-century Americana — is also a devastating index of Mrs. Mountchessington's worldview. Americans are a subject to be studied, like archery or French, and the right references will unlock the right responses.

Augusta, for her part, is a reluctant instrument: "I am so tired, ma, of admiring things I hate" (004-160). The line is the play's most economical critique of the marriage market — a young woman's exhaustion converted, through Taylor's dialogue, into something close to protest. Mrs. Mountchessington's reply is the market's logic in four words: "we must all make sacrifices to society" (004-161).

### Asa's Counter-Strategy

Asa enters "in Archery Dress" (004-172), already visually marked as an outsider performing a ritual he does not understand. The Mountchessington women open with flattery calibrated to American self-regard — the "boundless prairie," the buffalo hunt — and Asa responds with a flat Vermont correction: "Buffaloes down in Vermont" (004-186, aside).

The scene's decisive turn comes when Asa names the game: "Yes, we're 'cute, we are; know soft solder when we see it" (004-185). "Soft solder" — a metaphor from metalworking for a low-melting-point alloy used to join surfaces — is Asa's term for flattery, and his recognition of it dissolves the Mountchessington strategy. Augusta's aside — "Ma, I do believe he's laughing at us" (004-183) — confirms what the audience already knows: the hunter has become the hunted, and the American savage has better social perception than the English aristocrats.

### The Exit

Mrs. Mountchessington, routed but refusing to acknowledge it, retreats into French: "Au revoir, Mr. Trenchard" (004-192). Asa's reply — "Which?" (004-193) — is either genuine incomprehension or weaponized literalism. When she repeats "Au revoir," his response — "No, thank you, don't take any before dinner" (004-195, paraphrased) — closes the scene on a note of pure comic deflation. The Mountchessington campaign has been conducted entirely in a language (social, if not literal French) that Asa refuses to learn.

| Flattery | Buffalo, prairie, the "boundless" West | All three | Ironic, layered |
| Exposure | Asa names the game: "soft solder" | Asa, Augusta (aside) | Turning point |
| Retreat | Mrs. M exits in French; Asa mishears | Mrs. M, Augusta, Asa | Pure farce |

### Consequences

The matchmaking scene establishes that Asa cannot be captured by the English marriage market. His recognition of "soft solder" is the play's assertion of American perception as a form of immunity: he sees the scheme because he is not invested in the social system that makes the scheme legible as normal behavior. The scene also dooms the Mountchessington subplot: from this point forward, Mrs. Mountchessington's machinations are comic background noise, not genuine threats.

The archery setting pays off in the finale, where Asa's final "Wal, I won't, if you don't want me to" (010-131 et seq.) completes the work this scene began — defusing every Mountchessington secret with the same laconic grace that defused the archery-field seduction.

## Key References

> "I rather think he will prove the better match of the two, if what I hear of Mark Trenchard's property be correct."
>
> — Mrs. Mountchessington, Act II (004-156)

The arithmetic of the marriage market, stated without embarrassment. The "two" are Asa and De Boots; the calculation is purely financial.

> "I am so tired, ma, of admiring things I hate."
>
> — Augusta Mountchessington, Act II (004-160)

Taylor gives his most cynical character's daughter the play's most honest line. Augusta's exhaustion is the human cost of Mrs. Mountchessington's strategy — and, by extension, of the system that strategy serves.

> "we must all make sacrifices to society."
>
> — Mrs. Mountchessington, Act II (004-161)

The marriage market's motto, delivered with the serene confidence of someone who has made her peace with it. The line is chilling precisely because Mrs. Mountchessington means it as comfort.

> "Yes, we're 'cute, we are; know soft solder when we see it."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act II (004-185)

The scene's climax in five words. "Soft solder" is Asa's metaphor for flattery, and his recognition of it transforms the scene from seduction to exposure. The American is not the buffalo; he is the hunter — or, more precisely, the metallurgist who can tell real metal from the alloy.

## Historical Note

The "American Studies" reading list that Mrs. Mountchessington prescribes — Sam Slick (Thomas Chandler Haliburton's Yankee clockmaker), George Bancroft's *History of the United States*, William Howard Russell's *Letters* — is a precisely observed 1850s bibliography. Haliburton's Sam Slick stories were enormously popular in both America and Britain; Bancroft's multi-volume history was the standard American historical work of the period; and Russell's letters for *The Times* had made him the most famous foreign correspondent in the English-speaking world. Mrs. Mountchessington has done her homework. That it does her no good at all is Taylor's point: Americans cannot be managed through books.
