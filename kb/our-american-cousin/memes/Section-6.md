(006-001) John [R. C.] Anything more, Miss [[mary-meredith|Mary]]?

(006-002) Mary No, thank you, Wickens!

(006-003) John [Going.] Good morning, Miss Mary.

(006-004) Mary Good morning, Wickens.

(006-005) John [Returning.] Is there anything I can get for you, Miss Mary?

(006-006) Mary [Spinning.] Nothing, thank you.

(006-007) John Dang me if I wouldn't like to stop all day, and watch her pretty

(006-008) figure and run errands for her. [Exit R. 3 E., crosses behind fence.]

(006-009) Mary Poor Wickens is not the only one who thinks I am a very ill-used

(006-010) young body. Now I don't think so. Grandfather was rich, but he must

(006-011) have had a bad heart, or he never could have cast off poor mamma; had he

(006-012) adopted me, I should never have been so happy as I am now, uncle is kind

(006-013) to me in his pompous, patronizing way, and dear Florence loves me like a

(006-014) sister, and so I am happy. I am my own mistress here, and not anybody's

(006-015) humble servant, I sometimes find myself singing as the birds do, because

(006-016) I can't help it [Song, ``Maid with the milking pail,'' can be introduced

(006-017) here.]

(006-018) Enter Florence and Asa through gate, R. 3 E.

(006-019) [[florence-trenchard|Flo]] Come along, cousin, come along. I want to introduce you to my little

(006-020) cousin. [Kisses Mary.] I've brought you a visitor, Miss Mary Meredith,

(006-021) Mr. [[asa-trenchard|Asa Trenchard]], our American cousin. [They shake hands.] That will do

(006-022) for the present. This young gentleman has carried off the prize by three

(006-023) successive shots in the bull's eye.

(006-024) Mary I congratulate you, sir, and am happy to see you.

(006-025) Asa [Shakes hands again.] Thank you, Miss.

(006-026) Flo That will do for a beginning.

(006-027) Asa [ Aside.] And so that is Mark Trenchard's grandchild.

(006-028) Mary Why have you left the archery, Florence?

(006-029) Flo Because, after Mr. Asa's display, I felt in no humor for shooting,

(006-030) and I have some very grave business with my cousin here.

(006-031) Mary You? Grave business? Why I thought you never had any graver

(006-032) business than being very pretty, very amiable, and very ready to be

(006-033) amused.

(006-034) Asa Wal, Miss, I guess the first comes natural round these diggins.

(006-035) [Bows.]

(006-036) Mary You are very polite. This is my domain, sir, and I shall be happy

(006-037) to show you, that is, if you understand anything about a [[dairy-farming|dairy]].

(006-038) Flo Yes, by the way, do you understand anything about dairies in

(006-039) America?

(006-040) Asa Wal, I guess I do know something about cow juice. [They turn to

(006-041) smother laugh.] Why, if it ain't all as bright and clean as a fresh

(006-042) washed shirt just off the clover, and is this all your doin's, Miss?

(006-043) Mary Yes, sir, I milk the cows, set up the milk, superintend the

(006-044) churning and make the cheese.

(006-045) Asa Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've

(006-046) seen on this side the pond.

(006-047) Flo What's that, sir? Do you want to make me jealous?

(006-048) Asa Oh, no, you needn't get your back up, you are the right sort too,

(006-049) but you must own you're small potatoes, and few in a hill compared to a

(006-050) gal like that.

(006-051) Flo I'm what?

(006-052) Asa Small potatoes.

(006-053) Flo Will you be kind enough to translate that for me, for I don't

(006-054) understand American yet.

(006-055) Asa Yes, I'll put it in French for you, ``petite pommes des terres.''

(006-056) Flo Ah, it's very clear now; but, cousin, do tell me what you mean by

(006-057) calling me small potatoes.

(006-058) Asa Wal, you can sing and paint, and play on the pianner, and in your

(006-059) own particular circle you are some pumpkins.

(006-060) Flo Some pumpkins, first I am small potatoes, and now I'm some pumpkins.

(006-061) Asa But she, she can milk cows, set up the butter, make cheese,

(006-062) and, darn me, if them ain't what I call raal downright feminine

(006-063) accomplishments.

(006-064) Flo I do believe you are right cousin, so Mary do allow me to

(006-065) congratulate you upon not being small potatoes.

(006-066) Mary Well, I must look to my dairy or all my last week's milk will

(006-067) be spoiled. Good bye, Florence, dear. Good bye, Mr. Trenchard. Good

(006-068) morning, sir. [Exit into Cottage.]

(006-069) Asa [Following her to door.] Good morning, Miss. I'll call again.

(006-070) Flo Well, cousin, what do you think of her?

(006-071) Asa Ain't she a regular snorter?

(006-072) Flo A what?

(006-073) Asa Wal, perhaps I should make myself more intelligable, if I said, a

(006-074) squeeler, and to think I'm keepin' that everlasting angel of a gal out

(006-075) of her [[inheritance|fortune]] all along of this bit of paper here.

(006-076) Flo What is that? [Takes paper from pocket.]

(006-077) Asa Old Mark Trenchard's will.

(006-078) Flo Don't show it to me, I don't want to look at it, the [[fortune-and-wealth|fortune]] should

(006-079) have come to Mary, she is the only relation in the direct line.

(006-080) Asa Say, cousin, you've not told her that darned property was left to

(006-081) me, have you?

(006-082) Flo Do you think I had the heart to tell her of her misfortune?

(006-083) Asa Wal, darn me, if you didn't show your good sense at any rate. [Goes

(006-084) up to dairy.]

(006-085) Flo Well, what are you doing, showing _your_ good sense?

(006-086) Asa Oh, you go long.

(006-087) Flo Say, cousin, I guess I've got you on a string now, as I heard you

(006-088) say this morning.

(006-089) Asa Wal, what if you have, didn't I see you casting sheep's eyes at that

(006-090) sailor man this morning? Ah, I reckon I've got you on a string now. Say,

(006-091) has he got that ship yet?

(006-092) Flo No, he hasn't, though I've used all my powers of persuasion with

(006-093) that [[lord-dundreary|Lord Dundreary]], and his father has so much influence with the

(006-094) admiralty.

(006-095) Asa Wal, din't he drop like a smoked possum?

(006-096) Flo There you go, more American. No, he said he was very sorry, but he

(006-097) couldn't.

(006-098) Asa [Taking bottle out.] Oh, he did, did he? Wal, I guess he'll do his

(006-099) best all the same.

(006-100) Flo I shall be missed at the archery grounds. Will you take me back?

(006-101) Asa Like a streak of lightning. [Offers arm and takes her to dairy.]

(006-102) Flo That's not the way.

(006-103) Asa No, of course not. [Takes her round stage back to dairy.]

(006-104) Flo Well, but where are you going now?

(006-105) Asa I was just going round. I say, cousin, don't you think you could

(006-106) find your way back alone.

(006-107) Flo Why, what do _you_ want to do?

(006-108) Asa Wal, I just wanted to see how they make cheese is this darned

(006-109) country. [Exits into dairy.]

(006-110) Flo [Laughing.] And they call that man a savage; well, I only wish we

(006-111) had a few more such savages in [[england|England]].

(006-112) Dun [Without, R. 2 E.] This way, lovely sufferer.

(006-113) Flo Ah, here's Dundreary.

(006-114) [Dundreary enters with Georgina, places her in rustic chair, R.]

(006-115) Dun There, repothe yourself.

(006-116) Geo Thank you, my lord; you are so kind to me, and I am so delicate.

(006-117) Flo Yes, you look delicate, dear; how is she this morning any better?

(006-118) Dun When she recovers, she'll be better.

(006-119) Flo I'm afraid you don't take good care of her, you are so rough.

(006-120) Dun No, I'm not wruff, either. [Sings.] I'm gentle and I'm kind,

(006-121) I'm ---- I forget the rest

(006-122) Flo Well, good morning, dear--do take care of her--good day, Dundreary.

(006-123) [Exit through gate.]

(006-124) Dun Now, let me administer to your wants. How would you like a roast

(006-125) chestnut?

(006-126) Geo No, my lord, I'm too delicate.

(006-127) Dun Well, then, a peanut; there is a great deal of nourishment in

(006-128) peanuts.

(006-129) Geo No, thank you.

(006-130) Dun Then what can I do for you?

(006-131) Geo If you please, ask the dairy maid to let me have a seat in the

(006-132) dairy. I am afraid of the draft, here.

(006-133) Dun Oh! you want to get out of the draft, do you? Well, you're not the

(006-134) only one that wants to escape the draft. Is that the dairy on top of

(006-135) that stick? [Points to pigeon house.]

(006-136) Geo No, my lord, that's the pigeon house.

(006-137) Dun What do they keep in pigeon houses? Oh! pigeons, to be sure;

(006-138) they couldn't keep donkeys up there, could they? That's the dairy, I

(006-139) suppothe?

(006-140) Geo Yes, my lord.

(006-141) Dun What do they keep in dairies?

(006-142) Geo Eggs, milk, butter and cheese.

(006-143) Dun What's the name of that animal with a head on it? No, I don't

(006-144) mean that, all animals have heads. I mean those animals with something

(006-145) growing out of their heads.

(006-146) Geo A cow?

(006-147) Dun A cow growing out of his head?

(006-148) Geo No, no, horns.

(006-149) Dun A cow! well, that accounts for the milk and butter; but I don't see

(006-150) the eggs; cows don't give eggs; then there's the cheese--do you like

(006-151) cheese?

(006-152) Geo No, my lord.

(006-153) Dun Does your brother like cheese?

(006-154) Geo I have no brother. I'm so delicate.

(006-155) Dun She's so delicate, she hasn't got a brother. Well, if you had a

(006-156) brother do you think he'd like cheese?

(006-157) Geo I don't know; do please take me to the dairy.

(006-158) Dun Well, I will see if I can get you a broiled sardine. [Exit into

(006-159) dairy.]

(006-160) Geo [Jumps up.] Oh! I'm so glad he's gone. I am so dreadful hungry. I

(006-161) should like a plate of corn beef and cabbage, eggs and bacon, or a slice

(006-162) of cold ham and pickles.

(006-163) Dun [Outside] Thank you, thank you.

(006-164) Geo [Running back to seat.] Here he comes. Oh! I am so delicate.

(006-165) Enter Dundreary.

(006-166) Dun I beg you pardon, Miss Georgina, but I find upon enquiry that cows

(006-167) don't give sardines. But I've arranged it with the dairy maid so that

(006-168) you can have a seat by the window that overlooks the cow house and the

(006-169) pig sty, and all the pretty things.

(006-170) Geo I'm afraid I'm very troublesome.

(006-171) Dun Yes, you're very troublesome, you are. No, I mean you're a lovely

(006-172) sufferer, that's the idea. [They go up to cottage door.]

(006-173) Enter Asa, running against Dundreary.

(006-174) Dun There's that damned rhinoceros again. [Exit into cottage, with

(006-175) Georgina.]

(006-176) Asa There goes that benighted aristocrat and that little toad of a sick

(006-177) gal. [Looks off.] There he's a settling her in a chair and covering her

(006-178) all over with shawls. Ah! it's a caution, how these women do fix our

(006-179) flint for us. Here he comes. [Takes out bottle.] How are you, hair dye.

(006-180) [Goes behind dairy.]

(006-181) Enter Dundreary.

(006-182) Dun That lovely Georgina puts me in mind of that beautiful piece of

(006-183) poetry. Let me see how it goes. The rose is red, the violet's blue. [Asa

(006-184) tips his hat over his eyes.]

(006-185) Dun [Repeats.]

(006-186) Asa [Repeats business.]

(006-187) Dun [Comes down, takes off hat, looking in it.] There must be something

(006-188) alive in that hat. [Goes up, and commences again.] The rose is red, the

(006-189) violet's blue, sugar is sweet, and so is somebody, and so is somebody

(006-190) else.

(006-191) Asa puts yoke on Dundreary's shoulders gently. Dundreary comes down with

(006-192) pails.

(006-193) Dun I wonder what the devil that is? [Lowers one, then the other, they

(006-194) trip him up.] Oh, I see, somebody has been fishing and caught a pail.

(006-195) [Goes hopping up stage, stumbling over against spinning wheel. Looks at

(006-196) yarn on stick.] Why, what a little old man. [Sees Asa.] Say, Mr. Exile,

(006-197) what the devil is this?

(006-198) Asa That is a steam engine, and will bust in about a minute.

(006-199) Dun Well, I haven't a minute to spare, so I'll not wait till it busts.

(006-200) [Crosses to R., knocks against private box, R. H., apologizes.]

(006-201) Asa Say, whiskers, I want to ask a favor of you.

(006-202) Dun [Attempts to sneeze.] Now I've got it.

(006-203) Asa Wal, but say. [Dundreary's sneezing bus.]

(006-204) Asa [Takes his hand.] How are you. [Squeezes it.]

(006-205) Dun There, you've spoiled it.

(006-206) Asa Spoiled what?

(006-207) Dun Spoiled what! why a magnificent sneeze.

(006-208) Asa Oh! was that what you was trying to get through you?

(006-209) Dun Get through me: he's mad.

(006-210) Asa Wal, now, the naked truth is--[Leans arm on Dundreary's shoulder.

(006-211) Bus. by Dundreary.] Oh, come now, don't be putting on airs. Say, do you

(006-212) know Lieut. Vernon?
