## In the Play

The question of where one belongs — to a family, a country, a class, a home — runs through *Our American Cousin* as its deepest thematic current. The play's title itself announces the theme: "cousin" is a category of belonging, and "American" marks a distance from it. Every major character is negotiating their place in a social order that is uncertain about whether they fit.

### Asa's Double Belonging

Asa Trenchard arrives at Trenchard Manor as both insider and outsider. He is a Trenchard by blood — "born in Vermont, suckled on the banks of Muddy Creek" [002-043] — and therefore belongs in the family by inheritance. But he is also the "American cousin," whose habits, speech, and manners make him alien to the English drawing room. Florence's description of him as 17 feet high — "They are all 17 feet high in America" [002-095] — is a comic inflation that captures a real point: Asa does not fit in the frame.

The letter that introduces Asa traces his family's trajectory: the branch that "emigrated to America at the Restoration" [001-157] has now produced an heir who returns to claim the English inheritance. This circular movement — exile and return — structures the play's emotional geography. Asa belongs in both places and fully in neither: he is too American for England, and by the play's end he will have made himself too English (too generous, too honorable) for the caricature of the grasping Yankee.

### Mary Meredith's Precarious Position

Mary Meredith occupies the play's most unstable position. She is Sir Edward's niece but is forbidden the title of cousin: "how often must I request you not to speak of Mary Meredith as your cousin?" [001-103]. She presides over the dairy "like a duchess playing dairymaid" [001-103], inhabiting a social role that is neither servant nor family member. She belongs to the estate without belonging to the family, a distinction that the play's inheritance plot makes brutally material: she is the "poor cousin" whose grandmother's guineas have been taken.

Florence's loyalty to Mary — "I hate him, whatever he is, coming here to rob poor cousin Mary" [001-103] — is the play's clearest statement about the injustice of Mary's displacement. But Mary's displacement is not only economic. It is also emotional and social: she has been made a stranger in the house where she lives, a condition the play diagnoses without fully resolving until Asa's self-sacrifice at the end.

### The Old Country and the New

The transatlantic geography of belonging is explored most fully in the Act 2 dairy scene. Mary asks Asa to "tell me more about your home in America" and confesses: "I've listened to your stories until I'm half a backwoodsman's wife already" [007-043]. Asa's aside — "Wouldn't I like to make her a whole one" [007-044] — translates the theme of belonging into the language of courtship. To marry Asa is to choose a home in the backwoods; to belong to him is to leave England.

Mary's imaginative reconstruction of Asa's Vermont home — "I can shut my eyes and almost fancy I see your home in the backwoods. There are your two sisters running about" [007-043] — performs the emotional work of the play: making a foreign place feel like home through the act of sympathetic imagination. The catalogue of details — "the smoke curling from the chimney," "men and boys working in the fields," "the girls milking the cows" [007-043] — domesticates the American wilderness into a pastoral ideal.

### Home as Comic Motif

The language of home also serves comic functions. Asa's threat to tell Mary something that would make her "shut up school, and send me home feeling about as small as a tadpole" [007-054] uses the vocabulary of homecoming to express romantic anxiety. Coyle's drunken song — "We won't go home till morning" [009-092] — and Asa's rejoinder "I don't think you'll go home at all" [009-092] pun on the double meaning of home as both destination and final state.

Dundreary's repeated reference to Asa as "illustrious exile" [003-076, 006-068, 010-072] is a running joke that encodes a serious observation: the American cousin is in exile from his own country, an honorary foreigner whose foreignness is both his comic identity and, ultimately, his moral advantage.

## Cultural-Historical Context

The theme of transatlantic belonging in *Our American Cousin* reflects the mid-19th-century reality of Anglo-American migration and return. The "American cousin" who arrives to claim an English inheritance was a recognizable figure — not uncommon enough to be implausible, but unusual enough to be theatrically interesting. The Restoration emigration that Taylor assigns to the Trenchard family's American branch (the 1660s) places the family's transatlantic history within a specific historical frame: the political upheavals of the English Civil War and Restoration sent many English families across the Atlantic.

The play's treatment of Mary's displacement also reflects Victorian anxieties about the "poor relation" — the genteel woman of good family who has been dispossessed and must work for her living. This figure appears throughout Victorian fiction (from Jane Eyre to Becky Sharp) and represents a fault line in the ideology of class: birth and breeding are supposed to guarantee status, but without money they guarantee nothing.
