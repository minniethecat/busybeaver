# Sincerity vs. Pretension

The master opposition of the play: every character can be located on a spectrum from transparent honesty to calculated deception. Taylor's comic method is to place these characters in the same drawing room and let the collisions generate both the laughter and the moral argument. The sincere characters — Asa, Mary, eventually Florence — are vindicated; the pretenders are exposed and humiliated.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The inheritance crisis creates a laboratory for testing sincerity. When a fortune is at stake, who will tell the truth? Asa's refusal to conceal his lack of inheritance — "Oh, no" (008-093) — is the simplest and most consequential act of honesty in the play. It costs him Mrs. Mountchessington's favor, Augustus's friendship, and his social standing — and it is precisely this cost that proves its value.

The pretenders, by contrast, are undone by the complexity of their own schemes. Richard Coyle's legal machinations, Mrs. Mountchessington's social calculations, and Augustus's romantic performances all collapse under the weight of their internal contradictions. The play's moral physics is Newtonian: pretension, being heavier than sincerity, eventually falls.

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — sincerity embodied. His American idiom — "small potatoes" (006-055), "cow juice" (006-040), "darn me" (006-045) — is not vulgarity but the linguistic form of his refusal to pretend. He says what he means in the language he has, and the comedy arises from the collision between this language and the drawing room's code of indirection.

- **Mrs. Mountchessington** — pretension as a way of life. Her every utterance is a tactical calculation, her every compliment a valuation. She is the play's most skilled practitioner of the social lie, and her eventual humiliation is proportionate to her skill.

- **Richard Coyle** — pretension weaponized. The solicitor who uses legal forms to disguise moral theft, he represents the institutionalization of dishonesty. His schemes require the complicity of a system that values form over substance.

- **Mary Meredith** — sincerity without self-consciousness. Unlike Asa, whose honesty is a deliberate stance, Mary simply is what she is: a dairy worker who does her work well. Her reward — the fortune Asa renounces — is the play's most explicit argument that merit should receive its due.

- **Augustus Trenchard** — pretension without competence. His romantic posturing is so transparent that even his mother must coach him, and his collapse at the news of Asa's lost fortune (008-094) reveals that the performance had no substance behind it.

### Key References

> "Asa: Oh, no."
>
> — Act III (008-093)

Two syllables that function as the play's moral hinge. Asa's simple negation — he is *not* the heir — is delivered without drama, without self-pity, without calculation. In a play full of elaborate speeches, the most consequential utterance is also the briefest.

> "Asa: Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've seen on this side of the big pond."
>
> — Act I (006-045)

Asa's compliment to Mary is the play's definition of sincerity in action: he praises what he genuinely values (usefulness) rather than what convention dictates (beauty, grace, title). The comic register — "darn me," "raal," "big pond" — is integral to the sincerity: he will not translate his values into the language of pretense.

> "Flo: There you go, more American. No, he said he was very sorry, but he couldn't."
>
> — Act I (006-096)(006-097)

Florence's exasperated "more American" treats sincerity as a national characteristic — something foreign to the English drawing room. Her translation of "dropped like a smoked possum" into polite language performs the very code-switching that Asa refuses.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The sincerity/pretension opposition draws on a deep tradition in Anglo-American letters, from the Augustan satire of the 18th century to the Victorian "earnestness" that would receive its definitive treatment in Wilde's *The Importance of Being Earnest* (1895). Taylor's play belongs to the earlier phase of this tradition: sincerity is still possible, still legible, still capable of triumphing over its opposite.

The American inflection Taylor gives this theme reflects the transatlantic cultural politics of the 1850s. American manners were widely characterized in British discourse as "plain" or "rude" — but Taylor's innovation is to recode plainness as honesty and politeness as deceit. This reversal would become a staple of American self-representation for the next century and a half.
