# Reading and Literacy

## Overview

The world of *Our American Cousin* is saturated with acts of reading and writing. Letters arrive bearing crucial plot information; characters read faces as they would read texts; and the play's own existence as a written document—"evolved over the course of time, indeed in multiple directions" [^1]—foregrounds questions of textuality and interpretation. In a society where literacy was a marker of class and gender distinction, Taylor's treatment of reading practices illuminates the distribution of knowledge and power among his characters.

## Letters as Plot Mechanism

Letters function as the play's primary mechanism for conveying information across space and time. The mortgages and letters from the London agents that Coyle manages represent the financial textuality that drives the plot. "Here's your young man's letter" [^2]—the delivery of a letter from Harry Vernon to Florence—stages the private communication that sustains the romantic subplot. Each letter in the play is both a material object (handled, delivered, read aloud) and a bearer of meaning that transforms the situation of its recipient.

## Florence's Faith in Faces

Florence Trenchard's declaration—"Papa, I have implicit faith in my own judgement of faces" [^3]—treats physiognomy as a form of reading. The face, for Florence, is a text whose meanings she can decipher with greater accuracy than her father can read financial documents. This gendered distribution of interpretive authority—women read people, men read papers—structures the play's epistemology. Florence's confidence in face-reading ultimately proves more reliable than Sir Edward's confidence in financial management.

## The "Blessed Budget" of Documents

Asa's receipt of the mortgage documents is staged as a material transaction: "[Takes them.] Ah, blessed budget that descends" [^4]. The "budget" here is a physical bundle of papers, a portable archive of financial obligation whose materiality is emphasized by the stage direction. Asa's handling of these documents—he will later tear them up—transforms paper from instrument of power to symbol of renunciation. The play's attention to the physicality of documents reminds us that reading is always an embodied practice, a matter of hands as well as eyes.

## Mary Meredith's Ill-Use in Letters

Mary Meredith's complaint—"Poor Wickens is not the only one who thinks I am a very ill-used young body" [^5]—references the circulation of opinion through written and spoken report. The phrase "thinks I am" positions Mary as the subject of others' textual and verbal representations, a character written about rather than writing. Her ill-use is partly a matter of literacy politics: as a dependent without independent means, she lacks the authority to write her own social script.

## The Play as Evolving Text

The editorial apparatus notes that *Our American Cousin* "evolved over the course of time, indeed in multiple directions" [^1], acknowledging that the play exists not as a single authoritative text but as a series of performance variants and printed editions. This meta-textual awareness—the play's consciousness of its own textual instability—complicates any reading that treats the script as a fixed object. Taylor's play is, in part, about the impossibility of fixing a dramatic text, the way performance continually rewrites what writing has set down.

## Asa's Orality vs. English Literacy

Asa's American speech patterns—"Wal," "darn me," "I reckon"—mark him as an oral culture figure entering a world of written instruments and documentary authority. His ability to navigate both modes—he can read the mortgages, but also speak the direct language of feeling—positions him as a bridge between the oral culture of the American backwoods and the textual culture of the English manor. The play's resolution, in which Asa's spoken generosity overrides the written mortgage, privileges orality over textuality at the moment of moral climax.
