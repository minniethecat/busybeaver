# Laura Keene's Theatre

The New York theatre at 622 Broadway where [[our-american-cousin-play|"Our American Cousin"]] premiered on October 15, 1858. Managed by Laura Keene (1826–1873), one of the first successful female theatre managers in America, the venue was among the most important sites of mid-19th-century American theatrical production. The play's premiere at Keene's theatre marked the beginning of its extraordinary popularity — the original production ran for 150 nights, an exceptional achievement for the period.

## In the Play

### Role

Laura Keene's Theatre appears in the play only by reference — the opening credits of the published text name it as the venue of the original production (001-016). Laura Keene herself originated the role of Florence Trenchard (001-028), and her performance established the template for the character's blend of playful curiosity and romantic sincerity.

The theatre's significance to the play is therefore contextual rather than textual: Keene's decision to produce Taylor's comedy, her casting of E. A. Sothern as Lord Dundreary (a role Sothern would transform from a minor part into the play's signature character), and the venue's reputation as a home for fashionable New York entertainment all shaped the conditions under which "Our American Cousin" became a landmark of American theatre.

### Associated Characters

- **Laura Keene** — actress-manager who originated the role of Florence Trenchard. Her business acumen and artistic judgment made the production possible.
- **E. A. Sothern** — the actor who created Lord Dundreary at Keene's theatre, transforming a minor role of 47 lines into the most celebrated comic performance of the 1850s American stage.
- **Florence Trenchard** — the role originated by Keene herself, establishing a connection between the female lead and female management that was unusual for the period.

## Historical Context

Laura Keene's Theatre opened in 1856 at 622 Broadway, near the intersection of Broadway and Houston Street, in what was then New York's theatre district. Keene, an English-born actress who had emigrated to America in 1852, was one of a small number of women who managed their own theatrical companies in the antebellum period. Her theatre was known for its strong company, its fashionable audience, and its repertoire mixing English imports with American works.

The premiere of "Our American Cousin" on October 15, 1858, was a calculated risk. Taylor's script was an English comedy by an English playwright; its American setting and American protagonist were designed to appeal to transatlantic audiences, but there was no guarantee of success. The production's eventual run of 150 performances — extraordinary for a period when a month's run was considered successful — established both the play and Keene's theatre as major forces in American theatrical life.

> Laura Keene's was one of the few mid-century theatres where a woman exercised complete artistic and financial control. The irony that a play celebrating American democratic competence was produced by a British-born female entrepreneur in a male-dominated industry is one of the richer footnotes to the play's history.
