## In the Play

Comedy in *Our American Cousin* operates on multiple registers simultaneously: verbal, physical, situational, and cultural. The play's humor is not incidental but structural — the collision between American and English sensibilities generates comedy at every level of the text, from Dundreary's nonsense syllables to the grand plot mechanics of inheritance and disguise.

### The Failed Joke as Comic Device

The play's most insistent comic motif is the joke that does not land. Lord Dundreary's running refrain — "That wath a joke, that wath" [002-003] — is invariably followed by Florence's deflating question: "Where's the joke?" [002-004]. The pattern recurs: Dundreary attempts a pun on "drafts" and "draughts" involving "pieces of wound wood on square pieces of leather" [002-003]; Florence doesn't see it. He tries again with "especially green" as wordplay [002-064]; again Florence asks "Where was the joke?" [002-065].

This repeated failure is itself the joke. The comedy lies not in Dundreary's wit but in its absence — in the gap between his conviction that he is being clever and the audience's recognition that he is not. The stage direction "[Screams.] Ha! ha! ha!" [002-003] before delivering the punchline — laughing at his own joke before anyone else can — is a precise piece of comic characterization: Dundreary is a man who must supply his own audience because no one else will laugh.

### The American Joke

Tom Taylor introduces a second register of comedy through Asa Trenchard. When Asa proposes to give the "sick gal" a drink that will make her "squirm like an eel on a mud bank" [003-062], Florence explains: "Oh, don't mind him! That's only an American joke" [003-061]. Dundreary's response — "A joke! Do you call that a joke?" [003-062] — marks the cultural boundary that the play's comedy navigates.

The "American joke" is characterized by exaggeration, physical imagery, and a disregard for delicacy. The English joke, by contrast, aspires to wordplay and wit — though in Dundreary's case, the aspiration exceeds the execution. This taxonomy of national humor is one of Taylor's structural innovations: the play does not merely contain jokes but theorizes about them, making the nature of comedy itself a subject of dramatic interest.

### Laughter as Social Barometer

Laughter in the play functions as a social diagnostic tool. When Florence hides her laugh at Dundreary's donkey's milk regimen [002-048], the suppressed laugh marks the boundary between politeness and mockery — she is too well-bred to laugh openly at a guest, but too honest not to laugh at all. When Augusta observes "I do believe he's laughing at us" [004-186], she registers the shift in comic power: the American outsider has become the laugher, the English gentry the laughed-at.

The stage direction "all laugh, and keep it up till curtain falls" [004-147] — following Asa's shower-bath mishap — transforms laughter from individual reaction into collective ritual. The curtain falls on communal laughter, making the audience's own laughter continuous with the characters'.

### Laughter as Weapon and Relief

Mary's laughing observation that "ladies without a farthing in the world, ought to" [marry] [005-188] uses humor to acknowledge a painful economic truth. Florence's laugh at Asa's dairy knowledge — "And they call that man a savage; well, I only wish we had a few more such savages in England" [006-110] — uses laughter to mark a reversal of expectations that carries serious implications about national character.

## Cultural-Historical Context

The mid-Victorian theatre drew on multiple comic traditions: the punning wordplay of Restoration comedy, the physical business of farce, the social observation of the comedy of manners, and the emerging "stage Yankee" type that had been popular in British theatre since the late 18th century. Taylor's skill was in braiding these traditions together.

The play's most innovative comic move — making comedy itself a topic of conversation — reflects a broader Victorian interest in taxonomic thinking. Just as the period classified plants, animals, and geological strata, Taylor classifies jokes: the American joke, the English pun, the physical gag, the failed witticism. This meta-comic dimension distinguishes the play from the broader farce tradition and helps explain its enduring appeal: it is a comedy that knows it is a comedy and invites the audience to share that knowledge.

Lord Dundreary's joke-telling — his repeated "That wath a joke" — also reflects the Victorian cult of the pun, which reached its peak in the 1840s and 1850s. Magazines like *Punch* made punning a national pastime. Dundreary's failed puns are thus a satire not just of one character but of a widespread social practice: the man who tells jokes because it is expected of him, not because he has anything funny to say.
