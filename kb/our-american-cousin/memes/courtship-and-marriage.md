# Courtship and Marriage

In "Our American Cousin," marriage is the arena where inheritance, class ambition, and genuine affection collide. Every character's romantic position is simultaneously an emotional state and a financial proposition, and the play's comic resolution depends on disentangling the two.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The marriage plot runs parallel to the inheritance plot and intersects with it at every turn. Florence Trenchard is pursued by the fortune-hunting Augustus under the direction of his scheming mother, while her genuine affection leans toward the less calculating Harry Vernon. The pressure on Florence to marry for money rather than love — "On consideration of settling on you the Ravensdale Estate" (005-115) — makes explicit the transactional logic underlying genteel courtship.

Asa's own romantic trajectory moves from the drawing room to the dairy, mirroring the play's larger argument about authenticity. His initial comic unsuitability for aristocratic courtship (his "American savage" manners horrify Mrs. Mountchessington) is revealed as moral fitness: he, unlike the titled suitors, has nothing to hide.

### Associated Characters

- **Florence Trenchard** — the central figure in the marriage plot, caught between her family's financial desperation and her own romantic preferences. Her trajectory from teasing the American cousin (002-094)(002-097) to respecting his judgment tracks the play's movement from superficial to genuine valuation.

- **Augustus Trenchard** — the would-be suitor whose romantic posturing masks mercenary calculation. His dismay at learning Asa has "no fortune" (008-094) reveals courtship reduced to pure transaction.

- **Mrs. Mountchessington** — the architect of the mercenary marriage plot, who coaches Augustus to pursue Florence for her estate connection (005-115) while simultaneously dismissing Asa as unsuitable.

- **Harry Vernon** — the alternative to mercenary courtship, whose quieter presence represents the possibility of marriage based on genuine affection rather than financial calculation.

- **Asa Trenchard** — the wildcard whose American frankness disrupts the English courtship ritual. His eventual pairing with Mary Meredith (implied in the play's resolution) is the only union based on mutual recognition of work and character.

### Key References

> "Mrs M: On consideration of settling on you the Ravensdale Estate."
>
> — Act I (005-115)

Mrs. Mountchessington's blunt summary of the marriage calculus — the estate as consideration, Florence as the asset to be acquired — strips courtship of its romantic pretense.

> "Aug: What, no fortune?"
>
> — Act III (008-094)

Three words that collapse Augustus's entire romantic persona. His instant pivot from ardor to dismay reveals that courtship, in his practice, is simply fortune-hunting by another name.

> "Flo: And you're determined to give up this fortune to Mary Meredith?"
>
> — Act III (008-136)

Florence's question carries the weight of the play's marriage-plot resolution. Asa's renunciation of the fortune — and with it, his eligibility as a titled suitor — clears the way for marriage to be based on something other than property transfer.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The mid-Victorian marriage market was a complex institution in which romantic ideals of companionate marriage coexisted uneasily with the economic realities of dowries, settlements, and the doctrine of coverture (which subsumed a married woman's legal identity into her husband's). Taylor's play reflects a moment when these arrangements were increasingly subject to critique — the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had just reformed divorce law, and the "marriage question" was a staple of novels, pamphlets, and parliamentary debate.

The play's comic resolution — redirecting both the fortune and the romantic pairings toward merit rather than rank — offers a fantasy of reform without revolution. The social order remains intact; only the distribution of its rewards has been adjusted.
