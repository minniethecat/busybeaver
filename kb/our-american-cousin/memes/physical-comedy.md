## In the Play

Physical comedy is one of the principal engines of humor in *Our American Cousin*, and it is almost entirely concentrated in the character of Lord Dundreary. His bodily absurdity — the sneezing, stumbling, screaming, and pratfalls — creates a running physical gag that sustains the play's comic energy across three acts. Taylor's stage directions, unusually detailed for the period, orchestrate a precise choreography of comic misfortune.

### The Sneezing Business

The sneezing routine is the play's most sustained piece of physical comedy, running across Acts V through VII and returning in Act IX. Dundreary announces "I'm going to sneeze" [005-039] and the business builds through repeated false starts: "Now I've got it" [006-202], "Now I've got it. [Tries to sneeze.]" [007-011], and the frustrated "Wal, now, I say. [Dundreary trying to sneeze.]" [007-012]. Each interruption — Asa squeezing his hand, Buddicombe addressing him — triggers the same complaint: "There, you've spoiled it" [006-205], followed by the explanation "Spoiled what! why a magnificent sneeze" [006-207].

The sneeze is literally postponed across hundreds of lines of dialogue, creating what amounts to a running joke about delayed gratification. The routine's anatomy is explained by Dundreary himself: "I'll tell you what a sneeze is. Imagine a very large spider" [005-049]. Georgina's scream at the spider image — "Where, my lord?" [005-050] — and Dundreary's clarification "No, no, I don't mean a real spider" [005-051] layers comic misunderstanding onto the physical business. The delayed sneeze — an extended comic tease — is resolved only when Dundreary's sneezing is finally described as "a magnificent sneeze" [005-009][006-207], a punch line that rewards the audience's patience. The "sneezing business" [006-203] became a signature theatrical routine, so identified with the role that subsequent actors playing Dundreary were judged by their sneeze.

### The Shower Bath Scene

The shower bath scene (Act I, Scene 3) provides the play's most elaborate piece of physical comedy involving a prop. The scene is carefully set: a "Large shower bath near R. 3 E." dominates the chamber set [004-Chamber description]. Asa, encountering this unfamiliar English contraption, mistakes it for a "'skeeter net, only it 'ain't long enough for a feller to stand up in" [004-bath description]. When Binny asks "Will you take a baath before you dress?" Asa mishears it as "baath" and replies "I suppose you mean a bath. Wal, man, I calkalate I ain't going to expose myself to the shakes by getting into cold water" [004-bath exchange].

The climactic moment is a textbook piece of comic staging: "[Slips partly inside shower bath, pulls rope, water comes down.] Murder! help! fire! Water! I'm drown" [004-147]. The servants rush in — Skillet, Sharpe, Binny, Buddicombe — and the stage direction specifies "seeing Asa, all laugh, and keep it up till curtain falls" [004-147]. This curtain line is a masterstroke: the characters' laughter merges with the audience's, and the act ends on a wave of shared hilarity.

The shower bath trauma carries forward into the next scene. When Asa later sees a window curtain, he "[Pulls curtain, then starts back.] No you don't! One shower bath a day is enough for me" [004-curtain callback], a callback that turns his earlier mishap into a running joke.

### Pratfalls and Stumbles

The pratfall is another key physical device. When Asa gives Dundreary his "Jersey Lightning" concoction, the stage direction reads "[Dundreary falls back on Mrs. M. and Georgina]" [003-076] — a classic comic collapse that uses the body's loss of control to generate laughter. Later, "Dundreary goes up, stumbles" [007-014], sustaining the motif of physical ineptitude. In Act II, Dundreary "[Goes hopping up stage, stumbling over against spinning wheel. Looks at yarn on stick.] Why, what a little old man" [006-068] — the stumble leads directly to a comic misidentification, making the physical error the trigger for verbal absurdity.

### The Scream-and-Turn

The scream — or more precisely, the scream-and-turn — is Dundreary's reflexive physical response to confusion. Florence asks "Where's the joke?" and the stage direction specifies "[Dundreary screams and turns to Mrs. M.]" [002-004]. Georgina screams at the sight of Dundreary's whiskers [002-104][002-112], to which Dundreary protests "Don't scream" [002-113] — the screamer telling others not to scream, a layered physical gag. The table is turned when Georgina screams at something Asa says, and Dundreary's comic detachment is shattered [007-075].

### Asa's American Physicality

While Dundreary embodies physical ineptitude, Asa Trenchard represents a different kind of physical comedy: the American body in an English space. His entrance and manner — "about the tallest gunner, the slickest dancer, and generally the loudest critter in the state" [002-043] — is itself a physical joke, the oversized American filling a drawing room designed for smaller English bodies. His attempt to kiss Florence — "I ain't going to kiss you" [002-054] after being told cousins in the old country don't kiss — uses the threat of physical contact as comic tension. Where Dundreary's body fails him, Asa's body overwhelms others.

## Cultural-Historical Context

Physical comedy in *Our American Cousin* reflects the theatrical conventions of mid-19th-century farce, where bodily humor — pratfalls, silly walks, exaggerated facial expressions — was considered not crass but essential to the comic craft. The sneezing routine, in particular, belongs to a tradition of extended physical "business" that had been popularized by earlier farceurs like John Liston and would influence later comic performers from Charlie Chaplin to John Cleese.

The shower bath scene draws on the venerable theatrical tradition of the "wet comic" — a character drenched onstage for laughs — which dates back to commedia dell'arte and remains a staple of physical comedy to the present day. Taylor's innovation is to make the mishap a specifically cross-cultural one: it is Asa's American unfamiliarity with English domestic technology that causes the disaster.

Dundreary's physical vocabulary — sneezing, stumbling, screaming — translates class anxiety into body language, making visible the aristocracy's loss of physical as well as social control. The contrast with Asa's robust American physicality completes the pattern: in Taylor's comic world, the old country's body has atrophied while the new country's body is almost excessively vital.
