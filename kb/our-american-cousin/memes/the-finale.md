# The Finale

The closing sequence of *Our American Cousin* is less a single event than a cascade: marriages pair off, the Mountchessington scheme collapses into farce, and Asa Trenchard's Yankee humor orchestrates a resolution in which nobody — not even Dundreary — is permitted to take themselves seriously. It is Taylor's most efficient piece of theatrical machinery, resolving every outstanding plot line in a single sustained comic movement.

## In the Play

### Context

By the time the finale begins, the play's three intersecting crises have each reached their tipping point. The [[the-foreclosure-threat|mortgage crisis]] has been resolved by Asa's decision to renounce the inheritance. The [[the-cabinet-scene|cabinet scene]] has exposed Coyle's treachery and vindicated Murcott. And the romantic entanglements — Florence and Harry Vernon, Mary and Asa, the Mountchessington daughters and their reluctant suitors — await only Sir Edward's consent to become official.

The stage is crowded — deliberately so. Taylor's stage direction brings characters on "two by two, as if they were pairing for Noah's ark" (010-063)(010-065), a comic image that announces the finale's governing metaphor: pairing, matching, and the orderly (and disorderly) distribution of partners.

### What Happens

The finale unfolds as a series of short, overlapping negotiations, each conducted in Asa's presence and largely on his terms. Florence secures Sir Edward's consent to marry Harry Vernon, who is "not poor now, though he's got a ship" (010-035)(010-037). Mary Meredith, asked to name "the object of your choice," replies with a line that reverses the play's social calculus: "Rough-spun, honest-hearted Asa Trenchard" (010-045).

Asa's characteristic response to Florence's teasing — "I'm blushing all the way down my back" (010-059) — reasserts his idiom even as he becomes the romantic center of two proposals.

The Mountchessington subplot, the play's most sustained satire of aristocratic marriage markets, receives its closing humiliation. Mrs. Mountchessington, having failed to land Asa for either daughter, delivers a parting benediction that doubles as a verdict on her daughters: "Oh, you couple of fools" (010-092)(010-093), bumping their foreheads together in a gesture that literalizes the play's judgment on the marriage market.

### The Dundreary Business

Lord Dundreary's role in the finale is characteristically peripheral — he is present, he collides with people, he makes comments that miss their mark but land as comedy — and this is precisely the point. When De Boots observes "we're all getting married," Dundreary replies "Yes, it's catching, like the cholera" (010-101). The simile is vintage Dundreary: it is technically accurate (marriage is indeed "catching" in this scene), socially inappropriate (comparing marriage to cholera), and brilliantly funny for exactly that reason.

The physical comedy reaches its apex in the collision sequence: Dundreary sits in Georgina's lap, "turns to apologize, sits in Augusta's lap — same business with Mrs. M, then goes back to Georgina" (010-067). The stage business enacts what the dialogue has insisted throughout: Dundreary cannot navigate social space any more successfully than he navigates language.

### Asa as Stage Manager

Crucially, Asa does not merely participate in the finale — he conducts it. He summons each pair ("Hello! here's all the folks coming two by two" [010-063]), extracts Sir Edward's consent through comic indirection, and systematically defuses every potential source of embarrassment by offering to NOT reveal the secrets he holds over each character: Georgina's beefsteak appetite (010-069)(010-070), Augusta's boot purchase (010-078)(010-079), and Mrs. Mountchessington's attempt "to stick them on me first" (010-095). Each time, the refrain "Wal, I won't, if you don't want me to" (010-065, 010-072, 010-080, 010-097, 010-103) functions as both threat and absolution — and as the scene's comic engine.

### Scene Structure

| Phase | Action | Characters | Tone |
|-------|--------|------------|------|
| Consent | Florence and Mary secure Sir Edward's blessing | Florence, Mary, Sir Edward, Asa | Warm, sincere |
| Parade | Characters enter in pairs ("Noah's ark") | All | Comic, processional |
| Settlements | Each couple receives formal approval | Sir Edward, Binny, Buddicombe | Ritual, brisk |
| Humiliations | Asa parries the Mountchessingtons | Asa, Mrs. M, Georgina, Augusta | Farce, deflation |
| Collisions | Dundreary's physical comedy sequence | Dundreary, Georgina, Augusta, Mrs. M | Physical farce |

### Consequences

The finale's efficiency as dramatic machinery comes at a cost: characters are paired and dispatched with a speed that forecloses interiority. Mary's declaration for Asa is a single line; Florence's marriage to Harry Vernon is more mentioned than dramatized. But this is Taylor's design, not his failure — the play is a comedy of surfaces, and the finale's velocity is its argument: that happiness, once the obstacles of inheritance and class are cleared, is simple. The play ends not with a soliloquy but with business.

## Key References

> "Hello! here's all the folks coming two by two, as if they were pairing for Noah's ark."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act III (010-063)(010-065)

Asa's governing metaphor makes him the Noah of this comic ark — the one who sees the pattern, names it, and steers the pairs to their appointed places.

> "Rough-spun, honest-hearted Asa Trenchard."
>
> — Mary Meredith, Act III (010-045)

Mary's declaration inverts the social hierarchy the play has spent three acts satirizing. "Rough-spun" is an epithet of American manufacture, and it is offered — and received — as the highest compliment.

> "Yes, it's catching, like the cholera."
>
> — Lord Dundreary, Act III (010-101)

The definitive Dundrearyism: a true observation framed in the most socially catastrophic vehicle available. It is funny because Dundreary has, for once, perceived something accurately — and expressed it disastrously.

## Historical Note

The finale's structure — a rapid sequence of pairings culminating in a general benediction — is standard 19th-century comic practice, traceable through Sheridan and Goldsmith back to Restoration comedy. Taylor's innovation is to make the American character the *conductor* of the finale rather than merely one of its participants. Asa does not receive his happy ending; he manufactures everyone's, including his own. This is arguably the play's most American gesture: happiness as something you build, not something you inherit.
