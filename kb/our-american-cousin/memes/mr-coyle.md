# Mr. Coyle

Mr. Coyle is the Trenchard family's solicitor in *Our American Cousin*, a coolly professional figure whose business with the mortgage makes him the embodiment of the financial threat hanging over Trenchard Manor.

## In the Play

Coyle first appears in Act I, announced by Binny as "Mr. Coyle and hagent with papers" [002-082] — the papers being the mortgage documents that drive the play's central crisis. Sir Edward's description of him is a study in reluctant respect: "Coyle is a most intelligent" [002-079] agent, a man whose competence makes him dangerous to a client in financial distress. Sir Edward curses him — "Curse him, I wish I could!" [002-079] — but cannot dispense with his services.

When Coyle enters, he surveys the crowded household with professional detachment: "Very well. House full of company, I see, Mr. Binny" [003-082]. Binny's reply — "Cram full, Mr. Coyle. As one of the first families" [003-083] — attempts to impress the solicitor with the household's social standing, a tactic Coyle is clearly immune to. His response is to "rub his hands" [003-085] — a gesture of anticipatory satisfaction that marks him as a man who profits from others' distress.

Coyle's authority over the household staff is absolute. He demands of Murcott: "Are you sober, sirrah?" [003-095] and upon receiving an affirmative, snaps "Then see you keep so" [003-097] — a line that reveals the power dynamic between the family's legal representative and its servants. His command to "Get out those mortgage" [003-101] papers brings the play's financial subtext into the open, the documents laid on the table like weapons.

The actor J.G. Burnett, who originated the role, played Coyle as the kind of man Victorian audiences would recognize from their own anxious dealings with the legal profession: efficient, unsentimental, and entirely unbothered by the human cost of his paperwork.

## Character Analysis

Coyle is the play's closest approximation to an antagonist, though he operates within entirely legal bounds. His menace derives not from villainy but from institutional power — he represents the inexorable logic of debt and contract in a household built on social pretense. Taylor understood that the Victorian solicitor occupied a uniquely unsettling position: a servant of the law who could, through the quiet enforcement of a mortgage, dismantle an aristocratic family's entire way of life.

His interactions with the staff are especially revealing. Where the Trenchards treat their servants with the casual condescension of inherited rank, Coyle treats them as functionaries whose sobriety and competence directly affect his business. His professionalism cuts through the household's class theatre — he is there for the mortgage, not the manners.
