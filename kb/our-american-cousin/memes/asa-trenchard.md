# Asa Trenchard

The "American cousin" of the title — a Vermont farmer whose unexpected inheritance summons him to England, where his plain dealing, practical competence, and unshakeable moral compass expose the hypocrisies of the English gentry and resolve the family's financial and romantic crises.

## In the Play

### Role and Relationships

Asa Trenchard is the dramatic catalyst and moral center of the play. As the heir to the Trenchard estate through the American branch of the family (001-165), his arrival threatens to disrupt the financial arrangements that have sustained — barely — the English Trenchards' aristocratic lifestyle. But Asa proves to be not a threat but a salvation, using his American practicality and moral clarity to expose Richard Coyle's schemes, renounce a fortune to which his claim is legally questionable, and redirect the inheritance to Mary Meredith, whose merit he recognizes.

His relationships define the play's moral geography: with Florence Trenchard, a growing mutual respect that models the possibility of transatlantic understanding; with Lord Dundreary, a comic foil that contrasts American directness with English inanity; with Mary Meredith, a recognition of shared values that constitutes the play's most genuine connection; with Mrs. Mountchessington, a collision of sincerity and calculation that exposes both.

### Key Scenes

- **Act I — The Dairy**: Asa's first significant interaction with Mary Meredith, where his appreciation for her dairy work (006-043)(006-044) and his highest compliment — "useful" (006-045) — establish his value system in opposition to the drawing room's calculus of rank and property.

- **Act I — The Drawing Room**: Asa's encounter with the Mountchessingtons, where his American manners — "What an everlasting lot of things and fixins there is to be sure" (004-089) — are met with condescension that masks calculation. Mrs. Mountchessington's "pray be attentive to this American savage" (004-155) encapsulates the class prejudice Asa must navigate.

- **Act III — The Renunciation**: Asa's simple "Oh, no" (008-093) when asked if he is the heir, followed by his determination to give the fortune to Mary Meredith (008-136), constitutes the play's moral climax. The gesture is delivered without drama — and it is precisely this lack of performance that makes it powerful.

- **Act III — The Cabinet**: Asa's axe — "What key, why, Yankee" (009-113) — resolves the locked-cabinet crisis through applied force, literalizing the play's argument that American practicality can solve problems that English procedure cannot.

### Character Traits

Asa is defined by his plainness — not as a deficit but as a moral quality. His Vermont vernacular ("cow juice," "small potatoes," "darn me") is the linguistic form of his refusal to pretend. He judges by work rather than title, by usefulness rather than ornament, by sincerity rather than performance. His American identity is not a political stance but a way of being: practical, direct, honest, and fundamentally egalitarian.

## Historical Context

Asa Trenchard belongs to the theatrical tradition of the "stage Yankee" — the shrewd, plain-spoken American whose homespun wisdom confounds European sophistication. This type had been a staple of Anglo-American theatre since the late 18th century, but Taylor's treatment is distinctive in making the Yankee the play's unquestioned moral center rather than merely its comic relief.

The character's Vermont origins are significant. In the 1850s, Vermont was associated with independent smallholder agriculture, abolitionist politics, and a distinctive form of rural democracy — a far cry from the plantation South that Europeans often imagined as "American." Asa's Vermont identity marks him as a representative of free labor and democratic competence.
