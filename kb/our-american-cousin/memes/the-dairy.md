# The Dairy

The Dairy at Trenchard Manor is the working domain of Mary Meredith and the setting for some of the play's most thematically significant encounters. It serves as a physical counter-space to the manor's formal rooms—a place where honest labor, romantic sincerity, and American practicality converge.

## In the Play

### Stage Description and Setting

The dairy is introduced with unusually elaborate stage directions in Act II, Scene 2. Taylor calls for "an inside view of a dairy with sloping roof, painting backing to look like milk pans. The whole scene should have a picturesque appearance" [007-2078]. The setting includes a "Garden fence run across back, ornamental gate or archway, R. 3 E. Pigeon house on pole near dairy, L. C. Spinning wheel inside cottage door, one or two rustic benches" [007-2080]. This is one of the most detailed scenic specifications in the play, suggesting that Taylor envisioned the dairy as a visual set-piece—a pastoral counterpoint to the manor's Gothic interiors.

The dairy reappears in Act III, Scene 1, noted as "Dairy set as before in Act 2d, Scene 2" [009-2762], with Asa "discovered on bench, R. C., whittling stick" and "Mary busy with milk pans in dairy" [009-2764]. The repetition of the setting across acts establishes the dairy as a stable, grounding presence in the play's spatial economy.

### Significance

The dairy is more than a picturesque backdrop. It represents the world of productive labor that the drawing-room society of Trenchard Manor depends upon but rarely acknowledges. Mary Meredith—the disinherited granddaughter of old Mark Trenchard—presides over this space, and her competence within it becomes the measure of her moral worth.

The dairy encounter between Mary and Asa is the play's romantic turning point. Asa's initial response to Mary's domain is aesthetic appreciation—"if it ain't all as bright and clean as a fresh washed shirt just off the clover" [007-2223]—but this quickly gives way to moral admiration when he learns of Mary's actual labor: "Yes, sir, I milk the cows, set up the milk, superintend the churning and make the cheese" [007-2225]. His verdict—"Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've seen on this side the pond" [007-2228]—establishes the play's central value system: American pragmatism recognizes genuine worth that English class hierarchy obscures.

This judgment is crystallized in the play's most memorable transatlantic insult-compliment exchange. Asa tells Florence she is "small potatoes, and few in a hill compared to a gal like that" [007-2231], then translates for her: "I'll put it in French for you, 'petite pommes des terres'" [007-2234]. When she protests, he clarifies: "you can sing and paint, and play on the pianner, and in your own particular circle you are some pumpkins. But she, she can milk cows, set up the butter, make cheese, and, darn me, if them ain't what I call raal downright feminine accomplishments" [007-2257]. The comic vegetable metaphors encode a serious argument: useful labor trumps ornamental cultivation.

### Associated Scenes

**Act II, Scene 2 (Park and Dairy)**. The scene opens with John Wickens delivering milk pails on a yoke [007-2086] and assisting Mary with the cream—"don't shake it, the cream is foaming so beautifully" [007-2115]. Mary spins at her Welsh nurse's old spinning wheel, reflecting on her situation: "Grandfather was rich, but he must have had a bad heart, or he never could have cast off poor mamma" [007-2145]. Her contentment—"I am my own mistress here, and not anybody's humble servant" [007-2150]—establishes the dignity of labor before Asa's arrival. The scene culminates in Asa's decision to conceal the will that gives him Mary's rightful inheritance, and his comic attempt to linger: "I just wanted to see how they make cheese is this darned country" [007-2357].

**Act III, Scene 1 (Dairy)**. The dairy reappears as the play moves toward its resolution. Asa, now integrated into the estate's rhythms, is comfortable enough to whittle on a bench while Mary works [009-2764]. Florence's observation—"What a pretty couple they would make. He is so frank and bold, she is so fresh and modest" [009-2780]—makes explicit what the dairy setting has implied across two acts.

### Related Characters

- **Mary Meredith** — the dairy is her domain, and her identity is inseparable from it. She is introduced through her labor, not her lineage, and her mastery of the dairy's practical arts is what distinguishes her from the drawing-room women.
- **Asa Trenchard** — the dairy is where Asa's American values find their English counterpart. His recognition of Mary's worth, articulated through the language of agricultural labor ("cow juice" [007-2220], "small potatoes" [007-2231], "some pumpkins" [007-2240]), bridges the transatlantic divide.
- **John Wickens** — the rural servant who assists Mary, representing the English yeoman class that parallels Asa's Vermont origins. His devotion to Mary—"Dang me if I wouldn't like to stop all day, and watch her pretty figure and run errands for her" [007-2141]—is genuine and uncalculating.

## Historical/Real-World Context

The dairy held a particular place in the Victorian English imagination. In an era of rapid industrialization, the dairy represented a vanishing rural ideal—clean, wholesome, and feminine without being frivolous. The figure of the dairymaid carried associations of pastoral purity that Taylor both invokes and complicates. Mary is not a rustic stereotype; she is a displaced gentlewoman who has made necessity into virtue.

The detailed stage directions for the dairy—sloping roof, milk-pan backing, pigeon house, spinning wheel—reflect the mid-Victorian theatre's taste for pictorial realism. Taylor was writing for Laura Keene's Theatre in New York and later for the London stage, both of which invested in elaborate scenic design. The dairy's "picturesque appearance" [007-2079] was designed to delight audiences accustomed to the spectacle of meticulously constructed stage environments.

The Act III dairy scene also reflects Victorian gender ideology in transition. Mary's claim that she is "my own mistress here" [007-2150] articulates an ideal of female autonomy through productive work—a theme that resonated with both English and American audiences in the 1850s, as the "separate spheres" doctrine was increasingly contested by advocates of women's economic participation.
