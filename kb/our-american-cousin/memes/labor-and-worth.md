# Labor and Worth

The play's implicit argument that a person's value should be measured by what they do rather than what they inherit. Asa's and Mary's competence in practical work — dairying, axe-work, honest dealing — is consistently contrasted with the idleness of the titled characters, and the play's resolution rewards labor over lineage.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The labor/worth equation is introduced in Asa's first moments at the manor. His instinct is not to admire the architecture but to inspect the dairy, and his first connection with another character is based on shared practical knowledge: Mary's competence in milking, churning, and cheese-making (006-043)(006-044). His highest compliment — "useful" (006-045) — establishes a value system entirely orthogonal to the drawing room's calculus of title and property.

The titled characters, by contrast, are defined by their distance from productive labor. Lord Dundreary cannot complete a sentence; Augustus cannot sustain a courtship without his mother's coaching; Sir Edward cannot manage his own estate. Their idleness is the source of both their comedy and their moral failure.

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — the labor standard made flesh. His practical knowledge spans dairying, axe-handling, and honest dealing. His American identity is defined not by what he believes but by what he can *do*.

- **Mary Meredith** — the working woman whose labor is presented as genuine accomplishment. Her detailed catalogue of dairy duties (006-043)(006-044) is the play's most complete job description, and her reward — the fortune Asa renounces — is the play's most explicit argument that work should be compensated.

- **Lord Dundreary** — the comic extreme of labor-free existence. His verbal paralysis and mental vacancy are presented as the natural consequence of a life in which nothing need be done.

- **Sir Edward Trenchard** — the tragic dimension of the labor critique. His financial ruin is not caused by vice but by the structural impossibility of maintaining aristocratic status without productive income.

### Key References

> "Mary: Yes, sir, I milk the cows, set up the milk, superintend the churning and make the cheese."
>
> — Act I (006-043)(006-044)

Mary's catalogue of labor is the play's most sustained description of productive work. Each verb names a discrete skill, and the cumulative effect is a portrait of genuine competence — the quality the titled characters lack.

> "Asa: Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've seen on this side of the big pond."
>
> — Act I (006-045)

"Useful" is the highest praise in Asa's vocabulary, and its application to Mary marks her as his moral equal. The judgment cuts across class lines: the milkmaid is more valuable than the aristocrat because she *does* something.

> "Asa: What key, why, Yankee."
>
> — Act III (009-113)

When Asa produces an axe to break open the cabinet, he literalizes the labor metaphor. His solution to the locked cabinet is not legal argument or social negotiation but physical competence — the "Yankee" key that bypasses the entire apparatus of English procedure.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The "dignity of labor" was a central theme of mid-19th-century social thought on both sides of the Atlantic. Thomas Carlyle's *Past and Present* (1843) had celebrated work as a spiritual discipline; John Ruskin's writings on art and society insisted on the moral value of craftsmanship; and the American transcendentalists — Emerson, Thoreau — preached self-reliance as the highest virtue.

Taylor's play domesticates this broad intellectual current into a comic plot. But the argument is unmistakable: the future belongs to those who work, and the aristocracy's refusal to labor is not a privilege but a pathology.
