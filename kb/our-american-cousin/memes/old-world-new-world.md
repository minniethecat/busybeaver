# Old World vs. New World

The geographical and cultural polarity that structures the play's entire imaginative world. "Old World" England — hierarchical, tradition-bound, financially hollow — is set against "New World" America — egalitarian, pragmatic, and morally solvent. The comedy arises from the collision of these two worlds in the person of Asa Trenchard, and the resolution affirms the New World values as the basis for a reformed Old World order.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The Old World / New World opposition is established before Asa appears. The Trenchard family history includes the emigration of an ancestor to "America at the Restoration" (001-152), creating the transatlantic family structure that will drive the plot. This is not merely backstory but world-building: the Trenchards are a family divided by more than geography, representing two distinct social systems connected by a legal tie.

Asa's arrival activates the opposition. Every scene in which he appears contrasts American directness with English circumlocution, American practicality with English ornament, American honesty with English performance. The contrast is comic in its particulars but serious in its implications: the play argues that the New World has something to teach the Old about the conduct of life.

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — the New World embodied. His Vermont origins, his practical skills, his vernacular speech, and his moral directness are all presented as products of American social conditions — and as correctives to English social pathologies.

- **Sir Edward Trenchard** — the Old World in decline. His financial ruin is not personal but systemic: the aristocratic model of inherited wealth without productive labor is structurally unsustainable.

- **Lord Dundreary** — the Old World as farce. His mental vacancy and verbal paralysis are presented as the natural endpoint of a society that has substituted breeding for capability.

- **Mary Meredith** — the figure who bridges the two worlds. Her labor places her outside the Old World's class system, and her reward — the fortune Asa renounces — represents the New World principle of merit winning out within the Old World's institutional framework.

### Key References

> "America at the Restoration. They are now thriving in this State."
>
> — Act I, original cast notes (001-152)

The word "thriving" is carefully chosen. The American Trenchards have prospered through their own efforts, while the English Trenchards have declined through their own negligence. The Restoration reference places the divergence in historical time: two centuries of separate development have produced two distinct family types.

> "Asa: Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've seen on this side of the big pond."
>
> — Act I (006-045)

"On this side of the big pond" frames the entire English visit as a comparative exercise. Asa has crossed the Atlantic and found, in the Old World, one person who meets his New World standard. The geography of the compliment is its argument.

> "Asa: What key, why, Yankee."
>
> — Act III (009-113)

The axe as key is the play's most concentrated image of the Old World / New World opposition. The locked cabinet — English property, legal procedure, aristocratic secrecy — is opened not by a key but by applied force. "Yankee" names a method as much as an identity.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The Old World / New World binary was a staple of 19th-century transatlantic discourse. From Crevecoeur's *Letters from an American Farmer* (1782) to Tocqueville's *Democracy in America* (1835/1840) to the flood of British travel accounts of the United States, the comparison of European and American societies was a genre unto itself.

Taylor's play participates in this discourse at the level of popular entertainment, but its argument is no less pointed for being comic. The Old World's virtues — tradition, refinement, continuity — are acknowledged but shown to be hollow; the New World's virtues — energy, honesty, merit — are shown to be the qualities the Old World needs. It is a critique delivered with a smile, but a critique nonetheless.
