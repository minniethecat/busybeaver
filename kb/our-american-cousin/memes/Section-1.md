(001-001) OUR AMERICAN COUSIN

(001-002) A Drama, in 3 Acts.

(001-003) By Tom Taylor

(001-004) Transcriber's Note:

(001-005) This etext was produced by the Levin family, Englewood, CO. Like many

(001-006) plays, there is no authoritative version and it evolved over the course

(001-007) of time, indeed in multiple directions. The 1869 printing upon which

(001-008) this etext is primarily based was poorly printed and we have corrected

(001-009) outright punctuation and grammatical errors while maintaining its

(001-010) original, whimisical use of capitalization and punctuation. This version

(001-011) contains very few "[[dundrearyism|Dundrearyisms]]" such as "birds of a feather gather no

(001-012) moss" for which [[our-american-cousin-play|the play]] gained much of its popular appeal.

(001-013) Abraham Lincoln was watching this play when he was assassinated. (Act

(001-014) III, halfway through Scene 2.)

(001-034) OUR AMERICAN COUSIN.

(001-035) ACT I.

(001-036) Scene 1--Drawing room in 3. [[trenchard-manor|Trenchard Manor]], C. D., backed by interior,

(001-037) discovering table with luncheon spread. Large French window, R. 3 E.,

(001-038) through which a fine English park is seen. Open archway, L. 3 E.

(001-039) Set balcony behind. Table, R., books and [[letters-and-documents|papers]] on it. Work basket

(001-040) containing wools and embroidery frame. A fashionable arm chair and sofa,

(001-041) L. 2 E., small table near C. D. Stage handsomely set, costly furniture,

(001-042) carpet down, chairs, etc.

(001-043) Buddicombe discovered on sofa reading newspaper. Skillet and Sharpe

(001-044) busily arranging furniture as curtain rises.

(001-045) Sharpe I don't know how you may feel as a visitor, Mr. Buddicombe, but I

(001-046) think this is a most uncomfortable family.

(001-047) Bud Very uncomfortable. I have no curtain to my bed.

(001-048) Skil And no wine at the second table.

(001-049) Sharpe And meaner [[servants-and-service|servants]] I never seed.

(001-050) Bud I'm afraid Sir Edward is in a queer strait.

(001-051) Skil Yes, for only this morning, [[mr-binny|Mr. Binny]], Mrs. Skillet says he--

(001-052) Enter Binny, L. 3 E.

(001-053) Binny Mind your hown business instead hof your betters. I'm disgusted

(001-054) with you lower servants. When the wine merchant presents his bills, you

(001-055) men, hear me, say he's been pressing for the last six months, do you?

(001-056) Skil Nor I, that the last year's milliner's bills have not been paid.

(001-057) Sharpe Nor I, that Miss Florence has not had no new dresses from [[london|London]]

(001-058) all winter.

(001-059) Bud And I can solemnly swear that his lordship's hair has been

(001-060) faithfully bound in this bosom.

(001-061) Binny That'll do, that'll do; but to remember to check hidle curiosity

(001-062) is the first duty of men hin livery. Ha, 'ere hare the letters.

(001-063) Enter John Wickens, L. 3 E., with green baize bag. Binny takes bag,

(001-064) takes out letters and reads addresses.

(001-065) Binny Hah! bill, of course, Miss Augusta, Mrs. Mountchessington, Lord

(001-066) Dundreary, Capt. De Boots, Miss [[georgina-mountchessington|Georgina Mountchessington]], Lieut.

(001-067) Vernon, ah! that's from the admiralty. What's this? Miss Florence

(001-068) Trenchard, via [[brattleboro-vermont|Brattleboro']], [[vermont|Vermont]].

(001-069) Bud Where's that, Mr. Binny.

(001-070) John Why that be hin the [[united-states|United States]] of North Hamerica, and a main

(001-071) good place for poor folks.

(001-072) Binny John Wickens, you forget yourself.

(001-073) John Beg pardon, Mr. Binny.

(001-074) Binny John Wickens, leave the room.

(001-075) John But I know where Vermont be tho'.

(001-076) Binny John Wickens, get hout. [Exit John, L. 3 E.]

(001-077) Bud Dreadful low fellow, that.

(001-078) Binny Halways himpudent.

(001-079) Bud [Looking at letter in Binny's hand.] Why, that is Sir Edward's hand,

(001-080) Mr. Binny, he must have been sporting.

(001-081) Binny Yes, shooting the wild helephants and buffalos what abound there.

(001-082) Bud The nasty beasts. [Looking off, R. 2 E.] Hello, there comes Miss

(001-083) Florence tearing across the lane like a three year old colt.

(001-084) Sharp & Skil Oh, Gemini. [Run off, R. 2 E. Bud. runs off, L. 2 E.]

(001-085) Enter Florence, R. 2 E.

(001-086) Flo [As if after running.] Oh! I'm fairly out of breath. Good morning,

(001-087) Binny, the letter bag I saw coming, Wickens coming with it. I thought I

(001-088) could catch him before I reached the house. [Sits R.] So off I started,

(001-089) I forgot the pond, it was in or over. I got over, but my hat got in. I

(001-090) wish you'd fish it out for me, you won't find the pond very deep.

(001-091) Binny Me fish for an at? Does she take me for an hangler?

(001-092) Flo. Give me the letters. [Takes them.] Ah, blessed budget that descends

(001-093) upon Trenchard Manor, like rain on a duck pond. Tell papa and all, that

(001-094) the letters have come, you will find them on the terrace.

(001-095) Binny Yes, Miss. [Going, L. 3 E.]

(001-096) Flo And then go fish out my hat out of the pond, it's not very deep

(001-097) Binny [Aside.] Me fish for 'ats? I wonder if she takes me for an

(001-098) hangler? [Exit disgusted, R. 3 E.]

(001-099) Flo [Reading directions.] Lieut. Vernon. [This is a large letter with a

(001-100) large white envelope, red seal.] In her Majesty's service. Admiralty, R.

(001-101) N. Ah, that's an answer to Harry's application for a ship. Papa promised

(001-102) to use his influence for him. I hope he has succeeded, but then he will

(001-103) have to leave us, and who knows if he ever comes back. What a foolish

(001-104) girl I am, when I know that his rise in the service will depend upon it.

(001-105) I do hope he'll get it, and, if he must leave us, I'll bid him good bye

(001-106) as a lass who loves a sailor should.

(001-107) Enter Sir Edward, Mrs. M., Augusta, Capt. De Boots, Vernon, L. 3 E.

(001-108) Flo Papa, dear, here are letters for you, one for you, Mrs.

(001-109) Mountchessington, one for you, Capt. De Boots, and one for you, Harry.

(001-110) [Hiding letter behind her.]

(001-111) Ver Ah, one for me, Florence?

(001-112) Flo Now what will you give me for one?

(001-113) Ver Ah, then you have one?

(001-114) Flo Yes, there, Harry. [Gives it.]

(001-115) Ver Ah, for a ship. [Opens and reads.]

(001-116) Flo Ah! Mon ami, you are to leave us. Good news, or bad?

(001-117) Ver No ship yet, this promises another year of land lubbery. [Goes up.]

(001-118) Flo. I'm so sorry. [Aside.] I'm so glad he's not going away. But where's

(001-119) Dundreary. Has anybody seen Dundreary?

(001-120) Enter Dundreary.

(001-121) Dun Good morning, Miss Florence.

(001-122) Flo [Comes down, L.] Good morning, my Lord Dundreary. Who do you think

(001-123) has been here? What does the postman bring?

(001-124) Dun Well, sometimes he brings a bag with a lock on it, sometimes

(001-125) newspapers, and sometimes letters, I suppothe.

(001-126) Flo There. [Gives letter. Dundreary opens letter and Florence goes up R.

(001-127) Dun. knocks knees against chair, turns round knocks shins, and at last

(001-128) is seated extreme, R.]

(001-129) Dun Thank you. [Reads letter.]

(001-130) De B [Reading paper.] By Jove, old Soloman has made a crop of it.

(001-131) Dun A--what of it?

(001-132) De B I beg pardon, an event I am deeply interested in, that's all. I beg

(001-133) pardon.

(001-134) [[augustus-trenchard|Aug]] Ah! Florence, dear, there's a letter of yours got among mine. [Gives

(001-135) it.]

(001-136) Flo Why papa, it's from dear brother Ned.

(001-137) Sir E From my boy! Where is he? How is he? Read it.

(001-138) Flo He writes from Brattleboro' Vt. [Reading written letter.] ``Quite

(001-139) well, just come in from a shooting excursion, with a party of Crows,

(001-140) splendid fellows, six feet high.''

(001-141) Dun Birds six feet high, what tremendous animals they must be.

(001-142) Flo Oh, I see what my brother means; a tribe of indians called Crows,

(001-143) not birds.

(001-144) Dun Oh, I thought you meant those creatures with wigs on them.

(001-145) Flo Wigs!

(001-146) Dun I mean those things that move, breathe and walk, they look like

(001-147) animals with those things. [Moving his arms like wings.]

(001-148) Flo Wings.

(001-149) Dun Birds with wings, that's the idea.

(001-150) Flo [Reading written letter.] ``Bye-the-bye, I have lately come quite

(001-151) hap-hazard upon the other branch of our family, which emigrated to

(001-152) America at the Restoration. They are now thriving in this State, and

(001-153) discovering our relationship, they received me most hospitably. I have

(001-154) cleared up the mysterious death of old Mark Trenchard.''

(001-155) Sir E Of my uncle!

(001-156) Flo [Reading written letter.] ``It appears that when he quarreled with

(001-157) his daughter on her [[courtship-and-marriage|marriage]] with poor Meredith, he came here in search

(001-158) of this stray shoot of the family tree, found them and died in their

(001-159) house, leaving Asa, one of the sons, heir to his personal property in

(001-160) [[england|England]], which ought to belong to poor Mary Meredith. Asa is about to

(001-161) sail for the old country, to take possession. I gave him directions

(001-162) to find you out, and he should arrive almost as soon as this letter.

(001-163) Receive him kindly for the sake of the kindness he has shown to me, and

(001-164) let him see some of our shooting.'' Your affectionate brother, NED.

(001-165) Sir E An American branch of the family.

(001-166) Mrs M Oh, how interesting!

(001-167) Aug [Enthusiastically.] How delightfully romantic! I can imagine the

(001-168) wild young hunter. An Apollo of the prairie.

(001-169) Flo An Apollo of the prairie; yes, with a strong nasal twang, and a

(001-170) decided taste for tobacco and cobblers.

(001-171) Sir E Florence, you forget that he is a Trenchard, and no true Trenchard

(001-172) would have a liking for cobblers or low people of that kind.

(001-173) Flo I hate him, whatever he is, coming here to rob poor cousin Mary of

(001-174) her grandmother's guineas.

(001-175) Sir E Florence, how often must I request you not to speak of Mary

(001-176) Meredith as your cousin?

(001-177) Flo Why, she is my cousin, is she not? Besides she presides over her

(001-178) milk pail like a duchess playing dairymaid. [Sir E. goes up.] Ah!

(001-179) Papa won't hear me speak of my poor cousin, and then I'm so fond of

(001-180) syllabubs. Dundreary, do you know what syllabubs are?

(001-181) Dun Oh, yeth, I know what syllabubs is--yeth--yeth.

(001-182) Flo Why, I don't believe you do know what they are.

(001-183) Dun Not know what syllabubs are? That's a good idea. Why they

(001-184) are--syllabubs are--they are only babies, idiotic children; that's a

(001-185) good idea, that's good. [Bumps head against Florence.]

(001-186) Flo No, it's not a bit like the idea. What you mean are called

(001-187) cherubims.

(001-188) Dun What, those things that look like oranges, with wings on them?

(001-189) Flo Not a bit like it. Well, after luncheon you must go with me and I'll

(001-190) introduce you to my cousin Mary and syllabubs.

(001-191) Dun I never saw Mr. Syllabubs, I am sure.

(001-192) Flo Well, now, don't forget.

(001-193) Dun I never can forget--when I can recollect.

(001-194) Flo Then recollect that you have an appointment with me after luncheon.

(001-195) Dun Yeth, yeth.

(001-196) Flo Well, what have you after luncheon?

(001-197) Dun Well, sometimes I have a glass of brandy with an egg in

(001-198) it, sometimes a run 'round the duck-pond, sometimes a game of

(001-199) checkers--that's for exercise, and perhaps a game of billiards.

(001-200) Flo No, no; you have with me after luncheon, an ap--an ap--

(001-201) Dun An ap-- an ap--

(001-202) Flo An ap--an appoint--appointment.

(001-203) Dun An ointment, that's the idea. [Knocks against De Boots as they go up

(001-204) stage.]

(001-205) Mrs M [Aside.] That artful girl has designs upon Lord Dundreary.

(001-206) Augusta, dear, go and see how your poor, dear sister is this morning.

(001-207) Aug Yes, mamma. [Exit, L. 1 E.]

(001-208) Mrs M She is a great sufferer, my dear.

(001-209) Dun Yeth, but a lonely one.

(001-210) Flo What sort of a night had she?

(001-211) Mrs M Oh, a very refreshing one, thanks to the draught you were kind

(001-212) enough to prescribe for her, Lord Dundreary.

(001-213) Flo What! Has Lord Dundreary been prescribing for Georgina?

(001-214) Dun Yeth. You see I gave her a draught that cured the effect of the

(001-215) draught, and that draught was a draft that didn't pay the doctor's bill.

(001-216) Didn't that draught--

(001-217) Flo Good gracious! what a number of draughts. You have almost a game of

(001-218) draughts.
