# Debt and Credit

The hidden infrastructure of the play's social world. While the characters perform gentility in the drawing room, the mortgages, judgments, and foreclosures accumulating offstage threaten to collapse the entire edifice. Debt in "Our American Cousin" is not merely a plot device but a diagnostic tool: it reveals who actually has resources and who merely has appearances.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The financial crisis is announced before Asa even arrives. The Trenchard estate is encumbered by a mortgage held by Richard Coyle, and the creditors are closing in — "Boquet and Barker announce their intentions to foreclose" (003-112)(003-113). This financial pressure is what drives every character's calculation: Sir Edward's desperation, Mrs. Mountchessington's matchmaking, and ultimately the plot's resolution.

The mortgage on the Ravensdale estate (003-155) is the key financial instrument. What should be a routine transaction — the borrowing and repayment of money against landed security — has been weaponized by Coyle, who holds the mortgage not as a passive creditor but as an active predator. The missing release or receipt (003-156)(003-157) represents the vulnerability of a system that depends on paper records maintained by interested parties.

### Associated Characters

- **Sir Edward Trenchard** — the debtor whose aristocratic lifestyle has outpaced his actual resources. His mortgages and judgments (003-112) reveal that the landed gentry's wealth is often a matter of managed appearances rather than real substance.

- **Richard Coyle** — the creditor as villain. He does not merely hold the mortgage; he schemes to exploit it for his own advancement. His father's sudden death conveniently explains the absence of the release (003-158), making Coyle the sole arbiter of the Trenchards' financial fate.

- **Asa Trenchard** — the American heir who brings an entirely different relationship to money. His willingness to renounce the fortune (008-136) treats debt and credit not as instruments of power but as obligations of honor.

### Key References

> "Boquet and Barker announce their intentions to foreclose."
>
> — Act I (003-112)(003-113)

The foreclosure announcement transforms the play's financial situation from background condition to active crisis. The French name "Boquet" suggests the cosmopolitan network of credit that connects the English gentry to Continental finance.

> "A mortgage on the Ravensdale estate. But it must have been paid off, Mr. Coyle."
>
> — Sir Edward, Act I (003-155)

Sir Edward's anxious interpolation — "But it must have been paid off" — reveals that he does not actually know the state of his own finances. The gentry's relationship to debt is one of studied ignorance, outsourcing financial knowledge to solicitors like Coyle.

> "Coyle: Pardon me, the legal estate you have your equity of redemption. You have only to pay the money and the estate is yours as before."
>
> — Act I (003-173)(003-174)

Coyle's legal language — "equity of redemption" — is technically correct and morally hollow. He knows that Sir Edward cannot pay the money, making the equity of redemption a theoretical right with no practical value.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The 1850s saw significant reform in English debt law. The Bankruptcy Act of 1849 had modernized insolvency procedures, and the imprisonment for debt that had ruined Dickens's father (and inspired *Little Dorrit*) was increasingly viewed as barbaric. Yet the mortgage system that Taylor depicts — in which landed estates served as collateral for loans that funded consumption rather than investment — remained largely unreformed.

The play's American contrast is particularly pointed on the subject of debt. Asa's Vermont background represents a world where financial obligations are personal rather than institutional — where a "Yankee lawyer" who abused his position "would have Judge Lynch after him in no time" (005-148)(005-149). The implication is that English law protects creditors at the expense of debtors, while American custom protects honest men from legal predation.
