## In the Play

The language of illness runs through *Our American Cousin* primarily through two characters: Georgina Mountchessington, who is repeatedly described as a "sick girl" or "interesting invalid," and Lord Dundreary, whose physical fragility and eccentric health habits provide comic relief.

Georgina's invalidism is introduced in Act 2. Mrs. Mountchessington presents her daughter as an "interesting invalid" [002-042], a label Georgina immediately embraces: "I'm an interesting invalid" [002-043]. This self-identification suggests that illness in the play is as much a social posture as a physical condition. The phrase "interesting invalid" recurs with ironic undertones — Georgina's delicate health makes her a figure of romantic attention, particularly from Lord Dundreary, who positions himself as her protector: "Dundreary's by your side, he will protect..." [002-108].

The "sick girl" motif continues through the middle acts. References to "that sick gal" appear in Act 3 [003-058, 003-062], Act 5 [005-022], and Act 10 [010-034, 010-063, 010-064], spanning from the Trenchard Manor drawing room through to the final revelations. The colloquial "gal" form, used by the American characters Asa and others, contrasts with the formal "invalid" preferred by the English characters, highlighting a class and cultural divide in how illness is framed.

Lord Dundreary embodies a different register of illness — the hypochondriac as comic type. He is said to have been "prescribing for Georgina" [001-212], a detail that Florence finds absurd: "What! Has Lord Dundreary been prescribing for Georgina?" [001-213]. Dundreary's own health eccentricities include drinking "donkey's milk" on medical advice [002-047], and his physical mannerisms — described in stage directions as screaming, flinching, and general nervous agitation — paint a portrait of aristocratic hypochondria that serves as running comic business.

## Cultural-Historical Context

The Victorian era saw a flourishing of medical discourse around "invalidism," particularly among upper-class women. The cult of the "interesting invalid" — a woman whose frailty made her both pitiable and romantically appealing — was a recognizable social type by the 1850s. Tom Taylor draws on this convention for Georgina while simultaneously undercutting its seriousness through the broad comedy surrounding her.

The figure of the aristocratic male hypochondriac, represented by Lord Dundreary, has roots in Restoration and eighteenth-century comedy. Dundreary's fussiness about his health — the donkey's milk, the exaggerated startle responses — echoes the tradition of the fop whose bodily preoccupations signal effeminacy and triviality. Taylor's innovation is to set this familiar English comic type against the robust American characters, making health and vigor markers of national character.

The play's treatment of illness never becomes genuinely medical. No diagnosis is offered; no doctor appears onstage. The single reference to "the doctor's bill" [001-215] is a joke about expense, not treatment. The play's comic world has no room for real pathology — illness exists only as pretext for flirtation, comic business, and class commentary.
