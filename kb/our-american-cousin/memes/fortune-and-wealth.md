# Fortune and Wealth

Fortune in *Our American Cousin* is never merely money — it is social standing, marital currency, and the engine that drives every major plot turn from the opening mortgage crisis to the final-act revelation.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The play's dramatic machinery runs on fortune. Sir Edward Trenchard opens the action in desperate financial straits: "There must be money somewhere, I came into possession" [003-141] — a man who holds a manor but cannot keep it. The mortgage on Trenchard Manor hangs over every scene, threatening to reduce his children "to labor for their bread, to misery perhaps" [003-191]. Money is spoken of in hushed tones — "this is private business — money matters, my love" [002-070] — establishing it as the family's shameful secret even as it dominates every decision.

The revelation that Asa Trenchard "wasn't heir to the fortune" [008-091] triggers the play's most dramatic scene. Mrs Mountchessington's immediate response — "Not heir to the fortune, Mr. Trenchard?" [008-092] — and Augusta's blunt "What, no fortune?" [008-094] expose how completely wealth determines social worth in their world. Asa's response reframes the entire value system: "Now I've no fortune, but I'm filling" [008-108] — distinguishing between inherited wealth and personal substance.

The secondary fortune plot concerns Mary Meredith, whose financial interests have been secretly protected all along: "of her fortune all along of this bit of paper" [006-075]. When Asa declares he is "determined to give up this fortune to Mary Meredith" [008-136], he performs an act of economic justice that the English class system — for all its pretensions to honor — had failed to deliver.

| Character | Relationship to Fortune | Social Implication |

| Asa Trenchard | Inherits then renounces fortune | American: fortune is earned, not defining |
| Mary Meredith | Secretly entitled to fortune | True heir kept in ignorance through class prejudice |
| Mrs Mountchessington | Seeks fortune through daughter's marriage | Fortune as social ambition |

| Lord Dundreary | Has fortune, lacks sense | Inherited wealth without merit |

## Cultural/Historical Context

The 1850s English marriage market was explicitly financial. A young woman's "fortune" — her dowry or settlement — was publicly known or speculated upon, determining her social prospects as much as her beauty or breeding. Tom Taylor's audience would immediately recognize Mrs Mountchessington as a type: the genteel widow whose social standing depends entirely on securing wealthy matches for her daughters. The Mortgage Revelation scene lampoons this system through Asa's American lens — his refusal to be defined by his bank balance was genuinely radical to an audience for whom "no fortune" meant social death. Taylor also nods to the Victorian ideal of the "self-made man" through Asa, whose declaration "I'm filling" suggests a future fortune built on character rather than inheritance — though the play's romantic resolution ultimately renders this economic virtue moot.
