# Comedy of Manners

The genre within which "Our American Cousin" operates — a form of social satire that derives its comedy from the gap between what characters say and what they mean, between the roles they perform and the people they are. Taylor's contribution to the tradition is to introduce an American observer into the English drawing room, turning the comedy of manners into a comedy of national manners.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The comedy-of-manners structure shapes every scene. The drawing room is the genre's natural habitat — a space governed by rigid codes of address where every utterance is a performance and every silence a calculation. Taylor's innovation is the introduction of a character who does not know the code: Asa's American frankness functions as a solvent that dissolves the pretensions around him, revealing the substance (or lack thereof) beneath the manners.

Lord Dundreary represents the genre's extreme: a man whose entire existence is manner and whose manner is entirely absurd. His verbal tics and logical non sequiturs are the comedy of manners reduced to its purest form — language detached from meaning, social ritual detached from social function.

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — the character who exists outside the comedy of manners, and whose presence transforms it. His "American jokes" (003-061) are a different kind of comedy: not the witty evasion of meaning but the comic assertion of plain truth.

- **Lord Dundreary** — the comedy of manners as pathology. His verbal paralysis is the logical endpoint of a society that values form over content: when manner is everything, a man who cannot manage his manner is nothing.

- **Mrs. Mountchessington** — the comedy of manners as weapon. Her every compliment is a tactical move, her every politeness a valuation. She is the genre's most accomplished player and its most damning exhibit.

- **Florence Trenchard** — the character who learns to see through the comedy of manners. Her trajectory from participant to observer — from teasing the American cousin (002-094) to respecting his judgment — is the play's model of audience education.

### Key References

> "Flo: Oh, don't mind him! That's only an American joke."
>
> — Act I (003-061)

Florence's apology for Asa's behavior is a genre classification: "American joke" as a category of humor distinct from English wit. The line acknowledges that comedy itself has national manners.

> "Dun: For you. Ha! ha! One on that Yankee."
>
> — Act II (007-024)

Dundreary's single laugh line is the comedy of manners in miniature: a social interaction reduced to scorekeeping, wit reduced to partisanship, and a man whose entire verbal repertoire consists of filler syllables declaring victory.

> "Flo: There you go, more American."
>
> — Act I (006-096)

Florence's refrain marks the recurring collision of comic modes. "More American" is at once an observation, a complaint, and — as the play progresses — a growing compliment.

## Cultural and Historical Context

The comedy of manners has a lineage stretching from Molière through Restoration comedy (Congreve, Wycherley) to Goldsmith and Sheridan in the late 18th century. By the 1850s, the form had been domesticated into the Victorian "society comedy" — less sexually frank than its Restoration predecessors but no less interested in the hypocrisies of fashionable life.

Taylor's specifically transatlantic contribution was to make the collision of national manners the engine of the comedy. This was a canny commercial decision: the "stage Yankee" was a recognizable type in British theatre, and the "American cousin" premise allowed Taylor to exploit both the popular appetite for transatlantic comparison and the comic potential of cultural misunderstanding.
