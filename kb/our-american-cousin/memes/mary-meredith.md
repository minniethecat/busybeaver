# Mary Meredith

The milkmaid who "milks the cows, sets up the milk, superintends the churning and makes the cheese" (006-043)(006-044) — and who, by the play's end, receives the fortune that Asa renounces. Mary is the antidote to the drawing room: unpretentious, competent, and entirely herself.

## In the Play

### Role and Relationships

Mary occupies a unique structural position in the play. She is neither gentry nor servant in the conventional sense — her dairy work places her outside the class system that governs the other characters' calculations, and her exemption from that system is precisely what qualifies her for the play's reward.

Her relationship with Asa is the only connection in the play based on mutual recognition of competence rather than mutual calculation of advantage. When Asa enters the dairy and appreciates her work, the scene is the first moment of unmediated human connection in the play. Her detailed account of her duties is not a list of menial tasks but a resume of genuine accomplishment.

### Key Scenes

- **Act I — The Dairy**: Mary's catalogue of her labor — milking, setting up milk, churning, cheese-making (006-043)(006-044) — and Asa's response — "the first raal right down useful gal I've seen" (006-045) — establish the play's alternative value system.

- **Act II — The Backwoods Conversation**: Mary's genuine curiosity about Asa's American home — "I've listened to your stories until I'm half a backwoodsman's wife already" (007-139)(007-140) — represents sincere cross-cultural interest, in contrast to the Mountchessingtons' calculated flattery.

- **Act III — The Reward**: Mary's receipt of the fortune that Asa renounces is the play's most explicit moral statement. She does not scheme, calculate, or perform — and she is rewarded precisely because of it.

### Character Traits

Mary is defined by competence and sincerity. Her dairy skills are presented as genuine expertise, and her manner is free from the calculation that characterizes the drawing-room characters. She is the play's quiet argument for meritocracy — not as a political program but as a moral principle.

## Historical Context

The figure of the virtuous working woman was a staple of Victorian fiction and drama — from Dickens's heroines to the melodramatic tradition of the deserving poor. Mary Meredith belongs to this tradition but with a distinctive emphasis: her virtue is not passivity but competence. She is valued not for her suffering but for her skill, and her reward is not rescue by a male savior but the recognition of her own worth.
