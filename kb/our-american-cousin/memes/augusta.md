# Augusta Mountchessington

Augusta Mountchessington is Mrs Mountchessington's daughter and the play's most unapologetically mercenary young woman — a character whose pursuit of fortune through marriage Tom Taylor uses to satirize the transactional logic at the heart of Victorian courtship.

## In the Play

Augusta first appears among the guests at Trenchard Manor, introduced in the company of her mother and Capt. De Boots [001-107]. Her initial scenes establish her as a fashionable young woman whose interests run to archery [004-151] and the assessment of eligible gentlemen. Her mother's guidance is constant and calculating: "No, my dear Augusta, you must be very careful" [004-153] — the caution referring not to the archery field but to the matrimonial field on which Augusta is being positioned.

Augusta's defining moment arrives in the Mortgage Revelation scene. When Asa Trenchard announces he is not heir to the Trenchard fortune, Augusta's response is immediate and unvarnished: "What, no fortune?" [008-094]. The line lands with comic brutality — in four words, she exposes the financial calculus beneath every social pleasantry that has preceded it. Her mother promptly orders her from the room: "Augusta, dear, to your room" [008-115] — not as punishment for rudeness, but to cut losses on a now-worthless social investment.

Throughout the play, Augusta "casts sheep's eyes" [008-080] at Asa when she believes him wealthy, then dismisses him when his fortune evaporates. The actor E. Germon, who originated the role, played what Victorian theater called a "comic ingénue" — a young female role whose humor derives from the gap between romantic ideals and material realities.

## Character Analysis

Augusta is not a villain but a product. She has been trained by her mother to view marriage as a financial transaction, and she performs that training with an efficiency that is both appalling and, in Taylor's construction, darkly funny. Her transparency — she does not even attempt to disguise her motives — makes her less hypocritical than many of the play's more "respectable" characters.

Her relationship with her sister Georgina creates a study in contrasts: Georgina receives genuine romantic attention (from Capt. De Boots and others), while Augusta is positioned as the more aggressively marketed daughter. The archery scene — mother and daughter dressed for the sport, discussing marriage strategy — literalizes the metaphor: courtship as target practice, husbands as bullseyes.
