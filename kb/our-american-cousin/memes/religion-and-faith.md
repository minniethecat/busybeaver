# Religion and Faith

## Overview

Religious language and moral frameworks permeate *Our American Cousin* despite the play's primary identity as a comedy of manners. Characters invoke God, heaven, providence, and blessing with a frequency that reflects the Victorian era's saturation in Christian discourse. Taylor deploys religious language across the tonal spectrum—from the genuine pathos of Murcott's anguished cries to the sardonic humor of Coyle's worldly invocations—creating a world in which faith provides both consolation and a vocabulary for comic deflation.

## The Deathbed Scene and Minister's Office

The play's most sustained engagement with religious practice occurs in Asa's extended narration of his father's death. When the elder Asa Trenchard falls ill, "mother she went for a minister" [^1], establishing the clergy's role at life's threshold moments. The minister's intervention frames the deathbed as a site of spiritual struggle: the old man contends with "satan" before finally gaining "the upper hand" [^2]. This account of spiritual warfare casts the elder Asa's final charge—delivering the mortgages to the English Trenchards—as a sacred trust rather than a mere financial transaction. "The minister had gone the old man called me into his room again" [^3], suggesting that the spiritual work of the deathbed prepares the way for the practical work of inheritance.

## Murcott's Theological Anguish

Murcott is the character whose language most consistently reaches toward the theological. His confession to Florence—"I can't keep from it now, try as I will, and I try hard enough, God help me!" [^4]—frames his moral struggle in terms of divine aid sought and found wanting. The invocation of God here is not conventional but desperate, the cry of a man whose own will has proven insufficient. When he later implores, "for heaven's sake do not call back that time or I shall go mad! mad! mad!" [^5], heaven serves as the ultimate court of appeal against temporal suffering. Murcott's religious language belongs to the tradition of theatrical melodrama, where divine witness heightens the stakes of human suffering.

## Blessing and Benediction in the Final Act

The play's resolution is punctuated by a cascade of blessings. Asa's "God bless you, Miss Florence" [^6] carries the weight of his renunciation—he blesses what he cannot possess. Dundreary's comic "bless you, my children—bless you" [^7] reappropriates the language of patriarchal benediction for comic purposes while retaining its structural function as social sanction. Sir Edward's acknowledgment—"Heaven knows there is nothing that is" [^8]—and his later "may Heaven bless you" [^9] complete the circle of divine invocation that frames the play's reconciliations. These blessings are not merely conventional; they perform the work of restoring moral order after the disruptions of the plot.

## Heaven as Rhetorical Resource

Characters invoke heaven across the play's entire emotional range. Sir Edward's "Good heavens" [^10] expresses shock at the mortgage revelation. Florence's "Good Heavens, can this be true?" [^11] registers disbelief. The steward's "Heavens, is it so?" [^12] confirms a dawning recognition. Murcott's "thank Heaven" [^13] expresses relief. These invocations form a rhetorical pattern in which heaven serves as the ultimate witness to human affairs—called upon to authenticate surprise, confirm truth, and receive gratitude. The pattern is so consistent that it constitutes a minor theology of the play: a universe in which heaven is always watching, if never directly intervening.

## The Mother's Spirit

Asa's reference to his mother—"may my mother's bright spirit never look down to bless her child" [^14]—introduces a more personal, domestic form of spiritual presence. Unlike the abstract "Heaven" that characters routinely invoke, the mother's spirit represents a specific, intimate source of moral authority. The image of the departed mother looking down in blessing or judgment draws on Victorian conventions of domestic sainthood, in which the dead mother becomes a tutelary spirit watching over her children's moral choices.

## Faith as Trust and Credit

The play's financial plot intersects with its religious language through the word "faith." Florence's "I have implicit faith in my own judgement of faces" [^15] secularizes a theological term, applying the language of religious conviction to social perception. The "faithful servant" [^16] Coyle, whose fidelity to the Trenchard family is repeatedly asserted, embodies the lay meaning of faith as reliable service—a meaning that coexists with and is reinforced by its religious connotations. In a play structured around credit and debt, faith functions as the spiritual currency that underwrites all other forms of value.
