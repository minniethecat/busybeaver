# Lord Dundreary's Introduction

Lord Dundreary was not supposed to be the star of *Our American Cousin*. The play was written as a vehicle for Joshua Silsbee's Asa Trenchard — the American cousin as comic hero. But E.A. Sothern, cast as Dundreary, transformed a minor aristocrat with a handful of lines into the most celebrated comic creation of the 1858 London season. His entrance — languid, vague, trailing verbal wreckage — established a type so recognizable that "Dundrearyism" entered the language as a term for the kind of mangled aphorism the character specialized in. The introduction scene is a masterclass in making an impression through indirection: Dundreary says almost nothing coherent, and the audience cannot look away.

## In the Play

### Context

Dundreary's entrance occurs after Asa has already disrupted the drawing room with his American energy. The contrast is structural: where Asa enters shouting from offstage and demanding to see "the Squire" (002-137), Dundreary drifts in as if gravity were a suggestion. He is a guest at Trenchard Manor, a peer of the realm, and — crucially — the object of Mrs. Mountchessington's matrimonial ambitions for her daughter Georgina. His social rank is high; his intellectual wattage, by his own cheerful admission, is not.

### What Happens

**The Riddles.** Dundreary's first extended scene establishes his signature mode: the "whime," or riddle, which he cannot deliver correctly. "A whime is a widdle, you know" (002-010), he explains to Florence — the lisp transforming "rhyme" and "riddle" into sounds that require translation. His specimen: "Why is a dog's tail like the heart of a tree?" The answer — "Because it's the farthest from the bark" — is a pun he cannot land, and the comedy comes from watching him fail to explain what he himself does not understand.

His second attempt — "Why does a dog waggle his tail?" — produces an answer ("Because the tail can't waggle the dog") that is, against all odds, almost wisdom. Dundreary's "widdles" are verbal artifacts of a mind that generates linguistic patterns without quite grasping their content — the aristocratic intellect as a machine running without material.

**Donkey's Milk.** The riddles are interrupted by the revelation that Dundreary drinks donkey's milk on medical advice: "the doctor recommends me to drink donkey's milk" (002-047). The image — a peer of the realm sustained by the milk of an animal associated with stupidity — is Taylor's most compressed satirical portrait. Dundreary's frailty ("I'm so weak") is both physical and constitutional: the aristocracy has refined itself into incapacity.

**Georgina's Faint.** When Georgina Mountchessington faints — or performs a faint — at the prospect of meeting the American cousin, Dundreary's response is pure sympathetic panic: "Here, somebody get something and throw over her; a pail of water" (002-106). A gentleman's instinct, he assures Florence: "Don't scream, I won't lose my whiskers... Dundreary's by your side, he will protect you" (002-108–113). The self-reference in the third person, the fixation on his whiskers as the seat of his identity, the offer of protection from a man who cannot protect himself from a riddle — all of it constitutes the Dundreary brand.

**The Mutual Sighting.** The scene's most famous moment is the mutual sighting of Asa and Dundreary. The stage direction is precise: "Asa and Dundreary see each other for the first time. They stand looking at each other" (002-176). Two species, encountering. Asa's verdict — "Concentrated essence of baboons, what on earth is that?" (002-178) — is, in the play's economy, the American gaze reversed: the aristocrat as anthropological specimen.

Dundreary's response to Asa is not verbal but physical. When Asa punches Binny in the stomach — "This old shoat is fat enough to kill" (002-185) — Dundreary's diagnosis is immediate and utterly wrong: "Now he thinks Binny's an engine and has got a boiler" (002-190). The line is a perfect Dundrearyism: a metaphor that misidentifies its object but in doing so reveals the speaker's own mental furniture. Dundreary sees the world through the lens of mechanics he does not understand, applied to social situations he understands even less.

### Consequences

Dundreary's introduction establishes him as the play's second comic pole. If Asa is energy misapplied to decorum, Dundreary is decorum emptied of energy — the aristocrat as beautiful vacancy. The two will circle each other for the rest of the play, with Dundreary eventually proposing (and being accepted by) Georgina in the finale. But his real function is as a mirror: when Asa looks at Dundreary, he sees what English civilization has produced — and it confirms every American prejudice.

## Key References

> "A whime is a widdle, you know."
>
> — Lord Dundreary, Act I (002-010)

The lisp — "r" becoming "w" — is the audible signature of Dundreary's class. It is not a speech impediment so much as a class marker rendered as comic tic: the aristocratic mouth cannot form the sounds that commoners manage without effort.

> "the doctor recommends me to drink donkey's milk"
>
> — Lord Dundreary, Act I (002-047)

The line is delivered without irony — Dundreary has no irony — and the audience is left to supply what the character cannot: the recognition that a man who drinks donkey's milk and cannot tell a riddle is a walking argument against hereditary privilege.

> "Concentrated essence of baboons, what on earth is that?"
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act I (002-178)

The line was the play's breakout moment with American audiences. It is not merely an insult but an act of reclassification: the English lord, the apex of the social pyramid, is redefined as an ape by a man who has been in the country for ten minutes. The American Revolution, restaged as a sight gag.

> "Now he thinks Binny's an engine and has got a boiler."
>
> — Lord Dundreary, Act I (002-190)

A classic Dundrearyism: wrong in premise (Asa does not think Binny is an engine), wrong in reasoning (engines and boilers are not the relevant analogy), and yet, in its sideways way, diagnostic. Dundreary cannot understand Asa, but he knows something violent and mechanical is happening, and "engine" is the vocabulary he reaches for.

## Historical Note

E.A. Sothern initially resisted the role of Dundreary, considering it beneath him — a minor part with barely forty lines. He accepted only on condition that he be allowed to rewrite and expand the character. What Sothern created was a physical and vocal performance so distinctive that Dundreary became the play's commercial engine: audiences came for the American cousin and stayed for the English lord. "Dundrearyism" — the mangled aphorism — entered the language, and Dundreary whiskers became a fashion trend. The character's success is a lesson in the economics of 19th-century theatre: a star performance could transform a minor role into the main attraction, and a supporting character could, through sheer comic invention, become the reason a play survived.
