# Harry Vernon

The gentleman suitor whose presence offers Florence an alternative to mercenary marriage. Less flamboyant than Augustus, less disruptive than Asa, Harry Vernon represents the quiet decency that the play's more extravagant characters obscure but cannot extinguish.

## In the Play

### Role and Relationships

Harry functions as the baseline of normal decency against which the play's extremes — Dundreary's absurdity, Coyle's villainy, Asa's radical honesty — are measured. His affection for Florence is presented as genuine, and his participation in her teasing of Dundreary about American height — "Yes, that's about the average height" (002-098) — shows a man comfortable enough to play along without needing to dominate.

### Key Scenes

- **Act I — The American Joke**: Harry's participation in Florence's fabrication about American height (002-098) establishes him as a co-conspirator in her wit — a partnership that models the companionate marriage the play endorses.

### Character Traits

Harry is defined by decency, quiet competence, and the absence of the performative excess that marks the play's comic characters. He is not exciting — but in Taylor's moral scheme, not being exciting is itself a recommendation.
