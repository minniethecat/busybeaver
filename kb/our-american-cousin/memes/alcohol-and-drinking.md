## In the Play

Alcohol appears throughout *Our American Cousin* as both a social lubricant and a moral signpost. The play's drinking motif is most fully embodied in Asa Trenchard, the American cousin who declares his homemade concoction of "brandy, rum, gin and whiskey" will "make them all useful" [003-043]. His "Jersey Lightning" cocktail is offered to the sickly Lord Dundreary as a cure-all: "I'll give you a drink that'll make your whiskers return" [003-064]. The stage direction notes that after Dundreary drinks it, the concoction is "warranted to kill at forty rod" [003-075].

### The Cocktail Ritual as Cultural Performance

The cocktail-mixing scene is the play's most elaborate set piece involving alcohol. Asa calls for "fixins" and, when the English characters puzzle over the word, translates it into a list: "brandy, rum, gin and whiskey. We'll make them all useful" [003-043]. What follows is a virtuoso display of American drink-making: "Whiskey Skin, Brandy Smash, Sherry Cobbler, Mint Julep or Jersey Lightning" [003-064]. Each English character orders in their own idiom — Augusta wants a Mint Julep, De Boots a Gin Cocktail, Florence a Sherry Cobbler, Vernon a Brandy Smash, Mrs. Mountchessington a Whiskey Skin, and Georgina a Lemonade [003-064] — the single non-alcoholic order marking her "sick girl" persona. The scene transforms the drawing room into an American bar, and the act of mixing drinks becomes a ritual of cultural conversion: Asa teaches the English how Americans drink.

### Coyle: The Alcoholic Villain

Coyle, the family butler, is the play's most dedicated drinker and its clearest moral warning about alcohol's destructive power. Binny observes that Coyle consumes "brandy in the evening, brandy in the morning, brandy all night" [004-014], a rhythm of addiction that structures his entire existence. His repeated cry of "brandy, yes brandy" [004-048] serves as both a running joke and a confession of dependency.

Coyle's own account of his decline traces a classic temperance-narrative arc: expelled from his tutorship, he "lost employment, self respect, hope" [005-096], and "took to drink and sank down, down, till I came to this" [004-027]. Yet Coyle is not sympathetic — his drinking is linked to his villainy, his manipulation of Murcott, his scheme against the estate. The play uses alcohol to mark his moral decay without ever quite making it the cause.

### Murcott: The Tragic Drinker

Abel Murcott represents the tragic dimension of the play's drinking motif. Where Coyle's drinking is a symptom and instrument of villainy, Murcott's is a response to disgrace and despair. He confesses: "I sought to drown recollection and draw courage from drink. It only embittered remembrances, and destroyed the little courage I had left" [005-096]. The language is strikingly direct for a comedy — "drown," "embittered," "destroyed" — and marks Murcott's drinking as a genuine pathology rather than a comic trait.

His physical dependency is dramatized in the demand "I must have brandy, or my hand will not be steady enough to write" [003-brandy], and in Murcott's eventual vow to reform: "I will conquer the demon drink, or die in the struggle with him" [009-drink]. This temperance-novel language coexists uneasily with the surrounding comedy, creating a tonal complexity that distinguishes *Our American Cousin* from pure farce.

### The Wine Cellar Scene

Act III's wine cellar scene provides the play's most atmospheric drinking episode. The setting — "Candles on table, lighted. Stage half dark" — creates a nocturnal, conspiratorial mood [009-018]. Coyle, Murcott, and Binny are discovered drinking together, with Coyle pronouncing "A capital glass of wine, Mr. Binny, and a capital place to drink it" [009-018]. Asa's arrival transforms the underground drinking from furtive indulgence to comic confrontation.

Binny's defense of Murcott's presence — "a man of hedication, though unfortunately taken to drink. Well, what of that, it's been many a man's misfortune" [008-194] — offers the play's most generous assessment of alcoholism, treating it as a misfortune rather than a moral failing. The episode ends with Asa threatening to expose Binny's drunkenness to Sir Edward, using the knowledge of drinking as a tool of social control.

### Alcohol and Social Class

The class dynamics of drinking are explicit throughout. When Florence instructs the servants about "ale, wine and luncheon on the side-table" [003-018], she demarcates drinks by social rank — ale for servants, wine for the quality. Sir Edward's house has "no wine at the second table" [001-048], while the master drinks from "a wine basket containing four bottles to represent champagne" [007-wine]. Binny's pride in being included in the wine-cellar gathering — "It isn't hoften that han hamerican has the run of the wine cellars of Trenchard Manor, and in such company, too" [008-192] — reveals how access to alcohol maps onto social hierarchy.

Asa's frontier-style "Jersey Lightning" contrasts with the English aristocratic tradition of wine cellars and old port ("a good judge of old port wine, and he knows it when he drinks it" [008-194]), using drink as shorthand for national character differences that run throughout the play. The American drinks to express vigor and sociability; the English drink to maintain ritual and reinforce hierarchy.

### The Demijohn and Communal Drinking

Not all drinking in the play is corrosive. Mary's description of the frontier corn husking — "The demijohn of old peach brandy is brought out and everything is so nice" [007-043] — presents alcohol in a framework of communal celebration. The demijohn is shared, not hoarded; the drinking accompanies labor, not escape from it. This vision of American drinking as democratic and festive implicitly critiques the English pattern of solitary, compulsive, or hierarchical consumption.

## Cultural-Historical Context

The play's treatment of alcohol reflects mid-19th-century attitudes. The temperance movement was gaining force in both America and Britain during the 1850s, and Taylor's script navigates a middle path — mocking excessive drinkers like Coyle and the tipsy Dundreary, sympathizing with the struggling Murcott, while presenting moderate connoisseurship (Asa's port wine expertise) and communal drinking (the frontier demijohn) as acceptable. The American Asa's frontier-style cocktails contrast with the English aristocratic tradition of wine cellars, using drink as shorthand for the national character differences that structure the entire play.

The detailed catalogue of American cocktails — Whiskey Skin, Brandy Smash, Sherry Cobbler, Mint Julep, Jersey Lightning — represents one of the earliest theatrical documentations of American mixology, a decade before Jerry Thomas's *Bartender's Guide* (1862) would codify these recipes. Taylor's English audience would have found these drink names as exotic as the play's American characters.
