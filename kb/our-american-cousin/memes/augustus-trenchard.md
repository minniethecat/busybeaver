# Augustus Trenchard

The would-be romantic hero whose performance collapses the moment financial reality intrudes. Managed by his mother, motivated by calculation, and devoid of any quality that would justify his pretensions, Augustus is the play's comic demonstration that breeding without substance produces only absurdity.

## In the Play

### Role and Relationships

Augustus functions as the negative romantic option — the suitor Florence must be steered away from. His courtship is entirely scripted by his mother, who coaches him on targets and tactics. His romantic manner — "Romantically" (002-128) — is a costume he wears at his mother's direction, and it falls away the instant he learns Asa lacks a fortune: "What, no fortune?" (008-094).

### Key Scenes

- **Act I — The Romantic Pose**: Augustus's reception of the news of the American cousin — "Your American cousin. Oh, how delightfully romantic" (002-128) — is performance so transparent that even his mother must manage it.

- **Act III — The Collapse**: His three-word response to Asa's lost fortune — "What, no fortune?" (008-094) — is the play's most economical character demolition. Three words reduce the romantic suitor to the fortune-hunter he always was.

### Character Traits

Augustus is defined by transparency — his pretenses are so poorly performed that they function as self-exposure. He is mercenary without being clever, ambitious without being competent, and romantic without being sincere. He is what his mother would be if she lacked her intelligence.
