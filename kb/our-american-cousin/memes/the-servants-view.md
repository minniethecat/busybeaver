# The Servant's View

The servants' hall in *Our American Cousin* is not merely background noise — it is a running annotation on the main action, delivered in a register the drawing room cannot hear. Taylor gives the servants, particularly Binny and Sharpe, their own miniature drama of status competition, gossip, and class resentment. This subplot operates as a comic mirror: while the gentry upstairs fret over inheritance and courtship, the servants downstairs fret over precedence and perquisites, and the structural parallel is the point.

## In the Play

### Context

The play opens not with the Trenchard family but with the servants — a choice that signals Taylor's interest in the full household ecology. Before Sir Edward, Florence, or Asa has spoken a word, we hear Sharpe complaining about the "meaner servants" (001-049) and Binny asserting her authority over "lower servants" (001-054). The hierarchy below stairs is as rigid, and as policed, as the one above.

Binny, the senior female servant, occupies a position analogous to a housekeeper or upper maid. Her speech — marked throughout by dropped aitches and Cockney vowels — identifies her class as precisely as Sir Edward's rhetoric identifies his. She is not a comic stereotype but a dramatic function: the servant who sees everything and understands more than she is told.

### What Happens

The servant subplot unfolds through a series of entrances, exits, and collisions. Binny's first exchange with Sharpe establishes the pecking order immediately: "Mind your hown business instead hof your betters" (001-053). When Sharpe complains about the quality of the servants — "meaner servants I never seed" (001-049) — the irony is that Sharpe herself is being judged by the same standard she applies to others.

John Wickens, the footman, provides the next complication. His entrance with the letter bag triggers Binny's authority: "John Wickens, you forget yourself" (001-072), escalating to "John Wickens, leave the room" (001-074) and finally "John Wickens, get hout" (001-076). The progression from remonstrance to command to dismissal traces a miniature dramatic arc — and Wickens's "himpudence" (001-078) mirrors, in comic miniature, the insubordination that the gentry characters fear from their social inferiors.

Binny's exchange with Buddicombe over the letter reveals her cleverness. When Buddicombe notes Sir Edward's handwriting on a letter, Binny invents a fantasy of "shooting the wild helephants and buffalos what abound there" (001-081), referring to America. The absurdity of the claim — she knows it is false — is a small act of narrative power: Binny controls what the gentry believe about the world beyond the manor.

### Consequences

The servant scenes do not advance the main plot, but they establish the play's tonal register and its social argument. By placing class friction in the servants' hall before we see it in the drawing room, Taylor naturalizes hierarchy itself — everyone, at every level, polices their inferiors. The difference is that the servants' power struggles are openly acknowledged as petty, while the gentry's are cloaked in manners. Binny's running commentary — "Me fish for 'ats?" (001-097) — is the play's own sotto voce admission that much of what passes for social life is performance and surveillance.

## Key References

> "Mind your hown business instead hof your betters."
>
> — Binny, Act I (001-053)

The dropped aitches ("hown," "hof") perform class on the page. Binny's first line is a rebuke, and it establishes her as the enforcer of the servant hierarchy — the exact parallel to Sir Edward's role upstairs.

> "And meaner servants I never seed."
>
> — Sharpe, Act I (001-049)

Sharpe's complaint is structurally identical to every complaint the gentry make about their social inferiors: the people below me are not good enough. The play's recursive class logic is laid out in this one line.

> "John Wickens, get hout."
>
> — Binny, Act I (001-076)

The escalating sequence — "forget yourself" → "leave the room" → "get hout" — is a three-beat joke that also performs Binny's authority. Each command is more direct, more vulgar, and more effective than the last.

> "Me fish for 'ats?"
>
> — Binny [Aside], Act I (001-097)

Binny's aside — questioning whether she is being asked to fish for hats — is the play's smallest and most self-aware joke. It acknowledges that social performance, even downstairs, depends on pretending to understand what one does not.

## Historical Note

The comic servant was a stock figure of 19th-century British theatre, from Shakespeare's clowns through the Restoration fop's footmen to the Victorian butlers of farce. Taylor's innovation is to give the servants their own dramatic ecosystem rather than using them solely as plot devices. Binny is not merely a messenger or a malapropist; she enforces a hierarchy, manages information, and, in her asides, critiques the action. The Cockney stage dialect — standardized by mid-century through Dickens and Punch caricature — was familiar enough to audiences that Taylor could use it for both comedy and characterization without needing to explain it.
