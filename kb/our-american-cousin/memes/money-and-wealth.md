# Money and Wealth

## Overview

Money is the invisible protagonist of *Our American Cousin*—the force that sets the plot in motion, determines social relations, and ultimately provides the medium through which moral character is revealed. From the mortgage that threatens Trenchard Manor to the "half sheet of paper" that transforms Asa's position, financial instruments and monetary language saturate the play's dialogue. Taylor uses money not merely as plot mechanism but as a lens through which to examine character, class, and the moral economy of generosity.

## The Mortgage as Plot Engine

The entire action of the play turns on a financial instrument: the mortgage that Sir Edward Trenchard has taken on the family estate. His financial mismanagement—the result of aristocratic imprudence rather than villainy—creates the vulnerability that drives every subsequent plot development. The mortgage revelation transforms Asa from an amusing American cousin into the effective master of Trenchard Manor, inverting the expected power relations between English aristocracy and American rusticity. The financial plot thus serves as Taylor's structural critique of a class system in which inherited position no longer guarantees economic security.

## Asa's Relationship to Wealth

Asa Trenchard's attitude toward money defines his character. Unlike the English characters who treat wealth as a given of social position, Asa understands money as something earned, managed, and deployed. His account of the dying father's charge—to deliver the mortgages to the English relations—establishes Asa as the executor of a financial trust. His ultimate decision to tear up the mortgage represents not indifference to wealth but a moral judgment about its proper use: "it wouldn't have been a bad bargain" [^1] he reflects, but bargain is not the standard by which he finally acts.

## Coyle and the Servant's Economy

Richard Coyle, the family agent and "most faithful servant of the family" [^2], embodies the intersection of money and service. As the man who handles the mortgages and the London financial agents, Coyle understands the Trenchard finances better than Sir Edward himself. His position as a paid dependent who wields actual financial knowledge creates a recurring tension between formal subordination and practical power—a tension that Taylor exploits for both comic and dramatic effect.

## Mrs. Mountchessington's Financial Calculus

Mrs. Mountchessington's matchmaking is transparently financial. Her interest in Lord Dundreary as a husband for her daughter Augusta is an investment strategy, and her dismissal of Asa—"at the same time, you must be cautious" [^3]—reflects the cold arithmetic of the marriage market. When she complains that Asa has "got two of them gals off your hands" [^4], she reduces matrimony to a clearance sale. Taylor's satire of the marriage market as financial transaction is among the play's sharpest social criticism.

## The Language of Credit and Exchange

Financial language permeates the play's dialogue beyond the explicit mortgage plot. Characters speak of "exchange" [^5], of being "worth" something, of "bargains" and "prizes." Dundreary's courtship is described in the language of competition with a material prize. The recurrent vocabulary of credit and debt—both financial and moral—structures the play's understanding of social relations. To be "obliged" to someone is to owe them; to be "faithful" is to be creditworthy.

## Generosity as Moral Currency

The play's resolution elevates generosity above mere wealth. Asa's renunciation of his financial claim transforms money from a source of conflict into a medium of grace. The cascade of blessings in the final act—"God bless you, Miss Florence" [^6], "may Heaven bless you" [^7]—substitutes spiritual currency for the financial instruments that drove the plot. Taylor's moral economy ultimately values generosity over accumulation, a judgment that aligns American moral clarity with Christian charity against English aristocratic calculation.
