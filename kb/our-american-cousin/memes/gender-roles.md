# Gender Roles

## Overview

*Our American Cousin* navigates the gender conventions of Victorian society with a mixture of compliance and subversion. Female characters negotiate the constraints of the marriage market, while male characters perform competing models of masculinity—from Asa's American vigor to Dundreary's aristocratic effeteness. Taylor's comedy derives much of its energy from the gap between prescribed gender roles and individual behavior, exposing the artificiality of norms even as it ultimately restores characters to their conventional positions.

## The Marriage Market and Female Agency

The play's female characters operate within the narrow parameters of the marriage market. Mrs. Mountchessington's entire presence is organized around securing advantageous matches for her daughters—a project she pursues with calculating energy. Yet Taylor grants his heroines more agency than the system ostensibly permits. Florence Trenchard's assertion of her own romantic judgment—"Papa, I have implicit faith in my own judgement of faces" [^1]—claims epistemological authority within the very domain (courtship) where women were expected to be passive. Mary Meredith, the poor relation, speaks openly of her ill-use: "Poor Wickens is not the only one who thinks I am a very ill-used young body" [^2], giving voice to the structural injustice of a system that values women primarily for their marriageability.

## Mrs. Mountchessington and the "Old Woman of the World"

Mrs. Mountchessington embodies a specific female type: the worldly older woman who has mastered the marriage market through long experience. Her self-description as "an old woman of the world" [^3] is both admission and boast—she has survived the game and now plays it expertly on behalf of her daughters. Asa's irreverent responses to this self-presentation—"Be you an old woman of the world?" [^4], "Wal, old woman? Got two of them gals off your hands, haven't you?" [^5]—deflate her pretensions by reducing her worldly wisdom to a series of transactions. The comic repetition of "old woman" [^6] throughout their exchanges strips the phrase of its self-congratulatory edge and exposes the vulnerability beneath Mrs. Mountchessington's performance of mastery.

## Asa's American Masculinity

Asa Trenchard presents a model of masculinity explicitly identified with American identity. His physical appetites—the "half a dozen ham sandwiches, eight or ten boiled eggs, two or three pumpkin pies" consumed on the rail journey [^7]—establish a masculinity grounded in bodily vigor rather than social performance. His directness with women, his comfort with manual labor, and his ultimate generosity in renouncing his financial claim all articulate a masculine ideal that values action over appearance. Yet Asa's masculinity is not uncomplicated: his lament that the English ladies are "trying to get me on a string" [^8] reveals anxiety about female manipulation that coexists with his apparent confidence.

## Dundreary's Effete Aristocracy

Lord Dundreary represents everything that Asa's American masculinity is not: he is physically uncoordinated, intellectually muddled, and apparently indifferent to the romantic pursuits that drive the plot. His signature "dundrearyisms" mark him as a figure of compromised masculinity, unable to complete a thought or execute a physical action without comic failure. When set against Asa's competence, Dundreary's effeteness reads as a critique of aristocratic masculinity—a suggestion that inherited wealth and position produce not strength but enervation.

## Feminine Accomplishment and Domestic Ideology

The play references feminine accomplishment—the skills that defined the marriageable young woman—with characteristic ambiguity. Georgina Mountchessington's musical performances, Dundreary's comic engagement with "dairy" as feminine pursuit, and the general expectation that young ladies be "interesting" rather than competent all sketch a system of feminine education designed to produce marriageable surfaces rather than capable individuals. Taylor exposes the absurdity of this system without directly challenging its premises—a balancing act typical of the comedy of manners.

## Georgina's Secret Hunger

Georgina Mountchessington's appetite—both literal and figurative—complicates the play's gender dynamics. While her mother schemes to present her as a delicate, desirable commodity on the marriage market, Georgina's desires (for food, for attention, for autonomy) exceed the narrow bounds of acceptable feminine behavior. This gap between the performed role of the eligible young lady and the actual self that strains against its constraints generates some of the play's sharpest comic moments while gesturing toward a critique of the gender system that the play's resolution leaves unresolved.

## The Feminization of Moral Authority

Despite the patriarchal structures that govern the play's social world, Taylor assigns significant moral authority to his female characters. Florence's judgment of character proves more reliable than her father's. Mary Meredith's honesty about her own position generates the sympathy that ultimately secures her happiness. Even Mrs. Mountchessington, for all her calculating ways, operates as an agent within the system rather than a passive victim of it. The play's resolution—in which male generosity (Asa's renunciation) enables female happiness—may reassert patriarchal structure, but the moral intelligence that drives the plot toward that resolution belongs largely to the women.
