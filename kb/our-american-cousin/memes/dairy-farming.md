# Dairy Farming

Dairy farming in "Our American Cousin" functions as more than a picturesque backdrop — it is the symbolic language through which the play argues for the moral superiority of productive labor over inherited leisure. Asa's expertise in "cow juice" (006-040) and Mary Meredith's competence in the dairy establish a community of genuine skill that stands in deliberate contrast to the financial and moral bankruptcy of the drawing room.

## In the Play

### Role in the Plot

The dairy serves as the play's alternative social space — a site where competence, not rank, determines standing. When Asa enters the dairy and recognizes Mary's skill, the scene presents the first genuine connection in the play unmediated by inheritance concerns (006-040)(006-041)(006-042). His admiration is for her *work*, not her lineage. This valuation of productive labor over inherited status is the play's central moral argument, and the dairy is where it finds its fullest expression.

The dairy also serves as the site of cultural translation. Florence's inability to understand Asa's American idiom — "I don't understand American yet" (006-054) — is comically resolved when he translates "small potatoes" into French: the dairy becomes a space where class boundaries are rendered absurd rather than insurmountable (006-055).

### Associated Characters

- **Asa Trenchard** — his first instinct upon entering the English manor is to find the dairy and demonstrate his practical knowledge. His expertise in "cow juice" (006-040) and his appreciation for Mary's labor (006-045) establish him as a character who judges by work, not title.

- **Mary Meredith** — the milkmaid who "milks the cows, sets up the milk, superintends the churning and makes the cheese" (006-043)(006-044). Her competence with dairy work is presented not as menial labor but as authentic accomplishment — the quality that ultimately makes her the play's moral victor.

- **Florence Trenchard** — her encounter with Asa in the dairy forces her to confront a world where her aristocratic status carries no weight. Her teasing about American height and language (002-097) gives way to genuine engagement.

### Key References

> "Asa: Wal, I guess I do know something about cow juice."
>
> — Act I, the dairy (006-040)

Asa's first self-assertion in the English manor is a claim of practical agricultural knowledge. "Cow juice" — comic in its plainness — is also a declaration of identity: he knows what he knows, and he will not translate it into drawing-room language.

> "Mary: Yes, sir, I milk the cows, set up the milk, superintend the churning and make the cheese."
>
> — Act I (006-043)(006-044)

Mary's catalogue of her dairy duties is the play's most complete job description. In a world of vague aristocratic "expectations" and "prospects," she alone can name exactly what she does.

> "Asa: Wal, darn me if you ain't the first raal right down useful gal I've seen on this side of the big pond."
>
> — Act I (006-045)

Asa's highest compliment — "useful" — encapsulates the American value system the play champions. In a society that prizes ornamental accomplishment, he prizes utility.

## Cultural and Historical Context

Dairy farming in the 1850s was a distinctly gendered form of agricultural labor. In both England and America, dairy work was traditionally women's work — the "dairymaid" was a recognized rural occupation. Taylor's choice to make Mary a dairy worker and to have Asa knowledgeable about dairying positions both characters within a tradition of pastoral virtue that stretches back to classical literature.

For Asa's Vermont origins, dairy farming carried additional significance. By the 1850s, Vermont had established itself as a center of dairy production, particularly cheese-making. Asa's dairy knowledge is not generic rusticity but a specific regional marker — he is not just any American but a Vermont American, from a state associated with independent smallholders rather than plantation agriculture.
