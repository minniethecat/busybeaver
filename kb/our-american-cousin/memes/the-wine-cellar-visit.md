# The Wine Cellar Visit

The wine cellar scene is *Our American Cousin* at its most structurally audacious: a single set piece that is simultaneously a drinking bout, a pickpocketing, a physical brawl, and the hinge on which the entire inheritance plot turns. Asa descends into the cellar to get Coyle drunk, lift his keys, and search his office for the release document. He succeeds at all three, and the scene's farcical violence — knockings-down, kickings-over, blind pummeling in the dark — serves a genuine dramatic purpose. The American who talked his way through Acts I and II becomes, in the cellar, an operator.

## In the Play

### Context

Coyle, Sir Edward's agent, has been systematically defrauding the Trenchard estate. Abel Murcott, Coyle's alcoholic clerk, has confessed the scheme to Asa but cannot prove it: the release document that would clear the mortgage is locked in Coyle's private bureau. Earlier, Asa has predicted that "the key stone of their fraud is just the point your mighty cute rascals always leave unsecured" (005-155–157). The wine cellar scene is where Asa tests that theory — by getting Coyle too drunk to protect his keys.

The setting is carefully established: "Scene 3.--Wine cellar in 3. Coyle, Murcott and Binny discovered. Table L., with two cups and bottles. Candles on table, lighted. Door in flat with staircase discovered, dark. Stage half dark" (009-001–005). The darkness and the staircase are plot devices as much as atmosphere: Asa needs both the cover of darkness and the escape route the staircase provides.

### What Happens

**The Welcome.** Coyle, believing Asa to be a naive American who poses no threat, greets him with mock-ceremonial warmth: "Glad to see you, to welcome you to the vaults of your ancestors" (009-014). Asa's reply — "Wal, you seem to be punishing their spirits pretty well" (009-015–016) — carries the double edge Coyle misses: Asa is not merely making a drinking joke but noting that Coyle has been consuming Trenchard resources.

**The Acoustics.** Coyle, ever the conspirator, boasts about the cellar's soundproofing: "Here you might bawl yourself hoarse beneath these ribs of stone, and nobody hear you" (009-018–020). He shouts and sings to demonstrate. Asa's aside — "That's worth knowing" (009-021) — is the scene's turning point: what Coyle intends as a boast about privacy, Asa hears as a guarantee that violence in the cellar will go undetected.

**Binny's Disintegration.** Binny, already drunk before the scene begins, delivers an increasingly incoherent toast to his own gentility: "Hi always hacts and conducts myself has becomes a gentleman, hand Hi knows what's due to manners" (009-025–027). He falls into his chair. The butler's pretension — aspiring to the manners of the class he serves while literally collapsing from their wine — is the scene's most pointed class joke.

**The Keys.** When Coyle passes out, Asa acts: "Now, then, quick, Murcott, before the butler comes back, get his keys" (009-041). Murcott lifts the keys from Coyle's pocket. The bureau key, however, remains on Coyle's watch chain — "I can't get it off" (009-044). Asa's response — "Never mind, I'll take the responsibility" (009-046) — marks his transition from comic observer to moral agent. He is willing to steal what needs stealing.

**The Violence.** When Coyle stirs, Murcott hesitates. Asa does not: "Well, darn me, knock him down again" (009-049). When Murcott cannot, Asa can: "Can't you? Well, I can. [Pulls Murcott away. Knocks Coyle down; is going towards D. in F., meets Binny with tray and glasses; kicks it, knocks Binny down and exits up staircase, followed by Murcott, carrying candle]" (009-050–054). The stage direction is a miniature farce — the kick, the tray, the tumble — but its function is serious: Asa now has the keys and is heading to Coyle's office.

**The Aftermath.** Binny and Coyle rise blindly in the dark and "pummel soundly till change" (009-057) — each believing the other to be the intruder. The conspiratorial villain and his drunken butler, beating each other in absolute darkness, is the play's most purely farcical image. And it is earned: Asa has engineered a perfect comic exit, leaving his enemies to fight each other.

### Consequences

The wine cellar scene enables everything that follows. With Coyle's keys, Asa and Murcott search the office and eventually break open the cabinet (the-cabinet-scene), recovering the release document that exposes Coyle's fraud. More importantly, the scene completes Asa's character development: the man who entered the play as a talker has become a doer. The cellar — dark, subterranean, outside the drawing room's codes — is where Asa's American pragmatism finds its proper arena.

## Key References

> "Glad to see you, to welcome you to the vaults of your ancestors."
>
> — Coyle, Act III (009-014)

Coyle's irony — the vaults are not Asa's yet, and Coyle means to keep it that way — is the dramatic irony the audience savors. Coyle believes he is patronizing a rube; we know Asa is three moves ahead.

> "Here you might bawl yourself hoarse beneath these ribs of stone, and nobody hear you."
>
> — Coyle, Act III (009-018–020)

The line is a boast that becomes a self-indictment. Coyle has chosen the cellar because it is soundproof — and the soundproofing that protects him from being overheard will also, moments later, prevent anyone from hearing him being knocked unconscious.

> "Now, then, quick, Murcott, before the butler comes back, get his keys."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act III (009-041)

The line is a command, not a suggestion. Asa has assessed the situation and is directing the operation. The comic character has become the tactical leader — and Murcott, the trained clerk, obeys.

> "Can't you? Well, I can. [Knocks Coyle down.]"
>
> — Stage direction and Asa, Act III (009-050–053)

The stage direction does not elaborate — no struggle described, no resistance registered. Asa simply knocks Coyle down. The American's "stout arm" (005-137), promised in Act II, is here demonstrated. Physical competence is the scene's final argument.

## Historical Note

The drinking scene as plot mechanism has a long theatrical pedigree — from Falstaff's Boar's Head exploits through the Restoration comedy of intrigue to the Victorian farces of Taylor's contemporaries. What distinguishes Taylor's cellar scene is its integration of farce and thriller mechanics. The keys are not merely a prop but a genuine plot necessity; the drunkenness is not merely comic but strategically induced; and the violence, while farcical in staging, has real consequences. The wine cellar bridges the play's two modes — drawing-room comedy and inheritance melodrama — more efficiently than any other scene.
