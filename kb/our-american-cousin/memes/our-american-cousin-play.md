# Our American Cousin (play)

The three-act comedy by English playwright Tom Taylor (1817–1880) that premiered at [[laura-keenes-theatre]] in New York on October 15, 1858. The play's extraordinary popularity — 150 performances in its initial New York run, countless revivals on both sides of the Atlantic, and an indelible association with the [[dundrearyisms|cultural phenomenon of "Dundrearyisms"]] — made it one of the most commercially successful English-language comedies of the 19th century.

## Overview

"Our American Cousin" occupies a unique position in theatrical history. It was the play Abraham Lincoln was watching at Ford's Theatre on April 14, 1865, when he was assassinated — a historical accident that has permanently colored its reputation. But even without that tragic association, the play represents a significant moment in the development of transatlantic popular theatre: an English comedy that used American character and American values as the engine of its moral resolution, produced by a female theatre manager, and featuring a comic performance (E. A. Sothern's Lord Dundreary) that would become one of the most imitated in theatrical history.

The play's plot — an American cousin arrives at a financially distressed English manor, disrupts its class system with democratic directness, and ultimately saves the family through honest merit rather than aristocratic pretension — was Taylor's canny synthesis of several popular theatrical traditions: the stage Yankee, the comedy of manners, and the inheritance melodrama.

## Key Details

### Creation and Production

Tom Taylor wrote "Our American Cousin" in the late 1850s, drawing on his knowledge of both English society (as a barrister and civil servant) and American culture (through correspondence and travel accounts). The script was acquired by Laura Keene for her New York theatre, where it opened with Keene herself in the role of Florence Trenchard and E. A. Sothern as Lord Dundreary.

The play's most significant creative deviation from Taylor's text came from Sothern, who transformed the minor role of Lord Dundreary — originally a walk-on part with fewer than 50 lines — into a comic tour de force through improvisation, physical business, and the invention of "Dundrearyisms": absurd logical inversions delivered in an elaborate drawl. Sothern's performance was so popular that Taylor was effectively forced to expand the role for the play's London production.

### Significance

The play has three distinct legacies:

1. **Theatrical**: As a vehicle for Sothern's Lord Dundreary, the play established a new type of stage comedy — the dandified aristocrat as absurdist philosopher — that influenced comic performance for decades.

2. **Cultural**: The play's celebration of American democratic values over English aristocratic pretension captured a mid-19th-century transatlantic moment, when American audiences were eager to see their national character vindicated on stage.

3. **Historical**: The Lincoln assassination made "Our American Cousin" an unavoidable reference in American history, though the play itself has nothing to do with the tragedy that occurred during its performance.

## Notable Elements

> The published text contains "very few 'Dundrearyisms'" (001-011) because Sothern's improvisations were never fully incorporated into the authorized version. The script as printed is thus an incomplete record of the play as performed — a reminder that 19th-century theatre was an actor's medium, and the text was often a scaffold for performance rather than the performance itself.

> The cast list names Laura Keene as Florence Trenchard and E. A. Sothern as Lord Dundreary (001-017)(001-028), embedding the original production's personnel in the published text and creating a document that is simultaneously script and production history.
