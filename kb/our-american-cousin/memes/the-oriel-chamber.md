## Dramatic Role

The Oriel Chamber appears as the setting for Act II, Scene 1, noted in the stage directions simply as "Oriel Chamber in one" [005-1623]. The term "in one" is a 19th-century theatrical shorthand indicating a shallow set piece played before the front (first) set of grooves—a scene meant to be played close to the audience while a more elaborate set change occurs behind.

The scene hosted in the Oriel Chamber is a pivotal one for the play's social satire. Mrs. Mountchessington and her daughter Augusta enter "dressed for Archery Meeting" [005-1624], their costumes signaling their participation in the ritualized leisure activities of the English gentry. The ensuing dialogue exposes the nakedly mercenary calculations behind their aristocratic manners. Mrs. Mountchessington instructs Augusta: "I don't by any means want you to give up De Boots, his expectations are excellent, but, pray be attentive to this American savage, as I rather think he will prove the better match of the two" [005-1626].

The chamber thus becomes the setting for a tutorial in social predation. Mrs. Mountchessington's educational prescriptions for her daughter are a masterpiece of period satire: "I think if you read up Sam Slick a little, it might be useful, and just dip into Bancroft's History of the United States, or some of Russell's Letters; you should know something of George Washington, of whom the Americans are justly proud" [005-1638]. This reading list—a comic blend of genuine 19th-century Americanist texts—reveals the Mountchessingtons' view of America as an exotic territory to be studied for exploitation, not a culture to be engaged with seriously.

Asa's entrance "in Archery Dress" [005-1641] completes the scene's comedy of costume. Augusta's extravagant flattery—"Oh, Mr. Trenchard, why did you not bring me one of those lovely Indian's dresses of your boundless prairie?"—and Mrs. Mountchessington's echo—"Yes, one of those dresses in which you hunt the buffalo" [005-1646]—prompt Asa's deflating aside: "Buffaloes down in Vermont" [005-1679]. His deadpan response that "them dresses are principally the nateral skin, tipped off with paint, and the indians object to parting with them" [005-1680] demonstrates that the "American savage" sees through the performance perfectly.

The staging "in one" is significant: by bringing the scene to the front of the stage, Taylor ensures the audience catches every flicker of facial expression as the Mountchessingtons' flattery and Asa's ironic deflection play out in intimate proximity.

## Symbolic Function

The Oriel Chamber occupies an intermediate position in the manor's symbolic geography. Unlike the Drawing Room (formal public display) or the Chamber (private domesticity), the Oriel Chamber is a transitional space—a room for passing encounters rather than settled occupation. This architectural liminality mirrors the scene's dramatic function: the Mountchessingtons are "passing through" in their courtship campaign, and Asa is passing through their pretenses with his American clear-sightedness.

The chamber's defining feature—the oriel window—carries its own symbolic weight. An oriel window projects outward from the wall, offering a view that extends beyond the room's physical boundaries. In this scene, the Mountchessingtons are looking outward toward Asa's wealth, while Asa is looking through their social performance to the calculation beneath.

## Historical Basis

Oriel windows were a hallmark of English Gothic and Tudor architecture, particularly in manor houses built or renovated during the Gothic Revival of the early-to-mid 19th century. Trenchard Manor, as an old English country house, would plausibly feature such architectural elements. The Gothic Revival, championed by architects like Augustus Pugin, reached its peak in the 1840s–1850s—precisely when Taylor was writing *Our American Cousin*.

Mrs. Mountchessington's recommended reading list is historically authentic. Thomas Chandler Haliburton's *Sam Slick* stories (1836–) presented a Yankee clockmaker-philosopher to English readers and helped establish the stage Yankee archetype. George Bancroft's *History of the United States* (1834–1874) was the period's standard American history. William Howard Russell's *Letters* from the Crimean War and later from America were widely read. Taylor's inclusion of these specific titles suggests an audience that would recognize them—and appreciate the comedy of a scheming mother prescribing them as courtship research.

The archery meeting for which the Mountchessingtons are dressed was a authentic feature of Victorian country-house culture. Archery was one of the few sports in which mixed-gender participation was socially acceptable, making it an ideal setting for the kind of chaperoned courtship that the Mountchessingtons are pursuing.
