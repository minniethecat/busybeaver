(007-001) [[lord-dundreary|Dun]] Slightly.

(007-002) Asa Wal, what do you think of him, on an average?

(007-003) Dun Think of a man on an average?

(007-004) Asa Wal, I think he's a real hoss, and he wants a ship.

(007-005) Dun Well if he's a real hoss, he must want a carriage.

(007-006) Asa Darn me, if that ain't good.

(007-007) Dun That's good.

(007-008) Asa Yes, that is good.

(007-009) Dun Very good.

(007-010) Asa Very good, indeed, _for you_.

(007-011) Dun Now I've got it. [Tries to sneeze.]

(007-012) Asa Wal, now, I say. [Dundreary trying to sneeze.]

(007-013) Asa What, are you at that again?

(007-014) Dundreary business. Asa bites his finger. Dundreary goes up, stumbles

(007-015) against chair and comes down again.

(007-016) Dun I've got the influenza.

(007-017) Asa Got the what?

(007-018) Dun He says I've got a wart. I've got the influenza.

(007-019) Asa That's it exactly. I want your influence, sir, to get that ship.

(007-020) Dun That's good.

(007-021) Asa Yes, that's good, ain't it.

(007-022) Dun Very good.

(007-023) Asa Yes, darn me, if that ain't good.

(007-024) Dun For you. Ha! ha! One on that [[asa-trenchard|Yankee]].

(007-025) Asa Well done, Britisher. Wal, now, about that ship?

(007-026) Dun I want all my influence, sir, for my own w--w--welations.

(007-027) [Stammering.]

(007-028) Asa Oh! you want it for your own w--w--welations. [Mimicing.]

(007-029) Dun I say, sir. [Asa pretends deafness. This bus. is ad. lib.]

(007-030) Asa Eh?

(007-031) Dun He's hard of hearing, and thinks he's in a balloon. Mister.

(007-032) Asa Eh?

(007-033) Dun He thinks he can hear with his nose. I say--

(007-034) Asa Eh?

(007-035) Dundreary turns Asa's nose around with his thumb. Asa puts his two hands

(007-036) up to Dundreary's.

(007-037) Dun Now he thinks he's a musical instrument. I say--

(007-038) Asa What?

(007-039) Dun You stutter. I'll give you a k--k--k--

(007-040) Asa No you won't give me a kick.

(007-041) Dun I'll give you a c--c--card to a doctor and he'll c--c--c--

(007-042) Asa No he won't kick me, either.

(007-043) Dun He's idiotic. I don't mean that, he'll cure you.

(007-044) Asa Same one that cured you?

(007-045) Dun The same.

(007-046) Asa Wal, if you're cured I want to stay sick. He must be a mighty smart

(007-047) man.

(007-048) Dun A very clever man, he is.

(007-049) Asa Wal, darn me, if there ain't a physiological change taking place.

(007-050) Your whiskers at this moment--

(007-051) Dun My whiskers!

(007-052) Asa Yes, about the ends they're as black as a niggers in billing time,

(007-053) and near the roots they're all speckled and streaked.

(007-054) Dun [Horror struck.] My whiskers speckled and streaked?

(007-055) Asa [Showing bottle.] Now, this is a wonderful invention.

(007-056) Dun My hair dye. My dear sir.

(007-057) Asa [Squeezing his hand.] How are you?

(007-058) Dun Dear Mr. Trenchard.

(007-059) Puts arm on shoulder. Asa repeats Dundreary business, putting on

(007-060) eyeglass, hopping round the stage and stroking whiskers.

(007-061) Dun He's mad, he's deaf, he squints, stammers and he's a hopper.

(007-062) Asa Now, look here, you get the Lieut. a ship and I'll give you the

(007-063) bottle. It's a fine swap.

(007-064) Dun What the devil is a swap?

(007-065) Asa Well, you give me the ship, and I'll give you the bottle to boot.

(007-066) Dun What do I want of your boots? I haven't got a ship about me.

(007-067) Asa You'd better make haste or your whiskers will be changed again.

(007-068) They'll be a pea green in about a minute.

(007-069) Dun [Crosses to L.] Pea green! [Exits hastily into house.]

(007-070) Asa I guess I've got a ring in his nose now. I wonder how that sick gal

(007-071) is getting along? Wal, darn me, if the dying swallow ain't pitching into

(007-072) ham and eggs and home-made bread, wal, she's a walking into the fodder

(007-073) like a farmer arter a day's [[labor-and-worth|work]] rail splitting. I'll just give her a

(007-074) start. How de do, Miss, allow me to congratulate you on the return of

(007-075) your appetite. [Georgina scream.] Guess I've got a ring in her pretty

(007-076) nose now. [Looks off, R.] Hello! here comes the lickers and shooters,

(007-077) it's about time I took my medicine, I reckon.

(007-078) Enter, from R. 2. E., [[sir-edward-trenchard|Sir E]]., Mrs. M. Florence, Vernon, [[augusta|Augusta]], De

(007-079) Boots, Wickens, [[richard-coyle|Coyle]], [[sharpe|Sharpe]], [[binny|Binny]], [[skillet|Skillet]], [[buddicombe|Buddicombe]], two [[servants-and-service|servants]]

(007-080) in livery, carrying tray and glasses, a wine basket containing four

(007-081) bottles to represent champagne, knife to cut strings, some powerful acid

(007-082) in one bottle for Asa--pop sure.

(007-083) Sir E Now to distribute the prizes, and drink to the health of the

(007-084) winner of the golden arrow.

(007-085) [[florence-trenchard|Flo]] And there must stand the hero of the day. Come, kneel down.

(007-086) Asa Must I kneel down?

(007-087) Flo I am going to crown you Capt. of the Archers of [[trenchard-manor|Trenchard Manor]].

(007-088) Asa [Aside to Florence.] I've got the ship.

(007-089) Flo No; have you?

(007-090) Sir E Come, ladies and gentlemen, take from me. [Takes glasses, Starts

(007-091) on seeing me in livery.] Who are these strange faces?

(007-092) [[mr-coyle|Coyle]] [In his ear.] Bailiffs, Sir Edward.

(007-093) Sir E Bailiffs! Florence I am lost.

(007-094) [Florence supports her father. At the same moment Dundreary enters with

(007-095) letter and [[fortune-and-wealth|money]]. Georgina appears at [[dairy-farming|dairy]] door as Dundreary comes

(007-096) down, L. Asa cuts string of bottle, cork hits Dundreary. General

(007-097) commotion as drop descends.]

(007-098) ACT III.

(007-099) Scene 1--Dairy set as before in Act 2d, Scene 2.

(007-100) [Asa discovered on bench, R. C., whittling stick. [[mary-meredith|Mary]] busy with milk

(007-101) pans in dairy.]

(007-102) Asa Miss Mary, I wish you'd leave off those everlasting dairy fixings,

(007-103) and come and take a hand of chat along with me.

(007-104) Mary What, and leave my work? Why, when you first came here, you thought

(007-105) I could not be too industrious.

(007-106) Asa Well, I think so yet, Miss Mary, but I've got a heap to say to you,

(007-107) and I never can talk while you're moving about so spry among them pans,

(007-108) pails and cheeses. First you raise one hand and then the other, and

(007-109) well, it takes the gumption right out of me.

(007-110) Mary [Brings sewing down.] Well, then, I'll sit here--[sits on bench

(007-111) with Asa, vis-a-vis.] Well now, will that do?

(007-112) Asa Well, no, Miss Mary, that won't do, neither; them eyes of yourn

(007-113) takes my breath away.

(007-114) Mary What will I do, then?

(007-115) Asa Well, I don't know, Miss Mary, but, darn me, if you could do

(007-116) anything that wasn't so tarnal neat and handsome, that a fellow would

(007-117) want to keep on doing nothing else all the time.

(007-118) Mary Well, then, I'll go away. [Rises.]

(007-119) Asa [Stopping her.] No, don't do that, Miss Mary, for then I'll be left

(007-120) in total darkness. [She sits.] Somehow I feel kinder lost, if I haven't

(007-121) got you to talk to. Now that I've got the latitude and longitude of all

(007-122) them big folks, found out the length of every lady's foot, and the

(007-123) soft spot on everybody's head, they can't teach me nothing; but here,

(007-124) [Whittling.] here I come to school.

(007-125) Mary Then throw away that stick, and put away your knife, like a good

(007-126) boy. [Throws away stick up stage.] I must cure you of that dreadful

(007-127) trick of whittling.

(007-128) Asa Oh, if you only knew how it helps me to keep my eyes off you, Miss

(007-129) Mary.

(007-130) Mary But you needn't keep your eyes off me.

(007-131) Asa I'm afraid I must, my eyes are awful tale-tellers, and they might be

(007-132) saying something you wouldn't like to hear, and that might make you mad,

(007-133) and then you'd shut up school, and send me home feeling about as small

(007-134) as a tadpole with his tail bobbed off.

(007-135) Mary Don't be alarmed, I don't think I will listen to any tales that

(007-136) your eyes may tell unless they're tales I like and ought to hear.

(007-137) Asa If I thought they'd tell any other, Miss Mary, I pluck them right

(007-138) out and throw them in the first turnip patch I came to.

(007-139) Mary And now tell me more about your home in America. Do you know I've

(007-140) listened to your stories until I'm half a backwoodsman's wife already?

(007-141) Asa [Aside.] Wouldn't I like to make her a whole one.

(007-142) Mary Yes, I can shut my eyes and almost fancy I see your home in the

(007-143) backwoods. There are your two sisters running about in their sunbonnets.

(007-144) Asa Debby and Nab? Yes!

(007-145) Mary Then I can see the smoke curling from the chimney, then men and

(007-146) boys working in the fields.

(007-147) Asa Yes.

(007-148) Mary The girls milking the cows, and everybody so busy.

(007-149) Asa Yes.

(007-150) Mary And then at night, home come your four big brothers from the hunt

(007-151) laden with game, tired and foot sore, and covered with snow.

(007-152) Asa That's so.

(007-153) Mary Then how we lasses bustle about to prepare supper. The fire blazes

(007-154) on the hearth, while your good old mother cooks the slapjacks.

(007-155) Asa [Getting very excited.] Yes.

(007-156) Mary And then after supper the lads and lasses go to a corn husking. The

(007-157) demijohn of old peach brandy is brought out and everything is so nice.

(007-158) Asa I shall faint in about five minutes, Miss Mary you're a darned sight

(007-159) too good for this country. You ought to make tracks.

(007-160) Mary Make what?

(007-161) Asa Make tracks, pack up, and emigrate to the roaring old state of

(007-162) [[vermont|Vermont]], and live 'long with mother. She'd make you so comfortable, and

(007-163) there would be sister Debby and Nab, and well, I reckon I'd be there,

(007-164) too.

(007-165) Mary Oh! I'm afraid if I were there your mother would find the poor

(007-166) English girl a sad incumbrance.

(007-167) Asa Oh, she ain't proud, not a mite, besides they've all seen Britishers

(007-168) afore.

(007-169) Mary I suppose you allude to my cousin, Edward Trenchard?

(007-170) Asa Well, he wan't the only one, there was the old Squire, Mark

(007-171) Trenchard.

(007-172) Mary [Starting Aside.] My grandfather!

(007-173) Asa Oh! he was a fine old hoss, as game as a bison bull, and as gray as

(007-174) a coon in the fall; you see he was kinder mad with his folks here, so

(007-175) he came over to America to look after the original branch of the family,

(007-176) that's our branch. We're older than the Trenchard's on this side of the

(007-177) water. Yes we've got the start of the heap.

(007-178) Mary Tell me, Mr. Trenchard, did he never receive any letters from his

(007-179) daughter?

(007-180) Asa Oh yes, lots of them, but the old cuss never read them, though. He

(007-181) chucked them in the fire as soon as he made out who they come from.

(007-182) Mary [Aside.] My poor mother.

(007-183) Asa You see, as nigh as we could reckon it up, she had gone and got

(007-184) married again his will, and that made him mad, and well, he was a queer

(007-185) kind of a rusty fusty old coon, and it appeared that he got older, and

(007-186) rustier, and fustier and coonier every fall, you see it always took him

(007-187) in the fall, it was too much for him. He got took down with the ague, he

(007-188) was so bad the doctors gave him up, and mother she went for a minister,

(007-189) and while she was gone the old man called me in his room, `come in, Asa,

(007-190) boy,' says he, and his voice rang loud and clear as a bell, `come in,'

(007-191) says he. Well I comed in; `sit down,' says he; well I sot down. You see

(007-192) I was always a favorite with the old man. `Asa, my boy,' says he, takin'

(007-193) a great piece of paper, `when I die, this sheet of paper makes you heir

(007-194) to all my property in [[england|England]]'. Well, you can calculate I pricked up my

(007-195) ears about that time, bime-by the minister came, and I left the room,

(007-196) and I do believe he had a three day's fight with the devil, for that

(007-197) old man's soul, but he got the upper hand of satan at last, and when

(007-198) the minister had gone the old man called me into his room again. The

(007-199) old Squire was sitting up in his bed, his face as pale as the sheet that

(007-200) covered him, his silken hair flowing in silvery locks from under his red

(007-201) cap, and the tears rolling from his large blue eyes down his furrowed

(007-202) cheek, like two mill streams. Will you excuse my lighting a cigar? For

(007-203) the story is a long, awful moveing, and I don't think I could get on

(007-204) without a smoke. [Strikes match.] Wal, says he to me, and his voice was

(007-205) not as loud as it was afore--it was like the whisper of the wind in

(007-206) a pine forest, low and awful. `Asa, boy,' said he, 'I feel that I've

(007-207) sinned in hardening my heart against my own flesh and blood, but I will

(007-208) not wrong the last that is left of them; give me the light,' says he.

(007-209) Wal I gave him the candle that stood by his bedside, and he took the

(007-210) sheet of paper I was telling you of, just as I might take this. [Takes

(007-211) will from pocket.] And he twisted it up as I might this, [Lights will,]

(007-212) and he lights it just this way, and he watched it burn slowly and slowly

(007-213) away. Then, says he, `Asa, boy that act disinherits you, but it leaves

(007-214) all my property to one who has a better right to it. My own daughter's

(007-215) darling child, Mary Meredith,' and then he smiled, sank back upon his

(007-216) pillow, drew a long sigh as if he felt relieved, and that was the last

(007-217) of poor old Mark Trenchard.
