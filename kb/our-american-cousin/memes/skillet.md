# Skillet

Skillet is a senior servant at Trenchard Manor whose presence anchors the below-stairs world that mirrors and comments upon the family's social entanglements in *Our American Cousin*.

## In the Play

Skillet appears in the play's opening tableau — "discovered on sofa reading newspaper" [001-043] alongside Buddicombe and Sharpe — an image that immediately establishes the servant class's parallel domestic life. As a senior servant, Skillet exercises authority over the lower staff: Mr. Binny relays that "Mrs. Skillet says he—" [001-051], deferring to her domestic judgment. She appears in group servant scenes alongside Sharpe, Binny, and Buddicombe [004-146], particularly at moments when the household's normal order is disrupted by the family's schemes.

In the final act, Buddicombe reports that "Skillet and I have made so bold" [010-054], suggesting the servants' autonomous initiative — a quiet assertion of agency within their circumscribed roles. The actor Mrs. M. Levick, who originated the role, was part of the production's senior character-actress contingent.

## Character Analysis

Skillet represents the settled hierarchy of the Victorian country house. Unlike the younger, more ambitious servants who might challenge or mock their masters, Skillet embodies the established order — reading the newspaper in the servants' hall, managing the household's rhythms, and observing the family's crises with the detachment of long service. Her very name — "Skillet" — is a comic reduction to function, a common theatrical naming convention for servant characters that places her in a tradition stretching from Restoration comedy to Victorian farce.
