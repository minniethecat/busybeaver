# The Cabinet Scene

The most physically violent moment in *Our American Cousin* is also the play's most decisive plot turn. Asa Trenchard, presented throughout as a figure of comic talk, suddenly becomes a figure of action: he takes an axe to a locked cabinet and, in doing so, breaks open not just the furniture but the conspiracy that has driven the inheritance plot. The scene is Taylor's most efficient fusion of farce and thriller mechanics.

## In the Play

### Context

By Act III, the [[the-foreclosure-threat|mortgage crisis]] has been forestalled but not resolved. Coyle, Sir Edward's agent, has been manipulating the Trenchard finances for his own benefit. Abel Murcott, Coyle's clerk, knows the truth but lacks the evidence to prove it. The key — both literal and figurative — is in a locked cabinet "where his father kept old deeds" (009-105).

Asa has spent two and a half acts talking. The cabinet scene is where he acts — and the choice of tool is the play's most American gesture. Where an English character might summon a locksmith, Asa reaches for the nearest heavy object.

### What Happens

Murcott identifies the cabinet: "That's a cabinet where his father kept old deeds, the key he always" (009-105). The unfinished sentence is itself a plot mechanism — the key is missing, the lock is sealed, and the evidence that would clear Murcott's name is physically inaccessible.

Asa's solution appears in the stage direction: "[Re-enters with axe.] Here's a key that will open any lock that Hobb" (009-110). The pun on "key" — an axe is not a key, except that it is — condenses the scene's logic. American directness (apply force to the obstacle) bypasses English indirection (find the proper key, observe the proper forms).

The stage direction is blunt: "[Shows axe, begins to break open Cabinet.]" (009-113). In a play dominated by drawing-room conversation, this moment of physical destruction registers as both comedy and genuine dramatic stakes. The cabinet yields its contents, Coyle's treachery is exposed, and Murcott's redemption becomes possible.

### Consequences

The cabinet scene performs three functions simultaneously. It resolves the inheritance plot (the recovered deeds establish the true state of the Trenchard finances). It redeems Murcott (his knowledge is vindicated, his loyalty proven). And it completes Asa's character arc: the talker becomes a doer, the comic observer becomes the agent of resolution.

### Scene Structure

| Phase | Action | Characters | Tone |
|-------|--------|------------|------|
| Identification | Murcott reveals the cabinet's significance | Murcott, Asa | Tense, expository |
| Decision | Asa fetches the axe | Asa | Comic, decisive |
| Destruction | The cabinet is broken open | Asa, Murcott | Physical, climactic |
| Revelation | Documents recovered, Coyle exposed | All | Satisfying, conclusive |

## Key References

> "That's a cabinet where his father kept old deeds, the key he always"
>
> — Abel Murcott, Act III (009-105)

Murcott's interrupted sentence literalizes the problem: the key — to the cabinet, to the truth, to justice — is missing. The English system of proper procedure has failed.

> "Here's a key that will open any lock that Hobb"
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act III (009-110)

Asa's pun enacts his method: redefining the problem in American terms. A key is whatever opens the lock. An axe, properly applied, is a key. This is not philosophy — it is pragmatism, and it works.

> "[Shows axe, begins to break open Cabinet.]"
>
> — Stage direction, Act III (009-113)

The stage direction's economy mirrors the action it describes. No dialogue, no negotiation — just the swing. In a play of words, this moment of wordless action carries disproportionate dramatic weight.

## Historical Note

The "papers in the locked cabinet" is a standard device of 19th-century melodrama, traceable through Dickens and Collins. Taylor's innovation is to give the revelation scene to a comic character wielding an inappropriate tool. The axe is funny — and it is also, in a play about an American inheriting an English estate, a symbol of the force required to break open closed systems.
