# Time and Change

## Overview

Time and change pervade *Our American Cousin* as both structural device and thematic preoccupation. The play unfolds against a ticking clock—the mortgage deadline that threatens Sir Edward Trenchard's estate—while characters undergo personal transformations ranging from the comically superficial (Dundreary's changing whiskers) to the profoundly consequential (Asa's moral evolution from heir to benefactor). Taylor's handling of temporal urgency and metamorphosis reflects the mid-nineteenth-century preoccupation with change as both opportunity and threat.

## The Mortgage Deadline as Temporal Pressure

The plot's primary engine is temporal: Sir Edward's mortgage falls due, and ruin "stares me in the face" [^1]. This financial deadline creates dramatic urgency, compressing the play's courtship plots and class confrontations into a narrow window. Each scene registers the pressure of impending time—Florence must secure a match, Asa must decide whether to claim his inheritance, and the older generation must confront the consequences of past financial decisions. Taylor uses this temporal compression to heighten both comic misunderstandings and genuine pathos.

## Asa's Physiological Change

The play's most explicitly comic treatment of change occurs in Asa's observation of his own transformation under the influence of English surroundings. "Wal, darn me, if there ain't a physiological change taking place," Asa exclaims, noting that "your whiskers at this moment—" [^2] have begun to alter in color. The whisker comedy—"about the ends they're as black as a niggers in billing time" [^3]—and Dundreary's rejoinder that "you'd better make haste or your whiskers will be changed again" [^4] transforms the theme of personal metamorphosis into physical farce. Yet beneath the comedy lies a serious point: immersion in a new environment produces genuine change, a theme that resonates with the play's broader interest in the transformative encounter between America and England.

## Sudden Reversals of Fortune

Taylor repeatedly dramatizes the speed with which circumstances can change. Mary Meredith describes herself as "the penniless orphan of an hour ago, no longer penniless" [^5]—a transformation effected by Asa's generosity. The mortgage revelation, too, operates through temporal reversal: Sir Edward learns that the threat he has feared for months has already been neutralized by the very American cousin whose arrival he anticipated with anxiety. These reversals, compressed into tight dramatic time, suggest that change in *Our American Cousin* is not gradual but instantaneous, a matter of revelation rather than process.

## The Stage Direction of Change

Taylor's stage directions encode change spatially. The instruction "Two chairs and table brought on at change" [^6] marks a scene transition, while the recurring direction "[Change]" [^7] signals shifts in setting that parallel shifts in character relationships. These theatrical mechanisms remind the audience that change is not merely a theme but the medium of drama itself—each scene change carries the potential for transformed circumstances.

## Murcott and the Refusal of the Past

Murcott's anguished plea—"for heaven's sake do not call back that time or I shall go mad! mad! mad!" [^8]—represents the play's darkest treatment of temporal themes. Unlike the comic reversals that benefit the young lovers, Murcott's past is a source of trauma that resists integration into the present. His refusal to revisit earlier events contrasts sharply with Asa's willingness to transform inherited obligations into acts of generosity, suggesting that the difference between comic and tragic outcomes lies in one's relationship to time.

## Asa's Rail Journey: Time in Transit

Asa's description of his transatlantic journey—"comin' along on the cars I worried down half a dozen ham sandwiches, eight or ten boiled eggs, two or three pumpkin pies" [^9]—presents time in transit as a period of consumption and anticipation. The journey time between America and England, between old identity and new circumstances, is filled with the materiality of appetite. This temporal interlude, narrated rather than staged, establishes Asa's capacity for navigating change through practical action.

## Theatrical Time and Historical Time

*Our American Cousin* occupies a specific historical moment—1858, performed at Laura Keene's Theatre in New York—while depicting a world in transition. The play itself, as the editorial apparatus notes, "evolved over the course of time, indeed in multiple directions" [^10], its text as fluid as the social world it represents. This meta-theatrical awareness of time as both dramatic medium and historical condition lends the play's treatment of change a reflexive dimension absent from more static comedies of manners.
