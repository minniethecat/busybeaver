# Binny

## Overview

Binny is the housekeeper at Trenchard Manor, a figure of domestic authority whose Cockney accent—marked by dropped aspirates and distinctive vowel shifts—provides the play's most sustained example of class-marked speech. Her interactions with the footman John Wickens establish her as a force of household discipline, while her comic indignation at being accused of "fishing for hats" reveals a pride that transcends her servant status.

## Cockney Speech and Social Class

Binny's accent is the most phonetically rendered in the play. Her characteristic dropping of initial "h"—"Mind your hown business instead hof your betters" [^1], "John Wickens, get hout" [^2], "Halways himpudent" [^3]—marks her as Cockney, a London working-class dialect that would have been immediately recognizable to Taylor's audience. The orthographic representation of her speech serves both comic and social functions: it generates laughter through phonetic distortion while simultaneously positioning Binny within a specific class location that her authority within the household cannot transcend.

## The Letter Bag Scene

Binny's most sustained scene occurs around the arrival of the letter bag from America. Her entrance with the bag—"Enter Binny, L." [^4], carrying the documents that will transform the plot—establishes her as an agent of information flow within the household. When Buddicombe speculates that "Binny, he must have been sporting" [^5], Binny's response—"Yes, shooting the wild helephants and buffalos what abound there" [^6]—reveals a comic imagination of America populated by exotic fauna, a working-class fantasy of the New World that parallels but does not duplicate the gentry's misconceptions.

## "Me Fish for 'Ats?"

Binny's outrage at an implied slight—"Me fish for an at?" [^7], repeated in an aside as "Me fish for 'ats?" [^8]—is one of the play's most memorable comic moments. The accusation that she might be "fishing for hats" (angling for tips or gifts) wounds her dignity precisely because it touches on the servant's perpetual anxiety about the boundary between service and servility. Binny's repetition of the phrase, first aloud and then in an aside, suggests a mind that cannot let go of an insult, turning it over in search of an adequate response.

## Authority Over Wickens

Binny's interactions with John Wickens, the footman, establish a clear hierarchy within the servant class. Her escalating commands—"John Wickens, you forget yourself" [^9], "John Wickens, leave the room" [^10], "John Wickens, get hout" [^11]—move from admonition to expulsion with bureaucratic precision. The comic repetition of the full name "John Wickens" in each command mimics the formal address of a superior to a subordinate, the housekeeper's equivalent of a magistrate's sentence.

## The Housekeeper as Moral Guardian

Binny's authority extends beyond domestic management to moral supervision. Her instruction to "remember to check hidle curiosity" [^12] positions her as the guardian of household propriety, policing the boundaries between servants' legitimate duties and illegitimate snooping. Her parting judgment on Wickens—"Halways himpudent" [^13]—is a character assessment delivered with the finality of a judge, the dropped H lending comic weight to moral certainty.

## Significance

Binny exemplifies Taylor's treatment of servant characters as more than functional background. Her distinctive speech, her bristling authority, and her wounded pride make her a memorable comic creation in her own right. She also serves a structural function: her Cockney accent anchors the play's class system in audible difference, reminding the audience that in Victorian England, class was something you could hear.
