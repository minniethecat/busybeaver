# Richard Coyle

The lawyer whose father's convenient death has left him in sole possession of the documentary evidence that determines the Trenchard estate's fate. His schemes — foreclosure, forced marriage, financial manipulation — are presented in the language of legal precision, and the gap between his professional manner and his predatory intent is the play's most damning portrait of institutional corruption.

## In the Play

### Role and Relationships

Coyle is the play's primary antagonist, but his villainy is expressed through legal forms rather than overt malice. He holds the mortgage on the Ravensdale estate (003-155), and his report that "neither exists" (003-157)(003-158) — referring to the release or receipt that would prove the mortgage satisfied — places the Trenchards entirely in his power.

His motivation extends beyond profit to personal animus. His soliloquy — "there, you dog, will not that be a rare revenge" (004-037) — reveals a vendetta against Sir Edward that predates the events of the play. The legal precision of his schemes is powered by an emotional engine he cannot entirely conceal.

### Key Scenes

- **Act I — The Mortgage Scene**: Coyle's calm enumeration of the Trenchards' financial exposure — judgments, foreclosures, the missing release — establishes him as a man who wields legal knowledge as a weapon. His "Pardon me, the legal estate you have your equity of redemption" (003-173) is technically correct and morally hollow.

- **Act I — The Revenge Soliloquy**: Alone on stage, Coyle drops his professional mask: "there, you dog, will not that be a rare revenge" (004-037). The moment is the play's most explicit revelation of hidden motivation.

### Character Traits

Coyle is defined by the gap between professional form and personal content. His legal language is precise and his legal reasoning sound — which makes his villainy more disturbing, not less. He represents a system in which institutional procedures can be weaponized against the vulnerable, and his eventual defeat is not legal but moral: Asa's renunciation renders his schemes irrelevant.

## Historical Context

The figure of the predatory solicitor was a staple of Victorian fiction — from Dickens's Tulkinghorn in *Bleak House* to Trollope's dishonest lawyers. The legal profession's expansion in the 19th century, combined with the complexity of property law and the vulnerability of the indebted gentry, created real conditions that the fiction reflected and amplified.
