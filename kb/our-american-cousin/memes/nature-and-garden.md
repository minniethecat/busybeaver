## In the Play

Nature in *Our American Cousin* functions less as an active presence than as a framed backdrop — literally, in the case of the Act 1 stage direction that establishes a "fine English park" visible through the drawing-room window [001-038]. This framing is characteristic of the play's approach to the natural world: it is something to be viewed from within the civilized interior, admired for its pictorial qualities, not entered or engaged with.

The single explicit garden reference comes in Act 4, when a character invokes "Adam and Eve, in the garden of Eden" [004-136]. The biblical allusion is comic rather than reverent, reducing Eden to a punchline. A brief mention of "garden" in Act 5 [005-025] consists of a single-word question — too fragmentary to build thematic weight around, but confirming that garden imagery hovers at the edges of the dialogue.

The park, by contrast, carries more consistent meaning. The "fine English park" of Act 1 and the park reference in Act 3 [003-212] both appear in connection with the English landed gentry's aesthetic control of landscape. The English park — cultivated, designed, framed by architecture — embodies the values of order and tradition that Trenchard Manor represents.

Against this cultivated English nature, Taylor sets the American wilderness, concentrated in the single word "Vermont." Asa Trenchard's origin in "Vermont" [001-068, 004-178] — the "old country" contrast he carries [001-161] — represents an alternative relationship to the land. Vermont connotes rural authenticity, self-sufficiency, and distance from the mannered social world of the English drawing room. Asa's Vermont is "too good for this country" [007-159], in his own estimation, a judgment that inverts the expected hierarchy between fashionable England and provincial America.

## Cultural-Historical Context

The English landscape park was an established signifier of landed wealth by the 1850s. Designed by figures like Capability Brown in the previous century, the "natural-looking" park was in fact a highly artificial construction — a fact that resonates with the play's broader interest in performance and social artifice. The park visible through the window is, like so much in *Our American Cousin*, a staged view.

The American pastoral tradition, by contrast, emphasized the unmediated encounter with wilderness. Asa's Vermont represents this tradition in compressed form — not the romantic sublime of the Hudson River School painters, but a more pragmatic, vernacular relationship to the land. The play's nature imagery, thin as it is, participates in a larger nineteenth-century conversation about what "nature" means on opposite sides of the Atlantic.
