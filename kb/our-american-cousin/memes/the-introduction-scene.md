# The Introduction Scene

Asa Trenchard does not enter *Our American Cousin* — he erupts into it. His arrival is preceded by a letter, announced by a flustered butler, and met with a mixture of curiosity and dread by the assembled English gentry. From his first line — "Where's the Squire?" (002-137) — Asa violates every convention of the drawing room. He shouts from offstage, calls the butler a "shoat," tries to kiss his cousin, and upon seeing Lord Dundreary, delivers the play's most quoted line: "Concentrated essence of baboons" (002-178). The introduction scene is not merely exposition — it is Taylor's thesis statement, performed as farce.

## In the Play

### Context

Before Asa appears, the household has been primed by Sir Edward's letter from America, read aloud in the opening scene. The letter announces the imminent arrival of "Asa Trenchard, one of the sons" of the American branch, "heir to his personal property in England, which ought to belong to poor Mary Meredith" (001-159–160). Florence immediately declares: "I hate him, whatever he is, coming here to rob poor cousin Mary" (001-173). Sir Edward cautions: "Florence, you forget that he is a Trenchard, and no true Trenchard would do an unworthy act" (001-171).

The stage is thus set for a confrontation between English expectation (the heir as gentleman, the American as curiosity) and American reality (Asa as he actually is — brash, tactile, funny, and entirely unimpressed by English gentility).

### What Happens

The arrival unfolds in comic beats, each one escalating the disruption:

**Announcement.** Binny, flustered, announces "a gent what says he's hexpected" (002-120) — a man who refuses to give his name and instead offers to play the butler "a game of seven hup" (002-122). The impropriety begins before Asa is even visible.

**Offstage voice.** Asa shouts from outside the door: "Where's the Squire?" (002-137). Florence corrects him — "Do you mean Sir Edward Trenchard, sir?" — and Asa replies "Yes" without the slightest apology (002-138–139). The American has no time for titles.

**Physical entrance.** Asa enters and immediately assesses the room's occupants. His first target is Binny, whom he calls "the consarnedest old shoat I ever did see since I was baptized Asa Trenchard" (002-148–149). The self-introduction — "I'm Asa Trenchard, born in Vermont, suckled on sap, and raised on thunder" — is both declaration of identity and declaration of difference.

**The kiss.** Asa identifies Florence as "one of the family" and moves to kiss her: "Wal, I ain't got no objections to kiss you, as one cousin ought to kiss another" (002-155–156). Florence recoils. The English cousins, she explains, "content themselves with shaking hands" (002-162). Asa accepts the substitution: "That'll do about as well. I won't kiss you if you don't want me to" (002-164–165). The moment encapsulates the scene's method: Asa proposes an American intimacy, the English retreat to form, and a compromise is struck — but not without the English position having been shown as cold and self-denying.

**Dundreary.** The scene's climax is the mutual sighting of Asa and Lord Dundreary. "Asa and Dundreary see each other for the first time. They stand looking at each other" (002-176). Asa's verdict — "Concentrated essence of baboons, what on earth is that?" (002-178) — was the line that made the play famous with American audiences. It is the American looking at the English aristocrat and finding him not impressive but incomprehensible — a reversal of the expected power dynamic that audiences in 1858 found exhilarating.

### Consequences

The introduction scene establishes every dynamic the play will develop. Asa's refusal to observe English social forms marks him as the agent of disruption the plot requires. Florence's mixture of fascination and horror presages the romantic comedy. Dundreary's wounded incomprehension launches his running gag. And the central question — what happens when American energy enters an English system — is posed not as abstract debate but as physical comedy.

## Key References

> "If you please, Miss, 'ere's a gent what says he's hexpected."
>
> — Binny, Act I (002-120)

Binny's Cockney bewilderment — the man has no card, no name, only a pack of cards and an offer of "seven hup" — frames Asa's arrival as a category violation. He is "expected" but not recognizable within the system that expects him.

> "Where's the Squire?"
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act I (002-137)

The word "Squire" — rural, American, egalitarian — announces Asa's social register before we see him. Florence's correction to "Sir Edward Trenchard" is the play's first explicit collision of American directness with English formality.

> "Wal, I ain't got no objections to kiss you, as one cousin ought to kiss another."
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act I (002-155–156)

The line is both an assertion of kinship and a test of English manners. Asa knows what "one cousin ought" to do; the English have forgotten. His offer of physical warmth functions as social criticism disguised as American forwardness.

> "Concentrated essence of baboons, what on earth is that?"
>
> — Asa Trenchard, Act I (002-178)

The line that defined the play for its original American audience. It reverses the transatlantic gaze: the English aristocrat is the exotic, the incomprehensible, the object of anthropological curiosity. In a play about an American inheriting England, this is the moment the inheritance begins.

## Historical Note

The "American cousin" was a recognizable type on the mid-19th-century English stage — part bumpkin, part sage, entirely foreign. Taylor's innovation was to make the cousin the hero rather than the butt of the joke. Joshua Silsbee's performance as Asa (later replaced by E.A. Sothern as Dundreary, who stole the show) established the character as a vehicle for American self-assertion at a moment — 1858, three years before the Civil War — when American identity was being negotiated in every cultural register. The introduction scene's comedy depends on the audience understanding that Asa is right: his American manners are not worse than English manners, merely different, and perhaps more honest.
