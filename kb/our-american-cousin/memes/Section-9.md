(009-001) Scene 3.--Wine cellar in 3.

(009-002) [[richard-coyle|Coyle]], [[murcott|Murcott]] and [[binny|Binny]] discovered. Table L., with two cups and

(009-003) bottles. [[mr-coyle|Coyle]] L. of table, seated. [[mr-binny|Binny]] back of table. Murcott sitting

(009-004) on barrel, R. Door in flat with staircase discovered, dark. Stage half

(009-005) dark. Candles on table, lighted.

(009-006) Coyle A capital glass of wine, Mr. Binny, and a capital place to drink

(009-007) it.

(009-008) Asa [Without.] Bring a light here, can't you. I've broken my natural

(009-009) allowance of shins already.

(009-010) Enters D. in F., down stairs.

(009-011) Asa [To Murcott.] Is he tight yet?

(009-012) Mur Histered, but not quite gone yet.

(009-013) Coyle Oh, [[asa-trenchard|Mr. Trenchard]], glad to see you, to welcome you to the vaults

(009-014) of your ancestors.

(009-015) Asa Oh! these are the vaults of my ancestors, are they? Wal, you seem to

(009-016) be punishing their spirits pretty well.

(009-017) Binny Wines, Mr. Asa? The spirits are in the houter cellar.

(009-018) Coyle Oh, Mr. Asa, there is no place like a wine cellar for a hearty

(009-019) bout. Here you might bawl yourself hoarse beneath these ribs of stone,

(009-020) and nobody hear you. [He shouts and sings very loud.]

(009-021) Asa Oh, wouldn't they hear you? [Aside.] That's worth knowing.

(009-022) Binny [Very drunk--rising.] That's right, Mr. Coyle, make as much noise

(009-023) as you like, you are in the cellars of [[trenchard-manor|Trenchard Manor]], Mr. Coyle. Mr.

(009-024) Coyle, bless you, Mr. Coyle. Mr. Coyle, why his hit Mr. Coyle, I am

(009-025) sitting at the present time, in this present distinguished company? I

(009-026) will tell you, Mr. Coyle, hit his because Hi always hacts and conducts

(009-027) myself has becomes a gentleman, hand Hi knows what's due to [[class-and-manners|manners]].

(009-028) [Falls in chair.]

(009-029) Asa Steady, old hoss, steady.

(009-030) Binny Hi'm steady. Hi always was steady. [Staggers across to L.H.] Hi'm

(009-031) going to fetch clean glasses. [Exit, L. 3 R.]

(009-032) Asa Now, Mr. Coyle, suppose you give us a song.

(009-033) Coyle [Very drunk.] I can't sing, Mr. Trenchard, but I sometimes join in

(009-034) the chorus.

(009-035) Asa Wal, give us a chorus.

(009-036) Coyle Will you assist in the vocalization thereof?

(009-037) Asa [Mimicing.] Will do the best of my endeavors thereunto.

(009-038) Coyle [Sings.] ``We won't go home till morning.'' Repeat.

(009-039) Repeat [Falls off chair, senseless.]

(009-040) Asa [Finishing the strain.] ``I don't think you'll go home at all.''

(009-041) Now, then, quick, Murcott, before the butler comes back, get his keys.

(009-042) [Murcott gets keys from Coyle's pocket and throws them to Asa.] Is this

(009-043) all?

(009-044) Mur No; the key of his private bureau is on his watch chain, and I can't

(009-045) get it off.

(009-046) Asa Take watch and all.

(009-047) Mur No; he will accuse us of robbing him.

(009-048) Asa Never mind, I'll take the responsibility. [Coyle moves.]

(009-049) Mur He is getting up.

(009-050) Asa Well, darn me, knock him down again.

(009-051) Mur I can't.

(009-052) Asa Can't you? Well, I can.

(009-053) [Pulls Murcott away. Knocks Coyle down; is going towards D. in F., meets

(009-054) Binny with tray and glasses; kicks it, knocks Binny down and exits

(009-055) up staircase, followed by Murcott, carrying candle. Dark state. Binny

(009-056) rises; Coyle ditto. Blindly encounter each other and pummel soundly till

(009-057) change.

(009-058) Quick Change

(009-059) Scene 4--Chamber in 1, same as Scene 2.

(009-060) Enter [[lord-dundreary|Dundreary]] and Vernon, L. 1 E. Dundreary stops, C., and is seized

(009-061) with an inclination to sneeze. Motions with his hand to Vernon.

(009-062) [[harry-vernon|Ver]] My lord! [Business Dundreary sneezing.] Your lordship! [Dundreary

(009-063) same bus. Louder.] My lord!

(009-064) Dun There you go; now you've spoiled it.

(009-065) Ver Spoiled what, my lord?

(009-066) Dun Spoiled what? why a most magnificent sneeze.

(009-067) Ver I'm very sorry to interrupt your lordship's sneeze, but I merely

(009-068) wanted to express my [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|gratitude]] to you for getting me a ship.

(009-069) Dun Sir, I don't want your gratitude, I only want to sneeze.

(009-070) Ver Very well, my lord, then I will leave you, and this gives you an

(009-071) opportunity for sneezing. [Crosses to R.] But in return for what you

(009-072) have done for me, should you ever want a service a sailor can offer

(009-073) you, just hail Harry Vernon, and you'll find he'll weigh anchor and be

(009-074) alongside. [Hitches up breeches and exits, R. 1 E.]

(009-075) Dun Find him alongside? What does he mean by a long side? and he always

(009-076) wants to weigh anchor. What funny fellows the sailors are. Why the devil

(009-077) won't they keep a memorandum of the weight of their anchor? What's the

(009-078) matter with the sailor's side? [Imitates Vernon.] Oh I see, he's got the

(009-079) stomach ache. [Exit, R. 1 E.]

(009-080) Change Scene

(009-081) Scene 5--Library in Trenchard Manor in 3 or 4.

(009-082) Enter [[buddicombe|Buddicombe]], R. 1 E., following Lord Dundreary.

(009-083) Bud A letter, my lord.

(009-084) Dun [Takes letter.] You may go. [Exit Buddicombe, R. 1 E. Opens letter.]

(009-085) ``My dear Frederick.'' He calls me Frederick because my name is Robert.

(009-086) ``I wrote you on my arrival.'' Why, I never heard from him. ``But I

(009-087) am afraid you didn't get the letter, because I put no name on the

(009-088) envelope.'' That's the reason why I didn't get it, but who did get it?

(009-089) It must have been some fellow without any name. ``My dear brother, the

(009-090) other day a rap came to my door, and some fellows came in and proposed a

(009-091) quiet game of porker.'' A quiet game of porker, why, they wanted to kill

(009-092) him with a poker. ``I consented and got stuck--'' Sam's dead, I've got a

(009-093) dead lunatic for a brother--``for the drinks.'' He got on the other side

(009-094) of the paper, why couldn't he get stuck all on one side. ``P. S.--If you

(009-095) don't get this letter let me know, for I shall feel anxious.'' He's a

(009-096) mad lunatic. [Exit, R. 1 E.]

(009-097) Change Scene

(009-098) Scene 6--[[coyles-office|Coyle's Office]] in 2. High desk and stool, R. Modern box center

(009-099) against flat. Cabinet, L.

(009-100) Asa discovered looking over [[letters-and-documents|papers]] on box. Murcott looking in desk.

(009-101) Asa Have you found it?

(009-102) Mur No, Mr. Trenchard. I've searched all the drawers but can find no

(009-103) trace of it.

(009-104) Asa What's this?

(009-105) Mur That's a cabinet where his father kept old deeds, the key he always

(009-106) carries about him.

(009-107) Asa Oh, he does, does he? Well I reckon I saw a key as I came in that

(009-108) will open it. [Exit, R. 1 E.]

(009-109) Mur Key, oh, my poor muddled brain, what can he mean!

(009-110) Asa [Re-enters with axe.] Here's a key that will open any lock that Hobb

(009-111) ever invented.

(009-112) Mur Key? what key?

(009-113) Asa What key, why, Yankee. [Shows axe, begins to break open Cabinet.]

(009-114) Enter Coyle, R. 2 E.

(009-115) Coyle Villains! would you rob me?

(009-116) Mur Stand off, Mr. Coyle, we are desperate. [Now seizes him.]

(009-117) Asa Here it is a sure as there are snakes in Virginia. Let the old cuss

(009-118) go, Murcott.

(009-119) Coyle Burglars! oh, you shall dearly pay for this.

(009-120) Asa Yes, I'll pay--but I guess you'll find the change.

(009-121) Coyle The law--the law shall aid me.

(009-122) Asa Wal, perhaps it would be as well not to call in the law just yet. It

(009-123) might look a little further than might be convenient.

(009-124) Mur It's no use to blunder, Mr. Coyle, you are harmless to us now, for

(009-125) we have that, that will crush you.

(009-126) Coyle Well, what are your conditions? [[fortune-and-wealth|money]], how much?

(009-127) Asa Wal, we warn't thinking of coming down on your dollars. But you have

(009-128) an appointment with [[sir-edward-trenchard|Sir Edward]] at two, haven't you?

(009-129) Coyle Well?

(009-130) Asa Well, I want you to keep that appointment.

(009-131) Coyle Keep it?

(009-132) Asa Yes, and that's all I do want you to keep of his, and instead of

(009-133) saying you have come to foreclose the [[debt-and-credit|mortgage]], I want you to say, you

(009-134) have found the release which proves the mortgage to have been paid off.

(009-135) Coyle I accept. Is that all?

(009-136) Asa Not quite. Then I want you to pay off the execution debts.

(009-137) Coyle What, I pay Sir Edward's debts?

(009-138) Asa Yes, with Sir Edward's [[money-and-wealth|money]] that stuck to your fingers naturally

(009-139) while passing through your hands.

(009-140) Coyle [To Murcott.] Traitor!

(009-141) Mur He knows all, Mr. Coyle.

(009-142) Coyle Is there anything more!

(009-143) Asa yes, I want you to apologize to Miss [[florence-trenchard|Florence Trenchard]], for having

(009-144) the darned impudence to propose for her hand.

(009-145) Coyle What more?

(009-146) Asa Then you resign your stewardship in favor of your clerk, Abel

(009-147) Murcott.

(009-148) Coyle What, that drunkard vagabond?

(009-149) Asa Well, he was, but he's going to take the pledge at the first pump he

(009-150) comes to.

(009-151) Mur Yes, I _will_ conquer the demon drink, or die in the struggle with

(009-152) him.

(009-153) Coyle Well, anything more?

(009-154) Asa Yes, I think the next thing will be to get washed. You're not a

(009-155) handsome man at best, and now you're awful. [Coyle makes a dash at

(009-156) Murcott. Asa catches him and turns him round to R.] Mr. Coyle, in your

(009-157) present state of mind, you had better go first.

(009-158) Coyle [Bitterly.] Oh, sir, it is your turn now.

(009-159) Asa Yes, it is my turn, but you can have the first wash. Come along

(009-160) Murcott. [Exeunt, R. 1 E]

(009-162) Scene 7--Library in Trenchard Manor in 3 or 4.

(009-163) Sir Edward discovered seated R. of table.

(009-164) Sir E The clock is on the stroke of two, and Coyle is waiting my

(009-165) decision. In giving her to him, I know I shall be embittering her life

(009-166) to save my [[inheritance|fortune]], but appearances--no, no, I will not sacrifice her

(009-167) young life so full of promise, for a few short years of questionable

(009-168) state for myself, better leave her to the mercy of chance [Enter

(009-169) Florence, R. U. E.] than sell her to this scoundrel; and to myself,

(009-170) I will not survive the downfall of my house, but end it thus. [Raises

(009-171) pistol to his head. Florence seizes his arm and screams.]

(009-172) Flo Father, dear father, what despair is this? [Sir Edward buries his

(009-173) face in his hands.] If it is fear of poverty, do not think of me, I will

(009-174) marry this man if I drop dead in my bridal robes.

(009-175) Enter Binny, R. 1 E.

(009-176) Binny Mr. Coyle, sir who has come by happointment.

(009-177) Sir E I will not see him.

(009-178) Flo Yes, yes, show him up, Mr. Binny. [Exit Binny, R. 1 E.]

(009-179) Sir E Florence, I will not consent to this sacrifice.

(009-180) Enter Asa, Coyle and Murcott, R. 1 E.

(009-181) Sir E How is this Mr. Coyle, you are not alone?

(009-182) Asa No, you see, squire, Mr. Coyle wishes me and his clerk to witness

(009-183) the cutting off the seals from the mortgage, which he has been lucky

(009-185) Sir E Heavens, is it so?

(009-186) Coyle Yes, Sir Edward, there is the release executed by my father, which

(009-187) had become detached.

(009-188) Asa [To him.] Accidentally.

(009-189) Sir E Saved, saved at last from want!

(009-190) Coyle Meanwhile I have paid the execution debts out of a fine which has

(009-191) just fallen in.

(009-192) Asa Accidentally. It's astonishing how things have fallen in and out

(009-193) to-day.

(009-194) Sir E But your demand here? [Points to Florence.]

(009-195) Coyle I make none, Sir Edward. I regret that I should have conceived so

(009-196) mad a thought; it is enough to unfit me for longer holding position as

(009-197) your agent, which I beg humbly to resign--

(009-198) Asa [Aside to him.] Recommending as your successor--

(009-199) Coyle Recommending as my successor [[abel-murcott|Abel Murcott]], whose knowledge of your

(009-200) affairs, gained in my office, will render him as useful as I have been.

(009-201) Asa Yes, just about.

(009-202) Sir E Your request is granted, Mr. Coyle.

(009-203) Asa And now, my dear Mr. Coyle, you may a-b-s-q-u-a-t-u-l-a-t-e.

(009-204) Coyle I go, Sir Edward, with equal good wishes for all assembled here.

(009-205) [Darts a look at Murcott and exits, R. 1 E.]

(009-206) Asa That's a good man, Sir Edward.

(009-207) Sir E Yes.

(009-208) Asa Oh, he's a very good man.

(009-209) Sir E Yes, he is a good man.

(009-210) Asa But he can't keep a hotel.

(009-211) Sir E Mr. Murcott, your offence was heavy.

(009-212) Flo And so has been his reparation. Forgive him, papa. Mr. Murcott, you

(009-214) Mur Yes, I saved her, thank Heaven. I had strength enough for that.
