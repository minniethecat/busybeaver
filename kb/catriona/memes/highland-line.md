# Highland Line

The geographical and cultural boundary between the Scottish Highlands and Lowlands — more a gradient than a sharp line, but a real divide in language, law, dress, and social organization. In *Catriona*, crossing the Highland Line means crossing between two understandings of loyalty, justice, and identity.

## Definition

The Highland Line runs roughly from Dumbarton on the Clyde to Stonehaven on the North Sea coast, separating the mountainous, Gaelic-speaking, clan-based Highlands from the fertile, Scots/English-speaking, law-governed Lowlands. In the mid-18th century, the line was both a physical boundary — marked by differences in terrain, agriculture, and settlement patterns — and a psychological one, separating "civilised" from "barbarous" in the Lowland imagination (001-013).

## Historical Context

By the time of *Catriona* (the early 1750s), the Highland Line had acquired a new military and political significance. The Jacobite Rising of 1745 had been, in large part, a Highland army marching south across the Line into England. The government's response — disarmament, the abolition of heritable jurisdictions, the proscription of Highland dress — was an attempt to erase the Line's legal significance by imposing uniform Hanoverian law on both sides. Yet the cultural divide proved stubborn.

General Wade's military roads, built in the 1720s and 1730s, were explicitly designed to bridge the Highland Line for the movement of government troops. The forts at Fort William, Fort Augustus, and Fort George formed a chain of control along the Great Glen, anchoring Lowland authority in Highland territory. The novel's geography constantly invokes the Line — characters move back and forth across it, and with each crossing their legal status and social standing shift.

## Role in the Novel

### David Balfour: A Lowlander in the Highlands

David Balfour is, by birth and education, a Lowlander. His Whig Presbyterianism, his faith in the law, and his discomfort with the clan world all mark him as a man from south of the Line. Yet he has "friends of my own across the Highland line," and "the sound of that tongue comes friendly" to him (001-013). This ambivalence is central to his character: he is a Lowland Whig who finds himself defending Highland Jacobites.

### Highland or Lowland: A Question of Identity

Throughout the novel, characters are classified by which side of the Line they belong to. Catriona introduces herself: "I am Highland, as you see, and think myself the farther from my home" (001-016). The Highland soldiers who abduct David are "ragged Highland gillies" and "Highland cattle-thieves" (006-022, 013-037) — Lowland epithets that deny them military or political standing. The Duke of Argyll makes the distinction explicit: "We are Highlanders civilised, and it is not so with the great mass of our clans and families" (004-077).

### The Line as Legal Frontier

The Highland Line functioned as a de facto legal frontier. On the Highland side, clan justice and the authority of chiefs operated in parallel with — and sometimes in defiance of — Hanoverian law. The Appin Murder is a crime that makes sense only in the Highland legal world, where a Campbell factor on Stewart land was a provocation that clan honor demanded be answered. David, the Lowland lawyer-in-training, struggles to navigate this world where "Highland or Lowland" determines who is trustworthy (011-003).
