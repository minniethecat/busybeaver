Musselburgh is a historic town in East Lothian, lying on the Firth of Forth some six miles east of Edinburgh. In the 18th century, it was a coaching stop on the Great North Road and a burgh of some consequence. In *Catriona*, it appears as a waypoint on David Balfour and Alan Breck Stewart's flight eastward from Edinburgh after David's duel at Hope Park.

## Breakfast at Musselburgh

Having marched through the night from Edinburgh across the Figgate Whins, David and Alan reach Musselburgh in the morning. The breakfast they share there is a rare moment of ease in the midst of danger:

> "We had a haddock to our breakfast in Musselburgh, where it amused me vastly to hear Alan." [012-048]

Alan's conversation at breakfast — in high good humour despite being a proscribed rebel within hours of Edinburgh — is characteristic of his élan. The "vastly amusing" quality of his talk lightens what would otherwise be a grim flight.

## Evasion of Pursuit

The pair's stay in Musselburgh is brief and strategic. They are keenly aware that the Edinburgh coaches pass this way, and that they must not be seen:

> "We left Musselburgh before the first ninepenny coach was due from Edinburgh for (as Alan said) that was a rencounter we might very well avoid." [012-049]

The "ninepenny coach" — the public stage-coach — would carry passengers and news from Edinburgh. To be seen by its occupants would be to advertise their location to any pursuers. Alan's instinct to leave before the coach arrives is the practiced caution of a man who has lived as a fugitive since Culloden.

## Significance

Musselburgh is a minor but telling location in *Catriona*. It marks the point at which David and Alan's flight transitions from the immediate peril of Edinburgh to the longer journey eastward. The haddock breakfast and Alan's entertaining conversation lend a human scale to what is otherwise a tale of political persecution. The town's role as a coaching stop — a place of transit — mirrors David's own condition: a young man between worlds, between innocence and experience, between the law's demands and his own conscience.
