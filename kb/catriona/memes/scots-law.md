# Scots Law

Scots law is the legal system of Scotland — a hybrid system combining elements of Roman (civil) law and English common law — and the institutional framework within which the central events of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona* unfold. From David Balfour's legal studies to the trial of James Stewart of the Glens, from Lord Prestongrange's chambers to the Parliament House where advocates plead, the machinery of Scots law is both the novel's setting and its subject. Stevenson, himself the son of a Scottish advocate and trained in the law, brings an insider's precision to his depiction of legal Edinburgh — and an insider's disillusionment.

## Overview

Scots law in the 18th century was a sophisticated legal system, distinct from English law in both its sources and its procedures. It drew on Roman law (via the institutional writers Stair and Erskine), feudal law, and canon law, with English influence growing after the Union of 1707. The Court of Session, sitting in Parliament House in Edinburgh, was the supreme civil court; the High Court of Justiciary handled serious criminal cases. The Lord Advocate, a political appointee, combined the roles of chief prosecutor and government legal adviser — a concentration of power that is central to the plot of *Catriona*.

## In the Novel

### The Law as Instrument of Power

*Catriona* is, at its core, a novel about the relationship between law and justice — and Stevenson's conclusion is bleak. The Scots legal system is depicted as a finely calibrated instrument that can produce any result the powerful desire. Prestongrange's career is a case study: he is a "sound Scots lawyer" (009-013), skilled, intelligent, and not personally cruel. But his skill is deployed in service of political objectives, not justice. When Charles Stewart the Writer warns David that "Ye'll hang beside James Stewart" (002-056), he is describing not a legal finding but a political calculation.

The trial of James Stewart at Inveraray — before a jury packed with Campbells and presided over by the Campbell Duke of Argyll — is the novel's most damning portrait of law without justice. The forms are observed: there are counsel, evidence, arguments. But the outcome is predetermined. Simon Fraser's remark that "James is a dead man" (017-068) is not cynicism but realism: the law is functioning exactly as designed.

### David Balfour's Legal Education

David Balfour is introduced to Scots law from the inside. His journey begins at the door of Charles Stewart the Writer — "the longest stair ever mason set a hand to" (002-001) — and proceeds through the offices of lawyers, the chambers of the Lord Advocate, and eventually the Parliament House itself. This geography of legal Edinburgh is mapped with precision: each location represents a different layer of the legal hierarchy, from the working Writer to the Lord Advocate who shapes national policy.

David's ambition — "to make a revolution in the Parliament House" (017-023) — is the ambition of a young idealist who believes the law can be reformed from within. The novel systematically dismantles this belief. By the end, David has abandoned his legal career, and his summary of the experience is characteristically understated: "I thought I was acting for the sake of justice" (003-003) — the past tense conveying the distance between what he believed and what he learned.

### The Writer, the Advocate, and the Lord Advocate

Stevenson distinguishes carefully between the different roles within Scots law:

- **The Writer** (e.g., Charles Stewart) is a solicitor — a man who draws deeds, handles correspondence, and manages the practical business of law. He is vulnerable because his connection to Jacobite clients exposes him to prosecution.

- **The Advocate** (like Prestongrange) is a barrister — a member of the Faculty of Advocates who pleads before the Court of Session. The Lord Advocate is the most senior of these, combining legal and political authority.

- **The Judge** presides over the court but, in the case of James Stewart's trial, is effectively a political agent.

This hierarchy matters because it shows that legal power is not monolithic — it has layers, and David learns to navigate them. His tragedy is that he learns the navigation without changing the destination.

## Historical Context

Scots law in the mid-18th century was in a period of transition. The Union of 1707 had guaranteed the preservation of Scotland's distinct legal system, but English influence was growing, particularly through appeals to the House of Lords. The Heritable Jurisdictions Act of 1747 — which stripped clan chiefs of their judicial powers — was a major intervention by the London government in Scotland's legal landscape, and its effects are directly felt in *Catriona* through the prosecution of the Appin Stewart case.

The Lord Advocate in the 1750s was indeed a figure of immense power, combining prosecution authority with political influence. The character of Lord Prestongrange is based loosely on the historical Lord Advocate, though Stevenson adjusts the timeline and the specifics for dramatic purposes. Modern historians of Scots law have noted the accuracy of Stevenson's institutional portrait — the novel captures, with remarkable fidelity, the culture of deference, patronage, and political calculation that characterised 18th-century legal Edinburgh.
