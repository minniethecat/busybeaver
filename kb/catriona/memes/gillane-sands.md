Gillane Sands (modern Gullane Sands) is a stretch of beach on the East Lothian coast of Scotland, lying east of Edinburgh and south of the Firth of Forth. In the 18th century, Gillane was a small settlement on the far side of Gillane Ness, remote and little-frequented — "so that few parts of the coast are lonelier" [013-007]. This very loneliness made it a natural rendezvous point for fugitives and smugglers. In *Catriona*, Gillane Sands is the designated pickup point for Alan Breck Stewart's escape from Scotland, arranged through Andie Scougal (also known as Black Andie).

## The Rendezvous Plan

Alan Breck, a proscribed rebel since the 1745 Rising, has arranged passage to France through Andie Scougal, the captain of a ship called the *Thristle*. The plan is for Andie to pick Alan up at Gillane Sands:

> "'Brawly,' said he. 'Andie picks him up at Gillane sands to-morrow, Wednesday.'" [009-005]

The timing is critical: Alan must make his way east from Edinburgh, evading the Crown's forces, to reach the sands by Wednesday. The journey is long and exposed, and David Balfour accompanies him:

> "'So, by my way of it, we should creep out of this wood no further gone than just the inside of next minute, and hold away east for Gillane, where I'm to find my ship.'" [011-033]

## The Lonely Coast

Stevenson paints Gillane as a place of extraordinary isolation, perfectly suited to clandestine meetings but also — by that very same quality — perilous:

> "From north Berwick west to Gillane Ness there runs a string of four small islets, Craiglieth, the Lamb, Fidra, and Eyebrough, notable by their diversity of size and shape. Gillane is a small place on the far side of the Ness, the folk of Dirleton go to their business in the inland fields, and those of North Berwick straight to the sea-fishing from their haven; so that few parts of the coast are lonelier." [013-006 - 013-007]

The geography is drawn with precision: the four islets — Craiglieth, the Lamb, Fidra, and Eyebrough — form a chain running westward from North Berwick towards Gillane Ness, creating a distinctive coastal profile that any mariner would recognise.

## Betrayal on the Sands

Alan's instinct, honed by years of living as a hunted man, warns him that the rendezvous may be a trap:

> "'I think Andie Scougal's sold me — him or his mate wha kent some part of the affair — or else Charlie's clerk callant, which would be a pity too,' says Alan; 'and if you askit me for just my inward private conviction, I think there'll be heads cracked on Gillane sands.'" [012-078]

His prediction proves accurate. When they reach Gillane, they find not a ship waiting to carry Alan to safety, but an ambush. Soldiers have been tipped off, and what was meant to be a departure becomes a skirmish. David, watching from the dunes, sees distant figures appearing and disappearing among the sandhills:

> "Almost at the same moment of time, and perhaps half a mile away towards Gillane Ness, the figure of a man appeared for a blink upon a sandhill, waving with his arms; and though he was gone again in the same flash, the gulls in that part continued a little longer to fly wild." [013-019]

The ambush party is spread "among the bents towards Gillane" [013-037], caught off-guard themselves — a rare instance of Alan and David having the advantage of surprise.

## Significance

Gillane Sands is the setting for one of the novel's most vivid action sequences, but it is more than a backdrop. The lonely beach, with its chain of islets and its "linky, boggy" hinterland, embodies the condition of the Jacobite fugitive: isolated, exposed, dependent on unreliable allies, and constantly scanning the horizon for sails that may mean salvation — or soldiers that may mean death. That Alan Breck, the most resourceful man in the novel, can be betrayed here by those he trusted underscores the fragility of the Jacobite network in the post-Culloden years.

The narrator of the novel, looking back from middle age, counts Gillane among the places a "long-legged, hot-headed youth" should visit to retrace David Balfour's wanderings, alongside Dean, Silvermills, and the Bass Rock [P04-004] — a testament to its place in the geography of Stevenson's imagination.
