# Catriona

*Catriona* (published in the United States as *David Balfour*) is a historical novel by Robert Louis Stevenson, first published in 1893. Subtitled *Being Memoirs of the Further Adventures of David Balfour at Home and Abroad*, it is the sequel to *Kidnapped* (1886) and continues the story of David Balfour in the aftermath of the Appin Murder. Where *Kidnapped* is a novel of action and adventure, *Catriona* is a novel of law, politics, and love — a more mature work in which the swashbuckling certainties of youth give way to the moral complexities of adult life.

## Overview

The novel opens in Edinburgh in 1751, with David Balfour arriving to claim his inheritance and to testify on behalf of James Stewart of the Glens, who stands accused of the Appin Murder. David, now a young man of means and employed at the British Linen Company, finds himself caught between the demands of justice and the machinery of state power. His refusal to cooperate with Lord Prestongrange's attempt to manage his testimony leads to his sequestration on the Bass Rock, where his relationship with Catriona Drummond — the daughter of the Jacobite renegade James More MacGregor — deepens into love.

The novel's second half shifts to the Continent: David studies law at the University of Leyden, waiting for Catriona and for Alan Breck Stewart, and the converging plots culminate in a confrontation with James More at Dunkirk, where Alan Breck kills the treacherous MacGregor in a duel and secures the young couple's future.

## Historical Context

Stevenson wrote *Catriona* in Samoa, far from the Edinburgh he was describing, between 1892 and 1893. He was in poor health — he would die the following year at the age of 44 — and the novel has a valedictory quality, a sense of looking back at Scotland from a great distance. The historical research was conducted largely from memory and from books Stevenson had shipped to the South Pacific; the result is a novel that is remarkably accurate in its legal and political detail while necessarily impressionistic in its geography.

The critical reception was mixed. Contemporary reviewers, expecting another adventure story in the vein of *Kidnapped*, were puzzled by the novel's legal density and its introspective, morally ambivalent hero. Henry James, however, recognised its quality, writing to Stevenson that *Catriona* was "the done thing" — a novel of genuine literary achievement rather than merely a successful entertainment.

Modern criticism has been kinder. *Catriona* is now regarded as one of Stevenson's most accomplished works and one of the finest historical novels of the 19th century — a book that anticipated the 20th-century novel's concern with institutional power and moral ambiguity.

### Significance

*Catriona* stands as Stevenson's most sustained engagement with the relationship between law and justice. It is a novel in which the most important actions happen offstage (the murder, the trial) and the most important struggles happen inside the protagonist's conscience. David Balfour's journey from naive idealist to disillusioned exile is one of the most psychologically nuanced character arcs in 19th-century adventure fiction.

The novel is also notable for its treatment of Catriona Drummond, who — despite being the title character — appears only intermittently. Her scenes with David are among the most emotionally compelling in Stevenson's work, and her distinctive idiolect (Gaelic-influenced English speech patterns) represents one of the most careful attempts by a 19th-century novelist to render Highland speech without condescension.
