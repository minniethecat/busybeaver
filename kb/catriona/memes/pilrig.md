Pilrig is the estate of David Balfour's kinsman near Edinburgh. In *Catriona*,
it serves as David's initial base in the city and the source of the letter of
introduction to Lord Prestongrange that triggers the novel's central conflict.
The house — "a pleasant gabled house set in a garden" (003-016) — represents
the respectable Whig establishment with which David is uneasily connected.

## Location and Description

Stevenson places Pilrig among the Edinburgh-area estates of the gentry class:
"Silvermills, and Broughton, and Hope Park, and Pilrig, and poor old Lochend"
(P04-004). The house is approached from Edinburgh, and David describes coming
"in view of Pilrig" (003-016) on his arrival. The estate is a working
residence — the Balfour of Pilrig is a lawyer and man of affairs — set in
cultivated grounds.

Pilrig is a real house (Pilrig House still stands in Edinburgh, now surrounded
by the city that has grown around it), and the Balfours of Pilrig were a
historical family. Stevenson's use of the name connects his fictional David
Balfour to a real Edinburgh lineage.

## In the Novel

Pilrig functions as David's doorway into the Edinburgh legal establishment. His
kinsman provides him with a letter to Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate
(003-041, 004-004). This letter — well-meant, written in good faith — is the
instrument that delivers David into Prestongrange's web. Pilrig's patronage is
genuine, but the world into which it introduces David is morally compromised.

The letter itself becomes a recurring motif. Prestongrange reads it aloud
(004-009), and its language of recommendation is gradually subverted as
Prestongrange transforms David from a recommended kinsman into a managed
witness. Pilrig's good name is used against the very person it was meant to
help.

## Significance

Pilrig represents the respectable establishment — Whig, Presbyterian,
legally-minded — to which David nominally belongs. His connection to Pilrig
marks him as a gentleman rather than a Highlander, a member of the dominant
political class rather than the subjugated one. This identity is both a
privilege (it opens Prestongrange's door) and a burden (it makes his
resistance to Prestongrange's demands seem like ingratitude).

The estate also embodies a theme that runs through Stevenson's Scottish fiction:
the tension between Highland and Lowland, clan loyalty and legal order. Pilrig
is Lowland, law-abiding, prosperous — everything the Macgregors and Stewarts
are not. David's position between these worlds is the novel's central
structural tension.
