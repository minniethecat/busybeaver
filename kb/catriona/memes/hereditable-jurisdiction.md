# Hereditable Jurisdiction

Before 1747, Scottish clan chiefs and great lairds held private courts with powers up to and including the death penalty — a system known as **hereditable jurisdiction**. Its abolition after the Jacobite Rising of 1745 transformed Highland society and created the legal vacuum in which the Appin Murder trial of *Catriona* takes place.

## Definition

Hereditable (or heritable) jurisdictions were private legal powers attached to landownership, inheritable like the land itself. At their most extensive, in jurisdictions of **regality**, the landholder could try all crimes except treason, maintain prisons, and execute convicts. In lesser **barony** jurisdictions, the powers extended to theft and minor offences. The system had medieval origins but survived into the 18th century, particularly in the Highlands, where clan chiefs exercised de facto criminal jurisdiction over their people through a fusion of feudal grant and customary authority.

## Historical Context

The Heritable Jurisdictions (Scotland) Act 1747 (20 Geo. II, c. 43) was one of the government's most consequential responses to the '45 Rising. Passed in the wake of Culloden, it abolished all heritable jurisdictions and converted them into monetary compensation for the former holders. The Duke of Argyll received £21,000 for his jurisdictions; lesser chiefs received proportionally. The Act was designed to destroy the legal basis of clan power — without the right to hold courts, a chief's authority over his people was reduced to custom and sentiment.

In practice, the abolition had uneven effects. In the Lowlands, where sheriff courts and justices of the peace already handled most business, the change was modest. In the Highlands, where the chief's court had been the primary institution of justice for centuries, the abolition created a legal vacuum. Government sheriffs were distant, distrusted, and culturally alien. The result was a Highlands that was legally disarmed — clans could no longer enforce internal order — but not yet integrated into the Hanoverian legal system.

## Role in the Novel

### The Legal Context of the Appin Murder

The trial of James Stewart of the Glens for the Appin Murder (1752) takes place in the shadow of the Heritable Jurisdictions Act. Colin Campbell of Glenure — the murdered man — was the government factor appointed to manage the forfeited Stewart estate of Ardshiel. The factor system was itself a product of the government's Highland policy: when a Jacobite laird was attainted, the Crown took his estate and appointed a factor (often a Campbell or other reliable Whig) to administer it. The Ardshiel tenants, who had paid rent to their Stewart chief and now paid the same rent to a Campbell factor, saw this as theft legitimised by law.

The murder of Glenure was, in this light, not a random act of violence but a clan response to a legal order that had stripped the Stewarts of their land and their jurisdiction. The trial that followed — in which a Campbell-packed jury convicted James Stewart on thin evidence — was itself an exercise of the new legal order against the old. The novel's interest in the question of jurisdiction — who has the right to judge whom — runs through every scene in the Advocate's chambers and the courtroom.

### Prestongrange and the Two Laws

Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, embodies the tension between the abolished old law and the imposed new one. He is a "Highlander civilised" (004-077), a man who understands clan loyalty from the inside but prosecutes in the name of Hanoverian statute. When he warns that "disturbance in the Highlands" could produce "a '45 again with the Campbells on the other side" (004-079), he is acknowledging that the abolition of heritable jurisdictions has not solved the problem of Highland order — it has only displaced it.

### David Balfour's Legal Education

David, the young law student, arrives in Edinburgh believing in the impartiality of the law. His education over the course of the novel is in part an education in the limits of that belief. He learns that the law is a weapon wielded by the powerful — that "Prestongrange with all the King's authority behind him" can make a man disappear if it suits the government's purpose (009-001). The heritable jurisdictions are gone, but the concentration of legal power in the hands of the state is, in its own way, as arbitrary as the chief's court.
