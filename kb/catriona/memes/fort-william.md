Fort William is a British military fortress in the Scottish Highlands, situated
at the southern end of the Great Glen. In Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*,
it serves as the prison where James More Macgregor is held "shackled" (009-017),
a place that embodies the military occupation of the Highlands following the
Jacobite rising of 1745.

## Location and History

Fort William was built in the 1690s during the reign of William III (for whom
it was named), as part of a chain of fortifications designed to control the
Highland clans. Along with Fort Augustus and Fort George, it formed the military
spine of the government's presence in the Highlands. Its position at the foot
of Ben Nevis, commanding the route through the Great Glen, made it strategically
vital.

By the time of *Catriona* (1751–52), Fort William was the principal military
prison for Highland Jacobite detainees. The fortress held prisoners in "close
dungeons" under strict military guard, with restrictions on visitors:
"none allowed in to them, nor they to look upon the light of the day" (009-017).

## In the Novel

Fort William appears as a destination beyond reach. James More is held there
during the novel's central section, and Catriona's efforts to visit him are
rebuffed — the distance between Edinburgh and Fort William (over 100 miles
through Highland terrain) making the journey prohibitive for a young woman
without resources. The fortress's inaccessibility is political as much as
geographical: it represents Crown power at its most absolute, where legal
process gives way to military authority.

The road between Edinburgh and Fort William — "betwixt here and Fort William"
(009-019) — becomes a corridor of power in the novel's geography, along
which prisoners, messengers, and soldiers travel between the Lowland legal
world and the Highland military one.

## Significance

Fort William embodies the post-1745 settlement in its most coercive form.
Where Edinburgh represents the civil face of Hanoverian rule — courts,
parliament, legal forms — Fort William represents the military face:
soldiers, shackles, dungeons. James More's imprisonment there, and the
restrictions on his visitors, illustrate the reality of Crown power in
the Highlands: not law but force.

The fortress also serves as a spatial marker of the cultural division
between Highland and Lowland. David Balfour, the Lowland Whig, never visits
Fort William — his world is Edinburgh's courts and closes. The fortress
exists in the novel as a place heard about but not seen, a dark presence
on the edge of the story's geography that reminds the reader of the
violence underlying the legal proceedings in the capital.
