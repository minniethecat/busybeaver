The Flight to Dunkirk is the climactic event of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*,
occurring in the novel's final chapters (29–31). After the trial of James Stewart
concludes with a guilty verdict and David Balfour's sequestration on the Bass Rock
ends, the protagonists flee Scotland for France. The journey from Edinburgh to the
port of Dunkirk serves as the novel's resolution — a deliberately unconventional
ending in which legal justice is denied and narrative closure is achieved through
escape rather than vindication.

## Overview

The Flight to Dunkirk represents the culmination of the novel's political and
personal tensions. Following James Stewart's conviction and David's release from
the Bass Rock, the surviving characters must choose between continued entanglement
in Scottish legal politics and a clean break through flight. David, Catriona
Drummond, and Alan Breck Stewart travel through England and across the Channel to
Dunkirk — a French port that serves as a refuge for Jacobite exiles. James More
Macgregor also makes for Dunkirk, pursued by Alan, while Catriona remains
uncertain of her loyalties.

The flight is narrated with Stevenson's characteristic attention to landscape and
motion: the "bents by Dunkirk" and "the windmill sails" (030-033) form the visual
backdrop for the emotional resolution between David and Catriona.

## Historical Background

Dunkirk, a fortified port city in French Flanders, was a significant destination
for Jacobite exiles following the failed rising of 1745. France had historically
supported the Stuart cause, and French ports offered refuge to Highlanders who
could not safely remain in Britain. The choice of Dunkirk as the novel's
destination locates the story within this historical reality — the characters
are not simply "going abroad" but joining a diaspora of political exiles.

The journey from Edinburgh to the Channel ports and across to France would have
taken several days by horseback and packet boat in the 1750s. Stevenson
compresses the travel into a rapid narrative sequence while preserving the
sense of distance and danger.

## The Event in the Novel

### Depiction

The flight unfolds in three stages. First, David and his companions travel south
through England, crossing the border and leaving the jurisdiction of Scottish law.
Second, they make the Channel crossing — a passage that symbolically severs their
ties to the world of clans, courts, and Crown justice. Third, they arrive in
Dunkirk, where the novel's personal conflicts reach their resolution against the
backdrop of the French port's "bents" and windmills (030-033).

Stevenson uses the journey structure to wind down the novel's political tension
while focusing attention on the personal. The landscape shifts from Highland to
Lowland to English to Continental — a geographical progression that mirrors the
characters' movement from the particular world of clan politics toward a broader,
more cosmopolitan existence.

### Narrative Role

The flight serves as the novel's unconventional resolution. In a conventional
historical novel, justice would be served — James Stewart would be acquitted, or
the Appin murderers would be identified. Stevenson denies this closure. Instead,
the resolution is achieved through physical removal: by leaving Scotland, David
escapes the legal machinery that has threatened to destroy him. The flight is an
admission that the novel's central conflicts cannot be resolved within the
Scottish legal system.

The Dunkirk sequence also brings the love story to its conclusion. It is at
Dunkirk that Catriona definitively chooses David over her father, and the
relationship that has developed through the novel's trials is finally placed on
a secure footing.

## Aftermath

The flight's immediate consequence is the separation of the characters from the
Scottish legal-political world. David and Catriona are safe in France, beyond
the reach of Prestongrange's warrants. James More's schemes collapse, and Alan's
pursuit of him suggests a continuation of Highland feuds on foreign soil.

In the longer view, the flight represents David's coming of age. He arrived in
Edinburgh believing in law; he leaves Scotland understanding that law is an
instrument of power. The flight is not cowardice but wisdom — the recognition
that some conflicts cannot be won within the system and must be escaped from
without.

> "Well, and I'm glad I came to Dunkirk." — Catriona Drummond (030-096)
