# Catriona Drummond

Catriona Drummond (also Catriona Macgregor, later Catriona Balfour) is the title character and heroine of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona* (1893). The daughter of the Jacobite renegade James More MacGregor, she is introduced as a young Highland woman alone in Edinburgh, vulnerable to the political machinations that surround the Appin Murder case. Through her courage, her unshakeable sense of honour, and her deepening bond with David Balfour, she becomes one of Stevenson's most fully realised female characters — a woman who navigates the dangerous intersection of clan loyalty, political persecution, and love with a moral clarity that often exceeds David's own.

## Role in the Story

Catriona is the emotional and moral centre of the novel that bears her name. Though she appears less frequently than David or Alan Breck, each of her appearances is decisive. Her first meeting with David — when he comes to her aid in Edinburgh — establishes the novel's central relationship and its central tension: David's growing love for her must be balanced against his duty to justice and his complicated relationship with her father.

Catriona functions as a moral touchstone in the novel. Where David struggles with legal and political calculations, Catriona operates by a simpler code — Highland honour, personal loyalty, and the courage to act on her convictions. Her willingness to walk thirty miles across the Scottish countryside to reach safety (023-013) is typical: she does not complain, does not calculate, simply endures.

## Biography

### Background

Catriona is the daughter of James More MacGregor (also known as James MacGregor Drummond), a Highland chief and Jacobite officer who has spent years in prison and exile. Her mother is deceased, and Catriona has been raised largely in the shadow of her father's political entanglements — a circumstance that has made her resilient but vulnerable. At the novel's opening, she is in Edinburgh, placed in the care of a guardian whose motives are suspect, caught in the web of intrigue surrounding the Appin Murder trial.

### Key Actions

- Catriona and David's first meeting occurs when he intervenes to help her, marking the beginning of their relationship. From the start, she impresses him: "Catriona Drummond is the one I use" (001-024), and she quickly proves herself.

- She visits David on the Bass Rock during his sequestration, risking the dangerous crossing to bring him news and comfort — a demonstration of courage that deepens their bond. David reflects that "so long as I stood well with Catriona Drummond, the opinion of the rest of mankind was but moonshine" (014-021).

- When David arranges her escape from Scotland, she walks thirty miles alongside him without complaint (023-013). Her practical courage — she suggests they pose as a married couple to avoid suspicion — reveals a resourcefulness that David, for all his legal training, lacks. "And now I will be Catriona Balfour," she says (023-061), and her willingness to claim his name before she has any guarantee of his intentions is a measure of her trust.

- In Leyden, Catriona writes to David in her distinctive voice — the postscript to James More's formal letter is entirely in her hand — and her words move Alan Breck himself to admiration: "Impossible to deny a kind of decency to this Catriona, and sense forby!" (029-014).

- At the climactic scene in Dunkirk, Catriona holds her ground while her father attempts to betray Alan Breck. Her presence is what compels Alan and David to act, and her silent courage throughout the confrontation is recognised by Alan: "My dear, you're a true daughter of Alpin" (030-123).

### Fate

Catriona is united with David at the novel's end, their marriage secured by Alan Breck's intervention. Her fate is happier than her father's — James More dies by Alan's sword — but the novel does not sentimentalise her future. She will be the wife of an exile, living in Leyden or wherever David's uncertain prospects take them, but the core traits that defined her — courage, loyalty, love — remain intact.

- **[[james-more-macgregor]]** — her father, a Jacobite renegade whose schemes endanger everyone around him. Catriona's loyalty to him is tested to breaking point.
- **[[alan-breck-stewart]]** — David's friend, who becomes her champion. Alan's approval of Catriona — "a true daughter of Alpin" — is the novel's highest compliment.
- **[[lord-prestongrange]]** — the Lord Advocate, whose political machinations threaten Catriona's safety.
- **Miss Barbara Grant** — Prestongrange's daughter, who befriends Catriona in Edinburgh.

## Historical Note

Catriona Drummond is a fictional character, though her father James More MacGregor is based on the historical Rob Roy's son. Stevenson drew on the archetype of the Highland heroine — courageous, honourable, and bound by a code of conduct that the Lowland legal world cannot comprehend — but Catriona transcends the archetype. Her distinctive speech patterns (Gaelic-influenced English) and her moral seriousness make her one of the most memorable female characters in 19th-century Scottish fiction.
