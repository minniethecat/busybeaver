# Leyden

Leyden (modern Leiden) is a Dutch university city that serves as the European counterpoint to Edinburgh in the final third of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. It is the destination toward which the novel's plot moves — "the college of Leyden in Holland" (012-042) — a place of exile, study, and ultimately reunion for David Balfour, Alan Breck Stewart, and Catriona Drummond. In Stevenson's geography, Leyden represents the possibility of a life beyond the reach of British law, a sanctuary where Scottish clan politics cannot follow.

## Geography

Leyden is located in the province of South Holland, approximately 36 kilometres southwest of Amsterdam and 10 kilometres from the North Sea coast. Its university, founded in 1575, was one of the most prestigious in 18th-century Europe, particularly renowned for its faculties of law and medicine. The city is built on a network of canals — the Old Rhine (Oude Rijn) flows through its centre — and in the period of *Catriona* it was surrounded by defensive walls and accessible by canal boat from The Hague and Rotterdam.

Stevenson's knowledge of Leyden came from literary sources rather than personal experience — he had never visited the Netherlands — but his depiction is geographically coherent: the canal-side streets, the proximity to the sea, and the ease of communication with France (Dunkirk is approximately 200 kilometres south along the coast) are all accurate.

## In the Novel

### A Destination of Hope

Leyden is first proposed by Alan Breck as an escape plan — a place where he and David can study together safely, beyond the reach of the British authorities. "His studies — is the college of Leyden in Holland. Now, what say you, Alan?" (012-042). The plan has a touch of absurdity — the idea of the swashbuckling Alan Breck as a university student — but it also represents something serious: the possibility that the Highland fugitive might have a future beyond perpetual flight.

For David Balfour, Leyden becomes the anchor of his plans after the failure of the legal case in Edinburgh. He prepares "to make up my mails for Leyden" (020-049), and the city becomes a symbol of his determination to build a life independent of both Prestongrange's patronage and James More MacGregor's schemes. The journey to Leyden is, for David, a declaration of independence.

### Leyden as Sanctuary

Once in Leyden, David finds himself in a curious suspension — safe from British law but cut off from everything he knows. His correspondence with Catriona and Alan provides the only thread connecting him to Scotland. The city is described through its absences rather than its presences: Stevenson devotes little description to Leyden's architecture or society, focusing instead on David's internal state — his loneliness, his studies, his waiting.

> "Scarce a field or waterside near Leyden, scarce a street or lane thereof, but I had haunted it." (024-027)

This is exile rendered as emotional geography. David walks the city obsessively, memorising it, but Leyden never becomes home — it is a waiting room. The arrival of Catriona and the convergence of the novel's plot at nearby Dunkirk transform Leyden from a place of exile into a place of resolution.

### The Leyden-Dunkirk Axis

The proximity of Leyden to Dunkirk (in France) is a crucial plot element. Alan Breck's presence in Dunkirk — where James More MacGregor attempts to betray him to a British naval vessel, the *Seahorse* — creates the geographic tension of the novel's climax. David shuttles between the two cities: "I had still my letter on the Leyden merchant" (023-012), while Alan and James More play out their deadly game in Dunkirk. The novel's resolution — the duel, the marriage, the flight — all occur within this Leyden-Dunkirk corridor, a narrow strip of Continental Europe where Scottish fugitives can briefly find safety.

## Historical Context

Leiden University had strong historical connections to Scotland in the 17th and 18th centuries. Many Scottish lawyers, physicians, and clergymen studied there, particularly during periods when Presbyterian students faced restrictions at home. The university's law faculty was highly respected, and the curriculum — based on Roman-Dutch law — complemented the Scots law training David would have received. Newton's *Principia* was first published with the university's support, and the institution's intellectual atmosphere was notably more tolerant than most European universities of the period.
