King's Park is the royal parkland surrounding Holyrood Palace in Edinburgh, encompassing both the formal gardens of Hope Park and the wilder ground of the Hunter's Bog. In *Catriona*, it is the setting for one of the novel's most memorable set-pieces: David Balfour's duel with Lieutenant Hector Duncansby.

## Hope Park

David is led to Hope Park — "a beautiful pleasance, laid with gravel-walks, furnished with seats and summer-sheds, and warded by a keeper" [008-029] — by the three officers detailed to guard him. The formal elegance of the park contrasts sharply with the purpose of their visit: Lieutenant Duncansby, a "gawky, leering Highland boy" [008-030], has been put up to challenge David to a duel, almost certainly at the instigation of forces seeking to discredit or dispose of him.

David's handler, a friendly-faced officer among the three, understands the danger and quietly extracts him:

> "He took me by the sleeve with a nod and a wink and led me quietly outside Hope Park." [008-040]

## The Hunter's Bog

The duel party moves from Hope Park to the wilder ground beyond — the King's Park proper, and specifically the Hunter's Bog. The change of scene reflects the escalation from social embarrassment to actual danger:

> "We have swords at our hurdles, and here is the King's Park at hand." [008-042]

David's inner state as they enter the rough ground is vividly rendered:

> "As we came into that rough rocky desert of the King's Park I was tempted half-a-dozen times to take to my heels and run for it, so loath was I to show my ignorance in fencing, and so much averse to die or even to be wounded." [008-044]

The duel itself takes place in the Hunter's Bog:

> "We went about the end of the long craigs, and came into the Hunter's Bog." [008-045]

The encounter is farcical rather than heroic. Duncansby, discovering David's complete ignorance of swordsmanship, disarms him instantly and berates him for having "the impudence to stand up before 'shentlemans' when he did not know the back of a sword from the front of it" [008-049]. Yet this humiliation has a curious strategic value: when Duncansby is later called to testify about David's character, he becomes an unexpected witness for the defence, reporting that David "behaved himself as pretty as a shentlemans could ask it" in the Hunter's Bog [008-064].

## Significance

King's Park and the Hunter's Bog are the stage for one of Stevenson's most characteristic set-pieces: a duel that is not a duel, honour that is not honour, and a humiliation that paradoxically yields a kind of victory. The contrast between the manicured Hope Park and the "rough rocky desert" of the King's Park mirrors David's own transition from the orderly world he expects Edinburgh to be to the unpredictable, dangerous reality he actually finds.
