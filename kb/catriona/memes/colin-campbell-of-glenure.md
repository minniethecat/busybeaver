# Colin Campbell of Glenure

Colin Campbell of Glenure (also known as Colin Roy Campbell, "the Red Fox") was a Campbell laird and government factor whose assassination on 14 May 1752 — the Appin Murder — is the historical event at the centre of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. Though he never appears in the novel (he is already dead when the story opens), his killing drives every significant plot development: the warrant for Alan Breck Stewart, the arrest and trial of James Stewart of the Glens, David Balfour's summons to Edinburgh, and the political manoeuvring in Lord Prestongrange's chambers.

## Role in the Story

Glenure is the novel's structuring absence — a dead man whose murder reverberates through every scene. Stevenson's decision to keep him offstage is a crucial literary choice. By never showing Glenure alive, the novel denies the reader the satisfaction of a clear villain or a clear victim. The historical Glenure was, by most accounts, a competent and not particularly cruel administrator doing a difficult job in impossible circumstances. His appointment as factor of the forfeited Ardshiel estate — collecting rents from Stewart tenants on behalf of the government — was a political act, but Glenure himself was an instrument of policy rather than its author.

The ambiguity surrounding his character serves the novel's larger theme: that in the aftermath of Culloden, the line between justice and injustice, between lawful authority and oppression, had become impossible to draw. The man who speaks with Glenure before his death — "the boy who was speaking with Glenure when he was shot" (004-026) — is David Balfour, and David's presence at the scene makes him a crucial witness and a permanent threat to the government's narrative.

## Biography

### Background

Colin Campbell of Glenure was a member of the powerful Campbell clan and a relation of the Duke of Argyll, the most influential political figure in 18th-century Scotland. He was appointed factor (estate manager) of the Ardshiel estate after its Stewart chief, Charles Stewart of Ardshiel, was attainted for his role in the 1745 Jacobite Rising. The appointment was inflammatory — placing a Campbell in charge of Stewart lands — but was consistent with the government's policy of using Campbell loyalists to administer forfeited Jacobite estates.

### Key Actions

- As factor, Glenure was responsible for collecting rents from Stewart tenants and remitting them to the government — a role that made him the visible face of Hanoverian authority in Appin.

- On 14 May 1752, while travelling from Fort William to Duror with a small party, he was shot dead from ambush in the wood of Lettermore. The killer was never conclusively identified.

- His death sparked a political crisis that led to the arrest and execution of James Stewart of the Glens — a man widely believed to be innocent of the murder but guilty of being a Stewart.

### Fate

Glenure was killed by a single shot at the wood of Lettermore on 14 May 1752. His death — and the judicial murder of James Stewart that followed — remains one of the most controversial episodes in Scottish legal history.

## Historical Note

The historical Colin Campbell of Glenure (1708–1752) was, by contemporary accounts, a capable and fair-minded administrator. He was not personally unpopular among the Appin Stewarts, and his appointment as factor was a matter of government policy rather than personal ambition. The motive for his murder has been debated for centuries: some historians argue it was ordered by a Jacobite network seeking to eliminate an effective government agent; others suggest it was the act of a single Stewart clansman acting without authorisation from above.

Stevenson's treatment of Glenure is characteristically subtle. The novel never vilifies him — indeed, it barely mentions him except as the catalyst for events — but the reader understands that his death, however unjust, was the predictable consequence of a government policy that used Campbells to govern Stewarts in a region still raw from civil war. He is a victim, but he is also a symbol — and Stevenson, who understood the power of symbols, lets the ambiguity stand.
