# Scottish Highlands

The Scottish Highlands (Gàidhealtachd) are the mountainous northern and western region of Scotland, a landscape of lochs, glens, and heather-clad hills that serves as both physical terrain and moral geography in Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. For the novel's characters, the Highlands represent home and exile, sanctuary and danger — a place where clan loyalty still governs conduct and where the reach of Edinburgh law is uncertain. The tension between Highland honour and Lowland legality is one of the novel's central themes.

## Geography

The Highlands are separated from the Scottish Lowlands by the Highland Boundary Fault, a geological line running from Helensburgh in the west to Stonehaven in the east. The region is characterised by rugged mountains (including Ben Nevis, the highest peak in the British Isles), deep glacial lochs, and a heavily indented coastline. In the 18th century — the period of *Catriona* — the Highlands were sparsely populated, with communities organised around the clan system and an economy based on cattle-raising and subsistence agriculture.

Key locations in the novel include:

- **Appin** — the Stewart territory where the murder of Colin Campbell of Glenure takes place, and where Alan Breck Stewart hides as a fugitive
- **The heather** — the wild uplands that serve as refuge for Jacobite fugitives
- **The road from Fort William to Duror** — the route Colin Campbell of Glenure was travelling when he was shot

## In the Novel

### A Landscape of Danger and Loyalty

In *Catriona*, the Highlands are not merely scenery — they are a political and moral landscape. Lord Prestongrange warns that "disturbance in the Highlands makes the hour of our old watch" (004-079), articulating the government's fear that the region remains a powder keg after the '45 Rising. For the authorities, the Highlands are a problem to be managed; for the clans, they are home.

The clan system that structures Highland society is both a source of strength and a source of tragedy in the novel. It is clan loyalty that makes Alan Breck and James Stewart of the Glens targets of the state — "the power of the West Highlands" (009-001) is precisely what the government seeks to break. Yet it is also clan loyalty that sustains the characters: Alan Breck's network of Stewart connections allows him to survive as a fugitive, and David Balfour's acceptance into this network (symbolised by the silver button) is what gives him access to legal help in Edinburgh.

> "Disturbance in the Highlands makes the hour of our old watch." — Lord Prestongrange (004-079)

### Catriona and Highland Identity

Catriona Drummond embodies the novel's conflicted relationship with Highland identity. She describes herself as "a shame to the Highlands and the race she came of" (023-069) when she believes she has acted dishonourably — a framing that reveals how deeply clan honour codes shape personal identity. Her speech patterns (Gaelic-influenced English, rendered by Stevenson in a distinctive idiolect) mark her as Highland even when she is in Edinburgh society.

The Highlands in *Catriona* function as a mirror of character: David, the Lowland Whig, is initially an outsider to Highland codes of conduct, and his journey through the novel is partly a journey toward understanding them. Alan Breck, by contrast, is the Highlands personified — quick, honourable, dangerous, and impossible for the legal machinery of Edinburgh to contain.

## Historical Context

By the time *Catriona* is set (the early 1750s), the traditional Highland way of life was under systematic assault from the British government. The defeat of the Jacobite rising at Culloden (1746) was followed by the Disarming Act (which banned Highlanders from carrying weapons), the abolition of heritable jurisdictions (which stripped clan chiefs of their legal authority), and the forfeiture of Jacobite estates. The Appin Murder and its legal aftermath — the events at the centre of *Catriona* — cannot be understood outside this context of cultural destruction.

Stevenson, writing in the 1890s, was looking back at a Highland world that had largely vanished. The Clearances of the late 18th and early 19th centuries had depopulated the glens; the romanticised Highland of Scott and Stevenson was already a literary construction. Yet *Catriona* is more clear-eyed about this than many contemporary novels: Stevenson does not sentimentalise Highland life so much as mourn its destruction at the hands of a legal system that claimed to be just.
