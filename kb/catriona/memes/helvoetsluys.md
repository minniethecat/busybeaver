Helvoetsluys (modern Dutch: Hellevoetsluis) is a port town in the province of South Holland, situated on the Haringvliet estuary. In *Catriona*, it is the landfall for David Balfour and Catriona Macgregor Drummond after their crossing from Scotland aboard the merchant vessel *The Rose*. Catriona is to meet her father, James More Macgregor, at Helvoetsluys before continuing to France.

## Catriona's Destination

David learns of Helvoetsluys when Catriona explains her journey aboard *The Rose*. She is not travelling for pleasure but to rejoin her father, who awaits her at the Dutch port:

> "I go to meet my father at a place of the name of Helvoetsluys, and from there to France, to be exiles by the side of our chieftain." [021-023]

The phrase "to be exiles" captures the fate of the Jacobite Macgregors after the failed rising of 1745. France offers refuge under the protection of the clan chieftain Macgregor of Bohaldie — but the word "exile" reminds the reader that this is not a journey of choice.

## A Perilous Landing

The arrival at Helvoetsluys is dramatic. The *Rose* anchors outside the harbour in rough winter weather:

> "We came to an anchor about half-past eleven, outside the harbour of Helvoetsluys, in a place where the sea sometimes broke and the ship pitched outrageously." [022-001]

David's first sight of Holland — "a line of windmills birling in the breeze" — gives him "a near sense of foreign travel and a new world and life" [022-001]. But the difficulty of getting ashore tempers the romance. Captain Sang of *The Rose* is bound for Rotterdam and does not wish to delay; James More's arrangement to "tryst" with his daughter at Helvoet requires Catriona to transfer to a shore boat in dangerous seas [022-002].

The captain offers Catriona the safer option of continuing to Rotterdam and returning to Helvoet by canal-boat and wagon, but "Catriona would hear of no change" [022-004]. Her loyalty to her father's orders — despite the "bursting of the sprays" and the "green seas" — reveals both her courage and the hold James More has over her.

## David's Leap

Seeing Catriona's predicament, David makes a spontaneous decision:

> "I will go ashore with the young lady, Captain Sang," said I. "It is all one what way I go to Leyden." [022-008]

He leaps into the fishermen's boat — "which I managed not so elegantly but what I fell with two of the fishers in the bilge" [022-008]. From the pitching boat, the prospect of getting Catriona aboard seems "merely impossible," but David has underestimated her:

> "She had seen me leap with very little appearance (however much reality) of hesitation; to be sure, she was not to be beat by her discarded friend." [022-009]

## Alexander Sprott and James More's Absence

Once ashore at Helvoet, David and Catriona seek out Alexander Sprott, the Scottish merchant with whom James More was supposed to be staying. Sprott proves surly and unhelpful. When David asks "Is James More Macgregor now in Helvoet, sir?" [022-024], Sprott curtly replies, "I ken nobody by such a name" [022-025].

The merchant's evasiveness deepens when David presses with James More's aliases. Sprott's response — "he may be in Hell for what I ken, and for my part I wish he was" [022-027] — reveals not only that James More is absent but that he has left a trail of unpaid debts and broken trust behind him.

Sprott eventually concedes that James More "micht be coming here to Helvoet; he micht come here the morn, he michtnae come for a twalmouth; I would wonder at naething" [022-036]. The uncertainty leaves Catriona stranded at the port, her father's promised rendezvous unfulfilled — a pattern that will repeat throughout the novel.

## Significance

Helvoetsluys marks the beginning of the novel's Dutch sequence and David's first steps on continental soil. The rough landing, the absent father, and David's impulsive decision to abandon his own plans to accompany Catriona all prefigure the novel's central dynamic: David's steady loyalty in the face of James More's unreliability.
