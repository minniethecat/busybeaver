# Charles Stewart the Writer

Charles Stewart the Writer is an Edinburgh lawyer and the legal agent for the Stewart clan of Appin — the first person David Balfour contacts upon arriving in Edinburgh in Stevenson's *Catriona*. A figure poised uneasily between the Highland world of clan loyalty and the Lowland world of legal procedure, Stewart represents the precarious position of Jacobite sympathisers operating within the machinery of the law they cannot trust.

## Role in the Story

Charles Stewart serves as David's entry point into legal Edinburgh. It is to Stewart that David brings Alan Breck's silver button — the token of recognition that identifies David as a friend of the Stewart cause. Stewart's initial wariness ("I tell you beforehand, ye're either a Stewart or a Stewart sent ye. A good name it is... But I begin to grue at the sound of it," 002-008) establishes the novel's atmosphere of surveillance and danger: being a Stewart in post-Culloden Edinburgh is a liability, and helping one is potentially fatal.

Stewart's role is structurally significant. He occupies the lowest rung of the legal hierarchy David must climb — a Writer (solicitor), not an Advocate — and his cramped office at "the top of the longest stair ever mason set a hand to" (002-001) is a physical manifestation of his marginal position. From here, David ascends through the layers of legal Edinburgh: from Stewart to Lord Prestongrange, from the periphery of power to its centre.

## Biography

### Background

Charles Stewart is a Writer to the Signet — a senior solicitor with signing authority — and the designated legal agent for the Appin Stewarts. His surname and clan affiliation mark him as a potential target for government prosecution, and he operates under constant threat. His famous complaint — "It's been always my opinion that I would hang in a tow for this family of mine, and, dod! I believe the day's come now!" (002-012) — is both comic and genuine: the fear of execution for clan affiliation is real.

### Key Actions

- He receives David Balfour in his office on the High Street, recognising Alan Breck's silver button immediately: "The deevil's buckie, I ken the button of him!" (002-010). His recognition confirms David's bona fides and sets the legal plot in motion.

- He delivers the novel's most memorable warning: "Ye'll hang beside James Stewart. There's your fortune told" (002-056). The line encapsulates the novel's central theme — that in 18th-century Scotland, association with justice is more dangerous than association with crime.

- His visit to David is later discovered by the authorities: "my visit to Charles Stewart was discovered, and the deductions made from it" (005-042), demonstrating that even peripheral contact with a Jacobite lawyer is enough to compromise a witness.

### Fate

Charles Stewart's fate is left unresolved in the novel. He survives — we hear no more of him after the trial — but the threat that hung over him throughout remains. His position at the intersection of Highland loyalty and Edinburgh law is inherently unsustainable, and the novel implies that men like Stewart will eventually be crushed between the two.
