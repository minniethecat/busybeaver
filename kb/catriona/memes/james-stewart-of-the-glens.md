# James Stewart of the Glens

James Stewart of the Glens (Seumas a' Ghlinne) is the tragic figure at the heart of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. Wrongfully accused and condemned for the Appin Murder of 1752, his plight drives David Balfour's mission through the novel's legal landscape. Stevenson draws on the historical James Stewart — a real man executed in what was widely regarded as a judicial murder — to explore themes of justice, clan loyalty, and the corrupting influence of political power on the law.

## Role in the Story

James Stewart functions as the novel's moral centre of gravity — an absent presence whose fate hangs over every scene in Edinburgh. David's first words about his mission reveal his motivation: "First I said it was to save James Stewart" (003-002). Though James appears only indirectly (through David's prison visits and the trial proceedings), his predicament structures the entire legal drama.

Stevenson uses James Stewart to expose the machinery of 18th-century Scottish justice: a system where "James is a dead man" (017-068) before the trial even concludes, where the outcome is predetermined by clan politics. The finality of his execution — "poor James of the Glens was duly hanged at Lettermore" (020-046) — is rendered in a single devastating sentence, a stylistic choice that mirrors the brutal efficiency of the state's violence. His death transforms David from a naive justice-seeker into a man who understands that law and justice are not synonymous.

## Biography

### Background

James Stewart was the half-brother of Charles Stewart of Ardshiel, the chief of the Stewarts of Appin, and served as factor (estate manager) for the clan lands during Ardshiel's exile after the 1745 Jacobite Rising. He was a respected figure in Appin — a man of substance and standing — which made his prosecution all the more shocking to the Highland community.

### Key Actions

- James is arrested for the Appin Murder, the assassination of Colin Campbell of Glenure (the "Red Fox"), who had been appointed as government factor over the forfeited Stewart estates. David Balfour is present at the murder scene in *Kidnapped*, making him a crucial witness.

- Charles Stewart the Writer warns David of the danger of associating with James's case: "Ye'll hang beside James Stewart. There's your fortune told" (002-056). This establishes the political risk early.

- Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, explicitly names James as the target of prosecution: "I need not tell you that I mean James Stewart" (004-071). The candour is chilling — the state's machinery is already in motion.

- James's trial proceeds with "counsel four in number" (017-008), but the outcome is never in doubt. Even David's ally Simon Fraser tells him bluntly: "there is no question of James Stewart, for or against, James is a dead man" (017-068).

- James is hanged at Lettermore on 8 November 1752 (020-046). David's failure to save him — despite every effort — is the novel's central moral wound, one that shapes his subsequent relationship with Catriona and his ultimate rejection of the legal career he had sought.

### Fate

Executed by hanging at Lettermore in November 1752. The historical James Stewart died protesting his innocence, and his bones were left hanging in chains for years as a warning — a practice Stevenson notes with quiet outrage. His execution is now regarded by historians as one of the most notorious miscarriages of justice in 18th-century Scottish legal history.

## Historical Note

The historical James Stewart of the Glens (Seumas a' Ghlinne) was executed on 8 November 1752 at Ballachulish for the murder of Colin Campbell of Glenure — the "Appin Murder." Modern historical consensus holds that James Stewart was almost certainly innocent. He was tried at Inveraray by a jury composed entirely of Campbells (the clan of the victim), presided over by the Duke of Argyll, chief of Clan Campbell. The verdict was a foregone conclusion.

Stevenson uses this historical miscarriage of justice as the moral engine of *Catriona*. The novel is substantially faithful to the known facts: the clan politics, the predetermined verdict, the grim spectacle of the hanging. Where Stevenson departs from history is in inserting David Balfour as a witness and advocate — a literary device that gives the reader an intimate view of the legal machinery. The novel's James is dignified and stoic; the historical record suggests the real James Stewart was similarly composed at his execution, maintaining his innocence to the end.

The Appin Murder remains officially unsolved, though most historians now believe the actual shooter was a younger Stewart clansman. James Stewart's conviction was, in the words of one modern legal historian, "a sacrifice to the needs of state policy rather than the demands of justice."
