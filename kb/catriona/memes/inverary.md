Inverary (modern spelling: Inveraray) is a town in Argyll, western Scotland, situated on the shore of Loch Fyne. In the 18th century, it was the "head burgh of the Campbells" [009-013] — the seat of Clan Campbell's power and the administrative centre of Argyllshire. In *Catriona*, Inverary is the site of the trial of James Stewart of the Glens for the Appin Murder, a proceeding that forms the political and legal backdrop of the novel's early chapters.

## In the Novel

The trial at Inverary is first mentioned to David Balfour by the Lord Advocate, Prestongrange. David has come forward to testify on behalf of James Stewart, but Prestongrange informs him that his testimony has been accepted — a decision David quickly learns is a manipulation to control his movements:

> "Your testimony is after all to be received; and you may go, if you will, in my company to the trial, which is to be held at Inverary, Thursday, 21st proximo." [008-004]

The date — Thursday, 21st September 1752 — fixes a real historical event within the novel's timeline. By attaching David to his own party, Prestongrange ensures that the young Whig cannot independently reach the defence.

## The Seat of Campbell Power

Inverary's significance as a place lies not merely in its geography but in what it represents: the concentrated legal and political power of Clan Campbell. The choice of Inverary as the trial venue — deep within Campbell territory, far from the Stewart country of Appin — was itself a statement of that power.

When the Grant daughters tease David about his upcoming journey, their words underscore the danger:

> "Where, but at the cross of Inverary, the head burgh of the Campbells?" [009-013]

And Catriona's distress at the thought of David going there — "And I am not to see them until Inverary, when the court is set!" [009-023] — reveals her fear that a trial held on Campbell ground, with a Campbell jury and the full weight of clan politics behind the prosecution, can have only one outcome.

## Alan Breck's Intervention

Alan Breck Stewart, who has been watching over David from hiding, understands the political reality of Inverary better than anyone. He intercepts David before the journey and makes his own arrangements:

> "Five days before the trial, September the sixteen, get word to me at the King's Arms in Stirling; and if ye've managed for yourself as long as that, I'll see that ye reach Inverary." [009-044]

Alan's promise to ensure David reaches Inverary is a commitment to see him safely through Campbell country — a journey that, for a Stewart partisan, was fraught with danger.

## Historical Context

The real trial of James Stewart took place at Inverary on 21 September 1752, presided over by the Lord Justice-Clerk and the Lord Justice-General (Archibald Campbell, 3rd Duke of Argyll). The jury was composed of Campbell clansmen and dependents. The verdict — guilty — was widely regarded as a foregone conclusion, and the trial became a symbol of the politicised justice that followed the Jacobite rising of 1745.
