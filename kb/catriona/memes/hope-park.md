Hope Park is the country residence of Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate of
Scotland, located near Edinburgh. It serves as a key setting in Robert Louis
Stevenson's *Catriona*, where the private political machinations of the Crown's
case against James Stewart unfold against a backdrop of "beautiful pleasance"
and carefully cultivated gardens.

## Location and Description

Hope Park is described as "a beautiful pleasance, laid out with walks and
plantings" (008-029), situated within riding distance of Edinburgh. Stevenson
locates it alongside other notable Edinburgh-area estates — "Silvermills, and
Broughton, and Hope Park, and Pilrig, and poor old Lochend" (P04-004) —
placing it among the gentry residences that ringed the 18th-century city.

The estate's beauty is calculated: the gardens and walks serve Prestongrange's
political purposes, providing a setting where unofficial business can be
conducted under the guise of social hospitality. David Balfour is conducted
through the grounds, presented to guests, and managed — all within the
framework of aristocratic courtesy that Hope Park embodies.

## In the Novel

Hope Park is the setting for two crucial functions in *Catriona*. First, it
is where David is received and introduced to the circle of Crown officials
and collaborators, including Simon Fraser of Lovat and the Sheriff of Perth.
The dinner-party atmosphere — civilised, well-mannered, entirely proper —
masks the brutal political calculus being conducted at the table.

Second, it is where David's informal sequestration begins. He is a guest,
not a prisoner; he may walk the grounds but not leave them; he is treated
with every courtesy except the freedom to act on his own conscience. Hope
Park is thus the velvet glove over the iron fist of Crown power — the
setting that makes coercion taste like hospitality.

The estate also appears as a destination for young women of quality: Barbara
Grant receives visitors there, and the "three very tedious misses to Hope
Park" (019-047) suggests its role in Edinburgh society life beyond the
political sphere.

## Significance

Hope Park represents the spatial dimension of Prestongrange's power. In the
city, at Parliament House, the Lord Advocate is an official among officials.
At Hope Park, he is a host, and the shift from public to private space mirrors
the shift from legal argument to political management. The most consequential
decisions in the Appin Murder case are made not in court but at Prestongrange's
dinner table.

Stevenson uses the contrast between Hope Park's beauty and the ugly business
conducted within it to underscore the novel's critique of power. The
"beautiful pleasance" is not merely decorative — it is instrumental, part of
the apparatus by which inconvenient witnesses are managed and inconvenient
truths suppressed.
