# Royal Pardon

The royal pardon — the sovereign's prerogative power to remit punishment for crimes — is the invisible currency that drives much of the political intrigue in Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. Every Jacobite fugitive, from Alan Breck Stewart to James More MacGregor, lives in the shadow of the gallows and dreams of a pardon. The power to grant or withhold pardons is what makes Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, so formidable: he controls access to the sovereign's mercy, and characters scheme, betray, and kill to obtain what only he can give.

## Overview

In 18th-century Scots law, the royal pardon was a prerogative of the Crown, exercised on the advice of ministers — most significantly, the Lord Advocate. For Jacobite rebels, a pardon represented the difference between life as an outlaw and a return to civil society: the restoration of property, the right to move freely, the end of the perpetual threat of arrest. The system was inherently political: pardons were granted not on the basis of justice but on the basis of utility to the government.

## In the Novel

### The Pardon as MacGuffin

James More MacGregor's entire plot in *Catriona* is driven by the quest for a pardon. A former Jacobite officer, he has spent years in exile and imprisonment, and his return to Scotland is conditional on his usefulness to the government. The pardon he seeks is not justice — he is, by any honest measure, guilty of the crimes for which he was imprisoned — but convenience: the government finds him more useful as an informer than as a corpse.

The pardon functions as a narrative MacGuffin: it motivates James More's actions (his approach to David, his journey to Dunkirk, his attempted betrayal of Alan Breck) without ever being granted. The reader gradually realises that James More is chasing a phantom — the government never intended to pardon him, merely to use him. His death at Alan Breck's hands is the structural consequence of betting his life on an instrument of power that was never designed to serve him.

### Prestongrange's Discretionary Power

Lord Prestongrange's authority in the novel is not primarily judicial — he does not, in *Catriona*, sit as a judge or deliver a verdict. It is administrative: he controls the machinery of prosecution, and with it the machinery of pardon. When he warns David or offers terms, he is speaking from this position. David's refusal to cooperate — his insistence on telling the truth about Alan Breck's innocence — is a refusal to play the pardon game, and Prestongrange's sequestration of David on the Bass Rock is the predictable response of a system that cannot tolerate witnesses who refuse to be managed.

> "Under your pardon, my lord, I would have you to know..." — David Balfour to Prestongrange (004-070)

The phrase "under your pardon" is a formal courtesy, but it carries a double meaning in the novel: everything David does is under Prestongrange's pardon, in the sense that Prestongrange holds the power to destroy him or spare him. David's use of the phrase is polite; Prestongrange's understanding of it is literal.

### The Political Economy of Mercy

Stevenson's portrait of the pardon system is a study in the political economy of mercy. Pardons are not distributed according to desert but according to utility. Alan Breck Stewart — a genuinely innocent man (of the murder, at least) — cannot obtain one because he has nothing to offer the government. James More MacGregor — a manipulator and a traitor — cannot obtain one either, because what he offers (information about Alan Breck) can be taken from him without the government having to honour its side of the bargain.

The pardon thus functions as a critique of discretionary justice. It is the mirror image of the trial of James Stewart: where the trial is law without mercy, the pardon system is mercy without law — an arbitrary power that reinforces the very injustice it claims to remedy.

## Historical Context

The royal pardon was a well-established feature of 18th-century British law, used extensively in the aftermath of the 1745 Jacobite Rising. Many Jacobite officers received pardons in exchange for information or exile; others were executed. The system created a network of informers and a culture of distrust within Jacobite communities, as former rebels were turned against each other by the promise of royal mercy. This historical reality is the background against which Stevenson's fictional treatment takes shape.
