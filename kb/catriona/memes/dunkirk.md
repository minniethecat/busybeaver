Dunkirk (French: Dunkerque) is a port town in northern France, situated on the North Sea coast near the Belgian border. In *Catriona*, the town serves as the destination of the novel's climactic journey, where David Balfour and Alan Breck Stewart travel from Leyden to reunite with Catriona Macgregor Drummond and her father James More.

## In the Novel

David first learns of Dunkirk through Catriona's letters. After her departure from Leyden with James More, she writes to inform David of their arrival in the French port town:

> One was to announce their arrival in the town of Dunkirk in France, from which place James shortly after started alone upon a private mission. [029-001]

James More's "private mission" was, in fact, a journey to England to see Lord Holderness — a trip partially funded by David's own money, a fact that later stings him. The third and final letter from Catriona arrives while David is already planning his journey, and it proves perfectly timed: "James professed to be in some concern upon his daughter's health ... and finally proposed that I should visit them at Dunkirk" [029-023].

## The Journey and Arrival

David and Alan set out from Leyden, riding through the winter landscape of the Low Countries. They arrive at the town after dark on a January evening:

> It was near dark of a January day when we rode at last into the town of Dunkirk. We left our horses at the post, and found a guide to Bazin's Inn, which lay beyond the walls. Night was quite fallen, so that we were the last to leave that fortress, and heard the doors of it close behind us as we passed the bridge. [029-029]

The image of the fortress gates closing behind them is a resonant one — David and Alan have crossed into a place where the old rules of Scotland no longer apply, and where their confrontation with James More will play out on French soil.

## Bazin's Inn and the Reunion

Bazin's Inn, situated outside the town walls, becomes the setting for the novel's emotional center. Here David is finally reunited with Catriona after their long separation. The morning after their arrival, James More departs for Dunkirk proper while Alan follows him, leaving David alone with Catriona. Their reunion is charged with both joy and the unresolved tension of her father's presence.

David's description of the landscape around Dunkirk captures his state of happiness:

> I have never seen the place that looked so pretty as those bents by Dunkirk; and the windmill sails, as they bobbed over the knowe, were like a tune of music. [030-033]

The "bents" (grassy dunes) and windmills of the Flanders coast become, in David's eyes, the most beautiful landscape he has ever seen — coloured by his joy at being reunited with the woman he loves.

## The Confrontation and Departure

The idyll does not last. Alan Breck Stewart's true purpose in coming to Dunkirk is revealed when James More proposes a secret meeting on the sand-dunes. Alan's exclamation — "Well, and I'm glad I came to Dunkirk. And so this was your business, was it?" [030-096] — marks the turning point. The ensuing confrontation forces David and Alan to flee with Catriona across the bents, pursued by James More's men.

The chase back toward the town walls is desperate:

> For all which, it was a hot, brisk bit of work, so long as it lasted; Dunkirk was still far off; and when we popped over a knowe, and found a company of the garrison marching on the other side on some manœuvre, I could very well understand the thing to be highly dangerous. [030-130]

## The Council of War

Once safely back within the town walls, the three fugitives hold a council-of-war:

> No sooner were we safe within the walls of Dunkirk than we held a very necessary council-of-war on our position. We had taken a daughter from her father at the sword's point; any judge would give her back to him at once, and by all likelihood clap me and Alan into jail. [031-001]

The decision is made to carry Catriona to Paris, to the protection of her clan chieftain Macgregor of Bohaldie — a resolution that sets the course for the novel's final chapters.
