# Whig and Jacobite

The defining political division of 18th-century Scotland: Whigs supported the Hanoverian succession, the Union with England, and Presbyterian governance, while Jacobites sought the restoration of the exiled Stuart dynasty, and with it a more independent Scotland. In *Catriona*, this divide shapes every character's allegiances, legal standing, and personal fate.

## Definition

A **Whig** in mid-18th-century Scotland was a supporter of the Glorious Revolution (1688), the Hanoverian succession, the Act of Union (1707), and Presbyterian church government. A **Jacobite** (from Latin *Jacobus*, "James") was a supporter of the exiled House of Stuart — James II (deposed 1688), his son James Francis Edward Stuart ("the Old Pretender"), and his grandson Charles Edward Stuart ("Bonnie Prince Charlie"). The Jacobite cause was defeated militarily at Culloden (1746), but Jacobite sentiment persisted in the Highlands and among certain Lowland families (002-060).

## Historical Context

The Whig–Jacobite conflict was at once constitutional, dynastic, and religious. Whigs championed Parliament's supremacy over the Crown, while Jacobites held to the divine right of kings. In Scottish terms, the Whig cause was bound up with Presbyterianism and the Union with England; the Jacobite cause with Episcopalianism (or Catholicism) and Scottish independence.

After the failed Rising of 1745, the Hanoverian government pursued a policy of pacification in the Highlands — disarming clans, abolishing heritable jurisdictions, and proscribing Highland dress. Yet the political identities persisted. As one character observes, being "a good Whig" meant keeping one's head on one's shoulders (007-026). The Lord Advocate Prestongrange himself moves between Whig legality and Highland pragmatism, warning that "disturbance in the Highlands" could produce "a '45 again with the Campbells on the other side" (004-077, 004-079).

## Role in the Novel

### David Balfour's Whig Identity

David Balfour, the narrator, identifies himself plainly as a Whig: "I'm as big a Whig as grows" (002-015). His Whiggery is partly inherited from his father, the schoolmaster of Essendean, and partly the result of his own law-bound, Presbyterian character. It gives him standing with the authorities — his name on a letter is "a good honest Whig name" (004-035) — but also marks him as an outsider in the Highland world of clan loyalty and Jacobite intrigue.

### The Jacobite World

The novel's Jacobite characters are not cardboard villains. James More Macgregor — Catriona's father — is a Jacobite, but his loyalty is less to the Stuarts than to his own clan and self-interest. Alan Breck Stewart, the fiery Jacobite of *Kidnapped*, appears in the background as a wanted rebel with "two hundred pounds on his life" (002-016). The Duke of Argyll, though a Hanoverian power-broker, is himself a Highlander who understands clan politics from within (004-077).

### Whig as Insult and Identity

Throughout the novel, "Whig" functions as both a political label and a term of social judgment. Andie Dale calls the Master of Lovat "a braw Whig" with heavy irony (014-033), and wonders whether suspicious dealings could be the work of "guid sound Whigs and true-blue Presbyterians" (014-032). David himself reflects that he is "good enough Whig to sit quiet" but not "good enough Whig to be made a fool of" (007-026). The political becomes personal.

### Legal Consequences

The Whig–Jacobite divide had immediate legal consequences. Jacobites were subject to forfeiture, attainder, and execution. James Stewart of the Glens is tried and hanged not purely for the Appin Murder, but in a political trial where his Jacobite affiliation makes him presumptively guilty. David's Whig identity protects him — barely — from the same machinery.
