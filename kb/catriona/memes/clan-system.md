# Clan System

The kinship-based social and political structure of the Scottish Highlands, which in the mid-18th century remained a powerful force despite government efforts to dismantle it after the Jacobite rising of 1745.

## Definition

The clan (Gaelic: *clann*, "children" or "family") was a patrilineal kinship group bound by real or perceived common descent, territorial association, and loyalty to a chief. In the period of *Catriona* (set in the years following the 1745 Rising), the clan system was under assault from Hanoverian law but persisted as the primary social organization of Highland life (002-064).

## Historical Context

Following the defeat of the Jacobite cause at Culloden (1746), the British government enacted a series of measures to destroy clan power: the Disarming Act prohibited Highlanders from bearing arms, the Heritable Jurisdictions Act abolished the chiefs' legal authority, and the Act of Proscription banned Highland dress including the tartan and kilt. Yet the social bonds of clanship proved resilient — loyalty to chief and name persisted in exile, in the French service, and in the minds of those who had lost everything (006-005).

## Role in the Novel

### Clan Politics as Plot Driver

The events of *Catriona* are set in motion by clan politics. The Appin Murder (1752) — the shooting of Colin Campbell of Glenure, the government factor on the forfeited Stewart estate of Ardshiel — was a clan killing in miniature: a Campbell administrator killed in Stewart country, leading to the trial and execution of James Stewart of the Glens (007-057). David Balfour, the narrator, is caught between the demands of clan loyalty and the machinery of Hanoverian justice.

### Clan Macgregor

James More Macgregor — Catriona's father — is a Macgregor, a member of the most notorious of the proscribed clans. The name Macgregor was itself outlawed after 1603, and James More embodies the clan's reputation: proud, lawless, and recklessly brave. His daughter Catriona inherits both the clan's fierce loyalty and its precarious legal status (004-077).

### The Campbells

The great clan of Campbell — led by the Duke of Argyll — represents the establishment: Whig, Presbyterian, Hanoverian, and in possession of the power that clan politics once distributed more widely. Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate who prosecutes James Stewart, is a Campbell by affiliation if not by name. The narrative tension between Macgregor and Campbell, between proscribed and patronized clans, mirrors the larger Highland tragedy (017-007).

## Key Clans Referenced

- **Clan Macgregor** — Proscribed since 1603; James More and Catriona belong to this clan
- **Clan Campbell** — The dominant Whig clan; the Duke of Argyll and the Appin factor
- **Clan Stewart** — The clan of James Stewart of the Glens; the Jacobite Stewarts of Appin
- **Clan Fraser** — Simon Fraser, Lord Lovat, was its chief; a clan divided between Jacobite and government loyalties (006-005)
