# Bass Rock

A stark volcanic island rising 107 metres from the Firth of Forth, about five miles northeast of North Berwick — in the 18th century a formidable state prison, and in *Catriona* the place of David Balfour's enforced "sequestration." It is on the Bass that David endures his weeks of confinement, companioned by Andie Dale and guarded by a trio of Highland "cattle-thieves."

## Location

The Bass Rock lies in the outer Firth of Forth, visible from the East Lothian coast and from Edinburgh on a clear day. It is an ancient volcanic plug, its cliffs rising sheer from the sea, surmounted by the ruins of a castle and a chapel. The island is uninhabited except by seabirds — particularly the vast colony of gannets (called "solan geese" in Scots) that give the rock its white appearance. Access is by boat only, and in the 18th century the journey from the mainland was hazardous in all but the calmest weather.

## Role in the Story

### David's Sequestration

After David refuses Prestongrange's pressure to abandon James Stewart, the Lord Advocate arranges for David to be "sequestered" on the Bass Rock. The sequestration is a legal grey area — not quite an arrest, not quite a kidnapping — designed to keep David away from the trial without the embarrassment of charging him. David is seized by Highland men and taken by boat to the island, where he is placed under the nominal guard of Andie Dale, the rock's keeper (014-002, 014-019).

### Life on the Rock

The chapters set on the Bass (approximately chapters 14–19) form the novel's central interlude. David's days are spent in conversation with Andie Dale, in uneasy truce with his Highland guards, and in reflection on the events that have brought him here. The rock's isolation mirrors David's moral isolation — cut off from the trial, from Catriona, and from the world of law he had hoped to enter.

### Escape and Return

David's escape from the Bass — aided by Catriona and the complicated loyalties of his captors — is one of the novel's set pieces. His return to Edinburgh marks his transition from managed witness to active protagonist. He is no longer a man things are done to; he becomes a man who does things.

## Description

Stevenson's Bass Rock is a place of "dreadful but merry" sound — the crash of waves against the cliffs, the cries of the gannets, the wind whistling through the ruins. David observes that "when the waves were anyway great they roared about the rock like thunder and the drums of armies, dreadful but merry to hear; and it was in the calm days that a man could daunt himself with listening" (015-004). The rock's physical extremity — its height, its isolation, the constant presence of the sea — becomes a metaphor for David's psychological state.

## Historical Context

The Bass Rock has a long history as a prison. In the 1670s and 1680s, it was used to hold Covenanters — Presbyterian dissidents who refused to accept the king's authority over the church. Some of these prisoners were held in conditions of extreme hardship, and the rock acquired a dark reputation in Presbyterian memory. By David Balfour's time, the rock was a more routine place of confinement, but its reputation lingered. Andie Dale, the keeper, is something of a local historian, and his stories of the rock's past — "a place full of history, both human and divine" (014-019) — provide the novel with a deeper historical resonance.
