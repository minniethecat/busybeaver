# Sequestration on the Bass Rock

The sequestration of David Balfour on the Bass Rock is the central political event of the middle section of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. To prevent David from testifying on behalf of James Stewart of the Glens — who was on trial for the Appin Murder — the Lord Advocate Lord Prestongrange arranges for David to be confined on the Bass Rock, a fortress-island in the Firth of Forth. The sequestration is a masterful stroke of legal-political maneuvering: David is not imprisoned, not charged with any crime, and therefore has no right of appeal. He is simply... removed. The event exposes the gulf between the forms of law and the substance of justice.

## Overview

The sequestration occurs after David Balfour arrives in Edinburgh as a witness in the Appin Murder case. His knowledge of the events in Appin — and particularly his knowledge of Alan Breck Stewart's innocence — makes him a dangerous witness for the prosecution. Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, first attempts to persuade David to cooperate with the government's case. When David refuses, Prestongrange resorts to a more elegant solution: David is placed on the Bass Rock "for his own safety," a legal fiction that masks the true purpose of preventing his testimony.

> "The whole business of my sequestration was designed for privacy." (014-017)

The Bass Rock is not a dungeon; David is treated as a guest of the governor, with freedom to walk the island's cliffs and study the seabirds. But the effect is the same as imprisonment: while James Stewart faces trial at Inveraray, David is marooned on a rock in the sea, powerless to help.

## Historical Background

The Bass Rock is a real volcanic island in the Firth of Forth, approximately 1.2 kilometres offshore from North Berwick. In the 17th and 18th centuries, it served as a state prison — most famously housing Covenanting ministers during the "Killing Time" of the 1680s. By the time of *Catriona*, it had been converted into a less formal place of confinement, used for political detainees whom the government wished to remove from circulation without the awkwardness of a public trial.

Stevenson's choice of the Bass Rock as the site of David's sequestration is thematically rich. The Bass had been a symbol of resistance during the Covenanting era — prisoners had actually seized control of the fortress from their captors. For David, however, it represents the opposite: not resistance but impotence, the place where his legal idealism founders on the rocks of political reality.

## The Event in the Novel

### Narrative Function

The sequestration serves three crucial narrative functions in *Catriona*:

First, it isolates David from the legal proceedings, creating dramatic tension — the reader knows the trial is happening on the mainland while David can only wait for news. The physical separation mirrors David's psychological separation from his ideals: he came to Edinburgh believing in justice, and the Bass is where that belief is systematically dismantled.

Second, it provides the setting for the deepening of David's relationship with Catriona. Her visits to the Bass Rock are among the novel's most emotionally charged scenes — "I could hear my heart go" — and the enforced intimacy of the island setting strips away the formalities that constrained their earlier interactions in Edinburgh.

Third, it represents the novel's most explicit critique of legal power. Prestongrange's sequestration of David is perfectly legal: no charge is filed, no right is violated, no writ of habeas corpus can be issued because David is not technically a prisoner. It is law as pure power, stripped of any pretence of justice.

### Catriona's Visit

Catriona Drummond's visit to David on the Bass Rock is a turning point in their relationship. She arrives, having navigated the treacherous crossing, to deliver news and comfort. The scene, with the seabirds "crying about the top of the Bass" (014-006), is Stevenson's most vivid use of the setting — the wildness of the rock and sea mirroring the wildness of the emotions that David and Catriona struggle to express within the constraints of 18th-century propriety.

## Aftermath

David's sequestration ends when the trial of James Stewart concludes with a guilty verdict. With the verdict rendered, David's testimony is no longer a threat, and he is quietly released. The experience transforms him: the naive young man who arrived in Edinburgh believing in the justice of the law leaves the Bass Rock understanding that the law is an instrument of power, not truth.

> "I should embody the fact of his sequestration, and perhaps some heads of the trial." (017-032)

The Bass Rock sequence is one of the most original elements of *Catriona*, with no real parallel in 19th-century fiction. It anticipates Kafka's critique of legal bureaucracy by decades — a man is deprived of his liberty not through violence but through the quiet, correct application of administrative power.
