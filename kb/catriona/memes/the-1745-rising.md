# The 1745 Rising

The Jacobite Rising of 1745, known in Scotland simply as "the '45," was the last serious attempt by the exiled House of Stuart to recover the British throne. Led by Charles Edward Stuart — "Bonnie Prince Charlie" — the rising began in the Highlands, reached as far south as Derby, and ended in catastrophe at the Battle of Culloden. In *Catriona*, set six years after Culloden, the '45 is the recent past that shadows every character's present.

## Overview

In July 1745, Charles Edward Stuart landed in the Hebrides with seven men and no army. Within weeks, he had raised the Highland clans — primarily the Camerons, Macdonalds, and Stewarts of Appin — and marched on Edinburgh, which surrendered without a fight. The Jacobite army defeated the only government force in Scotland at Prestonpans, crossed into England, and reached Derby, 130 miles from London, before a council of war overruled the Prince and ordered a retreat. The retreat ended at Culloden Moor, near Inverness, on 16 April 1746, where the Duke of Cumberland's government army destroyed the Jacobite force in less than an hour.

## Historical Background

The '45 was the culmination of decades of Jacobite plotting. The "Old Pretender," James Francis Edward Stuart, had attempted an invasion in 1708 and a rising in 1715; both failed. His son Charles Edward was a more charismatic leader but a poorer strategist. The clans that rose for him did so for a mixture of reasons — loyalty to the Stuarts, hostility to the Hanoverians, clan politics, economic desperation, and the magnetic pull of a prince who spoke Gaelic and promised to restore a Scottish parliament.

The government's response after Culloden was ruthless. The Disarming Act, the Heritable Jurisdictions Act, and the Act of Proscription were designed to destroy the Highlands' capacity for rebellion. Chiefs were attainted, estates were forfeited, and the clan system was dismantled by statute. The '45 was a military defeat; its aftermath was a social revolution.

## The Event in the Novel

### Depiction

The '45 is never directly depicted in *Catriona* — it is the past, but a past that is not yet history. Stevenson evokes it through conversation and memory. Prestongrange warns David: "You are not so young but what you must remember very clearly the year '45 and the shock that went about the country" (004-077). The shock is still reverberating. The novel's action — the trial, the sequestration, the political calculations — are all aftershocks.

### Narrative Role

The '45 provides the political context for every action in the novel. James Stewart is tried not merely for murder but as a Jacobite — the Appin Murder is processed through a legal system that sees Jacobite and criminal as nearly synonymous. David's Whig identity, his Lowland Presbyterianism, his faith in the law — all are defined against the Jacobite cause that the '45 represented. And yet David, like Scotland itself, cannot simply dismiss the Jacobites as enemies. His friendship with Alan Breck, his love for Catriona, his respect for the courage of Highland soldiers — all complicate the simple Whig narrative.

## Aftermath

The consequences of the '45 are the conditions of the novel. The forfeited estates, the attainted chiefs, the proscribed clans, the disarmed Highlands, the Campbells in the ascendant, the Jacobites in exile or on the run — every character in *Catriona* lives in the world the '45 made. The novel, set in 1751–1752, inhabits the moment when the government was consolidating its victory and the Highlands were being forcibly integrated into the British state. David Balfour, the young Whig laird, is a beneficiary of that integration; Catriona Drummond, the daughter of a Jacobite proscript, is one of its casualties.

## Historical Record

The '45 has generated an enormous historical literature, from contemporary accounts to modern reassessments. The standard scholarly treatment is Christopher Duffy's *The '45* (2003), which argues that the rising was a closer-run thing than Cumberland's propaganda allowed. Murray Pittock's work has emphasized the European dimension — the '45 was part of the War of the Austrian Succession, with French backing that failed to materialize. Stevenson, writing in the 1890s, drew on the romanticized Jacobite historiography of the 19th century, but his psychological acuity gives his fictional treatment a depth that partisan history often lacks.
