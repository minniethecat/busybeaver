# Edinburgh

In the mid-18th century, Edinburgh was a city of striking contrasts — an ancient medieval Old Town clinging to the spine of the Royal Mile, with the law courts, the university, and the dense tenements of the legal and mercantile classes; and a cramped, filthy, but intensely social world that Samuel Johnson would later call "a city of palaces and dungeons." In *Catriona*, Edinburgh is the stage on which David Balfour's legal and romantic education unfolds.

## Location

Edinburgh sits on the southern shore of the Firth of Forth, in the Lothian region of Lowland Scotland. The Old Town, where most of the novel's Edinburgh scenes take place, runs along the ridge from the Castle at the top of the Royal Mile to the Palace of Holyroodhouse at the bottom. To the north, across the Nor' Loch (drained in the 19th century, now Princes Street Gardens), lay the "New Town" — still largely unbuilt at the period of the novel. To the south lay the Meadows and the village of Dean, where Catriona lodges (001-053).

## Role in the Story

### The Advocate's Chambers

Much of the novel's political action takes place in the chambers of Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate. David Balfour is summoned there repeatedly — to be examined as a witness, to be pressured into silence, and to confront the men who hold James Stewart's fate in their hands. The Advocate's office is both a place of law and a place of political intrigue, where "a word to Prestongrange" (003-017) can alter a man's fortunes.

### The Streets and Closes

It is on an Edinburgh street — in one of the dark closes off the High Street — that David first meets Catriona Drummond. She is standing with two ragged Highland gillies, trying to scrape together money for snuff for her imprisoned father. "It is the house of the Lord Advocate Grant of Prestongrange," she tells David, pointing to the door nearby (001-041). This chance meeting in the Edinburgh close sets the entire novel in motion.

### The City as Political Stage

Edinburgh in the 1750s was the centre of Scottish legal and political life. The Court of Session, the Faculty of Advocates, and the offices of the Crown all clustered within a few hundred yards of Parliament Square. David, a law student and a newly minted laird, walks these streets as both a participant in and an observer of the great game being played around the Appin Murder trial.

## Description

Stevenson's Edinburgh is a city of shadows and whispers. David moves through closes and staircases, past "the house of my enemy" (the Advocate's), through streets where a man might be watched without knowing it. The city's physical density mirrors its moral density — law and power are concentrated here, and a young man from the country must learn to navigate both.

## Historical Context

At the period of *Catriona* (1751–1752), Edinburgh was a city in transition. The trauma of the '45 Rising was still fresh. The city had been occupied by Jacobite forces in 1745, and the Hanoverian establishment was determined that such a thing would never happen again. The legal apparatus of the state — the Lord Advocate, the Court of Session, the Crown Office — was the instrument of that determination. Yet Edinburgh was also a city of Enlightenment: David Hume was publishing his philosophical works, the Select Society was debating political economy, and the university was training a generation of lawyers, ministers, and men of letters. David Balfour, the law student with a Whig conscience, is a product of both Edinburghs — the city of power and the city of ideas.
