Lettermore (or the Wood of Lettermore) is a location in the Appin district of Argyll, in the Scottish Highlands. It is the site of two of the most consequential events in *Catriona*: the murder of Colin Campbell of Glenure — the "Appin Murder" of May 1752 — and, months later, the execution of James Stewart of the Glens for that crime.

## The Murder

The Appin murder took place in the Wood of Lettermore. Colin Campbell of Glenure, the Crown's factor on the forfeited Stewart estates, was shot from ambush while riding through the wood. David Balfour, travelling with Alan Breck Stewart at the time, witnessed the immediate aftermath. The Lord Advocate, Prestongrange, interrogates David about his presence at the scene:

> "He spread a sheet of paper on the table and wrote a heading. 'It appears you were present, by the way, in the wood of Lettermore at the moment of the fatal shot,' he began. 'Was this by accident?'" [004-043]

David's presence at Lettermore is the central fact from which all the political intrigue of the novel unfolds. His knowledge of the true events — and the Crown's determination to suppress that knowledge in order to convict James Stewart — places him at the heart of a moral crisis.

## The Hanging

On November 8, 1752, James Stewart of the Glens was hanged at Lettermore, near Ballachulish, convicted of a murder he did not commit:

> "Prestongrange and his grace the Lord President may have heard of it (for what I know) on the deafest sides of their heads; they kept it to themselves, at least — the public was none the wiser; and in course of time, on November 8th, and in the midst of a prodigious storm of wind and rain, poor James of the Glens was duly hanged at Lettermore by Ballachulish." [020-046]

The execution site was deliberately chosen: to hang James Stewart on the very ground where Glenure was shot was a calculated act of political theatre, demonstrating the Crown's power over the Highland clans. The "prodigious storm of wind and rain" that accompanied the hanging adds a note of cosmic disapproval to the judicial murder.

## Significance

Lettermore encapsulates the novel's central political tragedy. It is both the scene of a crime and the scene of a graver injustice — the execution of a man who was, as Prestongrange himself privately acknowledged, "the unmistakable original of the deed" [004-071] rather than its perpetrator. David's helpless knowledge of the truth, and his eventual complicity in the system that carried out the execution, is the moral wound at the heart of *Catriona*.
