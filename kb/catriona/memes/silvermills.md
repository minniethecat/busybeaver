Silvermills is a village on the Water of Leith, lying to the northwest of 18th-century Edinburgh. In *Catriona*, it serves as Alan Breck Stewart's hiding place — a "bit scrog of wood" [011-001] east of the village where he lies low while monitoring David Balfour's dangerous entanglement with the Lord Advocate and the Appin murder case.

## Alan's Hiding Place

After David's testimony has been accepted and he is to travel to Inverary with Prestongrange, he learns from a contact that Alan Breck is concealed near Silvermills. Alan, ever watchful, has been keeping vigil over David's safety from the outskirts of Edinburgh:

> "But I can never deny that Alan is extremely keen of it, and is to lie this night by Silvermills on purpose." [009-046]

The choice of Silvermills as a hiding place is tactically sound: close enough to Edinburgh for Alan to receive word of developments, yet far enough from the city's patrols and informants to provide concealment. The "bit scrog of wood by east of Silvermills and by south the south mill-lade" [011-001] offers cover and a clear view of the approach from the city.

## David's Journey to the Tryst

David makes his way to the rendezvous at night, following the Water of Leith downstream:

> "My way was down the glen of the Leith River, towards Stockbridge and Silvermills. A path led in the foot of it." [010-047]

The urgency of his mission is clear in the pace of his travel:

> "I lost no time, but down through the valley and by Stockbridge and Silvermills as hard as I could stave." [011-001]

Arriving at the village, David finds it deserted in the small hours:

> "This brought my face to Silvermills; the path came past the village with a crook, but all plainly visible; and, Highland or Lowland, there was nobody stirring." [011-003]

## The Night Watch

David's vigil at the trysting place reveals the extent of Alan's precautions. Through the long night, he watches the eastern approach from Silvermills, noting that:

> "All that time not a foot of man had come east from Silvermills, and the few that had gone west were honest countryfolk and their wives upon the road to bed." [011-004]

The quiet of the village — no soldiers, no informants — confirms that Alan's hiding place remains secure. The "honest countryfolk" trudging home to bed are the only traffic, a detail that grounds the novel's political intrigue in the ordinary rhythms of rural life.

## Significance

Silvermills represents the hidden infrastructure of Jacobite resistance in the post-1745 period. While the public drama of the Appin trial plays out in Edinburgh and Inverary, Alan Breck — a proscribed rebel — survives in the interstices of Scottish society, aided by a network of sympathisers who provide shelter, food, and intelligence. The "bit scrog of wood" by Silvermills is a small but telling detail in the larger picture of Highland resilience.
