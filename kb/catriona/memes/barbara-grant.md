# Barbara Grant

Miss Barbara Grant is the sister of Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, and one of the most quietly effective characters in Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. She operates in the shadow of her powerful brother but exercises an influence of her own — befriending both David Balfour and Catriona Drummond, and serving as one of the few bridges across the novel's bitter political divides. Her practical kindness, unaccompanied by political calculation, makes her a rare figure in the novel's legal-patriarchal world.

## Role in the Story

Barbara Grant first appears as the hostess of her brother's household, where "my sister, Miss Grant, who is so good as keep my house for me" (005-025) establishes her domestic role. But this framing is misleading: Miss Grant is far more than a housekeeper. She is Prestongrange's confidante, a shrewd observer of Edinburgh politics, and — crucially — a woman who uses her position to help rather than to scheme.

Her friendship with Catriona Drummond is one of the novel's most understated but important relationships. When Catriona is alone and vulnerable in Edinburgh, it is Miss Grant who provides practical assistance and moral support. Catriona's assessment is unambiguous: "I think Miss Barbara Grant is the best lady in the world" (023-016). In a novel where almost every character has a political agenda, Miss Grant's agenda is simply kindness — and Stevenson presents this as a form of quiet radicalism.

Miss Grant also serves as David's ally within Prestongrange's household. She provides him with information her brother would prefer he not have, and her interventions on his behalf — always discreet, never acknowledged — help shape the novel's outcome. She embodies a principle the novel's legal characters cannot grasp: that influence exercised without the appearance of power is often the most effective.

## Biography

### Background

Barbara Grant is a member of the Grant family — the sister of William Grant, Lord Prestongrange. As an unmarried woman in 18th-century Edinburgh, her social position is defined by her brother's status, but she has parlayed that position into a genuine influence that operates through friendship and information rather than legal authority.

### Key Actions

- She receives David Balfour into Prestongrange's household and immediately distinguishes herself from her brother's political machinations through her warmth and candour.

- She befriends Catriona Drummond, providing the young Highland woman with protection and companionship in an Edinburgh society that regards her as the daughter of a Jacobite criminal. Their friendship is one of the few relationships in the novel untainted by political calculation.

- She acts as an informal intelligence network, passing information between characters who cannot safely communicate directly — a role that would be impossible for a man in her position but which her gender allows her to perform without suspicion.

### Fate

Miss Grant remains in Edinburgh after the novel's events, her quiet influence continuing in the world David has left behind. She is one of the few characters whose goodness the novel does not punish — a suggestion, perhaps, that decency can survive within the machinery of power, even if it cannot change it.
