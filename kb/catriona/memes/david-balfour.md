# David Balfour

The narrator of *Catriona* (and its predecessor *Kidnapped*), David Balfour of Shaws is a young Lowland gentleman — a Whig, a Presbyterian, and a student of law — whose inheritance of the lairdship of Shaws gives him the means and the standing to involve himself in the affairs of the mighty. His moral education over the course of the novel transforms him from a naive believer in the impartiality of the law into a man who understands that justice requires not only law but courage.

## Role in the Story

David Balfour is the first-person narrator, and every event in the novel is filtered through his consciousness. He arrives in Edinburgh fresh from his adventures in *Kidnapped*, newly confirmed as laird of Shaws, "a bank porter by me carrying my gold, recommendations in my pocket" (001-001). His intention is to study law and live quietly. Instead, he is drawn into the trial of James Stewart of the Glens — first as a witness, then as an advocate, and finally as a man who risks his own freedom to speak the truth.

## Biography

### Background

David Balfour is the son of Alexander Balfour, a schoolmaster of Essendean, and the nephew of Ebenezer Balfour of Shaws — the miserly uncle who, in *Kidnapped*, conspired to have David kidnapped and sold into indentured servitude. David's adventures in *Kidnapped* — his shipwreck, his journey across the Highlands with Alan Breck Stewart, and his eventual recovery of his inheritance — have made him a young man of unusual experience and tenacity. At the opening of *Catriona*, he is perhaps twenty years old, "a landed laird" with money in his pocket and letters of introduction to the great (001-001).

### Key Actions

David's first decisive act in the novel is to meet Catriona Drummond in an Edinburgh close and, moved by her distress, to give her money for her imprisoned father. This act of spontaneous generosity commits him to the Drummond cause — and to Catriona herself — in ways he cannot foresee (001-032).

His second decisive act is to refuse Prestongrange's pressure to abandon James Stewart. David's Whig principles compel him to testify for the defence, even when it is made clear that doing so will make him enemies in high places. The consequence is his abduction to the Bass Rock — a sequestration ordered by Prestongrange but executed by Highland "cattle-thieves" (013-037).

On the Bass Rock, David endures weeks of confinement, companioned by Andie Dale and the Highland guards. His escape, his reunion with Catriona, and his eventual confrontation with Prestongrange form the novel's climactic sequence. By the end, he has not only survived but grown — he can declare to Catriona that he has "come into my kingdom" (027-009).

### Fate

By the novel's end, David has secured his inheritance, won Catriona's love, and made his peace with the powerful men who tried to destroy him. His future lies in law and land — a Whig laird in a Scotland that is slowly, painfully, being integrated into the British state.

## Historical Note

David Balfour is a fictional character, but he is drawn from the same cloth as many historical figures of the period — young men of modest gentry background who rose through the law and the professions in Enlightenment Scotland. His Whig Presbyterianism, his faith in the law, and his growing understanding of the limits of both are Stevenson's portrait of a generation.
