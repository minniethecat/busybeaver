# Clan Macgregor

Clan Macgregor — known in Gaelic as Clann Ghriogair, "the Children of the Mist" — was the most famous of the proscribed clans of the Scottish Highlands. From 1603, the name Macgregor was itself illegal: to bear it was a crime, to use it was a provocation, to be a Macgregor was to be an outlaw by definition. In *Catriona*, the Macgregors are represented by James More Macgregor (alias Drummond) and his daughter Catriona — the clan's pride, poverty, and legal precariousness embodied in two of the novel's central characters.

## Overview

The Macgregors were a Clan Alpine sept, claiming descent from the ancient kings of the Picts. Their traditional territory lay in the Braes of Balquhidder and around Loch Katrine, at the edge of the Highlands north of Glasgow. By the 17th century, they had acquired a reputation for cattle raiding and clan warfare that made them enemies of the powerful Campbells. The proscription of 1603 — following the Macgregors' defeat of the Colquhouns at Glen Fruin — made the very name Macgregor a capital offence. Surviving Macgregors adopted aliases: Murray, Drummond, Graham, or Stewart. The proscription was not fully lifted until 1774, well after the period of the novel.

## History

The Macgregors' fall from legal existence was total. In 1603, after the Battle of Glen Fruin, James VI issued an Act of Privy Council that "abolished" the name Macgregor, forbade any Macgregor to bear arms or gather in numbers greater than four, and authorized any man to kill a Macgregor with impunity. The chief, Alasdair Macgregor of Glenstrae, was captured and hanged at Edinburgh's Mercat Cross in 1604, along with thirty of his leading men.

Despite — or because of — this persecution, the Macgregors survived. They fought for Montrose in the civil wars, for the Jacobites in the risings, and for themselves in the perennial cattle-raiding economy of the Highland Line. Rob Roy Macgregor (1671–1734), James More's father, became a folk hero — a cattle thief, a Jacobite, and a man who defied the Duke of Montrose and lived to tell the tale. Sir Walter Scott's novel *Rob Roy* (1817) made him a legend; Stevenson's James More is his less famous but equally complex son.

## Role in the Novel

### James More and Catriona

James More Macgregor — using the alias James Drummond — is the most fully drawn Macgregor in the novel. He embodies the clan's contradictions: proud but impoverished, loyal to his name but willing to betray anyone to save himself, a loving father who uses his daughter as a pawn. Catriona Drummond inherits the Macgregor legacy: "In all broad Scotland there was but the one name proscribed, and that was the name of the Macgregors" (001-025). Her father's alias — Drummond — is her legal name, but her identity is Macgregor through and through.

### The Glengyle Macgregors

The Glengyle branch of the Macgregors appears as a shadowy presence in the novel. They are Catriona's "kinsfolk," and David suspects them of being employed against him during his troubles in Edinburgh (010-001). The Glengyle Macgregors represent the clan network — a web of loyalty and obligation that operates beneath and sometimes against the law.

### The Proscription as Social Fact

The proscription of the Macgregor name is not merely a historical curiosity in the novel; it is a living fact that shapes character and plot. James More's alias is a survival mechanism. Catriona's ambiguous legal status — is she a Drummond or a Macgregor? — is part of what makes her vulnerable. The proscription is the extreme case of what the post-Culloden legislation attempted for all Highland clans: the erasure of identity by law.

## Historical Note

The historical Clan Macgregor survived its proscription and exists today, with a recognized chief — Sir Malcolm Macgregor of Macgregor, 7th Baronet, 24th Chief of Clan Gregor. The proscription was a unique event in Scottish legal history: no other clan was ever outlawed by name. The Macgregors' survival under these conditions made them emblematic of Highland resilience and, in the Romantic imagination, of a certain kind of Scottish heroism — defiant, lawless, and bound by codes older than statute.
