# Appin Murder

On 14 May 1752, Colin Campbell of Glenure — the government factor on the forfeited Stewart estate of Ardshiel — was shot dead from ambush in the wood of Lettermore, in the Appin district of Argyllshire. The killing, known as the Appin Murder, was one of the most notorious crimes of 18th-century Scotland, and its legal aftermath forms the central plot of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. The murder set in motion a chain of events — false accusation, judicial corruption, clan vendetta, and the execution of an innocent man — that exposed the fault lines of post-Culloden Highland society.

## Overview

Colin Campbell of Glenure, a Campbell laird and government placeman, had been appointed factor of the Ardshiel estate after its Stewart chief was attainted for his part in the '45 Rising. The appointment was politically inflammatory: it placed a Campbell in charge of Stewart lands, collecting rents from Stewart tenants, in a district where clan loyalty was the strongest social bond. On the day of the murder, Glenure was traveling from Fort William to Duror with a small party. At the wood of Lettermore, a shot was fired from the hillside above the road. Glenure fell, mortally wounded. The killer vanished into the heather.

## Historical Background

The murder must be understood in the context of the government's post-Culloden Highland policy. The Heritable Jurisdictions Act (1747) had stripped clan chiefs of their legal powers. The forfeiture of Jacobite estates placed vast tracts of Highland land under government administration, managed by factors who were often Campbell appointees of the Duke of Argyll. To the Appin Stewarts, Glenure was not just a Campbell but a symbol of conquest — a man collecting their rents on behalf of the government that had dispossessed their chief.

The authorities immediately suspected Alan Breck Stewart, a known Jacobite and Stewart clansman who had been in Appin at the time. But Alan Breck had an alibi — he was miles away — and the actual triggerman was never conclusively identified. The government, needing a Stewart to hang, arrested James Stewart of the Glens, the acting chief of the clan in Ardshiel's absence.

## The Event in the Novel

### Depiction

Stevenson does not dramatize the murder itself. It has already occurred before *Catriona* opens, and its consequences are what the novel processes. The murder is described in flashback and legal testimony — a crime that exists primarily in its effects: the warrant for Alan Breck, the arrest of James Stewart, the summons to David Balfour as a witness, the political maneuvering in Prestongrange's chambers.

The novel's oblique treatment of the murder is itself a literary choice. By keeping the killing offstage, Stevenson makes the point that the Appin Murder was less a crime to be solved than a political event to be exploited. David Balfour, present at the scene in *Kidnapped*, is now caught between his knowledge of Alan Breck's innocence and the state's determination to secure a conviction regardless of truth.

> "It was the Duke and the great clan of Campbell. Now here is a Campbell charged with the blood of a Stewart." (004-077)

### Narrative Role

The Appin Murder is the gravitational centre of the novel's plot. Every character's actions are shaped by it:

- David's summons to Edinburgh and his resistance to Lord Prestongrange's pressure (003-002)
- His sequestration on the Bass Rock, where he is held to prevent his testimony from aiding James Stewart
- Catriona Drummond's vulnerability as the daughter of a Jacobite prisoner, whose fate is entangled with the murder's political fallout
- Alan Breck Stewart's permanent flight, culminating in the Dunkirk duel where James More MacGregor attempts to sell Alan to the British navy in exchange for his own pardon

The murder is the event that tests every character's loyalties and reveals the machinery of state power. Prestongrange's chillingly direct admission — "I need not tell you that I mean James Stewart" (004-071) — shows that the legal process is merely the instrument of a political decision already made.

### Legal Aftermath in the Novel

The trial of James Stewart occupies the middle section of *Catriona*. James's counsel, "four in number" (017-008), argue valiantly but face a stacked jury and a predetermined verdict. David's efforts to testify are thwarted by his sequestration; his frustration at being unable to help — "It's lucky for James of the Glens that I have boasted beforehand" (003-003) — gives way to despair as the inevitability of the conviction becomes clear. Simon Fraser's blunt assessment — "James is a dead man" (017-068) — dispenses with any pretence of justice.

## Aftermath

James Stewart of the Glens was tried at Inveraray in September 1752 before a jury dominated by Campbells and presided over by the Duke of Argyll, chief of Clan Campbell. He was convicted on circumstantial evidence — the prosecution argued that as acting chief, he must have ordered the killing. He was hanged at Ballachulish on 8 November 1752, and his body was left on the gibbet as a warning.

> "Poor James of the Glens was duly hanged at Lettermore." (020-046)

Alan Breck Stewart escaped to France and was never brought to trial. The actual killer was never identified.

## Historical Record

The Appin Murder has been the subject of extensive historical and literary treatment. Andro Linklater's *The Appin Murder: The Killing of the Red Fox* (2013) argued that the killing was ordered not by James Stewart but by a shadowy Jacobite network seeking to eliminate an effective government agent. Other historians have suggested the shooter was a younger Stewart clansman acting independently.

The case remains one of Scotland's most famous unsolved crimes, and Stevenson's fictional treatment in *Kidnapped* and *Catriona* has shaped popular understanding of it as deeply as any historical account. The jury composed entirely of Campbells, presided over by the Campbell Duke of Argyll, is now cited in legal textbooks as a textbook example of judicial bias. In 2008, the Scottish Criminal Cases Review Commission declined to reopen the case — but the historical consensus is clear: James Stewart was innocent, and the Appin Murder was a judicial murder in service of state policy.
