The Luckenbooths were a row of merchant booths and shops situated on Edinburgh's Royal Mile, near St. Giles' Cathedral. In the 18th century, they formed the commercial heart of the Old Town — a place where a newcomer to the city could outfit himself from head to foot. In *Catriona*, David Balfour visits the Luckenbooths on his first morning in Edinburgh, marking his transition from country lad to a young man properly equipped for city life.

## David's Outfitting

Arriving from the country with the clothes on his back and little else, David makes straight for the Luckenbooths to prepare himself for his interview with the Lord Advocate. The choice is deliberate: he wishes to look respectable without pretension:

> "At a merchant's in the Luckenbooths I had myself fitted out: none too fine, for I had no idea to appear like a beggar on horseback; but comely and responsible, so that servants should respect me." [001-003]

From the merchant he proceeds to an armourer's, also in the Luckenbooths quarter, where he acquires a plain sword — a purchase that is both practical and symbolic for a young man stepping into the dangerous world of Edinburgh politics:

> "Thence to an armourer's, where I got a plain sword, to suit with my degree in life. I felt safer with the weapon, though (for one so ignorant of defence) it might be called an added danger." [001-003]

The porter at David's lodging confirms the quality of his purchases, judging his "accoutrement to be well chosen" [001-003] — a small but telling detail that assures David (and the reader) that he has not disgraced himself through ignorance of city ways.

## Historical Significance

The Luckenbooths — literally "locked booths" — were permanent stone-fronted shops that had stood on the High Street since the 15th century. By Stevenson's time they had been demolished (c. 1817), but in the 1751 setting of *Catriona* they were still a fixture of Edinburgh life. Their inclusion in the novel anchors David's story in a real, mappable Edinburgh, giving the political intrigue that follows a solid geographical foundation. The Luckenbooths also lend their name to the "luckenbooth brooch", a traditional Scottish love token, though the novel does not reference this directly.
