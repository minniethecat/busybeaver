# Trial of James Stewart

In September 1752, at Inveraray in Argyllshire, James Stewart of the Glens was tried for his life before a jury dominated by Campbells and a bench presided over by the Duke of Argyll. The charge was accessory to the murder of Colin Campbell of Glenure. The trial was, in the judgment of contemporaries and historians alike, a political prosecution — a judicial murder dressed in the forms of law. In *Catriona*, the trial is the event around which David Balfour's moral choices are organised.

## Overview

James Stewart of the Glens — known as Seumas a' Ghlinne, "James of the Glen" — was the acting chief of the Stewarts of Appin during the exile of the attainted chief, Charles Stewart of Ardshiel. When Colin Campbell of Glenure was murdered in May 1752, the government needed a Stewart to convict. Alan Breck Stewart, the named suspect, had escaped to France. James Stewart was the most prominent Stewart the authorities could reach. He was arrested at his home in Duror and taken to Inveraray for trial.

The prosecution case was that James Stewart, as acting chief, must have ordered or known of the murder. No direct evidence linked him to the crime. The defence argued that the evidence was entirely circumstantial and that the jury's composition made a fair trial impossible. The jury of fifteen included eleven Campbells. The presiding judge, the Duke of Argyll, was chief of Clan Campbell. The verdict — guilty — was a foregone conclusion.

## Historical Background

The trial must be understood in the context of the government's post-Culloden campaign to pacify the Highlands. The Appin Stewarts were a Jacobite clan; their chief was in exile; the man who had been murdered was a government factor appointed to manage their forfeited estate. A conviction would demonstrate that the government's arm could reach even into the remote glens of Argyllshire. It would teach a lesson: Jacobite resistance, whether by arms or by assassination, would be punished.

The trial was conducted in English, a language James Stewart spoke imperfectly. Witnesses for the defence were intimidated. The jury deliberated for a matter of hours. James Stewart was sentenced to death by hanging, and his body was ordered to be hung in chains on a gibbet at Ballachulish — within sight of his home.

## The Event in the Novel

### Depiction

Stevenson does not show the trial directly. In *Catriona*, the trial is the offstage event toward which the first half of the novel moves and from which its second half recedes. David's summons, his interviews with Prestongrange, his abduction to the Bass Rock — all are organized around the trial's date. The trial itself happens while David is sequestered on the Bass, unable to testify, unable to help, hearing news of the proceedings from Andie Dale and the Highland guards.

This narrative strategy — placing the trial offstage — is thematically resonant. David, the law student who believes in the impartiality of justice, is excluded from the very proceeding that would test that belief. The trial he cannot attend becomes the trial he cannot forget.

### Narrative Role

The trial is David's moral education. Before it, he believes that the law is an instrument of justice. After it, he knows that the law is also an instrument of power. Prestongrange's efforts to suppress David's testimony — not by legal means but by sequestration and threat — reveal that the state is more interested in a conviction than in the truth. David's refusal to be silenced, his willingness to risk his freedom to speak for a condemned man, is the moral centre of the novel.

## Aftermath

James Stewart was hanged at Ballachulish on 8 November 1752. His body was left on the gibbet as a warning to the district. The execution was widely condemned, even by some Whigs, as a miscarriage of justice. The Appin Murder itself remained unsolved. In the novel, the trial's aftermath drives David's actions for the remainder of the story — his escape from the Bass, his return to Edinburgh, his confrontation with Prestongrange, and his determination to live as a man who has seen what the law can do.

## Historical Record

The Trial of James Stewart has been extensively studied as a case study in 18th-century political justice. The trial transcript was published and circulated widely, contributing to the growth of public scepticism about the impartiality of the Scottish legal system. Modern legal historians, including David M. Walker and John W. Cairns, have treated the trial as a landmark example of jury packing and prosecutorial misconduct. Stevenson's fictional treatment is partisan — he clearly believes James Stewart was innocent — but his partisanship is in the service of a larger truth about the relationship between law and power.
