# Parliament House

Parliament House, located behind St Giles' Cathedral on Edinburgh's High Street, is the seat of the Court of Session — Scotland's supreme civil court — and the physical and institutional centre of legal Edinburgh in Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. It is the space where advocates plead, where judges deliberate, and where the fate of men like James Stewart of the Glens is determined. For David Balfour, Parliament House represents both aspiration and disillusionment: the place where he hopes "to make a revolution" (017-023) and the place where he learns that law serves power, not justice.

## Overview

Built in the 1630s to house the Scottish Parliament, the building became the home of the Court of Session after the Union of 1707 abolished the Scottish legislature. By the 1750s — the period of *Catriona* — Parliament House was the epicentre of Scottish legal life. The Great Hall, where advocates and writers conducted their business, was a space of professional encounter and political manoeuvre: "a crack in the Parliament House with other lawyer bodies" (002-062) was how legal business was transacted, in conversation and consensus rather than in formal proceedings.

The building's architecture embodied the hierarchy of the legal profession. The Outer House and Inner House (courtrooms) occupied the core; the Advocates' Library provided legal resources; the surrounding chambers and offices housed the Writers (solicitors) and clerks who constituted the legal profession's working infrastructure. Charles Stewart the Writer's office, at the top of "the longest stair ever mason set a hand to" (002-001), represents the periphery of this legal world — physically elevated but socially subordinate to the advocates who pleaded in the Great Hall below.

## History

Parliament House was constructed between 1632 and 1639 as the meeting place of the Parliament of Scotland. After the Union of 1707, the building was repurposed for the Court of Session, and the Great Hall became the central gathering place for Edinburgh's legal community. The Advocates' Library (founded 1682), housed within Parliament House, was one of the finest legal libraries in Europe, and its collection later formed the nucleus of the National Library of Scotland.

## Role in the Novel

### References

Parliament House is referenced at three key moments in *Catriona*:

- David's early ambition: "a crack in the Parliament House with other lawyer bodies" (002-062) — the casual social atmosphere of legal Edinburgh
- His defiant ambition: "to make a revolution in the Parliament House" (017-023) — his fantasy of reforming the system from within
- The consequences: "remarkable consequences in the Parliament House" (017-051) — the impact of his challenge to Prestongrange

### Significance

Parliament House functions in the novel as the architectural embodiment of institutional power. It is not a neutral space: its geography (who has access to which chambers, who can speak where) maps directly onto the social hierarchy of legal Edinburgh. David's journey through these spaces — from Charles Stewart's cramped office to Prestongrange's chambers — traces his movement from the periphery of legal power toward its centre, and his ultimate rejection of that centre is a rejection of everything Parliament House represents.

The phrase "a crack in the Parliament House" is significant: it suggests that legal power is exercised not only in formal proceedings but in conversation, in the informal exchange of views and favours that constitutes the real currency of institutional life. David, as an outsider, cannot participate in this exchange — he has no favours to trade and no standing to assert. His attempt to "make a revolution" is doomed not by formal opposition but by his inability to operate within the informal networks that actually govern the institution.

## Key Members

- **The Lords of Session** — the judges of the Court, including those who preside over James Stewart's trial
- **The Faculty of Advocates** — the barristers who plead before the Court, including Lord Prestongrange
- **The Writers to the Signet** — the senior solicitors who have signing authority, including Charles Stewart
- **The clerks and functionaries** — the administrative infrastructure of the Court

## Historical Note

The historical Parliament House remains the home of the Court of Session and the Supreme Courts of Scotland to this day. The Great Hall, with its hammerbeam roof and stained glass, is one of the finest surviving 17th-century interiors in Scotland. The Advocates' Library, designed by William Playfair in the 19th century, replaced the original library but preserves its institutional continuity.

Stevenson's portrayal of Parliament House is notably accurate. The "longest stair" leading to Charles Stewart's office is authentic to the building's vertical architecture — Writers to the Signet traditionally occupied the upper floors — and the social dynamics of "a crack in the Parliament House" remain recognisable to any modern observer of legal institutions. The building's dual identity — former parliament, current court — mirrors the novel's theme of political power disguised as legal authority.
