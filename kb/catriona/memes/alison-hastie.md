# Alison Hastie

**Alison Hastie** is a young woman from the village of Lime Kilns on the Firth of Forth, who assists Alan Breck Stewart and David Balfour in crossing the river in the novel _Kidnapped_. Though she appears only briefly, her kindness leaves a lasting mark on David's conscience, and she reappears in the epilogue of _Catriona_ in a surprising and touching role.

## The Forth Crossing

Alison Hastie first enters David Balfour's story during the events of _Kidnapped_, when she helps Alan and David cross the Firth of Forth during their flight after the Appin murder. The crossing is a critical moment in their escape, and Alison's assistance — given freely and at some personal risk — is essential to their survival.

David carries the memory of this kindness with him, and it weighs on his conscience in _Catriona_. When he finds himself in possession of money, he thinks immediately of repaying the debt:

> "Then there's the lass Alison Hastie, in Lime Kilns. Her that helped Alan and me across the Forth. I was thinking if I could get her a good Sunday gown, such as she could wear with decency in her degree, it would be an ease to my conscience; for the mere truth is, we owe her our two lives."[^1]

## Visit with Miss Grant

Later in the novel, David arranges for Miss Grant (Barbara Grant, the Lord Advocate's daughter) to meet Alison. Miss Grant, having been "taken with my account of Alison Hastie," desires "to see the lass herself."[^2] The two women visit Alison at Lime Kilns, where they find her "once more alone — indeed, I believe her father wrought all day in the fields." Alison "curtsied dutifully to the gentry-folk and the beautiful young lady in the riding-coat."[^2]

This visit serves as a quiet acknowledgment of the bond between the different social classes that the novel explores — the working-class Alison, the gentleman David, and the aristocratic Miss Grant.

## Epilogue: The Balfour Nursemaid

Alison's story comes full circle in the novel's epilogue. David reveals that Alison Hastie later became the nursemaid to his and Catriona's children at Shaws:

> "Alison Hastie in Limekilns was the lass that rocked your cradle when you were too small to know of it, and walked abroad with you in the policy when you were bigger."[^3]

This detail binds the novels' events together across time — the young woman who saved David's life becomes the trusted caretaker of his children, a quiet testament to the enduring value of simple, uncalculated kindness.

[^2]: Catriona, ch-29: "This was her own thought, for she had been taken with my account of Alison Hastie, and desired to see the lass herself."

[^3]: Catriona, ch-29 (Epilogue): "Alison Hastie in Limekilns was the lass that rocked your cradle when you were too small to know of it."
