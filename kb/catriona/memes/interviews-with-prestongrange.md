The Interviews with Prestongrange are a sequence of confrontations between David Balfour
and the Lord Advocate of Scotland, Lord Prestongrange, occupying much of the novel's
early Edinburgh section (chapters 4–8). In these meetings, Prestongrange attempts to
persuade, manipulate, and ultimately coerce David into cooperating with the Crown's
prosecution of James Stewart for the Appin Murder. David's resistance — polite but
immovable — sets the novel's central moral conflict in motion.

## Overview

David arrives at Prestongrange's house bearing a letter of introduction and the
conviction that the truth about the Appin Murder will be sufficient to secure
justice. Prestongrange receives him cordially, and what follows is a masterclass
in political manipulation: the Lord Advocate never directly threatens David,
never issues an ultimatum, and never admits that the Crown's case against James
Stewart is anything less than righteous. Instead, he offers David a place within
the machinery of state — a career, protection, advancement — in exchange for
cooperation.

David refuses. The interviews become a test of character. David's "I cannot see
beyond, my lord. It's the way that I am made" (004-080) is Stevenson's statement
of the novel's moral position: that some people cannot be bought, and that their
inability to see "beyond" is not a limitation but a virtue.

## Historical Background

The Lord Advocate of Scotland in the 1750s was the Crown's principal legal officer,
responsible for prosecuting crimes and representing the government's interests in
Scottish courts. The office combined prosecutorial authority with political
influence — the Lord Advocate sat in parliament and was expected to manage the
government's Scottish business, including the suppression of Jacobite sentiment.

Prestongrange's treatment of David reflects the historical reality of how the
Crown managed witnesses in politically sensitive cases. There was no formal witness
protection programme in the 18th century; instead, witnesses were managed through
a combination of patronage, intimidation, and, when necessary, sequestration.

## The Event in the Novel

### Depiction

The interviews span several days and occur at Prestongrange's house in Edinburgh.
The first interview (chapter 4) is the most extended and significant: David
presents his case, and Prestongrange listens, questions, and begins the process
of turning David's testimony to the Crown's advantage. The interview is conducted
with elaborate politeness — "Under your favour, my lord" (004-012) — that masks
the fundamental antagonism between the two men's purposes.

Subsequent interviews (chapters 5–8) escalate the pressure. Prestongrange reveals
that David's visit to Charles Stewart has been discovered (005-042), that a
warrant for David's arrest exists (006-024), and that men in prison are prepared
to testify against him (006-038). Each revelation is presented not as a threat but
as a statement of fact — the Lord Advocate is merely informing David of the
situation, with the implication that cooperation would make all these
difficulties disappear.

Simon Fraser of Lovat is present at the dinner table (006-003, 008-060), serving
as a living example of what cooperation with the Crown looks like: a former
Jacobite reborn as a government man. His presence is the unspoken offer —
survive, prosper, join us — that Prestongrange's words never quite articulate.

### Narrative Role

The interviews are the novel's moral fulcrum. Before them, David believes in the
justice of the law. During them, he discovers that the law serves power. After
them, he must decide what to do with this knowledge. His refusal to cooperate —
polite, stubborn, principled — establishes him as a character who will not be
broken by the system, even as the system proves impossible to defeat.

Stevenson structures the interviews as a gradual revelation: David (and the
reader) learns that the Crown's case against James Stewart is not about truth
but about political necessity. The Appin Murder must be punished, and James
Stewart is the designated scapegoat. David's testimony could complicate this
arrangement, but Prestongrange's skill lies in making David feel that his
resistance is not heroic but merely naive.

## Aftermath

The interviews' immediate consequence is David's sequestration on the Bass
Rock — Prestongrange's elegant solution to the problem of an inconvenient
witness. The longer consequence is David's moral education: he enters
Prestongrange's house as a young man who believes in law and leaves it as a
man who understands power.

The interviews also shape David's relationship with Catriona. His knowledge
of her father's imprisonment and his inability to speak of it — Prestongrange
has placed him under an implicit obligation of silence — creates the tension
that drives their subsequent interactions. He knows things he cannot tell her,
and this knowledge becomes the burden that the novel must resolve.
