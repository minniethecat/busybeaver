Lime Kilns (also spelled Limekilns) is a coastal village in Fife, on the northern shore of the Firth of Forth. In *Catriona*, it is the home of Alison Hastie, the young woman who assisted Alan Breck Stewart and David Balfour in crossing the Forth during their flight after the Appin murder — an event that occurred in the earlier novel *Kidnapped* but is frequently referenced in *Catriona*.

## Alison Hastie and the Forth Crossing

During David's conversation with Catriona and the Advocate's ladies, he recalls Alison with warmth and suggests she as a suitable travelling companion for Catriona:

> "'Then there's the lass Alison Hastie, in Lime Kilns,' said I. 'Her that helped Alan and me across the Forth.'" [002-031]

The reference is significant: Alison's assistance to David and Alan — two fugitives sought in connection with the Appin murder — demonstrates the quiet network of ordinary Scots who aided the Jacobite cause, not out of political conviction but out of personal loyalty. Catriona, hearing of Alison, is immediately drawn to her:

> "This was her own thought, for she had been taken with my account of Alison Hastie, and desired to see the lass herself." [020-009]

## A Deeper Connection

Later in the novel, a remarkable connection emerges. Bohaldie, the exiled Jacobite leader in France, reveals that Alison Hastie was in fact David's childhood nurse:

> "Alison Hastie in Limekilns was the lass that rocked your cradle when you were too small to know of it, and walked abroad with you in the policy when you were bigger." [031-011]

This belated recognition transforms Lime Kilns from a mere waypoint on David's flight into a place of profound personal significance — the home of the woman who nursed him as an infant, and who, years later and unknowingly, helped save his life.

## Significance

Lime Kilns represents the hidden threads of kinship and obligation that run beneath the surface of Scottish society in *Catriona*. The village itself is unremarkable — a small Fife settlement of lime-workers and fisherfolk — but through Alison Hastie it becomes a node in the novel's web of loyalty, memory, and unexpected reunion. The coincidence that David's childhood nurse should be the very woman who helped him cross the Forth is a characteristically Stevenson touch: Providence working through ordinary people and ordinary places.
