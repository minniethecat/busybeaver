Balwhidder is a district in the southern Highlands of Perthshire, Scotland, situated in the glen of the River Balvaig. In *Catriona*, it is mentioned as the place from which David Balfour has recently travelled, and it becomes a recurring touchstone in his conversations — a symbol of his Highland origins and the life he has left behind.

## David's Journey

When David first meets Catriona Macgregor Drummond on the road to Edinburgh, he tells her that he has come from the Highlands:

> "It is not yet a week since I passed the line," said I. "Less than a week ago I was on the braes of Balwhidder." [001-017]

The "line" David refers to is the Highland Line — the cultural and linguistic boundary between the Gaelic-speaking Highlands and the Scots-speaking Lowlands. To have "passed the line" only a week before is to be freshly arrived from a different world, and David's mention of "the braes of Balwhidder" marks him as a Highlander in Lowland dress.

## A Token of Identity

Balwhidder becomes a kind of private currency between David and Catriona. When they exchange names, David asks her to remember his for the sake of the place:

> "I wish you would keep my name in mind for the sake of Balwhidder," said I, "and I will yours for the sake of my lucky day." [001-023]

The "lucky day" is the day of their meeting, and Balwhidder stands in for David's identity — his home, his people, the landscape that shaped him. Later, Catriona recalls this exchange when speaking of David to her kinswoman:

> "I thought ye had just been a cuif — you and your saxpence, and your lucky day and your sake of Balwhidder" [007-017]

The "saxpence" (sixpence) is the coin that brought David good fortune — a silver piece given to him that he kept as a talisman. The coin, like Balwhidder, is a token of his past.

## Hospitality and Home

In an early exchange with Catriona, David invokes the hospitality of Balwhidder — a Highland virtue — to establish common ground:

> "And remember I have had the hospitality of your own country of Balwhidder." [001-036]

And in conversation with Catriona's hostess, David traces the lucky sixpence back to its origin:

> "That sixpence came with me by Balwhidder," said I. [001-042]

Both references use Balwhidder as shorthand for a set of Highland values — hospitality, loyalty, the significance of small tokens — that David carries with him into the political and legal intrigues of Edinburgh.

## Significance

Balwhidder functions in the novel less as a physical place than as a symbolic one. David never returns there in the course of the narrative; but it remains the point of origin for his character — the place from which he "passed the line" into a larger, more dangerous world. Its recurrence as a touchstone in conversation suggests that David, for all his adaption to Lowland life, has not forgotten where he came from.
