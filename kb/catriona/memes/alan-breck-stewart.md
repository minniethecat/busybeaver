# Alan Breck Stewart

Alan Breck Stewart is a Highland Jacobite soldier, fugitive, and one of the two co-protagonists of Robert Louis Stevenson's novel *Catriona* (1893). A historical figure transformed by Stevenson into a swashbuckling romantic hero, Alan is David Balfour's loyal friend and protector — quick with a sword, quicker with a quip, and perpetually one step ahead of the King's men. His presence drives much of the novel's tension, as his status as a wanted man suspected of the Appin Murder places everyone who shelters him in mortal danger.

## Role in the Story

Alan Breck functions as both the catalyst of David Balfour's involvement in the Appin affair and the emotional counterweight to Catriona Drummond in the novel's central relationships. When David arrives in Edinburgh seeking justice for James Stewart of the Glens, it is Alan's plight — "Alan Breck is innocent, and so is James" (002-019) — that gives David his mission. Alan's role in *Catriona* is structurally more complex than in *Kidnapped*: he appears less frequently but each appearance is decisive, culminating in the climactic duel with James More MacGregor at Dunkirk.

Stevenson positions Alan as the embodiment of Highland honour — a man who lives by a code of loyalty that the legal machinery of Edinburgh can neither comprehend nor accommodate. His absence for much of the novel's middle section creates a narrative vacuum that Catriona fills, making the convergence of these two relationships at Dunkirk the novel's emotional climax.

## Biography

### Background

Alan Breck Stewart was a historical figure, a former soldier in the French service who had fought at the Battle of Prestonpans and was subsequently a fugitive after the Jacobite defeat at Culloden. In Stevenson's fiction, he is the son of a Highland gentleman, "Ardshiel's Alan" — connected to the Stewart clan hierarchy and steeped in the codes of Highland loyalty and honour. After Culloden, he lived as an outlaw in the heather, the life Stevenson depicted in the earlier novel *Kidnapped*.

### Key Actions

- Alan sends David to Charles Stewart the Writer in Edinburgh with his silver button as a token, setting the entire legal plot in motion (002-009). The clerk's startled recognition — "Gosh, that'll no be Alan Breck!" (002-073) — establishes Alan's fearsome reputation before he even appears.

- He survives as a fugitive in the Highlands while David attempts to secure justice through legal channels in Edinburgh. His hiding in "a piece of a wood" (004-061) near the city underscores the physical proximity of danger.

- Alan maintains communication with David through intermediaries and, when the legal case collapses, proposes escape to France — "ready for another bit run with Alan Breck" (012-080). His plan to study at the University of Leyden shows a pragmatic, forward-looking side beneath the romantic adventurer.

- He travels to Dunkirk to meet David and Catriona, immediately sizing up the situation with James More. His appearance transforms the dynamic: Catriona greets him with warmth — "And you will be his friend, Alan Breck?" (029-034) — and James More recognizes a dangerous adversary.

- In the climactic confrontation at Bazin's inn, Alan exposes James More's treachery (colluding with the British navy to capture Alan in exchange for his own pardon) and kills him in a duel (030-103). He then secures Catriona and David's future with characteristic bravado: "this lady daughter of yours is to marry my friend Davie" (030-115).

### Fate

After killing James More, Alan flees Dunkirk with David and Catriona, one step ahead of the British authorities. His ultimate fate is left open — the historical Alan Breck Stewart disappears from records after the 1750s, and Stevenson respects this ambiguity, allowing him to vanish back into the mists of legend from which he came. "Alan Breck'll be a wee thing nearer to the gallows than he's caring for" (030-125) are fitting last words — a man whose life has been one long escape, still running.

## Relationships

- **[[david-balfour]]** — best friend and sworn companion. Alan is David's protector, mentor in Highland ways, and fellow fugitive. Their bond, forged in *Kidnapped*, is tested and deepened in *Catriona*.
- **[[james-stewart-of-the-glens]]** — fellow Stewart and fellow fugitive. Alan believes in James's innocence and considers it a matter of clan loyalty to help him.
- **[[catriona-drummond]]** — the woman David loves; Alan immediately recognizes her quality and becomes her champion, orchestrating the resolution that allows her and David to marry.
- **[[james-more-macgregor]]** — antagonist. Alan sees through James More's duplicity from the start (030-005) and ultimately kills him in a duel.
- **[[charles-stewart-the-writer]]** — clan connection in Edinburgh; Alan's silver button is the token of recognition that opens the legal door.

## Historical Note

The historical Alan Breck Stewart (c. 1711–1791) was a Scottish soldier and Jacobite who served in the French army and fought at Prestonpans. He was suspected of involvement in the Appin Murder of 1752 — the historical event that forms the backdrop of both *Kidnapped* and *Catriona*. Unlike the fictional Alan, who is characterised by charm and honour, the historical record is more ambiguous: Alan was certainly a professional soldier of fortune, and his involvement in the murder remains disputed by historians.

Stevenson substantially romanticised Alan. The historical figure was likely more pragmatic and less gallant than the novel's version, but the core elements — Highland loyalty, military skill, and perpetual flight from British authority — are authentic. Stevenson's genius was in fusing these historical facts with the literary archetype of the dashing Highland rogue, creating one of the most memorable characters in Scottish fiction. The fictional Alan's speech patterns (a mix of formal English and Scots) are Stevenson's invention, designed to convey both his education and his Highland identity.
