# Lord Prestongrange

William Grant, Lord Prestongrange (c. 1701–1764), was the Lord Advocate of Scotland — the Crown's chief law officer — during the period of *Catriona*. In the novel, he is the most politically complex figure: a "Highlander civilised" who understands clan loyalties from within, yet prosecutes in the name of Hanoverian law; a man who threatens David Balfour with ruin, yet also protects him from worse fates.

## Role in the Story

Prestongrange is the pivot on which the novel's legal and political action turns. He receives David Balfour as a witness in the Appin Murder case, but his interest in David is never purely judicial. He wishes to manage the young laird — to prevent him from testifying on behalf of James Stewart, to keep him away from the Jacobite underground, and, when management fails, to have him quietly "sequestered" on the Bass Rock. Yet he never acts with simple malice. His motives are a mixture of political calculation, professional duty, and a genuine, if paternalistic, concern for David's welfare.

## Biography

### Background

The historical William Grant was born around 1701, the second son of Sir Francis Grant of Cullen. He was admitted to the Faculty of Advocates in 1722, served as Solicitor General from 1737 to 1742, and was appointed Lord Advocate in 1746 — the year of Culloden. He held the office until 1754, when he was raised to the bench as Lord Prestongrange. He died in 1764. His tenure as Lord Advocate coincided with the government's campaign to pacify the Highlands, and the prosecution of James Stewart of the Glens was one of the most politically charged cases of his career.

### Key Actions

Prestongrange first receives David in his chambers at the Advocate's office, where he presents himself as a worldly, even sympathetic, figure. "But you are in the bit at last, for I am Prestongrange," he says, when David realizes who he has been speaking with (004-007). He tries to persuade David not to testify for James Stewart, offering the implicit threat of state power and the explicit lure of patronage.

When David refuses to be managed, Prestongrange arranges his abduction to the Bass Rock — a "sequestration" designed to keep him out of the way until the trial concludes. Yet he also ensures that David is treated as a gentleman rather than a common prisoner. His warning about Highland unrest is prophetic: "I give you my word we may have a '45 again with the Campbells on the other side" (004-079).

### Fate

The historical Prestongrange was elevated to the bench in 1754 and died a decade later, respected but not beloved. Stevenson's portrait captures the moral ambiguity of a man who serves the law while understanding its limits — a "Highlander civilised" who has made his peace with Hanoverian power but never forgets what that peace cost.

## Historical Note

Stevenson's Prestongrange is a reasonably faithful portrait of the historical William Grant. The real Prestongrange did prosecute James Stewart for the Appin Murder in 1752, in a trial that was widely regarded as a judicial murder — the jury was packed with Campbells, the evidence was thin, and the presiding judges (including the Duke of Argyll as justice-general) had clear political motives. Stevenson's genius is to make Prestongrange neither a villain nor a hero, but a man of his time and office, doing necessary work with a troubled conscience.
