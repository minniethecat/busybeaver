# Appin

A coastal district in Argyllshire, in the West Highlands of Scotland, lying between Loch Linnhe and Loch Leven. In the 1750s, Appin was Stewart country — the territory of the Stewarts of Appin, a Jacobite clan whose chief, Charles Stewart of Ardshiel, had been attainted for his part in the '45 Rising. It was here, in the wood of Lettermore, that Colin Campbell of Glenure was shot dead on 14 May 1752 — the Appin Murder, the event that sets the plot of *Catriona* in motion.

## Location

Appin lies on the western seaboard of Scotland, south of Fort William and north of Oban, facing the islands of Lismore and Mull across Loch Linnhe. In the 18th century, it was accessible only by rough tracks and by sea — a remote Highland district of scattered townships, hill farms, and clan strongholds. The Stewart chiefs held their seat at Ardshiel House, and the clan's territory extended across the braes and glens of the district. The isolation of Appin was part of its character, and part of the difficulty the Hanoverian government faced in imposing its authority there.

## Role in the Story

### The Appin Murder

On 14 May 1752, Colin Campbell of Glenure — the government factor appointed to manage the forfeited Stewart estate of Ardshiel — was shot from ambush in the wood of Lettermore, on his way from Fort William to Duror. The killing was immediately attributed to a Stewart clansman, Alan Breck Stewart, and the authorities embarked on a manhunt. James Stewart of the Glens, the acting chief of the Appin Stewarts (Ardshiel being in exile in France), was arrested, tried at Inveraray before a Campbell-packed jury, and hanged at Ballachulish.

### Appin as Political Landscape

In the novel, Appin is more than a crime scene. It is the symbolic heart of Highland Jacobite identity — a place where clan loyalty, resistance to Hanoverian rule, and the memory of the '45 Rising converge. David Balfour, the Lowland Whig, has crossed through Appin with Alan Breck in *Kidnapped*; in *Catriona*, the consequences of what happened there pursue him into the law courts of Edinburgh. The district's name recurs throughout the novel as a shorthand for everything the government fears and everything David is caught between.

### The Trial's Shadow

The trial of James Stewart of the Glens casts a long shadow over Appin itself. In David's narration, the district becomes a place of moral reckoning — where the "Campbell charged with the blood of a Stewart" meets the "Stewart charged with the blood of a Campbell" (004-077), in a cycle of vengeance that the law is powerless to break.

## Description

Stevenson's Appin is a place of "rain and heather and the calling of the muircocks," a landscape of mountain and sea-loch where the old clan world is dying but not yet dead. It is described most fully not in *Catriona* itself but in *Kidnapped*, where David and Alan cross Rannoch Moor and descend into Appin. The district's beauty — "a country of rocks and heather and birches" — is inseparable from its violence.

## Historical Context

The Appin Murder remains, in some respects, an unsolved crime. Alan Breck Stewart was almost certainly not the trigger-man (he was elsewhere that day), but the authorities needed a Stewart to hang, and James Stewart of the Glens was the Stewart they could get. The trial at Inveraray was a travesty: the jury of fifteen included eleven Campbells, the presiding judge was the Duke of Argyll (chief of Clan Campbell), and the evidence was wholly circumstantial. The execution of James Stewart was widely regarded, even at the time, as judicial murder.
