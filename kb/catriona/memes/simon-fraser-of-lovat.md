Simon Fraser, Master of Lovat, is a historical figure who appears in *Catriona* as a
Jacobite turncoat working alongside the Lord Advocate Prestongrange. As the son of the
executed Jacobite peer Simon Fraser, 11th Lord Lovat, he embodies the moral corrosion
of the post-1745 settlement, in which former rebels were forced to collaborate with
Hanoverian justice.

## Role in the Story

Simon Fraser functions as the novel's most intimate portrait of political corruption.
He is introduced by Prestongrange as "Mr. Simon Fraser, whom we used to call by another
name" (006-003) — a reference to his Jacobite past and subsequent rehabilitation. As
a former rebel turned Crown collaborator, Fraser represents the price of survival in
Hanoverian Scotland: the moral cost of switching sides.

Fraser's role in the story is both practical and symbolic. Practically, he is
Prestongrange's go-between with the Jacobite underground, using his Highland
connections to manage witnesses and suppress evidence favorable to James Stewart's
defense. Symbolically, he is the dark mirror of David Balfour — both are young men
caught between two worlds, but Fraser has chosen complicity where David chooses
resistance to injustice.

## Biography

### Background

The historical Simon Fraser (1726–1782) was the eldest son of Simon Fraser, 11th
Lord Lovat — the notorious Jacobite peer executed for treason after the 1745 Rising.
The younger Fraser fought on the government side at Culloden, a decision that saved
his life and allowed him to eventually regain the family estate and title. At the
time of *Catriona* (1751–52), he was a young man in his mid-twenties, working to
rehabilitate his family name through service to the Crown.

### Key Actions

Fraser first appears at Prestongrange's table, where David is introduced to him as a
guest of the Lord Advocate (006-003). Prestongrange's words — "whom we used to call
by another name" — hint at Fraser's Jacobite past even as he sits among Crown
officials.

David quickly forms a visceral dislike of Fraser. Catriona warns David that Fraser
is dangerous: "O, Simon Fraser! to think upon that man! For all the world and the
promises of life or ease, I would not put myself in his hands" (007-074). Fraser
dines at Prestongrange's table alongside the Sheriff of Perth, positioned as a
trusted government asset despite his rebel ancestry (008-060).

When David discovers Prestongrange's duplicity — suppressing exculpatory witnesses
while paying lip-service to justice — Fraser's presence at these councils marks him
as complicit in the conspiracy. James More Macgregor acknowledges Fraser as "my ain
kind" (011-017), confirming that Fraser's loyalties remain entangled with the
Jacobite network even as he serves the Crown.

As the trial approaches, the prosecution considers approaching Fraser for testimony
(017-019), revealing that his utility to the state extends to serving as a witness
against his former allies.

### Fate

Fraser's post-novel fate reflects his moral trajectory: the historical Simon Fraser
successfully petitioned for the restoration of the Lovat title in 1757, raised a
regiment for the Crown during the Seven Years' War, and died a respected member of
the British establishment in 1782. His survival and rehabilitation stand in stark
contrast to James Stewart's execution — the difference between betraying one's
principles and dying for them.

## Historical Note

Simon Fraser, Master of Lovat (1726–1782), was a complex historical figure whose
career exemplified the ambiguities of post-Jacobite Scotland. His father, the 11th
Lord Lovat, was beheaded on Tower Hill in 1747 — the last man executed by
beheading in Britain. Despite this Jacobite pedigree, the younger Fraser fought
for the government at Culloden and spent the following decades working to regain
the family's forfeited estate and title.

Stevenson's portrayal draws on this historical reality to create a character who
embodies the moral compromises of the era. The novel's Fraser is not a
caricatured villain but a man who has made the calculated choice to survive —
and the calculation has stained him. David's instinctive revulsion reflects
Stevenson's own judgment on the "rehabilitated" Jacobite turncoats who
prospered under the Hanoverian settlement while men of principle suffered.

In 1757 — five years after the events of *Catriona* — the historical Fraser
successfully regained the Lovat title, raised the 78th Fraser Highlanders for
Crown service in North America, and ended his career as a major-general and
member of Parliament. His rehabilitation was complete; the moral question
Stevenson poses is whether such rehabilitation was just.
