Testimony and evidence in 18th-century Scots criminal procedure are the legal mechanisms
at the heart of Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. The novel's plot turns on who will
testify, whose testimony will be heard, and whose will be suppressed — making the rules
of evidence not merely a background institution but the engine of the narrative.

## In 18th-Century Scots Law

Criminal trials in 18th-century Scotland relied heavily on witness testimony. Unlike
modern systems with elaborate rules of evidence and professional police investigation,
the Scottish courts of Stevenson's era depended on the statements of witnesses who
could speak to facts within their knowledge. The Lord Advocate, as public prosecutor,
exercised broad discretion over which witnesses to call and which evidence to present.

This discretion created the conditions for the political manipulation that Stevenson
depicts. Prestongrange's power over the Appin Murder case derives not from his ability
to fabricate evidence but from his ability to control which evidence reaches the
court. He does not need to lie; he needs only to decide whose testimony is "received."

## In the Novel

Testimony is the novel's central plot mechanism. David Balfour arrives in Edinburgh
believing that his testimony — his truthful account of events at Appin — will be
sufficient to secure justice. The novel's action consists of the systematic
dismantling of this belief.

Prestongrange first attempts to persuade David not to testify: "I do not want your
testimony" (004-075). When David insists, the Lord Advocate shifts to management
rather than refusal — David's testimony will be "received" (008-004), but on terms
that render it harmless to the prosecution's case. The distinction between having
one's testimony heard and having it matter is one that David learns through
bitter experience.

The novel's legal climax is not the trial itself (which occurs offstage) but the
struggle over who will testify and how. Prestongrange suppresses exculpatory
witnesses, sequesters David on the Bass Rock, and considers approaching Simon
Fraser for testimony that "would prove wings" to the prosecution's case
(018-015). Each of these actions is legal — the rules of evidence in
18th-century Scotland gave the Lord Advocate these powers. Stevenson's critique
is not that the law is violated but that the law is the instrument of violation.

## The Moral of Evidence

The novel's treatment of testimony has a larger moral dimension. David's faith
in testimony — speak the truth and justice will follow — is a faith in
institutions. His education consists of learning that institutions serve power,
not truth. The lesson is bitter but not cynical: David does not conclude that
truth is worthless, only that it requires more than mere utterance to prevail.

This theme connects *Catriona* to Stevenson's broader concerns with justice
and narrative. In *Kidnapped*, David's testimony is sufficient because the
novel's resolution is romantic. In *Catriona*, testimony is insufficient
because the novel's resolution is political. The difference between the two
novels is the difference between believing in justice and understanding how
justice is administered.
