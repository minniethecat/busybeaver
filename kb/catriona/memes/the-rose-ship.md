# The Rose (ship)

**_The Rose_** is a merchant vessel captained by Captain Sang, which carries David Balfour and Catriona Drummond from Leith, Scotland, to the Netherlands. The ship's voyage across the North Sea provides the setting for some of the most emotionally significant passages in the novel _Catriona_.

## The Voyage

The _Rose_ sails from Leith bound for the port of Rotterdam. On board are David and Catriona, along with six other passengers: "three solid merchants out of Leith, Kirkcaldy, and Dundee, all engaged in the same adventure into High Germany," a returning Dutchman, and two merchants' wives.[^1]

The crossing is notable for its good fortune in weather:

> "The weather being singularly fine for that season, with bright frosty days and nights, a steady, gentle wind, and scarce a sheet started all the way through the North Sea, we sat there (only now and again walking to and fro for warmth) from the first blink of the sun till eight or nine at night under the clear stars."[^1]

## David and Catriona's Relationship at Sea

The _Rose_'s voyage is the setting for the first extended period of intimacy between David and Catriona. The shipboard conditions bring them together in a way that life on land had not permitted. A fortunate circumstance — Mrs. Gebbie, the woman charged with Catriona's care, is severely seasick and confined to her cabin — leaves David and Catriona effectively alone together:

> "We were besides the only creatures at all young on board the _Rose_, except a white-faced boy that did my old duty to attend upon the table; and it came about that Catriona and I were left almost entirely to ourselves."[^1]

David describes attending on Catriona with "extraordinary pleasure," making her a soft place on deck with his cloak, and spending the days in conversation under the stars. The merchants and Captain Sang "would sometimes glance and smile upon us, or pass a merry word or two and give us the go-by again," but largely left the young couple to their own concerns.[^1]

## Arrival and Complications

On arrival off the Dutch coast, a complication arises. The _Rose_ is bound for Rotterdam, where the other passengers have a connecting conveyance to Upper Germany. Captain Sang had agreed to call at Helvoetsluys to put Catriona ashore, but both the captain and the local boatman are reluctant to risk a shore boat in the weather conditions. After a tense negotiation, Catriona and David are put ashore in a small boat, while the _Rose_ proceeds immediately to Rotterdam.[^2]

The _Rose_ reappears later when David and Catriona, lost in Rotterdam, encounter Captain Sang by chance. He informs them that the ship had made "the most incredible brief time" with a strong following wind, and all passengers had already departed for Germany.[^3] Captain Sang proves friendly and helpful, arranging lodgings for Catriona and promising to carry her back to Leith free of charge when the _Rose_ is next loaded.

## Captain Sang

Captain Sang, the master of the _Rose_, is portrayed as a rough but fundamentally decent man. His one flaw — a tendency to heavy drinking — is exposed when he becomes "unutterably tipsy" on Rhenish wine and behaves "scandalous to the young lady, jesting most ill-favouredly at the figure she had made on the ship's rail," forcing David to abruptly remove Catriona from his company.[^3]
