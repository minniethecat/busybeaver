# James More Macgregor

Catriona's father — a Macgregor chieftain, a veteran of the Jacobite cause, and a man whose charm, unreliability, and survival instinct make him one of Stevenson's most complex characters. Known also as James Drummond (the alias he uses to evade the proscription of the Macgregor name), James More alternately protects and endangers his daughter, befriends and betrays David Balfour, and serves and defies the Hanoverian authorities.

## Role in the Story

James More Macgregor is the catalyst for David Balfour's entanglement with the Drummonds and, by extension, with the Appin Murder case. It is his daughter Catriona whom David meets in the Edinburgh close, weeping because she cannot afford snuff for her imprisoned father (001-041). James More's imprisonment, his shifting testimony, and his eventual escape to France frame the novel's central action. He is at once a loving father, a reckless adventurer, and a man whose word cannot be trusted — a figure who embodies the contradictions of the post-1745 Highland world.

## Biography

### Background

James More is a Macgregor — a member of the most notorious of the proscribed clans. "In all broad Scotland there was but the one name proscribed, and that was the name of the Macgregors" (001-025). The name was outlawed after 1603, and Macgregors were forced to adopt aliases. James More uses the name Drummond, though his Highland identity is never in doubt. He fought in the Jacobite cause, possibly at Culloden, and has spent years as a soldier of fortune and political prisoner.

### Key Actions

James More first appears as a prisoner in Edinburgh Castle, brought daily to the Advocate's chambers for examination about the Appin Murder (001-039). He maneuvers between the authorities and the Jacobite underground, offering to testify — or to withhold testimony — depending on what serves his interests. David Balfour meets him in Prestongrange's antechamber, where James More alternately threatens and flatters the young laird (005-002, 005-010).

His most consequential act is his escape to France, arranged with the tacit permission of Prestongrange, who wants a troublesome witness removed. James More takes passage on a ship and vanishes from the British Isles, leaving Catriona behind — but not before extracting promises from David to protect her. His departure is both a betrayal and, in the logic of clan survival, a necessity.

### Fate

By the novel's end, James More is in France, where he continues his career as a soldier and intriguer. The historical James More Macgregor (or Drummond) was a real figure — Rob Roy Macgregor's son — and his exploits after the '45 were the stuff of Highland legend. Stevenson takes liberties with the chronology but preserves the essential character: a man of courage, cunning, and unreliable loyalties.

## Historical Note

The historical James Mor (or James More) Macgregor was the son of the famous Rob Roy Macgregor. Like his father, he was a Jacobite and a man of the proscribed name, living by his wits and his sword. Stevenson's portrayal is broadly consistent with the historical record: a man of charm, physical courage, and flexible morality, navigating a world where his clan identity made him an outlaw. Stevenson compresses the timeline — the real James More was older at the time of the Appin Murder than the novel suggests — but the psychological portrait is acute.
