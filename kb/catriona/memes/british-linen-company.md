# British Linen Company

The British Linen Company is the Edinburgh commercial institution where David Balfour begins his career — "I, David Balfour, came forth of the British Linen Company" (001-001) — and the anchor of his identity as a respectable Lowland Scot in Robert Louis Stevenson's *Catriona*. Founded to promote the Scottish linen industry, the Company had by the 1750s evolved into a financial institution, and its office on the High Street represents the new commercial Scotland that is steadily displacing the old clan-based social order.

## Overview

David Balfour's employment at the British Linen Company is established in the novel's opening sentence — the very first fact we learn about him after his name. This is deliberate: David is being presented as a man of commerce and respectability, the antithesis of the Highland adventurer Alan Breck Stewart. The Company provides David with a salary, a social position, and (crucially) the financial independence that allows him to resist Lord Prestongrange's pressure — "an acknowledgment from the British Linen Company for a considerable sum" (014-024) arrives while he is sequestered on the Bass Rock, demonstrating that commercial credit operates independently of political favour.

## History

The British Linen Company was established by royal charter in 1746, originally to promote the manufacture of linen in Scotland. By the 1750s (the period of *Catriona*), it had effectively become a bank, issuing notes and providing financial services to the Scottish commercial classes. Its evolution from industrial promoter to financial institution mirrors the broader transformation of the Scottish economy in the decades after the Union of 1707.

Stevenson's choice of the British Linen Company as David's employer is historically precise. The Company was one of the most respected commercial institutions in Edinburgh, and its employees represented the new professional class — men whose status derived from competence and capital rather than land and lineage. David, the son of a modest schoolmaster, is exactly the sort of young man who would have found a place there.

## Role in the Novel

### References

The British Linen Company appears explicitly three times in *Catriona*:

- In the opening sentence, establishing David's identity: "I, David Balfour, came forth of the British Linen Company, a porter attending me with a bag of money" (001-001)
- When David receives funds while on the Bass Rock: "an acknowledgment from the British Linen Company for a considerable sum" (014-024)
- In a retrospective passage (the dedication): "for more than a lustre in the British Linen Company's office" (P04-004)

### Significance

The Company's narrative function is to establish David's position in the social order before the Appin affair disrupts it. The contrast is structural: David begins the novel as a Company man — punctual, solvent, respectable — and ends it as an exile in Leyden, his legal career abandoned, his future uncertain. The Company represents the life David might have had: a safe, prosperous, anonymous existence in commercial Edinburgh. The novel is, in part, the story of why he cannot have that life — why justice, once glimpsed, makes respectability impossible.

The Company also represents a form of power that operates differently from political power. Prestongrange can sequester David; he cannot sequester David's money. The "acknowledgment for a considerable sum" that arrives on the Bass Rock is a reminder that commercial credit respects contracts, not patronage — a small but significant counterpoint to the novel's otherwise bleak portrait of institutional power.

## Key Members

- **David Balfour** — employee, "a lustre" (five years) of service
- The unnamed porter who attends David with his bag of money in the opening scene
- The Company's directors and shareholders, who exist offstage but whose institutional authority gives David the financial independence he needs

## Historical Note

The historical British Linen Company (established 1746) was one of Scotland's most successful chartered companies. It issued its own banknotes and played a significant role in financing Scotland's industrial development. In 1906 it formally became the British Linen Bank, and it operated as an independent institution until its acquisition by the Bank of Scotland in 1971. Its notes continued to circulate in Scotland until the late 20th century.

Stevenson's depiction of the Company is notably accurate for the period. The "lustre" (French for "five years," adopted in Scots usage) of David's service corresponds to historical employment patterns, and the ability of a clerk to receive funds while geographically separated from his office reflects the Company's established credit networks. Unlike some of the novel's more romanticised institutions, the British Linen Company is rendered with documentary precision.
