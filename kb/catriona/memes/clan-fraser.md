Clan Fraser is a Highland clan whose chief, Simon Fraser, 11th Lord Lovat, was
executed for treason after the 1745 Jacobite Rising. In *Catriona*, the clan
appears through the figure of Simon Fraser, Master of Lovat — the executed
chief's son — who navigates the post-1745 settlement as a Crown collaborator.

## Overview

The Frasers of Lovat were a significant Highland clan with lands in Inverness-shire.
Their history is marked by the political calculation that characterised many
Highland clans in the 18th century: the 11th Lord Lovat played both sides of the
Jacobite conflict, and his execution in 1747 left his son to rebuild the family's
fortunes through service to the Hanoverian state.

Stevenson introduces the clan through Prestongrange's words: "the chief of the
great clan Fraser" (006-005). The adjective "great" is ironic — the clan's
greatness has been reduced to a single compromised heir dining at the Lord
Advocate's table.

## In the Novel

Clan Fraser appears primarily through Simon Fraser of Lovat, who David meets at
Prestongrange's dinner table. James More Macgregor acknowledges Fraser as "my
ain kind" (011-017), recognising the clan bond that persists despite political
divisions. The Fraser connection serves as a demonstration of how Highland clan
identity adapted to the post-1745 world: not through resistance (like the
Macgregors) but through assimilation.

The clan's role in the novel is small but significant. It represents the path
that David refuses — survival through collaboration. When Prestongrange
introduces Simon Fraser as "whom we used to call by another name" (006-003),
the implication is clear: the Frasers have traded their Jacobite past for
Hanoverian present, and the transaction has left its mark.

## Significance

Clan Fraser exemplifies the novel's treatment of clan identity as a site of
moral complexity. The clan system that Stevenson depicts is not a simple
opposition of Highland honour against Lowland law. Clan loyalty can be
instrumentalised — James More uses clan bonds to manipulate, and Simon Fraser
uses them to survive. The Frasers' story is the story of how clan identity
functioned not as refuge from the modern world but as a medium through which
that world's compromises were negotiated.
