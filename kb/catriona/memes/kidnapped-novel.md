# Kidnapped

*Kidnapped* (full title: *Kidnapped: Being Memoirs of the Adventures of David Balfour in the Year 1751*) is a historical adventure novel by Robert Louis Stevenson, first published in serial form in *Young Folks* magazine in 1886. It tells the story of David Balfour, a young Lowland Scot who is kidnapped by his uncle, shipwrecked on the coast of Mull, and forced to flee through the Highlands as a fugitive alongside Alan Breck Stewart. The novel's events are the direct prequel to *Catriona*, and the characters and situations established in *Kidnapped* — particularly the bond between David and Alan — are the foundation on which the later novel builds.

## Overview

David Balfour, a seventeen-year-old orphan, travels to the House of Shaws to claim his inheritance from his uncle Ebenezer Balfour. Ebenezer arranges for David to be kidnapped and sold into indentured servitude aboard the brig *Covenant*, bound for the Carolinas. When the *Covenant* strikes a boat in the fog, the sole survivor — Alan Breck Stewart, a Jacobite fugitive — is taken aboard. David and Alan form an alliance, and after the ship is wrecked on the coast of Mull, they traverse the Highlands together, pursued by government soldiers.

The novel's action culminates in the Appin Murder — the assassination of Colin Campbell of Glenure, the "Red Fox" — which David witnesses. The murder, and David and Alan's proximity to it, are the direct link to *Catriona*: the sequel opens with David arriving in Edinburgh as a witness to the events *Kidnapped* described.

## Historical Context

*Kidnapped* was written during what Stevenson called his "great creative period" in Bournemouth (1884–1887), alongside *The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* and *The Master of Ballantrae*. It was serialised in *Young Folks* (a boys' magazine) from May to July 1886, and published in book form the same year. Stevenson drew on the historical Appin Murder of 1752 for the novel's climax, and the character of Alan Breck Stewart is based on a historical figure of the same name.

The novel was an immediate success with both juvenile and adult readers. Henry James praised its "noble clarity" and its fusion of adventure narrative with moral seriousness. It has never been out of print and remains one of the most widely read Scottish novels in the English language.

## Role in the Novel

### References

*Kidnapped* is referenced throughout *Catriona* as the backstory that David must narrate (or conceal) in his dealings with lawyers and politicians. The "various passages of our escape among the heather" (002-021) are common knowledge to the Edinburgh legal establishment, and David's connection to Alan Breck — forged in *Kidnapped* — is what makes him both valuable and dangerous to Prestongrange.

### Significance

Without *Kidnapped*, *Catriona* cannot be understood. The bond between David and Alan, the events at Appin, and David's recovery of his estate are all established in the earlier novel. *Catriona* deepens and complicates the world of *Kidnapped* — transforming Alan from a romantic adventurer into a tragic fugitive, and David from a boy-hero into a morally conflicted adult — but it does not replace it. The two novels form a diptych, with *Kidnapped* as the outward journey (geographical adventure) and *Catriona* as the inward journey (moral and legal struggle).
