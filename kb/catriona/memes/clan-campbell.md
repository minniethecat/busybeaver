# Clan Campbell

Clan Campbell was the most powerful Highland clan of the 18th century — Whig, Presbyterian, and Hanoverian, led by the Duke of Argyll, and the indispensable ally of the British government in the pacification of the Highlands. In *Catriona*, the Campbells are everywhere and nowhere: the Duke is a power behind the throne, the Campbell-packed jury convicts James Stewart, and "a Campbell charged with the blood of a Stewart" (004-077) is the novel's emblem of the new order.

## Overview

The Campbells' rise to dominance began in the late medieval period and accelerated after the Reformation, when they aligned themselves with the Protestant cause and the Scottish Crown. By the 18th century, the chief of Clan Campbell — Archibald Campbell, 3rd Duke of Argyll (1682–1761) — was the most powerful man in Scotland after the king's representative. The clan's territory stretched from Argyllshire in the west to Angus in the east, and its influence reached into every corner of Scottish political life: the law, the church, the army, and the administration of the forfeited Jacobite estates.

## History

The Campbells of Argyll were raised to the peerage in the 15th century and to the dukedom in 1701. Archibald Campbell, the 3rd Duke, was the architect of the clan's mid-century preeminence. He served as Lord Justice General of Scotland, effectively controlling the judiciary; his brother, the Earl of Ilay, managed Scottish patronage from London. Together, the Campbell brothers ran Scotland for the Hanoverian interest.

In the Highlands, the Campbells were the enforcers of government policy. After the '45 Rising, Campbell factors were appointed to manage forfeited Jacobite estates — most notoriously Colin Campbell of Glenure on the Stewart estate of Ardshiel. The Appin Murder was a direct consequence of this policy: a Campbell placed in authority over Stewarts, collecting their rents, was a provocation that clan honour could not ignore.

## Role in the Novel

### The Duke and the Trial

The Duke of Argyll is never a speaking character in *Catriona*, but his presence is felt throughout. He is the justice-general who will preside over James Stewart's trial. He is "the Duke with the power of the West Highlands" (009-001), the man whose influence can make or break any career, any case, any life. Prestongrange — himself a "Highlander civilised" — serves at the Duke's pleasure.

### "A Campbell Quarrel"

Alan Breck Stewart puts the matter bluntly: James Stewart will be "tried in court before a Campbell jury and a Campbell judge, and that in a Campbell country and upon a Campbell quarrel" (002-058). The word "Campbell" here functions as an adjective for a system of power — judicial, territorial, political — that the Stewarts cannot escape. The trial of James Stewart is not a legal proceeding in any neutral sense; it is the continuation of clan warfare by other means.

### Colin Campbell of Glenure

The murdered factor is a Campbell — not a great lord but a working agent of Campbell power, the man on the ground in Appin. His death at the hands of an unknown Stewart assassin is both a crime and, in the logic of clan politics, an act of war. The novel's interest in Glenure is not in his character (he is barely sketched) but in his symbolic function: "a Campbell who has fallen martyr to his duty — as who else but the Campbells have ever put themselves foremost on that path?" (004-033).

## Historical Note

The historical Clan Campbell were and remain one of Scotland's great families. The 3rd Duke of Argyll was a genuine statesman — he had fought at Sheriffmuir in 1715 (on the government side), served in Walpole's cabinet, and was instrumental in the Union settlement. His role in the Appin Murder trial was, by modern standards, a scandal: no man should preside over the trial of his clan's enemies. But by the standards of the time, it was how business was done. Stevenson's portrait of Campbell power is not a caricature but a clinical account of how clan politics worked in the 18th-century British state.
