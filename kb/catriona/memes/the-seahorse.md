# The Seahorse (warship)

**_The Seahorse_** is a Royal Navy warship under the command of Captain Palliser, stationed in the Firth of Forth during the events of the novel _Catriona_. The ship appears at two critical points in the narrative and plays a role in the dangers surrounding Alan Breck Stewart.

## The Bass Rock Incident

The _Seahorse_ first appears while David Balfour is imprisoned on the Bass Rock. The ship is described as "cruising in the month of September, plying between Fife and Lothian, and sounding for sunk dangers." One morning she approaches dangerously close to the Bass:

> "Early one fine morning she was seen about two miles to east of us, where she lowered a boat, and seemed to examine the Wildfire Rocks and Satan's Bush, famous dangers of that coast. And presently after having got her boat again, she came before the wind and was headed directly for the Bass."[^1]

This causes considerable alarm for Andie Scougal and the Highlanders guarding David, as his imprisonment was "designed for privacy." David, fearing that a navy captain coming ashore might worsen rather than improve his situation, gives his parole and is hidden at the summit of the rock. From there, the witnesses observe the _Seahorse_ come close enough that "she would have struck" before suddenly turning and firing a volley of guns:

> "The rock was shaken with the thunder of the sound, the smoke flowed over our heads, and the geese rose in number beyond computation or belief."[^1]

David deduces that Captain Palliser's true purpose was the "somewhat childish pleasure" of startling the vast seabird colony on the Bass. However, David makes a careful observation of the ship's rigging during this approach, noting a particular characteristic "by which I ever after knew it miles away." This observation later proves providential: "this was a means (under Providence) of my averting from a friend a great calamity, and inflicting on Captain Palliser himself a sensible disappointment."[^1]

## The French Coast Encounter

The _Seahorse_ reappears later in the novel off the coast of France. While David and Alan are near Dunkirk, David spots the vessel and immediately recognises it by its distinctive rigging:

> "One of these was extremely close in to be so great a vessel; and I was aware of a shock of new suspicion, when I recognised the trim of the _Seahorse_."[^2]

The presence of an English warship so close to the French coast raises David's suspicions. He connects several troubling facts: Alan Breck is nearby, the _Seahorse_ is within reach, and Catriona (daughter of James More) is walking alone to the beach. David watches as a boat from the _Seahorse_ comes ashore and Catriona meets its officer, exchanging a letter before returning. The officer remains behind, hidden among the dunes.

This sequence confirms David's fears that James More has been conspiring with the authorities. Alan Breck, a deserter, rebel, and condemned murderer, would face grave danger if captured and taken aboard the _Seahorse_. David reflects with a shudder on "how little that jacket would avail him, if he were once caught and flung in a skiff, and carried on board of the _Seahorse_."[^3]

## Captain Palliser

Captain Palliser, the commander of the _Seahorse_, is never directly confronted in the novel but appears in references. He is portrayed as having a taste for frivolous diversions (the Bass Rock cannonade) but is also clearly involved in the serious business of pursuing Jacobite fugitives. David's ability to recognise his ship by its rigging — and Palliser's identification with the vessel — becomes a key plot device in the final chapters.

[^1]: Catriona, ch-15: "There was a warship at this time stationed in the Firth, the *Seahorse*, Captain Palliser... The *Seahorse* came straight on till I thought she would have struck."

[^2]: Catriona, ch-29: "What should an English ship be doing so near in to France? Why was Alan brought into her neighbourhood, and that in a place so far from any hope of rescue?"

[^3]: Catriona, ch-29: "I could not avoid to shudder when I thought how little that jacket would avail him, if he were once caught and flung in a skiff, and carried on board of the *Seahorse*, a deserter, a rebel, and now a condemned murderer."
