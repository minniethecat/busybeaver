# Laird

In 18th-century Scotland, a **laird** was a landed proprietor — a man who held land by feudal tenure or freehold, ranking below a lord of Parliament but above a tenant farmer. For David Balfour, the narrator of *Catriona*, inheriting the lairdship of Shaws is the event that transforms him from a schoolmaster's son into a man of property, standing, and consequence.

## Definition

The term "laird" (from Scots, cognate with Middle English *lard*, "lord") denoted a landowner who was not a peer of the realm. Unlike an English "lord of the manor," the laird's title attached to the land itself rather than to a grant of nobility — a man was "laird of" his estate, not "Lord" in the peerage sense. The laird's house was the "big house" of the neighbourhood, and his authority, though not feudal in the old sense, carried weight in local affairs, kirk sessions, and the dispensing of patronage (001-001, 003-016).

## Historical Context

In post-Union Scotland, the lairds formed the backbone of the political nation. They were the men who elected Members of Parliament, served as justices of the peace, and administered local government. The laird class was diverse: some, like the laird of Pilrig, were "long-headed, sensible" men, "good Whig and Presbyterian," who read their pocket Bibles daily (014-016); others, like David's uncle Ebenezer Balfour in *Kidnapped*, were miserly and morally bankrupt. The lairdship was a position of responsibility as much as privilege — a laird was expected to manage his estate, care for his tenants, and uphold the law.

## Role in the Novel

### David Balfour, Laird of Shaws

The novel opens with David having just been "served heir to my position in life, a landed laird, a bank porter by me carrying my gold, recommendations in my pocket" (001-001). This inheritance is the foundation of his independence: it gives him the means to travel to Edinburgh, to engage a lawyer, and to pursue justice for James Stewart of the Glens. Without the lairdship, David would be a penniless student with no standing; with it, he is a man whose name carries weight — "a good honest Whig name" on a letter from "the laird of Pilrig" opens doors at the Advocate's chambers (004-004, 004-035).

### The Laird of Pilrig

Mr. Rankeillor's letter of introduction sends David to the laird of Pilrig, one of the novel's minor but morally weighty figures. Pilrig is everything a Whig laird should be: sensible, pious, law-abiding, and quietly influential. He receives David "in the midst of learned works and musical instruments" (003-016), a model of the enlightened Scottish gentry. His letter is David's passport into the world of Edinburgh politics and law.

### Lairdship and Identity

When James More Macgregor tells David "I think you will soon be the laird indeed, Mr. Davie" (020-007), he is acknowledging something important: David has grown into the role. At the novel's climax, David declares to Catriona: "I am come into my kingdom" (027-009) — meaning not just his estate but his full stature as a man. The lairdship is the outward sign of David's moral coming-of-age.

### Laird of Prestongrange

Lord Prestongrange, the Lord Advocate, is "the Laird of Prestongrange" (021-026) — a peer who is also a landed proprietor, combining legal authority with territorial power. His dual identity as laird and law officer embodies the fusion of land, law, and politics that defined 18th-century Scottish governance.
