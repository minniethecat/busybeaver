# Community

The web of mutual [[duty|obligation]] and shared identity that binds [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s inhabitants — from the "[[neighbor|community]] like this" where [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] finds himself unexpectedly at [[belonging|home]] (036-015), to the neighbor who is mourned as "a great [[death|loss]] to the [[neighbor|community]]" (041-053), [[neighbor|community]] is the novel's most pervasive and defining concept.

## Definition

Community in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is not an abstraction — it is the daily texture of interconnected lives. Montgomery presents [[avonlea|Avonlea]] as a [[belonging|place]] where every action ripples outward: [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching shapes the next generation; a neighbor's death is felt by all; a stranger's arrival alters the social landscape. The word "[[neighbor|community]]" itself appears at key moments — when [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] acknowledges he has come to value "a [[neighbor|community]] like this, where he can't play" the isolated bachelor (036-015), and when a departed neighbor is eulogized as "a great [[death|loss]] to the [[neighbor|community]]" (041-053).

Montgomery's [[neighbor|community]] is neither idealized nor condemned. It is intrusive — gossip circulates freely, judgments are swift, and individual eccentricity is noted and discussed. But it is also sustaining: the same neighbors who pry into your business will bring casseroles when you are ill, offer encouragement when you are discouraged, and remember you when you are gone. Community, in this vision, is the necessary cost of be[[envy|longing]].

## Examples in the Text

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s Conversion (Chapter 36).** The irascible neighbor who initially terrified [[anne-shirley|Anne]] admits that "a [[neighbor|community]] like this, where he can't play" the isolated bachelor (036-015) is unexpectedly sustaining. The admission is grudging — [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] would never sentimentalize his change of heart — but Montgomery makes it clear: [[neighbor|community]] has claimed him despite himself. He has been absorbed into [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s web of mutual [[duty|obligation]], and he is better for it.

**A Loss to the Community (Chapter 41).** A death in the village is assessed as "a great [[death|loss]] to the [[neighbor|community]] . . . though I always did think" (041-053) — the qualifying clause is pure Montgomery: even [[death|grief]] is filtered through [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s habit of candid assessment. The [[neighbor|community]] mourns but also evaluates; be[[envy|longing]] means being known, and being known means being judged. The two cannot be separated.

**The School as Micro-Community.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s classroom functions as a [[neighbor|community]] in miniature — a [[belonging|place]] where children learn not just lessons but social existence: cooperation, competition, [[faith|loyalty]], and [[justice|fairness]]. The school is where [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s future [[neighbor|community]] is being formed, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching philosophy — valuing each child as an individual while insisting on collective [[duty|responsibility]] — mirrors Montgomery's vision of the ideal village.

## Thematic Significance

Community is the novel's answer to loneliness. For every character who experiences [[loneliness|isolation]] — [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], the orphaned [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], the widowed [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] — the novel offers [[neighbor|community]] as the remedy. The process is not automatic; [[neighbor|community]] must be *entered*, and entry requires vulnerability: admitting need, accepting help, tolerating the scrutiny that comes [[humor|wit]]h be[[envy|longing]].

Montgomery also shows the darker side of [[neighbor|community]]: the gossip that wounds, the conformity that stifles, the expectations that constrain. But the novel's judgment is ultimately for[[generosity|giving]] — these are the costs of a system that also provides meaning, identity, and care. The alternative — [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s initial [[loneliness|isolation]] — is presented as not merely lonely but impoverished, a life cut off from the nourishment of shared existence.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Community as Support vs. Community as Constraint.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] experiences [[neighbor|community]] as predominantly supportive — the encouragement of [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] (007-001), the [[love|affection]] of her pupils, the friendship of [[diana-barry|Diana]]. But other characters experience its constraints: the pressure to conform, the weight of public opinion, the impossibility of privacy. Montgomery does not resolve this tension but presents it as inherent to [[neighbor|community]] life.

**The Insider's Community vs. The Outsider's Community.** [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s [[neighbor|community]] is most visible from the outside. [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], the newcomer, sees it clearly (036-015); [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], the servant from away, experiences its strangeness. For lifelong residents, [[neighbor|community]] is invisible — it is simply the water they swim in. Montgomery uses outsider perspectives to make the familiar strange and to reveal [[neighbor|community]] as a choice, not just a circumstance.
