[021-001] “Let us hope not, dear,” she said cheerfully. “How are your rock people

[021-002] coming on? Does the oldest Twin still continue to behave himself?”

[021-003] “He _has_ to,” said [[paul-irving|Paul]] emphatically. “He knows I won’t associate with

[021-004] him if he doesn’t. He is really full of wickedness, _I_ think.”

[021-005] “And has Nora found out about the Golden Lady yet?”

[021-006] “No; but I think she suspects. I’m almost sure she watched me the last

[021-007] time I went to the cave. _I_ don’t mind if she finds out . . . it is

[021-008] only for _her_ sake I don’t want her to . . . so that her feelings

[021-009] won’t be hurt. But if she is _determined_ to have her feelings hurt it

[021-010] can’t be helped.”

[021-011] “If I were to go to the shore some night with you do you think I could

[021-012] see your rock people too?”

[021-013] Paul shook his head gravely.

[021-014] “No, I don’t think you could see _my_ rock people. I’m the only person

[021-015] who can see them. But you could see rock people of your own. You’re one

[021-016] of the kind that can. We’re both that kind. _You_ know, teacher,” he

[021-017] added, squeezing her hand chummily. “Isn’t it splendid to be that kind,

[021-018] teacher?”

[021-019] “Splendid,” [[anne-shirley|Anne]] agreed, gray shining eyes looking down into blue

[021-020] shining ones. Anne and Paul both knew

[021-021] “How fair the realm

[021-022] [[imagination|Imagination]] opens to the view,”

[021-023] and both knew the way to that happy land. There the rose of [[laughter|joy]] bloomed

[021-024] immortal by dale and stream; clouds never darkened the sunny sky; sweet

[021-025] bells never jangled out of tune; and [[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded. The

[021-026] knowledge of that land’s geography . . . “east o’ the sun, west o’ the

[021-027] moon” . . . is priceless lore, not to be bought in any market [[belonging|place]]. It

[021-028] must be the gift of the good fairies at birth and the years can never

[021-029] deface it or take it away. It is better to possess it, living in a

[021-030] garret, than to be the inhabitant of palaces without it.

[021-031] The [[avonlea|Avonlea]] graveyard was as yet the grass-grown [[loneliness|solitude]] it had always

[021-032] been. To be sure, the Improvers had an eye on it, and [[priscilla-grant|Priscilla Grant]]

[021-033] had read a paper on cemeteries before the last meeting of the Society.

[021-034] At some future time the Improvers meant to have the lichened, wayward

[021-035] old board fence replaced by a neat wire railing, the grass mown and the

[021-036] leaning monuments straightened up.

[021-037] Anne put on Matthew’s grave the flowers she had brought for it, and

[021-038] then went over to the little poplar shaded corner where [[hester-gray|Hester Gray]]

[021-039] slept. Ever since the day of the spring picnic Anne had put flowers on

[021-040] Hester’s grave when she visited Matthew’s. The evening before she had

[021-041] made a pilgrimage back to the little deserted garden in the woods and

[021-042] brought therefrom some of Hester’s own white roses.

[021-043] “I thought you would like them better than any others, dear,” she said

[021-044] softly.

[021-045] Anne was still sitting there when a shadow fell over the grass and she

[021-046] looked up to see [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]. They walked home together.

[021-047] Mrs. Allan’s face was not the face of the girlbride whom [[mr-allan|the minister]]

[021-048] had brought to Avonlea five years before. It had lost some of its bloom

[021-049] and youthful curves, and there were fine, patient lines about eyes and

[021-050] mouth. A tiny grave in that very cemetery accounted for some of them;

[021-051] and some new ones had come during the recent illness, now happily over,

[021-052] of her little son. But Mrs. Allan’s dimples were as sweet and sudden as

[021-053] ever, her eyes as clear and bright and true; and what her face lacked

[021-054] of girlish beauty was now more than atoned for in added tenderness and

[021-055] strength.

[021-056] “I suppose you are looking forward to your vacation, Anne?” she said,

[021-057] as they left the graveyard.

[021-058] Anne nodded.

[021-059] “Yes. . . . I could roll the word as a sweet morsel under my tongue. I

[021-060] think the summer is going to be lovely. For one thing, Mrs. Morgan is

[021-061] coming to [[prince-edward-island|the Island]] in July and Priscilla is going to bring her up. I

[021-062] feel one of my old ‘thrills’ at the mere thought.”

[021-063] “I hope you’ll have a good time, Anne. You’ve worked very hard this

[021-064] past year and you have succeeded.”

[021-065] “Oh, I don’t know. I’ve come so far short in so many things. I haven’t

[021-066] done what I meant to do when I began to teach last fall. I haven’t

[021-067] lived up to my ideals.”

[021-068] “None of us ever do,” said Mrs. Allan with a sigh. “But then, Anne, you

[021-069] know what Lowell says, ‘Not failure but low aim is crime.’ We must have

[021-070] ideals and try to live up to them, even if we never quite succeed. Life

[021-071] would be a sorry business without them. With them it’s grand and great.

[021-072] Hold fast to your ideals, Anne.”

[021-073] “I shall try. But I have to let go most of my theories,” said Anne,

[021-074] laughing a little. “I had the most beautiful set of theories you ever

[021-075] knew when I started out as a schoolma’am, but every one of them has

[021-076] failed me at some pinch or another.”

[021-077] “Even the theory on corporal punishment,” teased Mrs. Allan.

[021-078] But Anne flushed.

[021-079] “I shall never forgive myself for whipping [[anthony-pye|Anthony]].”

[021-080] “Nonsense, dear, he deserved it. And it agreed with him. You have had

[021-081] no trouble with him since and he has come to think there’s nobody like

[021-082] you. Your kindness won his [[romance|love]] after the idea that a ‘girl was no

[021-083] good’ was rooted out of his stubborn mind.”

[021-084] “He may have deserved it, but that is not the point. If I had calmly

[021-085] and deliberately decided to whip him because I thought it a just

[021-086] punishment for him I would not feel over it as I do. But the truth is,

[021-087] Mrs. Allan, that I just flew into a temper and whipped him because of

[021-088] that. I wasn’t thinking whether it was just or unjust . . . even if he

[021-089] hadn’t deserved it I’d have done it just the same. That is what

[021-090] humiliates me.”

[021-091] “Well, we all make mistakes, dear, so just put it behind you. We should

[021-092] regret our mistakes and learn from them, but never carry them forward

[021-093] into the future with us. There goes [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] on his wheel . . .

[021-094] home for his vacation too, I suppose. How are you and he getting on

[021-095] with your studies?”

[021-096] “Pretty well. We plan to finish the Virgil tonight . . . there are only

[021-097] twenty lines to do. Then we are not going to study any more until

[021-098] September.”

[021-099] “Do you think you will ever get to college?”

[021-100] “Oh, I don’t know.” Anne looked dreamily afar to the opal-tinted

[021-101] horizon. “[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]’s eyes will never be much better than they are now,

[021-102] although we are so thankful to think that they will not get worse. And

[021-103] then there are the twins . . . somehow I don’t believe their uncle will

[021-104] ever really send for them. Perhaps college may be around the bend in

[021-105] the road, but I haven’t got to the bend yet and I don’t think much

[021-106] about it lest I might grow discontented.”

[021-107] “Well, I should like to see you go to college, Anne; but if you never

[021-108] do, don’t be discontented about it. We make our own lives wherever we

[021-109] are, after all . . . college can only help us to do it more easily.

[021-110] They are broad or narrow according to what we put into them, not what

[021-111] we get out. Life is rich and full here . . . everywhere . . . if we can

[021-112] only learn how to open our whole hearts to its richness and fulness.”

[021-113] “I think I understand what you mean,” said Anne thoughtfully, “and I

[021-114] know I have so much to feel thankful for . . . oh, so much . . . my

[021-115] work, and Paul Irving, and the dear twins, and all my friends. Do you

[021-116] know, Mrs. Allan, I’m so thankful for friendship. It beautifies life so

[021-117] much.”

[021-118] “True friendship is a very helpful thing indeed,” said Mrs. Allan, “and

[021-119] we should have a very high ideal of it, and never sully it by any

[021-120] failure in truth and sincerity. I fear the name of friendship is often

[021-121] degraded to a kind of intimacy that has nothing of real friendship in

[021-122] it.”

[021-123] “Yes . . . like [[gertie-pye|Gertie Pye]]’s and Julia Bell’s. They are very intimate

[021-124] and go everywhere together; but Gertie is always saying nasty things of

[021-125] Julia behind her back and everybody thinks she is jealous of her

[021-126] because she is always so pleased when anybody criticizes Julia. I think

[021-127] it is desecration to call _that_ friendship. If we have friends we

[021-128] should look only for the best in them and give them the best that is in

[021-129] us, don’t you think? Then friendship would be the most beautiful thing

[021-130] in the world.”

[021-131] “Friendship _is_ very beautiful,” smiled Mrs. Allan, “but some day . .

[021-132] .”

[021-133] Then she paused abruptly. In the delicate, white-browed face beside

[021-134] her, with its candid eyes and mobile features, there was still far more

[021-135] of the child than of the woman. Anne’s heart so far harbored only

[021-136] [[ambition|dreams]] of friendship and ambition, and Mrs. Allan did not wish to brush

[021-137] the bloom from her sweet unconsciousness. So she left her sentence for

[021-138] the future years to finish.

[021-139] XVI

[021-140] The Substance of Things Hoped For

[021-141] “Anne,” said [[davy-keith|Davy]] appealingly, scrambling up on the shiny,

[021-142] leather-covered sofa in the [[green-gables|Green Gables]] kitchen, where Anne sat,

[021-143] reading a letter, “Anne, I’m _awful_ hungry. You’ve no idea.”

[021-144] “I’ll get you a piece of bread and butter in a minute,” said Anne

[021-145] absently. Her letter evidently contained some exciting news, for her

[021-146] cheeks were as pink as the roses on the big bush outside, and her eyes

[021-147] were as starry as only Anne’s eyes could be.

[021-148] “But I ain’t bread and butter hungry,” said Davy in a disgusted tone.

[021-149] “I’m plum cake hungry.”

[021-150] “Oh,” laughed Anne, laying down her letter and putting her arm about

[021-151] Davy to give him a squeeze, “that’s a kind of hunger that can be

[021-152] endured very comfortably, Davy-boy. You know it’s one of Marilla’s

[021-153] rules that you can’t have anything but bread and butter between meals.”

[021-154] “Well, gimme a piece then . . . please.”

[021-155] Davy had been at last taught to say “please,” but he generally tacked

[021-156] it on as an afterthought. He looked with approval at the generous slice

[021-157] Anne presently brought to him. “You always put such a nice lot of

[021-158] butter on it, Anne. Marilla spreads it pretty thin. It slips down a lot

[021-159] easier when there’s plenty of butter.”

[021-160] The slice “slipped down” with tolerable ease, judging from its rapid

[021-161] disappearance. Davy slid head first off the sofa, turned a double

[021-162] somersault on the rug, and then sat up and announced decidedly,

[021-163] “Anne, I’ve made up my mind about heaven. I don’t want to go there.”

[021-164] “Why not?” asked Anne gravely.

[021-165] “Cause heaven is in Simon Fletcher’s garret, and I don’t like Simon

[021-166] Fletcher.”

[021-167] “Heaven in . . . Simon Fletcher’s garret!” gasped Anne, too amazed even

[021-168] to laugh. “Davy Keith, whatever put such an extraordinary idea into

[021-169] your head?”

[021-170] “Milty Boulter says that’s where it is. It was last Sunday in Sunday

[021-171] School. The lesson was about Elijah and Elisha, and I up and asked Miss

[021-172] Rogerson where heaven was. Miss Rogerson looked awful offended. She was

[021-173] cross anyhow, because when she’d asked us what Elijah left Elisha when

[021-174] he went to heaven Milty Boulter said, ‘His old clo’es,’ and us fellows

[021-175] all laughed before we thought. I wish you could think first and do

[021-176] things afterwards, ’cause then you wouldn’t do them. But Milty didn’t

[021-177] mean to be disrespeckful. He just couldn’t think of the name of the

[021-178] thing. Miss Rogerson said heaven was where God was and I wasn’t to ask

[021-179] questions like that. Milty nudged me and said in a whisper, ‘Heaven’s

[021-180] in Uncle Simon’s garret and I’ll esplain about it on the road home.’ So

[021-181] when we was coming home he esplained. Milty’s a great hand at

[021-182] esplaining things. Even if he don’t know anything about a thing he’ll

[021-183] make up a lot of stuff and so you get it esplained all the same. His

[021-184] mother is Mrs. Simon’s sister and he went with her to the funeral when

[021-185] his cousin, [[jane-andrews|Jane]] Ellen, died. The minister said she’d gone to heaven,

[021-186] though Milty says she was lying right before them in the coffin. But he

[021-187] s’posed they carried the coffin to the garret afterwards. Well, when

[021-188] Milty and his mother went upstairs after it was all over to get her

[021-189] bonnet he asked her where heaven was that Jane Ellen had gone to, and

[021-190] she pointed right to the ceiling and said, ‘Up there.’ Milty knew there

[021-191] wasn’t anything but the garret over the ceiling, so that’s how _he_

[021-192] found out. And he’s been awful scared to go to his Uncle Simon’s ever

[021-193] since.”
