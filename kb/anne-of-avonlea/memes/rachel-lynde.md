# Rachel Lynde

[[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s self-appointed guardian of propriety and morality — a woman whose conviction that "it is her duty to inform" others of unpleasant truths (009-029) makes her both a comic figure and a genuine force in the [[neighbor|community]].

## Character Overview

[[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] is one of Montgomery's most fully realized comic creations — a woman of boundless self-assurance whose belief in her own [[justice|rightness]] is matched only by her willingness to share her opinions. She is [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s preeminent gossip, a woman who believes "it is their duty to tell you a certain thing" whether welcome or not (009-032), and who takes it upon herself "to inform" [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] of matters great and small (009-029).

Yet [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is not a villain or even, ultimately, a figure of ridicule. Montgomery's treatment is [[love|affection]]ately satirical — [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is ridiculous but also capable of genuine kindness, and her commitment to [[neighbor|community]], however intrusively expressed, is real.

## Role in the Story

[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] functions as both comic relief and moral counterpoint. Her self-serving invocation of "duty" as a justification for gossip (009-029) contrasts [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s sincere and costly [[wisdom|understanding]] of the same concept. Her pronouncements on village affairs provide a running commentary that is both amusing and, occasionally, [[wisdom|insight]]ful.

She appears throughout the novel as a constant presence in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] life — at church, at [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] meetings, and in [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s kitchen. Her predictability is part of her charm: the reader knows what [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] will say before she says it, and the pleasure lies in watching her say it anyway.

## Relationships

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** is [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s friend and frequent recipient of her unsolicited advice. Their relationship is one of mutual, if occasionally strained, respect.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is the recipient of [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s "duty" to inform — a role [[anne-shirley|Anne]] navigates [[humor|wit]]h characteristic diplomacy, "skillfully" managing [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s intrusions [[humor|wit]]hout alienating her (009-128).

**[[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]]** is [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s long-suffering husband, mentioned but rarely seen.

## Key Scenes

**The [[duty|Duty]] to Inform (Chapter 9).** [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s declaration that it is "her duty to inform" [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] of various matters (009-029, 009-032) is both the novel's most concentrated satire of her character and its sharpest critique of duty weaponized for self-serving ends.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[diana-barry|Di]]plomacy (Chapter 9).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s skillful handling of [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s intrusions — she "did what she believed to be her duty" (009-128) in responding gracefully — demonstrates her maturation from the girl who once exploded at [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s comments about her red hair.

## Character Analysis

[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] represents the double-edged [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of [[neighbor|community]] in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*. Her gossip is genuinely intrusive and occasionally harmful, yet her engagement [[humor|wit]]h her [[community|neighbors]]' lives is also a form of care — she knows what is happening because she pays attention, and she pays attention because she cares. Montgomery's genius is to hold both truths in balance.
