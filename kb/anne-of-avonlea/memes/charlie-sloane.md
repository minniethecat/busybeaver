# Charlie Sloane

**Charlie Sloane** is a young man of [[avonlea|Avonlea]], a member of the extensive [[sloane-family|Sloane family]], who appears in _[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]_ as a minor figure in the village's social landscape. He is best known for his literary inclinations and for being cast — by the gossip mill of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] — as [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]]'s rival for [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s [[love|affection]]s.

## Character Overview

Charlie Sloane occupies a familiar role in Montgomery's [[avonlea|Avonlea]]: the young man of respectable family whose ambitions tilt toward the intellectual. That village gossip credits him [[humor|wit]]h the anonymous "[[avonlea|Avonlea]] Notes" in the [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] _Daily Enterprise_ testifies to his established reputation as a writer — he had "indulged in similar literary flights in times past" [033-104]. The fact that the actual authors were [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] and [[anne-shirley|Anne]], operating under a pseudonym, is one of Montgomery's small ironies at the expense of small-town certainty.

## Role in Story

Charlie's narrative function is primarily as a foil in the unspoken romantic competition between him and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] — a [[jealousy|rivalry]] that exists far more vividly in the imaginations of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s gossips than in the minds of its supposed participants. His presence helps illuminate how thoroughly the village [[neighbor|community]] constructs narratives around its young people, particularly around [[anne-shirley|Anne]].

## Key Scenes

### The Avonlea Notes

> [033-103] Gossip ascribed the authorship thereof to Charlie Sloane, partly because the said Charlie had indulged in similar literary flights in times past, and partly because one of the notes seemed to embody a sneer at Gilbert Blythe.

When anonymous "[[avonlea|Avonlea]] Notes" appear in the [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] _Daily Enterprise_ [033-101–110], the village immediately attributes them to Charlie Sloane [033-103]. The reasoning is twofold: Charlie has a known history of literary endeavors, and one of the notes appears to mock [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] — confirmation, to the village mind, of romantic [[jealousy|rivalry]] [033-107]. The truth — that [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] co-authored the notes as a blind — reveals the gap between [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s narrative instincts and reality.

### The Perceived Rivalry

> [033-107] Avonlea juvenile society persisted in regarding Gilbert Blythe and Charlie Sloane as rivals in the good graces of a certain damsel with gray eyes and an imagination.

The narrator's amused report that [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s "juvenile [[community|society]]" casts Charlie and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] as rivals for [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [033-107] is characteristic of Montgomery's gentle satire of [[community|village life]]. The phrase "a certain damsel [[humor|wit]]h gray eyes and an imagination" elegantly identifies [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[humor|wit]]hout naming her, underlining how thoroughly the [[neighbor|community]] has mapped its romantic expectations onto its young people.

## Relationships

Charlie's primary relationship in the narrative is his perceived [[jealousy|rivalry]] [[humor|wit]]h [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] over [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] [033-107]. He belongs to the sprawling [[sloane-family|Sloane clan]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]], which includes [[oliver-sloane|Oliver Sloane]], [[peter-sloane|Peter Sloane]], and numerous other Sloanes who populate the village's social and institutional life.

## Character Analysis

Charlie Sloane exemplifies Montgomery's technique of populating [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[humor|wit]]h minor characters who, in a few strokes, suggest a full life beyond the page. His literary ambitions mark him as one of the more intellectually inclined young men in the village — a quality that, combined [[humor|wit]]h his family's solid standing, makes him a plausible romantic prospect in the village's social calculus. That he is entirely unaware of being cast as [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s rival is part of the joke: the novel repeatedly shows how the stories [[avonlea|Avonlea]] tells itself about its residents have little to do [[humor|wit]]h their actual feelings or intentions.
