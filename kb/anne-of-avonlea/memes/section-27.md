[027-001] “Your mother was very lovely, [[paul-irving|Paul]], and you look a little like her. But

[027-002] her eyes and hair are darker than yours.”

[027-003] “My eyes are the same color as father’s,” said Paul, flying about the

[027-004] room to heap all available cushions on the window seat, “but father’s

[027-005] hair is gray. He has lots of it, but it is gray. You see, father is

[027-006] nearly fifty. That’s ripe old age, isn’t it? But it’s only _outside_

[027-007] he’s old. _Inside_ he’s just as young as anybody. Now, teacher, please

[027-008] sit here; and I’ll sit at your feet. May I lay my head against your

[027-009] knee? That’s the way my little mother and I used to sit. Oh, this is

[027-010] real splendid, I think.”

[027-011] “Now, I want to hear those thoughts which [[mary-joe|Mary Joe]] pronounces so

[027-012] queer,” said [[anne-shirley|Anne]], patting the mop of curls at her side. Paul never

[027-013] needed any coaxing to tell his thoughts . . . at least, to congenial

[027-014] souls.

[027-015] “I thought them out in the fir grove one night,” he said dreamily. “Of

[027-016] course I didn’t _believe_ them but I _thought_ them. _You_ know,

[027-017] teacher. And then I wanted to tell them to somebody and there was

[027-018] nobody but Mary Joe. Mary Joe was in the pantry setting bread and I sat

[027-019] down on the bench beside her and I said, ‘Mary Joe, do you know what I

[027-020] think? I think the evening star is a lighthouse on the land where the

[027-021] fairies dwell.’ And Mary Joe said, ‘Well, yous are de queer one. Dare

[027-022] ain’t no such ting as fairies.’ I was very much provoked. Of course, I

[027-023] knew there are no fairies; but that needn’t prevent my thinking there

[027-024] is. _You_ know, teacher. But I tried again quite patiently. I said,

[027-025] ‘Well then, Mary Joe, do you know what I think? I think an angel walks

[027-026] over the world after the sun sets . . . a great, tall, white angel,

[027-027] with silvery folded wings . . . and sings the flowers and birds to

[027-028] sleep. Children can hear him if they know how to listen.’ Then Mary Joe

[027-029] held up her hands all over flour and said, ‘Well, yous are de queer

[027-030] leetle boy. Yous make me feel scare.’ And she really did looked scared.

[027-031] I went out then and whispered the rest of my thoughts to the garden.

[027-032] There was a little birch tree in the garden and it died. Grandma says

[027-033] the salt spray killed it; but I think the dryad belonging to it was a

[027-034] foolish dryad who wandered away to see the world and got lost. And the

[027-035] little tree was so lonely it died of a broken heart.”

[027-036] “And when the poor, foolish little dryad gets tired of the world and

[027-037] comes back to her tree _her_ heart will break,” said Anne.

[027-038] “Yes; but if dryads are foolish they must take the consequences, just

[027-039] as if they were real people,” said Paul gravely. “Do you know what I

[027-040] think about the new moon, teacher? I think it is a little golden boat

[027-041] full of [[ambition|dreams]].”

[027-042] “And when it tips on a cloud some of them spill out and fall into your

[027-043] sleep.”

[027-044] “Exactly, teacher. Oh, you _do_ know. And I think the violets are

[027-045] little snips of the sky that fell down when the angels cut out holes

[027-046] for the stars to shine through. And the buttercups are made out of old

[027-047] sunshine; and I think the sweet peas will be butterflies when they go

[027-048] to heaven. Now, teacher, do you see anything so very queer about those

[027-049] thoughts?”

[027-050] “No, laddie dear, they are not queer at all; they are strange and

[027-051] beautiful thoughts for a little boy to think, and so people who

[027-052] couldn’t think anything of the sort themselves, if they tried for a

[027-053] hundred years, think them queer. But keep on thinking them, Paul . . .

[027-054] some day you are going to be a poet, I believe.”

[027-055] When Anne reached [[belonging|home]] she found a very different type of boyhood

[027-056] waiting to be put to bed. [[davy-keith|Davy]] was sulky; and when Anne had undressed

[027-057] him he bounced into bed and buried his face in the pillow.

[027-058] “Davy, you have forgotten to say your prayers,” said Anne rebukingly.

[027-059] “No, I didn’t forget,” said Davy defiantly, “but I ain’t going to say

[027-060] my prayers any more. I’m going to give up trying to be good, ’cause no

[027-061] matter how good I am you’d like Paul Irving better. So I might as well

[027-062] be bad and have the fun of it.”

[027-063] “I don’t like Paul Irving _better_,” said Anne seriously. “I like you

[027-064] just as well, only in a different way.”

[027-065] “But I want you to like me the same way,” pouted Davy.

[027-066] “You can’t like different people the same way. You don’t like [[dora-keith|Dora]] and

[027-067] me the same way, do you?”

[027-068] Davy sat up and reflected.

[027-069] “No . . . o . . . o,” he admitted at last, “I like Dora because she’s

[027-070] my sister but I like you because you’re _you_.”

[027-071] “And I like Paul because he is Paul and Davy because he is Davy,” said

[027-072] Anne gaily.

[027-073] “Well, I kind of wish I’d said my prayers then,” said Davy, convinced

[027-074] by this logic. “But it’s too much bother getting out now to say them.

[027-075] I’ll say them twice over in the morning, Anne. Won’t that do as well?”

[027-076] No, Anne was positive it would not do as well. So Davy scrambled out

[027-077] and knelt down at her knee. When he had finished his devotions he

[027-078] leaned back on his little, bare, brown heels and looked up at her.

[027-079] “Anne, I’m gooder than I used to be.”

[027-080] “Yes, indeed you are, Davy,” said Anne, who never hesitated to give

[027-081] credit where credit was due.

[027-082] “I _know_ I’m gooder,” said Davy confidently, “and I’ll tell you how I

[027-083] know it. Today [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] give me two pieces of bread and jam, one for me

[027-084] and one for Dora. One was a good deal bigger than the other and Marilla

[027-085] didn’t say which was mine. But I give the biggest piece to Dora. That

[027-086] was good of me, wasn’t it?”

[027-087] “Very good, and very manly, Davy.”

[027-088] “Of course,” admitted Davy, “Dora wasn’t very hungry and she only et

[027-089] half her slice and then she give the rest to me. But I didn’t know she

[027-090] was going to do that when I give it to her, so I _was_ good, Anne.”

[027-091] In the twilight Anne sauntered down to the Dryad’s Bubble and saw

[027-092] [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] coming down through the dusky [[haunted-wood|Haunted Wood]]. She had a

[027-093] sudden realization that Gilbert was a schoolboy no longer. And how

[027-094] manly he looked—the tall, frank-faced fellow, with the clear,

[027-095] straightforward eyes and the broad shoulders. Anne thought Gilbert was

[027-096] a very handsome lad, even though he didn’t look at all like her ideal

[027-097] man. She and [[diana-barry|Diana]] had long ago decided what kind of a man they admired

[027-098] and their tastes seemed exactly similar. He must be very tall and

[027-099] distinguished looking, with melancholy, inscrutable eyes, and a

[027-100] melting, sympathetic voice. There was nothing either melancholy or

[027-101] inscrutable in Gilbert’s physiognomy, but of course that didn’t matter

[027-102] in friendship!

[027-103] Gilbert stretched himself out on the ferns beside the Bubble and looked

[027-104] approvingly at Anne. If Gilbert had been asked to describe his ideal

[027-105] woman the description would have answered point for point to Anne, even

[027-106] to those seven tiny freckles whose obnoxious presence still continued

[027-107] to vex her soul. Gilbert was as yet little more than a boy; but a boy

[027-108] has his dreams as have others, and in Gilbert’s future there was always

[027-109] a girl with big, limpid gray eyes, and a face as fine and delicate as a

[027-110] flower. He had made up his mind, also, that his future must be worthy

[027-111] of its goddess. Even in quiet [[avonlea|Avonlea]] there were temptations to be met

[027-112] and faced. [[white-sands|White Sands]] youth were a rather “fast” set, and Gilbert was

[027-113] popular wherever he went. But he meant to keep himself worthy of Anne’s

[027-114] friendship and perhaps some distant day her [[romance|love]]; and he watched over

[027-115] word and thought and deed as jealously as if her clear eyes were to

[027-116] pass in judgment on it. She held over him the unconscious influence

[027-117] that every girl, whose ideals are high and pure, wields over her

[027-118] friends; an influence which would endure as long as she was faithful to

[027-119] those ideals and which she would as certainly lose if she were ever

[027-120] false to them. In Gilbert’s eyes Anne’s greatest charm was the fact

[027-121] that she never stooped to the petty practices of so many of the Avonlea

[027-122] girls—the small jealousies, the little deceits and rivalries, the

[027-123] palpable bids for favor. Anne held herself apart from all this, not

[027-124] consciously or of design, but simply because anything of the sort was

[027-125] utterly foreign to her transparent, impulsive [[nature-appreciation|nature]], crystal clear in

[027-126] its motives and aspirations.

[027-127] But Gilbert did not attempt to put his thoughts into words, for he had

[027-128] already too good reason to know that Anne would mercilessly and

[027-129] frostily nip all attempts at sentiment in the bud—or laugh at him,

[027-130] which was ten times worse.

[027-131] “You look like a real dryad under that birch tree,” he said teasingly.

[027-132] “I [[romance|love]] birch trees,” said Anne, laying her cheek against the creamy

[027-133] satin of the slim bole, with one of the pretty, caressing gestures that

[027-134] came so natural to her.

[027-135] “Then you’ll be glad to hear that Mr. Major Spencer has decided to set

[027-136] out a row of white birches all along the road front of his farm, by way

[027-137] of encouraging the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]],” said Gilbert. “He was talking to me about

[027-138] it today. Major Spencer is the most progressive and public-spirited man

[027-139] in Avonlea. And Mr. William Bell is going to set out a spruce hedge

[027-140] along his road front and up his lane. Our Society is getting on

[027-141] splendidly, Anne. It is past the experimental stage and is an accepted

[027-142] fact. The older folks are beginning to take an interest in it and the

[027-143] White Sands people are talking of starting one too. Even Elisha Wright

[027-144] has come around since that day the Americans from the hotel had the

[027-145] [[the-picnic|picnic at the shore]]. They praised our roadsides so highly and said they

[027-146] were so much prettier than in any other part of [[prince-edward-island|the Island]]. And when,

[027-147] in due time, the other farmers follow [[mr-spencer|Mr. Spencer]]’s good example and

[027-148] plant ornamental trees and hedges along their road fronts Avonlea will

[027-149] be the prettiest settlement in the province.”

[027-150] “The Aids are talking of taking up the graveyard,” said Anne, “and I

[027-151] hope they will, because there will have to be a subscription for that,

[027-152] and it would be no use for the Society to try it after the hall affair.

[027-153] But the Aids would never have stirred in the matter if the Society

[027-154] hadn’t put it into their thoughts unofficially. Those trees we planted

[027-155] on [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]] grounds are flourishing, and the trustees have promised

[027-156] me that they will fence in the school grounds next year. If they do

[027-157] I’ll have an arbor day and every scholar shall plant a tree; and we’ll

[027-158] have a garden in the corner by the road.”

[027-159] “We’ve succeeded in almost all our plans so far, except in getting the

[027-160] old Boulter house removed,” said Gilbert, “and I’ve given _that_ up in

[027-161] despair. Levi won’t have it taken down just to vex us. There’s a

[027-162] contrary streak in all the Boulters and it’s strongly developed in

[027-163] him.”

[027-164] “Julia Bell wants to send another committee to him, but I think the

[027-165] better way will just be to leave him severely alone,” said Anne sagely.

[027-166] “And [[faith|trust]] to Providence, as [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says,” smiled Gilbert.

[027-167] “Certainly, no more committees. They only aggravate him. Julia Bell

[027-168] thinks you can do anything, if you only have a committee to attempt it.

[027-169] Next spring, Anne, we must start an agitation for nice lawns and

[027-170] grounds. We’ll sow good seed betimes this winter. I’ve a treatise here

[027-171] on lawns and lawnmaking and I’m going to prepare a paper on the subject

[027-172] soon. Well, I suppose our vacation is almost over. School opens Monday.

[027-173] Has [[ruby-gillis|Ruby Gillis]] got the [[carmody|Carmody]] school?”
