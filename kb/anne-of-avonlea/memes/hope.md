# Hope

The resilient emotional force that wells up in moments of [[despair|discouragement]] — in Montgomery's phrase, "hope upwelled in her heart like a spring" (006-158) — enabling characters to persist through setbacks, [[death|loss]], and uncertainty.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, hope is not merely wishful thinking but a practical emotional resource. Characters draw on hope to navigate disappointment and uncertainty. Montgomery presents hope as something that can be cultivated and renewed — an inner spring that replenishes even after [[despair|discouragement]]. [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]], facing the potential [[death|loss]] of her husband, had "not entirely given up hope" (007-132), demonstrating hope as tenacity rather than naive optimism.

[[anne-shirley|Anne]] embodies this tenacious hopefulness throughout the novel, approaching each challenge — from difficult pupils to [[neighbor|community]] projects — [[humor|wit]]h an expectation that effort will yield good results. She speaks "earnestly, hopefully, as youth [[romance|love]]s to talk" (009-060), revealing hope as a quality associated [[humor|wit]]h youth but not limited to it.

## Examples in the Text

**Hope Renewed After [[diana-barry|Di]]scouragement (Chapter 6).** After a period of [[despair|discouragement]] in her teaching, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] experiences a renewal: "hope upwelled in her heart like a spring" (006-158). This image of hope as a natural, self-renewing resource is central to Montgomery's treatment of the theme — hope is not something manufactured by willpower but something that rises naturally when the weight of [[despair|discouragement]] lifts.

**[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s Steadfast Hope (Chapter 7).** When [[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]] falls gravely ill, Mrs. [[rachel-lynde|Rachel]] "had not entirely given up hope" (007-132). Her hope persists alongside clear-eyed realism; she acknowledges the severity of the situation [[humor|wit]]hout surrendering to despair. The contrast [[humor|wit]]h Eliza, who "was born a pessimist," underscores hope as a character trait rather than a response to circumstances.

**Youth and Hopeful Vision (Chapter 9).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and her friends speak "earnestly, hopefully, as youth [[romance|love]]s to talk, of the future they were to share" (009-060). This passage links hope to youth's forward-looking perspective — the belief that the future is full of possibility and shaped by present effort.

**Hope in a New Beginning (Chapter 13).** As [[anne-shirley|Anne]] begins her teaching career, she is in a "very hopeful frame of mind" (013-024). The new house, new responsibilities, and new possibilities all feed her hopeful outlook. Montgomery shows that hope flourishes most readily at beginnings, when the future is unwritten.

## Thematic Significance

**Hope as Resilience.** Montgomery treats hope not as the absence of difficulty but as the capacity to maintain forward movement through difficulty. Characters do not hope *instead of* facing hardship; they hope *while* facing it. This makes hope a form of courage — the determination to believe in better outcomes even when evidence is mixed.

**The Communal [[diana-barry|Di]]mension of Hope.** Hope in the novel is often shared and reinforced through relationships. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s hopefulness inspires others; the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] is itself an expression of communal hope — the belief that a group of determined people can improve their village. Hope, in Montgomery's vision, is contagious.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Eliza's Pessimism** provides the starkest contrast to hope. Described as "born a pessimist" (007-132), Eliza expects the worst and finds confirmation in every difficulty. Her outlook is presented not as villainy but as deprivation — she lacks the capacity for hope that makes life bearable.

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Cautious Hope.** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]] tempers hope [[humor|wit]]h practicality. She "hopes" things will turn out well but hedges her hopes [[humor|wit]]h qualifications — "I hope it's good all the way through" (004-085). Her version of hope is more guarded than [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s but no less genuine.
