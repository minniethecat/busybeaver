[036-001] “Sit down, [[anne-shirley|Anne]], sit down,” said [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] in a tone but two degrees

[036-002] removed from that which [[avonlea|Avonlea]] people used at funerals. “Emily’s gone

[036-003] over to [[carmody|Carmody]] with [[rachel-lynde|Rachel Lynde]] . . . she’s struck up a lifelong

[036-004] friendship already with [[mrs-lynde|Rachel Lynde]]. Beats all how contrary women are.

[036-005] Well, Anne, my easy times are over . . . all over. It’s neatness and

[036-006] tidiness for me for the rest of my natural life, I suppose.”

[036-007] Mr. Harrison did his best to speak dolefully, but an irrepressible

[036-008] twinkle in his eye betrayed him.

[036-009] “Mr. Harrison, you are glad your wife is come back,” cried Anne,

[036-010] shaking her finger at him. “You needn’t pretend you’re not, because I

[036-011] can see it plainly.”

[036-012] Mr. Harrison relaxed into a sheepish smile.

[036-013] “Well . . . well . . . I’m getting used to it,” he conceded. “I can’t

[036-014] say I was sorry to see Emily. A man really needs some protection in a

[036-015] [[neighbor|community]] like this, where he can’t play a game of checkers with a

[036-016] neighbor without being accused of wanting to marry that neighbor’s

[036-017] sister and having it put in the paper.”

[036-018] “Nobody would have supposed you went to see Isabella Andrews if you

[036-019] hadn’t pretended to be unmarried,” said Anne severely.

[036-020] “I didn’t pretend I was. If anybody’d have asked me if I was married

[036-021] I’d have said I was. But they just took it for granted. I wasn’t

[036-022] anxious to talk about the matter . . . I was feeling too sore over it.

[036-023] It would have been nuts for [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] if she had known my wife

[036-024] had left me, wouldn’t it now?”

[036-025] “But some people say that you left her.”

[036-026] “She started it, Anne, she started it. I’m going to tell you the whole

[036-027] story, for I don’t want you to think worse of me than I deserve . . .

[036-028] nor of Emily neither. But let’s go out on the veranda. Everything is so

[036-029] fearful neat in here that it kind of makes me homesick. I suppose I’ll

[036-030] get used to it after awhile but it eases me up to look at the yard.

[036-031] Emily hasn’t had time to tidy _it_ up yet.”

[036-032] As soon as they were comfortably seated on the veranda Mr. Harrison

[036-033] began his tale of woe.

[036-034] “I lived in Scottsford, New Brunswick, before I came here, Anne. My

[036-035] sister kept house for me and she suited me fine; she was just

[036-036] reasonably tidy and she let me alone and spoiled me . . . so Emily

[036-037] says. But three years ago she died. Before she died she worried a lot

[036-038] about what was to become of me and finally she got me to promise I’d

[036-039] get married. She advised me to take Emily Scott because Emily had money

[036-040] of her own and was a pattern housekeeper. I said, says I, ‘Emily Scott

[036-041] wouldn’t look at me.’ ‘You ask her and see,’ says my sister; and just

[036-042] to ease her mind I promised her I would . . . and I did. And Emily said

[036-043] she’d have me. Never was so surprised in my life, Anne . . . a smart

[036-044] pretty little woman like her and an old fellow like me. I tell you I

[036-045] thought at first I was in luck. Well, we were married and took a little

[036-046] wedding trip to St. John for a fortnight and then we went [[belonging|home]]. We got

[036-047] home at ten o’clock at night, and I give you my word, Anne, that in

[036-048] half an hour that woman was at work housecleaning. Oh, I know you’re

[036-049] thinking my house needed it . . . you’ve got a very expressive face,

[036-050] Anne; your thoughts just come out on it like print . . . but it didn’t,

[036-051] not that bad. It had got pretty mixed up while I was keeping bachelor’s

[036-052] hall, I admit, but I’d got a woman to come in and clean it up before I

[036-053] was married and there’d been considerable painting and fixing done. I

[036-054] tell you if you took Emily into a brand new white marble palace she’d

[036-055] be into the scrubbing as soon as she could get an old dress on. Well,

[036-056] she cleaned house till one o’clock that night and at four she was up

[036-057] and at it again. And she kept on that way . . . far’s I could see she

[036-058] never stopped. It was scour and sweep and dust everlasting, except on

[036-059] Sundays, and then she was just [[envy|longing]] for Monday to begin again. But

[036-060] it was her way of amusing herself and I could have reconciled myself to

[036-061] it if she’d left me alone. But that she wouldn’t do. She’d set out to

[036-062] make me over but she hadn’t caught me young enough. I wasn’t allowed to

[036-063] come into the house unless I changed my boots for slippers at the door.

[036-064] I darsn’t smoke a pipe for my life unless I went to the barn. And I

[036-065] didn’t use good enough grammar. Emily’d been a schoolteacher in her

[036-066] early life and she’d never got over it. Then she hated to see me eating

[036-067] with my knife. Well, there it was, pick and nag everlasting. But I

[036-068] s’pose, Anne, to be fair, _I_ was cantankerous too. I didn’t try to

[036-069] improve as I might have done . . . I just got cranky and disagreeable

[036-070] when she found fault. I told her one day she hadn’t complained of my

[036-071] grammar when I proposed to her. It wasn’t an overly tactful thing to

[036-072] say. A woman would forgive a man for beating her sooner than for

[036-073] hinting she was too much pleased to get him. Well, we bickered along

[036-074] like that and it wasn’t exactly pleasant, but we might have got used to

[036-075] each other after a spell if it hadn’t been for Ginger. Ginger was the

[036-076] rock we split on at last. Emily didn’t like parrots and she couldn’t

[036-077] stand Ginger’s profane habits of speech. I was attached to the bird for

[036-078] my brother the sailor’s sake. My brother the sailor was a pet of mine

[036-079] when we were little tads and he’d sent Ginger to me when he was dying.

[036-080] I didn’t see any sense in getting worked up over his swearing. There’s

[036-081] nothing I hate worse’n profanity in a human being, but in a parrot,

[036-082] that’s just repeating what it’s heard with no more [[wisdom|understanding]] of it

[036-083] than I’d have of Chinese, allowances might be made. But Emily couldn’t

[036-084] see it that way. Women ain’t logical. She tried to break Ginger of

[036-085] swearing but she hadn’t any better success than she had in trying to

[036-086] make me stop saying ‘I seen’ and ‘them things.’ Seemed as if the more

[036-087] she tried the worse Ginger got, same as me.

[036-088] “Well, things went on like this, both of us getting raspier, till the

[036-089] cli_max_ came. Emily invited our minister and his wife to tea, and

[036-090] another minister and _his_ wife that was visiting them. I’d promised to

[036-091] put Ginger away in some safe place where nobody would hear him . . .

[036-092] Emily wouldn’t touch his cage with a ten-foot pole . . . and I meant to

[036-093] do it, for I didn’t want the ministers to hear anything unpleasant in

[036-094] my house. But it slipped my mind . . . Emily was worrying me so much

[036-095] about clean collars and grammar that it wasn’t any wonder . . . and I

[036-096] never thought of that poor parrot till we sat down to tea. Just as

[036-097] minister number one was in the very middle of saying grace, Ginger, who

[036-098] was on the veranda outside the dining room window, lifted up _his_

[036-099] voice. The gobbler had come into view in the yard and the sight of a

[036-100] gobbler always had an unwholesome effect on Ginger. He surpassed

[036-101] himself that time. You can smile, Anne, and I don’t deny I’ve chuckled

[036-102] some over it since myself, but at the time I felt almost as much

[036-103] mortified as Emily. I went out and carried Ginger to the barn. I can’t

[036-104] say I enjoyed the meal. I knew by the look of Emily that there was

[036-105] trouble brewing for Ginger and James A. When the folks went away I

[036-106] started for the cow pasture and on the way I did some thinking. I felt

[036-107] sorry for Emily and kind of fancied I hadn’t been so thoughtful of her

[036-108] as I might; and besides, I wondered if the ministers would think that

[036-109] Ginger had learned his vocabulary from _me_. The long and short of it

[036-110] was, I decided that Ginger would have to be mercifully disposed of and

[036-111] when I’d druv the cows home I went in to tell Emily so. But there was

[036-112] no Emily and there _was_ a letter on the table . . . just according to

[036-113] the rule in story books. Emily writ that I’d have to choose between her

[036-114] and Ginger; she’d gone back to her own house and there she would stay

[036-115] till I went and told her I’d got rid of that parrot.

[036-116] “I was all riled up, Anne, and I said she might stay till doomsday if

[036-117] she waited for that; and I stuck to it. I packed up her belongings and

[036-118] sent them after her. It made an awful lot of talk . . . Scottsford was

[036-119] pretty near as bad as Avonlea for gossip . . . and everybody

[036-120] sympathized with Emily. It kept me all cross and cantankerous and I saw

[036-121] I’d have to get out or I’d never have any peace. I concluded I’d come

[036-122] to [[prince-edward-island|the Island]]. I’d been here when I was a boy and I liked it; but Emily

[036-123] had always said she wouldn’t live in a place where folks were scared to

[036-124] walk out after dark for fear they’d fall off the edge. So, just to be

[036-125] contrary, I moved over here. And that’s all there is to it. I hadn’t

[036-126] ever heard a word from or about Emily till I come home from the back

[036-127] field Saturday and found her scrubbing the floor but with the first

[036-128] decent dinner I’d had since she left me all ready on the table. She

[036-129] told me to eat it first and then we’d talk . . . by which I concluded

[036-130] that Emily had learned some lessons about getting along with a man. So

[036-131] she’s here and she’s going to stay . . . seeing that Ginger’s dead and

[036-132] the Island’s some bigger than she thought. There’s Mrs. Lynde and her

[036-133] now. No, don’t go, Anne. Stay and get acquainted with Emily. She took

[036-134] quite a notion to you Saturday . . . wanted to know who that handsome

[036-135] redhaired girl was at the next house.”

[036-136] [[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]] welcomed Anne radiantly and insisted on her staying to

[036-137] tea.

[036-138] “James A. has been telling me all about you and how kind you’ve been,

[036-139] making cakes and things for him,” she said. “I want to get acquainted

[036-140] with all my new [[community|neighbors]] just as soon as possible. Mrs. Lynde is a

[036-141] lovely woman, isn’t she? So friendly.”

[036-142] When Anne went home in the sweet June dusk, Mrs. Harrison went with her

[036-143] across the fields where the fireflies were lighting their starry lamps.

[036-144] “I suppose,” said Mrs. Harrison confidentially, “that James A. has told

[036-145] you our story?”

[036-146] “Yes.”

[036-147] “Then I needn’t tell it, for James A. is a just man and he would tell

[036-148] the truth. The blame was far from being all on his side. I can see that

[036-149] now. I wasn’t back in my own house an hour before I wished I hadn’t

[036-150] been so hasty but I wouldn’t give in. I see now that I expected too

[036-151] much of a man. And I was real foolish to mind his bad grammar. It

[036-152] doesn’t matter if a man does use bad grammar so long as he is a good

[036-153] provider and doesn’t go poking round the pantry to see how much sugar

[036-154] you’ve used in a week. I feel that James A. and I are going to be real

[036-155] happy now. I wish I knew who ‘Observer’ is, so that I could thank him.

[036-156] I owe him a real debt of gratitude.”
