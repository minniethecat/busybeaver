# Lorenzo White

**Lorenzo White** is a farmer of [[white-sands|White Sands]] whose reputation for stinginess is legendary in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] — and whose one moment of extravagant [[generous|generosity]], prompted by the birth of a son after seven daughters, provides [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] [[humor|wit]]h a canvassing triumph for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Avonlea Village Improvement Society]].

## Character Overview

Lorenzo White is introduced as a man "dreadfully mean" [008-125] — a judgment the narrator presents as established [[neighbor|community]] consensus. Yet Montgomery immediately complicates this portrait: Lorenzo's meanness is, in this particular moment, overridden by paternal pride. The birth of a son after seven daughters has made him "subscribe to _anything_ just now" [008-125], and [[anne-shirley|Anne]], [[humor|wit]]h her characteristic blend of shrewdness and charm, seizes the opportunity.

## Role in Story

Lorenzo White appears in a single scene during [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s canvassing expedition for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] subscription drive. His function is twofold: as a comic portrait of country frugality temporarily suspended by happiness, and as a demonstration of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s practical wisdom — her instinct to strike while the iron is hot yields the largest single subscription to the Society.

## Key Scenes

### The Canvassing Victory

> [008-125] Lorenzo White is dreadfully mean but he will subscribe to _anything_ just now. We mustn't let such a golden opportunity slip, Diana.

Despite [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s objection that Lorenzo lives out of their way on [[white-sands-road|the White Sands road]] [008-121–122], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] insists on visiting immediately: the birth of his son has created a brief window of [[generous|generosity]] that will not last [008-124–126]. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s strategic thinking is vindicated:

> [008-127] Mr. White met them in the yard, beaming like the sun upon an Easter day.

Lorenzo not only subscribes but insists on being the largest donor: "Just put me down for a dollar more than the highest subscription you've got" [008-130–131] — five dollars, paid on the spot [008-134].

### The New Son

> [008-142] The baby _was_ pretty, however, and Mr. White felt that he got his five dollars' worth of the girls' honest delight over the plump little newcomer.

Lorenzo's pride in his new son transforms the transaction from a charitable canvass into something closer to a celebration. The girls' genuine admiration gives him "his five dollars' worth" [008-142–143], a phrase that subtly acknowledges that even this moment of [[generous|generosity]] operates [[humor|wit]]hin Lorenzo's habitual accounting framework.

> [008-144–145] But that was the first, last, and only time that Lorenzo White ever subscribed to anything.

The narrator's closing note restores Lorenzo to his established character: the moment of paternal extravagance was an aberration, not a conversion. The triple emphasis — "first, last, and only" — is a comic coda that confirms the [[neighbor|community]]'s assessment: Lorenzo White is, and remains, "dreadfully mean."

## Character Analysis

Lorenzo White is a small masterpiece of Montgomery's comic portraiture. In barely twenty paragraphs, she sketches a man whose defining trait — stinginess — is momentarily suspended by an emotion that even he cannot resist. Yet the suspension is precisely that: temporary. The line "that was the first, last, and only time that Lorenzo White ever subscribed to anything" [008-144–145] closes the portrait [[humor|wit]]h [[love|affection]]ate finality, restoring the man to his reputation while allowing the reader to have [[humor|wit]]nessed — and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] to have profited from — his one bright moment of [[generous|generosity]].
