# The Catechism

The Catechism — specifically the Westminster Shorter Catechism used by the Presbyterian Church — is a central referenced work in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*. It serves as the foundation of religious [[teaching|education]] for the children of [[avonlea|Avonlea]], most memorably for [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], whose literal-minded engagement [[humor|wit]]h its doctrines generates both [[humor|comedy]] and genuine theological inquiry.

## About the Work

The Westminster Shorter Catechism is a Protestant doctrinal text produced by the Westminster Assembly in the 1640s. Organized as a series of questions and answers, it covers fundamental Christian doctrines including the [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of God, sin, salvation, the Ten Commandments, the Lord's Prayer, and the sacraments. By Montgomery's time, catechism memorization was a standard element of Presbyterian religious [[teaching|education]], and children in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] would be expected to learn and recite its questions and answers.

In the novel, the catechism is referenced primarily through [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]'s [[teaching|education]]al regime: "[[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora]] were taught a hymn, a catechism question, and two Bible verses every Sunday." The specific question [[davy-keith|Davy]] fixates on involves the [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of repentance and punishment — a doctrine he finds deeply unsatisfactory given his own experience of being punished for misbehavior. [026-175]

## Relevance to the Story

The catechism enters the narrative largely through [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s engagement [[humor|wit]]h it. He is described as knowing "the catechism" and, characteristically, using it to advance his own theological arguments. When confronted [[humor|wit]]h the doctrine that punishment atones for wrongdoing, [[davy-keith|Davy]] argues that punishment received negates the need for repentance — a logic that horrifies [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] but reflects [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s practical, transactional approach to morality.

This engagement [[humor|wit]]h the catechism serves multiple narrative purposes. It establishes the religious context of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] life, develops [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s character as a literal-minded but intellectually lively child, and provides moments of [[laughter|humor]] that also carry thematic weight about the relationship between doctrine and lived experience.

[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s engagement [[humor|wit]]h the catechism also manifests in his broader questions about God, prayer, and morality. He asks [[anne-shirley|Anne]] whether God is like [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]], discusses the mechanics of prayer, and seeks to understand divine justice through the lens of his own experiences of punishment and reward. These questions, filtered through the framework of catechism study, reveal a child genuinely wrestling [[humor|wit]]h theological concepts rather than merely memorizing them.

## Significance

The catechism in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* represents the intersection of formal religious [[teaching|education]] and lived experience. [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s creative (mis)interpretations of its doctrines expose the gap between theological abstraction and the messy reality of childhood morality. The catechism is meant to provide clear answers, but [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s engagement [[humor|wit]]h it generates more questions — a pattern Montgomery treats [[humor|wit]]h [[love|affection]]ate [[laughter|humor]] rather than censure.

The catechism also situates [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[humor|wit]]hin a specific religious tradition. The Presbyterian Church's emphasis on doctrinal literacy, expressed through catechism memorization, is shown to be a formative influence on [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s children — even when, as [[humor|wit]]h [[davy-keith|Davy]], the formation takes unexpected directions. The catechism is thus both a cultural artifact of Presbyterian [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] and a character-development tool through which Montgomery explores the tension between institutional religion and individual conscience.
