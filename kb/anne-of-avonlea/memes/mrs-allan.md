# Mrs. Allan

The minister's wife whose friendship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] offers a vision of adult womanhood in which imagination, duty, and [[neighbor|community]] coexist harmoniously — a model that helps [[anne-shirley|Anne]] envision her own future.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Allan is one of the novel's most quietly influential characters. As the minister's wife, she occupies a position of moral authority in [[avonlea|Avonlea]], but she wears that authority lightly. Her conversations [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] are characterized by genuine intellectual exchange rather than condescension — she shares [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "[[ambition|dreams]] of friendship and ambition" (021-136) as an equal, not a superior.

Her discussion of friendship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] provides the novel's most explicit philosophical treatment of the theme: "True friendship is a very helpful thing indeed . . . and we ought to be very careful not to defile it" (021-118). She distinguishes sharply between "real friendship" and intimacy that is "desecration to call *that* friendship" (021-127).

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Allan functions as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s mentor and model. She demonstrates that a woman can be married, religious, and socially prominent [[humor|wit]]hout sacrificing the qualities [[anne-shirley|Anne]] values most — imagination, intellectual curiosity, and the capacity for deep friendship. Her conversations [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] are among the novel's most intellectually substantive passages.

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Mrs. Allan's friend and intellectual companion. Their relationship models intergenerational female friendship based on mutual respect.

**[[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]]** is Mrs. Allan's husband, [[mr-allan|the minister]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s Presbyterian church.

## Key Scenes

**The Conversation [[About]] [[friendship|Friendship]] (Chapter 21).** Mrs. Allan and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s discussion of friendship — including the danger of "desecration to call *that* friendship" (021-127) when intimacy lacks sincerity — is the novel's philosophical center on relationships.

**Dreams Shared (Chapter 21).** The two women share "[[ambition|dreams]] of friendship and ambition" (021-136), Mrs. Allan treating [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[ambition|aspiration]]s [[humor|wit]]h the seriousness they deserve.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Allan is the novel's most complete model of integrated adulthood. She has not sacrificed imagination to duty or [[love|romance]] to practicality; she embodies the harmony [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is [[education|learning]] to achieve. Her presence is brief but significant — she represents the woman [[anne-shirley|Anne]] might become.
