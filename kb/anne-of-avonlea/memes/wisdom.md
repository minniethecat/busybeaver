# Wisdom

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, wisdom appears in both its humble and its ironic forms — the "innocent wisdom" of children (041-086) and the "maddening air of superior wisdom" (043-109) that Montgomery gently satirizes in adults who presume too much.

## Definition

Montgomery treats wisdom not as a fixed possession but as a quality distributed unevenly across age and station. The novel twice draws the reader's attention to the word — once in reference to "innocent wisdom, lore of" (041-086), suggesting that the young possess an intuitive understanding that eludes adults, and once in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s acerbic observation about "that maddening air of superior wisdom always assumed by those who" offer unsolicited advice (043-109). The juxtaposition is deliberate: true wisdom is associated [[humor|wit]]h innocence and simplicity, while the *performance* of wisdom is satirized.

[[anne-shirley|Anne]] herself grows in wisdom through the novel — not through formal [[teaching|instruction]] but through the accumulated experience of teaching, managing disappointments, and [[education|learning]] to read people. Her classroom management evolves from the impetuous confrontation [[humor|wit]]h [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] to the patient guidance of pupils like [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]. Montgomery suggests that wisdom is a by-product of living attentively, not a credential one can claim.

## Examples in the Text

**Innocent Wisdom (Chapter 41).** The phrase "innocent wisdom, lore of" (041-086) connects childlike insight [[humor|wit]]h accumulated folk knowledge. Montgomery implies that wisdom in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] is not the province of the formally educated but of those — young and old — who have paid close attention to the rhythms of life and [[neighbor|community]].

**The Performance of Wisdom (Chapter 43).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] encounters the "maddening air of superior wisdom always assumed by those who" dispense advice [[humor|wit]]hout understanding (043-109). Montgomery's narrative voice is sharply ironic here: the people most eager to appear wise are often those least entitled to the claim. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], who rarely claims wisdom for herself, is the novel's actual exemplar.

**The Wisdom of Restraint (Chapter 4).** When "[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s pantry horrified [[anne-shirley|Anne]], but she wisely said nothing" (004-101), Montgomery shows wisdom in its practical, domestic form. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] has learned that silence is sometimes the wiser course — a lesson the younger [[anne-shirley|Anne]] of *[[green-gables|Green Gables]]* would not have absorbed. "He otherwise conducted himself in keeping" (004-045) [[humor|wit]]h the situation, demonstrating that wisdom includes knowing when *not* to speak.

## Thematic Significance

Wisdom in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is inseparable from humility. The novel consistently rewards characters who admit the limits of their understanding and gently mocks those who posture. This aligns [[humor|wit]]h Montgomery's broader thematic interest in the value of lived experience over [[education|formal education]]: book-[[education|learning]] alone — represented by the overly theoretical [[teaching|pedagogy]] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] initially brings to the classroom — is insufficient [[humor|wit]]hout the wisdom that comes from knowing one's pupils as individuals.

The novel also draws a line between inherited folk wisdom and the presumptuous generalizations of would-be authorities. [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s oral culture — the "lore" passed between [[community|neighbors]] — carries genuine insight precisely because it is communal and unpretentious.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Innocent Wisdom vs. Pedantic Authority.** The "innocent wisdom" (041-086) of children and simple folk stands in contrast to the "maddening air of superior wisdom" (043-109) of those who speak from position rather than understanding. Montgomery does not equate age or station [[humor|wit]]h wisdom — she treats it as a quality of attention, not status.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Growth vs. Static Characters.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s wisdom deepens through the novel as she learns from mistakes; contrast this [[humor|wit]]h characters like [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], whose wisdom is already mature but sometimes expressed in the very "superior" tones Montgomery satirizes. The novel suggests that wisdom is more admirable when it remains open to correction.
