[018-001] “Oh, what a dear story,” sighed [[anne-shirley|Anne]], wiping away her tears.

[018-002] “What became of Jordan?” asked Priscilla.

[018-003] “He sold the farm after Hester died and went back to Boston. Mr. Jabez

[018-004] Sloane bought the farm and hauled the little house out to the road.

[018-005] Jordan died about ten years after and he was brought [[belonging|home]] and buried

[018-006] beside Hester.”

[018-007] “I can’t understand how she could have wanted to live back here, away

[018-008] from everything,” said [[jane-andrews|Jane]].

[018-009] “Oh, I can easily understand _that_,” said Anne thoughtfully. “I

[018-010] wouldn’t want it myself for a steady thing, because, although I [[romance|love]]

[018-011] the fields and woods, I [[romance|love]] people too. But I can understand it in

[018-012] Hester. She was tired to death of the noise of the big city and the

[018-013] crowds of people always coming and going and caring nothing for her.

[018-014] She just wanted to escape from it all to some still, green, friendly

[018-015] place where she could rest. And she got just what she wanted, which is

[018-016] something very few people do, I believe. She had four beautiful years

[018-017] before she died. . . four years of perfect happiness, so I think she

[018-018] was to be envied more than pitied. And then to shut your eyes and fall

[018-019] asleep among roses, with the one you loved best on earth smiling down

[018-020] at you . . . oh, I think it was beautiful!”

[018-021] “She set out those cherry trees over there,” said [[diana-barry|Diana]]. “She told

[018-022] mother she’d never live to eat their fruit, but she wanted to think

[018-023] that something she had planted would go on living and helping to make

[018-024] the world beautiful after she was dead.”

[018-025] “I’m so glad we came this way,” said Anne, the shining-eyed. “This is

[018-026] my adopted birthday, you know, and this garden and its story is the

[018-027] birthday gift it has given me. Did your mother ever tell you what

[018-028] [[hester-gray|Hester Gray]] looked like, Diana?”

[018-029] “No . . . only just that she was pretty.”

[018-030] “I’m rather glad of that, because I can imagine what she looked like,

[018-031] without being hampered by facts. I think she was very slight and small,

[018-032] with softly curling dark hair and big, sweet, timid brown eyes, and a

[018-033] little wistful, pale face.”

[018-034] The girls left their baskets in Hester’s garden and spent the rest of

[018-035] the afternoon rambling in the woods and fields surrounding it,

[018-036] discovering many pretty nooks and lanes. When they got hungry they had

[018-037] lunch in the prettiest spot of all . . . on the steep bank of a

[018-038] gurgling brook where white birches shot up out of long feathery

[018-039] grasses. The girls sat down by the roots and did full justice to Anne’s

[018-040] dainties, even the unpoetical sandwiches being greatly appreciated by

[018-041] hearty, unspoiled appetites sharpened by all the fresh air and exercise

[018-042] they had enjoyed. Anne had brought glasses and lemonade for her guests,

[018-043] but for her own part drank cold brook water from a cup fashioned out of

[018-044] birch bark. The cup leaked, and the water tasted of earth, as brook

[018-045] water is apt to do in spring; but Anne thought it more appropriate to

[018-046] the occasion than lemonade.

[018-047] “Look do you see that poem?” she said suddenly, pointing.

[018-048] “Where?” Jane and Diana stared, as if expecting to see Runic rhymes on

[018-049] the birch trees.

[018-050] “There . . . down in the brook . . . that old green, mossy log with the

[018-051] water flowing over it in those smooth ripples that look as if they’d

[018-052] been combed, and that single shaft of sunshine falling right athwart

[018-053] it, far down into the pool. Oh, it’s the most beautiful poem I ever

[018-054] saw.”

[018-055] “I should rather call it a picture,” said Jane. “A poem is lines and

[018-056] verses.”

[018-057] “Oh dear me, no.” Anne shook her head with its fluffy wild cherry

[018-058] coronal positively. “The lines and verses are only the outward garments

[018-059] of the poem and are no more really it than your ruffles and flounces

[018-060] are _you_, Jane. The real poem is the soul within them . . . and that

[018-061] beautiful bit is the soul of an unwritten poem. It is not every day one

[018-062] sees a soul . . . even of a poem.”

[018-063] “I wonder what a soul . . . a person’s soul . . . would look like,”

[018-064] said Priscilla dreamily.

[018-065] “Like that, I should think,” answered Anne, pointing to a radiance of

[018-066] sifted sunlight streaming through a birch tree. “Only with shape and

[018-067] features of course. I like to fancy souls as being made of light. And

[018-068] some are all shot through with rosy stains and quivers . . . and some

[018-069] have a soft glitter like moonlight on the sea . . . and some are pale

[018-070] and transparent like mist at dawn.”

[018-071] “I read somewhere once that souls were like flowers,” said Priscilla.

[018-072] “Then your soul is a golden narcissus,” said Anne, “and Diana’s is like

[018-073] a red, red rose. Jane’s is an apple blossom, pink and wholesome and

[018-074] sweet.”

[018-075] “And your own is a white violet, with purple streaks in its heart,”

[018-076] finished Priscilla.

[018-077] Jane whispered to Diana that she really could not understand what they

[018-078] were talking about. Could she?

[018-079] The girls went home by the light of a calm golden sunset, their baskets

[018-080] filled with narcissus blossoms from Hester’s garden, some of which Anne

[018-081] carried to the cemetery next day and laid upon Hester’s grave. Minstrel

[018-082] robins were whistling in the firs and the frogs were singing in the

[018-083] marshes. All the basins among the hills were brimmed with topaz and

[018-084] emerald light.

[018-085] “Well, we have had a lovely time after all,” said Diana, as if she had

[018-086] hardly expected to have it when she set out.

[018-087] “It has been a truly golden day,” said Priscilla.

[018-088] “I’m really awfully fond of the woods myself,” said Jane.

[018-089] Anne said nothing. She was looking afar into the western sky and

[018-090] thinking of little Hester Gray.

[018-091] XIV

[018-092] A Danger Averted

[018-093] Anne, walking home from the post office one Friday evening, was joined

[018-094] by [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], who was as usual cumbered with all the cares of church

[018-095] and state.

[018-096] “I’ve just been down to Timothy Cotton’s to see if I could get Alice

[018-097] Louise to help me for a few days,” she said. “I had her last week, for,

[018-098] though she’s too slow to stop quick, she’s better than nobody. But

[018-099] she’s sick and can’t come. Timothy’s sitting there, too, coughing and

[018-100] complaining. He’s been dying for ten years and he’ll go on dying for

[018-101] ten years more. That kind can’t even die and have done with it . . .

[018-102] they can’t stick to anything, even to being sick, long enough to finish

[018-103] it. They’re a terrible shiftless family and what is to become of them I

[018-104] don’t know, but perhaps Providence does.”

[018-105] [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] sighed as if she rather doubted the extent of Providential

[018-106] knowledge on the subject.

[018-107] “[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] was in about her eyes again Tuesday, wasn’t she? What did the

[018-108] specialist think of them?” she continued.

[018-109] “He was much pleased,” said Anne brightly. “He says there is a great

[018-110] improvement in them and he thinks the danger of her losing her sight

[018-111] completely is past. But he says she’ll never be able to read much or do

[018-112] any fine hand-work again. How are your preparations for your bazaar

[018-113] coming on?”

[018-114] The Ladies’ Aid Society was preparing for a fair and supper, and Mrs.

[018-115] Lynde was the head and front of the enterprise.

[018-116] “Pretty well . . . and that reminds me. [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] thinks it would be

[018-117] nice to fix up a booth like an old-time kitchen and serve a supper of

[018-118] baked beans, doughnuts, pie, and so on. We’re collecting old-fashioned

[018-119] fixings everywhere. Mrs. Simon Fletcher is going to lend us her

[018-120] mother’s braided rugs and Mrs. Levi Boulter some old chairs and Aunt

[018-121] Mary Shaw will lend us her cupboard with the glass doors. I suppose

[018-122] Marilla will let us have her brass candlesticks? And we want all the

[018-123] old dishes we can get. Mrs. Allan is specially set on having a real

[018-124] blue willow ware platter if we can find one. But nobody seems to have

[018-125] one. Do you know where we could get one?”

[018-126] “Miss Josephine Barry has one. I’ll write and ask her if she’ll lend it

[018-127] for the occasion,” said Anne.

[018-128] “Well, I wish you would. I guess we’ll have the supper in about a

[018-129] fortnight’s time. [[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe Andrews]] is prophesying rain and storms for

[018-130] about that time; and that’s a pretty sure sign we’ll have fine

[018-131] weather.”

[018-132] The said “Uncle Abe,” it may be mentioned, was at least like other

[018-133] prophets in that he had small honor in his own country. He was, in

[018-134] fact, considered in the light of a standing joke, for few of his

[018-135] weather predictions were ever fulfilled. Mr. Elisha Wright, who labored

[018-136] under the impression that he was a local [[humor|wit]], used to say that nobody

[018-137] in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] ever thought of looking in [[the-newspaper|the Charlottetown dailies]] for

[018-138] weather probabilities. No; they just asked Uncle Abe what it was going

[018-139] to be tomorrow and expected the opposite. Nothing daunted, Uncle Abe

[018-140] kept on prophesying.

[018-141] “We want to have the fair over before the election comes off,”

[018-142] continued [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], “for the candidates will be sure to come and

[018-143] spend lots of money. The Tories are bribing right and left, so they

[018-144] might as well be given a chance to spend their money honestly for

[018-145] once.”

[018-146] Anne was a red-hot Conservative, out of [[faith|loyalty]] to Matthew’s memory,

[018-147] but she said nothing. She knew better than to get Mrs. Lynde started on

[018-148] politics. She had a letter for Marilla, postmarked from a town in

[018-149] British Columbia.

[018-150] “It’s probably from the children’s uncle,” she said excitedly, when she

[018-151] got home. “Oh, Marilla, I wonder what he says about them.”

[018-152] “The best plan might be to open it and see,” said Marilla curtly. A

[018-153] close observer might have thought that she was excited also, but she

[018-154] would rather have died than show it.

[018-155] Anne tore open the letter and glanced over the somewhat untidy and

[018-156] poorly written contents.

[018-157] “He says he can’t take the children this spring . . . he’s been sick

[018-158] most of the winter and his wedding is put off. He wants to know if we

[018-159] can keep them till the fall and he’ll try and take them then. We will,

[018-160] of course, won’t we Marilla?”

[018-161] “I don’t see that there is anything else for us to do,” said Marilla

[018-162] rather grimly, although she felt a secret relief. “Anyhow they’re not

[018-163] so much trouble as they were . . . or else we’ve got used to them. [[davy-keith|Davy]]

[018-164] has improved a great deal.”

[018-165] “His _manners_ are certainly much better,” said Anne cautiously, as if

[018-166] she were not prepared to say as much for his morals.

[018-167] Anne had come home from school the previous evening, to find Marilla

[018-168] away at an Aid meeting, [[dora-keith|Dora]] asleep on the kitchen sofa, and Davy in

[018-169] the sitting room closet, blissfully absorbing the contents of a jar of

[018-170] Marilla’s famous yellow plum preserves . . . “company jam,” Davy called

[018-171] it . . . which he had been forbidden to touch. He looked very guilty

[018-172] when Anne pounced on him and whisked him out of the closet.

[018-173] “Davy Keith, don’t you know that it is very wrong of you to be eating

[018-174] that jam, when you were told never to meddle with anything in _that_

[018-175] closet?”

[018-176] “Yes, I knew it was wrong,” admitted Davy uncomfortably, “but plum jam

[018-177] is awful nice, Anne. I just peeped in and it looked so good I thought

[018-178] I’d take just a weeny taste. I stuck my finger in . . .” Anne groaned .

[018-179] . . “and licked it clean. And it was so much gooder than I’d ever

[018-180] thought that I got a spoon and just _sailed in_.”

[018-181] Anne gave him such a serious lecture on the sin of stealing plum jam

[018-182] that Davy became conscience stricken and promised with repentant kisses

[018-183] never to do it again.
