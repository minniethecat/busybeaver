[031-001] That was a merry and memorable meal; and when it was over they all went

[031-002] out to the garden, lying in the glamor of sunset.

[031-003] “I do think you have the loveliest [[belonging|place]] here,” said [[diana-barry|Diana]], looking

[031-004] round her admiringly.

[031-005] “Why do you call it [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]]?” asked [[anne-shirley|Anne]].

[031-006] “[[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]],” said [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], “go into the house and bring out the

[031-007] little tin horn that is hanging over the clock shelf.”

[031-008] Charlotta the Fourth skipped off and returned with the horn.

[031-009] “Blow it, Charlotta,” commanded [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]].

[031-010] Charlotta accordingly blew, a rather raucous, strident blast. There was

[031-011] moment’s stillness . . . and then from the woods over the river came a

[031-012] multitude of fairy echoes, sweet, elusive, silvery, as if all the

[031-013] “horns of elfland” were blowing against the sunset. Anne and Diana

[031-014] exclaimed in delight.

[031-015] “Now laugh, Charlotta . . . laugh loudly.”

[031-016] Charlotta, who would probably have obeyed if Miss Lavendar had told her

[031-017] to stand on her head, climbed upon the stone bench and laughed loud and

[031-018] heartily. Back came the echoes, as if a host of pixy people were

[031-019] mimicking her [[humor|laughter]] in the purple woodlands and along the

[031-020] fir-fringed points.

[031-021] “People always admire my echoes very much,” said Miss Lavendar, as if

[031-022] the echoes were her personal property. “I [[romance|love]] them myself. They are

[031-023] very good company . . . with a little pretending. On calm evenings

[031-024] Charlotta the Fourth and I often sit out here and amuse ourselves with

[031-025] them. Charlotta, take back the horn and hang it carefully in its

[031-026] place.”

[031-027] “Why do you call her Charlotta the Fourth?” asked Diana, who was

[031-028] bursting with curiosity on this point.

[031-029] “Just to keep her from getting mixed up with other Charlottas in my

[031-030] thoughts,” said Miss Lavendar seriously. “They all look so much alike

[031-031] there’s no telling them apart. Her name isn’t really Charlotta at all.

[031-032] It is . . . let me see . . . what is it? I _think_ it’s Leonora . . .

[031-033] yes, it _is_ Leonora. You see, it is this way. When mother died ten

[031-034] years ago I couldn’t stay here alone . . . and I couldn’t afford to pay

[031-035] the wages of a grown-up girl. So I got little Charlotta Bowman to come

[031-036] and stay with me for board and clothes. Her name really was Charlotta .

[031-037] . . she was Charlotta the First. She was just thirteen. She stayed with

[031-038] me till she was sixteen and then she went away to Boston, because she

[031-039] could do better there. Her sister came to stay with me then. Her name

[031-040] was Julietta . . . Mrs. Bowman had a weakness for fancy names I think .

[031-041] . . but she looked so like Charlotta that I kept calling her that all

[031-042] the time . . .and she didn’t mind. So I just gave up trying to remember

[031-043] her right name. She was Charlotta the Second, and when she went away

[031-044] Evelina came and she was Charlotta the Third. Now I have Charlotta the

[031-045] Fourth; but when she is sixteen . . . she’s fourteen now . . . she will

[031-046] want to go to Boston too, and what I shall do then I really do not

[031-047] know. Charlotta the Fourth is the last of the Bowman girls, and the

[031-048] best. The other Charlottas always let me see that they thought it silly

[031-049] of me to pretend things but Charlotta the Fourth never does, no matter

[031-050] what she may really think. I don’t care what people think about me if

[031-051] they don’t let me see it.”

[031-052] “Well,” said Diana looking regretfully at the setting sun. “I suppose

[031-053] we must go if we want to get to Mr. Kimball’s before dark. We’ve had a

[031-054] lovely time, Miss Lewis.”

[031-055] “Won’t you come again to see me?” pleaded Miss Lavendar.

[031-056] Tall Anne put her arm about the little lady.

[031-057] “Indeed we shall,” she promised. “Now that we have discovered you we’ll

[031-058] wear out our welcome coming to see you. Yes, we must go . . . ‘we must

[031-059] tear ourselves away,’ as [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] says every time he comes to Green

[031-060] Gables.”

[031-061] “Paul Irving?” There was a subtle change in Miss Lavendar’s voice. “Who

[031-062] is he? I didn’t think there was anybody of that name in [[avonlea|Avonlea]].”

[031-063] Anne felt vexed at her own heedlessness. She had forgotten about Miss

[031-064] Lavendar’s old romance when Paul’s name slipped out.

[031-065] “He is a little pupil of mine,” she explained slowly. “He came from

[031-066] Boston last year to live with his grandmother, [[mrs-irving|Mrs. Irving]] of the shore

[031-067] road.”

[031-068] “Is he [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]’s son?” Miss Lavendar asked, bending over her

[031-069] namesake border so that her face was hidden.

[031-070] “Yes.”

[031-071] “I’m going to give you girls a bunch of lavendar apiece,” said Miss

[031-072] Lavendar brightly, as if she had not heard the answer to her question.

[031-073] “It’s very sweet, don’t you think? Mother always loved it. She planted

[031-074] these borders long ago. Father named me Lavendar because he was so fond

[031-075] of it. The very first time he saw mother was when he visited her home

[031-076] in [[grafton|East Grafton]] with her brother. He fell in [[romance|love]] with her at first

[031-077] sight; and they put him in the spare room bed to sleep and the sheets

[031-078] were scented with lavendar and he lay awake all night and thought of

[031-079] her. He always loved the scent of lavendar after that . . . and that

[031-080] was why he gave me the name. Don’t forget to come back soon, girls

[031-081] dear. We’ll be looking for you, Charlotta the Fourth and I.”

[031-082] She opened the gate under the firs for them to pass through. She looked

[031-083] suddenly old and tired; the glow and radiance had faded from her face;

[031-084] her parting smile was as sweet with ineradicable youth as ever, but

[031-085] when the girls looked back from the first curve in the lane they saw

[031-086] her sitting on the old stone bench under the silver poplar in the

[031-087] middle of the garden with her head leaning wearily on her hand.

[031-088] “She does look lonely,” said Diana softly. “We must come often to see

[031-089] her.”

[031-090] “I think her parents gave her the only right and fitting name that

[031-091] could possibly be given her,” said Anne. “If they had been so blind as

[031-092] to name her Elizabeth or Nellie or Muriel she must have been called

[031-093] Lavendar just the same, I think. It’s so suggestive of sweetness and

[031-094] old-fashioned graces and ‘silk attire.’ Now, my name just smacks of

[031-095] bread and butter, patchwork and chores.”

[031-096] “Oh, I don’t think so,” said Diana. “Anne seems to me real stately and

[031-097] like a queen. But I’d like Kerrenhappuch if it happened to be your

[031-098] name. I think people make their names nice or ugly just by what they

[031-099] are themselves. I can’t bear Josie or Gertie for names now but before I

[031-100] knew the Pye girls I thought them real pretty.”

[031-101] “That’s a lovely idea, Diana,” said Anne enthusiastically. “Living so

[031-102] that you beautify your name, even if it wasn’t beautiful to begin with

[031-103] . . . making it stand in people’s thoughts for something so lovely and

[031-104] pleasant that they never think of it by itself. Thank you, Diana.”

[031-105] XXII

[031-106] Odds and Ends

[031-107] “So you had tea at the stone house with Lavendar Lewis?” said [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]

[031-108] at the breakfast table next morning. “What is she like now? It’s over

[031-109] fifteen years since I saw her last . . . it was one Sunday in Grafton

[031-110] church. I suppose she has changed a great deal. [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], when you

[031-111] want something you can’t reach, ask to have it passed and don’t spread

[031-112] yourself over the table in that fashion. Did you ever see Paul Irving

[031-113] doing that when he was here to meals?”

[031-114] “But Paul’s arms are longer’n mine,” brumbled Davy. “They’ve had eleven

[031-115] years to grow and mine’ve only had seven. ‘Sides, I _did_ ask, but you

[031-116] and Anne was so busy talking you didn’t pay any ‘tention. ‘Sides,

[031-117] Paul’s never been here to any meal escept tea, and it’s easier to be

[031-118] p’lite at tea than at breakfast. You ain’t half as hungry. It’s an

[031-119] awful long while between supper and breakfast. Now, Anne, that spoonful

[031-120] ain’t any bigger than it was last year and _I_’m ever so much bigger.”

[031-121] “Of course, I don’t know what Miss Lavendar used to look like but I

[031-122] don’t fancy somehow that she has changed a great deal,” said Anne,

[031-123] after she had helped Davy to maple syrup, [[generosity|giving]] him two spoonfuls to

[031-124] pacify him. “Her hair is snow-white but her face is fresh and almost

[031-125] girlish, and she has the sweetest brown eyes . . . such a pretty shade

[031-126] of wood-brown with little golden glints in them . . . and her voice

[031-127] makes you think of white satin and tinkling water and fairy bells all

[031-128] mixed up together.”

[031-129] “She was reckoned a great beauty when she was a girl,” said Marilla. “I

[031-130] never knew her very well but I liked her as far as I did know her. Some

[031-131] folks thought her peculiar even then. _Davy_, if ever I catch you at

[031-132] such a trick again you’ll be made to wait for your meals till everyone

[031-133] else is done, like the French.”

[031-134] Most conversations between Anne and Marilla in the presence of the

[031-135] twins, were punctuated by these rebukes Davy-ward. In this instance,

[031-136] Davy, sad to relate, not being able to scoop up the last drops of his

[031-137] syrup with his spoon, had solved the difficulty by lifting his plate in

[031-138] both hands and applying his small pink tongue to it. Anne looked at him

[031-139] with such horrified eyes that the little sinner turned red and said,

[031-140] half shamefacedly, half defiantly,

[031-141] “There ain’t any wasted that way.”

[031-142] “People who are different from other people are always called

[031-143] peculiar,” said Anne. “And Miss Lavendar is certainly different, though

[031-144] it’s hard to say just where the difference comes in. Perhaps it is

[031-145] because she is one of those people who never grow old.”

[031-146] “One might as well grow old when all your generation do,” said Marilla,

[031-147] rather reckless of her pronouns. “If you don’t, you don’t fit in

[031-148] anywhere. Far as I can learn Lavendar Lewis has just dropped out of

[031-149] everything. She’s lived in that out of the way place until everybody

[031-150] has forgotten her. That stone house is one of the oldest on [[prince-edward-island|the Island]].

[031-151] Old Mr. Lewis built it eighty years ago when he came out from England.

[031-152] Davy, stop joggling [[dora-keith|Dora]]’s elbow. Oh, I saw you! You needn’t try to

[031-153] look innocent. What does make you behave so this morning?”

[031-154] “Maybe I got out of the wrong side of the bed,” suggested Davy. “Milty

[031-155] Boulter says if you do that things are bound to go wrong with you all

[031-156] day. His grandmother told him. But which _is_ the right side? And what

[031-157] are you to do when your bed’s against the wall? I want to know.”

[031-158] “I’ve always wondered what went wrong between Stephen Irving and

[031-159] Lavendar Lewis,” continued Marilla, ignoring Davy. “They were certainly

[031-160] engaged twenty-five years ago and then all at once it was broken off. I

[031-161] don’t know what the trouble was but it must have been something

[031-162] terrible, for he went away to the States and never come home since.”

[031-163] “Perhaps it was nothing very dreadful after all. I think the little

[031-164] things in life often make more trouble than the big things,” said Anne,

[031-165] with one of those flashes of [[wisdom|insight]] which experience could not have

[031-166] bettered. “Marilla, please don’t say anything about my being at Miss

[031-167] Lavendar’s to [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]. She’d be sure to ask a hundred questions and

[031-168] somehow I wouldn’t like it . . . nor Miss Lavendar either if she knew,

[031-169] I feel sure.”

[031-170] “I daresay Rachel would be curious,” admitted Marilla, “though she

[031-171] hasn’t as much time as she used to have for looking after other

[031-172] people’s affairs. She’s tied home now on account of Thomas; and she’s

[031-173] feeling pretty downhearted, for I think she’s beginning to lose hope of

[031-174] his ever getting better. Rachel will be left pretty lonely if anything

[031-175] happens to him, with all her children settled out west, except Eliza in

[031-176] town; and she doesn’t like her husband.”
