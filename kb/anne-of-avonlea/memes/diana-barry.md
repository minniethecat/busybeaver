# Diana Barry

[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s loyal and beautiful "[[kindred-spirits|bosom friend]]" — the steadfast companion of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s girlhood who, in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, navigates her own transition into young womanhood and first [[romance|love]].

## Character Overview

Diana Barry is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s oldest and dearest friend, the dark-haired beauty of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] whose "beauty of crimson cheek and g[[death|loss]]y black eyes" is renowned (040-055). Unlike [[anne-shirley|Anne]], Diana lacks the intense [[imagination|imaginative faculty]] that defines her friend — she is practical, [[tradition|convention]]ally minded, and less given to romantic fancy. Yet the novel presents this difference not as a deficiency but as a complement: Diana grounds [[anne-shirley|Anne]], and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] elevates Diana.

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, Diana's role evolves from that of schoolgirl companion to young woman experiencing first [[romance|love]]. Her [[love|romance]] [[humor|wit]]h [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] provides one of the novel's central emotional arcs, and her difficulty articulating her feelings — and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s empathetic [[wisdom|understanding]] of them (043-118) — is among the novel's most touching demonstrations of friendship.

## Role in the Story

Diana's primary role is as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s confidante and emotional anchor. She appears throughout the novel as the constant presence in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s life — walking to school, sharing confidences, and providing the steady friendship that sustains [[anne-shirley|Anne]] through her various trials.

Her [[love|romance]] [[humor|wit]]h [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] represents the [[tradition|convention]]al path of young [[romance|love]] — a [[romance|courtship]] that follows small-town patterns but is rendered fresh by the depth of feeling Montgomery invests in it. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s initial sadness — "she felt very sad over it" (041-098) — acknowledges that Diana's marriage will alter their friendship, but [[anne-shirley|Anne]] comes to appreciate "the [[love|romance]] in the idea" (041-098).

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Diana's "[[kindred-spirits|bosom friend]]" — the relationship that defines both their girlhoods. Their bond survives [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s new responsibilities as teacher, Diana's romantic involvement, and the natural drift that adulthood brings.

**[[fred-wright|Fred Wright]]** is Diana's suitor, a young man whose [[romance|courtship]] follows [[tradition|convention]]al [[avonlea|Avonlea]] patterns but whose genuine [[love|affection]] for Diana is clear.

**Mrs. Barry** is Diana's mother, part of the established [[avonlea|Avonlea]] social order.

**Diana's Confession (Chapter 43).** Diana struggles to explain her feelings for [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] to [[anne-shirley|Anne]], who bridges the gap [[humor|wit]]h her imagination: "What is an imagination for if not to enable you to peep at life through other people's eyes?" (043-118).

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Sadness (Chapter 41).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] acknowledges her complex feelings about Diana's [[love|romance]]: she "felt very sad over it" yet recognizes "the [[love|romance]] in the idea" (041-098).

## Character Analysis

Diana represents the [[tradition|convention]]al path that [[anne-shirley|Anne]] does not take — the beauty who follows the expected trajectory of small-town [[love|romance]] and marriage. Yet Montgomery refuses to diminish Diana for her [[tradition|convention]]ality. She is loyal, kind, and genuinely loving; her path is different from [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s, not lesser.

Diana's function is partly structural — she is the "normal" against which [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s exceptionality is measured. But she is also genuinely be[[romance|love]]d, both by [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and by the reader, and her happiness matters on its own terms.
