[004-001] “Don’t you mind that parrot,” said [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], casting a furious

[004-002] glance at Ginger. “He’s . . . he’s always talking nonsense. I got him

[004-003] from my brother who was a sailor. Sailors don’t always use the choicest

[004-004] language, and parrots are very imitative birds.”

[004-005] “So I should think,” said poor [[anne-shirley|Anne]], the remembrance of her errand

[004-006] quelling her [[jealousy|resentment]]. She couldn’t afford to snub Mr. Harrison under

[004-007] the circumstances, that was certain. When you had just sold a man’s

[004-008] Jersey cow offhand, without his knowledge or consent you must not mind

[004-009] if his parrot repeated uncomplimentary things. Nevertheless, the

[004-010] “redheaded snippet” was not quite so meek as she might otherwise have

[004-011] been.

[004-012] “I’ve come to confess something to you, Mr. Harrison,” she said

[004-013] resolutely. “It’s . . . it’s about . . . that Jersey cow.”

[004-014] “Bless my soul,” exclaimed Mr. Harrison nervously, “has she gone and

[004-015] broken into my oats again? Well, never mind . . . never mind if she

[004-016] has. It’s no difference . . . none at all, I . . . I was too hasty

[004-017] yesterday, that’s a fact. Never mind if she has.”

[004-018] “Oh, if it were only that,” sighed Anne. “But it’s ten times worse. I

[004-019] don’t . . .”

[004-020] “Bless my soul, do you mean to say she’s got into my wheat?”

[004-021] “No . . . no . . . not the wheat. But . . .”

[004-022] “Then it’s the cabbages! She’s broken into my cabbages that I was

[004-023] raising for Exhibition, hey?”

[004-024] “It’s _not_ the cabbages, Mr. Harrison. I’ll tell you everything . . .

[004-025] that is what I came for—but please don’t interrupt me. It makes me so

[004-026] nervous. Just let me tell my story and don’t say anything till I get

[004-027] through—and then no doubt you’ll say plenty,” Anne concluded, but in

[004-028] thought only.

[004-029] “I won’t say another word,” said Mr. Harrison, and he didn’t. But

[004-030] Ginger was not bound by any contract of silence and kept ejaculating,

[004-031] “Redheaded snippet” at intervals until Anne felt quite wild.

[004-032] “I shut my Jersey cow up in our pen yesterday. This morning I went to

[004-033] [[carmody|Carmody]] and when I came back I saw a Jersey cow in your oats. [[diana-barry|Diana]] and

[004-034] I chased her out and you can’t imagine what a hard time we had. I was

[004-035] so dreadfully wet and tired and vexed—and Mr. Shearer came by that very

[004-036] minute and offered to buy the cow. I sold her to him on the spot for

[004-037] twenty dollars. It was wrong of me. I should have waited and consulted

[004-038] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], of course. But I’m dreadfully given to doing things without

[004-039] thinking—everybody who knows me will tell you that. Mr. Shearer took

[004-040] the cow right away to ship her on the afternoon train.”

[004-041] “Redheaded snippet,” quoted Ginger in a tone of profound contempt.

[004-042] At this point Mr. Harrison arose and, with an expression that would

[004-043] have struck terror into any bird but a parrot, carried Ginger’s cage

[004-044] into an adjoining room and shut the door. Ginger shrieked, swore, and

[004-045] otherwise conducted himself in keeping with his reputation, but finding

[004-046] himself left alone, relapsed into sulky silence.

[004-047] “Excuse me and go on,” said Mr. Harrison, sitting down again. “My

[004-048] brother the sailor never taught that bird any manners.”

[004-049] “I went [[belonging|home]] and after tea I went out to the milking pen. Mr.

[004-050] Harrison,” . . . Anne leaned forward, clasping her hands with her old

[004-051] childish gesture, while her big gray eyes gazed imploringly into Mr.

[004-052] Harrison’s embarrassed face . . . “I found my cow still shut up in the

[004-053] pen. It was _your_ cow I had sold to Mr. Shearer.”

[004-054] “Bless my soul,” exclaimed Mr. Harrison, in blank amazement at this

[004-055] unlooked-for conclusion. “What a _very_ extraordinary thing!”

[004-056] “Oh, it isn’t in the least extraordinary that I should be getting

[004-057] myself and other people into scrapes,” said Anne mournfully. “I’m noted

[004-058] for that. You might suppose I’d have grown out of it by this time . . .

[004-059] I’ll be seventeen next March . . . but it seems that I haven’t. Mr.

[004-060] Harrison, is it too much to hope that you’ll forgive me? I’m afraid

[004-061] it’s too late to get your cow back, but here is the money for her . . .

[004-062] or you can have mine in exchange if you’d rather. She’s a very good

[004-063] cow. And I can’t express how sorry I am for it all.”

[004-064] “Tut, tut,” said Mr. Harrison briskly, “don’t say another word about

[004-065] it, miss. It’s of no consequence . . . no consequence whatever.

[004-066] Accidents will happen. I’m too hasty myself sometimes, miss . . . far

[004-067] too hasty. But I can’t help speaking out just what I think and folks

[004-068] must take me as they find me. If that cow had been in my cabbages now .

[004-069] . . but never mind, she wasn’t, so it’s all right. I think I’d rather

[004-070] have your cow in exchange, since you want to be rid of her.”

[004-071] “Oh, thank you, Mr. Harrison. I’m so glad you are not vexed. I was

[004-072] afraid you would be.”

[004-073] “And I suppose you were scared to death to come here and tell me, after

[004-074] the fuss I made yesterday, hey? But you mustn’t mind me, I’m a terrible

[004-075] outspoken old fellow, that’s all . . . awful apt to tell the truth, no

[004-076] matter if it is a bit plain.”

[004-077] “So is [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]],” said Anne, before she could prevent herself.

[004-078] “Who? [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]? Don’t you tell me I’m like that old gossip,” said Mr.

[004-079] Harrison irritably. “I’m not . . . not a bit. What have you got in that

[004-080] box?”

[004-081] “A cake,” said Anne archly. In her relief at Mr. Harrison’s unexpected

[004-082] amiability her spirits soared upward feather-light. “I brought it over

[004-083] for you . . . I thought perhaps you didn’t have cake very often.”

[004-084] “I don’t, that’s a fact, and I’m mighty fond of it, too. I’m much

[004-085] obliged to you. It looks good on top. I hope it’s good all the way

[004-086] through.”

[004-087] “It is,” said Anne, gaily confident. “I have made cakes in my time that

[004-088] were _not_, as [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] could tell you, but this one is all right. I

[004-089] made it for the Improvement Society, but I can make another for them.”

[004-090] “Well, I’ll tell you what, miss, you must help me eat it. I’ll put the

[004-091] kettle on and we’ll have a cup of tea. How will that do?”

[004-092] “Will you let me make the tea?” said Anne dubiously.

[004-093] Mr. Harrison chuckled.

[004-094] “I see you haven’t much confidence in my ability to make tea. You’re

[004-095] wrong . . . I can brew up as good a jorum of tea as you ever drank. But

[004-096] go ahead yourself. Fortunately it rained last Sunday, so there’s plenty

[004-097] of clean dishes.”

[004-098] Anne hopped briskly up and went to work. She washed the teapot in

[004-099] several waters before she put the tea to steep. Then she swept the

[004-100] stove and set the table, bringing the dishes out of the pantry. The

[004-101] state of that pantry horrified Anne, but she wisely said nothing. Mr.

[004-102] Harrison told her where to find the bread and butter and a can of

[004-103] peaches. Anne adorned the table with a bouquet from the garden and shut

[004-104] her eyes to the stains on the tablecloth. Soon the tea was ready and

[004-105] Anne found herself sitting opposite Mr. Harrison at his own table,

[004-106] pouring his tea for him, and chatting freely to him about her school

[004-107] and friends and plans. She could hardly believe the evidence of her

[004-108] senses.

[004-109] Mr. Harrison had brought Ginger back, averring that the poor bird would

[004-110] be lonesome; and Anne, feeling that she could forgive everybody and

[004-111] everything, offered him a walnut. But Ginger’s feelings had been

[004-112] grievously hurt and he rejected all overtures of friendship. He sat

[004-113] moodily on his perch and ruffled his feathers up until he looked like a

[004-114] mere ball of green and gold.

[004-115] “Why do you call him Ginger?” asked Anne, who liked appropriate names

[004-116] and thought Ginger accorded not at all with such gorgeous plumage.

[004-117] “My brother the sailor named him. Maybe it had some reference to his

[004-118] temper. I think a lot of that bird though . . . you’d be surprised if

[004-119] you knew how much. He has his faults of course. That bird has cost me a

[004-120] good deal one way and another. Some people object to his swearing

[004-121] habits but he can’t be broken of them. I’ve tried . . . other people

[004-122] have tried. Some folks have prejudices against parrots. Silly, ain’t

[004-123] it? I like them myself. Ginger’s a lot of company to me. Nothing would

[004-124] induce me to give that bird up . . . nothing in the world, miss.”

[004-125] Mr. Harrison flung the last sentence at Anne as explosively as if he

[004-126] suspected her of some latent design of persuading him to give Ginger

[004-127] up. Anne, however, was beginning to like the queer, fussy, fidgety

[004-128] little man, and before the meal was over they were quite good friends.

[004-129] Mr. Harrison found out about the Improvement Society and was disposed

[004-130] to approve of it.

[004-131] “That’s right. Go ahead. There’s lots of room for improvement in this

[004-132] settlement . . . and in the people too.”

[004-133] “Oh, I don’t know,” flashed Anne. To herself, or to her particular

[004-134] cronies, she might admit that there were some small imperfections,

[004-135] easily removable, in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] and its inhabitants. But to hear a

[004-136] practical outsider like Mr. Harrison saying it was an entirely

[004-137] different thing. “I think Avonlea is a lovely place; and the people in

[004-138] it are very nice, too.”

[004-139] “I guess you’ve got a spice of temper,” commented Mr. Harrison,

[004-140] surveying the flushed cheeks and indignant eyes opposite him. “It goes

[004-141] with hair like yours, I reckon. Avonlea is a pretty decent place or I

[004-142] wouldn’t have located here; but I suppose even you will admit that it

[004-143] has _some_ faults?”

[004-144] “I like it all the better for them,” said loyal Anne. “I don’t like

[004-145] places or people either that haven’t any faults. I think a truly

[004-146] perfect person would be very uninteresting. Mrs. Milton White says she

[004-147] never met a perfect person, but she’s heard enough about one . . . her

[004-148] husband’s first wife. Don’t you think it must be very uncomfortable to

[004-149] be married to a man whose first wife was perfect?”

[004-150] “It would be more uncomfortable to be married to the perfect wife,”

[004-151] declared Mr. Harrison, with a sudden and inexplicable warmth.

[004-152] When tea was over Anne insisted on washing the dishes, although Mr.

[004-153] Harrison assured her that there were enough in the house to do for

[004-154] weeks yet. She would dearly have loved to sweep the floor also, but no

[004-155] broom was visible and she did not like to ask where it was for fear

[004-156] there wasn’t one at all.

[004-157] “You might run across and talk to me once in a while,” suggested Mr.

[004-158] Harrison when she was leaving. “’Tisn’t far and folks ought to be

[004-159] neighborly. I’m kind of interested in that [[community|society]] of yours. Seems to

[004-160] me there’ll be some fun in it. Who are you going to tackle first?”

[004-161] “We are not going to meddle with _people_ . . . it is only _places_ we

[004-162] mean to improve,” said Anne, in a dignified tone. She rather suspected

[004-163] that Mr. Harrison was making fun of the project.

[004-164] When she had gone Mr. Harrison watched her from the window . . . a

[004-165] lithe, girlish shape, tripping lightheartedly across the fields in the

[004-166] sunset afterglow.

[004-167] “I’m a crusty, lonesome, crabbed old chap,” he said aloud, “but there’s

[004-168] something about that little girl makes me feel young again . . . and

[004-169] it’s such a pleasant sensation I’d like to have it repeated once in a

[004-170] while.”

[004-171] “Redheaded snippet,” croaked Ginger mockingly.

[004-172] Mr. Harrison shook his fist at the parrot.

[004-173] “You ornery bird,” he muttered, “I almost wish I’d wrung your neck when

[004-174] my brother the sailor brought you home. Will you never be done getting

[004-175] me into trouble?”
