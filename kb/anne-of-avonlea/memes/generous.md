# Generous

The disposition to give more than is required or expected — tested in Mrs. Blair's failure ("she has to be just before she's generous," 008-020), modeled in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s quiet acts ("[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] would have refused but [[anne-shirley|Anne]] cut a generous second slice," 010-058), and quietly affirmed in domestic welcome ("He looked [[humor|wit]]h approval at the generous slice," 021-156).

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, generosity is not a grand virtue displayed in dramatic moments but a domestic habit expressed in small, everyday acts. The [[generosity|giving]] of food — an extra slice of bread, a larger portion of cake, a well-stocked picnic basket — serves as Montgomery's primary metaphor for a larger spiritual generosity. Those who give freely of their table also give freely of themselves.

Montgomery distinguishes true generosity from calculated [[generosity|giving]]. True generosity, in her vision, is spontaneous and unmeasured: [[anne-shirley|Anne]] does not weigh the cost when she cuts [[davy-keith|Davy]] a second slice. False generosity — or the absence of generosity — is exemplified by Mrs. Blair, who claims to be "just before she's generous" but, as [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] observes, is "so much 'before' that generosity never catches up [[humor|wit]]h her" (008-021).

## Examples in the Text

**Mrs. Blair's Stinginess (Chapter 8).** When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] canvass for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] subscription, they encounter Mrs. Blair — a woman known for her parsimony. "She says she has to be just before she's generous. But [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says she's so much 'before' that generosity never catches up [[humor|wit]]h her" (008-020–021). [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s aphorism exposes the self-deception in Mrs. Blair's motto: "just before generous" is a rationalization for never being generous at all.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Second Slice (Chapter 10).** In a domestic moment [[humor|wit]]h [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], a hungry orphan boy newly arrived at [[green-gables|Green Gables]]: "[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] would have refused but [[anne-shirley|Anne]] cut a generous second slice" (010-058). Montgomery contrasts [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s instinct toward frugal restraint [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s instinct toward openhanded [[generosity|giving]]. The act is small — a slice of bread — but it embodies the generous spirit that [[anne-shirley|Anne]] brings to all her relationships.

**A Welcoming Slice (Chapter 21).** Later in the novel, [[davy-keith|Davy]] has learned to say "please" — "but he generally tacked it on as an afterthought" — and "He looked [[humor|wit]]h approval at the generous slice [[anne-shirley|Anne]] presently brought to him" (021-156). [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s eye for portion size is comic, but the repeated motif of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s generous slicing establishes a pattern: generosity is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s default mode, not an occasional gesture.

**[[generosity|Generosity]] of Spirit (Chapter 20).** Beyond the [[generosity|giving]] of food, Montgomery explores generosity of spirit. [[mrs-irving|Paul Irving's grandmother]] gives "such generous helpings" (020-154) not only of food but of welcome, treating [[anne-shirley|Anne]] as an honored guest. [[generosity|Generosity]], Montgomery suggests, is a quality of the whole person — one who cuts generous slices of bread is also one who gives generous portions of time, attention, and care.

## Thematic Significance

[[generosity|Generosity]] in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is the opposite of calculation. Mrs. Blair's "just before generous" is a formula for never [[generosity|giving]] — a way of making stinginess sound principled. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s instinctive generosity — cutting the second slice [[humor|wit]]hout being asked, sharing what she has [[humor|wit]]hout measuring the cost — represents Montgomery's ideal of spontaneous, uncalculated [[generosity|giving]].

The novel also links generosity to [[neighbor|community]]. The generous person strengthens the social fabric; the stingy person — like Mrs. Blair — weakens it. [[generosity|Generosity]] is not merely a private virtue but a public one, essential to the health of [[community|village life]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Spontaneous [[generosity|Generosity]] vs. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Frugal Caution.** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] has spent a lifetime practicing frugality; her instinct is to refuse the extra slice. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], raised [[humor|wit]]h less material security but more emotional warmth, gives freely. Montgomery does not condemn [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] — frugality is a survival skill — but she clearly prefers [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s way.

**Mrs. Blair's Calculated Stinginess vs. [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s Practical [[generosity|Generosity]].** [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is not rich, but she gives what she can — time, advice, food, and her sharp tongue. Her generosity is practical, not sentimental. Mrs. Blair's calculated restraint, by contrast, is exposed as a failure of [[neighbor|community]] membership — she takes the benefits of [[community|village life]] [[humor|wit]]hout contributing to its informal economy of care.
