# Paul Irving

The dreamy, imaginative boy who becomes [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s youngest "[[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]]" — a pupil whose soul resonates at the same frequency as her own and whose presence validates [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s conviction that imagination is a gift to be nurtured, not corrected.

## Character Overview

Paul Irving is not an ordinary [[avonlea|Avonlea]] schoolboy. He lives [[humor|wit]]h his grandmother while his father, [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]], is in the United States, and he possesses an imaginative sensibility that sets him apart from his peers. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] recognizes him instantly as a [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]] — someone who shares her geography of the imagination, where "clouds never darkened the sunny sky" and "[[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded" (021-025).

Paul's relationship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is one of mutual recognition. She sees in him the child she was; he sees in her the adult who understands him. Their bond is intellectual and spiritual rather than merely pedagogical — they are companions in the realm "[[imagination|Imagination]] opens to the view" (021-022).

## Role in the Story

Paul serves as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imaginative mirror. His presence validates her worldview — proof that her sensibility is not a personal eccentricity but a genuine mode of perception that others can share. Through Paul, Montgomery argues that imagination is not childish fancy but a lasting capacity that enriches life at any age.

Paul also serves an important plot function: his identity as [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]'s son leads [[anne-shirley|Anne]] to [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]], setting in motion the novel's central [[love|romance]] plot. His innocent revelation — his father's name spoken in [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s presence — reopens a quarter-century-old wound and makes healing possible.

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Paul's teacher and spiritual companion. Their relationship transcends the classroom — they are fellow travelers in the geography of imagination.

**[[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]** is Paul's father, whose long-ago [[love|romance]] [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] is rekindled through Paul's un[[humor|wit]]ting intervention.

**Grandma Irving** is Paul's guardian in [[avonlea|Avonlea]], toward whom Paul feels a touching sense of "duty" to [[romance|love]] her best (020-133).

## Key Scenes

**The Realm of [[imagination|Imagination]] (Chapter 21).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and Paul share the vision of "How fair the realm / [[imagination|Imagination]] opens to the view" (021-022), a landscape where "[[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded" (021-025).

**Filial [[duty|Duty]] (Chapter 20).** Paul's declaration that "next to father if it wasn't my *duty* to [[romance|love]] Grandma Irving best" (020-133) reveals a child's wisdom about the inseparability of [[romance|love]] and [[duty|obligation]].

**The Accidental Revelation (Chapter 30).** Paul's mention of his father's name in [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s presence — and her reaction that "revealed a [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]]" (030-137) — sets the [[love|romance]] plot in motion.

## Character Analysis

Paul is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imaginative heir — the child who proves that her way of being in the world is transmissible. His presence provides hope that the imagination [[anne-shirley|Anne]] so values will survive into the next generation. He is also the agent of the novel's most significant plot development: the reunion of his father and [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]].
