# Teaching

[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s chosen profession and the central activity through which she matures from imaginative girl to responsible young woman — a vocation she approaches [[humor|wit]]h idealism, determination, and a growing conviction that teaching is as much about character as about curriculum.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, teaching is presented as a calling rather than a job. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s philosophy is articulated through her actions more than her words: she believes in finding and developing each child's potential (005-076), in winning respect before [[love|affection]], and in the power of beauty and imagination to enrich even the most routine school day. Teaching is also the crucible in which [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s character is tested — through difficult pupils, skeptical parents, and her own doubts about her suitability for the work.

The novel explores teaching from multiple angles: the novice's anxiety ("it was not she who was teaching but somebody else," 006-068), the veteran's cynicism (the previous teacher's reliance on the whip, 005-045), and the idealist's persistence. Montgomery avoids both easy triumphalism and cynical resignation, charting a middle course in which teaching is difficult, sometimes thankless, but ultimately meaningful.

## Examples in the Text

**The Novice Teacher (Chapter 6).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s early days in the classroom are marked by self-doubt. She feels "as if it were not she who was teaching but somebody else" (006-068), a dissociative sensation that captures the gap between idealism and reality. She briefly fears "she would never learn to like teaching" (006-094), yet perseveres — and discovers that perseverance transforms both the teacher and the taught.

**Teaching as Growth (Chapter 9).** In conversation [[humor|wit]]h [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] reflects that she likes "overcoming difficulties and teaching is really very interesting" (009-017). The word "interesting" is characteristically understated — for [[anne-shirley|Anne]], teaching has become an intellectual challenge as well as a moral one. [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], himself teaching to fund medical school, agrees: "I like teaching, too. It's good training" (009-022). Their exchange establishes teaching as a shared experience that deepens their bond.

**Pleasures and Pains (Chapter 14).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s evening reflection captures the duality of teaching: "I realized afresh that teaching has its pleasures as well as its pains" (014-100). The observation is both banal and profound — teaching, like all meaningful work, contains both. Montgomery validates this balanced view as the sign of maturity, contrasting it [[humor|wit]]h the romantic extremism of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s girlhood.

**The [[diana-barry|Di]]fficult Art (Chapter 14).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] identifies the core challenge: the "most difficult thing in teaching, as well as the most important" (014-063) is reaching each individual child. This philosophy — that teaching is fundamentally relational rather than instructional — guides her approach and distinguishes her from the whip-wielding predecessors.

**Busy Purpose (Chapter 12).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s life fills [[humor|wit]]h the demands of her vocation: "I'm so busy [[humor|wit]]h teaching and studying" (012-115). The statement is factual but the tone conveys satisfaction — busyness in a worthy cause is not complaint but contentment. Teaching gives [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s days structure and meaning.

## Thematic Significance

**Teaching as Moral Formation.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s pedagogy extends beyond academics to character. Her handling of [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]], her encouragement of [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s imagination, and her patience [[humor|wit]]h struggling students all reflect a belief that the teacher's duty is to develop the whole person — "a teacher's duty to find and develop" each child's potential (005-076).

**The Teacher's Own [[education|Education]].** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] learns as much as she teaches. Her pupils — especially the difficult ones — instruct her in patience, firmness, and the limits of idealism. Teaching is the vehicle of her own moral education, the means by which imagination is tempered by [[duty|responsibility]].

**Teaching and [[community|Community]].** The [[avonlea|Avonlea]] school is a microcosm of the village, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s work there [[belonging|place]]s her at the center of [[neighbor|community]] life. Through teaching, she earns the respect of parents, the [[love|affection]] of children, and a permanent [[belonging|place]] in [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s social fabric — a significant achievement for an orphan who arrived as a stranger.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Mr. Phillips (the previous teacher).** The unnamed or barely-named predecessor who ruled by the whip represents everything [[anne-shirley|Anne]] rejects. His method — "always whipping" — achieved "perfect order" but no genuine education (005-045). [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s alternative approach is defined in opposition to this model.

**[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]].** [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s parallel experience teaching at a different school provides comparison and [[friendship|companionship]]. His comment that "it's good training" (009-022) reflects a more pragmatic approach — teaching as preparation for other work — that contrasts [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s vocational commitment while respecting it.
