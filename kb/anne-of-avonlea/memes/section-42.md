[042-001] “I wonder how much you know,” he said.

[042-002] “I know all about it,” replied [[anne-shirley|Anne]] promptly. “You see,” she explained

[042-003] hastily, “[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] and I are very intimate. She wouldn’t tell

[042-004] things of such a sacred [[nature-appreciation|nature]] to everybody. We are [[friendship|kindred spirits]].”

[042-005] “Yes, I believe you are. Well, I am going to ask a favor of you. I

[042-006] would like to go and see [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] if she will let me. Will you ask

[042-007] her if I may come?”

[042-008] Would she not? Oh, indeed she would! Yes, this was romance, the very,

[042-009] the real thing, with all the charm of rhyme and story and dream. It was

[042-010] a little belated, perhaps, like a rose blooming in October which should

[042-011] have bloomed in June; but none the less a rose, all sweetness and

[042-012] fragrance, with the gleam of gold in its heart. Never did Anne’s feet

[042-013] bear her on a more willing errand than on that walk through the

[042-014] beechwoods to [[grafton|Grafton]] the next morning. She found Miss Lavendar in the

[042-015] garden. Anne was fearfully excited. Her hands grew cold and her voice

[042-016] trembled.

[042-017] “Miss Lavendar, I have something to tell you . . . something very

[042-018] important. Can you guess what it is?”

[042-019] Anne never supposed that Miss Lavendar _could_ guess; but Miss

[042-020] Lavendar’s face grew very pale and Miss Lavendar said in a quiet, still

[042-021] voice, from which all the color and sparkle that Miss Lavendar’s voice

[042-022] usually suggested had faded.

[042-023] “[[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] is [[belonging|home]]?”

[042-024] “How did you know? Who told you?” cried Anne disappointedly, vexed that

[042-025] her great revelation had been anticipated.

[042-026] “Nobody. I knew that must be it, just from the way you spoke.”

[042-027] “He wants to come and see you,” said Anne. “May I send him word that he

[042-028] may?”

[042-029] “Yes, of course,” fluttered Miss Lavendar. “There is no reason why he

[042-030] shouldn’t. He is only coming as any old friend might.”

[042-031] Anne had her own opinion about that as she hastened into the house to

[042-032] write a note at Miss Lavendar’s desk.

[042-033] “Oh, it’s delightful to be living in a storybook,” she thought gaily.

[042-034] “It will come out all right of course . . . it must . . . and [[paul-irving|Paul]] will

[042-035] have a mother after his own heart and everybody will be happy. But Mr.

[042-036] Irving will take Miss Lavendar away . . . and dear knows what will

[042-037] happen to the little stone house . . . and so there are two sides to

[042-038] it, as there seems to be to everything in this world.” The important

[042-039] note was written and Anne herself carried it to the Grafton post

[042-040] office, where she waylaid the mail carrier and asked him to leave it at

[042-041] the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] office.

[042-042] “It’s so very important,” Anne assured him anxiously. The mail carrier

[042-043] was a rather grumpy old personage who did not at all look the part of a

[042-044] messenger of Cupid; and Anne was none too certain that his memory was

[042-045] to be trusted. But he said he would do his best to remember and she had

[042-046] to be contented with that.

[042-047] [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] felt that some mystery pervaded the stone house

[042-048] that afternoon . . . a mystery from which she was excluded. Miss

[042-049] Lavendar roamed about the garden in a distracted fashion. Anne, too,

[042-050] seemed possessed by a demon of unrest, and walked to and fro and went

[042-051] up and down. Charlotta the Fourth endured it till patience ceased to be

[042-052] a virtue; then she confronted Anne on the occasion of that romantic

[042-053] young person’s third aimless peregrination through the kitchen.

[042-054] “Please, Miss Shirley, ma’am,” said Charlotta the Fourth, with an

[042-055] indignant toss of her very blue bows, “it’s plain to be seen you and

[042-056] Miss Lavendar have got a secret and I think, begging your [[forgiveness|pardon]] if I’m

[042-057] too forward, Miss Shirley, ma’am, that it’s real mean not to tell me

[042-058] when we’ve all been such chums.”

[042-059] “Oh, Charlotta dear, I’d have told you all about it if it were my

[042-060] secret . . . but it’s Miss Lavendar’s, you see. However, I’ll tell you

[042-061] this much . . . and if nothing comes of it you must never breathe a

[042-062] word about it to a living soul. You see, Prince Charming is coming

[042-063] tonight. He came long ago, but in a foolish moment went away and

[042-064] wandered afar and forgot the secret of the magic pathway to the

[042-065] enchanted castle, where the princess was weeping her faithful heart out

[042-066] for him. But at last he remembered it again and the princess is waiting

[042-067] still. . . because nobody but her own dear prince could carry her off.”

[042-068] “Oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am, what is that in prose?” gasped the mystified

[042-069] Charlotta.

[042-070] Anne laughed.

[042-071] “In prose, an old friend of Miss Lavendar’s is coming to see her

[042-072] tonight.”

[042-073] “Do you mean an old beau of hers?” demanded the literal Charlotta.

[042-074] “That is probably what I do mean . . . in prose,” answered Anne

[042-075] gravely. “It is Paul’s father . . . Stephen Irving. And goodness knows

[042-076] what will come of it, but let us hope for the best, Charlotta.”

[042-077] “I hope that he’ll marry Miss Lavendar,” was Charlotta’s unequivocal

[042-078] response. “Some women’s intended from the start to be old maids, and

[042-079] I’m afraid I’m one of them, Miss Shirley, ma’am, because I’ve awful

[042-080] little patience with the men. But Miss Lavendar never was. And I’ve

[042-081] been awful worried, thinking what on earth she’d do when I got so big

[042-082] I’d _have_ to go to Boston. There ain’t any more girls in our family

[042-083] and dear knows what she’d do if she got some stranger that might laugh

[042-084] at her pretendings and leave things lying round out of their place and

[042-085] not be willing to be called Charlotta the Fifth. She might get someone

[042-086] who wouldn’t be as unlucky as me in breaking dishes but she’d never get

[042-087] anyone who’d [[romance|love]] her better.”

[042-088] And the faithful little handmaiden dashed to the oven door with a

[042-089] sniff.

[042-090] They went through the form of having tea as usual that night at Echo

[042-091] Lodge; but nobody really ate anything. After tea Miss Lavendar went to

[042-092] her room and put on her new forget-me-not organdy, while Anne did her

[042-093] hair for her. Both were dreadfully excited; but Miss Lavendar pretended

[042-094] to be very calm and indifferent.

[042-095] “I must really mend that rent in the curtain tomorrow,” she said

[042-096] anxiously, inspecting it as if it were the only thing of any importance

[042-097] just then. “Those curtains have not worn as well as they should,

[042-098] considering the price I paid. Dear me, Charlotta has forgotten to dust

[042-099] the stair railing _again_. I really _must_ speak to her about it.”

[042-100] Anne was sitting on the porch steps when Stephen Irving came down the

[042-101] lane and across the garden.

[042-102] “This is the one place where time stands still,” he said, looking

[042-103] around him with delighted eyes. “There is nothing changed about this

[042-104] house or garden since I was here twenty-five years ago. It makes me

[042-105] feel young again.”

[042-106] “You know time always does stand still in an enchanted palace,” said

[042-107] Anne seriously. “It is only when the prince comes that things begin to

[042-108] happen.”

[042-109] Mr. Irving smiled a little sadly into her uplifted face, all astar with

[042-110] its youth and promise.

[042-111] “Sometimes the prince comes too late,” he said. He did not ask Anne to

[042-112] translate her remark into prose. Like all kindred spirits he

[042-113] “understood.”

[042-114] “Oh, no, not if he is the real prince coming to the true princess,”

[042-115] said Anne, shaking her red head decidedly, as she opened the parlor

[042-116] door. When he had gone in she shut it tightly behind him and turned to

[042-117] confront Charlotta the Fourth, who was in the hall, all “nods and becks

[042-118] and wreathed smiles.”

[042-119] “Oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am,” she breathed, “I peeked from the kitchen

[042-120] window . . . and he’s awful handsome . . . and just the right age for

[042-121] Miss Lavendar. And oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am, do you think it would be

[042-122] much harm to listen at the door?”

[042-123] “It would be dreadful, Charlotta,” said Anne firmly, “so just you come

[042-124] away with me out of the reach of temptation.”

[042-125] “I can’t do anything, and it’s awful to hang round just waiting,”

[042-126] sighed Charlotta. “What if he don’t propose after all, Miss Shirley,

[042-127] ma’am? You can never be sure of them men. My older sister, Charlotta

[042-128] the First, thought she was engaged to one once. But it turned out _he_

[042-129] had a different opinion and she says she’ll never [[faith|trust]] one of them

[042-130] again. And I heard of another case where a man thought he wanted one

[042-131] girl awful bad when it was really her sister he wanted all the time.

[042-132] When a man don’t know his own mind, Miss Shirley, ma’am, how’s a poor

[042-133] woman going to be sure of it?”

[042-134] “We’ll go to the kitchen and clean the silver spoons,” said Anne.

[042-135] “That’s a task which won’t require much thinking fortunately . . . for

[042-136] I _couldn’t_ think tonight. And it will pass the time.”

[042-137] It passed an hour. Then, just as Anne laid down the last shining spoon,

[042-138] they heard the front door shut. Both sought comfort fearfully in each

[042-139] other’s eyes.

[042-140] “Oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am,” gasped Charlotta, “if he’s going away this

[042-141] early there’s nothing into it and never will be.” They flew to the

[042-142] window. Mr. Irving had no intention of going away. He and Miss Lavendar

[042-143] were strolling slowly down the middle path to the stone bench.

[042-144] “Oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am, he’s got his arm around her waist,” whispered

[042-145] Charlotta the Fourth delightedly. “He _must_ have proposed to her or

[042-146] she’d never allow it.”

[042-147] Anne caught Charlotta the Fourth by her own plump waist and danced her

[042-148] around the kitchen until they were both out of breath.

[042-149] “Oh, Charlotta,” she cried gaily, “I’m neither a prophetess nor the

[042-150] daughter of a prophetess but I’m going to make a prediction. There’ll

[042-151] be a wedding in this old stone house before the maple leaves are red.

[042-152] Do you want that translated into prose, Charlotta?”

[042-153] “No, I can understand that,” said Charlotta. “A wedding ain’t poetry.

[042-154] Why, Miss Shirley, ma’am, you’re crying! What for?”

[042-155] “Oh, because it’s all so beautiful . . . and story bookish . . . and

[042-156] romantic . . . and sad,” said Anne, winking the tears out of her eyes.

[042-157] “It’s all perfectly lovely . . . but there’s a little sadness mixed up

[042-158] in it too, somehow.”

[042-159] “Oh, of course there’s a resk in marrying anybody,” conceded Charlotta

[042-160] the Fourth, “but, when all’s said and done, Miss Shirley, ma’am,

[042-161] there’s many a worse thing than a husband.”

[042-162] XXIX

[042-163] Poetry and Prose

[042-164] For the next month Anne lived in what, for Avonlea, might be called a

[042-165] whirl of excitement. The preparation of her own modest outfit for

[042-166] [[redmond-college|Redmond]] was of secondary importance. Miss Lavendar was getting ready to

[042-167] be married and the stone house was the scene of endless consultations

[042-168] and plannings and discussions, with Charlotta the Fourth hovering on

[042-169] the outskirts of things in agitated delight and wonder. Then the

[042-170] dressmaker came, and there was the rapture and wretchedness of choosing

[042-171] fashions and being fitted. Anne and [[diana-barry|Diana]] spent half their time at Echo

[042-172] Lodge and there were nights when Anne could not sleep for wondering

[042-173] whether she had done right in advising Miss Lavendar to select brown

[042-174] rather than navy blue for her traveling dress, and to have her gray

[042-175] silk made princess.

[042-176] Everybody concerned in Miss Lavendar’s story was very happy. Paul

[042-177] Irving rushed to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] to talk the news over with Anne as soon

[042-178] as his father had told him.

[042-179] “I knew I could trust father to pick me out a nice little second

[042-180] mother,” he said proudly. “It’s a fine thing to have a father you can

[042-181] depend on, teacher. I just [[romance|love]] Miss Lavendar. Grandma is pleased, too.

[042-182] She says she’s real glad father didn’t pick out an American for his

[042-183] second wife, because, although it turned out all right the first time,

[042-184] such a thing wouldn’t be likely to happen twice. [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says she

[042-185] thoroughly approves of the match and thinks its likely Miss Lavendar

[042-186] will give up her queer notions and be like other people, now that she’s

[042-187] going to be married. But I hope she won’t give her queer notions up,

[042-188] teacher, because I like them. And I don’t want her to be like other

[042-189] people. There are too many other people around as it is. _You_ know,

[042-190] teacher.”
