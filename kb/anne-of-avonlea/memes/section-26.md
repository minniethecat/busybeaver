[026-001] “Well, I’ll see,” said Miss Sarah cautiously. “That platter is mine

[026-002] fortunately, or I’d never dare to sell it when Martha wasn’t here. As

[026-003] it is, I daresay she’ll raise a fuss. Martha’s the boss of this

[026-004] establishment I can tell you. I’m getting awful tired of living under

[026-005] another woman’s thumb. But come in, come in. You must be real tired and

[026-006] hungry. I’ll do the best I can for you in the way of tea but I warn you

[026-007] not to expect anything but bread and butter and some cowcumbers. Martha

[026-008] locked up all the cake and cheese and preserves afore she went. She

[026-009] always does, because she says I’m too extravagant with them if company

[026-010] comes.”

[026-011] The girls were hungry enough to do justice to any fare, and they

[026-012] enjoyed Miss Sarah’s excellent bread and butter and “cowcumbers”

[026-013] thoroughly. When the meal was over Miss Sarah said,

[026-014] “I don’t know as I mind selling the platter. But it’s worth twenty-five

[026-015] dollars. It’s a very old platter.”

[026-016] [[diana-barry|Diana]] gave [[anne-shirley|Anne]]’s foot a gentle kick under the table, meaning, “Don’t

[026-017] agree—she’ll let it go for twenty if you hold out.” But Anne was not

[026-018] minded to take any chances in regard to that precious platter. She

[026-019] promptly agreed to give twenty-five and Miss Sarah looked as if she

[026-020] felt sorry she hadn’t asked for thirty.

[026-021] “Well, I guess you may have it. I want all the money I can scare up

[026-022] just now. The fact is—” Miss Sarah threw up her head importantly, with

[026-023] a proud flush on her thin cheeks—“I’m going to be married—to Luther

[026-024] Wallace. He wanted me twenty years ago. I liked him real well but he

[026-025] was poor then and father packed him off. I s’pose I shouldn’t have let

[026-026] him go so meek but I was timid and frightened of father. Besides, I

[026-027] didn’t know men were so skurse.”

[026-028] When the girls were safely away, Diana driving and Anne holding the

[026-029] coveted platter carefully on her lap, the green, rain-freshened

[026-030] solitudes of the Tory Road were enlivened by ripples of girlish

[026-031] [[humor|laughter]].

[026-032] “I’ll amuse your [[aunt-josephine|Aunt Josephine]] with the ‘strange eventful history’ of

[026-033] this afternoon when I go to town tomorrow. We’ve had a rather trying

[026-034] time but it’s over now. I’ve got the platter, and that rain has laid

[026-035] the dust beautifully. So ‘all’s well that ends well.’”

[026-036] “We’re not [[belonging|home]] yet,” said Diana rather pessimistically, “and there’s

[026-037] no telling what may happen before we are. You’re such a girl to have

[026-038] adventures, Anne.”

[026-039] “Having adventures comes natural to some people,” said Anne serenely.

[026-040] “You just have a gift for them or you haven’t.”

[026-041] XIX

[026-042] Just a Happy Day

[026-043] “After all,” Anne had said to [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] once, “I believe the nicest and

[026-044] sweetest days are not those on which anything very splendid or

[026-045] wonderful or exciting happens but just those that bring simple little

[026-046] pleasures, following one another softly, like pearls slipping off a

[026-047] string.”

[026-048] Life at [[green-gables|Green Gables]] was full of just such days, for Anne’s adventures

[026-049] and misadventures, like those of other people, did not all happen at

[026-050] once, but were sprinkled over the year, with long stretches of

[026-051] harmless, happy days between, filled with work and [[ambition|dreams]] and laughter

[026-052] and lessons. Such a day came late in August. In the forenoon Anne and

[026-053] Diana rowed the delighted twins down the pond to the sandshore to pick

[026-054] “sweet grass” and paddle in the surf, over which the wind was harping

[026-055] an old lyric learned when the world was young.

[026-056] In the afternoon Anne walked down to the old Irving place to see [[paul-irving|Paul]].

[026-057] She found him stretched out on the grassy bank beside the thick fir

[026-058] grove that sheltered the house on the north, absorbed in a book of

[026-059] fairy tales. He sprang up radiantly at sight of her.

[026-060] “Oh, I’m so glad you’ve come, teacher,” he said eagerly, “because

[026-061] Grandma’s away. You’ll stay and have tea with me, won’t you? It’s so

[026-062] lonesome to have tea all by oneself. _You_ know, teacher. I’ve had

[026-063] serious thoughts of asking [[mary-joe|Young Mary Joe]] to sit down and eat her tea

[026-064] with me, but I expect Grandma wouldn’t approve. She says the French

[026-065] have to be kept in their place. And anyhow, it’s difficult to talk with

[026-066] Young Mary Joe. She just laughs and says, ‘Well, yous do beat all de

[026-067] kids I ever knowed.’ That isn’t my idea of conversation.”

[026-068] “Of course I’ll stay to tea,” said Anne gaily. “I was dying to be

[026-069] asked. My mouth has been watering for some more of your grandma’s

[026-070] delicious shortbread ever since I had tea here before.”

[026-071] Paul looked very sober.

[026-072] “If it depended on me, teacher,” he said, standing before Anne with his

[026-073] hands in his pockets and his beautiful little face shadowed with sudden

[026-074] care, “You should have shortbread with a right good will. But it

[026-075] depends on Mary Joe. I heard Grandma tell her before she left that she

[026-076] wasn’t to give me any shortcake because it was too rich for little

[026-077] boys’ stomachs. But maybe Mary Joe will cut some for you if I promise I

[026-078] won’t eat any. Let us hope for the best.”

[026-079] “Yes, let us,” agreed Anne, whom this cheerful philosophy suited

[026-080] exactly, “and if Mary Joe proves hard-hearted and won’t give me any

[026-081] shortbread it doesn’t matter in the least, so you are not to worry over

[026-082] that.”

[026-083] “You’re sure you won’t mind if she doesn’t?” said Paul anxiously.

[026-084] “Perfectly sure, dear heart.”

[026-085] “Then I won’t worry,” said Paul, with a long breath of relief,

[026-086] “especially as I really think Mary Joe will listen to reason. She’s not

[026-087] a naturally unreasonable person, but she has learned by experience that

[026-088] it doesn’t do to disobey Grandma’s orders. Grandma is an excellent

[026-089] woman but people must do as she tells them. She was very much pleased

[026-090] with me this morning because I managed at last to eat all my plateful

[026-091] of porridge. It was a great effort but I succeeded. Grandma says she

[026-092] thinks she’ll make a man of me yet. But, teacher, I want to ask you a

[026-093] very important question. You will answer it truthfully, won’t you?”

[026-094] “I’ll try,” promised Anne.

[026-095] “Do you think I’m wrong in my upper story?” asked Paul, as if his very

[026-096] existence depended on her reply.

[026-097] “Goodness, no, Paul,” exclaimed Anne in amazement. “Certainly you’re

[026-098] not. What put such an idea into your head?”

[026-099] “Mary Joe . . . but she didn’t know I heard her. Mrs. [[peter-sloane|Peter Sloane]]’s

[026-100] hired girl, Veronica, came to see Mary Joe last evening and I heard

[026-101] them talking in the kitchen as I was going through the hall. I heard

[026-102] Mary Joe say, ‘Dat Paul, he is de queeres’ leetle boy. He talks dat

[026-103] queer. I tink dere’s someting wrong in his upper story.’ I couldn’t

[026-104] sleep last night for ever so long, thinking of it, and wondering if

[026-105] Mary Joe was right. I couldn’t bear to ask Grandma about it somehow,

[026-106] but I made up my mind I’d ask you. I’m so glad you think I’m all right

[026-107] in my upper story.”

[026-108] “Of course you are. Mary Joe is a silly, ignorant girl, and you are

[026-109] never to worry about anything she says,” said Anne indignantly,

[026-110] secretly resolving to give [[mrs-irving|Mrs. Irving]] a discreet hint as to the

[026-111] advisability of restraining Mary Joe’s tongue.

[026-112] “Well, that’s a weight off my mind,” said Paul. “I’m perfectly happy

[026-113] now, teacher, thanks to you. It wouldn’t be nice to have something

[026-114] wrong in your upper story, would it, teacher? I suppose the reason Mary

[026-115] Joe imagines I have is because I tell her what I think about things

[026-116] sometimes.”

[026-117] “It _is_ a rather dangerous practice,” admitted Anne, out of the depths

[026-118] of her own experience.

[026-119] “Well, by and by I’ll tell you the thoughts I told Mary Joe and you can

[026-120] see for yourself if there’s anything queer in them,” said Paul, “but

[026-121] I’ll wait till it begins to get dark. That is the time I ache to tell

[026-122] people things, and when nobody else is handy I just _have_ to tell Mary

[026-123] Joe. But after this I won’t, if it makes her imagine I’m wrong in my

[026-124] upper story. I’ll just ache and bear it.”

[026-125] “And if the ache gets too bad you can come up to Green Gables and tell

[026-126] me your thoughts,” suggested Anne, with all the gravity that endeared

[026-127] her to children, who so dearly [[romance|love]] to be taken seriously.

[026-128] “Yes, I will. But I hope [[davy-keith|Davy]] won’t be there when I go because he makes

[026-129] faces at me. I don’t mind _very_ much because he is such a little boy

[026-130] and I am quite a big one, but still it is not pleasant to have faces

[026-131] made at you. And Davy makes such terrible ones. Sometimes I am

[026-132] frightened he will never get his face straightened out again. He makes

[026-133] them at me in church when I ought to be thinking of sacred things. [[dora-keith|Dora]]

[026-134] likes me though, and I like her, but not so well as I did before she

[026-135] told [[minnie-may-barry|Minnie May Barry]] that she meant to marry me when I grew up. I may

[026-136] marry somebody when I grow up but I’m far too young to be thinking of

[026-137] it yet, don’t you think, teacher?”

[026-138] “Rather young,” agreed teacher.

[026-139] “Speaking of marrying, reminds me of another thing that has been

[026-140] troubling me of late,” continued Paul. “[[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] was down here one

[026-141] day last week having tea with Grandma, and Grandma made me show her my

[026-142] little mother’s picture . . . the one father sent me for my birthday

[026-143] present. I didn’t exactly want to show it to [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]. [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is

[026-144] a good, kind woman, but she isn’t the sort of person you want to show

[026-145] your mother’s picture to. _You_ know, teacher. But of course I obeyed

[026-146] Grandma. Mrs. Lynde said she was very pretty but kind of actressy

[026-147] looking, and must have been an awful lot younger than father. Then she

[026-148] said, ‘Some of these days your pa will be marrying again likely. How

[026-149] will you like to have a new ma, Master Paul?’ Well, the idea almost

[026-150] took my breath away, teacher, but I wasn’t going to let Mrs. Lynde see

[026-151] _that_. I just looked her straight in the face . . . like this . . .

[026-152] and I said, ‘Mrs. Lynde, father made a pretty good job of picking out

[026-153] my first mother and I could [[faith|trust]] him to pick out just as good a one

[026-154] the second time.’ And I _can_ trust him, teacher. But still, I hope, if

[026-155] he ever does give me a new mother, he’ll ask my opinion about her

[026-156] before it’s too late. There’s Mary Joe coming to call us to tea. I’ll

[026-157] go and consult with her about the shortbread.”

[026-158] As a result of the “consultation,” Mary Joe cut the shortbread and

[026-159] added a dish of preserves to the bill of fare. Anne poured the tea and

[026-160] she and Paul had a very merry meal in the dim old sitting room whose

[026-161] windows were open to the gulf breezes, and they talked so much

[026-162] “nonsense” that Mary Joe was quite scandalized and told Veronica the

[026-163] next evening that “de school mees” was as queer as Paul. After tea Paul

[026-164] took Anne up to his room to show her his mother’s picture, which had

[026-165] been the mysterious birthday present kept by Mrs. Irving in the

[026-166] bookcase. Paul’s little low-ceilinged room was a soft whirl of ruddy

[026-167] light from the sun that was setting over the sea and swinging shadows

[026-168] from the fir trees that grew close to the square, deep-set window. From

[026-169] out this soft glow and glamor shone a sweet, girlish face, with tender

[026-170] mother eyes, that was hanging on the wall at the foot of the bed.

[026-171] “That’s my little mother,” said Paul with loving pride. “I got Grandma

[026-172] to hang it there where I’d see it as soon as I opened my eyes in the

[026-173] morning. I never mind not having the light when I go to bed now,

[026-174] because it just seems as if my little mother was right here with me.

[026-175] Father knew just what I would like for a birthday present, although he

[026-176] never asked me. Isn’t it wonderful how much fathers _do_ know?”
