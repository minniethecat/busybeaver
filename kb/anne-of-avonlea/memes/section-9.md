[009-001] “You are getting on splendidly in school, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]. All the children like

[009-002] you,” said [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], sitting down on the stone step.

[009-003] “No, not all. [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] doesn’t and _won’t_ like me. What is worse,

[009-004] he doesn’t respect me . . . no, he doesn’t. He simply holds me in

[009-005] contempt and I don’t mind confessing to you that it worries me

[009-006] miserably. It isn’t that he is so very bad . . . he is only rather

[009-007] mischievous, but no worse than some of the others. He seldom disobeys

[009-008] me; but he obeys with a scornful air of toleration as if it wasn’t

[009-009] worthwhile disputing the point or he would . . . and it has a bad

[009-010] effect on the others. I’ve tried every way to win him but I’m beginning

[009-011] to fear I never shall. I want to, for he’s rather a cute little lad, if

[009-012] he _is_ a Pye, and I could like him if he’d let me.”

[009-013] “Probably it’s merely the effect of what he hears at [[belonging|home]].”

[009-014] “Not altogether. Anthony is an independent little chap and makes up his

[009-015] own mind about things. He has always gone to men before and he says

[009-016] girl teachers are no good. Well, we’ll see what patience and kindness

[009-017] will do. I like overcoming difficulties and teaching is really very

[009-018] interesting work. [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] makes up for all that is lacking in the

[009-019] others. That child is a perfect darling, Gilbert, and a genius into the

[009-020] bargain. I’m persuaded the world will hear of him some day,” concluded

[009-021] Anne in a tone of conviction.

[009-022] “I like teaching, too,” said Gilbert. “It’s good training, for one

[009-023] thing. Why, Anne, I’ve learned more in the weeks I’ve been teaching the

[009-024] young ideas of [[white-sands|White Sands]] than I learned in all the years I went to

[009-025] school myself. We all seem to be getting on pretty well. The [[newbridge|Newbridge]]

[009-026] people like [[jane-andrews|Jane]], I hear; and I think White Sands is tolerably

[009-027] satisfied with your humble servant . . . all except Mr. Andrew Spencer.

[009-028] I met Mrs. Peter Blewett on my way home last night and she told me she

[009-029] thought it her duty to inform me that [[mr-spencer|Mr. Spencer]] didn’t approve of my

[009-030] methods.”

[009-031] “Have you ever noticed,” asked Anne reflectively, “that when people say

[009-032] it is their duty to tell you a certain thing you may prepare for

[009-033] something disagreeable? Why is it that they never seem to think it a

[009-034] duty to tell you the pleasant things they hear about you? Mrs. H. B.

[009-035] Don_nell_ called at the school again yesterday and told me she thought

[009-036] it _her_ duty to inform me that [[mrs-harmon-andrews|Mrs. Harmon Andrews]] didn’t approve of

[009-037] my reading fairy tales to the children, and that Mr. Rogerson thought

[009-038] Prillie wasn’t coming on fast enough in arithmetic. If Prillie would

[009-039] spend less time making eyes at the boys over her slate she might do

[009-040] better. I feel quite sure that Jack Gillis works her class sums for

[009-041] her, though I’ve never been able to catch him red-handed.”

[009-042] “Have you succeeded in reconciling Mrs. Don_nell’s_ hopeful son to his

[009-043] saintly name?”

[009-044] “Yes,” laughed Anne, “but it was really a difficult task. At first,

[009-045] when I called him ‘St. Clair’ he would not take the least notice until

[009-046] I’d spoken two or three times; and then, when the other boys nudged

[009-047] him, he would look up with such an aggrieved air, as if I’d called him

[009-048] John or Charlie and he couldn’t be expected to know I meant him. So I

[009-049] kept him in after school one night and talked kindly to him. I told him

[009-050] his mother wished me to call him St. Clair and I couldn’t go against

[009-051] her wishes. He saw it when it was all explained out . . . he’s really a

[009-052] very reasonable little fellow . . . and he said _I_ could call him St.

[009-053] Clair but that he’d ‘lick the stuffing’ out of any of the boys that

[009-054] tried it. Of course, I had to rebuke him again for using such shocking

[009-055] language. Since then _I_ call him St. Clair and the boys call him Jake

[009-056] and all goes smoothly. He informs me that he means to be a carpenter,

[009-057] but Mrs. Don_nell_ says I am to make a college professor out of him.”

[009-058] The mention of college gave a new direction to Gilbert’s thoughts, and

[009-059] they talked for a time of their plans and wishes . . . gravely,

[009-060] earnestly, hopefully, as youth loves to talk, while the future is yet

[009-061] an untrodden path full of wonderful possibilities.

[009-062] Gilbert had finally made up his mind that he was going to be a doctor.

[009-063] “It’s a splendid profession,” he said enthusiastically. “A fellow has

[009-064] to fight something all through life . . . didn’t somebody once define

[009-065] man as a fighting animal? . . . and I want to fight disease and pain

[009-066] and ignorance . . . which are all members one of another. I want to do

[009-067] my share of honest, real work in the world, Anne . . . add a little to

[009-068] the sum of human knowledge that all the good men have been accumulating

[009-069] since it began. The folks who lived before me have done so much for me

[009-070] that I want to show my gratitude by doing something for the folks who

[009-071] will live after me. It seems to me that is the only way a fellow can

[009-072] get square with his obligations to the race.”

[009-073] “I’d like to add some beauty to life,” said Anne dreamily. “I don’t

[009-074] exactly want to make people _know_ more . . . though I know that _is_

[009-075] the noblest ambition . . . but I’d [[romance|love]] to make them have a pleasanter

[009-076] time because of me . . . to have some little [[laughter|joy]] or happy thought that

[009-077] would never have existed if I hadn’t been born.”

[009-078] “I think you’re fulfilling that ambition every day,” said Gilbert

[009-079] admiringly.

[009-080] And he was right. Anne was one of the children of light by birthright.

[009-081] After she had passed through a life with a smile or a word thrown

[009-082] across it like a gleam of sunshine the owner of that life saw it, for

[009-083] the time being at least, as hopeful and lovely and of good report.

[009-084] Finally Gilbert rose regretfully.

[009-085] “Well, I must run up to MacPhersons’. Moody Spurgeon came home from

[009-086] Queen’s today for Sunday and he was to bring me out a book Professor

[009-087] Boyd is lending me.”

[009-088] “And I must get [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]’s tea. She went to see Mrs. Keith this evening

[009-089] and she will soon be back.”

[009-090] Anne had tea ready when Marilla came home; the fire was crackling

[009-091] cheerily, a vase of frost-bleached ferns and ruby-red maple leaves

[009-092] adorned the table, and delectable odors of ham and toast pervaded the

[009-093] air. But Marilla sank into her chair with a deep sigh.

[009-094] “Are your eyes troubling you? Does your head ache?” queried Anne

[009-095] anxiously.

[009-096] “No. I’m only tired . . . and worried. It’s about Mary and those

[009-097] children . . . Mary is worse . . . she can’t last much longer. And as

[009-098] for the twins, _I_ don’t know what is to become of them.”

[009-099] “Hasn’t their uncle been heard from?”

[009-100] “Yes, Mary had a letter from him. He’s working in a lumber camp and

[009-101] ‘shacking it,’ whatever that means. Anyway, he says he can’t possibly

[009-102] take the children till the spring. He expects to be married then and

[009-103] will have a home to take them to; but he says she must get some of the

[009-104] [[community|neighbors]] to keep them for the winter. She says she can’t bear to ask

[009-105] any of them. Mary never got on any too well with the [[grafton|East Grafton]]

[009-106] people and that’s a fact. And the long and short of it is, Anne, that

[009-107] I’m sure Mary wants me to take those children . . . she didn’t say so

[009-108] but she _looked_ it.”

[009-109] “Oh!” Anne clasped her hands, all athrill with excitement. “And of

[009-110] course you will, Marilla, won’t you?”

[009-111] “I haven’t made up my mind,” said Marilla rather tartly. “I don’t rush

[009-112] into things in your headlong way, Anne. Third cousinship is a pretty

[009-113] slim claim. And it will be a fearful [[duty|responsibility]] to have two

[009-114] children of six years to look after . . . twins, at that.”

[009-115] Marilla had an idea that twins were just twice as bad as single

[009-116] children.

[009-117] “Twins are very interesting . . . at least one pair of them,” said

[009-118] Anne. “It’s only when there are two or three pairs that it gets

[009-119] monotonous. And I think it would be real nice for you to have something

[009-120] to amuse you when I’m away in school.”

[009-121] “I don’t reckon there’d be much amusement in it . . . more worry and

[009-122] bother than anything else, I should say. It wouldn’t be so risky if

[009-123] they were even as old as you were when I took you. I wouldn’t mind [[dora-keith|Dora]]

[009-124] so much . . . she seems good and quiet. But that [[davy-keith|Davy]] is a limb.”

[009-125] Anne was fond of children and her heart yearned over the Keith twins.

[009-126] The remembrance of her own neglected childhood was very vivid with her

[009-127] still. She knew that Marilla’s only vulnerable point was her stern

[009-128] devotion to what she believed to be her duty, and Anne skillfully

[009-129] marshalled her arguments along this line.

[009-130] “If Davy is naughty it’s all the more reason why he should have good

[009-131] training, isn’t it, Marilla? If we don’t take them we don’t know who

[009-132] will, nor what kind of influences may surround them. Suppose Mrs.

[009-133] Keith’s next door neighbors, the Sprotts, were to take them. [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[009-134] says Henry Sprott is the most profane man that ever lived and you can’t

[009-135] believe a word his children say. Wouldn’t it be dreadful to have the

[009-136] twins learn anything like that? Or suppose they went to the Wiggins’.

[009-137] [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says that Mr. Wiggins sells everything off the place that

[009-138] can be sold and brings his family up on skim milk. You wouldn’t like

[009-139] your relations to be starved, even if they were only third cousins,

[009-140] would you? It seems to me, Marilla, that it is our duty to take them.”

[009-141] “I suppose it is,” assented Marilla gloomily. “I daresay I’ll tell Mary

[009-142] I’ll take them. You needn’t look so delighted, Anne. It will mean a

[009-143] good deal of extra work for you. I can’t sew a stitch on account of my

[009-144] eyes, so you’ll have to see to the making and mending of their clothes.

[009-145] And you don’t like sewing.”

[009-146] “I hate it,” said Anne calmly, “but if you are willing to take those

[009-147] children from a sense of duty surely I can do their sewing from a sense

[009-148] of duty. It does people good to have to do things they don’t like . . .

[009-149] in moderation.”

[009-150] VIII

[009-151] Marilla Adopts Twins

[009-152] [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] was sitting at her kitchen window, knitting a quilt,

[009-153] just as she had been sitting one evening several years previously when

[009-154] Matthew Cuthbert had driven down over the hill with what Mrs. Rachel

[009-155] called “his imported orphan.” But that had been in springtime; and this

[009-156] was late autumn, and all the woods were leafless and the fields sere

[009-157] and brown. The sun was just setting with a great deal of purple and

[009-158] golden pomp behind the dark woods west of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] when a buggy drawn by

[009-159] a comfortable brown nag came down the hill. Mrs. Rachel peered at it

[009-160] eagerly.

[009-161] “There’s Marilla getting home from the funeral,” she said to her

[009-162] husband, who was lying on the kitchen lounge. [[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]] lay more on

[009-163] the lounge nowadays than he had been used to do, but Mrs. Rachel, who

[009-164] was so sharp at noticing anything beyond her own household, had not as

[009-165] yet noticed this. “And she’s got the twins with her, . . . yes, there’s

[009-166] Davy leaning over the dashboard grabbing at the pony’s tail and Marilla

[009-167] jerking him back. Dora’s sitting up on the seat as prim as you please.

[009-168] She always looks as if she’d just been starched and ironed. Well, poor

[009-169] Marilla is going to have her hands full this winter and no mistake.

[009-170] Still, I don’t see that she could do anything less than take them,

[009-171] under the circumstances, and she’ll have Anne to help her. Anne’s

[009-172] tickled to death over the whole business, and she has a real knacky way

[009-173] with children, I must say. Dear me, it doesn’t seem a day since poor

[009-174] Matthew brought Anne herself home and everybody laughed at the idea of

[009-175] Marilla bringing up a child. And now she has adopted twins. You’re

[009-176] never safe from being surprised till you’re dead.”
