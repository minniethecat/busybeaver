# Miss Lavendar's Wedding

The marriage of [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] and [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] — the novel's emotional climax, celebrating a [[romance|love]] that survived twenty-five years of separation and silence.

## Overview

After a quarter-century of estrangement caused by a trivial quarrel and sustained by pride, [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] and [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] are reunited through the agency of [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]. Their wedding at Echo Lodge is the culmination of the novel's central [[love|romance]] plot. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], observing the ceremony, thinks to herself: "Yes, this was [[love|romance]], the very" essence of it (042-008).

## Significance

The wedding is Montgomery's most powerful argument for the [[patience|endurance]] of [[romance|love]] and the possibility of second chances. That happiness can arrive at forty-five rather than twenty is a radical proposition in a culture that associates [[love|romance]] [[humor|wit]]h youth. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s late [[laughter|joy]] validates the novel's belief that "there was more [[love|romance]] in the world than that which had been written down in books" (043-070).
