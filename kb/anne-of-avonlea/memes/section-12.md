[012-001] The sight affected [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] oddly. She dropped the reins, held up her

[012-002] hands, and said “Gracious Providence!” She stared as if she could not

[012-003] believe her eyes. Then she laughed almost hysterically.

[012-004] “There must be some mistake . . . there _must_. I knew those Pyes would

[012-005] make a mess of things.”

[012-006] [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] drove [[belonging|home]], meeting several people on the road and stopping

[012-007] to tell them about the hall. The news flew like wildfire. [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]

[012-008] Blythe, poring over a text book at home, heard it from his father’s

[012-009] hired boy at sunset, and rushed breathlessly to [[green-gables|Green Gables]], joined on

[012-010] the way by [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]]. They found [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]], [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]], and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]

[012-011] Shirley, despair personified, at the yard gate of Green Gables, under

[012-012] the big leafless willows.

[012-013] “It isn’t true surely, Anne?” exclaimed Gilbert.

[012-014] “It is true,” answered Anne, looking like the muse of tragedy. “Mrs.

[012-015] Lynde called on her way from [[carmody|Carmody]] to tell me. Oh, it is simply

[012-016] dreadful! _What_ is the use of trying to improve anything?”

[012-017] “What is dreadful?” asked [[oliver-sloane|Oliver Sloane]], arriving at this moment with a

[012-018] bandbox he had brought from town for [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]].

[012-019] “Haven’t you heard?” said Jane wrathfully. “Well, its simply this. . .

[012-020] Joshua Pye _has gone and painted the hall blue instead of green_ . . .

[012-021] a deep, brilliant blue, the shade they use for painting carts and

[012-022] wheelbarrows. And [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says it is the most hideous color for a

[012-023] building, especially when combined with a red roof, that she ever saw

[012-024] or imagined. You could simply have knocked me down with a feather when

[012-025] I heard it. It’s heartbreaking, after all the trouble we’ve had.”

[012-026] “How on earth could such a mistake have happened?” wailed Diana.

[012-027] The blame of this unmerciful disaster was eventually narrowed down to

[012-028] the Pyes. The Improvers had decided to use Morton-Harris paints and the

[012-029] Morton-Harris paint cans were numbered according to a color card. A

[012-030] purchaser chose his shade on the card and ordered by the accompanying

[012-031] number. Number 147 was the shade of green desired and when Mr. Roger

[012-032] Pye sent word to the Improvers by his son, John Andrew, that he was

[012-033] going to town and would get their paint for them, the Improvers told

[012-034] John Andrew to tell his father to get 147. John Andrew always averred

[012-035] that he did so, but Mr. Roger Pye as stanchly declared that John Andrew

[012-036] told him 157; and there the matter stands to this day.

[012-037] That night there was blank dismay in every [[avonlea|Avonlea]] house where an

[012-038] Improver lived. The gloom at Green Gables was so intense that it

[012-039] quenched even [[davy-keith|Davy]]. Anne wept and would not be comforted.

[012-040] “I _must_ cry, even if I am almost seventeen, Marilla,” she sobbed. “It

[012-041] is so mortifying. And it sounds the death knell of our [[community|society]]. We’ll

[012-042] simply be laughed out of existence.”

[012-043] In life, as in [[ambition|dreams]], however, things often go by contraries. The

[012-044] Avonlea people did not laugh; they were too angry. _Their_ money had

[012-045] gone to paint the hall and consequently _they_ felt themselves bitterly

[012-046] aggrieved by the mistake. Public indignation centered on the Pyes.

[012-047] Roger Pye and John Andrew had bungled the matter between them; and as

[012-048] for Joshua Pye, he must be a born fool not to suspect there was

[012-049] something wrong when he opened the cans and saw the color of the paint.

[012-050] Joshua Pye, when thus animadverted upon, retorted that the Avonlea

[012-051] taste in colors was no business of his, whatever his private opinion

[012-052] might be; he had been hired to paint the hall, not to talk about it;

[012-053] and he meant to have his money for it.

[012-054] The Improvers paid him his money in bitterness of spirit, after

[012-055] consulting [[peter-sloane|Mr. Peter Sloane]], who was a magistrate.

[012-056] “You’ll have to pay it,” Peter told him. “You can’t hold him

[012-057] responsible for the mistake, since he claims he was never told what the

[012-058] color was supposed to be but just given the cans and told to go ahead.

[012-059] But it’s a burning shame and that hall certainly does look awful.”

[012-060] The luckless Improvers expected that Avonlea would be more prejudiced

[012-061] than ever against them; but instead, public sympathy veered around in

[012-062] their favor. People thought the eager, enthusiastic little band who had

[012-063] worked so hard for their object had been badly used. Mrs. Lynde told

[012-064] them to keep on and show the Pyes that there really were people in the

[012-065] world who could do things without making a muddle of them. Mr. Major

[012-066] Spencer sent them word that he would clean out all the stumps along the

[012-067] road front of his farm and seed it down with grass at his own expense;

[012-068] and Mrs. Hiram Sloane called at the school one day and beckoned Anne

[012-069] mysteriously out into the porch to tell her that if the “Sassiety”

[012-070] wanted to make a geranium bed at the crossroads in the spring they

[012-071] needn’t be afraid of her cow, for she would see that the marauding

[012-072] animal was kept within safe bounds. Even [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] chuckled, if he

[012-073] chuckled at all, in private, and was all sympathy outwardly.

[012-074] “Never mind, Anne. Most paints fade uglier every year but that blue is

[012-075] as ugly as it can be to begin with, so it’s bound to fade prettier. And

[012-076] the roof is shingled and painted all right. Folks will be able to sit

[012-077] in the hall after this without being leaked on. You’ve accomplished so

[012-078] much anyhow.”

[012-079] “But Avonlea’s blue hall will be a byword in all the neighboring

[012-080] settlements from this time out,” said Anne bitterly.

[012-081] And it must be confessed that it was.

[012-082] X

[012-083] Davy in Search of a Sensation

[012-084] Anne, walking home from school through the [[birch-path|Birch Path]] one November

[012-085] afternoon, felt convinced afresh that life was a very wonderful thing.

[012-086] The day had been a good day; all had gone well in her little kingdom.

[012-087] St. Clair Donnell had _not_ fought any of the other boys over the

[012-088] question of his name; Prillie Rogerson’s face had been so puffed up

[012-089] from the effects of toothache that she did not once try to coquette

[012-090] with the boys in her vicinity. [[barbara-shaw|Barbara Shaw]] had met with only _one_

[012-091] accident . . . spilling a dipper of water over the floor . . . and

[012-092] [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] had not been in school at all.

[012-093] “What a nice month this November has been!” said Anne, who had never

[012-094] quite got over her childish habit of talking to herself. “November is

[012-095] usually such a disagreeable month . . . as if the year had suddenly

[012-096] found out that she was growing old and could do nothing but weep and

[012-097] fret over it. This year is growing old gracefully . . . just like a

[012-098] stately old lady who knows she can be charming even with gray hair and

[012-099] wrinkles. We’ve had lovely days and delicious twilights. This last

[012-100] fortnight has been so peaceful, and even Davy has been almost

[012-101] well-behaved. I really think he is improving a great deal. How quiet

[012-102] the woods are today . . . not a murmur except that soft wind purring in

[012-103] the treetops! It sounds like surf on a faraway shore. How dear the

[012-104] woods are! You beautiful trees! I [[romance|love]] every one of you as a friend.”

[012-105] Anne paused to throw her arm about a slim young birch and kiss its

[012-106] cream-white trunk. Diana, rounding a curve in the path, saw her and

[012-107] laughed.

[012-108] “Anne Shirley, you’re only pretending to be grown up. I believe when

[012-109] you’re alone you’re as much a little girl as you ever were.”

[012-110] “Well, one can’t get over the habit of being a little girl all at

[012-111] once,” said Anne gaily. “You see, I was little for fourteen years and

[012-112] I’ve only been grown-uppish for scarcely three. I’m sure I shall always

[012-113] feel like a child in the woods. These walks home from school are almost

[012-114] the only time I have for dreaming . . . except the half-hour or so

[012-115] before I go to sleep. I’m so busy with teaching and studying and

[012-116] helping Marilla with the twins that I haven’t another moment for

[012-117] imagining things. You don’t know what splendid adventures I have for a

[012-118] little while after I go to bed in the east gable every night. I always

[012-119] imagine I’m something very brilliant and triumphant and splendid . . .

[012-120] a great prima donna or a Red Cross nurse or a queen. Last night I was a

[012-121] queen. It’s really splendid to imagine you are a queen. You have all

[012-122] the fun of it without any of the inconveniences and you can stop being

[012-123] a queen whenever you want to, which you couldn’t in real life. But here

[012-124] in the woods I like best to imagine quite different things . . . I’m a

[012-125] dryad living in an old pine, or a little brown wood-elf hiding under a

[012-126] crinkled leaf. That white birch you caught me kissing is a sister of

[012-127] mine. The only difference is, she’s a tree and I’m a girl, but that’s

[012-128] no real difference. Where are you going, Diana?”

[012-129] “Down to the Dicksons. I promised to help Alberta cut out her new

[012-130] dress. Can’t you walk down in the evening, Anne, and come home with

[012-131] me?”

[012-132] “I might . . . since Fred Wright is away in town,” said Anne with a

[012-133] rather too innocent face.

[012-134] Diana blushed, tossed her head, and walked on. She did not look

[012-135] offended, however.

[012-136] Anne fully intended to go down to the Dicksons’ that evening, but she

[012-137] did not. When she arrived at Green Gables she found a state of affairs

[012-138] which banished every other thought from her mind. Marilla met her in

[012-139] the yard . . . a wild-eyed Marilla.

[012-140] “Anne, [[dora-keith|Dora]] is lost!”

[012-141] “Dora! Lost!” Anne looked at Davy, who was swinging on the yard gate,

[012-142] and detected [[laughter|merriment]] in his eyes. “Davy, do you know where she is?”

[012-143] “No, I don’t,” said Davy stoutly. “I haven’t seen her since dinner

[012-144] time, cross my heart.”

[012-145] “I’ve been away ever since one o’clock,” said Marilla. “[[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]]

[012-146] took sick all of a sudden and Rachel sent up for me to go at once. When

[012-147] I left here Dora was playing with her doll in the kitchen and Davy was

[012-148] making mud pies behind the barn. I only got home half an hour ago . . .

[012-149] and no Dora to be seen. Davy declares he never saw her since I left.”

[012-150] “Neither I did,” avowed Davy solemnly.

[012-151] “She must be somewhere around,” said Anne. “She would never wander far

[012-152] away alone . . . you know how timid she is. Perhaps she has fallen

[012-153] asleep in one of the rooms.”

[012-154] Marilla shook her head.

[012-155] “I’ve hunted the whole house through. But she may be in some of the

[012-156] buildings.”

[012-157] A thorough search followed. Every corner of house, yard, and

[012-158] outbuildings was ransacked by those two distracted people. Anne roved

[012-159] the orchards and [[haunted-wood|the Haunted Wood]], calling Dora’s name. Marilla took a

[012-160] candle and explored the cellar. Davy accompanied each of them in turn,

[012-161] and was fertile in thinking of places where Dora could possibly be.

[012-162] Finally they met again in the yard.

[012-163] “It’s a most mysterious thing,” groaned Marilla.

[012-164] “Where can she be?” said Anne miserably

[012-165] “Maybe she’s tumbled into the well,” suggested Davy cheerfully.

[012-166] Anne and Marilla looked fearfully into each other’s eyes. The thought

[012-167] had been with them both through their entire search but neither had

[012-168] dared to put it into words.

[012-169] “She . . . she might have,” whispered Marilla.

[012-170] Anne, feeling faint and sick, went to the wellbox and peered over. The

[012-171] bucket sat on the shelf inside. Far down below was a tiny glimmer of

[012-172] still water. The Cuthbert well was the deepest in Avonlea. If Dora. . .

[012-173] but Anne could not face the idea. She shuddered and turned away.

[012-174] “Run across for Mr. Harrison,” said Marilla, wringing her hands.
