# Marilla Cuthbert

The stern but loving woman who adopted [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] as a girl and who, in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, faces the challenges of aging, the care of two additional orphaned children, and the quiet satisfaction of watching [[anne-shirley|Anne]] grow into young womanhood.

## Character Overview

Marilla Cuthbert is the moral backbone of [[green-gables|Green Gables]]. Where [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is imaginative and expressive, Marilla is practical and reserved — yet the novel consistently reveals the tenderness beneath her severity. Her "[[teaching|education]] had made" her more flexible in her thinking over the years (032-025), and her relationship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] has softened her [[humor|wit]]hout diminishing her [[pride|dignity]].

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, Marilla faces new challenges: her eyesight is weakening, she has taken in the orphaned twins [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora]], and she must navigate the shifting dynamics of a household in which [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is no longer a child. Marilla meets these challenges [[humor|wit]]h characteristic steadiness, and her pride in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is one of the novel's quiet [[laughter|joy]]s — she feels "pride in her as she led him into the school" (010-129).

## Role in the Story

Marilla's role is primarily domestic — she manages [[green-gables|Green Gables]], cares for the twins, and provides the stable [[belonging|home]] base from which [[anne-shirley|Anne]] ventures into her teaching career. But her importance transcends her page count. Marilla represents the world of duty and practicality that [[anne-shirley|Anne]] must learn to navigate, and her approval — when it comes — carries moral weight.

Her decision to take in [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora Keith]] after their mother's death, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s support of that decision, provides one of the novel's key moral moments: "it is our duty to take them" (009-140).

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Marilla's adopted daughter — the orphan who arrived by mistake and became the center of Marilla's world. Their relationship has deepened from the early days of mutual bewilderment into a bond of deep, if understated, [[romance|love]].

**[[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora Keith]]** are the twins Marilla takes in, extending her maternal role into a new generation.

**Matthew Cuthbert**, Marilla's late brother, is a remembered presence whose gentle spirit is missed.

## Key Scenes

**Marilla's [[pride|Pride]] (Chapter 10).** Marilla's "pride in her as she led him into the school" (010-129) captures the quiet, deep satisfaction she takes in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s achievements.

**Marilla's Growth (Chapter 32).** The observation that "Marilla's [[teaching|education]] had made" her more patient (032-025) is a touching acknowledgment that she, too, has been transformed by [[romance|love]].

## Character Analysis

Marilla embodies the novel's argument that [[romance|love]] need not be demonstrative to be profound. Her character arc — from the stern spinster who "didn't believe in" imagination to the woman who "came in to criticize and remained to praise" [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s decorated schoolroom (023-017) — is a quiet testimony to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s transformative influence.

Marilla is also the novel's representative of aging and its challenges. Her weakening eyesight and her reliance on [[anne-shirley|Anne]] for support reverse the earlier dependency, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[love|devotion]] to Marilla is the novel's most powerful expression of duty as [[romance|love]].
