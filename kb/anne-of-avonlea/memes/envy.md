# Envy

The wistful longing for what another has that surfaces in [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s confession: "I do [[jealousy|envy]] you" (032-117) — aimed at [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s youth, her future, her life still unfolding. Montgomery treats [[jealousy|envy]] not as a sin but as a natural human response to the awareness of one's own limitations.

## Definition

Envy in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is portrayed [[humor|wit]]h unusual [[kindness|gentleness]]. Montgomery does not condemn it; she understands it as a byproduct of self-awareness — the recognition that time has passed, opportunities have been missed, and others possess what one cannot have. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s "I do [[jealousy|envy]] you" (032-117) is not malicious; it is an honest acknowledgment of what [[anne-shirley|Anne]] represents: youth, possibility, a life still ahead.

This treatment distinguishes [[jealousy|envy]] from jealousy. [[jealousy|Jealousy]] in the novel (embodied in characters like Josie Pye) is possessive and competitive; [[jealousy|envy]], as [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] experiences it, is more like admiration tinged [[humor|wit]]h regret. It does not wish to take anything from [[anne-shirley|Anne]]; it simply wishes it could have had more of the same.

## Examples in the Text

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Confession (Chapter 32).** "To be seventeen, isn't it? I do [[jealousy|envy]] you" (032-117) — [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] speaks these words to [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[humor|wit]]hout bitterness. Her [[jealousy|envy]] is transparent, almost generous: she is happy for [[anne-shirley|Anne]] while also aware of what she herself has missed. Montgomery presents this as emotional honesty, not moral failing. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s capacity to name her [[jealousy|envy]] [[humor|wit]]hout acting on it is, paradoxically, a sign of character.

## Thematic Significance

Envy serves as a counterweight to the novel's theme of contentment. [[avonlea|Avonlea]] is a [[belonging|place]] where people are generally satisfied [[humor|wit]]h their lot — but Montgomery is too honest a writer to pretend that satisfaction is universal or complete. Envy flickers at the edges of the narrative: in the spinster's glance at the young teacher, in the poor child's observation of the rich one's lunch, in the older woman's awareness of the younger one's freedom.

Montgomery's treatment aligns [[humor|wit]]h her broader ethical vision: emotions are not sins; actions are. Feeling [[jealousy|envy]] is human; acting on it — through spite, sabotage, or self-pity — is the failure. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] feels [[jealousy|envy]] and transforms it into a compliment; this is the novel's model for handling the emotion.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Envy as Recognition vs. [[jealousy|Jealousy]] as Possession.** Envy in Montgomery's hands is a form of recognition — "I see what you have, and I honor it by wanting it too." [[jealousy|Jealousy]], by contrast, wants to *take* — to possess, to diminish the other. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s [[jealousy|envy]] leaves [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s youth intact; Josie Pye's jealousy would, if it could, take something from [[anne-shirley|Anne]].

**Youth Envied vs. Age Valued.** The novel complicates [[jealousy|envy]] by also showing what youth lacks — wisdom, perspective, the settled peace that [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] has achieved in her echo lodge. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] may be envied for her future, but [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] is valued for her depth. Montgomery suggests that [[jealousy|envy]] is often based on incomplete information: we [[jealousy|envy]] what we cannot have while undervaluing what we do.
