[017-001] “It _sounds_ awfully nice,” said [[diana-barry|Diana]], with some inward distrust of

[017-002] [[anne-shirley|Anne]]’s magic of words. “But won’t it be very damp in some places yet?”

[017-003] “Oh, we’ll wear rubbers,” was Anne’s concession to practicalities. “And

[017-004] I want you to come over early Saturday morning and help me prepare

[017-005] lunch. I’m going to have the daintiest things possible . . . things

[017-006] that will match the spring, you understand . . . little jelly tarts and

[017-007] lady fingers, and drop cookies frosted with pink and yellow icing, and

[017-008] buttercup cake. And we must have sandwiches too, though they’re _not_

[017-009] very poetical.”

[017-010] Saturday proved an ideal day for a picnic . . . a day of breeze and

[017-011] blue, warm, sunny, with a little rollicking wind blowing across meadow

[017-012] and orchard. Over every sunlit upland and field was a delicate,

[017-013] flower-starred green.

[017-014] [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], harrowing at the back of his farm and feeling some of the

[017-015] spring witch-work even in his sober, middle-aged blood, saw four girls,

[017-016] basket laden, tripping across the end of his field where it joined a

[017-017] fringing woodland of birch and fir. Their blithe voices and [[humor|laughter]]

[017-018] echoed down to him.

[017-019] “It’s so easy to be happy on a day like this, isn’t it?” Anne was

[017-020] saying, with true Anneish philosophy. “Let’s try to make this a really

[017-021] golden day, girls, a day to which we can always look back with delight.

[017-022] We’re to seek for beauty and refuse to see anything else. ‘Begone, dull

[017-023] care!’ [[jane-andrews|Jane]], you are thinking of something that went wrong in school

[017-024] yesterday.”

[017-025] “How do you know?” gasped Jane, amazed.

[017-026] “Oh, I know the expression . . . I’ve felt it often enough on my own

[017-027] face. But put it out of your mind, there’s a dear. It will keep till

[017-028] Monday . . . or if it doesn’t so much the better. Oh, girls, girls, see

[017-029] that patch of violets! There’s something for memory’s picture gallery.

[017-030] When I’m eighty years old . . . if I ever am . . . I shall shut my eyes

[017-031] and see those violets just as I see them now. That’s the first good

[017-032] gift our day has given us.”

[017-033] “If a kiss could be seen I think it would look like a violet,” said

[017-034] Priscilla.

[017-035] Anne glowed.

[017-036] “I’m so glad you _spoke_ that thought, Priscilla, instead of just

[017-037] thinking it and keeping it to yourself. This world would be a much more

[017-038] interesting [[belonging|place]] . . . although it _is_ very interesting anyhow . . .

[017-039] if people spoke out their real thoughts.”

[017-040] “It would be too hot to hold some folks,” quoted Jane sagely.

[017-041] “I suppose it might be, but that would be their own faults for thinking

[017-042] nasty things. Anyhow, we can tell all our thoughts today because we are

[017-043] going to have nothing but beautiful thoughts. Everybody can say just

[017-044] what comes into her head. _That_ is conversation. Here’s a little path

[017-045] I never saw before. Let’s explore it.”

[017-046] The path was a winding one, so narrow that the girls walked in single

[017-047] file and even then the fir boughs brushed their faces. Under the firs

[017-048] were velvety cushions of moss, and further on, where the trees were

[017-049] smaller and fewer, the ground was rich in a variety of green growing

[017-050] things.

[017-051] “What a lot of elephant’s ears,” exclaimed Diana. “I’m going to pick a

[017-052] big bunch, they’re so pretty.”

[017-053] “How did such graceful feathery things ever come to have such a

[017-054] dreadful name?” asked Priscilla.

[017-055] “Because the person who first named them either had no imagination at

[017-056] all or else far too much,” said Anne, “Oh, girls, look at that!”

[017-057] “That” was a shallow woodland pool in the center of a little open glade

[017-058] where the path ended. Later on in the season it would be dried up and

[017-059] its place filled with a rank growth of ferns; but now it was a

[017-060] glimmering placid sheet, round as a saucer and clear as crystal. A ring

[017-061] of slender young birches encircled it and little ferns fringed its

[017-062] margin.

[017-063] “_How_ sweet!” said Jane.

[017-064] “Let us dance around it like wood-nymphs,” cried Anne, dropping her

[017-065] basket and extending her hands.

[017-066] But the dance was not a success for the ground was boggy and Jane’s

[017-067] rubbers came off.

[017-068] “You can’t be a wood-nymph if you have to wear rubbers,” was her

[017-069] decision.

[017-070] “Well, we must name this place before we leave it,” said Anne, yielding

[017-071] to the indisputable logic of facts. “Everybody suggest a name and we’ll

[017-072] draw lots. Diana?”

[017-073] “Birch Pool,” suggested Diana promptly.

[017-074] “Crystal Lake,” said Jane.

[017-075] Anne, standing behind them, implored Priscilla with her eyes not to

[017-076] perpetrate another such name and Priscilla rose to the occasion with

[017-077] “Glimmer-glass.” Anne’s selection was “The Fairies’ Mirror.”

[017-078] The names were written on strips of birch bark with a pencil

[017-079] Schoolma’am Jane produced from her pocket, and placed in Anne’s hat.

[017-080] Then Priscilla shut her eyes and drew one. “Crystal Lake,” read Jane

[017-081] triumphantly. Crystal Lake it was, and if Anne thought that chance had

[017-082] played the pool a shabby trick she did not say so.

[017-083] Pushing through the undergrowth beyond, the girls came out to the young

[017-084] green seclusion of Mr. Silas Sloane’s back pasture. Across it they

[017-085] found the entrance to a lane striking up through the woods and voted to

[017-086] explore it also. It rewarded their quest with a succession of pretty

[017-087] surprises. First, skirting Mr. Sloane’s pasture, came an archway of

[017-088] wild cherry trees all in bloom. The girls swung their hats on their

[017-089] arms and wreathed their hair with the creamy, fluffy blossoms. Then the

[017-090] lane turned at right angles and plunged into a spruce wood so thick and

[017-091] dark that they walked in a gloom as of twilight, with not a glimpse of

[017-092] sky or sunlight to be seen.

[017-093] “This is where the bad wood elves dwell,” whispered Anne. “They are

[017-094] impish and malicious but they can’t harm us, because they are not

[017-095] allowed to do evil in the spring. There was one peeping at us around

[017-096] that old twisted fir; and didn’t you see a group of them on that big

[017-097] freckly toadstool we just passed? The good fairies always dwell in the

[017-098] sunshiny places.”

[017-099] “I wish there really were fairies,” said Jane. “Wouldn’t it be nice to

[017-100] have three wishes granted you . . . or even only one? What would you

[017-101] wish for, girls, if you could have a wish granted? I’d wish to be rich

[017-102] and beautiful and clever.”

[017-103] “I’d wish to be tall and slender,” said Diana.

[017-104] “I would wish to be famous,” said Priscilla. Anne thought of her hair

[017-105] and then dismissed the thought as unworthy.

[017-106] “I’d wish it might be spring all the time and in everybody’s heart and

[017-107] all our lives,” she said.

[017-108] “But that,” said Priscilla, “would be just wishing this world were like

[017-109] heaven.”

[017-110] “Only like a part of heaven. In the other parts there would be summer

[017-111] and autumn . . . yes, and a bit of winter, too. I think I want

[017-112] glittering snowy fields and white frosts in heaven sometimes. Don’t

[017-113] you, Jane?”

[017-114] “I . . . I don’t know,” said Jane uncomfortably. Jane was a good girl,

[017-115] a member of [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]], who tried conscientiously to live up to her

[017-116] profession and believed everything she had been taught. But she never

[017-117] thought about heaven any more than she could help, for all that.

[017-118] “[[minnie-may-barry|Minnie May]] asked me the other day if we would wear our best dresses

[017-119] every day in heaven,” laughed Diana.

[017-120] “And didn’t you tell her we would?” asked Anne.

[017-121] “Mercy, no! I told her we wouldn’t be thinking of dresses at all

[017-122] there.”

[017-123] “Oh, I think we will . . . a _little_,” said Anne earnestly. “There’ll

[017-124] be plenty of time in all eternity for it without neglecting more

[017-125] important things. I believe we’ll all wear beautiful dresses . . . or I

[017-126] suppose _raiment_ would be a more suitable way of speaking. I shall

[017-127] want to wear pink for a few centuries at first . . . it would take me

[017-128] that long to get tired of it, I feel sure. I do [[romance|love]] pink so and I can

[017-129] never wear it in _this_ world.”

[017-130] Past the spruces the lane dipped down into a sunny little open where a

[017-131] log bridge spanned a brook; and then came the glory of a sunlit

[017-132] beechwood where the air was like transparent golden wine, and the

[017-133] leaves fresh and green, and the wood floor a mosaic of tremulous

[017-134] sunshine. Then more wild cherries, and a little valley of lissome firs,

[017-135] and then a hill so steep that the girls lost their breath climbing it;

[017-136] but when they reached the top and came out into the open the prettiest

[017-137] surprise of all awaited them.

[017-138] Beyond were the “back fields” of the farms that ran out to the upper

[017-139] [[carmody|Carmody]] road. Just before them, hemmed in by beeches and firs but open

[017-140] to the south, was a little corner and in it a garden . . . or what had

[017-141] once been a garden. A tumbledown stone dyke, overgrown with mosses and

[017-142] grass, surrounded it. Along the eastern side ran a row of garden cherry

[017-143] trees, white as a snowdrift. There were traces of old paths still and a

[017-144] double line of rosebushes through the middle; but all the rest of the

[017-145] space was a sheet of yellow and white narcissi, in their airiest, most

[017-146] lavish, wind-swayed bloom above the lush green grasses.

[017-147] “Oh, how perfectly lovely!” three of the girls cried. Anne only gazed

[017-148] in eloquent silence.

[017-149] “How in the world does it happen that there ever was a garden back

[017-150] here?” said Priscilla in amazement.

[017-151] “It must be [[hester-gray|Hester Gray]]’s garden,” said Diana. “I’ve heard mother speak

[017-152] of it but I never saw it before, and I wouldn’t have supposed that it

[017-153] could be in existence still. You’ve heard the story, Anne?”

[017-154] “No, but the name seems familiar to me.”

[017-155] “Oh, you’ve seen it in the graveyard. She is buried down there in the

[017-156] poplar corner. You know the little brown stone with the opening gates

[017-157] carved on it and ‘Sacred to the memory of Hester Gray, aged

[017-158] twenty-two.’ Jordan Gray is buried right beside her but there’s no

[017-159] stone to him. It’s a wonder [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] never told you about it, Anne. To

[017-160] be sure, it happened thirty years ago and everybody has forgotten.”

[017-161] “Well, if there’s a story we must have it,” said Anne. “Let’s sit right

[017-162] down here among the narcissi and Diana will tell it. Why, girls, there

[017-163] are hundreds of them . . . they’ve spread over everything. It looks as

[017-164] if the garden were carpeted with moonshine and sunshine combined. This

[017-165] is a discovery worth making. To think that I’ve lived within a mile of

[017-166] this place for six years and have never seen it before! Now, Diana.”

[017-167] “Long ago,” began Diana, “this farm belonged to old Mr. David Gray. He

[017-168] didn’t live on it . . . he lived where Silas Sloane lives now. He had

[017-169] one son, Jordan, and he went up to Boston one winter to work and while

[017-170] he was there he fell in [[romance|love]] with a girl named Hester Murray. She was

[017-171] working in a store and she hated it. She’d been brought up in the

[017-172] country and she always wanted to get back. When Jordan asked her to

[017-173] marry him she said she would if he’d take her away to some quiet spot

[017-174] where she’d see nothing but fields and trees. So he brought her to

[017-175] [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] said he was taking a fearful risk in marrying a

[017-176] Yankee, and it’s certain that Hester was very delicate and a very poor

[017-177] housekeeper; but mother says she was very pretty and sweet and Jordan

[017-178] just worshipped the ground she walked on. Well, Mr. Gray gave Jordan

[017-179] this farm and he built a little house back here and Jordan and Hester

[017-180] lived in it for four years. She never went out much and hardly anybody

[017-181] went to see her except mother and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]. Jordan made her this

[017-182] garden and she was crazy about it and spent most of her time in it. She

[017-183] wasn’t much of a housekeeper but she had a knack with flowers. And then

[017-184] she got sick. Mother says she thinks she was in consumption before she

[017-185] ever came here. She never really laid up but just grew weaker and

[017-186] weaker all the time. Jordan wouldn’t have anybody to wait on her. He

[017-187] did it all himself and mother says he was as tender and gentle as a

[017-188] woman. Every day he’d wrap her in a shawl and carry her out to the

[017-189] garden and she’d lie there on a bench quite happy. They say she used to

[017-190] make Jordan kneel down by her every night and morning and pray with her

[017-191] that she might die out in the garden when the time came. And her prayer

[017-192] was answered. One day Jordan carried her out to the bench and then he

[017-193] picked all the roses that were out and heaped them over her; and she

[017-194] just smiled up at him . . . and closed her eyes . . . and that,”

[017-195] concluded Diana softly, “was the end.”
