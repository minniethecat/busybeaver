# Miss Lavendar Lewis

The owner of [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] and the heroine of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*'s most beautiful [[romance|love]] story — a woman who has kept "the freshness of youth in her heart and soul" (032-080) through twenty-five years of [[loneliness|solitude]] and whose reunion [[humor|wit]]h her lost [[romance|love]] is the novel's crowning affirmation of second chances.

## Character Overview

Miss Lavendar Lewis is one of Montgomery's most enchanting creations. When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] meets her, she is a middle-aged woman living alone at [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] [[humor|wit]]h only her maid [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] for company — a "little lady" whose "lonely, sequestered life of [[ambition|dreams]]" (032-082) has not embittered her. She has preserved a youthful spirit that resonates [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own, and between them springs "one of those fervent, helpful friendships possible only between a woman who has kept the freshness of youth in her heart and soul, and a girl whose imagination and intuition supplied the [[belonging|place]] of experience" (032-080).

Her story — a quarrel [[humor|wit]]h her [[romance|love]]r [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] twenty-five years ago, followed by decades of proud silence — is the novel's most powerful argument against the destructiveness of pride and the necessity of forgiveness.

## Role in the Story

[[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s [[love|romance]] plot is the novel's emotional centerpiece. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], through her friendship [[humor|wit]]h [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], discovers [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s past connection to [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] and becomes the un[[humor|wit]]ting catalyst for their reunion. The plot demonstrates that [[romance|love]] can survive decades of silence — and that "the very" essence of [[love|romance]] (042-008) is not youthful passion but enduring [[faith|fidelity]].

**[[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]** is [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s lost [[romance|love]], separated from her by a quarter-century quarrel and reunited through [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s intervention and [[paul-irving|Paul]]'s innocent revelation (031-064).

## Character Analysis

[[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] embodies the novel's argument that life's best gifts can arrive late. Her happiness at forty-five is no less real — perhaps more real — than the youthful passion she lost at twenty. Montgomery refuses the [[tradition|convention]]al narrative that [[love|romance]] belongs only to the young, offering instead a vision of [[romance|love]] that is patient, enduring, and ultimately triumphant.
