## Character Overview

Mrs. Harmon Andrews is the matriarch of the Andrews family in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] and the mother of [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]], one of [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s school friends. She is a figure of social authority whose disapproval carries weight in the [[neighbor|community]], and she participates actively in the networks of gossip, social gatherings, and moral surveillance that characterize [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s matronly establishment.

## Role in Story

Mrs. Harmon Andrews first appears through reported speech — someone considers it "her duty to inform me that Mrs. Harmon Andrews didn't approve" [009-036] of a particular matter. This introduction establishes her as a social arbiter whose opinions are both known and consequential.

[[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]], her husband, appears in several practical contexts: the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] meets at his house [019-049], he offers agricultural advice [020-081], and he is seen in the [[neighbor|community]] by [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] [024-085]. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] describes dealing [[humor|wit]]h him as a challenge, noting "I've heard him" [[humor|wit]]h a tone of exasperation [024-092].

Mrs. Harmon Andrews herself appears at social gatherings alongside other [[avonlea|Avonlea]] matrons [041-026], and the narrative notes that [[avonlea|Avonlea]] gossip whispered certain things about her [041-046], placing her [[humor|wit]]hin the reciprocal scrutiny that defines small-town social life.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Harmon Andrews represents a recognizable type in small-[[neighbor|community]] fiction: the matron whose opinions serve as a form of informal regulation. That her disapproval is reported as a matter of "duty" [009-036] reveals the self-sustaining [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s moral economy — information about judgment circulates as efficiently as information about events.

The Andrews family as a whole — Mr. and Mrs. Harmon Andrews, their children [[jane-andrews|Jane]], Billy, and Prissy — constitutes one of the novel's extended [[avonlea|Avonlea]] clans, alongside the Sloanes, the Pyes, and the Barrys. Mrs. Harmon Andrews's role [[humor|wit]]hin this family structure is to uphold standards of propriety and to participate in the social rituals that bind the [[neighbor|community]] together. The gossip that "whispered" about her [041-046] suggests that even those who judge are themselves judged — a hallmark of Montgomery's balanced, humane portrayal of small-town life.
