# Loneliness

The ache of isolation that surfaces throughout *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — in [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]]'s confession of being "in [[romance|love]] [[humor|wit]]h loneliness" (040-117), in the "lonely and sorrowful" landscape that echoes a character's inner state (003-017), and in the quiet observations of those who watch from the margins of [[neighbor|community]] life.

## Definition

Montgomery treats loneliness not as a pathology but as a universal human experience — one that touches the young and old, the newcomer and the longtime resident, the orphan and the woman surrounded by family. Loneliness in the novel takes multiple forms: the solitude of the landscape (003-017), the isolation of the orphan (030-126), the emotional distance between people who share a household, and the peculiar loneliness of the eccentric who does not fit [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s social patterns.

The novel is notably [[kindness|compassion]]ate about loneliness. Characters who experience it — [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], even [[anne-shirley|Anne]] herself in her quieter moments — are not judged for it. Montgomery presents loneliness as a condition that deserves remedy, not a character flaw that deserves censure.

## Examples in the Text

**The Landscape of Loneliness (Chapter 3).** On her walk to confront [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] passes through a landscape "through it but the wind. How lonely and sorrowful" (003-017). Montgomery externalizes [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s inner state — her dread, her isolation as a young teacher facing an irate neighbor — through [[nature-appreciation|the natural world]]. The lonely landscape mirrors the lonely heart, a technique Montgomery uses throughout the novel.

**[[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]]'s Confession (Chapter 30).** "You see, I was so lonely. I [[romance|love]] company" (030-126) — [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]]'s admission is matter-of-fact, almost cheerful, but it reveals a life shaped by isolation. "[[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] was lonely too. So I just" (030-128) — the sentence trails off, suggesting that loneliness leads to action, not paralysis: [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]] found [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], as [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] found her, because loneliness drove both to seek connection.

**Contented Loneliness (Chapter 40).** [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]] tells [[anne-shirley|Anne]] she is "in [[romance|love]] [[humor|wit]]h loneliness, [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]], she says to me" (040-117) — this is the novel's most sophisticated treatment of the theme. Loneliness can become a companion, even a comfort, for those who have lived [[humor|wit]]h it long enough. Montgomery does not romanticize this — [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]]'s version of loneliness is tinged [[humor|wit]]h self-protection — but she acknowledges it as a genuine adaptation. Not everyone who is alone is unhappy about it.

**The Lonely Tree (Chapter 27).** [[davy-keith|Davy]] reports that "the little tree was so lonely it died of a broken heart" (027-035) — a child's anthropomorphic observation that carries genuine psychological [[wisdom|insight]]. Montgomery uses [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s simple statement to suggest that loneliness is not merely a feeling but a condition that can harm, even destroy, living things. The tree's death is a quietly devastating metaphor.

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Seclusion (Chapter 32).** The narrative visits "the little lady's lonely, sequestered life" (032-082) — [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s loneliness is of the self-chosen variety, born of romantic disappointment and the passage of time. Montgomery treats it [[humor|wit]]h particular tenderness: this is loneliness as refuge, not punishment, a way of protecting a tender heart from further wounding.

## Thematic Significance

Loneliness is the novel's emotional ground bass — the note that sounds beneath even the sunniest passages. Montgomery's [[avonlea|Avonlea]] is a [[neighbor|community]], but [[neighbor|community]] does not automatically banish loneliness; it coexists [[humor|wit]]h it. The novel's most powerful moments often come when a character is alone: [[anne-shirley|Anne]] walking [[belonging|home]] from school in the twilight, [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] watching the stars, [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] in her echo lodge.

Montgomery answers loneliness [[humor|wit]]h connection — but the connection is always particular, never generic. [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta]] finds [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]; [[anne-shirley|Anne]] finds [[paul-irving|Paul]]; [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] finds Stephen. The cure for loneliness is not "the [[neighbor|community]]" in the abstract but a specific person who sees and understands. The novel's romantic subplots — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] and Stephen — are, at their core, stories about loneliness being answered by [[romance|love]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Loneliness as Suffering vs. Loneliness as Sanctuary.** For [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], loneliness is suffering — she seeks company because isolation hurts. For [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], loneliness is partly sanctuary — it protects her from further disappointment. Montgomery validates both experiences [[humor|wit]]hout ranking them. The test is not whether one is alone but whether one's aloneness is freely chosen.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Loneliness vs. [[paul-irving|Paul]]'s Loneliness.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s loneliness is adult — the isolation of [[duty|responsibility]], the solitude of leadership, the distance between teacher and pupil. [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s loneliness is the child's version — the ache for a parent, the feeling of being different from other children. Montgomery's parallel treatment of both shows her [[wisdom|understanding]] that loneliness does not discriminate by age.
