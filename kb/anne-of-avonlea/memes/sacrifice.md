# Sacrifice

The act of forgoing personal desire for a larger good — whether [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s renunciation of the summer school to teach, the "sacrifice" of possessions mentioned in passing (022-053), or the quieter daily sacrifices that constitute responsible adulthood in [[avonlea|Avonlea]].

## Definition

Sacrifice in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is treated as a quiet, often unremarkable virtue. Montgomery does not dramatize sacrifice as heroic renunciation but embeds it in the texture of ordinary life: the flowers that "would have to be sacrificed sometime" (022-053), the personal pleasures deferred for duty, the ambitions set aside for [[neighbor|community]] [[duty|obligation]]s. Sacrifice is presented not as [[death|loss]] but as a form of investment — what is given up today enables something larger tomorrow.

The novel is notably unsentimental about sacrifice. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] does not agonize over her decision to postpone college; she simply does it, because she recognizes it as the right thing to do. Montgomery's treatment aligns [[humor|wit]]h her broader ethical vision: the truly virtuous act is the one done [[humor|wit]]hout fanfare, not the one loudly proclaimed.

## Examples in the Text

**The Garden's Sacrifice (Chapter 22).** "I know they would have to be sacrificed sometime" (022-053) — the context is domestic and small-scale, but Montgomery invests it [[humor|wit]]h thematic weight. The flowers that must be given up for the garden's larger design mirror [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own quiet renunciations: college postponed, ambitions deferred, personal pleasures set aside for the good of others. Sacrifice, in this vision, is not dramatic martyrdom but the steady, unglamorous work of responsible living.

**[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s [[education|Education]] vs. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Peace (Chapter 37).** "The future can't be sacrificed to [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s thirst for information" (037-168) — the line is comic in context, but it reveals Montgomery's careful calibration of what sacrifice means. Not every demand deserves to be met; not every need should be prioritized over peace and balance. Sacrifice, rightly understood, requires discernment about what is worth giving up and what is not.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Postponement of College.** The novel's central sacrifice — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] giving up [[redmond-college|Redmond]] to stay [[humor|wit]]h [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] — occurs in the space between *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[green-gables|Green Gables]]* and *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, but its shadow falls across the entire narrative. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching career, her maturation into a [[neighbor|community]] leader, and her deepening relationship [[humor|wit]]h [[avonlea|Avonlea]] all flow from this initial act of self-denial. Montgomery never allows [[anne-shirley|Anne]] to complain about it; the sacrifice is presented as so obviously right that it requires no narrative fanfare.

## Thematic Significance

Sacrifice in Montgomery's moral universe is inseparable from growth. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] becomes an adult not by achieving what she wants but by choosing what is right. The novel suggests — [[humor|wit]]hout ever stating it directly — that sacrifice is the price of maturity, and that those who refuse to pay it remain, like the more self-centered villagers, in a state of arrested development.

Montgomery also distinguishes between meaningful and meaningless sacrifice. The flowers "sacrificed" for the garden design (022-053) contribute to a larger beauty; the "future sacrificed to [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s thirst for information" (037-168) would be squandered on a trivial demand. The test of a sacrifice's worth is whether it serves genuine growth — one's own or another's.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Sacrifice as [[duty|Duty]] vs. Sacrifice as Self-[[diana-barry|Di]]minishment.** The novel presents sacrifice primarily as duty — the natural [[duty|obligation]] of a responsible person to others. But it also acknowledges, through its treatment of characters like [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], that excessive sacrifice can become self-diminishment. The healthy characters in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] sacrifice from strength, not from a belief that they deserve less.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Sacrifice vs. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Sacrifice.** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s life has been shaped by a series of sacrifices — caring for Matthew, raising [[anne-shirley|Anne]], managing [[green-gables|Green Gables]] — that predate the novel. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s sacrifice is newer, more conscious, and potentially temporary (college may come later). Montgomery suggests, [[humor|wit]]hout moralizing, that sacrifice made freely ([[anne-shirley|Anne]]) is healthier than sacrifice assumed as identity ([[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]).
