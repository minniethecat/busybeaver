[013-001] “[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] and John Henry are both away . . . they went to town

[013-002] today. I’ll go for [[mr-barry|Mr. Barry]].”

[013-003] Mr. Barry came back with [[anne-shirley|Anne]], carrying a coil of rope to which was

[013-004] attached a claw-like instrument that had been the business end of a

[013-005] grubbing fork. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] and Anne stood by, cold and shaken with horror

[013-006] and dread, while Mr. Barry dragged the well, and [[davy-keith|Davy]], astride the

[013-007] gate, watched the group with a face indicative of huge enjoyment.

[013-008] Finally Mr. Barry shook his head, with a relieved air.

[013-009] “She can’t be down there. It’s a mighty curious thing where she could

[013-010] have got to, though. Look here, young man, are you sure you’ve no idea

[013-011] where your sister is?”

[013-012] “I’ve told you a dozen times that I haven’t,” said Davy, with an

[013-013] injured air. “Maybe a tramp come and stole her.”

[013-014] “Nonsense,” said Marilla sharply, relieved from her horrible fear of

[013-015] the well. “Anne, do you suppose she could have strayed over to Mr.

[013-016] Harrison’s? She has always been talking about his parrot ever since

[013-017] that time you took her over.”

[013-018] “I can’t believe [[dora-keith|Dora]] would venture so far alone but I’ll go over and

[013-019] see,” said Anne.

[013-020] Nobody was looking at Davy just then or it would have been seen that a

[013-021] very decided change came over his face. He quietly slipped off the gate

[013-022] and ran, as fast as his fat legs could carry him, to the barn.

[013-023] Anne hastened across the fields to the Harrison establishment in no

[013-024] very hopeful frame of mind. The house was locked, the window shades

[013-025] were down, and there was no sign of anything living about the [[belonging|place]].

[013-026] She stood on the veranda and called Dora loudly.

[013-027] Ginger, in the kitchen behind her, shrieked and swore with sudden

[013-028] fierceness; but between his outbursts Anne heard a plaintive cry from

[013-029] the little building in the yard which served Mr. Harrison as a

[013-030] toolhouse. Anne flew to the door, unhasped it, and caught up a small

[013-031] mortal with a tearstained face who was sitting forlornly on an upturned

[013-032] nail keg.

[013-033] “Oh, Dora, Dora, what a fright you have given us! How came you to be

[013-034] here?”

[013-035] “Davy and I came over to see Ginger,” sobbed Dora, “but we couldn’t see

[013-036] him after all, only Davy made him swear by kicking the door. And then

[013-037] Davy brought me here and run out and shut the door; and I couldn’t get

[013-038] out. I cried and cried, I was frightened, and oh, I’m so hungry and

[013-039] cold; and I thought you’d never come, Anne.”

[013-040] “Davy?” But Anne could say no more. She carried Dora home with a heavy

[013-041] heart. Her [[laughter|joy]] at finding the child safe and sound was drowned out in

[013-042] the pain caused by Davy’s behavior. The freak of shutting Dora up might

[013-043] easily have been pardoned. But Davy had told falsehoods . . . downright

[013-044] coldblooded falsehoods about it. That was the ugly fact and Anne could

[013-045] not shut her eyes to it. She could have sat down and cried with sheer

[013-046] disappointment. She had grown to [[romance|love]] Davy dearly . . . how dearly she

[013-047] had not known until this minute . . . and it hurt her unbearably to

[013-048] discover that he was guilty of deliberate falsehood.

[013-049] Marilla listened to Anne’s tale in a silence that boded no good

[013-050] Davy-ward; Mr. Barry laughed and advised that Davy be summarily dealt

[013-051] with. When he had gone home Anne soothed and warmed the sobbing,

[013-052] shivering Dora, got her her supper and put her to bed. Then she

[013-053] returned to the kitchen, just as Marilla came grimly in, leading, or

[013-054] rather pulling, the reluctant, cobwebby Davy, whom she had just found

[013-055] hidden away in the darkest corner of the stable.

[013-056] She jerked him to the mat on the middle of the floor and then went and

[013-057] sat down by the east window. Anne was sitting limply by the west

[013-058] window. Between them stood the culprit. His back was toward Marilla and

[013-059] it was a meek, subdued, frightened back; but his face was toward Anne

[013-060] and although it was a little shamefaced there was a gleam of

[013-061] comradeship in Davy’s eyes, as if he knew he had done wrong and was

[013-062] going to be punished for it, but could count on a laugh over it all

[013-063] with Anne later on.

[013-064] But no half hidden smile answered him in Anne’s gray eyes, as there

[013-065] might have done had it been only a question of mischief. There was

[013-066] something else . . . something ugly and repulsive.

[013-067] “How could you behave so, Davy?” she asked sorrowfully.

[013-068] Davy squirmed uncomfortably.

[013-069] “I just did it for fun. Things have been so awful quiet here for so

[013-070] long that I thought it would be fun to give you folks a big scare. It

[013-071] was, too.”

[013-072] In spite of fear and a little [[regret|remorse]] Davy grinned over the

[013-073] recollection.

[013-074] “But you told a falsehood about it, Davy,” said Anne, more sorrowfully

[013-075] than ever.

[013-076] Davy looked puzzled.

[013-077] “What’s a falsehood? Do you mean a whopper?”

[013-078] “I mean a story that was not true.”

[013-079] “Course I did,” said Davy frankly. “If I hadn’t you wouldn’t have been

[013-080] scared. I _had_ to tell it.”

[013-081] Anne was feeling the reaction from her fright and exertions. Davy’s

[013-082] impenitent attitude gave the finishing touch. Two big tears brimmed up

[013-083] in her eyes.

[013-084] “Oh, Davy, how could you?” she said, with a quiver in her voice. “Don’t

[013-085] you know how wrong it was?”

[013-086] Davy was aghast. Anne crying . . . he had made Anne cry! A flood of

[013-087] real remorse rolled like a wave over his warm little heart and engulfed

[013-088] it. He rushed to Anne, hurled himself into her lap, flung his arms

[013-089] around her neck, and burst into tears.

[013-090] “I didn’t know it was wrong to tell whoppers,” he sobbed. “How did you

[013-091] expect me to know it was wrong? All Mr. Sprott’s children told them

[013-092] _regular_ every day, and cross their hearts too. I s’pose [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]

[013-093] never tells whoppers and here I’ve been trying awful hard to be as good

[013-094] as him, but now I s’pose you’ll never [[romance|love]] me again. But I think you

[013-095] might have told me it was wrong. I’m awful sorry I’ve made you cry,

[013-096] Anne, and I’ll never tell a whopper again.”

[013-097] Davy buried his face in Anne’s shoulder and cried stormily. Anne, in a

[013-098] sudden glad flash of [[wisdom|understanding]], held him tight and looked over his

[013-099] curly thatch at Marilla.

[013-100] “He didn’t know it was wrong to tell falsehoods, Marilla. I think we

[013-101] must forgive him for that part of it this time if he will promise never

[013-102] to say what isn’t true again.”

[013-103] “I never will, now that I know it’s bad,” asseverated Davy between

[013-104] sobs. “If you ever catch me telling a whopper again you can . . .” Davy

[013-105] groped mentally for a suitable penance . . . “you can skin me alive,

[013-106] Anne.”

[013-107] “Don’t say ‘whopper,’ Davy . . . say ‘falsehood,’” said the

[013-108] schoolma’am.

[013-109] “Why?” queried Davy, settling comfortably down and looking up with a

[013-110] tearstained, investigating face. “Why ain’t whopper as good as

[013-111] falsehood? I want to know. It’s just as big a word.”

[013-112] “It’s slang; and it’s wrong for little boys to use slang.”

[013-113] “There’s an awful lot of things it’s wrong to do,” said Davy with a

[013-114] sigh. “I never s’posed there was so many. I’m sorry it’s wrong to tell

[013-115] whop . . . falsehoods, ’cause it’s awful handy, but since it is I’m

[013-116] never going to tell any more. What are you going to do to me for

[013-117] telling them this time? I want to know.” Anne looked beseechingly at

[013-118] Marilla.

[013-119] “I don’t want to be too hard on the child,” said Marilla. “I daresay

[013-120] nobody ever did tell him it was wrong to tell lies, and those Sprott

[013-121] children were no fit companions for him. Poor Mary was too sick to

[013-122] train him properly and I presume you couldn’t expect a six-year-old

[013-123] child to know things like that by instinct. I suppose we’ll just have

[013-124] to assume he doesn’t know _anything_ right and begin at the beginning.

[013-125] But he’ll have to be punished for shutting Dora up, and I can’t think

[013-126] of any way except to send him to bed without his supper and we’ve done

[013-127] that so often. Can’t you suggest something else, Anne? I should think

[013-128] you ought to be able to, with that imagination you’re always talking

[013-129] of.”

[013-130] “But punishments are so horrid and I like to imagine only pleasant

[013-131] things,” said Anne, cuddling Davy. “There are so many unpleasant things

[013-132] in the world already that there is no use in imagining any more.”

[013-133] In the end Davy was sent to bed, as usual, there to remain until noon

[013-134] next day. He evidently did some thinking, for when Anne went up to her

[013-135] room a little later she heard him calling her name softly. Going in,

[013-136] she found him sitting up in bed, with his elbows on his knees and his

[013-137] chin propped on his hands.

[013-138] “Anne,” he said solemnly, “is it wrong for everybody to tell whop . . .

[013-139] falsehoods? I want to know?”

[013-140] “Yes, indeed.”

[013-141] “Is it wrong for a grown-up person?”

[013-142] “Yes.”

[013-143] “Then,” said Davy decidedly, “Marilla is bad, for _she_ tells them. And

[013-144] she’s worse’n me, for I didn’t know it was wrong but she does.”

[013-145] “Davy Keith, Marilla never told a story in her life,” said Anne

[013-146] indignantly.

[013-147] “She did so. She told me last Tuesday that something dreadful _would_

[013-148] happen to me if I didn’t say my prayers every night. And I haven’t said

[013-149] them for over a week, just to see what would happen . . . and nothing

[013-150] has,” concluded Davy in an aggrieved tone.

[013-151] Anne choked back a mad desire to laugh with the conviction that it

[013-152] would be fatal, and then earnestly set about saving Marilla’s

[013-153] reputation.

[013-154] “Why, Davy Keith,” she said solemnly, “something dreadful _has_

[013-155] happened to you this very day.”

[013-156] Davy looked sceptical.

[013-157] “I s’pose you mean being sent to bed without any supper,” he said

[013-158] scornfully, “but _that_ isn’t dreadful. Course, I don’t like it, but

[013-159] I’ve been sent to bed so much since I come here that I’m getting used

[013-160] to it. And you don’t save anything by making me go without supper

[013-161] either, for I always eat twice as much for breakfast.”

[013-162] “I don’t mean your being sent to bed. I mean the fact that you told a

[013-163] falsehood today. And, Davy,” . . . Anne leaned over the footboard of

[013-164] the bed and shook her finger impressively at the culprit . . . “for a

[013-165] boy to tell what isn’t true is almost the worst thing that _could_

[013-166] happen to him . . . almost the very worst. So you see Marilla told you

[013-167] the truth.”

[013-168] “But I thought the something bad would be exciting,” protested Davy in

[013-169] an injured tone.

[013-170] “Marilla isn’t to blame for what you thought. Bad things aren’t always

[013-171] exciting. They’re very often just nasty and stupid.”

[013-172] “It was awful funny to see Marilla and you looking down the well,

[013-173] though,” said Davy, hugging his knees.

[013-174] Anne kept a sober face until she got downstairs and then she collapsed

[013-175] on the sitting room lounge and laughed until her sides ached.

[013-176] “I wish you’d tell me the joke,” said Marilla, a little grimly. “I

[013-177] haven’t seen much to laugh at today.”

[013-178] “You’ll laugh when you hear this,” assured Anne. And Marilla did laugh,

[013-179] which showed how much her [[teaching|education]] had advanced since the adoption of

[013-180] Anne. But she sighed immediately afterwards.

[013-181] “I suppose I shouldn’t have told him that, although I heard a minister

[013-182] say it to a child once. But he did aggravate me so. It was that night

[013-183] you were at the [[carmody|Carmody]] concert and I was putting him to bed. He said

[013-184] he didn’t see the good of praying until he got big enough to be of some

[013-185] importance to God. Anne, I do not know what we are going to do with

[013-186] that child. I never saw his beat. I’m feeling clean discouraged.”
