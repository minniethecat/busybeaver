# Gratitude

A quiet virtue that runs through *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — less dramatic than [[ambition|ambition]] or [[love]], but presented by Montgomery as essential to a well-lived life. Gratitude appears not as mere politeness but as a spiritual practice, a way of seeing the world that transforms ordinary experience into [[joy]].

## Definition

In the moral universe of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, gratitude is the conscious recognition of goodness received. It is not an automatic feeling but a discipline — something one must "remember to be" (033-143). Montgomery distinguishes between the natural thankfulness that arises in moments of beauty and the deliberate practice of gratitude during ordinary times, suggesting that the latter is the truer test of character.

The novel treats ingratitude as a genuine moral failing. When [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] recalls a child's "ingratitude" (006-136), the word carries weight — ingratitude is not merely bad manners but a failure to recognize the web of obligation and care that sustains us. Conversely, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "owe[s] him a real debt of gratitude" (036-156) — the language of debt implying that gratitude is a moral obligation, not an optional sentiment.

## Examples in the Text

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Quiet Thankfulness (Chapter 10).** After a long day, "Marilla was a thankful woman when she drove into her own yard" (010-043). The thankfulness is not for any grand blessing but for the simple fact of home and rest. Marilla's gratitude is characteristically undemonstrative — a private satisfaction rather than a public virtue — but it is no less real for its quietness.

**Gratitude for [[friendship]] (Chapter 21).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] tells [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]: "I'm so thankful for friendship. It beautifies life" (021-116). The statement reveals gratitude as Anne's response to the gift of [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirits]]. Gratitude here is not passive — it "beautifies" the life of the grateful person, transforming the received gift into an active source of joy.

**The Discipline of Thankfulness (Chapter 33).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] reflects: "In November you have to remember to be thankful; but in May one simply can't help being thankful . . . that they are alive, if for nothing else" (033-143–144). This is the novel's clearest statement on gratitude as a practice. In dark seasons, gratitude must be willed; in seasons of abundance, it arises spontaneously. The wisdom lies in recognizing both and practicing the former when the latter fails.

**A Debt of Gratitude (Chapter 36).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] acknowledges: "I owe him a real debt of gratitude" (036-156). The metaphor of debt frames gratitude as a moral obligation — something owed, whether or not it can be repaid. This language appears elsewhere in Montgomery's moral vocabulary, linking gratitude to [[duty]] and [[justice]].

## Thematic Significance

**Gratitude as a Discipline.** The novel's most distinctive contribution to the theme is the idea that gratitude must be practiced, not merely felt. The November/May distinction (033-143) is a small piece of practical wisdom: when gratitude comes easily, enjoy it; when it does not, remember to be thankful anyway.

**Ingratitude as Moral Failure.** Montgomery treats ingratitude seriously — not as a social faux pas but as a character flaw. The children whose ingratitude Marilla recalls are not merely rude; they have failed to understand their place in a network of care. Gratitude, in this view, is the foundation of [[community]] — the recognition that we are all debtors to one another.

**Gratitude and [[generosity]].** The two virtues are linked throughout the novel. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s desire to "show my gratitude by doing something for the folk" (009-070) connects thankfulness to action. Gratitude that remains merely a feeling is incomplete; true gratitude expresses itself in [[generosity]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Marilla's Quiet Gratitude vs. Anne's Expressive Thankfulness.** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] feels gratitude privately; [[anne-shirley|Anne]] names it and shares it. Neither approach is presented as superior — Montgomery allows both the introvert's and the extrovert's modes of thankfulness. Marilla's quiet "thankful woman" (010-043) is as genuine as Anne's effusive declarations.

**Spontaneous vs. Deliberate Gratitude.** The November/May contrast (033-143) is not just seasonal but spiritual. In times of natural abundance (May, youth, health), gratitude is easy. In times of scarcity (November, difficulty, loss), gratitude requires effort. The novel gently insists that the latter is the more important practice.
