[001-044] I

[001-045] An Irate Neighbor

[001-046] A tall, slim girl, “half-past sixteen,” with serious gray eyes and hair

[001-047] which her friends called auburn, had sat down on the broad red

[001-048] sandstone doorstep of a [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] farmhouse one ripe

[001-049] afternoon in August, firmly resolved to construe so many lines of

[001-050] Virgil.

[001-051] But an August afternoon, with blue hazes scarfing the harvest slopes,

[001-052] little winds whispering elfishly in the poplars, and a dancing slendor

[001-053] of red poppies outflaming against the dark coppice of young firs in a

[001-054] corner of the cherry orchard, was fitter for [[ambition|dreams]] than dead

[001-055] languages. The Virgil soon slipped unheeded to the ground, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]],

[001-056] her chin propped on her clasped hands, and her eyes on the splendid

[001-057] mass of fluffy clouds that were heaping up just over Mr. J. A.

[001-058] Harrison’s house like a great white mountain, was far away in a

[001-059] delicious world where a certain schoolteacher was doing a wonderful

[001-060] work, shaping the destinies of future statesmen, and inspiring youthful

[001-061] minds and hearts with high and lofty ambitions.

[001-062] To be sure, if you came down to harsh facts . . . which, it must be

[001-063] confessed, Anne seldom did until she had to . . . it did not seem

[001-064] likely that there was much promising material for celebrities in

[001-065] Avonlea school; but you could never tell what might happen if a teacher

[001-066] used her influence for good. Anne had certain rose-tinted ideals of

[001-067] what a teacher might accomplish if she only went the right way about

[001-068] it; and she was in the midst of a delightful scene, forty years hence,

[001-069] with a famous personage . . . just exactly what he was to be famous for

[001-070] was left in convenient haziness, but Anne thought it would be rather

[001-071] nice to have him a college president or a Canadian premier . . . bowing

[001-072] low over her wrinkled hand and assuring her that it was she who had

[001-073] first kindled his ambition, and that all his success in life was due to

[001-074] the lessons she had instilled so long ago in Avonlea school. This

[001-075] pleasant vision was shattered by a most unpleasant interruption.

[001-076] A demure little Jersey cow came scuttling down the lane and five

[001-077] seconds later Mr. Harrison arrived . . . if “arrived” be not too mild a

[001-078] term to describe the manner of his irruption into the yard.

[001-079] He bounced over the fence without waiting to open the gate, and angrily

[001-080] confronted astonished Anne, who had risen to her feet and stood looking

[001-081] at him in some bewilderment. Mr. Harrison was their new righthand

[001-082] neighbor and she had never met him before, although she had seen him

[001-083] once or twice.

[001-084] In early April, before Anne had come [[belonging|home]] from Queen’s, Mr. Robert

[001-085] Bell, whose farm adjoined the Cuthbert place on the west, had sold out

[001-086] and moved to [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]]. His farm had been bought by a certain Mr.

[001-087] J. A. Harrison, whose name, and the fact that he was a New Brunswick

[001-088] man, were all that was known about him. But before he had been a month

[001-089] in Avonlea he had won the reputation of being an odd person . . . “a

[001-090] crank,” [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] said. Mrs. Rachel was an outspoken lady, as

[001-091] those of you who may have already made her acquaintance will remember.

[001-092] Mr. Harrison was certainly different from other people . . . and that

[001-093] is the essential characteristic of a crank, as everybody knows.

[001-094] In the first place he kept house for himself and had publicly stated

[001-095] that he wanted no fools of women around his diggings. Feminine Avonlea

[001-096] took its revenge by the gruesome tales it related about his

[001-097] house-keeping and cooking. He had hired little John Henry Carter of

[001-098] [[white-sands|White Sands]] and John Henry started the stories. For one thing, there

[001-099] was never any stated time for meals in the Harrison establishment. Mr.

[001-100] Harrison “got a bite” when he felt hungry, and if John Henry were

[001-101] around at the time, he came in for a share, but if he were not, he had

[001-102] to wait until Mr. Harrison’s next hungry spell. John Henry mournfully

[001-103] averred that he would have starved to death if it wasn’t that he got

[001-104] home on Sundays and got a good filling up, and that his mother always

[001-105] gave him a basket of “grub” to take back with him on Monday mornings.

[001-106] As for washing dishes, Mr. Harrison never made any pretence of doing it

[001-107] unless a rainy Sunday came. Then he went to work and washed them all at

[001-108] once in the rainwater hogshead, and left them to drain dry.

[001-109] Again, Mr. Harrison was “close.” When he was asked to subscribe to the

[001-110] Rev. [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]]’s salary he said he’d wait and see how many dollars’

[001-111] worth of good he got out of his preaching first . . . he didn’t believe

[001-112] in buying a pig in a poke. And when [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] went to ask for a

[001-113] contribution to missions . . . and incidentally to see the inside of

[001-114] the house . . . he told her there were more heathens among the old

[001-115] woman gossips in Avonlea than anywhere else he knew of, and he’d

[001-116] cheerfully contribute to a mission for Christianizing them if she’d

[001-117] undertake it. Mrs. Rachel got herself away and said it was a mercy poor

[001-118] Mrs. Robert Bell was safe in her grave, for it would have broken her

[001-119] heart to see the state of her house in which she used to take so much

[001-120] pride.

[001-121] “Why, she scrubbed the kitchen floor every second day,” [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] told

[001-122] Marilla Cuthbert indignantly, “and if you could see it now! I had to

[001-123] hold up my skirts as I walked across it.”

[001-124] Finally, Mr. Harrison kept a parrot called Ginger. Nobody in Avonlea

[001-125] had ever kept a parrot before; consequently that proceeding was

[001-126] considered barely respectable. And such a parrot! If you took John

[001-127] Henry Carter’s word for it, never was such an unholy bird. It swore

[001-128] terribly. Mrs. Carter would have taken John Henry away at once if she

[001-129] had been sure she could get another place for him. Besides, Ginger had

[001-130] bitten a piece right out of the back of John Henry’s neck one day when

[001-131] he had stooped down too near the cage. Mrs. Carter showed everybody the

[001-132] mark when the luckless John Henry went home on Sundays.

[001-133] All these things flashed through Anne’s mind as Mr. Harrison stood,

[001-134] quite speechless with wrath apparently, before her. In his most amiable

[001-135] mood Mr. Harrison could not have been considered a handsome man; he was

[001-136] short and fat and bald; and now, with his round face purple with rage

[001-137] and his prominent blue eyes almost sticking out of his head, Anne

[001-138] thought he was really the ugliest person she had ever seen.

[001-139] All at once Mr. Harrison found his voice.

[001-140] “I’m not going to put up with this,” he spluttered, “not a day longer,

[001-141] do you hear, miss. Bless my soul, this is the third time, miss . . .

[001-142] the third time! [[patience|Patience]] has ceased to be a virtue, miss. I warned your

[001-143] aunt the last time not to let it occur again . . . and she’s let it . .

[001-144] . she’s done it . . . what does she mean by it, that is what I want to

[001-145] know. That is what I’m here about, miss.”

[001-146] “Will you explain what the trouble is?” asked Anne, in her most

[001-147] dignified manner. She had been practicing it considerably of late to

[001-148] have it in good working order when school began; but it had no apparent

[001-149] effect on the irate J. A. Harrison.

[001-150] “Trouble, is it? Bless my soul, trouble enough, I should think. The

[001-151] trouble is, miss, that I found that Jersey cow of your aunt’s in my

[001-152] oats again, not half an hour ago. The third time, mark you. I found her

[001-153] in last Tuesday and I found her in yesterday. I came here and told your

[001-154] aunt not to let it occur again. She has let it occur again. Where’s

[001-155] your aunt, miss? I just want to see her for a minute and give her a

[001-156] piece of my mind . . . a piece of J. A. Harrison’s mind, miss.”

[001-157] “If you mean Miss Marilla Cuthbert, she is not my aunt, and she has

[001-158] gone down to [[grafton|East Grafton]] to see a distant relative of hers who is very

[001-159] ill,” said Anne, with due increase of [[pride|dignity]] at every word. “I am very

[001-160] sorry that my cow should have broken into your oats . . . she is my cow

[001-161] and not Miss Cuthbert’s . . . Matthew gave her to me three years ago

[001-162] when she was a little calf and he bought her from [[mr-bell|Mr. Bell]].”

[001-163] “Sorry, miss! Sorry isn’t going to help matters any. You’d better go

[001-164] and look at the havoc that animal has made in my oats . . . trampled

[001-165] them from center to circumference, miss.”

[001-166] “I am very sorry,” repeated Anne firmly, “but perhaps if you kept your

[001-167] fences in better repair Dolly might not have broken in. It is your part

[001-168] of the line fence that separates your oatfield from our pasture and I

[001-169] noticed the other day that it was not in very good condition.”

[001-170] “My fence is all right,” snapped Mr. Harrison, angrier than ever at

[001-171] this carrying of the war into the enemy’s country. “The jail fence

[001-172] couldn’t keep a demon of a cow like that out. And I can tell you, you

[001-173] redheaded snippet, that if the cow is yours, as you say, you’d be

[001-174] better employed in watching her out of other people’s grain than in

[001-175] sitting round reading yellow-covered novels,” . . . with a scathing

[001-176] glance at the innocent tan-colored Virgil by Anne’s feet.

[001-177] Something at that moment was red besides Anne’s hair . . . which had

[001-178] always been a tender point with her.

[001-179] “I’d rather have red hair than none at all, except a little fringe

[001-180] round my ears,” she flashed.

[001-181] The shot told, for Mr. Harrison was really very sensitive about his

[001-182] bald head. His anger choked him up again and he could only glare

[001-183] speechlessly at Anne, who recovered her temper and followed up her

[001-184] advantage.

[001-185] “I can make allowance for you, Mr. Harrison, because I have an

[001-186] imagination. I can easily imagine how very trying it must be to find a

[001-187] cow in your oats and I shall not cherish any hard feelings against you

[001-188] for the things you’ve said. I promise you that Dolly shall never break

[001-189] into your oats again. I give you my word of honor on _that_ point.”
