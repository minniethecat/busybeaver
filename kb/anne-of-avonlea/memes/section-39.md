[039-001] “Dear old [[jane-andrews|Jane]] is a jewel,” agreed [[anne-shirley|Anne]], “but,” she added, leaning

[039-002] forward to bestow a tender pat on the plump, dimpled little hand

[039-003] hanging over her pillow, “there’s nobody like my own [[diana-barry|Diana]] after all.

[039-004] Do you remember that evening we first met, Diana, and ‘swore’ eternal

[039-005] friendship in your garden? We’ve kept that ‘oath,’ I think . . . we’ve

[039-006] never had a quarrel nor even a coolness. I shall never forget the

[039-007] thrill that went over me the day you told me you loved me. I had had

[039-008] such a lonely, starved heart all through my childhood. I’m just

[039-009] beginning to realize how starved and lonely it really was. Nobody cared

[039-010] anything for me or wanted to be bothered with me. I should have been

[039-011] miserable if it hadn’t been for that strange little dream-life of mine,

[039-012] wherein I imagined all the friends and [[romance|love]] I craved. But when I came

[039-013] to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] everything was changed. And then I met you. You don’t

[039-014] know what your friendship meant to me. I want to thank you here and

[039-015] now, dear, for the warm and true affection you’ve always given me.”

[039-016] “And always, always will,” sobbed Diana. “I shall _never_ [[romance|love]] anybody

[039-017] . . . any _girl_ . . . half as well as I love you. And if I ever do

[039-018] marry and have a little girl of my own I’m going to name her _Anne_.”

[039-019] XXVII

[039-020] An Afternoon at the Stone House

[039-021] “Where are you going, all dressed up, Anne?” [[davy-keith|Davy]] wanted to know. “You

[039-022] look bully in that dress.”

[039-023] Anne had come down to dinner in a new dress of pale green muslin . . .

[039-024] the first color she had worn since Matthew’s death. It became her

[039-025] perfectly, bringing out all the delicate, flower-like tints of her face

[039-026] and the gloss and burnish of her hair.

[039-027] “Davy, how many times have I told you that you mustn’t use that word,”

[039-028] she rebuked. “I’m going to [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]].”

[039-029] “Take me with you,” entreated Davy.

[039-030] “I would if I were driving. But I’m going to walk and it’s too far for

[039-031] your eight-year-old legs. Besides, [[paul-irving|Paul]] is going with me and I fear you

[039-032] don’t enjoy yourself in his company.”

[039-033] “Oh, I like Paul lots better’n I did,” said Davy, beginning to make

[039-034] fearful inroads into his pudding. “Since I’ve got pretty good myself I

[039-035] don’t mind his being gooder so much. If I can keep on I’ll catch up

[039-036] with him some day, both in legs and goodness. ‘Sides, Paul’s real nice

[039-037] to us second primer boys in school. He won’t let the other big boys

[039-038] meddle with us and he shows us lots of games.”

[039-039] “How came Paul to fall into the brook at noon hour yesterday?” asked

[039-040] Anne. “I met him on the playground, such a dripping figure that I sent

[039-041] him promptly [[belonging|home]] for clothes without waiting to find out what had

[039-042] happened.”

[039-043] “Well, it was partly a zacksident,” explained Davy. “He stuck his head

[039-044] in on purpose but the rest of him fell in zacksidentally. We was all

[039-045] down at the brook and Prillie Rogerson got mad at Paul about something

[039-046] . . . she’s awful mean and horrid anyway, if she IS pretty . . . and

[039-047] said that his grandmother put his hair up in curl rags every night.

[039-048] Paul wouldn’t have minded what she said, I guess, but Gracie Andrews

[039-049] laughed, and Paul got awful red, ’cause Gracie’s his girl, you know.

[039-050] He’s _clean gone_ on her . . . brings her flowers and carries her books

[039-051] as far as the shore road. He got as red as a beet and said his

[039-052] grandmother didn’t do any such thing and his hair was born curly. And

[039-053] then he laid down on the bank and stuck his head right into the spring

[039-054] to show them. Oh, it wasn’t the spring we drink out of . . .” seeing a

[039-055] horrified look on [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]’s face . . . “it was the little one lower

[039-056] down. But the bank’s awful slippy and Paul went right in. I tell you he

[039-057] made a bully splash. Oh, Anne, Anne, I didn’t mean to say that . . . it

[039-058] just slipped out before I thought. He made a _splendid_ splash. But he

[039-059] looked so funny when he crawled out, all wet and muddy. The girls

[039-060] laughed more’n ever, but Gracie didn’t laugh. She looked sorry.

[039-061] Gracie’s a nice girl but she’s got a snub nose. When I get big enough

[039-062] to have a girl I won’t have one with a snub nose . . . I’ll pick one

[039-063] with a pretty nose like yours, Anne.”

[039-064] “A boy who makes such a mess of syrup all over his face when he is

[039-065] eating his pudding will never get a girl to look at him,” said Marilla

[039-066] severely.

[039-067] “But I’ll wash my face before I go courting,” protested Davy, trying to

[039-068] improve matters by rubbing the back of his hand over the smears. “And

[039-069] I’ll wash behind my ears too, without being told. I remembered to this

[039-070] morning, Marilla. I don’t forget half as often as I did. But . . .” and

[039-071] Davy sighed . . . “there’s so many corners about a fellow that it’s

[039-072] awful hard to remember them all. Well, if I can’t go to [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]’s

[039-073] I’ll go over and see Mrs. [[mr-harrison|Harrison]]. [[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]]’s an awful nice

[039-074] woman, I tell you. She keeps a jar of cookies in her pantry a-purpose

[039-075] for little boys, and she always gives me the scrapings out of a pan

[039-076] she’s mixed up a plum cake in. A good many plums stick to the sides,

[039-077] you see. Mr. Harrison was always a nice man, but he’s twice as nice

[039-078] since he got married over again. I guess getting married makes folks

[039-079] nicer. Why don’t _you_ get married, Marilla? I want to know.”

[039-080] Marilla’s state of single blessedness had never been a sore point with

[039-081] her, so she answered amiably, with an exchange of significant looks

[039-082] with Anne, that she supposed it was because nobody would have her.

[039-083] “But maybe you never asked anybody to have you,” protested Davy.

[039-084] “Oh, Davy,” said [[dora-keith|Dora]] primly, shocked into speaking without being

[039-085] spoken to, “it’s the _men_ that have to do the asking.”

[039-086] “I don’t know why they have to do it _always_,” grumbled Davy. “Seems

[039-087] to me everything’s put on the men in this world. Can I have some more

[039-088] pudding, Marilla?”

[039-089] “You’ve had as much as was good for you,” said Marilla; but she gave

[039-090] him a moderate second helping.

[039-091] “I wish people could live on pudding. Why can’t they, Marilla? I want

[039-092] to know.”

[039-093] “Because they’d soon get tired of it.”

[039-094] “I’d like to try that for myself,” said skeptical Davy. “But I guess

[039-095] it’s better to have pudding only on fish and company days than none at

[039-096] all. They never have any at Milty Boulter’s. Milty says when company

[039-097] comes his mother gives them cheese and cuts it herself . . . one little

[039-098] bit apiece and one over for manners.”

[039-099] “If Milty Boulter talks like that about his mother at least you needn’t

[039-100] repeat it,” said Marilla severely.

[039-101] “Bless my soul,” . . . Davy had picked this expression up from Mr.

[039-102] Harrison and used it with great gusto . . . “Milty meant it as a

[039-103] compelment. He’s awful proud of his mother, cause folks say she could

[039-104] scratch a living on a rock.”

[039-105] “I . . . I suppose them pesky hens are in my pansy bed again,” said

[039-106] Marilla, rising and going out hurriedly.

[039-107] The slandered hens were nowhere near the pansy bed and Marilla did not

[039-108] even glance at it. Instead, she sat down on the cellar hatch and

[039-109] laughed until she was ashamed of herself.

[039-110] When Anne and Paul reached the stone house that afternoon they found

[039-111] [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] and [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] in the garden, weeding, raking,

[039-112] clipping, and trimming as if for dear life. Miss Lavendar herself, all

[039-113] gay and sweet in the frills and laces she loved, dropped her shears and

[039-114] ran joyously to meet her guests, while Charlotta the Fourth grinned

[039-115] cheerfully.

[039-116] “Welcome, Anne. I thought you’d come today. You belong to the afternoon

[039-117] so it brought you. Things that belong together are sure to come

[039-118] together. What a lot of trouble that would save some people if they

[039-119] only knew it. But they don’t . . . and so they waste beautiful energy

[039-120] moving heaven and earth to bring things together that _don’t_ belong.

[039-121] And you, Paul . . . why, you’ve grown! You’re half a head taller than

[039-122] when you were here before.”

[039-123] “Yes, I’ve begun to grow like pigweed in the night, as [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[039-124] says,” said Paul, in frank delight over the fact. “Grandma says it’s

[039-125] the porridge taking effect at last. Perhaps it is. Goodness knows . .

[039-126] .” Paul sighed deeply . . . “I’ve eaten enough to make anyone grow. I

[039-127] do hope, now that I’ve begun, I’ll keep on till I’m as tall as father.

[039-128] He is six feet, you know, Miss Lavendar.”

[039-129] Yes, Miss Lavendar did know; the flush on her pretty cheeks deepened a

[039-130] little; she took Paul’s hand on one side and Anne’s on the other and

[039-131] walked to the house in silence.

[039-132] “Is it a good day for the echoes, Miss Lavendar?” queried Paul

[039-133] anxiously. The day of his first visit had been too windy for echoes and

[039-134] Paul had been much disappointed.

[039-135] “Yes, just the best kind of a day,” answered Miss Lavendar, rousing

[039-136] herself from her reverie. “But first we are all going to have something

[039-137] to eat. I know you two folks didn’t walk all the way back here through

[039-138] those beechwoods without getting hungry, and Charlotta the Fourth and I

[039-139] can eat any hour of the day . . . we have such obliging appetites. So

[039-140] we’ll just make a raid on the pantry. Fortunately it’s lovely and full.

[039-141] I had a presentiment that I was going to have company today and

[039-142] Charlotta the Fourth and I prepared.”

[039-143] “I think you are one of the people who always have nice things in their

[039-144] pantry,” declared Paul. “Grandma’s like that too. But she doesn’t

[039-145] approve of snacks between meals. I wonder,” he added meditatively, “if

[039-146] I _ought_ to eat them away from home when I know she doesn’t approve.”

[039-147] “Oh, I don’t think she would disapprove after you have had a long walk.

[039-148] That makes a difference,” said Miss Lavendar, exchanging amused glances

[039-149] with Anne over Paul’s brown curls. “I suppose that snacks _are_

[039-150] extremely unwholesome. That is why we have them so often at Echo Lodge.

[039-151] We. . . Charlotta the Fourth and I . . . live in defiance of every

[039-152] known law of diet. We eat all sorts of indigestible things whenever we

[039-153] happen to think of it, by day or night; and we flourish like green bay

[039-154] trees. We are always intending to reform. When we read any article in a

[039-155] paper warning us against something we like we cut it out and pin it up

[039-156] on the kitchen wall so that we’ll remember it. But we never can somehow

[039-157] . . . until after we’ve gone and eaten that very thing. Nothing has

[039-158] ever killed us yet; but Charlotta the Fourth has been known to have bad

[039-159] [[ambition|dreams]] after we had eaten doughnuts and mince pie and fruit cake before

[039-160] we went to bed.”

[039-161] “Grandma lets me have a glass of milk and a slice of bread and butter

[039-162] before I go to bed; and on Sunday nights she puts jam on the bread,”

[039-163] said Paul. “So I’m always glad when it’s Sunday night . . . for more

[039-164] reasons than one. Sunday is a very long day on the shore road. Grandma

[039-165] says it’s all too short for her and that father never found Sundays

[039-166] tiresome when he was a little boy. It wouldn’t seem so long if I could

[039-167] talk to my rock people but I never do that because Grandma doesn’t

[039-168] approve of it on Sundays. I think a good deal; but I’m afraid my

[039-169] thoughts are worldly. Grandma says we should never think anything but

[039-170] religious thoughts on Sundays. But teacher here said once that every

[039-171] really beautiful thought was religious, no matter what it was about, or

[039-172] what day we thought it on. But I feel sure Grandma thinks that sermons

[039-173] and Sunday School lessons are the only things you can think truly

[039-174] religious thoughts about. And when it comes to a difference of opinion

[039-175] between Grandma and teacher I don’t know what to do. In my heart” . . .

[039-176] Paul laid his hand on his breast and raised very serious blue eyes to

[039-177] Miss Lavendar’s immediately sympathetic face . . . “I agree with

[039-178] teacher. But then, you see, Grandma has brought father up _her_ way and

[039-179] made a brilliant success of him; and teacher has never brought anybody

[039-180] up yet, though she’s helping with Davy and Dora. But you can’t tell how

[039-181] they’ll turn out till they _are_ grown up. So sometimes I feel as if it

[039-182] might be safer to go by Grandma’s opinions.”
