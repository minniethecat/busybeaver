# Nature Appreciation

[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s profound and sustaining relationship [[humor|wit]]h the natural world — a sensibility that transforms [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s woods, fields, and shores into a living presence that consoles, inspires, and teaches.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, nature is not scenery but a companion. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s relationship [[humor|wit]]h the natural world is active and reciprocal: she names trees, converses [[humor|wit]]h brooks, and finds in the landscape a constancy that human relationships cannot always provide. This sensibility is not presented as childish anthropomorphism but as a legitimate mode of perception — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] sees more truly than those who dismiss her "fancies" because she perceives the living quality of the non-human world.

The novel positions nature appreciation as both an aesthetic capacity and a moral one. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s love of nature is inseparable from her capacity for wonder, and wonder is the foundation of her ethical relationship to the world. Characters who cannot see beauty in a November wood are, in Montgomery's implied judgment, morally impoverished.

## Examples in the Text

**The Precarious Footing (Chapter 25).** In a moment of characteristic self-forgetfulness, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] becomes so absorbed in [[beauty|natural beauty]] that she forgets "the precarious nature of her footing" (025-065). The episode is comic but revealing — nature has the power to lift [[anne-shirley|Anne]] entirely out of practical considerations, a capacity that is both her gift and, occasionally, her liability.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Solitary Walks (Chapter 32).** Walking alone through the beechlands in December, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "never found it lonely; her imagination peopled her path [[humor|wit]]h merry companions" (032-097). Nature provides both the setting and the stimulus for her imaginative life, and [[loneliness|solitude]] in nature is never truly solitary — the landscape itself is company.

**The Study of Nature (Chapter 14).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s nature study class (014-051) represents the formal dimension of her engagement [[humor|wit]]h the natural world. [[teaching|Teaching]] children to observe and appreciate nature is an extension of her own sensibility — she gives her pupils the gift of seeing.

**[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s Hidden Nature (Chapter 20).** Montgomery extends the nature metaphor to human character: [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] possesses a "tender nature which he kept hidden scrupulously" (020-128). The parallel between [[beauty|natural beauty]] and human depth is characteristic — in Montgomery's universe, the same perceptiveness that appreciates a landscape can discern a soul.

**Ill-Naturedness in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] (Chapter 8).** The counter-example is provided by those who find "something ill-natured about everyone in [[avonlea|Avonlea]]" (008-096). The inability to perceive good in people is linked, implicitly, to the inability to perceive beauty in the world — both are failures of vision.

## Thematic Significance

**Nature as Spiritual Resource.** For [[anne-shirley|Anne]], nature is a primary source of spiritual sustenance. When human relationships disappoint or fatigue, the natural world remains — constant, beautiful, and undemanding. Her solitary walks are not escapes but renewals.

**Perception as Moral Faculty.** Montgomery consistently links the capacity to appreciate nature [[humor|wit]]h the capacity for moral perception. Those who see beauty in the world are also those who see good in people — and those blind to one are typically blind to the other.

**Nature and the [[imagination|Imagination]].** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imagination does not falsify nature but completes it — seeing the poetry latent in the landscape. Her naming of natural features (the [[birch-path|Birch Path]], [[love|Love]]r's Lane) is an act of creative perception that honors rather than distorts what is actually there.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** appreciates nature practically (good weather for crops) rather than aesthetically. Her worldview is not wrong but incomplete — [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s sensibility adds a dimension that [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s pragmatism cannot access.

**The Indifferent.** Characters who are oblivious to [[beauty|natural beauty]] represent a genuine poverty of spirit in Montgomery's scheme. They are not villains but they are diminished, unable to access one of life's primary consolations.
