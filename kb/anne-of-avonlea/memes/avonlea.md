# Avonlea

The small [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] village that serves as the entire world of Montgomery's novel — a [[neighbor|community]] where everyone knows everyone, where gossip circulates as currency, and where [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] has found her [[belonging|place]] after years of dis[[belonging|place]]ment.

## Location and Setting

Avonlea is a fictional rural [[neighbor|community]] on [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]], modeled on Montgomery's own experience of Cavendish. It comprises scattered farms, a schoolhouse, a Presbyterian church, and the social network that binds them. Surrounding communities — [[carmody|Carmody]], [[white-sands|White Sands]], [[newbridge|Newbridge]], [[grafton|East Grafton]] — provide contrast and occasional destinations, but Avonlea is the novel's geographic and emotional center.

## Community Life

Avonlea is characterized by its dense social fabric. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] observes that there are more "women gossips in Avonlea than anywhere else" (001-115), and the village's talk is both its binding agent and its occasional solvent. The [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Avonlea Village Improvement Society]] channels [[neighbor|community]] energy toward beautification, while the school serves as the primary institution around which daily life revolves.

## Inhabitants

The village's key residents include **[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]**, the schoolteacher; **[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]**, proprietor of [[green-gables|Green Gables]]; **[[diana-barry|Diana Barry]]** and her family at [[orchard-slope|Orchard Slope]]; **[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]]**; **[[rachel-lynde|Rachel Lynde]]**, the [[neighbor|community]]'s self-appointed moral guardian; **[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]**, the initially reluctant neighbor; and numerous pupils, farmers, and churchgoers who populate the novel's social landscape.

## Role in the Story

Avonlea is more than setting — it is the novel's subject. The [[neighbor|community]]'s expectations, its gossip, its institutions, and its capacity for both kindness and judgment shape every character's experience. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s integration into Avonlea — from outsider to be[[romance|love]]d teacher — is the novel's central arc of be[[envy|longing]].
