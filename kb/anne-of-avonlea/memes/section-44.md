[044-001] XXX

[044-002] A Wedding at the Stone House

[044-003] The last week in August came. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] was to be married in it.

[044-004] Two weeks later [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] would leave for [[redmond-college|Redmond College]]. In a

[044-005] week’s time [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] would move to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] and set up her

[044-006] lares and penates in the erstwhile spare room, which was already

[044-007] prepared for her coming. She had sold all her superfluous household

[044-008] plenishings by auction and was at present reveling in the congenial

[044-009] occupation of helping the Allans pack up. [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] was to preach his

[044-010] farewell sermon the next Sunday. The old order was changing rapidly to

[044-011] give [[belonging|place]] to the new, as Anne felt with a little sadness threading all

[044-012] her excitement and happiness.

[044-013] “Changes ain’t totally pleasant but they’re excellent things,” said Mr.

[044-014] [[mr-harrison|Harrison]] philosophically. “Two years is about long enough for things to

[044-015] stay exactly the same. If they stayed put any longer they might grow

[044-016] mossy.”

[044-017] Mr. Harrison was smoking on his veranda. His wife had

[044-018] self-sacrificingly told that he might smoke in the house if he took

[044-019] care to sit by an open window. Mr. Harrison rewarded this concession by

[044-020] going outdoors altogether to smoke in fine weather, and so mutual

[044-021] [[kindness|goodwill]] reigned.

[044-022] Anne had come over to ask [[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]] for some of her yellow dahlias.

[044-023] She and [[diana-barry|Diana]] were going through to [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] that evening to help

[044-024] [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] and [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] with their final preparations

[044-025] for the morrow’s bridal. Miss Lavendar herself never had dahlias; she

[044-026] did not like them and they would not have suited the fine retirement of

[044-027] her old-fashioned garden. But flowers of any kind were rather scarce in

[044-028] [[avonlea|Avonlea]] and the neighboring districts that summer, thanks to Uncle

[044-029] Abe’s storm; and Anne and Diana thought that a certain old

[044-030] cream-colored stone jug, usually kept sacred to doughnuts, brimmed over

[044-031] with yellow dahlias, would be just the thing to set in a dim angle of

[044-032] the stone house stairs, against the dark background of red hall paper.

[044-033] “I s’pose you’ll be starting off for college in a fortnight’s time?”

[044-034] continued Mr. Harrison. “Well, we’re going to miss you an awful lot,

[044-035] Emily and me. To be sure, [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]’ll be over there in your place.

[044-036] There ain’t nobody but a substitute can be found for them.”

[044-037] The irony of Mr. Harrison’s tone is quite untransferable to paper. In

[044-038] spite of his wife’s intimacy with [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], the best that could be

[044-039] said of the relationship between her and Mr. Harrison even under the

[044-040] new regime, was that they preserved an armed neutrality.

[044-041] “Yes, I’m going,” said Anne. “I’m very glad with my head . . . and very

[044-042] sorry with my heart.”

[044-043] “I s’pose you’ll be scooping up all the honors that are lying round

[044-044] loose at Redmond.”

[044-045] “I may try for one or two of them,” confessed Anne, “but I don’t care

[044-046] so much for things like that as I did two years ago. What I want to get

[044-047] out of my college course is some knowledge of the best way of living

[044-048] life and doing the most and best with it. I want to learn to understand

[044-049] and help other people and myself.”

[044-050] Mr. Harrison nodded.

[044-051] “That’s the idea exactly. That’s what college ought to be for, instead

[044-052] of for turning out a lot of B.A.‘s, so chock full of book-[[education|learning]] and

[044-053] [[pride|vanity]] that there ain’t room for anything else. You’re all right.

[044-054] College won’t be able to do you much harm, I reckon.”

[044-055] Diana and Anne drove over to Echo Lodge after tea, taking with them all

[044-056] the flowery spoil that several predatory expeditions in their own and

[044-057] their [[community|neighbors]]’ gardens had yielded. They found the stone house agog

[044-058] with excitement. Charlotta the Fourth was flying around with such vim

[044-059] and briskness that her blue bows seemed really to possess the power of

[044-060] being everywhere at once. Like the helmet of Navarre, Charlotta’s blue

[044-061] bows waved ever in the thickest of the fray.

[044-062] “Praise be to goodness you’ve come,” she said devoutly, “for there’s

[044-063] heaps of things to do . . . and the frosting on that cake _won’t_

[044-064] harden . . . and there’s all the silver to be rubbed up yet . . . and

[044-065] the horsehair trunk to be packed . . . and the roosters for the chicken

[044-066] salad are running out there beyant the henhouse yet, _crowing_, Miss

[044-067] Shirley, ma’am. And Miss Lavendar ain’t to be trusted to do a thing. I

[044-068] was thankful when [[stephen-irving|Mr. Irving]] came a few minutes ago and took her off

[044-069] for a walk in the woods. Courting’s all right in its place, Miss

[044-070] Shirley, ma’am, but if you try to mix it up with cooking and scouring

[044-071] everything’s spoiled. That’s _my_ opinion, Miss Shirley, ma’am.”

[044-072] Anne and Diana worked so heartily that by ten o’clock even Charlotta

[044-073] the Fourth was satisfied. She braided her hair in innumerable plaits

[044-074] and took her weary little bones off to bed.

[044-075] “But I’m sure I shan’t sleep a blessed wink, Miss Shirley, ma’am, for

[044-076] fear that something’ll go wrong at the last minute . . . the cream

[044-077] won’t whip . . . or Mr. Irving’ll have a stroke and not be able to

[044-078] come.”

[044-079] “He isn’t in the habit of having strokes, is he?” asked Diana, the

[044-080] dimpled corners of her mouth twitching. To Diana, Charlotta the Fourth

[044-081] was, if not exactly a thing of beauty, certainly a [[laughter|joy]] forever.

[044-082] “They’re not things that go by habit,” said Charlotta the Fourth with

[044-083] dignity. “They just _happen_ . . . and there you are. _Anybody_ can

[044-084] have a stroke. You don’t have to learn how. Mr. Irving looks a lot like

[044-085] an uncle of mine that had one once just as he was sitting down to

[044-086] dinner one day. But maybe everything’ll go all right. In this world

[044-087] you’ve just got to hope for the best and prepare for the worst and take

[044-088] whatever God sends.”

[044-089] “The only thing I’m worried about is that it won’t be fine tomorrow,”

[044-090] said Diana. “[[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe]] predicted rain for the middle of the week, and

[044-091] ever since the big storm I can’t help believing there’s a good deal in

[044-092] what Uncle Abe says.”

[044-093] Anne, who knew better than Diana just how much Uncle Abe had to do with

[044-094] the storm, was not much disturbed by this. She slept the sleep of the

[044-095] just and weary, and was roused at an unearthly hour by Charlotta the

[044-096] Fourth.

[044-097] “Oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am, it’s awful to call you so early,” came

[044-098] wailing through the keyhole, “but there’s so much to do yet . . . and

[044-099] oh, Miss Shirley, ma’am, I’m skeered it’s going to rain and I wish

[044-100] you’d get up and tell me you think it ain’t.” Anne flew to the window,

[044-101] hoping against hope that Charlotta the Fourth was saying this merely by

[044-102] way of rousing her effectually. But alas, the morning did look

[044-103] unpropitious. Below the window Miss Lavendar’s garden, which should

[044-104] have been a glory of pale virgin sunshine, lay dim and windless; and

[044-105] the sky over the firs was dark with moody clouds.

[044-106] “Isn’t it too mean!” said Diana.

[044-107] “We must hope for the best,” said Anne determinedly. “If it only

[044-108] doesn’t actually rain, a cool, pearly gray day like this would really

[044-109] be nicer than hot sunshine.”

[044-110] “But it will rain,” mourned Charlotta, creeping into the room, a figure

[044-111] of fun, with her many braids wound about her head, the ends, tied up

[044-112] with white thread, sticking out in all directions. “It’ll hold off till

[044-113] the last minute and then pour cats and dogs. And all the folks will get

[044-114] sopping . . . and track mud all over the house . . . and they won’t be

[044-115] able to be married under the honeysuckle . . . and it’s awful unlucky

[044-116] for no sun to shine on a bride, say what you will, Miss Shirley, ma’am.

[044-117] I _knew_ things were going too well to last.”

[044-118] Charlotta the Fourth seemed certainly to have borrowed a leaf out of

[044-119] Miss Eliza Andrews’ book.

[044-120] It did not rain, though it kept on looking as if it meant to. By noon

[044-121] the rooms were decorated, the table beautifully laid; and upstairs was

[044-122] waiting a bride, “adorned for her husband.”

[044-123] “You do look sweet,” said Anne rapturously.

[044-124] “Lovely,” echoed Diana.

[044-125] “Everything’s ready, Miss Shirley, ma’am, and nothing dreadful has

[044-126] happened _yet_,” was Charlotta’s cheerful statement as she betook

[044-127] herself to her little back room to dress. Out came all the braids; the

[044-128] resultant rampant crinkliness was plaited into two tails and tied, not

[044-129] with two bows alone, but with four, of brand-new ribbon, brightly blue.

[044-130] The two upper bows rather gave the impression of overgrown wings

[044-131] sprouting from Charlotta’s neck, somewhat after the fashion of

[044-132] Raphael’s cherubs. But Charlotta the Fourth thought them very

[044-133] beautiful, and after she had rustled into a white dress, so stiffly

[044-134] starched that it could stand alone, she surveyed herself in her glass

[044-135] with great satisfaction . . . a satisfaction which lasted until she

[044-136] went out in the hall and caught a glimpse through the spare room door

[044-137] of a tall girl in some softly clinging gown, pinning white, star-like

[044-138] flowers on the smooth ripples of her ruddy hair.

[044-139] “Oh, I’ll _never_ be able to look like Miss Shirley,” thought poor

[044-140] Charlotta despairingly. “You just have to be born so, I guess . . .

[044-141] don’t seem’s if any amount of practice could give you that _air_.”

[044-142] By one o’clock the guests had come, including Mr. and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]], for

[044-143] Mr. Allan was to perform the ceremony in the absence of the [[grafton|Grafton]]

[044-144] minister on his vacation. There was no formality about the marriage.

[044-145] Miss Lavendar came down the stairs to meet her bridegroom at the foot,

[044-146] and as he took her hand she lifted her big brown eyes to his with a

[044-147] look that made Charlotta the Fourth, who intercepted it, feel queerer

[044-148] than ever. They went out to the honeysuckle arbor, where Mr. Allan was

[044-149] awaiting them. The guests grouped themselves as they pleased. Anne and

[044-150] Diana stood by the old stone bench, with Charlotta the Fourth between

[044-151] them, desperately clutching their hands in her cold, tremulous little

[044-152] paws.

[044-153] Mr. Allan opened his blue book and the ceremony proceeded. Just as Miss

[044-154] Lavendar and Stephen Irving were pronounced man and wife a very

[044-155] beautiful and symbolic thing happened. The sun suddenly burst through

[044-156] the gray and poured a flood of radiance on the happy bride. Instantly

[044-157] the garden was alive with dancing shadows and flickering lights.

[044-158] “What a lovely omen,” thought Anne, as she ran to kiss the bride. Then

[044-159] the three girls left the rest of the guests laughing around the bridal

[044-160] pair while they flew into the house to see that all was in readiness

[044-161] for the feast.

[044-162] “Thanks be to goodness, it’s over, Miss Shirley, ma’am,” breathed

[044-163] Charlotta the Fourth, “and they’re married safe and sound, no matter

[044-164] what happens now. The bags of rice are in the pantry, ma’am, and the

[044-165] old shoes are behind the door, and the cream for whipping is on the

[044-166] sullar steps.”

[044-167] At half past two Mr. and [[mrs-irving|Mrs. Irving]] left, and everybody went to Bright

[044-168] River to see them off on the afternoon train. As Miss Lavendar . . . I

[044-169] beg her [[forgiveness|pardon]], Mrs. Irving . . . stepped from the door of her old home

[044-170] Gilbert and the girls threw the rice and Charlotta the Fourth hurled an

[044-171] old shoe with such excellent aim that she struck Mr. Allan squarely on

[044-172] the head. But it was reserved for [[paul-irving|Paul]] to give the prettiest send-off.

[044-173] He popped out of the porch ringing furiously a huge old brass dinner

[044-174] bell which had adorned the dining room mantel. Paul’s only motive was

[044-175] to make a joyful noise; but as the clangor died away, from point and

[044-176] curve and hill across the river came the chime of “fairy wedding

[044-177] bells,” ringing clearly, sweetly, faintly and more faint, as if Miss

[044-178] Lavendar’s beloved echoes were bidding her greeting and farewell. And

[044-179] so, amid this benediction of sweet sounds, Miss Lavendar drove away

[044-180] from the old life of [[ambition|dreams]] and make-believes to a fuller life of

[044-181] realities in the busy world beyond.
