# Neighbor

The relationship defined by proximity rather than choice — from the opening chapter's "Irate Neighbor" (001-045) that tests [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s courage, to the closing acknowledgment that "[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is a good woman and a kind neighbor" (037-097) despite her faults.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, the neighbor relationship is the backbone of village social life. Neighbors are not chosen; they are inherited by geography. Montgomery explores the full spectrum of this involuntary bond: the friction of incompatible personalities living side by side, the mutual [[duty|obligation]]s that sustain rural communities, and the slow transformation of a stranger-next-door into a known and valued presence.

[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], introduced in the novel's very first chapter as "An Irate Neighbor" (001-045), embodies the neighbor-as-adversary. Yet over the course of the novel, he becomes a friend — Montgomery's demonstration that neighborliness is a process, not a fixed state. The neighbor who begins as a trial may become a treasure.

## Examples in the Text

**The Irate Neighbor (Chapter 1).** The novel announces its concern [[humor|wit]]h neighbor relations in its opening chapter title: "CHAPTER I. An Irate Neighbor" (001-014). [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s cow has trespassed into [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s oat field, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] must face an angry man she has never met — "[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] was their new righthand neighbor and she had never met him before, although she had seen him" (001-082). The episode establishes the neighbor relationship as one defined by proximity, vulnerability, and the potential for conflict.

**The [[generosity|Generosity]] Test (Chapter 8).** The Blair household illustrates neighborly stinginess through [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s memorable quip: "She says she has to be just before she's generous. But [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says she's so much 'before' that [[generous|generosity]] never catches up [[humor|wit]]h her" (008-020–021). The passage shows how neighborly reputation is built — or damaged — through small acts of hospitality and their conspicuous absence.

**[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s [[generous|Generous]] Appetite (Chapter 10).** In a domestic scene, "[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] would have refused but [[anne-shirley|Anne]] cut a generous second slice" (010-058). [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s quiet [[generous|generosity]] toward the hungry [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]] exemplifies the informal economy of care that neighborliness, at its best, represents: [[generosity|giving]] not because one must, but because one can.

**The Kind Neighbor Acknowledged (Chapter 37).** Near the novel's close, as [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] contemplates inviting [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] to live at [[green-gables|Green Gables]], she hesitates: "'[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is a good woman and a kind neighbor, but...'" (037-097). The unfinished sentence, [[humor|wit]]h its "but," captures the complexity of neighborly feeling — genuine appreciation mingled [[humor|wit]]h the friction of incompatible temperaments. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] chooses to accept [[rachel-lynde|Rachel Lynde]], faults and all, affirming that neighborliness is ultimately about accommodation.

## Thematic Significance

The neighbor theme in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is a study in *accommodation*. Montgomery does not romanticize [[community|village life]] — she shows its irritations, its gossip, its small-mindedness — but she also shows its compensations. A neighbor may be irate, stingy, or meddlesome, but they are also *present*. They show up. They bring encouragement after a difficult day. They offer a [[belonging|home]] when it is needed.

The arc from "An Irate Neighbor" to "a kind neighbor" traces the novel's central thesis about community: proximity, honestly navigated, can transform strangers into something like family. The key is not compatibility but willingness — the decision, like [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s, to "put up [[humor|wit]]h far worse faults" rather than live [[humor|wit]]hout the neighbor who has become essential.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] vs. [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] as Neighbors.** [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] begins as the novel's most difficult neighbor — gruff, explosive, unapproachable. Yet he becomes one of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s closest confidants. [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], by contrast, has always been a "good" neighbor, but her goodness comes [[humor|wit]]h an overbearing quality that wears on [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]. Montgomery refuses to rank these neighborly styles; both have value and both exact a cost.

**The Blair Family.** Mrs. Blair embodies the neighbor who fails the test of [[generous|generosity]]. Her insistence on being "just before she's generous" (008-020) is exposed by [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] as a cover for stinginess — a refusal to participate in the informal economy of neighborly [[generosity|giving]] that sustains the community.
