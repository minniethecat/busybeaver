# Dora Keith

The prim and proper twin sister of the irrepressible [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]] — a child so well-behaved that she sometimes disappears from notice, but whose quiet goodness is a genuine, if understated, virtue.

## Character Overview

Dora Keith is [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s opposite in every respect. Where [[davy-keith|Davy]] is wild, Dora is tame; where [[davy-keith|Davy]] disrupts, Dora conforms; where [[davy-keith|Davy]] demands attention, Dora fades into the background. She is described as "prim Dora" who [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] has educated (032-025), a child who follows rules as naturally as [[davy-keith|Davy]] breaks them.

Montgomery is too wise a novelist to suggest that Dora's goodness is less valuable than [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s energy. But she is also too honest to pretend that Dora is as interesting. Dora functions primarily as [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s foil — a reminder that for every child who demands attention through mischief, there is another who earns approval through obedience and whose reward is to be overlooked.

## Role in the Story

Dora's role is quiet and minimal. She provides contrast to [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s chaos and represents the "easy" child whom no one worries about — and whom, consequently, no one writes stories about. Her presence is a gentle critique of the narrative tendency to valorize the difficult child at the expense of the good one.

## Relationships

**[[davy-keith|Davy Keith]]** is Dora's twin brother, whose mischief defines her by contrast.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** and **[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** are Dora's guardians, whose attention is inevitably captured by [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s crises while Dora's steady goodness goes unremarked.

## Character Analysis

Dora is the novel's quiet acknowledgment that goodness [[humor|wit]]hout drama is easily overlooked. In a narrative that celebrates [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s exceptionality and [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s mischief, Dora represents the quiet majority — children who cause no trouble and receive no attention. Her presence is a small, implicit critique of the narrative biases Montgomery herself participates in.
