# Justice

The satisfaction of receiving one's fair portion — whether "the girls sat down by the roots and did full justice to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s dainties" at a picnic (018-039), or the deeper moral principle of [[generosity|giving]] each person their due in the social dealings of [[avonlea|Avonlea]].

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, the word "justice" appears most often in the idiomatic phrase "to do justice to" — meaning to appreciate fully, to consume [[humor|wit]]h satisfaction, to give something the attention it deserves. The girls "did full justice to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s dainties" at a Golden Picnic (018-039); hungry travelers "were hungry enough to do justice to any fare" (026-011). This idiomatic usage, though light in tone, points toward a deeper meaning: justice is about [[generosity|giving]] each thing — each person, each moment, each meal — its proper due.

Beyond the idiom, Montgomery weaves the concept of justice into the social fabric of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. Justice means treating pupils fairly, keeping promises, acknowledging when one has been wrong, and refusing to prejudge. It is the thread that keeps [[community|village life]] from unraveling into favoritism and grievance.

## Examples in the Text

**Doing Justice to a Picnic (Chapter 18).** During the Golden Picnic, "the girls sat down by the roots and did full justice to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s dainties, even the unpoetical sandwiches being greatly appreciated by hearty, unspoiled appetites sharpened by all the fresh air and exercise" (018-039–040). To "do justice" to food means more than eating — it means honoring the care that went into its preparation. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s "dainties" receive their proper appreciation from hungry, grateful friends.

**Justice to Any Fare (Chapter 26).** Visiting Miss Sarah at the Copp house, "the girls were hungry enough to do justice to any fare, and they en[[laughter|joy]]ed Miss Sarah's excellent bread and butter and 'cowcumbers'" (026-011). Here the idiom appears again — hunger makes any food welcome, but good food, honestly appreciated, is a form of justice done.

**Justice as Fairness in the Classroom.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching is shaped by a commitment to fairness. She resists favoritism, disciplines even her favorite pupils when they misbehave, and refuses to punish the innocent. Her classroom justice is imperfect — she learns as she goes — but it represents a conscious effort to be fair in a position of authority.

**Mrs. Blair's Deferred Justice (Chapter 8).** The phrase "she has to be just before she's generous" (008-020) ties justice and [[generous|generosity]] together. Mrs. Blair uses "justice" as a shield against [[generous|generosity]] — she must be *just* (fair, correct, prudent) before she can be *generous*. Montgomery exposes the emptiness of this principle through [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s commentary: the "before" stretches so long that [[generous|generosity]] never arrives. True justice, the novel suggests, does not preclude [[generous|generosity]] — it requires it.

## Thematic Significance

Montgomery treats justice not as an abstract philosophical principle but as a practice — something enacted in daily life. Justice is done at the picnic table when food is appreciated, at the school desk when pupils are treated fairly, in the village when [[community|neighbors]] deal honestly [[humor|wit]]h one another.

The novel's light touch [[humor|wit]]h the word — appearing most often in an idiom about eating — is itself significant. Justice, Montgomery implies, is not remote or theoretical; it is as close as the next meal, as ordinary as the fairness [[humor|wit]]h which one treats the person across the table. The everydayness of justice is what gives it its power.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Instinctive Justice vs. Mrs. Blair's Calculated Justice.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] practices justice as a reflex — she gives each person what they are due [[humor|wit]]hout stopping to calculate. Mrs. Blair uses justice as a calculation — she must be "just" before she can be "generous," and the calculation ensures she is never either. Montgomery presents these as two fundamentally different orientations: justice as openness versus justice as [[humor|wit]]hholding.

**Justice and Mercy in the Classroom.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s disciplinary encounters — particularly [[humor|wit]]h [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] — test the boundary between justice and mercy. When does fairness require punishment, and when does it call for forgiveness? Montgomery does not resolve this tension but presents it as one of the central challenges of authority.
