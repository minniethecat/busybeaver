[015-001] “‘It wasn’t father,’ sobbed [[annetta-bell|Annetta]], ‘it was someone who was studying

[015-002] for a minister, and so he could write lovely letters, but ma didn’t

[015-003] marry him after all. She said she couldn’t make out what he was driving

[015-004] at half the time. But I thought the letters were sweet and that I’d

[015-005] just copy things out of them here and there to write you. I put

[015-006] “teacher” where he put “lady” and I put in something of my own when I

[015-007] could think of it and I changed some words. I put “dress” in [[belonging|place]] of

[015-008] “mood.” I didn’t know just what a “mood” was but I s’posed it was

[015-009] something to wear. I didn’t s’pose you’d know the difference. I don’t

[015-010] see how you found out it wasn’t all mine. You must be awful clever,

[015-011] teacher.’

[015-012] “I told Annetta it was very wrong to copy another person’s letter and

[015-013] pass it off as her own. But I’m afraid that all Annetta repented of was

[015-014] being found out.

[015-015] “‘And I do [[romance|love]] you, teacher,’ she sobbed. ‘It was all true, even if

[015-016] [[mr-allan|the minister]] wrote it first. I do [[romance|love]] you with all my heart.’

[015-017] “It’s very difficult to scold anybody properly under such

[015-018] circumstances.

[015-021] “‘Dear teacher,

[015-022] “‘You said we might write about a visit. I never visited but once. It

[015-023] was at my Aunt Mary’s last winter. My Aunt Mary is a very particular

[015-024] woman and a great housekeeper. The first night I was there we were at

[015-025] tea. I knocked over a jug and broke it. Aunt Mary said she had had that

[015-026] jug ever since she was married and nobody had ever broken it before.

[015-027] When we got up I stepped on her dress and all the gathers tore out of

[015-028] the skirt. The next morning when I got up I hit the pitcher against the

[015-029] basin and cracked them both and I upset a cup of tea on the tablecloth

[015-030] at breakfast. When I was helping Aunt Mary with the dinner dishes I

[015-031] dropped a china plate and it smashed. That evening I fell downstairs

[015-032] and sprained my ankle and had to stay in bed for a week. I heard Aunt

[015-033] Mary tell Uncle Joseph it was a mercy or I’d have broken everything in

[015-034] the house. When I got better it was time to go home. I don’t like

[015-035] visiting very much. I like going to school better, especially since I

[015-036] came to [[avonlea|Avonlea]].

[015-037] “‘Yours respectfully,

[015-038] “‘Barbara Shaw.’”

[015-068] “Paul’s Letter.

[015-069] “‘My dear teacher,

[015-070] “‘You told us we might write you about some interesting people we knew.

[015-071] I think the most interesting people I know are my rock people and I

[015-072] mean to tell you about them. I have never told anybody about them

[015-073] except grandma and father but I would like to have you know about them

[015-074] because you understand things. There are a great many people who do not

[015-075] understand things so there is no use in telling them.’

[015-076] “‘My rock people live at the shore. I used to visit them almost every

[015-077] evening before the winter came. Now I can’t go till spring, but they

[015-078] will be there, for people like that never change . . . that is the

[015-079] splendid thing about them. Nora was the first one of them I got

[015-080] acquainted with and so I think I love her the best. She lives in

[015-081] Andrews’ Cove and she has black hair and black eyes, and she knows all

[015-082] about the mermaids and the water kelpies. You ought to hear the stories

[015-083] she can tell. Then there are the Twin Sailors. They don’t live

[015-084] anywhere, they sail all the time, but they often come ashore to talk to

[015-085] me. They are a pair of jolly tars and they have seen everything in the

[015-086] world. . . and more than what is in the world. Do you know what

[015-087] happened to the youngest Twin Sailor once? He was sailing and he sailed

[015-088] right into a moonglade. A moonglade is the track the full moon makes on

[015-089] the water when it is rising from the sea, you know, teacher. Well, the

[015-090] youngest Twin Sailor sailed along the moonglade till he came right up

[015-091] to the moon, and there was a little golden door in the moon and he

[015-092] opened it and sailed right through. He had some wonderful adventures in

[015-093] the moon but it would make this letter too long to tell them.’

[015-094] “‘Then there is the Golden Lady of the cave. One day I found a big cave

[015-095] down on the shore and I went away in and after a while I found the

[015-096] Golden Lady. She has golden hair right down to her feet and her dress

[015-097] is all glittering and glistening like gold that is alive. And she has a

[015-098] golden harp and plays on it all day long . . . you can hear the music

[015-099] any time along shore if you listen carefully but most people would

[015-100] think it was only the wind among the rocks. I’ve never told Nora about

[015-101] the Golden Lady. I was afraid it might hurt her feelings. It even hurt

[015-102] her feelings if I talked too long with the Twin Sailors.’

[015-103] “‘I always met the Twin Sailors at the Striped Rocks. The youngest Twin

[015-104] Sailor is very good-tempered but the oldest Twin Sailor can look

[015-105] dreadfully fierce at times. I have my suspicions about that oldest

[015-106] Twin. I believe he’d be a pirate if he dared. There’s really something

[015-107] very mysterious about him. He swore once and I told him if he ever did

[015-108] it again he needn’t come ashore to talk to me because I’d promised

[015-109] grandmother I’d never associate with anybody that swore. He was pretty

[015-110] well scared, I can tell you, and he said if I would forgive him he

[015-111] would take me to the sunset. So the next evening when I was sitting on

[015-112] the Striped Rocks the oldest Twin came sailing over the sea in an

[015-113] enchanted boat and I got in her. The boat was all pearly and rainbowy,

[015-114] like the inside of the mussel shells, and her sail was like moonshine.

[015-115] Well, we sailed right across to the sunset. Think of that, teacher,

[015-116] I’ve been in the sunset. And what do you suppose it is? The sunset is a

[015-117] land all flowers. We sailed into a great garden, and the clouds are

[015-118] beds of flowers. We sailed into a great harbor, all the color of gold,

[015-119] and I stepped right out of the boat on a big meadow all covered with

[015-120] buttercups as big as roses. I stayed there for ever so long. It seemed

[015-121] nearly a year but the Oldest Twin says it was only a few minutes. You

[015-122] see, in the sunset land the time is ever so much longer than it is

[015-123] here.’

[015-124] “‘Your loving pupil,

[015-125] “‘Paul Irving.’

[015-126] “‘P. S. of course, this letter isn’t really true, teacher.

[015-127] P.I.’”

[015-128] XII

[015-129] A Jonah Day

[015-130] It really began the night before with a restless, wakeful vigil of

[015-131] grumbling toothache. When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] arose in the dull, bitter winter morning

[015-132] she felt that life was flat, stale, and unprofitable.

[015-133] She went to school in no angelic mood. Her cheek was swollen and her

[015-134] face ached. The schoolroom was cold and smoky, for the fire refused to

[015-135] burn and the children were huddled about it in shivering groups. Anne

[015-136] sent them to their seats with a sharper tone than she had ever used

[015-137] before. [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] strutted to his with his usual impertinent swagger

[015-138] and she saw him whisper something to his seat-mate and then glance at

[015-139] her with a grin.

[015-140] Never, so it seemed to Anne, had there been so many squeaky pencils as

[015-141] there were that morning; and when Barbara Shaw came up to the desk with

[015-142] a sum she tripped over the coal scuttle with disastrous results. The

[015-143] coal rolled to every part of the room, her slate was broken into

[015-144] fragments, and when she picked herself up, her face, stained with coal

[015-145] dust, sent the boys into roars of [[humor|laughter]].

[015-146] Anne turned from the second reader class which she was hearing.

[015-147] “Really, Barbara,” she said icily, “if you cannot move without falling

[015-148] over something you’d better remain in your seat. It is positively

[015-149] disgraceful for a girl of your age to be so awkward.”

[015-150] Poor Barbara stumbled back to her desk, her tears combining with the

[015-151] coal dust to produce an effect truly grotesque. Never before had her

[015-152] beloved, sympathetic teacher spoken to her in such a tone or fashion,

[015-153] and Barbara was heartbroken. Anne herself felt a prick of conscience

[015-154] but it only served to increase her mental irritation, and the second

[015-155] reader class remember that lesson yet, as well as the unmerciful

[015-156] infliction of arithmetic that followed. Just as Anne was snapping the

[015-157] sums out St. Clair Donnell arrived breathlessly.

[015-158] “You are half an hour late, St. Clair,” Anne reminded him frigidly.

[015-159] “Why is this?”

[015-160] “Please, miss, I had to help ma make a pudding for dinner ’cause we’re

[015-161] expecting company and Clarice Almira’s sick,” was St. Clair’s answer,

[015-162] given in a perfectly respectful voice but nevertheless provocative of

[015-163] great [[laughter|mirth]] among his mates.

[015-164] “Take your seat and work out the six problems on page eighty-four of

[015-165] your arithmetic for punishment,” said Anne. St. Clair looked rather

[015-166] amazed at her tone but he went meekly to his desk and took out his

[015-167] slate. Then he stealthily passed a small parcel to Joe Sloane across

[015-168] the aisle. Anne caught him in the act and jumped to a fatal conclusion

[015-169] about that parcel.

[015-170] Old Mrs. Hiram Sloane had lately taken to making and selling “nut

[015-171] cakes” by way of adding to her scanty income. The cakes were specially

[015-172] tempting to small boys and for several weeks Anne had had not a little

[015-173] trouble in regard to them. On their way to school the boys would invest

[015-174] their spare cash at Mrs. Hiram’s, bring the cakes along with them to

[015-175] school, and, if possible, eat them and treat their mates during school

[015-176] hours. Anne had warned them that if they brought any more cakes to

[015-177] school they would be confiscated; and yet here was St. Clair Donnell

[015-178] coolly passing a parcel of them, wrapped up in the blue and white

[015-179] striped paper Mrs. Hiram used, under her very eyes.
