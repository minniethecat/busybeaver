[014-001] “Oh, don’t say that, [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]. Remember how bad I was when I came here.”

[014-002] “[[anne-shirley|Anne]], you never were bad . . . _never_. I see that now, when I’ve

[014-003] learned what real badness is. You were always getting into terrible

[014-004] scrapes, I’ll admit, but your motive was always good. [[davy-keith|Davy]] is just bad

[014-005] from sheer [[romance|love]] of it.”

[014-006] “Oh, no, I don’t think it is real badness with him either,” pleaded

[014-007] Anne. “It’s just mischief. And it is rather quiet for him here, you

[014-008] know. He has no other boys to play with and his mind has to have

[014-009] something to occupy it. [[dora-keith|Dora]] is so prim and proper she is no good for a

[014-010] boy’s playmate. I really think it would be better to let them go to

[014-011] school, Marilla.”

[014-012] “No,” said Marilla resolutely, “my father always said that no child

[014-013] should be cooped up in the four walls of a school until it was seven

[014-014] years old, and [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] says the same thing. The twins can have a few

[014-015] lessons at [[belonging|home]] but go to school they shan’t till they’re seven.”

[014-016] “Well, we must try to reform Davy at home then,” said Anne cheerfully.

[014-017] “With all his faults he’s really a dear little chap. I can’t help

[014-018] loving him. Marilla, it may be a dreadful thing to say, but honestly, I

[014-019] like Davy better than Dora, for all she’s so good.”

[014-020] “I don’t know but that I do, myself,” confessed Marilla, “and it isn’t

[014-021] fair, for Dora isn’t a bit of trouble. There couldn’t be a better child

[014-022] and you’d hardly know she was in the house.”

[014-023] “Dora is too good,” said Anne. “She’d behave just as well if there

[014-024] wasn’t a soul to tell her what to do. She was born already brought up,

[014-025] so she doesn’t need us; and I think,” concluded Anne, hitting on a very

[014-026] vital truth, “that we always [[romance|love]] best the people who need us. Davy

[014-027] needs us badly.”

[014-028] “He certainly needs something,” agreed Marilla. “[[rachel-lynde|Rachel Lynde]] would say

[014-029] it was a good spanking.”

[014-030] XI

[014-031] Facts and Fancies

[014-032] “[[teaching|Teaching]] is really very interesting work,” wrote Anne to a Queen’s

[014-033] Academy chum. “[[jane-andrews|Jane]] says she thinks it is monotonous but I don’t find

[014-034] it so. Something funny is almost sure to happen every day, and the

[014-035] children say such amusing things. Jane says she punishes her pupils

[014-036] when they make funny speeches, which is probably why she finds teaching

[014-037] monotonous. This afternoon little Jimmy Andrews was trying to spell

[014-038] ‘speckled’ and couldn’t manage it. ‘Well,’ he said finally, ‘I can’t

[014-039] spell it but I know what it means.’

[014-040] “‘What?’ I asked.

[014-041] “‘St. Clair Donnell’s face, miss.’

[014-042] “St. Clair is certainly very much freckled, although I try to prevent

[014-043] the others from commenting on it . . . for _I_ was freckled once and

[014-044] well do I remember it. But I don’t think St. Clair minds. It was

[014-045] because Jimmy called him ‘St. Clair’ that St. Clair pounded him on the

[014-046] way home from school. I heard of the pounding, but not officially, so I

[014-047] don’t think I’ll take any notice of it.

[014-048] “Yesterday I was trying to teach Lottie Wright to do addition. I said,

[014-049] ‘If you had three candies in one hand and two in the other, how many

[014-050] would you have altogether?’ ‘A mouthful,’ said Lottie. And in the

[014-051] [[nature-appreciation|nature]] study class, when I asked them to give me a good reason why

[014-052] toads shouldn’t be killed, Benjie Sloane gravely answered, ‘Because it

[014-053] would rain the next day.’

[014-054] “It’s so hard not to laugh, Stella. I have to save up all my amusement

[014-055] until I get home, and Marilla says it makes her nervous to hear wild

[014-056] shrieks of [[laughter|mirth]] proceeding from the east gable without any apparent

[014-057] cause. She says a man in [[grafton|Grafton]] went insane once and that was how it

[014-058] began.

[014-059] “Did you know that Thomas a Becket was canonized as a _snake?_ Rose

[014-060] Bell says he was . . . also that William Tyndale _wrote_ the New

[014-061] Testament. Claude White says a ‘glacier’ is a man who puts in window

[014-062] frames!

[014-063] “I think the most difficult thing in teaching, as well as the most

[014-064] interesting, is to get the children to tell you their real thoughts

[014-065] about things. One stormy day last week I gathered them around me at

[014-066] dinner hour and tried to get them to talk to me just as if I were one

[014-067] of themselves. I asked them to tell me the things they most wanted.

[014-068] Some of the answers were commonplace enough . . . dolls, ponies, and

[014-069] skates. Others were decidedly original. Hester Boulter wanted ‘to wear

[014-070] her Sunday dress every day and eat in the sitting room.’ Hannah Bell

[014-071] wanted ‘to be good without having to take any trouble about it.’

[014-072] Marjory White, aged ten, wanted to be a _widow_. Questioned why, she

[014-073] gravely said that if you weren’t married people called you an old maid,

[014-074] and if you were your husband bossed you; but if you were a widow

[014-075] there’d be no danger of either. The most remarkable wish was Sally

[014-076] Bell’s. She wanted a ‘honeymoon.’ I asked her if she knew what it was

[014-077] and she said she thought it was an extra nice kind of bicycle because

[014-078] her cousin in Montreal went on a honeymoon when he was married and he

[014-079] had always had the very latest in bicycles!

[014-080] “Another day I asked them all to tell me the naughtiest thing they had

[014-081] ever done. I couldn’t get the older ones to do so, but the third class

[014-082] answered quite freely. Eliza Bell had ‘set fire to her aunt’s carded

[014-083] rolls.’ Asked if she meant to do it she said, ‘not altogether.’ She

[014-084] just tried a little end to see how it would burn and the whole bundle

[014-085] blazed up in a jiffy. Emerson Gillis had spent ten cents for candy when

[014-086] he should have put it in his missionary box. [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]]’s worst crime

[014-087] was ‘eating some blueberries that grew in the graveyard.’ Willie White

[014-088] had ‘slid down the sheephouse roof a lot of times with his Sunday

[014-089] trousers on.’ ‘But I was punished for it ’cause I had to wear patched

[014-090] pants to Sunday School all summer, and when you’re punished for a thing

[014-091] you don’t have to repent of it,’ declared Willie.

[014-092] “I wish you could see some of their compositions . . . so much do I

[014-093] wish it that I’ll send you copies of some written recently. Last week I

[014-094] told the fourth class I wanted them to write me letters about anything

[014-095] they pleased, adding by way of suggestion that they might tell me of

[014-096] some place they had visited or some interesting thing or person they

[014-097] had seen. They were to write the letters on real note paper, seal them

[014-098] in an envelope, and address them to me, all without any assistance from

[014-099] other people. Last Friday morning I found a pile of letters on my desk

[014-100] and that evening I realized afresh that teaching has its pleasures as

[014-101] well as its pains. Those compositions would atone for much. Here is Ned

[014-102] Clay’s, address, spelling, and grammar as originally penned.

[014-122] “‘Dear Miss Shirley

[014-123] “‘You told us to describe something strange we have seen. I will

[014-124] describe the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] Hall. It has two doors, an inside one and an

[014-125] outside one. It has six windows and a chimney. It has two ends and two

[014-126] sides. It is painted blue. That is what makes it strange. It is built

[014-127] on the lower [[carmody|Carmody]] road. It is the third most important building in

[014-128] Avonlea. The others are [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]] and the blacksmith shop. They hold

[014-129] debating clubs and lectures in it and concerts.

[014-130] “‘Yours truly,

[014-131] “‘Jacob Donnell.

[014-133] “Annetta Bell’s letter was quite long, which surprised me, for writing

[014-134] essays is not Annetta’s forte, and hers are generally as brief as St.

[014-135] Clair’s. Annetta is a quiet little puss and a model of good behavior,

[014-136] but there isn’t a shadow of originality in her. Here is her letter.—

[014-137] “‘Dearest teacher,

[014-138] “‘I think I will write you a letter to tell you how much I love you. I

[014-139] love you with my whole heart and soul and mind . . . with all there is

[014-140] of me to love . . . and I want to serve you for ever. It would be my

[014-141] highest privilege. That is why I try so hard to be good in school and

[014-142] learn my lessuns.

[014-143] “‘You are so beautiful, my teacher. Your voice is like music and your

[014-144] eyes are like pansies when the dew is on them. You are like a tall

[014-145] stately queen. Your hair is like rippling gold. [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] says it is

[014-146] red, but you needn’t pay any attention to Anthony.

[014-147] “‘I have only known you for a few months but I cannot realize that

[014-148] there was ever a time when I did not know you . . . when you had not

[014-149] come into my life to bless and hallow it. I will always look back to

[014-150] this year as the most wonderful in my life because it brought you to

[014-151] me. Besides, it’s the year we moved to Avonlea from [[newbridge|Newbridge]]. My love

[014-152] for you has made my life very rich and it has kept me from much of harm

[014-153] and evil. I owe this all to you, my sweetest teacher.

[014-154] “‘I shall never forget how sweet you looked the last time I saw you in

[014-155] that black dress with flowers in your hair. I shall see you like that

[014-156] for ever, even when we are both old and gray. You will always be young

[014-157] and fair to me, dearest teacher. I am thinking of you all the time. . .

[014-158] in the morning and at the noontide and at the twilight. I love you when

[014-159] you laugh and when you sigh . . . even when you look disdainful. I

[014-160] never saw you look cross though Anthony Pye says you always look so but

[014-161] I don’t wonder you look cross at him for he deserves it. I love you in

[014-162] every dress . . . you seem more adorable in each new dress than the

[014-163] last.

[014-164] “‘Dearest teacher, good night. The sun has set and the stars are

[014-165] shining . . . stars that are as bright and beautiful as your eyes. I

[014-166] kiss your hands and face, my sweet. May God watch over you and protect

[014-167] you from all harm.

[014-168] “‘Your afecksionate pupil,

[014-169] “‘Annetta Bell.’”

[014-170] “This extraordinary letter puzzled me not a little. I knew Annetta

[014-171] couldn’t have composed it any more than she could fly. When I went to

[014-172] school the next day I took her for a walk down to the brook at recess

[014-173] and asked her to tell me the truth about the letter. Annetta cried and

[014-174] ‘fessed up freely. She said she had never written a letter and she

[014-175] didn’t know how to, or what to say, but there was a bundle of love

[014-176] letters in her mother’s top bureau drawer which had been written to her

[014-177] by an old ‘beau.’
