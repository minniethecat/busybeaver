# Gilbert Blythe

The steady, intelligent young man who has been [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s friend, rival, and intellectual companion since their school days — now teaching to fund his medical [[teaching|education]] and quietly deepening a bond that will shape both their futures.

## Character Overview

Gilbert Blythe in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* occupies a quietly pivotal role. He is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s fellow teacher, teaching at a nearby school to earn money for medical college (009-022). Where [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is effusive and romantic, Gilbert is steady and practical — yet he shares her values, her ambition, and her commitment to [[neighbor|community]]. Their compatibility is demonstrated not through dramatic declarations but through shared work, mutual encouragement, and the kind of easy [[wisdom|understanding]] that marks the novel's deepest relationships.

Gilbert's father, John Blythe, is mentioned as part of the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] social world, but Gilbert himself stands as an independent young man carving his own path — mirroring [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s journey in a different key.

## Role in the Story

Gilbert's role in the novel is primarily as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s colleague and confidant. He teaches at his own school but appears regularly in [[avonlea|Avonlea]], supporting [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s initiatives and providing the [[friendship|companionship]] of an intellectual equal. His comment that teaching is "good training" (009-022) reflects his characteristic pragmatism — where [[anne-shirley|Anne]] treats teaching as a vocation, Gilbert treats it as preparation.

His most significant action is his encouragement of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s continuing [[teaching|education]]: "You ought to go on [[humor|wit]]h your [[teaching|education]]. You've saved enough" (037-072). This is not merely practical advice but an act of faith — Gilbert believes in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s potential and wants her to fulfill it.

Gilbert also supports the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Avonlea Village Improvement Society]] (027-137), aligning himself [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s civic vision. His participation is both genuine and strategic — it brings him into [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s orbit while contributing to the [[neighbor|community]] they both serve.

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Gilbert's central relationship, though the [[love|romance]] remains largely unspoken in this novel. Their bond is characterized by intellectual [[friendship|companionship]], shared professional experience, and mutual respect. Gilbert's feelings are evident in his attentiveness and encouragement; [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s remain unacknowledged even to herself.

**John Blythe**, Gilbert's father, is part of the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[neighbor|community]] and represents the family [[duty|obligation]]s that shape Gilbert's path.

## Key Scenes

**The Conversation [[About]] [[teaching|Teaching]] (Chapter 9).** Gilbert and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] discuss their shared profession: "I like teaching, too. It's good training" (009-022). The exchange establishes their intellectual equality and the easy rapport that characterizes their relationship.

**Encouraging [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[ambition|Ambition]] (Chapter 37).** Gilbert's insistence that [[anne-shirley|Anne]] continue her [[teaching|education]] — "You ought to go on [[humor|wit]]h your [[teaching|education]]" (037-072) — is the novel's most significant expression of his faith in her and his hope for their shared future.

**Supporting the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] (Chapter 27).** Gilbert's active encouragement of the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Village Improvement Society]] (027-137) demonstrates his alignment [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s values and his willingness to invest in the [[neighbor|community]] they share.

## Character Analysis

Gilbert functions as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s complement — steady where she is mercurial, practical where she is romantic, reserved where she is expressive. Yet Montgomery is careful to show that these differences are complementary rather than oppositional: Gilbert shares [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s core values ([[teaching|education]], [[neighbor|community]], ambition in service of others) while providing the grounding that her flights of imagination benefit from.

His arc in this novel is subtle — he is not transformed so much as revealed. Each appearance confirms his reliability, his intelligence, and his [[love|devotion]], building the foundation for the relationship that will flower in later books. Gilbert in *[[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is the promise of a future the novel only hints at.
