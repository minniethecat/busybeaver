# Mrs. Theodore White

A resident of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] whose household [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] visit during their canvassing campaign for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Village Improvement Society]]. She appears as a figure of formidable domestic standards — "the neatest woman in Avonlea" (008-090) — whose approval must be carefully managed.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Theodore White is introduced in Chapter 8, when [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] are soliciting subscriptions for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]]. Her house is imposing: "Theodore White's was the next stopping place. Neither Anne nor Diana had ever been there before, and they had only a slight acquaintance with Mrs. Theodore, who was not given to hospitality" (008-065–067). The description establishes her as a figure of some social distance — not unfriendly, but not warm either.

Her reputation precedes her. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] remembers: "I've always heard that Mrs. Theodore White was the neatest woman in Avonlea" (008-090). The observation explains the state of her parlor: "The newspapers were all gathered up, the floor was swept, and the chairs were all in their places" (008-087). Mrs. White's domestic orderliness is her defining trait.

## Role in Story

Mrs. White's primary narrative function is as a minor obstacle in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s fundraising campaign. When the girls arrive at her door, they are "wretchedly guilty" (008-085) — self-conscious about their mission and unsure of their welcome. Mrs. White hears them "politely" (008-082) but her initial reserve creates tension.

The turning point comes when Mrs. White "subscribed two dollars" (008-085) — a generous contribution that surprises the girls. The moment reveals that her reserve is not stinginess but dignity; she can be both formidable and generous.

The subscription visit also features a brief appearance by "old Mrs. James White" who "called in" (008-115), suggesting a wider White family network in Avonlea. Mrs. Milton White is also mentioned in passing at 004-146.

## Relationships

Mrs. White is married to Theodore White and resides at the White homestead. Her household includes or is visited by Mrs. James White, indicating extended family connections in the [[white-sands|White Sands]] area. Her social standing in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] is solid but not intimate — she is respected rather than loved, known for her standards rather than her warmth.

## Key Scenes

**The Subscription Visit (Chapter 8).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] arrive at the White home, where Mrs. Theodore appears at the front door after a brief consultation (008-069). The house is immaculate — the "neatest woman in Avonlea" lives up to her reputation (008-090). Despite initial awkwardness, Mrs. White subscribes two dollars, validating the girls' mission.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Theodore White belongs to Montgomery's gallery of minor Avonlea characters who illuminate the community's social texture. She represents a particular type: the respectable matron whose domestic excellence commands admiration but whose reserve keeps neighbors at a distance. Her subscription of two dollars — a substantial sum — suggests that beneath the formality lies genuine community spirit.
