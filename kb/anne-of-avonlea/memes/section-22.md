[022-001] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] took [[davy-keith|Davy]] on her knee and did her best to straighten out this

[022-002] theological tangle also. She was much better fitted for the task than

[022-003] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], for she remembered her own childhood and had an instinctive

[022-004] [[wisdom|understanding]] of the curious ideas that seven-year-olds sometimes get

[022-005] about matters that are, of course, very plain and simple to grown up

[022-006] people. She had just succeeded in convincing Davy that heaven was _not_

[022-007] in Simon Fletcher’s garret when Marilla came in from the garden, where

[022-008] she and [[dora-keith|Dora]] had been picking peas. Dora was an industrious little soul

[022-009] and never happier than when “helping” in various small tasks suited to

[022-010] her chubby fingers. She fed chickens, picked up chips, wiped dishes,

[022-011] and ran errands galore. She was neat, faithful and observant; she never

[022-012] had to be told how to do a thing twice and never forgot any of her

[022-013] little duties. Davy, on the other hand, was rather heedless and

[022-014] forgetful; but he had the born knack of winning [[romance|love]], and even yet Anne

[022-015] and Marilla liked him the better.

[022-016] While Dora proudly shelled the peas and Davy made boats of the pods,

[022-017] with masts of matches and sails of paper, Anne told Marilla about the

[022-018] wonderful contents of her letter.

[022-019] “Oh, Marilla, what do you think? I’ve had a letter from Priscilla and

[022-020] she says that Mrs. Morgan is on [[prince-edward-island|the Island]], and that if it is fine

[022-021] Thursday they are going to drive up to [[avonlea|Avonlea]] and will reach here

[022-022] about twelve. They will spend the afternoon with us and go to the hotel

[022-023] at [[white-sands|White Sands]] in the evening, because some of Mrs. Morgan’s American

[022-024] friends are staying there. Oh, Marilla, isn’t it wonderful? I can

[022-025] hardly believe I’m not dreaming.”

[022-026] “I daresay Mrs. Morgan is a lot like other people,” said Marilla drily,

[022-027] although she did feel a trifle excited herself. Mrs. Morgan was a

[022-028] famous woman and a visit from her was no commonplace occurrence.

[022-029] “They’ll be here to dinner, then?”

[022-030] “Yes; and oh, Marilla, may I cook every bit of the dinner myself? I

[022-031] want to feel that I can do something for the author of ‘The Rosebud

[022-032] Garden,’ if it is only to cook a dinner for her. You won’t mind, will

[022-033] you?”

[022-034] “Goodness, I’m not so fond of stewing over a hot fire in July that it

[022-035] would vex me very much to have someone else do it. You’re quite welcome

[022-036] to the job.”

[022-037] “Oh, thank you,” said Anne, as if Marilla had just conferred a

[022-038] tremendous favor, “I’ll make out the menu this very night.”

[022-039] “You’d better not try to put on too much style,” warned Marilla, a

[022-040] little alarmed by the high-flown sound of ‘menu.’ “You’ll likely come

[022-041] to [[death|grief]] if you do.”

[022-042] “Oh, I’m not going to put on any ‘style,’ if you mean trying to do or

[022-043] have things we don’t usually have on festal occasions,” assured Anne.

[022-044] “That would be affectation, and, although I know I haven’t as much

[022-045] sense and steadiness as a girl of seventeen and a schoolteacher ought

[022-046] to have, I’m not so silly as _that_. But I want to have everything as

[022-047] nice and dainty as possible. Davy-boy, don’t leave those peapods on the

[022-048] back stairs . . . someone might slip on them. I’ll have a light soup to

[022-049] begin with . . . you know I can make lovely cream-of-onion soup . . .

[022-050] and then a couple of roast fowls. I’ll have the two white roosters. I

[022-051] have real affection for those roosters and they’ve been pets ever since

[022-052] the gray hen hatched out just the two of them . . . little balls of

[022-053] yellow down. But I know they would have to be sacrificed sometime, and

[022-054] surely there couldn’t be a worthier occasion than this. But oh,

[022-055] Marilla, _I_ cannot kill them . . . not even for Mrs. Morgan’s sake.

[022-056] I’ll have to ask John Henry Carter to come over and do it for me.”

[022-057] “I’ll do it,” volunteered Davy, “if Marilla’ll hold them by the legs,

[022-058] ’cause I guess it’d take both my hands to manage the axe. It’s awful

[022-059] jolly fun to see them hopping about after their heads are cut off.”

[022-060] “Then I’ll have peas and beans and creamed potatoes and a lettuce

[022-061] salad, for vegetables,” resumed Anne, “and for dessert, lemon pie with

[022-062] whipped cream, and coffee and cheese and lady fingers. I’ll make the

[022-063] pies and lady fingers tomorrow and do up my white muslin dress. And I

[022-064] must tell [[diana-barry|Diana]] tonight, for she’ll want to do up hers. Mrs. Morgan’s

[022-065] heroines are nearly always dressed in white muslin, and Diana and I

[022-066] have always resolved that that was what we would wear if we ever met

[022-067] her. It will be such a delicate compliment, don’t you think? Davy,

[022-068] dear, you mustn’t poke peapods into the cracks of the floor. I must ask

[022-069] Mr. and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] and [[miss-stacy|Miss Stacy]] to dinner, too, for they’re all very

[022-070] anxious to meet Mrs. Morgan. It’s so fortunate she’s coming while Miss

[022-071] Stacy is here. Davy dear, don’t sail the peapods in the water bucket .

[022-072] . . go out to the trough. Oh, I do hope it will be fine Thursday, and I

[022-073] think it will, for [[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe]] said last night when he called at Mr.

[022-074] [[mr-harrison|Harrison]]’s, that it was going to rain most of this week.”

[022-075] “That’s a good sign,” agreed Marilla.

[022-076] Anne ran across to [[orchard-slope|Orchard Slope]] that evening to tell the news to

[022-077] Diana, who was also very much excited over it, and they discussed the

[022-078] matter in the hammock swung under the big willow in the Barry garden.

[022-079] “Oh, Anne, mayn’t I help you cook the dinner?” implored Diana. “You

[022-080] know I can make splendid lettuce salad.”

[022-081] “Indeed you, may” said Anne unselfishly. “And I shall want you to help

[022-082] me decorate too. I mean to have the parlor simply a _bower_ of blossoms

[022-083] . . . and the dining table is to be adorned with wild roses. Oh, I do

[022-084] hope everything will go smoothly. Mrs. Morgan’s heroines _never_ get

[022-085] into scrapes or are taken at a disadvantage, and they are always so

[022-086] selfpossessed and such good housekeepers. They seem to be _born_ good

[022-087] housekeepers. You remember that _Gertrude_ in ‘Edgewood Days’ kept

[022-088] house for her father when she was only eight years old. When I was

[022-089] eight years old I hardly knew how to do a thing except bring up

[022-090] children. Mrs. Morgan must be an authority on girls when she has

[022-091] written so much about them, and I do want her to have a good opinion of

[022-092] us. I’ve imagined it all out a dozen different ways . . . what she’ll

[022-093] look like, and what she’ll say, and what I’ll say. And I’m so anxious

[022-094] about my nose. There are seven freckles on it, as you can see. They

[022-095] came at the A.V.I S. picnic, when I went around in the sun without my

[022-096] hat. I suppose it’s ungrateful of me to worry over them, when I should

[022-097] be thankful they’re not spread all over my face as they once were; but

[022-098] I do wish they hadn’t come . . . all Mrs. Morgan’s heroines have such

[022-099] perfect complexions. I can’t recall a freckled one among them.”

[022-100] “Yours are not very noticeable,” comforted Diana. “Try a little lemon

[022-101] juice on them tonight.”

[022-102] The next day Anne made her pies and lady fingers, did up her muslin

[022-103] dress, and swept and dusted every room in the house . . . a quite

[022-104] unnecessary proceeding, for [[green-gables|Green Gables]] was, as usual, in the apple

[022-105] pie order dear to Marilla’s heart. But Anne felt that a fleck of dust

[022-106] would be a desecration in a house that was to be honored by a visit

[022-107] from Charlotte E. Morgan. She even cleaned out the “catch-all” closet

[022-108] under the stairs, although there was not the remotest possibility of

[022-109] Mrs. Morgan’s seeing its interior.

[022-110] “But I want to _feel_ that it is in perfect order, even if she isn’t to

[022-111] see it,” Anne told Marilla. “You know, in her book ‘Golden Keys,’ she

[022-112] makes her two heroines _Alice_ and _Louisa_ take for their motto that

[022-113] verse of Longfellow’s,

[022-114] ‘In the elder days of art

[022-115] Builders wrought with greatest care

[022-116] Each minute and unseen part,

[022-117] For the gods see everywhere,’

[022-118] and so they always kept their cellar stairs scrubbed and never forgot

[022-119] to sweep under the beds. I should have a guilty conscience if I thought

[022-120] this closet was in disorder when Mrs. Morgan was in the house. Ever

[022-121] since we read ‘Golden Keys,’ last April, Diana and I have taken that

[022-122] verse for our motto too.”

[022-123] That night John Henry Carter and Davy between them contrived to execute

[022-124] the two white roosters, and Anne dressed them, the usually distasteful

[022-125] task glorified in her eyes by the destination of the plump birds.

[022-126] “I don’t like picking fowls,” she told Marilla, “but isn’t it fortunate

[022-127] we don’t have to put our souls into what our hands may be doing? I’ve

[022-128] been picking chickens with my hands but in imagination I’ve been

[022-129] roaming the Milky Way.”

[022-130] “I thought you’d scattered more feathers over the floor than usual,”

[022-131] remarked Marilla.

[022-132] Then Anne put Davy to bed and made him promise that he would behave

[022-133] perfectly the next day.

[022-134] “If I’m as good as good can be all day tomorrow will you let me be just

[022-135] as bad as I like all the next day?” asked Davy.

[022-136] “I couldn’t do that,” said Anne discreetly, “but I’ll take you and Dora

[022-137] for a row in the flat right to the bottom of the pond, and we’ll go

[022-138] ashore on the sandhills and have a picnic.”

[022-139] “It’s a bargain,” said Davy. “I’ll be good, you bet. I meant to go over

[022-140] to Mr. Harrison’s and fire peas from my new popgun at Ginger but

[022-141] another day’ll do as well. I espect it will be just like Sunday, but a

[022-142] [[the-picnic|picnic at the shore]]’ll make up for _that_.”

[022-143] XVII

[022-144] A Chapter of Accidents

[022-145] Anne woke three times in the night and made pilgrimages to her window

[022-146] to make sure that Uncle Abe’s prediction was not coming true. Finally

[022-147] the morning dawned pearly and lustrous in a sky full of silver sheen

[022-148] and radiance, and the wonderful day had arrived.

[022-149] Diana appeared soon after breakfast, with a basket of flowers over one

[022-150] arm and _her_ muslin dress over the other . . . for it would not do to

[022-151] don it until all the dinner preparations were completed. Meanwhile she

[022-152] wore her afternoon pink print and a lawn apron fearfully and

[022-153] wonderfully ruffled and frilled; and very neat and pretty and rosy she

[022-154] was.

[022-155] “You look simply sweet,” said Anne admiringly.

[022-156] Diana sighed.

[022-157] “But I’ve had to let out every one of my dresses _again_. I weigh four

[022-158] pounds more than I did in July. Anne, _where_ will this end? Mrs.

[022-159] Morgan’s heroines are all tall and slender.”

[022-160] “Well, let’s forget our troubles and think of our mercies,” said Anne

[022-161] gaily. “Mrs. Allan says that whenever we think of anything that is a

[022-162] trial to us we should also think of something nice that we can set over

[022-163] against it. If you are slightly too plump you’ve got the dearest

[022-164] dimples; and if I have a freckled nose the _shape_ of it is all right.

[022-165] Do you think the lemon juice did any good?”

[022-166] “Yes, I really think it did,” said Diana critically; and, much elated,

[022-167] Anne led the way to the garden, which was full of airy shadows and

[022-168] wavering golden lights.

[022-169] “We’ll decorate the parlor first. We have plenty of time, for Priscilla

[022-170] said they’d be here about twelve or half past at the latest, so we’ll

[022-171] have dinner at one.”

[022-172] There may have been two happier and more excited girls somewhere in

[022-173] Canada or the United States at that moment, but I doubt it. Every snip

[022-174] of the scissors, as rose and peony and bluebell fell, seemed to chirp,

[022-175] “Mrs. Morgan is coming today.” Anne wondered how Mr. Harrison _could_

[022-176] go on placidly mowing hay in the field across the lane, just as if

[022-177] nothing were going to happen.
