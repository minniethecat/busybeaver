[030-001] “We’d better turn back and go [[belonging|home]],” suggested [[anne-shirley|Anne]] humbly. But [[diana-barry|Diana]],

[030-002] after consideration, vetoed this.

[030-003] “No, we may as well go and spend the evening, since we have come this

[030-004] far.”

[030-005] A few yards further on the girls came to a place where the road forked

[030-006] again.

[030-007] “Which of these do we take?” asked Diana dubiously.

[030-008] Anne shook her head.

[030-009] “I don’t know and we can’t afford to make any more mistakes. Here is a

[030-010] gate and a lane leading right into the wood. There must be a house at

[030-011] the other side. Let us go down and inquire.”

[030-012] “What a romantic old lane this it,” said Diana, as they walked along

[030-013] its twists and turns. It ran under patriarchal old firs whose branches

[030-014] met above, creating a perpetual gloom in which nothing except moss

[030-015] could grow. On either hand were brown wood floors, crossed here and

[030-016] there by fallen lances of sunlight. All was very still and remote, as

[030-017] if the world and the cares of the world were far away.

[030-018] “I feel as if we were walking through an enchanted forest,” said Anne

[030-019] in a hushed tone. “Do you suppose we’ll ever find our way back to the

[030-020] real world again, Diana? We shall presently come to a palace with a

[030-021] spellbound princess in it, I think.”

[030-022] Around the next turn they came in sight, not indeed of a palace, but of

[030-023] a little house almost as surprising as a palace would have been in this

[030-024] province of conventional wooden farmhouses, all as much alike in

[030-025] general characteristics as if they had grown from the same seed. Anne

[030-026] stopped short in rapture and Diana exclaimed, “Oh, I know where we are

[030-027] now. That is the little stone house where [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] lives . .

[030-028] . [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]], she calls it, I think. I’ve often heard of it but I’ve

[030-029] never seen it before. Isn’t it a romantic spot?”

[030-030] “It’s the sweetest, prettiest place I ever saw or imagined,” said Anne

[030-031] delightedly. “It looks like a bit out of a story book or a dream.”

[030-032] The house was a low-eaved structure built of undressed blocks of red

[030-033] Island sandstone, with a little peaked roof out of which peered two

[030-034] dormer windows, with quaint wooden hoods over them, and two great

[030-035] chimneys. The whole house was covered with a luxuriant growth of ivy,

[030-036] finding easy foothold on the rough stonework and turned by autumn

[030-037] frosts to most beautiful bronze and wine-red tints.

[030-038] Before the house was an oblong garden into which the lane gate where

[030-039] the girls were standing opened. The house bounded it on one side; on

[030-040] the three others it was enclosed by an old stone dyke, so overgrown

[030-041] with moss and grass and ferns that it looked like a high, green bank.

[030-042] On the right and left the tall, dark spruces spread their palm-like

[030-043] branches over it; but below it was a little meadow, green with clover

[030-044] aftermath, sloping down to the blue loop of the [[grafton|Grafton]] River. No other

[030-045] house or clearing was in sight . . . nothing but hills and valleys

[030-046] covered with feathery young firs.

[030-047] “I wonder what sort of a person Miss Lewis is,” speculated Diana as

[030-048] they opened the gate into the garden. “They say she is very peculiar.”

[030-049] “She’ll be interesting then,” said Anne decidedly. “Peculiar people are

[030-050] always that at least, whatever else they are or are not. Didn’t I tell

[030-051] you we would come to an enchanted palace? I knew the elves hadn’t woven

[030-052] magic over that lane for nothing.”

[030-053] “But [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] is hardly a spellbound princess,” laughed

[030-054] Diana. “She’s an old maid . . . she’s forty-five and quite gray, I’ve

[030-055] heard.”

[030-056] “Oh, that’s only part of the spell,” asserted Anne confidently. “At

[030-057] heart she’s young and beautiful still . . . and if we only knew how to

[030-058] unloose the spell she would step forth radiant and fair again. But we

[030-059] don’t know how . . . it’s always and only the prince who knows that . .

[030-060] . and Miss Lavendar’s prince hasn’t come yet. Perhaps some fatal

[030-061] mischance has befallen him . . . though _that’s_ against the law of all

[030-062] fairy tales.”

[030-063] “I’m afraid he came long ago and went away again,” said Diana. “They

[030-064] say she used to be engaged to [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] . . . [[paul-irving|Paul]]’s father . . .

[030-065] when they were young. But they quarreled and parted.”

[030-066] “Hush,” warned Anne. “The door is open.”

[030-067] The girls paused in the porch under the tendrils of ivy and knocked at

[030-068] the open door. There was a patter of steps inside and a rather odd

[030-069] little personage presented herself . . . a girl of about fourteen, with

[030-070] a freckled face, a snub nose, a mouth so wide that it did really seem

[030-071] as if it stretched “from ear to ear,” and two long braids of fair hair

[030-072] tied with two enormous bows of blue ribbon.

[030-073] “Is Miss Lewis at home?” asked Diana.

[030-074] “Yes, ma’am. Come in, ma’am. I’ll tell Miss Lavendar you’re here,

[030-075] ma’am. She’s upstairs, ma’am.”

[030-076] With this the small handmaiden whisked out of sight and the girls, left

[030-077] alone, looked about them with delighted eyes. The interior of this

[030-078] wonderful little house was quite as interesting as its exterior.

[030-079] The room had a low ceiling and two square, small-paned windows,

[030-080] curtained with muslin frills. All the furnishings were old-fashioned,

[030-081] but so well and daintily kept that the effect was delicious. But it

[030-082] must be candidly admitted that the most attractive feature, to two

[030-083] healthy girls who had just tramped four miles through autumn air, was a

[030-084] table, set out with pale blue china and laden with delicacies, while

[030-085] little golden-hued ferns scattered over the cloth gave it what Anne

[030-086] would have termed “a festal air.”

[030-087] “Miss Lavendar must be expecting company to tea,” she whispered. “There

[030-088] are six places set. But what a funny little girl she has. She looked

[030-089] like a messenger from pixy land. I suppose she could have told us the

[030-090] road, but I was curious to see Miss Lavendar. S . . . s . . . sh, she’s

[030-091] coming.”

[030-092] And with that Miss Lavendar Lewis was standing in the doorway. The

[030-093] girls were so surprised that they forgot good manners and simply

[030-094] stared. They had unconsciously been expecting to see the usual type of

[030-095] elderly spinster as known to their experience . . . a rather angular

[030-096] personage, with prim gray hair and spectacles. Nothing more unlike Miss

[030-097] Lavendar could possibly be imagined.

[030-098] She was a little lady with snow-white hair beautifully wavy and thick,

[030-099] and carefully arranged in becoming puffs and coils. Beneath it was an

[030-100] almost girlish face, pink cheeked and sweet lipped, with big soft brown

[030-101] eyes and dimples . . . actually dimples. She wore a very dainty gown of

[030-102] cream muslin with pale-hued roses on it . . . a gown which would have

[030-103] seemed ridiculously juvenile on most women of her age, but which suited

[030-104] Miss Lavendar so perfectly that you never thought about it at all.

[030-105] “[[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] says that you wished to see me,” she said, in a

[030-106] voice that matched her appearance.

[030-107] “We wanted to ask the right road to West Grafton,” said Diana. “We are

[030-108] invited to tea at Mr. Kimball’s, but we took the wrong path coming

[030-109] through the woods and came out to the base line instead of the West

[030-110] Grafton road. Do we take the right or left turning at your gate?”

[030-111] “The left,” said Miss Lavendar, with a hesitating glance at her tea

[030-112] table. Then she exclaimed, as if in a sudden little burst of

[030-113] resolution,

[030-114] “But oh, won’t you stay and have tea with me? Please, do. Mr. Kimball’s

[030-115] will have tea over before you get there. And Charlotta the Fourth and I

[030-116] will be so glad to have you.”

[030-117] Diana looked mute inquiry at Anne.

[030-118] “We’d like to stay,” said Anne promptly, for she had made up her mind

[030-119] that she wanted to know more of this surprising Miss Lavendar, “if it

[030-120] won’t inconvenience you. But you are expecting other guests, aren’t

[030-121] you?”

[030-122] Miss Lavendar looked at her tea table again, and blushed.

[030-123] “I know you’ll think me dreadfully foolish,” she said. “I _am_ foolish

[030-124] . . . and I’m ashamed of it when I’m found out, but never unless I _am_

[030-125] found out. I’m not expecting anybody . . . I was just pretending I was.

[030-126] You see, I was so lonely. I [[romance|love]] company . . . that is, the right kind

[030-127] of company . . . but so few people ever come here because it is so far

[030-128] out of the way. Charlotta the Fourth was lonely too. So I just

[030-129] pretended I was going to have a tea party. I cooked for it . . . and

[030-130] decorated the table for it . . . and set it with my mother’s wedding

[030-131] china . . . and I dressed up for it.”

[030-132] Diana secretly thought Miss Lavendar quite as peculiar as report had

[030-133] pictured her. The idea of a woman of forty-five playing at having a tea

[030-134] party, just as if she were a little girl! But Anne of the shining eyes

[030-135] exclaimed joyfuly,

[030-136] “Oh, do _you_ imagine things too?”

[030-137] That “too” revealed a [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]] to Miss Lavendar.

[030-138] “Yes, I do,” she confessed, boldly. “Of course it’s silly in anybody as

[030-139] old as I am. But what is the use of being an independent old maid if

[030-140] you can’t be silly when you want to, and when it doesn’t hurt anybody?

[030-141] A person must have some compensations. I don’t believe I could live at

[030-142] times if I didn’t pretend things. I’m not often caught at it though,

[030-143] and Charlotta the Fourth never tells. But I’m glad to be caught today,

[030-144] for you have really come and I have tea all ready for you. Will you go

[030-145] up to the spare room and take off your hats? It’s the white door at the

[030-146] head of the stairs. I must run out to the kitchen and see that

[030-147] Charlotta the Fourth isn’t letting the tea boil. Charlotta the Fourth

[030-148] is a very good girl but she _will_ let the tea boil.”

[030-149] Miss Lavendar tripped off to the kitchen on hospitable thoughts intent

[030-150] and the girls found their way up to the spare room, an apartment as

[030-151] white as its door, lighted by the ivy-hung dormer window and looking,

[030-152] as Anne said, like the place where happy [[ambition|dreams]] grew.

[030-153] “This is quite an adventure, isn’t it?” said Diana. “And isn’t Miss

[030-154] Lavendar sweet, if she _is_ a little odd? She doesn’t look a bit like

[030-155] an old maid.”

[030-156] “She looks just as music sounds, I think,” answered Anne.

[030-157] When they went down Miss Lavendar was carrying in the teapot, and

[030-158] behind her, looking vastly pleased, was Charlotta the Fourth, with a

[030-159] plate of hot biscuits.

[030-160] “Now, you must tell me your names,” said Miss Lavendar. “I’m so glad

[030-161] you are young girls. I [[romance|love]] young girls. It’s so easy to pretend I’m a

[030-162] girl myself when I’m with them. I do hate” . . . with a little grimace

[030-163] . . . “to believe I’m old. Now, who are you . . . just for convenience’

[030-164] sake? Diana Barry? And Anne Shirley? May I pretend that I’ve known you

[030-165] for a hundred years and call you Anne and Diana right away?”

[030-166] “You, may” the girls said both together.

[030-167] “Then just let’s sit comfily down and eat everything,” said Miss

[030-168] Lavendar happily. “Charlotta, you sit at the foot and help with the

[030-169] chicken. It is so fortunate that I made the sponge cake and doughnuts.

[030-170] Of course, it was foolish to do it for imaginary guests . . . I know

[030-171] Charlotta the Fourth thought so, didn’t you, Charlotta? But you see how

[030-172] well it has turned out. Of course they wouldn’t have been wasted, for

[030-173] Charlotta the Fourth and I could have eaten them through time. But

[030-174] sponge cake is not a thing that improves with time.”
