# Regret

The reflective emotion that accompanies parting, change, and the acknowledgment of error — from [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] rising "regretfully" at the end of a visit (009-084) to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s twilight meditation where "Joy and regret struggled together in her heart" (037-128).

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, regret appears in two distinct forms. The first is the gentle regret of parting — the reluctance to end a pleasant conversation, the wistful glance at a setting sun, the awareness that a moment is passing. This form of regret is tinged [[humor|wit]]h sweetness; it confirms that something was valued enough to be missed.

The second form is the regret of acknowledged error — "We should regret our mistakes and learn from them, but never carry them forward into the future [[humor|wit]]h us" (021-092). Montgomery, speaking through [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s advice to [[anne-shirley|Anne]], draws a careful line: regret is useful as a teacher but harmful as a burden. The past should inform, not imprison.

## Examples in the Text

**The Regret of Parting (Chapter 9).** After an evening of earnest conversation among the Improvement Society members, "Finally [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] rose regretfully" (009-084). The reluctance to end a conversation that has been meaningful is a form of regret that speaks to the quality of the connection — one regrets leaving precisely because the time has been good.

**Regret as Teacher (Chapter 21).** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] counsels [[anne-shirley|Anne]] after a humiliating incident: "'Well, we all make mistakes, dear, so just put it behind you. We should regret our mistakes and learn from them, but never carry them forward into the future [[humor|wit]]h us'" (021-092). This passage crystallizes Montgomery's philosophy: regret has a pedagogical function — it teaches — but must not become a permanent weight.

**The Wistful Gaze at a Picnic (Chapter 23).** After [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]]'s mishap [[humor|wit]]h the lemon pie, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] looks "regretfully at the wreck and ruin" (023-127). The moment is comic in tone — the regret is for lost dessert — but it captures regret's everyday presence: the small disappointments that attend even pleasant occasions.

**A Sunset Farewell (Chapter 31).** Near the novel's close, [[diana-barry|Diana]] says farewell to [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], "looking regretfully at the setting sun" (031-052). [[diana-barry|Diana]] regrets not only the end of a pleasant day but, more deeply, the closing of a chapter in their lives — [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s marriage and departure from [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] mark an ending.

**Joy and Regret Entwined (Chapter 37).** On the eve of a major life transition, "[[anne-shirley|Anne]] had a long meditation at her window that night. Joy and regret struggled together in her heart" (037-128). She has come at last to the bend in the road; college is [[humor|wit]]hin reach, but so is the awareness of all she will leave behind. Montgomery presents regret not as the opposite of [[laughter|joy]] but as its companion in moments of profound change.

## Thematic Significance

Regret in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is an emotion of *maturity*. Children, Montgomery implies, do not regret — they move forward [[humor|wit]]hout looking back. Regret arrives [[humor|wit]]h the capacity to reflect, to weigh what was against what might have been. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s experience of regret marks her transition from girlhood to young womanhood.

Montgomery is careful to distinguish productive regret from destructive regret. Regret that teaches — that prompts one to do better — is valuable. Regret that paralyzes — that becomes a permanent companion — is a burden to be set down. "Never carry them forward into the future [[humor|wit]]h us" (021-092) is the novel's counsel.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Reflective Regret vs. [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s Impulsive Action.** [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], the young boy [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] adopts, lives almost entirely [[humor|wit]]hout regret — he acts on impulse and moves on. This contrast highlights regret as a developmental achievement: it requires a sense of consequence and a capacity for self-evaluation that children have not yet developed.

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Acceptance.** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] has lived [[humor|wit]]h choices that might have produced regret — a long [[loneliness|solitude]], a [[love|romance]] delayed for decades. Yet Montgomery presents her as free of corrosive regret; she has accepted her past and made peace [[humor|wit]]h it, embodying [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s counsel to learn from mistakes [[humor|wit]]hout carrying them forward.
