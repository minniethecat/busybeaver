[025-001] The next afternoon the girls fared forth on their platter hunting

[025-002] expedition. It was ten miles to [[spencervale|Spencervale]] and the day was not

[025-003] especially pleasant for traveling. It was very warm and windless, and

[025-004] the dust on the road was such as might have been expected after six

[025-005] weeks of dry weather.

[025-006] “Oh, I do wish it would rain soon,” sighed [[anne-shirley|Anne]]. “Everything is so

[025-007] parched up. The poor fields just seem pitiful to me and the trees seem

[025-008] to be stretching out their hands pleading for rain. As for my garden,

[025-009] it hurts me every time I go into it. I suppose I shouldn’t complain

[025-010] about a garden when the farmers’ crops are suffering so. [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]

[025-011] says his pastures are so scorched up that his poor cows can hardly get

[025-012] a bite to eat and he feels guilty of cruelty to animals every time he

[025-013] meets their eyes.”

[025-014] After a wearisome drive the girls reached Spencervale and turned down

[025-015] the “Tory” Road . . . a green, solitary highway where the strips of

[025-016] grass between the wheel tracks bore evidence to lack of travel. Along

[025-017] most of its extent it was lined with thick-set young spruces crowding

[025-018] down to the roadway, with here and there a break where the back field

[025-019] of a Spencervale farm came out to the fence or an expanse of stumps was

[025-020] aflame with fireweed and goldenrod.

[025-021] “Why is it called the Tory Road?” asked Anne.

[025-022] “[[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] says it is on the principle of calling a [[belonging|place]] a grove

[025-023] because there are no trees in it,” said [[diana-barry|Diana]], “for nobody lives along

[025-024] the road except the Copp girls and old [[martin|Martin Bovyer]] at the further

[025-025] end, who is a Liberal. The Tory government ran the road through when

[025-026] they were in power just to show they were doing something.”

[025-027] Diana’s father was a Liberal, for which reason she and Anne never

[025-028] discussed politics. [[green-gables|Green Gables]] folk had always been Conservatives.

[025-029] Finally the girls came to the old Copp homestead . . . a place of such

[025-030] exceeding external neatness that even Green Gables would have suffered

[025-031] by contrast. The house was a very old-fashioned one, situated on a

[025-032] slope, which fact had necessitated the building of a stone basement

[025-033] under one end. The house and out-buildings were all whitewashed to a

[025-034] condition of blinding perfection and not a weed was visible in the prim

[025-035] kitchen garden surrounded by its white paling.

[025-036] “The shades are all down,” said Diana ruefully. “I believe that nobody

[025-037] is home.”

[025-038] This proved to be the case. The girls looked at each other in

[025-039] perplexity.

[025-040] “I don’t know what to do,” said Anne. “If I were sure the platter was

[025-041] the right kind I would not mind waiting until they came home. But if it

[025-042] isn’t it may be too late to go to [[wesley-keyson|Wesley Keyson]]’s afterward.”

[025-043] Diana looked at a certain little square window over the basement.

[025-044] “That is the pantry window, I feel sure,” she said, “because this house

[025-045] is just like Uncle Charles’ at [[newbridge|Newbridge]], and that is their pantry

[025-046] window. The shade isn’t down, so if we climbed up on the roof of that

[025-047] little house we could look into the pantry and might be able to see the

[025-048] platter. Do you think it would be any harm?”

[025-049] “No, I don’t think so,” decided Anne, after due reflection, “since our

[025-050] motive is not idle curiosity.”

[025-051] This important point of ethics being settled, Anne prepared to mount

[025-052] the aforesaid “little house,” a construction of laths, with a peaked

[025-053] roof, which had in times past served as a habitation for ducks. The

[025-054] Copp girls had given up keeping ducks . . . “because they were such

[025-055] untidy birds”. . . and the house had not been in use for some years,

[025-056] save as an abode of correction for setting hens. Although scrupulously

[025-057] whitewashed it had become somewhat shaky, and Anne felt rather dubious

[025-058] as she scrambled up from the vantage point of a keg placed on a box.

[025-059] “I’m afraid it won’t bear my weight,” she said as she gingerly stepped

[025-060] on the roof.

[025-061] “Lean on the window sill,” advised Diana, and Anne accordingly leaned.

[025-062] Much to her delight, she saw, as she peered through the pane, a

[025-063] willow-ware platter, exactly such as she was in quest of, on the shelf

[025-064] in front of the window. So much she saw before the catastrophe came. In

[025-065] her [[laughter|joy]] Anne forgot the precarious [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of her footing, incautiously

[025-066] ceased to lean on the window sill, gave an impulsive little hop of

[025-067] pleasure . . . and the next moment she had crashed through the roof up

[025-068] to her armpits, and there she hung, quite unable to extricate herself.

[025-069] Diana dashed into the duck house and, seizing her unfortunate friend by

[025-070] the waist, tried to draw her down.

[025-071] “Ow . . . don’t,” shrieked poor Anne. “There are some long splinters

[025-072] sticking into me. See if you can put something under my feet . . . then

[025-073] perhaps I can draw myself up.”

[025-074] Diana hastily dragged in the previously mentioned keg and Anne found

[025-075] that it was just sufficiently high to furnish a secure resting place

[025-076] for her feet. But she could not release herself.

[025-077] “Could I pull you out if I crawled up?” suggested Diana.

[025-078] Anne shook her head hopelessly.

[025-079] “No . . . the splinters hurt too badly. If you can find an axe you

[025-080] might chop me out, though. Oh dear, I do really begin to believe that I

[025-081] _was_ born under an ill-omened star.”

[025-082] Diana searched faithfully but no axe was to be found.

[025-083] “I’ll have to go for help,” she said, returning to the prisoner.

[025-084] “No, indeed, you won’t,” said Anne vehemently. “If you do the story of

[025-085] this will get out everywhere and I shall be ashamed to show my face.

[025-086] No, we must just wait until the Copp girls come home and bind them to

[025-087] secrecy. They’ll know where the axe is and get me out. I’m not

[025-088] uncomfortable, as long as I keep perfectly still . . . not

[025-089] uncomfortable in _body_ I mean. I wonder what the Copp girls value this

[025-090] house at. I shall have to pay for the damage I’ve done, but I wouldn’t

[025-091] mind that if I were only sure they would understand my motive in

[025-092] peeping in at their pantry window. My sole comfort is that the platter

[025-093] is just the kind I want and if Miss Copp will only sell it to me I

[025-094] shall be resigned to what has happened.”

[025-095] “What if the Copp girls don’t come home until after night . . . or till

[025-096] tomorrow?” suggested Diana.

[025-097] “If they’re not back by sunset you’ll have to go for other assistance,

[025-098] I suppose,” said Anne reluctantly, “but you mustn’t go until you really

[025-099] have to. Oh dear, this is a dreadful predicament. I wouldn’t mind my

[025-100] misfortunes so much if they were romantic, as Mrs. Morgan’s heroines’

[025-101] always are, but they are always just simply ridiculous. Fancy what the

[025-102] Copp girls will think when they drive into their yard and see a girl’s

[025-103] head and shoulders sticking out of the roof of one of their outhouses.

[025-104] Listen . . . is that a wagon? No, Diana, I believe it is thunder.”

[025-105] Thunder it was undoubtedly, and Diana, having made a hasty pilgrimage

[025-106] around the house, returned to announce that a very black cloud was

[025-107] rising rapidly in the northwest.

[025-108] “I believe we’re going to have a heavy thunder-shower,” she exclaimed

[025-109] in dismay, “Oh, Anne, what will we do?”

[025-110] “We must prepare for it,” said Anne tranquilly. A thunderstorm seemed a

[025-111] trifle in comparison with what had already happened. “You’d better

[025-112] drive the horse and buggy into that open shed. Fortunately my parasol

[025-113] is in the buggy. Here . . . take my hat with you. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] told me I was

[025-114] a goose to put on my best hat to come to the Tory Road and she was

[025-115] right, as she always is.”

[025-116] Diana untied the pony and drove into the shed, just as the first heavy

[025-117] drops of rain fell. There she sat and watched the resulting downpour,

[025-118] which was so thick and heavy that she could hardly see Anne through it,

[025-119] holding the parasol bravely over her bare head. There was not a great

[025-120] deal of thunder, but for the best part of an hour the rain came merrily

[025-121] down. Occasionally Anne slanted back her parasol and waved an

[025-122] encouraging hand to her friend; But conversation at that distance was

[025-123] quite out of the question. Finally the rain ceased, the sun came out,

[025-124] and Diana ventured across the puddles of the yard.

[025-125] “Did you get very wet?” she asked anxiously.

[025-126] “Oh, no,” returned Anne cheerfully. “My head and shoulders are quite

[025-127] dry and my skirt is only a little damp where the rain beat through the

[025-128] laths. Don’t pity me, Diana, for I haven’t minded it at all. I kept

[025-129] thinking how much good the rain will do and how glad my garden must be

[025-130] for it, and imagining what the flowers and buds would think when the

[025-131] drops began to fall. I imagined out a most interesting dialogue between

[025-132] the asters and the sweet peas and the wild canaries in the lilac bush

[025-133] and the guardian spirit of the garden. When I go home I mean to write

[025-134] it down. I wish I had a pencil and paper to do it now, because I

[025-135] daresay I’ll forget the best parts before I reach home.”

[025-136] Diana the faithful had a pencil and discovered a sheet of wrapping

[025-137] paper in the box of the buggy. Anne folded up her dripping parasol, put

[025-138] on her hat, spread the wrapping paper on a shingle Diana handed up, and

[025-139] wrote out her garden idyl under conditions that could hardly be

[025-140] considered as favorable to literature. Nevertheless, the result was

[025-141] quite pretty, and Diana was “enraptured” when Anne read it to her.

[025-142] “Oh, Anne, it’s sweet . . . just sweet. _Do_ send it to the _Canadian

[025-143] Woman_.”

[025-144] Anne shook her head.

[025-145] “Oh, no, it wouldn’t be suitable at all. There is no _plot_ in it, you

[025-146] see. It’s just a string of fancies. I like writing such things, but of

[025-147] course nothing of the sort would ever do for publication, for editors

[025-148] insist on plots, so Priscilla says. Oh, there’s Miss Sarah Copp now.

[025-149] _Please_, Diana, go and explain.”

[025-150] Miss Sarah Copp was a small person, garbed in shabby black, with a hat

[025-151] chosen less for vain adornment than for qualities that would wear well.

[025-152] She looked as amazed as might be expected on seeing the curious tableau

[025-153] in her yard, but when she heard Diana’s explanation she was all

[025-154] sympathy. She hurriedly unlocked the back door, produced the axe, and

[025-155] with a few skillfull blows set Anne free. The latter, somewhat tired

[025-156] and stiff, ducked down into the interior of her prison and thankfully

[025-157] emerged into liberty once more.

[025-158] “Miss Copp,” she said earnestly. “I assure you I looked into your

[025-159] pantry window _only_ to discover if you had a willow-ware platter. I

[025-160] didn’t see anything else—I didn’t _look_ for anything else.”

[025-161] “Bless you, that’s all right,” said Miss Sarah amiably. “You needn’t

[025-162] worry—there’s no harm done. Thank goodness, we Copps keep our pantries

[025-163] presentable at all times and don’t care who sees into them. As for that

[025-164] old duckhouse, I’m glad it’s smashed, for maybe now Martha will agree

[025-165] to having it taken down. She never would before for fear it might come

[025-166] in handy sometime and I’ve had to whitewash it every spring. But you

[025-167] might as well argue with a post as with Martha. She went to town

[025-168] today—I drove her to the station. And you want to buy my platter. Well,

[025-169] what will you give for it?”

[025-170] “Twenty dollars,” said Anne, who was never meant to match business wits

[025-171] with a Copp, or she would not have offered her price at the start.
