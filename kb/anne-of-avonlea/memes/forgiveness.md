# Forgiveness

The moral capacity to release grievance and restore broken relationship — presented in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* as one of the essential virtues [[humor|wit]]hout which [[romance|love]], friendship, and [[neighbor|community]] cannot flourish.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, forgiveness is not merely the absence of [[jealousy|resentment]] but an active, generous choice to restore connection. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates this in her characteristic idiom: feeling "that she could forgive everybody and everything" (004-110). This expansive forgiveness is presented not as weakness but as strength — the mark of a large spirit rather than a small one.

The novel examines forgiveness in multiple registers: the small daily pardons required by [[community|village life]], the difficult forgiveness of genuine injury, and the catastrophic consequences of forgiveness [[humor|wit]]hheld. The [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] plotline provides the novel's most powerful case study — a relationship destroyed not by lack of [[romance|love]] but by lack of forgiveness, and redeemed only when forgiveness finally arrives.

## Examples in the Text

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[generous|Generous]] Spirit (Chapter 4).** After resolving her quarrel [[humor|wit]]h [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] over the cow, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] feels "that she could forgive everybody and everything" (004-110). The statement captures the expansive, almost euphoric quality of genuine forgiveness — it liberates the forgiver as much as the forgiven.

**The Plea for Pardon (Chapter 4).** [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s tentative question — "Is it too much to hope that you'll forgive me?" (004-060) — reveals the vulnerability that asking for forgiveness entails. His gruff exterior conceals a genuine desire for reconciliation that [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s generous response honors.

**[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s Repeated Offenses (Chapter 11).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] finds herself "resolving to forgive [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s next offence, whatever it might be" (011-156). This preemptive forgiveness — extended before the offense is even committed — is both comic and touching. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] knows [[davy-keith|Davy]] will transgress again, and she decides in advance to forgive him.

**The Hardest Forgiveness (Chapter 13).** The acknowledgment that "you must forgive him for that part of it" (013-101) acknowledges that forgiveness can be partial and difficult — a process rather than a single act. Not everything is easy to forgive, and the novel does not pretend otherwise.

**[[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]'s Repentance (Chapter 15).** When [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] apologizes and asks "if I would forgive him" (015-110), [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s acceptance marks a turning point in their relationship. Forgiveness here is transformative — it converts an enemy into a pupil, resistance into cooperation.

**The Cost of Unforgiveness (Chapter 33).** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s story is the novel's most powerful negative example: "I wouldn't forgive him; and so he went away" (033-014). A lifetime of loneliness follows a moment of refused forgiveness. The novel is unequivocal: forgiveness refused damages the refuser most of all.

**The Gender of Forgiveness (Chapter 36).** The wry observation that "a woman would forgive a man for beating her sooner than" for a particular slight (036-072) is one of Montgomery's sharpest social observations. Forgiveness has a gendered dimension, and women are expected to forgive more readily than men — a double standard the novel quietly critiques.

## Thematic Significance

**Forgiveness as Liberation.** Montgomery presents forgiveness as liberating the forgiver more than the forgiven. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s expansive "I could forgive everybody" moment follows her own release from [[jealousy|resentment]] — forgiveness is a gift one gives oneself.

**The Cost of Refusal.** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s story argues that refusing to forgive is ultimately self-destructive. Her quarter-century of loneliness punishes no one but herself.

**Forgiveness and [[community|Community]].** Village life in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] depends on the capacity to forgive small slights and daily irritations. Without forgiveness, the close quarters of rural life would be intolerable; forgiveness is the lubricant of [[neighbor|community]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Expansive Forgiveness vs. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Withheld Pardon.** The novel's central contrast on the theme is between [[anne-shirley|Anne]], who forgives freely and is happy, and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], who refused to forgive and was miserable for decades. The moral is clear [[humor|wit]]hout being preachy.

**Forgiveness Earned vs. Forgiveness Given.** Some forgiveness in the novel follows apology and repentance ([[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]); other forgiveness is preemptive ([[davy-keith|Davy]]'s "next offence"). Montgomery validates both — forgiveness can be a response to repentance or a gift extended in anticipation of future failing.
