[032-001] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]’s pronouns slandered Eliza, who was very fond of her husband.

[032-002] “[[rachel-lynde|Rachel]] says if he’d only brace up and exert his will power he’d get

[032-003] better. But what is the use of asking a jellyfish to sit up straight?”

[032-004] continued Marilla. “[[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]] never had any will power to exert. His

[032-005] mother ruled him till he married and then Rachel carried it on. It’s a

[032-006] wonder he dared to get sick without asking her permission. But there, I

[032-007] shouldn’t talk so. Rachel has been a good wife to him. He’d never have

[032-008] amounted to anything without her, that’s certain. He was born to be

[032-009] ruled; and it’s well he fell into the hands of a clever, capable

[032-010] manager like Rachel. He didn’t mind her way. It saved him the bother of

[032-011] ever making up his own mind about anything. [[davy-keith|Davy]], do stop squirming

[032-012] like an eel.”

[032-013] “I’ve nothing else to do,” protested Davy. “I can’t eat any more, and

[032-014] it’s no fun watching you and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] eat.”

[032-015] “Well, you and [[dora-keith|Dora]] go out and give the hens their wheat,” said

[032-016] Marilla. “And don’t you try to pull any more feathers out of the white

[032-017] rooster’s tail either.”

[032-018] “I wanted some feathers for an Injun headdress,” said Davy sulkily.

[032-019] “Milty Boulter has a dandy one, made out of the feathers his mother

[032-020] give him when she killed their old white gobbler. You might let me have

[032-021] some. That rooster’s got ever so many more’n he wants.”

[032-022] “You may have the old feather duster in the garret,” said Anne, “and

[032-023] I’ll dye them green and red and yellow for you.”

[032-024] “You do spoil that boy dreadfully,” said Marilla, when Davy, with a

[032-025] radiant face, had followed prim Dora out. Marilla’s [[teaching|education]] had made

[032-026] great strides in the past six years; but she had not yet been able to

[032-027] rid herself of the idea that it was very bad for a child to have too

[032-028] many of its wishes indulged.

[032-029] “All the boys of his class have Indian headdresses, and Davy wants one

[032-030] too,” said Anne. “_I_ know how it feels . . . I’ll never forget how I

[032-031] used to long for puffed sleeves when all the other girls had them. And

[032-032] Davy isn’t being spoiled. He is improving every day. Think what a

[032-033] difference there is in him since he came here a year ago.”

[032-034] “He certainly doesn’t get into as much mischief since he began to go to

[032-035] school,” acknowledged Marilla. “I suppose he works off the tendency

[032-036] with the other boys. But it’s a wonder to me we haven’t heard from

[032-037] Richard Keith before this. Never a word since last May.”

[032-038] “I’ll be afraid to hear from him,” sighed Anne, beginning to clear away

[032-039] the dishes. “If a letter should come I’d dread opening it, for fear it

[032-040] would tell us to send the twins to him.”

[032-041] A month later a letter did come. But it was not from Richard Keith. A

[032-042] friend of his wrote to say that Richard Keith had died of consumption a

[032-043] fortnight previously. The writer of the letter was the executor of his

[032-044] will and by that will the sum of two thousand dollars was left to Miss

[032-045] Marilla Cuthbert in [[faith|trust]] for David and Dora Keith until they came of

[032-046] age or married. In the meantime the interest was to be used for their

[032-047] maintenance.

[032-048] “It seems dreadful to be glad of anything in connection with a death,”

[032-049] said Anne soberly. “I’m sorry for poor Mr. Keith; but I _am_ glad that

[032-050] we can keep the twins.”

[032-051] “It’s a very good thing about the money,” said Marilla practically. “I

[032-052] wanted to keep them but I really didn’t see how I could afford to do

[032-053] it, especially when they grew older. The rent of the farm doesn’t do

[032-054] any more than keep the house and I was bound that not a cent of your

[032-055] money should be spent on them. You do far too much for them as it is.

[032-056] Dora didn’t need that new hat you bought her any more than a cat needs

[032-057] two tails. But now the way is made clear and they are provided for.”

[032-058] Davy and Dora were delighted when they heard that they were to live at

[032-059] [[green-gables|Green Gables]], “for good.” The death of an uncle whom they had never

[032-060] seen could not weigh a moment in the balance against that. But Dora had

[032-061] one misgiving.

[032-062] “Was Uncle Richard buried?” she whispered to Anne.

[032-063] “Yes, dear, of course.”

[032-064] “He . . . he . . . isn’t like [[mirabel-cotton|Mirabel Cotton]]’s uncle, is he?” in a

[032-065] still more agitated whisper. “He won’t walk about houses after being

[032-066] buried, will he, Anne?”

[032-067] XXIII

[032-068] [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]’s [[romance|Romance]]

[032-069] “I think I’ll take a walk through to [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] this evening,” said

[032-070] Anne, one Friday afternoon in December.

[032-071] “It looks like snow,” said Marilla dubiously.

[032-072] “I’ll be there before the snow comes and I mean to stay all night.

[032-073] [[diana-barry|Diana]] can’t go because she has company, and I’m sure [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] will

[032-074] be looking for me tonight. It’s a whole fortnight since I was there.”

[032-075] Anne had paid many a visit to Echo Lodge since that October day.

[032-076] Sometimes she and Diana drove around by the road; sometimes they walked

[032-077] through the woods. When Diana could not go Anne went alone. Between her

[032-078] and Miss Lavendar had sprung up one of those fervent, helpful

[032-079] friendships possible only between a woman who has kept the freshness of

[032-080] youth in her heart and soul, and a girl whose imagination and intuition

[032-081] supplied the [[belonging|place]] of experience. Anne had at last discovered a real

[032-082] “[[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]],” while into the little lady’s lonely, sequestered life

[032-083] of [[ambition|dreams]] Anne and Diana came with the wholesome [[laughter|joy]] and exhilaration

[032-084] of the outer existence, which Miss Lavendar, “the world forgetting, by

[032-085] the world forgot,” had long ceased to share; they brought an atmosphere

[032-086] of youth and reality to the little stone house. [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]]

[032-087] always greeted them with her very widest smile . . . and Charlotta’s

[032-088] smiles _were_ fearfully wide . . . loving them for the sake of her

[032-089] adored mistress as well as for their own. Never had there been such

[032-090] “high jinks” held in the little stone house as were held there that

[032-091] beautiful, late-lingering autumn, when November seemed October over

[032-092] again, and even December aped the sunshine and hazes of summer.

[032-093] But on this particular day it seemed as if December had remembered that

[032-094] it was time for winter and had turned suddenly dull and brooding, with

[032-095] a windless hush predictive of coming snow. Nevertheless, Anne keenly

[032-096] enjoyed her walk through the great gray maze of the beechlands; though

[032-097] alone she never found it lonely; her imagination peopled her path with

[032-098] merry companions, and with these she carried on a gay, pretended

[032-099] conversation that was wittier and more fascinating than conversations

[032-100] are apt to be in real life, where people sometimes fail most lamentably

[032-101] to talk up to the requirements. In a “make believe” assembly of choice

[032-102] spirits everybody says just the thing you want her to say and so gives

[032-103] you the chance to say just what _you_ want to say. Attended by this

[032-104] invisible company, Anne traversed the woods and arrived at the fir lane

[032-105] just as broad, feathery flakes began to flutter down softly.

[032-106] At the first bend she came upon Miss Lavendar, standing under a big,

[032-107] broad-branching fir. She wore a gown of warm, rich red, and her head

[032-108] and shoulders were wrapped in a silvery gray silk shawl.

[032-109] “You look like the queen of the fir wood fairies,” called Anne merrily.

[032-110] “I thought you would come tonight, Anne,” said Miss Lavendar, running

[032-111] forward. “And I’m doubly glad, for Charlotta the Fourth is away. Her

[032-112] mother is sick and she had to go home for the night. I should have been

[032-113] very lonely if you hadn’t come . . . even the dreams and the echoes

[032-114] wouldn’t have been enough company. Oh, Anne, how pretty you are,” she

[032-115] added suddenly, looking up at the tall, slim girl with the soft

[032-116] rose-flush of walking on her face. “How pretty and how young! It’s so

[032-117] delightful to be seventeen, isn’t it? I do [[jealousy|envy]] you,” concluded Miss

[032-118] Lavendar candidly.

[032-119] “But you are only seventeen at heart,” smiled Anne.

[032-120] “No, I’m old . . . or rather middle-aged, which is far worse,” sighed

[032-121] Miss Lavendar. “Sometimes I can pretend I’m not, but at other times I

[032-122] realize it. And I can’t reconcile myself to it as most women seem to.

[032-123] I’m just as rebellious as I was when I discovered my first gray hair.

[032-124] Now, Anne, don’t look as if you were trying to understand. Seventeen

[032-125] _can’t_ understand. I’m going to pretend right away that I am seventeen

[032-126] too, and I can do it, now that you’re here. You always bring youth in

[032-127] your hand like a gift. We’re going to have a jolly evening. Tea first .

[032-128] . . what do you want for tea? We’ll have whatever you like. Do think of

[032-129] something nice and indigestible.”

[032-130] There were sounds of riot and mirth in the little stone house that

[032-131] night. What with cooking and feasting and making candy and laughing and

[032-132] “pretending,” it is quite true that Miss Lavendar and Anne comported

[032-133] themselves in a fashion entirely unsuited to the [[pride|dignity]] of a spinster

[032-134] of forty-five and a sedate schoolma’am. Then, when they were tired,

[032-135] they sat down on the rug before the grate in the parlor, lighted only

[032-136] by the soft fireshine and perfumed deliciously by Miss Lavendar’s open

[032-137] rose-jar on the mantel. The wind had risen and was sighing and wailing

[032-138] around the eaves and the snow was thudding softly against the windows,

[032-139] as if a hundred storm sprites were tapping for entrance.

[032-140] “I’m so glad you’re here, Anne,” said Miss Lavendar, nibbling at her

[032-141] candy. “If you weren’t I should be blue . . . very blue . . . almost

[032-142] navy blue. Dreams and make-believes are all very well in the daytime

[032-143] and the sunshine, but when dark and storm come they fail to satisfy.

[032-144] One wants real things then. But you don’t know this . . . seventeen

[032-145] never knows it. At seventeen dreams DO satisfy because you think the

[032-146] realities are waiting for you further on. When I was seventeen, Anne, I

[032-147] didn’t think forty-five would find me a white-haired little old maid

[032-148] with nothing but dreams to fill my life.”

[032-149] “But you aren’t an old maid,” said Anne, smiling into Miss Lavendar’s

[032-150] wistful woodbrown eyes. “Old maids are _born_ . . . they don’t

[032-151] _become_.”

[032-152] “Some are born old maids, some achieve old maidenhood, and some have

[032-153] old maidenhood thrust upon them,” parodied Miss Lavendar whimsically.

[032-154] “You are one of those who have achieved it then,” laughed Anne, “and

[032-155] you’ve done it so beautifully that if every old maid were like you they

[032-156] would come into the fashion, I think.”

[032-157] “I always like to do things as well as possible,” said Miss Lavendar

[032-158] meditatively, “and since an old maid I had to be I was determined to be

[032-159] a very nice one. People say I’m odd; but it’s just because I follow my

[032-160] own way of being an old maid and refuse to copy the traditional

[032-161] pattern. Anne, did anyone ever tell you anything about [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]

[032-162] and me?”

[032-163] “Yes,” said Anne candidly, “I’ve heard that you and he were engaged

[032-164] once.”

[032-165] “So we were . . . twenty-five years ago . . . a lifetime ago. And we

[032-166] were to have been married the next spring. I had my wedding dress made,

[032-167] although nobody but mother and Stephen ever knew _that_. We’d been

[032-168] engaged in a way almost all our lives, you might say. When Stephen was

[032-169] a little boy his mother would bring him here when she came to see my

[032-170] mother; and the second time he ever came . . . he was nine and I was

[032-171] six . . . he told me out in the garden that he had pretty well made up

[032-172] his mind to marry me when he grew up. I remember that I said ‘Thank

[032-173] you’; and when he was gone I told mother very gravely that there was a

[032-174] great weight off my mind, because I wasn’t frightened any more about

[032-175] having to be an old maid. How poor mother laughed!”
