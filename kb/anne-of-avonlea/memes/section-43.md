[043-001] [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] was another radiant person.

[043-002] “Oh, [[anne-shirley|Miss Shirley]], ma’am, it has all turned out so beautiful. When Mr.

[043-003] Irving and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] come back from their tower I’m to go up to

[043-004] Boston and live with them . . . and me only fifteen, and the other

[043-005] girls never went till they were sixteen. Ain’t [[stephen-irving|Mr. Irving]] splendid? He

[043-006] just worships the ground she treads on and it makes me feel so queer

[043-007] sometimes to see the look in his eyes when he’s watching her. It

[043-008] beggars description, Miss Shirley, ma’am. I’m awful thankful they’re so

[043-009] fond of each other. It’s the best way, when all’s said and done, though

[043-010] some folks can get along without it. I’ve got an aunt who has been

[043-011] married three times and says she married the first time for [[romance|love]] and

[043-012] the last two times for strictly business, and was happy with all three

[043-013] except at the times of the funerals. But I think she took a resk, Miss

[043-014] Shirley, ma’am.”

[043-015] “Oh, it’s all so romantic,” breathed Anne to [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] that night. “If I

[043-016] hadn’t taken the wrong path that day we went to Mr. Kimball’s I’d never

[043-017] have known [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]; and if I hadn’t met her I’d never have taken

[043-018] [[paul-irving|Paul]] there . . . and he’d never have written to his father about

[043-019] visiting Miss Lavendar just as Mr. Irving was starting for San

[043-020] Francisco. Mr. Irving says whenever he got that letter he made up his

[043-021] mind to send his partner to San Francisco and come here instead. He

[043-022] hadn’t heard anything of Miss Lavendar for fifteen years. Somebody had

[043-023] told him then that she was to be married and he thought she was and

[043-024] never asked anybody anything about her. And now everything has come

[043-025] right. And I had a hand in bringing it about. Perhaps, as [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[043-026] says, everything is foreordained and it was bound to happen anyway. But

[043-027] even so, it’s nice to think one was an instrument used by

[043-028] predestination. Yes indeed, it’s very romantic.”

[043-029] “I can’t see that it’s so terribly romantic at all,” said Marilla

[043-030] rather crisply. Marilla thought Anne was too worked up about it and had

[043-031] plenty to do with getting ready for college without “traipsing” to Echo

[043-032] Lodge two days out of three helping Miss Lavendar. “In the first [[belonging|place]]

[043-033] two young fools quarrel and turn sulky; then Steve Irving goes to the

[043-034] States and after a spell gets married up there and is perfectly happy

[043-035] from all accounts. Then his wife dies and after a decent interval he

[043-036] thinks he’ll come home and see if his first fancy’ll have him.

[043-037] Meanwhile, she’s been living single, probably because nobody nice

[043-038] enough came along to want her, and they meet and agree to be married

[043-039] after all. Now, where is the romance in all that?”

[043-040] “Oh, there isn’t any, when you put it that way,” gasped Anne, rather as

[043-041] if somebody had thrown cold water over her. “I suppose that’s how it

[043-042] looks in prose. But it’s very different if you look at it through

[043-043] poetry . . . and _I_ think it’s nicer . . .” Anne recovered herself and

[043-044] her eyes shone and her cheeks flushed . . . “to look at it through

[043-045] poetry.”

[043-046] Marilla glanced at the radiant young face and refrained from further

[043-047] sarcastic comments. Perhaps some realization came to her that after all

[043-048] it was better to have, like Anne, “the vision and the faculty divine” .

[043-049] . . that gift which the world cannot bestow or take away, of looking at

[043-050] life through some transfiguring . . . or revealing? . . . medium,

[043-051] whereby everything seemed apparelled in celestial light, wearing a

[043-052] glory and a freshness not visible to those who, like herself and

[043-053] Charlotta the Fourth, looked at things only through prose.

[043-054] “When’s the wedding to be?” she asked after a pause.

[043-055] “The last Wednesday in August. They are to be married in the garden

[043-056] under the honeysuckle trellis . . . the very spot where Mr. Irving

[043-057] proposed to her twenty-five years ago. Marilla, that _is_ romantic,

[043-058] even in prose. There’s to be nobody there except [[mrs-irving|Mrs. Irving]] and Paul

[043-059] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] and I, and Miss Lavendar’s cousins. And they will

[043-060] leave on the six o’clock train for a trip to the Pacific coast. When

[043-061] they come back in the fall Paul and Charlotta the Fourth are to go up

[043-062] to Boston to live with them. But [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] is to be left just as it

[043-063] is. . . only of course they’ll sell the hens and cow, and board up the

[043-064] windows . . . and every summer they’re coming down to live in it. I’m

[043-065] so glad. It would have hurt me dreadfully next winter at [[redmond-college|Redmond]] to

[043-066] think of that dear stone house all stripped and deserted, with empty

[043-067] rooms . . . or far worse still, with other people living in it. But I

[043-068] can think of it now, just as I’ve always seen it, waiting happily for

[043-069] the summer to bring life and [[humor|laughter]] back to it again.”

[043-070] There was more romance in the world than that which had fallen to the

[043-071] share of the middle-aged lovers of the stone house. Anne stumbled

[043-072] suddenly on it one evening when she went over to [[orchard-slope|Orchard Slope]] by the

[043-073] wood cut and came out into the Barry garden. Diana Barry and Fred

[043-074] Wright were standing together under the big willow. Diana was leaning

[043-075] against the gray trunk, her lashes cast down on very crimson cheeks.

[043-076] One hand was held by Fred, who stood with his face bent toward her,

[043-077] stammering something in low earnest tones. There were no other people

[043-078] in the world except their two selves at that magic moment; so neither

[043-079] of them saw Anne, who, after one dazed glance of comprehension, turned

[043-080] and sped noiselessly back through the spruce wood, never stopping till

[043-081] she gained her own gable room, where she sat breathlessly down by her

[043-082] window and tried to collect her scattered wits.

[043-083] “Diana and Fred are in [[romance|love]] with each other,” she gasped. “Oh, it does

[043-084] seem so . . . so . . . so _hopelessly_ grown up.”

[043-085] Anne, of late, had not been without her suspicions that Diana was

[043-086] proving false to the melancholy Byronic hero of her early [[ambition|dreams]]. But

[043-087] as “things seen are mightier than things heard,” or suspected, the

[043-088] realization that it was actually so came to her with almost the shock

[043-089] of perfect surprise. This was succeeded by a queer, little lonely

[043-090] feeling . . . as if, somehow, Diana had gone forward into a new world,

[043-091] shutting a gate behind her, leaving Anne on the outside.

[043-092] “Things are changing so fast it almost frightens me,” Anne thought, a

[043-093] little sadly. “And I’m afraid that this can’t help making some

[043-094] difference between Diana and me. I’m sure I can’t tell her all my

[043-095] secrets after this . . . she might tell Fred. And what _can_ she see in

[043-096] Fred? He’s very nice and jolly . . . but he’s just [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]].”

[043-097] It is always a very puzzling question . . . what can somebody see in

[043-098] somebody else? But how fortunate after all that it is so, for if

[043-099] everybody saw alike . . . well, in that case, as the old Indian said,

[043-100] “Everybody would want my squaw.” It was plain that Diana _did_ see

[043-101] something in Fred Wright, however Anne’s eyes might be holden. Diana

[043-102] came to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] the next evening, a pensive, shy young lady, and

[043-103] told Anne the whole story in the dusky seclusion of the east gable.

[043-104] Both girls cried and kissed and laughed.

[043-105] “I’m so happy,” said Diana, “but it does seem ridiculous to think of me

[043-106] being engaged.”

[043-107] “What is it really like to be engaged?” asked Anne curiously.

[043-108] “Well, that all depends on who you’re engaged to,” answered Diana, with

[043-109] that maddening air of superior wisdom always assumed by those who are

[043-110] engaged over those who are not. “It’s perfectly lovely to be engaged to

[043-111] Fred . . . but I think it would be simply horrid to be engaged to

[043-112] anyone else.”

[043-113] “There’s not much comfort for the rest of us in that, seeing that there

[043-114] is only one Fred,” laughed Anne.

[043-115] “Oh, Anne, you don’t understand,” said Diana in vexation. “I didn’t

[043-116] mean _that_ . . . it’s so hard to explain. Never mind, you’ll

[043-117] understand sometime, when your own turn comes.”

[043-118] “Bless you, dearest of Dianas, I understand now. What is an imagination

[043-119] for if not to enable you to peep at life through other people’s eyes?”

[043-120] “You must be my bridesmaid, you know, Anne. Promise me that . . .

[043-121] wherever you may be when I’m married.”

[043-122] “I’ll come from the ends of the earth if necessary,” promised Anne

[043-123] solemnly.

[043-124] “Of course, it won’t be for ever so long yet,” said Diana, blushing.

[043-125] “Three years at the very least . . . for I’m only eighteen and mother

[043-126] says no daughter of hers shall be married before she’s twenty-one.

[043-127] Besides, Fred’s father is going to buy the Abraham Fletcher farm for

[043-128] him and he says he’s got to have it two thirds paid for before he’ll

[043-129] give it to him in his own name. But three years isn’t any too much time

[043-130] to get ready for housekeeping, for I haven’t a speck of fancy work made

[043-131] yet. But I’m going to begin crocheting doilies tomorrow. Myra Gillis

[043-132] had thirty-seven doilies when she was married and I’m determined I

[043-133] shall have as many as she had.”

[043-134] “I suppose it would be perfectly impossible to keep house with only

[043-135] thirty-six doilies,” conceded Anne, with a solemn face but dancing

[043-136] eyes.

[043-137] Diana looked hurt.

[043-138] “I didn’t think you’d make fun of me, Anne,” she said reproachfully.

[043-139] “Dearest, I wasn’t making fun of you,” cried Anne repentantly. “I was

[043-140] only teasing you a bit. I think you’ll make the sweetest little

[043-141] housekeeper in the world. And I think it’s perfectly lovely of you to

[043-142] be planning already for your home o’dreams.”

[043-143] Anne had no sooner uttered the phrase, “home o’dreams,” than it

[043-144] captivated her fancy and she immediately began the erection of one of

[043-145] her own. It was, of course, tenanted by an ideal master, dark, proud,

[043-146] and melancholy; but oddly enough, Gilbert Blythe persisted in hanging

[043-147] about too, helping her arrange pictures, lay out gardens, and

[043-148] accomplish sundry other tasks which a proud and melancholy hero

[043-149] evidently considered beneath his [[pride|dignity]]. Anne tried to banish

[043-150] Gilbert’s image from her castle in Spain but, somehow, he went on being

[043-151] there, so Anne, being in a hurry, gave up the attempt and pursued her

[043-152] aerial architecture with such success that her “home o’dreams” was

[043-153] built and furnished before Diana spoke again.

[043-154] “I suppose, Anne, you must think it’s funny I should like Fred so well

[043-155] when he’s so different from the kind of man I’ve always said I would

[043-156] marry . . . the tall, slender kind? But somehow I wouldn’t want Fred to

[043-157] be tall and slender . . . because, don’t you see, he wouldn’t be Fred

[043-158] then. Of course,” added Diana rather dolefully, “we will be a

[043-159] dreadfully pudgy couple. But after all that’s better than one of us

[043-160] being short and fat and the other tall and lean, like Morgan Sloane and

[043-161] his wife. [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says it always makes her think of the long and

[043-162] short of it when she sees them together.”

[043-163] “Well,” said Anne to herself that night, as she brushed her hair before

[043-164] her gilt framed mirror, “I am glad Diana is so happy and satisfied. But

[043-165] when my turn comes . . . if it ever does . . . I do hope there’ll be

[043-166] something a little more thrilling about it. But then Diana thought so

[043-167] too, once. I’ve heard her say time and again she’d never get engaged

[043-168] any poky commonplace way . . . he’d _have_ to do something splendid to

[043-169] win her. But she has changed. Perhaps I’ll change too. But I won’t . .

[043-170] . and I’m determined I won’t. Oh, I think these engagements are

[043-171] dreadfully unsettling things when they happen to your intimate

[043-172] friends.”
