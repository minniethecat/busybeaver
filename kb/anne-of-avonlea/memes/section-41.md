[041-001] “He has blue eyes and curly hair. He steals in and wakens me with a

[041-002] kiss every morning. Then all day he plays here in the garden . . . and

[041-003] I play with him. Such games as we have. We run races and talk with the

[041-004] echoes; and I tell him stories. And when twilight comes . . .”

[041-005] “_I_ know,” interrupted [[paul-irving|Paul]] eagerly. “He comes and sits beside you . .

[041-006] . _so_ . . . because of course at twelve he’d be too big to climb into

[041-007] your lap . . . and lays his head on your shoulder . . . _so_ . . . and

[041-008] you put your arms about him and hold him tight, tight, and rest your

[041-009] cheek on his head . . . yes, that’s the very way. Oh, you _do_ know,

[041-010] [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]].”

[041-011] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] found the two of them there when she came out of the stone house,

[041-012] and something in [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]’s face made her hate to disturb them.

[041-013] “I’m afraid we must go, Paul, if we want to get [[belonging|home]] before dark. Miss

[041-014] Lavendar, I’m going to invite myself to [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] for a whole week

[041-015] pretty soon.”

[041-016] “If you come for a week I’ll keep you for two,” threatened Miss

[041-017] Lavendar.

[041-018] XXVIII

[041-019] The Prince Comes Back to the Enchanted Palace

[041-020] The last day of school came and went. A triumphant “semi-annual

[041-021] examination” was held and Anne’s pupils acquitted themselves

[041-022] splendidly. At the close they gave her an address and a writing desk.

[041-023] All the girls and ladies present cried, and some of the boys had it

[041-024] cast up to them later on that they cried too, although they always

[041-025] denied it.

[041-026] [[mrs-harmon-andrews|Mrs. Harmon Andrews]], Mrs. [[peter-sloane|Peter Sloane]], and Mrs. William Bell walked

[041-027] home together and talked things over.

[041-028] “I do think it is such a pity Anne is leaving when the children seem so

[041-029] much attached to her,” sighed Mrs. Peter Sloane, who had a habit of

[041-030] sighing over everything and even finished off her jokes that way. “To

[041-031] be sure,” she added hastily, “we all know we’ll have a good teacher

[041-032] next year too.”

[041-033] “[[jane-andrews|Jane]] will do her duty, I’ve no doubt,” said Mrs. Andrews rather

[041-034] stiffly. “I don’t suppose she’ll tell the children quite so many fairy

[041-035] tales or spend so much time roaming about the woods with them. But she

[041-036] has her name on the Inspector’s Roll of Honor and the [[newbridge|Newbridge]] people

[041-037] are in a terrible state over her leaving.”

[041-038] “I’m real glad Anne is going to college,” said [[mrs-bell|Mrs. Bell]]. “She has

[041-039] always wanted it and it will be a splendid thing for her.”

[041-040] “Well, I don’t know.” Mrs. Andrews was determined not to agree fully

[041-041] with anybody that day. “I don’t see that Anne needs any more [[teaching|education]].

[041-042] She’ll probably be marrying [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], if his infatuation for her

[041-043] lasts till he gets through college, and what good will Latin and Greek

[041-044] do her then? If they taught you at college how to manage a man there

[041-045] might be some sense in her going.”

[041-046] Mrs. [[mr-harmon-andrews|Harmon Andrews]], so [[avonlea|Avonlea]] gossip whispered, had never learned how

[041-047] to manage her “man,” and as a result the Andrews household was not

[041-048] exactly a model of domestic happiness.

[041-049] “I see that the [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] call to [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] is up before the

[041-050] Presbytery,” said Mrs. Bell. “That means we’ll be losing him soon, I

[041-051] suppose.”

[041-052] “They’re not going before September,” said Mrs. Sloane. “It will be a

[041-053] great [[death|loss]] to the [[neighbor|community]] . . . though I always did think that Mrs.

[041-054] Allan dressed rather too gay for a minister’s wife. But we are none of

[041-055] us perfect. Did you notice how neat and snug [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] looked today?

[041-056] I never saw such a changed man. He goes to church every Sunday and has

[041-057] subscribed to the salary.”

[041-058] “Hasn’t that Paul Irving grown to be a big boy?” said Mrs. Andrews. “He

[041-059] was such a mite for his age when he came here. I declare I hardly knew

[041-060] him today. He’s getting to look a lot like his father.”

[041-061] “He’s a smart boy,” said Mrs. Bell.

[041-062] “He’s smart enough, but” . . . Mrs. Andrews lowered her voice . . . “I

[041-063] believe he tells queer stories. Gracie came home from school one day

[041-064] last week with the greatest rigmarole he had told her about people who

[041-065] lived down at the shore . . . stories there couldn’t be a word of truth

[041-066] in, you know. I told Gracie not to believe them, and she said Paul

[041-067] didn’t intend her to. But if he didn’t what did he tell them to her

[041-068] for?”

[041-069] “Anne says Paul is a genius,” said Mrs. Sloane.

[041-070] “He may be. You never know what to expect of them Americans,” said Mrs.

[041-071] Andrews. Mrs. Andrews’ only acquaintance with the word “genius” was

[041-072] derived from the colloquial fashion of calling any eccentric individual

[041-073] “a queer genius.” She probably thought, with [[mary-joe|Mary Joe]], that it meant a

[041-074] person with something wrong in his upper story.

[041-075] Back in the schoolroom Anne was sitting alone at her desk, as she had

[041-076] sat on the first day of school two years before, her face leaning on

[041-077] her hand, her dewy eyes looking wistfully out of the window to the Lake

[041-078] of Shining Waters. Her heart was so wrung over the parting with her

[041-079] pupils that for a moment college had lost all its charm. She still felt

[041-080] the clasp of [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]]’s arms about her neck and heard the childish

[041-081] wail, “I’ll _never_ [[romance|love]] any teacher as much as you, Miss Shirley,

[041-082] never, never.”

[041-083] For two years she had worked earnestly and faithfully, making many

[041-084] mistakes and [[education|learning]] from them. She had had her reward. She had taught

[041-085] her scholars something, but she felt that they had taught her much more

[041-086] . . . lessons of tenderness, self-control, innocent wisdom, lore of

[041-087] childish hearts. Perhaps she had not succeeded in “inspiring” any

[041-088] wonderful ambitions in her pupils, but she had taught them, more by her

[041-089] own sweet personality than by all her careful precepts, that it was

[041-090] good and necessary in the years that were before them to live their

[041-091] lives finely and graciously, holding fast to truth and courtesy and

[041-092] kindness, keeping aloof from all that savored of falsehood and meanness

[041-093] and vulgarity. They were, perhaps, all unconscious of having learned

[041-094] such lessons; but they would remember and practice them long after they

[041-095] had forgotten the capital of Afghanistan and the dates of the Wars of

[041-096] the Roses.

[041-097] “Another chapter in my life is closed,” said Anne aloud, as she locked

[041-098] her desk. She really felt very sad over it; but the romance in the idea

[041-099] of that “closed chapter” did comfort her a little.

[041-100] Anne spent a fortnight at Echo Lodge early in her vacation and

[041-101] everybody concerned had a good time.

[041-102] She took Miss Lavendar on a shopping expedition to town and persuaded

[041-103] her to buy a new organdy dress; then came the excitement of cutting and

[041-104] making it together, while the happy [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]] basted and

[041-105] swept up clippings. Miss Lavendar had complained that she could not

[041-106] feel much interest in anything, but the sparkle came back to her eyes

[041-107] over her pretty dress.

[041-108] “What a foolish, frivolous person I must be,” she sighed. “I’m

[041-109] wholesomely ashamed to think that a new dress . . . even it is a

[041-110] forget-me-not organdy . . . should exhilarate me so, when a good

[041-111] conscience and an extra contribution to Foreign Missions couldn’t do

[041-112] it.”

[041-113] Midway in her visit Anne went home to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] for a day to mend

[041-114] the twins’ stockings and settle up [[davy-keith|Davy]]’s accumulated store of

[041-115] questions. In the evening she went down to the shore road to see Paul

[041-116] Irving. As she passed by the low, square window of the Irving sitting

[041-117] room she caught a glimpse of Paul on somebody’s lap; but the next

[041-118] moment he came flying through the hall.

[041-119] “Oh, Miss Shirley,” he cried excitedly, “you can’t think what has

[041-120] happened! Something so splendid. Father is here . . . just think of

[041-121] that! Father is here! Come right in. Father, this is my beautiful

[041-122] teacher. _You_ know, father.”

[041-123] [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] came forward to meet Anne with a smile. He was a tall,

[041-124] handsome man of middle age, with iron-gray hair, deep-set, dark blue

[041-125] eyes, and a strong, sad face, splendidly modeled about chin and brow.

[041-126] Just the face for a hero of romance, Anne thought with a thrill of

[041-127] intense satisfaction. It was so disappointing to meet someone who ought

[041-128] to be a hero and find him bald or stooped, or otherwise lacking in

[041-129] manly beauty. Anne would have thought it dreadful if the object of Miss

[041-130] Lavendar’s romance had not looked the part.

[041-131] “So this is my little son’s ‘beautiful teacher,’ of whom I have heard

[041-132] so much,” said Mr. Irving with a hearty handshake. “Paul’s letters have

[041-133] been so full of you, Miss Shirley, that I feel as if I were pretty well

[041-134] acquainted with you already. I want to thank you for what you have done

[041-135] for Paul. I think that your influence has been just what he needed.

[041-136] Mother is one of the best and dearest of women; but her robust,

[041-137] matter-of-fact Scotch common sense could not always understand a

[041-138] temperament like my laddie’s. What was lacking in her you have

[041-139] supplied. Between you, I think Paul’s training in these two past years

[041-140] has been as nearly ideal as a motherless boy’s could be.”

[041-141] Everybody likes to be appreciated. Under Mr. Irving’s praise Anne’s

[041-142] face “burst flower like into rosy bloom,” and the busy, weary man of

[041-143] the world, looking at her, thought he had never seen a fairer, sweeter

[041-144] slip of girlhood than this little “down east” schoolteacher with her

[041-145] red hair and wonderful eyes.

[041-146] Paul sat between them blissfully happy.

[041-147] “I never dreamed father was coming,” he said radiantly. “Even Grandma

[041-148] didn’t know it. It was a great surprise. As a general thing . . .” Paul

[041-149] shook his brown curls gravely . . . “I don’t like to be surprised. You

[041-150] lose all the fun of expecting things when you’re surprised. But in a

[041-151] case like this it is all right. Father came last night after I had gone

[041-152] to bed. And after Grandma and Mary Joe had stopped being surprised he

[041-153] and Grandma came upstairs to look at me, not meaning to wake me up till

[041-154] morning. But I woke right up and saw father. I tell you I just sprang

[041-155] at him.”

[041-156] “With a hug like a bear’s,” said Mr. Irving, putting his arms around

[041-157] Paul’s shoulder smilingly. “I hardly knew my boy, he had grown so big

[041-158] and brown and sturdy.”

[041-159] “I don’t know which was the most pleased to see father, Grandma or I,”

[041-160] continued Paul. “Grandma’s been in kitchen all day making the things

[041-161] father likes to eat. She wouldn’t [[faith|trust]] them to Mary Joe, she says.

[041-162] That’s _her_ way of showing gladness. _I_ like best just to sit and

[041-163] talk to father. But I’m going to leave you for a little while now if

[041-164] you’ll excuse me. I must get the cows for Mary Joe. That is one of my

[041-165] daily duties.”

[041-166] When Paul had scampered away to do his “daily duty” Mr. Irving talked

[041-167] to Anne of various matters. But Anne felt that he was thinking of

[041-168] something else underneath all the time. Presently it came to the

[041-169] surface.

[041-170] “In Paul’s last letter he spoke of going with you to visit an old . . .

[041-171] friend of mine . . . Miss Lewis at the stone house in [[grafton|Grafton]]. Do you

[041-172] know her well?”

[041-173] “Yes, indeed, she is a _very_ dear friend of mine,” was Anne’s demure

[041-174] reply, which gave no hint of the sudden thrill that tingled over her

[041-175] from head to foot at Mr. Irving’s question. Anne “felt instinctively”

[041-176] that romance was peeping at her around a corner.

[041-177] Mr. Irving rose and went to the window, looking out on a great, golden,

[041-178] billowing sea where a wild wind was harping. For a few moments there

[041-179] was silence in the little dark-walled room. Then he turned and looked

[041-180] down into Anne’s sympathetic face with a smile, half-whimsical,

[041-181] half-tender.
