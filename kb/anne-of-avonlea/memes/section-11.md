[011-001] “I told him he might,” said [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] wearily. “I thought it would keep

[011-002] him out of worse mischief. He can only get dirty at that. We’ll have

[011-003] our teas over before we call him to his. [[dora-keith|Dora]] can have hers with us,

[011-004] but I would never dare to let [[davy-keith|Davy]] sit down at the table with all the

[011-005] Aids here.”

[011-006] When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] went to call the Aids to tea she found that Dora was not in

[011-007] the parlor. Mrs. Jasper Bell said Davy had come to the front door and

[011-008] called her out. A hasty consultation with Marilla in the pantry

[011-009] resulted in a decision to let both children have their teas together

[011-010] later on.

[011-011] Tea was half over when the dining room was invaded by a forlorn figure.

[011-012] Marilla and Anne stared in dismay, the Aids in amazement. Could that be

[011-013] Dora . . . that sobbing nondescript in a drenched, dripping dress and

[011-014] hair from which the water was streaming on Marilla’s new coin-spot rug?

[011-015] “Dora, what has happened to you?” cried Anne, with a guilty glance at

[011-016] Mrs. Jasper Bell, whose family was said to be the only one in the world

[011-017] in which accidents never occurred.

[011-018] “Davy made me walk the pigpen fence,” wailed Dora. “I didn’t want to

[011-019] but he called me a fraid-cat. And I fell off into the pigpen and my

[011-020] dress got all dirty and the pig runned right over me. My dress was just

[011-021] awful but Davy said if I’d stand under the pump he’d wash it clean, and

[011-022] I did and he pumped water all over me but my dress ain’t a bit cleaner

[011-023] and my pretty sash and shoes is all spoiled.”

[011-024] Anne did the honors of the table alone for the rest of the meal while

[011-025] Marilla went upstairs and redressed Dora in her old clothes. Davy was

[011-026] caught and sent to bed without any supper. Anne went to his room at

[011-027] twilight and talked to him seriously . . . a method in which she had

[011-028] great faith, not altogether unjustified by results. She told him she

[011-029] felt very badly over his conduct.

[011-030] “I feel sorry now myself,” admitted Davy, “but the trouble is I never

[011-031] feel sorry for doing things till after I’ve did them. Dora wouldn’t

[011-032] help me make pies, cause she was afraid of messing her clo’es and that

[011-033] made me hopping mad. I s’pose [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] wouldn’t have made _his_

[011-034] sister walk a pigpen fence if he knew she’d fall in?”

[011-035] “No, he would never dream of such a thing. Paul is a perfect little

[011-036] gentleman.”

[011-037] Davy screwed his eyes tight shut and seemed to meditate on this for a

[011-038] time. Then he crawled up and put his arms about Anne’s neck, snuggling

[011-039] his flushed little face down on her shoulder.

[011-040] “Anne, don’t you like me a little bit, even if I ain’t a good boy like

[011-041] Paul?”

[011-042] “Indeed I do,” said Anne sincerely. Somehow, it was impossible to help

[011-043] liking Davy. “But I’d like you better still if you weren’t so naughty.”

[011-044] “I . . . did something else today,” went on Davy in a muffled voice.

[011-045] “I’m sorry now but I’m awful scared to tell you. You won’t be very

[011-046] cross, will you? And you won’t tell Marilla, will you?”

[011-047] “I don’t know, Davy. Perhaps I ought to tell her. But I think I can

[011-048] promise you I won’t if you promise me that you will never do it again,

[011-049] whatever it is.”

[011-050] “No, I never will. Anyhow, it’s not likely I’d find any more of them

[011-051] this year. I found this one on the cellar steps.”

[011-052] “Davy, what is it you’ve done?”

[011-053] “I put a toad in Marilla’s bed. You can go and take it out if you like.

[011-054] But say, Anne, wouldn’t it be fun to leave it there?”

[011-055] “Davy Keith!” Anne sprang from Davy’s clinging arms and flew across the

[011-056] hall to Marilla’s room. The bed was slightly rumpled. She threw back

[011-057] the blankets in nervous haste and there in very truth was the toad,

[011-058] blinking at her from under a pillow.

[011-059] “How can I carry that awful thing out?” moaned Anne with a shudder. The

[011-060] fire shovel suggested itself to her and she crept down to get it while

[011-061] Marilla was busy in the pantry. Anne had her own troubles carrying that

[011-062] toad downstairs, for it hopped off the shovel three times and once she

[011-063] thought she had lost it in the hall. When she finally deposited it in

[011-064] the cherry orchard she drew a long breath of relief.

[011-065] “If Marilla knew she’d never feel safe getting into bed again in her

[011-066] life. I’m so glad that little sinner repented in time. There’s [[diana-barry|Diana]]

[011-067] signaling to me from her window. I’m glad . . . I really feel the need

[011-068] of some diversion, for what with [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] in school and Davy Keith

[011-069] at [[belonging|home]] my nerves have had about all they can endure for one day.”

[011-070] IX

[011-071] A Question of Color

[011-072] “That old nuisance of a [[rachel-lynde|Rachel Lynde]] was here again today, pestering me

[011-073] for a subscription towards buying a carpet for the vestry room,” said

[011-074] [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] wrathfully. “I detest that woman more than anybody I know.

[011-075] She can put a whole sermon, text, comment, and application, into six

[011-076] words, and throw it at you like a brick.”

[011-077] Anne, who was perched on the edge of the veranda, enjoying the charm of

[011-078] a mild west wind blowing across a newly ploughed field on a gray

[011-079] November twilight and piping a quaint little melody among the twisted

[011-080] firs below the garden, turned her dreamy face over her shoulder.

[011-081] “The trouble is, you and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] don’t understand one another,” she

[011-082] explained. “That is always what is wrong when people don’t like each

[011-083] other. I didn’t like [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] at first either; but as soon as I came

[011-084] to understand her I learned to.”

[011-085] “Mrs. Lynde may be an acquired taste with some folks; but I didn’t keep

[011-086] on eating bananas because I was told I’d learn to like them if I did,”

[011-087] growled Mr. Harrison. “And as for [[wisdom|understanding]] her, I understand that

[011-088] she is a confirmed busybody and I told her so.”

[011-089] “Oh, that must have hurt her feelings very much,” said Anne

[011-090] reproachfully. “How could you say such a thing? _I_ said some dreadful

[011-091] things to Mrs. Lynde long ago but it was when I had lost my temper. I

[011-092] couldn’t say them _deliberately_.”

[011-093] “It was the truth and I believe in telling the truth to everybody.”

[011-094] “But you don’t tell the whole truth,” objected Anne. “You only tell the

[011-095] disagreeable part of the truth. Now, you’ve told me a dozen times that

[011-096] my hair was red, but you’ve never once told me that I had a nice nose.”

[011-097] “I daresay you know it without any telling,” chuckled Mr. Harrison.

[011-098] “I know I have red hair too . . . although it’s _much_ darker than it

[011-099] used to be . . . so there’s no need of telling me that either.”

[011-100] “Well, well, I’ll try and not mention it again since you’re so

[011-101] sensitive. You must excuse me, Anne. I’ve got a habit of being

[011-102] outspoken and folks mustn’t mind it.”

[011-103] “But they can’t help minding it. And I don’t think it’s any help that

[011-104] it’s your habit. What would you think of a person who went about

[011-105] sticking pins and needles into people and saying, ‘Excuse me, you

[011-106] mustn’t mind it . . . it’s just a habit I’ve got.’ You’d think he was

[011-107] crazy, wouldn’t you? And as for Mrs. Lynde being a busybody, perhaps

[011-108] she is. But did you tell her she had a very kind heart and always

[011-109] helped the poor, and never said a word when Timothy Cotton stole a

[011-110] crock of butter out of her dairy and told his wife he’d bought it from

[011-111] her? Mrs. Cotton cast it up to her the next time they met that it

[011-112] tasted of turnips and Mrs. Lynde just said she was sorry it had turned

[011-113] out so poorly.”

[011-114] “I suppose she has some good qualities,” conceded Mr. Harrison

[011-115] grudgingly. “Most folks have. I have some myself, though you might

[011-116] never suspect it. But anyhow I ain’t going to give anything to that

[011-117] carpet. Folks are everlasting begging for money here, it seems to me.

[011-118] How’s your project of painting the hall coming on?”

[011-119] “Splendidly. We had a meeting of the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] last Friday night and

[011-120] found that we had plenty of money subscribed to paint the hall and

[011-121] shingle the roof too. _Most_ people gave very liberally, Mr. Harrison.”

[011-122] Anne was a sweet-souled lass, but she could instill some venom into

[011-123] innocent italics when occasion required.

[011-124] “What color are you going to have it?”

[011-125] “We have decided on a very pretty green. The roof will be dark red, of

[011-126] course. Mr. Roger Pye is going to get the paint in town today.”

[011-127] “Who’s got the job?”

[011-128] “Mr. Joshua Pye of [[carmody|Carmody]]. He has nearly finished the shingling. We

[011-129] had to give him the contract, for every one of the Pyes . . . and there

[011-130] are four families, you know . . . said they wouldn’t give a cent unless

[011-131] Joshua got it. They had subscribed twelve dollars between them and we

[011-132] thought that was too much to lose, although some people think we

[011-133] shouldn’t have given in to the Pyes. Mrs. Lynde says they try to run

[011-134] everything.”

[011-135] “The main question is will this Joshua do his work well. If he does I

[011-136] don’t see that it matters whether his name is Pye or Pudding.”

[011-137] “He has the reputation of being a good workman, though they say he’s a

[011-138] very peculiar man. He hardly ever talks.”

[011-139] “He’s peculiar enough all right then,” said Mr. Harrison drily. “Or at

[011-140] least, folks here will call him so. I never was much of a talker till I

[011-141] came to [[avonlea|Avonlea]] and then I had to begin in self-defense or Mrs. Lynde

[011-142] would have said I was dumb and started a subscription to have me taught

[011-143] sign language. You’re not going yet, Anne?”

[011-144] “I must. I have some sewing to do for Dora this evening. Besides, Davy

[011-145] is probably breaking Marilla’s heart with some new mischief by this

[011-146] time. This morning the first thing he said was, ‘Where does the dark

[011-147] go, Anne? I want to know.’ I told him it went around to the other side

[011-148] of the world but after breakfast he declared it didn’t . . . that it

[011-149] went down the well. Marilla says she caught him hanging over the

[011-150] well-box four times today, trying to reach down to the dark.”

[011-151] “He’s a limb,” declared Mr. Harrison. “He came over here yesterday and

[011-152] pulled six feathers out of Ginger’s tail before I could get in from the

[011-153] barn. The poor bird has been moping ever since. Those children must be

[011-154] a sight of trouble to you folks.”

[011-155] “Everything that’s worth having is some trouble,” said Anne, secretly

[011-156] resolving to forgive Davy’s next offence, whatever it might be, since

[011-157] he had avenged her on Ginger.

[011-158] Mr. Roger Pye brought the hall paint home that night and Mr. Joshua

[011-159] Pye, a surly, taciturn man, began painting the next day. He was not

[011-160] disturbed in his task. The hall was situated on what was called “the

[011-161] lower road.” In late autumn this road was always muddy and wet, and

[011-162] people going to Carmody traveled by the longer “upper” road. The hall

[011-163] was so closely surrounded by fir woods that it was invisible unless you

[011-164] were near it. Mr. Joshua Pye painted away in the [[loneliness|solitude]] and

[011-165] independence that were so dear to his unsociable heart.

[011-166] Friday afternoon he finished his job and went home to Carmody. Soon

[011-167] after his departure Mrs. Rachel Lynde drove by, having braved the mud

[011-168] of the lower road out of curiosity to see what the hall looked like in

[011-169] its new coat of paint. When she rounded the spruce curve she saw.
