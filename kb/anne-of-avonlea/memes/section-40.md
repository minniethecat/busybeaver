[040-001] “I think it would,” agreed [[anne-shirley|Anne]] solemnly. “Anyway, I daresay that if

[040-002] your Grandma and I both got down to what we really do mean, under our

[040-003] different ways of expressing it, we’d find out we both meant much the

[040-004] same thing. You’d better go by her way of expressing it, since it’s

[040-005] been the result of experience. We’ll have to wait until we see how the

[040-006] twins do turn out before we can be sure that my way is equally good.”

[040-007] After lunch they went back to the garden, where [[paul-irving|Paul]] made the

[040-008] acquaintance of the echoes, to his wonder and delight, while Anne and

[040-009] [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] sat on the stone bench under the poplar and talked.

[040-010] “So you are going away in the fall?” said [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] wistfully. “I

[040-011] ought to be glad for your sake, Anne . . . but I’m horribly, selfishly

[040-012] sorry. I shall miss you so much. Oh, sometimes, I think it is of no use

[040-013] to make friends. They only go out of your life after awhile and leave a

[040-014] hurt that is worse than the emptiness before they came.”

[040-015] “That sounds like something Miss Eliza Andrews might say but never Miss

[040-016] Lavendar,” said Anne. “_Nothing_ is worse than emptiness . . . and I’m

[040-017] not going out of your life. There are such things as letters and

[040-018] vacations. Dearest, I’m afraid you’re looking a little pale and tired.”

[040-019] “Oh . . . hoo . . . hoo . . . hoo,” went Paul on the dyke, where he had

[040-020] been making noises diligently . . . not all of them melodious in the

[040-021] making, but all coming back transmuted into the very gold and silver of

[040-022] sound by the fairy alchemists over the river. Miss Lavendar made an

[040-023] impatient movement with her pretty hands.

[040-024] “I’m just tired of everything . . . even of the echoes. There is

[040-025] nothing in my life but echoes . . . echoes of lost hopes and [[ambition|dreams]] and

[040-026] joys. They’re beautiful and mocking. Oh Anne, it’s horrid of me to talk

[040-027] like this when I have company. It’s just that I’m getting old and it

[040-028] doesn’t agree with me. I know I’ll be fearfully cranky by the time I’m

[040-029] sixty. But perhaps all I need is a course of blue pills.” At this

[040-030] moment [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]], who had disappeared after lunch, returned,

[040-031] and announced that the northeast corner of Mr. John Kimball’s pasture

[040-032] was red with early strawberries, and wouldn’t Miss Shirley like to go

[040-033] and pick some.

[040-034] “Early strawberries for tea!” exclaimed Miss Lavendar. “Oh, I’m not so

[040-035] old as I thought . . . and I don’t need a single blue pill! Girls, when

[040-036] you come back with your strawberries we’ll have tea out here under the

[040-037] silver poplar. I’ll have it all ready for you with [[belonging|home]]-grown cream.”

[040-038] Anne and Charlotta the Fourth accordingly betook themselves back to Mr.

[040-039] Kimball’s pasture, a green remote place where the air was as soft as

[040-040] velvet and fragrant as a bed of violets and golden as amber.

[040-041] “Oh, isn’t it sweet and fresh back here?” breathed Anne. “I just feel

[040-042] as if I were drinking in the sunshine.”

[040-043] “Yes, ma’am, so do I. That’s just exactly how I feel too, ma’am,”

[040-044] agreed Charlotta the Fourth, who would have said precisely the same

[040-045] thing if Anne had remarked that she felt like a pelican of the

[040-046] wilderness. Always after Anne had visited [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] Charlotta the

[040-047] Fourth mounted to her little room over the kitchen and tried before her

[040-048] looking glass to speak and look and move like Anne. Charlotta could

[040-049] never flatter herself that she quite succeeded; but practice makes

[040-050] perfect, as Charlotta had learned at school, and she fondly hoped that

[040-051] in time she might catch the trick of that dainty uplift of chin, that

[040-052] quick, starry outflashing of eyes, that fashion of walking as if you

[040-053] were a bough swaying in the wind. It seemed so easy when you watched

[040-054] Anne. Charlotta the Fourth admired Anne wholeheartedly. It was not that

[040-055] she thought her so very handsome. [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]]’s beauty of crimson cheek

[040-056] and black curls was much more to Charlotta the Fourth’s taste than

[040-057] Anne’s moonshine charm of luminous gray eyes and the pale, everchanging

[040-058] roses of her cheeks.

[040-059] “But I’d rather look like you than be pretty,” she told Anne sincerely.

[040-060] Anne laughed, sipped the honey from the tribute, and cast away the

[040-061] sting. She was used to taking her compliments mixed. Public opinion

[040-062] never agreed on Anne’s looks. People who had heard her called handsome

[040-063] met her and were disappointed. People who had heard her called plain

[040-064] saw her and wondered where other people’s eyes were. Anne herself would

[040-065] never believe that she had any claim to beauty. When she looked in the

[040-066] glass all she saw was a little pale face with seven freckles on the

[040-067] nose thereof. Her mirror never revealed to her the elusive,

[040-068] ever-varying play of feeling that came and went over her features like

[040-069] a rosy illuminating flame, or the charm of dream and [[humor|laughter]]

[040-070] alternating in her big eyes.

[040-071] While Anne was not beautiful in any strictly defined sense of the word

[040-072] she possessed a certain evasive charm and distinction of appearance

[040-073] that left beholders with a pleasurable sense of satisfaction in that

[040-074] softly rounded girlhood of hers, with all its strongly felt

[040-075] potentialities. Those who knew Anne best felt, without realizing that

[040-076] they felt it, that her greatest attraction was the aura of possibility

[040-077] surrounding her. . . the power of future development that was in her.

[040-078] She seemed to walk in an atmosphere of things about to happen.

[040-079] As they picked, Charlotta the Fourth confided to Anne her fears

[040-080] regarding Miss Lavendar. The warm-hearted little handmaiden was

[040-081] honestly worried over her adored mistress’ condition.

[040-082] “Miss Lavendar isn’t well, Miss Shirley, ma’am. I’m sure she isn’t,

[040-083] though she never complains. She hasn’t seemed like herself this long

[040-084] while, ma’am . . . not since that day you and Paul were here together

[040-085] before. I feel sure she caught cold that night, ma’am. After you and

[040-086] him had gone she went out and walked in the garden for long after dark

[040-087] with nothing but a little shawl on her. There was a lot of snow on the

[040-088] walks and I feel sure she got a chill, ma’am. Ever since then I’ve

[040-089] noticed her acting tired and lonesome like. She don’t seem to take an

[040-090] interest in anything, ma’am. She never pretends company’s coming, nor

[040-091] fixes up for it, nor nothing, ma’am. It’s only when you come she seems

[040-092] to chirk up a bit. And the worst sign of all, Miss Shirley, ma’am . .

[040-093] .” Charlotta the Fourth lowered her voice as if she were about to tell

[040-094] some exceedingly weird and awful symptom indeed . . . “is that she

[040-095] never gets cross now when I breaks things. Why, Miss Shirley, ma’am,

[040-096] yesterday I bruk her green and yaller bowl that’s always stood on the

[040-097] bookcase. Her grandmother brought it out from England and Miss Lavendar

[040-098] was awful choice of it. I was dusting it just as careful, Miss Shirley,

[040-099] ma’am, and it slipped out, so fashion, afore I could grab holt of it,

[040-100] and bruk into about forty millyun pieces. I tell you I was sorry and

[040-101] scared. I thought Miss Lavendar would scold me awful, ma’am; and I’d

[040-102] ruther she had than take it the way she did. She just come in and

[040-103] hardly looked at it and said, ‘It’s no matter, Charlotta. Take up the

[040-104] pieces and throw them away.’ Just like that, Miss Shirley, ma’am . . .

[040-105] ‘take up the pieces and throw them away,’ as if it wasn’t her

[040-106] grandmother’s bowl from England. Oh, she isn’t well and I feel awful

[040-107] bad about it. She’s got nobody to look after her but me.”

[040-108] Charlotta the Fourth’s eyes brimmed up with tears. Anne patted the

[040-109] little brown paw holding the cracked pink cup sympathetically.

[040-110] “I think Miss Lavendar needs a change, Charlotta. She stays here alone

[040-111] too much. Can’t we induce her to go away for a little trip?”

[040-112] Charlotta shook her head, with its rampant bows, disconsolately.

[040-113] “I don’t think so, Miss Shirley, ma’am. Miss Lavendar hates visiting.

[040-114] She’s only got three relations she ever visits and she says she just

[040-115] goes to see them as a family duty. Last time when she come home she

[040-116] said she wasn’t going to visit for family duty no more. ‘I’ve come home

[040-117] in [[romance|love]] with loneliness, Charlotta,’ she says to me, ‘and I never want

[040-118] to stray from my own vine and fig tree again. My relations try so hard

[040-119] to make an old lady of me and it has a bad effect on me.’ Just like

[040-120] that, Miss Shirley, ma’am. ‘It has a very bad effect on me.’ So I don’t

[040-121] think it would do any good to coax her to go visiting.”

[040-122] “We must see what can be done,” said Anne decidedly, as she put the

[040-123] last possible berry in her pink cup. “Just as soon as I have my

[040-124] vacation I’ll come through and spend a whole week with you. We’ll have

[040-125] a picnic every day and pretend all sorts of interesting things, and see

[040-126] if we can’t cheer Miss Lavendar up.”

[040-127] “That will be the very thing, Miss Shirley, ma’am,” exclaimed Charlotta

[040-128] the Fourth in rapture. She was glad for Miss Lavendar’s sake and for

[040-129] her own too. With a whole week in which to study Anne constantly she

[040-130] would surely be able to learn how to move and behave like her.

[040-131] When the girls got back to Echo Lodge they found that Miss Lavendar and

[040-132] Paul had carried the little square table out of the kitchen to the

[040-133] garden and had everything ready for tea. Nothing ever tasted so

[040-134] delicious as those strawberries and cream, eaten under a great blue sky

[040-135] all curdled over with fluffy little white clouds, and in the long

[040-136] shadows of the wood with its lispings and its murmurings. After tea

[040-137] Anne helped Charlotta wash the dishes in the kitchen, while Miss

[040-138] Lavendar sat on the stone bench with Paul and heard all about his rock

[040-139] people. She was a good listener, this sweet Miss Lavendar, but just at

[040-140] the last it struck Paul that she had suddenly lost interest in the Twin

[040-141] Sailors.

[040-142] “Miss Lavendar, why do you look at me like that?” he asked gravely.

[040-143] “How do I look, Paul?”

[040-144] “Just as if you were looking through me at somebody I put you in mind

[040-145] of,” said Paul, who had such occasional flashes of uncanny [[wisdom|insight]] that

[040-146] it wasn’t quite safe to have secrets when he was about.

[040-147] “You do put me in mind of somebody I knew long ago,” said Miss Lavendar

[040-148] dreamily.

[040-149] “When you were young?”

[040-150] “Yes, when I was young. Do I seem very old to you, Paul?”

[040-151] “Do you know, I can’t make up my mind about that,” said Paul

[040-152] confidentially. “Your hair looks old . . . I never knew a young person

[040-153] with white hair. But your eyes are as young as my beautiful teacher’s

[040-154] when you laugh. I tell you what, Miss Lavendar” . . . Paul’s voice and

[040-155] face were as solemn as a judge’s . . . “I think you would make a

[040-156] splendid mother. You have just the right look in your eyes . . . the

[040-157] look my little mother always had. I think it’s a pity you haven’t any

[040-158] boys of your own.”

[040-159] “I have a little dream boy, Paul.”

[040-160] “Oh, have you really? How old is he?”

[040-161] “[[About]] your age I think. He ought to be older because I dreamed him

[040-162] long before you were born. But I’ll never let him get any older than

[040-163] eleven or twelve; because if I did some day he might grow up altogether

[040-164] and then I’d lose him.”

[040-165] “I know,” nodded Paul. “That’s the beauty of dream-people . . . they

[040-166] stay any age you want them. You and my beautiful teacher and me myself

[040-167] are the only folks in the world that I know of that have dream-people.

[040-168] Isn’t it funny and nice we should all know each other? But I guess that

[040-169] kind of people always find each other out. Grandma never has

[040-170] dream-people and [[mary-joe|Mary Joe]] thinks I’m wrong in the upper story because I

[040-171] have them. But I think it’s splendid to have them. _You_ know, Miss

[040-172] Lavendar. Tell me all about your little dream-boy.”
