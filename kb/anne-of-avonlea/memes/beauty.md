# Beauty

The aesthetic principle that animates [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s engagement [[humor|wit]]h the world — her lifelong mission "to add some beauty to life" (009-073) through appreciation of [[nature-appreciation|nature]], cultivation of surroundings, and recognition of beauty in unexpected [[belonging|place]]s.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, beauty is not a passive quality to be observed but an active principle to be sought, created, and defended. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates this in her characteristic blend of idealism and determination: "I'd like to add some beauty to life . . . I don't exactly mean in the way of paint and poetry . . . I mean just beauty in everyday life" (009-073). For Montgomery, the pursuit of beauty is a moral endeavor — it ennobles the seeker and enriches the [[neighbor|community]].

The novel opens [[humor|wit]]h a poetic allusion to "flowing curves of beauty" (001-011), immediately signaling that aesthetic perception will be central to the narrative. Throughout, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s capacity to perceive beauty where others see only the ordinary — a gray December wood, a dismal schoolroom, a lonely spinster's cottage — serves as her defining gift.

## Examples in the Text

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Aesthetic Mission (Chapter 9).** In a conversation [[humor|wit]]h [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] declares her life's purpose: "I'd like to add some beauty to life" (009-073). This is not idle romanticism but a practical program — she will beautify her schoolroom, her [[belonging|home]], and her [[neighbor|community]]. The statement establishes beauty as an active force rather than a passive pleasure.

**The Schoolroom Transformed (Chapter 23).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s decorative transformation of the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] schoolroom — filling it [[humor|wit]]h wild roses and honeysuckle — is her most concrete act of beauty-making. The room becomes "the veritable 'bower' of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imagination" (023-017), and even skeptical [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] "came in to criticize and remained to praise" (023-017).

**[[diana-barry|Diana]]'s Physical Beauty (Chapter 40).** Montgomery acknowledges physical beauty through [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]], whose "beauty of crimson cheek and g[[death|loss]]y black eyes" is "very handsome" (040-055). Yet the novel consistently subordinates physical beauty to qualities of character — [[anne-shirley|Anne]], who doubts "that she had any claim to beauty" (040-065), is the novel's true aesthetic force because beauty flows from her perceptions, not her features.

**Beauty in Age (Chapter 31).** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], though no longer young, is remembered as having been "a great beauty when she was a girl" (031-129). Her story demonstrates that beauty evolves rather than disappears — in middle age, she possesses a different but equally compelling loveliness born of character and experience.

**The Transformative Power of Enthusiasm (Chapter 37).** Montgomery describes how enthusiasm can transform experience "into beauty and delight" (037-135). This is the novel's most compressed statement of its aesthetic philosophy: beauty is as much a way of seeing as a quality of the thing seen.

**A Shared Aesthetic Vision (Chapter 17).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s philosophy finds its credo in a statement attributed to her: "We're to seek for beauty and refuse to see anything else" (017-022). The self-conscious idealism of this position is characteristic — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] knows it is a choice, and she makes it deliberately.

## Thematic Significance

**Beauty as Resistance.** In a world of practical concerns, disappointing [[community|neighbors]], and difficult pupils, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s insistence on beauty is a form of resistance — a refusal to let the mundane extinguish the marvellous. Her decorated schoolroom and her delight in winter landscapes are small acts of defiance against the tyranny of the ordinary.

**The Democracy of Beauty.** Montgomery democratizes beauty by locating it in perception rather than possession. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], an orphan [[humor|wit]]h modest means, experiences more beauty than the wealthy because she has cultivated the capacity to see it. The novel argues that beauty is available to all who will make the effort to perceive it.

**Beauty and [[community|Community]].** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s aesthetic projects — the schoolroom, the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] improvements, her garden — all serve the [[neighbor|community]]. Beauty in Montgomery's vision is not a private luxury but a public good, enriching the lives of all who encounter it.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** initially exemplifies the practical mindset that dismisses beauty as frivolous. Her conversion — from skepticism to praise of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s decorated schoolroom (023-017) — enacts the novel's argument that beauty can win over even its most determined skeptics.

**The "Unimaginative" Majority.** Characters described as lacking imagination — those who "hadn't a particle of imagination" (024-116) — are implicitly those who cannot perceive beauty. Montgomery presents this not as a neutral difference but as a genuine deprivation.
