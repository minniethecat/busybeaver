# Jane Andrews

## Character Overview

Jane Andrews represents a path [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] does not take. Where [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[ambition|dreams]] of "adding beauty to life" and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] aspires to medicine, Jane has no "further ambitions" beyond the honor roll (005-036). Montgomery presents this not as failure but as a legitimate mode of being — Jane is content, and contentment is not a flaw.

Her presence serves to broaden the novel's vision of what a good life looks like. Not everyone needs to change the world; some people, like Jane, simply live well in their corner of it, and the novel honors that choice.

## Role in the Story

Jane's role is minor but thematically significant. She provides contrast to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s ambition, demonstrating that the novel's celebration of striving is not a condemnation of contentment. Her presence in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s classroom is a reminder that a good teacher serves all pupils — the ambitious and the contented alike.

## Character Analysis

Jane Andrews is a quiet argument for the validity of ordinary life. In a novel that celebrates [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s exceptionality, Jane represents the majority — those whose ambitions are modest but whose lives are nonetheless meaningful. Montgomery does not elevate Jane above [[anne-shirley|Anne]], but neither does she diminish her.
