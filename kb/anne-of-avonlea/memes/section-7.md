[007-001] [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] came up later on with more encouragement. That good lady had

[007-002] waylaid the schoolchildren at her gate and demanded of them how they

[007-003] liked their new teacher.

[007-004] “And every one of them said they liked you splendid, [[anne-shirley|Anne]], except

[007-005] [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]. I must admit he didn’t. He said you ‘weren’t any good,

[007-006] just like all girl teachers.’ There’s the Pye leaven for you. But never

[007-007] mind.”

[007-008] “I’m not going to mind,” said Anne quietly, “and I’m going to make

[007-009] Anthony Pye like me yet. [[patience|Patience]] and kindness will surely win him.”

[007-010] “Well, you can never tell about a Pye,” said Mrs. Rachel cautiously.

[007-011] “They go by contraries, like [[ambition|dreams]], often as not. As for that

[007-012] _Don_nell woman, she’ll get no Don_nell_ing from me, I can assure you.

[007-013] The name is _Don_nell and always has been. The woman is crazy, that’s

[007-014] what. She has a pug dog she calls Queenie and it has its meals at the

[007-015] table along with the family, eating off a china plate. I’d be afraid of

[007-016] a judgment if I was her. Thomas says Donnell himself is a sensible,

[007-017] hard-working man, but he hadn’t much gumption when he picked out a

[007-018] wife, that’s what.”

[007-019] VI

[007-020] All Sorts and Conditions of Men . . . and women

[007-021] A September day on [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] hills; a crisp wind blowing up

[007-022] over the sand dunes from the sea; a long red road, winding through

[007-023] fields and woods, now looping itself about a corner of thick set

[007-024] spruces, now threading a plantation of young maples with great feathery

[007-025] sheets of ferns beneath them, now dipping down into a hollow where a

[007-026] brook flashed out of the woods and into them again, now basking in open

[007-027] sunshine between ribbons of golden-rod and smoke-blue asters; air

[007-028] athrill with the pipings of myriads of crickets, those glad little

[007-029] pensioners of the summer hills; a plump brown pony ambling along the

[007-030] road; two girls behind him, full to the lips with the simple, priceless

[007-031] [[laughter|joy]] of youth and life.

[007-032] “Oh, this is a day left over from Eden, isn’t it, [[diana-barry|Diana]]?” . . . and

[007-033] Anne sighed for sheer happiness. “The air has magic in it. Look at the

[007-034] purple in the cup of the harvest valley, Diana. And oh, do smell the

[007-035] dying fir! It’s coming up from that little sunny hollow where Mr. Eben

[007-036] Wright has been cutting fence poles. Bliss is it on such a day to be

[007-037] alive; but to smell dying fir is very heaven. That’s two thirds

[007-038] Wordsworth and one third Anne Shirley. It doesn’t seem possible that

[007-039] there should be dying fir in heaven, does it? And yet it doesn’t seem

[007-040] to me that heaven would be quite perfect if you couldn’t get a whiff of

[007-041] dead fir as you went through its woods. Perhaps we’ll have the odor

[007-042] there without the death. Yes, I think that will be the way. That

[007-043] delicious aroma must be the souls of the firs . . . and of course it

[007-044] will be just souls in heaven.”

[007-045] “Trees haven’t souls,” said practical Diana, “but the smell of dead fir

[007-046] is certainly lovely. I’m going to make a cushion and fill it with fir

[007-047] needles. You’d better make one too, Anne.”

[007-048] “I think I shall . . . and use it for my naps. I’d be certain to dream

[007-049] I was a dryad or a woodnymph then. But just this minute I’m well

[007-050] content to be Anne Shirley, [[avonlea|Avonlea]] schoolma’am, driving over a road

[007-051] like this on such a sweet, friendly day.”

[007-052] “It’s a lovely day but we have anything but a lovely task before us,”

[007-053] sighed Diana. “Why on earth did you offer to canvass this road, Anne?

[007-054] Almost all the cranks in Avonlea live along it, and we’ll probably be

[007-055] treated as if we were begging for ourselves. It’s the very worst road

[007-056] of all.”

[007-057] “That is why I chose it. Of course [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] and Fred would have taken

[007-058] this road if we had asked them. But you see, Diana, I feel myself

[007-059] responsible for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]], since I was the first to suggest it, and

[007-060] it seems to me that I ought to do the most disagreeable things. I’m

[007-061] sorry on your account; but you needn’t say a word at the cranky places.

[007-062] I’ll do all the talking . . . [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] would say I was well able to.

[007-063] [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] doesn’t know whether to approve of our enterprise or not.

[007-064] She inclines to, when she remembers that Mr. and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] are in

[007-065] favor of it; but the fact that village improvement societies first

[007-066] originated in the States is a count against it. So she is halting

[007-067] between two opinions and only success will justify us in Mrs. Lynde’s

[007-068] eyes. Priscilla is going to write a paper for our next Improvement

[007-069] meeting, and I expect it will be good, for her aunt is such a clever

[007-070] writer and no doubt it runs in the family. I shall never forget the

[007-071] thrill it gave me when I found out that Mrs. Charlotte E. Morgan was

[007-072] Priscilla’s aunt. It seemed so wonderful that I was a friend of the

[007-073] girl whose aunt wrote ‘Edgewood Days’ and ‘The Rosebud Garden.’”

[007-074] “Where does Mrs. Morgan live?”

[007-075] “In Toronto. And Priscilla says she is coming to the Island for a visit

[007-076] next summer, and if it is possible Priscilla is going to arrange to

[007-077] have us meet her. That seems almost too good to be true—but it’s

[007-078] something pleasant to imagine after you go to bed.”

[007-079] The Avonlea Village Improvement Society was an organized fact. Gilbert

[007-080] Blythe was president, [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] vice-president, Anne Shirley

[007-081] secretary, and Diana Barry treasurer. The “Improvers,” as they were

[007-082] promptly christened, were to meet once a fortnight at the homes of the

[007-083] members. It was admitted that they could not expect to affect many

[007-084] improvements so late in the season; but they meant to plan the next

[007-085] summer’s campaign, collect and discuss ideas, write and read papers,

[007-086] and, as Anne said, educate the public sentiment generally.

[007-087] There was some disapproval, of course, and . . . which the Improvers

[007-088] felt much more keenly . . . a good deal of ridicule. Mr. Elisha Wright

[007-089] was reported to have said that a more appropriate name for the

[007-090] organization would be Courting Club. Mrs. Hiram Sloane declared she had

[007-091] heard the Improvers meant to plough up all the roadsides and set them

[007-092] out with geraniums. Mr. Levi Boulter warned his [[community|neighbors]] that the

[007-093] Improvers would insist that everybody pull down his house and rebuild

[007-094] it after plans approved by the society. Mr. James Spencer sent them

[007-095] word that he wished they would kindly shovel down [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]] hill. Eben

[007-096] Wright told Anne that he wished the Improvers could induce old Josiah

[007-097] Sloane to keep his whiskers trimmed. Mr. Lawrence Bell said he would

[007-098] whitewash his barns if nothing else would please them but he would

[007-099] _not_ hang lace curtains in the cowstable windows. Mr. Major Spencer

[007-100] asked Clifton Sloane, an Improver who drove the milk to the [[carmody|Carmody]]

[007-101] cheese factory, if it was true that everybody would have to have his

[007-102] milk-stand hand-painted next summer and keep an embroidered centerpiece

[007-103] on it.

[007-104] In spite of . . . or perhaps, human [[nature-appreciation|nature]] being what it is, because of

[007-105] . . . this, the Society went gamely to work at the only improvement

[007-106] they could hope to bring about that fall. At the second meeting, in the

[007-107] Barry parlor, [[oliver-sloane|Oliver Sloane]] moved that they start a subscription to

[007-108] re-shingle and paint the hall; Julia Bell seconded it, with an uneasy

[007-109] feeling that she was doing something not exactly ladylike. Gilbert put

[007-110] the motion, it was carried unanimously, and Anne gravely recorded it in

[007-111] her minutes. The next thing was to appoint a committee, and [[gertie-pye|Gertie Pye]],

[007-112] determined not to let Julia Bell carry off all the laurels, boldly

[007-113] moved that Miss [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]] be chairman of said committee. This motion

[007-114] being also duly seconded and carried, Jane returned the compliment by

[007-115] appointing Gertie on the committee, along with Gilbert, Anne, Diana,

[007-116] and Fred Wright. The committee chose their routes in private conclave.

[007-117] Anne and Diana were told off for the [[newbridge|Newbridge]] road, Gilbert and Fred

[007-118] for the [[white-sands|White Sands]] road, and Jane and Gertie for the Carmody road.

[007-119] “Because,” explained Gilbert to Anne, as they walked [[belonging|home]] together

[007-120] through [[haunted-wood|the Haunted Wood]], “the Pyes all live along that road and they

[007-121] won’t give a cent unless one of themselves canvasses them.”

[007-122] The next Saturday Anne and Diana started out. They drove to the end of

[007-123] the road and canvassed homeward, calling first on the “Andrew girls.”

[007-124] “If Catherine is alone we may get something,” said Diana, “but if Eliza

[007-125] is there we won’t.”

[007-126] Eliza was there . . . very much so . . . and looked even grimmer than

[007-127] usual. Miss Eliza was one of those people who give you the impression

[007-128] that life is indeed a vale of tears, and that a smile, never to speak

[007-129] of a laugh, is a waste of nervous energy truly reprehensible. The

[007-130] Andrew girls had been “girls” for fifty odd years and seemed likely to

[007-131] remain girls to the end of their earthly pilgrimage. Catherine, it was

[007-132] said, had not entirely given up hope, but Eliza, who was born a

[007-133] pessimist, had never had any. They lived in a little brown house built

[007-134] in a sunny corner scooped out of Mark Andrew’s beech woods. Eliza

[007-135] complained that it was terrible hot in summer, but Catherine was wont

[007-136] to say it was lovely and warm in winter.

[007-137] Eliza was sewing patchwork, not because it was needed but simply as a

[007-138] protest against the frivolous lace Catherine was crocheting. Eliza

[007-139] listened with a frown and Catherine with a smile, as the girls

[007-140] explained their errand. To be sure, whenever Catherine caught Eliza’s

[007-141] eye she discarded the smile in guilty confusion; but it crept back the

[007-142] next moment.

[007-143] “If I had money to waste,” said Eliza grimly, “I’d burn it up and have

[007-144] the fun of seeing a blaze maybe; but I wouldn’t give it to that hall,

[007-145] not a cent. It’s no benefit to the settlement . . . just a place for

[007-146] young folks to meet and carry on when they’s better be home in their

[007-147] beds.”

[007-148] “Oh, Eliza, young folks must have some amusement,” protested Catherine.

[007-149] “I don’t see the necessity. _We_ didn’t gad about to halls and places

[007-150] when we were young, Catherine Andrews. This world is getting worse

[007-151] every day.”

[007-152] “I think it’s getting better,” said Catherine firmly.

[007-153] “_You_ think!” Miss Eliza’s voice expressed the utmost contempt. “It

[007-154] doesn’t signify what you _think_, Catherine Andrews. Facts is facts.”

[007-155] “Well, I always like to look on the bright side, Eliza.”

[007-156] “There isn’t any bright side.”

[007-157] “Oh, indeed there is,” cried Anne, who couldn’t endure such heresy in

[007-158] silence. “Why, there are ever so many bright sides, Miss Andrews. It’s

[007-159] really a beautiful world.”

[007-160] “You won’t have such a high opinion of it when you’ve lived as long in

[007-161] it as I have,” retorted Miss Eliza sourly, “and you won’t be so

[007-162] enthusiastic about improving it either. How is your mother, Diana? Dear

[007-163] me, but she has failed of late. She looks terrible run down. And how

[007-164] long is it before [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] expects to be stone blind, Anne?”

[007-165] “The doctor thinks her eyes will not get any worse if she is very

[007-166] careful,” faltered Anne.

[007-167] Eliza shook her head.

[007-168] “Doctors always talk like that just to keep people cheered up. I

[007-169] wouldn’t have much hope if I was her. It’s best to be prepared for the

[007-170] worst.”

[007-171] “But oughtn’t we be prepared for the best too?” pleaded Anne. “It’s

[007-172] just as likely to happen as the worst.”

[007-173] “Not in my experience, and I’ve fifty-seven years to set against your

[007-174] sixteen,” retorted Eliza. “Going, are you? Well, I hope this new

[007-175] society of yours will be able to keep Avonlea from running any further

[007-176] down hill but I haven’t much hope of it.”
