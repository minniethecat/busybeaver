# Romance

The varied experience of romantic love in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — explored through multiple couples and offering a nuanced vision of love as sometimes sudden, sometimes long-delayed, and always transforming those it touches.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, [[love|romance]] is treated [[humor|wit]]h Montgomery's characteristic blend of sentiment and clear-eyed observation. The novel presents [[love|romance]] not as a single experience but as a spectrum: the patient, decades-spanning [[love|devotion]] of [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] for [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]; the gradual, natural unfolding of [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]]'s feelings for [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]]; and the barely acknowledged stirrings between [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]. Across these variations, [[love|romance]] is consistently presented as a force that enriches life [[humor|wit]]hout defining it — characters are enlarged by love, not consumed by it.

The novel's closing chapters affirm that "there was more [[love|romance]] in the world than that which had been written down in books" (043-070), suggesting that lived love exceeds literary [[tradition|convention]] — a characteristic Montgomery touch that values genuine feeling over sentimental cliché.

## Examples in the Text

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Story (Chapters 31–41).** The novel's most fully realized [[love|romance]] is the reunion of [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] and [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] after a quarter-century estrangement caused by a petty quarrel. When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] inadvertently reveals [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s identity through [[paul-irving|Paul]]'s name, the "old [[love|romance]]" is resurrected (031-064). The story arc culminates in their wedding, which [[anne-shirley|Anne]] observes [[humor|wit]]h the reflection that it is "the very" essence of [[love|romance]] (042-008). This plotline argues that true love can survive decades of separation — and that second chances are among life's greatest gifts.

**[[diana-barry|Diana]] and [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] (Chapters 33–43).** [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]]'s [[love|romance]] [[humor|wit]]h [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] represents the more [[tradition|convention]]al path of young love. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] initially feels "very sad over it" (041-098), recognizing that [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s marriage will alter their friendship. Yet she comes to appreciate "the [[love|romance]] in the idea" of her friend's happiness (041-098). [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s struggle to articulate her feelings — and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s empathetic [[wisdom|understanding]] (043-118) — illustrates how [[love|romance]] reshapes even the deepest platonic bonds.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Awakening Awareness (Chapter 41).** Though [[anne-shirley|Anne]] herself is not the subject of a developed [[love|romance]] plot, the novel plants seeds of her future [[humor|wit]]h [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] finds in [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] "just the face for a hero of [[love|romance]]" (041-126), and the observation that [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s actual lover "had not looked the part" (041-130) hints at [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own dawning recognition that love may arrive in unexpected forms. The final chapter notes "[[love|romance]] was peeping at her around a corner" (041-176), preparing the reader for developments beyond the novel's scope.

**Romance and Dreams (Chapter 33).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s friend expresses the tension between "[[ambition|dreams]] and [[love|romance]]" and practical reality (033-066) — a theme that runs throughout. Montgomery does not dismiss romantic dreaming but gently suggests that lived love, while different from fantasy, is ultimately richer.

**The Wedding at [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] (Chapter 42).** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s wedding brings the [[love|romance]] plot to its culmination. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s exclamation — "Yes, this was [[love|romance]]" (042-008) — is both an observation and a benediction, affirming that the best love stories are those lived rather than merely imagined.

## Thematic Significance

**Romance as Enrichment, Not Completion.** Montgomery carefully avoids suggesting that romantic love is necessary for a woman's fulfillment. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is complete [[humor|wit]]hout it; [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] has built a rich, independent life during her years of [[loneliness|solitude]]. Romance adds to life; it does not constitute it.

**The [[patience|Patience]] of True [[love|Love]].** [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]'s quarter-century [[faith|fidelity]] to the memory of his quarrel [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] is the novel's strongest statement about love's [[patience|endurance]]. Romance in Montgomery's vision is not the hot impulse of youth but the steady flame that survives decades of silence.

**[[love|Love]] and [[friendship|Friendship]].** The novel blurs the line between romantic and platonic love, suggesting they exist on a continuum. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s love for [[diana-barry|Diana]], for [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], and (unacknowledged) for [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] all partake of the same fundamental capacity for deep connection that characterizes all her significant relationships.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Youthful Idealism vs. Mature [[love|Love]].** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s "[[ambition|dreams]] and [[love|romance]]" (033-066) represent the literary, idealized conception of love. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s lived experience — a real quarrel, real loneliness, real reunion — provides the corrective. The novel privileges experience over idealization.

**Conventional vs. Un[[tradition|convention]]al Romance.** [[diana-barry|Diana]] and Fred's courtship follows a predictable small-town pattern. [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] and Stephen's is extraordinary — a love deferred for decades. Montgomery validates both while clearly finding the latter more narratively compelling.
