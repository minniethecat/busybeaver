[008-001] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] got themselves thankfully out, and drove away as fast as

[008-002] the fat pony could go. As they rounded the curve below the beech wood a

[008-003] plump figure came speeding over Mr. Andrews’ pasture, waving to them

[008-004] excitedly. It was Catherine Andrews and she was so out of breath that

[008-005] she could hardly speak, but she thrust a couple of quarters into Anne’s

[008-006] hand.

[008-007] “That’s my contribution to painting the hall,” she gasped. “I’d like to

[008-008] give you a dollar but I don’t dare take more from my egg money for

[008-009] Eliza would find it out if I did. I’m real interested in your [[community|society]]

[008-010] and I believe you’re going to do a lot of good. I’m an optimist. I

[008-011] _have_ to be, living with Eliza. I must hurry back before she misses me

[008-012] . . . she thinks I’m feeding the hens. I hope you’ll have good luck

[008-013] canvassing, and don’t be cast down over what Eliza said. The world _is_

[008-014] getting better . . . it certainly is.”

[008-015] The next house was Daniel Blair’s.

[008-016] “Now, it all depends on whether his wife is [[belonging|home]] or not,” said Diana,

[008-017] as they jolted along a deep-rutted lane. “If she is we won’t get a

[008-018] cent. Everybody says Dan Blair doesn’t dare have his hair cut without

[008-019] asking her permission; and it’s certain she’s very close, to state it

[008-020] moderately. She says she has to be just before she’s generous. But Mrs.

[008-021] Lynde says she’s so much ‘before’ that [[generous|generosity]] never catches up with

[008-022] her at all.”

[008-023] Anne related their experience at the Blair place to [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] that

[008-024] evening.

[008-025] “We tied the horse and then rapped at the kitchen door. Nobody came but

[008-026] the door was open and we could hear somebody in the pantry, going on

[008-027] dreadfully. We couldn’t make out the words but Diana says she knows

[008-028] they were swearing by the sound of them. I can’t believe that of Mr.

[008-029] Blair, for he is always so quiet and meek; but at least he had great

[008-030] provocation, for Marilla, when that poor man came to the door, red as a

[008-031] beet, with perspiration streaming down his face, he had on one of his

[008-032] wife’s big gingham aprons. ‘I can’t get this durned thing off,’ he

[008-033] said, ‘for the strings are tied in a hard knot and I can’t bust ’em, so

[008-034] you’ll have to excuse me, ladies.’ We begged him not to mention it and

[008-035] went in and sat down. Mr. Blair sat down too; he twisted the apron

[008-036] around to his back and rolled it up, but he did look so ashamed and

[008-037] worried that I felt sorry for him, and Diana said she feared we had

[008-038] called at an inconvenient time. ‘Oh, not at all,’ said Mr. Blair,

[008-039] trying to smile . . . you know he is always very polite . . . ‘I’m a

[008-040] little busy . . . getting ready to bake a cake as it were. My wife got

[008-041] a telegram today that her sister from Montreal is coming tonight and

[008-042] she’s gone to the train to meet her and left orders for me to make a

[008-043] cake for tea. She writ out the recipe and told me what to do but I’ve

[008-044] clean forgot half the directions already. And it says, ‘flavor

[008-045] according to taste.’ What does that mean? How can you tell? And what if

[008-046] my taste doesn’t happen to be other people’s taste? Would a tablespoon

[008-047] of vanilla be enough for a small layer cake?”

[008-048] “I felt sorrier than ever for the poor man. He didn’t seem to be in his

[008-049] proper sphere at all. I had heard of henpecked husbands and now I felt

[008-050] that I saw one. It was on my lips to say, ‘Mr. Blair, if you’ll give us

[008-051] a subscription for the hall I’ll mix up your cake for you.’ But I

[008-052] suddenly thought it wouldn’t be neighborly to drive too sharp a bargain

[008-053] with a fellow creature in distress. So I offered to mix the cake for

[008-054] him without any conditions at all. He just jumped at my offer. He said

[008-055] he’d been used to making his own bread before he was married but he

[008-056] feared cake was beyond him, and yet he hated to disappoint his wife. He

[008-057] got me another apron, and Diana beat the eggs and I mixed the cake. Mr.

[008-058] Blair ran about and got us the materials. He had forgotten all about

[008-059] his apron and when he ran it streamed out behind him and Diana said she

[008-060] thought she would die to see it. He said he could bake the cake all

[008-061] right . . . he was used to that . . . and then he asked for our list

[008-062] and he put down four dollars. So you see we were rewarded. But even if

[008-063] he hadn’t given a cent I’d always feel that we had done a truly

[008-064] Christian act in helping him.”

[008-065] Theodore White’s was the next stopping place. Neither Anne nor Diana

[008-066] had ever been there before, and they had only a very slight

[008-067] acquaintance with Mrs. Theodore, who was not given to hospitality.

[008-068] Should they go to the back or front door? While they held a whispered

[008-069] consultation Mrs. Theodore appeared at the front door with an armful of

[008-070] newspapers. Deliberately she laid them down one by one on the porch

[008-071] floor and the porch steps, and then down the path to the very feet of

[008-072] her mystified callers.

[008-073] “Will you please wipe your feet carefully on the grass and then walk on

[008-074] these papers?” she said anxiously. “I’ve just swept the house all over

[008-075] and I can’t have any more dust tracked in. The path’s been real muddy

[008-076] since the rain yesterday.”

[008-077] “Don’t you dare laugh,” warned Anne in a whisper, as they marched along

[008-078] the newspapers. “And I implore you, Diana, not to look at me, no matter

[008-079] what she says, or I shall not be able to keep a sober face.”

[008-080] The papers extended across the hall and into a prim, fleckless parlor.

[008-081] Anne and Diana sat down gingerly on the nearest chairs and explained

[008-082] their errand. Mrs. White heard them politely, interrupting only twice,

[008-083] once to chase out an adventurous fly, and once to pick up a tiny wisp

[008-084] of grass that had fallen on the carpet from Anne’s dress. Anne felt

[008-085] wretchedly guilty; but Mrs. White subscribed two dollars and paid the

[008-086] money down . . . “to prevent us from having to go back for it,” Diana

[008-087] said when they got away. Mrs. White had the newspapers gathered up

[008-088] before they had their horse untied and as they drove out of the yard

[008-089] they saw her busily wielding a broom in the hall.

[008-090] “I’ve always heard that Mrs. Theodore White was the neatest woman alive

[008-091] and I’ll believe it after this,” said Diana, [[generosity|giving]] way to her

[008-092] suppressed [[humor|laughter]] as soon as it was safe.

[008-093] “I am glad she has no children,” said Anne solemnly. “It would be

[008-094] dreadful beyond words for them if she had.”

[008-095] At the Spencers’ Mrs. Isabella Spencer made them miserable by saying

[008-096] something ill-natured about everyone in [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. Mr. Thomas Boulter

[008-097] refused to give anything because the hall, when it had been built,

[008-098] twenty years before, hadn’t been built on the site he recommended. Mrs.

[008-099] Esther Bell, who was the picture of health, took half an hour to detail

[008-100] all her aches and pains, and sadly put down fifty cents because she

[008-101] wouldn’t be there that time next year to do it . . . no, she would be

[008-102] in her grave.

[008-103] Their worst reception, however, was at Simon Fletcher’s. When they

[008-104] drove into the yard they saw two faces peering at them through the

[008-105] porch window. But although they rapped and waited patiently and

[008-106] persistently nobody came to the door. Two decidedly ruffled and

[008-107] indignant girls drove away from Simon Fletcher’s. Even Anne admitted

[008-108] that she was beginning to feel discouraged. But the tide turned after

[008-109] that. Several Sloane homesteads came next, where they got liberal

[008-110] subscriptions, and from that to the end they fared well, with only an

[008-111] occasional snub. Their last place of call was at Robert Dickson’s by

[008-112] the pond bridge. They stayed to tea here, although they were nearly

[008-113] home, rather than risk offending Mrs. Dickson, who had the reputation

[008-114] of being a very “touchy” woman.

[008-115] While they were there old Mrs. James White called in.

[008-116] “I’ve just been down to Lorenzo’s,” she announced. “He’s the proudest

[008-117] man in Avonlea this minute. What do you think? There’s a brand new boy

[008-118] there . . . and after seven girls that’s quite an event, I can tell

[008-119] you.” Anne pricked up her ears, and when they drove away she said.

[008-120] “I’m going straight to [[lorenzo-white|Lorenzo White]]’s.”

[008-121] “But he lives on the [[white-sands|White Sands]] road and it’s quite a distance out of

[008-122] our way,” protested Diana. “[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] and Fred will canvass him.”

[008-123] “They are not going around until next Saturday and it will be too late

[008-124] by then,” said Anne firmly. “The novelty will be worn off. Lorenzo

[008-125] White is dreadfully mean but he will subscribe to _anything_ just now.

[008-126] We mustn’t let such a golden opportunity slip, Diana.” The result

[008-127] justified Anne’s foresight. Mr. White met them in the yard, beaming

[008-128] like the sun upon an Easter day. When Anne asked for a subscription he

[008-129] agreed enthusiastically.

[008-130] “Certain, certain. Just put me down for a dollar more than the highest

[008-131] subscription you’ve got.”

[008-132] “That will be five dollars . . . Mr. Daniel Blair put down four,” said

[008-133] Anne, half afraid. But Lorenzo did not flinch.

[008-134] “Five it is . . . and here’s the money on the spot. Now, I want you to

[008-135] come into the house. There’s something in there worth seeing . . .

[008-136] something very few people have seen as yet. Just come in and pass

[008-137] _your_ opinion.”

[008-138] “What will we say if the baby isn’t pretty?” whispered Diana in

[008-139] trepidation as they followed the excited Lorenzo into the house.

[008-140] “Oh, there will certainly be something else nice to say about it,” said

[008-141] Anne easily. “There always is about a baby.”

[008-142] The baby _was_ pretty, however, and Mr. White felt that he got his five

[008-143] dollars’ worth of the girls’ honest delight over the plump little

[008-144] newcomer. But that was the first, last, and only time that Lorenzo

[008-145] White ever subscribed to anything.

[008-146] Anne, tired as she was, made one more effort for the public weal that

[008-147] night, slipping over the fields to interview [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], who was as

[008-148] usual smoking his pipe on the veranda with Ginger beside him. Strickly

[008-149] speaking he was on the [[carmody|Carmody]] road; but [[jane-andrews|Jane]] and Gertie, who were not

[008-150] acquainted with him save by doubtful report, had nervously begged Anne

[008-151] to canvass him.

[008-152] Mr. Harrison, however, flatly refused to subscribe a cent, and all

[008-153] Anne’s wiles were in vain.

[008-154] “But I thought you approved of our society, Mr. Harrison,” she mourned.

[008-155] “So I do . . . so I do . . . but my approval doesn’t go as deep as my

[008-156] pocket, Anne.”

[008-157] “A few more experiences such as I have had today would make me as much

[008-158] of a pessimist as Miss Eliza Andrews,” Anne told her reflection in the

[008-159] east gable mirror at bedtime.

[008-160] VII

[008-161] The Pointing of [[duty|Duty]]

[008-162] Anne leaned back in her chair one mild October evening and sighed. She

[008-163] was sitting at a table covered with text books and exercises, but the

[008-164] closely written sheets of paper before her had no apparent connection

[008-165] with studies or school work.

[008-166] “What is the matter?” asked Gilbert, who had arrived at the open

[008-167] kitchen door just in time to hear the sigh.

[008-168] Anne colored, and thrust her writing out of sight under some school

[008-169] compositions.

[008-170] “Nothing very dreadful. I was just trying to write out some of my

[008-171] thoughts, as Professor Hamilton advised me, but I couldn’t get them to

[008-172] please me. They seem so still and foolish directly they’re written down

[008-173] on white paper with black ink. Fancies are like shadows . . . you can’t

[008-174] cage them, they’re such wayward, dancing things. But perhaps I’ll learn

[008-175] the secret some day if I keep on trying. I haven’t a great many spare

[008-176] moments, you know. By the time I finish correcting school exercises and

[008-177] compositions, I don’t always feel like writing any of my own.”
