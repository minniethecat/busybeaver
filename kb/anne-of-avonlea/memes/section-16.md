[016-001] “Joseph,” said [[anne-shirley|Anne]] quietly, “bring that parcel here.”

[016-002] Joe, startled and abashed, obeyed. He was a fat urchin who always

[016-003] blushed and stuttered when he was frightened. Never did anybody look

[016-004] more guilty than poor Joe at that moment.

[016-005] “Throw it into the fire,” said Anne.

[016-006] Joe looked very blank.

[016-007] “P . . . p . . . p . . . lease, m . . . m . . . miss,” he began.

[016-008] “Do as I tell you, Joseph, without any words about it.”

[016-009] “B . . . b . . . but m . . . m . . . miss . . . th . . . th . . .

[016-010] they’re . . .” gasped Joe in desperation.

[016-011] “Joseph, are you going to obey me or are you _not?_” said Anne.

[016-012] A bolder and more self-possessed lad than Joe Sloane would have been

[016-013] overawed by her tone and the dangerous flash of her eyes. This was a

[016-014] new Anne whom none of her pupils had ever seen before. Joe, with an

[016-015] agonized glance at St. Clair, went to the stove, opened the big, square

[016-016] front door, and threw the blue and white parcel in, before St. Clair,

[016-017] who had sprung to his feet, could utter a word. Then he dodged back

[016-018] just in time.

[016-019] For a few moments the terrified occupants of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] school did not

[016-020] know whether it was an earthquake or a volcanic explosion that had

[016-021] occurred. The innocent looking parcel which Anne had rashly supposed to

[016-022] contain Mrs. Hiram’s nut cakes really held an assortment of

[016-023] firecrackers and pinwheels for which Warren Sloane had sent to town by

[016-024] St. Clair Donnell’s father the day before, intending to have a birthday

[016-025] celebration that evening. The crackers went off in a thunderclap of

[016-026] noise and the pinwheels bursting out of the door spun madly around the

[016-027] room, hissing and spluttering. Anne dropped into her chair white with

[016-028] dismay and all the girls climbed shrieking upon their desks. Joe Sloane

[016-029] stood as one transfixed in the midst of the commotion and St. Clair,

[016-030] helpless with [[humor|laughter]], rocked to and fro in the aisle. Prillie

[016-031] Rogerson fainted and [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]] went into hysterics.

[016-032] It seemed a long time, although it was really only a few minutes,

[016-033] before the last pinwheel subsided. Anne, recovering herself, sprang to

[016-034] open doors and windows and let out the gas and smoke which filled the

[016-035] room. Then she helped the girls carry the unconscious Prillie into the

[016-036] porch, where [[barbara-shaw|Barbara Shaw]], in an agony of desire to be useful, poured a

[016-037] pailful of half frozen water over Prillie’s face and shoulders before

[016-038] anyone could stop her.

[016-039] It was a full hour before quiet was restored . . . but it was a quiet

[016-040] that might be felt. Everybody realized that even the explosion had not

[016-041] cleared the teacher’s mental atmosphere. Nobody, except [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]],

[016-042] dared whisper a word. Ned Clay accidentally squeaked his pencil while

[016-043] working a sum, caught Anne’s eye and wished the floor would open and

[016-044] swallow him up. The geography class were whisked through a continent

[016-045] with a speed that made them dizzy. The grammar class were parsed and

[016-046] analyzed within an inch of their lives. Chester Sloane, spelling

[016-047] “odoriferous” with two f’s, was made to feel that he could never live

[016-048] down the disgrace of it, either in this world or that which is to come.

[016-049] Anne knew that she had made herself ridiculous and that the incident

[016-050] would be laughed over that night at a score of tea-tables, but the

[016-051] knowledge only angered her further. In a calmer mood she could have

[016-052] carried off the situation with a laugh but now that was impossible; so

[016-053] she ignored it in icy disdain.

[016-054] When Anne returned to the school after dinner all the children were as

[016-055] usual in their seats and every face was bent studiously over a desk

[016-056] except Anthony Pye’s. He peered across his book at Anne, his black eyes

[016-057] sparkling with curiosity and mockery. Anne twitched open the drawer of

[016-058] her desk in search of chalk and under her very hand a lively mouse

[016-059] sprang out of the drawer, scampered over the desk, and leaped to the

[016-060] floor.

[016-061] Anne screamed and sprang back, as if it had been a snake, and Anthony

[016-062] Pye laughed aloud.

[016-063] Then a silence fell . . . a very creepy, uncomfortable silence. Annetta

[016-064] Bell was of two minds whether to go into hysterics again or not,

[016-065] especially as she didn’t know just where the mouse had gone. But she

[016-066] decided not to. Who could take any comfort out of hysterics with a

[016-067] teacher so white-faced and so blazing-eyed standing before one?

[016-068] “Who put that mouse in my desk?” said Anne. Her voice was quite low but

[016-069] it made a shiver go up and down [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]’s spine. Joe Sloane caught

[016-070] her eye, felt responsible from the crown of his head to the sole of his

[016-071] feet, but stuttered out wildly,

[016-072] “N . . . n . . . not m . . . m . . . me t . . . t . . . teacher, n . .

[016-073] . n . . . not m . . . m . . . me.”

[016-074] Anne paid no attention to the wretched Joseph. She looked at Anthony

[016-075] Pye, and Anthony Pye looked back unabashed and unashamed.

[016-076] “Anthony, was it you?”

[016-077] “Yes, it was,” said Anthony insolently.

[016-078] Anne took her pointer from her desk. It was a long, heavy hardwood

[016-079] pointer.

[016-080] “Come here, Anthony.”

[016-081] It was far from being the most severe punishment Anthony Pye had ever

[016-082] undergone. Anne, even the stormy-souled Anne she was at that moment,

[016-083] could not have punished any child cruelly. But the pointer nipped

[016-084] keenly and finally Anthony’s bravado failed him; he winced and the

[016-085] tears came to his eyes.

[016-086] Anne, conscience-stricken, dropped the pointer and told Anthony to go

[016-087] to his seat. She sat down at her desk feeling ashamed, repentant, and

[016-088] bitterly mortified. Her quick anger was gone and she would have given

[016-089] much to have been able to seek relief in tears. So all her boasts had

[016-090] come to this . . . she had actually whipped one of her pupils. How [[jane-andrews|Jane]]

[016-091] would triumph! And how [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] would chuckle! But worse than this,

[016-092] bitterest thought of all, she had lost her last chance of winning

[016-093] Anthony Pye. Never would he like her now.

[016-094] Anne, by what somebody has called “a Herculaneum effort,” kept back her

[016-095] tears until she got [[belonging|home]] that night. Then she shut herself in the east

[016-096] gable room and wept all her shame and [[regret|remorse]] and disappointment into

[016-097] her pillows . . . wept so long that [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] grew alarmed, invaded the

[016-098] room, and insisted on knowing what the trouble was.

[016-099] “The trouble is, I’ve got things the matter with my conscience,” sobbed

[016-100] Anne. “Oh, this has been such a Jonah day, Marilla. I’m so ashamed of

[016-101] myself. I lost my temper and whipped Anthony Pye.”

[016-102] “I’m glad to hear it,” said Marilla with decision. “It’s what you

[016-103] should have done long ago.”

[016-104] “Oh, no, no, Marilla. And I don’t see how I can ever look those

[016-105] children in the face again. I feel that I have humiliated myself to the

[016-106] very dust. You don’t know how cross and hateful and horrid I was. I

[016-107] can’t forget the expression in Paul Irving’s eyes . . . he looked so

[016-108] surprised and disappointed. Oh, Marilla, I _have_ tried so hard to be

[016-109] patient and to win Anthony’s liking . . . and now it has all gone for

[016-110] nothing.”

[016-111] Marilla passed her hard work-worn hand over the girl’s glossy, tumbled

[016-112] hair with a wonderful tenderness. When Anne’s sobs grew quieter she

[016-113] said, very gently for her,

[016-114] “You take things too much to heart, Anne. We all make mistakes . . .

[016-115] but people forget them. And Jonah days come to everybody. As for

[016-116] Anthony Pye, why need you care if he does dislike you? He is the only

[016-117] one.”

[016-118] “I can’t help it. I want everybody to [[romance|love]] me and it hurts me so when

[016-119] anybody doesn’t. And Anthony never will now. Oh, I just made an idiot

[016-120] of myself today, Marilla. I’ll tell you the whole story.”

[016-121] Marilla listened to the whole story, and if she smiled at certain parts

[016-122] of it Anne never knew. When the tale was ended she said briskly,

[016-123] “Well, never mind. This day’s done and there’s a new one coming

[016-124] tomorrow, with no mistakes in it yet, as you used to say yourself. Just

[016-125] come downstairs and have your supper. You’ll see if a good cup of tea

[016-126] and those plum puffs I made today won’t hearten you up.”

[016-127] “Plum puffs won’t minister to a mind diseased,” said Anne

[016-128] disconsolately; but Marilla thought it a good sign that she had

[016-129] recovered sufficiently to adapt a quotation.

[016-130] The cheerful supper table, with the twins’ bright faces, and Marilla’s

[016-131] matchless plum puffs . . . of which [[davy-keith|Davy]] ate four . . . did “hearten

[016-132] her up” considerably after all. She had a good sleep that night and

[016-133] awakened in the morning to find herself and the world transformed. It

[016-134] had snowed softly and thickly all through the hours of darkness and the

[016-135] beautiful whiteness, glittering in the frosty sunshine, looked like a

[016-136] mantle of charity cast over all the mistakes and humiliations of the

[016-137] past.

[016-138] “Every morn is a fresh beginning,

[016-139] Every morn is the world made new,”

[016-140] sang Anne, as she dressed.

[016-141] Owing to the snow she had to go around by the road to school and she

[016-142] thought it was certainly an impish coincidence that Anthony Pye should

[016-143] come ploughing along just as she left the [[green-gables|Green Gables]] lane. She felt

[016-144] as guilty as if their positions were reversed; but to her unspeakable

[016-145] astonishment Anthony not only lifted his cap . . . which he had never

[016-146] done before . . . but said easily,

[016-147] “Kind of bad walking, ain’t it? Can I take those books for you,

[016-148] teacher?”

[016-149] Anne surrendered her books and wondered if she could possibly be awake.

[016-150] Anthony walked on in silence to the school, but when Anne took her

[016-151] books she smiled down at him . . . not the stereotyped “kind” smile she

[016-152] had so persistently assumed for his benefit but a sudden outflashing of

[016-153] good comradeship. Anthony smiled . . . no, if the truth must be told,

[016-154] Anthony _grinned_ back. A grin is not generally supposed to be a

[016-155] respectful thing; yet Anne suddenly felt that if she had not yet won

[016-156] Anthony’s liking she had, somehow or other, won his respect.

[016-157] [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]] came up the next Saturday and confirmed this.

[016-158] “Well, Anne, I guess you’ve won over Anthony Pye, that’s what. He says

[016-159] he believes you are some good after all, even if you are a girl. Says

[016-160] that whipping you gave him was ‘just as good as a man’s.’”

[016-161] “I never expected to win him by whipping him, though,” said Anne, a

[016-162] little mournfully, feeling that her ideals had played her false

[016-163] somewhere. “It doesn’t seem right. I’m _sure_ my theory of kindness

[016-164] _can’t_ be wrong.”

[016-165] “No, but the Pyes are an exception to every known rule, that’s what,”

[016-166] declared Mrs. Rachel with conviction.

[016-167] Mr. Harrison said, “Thought you’d come to it,” when he heard it, and

[016-168] Jane rubbed it in rather unmercifully.

[016-169] XIII

[016-170] A Golden Picnic

[016-171] Anne, on her way to [[orchard-slope|Orchard Slope]], met [[diana-barry|Diana]], bound for Green Gables,

[016-172] just where the mossy old log bridge spanned the brook below the Haunted

[016-173] Wood, and they sat down by the margin of the Dryad’s Bubble, where tiny

[016-174] ferns were unrolling like curly-headed green pixy folk wakening up from

[016-175] a nap.

[016-176] “I was just on my way over to invite you to help me celebrate my

[016-177] birthday on Saturday,” said Anne.

[016-178] “Your birthday? But your birthday was in March!”

[016-179] “That wasn’t my fault,” laughed Anne. “If my parents had consulted me

[016-180] it would never have happened then. I should have chosen to be born in

[016-181] spring, of course. It must be delightful to come into the world with

[016-182] the mayflowers and violets. You would always feel that you were their

[016-183] foster sister. But since I didn’t, the next best thing is to celebrate

[016-184] my birthday in the spring. Priscilla is coming over Saturday and Jane

[016-185] will be home. We’ll all four start off to the woods and spend a golden

[016-186] day making the acquaintance of the spring. We none of us really know

[016-187] her yet, but we’ll meet her back there as we never can anywhere else. I

[016-188] want to explore all those fields and lonely places anyhow. I have a

[016-189] conviction that there are scores of beautiful nooks there that have

[016-190] never really been _seen_ although they may have been _looked_ at. We’ll

[016-191] make friends with wind and sky and sun, and bring home the spring in

[016-192] our hearts.”
