# Lauretta White

**Lauretta White** is a plump, placid-looking child of eight from [[white-sands|White Sands]], whose claim to narrative fame in _[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]_ is being the victim of one of [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]]'s most notorious misadventures: a caterpillar dropped down her neck during [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]]'s prayer.

## Character Overview

Montgomery introduces Lauretta White in a single scene — a church service — but sketches her [[humor|wit]]h characteristic economy and precision. She is described as "a fat, placid-looking child of eight, who had conducted herself irreproachably in church from the very first day her mother carried her there, an infant of six months" [010-137–139]. This detail establishes Lauretta's [[nature-appreciation|nature]] — quiet, well-behaved, entirely undeserving of the calamity about to befall her — and thereby heightens the comic horror of what follows.

## Role in Story

Lauretta's function in the narrative is as the un[[humor|wit]]ting foil to [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]]'s irrepressible mischief. Her perfect church-attendance record makes the disruption all the more shocking; the congregation's sympathy is entirely hers, and [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s transgression is rendered all the more vivid by contrast [[humor|wit]]h her innocence.

## Key Scenes

### The Caterpillar Incident

> [010-134] Lauretta White was sitting in front of Davy, her head slightly bent and her fair hair hanging in two long braids, between which a tempting expanse of white neck showed, encased in a loose lace frill.

During [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]]'s prayer, [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]] produces a "furry, squirming caterpillar" from his pocket and drops it down Lauretta's neck [010-140–142]. The resulting chaos — Lauretta "dancing up and down in her pew, clutching frantically at the back of her dress" [010-145–146] — brings the service to a halt. Her shrieks of "Ow . . . mommer . . . mommer . . . ow . . . take it off" [010-147–150] are rendered in Montgomery's characteristic phonetic transcription of childish distress, capturing both the [[laughter|humor]] and the genuine discomfort of the moment.

> [010-151] Mrs. White rose and with a set face carried the hysterical, writhing Lauretta out of church.

Mrs. White's dignified extraction of her daughter — "[[humor|wit]]h a set face" — is a small masterstroke of characterization: in four words, Montgomery conveys maternal outrage, public embarrassment, and implacable purpose [010-151]. The scene's aftermath leaves the service irreparably damaged — "everybody felt that it was a failure that day" — and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "[[humor|wit]]h scarlet cheeks of mortification" [010-154–155], bearing the reflected shame of her young charge's behavior.

## Relationships

Lauretta's only significant relationship in the narrative is [[humor|wit]]h [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], whose caterpillar prank makes her the center of the novel's most memorable church-disruption scene [010-134–155]. Her mother, Mrs. White, appears only as the dignified agent of Lauretta's extraction from the sanctuary [010-151].

## Character Analysis

Lauretta White embodies a specific type in Montgomery's child-character repertoire: the well-behaved child who suffers for another's mischief. Her "irreproachable" church record [010-138] is a detail that serves both [[humor|comedy]] and pathos — the injustice of her ordeal is part of what makes the scene funny, and part of what makes [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s transgression genuinely shocking. Like [[minnie-may-barry|Minnie May Barry]], Lauretta is one of the younger-generation children whose encounters [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s world illuminate the moral and social textures of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] life.
