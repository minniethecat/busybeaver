# Joy

The emotional keynote of [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s character and, by extension, of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* itself. Joy in Montgomery's novel is not simply a pleasant feeling but a way of being in the world — an openness to [[beauty]], to [[friendship]], and to the "little joy or happy thought" (009-076) that sustains a life through difficulty.

## Definition

Montgomery distinguishes joy from mere pleasure or amusement. Joy is the deeper capacity to find happiness in ordinary experience — a sunset, a conversation, the "rose of joy" that blooms in shared understanding (021-023). It is both a temperamental gift ([[anne-shirley|Anne]] possesses it naturally) and a moral practice (she cultivates it deliberately).

The novel also acknowledges joy's fragility. The landscape can be "mourning for its old-time joys" (003-013), and joy must be protected against the forces that would diminish it — [[gossip]], [[despair]], and the grinding routines of daily life. Joy is resilient but not invulnerable; it requires tending.

## Examples in the Text

**The Joy of Youth and Life (Chapter 7).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] sighs "for sheer happiness" and declares: "The air has magic in it. Look at the joy of youth and life in that tree" (007-031, 007-033). Joy here is not internal but everywhere — in the air, in trees, in youth itself. Montgomery's [[nature-appreciation|nature writing]] consistently frames the natural world as a source and mirror of joy.

**A Little Joy or Happy Thought (Chapter 9).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching philosophy includes the aspiration "to have some little joy or happy thought" (009-076) that she can share with her pupils. Joy is not a private luxury but a communal resource — something to be spread rather than hoarded. This is joy as vocation.

**The Rose of Joy (Chapter 21).** In conversation with [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] experiences a moment of profound connection: "Both knew the way to that happy land. There the rose of joy bloomed" (021-023). The image of joy as a rose — beautiful, living, requiring cultivation — is characteristic of Montgomery's poetic treatment of the theme.

**Mourning for Old-Time Joys (Chapter 3).** The old house can "dream it is young and joyous again" (003-020–021), but it is also "mourning for its old-time joys" (003-013). Joy has a temporal dimension — it can be lost, remembered, and mourned. The passage introduces a note of elegy that gives depth to the novel's otherwise buoyant emotional register.

## Thematic Significance

**Joy as Resilience.** Montgomery presents joy not as the absence of sorrow but as the capacity to persist through it. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s joy coexists with her [[loneliness]], her struggles as a teacher, her anxieties about the future. Joy does not eliminate difficulty; it makes difficulty bearable.

**Joy and [[beauty]].** The two are inseparable in Montgomery's philosophy. Joy arises from the perception of [[beauty]] — in landscape, in character, in acts of [[kindness]]. To cultivate joy is to cultivate attention to [[beauty]].

**Joy as Generosity.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s joy is not self-enclosed; it spills over into her teaching (009-076), her [[friendship|friendships]], her daily interactions. Joy that remains private is incomplete; true joy "beautifies" not just the self but the [[community]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Anne's Exuberance vs. Marilla's Reserve.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] expresses joy openly and extravagantly; [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] feels it quietly. Montgomery validates both modes — [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] is a "thankful woman" (010-043) whose quiet satisfaction is as real as Anne's effusions. Joy has many dialects.

**Joy vs. Contentment.** The novel distinguishes between the active, creative quality of joy and the quieter state of contentment represented by characters like [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]]. Both are valid, but joy — with its forward momentum and generative energy — is the quality that drives the novel's plot and transforms its world.
