[033-001] “And what went wrong?” asked [[anne-shirley|Anne]] breathlessly.

[033-002] “We had just a stupid, silly, commonplace quarrel. So commonplace that,

[033-003] if you’ll believe me, I don’t even remember just how it began. I hardly

[033-004] know who was the more to blame for it. Stephen did really begin it, but

[033-005] I suppose I provoked him by some foolishness of mine. He had a rival or

[033-006] two, you see. I was vain and coquettish and liked to tease him a

[033-007] little. He was a very high-strung, sensitive fellow. Well, we parted in

[033-008] a temper on both sides. But I thought it would all come right; and it

[033-009] would have if Stephen hadn’t come back too soon. Anne, my dear, I’m

[033-010] sorry to say” . . . [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] dropped her voice as if she were

[033-011] about to confess a predilection for murdering people, “that I am a

[033-012] dreadfully sulky person. Oh, you needn’t smile, . . . it’s only too

[033-013] true. I _do_ sulk; and Stephen came back before I had finished sulking.

[033-014] I wouldn’t listen to him and I wouldn’t forgive him; and so he went

[033-015] away for good. He was too proud to come again. And then I sulked

[033-016] because he didn’t come. I might have sent for him perhaps, but I

[033-017] couldn’t humble myself to do that. I was just as proud as he was . . .

[033-018] pride and sulkiness make a very bad combination, Anne. But I could

[033-019] never care for anybody else and I didn’t want to. I knew I would rather

[033-020] be an old maid for a thousand years than marry anybody who wasn’t

[033-021] [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]]. Well, it all seems like a dream now, of course. How

[033-022] sympathetic you look, Anne . . . as sympathetic as only seventeen can

[033-023] look. But don’t overdo it. I’m really a very happy, contented little

[033-024] person in spite of my broken heart. My heart did break, if ever a heart

[033-025] did, when I realized that Stephen Irving was not coming back. But,

[033-026] Anne, a broken heart in real life isn’t half as dreadful as it is in

[033-027] books. It’s a good deal like a bad tooth . . . though you won’t think

[033-028] _that_ a very romantic simile. It takes spells of aching and gives you

[033-029] a sleepless night now and then, but between times it lets you enjoy

[033-030] life and [[ambition|dreams]] and echoes and peanut candy as if there were nothing

[033-031] the matter with it. And now you’re looking disappointed. You don’t

[033-032] think I’m half as interesting a person as you did five minutes ago when

[033-033] you believed I was always the prey of a tragic memory bravely hidden

[033-034] beneath external smiles. That’s the worst . . . or the best . . . of

[033-035] real life, Anne. It _won’t_ let you be miserable. It keeps on trying to

[033-036] make you comfortable . . . and succeeding...even when you’re determined

[033-037] to be unhappy and romantic. Isn’t this candy scrumptious? I’ve eaten

[033-038] far more than is good for me already but I’m going to keep recklessly

[033-039] on.”

[033-040] After a little silence [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] said abruptly,

[033-041] “It gave me a shock to hear about Stephen’s son that first day you were

[033-042] here, Anne. I’ve never been able to mention him to you since, but I’ve

[033-043] wanted to know all about him. What sort of a boy is he?”

[033-044] “He is the dearest, sweetest child I ever knew, Miss Lavendar . . . and

[033-045] he pretends things too, just as you and I do.”

[033-046] “I’d like to see him,” said Miss Lavendar softly, as if talking to

[033-047] herself. “I wonder if he looks anything like the little dream-boy who

[033-048] lives here with me . . . _my_ little dream-boy.”

[033-049] “If you would like to see [[paul-irving|Paul]] I’ll bring him through with me

[033-050] sometime,” said Anne.

[033-051] “I _would_ like it . . . but not too soon. I want to get used to the

[033-052] thought. There might be more pain than pleasure in it . . . if he

[033-053] looked too much like Stephen . . . or if he didn’t look enough like

[033-054] him. In a month’s time you may bring him.”

[033-055] Accordingly, a month later Anne and Paul walked through the woods to

[033-056] the stone house, and met Miss Lavendar in the lane. She had not been

[033-057] expecting them just then and she turned very pale.

[033-058] “So this is Stephen’s boy,” she said in a low tone, taking Paul’s hand

[033-059] and looking at him as he stood, beautiful and boyish, in his smart

[033-060] little fur coat and cap. “He . . . he is very like his father.”

[033-061] “Everybody says I’m a chip off the old block,” remarked Paul, quite at

[033-062] his ease.

[033-063] Anne, who had been watching the little scene, drew a relieved breath.

[033-064] She saw that Miss Lavendar and Paul had “taken” to each other, and that

[033-065] there would be no constraint or stiffness. Miss Lavendar was a very

[033-066] sensible person, in spite of her dreams and [[love|romance]], and after that

[033-067] first little betrayal she tucked her feelings out of sight and

[033-068] entertained Paul as brightly and naturally as if he were anybody’s son

[033-069] who had come to see her. They all had a jolly afternoon together and

[033-070] such a feast of fat things by way of supper as would have made old Mrs.

[033-071] Irving hold up her hands in horror, believing that Paul’s digestion

[033-072] would be ruined for ever.

[033-073] “Come again, laddie,” said Miss Lavendar, shaking hands with him at

[033-074] parting.

[033-075] “You may kiss me if you like,” said Paul gravely.

[033-076] Miss Lavendar stooped and kissed him.

[033-077] “How did you know I wanted to?” she whispered.

[033-078] “Because you looked at me just as my little mother used to do when she

[033-079] wanted to kiss me. As a rule, I don’t like to be kissed. Boys don’t.

[033-080] _You_ know, Miss Lewis. But I think I rather like to have you kiss me.

[033-081] And of course I’ll come to see you again. I think I’d like to have you

[033-082] for a particular friend of mine, if you don’t object.”

[033-083] “I . . . I don’t think I shall object,” said Miss Lavendar. She turned

[033-084] and went in very quickly; but a moment later she was waving a gay and

[033-085] smiling good-bye to them from the window.

[033-086] “I like Miss Lavendar,” announced Paul, as they walked through the

[033-087] beech woods. “I like the way she looked at me, and I like her stone

[033-088] house, and I like [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]]. I wish Grandma Irving had a

[033-089] Charlotta the Fourth instead of a [[mary-joe|Mary Joe]]. I feel sure Charlotta the

[033-090] Fourth wouldn’t think I was wrong in my upper story when I told her

[033-091] what I think about things. Wasn’t that a splendid tea we had, teacher?

[033-092] Grandma says a boy shouldn’t be thinking about what he gets to eat, but

[033-093] he can’t help it sometimes when he is real hungry. _You_ know, teacher.

[033-094] I don’t think Miss Lavendar would make a boy eat porridge for breakfast

[033-095] if he didn’t like it. She’d get things for him he did like. But of

[033-096] course” . . . Paul was nothing if not fair-minded . . . “that mightn’t

[033-097] be very good for him. It’s very nice for a change though, teacher.

[033-098] _You_ know.”

[033-099] XXIV

[033-100] A Prophet in His Own Country

[033-101] One May day [[avonlea|Avonlea]] folks were mildly excited over some “Avonlea

[033-102] Notes,” signed “Observer,” which appeared in the [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] _Daily

[033-103] Enterprise_. [[gossip|Gossip]] ascribed the authorship thereof to [[charlie-sloane|Charlie Sloane]],

[033-104] partly because the said Charlie had indulged in similar literary

[033-105] flights in times past, and partly because one of the notes seemed to

[033-106] embody a sneer at [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]]. Avonlea juvenile [[community|society]] persisted in

[033-107] regarding Gilbert Blythe and Charlie Sloane as rivals in the good

[033-108] graces of a certain damsel with gray eyes and an imagination.

[033-109] Gossip, as usual, was wrong. Gilbert Blythe, aided and abetted by Anne,

[033-110] had written the notes, putting in the one about himself as a blind.

[033-111] Only two of the notes have any bearing on this history:

[033-112] “Rumor has it that there will be a wedding in our village ere the

[033-113] daisies are in bloom. A new and highly respected citizen will lead to

[033-114] the hymeneal altar one of our most popular ladies.

[033-115] “[[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe]], our well-known weather prophet, predicts a violent storm of

[033-116] thunder and lightning for the evening of the twenty-third of May,

[033-117] beginning at seven o’clock sharp. The area of the storm will extend

[033-118] over the greater part of the Province. People traveling that evening

[033-119] will do well to take umbrellas and mackintoshes with them.”

[033-120] “Uncle Abe really has predicted a storm for sometime this spring,” said

[033-121] Gilbert, “but do you suppose [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] really does go to see

[033-122] Isabella Andrews?”

[033-123] “No,” said Anne, laughing, “I’m sure he only goes to play checkers with

[033-124] Mr. Harrison Andrews, but [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says she knows Isabella Andrews

[033-125] must be going to get married, she’s in such good spirits this spring.”

[033-126] Poor old Uncle Abe felt rather indignant over the notes. He suspected

[033-127] that “Observer” was making fun of him. He angrily denied having

[033-128] assigned any particular date for his storm but nobody believed him.

[033-129] Life in Avonlea continued on the smooth and even tenor of its way. The

[033-130] “planting” was put in; the Improvers celebrated an Arbor Day. Each

[033-131] Improver set out, or caused to be set out, five ornamental trees. As

[033-132] the society now numbered forty members, this meant a total of two

[033-133] hundred young trees. Early oats greened over the red fields; apple

[033-134] orchards flung great blossoming arms about the farmhouses and the Snow

[033-135] Queen adorned itself as a bride for her husband. Anne liked to sleep

[033-136] with her window open and let the cherry fragrance blow over her face

[033-137] all night. She thought it very poetical. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] thought she was

[033-138] risking her life.

[033-139] “Thanksgiving should be celebrated in the spring,” said Anne one

[033-140] evening to Marilla, as they sat on the front door steps and listened to

[033-141] the silver-sweet chorus of the frogs. “I think it would be ever so much

[033-142] better than having it in November when everything is dead or asleep.

[033-143] Then you have to remember to be thankful; but in May one simply can’t

[033-144] help being thankful . . . that they are alive, if for nothing else. I

[033-145] feel exactly as Eve must have felt in the garden of Eden before the

[033-146] trouble began. _Is_ that grass in the hollow green or golden? It seems

[033-147] to me, Marilla, that a pearl of a day like this, when the blossoms are

[033-148] out and the winds don’t know where to blow from next for sheer crazy

[033-149] delight must be pretty near as good as heaven.”

[033-150] Marilla looked scandalized and glanced apprehensively around to make

[033-151] sure the twins were not within earshot. They came around the corner of

[033-152] the house just then.

[033-153] “Ain’t it an awful nice-smelling evening?” asked [[davy-keith|Davy]], sniffing

[033-154] delightedly as he swung a hoe in his grimy hands. He had been working

[033-155] in his garden. That spring Marilla, by way of turning Davy’s passion

[033-156] for reveling in mud and clay into useful channels, had given him and

[033-157] [[dora-keith|Dora]] a small plot of ground for a garden. Both had eagerly gone to work

[033-158] in a characteristic fashion. Dora planted, weeded, and watered

[033-159] carefully, systematically, and dispassionately. As a result, her plot

[033-160] was already green with prim, orderly little rows of vegetables and

[033-161] annuals. Davy, however, worked with more zeal than discretion; he dug

[033-162] and hoed and raked and watered and transplanted so energetically that

[033-163] his seeds had no chance for their lives.

[033-164] “How is your garden coming on, Davy-boy?” asked Anne.

[033-165] “Kind of slow,” said Davy with a sigh. “I don’t know why the things

[033-166] don’t grow better. Milty Boulter says I must have planted them in the

[033-167] dark of the moon and that’s the whole trouble. He says you must never

[033-168] sow seeds or kill pork or cut your hair or do any ‘portant thing in the

[033-169] wrong time of the moon. Is that true, Anne? I want to know.”
