# Faith

The steady, quiet virtue of trust in others and in life's ultimate goodness — "great faith, not altogether unjustified" (011-028) — that sustains characters through difficulty and binds the [[neighbor|community]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] together.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, faith operates on multiple levels. There is the practical faith of trust between individuals: [[diana-barry|Diana]] the faithful friend who "searched faithfully but no axe was to be found" (025-082). There is the deeper faith in one's own character and calling: enduring "as long as she was faithful to" (027-118) her principles. And there is the communal faith that "she had worked earnestly and faithfully, making many" (041-083) contributions to the life of the village.

Montgomery presents faith not as religious doctrine but as a quality of character — the capacity to remain steadfast in trust and commitment when circumstances test that trust. It is the foundation upon which the novel's deepest relationships are built.

## Examples in the Text

**[[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]'s Faith in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] (Chapter 11).** [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] [[belonging|place]]s "great faith, not altogether unjustified" (011-028) in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s potential. This faith — an adult's belief in a young person's future — is presented as one of the most valuable gifts one character can give another. It is faith that calls forth the best in its recipient.

**[[diana-barry|Diana]]'s Faithful [[friendship|Friendship]] (Chapter 25).** When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] needs help, "[[diana-barry|Diana]] the faithful" (025-136) is always there. [[diana-barry|Diana]] "searched faithfully" (025-082) for the missing axe and "had a pencil and discovered" (025-136) solutions to problems. Her faithfulness is the steady, reliable quality that makes the friendship endure across years and circumstances.

**Faithfulness to Principle (Chapter 27).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] resolves to endure "as long as she was faithful to" (027-118) her commitments. Here faith is aligned [[humor|wit]]h integrity — the determination to be true to one's word and values regardless of cost.

**A Life of Faithful Service (Chapter 41).** Looking back, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is described as having "worked earnestly and faithfully, making many" (041-083) contributions. Faithfulness over time — consistent, diligent, unglamorous — is the substance of a well-lived life in Montgomery's vision.

## Thematic Significance

**Faith as the Foundation of [[community|Community]].** The novel's [[neighbor|community]] depends on faith — [[community|neighbors]] trusting [[community|neighbors]], teachers believing in pupils, friends remaining loyal across distance and time. Without this web of mutual faith, [[avonlea|Avonlea]] would unravel into isolated individuals.

**Faith and Action.** Montgomery's faith is active, not passive. To have faith in someone is to invest in them — to give them responsibilities, to expect good things from them, to stand by them when they falter. Faith in this sense is a verb, not a noun.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**The Pessimist's Lack of Faith.** Characters like Eliza, described as "born a pessimist" (007-132), represent the absence of faith — the assumption that things will go wrong and people will disappoint. Their worldview is presented as a genuine [[death|loss]] rather than merely a different opinion.

**Faith Tested by [[diana-barry|Di]]sappointment.** [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] tests [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s faith in her own teaching ability. The tension between her faith in [[teaching|education]]'s transformative power and the reality of a recalcitrant pupil is one of the novel's central dramas — and her eventual vindication confirms that faith, properly tested, emerges stronger.
