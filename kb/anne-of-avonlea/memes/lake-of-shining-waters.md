# Lake of Shining Waters

The **Lake of Shining Waters** is a small lake near [[avonlea|Avonlea]] on [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]], situated below Robert [[diana-barry|Di]]ckson's beech wood along the shore road. Named by [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]], it embodies the central tension of Montgomery's landscape imagination — and a quiet joke at the expense of the unimaginative.

## Character Overview

> [020-005] above the Lake of Shining Waters . . . known to unimaginative people as…

The Lake of Shining Waters is one of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s earliest acts of re-naming: what the prosaic world calls "Barry's Pond," [[anne-shirley|Anne]] transforms into something luminous [020-005]. The parenthetical explanation — "known to unimaginative people as Barry's Pond" — is a single line that encapsulates Montgomery's entire aesthetic: the world is what you name it, and the choice between "Lake of Shining Waters" and "Barry's Pond" is the choice between poetry and prose.

## Key References

### Anne's Footpath

> [020-001–005] Anne had been down to old Mrs. Irving's on the shore road the preceding evening and had come home by a short cut which led her first over the low-lying shore fields, and then through the beech wood below Robert Dickson's, by a little footpath that ran out to the main road just above the Lake of Shining Waters . . .

[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s shortcut [[belonging|home]] from [[mrs-irving|Mrs. Irving]]'s [020-001–005] traces a path through the landscape of her own naming. The description is precise — shore fields, beech wood, footpath, main road — and the Lake of Shining Waters sits at its center, a landmark in both the physical and imaginative geography of her world. The ellipsis before "known to unimaginative people" is Montgomery's pause for effect: the beautiful name first, the deflating alternative second, the reader left smiling at the difference.

## Geography

The lake lies low, approached by shore fields and a beech wood on the property of Robert [[diana-barry|Di]]ckson. A footpath from the main road passes just above it, connecting to the shore road where [[mrs-irving|old Mrs. Irving]] lives. It is close enough to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] to be part of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s regular walking routes.

## Cultural Significance

No [[belonging|place]] in Montgomery's [[avonlea|Avonlea]] more perfectly condenses the novel's philosophy of naming than the Lake of Shining Waters. It is, objectively, a pond — specifically, Barry's Pond. But [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s name is not a lie; it is a seeing. The lake _does_ shine, and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is the one who noticed. That the name has stuck — that characters in the novel use it [[humor|wit]]hout irony — is a measure of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s influence on her world. She has not merely renamed the landscape; she has taught others to see it as she does.
