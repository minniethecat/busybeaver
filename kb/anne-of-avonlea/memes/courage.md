# Courage

The quality of meeting difficulty [[humor|wit]]hout shrinking — tested in "the remnant of her courage" nearly swept from [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s heart as she faced a cross neighbor (003-162), and renewed through encouragement that restores the spirit for the tasks ahead.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, courage is a practical, often quiet virtue rather than a dramatic display of heroism. It manifests in the daily acts of facing difficulty: a young teacher confronting a hostile neighbor, a schoolma'am persisting through [[despair|discouragement]], or a character holding steady under social pressure. Montgomery portrays courage as a resource that can be depleted — "it nearly swept the remnant of her courage from [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s heart" (003-162) — but also replenished by encouragement and small kindnesses.

The novel does not treat courage as the absence of fear; [[anne-shirley|Anne]] feels fear acutely when rapping at [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s door. Courage is the choice to act despite fear, and Montgomery suggests it is sustained through [[neighbor|community]]: [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s encouragement (007-001) and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s simple affirmation (006-155) both restore what [[despair|discouragement]] has drained.

## Examples in the Text

**Facing an Irate [[neighbor|Neighbor]] (Chapter 3).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] must confront [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] after her cow has trespassed into his oat field. His unexpected flight into the house — "merely the uncomfortable result of his surprise, mingled [[humor|wit]]h a good deal of shame over his outburst of temper the day before" — "nearly swept the remnant of her courage from [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s heart" (003-162). She reflects miserably, "'If he's so cross now what will he be when he hears what I've done'" (003-164), yet still raps at the door. Her courage is not the absence of dread but persistence through it.

**[[diana-barry|Di]]scouragement in [[teaching|Teaching]] (Chapter 6).** After a difficult day at school, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] drops wearily into her chair: "There was no real reason for [[despair|discouragement]], since nothing very dreadful had occurred; but [[anne-shirley|Anne]] was very tired and inclined to believe that she would never learn to like teaching" (006-092). Montgomery names [[despair|discouragement]] as an experience, not a character flaw — it is the natural consequence of fatigue and self-doubt. The restoration comes not through argument but through [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s gift of orchids and his simple words: "I like you, teacher" (006-155). "As if [[paul-irving|Paul]]'s words had been a spell of magic, [[despair|discouragement]] and weariness passed from her spirit" (006-157).

**The Role of Encouragement (Chapter 7).** "[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] came up later on [[humor|wit]]h more encouragement" (007-001). The good lady had waylaid the schoolchildren to ask how they liked their new teacher — "And every one of them said they liked you splendid, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]" (007-004). Montgomery shows that courage is sustained not in [[loneliness|isolation]] but through [[neighbor|community]]: a neighbor's reassurance, a pupil's [[love|affection]], a friend's confidence. Encouragement is the antidote to the courage-depleting effects of doubt.

## Thematic Significance

Courage in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is demystified. It is not the battlefield virtue of epic literature but the domestic, everyday courage required to teach reluctant pupils, face irate [[community|neighbors]], and persist through repeated small failures. Montgomery's treatment aligns courage [[humor|wit]]h hope and resilience — all three form a triad of emotional resources that make ordinary life bearable and growth possible.

The novel also emphasizes the *social* [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of courage: it is not a fixed character trait but a flame that others can kindle or extinguish. [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s orchids rekindle [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s courage; [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s encouragement shores it up; [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s gruffness nearly extinguishes it. Courage, in Montgomery's vision, is sustained through relationships.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Courage vs. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Caution.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] meets difficulty [[humor|wit]]h an emotional, forward-leaning courage — she feels fear keenly but acts anyway. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]'s courage is more stoic, expressed through duty and [[patience|endurance]] rather than emotional engagement. Both forms are valid; Montgomery does not rank them.

**[[diana-barry|Di]]scouragement as the Opposite of Courage.** The novel pairs courage [[humor|wit]]h its shadow: [[despair|discouragement]] is named repeatedly (006-092, 006-157, 007-001), suggesting that the primary threat to courage is not cowardice but the slow erosion of spirit through fatigue and disappointment. Montgomery treats [[despair|discouragement]] [[humor|wit]]h sympathy, not judgment.
