# Judson Parker

Judson Parker is a farmer living in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] who becomes a central figure of contention in the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] road improvement project. His refusal to grant a right-of-way through his land tests [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s diplomatic skills and reveals the complex social dynamics of village improvement efforts.

## Character Overview

Mr. Judson Parker is an [[avonlea|Avonlea]] farmer who owns a property along the road that the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Village Improvement Society]] wishes to beautify and widen. He is depicted as a financially motivated man whose reputation precedes him — "everybody knows what Judson Parker is," the [[neighbor|community]] says, suspecting he will exploit any situation for profit. His first appearance in the narrative comes during the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] road improvement initiative, where his land becomes the primary obstacle. [019-083]

## Role in the Story

Parker's role centers on the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] road beautification project. The committee needs a strip of his land to widen the road, but Parker initially refuses to grant the right-of-way. [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] volunteers to persuade him — a task others consider hopeless. [019-107]

In a pivotal encounter, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] appeals to Parker's better [[nature-appreciation|nature]], and to the [[neighbor|community]]'s astonishment, he relents. He not only grants the right-of-way but also offers to contribute spruce trees for the beautification effort. This transformation, however, proves incomplete when the facts behind his change of heart come to light — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] later discovers that Parker was motivated by a business arrangement rather than pure civic spirit. ## Key Scenes

**The Committee's Assessment**: The [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] committee discusses Parker's reputation as "the only man in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] who would refuse such a reasonable request" and debates whether to approach him at all. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] volunteers to make the personal appeal. [019-107] [019-163]

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Persuasion Visit**: [[anne-shirley|Anne]] calls on Judson Parker at his farm, making an impassioned case for the road improvement. Her sincerity and charm prove effective — Parker agrees to grant the right-of-way, stunning the committee and the wider [[neighbor|community]]. [019-141]

**The Aftermath**: The [[neighbor|community]]'s opinion of Parker shifts toward approval until the truth emerges — Parker had an ulterior motive involving a business transaction that made his concession profitable. [[anne-shirley|Anne]], who believed she had appealed to his better [[nature-appreciation|nature]], is disillusioned. [019-167]

**The Road Encounter**: Parker overtakes [[anne-shirley|Anne]] on the road and offers her an explanation, though one that leaves her [[humor|wit]]h mixed feelings about the whole affair. [019-180]

## Character Analysis

Judson Parker serves as a minor but instructive character in the novel. He represents the tension between self-interest and [[neighbor|community]] spirit that runs throughout the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] storyline. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s initial idealism — believing she can appeal to anyone's innate goodness — meets the more complicated reality of human motivation. Parker is not a villain; he does grant the right-of-way and even contributes trees. But his actions are rooted in calculation rather than civic virtue, providing [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[humor|wit]]h a nuanced lesson about the limits of moral persuasion and the complex motivations that drive people in a small [[neighbor|community]].
