[023-001] The parlor at [[green-gables|Green Gables]] was a rather severe and gloomy apartment,

[023-002] with rigid horsehair furniture, stiff lace curtains, and white

[023-003] antimacassars that were always laid at a perfectly correct angle,

[023-004] except at such times as they clung to unfortunate people’s buttons.

[023-005] Even [[anne-shirley|Anne]] had never been able to infuse much grace into it, for [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]

[023-006] would not permit any alterations. But it is wonderful what flowers can

[023-007] accomplish if you give them a fair chance; when Anne and [[diana-barry|Diana]] finished

[023-008] with the room you would not have recognized it.

[023-009] A great blue bowlful of snowballs overflowed on the polished table. The

[023-010] shining black mantelpiece was heaped with roses and ferns. Every shelf

[023-011] of the what-not held a sheaf of bluebells; the dark corners on either

[023-012] side of the grate were lighted up with jars full of glowing crimson

[023-013] peonies, and the grate itself was aflame with yellow poppies. All this

[023-014] splendor and color, mingled with the sunshine falling through the

[023-015] honeysuckle vines at the windows in a leafy riot of dancing shadows

[023-016] over walls and floor, made of the usually dismal little room the

[023-017] veritable “bower” of Anne’s imagination, and even extorted a tribute of

[023-018] admiration from Marilla, who came in to criticize and remained to

[023-019] praise.

[023-020] “Now, we must set the table,” said Anne, in the tone of a priestess

[023-021] about to perform some sacred rite in honor of a divinity. “We’ll have a

[023-022] big vaseful of wild roses in the center and one single rose in front of

[023-023] everybody’s plate—and a special bouquet of rosebuds only by Mrs.

[023-024] Morgan’s—an allusion to ‘The Rosebud Garden’ you know.”

[023-025] The table was set in the sitting room, with Marilla’s finest linen and

[023-026] the best china, glass, and silver. You may be perfectly certain that

[023-027] every article placed on it was polished or scoured to the highest

[023-028] possible perfection of gloss and glitter.

[023-029] Then the girls tripped out to the kitchen, which was filled with

[023-030] appetizing odors emanating from the oven, where the chickens were

[023-031] already sizzling splendidly. Anne prepared the potatoes and Diana got

[023-032] the peas and beans ready. Then, while Diana shut herself into the

[023-033] pantry to compound the lettuce salad, Anne, whose cheeks were already

[023-034] beginning to glow crimson, as much with excitement as from the heat of

[023-035] the fire, prepared the bread sauce for the chickens, minced her onions

[023-036] for the soup, and finally whipped the cream for her lemon pies.

[023-037] And what about [[davy-keith|Davy]] all this time? Was he redeeming his promise to be

[023-038] good? He was, indeed. To be sure, he insisted on remaining in the

[023-039] kitchen, for his curiosity wanted to see all that went on. But as he

[023-040] sat quietly in a corner, busily engaged in untying the knots in a piece

[023-041] of herring net he had brought [[belonging|home]] from his last trip to the shore,

[023-042] nobody objected to this.

[023-043] At half past eleven the lettuce salad was made, the golden circles of

[023-044] the pies were heaped with whipped cream, and everything was sizzling

[023-045] and bubbling that ought to sizzle and bubble.

[023-046] “We’d better go and dress now,” said Anne, “for they may be here by

[023-047] twelve. We must have dinner at sharp one, for the soup must be served

[023-048] as soon as it’s done.”

[023-049] Serious indeed were the toilet rites presently performed in the east

[023-050] gable. Anne peered anxiously at her nose and rejoiced to see that its

[023-051] freckles were not at all prominent, thanks either to the lemon juice or

[023-052] to the unusual flush on her cheeks. When they were ready they looked

[023-053] quite as sweet and trim and girlish as ever did any of “Mrs. Morgan’s

[023-054] heroines.”

[023-055] “I do hope I’ll be able to say something once in a while, and not sit

[023-056] like a mute,” said Diana anxiously. “All Mrs. Morgan’s heroines

[023-057] converse so beautifully. But I’m afraid I’ll be tongue-tied and stupid.

[023-058] And I’ll be sure to say ‘I seen.’ I haven’t often said it since Miss

[023-059] Stacy taught here; but in moments of excitement it’s sure to pop out.

[023-060] Anne, if I were to say ‘I seen’ before Mrs. Morgan I’d die of

[023-061] mortification. And it would be almost as bad to have nothing to say.”

[023-062] “I’m nervous about a good many things,” said Anne, “but I don’t think

[023-063] there is much fear that I won’t be able to _talk_.”

[023-064] And, to do her justice, there wasn’t.

[023-065] Anne shrouded her muslin glories in a big apron and went down to

[023-066] concoct her soup. Marilla had dressed herself and the twins, and looked

[023-067] more excited than she had ever been known to look before. At half past

[023-068] twelve the Allans and [[miss-stacy|Miss Stacy]] came. Everything was going well but

[023-069] Anne was beginning to feel nervous. It was surely time for Priscilla

[023-070] and Mrs. Morgan to arrive. She made frequent trips to the gate and

[023-071] looked as anxiously down the lane as ever her namesake in the Bluebeard

[023-072] story peered from the tower casement.

[023-073] “Suppose they don’t come at all?” she said piteously.

[023-074] “Don’t suppose it. It would be too mean,” said Diana, who, however, was

[023-075] beginning to have uncomfortable misgivings on the subject.

[023-076] “Anne,” said Marilla, coming out from the parlor, “Miss Stacy wants to

[023-077] see Miss Barry’s willowware platter.”

[023-078] Anne hastened to the sitting room closet to get the platter. She had,

[023-079] in accordance with her promise to [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]], written to Miss Barry of

[023-080] [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]], asking for the loan of it. Miss Barry was an old friend

[023-081] of Anne’s, and she promptly sent the platter out, with a letter

[023-082] exhorting Anne to be very careful of it, for she had paid twenty

[023-083] dollars for it. The platter had served its purpose at the Aid bazaar

[023-084] and had then been returned to the Green Gables closet, for Anne would

[023-085] not [[faith|trust]] anybody but herself to take it back to town.

[023-086] She carried the platter carefully to the front door where her guests

[023-087] were enjoying the cool breeze that blew up from the brook. It was

[023-088] examined and admired; then, just as Anne had taken it back into her own

[023-089] hands, a terrific crash and clatter sounded from the kitchen pantry.

[023-090] Marilla, Diana, and Anne fled out, the latter pausing only long enough

[023-091] to set the precious platter hastily down on the second step of the

[023-092] stairs.

[023-093] When they reached the pantry a truly harrowing spectacle met their eyes

[023-094] . . . a guilty looking small boy scrambling down from the table, with

[023-095] his clean print blouse liberally plastered with yellow filling, and on

[023-096] the table the shattered remnants of what had been two brave, becreamed

[023-097] lemon pies.

[023-098] Davy had finished ravelling out his herring net and had wound the twine

[023-099] into a ball. Then he had gone into the pantry to put it up on the shelf

[023-100] above the table, where he already kept a score or so of similar balls,

[023-101] which, so far as could be discovered, served no useful purpose save to

[023-102] yield the [[laughter|joy]] of possession. Davy had to climb on the table and reach

[023-103] over to the shelf at a dangerous angle . . . something he had been

[023-104] forbidden by Marilla to do, as he had come to [[death|grief]] once before in the

[023-105] experiment. The result in this instance was disastrous. Davy slipped

[023-106] and came sprawling squarely down on the lemon pies. His clean blouse

[023-107] was ruined for that time and the pies for all time. It is, however, an

[023-108] ill wind that blows nobody good, and the pig was eventually the gainer

[023-109] by Davy’s mischance.

[023-110] “Davy Keith,” said Marilla, shaking him by the shoulder, “didn’t I

[023-111] forbid you to climb up on that table again? Didn’t I?”

[023-112] “I forgot,” whimpered Davy. “You’ve told me not to do such an awful lot

[023-113] of things that I can’t remember them all.”

[023-114] “Well, you march upstairs and stay there till after dinner. Perhaps

[023-115] you’ll get them sorted out in your memory by that time. No, Anne, never

[023-116] you mind interceding for him. I’m not punishing him because he spoiled

[023-117] your pies . . . that was an accident. I’m punishing him for his

[023-118] disobedience. Go, Davy, I say.”

[023-119] “Ain’t I to have any dinner?” wailed Davy.

[023-120] “You can come down after dinner is over and have yours in the kitchen.”

[023-121] “Oh, all right,” said Davy, somewhat comforted. “I know Anne’ll save

[023-122] some nice bones for me, won’t you, Anne? ’Cause you know I didn’t mean

[023-123] to fall on the pies. Say, Anne, since they _are_ spoiled can’t I take

[023-124] some of the pieces upstairs with me?”

[023-125] “No, no lemon pie for you, Master Davy,” said Marilla, pushing him

[023-126] toward the hall.

[023-127] “What shall we do for dessert?” asked Anne, looking regretfully at the

[023-128] wreck and ruin.

[023-129] “Get out a crock of strawberry preserves,” said Marilla consolingly.

[023-130] “There’s plenty of whipped cream left in the bowl for it.”

[023-131] One o’clock came . . . but no Priscilla or Mrs. Morgan. Anne was in an

[023-132] agony. Everything was done to a turn and the soup was just what soup

[023-133] should be, but couldn’t be depended on to remain so for any length of

[023-134] time.

[023-135] “I don’t believe they’re coming after all,” said Marilla crossly.

[023-136] Anne and Diana sought comfort in each other’s eyes.

[023-137] At half past one Marilla again emerged from the parlor.

[023-138] “Girls, we _must_ have dinner. Everybody is hungry and it’s no use

[023-139] waiting any longer. Priscilla and Mrs. Morgan are not coming, that’s

[023-140] plain, and nothing is being improved by waiting.”

[023-141] Anne and Diana set about lifting the dinner, with all the zest gone out

[023-142] of the performance.

[023-143] “I don’t believe I’ll be able to eat a mouthful,” said Diana dolefully.

[023-144] “Nor I. But I hope everything will be nice for Miss Stacy’s and Mr. and

[023-145] [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]’s sakes,” said Anne listlessly.

[023-146] When Diana dished the peas she tasted them and a very peculiar

[023-147] expression crossed her face.

[023-148] “Anne, did _you_ put sugar in these peas?”

[023-149] “Yes,” said Anne, mashing the potatoes with the air of one expected to

[023-150] do her duty. “I put a spoonful of sugar in. We always do. Don’t you

[023-151] like it?”

[023-152] “But _I_ put a spoonful in too, when I set them on the stove,” said

[023-153] Diana.

[023-154] Anne dropped her masher and tasted the peas also. Then she made a

[023-155] grimace.

[023-156] “How awful! I never dreamed you had put sugar in, because I knew your

[023-157] mother never does. I happened to think of it, for a wonder . . . I’m

[023-158] always forgetting it . . . so I popped a spoonful in.”

[023-159] “It’s a case of too many cooks, I guess,” said Marilla, who had

[023-160] listened to this dialogue with a rather guilty expression. “I didn’t

[023-161] think you’d remember about the sugar, Anne, for I’m perfectly certain

[023-162] you never did before . . . so _I_ put in a spoonful.”

[023-163] The guests in the parlor heard peal after peal of [[humor|laughter]] from the

[023-164] kitchen, but they never knew what the fun was about. There were no

[023-165] green peas on the dinner table that day, however.

[023-166] “Well,” said Anne, sobering down again with a sigh of recollection, “we

[023-167] have the salad anyhow and I don’t think anything has happened to the

[023-168] beans. Let’s carry the things in and get it over.”

[023-169] It cannot be said that that dinner was a notable success socially. The

[023-170] Allans and Miss Stacy exerted themselves to save the situation and

[023-171] Marilla’s customary placidity was not noticeably ruffled. But Anne and

[023-172] Diana, between their disappointment and the reaction from their

[023-173] excitement of the forenoon, could neither talk nor eat. Anne tried

[023-174] heroically to bear her part in the conversation for the sake of her

[023-175] guests; but all the sparkle had been quenched in her for the time

[023-176] being, and, in spite of her [[romance|love]] for the Allans and Miss Stacy, she

[023-177] couldn’t help thinking how nice it would be when everybody had gone

[023-178] home and she could bury her weariness and disappointment in the pillows

[023-179] of the east gable.

[023-180] There is an old proverb that really seems at times to be inspired . . .

[023-181] “it never rains but it pours.” The measure of that day’s tribulations

[023-182] was not yet full. Just as [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] had finished returning thanks there

[023-183] arose a strange, ominous sound on the stairs, as of some hard, heavy

[023-184] object bounding from step to step, finishing up with a grand smash at

[023-185] the bottom. Everybody ran out into the hall. Anne gave a shriek of

[023-186] dismay.
