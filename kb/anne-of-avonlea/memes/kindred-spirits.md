# Kindred Spirits

[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s most distinctive relational category — the recognition of a soul that resonates at the same frequency as one's own, whether in a child, a contemporary, or an elderly recluse.

## Definition

"Kindred spirit" is [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s term for the deepest form of human connection — a bond that transcends age, circumstance, and [[tradition|convention]]al social categories. It is first introduced when [[anne-shirley|Anne]] meets a young man who, she senses, is "of the kindred spirit about *him*" (002-012). [[anne-shirley|Anne]] keeps "always a weather eye open for [[friendship|kindred spirits]]" (002-013), treating their discovery as one of life's primary pleasures and purposes.

The concept receives its fullest poetic treatment in the description of the imaginative realm shared by [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], where "[[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded" (021-025). For Montgomery, [[friendship|kindred spirits]] are those rare individuals who share "the geography of that land's geography . . . 'east o' the sun, west o' the moon'" (021-025) — the imaginative landscape that is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s true [[belonging|home]]. The concept transcends mere friendship: a kindred spirit is someone who validates your way of being in the world, who proves you are not alone in your peculiar sensibility.

## Examples in the Text

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Perpetual Search (Chapter 2).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] keeps "always a weather eye open for [[friendship|kindred spirits]]" (002-013), treating the search as a lifelong quest. The phrase captures both hope and discernment — she is always looking, but she will not bestow the designation lightly.

**The Realm of [[imagination|Imagination]] (Chapter 21).** The novel's most beautiful articulation of the concept comes in the description of the world shared by [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]: a realm where "clouds never darkened the sunny sky" and "[[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded" (021-025). This utopian vision of spiritual [[friendship|companionship]] is both childlike and profound — it describes a space where [[wisdom|understanding]] is perfect and judgment absent.

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] Recognized (Chapter 30).** The exclamation "That 'too' revealed a kindred spirit to [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]" (030-137) captures the instantaneous recognition that defines the kindred spirit experience. A single word — an unexpected alignment of sensibility — can reveal a lifelong connection.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] (Chapter 32).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] discovers in [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] a true "kindred spirit" (032-082). Their friendship bridges a generation, demonstrating that kindred-spirithood is immune to age. What matters is shared sensibility, not shared biography.

**Universal Application (Chapter 42).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s declaration that "we are [[friendship|kindred spirits]]" (042-004) extends the concept beyond her inner circle to a broader vision of human connection. And the observation that "like all [[friendship|kindred spirits]] he understood" (042-112) suggests that the capacity for intuitive [[wisdom|understanding]] is itself the defining mark of the kindred spirit.

## Thematic Significance

**Validation of the Self.** Kindred spirits serve a crucial psychological function in Montgomery's universe: they validate [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s peculiar sensibility. In a world that often finds her "queer," the discovery of another person who shares her imaginative orientation is redemptive — proof that her way of seeing is not wrong but rare.

**The Democracy of Connection.** Kindred spirits cross boundaries of age, gender, and social position. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] finds them in a young boy ([[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]), a middle-aged recluse ([[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]), and peers her own age. The concept is radically egalitarian — what matters is the soul, not the circumstance.

**The Antidote to [[loneliness|Loneliness]].** Kindred spirits protect against the loneliness that threatens sensitive souls. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s capacity to find them — and her determination to keep looking — is her defense against the [[loneliness|isolation]] that her intensity might otherwise produce.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Kindred Spirit vs. Ordinary [[friendship|Friendship]].** The novel distinguishes between friendship in general and kindred-spirithood in particular. [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]] is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s dearest friend, but she is not, strictly speaking, a kindred spirit — she lacks the imaginative intensity that defines the category. This distinction is not a hierarchy of value ([[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[romance|love]]s [[diana-barry|Diana]] no less) but a recognition of different modes of connection.

**Those Who Cannot Recognize Kindred Spirits.** Characters like [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] lack the sensibility to perceive [[friendship|kindred spirits]]. They are not hostile to the concept — they simply do not operate on that frequency. Their inability is presented as a limitation, however gently rendered.
