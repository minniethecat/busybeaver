# Kindness

The practical virtue that [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] brings to her teaching, her guardianship, and her [[neighbor|community]] life — a quality Montgomery presents not as mere softness but as an active force capable of winning over the most resistant hearts.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, kindness is an active, strategic virtue rather than a passive temperament. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s approach to difficult pupils is explicit: "[[patience|Patience]] and kindness will surely win him" (007-009). Kindness is a method, a deliberate practice chosen because it works, not merely because it is nice. This pragmatic dimension distinguishes Montgomery's treatment of kindness from mere sentimentality — kindness in the novel has teeth.

[[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates her theory of kindness [[humor|wit]]h conviction: "I'm *sure* my theory of kindness is right" (016-163). The italics on "sure" capture both her idealism and her determination. Kindness is not a vague hope but a tested principle, and the novel provides evidence for its effectiveness: "Your kindness won his [[romance|love]] after the idea took root" (021-082).

## Examples in the Text

**Winning [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] (Chapter 7).** Faced [[humor|wit]]h a defiant, unlikable pupil, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] declares her strategy: "[[patience|Patience]] and kindness will surely win him" (007-009). The confidence is characteristic — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] believes in kindness as a force, not a sentiment, and she is prepared to persist until it proves effective.

**The Theory Tested (Chapter 9).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s affirmation — "we'll see what patience and kindness can do" (009-016) — frames kindness as an experiment. She is testing her philosophy against reality, and the novel charts the results [[humor|wit]]h care. Kindness is not magic; it takes time and repeated application.

**Vindication (Chapter 16).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s confident assertion — "I'm *sure* my theory of kindness is right" (016-163) — marks the midpoint of her campaign. The italics convey both conviction and a hint of defensiveness — she is arguing against those who would dismiss kindness as weakness.

**Kindness Rewarded (Chapter 21).** The narrator reports that "your kindness won his [[romance|love]] after the idea took root" (021-082), acknowledging both the efficacy of kindness and the time it requires. Kindness works, but it works slowly — the "idea" must "take root" before results appear.

**Kindness as [[diana-barry|Di]]stinction (Chapter 41).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is described as practicing "kindness, keeping aloof from all that" was petty or mean (041-092). By the novel's end, kindness has become [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s defining public quality — the trait that distinguishes her in the [[neighbor|community]] and earns her the respect she once sought through more dramatic means.

## Thematic Significance

**Kindness as Strength.** Montgomery consistently presents kindness as a form of strength rather than weakness. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s patient campaign to win [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] requires more [[courage|fortitude]] than the whip-wielding methods of her predecessor.

**The Pragmatic Case for Kindness.** The novel makes a practical argument for kindness: it works. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s success [[humor|wit]]h difficult pupils is the evidence. Montgomery avoids sentimentality by grounding kindness in results.

**Kindness and [[community|Community]].** Kindness in the novel is fundamentally social. It builds bonds, heals wounds, and transforms enemies into allies. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s kindness to [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], to her pupils, to the twins — all produce tangible [[neighbor|community]] benefits.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Kindness vs. Strictness.** The previous teacher's reliance on the whip represents the anti-kindness philosophy — the belief that only fear produces order. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s alternative approach is vindicated by results: her classroom is more orderly and her pupils more genuinely educated.

**Kindness as Naivete?** Skeptical voices suggest that kindness is naive, that children need firmness more than gentleness. The novel engages this critique seriously and answers it [[humor|wit]]h evidence — [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s kindness is not naive because it is effective.
