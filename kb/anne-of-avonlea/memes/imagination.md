# Imagination

The defining character trait of [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and the lens through which she experiences the world — an active, creative faculty that transforms the mundane into the marvellous, enables deep empathy, and serves as the novel's central thematic engine.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, imagination is not mere daydreaming or escapism. It is an active creative power that enables its possessor to see beauty where others see only the ordinary, to people [[loneliness|solitude]] [[humor|wit]]h merry companions, and to enter sympathetically into the lives of others. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] herself defines it as the faculty "to peep at life through other people's eyes" (043-118). For Montgomery, imagination is a moral and emotional capacity — it bridges the gap between self and other, youth and age, loneliness and [[neighbor|community]].

The novel presents imagination as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s most distinguishing characteristic from the opening pages, where she tells the irascible [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]: "I can make allowance for you, [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], because I have an imagination. I can easily imagine how very trying it must be to find a cow in your oats" (001-186). This early exchange establishes imagination not as mere fancy but as a practical tool for empathy and [[forgiveness|reconciliation]].

## Examples in the Text

**Peacemaking [[humor|wit]]h [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] (Chapter 1).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s first significant act in the novel is to use her imagination to bridge a conflict. Rather than taking offense at [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s rudeness, she imaginatively inhabits his position and extends forgiveness (001-186). The scene immediately signals that [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s girlhood imagination has matured into a social skill.

**The Schoolroom Transformed (Chapter 23).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] applies her imagination to the physical world, turning the "usually dismal little room" of the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] school into what the narrator calls "the veritable 'bower' of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imagination" (023-017). Her decorative efforts [[humor|wit]]h wild roses and honeysuckle vines "even extorted a tribute of admiration from [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], who came in to criticize and remained to praise" (023-017).

**Solitude Peopled (Chapter 32).** Walking alone through the beechlands in December, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "never found it lonely; her imagination peopled her path [[humor|wit]]h merry companions, and [[humor|wit]]h these she carried on a gay, pretended conversation that was [[humor|wit]]tier and more fascinating than conversations are apt to be in real life" (032-097). This passage reveals imagination as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s inner resource against loneliness — a faculty she has possessed since childhood but now wields [[humor|wit]]h adult self-awareness.

**The Geography of a Shared Realm (Chapter 21).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] discovers in her pupil [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] a true imaginative peer. Together they inhabit what Montgomery describes through poetic allusion: "How fair the realm / Imagination opens to the view" (021-022). The narrator notes that both "knew the way to that happy land" where "the rose of [[laughter|joy]] bloomed immortal" and "[[friendship|kindred spirits]] abounded" (021-025). This shared imaginative landscape forms the foundation of one of the novel's most tender relationships.

**Empathy Across Experience (Chapter 43).** When [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]] struggles to explain her feelings for [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] bridges the gap [[humor|wit]]h her imagination: "Bless you, dearest of [[diana-barry|Diana]]s, I understand now. What is an imagination for if not to enable you to peep at life through other people's eyes?" (043-118). This exchange distills imagination to its essential moral function — it is the faculty of [[wisdom|understanding]], not invention.

**The Bond [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] (Chapter 32).** Between [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] springs "one of those fervent, helpful friendships possible only between a woman who has kept the freshness of youth in her heart and soul, and a girl whose imagination and intuition supplied the [[belonging|place]] of experience" (032-080). Here imagination is positioned as a legitimate substitute for worldly knowledge — a way of knowing that rivals experience itself.

## Thematic Significance

Imagination serves as the novel's unifying thematic thread, connecting character development, plot, and Montgomery's broader philosophical vision:

**Growth and Maturation.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imagination in *[[avonlea|Avonlea]]* differs from the fanciful daydreaming of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[green-gables|Green Gables]]*. It has matured into an instrument of practical empathy and aesthetic action — she uses it to teach, to decorate, to reconcile, and to understand. Her growth is measured not by the dimming of imagination but by its refinement.

**[[community|Community]] and Connection.** Imagination in the novel is fundamentally *social*. It enables [[anne-shirley|Anne]] to forgive her enemies (001-186), to create a beautiful shared space for her pupils (023-017), to recognize "[[friendship|kindred spirits]]" in [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] (021-025), and to understand [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s romantic feelings (043-118). Far from isolating the dreamer, imagination in Montgomery's vision builds bridges between people.

**The Romantic Vision of Everyday Life.** Montgomery inherits the Romantic belief that imagination reveals a deeper reality hidden beneath surface appearances. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imagination does not falsify the world — it *completes* it, seeing the potential beauty in a dismal schoolroom or a gray December wood. This is the novel's most persistent argument: that the imaginative person lives more fully, not less truthfully.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** represents the practical, no-nonsense worldview that initially dis[[faith|trust]]s imagination. Yet [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s trajectory is revealing — she "came in to criticize and remained to praise" [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s decorated schoolroom (023-017). Montgomery does not set imagination and practicality in permanent opposition; she shows imagination winning over even its sternest critics.

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]** begins the novel as imagination's antagonist — blunt, irritable, and apparently immune to charm. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imaginative empathy disarms him (001-186), and he becomes one of her staunchest allies. His conversion suggests that imagination has persuasive power even over those who claim to despise it.

**[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]** is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imaginative mirror — a child who "knew the way to that happy land" (021-025). Through [[paul-irving|Paul]], Montgomery shows that imagination is not peculiar to [[anne-shirley|Anne]] but is a universal faculty, present wherever a sensitive soul encounters beauty. [[paul-irving|Paul]] validates [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s worldview by sharing it.

**[[diana-barry|Diana Barry]]**, by contrast, lacks [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s imaginative intensity. Her difficulty articulating her feelings for [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]] (043-118) shows the limits of a perspective unaided by imagination — and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s ability to "peep at life through other people's eyes" bridges the gap.
