[019-001] “Anyhow, there’ll be plenty of jam in heaven, that’s one comfort,” he

[019-002] said complacently.

[019-003] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] nipped a smile in the bud.

[019-004] “Perhaps there will . . . if we want it,” she said, “But what makes you

[019-005] think so?”

[019-006] “Why, it’s in the catechism,” said [[davy-keith|Davy]].

[019-007] “Oh, no, there is nothing like _that_ in the catechism, Davy.”

[019-008] “But I tell you there is,” persisted Davy. “It was in that question

[019-009] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] taught me last Sunday. ‘Why should we [[romance|love]] God?’ It says,

[019-010] ‘Because He makes preserves, and redeems us.’ Preserves is just a holy

[019-011] way of saying jam.”

[019-012] “I must get a drink of water,” said Anne hastily. When she came back it

[019-013] cost her some time and trouble to explain to Davy that a certain comma

[019-014] in the said catechism question made a great deal of difference in the

[019-015] meaning.

[019-016] “Well, I thought it was too good to be true,” he said at last, with a

[019-017] sigh of disappointed conviction. “And besides, I didn’t see when He’d

[019-018] find time to make jam if it’s one endless Sabbath day, as the hymn

[019-019] says. I don’t believe I want to go to heaven. Won’t there ever be any

[019-020] Saturdays in heaven, Anne?”

[019-021] “Yes, Saturdays, and every other kind of beautiful days. And every day

[019-022] in heaven will be more beautiful than the one before it, Davy,” assured

[019-023] Anne, who was rather glad that Marilla was not by to be shocked.

[019-024] Marilla, it is needless to say, was bringing the twins up in the good

[019-025] old ways of theology and discouraged all fanciful speculations

[019-026] thereupon. Davy and [[dora-keith|Dora]] were taught a hymn, a catechism question, and

[019-027] two Bible verses every Sunday. Dora learned meekly and recited like a

[019-028] little machine, with perhaps as much [[wisdom|understanding]] or interest as if

[019-029] she were one. Davy, on the contrary, had a lively curiosity, and

[019-030] frequently asked questions which made Marilla tremble for his fate.

[019-031] “Chester Sloane says we’ll do nothing all the time in heaven but walk

[019-032] around in white dresses and play on harps; and he says he hopes he

[019-033] won’t have to go till he’s an old man, ’cause maybe he’ll like it

[019-034] better then. And he thinks it will be horrid to wear dresses and I

[019-035] think so too. Why can’t men angels wear trousers, Anne? Chester Sloane

[019-036] is interested in those things, ’cause they’re going to make a minister

[019-037] of him. He’s got to be a minister ’cause his grandmother left the money

[019-038] to send him to college and he can’t have it unless he is a minister.

[019-039] She thought a minister was such a ‘spectable thing to have in a family.

[019-040] Chester says he doesn’t mind much . . . though he’d rather be a

[019-041] blacksmith . . . but he’s bound to have all the fun he can before he

[019-042] begins to be a minister, ’cause he doesn’t expect to have much

[019-043] afterwards. _I_ ain’t going to be a minister. I’m going to be a

[019-044] storekeeper, like Mr. Blair, and keep heaps of candy and bananas. But

[019-045] I’d rather like going to your kind of a heaven if they’d let me play a

[019-046] mouth organ instead of a harp. Do you s’pose they would?”

[019-047] “Yes, I think they would if you wanted it,” was all Anne could [[faith|trust]]

[019-048] herself to say.

[019-049] The [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] met at [[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]]’ that evening and a full

[019-050] attendance had been requested, since important business was to be

[019-051] discussed. The A.V.I.S. was in a flourishing condition, and had already

[019-052] accomplished wonders. Early in the spring Mr. Major Spencer had

[019-053] redeemed his promise and had stumped, graded, and seeded down all the

[019-054] road front of his farm. A dozen other men, some prompted by a

[019-055] determination not to let a Spencer get ahead of them, others goaded

[019-056] into action by Improvers in their own households, had followed his

[019-057] example. The result was that there were long strips of smooth velvet

[019-058] turf where once had been unsightly undergrowth or brush. The farm

[019-059] fronts that had not been done looked so badly by contrast that their

[019-060] owners were secretly shamed into resolving to see what they could do

[019-061] another spring. The triangle of ground at the cross roads had also been

[019-062] cleared and seeded down, and Anne’s bed of geraniums, unharmed by any

[019-063] marauding cow, was already set out in the center.

[019-064] Altogether, the Improvers thought that they were getting on

[019-065] beautifully, even if Mr. Levi Boulter, tactfully approached by a

[019-066] carefully selected committee in regard to the old house on his upper

[019-067] farm, did bluntly tell them that he wasn’t going to have it meddled

[019-068] with.

[019-069] At this especial meeting they intended to draw up a petition to the

[019-070] school trustees, humbly praying that a fence be put around the school

[019-071] grounds; and a plan was also to be discussed for planting a few

[019-072] ornamental trees by [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]], if the funds of the [[community|society]] would

[019-073] permit of it . . . for, as Anne said, there was no use in starting

[019-074] another subscription as long as the hall remained blue. The members

[019-075] were assembled in the Andrews’ parlor and [[jane-andrews|Jane]] was already on her feet

[019-076] to move the appointment of a committee which should find out and report

[019-077] on the price of said trees, when [[gertie-pye|Gertie Pye]] swept in, pompadoured and

[019-078] frilled within an inch of her life. Gertie had a habit of being late .

[019-079] . . “to make her entrance more effective,” spiteful people said.

[019-080] Gertie’s entrance in this instance was certainly effective, for she

[019-081] paused dramatically on the middle of the floor, threw up her hands,

[019-082] rolled her eyes, and exclaimed, “I’ve just heard something perfectly

[019-083] awful. What _do_ you think? [[judson-parker|Mr. Judson Parker]] _is going to rent all the

[019-084] road fence of his farm to a patent medicine company to paint

[019-085] advertisements on_.”

[019-086] For once in her life Gertie Pye made all the sensation she desired. If

[019-087] she had thrown a bomb among the complacent Improvers she could hardly

[019-088] have made more.

[019-089] “It _can’t_ be true,” said Anne blankly.

[019-090] “That’s just what _I_ said when I heard it first, don’t you know,” said

[019-091] Gertie, who was enjoying herself hugely. “_I_ said it couldn’t be true

[019-092] . . . that Judson Parker wouldn’t have the _heart_ to do it, don’t you

[019-093] know. But father met him this afternoon and asked him about it and he

[019-094] said it WAS true. Just fancy! His farm is side-on to the [[newbridge|Newbridge]] road

[019-095] and how perfectly awful it will look to see advertisements of pills and

[019-096] plasters all along it, don’t you know?”

[019-097] The Improvers _did_ know, all too well. Even the least imaginative

[019-098] among them could picture the grotesque effect of half a mile of board

[019-099] fence adorned with such advertisements. All thought of church and

[019-100] school grounds vanished before this new danger. Parliamentary rules and

[019-101] regulations were forgotten, and Anne, in despair, gave up trying to

[019-102] keep minutes at all. Everybody talked at once and fearful was the

[019-103] hubbub.

[019-104] “Oh, let us keep calm,” implored Anne, who was the most excited of them

[019-105] all, “and try to think of some way of preventing him.”

[019-106] “I don’t know how you’re going to prevent him,” exclaimed Jane

[019-107] bitterly. “Everybody knows what Judson Parker is. He’d do _anything_

[019-108] for money. He hasn’t a _spark_ of public spirit or _any_ sense of the

[019-109] beautiful.”

[019-110] The prospect looked rather unpromising. Judson Parker and his sister

[019-111] were the only Parkers in [[avonlea|Avonlea]], so that no leverage could be exerted

[019-112] by family connections. Martha Parker was a lady of all too certain age

[019-113] who disapproved of young people in general and the Improvers in

[019-114] particular. Judson was a jovial, smooth-spoken man, so uniformly

[019-115] goodnatured and bland that it was surprising how few friends he had.

[019-116] Perhaps he had got the better in too many business transactions. . .

[019-117] which seldom makes for popularity. He was reputed to be very “sharp”

[019-118] and it was the general opinion that he “hadn’t much principle.”

[019-119] “If Judson Parker has a chance to ‘turn an honest penny,’ as he says

[019-120] himself, he’ll never lose it,” declared [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]].

[019-121] “Is there _nobody_ who has any influence over him?” asked Anne

[019-122] despairingly.

[019-123] “He goes to see Louisa Spencer at [[white-sands|White Sands]],” suggested Carrie

[019-124] Sloane. “Perhaps she could coax him not to rent his fences.”

[019-125] “Not she,” said [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] emphatically. “I know Louisa Spencer well. She

[019-126] doesn’t ‘believe’ in Village Improvement Societies, but she _does_

[019-127] believe in dollars and cents. She’d be more likely to urge Judson on

[019-128] than to dissuade him.”

[019-129] “The only thing to do is to appoint a committee to wait on him and

[019-130] protest,” said Julia Bell, “and you must send girls, for he’d hardly be

[019-131] civil to boys . . . but _I_ won’t go, so nobody need nominate me.”

[019-132] “Better send Anne alone,” said [[oliver-sloane|Oliver Sloane]]. “She can talk Judson over

[019-133] if anybody can.”

[019-134] Anne protested. She was willing to go and do the talking; but she must

[019-135] have others with her “for moral support.” [[diana-barry|Diana]] and Jane were therefore

[019-136] appointed to support her morally and the Improvers broke up, buzzing

[019-137] like angry bees with indignation. Anne was so worried that she didn’t

[019-138] sleep until nearly morning, and then she dreamed that the trustees had

[019-139] put a fence around the school and painted “Try Purple Pills” all over

[019-140] it.

[019-141] The committee waited on Judson Parker the next afternoon. Anne pleaded

[019-142] eloquently against his nefarious design and Jane and Diana supported

[019-143] her morally and valiantly. Judson was sleek, suave, flattering; paid

[019-144] them several compliments of the delicacy of sunflowers; felt real bad

[019-145] to refuse such charming young ladies . . . but business was business;

[019-146] couldn’t afford to let sentiment stand in the way these hard times.

[019-147] “But I’ll tell what I _will_ do,” he said, with a twinkle in his light,

[019-148] full eyes. “I’ll tell the agent he must use only handsome, tasty colors

[019-149] . . . red and yellow and so on. I’ll tell him he mustn’t paint the ads

[019-150] _blue_ on any account.”

[019-151] The vanquished committee retired, thinking things not lawful to be

[019-152] uttered.

[019-153] “We have done all we can do and must simply trust the rest to

[019-154] Providence,” said Jane, with an unconscious imitation of [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]’s

[019-155] tone and manner.

[019-156] “I wonder if [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] could do anything,” reflected Diana.

[019-157] Anne shook her head.

[019-158] “No, it’s no use to worry Mr. Allan, especially now when the baby’s so

[019-159] sick. Judson would slip away from him as smoothly as from us, although

[019-160] he _has_ taken to going to church quite regularly just now. That is

[019-161] simply because Louisa Spencer’s father is an elder and very particular

[019-162] about such things.”

[019-163] “Judson Parker is the only man in Avonlea who would dream of renting

[019-164] his fences,” said Jane indignantly. “Even Levi Boulter or [[lorenzo-white|Lorenzo White]]

[019-165] would never stoop to that, tightfisted as they are. They would have too

[019-166] much respect for public opinion.”

[019-167] Public opinion was certainly down on Judson Parker when the facts

[019-168] became known, but that did not help matters much. Judson chuckled to

[019-169] himself and defied it, and the Improvers were trying to reconcile

[019-170] themselves to the prospect of seeing the prettiest part of the

[019-171] Newbridge road defaced by advertisements, when Anne rose quietly at the

[019-172] president’s call for reports of committees on the occasion of the next

[019-173] meeting of the Society, and announced that Mr. Judson Parker had

[019-174] instructed her to inform the Society that he was _not_ going to rent

[019-175] his fences to the Patent Medicine Company.

[019-176] Jane and Diana stared as if they found it hard to believe their ears.

[019-177] Parliamentary etiquette, which was generally very strictly enforced in

[019-178] the A.V.I.S., forbade them [[generosity|giving]] instant vent to their curiosity, but

[019-179] after the Society adjourned Anne was besieged for explanations. Anne

[019-180] had no explanation to give. Judson Parker had overtaken her on the road

[019-181] the preceding evening and told her that he had decided to [[laughter|humor]] the

[019-182] A.V.I.S. in its peculiar prejudice against patent medicine

[019-183] advertisements. That was all Anne would say, then or ever afterwards,

[019-184] and it was the simple truth; but when Jane Andrews, on her way [[belonging|home]],

[019-185] confided to Oliver Sloane her firm belief that there was more behind

[019-186] Judson Parker’s mysterious change of heart than Anne Shirley had

[019-187] revealed, she spoke the truth also.
