[020-001] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] had been down to [[mrs-irving|old Mrs. Irving]]’s on the shore road the preceding

[020-002] evening and had come [[belonging|home]] by a short cut which led her first over the

[020-003] low-lying shore fields, and then through the beech wood below Robert

[020-004] Dickson’s, by a little footpath that ran out to the main road just

[020-005] above [[lake-of-shining-waters|the Lake of Shining Waters]] . . . known to unimaginative people as

[020-006] Barry’s pond.

[020-007] Two men were sitting in their buggies, reined off to the side of the

[020-008] road, just at the entrance of the path. One was [[judson-parker|Judson Parker]]; the

[020-009] other was [[jerry-corcoran|Jerry Corcoran]], a [[newbridge|Newbridge]] man against whom, as [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[020-010] would have told you in eloquent italics, nothing shady had ever been

[020-011] _proved_. He was an agent for agricultural implements and a prominent

[020-012] personage in matters political. He had a finger . . . some people said

[020-013] _all_ his fingers . . . in every political pie that was cooked; and as

[020-014] Canada was on the eve of a general election Jerry Corcoran had been a

[020-015] busy man for many weeks, canvassing the county in the interests of his

[020-016] party’s candidate. Just as Anne emerged from under the overhanging

[020-017] beech boughs she heard Corcoran say, “If you’ll vote for Amesbury,

[020-018] Parker . . . well, I’ve a note for that pair of harrows you’ve got in

[020-019] the spring. I suppose you wouldn’t object to having it back, eh?”

[020-020] “We . . . ll, since you put it in that way,” drawled Judson with a

[020-021] grin, “I reckon I might as well do it. A man must look out for his own

[020-022] interests in these hard times.”

[020-023] Both saw Anne at this moment and conversation abruptly ceased. Anne

[020-024] bowed frostily and walked on, with her chin slightly more tilted than

[020-025] usual. Soon Judson Parker overtook her.

[020-026] “Have a lift, Anne?” he inquired genially.

[020-027] “Thank you, no,” said Anne politely, but with a fine, needle-like

[020-028] disdain in her voice that pierced even Judson Parker’s none too

[020-029] sensitive consciousness. His face reddened and he twitched his reins

[020-030] angrily; but the next second prudential considerations checked him. He

[020-031] looked uneasily at Anne, as she walked steadily on, glancing neither to

[020-032] the right nor to the left. Had she heard Corcoran’s unmistakable offer

[020-033] and his own too plain acceptance of it? Confound Corcoran! If he

[020-034] couldn’t put his meaning into less dangerous phrases he’d get into

[020-035] trouble some of these long-come-shorts. And confound redheaded

[020-036] school-ma’ams with a habit of popping out of beechwoods where they had

[020-037] no business to be. If Anne had heard, Judson Parker, measuring her corn

[020-038] in his own half bushel, as the country saying went, and cheating

[020-039] himself thereby, as such people generally do, believed that she would

[020-040] tell it far and wide. Now, Judson Parker, as has been seen, was not

[020-041] overly regardful of public opinion; but to be known as having accepted

[020-042] a bribe would be a nasty thing; and if it ever reached Isaac Spencer’s

[020-043] ears farewell forever to all hope of winning Louisa [[jane-andrews|Jane]] with her

[020-044] comfortable prospects as the heiress of a well-to-do farmer. Judson

[020-045] Parker knew that [[mr-spencer|Mr. Spencer]] looked somewhat askance at him as it was;

[020-046] he could not afford to take any risks.

[020-047] “Ahem . . . Anne, I’ve been wanting to see you about that little matter

[020-048] we were discussing the other day. I’ve decided not to let my fences to

[020-049] that company after all. A [[community|society]] with an aim like yours ought to be

[020-050] encouraged.”

[020-051] Anne thawed out the merest trifle.

[020-052] “Thank you,” she said.

[020-053] “And . . . and . . . you needn’t mention that little conversation of

[020-054] mine with Jerry.”

[020-055] “I have no intention of mentioning it in any case,” said Anne icily,

[020-056] for she would have seen every fence in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] painted with

[020-057] advertisements before she would have stooped to bargain with a man who

[020-058] would sell his vote.

[020-059] “Just so . . . just so,” agreed Judson, imagining that they understood

[020-060] each other beautifully. “I didn’t suppose you would. Of course, I was

[020-061] only stringing Jerry . . . he thinks he’s so all-fired cute and smart.

[020-062] I’ve no intention of voting for Amesbury. I’m going to vote for Grant

[020-063] as I’ve always done . . . you’ll see that when the election comes off.

[020-064] I just led Jerry on to see if he would commit himself. And it’s all

[020-065] right about the fence . . . you can tell the Improvers that.”

[020-066] “It takes all sorts of people to make a world, as I’ve often heard, but

[020-067] I think there are some who could be spared,” Anne told her reflection

[020-068] in the east gable mirror that night. “I wouldn’t have mentioned the

[020-069] disgraceful thing to a soul anyhow, so my conscience is clear on _that_

[020-070] score. I really don’t know who or what is to be thanked for this. _I_

[020-071] did nothing to bring it about, and it’s hard to believe that Providence

[020-072] ever works by means of the kind of politics men like Judson Parker and

[020-073] Jerry Corcoran have.”

[020-074] XV

[020-075] The Beginning of Vacation

[020-076] Anne locked the schoolhouse door on a still, yellow evening, when the

[020-077] winds were purring in the spruces around the playground, and the

[020-078] shadows were long and lazy by the edge of the woods. She dropped the

[020-079] key into her pocket with a sigh of satisfaction. The school year was

[020-080] ended, she had been reengaged for the next, with many expressions of

[020-081] satisfaction. . . . only [[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]] told her she ought to use

[020-082] the strap oftener . . . and two delightful months of a well-earned

[020-083] vacation beckoned her invitingly. Anne felt at peace with the world and

[020-084] herself as she walked down the hill with her basket of flowers in her

[020-085] hand. Since the earliest mayflowers Anne had never missed her weekly

[020-086] pilgrimage to Matthew’s grave. Everyone else in Avonlea, except

[020-087] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], had already forgotten quiet, shy, unimportant Matthew

[020-088] Cuthbert; but his memory was still green in Anne’s heart and always

[020-089] would be. She could never forget the kind old man who had been the

[020-090] first to give her the [[romance|love]] and sympathy her starved childhood had

[020-091] craved.

[020-092] At the foot of the hill a boy was sitting on the fence in the shadow of

[020-093] the spruces . . . a boy with big, dreamy eyes and a beautiful,

[020-094] sensitive face. He swung down and joined Anne, smiling; but there were

[020-095] traces of tears on his cheeks.

[020-096] “I thought I’d wait for you, teacher, because I knew you were going to

[020-097] the graveyard,” he said, slipping his hand into hers. “I’m going there,

[020-098] too . . . I’m taking this bouquet of geraniums to put on Grandpa

[020-099] Irving’s grave for grandma. And look, teacher, I’m going to put this

[020-100] bunch of white roses beside Grandpa’s grave in memory of my little

[020-101] mother. . . because I can’t go to her grave to put it there. But don’t

[020-102] you think she’ll know all about it, just the same?”

[020-103] “Yes, I am sure she will, [[paul-irving|Paul]].”

[020-104] “You see, teacher, it’s just three years today since my little mother

[020-105] died. It’s such a long, long time but it hurts just as much as ever . .

[020-106] . and I miss her just as much as ever. Sometimes it seems to me that I

[020-107] just can’t bear it, it hurts so.”

[020-108] Paul’s voice quivered and his lip trembled. He looked down at his

[020-109] roses, hoping that his teacher would not notice the tears in his eyes.

[020-110] “And yet,” said Anne, very softly, “you wouldn’t want it to stop

[020-111] hurting . . . you wouldn’t want to forget your little mother even if

[020-112] you could.”

[020-113] “No, indeed, I wouldn’t . . . that’s just the way I feel. You’re so

[020-114] good at [[wisdom|understanding]], teacher. Nobody else understands so well . . .

[020-115] not even grandma, although she’s so good to me. Father understood

[020-116] pretty well, but still I couldn’t talk much to him about mother,

[020-117] because it made him feel so bad. When he put his hand over his face I

[020-118] always knew it was time to stop. Poor father, he must be dreadfully

[020-119] lonesome without me; but you see he has nobody but a housekeeper now

[020-120] and he thinks housekeepers are no good to bring up little boys,

[020-121] especially when he has to be away from home so much on business.

[020-122] Grandmothers are better, next to mothers. Someday, when I’m brought up,

[020-123] I’ll go back to father and we’re never going to be parted again.”

[020-124] Paul had talked so much to Anne about his mother and father that she

[020-125] felt as if she had known them. She thought his mother must have been

[020-126] very like what he was himself, in temperament and disposition; and she

[020-127] had an idea that [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] was a rather reserved man with a deep

[020-128] and tender [[nature-appreciation|nature]] which he kept hidden scrupulously from the world.

[020-129] “Father’s not very easy to get acquainted with,” Paul had said once. “I

[020-130] never got really acquainted with him until after my little mother died.

[020-131] But he’s splendid when you do get to know him. I [[romance|love]] him the best in

[020-132] all the world, and Grandma Irving next, and then you, teacher. I’d love

[020-133] you next to father if it wasn’t my _duty_ to love Grandma Irving best,

[020-134] because she’s doing so much for me. _You_ know, teacher. I wish she

[020-135] would leave the lamp in my room till I go to sleep, though. She takes

[020-136] it right out as soon as she tucks me up because she says I mustn’t be a

[020-137] coward. I’m _not_ scared, but I’d _rather_ have the light. My little

[020-138] mother used always to sit beside me and hold my hand till I went to

[020-139] sleep. I expect she spoiled me. Mothers do sometimes, you know.”

[020-140] No, Anne did not know this, although she might imagine it. She thought

[020-141] sadly of _her_ “little mother,” the mother who had thought her so

[020-142] “perfectly beautiful” and who had died so long ago and was buried

[020-143] beside her boyish husband in that unvisited grave far away. Anne could

[020-144] not remember her mother and for this reason she almost envied Paul.

[020-145] “My birthday is next week,” said Paul, as they walked up the long red

[020-146] hill, basking in the June sunshine, “and father wrote me that he is

[020-147] sending me something that he thinks I’ll like better than anything else

[020-148] he could send. I believe it has come already, for Grandma is keeping

[020-149] the bookcase drawer locked and that is something new. And when I asked

[020-150] her why, she just looked mysterious and said little boys mustn’t be too

[020-151] curious. It’s very exciting to have a birthday, isn’t it? I’ll be

[020-152] eleven. You’d never think it to look at me, would you? Grandma says I’m

[020-153] very small for my age and that it’s all because I don’t eat enough

[020-154] porridge. I do my very best, but Grandma gives such _generous_

[020-155] platefuls . . . there’s nothing mean about Grandma, I can tell you.

[020-156] Ever since you and I had that talk about praying going home from Sunday

[020-157] School that day, teacher . . . when you said we ought to pray about all

[020-158] our difficulties . . . I’ve prayed every night that God would give me

[020-159] enough grace to enable me to eat every bit of my porridge in the

[020-160] mornings. But I’ve never been able to do it yet, and whether it’s

[020-161] because I have too little grace or too much porridge I really can’t

[020-162] decide. Grandma says father was brought up on porridge, and it

[020-163] certainly did work well in his case, for you ought to see the shoulders

[020-164] he has. But sometimes,” concluded Paul with a sigh and a meditative air

[020-165] “I really think porridge will be the death of me.”

[020-166] Anne permitted herself a smile, since Paul was not looking at her. All

[020-167] Avonlea knew that old Mrs. Irving was bringing her grandson up in

[020-168] accordance with the good, old-fashioned methods of diet and morals.
