# Laughter

The irrepressible sound of joy that echoes through [[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] — from "peal after peal of [[humor|laughter]] from the parlor" (023-163) to the "blithe voices and [[humor|laughter]]" (017-017) of young people in the woods, marking moments of connection, release, and shared delight.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, [[humor|laughter]] functions as more than a response to the humorous. It is a marker of [[neighbor|community]], an index of emotional health, and a form of resistance against sorrow and hardship. Montgomery uses [[humor|laughter]] to distinguish the lively from the dour, the imaginative from the literal-minded, and the young from those who have forgotten how to take delight in the world.

The novel is suffused [[humor|wit]]h [[humor|laughter]] — children's [[humor|laughter]], young people's [[humor|laughter]], even the suppressed [[humor|laughter]] of adults who know they should be more dignified. This pervasive mirth establishes [[avonlea|Avonlea]] as a world where joy is not only permitted but celebrated.

## Examples in the Text

**A House Filled [[humor|wit]]h Laughter (Chapter 3).** The novel contrasts the empty, silent house [[humor|wit]]h the memory of "little children and [[humor|laughter]] and songs" (003-016). Laughter here represents life itself — the animation and warmth that children bring to a [[belonging|home]], and the [[death|loss]] felt when they depart.

**The Schoolroom Outburst (Chapter 8).** When something strikes the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] schoolchildren as funny, they dissolve into "suppressed [[humor|laughter]] as soon as it was safe" (008-092). This suppressed-then-released [[humor|laughter]] captures the irrepressible [[nature-appreciation|nature]] of juvenile mirth — it cannot be permanently contained.

**[[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]'s Defeat (Chapter 15).** One of the novel's most memorable comic scenes involves the boys sent "into roars of [[humor|laughter]]" (015-145) at [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]'s expense. Laughter here serves a social function — puncturing pretension and restoring equilibrium.

**The Parlor's Peals (Chapter 23).** From the parlor, "peal after peal of [[humor|laughter]]" (023-163) is heard, signaling the warmth and connection of shared amusement. This domestic [[humor|laughter]] is the sound of [[neighbor|community]] at its most intimate and authentic.

**A Life of "Work and Dreams and Laughter" (Chapter 26).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s life is described as "filled [[humor|wit]]h work and [[ambition|dreams]] and [[humor|laughter]]" (026-051). The trinity of work, [[ambition|dreams]], and [[humor|laughter]] defines the balance Montgomery endorses — practical effort, imaginative vision, and joyful expression.

## Thematic Significance

**Laughter as [[community|Community]].** Shared [[humor|laughter]] in the novel signals be[[envy|longing]]. When characters laugh together, they are momentarily unified — distinctions of age, status, and temperament dissolve in the warmth of shared mirth. The "peal after peal of [[humor|laughter]]" from the parlor is the sound of [[neighbor|community]] renewing itself.

**Laughter and Resilience.** The novel's characters laugh in the face of difficulty. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s capacity to find humor in her own misadventures is a form of resilience — [[humor|laughter]] lightens burdens that would otherwise be crushing. In this sense, [[humor|laughter]] is allied [[humor|wit]]h hope as an emotional resource.

**Laughter as Youth.** Montgomery associates spontaneous, joyful [[humor|laughter]] [[humor|wit]]h youth, though she does not limit it exclusively to the young. The "blithe voices and [[humor|laughter]]" (017-017) of young people in the woods represents a kind of joy that adults sometimes lose — and that characters like [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] manage to preserve.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**The Unimaginative and the [[humor|Humor]]less.** Characters who lack imagination tend to lack a sense of humor as well. Those who cannot laugh at themselves or at the world's absurdities are presented as incomplete — cut off from one of life's essential consolations.

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Suppressed Mirth.** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]] represents the struggle between natural appreciation for humor and the restraint expected of dignified adults. That she often "remained to praise" where she came to criticize suggests that [[humor|laughter]], like beauty, can win over even determined skeptics.
