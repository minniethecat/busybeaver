[010-001] The fat pony jogged over the bridge in Lynde’s Hollow and along the

[010-002] [[green-gables|Green Gables]] lane. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]’s face was rather grim. It was ten miles

[010-003] from [[grafton|East Grafton]] and [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]] seemed to be possessed with a passion

[010-004] for perpetual motion. It was beyond Marilla’s power to make him sit

[010-005] still and she had been in an agony the whole way lest he fall over the

[010-006] back of the wagon and break his neck, or tumble over the dashboard

[010-007] under the pony’s heels. In despair she finally threatened to whip him

[010-008] soundly when she got him [[belonging|home]]. Whereupon Davy climbed into her lap,

[010-009] regardless of the reins, flung his chubby arms about her neck and gave

[010-010] her a bear-like hug.

[010-011] “I don’t believe you mean it,” he said, smacking her wrinkled cheek

[010-012] affectionately. “You don’t _look_ like a lady who’d whip a little boy

[010-013] just ’cause he couldn’t keep still. Didn’t you find it awful hard to

[010-014] keep still when you was only ‘s old as me?”

[010-015] “No, I always kept still when I was told,” said Marilla, trying to

[010-016] speak sternly, albeit she felt her heart waxing soft within her under

[010-017] Davy’s impulsive caresses.

[010-018] “Well, I s’pose that was ’cause you was a girl,” said Davy, squirming

[010-019] back to his place after another hug. “You _was_ a girl once, I s’pose,

[010-020] though it’s awful funny to think of it. [[dora-keith|Dora]] can sit still . . . but

[010-021] there ain’t much fun in it _I_ don’t think. Seems to me it must be slow

[010-022] to be a girl. Here, Dora, let me liven you up a bit.”

[010-023] Davy’s method of “livening up” was to grasp Dora’s curls in his fingers

[010-024] and give them a tug. Dora shrieked and then cried.

[010-025] “How can you be such a naughty boy and your poor mother just laid in

[010-026] her grave this very day?” demanded Marilla despairingly.

[010-027] “But she was glad to die,” said Davy confidentially. “I know, ’cause

[010-028] she told me so. She was awful tired of being sick. We’d a long talk the

[010-029] night before she died. She told me you was going to take me and Dora

[010-030] for the winter and I was to be a good boy. I’m going to be good, but

[010-031] can’t you be good running round just as well as sitting still? And she

[010-032] said I was always to be kind to Dora and stand up for her, and I’m

[010-033] going to.”

[010-034] “Do you call pulling her hair being kind to her?”

[010-035] “Well, I ain’t going to let anybody else pull it,” said Davy, doubling

[010-036] up his fists and frowning. “They’d just better try it. I didn’t hurt

[010-037] her much . . . she just cried ’cause she’s a girl. I’m glad I’m a boy

[010-038] but I’m sorry I’m a twin. When Jimmy Sprott’s sister conterdicks him he

[010-039] just says, ‘I’m oldern you, so of course I know better,’ and that

[010-040] settles _her_. But I can’t tell Dora that, and she just goes on

[010-041] thinking diffrunt from me. You might let me drive the gee-gee for a

[010-042] spell, since I’m a man.”

[010-043] Altogether, Marilla was a thankful woman when she drove into her own

[010-044] yard, where the wind of the autumn night was dancing with the brown

[010-045] leaves. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] was at the gate to meet them and lift the twins out. Dora

[010-046] submitted calmly to be kissed, but Davy responded to Anne’s welcome

[010-047] with one of his hearty hugs and the cheerful announcement, “I’m Mr.

[010-048] Davy Keith.”

[010-049] At the supper table Dora behaved like a little lady, but Davy’s manners

[010-050] left much to be desired.

[010-051] “I’m so hungry I ain’t got time to eat p’litely,” he said when Marilla

[010-052] reproved him. “Dora ain’t half as hungry as I am. Look at all the

[010-053] ex’cise I took on the road here. That cake’s awful nice and plummy. We

[010-054] haven’t had any cake at home for ever’n ever so long, ’cause mother was

[010-055] too sick to make it and Mrs. Sprott said it was as much as she could do

[010-056] to bake our bread for us. And Mrs. Wiggins never puts any plums in

[010-057] _her_ cakes. Catch her! Can I have another piece?”

[010-058] Marilla would have refused but Anne cut a generous second slice.

[010-059] However, she reminded Davy that he ought to say “Thank you” for it.

[010-060] Davy merely grinned at her and took a huge bite. When he had finished

[010-061] the slice he said,

[010-062] “If you’ll give me _another_ piece I’ll say thank you for _it_.”

[010-063] “No, you have had plenty of cake,” said Marilla in a tone which Anne

[010-064] knew and Davy was to learn to be final.

[010-065] Davy winked at Anne, and then, leaning over the table, snatched Dora’s

[010-066] first piece of cake, from which she had just taken one dainty little

[010-067] bite, out of her very fingers and, opening his mouth to the fullest

[010-068] extent, crammed the whole slice in. Dora’s lip trembled and Marilla was

[010-069] speechless with horror. Anne promptly exclaimed, with her best

[010-070] “schoolma’am” air,

[010-071] “Oh, Davy, gentlemen don’t do things like that.”

[010-072] “I know they don’t,” said Davy, as soon as he could speak, “but I ain’t

[010-073] a gemplum.”

[010-074] “But don’t you want to be?” said shocked Anne.

[010-075] “Course I do. But you can’t be a gemplum till you grow up.”

[010-076] “Oh, indeed you can,” Anne hastened to say, thinking she saw a chance

[010-077] to sow good seed betimes. “You can begin to be a gentleman when you are

[010-078] a little boy. And gentlemen _never_ snatch things from ladies . . . or

[010-079] forget to say thank you . . . or pull anybody’s hair.”

[010-080] “They don’t have much fun, that’s a fact,” said Davy frankly. “I guess

[010-081] I’ll wait till I’m grown up to be one.”

[010-082] Marilla, with a resigned air, had cut another piece of cake for Dora.

[010-083] She did not feel able to cope with Davy just then. It had been a hard

[010-084] day for her, what with the funeral and the long drive. At that moment

[010-085] she looked forward to the future with a pessimism that would have done

[010-086] credit to Eliza Andrews herself.

[010-087] The twins were not noticeably alike, although both were fair. Dora had

[010-088] long sleek curls that never got out of order. Davy had a crop of fuzzy

[010-089] little yellow ringlets all over his round head. Dora’s hazel eyes were

[010-090] gentle and mild; Davy’s were as roguish and dancing as an elf’s. Dora’s

[010-091] nose was straight, Davy’s a positive snub; Dora had a “prunes and

[010-092] prisms” mouth, Davy’s was all smiles; and besides, he had a dimple in

[010-093] one cheek and none in the other, which gave him a dear, comical,

[010-094] lopsided look when he laughed. Mirth and mischief lurked in every

[010-095] corner of his little face.

[010-096] “They’d better go to bed,” said Marilla, who thought it was the easiest

[010-097] way to dispose of them. “Dora will sleep with me and you can put Davy

[010-098] in the west gable. You’re not afraid to sleep alone, are you, Davy?”

[010-099] “No; but I ain’t going to bed for ever so long yet,” said Davy

[010-100] comfortably.

[010-101] “Oh, yes, you are.” That was all the much-tried Marilla said, but

[010-102] something in her tone squelched even Davy. He trotted obediently

[010-103] upstairs with Anne.

[010-104] “When I’m grown up the very first thing I’m going to do is stay up

[010-105] _all_ night just to see what it would be like,” he told her

[010-106] confidentially.

[010-107] In after years Marilla never thought of that first week of the twins’

[010-108] sojourn at Green Gables without a shiver. Not that it really was so

[010-109] much worse than the weeks that followed it; but it seemed so by reason

[010-110] of its novelty. There was seldom a waking minute of any day when Davy

[010-111] was not in mischief or devising it; but his first notable exploit

[010-112] occurred two days after his arrival, on Sunday morning . . . a fine,

[010-113] warm day, as hazy and mild as September. Anne dressed him for church

[010-114] while Marilla attended to Dora. Davy at first objected strongly to

[010-115] having his face washed.

[010-116] “Marilla washed it yesterday . . . and Mrs. Wiggins scoured me with

[010-117] hard soap the day of the funeral. That’s enough for one week. I don’t

[010-118] see the good of being so awful clean. It’s lots more comfable being

[010-119] dirty.”

[010-120] “[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] washes his face every day of his own accord,” said Anne

[010-121] astutely.

[010-122] Davy had been an inmate of Green Gables for little over forty-eight

[010-123] hours; but he already worshipped Anne and hated Paul Irving, whom he

[010-124] had heard Anne praising enthusiastically the day after his arrival. If

[010-125] Paul Irving washed his face every day, that settled it. He, Davy Keith,

[010-126] would do it too, if it killed him. The same consideration induced him

[010-127] to submit meekly to the other details of his toilet, and he was really

[010-128] a handsome little lad when all was done. Anne felt an almost maternal

[010-129] pride in him as she led him into the old Cuthbert pew.

[010-130] Davy behaved quite well at first, being occupied in casting covert

[010-131] glances at all the small boys within view and wondering which was Paul

[010-132] Irving. The first two hymns and the Scripture reading passed off

[010-133] uneventfully. [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]] was praying when the sensation came.

[010-134] [[lauretta-white|Lauretta White]] was sitting in front of Davy, her head slightly bent and

[010-135] her fair hair hanging in two long braids, between which a tempting

[010-136] expanse of white neck showed, encased in a loose lace frill. Lauretta

[010-137] was a fat, placid-looking child of eight, who had conducted herself

[010-138] irreproachably in church from the very first day her mother carried her

[010-139] there, an infant of six months.

[010-140] Davy thrust his hand into his pocket and produced . . . a caterpillar,

[010-141] a furry, squirming caterpillar. Marilla saw and clutched at him but she

[010-142] was too late. Davy dropped the caterpillar down Lauretta’s neck.

[010-143] Right into the middle of Mr. Allan’s prayer burst a series of piercing

[010-144] shrieks. The minister stopped appalled and opened his eyes. Every head

[010-145] in the congregation flew up. Lauretta White was dancing up and down in

[010-146] her pew, clutching frantically at the back of her dress.

[010-147] “Ow . . . mommer . . . mommer . . . ow . . . take it off . . . ow . . .

[010-148] get it out . . . ow . . . that bad boy put it down my neck . . . ow . .

[010-149] . mommer . . . it’s going further down . . . ow . . . ow . . . ow. . .

[010-150] .”

[010-151] Mrs. White rose and with a set face carried the hysterical, writhing

[010-152] Lauretta out of church. Her shrieks died away in the distance and Mr.

[010-153] Allan proceeded with the service. But everybody felt that it was a

[010-154] failure that day. For the first time in her life Marilla took no notice

[010-155] of the text and Anne sat with scarlet cheeks of mortification.

[010-156] When they got home Marilla put Davy to bed and made him stay there for

[010-157] the rest of the day. She would not give him any dinner but allowed him

[010-158] a plain tea of bread and milk. Anne carried it to him and sat

[010-159] sorrowfully by him while he ate it with an unrepentant relish. But

[010-160] Anne’s mournful eyes troubled him.

[010-161] “I s’pose,” he said reflectively, “that Paul Irving wouldn’t have

[010-162] dropped a caterpillar down a girl’s neck in church, would he?”

[010-163] “Indeed he wouldn’t,” said Anne sadly.

[010-164] “Well, I’m kind of sorry I did it, then,” conceded Davy. “But it was

[010-165] such a jolly big caterpillar . . . I picked him up on [[avonlea-presbyterian-church|the church]] steps

[010-166] just as we went in. It seemed a pity to waste him. And say, wasn’t it

[010-167] fun to hear that girl yell?”

[010-168] Tuesday afternoon the Aid Society met at Green Gables. Anne hurried

[010-169] home from school, for she knew that Marilla would need all the

[010-170] assistance she could give. Dora, neat and proper, in her nicely

[010-171] starched white dress and black sash, was sitting with the members of

[010-172] the Aid in the parlor, speaking demurely when spoken to, keeping

[010-173] silence when not, and in every way comporting herself as a model child.

[010-174] Davy, blissfully dirty, was making mud pies in the barnyard.
