[005-001] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] ran [[belonging|home]] blithely and recounted her adventures to [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], who had

[005-002] been not a little alarmed by her long absence and was on the point of

[005-003] starting out to look for her.

[005-004] “It’s a pretty good world, after all, isn’t it, Marilla?” concluded

[005-005] Anne happily. “[[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] was complaining the other day that it wasn’t

[005-006] much of a world. She said whenever you looked forward to anything

[005-007] pleasant you were sure to be more or less disappointed . . . perhaps

[005-008] that is true. But there is a good side to it too. The bad things don’t

[005-009] always come up to your expectations either . . . they nearly always

[005-010] turn out ever so much better than you think. I looked forward to a

[005-011] dreadfully unpleasant experience when I went over to [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]’s

[005-012] tonight; and instead he was quite kind and I had almost a nice time. I

[005-013] think we’re going to be real good friends if we make plenty of

[005-014] allowances for each other, and everything has turned out for the best.

[005-015] But all the same, Marilla, I shall certainly never again sell a cow

[005-016] before making sure to whom she belongs. And I do _not_ like parrots!”

[005-017] IV

[005-018] Different Opinions

[005-019] One evening at sunset, [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]], [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], and Anne Shirley

[005-020] were lingering by a fence in the shadow of gently swaying spruce

[005-021] boughs, where a wood cut known as the [[birch-path|Birch Path]] joined the main road.

[005-022] Jane had been up to spend the afternoon with Anne, who walked part of

[005-023] the way home with her; at the fence they met Gilbert, and all three

[005-024] were now talking about the fateful morrow; for that morrow was the

[005-025] first of September and the schools would open. Jane would go to

[005-026] [[newbridge|Newbridge]] and Gilbert to [[white-sands|White Sands]].

[005-027] “You both have the advantage of me,” sighed Anne. “You’re going to

[005-028] teach children who don’t know you, but I have to teach my own old

[005-029] schoolmates, and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says she’s afraid they won’t respect me as

[005-030] they would a stranger unless I’m very cross from the first. But I don’t

[005-031] believe a teacher should be cross. Oh, it seems to me such a

[005-032] [[duty|responsibility]]!”

[005-033] “I guess we’ll get on all right,” said Jane comfortably. Jane was not

[005-034] troubled by any aspirations to be an influence for good. She meant to

[005-035] earn her salary fairly, please the trustees, and get her name on the

[005-036] School Inspector’s roll of honor. Further ambitions Jane had none. “The

[005-037] main thing will be to keep order and a teacher has to be a little cross

[005-038] to do that. If my pupils won’t do as I tell them I shall punish them.”

[005-039] “How?”

[005-040] “Give them a good whipping, of course.”

[005-041] “Oh, Jane, you wouldn’t,” cried Anne, shocked. “Jane, you _couldn’t!_”

[005-042] “Indeed, I could and would, if they deserved it,” said Jane decidedly.

[005-043] “I could _never_ whip a child,” said Anne with equal decision. “I don’t

[005-044] believe in it _at all_. [[miss-stacy|Miss Stacy]] never whipped any of us and she had

[005-045] perfect order; and Mr. Phillips was always whipping and he had no order

[005-046] at all. No, if I can’t get along without whipping I shall not try to

[005-047] teach school. There are better ways of managing. I shall try to win my

[005-048] pupils’ affections and then they will _want_ to do what I tell them.”

[005-049] “But suppose they don’t?” said practical Jane.

[005-050] “I wouldn’t whip them anyhow. I’m sure it wouldn’t do any good. Oh,

[005-051] don’t whip your pupils, Jane dear, no matter what they do.”

[005-052] “What do you think about it, Gilbert?” demanded Jane. “Don’t you think

[005-053] there are some children who really need a whipping now and then?”

[005-054] “Don’t you think it’s a cruel, barbarous thing to whip a child . . .

[005-055] _any_ child?” exclaimed Anne, her face flushing with earnestness.

[005-056] “Well,” said Gilbert slowly, torn between his real convictions and his

[005-057] wish to measure up to Anne’s ideal, “there’s something to be said on

[005-058] both sides. I don’t believe in whipping children _much_. I think, as

[005-059] you say, Anne, that there are better ways of managing as a rule, and

[005-060] that corporal punishment should be a last resort. But on the other

[005-061] hand, as Jane says, I believe there is an occasional child who can’t be

[005-062] influenced in any other way and who, in short, needs a whipping and

[005-063] would be improved by it. Corporal punishment as a last resort is to be

[005-064] my rule.”

[005-065] Gilbert, having tried to please both sides, succeeded, as is usual and

[005-066] eminently right, in pleasing neither. Jane tossed her head.

[005-067] “I’ll whip my pupils when they’re naughty. It’s the shortest and

[005-068] easiest way of convincing them.”

[005-069] Anne gave Gilbert a disappointed glance.

[005-070] “I shall never whip a child,” she repeated firmly. “I feel sure it

[005-071] isn’t either right or necessary.”

[005-072] “Suppose a boy sauced you back when you told him to do something?” said

[005-073] Jane.

[005-074] “I’d keep him in after school and talk kindly and firmly to him,” said

[005-075] Anne. “There is some good in every person if you can find it. It is a

[005-076] teacher’s duty to find and develop it. That is what our School

[005-077] Management professor at Queen’s told us, you know. Do you suppose you

[005-078] could find any good in a child by whipping him? It’s far more important

[005-079] to influence the children aright than it is even to teach them the

[005-080] three R’s, Professor Rennie says.”

[005-081] “But the Inspector examines them in the three R’s, mind you, and he

[005-082] won’t give you a good report if they don’t come up to his standard,”

[005-083] protested Jane.

[005-084] “I’d rather have my pupils [[romance|love]] me and look back to me in after years

[005-085] as a real helper than be on the roll of honor,” asserted Anne

[005-086] decidedly.

[005-087] “Wouldn’t you punish children at all, when they misbehaved?” asked

[005-088] Gilbert.

[005-089] “Oh, yes, I suppose I shall have to, although I know I’ll hate to do

[005-090] it. But you can keep them in at recess or stand them on the floor or

[005-091] give them lines to write.”

[005-092] “I suppose you won’t punish the girls by making them sit with the

[005-093] boys?” said Jane slyly.

[005-094] Gilbert and Anne looked at each other and smiled rather foolishly. Once

[005-095] upon a time, Anne had been made to sit with Gilbert for punishment and

[005-096] sad and bitter had been the consequences thereof.

[005-097] “Well, time will tell which is the best way,” said Jane philosophically

[005-098] as they parted.

[005-099] Anne went back to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] by way of Birch Path, shadowy, rustling,

[005-100] fern-scented, through [[violet-vale|Violet Vale]] and past [[willowmere|Willowmere]], where dark and

[005-101] light kissed each other under the firs, and down through Lover’s Lane .

[005-102] . . spots she and [[diana-barry|Diana]] had so named long ago. She walked slowly,

[005-103] enjoying the sweetness of wood and field and the starry summer

[005-104] twilight, and thinking soberly about the new duties she was to take up

[005-105] on the morrow. When she reached the yard at Green Gables [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]’s

[005-106] loud, decided tones floated out through the open kitchen window.

[005-107] “Mrs. Lynde has come up to give me good advice about tomorrow,” thought

[005-108] Anne with a grimace, “but I don’t believe I’ll go in. Her advice is

[005-109] much like pepper, I think . . . excellent in small quantities but

[005-110] rather scorching in her doses. I’ll run over and have a chat with Mr.

[005-111] Harrison instead.”

[005-112] This was not the first time Anne had run over and chatted with Mr.

[005-113] Harrison since the notable affair of the Jersey cow. She had been there

[005-114] several evenings and Mr. Harrison and she were very good friends,

[005-115] although there were times and seasons when Anne found the outspokenness

[005-116] on which he prided himself rather trying. Ginger still continued to

[005-117] regard her with suspicion, and never failed to greet her sarcastically

[005-118] as “redheaded snippet.” Mr. Harrison had tried vainly to break him of

[005-119] the habit by jumping excitedly up whenever he saw Anne coming and

[005-120] exclaiming,

[005-121] “Bless my soul, here’s that pretty little girl again,” or something

[005-122] equally flattering. But Ginger saw through the scheme and scorned it.

[005-123] Anne was never to know how many compliments Mr. Harrison paid her

[005-124] behind her back. He certainly never paid her any to her face.

[005-125] “Well, I suppose you’ve been back in the woods laying in a supply of

[005-126] switches for tomorrow?” was his greeting as Anne came up the veranda

[005-127] steps.

[005-128] “No, indeed,” said Anne indignantly. She was an excellent target for

[005-129] teasing because she always took things so seriously. “I shall never

[005-130] have a switch in my school, Mr. Harrison. Of course, I shall have to

[005-131] have a pointer, but I shall use it for pointing _only_.”

[005-132] “So you mean to strap them instead? Well, I don’t know but you’re

[005-133] right. A switch stings more at the time but the strap smarts longer,

[005-134] that’s a fact.”

[005-135] “I shall not use anything of the sort. I’m not going to whip my

[005-136] pupils.”

[005-137] “Bless my soul,” exclaimed Mr. Harrison in genuine astonishment, “how

[005-138] do you lay out to keep order then?”

[005-139] “I shall govern by affection, Mr. Harrison.”

[005-140] “It won’t do,” said Mr. Harrison, “won’t do at all, Anne. ‘Spare the

[005-141] rod and spoil the child.’ When I went to school the master whipped me

[005-142] regular every day because he said if I wasn’t in mischief just then I

[005-143] was plotting it.”

[005-144] “Methods have changed since your schooldays, Mr. Harrison.”

[005-145] “But human [[nature-appreciation|nature]] hasn’t. Mark my words, you’ll never manage the young

[005-146] fry unless you keep a rod in pickle for them. The thing is impossible.”

[005-147] “Well, I’m going to try my way first,” said Anne, who had a fairly

[005-148] strong will of her own and was apt to cling very tenaciously to her

[005-149] theories.

[005-150] “You’re pretty stubborn, I reckon,” was Mr. Harrison’s way of putting

[005-151] it. “Well, well, we’ll see. Someday when you get riled up . . . and

[005-152] people with hair like yours are desperate apt to get riled . . . you’ll

[005-153] forget all your pretty little notions and give some of them a whaling.

[005-154] You’re too young to be teaching anyhow . . . far too young and

[005-155] childish.”

[005-156] Altogether, Anne went to bed that night in a rather pessimistic mood.

[005-157] She slept poorly and was so pale and tragic at breakfast next morning

[005-158] that Marilla was alarmed and insisted on making her take a cup of

[005-159] scorching ginger tea. Anne sipped it patiently, although she could not

[005-160] imagine what good ginger tea would do. Had it been some magic brew,

[005-161] potent to confer age and experience, Anne would have swallowed a quart

[005-162] of it without flinching.

[005-163] “Marilla, what if I fail!”

[005-164] “You’ll hardly fail completely in one day and there’s plenty more days

[005-165] coming,” said Marilla. “The trouble with you, Anne, is that you’ll

[005-166] expect to teach those children everything and reform all their faults

[005-167] right off, and if you can’t you’ll think you’ve failed.”

[005-168] V

[005-169] A Full-fledged Schoolma’am

[005-170] When Anne reached the school that morning . . . for the first time in

[005-171] her life she had traversed the Birch Path deaf and blind to its

[005-172] beauties . . . all was quiet and still. The preceding teacher had

[005-173] trained the children to be in their places at her arrival, and when

[005-174] Anne entered the schoolroom she was confronted by prim rows of “shining

[005-175] morning faces” and bright, inquisitive eyes. She hung up her hat and

[005-176] faced her pupils, hoping that she did not look as frightened and

[005-177] foolish as she felt and that they would not perceive how she was

[005-178] trembling.

[005-179] She had sat up until nearly twelve the preceding night composing a

[005-180] speech she meant to make to her pupils upon opening the school. She had

[005-181] revised and improved it painstakingly, and then she had learned it off

[005-182] by heart. It was a very good speech and had some very fine ideas in it,

[005-183] especially about mutual help and earnest striving after knowledge. The

[005-184] only trouble was that she could not now remember a word of it.
