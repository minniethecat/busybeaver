[003-001] “Splendid,” agreed [[anne-shirley|Anne]] gaily. “And have a rustic seat put under the

[003-002] birches. And when spring comes we’ll have a flower-bed made in the

[003-003] middle of it and plant geraniums.”

[003-004] “Yes; only we’ll have to devise some way of getting old Mrs. Hiram

[003-005] Sloane to keep her cow off the road, or she’ll eat our geraniums up,”

[003-006] laughed [[diana-barry|Diana]]. “I begin to see what you mean by educating public

[003-007] sentiment, Anne. There’s the old Boulter house now. Did you ever see

[003-008] such a rookery? And perched right close to the road too. An old house

[003-009] with its windows gone always makes me think of something dead with its

[003-010] eyes picked out.”

[003-011] “I think an old, deserted house is such a sad sight,” said Anne

[003-012] dreamily. “It always seems to me to be thinking about its past and

[003-013] mourning for its old-time joys. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] says that a large family was

[003-014] raised in that old house long ago, and that it was a real pretty [[belonging|place]],

[003-015] with a lovely garden and roses climbing all over it. It was full of

[003-016] little children and [[humor|laughter]] and songs; and now it is empty, and

[003-017] nothing ever wanders through it but the wind. How lonely and sorrowful

[003-018] it must feel! Perhaps they all come back on moonlit nights . . . the

[003-019] ghosts of the little children of long ago and the roses and the songs .

[003-020] . . and for a little while the old house can dream it is young and

[003-021] joyous again.”

[003-022] Diana shook her head.

[003-023] “I never imagine things like that about places now, Anne. Don’t you

[003-024] remember how cross mother and Marilla were when we imagined ghosts into

[003-025] [[haunted-wood|the Haunted Wood]]? To this day I can’t go through that bush comfortably

[003-026] after dark; and if I began imagining such things about the old Boulter

[003-027] house I’d be frightened to pass it too. Besides, those children aren’t

[003-028] dead. They’re all grown up and doing well . . . and one of them is a

[003-029] butcher. And flowers and songs couldn’t have ghosts anyhow.”

[003-030] Anne smothered a little sigh. She loved Diana dearly and they had

[003-031] always been good comrades. But she had long ago learned that when she

[003-032] wandered into the realm of fancy she must go alone. The way to it was

[003-033] by an enchanted path where not even her dearest might follow her.

[003-034] A thunder-shower came up while the girls were at [[carmody|Carmody]]; it did not

[003-035] last long, however, and the drive home, through lanes where the

[003-036] raindrops sparkled on the boughs and little leafy valleys where the

[003-037] drenched ferns gave out spicy odors, was delightful. But just as they

[003-038] turned into the Cuthbert lane Anne saw something that spoiled the

[003-039] beauty of the landscape for her.

[003-040] Before them on the right extended [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]’s broad, gray-green

[003-041] field of late oats, wet and luxuriant; and there, standing squarely in

[003-042] the middle of it, up to her sleek sides in the lush growth, and

[003-043] blinking at them calmly over the intervening tassels, was a Jersey cow!

[003-044] Anne dropped the reins and stood up with a tightening of the lips that

[003-045] boded no good to the predatory quadruped. Not a word said she, but she

[003-046] climbed nimbly down over the wheels, and whisked across the fence

[003-047] before Diana understood what had happened.

[003-048] “Anne, come back,” shrieked the latter, as soon as she found her voice.

[003-049] “You’ll ruin your dress in that wet grain . . . ruin it. She doesn’t

[003-050] hear me! Well, she’ll never get that cow out by herself. I must go and

[003-051] help her, of course.”

[003-052] Anne was charging through the grain like a mad thing. Diana hopped

[003-053] briskly down, tied the horse securely to a post, turned the skirt of

[003-054] her pretty gingham dress over her shoulders, mounted the fence, and

[003-055] started in pursuit of her frantic friend. She could run faster than

[003-056] Anne, who was hampered by her clinging and drenched skirt, and soon

[003-057] overtook her. Behind them they left a trail that would break Mr.

[003-058] Harrison’s heart when he should see it.

[003-059] “Anne, for mercy’s sake, stop,” panted poor Diana. “I’m right out of

[003-060] breath and you are wet to the skin.”

[003-061] “I must . . . get . . . that cow . . . out . . . before . . . Mr.

[003-062] Harrison . . . sees her,” gasped Anne. “I don’t . . . care . . . if I’m

[003-063] . . . drowned . . . if we . . . can . . . only . . . do that.”

[003-064] But the Jersey cow appeared to see no good reason for being hustled out

[003-065] of her luscious browsing ground. No sooner had the two breathless girls

[003-066] got near her than she turned and bolted squarely for the opposite

[003-067] corner of the field.

[003-068] “Head her off,” screamed Anne. “Run, Diana, run.”

[003-069] Diana did run. Anne tried to, and the wicked Jersey went around the

[003-070] field as if she were possessed. Privately, Diana thought she was. It

[003-071] was fully ten minutes before they headed her off and drove her through

[003-072] the corner gap into the Cuthbert lane.

[003-073] There is no denying that Anne was in anything but an angelic temper at

[003-074] that precise moment. Nor did it soothe her in the least to behold a

[003-075] buggy halted just outside the lane, wherein sat Mr. Shearer of Carmody

[003-076] and his son, both of whom wore a broad smile.

[003-077] “I guess you’d better have sold me that cow when I wanted to buy her

[003-078] last week, Anne,” chuckled Mr. Shearer.

[003-079] “I’ll sell her to you now, if you want her,” said her flushed and

[003-080] disheveled owner. “You may have her this very minute.”

[003-081] “Done. I’ll give you twenty for her as I offered before, and Jim here

[003-082] can drive her right over to Carmody. She’ll go to town with the rest of

[003-083] the shipment this evening. Mr. Reed of Brighton wants a Jersey cow.”

[003-084] Five minutes later Jim Shearer and the Jersey cow were marching up the

[003-085] road, and impulsive Anne was driving along the [[green-gables|Green Gables]] lane with

[003-086] her twenty dollars.

[003-087] “What will Marilla say?” asked Diana.

[003-088] “Oh, she won’t care. Dolly was my own cow and it isn’t likely she’d

[003-089] bring more than twenty dollars at the auction. But oh dear, if Mr.

[003-090] Harrison sees that grain he will know she has been in again, and after

[003-091] my [[generosity|giving]] him my word of honor that I’d never let it happen! Well, it

[003-092] has taught me a lesson not to give my word of honor about cows. A cow

[003-093] that could jump over or break through our milk-pen fence couldn’t be

[003-094] trusted anywhere.”

[003-095] Marilla had gone down to [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]’s, and when she returned knew all

[003-096] about Dolly’s sale and transfer, for [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] had seen most of the

[003-097] transaction from her window and guessed the rest.

[003-098] “I suppose it’s just as well she’s gone, though you _do_ do things in a

[003-099] dreadful headlong fashion, Anne. I don’t see how she got out of the

[003-100] pen, though. She must have broken some of the boards off.”

[003-101] “I didn’t think of looking,” said Anne, “but I’ll go and see now.

[003-102] [[martin|Martin]] has never come back yet. Perhaps some more of his aunts have

[003-103] died. I think it’s something like [[peter-sloane|Mr. Peter Sloane]] and the

[003-104] octogenarians. The other evening Mrs. Sloane was reading a newspaper

[003-105] and she said to Mr. Sloane, ‘I see here that another octogenarian has

[003-106] just died. What is an octogenarian, Peter?’ And Mr. Sloane said he

[003-107] didn’t know, but they must be very sickly creatures, for you never

[003-108] heard tell of them but they were dying. That’s the way with Martin’s

[003-109] aunts.”

[003-110] “Martin’s just like all the rest of those French,” said Marilla in

[003-111] disgust. “You can’t depend on them for a day.” Marilla was looking over

[003-112] Anne’s Carmody purchases when she heard a shrill shriek in the

[003-113] barnyard. A minute later Anne dashed into the kitchen, wringing her

[003-114] hands.

[003-115] “Anne Shirley, what’s the matter now?”

[003-116] “Oh, Marilla, whatever shall I do? This is terrible. And it’s all my

[003-117] fault. Oh, will I _ever_ learn to stop and reflect a little before

[003-118] doing reckless things? [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] always told me I would do something

[003-119] dreadful some day, and now I’ve done it!”

[003-120] “Anne, you are the most exasperating girl! _What_ is it you’ve done?”

[003-121] “Sold Mr. Harrison’s Jersey cow . . . the one he bought from [[mr-bell|Mr. Bell]] .

[003-122] . . to Mr. Shearer! Dolly is out in the milking pen this very minute.”

[003-123] “Anne Shirley, are you dreaming?”

[003-124] “I only wish I were. There’s no dream about it, though it’s very like a

[003-125] nightmare. And Mr. Harrison’s cow is in [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] by this time. Oh,

[003-126] Marilla, I thought I’d finished getting into scrapes, and here I am in

[003-127] the very worst one I ever was in in my life. What can I do?”

[003-128] “Do? There’s nothing to do, child, except go and see Mr. Harrison about

[003-129] it. We can offer him our Jersey in exchange if he doesn’t want to take

[003-130] the money. She is just as good as his.”

[003-131] “I’m sure he’ll be awfully cross and disagreeable about it, though,”

[003-132] moaned Anne.

[003-133] “I daresay he will. He seems to be an irritable sort of a man. I’ll go

[003-134] and explain to him if you like.”

[003-135] “No, indeed, I’m not as mean as that,” exclaimed Anne. “This is all my

[003-136] fault and I’m certainly not going to let you take my punishment. I’ll

[003-137] go myself and I’ll go at once. The sooner it’s over the better, for it

[003-138] will be terribly humiliating.”

[003-139] Poor Anne got her hat and her twenty dollars and was passing out when

[003-140] she happened to glance through the open pantry door. On the table

[003-141] reposed a nut cake which she had baked that morning . . . a

[003-142] particularly toothsome concoction iced with pink icing and adorned with

[003-143] walnuts. Anne had intended it for Friday evening, when the youth of

[003-144] [[avonlea|Avonlea]] were to meet at Green Gables to organize the Improvement

[003-145] Society. But what were they compared to the justly offended Mr.

[003-146] Harrison? Anne thought that cake ought to soften the heart of any man,

[003-147] especially one who had to do his own cooking, and she promptly popped

[003-148] it into a box. She would take it to Mr. Harrison as a peace offering.

[003-149] “That is, if he gives me a chance to say anything at all,” she thought

[003-150] ruefully, as she climbed the lane fence and started on a short cut

[003-151] across the fields, golden in the light of the dreamy August evening. “I

[003-152] know now just how people feel who are being led to execution.”

[003-153] III

[003-154] Mr. Harrison at Home

[003-155] Mr. Harrison’s house was an old-fashioned, low-eaved, whitewashed

[003-156] structure, set against a thick spruce grove.

[003-157] Mr. Harrison himself was sitting on his vineshaded veranda, in his

[003-158] shirt sleeves, enjoying his evening pipe. When he realized who was

[003-159] coming up the path he sprang suddenly to his feet, bolted into the

[003-160] house, and shut the door. This was merely the uncomfortable result of

[003-161] his surprise, mingled with a good deal of shame over his outburst of

[003-162] temper the day before. But it nearly swept the remnant of her courage

[003-163] from Anne’s heart.

[003-164] “If he’s so cross now what will he be when he hears what I’ve done,”

[003-165] she reflected miserably, as she rapped at the door.

[003-166] But Mr. Harrison opened it, smiling sheepishly, and invited her to

[003-167] enter in a tone quite mild and friendly, if somewhat nervous. He had

[003-168] laid aside his pipe and donned his coat; he offered Anne a very dusty

[003-169] chair very politely, and her reception would have passed off pleasantly

[003-170] enough if it had not been for the telltale of a parrot who was peering

[003-171] through the bars of his cage with wicked golden eyes. No sooner had

[003-172] Anne seated herself than Ginger exclaimed,

[003-173] “Bless my soul, what’s that redheaded snippet coming here for?”

[003-174] It would be hard to say whose face was the redder, Mr. Harrison’s or

[003-175] Anne’s.
