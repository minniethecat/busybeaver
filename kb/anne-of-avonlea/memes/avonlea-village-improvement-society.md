# Avonlea Village Improvement Society

The civic organization that [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] helps found in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] — simultaneously a vehicle for her beautification ideals, a source of comic misadventure, and a symbol of her integration into the [[neighbor|community]] she once entered as an outsider.

## Definition

The Avonlea Village Improvement Society (A.V.I.S.) represents [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s impulse toward practical beautification translated into collective action. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is "responsible for the A.V.I.S., since I was the first to suggest it" (007-059), taking pride in her role as initiator. The Society embodies the novel's conviction that aesthetic improvement is a form of civic virtue — the belief that making a [[belonging|place]] more beautiful makes its inhabitants better.

Yet Montgomery treats the A.V.I.S. [[humor|wit]]h characteristic [[laughter|humor]]. The Society's meetings are arenas of village politics, its projects frequently go awry, and its members include characters whose commitment to "improvement" is uneven at best. The A.V.I.S. is never merely earnest — it is also funny, and this comic dimension saves it from the sanctimony that might otherwise attend a novel about "village improvement."

## Examples in the Text

**Founding and Early Enthusiasm (Chapters 7, 11).** The Society's origins lie in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s declaration that "I'd like to add some beauty to life" (009-073). By Chapter 11, it has become a functioning organization: "We had a meeting of the A.V.I.S. last Friday night" (011-119). The rapid transition from idea to institution reflects both [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s energy and the [[neighbor|community]]'s readiness for collective improvement.

**Flourishing Condition (Chapter 19).** The A.V.I.S. meets at [[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]]'s [[belonging|home]], and by this point "the A.V.I.S. was in a flourishing condition" (019-051). The choice of meeting venue — a private [[belonging|home]] rather than a public hall — emphasizes the Society's grassroots, domestic character. Village improvement in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] is personal, conducted in parlors rather than council chambers.

**Resistance and Prejudice (Chapter 19).** Not everyone embraces the Society's mission. Some residents harbor "peculiar prejudice against the A.V.I.S." (019-182), forbidding immediate action on certain proposals. This resistance is treated [[humor|wit]]h amused tolerance rather than outrage — Montgomery understands that village improvement must proceed at the pace of village consensus.

**[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s Support (Chapter 27).** [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]]'s active encouragement of the A.V.I.S. (027-137) signals both his alignment [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s values and his own investment in the [[neighbor|community]]. His support is practical rather than romantic, but the shared commitment to civic improvement quietly deepens their bond.

**Gardens and Papers (Chapter 28).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s A.V.I.S. paper on gardens (028-018) merges her aesthetic sensibility [[humor|wit]]h the Society's practical mission. The paper is both a labor of [[romance|love]] and a civic duty — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] fulfilling her [[duty|obligation]] to the organization she helped create.

## Thematic Significance

**[[beauty|Beauty]] as Civic Virtue.** The A.V.I.S. translates [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s personal aesthetic mission into collective action. Her belief that "I'd like to add some beauty to life" becomes a [[neighbor|community]] project, demonstrating that individual idealism can have social consequences.

**The Comedy of Improvement.** Montgomery's treatment of the A.V.I.S. is [[love|affection]]ately satirical. The Society's earnest minutes, its fractious meetings, and the gap between its ambitions and achievements are sources of gentle [[laughter|humor]]. The novel never mocks the impulse to improve, but it smiles at the human foibles that attend any collective endeavor.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Integration into [[avonlea|Avonlea]].** The A.V.I.S. marks [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s transition from outsider to [[neighbor|community]] leader. The orphan who arrived by mistake becomes the young woman who organizes her [[community|neighbors]]. The Society is the institutional expression of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s be[[envy|longing]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**The Enthusiasts ([[anne-shirley|Anne]], [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]).** For [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], the A.V.I.S. is a genuine vehicle for improvement — a way to make [[avonlea|Avonlea]] more beautiful and its residents more thoughtful.

**The Skeptics.** Some [[avonlea|Avonlea]] residents view the Society [[humor|wit]]h suspicion, harboring "peculiar prejudice" against its initiatives (019-182). Their resistance represents the conservative impulse that any reform movement must navigate.

**The Indifferent.** Characters like [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]], who initially scorns [[neighbor|community]] involvement, represent the alternative of disengagement. His eventual participation signals the Society's — and the [[neighbor|community]]'s — gravitational pull.
