# Ambition

The engine of personal aspiration in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — a quality that distinguishes the novel's most dynamic characters and whose proper channeling is presented as one of the central tasks of young adulthood.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, ambition is treated [[humor|wit]]h careful ambivalence. The novel opens [[humor|wit]]h a poetic invocation of "hearts [[humor|wit]]h high and lofty ambitions" (001-061), immediately establishing aspiration as a noble quality. When ambition is wedded to character, it produces achievement: a person's "first kindled his ambition, and that all his success" flows from that initial spark (001-073).

Yet Montgomery also recognizes ambition's potential to narrow vision. [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]], who has no "further ambitions" beyond the honor roll (005-036), represents the contentment that ambition's enthusiasts might undervalue. And [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own ambition — to teach, to beautify, to become her best self — must be balanced against the competing claims of duty, [[neighbor|community]], and [[romance|love]]. The novel's wisdom lies in its refusal to either sanctify or condemn ambition, insisting instead on its proper integration into a well-lived life.

## Examples in the Text

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Noble Ambition (Chapter 9).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates her life's purpose: "I'd like to add some beauty to life . . . the noblest ambition" (009-075). [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s response — "I think you're fulfilling that ambition every day" (009-078) — validates both the ambition and its fulfillment. The exchange is quietly romantic: [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] sees and honors [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s deepest aspirations.

**[[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]]'s Contentment (Chapter 5).** [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]] represents the alternative to ambition — satisfaction [[humor|wit]]h modest achievement. She has no "further ambitions" beyond the honor roll (005-036). Montgomery does not mock this; [[jane-andrews|Jane]]'s contentment is genuine and her life is not portrayed as impoverished. The novel allows multiple valid relationships to ambition.

**Dreams of [[friendship|Friendship]] and Ambition (Chapter 21).** [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] shares [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "dreams of friendship and ambition" (021-136), modeling a life in which personal aspiration and relational commitment coexist. The minister's wife demonstrates that ambition need not be sacrificed to domesticity — the two can be woven together.

**Dusting Off Ambitions (Chapter 37).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s resolve to "get out all my ambitions and dust them" (037-142) reveals a mature relationship to aspiration. Ambitions are not constant companions but treasures stored and periodically revisited. The metaphor of "dusting" suggests care and maintenance — ambitions must be tended, refreshed, and sometimes reconsidered.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Pupils' Ambitions (Chapter 41).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] takes pride in having "wonderful ambitions in her pupils" (041-088). Her teaching is validated not by test scores but by the aspirations she has kindled — a teacher's success is measured in the dreams she inspires.

## Thematic Significance

**Ambition and Gender.** Montgomery presents female ambition [[humor|wit]]hout apology. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s aspirations are as serious and worthy as [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s, and the novel never suggests that her eventual marriage will require their abandonment. This quiet feminism is characteristic of Montgomery's worldview.

**The Proper Scale of Ambition.** The novel endorses ambition scaled to life — [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s desire to "add some beauty" is grand enough to inspire and modest enough to fulfill. Grandiose ambition (conquest, fame) is absent from the novel's moral universe; the ambitions that matter are those that enrich the daily lives of real people.

**Ambition and [[community|Community]].** Ambition in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is never purely individual. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s aspirations benefit [[avonlea|Avonlea]]; [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s medical ambitions will serve patients. The novel insists that worthy ambition is ambition that gives as much as it takes.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]] vs. [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]].** [[jane-andrews|Jane]]'s contentment and [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s striving represent two valid orientations. The novel prefers [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s — it is, after all, an [[anne-shirley|Anne]] novel — but it does not condemn [[jane-andrews|Jane]]'s. Both are ways of being in the world, and Montgomery's [[generous|generosity]] allows for both.

**[[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s Pragmatic Ambition.** [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]]'s medical aspirations are more [[tradition|convention]]ally ambitious than [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s aesthetic ones, but they share the same moral structure: both are ambitions in service of others. Their compatibility on this point is one of the quiet foundations of their relationship.
