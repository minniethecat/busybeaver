# Stephen Irving

The absent father of [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] and the long-lost [[romance|love]]r of [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] — a man whose quarter-century of [[faith|fidelity]] and whose eventual return to [[avonlea|Avonlea]] provides the novel's most romantic and emotionally satisfying conclusion.

## Character Overview

Stephen Irving is a figure of [[love|romance]] and regret. Twenty-five years before the novel opens, he quarreled [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] over a trivial matter; pride on both sides prevented [[forgiveness|reconciliation]], and he left for the United States, where he built a life and raised a son, [[paul-irving|Paul]]. His [[romance|love]] for [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] never dimmed, and when fate — through [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and his own son — brings him back into her orbit, he seizes the second chance [[humor|wit]]h the eagerness of a man who has waited half a lifetime.

His physical appearance surprises [[anne-shirley|Anne]]: he has "just the face for a hero of [[love|romance]]" (041-126), and the observation that [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]]'s actual [[romance|love]]r "had not looked the part" (041-130) is a gentle reminder that [[love|romance]] in real life does not always conform to literary type.

## Role in the Story

Stephen Irving's return and [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] is the novel's culminating romantic plot. His presence is felt throughout through [[paul-irving|Paul]] — the son who shares his father's sensitive [[nature-appreciation|nature]] and who, unknowingly, becomes the agent of his father's happiness.

## Relationships

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]]** is Stephen's lost [[romance|love]] and eventual wife. Their reunion after twenty-five years is the novel's most powerful argument for the [[patience|endurance]] of true [[romance|love]] and the necessity of forgiveness.

**[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]** is Stephen's son, the boy whose [[belonging|place]]ment in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[humor|wit]]h his grandmother sets the entire [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] plot in motion.

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is the catalyst — her discovery of [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] and her recognition of the connection between [[paul-irving|Paul]] and the "old [[love|romance]]" (031-064) makes the reunion possible.

## Key Scenes

**The Hero's Face (Chapter 41).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s observation that Stephen Irving has "just the face for a hero of [[love|romance]]" (041-126) introduces him as the embodiment of the romantic ideal.

**The Wedding (Chapter 42).** The marriage of Stephen and [[miss-lavendar|Miss Lavendar]] is the novel's emotional climax — "Yes, this was [[love|romance]], the very" essence of it (042-008).

## Character Analysis

Stephen Irving is less a fully realized character than an embodiment of romantic [[faith|fidelity]]. His twenty-five-year [[love|devotion]] to the memory of a lost quarrel — and his eagerness to mend what was broken — make him the novel's ideal of masculine constancy. He is the proof that [[love|romance]] is not merely a youthful indulgence but a force that can shape an entire life.
