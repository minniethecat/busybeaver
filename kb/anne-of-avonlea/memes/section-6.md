[006-001] After what seemed to her a year . . . about ten seconds in reality . .

[006-002] . she said faintly, “Take your Testaments, please,” and sank

[006-003] breathlessly into her chair under cover of the rustle and clatter of

[006-004] desk lids that followed. While the children read their verses [[anne-shirley|Anne]]

[006-005] marshalled her shaky wits into order and looked over the array of

[006-006] little pilgrims to the Grownup Land.

[006-007] Most of them were, of course, quite well known to her. Her own

[006-008] classmates had passed out in the preceding year but the rest had all

[006-009] gone to school with her, excepting the primer class and ten newcomers

[006-010] to [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. Anne secretly felt more interest in these ten than in those

[006-011] whose possibilities were already fairly well mapped out to her. To be

[006-012] sure, they might be just as commonplace as the rest; but on the other

[006-013] hand there _might_ be a genius among them. It was a thrilling idea.

[006-014] Sitting by himself at a corner desk was [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]. He had a dark,

[006-015] sullen little face, and was staring at Anne with a hostile expression

[006-016] in his black eyes. Anne instantly made up her mind that she would win

[006-017] that boy’s [[love|affection]] and discomfit the Pyes utterly.

[006-018] In the other corner another strange boy was sitting with Arty Sloane. .

[006-019] . a jolly looking little chap, with a snub nose, freckled face, and

[006-020] big, light blue eyes, fringed with whitish lashes . . . probably the

[006-021] Don_nell_ boy; and if resemblance went for anything, his sister was

[006-022] sitting across the aisle with Mary Bell. Anne wondered what sort of

[006-023] mother the child had, to send her to school dressed as she was. She

[006-024] wore a faded pink silk dress, trimmed with a great deal of cotton lace,

[006-025] soiled white kid slippers, and silk stockings. Her sandy hair was

[006-026] tortured into innumerable kinky and unnatural curls, surmounted by a

[006-027] flamboyant bow of pink ribbon bigger than her head. Judging from her

[006-028] expression she was very well satisfied with herself.

[006-029] A pale little thing, with smooth ripples of fine, silky, fawn-colored

[006-030] hair flowing over her shoulders, must, Anne thought, be [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]],

[006-031] whose parents had formerly lived in the [[newbridge|Newbridge]] school district, but,

[006-032] by reason of hauling their house fifty yards north of its old site were

[006-033] now in Avonlea. Three pallid little girls crowded into one seat were

[006-034] certainly Cottons; and there was no doubt that the small beauty with

[006-035] the long brown curls and hazel eyes, who was casting coquettish looks

[006-036] at Jack Gills over the edge of her Testament, was Prillie Rogerson,

[006-037] whose father had recently married a second wife and brought Prillie

[006-038] [[belonging|home]] from her grandmother’s in [[grafton|Grafton]]. A tall, awkward girl in a back

[006-039] seat, who seemed to have too many feet and hands, Anne could not place

[006-040] at all, but later on discovered that her name was [[barbara-shaw|Barbara Shaw]] and that

[006-041] she had come to live with an Avonlea aunt. She was also to find that if

[006-042] Barbara ever managed to walk down the aisle without falling over her

[006-043] own or somebody else’s feet the Avonlea scholars wrote the unusual fact

[006-044] up on the porch wall to commemorate it.

[006-045] But when Anne’s eyes met those of the boy at the front desk facing her

[006-046] own, a queer little thrill went over her, as if she had found her

[006-047] genius. She knew this must be [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] and that [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]]

[006-048] had been right for once when she prophesied that he would be unlike the

[006-049] Avonlea children. More than that, Anne realized that he was unlike

[006-050] other children anywhere, and that there was a soul subtly akin to her

[006-051] own gazing at her out of the very dark blue eyes that were watching her

[006-052] so intently.

[006-053] She knew Paul was ten but he looked no more than eight. He had the most

[006-054] beautiful little face she had ever seen in a child . . . features of

[006-055] exquisite delicacy and refinement, framed in a halo of chestnut curls.

[006-056] His mouth was delicious, being full without pouting, the crimson lips

[006-057] just softly touching and curving into finely finished little corners

[006-058] that narrowly escaped being dimpled. He had a sober, grave, meditative

[006-059] expression, as if his spirit was much older than his body; but when

[006-060] Anne smiled softly at him it vanished in a sudden answering smile,

[006-061] which seemed an illumination of his whole being, as if some lamp had

[006-062] suddenly kindled into flame inside of him, irradiating him from top to

[006-063] toe. Best of all, it was involuntary, born of no external effort or

[006-064] motive, but simply the outflashing of a hidden personality, rare and

[006-065] fine and sweet. With a quick interchange of smiles Anne and Paul were

[006-066] fast friends forever before a word had passed between them.

[006-067] The day went by like a dream. Anne could never clearly recall it

[006-068] afterwards. It almost seemed as if it were not she who was teaching but

[006-069] somebody else. She heard classes and worked sums and set copies

[006-070] mechanically. The children behaved quite well; only two cases of

[006-071] discipline occurred. Morley Andrews was caught driving a pair of

[006-072] trained crickets in the aisle. Anne stood Morley on the platform for an

[006-073] hour and . . . which Morley felt much more keenly . . . confiscated his

[006-074] crickets. She put them in a box and on the way from school set them

[006-075] free in [[violet-vale|Violet Vale]]; but Morley believed, then and ever afterwards,

[006-076] that she took them home and kept them for her own amusement.

[006-077] The other culprit was Anthony Pye, who poured the last drops of water

[006-078] from his slate bottle down the back of Aurelia Clay’s neck. Anne kept

[006-079] Anthony in at recess and talked to him about what was expected of

[006-080] gentlemen, admonishing him that they never poured water down ladies’

[006-081] necks. She wanted all her boys to be gentlemen, she said. Her little

[006-082] lecture was quite kind and touching; but unfortunately Anthony remained

[006-083] absolutely untouched. He listened to her in silence, with the same

[006-084] sullen expression, and whistled scornfully as he went out. Anne sighed;

[006-085] and then cheered herself up by remembering that winning a Pye’s

[006-086] affections, like the building of Rome, wasn’t the work of a day. In

[006-087] fact, it was doubtful whether some of the Pyes had any affections to

[006-088] win; but Anne hoped better things of Anthony, who looked as if he might

[006-089] be a rather nice boy if one ever got behind his sullenness.

[006-090] When school was dismissed and the children had gone Anne dropped

[006-091] wearily into her chair. Her head ached and she felt woefully

[006-092] discouraged. There was no real reason for [[despair|discouragement]], since nothing

[006-093] very dreadful had occurred; but Anne was very tired and inclined to

[006-094] believe that she would never learn to like teaching. And how terrible

[006-095] it would be to be doing something you didn’t like every day for . . .

[006-096] well, say forty years. Anne was of two minds whether to have her cry

[006-097] out then and there, or wait till she was safely in her own white room

[006-098] at home. Before she could decide there was a click of heels and a

[006-099] silken swish on the porch floor, and Anne found herself confronted by a

[006-100] lady whose appearance made her recall a recent criticism of Mr.

[006-101] [[mr-harrison|Harrison]]’s on an overdressed female he had seen in a [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]]

[006-102] store. “She looked like a head-on collision between a fashion plate and

[006-103] a nightmare.”

[006-104] The newcomer was gorgeously arrayed in a pale blue summer silk, puffed,

[006-105] frilled, and shirred wherever puff, frill, or shirring could possibly

[006-106] be placed. Her head was surmounted by a huge white chiffon hat,

[006-107] bedecked with three long but rather stringy ostrich feathers. A veil of

[006-108] pink chiffon, lavishly sprinkled with huge black dots, hung like a

[006-109] flounce from the hat brim to her shoulders and floated off in two airy

[006-110] streamers behind her. She wore all the jewelry that could be crowded on

[006-111] one small woman, and a very strong odor of perfume attended her.

[006-112] “I am Mrs. Don_nell_ . . . Mrs. H. B. Don_nell_,” announced this

[006-113] vision, “and I have come in to see you about something Clarice Almira

[006-114] told me when she came home to dinner today. It annoyed me

[006-115] _excessively_.”

[006-116] “I’m sorry,” faltered Anne, vainly trying to recollect any incident of

[006-117] the morning connected with the Donnell children.

[006-118] “Clarice Almira told me that you pronounced our name _Don_nell. Now,

[006-119] Miss Shirley, the correct pronunciation of our name is Don_nell_ . . .

[006-120] accent on the last syllable. I _hope_ you’ll remember this in future.”

[006-121] “I’ll try to,” gasped Anne, choking back a wild desire to laugh. “I

[006-122] know by experience that it’s very unpleasant to have one’s name

[006-123] _spelled_ wrong and I suppose it must be even worse to have it

[006-124] pronounced wrong.”

[006-125] “Certainly it is. And Clarice Almira also informed me that you call my

[006-126] son Jacob.”

[006-127] “He told me his name was Jacob,” protested Anne.

[006-128] “I might have expected that,” said Mrs. H. B. Don_nell_, in a tone

[006-129] which implied that gratitude in children was not to be looked for in

[006-130] this degenerate age. “That boy has such plebeian tastes, Miss Shirley.

[006-131] When he was born I wanted to call him St. Clair . . . it sounds _so_

[006-132] aristocratic, doesn’t it? But his father insisted he should be called

[006-133] Jacob after his uncle. I yielded, because Uncle Jacob was a rich old

[006-134] bachelor. And what do you think, Miss Shirley? When our innocent boy

[006-135] was five years old Uncle Jacob actually went and got married and now he

[006-136] has three boys of his own. Did you ever hear of such ingratitude? The

[006-137] moment the invitation to the wedding . . . for he had the impertinence

[006-138] to send us an invitation, Miss Shirley . . . came to the house I said,

[006-139] ‘No more Jacobs for me, thank you.’ From that day I called my son St.

[006-140] Clair and St. Clair I am determined he shall be called. His father

[006-141] obstinately continues to call him Jacob, and the boy himself has a

[006-142] perfectly unaccountable preference for the vulgar name. But St. Clair

[006-143] he is and St. Clair he shall remain. You will kindly remember this,

[006-144] Miss Shirley, will you not? _Thank_ you. I told Clarice Almira that I

[006-145] was sure it was only a misunderstanding and that a word would set it

[006-146] right. Don_nell_. . . accent on the last syllable . . . and St. Clair .

[006-147] . . on _no_ account Jacob. You’ll remember? _Thank_ you.”

[006-148] When Mrs. H. B. Don_nell_ had skimmed away Anne locked the school door

[006-149] and went home. At the foot of the hill she found Paul Irving by the

[006-150] [[birch-path|Birch Path]]. He held out to her a cluster of the dainty little wild

[006-151] orchids which Avonlea children called “rice lillies.”

[006-152] “Please, teacher, I found these in Mr. Wright’s field,” he said shyly,

[006-153] “and I came back to give them to you because I thought you were the

[006-154] kind of lady that would like them, and because . . .” he lifted his big

[006-155] beautiful eyes . . . “I like you, teacher.”

[006-156] “You darling,” said Anne, taking the fragrant spikes. As if Paul’s

[006-157] words had been a spell of magic, discouragement and weariness passed

[006-158] from her spirit, and hope upwelled in her heart like a dancing

[006-159] fountain. She went through the Birch Path light-footedly, attended by

[006-160] the sweetness of her orchids as by a benediction.

[006-161] “Well, how did you get along?” [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] wanted to know.

[006-162] “Ask me that a month later and I may be able to tell you. I can’t now .

[006-163] . . I don’t know myself . . . I’m too near it. My thoughts feel as if

[006-164] they had been all stirred up until they were thick and muddy. The only

[006-165] thing I feel really sure of having accomplished today is that I taught

[006-166] Cliffie Wright that A is A. He never knew it before. Isn’t it something

[006-167] to have started a soul along a path that may end in Shakespeare and

[006-168] Paradise Lost?”
