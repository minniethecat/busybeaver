# Friendship

The sustaining relational framework of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — celebrated by [[anne-shirley|Anne]] as one of life's greatest blessings and explored through her bonds [[humor|wit]]h [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]], [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]].

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, friendship is not a casual social arrangement but a sacred bond that "beautifies life so much" (021-116). [[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates the novel's philosophy in conversation [[humor|wit]]h [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]: "True friendship is a very helpful thing indeed . . . and we ought to be very careful not to defile it" (021-118). Montgomery presents friendship as a moral category — something that demands sincerity, effort, and careful tending. The novel distinguishes sharply between "real friendship" and "intimacy that has nothing of real friendship in it" (021-121), establishing an ethical standard for human connection.

The concept reaches its fullest expression in the recurring motif of "kindred spirits" — those rare individuals [[humor|wit]]h whom one shares an intuitive, almost mystical [[wisdom|understanding]]. As [[anne-shirley|Anne]] reflects, "If we have friends we should think of them as sacred things" (021-129), then friendship would be "the most beautiful thing in the world" (021-129).

## Examples in the Text

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] (throughout).** The friendship between [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]] is the novel's central relationship. Their bond survives distance, new responsibilities, and [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s eventual romantic attachment to [[fred-wright|Fred Wright]]. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s willingness to understand [[diana-barry|Diana]]'s feelings — even those she has not yet experienced herself — demonstrates friendship's capacity for empathetic imagination (021-136).

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] (Chapter 21).** The conversation between [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[mrs-allan|the minister's wife]] about friendship constitutes the novel's most explicit philosophical treatment of the theme. [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] articulates the danger of "desecration to call *that* friendship" when speaking of shallow social connections (021-127), and the two women share "[[ambition|dreams]] of friendship and ambition" (021-136) in a scene that models intergenerational female friendship.

**The "Kindred Spirit" Bonds.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] discovers in [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]] two new "kindred spirits" whose friendship enriches her life immeasurably. With [[paul-irving|Paul]], she shares the geography of imagination (021-022); [[humor|wit]]h [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], she experiences "one of those fervent, helpful friendships possible only between a woman who has kept the freshness of youth in her heart and soul, and a girl whose imagination and intuition supplied the [[belonging|place]] of experience" (032-080).

**Friendship Endures (Chapter 27).** The novel does not present friendship as effortless. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s relationships are tested by mis[[wisdom|understanding]], distance, and the competing claims of other [[duty|obligation]]s. Yet each test ultimately strengthens rather than weakens the bond, looking toward "friendship and perhaps some distant day" (027-114) of even deeper connection.

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s Integration (Chapter 4).** Even the initially misanthropic [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] illustrates the novel's thesis that friendship is transformative. His gradual movement from rejecting "all overtures of friendship" (004-112) to becoming an integral part of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s social world demonstrates Montgomery's belief that friendship is both universally needed and universally available.

## Thematic Significance

**Friendship as Moral [[education|Education]].** Through her friendships, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] learns patience, forgiveness, and the art of putting another's needs before her own. Her relationship [[humor|wit]]h the mischievous [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]], while not a friendship of equals, teaches similar lessons in [[patience|forbearance]] and [[wisdom|understanding]].

**The Social Fabric of [[avonlea|Avonlea]].** Friendship in the novel extends beyond individual relationships to form the very fabric of [[neighbor|community]] life. The bonds between women — [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] (despite friction), [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] — create networks of mutual support that sustain the village.

**Friendship and Growth.** Each of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s significant friendships marks a stage in her development. [[diana-barry|Diana]] represents the loyal companion of girlhood; [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], the intellectual equal and future partner; [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], the imaginative [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]]; [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]], the mentor-friend who bridges youth and maturity.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**The Superficial Alternative.** [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] warns that "the name of friendship is often desecrated" by being applied to relationships that lack "sincerity" and have "nothing of real friendship in them" (021-120, 021-121). The novel implicitly contrasts [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s deep, intentional friendships [[humor|wit]]h the shallow social connections that characterize less thoughtful characters.

**Solitude as Contrast.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s capacity for [[loneliness|solitude]] — walking alone through the beechlands, her imagination peopling her path (032-097) — provides the counterpoint to friendship. She does not need constant companionship because her inner world is rich; this very richness makes her friendships deeper, chosen rather than desperate.
