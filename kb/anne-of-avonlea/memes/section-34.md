[034-001] “Maybe if you didn’t pull your plants up by the roots every other day

[034-002] to see how they’re getting on ‘at the other end,’ they’d do better,”

[034-003] said [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] sarcastically.

[034-004] “I only pulled six of them up,” protested [[davy-keith|Davy]]. “I wanted to see if

[034-005] there was grubs at the roots. Milty Boulter said if it wasn’t the

[034-006] moon’s fault it must be grubs. But I only found one grub. He was a

[034-007] great big juicy curly grub. I put him on a stone and got another stone

[034-008] and smashed him flat. He made a jolly sqush I tell you. I was sorry

[034-009] there wasn’t more of them. [[dora-keith|Dora]]’s garden was planted same time’s mine

[034-010] and her things are growing all right. It _can’t_ be the moon,” Davy

[034-011] concluded in a reflective tone.

[034-012] “Marilla, look at that apple tree,” said [[anne-shirley|Anne]]. “Why, the thing is

[034-013] human. It is reaching out long arms to pick its own pink skirts

[034-014] daintily up and provoke us to admiration.”

[034-015] “Those Yellow Duchess trees always bear well,” said Marilla

[034-016] complacently. “That tree’ll be loaded this year. I’m real glad. . .

[034-017] they’re great for pies.”

[034-018] But neither Marilla nor Anne nor anybody else was fated to make pies

[034-019] out of Yellow Duchess apples that year.

[034-020] The twenty-third of May came . . . an unseasonably warm day, as none

[034-021] realized more keenly than Anne and her little beehive of pupils,

[034-022] sweltering over fractions and syntax in the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] schoolroom. A hot

[034-023] breeze blew all the forenoon; but after noon hour it died away into a

[034-024] heavy stillness. At half past three Anne heard a low rumble of thunder.

[034-025] She promptly dismissed school at once, so that the children might get

[034-026] [[belonging|home]] before the storm came.

[034-027] As they went out to the playground Anne perceived a certain shadow and

[034-028] gloom over the world in spite of the fact that the sun was still

[034-029] shining brightly. [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]] caught her hand nervously.

[034-030] “Oh, teacher, look at that awful cloud!”

[034-031] Anne looked and gave an exclamation of dismay. In the northwest a mass

[034-032] of cloud, such as she had never in all her life beheld before, was

[034-033] rapidly rolling up. It was dead black, save where its curled and

[034-034] fringed edges showed a ghastly, livid white. There was something about

[034-035] it indescribably menacing as it gloomed up in the clear blue sky; now

[034-036] and again a bolt of lightning shot across it, followed by a savage

[034-037] growl. It hung so low that it almost seemed to be touching the tops of

[034-038] the wooded hills.

[034-039] [[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]] came clattering up the hill in his truck wagon,

[034-040] urging his team of grays to their utmost speed. He pulled them to a

[034-041] halt opposite the school.

[034-042] “Guess [[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe]]’s hit it for once in his life, Anne,” he shouted. “His

[034-043] storm’s coming a leetle ahead of time. Did ye ever see the like of that

[034-044] cloud? Here, all you young ones, that are going my way, pile in, and

[034-045] those that ain’t scoot for the post office if ye’ve more’n a quarter of

[034-046] a mile to go, and stay there till the shower’s over.”

[034-047] Anne caught Davy and Dora by the hands and flew down the hill, along

[034-048] the [[birch-path|Birch Path]], and past [[violet-vale|Violet Vale]] and [[willowmere|Willowmere]], as fast as the

[034-049] twins’ fat legs could go. They reached [[green-gables|Green Gables]] not a moment too

[034-050] soon and were joined at the door by Marilla, who had been hustling her

[034-051] ducks and chickens under shelter. As they dashed into the kitchen the

[034-052] light seemed to vanish, as if blown out by some mighty breath; the

[034-053] awful cloud rolled over the sun and a darkness as of late twilight fell

[034-054] across the world. At the same moment, with a crash of thunder and a

[034-055] blinding glare of lightning, the hail swooped down and blotted the

[034-056] landscape out in one white fury.

[034-057] Through all the clamor of the storm came the thud of torn branches

[034-058] striking the house and the sharp crack of breaking glass. In three

[034-059] minutes every pane in the west and north windows was broken and the

[034-060] hail poured in through the apertures covering the floor with stones,

[034-061] the smallest of which was as big as a hen’s egg. For three quarters of

[034-062] an hour the storm raged unabated and no one who underwent it ever

[034-063] forgot it. Marilla, for once in her life shaken out of her composure by

[034-064] sheer terror, knelt by her rocking chair in a corner of the kitchen,

[034-065] gasping and sobbing between the deafening thunder peals. Anne, white as

[034-066] paper, had dragged the sofa away from the window and sat on it with a

[034-067] twin on either side. Davy at the first crash had howled, “Anne, Anne,

[034-068] is it the Judgment Day? Anne, Anne, I never _meant_ to be naughty,” and

[034-069] then had buried his face in Anne’s lap and kept it there, his little

[034-070] body quivering. Dora, somewhat pale but quite composed, sat with her

[034-071] hand clasped in Anne’s, quiet and motionless. It is doubtful if an

[034-072] earthquake would have disturbed Dora.

[034-073] Then, almost as suddenly as it began, the storm ceased. The hail

[034-074] stopped, the thunder rolled and muttered away to the eastward, and the

[034-075] sun burst out merry and radiant over a world so changed that it seemed

[034-076] an absurd thing to think that a scant three quarters of an hour could

[034-077] have effected such a transformation.

[034-078] Marilla rose from her knees, weak and trembling, and dropped on her

[034-079] rocker. Her face was haggard and she looked ten years older.

[034-080] “Have we all come out of that alive?” she asked solemnly.

[034-081] “You bet we have,” piped Davy cheerfully, quite his own man again. “I

[034-082] wasn’t a bit scared either . . . only just at the first. It come on a

[034-083] fellow so sudden. I made up my mind quick as a wink that I wouldn’t

[034-084] fight Teddy Sloane Monday as I’d promised; but now maybe I will. Say,

[034-085] Dora, was you scared?”

[034-086] “Yes, I was a little scared,” said Dora primly, “but I held tight to

[034-087] Anne’s hand and said my prayers over and over again.”

[034-088] “Well, I’d have said my prayers too if I’d have thought of it,” said

[034-089] Davy; “but,” he added triumphantly, “you see I came through just as

[034-090] safe as you for all I didn’t say them.”

[034-091] Anne got Marilla a glassful of her potent currant wine . . . _how_

[034-092] potent it was Anne, in her earlier days, had had all too good reason to

[034-093] know . . . and then they went to the door to look out on the strange

[034-094] scene.

[034-095] Far and wide was a white carpet, knee deep, of hailstones; drifts of

[034-096] them were heaped up under the eaves and on the steps. When, three or

[034-097] four days later, those hailstones melted, the havoc they had wrought

[034-098] was plainly seen, for every green growing thing in the field or garden

[034-099] was cut off. Not only was every blossom stripped from the apple trees

[034-100] but great boughs and branches were wrenched away. And out of the two

[034-101] hundred trees set out by the Improvers by far the greater number were

[034-102] snapped off or torn to shreds.

[034-103] “Can it possibly be the same world it was an hour ago?” asked Anne,

[034-104] dazedly. “It _must_ have taken longer than that to play such havoc.”

[034-105] “The like of this has never been known in [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]],” said

[034-106] Marilla, “never. I remember when I was a girl there was a bad storm,

[034-107] but it was nothing to this. We’ll hear of terrible destruction, you may

[034-108] be sure.”

[034-109] “I do hope none of the children were caught out in it,” murmured Anne

[034-110] anxiously. As it was discovered later, none of the children had been,

[034-111] since all those who had any distance to go had taken Mr. Andrews’

[034-112] excellent advice and sought refuge at the post office.

[034-113] “There comes John Henry Carter,” said Marilla.

[034-114] John Henry came wading through the hailstones with a rather scared

[034-115] grin.

[034-116] “Oh, ain’t this awful, Miss Cuthbert? [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] sent me over to see

[034-117] if yous had come out all right.”

[034-118] “We’re none of us killed,” said Marilla grimly, “and none of the

[034-119] buildings was struck. I hope you got off equally well.”

[034-120] “Yas’m. Not quite so well, ma’am. We was struck. The lightning knocked

[034-121] over the kitchen chimbly and come down the flue and knocked over

[034-122] Ginger’s cage and tore a hole in the floor and went into the sullar.

[034-123] Yas’m.”

[034-124] “Was Ginger hurt?” queried Anne.

[034-125] “Yas’m. He was hurt pretty bad. He was killed.” Later on Anne went over

[034-126] to comfort Mr. Harrison. She found him sitting by the table, stroking

[034-127] Ginger’s gay dead body with a trembling hand.

[034-128] “Poor Ginger won’t call you any more names, Anne,” he said mournfully.

[034-129] Anne could never have imagined herself crying on Ginger’s account, but

[034-130] the tears came into her eyes.

[034-131] “He was all the company I had, Anne . . . and now he’s dead. Well,

[034-132] well, I’m an old fool to care so much. I’ll let on I don’t care. I know

[034-133] you’re going to say something sympathetic as soon as I stop talking . .

[034-134] . but don’t. If you did I’d cry like a baby. Hasn’t this been a

[034-135] terrible storm? I guess folks won’t laugh at Uncle Abe’s predictions

[034-136] again. Seems as if all the storms that he’s been prophesying all his

[034-137] life that never happened came all at once. Beats all how he struck the

[034-138] very day though, don’t it? Look at the mess we have here. I must hustle

[034-139] round and get some boards to patch up that hole in the floor.”

[034-140] Avonlea folks did nothing the next day but visit each other and compare

[034-141] damages. The roads were impassable for wheels by reason of the

[034-142] hailstones, so they walked or rode on horseback. The mail came late

[034-143] with ill tidings from all over the province. Houses had been struck,

[034-144] people killed and injured; the whole telephone and telegraph system had

[034-145] been disorganized, and any number of young stock exposed in the fields

[034-146] had perished.

[034-147] Uncle Abe waded out to the blacksmith’s forge early in the morning and

[034-148] spent the whole day there. It was Uncle Abe’s hour of triumph and he

[034-149] enjoyed it to the full. It would be doing Uncle Abe an injustice to say

[034-150] that he was glad the storm had happened; but since it had to be he was

[034-151] very glad he had predicted it . . . to the very day, too. Uncle Abe

[034-152] forgot that he had ever denied setting the day. As for the trifling

[034-153] discrepancy in the hour, that was nothing.

[034-154] [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] arrived at Green Gables in the evening and found Marilla and

[034-155] Anne busily engaged in nailing strips of oilcloth over the broken

[034-156] windows.

[034-157] “Goodness only knows when we’ll get glass for them,” said Marilla. “Mr.

[034-158] Barry went over to [[carmody|Carmody]] this afternoon but not a pane could he get

[034-159] for [[romance|love]] or money. Lawson and Blair were cleaned out by the Carmody

[034-160] people by ten o’clock. Was the storm bad at [[white-sands|White Sands]], Gilbert?”

[034-161] “I should say so. I was caught in the school with all the children and

[034-162] I thought some of them would go mad with fright. Three of them fainted,

[034-163] and two girls took hysterics, and Tommy Blewett did nothing but shriek

[034-164] at the top of his voice the whole time.”

[034-165] “I only squealed once,” said Davy proudly. “My garden was all smashed

[034-166] flat,” he continued mournfully, “but so was Dora’s,” he added in a tone

[034-167] which indicated that there was yet balm in Gilead.

[034-168] Anne came running down from the west gable.

[034-169] “Oh, Gilbert, have you heard the news? Mr. Levi Boulter’s old house was

[034-170] struck and burned to the ground. It seems to me that I’m dreadfully

[034-171] wicked to feel glad over _that_, when so much damage has been done. Mr.

[034-172] Boulter says he believes the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] magicked up that storm on

[034-173] purpose.”

[034-174] “Well, one thing is certain,” said Gilbert, laughing, “‘Observer’ has

[034-175] made Uncle Abe’s reputation as a weather prophet. ‘Uncle Abe’s storm’

[034-176] will go down in local history. It is a most extraordinary coincidence

[034-177] that it should have come on the very day we selected. I actually have a

[034-178] half guilty feeling, as if I really had ‘magicked’ it up. We may as

[034-179] well rejoice over the old house being removed, for there’s not much to

[034-180] rejoice over where our young trees are concerned. Not ten of them have

[034-181] escaped.”
