# Humor

The comic current that runs through *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — from the "awful funny" spectacle of dignitaries caught in awkward moments (010-020), to [[davy-keith|Davy]]'s mischievous observations, to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own self-deprecating recognition that "something funny is almost sure to happen" (014-034) in her daily life.

## Definition

Humor in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is the novel's most reliable narrative lubricant. Montgomery deploys it strategically: to relieve tension, to reveal character, and to hold the reader's [[love|affection]] for [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s small dramas. The [[laughter|humor]] is never cruel — it is the gentle, observational comedy of rural life, where "funny speeches" at public meetings (014-036) and the misadventures of children supply an endless stream of gentle amusement.

Humor also serves as a marker of character. Those who can laugh at themselves — [[anne-shirley|Anne]], [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]], [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] — are the novel's most sympathetic figures. Those who cannot — the [[laughter|humor]]less moralists and self-important villagers — are gently satirized. Montgomery's [[laughter|humor]] is democratic: it cuts across age, class, and station, finding comedy in the schoolroom, the parlor, and the orchard with equal [[love|affection]].

## Examples in the Text

**The [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] Meeting (Chapter 10).** The [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Avonlea Village Improvement Society]]'s inaugural meeting produces the observation that "though it's awful funny to think of it" (010-020), the Improvement Society is taken seriously by its members. The [[laughter|humor]] lies in the gap between high [[ambition|aspiration]] and humble reality — Montgomery's favorite comic territory.

**Public Oratory (Chapter 14).** Montgomery notes that "something funny is almost sure to happen" (014-034) at [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s public gatherings, "when they make funny speeches, which is probably" unintentional (014-036). The [[laughter|humor]] arises from the mismatch between the orator's earnestness and the absurdity of what actually emerges from their mouths. Montgomery treats this with [[love|affection]]ate tolerance rather than mockery.

**[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s Perspective (Chapter 24).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] reflects that "events of today have a funny side too. Perhaps when [[diana-barry|Diana]]" (024-036) — the [[laughter|humor]] is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own coping mechanism, a conscious choice to find the comic angle in trying circumstances. This is the mature [[laughter|humor]] of perspective, distinct from the slapstick of children's mishaps.

**The Self-Deprecating Reflection (Chapter 40).** "Isn't it funny and nice we should all know each other" (040-168) — the [[laughter|humor]] here is tender and communal, the recognition that [[avonlea|Avonlea]]'s web of relationships is both absurd and precious. Montgomery's [[laughter|humor]] reaches its highest note when it is indistinguishable from [[love|affection]].

## Thematic Significance

Humor in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is Montgomery's primary tool for managing tone. The novel deals with genuinely weighty themes — loneliness, poverty, ambition, [[death|loss]] — but never becomes oppressive because Montgomery consistently finds the comic angle. This is not evasion but artistic strategy: [[laughter|humor]] makes the serious moments more affecting by contrast.

Humor is also a democratizing force. In a novel concerned with hierarchy — teacher/student, adult/child, urban/rural — [[laughter|humor]] is the great leveler. A funny observation from [[davy-keith|Davy]] carries as much weight as one from [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]; [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s most endearing quality is her capacity to laugh at herself.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**Humor as Relief vs. Humor as Avoidance.** Montgomery's [[laughter|humor]] largely functions as relief — a release of tension that lets the reader breathe. But the novel also hints at characters who use [[laughter|humor]] to avoid genuine engagement. The distinction lies in intent: [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[laughter|humor]] is connective (it invites others to share the joke), while some villagers' wit is performative (it aims to impress).

**[[davy-keith|Davy]]'s Humor vs. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s Humor.** [[davy-keith|Davy Keith]] represents the novel's *unconscious* [[laughter|humor]] — he is funny without intending to be, his mischief producing comedy through the gap between his worldview and adult expectations. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s [[laughter|humor]] is increasingly *conscious* — she chooses to find funny angles, exercising what Montgomery presents as emotional intelligence.
