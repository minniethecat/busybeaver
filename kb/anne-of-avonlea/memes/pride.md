# Pride

A recurring moral theme in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — presented as a quality that can elevate character when it manifests as proper self-respect or damage relationships when it hardens into stubbornness and sulkiness.

## Definition

Montgomery treats pride [[humor|wit]]h careful discrimination in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*. The novel distinguishes between "proper pride" — the dignified self-respect that prevents a person from accepting unfair treatment — and destructive pride, which "and sulkiness make a very bad combination" (033-018). Pride is neither simply virtuous nor simply vicious; its moral valence depends on its object and its expression.

Throughout the narrative, characters are tested by pride — their own and others' — and the novel's wisdom lies in its nuanced treatment of an emotion that can be both armor and weapon.

## Examples in the Text

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s Pride (Chapter 5).** The irascible [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] illustrates pride's difficult edge. His "methods" are described as something "on which he prided himself" (005-116), and the irony is gentle but clear: pride in one's own crotchetiness is still pride, and it isolates as much as it dignifies.

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s Pride in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] (Chapter 10).** [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s "pride in her as she led him into the school" (010-129) represents pride in its most benevolent form — the [[laughter|joy]] a parent takes in a child's achievement. This pride is selfless, directed outward, and it enriches rather than isolates.

**[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s Loving Pride (Chapter 26).** Young [[paul-irving|Paul]] speaks of his father "[[humor|wit]]h loving pride" (026-171), and the phrase captures the novel's ideal: pride infused [[humor|wit]]h [[romance|love]], expressing admiration rather than self-regard. [[paul-irving|Paul]]'s pride in his father is generous rather than competitive.

**The Danger of Pride and Sulkiness (Chapter 33).** Montgomery's most direct moral statement on the subject — "pride and sulkiness make a very bad combination" (033-018) — appears in the context of a relationship damaged by stubborn refusal to reconcile. The [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] plotline turns on this [[wisdom|insight]]: a quarter-century of loneliness caused by pride that would not bend.

## Thematic Significance

**Pride as Obstacle to Reconciliation.** The novel's central [[love|romance]] plot — [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] and [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] — is a study in pride's destructive potential. Two people who [[romance|love]] each other spend twenty-five years apart because neither will take the first step toward [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. Pride is presented as the enemy of [[romance|love]].

**Proper Pride as Self-Respect.** The novel distinguishes between stubborn pride and "proper pride" — the refusal to accept unjust treatment that is a component of dignity. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s self-respect, which prevents her from being a doormat, is a form of pride the novel endorses.

**Pride and [[community|Community]].** Pride can isolate ([[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s early behavior) or connect ([[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s pride in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is shared [[humor|wit]]h the [[neighbor|community]]). The novel's moral algebra on pride is simple: pride in others builds bonds; pride in self builds walls.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] vs. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]].** [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]'s pride is self-directed and isolating; [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s pride in [[anne-shirley|Anne]] is other-directed and connecting. The contrast illustrates the novel's core distinction between pride that diminishes and pride that enlarges.

**[[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s Evolution.** [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s story arc is a journey from destructive pride (refusing to forgive) to redemptive humility (accepting Stephen's return). Her late happiness is the novel's most powerful argument against pride's tyranny.
