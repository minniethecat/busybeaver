[029-001] “What is the matter with it?” asked [[diana-barry|Diana]], curiosity overcoming

[029-002] delicacy.

[029-003] “I thought I was rubbing my freckle lotion on it, but I must have used

[029-004] that red dye [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] has for marking the pattern on her rugs,” was the

[029-005] despairing response. “What shall I do?”

[029-006] “Wash it off,” said Diana practically.

[029-007] “Perhaps it won’t wash off. First I dye my hair; then I dye my nose.

[029-008] Marilla cut my hair off when I dyed it but that remedy would hardly be

[029-009] practicable in this case. Well, this is another punishment for [[pride|vanity]]

[029-010] and I suppose I deserve it . . . though there’s not much comfort in

[029-011] _that_. It is really almost enough to make one believe in ill-luck,

[029-012] though [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says there is no such thing, because everything is

[029-013] foreordained.”

[029-014] Fortunately the dye washed off easily and [[anne-shirley|Anne]], somewhat consoled,

[029-015] betook herself to the east gable while Diana ran [[belonging|home]]. Presently Anne

[029-016] came down again, clothed and in her right mind. The muslin dress she

[029-017] had fondly hoped to wear was bobbing merrily about on the line outside,

[029-018] so she was forced to content herself with her black lawn. She had the

[029-019] fire on and the tea steeping when Diana returned; the latter wore _her_

[029-020] muslin, at least, and carried a covered platter in her hand.

[029-021] “Mother sent you this,” she said, lifting the cover and displaying a

[029-022] nicely carved and jointed chicken to Anne’s greatful eyes.

[029-023] The chicken was supplemented by light new bread, excellent butter and

[029-024] cheese, Marilla’s fruit cake and a dish of preserved plums, floating in

[029-025] their golden syrup as in congealed summer sunshine. There was a big

[029-026] bowlful of pink-and-white asters also, by way of decoration; yet the

[029-027] spread seemed very meager beside the elaborate one formerly prepared

[029-028] for Mrs. Morgan.

[029-029] Anne’s hungry guests, however, did not seem to think anything was

[029-030] lacking and they ate the simple viands with apparent enjoyment. But

[029-031] after the first few moments Anne thought no more of what was or was not

[029-032] on her bill of fare. Mrs. Morgan’s appearance might be somewhat

[029-033] disappointing, as even her loyal worshippers had been forced to admit

[029-034] to each other; but she proved to be a delightful conversationalist. She

[029-035] had traveled extensively and was an excellent storyteller. She had seen

[029-036] much of men and women, and crystalized her experiences into witty

[029-037] little sentences and epigrams which made her hearers feel as if they

[029-038] were listening to one of the people in clever books. But under all her

[029-039] sparkle there was a strongly felt undercurrent of true, womanly

[029-040] sympathy and kindheartedness which won [[love|affection]] as easily as her

[029-041] brilliancy won admiration. Nor did she monopolize the conversation. She

[029-042] could draw others out as skillfully and fully as she could talk

[029-043] herself, and Anne and Diana found themselves chattering freely to her.

[029-044] Mrs. Pendexter said little; she merely smiled with her lovely eyes and

[029-045] lips, and ate chicken and fruit cake and preserves with such exquisite

[029-046] grace that she conveyed the impression of dining on ambrosia and

[029-047] honeydew. But then, as Anne said to Diana later on, anybody so divinely

[029-048] beautiful as Mrs. Pendexter didn’t need to talk; it was enough for her

[029-049] just to _look_.

[029-050] After dinner they all had a walk through Lover’s Lane and [[violet-vale|Violet Vale]]

[029-051] and the [[birch-path|Birch Path]], then back through [[haunted-wood|the Haunted Wood]] to the Dryad’s

[029-052] Bubble, where they sat down and talked for a delightful last half hour.

[029-053] Mrs. Morgan wanted to know how the Haunted Wood came by its name, and

[029-054] laughed until she cried when she heard the story and Anne’s dramatic

[029-055] account of a certain memorable walk through it at the witching hour of

[029-056] twilight.

[029-057] “It has indeed been a feast of reason and flow of soul, hasn’t it?”

[029-058] said Anne, when her guests had gone and she and Diana were alone again.

[029-059] “I don’t know which I enjoyed more . . . listening to Mrs. Morgan or

[029-060] gazing at Mrs. Pendexter. I believe we had a nicer time than if we’d

[029-061] known they were coming and been cumbered with much serving. You must

[029-062] stay to tea with me, Diana, and we’ll talk it all over.”

[029-063] “Priscilla says Mrs. Pendexter’s husband’s sister is married to an

[029-064] English earl; and yet she took a second helping of the plum preserves,”

[029-065] said Diana, as if the two facts were somehow incompatible.

[029-066] “I daresay even the English earl himself wouldn’t have turned up his

[029-067] aristocratic nose at Marilla’s plum preserves,” said Anne proudly.

[029-068] Anne did not mention the misfortune which had befallen _her_ nose when

[029-069] she related the day’s history to Marilla that evening. But she took the

[029-070] bottle of freckle lotion and emptied it out of the window.

[029-071] “I shall never try any beautifying messes again,” she said, darkly

[029-072] resolute. “They may do for careful, deliberate people; but for anyone

[029-073] so hopelessly given over to making mistakes as I seem to be it’s

[029-074] tempting fate to meddle with them.”

[029-075] XXI

[029-076] Sweet [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]

[029-077] School opened and Anne returned to her work, with fewer theories but

[029-078] considerably more experience. She had several new pupils, six- and

[029-079] seven-year-olds just venturing, round-eyed, into a world of wonder.

[029-080] Among them were [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora]]. Davy sat with Milty Boulter, who had

[029-081] been going to school for a year and was therefore quite a man of the

[029-082] world. Dora had made a compact at Sunday School the previous Sunday to

[029-083] sit with Lily Sloane; but Lily Sloane not coming the first day, she was

[029-084] temporarily assigned to [[mirabel-cotton|Mirabel Cotton]], who was ten years old and

[029-085] therefore, in Dora’s eyes, one of the “big girls.”

[029-086] “I think school is great fun,” Davy told Marilla when he got home that

[029-087] night. “You said I’d find it hard to sit still and I did . . . you

[029-088] mostly do tell the truth, I notice . . . but you can wriggle your legs

[029-089] about under the desk and that helps a lot. It’s splendid to have so

[029-090] many boys to play with. I sit with Milty Boulter and he’s fine. He’s

[029-091] longer than me but I’m wider. It’s nicer to sit in the back seats but

[029-092] you can’t sit there till your legs grow long enough to touch the floor.

[029-093] Milty drawed a picture of Anne on his slate and it was awful ugly and I

[029-094] told him if he made pictures of Anne like that I’d lick him at recess.

[029-095] I thought first I’d draw one of him and put horns and a tail on it, but

[029-096] I was afraid it would hurt his feelings, and Anne says you should never

[029-097] hurt anyone’s feelings. It seems it’s dreadful to have your feelings

[029-098] hurt. It’s better to knock a boy down than hurt his feelings if you

[029-099] _must_ do something. Milty said he wasn’t scared of me but he’d just as

[029-100] soon call it somebody else to ‘blige me, so he rubbed out Anne’s name

[029-101] and printed [[barbara-shaw|Barbara Shaw]]’s under it. Milty doesn’t like Barbara ’cause

[029-102] she calls him a sweet little boy and once she patted him on his head.”

[029-103] Dora said primly that she liked school; but she was very quiet, even

[029-104] for her; and when at twilight Marilla bade her go upstairs to bed she

[029-105] hesitated and began to cry.

[029-106] “I’m . . . I’m frightened,” she sobbed. “I . . . I don’t want to go

[029-107] upstairs alone in the dark.”

[029-108] “What notion have you got into your head now?” demanded Marilla. “I’m

[029-109] sure you’ve gone to bed alone all summer and never been frightened

[029-110] before.”

[029-111] Dora still continued to cry, so Anne picked her up, cuddled her

[029-112] sympathetically, and whispered,

[029-113] “Tell Anne all about it, sweetheart. What are you frightened of?”

[029-114] “Of . . . of Mirabel Cotton’s uncle,” sobbed Dora. “Mirabel Cotton told

[029-115] me all about her family today in school. Nearly everybody in her family

[029-116] has died . . . all her grandfathers and grandmothers and ever so many

[029-117] uncles and aunts. They have a habit of dying, Mirabel says. Mirabel’s

[029-118] awful proud of having so many dead relations, and she told me what they

[029-119] all died of, and what they said, and how they looked in their coffins.

[029-120] And Mirabel says one of her uncles was seen walking around the house

[029-121] after he was buried. Her mother saw him. I don’t mind the rest so much

[029-122] but I can’t help thinking about that uncle.”

[029-123] Anne went upstairs with Dora and sat by her until she fell asleep. The

[029-124] next day Mirabel Cotton was kept in at recess and “gently but firmly”

[029-125] given to understand that when you were so unfortunate as to possess an

[029-126] uncle who persisted in walking about houses after he had been decently

[029-127] interred it was not in good taste to talk about that eccentric

[029-128] gentleman to your deskmate of tender years. Mirabel thought this very

[029-129] harsh. The Cottons had not much to boast of. How was she to keep up her

[029-130] prestige among her schoolmates if she were forbidden to make capital

[029-131] out of the family ghost?

[029-132] September slipped by into a gold and crimson graciousness of October.

[029-133] One Friday evening Diana came over.

[029-134] “I’d a letter from Ella Kimball today, Anne, and she wants us to go

[029-135] over to tea tomorrow afternoon to meet her cousin, Irene Trent, from

[029-136] town. But we can’t get one of our horses to go, for they’ll all be in

[029-137] use tomorrow, and your pony is lame . . . so I suppose we can’t go.”

[029-138] “Why can’t we walk?” suggested Anne. “If we go straight back through

[029-139] the woods we’ll strike the [[grafton|West Grafton]] road not far from the Kimball

[029-140] place. I was through that way last winter and I know the road. It’s no

[029-141] more than four miles and we won’t have to walk home, for Oliver Kimball

[029-142] will be sure to drive us. He’ll be only too glad of the excuse, for he

[029-143] goes to see Carrie Sloane and they say his father will hardly ever let

[029-144] him have a horse.”

[029-145] It was accordingly arranged that they should walk, and the following

[029-146] afternoon they set out, going by way of Lover’s Lane to the back of the

[029-147] Cuthbert farm, where they found a road leading into the heart of acres

[029-148] of glimmering beech and maple woods, which were all in a wondrous glow

[029-149] of flame and gold, lying in a great purple stillness and peace.

[029-150] “It’s as if the year were kneeling to pray in a vast cathedral full of

[029-151] mellow stained light, isn’t it?” said Anne dreamily. “It doesn’t seem

[029-152] right to hurry through it, does it? It seems irreverent, like running

[029-153] in a church.”

[029-154] “We _must_ hurry though,” said Diana, glancing at her watch. “We’ve

[029-155] left ourselves little enough time as it is.”

[029-156] “Well, I’ll walk fast but don’t ask me to talk,” said Anne, quickening

[029-157] her pace. “I just want to drink the day’s [[beauty|loveliness]] in . . . I feel as

[029-158] if she were holding it out to my lips like a cup of airy wine and I’ll

[029-159] take a sip at every step.”

[029-160] Perhaps it was because she was so absorbed in “drinking it in” that

[029-161] Anne took the left turning when they came to a fork in the road. She

[029-162] should have taken the right, but ever afterward she counted it the most

[029-163] fortunate mistake of her life. They came out finally to a lonely,

[029-164] grassy road, with nothing in sight along it but ranks of spruce

[029-165] saplings.

[029-166] “Why, where are we?” exclaimed Diana in bewilderment. “This isn’t the

[029-167] West Grafton road.”

[029-168] “No, it’s the base line road in Middle Grafton,” said Anne, rather

[029-169] shamefacedly. “I must have taken the wrong turning at the fork. I don’t

[029-170] know where we are exactly, but we must be all of three miles from

[029-171] Kimballs’ still.”

[029-172] “Then we can’t get there by five, for it’s half past four now,” said

[029-173] Diana, with a despairing look at her watch. “We’ll arrive after they

[029-174] have had their tea, and they’ll have all the bother of getting ours

[029-175] over again.”
