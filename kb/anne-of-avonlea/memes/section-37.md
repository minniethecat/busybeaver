[037-001] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] kept her own counsel and Mrs. [[mr-harrison|Harrison]] never knew that her

[037-002] gratitude found its way to its object. Anne felt rather bewildered over

[037-003] the far-reaching consequences of those foolish “notes.” They had

[037-004] reconciled a man to his wife and made the reputation of a prophet.

[037-005] [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] was in the [[green-gables|Green Gables]] kitchen. She had been telling the

[037-006] whole story to [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]].

[037-007] “Well, and how do you like [[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]]?” she asked Anne.

[037-008] “Very much. I think she’s a real nice little woman.”

[037-009] “That’s exactly what she is,” said Mrs. Rachel with emphasis, “and as

[037-010] I’ve just been sayin’ to Marilla, I think we ought all to overlook Mr.

[037-011] Harrison’s peculiarities for her sake and try to make her feel at [[belonging|home]]

[037-012] here, that’s what. Well, I must get back. Thomas’ll be wearying for me.

[037-013] I get out a little since Eliza came and he’s seemed a lot better these

[037-014] past few days, but I never like to be long away from him. I hear

[037-015] [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] has resigned from [[white-sands|White Sands]]. He’ll be off to college

[037-016] in the fall, I suppose.”

[037-017] Mrs. Rachel looked sharply at Anne, but Anne was bending over a sleepy

[037-018] [[davy-keith|Davy]] nodding on the sofa and nothing was to be read in her face. She

[037-019] carried Davy away, her oval girlish cheek pressed against his curly

[037-020] yellow head. As they went up the stairs Davy flung a tired arm about

[037-021] Anne’s neck and gave her a warm hug and a sticky kiss.

[037-022] “You’re awful nice, Anne. Milty Boulter wrote on his slate today and

[037-023] showed it to Jennie Sloane,

[037-024] “‘Roses red and vi’lets blue,

[037-025] Sugar’s sweet, and so are you”

[037-026] and that ’spresses my feelings for you ezackly, Anne.”

[037-027] XXVI

[037-028] Around the Bend

[037-029] [[thomas-lynde|Thomas Lynde]] faded out of life as quietly and unobtrusively as he had

[037-030] lived it. His wife was a tender, patient, unwearied nurse. Sometimes

[037-031] Rachel had been a little hard on her Thomas in health, when his

[037-032] slowness or meekness had provoked her; but when he became ill no voice

[037-033] could be lower, no hand more gently skillful, no vigil more

[037-034] uncomplaining.

[037-035] “You’ve been a good wife to me, Rachel,” he once said simply, when she

[037-036] was sitting by him in the dusk, holding his thin, blanched old hand in

[037-037] her work-hardened one. “A good wife. I’m sorry I ain’t leaving you

[037-038] better off; but the children will look after you. They’re all smart,

[037-039] capable children, just like their mother. A good mother . . . a good

[037-040] woman . . . .”

[037-041] He had fallen asleep then, and the next morning, just as the white dawn

[037-042] was creeping up over the pointed firs in the hollow, Marilla went

[037-043] softly into the east gable and wakened Anne.

[037-044] “Anne, Thomas Lynde is gone . . . their hired boy just brought the

[037-045] word. I’m going right down to Rachel.”

[037-046] On the day after Thomas Lynde’s funeral Marilla went about Green Gables

[037-047] with a strangely preoccupied air. Occasionally she looked at Anne,

[037-048] seemed on the point of saying something, then shook her head and

[037-049] buttoned up her mouth. After tea she went down to see Mrs. Rachel; and

[037-050] when she returned she went to the east gable, where Anne was correcting

[037-051] school exercises.

[037-052] “How is [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] tonight?” asked the latter.

[037-053] “She’s feeling calmer and more composed,” answered Marilla, sitting

[037-054] down on Anne’s bed . . . a proceeding which betokened some unusual

[037-055] mental excitement, for in Marilla’s code of household ethics to sit on

[037-056] a bed after it was made up was an unpardonable offense. “But she’s very

[037-057] lonely. Eliza had to go home today . . . her son isn’t well and she

[037-058] felt she couldn’t stay any longer.”

[037-059] “When I’ve finished these exercises I’ll run down and chat awhile with

[037-060] [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]],” said Anne. “I had intended to study some Latin composition

[037-061] tonight but it can wait.”

[037-062] “I suppose Gilbert Blythe is going to college in the fall,” said

[037-063] Marilla jerkily. “How would you like to go too, Anne?”

[037-064] Anne looked up in astonishment.

[037-065] “I would like it, of course, Marilla. But it isn’t possible.”

[037-066] “I guess it can be made possible. I’ve always felt that you should go.

[037-067] I’ve never felt easy to think you were [[generosity|giving]] it all up on my account.”

[037-068] “But Marilla, I’ve never been sorry for a moment that I stayed home.

[037-069] I’ve been so happy . . . Oh, these past two years have just been

[037-070] delightful.”

[037-071] “Oh, yes, I know you’ve been contented enough. But that isn’t the

[037-072] question exactly. You ought to go on with your [[teaching|education]]. You’ve saved

[037-073] enough to put you through one year at [[redmond-college|Redmond]] and the money the stock

[037-074] brought in will do for another year . . . and there’s scholarships and

[037-075] things you might win.”

[037-076] “Yes, but I can’t go, Marilla. Your eyes are better, of course; but I

[037-077] can’t leave you alone with the twins. They need so much looking after.”

[037-078] “I won’t be alone with them. That’s what I meant to discuss with you. I

[037-079] had a long talk with Rachel tonight. Anne, she’s feeling dreadful bad

[037-080] over a good many things. She’s not left very well off. It seems they

[037-081] mortgaged the farm eight years ago to give the youngest boy a start

[037-082] when he went west; and they’ve never been able to pay much more than

[037-083] the interest since. And then of course Thomas’ illness has cost a good

[037-084] deal, one way or another. The farm will have to be sold and Rachel

[037-085] thinks there’ll be hardly anything left after the bills are settled.

[037-086] She says she’ll have to go and live with Eliza and it’s breaking her

[037-087] heart to think of leaving [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. A woman of her age doesn’t make new

[037-088] friends and interests easy. And, Anne, as she talked about it the

[037-089] thought came to me that I would ask her to come and live with me, but I

[037-090] thought I ought to talk it over with you first before I said anything

[037-091] to her. If I had Rachel living with me you could go to college. How do

[037-092] you feel about it?”

[037-093] “I feel . . . as if . . . somebody . . . had handed me . . . the moon .

[037-094] . . and I didn’t know . . . exactly . . . what to do . . . with it,”

[037-095] said Anne dazedly. “But as for asking Mrs. Lynde to come here, that is

[037-096] for you to decide, Marilla. Do you think . . . are you sure . . . you

[037-097] would like it? Mrs. Lynde is a good woman and a kind neighbor, but . .

[037-098] . but . . .”

[037-099] “But she’s got her faults, you mean to say? Well, she has, of course;

[037-100] but I think I’d rather put up with far worse faults than see Rachel go

[037-101] away from Avonlea. I’d miss her terrible. She’s the only close friend

[037-102] I’ve got here and I’d be lost without her. We’ve been [[community|neighbors]] for

[037-103] forty-five years and we’ve never had a quarrel . . . though we came

[037-104] rather near it that time you flew at Mrs. Rachel for calling you homely

[037-105] and redhaired. Do you remember, Anne?”

[037-106] “I should think I do,” said Anne ruefully. “People don’t forget things

[037-107] like that. How I hated poor Mrs. Rachel at that moment!”

[037-108] “And then that ‘apology’ you made her. Well, you were a handful, in all

[037-109] conscience, Anne. I did feel so puzzled and bewildered how to manage

[037-110] you. Matthew understood you better.”

[037-111] “Matthew understood everything,” said Anne softly, as she always spoke

[037-112] of him.

[037-113] “Well, I think it could be managed so that Rachel and I wouldn’t clash

[037-114] at all. It always seemed to me that the reason two women can’t get

[037-115] along in one house is that they try to share the same kitchen and get

[037-116] in each other’s way. Now, if Rachel came here, she could have the north

[037-117] gable for her bedroom and the spare room for a kitchen as well as not,

[037-118] for we don’t really need a spare room at all. She could put her stove

[037-119] there and what furniture she wanted to keep, and be real comfortable

[037-120] and independent. She’ll have enough to live on of course...her

[037-121] children’ll see to that...so all I’d be giving her would be house room.

[037-122] Yes, Anne, far as I’m concerned I’d like it.”

[037-123] “Then ask her,” said Anne promptly. “I’d be very sorry myself to see

[037-124] Mrs. Rachel go away.”

[037-125] “And if she comes,” continued Marilla, “You can go to college as well

[037-126] as not. She’ll be company for me and she’ll do for the twins what I

[037-127] can’t do, so there’s no reason in the world why you shouldn’t go.”

[037-128] Anne had a long meditation at her window that night. Joy and regret

[037-129] struggled together in her heart. She had come at last . . . suddenly

[037-130] and unexpectedly . . . to the bend in the road; and college _was_

[037-131] around it, with a hundred rainbow hopes and visions; but Anne realized

[037-132] as well that when she rounded that curve she must leave many sweet

[037-133] things behind. . . all the little simple duties and interests which had

[037-134] grown so dear to her in the last two years and which she had glorified

[037-135] into beauty and delight by the enthusiasm she had put into them. She

[037-136] must give up her school . . . and she loved every one of her pupils,

[037-137] even the stupid and naughty ones. The mere thought of [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] made

[037-138] her wonder if Redmond were such a name to conjure with after all.

[037-139] “I’ve put out a lot of little roots these two years,” Anne told the

[037-140] moon, “and when I’m pulled up they’re going to hurt a great deal. But

[037-141] it’s best to go, I think, and, as Marilla says, there’s no good reason

[037-142] why I shouldn’t. I must get out all my ambitions and dust them.”

[037-143] Anne sent in her resignation the next day; and Mrs. Rachel, after a

[037-144] heart to heart talk with Marilla, gratefully accepted the offer of a

[037-145] home at Green Gables. She elected to remain in her own house for the

[037-146] summer, however; the farm was not to be sold until the fall and there

[037-147] were many arrangements to be made.

[037-148] “I certainly never thought of living as far off the road as Green

[037-149] Gables,” sighed Mrs. Rachel to herself. “But really, Green Gables

[037-150] doesn’t seem as out of the world as it used to do . . . Anne has lots

[037-151] of company and the twins make it real lively. And anyhow, I’d rather

[037-152] live at the bottom of a well than leave Avonlea.”

[037-153] These two decisions being noised abroad speedily ousted the arrival of

[037-154] Mrs. Harrison in popular gossip. Sage heads were shaken over Marilla

[037-155] Cuthbert’s rash step in asking Mrs. Rachel to live with her. People

[037-156] opined that they wouldn’t get on together. They were both “too fond of

[037-157] their own way,” and many doleful predictions were made, none of which

[037-158] disturbed the parties in question at all. They had come to a clear and

[037-159] distinct [[wisdom|understanding]] of the respective duties and rights of their new

[037-160] arrangements and meant to abide by them.

[037-161] “I won’t meddle with you nor you with me,” Mrs. Rachel had said

[037-162] decidedly, “and as for the twins, I’ll be glad to do all I can for

[037-163] them; but I won’t undertake to answer Davy’s questions, that’s what.

[037-164] I’m not an encyclopedia, neither am I a Philadelphia lawyer. You’ll

[037-165] miss Anne for that.”

[037-166] “Sometimes Anne’s answers were about as queer as Davy’s questions,”

[037-167] said Marilla drily. “The twins will miss her and no mistake; but her

[037-168] future can’t be sacrificed to Davy’s thirst for information. When he

[037-169] asks questions I can’t answer I’ll just tell him children should be

[037-170] seen and not heard. That was how _I_ was brought up, and I don’t know

[037-171] but what it was just as good a way as all these new-fangled notions for

[037-172] training children.”
