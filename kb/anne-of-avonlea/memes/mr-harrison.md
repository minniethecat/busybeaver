# Mr. Harrison

The irritable, plain-spoken farmer who lives next door to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] — a man whose initial hostility masks loneliness and whose unlikely friendship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] becomes one of the novel's most satisfying relationship arcs.

## Character Overview

Mr. Harrison enters the novel as a comic antagonist — a man who "rejected all overtures of friendship" (004-112) and whose cow Dolly's repeated invasions of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s oats patch provide the novel's opening conflict. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s response to his rudeness is characteristic: "I can make allowance for you, Mr. Harrison, because I have an imagination. I can easily imagine how very trying it must be to find a cow in your oats" (001-186).

This imaginative empathy disarms Mr. Harrison, and his subsequent question — "Is it too much to hope that you'll forgive me?" (004-060) — reveals the vulnerability beneath the bluster. Over the course of the novel, Mr. Harrison evolves from misanthropic recluse to engaged [[neighbor|community]] member, participating in the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]] (027-137) and eventually remarrying.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Harrison functions as [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s first success story — the difficult person her philosophy of kindness wins over. His transformation from antagonist to ally demonstrates the novel's central thesis: that imagination and empathy can bridge even the widest gaps between people.

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Mr. Harrison's neighbor and the person whose imaginative empathy first disarms him. Their unlikely friendship is a testament to [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s power to transform antagonism into [[love|affection]].

**[[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]]** (later in the novel) becomes the subject of "popular gossip" (037-154) when Mr. Harrison remarries.

## Key Scenes

**The Cow in the Oats (Chapter 1).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s diplomatic response to Mr. Harrison's anger — "I can make allowance for you, Mr. Harrison, because I have an imagination" (001-186) — establishes the novel's central moral premise.

**The Plea for [[forgiveness|Forgiveness]] (Chapter 4).** Mr. Harrison's vulnerable question — "Is it too much to hope that you'll forgive me?" (004-060) — reveals the gentle heart beneath the gruff exterior.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Harrison is the novel's most fully realized example of transformation through relationship. His arc from [[loneliness|isolation]] to [[neighbor|community]] mirrors [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s own integration into [[avonlea|Avonlea]], and his eventual happiness — remarriage, [[neighbor|community]] participation — is presented as a quiet triumph of the social fabric [[anne-shirley|Anne]] helps to weave.
