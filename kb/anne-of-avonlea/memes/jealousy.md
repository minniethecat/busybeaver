# Jealousy

The petty, corrosive emotion that Montgomery names among "the small jealousies, the little deceits and rivalries" (027-122) that complicate the social life of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] — a minor vice [[humor|wit]]h outsized consequences for [[neighbor|community]] and character.

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, [[envy|jealousy]] is treated as one of the "small" moral failings — not dramatic enough to be a sin, but persistent enough to cause real damage. It appears in children and adults alike, manifesting as possessiveness over [[love|affection]], resentment of others' successes, and the suspicion that poisons friendship.

Montgomery's treatment of [[envy|jealousy]] is characteristically generous: she acknowledges its reality [[humor|wit]]hout condemning those who feel it. The novel's approach is diagnostic rather than punitive — [[envy|jealousy]] is a flaw to be recognized, understood, and overcome, not a mark of irredeemable character.

## Examples in the Text

**Emerson's Jealousy of the Brave Dog (Chapter 15).** In one of the novel's more comic treatments, a boy named Emerson "is jealous because he hasn't got a Brave" (015-050) dog like another child. This childhood [[envy|jealousy]] — wanting what another possesses — is presented [[humor|wit]]h gentle [[laughter|humor]], recognizing the emotion's universality [[humor|wit]]hout inflating its significance.

**Social Jealousy Among Adults (Chapter 21).** Montgomery observes how "everybody thinks she is jealous of her" (021-125) in a social context, capturing how [[envy|jealousy]] becomes a matter of public perception and gossip. The [[neighbor|community]]'s willingness to attribute [[envy|jealousy]] to others is itself a form of social scrutiny.

**The Jealous Guardianship of Character (Chapter 27).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] guards her "word and thought and deed as jealously as if her clear eyes were to" (027-115) judge them. Here "jealously" is used in its older sense — protective, vigilant — suggesting that [[envy|jealousy]]'s root emotion (the desire to protect what is valued) is not inherently negative.

**Small Jealousies Among Girls (Chapter 27).** Montgomery catalogs "the small jealousies, the little deceits and rivalries" (027-122) that characterize the social dynamics of young women. This acknowledgment of [[envy|jealousy]] as a social reality — neither sensationalized nor ignored — is characteristic of the novel's honest treatment of human [[nature-appreciation|nature]].

## Thematic Significance

**Jealousy as Social Friction.** The "small jealousies" of [[avonlea|Avonlea]] function as friction in the social machinery. They complicate relationships, generate gossip, and create obstacles to the kind of open-hearted [[neighbor|community]] the novel celebrates. Yet Montgomery treats them as inevitable — part of the texture of human [[community|society]] rather than a [[gossip|scandal]] to be eliminated.

**The Scale of Jealousy.** By repeatedly describing [[envy|jealousy]] as "small," Montgomery puts it in perspective. Jealousy is real, but it is not the most important thing. The novel's moral hierarchy [[belonging|place]]s [[envy|jealousy]] well below the cardinal virtues of kindness, imagination, and hope — a ranking that is itself a moral statement.

**Jealousy and Growth.** The novel's jealous characters — like those who gossip — are not villains but people in need of growth. Jealousy is presented as an emotion to be outgrown, something that diminishes as character deepens and perspective widens.

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s "Jealous" Guarding.** [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s "jealous" protection of her own conduct (027-115) redeems the root impulse behind [[envy|jealousy]] — the desire to safeguard what is valuable. When directed inward, as self-discipline, the jealous impulse becomes virtuous. The key difference is whether the emotion protects or attacks.

**The Grown-Ups' Perspective.** Adult characters in the novel tend to see [[envy|jealousy]] as a youthful failing to be outgrown. Their own jealousies, when they exist, are subtler — disguised as concern or principle — but no less real for being less acknowledged.
