[024-001] At the bottom of the stairs lay a big pink conch shell amid the

[024-002] fragments of what had been Miss Barry’s platter; and at the top of the

[024-003] stairs knelt a terrified [[davy-keith|Davy]], gazing down with wide-open eyes at the

[024-004] havoc.

[024-005] “Davy,” said [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] ominously, “did you throw that conch down _on

[024-006] purpose?_”

[024-007] “No, I never did,” whimpered Davy. “I was just kneeling here, quiet as

[024-008] quiet, to watch you folks through the bannisters, and my foot struck

[024-009] that old thing and pushed it off . . . and I’m awful hungry . . . and I

[024-010] do wish you’d lick a fellow and have done with it, instead of always

[024-011] sending him upstairs to miss all the fun.”

[024-012] “Don’t blame Davy,” said [[anne-shirley|Anne]], gathering up the fragments with

[024-013] trembling fingers. “It was my fault. I set that platter there and

[024-014] forgot all about it. I am properly punished for my carelessness; but

[024-015] oh, what will Miss Barry say?”

[024-016] “Well, you know she only bought it, so it isn’t the same as if it was

[024-017] an heirloom,” said [[diana-barry|Diana]], trying to console.

[024-018] The guests went away soon after, feeling that it was the most tactful

[024-019] thing to do, and Anne and Diana washed the dishes, talking less than

[024-020] they had ever been known to do before. Then Diana went [[belonging|home]] with a

[024-021] headache and Anne went with another to the east gable, where she stayed

[024-022] until Marilla came home from the post office at sunset, with a letter

[024-023] from Priscilla, written the day before. Mrs. Morgan had sprained her

[024-024] ankle so severely that she could not leave her room.

[024-025] “And oh, Anne dear,” wrote Priscilla, “I’m so sorry, but I’m afraid we

[024-026] won’t get up to [[green-gables|Green Gables]] at all now, for by the time Aunty’s ankle

[024-027] is well she will have to go back to Toronto. She has to be there by a

[024-028] certain date.”

[024-029] “Well,” sighed Anne, laying the letter down on the red sandstone step

[024-030] of the back porch, where she was sitting, while the twilight rained

[024-031] down out of a dappled sky, “I always thought it was too good to be true

[024-032] that Mrs. Morgan should really come. But there . . . that speech sounds

[024-033] as pessimistic as Miss Eliza Andrews and I’m ashamed of making it.

[024-034] After all, it was _not_ too good to be true . . . things just as good

[024-035] and far better are coming true for me all the time. And I suppose the

[024-036] events of today have a funny side too. Perhaps when Diana and I are old

[024-037] and gray we shall be able to laugh over them. But I feel that I can’t

[024-038] expect to do it before then, for it has truly been a bitter

[024-039] disappointment.”

[024-040] “You’ll probably have a good many more and worse disappointments than

[024-041] that before you get through life,” said Marilla, who honestly thought

[024-042] she was making a comforting speech. “It seems to me, Anne, that you are

[024-043] never going to outgrow your fashion of setting your heart so on things

[024-044] and then crashing down into despair because you don’t get them.”

[024-045] “I know I’m too much inclined that, way” agreed Anne ruefully. “When I

[024-046] think something nice is going to happen I seem to fly right up on the

[024-047] wings of anticipation; and then the first thing I realize I drop down

[024-048] to earth with a thud. But really, Marilla, the flying part _is_

[024-049] glorious as long as it lasts . . . it’s like soaring through a sunset.

[024-050] I think it almost pays for the thud.”

[024-051] “Well, maybe it does,” admitted Marilla. “I’d rather walk calmly along

[024-052] and do without both flying and thud. But everybody has her own way of

[024-053] living . . . I used to think there was only one right way . . . but

[024-054] since I’ve had you and the twins to bring up I don’t feel so sure of

[024-055] it. What are you going to do about Miss Barry’s platter?”

[024-056] “Pay her back the twenty dollars she paid for it, I suppose. I’m so

[024-057] thankful it wasn’t a cherished heirloom because then no money could

[024-058] replace it.”

[024-059] “Maybe you could find one like it somewhere and buy it for her.”

[024-060] “I’m afraid not. Platters as old as that are very scarce. [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[024-061] couldn’t find one anywhere for the supper. I only wish I could, for of

[024-062] course Miss Barry would just as soon have one platter as another, if

[024-063] both were equally old and genuine. Marilla, look at that big star over

[024-064] [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]’s maple grove, with all that holy hush of silvery sky

[024-065] about it. It gives me a feeling that is like a prayer. After all, when

[024-066] one can see stars and skies like that, little disappointments and

[024-067] accidents can’t matter so much, can they?”

[024-068] “Where’s Davy?” said Marilla, with an indifferent glance at the star.

[024-069] “In bed. I’ve promised to take him and [[dora-keith|Dora]] to the shore for a picnic

[024-070] tomorrow. Of course, the original agreement was that he must be good.

[024-071] But he _tried_ to be good . . . and I hadn’t the heart to disappoint

[024-072] him.”

[024-073] “You’ll drown yourself or the twins, rowing about the pond in that

[024-074] flat,” grumbled Marilla. “I’ve lived here for sixty years and I’ve

[024-075] never been on the pond yet.”

[024-076] “Well, it’s never too late to mend,” said Anne roguishly. “Suppose you

[024-077] come with us tomorrow. We’ll shut Green Gables up and spend the whole

[024-078] day at the shore, daffing the world aside.”

[024-079] “No, thank you,” said Marilla, with indignant emphasis. “I’d be a nice

[024-080] sight, wouldn’t I, rowing down the pond in a flat? I think I hear

[024-081] Rachel pronouncing on it. There’s Mr. Harrison driving away somewhere.

[024-082] Do you suppose there is any truth in the gossip that Mr. Harrison is

[024-083] going to see Isabella Andrews?”

[024-084] “No, I’m sure there isn’t. He just called there one evening on business

[024-085] with [[mr-harmon-andrews|Mr. Harmon Andrews]] and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] saw him and said she knew he was

[024-086] courting because he had a white collar on. I don’t believe Mr. Harrison

[024-087] will ever marry. He seems to have a prejudice against marriage.”

[024-088] “Well, you can never tell about those old bachelors. And if he had a

[024-089] white collar on I’d agree with Rachel that it looks suspicious, for I’m

[024-090] sure he never was seen with one before.”

[024-091] “I think he only put it on because he wanted to conclude a business

[024-092] deal with Harmon Andrews,” said Anne. “I’ve heard him say that’s the

[024-093] only time a man needs to be particular about his appearance, because if

[024-094] he looks prosperous the party of the second part won’t be so likely to

[024-095] try to cheat him. I really feel sorry for Mr. Harrison; I don’t believe

[024-096] he feels satisfied with his life. It must be very lonely to have no one

[024-097] to care about except a parrot, don’t you think? But I notice Mr.

[024-098] Harrison doesn’t like to be pitied. Nobody does, I imagine.”

[024-099] “There’s [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert]] coming up the lane,” said Marilla. “If he wants you to

[024-100] go for a row on the pond mind you put on your coat and rubbers. There’s

[024-101] a heavy dew tonight.”

[024-102] XVIII

[024-103] An Adventure on the Tory Road

[024-104] “Anne,” said Davy, sitting up in bed and propping his chin on his

[024-105] hands, “Anne, where is sleep? People go to sleep every night, and of

[024-106] course I know it’s the place where I do the things I dream, but I want

[024-107] to know _where_ it is and how I get there and back without knowing

[024-108] anything about it . . . and in my nighty too. Where is it?”

[024-109] Anne was kneeling at the west gable window watching the sunset sky that

[024-110] was like a great flower with petals of crocus and a heart of fiery

[024-111] yellow. She turned her head at Davy’s question and answered dreamily,

[024-112] “‘Over the mountains of the moon,

[024-113] Down the valley of the shadow.’”

[024-114] [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] would have known the meaning of this, or made a meaning out

[024-115] of it for himself, if he didn’t; but practical Davy, who, as Anne often

[024-116] despairingly remarked, hadn’t a particle of imagination, was only

[024-117] puzzled and disgusted.

[024-118] “Anne, I believe you’re just talking nonsense.”

[024-119] “Of course, I was, dear boy. Don’t you know that it is only very

[024-120] foolish folk who talk sense all the time?”

[024-121] “Well, I think you might give a sensible answer when I ask a sensible

[024-122] question,” said Davy in an injured tone.

[024-123] “Oh, you are too little to understand,” said Anne. But she felt rather

[024-124] ashamed of saying it; for had she not, in keen remembrance of many

[024-125] similar snubs administered in her own early years, solemnly vowed that

[024-126] she would never tell any child it was too little to understand? Yet

[024-127] here she was doing it . . . so wide sometimes is the gulf between

[024-128] theory and practice.

[024-129] “Well, I’m doing my best to grow,” said Davy, “but it’s a thing you

[024-130] can’t hurry much. If Marilla wasn’t so stingy with her jam I believe

[024-131] I’d grow a lot faster.”

[024-132] “Marilla is not stingy, Davy,” said Anne severely. “It is very

[024-133] ungrateful of you to say such a thing.”

[024-134] “There’s another word that means the same thing and sounds a lot

[024-135] better, but I don’t just remember it,” said Davy, frowning intently. “I

[024-136] heard Marilla say she was it, herself, the other day.”

[024-137] “If you mean _economical_, it’s a _very_ different thing from being

[024-138] stingy. It is an excellent trait in a person if she is economical. If

[024-139] Marilla had been stingy she wouldn’t have taken you and Dora when your

[024-140] mother died. Would you have liked to live with Mrs. Wiggins?”

[024-141] “You just bet I wouldn’t!” Davy was emphatic on that point. “Nor I

[024-142] don’t want to go out to Uncle Richard neither. I’d far rather live

[024-143] here, even if Marilla is that long-tailed word when it comes to jam,

[024-144] ’cause _you’re_ here, Anne. Say, Anne, won’t you tell me a story ’fore

[024-145] I go to sleep? I don’t want a fairy story. They’re all right for girls,

[024-146] I s’pose, but I want something exciting . . . lots of killing and

[024-147] shooting in it, and a house on fire, and in’trusting things like that.”

[024-148] Fortunately for Anne, Marilla called out at this moment from her room.

[024-149] “Anne, Diana’s signaling at a great rate. You’d better see what she

[024-150] wants.”

[024-151] Anne ran to the east gable and saw flashes of light coming through the

[024-152] twilight from Diana’s window in groups of five, which meant, according

[024-153] to their old childish code, “Come over at once for I have something

[024-154] important to reveal.” Anne threw her white shawl over her head and

[024-155] hastened through [[haunted-wood|the Haunted Wood]] and across [[mr-bell|Mr. Bell]]’s pasture corner

[024-156] to [[orchard-slope|Orchard Slope]].

[024-157] “I’ve good news for you, Anne,” said Diana. “Mother and I have just got

[024-158] home from [[carmody|Carmody]], and I saw Mary Sentner from Spencer vale in Mr.

[024-159] Blair’s store. She says the old Copp girls on the Tory Road have a

[024-160] willow-ware platter and she thinks it’s exactly like the one we had at

[024-161] the supper. She says they’ll likely sell it, for Martha Copp has never

[024-162] been known to keep anything she _could_ sell; but if they won’t there’s

[024-163] a platter at [[wesley-keyson|Wesley Keyson]]’s at [[spencervale|Spencervale]] and she knows they’d sell

[024-164] it, but she isn’t sure it’s just the same kind as [[aunt-josephine|Aunt Josephine]]’s.”

[024-165] “I’ll go right over to Spencervale after it tomorrow,” said Anne

[024-166] resolutely, “and you must come with me. It will be such a weight off my

[024-167] mind, for I have to go to town day after tomorrow and how can I face

[024-168] your Aunt Josephine without a willow-ware platter? It would be even

[024-169] worse than the time I had to confess about jumping on the spare room

[024-170] bed.”

[024-171] Both girls laughed over the old memory . . . concerning which, if any

[024-172] of my readers are ignorant and curious, I must refer them to Anne’s

[024-173] earlier history.
