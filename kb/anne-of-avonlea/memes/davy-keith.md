# Davy Keith

The irrepressible orphan twin whose arrival at [[green-gables|Green Gables]] brings chaos, [[humor|comedy]], and unexpected tenderness into [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s life — a boy whose endless mischief is matched only by his capacity for [[love|affection]].

## Character Overview

Davy Keith arrives at [[green-gables|Green Gables]] [[humor|wit]]h his twin sister [[dora-keith|Dora]] after their mother's death. Where [[dora-keith|Dora]] is prim and well-behaved, Davy is a force of [[nature-appreciation|nature]] — impulsive, curious, and constitutionally incapable of staying out of trouble. He is, in many ways, the child [[anne-shirley|Anne]] might have been had she lacked imagination's channeling influence: pure, undirected energy [[humor|wit]]h a good heart but no governor.

[[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s response to Davy defines her emerging maturity. Rather than resorting to punishment, she practices what she calls her "theory of kindness" (016-163), resolving "to forgive Davy's next offence, whatever it might be" (011-156) even before it occurs. Davy, in turn, adores [[anne-shirley|Anne]] [[humor|wit]]h the wholehearted [[love|devotion]] of a child who knows he is [[romance|love]]d despite — perhaps because of — his flaws.

## Role in the Story

Davy provides much of the novel's [[humor|comedy]]. His escapades — involving everything from inappropriate questions at prayer to unauthorized explorations of [[community|neighbors]]' property — are a source of both exasperation and delight. But Davy also serves a serious narrative function: he is the test case for [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s philosophy of kindness and forgiveness.

His relationship [[humor|wit]]h [[anne-shirley|Anne]] demonstrates that [[romance|love]] is not contingent on good behavior. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s preemptive forgiveness (011-156) is the novel's most radical statement of unconditional [[romance|love]] — she [[romance|love]]s Davy not despite his mischief but through it, seeing the good heart beneath the naughty surface.

## Relationships

**[[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]** is Davy's guardian and the primary recipient of both his mischief and his [[love|affection]]. Their relationship is one of the novel's most touching — [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s patience [[humor|wit]]h Davy is a form of [[romance|love]] he has rarely experienced.

**[[dora-keith|Dora Keith]]** is Davy's twin sister, prim and well-behaved where Davy is wild. She provides both contrast and occasional co-conspirator.

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** is Davy's guardian, whose stern exterior conceals genuine [[love|affection]] for the troublesome boy.

## Key Scenes

**The Preemptive [[forgiveness|Forgiveness]] (Chapter 11).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] resolves "to forgive Davy's next offence, whatever it might be" (011-156) — a moment of comic anticipation that reveals deep truth about unconditional [[romance|love]].

## Character Analysis

Davy represents the child [[anne-shirley|Anne]] might have been — all impulse and no reflection. His presence allows Montgomery to show how far [[anne-shirley|Anne]] has come: the girl who once exploded at [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] is now the young woman who forgives Davy in advance. Davy is [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s past, and her patience [[humor|wit]]h him is a form of self-[[kindness|compassion]].
