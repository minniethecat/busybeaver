[038-001] “Well, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]’s methods seem to have worked fairly well with [[davy-keith|Davy]],” said

[038-002] [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] smilingly. “He is a reformed character, that’s what.”

[038-003] “He isn’t a bad little soul,” conceded [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]. “I never expected to

[038-004] get as fond of those children as I have. Davy gets round you somehow .

[038-005] . . and [[dora-keith|Dora]] is a lovely child, although she is . . . kind of . . .

[038-006] well, kind of . . .”

[038-007] “Monotonous? Exactly,” supplied Mrs. Rachel. “Like a book where every

[038-008] page is the same, that’s what. Dora will make a good, reliable woman

[038-009] but she’ll never set the pond on fire. Well, that sort of folks are

[038-010] comfortable to have round, even if they’re not as interesting as the

[038-011] other kind.”

[038-012] [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]] was probably the only person to whom the news of Anne’s

[038-013] resignation brought unmixed pleasure. Her pupils looked upon it as a

[038-014] sheer catastrophe. [[annetta-bell|Annetta Bell]] had hysterics when she went [[belonging|home]].

[038-015] [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]] fought two pitched and unprovoked battles with other boys

[038-016] by way of relieving his feelings. [[barbara-shaw|Barbara Shaw]] cried all night. [[paul-irving|Paul]]

[038-017] Irving defiantly told his grandmother that she needn’t expect him to

[038-018] eat any porridge for a week.

[038-019] “I can’t do it, Grandma,” he said. “I don’t really know if I can eat

[038-020] _anything_. I feel as if there was a dreadful lump in my throat. I’d

[038-021] have cried coming home from school if Jake Donnell hadn’t been watching

[038-022] me. I believe I will cry after I go to bed. It wouldn’t show on my eyes

[038-023] tomorrow, would it? And it would be such a relief. But anyway, I can’t

[038-024] eat porridge. I’m going to need all my strength of mind to bear up

[038-025] against this, Grandma, and I won’t have any left to grapple with

[038-026] porridge. Oh Grandma, I don’t know what I’ll do when my beautiful

[038-027] teacher goes away. Milty Boulter says he bets [[jane-andrews|Jane Andrews]] will get the

[038-028] school. I suppose Miss Andrews is very nice. But I know she won’t

[038-029] understand things like Miss Shirley.”

[038-030] [[diana-barry|Diana]] also took a very pessimistic view of affairs.

[038-031] “It will be horribly lonesome here next winter,” she mourned, one

[038-032] twilight when the moonlight was raining “airy silver” through the

[038-033] cherry boughs and filling the east gable with a soft, dream-like

[038-034] radiance in which the two girls sat and talked, Anne on her low rocker

[038-035] by the window, Diana sitting Turkfashion on the bed. “You and Gilbert

[038-036] will be gone . . . and the Allans too. They are going to call [[mr-allan|Mr. Allan]]

[038-037] to [[charlottetown|Charlottetown]] and of course he’ll accept. It’s too mean. We’ll be

[038-038] vacant all winter, I suppose, and have to listen to a long string of

[038-039] candidates . . . and half of them won’t be any good.”

[038-040] “I hope they won’t call Mr. Baxter from [[grafton|East Grafton]] here, anyhow,”

[038-041] said Anne decidedly. “He wants the call but he does preach such gloomy

[038-042] sermons. [[mr-bell|Mr. Bell]] says he’s a minister of the old school, but Mrs.

[038-043] Lynde says there’s nothing whatever the matter with him but

[038-044] indigestion. His wife isn’t a very good cook, it seems, and [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[038-045] says that when a man has to eat sour bread two weeks out of three his

[038-046] theology is bound to get a kink in it somewhere. [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] feels very

[038-047] badly about going away. She says everybody has been so kind to her

[038-048] since she came here as a bride that she feels as if she were leaving

[038-049] lifelong friends. And then, there’s the baby’s grave, you know. She

[038-050] says she doesn’t see how she can go away and leave that . . . it was

[038-051] such a little mite of a thing and only three months old, and she says

[038-052] she is afraid it will miss its mother, although she knows better and

[038-053] wouldn’t say so to Mr. Allan for anything. She says she has slipped

[038-054] through the birch grove back of the manse nearly every night to the

[038-055] graveyard and sung a little lullaby to it. She told me all about it

[038-056] last evening when I was up putting some of those early wild roses on

[038-057] Matthew’s grave. I promised her that as long as I was in [[avonlea|Avonlea]] I

[038-058] would put flowers on the baby’s grave and when I was away I felt sure

[038-059] that . . .”

[038-060] “That I would do it,” supplied Diana heartily. “Of course I will. And

[038-061] I’ll put them on Matthew’s grave too, for your sake, Anne.”

[038-062] “Oh, thank you. I meant to ask you to if you would. And on little

[038-063] [[hester-gray|Hester Gray]]’s too? Please don’t forget hers. Do you know, I’ve thought

[038-064] and dreamed so much about little Hester Gray that she has become

[038-065] strangely real to me. I think of her, back there in her little garden

[038-066] in that cool, still, green corner; and I have a fancy that if I could

[038-067] steal back there some spring evening, just at the magic time ’twixt

[038-068] light and dark, and tiptoe so softly up the beech hill that my

[038-069] footsteps could not frighten her, I would find the garden just as it

[038-070] used to be, all sweet with June lilies and early roses, with the tiny

[038-071] house beyond it all hung with vines; and little Hester Gray would be

[038-072] there, with her soft eyes, and the wind ruffling her dark hair,

[038-073] wandering about, putting her fingertips under the chins of the lilies

[038-074] and whispering secrets with the roses; and I would go forward, oh, so

[038-075] softly, and hold out my hands and say to her, ‘Little Hester Gray,

[038-076] won’t you let me be your playmate, for I [[romance|love]] the roses too?’ And we

[038-077] would sit down on the old bench and talk a little and dream a little,

[038-078] or just be beautifully silent together. And then the moon would rise

[038-079] and I would look around me . . . and there would be no Hester Gray and

[038-080] no little vine-hung house, and no roses . . . only an old waste garden

[038-081] starred with June lilies amid the grasses, and the wind sighing, oh, so

[038-082] sorrowfully in the cherry trees. And I would not know whether it had

[038-083] been real or if I had just imagined it all.” Diana crawled up and got

[038-084] her back against the headboard of the bed. When your companion of

[038-085] twilight hour said such spooky things it was just as well not to be

[038-086] able to fancy there was anything behind you.

[038-087] “I’m afraid the Improvement Society will go down when you and Gilbert

[038-088] are both gone,” she remarked dolefully.

[038-089] “Not a bit of fear of it,” said Anne briskly, coming back from

[038-090] dreamland to the affairs of practical life. “It is too firmly

[038-091] established for that, especially since the older people are becoming so

[038-092] enthusiastic about it. Look what they are doing this summer for their

[038-093] lawns and lanes. Besides, I’ll be watching for hints at [[redmond-college|Redmond]] and

[038-094] I’ll write a paper for it next winter and send it over. Don’t take such

[038-095] a gloomy view of things, Diana. And don’t grudge me my little hour of

[038-096] gladness and jubilation now. Later on, when I have to go away, I’ll

[038-097] feel anything but glad.”

[038-098] “It’s all right for you to be glad . . . you’re going to college and

[038-099] you’ll have a jolly time and make heaps of lovely new friends.”

[038-100] “I hope I shall make new friends,” said Anne thoughtfully. “The

[038-101] possibilities of making new friends help to make life very fascinating.

[038-102] But no matter how many friends I make they’ll never be as dear to me as

[038-103] the old ones . . . especially a certain girl with black eyes and

[038-104] dimples. Can you guess who she is, Diana?”

[038-105] “But there’ll be so many clever girls at Redmond,” sighed Diana, “and

[038-106] I’m only a stupid little country girl who says ‘I seen’ sometimes. . .

[038-107] though I really know better when I stop to think. Well, of course these

[038-108] past two years have really been too pleasant to last. I know _somebody_

[038-109] who is glad you are going to Redmond anyhow. Anne, I’m going to ask you

[038-110] a question . . . a serious question. Don’t be vexed and do answer

[038-111] seriously. Do you care anything for Gilbert?”

[038-112] “Ever so much as a friend and not a bit in the way you mean,” said Anne

[038-113] calmly and decidedly; she also thought she was speaking sincerely.

[038-114] Diana sighed. She wished, somehow, that Anne had answered differently.

[038-115] “Don’t you mean _ever_ to be married, Anne?”

[038-116] “Perhaps . . . some day . . . when I meet the right one,” said Anne,

[038-117] smiling dreamily up at the moonlight.

[038-118] “But how can you be sure when you do meet the right one?” persisted

[038-119] Diana.

[038-120] “Oh, I should know him . . . _something_ would tell me. You know what

[038-121] my ideal is, Diana.”

[038-122] “But people’s ideals change sometimes.”

[038-123] “Mine won’t. And I _couldn’t_ care for any man who didn’t fulfill it.”

[038-124] “What if you never meet him?”

[038-125] “Then I shall die an old maid,” was the cheerful response. “I daresay

[038-126] it isn’t the hardest death by any means.”

[038-127] “Oh, I suppose the dying would be easy enough; it’s the living an old

[038-128] maid I shouldn’t like,” said Diana, with no intention of being

[038-129] humorous. “Although I wouldn’t mind being an old maid _very_ much if I

[038-130] could be one like [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]. But I never could be. When I’m

[038-131] forty-five I’ll be horribly fat. And while there might be some romance

[038-132] about a thin old maid there couldn’t possibly be any about a fat one.

[038-133] Oh, mind you, Nelson Atkins proposed to [[ruby-gillis|Ruby Gillis]] three weeks ago.

[038-134] Ruby told me all about it. She says she never had any intention of

[038-135] taking him, because any one who married him will have to go in with the

[038-136] old folks; but Ruby says that he made such a perfectly beautiful and

[038-137] romantic proposal that it simply swept her off her feet. But she didn’t

[038-138] want to do anything rash so she asked for a week to consider; and two

[038-139] days later she was at a meeting of the Sewing Circle at his mother’s

[038-140] and there was a book called ‘The Complete Guide to Etiquette,’ lying on

[038-141] the parlor table. Ruby said she simply couldn’t describe her feelings

[038-142] when in a section of it headed, ‘The Deportment of Courtship and

[038-143] Marriage,’ she found the very proposal Nelson had made, word for word.

[038-144] She went home and wrote him a perfectly scathing refusal; and she says

[038-145] his father and mother have taken turns watching him ever since for fear

[038-146] he’ll drown himself in the river; but Ruby says they needn’t be afraid;

[038-147] for in the Deportment of Courtship and Marriage it told how a rejected

[038-148] lover should behave and there’s nothing about drowning in _that_. And

[038-149] she says Wilbur Blair is literally pining away for her but she’s

[038-150] perfectly helpless in the matter.”

[038-151] Anne made an impatient movement.

[038-152] “I hate to say it . . . it seems so disloyal . . . but, well, I don’t

[038-153] like Ruby Gillis now. I liked her when we went to school and Queen’s

[038-154] together . . . though not so well as you and Jane of course. But this

[038-155] last year at [[carmody|Carmody]] she seems so different . . . so . . . so . . .”

[038-156] “I know,” nodded Diana. “It’s the Gillis coming out in her . . . she

[038-157] can’t help it. [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says that if ever a Gillis girl thought about

[038-158] anything but the boys she never showed it in her walk and conversation.

[038-159] She talks about nothing but boys and what compliments they pay her, and

[038-160] how crazy they all are about her at Carmody. And the strange thing is,

[038-161] they _are_, too . . .” Diana admitted this somewhat resentfully. “Last

[038-162] night when I saw her in Mr. Blair’s store she whispered to me that

[038-163] she’d just made a new ‘mash.’ I wouldn’t ask her who it was, because I

[038-164] knew she was dying to _be_ asked. Well, it’s what Ruby always wanted, I

[038-165] suppose. You remember even when she was little she always said she

[038-166] meant to have dozens of beaus when she grew up and have the very gayest

[038-167] time she could before she settled down. She’s so different from Jane,

[038-168] isn’t she? Jane is such a nice, sensible, lady-like girl.”
