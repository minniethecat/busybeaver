# Tennyson's Poems

The poetry of Alfred, Lord Tennyson, which serves as a recurring literary touchstone in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* — a shared reference point for [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]], and a marker of the romantic sensibility they embody.

## Overview

Tennyson's poetry is woven into the fabric of the novel through quotation and allusion. When [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] share the vision of "How fair the realm / [[imagination|Imagination]] opens to the view" (021-022), they are drawing on a Tennysonian vocabulary of beauty and transcendence that Montgomery's original readers would have recognized immediately.

## Significance

The novel's use of Tennyson positions [[anne-shirley|Anne]] and [[paul-irving|Paul]] [[humor|wit]]hin a literary tradition that values imagination, beauty, and emotional depth. Their shared [[romance|love]] of poetry is a marker of their "[[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]]" status — both belong to the [[neighbor|community]] of readers for whom literature is not decoration but a way of being in the world.
