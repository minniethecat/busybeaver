# Love

Love is one of the most pervasive themes in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, manifesting in multiple forms: the romantic [[envy|longing]] of [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]], the deepening bond between [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]] and [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], the affection [[anne-shirley|Anne]] holds for her pupils, and the everyday kindnesses that knit the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[neighbor|community]] together. L.M. Montgomery treats [[romance|love]] not as a single dramatic force but as a spectrum of feeling that shapes character, motivates action, and gives meaning to ordinary life.

## Definition

In the world of *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, [[romance|love]] is defined broadly. It encompasses romantic attachment — most vividly in the story of [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar Lewis]], who was "jilted" in youth and who finds [[romance|love]] again [[humor|wit]]h [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]] — but it extends far beyond [[romance|courtship]]. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s teaching philosophy is built on [[romance|love]]: "I'd rather have my pupils [[romance|love]] me and look back to me in after years [[humor|wit]]h affection." [005-084]

The [[romance|love]] between [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]] and the twins [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora Keith]] grows gradually through patience and care. Even the prickly [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] reveals a capacity for [[romance|love]] beneath his gruff exterior. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s delight in the beauty of [[green-gables|Green Gables]] and the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] landscape is itself a form of [[romance|love]] — the imaginative [[romance|love]] that transforms the ordinary into the extraordinary.

## Examples in the Text

**Romantic Love**: [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]]'s story is the novel's clearest romance arc. Jilted in her youth by [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]], she has lived alone at [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]] for twenty-five years. When Stephen returns, their reunion — culminating in [[miss-lavendars-wedding|Miss Lavendar's Wedding]] — demonstrates that [[romance|love]] can endure across decades of separation. [002-083]

**Pedagogical Love**: [[anne-shirley|Anne]] defines her teaching vocation in terms of [[romance|love]] rather than discipline. She wants her pupils to [[romance|love]] her, to remember her fondly, and to find in school a [[belonging|place]] of warmth rather than fear. This philosophy contrasts sharply [[humor|wit]]h the corporal punishment methods of her predecessor. [005-084]

**Familial Affection**: [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s quiet attachment to [[diana-barry|Diana]] after her friend's marriage, [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]]'s growing devotion to the twins, and [[charlotta-the-fourth|Charlotta the Fourth]]'s fierce [[faith|loyalty]] to [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Miss Lavendar]] all illustrate the novel's conviction that [[romance|love]] is the foundation of family — whether biological or chosen.

**[[community|Community]] [[kindness|Kindness]]**: The novel is threaded [[humor|wit]]h small acts of [[romance|love]]-as-kindness: [[community|neighbors]] helping [[community|neighbors]], the Ladies' Aid sewing for charity, [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s patient efforts to beautify the school. These acts, individually modest, collectively define [[avonlea|Avonlea]] as a [[neighbor|community]] where [[romance|love]] is performed through daily action. [004-137]

**Love of Place**: [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s rapturous descriptions of the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] landscape — [[green-gables|Green Gables]], the [[birch-path|Birch Path]], Lover's Lane — express [[romance|love]] directed at [[nature-appreciation|the natural world]]. This [[romance|love]] of [[belonging|place]] is a defining feature of her character and of Montgomery's narrative voice.

## Thematic Significance

Love in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]* is inseparable from the novel's other central themes. It fuels [[ambition|ambition]] — [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s desire to be a good teacher is rooted in [[romance|love]] for her pupils. It tests [[pride|pride]] — several characters must humble themselves to express [[romance|love]]. It motivates [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] — as when [[anne-shirley|Anne]] forgives those who slight her. And it undergirds [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirits]] — the novel's sig[[nature-appreciation|nature]] concept of elective affinity between souls.

Montgomery presents [[romance|love]] not as an escape from ordinary life but as the quality that transforms the ordinary into the extraordinary. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s "scope for imagination" is, at its core, a way of loving the world more deeply — of seeing [[beauty|loveliness]] where others see only the mundane.

> "I'd rather have my pupils love me and look back to me in after years with affection, than have the reputation of being the smartest teacher in the Island." — Anne Shirley [005-084]
