[028-001] “Yes; Priscilla wrote that she had taken her own [[belonging|home]] school, so the

[028-002] [[carmody|Carmody]] trustees gave it to Ruby. I’m sorry Priscilla is not coming

[028-003] back, but since she can’t I’m glad Ruby has got the school. She will be

[028-004] home for Saturdays and it will seem like old times, to have her and

[028-005] [[jane-andrews|Jane]] and [[diana-barry|Diana]] and myself all together again.”

[028-006] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]], just home from [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]’s, was sitting on the back porch

[028-007] step when [[anne-shirley|Anne]] returned to the house.

[028-008] “Rachel and I have decided to have our cruise to town tomorrow,” she

[028-009] said. “[[thomas-lynde|Mr. Lynde]] is feeling better this week and Rachel wants to go

[028-010] before he has another sick spell.”

[028-011] “I intend to get up extra early tomorrow morning, for I’ve ever so much

[028-012] to do,” said Anne virtuously. “For one thing, I’m going to shift the

[028-013] feathers from my old bedtick to the new one. I ought to have done it

[028-014] long ago but I’ve just kept putting it off . . . it’s such a detestable

[028-015] task. It’s a very bad habit to put off disagreeable things, and I never

[028-016] mean to again, or else I can’t comfortably tell my pupils not to do it.

[028-017] That would be inconsistent. Then I want to make a cake for [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]]

[028-018] and finish my paper on gardens for the [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|A.V.I.S.]], and write Stella, and

[028-019] wash and starch my muslin dress, and make [[dora-keith|Dora]]’s new apron.”

[028-020] “You won’t get half done,” said Marilla pessimistically. “I never yet

[028-021] planned to do a lot of things but something happened to prevent me.”

[028-022] XX

[028-023] The Way It Often Happens

[028-024] Anne rose betimes the next morning and blithely greeted the fresh day,

[028-025] when the banners of the sunrise were shaken triumphantly across the

[028-026] pearly skies. [[green-gables|Green Gables]] lay in a pool of sunshine, flecked with the

[028-027] dancing shadows of poplar and willow. Beyond the land was Mr.

[028-028] Harrison’s wheatfield, a great, windrippled expanse of pale gold. The

[028-029] world was so beautiful that Anne spent ten blissful minutes hanging

[028-030] idly over the garden gate drinking the [[beauty|loveliness]] in.

[028-031] After breakfast Marilla made ready for her journey. Dora was to go with

[028-032] her, having been long promised this treat.

[028-033] “Now, [[davy-keith|Davy]], you try to be a good boy and don’t bother Anne,” she

[028-034] straitly charged him. “If you are good I’ll bring you a striped candy

[028-035] cane from town.”

[028-036] For alas, Marilla had stooped to the evil habit of bribing people to be

[028-037] good!

[028-038] “I won’t be bad on purpose, but s’posen I’m bad zacksidentally?” Davy

[028-039] wanted to know.

[028-040] “You’ll have to guard against accidents,” admonished Marilla. “Anne, if

[028-041] Mr. Shearer comes today get a nice roast and some steak. If he doesn’t

[028-042] you’ll have to kill a fowl for dinner tomorrow.”

[028-043] Anne nodded.

[028-044] “I’m not going to bother cooking any dinner for just Davy and myself

[028-045] today,” she said. “That cold ham bone will do for noon lunch and I’ll

[028-046] have some steak fried for you when you come home at night.”

[028-047] “I’m going to help Mr. Harrison haul dulse this morning,” announced

[028-048] Davy. “He asked me to, and I guess he’ll ask me to dinner too. Mr.

[028-049] Harrison is an awful kind man. He’s a real sociable man. I hope I’ll be

[028-050] like him when I grow up. I mean _behave_ like him . . . I don’t want to

[028-051] _look_ like him. But I guess there’s no danger, for [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] says I’m

[028-052] a very handsome child. Do you s’pose it’ll last, Anne? I want to know?”

[028-053] “I daresay it will,” said Anne gravely. “You _are_ a handsome boy,

[028-054] Davy,” . . . Marilla looked volumes of disapproval . . . “but you must

[028-055] live up to it and be just as nice and gentlemanly as you look to be.”

[028-056] “And you told [[minnie-may-barry|Minnie May Barry]] the other day, when you found her crying

[028-057] ’cause some one said she was ugly, that if she was nice and kind and

[028-058] loving people wouldn’t mind her looks,” said Davy discontentedly.

[028-059] “Seems to me you can’t get out of being good in this world for some

[028-060] reason or ‘nother. You just _have_ to behave.”

[028-061] “Don’t you want to be good?” asked Marilla, who had learned a great

[028-062] deal but had not yet learned the futility of asking such questions.

[028-063] “Yes, I want to be good but not _too_ good,” said Davy cautiously. “You

[028-064] don’t have to be very good to be a Sunday School superintendent. Mr.

[028-065] Bell’s that, and he’s a real bad man.”

[028-066] “Indeed he’s not,” said Marila indignantly.

[028-067] “He is . . . he says he is himself,” asseverated Davy. “He said it when

[028-068] he prayed in Sunday School last Sunday. He said he was a vile worm and

[028-069] a miserable sinner and guilty of the blackest ‘niquity. What did he do

[028-070] that was so bad, Marilla? Did he kill anybody? Or steal the collection

[028-071] cents? I want to know.”

[028-072] Fortunately [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] came driving up the lane at this moment and

[028-073] Marilla made off, feeling that she had escaped from the snare of the

[028-074] fowler, and wishing devoutly that [[mr-bell|Mr. Bell]] were not quite so highly

[028-075] figurative in his public petitions, especially in the hearing of small

[028-076] boys who were always “wanting to know.”

[028-077] Anne, left alone in her glory, worked with a will. The floor was swept,

[028-078] the beds made, the hens fed, the muslin dress washed and hung out on

[028-079] the line. Then Anne prepared for the transfer of feathers. She mounted

[028-080] to the garret and donned the first old dress that came to hand . . . a

[028-081] navy blue cashmere she had worn at fourteen. It was decidedly on the

[028-082] short side and as “skimpy” as the notable wincey Anne had worn upon the

[028-083] occasion of her debut at Green Gables; but at least it would not be

[028-084] materially injured by down and feathers. Anne completed her toilet by

[028-085] tying a big red and white spotted handkerchief that had belonged to

[028-086] Matthew over her head, and, thus accoutred, betook herself to the

[028-087] kitchen chamber, whither Marilla, before her departure, had helped her

[028-088] carry the feather bed.

[028-089] A cracked mirror hung by the chamber window and in an unlucky moment

[028-090] Anne looked into it. There were those seven freckles on her nose, more

[028-091] rampant than ever, or so it seemed in the glare of light from the

[028-092] unshaded window.

[028-093] “Oh, I forgot to rub that lotion on last night,” she thought. “I’d

[028-094] better run down to the pantry and do it now.”

[028-095] Anne had already suffered many things trying to remove those freckles.

[028-096] On one occasion the entire skin had peeled off her nose but the

[028-097] freckles remained. A few days previously she had found a recipe for a

[028-098] freckle lotion in a magazine and, as the ingredients were within her

[028-099] reach, she straightway compounded it, much to the disgust of Marilla,

[028-100] who thought that if Providence had placed freckles on your nose it was

[028-101] your bounden duty to leave them there.

[028-102] Anne scurried down to the pantry, which, always dim from the big willow

[028-103] growing close to the window, was now almost dark by reason of the shade

[028-104] drawn to exclude flies. Anne caught the bottle containing the lotion

[028-105] from the shelf and copiously anointed her nose therewith by means of a

[028-106] little sponge sacred to the purpose. This important duty done, she

[028-107] returned to her work. Any one who has ever shifted feathers from one

[028-108] tick to another will not need to be told that when Anne finished she

[028-109] was a sight to behold. Her dress was white with down and fluff, and her

[028-110] front hair, escaping from under the handkerchief, was adorned with a

[028-111] veritable halo of feathers. At this auspicious moment a knock sounded

[028-112] at the kitchen door.

[028-113] “That must be Mr. Shearer,” thought Anne. “I’m in a dreadful mess but

[028-114] I’ll have to run down as I am, for he’s always in a hurry.”

[028-115] Down flew Anne to the kitchen door. If ever a charitable floor did open

[028-116] to swallow up a miserable, befeathered damsel the Green Gables porch

[028-117] floor should promptly have engulfed Anne at that moment. On the

[028-118] doorstep were standing [[priscilla-grant|Priscilla Grant]], golden and fair in silk attire,

[028-119] a short, stout gray-haired lady in a tweed suit, and another lady, tall

[028-120] stately, wonderfully gowned, with a beautiful, highbred face and large,

[028-121] black-lashed violet eyes, whom Anne “instinctively felt,” as she would

[028-122] have said in her earlier days, to be Mrs. Charlotte E. Morgan.

[028-123] In the dismay of the moment one thought stood out from the confusion of

[028-124] Anne’s mind and she grasped at it as at the proverbial straw. All Mrs.

[028-125] Morgan’s heroines were noted for “rising to the occasion.” No matter

[028-126] what their troubles were, they invariably rose to the occasion and

[028-127] showed their superiority over all ills of time, space, and quantity.

[028-128] Anne therefore felt it was _her_ duty to rise to the occasion and she

[028-129] did it, so perfectly that Priscilla afterward declared she never

[028-130] admired Anne Shirley more than at that moment. No matter what her

[028-131] outraged feelings were she did not show them. She greeted Priscilla and

[028-132] was introduced to her companions as calmly and composedly as if she had

[028-133] been arrayed in purple and fine linen. To be sure, it was somewhat of a

[028-134] shock to find that the lady she had instinctively felt to be Mrs.

[028-135] Morgan was not Mrs. Morgan at all, but an unknown Mrs. Pendexter, while

[028-136] the stout little gray-haired woman was Mrs. Morgan; but in the greater

[028-137] shock the lesser lost its power. Anne ushered her guests to the spare

[028-138] room and thence into the parlor, where she left them while she hastened

[028-139] out to help Priscilla unharness her horse.

[028-140] “It’s dreadful to come upon you so unexpectedly as this,” apologized

[028-141] Priscilla, “but I did not know till last night that we were coming.

[028-142] Aunt Charlotte is going away Monday and she had promised to spend today

[028-143] with a friend in town. But last night her friend telephoned to her not

[028-144] to come because they were quarantined for scarlet fever. So I suggested

[028-145] we come here instead, for I knew you were [[envy|longing]] to see her. We called

[028-146] at the [[white-sands|White Sands]] Hotel and brought Mrs. Pendexter with us. She is a

[028-147] friend of aunt’s and lives in New York and her husband is a

[028-148] millionaire. We can’t stay very long, for Mrs. Pendexter has to be back

[028-149] at the hotel by five o’clock.”

[028-150] Several times while they were putting away the horse Anne caught

[028-151] Priscilla looking at her in a furtive, puzzled way.

[028-152] “She needn’t stare at me so,” Anne thought a little resentfully. “If

[028-153] she doesn’t _know_ what it is to change a feather bed she might

[028-154] _imagine_ it.”

[028-155] When Priscilla had gone to the parlor, and before Anne could escape

[028-156] upstairs, Diana walked into the kitchen. Anne caught her astonished

[028-157] friend by the arm.

[028-158] “Diana Barry, who do you suppose is in that parlor at this very moment?

[028-159] Mrs. Charlotte E. Morgan . . . and a New York millionaire’s wife . . .

[028-160] and here I am like _this_ . . . and _not a thing in the house for

[028-161] dinner but a cold ham bone_, Diana!”

[028-162] By this time Anne had become aware that Diana was staring at her in

[028-163] precisely the same bewildered fashion as Priscilla had done. It was

[028-164] really too much.

[028-165] “Oh, Diana, don’t look at me so,” she implored. “_You_, at least, must

[028-166] know that the neatest person in the world couldn’t empty feathers from

[028-167] one tick into another and remain neat in the process.”

[028-168] “It . . . it . . . isn’t the feathers,” hesitated Diana. “It’s . . .

[028-169] it’s . . . your nose, Anne.”

[028-170] “My nose? Oh, Diana, surely nothing has gone wrong with it!”

[028-171] Anne rushed to the little looking glass over the sink. One glance

[028-172] revealed the fatal truth. Her nose was a brilliant scarlet!

[028-173] Anne sat down on the sofa, her dauntless spirit subdued at last.
