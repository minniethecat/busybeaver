[035-001] “Ah, well, we’ll just have to plant them over again next spring,” said

[035-002] [[anne-shirley|Anne]] philosophically. “That is one good thing about this world . . .

[035-003] there are always sure to be more springs.”

[035-004] XXV

[035-005] An [[avonlea|Avonlea]] Scandal

[035-006] One blithe June morning, a fortnight after [[uncle-abe|Uncle Abe]]’s storm, Anne came

[035-007] slowly through the [[green-gables|Green Gables]] yard from the garden, carrying in her

[035-008] hands two blighted stalks of white narcissus.

[035-009] “Look, [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]],” she said sorrowfully, holding up the flowers before

[035-010] the eyes of a grim lady, with her hair coifed in a green gingham apron,

[035-011] who was going into the house with a plucked chicken, “these are the

[035-012] only buds the storm spared . . . and even they are imperfect. I’m so

[035-013] sorry . . . I wanted some for Matthew’s grave. He was always so fond of

[035-014] June lilies.”

[035-015] “I kind of miss them myself,” admitted Marilla, “though it doesn’t seem

[035-016] right to lament over them when so many worse things have happened. . .

[035-017] all the crops destroyed as well as the fruit.”

[035-018] “But people have sown their oats over again,” said Anne comfortingly,

[035-019] “and [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] says he thinks if we have a good summer they will

[035-020] come out all right though late. And my annuals are all coming up again

[035-021] . . . but oh, nothing can replace the June lilies. Poor little Hester

[035-022] Gray will have none either. I went all the way back to her garden last

[035-023] night but there wasn’t one. I’m sure she’ll miss them.”

[035-024] “I don’t think it’s right for you to say such things, Anne, I really

[035-025] don’t,” said Marilla severely. “[[hester-gray|Hester Gray]] has been dead for thirty

[035-026] years and her spirit is in heaven . . . I hope.”

[035-027] “Yes, but I believe she loves and remembers her garden here still,”

[035-028] said Anne. “I’m sure no matter how long I’d lived in heaven I’d like to

[035-029] look down and see somebody putting flowers on my grave. If I had had a

[035-030] garden here like Hester Gray’s it would take me more than thirty years,

[035-031] even in heaven, to forget being homesick for it by spells.”

[035-032] “Well, don’t let the twins hear you talking like that,” was Marilla’s

[035-033] feeble protest, as she carried her chicken into the house.

[035-034] Anne pinned her narcissi on her hair and went to the lane gate, where

[035-035] she stood for awhile sunning herself in the June brightness before

[035-036] going in to attend to her Saturday morning duties. The world was

[035-037] growing lovely again; old Mother Nature was doing her best to remove

[035-038] the traces of the storm, and, though she was not to succeed fully for

[035-039] many a moon, she was really accomplishing wonders.

[035-040] “I wish I could just be idle all day today,” Anne told a bluebird, who

[035-041] was singing and swinging on a willow bough, “but a schoolma’am, who is

[035-042] also helping to bring up twins, can’t indulge in laziness, birdie. How

[035-043] sweet you are singing, little bird. You are just putting the feelings

[035-044] of my heart into song ever so much better than I could myself. Why, who

[035-045] is coming?”

[035-046] An express wagon was jolting up the lane, with two people on the front

[035-047] seat and a big trunk behind. When it drew near Anne recognized the

[035-048] driver as the son of the station agent at [[bright-river|Bright River]]; but his

[035-049] companion was a stranger . . . a scrap of a woman who sprang nimbly

[035-050] down at the gate almost before the horse came to a standstill. She was

[035-051] a very pretty little person, evidently nearer fifty than forty, but

[035-052] with rosy cheeks, sparkling black eyes, and shining black hair,

[035-053] surmounted by a wonderful beflowered and beplumed bonnet. In spite of

[035-054] having driven eight miles over a dusty road she was as neat as if she

[035-055] had just stepped out of the proverbial bandbox.

[035-056] “Is this where Mr. James A. Harrison lives?” she inquired briskly.

[035-057] “No, Mr. Harrison lives over there,” said Anne, quite lost in

[035-058] astonishment.

[035-059] “Well, I _did_ think this [[belonging|place]] seemed too tidy . . . _much_ too tidy

[035-060] for James A. to be living here, unless he has greatly changed since I

[035-061] knew him,” chirped the little lady. “Is it true that James A. is going

[035-062] to be married to some woman living in this settlement?”

[035-063] “No, oh no,” cried Anne, flushing so guiltily that the stranger looked

[035-064] curiously at her, as if she half suspected her of matrimonial designs

[035-065] on Mr. Harrison.

[035-066] “But I saw it in an Island paper,” persisted the Fair Unknown. “A

[035-067] friend sent a marked copy to me . . . friends are always so ready to do

[035-068] such things. James A.‘s name was written in over ‘new citizen.’”

[035-069] “Oh, that note was only meant as a joke,” gasped Anne. “Mr. Harrison

[035-070] has no intention of marrying _anybody_. I assure you he hasn’t.”

[035-071] “I’m very glad to hear it,” said the rosy lady, climbing nimbly back to

[035-072] her seat in the wagon, “because he happens to be married already. _I_

[035-073] am his wife. Oh, you may well look surprised. I suppose he has been

[035-074] masquerading as a bachelor and breaking hearts right and left. Well,

[035-075] well, James A.,” nodding vigorously over the fields at the long white

[035-076] house, “your fun is over. I am here . . . though I wouldn’t have

[035-077] bothered coming if I hadn’t thought you were up to some mischief. I

[035-078] suppose,” turning to Anne, “that parrot of his is as profane as ever?”

[035-079] “His parrot . . . is dead . . . I _think_,” gasped poor Anne, who

[035-080] couldn’t have felt sure of her own name at that precise moment.

[035-081] “Dead! Everything will be all right then,” cried the rosy lady

[035-082] jubilantly. “I can manage James A. if that bird is out of the way.”

[035-083] With which cryptic utterance she went joyfully on her way and Anne flew

[035-084] to the kitchen door to meet Marilla.

[035-085] “Anne, who was that woman?”

[035-086] “Marilla,” said Anne solemnly, but with dancing eyes, “do I look as if

[035-087] I were crazy?”

[035-088] “Not more so than usual,” said Marilla, with no thought of being

[035-089] sarcastic.

[035-090] “Well then, do you think I am awake?”

[035-091] “Anne, what nonsense has got into you? Who was that woman, I say?”

[035-092] “Marilla, if I’m not crazy and not asleep she can’t be such stuff as

[035-093] [[ambition|dreams]] are made of . . . she must be real. Anyway, I’m sure I couldn’t

[035-094] have imagined such a bonnet. She says she is Mr. Harrison’s wife,

[035-095] Marilla.”

[035-096] Marilla stared in her turn.

[035-097] “His wife! Anne Shirley! Then what has he been passing himself off as

[035-098] an unmarried man for?”

[035-099] “I don’t suppose he did, really,” said Anne, trying to be just. “He

[035-100] never said he wasn’t married. People simply took it for granted. Oh

[035-101] Marilla, what will [[rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] say to this?”

[035-102] They found out what [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] had to say when she came up that

[035-103] evening. [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] wasn’t surprised! Mrs. Lynde had always expected

[035-104] something of the sort! Mrs. Lynde had always known there was

[035-105] _something_ about Mr. Harrison!

[035-106] “To think of his deserting his wife!” she said indignantly. “It’s like

[035-107] something you’d read of in the States, but who would expect such a

[035-108] thing to happen right here in Avonlea?”

[035-109] “But we don’t know that he deserted her,” protested Anne, determined to

[035-110] believe her friend innocent till he was proved guilty. “We don’t know

[035-111] the rights of it at all.”

[035-112] “Well, we soon will. I’m going straight over there,” said Mrs. Lynde,

[035-113] who had never learned that there was such a word as delicacy in the

[035-114] dictionary. “I’m not supposed to know anything about her arrival, and

[035-115] Mr. Harrison was to bring some medicine for Thomas from [[carmody|Carmody]] today,

[035-116] so that will be a good excuse. I’ll find out the whole story and come

[035-117] in and tell you on the way back.”

[035-118] Mrs. Lynde rushed in where Anne had feared to tread. Nothing would have

[035-119] induced the latter to go over to the Harrison place; but she had her

[035-120] natural and proper share of curiosity and she felt secretly glad that

[035-121] Mrs. Lynde was going to solve the mystery. She and Marilla waited

[035-122] expectantly for that good lady’s return, but waited in vain. Mrs. Lynde

[035-123] did not revisit Green Gables that night. [[davy-keith|Davy]], arriving home at nine

[035-124] o’clock from the Boulter place, explained why.

[035-125] “I met Mrs. Lynde and some strange woman in the Hollow,” he said, “and

[035-126] gracious, how they were talking both at once! Mrs. Lynde said to tell

[035-127] you she was sorry it was too late to call tonight. Anne, I’m awful

[035-128] hungry. We had tea at Milty’s at four and I think Mrs. Boulter is real

[035-129] mean. She didn’t give us any preserves or cake . . . and even the bread

[035-130] was skurce.”

[035-131] “Davy, when you go visiting you must never criticize anything you are

[035-132] given to eat,” said Anne solemnly. “It is very bad manners.”

[035-133] “All right . . . I’ll only think it,” said Davy cheerfully. “Do give a

[035-134] fellow some supper, Anne.”

[035-135] Anne looked at Marilla, who followed her into the pantry and shut the

[035-136] door cautiously.

[035-137] “You can give him some jam on his bread, I know what tea at Levi

[035-138] Boulter’s is apt to be.”

[035-139] Davy took his slice of bread and jam with a sigh.

[035-140] “It’s a kind of disappointing world after all,” he remarked. “Milty has

[035-141] a cat that takes fits . . . she’s took a fit regular every day for

[035-142] three weeks. Milty says it’s awful fun to watch her. I went down today

[035-143] on purpose to see her have one but the mean old thing wouldn’t take a

[035-144] fit and just kept healthy as healthy, though Milty and me hung round

[035-145] all the afternoon and waited. But never mind” . . . Davy brightened up

[035-146] as the insidious comfort of the plum jam stole into his soul . . .

[035-147] “maybe I’ll see her in one sometime yet. It doesn’t seem likely she’d

[035-148] stop having them all at once when she’s been so in the habit of it,

[035-149] does it? This jam is awful nice.”

[035-150] Davy had no sorrows that plum jam could not cure.

[035-151] Sunday proved so rainy that there was no stirring abroad; but by Monday

[035-152] everybody had heard some version of the Harrison story. The school

[035-153] buzzed with it and Davy came home, full of information.

[035-154] “Marilla, Mr. Harrison has a new wife . . . well, not ezackly new, but

[035-155] they’ve stopped being married for quite a spell, Milty says. I always

[035-156] s’posed people had to keep on being married once they’d begun, but

[035-157] Milty says no, there’s ways of stopping if you can’t agree. Milty says

[035-158] one way is just to start off and leave your wife, and that’s what Mr.

[035-159] Harrison did. Milty says Mr. Harrison left his wife because she throwed

[035-160] things at him . . . _hard_ things . . . and Arty Sloane says it was

[035-161] because she wouldn’t let him smoke, and Ned Clay says it was ’cause she

[035-162] never let up scolding him. I wouldn’t leave MY wife for anything like

[035-163] that. I’d just put my foot down and say, ‘Mrs. Davy, you’ve just got to

[035-164] do what’ll please _me_ ’cause I’m a _man_.’ _That’d_ settle her pretty

[035-165] quick I guess. But [[annetta-bell|Annetta]] Clay says _she_ left _him_ because he

[035-166] wouldn’t scrape his boots at the door and she doesn’t blame her. I’m

[035-167] going right over to Mr. Harrison’s this minute to see what she’s like.”

[035-168] Davy soon returned, somewhat cast down.

[035-169] “[[mrs-harrison|Mrs. Harrison]] was away . . . she’s gone to Carmody with Mrs. Rachel

[035-170] Lynde to get new paper for the parlor. And Mr. Harrison said to tell

[035-171] Anne to go over and see him ’cause he wants to have a talk with her.

[035-172] And say, the floor is scrubbed, and Mr. Harrison is shaved, though

[035-173] there wasn’t any preaching yesterday.”

[035-174] The Harrison kitchen wore a very unfamiliar look to Anne. The floor was

[035-175] indeed scrubbed to a wonderful pitch of purity and so was every article

[035-176] of furniture in the room; the stove was polished until she could see

[035-177] her face in it; the walls were whitewashed and the window panes

[035-178] sparkled in the sunlight. By the table sat Mr. Harrison in his working

[035-179] clothes, which on Friday had been noted for sundry rents and tatters

[035-180] but which were now neatly patched and brushed. He was sprucely shaved

[035-181] and what little hair he had was carefully trimmed.
