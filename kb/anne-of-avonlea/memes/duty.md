# Duty

The moral principle that shapes [[anne-shirley|Anne Shirley]]'s transition from imaginative girl to responsible young woman — a framework of obligation that governs her teaching, her guardianship of [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora]], and her [[belonging|place]] in the [[avonlea|Avonlea]] [[neighbor|community]].

## Definition

In *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*, duty represents the counterweight to imagination in [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s developing character. The novel opens [[humor|wit]]h an allusion to "the careful ways of duty" (001-009), immediately signaling that adult responsibility will be a central concern. Where imagination opens possibilities, duty imposes obligations — yet Montgomery presents duty not as a burden but as a form of [[romance|love]] expressed through action. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] articulates her mature view when she reflects that teaching is a "teacher's duty to find and develop" each pupil's potential (005-076), framing obligation as an act of care rather than mere compliance.

The novel consistently shows that duty freely accepted transforms character: "It does people good to have to do things they don't like . . . in a spirit of duty" (009-148). This conviction — that duty is formative, not merely restrictive — underpins the entire narrative arc.

## Examples in the Text

**Taking in the Twins (Chapter 9).** When [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] contemplates adopting [[davy-keith|Davy]] and [[dora-keith|Dora Keith]] after their mother's death, [[anne-shirley|Anne]] does not hesitate: "it is our duty to take them" (009-140). She acknowledges the difficulty — "if I can take the children from a sense of duty surely I can do their sewing" (009-147) — but this self-deprecating [[laughter|humor]] reveals a deep truth: for [[anne-shirley|Anne]], duty is not separate from [[kindness|compassion]] but its natural expression. [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] notes admiringly that [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "did what she believed to be her duty" (009-128), and this phrase captures the essence of [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s moral character throughout the novel.

**The Teacher's Vocation (Chapter 5).** [[anne-shirley|Anne]] approaches teaching as a moral calling. She believes it is "a teacher's duty to find and develop" each child's unique gifts (005-076), a philosophy that guides her patient work [[humor|wit]]h struggling students and her firm handling of the defiant [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]. Her commitment is not to popularity but to genuine [[teaching|education]], measured by growth rather than [[love|affection]].

**[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s Conception of Duty (Chapter 9).** Montgomery provides comic counterpoint through [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]], who takes it upon herself to convey unpleasant news under the banner of obligation. She tells [[anne-shirley|Anne]] "it was her duty to inform" [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla]] of various matters (009-029), and believes "it is their duty to tell you a certain thing" whether welcome or not (009-032). The irony is gentle but unmistakable: duty invoked as a pretext for gossip reveals how the language of obligation can be weaponized. [[anne-shirley|Anne]] navigates this [[humor|wit]]h characteristic diplomacy, "skillfully" managing [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]'s intrusions (009-128).

**[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]'s Filial Duty (Chapter 20).** Young [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] articulates a touching child's version of duty when he explains his relationship [[humor|wit]]h his grandmother: "next to father if it wasn't my *duty* to [[romance|love]] Grandma Irving best" (020-133). For [[paul-irving|Paul]], duty and [[romance|love]] are inseparably fused — he [[romance|love]]s because he ought, and he ought because he [[romance|love]]s. This innocent wisdom reflects the novel's central thesis that true duty is [[romance|love]] made visible.

**Daily Duty as Life's Rhythm (Chapter 41).** Near the novel's end, the theme finds quiet resolution. [[stephen-irving|Mr. Irving]] "tramped away to do his 'daily duty'" (041-166), and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]] remarks that "[[jane-andrews|Jane]] will do her duty, I've no doubt" (041-033). Duty has become not a dramatic struggle but the steady rhythm of a well-lived life — the final statement of a novel that began [[humor|wit]]h "the careful ways of duty" (001-009) and ends [[humor|wit]]h duty fulfilled in [[romance|love]] and contentment.

## Thematic Significance

**The Architecture of Adulthood.** Duty is the structural principle of grown-up life in *[[anne-shirley|Anne]] of [[avonlea|Avonlea]]*. [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s girlhood was defined by imagination and mishap; her young womanhood is defined by responsibilities willingly embraced. The novel argues that maturity is measured not by age but by the duties one undertakes and fulfills.

**Duty as Moral [[education|Education]].** Montgomery presents duty as a teacher in its own right: "It does people good to have to do things they don't like" (009-148). The difficulties that duty imposes are not punishments but opportunities for growth. This is a distinctly Victorian moral sensibility, rendered [[humor|wit]]hout sanctimony through [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s warm, self-aware narration.

**[[community|Community]] and Be[[envy|longing]].** Each duty [[anne-shirley|Anne]] accepts — teaching, caring for the twins, participating in [[community|village life]] — deepens her roots in [[avonlea|Avonlea]]. Duty is the mechanism by which she becomes not merely a resident but a member of a living [[neighbor|community]].

## Contrasting Perspectives

**[[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Rachel Lynde]]** represents the perversion of duty into self-righteous meddling. Her conviction that "it was her duty to inform" others of unpleasant truths (009-029) reveals how duty's language can be appropriated for less noble purposes. Montgomery's satire is gentle but effective.

**[[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]]** embodies a sterner, more traditional conception of duty. Her decision to take in the twins springs from moral obligation rather than warmth, yet her steady, unromantic fulfillment of responsibility provides the foundation on which [[anne-shirley|Anne]]'s more emotionally expressive duty can flourish.

**[[paul-irving|Paul Irving]]** offers the novel's most innocent and integrated vision of duty — one in which [[romance|love]] and obligation are indistinguishable. "Next to father if it wasn't my *duty* to [[romance|love]] Grandma Irving best" (020-133) expresses a wisdom the novel ultimately endorses.
