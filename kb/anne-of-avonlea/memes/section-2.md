[002-001] “Well, mind you she doesn’t,” muttered [[mr-harrison|Mr. Harrison]] in a somewhat

[002-002] subdued tone; but he stamped off angrily enough and [[anne-shirley|Anne]] heard him

[002-003] growling to himself until he was out of earshot.

[002-004] Grievously disturbed in mind, Anne marched across the yard and shut the

[002-005] naughty Jersey up in the milking pen.

[002-006] “She can’t possibly get out of that unless she tears the fence down,”

[002-007] she reflected. “She looks pretty quiet now. I daresay she has sickened

[002-008] herself on those oats. I wish I’d sold her to Mr. Shearer when he

[002-009] wanted her last week, but I thought it was just as well to wait until

[002-010] we had the auction of the stock and let them all go together. I believe

[002-011] it is true about Mr. Harrison being a crank. Certainly there’s nothing

[002-012] of the [[kindred-spirits|kindred spirit]] about _him_.”

[002-013] Anne had always a weather eye open for [[friendship|kindred spirits]].

[002-014] [[marilla-cuthbert|Marilla Cuthbert]] was driving into the yard as Anne returned from the

[002-015] house, and the latter flew to get tea ready. They discussed the matter

[002-016] at the tea table.

[002-017] “I’ll be glad when the auction is over,” said Marilla. “It is too much

[002-018] [[duty|responsibility]] having so much stock about the [[belonging|place]] and nobody but that

[002-019] unreliable [[martin|Martin]] to look after them. He has never come back yet and he

[002-020] promised that he would certainly be back last night if I’d give him the

[002-021] day off to go to his aunt’s funeral. I don’t know how many aunts he has

[002-022] got, I am sure. That’s the fourth that’s died since he hired here a

[002-023] year ago. I’ll be more than thankful when the crop is in and [[mr-barry|Mr. Barry]]

[002-024] takes over the farm. We’ll have to keep Dolly shut up in the pen till

[002-025] Martin comes, for she must be put in the back pasture and the fences

[002-026] there have to be fixed. I declare, it is a world of trouble, as [[rachel-lynde|Rachel]]

[002-027] says. Here’s poor Mary Keith dying and what is to become of those two

[002-028] children of hers is more than I know. She has a brother in British

[002-029] Columbia and she has written to him about them, but she hasn’t heard

[002-030] from him yet.”

[002-031] “What are the children like? How old are they?”

[002-032] “Six past . . . they’re twins.”

[002-033] “Oh, I’ve always been especially interested in twins ever since Mrs.

[002-034] Hammond had so many,” said Anne eagerly. “Are they pretty?”

[002-035] “Goodness, you couldn’t tell . . . they were too dirty. [[davy-keith|Davy]] had been

[002-036] out making mud pies and [[dora-keith|Dora]] went out to call him in. Davy pushed her

[002-037] headfirst into the biggest pie and then, because she cried, he got into

[002-038] it himself and wallowed in it to show her it was nothing to cry about.

[002-039] Mary said Dora was really a very good child but that Davy was full of

[002-040] mischief. He has never had any bringing up you might say. His father

[002-041] died when he was a baby and Mary has been sick almost ever since.”

[002-042] “I’m always sorry for children that have no bringing up,” said Anne

[002-043] soberly. “You know _I_ hadn’t any till you took me in hand. I hope

[002-044] their uncle will look after them. Just what relation is Mrs. Keith to

[002-045] you?”

[002-046] “Mary? None in the world. It was her husband . . . he was our third

[002-047] cousin. There’s [[mrs-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]] coming through the yard. I thought she’d be

[002-048] up to hear about Mary.”

[002-049] “Don’t tell her about Mr. Harrison and the cow,” implored Anne.

[002-050] Marilla promised; but the promise was quite unnecessary, for [[mrs-rachel-lynde|Mrs. Lynde]]

[002-051] was no sooner fairly seated than she said,

[002-052] “I saw Mr. Harrison chasing your Jersey out of his oats today when I

[002-053] was coming home from [[carmody|Carmody]]. I thought he looked pretty mad. Did he

[002-054] make much of a rumpus?”

[002-055] Anne and Marilla furtively exchanged amused smiles. Few things in

[002-056] [[avonlea|Avonlea]] ever escaped Mrs. Lynde. It was only that morning Anne had

[002-057] said,

[002-058] “If you went to your own room at midnight, locked the door, pulled down

[002-059] the blind, and _sneezed_, Mrs. Lynde would ask you the next day how

[002-060] your cold was!”

[002-061] “I believe he did,” admitted Marilla. “I was away. He gave Anne a piece

[002-062] of his mind.”

[002-063] “I think he is a very disagreeable man,” said Anne, with a resentful

[002-064] toss of her ruddy head.

[002-065] “You never said a truer word,” said Mrs. Rachel solemnly. “I knew

[002-066] there’d be trouble when Robert Bell sold his place to a New Brunswick

[002-067] man, that’s what. I don’t know what Avonlea is coming to, with so many

[002-068] strange people rushing into it. It’ll soon not be safe to go to sleep

[002-069] in our beds.”

[002-070] “Why, what other strangers are coming in?” asked Marilla.

[002-071] “Haven’t you heard? Well, there’s a family of Donnells, for one thing.

[002-072] They’ve rented [[peter-sloane|Peter Sloane]]’s old house. Peter has hired the man to run

[002-073] his mill. They belong down east and nobody knows anything about them.

[002-074] Then that shiftless Timothy Cotton family are going to move up from

[002-075] [[white-sands|White Sands]] and they’ll simply be a burden on the public. He is in

[002-076] consumption . . . when he isn’t stealing . . . and his wife is a

[002-077] slack-twisted creature that can’t turn her hand to a thing. She washes

[002-078] her dishes _sitting down_. Mrs. George Pye has taken her husband’s

[002-079] orphan nephew, [[anthony-pye|Anthony Pye]]. He’ll be going to school to you, Anne, so

[002-080] you may expect trouble, that’s what. And you’ll have another strange

[002-081] pupil, too. [[paul-irving|Paul Irving]] is coming from the States to live with his

[002-082] grandmother. You remember his father, Marilla . . . [[stephen-irving|Stephen Irving]], him

[002-083] that jilted [[miss-lavendar-lewis|Lavendar Lewis]] over at [[grafton|Grafton]]?”

[002-084] “I don’t think he jilted her. There was a quarrel . . . I suppose there

[002-085] was blame on both sides.”

[002-086] “Well, anyway, he didn’t marry her, and she’s been as queer as possible

[002-087] ever since, they say . . . living all by herself in that little stone

[002-088] house she calls [[echo-lodge|Echo Lodge]]. Stephen went off to the States and went

[002-089] into business with his uncle and married a Yankee. He’s never been home

[002-090] since, though his mother has been up to see him once or twice. His wife

[002-091] died two years ago and he’s sending the boy home to his mother for a

[002-092] spell. He’s ten years old and I don’t know if he’ll be a very desirable

[002-093] pupil. You can never tell about those Yankees.”

[002-094] Mrs Lynde looked upon all people who had the misfortune to be born or

[002-095] brought up elsewhere than in [[prince-edward-island|Prince Edward Island]] with a decided

[002-096] can-any-good-thing-come-out-of-Nazareth air. They _might_ be good

[002-097] people, of course; but you were on the safe side in doubting it. She

[002-098] had a special prejudice against “Yankees.” Her husband had been cheated

[002-099] out of ten dollars by an employer for whom he had once worked in Boston

[002-100] and neither angels nor principalities nor powers could have convinced

[002-101] Mrs. Rachel that the whole United States was not responsible for it.

[002-102] “Avonlea school won’t be the worse for a little new blood,” said

[002-103] Marilla drily, “and if this boy is anything like his father he’ll be

[002-104] all right. Steve Irving was the nicest boy that was ever raised in

[002-105] these parts, though some people did call him proud. I should think Mrs.

[002-106] Irving would be very glad to have the child. She has been very lonesome

[002-107] since her husband died.”

[002-108] “Oh, the boy may be well enough, but he’ll be different from Avonlea

[002-109] children,” said Mrs. Rachel, as if that clinched the matter. Mrs.

[002-110] Rachel’s opinions concerning any person, place, or thing, were always

[002-111] warranted to wear. “What’s this I hear about your going to start up a

[002-112] [[avonlea-village-improvement-society|Village Improvement Society]], Anne?”

[002-113] “I was just talking it over with some of the girls and boys at the last

[002-114] Debating Club,” said Anne, flushing. “They thought it would be rather

[002-115] nice . . . and so do Mr. and [[mrs-allan|Mrs. Allan]]. Lots of villages have them

[002-116] now.”

[002-117] “Well, you’ll get into no end of hot water if you do. Better leave it

[002-118] alone, Anne, that’s what. People don’t like being improved.”

[002-119] “Oh, we are not going to try to improve the _people_. It is Avonlea

[002-120] itself. There are lots of things which might be done to make it

[002-121] prettier. For instance, if we could coax Mr. Levi Boulter to pull down

[002-122] that dreadful old house on his upper farm wouldn’t that be an

[002-123] improvement?”

[002-124] “It certainly would,” admitted Mrs. Rachel. “That old ruin has been an

[002-125] eyesore to the settlement for years. But if you Improvers can coax Levi

[002-126] Boulter to do anything for the public that he isn’t to be paid for

[002-127] doing, may I be there to see and hear the process, that’s what. I don’t

[002-128] want to discourage you, Anne, for there may be something in your idea,

[002-129] though I suppose you did get it out of some rubbishy Yankee magazine;

[002-130] but you’ll have your hands full with your school and I advise you as a

[002-131] friend not to bother with your improvements, that’s what. But there, I

[002-132] know you’ll go ahead with it if you’ve set your mind on it. You were

[002-133] always one to carry a thing through somehow.”

[002-134] Something about the firm outlines of Anne’s lips told that Mrs. Rachel

[002-135] was not far astray in this estimate. Anne’s heart was bent on forming

[002-136] the Improvement Society. [[gilbert-blythe|Gilbert Blythe]], who was to teach in White

[002-137] Sands but would always be home from Friday night to Monday morning, was

[002-138] enthusiastic about it; and most of the other folks were willing to go

[002-139] in for anything that meant occasional meetings and consequently some

[002-140] “fun.” As for what the “improvements” were to be, nobody had any very

[002-141] clear idea except Anne and Gilbert. They had talked them over and

[002-142] planned them out until an ideal Avonlea existed in their minds, if

[002-143] nowhere else.

[002-144] Mrs. Rachel had still another item of news.

[002-145] “They’ve given the Carmody school to a [[priscilla-grant|Priscilla Grant]]. Didn’t you go

[002-146] to Queen’s with a girl of that name, Anne?”

[002-147] “Yes, indeed. Priscilla to teach at Carmody! How perfectly lovely!”

[002-148] exclaimed Anne, her gray eyes lighting up until they looked like

[002-149] evening stars, causing Mrs. Lynde to wonder anew if she would ever get

[002-150] it settled to her satisfaction whether Anne Shirley were really a

[002-151] pretty girl or not.

[002-152] II

[002-153] Selling in Haste and Repenting at Leisure

[002-154] Anne drove over to Carmody on a shopping expedition the next afternoon

[002-155] and took [[diana-barry|Diana Barry]] with her. Diana was, of course, a pledged member

[002-156] of the Improvement Society, and the two girls talked about little else

[002-157] all the way to Carmody and back.

[002-158] “The very first thing we ought to do when we get started is to have

[002-159] that hall painted,” said Diana, as they drove past the Avonlea hall, a

[002-160] rather shabby building set down in a wooded hollow, with spruce trees

[002-161] hooding it about on all sides. “It’s a disgraceful looking place and we

[002-162] must attend to it even before we try to get Mr. Levi Boulder to pull

[002-163] his house down. Father says we’ll never succeed in doing _that_ . . .

[002-164] Levi Boulter is too mean to spend the time it would take.”

[002-165] “Perhaps he’ll let the boys take it down if they promise to haul the

[002-166] boards and split them up for him for kindling wood,” said Anne

[002-167] hopefully. “We must do our best and be content to go slowly at first.

[002-168] We can’t expect to improve everything all at once. We’ll have to

[002-169] educate public sentiment first, of course.”

[002-170] Diana wasn’t exactly sure what educating public sentiment meant; but it

[002-171] sounded fine and she felt rather proud that she was going to belong to

[002-172] a [[community|society]] with such an aim in view.

[002-173] “I thought of something last night that we could do, Anne. You know

[002-174] that three-cornered piece of ground where the roads from Carmody and

[002-175] [[newbridge|Newbridge]] and White Sands meet? It’s all grown over with young spruce;

[002-176] but wouldn’t it be nice to have them all cleared out, and just leave

[002-177] the two or three birch trees that are on it?”
