## Identity

Cassandra is a daughter of Priam and Hecuba, described by Homer as "the fairest of Priam's daughters" (013-321) and likened in beauty to "golden Venus" (024-614). She is a princess of Troy's royal house, sister to Hector, Paris, Deiphobus, and many others. In the *Iliad*, her prophetic gift — so central to later myth — goes unmentioned; instead, she appears as a figure of domestic and civic significance, her beauty and her place in the royal family defining her role.

Cassandra's name in Homer carries no hint of the tragic prophetess of later tradition (Aeschylus' *Agamemnon*, Euripides' *Trojan Women*). The *Iliad* presents her simply as Priam's most beautiful daughter, a prize sought by warriors and a witness to her family's sorrow. This Homeric restraint is characteristic of the epic's treatment of many figures whose post-Homeric legends would expand dramatically.

## Role in the Epic

Cassandra appears twice in the *Iliad*, each appearance framing a moment of profound significance for the Trojan royal house.

**The Suitor Othryoneus (Book XIII):** Cassandra is first mentioned as the bride sought by Othryoneus, a warrior from Cabesus who had "but lately come to take part in the war" (013-320). Othryoneus sought Cassandra in marriage "but offered no gifts of wooing, for he promised a great thing, to wit, that he would drive the sons of the Achaeans willy nilly from Troy" (013-322). Priam consented and promised Cassandra to him, "whereon he fought on the strength of the promises thus made" (013-325). Othryoneus is killed in battle, struck by Idomeneus' spear — his hopes of Cassandra and glory alike cut short. This episode reveals Cassandra as a token of the marriage economy that binds the heroic world: her hand is promised in exchange for martial service, her fate determined by the calculations of men at war.

**The Return of Hector's Body (Book XXIV):** Cassandra's second and final appearance is among the most visually striking moments in the epic. As Priam returns from the Greek camp with Hector's body, "no one neither man nor woman saw them, till Cassandra, fair as golden Venus standing on Pergamus, caught sight of her dear father in his chariot" (024-613). She is the first to see them — positioned on the citadel, her vantage point elevated above the city. With "a loud cry she went about the city saying, 'Come hither Trojans, men and women, and look on Hector; if ever you rejoiced to see him coming from battle when he was alive, look now on him that was the glory of our city and all our people'" (024-617).

Her cry rouses the entire city: "At this there was not man nor woman left in the city, so great a" grief seized them (024-622). Cassandra thus becomes the herald of grief — the first voice to announce Hector's return to Troy, not as the living champion but as a corpse. Her announcement transforms the private sorrow of Priam's embassy into communal mourning, and her position "on Pergamus" — the highest point of Troy — makes her, for this moment, the city's eyes and voice.

## Notable Speeches

> "Come hither Trojans, men and women, and look on Hector; if ever you rejoiced to see him coming from battle when he was alive, look now on him that was the glory of our city and all our people." (024-618)

Cassandra's only direct speech in the *Iliad* is a public summons to mourning. It is not a personal lament (unlike the speeches of Hecuba and Andromache that follow) but a civic proclamation — the princess calling her people to witness what war has taken from them.

## Relationships

**Priam:** Cassandra is Priam's daughter, and her marriage was promised by "old King Priam" himself (013-324). Her sighting of "her dear father in his chariot" (024-615) at the epic's close reunites father and daughter in shared grief.

**Hecuba:** As daughter of Hecuba, Cassandra shares her mother's sorrow for Hector, though their modes of expression differ — Hecuba's lament is intimate and maternal, while Cassandra's is public and declarative.

**Hector:** Cassandra's love for her brother Hector is implicit in her cry to the Trojans. She does not mourn him privately but summons the entire city to look upon him, transforming personal loss into communal ritual. Her description of him as "the glory of our city and all our people" (024-620) echoes the epic's own assessment of Hector's worth.

**Othryoneus:** Cassandra's suitor, who fought for her hand without bride-gifts, promising instead to drive the Greeks from Troy (013-322). His death at Idomeneus' hands (Book XIII) is one of many examples in the *Iliad* of promised marriages severed by war.

**Andromache:** As Hector's wife, Andromache shares with Cassandra the grief of losing the city's greatest protector. Following Cassandra's public announcement, Andromache's more personal lament forms the next phase of the communal mourning.

**Helen:** Helen's relationship with Cassandra is one of shared household — both are women of Priam's extended family (Helen as daughter-in-law, Cassandra as daughter). At Hector's funeral, Helen delivers the third lament, after Hecuba and Andromache, with Cassandra's earlier public summons having initiated the ritual.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Cassandra's death. In post-Homeric tradition — most powerfully in Aeschylus' *Agamemnon* — Cassandra is a prophetess cursed by Apollo: she foresees the future but is never believed. After the fall of Troy, she is taken as Agamemnon's concubine and murdered by Clytemnestra upon her arrival at Mycenae. The *Iliad* knows none of this — or, if it does, it chooses not to tell it. Homer's Cassandra is simply the beautiful princess who becomes the voice of her city's grief, her cry from Pergamus the first note in the symphony of lamentation that closes the epic.

## Scholarly Notes

Cassandra's role in the *Iliad* is notable for what it omits. The absence of any reference to her prophetic powers has prompted extensive scholarly discussion. Some scholars argue that the prophetic Cassandra was a later development, unknown to Homer; others suggest that Homer deliberately suppressed the tradition, preferring to present her in a purely human role appropriate to the epic's domestic and civic concerns.

The image of Cassandra "fair as golden Venus standing on Pergamus" (024-614) is one of the most vivid visual tableaux in the epic. Her elevated position — on the citadel, looking down at the plain from which her father approaches — recalls Helen's position on the walls during the *Teichoscopia* (Book III), another beautiful woman whose perspective frames the city's fate. Helen looked out at the Greek heroes and identified them for Priam; Cassandra looks out and sees her father bringing home her dead brother. The two moments form a tragic arc across the poem.

The suitor Othryoneus episode (013-321) reveals the marriage economy that structures Homeric society: Cassandra's hand is a prize promised in exchange for martial service. Othryoneus' death before the marriage can be consummated is one of many "broken betrothals" in the epic, a motif that underscores war's destruction of the domestic sphere. Idomeneus' gloating over Othryoneus' corpse — mocking the dead man's failed ambition — adds a grim irony: the bride-price of Cassandra was to be the deliverance of Troy, and her suitor lies dead while Troy still burns.
