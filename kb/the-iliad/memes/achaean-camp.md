# Achaean Camp

The Achaean camp is the Greeks' temporary city on the Trojan shore. Built around the beached ships that brought them to Troy, it includes the tents of the kings, the defensive wall and ditch, the central assembly ground, and the altars where sacrifices are offered. The camp is both a military base and a community — a microcosm of the Achaean world, with its own hierarchies, rituals, and internal conflicts.

## Geography

The camp extends along the shore of the Hellespont, with the ships drawn up on the beach in rows. Key locations within the camp include: **Achilles' quarters** — at the far end of the line, where the embassy of Book IX finds him playing the lyre with Patroclus; **Agamemnon's tent** — the center of authority, where the council of elders meets; **the assembly ground** — where the army gathers for deliberation (Books I, II); **the defensive wall and ditch** — built in Book VII, the focus of the Trojan assault in Books XII–XV; and **the funeral site** — where Patroclus's pyre is built and where the funeral games of Book XXIII are held.

## Significance

The camp is the Achaeans' vulnerable homeland in exile. When Hector breaches the wall and reaches the ships, it is an existential crisis — not merely a military setback but the potential annihilation of the entire Achaean world. The camp's geography is also the geography of Achilles' withdrawal: his refusal to fight is physically expressed by his remaining at his ships, at the farthest remove from the communal center.
