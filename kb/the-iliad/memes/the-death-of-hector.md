# The Death of Hector

The death of Hector is the *Iliad*'s narrative and emotional climax. For twenty-two books, the epic has moved toward this moment: the greatest Greek warrior confronting the greatest Trojan warrior before the walls of the city Hector has defended. The duel is profoundly unequal — Achilles is protected by divine armor and driven by superhuman rage — and its outcome is never in doubt. What makes it unforgettable is not the fight but what surrounds it: Hector's solitary decision to face death, his parents' desperate pleas from the walls, and the terrible dignity with which he meets his end.

## Context

After Patroclus's death, Achilles has returned to battle in divine armor forged by Hephaestus. He has slaughtered his way through the Trojan army, choking the river Scamander with corpses, and now pursues Hector alone. All the Trojans have fled inside the city walls — all except Hector, who stands alone before the Scaean Gates, awaiting his killer.

## Participants

- **Achilles**: The pursuer, wearing divine armor, blazing with grief-driven fury
- **Hector**: The pursued, alone before his city, wrestling with shame and fate
- **Priam and Hecuba**: Watch from the walls, begging their son to come inside
- **Athena**: Deceives Hector by appearing as his brother Deiphobus, promising aid, then abandoning him
- **Apollo**: Having protected Hector throughout the epic, now abandons him to his fate

## Narrative

Hector's final moments are dominated by an extraordinary internal monologue. He considers fleeing into the city but is stopped by *aidōs* — the shame of facing the Trojans after leading them to disaster through his folly. He briefly imagines offering to return Helen and the stolen treasure, then realizes Achilles would kill him before he could speak. He decides to stand and fight.

When Achilles approaches, blazing like the Dog Star, Hector's courage fails. He runs. Three times around the walls of Troy they race — the killer and the killed — while the gods watch from Olympus. Zeus lifts his golden scales: Hector's fate sinks toward Hades. Athena descends, takes the form of Hector's brother Deiphobus, and convinces him to turn and fight together. But when Hector turns and throws his spear, he finds no brother beside him — only Athena, and Achilles, and death.

Achilles' spear finds the one gap in Hector's armor — his throat. As he dies, Hector prophesies once more: Achilles too will die, killed by Paris and Apollo at the Scaean Gates. Achilles' response — "Die, dog, and I will meet my fate whenever Zeus wills" — is the epic's coldest moment of inhuman rage.

## Outcome

Hector is dead. Achilles strips his body, pierces his ankles, ties him to his chariot, and drags him through the dust before the eyes of Priam and Hecuba. For twelve days, Achilles will defile Hector's body — and every morning, Apollo will restore it, preserving it for the burial that will close the epic.

## Significance

The death of Hector completes Achilles' revenge but not the epic. The desecration of the body that follows demonstrates that *mēnis*, once unleashed, cannot be satisfied by killing alone — it must degrade, humiliate, and destroy. Only Priam's supplication in Book XXIV will begin to heal the wound that Hector's death, paradoxically, has deepened rather than closed.

## Notable Passages

> "Then Hector knew the truth and said: 'Alas! The gods have called me to my death. I thought Deiphobus was near — but he is within the wall, and Athena has deceived me.'" (Book XXII)

> "Die, dog, and I will meet my fate whenever Zeus and the other immortals choose to send it." (Achilles to the dying Hector, Book XXII)
