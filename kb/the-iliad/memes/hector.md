# Hector

Hector is the crown prince of [[troy|Troy]], the eldest son of King [[priam|Priam]], and the foremost defender of his city against the Achaean invaders. Unlike [[achilles|Achilles]], who fights for personal [[glory|Glory (Kleos)]], Hector fights out of duty to his family, his people, and his homeland — making him the most fully human and sympathetic warrior in the Iliad. His farewell to Andromache at the Scaean Gate, his slaying of [[patroclus|Patroclus]], and his final, doomed confrontation with Achilles are among the most celebrated episodes in Western literature. His death and the ransom of his body by Priam form the epic's closing movement, transforming the poem from a tale of [[wrath|Wrath (Mēnis)]] into a meditation on mortality, compassion, and the cost of war.

## Identity

Hector is the eldest son of King Priam and Queen Hecuba, crown prince of Troy, and the commander-in-chief of the Trojan forces. He is the husband of Andromache and father of the infant Astyanax. Known as "Tamer of Horses" (*hippodamos*) and "Hector of the Gleaming Helmet" (*korythaiolos*), he embodies the ideal of the Homeric warrior: brave in battle, devoted to family, and dutiful to his city. Unlike many other heroes, Hector fights not for personal glory but out of a profound sense of obligation to protect his people and his homeland (001-031). He is also the Trojans' intermediary with the gods — when Iris delivers a divine message, Hector immediately recognizes the goddess and acts upon her command (002-655).

## Role in the Epic

Hector is the foremost Trojan champion and the central figure around whom the city's defense revolves. His battlefield prowess is unmatched among the Trojans, and the *Iliad* traces his arc from mighty defender to doomed hero.

Before major engagements, Hector is often seen rallying the Trojan ranks. He leads the assault that breaches the Greek wall, sets fire to the Greek ships, and kills Patroclus in Book 16 — an act that transforms Achilles' passive wrath into active vengeance and sets the stage for the epic's climax. After slaying Patroclus, Hector strips him of Achilles' borrowed armor, a symbolic act of triumph that seals his own fate. His death at the hands of Achilles in Book 22 represents the poem's tragic and emotional peak: the defender of civilization falls to the embodiment of unstoppable force.

## Notable Speeches

Hector's speeches are among the most memorable in the epic. He rebukes Paris for shirking battle, demonstrating his role as the moral conscience of his family:

> "Paris," said he, "your rebuke is just."
> — Hector to Paris, accepting his brother's willingness to fight (003-034)

> "Hector, your rebuke is just. You are hard as the axe that a shipwright wields."
> — Alexandrus (Paris) conceding to Hector's authority (003-051)

When Hector hears Paris agree to fight, he is glad and moves swiftly to arrange the truce (003-065). His farewell to Andromache in Book 6 is one of the most celebrated passages in Western literature, revealing his tenderness and tragic awareness that Troy will fall and his wife will be enslaved. His final soliloquy before the Scaean Gate, as Achilles approaches, is a masterful depiction of a hero confronting his fate:

> He stood as a serpent of the mountains, full fed on poisonous herbs, coiled and waiting; and a shudder ran through the Trojans as they looked on him.
> — Hector awaiting Achilles outside the walls of Troy (022-001–022-100)

## Relationships

**Family:** Hector is deeply devoted to his parents Priam and Hecuba, his wife Andromache, and his infant son Astyanax. The domestic scene in Book 6, where Astyanax recoils at his father's plumed helmet, humanizes Hector and underscores the personal cost of war. His relationship with each family member reveals a different facet of his character: filial piety toward Priam, protective love for Andromache, tenderness toward his son.

**Brothers:** His relationship with Paris is strained — Hector sees Paris as a source of Troy's suffering — yet he remains protective. He upbraids Paris publicly (003-034) but accepts his brother's willingness to fight (003-051). This fraternal tension encapsulates the broader Trojan dilemma: the war is fought for a cause Hector himself deplores.

**Allies:** Hector is served by loyal warriors throughout the Trojan ranks (005-191). He fights alongside Aeneas, Sarpedon, and Glaucus, and commands the respect of the Lycian and Dardanian allies. His leadership inspires devotion; even in defeat, his troops fight on in his name.

**Enemies:** His great adversary is [[achilles|Achilles]]. The contrast between the two — Hector the defender, Achilles the destroyer — is central to the poem's moral architecture. Hector also faces [[ajax-the-greater|Ajax (the Greater)]] in a duel (Book 7), fights [[diomedes|Diomedes]], and kills Patroclus while the latter wears Achilles' armor. His killing of Patroclus is the pivot upon which the entire second half of the poem turns.

## Death and Fate

Hector's death in Book 22 is the *Iliad*'s defining tragedy. After killing Patroclus, Hector faces Achilles in single combat outside the walls of Troy. Despite his courage, he is outmatched — his breastplate is no shield against Achilles' spear (002-335). In a moment of terrible clarity, Hector realizes that the gods have abandoned him: Athene has deceived him, and Apollo has withdrawn his protection. Achilles kills him with a spear-thrust to the throat, the only vulnerable point in Hector's captured armor. Achilles then ties his body to a chariot and drags it around the city walls before the eyes of his parents and wife.

The epic concludes not with the fall of Troy but with Hector's funeral. [[priam|Priam]]'s ransom of his son's body, facilitated by divine intervention and Achilles' compassion, occupies all of Book 24. The lamentations of Andromache, Hecuba, and [[helen|Helen]] over Hector's body affirm his centrality to the poem's meaning: he was the shield of Troy, and with his death, the city's fall is inevitable.

## Scholarly Notes

Hector is widely regarded as the most sympathetic character in the *Iliad*. Scholars note that he is the only hero shown in a full domestic context, making his death resonate as a human loss rather than a martial necessity. His epithet "of the gleaming helmet" (*korythaiolos*) emphasizes his martial brilliance, while "tamer of horses" (*hippodamos*) reflects his civilizing role. The name Hector itself derives from the Greek *ekhein* ("to hold, to protect"), reinforcing his identity as the protector of Troy. Samuel Butler's translation renders Hector's speeches with particular clarity and directness, capturing the psychological realism that makes the Trojan hero so enduring. Modern scholarship has increasingly focused on Hector as a figure of tragic heroism — a man who knows his cause is doomed yet fights to the end out of love for those he protects.
