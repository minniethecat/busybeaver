# Dream (Oneiros)

In the *Iliad*, a dream is not the random firing of a sleeping brain but a god-sent visitation. Dreams arrive as messengers, speaking in human form with divine authority. The most consequential dream in the epic is a lie — Zeus sends a "Lying Dream" to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in Book 2, promising swift victory and thereby setting in motion the catastrophe that proves [[achilles|Achilles]]' indispensability (002-002). This episode establishes the Homeric understanding of dreams as instruments of divine will, capable of truthful revelation or calculated deception.

## Definition

The Homeric *oneiros* (Ὄνειρος) is a personified entity — not an experience but a being. Dreams are sent by the gods, particularly Zeus, to deliver messages that shape mortal action. The dream appears in recognizable form (often as someone the dreamer knows and trusts), speaks directly to the dreamer, and departs once its message is delivered. The dreamer's response is not to interpret the dream psychologically but to act upon it as divine communication — sometimes to their own ruin (002-002). The poet distinguishes between truthful dreams and deceptive ones: "dreams, too, are of Jove," and a "reader of dreams" is a recognized social role, though one that can be fatal when the dream itself is misleading (001-201).

The Lying Dream is the most detailed dream narrative. Zeus summons a dream, gives it precise instructions, and sends it to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s tent. The dream finds the king sleeping, takes the form of [[nestor|Nestor]] (the most trusted counselor), and delivers its false promise (002-016). [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] wakes and immediately convenes the council of elders, reporting, "I have had a dream from heaven in the dead of night" (002-048). He repeats the dream's words verbatim — a [[prophecy|sign]] of how literally dreams are taken in Homer's world. When [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] proposes testing the army's morale by suggesting retreat, the plan backfires spectacularly: the army rushes to the [[achaean-camp|ships]], ready to abandon Troy, and only [[odysseus|Odysseus]]' intervention prevents a rout (002-152).

## Contrasts and Tensions

The Homeric dream is fundamentally unlike the modern psychological dream. In Homer, the dream does not originate in the dreamer's mind — it is sent, it speaks, it has agency independent of the sleeper. The dreamer is a passive recipient of divine communication, and the question is not "what does this dream mean about me?" but "is this dream true or false?"

This creates a theological problem central to the *Iliad*: if dreams come from Zeus, and Zeus sends a lying dream to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], then the gods are capable of deliberate deception. The poem does not resolve this tension — it simply presents it. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] acts on the dream as if it were true, with consequences that unfold across the subsequent books. The dream becomes an instrument of Zeus's larger plan: to honor [[achilles|Achilles]] (as promised to Thetis) by demonstrating that the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] cannot win without him (001-293).

The figure of the dream-interpreter (Eurydamas, whose sons "never came back for him to read them any more dreams," 005-139) suggests that dream interpretation was a recognized practice, but the poem does not depict it as reliable — the interpreter's sons died at Troy despite (or because of?) his readings.
