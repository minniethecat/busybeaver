## Identity

Helen is the daughter of Zeus and Leda, sister of Clytemnestra, and wife of Menelaus — until her abduction (or elopement) with Paris brings her to Troy. She is the most beautiful woman in the world, and her face is famously said to have "launched a thousand ships." In the *Iliad*, Helen is a deeply complex figure: both the ostensible cause of the war and one of its most perceptive critics. She is acutely aware of her role in the conflict and is tormented by self-reproach, yet she is also subject to the will of Aphrodite, who compels her actions.

Helen is referred to as both "Argive Helen" (marking her Greek origins) and "Helen of Troy" (marking her present position). She weaves a great purple web depicting the battles fought for her sake (003-005), a poignant symbol of her dual role as observer and cause of the suffering around her.

## Role in the Epic

Helen appears in several crucial scenes throughout the *Iliad*, each revealing different dimensions of her character. She is present at the beginning of the war's narrative arc in Book III and appears at its emotional climax in Book XXIV — the only character besides Achilles whose presence spans the full moral range of the poem.

**The Teichoscopia (Book III):** Summoned by Iris disguised as her sister-in-law Laodice, Helen goes to the Scaean gates to view the Greek army from the walls (003-101). "Thus spoke the goddess, and Helen's heart yearned after her former husband, her city, and her parents" (003-114). When the Trojan elders see her approaching, they murmur that "no one could wonder that Trojans and Achaeans should endure so much and so long for the sake of a woman so marvellously and divinely lovely" (003-125-128). When Priam kindly bids her sit beside him, she answers with bitter self-reproach: "Sir, father of my husband, dear and reverend in my eyes, would that I had died on the day when I followed your son to Troy" (003-138-140). She then identifies the Greek heroes for Priam — Agamemnon, Odysseus (003-158), Ajax (003-182) — in a scene that humanises both Helen and the enemy, transforming her from a trophy of war into an intelligent and articulate witness.

**The Duel of Paris and Menelaus (Book III):** Helen is present for the single combat between her two husbands (003-201). When Paris is spirited away by Aphrodite, the goddess — disguised as an old wool-worker — summons Helen to Paris's bedchamber. Helen recognizes the goddess and defies her in one of the most remarkable speeches in the poem: "Bold hussy, do not provoke me; if you do, I shall leave you to your fate and hate you as much as I have loved you" — these are Aphrodite's enraged words in reply, but Helen's defiance is the catalyst. "At this Helen was frightened. She wrapped her mantle about her and went in silence, following the goddess" (003-324). In Paris's chamber, Helen looks at him with eyes askance and upbraids him: "Would that you had fallen rather by the hand of that brave man who was my husband" (003-330-332).

**At Hector's Funeral (Book XXIV):** Helen delivers the third and final lament over Hector's body, and it is arguably the most moving. "Hector," she says, "dearest of all my brothers-in-law — for I am wife to Alexandrus who brought me hither to Troy — would that I had died ere he did so. Twenty years are come and gone since I left my home and came from over the sea, but I have never heard one word of insult or unkindness from you. When another would chide with me, as it might be one of your brothers or sisters or of your brothers' wives, or my mother-in-law — for Priam was as kind to me as though he were my own father — you would rebuke and check them with words of gentleness and goodwill. Therefore my tears flow both for you and for my unhappy self, for there is no one else in Troy who is kind to me, but all shrink and shudder as they go by me."

This lament reveals Helen's profound isolation in Troy and her genuine grief for the one man who treated her with consistent decency. It transforms Helen from a passive object of contention into an active moral voice — a woman who, in the moment of Troy's deepest grief, speaks not of her own suffering but of the kindness she received from a man who was, by every political logic, her enemy.

Throughout the poem, Helen is a figure for whom both sides fight — Menelaus seeks to reclaim her, while "keeping Helen" is the Trojans' stated aim (002-129, 002-141). Menelaus declares that they fight "in the midst of you for Helen and all her wealth" (003-075). Numerous Argives "have suffered for the sake of Helen" (002-285, 002-476).

## Notable Speeches

Helen's speeches are marked by profound self-awareness and self-condemnation. She is one of the few characters in Homer who speaks of herself with genuine moral complexity — neither exculpating herself nor accepting the blame that others heap upon her without qualification.

> "Sir, father of my husband, dear and reverend in my eyes, would that I had died on the day when I followed your son to Troy, leaving my bridal chamber, my brothers and sisters, my dear daughter, and the companions of my youth." (003-138-140)

> And Helen answered, "He is Ulysses, a man of great craft, son of Laertes, who was bred in rocky Ithaca. He knows every manner of stratagem and cunning device." (003-158-160)

> When Venus was very angry, she said, "Bold hussy, do not provoke me; if you do, I shall leave you to your fate and hate you as much as I have loved you." At this Helen was frightened. She wrapped her mantle about her and went in silence, following the goddess. (003-307, 003-324)

> "So you are come from the fight. Would that you had fallen rather by the hand of that brave man who was my husband. You used to brag that you were a better man with hands and spear than Menelaus." (003-330-332)

> "Hector, dearest of all my brothers-in-law — for I am wife to Alexandrus who brought me hither to Troy — would that I had died ere he did so. Twenty years are come and gone since I left my home and came from over the sea, but I have never heard one word of insult or unkindness from you." (Book XXIV)

The lament for Hector is the emotional climax of Helen's role in the *Iliad*. In praising the one man who treated her with kindness, she performs an act of moral generosity — mourning her husband's enemy — and reveals the depth of her isolation: "there is no one else in Troy who is kind to me, but all shrink and shudder as they go by me."

## Relationships

**Paris (Alexander):** Helen's relationship with Paris is strained and complex. When Aphrodite compels her to return to him after his duel with Menelaus, she looks at him with contempt and wishes Menelaus had killed him. Yet she remains with him, bound by the goddess's power. Paris, for his part, dismisses her reproach with the defensive retort, "Wife, do not vex me" (003-338), and seems more interested in her beauty than in her feelings. His response to her upbraiding — "Never yet was I so passionately enamoured of you as at this moment — not even when I first carried you off from Lacedaemon" (003-340-342) — is one of the poem's most jarring juxtapositions of erotic desire and moral obliviousness.

**Menelaus:** Helen's first husband, who fights to reclaim her. She speaks of him without hostility and yearns for her former life (003-114). The *Iliad* implies that Helen still thinks of herself as Menelaus's wife, and her position in Troy is always provisional. Their relationship is the human face of the war's stated cause — and Helen's affection for Menelaus complicates any simple reading of her as a willing adulteress.

**Priam:** The aged king treats Helen with unexpected kindness, calling her "my child" and refusing to blame her for the war: "I do not blame you; it is the gods who have brought about this terrible war with the Achaeans" (003-131). Helen addresses him as "father of my husband, dear and reverend" (003-138), and this filial respect is one of the few uncomplicated relationships she has in Troy.

**Aphrodite:** The goddess both protects and controls Helen, and Helen is one of the few mortals bold enough to resist her. The tension between divine compulsion and human agency is starkly dramatised in their confrontation (003-307-324). When Aphrodite threatens to "hate you as much as I have loved you" and leave Helen to "a bad end," Helen yields — but the coercion is explicit, and the poem leaves the question of Helen's moral responsibility deliberately unresolved.

**Hector:** The one figure in Troy who treated Helen with consistent kindness. "I have never heard one word of insult or unkindness from you," she says in her lament, recalling how he would "rebuke and check them with words of gentleness and goodwill" when others reviled her. Her lament for him in Book XXIV is a testament to his character and to her isolation. Hector's death takes from Helen her only protector within the Trojan royal house.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Helen's fate, but it is richly attested in later tradition. After the fall of Troy, Menelaus reclaims her; according to some accounts, he raises his sword to kill her but is disarmed by her beauty. She returns to Sparta with Menelaus and, in the *Odyssey*, appears as a restored queen, dispensing drugs and telling stories of the war — a figure who has survived the cataclysm she caused and found, if not peace, at least a new equilibrium. The *Odyssey*'s Helen is poised, urbane, and strangely untroubled — a version of the character that the *Iliad*'s self-lacerating Helen would scarcely recognize.

## Scholarly Notes

Helen is one of the most discussed figures in Homeric scholarship. Feminist critics have examined her as both object of male contestation and as a voice of resistance to the patriarchal and divine structures that constrain her. The tension between Helen's self-blame and the poem's insistence that she acts under divine compulsion raises profound questions about agency and responsibility in the Homeric world.

Her weaving of the battle scenes (003-005) has been read as a metaphor for the poet's own craft — Helen is, in a sense, the internal narrator of the war, weaving the very story in which she is a character. This meta-narrative dimension sets Helen apart from every other character in the poem: she alone sees the war from both the inside and the outside, as both cause and observer.

Some scholars argue that Helen functions as a scapegoat figure: the Greeks and Trojans project their own violent impulses onto her, and she internalises this blame, allowing both sides to disavow responsibility for the war's devastation. Her self-condemnation — calling herself a "shameless creature," wishing she had died — can be read as the internalisation of a communal need for someone to blame.

The three laments over Hector's body (Book XXIV) — by Andromache, Hecuba, and Helen — form a triptych of female grief. Andromache speaks as wife, Hecuba as mother, and Helen as outsider. That Helen, the foreign woman, is given the last word is a remarkable structural choice: the *Iliad* ends its lamentation sequence not with the grieving wife or mother but with the woman whose presence caused the war, mourning the one Trojan who treated her as human.
