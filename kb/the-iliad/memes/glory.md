# Glory (Kleos)

The hero of the *Iliad* fights not for territory, wealth, or even victory, but for *kleos* — the undying [[honor|glory]] that will be sung by poets for generations after his death. When [[achilles|Achilles]] chooses the short, glorious life over the long, obscure one, he is choosing *kleos* over everything else the world can offer (001-293). The *Iliad* itself is the vehicle of *kleos*: the poem is the [[honor|glory]] that [[achilles|Achilles]], [[hector|Hector]], and all the warriors at Troy won through their suffering and death. In this sense, every [[grief|Grief (Akhos)]] the poem records is also an act of immortalization — the dead live on in the song that mourns them.

## Definition

*Kleos* (κλέος) is etymologically connected to *kluein* (κλύειν) — "to hear." It is literally "what is heard" about a person, the reputation that circulates in speech and song. *Kleos aphthiton* (κλέος ἄφθιτον) — "imperishable [[honor|glory]]" — is the highest prize a Homeric hero can win. Unlike material prizes (*gerata*) that can be seized, or [[honor|Honor (Timē)]] that can be insulted, *kleos* endures beyond death and beyond the collapse of cities. The poet who sings the *Iliad* is the custodian of *kleos*; without the song, the deeds would be lost, and the hero's death would be mere oblivion.

## Manifestations in the Epic

The entire *Iliad* can be read as a machine for generating *kleos*. The poem operates at multiple levels to ensure that heroic deeds are remembered:

| Mechanism of Kleos | Example | Function |
|-------------------|---------|----------|
| Epithets | "swift-footed [[achilles|Achilles]]," "[[hector|Hector]] of [[helmet-of-achilles|the gleaming helmet]]" | Fixes the hero's identity in formulaic, memorable language |
| Battle biographies | Named warriors' parentage and homeland at death | Preserves even the defeated in the permanent record |
| Direct [[prophecy]] | [[achilles|Achilles]]' choice foretold by Thetis (001-293) | Ch[[curse|ara]]cters within the poem anticipate their own *kleos* |
| Metapoetic reflection | [[helen|Helen]]'s speech in Book 6 | Ch[[curse|ara]]cters recognize they exist to become "subjects of song" |
| The poem itself | The entire *Iliad* | The vehicle that carries *kleos* across millennia |

Every named warrior killed on the battlefield receives a miniature biography — his parentage, his homeland, the details of his life — ensuring that even the defeated are remembered. The epic does not distinguish between Greek and Trojan in this [[shame|respect]]: [[hector|Hector]]'s *kleos* is as carefully preserved as [[achilles|Achilles]]'. The poem's very structure enacts the egalitarianism of *kleos*: to be sung is to be immortal, regardless of which side you fought for.

[[helen|Helen]]'s speech in Book 6 is the epic's most self-conscious meditation on *kleos*. Looking out over the battlefield, she tells [[hector|Hector]] that Zeus has given them an evil fate "so that we may be subjects of song for men yet to be." This extraordinary m[[prophecy|omen]]t — a ch[[curse|ara]]cter recognizing that her suffering exists to generate poetry — collapses the distance between the world of the heroes and the world of the audience hearing their story.

## Greek Terminology

| Term | Greek | Meaning | Distinction |
|------|-------|---------|-------------|
| **kleos** | κλέος | Glory, fame, "what is heard" | Enduring fame won through deeds |
| **kleos aphthiton** | κλέος ἄφθιτον | Imperishable [[honor|glory]] | The highest prize, surviving death |
| **klea andrōn** | κλέα ἀνδρῶν | "Glories of men" | Traditional subject matter of epic |
| **kudos** | κῦδος | Temporary battle-[[honor|glory]] | Granted by gods, m[[prophecy|omen]]tary; distinct from lasting *kleos* |
| **[[honor|timē]]** | τιμή | Honor, status, material due | What one has in life; *kleos* is what survives death |

The distinction between *kudos* (temporary, god-given battle success) and *kleos* (permanent, song-preserved fame) is crucial. A warrior may win *kudos* in a single combat, but *kleos* requires a poet to sing of it afterward. The *Iliad* itself is the bridge between the two.

## Exemplary Passages

> Zeus has given us an evil fate, so that we may be subjects of song for men yet to be.
> — Helen to Hector, Book 6

> My mother Thetis tells me that there are two ways in which I may meet my end. If I stay here and fight, I shall lose my safe homecoming but have imperishable glory (kleos aphthiton). If I return home, my glory is lost but my life shall be long.
> — Achilles' choice, summarized from his speech (001-293)

> Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus, that brought countless ills upon the Achaeans.
> — The opening invocation (001-001): the poem itself as *kleos*

## Contrasts and Tensions

*Kleos* is purchased at the price of life. [[achilles|Achilles]] must die to win it; [[hector|Hector]]'s *kleos* is insep[[curse|ara]]ble from his defeat and death. The epic does not flinch from this cost. When [[odysseus|Odysseus]] encounters [[achilles|Achilles]] in the underworld (in the *Odyssey*), the dead hero says he would rather be a slave among the living than king among the dead — a stunning repudiation of the *kleos* he chose. Yet the *Iliad* itself preserves that *kleos*, and every audience that hears the poem ratifies [[achilles|Achilles]]' choice.

The tension between *kleos* and *nostos* (homecoming) structures the entire Epic Cycle:

| Hero | Choice | Outcome | Epic |
|------|--------|---------|------|
| [[achilles|Achilles]] | *Kleos* over *nostos* | Dies young at Troy, immortal fame | *Iliad* |
| [[odysseus|Odysseus]] | *Nostos* over *kleos* | Survives, returns home after long wandering | *Odyssey* |
| [[hector|Hector]] | *Kleos* through duty | Dies defending Troy, immortalized in defeat | *Iliad* |

The *Iliad* explores the consequences of [[achilles|Achilles]]' choice — the [[honor|glory]] and the horror together. The poem never lets us forget that *kleos* is built on a mountain of corpses.
