# Athena (Minerva)

Athena, called Minerva in Samuel Butler's translation, is the goddess of wisdom, strategic warfare, and counsel (*mētis*). Daughter of Zeus, born fully armed from his head, she is the most active divine champion of the Achaean cause in the Iliad. Unlike Ares, who embodies the mindless fury of combat, Athena represents the intelligence that governs effective warfare — strategy, discipline, and the art of persuasion. Her epithets include "flashing-eyed" (*glaukōpis*), "daughter of aegis-bearing Zeus," and "Tritogeneia." She restrains [[achilles|Achilles]] from killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in Book 1, breaks the truce on Zeus' orders in Book 4, and guides [[diomedes|Diomedes]] in his aristeia in Book 5.

## Identity

Athena is uniquely positioned among the gods as the daughter who shares her father's mind. Born from Zeus' head, she is the embodiment of his strategic intelligence. In the Iliad, she functions as Zeus' most trusted executor — when Zeus wishes to influence events on the battlefield without intervening directly, he dispatches Athena. Butler renders her name as "Minerva," following the Roman convention, though the Homeric character bears the distinctively Greek association with wisdom and craft.

Her dual identity as a goddess of both wisdom and war sets her apart. She does not love battle for its own sake, like Ares, but for the skill and victory it brings. She is the patron of heroes who combine intelligence with courage — [[odysseus|Odysseus]] most of all, and [[diomedes|Diomedes]] in the Iliad. Her "flashing eyes" (*glaukōpis*) are her most distinctive attribute, suggesting both divine radiance and penetrating insight.

## Role in the Epic

Athena intervenes at crucial moments throughout the Iliad, always in service of Zeus' broader plan and the Achaean cause.

- **Book 1 — Restraining Wrath:** When Achilles draws his sword to kill Agamemnon, Athena descends from Olympus, visible only to Achilles, and seizes him by his hair (001-001). She promises him threefold recompense if he restrains his fury. Achilles obeys: "Goddess, however angry a man may be, he must do as you two command him" (001-008). This is the Iliad's first divine intervention and establishes Athena as the voice of strategic patience over impulsive violence.

- **Book 2 — Saving the Exodus:** As the Achaean army, demoralized by Agamemnon's test, rushes for the ships, Hera sends Athena to intervene (002-134). Disguised as a herald, she moves through the ranks, urging each man to remember his duty. She finds Odysseus and inspires him to rally the troops, preventing the army's disintegration (002-223).

- **Book 4 — The Broken Truce:** Zeus, yielding to Hera's demand that Troy be destroyed, sends Athena to break the truce between the two armies (004-053). She descends "like a star" and persuades Pandarus the Lycian archer to shoot an arrow at Menelaus, wounding him and reigniting the war. This act establishes Athena as the divine agent of continued conflict.

- **Book 5 — Diomedes' Aristeia:** Athena's most spectacular battlefield intervention comes when she grants Diomedes the power to distinguish gods from mortals on the battlefield, enabling him to wound Aphrodite and even Ares himself (005-001–005-366). She mounts Diomedes' chariot beside him and guides his spear directly into Ares, driving the war god from the field. This scene demonstrates her supremacy over the brute force of war.

- **Book 22 — The Final Deception:** In the poem's climactic duel, Athena takes the form of Hector's brother Deiphobus and stands beside him, encouraging him to face Achilles (022-001). When Hector turns for a second spear and calls for Deiphobus, Athena has vanished. Hector realizes the deception and speaks the devastating line: "The gods have called me to my death" (022-130). This divine trickery seals Hector's fate.

> — Pandarus, confessing Athena's role in his arrow-shot (004-093)

## Relationships

- **Zeus (father):** Athena is Zeus' most trusted divine agent, carrying out his commands with intelligence and initiative. Unlike Hera, who defies Zeus, Athena works within his will — she is the executor who makes Zeus' plan manifest on the battlefield.

- **Hera (ally):** Athena and Hera share a fierce commitment to the Achaean cause and frequently collaborate. Hera sends Athena to rally the troops in Book 2 (002-133), and they ride into battle together in Book 5, "their hearts full of rage against the Trojans" (004-023).

- **Achilles (protégé):** Athena's relationship with Achilles is intimate and protective. She restrains him in Book 1 and continues to watch over his interests throughout the poem. At Achilles' lowest moment in Book 18, she cloaks him in the aegis and crowns him with flame, terrifying the Trojan army (018-001).

- **Diomedes (champion):** In Book 5, Athena makes Diomedes her instrument on the battlefield, granting him the divine sight to distinguish gods from mortals. She rides beside him in his chariot, directing his spear against Ares himself (005-366).

- **Odysseus (favourite):** Though Odysseus' role in the Iliad is secondary, Athena's special favour for him is evident. She inspires him to rally the Achaeans in Book 2 and guides the night raid of Book 10.

## Death or Fate

As an immortal goddess, Athena does not face death, but the Iliad explores a different facet of her being: her role as the embodiment of strategic intelligence in a world that too easily descends into brute violence. Her triumph over Ares in Book 5 is a theological statement — the poem asserts that wisdom-governed warfare prevails over mindless rage. Her deceptions (of Pandarus in Book 4, of Hector in Book 22) raise complex moral questions about divine manipulation of mortal agency, questions that the poem itself leaves unresolved.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler consistently translates Athena as "Minerva," the Roman name for the goddess. While Roman Minerva shared Athena's association with wisdom and craft, the Homeric Athena has a pronounced martial dimension — she is a warrior goddess in a way her Roman counterpart is not. The epithet *glaukōpis* (γλαυκῶπις), rendered variously as "flashing-eyed," "grey-eyed," or "owl-eyed," may refer to the gleaming quality of divine vision. The compound epithet *Tritogeneia* is of uncertain etymology — ancient scholars associated it with Lake Tritonis in Libya or with the word for "head" (*tritō*), referencing her birth from Zeus' head.

Athena's role as the divine restrainer of Achilles in Book 1 is philosophically significant: it presents the suppression of impulsive violence as a divine act, aligning wisdom (*sophia*) with civilization against the chaos of unchecked wrath. This pattern recurs throughout the poem — Athena is the god who says "wait," "think," "there is a better way."
