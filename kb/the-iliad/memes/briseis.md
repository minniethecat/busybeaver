# Briseis

Briseis is the daughter of Briseus of Lyrnessus, a city in the Troad that fell to [[achilles|Achilles]] during a Greek raid. Awarded to Achilles as a prize of honour (*geras*), she was seized by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] to compensate for the loss of his own prize, Chryseis. This seizure — a calculated insult to Achilles' honour — causes the hero's withdrawal from battle, the central event around which the entire Iliad is structured. Briseis herself speaks only once in the poem: her lament over the body of [[patroclus|Patroclus]] in Book 19, which transforms her from a silent object of contention into a voice of genuine grief. Butler describes her as "fair Briseis, golden-haired" and "fair as Venus" (019-224), emphasizing the beauty that made her a prize worth fighting over.

## Identity

Briseis is the daughter of Briseus (from whom she takes her patronymic name) and was the wife of Mynes, king of Lyrnessus, until Achilles killed her husband and her three brothers during the sack of the city (019-049). She was then taken as a captive and became Achilles' prize — a marker of his status and martial achievement. Her original name may have been Hippodameia ("horse-tamer"), with "Briseis" being the patronymic form meaning "daughter of Briseus," but in the Homeric tradition she is known exclusively by the patronymic.

As a captive woman in the Homeric world, Briseis has no agency of her own. Her value is entirely symbolic: she is the *geras*, the tangible token of the honour the Greek army has bestowed upon Achilles. When Agamemnon takes her, he is not merely appropriating a woman — he is stripping Achilles of the visible proof of his status. The entire conflict of the Iliad turns on what Briseis represents, not on who she is. Yet when she finally speaks in Book 19, her words reveal a humanity that the war-prize framework had concealed.

## Role in the Epic

Briseis is present in the background of Books 1 and 9, but her defining moment comes in Book 19.

- **Book 1 — The Seizure:** When Agamemnon is forced to return Chryseis to her father, he demands compensation. He seizes Briseis from Achilles as a public assertion of his superior authority. Achilles does not resist physically — to do so would be to challenge his own commander — but he withdraws from battle entirely, and his withdrawal is the Iliad's central crisis. The heralds Talthybius and Eurybates come to Achilles' tent, and "Patroclus brought Briseis from the tent and gave her over to the heralds" (001-114). She goes "unwillingly" (*aekousa*), the single word that conveys her own perspective.

- **Book 9 — The Embassy:** When Agamemnon offers to return Briseis along with many other gifts to persuade Achilles to return to battle, Achilles' response reveals the depth of his wounded honour. "Agamemnon has taken Briseis from my tent against my judgment" (009-095). He also swears that he has never slept with her — "I have never gone up into the couch of Briseis, nor been with her as man with woman" — emphasizing that his attachment is one of honour, not mere possession. He refuses the embassy's offer, and the Greeks continue to suffer.

- **Book 19 — The Return and Lament:** After Patroclus' death, Agamemnon returns Briseis to Achilles with solemn oaths that he has not touched her. The return is meant to resolve the quarrel, but the emotional heart of the scene is not reconciliation — it is Briseis' lament over Patroclus' body (019-224). She speaks of Patroclus' kindness to her: "You would not let me weep, but said you would make me the wedded wife of Achilles" (019-224). Her words reveal that Patroclus was the one person who treated her as a human being rather than a prize, and his death is for her a second bereavement — after losing her husband and brothers to Achilles, she has now lost the one man who promised her a future.

## Notable Speeches

> Briseis, fair as Venus, when she saw Patroclus lying dead, flung herself down upon him and tore her breast and her fair cheeks. And she cried aloud: "Patroclus, dearest to my sorrowing heart — I left you alive when I went from the tent, and now I find you dead on my return, you who were always kind."
> — Briseis' lament for Patroclus (019-224)

> "You would not let me weep when Achilles killed my husband and sacked the city of Mynes; you told me that you would make me the wedded wife of Achilles, and that you would take me to Phthia and give me a marriage feast."
> — Briseis recalling Patroclus' kindness (019-224)

## Relationships

- **Achilles (captor and future husband):** Briseis' relationship with Achilles is complex. He killed her husband and brothers during the sack of Lyrnessus, yet he also values her as his prize of honour. Achilles' claim that he loved Briseis "though she was but a captive" (009-095) expresses both affection and the fundamental inequality of their relationship. Patroclus had promised Briseis that Achilles would make her his wedded wife, suggesting a future in which her status would change from captive to spouse — a future that Patroclus' death has now put in doubt.

- **Patroclus (protector and comforter):** Briseis' lament reveals that Patroclus was the one person who showed her kindness during her captivity. When Achilles killed her family, it was Patroclus who comforted her and promised her a better future. His death devastates her precisely because he was the sole source of human warmth in a world of violence. Her lament adds a dimension to Patroclus' character that the rest of the poem only hints at — his gentleness, his capacity for emotional care.

- **Agamemnon (the seizer):** Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis is the act that sets the plot in motion, but he never interacts with her directly. She is a symbol to him, not a person — a marker of status to be transferred from one man to another. Her return to Achilles, accompanied by Agamemnon's oaths that he has not touched her, is meant to erase the insult, but it cannot undo the damage that the quarrel has caused.

## Death or Fate

Briseis survives the Iliad, but her fate after the poem is ambiguous. Patroclus' promise to make her Achilles' wedded wife in Phthia remains unfulfilled at the poem's end. In the post-Homeric tradition, she remains with Achilles until his death and is subsequently given to one of his companions. But within the Iliad, her story ends with her lament — a captive woman weeping over the body of the one man who treated her with kindness, her future now as uncertain as it was when she was first taken from Lyrnessus.

## Scholarly Notes

Briseis is a figure of enormous scholarly interest because she embodies the Iliad's treatment of women as both human beings and objects of exchange. Her name — a patronymic, not a personal name — emphasizes her status as property: she is defined by her relationship to her father, not by her own identity. The poem's single attribution of a personal name to her (Hippodameia) is uncertain and comes from later sources.

The comparison between Briseis and Chryseis is structurally significant. Both are captive women; both are prizes of honour; both are seized by Agamemnon. But where Chryseis' father has the power of a god's priesthood to reclaim her, Briseis has no father to intervene — her father has already been killed by Achilles. This asymmetry reflects the Iliad's interest in the vulnerability of those who lack the networks of protection that Homeric society provides.

Briseis' lament in Book 19 is the only time she speaks, and it transforms her from symbol to person. The lament follows the formal conventions of Greek funerary mourning but adds a personal dimension unique in the poem: she mourns not only Patroclus but also the future he had promised her. The lament thus connects Briseis to [[andromache|Andromache]], whose grief over Hector in Books 6 and 24 also focuses on the loss of a promised future. Both women are witnesses to what war costs those who do not fight.
