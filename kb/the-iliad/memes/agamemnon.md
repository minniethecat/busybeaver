# Agamemnon

## Identity

Agamemnon, son of Atreus and brother of Menelaus, is the king of Mycenae and the commander-in-chief of the Achaean forces assembled against Troy. He is frequently referred to by the patronymic "Atreides" (Son of Atreus) throughout the epic. As the wealthiest and most powerful of the Greek kings, he holds the position of supreme authority over the expedition.

## Role in the Epic

Agamemnon's quarrel with Achilles over the captive woman Briseis forms the central conflict of the *Iliad*. In Book 1, after being compelled to return his own prize Chryseis to appease Apollo's plague, Agamemnon asserts his authority by seizing Briseis from Achilles — an act that dishonors the greatest Achaean warrior and prompts him to withdraw from battle with catastrophic consequences for the Greek army (001-121).

Agamemnon's leadership is marked by both regal authority and flawed judgment. He is portrayed as a proud and sometimes shortsighted commander whose high-handedness alienates his best warrior. His testing of the army's morale in Book 2 — urging them to sail home only to see them rush to the ships — reveals the fragile state of Achaean morale under his command.

Despite his flaws, Agamemnon is a capable warrior in his own right. His *aristeia* (scene of martial excellence) in Book 11 demonstrates his prowess on the battlefield, where he cuts down numerous Trojans before being wounded and forced to withdraw (001-142). He is distinguished not only by his status but by his physical stature and ability in combat.

## Notable Speeches

Agamemnon's rhetoric reveals the complexity of his character. In his clash with Achilles, he speaks with the blunt authority of a king unwilling to bend:

> Therefore, Agamemnon, though you be strong, do not take the girl from him but let him keep her, the prize the sons of the Achaeans gave him. (001-058)

Yet when Chryses appeals for his daughter, Agamemnon's response is described as harsh and dismissive:

> And Agamemnon answered, "Sir, all that you have said is reasonable, but I would have you know that I will not yield to you." (001-067)

His anger is repeatedly noted as a defining trait:

> ...by the fierceness of Agamemnon's anger, that if ever again there be need of me to save the army from ruin, they shall not find. Agamemnon is mad with rage and knows no more how to look before and after that the Achaeans may fight safely by their ships. (001-108)

Achilles himself denounces the king's arrogance:

> Agamemnon, son of Atreus, has done me dishonour, and has taken my prize by force. (001-121)

And the consequences of his actions are made explicit:

> ...that Agamemnon may rue his blindness in offering dishonour to the best of the Achaeans. (001-168)

## Relationships

- **Achilles**: The defining antagonistic relationship of the epic. Agamemnon's seizure of Briseis dishonors Achilles, setting in motion the wrath that drives the entire narrative. Their reconciliation in Book 19 is a pivotal moment.
- **Menelaus**: Agamemnon's younger brother, whose wife Helen was taken by Paris — the *casus belli* of the Trojan War. Agamemnon acts as his protector and champion.
- **Chryses**: The priest of Apollo whose daughter Chryseis was held as Agamemnon's prize. Agamemnon's refusal to return her brings Apollo's plague upon the Achaean camp.
- **Nestor**: The aged counselor who frequently advises Agamemnon, attempting to moderate his pride with wisdom.
- **Diomedes**: A fellow Achaean commander who, like Achilles, is not afraid to speak frankly to Agamemnon when the king's judgment falters.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not depict Agamemnon's death, which occurs after the events of the poem. According to later tradition (the *Odyssey* and the tragic cycle), Agamemnon returns home victorious but is murdered by his wife Clytemnestra and her lover Aegisthus. This shadow hangs over his character — the audience of the *Iliad* would have known his ultimate fate, adding tragic irony to his portrayal as the triumphant commander.

## Scholarly Notes

Agamemnon embodies the Homeric tension between authority and merit. His claim to command is based on hereditary kingship and material wealth (*anax andron*, "lord of men"), while Achilles' claim is based on martial excellence. This conflict — between the man of status and the man of ability — is one of the deep structural themes of the epic. Scholars note that Agamemnon's character arc traces a pattern of error, suffering, and eventual recognition (*hamartia*, *pathos*, *anagnorisis*), though his recognition comes late and at great cost to his army.

His title "lord of men" (*anax andron*) and the symbol of the scepter, crafted by Hephaestus and handed down from Zeus, reinforce his divinely sanctioned authority — an authority that, in the world of the *Iliad*, cannot be challenged without consequence, even when it is exercised unwisely.
