# Book XV

[015-001]BOOK XV.

[015-002]Jove awakes, tells Apollo to heal [[hector|Hector]], and the Trojans again

[015-003]become victorious.

[015-004]But when their flight had taken them past the trench and the set

[015-005]stakes, and many had fallen by the hands of the Danaans, the

[015-006]Trojans made a halt on reaching their chariots, routed and pale

[015-007]with fear. Jove now woke on the crests of [[mount-ida|Ida]], where he was lying

[015-008]with golden-throned Juno by his side, and starting to his feet he

[015-009]saw the Trojans and Achaeans, the one thrown into confusion, and

[015-010]the others driving them pell-mell before them with King Neptune

[015-011]in their midst. He saw Hector lying on the ground with his

[015-012]comrades gathered round him, gasping for breath, wandering in

[015-013]mind and vomiting blood, for it was not the feeblest of the

[015-014]Achaeans who struck him.

[015-015]The sire of gods and men had pity on him, and looked fiercely on

[015-016]Juno. “I see, Juno,” said he, “you mischief-making trickster,

[015-017]that your cunning has stayed Hector from fighting and has caused

[015-018]the rout of his [[guest-friendship|host]]. I am in half a mind to thrash you, in which

[015-019]case you will be the first to reap the fruits of your scurvy

[015-020]knavery. Do you not remember how once upon a time I had you

[015-021]hanged? I fastened two anvils on to your feet, and bound your

[015-022]hands in a chain of gold which none might break, and you hung in

[015-023]mid-air among the clouds. All the gods in [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] were in a [[wrath|fury]],

[015-024]but they could not reach you to set you free; when I caught any

[015-025]one of them I gripped him and hurled him from the heavenly

[015-026]threshold till he came fainting down to earth; yet even this did

[015-027]not relieve my mind from the incessant anxiety which I felt about

[015-028]noble Hercules whom you and Boreas had spitefully conveyed beyond

[015-029]the seas to Cos, after suborning the tempests; but I rescued him,

[015-030]and notwithstanding all his mighty labours I brought him back

[015-031]again to Argos. I would remind you of this that you may learn to

[015-032]leave off being so deceitful, and discover how much you are

[015-033]likely to gain by the embraces out of which you have come here to

[015-034]trick me.”

[015-035]Juno trembled as he spoke, and said, “May heaven above and earth

[015-036]below be my witnesses, with the waters of the river Styx—and this

[015-037]is the most solemn oath that a blessed god can take—nay, I [[oath|swear]]

[015-038]also by your own almighty head and by our bridal bed—things over

[015-039]which I could never possibly perjure myself—that Neptune is not

[015-040]punishing Hector and the Trojans and helping the Achaeans through

[015-041]any doing of mine; it is all of his own mere motion because he

[015-042]was sorry to see the Achaeans hard pressed at their [[achaean-camp|ships]]: if I

[015-043]were advising him, I should tell him to do as you bid him.”

[015-044]The sire of gods and men smiled and answered, “If you, Juno, were

[015-045]always to support me when we sit in council of the gods, Neptune,

[015-046]like it or no, would soon come round to your and my way of

[015-047]thinking. If, then, you are speaking the truth and mean what you

[015-048]say, go among the rank and file of the gods, and tell Iris and

[015-049]Apollo lord of the bow, that I want them—Iris, that she may go to

[015-050]the Achaean host and tell Neptune to leave off fighting and go

[015-051]home, and Apollo, that he may send Hector again into battle and

[015-052]give him fresh strength; he will thus forget his present

[015-053]sufferings, and drive the Achaeans back in confusion till they

[015-054]fall among the ships of [[achilles|Achilles]] son of Peleus. Achilles will

[015-055]then send his comrade [[patroclus|Patroclus]] into battle, and Hector will kill

[015-056]him in front of Ilius after he has slain many warriors, and among

[015-057]them my own noble son Sarpedon. Achilles will kill Hector to

[015-058]avenge Patroclus, and from that time I will bring it about that

[015-059]the Achaeans shall persistently drive the Trojans back till they

[015-060]fulfil the counsels of Minerva and take Ilius. But I will not

[015-061]stay my anger, nor permit any god to help the Danaans till I have

[015-062]accomplished the desire of the son of Peleus, according to the

[015-063]promise I made by bowing my head on the day when Thetis touched

[015-064]my knees and besought me to give him [[honor|honour]].”

[015-065]Juno heeded his words and went from the heights of Ida to great

[015-066]Olympus. Swift as the thought of one whose fancy carries him over

[015-067]vast continents, and he says to himself, “Now I will be here, or

[015-068]there,” and he would have all manner of things—even so swiftly

[015-069]did Juno wing her way till she came to high Olympus and went in

[015-070]among the gods who were gathered in the house of Jove. When they

[015-071]saw her they all of them came up to her, and held out their cups

[015-072]to her by way of greeting. She let the others be, but took the

[015-073]cup offered her by lovely Themis, who was first to come running

[015-074]up to her. “Juno,” said she, “why are you here? And you seem

[015-075]troubled—has your husband the son of Saturn been frightening

[015-076]you?”

[015-077]And Juno answered, “Themis, do not ask me about it. You know what

[015-078]a proud and cruel disposition my husband has. Lead the gods to

[015-079]table, where you and all the immortals can hear the wicked

[015-080]designs which he has avowed. Many a one, mortal and immortal,

[015-081]will be angered by them, however peaceably he may be feasting

[015-082]now.”

[015-083]On this Juno sat down, and the gods were troubled throughout the

[015-084]house of Jove. Laughter sat on her lips but her brow was furrowed

[015-085]with care, and she spoke up in a rage. “Fools that we are,” she

[015-086]cried, “to be thus madly angry with Jove; we keep on wanting to

[015-087]go up to him and stay him by force or by persuasion, but he sits

[015-088]aloof and cares for nobody, for he knows that he is much stronger

[015-089]than any other of the immortals. Make the best, therefore, of

[015-090]whatever ills he may choose to send each one of you; Mars, I take

[015-091]it, has had a taste of them already, for his son Ascalaphus has

[015-092]fallen in battle—the man whom of all others he loved most dearly

[015-093]and whose father he owns himself to be.”

[015-094]When he heard this Mars smote his two sturdy thighs with the flat

[015-095]of his hands, and said in anger, “Do not blame me, you gods that

[015-096]dwell in heaven, if I go to the ships of the Achaeans and avenge

[015-097]the death of my son, even though it end in my being struck by

[015-098]Jove’s lightning and lying in blood and dust among the corpses.”

[015-099]As he spoke he gave orders to yoke his horses Panic and Rout,

[015-100]while he put on his armour. On this, Jove would have been roused

[015-101]to still more fierce and implacable enmity against the other

[015-102]immortals, had not Minerva, alarmed for the safety of the gods,

[015-103]sprung from her seat and hurried outside. She tore the helmet

[015-104]from his head and the shield from his shoulders, and she took the

[015-105]bronze spear from his strong hand and set it on one side; then

[015-106]she said to Mars, “Madman, you are undone; you have ears that

[015-107]hear not, or you have lost all judgement and understanding; have

[015-108]you not heard what Juno has said on coming straight from the

[015-109]presence of Olympian Jove? Do you wish to go through all kinds of

[015-110]suffering before you are brought back sick and sorry to Olympus,

[015-111]after having caused infinite mischief to all us others? Jove

[015-112]would instantly leave the Trojans and Achaeans to themselves; he

[015-113]would come to Olympus to punish us, and would grip us up one

[015-114]after another, guilty or not guilty. Therefore lay aside your

[015-115]anger for the death of your son; better men than he have either

[015-116]been killed already or will fall hereafter, and one cannot

[015-117]protect every one’s whole family.”

## Section 42

[015-118]With these words she took Mars back to his seat. Meanwhile Juno

[015-119]called Apollo outside, with Iris the messenger of the gods.

[015-120]“Jove,” she said to them, “desires you to go to him at once on

[015-121]Mt. Ida; when you have seen him you are to do as he may then bid

[015-122]you.”

[015-123]Thereon Juno left them and resumed her seat inside, while Iris

[015-124]and Apollo made all haste on their way. When they reached

[015-125]many-fountained Ida, mother of wild beasts, they found Jove

[015-126]seated on topmost Gargarus with a fragrant cloud encircling his

[015-127]head as with a diadem. They stood before his presence, and he was

[015-128]pleased with them for having been so quick in obeying the orders

[015-129]his wife had given them.

[015-130]He spoke to Iris first. “Go,” said he, “fleet Iris, tell King

[015-131]Neptune what I now bid you—and tell him true. Bid him leave off

[015-132]fighting, and either join the company of the gods, or go down

[015-133]into the sea. If he takes no heed and disobeys me, let him

[015-134]consider well whether he is strong enough to hold his own against

[015-135]me if I attack him. I am older and much stronger than he is; yet

[015-136]he is not afraid to set himself up as on a level with myself, of

[015-137]whom all the other gods stand in awe.”

[015-138]Iris, fleet as the wind, obeyed him, and as the cold hail or

[015-139]snow-flakes that fly from out the clouds before the blast of

[015-140]Boreas, even so did she wing her way till she came close up to

[015-141]the great shaker of the earth. Then she said, “I have come, O

[015-142]dark-haired king that holds the world in his embrace, to bring

[015-143]you a message from Jove. He bids you leave off fighting, and

[015-144]either join the company of the gods or go down into the sea; if,

[015-145]however, you take no heed and disobey him, he says he will come

[015-146]down here and fight you. He would have you keep out of his reach,

[015-147]for he is older and much stronger than you are, and yet you are

[015-148]not afraid to set yourself up as on a level with himself, of whom

[015-149]all the other gods stand in awe.”

[015-150]Neptune was very angry and said, “Great heavens! strong as Jove

[015-151]may be, he has said more than he can do if he has threatened

[015-152]violence against me, who am of like honour with himself. We were

[015-153]three brothers whom Rhea bore to Saturn—Jove, myself, and Hades

[015-154]who rules the world below. Heaven and earth were divided into

[015-155]three parts, and each of us was to have an equal share. When we

[015-156]cast lots, it fell to me to have my dwelling in the sea for

[015-157]evermore; Hades took the darkness of the realms under the earth,

[015-158]while air and sky and clouds were the [[fate|portion]] that fell to Jove;

[015-159]but earth and great Olympus are the common property of all.

[015-160]Therefore I will not walk as Jove would have me. For all his

[015-161]strength, let him keep to his own third share and be contented

[015-162]without threatening to lay hands upon me as though I were nobody.

[015-163]Let him keep his bragging talk for his own sons and daughters,

[015-164]who must perforce obey him.”

[015-165]Iris fleet as the wind then answered, “Am I really, Neptune, to

[015-166]take this daring and unyielding message to Jove, or will you

[015-167]reconsider your answer? Sensible people are open to argument, and

[015-168]you know that the Erinyes always range themselves on the side of

[015-169]the older person.”

[015-170]Neptune answered, “Goddess Iris, your words have been spoken in

[015-171]season. It is well when a messenger shows so much discretion.

[015-172]Nevertheless it cuts me to the very heart that any one should

[015-173]rebuke so angrily another who is his own peer, and of like empire

[015-174]with himself. Now, however, I will give way in spite of my

[015-175]displeasure; furthermore let me tell you, and I mean what I

[015-176]say—if contrary to the desire of myself, Minerva driver of the

[015-177][[prize|spoil]], Juno, Mercury, and King Vulcan, Jove spares steep Ilius,

[015-178]and will not let the Achaeans have the great triumph of sacking

[015-179]it, let him understand that he will incur our implacable

[015-180]resentment.”

[015-181]Neptune now left the field to go down under the sea, and sorely

[015-182]did the Achaeans miss him. Then Jove said to Apollo, “Go, dear

[015-183]Phoebus, to Hector, for Neptune who holds the earth in his

[015-184]embrace has now gone down under the sea to avoid the severity of

[015-185]my displeasure. Had he not done so those gods who are below with

[015-186]Saturn would have come to hear of the fight between us. It is

[015-187]better for both of us that he should have curbed his anger and

[015-188]kept out of my reach, for I should have had much trouble with

[015-189]him. Take, then, your tasselled aegis, and shake it furiously, so

[015-190]as to set the Achaean heroes in a panic; take, moreover, brave

[015-191]Hector, O Far-Darter, into your own care, and rouse him to deeds

[015-192]of daring, till the Achaeans are sent flying back to their ships

[015-193]and to the Hellespont. From that point I will think it well over,

[015-194]how the Achaeans may have a respite from their troubles.”

[015-195]Apollo obeyed his father’s saying, and left the crests of Ida,

[015-196]flying like a falcon, bane of doves and swiftest of all birds. He

[015-197]found Hector no longer lying upon the ground, but sitting up, for

[015-198]he had just come to himself again. He knew those who were about

[015-199]him, and the sweat and hard breathing had left him from the

[015-200]moment when the will of aegis-bearing Jove had revived him.

[015-201]Apollo stood beside him and said, “Hector son of [[priam|Priam]], why are

[015-202]you so faint, and why are you here away from the others? Has any

[015-203]mishap befallen you?”

[015-204]Hector in a weak voice answered, “And which, kind sir, of the

[015-205]gods are you, who now ask me thus? Do you not know that Ajax

[015-206]struck me on the chest with a stone as I was killing his comrades

[015-207]at the ships of the Achaeans, and compelled me to leave off

[015-208]fighting? I made sure that this very day I should breathe my last

[015-209]and go down into the house of Hades.”

[015-210]Then King Apollo said to him, “Take heart; the son of Saturn has

[015-211]sent you a mighty helper from Ida to stand by you and defend you,

[015-212]even me, Phoebus Apollo of the golden sword, who have been

[015-213]guardian hitherto not only of yourself but of your city. Now,

[015-214]therefore, order your horsemen to drive their chariots to the

[015-215]ships in great multitudes. I will go before your horses to smooth

[015-216]the way for them, and will turn the Achaeans in flight.”

[015-217]As he spoke he infused great strength into the shepherd of his

[015-218]people. And as a horse, stabled and full-fed, breaks loose and

[015-219]gallops gloriously over the plain to the place where he is wont

[015-220]to take his bath in the river—he tosses his head, and his mane

[015-221]streams over his shoulders as in all the pride of his strength he

[015-222]flies full speed to the pastures where the mares are feeding—even

[015-223]so Hector, when he heard what the god said, urged his horsemen

[015-224]on, and sped forward as fast as his limbs could take him. As

[015-225]country peasants set their hounds on to a homed stag or wild

[015-226]goat—he has taken shelter under rock or thicket, and they cannot

[015-227]find him, but, lo, a bearded lion whom their shouts have roused

[015-228]stands in their path, and they are in no further humour for the

[015-229]chase—even so the Achaeans were still charging on in a body,

[015-230]using their swords and spears pointed at both ends, but when they

[015-231]saw Hector going about among his men they were afraid, and their

[015-232]hearts fell down into their feet.

[015-233]Then spoke Thoas son of Andraemon, leader of the Aetolians, a man

[015-234]who could throw a good throw, and who was staunch also in close

[015-235]fight, while few could surpass him in [[counsel|debate]] when opinions were

[015-236]divided. He then with all sincerity and goodwill addressed them

[015-237]thus: “What, in heaven’s name, do I now see? Is it not Hector

[015-238]come to life again? Every one made sure he had been killed by

[015-239]Ajax son of Telamon, but it seems that one of the gods has again

[015-240]rescued him. He has killed many of us Danaans already, and I take

[015-241]it will yet do so, for the hand of Jove must be with him or he

[015-242]would never dare show himself so masterful in the forefront of

[015-243]the battle. Now, therefore, let us all do as I say; let us order

[015-244]the main body of our forces to fall back upon the ships, but let

[015-245]those of us who profess to be the flower of the army stand firm,

[015-246]and see whether we cannot hold Hector back at the point of our

[015-247]spears as soon as he comes near us; I conceive that he will then

[015-248]think better of it before he tries to charge into the press of

[015-249]the Danaans.”

[015-250]Thus did he speak, and they did even as he had said. Those who

[015-251]were about Ajax and King Idomeneus, the followers moreover of

[015-252]Teucer, Meriones, and Meges peer of Mars called all their best

[015-253]men about them and sustained the fight against Hector and the

[015-254]Trojans, but the main body fell back upon the ships of the

[015-255]Achaeans.

[015-256]The Trojans pressed forward in a dense body, with Hector striding

[015-257]on at their head. Before him went Phoebus Apollo shrouded in

[015-258]cloud about his shoulders. He bore aloft the terrible aegis with

[015-259]its shaggy fringe, which Vulcan the smith had given Jove to

[015-260]strike terror into the hearts of men. With this in his hand he

[015-261]led on the Trojans.

[015-262]The Argives held together and stood their ground. The cry of

[015-263]battle rose high from either side, and the arrows flew from the

[015-264]bowstrings. Many a spear sped from strong hands and fastened in

[015-265]the bodies of many a valiant warrior, while others fell to earth

[015-266]midway, before they could taste of man’s fair flesh and glut

[015-267]themselves with blood. So long as Phoebus Apollo held his aegis

[015-268]quietly and without shaking it, the weapons on either side took

[015-269]effect and the people fell, but when he shook it straight in the

[015-270]face of the Danaans and raised his mighty battle-cry their hearts

[015-271]fainted within them and they forgot their former [[aristeia|prowess]]. As when

[015-272]two wild beasts spring in the dead of night on a herd of cattle

[015-273]or a large flock of sheep when the herdsman is not there—even so

[015-274]were the Danaans struck helpless, for Apollo filled them with

[015-275]panic and gave victory to Hector and the Trojans.

[015-276]The fight then became more scattered and they killed one another

[015-277]where they best could. Hector killed Stichius and Arcesilaus, the

[015-278]one, leader of the Boeotians, and the other, friend and comrade

[015-279]of Menestheus. Aeneas killed Medon and Iasus. The first was

[015-280]bastard son to Oileus, and brother to Ajax, but he lived in

[015-281]Phylace away from his own country, for he had killed a man, a

[015-282]kinsman of his stepmother Eriopis whom Oileus had married. Iasus

[015-283]had become a leader of the Athenians, and was son of Sphelus the

[015-284]son of Boucolos. Polydamas killed Mecisteus, and Polites Echius,

[015-285]in the front of the battle, while Agenor slew Clonius. Paris

[015-286]struck Deiochus from behind in the lower part of the shoulder, as

[015-287]he was flying among the foremost, and the point of the spear went

[015-288]clean through him.

[015-289]While they were spoiling these heroes of their armour, the

[015-290]Achaeans were flying pell-mell to the trench and the set stakes,

[015-291]and were forced back within their wall. Hector then cried out to

[015-292]the Trojans, “Forward to the ships, and let the spoils be. If I

[015-293]see any man keeping back on the other side the wall away from the

[015-294]ships I will have him killed: his kinsmen and kinswomen shall not

[015-295]give him his dues of fire, but dogs shall tear him in pieces in

[015-296]front of our city.”

## Section 43

[015-297]As he spoke he laid his whip about his horses’ shoulders and

[015-298]called to the Trojans throughout their ranks; the Trojans shouted

[015-299]with a cry that rent the air, and kept their horses neck and neck

[015-300]with his own. Phoebus Apollo went before, and kicked down the

[015-301]banks of the deep trench into its middle so as to make a great

[015-302]broad bridge, as broad as the throw of a spear when a man is

[015-303]trying his strength. The Trojan battalions poured over the

[015-304]bridge, and Apollo with his redoubtable aegis led the way. He

[015-305]kicked down the wall of the Achaeans as easily as a child who

[015-306]playing on the sea-shore has built a house of sand and then kicks

[015-307]it down again and destroys it—even so did you, O Apollo, shed

[015-308]toil and trouble upon the Argives, filling them with panic and

[015-309]confusion.

[015-310]Thus then were the Achaeans hemmed in at their ships, calling out

[015-311]to one another and raising their hands with loud cries every man

[015-312]to heaven. [[nestor|Nestor]] of Gerene, tower of strength to the Achaeans,

[015-313]lifted up his hands to the starry firmament of heaven, and prayed

[015-315]any one in wheat-growing Argos burned you fat thigh-bones of

[015-316]sheep or heifer and prayed that he might return safely home,

[015-317]whereon you bowed your head to him in assent, bear it in mind

[015-318]now, and suffer not the Trojans to triumph thus over the

[015-319]Achaeans.”

[015-329]wielding the long pikes that were lying on the decks ready for

[015-335]his pain. When, however, he saw the Trojans swarming through the

[015-336]breach in the wall, while the Achaeans were clamouring and struck

[015-338]flat of his hands. “Eurypylus,” said he in his dismay, “I know

[015-339]you want me badly, but I cannot stay with you any longer, for

[015-340]there is hard fighting going on; a servant shall take care of you

[015-341]now, for I must make all speed to Achilles, and induce him to

[015-342]fight if I can; who knows but with heaven’s help I may persuade

[015-343]him. A man does well to listen to the advice of a friend.”

[015-344]When he had thus spoken he went his way. The Achaeans stood firm

[015-345]and resisted the attack of the Trojans, yet though these were

[015-346]fewer in number, they could not drive them back from the ships,

[015-347]neither could the Trojans break the Achaean ranks and make their

[015-352]two sides, as they fought some round one and some round another.

[015-353]Hector made straight for Ajax, and the two fought fiercely about

[015-354]the same ship. Hector could not force Ajax back and fire the

[015-355]ship, nor yet could Ajax drive Hector from the spot to which

[015-356]heaven had brought him.

[015-357]Then Ajax struck Caletor son of Clytius in the chest with a spear

[015-358]as he was bringing fire towards the ship. He fell heavily to the

[015-359]ground and the torch dropped from his hand. When Hector saw his

[015-360]cousin fallen in front of the ship he shouted to the Trojans and

[015-361]Lycians saying, “Trojans, Lycians, and Dardanians good in close

[015-362]fight, bate not a jot, but rescue the son of Clytius lest the

[015-363]Achaeans strip him of his armour now that he has fallen.”
