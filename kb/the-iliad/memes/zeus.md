# Zeus (Jove)

Zeus, called Jove in Samuel Butler's translation, is the supreme god of the Greek pantheon — the son of Cronus (Saturn), ruler of Olympus, and arbiter of mortal and divine affairs. In the Iliad, his will is the ultimate force governing the war's trajectory: he grants [[thetis|Thetis]]' request to honour [[achilles|Achilles]] by granting the Trojans victory, he weighs the fates of heroes on his golden scales, and his divine plan (*Dios boulē*) provides the poem's cosmic framework. His epithets include "cloud-gatherer," "lord of thunder," "aegis-bearing," and "son of Cronus" (Kronides).

## Identity

Zeus is the most powerful of the Olympian gods, possessing authority over heaven, earth, and the other immortals. He is the son of Cronus (rendered "Saturn" in Butler's translation) and Rhea, brother to [[hera|Hera]] (Juno), [[poseidon|Poseidon]] (Neptune), and [[hades|Hades]] (Pluto). His primary attributes are the thunderbolt — forged by [[hephaestus|Hephaestus]] (Vulcan) — and the aegis, a divine shield or breastplate whose very sight inspires terror. Butler consistently renders Zeus as "Jove," following the Latin convention familiar to English readers of his era (001-014).

Zeus' authority, while supreme, is not unchallenged. [[hera|Hera]] defies his commands in Book 1 (001-295), and in Book 15, he acknowledges that fate and the other gods can constrain even his will. The tension between Zeus' omnipotence and the limits imposed by destiny ([[fate|Fate (Moira)]]) is one of the poem's central theological concerns.

## Role in the Epic

Zeus' involvement spans the entire poem, from the opening quarrel to the final reconciliation in Book 24. His decisions — and the deceptions practiced upon him — drive the epic's plot at every stage.

- **Book 1 — Thetis' Petition:** Thetis, mother of Achilles, approaches Zeus on Olympus and clasps his knees in supplication, begging him to grant the Trojans victory until the Achaeans restore her son's honour (001-238). Zeus nods his immortal head in assent, shaking Olympus itself (001-252). This act sets the entire plot in motion — from this moment, all events unfold according to Zeus' will.

- **Book 2 — The Lying Dream:** Zeus sends a deceptive dream to Agamemnon in the likeness of Nestor, urging him to arm the Achaeans for a decisive assault on Troy, promising that "the immortals are now of one mind" (002-023). The dream is an instrument of Zeus' plan to honour Achilles by demonstrating Agamemnon's vulnerability without his greatest warrior.

- **Book 4 — The Olympus Quarrel:** When Zeus suggests making peace between Greeks and Trojans, Hera and Athena object violently (004-001–004-053). Zeus yields, but not without resentment: "Wife, I can do nothing but you suspect me and find it out" (004-080). He sends Athena down to break the truce, and the fighting resumes.

- **Book 8 — Divine Prohibition:** Zeus assembles the gods and forbids any of them from aiding either side in the coming battle (008-001–008-030). He threatens any disobedient deity with being hurled into Tartarus, asserting the supremacy of his power. This prohibition holds until Book 20, when he lifts it for the theomachy.

- **Book 15 — Reassertion of Will:** After Hera seduces him and puts him to sleep in Book 14, allowing Poseidon to aid the Achaeans, Zeus awakens in fury (015-001). He commands Hera to summon Iris and Apollo, and through them restores the Trojan advance according to his original plan. His anger at Hera is tempered by his memory of her earlier punishment — when he hung her from Olympus with anvils on her feet (015-025).

- **Book 16 — Sarpedon's Fate:** Zeus' mortal son Sarpedon, leader of the Lycians, is fated to die at Patroclus' hands. Zeus debates saving him: "My heart is divided — shall I snatch him out of the battle and set him down alive in Lycia, or shall I let him fall?" (016-256). Hera reminds him that all the gods would follow his example if he saves his son from fate. Zeus yields and allows Sarpedon to die, weeping bloody tears upon the earth (016-268).

- **Book 22 — The Golden Scales:** As Achilles pursues Hector around the walls of Troy, Zeus takes up his golden scales and places in them "two lots of death" — one for Achilles, one for Hector. Hector's lot sinks down to Hades (022-001–022-022), sealing his doom. Zeus does not intervene; the scales affirm that fate, once set, is immutable.

- **Book 24 — Divine Mercy:** In the poem's final book, Zeus summons Thetis to Olympus and commands her to instruct Achilles to accept ransom for Hector's body (024-060–024-150). He then sends Iris to Priam, guiding the Trojan king through the Greek camp. Zeus' decree of mercy provides the divine framework for the epic's transcendent conclusion.

## Notable Speeches

> "Wife," said Jove, "I can do nothing but you suspect me and find it out. You will not succeed in turning me from my resolve. You shall have your way, but it shall be a sore vexation to you."
> — Zeus to Hera, yielding but asserting his ultimate authority (001-295)

> "Hear me, all gods and all goddesses, as I speak what my heart prompts me. Let not any goddess nor yet any god presume to set at naught my word — obey it, every one of you, that I may bring these matters to an end."
> — Zeus forbidding divine intervention (008-001)

> "My heart is divided — shall I snatch him out of the battle and set him down alive in Lycia, or shall I let him fall now beneath the hands of the son of Menoetius?"
> — Zeus debating the fate of his son Sarpedon (016-256)

> "How prone you are, my poor mortals, to blame the gods! It is we who send you this misery, but you yourselves are the cause of your own undoing."
> — Zeus reflecting on mortal suffering (Book 1, Butler's rendering)

## Relationships

- **Hera (wife and antagonist):** Zeus' marriage to Hera is the most contentious relationship on Olympus. She defies him, deceives him (the seduction of Book 14), and openly opposes his plans. Zeus' authority is most tested in his dealings with her, and his occasional violence toward her serves as a demonstration of divine hierarchy.

- **Thetis (suppliant):** The sea-goddess approaches Zeus as a suppliant on behalf of her son Achilles (001-238). Zeus' willingness to grant her request — despite knowing it will bring him into conflict with Hera — shows both the power of the supplication ritual and Zeus' personal regard for Thetis, whom he once courted.

- **Athena (daughter):** Born from Zeus' head, [[athena|Athena]] is his most trusted divine agent. He dispatches her to break the truce in Book 4 (004-053) and often consults with her on matters of strategy.

- **Apollo (son and agent):** Zeus employs [[apollo|Apollo]] as his executor, sending him to protect Hector's body (024-030) and to guide Priam through the Achaean camp.

- **Sarpedon (mortal son):** Zeus' love for Sarpedon, leader of the Lycian contingent, produces one of the poem's most poignant moments. He debates saving his son from death but yields to fate and to Hera's counsel (016-256). His bloody tears for Sarpedon are a rare display of divine grief.

## Death or Fate

As a god, Zeus is immortal, but the Iliad explores a different question: the limits of divine power in a world governed by fate (*moira*). Zeus possesses the golden scales in which he weighs the fates of heroes, yet he cannot alter the outcome. He acknowledges that even he must yield to destiny — as when he allows Sarpedon to die (016-256) and when Hector's fate tips the scales (022-001). His role is not to override fate but to administer it. The poet presents Zeus neither as omnipotent in the theological sense nor as a mere observer, but as the cosmic steward of a destiny that even he respects.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler consistently translates Zeus as "Jove," the Latin nominative form of *Jupiter* (from *Iovis*, the genitive of *Iuppiter*). This convention was standard in 19th-century English translations of Homer and reflects the influence of Latin literary tradition. The Greek name Zeus (Ζεύς) derives from the Proto-Indo-European *Dyēus*, meaning "sky-father," cognate with Latin *Deus* and Sanskrit *Dyaus*.

The concept of *Dios boulē* (Διὸς βουλή), "the will of Zeus," is invoked in the Iliad's opening lines as the force that brought countless woes upon the Achaeans (001-001). Scholars debate whether this divine will operates as a personal, conscious plan or as a metaphor for the impersonal workings of fate. The poem itself seems to hold both possibilities in tension: Zeus' will is real, but it operates within the framework of destiny.

Homeric epithets for Zeus include *euryopa* (far-seeing), *nephelēgereta* (cloud-gatherer), *terpikeraunos* (who delights in thunder), and *aigiochos* (aegis-bearing). These compound epithets are formulaic elements of oral poetry that encode Zeus' attributes into the very rhythm of the hexameter line.
