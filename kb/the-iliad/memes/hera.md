# Hera

Hera (called Juno in Butler's translation) is the queen of the Olympian gods, wife and sister of [[zeus|Zeus]], and the most passionate divine partisan of the Achaean cause. Her hatred of Troy — born from the Judgment of Paris, in which the Trojan prince spurned her beauty in favor of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] — drives much of the divine action of the *Iliad*. She is imperious, cunning, and relentless in pursuit of Troy's destruction. Her relationship with Zeus is a tempestuous mixture of rivalry and submission: she schemes behind his back, defies him openly, and, when he asserts his supremacy, submits with terrified obedience.

## Identity

Hera is the daughter of Cronus and Rhea, making her one of the eldest Olympian deities. She is simultaneously the wife and sister of Zeus, a dual relationship that reflects the complex, often adversarial intimacy of their bond. Her epithets include "of the golden throne" (001-337), "white-armed" (008-323), and "of Argos" (004-013, 005-745), the last reflecting her special patronage of that city. In the divine hierarchy she ranks second only to Zeus, and she wields her status through a combination of seniority, cunning, and sheer force of will.

## Role in the Epic

Hera is the divine engine of Achaean victory. While Zeus initially favors the Trojans (at Thetis's request), Hera works tirelessly — and often deviously — to subvert his will and aid the Greeks.

Her most spectacular intervention comes in Book 5, when she and Athena descend to the battlefield in a chariot, scattering the Trojans and rallying the Greeks. When Zeus forbids further intervention in Book 8, her rage makes Olympus itself tremble (008-172). When she can no longer act openly, she turns to guile: in Book 14, she seduces Zeus — borrowing Aphrodite's magic girdle — to distract him while Poseidon aids the Achaeans. This episode reveals Hera's resourcefulness: when thwarted by superior power, she finds subtler means to achieve her ends.

## Notable Speeches

Hera's voice is sharp, urgent, and often insubordinate. When Zeus suggests ending the war with a negotiated peace, she explodes:

> Juno could not contain herself. "Dread son of Saturn," she answered, "what are you talking about?"
> — Hera protesting Zeus's proposed truce (004-025)

Her imperiousness is balanced by moments of genuine fear. When Zeus threatens her with violence, she submits immediately, recognizing the limits of her power:

> On this Juno was frightened, so she curbed her stubborn will and sat down in silence.
> — Hera after Zeus's rebuke (001-301)

Yet even in submission, her anger smolders. When Iris delivers Zeus's threats in Book 8, she makes Olympus "quake" as she writhes on her throne (008-172). Her son Hephaestus alone can calm her, coaxing a smile with wine and gentle words (001-324).

## Relationships

**Zeus:** The central relationship of Hera's existence is with her husband-brother Zeus. Their dynamic is a cosmic power struggle: she resents his authority, schemes to undermine it, and ultimately yields to it. Zeus, for his part, regards her with a mixture of exasperation, menace, and — occasionally — affection. He tells her in Book 1 that she makes him "quarrel" with her provocations (001-258), but he also shares his golden throne with her (001-337).

**Athena:** Hera's closest divine ally. The two goddesses are consistently paired in their support for the Achaeans, descending together to the battlefield and grieving together over Greek losses (004-012, 008-298). Their alliance is tactical as much as ideological: both were humiliated in the Judgment of Paris, and both are relentless in their pursuit of Troy's fall.

**Hephaestus:** Hera's son, the smith god, serves as peacemaker between his parents. When Zeus threatens Hera, Hephaestus counsels her to yield, reminding her of Zeus's unstoppable power from personal experience (001-304).

**Achaeans:** Hera's commitment to the Greek cause is absolute. Her favorite cities are Argos, Sparta, and Mycenae (004-044), and she is willing to sacrifice these to see Troy destroyed — a measure of the depth of her hatred.

## Death or Fate

As an immortal goddess, Hera cannot die. Her fate within the *Iliad* is to be perpetually thwarted yet never defeated: she schemes, is rebuked, submits, and schemes again. Her ultimate victory — the destruction of Troy — lies outside the poem's scope, but the *Iliad* makes clear that it is inevitable and that Hera's relentless will is one of its principal engines. Her punishment by Zeus — including the famous incident where he hung her from Olympus with anvils on her feet (recalled in Book 15) — is part of their ongoing power dynamic, a reminder that her authority, however great, is subordinate to his.

## Scholarly Notes

Hera in the *Iliad* is a complex combination of majesty and pettiness. Her hatred of Troy, rooted in the Judgment of Paris, is a divine magnification of human grievance — a goddess who, like Achilles, nurses a wound to her honor and pursues vengeance without compromise. Samuel Butler's translation uses "Juno" throughout, following the Roman convention, and captures her imperious voice with particular vividness. The epithet "white-armed" (*leukōlenos*) emphasizes her beauty, while "of the golden throne" (*khrysothronos*) emphasizes her status — together they create the image of a goddess whose power is inseparable from her majesty.

Scholars have noted the structural parallel between Hera and Achilles: both are the most powerful beings on their respective planes, both nurse a grievance that drives the plot, and both must ultimately yield to a higher authority (Zeus / fate). Her seduction of Zeus in Book 14 is one of the earliest and most psychologically nuanced depictions of marital manipulation in Western literature.
