# Armour of Achilles

The armour of [[achilles|Achilles]] — forged by the god [[hephaestus|Hephaestus]] in Book 18 of the *Iliad* — is the most celebrated set of arms in Western literature. After [[the-death-of-patroclus|Patroclus's death]] and [[hector|Hector]]'s seizure of [[achilles|Achilles]]' original armour (which had belonged to Peleus), [[thetis|Thetis]] ascends to [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] to commission a new set from the divine smith. What Hephaestus creates is not merely protective gear but a cosmic artwork: a shield depicting the entire world — city and country, peace and war, cultivation and conflict — surrounded by a helmet, cuirass, and greaves of unimaginable splendour. When [[achilles|Achilles]] dons this armour, he ceases to be a grieving mortal and becomes something closer to a force of nature — an agent of divine vengeance whose return to battle will consume everything in its path.

## Creation

The forging of [[achilles|Achilles]]' armour occupies the whole of Book 18 and is one of the *Iliad*'s most extraordinary passages:

| Phase | Action | Significance |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Thetis's Plea | Thetis finds Hephaestus in his workshop, recounts [[achilles|Achilles]]' grief | A mother's love sets divine craftsmanship in motion |
| The Forge | Hephaestus kindles twenty bellows and sets to work | The forge is both a workshop and a cosmic creative force |
| The Shield | The first and most elaborate creation — a microcosm of the world | The shield is the poem within the poem: a vision of life in its totality |
| The Cuirass | A breastplate brighter than fire | Divine radiance made wearable |
| The Helmet | A golden helmet with a golden crest | The crest that will terrify the [[trojans|Trojans]] from the trench |
| The Greaves | Greaves of pliant tin | Completing the divine panoply |

## The Shield of Achilles

The shield is the centrepiece of the armour and one of the most famous ekphrastic passages in all literature. Hephaestus engraves upon it:

- **The Cosmos**: Earth, sky, sea, sun, moon, and all the constellations
- **Two Cities**: One at peace (a wedding, a legal dispute in the marketplace), one at war (a siege, an ambush, slaughter in the fields)
- **Agricultural Scenes**: Ploughing, reaping, a vineyard heavy with grapes, a herd of cattle attacked by lions
- **A Dancing Floor**: Young men and w[[prophecy|omen]] dancing, "like the circle a potter makes to test his wheel"
- **The River Ocean**: Encircling the rim — the boundary of the known world

The shield is not a military object — it is a vision of the entire human world that the war will destroy. [[achilles|Achilles]] bears into battle an image of everything worth preserving, an unbearable juxtaposition of beauty and violence.

## Significance

The armour embodies a central paradox of the *Iliad*:

- **Protection and Destruction**: The armour protects [[achilles|Achilles]], but its purpose is to enable destruction — specifically, the slaughter of [[hector|Hector]] and countless [[trojans|Trojans]].
- **Divine and Mortal**: The armour is divine in origin but worn by a mortal. It allows [[achilles|Achilles]] to fight at a level beyond human capacity, yet cannot save him from his fated death.
- **Grief Made Manifest**: The armour is born from Thetis's grief and [[achilles|Achilles]]' [[grief|sorrow]]. It is love transformed into an instrument of war — a mother's desperate gift to a son she knows will die.
