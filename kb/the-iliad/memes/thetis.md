# Thetis

Thetis is a sea-nymph of the Nereids, daughter of the sea-god Nereus, and the mortal mother of [[achilles|Achilles]]. Her presence in the Iliad is defined by maternal love and intercession: she approaches [[zeus|Zeus]] (Jove) on Olympus to beg that the Trojans be granted victory until the Achaeans restore her son's honour (001-238). Her supplication sets the epic's entire plot in motion. Throughout the poem, she appears at moments of Achilles' deepest need — comforting him by the shore, commissioning divine armour from [[hephaestus|Hephaestus]] (Vulcan), and carrying Zeus' commands. Butler renders her epithet as "silver-footed" (*argyropeza* in the Greek), an image of divine radiance touching the sea.

## Identity

As a goddess of the sea, Thetis inhabits the depths with her father Nereus, the Old Man of the Sea. Her dual nature — divine, yet bound to a mortal son — defines her tragic dimension. She married the mortal Peleus under Zeus' arrangement, a union that produced Achilles, the greatest of Greek warriors. Thetis knows from the beginning that her son will die young if he fights at Troy, and every one of her interventions is shaped by this knowledge. She is among the Iliad's most sympathetic divine figures: powerful enough to sway the king of the gods, yet helpless to change her son's fate.

Thetis is described as "silver-footed" (a compound of *argyros* — silver — and *peza* — foot), a visual epithet suggesting the gleam of light on the sea's surface as she rises from the depths. She is sometimes called "Thetis of the lovely locks," emphasizing her beauty. In Butler's translation, she is consistently rendered as Thetis, retaining her Greek name unlike deities who received Latin equivalents.

## Role in the Epic

Thetis appears at critical junctures in the narrative, each time in direct response to her son's suffering or need.

- **Book 1 — The Supplication:** After Agamemnon seizes Briseis, Achilles withdraws to the shore and prays to his mother. Thetis rises from the sea "like a mist" and listens to his grief (001-170). She agrees to intercede with Zeus, ascending to Olympus where she clasps the god's knees in the formal ritual of supplication (001-238). She reminds Zeus of her past service to him — how she alone among the gods saved him when the other Olympians bound him. Zeus nods his immortal head, and from this moment, the entire war turns against the Achaeans.

- **Book 18 — The Cry:** When Patroclus falls to [[hector|Hector]], Achilles' cry of grief is so powerful that Thetis hears it from the depths of the sea, where she sits beside her father Nereus (018-001). She rises with her sister Nereids and comes to her son, lamenting: "My son, why do you weep? What sorrow has come upon your heart?" (018-002). Learning that Patroclus has died and Hector has taken Achilles' armour, she promises to bring new armour forged by Hephaestus himself.

- **Book 18 — At Hephaestus' Forge:** Thetis travels to Olympus and enters the workshop of Hephaestus, the lame smith god (018-200). She is received with honour by Hephaestus and his wife Charis, and the god agrees to forge armour for her son in gratitude for Thetis having saved him when Hera cast him from Olympus. The forging of the Shield of Achilles — the most detailed ekphrasis in ancient literature — is the direct result of Thetis' intercession.

- **Book 24 — Zeus' Summons:** In the epic's final book, Zeus summons Thetis to Olympus and instructs her to tell Achilles that the gods are angry with him for desecrating Hector's body, and that he must accept ransom from [[priam|Priam]] (024-060). Thetis descends to deliver the message. Her final appearance in the poem is as a vehicle of divine mercy, guiding her son toward the compassion that brings the epic to its transcendent close.

## Relationships

- **Achilles (son):** Thetis' entire existence in the Iliad revolves around her son. She knows his fate — a short, glorious life or a long, obscure one — and supports his choice of glory. Her grief is a mirror of his: when Achilles weeps, Thetis weeps. No other divine-mortal relationship in the poem is so emotionally intimate.

- **Zeus (debtor and granter):** Thetis once saved Zeus when the other gods bound him, summoning the hundred-handed giant Briareus to his aid (001-275). Zeus is bound by this debt of gratitude, and Thetis invokes it when she supplicates him on Achilles' behalf. Their interaction demonstrates the network of reciprocal obligation that governs even divine relationships.

- **Hephaestus (debtor and benefactor):** Thetis saved the infant Hephaestus when Hera cast him from Olympus, hiding him in the sea for nine years (018-200). Hephaestus remembers this debt with warmth and repays it by forging the most magnificent armour ever described in literature. Their relationship illustrates how Thetis' kindness to the gods has created a web of obligation that she can call upon for her son.

- **Peleus (husband):** Though Peleus never appears in the Iliad directly, his absence from Thetis' life (she left him after their marriage) and his role as Achilles' mortal father form the background to Thetis' tragedy — she is a goddess who must watch her mortal son die because of his mortal father's lineage.

## Death or Fate

Thetis herself is immortal, but her fate is to outlive her son, and this knowledge suffuses every appearance she makes. Unlike the other gods, whose interventions are driven by pride, favouritism, or vengeance, Thetis acts from pure maternal love — yet she cannot change what destiny has decreed. She is the Iliad's most poignant embodiment of the truth that even the gods must yield to fate. Her lament in Book 18 — "Why did I rear you, if I did so to a terrible end?" (018-002) — echoes across the poem as a mother's protest against the cosmic order.

## Scholarly Notes

Thetis' epithet *argyropeza* (ἀργυρόπεζα), "silver-footed," is one of the most distinctive in Homeric poetry. It may describe the gleaming beauty of her feet as she moves through the sea-foam, or it may be a fossilized formula whose original meaning was already obscure to Homer's audience. Butler translates it literally as "silver-footed."

Thetis occupies a unique position in Greek mythology. Zeus and Poseidon both desired her, but an oracle prophesied that her son would be greater than his father — so the gods arranged her marriage to the mortal Peleus instead. This backstory, though not narrated in the Iliad, underlies her tragedy: the goddess who could have produced a son to overthrow Zeus instead produces Achilles, whose glory comes at the cost of early death.

The scene of Thetis supplicating Zeus (001-238–001-290) is one of the most complete depictions of the ancient Greek supplication ritual in literature. She approaches, clasps his knees with one hand and his chin with the other, and speaks her plea — the formal posture lending divine authority to her request.
