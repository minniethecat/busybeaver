# Ajax (the Greater)

## Identity

Ajax, son of Telamon, is the king of Salamis and the greatest defensive warrior among the Achaeans. In the Catalog of Ships, he is described as the foremost fighter among the Greeks while Achilles remained absent from battle (002-427). His most distinctive attribute is his massive shield, described as being "like a tower" (008-231) — a shield of seven layers of ox-hide covered with bronze, so heavy that few other men could lift it. In the teichoscopia (viewing from the walls), Helen identifies him to Priam as "huge Ajax, bulwark of the Achaeans" (003-182), an epithet that defines his essential function in the epic: he is the defensive anchor, the unbreakable wall against which Trojan assaults repeatedly shatter.

Physically, Ajax is distinguished by his enormous size and strength. He fights without divine assistance — unlike Diomedes, Achilles, or Hector, he receives no direct divine aid in battle. His heroism is purely human, grounded in brute strength and unwavering courage.

## Role in the Epic

Ajax appears throughout the Iliad in moments of crisis, always as the last line of defense and the most reliable of warriors.

- **Book 2 — Catalog of Ships:** Ajax brings twelve ships from Salamis (002-450) and is identified as the best fighter while Achilles is absent (002-427).

- **Book 3 — The Teichoscopia:** When Priam sees Ajax from the walls of Troy, Helen identifies him as "huge Ajax, bulwark of the Achaeans" (003-182) — the epithet that would forever define his role and legacy.

- **Book 6 — Tower of Strength:** In the thick of battle, Ajax is described as the "tower of strength to the Achaeans" (006-008) as he breaks the Trojan line, a moment that captures his function as both physical and psychological anchor for the Greek army.

- **Book 7 — The Duel with Hector:** Ajax is chosen by lot to face Hector in single combat. He advances with his great shield before him and comes "close up to Hector" (007-179). The two champions fight to a standstill through the failing light, and their duel ends with mutual respect and the exchange of gifts — Hector gives Ajax a sword, and Ajax gives Hector a belt. It is one of the few moments in the epic where two enemies honor each other as equals.

- **Books 9 — The Embassy to Achilles:** Ajax is among the three ambassadors (with Odysseus and Phoenix) sent to persuade Achilles to return to battle. His speech is characteristically blunt and direct — he appeals not to material compensation but to the bonds of comradeship, arguing that Achilles' refusal shames the very basis of heroic society.

- **Books 13–16 — Defense of the Ships:** When Hector breaches the Achaean wall and the Trojans threaten to burn the Greek ships, Ajax is the central figure in the desperate defense. He fights alone on the decks of the ships, wielding a massive boarding pike, while the Trojan assault surges around him. His stand at the ships is one of the most dramatic sequences in the epic — Ajax alone, refusing to yield ground, holding back the entire Trojan advance.

## Notable Speeches

> Noble Ajax, son of Telamon, captain of the host.
> — Hector addressing Ajax before their duel (007-186)

> Ajax son of Telamon answered, "Idaeus, bid Hector..."
> — Ajax responding to the herald's proposal to end the duel (007-225)

Ajax is not the most eloquent of speakers — his strength is in action, not words — but his bluntness carries its own power. In the embassy to Achilles, while Odysseus deploys rhetorical skill and Phoenix appeals to emotion, Ajax speaks directly to the shame of abandoning one's comrades, and his words come closest to moving Achilles.

## Relationships

- **Achilles (comrade):** Ajax and Achilles share a bond of mutual respect as the two greatest Achaean warriors. In the embassy, Ajax's direct appeal touches Achilles more than the elaborate speeches of Odysseus and Phoenix.
- **Hector (worthy adversary):** The relationship between Ajax and Hector is defined by their duel in Book 7, which ends in mutual respect and the exchange of gifts. Ajax is the only Achaean besides Achilles whom Hector treats as an equal.
- **Teucer (half-brother):** Teucer, the great Achaean archer, often fights from behind Ajax's shield — Ajax crouches to protect him while Teucer fires arrows over the rim, a partnership that embodies Ajax's protective role.
- **Odysseus (rival):** Ajax and Odysseus have a competitive relationship that reaches its tragic climax after the Iliad, in the contest for the arms of Achilles. Ajax loses the contest to Odysseus' eloquence and, in his madness and shame, takes his own life — a story told in Sophocles' *Ajax* but not in the Iliad itself.
- **The Other Ajax:** Ajax, son of Telamon, is distinguished from Ajax, son of Oileus (the Lesser Ajax, or "fleet son of Oileus" as in 002-426). The two are often mentioned fighting side by side as "the two Ajaxes."

## Death or Fate

The Iliad does not depict Ajax's death, but it is one of the most famous and tragic in the Greek tradition. After Achilles' death, a contest is held to award his divine armor to the worthiest surviving warrior. Ajax and Odysseus are the two final contenders. When the armor is awarded to Odysseus, Ajax is driven mad by Athena. In his delusion, he slaughters a herd of cattle, believing them to be the Greek commanders who wronged him. When he recovers his senses and realizes what he has done, he takes his own life with the sword Hector gave him. Sophocles' tragedy *Ajax* is the classic treatment of this story. The irony is profound: the bravest and most loyal of the Achaeans is destroyed not by any enemy but by shame, the very force that, in Homeric culture, was meant to motivate heroic excellence.

## Scholarly Notes

Ajax represents a distinct type of heroic ideal in the Iliad — the hero of *alke*, or defensive strength, as opposed to the hero of *bie*, offensive might (Achilles), or the hero of *metis*, cunning intelligence (Odysseus). His unmovable steadfastness is the necessary complement to Achilles' world-shattering violence. Without Ajax, the Achaeans would have no center to hold; without Achilles, they would have no sword to strike.

The contrast between Ajax and Odysseus — brute strength versus persuasive speech — is one of the fundamental oppositions in Greek thought, explored not only in the contest for Achilles' arms but throughout the epic tradition. Ajax's silence in the underworld in Book 11 of the Odyssey, where he turns away from Odysseus without a word, is one of the most haunting images in all of ancient literature — the unbreakable warrior, finally broken, unable to forgive even in death.
