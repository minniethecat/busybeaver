# Book IV

[004-001]BOOK IV.

[004-002]A quarrel in [[mount-olympus|Olympus]]—Minerva goes down and persuades Fandarus to

[004-003]violate the oaths by wounding Menelaus with an arrow—[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]

[004-004]makes a speech and sends for Machaon—He then goes about among his

[004-005]captains and upbraids [[odysseus|Ulysses]] and Sthenelus, who each of them

[004-006]retort fiercely—Diomed checks Sthenelus, and the two hosts then

[004-007]engage, with great slaughter on either side.

[004-008]Now the gods were sitting with Jove in council upon the golden

[004-009]floor while Hebe went round pouring out nectar for them to drink,

[004-010]and as they pledged one another in their cups of gold they looked

[004-011]down upon the town of Troy. The son of Saturn then began to tease

[004-012]Juno, talking at her so as to provoke her. “Menelaus,” said he,

[004-013]“has two good friends among the goddesses, Juno of Argos, and

[004-014]Minerva of Alalcomene, but they only sit still and look on, while

[004-015]Venus keeps ever by [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]]’ side to defend him in any danger;

[004-016]indeed she has just rescued him when he made sure that it was all

[004-017]over with him—for the victory really did lie with Menelaus. We

[004-018]must consider what we shall do about all this; shall we set them

[004-019]fighting anew or make peace between them? If you will agree to

[004-020]this last Menelaus can take back [[helen|Helen]] and the city of [[priam|Priam]] may

[004-021]remain still inhabited.”

[004-022]Minerva and Juno muttered their discontent as they sat side by

[004-023]side hatching mischief for the Trojans. Minerva scowled at her

[004-024]father, for she was in a furious passion with him, and said

[004-025]nothing, but Juno could not contain herself. “Dread son of

[004-026]Saturn,” said she, “what, pray, is the meaning of all this? Is my

[004-027]trouble, then, to go for nothing, and the sweat that I have

[004-028]sweated, to say nothing of my horses, while getting the people

[004-029]together against Priam and his children? Do as you will, but we

[004-030]other gods shall not all of us approve your counsel.”

[004-031]Jove was angry and answered, “My dear, what harm have Priam and

[004-032]his sons done you that you are so hotly bent on sacking the city

[004-033]of Ilius? Will nothing do for you but you must go within their walls

[004-034]and eat Priam raw, with his sons and all the other Trojans to

[004-035]boot? Have it your own way then; for I would not have this matter

[004-036]become a bone of contention between us. I say further, and lay my

[004-037]saying to your heart, if ever I want to sack a city belonging to

[004-038]friends of yours, you must not try to stop me; you will have to

[004-039]let me do it, for I am giving in to you sorely against my will.

[004-040]Of all inhabited cities under the sun and stars of heaven, there

[004-041]was none that I so much respected as Ilius with Priam and his

[004-042]whole people. Equitable feasts were never wanting about my [[sacrifice|altar]],

[004-043]nor the savour of burning fat, which is [[honor|honour]] due to ourselves.”

[004-044]“My own three favourite cities,” answered Juno, “are Argos,

[004-045]Sparta, and Mycenae. Sack them whenever you may be displeased

[004-046]with them. I shall not defend them and I shall not care. Even if

[004-047]I did, and tried to stay you, I should take nothing by it, for

[004-048]you are much stronger than I am, but I will not have my own work

[004-049]wasted. I too am a god and of the same race with yourself. I am

[004-050]Saturn’s eldest daughter, and am honourable not on this ground

[004-051]only, but also because I am your wife, and you are king over the

[004-052]gods. Let it be a case, then, of give-and-take between us, and

[004-053]the rest of the gods will follow our lead. Tell Minerva to go and

[004-054]take part in the fight at once, and let her contrive that the

[004-055]Trojans shall be the first to break their oaths and set upon the

[004-056]Achaeans.”

[004-057]The sire of gods and men heeded her words, and said to Minerva,

[004-058]“Go at once into the Trojan and Achaean hosts, and contrive that

[004-059]the Trojans shall be the first to break their oaths and set upon

[004-060]the Achaeans.”

[004-061]This was what Minerva was already eager to do, so down she darted

[004-062]from the topmost summits of Olympus. She shot through the sky as

[004-063]some brilliant meteor which the son of scheming Saturn has sent

[004-064]as a [[prophecy|sign]] to mariners or to some great army, and a fiery train of

[004-065]light follows in its wake. The Trojans and Achaeans were struck

[004-066]with awe as they beheld, and one would turn to his neighbour,

[004-067]saying, “Either we shall again have war and din of combat, or

[004-068]Jove the lord of battle will now make peace between us.”

## Section 10

[004-069]Thus did they converse. Then Minerva took the form of Laodocus,

[004-070]son of Antenor, and went through the ranks of the Trojans to find

[004-071]Pandarus, the redoubtable son of Lycaon. She found him standing

[004-072]among the stalwart heroes who had followed him from the banks of

[004-073]the Aesopus, so she went close up to him and said, “Brave son of

[004-074]Lycaon, will you do as I tell you? If you dare send an arrow at

[004-075]Menelaus you will win honour and thanks from all the Trojans, and

[004-076]especially from prince Alexandrus—he would be the first to

[004-077]requite you very handsomely if he could see Menelaus mount his

[004-078]funeral pyre, slain by an arrow from your hand. Take your home

[004-079]aim then, and pray to Lycian Apollo, the famous archer; [[oath|vow]] that

[004-080]when you get home to your strong city of Zelea you will offer a

[004-081]hecatomb of firstling lambs in his honour.”

[004-082]His fool’s heart was persuaded, and he took his bow from its

[004-083]case. This bow was made from the horns of a wild ibex which he

[004-084]had killed as it was bounding from a rock; he had stalked it, and

[004-085]it had fallen as the arrow struck it to the heart. Its horns were

[004-086]sixteen palms long, and a worker in horn had made them into a

[004-087]bow, smoothing them well down, and giving them tips of gold. When

[004-088]Pandarus had strung his bow he laid it carefully on the ground,

[004-089]and his brave followers held their shields before him lest the

[004-090]Achaeans should set upon him before he had shot Menelaus. Then he

[004-091]opened the lid of his quiver and took out a winged arrow that had

[004-092]not yet been shot, fraught with the pangs of death. He laid the

[004-093]arrow on the string and prayed to Lycian Apollo, the famous

[004-094]archer, vowing that when he got home to his strong city of Zelea

[004-095]he would offer a hecatomb of firstling lambs in his honour. He

[004-096]laid the notch of the arrow on the ox-hide bowstring, and drew

[004-097]both notch and string to his breast till the arrow-head was near

[004-098]the bow; then when the bow was arched into a half-circle he let

[004-099]fly, and the bow twanged, and the string sang as the arrow flew

[004-100]gladly on over the heads of the throng.

[004-101]But the blessed gods did not forget thee, O Menelaus, and Jove’s

[004-102]daughter, driver of the [[prize|spoil]], was the first to stand before thee

[004-103]and ward off the piercing arrow. She turned it from his skin as a

[004-104]mother whisks a fly from off her child when it is sleeping

[004-105]sweetly; she guided it to the part where the golden buckles of

[004-106]the belt that passed over his double cuirass were fastened, so

[004-107]the arrow struck the belt that went tightly round him. It went

[004-108]right through this and through the cuirass of cunning

[004-109]workmanship; it also pierced the belt beneath it, which he wore

[004-110]next his skin to keep out darts or arrows; it was this that

[004-111]served him in the best stead, nevertheless the arrow went through

[004-112]it and grazed the top of the skin, so that blood began flowing

[004-113]from the wound.

[004-114]As when some woman of Meonia or Caria strains purple dye on to a

[004-115]piece of ivory that is to be the cheek-piece of a horse, and is

[004-116]to be laid up in a treasure house—many a knight is fain to bear

[004-117]it, but the king keeps it as an ornament of which both horse and

[004-118]driver may be proud—even so, O Menelaus, were your shapely thighs

[004-119]and your legs down to your fair ancles stained with blood.

[004-120]When King Agamemnon saw the blood flowing from the wound he was

[004-121]afraid, and so was brave Menelaus himself till he saw that the

[004-122]barbs of the arrow and the thread that bound the arrow-head to

[004-123]the shaft were still outside the wound. Then he took heart, but

[004-124]Agamemnon heaved a deep sigh as he held Menelaus’s hand in his

[004-125]own, and his comrades made moan in concert. “Dear brother,” he

[004-126]cried, “I have been the death of you in pledging this covenant

[004-127]and letting you come forward as our champion. The Trojans have

[004-128]trampled on their oaths and have wounded you; nevertheless the

[004-129]oath, the blood of lambs, the drink-offerings and the right hands

[004-130]of fellowship in which we have put our trust shall not be vain.

[004-131]If he that rules Olympus fulfil it not here and now, he will yet

[004-132]fulfil it hereafter, and they shall pay dearly with their lives

[004-133]and with their wives and children. The day will surely come when

[004-134]mighty Ilius shall be laid low, with Priam and Priam’s people,

[004-135]when the son of Saturn from his high throne shall overshadow them

[004-136]with his awful aegis in punishment of their present treachery.

[004-137]This shall surely be; but how, Menelaus, shall I mourn you, if it

[004-138]be your lot now to die? I should return to Argos as a by-word,

[004-139]for the Achaeans will at once go home. We shall leave Priam and

[004-140]the Trojans the glory of still keeping Helen, and the earth will

[004-141]rot your bones as you lie here at Troy with your purpose not

[004-142]fulfilled. Then shall some braggart Trojan leap upon your tomb

[004-143]and say, ‘Ever thus may Agamemnon wreak his vengeance; he brought

[004-144]his army in vain; he is gone home to his own land with empty

[004-145][[achaean-camp|ships]], and has left Menelaus behind him.’ Thus will one of them

[004-146]say, and may the earth then swallow me.”

[004-147]But Menelaus reassured him and said, “Take heart, and do not

[004-148]alarm the people; the arrow has not struck me in a mortal part,

[004-149]for my outer belt of burnished metal first stayed it, and under

[004-150]this my cuirass and the belt of mail which the bronze-smiths made

[004-151]me.”

[004-152]And Agamemnon answered, “I trust, dear Menelaus, that it may be

[004-153]even so, but the surgeon shall examine your wound and lay herbs

[004-154]upon it to relieve your pain.”

[004-155]He then said to Talthybius, “Talthybius, tell Machaon, son to the

[004-156]great physician, Aesculapius, to come and see Menelaus

[004-157]immediately. Some Trojan or Lycian archer has wounded him with an

[004-158]arrow to our dismay, and to his own great glory.”

[004-159]Talthybius did as he was told, and went about the [[guest-friendship|host]] trying to

[004-160]find Machaon. Presently he found standing amid the brave warriors

[004-161]who had followed him from Tricca; thereon he went up to him and

[004-162]said, “Son of Aesculapius, King Agamemnon says you are to come

[004-163]and see Menelaus immediately. Some Trojan or Lycian archer has

[004-164]wounded him with an arrow to our dismay and to his own great

[004-165]glory.”

[004-166]Thus did he speak, and Machaon was moved to go. They passed

[004-167]through the spreading host of the Achaeans and went on till they

[004-168]came to the place where Menelaus had been wounded and was lying

[004-169]with the chieftains gathered in a circle round him. Machaon

[004-170]passed into the middle of the ring and at once drew the arrow

[004-171]from the belt, bending its barbs back through the force with

[004-172]which he pulled it out. He undid the burnished belt, and beneath

[004-173]this the cuirass and the belt of mail which the bronze-smiths had

[004-174]made; then, when he had seen the wound, he wiped away the blood

[004-175]and applied some soothing drugs which Chiron had given to

[004-176]Aesculapius out of the good will he bore him.

[004-177]While they were thus busy about Menelaus, the Trojans came

[004-178]forward against them, for they had put on their armour, and now

[004-179]renewed the fight.

[004-180]You would not have then found Agamemnon asleep nor cowardly and

[004-181]unwilling to fight, but eager rather for the fray. He left his

[004-182]chariot rich with bronze and his panting steeds in charge of

[004-183]Eurymedon, son of Ptolemaeus the son of Peiraeus, and bade him

[004-184]hold them in readiness against the time his limbs should weary of

[004-185]going about and giving orders to so many, for he went among the

[004-186]ranks on foot. When he saw men hasting to the front he stood by

[004-187]them and cheered them on. “Argives,” said he, “slacken not one

[004-188]whit in your onset; father Jove will be no helper of liars; the

[004-189]Trojans have been the first to break their oaths and to attack

[004-190]us; therefore they shall be devoured of vultures; we shall take

[004-191]their city and carry off their wives and children in our ships.”

[004-192]But he angrily rebuked those whom he saw shirking and disinclined

[004-193]to fight. “Argives,” he cried, “cowardly miserable creatures,

[004-194]have you no shame to stand here like frightened fawns who, when

[004-195]they can no longer scud over the plain, huddle together, but show

[004-196]no fight? You are as dazed and spiritless as deer. Would you wait

[004-197]till the Trojans reach the sterns of our ships as they lie on the

[004-198]shore, to see whether the son of Saturn will hold his hand over

[004-199]you to protect you?”

[004-200]Thus did he go about giving his orders among the ranks. Passing

[004-201]through the crowd, he came presently on the Cretans, arming round

[004-202]Idomeneus, who was at their head, fierce as a wild boar, while

[004-203]Meriones was bringing up the battalions that were in the rear.

[004-204]Agamemnon was glad when he saw him, and spoke him fairly.

[004-205]“Idomeneus,” said he, “I treat you with greater distinction than

[004-206]I do any others of the Achaeans, whether in war or in other

[004-207]things, or at table. When the princes are mixing my choicest

[004-208]wines in the mixing-bowls, they have each of them a fixed

[004-209]allowance, but your cup is kept always full like my own, that you

[004-210]may drink whenever you are minded. Go, therefore, into battle,

[004-211]and show yourself the man you have been always proud to be.”

[004-212]Idomeneus answered, “I will be a trusty comrade, as I promised

[004-213]you from the first I would be. Urge on the other Achaeans, that

[004-214]we may join battle at once, for the Trojans have trampled upon

[004-215]their covenants. Death and destruction shall be theirs, seeing

[004-216]they have been the first to break their oaths and to attack us.”

[004-217]The son of Atreus went on, glad at heart, till he came upon the

[004-218]two Ajaxes arming themselves amid a host of foot soldiers. As

[004-219]when a goat-herd from some high post watches a storm drive over

[004-220]the deep before the west wind—black as pitch is the offing and a

[004-221]mighty whirlwind draws towards him, so that he is afraid and

[004-222]drives his flock into a cave—even thus did the ranks of stalwart

[004-223]youths move in a dark mass to battle under the Ajaxes, horrid

[004-224]with shield and spear. Glad was King Agamemnon when he saw them.

[004-225]“No need,” he cried, “to give orders to such leaders of the

[004-226]Argives as you are, for of your own selves you spur your men on

[004-227]to fight with might and main. Would, by father Jove, Minerva, and

[004-228]Apollo that all were so minded as you are, for the city of Priam

[004-229]would then soon fall beneath our hands, and we should sack it.”

[004-230]With this he left them and went onward to [[nestor|Nestor]], the facile

[004-231]speaker of the Pylians, who was marshalling his men and urging

[004-232]them on, in company with Pelagon, Alastor, Chromius, Haemon, and

[004-233]Bias shepherd of his people. He placed his knights with their

[004-234]chariots and horses in the front rank, while the foot soldiers,

[004-235]brave men and many, whom he could trust, were in the rear. The

[004-236]cowards he drove into the middle, that they might fight whether

[004-237]they would or no. He gave his orders to the knights first,

[004-238]bidding them hold their horses well in hand, so as to avoid

[004-239]confusion. “Let no man,” he said, “relying on his strength or

[004-240]horsemanship, get before the others and engage singly with the

[004-241]Trojans, nor yet let him lag behind or you will weaken your

[004-242]attack; but let each when he meets an enemy’s chariot throw his

[004-243]spear from his own; this be much the best; this is how the men of

[004-244]old took towns and strongholds; in this wise were they minded.”

## Section 11

[004-245]Thus did the old man charge them, for he had been in many a

[004-246]fight, and King Agamemnon was glad. “I wish,” he said to him,

[004-247]“that your limbs were as supple and your strength as sure as your

[004-248]judgment is; but age, the common enemy of mankind, has laid his

[004-249]hand upon you; would that it had fallen upon some other, and that

[004-250]you were still young.”

[004-251]And Nestor, knight of Gerene, answered, “Son of Atreus, I too

[004-252]would gladly be the man I was when I slew mighty Ereuthalion; but

[004-253]the gods will not give us everything at one and the same time. I

[004-254]was then young, and now I am old; still I can go with my knights

[004-255]and give them that counsel which old men have a right to give.

[004-256]The wielding of the spear I leave to those who are younger and

[004-257]stronger than myself.”

[004-258]Agamemnon went his way rejoicing, and presently found Menestheus,

[004-259]son of Peteos, tarrying in his place, and with him were the

[004-260]Athenians loud of tongue in battle. Near him also tarried cunning

[004-261]Ulysses, with his sturdy Cephallenians round him; they had not

[004-262]yet heard the battle-cry, for the ranks of Trojans and Achaeans

[004-263]had only just begun to move, so they were standing still, waiting

[004-264]for some other columns of the Achaeans to attack the Trojans and

[004-265]begin the fighting. When he saw this Agamemnon rebuked them and

[004-266]said, “Son of Peteos, and you other, steeped in cunning, heart of

[004-267]guile, why stand you here cowering and waiting on others? You two

[004-268]should be of all men foremost when there is hard fighting to be

[004-269]done, for you are ever foremost to accept my invitation when we

[004-270]councillors of the Achaeans are holding feast. You are glad

[004-271]enough then to take your fill of roast meats and to drink wine as

[004-272]long as you please, whereas now you would not care though you saw

[004-273]ten columns of Achaeans engage the enemy in front of you.”

[004-274]Ulysses glared at him and answered, “Son of Atreus, what are you

[004-275]talking about? How can you say that we are slack? When the

[004-276]Achaeans are in full fight with the Trojans, you shall see, if

[004-277]you care to do so, that the father of Telemachus will join battle

[004-278]with the foremost of them. You are talking idly.”

[004-279]When Agamemnon saw that Ulysses was angry, he smiled pleasantly

[004-280]at him and withdrew his words. “Ulysses,” said he, “noble son of

[004-281]Laertes, excellent in all good counsel, I have neither fault to

[004-282]find nor orders to give you, for I know your heart is right, and

[004-283]that you and I are of a mind. Enough; I will make you amends for

[004-284]what I have said, and if any ill has now been spoken may the gods

[004-285]bring it to nothing.”

[004-286]He then left them and went on to others. Presently he saw the son

[004-287]of Tydeus, noble Diomed, standing by his chariot and horses, with

[004-288]Sthenelus the son of Capaneus beside him; whereon he began to

[004-289]upbraid him. “Son of Tydeus,” he said, “why stand you cowering

[004-290]here upon the brink of battle? Tydeus did not shrink thus, but

[004-291]was ever ahead of his men when leading them on against the

[004-292]foe—so, at least, say they that saw him in battle, for I never

[004-293]set eyes upon him myself. They say that there was no man like

[004-294]him. He came once to Mycenae, not as an enemy but as a guest, in

[004-295]company with Polynices to recruit his forces, for they were

[004-296]levying war against the strong city of Thebes, and prayed our

[004-297]people for a body of picked men to help them. The men of Mycenae

[004-298]were willing to let them have one, but Jove dissuaded them by

[004-299]showing them unfavourable omens. Tydeus, therefore, and Polynices

[004-300]went their way. When they had got as far the deep-meadowed and

[004-301]rush-grown banks of the Aesopus, the Achaeans sent Tydeus as

[004-302]their envoy, and he found the Cadmeans gathered in great numbers

[004-303]to a banquet in the house of Eteocles. Stranger though he was, he

[004-304]knew no fear on finding himself single-handed among so many, but

[004-305]challenged them to contests of all kinds, and in each one of them

[004-306]was at once victorious, so mightily did Minerva help him. The

[004-307]Cadmeans were incensed at his success, and set a force of fifty

[004-308]youths with two captains—the godlike hero Maeon, son of Haemon,

[004-309]and Polyphontes, son of Autophonus—at their head, to lie in wait

[004-310]for him on his return journey; but Tydeus slew every man of them,

[004-311]save only Maeon, whom he let go in obedience to heaven’s omens.

[004-312]Such was Tydeus of Aetolia. His son can talk more glibly, but he

[004-313]cannot fight as his father did.”

[004-314]Diomed made no answer, for he was shamed by the rebuke of

[004-315]Agamemnon; but the son of Capaneus took up his words and said,

[004-316]“Son of Atreus, tell no lies, for you can speak truth if you

[004-317]will. We boast ourselves as even better men than our fathers; we

[004-318]took seven-gated Thebes, though the wall was stronger and our men

[004-319]were fewer in number, for we trusted in the omens of the gods and

[004-320]in the help of Jove, whereas they perished through their own

[004-321]sheer folly; hold not, then, our fathers in like honour with us.”

[004-322]Diomed looked sternly at him and said, “Hold your peace, my

[004-323]friend, as I bid you. It is not amiss that Agamemnon should urge

[004-324]the Achaeans forward, for the glory will be his if we take the

[004-325]city, and his the shame if we are vanquished. Therefore let us

[004-326]acquit ourselves with valour.”

[004-327]As he spoke he sprang from his chariot, and his armour rang so

[004-328]fiercely about his body that even a brave man might well have

[004-329]been scared to hear it.

[004-330]As when some mighty wave that thunders on the beach when the west

[004-331]wind has lashed it into [[wrath|fury]]—it has reared its head afar and now

[004-332]comes crashing down on the shore; it bows its arching crest high

[004-333]over the jagged rocks and spews its salt foam in all

[004-334]directions—even so did the serried phalanxes of the Danaans march

[004-335]steadfastly to battle. The chiefs gave orders each to his own

[004-336]people, but the men said never a word; no man would think it, for

[004-337]huge as the host was, it seemed as though there was not a tongue

[004-338]among them, so silent were they in their obedience; and as they

[004-339]marched the armour about their bodies glistened in the sun. But

[004-340]the clamour of the Trojan ranks was as that of many thousand ewes

[004-341]that stand waiting to be milked in the yards of some rich

[004-342]flock-master, and bleat incessantly in answer to the bleating of

[004-343]their lambs; for they had not one speech nor language, but their

[004-344]tongues were diverse, and they came from many different places.

[004-345]These were inspired of Mars, but the others by Minerva—and with

[004-346]them came Panic, Rout, and Strife whose fury never tires, sister

[004-347]and friend of murderous Mars, who, from being at first but low in

[004-348]stature, grows till she uprears her head to heaven, though her

[004-349]feet are still on earth. She it was that went about among them

[004-350]and flung down discord to the waxing of [[grief|sorrow]] with even hand

[004-351]between them.

[004-352]When they were got together in one place shield clashed with

[004-353]shield and spear with spear in the rage of battle. The bossed

[004-354]shields beat one upon another, and there was a tramp as of a

[004-355]great multitude—death-cry and shout of triumph of slain and

[004-356]slayers, and the earth ran red with blood. As torrents swollen

[004-357]with rain course madly down their deep channels till the angry

[004-358]floods meet in some gorge, and the shepherd on the hillside hears

[004-361]First Antilochus slew an armed warrior of the Trojans, Echepolus,

[004-362]son of Thalysius, fighting in the foremost ranks. He struck at

[004-363]the projecting part of his helmet and drove the spear into his

[004-364]brow; the point of bronze pierced the bone, and darkness veiled

[004-365]his eyes; headlong as a tower he fell amid the press of the

[004-366]fight, and as he dropped King Elephenor, son of Chalcodon and

[004-367]captain of the proud Abantes began dragging him out of reach of

[004-368]the darts that were falling around him, in haste to strip him of

[004-369]his armour. But his purpose was not for long; Agenor saw him

[004-370]haling the body away, and smote him in the side with his

[004-371]bronze-shod spear—for as he stooped his side was left unprotected

[004-372]by his shield—and thus he perished. Then the fight between

[004-373]Trojans and Achaeans grew furious over his body, and they flew

[004-374]upon each other like wolves, man and man crushing one upon the

[004-375]other.

[004-376]Forthwith Ajax, son of Telamon, slew the fair youth Simoeisius,

[004-377]son of Anthemion, whom his mother bore by the banks of the

[004-378]Simois, as she was coming down from Mt. [[mount-ida|Ida]], where she had been

[004-379]with her parents to see their flocks. Therefore he was named

[004-380]Simoeisius, but he did not live to pay his parents for his

[004-381]rearing, for he was cut off untimely by the spear of mighty Ajax,

[004-382]who struck him in the breast by the right nipple as he was coming

[004-383]on among the foremost fighters; the spear went right through his

[004-384]shoulder, and he fell as a poplar that has grown straight and

[004-385]tall in a meadow by some mere, and its top is thick with

[004-386]branches. Then the wheelwright lays his axe to its roots that he

[004-387]may fashion a felloe for the wheel of some goodly chariot, and it

[004-388]lies seasoning by the waterside. In such wise did Ajax fell to

[004-389]earth Simoeisius, son of Anthemion. Thereon Antiphus of the

[004-390]gleaming corslet, son of Priam, hurled a spear at Ajax from amid

[004-391]the crowd and missed him, but he hit Leucus, the brave comrade of

[004-392]Ulysses, in the groin, as he was dragging the body of Simoeisius

[004-393]over to the other side; so he fell upon the body and loosed his

[004-394]hold upon it. Ulysses was furious when he saw Leucus slain, and

[004-395]strode in full armour through the front ranks till he was quite

[004-396]close; then he glared round about him and took aim, and the

[004-397]Trojans fell back as he did so. His dart was not sped in vain,

[004-398]for it struck Democoon, the bastard son of Priam, who had come to

[004-399]him from Abydos, where he had charge of his father’s mares.

[004-400]Ulysses, infuriated by the death of his comrade, hit him with his

[004-401]spear on one temple, and the bronze point came through on the

[004-402]other side of his forehead. Thereon darkness veiled his eyes, and

[004-403]his armour rang rattling round him as he fell heavily to the

[004-404]ground. [[hector|Hector]], and they that were in front, then gave round

[004-405]while the Argives raised a shout and drew off the dead, pressing

[004-406]further forward as they did so. But Apollo looked down from

[004-407][[troy|Pergamus]] and called aloud to the Trojans, for he was displeased.

[004-408]“Trojans,” he cried, “rush on the foe, and do not let yourselves

[004-409]be thus beaten by the Argives. Their skins are not stone nor iron

[004-410]that when you hit them you do them no harm. Moreover, [[achilles|Achilles]], the

[004-411]son of lovely Thetis, is not fighting, but is nursing his anger

[004-412]at the ships.”

[004-413]Thus spoke the mighty god, crying to them from the city, while

[004-414]Jove’s redoubtable daughter, the Trito-born, went about among the

[004-415]host of the Achaeans, and urged them forward whenever she beheld

[004-416]them slackening.

[004-417]Then fate fell upon Diores, son of Amarynceus, for he was struck

[004-418]by a jagged stone near the ancle of his right leg. He that hurled

[004-419]it was Peirous, son of Imbrasus, captain of the Thracians, who

[004-420]had come from Aenus; the bones and both the tendons were crushed

[004-421]by the pitiless stone. He fell to the ground on his back, and in

[004-422]his death-throes stretched out his hands towards his comrades.

[004-423]But Peirous, who had wounded him, sprang on him and thrust a

[004-424]spear into his belly, so that his bowels came gushing out upon

[004-425]the ground, and darkness veiled his eyes. As he was leaving the

[004-426]body, Thoas of Aetolia struck him in the chest near the nipple,

[004-427]and the point fixed itself in his lungs. Thoas came close up to

[004-428]him, pulled the spear out of his chest, and then drawing his

[004-429]sword, smote him in the middle of the belly so that he died; but

[004-430]he did not strip him of his armour, for his Thracian comrades,

[004-431]men who wear their hair in a tuft at the top of their heads,

[004-432]stood round the body and kept him off with their long spears for

[004-433]all his great stature and valour; so he was driven back. Thus the

[004-434]two corpses lay stretched on earth near to one another, the one

[004-435]captain of the Thracians and the other of the Epeans; and many

[004-436]another fell round them.
