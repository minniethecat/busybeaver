# Guest-Friendship (Xenia)

In the Homeric world, the stranger at the door is under the protection of Zeus himself. *Xenia* — the institution of guest-friendship — is the [[sacrifice|ritual]]ized hospitality that transforms a potentially d[[wrath|anger]]ous encounter between strangers into a sacred bond. To violate *xenia* is to insult Zeus Xenios, the god who watches over guests; to honor it is to create alliances that span generations. The Trojan War itself is, in Homer's telling, a punishment for the violation of guest-friendship: Paris abused Menelaus's hospitality by carrying off [[helen|Helen]].

## Definition

*Xenia* (ξενία) is the reciprocal relationship between host and guest, governed by a set of [[sacrifice|ritual]] expectations. The host offers food, shelter, and protection before asking the guest's identity; the guest accepts what is offered and reciprocates with news, stories, and a future commitment to host in turn. The relationship is hereditary — the children of guest-friends inherit the bond — making *xenia* a mechanism for creating durable inter-familial and even inter-city alliances.

## Manifestations in the Epic

The most famous guest-friendship in the *Iliad* is between Glaucus and [[diomedes|Diomedes]]. Meeting on the battlefield in Book VI, they discover that their grandfathers were guest-friends, and they immediately cease fighting, exchange armor, and [[oath|pledge]] continued friendship — even though they are on opposite sides of the war. This episode demonstrates the power of *xenia* to override even the most intense martial hostility.

The supreme violation of *xenia* is Paris's abduction of [[helen|Helen]] from Menelaus's house. The entire Trojan War is thus framed as punishment for a hospitality crime. This framing gives the Greek cause a divine sanction that transcends the political dispute over a woman.

The poem's closing episode — [[achilles|Achilles]] hosting [[priam|Priam]] in his tent — is a powerful restoration of *xenia*. After slaughtering [[priam|Priam]]'s son and desecrating his body, [[achilles|Achilles]] receives the old king as a guest, offers him food and a bed, and returns [[hector|Hector]]'s body. This act of hospitality between enemies is the moral climax of the epic.

## Greek Terminology

**ξενία (xenia)** — the institution of guest-friendship, encompassing both the relationship and its [[sacrifice|ritual]]s. **ξένος (xenos)** — the guest, the host, the stranger, and the friend — a single word covering all four roles, reflecting their insep[[curse|ara]]bility. **Ζεὺς Ξένιος (Zeus Xenios)** — Zeus in his aspect as protector of guests and strangers. **ἱκέτης (hiketēs)** — a suppliant, one who comes as a [[supplication|petition]]er under divine protection.

## Exemplary Passages

> Let us therefore make exchange of armour, that these men here may know that we are guest-friends from our fathers' days. (Book VI, Glaucus to Diomedes)

> Think of your own father, O Achilles, who is such as I am. And yet I have endured what no other man on earth has endured — to bring to my lips the hand of the man who killed my son. (Book XXIV, Priam to Achilles)

## Contrasts and Tensions

*Xenia* creates a p[[curse|ara]]llel ethical universe alongside the [[heroic-code|warrior code]]. The battlefield demands killing; guest-friendship demands protection. The story of Glaucus and [[diomedes|Diomedes]] shows these two systems colliding and *xenia* prevailing — a rare m[[prophecy|omen]]t when the bonds of peace overcome the bonds of war.

The institution also has a complex relationship with the position of w[[prophecy|omen]]. [[helen|Helen]]'s abduction is framed as a violation of *xenia*, but [[helen|Helen]] herself is treated as property, her own consent invisible. The tension between *xenia*'s noble principles and its patriarchal assumptions is one the epic does not fully resolve.
