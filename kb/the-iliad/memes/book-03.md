# Book III

[003-001]BOOK III.

[003-002]Alexandria, also called Paris, challenges Menelaus—[[helen|Helen]] and

[003-003][[priam|Priam]] view the Achaeans from the wall—The [[oath|covenant]]—Paris and

[003-004]Menelaus fight, and Paris is worsted—Venus carries him off to

[003-005]save him—Scene between him and Helen.

[003-006]When the companies were thus arrayed, each under its own captain,

[003-007]the Trojans advanced as a flight of wild-fowl or cranes that

[003-008]scream overhead when rain and winter drive them over the flowing

[003-009]waters of Oceanus to bring death and destruction on the Pygmies,

[003-010]and they wrangle in the air as they fly; but the Achaeans marched

[003-011]silently, in high heart, and minded to stand by one another.

[003-012]As when the south wind spreads a curtain of mist upon the

[003-013]mountain tops, bad for shepherds but better than night for

[003-014]thieves, and a man can see no further than he can throw a stone,

[003-015]even so rose the dust from under their feet as they made all

[003-016]speed over the plain.

[003-017]When they were close up with one another, [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] came forward

[003-018]as champion on the Trojan side. On his shoulders he bore the skin

[003-019]of a panther, his bow, and his sword, and he brandished two

[003-020]spears shod with bronze as a challenge to the bravest of the

[003-021]Achaeans to meet him in single fight. Menelaus saw him thus

[003-022]stride out before the ranks, and was glad as a hungry lion that

[003-023]lights on the carcase of some goat or horned stag, and devours it

[003-024]there and then, though dogs and youths set upon him. Even thus

[003-025]was Menelaus glad when his eyes caught sight of Alexandrus, for

[003-026]he deemed that now he should be revenged. He sprang, therefore,

[003-027]from his chariot, clad in his suit of armour.

[003-028]Alexandrus quailed as he saw Menelaus come forward, and shrank in

[003-029]fear of his life under cover of his men. As one who starts back

[003-030]affrighted, trembling and pale, when he comes suddenly upon a

[003-031]serpent in some mountain glade, even so did Alexandrus plunge

[003-032]into the throng of Trojan warriors, terror-stricken at the sight

[003-033]of the son of Atreus.

[003-034]Then [[hector|Hector]] upbraided him. “Paris,” said he, “evil-hearted Paris,

[003-035]fair to see, but woman-mad, and false of tongue, would that you

[003-036]had never been born, or that you had died unwed. Better so, than

[003-037]live to be disgraced and looked askance at. Will not the Achaeans

[003-038]mock at us and say that we have sent one to champion us who is

[003-039]fair to see but who has neither wit nor courage? Did you not,

[003-040]such as you are, get your following together and sail beyond the

[003-041]seas? Did you not from your a far country carry off a lovely

[003-042]woman wedded among a people of warriors—to bring [[grief|sorrow]] upon your

[003-043]father, your city, and your whole country, but joy to your

[003-044]enemies, and hang-dog shamefacedness to yourself? And now can you

[003-045]not dare face Menelaus and learn what manner of man he is whose

[003-046]wife you have stolen? Where indeed would be your lyre and your

[003-047]love-tricks, your comely locks and your fair favour, when you

[003-048]were lying in the dust before him? The Trojans are a weak-kneed

[003-049]people, or ere this you would have had a shirt of stones for the

[003-050]wrongs you have done them.”

[003-051]And Alexandrus answered, “Hector, your rebuke is just. You are

[003-052]hard as the axe which a shipwright wields at his work, and

[003-053]cleaves the timber to his liking. As the axe in his hand, so keen

[003-054]is the edge of your scorn. Still, taunt me not with the gifts

[003-055]that golden Venus has given me; they are precious; let not a man

[003-056]disdain them, for the gods give them where they are minded, and

[003-057]none can have them for the asking. If you would have me do battle

[003-058]with Menelaus, bid the Trojans and Achaeans take their seats,

[003-059]while he and I fight in their midst for Helen and all her wealth.

[003-060]Let him who shall be victorious and prove to be the better man

[003-061]take the woman and all she has, to bear them to his home, but let

[003-062]the rest swear to a solemn covenant of peace whereby you Trojans

[003-063]shall stay here in Troy, while the others go home to Argos and

[003-064]the land of the Achaeans.”

## Section 8

[003-065]When Hector heard this he was glad, and went about among the

[003-066]Trojan ranks holding his spear by the middle to keep them back,

[003-067]and they all sat down at his bidding: but the Achaeans still

[003-068]aimed at him with stones and arrows, till [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] shouted to

[003-069]them saying, “Hold, Argives, shoot not, sons of the Achaeans;

[003-070]Hector desires to speak.”

[003-071]They ceased taking aim and were still, whereon Hector spoke.

[003-072]“Hear from my mouth,” said he, “Trojans and Achaeans, the saying

[003-073]of Alexandrus, through whom this quarrel has come about. He bids

[003-074]the Trojans and Achaeans lay their armour upon the ground, while

[003-075]he and Menelaus fight in the midst of you for Helen and all her

[003-076]wealth. Let him who shall be victorious and prove to be the

[003-077]better man take the woman and all she has, to bear them to his

[003-078]own home, but let the rest swear to a solemn covenant of peace.”

[003-079]Thus he spoke, and they all held their peace, till Menelaus of

[003-080]the loud battle-cry addressed them. “And now,” he said, “hear me

[003-081]too, for it is I who am the most aggrieved. I deem that the

[003-082]parting of Achaeans and Trojans is at hand, as well it may be,

[003-083]seeing how much have suffered for my quarrel with Alexandrus and

[003-084]the wrong he did me. Let him who shall die, die, and let the

[003-085]others fight no more. Bring, then, two lambs, a white ram and a

[003-086]black ewe, for Earth and Sun, and we will bring a third for Jove.

[003-087]Moreover, you shall bid Priam come, that he may swear to the

[003-088]covenant himself; for his sons are high-handed and ill to trust,

[003-089]and the oaths of Jove must not be transgressed or taken in vain.

[003-090]Young men’s minds are light as air, but when an old man comes he

[003-091]looks before and after, deeming that which shall be fairest upon

[003-092]both sides.”

[003-093]The Trojans and Achaeans were glad when they heard this, for they

[003-094]thought that they should now have rest. They backed their

[003-095]chariots toward the ranks, got out of them, and put off their

[003-096]armour, laying it down upon the ground; and the hosts were near

[003-097]to one another with a little space between them. Hector sent two

[003-098]messengers to the city to bring the lambs and to bid Priam come,

[003-099]while Agamemnon told Talthybius to fetch the other lamb from the

[003-100][[achaean-camp|ships]], and he did as Agamemnon had said.

[003-101]Meanwhile Iris went to Helen in the form of her sister-in-law,

[003-102]wife of the son of Antenor, for Helicaon, son of Antenor, had

[003-103]married Laodice, the fairest of Priam’s daughters. She found her

[003-104]in her own room, working at a great web of purple linen, on which

[003-105]she was embroidering the battles between Trojans and Achaeans,

[003-106]that Mars had made them fight for her sake. Iris then came close

[003-107]up to her and said, “Come hither, child, and see the strange

[003-108]doings of the Trojans and Achaeans. Till now they have been

[003-109]warring upon the plain, mad with lust of battle, but now they

[003-110]have left off fighting, and are leaning upon their shields,

[003-111]sitting still with their spears planted beside them. Alexandrus

[003-112]and Menelaus are going to fight about yourself, and you are to be

[003-113]the wife of him who is the victor.”

[003-114]Thus spoke the goddess, and Helen’s heart yearned after her

[003-115]former husband, her city, and her parents. She threw a white

[003-116]mantle over her head, and hurried from her room, weeping as she

[003-117]went, not alone, but attended by two of her handmaids, Aethrae,

[003-118]daughter of Pittheus, and Clymene. And straightway they were at

[003-119]the Scaean gates.

[003-120]The two sages, Ucalegon and Antenor, elders of the people, were

[003-121]seated by the Scaean gates, with Priam, Panthous, Thymoetes,

[003-122]Lampus, Clytius, and Hiketaon of the race of Mars. These were too

[003-123]old to fight, but they were fluent orators, and sat on the tower

[003-124]like cicales that chirrup delicately from the boughs of some high

[003-125]tree in a wood. When they saw Helen coming towards the tower,

[003-126]they said softly to one another, “Small wonder that Trojans and

[003-127]Achaeans should endure so much and so long, for the sake of a

[003-128]woman so marvellously and divinely lovely. Still, fair though she

[003-129]be, let them take her and go, or she will breed sorrow for us and

[003-130]for our children after us.”

[003-131]But Priam bade her draw nigh. “My child,” said he, “take your

[003-132]seat in front of me that you may see your former husband, your

[003-133]kinsmen and your friends. I lay no blame upon you, it is the

[003-134]gods, not you who are to blame. It is they that have brought

[003-135]about this terrible war with the Achaeans. Tell me, then, who is

[003-136]yonder huge hero so great and goodly? I have seen men taller by a

[003-137]head, but none so comely and so royal. Surely he must be a king.”

[003-138]“Sir,” answered Helen, “father of my husband, dear and reverend

[003-139]in my eyes, would that I had chosen death rather than to have

[003-140]come here with your son, far from my bridal chamber, my friends,

[003-141]my darling daughter, and all the companions of my girlhood. But

[003-142]it was not to be, and my lot is one of tears and sorrow. As for

[003-143]your question, the hero of whom you ask is Agamemnon, son of

[003-144]Atreus, a good king and a brave soldier, brother-in-law as surely

[003-145]as that he lives, to my abhorred and miserable self.”

[003-146]The old man marvelled at him and said, “Happy son of Atreus,

[003-147]child of good fortune. I see that the Achaeans are subject to you

[003-148]in great multitudes. When I was in Phrygia I saw much horsemen,

[003-149]the people of Otreus and of Mygdon, who were camping upon the

[003-150]banks of the river Sangarius; I was their ally, and with them

[003-151]when the Amazons, peers of men, came up against them, but even

[003-152]they were not so many as the Achaeans.”

[003-153]The old man next looked upon [[odysseus|Ulysses]]; “Tell me,” he said, “who is

[003-154]that other, shorter by a head than Agamemnon, but broader across

[003-155]the chest and shoulders? His armour is laid upon the ground, and

[003-156]he stalks in front of the ranks as it were some great woolly ram

[003-157]ordering his ewes.”

[003-158]And Helen answered, “He is Ulysses, a man of great craft, son of

[003-159]Laertes. He was born in rugged Ithaca, and excels in all manner

[003-160]of stratagems and subtle cunning.”

[003-161]On this Antenor said, “Madam, you have spoken truly. Ulysses once

[003-162]came here as envoy about yourself, and Menelaus with him. I

[003-163]received them in my own house, and therefore know both of them by

[003-164]sight and conversation. When they stood up in presence of the

[003-165]assembled Trojans, Menelaus was the broader shouldered, but when

[003-166]both were seated Ulysses had the more royal presence. After a

[003-167]time they delivered their message, and the speech of Menelaus ran

[003-168]trippingly on the tongue; he did not say much, for he was a man

[003-169]of few words, but he spoke very clearly and to the point, though

[003-170]he was the younger man of the two; Ulysses, on the other hand,

[003-171]when he rose to speak, was at first silent and kept his eyes

[003-172]fixed upon the ground. There was no play nor graceful movement of

[003-173]his sceptre; he kept it straight and stiff like a man unpractised

[003-174]in oratory—one might have taken him for a mere churl or

[003-175]simpleton; but when he raised his voice, and the words came

[003-176]driving from his deep chest like winter snow before the wind,

[003-177]then there was none to touch him, and no man thought further of

[003-178]what he looked like.”

[003-179]Priam then caught sight of Ajax and asked, “Who is that great and

[003-180]goodly warrior whose head and broad shoulders tower above the

[003-181]rest of the Argives?”

[003-182]“That,” answered Helen, “is huge Ajax, bulwark of the Achaeans,

[003-183]and on the other side of him, among the Cretans, stands Idomeneus

[003-184]looking like a god, and with the captains of the Cretans round

[003-185]him. Often did Menelaus receive him as a guest in our house when

[003-186]he came visiting us from Crete. I see, moreover, many other

[003-187]Achaeans whose names I could tell you, but there are two whom I

[003-188]can nowhere find, Castor, breaker of horses, and Pollux the

[003-189]mighty boxer; they are children of my mother, and own brothers to

[003-190]myself. Either they have not left Lacedaemon, or else, though

[003-191]they have brought their ships, they will not show themselves in

[003-192]battle for the shame and [[shame|disgrace]] that I have brought upon them.”

[003-193]She knew not that both these heroes were already lying under the

[003-194]earth in their own land of Lacedaemon.

[003-195]Meanwhile the heralds were bringing the holy oath-offerings

[003-196]through the city—two lambs and a goatskin of wine, the gift of

[003-197]earth; and Idaeus brought the mixing-bowl and the cups of gold.

[003-198]He went up to Priam and said, “Son of Laomedon, the princes of

[003-199]the Trojans and Achaeans bid you come down on to the plain and

[003-200]swear to a solemn covenant. Alexandrus and Menelaus are to fight

[003-201]for Helen in single combat, that she and all her wealth may go

[003-202]with him who is the victor. We are to swear to a solemn covenant

[003-203]of peace whereby we others shall dwell here in Troy, while the

[003-204]Achaeans return to Argos and the land of the Achaeans.”

[003-205]The old man trembled as he heard, but bade his followers yoke the

[003-206]horses, and they made all haste to do so. He mounted the chariot,

[003-207]gathered the reins in his hand, and Antenor took his seat beside

[003-208]him; they then drove through the Scaean gates on to the plain.

[003-209]When they reached the ranks of the Trojans and Achaeans they left

[003-210]the chariot, and with measured pace advanced into the space

[003-211]between the hosts.

[003-212]Agamemnon and Ulysses both rose to meet them. The attendants

[003-213]brought on the oath-offerings and mixed the wine in the

[003-214]mixing-bowls; they poured water over the hands of the chieftains,

[003-215]and the son of Atreus drew the dagger that hung by his sword, and

[003-216]cut wool from the lambs’ heads; this the men-servants gave about

[003-217]among the Trojan and Achaean princes, and the son of Atreus

[003-218]lifted up his hands in prayer. “Father Jove,” he cried, “that

[003-219]rulest in [[mount-ida|Ida]], most glorious in power, and thou oh Sun, that

[003-220]seest and givest ear to all things, Earth and Rivers, and ye who

[003-221]in the realms below chastise the soul of him that has broken his

[003-222]oath, witness these rites and guard them, that they be not vain.

[003-223]If Alexandrus kills Menelaus, let him keep Helen and all her

[003-224]wealth, while we sail home with our ships; but if Menelaus kills

[003-225]Alexandrus, let the Trojans give back Helen and all that she has;

[003-226]let them moreover pay such fine to the Achaeans as shall be

[003-227]agreed upon, in testimony among those that shall be born

[003-228]hereafter. And if Priam and his sons refuse such fine when

[003-229]Alexandrus has fallen, then will I stay here and fight on till I

[003-230]have got satisfaction.”

[003-231]As he spoke he drew his knife across the throats of the victims,

[003-232]and laid them down gasping and dying upon the ground, for the

[003-233]knife had reft them of their strength. Then they poured wine from

[003-234]the mixing-bowl into the cups, and prayed to the everlasting

[003-235]gods, saying, Trojans and Achaeans among one another, “Jove, most

[003-236]great and glorious, and ye other everlasting gods, grant that the

[003-237]brains of them who shall first sin against their oaths—of them

[003-238]and their children—may be shed upon the ground even as this wine,

[003-239]and let their wives become the slaves of strangers.”

[003-240]Thus they prayed, but not as yet would Jove grant them their

[003-241]prayer. Then Priam, descendant of Dardanus, spoke, saying, “Hear

[003-242]me, Trojans and Achaeans, I will now go back to the wind-beaten

[003-243]city of Ilius: I dare not with my own eyes witness this fight

[003-244]between my son and Menelaus, for Jove and the other immortals

[003-245]alone know which shall fall.”

## Section 9

[003-246]On this he laid the two lambs on his chariot and took his seat.

[003-247]He gathered the reins in his hand, and Antenor sat beside him;

[003-248]the two then went back to Ilius. Hector and Ulysses measured the

[003-249]ground, and cast lots from a helmet of bronze to see which should

[003-250]take aim first. Meanwhile the two hosts lifted up their hands and

[003-251]prayed saying, “Father Jove, that rulest from Ida, most glorious

[003-252]in power, grant that he who first brought about this war between

[003-253]us may die, and enter the house of Hades, while we others remain

[003-254]at peace and abide by our oaths.”

[003-255]Great Hector now turned his head aside while he shook the helmet,

[003-256]and the lot of Paris flew out first. The others took their

[003-257]several stations, each by his horses and the place where his arms

[003-258]were lying, while Alexandrus, husband of lovely Helen, put on his

[003-259]goodly armour. First he greaved his legs with greaves of good

[003-260]make and fitted with ancle-clasps of silver; after this he donned

[003-261]the cuirass of his brother Lycaon, and fitted it to his own body;

[003-262]he hung his silver-studded sword of bronze about his shoulders,

[003-263]and then his mighty shield. On his comely head he set his helmet,

[003-264]well wrought, with a crest of horse-hair that nodded menacingly

[003-265]above it, and he grasped a redoubtable spear that suited his

[003-266]hands. In like fashion Menelaus also put on his armour.

[003-267]When they had thus armed, each amid his own people, they strode

[003-268]fierce of aspect into the open space, and both Trojans and

[003-269]Achaeans were struck with awe as they beheld them. They stood

[003-270]near one another on the measured ground, brandishing their

[003-271]spears, and each furious against the other. Alexandrus aimed

[003-272]first, and struck the round shield of the son of Atreus, but the

[003-273]spear did not pierce it, for the shield turned its point.

[003-274]Menelaus next took aim, praying to Father Jove as he did so.

[003-275]“King Jove,” he said, “grant me revenge on Alexandrus who has

[003-276]wronged me; subdue him under my hand that in ages yet to come a

[003-277]man may shrink from doing ill deeds in the house of his [[guest-friendship|host]].”

[003-278]He poised his spear as he spoke, and hurled it at the shield of

[003-279]Alexandrus. Through shield and cuirass it went, and tore the

[003-280]shirt by his flank, but Alexandrus swerved aside, and thus saved

[003-281]his life. Then the son of Atreus drew his sword, and drove at the

[003-282]projecting part of his helmet, but the sword fell shivered in

[003-283]three or four pieces from his hand, and he cried, looking towards

[003-284]Heaven, “Father Jove, of all gods thou art the most despiteful; I

[003-285]made sure of my revenge, but the sword has broken in my hand, my

[003-286]spear has been hurled in vain, and I have not killed him.”

[003-287]With this he flew at Alexandrus, caught him by the horse-hair

[003-288]plume of his helmet, and began dragging him towards the Achaeans.

[003-289]The strap of the helmet that went under his chin was choking him,

[003-290]and Menelaus would have dragged him off to his own great [[honor|glory]]

[003-291]had not Jove’s daughter Venus been quick to mark and to break the

[003-292]strap of ox-hide, so that the empty helmet came away in his hand.

[003-293]This he flung to his comrades among the Achaeans, and was again

[003-294]springing upon Alexandrus to run him through with a spear, but

[003-295]Venus snatched him up in a moment (as a god can do), hid him

[003-296]under a cloud of darkness, and conveyed him to his own

[003-297]bedchamber.

[003-298]Then she went to call Helen, and found her on a high tower with

[003-299]the Trojan women crowding round her. She took the form of an old

[003-300]woman who used to dress wool for her when she was still in

[003-301]Lacedaemon, and of whom she was very fond. Thus disguised she

[003-302]plucked her by perfumed robe and said, “Come hither; Alexandrus

[003-303]says you are to go to the house; he is on his bed in his own

[003-304]room, radiant with beauty and dressed in gorgeous apparel. No one

[003-305]would think he had just come from fighting, but rather that he

[003-306]was going to a dance, or had done dancing and was sitting down.”

[003-307]With these words she moved the heart of Helen to [[wrath|anger]]. When she

[003-308]marked the beautiful neck of the goddess, her lovely bosom, and

[003-309]sparkling eyes, she marvelled at her and said, “Goddess, why do

[003-310]you thus beguile me? Are you going to send me afield still

[003-311]further to some man whom you have taken up in Phrygia or fair

[003-312]Meonia? Menelaus has just vanquished Alexandrus, and is to take

[003-313]my hateful self back with him. You are come here to betray me. Go

[003-314]sit with Alexandrus yourself; henceforth be goddess no longer;

[003-315]never let your feet carry you back to [[mount-olympus|Olympus]]; worry about him

[003-316]and look after him till he make you his wife, or, for the matter

[003-317]of that, his slave—but me? I shall not go; I can garnish his bed

[003-318]no longer; I should be a by-word among all the women of Troy.

[003-319]Besides, I have trouble on my mind.”

[003-320]Venus was very angry, and said, “Bold hussy, do not provoke me;

[003-321]if you do, I shall leave you to your fate and hate you as much as

[003-322]I have loved you. I will stir up fierce hatred between Trojans

[003-323]and Achaeans, and you shall come to a bad end.”

[003-324]At this Helen was frightened. She wrapped her mantle about her

[003-325]and went in silence, following the goddess and unnoticed by the

[003-326]Trojan women.

[003-327]When they came to the house of Alexandrus the maid-servants set

[003-328]about their work, but Helen went into her own room, and the

[003-329]laughter-loving goddess took a seat and set it for her facing

[003-330]Alexandrus. On this Helen, daughter of aegis-bearing Jove, sat

[003-331]down, and with eyes askance began to upbraid her husband.

[003-332]“So you are come from the fight,” said she; “would that you had

[003-333]fallen rather by the hand of that brave man who was my husband.

[003-334]You used to brag that you were a better man with hands and spear

[003-335]than Menelaus. Go, then, and challenge him again—but I should

[003-336]advise you not to do so, for if you are foolish enough to meet

[003-337]him in single combat, you will soon fall by his spear.”

[003-338]And Paris answered, “Wife, do not vex me with your reproaches.

[003-339]This time, with the help of Minerva, Menelaus has vanquished me;

[003-340]another time I may myself be victor, for I too have gods that

[003-341]will stand by me. Come, let us lie down together and make

[003-342]friends. Never yet was I so passionately enamoured of you as at

[003-343]this moment—not even when I first carried you off from Lacedaemon

[003-344]and sailed away with you—not even when I had converse with you

[003-345]upon the couch of love in the island of Cranae was I so

[003-346]enthralled by desire of you as now.” On this he led her towards

[003-347]the bed, and his wife went with him.

[003-348]Thus they laid themselves on the bed together; but the son of

[003-349]Atreus strode among the throng, looking everywhere for

[003-350]Alexandrus, and no man, neither of the Trojans nor of the allies,

[003-351]could find him. If they had seen him they were in no mind to hide

[003-352]him, for they all of them hated him as they did death itself.

[003-353]Then Agamemnon, king of men, spoke, saying, “Hear me, Trojans,

[003-354]Dardanians, and allies. The victory has been with Menelaus;

[003-355]therefore give back Helen with all her wealth, and pay such fine

[003-356]as shall be agreed upon, in testimony among them that shall be

[003-357]born hereafter.”

[003-358]Thus spoke the son of Atreus, and the Achaeans shouted in

[003-359]applause.
