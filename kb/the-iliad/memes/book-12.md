# Book XII

[012-001]BOOK XII.

[012-002]The Trojans and their allies break the wall, led on by [[hector|Hector]].

[012-003]So the son of Menoetius was attending to the hurt of Eurypylus

[012-004]within the tent, but the Argives and Trojans still fought

[012-005]desperately, nor were the trench and the high wall above it, to

[012-006]keep the Trojans in check longer. They had built it to protect

[012-007]their [[achaean-camp|ships]], and had dug the trench all round it that it might

[012-008]safeguard both the ships and the rich spoils which they had

[012-009]taken, but they had not offered hecatombs to the gods. It had

[012-010]been built without the consent of the immortals, and therefore it

[012-011]did not last. So long as Hector lived and [[achilles|Achilles]] nursed his

[012-012][[wrath|anger]], and so long as the city of [[priam|Priam]] remained untaken, the

[012-013]great wall of the Achaeans stood firm; but when the bravest of

[012-014]the Trojans were no more, and many also of the Argives, though

[012-015]some were yet left alive—when, moreover, the city was sacked in

[012-016]the tenth year, and the Argives had gone back with their ships to

[012-017]their own country—then Neptune and Apollo took counsel to destroy

[012-018]the wall, and they turned on to it the streams of all the rivers

[012-019]from [[mount-ida|Mount Ida]] into the sea, Rhesus, Heptaporus, Caresus,

[012-020]Rhodius, Grenicus, Aesopus, and goodly Scamander, with Simois,

[012-021]where many a shield and helm had fallen, and many a hero of the

[012-022]race of demigods had bitten the dust. Phoebus Apollo turned the

[012-023]mouths of all these rivers together and made them flow for nine

[012-024]days against the wall, while Jove rained the whole time that he

[012-025]might wash it sooner into the sea. Neptune himself, trident in

[012-026]hand, surveyed the work and threw into the sea all the

[012-027]foundations of beams and stones which the Achaeans had laid with

[012-028]so much toil; he made all level by the mighty stream of the

[012-029]Hellespont, and then when he had swept the wall away he spread a

[012-030]great beach of sand over the place where it had been. This done

[012-031]he turned the rivers back into their old courses.

[012-032]This was what Neptune and Apollo were to do in after time; but as

[012-033]yet battle and turmoil were still raging round the wall till its

[012-034]timbers rang under the blows that rained upon them. The Argives,

[012-035]cowed by the scourge of Jove, were hemmed in at their ships in

[012-036]fear of Hector the mighty minister of Rout, who as heretofore

[012-037]fought with the force and fury of a whirlwind. As a lion or wild

[012-038]boar turns fiercely on the dogs and men that attack him, while

[012-039]these form a solid wall and shower their javelins as they face

[012-040]him—his courage is all undaunted, but his high spirit will be the

[012-041]death of him; many a time does he charge at his pursuers to

[012-042]scatter them, and they fall back as often as he does so—even so

[012-043]did Hector go about among the [[guest-friendship|host]] exhorting his men, and

[012-044]cheering them on to cross the trench.

[012-045]But the horses dared not do so, and stood neighing upon its

[012-046]brink, for the width frightened them. They could neither jump it

[012-047]nor cross it, for it had overhanging banks all round upon either

[012-048]side, above which there were the sharp stakes that the sons of

[012-049]the Achaeans had planted so close and strong as a defence against

[012-050]all who would assail it; a horse, therefore, could not get into

[012-051]it and draw his chariot after him, but those who were on foot

[012-052]kept trying their very utmost. Then Polydamas went up to Hector

[012-053]and said, “Hector, and you other captains of the Trojans and

[012-054]allies, it is madness for us to try and drive our horses across

[012-055]the trench; it will be very hard to cross, for it is full of

[012-056]sharp stakes, and beyond these there is the wall. Our horses

[012-057]therefore cannot get down into it, and would be of no use if they

[012-058]did; moreover it is a narrow place and we should come to harm.

[012-059]If, indeed, great Jove is minded to help the Trojans, and in his

[012-060]anger will utterly destroy the Achaeans, I would myself gladly

[012-061]see them perish now and here far from Argos; but if they should

[012-062]rally and we are driven back from the ships pell-mell into the

[012-063]trench there will be not so much as a man get back to the city to

[012-064]tell the tale. Now, therefore, let us all do as I say; let our

[012-065]squires hold our horses by the trench, but let us follow Hector

[012-066]in a body on foot, clad in full armour, and if the day of their

[012-067][[fate|doom]] is at hand the Achaeans will not be able to withstand us.”

## Section 33

[012-068]Thus spoke Polydamas and his saying pleased Hector, who sprang in

[012-069]full armour to the ground, and all the other Trojans, when they

[012-070]saw him do so, also left their chariots. Each man then gave his

[012-071]horses over to his charioteer in charge to hold them ready for

[012-072]him at the trench. Then they formed themselves into companies,

[012-073]made themselves ready, and in five bodies followed their leaders.

[012-074]Those that went with Hector and Polydamas were the bravest and

[012-075]most in number, and the most determined to break through the wall

[012-076]and fight at the ships. Cebriones was also joined with them as

[012-077]third in command, for Hector had left his chariot in charge of a

[012-078]less valiant soldier. The next company was led by Paris,

[012-079]Alcathous, and Agenor; the third by Helenus and Deiphobus, two

[012-080]sons of Priam, and with them was the hero Asius—Asius, the son of

[012-081]Hyrtacus, whose great black horses of the breed that comes from

[012-082]the river Selleis had brought him from Arisbe. Aeneas, the

[012-083]valiant son of Anchises, led the fourth; he and the two sons of

[012-084]Antenor, Archelochus and Acamas, men well versed in all the arts

[012-085]of war. Sarpedon was captain over the allies, and took with him

[012-086]Glaucus and Asteropaeus whom he deemed most valiant after

[012-087]himself—for he was far the best man of them all. These helped to

[012-088]array one another in their ox-hide shields, and then charged

[012-089]straight at the Danaans, for they felt sure that they would not

[012-090]hold out longer and that they should themselves now fall upon the

[012-091]ships.

[012-092]The rest of the Trojans and their allies now followed the counsel

[012-093]of Polydamas but Asius, son of Hyrtacus, would not leave his

[012-094]horses and his esquire behind him; in his foolhardiness he took

[012-095]them on with him towards the ships, nor did he fail to come by

[012-096]his end in consequence. Nevermore was he to return to wind-beaten

[012-097]Ilius, exulting in his chariot and his horses; ere he could do

[012-098]so, death of ill-omened name had overshadowed him and he had

[012-099]fallen by the spear of Idomeneus the noble son of Deucalion. He

[012-100]had driven towards the left wing of the ships, by which way the

[012-101]Achaeans used to return with their chariots and horses from the

[012-102]plain. Hither he drove and found the gates with their doors

[012-103]opened wide, and the great bar down—for the gatemen kept them

[012-104]open so as to let those of their comrades enter who might be

[012-105]flying towards the ships. Hither of set purpose did he direct his

[012-106]horses, and his men followed him with a loud cry, for they felt

[012-107]sure that the Achaeans would not hold out longer, and that they

[012-108]should now fall upon the ships. Little did they know that at the

[012-109]gates they should find two of the bravest chieftains, proud sons

[012-110]of the fighting Lapithae—the one, Polypoetes, mighty son of

[012-111]Pirithous, and the other Leonteus, peer of murderous Mars. These

[012-112]stood before the gates like two high oak trees upon the

[012-113]mountains, that tower from their wide-spreading roots, and year

[012-114]after year battle with wind and rain—even so did these two men

[012-115]await the onset of great Asius confidently and without flinching.

[012-116]The Trojans led by him and by Iamenus, Orestes, Adamas the son of

[012-117]Asius, Thoon and Oenomaus, raised a loud cry of battle and made

[012-118]straight for the wall, holding their shields of dry ox-hide above

[012-119]their heads; for a while the two defenders remained inside and

[012-120]cheered the Achaeans on to stand firm in the defence of their

[012-121]ships; when, however, they saw that the Trojans were attacking

[012-122]the wall, while the Danaans were crying out for help and being

[012-123]routed, they rushed outside and fought in front of the gates like

[012-124]two wild boars upon the mountains that abide the attack of men

[012-125]and dogs, and charging on either side break down the wood all

[012-126]round them tearing it up by the roots, and one can hear the

[012-127]clattering of their tusks, till some one hits them and makes an

[012-128]end of them—even so did the gleaming bronze rattle about their

[012-129]breasts, as the weapons fell upon them; for they fought with

[012-130]great fury, trusting to their own [[aristeia|prowess]] and to those who were

[012-131]on the wall above them. These threw great stones at their

[012-132]assailants in defence of themselves their tents and their ships.

[012-133]The stones fell thick as the flakes of snow which some fierce

[012-134]blast drives from the dark clouds and showers down in sheets upon

[012-135]the earth—even so fell the weapons from the hands alike of

[012-136]Trojans and Achaeans. Helmet and shield rang out as the great

[012-137]stones rained upon them, and Asius, the son of Hyrtacus, in his

[012-138]dismay cried aloud and smote his two thighs. “Father Jove,” he

[012-139]cried, “of a truth you too are altogether given to lying. I made

[012-140]sure the Argive heroes could not withstand us, whereas like

[012-141]slim-waisted wasps, or bees that have their nests in the rocks by

[012-142]the wayside—they leave not the holes wherein they have built

[012-143]undefended, but fight for their little ones against all who would

[012-144]take them—even so these men, though they be but two, will not be

[012-145]driven from the gates, but stand firm either to slay or be

[012-146]slain.”

[012-147]He spoke, but moved not the mind of Jove, whose counsel it then

[012-148]was to give [[honor|glory]] to Hector. Meanwhile the rest of the Trojans

[012-149]were fighting about the other gates; I, however, am no god to be

[012-150]able to tell about all these things, for the battle raged

[012-151]everywhere about the stone wall as it were a fiery furnace. The

[012-152]Argives, discomfited though they were, were forced to defend

[012-153]their ships, and all the gods who were defending the Achaeans

[012-154]were vexed in spirit; but the Lapithae kept on fighting with

[012-155]might and main.

[012-156]Thereon Polypoetes, mighty son of Pirithous, hit Damasus with a

[012-157]spear upon his cheek-pierced helmet. The helmet did not protect

[012-158]him, for the point of the spear went through it, and broke the

[012-159]bone, so that the brain inside was scattered about, and he died

[012-160]fighting. He then slew Pylon and Ormenus. Leonteus, of the race

[012-161]of Mars, killed Hippomachus the son of Antimachus by striking him

[012-162]with his spear upon the girdle. He then drew his sword and sprang

[012-163]first upon Antiphates whom he killed in combat, and who fell face

[012-164]upwards on the earth. After him he killed Menon, Iamenus, and

[012-165]Orestes, and laid them low one after the other.

[012-166]While they were busy stripping the armour from these heroes, the

[012-167]youths who were led on by Polydamas and Hector (and these were

[012-168]the greater part and the most valiant of those that were trying

[012-169]to break through the wall and fire the ships) were still standing

[012-170]by the trench, uncertain what they should do; for they had seen a

[012-171][[prophecy|sign]] from heaven when they had essayed to cross it—a soaring

[012-172]eagle that flew skirting the left wing of their host, with a

[012-173]monstrous blood-red snake in its talons still alive and

[012-174]struggling to escape. The snake was still bent on revenge,

[012-175]wriggling and twisting itself backwards till it struck the bird

[012-176]that held it, on the neck and breast; whereon the bird being in

[012-177]pain, let it fall, dropping it into the middle of the host, and

[012-178]then flew down the wind with a sharp cry. The Trojans were struck

[012-179]with terror when they saw the snake, portent of aegis-bearing

[012-180]Jove, writhing in the midst of them, and Polydamas went up to

[012-181]Hector and said, “Hector, at our councils of war you are ever

[012-182]given to rebuke me, even when I speak wisely, as though it were

[012-183]not well, forsooth, that one of the people should cross your will

[012-184]either in the field or at the council board; you would have them

[012-185]support you always: nevertheless I will say what I think will be

[012-186]best; let us not now go on to fight the Danaans at their ships,

[012-187]for I know what will happen if this soaring eagle which skirted

[012-188]the left wing of our host with a monstrous blood-red snake in its

[012-189]talons (the snake being still alive) was really sent as an omen

[012-190]to the Trojans on their essaying to cross the trench. The eagle

[012-191]let go her hold; she did not succeed in taking it home to her

[012-192]little ones, and so will it be—with ourselves; even though by a

[012-193]mighty effort we break through the gates and wall of the

[012-194]Achaeans, and they give way before us, still we shall not return

[012-195]in good order by the way we came, but shall leave many a man

[012-196]behind us whom the Achaeans will do to death in defence of their

[012-197]ships. Thus would any seer who was expert in these matters, and

[012-198]was trusted by the people, read the portent.”

[012-199]Hector looked fiercely at him and said, “Polydamas, I like not of

[012-200]your reading. You can find a better saying than this if you will.

[012-201]If, however, you have spoken in good earnest, then indeed has

[012-202]heaven robbed you of your reason. You would have me pay no heed

[012-203]to the counsels of Jove, nor to the promises he made me—and he

[012-204]bowed his head in confirmation; you bid me be ruled rather by the

[012-205]flight of wild-fowl. What care I whether they fly towards dawn or

[012-206]dark, and whether they be on my right hand or on my left? Let us

[012-207]put our trust rather in the counsel of great Jove, king of

[012-208]mortals and immortals. There is one omen, and one only—that a man

[012-209]should fight for his country. Why are you so fearful? Though we

[012-210]be all of us slain at the ships of the Argives you are not likely

[012-211]to be killed yourself, for you are not steadfast nor courageous.

[012-212]If you will not fight, or would talk others over from doing so,

[012-213]you shall fall forthwith before my spear.”

[012-214]With these words he led the way, and the others followed after

[012-215]with a cry that rent the air. Then Jove the lord of thunder sent

[012-216]the blast of a mighty wind from the mountains of Ida, that bore

[012-217]the dust down towards the ships; he thus lulled the Achaeans into

[012-218]security, and gave victory to Hector and to the Trojans, who,

[012-219]trusting to their own might and to the signs he had shown them,

[012-220]essayed to break through the great wall of the Achaeans. They

[012-221]tore down the breastworks from the walls, and overthrew the

[012-222]battlements; they upheaved the buttresses, which the Achaeans had

[012-223]set in front of the wall in order to support it; when they had

[012-224]pulled these down they made sure of breaking through the wall,

[012-225]but the Danaans still showed no sign of giving ground; they still

[012-226]fenced the battlements with their shields of ox-hide, and hurled

[012-227]their missiles down upon the foe as soon as any came below the

[012-228]wall.

[012-229]The two Ajaxes went about everywhere on the walls cheering on the

[012-230]Achaeans, giving fair words to some while they spoke sharply to

[012-231]any one whom they saw to be remiss. “My friends,” they cried,

[012-232]“Argives one and all—good bad and indifferent, for there was

[012-233]never fight yet, in which all were of equal prowess—there is now

[012-234]work enough, as you very well know, for all of you. See that you

[012-235]none of you turn in flight towards the ships, daunted by the

[012-236]shouting of the foe, but press forward and keep one another in

[012-237]heart, if it may so be that Olympian Jove the lord of lightning

[012-238]will vouchsafe us to repel our foes, and drive them back towards

[012-239]the city.”

[012-240]Thus did the two go about shouting and cheering the Achaeans on.

[012-241]As the flakes that fall thick upon a winter’s day, when Jove is

[012-242]minded to snow and to display these his arrows to mankind—he

[012-243]lulls the wind to rest, and snows hour after hour till he has

[012-244]buried the tops of the high mountains, the headlands that jut

[012-245]into the sea, the grassy plains, and the tilled fields of men;

[012-246]the snow lies deep upon the forelands, and havens of the grey

[012-247]sea, but the waves as they come rolling in stay it that it can

[012-248]come no further, though all else is wrapped as with a mantle, so

[012-249]heavy are the heavens with snow—even thus thickly did the stones

[012-250]fall on one side and on the other, some thrown at the Trojans,

[012-251]and some by the Trojans at the Achaeans; and the whole wall was

[012-252]in an uproar.

## Section 34

[012-253]Still the Trojans and brave Hector would not yet have broken down

[012-254]the gates and the great bar, had not Jove turned his son Sarpedon

[012-255]against the Argives as a lion against a herd of horned cattle.

[012-256]Before him he held his shield of hammered bronze, that the smith

[012-257]had beaten so fair and round, and had lined with ox-hides which

[012-258]he had made fast with rivets of gold all round the shield; this

[012-259]he held in front of him, and brandishing his two spears came on

[012-260]like some lion of the wilderness, who has been long famished for

[012-261]want of meat and will dare break even into a well-fenced

[012-262]homestead to try and get at the sheep. He may find the shepherds

[012-263]keeping watch over their flocks with dogs and spears, but he is

[012-264]in no mind to be driven from the fold till he has had a try for

[012-265]it; he will either spring on a sheep and carry it off, or be hit

[012-266]by a spear from some strong hand—even so was Sarpedon fain to

[012-267]attack the wall and break down its battlements. Then he said to

[012-268]Glaucus son of Hippolochus, “Glaucus, why in Lycia do we receive

[012-269]especial honour as regards our place at table? Why are the

[012-270]choicest portions served us and our cups kept brimming, and why

[012-271]do men look up to us as though we were gods? Moreover we hold a

[012-272]large estate by the banks of the river [[scamander|Xanthus]], fair with orchard

[012-273]lawns and wheat-growing land; it becomes us, therefore, to take

[012-274]our stand at the head of all the Lycians and bear the brunt of

[012-275]the fight, that one may say to another, ‘Our princes in Lycia eat

[012-276]the fat of the land and drink best of wine, but they are fine

[012-277]fellows; they fight well and are ever at the front in battle.’ My

[012-278]good friend, if, when we were once out of this fight, we could

[012-279]escape old age and death thenceforward and forever, I should

[012-280]neither press forward myself nor bid you do so, but death in ten

[012-281]thousand shapes hangs ever over our heads, and no man can elude

[012-282]him; therefore let us go forward and either win glory for

[012-283]ourselves, or yield it to another.”

[012-284]Glaucus heeded his saying, and the pair forthwith led on the host

[012-285]of Lycians. Menestheus son of Peteos was dismayed when he saw

[012-286]them, for it was against his part of the wall that they

[012-287]came—bringing destruction with them; he looked along the wall for

[012-288]some chieftain to support his comrades and saw the two Ajaxes,

[012-289]men ever eager for the fray, and Teucer, who had just come from

[012-290]his tent, standing near them; but he could not make his voice

[012-291]heard by shouting to them, so great an uproar was there from

[012-292]crashing shields and helmets and the battering of gates with a

[012-293]din which reached the skies. For all the gates had been closed,

[012-294]and the Trojans were hammering at them to try and break their way

[012-295]through them. Menestheus, therefore, sent Thootes with a message

[012-296]to Ajax. “Run, good Thootes,” he said, “and call Ajax, or better

[012-297]still bid both come, for it will be all over with us here

[012-298]directly; the leaders of the Lycians are upon us, men who have

[012-299]ever fought desperately heretofore. But if they have too much on

[012-300]their hands to let them come, at any rate let Ajax son of Telamon

[012-301]do so, and let Teucer, the famous bowman, come with him.”

[012-302]The messenger did as he was told, and set off running along the

[012-303]wall of the Achaeans. When he reached the Ajaxes he said to them,

[012-304]“Sirs, princes of the Argives, the son of noble Peteos bids you

[012-305]come to him for a while and help him. You had better both come if

[012-306]you can, or it will be all over with him directly; the leaders of

[012-307]the Lycians are upon him, men who have ever fought desperately

[012-308]heretofore; if you have too much on your hands to let both come,

[012-309]at any rate let Ajax, son of Telamon, do so, and let Teucer, the

[012-310]famous bowman, come with him.”

[012-311][[ajax-the-greater|Great Ajax]] son of Telamon heeded the message, and at once spoke

[012-312]to the son of Oileus. “Ajax,” said he, “do you two, yourself and

[012-313]brave Lycomedes, stay here and keep the Danaans in heart to fight

[012-314]their hardest. I will go over yonder, and bear my part in the

[012-315]fray, but I will come back here at once as soon as I have given

[012-316]them the help they need.”

[012-317]With this, Ajax son of Telamon set off, and Teucer, his brother

[012-318]by the same father, went also, with Pandion to carry Teucer’s

[012-319]bow. They went along inside the wall, and when they came to the

[012-320]tower where Menestheus was (and hard pressed indeed did they find

[012-321]him) the brave captains and leaders of the Lycians were storming

[012-322]the battlements as it were a thick dark cloud, fighting in close

[012-323]quarters, and raising the battle-cry aloud.

[012-324]First, Ajax son of Telamon killed brave Epicles, a comrade of

[012-325]Sarpedon, hitting him with a jagged stone that lay by the

[012-326]battlements at the very top of the wall. As men now are, even one

[012-327]who is in the bloom of youth could hardly lift it with his two

[012-328]hands, but Ajax raised it high aloft and flung it down, smashing

[012-329]Epicles’ four-crested helmet so that the bones of his head were

[012-330]crushed to pieces, and he fell from the high wall as though he

[012-331]were diving, with no more life left in him. Then Teucer wounded

[012-332]Glaucus the brave son of Hippolochus as he was coming on to

[012-333]attack the wall. He saw his shoulder bare and aimed an arrow at

[012-334]it, which made Glaucus leave off fighting. Thereon he sprang

[012-335]covertly down for fear some of the Achaeans might see that he was

[012-336]wounded and taunt him. Sarpedon was stung with grief when he saw

[012-337]Glaucus leave him, still he did not leave off fighting, but aimed

[012-338]his spear at Alcmaon the son of Thestor and hit him. He drew his

[012-339]spear back again and Alcmaon came down headlong after it with his

[012-340]bronzed armour rattling round him. Then Sarpedon seized the

[012-341]battlement in his strong hands, and tugged at it till it all gave

[012-342]way together, and a breach was made through which many might

[012-343]pass.

[012-344]Ajax and Teucer then both of them attacked him. Teucer hit him

[012-345]with an arrow on the band that bore the shield which covered his

[012-346]body, but Jove saved his son from destruction that he might not

[012-347]fall by the ships’ sterns. Meanwhile Ajax sprang on him and

[012-348]pierced his shield, but the spear did not go clean through,

[012-349]though it hustled him back that he could come on no further. He

[012-350]therefore retired a little space from the battlement, yet without

[012-351]losing all his ground, for he still thought to cover himself with

[012-352]glory. Then he turned round and shouted to the brave Lycians

[012-353]saying, “Lycians, why do you thus fail me? For all my prowess I

[012-354]cannot break through the wall and open a way to the ships

[012-355]single-handed. Come close on behind me, for the more there are of

[012-356]us the better.”

[012-357]The Lycians, shamed by his rebuke, pressed closer round him who

[012-358]was their counsellor and their king. The Argives on their part

[012-359]got their men in fighting order within the wall, and there was a

[012-360]deadly struggle between them. The Lycians could not break through
