# Chryses

Chryses is a priest of [[apollo|Apollo]] (Smintheus) whose daughter Chryseis was taken as a prize of war by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] during a Greek raid. When Agamemnon refuses Chryses' supplication and offer of ransom, the priest prays to Apollo, who answers by unleashing a devastating plague upon the Greek camp. This act — a father's plea answered by a god — is the catalyst that triggers the quarrel between Agamemnon and [[achilles|Achilles]], and thus the entire narrative of the Iliad. Chryses himself appears only in the poem's opening lines (Book 1), but his role is structurally fundamental: without Chryses' supplication, there is no plague; without the plague, there is no quarrel; and without the quarrel, there is no *Iliad*. Butler renders him with the title "Chryses, priest of Apollo" (001-134), emphasizing his religious authority.

## Identity

Chryses is a priest of Apollo Smintheus — Apollo in his aspect as the "mouse-god," a bringer of both plague and healing. His city, Chryse, is a coastal settlement near Troy, and his name ("the golden one," from Greek *chrysos*) associates him with wealth and temple treasures. He arrives at the Greek ships bearing the insignia of his office: a golden sceptre wrapped with the sacred woollen fillets of Apollo, the visual symbols of a suppliant's inviolable status.

As a priest, Chryses occupies a special position in Homeric society. Priests are intermediaries between gods and mortals, and mistreating a priest — especially one bearing the symbols of his god — is an offence against the god himself. Agamemnon's rejection of Chryses is therefore not merely cruel but sacrilegious, and the plague that follows is divine retribution for a violation of religious norms.

## Role in the Epic

Chryses' role is concentrated entirely in Book 1, but his actions reverberate through the entire poem.

- **The Supplication:** Chryses approaches the Greek ships "bearing the sacred fillets of Apollo on a golden sceptre" (001-134) and offers "boundless ransom" for his daughter. The assembly of Greeks, led by Agamemnon and Achilles, is moved; they urge Agamemnon to accept. But Agamemnon refuses harshly, threatening the old priest: "Let me not find you, old man, lingering by the ships — the sceptre and fillets of the god will not protect you" (001-134). This refusal is a calculated insult to Apollo himself, and it demonstrates Agamemnon's fundamental flaw: arrogance toward those who deserve reverence.

- **The Prayer:** Driven away, Chryses walks along the shore of the "loud-roaring sea" and prays to Apollo Smintheus (001-192). His prayer is formal and effective: he reminds Apollo of the temple he has built and the sacrifices he has offered, and asks in return that the god "let the Danaans pay for my tears with your arrows." Apollo hears and descends from Olympus, and "the arrows rattled on his shoulders as he went, and his coming was as the night" (001-192). For nine days, Apollo's arrows — the plague — strike the Greek camp.

- **The Return of Chryseis:** After the quarrel between Agamemnon and Achilles forces the issue, the Greeks send Chryseis back to her father under Odysseus' escort. Chryses receives his daughter with joy, and immediately prays again to Apollo — this time to lift the plague (001-199–001-210). He offers a hecatomb (a sacrifice of a hundred oxen), and the Greeks feast and sing paeans to Apollo. Chryses' second prayer is answered as quickly as the first, and the plague ends. The symmetry — prayer for plague, prayer for healing — demonstrates both the power of proper ritual and Apollo's dual nature as bringer of sickness and health.

## Notable Speeches

> "Hear me, god of the silver bow, you who stand over Chryse and holy Cilla and rule Tenedos with your might. If ever I built a temple that pleased you, or burned the fat thigh-pieces of bulls and goats, grant me this prayer: let the Danaans pay for my tears with your arrows."
> — Chryses' prayer to Apollo (001-192)

> "Chryses, King Agamemnon has sent me to bring back your daughter and to offer a hecatomb to Apollo on behalf of the Danaans, that we may appease the god who has dealt so grievously with the Argives."
> — Odysseus delivering Chryseis back to her father (001-199)

## Relationships

- **Apollo (divine patron):** Chryses' relationship with Apollo is the central fact of his existence and the mechanism by which he acts in the plot. He has served Apollo faithfully, and Apollo honours that service. The swiftness of Apollo's response — "Apollo heard his prayer and was angry" (001-192) — establishes the god as a reliable protector of his priests, and Chryses as a man whose piety commands real power.

- **Agamemnon (antagonist):** Agamemnon's treatment of Chryses is the insult that sets the plot in motion. Agamemnon values his own honour above religious obligation, and his harsh words to the old priest — "I will not free her; she shall grow old in my house in Argos, far from her native land" — are the first example of the kind of arrogance (*hybris*) that the Iliad consistently punishes. Chryses never confronts Agamemnon directly again, but Apollo's arrows deliver a more effective rebuke than any words could.

- **Chryseis (daughter):** Chryses' love for his daughter motivates his entire intervention. The poem does not give Chryseis a voice, but the fact that Chryses is willing to risk approaching an armed camp alone, in his old age, speaks to the depth of his devotion. Her return brings him joy, and his immediate turn to prayer — thanksgiving before personal reunion — shows the priority of piety in his character.

## Death or Fate

Chryses survives the Iliad and, after receiving Chryseis back, fades from the narrative. He is a figure of the poem's opening frame — the man whose prayer begins everything — and once the plot he has triggered is in motion, he has no further role. Yet his structural importance cannot be overstated. The Iliad begins not with Achilles or Agamemnon or Hector, but with a father begging for his child. In this sense, Chryses prefigures [[priam|Priam]]'s supplication to Achilles in Book 24 — another old man, another father, another act of desperate love that moves the poem toward its resolution.

## Scholarly Notes

Chryses' prayer to Apollo is one of the earliest and most complete examples of the formal structure of Greek prayer (*euchē*) in literature. The pattern — invocation of the god by name and epithets, reminder of past services rendered, specific request, and promise of future offerings — became the standard template for Greek and Roman prayer texts. The *Iliad* thus opens with a demonstration of how correctly to approach the divine, in implicit contrast to Agamemnon's sacrilegious behaviour.

The epithet "Smintheus" (Σμινθεύς), which Apollo bears as Chryses' patron, means "mouse-god" and is associated with both plague and agriculture. Archaeological evidence from the Troad confirms the existence of a cult of Apollo Smintheus in the region, lending Chryses' city of Chryse a historical plausibility that some Homeric locations lack.

Butler translates Chryses' golden sceptre and the "sacred fillets" (woollen bands) precisely. In ancient Greek religion, suppliants who touched a sacred object were under divine protection, and harming them was an act of sacrilege that the gods themselves punished. Agamemnon's dismissal of Chryses — "the sceptre and fillets of the god will not protect you" — is therefore not mere discourtesy but a direct challenge to the religious norms that sustain Greek society.
