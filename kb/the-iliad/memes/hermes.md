# Hermes

Hermes (called Mercury in Butler's translation) is the son of [[zeus|Zeus]] (Jove) and the nymph Maia, and serves as the messenger of the gods, the guide of travellers, and the conductor of souls to the underworld (*psychopompos*). In the Iliad, his defining role comes in Book 24, when Zeus sends him to escort [[priam|Priam]] safely through the Greek camp to ransom [[hector|Hector]]'s body from [[achilles|Achilles]]. This mission — an act of divine compassion at the poem's close — makes Hermes the agent of the epic's emotional resolution. Butler translates his name as "Mercury" throughout, following the Roman convention, and renders his epithets as "slayer of Argus," "guide and guardian," and "bringer of good luck" — each emphasizing a different aspect of this most versatile of Olympians.

## Identity

Hermes is among the most multifaceted of the Greek gods. He is the divine messenger, carrying Zeus' commands from Olympus to the mortal world; the *psychopompos*, who guides the souls of the dead to Hades; the protector of travellers and herdsmen; the giver of luck and prosperity; and the patron of thieves and tricksters. His epithet "slayer of Argus" (*Argeiphontēs*) refers to his killing of the hundred-eyed giant Argus, who guarded Io at Hera's command — an early demonstration of his cunning.

In the Iliad, Hermes appears most prominently in a single extended sequence (Book 24), but his presence earlier in the poem — particularly in the list of gods in Book 20 — establishes him as a consistent member of the Olympian pantheon. He is among the younger generation of gods, a son of Zeus born to the nymph Maia in a cave on Mount Cyllene in Arcadia.

Unlike the major combatant gods (Athena, Apollo, Ares), Hermes does not take sides in the Trojan War. His role is neutral and transactional: he does what Zeus commands, and his power is one of facilitation rather than force.

- **Book 20 — The Divine Council:** Zeus summons the gods and permits them to join the battle on whichever side they choose. Hermes is listed among the gods present, and while he does not fight, his appearance in the catalogue establishes him as an active Olympian participant in the poem's divine sphere. Butler calls him "Mercury the lusty bringer of good luck" (020-073).

- **Book 24 — The Mission:** Zeus, moved by the continued defilement of Hector's body, summons Hermes and gives him a direct command: "Mercury, for it is you who are the most ready to go wherever we send you, go and tell Priam that he is to ransom his son's body" (024-297). Hermes descends "like a young man in the flower of his youth, with the first down upon his lip" (024-297), and approaches Priam, who is camped with his herald Idaeus on the plain outside Troy.

- **Book 24 — The Guide:** Hermes does not merely deliver the message; he personally escorts Priam's wagon through the Greek camp under cover of darkness. At Hermes' touch, the gates of the Greek fortifications open as if of their own accord (024-403). When Idaeus sees Hermes and grows afraid, Hermes reveals himself: "Fear not, old man; it is I, immortal Mercury, that am come with you, for my father Jove has sent me" (024-406). He guides Priam to Achilles' tent, delivers him safely, and then reveals his true identity before departing: "I am Mercury, the son of Jove, who has come to guide you" (024-412). He returns to Olympus while Priam enters Achilles' presence alone — the divine guide withdrawing at the moment the human encounter must happen on its own terms.

- **Book 24 — The Departure:** After Priam secures Hector's body, Hermes returns to escort him back through the Greek lines and out of danger, yoking the horses and mules himself (024-607). Only when Priam is safe does Hermes depart for Olympus. His final act in the poem is one of pure service — no demand for sacrifice, no assertion of divine power, simply the messenger doing what his father asked.

## Relationships

- **Zeus (father and commander):** Hermes' relationship with Zeus is one of obedient service. When Zeus commands, Hermes acts — and Zeus singles him out as "the most ready to go wherever we send you" (024-297). This suggests both Hermes' particular function as divine messenger and his trusted position in Zeus' court. Unlike many of Zeus' children (Athena, Apollo, Artemis), Hermes never challenges his father's will.

- **Priam (mortal charge):** Hermes' encounter with Priam is the Iliad's most sustained depiction of a god comforting a mortal. Hermes approaches Priam disguised as a young Myrmidon, and when he reveals his divine identity, his words are gentle: "Fear not, old man." The relationship is asymmetrical — god to mortal, young to old — but marked by genuine care. Hermes protects Priam not for any promised reward but because Zeus has commanded it and because Hermes' nature is to guide.

- **Achilles (indirect beneficiary):** Hermes does not interact directly with Achilles, but his mission to Priam creates the conditions for the poem's greatest moment of reconciliation. By bringing Priam safely to Achilles' tent, Hermes enables the encounter in which Achilles returns Hector's body and the two enemies share a meal. Hermes is the facilitator of mercy, not its author.

## Death or Fate

As an immortal god, Hermes cannot die. His Iliadic role ends when he escorts Priam safely back to Troy and returns to Olympus. But his final action — yoking Priam's horses and mules before departing (024-607) — is a gesture of extraordinary humility for a god. The messenger of Zeus, the slayer of Argus, the conductor of souls, performs a stable-boy's task for a mortal king. This final image of Hermes, tending to Priam's animals in the darkness before dawn, is the Iliad's quietest and most humane depiction of a god.

## Scholarly Notes

Hermes (Ἑρμῆς) is one of the most ancient Greek gods, with a name that may derive from *herma* (a stone boundary-marker or cairn), reflecting his role as a god of boundaries and transitions. His function as psychopomp — conductor of souls — connects him to the passage between life and death, and his guidance of Priam in Book 24, which is itself a kind of journey to the land of the dead (Achilles' tent, where Hector's body lies), carries overtones of this darker role.

Butler's use of "Mercury" rather than "Hermes" follows the Roman convention standard in English translations until the twentieth century. The epithet "slayer of Argus" (*Argeiphontēs*) preserves the memory of Hermes' most famous myth — the killing of the hundred-eyed giant — which is referenced elsewhere in the Homeric corpus but not narrated in the Iliad.

Hermes' role in Book 24 is structurally significant as a divine *xenia* (guest-friendship) facilitator. Zeus sends his own son to ensure Priam is received as a guest, not killed as an enemy. The Iliad, which began with the violation of *xenia* (Paris' abduction of Helen while a guest in Menelaus' house), ends with its perfect observance — and Hermes, the god of travellers and boundaries, is the instrument of that restoration.
