# Tripod

The tripod (τρίπους) is one of the most versatile objects in the *Iliad*'s material world: it is a prize, a gift of reconciliation, a bath-vessel, and — in the forge of [[hephaestus|Hephaestus]] — a self-moving automaton. Its appearances span the domestic, the athletic, and the divine, making it an artifact that links the three realms in which the poem operates: the household economy, the heroic contest, and the workshop of the gods.

## Description

Tripods come in two distinct varieties in the *Iliad*: mortal and divine.

| Feature | Mortal Tripod | Divine Tripod (Book 18) |
|---------|--------------|------------------------|
| Material | Bronze | Bronze with gold wheels |
| Mobility | Stationary; carried by men | Self-moving; "runs of its own accord" |
| Function | Prize, gift, cooking/bath vessel | Autonomous attendant at Hephaestus' forge |
| Decoration | Not described | Gold wheels, "that they might enter the [[counsel|assembly]] of the gods and return home again — a wonder to behold" |
| Value | One tripod ≈ several oxen or a skilled slave | Beyond mortal valuation |

The mortal tripod is a three-legged bronze stand de[[prophecy|sign]]ed to hold a cauldron or basin over a fire. The divine tripods are automata — living machines crafted by the god of the forge, able to move without being pushed or carried.

## Origin and Craftsmanship

The distinction between mortal and divine tripods is absolute:

| Type | Maker | Origin |
|------|-------|--------|
| Prize tripods | Unknown Aegean smiths | Bronze-age workshops; Crete, Cyprus, or the Argolid |
| Divine tripods | Hephaestus (018-312) | The Olympian forge; "he was making twenty tripods that were to stand by the wall of his well-built hall" |

Hephaestus is described as actively making tripods when Thetis arrives at his forge in Book 18 — not the Shield of [[achilles|Achilles]], not divine armour, but tripods. The detail humanizes the smith-god even as it reveals his superhuman capability: he makes household objects, but of a quality and animation that no mortal workshop could approach.

## Role in the Epic

Tripods appear at strategic m[[prophecy|omen]]ts in the poem's exchange economy:

- **Book 8 — The Promise of Prizes** (008-249): [[hector|Hector]], rallying the [[trojans|Trojans]], offers a tripod (or two horses) to any man who will venture into the Achaean camp. The tripod here is a military incentive — [[honor|honour]] made material.

- **Book 9 — [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s Offer** (009-108, 009-225, 009-340): Among the lavish gifts [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] offers to [[achilles|Achilles]] are "seven tripods that have never yet been on the fire." The number seven and the emphasis on newness mark this as an extraordinary gesture — not the offhand offer of a single tripod but a princely tribute in multiples. [[achilles|Achilles]]' rejection of these gifts (009-340) is the poem's most dramatic refusal of the entire gift-economy logic.

- **Book 18 — The Forge of Hephaestus** (018-288–018-312): The divine tripods are set on the fire to heat bath-water for Thetis' arrival, then move of their own accord to attend the goddess. Homer lingers on the detail: "he was making twenty tripods that were to stand by the wall of his well-built hall, and he set golden wheels under the base of each, that they might enter the [[counsel|assembly]] of the gods of their own accord and return home again — a wonder to behold" (018-312).

- **Book 23 — The Funeral Games** (023-231, 023-422): Tripods are among the prizes [[achilles|Achilles]] brings from the [[achaean-camp|ships]]. They are awarded alongside cauldrons, horses, and w[[prophecy|omen]] — the standard currency of heroic [[honor|honour]].

## Symbolism

The tripod's dual nature — mortal prize and divine automaton — embodies one of the *Iliad*'s recurring tensions:

- **The Limits of Mortal Craft**: The mortal tripod is stationary; the divine tripod moves. The difference between the two is the difference between human and divine making. Hephaestus' automata prefigure the self-moving servants of later Greek imagination (the golden maidens who attend him in Book 18, Talos the bronze giant).

- **Honour Quantified**: The tripod, like the [[cauldron|Cauldron]], functions as a unit of convertible [[honor|honour]]. But [[achilles|Achilles]]' refusal of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s seven tripods reveals the limit of this logic: when [[honor|honour]] has been wounded beyond a certain point, no quantity of tripods can restore it.

- **The Workshop as Wonder**: Hephaestus making tripods rather than weapons reminds us that the divine smith is not only a fabricator of martial equipment but a maker of a complete world — a world in which even the furniture is alive.

## Notable Passages

The divine tripods are one of Homer's most concentrated images of supernatural craft:

> "He was making twenty tripods that were to stand by the wall of his well-built hall, and he set golden wheels under the base of each, that they might enter the assembly of the gods of their own accord and return home again — a wonder to behold." (018-312)

The wonder is not that the tripods exist but that they *go* — they cross the threshold between object and agent, between the made and the living.
