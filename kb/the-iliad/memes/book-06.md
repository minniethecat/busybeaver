# Book VI

[006-001]BOOK VI.

[006-002]Glaucus and Diomed—The story of Bellerophon—[[hector|Hector]] and

[006-003]Andromache.

[006-004]The fight between Trojans and Achaeans was now left to [[wrath|rage]] as it

[006-005]would, and the tide of war surged hither and thither over the

[006-006]plain as they aimed their bronze-shod spears at one another

[006-007]between the streams of Simois and [[scamander|Xanthus]].

[006-008]First, Ajax son of Telamon, tower of strength to the Achaeans,

[006-009]broke a phalanx of the Trojans, and came to the assistance of his

[006-010]comrades by killing Acamas son of Eussorus, the best man among

[006-011]the Thracians, being both brave and of great stature. The spear

[006-012]struck the projecting peak of his helmet: its bronze point then

[006-013]went through his forehead into the brain, and darkness veiled his

[006-014]eyes.

[006-015]Then Diomed killed Axylus son of Teuthranus, a rich man who lived

[006-016]in the strong city of Arisbe, and was beloved by all men; for he

[006-017]had a house by the roadside, and entertained every one who

[006-018]passed; howbeit not one of his guests stood before him to save

[006-019]his life, and Diomed killed both him and his squire Calesius, who

[006-020]was then his charioteer—so the pair passed beneath the earth.

[006-021]Euryalus killed Dresus and Opheltius, and then went in pursuit of

[006-022]Aesepus and Pedasus, whom the naiad nymph Abarbarea had borne to

[006-023]noble Bucolion. Bucolion was eldest son to Laomedon, but he was a

[006-024]bastard. While tending his sheep he had converse with the nymph,

[006-025]and she conceived twin sons; these the son of Mecisteus now slew,

[006-026]and he stripped the armour from their shoulders. Polypoetes then

[006-027]killed Astyalus, [[odysseus|Ulysses]] Pidytes of Percote, and Teucer Aretaon.

[006-028]Ablerus fell by the spear of [[nestor|Nestor]]’s son Antilochus, and

[006-029][[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], king of men, killed Elatus who dwelt in Pedasus by the

[006-030]banks of the river Satnioeis. Leitus killed Phylacus as he was

[006-031]flying, and Eurypylus slew Melanthus.

[006-032]Then Menelaus of the loud war-cry took Adrestus alive, for his

[006-033]horses ran into a tamarisk bush, as they were flying wildly over

[006-034]the plain, and broke the pole from the car; they went on towards

[006-035]the city along with the others in full flight, but Adrestus

[006-036]rolled out, and fell in the dust flat on his face by the wheel of

[006-037]his chariot; Menelaus came up to him spear in hand, but Adrestus

[006-038]caught him by the knees begging for his life. “Take me alive,” he

[006-039]cried, “son of Atreus, and you shall have a full ransom for me:

[006-040]my father is rich and has much treasure of gold, bronze, and

[006-041]wrought iron laid by in his house. From this store he will give

[006-042]you a large ransom should he hear of my being alive and at the

[006-043][[achaean-camp|ships]] of the Achaeans.”

[006-044]Thus did he plead, and Menelaus was for yielding and giving him

[006-045]to a squire to take to the ships of the Achaeans, but Agamemnon

[006-046]came running up to him and rebuked him. “My good Menelaus,” said

[006-047]he, “this is no time for giving quarter. Has, then, your house

[006-048]fared so well at the hands of the Trojans? Let us not spare a

[006-049]single one of them—not even the child unborn and in its mother’s

[006-050]womb; let not a man of them be left alive, but let all in Ilius

[006-051]perish, unheeded and forgotten.”

[006-052]Thus did he speak, and his brother was persuaded by him, for his

[006-053]words were just. Menelaus, therefore, thrust Adrestus from him,

[006-054]whereon King Agamemnon struck him in the flank, and he fell: then

[006-055]the son of Atreus planted his foot upon his breast to draw his

[006-056]spear from the body.

[006-057]Meanwhile Nestor shouted to the Argives, saying, “My friends,

[006-058]Danaan warriors, servants of Mars, let no man lag that he may

[006-059][[prize|spoil]] the dead, and bring back much booty to the ships. Let us

[006-060]kill as many as we can; the bodies will lie upon the plain, and

[006-061]you can despoil them later at your leisure.”

[006-062]With these words he put heart and soul into them all. And now the

[006-063]Trojans would have been routed and driven back into Ilius, had

[006-064]not [[priam|Priam]]’s son Helenus, wisest of augurs, said to Hector and

[006-065]Aeneas, “Hector and Aeneas, you two are the mainstays of the

[006-066]Trojans and Lycians, for you are foremost at all times, alike in

[006-067]fight and counsel; hold your ground here, and go about among the

[006-068][[guest-friendship|host]] to rally them in front of the gates, or they will fling

[006-069]themselves into the arms of their wives, to the great joy of our

[006-070]foes. Then, when you have put heart into all our companies, we

[006-071]will stand firm here and fight the Danaans however hard they

[006-072]press us, for there is nothing else to be done. Meanwhile do you,

[006-073]Hector, go to the city and tell our mother what is happening.

[006-074]Tell her to bid the matrons gather at the temple of Minerva in

[006-075]the acropolis; let her then take her key and open the doors of

[006-076]the sacred building; there, upon the knees of Minerva, let her

[006-077]lay the largest, fairest robe she has in her house—the one she

[006-078]sets most store by; let her, moreover, promise to sacrifice

[006-079]twelve yearling heifers that have never yet felt the goad, in the

[006-080]temple of the goddess, if she will take pity on the town, with

[006-081]the wives and little ones of the Trojans, and keep the son of

[006-082]Tydeus from falling on the goodly city of Ilius; for he fights

[006-083]with fury and fills men’s souls with panic. I hold him mightiest

[006-084]of them all; we did not fear even their great champion [[achilles|Achilles]],

[006-085]son of a goddess though he be, as we do this man: his rage is

[006-086]beyond all bounds, and there is none can vie with him in

[006-087][[aristeia|prowess]].”

[006-088]Hector did as his brother bade him. He sprang from his chariot,

[006-089]and went about everywhere among the host, brandishing his spears,

[006-090]urging the men on to fight, and raising the dread cry of battle.

[006-091]Thereon they rallied and again faced the Achaeans, who gave

[006-092]ground and ceased their murderous onset, for they deemed that

[006-093]some one of the immortals had come down from starry heaven to

[006-094]help the Trojans, so strangely had they rallied. And Hector

[006-095]shouted to the Trojans, “Trojans and allies, be men, my friends,

[006-096]and fight with might and main, while I go to Ilius and tell the

[006-097]old men of our council and our wives to pray to the gods and [[oath|vow]]

[006-098]hecatombs in their [[honor|honour]].”

[006-099]With this he went his way, and the black rim of hide that went

[006-100]round his shield beat against his neck and his ancles.

[006-101]Then Glaucus son of Hippolochus, and the son of Tydeus went into

[006-102]the open space between the hosts to fight in single combat. When

[006-103]they were close up to one another Diomed of the loud war-cry was

[006-104]the first to speak. “Who, my good sir,” said he, “who are you

[006-105]among men? I have never seen you in battle until now, but you are

[006-106]daring beyond all others if you abide my onset. Woe to those

[006-107]fathers whose sons face my might. If, however, you are one of the

[006-108]immortals and have come down from heaven, I will not fight you;

[006-109]for even valiant Lycurgus, son of Dryas, did not live long when

[006-110]he took to fighting with the gods. He it was that drove the

[006-111]nursing women who were in charge of frenzied Bacchus through the

[006-112]land of Nysa, and they flung their thyrsi on the ground as

[006-113]murderous Lycurgus beat them with his oxgoad. Bacchus himself

[006-114]plunged terror-stricken into the sea, and Thetis took him to her

[006-115]bosom to comfort him, for he was scared by the fury with which

[006-116]the man reviled him. Thereon the gods who live at ease were angry

[006-117]with Lycurgus and the son of Saturn struck him blind, nor did he

[006-118]live much longer after he had become hateful to the immortals.

[006-119]Therefore I will not fight with the blessed gods; but if you are

[006-120]of them that eat the fruit of the ground, draw near and meet your

[006-121][[fate|doom]].”

[006-122]And the son of Hippolochus answered, “Son of Tydeus, why ask me

[006-123]of my lineage? Men come and go as leaves year by year upon the

[006-124]trees. Those of autumn the wind sheds upon the ground, but when

[006-125]spring returns the forest buds forth with fresh vines. Even so is

[006-126]it with the generations of mankind, the new spring up as the old

[006-127]are passing away. If, then, you would learn my descent, it is one

[006-128]that is well known to many. There is a city in the heart of

[006-129]Argos, pasture land of horses, called Ephyra, where Sisyphus

[006-130]lived, who was the craftiest of all mankind. He was the son of

[006-131]Aeolus, and had a son named Glaucus, who was father to

[006-132]Bellerophon, whom heaven endowed with the most surpassing

[006-133]comeliness and beauty. But Proetus devised his ruin, and being

[006-134]stronger than he, drove him from the land of the Argives, over

[006-135]which Jove had made him ruler. For Antea, wife of Proetus, lusted

[006-136]after him, and would have had him lie with her in secret; but

[006-137]Bellerophon was an honourable man and would not, so she told lies

[006-138]about him to Proetus. ‘Proetus,’ said she, ‘kill Bellerophon or

[006-139]die, for he would have had converse with me against my will.’ The

[006-140]king was angered, but shrank from killing Bellerophon, so he sent

[006-141]him to Lycia with lying letters of introduction, written on a

[006-142]folded tablet, and containing much ill against the bearer. He

[006-143]bade Bellerophon show these letters to his father-in-law, to the

[006-144]end that he might thus perish; Bellerophon therefore went to

[006-145]Lycia, and the gods convoyed him safely.

[006-146]“When he reached the river Xanthus, which is in Lycia, the king

[006-147]received him with all goodwill, feasted him nine days, and killed

[006-148]nine heifers in his honour, but when rosy-fingered morning

[006-149]appeared upon the tenth day, he questioned him and desired to see

[006-150]the letter from his son-in-law Proetus. When he had received the

[006-151]wicked letter he first commanded Bellerophon to kill that savage

[006-152]monster, the Chimaera, who was not a human being, but a goddess,

[006-153]for she had the head of a lion and the tail of a serpent, while

[006-154]her body was that of a goat, and she breathed forth flames of

[006-155]fire; but Bellerophon slew her, for he was guided by signs from

[006-156]heaven. He next fought the far-famed Solymi, and this, he said,

[006-157]was the hardest of all his battles. Thirdly, he killed the

[006-158]Amazons, women who were the peers of men, and as he was returning

[006-159]thence the king devised yet another plan for his destruction; he

[006-160]picked the bravest warriors in all Lycia, and placed them in

[006-161]ambuscade, but not a man ever came back, for Bellerophon killed

[006-162]every one of them. Then the king knew that he must be the valiant

[006-163]offspring of a god, so he kept him in Lycia, gave him his

[006-164]daughter in marriage, and made him of equal honour in the kingdom

[006-165]with himself; and the Lycians gave him a piece of land, the best

[006-166]in all the country, fair with vineyards and tilled fields, to

[006-167]have and to hold.

[006-168]“The king’s daughter bore Bellerophon three children, Isander,

[006-169]Hippolochus, and Laodameia. Jove, the lord of counsel, lay with

[006-170]Laodameia, and she bore him noble Sarpedon; but when Bellerophon

[006-171]came to be hated by all the gods, he wandered all desolate and

[006-172]dismayed upon the Alean plain, gnawing at his own heart, and

[006-173]shunning the path of man. Mars, insatiate of battle, killed his

[006-174]son Isander while he was fighting the Solymi; his daughter was

[006-175]killed by Diana of the golden reins, for she was angered with

[006-176]her; but Hippolochus was father to myself, and when he sent me to

[006-177]Troy he urged me again and again to fight ever among the foremost

[006-178]and outvie my peers, so as not to shame the blood of my fathers

[006-179]who were the noblest in Ephyra and in all Lycia. This, then, is

[006-180]the descent I claim.”

[006-181]Thus did he speak, and the heart of Diomed was glad. He planted

[006-182]his spear in the ground, and spoke to him with friendly words.

[006-183]“Then,” he said, “you are an old friend of my father’s house.

[006-184]Great Oeneus once entertained Bellerophon for twenty days, and

[006-185]the two exchanged presents. Oeneus gave a belt rich with purple,

[006-186]and Bellerophon a double cup, which I left at home when I set out

[006-187]for Troy. I do not remember Tydeus, for he was taken from us

[006-188]while I was yet a child, when the army of the Achaeans was cut to

[006-189]pieces before Thebes. Henceforth, however, I must be your host in

[006-190]middle Argos, and you mine in Lycia, if I should ever go there;

[006-191]let us avoid one another’s spears even during a general

[006-192]engagement; there are many noble Trojans and allies whom I can

[006-193]kill, if I overtake them and heaven delivers them into my hand;

[006-194]so again with yourself, there are many Achaeans whose lives you

[006-195]may take if you can; we two, then, will exchange armour, that all

[006-196]present may know of the old ties that subsist between us.”

[006-197]With these words they sprang from their chariots, grasped one

[006-198]another’s hands, and plighted friendship. But the son of Saturn

[006-199]made Glaucus take leave of his wits, for he exchanged golden

[006-200]armour for bronze, the worth of a hundred head of cattle for the

[006-201]worth of nine.

[006-202]Now when Hector reached the Scaean gates and the oak tree, the

[006-203]wives and daughters of the Trojans came running towards him to

[006-204]ask after their sons, brothers, kinsmen, and husbands: he told

[006-205]them to set about praying to the gods, and many were made

[006-206]sorrowful as they heard him.

[006-207]Presently he reached the splendid palace of King Priam, adorned

[006-208]with colonnades of hewn stone. In it there were fifty

[006-209]bedchambers—all of hewn stone—built near one another, where the

[006-210]sons of Priam slept, each with his wedded wife. Opposite these,

[006-211]on the other side the courtyard, there were twelve upper rooms

[006-212]also of hewn stone for Priam’s daughters, built near one another,

[006-213]where his sons-in-law slept with their wives. When Hector got

[006-214]there, his fond mother came up to him with Laodice the fairest of

[006-215]her daughters. She took his hand within her own and said, “My

[006-216]son, why have you left the battle to come hither? Are the

[006-217]Achaeans, woe betide them, pressing you hard about the city that

[006-218]you have thought fit to come and uplift your hands to Jove from

[006-219]the citadel? Wait till I can bring you wine that you may make

[006-220][[sacrifice|offering]] to Jove and to the other immortals, and may then drink

[006-221]and be refreshed. Wine gives a man fresh strength when he is

[006-222]wearied, as you now are with fighting on behalf of your kinsmen.”

[006-223]And Hector answered, “Honoured mother, bring no wine, lest you

[006-224]unman me and I forget my strength. I dare not make a

[006-225]drink-offering to Jove with unwashed hands; one who is

[006-226]bespattered with blood and filth may not pray to the son of

[006-227]Saturn. Get the matrons together, and go with offerings to the

[006-228]temple of Minerva driver of the spoil; there, upon the knees of

[006-229]Minerva, lay the largest and fairest robe you have in your

[006-230]house—the one you set most store by; promise, moreover, to

[006-231]sacrifice twelve yearling heifers that have never yet felt the

[006-232]goad, in the temple of the goddess if she will take pity on the

[006-233]town, with the wives and little ones of the Trojans, and keep the

[006-234]son of Tydeus from off the goodly city of Ilius, for he fights

[006-235]with fury, and fills men’s souls with panic. Go, then, to the

[006-236]temple of Minerva, while I seek Paris and exhort him, if he will

[006-237]hear my words. Would that the earth might open her jaws and

[006-238]swallow him, for Jove bred him to be the bane of the Trojans, and

[006-239]of Priam and Priam’s sons. Could I but see him go down into the

[006-240]house of Hades, my heart would forget its heaviness.”

[006-241]His mother went into the house and called her waiting-women who

[006-242]gathered the matrons throughout the city. She then went down into

[006-243]her fragrant store-room, where her embroidered robes were kept,

[006-244]the work of Sidonian women, whom [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] had brought over from

[006-245]Sidon when he sailed the seas upon that voyage during which he

[006-246]carried off [[helen|Helen]]. Hecuba took out the largest robe, and the one

[006-247]that was most beautifully enriched with embroidery, as an

[006-248]offering to Minerva: it glittered like a star, and lay at the

[006-249]very bottom of the chest. With this she went on her way and many

[006-250]matrons with her.

[006-251]When they reached the temple of Minerva, lovely Theano, daughter

[006-252]of Cisseus and wife of Antenor, opened the doors, for the Trojans

[006-253]had made her priestess of Minerva. The women lifted up their

[006-254]hands to the goddess with a loud cry, and Theano took the robe to

[006-255]lay it upon the knees of Minerva, praying the while to the

[006-256]daughter of great Jove. “Holy Minerva,” she cried, “protectress

[006-257]of our city, mighty goddess, break the spear of Diomed and lay

[006-258]him low before the Scaean gates. Do this, and we will sacrifice

[006-259]twelve heifers that have never yet known the goad, in your

[006-260]temple, if you will have pity upon the town, with the wives and

[006-261]little ones of the Trojans.” Thus she prayed, but Pallas Minerva

[006-262]granted not her prayer.

[006-263]While they were thus praying to the daughter of great Jove,

[006-264]Hector went to the fair house of Alexandrus, which he had built

[006-265]for him by the foremost builders in the land. They had built him

[006-266]his house, storehouse, and courtyard near those of Priam and

[006-267]Hector on the acropolis. Here Hector entered, with a spear eleven

[006-268]cubits long in his hand; the bronze point gleamed in front of

[006-269]him, and was fastened to the shaft of the spear by a ring of

[006-270]gold. He found Alexandrus within the house, busied about his

[006-271]armour, his shield and cuirass, and handling his curved bow;

[006-272]there, too, sat Argive Helen with her women, setting them their

[006-273]several tasks; and as Hector saw him he rebuked him with words of

[006-274]scorn. “Sir,” said he, “you do ill to nurse this rancour; the

[006-275]people perish fighting round this our town; you would yourself

[006-276]chide one whom you saw shirking his part in the combat. Up then,

[006-277]or ere long the city will be in a blaze.”

[006-278]And Alexandrus answered, “Hector, your rebuke is just; listen

[006-279]therefore, and believe me when I tell you that I am not here so

[006-280]much through rancour or ill-will towards the Trojans, as from a

[006-281]desire to indulge my grief. My wife was even now gently urging me

[006-282]to battle, and I hold it better that I should go, for victory is

[006-283]ever fickle. Wait, then, while I put on my armour, or go first

[006-284]and I will follow. I shall be sure to overtake you.”

[006-285]Hector made no answer, but Helen tried to soothe him. “Brother,”

[006-286]said she, “to my abhorred and sinful self, would that a whirlwind

[006-287]had caught me up on the day my mother brought me forth, and had

[006-288]borne me to some mountain or to the waves of the roaring sea that

[006-289]should have swept me away ere this mischief had come about. But,

[006-290]since the gods have devised these evils, would, at any rate, that

[006-291]I had been wife to a better man—to one who could smart under

[006-292][[shame|dishonour]] and men’s evil speeches. This fellow was never yet to

[006-293]be depended upon, nor never will be, and he will surely reap what

[006-294]he has sown. Still, brother, come in and rest upon this seat, for

[006-295]it is you who bear the brunt of that toil that has been caused by

[006-296]my hateful self and by the sin of Alexandrus—both of whom Jove

[006-297]has doomed to be a theme of song among those that shall be born

[006-298]hereafter.”

[006-299]And Hector answered, “Bid me not be seated, Helen, for all the

[006-300]goodwill you bear me. I cannot stay. I am in haste to help the

[006-301]Trojans, who miss me greatly when I am not among them; but urge

[006-302]your husband, and of his own self also let him make haste to

[006-303]overtake me before I am out of the city. I must go home to see my

[006-304]household, my wife and my little son, for I know not whether I

[006-305]shall ever again return to them, or whether the gods will cause

[006-306]me to fall by the hands of the Achaeans.”

[006-307]Then Hector left her, and forthwith was at his own house. He did

[006-308]not find Andromache, for she was on the wall with her child and

[006-309]one of her maids, weeping bitterly. Seeing, then, that she was

[006-310]not within, he stood on the threshold of the women’s rooms and

[006-311]said, “Women, tell me, and tell me true, where did Andromache go

[006-312]when she left the house? Was it to my sisters, or to my brothers’

[006-313]wives? or is she at the temple of Minerva where the other women

[006-314]are propitiating the awful goddess?”

[006-315]His good housekeeper answered, “Hector, since you bid me tell you

[006-316]truly, she did not go to your sisters nor to your brothers’

[006-317]wives, nor yet to the temple of Minerva, where the other women

[006-318]are propitiating the awful goddess, but she is on the high wall

[006-319]of Ilius, for she had heard the Trojans were being hard pressed,

[006-320]and that the Achaeans were in great force: she went to the wall

[006-321]in frenzied haste, and the nurse went with her carrying the

[006-322]child.”

[006-323]Hector hurried from the house when she had done speaking, and

[006-324]went down the streets by the same way that he had come. When he

[006-325]had gone through the city and had reached the Scaean gates

[006-326]through which he would go out on to the plain, his wife came

[006-327]running towards him, Andromache, daughter of great Eetion who

[006-328]ruled in Thebe under the wooded slopes of Mt. Placus, and was

[006-329]king of the Cilicians. His daughter had married Hector, and now

[006-330]came to meet him with a nurse who carried his little child in her

[006-331]bosom—a mere babe. Hector’s darling son, and lovely as a star.

[006-332]Hector had named him Scamandrius, but the people called him

[006-333]Astyanax, for his father stood alone as chief guardian of Ilius.

[006-334]Hector smiled as he looked upon the boy, but he did not speak,

[006-335]and Andromache stood by him weeping and taking his hand in her

[006-336]own. “Dear husband,” said she, “your valour will bring you to

[006-337]destruction; think on your infant son, and on my hapless self who

[006-338]ere long shall be your widow—for the Achaeans will set upon you

[006-339]in a body and kill you. It would be better for me, should I lose

[006-340]you, to lie dead and buried, for I shall have nothing left to

[006-341]comfort me when you are gone, save only [[grief|sorrow]]. I have neither

[006-342]father nor mother now. Achilles slew my father when he sacked

[006-343]Thebe the goodly city of the Cilicians. He slew him, but did not

[006-344]for very shame despoil him; when he had burned him in his

[006-345]wondrous armour, he raised a barrow over his ashes and the

[006-346]mountain nymphs, daughters of aegis-bearing Jove, planted a grove

[006-347]of elms about his tomb. I had seven brothers in my father’s

[006-348]house, but on the same day they all went within the house of

[006-349]Hades. Achilles killed them as they were with their sheep and

[006-350]cattle. My mother—her who had been queen of all the land under

[006-351]Mt. Placus—he brought hither with the spoil, and freed her for a

[006-352]great sum, but the archer-queen Diana took her in the house of

[006-353]your father. Nay—Hector—you who to me are father, mother,

[006-354]brother, and dear husband—have mercy upon me; stay here upon this

[006-355]wall; make not your child fatherless, and your wife a widow; as

## Section 18

[006-362]And Hector answered, “Wife, I too have thought upon all this, but

[006-363]with what face should I look upon the Trojans, men or women, if I

[006-364]shirked battle like a coward? I cannot do so: I know nothing save

[006-365]to fight bravely in the forefront of the Trojan host and win

[006-366][[glory|renown]] alike for my father and myself. Well do I know that the

[006-367]day will surely come when mighty Ilius shall be destroyed with

[006-368]Priam and Priam’s people, but I grieve for none of these—not even

[006-369]for Hecuba, nor King Priam, nor for my brothers many and brave

[006-370]who may fall in the dust before their foes—for none of these do I

[006-371]grieve as for yourself when the day shall come on which some one

[006-372]of the Achaeans shall rob you for ever of your freedom, and bear

[006-373]you weeping away. It may be that you will have to ply the loom in

[006-374]Argos at the bidding of a mistress, or to fetch water from the

[006-375]springs Messeis or Hypereia, treated brutally by some cruel

[006-376]task-master; then will one say who sees you weeping, ‘She was

[006-377]wife to Hector, the bravest warrior among the Trojans during the

[006-378]war before Ilius.’ On this your tears will break forth anew for

[006-379]him who would have put away the day of captivity from you. May I

[006-380]lie dead under the barrow that is heaped over my body ere I hear

[006-381]your cry as they carry you into bondage.”

[006-382]He stretched his arms towards his child, but the boy cried and

[006-383]nestled in his nurse’s bosom, scared at the sight of his father’s

[006-384]armour, and at the horse-hair plume that nodded fiercely from his

[006-385]helmet. His father and mother laughed to see him, but Hector took

[006-386]the helmet from his head and laid it all gleaming upon the

[006-387]ground. Then he took his darling child, kissed him, and dandled

[006-388]him in his arms, praying over him the while to Jove and to all

[006-389]the gods. “Jove,” he cried, “grant that this my child may be even

[006-390]as myself, chief among the Trojans; let him be not less excellent

[006-391]in strength, and let him rule Ilius with his might. Then may one

[006-392]say of him as he comes from battle, ‘The son is far better than

[006-393]the father.’ May he bring back the blood-stained spoils of him

[006-394]whom he has laid low, and let his mother’s heart be glad.”

[006-395]With this he laid the child again in the arms of his wife, who

[006-396]took him to her own soft bosom, smiling through her tears. As her

[006-397]husband watched her his heart yearned towards her and he caressed

[006-398]her fondly, saying, “My own wife, do not take these things too

[006-399]bitterly to heart. No one can hurry me down to Hades before my

[006-400]time, but if a man’s hour is come, be he brave or be he coward,

[006-401]there is no escape for him when he has once been born. Go, then,

[006-402]within the house, and busy yourself with your daily duties, your

[006-403]loom, your distaff, and the ordering of your servants; for war is

[006-404]man’s matter, and mine above all others of them that have been

[006-405]born in Ilius.”

[006-406]He took his plumed helmet from the ground, and his wife went back

[006-407]again to her house, weeping bitterly and often looking back

[006-408]towards him. When she reached her home she found her maidens

[006-409]within, and bade them all join in her lament; so they mourned

[006-410]Hector in his own house though he was yet alive, for they deemed

[006-411]that they should never see him return safe from battle, and from

[006-412]the furious hands of the Achaeans.

[006-413]Paris did not remain long in his house. He donned his goodly

[006-414]armour overlaid with bronze, and hasted through the city as fast

[006-415]as his feet could take him. As a horse, stabled and fed, breaks

[006-416]loose and gallops gloriously over the plain to the place where he

[006-417]is wont to bathe in the fair-flowing river—he holds his head

[006-418]high, and his mane streams upon his shoulders as he exults in his

[006-419]strength and flies like the wind to the haunts and feeding ground

[006-420]of the mares—even so went forth Paris from high [[troy|Pergamus]],

[006-421]gleaming like sunlight in his armour, and he laughed aloud as he

[006-422]sped swiftly on his way. Forthwith he came upon his brother

[006-423]Hector, who was then turning away from the place where he had

[006-424]held converse with his wife, and he was himself the first to

[006-425]speak. “Sir,” said he, “I fear that I have kept you waiting when

[006-426]you are in haste, and have not come as quickly as you bade me.”

[006-427]“My good brother,” answered Hector, “you fight bravely, and no

[006-428]man with any justice can make light of your doings in battle. But

[006-429]you are careless and wilfully remiss. It grieves me to the heart

[006-430]to hear the ill that the Trojans speak about you, for they have

[006-431]suffered much on your account. Let us be going, and we will make

[006-432]things right hereafter, should Jove vouchsafe us to set the cup

[006-433]of our deliverance before ever-living gods of heaven in our own

[006-434]homes, when we have chased the Achaeans from Troy.”
