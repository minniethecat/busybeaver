# Honor (Timē)

The foundational ethical concept of Homeric society — a warrior's publicly recognized worth, measured by the material prizes, deference, and prestige accorded by his peers. In the *Iliad*, the violation of honor drives the entire narrative: [[achilles|Achilles]]' withdrawal stems from [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s insult to his timē (001-062).

## Definition

In the Homeric world, honor is not an internal feeling of pride but an external, social reality. A warrior's timē is his "due share" of the [[prize|spoil]]s of war — the physical proof of his [[aristeia|excellence]] and divine favor. To be dishonored is to have that share unjustly taken, reducing one's standing before gods and men alike. This public dimension distinguishes Homeric honor from modern notions of self-esteem or integrity (001-062).

## Manifestations in the Epic

The *Iliad* opens with a crisis of honor: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s seizure of Briseis from [[achilles|Achilles]]. When [[achilles|Achilles]] declares that [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] has "dishonoured him by taking his prize," he articulates the fundamental warrior grievance — the ruler who should ap[[fate|portion]] honor has instead seized it for himself (001-247). The distribution of honor extends beyond individuals to entire armies; the [[trojans|Trojans]] are said to have won honor by keeping [[helen|Helen]], and the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] seek to restore theirs by sacking Troy (002-129).

Honor also binds the gods. Thetis appeals to Zeus by reminding him that she once saved him from a rebellion of the other Olympians, and asks that he "do honour to my son" by granting the [[trojans|Trojans]] victory during [[achilles|Achilles]]' absence (001-246). This divine economy of honor mirrors and sanctions the human one.

Throughout the battle books, commanders rally their troops by invoking honor — and its opposite, dishonor ([[shame|aidōs]]). "Be men, and fear dishonour," Ajax cries to the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] (015-475). The shame of retreating when comrades fall is a more powerful motivator than the abstract prospect of victory.

The funeral games for [[patroclus|Patroclus]] ([[the-death-of-hector|Book XXII]]I) are the epic's most concentrated pageant of honor distribution: prizes awarded, disputed, and reallocated in a [[sacrifice|ritual]]ized expression of the same competitive dynamics that drive the war (023-542) (023-557) (023-672).

## Greek Terminology

The primary Greek term is **τιμή (timē)** — the honor, value, or price one commands. Its opposite is **ἀτιμία (atimia)** — dishonor, the loss of standing. Closely related is **κῦδος (kudos)** — the glory that the gods grant in battle, a temporary gift of triumphant strength. **κλέος ([[glory|kleos]])** is the immortal [[glory|fame]] that survives a hero's death — honor projected into eternal memory. Butler's translation consistently renders *timē* as "honour" and *atimia* as "dishonour," preserving the centrality of the concept.

## Exemplary Passages

> King Agamemnon has dishonoured him by taking his prize, and keeps her in his own tent. (001-247)

> Think, each one of you, of his own honour and credit. (013-112)

> Their honour shall not be equal while you are still alive. (024-061)

## Contrasts and Tensions

Honor in the *Iliad* exists in constant tension with other values. [[achilles|Achilles]]' pursuit of personal honor leads him to reject [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s embassy [[sacrifice|offering]] material compensation — he values his wounded timē more than the lavish gifts that would normally restore it. [[hector|Hector]] faces the tragic tension between honor on the battlefield and his obligations to his family: to flee [[achilles|Achilles]] would be dishonorable, yet to stand and die abandons Andromache and Astyanax.

The concept also reveals a structural inequality in the heroic world. When [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] tests the army by suggesting they abandon the siege, the rank-and-file rush to the [[achaean-camp|ships]] — they have no personal stake in restoring Menelaus's honor. Honor motivates the *aristoi* (the best men), not the mass of common soldiers who bear the war's cost.
