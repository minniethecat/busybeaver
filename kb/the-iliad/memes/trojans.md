# Trojans

The Trojans (Τρῶες) and their allies are the defending force in the *Iliad*, fighting to preserve their city, their families, and their way of life against the Achaean siege. Unlike the Achaeans — a naval expeditionary force far from home — the Trojans fight with their wives and children watching from the walls, their city's destruction the certain consequence of defeat. This existential stake gives Trojan warfare a desperate urgency that contrasts with the Achaeans' concern for honour and spoils. Under [[hector|Hector]]'s military leadership and [[priam|Priam]]'s kingship, Troy's defenders achieve remarkable success — driving the Achaeans to their ships in the poem's middle books — before the return of [[achilles|Achilles]] makes their destruction inevitable.

## Composition

The Trojan force consists of the Trojans themselves (the inhabitants of Troy under Priam's rule) and a coalition of allies from across Anatolia. The Trojan Catalogue in Book 2 enumerates these contingents:

| Contingent | Leader(s) | Origin | Notable Feature |
|-----------|-----------|--------|----------------|
| Trojans | Hector | Troy itself | The core defending force; Hector is "the one defender" of the city |
| Dardanians | Aeneas, Archelochus, Acamas | The Troad (Dardania) | Aeneas is destined to survive and found the Roman line |
| Lycians | Sarpedon, Glaucus | Southwestern Anatolia | The most distinguished allied contingent; Sarpedon is Zeus's son |
| Paeonians | Pyraechmes | Northern Macedonia/Thrace | Noted for their curved bows |
| Paphlagonians | Pylaemenes | Northern Anatolia (Black Sea coast) | — |
| Thracians | Rhesus (arrives in Book 10) | Thrace | Rhesus's arrival and death form the subject of Book 10 (the Doloneia) |
| Carians, Leleges, Pelasgians | Various | Western and coastal Anatolia | Numerous but individually less distinguished |

The allies speak many languages — Homer describes the Trojan force as a "confused clamour of many tongues," contrasting with the unified Achaean command structure. This multilingual character adds to the sense of Troy as a cosmopolitan, commercially connected city.

## Leadership

The Trojan command structure is more centralized than the Achaean — a monarchy rather than a quarrelsome federation — but also more fragile, because the entire defense depends on Hector:

| Role | Holder | Authority Basis |
|------|--------|----------------|
| King | Priam | Dynastic rule; too old to fight but presides over the city's political life |
| Commander-in-Chief | Hector | Priam's eldest son; the war's burden rests entirely on his shoulders |
| Second in Council | Polydamas | Hector's companion; voice of prudence — often right but overruled |
| Divine Champion | Aeneas | Son of Aphrodite; protected by the gods for a destiny beyond Troy |
| Allied Commander | Sarpedon | King of Lycia, son of Zeus; the most formidable ally |

Hector's death in Book 22 is therefore not just a personal tragedy but a military catastrophe: without him, there is no defense. The city's fall, though outside the poem's frame, is structurally inevitable from the moment Hector dies.

## Role in the Epic

The Trojans serve as more than adversaries. Through them, Homer explores:

- **The Perspective of the Defeated**: The poem's profound sympathy for Troy — most powerfully in Book 6 (Hector and Andromache) and Book 24 (Priam's embassy) — gives the Trojans a humanity that complicates any simple "us vs. them" reading.
- **The Cost of War for the Defending Side**: Trojan deaths are not abstractions. Andromache's anticipation of widowhood and Astyanax's orphaning give the war a human cost measured in shattered families.
- **Fate and Agency**: The Trojans know — through omens, prophecies, and the words of their own counsellors — that they are doomed. They fight anyway. This is the poem's most profound statement about human dignity in the face of fate.

## Notable Passages

> "Hector, you are father and mother to me, and brother, and my young husband also. Stay here, then, and do not make your child an orphan and your wife a widow."
> — Andromache to Hector, Book 6 — the Trojans' war in its most intimate register

> "The day shall come when sacred Ilios shall be laid low, and Priam, and the people of Priam of the strong ash spear."
> — Hector's prophecy of Troy's fall, spoken as he dies, Book 22
