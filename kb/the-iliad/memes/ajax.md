# Ajax (the Greater)

Ajax, son of Telamon, is the bulwark of the Achaeans — the largest and strongest Greek warrior after [[achilles|Achilles]]. He commands the contingent from Salamis and fights with a distinctive tower-like shield of seven ox-hides covered in bronze. Unlike Achilles, whose prowess is linked to speed and fury, Ajax embodies the virtues of endurance and defensive steadfastness. He is the hero the Achaeans turn to when the line must hold: he duels Hector to a standstill in Book 7, defends the ships in Books 13–16, and recovers the body of Patroclus in Book 17. He is one of the few major Greek heroes who fights purely for his comrades — he has no personal quarrel with the Trojans, no divine patronage driving him, and no private agenda beyond duty.

## Identity

Ajax is the son of Telamon, king of Salamis, and through his father's brother Peleus he is the cousin of [[achilles|Achilles]]. He brings twelve ships to Troy (002-450). His physical stature sets him apart: when Priam sees him from the walls, Helen identifies him as "huge Ajax, bulwark of the Achaeans" (003-182). His epithets — "tower of strength" (006-008), "bulwark of the Achaeans" — emphasize his role as the defensive anchor of the Greek army. His shield is unique: constructed of seven layers of ox-hide with a facing of bronze, carried like a tower before him (007-179). It is a symbol of his character — immovable, layered, built for defense rather than speed.

## Role in the Epic

Ajax is the Achaeans' fallback champion whenever Achilles is absent. His arc spans the entire poem, and he is never wounded in combat — a remarkable distinction among the major Greek heroes.

The duel with Hector in Book 7 is Ajax's defining moment. When lots are drawn among the Greek champions, Ajax rejoices at his selection (007-152). He advances bearing his tower-shield, and the duel is evenly matched: Hector's spear penetrates six of the seven ox-hide layers before stopping (007-197); Ajax's return throw pierces Hector's shield entirely. As night falls, the combatants exchange gifts — Hector gives Ajax a silver-studded sword, Ajax gives Hector a purple baldric (007-241, 007-242) — in one of the epic's rare moments of mutual respect between enemies.

In the defense of the ships (Books 15–16), Ajax fights alone when others retreat, his massive shield moving from ship to ship as he holds back the Trojan fire. He is the embodiment of stalwart resistance: immovable, tireless, unbreakable.

## Notable Speeches

Ajax's words are few but weighty, matching his character. Accepting his selection for the duel with Hector:

> When Ajax saw his mark he knew it and was glad.
> — Ajax rejoicing at being chosen to fight Hector (007-152)

As Hector approaches, Ajax speaks with the grim determination of a man who trusts in his shield and spear:

> "Noble Ajax, son of Telamon, captain of the host..."
> — Hector addressing Ajax before their duel (007-186)

Ajax's reply is direct, calling on Hector to learn what manner of champion the Achaeans possess even without Achilles.

## Relationships

**Cousin:** Ajax is the first cousin of [[achilles|Achilles]] through the brotherhood of their fathers Telamon and Peleus. This blood tie places Ajax in the highest tier of the Greek aristocracy, and his status as second-best fighter reflects this kinship.

**Allies:** Ajax is often paired with his namesake Ajax, the son of Oileus (the Lesser Ajax), and the two are referred to as "the two Ajaxes" (002-327, 004-218, 005-437). He fights alongside his brother Teucer, the archer who shelters behind Ajax's shield. He also fights closely with [[menelaus|Menelaus]] in the defense of Patroclus's body.

**Enemies:** His principal adversary is [[hector|Hector]]. Their duel in Book 7 establishes a mutual respect that survives their enmity. The exchange of gifts between them is one of the poem's most celebrated gestures of warrior honor. Ajax also fights the Trojans collectively — he is the embodiment of collective defense against individual heroics.

## Death or Fate

Ajax does not die within the *Iliad*. Post-Homeric tradition, most famously in Sophocles' tragedy *Ajax*, tells that after Achilles' death, Ajax contested with [[odysseus|Odysseus]] for Achilles' divine armor. When the arms were awarded to Odysseus, Ajax was driven mad by Athena and slaughtered a herd of cattle, believing them to be the Greek commanders. On recovering his senses and realizing his dishonor, he fell on his sword — the very sword Hector had given him in Book 7, completing a tragic irony that spans beyond the *Iliad*. Within the epic, however, Ajax remains unbroken and undefeated, a stark contrast to the tragic figure of later tradition.

## Scholarly Notes

Ajax represents a distinct type of Homeric heroism: the hero of endurance rather than offense. Where Achilles is *pódas ōkýs* (swift-footed), Ajax is *pelṓrios* (gigantic). His shield — seven ox-hides thick — is a metaphor for his character: layered, defensive, built to withstand. Samuel Butler's translation renders Ajax's speeches with a bluntness that matches his physical directness; he is not a man of many words, but his actions speak for him.

Scholars have noted the structural symmetry between Ajax and Hector: both are the defensive champions of their respective armies, both fight out of duty rather than passion, and both are contrasted with the more volatile, glory-driven heroes (Achilles and Paris). The exchange of gifts in Book 7 is one of the earliest and most influential examples in Western literature of the "noble enemy" motif — two warriors who, in a different world, would have been allies rather than adversaries.
