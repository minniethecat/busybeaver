## Identity

Hecuba (also called Hecabe) is the queen of Troy, daughter of Dymas of Phrygia by the waters of the river Sangarius, and wife of King Priam (016-638). She is the mother of nineteen of Priam's fifty sons, most notably Hector, as well as numerous daughters (024-658). Her name is synonymous with the sorrow of Troy — she embodies the grief of a mother who witnesses the destruction of her family and city. Hector himself refers to "all my brothers, children of Hecuba and Priam" (022-205), acknowledging her as the matriarch of Troy's royal household.

Hecuba's father Dymas lived "in Phrygia by the waters of the river Sangarius" (016-638), making her a Phrygian princess by birth. Her brother Asius, a Trojan ally who fought and died in the war, shared this lineage (016-638).

## Role in the Epic

Hecuba appears at critical moments throughout the *Iliad*, her role intensifying as the tragedy of Troy deepens. In Book VI, when the Trojan women go to the temple of Athena to pray for the city's deliverance, Hecuba leads the ritual: she "took out the largest robe, and the one that was most beautifully enriched with embroidery" (006-246) as an offering to the goddess — a gesture combining queenly magnificence with maternal desperation.

Her most powerful moments come in the final books of the epic. In Book XXII, upon hearing the news of Hector's death and seeing Priam's grief, "Hecuba then raised the cry of wailing among the Trojans" (022-382). Her lament — "what have I left to live for now that you are no more? Night and day did I glory in you throughout the city, for you were a tower of strength to all in Troy" (022-383) — transforms personal grief into communal mourning. She continues: "both men and women alike hailed you as a god. So long as you lived you were their pride, but now death and destruction have fallen upon you" (022-387).

In Book XXIV, Hecuba plays two vital roles. First, she attempts to dissuade Priam from his embassy to Achilles, her voice the voice of a mother who has lost too many sons already. When Priam announces his intention to ransom Hector's body, "his wife cried aloud as she heard him" (024-178), warning that Achilles "will know neither respect nor pity" (024-184). She speaks with raw anguish: "on whose liver I would fain fasten and devour it" (024-188) — a visceral expression of hatred toward her son's killer. When Priam insists on going, she does not obstruct but instead brings "a golden goblet of wine in her right hand" (024-251) and instructs him to make a drink-offering to Zeus, praying for a favourable omen (024-253).

Finally, at Hector's funeral, Hecuba delivers the central lament. She takes "up the strains of woe" (024-656), addressing her dead son: "dearest to me of all my children. So long as you were alive the gods loved you well, and even in death they have not been utterly unmindful of you" (024-657). She notes that while Achilles sold her other sons across the seas, Hector's body lies "all fresh as dew, and comely as one whom Apollo has slain with his painless shafts" (024-664).

## Notable Speeches

> "Alas, my son, what have I left to live for now that you are no more? Night and day did I glory in you throughout the city, for you were a tower of strength to all in Troy, and both men and women alike hailed you as a god." (022-383)

Hecuba's first lament captures the public dimension of her grief — Hector was not only her son but the city's protector.

> "Alas, what has become of that judgement for which you have been ever famous both among strangers and your own people? How can you go alone to the ships of the Achaeans, and look into the face of him who has slain so many of your brave sons? Surely his heart is of iron; if the cruel savage sees you and lays hold on you, he will know neither respect nor pity." (024-178)

Her warning to Priam is sharp with the wisdom of a mother who sees the danger her husband cannot.

> "Hector, dearest to me of all my children. So long as you were alive the gods loved you well, and even in death they have not been utterly unmindful of you." (024-657)

Her funeral lament blends maternal tenderness with theological reflection — the gods who loved Hector in life have preserved his body in death.

## Relationships

**Priam:** Hecuba's relationship with Priam is one of deep partnership. She counsels him against the embassy (024-178), but when he insists, she supports him with practical piety — bringing wine for the libation. Priam calls her to consult on matters of state: "he called Hecuba his wife" to share the divine message he has received (024-171).

**Hector:** Her bond with Hector is the emotional heart of her role. She calls him "dearest to me of all my children" (024-657) and her laments for him in Books XXII and XXIV are among the most powerful expressions of maternal love in ancient literature. Hector reciprocates this love, naming himself and his brothers as "children of Hecuba and Priam" (022-205).

**Paris (Alexandrus):** Though Hecuba gives birth to Paris, the *Iliad* does not dwell on their relationship directly. The robe she selects for Athena's offering was one that "Alexandrus had brought over from Sidon when he sailed the seas upon that voyage during which he carried off Helen" (006-246) — an ironic detail linking her act of piety to her son's transgression.

**Andromache:** As Hector's wife, Andromache shares Hecuba's grief. The two women's laments frame the epic's closing — Andromache's personal devastation and Hecuba's matriarchal sorrow together form the communal voice of Trojan women's mourning.

**Helen:** Hecuba and Helen's relationship is complex. Helen is both the cause of the war that kills Hecuba's sons and a fellow mourner at Hector's funeral, where Helen delivers the third lament after Hecuba and Andromache (024-668).

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Hecuba's death. Later tradition (the Euripidean *Hecuba* and *Trojan Women*) depicts her as a survivor of Troy's fall, taken as a slave by the Greeks. Within the epic, her fate is tied to the destruction of her city and family. Hector foresees "the day will surely come when mighty Ilius shall be destroyed with Priam and Priam's people" (006-369), a prophecy that encompasses Hecuba's future. The image of Hecuba weeping over Hector's body — her last appearance in the *Iliad* — stands as an emblem of the cost of war borne by mothers.

## Scholarly Notes

Hecuba's name exists in two forms: Hecuba (Latinised) and Hecabe (Greek Ἑκάβη). The *Iliad* uses both. Her parentage varies in ancient sources — Homer names her father as Dymas (016-638), while other traditions make her a daughter of Cisseus or the river god Sangarius. In Homer, her brother Asius is a Trojan ally killed in battle.

Scholars have noted the structural importance of Hecuba's laments. Together with Andromache's and Helen's funeral speeches (Book XXIV), they form a triptych of female grief that closes the epic. Each lament addresses a different relationship to Hector — mother, wife, and sister-in-law — creating a complete portrait of the dead hero through the eyes of mourning women.

Hecuba's warning to Priam (024-178) has been read as an example of female wisdom within the epic. Unlike Priam, who is swayed by the divine messenger Iris, Hecuba judges by experience: she has seen Achilles kill her sons and has no illusions about his capacity for pity. Her judgement is vindicated when Priam's embassy succeeds — but only through divine intervention (Hermes' guidance and Achilles' own divine prompting). Hecuba's realism and Priam's piety form complementary modes of understanding the world Homer depicts.

The epithet often applied to Hecuba and her children is simply "of Priam" — her identity is bound to the royal house. Yet her speeches transcend her role as queen to speak as a mother, giving voice to the suffering that the heroic code both creates and cannot contain.
