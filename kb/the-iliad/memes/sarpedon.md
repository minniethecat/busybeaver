# Sarpedon

Sarpedon is the king of the Lycians, the son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and Laodameia, and the grandson of the great Corinthian hero Bellerophon. He leads the Lycian contingent as the most prominent of Troy's allies and is one of the few mortals in the *Iliad* whose death is mourned by a god before it happens. His death at the hands of [[patroclus|Patroclus]] in Book 16 is one of the epic's most celebrated episodes: Zeus, bound by fate and the counsel of the other gods, must allow his beloved son to die. Sarpedon's nobility, his famous speech on the obligations of kingship, and the pathos of his death make him one of the most memorable secondary characters in the poem.

## Identity

Sarpedon is the son of Zeus by Laodameia, the daughter of Bellerophon (006-170). His lineage is doubly distinguished: on his father's side he is divine, the offspring of the king of the gods; on his mother's side he descends from one of the greatest heroes of the generation before the Trojan War. He rules the Lycians, a people from the southwestern coast of Anatolia, and leads them as Troy's most valued allied contingent. He shares command of the Lycians with his cousin [[glaucus|Glaucus]], the grandson of Bellerophon through a different line. Together they represent the aristocratic ideal of the *hetairos* — the comrade-in-arms bound by shared ancestry, mutual obligation, and the heroic code.

## Role in the Epic

Sarpedon is the moral and military leader of the Trojan allies. His most significant appearances come in Books 5, 12, and 16, with his death forming the emotional core of the middle section of the poem.

Sarpedon's rebuke of Hector in Book 5 is a turning point in the battle. He accuses Hector of shirking the fight and allowing the allies to bear the brunt of the Achaean assault — a charge that stings Hector into renewed action (005-398, 005-415). His duel with Tlepolemus, the son of Heracles, pits two sons of Zeus against each other in a battle that wounds both: Sarpedon kills Tlepolemus but is himself severely wounded and carried from the field (005-548–005-574).

In Book 12, Sarpedon's speech to Glaucus on the obligations of leadership is one of the most famous passages in the *Iliad* — an articulation of the heroic code that binds kings to fight in the front ranks, earning their privilege through courage. He then leads the assault on the Achaean wall, personally tearing down a section of the parapet and opening a breach for the Trojan forces (012-325–012-340).

## Notable Speeches

Sarpedon's speech to Glaucus in Book 12 is one of the defining statements of the Homeric heroic ethos:

> "Glaucus, why are we honoured with the best seats at the banquet, with the finest cuts of meat and the fullest cups of wine, in Lycia? Why do our people look to us as gods? It is so that we may fight in the forefront of the battle and earn the glory that justifies our privilege."
> — Sarpedon to Glaucus on the obligations of kingship (Book 12)

His rebuke of Hector reveals the frustration of the allies who fight for a city not their own:

> So spoke Sarpedon, and Hector smarted under his rebuke.
> — The effect of Sarpedon's words on Hector (005-415)

Before his fatal encounter with Patroclus, Sarpedon speaks with the grim clarity of a warrior who knows his death may be near:

> Sarpedon now took a second aim at Patroclus, but the spear flew wide.
> — Sarpedon's final moments in combat (016-432)

## Relationships

**Zeus:** The father-son relationship between Zeus and Sarpedon is the emotional heart of Sarpedon's story. Zeus loves his son and wishes to save him, but is constrained by fate and the counsel of the gods. His debate with himself — and with Hera — over whether to intervene to save Sarpedon is one of the most intimate depictions of divine emotion in the poem (016-398, 016-407). In the end, Zeus yields to fate, but his grief is manifest: he sends blood-rain to the earth as a sign of mourning, and after Sarpedon's death, dispatches Apollo and the twin gods Sleep and Death to carry his son's body back to Lycia for proper burial.

**Glaucus:** Sarpedon's cousin and co-commander. Their relationship embodies the ideal of aristocratic comradeship — shared lineage, shared command, and shared obligation. Sarpedon's speech to Glaucus in Book 12 is the fullest expression of their bond and the code that binds them.

**Hector:** Sarpedon respects Hector as the foremost Trojan champion but is not afraid to rebuke him when honor demands it (005-398). The tension between native Trojans and their allies is a recurring theme, and Sarpedon speaks for the allies with an authority that reflects his own royal stature.

**Patroclus:** Sarpedon's killer, and in a sense his double — both are beloved companions (Sarpedon to Glaucus, Patroclus to Achilles), both are fated to die in Book 16, and both deaths precipitate the final movement of the epic.

## Death and Fate

Sarpedon's death in Book 16 is the poem's most elaborate treatment of a warrior's end. Patroclus strikes him down in the thick of battle, but the moment is freighted with cosmic significance. Zeus, watching from Olympus, debates whether to save his son or allow fate to take its course. [[hera|Hera]] intervenes to remind him that if he saves Sarpedon, every other god will demand the same for their mortal offspring. Zeus yields — but his grief is tangible. He sends a rain of blood to the earth, a portent visible to both armies (016-398).

After Sarpedon's death, a fierce battle erupts over his body, paralleling the later fight over Patroclus. Zeus eventually commands Apollo to rescue the corpse, and Sleep and Death carry it to Lycia for the funeral rites befitting a king. This apotheosis — a mortal raised by divine hands — is a rare honor in the *Iliad*, granted only to those of exceptional lineage and virtue.

## Scholarly Notes

Sarpedon is one of the most fully realized secondary characters in the *Iliad*. His speech to Glaucus on the obligations of kingship has been quoted and discussed for nearly three millennia as a definitive statement of noblesse oblige — the idea that privilege is justified only by service. Samuel Butler's translation captures the gravity of this speech with restrained eloquence.

The episode of Sarpedon's death also illuminates a central Homeric theme: the tension between divine will and mortal fate. Zeus, the most powerful of the gods, cannot save his own son — a reminder that even the Olympians are subject to forces beyond their control. The image of Sleep and Death carrying Sarpedon's body home to Lycia has been a favorite subject of ancient art, most famously on the Euphronios krater, and remains one of the most resonant images of the heroic afterlife.
