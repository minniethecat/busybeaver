# Golden Armour of Glaucus

The Golden Armour of Glaucus is the centrepiece of one of the *Iliad*'s strangest and most beautiful episodes: the encounter between [[glaucus|Glaucus]] the Lycian and [[diomed|Diomed]] the Achaean in Book 6. When the two champions meet between the armies and discover that their grandfathers were guest-friends, they refuse to fight. Instead, they exchange armour — and Glaucus, Homer tells us, "took leave of his wits," giving away golden armour worth a hundred oxen in return for Diomed's bronze, worth nine. The exchange is at once a gesture of ancestral loyalty, a m[[prophecy|omen]]t of battlefield *[[guest-friendship|xenia]]*, and an economic absurdity — and it is the absurdity that Homer wants us to notice.

## Description

Homer describes the armour only by its material and its value, not by its appearance:

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Material | Gold (χρύσεα) |
| Value | Worth one hundred head of cattle (ἑκατόμβοια) |
| Components | Not specified — presumably a full panoply (cuirass, greaves, helmet) |
| Comparative value | Diomed's bronze armour is worth nine oxen (ἐννεαβοίων) |
| Origin | Lycian craftsmanship; likely an heirloom of Glaucus' royal house |

The armour is emphatically *not* of divine manufacture. It is the product of mortal wealth and craft, but wealth of an extraordinary order. A hundred oxen is a princely sum in the Homeric economy — the kind of value that defines a ruling house. That Glaucus wears such armour into battle marks him as a man of immense inherited wealth, the grandson of Bellerophon and scion of a great Lycian dynasty.

## Origin and Ownership

The armour belongs to the world of Lycian aristocracy. Glaucus is the son of Hippolochus and grandson of Bellerophon, the hero who slew the Chimera (006-131–006-195). The golden armour is likely an heirloom of this lineage — the kind of object that carries not just wealth but genealogical identity.

| Order | Owner | Context |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Glaucus son of Hippolochus | Worn into battle at Troy; a mark of Lycian princely status |
| 2 | Diomed son of Tydeus | Received in exchange after the discovery of ancestral guest-friendship |

The transfer is a gift-exchange that transforms the meaning of both armours. Glaucus' gold passes into Achaean hands; Diomed's bronze passes into Lycian hands. Each man now wears the other's identity into battle.

## Role in the Epic

The encounter occupies a single passage in Book 6, but it is one of the most discussed scenes in the poem:

- **The Meeting** (006-101–006-236): Diomed and Glaucus advance to fight. Diomed, still in the grip of his great *[[battle-fury|aristeia]]*, asks Glaucus to identify himself — not wanting to fight a god. Glaucus' reply is the famous "generation of leaves" speech (006-131): "As the generation of leaves, so is the generation of men." He then traces his lineage back to Bellerophon.

- **The Discovery**: Diomed recognizes the name. Hippolochus, Glaucus' father, had [[guest-friendship|host]]ed Diomed's grandfather Oeneus as a guest-friend. The two men are bound by *[[guest-friendship|xenia]]*, the sacred duty of [[guest-friendship|hospitality]] that passes from father to son. To fight would be to violate an ancestral bond.

- **The Exchange** (006-185–006-200): Diomed drives his spear into the ground and proposes friendship instead of combat, recalling the gifts their grandfathers exchanged. Then comes the famous exchange:

> "But the son of Saturn made Glaucus take leave of his wits, for he exchanged golden armour for bronze, the worth of a hundred head of cattle for the worth of nine." (006-199–006-200)

The dispro[[fate|portion]] is the point. Glaucus gives away the material value of his armour because, in the context of *[[guest-friendship|xenia]]*, value is measured in bonds, not oxen.

## Symbolism

The golden armour encapsulates several of the *Iliad*'s deepest concerns:

- **Ancestral Identity vs. Present Conflict**: The encounter shows that the lines of the Trojan War are not absolute. Men who should be enemies discover that they are, by older and more sacred laws, friends. The *Iliad* does not resolve this tension — it simply presents it, in all its uncomfortable complexity.

- **The Economics of Honour**: Homer draws explicit attention to the economic absurdity of the exchange — gold for bronze, a hundred oxen for nine. But the absurdity only appears if one measures by the wrong scale. In the currency of *[[guest-friendship|xenia]]*, Glaucus' gift is not excessive; it is exactly right. He gives everything because the bond is everything.

- **Divine Madness or Divine Wisdom?**: Homer attributes the exchange to Zeus "taking away Glaucus' wits." But the line is deeply ambiguous. Is Glaucus a fool, or is he performing the only act that makes sense under the older, stronger law of guest-friendship? The poem allows both readings.

- **Armour as Identity**: When Glaucus and Diomed exchange armour, they literally put on each other's identities. For the rest of the war, Diomed fights in Lycian gold and Glaucus in Argive bronze — a permanent, visible reminder that the man you are trying to kill may be the grandson of your grandfather's friend.

## Notable Passages

The exchange is narrated in just a few lines that have generated centuries of commentary:

> "Let us, then, exchange armour, that all may know that we are friends from our fathers' days." — Diomed (006-195)

> "But the son of Saturn made Glaucus take leave of his wits, for he exchanged golden armour for bronze, the worth of a hundred head of cattle for the worth of nine." (006-199–006-200)

The contrast between Diomed's rational proposal and Homer's verdict on Glaucus' state of mind is the interpretive crux: is this the noblest m[[prophecy|omen]]t in the *Iliad*, or the most foolish? The poem refuses to decide.
