# Andromache

Andromache is the daughter of Eetion, king of Cilician Thebe, and the devoted wife of [[hector|Hector]]. Her name means "battle of men" (*andr-* + *machē*) — an irony that runs through her story, for she has lost every man she loved to the violence of war. Achilles killed her father and seven brothers during the sack of Thebe; her mother died of sickness; and now her husband Hector stands between her and utter desolation. Her farewell to Hector at the Scaean Gate in Book 6, and her lament over his body in Book 24, are two of the most powerful expressions of the human cost of war in the entire epic tradition. In Butler's translation, she is "Andromache of the lovely arms" — a physical epithet that underscores the tenderness she represents in a poem otherwise dominated by violence.

## Identity

Andromache is the wife of Hector, Troy's greatest warrior, and the mother of their infant son Astyanax (called Scamandrius by his father but "Astyanax" — "lord of the city" — by the Trojans, because Hector alone defended the city). She is a woman defined entirely by her relationships to the men in her life: daughter of Eetion, wife of Hector, mother of Astyanax. Yet she is not merely a passive figure. Her speech to Hector at the Scaean Gate is strategically informed and emotionally direct, arguing against his recklessness with tactical insight that no other character in the poem offers.

She hails from Cilician Thebe, a city in the Troad that Achilles sacked earlier in the war (006-327). Her father Eetion and her seven brothers were all killed by Achilles in that raid; her mother was ransomed but died soon after. This history makes Andromache's position uniquely vulnerable: she has no family to return to, no protection beyond Hector, and her devotion to her husband is inseparable from her terror of becoming utterly alone.

## Role in the Epic

Andromache appears in three pivotal scenes, each corresponding to a different emotional register: love (Book 6), foreshadowing of disaster (Book 17), and grief (Book 24).

- **Book 6 — The Farewell:** Hector returns to Troy during a lull in the fighting and seeks Andromache. She is not at home; she has rushed to the city wall "like one distracted, with a nurse carrying the child" (006-308), desperate for news of the battle. When Hector finds her at the Scaean Gate, she takes his hand and speaks one of the most famous speeches in the Iliad (006-327). She reminds him that she has no father, no mother, no brothers — all killed by Achilles — and that Hector is "father, mother, brother, and husband" to her. She begs him to defend the city from the wall rather than fighting in the open plain, showing strategic sense. But Hector cannot: he was raised to be a warrior and fears the shame of holding back. The scene climaxes when Hector reaches for his infant son, who screams in terror at the gleaming horsehair crest of his father's helmet (006-335). Hector and Andromache laugh through their tears, and Hector prays that Astyanax may one day be greater than his father. Andromache takes the child back "smiling through her tears" — a phrase (*dakryoen gelasasa*) that has become one of the most quoted lines in Greek literature.

- **Book 17 — The Corn:** In a brief but telling reference, it is noted that Andromache "daughter of great Eetion" had given Hector's horses "honey-sweet corn" (017-165). This small domestic detail grounds the immensity of the war in the everyday care Andromache provides — care that will soon be rendered meaningless by Hector's death.

- **Book 22 — The Unfinished Bath:** In one of the Iliad's most devastating narrative ironies, Andromache is at home weaving a purple double-folded robe and ordering her handmaidens to heat water for Hector's bath when he returns from battle (022-300+). The audience knows — and she does not — that Hector has already been killed by Achilles and his body is being dragged behind the Greek chariot around the walls of Troy. The domestic scene of welcome, so carefully prepared, becomes a monument to everything the war destroys.

- **Book 24 — The Lament:** When Priam returns with Hector's body, Andromache leads the women's ritual lament (024-636). She holds Hector's head in her hands and speaks of the future she and Astyanax now face: "Your father in the grim encounter has not slain you, and you are alive — but what will become of you now that he who was the sole defence of the city and its people has fallen?" (024-636). Her lament is the last great speech of a mortal woman in the poem, and it closes the epic's emotional arc — from wrath to grief, from [[achilles|Achilles]]' rage to Andromache's sorrow.

## Notable Speeches

> "Hector, you are father, mother, brother, and husband to me. Take pity on me now, and stay here upon the wall, lest you make your son an orphan and your wife a widow."
> — Andromache to Hector at the Scaean Gate (006-327)

> "Hector, you are possessed. This bravery of yours will be your end. You have no pity on your helpless child or on me, your ill-starred wife, who will soon be your widow."
> — Andromache's plea (006-327)

> Andromache led the wailing as she clasped the head of Hector in her hands. "Husband," she cried, "you have died young, and left me a widow in your house. Our child is still an infant — he whom we, you and I, unhappy parents, brought into the world."
> — Andromache's lament over Hector's body (024-636)

## Relationships

- **Hector (husband):** Andromache's relationship with Hector is the Iliad's most intimate portrait of marriage. She loves him with a devotion intensified by her total dependence on him; he loves her but cannot give her what she most needs — his survival. Their farewell scene in Book 6 balances tenderness with terrible foreknowledge, and his death in Book 22 makes her the poem's most sympathetic figure of loss.

- **Astyanax (son):** Andromache's infant son is a constant presence in her scenes, a visual reminder of what the war will orphan. When the baby recoils from Hector's helmet, the laughter the parents share — "Hector laughed, and Andromache smiled through her tears" (006-335) — captures the fragility of happiness in a world shaped by violence. After Hector's death, Astyanax will be thrown from the walls of Troy, though this event lies beyond the Iliad's chronological scope.

- **Achilles (destroyer):** Achilles killed Andromache's father and seven brothers during the sack of Thebe, and he will later kill Hector. She never confronts Achilles directly, but his actions define the shape of her life. Her plea to Hector — "stay here upon the wall" — is indirectly a plea against the force that Achilles represents: the unstoppable warrior whose honour demands the death of others.

- **Hecuba and Priam (parents-in-law):** Andromache is integrated into Hector's family, and her lament in Book 24 follows those of Hecuba and Helen. The three women — mother, wife, and the woman who caused the war — together mourn Hector, each from a different relationship to the dead man.

## Death or Fate

Andromache survives the Iliad, but her survival is itself a form of tragedy. She has lost her father, her seven brothers, her mother, and now her husband. Her son Astyanax will be killed when Troy falls. In the post-Homeric tradition, she is taken as a concubine by Neoptolemus, Achilles' son, and later marries Helenus, Hector's brother, founding a new city in Epirus. But within the Iliad itself, Andromache's fate is defined by what she has lost and will lose — she is the poem's most complete witness to the human cost of the heroic code.

## Scholarly Notes

Andromache's name — "battle of men" — has been the subject of extensive scholarly interpretation. It encodes the paradox of her existence: she is a woman defined by the warrior culture that destroys everything she loves. The name may reflect the Homeric convention of women embodying the values of the male world they inhabit, or it may be an older formula whose literal meaning was already remote to Homer's audience.

The farewell scene at the Scaean Gate (Book 6) is one of the most studied passages in the Iliad. Its structure — domestic departure, the child's fear, the prayer for the son — parallels similar scenes in other ancient Near Eastern epic traditions, including the Ugaritic *Epic of Aqhat*. The *dakryoen gelasasa* ("smiling through tears") motif appears nowhere else in Homer with quite this emotional intensity, and it has influenced Western literature from Virgil's *Aeneid* to modern war poetry.

Butler translates Andromache's epithet as "of the lovely arms" (*leukōlenos* in Greek, literally "white-armed"), a formula shared with Hera and other goddesses. The epithet's application to a mortal woman is unusual and emphasizes Andromache's beauty and the tenderness of the domestic sphere she represents.
