## Identity

Paris, also called Alexander (or Alexandrus), is a son of King Priam and Queen Hecuba of Troy. He is defined by a fundamental paradox: his extraordinary physical beauty, which makes him Aphrodite's favourite, is matched by a marked deficiency in martial courage. Where his brother Hector embodies the warrior ideal, Paris embodies its opposite — the man who prefers the bedchamber to the battlefield, whose beauty is a source of trouble rather than glory. The *Iliad* explicitly introduces him with both names: "Alexandrus, also called Paris" (003-002).

Paris's identity was shaped before his birth: Hecuba dreamed she would give birth to a firebrand that would consume Troy. The infant Paris was exposed on Mount Ida to die, but was rescued by shepherds and raised in obscurity. His return to Troy and recognition as a prince set in motion the events that would lead to the war.

## Role in the Epic

Paris's role in the *Iliad* is concentrated in a few pivotal scenes, each of which emphasises the gap between his responsibility for the war and his inability to bear its consequences.

**The Duel with Menelaus (Book 3):** Paris steps forward from the Trojan ranks, gorgeously armed, to challenge the Greeks to single combat (003-017). But when Menelaus accepts, Paris "quailed as he saw Menelaus" (003-028) and "plunged" back into the ranks (003-031). Hector publicly upbraids him: "Paris," said he, "evil-hearted Paris" (003-034). Paris concedes the justice of the rebuke — "Hector, your rebuke is just. You are hard as the axe that a shipwright wields" (003-051) — and agrees to fight. The duel proceeds: Paris and Menelaus are "furious against each other" (003-271), but when Menelaus gains the upper hand, Aphrodite snatches Paris away in a mist and deposits him safely in his perfumed bedchamber.

**In the Bedchamber (Book 3):** Aphrodite compels Helen to join Paris. She looks at him with contempt, but Paris, unashamed, says only, "Wife, do not vex me" (003-338) — a remarkably casual response from a man who has just been rescued from certain death.

**Return to Battle (Book 6):** Hector, having visited his own wife and child, seeks out Paris, who has been lingering at home. Paris "did not remain long in his house" (006-413) and arms himself, going "forth from high Pergamus" (006-420) in gleaming armour, compared to a stallion breaking free from its stall. This scene shows Paris at his most sympathetic — he does eventually fight, and his archery proves effective.

**As Archer (Book 11):** Paris wounds Diomedes with an arrow (011-335); the shot strikes true and "fixed itself in the ground" (011-338). Paris cheers at his success. This episode reinforces Paris's identity as an archer rather than a spearman — the weapon of the less courageous warrior in Homeric culture.

Elsewhere, Paris leads a company of Trojans (012-078), and after speaking in the assembly, takes his seat (007-291). He is a competent leader when not required to face a superior warrior in single combat.

## Notable Speeches

Paris's most significant speech is his response to Hector's rebuke in Book 3. He concedes the justice of Hector's criticism with surprising humility: "Hector, your rebuke is just. You are hard as the axe that a shipwright wields" (003-051). This speech establishes Paris's self-awareness — he knows what he is and what he is not — and his willingness to accept Hector's moral authority.

Yet this self-awareness never translates into reform. His address to Helen in the bedchamber — "Wife, do not vex me" (003-338) — reveals a man uninterested in moral reckoning, content to enjoy the gifts Aphrodite has given him without confronting their cost. The contrast between Paris's eloquence and his shallowness is one of the most subtle character studies in the poem.

## Relationships

**Hector:** The defining relationship in Paris's narrative. Hector is everything Paris is not — brave, dutiful, self-sacrificing — and their interactions are structured around the theme of rebuke and acceptance. Hector's scorn is fierce, but Paris accepts it without resentment, acknowledging his brother's moral superiority.

**Helen:** Their relationship is strangely empty. Helen despises Paris; he desires her but shows no understanding of her feelings. Their marriage is a product of Aphrodite's manipulation rather than mutual affection, and the poem suggests a union hollowed out by the destruction it has caused.

**Aphrodite:** Paris is Aphrodite's favourite, having awarded her the golden apple in the Judgment of Paris. She protects him on the battlefield and compels Helen to return to him. Paris is, in a sense, her creature — his beauty and his cowardice are both manifestations of her domain.

**Menelaus:** Paris's rival, the wronged husband. Their duel dramatises the fundamental asymmetry of the conflict: Menelaus fights for honour and reclamation; Paris fights at Aphrodite's prompting and survives only through her intervention.

**Priam and Hecuba:** Paris is their son, but the poem records no direct, tender exchange between Paris and his parents. Unlike Hector, who is firmly embedded in the web of familial affection, Paris seems oddly isolated within his own family.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Paris's death, but it is a prominent feature of later tradition. According to the cyclic epics, Paris is killed by Philoctetes' bow (the bow of Heracles) during the fall of Troy. In some versions, he is mortally wounded and seeks out his first wife, the nymph Oenone, on Mount Ida — the woman he abandoned for Helen. Oenone, still bitter, refuses to heal him, and he dies. She then, in remorse, throws herself onto his funeral pyre.

This later tradition completes the arc of Paris's character: the beautiful prince who chose Aphrodite's gift over power and wisdom dies alone on the mountain where he was raised, rejected by the woman he wronged before Helen. His death, like his life, is defined by beauty, desire, and the destruction they leave behind.

## Scholarly Notes

Paris is one of the most morally ambiguous figures in the *Iliad*. Scholars have debated whether he is simply a villain or a more complex figure trapped by divine favour. His association with Aphrodite aligns him with the realm of *eros* and beauty, which the epic consistently presents as both alluring and destructive. The contrast between Paris and Hector has been read as a meditation on different models of masculinity — the warrior versus the lover — with the poem showing the catastrophic consequences when the latter is elevated over the former in a time of war.

The dual name (Paris/Alexander) has attracted scholarly attention. "Alexander" may mean "defender of men," an ironic name for a man whose actions brought destruction to his city. The name "Paris" may be of non-Greek origin, possibly Luwian, reflecting the Anatolian setting of Troy. His skill with the bow, while denigrated in the *Iliad*, may reflect an authentic Trojan or Near Eastern tradition of archery that the Greek epic tradition recast as unmanly.
