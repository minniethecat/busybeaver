# Book VII

[007-001]BOOK VII.

[007-002][[hector|Hector]] and Ajax fight—Hector is getting worsted when night comes

[007-003]on and parts them—They exchange presents—The burial of the dead,

[007-004]and the building of a wall round their [[achaean-camp|ships]] by the Achaeans—The

[007-005]Achaeans buy their wine of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and Menelaus.

[007-006]With these words Hector passed through the gates, and his brother

[007-007][[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] with him, both eager for the fray. As when heaven

[007-008]sends a breeze to sailors who have long looked for one in vain,

[007-009]and have laboured at their oars till they are faint with toil,

[007-010]even so welcome was the sight of these two heroes to the Trojans.

[007-011]Thereon Alexandrus killed Menesthius the son of Areithous; he

[007-012]lived in Arne, and was son of Areithous the Mace-man, and of

[007-013]Phylomedusa. Hector threw a spear at Eioneus and struck him dead

[007-014]with a wound in the neck under the bronze rim of his helmet.

[007-015]Glaucus, moreover, son of Hippolochus, captain of the Lycians, in

[007-016]hard hand-to-hand fight smote Iphinous son of Dexius on the

[007-017]shoulder, as he was springing on to his chariot behind his fleet

[007-018]mares; so he fell to earth from the car, and there was no life

[007-019]left in him.

[007-020]When, therefore, Minerva saw these men making havoc of the

[007-021]Argives, she darted down to Ilius from the summits of [[mount-olympus|Olympus]],

[007-022]and Apollo, who was looking on from [[troy|Pergamus]], went out to meet

[007-023]her; for he wanted the Trojans to be victorious. The pair met by

[007-024]the oak tree, and King Apollo son of Jove was first to speak.

[007-025]“What would you have,” said he, “daughter of great Jove, that

[007-026]your proud spirit has sent you hither from Olympus? Have you no

[007-027]pity upon the Trojans, and would you incline the scales of

[007-028]victory in favour of the Danaans? Let me persuade you—for it will

[007-029]be better thus—stay the combat for to-day, but let them renew the

[007-030]fight hereafter till they compass the [[fate|doom]] of Ilius, since you

[007-031]goddesses have made up your minds to destroy the city.”

[007-032]And Minerva answered, “So be it, Far-Darter; it was in this mind

[007-033]that I came down from Olympus to the Trojans and Achaeans. Tell

[007-034]me, then, how do you propose to end this present fighting?”

[007-035]Apollo, son of Jove, replied, “Let us incite great Hector to

[007-036]challenge some one of the Danaans in single combat; on this the

[007-037]Achaeans will be shamed into finding a man who will fight him.”

[007-038]Minerva assented, and Helenus son of [[priam|Priam]] divined the counsel of

[007-039]the gods; he therefore went up to Hector and said, “Hector son of

[007-040]Priam, peer of gods in counsel, I am your brother, let me then

[007-041]persuade you. Bid the other Trojans and Achaeans all of them take

[007-042]their seats, and challenge the best man among the Achaeans to

[007-043]meet you in single combat. I have heard the voice of the

[007-044]ever-living gods, and the hour of your doom is not yet come.”

[007-045]Hector was glad when he heard this saying, and went in among the

[007-046]Trojans, grasping his spear by the middle to hold them back, and

[007-047]they all sat down. Agamemnon also bade the Achaeans be seated.

[007-048]But Minerva and Apollo, in the likeness of vultures, perched on

[007-049]father Jove’s high oak tree, proud of their men; and the ranks

[007-050]sat close ranged together, bristling with shield and helmet and

[007-051]spear. As when the rising west wind furs the face of the sea and

[007-052]the waters grow dark beneath it, so sat the companies of Trojans

[007-053]and Achaeans upon the plain. And Hector spoke thus:—

[007-054]“Hear me, Trojans and Achaeans, that I may speak even as I am

[007-055]minded; Jove on his high throne has brought our oaths and

[007-056]covenants to nothing, and foreshadows ill for both of us, till

[007-057]you either take the towers of Troy, or are yourselves vanquished

[007-058]at your ships. The princes of the Achaeans are here present in

[007-059]the midst of you; let him, then, that will fight me stand forward

[007-060]as your champion against Hector. Thus I say, and may Jove be

[007-061]witness between us. If your champion slay me, let him strip me of

[007-062]my armour and take it to your ships, but let him send my body

[007-063]home that the Trojans and their wives may give me my dues of fire

[007-064]when I am dead. In like manner, if Apollo vouchsafe me [[honor|glory]] and

[007-065]I slay your champion, I will strip him of his armour and take it

[007-066]to the city of Ilius, where I will hang it in the temple of

[007-067]Apollo, but I will give up his body, that the Achaeans may bury

[007-068]him at their ships, and then build him a mound by the wide waters

[007-069]of the Hellespont. Then will one say hereafter as he sails his

[007-070]ship over the sea, ‘This is the monument of one who died long

[007-071]since a champion who was slain by mighty Hector.’ Thus will one

[007-072]say, and my [[glory|fame]] shall not be lost.”

[007-073]Thus did he speak, but they all held their peace, ashamed to

[007-074]decline the challenge, yet fearing to accept it, till at last

[007-075]Menelaus rose and rebuked them, for he was angry. “Alas,” he

[007-076]cried, “vain braggarts, women forsooth not men, double-dyed

[007-077]indeed will be the stain upon us if no man of the Danaans will

[007-078]now face Hector. May you be turned every man of you into earth

[007-079]and water as you sit spiritless and inglorious in your places. I

[007-080]will myself go out against this man, but the upshot of the fight

[007-081]will be from on high in the hands of the immortal gods.”

[007-082]With these words he put on his armour; and then, O Menelaus, your

[007-083]life would have come to an end at the hands of hands of Hector,

[007-084]for he was far better the man, had not the princes of the

[007-085]Achaeans sprung upon you and checked you. King Agamemnon caught

[007-086]him by the right hand and said, “Menelaus, you are mad; a [[oath|truce]]

[007-087]to this folly. Be patient in spite of passion, do not think of

[007-088]fighting a man so much stronger than yourself as Hector son of

[007-089]Priam, who is feared by many another as well as you. Even

[007-090][[achilles|Achilles]], who is far more doughty than you are, shrank from

[007-091]meeting him in battle. Sit down your own people, and the Achaeans

[007-092]will send some other champion to fight Hector; fearless and fond

[007-093]of battle though he be, I ween his knees will bend gladly under

[007-094]him if he comes out alive from the hurly-burly of this fight.”

[007-095]With these words of reasonable counsel he persuaded his brother,

[007-096]whereon his squires gladly stripped the armour from off his

[007-097]shoulders. Then [[nestor|Nestor]] rose and spoke, “Of a truth,” said he,

[007-098]“the Achaean land is fallen upon evil times. The old knight

[007-099]Peleus, counsellor and orator among the Myrmidons, loved when I

[007-100]was in his house to question me concerning the race and lineage

[007-101]of all the Argives. How would it not grieve him could he hear of

[007-102]them as now quailing before Hector? Many a time would he lift his

[007-103]hands in prayer that his soul might leave his body and go down

[007-104]within the house of Hades. Would, by father Jove, Minerva, and

[007-105]Apollo, that I were still young and strong as when the Pylians

[007-106]and Arcadians were gathered in fight by the rapid river Celadon

[007-107]under the walls of Pheia, and round about the waters of the river

[007-108]Iardanus. The godlike hero Ereuthalion stood forward as their

[007-109]champion, with the armour of King Areithous upon his

[007-110]shoulders—Areithous whom men and women had surnamed ‘the

[007-111]Mace-man,’ because he fought neither with bow nor spear, but

[007-112]broke the battalions of the foe with his iron mace. Lycurgus

[007-113]killed him, not in fair fight, but by entrapping him in a narrow

[007-114]way where his mace served him in no stead; for Lycurgus was too

[007-115]quick for him and speared him through the middle, so he fell to

[007-116]earth on his back. Lycurgus then spoiled him of the armour which

[007-117]Mars had given him, and bore it in battle thenceforward; but when

[007-118]he grew old and stayed at home, he gave it to his faithful squire

[007-119]Ereuthalion, who in this same armour challenged the foremost men

[007-120]among us. The others quaked and quailed, but my high spirit bade

[007-121]me fight him though none other would venture; I was the youngest

[007-122]man of them all; but when I fought him Minerva vouchsafed me

[007-123]victory. He was the biggest and strongest man that ever I killed,

[007-124]and covered much ground as he lay sprawling upon the earth. Would

[007-125]that I were still young and strong as I then was, for the son of

[007-126]Priam would then soon find one who would face him. But you,

[007-127]foremost among the whole [[guest-friendship|host]] though you be, have none of you any

[007-128]stomach for fighting Hector.”

## Section 19

[007-129]Thus did the old man rebuke them, and forthwith nine men started

[007-130]to their feet. Foremost of all uprose King Agamemnon, and after

[007-131]him brave Diomed the son of Tydeus. Next were the two Ajaxes, men

[007-132]clothed in valour as with a garment, and then Idomeneus, and

[007-133]Meriones his brother in arms. After these Eurypylus son of

[007-134]Euaemon, Thoas the son of Andraemon, and [[odysseus|Ulysses]] also rose. Then

[007-135]Nestor knight of Gerene again spoke, saying: “Cast lots among you

[007-136]to see who shall be chosen. If he come alive out of this fight he

[007-137]will have done good service alike to his own soul and to the

[007-138]Achaeans.”

[007-139]Thus he spoke, and when each of them had marked his lot, and had

[007-140]thrown it into the helmet of Agamemnon son of Atreus, the people

[007-141]lifted their hands in prayer, and thus would one of them say as

[007-142]he looked into the vault of heaven, “Father Jove, grant that the

[007-143]lot fall on Ajax, or on the son of Tydeus, or upon the king of

[007-144]rich Mycene himself.”

[007-145]As they were speaking, Nestor knight of Gerene shook the helmet,

[007-146]and from it there fell the very lot which they wanted—the lot of

[007-147]Ajax. The herald bore it about and showed it to all the

[007-148]chieftains of the Achaeans, going from left to right; but they

[007-149]none of them owned it. When, however, in due course he reached

[007-150]the man who had written upon it and had put it into the helmet,

[007-151]brave Ajax held out his hand, and the herald gave him the lot.

[007-152]When Ajax saw his mark he knew it and was glad; he threw it to

[007-153]the ground and said, “My friends, the lot is mine, and I rejoice,

[007-154]for I shall vanquish Hector. I will put on my armour; meanwhile,

[007-155]pray to King Jove in silence among yourselves that the Trojans

[007-156]may not hear you—or aloud if you will, for we fear no man. None

[007-157]shall overcome me, neither by force nor cunning, for I was born

[007-158]and bred in Salamis, and can hold my own in all things.”

[007-159]With this they fell praying to King Jove the son of Saturn, and

[007-160]thus would one of them say as he looked into the vault of heaven,

[007-161]“Father Jove that rulest from [[mount-ida|Ida]], most glorious in power,

[007-162]vouchsafe victory to Ajax, and let him win great glory: but if

[007-163]you wish well to Hector also and would protect him, grant to each

[007-164]of them equal fame and [[aristeia|prowess]].”

[007-165]Thus they prayed, and Ajax armed himself in his suit of gleaming

[007-166]bronze. When he was in full array he sprang forward as monstrous

[007-167]Mars when he takes part among men whom Jove has set fighting with

[007-168]one another—even so did huge Ajax, bulwark of the Achaeans,

[007-169]spring forward with a grim smile on his face as he brandished his

[007-170]long spear and strode onward. The Argives were elated as they

[007-171]beheld him, but the Trojans trembled in every limb, and the heart

[007-172]even of Hector beat quickly, but he could not now retreat and

[007-173]withdraw into the ranks behind him, for he had been the

[007-174]challenger. Ajax came up bearing his shield in front of him like

[007-175]a wall—a shield of bronze with seven folds of ox-hide—the work of

[007-176]Tychius, who lived in Hyle and was by far the best worker in

[007-177]leather. He had made it with the hides of seven full-fed bulls,

[007-178]and over these he had set an eighth layer of bronze. Holding this

[007-179]shield before him, Ajax son of Telamon came close up to Hector,

[007-180]and menaced him saying, “Hector, you shall now learn, man to man,

[007-181]what kind of champions the Danaans have among them even besides

[007-182]lion-hearted Achilles cleaver of the ranks of men. He now abides

[007-183]at the ships in [[wrath|anger]] with Agamemnon shepherd of his people, but

[007-184]there are many of us who are well able to face you; therefore

[007-185]begin the fight.”

[007-186]And Hector answered, “Noble Ajax, son of Telamon, captain of the

[007-187]host, treat me not as though I were some puny boy or woman that

[007-188]cannot fight. I have been long used to the blood and butcheries

[007-189]of battle. I am quick to turn my leathern shield either to right

[007-190]or left, for this I deem the main thing in battle. I can charge

[007-191]among the chariots and horsemen, and in hand to hand fighting can

[007-192]delight the heart of Mars; howbeit I would not take such a man as

[007-193]you are off his guard—but I will smite you openly if I can.”

[007-194]He poised his spear as he spoke, and hurled it from him. It

[007-195]struck the sevenfold shield in its outermost layer—the eighth,

[007-196]which was of bronze—and went through six of the layers but in the

[007-197]seventh hide it stayed. Then Ajax threw in his turn, and struck

[007-198]the round shield of the son of Priam. The terrible spear went

[007-199]through his gleaming shield, and pressed onward through his

[007-200]cuirass of cunning workmanship; it pierced the shirt against his

[007-201]side, but he swerved and thus saved his life. They then each of

[007-202]them drew out the spear from his shield, and fell on one another

[007-203]like savage lions or wild boars of great strength and endurance:

[007-204]the son of Priam struck the middle of Ajax’s shield, but the

[007-205]bronze did not break, and the point of his dart was turned. Ajax

[007-206]then sprang forward and pierced the shield of Hector; the spear

[007-207]went through it and staggered him as he was springing forward to

[007-208]attack; it gashed his neck and the blood came pouring from the

[007-209]wound, but even so Hector did not cease fighting; he gave ground,

[007-210]and with his brawny hand seized a stone, rugged and huge, that

[007-211]was lying upon the plain; with this he struck the shield of Ajax

[007-212]on the boss that was in its middle, so that the bronze rang

[007-213]again. But Ajax in turn caught up a far larger stone, swung it

[007-214]aloft, and hurled it with prodigious force. This millstone of a

[007-215]rock broke Hector’s shield inwards and threw him down on his back

[007-216]with the shield crushing him under it, but Apollo raised him at

[007-217]once. Thereon they would have hacked at one another in close

[007-218]combat with their swords, had not heralds, messengers of gods and

[007-219]men, come forward, one from the Trojans and the other from the

[007-220]Achaeans—Talthybius and Idaeus both of them honourable men; these

[007-221]parted them with their staves, and the good herald Idaeus said,

[007-222]“My sons, fight no longer, you are both of you valiant, and both

[007-223]are dear to Jove; we know this; but night is now falling, and the

[007-224]behests of night may not be well gainsaid.”

[007-225]Ajax son of Telamon answered, “Idaeus, bid Hector say so, for it

[007-226]was he that challenged our princes. Let him speak first and I

[007-227]will accept his saying.”

[007-228]Then Hector said, “Ajax, heaven has vouchsafed you stature and

[007-229]strength, and judgement; and in wielding the spear you excel all

[007-230]others of the Achaeans. Let us for this day cease fighting;

[007-231]hereafter we will fight anew till heaven decide between us, and

[007-232]give victory to one or to the other; night is now falling, and

[007-233]the behests of night may not be well gainsaid. Gladden, then, the

[007-234]hearts of the Achaeans at your ships, and more especially those

[007-235]of your own followers and clansmen, while I, in the great city of

[007-236]King Priam, bring comfort to the Trojans and their women, who vie

[007-237]with one another in their prayers on my behalf. Let us, moreover,

[007-238]exchange presents that it may be said among the Achaeans and

[007-239]Trojans, ‘They fought with might and main, but were reconciled

[007-240]and parted in friendship.’”

[007-241]On this he gave Ajax a silver-studded sword with its sheath and

[007-242]leathern baldric, and in return Ajax gave him a girdle dyed with

[007-243]purple. Thus they parted, the one going to the host of the

[007-244]Achaeans, and the other to that of the Trojans, who rejoiced when

[007-245]they saw their hero come to them safe and unharmed from the

[007-246]strong hands of mighty Ajax. They led him, therefore, to the city

[007-247]as one that had been saved beyond their hopes. On the other side

[007-248]the Achaeans brought Ajax elated with victory to Agamemnon.

[007-249]When they reached the quarters of the son of Atreus, Agamemnon

[007-250]sacrificed for them a five-year-old bull in honour of Jove the

[007-251]son of Saturn. They flayed the carcass, made it ready, and

[007-252]divided it into joints; these they cut carefully up into smaller

[007-253]pieces, putting them on the spits, roasting them sufficiently,

[007-254]and then drawing them off. When they had done all this and had

[007-255]prepared the feast, they ate it, and every man had his full and

[007-256]equal share, so that all were satisfied, and King Agamemnon gave

[007-257]Ajax some slices cut lengthways down the loin, as a mark of

[007-258]special honour. As soon as they had had enough to eat and drink,

[007-259]old Nestor whose counsel was ever truest began to speak; with all

[007-260]sincerity and goodwill, therefore, he addressed them thus:—

[007-261]“Son of Atreus, and other chieftains, inasmuch as many of the

[007-262]Achaeans are now dead, whose blood Mars has shed by the banks of

[007-263]the Scamander, and their souls have gone down to the house of

[007-264]Hades, it will be well when morning comes that we should cease

[007-265]fighting; we will then wheel our dead together with oxen and

[007-266]mules and burn them not far from the ships, that when we sail

[007-267]hence we may take the bones of our comrades home to their

[007-268]children. Hard by the funeral pyre we will build a barrow that

[007-269]shall be raised from the plain for all in common; near this let

[007-270]us set about building a high wall, to shelter ourselves and our

[007-271]ships, and let it have well-made gates that there may be a way

[007-272]through them for our chariots. Close outside we will dig a deep

[007-273]trench all round it to keep off both horse and foot, that the

[007-274]Trojan chieftains may not bear hard upon us.”

[007-275]Thus he spoke, and the princes shouted in applause. Meanwhile

[007-276]the Trojans held a council, angry and full of discord, on the

[007-277]acropolis by the gates of King Priam’s palace; and wise Antenor

[007-278]spoke. “Hear me,” he said, “Trojans, Dardanians, and allies, that

[007-279]I may speak even as I am minded. Let us give up [[helen|Argive Helen]] and

[007-280]her wealth to the sons of Atreus, for we are now fighting in

[007-281]violation of our solemn covenants, and shall not prosper till we

[007-282]have done as I say.”

[007-283]He then sat down and Alexandrus husband of lovely Helen rose to

[007-284]speak. “Antenor,” said he, “your words are not to my liking; you

[007-285]can find a better saying than this if you will; if, however, you

[007-286]have spoken in good earnest, then indeed has heaven robbed you of

[007-287]your reason. I will speak plainly, and hereby notify to the

[007-288]Trojans that I will not give up the woman; but the wealth that I

[007-289]brought home with her from Argos I will restore, and will add yet

[007-290]further of my own.”

[007-291]On this, when Paris had spoken and taken his seat, Priam of the

[007-292]race of Dardanus, peer of gods in council, rose and with all

[007-293]sincerity and goodwill addressed them thus: “Hear me, Trojans,

[007-294]Dardanians, and allies, that I may speak even as I am minded. Get

[007-295]your suppers now as hitherto throughout the city, but keep your

[007-296]watches and be wakeful. At daybreak let Idaeus go to the ships,

[007-297]and tell Agamemnon and Menelaus sons of Atreus the saying of

[007-298]Alexandrus through whom this quarrel has come about; and let him

[007-299]also be instant with them that they now cease fighting till we

[007-300]burn our dead; hereafter we will fight anew, till heaven decide

[007-301]between us and give victory to one or to the other.”

[007-302]Thus did he speak, and they did even as he had said. They took

[007-303]supper in their companies and at daybreak Idaeus went his way to

[007-304]the ships. He found the Danaans, servants of Mars, in council at

[007-305]the stern of Agamemnon’s ship, and took his place in the midst of

[007-306]them. “Son of Atreus,” he said, “and princes of the Achaean host,

[007-307]Priam and the other noble Trojans have sent me to tell you the

[007-308]saying of Alexandrus through whom this quarrel has come about, if

[007-309]so be that you may find it acceptable. All the treasure he took

[007-310]with him in his ships to Troy—would that he had sooner

[007-311]perished—he will restore, and will add yet further of his own,

[007-312]but he will not give up the wedded wife of Menelaus, though the

[007-313]Trojans would have him do so. Priam bade me inquire further if

[007-314]you will cease fighting till we burn our dead; hereafter we will

[007-315]fight anew, till heaven decide between us and give victory to one

[007-316]or to the other.”

## Section 20

[007-317]They all held their peace, but presently Diomed of the loud

[007-318]war-cry spoke, saying, “Let there be no taking, neither treasure,

[007-319]nor yet Helen, for even a child may see that the doom of the

[007-320]Trojans is at hand.”

[007-321]The sons of the Achaeans shouted applause at the words that

[007-322]Diomed had spoken, and thereon King Agamemnon said to Idaeus,

[007-323]“Idaeus, you have heard the answer the Achaeans make you and I

[007-324]with them. But as concerning the dead, I give you leave to burn

[007-325]them, for when men are once dead there should be no grudging them

[007-326]the rites of fire. Let Jove the mighty husband of Juno be witness

[007-327]to this covenant.”

[007-328]As he spoke he upheld his sceptre in the sight of all the gods,

[007-329]and Idaeus went back to the strong city of Ilius. The Trojans and

[007-330]Dardanians were gathered in council waiting his return; when he

[007-331]came, he stood in their midst and delivered his message. As soon

[007-332]as they heard it they set about their twofold labour, some to

[007-333]gather the corpses, and others to bring in wood. The Argives on

[007-334]their part also hastened from their ships, some to gather the

[007-335]corpses, and others to bring in wood.

[007-336]The sun was beginning to beat upon the fields, fresh risen into

[007-337]the vault of heaven from the slow still currents of deep Oceanus,

[007-338]when the two armies met. They could hardly recognise their dead,

[007-339]but they washed the clotted gore from off them, shed tears over

[007-340]them, and lifted them upon their waggons. Priam had forbidden the

[007-341]Trojans to wail aloud, so they heaped their dead sadly and

[007-342]silently upon the pyre, and having burned them went back to the

[007-343]city of Ilius. The Achaeans in like manner heaped their dead

[007-344]sadly and silently on the pyre, and having burned them went back

[007-345]to their ships.

[007-346]Now in the twilight when it was not yet dawn, chosen bands of the

[007-347]Achaeans were gathered round the pyre and built one barrow that

[007-348]was raised in common for all, and hard by this they built a high

[007-349]wall to shelter themselves and their ships; they gave it strong

[007-350]gates that there might be a way through them for their chariots,

[007-351]and close outside it they dug a trench deep and wide, and they

[007-352]planted it within with stakes.

[007-353]Thus did the Achaeans toil, and the gods, seated by the side of

[007-354]Jove the lord of lightning, marvelled at their great work; but

[007-355]Neptune, lord of the earthquake, spoke, saying, “Father Jove,

[007-356]what mortal in the whole world will again take the gods into his

[007-357]counsel? See you not how the Achaeans have built a wall about

[007-358]their ships and driven a trench all round it, without [[sacrifice|offering]]

[007-359]hecatombs to the gods? The fame of this wall will reach as far as

[007-360]dawn itself, and men will no longer think anything of the one

[007-361]which Phoebus Apollo and myself built with so much labour for

[007-362]Laomedon.”

[007-363]Jove was displeased and answered, “What, O shaker of the earth,

[007-364]are you talking about? A god less powerful than yourself might be

[007-365]alarmed at what they are doing, but your fame reaches as far as

[007-366]dawn itself. Surely when the Achaeans have gone home with their

[007-367]ships, you can shatter their wall and fling it into the sea; you

[007-368]can cover the beach with sand again, and the great wall of the

[007-369]Achaeans will then be utterly effaced.”

[007-370]Thus did they converse, and by sunset the work of the Achaeans

[007-371]was completed; they then slaughtered oxen at their tents and got

[007-372]their supper. Many ships had come with wine from Lemnos, sent by

[007-373]Eueneus the son of Jason, born to him by Hypsipyle. The son of

[007-374]Jason freighted them with ten thousand measures of wine, which he

[007-375]sent specially to the sons of Atreus, Agamemnon and Menelaus.

[007-376]From this supply the Achaeans bought their wine, some with

[007-377]bronze, some with iron, some with hides, some with whole heifers,

[007-378]and some again with captives. They spread a goodly banquet and

[007-379]feasted the whole night through, as also did the Trojans and

[007-380]their allies in the city. But all the time Jove boded them ill

[007-381]and roared with his portentous thunder. Pale fear got hold upon

[007-382]them, and they spilled the wine from their cups on to the ground,

[007-383]nor did any dare drink till he had made offerings to the most

[007-384]mighty son of Saturn. Then they laid themselves down to rest and

[007-385]enjoyed the boon of sleep.
