# Book II

[002-001]BOOK II.

[002-002]Jove sends a lying dream to [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], who thereon calls the

[002-003]chiefs in [[counsel|assembly]], and proposes to sound the mind of his army—In

[002-004]the end they march to fight—Catalogue of the Achaean and Trojan

[002-005]forces.

[002-006]Now the other gods and the armed warriors on the plain slept

[002-007]soundly, but Jove was wakeful, for he was thinking how to do

[002-008][[honor|honour]] to [[achilles|Achilles]], and destroyed much people at the [[achaean-camp|ships]] of the

[002-009]Achaeans. In the end he deemed it would be best to send a lying

[002-010]dream to King Agamemnon; so he called one to him and said to it,

[002-011]“Lying Dream, go to the ships of the Achaeans, into the tent of

[002-012]Agamemnon, and say to him word for word as I now bid you. Tell

[002-013]him to get the Achaeans instantly under arms, for he shall take

[002-014]Troy. There are no longer divided counsels among the gods; Juno

[002-015]has brought them to her own mind, and woe betides the Trojans.”

[002-016]The dream went when it had heard its message, and soon reached

[002-017]the ships of the Achaeans. It sought Agamemnon son of Atreus and

[002-018]found him in his tent, wrapped in a profound slumber. It hovered

[002-019]over his head in the likeness of [[nestor|Nestor]], son of Neleus, whom

[002-020]Agamemnon honoured above all his councillors, and said:—

[002-021]“You are sleeping, son of Atreus; one who has the welfare of his

[002-022][[guest-friendship|host]] and so much other care upon his shoulders should dock his

[002-023]sleep. Hear me at once, for I come as a messenger from Jove, who,

[002-024]though he be not near, yet takes thought for you and pities you.

[002-025]He bids you get the Achaeans instantly under arms, for you shall

[002-026]take Troy. There are no longer divided counsels among the gods;

[002-027]Juno has brought them over to her own mind, and woe betides the

[002-028]Trojans at the hands of Jove. Remember this, and when you wake

[002-029]see that it does not escape you.”

## Section 4

[002-030]The dream then left him, and he thought of things that were

[002-031]surely not to be accomplished. He thought that on that same day

[002-032]he was to take the city of [[priam|Priam]], but he little knew what was in

[002-033]the mind of Jove, who had many another hard-fought fight in store

[002-034]alike for Danaans and Trojans. Then presently he woke, with the

[002-035]divine message still ringing in his ears; so he sat upright, and

[002-036]put on his soft shirt so fair and new, and over this his heavy

[002-037]cloak. He bound his sandals on to his comely feet, and slung his

[002-038]silver-studded sword about his shoulders; then he took the

[002-039]imperishable staff of his father, and sallied forth to the ships

[002-040]of the Achaeans.

[002-041]The goddess Dawn now wended her way to vast [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] that she

[002-042]might herald day to Jove and to the other immortals, and

[002-043]Agamemnon sent the criers round to call the people in assembly;

[002-044]so they called them and the people gathered thereon. But first he

[002-045]summoned a meeting of the elders at the ship of Nestor king of

[002-046]Pylos, and when they were assembled he laid a cunning counsel

[002-047]before them.

[002-048]“My friends,” said he, “I have had a dream from heaven in the

[002-049]dead of night, and its face and figure resembled none but

[002-050]Nestor’s. It hovered over my head and said, ‘You are sleeping,

[002-051]son of Atreus; one who has the welfare of his host and so much

[002-052]other care upon his shoulders should dock his sleep. Hear me at

[002-053]once, for I am a messenger from Jove, who, though he be not near,

[002-054]yet takes thought for you and pities you. He bids you get the

[002-055]Achaeans instantly under arms, for you shall take Troy. There are

[002-056]no longer divided counsels among the gods; Juno has brought them

[002-057]over to her own mind, and woe betides the Trojans at the hands of

[002-058]Jove. Remember this.’ The dream then vanished and I awoke. Let us

[002-059]now, therefore, arm the sons of the Achaeans. But it will be well

[002-060]that I should first sound them, and to this end I will tell them

[002-061]to fly with their ships; but do you others go about among the

[002-062]host and prevent their doing so.”

[002-063]He then sat down, and Nestor the prince of Pylos with all

[002-064]sincerity and goodwill addressed them thus: “My friends,” said

[002-065]he, “princes and councillors of the Argives, if any other man of

[002-066]the Achaeans had told us of this dream we should have declared it

[002-067]false, and would have had nothing to do with it. But he who has

[002-068]seen it is the foremost man among us; we must therefore set about

[002-069]getting the people under arms.”

[002-070]With this he led the way from the assembly, and the other

[002-071]sceptred kings rose with him in obedience to the word of

[002-072]Agamemnon; but the people pressed forward to hear. They swarmed

[002-073]like bees that sally from some hollow cave and flit in countless

[002-074]throng among the spring flowers, bunched in knots and clusters;

[002-075]even so did the mighty multitude pour from ships and tents to the

[002-076]assembly, and range themselves upon the wide-watered shore, while

[002-077]among them ran Wildfire Rumour, messenger of Jove, urging them

[002-078]ever to the fore. Thus they gathered in a pell-mell of mad

[002-079]confusion, and the earth groaned under the tramp of men as the

[002-080]people sought their places. Nine heralds went crying about among

[002-081]them to stay their tumult and bid them listen to the kings, till

[002-082]at last they were got into their several places and ceased their

[002-083]clamour. Then King Agamemnon rose, holding his sceptre. This was

[002-084]the work of Vulcan, who gave it to Jove the son of Saturn. Jove

[002-085]gave it to Mercury, slayer of Argus, guide and guardian. King

[002-086]Mercury gave it to Pelops, the mighty charioteer, and Pelops to

[002-087]Atreus, shepherd of his people. Atreus, when he died, left it to

[002-088]Thyestes, rich in flocks, and Thyestes in his turn left it to be

[002-089]borne by Agamemnon, that he might be lord of all Argos and of the

[002-090]isles. Leaning, then, on his sceptre, he addressed the Argives.

[002-091]“My friends,” he said, “heroes, servants of Mars, the hand of

[002-092]heaven has been laid heavily upon me. Cruel Jove gave me his

[002-093]solemn promise that I should sack the city of Priam before

[002-094]returning, but he has played me false, and is now bidding me go

[002-095]ingloriously back to Argos with the loss of much people. Such is

[002-096]the will of Jove, who has laid many a proud city in the dust, as

[002-097]he will yet lay others, for his power is above all. It will be a

[002-098]sorry tale hereafter that an Achaean host, at once so great and

[002-099]valiant, battled in vain against men fewer in number than

[002-100]themselves; but as yet the end is not in sight. Think that the

[002-101]Achaeans and Trojans have sworn to a solemn [[oath|covenant]], and that

[002-102]they have each been numbered—the Trojans by the roll of their

[002-103]householders, and we by companies of ten; think further that each

[002-104]of our companies desired to have a Trojan householder to pour out

[002-105]their wine; we are so greatly more in number that full many a

[002-106]company would have to go without its cup-bearer. But they have in

[002-107]the town allies from other places, and it is these that hinder me

[002-108]from being able to sack the rich city of Ilius. Nine of Jove’s

[002-109]years are gone; the timbers of our ships have rotted; their

[002-110]tackling is sound no longer. Our wives and little ones at home

[002-111]look anxiously for our coming, but the work that we came hither

[002-112]to do has not been done. Now, therefore, let us all do as I say:

[002-113]let us sail back to our own land, for we shall not take Troy.”

[002-114]With these words he moved the hearts of the multitude, so many of

[002-115]them as knew not the cunning counsel of Agamemnon. They surged to

[002-116]and fro like the waves of the Icarian Sea, when the east and

[002-117]south winds break from heaven’s clouds to lash them; or as when

[002-118]the west wind sweeps over a field of corn and the ears bow

[002-119]beneath the blast, even so were they swayed as they flew with

[002-120]loud cries towards the ships, and the dust from under their feet

[002-121]rose heavenward. They cheered each other on to draw the ships

[002-122]into the sea; they cleared the channels in front of them; they

[002-123]began taking away the stays from underneath them, and the welkin

[002-124]rang with their glad cries, so eager were they to return.

[002-125]Then surely the Argives would have returned after a fashion that

[002-126]was not fated. But Juno said to Minerva, “Alas, daughter of

[002-127]aegis-bearing Jove, unweariable, shall the Argives fly home to

[002-128]their own land over the broad sea, and leave Priam and the

[002-129]Trojans the glory of still keeping [[helen|Helen]], for whose sake so many

[002-130]of the Achaeans have died at Troy, far from their homes? Go about

[002-131]at once among the host, and speak fairly to them, man by man,

[002-132]that they draw not their ships into the sea.”

[002-133]Minerva was not slack to do her bidding. Down she darted from the

[002-134]topmost summits of Olympus, and in a moment she was at the ships

[002-135]of the Achaeans. There she found [[odysseus|Ulysses]], peer of Jove in

[002-136]counsel, standing alone. He had not as yet laid a hand upon his

[002-137]ship, for he was grieved and sorry; so she went close up to him

[002-138]and said, “Ulysses, noble son of Laertes, are you going to fling

[002-139]yourselves into your ships and be off home to your own land in

[002-140]this way? Will you leave Priam and the Trojans the glory of still

[002-141]keeping Helen, for whose sake so many of the Achaeans have died

[002-142]at Troy, far from their homes? Go about at once among the host,

[002-143]and speak fairly to them, man by man, that they draw not their

[002-144]ships into the sea.”

[002-145]Ulysses knew the voice as that of the goddess: he flung his cloak

[002-146]from him and set off to run. His servant Eurybates, a man of

[002-147]Ithaca, who waited on him, took charge of the cloak, whereon

[002-148]Ulysses went straight up to Agamemnon and received from him his

[002-149]ancestral, imperishable staff. With this he went about among the

[002-150]ships of the Achaeans.

[002-151]Whenever he met a king or chieftain, he stood by him and spoke

[002-152]him fairly. “Sir,” said he, “this flight is cowardly and

[002-153]unworthy. Stand to your post, and bid your people also keep their

[002-154]places. You do not yet know the full mind of Agamemnon; he was

[002-155]sounding us, and ere long will visit the Achaeans with his

[002-156]displeasure. We were not all of us at the council to hear what he

[002-157]then said; see to it lest he be angry and do us a mischief; for

[002-158]the pride of kings is great, and the hand of Jove is with them.”

[002-159]But when he came across any common man who was making a noise, he

[002-160]struck him with his staff and rebuked him, saying, “Sirrah, hold

[002-161]your peace, and listen to better men than yourself. You are a

[002-162]coward and no soldier; you are nobody either in fight or council;

[002-163]we cannot all be kings; it is not well that there should be many

[002-164]masters; one man must be supreme—one king to whom the son of

[002-165]scheming Saturn has given the sceptre of sovereignty over you

[002-166]all.”

[002-167]Thus masterfully did he go about among the host, and the people

[002-168]hurried back to the council from their tents and ships with a

[002-169]sound as the thunder of surf when it comes crashing down upon the

[002-170]shore, and all the sea is in an uproar.

[002-171]The rest now took their seats and kept to their own several

[002-172]places, but Thersites still went on wagging his unbridled

[002-173]tongue—a man of many words, and those unseemly; a monger of

[002-174]sedition, a railer against all who were in authority, who cared

[002-175]not what he said, so that he might set the Achaeans in a laugh.

[002-176]He was the ugliest man of all those that came before

[002-177]Troy—bandy-legged, lame of one foot, with his two shoulders

[002-178]rounded and hunched over his chest. His head ran up to a point,

[002-179]but there was little hair on the top of it. Achilles and Ulysses

[002-180]hated him worst of all, for it was with them that he was most

[002-181]wont to wrangle; now, however, with a shrill squeaky voice he

[002-182]began heaping his abuse on Agamemnon. The Achaeans were angry and

[002-183]disgusted, yet none the less he kept on brawling and bawling at

[002-184]the son of Atreus.

[002-185]“Agamemnon,” he cried, “what ails you now, and what more do you

[002-186]want? Your tents are filled with bronze and with fair women, for

[002-187]whenever we take a town we give you the pick of them. Would you

[002-188]have yet more gold, which some Trojan is to give you as a ransom

[002-189]for his son, when I or another Achaean has taken him prisoner? or

[002-190]is it some young girl to hide and lie with? It is not well that

[002-191]you, the ruler of the Achaeans, should bring them into such

[002-192]misery. Weakling cowards, women rather than men, let us sail

[002-193]home, and leave this fellow here at Troy to stew in his own meeds

[002-194]of honour, and discover whether we were of any service to him or

[002-195]no. Achilles is a much better man than he is, and see how he has

[002-196]treated him—robbing him of his prize and keeping it himself.

[002-197]Achilles takes it meekly and shows no fight; if he did, son of

[002-198]Atreus, you would never again insult him.”

[002-199]Thus railed Thersites, but Ulysses at once went up to him and

[002-200]rebuked him sternly. “Check your glib tongue, Thersites,” said

[002-201]he, “and babble not a word further. Chide not with princes when

[002-202]you have none to back you. There is no viler creature come before

[002-203]Troy with the sons of Atreus. Drop this chatter about kings, and

[002-204]neither revile them nor keep harping about going home. We do not

[002-205]yet know how things are going to be, nor whether the Achaeans are

[002-206]to return with good success or evil. How dare you gibe at

[002-207]Agamemnon because the Danaans have awarded him so many prizes? I

[002-208]tell you, therefore—and it shall surely be—that if I again catch

[002-209]you talking such nonsense, I will either forfeit my own head and

[002-210]be no more called father of Telemachus, or I will take you, strip

[002-211]you stark naked, and whip you out of the assembly till you go

[002-212]blubbering back to the ships.”

## Section 5

[002-213]On this he beat him with his staff about the back and shoulders

[002-214]till he dropped and fell a-weeping. The golden sceptre raised a

[002-215]bloody weal on his back, so he sat down frightened and in pain,

[002-216]looking foolish as he wiped the tears from his eyes. The people

[002-217]were sorry for him, yet they laughed heartily, and one would turn

[002-218]to his neighbour saying, “Ulysses has done many a good thing ere

[002-219]now in fight and council, but he never did the Argives a better

[002-220]turn than when he stopped this fellow’s mouth from prating

[002-221]further. He will give the kings no more of his insolence.”

[002-222]Thus said the people. Then Ulysses rose, sceptre in hand, and

[002-223]Minerva in the likeness of a herald bade the people be still,

[002-224]that those who were far off might hear him and consider his

[002-225]council. He therefore with all sincerity and goodwill addressed

[002-226]them thus:—

[002-227]“King Agamemnon, the Achaeans are for making you a by-word among

[002-228]all mankind. They forget the promise they made you when they set

[002-229]out from Argos, that you should not return till you had sacked

[002-230]the town of Troy, and, like children or widowed women, they

[002-231]murmur and would set off homeward. True it is that they have had

[002-232]toil enough to be disheartened. A man chafes at having to stay

[002-233]away from his wife even for a single month, when he is on

[002-234]shipboard, at the mercy of wind and sea, but it is now nine long

[002-235]years that we have been kept here; I cannot, therefore, blame the

[002-236]Achaeans if they turn restive; still we shall be shamed if we go

[002-237]home empty after so long a stay—therefore, my friends, be patient

[002-238]yet a little longer that we may learn whether the prophesyings of

[002-239]Calchas were false or true.

[002-240]“All who have not since perished must remember as though it were

[002-241]yesterday or the day before, how the ships of the Achaeans were

[002-242]detained in Aulis when we were on our way hither to make war on

[002-243]Priam and the Trojans. We were ranged round about a fountain

[002-244][[sacrifice|offering]] hecatombs to the gods upon their holy altars, and there

[002-245]was a fine plane-tree from beneath which there welled a stream of

[002-246]pure water. Then we saw a prodigy; for Jove sent a fearful

[002-247]serpent out of the ground, with blood-red stains upon its back,

[002-248]and it darted from under the altar on to the plane-tree. Now

[002-249]there was a brood of young sparrows, quite small, upon the

[002-250]topmost bough, peeping out from under the leaves, eight in all,

[002-251]and their mother that hatched them made nine. The serpent ate the

[002-252]poor cheeping things, while the old bird flew about lamenting her

[002-253]little ones; but the serpent threw his coils about her and caught

[002-254]her by the wing as she was screaming. Then, when he had eaten

[002-255]both the sparrow and her young, the god who had sent him made him

[002-256]become a [[prophecy|sign]]; for the son of scheming Saturn turned him into

[002-257]stone, and we stood there wondering at that which had come to

[002-258]pass. Seeing, then, that such a fearful portent had broken in

[002-259]upon our hecatombs, Calchas forthwith declared to us the oracles

[002-260]of heaven. ‘Why, Achaeans,’ said he, ‘are you thus speechless?

[002-261]Jove has sent us this sign, long in coming, and long ere it be

[002-262]fulfilled, though its [[glory|fame]] shall last for ever. As the serpent

[002-263]ate the eight fledglings and the sparrow that hatched them, which

[002-264]makes nine, so shall we fight nine years at Troy, but in the

[002-265]tenth shall take the town.’ This was what he said, and now it is

[002-266]all coming true. Stay here, therefore, all of you, till we take

[002-267]the city of Priam.”

[002-268]On this the Argives raised a shout, till the ships rang again

[002-269]with the uproar. Nestor, knight of Gerene, then addressed them.

[002-270]“Shame on you,” he cried, “to stay talking here like children,

[002-271]when you should fight like men. Where are our covenants now, and

[002-272]where the oaths that we have taken? Shall our counsels be flung

[002-273]into the fire, with our drink-offerings and the right hands of

[002-274]fellowship wherein we have put our trust? We waste our time in

[002-275]words, and for all our talking here shall be no further forward.

[002-276]Stand, therefore, son of Atreus, by your own steadfast purpose;

[002-277]lead the Argives on to battle, and leave this handful of men to

[002-278]rot, who scheme, and scheme in vain, to get back to Argos ere

[002-279]they have learned whether Jove be true or a liar. For the mighty

[002-280]son of Saturn surely promised that we should succeed, when we

[002-281]Argives set sail to bring death and destruction upon the Trojans.

[002-282]He showed us favourable signs by flashing his lightning on our

[002-283]right hands; therefore let none make haste to go till he has

[002-284]first lain with the wife of some Trojan, and avenged the toil and

[002-285][[grief|sorrow]] that he has suffered for the sake of Helen. Nevertheless,

[002-286]if any man is in such haste to be at home again, let him lay his

[002-287]hand to his ship that he may meet his [[fate|doom]] in the sight of all.

[002-288]But, O king, consider and give ear to my counsel, for the word

[002-289]that I say may not be neglected lightly. Divide your men,

[002-290]Agamemnon, into their several tribes and clans, that clans and

[002-291]tribes may stand by and help one another. If you do this, and if

[002-292]the Achaeans obey you, you will find out who, both chiefs and

[002-293]peoples, are brave, and who are cowards; for they will vie

[002-294]against the other. Thus you shall also learn whether it is

[002-295]through the counsel of heaven or the cowardice of man that you

[002-296]shall fail to take the town.”

[002-297]And Agamemnon answered, “Nestor, you have again outdone the sons

[002-298]of the Achaeans in counsel. Would, by Father Jove, Minerva, and

[002-299]Apollo, that I had among them ten more such councillors, for the

[002-300]city of King Priam would then soon fall beneath our hands, and we

[002-301]should sack it. But the son of Saturn afflicts me with bootless

[002-302]wranglings and strife. Achilles and I are quarrelling about this

[002-303]girl, in which matter I was the first to offend; if we can be of

[002-304]one mind again, the Trojans will not stave off destruction for a

[002-305]day. Now, therefore, get your morning meal, that our hosts join

[002-306]in fight. Whet well your spears; see well to the ordering of your

[002-307]shields; give good feeds to your horses, and look your chariots

[002-308]carefully over, that we may do battle the livelong day; for we

[002-309]shall have no rest, not for a moment, till night falls to part

[002-310]us. The bands that bear your shields shall be wet with the sweat

[002-311]upon your shoulders, your hands shall weary upon your spears,

[002-312]your horses shall steam in front of your chariots, and if I see

[002-313]any man shirking the fight, or trying to keep out of it at the

[002-314]ships, there shall be no help for him, but he shall be a prey to

[002-315]dogs and vultures.”

[002-316]Thus he spoke, and the Achaeans roared applause. As when the

[002-317]waves run high before the blast of the south wind and break on

[002-318]some lofty headland, dashing against it and buffeting it without

[002-319]ceasing, as the storms from every quarter drive them, even so did

[002-320]the Achaeans rise and hurry in all directions to their ships.

[002-321]There they lighted their fires at their tents and got dinner,

[002-322]offering sacrifice every man to one or other of the gods, and

[002-323]praying each one of them that he might live to come out of the

[002-324]fight. Agamemnon, king of men, sacrificed a fat five-year-old

[002-325]bull to the mighty son of Saturn, and invited the princes and

[002-326]elders of his host. First he asked Nestor and King Idomeneus,

[002-327]then the two Ajaxes and the son of Tydeus, and sixthly Ulysses,

[002-328]peer of gods in counsel; but Menelaus came of his own accord, for

[002-329]he knew how busy his brother then was. They stood round the bull

[002-330]with the barley-meal in their hands, and Agamemnon prayed,

[002-331]saying, “Jove, most glorious, supreme, that dwellest in heaven,

[002-332]and ridest upon the storm-cloud, grant that the sun may not go

[002-333]down, nor the night fall, till the palace of Priam is laid low,

[002-334]and its gates are consumed with fire. Grant that my sword may

[002-335]pierce the shirt of [[hector|Hector]] about his heart, and that full many of

[002-336]his comrades may bite the dust as they fall dying round him.”

[002-337]Thus he prayed, but the son of Saturn would not fulfil his

[002-338]prayer. He accepted the sacrifice, yet none the less increased

[002-339]their toil continually. When they had done praying and sprinkling

[002-340]the barley-meal upon the victim, they drew back its head, killed

[002-341]it, and then flayed it. They cut out the thigh-bones, wrapped

[002-342]them round in two layers of fat, and set pieces of raw meat on

[002-343]the top of them. These they burned upon the split logs of

[002-344]firewood, but they spitted the inward meats, and held them in the

[002-345]flames to cook. When the thigh-bones were burned, and they had

[002-346]tasted the inward meats, they cut the rest up small, put the

[002-347]pieces upon spits, roasted them till they were done, and drew

[002-348]them off; then, when they had finished their work and the feast

[002-349]was ready, they ate it, and every man had his full share, so that

[002-350]all were satisfied. As soon as they had had enough to eat and

[002-351]drink, Nestor, knight of Gerene, began to speak. “King

[002-352]Agamemnon,” said he, “let us not stay talking here, nor be slack

[002-353]in the work that heaven has put into our hands. Let the heralds

[002-354]summon the people to gather at their several ships; we will then

[002-355]go about among the host, that we may begin fighting at once.”

[002-356]Thus did he speak, and Agamemnon heeded his words. He at once

[002-357]sent the criers round to call the people in assembly. So they

[002-713]the river Maeander and the lofty crests of Mt. Mycale. These were

[002-714]commanded by Nastes and Amphimachus, the brave sons of Nomion. He

[002-715]came into the fight with gold about him, like a girl; fool that

[002-716]he was, his gold was of no avail to save him, for he fell in the

[002-717]river by the hand of the fleet descendant of Aeacus, and Achilles

[002-718]bore away his gold.

[002-719]Sarpedon and Glaucus led the Lycians from their distant land, by

[002-720]the eddying waters of the [[scamander|Xanthus]].
