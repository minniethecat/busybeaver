# Book XXIII

[023-001]BOOK XXIII.

[023-002]The funeral of [[patroclus|Patroclus]], and the funeral games.

[023-003]Thus did they make their moan throughout the city, while the

[023-004]Achaeans when they reached the Hellespont went back every man to

[023-005]his own ship. But [[achilles|Achilles]] would not let the Myrmidons go, and

[023-006]spoke to his brave comrades saying, “Myrmidons, famed horsemen

[023-007]and my own trusted friends, not yet, forsooth, let us unyoke, but

[023-008]with horse and chariot draw near to the body and mourn Patroclus,

[023-009]in due [[honor|honour]] to the dead. When we have had full comfort of

[023-010][[grief|lamentation]] we will unyoke our horses and take supper all of us

[023-011]here.”

[023-012]On this they all joined in a cry of wailing and Achilles led them

[023-013]in their lament. Thrice did they drive their chariots all

[023-014]sorrowing round the body, and Thetis stirred within them a still

[023-015]deeper yearning. The sands of the sea-[[achaean-camp|shore]] and the men’s armour

[023-016]were wet with their weeping, so great a minister of fear was he

[023-017]whom they had lost. Chief in all their mourning was the son of

[023-018]Peleus: he laid his blood-stained hand on the breast of his

[023-019]friend. “Fare well,” he cried, “Patroclus, even in the house of

[023-020]Hades. I will now do all that I erewhile promised you; I will

[023-021]drag [[hector|Hector]] hither and let dogs devour him raw; twelve noble sons

[023-022]of Trojans will I also slay before your pyre to avenge you.”

[023-023]As he spoke he treated the body of noble Hector with contumely,

[023-024]laying it at full length in the dust beside the bier of

[023-025]Patroclus. The others then put off every man his armour, took the

[023-026]horses from their chariots, and seated themselves in great

[023-027]multitude by the ship of the fleet descendant of Aeacus, who

[023-028]thereon feasted them with an abundant funeral banquet. Many a

[023-029]goodly ox, with many a sheep and bleating goat did they butcher

[023-030]and cut up; many a tusked boar moreover, fat and well-fed, did

[023-031]they singe and set to roast in the flames of Vulcan; and rivulets

[023-032]of blood flowed all round the place where the body was lying.

[023-033]Then the princes of the Achaeans took the son of Peleus to

[023-034][[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], but hardly could they persuade him to come with them,

[023-035]so wroth was he for the death of his comrade. As soon as they

[023-036]reached Agamemnon’s tent they told the serving-men to set a large

[023-037]tripod over the fire in case they might persuade the son of

[023-038]Peleus to wash the clotted gore from this body, but he denied

[023-039]them sternly, and swore it with a solemn oath, saying, “Nay, by

[023-040]King Jove, first and mightiest of all gods, it is not meet that

[023-041]water should touch my body, till I have laid Patroclus on the

[023-042]flames, have built him a barrow, and shaved my head—for so long

[023-043]as I live no such second sorrow shall ever draw nigh me. Now,

[023-044]therefore, let us do all that this sad festival demands, but at

[023-045]break of day, King Agamemnon, bid your men bring wood, and

[023-046]provide all else that the dead may duly take into the realm of

[023-047]darkness; the fire shall thus burn him out of our sight the

[023-048]sooner, and the people shall turn again to their own labours.”

[023-049]Thus did he speak, and they did even as he had said. They made

[023-050]haste to prepare the meal, they ate, and every man had his full

[023-051]share so that all were satisfied. As soon as they had had enough

[023-052]to eat and drink, the others went to their rest each in his own

[023-053]tent, but the son of Peleus lay grieving among his Myrmidons by

[023-054]the shore of the sounding sea, in an open place where the waves

[023-055]came surging in one after another. Here a very deep slumber took

[023-056]hold upon him and eased the burden of his sorrows, for his limbs

[023-057]were weary with chasing Hector round windy Ilius. Presently the

[023-058]sad spirit of Patroclus drew near him, like what he had been in

[023-059]stature, voice, and the light of his beaming eyes, clad, too, as

[023-060]he had been clad in life. The spirit hovered over his head and

[023-061]said—

[023-062]“You sleep, Achilles, and have forgotten me; you loved me living,

[023-063]but now that I am dead you think for me no further. Bury me with

[023-064]all speed that I may pass the gates of Hades; the ghosts, vain

[023-065]shadows of men that can labour no more, drive me away from them;

[023-066]they will not yet suffer me to join those that are beyond the

[023-067]river, and I wander all desolate by the wide gates of the house

[023-068]of Hades. Give me now your hand I pray you, for when you have

[023-069]once given me my dues of fire, never shall I again come forth out

[023-070]of the house of Hades. Nevermore shall we sit apart and take

[023-071]sweet counsel among the living; the cruel fate which was my

[023-072]birth-right has yawned its wide jaws around me—nay, you too

[023-073]Achilles, peer of gods, are doomed to die beneath the wall of the

[023-074]noble Trojans.

[023-075]“One prayer more will I make you, if you will grant it; let not

[023-076]my bones be laid apart from yours, Achilles, but with them; even

[023-077]as we were brought up together in your own home, what time

[023-078]Menoetius brought me to you as a child from Opoeis because by a

[023-079]sad spite I had killed the son of Amphidamas—not of set purpose,

[023-080]but in childish quarrel over the dice. The knight Peleus took me

[023-081]into his house, entreated me kindly, and named me to be your

[023-082]squire; therefore let our bones lie in but a single urn, the

[023-083]two-handled golden vase given to you by your mother.”

[023-084]And Achilles answered, “Why, true heart, are you come hither to

[023-085]lay these charges upon me? I will of my own self do all as you

[023-086]have bidden me. Draw closer to me, let us once more throw our

[023-087]arms around one another, and find sad comfort in the sharing of

[023-088]our sorrows.”

[023-089]He opened his arms towards him as he spoke and would have clasped

[023-090]him in them, but there was nothing, and the spirit vanished as a

[023-091]vapour, gibbering and whining into the earth. Achilles sprang to

[023-092]his feet, smote his two hands, and made lamentation saying, “Of a

[023-093]truth even in the house of Hades there are ghosts and phantoms

[023-094]that have no life in them; all night long the sad spirit of

[023-095]Patroclus has hovered over head making piteous moan, telling me

[023-096]what I am to do for him, and looking wondrously like himself.”

[023-097]Thus did he speak and his words set them all weeping and mourning

[023-098]about the poor dumb dead, till rosy-fingered morn appeared. Then

[023-099]King Agamemnon sent men and mules from all parts of the camp, to

[023-100]bring wood, and Meriones, squire to Idomeneus, was in charge over

[023-101]them. They went out with woodmen’s axes and strong ropes in their

[023-102]hands, and before them went the mules. Up hill and down dale did

[023-103]they go, by straight ways and crooked, and when they reached the

[023-104]heights of many-fountained [[mount-ida|Ida]], they laid their axes to the roots

[023-105]of many a tall branching oak that came thundering down as they

[023-106]felled it. They split the trees and bound them behind the mules,

[023-107]which then wended their way as they best could through the thick

[023-108]brushwood on to the plain. All who had been cutting wood bore

[023-109]logs, for so Meriones squire to Idomeneus had bidden them, and

[023-110]they threw them down in a line upon the sea-shore at the place

[023-111]where Achilles would make a mighty monument for Patroclus and for

[023-112]himself.

[023-113]When they had thrown down their great logs of wood over the whole

[023-114]ground, they stayed all of them where they were, but Achilles

[023-115]ordered his brave Myrmidons to gird on their armour, and to yoke

[023-116]each man his horses; they therefore rose, girded on their armour

[023-117]and mounted each his chariot—they and their charioteers with

[023-118]them. The chariots went before, and they that were on foot

[023-119]followed as a cloud in their tens of thousands after. In the

[023-120]midst of them his comrades bore Patroclus and covered him with

[023-121]the locks of their hair which they cut off and threw upon his

[023-122]body. Last came Achilles with his head bowed for sorrow, so noble

[023-123]a comrade was he taking to the house of Hades.

[023-124]When they came to the place of which Achilles had told them they

[023-125]laid the body down and built up the wood. Achilles then bethought

[023-126]him of another matter. He went a space away from the pyre, and

[023-127]cut off the yellow lock which he had let grow for the river

[023-128]Spercheius. He looked all sorrowfully out upon the dark sea, and

[023-129]said, “Spercheius, in vain did my father Peleus [[oath|vow]] to you that

[023-130]when I returned home to my loved native land I should cut off

[023-131]this lock and offer you a holy [[sacrifice|hecatomb]]; fifty she-goats was I to

[023-132]sacrifice to you there at your springs, where is your grove and

[023-133]your altar fragrant with burnt-offerings. Thus did my father vow,

[023-134]but you have not fulfilled his prayer; now, therefore, that I

[023-135]shall see my home no more, I give this lock as a keepsake to the

[023-136]hero Patroclus.”

[023-137]As he spoke he placed the lock in the hands of his dear comrade,

[023-138]and all who stood by were filled with yearning and lamentation.

[023-139]The sun would have gone down upon their mourning had not Achilles

[023-140]presently said to Agamemnon, “Son of Atreus, for it is to you

[023-141]that the people will give ear, there is a time to mourn and a

[023-142]time to cease from mourning; bid the people now leave the pyre

[023-143]and set about getting their dinners: we, to whom the dead is

[023-144]dearest, will see to what is wanted here, and let the other

[023-145]princes also stay by me.”

[023-146]When King Agamemnon heard this he dismissed the people to their

[023-147]ships, but those who were about the dead heaped up wood and built

[023-148]a pyre a hundred feet this way and that; then they laid the dead

[023-149]all sorrowfully upon the top of it. They flayed and dressed many

[023-150]fat sheep and oxen before the pyre, and Achilles took fat from

[023-151]all of them and wrapped the body therein from head to foot,

[023-152]heaping the flayed carcases all round it. Against the bier he

[023-153]leaned two-handled jars of honey and unguents; four proud horses

[023-154]did he then cast upon the pyre, groaning the while he did so. The

[023-155]dead hero had had house-dogs; two of them did Achilles slay and

[023-156]threw upon the pyre; he also put twelve brave sons of noble

[023-157]Trojans to the sword and laid them with the rest, for he was full

[023-158]of bitterness and [[wrath|fury]]. Then he committed all to the resistless

[023-159]and devouring might of the fire; he groaned aloud and called on

[023-160]his dead comrade by name. “Fare well,” he cried, “Patroclus, even

[023-161]in the house of Hades; I am now doing all that I have promised

[023-162]you. Twelve brave sons of noble Trojans shall the flames consume

[023-163]along with yourself, but dogs, not fire, shall devour the flesh

[023-164]of Hector son of [[priam|Priam]].”

[023-165]Thus did he vaunt, but the dogs came not about the body of

[023-166]Hector, for Jove’s daughter Venus kept them off him night and

[023-167]day, and anointed him with ambrosial oil of roses that his flesh

[023-168]might not be torn when Achilles was dragging him about. Phoebus

[023-169]Apollo moreover sent a dark cloud from heaven to earth, which

[023-170]gave shade to the whole place where Hector lay, that the heat of

[023-171]the sun might not parch his body.

[023-172]Now the pyre about dead Patroclus would not kindle. Achilles

[023-173]therefore bethought him of another matter; he went apart and

[023-174]prayed to the two winds Boreas and Zephyrus vowing them goodly

[023-175]offerings. He made them many drink-offerings from the golden cup

[023-176]and besought them to come and help him that the wood might make

[023-177]haste to kindle and the dead bodies be consumed. Fleet Iris heard

[023-178]him praying and started off to fetch the winds. They were holding

[023-179]high feast in the house of boisterous Zephyrus when Iris came

[023-180]running up to the stone threshold of the house and stood there,

[023-181]but as soon as they set eyes on her they all came towards her and

[023-182]each of them called her to him, but Iris would not sit down. “I

[023-183]cannot stay,” she said, “I must go back to the streams of Oceanus

[023-184]and the land of the Ethiopians who are offering hecatombs to the

[023-185]immortals, and I would have my share; but Achilles prays that

[023-186]Boreas and shrill Zephyrus will come to him, and he vows them

[023-187]goodly offerings; he would have you blow upon the pyre of

[023-188]Patroclus for whom all the Achaeans are lamenting.”

[023-189]With this she left them, and the two winds rose with a cry that

[023-190]rent the air and swept the clouds before them. They blew on and

[023-191]on until they came to the sea, and the waves rose high beneath

[023-192]them, but when they reached Troy they fell upon the pyre till the

[023-193]mighty flames roared under the blast that they blew. All night

[023-194]long did they blow hard and beat upon the fire, and all night

[023-195]long did Achilles grasp his double cup, drawing wine from a

[023-196]mixing-bowl of gold, and calling upon the spirit of dead

[023-197]Patroclus as he poured it upon the ground until the earth was

[023-198]drenched. As a father mourns when he is burning the bones of his

[023-199]bridegroom son whose death has wrung the hearts of his parents,

[023-200]even so did Achilles mourn while burning the body of his comrade,

[023-201]pacing round the bier with piteous groaning and lamentation.

[023-202]At length as the Morning Star was beginning to herald the light

[023-203]which saffron-mantled Dawn was soon to suffuse over the sea, the

[023-204]flames fell and the fire began to die. The winds then went home

[023-205]beyond the Thracian sea, which roared and boiled as they swept

[023-206]over it. The son of Peleus now turned away from the pyre and lay

[023-207]down, overcome with toil, till he fell into a sweet slumber.

[023-208]Presently they who were about the son of Atreus drew near in a

[023-209]body, and roused him with the noise and tramp of their coming. He

[023-210]sat upright and said, “Son of Atreus, and all other princes of

[023-211]the Achaeans, first pour red wine everywhere upon the fire and

[023-212]quench it; let us then gather the bones of Patroclus son of

[023-213]Menoetius, singling them out with care; they are easily found,

[023-214]for they lie in the middle of the pyre, while all else, both men

[023-215]and horses, has been thrown in a heap and burned at the outer

[023-216]edge. We will lay the bones in a golden urn, in two layers of

[023-217]fat, against the time when I shall myself go down into the house

[023-218]of Hades. As for the barrow, labour not to raise a great one now,

[023-219]but such as is reasonable. Afterwards, let those Achaeans who may

[023-220]be left at the ships when I am gone, build it both broad and

[023-221]high.”

[023-222]Thus he spoke and they obeyed the word of the son of Peleus.

[023-223]First they poured red wine upon the thick layer of ashes and

[023-224]quenched the fire. With many tears they singled out the whitened

[023-225]bones of their loved comrade and laid them within a golden urn in

[023-226]two layers of fat: they then covered the urn with a linen cloth

[023-227]and took it inside the tent. They marked off the circle where the

[023-228]barrow should be, made a foundation for it about the pyre, and

[023-229]forthwith heaped up the earth. When they had thus raised a mound

[023-230]they were going away, but Achilles stayed the people and made

[023-231]them sit in [[counsel|assembly]]. He brought prizes from the ships—cauldrons,

[023-232]tripods, horses and mules, noble oxen, women with fair girdles,

[023-233]and swart iron.

[023-234]The first prize he offered was for the chariot races—a woman

[023-235]skilled in all useful arts, and a three-legged cauldron that had

[023-236]ears for handles, and would hold twenty-two measures. This was

[023-237]for the man who came in first. For the second there was a

[023-238]six-year old mare, unbroken, and in foal to a he-ass; the third

[023-239]was to have a goodly cauldron that had never yet been on the

[023-240]fire; it was still bright as when it left the maker, and would

[023-241]hold four measures. The fourth prize was two talents of gold, and

[023-242]the fifth a two-handled urn as yet unsoiled by smoke. Then he

[023-243]stood up and spoke among the Argives saying—

[023-244]“Son of Atreus, and all other Achaeans, these are the prizes that

[023-245]lie waiting the winners of the chariot races. At any other time I

[023-246]should carry off the first prize and take it to my own tent; you

[023-247]know how far my steeds excel all others—for they are immortal;

[023-248]Neptune gave them to my father Peleus, who in his turn gave them

[023-249]to myself; but I shall hold aloof, I and my steeds that have lost

[023-250]their brave and kind driver, who many a time has washed them in

[023-251]clear water and anointed their manes with oil. See how they stand

[023-252]weeping here, with their manes trailing on the ground in the

[023-253]extremity of their sorrow. But do you others set yourselves in

[023-254]order throughout the [[guest-friendship|host]], whosoever has confidence in his horses

[023-255]and in the strength of his chariot.”

[023-256]Thus spoke the son of Peleus and the drivers of chariots

[023-257]bestirred themselves. First among them all uprose Eumelus, king

[023-258]of men, son of Admetus, a man excellent in horsemanship. Next to

[023-259]him rose mighty Diomed son of Tydeus; he yoked the Trojan horses

[023-260]which he had taken from Aeneas, when Apollo bore him out of the

[023-261]fight. Next to him, yellow-haired Menelaus son of Atreus rose and

[023-262]yoked his fleet horses, Agamemnon’s mare Aethe, and his own horse

[023-263]Podargus. The mare had been given to Agamemnon by Echepolus son

[023-264]of Anchises, that he might not have to follow him to Ilius, but

[023-265]might stay at home and take his ease; for Jove had endowed him

[023-266]with great wealth and he lived in spacious Sicyon. This mare, all

[023-267]eager for the race, did Menelaus put under the yoke.

[023-268]Fourth in order Antilochus, son to noble [[nestor|Nestor]] son of Neleus,

[023-269]made ready his horses. These were bred in Pylos, and his father

[023-270]came up to him to give him good advice of which, however, he

[023-271]stood in but little need. “Antilochus,” said Nestor, “you are

[023-272]young, but Jove and Neptune have loved you well, and have made

[023-273]you an excellent horseman. I need not therefore say much by way

[023-274]of instruction. You are skilful at wheeling your horses round the

[023-275]post, but the horses themselves are very slow, and it is this

[023-276]that will, I fear, mar your chances. The other drivers know less

[023-277]than you do, but their horses are fleeter; therefore, my dear

[023-278]son, see if you cannot hit upon some artifice whereby you may

[023-279]insure that the prize shall not slip through your fingers. The

[023-280]woodman does more by skill than by brute force; by skill the

[023-281]pilot guides his storm-tossed barque over the sea, and so by

[023-282]skill one driver can beat another. If a man go wide in rounding

[023-283]this way and that, whereas a man who knows what he is doing may

[023-284]have worse horses, but he will keep them well in hand when he

[023-285]sees the doubling-post; he knows the precise moment at which to

[023-286]pull the rein, and keeps his eye well on the man in front of him.

[023-287]I will give you this certain token which cannot escape your

[023-288]notice. There is a stump of a dead tree—oak or pine as it may

[023-289]be—some six feet above the ground, and not yet rotted away by

[023-290]rain; it stands at the fork of the road; it has two white stones

[023-291]set one on each side, and there is a clear course all round it.

[023-292]It may have been a monument to some one long since dead, or it

[023-293]may have been used as a doubling-post in days gone by; now,

[023-294]however, it has been fixed on by Achilles as the mark round which

[023-295]the chariots shall turn; hug it as close as you can, but as you

[023-296]stand in your chariot lean over a little to the left; urge on

[023-297]your right-hand horse with voice and lash, and give him a loose

[023-298]rein, but let the left-hand horse keep so close in, that the nave

[023-299]of your wheel shall almost graze the post; but mind the stone, or

[023-300]you will wound your horses and break your chariot in pieces,

[023-301]which would be sport for others but confusion for yourself.

[023-302]Therefore, my dear son, mind well what you are about, for if you

[023-303]can be first to round the post there is no chance of any one

[023-304]giving you the go-by later, not even though you had Adrestus’s

[023-305]horse Arion behind you—a horse which is of divine race—or those

[023-306]of Laomedon, which are the noblest in this country.”

[023-307]When Nestor had made an end of counselling his son he sat down in

[023-308]his place, and fifth in order Meriones got ready his horses. They

[023-309]then all mounted their chariots and cast lots. Achilles shook the

[023-310]helmet, and the lot of Antilochus son of Nestor fell out first;

[023-311]next came that of King Eumelus, and after his, those of Menelaus

[023-312]son of Atreus and of Meriones. The last place fell to the lot of

[023-313]Diomed son of Tydeus, who was the best man of them all. They took

[023-314]their places in line; Achilles showed them the doubling-post

[023-315]round which they were to turn, some way off upon the plain; here

[023-316]he stationed his father’s follower Phoenix as umpire, to note the

[023-317]running, and report truly.

[023-318]At the same instant they all of them lashed their horses, struck

[023-319]them with the reins, and shouted at them with all their might.

[023-320]They flew full speed over the plain away from the ships, the dust

[023-321]rose from under them as it were a cloud or whirlwind, and their

[023-322]manes were all flying in the wind. At one moment the chariots

[023-323]seemed to touch the ground, and then again they bounded into the

[023-324]air; the drivers stood erect, and their hearts beat fast and

[023-325]furious in their lust of victory. Each kept calling on his

[023-326]horses, and the horses scoured the plain amid the clouds of dust

[023-327]that they raised.

[023-328]It was when they were doing the last part of the course on their

[023-329]way back towards the sea that their pace was strained to the

[023-330]utmost and it was seen what each could do. The horses of the

[023-331]descendant of Pheres now took the lead, and close behind them

[023-332]came the Trojan stallions of Diomed. They seemed as if about to

[023-333]mount Eumelus’s chariot, and he could feel their warm breath on

[023-334]his back and on his broad shoulders, for their heads were close

[023-335]to him as they flew over the course. Diomed would have now passed

[023-336]him, or there would have been a dead heat, but Phoebus Apollo to

[023-337]spite him made him drop his whip. Tears of anger fell from his

[023-338]eyes as he saw the mares going on faster than ever, while his own

[023-339]horses lost ground through his having no whip. Minerva saw the

[023-340]trick which Apollo had played the son of Tydeus, so she brought

[023-341]him his whip and put spirit into his horses; moreover she went

[023-342]after the son of Admetus in a rage and broke his yoke for him;

[023-343]the mares went one to one side of the course, and the other to

[023-344]the other, and the pole was broken against the ground. Eumelus

[023-345]was thrown from his chariot close to the wheel; his elbows,

[023-346]mouth, and nostrils were all torn, and his forehead was bruised

[023-347]above his eyebrows; his eyes filled with tears and he could find

[023-348]no utterance. But the son of Tydeus turned his horses aside and

[023-349]shot far ahead, for Minerva put fresh strength into them and

[023-350]covered Diomed himself with glory.

[023-351]Menelaus son of Atreus came next behind him, but Antilochus

[023-352]called to his father’s horses. “On with you both,” he cried, “and

[023-353]do your very utmost. I do not bid you try to beat the steeds of

[023-354]the son of Tydeus, for Minerva has put running into them, and has

[023-355]covered Diomed with glory; but you must overtake the horses of

[023-356]the son of Atreus and not be left behind, or Aethe who is so

[023-357]fleet will taunt you. Why, my good fellows, are you lagging? I

[023-358]tell you, and it shall surely be—Nestor will keep neither of you,

[023-359]but will put both of you to the sword, if we win any the worse a

[023-727]and Epeus took the quoit, whirled it, and flung it from him,

[023-728]which set all the Achaeans laughing. After him threw Leonteus of

[023-729]the race of Mars. Ajax son of Telamon threw third, and sent the

[023-730]quoit beyond any mark that had been made yet, but when mighty

[023-731]Polypoetes took the quoit he hurled it as though it had been a

[023-732]stockman’s stick which he sends flying about among his cattle

[023-733]when he is driving them, so far did his throw out-distance those

[023-734]of the others. All who saw it roared applause, and his comrades

[023-735]carried the prize for him and set it on board his ship.

## Section 69

[023-736]Achilles next offered a prize of iron for archery—ten

[023-737]double-edged axes and ten with single edges: he set up a ship’s

[023-738]mast, some way off upon the sands, and with a fine string tied a

[023-739]pigeon to it by the foot; this was what they were to aim at.

[023-740]“Whoever,” he said, “can hit the pigeon shall have all the axes

[023-741]and take them away with him; he who hits the string without

[023-742]hitting the bird will have taken a worse aim and shall have the

[023-743]single-edged axes.”

[023-744]Then uprose King Teucer, and Meriones the stalwart squire of

[023-745]Idomeneus rose also, They cast lots in a bronze helmet and the

[023-746]lot of Teucer fell first. He let fly with his arrow forthwith,

[023-747]but he did not promise hecatombs of firstling lambs to King

[023-748]Apollo, and missed his bird, for Apollo foiled his aim; but he

[023-749]hit the string with which the bird was tied, near its foot; the

[023-750]arrow cut the string clean through so that it hung down towards

[023-751]the ground, while the bird flew up into the sky, and the Achaeans

[023-752]shouted applause. Meriones, who had his arrow ready while Teucer

[023-753]was aiming, snatched the bow out of his hand, and at once

[023-754]promised that he would sacrifice a hecatomb of firstling lambs to

[023-755]Apollo lord of the bow; then espying the pigeon high up under the

[023-756]clouds, he hit her in the middle of the wing as she was circling

[023-757]upwards; the arrow went clean through the wing and fixed itself

[023-758]in the ground at Meriones’ feet, but the bird perched on the

[023-759]ship’s mast hanging her head and with all her feathers drooping;

[023-760]the life went out of her, and she fell heavily from the mast.

[023-761]Meriones, therefore, took all ten double-edged axes, while Teucer

[023-762]bore off the single-edged ones to his ships.

[023-763]Then the son of Peleus brought in a spear and a cauldron that had

[023-764]never been on the fire; it was worth an ox, and was chased with a

[023-765]pattern of flowers; and those that throw the javelin stood up—to

[023-766]wit the son of Atreus, king of men Agamemnon, and Meriones,

[023-767]stalwart squire of Idomeneus. But Achilles spoke saying, “Son of

[023-768]Atreus, we know how far you excel all others both in power and in

[023-769]throwing the javelin; take the cauldron back with you to your

[023-770]ships, but if it so please you, let us give the spear to

[023-771]Meriones; this at least is what I should myself wish.”

[023-772]King Agamemnon assented. So he gave the bronze spear to Meriones,

[023-773]and handed the goodly cauldron to Talthybius his esquire.
