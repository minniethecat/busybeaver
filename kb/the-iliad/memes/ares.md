# Ares (Mars)

Ares, called Mars in Samuel Butler's translation, is the god of war in its most elemental and destructive form — not the strategic warfare governed by [[athena|Athena]] but the mindless fury of combat, the bloodlust that drives men to kill and be killed. His Homeric epithets tell the story: "murderous Mars" (*miaiphonos*), "bane of men" (*brotoloigos*), "blood-stained." He fights on the Trojan side, but the Iliad systematically humiliates him: he is wounded by the mortal Diomedes in Book 5, complains petulantly to Zeus, and is contemptuously dismissed. Where Athena embodies warfare as a tool of civilisation, Ares embodies warfare as an end in itself — and the poem judges him accordingly.

## Identity

Ares is the son of Zeus and Hera (005-034), and in the Iliad's divine economy he represents the least governable aspect of war: its rage, its noise, its indifference to justice or outcome. He "rages indiscriminately" (005-034), killing Greeks and Trojans alike, caring only for the act of destruction. Butler's translation follows the Roman convention, rendering Ares as "Mars," but the Latin Mars — a dignified father of the Roman people — bears little resemblance to the Homeric Ares, who is despised even by his own parents.

Zeus' words to Ares in Book 5 are among the harshest any god receives from the king of Olympus: "You are the most hateful to me of all the gods on Olympus, for you ever love strife and wars and battles" (005-034). This divine rejection is unique — no other Olympian is so thoroughly condemned by Zeus. Ares' isolation on Olympus reflects the poem's moral architecture: war without purpose, without strategy, without justice, is not merely destructive but contemptible.

## Role in the Epic

Ares appears in Books 5, 15, 20, and 21, but his defining episode is the wounding in Book 5 — the Iliad's most extended meditation on the nature of war and its proper place in the cosmic order.

- **Book 5 — The Death of Pandarus, Wounding of Ares:** After Diomedes kills Pandarus and wounds Aeneas, Aphrodite descends to rescue her son and is herself wounded by Diomedes (005-268). Athena then turns her attention to Ares. Finding him "waiting on the left of the battle, wrapped in mist" (005-303), she mounts Diomedes' chariot beside him, takes the reins, and guides his spear directly into Ares' belly. Ares "bellowed as loud as nine or ten thousand men in the fury of battle" (005-328) and fled to Olympus, where he displayed his wound to Zeus.

    This episode is the Iliad's theological centrepiece on the nature of war. Athena — wisdom-governed warfare — defeats Ares — mindless violence — using a mortal as her instrument. The message is unmistakable: brute force, unaided by intelligence and purpose, is powerless.

- **Book 5 — Zeus' Rebuke:** When Ares comes whimpering to Zeus, the king of gods looks at him "with an angry frown" and delivers the poem's most devastating assessment of a god's character (005-328). Zeus recalls that Ares takes after his mother Hera in his "unmanageable and wilful" nature, but adds that "as you are my son, and your mother bore you to me, I cannot see you in agony without grieving." He orders Paeëon to heal the wound. The rebuke mixes paternal tenderness with moral condemnation — Ares is loved because he is Zeus' son, not because he is worthy of love.

- **Book 15 — The Death of Ascalaphus:** When Ares learns that his mortal son Ascalaphus has been killed, he "struck his sturdy thighs with the flat of his hands and cried in grief" (015-279). He immediately prepares to descend and avenge his son, even if it means defying Zeus. Athena intercepts him, reminding him that Zeus has forbidden divine intervention, and Ares reluctantly submits. This episode shows a different side of Ares: the grief of a father, however brutal the father may be.

- **Book 20 — Theomachy:** In the divine battle that opens Book 20, Ares confronts Athena directly. He hurls his spear at her aegis — the one shield even Zeus' thunderbolt cannot pierce — and Athena, picking up a great stone, strikes him on the neck, "loosing his limbs" (020-043). Aphrodite leads him from the field, echoing her earlier rescue of the wounded Aeneas. The pattern is complete: every time Ares fights Athena, he loses.

> — Athena, pretending to counsel Ares while preparing to attack him (005-034)

> "You are the most hateful to me of all the gods on Olympus, for you ever love strife and wars and battles. You have your mother's unmanageable and wilful temper — it is all I can do to control her."
> — Zeus rebuking Ares, Book 5 (005-328)

## Relationships

- **Zeus (father):** Zeus' relationship with Ares is defined by contempt tempered with paternal obligation. "You are the most hateful to me of all the gods" (005-328) is the harshest sentence any Olympian receives from Zeus, yet Zeus immediately orders Ares healed — because he is his son. The tension between moral condemnation and familial duty is the core of their dynamic.

- **Hera (mother):** Ares inherits his temperament from Hera, according to Zeus (005-328), and Hera seems to share Zeus' low opinion of their son. When Hera and Athena prepare to aid the Achaeans, they pointedly do not recruit Ares.

- **Athena (antagonist):** The opposition between Athena and Ares structures the Iliad's theology of war. Athena represents war governed by intelligence, strategy, and purpose; Ares represents war as mindless destruction. Athena defeats Ares in every encounter (Books 5 and 20), and the poem leaves no doubt about which model of warfare it endorses.

- **Aphrodite (ally and lover):** Ares and Aphrodite share an affinity — both are gods of the passions, both fight on the Trojan side, and both are wounded by Diomedes and driven from the battlefield. In later tradition (not Homeric), they are lovers; the Iliad hints at this connection when Aphrodite leads the wounded Ares from the field in Book 20 (020-043).

- **Diomedes (mortal nemesis):** Diomedes, empowered by Athena, is the mortal who wounds Ares — a feat no other mortal achieves in the Iliad. The wounding is a theological demonstration: the right mortal, guided by the right god, can defeat even the embodiment of war.

## Death or Fate

As a god, Ares is immortal, but the Iliad repeatedly strips him of dignity. He is wounded (Book 5), rebuked (Book 5), thwarted (Book 15), and knocked down (Book 20). No other god suffers so many humiliations, and the pattern is not random: the poem systematically diminishes Ares because the values he represents — violence for its own sake, rage without purpose — are antithetical to the heroic code the Iliad celebrates. The true Homeric warrior fights for honour, for comrades, for kleos; Ares fights because fighting is what he is. The poem cannot kill him, so it does the next worst thing: it makes him ridiculous.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler's "Mars" for Ares follows the Roman convention, but the difference between the Greek Ares and the Roman Mars is profound. Mars was a major Roman deity — father of Romulus and Remus, god of agriculture as well as war, a figure of national dignity. Homer's Ares is none of these things. He is a figure of almost comic brutality, defeated and humiliated at every turn.

The wounding of Ares in Book 5 is unique in epic — a god wounded by a mortal, and not just wounded but sent fleeing with a bellow that shakes the battlefield. The scene has been interpreted variously as comic relief (the god of war routed by a man), theological allegory (wisdom defeating brute force), and political commentary (the ascendancy of Athena's Athens over Ares' more primitive cult centres).

Ares' Homeric epithet *brotoloigos* (βροτολοιγός, "bane of men") is one of the most negative divine epithets in the poem. Where Zeus is "father of gods and men" and Athena is "flashing-eyed," Ares is "murderous," "blood-stained," "bane of men." The epithets encode a moral judgment: Ares is what war would be without the civilising constraints of honour, strategy, and justice.
