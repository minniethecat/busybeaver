# Glaucus

Glaucus, son of Hippolochus and grandson of the Corinthian hero Bellerophon, is co-commander of the Lycian contingent alongside [[sarpedon|Sarpedon]] in the Trojan alliance. He appears in four major episodes in the Iliad, and his narrative arc traces the costs of fighting far from home: from the noble idealism of the Diomedes encounter in Book 6, through the rousing call to valour in Book 12, to the grief and desperate prayer of Book 16, and finally to the bitter rebuke of Book 17. The Diomedes episode — in which Glaucus exchanges his golden armour, worth a hundred oxen, for Diomedes' bronze, worth nine — is one of the most celebrated scenes in the epic, simultaneously a testament to the sanctity of guest-friendship (*xenia*) and a wry comment on the irrationality that even the gods can induce in men.

## Identity

Glaucus is introduced as the grandson of [[bellerophon|Bellerophon]], the Corinthian hero who tamed Pegasus and slew the Chimera (006-131). His lineage, recounted at length in Book 6, traces through Hippolochus back to Sisyphus of Ephyra (Corinth). This genealogy serves both as a narrative device — it establishes the hereditary guest-friendship between Glaucus and Diomedes — and as a thematic marker: Glaucus, like many Trojan allies, fights for a city that is not his own, motivated by ties of honour rather than homeland defence.

The Lycians, a people from south-western Anatolia "by the eddying waters of the Xanthus" (012-268), are among the most distinguished of Troy's allies. Glaucus shares command of the Lycian contingent with Sarpedon, a son of Zeus (002-719). Their partnership — Sarpedon the demigod prince and Glaucus the mortal captain — embodies the Iliad's pattern of complementary warrior pairs.

Butler's translation preserves the patronymic formula "Glaucus son of Hippolochus" as a recurring epithet, mirroring the Homeric oral-formulaic style.

## Role in the Epic

Glaucus fights in the Lycian vanguard throughout the middle and later books, but his most memorable moments occur off the battlefield — in conversation, prayer, and rebuke.

- **Book 6 — The Golden Armour:** When Glaucus and Diomedes meet in the no-man's-land between the armies, Diomedes asks his lineage, wary of fighting a god. Glaucus responds with the full genealogy of Bellerophon — one of the longest embedded narratives in the Iliad (006-101–006-131). Diomedes recognises Glaucus as a hereditary guest-friend: his grandfather Oeneus once entertained Bellerophon for twenty days (006-131). The two warriors descend from their chariots, clasp hands, and pledge to avoid each other in battle. Then — in a detail that has delighted readers for millennia — Zeus "made Glaucus take leave of his wits, for he exchanged golden armour for bronze, the worth of a hundred head of cattle for the worth of nine" (006-199). The scene is at once a celebration of *xenia*'s power to transcend war and a Homeric wink at the folly that even noble sentiment can produce.

- **Book 12 — The Call to Valour:** As the Trojans assault the Achaean wall, Sarpedon turns to Glaucus and delivers the Iliad's most concentrated expression of the heroic code (012-268). "Glaucus, why in Lycia do we receive especial honour as regards our place at table? ... It becomes us, therefore, to take our stand at the head of all the Lycians." Sarpedon's speech — that privilege entails obligation, that honour must be earned in battle, and that death is inescapable — defines the *noblesse oblige* that governs the Homeric hero. Glaucus heeds the call without hesitation: "the pair forthwith led on the host of Lycians" (012-284).

- **Book 16 — Grief and Prayer:** When Sarpedon falls to Patroclus, Glaucus is overwhelmed with grief — and helpless. He has been wounded by Teucer's arrow and cannot fight (016-459). In one of the Iliad's most moving prayers, he calls on Apollo across the distance from Troy to Lycia: "Hear me, O king ... I have a grievous wound; my hand is aching with pain ... heal me of my wound, ease my pain and grant me strength both to cheer on the Lycians and to fight along with them round the body of him who has fallen" (016-463). Apollo answers immediately, staunching the blood and filling Glaucus with renewed strength. His first act, healed, is to rally the Lycian captains and then to seek out Hector.

- **Book 17 — The Rebuke:** Finding Hector, Glaucus unleashes a torrent of reproach (017-110). He accuses Hector of abandoning Sarpedon — "at once your guest and comrade in arms" — to the dogs. He threatens that the Lycians will sail home if this is how Troy repays its allies (017-133). The speech is remarkable as one of the few instances in the Iliad where an ally openly rebukes a Trojan prince. It exposes the fragility of the alliance and the moral cost of Hector's leadership failures.

## Notable Speeches

> "Glaucus, why in Lycia do we receive especial honour as regards our place at table? Why are the choicest portions served us and our cups kept brimming, and why do men look up to us as though we were gods? ... My good friend, if, when we were once out of this fight, we could escape old age and death thenceforward and forever, I should neither press forward myself nor bid you do so, but death in ten thousand shapes hangs ever over our heads, and no man can elude him; therefore let us go forward and either win glory for ourselves, or yield it to another."
> — Sarpedon to Glaucus, Book 12 (012-268)

> "Hear me O king from your seat, may be in the rich land of Lycia, or may be in Troy, for in all places you can hear the prayer of one who is in distress, as I now am. I have a grievous wound; my hand is aching with pain, there is no staunching the blood, and my whole arm drags by reason of my hurt, so that I cannot grasp my sword nor go among my foes and fight them, though our prince, Jove's son Sarpedon, is slain."
> — Glaucus praying to Apollo, Book 16 (016-463)

> "Hector, you make a brave show, but in fight you are sadly wanting. A runaway like yourself has no claim to so great a reputation. ... If the Lycians will listen to me, they will go home and leave Troy to its fate."
> — Glaucus rebuking Hector, Book 17 (017-133)

## Relationships

- **Sarpedon (co-commander and friend):** Glaucus' defining relationship is with Sarpedon, the son of Zeus and his fellow Lycian commander. Their bond structures Glaucus' entire narrative arc: he fights alongside Sarpedon in Books 6 and 12, is devastated by his death in Book 16, and defends his body and memory in Book 17. Sarpedon's dying words — "Glaucus, my brother, hero among heroes" (016-445) — captures the fraternal intensity of their partnership.

- **Diomedes (hereditary guest-friend):** The encounter between Glaucus and Diomedes in Book 6 is the Iliad's most famous illustration of *xenia* — the sacred bond of guest-friendship that transcends even war. Diomedes discovers that his grandfather Oeneus once hosted Glaucus' grandfather Bellerophon. The two men refuse to fight, exchange armour, and part as friends. The scene complicates any simple reading of the war as Greeks-versus-Trojans: bonds of kinship and hospitality cut across battle lines.

- **Hector (prince and object of reproach):** Glaucus' relationship with Hector evolves from alliance to disillusionment. In Book 16, he seeks Hector's help to recover Sarpedon's body; in Book 17, having seen Hector's failure, he rebukes him in the harshest terms any ally uses in the poem. The rebuke is both personal (grief for Sarpedon) and political (a warning that Troy's allies may abandon the cause).

- **Apollo (divine healer):** Glaucus' prayer to Apollo in Book 16 (016-463) is answered immediately — Apollo heals his wound and restores his strength. This divine intervention, rare in its directness, underscores Glaucus' piety and his importance to the Lycian contingent.

## Death or Fate

The Iliad does not narrate Glaucus' death. He survives the poem, though his narrative arc ends on a note of bitterness — his rebuke of Hector in Book 17 is his last major appearance. Later tradition (not Homeric) records that Glaucus was killed by Ajax the Greater in the fighting over Achilles' body after the Iliad's close. Within the poem, however, his fate is uncertain: he is last seen fighting over Patroclus' body, aligned with the Lycians who remain at Troy despite his threat to withdraw them.

## Scholarly Notes

The Glaucus-Diomedes episode (Book 6) is one of the most analysed passages in Homeric scholarship. The exchange of armour — gold for bronze, a hundred oxen's worth for nine — has been interpreted in various ways: as evidence of Zeus-induced folly (*atē*), as a ritualised gesture in which the inequality of the exchange symbolises the inequality of the guest-host relationship, and as a Homeric joke at the expense of a non-Greek. The episode also contains one of the longest embedded narratives in the poem — the story of Bellerophon, whose tragic arc (rise, fall, wandering in madness) prefigures the fates of many Iliadic heroes.

Glaucus' name (Γλαῦκος, "gleaming" or "grey-blue") may be etymologically related to *glaukōpis*, the epithet of Athena. The Lycians were a historical people of south-western Anatolia, and the Iliad's portrayal of their special status — honoured with choice portions, owning rich estates by the Xanthus — may preserve a memory of Lycian prosperity in the Late Bronze Age.

The prayer to Apollo in Book 16 (016-463) is a textbook example of Homeric prayer structure: invocation, claim to divine favour, specific petition. Apollo's immediate response — healing the wound, staunching the blood — is one of the most direct divine answers to prayer in the poem.
