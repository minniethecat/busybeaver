# Mount Ida

Mount Ida dominates the landscape of the Troad. Rising south of Troy, its peak Gargarus provides the vantage point from which Zeus surveys the battlefield. Ida is the setting for two of the epic's most significant divine episodes: Zeus's isolation from the other gods as he watches the battle unfold, and Hera's seduction of Zeus — aided by the enchanted girdle of Aphrodite and the assistance of Sleep — to distract him while Poseidon aids the Achaeans.

## Geography

Homer calls Ida "many-valleyed" and "mother of wild beasts," emphasizing its wild, untamed character. Its peak, Gargarus, pierces the clouds and offers a clear view of the entire Trojan plain — making it the ideal divine observation post. The mountain is associated with Zeus, who has a sacred precinct on Gargarus, and with the goddess Cybele in later tradition.

## The Seduction of Zeus (Book XIV)

Hera's seduction of Zeus on Mount Ida is one of the epic's most remarkable episodes. Desperate to aid the Achaeans while Zeus's decree favors the Trojans, Hera borrows Aphrodite's enchanted girdle (which makes its wearer irresistible), seduces Zeus on the mountain peak, and has Sleep cast a deep slumber over him after their lovemaking. While Zeus sleeps, Poseidon freely intervenes on the Achaeans' behalf, turning the tide of battle. When Zeus awakens and realizes the deception, his rage is terrible — but the Achaeans have already been saved.
