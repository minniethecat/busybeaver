# Apollo

Apollo, the son of Zeus (Jove) and Leto (Latona), is the god of archery, prophecy, music, and plague. In the Iliad, he is the primary divine protector of Troy, opposing the Achaeans with his silver bow and his gift of prophecy. The poem opens with his intervention: when [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] refuses to ransom Chryseis, the daughter of Apollo's priest Chryses, Apollo descends from Olympus "with his bow and quiver" and rains plague upon the Achaean camp for nine days (001-134–001-148). His epithets include "god of the silver bow," "far-darter" (*hekaergos*), and "Lycian Apollo." In Butler's translation, he retains his Greek name throughout.

## Identity

Apollo is one of the most versatile Olympian deities. He is the archer whose arrows bring sudden death, the prophet who speaks through oracles, the musician whose lyre delights the gods, and the healer who can also send plague. In the Iliad, his martial aspect dominates — he fights for Troy, protecting [[hector|Hector]], deceiving [[achilles|Achilles]] on the battlefield, and punishing mortal arrogance.

His title "Lycian" connects him to the region of Lycia in Asia Minor, where he had a major cult centre. The epithet *hekaergos* (ἑκάεργος), "far-darter" or "far-working," captures the lethal range of his bow — an image of divine power that strikes from an unbridgeable distance. He is sometimes called Phoebus ("bright" or "pure"), a name that associates him with light and clarity, contrasting with the darkness of the plague he sends.

## Role in the Epic

Apollo's actions shape the Iliad from its opening lines to its final reconciliation.

- **Book 1 — The Plague:** When Chryses, Apollo's priest, comes to ransom his daughter Chryseis and is dishonoured by Agamemnon, the old man prays to Apollo for vengeance. Apollo hears him and descends from Olympus "with his bow and quiver, the arrows rattling on his shoulders in his rage" (001-134). For nine days, his arrows — interpreted as plague — rain upon the Achaean camp, killing men and animals in heaps. The plague only ends when Chryseis is returned and a hecatomb is sacrificed in Apollo's honour (001-226). This episode establishes Apollo as a god who punishes human arrogance (*hubris*) with terrifying efficiency.

- **Book 16 — The Death of Patroclus:** Apollo delivers the decisive blow that allows Hector to kill Patroclus. Striking Patroclus from behind, Apollo shatters his helmet, breaks his spear, and strips his armour, leaving him "dazed and defenceless" (016-330). This divine intervention is critical — without Apollo's intervention, Patroclus might have breached Troy itself. It is one of the poem's most explicit demonstrations that mortal combat is always at the mercy of divine will.

- **Book 20 — Saving Aeneas:** When Achilles encounters Aeneas on the battlefield, Apollo warns Aeneas not to fight him — "Achilles is a greater man than you, and dearer to the gods" (020-001). When Aeneas nevertheless engages, Apollo spirits him away from certain death, preserving Aeneas for his destined future beyond the Iliad.

- **Book 22 — The Chase:** As Achilles pursues Hector around the walls of Troy, Apollo runs beside Hector, breathing strength into him (022-001). This divine aid prolongs the chase, but when Zeus' golden scales tip against Hector, Apollo abandons him. Hector realizes: "the gods have called me to my death" (022-130). Apollo's withdrawal is as decisive as his intervention.

- **Book 24 — Divine Protection:** After Hector's death, Apollo protects his body from Achilles' desecration. He shrouds the corpse in a golden aegis, keeping it intact despite Achilles' attempts to mutilate it by dragging it behind his chariot (024-001–024-030). When Apollo speaks in council against Achilles' brutality, he frames his argument as a matter of cosmic decency, and Zeus concurs.

## Relationships

- **Zeus (father):** Apollo is Zeus' son and trusted agent on the Trojan side. Zeus dispatches him to protect Hector's body in Book 24 and to guide Priam through the Achaean camp. Unlike Athena, who serves Zeus' will through strategy, Apollo serves through direct physical intervention.

- **Hector (protégé):** Apollo is Hector's divine patron, strengthening him for combat and protecting him in crisis. Their relationship is mutual — Hector honours Apollo with sacrifices, and Apollo shields him even beyond death. When Apollo finally abandons Hector in Book 22, it is because he must — the scales of fate have spoken.

- **Achilles (antagonist):** Apollo's opposition to Achilles is one of the Iliad's central divine-mortal conflicts. He saves Aeneas from Achilles (Book 20), aids Hector against him (Book 22), and condemns his treatment of Hector's body in the divine council (Book 24). Their antagonism is impersonal — it reflects Apollo's role as Troy's protector, not personal hatred.

- **Chryses (priest):** The relationship between Apollo and his priest Chryses opens the epic. When Agamemnon dishonours Chryses, Apollo responds immediately with plague. This demonstrates that the gods defend not only their own honour but also that of their mortal servants.

- **Artemis (sister):** Apollo's twin sister Artemis appears only briefly in the Iliad (notably in Book 21, where Hera boxes her ears), but their paired identity as divine archers — Apollo of the bow that brings plague, Artemis of the bow of sudden death — is a persistent mythological theme.

## Death or Fate

As an immortal god, Apollo does not face death, but his role in the Iliad concerns the relationship between divine power and mortal destiny. He kills effortlessly and from afar (the plague arrows), protects whom he chooses (Aeneas, Hector), and abandons when fate demands (Hector in Book 22). The poem presents Apollo as both terrifying and magnificent — the god whose arrows bring plague is also the god of the lyre who delights Olympus with music (001-331). This duality is central to his identity: the power to destroy and the power to create are aspects of the same divine nature.

## Scholarly Notes

Apollo's role as the bringer of plague in Book 1 reflects the ancient Greek understanding of disease as divine punishment. The image of Apollo shooting arrows that cause plague is consistent with the belief that the god could send and also avert epidemics — his son Asclepius became the god of medicine.

The epithet *Lykeios* (Λύκειος), "Lycian," connects Apollo to the region of Lycia in southwestern Anatolia, where he had a major cult centre. The Lycian archer Pandarus, who wounds Menelaus in Book 4, prays specifically to "Lycian Apollo" (004-079), suggesting a regional cult connection that Homer's audience would have recognized.

Apollo's inviolable protection of Hector's body in Book 24 is thematically significant: it asserts that even in a world of total war, some boundaries must hold. The gods' insistence that Achilles return Hector's body elevates the poem's conclusion from revenge tragedy to a meditation on shared mortality and the limits of righteous anger.
