# Diomedes

## Identity

Diomedes, son of Tydeus (and thus frequently called Tydides), is the king of Argos and one of the youngest yet most formidable of the Achaean commanders. He is introduced in the Catalog of Ships as the leader of the Argive contingent, commanding eighty ships (002-327). In stature and experience he is described as second only to Achilles among the Achaean warriors, yet he is distinguished from Achilles by his discipline, his respect for divine authority, and his willingness to accept counsel. Where Achilles is consumed by private wrath, Diomedes is the model of the cooperative hero who places the needs of the army above personal honor.

His epithets emphasize his martial character: "Diomed of the loud war-cry" (002-455) and "noble Diomed" (004-287). He is the son of Tydeus, one of the Seven Against Thebes, and carries his father's heroic legacy as both inspiration and burden — a theme that runs through his interactions, particularly in his exchange with Glaucus and in his response to Agamemnon's rebuke.

## Role in the Epic

Diomedes' presence spans the entire epic, and his contributions to the Achaean cause are matched by few others.

- **Book 4 — Mustering and Rebuke:** When Agamemnon reviews his troops, he finds Diomedes standing by his chariot (004-287) and attempts to shame him into battle by invoking the greater prowess of his father Tydeus. Unlike Achilles, Diomedes accepts the rebuke with disciplined silence (004-314), a moment that contrasts the two warriors' temperaments.

- **Book 5 — The Aristeia of Diomedes:** This is Diomedes' supreme moment. The "exploits of Diomed" (005-002) dominate the entire book. Blessed with extraordinary strength and the ability to distinguish gods from mortals on the battlefield by Athena, he becomes an unstoppable force. He is wounded by Pandarus (005-030) but fights on with even greater fury. He slays Pandarus, drives Aeneas from the field, and — in an unprecedented act — wounds Aphrodite herself as she tries to rescue her son Aeneas. Later, with Athena's explicit encouragement, he wounds Ares (Mars) in the belly, sending the war god fleeing back to Olympus in pain (005-008). No other mortal hero in the Iliad achieves so much against the gods themselves.

- **Book 6 — Glaucus and the Exchange of Armor:** In one of the most famous episodes of the epic, Diomedes encounters Glaucus the Lycian on the battlefield (006-002). Before fighting, Diomedes asks his opponent's lineage, and upon discovering that their grandfathers were guest-friends, he proposes they avoid combat and exchange armor as a token of friendship. The episode is famous for its "gold for bronze" exchange — Glaucus, his wits taken by Zeus (006-199), gives his golden armor worth a hundred oxen for Diomedes' bronze, worth nine.

- **Book 10 — The Night Raid (Doloneia):** Diomedes volunteers for a night reconnaissance mission alongside Odysseus. The two capture and interrogate the Trojan spy Dolon (010-257), then infiltrate the camp of the newly arrived Thracian king Rhesus, killing him and his men and stealing his magnificent horses. The episode showcases Diomedes' versatility as a warrior — effective not only in open battle but in stealth and cunning.

- **Books 7–9 — Counsel and Embassy:** Diomedes consistently speaks with frankness in counsel. When Agamemnon proposes abandoning the war in Book 9, Diomedes rebukes him publicly, declaring that he and Sthenelus will fight on alone if necessary. His moral courage is as notable as his physical prowess.

## Notable Speeches

> Diomed made no answer, for he was shamed before the rebuke of the king.
> — Diomedes accepting Agamemnon's harsh words in silence (004-314)

> Diomed, son of Tydeus, man after my own heart, I see you.
> — Athena's words of favor, singling out Diomedes (005-213)

> The exploits of Diomed, who, though wounded by Pandarus, continued to fight.
> — The argument of Book 5, setting the stage for his aristeia (005-002)

Diomedes' speech to Glaucus, proposing friendship across enemy lines, is one of the most humane moments in the epic — a recognition that the obligations of *xenia* (guest-friendship) transcend even the divisions of war.

## Relationships

- **Athena (divine patron):** Diomedes is Athena's favorite among the Achaeans after Achilles. She grants him the power to distinguish gods from mortals on the battlefield and personally drives his chariot during his assault on Ares. Their relationship represents the ideal of mortal-divine cooperation in Homeric thought.
- **Odysseus (companion in arms):** Diomedes' most frequent partner in dangerous undertakings. Their night raid in Book 10 is a model of complementary skills — Diomedes' raw fighting power paired with Odysseus' craft and persuasion.
- **Glaucus (guest-friend):** The exchange of armor between Diomedes and the Lycian Glaucus (Book 6) is one of the epic's most discussed episodes, embodying the tension between the warrior code and the older obligations of guest-friendship.
- **Sthenelus (charioteer and companion):** Sthenelus, son of Capaneus, is Diomedes' loyal charioteer and companion (005-212). He fights alongside Diomedes and shares his frankness in counsel.
- **Agamemnon (commander):** Diomedes' relationship with Agamemnon is complex — he accepts the king's rebukes with discipline but is also willing to speak frankly against the king's defeatism when the situation demands it.

## Death or Fate

The Iliad does not narrate Diomedes' death. According to later tradition, he survived the Trojan War and returned home safely to Argos, only to find his wife unfaithful. He subsequently left Greece and founded cities in Italy, becoming a culture hero of the Italian peninsula. In Homer's Odyssey, he is one of the few Achaean heroes to enjoy a safe homecoming (*nostos*). His survival stands in marked contrast to the fates of Achilles, Agamemnon, Ajax, and many others, marking him as uniquely favored or wise among the Greek commanders.

## Scholarly Notes

Diomedes is increasingly recognized by scholars as the Iliad's most complete hero — one who combines martial excellence, rhetorical skill, religious piety, and respect for the collective good. His aristeia in Book 5 is the most sustained and divinely charged of any warrior's moment of glory in the poem. Where Achilles embodies heroic individualism taken to its destructive extreme, Diomedes represents the possibility of heroism within the framework of social and divine order. His willingness to be shamed by Agamemnon (in contrast to Achilles' furious retaliation) and his recognition of guest-friendship with Glaucus mark him as a figure of unusual moral complexity for epic poetry.

The "gold for bronze" exchange with Glaucus has generated extensive scholarly discussion. Some read it as a comic episode — Glaucus temporarily "loses his wits" — while others see it as a profound statement about the incommensurability of material value and human connection.
