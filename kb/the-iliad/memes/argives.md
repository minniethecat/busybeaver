# Argives

The Argives (Ἀργεῖοι) are one of the three principal Homeric names for the Greek forces at Troy, alongside "Achaeans" and "Danaans." While the term can refer specifically to the contingent from Argos and the Argolid led by Diomed, Homer most often uses it as a general designation for the entire Greek host (001-145).

## Identity

The name "Argives" derives from Argos, the principal city of the Argolid plain and one of the most important Mycenaean centres. In Homeric usage, "Argives" functions interchangeably with "Achaeans" for the entire Greek army, though it carries a particular association with the Peloponnesian contingents. Homer shifts between "Argives," "Achaeans," and "Danaans" throughout the poem for metrical variety, without indicating any meaningful distinction in the identity of the forces described (003-069).

## Leadership

The Argive contingent proper is commanded by Diomed, son of Tydeus, who is described as the preeminent Argive champion and the equal of gods in battle (005-011). At the supreme command level, the Argives — like all Greeks — answer to Agamemnon, who addresses the assembled host as "Argives" when rallying them for battle (004-187) or rebuking their hesitation: "Argives, cowardly miserable wretches" (004-193). The term "princes and councillors of the Argives" refers collectively to the Greek war council (002-065).

## Role in the War

The Argives are present throughout the *Iliad* as the attacking force:

- **Book 1**: Apollo's plague arrow strikes the Argives first, and "the people died thick" as a result of Agamemnon's offence to Chryses (001-145). Achilles laments that the god "brought sorrow upon the Argives" (001-195).
- **Book 2**: Agamemnon, leaning on his sceptre, addresses the Argives (002-090). The Argives nearly abandon the war after Agamemnon's test of morale, and "would have returned after a fashion that was not fated" had Hera not intervened (002-125). When Odysseus rallies them, the Argives "raised a shout, till the ships echoed" (002-268), and they would have set sail "to bring death and destruction to the Trojans" (002-281).
- **Book 3**: Menelaus calls out "Hold, Argives, shoot not" as he prepares for single combat with Paris (003-069).
- **Book 4**: Agamemnon cheers the Argives on: "slacken not one whit" (004-187). Despite heavy losses, the Argives "stood compact and firm" against Trojan assaults, and their enemies noted with frustration that "their skins are not stone nor iron" (004-409).
- **Book 5**: The Argives fight under Diomed's inspired leadership, and Athena grants him preeminence "that he might excel all the other Argives" (005-011). The Argive line holds its ground even as the Trojans press hard (005-419).

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

The Argive contingent proper, under Diomed, is listed in the Catalogue of Ships as contributing eighty ships from Argos, Tiryns, Hermione, Asine, Troezen, Eionae, Epidaurus, Aegina, and Mases (Book 2). The broader term "Argives" encompasses the entire twenty-nine contingents.
