## Identity

Phoenix is the son of Amyntor, son of Ormenus (009-368), a figure whose identity is defined by three intertwined roles: exiled son, surrogate father, and persuasive counsellor. Having fled his homeland of Hellas after a curse from his father, he was taken in by Peleus, king of Phthia, who "made me welcome and treated me as a father treats an only son" (009-392). Peleus "made me rich and set me over much people, establishing me on the borders of Phthia where I was chief ruler over the Dolopians" (009-395). In return, Phoenix became the tutor and foster-father of the young Achilles, a bond that shapes his every action in the *Iliad*.

Phoenix is described repeatedly as "the old knight Phoenix" (016-173, 019-246), and Achilles addresses him as "old friend and father" (009-496). His age and the paternal role he assumes towards Achilles are central to his character — he is the voice of experience, of memory, and of the obligations that bind generations. Athena herself takes "the form and voice of Phoenix" (017-458) when she speaks to Menelaus, a mark of the trust and authority Phoenix's presence commands.

## Role in the Epic

Phoenix appears exclusively in the latter half of the *Iliad*, his presence concentrated around moments that concern Achilles. He commands the fourth company of the Myrmidons (016-173), but his martial role is secondary to his rhetorical and emotional function.

**The Embassy to Achilles (Book IX):** Phoenix's defining moment is his speech during the embassy to Achilles in Book IX, where he accompanies Odysseus and Ajax to persuade Achilles to return to battle. His is the longest and most elaborate speech of the three ambassadors. Unlike Odysseus, who appeals to pragmatism and the offered gifts, or Ajax, who appeals to comradeship and honour, Phoenix deploys an autobiographical narrative. He tells the story of his own youthful rage against his father Amyntor (009-368), creating a parallel between himself and Achilles: both are men who nursed a grievous anger against those who should have honoured them.

Phoenix frames his entire life story as a cautionary example. He recounts how his mother, wronged by Amyntor's infatuation with a concubine, "prayed me without ceasing to lie with the woman myself, that so she hate my father" (009-371). When Amyntor discovered the deed, he "cursed me bitterly, calling the dread Erinyes to witness. He prayed that no son of mine might ever sit upon my knees" (009-374). The gods fulfilled the curse. Phoenix fled, breaking through locked doors and climbing walls to escape his kinsmen's guard — "I broke through the closed doors of my room, and climbed the wall of the outer court" (009-388) — and found refuge with Peleus.

It was Peleus who entrusted the infant Achilles to Phoenix: "It was I, Achilles, who had the making of you; I loved you with all my heart" (009-397). Phoenix recalls feeding the child, cutting up his food, and holding the wine-cup to his lips: "Many a time have you slobbered your wine in baby helplessness over my shirt" (009-401). Having no son of his own — Amyntor's curse having taken effect — Phoenix "made a son of you, Achilles, that in my hour of need you might protect me" (009-404).

He then delivers the famous parable of the Prayers: "For prayers are as daughters to great Jove; halt, wrinkled, with eyes askance, they follow in the footsteps of sin, who, being fierce and fleet of foot, leaves them far behind him" (009-410). The allegory personifies prayers as lame, aged women who pursue Sin (Ate) across the earth, healing those who receive them and punishing those who reject them. He urges Achilles: "Therefore, Achilles, give these daughters of Jove reverence" (009-420).

The speech culminates in the story of Meleager (009-435), an Aetolian hero who, like Achilles, withdrew from battle in anger. Meleager "stayed at home with Cleopatra, nursing the anger which he felt by reason of the curses of his mother" (009-461). Only when his city was burning and his wife pleaded with him — "her knees and her bosom was wet with tears" (009-466) — did Meleager relent and return to battle, but too late to receive the gifts he had been offered. Phoenix concludes: "when he heard all this, his heart was touched, and he went" (009-486); but he warns Achilles not to repeat Meleager's mistake of returning "without gifts" (009-493).

Achilles is moved but unmoved: "Phoenix, old friend and father, I have no need of such honour. I have honour from Jove himself" (009-496). Yet he asks Phoenix to stay the night — "the old man Phoenix stayed where he was to sleep, for so Achilles would have it, that he might go home with him" (009-560) — a testament to their bond even in disagreement.

**At Patroclus' Departure (Book XVI):** Phoenix commands the fourth company of Myrmidons as they march out under Patroclus' leadership (016-173). His presence among the captains underscores the generational structure of Achilles' forces: the young warriors fight while the aged counsellor watches and waits.

**Athena's Disguise (Book XVII):** In a striking detail, when Athena descends to rally the Greeks around Patroclus' body, "she took the form and voice of Phoenix" (017-458) to speak to Menelaus. This divine impersonation suggests that Phoenix represents a particular kind of persuasive authority — the wise elder whose words carry weight precisely because of his age and experience.

**The Reconciliation (Book XIX):** Phoenix is present at the assembly where Achilles and Agamemnon reconcile, listed among the counsellors: "Nestor, Idomeneus, and the knight Phoenix" (019-246).

**Funeral Games (Book XXIII):** At the funeral games for Patroclus, Achilles stations Phoenix "as umpire, to note the running, and report truly" (023-316). The role of umpire — a position requiring impartial judgement and respected authority — is Phoenix's final appearance in the epic, a fitting capstone for a character defined by counsel and trustworthiness.

## Notable Speeches

> "It was I, Achilles, who had the making of you; I loved you with all my heart: for you would eat neither at home nor when you had gone out elsewhere, till I had first set you upon my knees, cut up the dainty morsel that you were to eat, and held the wine-cup to your lips." (009-397)

This recollection of Achilles' infancy is the most intimate memory in the *Iliad*, grounding Phoenix's rhetorical appeal in the physical reality of fatherhood.

> "For prayers are as daughters to great Jove; halt, wrinkled, with eyes askance, they follow in the footsteps of sin, who, being fierce and fleet of foot, leaves them far behind him." (009-410)

The allegory of the Prayers (Litai) is one of the most philosophically rich passages in Homer, personifying the mechanism of moral repair.

> "Therefore, Achilles, give these daughters of Jove reverence." (009-420)

With this line, Phoenix pivots from myth to direct appeal, urging Achilles to accept the embassy's gifts and return to battle.

## Relationships

**Achilles:** Phoenix's relationship with Achilles defines his entire role in the epic. He raised Achilles from infancy (009-397), and Achilles calls him "old friend and father" (009-496). Even when Achilles rejects the embassy's plea, he insists that Phoenix stay the night and return to Phthia with him (009-560) — Achilles cannot bring himself to send Phoenix away. Athena's later decision to take Phoenix's form (017-458) reinforces the unique persuasive authority Phoenix holds over Achilles' world.

**Peleus:** Peleus is Phoenix's benefactor, the king who "made me welcome and treated me as a father treats an only son" (009-392). Peleus entrusted Achilles to Phoenix's care, binding the two men in a shared obligation to the house of Peleus. Phoenix refers to Peleus as "your father Peleus" (009-361), positioning himself as Peleus' agent in Achilles' life.

**Amyntor:** Phoenix's biological father, whose curse — that no son of Phoenix would ever sit upon his knees — was fulfilled by the gods (009-374). This curse is the foundational trauma of Phoenix's life, driving him into exile and making his relationship with Achilles all the more precious.

**Patroclus:** Phoenix commands a Myrmidon company alongside Patroclus (016-173). After Patroclus' death, Athena-in-Phoenix's-form rallies Menelaus to defend the body (017-458), indirectly honouring the bond between the old tutor and the young warrior.

**Odysseus and Ajax:** As fellow ambassadors in Book IX, Odysseus and Ajax represent different rhetorical modes — cunning and comradeship — against which Phoenix's autobiographical appeal stands in relief.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Phoenix's death. His final appearance at Patroclus' funeral games as umpire (023-316) casts him in a role of authority and continuity. The epic's silence on his fate leaves open the question raised by the embassy itself: did Phoenix ever return to Phthia with Achilles, or did he die at Troy? The ambiguity is fitting for a character whose role is to embody the claims of the past upon the present — his future, like the outcome of his counsel, remains unresolved within the poem's temporal frame.

## Scholarly Notes

Phoenix is unique among the Homeric characters in his combination of roles: warrior, counsellor, surrogate father, and autobiographer. His speech in Book IX has been extensively studied as a masterpiece of Homeric rhetoric. The autobiographical digression — Phoenix's own story of exile — is unprecedented in the epic: no other character in the *Iliad* narrates their own past at such length. Scholars have noted the structural parallel between Phoenix's story and Achilles' own situation (both are men dishonoured by a figure of authority), as well as the irony that Phoenix's tale of successful reconciliation — he found a new home with Peleus — fails to persuade Achilles to reconcile with Agamemnon.

The allegory of the Prayers (Litai, 009-410) has deep roots in Greek religious thought. The personification of prayers as "daughters of Jove" who follow after Sin (Ate) reflects a theology in which divine justice operates through delay rather than absence: the wrongdoer may outrun the consequences of his actions for a time, but the Prayers will eventually catch up. This allegory has been cited as evidence of an early Greek concept of moral causation.

The Meleager story (009-435) is Phoenix's second embedded narrative and has attracted scholarly attention as an example of Homeric paradeigma (exemplary myth). The Meleager tale parallels Achilles' situation — a hero withdraws from battle in anger, is offered gifts, refuses, and returns too late — while differing in crucial details that Phoenix manipulates for rhetorical effect. The fact that Achilles rejects the parallel (he dismisses the gifts as unnecessary, 009-496) suggests that Phoenix's rhetorical strategy, however artful, founders on Achilles' absolute valuation of honour over material compensation.

Phoenix's name may be connected to the mythological bird Phoenix, associated with renewal and rebirth — a fitting resonance for a character who "made" Achilles and whose own life was remade by Peleus after his exile.
