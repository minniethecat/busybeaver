# Dardanians

The Dardanians (Δάρδανοι) are the people of Dardania, a region in the Troad closely associated with Troy through shared descent from Dardanus. Led by Aeneas, son of Anchises and the goddess Aphrodite, they fight as a distinct contingent within the broader Trojan alliance and are frequently invoked in battle exhortations alongside the Trojans and Lycians (002-666).

## Identity

The Dardanians share a common ancestry with the Trojans: Dardanus was the founder of the Trojan royal line, father of Erichthonius, grandfather of Tros, and great-grandfather of Ilus (founder of Ilium) and Assaracus (ancestor of Aeneas). This genealogy places the Dardanians as a closely kindred people to the Trojans, explaining their integral role in the defence of Troy. In Homeric battle rhetoric, "Trojans, Lycians, and Dardanians" forms a standard tripartite address directed at the core defenders of the city (008-151, 015-361, 017-145).

## Leadership

"The Dardanians were led by brave Aeneas, whom Aphrodite bore to Anchises" (002-666). Aeneas is the son of a goddess and a mortal prince of the Dardanian line, making him second only to Hector in prominence among Troy's defenders. Throughout the *Iliad*, Aeneas is portrayed as a pious warrior who enjoys the special protection of the gods — Apollo, Aphrodite, and Poseidon all intervene to save him at critical moments — reflecting his destined role as the future ruler of the Trojan people beyond the fall of Troy.

## Role in the War

The Dardanians fight throughout the major battles of the *Iliad*:

- **Book 2**: The Dardanians are listed in the Trojan Catalogue under Aeneas, with Antenor's sons Archelochus and Acamas as subordinate commanders (002-666).
- **Book 3**: The Trojans, Dardanians, and allies are named as the defending force from whom "the victory" over Menelaus is momentarily snatched by Aphrodite's intervention (003-354).
- **Book 7**: The Dardanians are summoned to council, where Antenor urges the return of Helen to end the war, and Priam calls upon them to hear Idaeus's proposal of a truce (007-278, 007-330).
- **Book 8**: Hector addresses "Trojans, Lycians, and Dardanians" as he rallies the defence (008-151), and the three groups are named together again in Book 15 as Apollo rouses them to renewed assault (015-361, 015-413).
- **Book 13**: The Dardanians are described as "fighters in close combat" as they bear the brunt of the Achaean counter-attack around the ships (013-141).

The Dardanians' consistent grouping with Trojans and Lycians in formulaic battle addresses marks them as one of the three essential pillars of Troy's defensive force.

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

In the Trojan Catalogue (Book 2), the Dardanians are led by "brave Aeneas, whom Aphrodite bore to Anchises amid the spurs of Ida, a goddess mated with a mortal man," with Archelochus and Acamas, sons of Antenor, as co-commanders. The contingent is described but no specific ship or troop count is given, in keeping with the less quantitative style of the Trojan Catalogue compared to the Achaean list (002-666).
