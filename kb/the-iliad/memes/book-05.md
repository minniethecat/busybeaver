# Book V

[005-001]BOOK V.

[005-002]The exploits of Diomed, who, though wounded by Pandarus,

[005-003]continues fighting—He kills Pandarus and wounds AEneas—Venus

[005-004]rescues AEneas, but being wounded by Diomed, commits him to the

[005-005]care of Apollo and goes to [[mount-olympus|Olympus]], where she is tended by her

[005-006]mother Dione—Mars encourages the Trojans, and AEneas returns to

[005-007]the fight cured of his wound—Minerva and Juno help the Achaeans,

[005-008]and by the advice of the former Diomed wounds Mars, who returns

[005-009]to Olympus to get cured.

[005-010]Then Pallas Minerva put valour into the heart of Diomed, son of

[005-011]Tydeus, that he might excel all the other Argives, and cover

[005-012]himself with [[honor|glory]]. She made a stream of fire flare from his

[005-013]shield and helmet like the star that shines most brilliantly in

[005-014]summer after its bath in the waters of Oceanus—even such a fire

[005-015]did she kindle upon his head and shoulders as she bade him speed

[005-016]into the thickest hurly-burly of the fight.

[005-017]Now there was a certain rich and honourable man among the

[005-018]Trojans, priest of Vulcan, and his name was Dares. He had two

[005-019]sons, Phegeus and Idaeus, both of them skilled in all the arts of

[005-020]war. These two came forward from the main body of Trojans, and

[005-021]set upon Diomed, he being on foot, while they fought from their

[005-022]chariot. When they were close up to one another, Phegeus took aim

[005-023]first, but his spear went over Diomed’s left shoulder without

[005-024]hitting him. Diomed then threw, and his spear sped not in vain,

[005-025]for it hit Phegeus on the breast near the nipple, and he fell

[005-026]from his chariot. Idaeus did not dare to bestride his brother’s

[005-027]body, but sprang from the chariot and took to flight, or he would

[005-028]have shared his brother’s fate; whereon Vulcan saved him by

[005-029]wrapping him in a cloud of darkness, that his old father might

[005-030]not be utterly overwhelmed with grief; but the son of Tydeus

[005-031]drove off with the horses, and bade his followers take them to

[005-032]the [[achaean-camp|ships]]. The Trojans were scared when they saw the two sons of

[005-033]Dares, one of them in fright and the other lying dead by his

[005-034]chariot. Minerva, therefore, took Mars by the hand and said,

[005-035]“Mars, Mars, bane of men, blood-stained stormer of cities, may we

[005-036]not now leave the Trojans and Achaeans to fight it out, and see

[005-037]to which of the two Jove will vouchsafe the victory? Let us go

[005-038]away, and thus avoid his [[wrath|anger]].”

[005-039]So saying, she drew Mars out of the battle, and set him down upon

[005-040]the steep banks of the Scamander. Upon this the Danaans drove the

[005-041]Trojans back, and each one of their chieftains killed his man.

[005-042]First King [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] flung mighty Odius, captain of the Halizoni,

[005-043]from his chariot. The spear of Agamemnon caught him on the broad

[005-044]of his back, just as he was turning in flight; it struck him

[005-045]between the shoulders and went right through his chest, and his

[005-046]armour rang rattling round him as he fell heavily to the ground.

[005-047]Then Idomeneus killed Phaesus, son of Borus the Meonian, who had

[005-048]come from Varne. Mighty Idomeneus speared him on the right

[005-049]shoulder as he was mounting his chariot, and the darkness of

[005-050]death enshrouded him as he fell heavily from the car.

[005-051]The squires of Idomeneus spoiled him of his armour, while

[005-052]Menelaus, son of Atreus, killed Scamandrius the son of Strophius,

[005-053]a mighty huntsman and keen lover of the chase. Diana herself had

[005-054]taught him how to kill every kind of wild creature that is bred

[005-055]in mountain forests, but neither she nor his famed skill in

[005-056]archery could now save him, for the spear of Menelaus struck him

[005-057]in the back as he was flying; it struck him between the shoulders

[005-058]and went right through his chest, so that he fell headlong and

[005-059]his armour rang rattling round him.

[005-060]Meriones then killed Phereclus the son of Tecton, who was the son

[005-061]of Hermon, a man whose hand was skilled in all manner of cunning

[005-062]workmanship, for Pallas Minerva had dearly loved him. He it was

[005-063]that made the ships for [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]], which were the beginning of

[005-064]all mischief, and brought evil alike both on the Trojans and on

[005-065]Alexandrus himself; for he heeded not the decrees of heaven.

[005-066]Meriones overtook him as he was flying, and struck him on the

[005-067]right buttock. The point of the spear went through the bone into

[005-068]the bladder, and death came upon him as he cried aloud and fell

[005-069]forward on his knees.

[005-070]Meges, moreover, slew Pedaeus, son of Antenor, who, though he was

[005-071]a bastard, had been brought up by Theano as one of her own

[005-072]children, for the love she bore her husband. The son of Phyleus

[005-073]got close up to him and drove a spear into the nape of his neck:

[005-074]it went under his tongue all among his teeth, so he bit the cold

[005-075]bronze, and fell dead in the dust.

[005-076]And Eurypylus, son of Euaemon, killed Hypsenor, the son of noble

[005-077]Dolopion, who had been made priest of the river Scamander, and

[005-078]was honoured among the people as though he were a god. Eurypylus

[005-079]gave him chase as he was flying before him, smote him with his

[005-080]sword upon the arm, and lopped his strong hand from off it. The

[005-081]bloody hand fell to the ground, and the shades of death, with

[005-082]fate that no man can withstand, came over his eyes.

[005-083]Thus furiously did the battle rage between them. As for the son

[005-084]of Tydeus, you could not say whether he was more among the

[005-085]Achaeans or the Trojans. He rushed across the plain like a winter

[005-086]torrent that has burst its barrier in full flood; no dykes, no

[005-087]walls of fruitful vineyards can embank it when it is swollen with

[005-088]rain from heaven, but in a moment it comes tearing onward, and

[005-089]lays many a field waste that many a strong man’s hand has

[005-090]reclaimed—even so were the dense phalanxes of the Trojans driven

[005-091]in rout by the son of Tydeus, and many though they were, they

[005-092]dared not abide his onslaught.

[005-093]Now when the son of Lycaon saw him scouring the plain and driving

[005-094]the Trojans pell-mell before him, he aimed an arrow and hit the

[005-095]front part of his cuirass near the shoulder: the arrow went right

[005-096]through the metal and pierced the flesh, so that the cuirass was

[005-097]covered with blood. On this the son of Lycaon shouted in triumph,

[005-098]“Knights Trojans, come on; the bravest of the Achaeans is

[005-099]wounded, and he will not hold out much longer if King Apollo was

[005-100]indeed with me when I sped from Lycia hither.”

[005-101]Thus did he vaunt; but his arrow had not killed Diomed, who

[005-102]withdrew and made for the chariot and horses of Sthenelus, the

[005-103]son of Capaneus. “Dear son of Capaneus,” said he, “come down from

[005-104]your chariot, and draw the arrow out of my shoulder.”

[005-105]Sthenelus sprang from his chariot, and drew the arrow from the

[005-106]wound, whereon the blood came spouting out through the hole that

[005-107]had been made in his shirt. Then Diomed prayed, saying, “Hear me,

[005-108]daughter of aegis-bearing Jove, unweariable, if ever you loved my

[005-109]father well and stood by him in the thick of a fight, do the like

[005-110]now by me; grant me to come within a spear’s throw of that man

[005-111]and kill him. He has been too quick for me and has wounded me;

[005-112]and now he is boasting that I shall not see the light of the sun

[005-113]much longer.”

[005-114]Thus he prayed, and Pallas Minerva heard him; she made his limbs

[005-115]supple and quickened his hands and his feet. Then she went up

[005-116]close to him and said, “Fear not, Diomed, to do battle with the

[005-117]Trojans, for I have set in your heart the spirit of your knightly

[005-118]father Tydeus. Moreover, I have withdrawn the veil from your

[005-119]eyes, that you know gods and men apart. If, then, any other god

[005-120]comes here and offers you battle, do not fight him; but should

[005-121]Jove’s daughter Venus come, strike her with your spear and wound

[005-122]her.”

[005-123]When she had said this Minerva went away, and the son of Tydeus

[005-124]again took his place among the foremost fighters, three times

[005-125]more fierce even than he had been before. He was like a lion that

[005-126]some mountain shepherd has wounded, but not killed, as he is

[005-127]springing over the wall of a sheep-yard to attack the sheep. The

[005-128]shepherd has roused the brute to fury but cannot defend his

[005-129]flock, so he takes shelter under cover of the buildings, while

[005-130]the sheep, panic-stricken on being deserted, are smothered in

[005-131]heaps one on top of the other, and the angry lion leaps out over

[005-132]the sheep-yard wall. Even thus did Diomed go furiously about

[005-133]among the Trojans.

[005-134]He killed Astynous, and Hypeiron shepherd of his people, the one

[005-135]with a thrust of his spear, which struck him above the nipple,

[005-136]the other with a sword-cut on the collar-bone, that severed his

[005-137]shoulder from his neck and back. He let both of them lie, and

[005-138]went in pursuit of Abas and Polyidus, sons of the old reader of

[005-139]dreams Eurydamas: they never came back for him to read them any

[005-140]more dreams, for mighty Diomed made an end of them. He then gave

[005-141]chase to [[scamander|Xanthus]] and Thoon, the two sons of Phaenops, both of

[005-142]them very dear to him, for he was now worn out with age, and

[005-143]begat no more sons to inherit his possessions. But Diomed took

[005-144]both their lives and left their father sorrowing bitterly, for he

[005-145]nevermore saw them come home from battle alive, and his kinsmen

[005-146]divided his wealth among themselves.

[005-147]Then he came upon two sons of [[priam|Priam]], Echemmon and Chromius, as

[005-148]they were both in one chariot. He sprang upon them as a lion

[005-149]fastens on the neck of some cow or heifer when the herd is

[005-150]feeding in a coppice. For all their vain struggles he flung them

[005-151]both from their chariot and stripped the armour from their

[005-152]bodies. Then he gave their horses to his comrades to take them

[005-153]back to the ships.

[005-154]When Aeneas saw him thus making havoc among the ranks, he went

[005-155]through the fight amid the rain of spears to see if he could find

[005-156]Pandarus. When he had found the brave son of Lycaon he said,

[005-157]“Pandarus, where is now your bow, your winged arrows, and your

[005-158][[glory|renown]] as an archer, in [[shame|respect]] of which no man here can rival

[005-159]you nor is there any in Lycia that can beat you? Lift then your

[005-160]hands to Jove and send an arrow at this fellow who is going so

[005-161]masterfully about, and has done such deadly work among the

[005-162]Trojans. He has killed many a brave man—unless indeed he is some

[005-163]god who is angry with the Trojans about their sacrifices, and

[005-164]has set his hand against them in his displeasure.”

[005-165]And the son of Lycaon answered, “Aeneas, I take him for none

[005-166]other than the son of Tydeus. I know him by his shield, the visor

[005-167]of his helmet, and by his horses. It is possible that he may be a

[005-168]god, but if he is the man I say he is, he is not making all this

[005-169]havoc without heaven’s help, but has some god by his side who is

[005-170]shrouded in a cloud of darkness, and who turned my arrow aside

[005-171]when it had hit him. I have taken aim at him already and hit him

[005-172]on the right shoulder; my arrow went through the breast-piece of

[005-173]his cuirass; and I made sure I should send him hurrying to the

[005-174]world below, but it seems that I have not killed him. There must

[005-175]be a god who is angry with me. Moreover I have neither horse nor

[005-176]chariot. In my father’s stables there are eleven excellent

[005-177]chariots, fresh from the builder, quite new, with cloths spread

[005-178]over them; and by each of them there stand a pair of horses,

[005-179]champing barley and rye; my old father Lycaon urged me again and

[005-180]again when I was at home and on the point of starting, to take

[005-181]chariots and horses with me that I might lead the Trojans in

[005-182]battle, but I would not listen to him; it would have been much

[005-183]better if I had done so, but I was thinking about the horses,

[005-184]which had been used to eat their fill, and I was afraid that in

[005-185]such a great gathering of men they might be ill-fed, so I left

[005-186]them at home and came on foot to Ilius armed only with my bow and

[005-187]arrows. These it seems, are of no use, for I have already hit two

[005-188]chieftains, the sons of Atreus and of Tydeus, and though I drew

[005-189]blood surely enough, I have only made them still more furious. I

[005-190]did ill to take my bow down from its peg on the day I led my band

[005-191]of Trojans to Ilius in [[hector|Hector]]’s service, and if ever I get home

[005-192]again to set eyes on my native place, my wife, and the greatness

[005-193]of my house, may some one cut my head off then and there if I do

[005-194]not break the bow and set it on a hot fire—such pranks as it

[005-195]plays me.”

[005-196]Aeneas answered, “Say no more. Things will not mend till we two

[005-197]go against this man with chariot and horses and bring him to a

[005-198]trial of arms. Mount my chariot, and note how cleverly the horses

[005-199]of Tros can speed hither and thither over the plain in pursuit or

[005-200]flight. If Jove again vouchsafes glory to the son of Tydeus they

[005-201]will carry us safely back to the city. Take hold, then, of the

[005-202]whip and reins while I stand upon the car to fight, or else do

[005-203]you wait this man’s onset while I look after the horses.”

[005-204]“Aeneas,” replied the son of Lycaon, “take the reins and drive;

[005-205]if we have to fly before the son of Tydeus the horses will go

[005-206]better for their own driver. If they miss the sound of your voice

[005-207]when they expect it they may be frightened, and refuse to take us

[005-208]out of the fight. The son of Tydeus will then kill both of us and

[005-209]take the horses. Therefore drive them yourself and I will be

[005-210]ready for him with my spear.”

[005-211]They then mounted the chariot and drove full speed towards the

[005-212]son of Tydeus. Sthenelus, son of Capaneus, saw them coming and

[005-213]said to Diomed, “Diomed, son of Tydeus, man after my own heart, I

[005-214]see two heroes speeding towards you, both of them men of might

[005-215]the one a skilful archer, Pandarus son of Lycaon, the other,

[005-216]Aeneas, whose sire is Anchises, while his mother is Venus. Mount

[005-217]the chariot and let us retreat. Do not, I pray you, press so

[005-218]furiously forward, or you may get killed.”

[005-219]Diomed looked angrily at him and answered: “Talk not of flight,

[005-220]for I shall not listen to you: I am of a race that knows neither

[005-221]flight nor fear, and my limbs are as yet unwearied. I am in no

[005-222]mind to mount, but will go against them even as I am; Pallas

[005-223]Minerva bids me be afraid of no man, and even though one of them

[005-224]escape, their steeds shall not take both back again. I say

[005-225]further, and lay my saying to your heart—if Minerva sees fit to

[005-226]vouchsafe me the glory of killing both, stay your horses here and

[005-227]make the reins fast to the rim of the chariot; then be sure you

[005-228]spring Aeneas’ horses and drive them from the Trojan to the

[005-229]Achaean ranks. They are of the stock that great Jove gave to Tros

[005-230]in payment for his son Ganymede, and are the finest that live and

[005-231]move under the sun. King Anchises stole the blood by putting his

[005-232]mares to them without Laomedon’s knowledge, and they bore him six

[005-233]foals. Four are still in his stables, but he gave the other two

[005-234]to Aeneas. We shall win great glory if we can take them.”

[005-235]Thus did they converse, but the other two had now driven close up

[005-236]to them, and the son of Lycaon spoke first. “Great and mighty

[005-237]son,” said he, “of noble Tydeus, my arrow failed to lay you low,

[005-238]so I will now try with my spear.”

[005-239]He poised his spear as he spoke and hurled it from him. It struck

[005-240]the shield of the son of Tydeus; the bronze point pierced it and

[005-241]passed on till it reached the breastplate. Thereon the son of

[005-242]Lycaon shouted out and said, “You are hit clean through the

[005-243]belly; you will not stand out for long, and the glory of the

[005-244]fight is mine.”

[005-245]But Diomed all undismayed made answer, “You have missed, not hit,

[005-246]and before you two see the end of this matter one or other of you

[005-247]shall glut tough-shielded Mars with his blood.”

[005-248]With this he hurled his spear, and Minerva guided it on to

[005-249]Pandarus’s nose near the eye. It went crashing in among his white

[005-250]teeth; the bronze point cut through the root of his tongue,

[005-251]coming out under his chin, and his glistening armour rang

[005-252]rattling round him as he fell heavily to the ground. The horses

[005-253]started aside for fear, and he was reft of life and strength.

[005-254]Aeneas sprang from his chariot armed with shield and spear,

[005-255]fearing lest the Achaeans should carry off the body. He bestrode

[005-256]it as a lion in the pride of strength, with shield and spear

[005-257]before him and a cry of battle on his lips resolute to kill the

[005-258]first that should dare face him. But the son of Tydeus caught up

[005-259]a mighty stone, so huge and great that as men now are it would

[005-260]take two to lift it; nevertheless he bore it aloft with ease

[005-261]unaided, and with this he struck Aeneas on the groin where the

[005-262]hip turns in the joint that is called the “cup-bone.” The stone

[005-263]crushed this joint, and broke both the sinews, while its jagged

[005-264]edges tore away all the flesh. The hero fell on his knees, and

[005-265]propped himself with his hand resting on the ground till the

[005-266]darkness of night fell upon his eyes. And now Aeneas, king of

[005-267]men, would have perished then and there, had not his mother,

[005-268]Jove’s daughter Venus, who had conceived him by Anchises when he

[005-269]was herding cattle, been quick to mark, and thrown her two white

[005-270]arms about the body of her dear son. She protected him by

[005-271]covering him with a fold of her own fair garment, lest some

[005-272]Danaan should drive a spear into his breast and kill him.

[005-273]Thus, then, did she bear her dear son out of the fight. But the

[005-274]son of Capaneus was not unmindful of the orders that Diomed had

[005-275]given him. He made his own horses fast, away from the

[005-276]hurly-burly, by binding the reins to the rim of the chariot. Then

[005-277]he sprang upon Aeneas’s horses and drove them from the Trojan to

[005-278]the Achaean ranks. When he had so done he gave them over to his

[005-279]chosen comrade Deipylus, whom he valued above all others as the

[005-280]one who was most like-minded with himself, to take them on to the

[005-281]ships. He then remounted his own chariot, seized the reins, and

[005-282]drove with all speed in search of the son of Tydeus.

[005-283]Now the son of Tydeus was in pursuit of the Cyprian goddess,

[005-284]spear in hand, for he knew her to be feeble and not one of those

[005-285]goddesses that can lord it among men in battle like Minerva or

[005-286]Enyo the waster of cities, and when at last after a long chase he

[005-287]caught her up, he flew at her and thrust his spear into the flesh

[005-288]of her delicate hand. The point tore through the ambrosial robe

[005-289]which the Graces had woven for her, and pierced the skin between

[005-290]her wrist and the palm of her hand, so that the immortal blood,

[005-291]or ichor, that flows in the veins of the blessed gods, came

[005-292]pouring from the wound; for the gods do not eat bread nor drink

[005-293]wine, hence they have no blood such as ours, and are immortal.

[005-294]Venus screamed aloud, and let her son fall, but Phoebus Apollo

[005-295]caught him in his arms, and hid him in a cloud of darkness, lest

[005-296]some Danaan should drive a spear into his breast and kill him;

[005-297]and Diomed shouted out as he left her, “Daughter of Jove, leave

[005-298]war and battle alone, can you not be contented with beguiling

[005-299]silly women? If you meddle with fighting you will get what will

[005-300]make you shudder at the very name of war.”

[005-301]The goddess went dazed and discomfited away, and Iris, fleet as

[005-302]the wind, drew her from the throng, in pain and with her fair

[005-303]skin all besmirched. She found fierce Mars waiting on the left of

[005-304]the battle, with his spear and his two fleet steeds resting on a

[005-305]cloud; whereon she fell on her knees before her brother and

[005-306]implored him to let her have his horses. “Dear brother,” she

[005-307]cried, “save me, and give me your horses to take me to Olympus

[005-308]where the gods dwell. I am badly wounded by a mortal, the son of

[005-309]Tydeus, who would now fight even with father Jove.”

[005-310]Thus she spoke, and Mars gave her his gold-bedizened steeds. She

[005-311]mounted the chariot sick and sorry at heart, while Iris sat

[005-312]beside her and took the reins in her hand. She lashed her horses

[005-313]on and they flew forward nothing loth, till in a trice they were

[005-314]at high Olympus, where the gods have their dwelling. There she

[005-315]stayed them, unloosed them from the chariot, and gave them their

[005-316]ambrosial forage; but Venus flung herself on to the lap of her

[005-317]mother Dione, who threw her arms about her and caressed her,

[005-318]saying, “Which of the heavenly beings has been treating you in

[005-319]this way, as though you had been doing something wrong in the

[005-320]face of day?”

[005-321]And laughter-loving Venus answered, “Proud Diomed, the son of

[005-322]Tydeus, wounded me because I was bearing my dear son Aeneas, whom

[005-323]I love best of all mankind, out of the fight. The war is no

[005-324]longer one between Trojans and Achaeans, for the Danaans have now

[005-325]taken to fighting with the immortals.”

[005-326]“Bear it, my child,” replied Dione, “and make the best of it. We

[005-327]dwellers in Olympus have to put up with much at the hands of men,

[005-328]and we lay much suffering on one another. Mars had to suffer when

[005-329]Otus and Ephialtes, children of Aloeus, bound him in cruel bonds,

[005-330]so that he lay thirteen months imprisoned in a vessel of bronze.

[005-331]Mars would have then perished had not fair Eeriboea, stepmother

[005-332]to the sons of Aloeus, told Mercury, who stole him away when he

[005-333]was already well-nigh worn out by the severity of his bondage.

[005-334]Juno, again, suffered when the mighty son of Amphitryon wounded

[005-335]her on the right breast with a three-barbed arrow, and nothing

[005-336]could assuage her pain. So, also, did huge Hades, when this same

[005-337]man, the son of aegis-bearing Jove, hit him with an arrow even at

[005-338]the gates of hell, and hurt him badly. Thereon Hades went to the

[005-339]house of Jove on great Olympus, angry and full of pain; and the

[005-340]arrow in his brawny shoulder caused him great anguish till Paeeon

[005-341]healed him by spreading soothing herbs on the wound, for Hades

[005-342]was not of mortal mould. Daring, headstrong, evildoer who recked

[005-343]not of his sin in shooting the gods that dwell in Olympus. And

[005-344]now Minerva has egged this son of Tydeus on against yourself,

[005-345]fool that he is for not reflecting that no man who fights with

[005-346]gods will live long or hear his children prattling about his

[005-347]knees when he returns from battle. Let, then, the son of Tydeus

[005-348]see that he does not have to fight with one who is stronger than

[005-349]you are. Then shall his brave wife Aegialeia, daughter of

[005-350]Adrestus, rouse her whole house from sleep, wailing for the loss

[005-351]of her wedded lord, Diomed the bravest of the Achaeans.”

[005-352]So saying, she wiped the ichor from the wrist of her daughter

[005-353]with both hands, whereon the pain left her, and her hand was

[005-354]healed. But Minerva and Juno, who were looking on, began to taunt

[005-355]Jove with their mocking talk, and Minerva was first to speak.

## Section 16

[005-728]Jove looked angrily at him and said, “Do not come whining here,

[005-729]Sir Facing-both-ways. I hate you worst of all the gods in

[005-730]Olympus, for you are ever fighting and making mischief. You have

[005-731]the intolerable and stubborn spirit of your mother Juno: it is

[005-732]all I can do to manage her, and it is her doing that you are now

[005-733]in this plight: still, I cannot let you remain longer in such

[005-734]great pain; you are my own offspring, and it was by me that your

[005-735]mother conceived you; if, however, you had been the son of any

[005-736]other god, you are so destructive that by this time you should

[005-737]have been lying lower than the Titans.”

[005-738]He then bade Paeeon heal him, whereon Paeeon spread pain-killing

[005-739]herbs upon his wound and cured him, for he was not of mortal

[005-740]mould. As the juice of the fig-tree curdles milk, and thickens it

[005-741]in a moment though it is liquid, even so instantly did Paeeon

[005-742]cure fierce Mars. Then Hebe washed him, and clothed him in goodly

[005-743]raiment, and he took his seat by his father Jove all glorious to

[005-744]behold.

[005-745]But Juno of Argos and Minerva of Alalcomene, now that they had

[005-746]put a stop to the murderous doings of Mars, went back again to

[005-747]the house of Jove.
