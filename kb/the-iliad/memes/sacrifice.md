# Sacrifice (Hecatomb)

Sacrifice is the primary language of communication between mortals and gods in the *Iliad*. From the plague-appeasing hecatomb for Apollo in Book I to the funeral offerings for [[patroclus|Patroclus]] in [[the-death-of-hector|Book XXII]]I, sacrificial ritual structures the relationship between the human and [[mount-olympus|divine realm]]s (001-089).

## Definition

The *hecatomb* (ἑκατόμβη) — literally "a hundred oxen" — is the grandest form of animal sacrifice, though in Homer the term often denotes any large-scale ritual offering. Sacrifice serves multiple functions: honoring the gods, seeking favor or appeasement, consecrating oaths, and accompanying funerals. The ritual involves unblemished victims, ritual slaughter at an altar, burning thigh-bones as the god's [[fate|portion]], and a communal feast (001-090).

## Manifestations in the Epic

The first major sacrifice is the hecatomb sent to Chryse to appease Apollo's wrath (001-089). The entire ritual sequence — ship's voyage, landing, procession, washing of hands, barley grains, prayers, slaughter, burning, feast — establishes the normative pattern (001-185) (001-190). Sacrifices punctuate key m[[prophecy|omen]]ts: before the duel of Paris and Menelaus in Book III, an oath-sacrifice seals the terms of combat. The funeral of [[patroclus|Patroclus]] involves massive animal slaughter as offerings to the dead (023-672).

## Greek Terminology

**ἑκατόμβη (hecatombē)** — the hundred-ox offering. **θυσία (thusia)** — general term for sacrifice. **βωμός (bōmos)** — the altar. **κνίση (knisē)** — the savour or fat-smoke rising to heaven, the sensory medium through which gods receive their [[fate|portion]].

## Exemplary Passages

> They offered hecatombs of bulls and goats without blemish on the sea-shore. (001-089)

> The smoke with the savour of their sacrifice rose curling up towards heaven. (001-090)

> Ulysses led her to the altar to deliver her to her father. (001-191)

## Contrasts and Tensions

The epic explores a troubling asymmetry: mortals give, but gods need not reciprocate. [[achilles|Achilles]] repeatedly sacrifices to Zeus, yet the god grants only partial fulfillment of his prayers. Human sacrifice, though absent from the *Iliad*'s action, haunts its margins: [[achilles|Achilles]]' slaughter of twelve Trojan captives on [[patroclus|Patroclus]]'s pyre blurs the line between ritual offering and vengeful atrocity.
