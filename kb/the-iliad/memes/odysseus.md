# Odysseus

In an epic dominated by physical prowess, Odysseus represents the power of intelligence. He is "excellent in fight and counsel" — a capable warrior but, more importantly, the Achaeans' most skilled diplomat, strategist, and speaker. When Agamemnon's authority fails, when Achilles' pride obstructs, when the army's morale collapses, it is Odysseus who finds the words to restore order.

## Identity

Son of Laertes, king of the rocky island of Ithaca, Odysseus is known by his epithet *polymetis* — "of many wiles." His trademark is *mētis* (cunning intelligence), the quality that sets him apart from warriors who rely on strength alone. Butler's translation uses the Latinized form "Ulysses." Helen describes him to Priam from the walls of Troy as "a man of great craft, son of Laertes" who was "bred in rocky Ithaca" (003-158-160). In council, he is "peer of Jove" in wisdom (002-135, 002-512).

## Role in the Epic

Odysseus appears at nearly every critical juncture of the *Iliad*.

**The Return of Chryseis (Book I):** When Apollo's plague strikes the Achaean camp, Odysseus is chosen to lead the embassy that returns Chryseis to her father. He captains the ship to Chryse, delivers the girl, and offers a hecatomb to Apollo (001-085, 001-184, 001-191). This mission — an act of restitution and piety — ends the plague and demonstrates Odysseus's role as the Achaeans' trusted envoy.

**The Assembly and the Flight to the Ships (Book II):** When Agamemnon's misguided test of morale causes the army to rush for the ships, Athena finds Odysseus "standing alone," grieved and sorry, and commands him to intervene: "Go about at once among the host, and speak fairly to them, man by man, that they draw not their ships into the sea" (002-135-141). Odysseus obeys. He takes Agamemnon's sceptre and moves through the army — speaking gently to the kings and chieftains, but striking common soldiers with the sceptre and rebuking them sharply. His most celebrated moment in this sequence is his silencing of Thersites, the misshapen railer who had been abusing Agamemnon. Odysseus delivers a stinging public rebuke, striking Thersites on the back and shoulders with the sceptre until the man sits down weeping and the army laughs in approval (002-199-221).

**The Embassy to Achilles (Book IX):** Odysseus is the lead speaker in the embassy sent to persuade Achilles to return to battle. He delivers Agamemnon's catalogue of gifts — tripods, gold, horses, women, cities, and the hand of Agamemnon's own daughter — in a speech of calculated persuasion. But Achilles, who values honour above all material wealth, rejects the offer with uncompromising clarity. His reply to Odysseus is one of the great speeches of the poem: "Him do I hate even as the gates of hell who says one thing while he hides another in his heart; therefore I will say what I mean" (009-312-313). He declares that "coward and hero are held in equal honour, and death deals like measure to him who works and him who is idle" (009-319-320), and recounts his countless hardships — "many a long night have I been wakeful, and many a bloody battle have I waged by day" (009-325-326) — all for a commander who took the woman he loved. The embassy fails, but not through any fault of Odysseus's rhetoric; Achilles' wounded honour is beyond the reach of words.

**The Night Raid (Book X, the *Doloneia*):** Odysseus's most celebrated exploit is the night raid undertaken with Diomedes. The two infiltrate the Trojan camp, capture and interrogate the spy Dolon, extract intelligence about the Thracian contingent, and then slaughter the newly-arrived Thracian king Rhesus and twelve of his men. Diomedes does the killing; Odysseus, characteristically, unties the captured horses, "binding them together with the bow of the chariot, and drove them out of the camp, for he had not thought of taking the splendid chariot" (010-475-477). The raid is a triumph of *mētis* over brute force — a demonstration that intelligence and stealth can achieve what strength alone cannot.

**Battlefield Leadership:** Odysseus is also a capable warrior. He leads the Cephallenians — the contingent from Ithaca and the surrounding islands — in the Catalogue of Ships (002-509-512). In the thick of battle, he fights alongside Diomedes, and when Diomedes is wounded by an arrow from Paris, Odysseus stands over him, holding off the Trojans until his comrade can be rescued (011-350-370). His presence in the fighting, though less spectacular than that of Diomedes or Ajax, is steady and reliable.

## Notable Speeches

Odysseus is the acknowledged master of Homeric rhetoric, and his speeches are among the most artful in the poem.

> "Ulysses, peer of Jove in counsel, stood alone. He had not as yet laid a hand upon his ship, for he was grieved and sorry; so she went close up to him and said, 'Ulysses, noble son of Laertes, are you going to fling yourselves into your ships and be off home to your own land in this way? Will you leave Priam and the Trojans the glory of still keeping Helen, for whose sake so many of the Achaeans have died at Troy, far from their homes?'" (002-135-141)

> "Thus railed Thersites, but Ulysses at once went up to him and rebuked him sternly. 'Check your glib tongue, Thersites,' said he, 'and babble not a word further. Do not presume to quarrel with princes, you who are the meanest man among all those who came to Troy. You shall be soundly beaten and driven in tears from the assembly to the ships.'" (002-199-207)

> "Achilles answered, 'Ulysses, noble son of Laertes, I should give you formal notice plainly and in all fixity of purpose that there be no more of this cajoling, from whatsoever quarter it may come. Him do I hate even as the gates of hell who says one thing while he hides another in his heart; therefore I will say what I mean. I will be appeased neither by Agamemnon son of Atreus nor by any other of the Danaans, for I see that I have no thanks for all my fighting.'" (009-312-318)

In his speech to the Greeks after Thersites' rebuke, Odysseus reminds the army of the prophecy made at Aulis — that after nine years of war, Troy would fall in the tenth — and urges them to endure. His argument is strategic and psychological: the long wait is itself proof that deliverance is near. This speech exemplifies Odysseus's ability to read a crowd and deploy exactly the argument that will restore its morale.

## Relationships

**Achilles:** The object of Odysseus's most important — and ultimately failed — persuasive effort. In the embassy of Book IX, Odysseus deploys his full rhetorical arsenal: the catalogue of gifts, the appeal to Agamemnon's authority, the strategic argument that Hector is momentarily vulnerable. Achilles' rejection demonstrates the limit of rhetoric against the wounded heart. Yet the two men respect each other; in Book XIX, when Achilles wishes to lead the army into battle without food, it is Odysseus who insists the men must eat first — and Achilles yields.

**Agamemnon:** Odysseus is the king's most reliable agent and fixer. He mediates between Agamemnon's blundering authority and the army's volatile morale, translating the commander's will into politically sustainable action. When Agamemnon tests the army's resolve (Book II), Odysseus cleans up the mess. When Agamemnon's quarrel with Achilles threatens the war effort, Odysseus serves as envoy.

**Diomedes:** Battlefield partner in the night raid of Book X and frequent ally in council. Their partnership embodies the ideal combination of martial prowess and strategic intelligence. Diomedes, the younger and more impetuous of the two, provides the fighting strength; Odysseus provides the planning and the cunning.

**Nestor:** Fellow elder statesman; together they represent the Achaeans' deliberative wisdom. Nestor's speeches tend toward recollection of past glories; Odysseus's toward strategic analysis of the present. The two men frequently support each other's proposals in council.

**Athena:** Odysseus is under the special protection of Athena, who favours him above almost all other mortals. It is Athena who rouses him to stop the flight to the ships (002-135), and it is Athena's favour that marks him as the Achaeans' wisest counselor.

## Death or Fate

Odysseus survives the *Iliad*; his fate is the subject of the *Odyssey*, where his *nostos* (homecoming) takes ten years of wandering. Homer explicitly contrasts his fate with Achilles': Odysseus chooses the long life with homecoming over the short life with undying glory. The seeds of the *Odyssey* are visible in the *Iliad*: the resourcefulness, the endurance, the capacity to suffer and adapt — these are the qualities that will carry him through his ten years of wandering.

## Scholarly Notes

The figure of Odysseus in the *Iliad* differs from his portrayal in the *Odyssey*. Here he is the supreme diplomat and problem-solver, not the suffering wanderer. His *mētis* is consistently portrayed as a social good — the intelligence that holds the Achaean coalition together — rather than the self-serving trickery it sometimes becomes in later tradition.

The *Doloneia* (Book X) has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. Some ancient critics considered it a later interpolation, noting stylistic differences from the rest of the poem and the unusual prominence it gives to Odysseus and Diomedes at the expense of Achilles. Modern scholarship is divided, but the book's thematic function is clear: it provides a showcase for the kind of warfare — nocturnal, deceptive, intelligence-driven — that stands in deliberate contrast to the heroic daylight combat of the rest of the epic. Odysseus's role in the *Doloneia* is the purest expression of his *mētis* in action.

The embassy to Achilles in Book IX is one of the most studied episodes in the *Iliad*. The three speakers — Odysseus, Phoenix, and Ajax — deploy three distinct modes of persuasion: Odysseus appeals to reason and self-interest (the gifts), Phoenix to emotion and shared history (his own story and the parable of the Prayers), and Ajax to honour and comradeship (the obligations of friendship). The failure of all three speeches is a measure of the depth of Achilles' alienation.
