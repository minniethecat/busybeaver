# The Embassy to Achilles

The Embassy to Achilles in Book IX is the *Iliad*'s great study in the limits of persuasion. With the Achaeans facing disaster — Hector at the ships, the army in panic — Agamemnon finally acknowledges his error and sends his three most persuasive emissaries to Achilles with an offer of immense material compensation. The embassy's failure demonstrates that some wounds cannot be healed by gifts, however extravagant.

## Context

After two days of devastating Trojan victories following Zeus's promise to Thetis, the Achaean situation is desperate. Agamemnon, in tears, finally admits he was "mad" to insult Achilles and agrees to offer enormous compensation: tripods, gold, horses, seven captive women, the return of Briseis (whom he swears he has not touched), marriage to one of his daughters, and seven cities. He sends Odysseus, Phoenix, and Ajax to deliver the offer (Book IX).

## Participants

- **Odysseus**: Lead speaker, delivers the material offer with diplomatic precision
- **Phoenix**: Achilles' aged tutor, makes the emotional appeal based on their shared history
- **Ajax**: The blunt warrior, appeals to comradeship and shame
- **Achilles**: Refuses the offer, articulating a profound critique of the honor system itself

## Narrative

The three emissaries find Achilles playing the lyre with Patroclus, singing of the glories of heroes — an image of the life he has chosen over battle. Odysseus delivers Agamemnon's offer faithfully, listing every gift. Achilles' response is devastating: he rejects not only the offer but the entire system of value it represents. "I hate the man like the gates of Hades who says one thing and hides another in his heart." Nothing Agamemnon can give will restore what he took — Achilles' honor.

Phoenix attempts the emotional appeal, reminding Achilles of how he raised him as a child. Achilles softens slightly but does not yield. Ajax's blunt speech — "you are implacable; the Achaeans are dying for your sake" — wounds Achilles most, forcing him to acknowledge the truth of the charge. He modifies his position: he will not fight *until Hector reaches the Myrmidon ships*, but he will not leave Troy either.

## Outcome

The embassy fails in its primary mission. Achilles will not return. Agamemnon's offer, which would have satisfied any ordinary warrior's honor, proves insufficient for a wound that is not material but existential. The embassy's failure means the Trojans will continue to advance, leading directly to Patroclus's intervention and death.

## Significance

The Embassy is the epic's most sustained exploration of the limits of language and persuasion. Three different rhetorical strategies — diplomatic (Odysseus), emotional (Phoenix), and moral (Ajax) — all fail, demonstrating that some breaches cannot be repaired by words. Achilles' critique of the honor system is radical: if a king can seize honor at will, then the system is corrupt and no gift can restore faith in it.

## Notable Passages

> "I hate the man like the gates of Hades who says one thing and hides another in his heart." (Achilles to Odysseus, Book IX)

> "You are implacable, Achilles. The Achaeans are dying for your sake, and yet you will not lift a hand." (Ajax, Book IX)
