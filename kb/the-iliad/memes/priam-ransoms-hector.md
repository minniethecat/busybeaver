# Priam Ransoms Hector's Body

The *Iliad* ends not with the fall of Troy or the death of Achilles but with an act of profound humanity: an old king kneels before his son's killer, kisses the hands that slaughtered his child, and asks for the body to be returned for burial. In that moment — when Achilles and Priam weep together, each for his own dead — the epic transcends its subject matter and becomes something closer to tragedy's deepest insight: that suffering is the one experience all humans share.

## Context

For twelve days after Hector's death, Achilles has dragged the body behind his chariot around Patroclus's tomb. Each morning, Apollo restores the corpse, preserving it from decay. The gods, even those who supported the Achaeans, are troubled. Zeus sends Thetis to tell Achilles he must accept ransom and return the body. Iris is sent to Priam with instructions to go alone, with only one aged herald and a wagon of treasure.

## Participants

- **Priam**: The aged king of Troy, who makes the journey to the killer's tent
- **Achilles**: The killer, whose grief is beginning to exhaust itself
- **Hermes**: Guides Priam safely through the Achaean camp in disguise
- **Zeus**: Orders the ransom from above, restoring cosmic balance
- **Thetis**: Delivers Zeus's command to her son
- **Hecuba and Andromache**: Priam's wife and daughter-in-law, who believe he goes to his death

## Narrative

Guided by Hermes, Priam crosses the Trojan plain at night and enters the Achaean camp unseen. He finds Achilles alone, having just finished his evening meal. The old king kneels, grasps Achilles' knees — the ritual posture of supplication — and speaks the words that change everything:

"Think of your own father, O Achilles, who is such as I am, on the threshold of old age. He too may be hard-pressed by those who dwell around him, with no one to protect him. Yet he knows you are still alive, and each day he hopes to see his son returning from Troy. But I — I have lost all. I had fifty sons when the Achaeans came. Nineteen from one womb, the rest from the women of my house. Most are gone. The one who was left to me, who defended the city and its people — him you killed yesterday as he fought for his country: Hector. I have come to ransom his body, bringing great treasure. Respect the gods, Achilles, and have pity on me, remembering your own father."

Achilles weeps — for his own father, for Patroclus, for everything. He raises Priam from his knees, accepts the ransom, and orders Hector's body washed and wrapped. He offers Priam a meal (for even in grief, one must eat) and a bed for the night. In the morning, he grants an eleven-day truce for Hector's funeral — and the old king returns to Troy with his son's body.

## Outcome

Hector's body is returned. The epic ends with his funeral: three deaths — Hector, Patroclus, and (by implication) Achilles — have been mourned, and a fragile peace, born of shared grief, has momentarily replaced the fury of war. The *Iliad* closes on this note of exhausted reconciliation, leaving the fall of Troy for another song.

## Significance

This episode is the moral climax of the *Iliad*. It demonstrates that the institutions of *xenia* (guest-friendship) and *hikesia* (supplication) can, in the right circumstances, overcome even the most justified rage. More profoundly, it shows that grief is the universal human experience — the one thing that makes the killer and the father of the killed recognizable to each other. Achilles' acceptance of Priam's supplication does not undo his atrocities, but it restores the possibility of moral order in a world dominated by violence.

## Notable Passages

> "Think of your own father, O Achilles, who is such as I am, on the threshold of old age... I have endured what no other man on earth has endured — to bring to my lips the hand of the man who killed my son." (Priam to Achilles, Book XXIV)

> "So the two of them wept — Priam for Hector, crouched at Achilles' feet, and Achilles for his own father, and then again for Patroclus. And the house was filled with the sound of their grief." (Book XXIV)
