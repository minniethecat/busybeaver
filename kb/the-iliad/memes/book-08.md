# Book VIII

[008-001]BOOK VIII.

[008-002]Jove forbids the gods to interfere further—There is an even fight

[008-003]till midday, but then Jove inclines the scales of victory in

[008-004]favour of the Trojans, who eventually chase the Achaeans within

[008-005]their wall—Juno and Minerva set out to help the Trojans: Jove

[008-006]sends Iris to turn them back, but later on he promises Juno that

[008-007]she shall have her way in the end—[[hector|Hector]]’s triumph is stayed by

[008-008]nightfall—The Trojans bivouac on the plain.

[008-009]Now when Morning, clad in her robe of saffron, had begun to

[008-010]suffuse light over the earth, Jove called the gods in council on

[008-011]the topmost crest of serrated [[mount-olympus|Olympus]]. Then he spoke and all the

[008-012]other gods gave ear. “Hear me,” said he, “gods and goddesses,

[008-013]that I may speak even as I am minded. Let none of you neither

[008-014]goddess nor god try to cross me, but obey me every one of you

[008-015]that I may bring this matter to an end. If I see anyone acting

[008-016]apart and helping either Trojans or Danaans, he shall be beaten

[008-017]inordinately ere he come back again to Olympus; or I will hurl

[008-018]him down into dark Tartarus far into the deepest pit under the

[008-019]earth, where the gates are iron and the floor bronze, as far

[008-020]beneath Hades as heaven is high above the earth, that you may

[008-021]learn how much the mightiest I am among you. Try me and find out

[008-022]for yourselves. Hangs me a golden chain from heaven, and lay hold

[008-023]of it all of you, gods and goddesses together—tug as you will,

[008-024]you will not drag Jove the supreme counsellor from heaven to

[008-025]earth; but were I to pull at it myself I should draw you up with

[008-026]earth and sea into the bargain, then would I bind the chain about

[008-027]some pinnacle of Olympus and leave you all dangling in the mid

[008-028]firmament. So far am I above all others either of gods or men.”

[008-029]They were frightened and all of them of held their peace, for he

[008-030]had spoken masterfully; but at last Minerva answered, “Father,

[008-031]son of Saturn, king of kings, we all know that your might is not

[008-032]to be gainsaid, but we are also sorry for the Danaan warriors,

[008-033]who are perishing and coming to a bad end. We will, however,

[008-034]since you so bid us, refrain from actual fighting, but we will

[008-035]make serviceable suggestions to the Argives that they may not all

[008-036]of them perish in your displeasure.”

[008-037]Jove smiled at her and answered, “Take heart, my child,

[008-038]Trito-born; I am not really in earnest, and I wish to be kind to

[008-039]you.”

[008-040]With this he yoked his fleet horses, with hoofs of bronze and

[008-041]manes of glittering gold. He girded himself also with gold about

[008-042]the body, seized his gold whip and took his seat in his chariot.

[008-043]Thereon he lashed his horses and they flew forward nothing loth

[008-044]midway twixt earth and starry heaven. After a while he reached

[008-045]many-fountained [[mount-ida|Ida]], mother of wild beasts, and Gargarus, where

[008-046]are his grove and fragrant [[sacrifice|altar]]. There the father of gods and

[008-047]men stayed his horses, took them from the chariot, and hid them

[008-048]in a thick cloud; then he took his seat all glorious upon the

[008-049]topmost crests, looking down upon the city of Troy and the [[achaean-camp|ships]]

[008-050]of the Achaeans.

[008-051]The Achaeans took their morning meal hastily at the ships, and

[008-052]afterwards put on their armour. The Trojans on the other hand

[008-053]likewise armed themselves throughout the city, fewer in numbers

[008-054]but nevertheless eager perforce to do battle for their wives and

[008-055]children. All the gates were flung wide open, and horse and foot

[008-056]sallied forth with the tramp as of a great multitude.

[008-057]When they were got together in one place, shield clashed with

[008-058]shield, and spear with spear, in the conflict of mail-clad men.

[008-059]Mighty was the din as the bossed shields pressed hard on one

[008-060]another—death—cry and shout of triumph of slain and slayers, and

[008-061]the earth ran red with blood.

[008-062]Now so long as the day waxed and it was still morning their

[008-063]weapons beat against one another, and the people fell, but when

[008-064]the sun had reached mid-heaven, the sire of all balanced his

[008-065]golden scales, and put two fates of death within them, one for

[008-066]the Trojans and the other for the Achaeans. He took the balance

[008-067]by the middle, and when he lifted it up the day of the Achaeans

[008-068]sank; the death-fraught scale of the Achaeans settled down upon

[008-069]the ground, while that of the Trojans rose heavenwards. Then he

[008-070]thundered aloud from Ida, and sent the glare of his lightning

[008-071]upon the Achaeans; when they saw this, pale fear fell upon them

[008-072]and they were sore afraid.

[008-073]Idomeneus dared not stay nor yet [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], nor did the two

[008-074]Ajaxes, servants of Mars, hold their ground. [[nestor|Nestor]] knight of

[008-075]Gerene alone stood firm, bulwark of the Achaeans, not of his own

[008-076]will, but one of his horses was disabled. [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] husband of

[008-077]lovely [[helen|Helen]] had hit it with an arrow just on the top of its head

[008-078]where the mane begins to grow away from the skull, a very deadly

[008-079]place. The horse bounded in his anguish as the arrow pierced his

[008-080]brain, and his struggles threw others into confusion. The old man

[008-081]instantly began cutting the traces with his sword, but Hector’s

[008-082]fleet horses bore down upon him through the rout with their bold

[008-083]charioteer, even Hector himself, and the old man would have

[008-084]perished there and then had not Diomed been quick to mark, and

[008-085]with a loud cry called [[odysseus|Ulysses]] to help him.

[008-086]“Ulysses,” he cried, “noble son of Laertes where are you flying

[008-087]to, with your back turned like a coward? See that you are not

[008-088]struck with a spear between the shoulders. Stay here and help me

[008-089]to defend Nestor from this man’s furious onset.”

[008-090]Ulysses would not give ear, but sped onward to the ships of the

[008-091]Achaeans, and the son of Tydeus flinging himself alone into the

[008-092]thick of the fight took his stand before the horses of the son of

[008-093]Neleus. “Sir,” said he, “these young warriors are pressing you

[008-094]hard, your force is spent, and age is heavy upon you, your squire

[008-095]is naught, and your horses are slow to move. Mount my chariot and

[008-096]see what the horses of Tros can do—how cleverly they can scud

[008-097]hither and thither over the plain either in flight or in pursuit.

[008-098]I took them from the hero Aeneas. Let our squires attend to your

[008-099]own steeds, but let us drive mine straight at the Trojans, that

[008-100]Hector may learn how furiously I too can wield my spear.”

[008-101]Nestor knight of Gerene hearkened to his words. Thereon the

[008-102]doughty squires, Sthenelus and kind-hearted Eurymedon, saw to

[008-103]Nestor’s horses, while the two both mounted Diomed’s chariot.

[008-104]Nestor took the reins in his hands and lashed the horses on; they

[008-105]were soon close up with Hector, and the son of Tydeus aimed a

[008-106]spear at him as he was charging full speed towards them. He

[008-107]missed him, but struck his charioteer and squire Eniopeus son of

[008-108]noble Thebaeus in the breast by the nipple while the reins were

[008-109]in his hands, so that he died there and then, and the horses

[008-110]swerved as he fell headlong from the chariot. Hector was greatly

[008-111]grieved at the loss of his charioteer, but let him lie for all

[008-112]his [[grief|sorrow]], while he went in quest of another driver; nor did his

[008-113]steeds have to go long without one, for he presently found brave

[008-114]Archeptolemus the son of Iphitus, and made him get up behind the

[008-115]horses, giving the reins into his hand.

[008-116]All had then been lost and no help for it, for they would have

[008-117]been penned up in Ilius like sheep, had not the sire of gods and

[008-118]men been quick to mark, and hurled a fiery flaming thunderbolt

[008-119]which fell just in front of Diomed’s horses with a flare of

[008-120]burning brimstone. The horses were frightened and tried to back

[008-121]beneath the car, while the reins dropped from Nestor’s hands.

[008-122]Then he was afraid and said to Diomed, “Son of Tydeus, turn your

[008-123]horses in flight; see you not that the hand of Jove is against

[008-124]you? To-day he vouchsafes victory to Hector; to-morrow, if it so

[008-125]please him, he will again grant it to ourselves; no man, however

[008-126]brave, may thwart the purpose of Jove, for he is far stronger

[008-127]than any.”

[008-128]Diomed answered, “All that you have said is true; there is a

[008-129]grief however which pierces me to the very heart, for Hector will

[008-130]talk among the Trojans and say, ‘The son of Tydeus fled before me

[008-131]to the ships.’ This is the vaunt he will make, and may earth then

[008-132]swallow me.”

[008-133]“Son of Tydeus,” replied Nestor, “what mean you? Though Hector

[008-134]say that you are a coward the Trojans and Dardanians will not

[008-135]believe him, nor yet the wives of the mighty warriors whom you

[008-136]have laid low.”

[008-137]So saying he turned the horses back through the thick of the

[008-138]battle, and with a cry that rent the air the Trojans and Hector

[008-139]rained their darts after them. Hector shouted to him and said,

[008-140]“Son of Tydeus, the Danaans have done you [[honor|honour]] hitherto as

[008-141]regards your place at table, the meals they give you, and the

[008-142]filling of your cup with wine. Henceforth they will despise you,

[008-143]for you are become no better than a woman. Be off, girl and

[008-144]coward that you are, you shall not scale our walls through any

[008-145]flinching upon my part; neither shall you carry off our wives in

[008-146]your ships, for I shall kill you with my own hand.”

[008-147]The son of Tydeus was in two minds whether or no to turn his

[008-148]horses round again and fight him. Thrice did he doubt, and thrice

[008-149]did Jove thunder from the heights of Ida in token to the Trojans

[008-150]that he would turn the battle in their favour. Hector then

[008-151]shouted to them and said, “Trojans, Lycians, and Dardanians,

[008-152]lovers of close fighting, be men, my friends, and fight with

[008-153]might and with main; I see that Jove is minded to vouchsafe

[008-154]victory and great glory to myself, while he will deal destruction

[008-155]upon the Danaans. Fools, for having thought of building this weak

[008-156]and worthless wall. It shall not stay my [[wrath|fury]]; my horses will

[008-157]spring lightly over their trench, and when I am at their ships

[008-158]forget not to bring me fire that I may burn them, while I

[008-159]slaughter the Argives who will be all dazed and bewildered by the

[008-160]smoke.”

[008-161]Then he cried to his horses, “[[scamander|Xanthus]] and Podargus, and you

[008-162]Aethon and goodly Lampus, pay me for your keep now and for all

[008-163]the honey-sweet corn with which Andromache daughter of great

[008-164]Eetion has fed you, and for she has mixed wine and water for you

[008-165]to drink whenever you would, before doing so even for me who am

[008-166]her own husband. Haste in pursuit, that we may take the shield of

[008-167]Nestor, the [[glory|fame]] of which ascends to heaven, for it is of solid

[008-168]gold, arm-rods and all, and that we may strip from the shoulders

[008-169]of Diomed the cuirass which Vulcan made him. Could we take these

[008-170]two things, the Achaeans would set sail in their ships this

[008-171]self-same night.”

[008-172]Thus did he vaunt, but Queen Juno made high Olympus quake as she

[008-173]shook with rage upon her throne. Then said she to the mighty god

[008-174]of Neptune, “What now, wide ruling lord of the earthquake? Can

[008-175]you find no compassion in your heart for the dying Danaans, who

[008-176]bring you many a welcome offering to Helice and to Aegae? Wish

[008-177]them well then. If all of us who are with the Danaans were to

[008-178]drive the Trojans back and keep Jove from helping them, he would

[008-179]have to sit there sulking alone on Ida.”

[008-180]King Neptune was greatly troubled and answered, “Juno, rash of

[008-181]tongue, what are you talking about? We other gods must not set

[008-182]ourselves against Jove, for he is far stronger than we are.”

[008-183]Thus did they converse; but the whole space enclosed by the

[008-184]ditch, from the ships even to the wall, was filled with horses

[008-185]and warriors, who were pent up there by Hector son of [[priam|Priam]], now

[008-186]that the hand of Jove was with him. He would even have set fire

[008-187]to the ships and burned them, had not Queen Juno put it into the

[008-188]mind of Agamemnon, to bestir himself and to encourage the

[008-189]Achaeans. To this end he went round the ships and tents carrying

[008-190]a great purple cloak, and took his stand by the huge black hull

[008-191]of Ulysses’ ship, which was middlemost of all; it was from this

[008-192]place that his voice would carry farthest, on the one hand

[008-193]towards the tents of Ajax son of Telamon, and on the other

[008-194]towards those of [[achilles|Achilles]]—for these two heroes, well assured of

[008-195]their own strength, had valorously drawn up their ships at the

[008-196]two ends of the line. From this spot then, with a voice that

[008-197]could be heard afar, he shouted to the Danaans, saying, “Argives,

[008-198]shame on you cowardly creatures, brave in semblance only; where

[008-199]are now our vaunts that we should prove victorious—the vaunts we

[008-200]made so vaingloriously in Lemnos, when we ate the flesh of horned

[008-201]cattle and filled our mixing-bowls to the brim? You vowed that

[008-202]you would each of you stand against a hundred or two hundred men,

[008-203]and now you prove no match even for one—for Hector, who will be

[008-204]ere long setting our ships in a blaze. Father Jove, did you ever

[008-205]so ruin a great king and rob him so utterly of his greatness?

[008-206]Yet, when to my sorrow I was coming hither, I never let my ship

[008-207]pass your altars without offering the fat and thigh-bones of

[008-208]heifers upon every one of them, so eager was I to sack the city

[008-209]of Troy. Vouchsafe me then this prayer—suffer us to escape at any

[008-210]rate with our lives, and let not the Achaeans be so utterly

[008-211]vanquished by the Trojans.”

[008-212]Thus did he pray, and father Jove pitying his tears vouchsafed

[008-213]him that his people should live, not die; forthwith he sent them

[008-214]an eagle, most unfailingly portentous of all birds, with a young

[008-215]fawn in its talons; the eagle dropped the fawn by the altar on

[008-216]which the Achaeans sacrificed to Jove the lord of omens; when,

[008-217]therefore, the people saw that the bird had come from Jove, they

[008-218]sprang more fiercely upon the Trojans and fought more boldly.

[008-219]There was no man of all the many Danaans who could then boast

[008-220]that he had driven his horses over the trench and gone forth to

[008-221]fight sooner than the son of Tydeus; long before any one else

[008-222]could do so he slew an armed warrior of the Trojans, Agelaus the

[008-223]son of Phradmon. He had turned his horses in flight, but the

[008-224]spear struck him in the back midway between his shoulders and

[008-225]went right through his chest, and his armour rang rattling round

[008-226]him as he fell forward from his chariot.

[008-227]After him came Agamemnon and Menelaus, sons of Atreus, the two

[008-228]Ajaxes clothed in valour as with a garment, Idomeneus and his

[008-229]companion in arms Meriones, peer of murderous Mars, and Eurypylus

[008-230]the brave son of Euaemon. Ninth came Teucer with his bow, and

[008-231]took his place under cover of the shield of Ajax son of Telamon.

[008-232]When Ajax lifted his shield Teucer would peer round, and when he

[008-233]had hit any one in the throng, the man would fall dead; then

[008-234]Teucer would hie back to Ajax as a child to its mother, and again

[008-235]duck down under his shield.

[008-236]Which of the Trojans did brave Teucer first kill? Orsilochus, and

[008-237]then Ormenus and Ophelestes, Daetor, Chromius, and godlike

[008-238]Lycophontes, Amopaon son of Polyaemon, and Melanippus. All these

[008-239]in turn did he lay low upon the earth, and King Agamemnon was

[008-240]glad when he saw him making havoc of the Trojans with his mighty

[008-241]bow. He went up to him and said, “Teucer, man after my own heart,

[008-242]son of Telamon, captain among the [[guest-friendship|host]], shoot on, and be at once

[008-243]the saving of the Danaans and the glory of your father Telamon,

[008-244]who brought you up and took care of you in his own house when you

[008-245]were a child, bastard though you were. Cover him with glory

[008-246]though he is far off; I will promise and I will assuredly

[008-247]perform; if aegis-bearing Jove and Minerva grant me to sack the

[008-248]city of Ilius, you shall have the next best [[prize|meed]] of honour after

[008-249]my own—a tripod, or two horses with their chariot, or a woman who

[008-250]shall go up into your bed.”

[008-251]And Teucer answered, “Most noble son of Atreus, you need not urge

[008-252]me; from the moment we began to drive them back to Ilius, I have

[008-253]never ceased so far as in me lies to look out for men whom I can

[008-254]shoot and kill; I have shot eight barbed shafts, and all of them

[008-255]have been buried in the flesh of warlike youths, but this mad dog

[008-256]I cannot hit.”

[008-257]As he spoke he aimed another arrow straight at Hector, for he was

[008-258]bent on hitting him; nevertheless he missed him, and the arrow

[008-259]hit Priam’s brave son Gorgythion in the breast. His mother, fair

[008-260]Castianeira, lovely as a goddess, had been married from Aesyme,

[008-261]and now he bowed his head as a garden poppy in full bloom when it

[008-262]is weighed down by showers in spring—even thus heavy bowed his

[008-263]head beneath the weight of his helmet.

[008-264]Again he aimed at Hector, for he was longing to hit him, and

[008-265]again his arrow missed, for Apollo turned it aside; but he hit

[008-266]Hector’s brave charioteer Archeptolemus in the breast, by the

[008-267]nipple, as he was driving furiously into the fight. The horses

[008-268]swerved aside as he fell headlong from the chariot, and there was

[008-269]no life left in him. Hector was greatly grieved at the loss of

[008-270]his charioteer, but for all his sorrow he let him lie where he

[008-271]fell, and bade his brother Cebriones, who was hard by, take the

[008-272]reins. Cebriones did as he had said. Hector thereon with a loud

[008-273]cry sprang from his chariot to the ground, and seizing a great

[008-274]stone made straight for Teucer with intent to kill him. Teucer

[008-275]had just taken an arrow from his quiver and had laid it upon the

[008-276]bowstring, but Hector struck him with the jagged stone as he was

[008-277]taking aim and drawing the string to his shoulder; he hit him

[008-278]just where the collar-bone divides the neck from the chest, a

[008-279]very deadly place, and broke the sinew of his arm so that his

[008-280]wrist was less, and the bow dropped from his hand as he fell

[008-281]forward on his knees. Ajax saw that his brother had fallen, and

[008-282]running towards him bestrode him and sheltered him with his

[008-283]shield. Meanwhile his two trusty squires, Mecisteus son of

[008-284]Echius, and Alastor, came up and bore him to the ships groaning

[008-285]in his great pain.

[008-286]Jove now again put heart into the Trojans, and they drove the

[008-287]Achaeans to their deep trench with Hector in all his glory at

[008-288]their head. As a hound grips a wild boar or lion in flank or

[008-289]buttock when he gives him chase, and watches warily for his

[008-290]wheeling, even so did Hector follow close upon the Achaeans, ever

[008-291]killing the hindmost as they rushed panic-stricken onwards. When

[008-292]they had fled through the set stakes and trench and many Achaeans

[008-293]had been laid low at the hands of the Trojans, they halted at

[008-294]their ships, calling upon one another and praying every man

[008-295]instantly as they lifted up their hands to the gods; but Hector

[008-296]wheeled his horses this way and that, his eyes glaring like those

[008-297]of Gorgo or murderous Mars.

[008-298]Juno when she saw them had pity upon them, and at once said to

[008-299]Minerva, “Alas, child of aegis-bearing Jove, shall you and I take

[008-300]no more thought for the dying Danaans, though it be the last time

[008-301]we ever do so? See how they perish and come to a bad end before

[008-302]the onset of but a single man. Hector the son of Priam rages with

[008-303]intolerable fury, and has already done great mischief.”

[008-304]Minerva answered, “Would, indeed, this fellow might die in his

[008-305]own land, and fall by the hands of the Achaeans; but my father

[008-306]Jove is mad with spleen, ever foiling me, ever headstrong and

[008-307]unjust. He forgets how often I saved his son when he was worn out

[008-308]by the labours Eurystheus had laid on him. He would weep till his

[008-309]cry came up to heaven, and then Jove would send me down to help

[008-310]him; if I had had the sense to foresee all this, when Eurystheus

[008-311]sent him to the house of Hades, to fetch the hell-hound from

[008-312]Erebus, he would never have come back alive out of the deep

[008-313]waters of the river Styx. And now Jove hates me, while he lets

[008-314]Thetis have her way because she kissed his knees and took hold of

[008-315]his beard, when she was begging him to do honour to Achilles. I

[008-316]shall know what to do next time he begins calling me his

[008-317]grey-eyed darling. Get our horses ready, while I go within the

[008-318]house of aegis-bearing Jove and put on my armour; we shall then

[008-319]find out whether Priam’s son Hector will be glad to meet us in

[008-320]the highways of battle, or whether the Trojans will glut hounds

[008-321]and vultures with the fat of their flesh as they be dead by the

[008-322]ships of the Achaeans.”

[008-323]Thus did she speak and white-armed Juno, daughter of great

[008-324]Saturn, obeyed her words; she set about harnessing her

[008-325]gold-bedizened steeds, while Minerva daughter of aegis-bearing

[008-326]Jove flung her richly vesture, made with her own hands, on to the

[008-327]threshold of her father, and donned the shirt of Jove, arming

[008-328]herself for battle. Then she stepped into her flaming chariot,

[008-329]and grasped the spear so stout and sturdy and strong with which

[008-331]lashed her horses, and the gates of heaven bellowed as they flew

[008-332]open of their own accord—gates over which the Hours preside, in

[008-333]whose hands are heaven and Olympus, either to open the dense

[008-334]cloud that hides them or to close it. Through these the goddesses

[008-335]drove their obedient steeds.

[008-336]But father Jove when he saw them from Ida was very angry, and

[008-337]sent winged Iris with a message to them. “Go,” said he, “fleet

[008-338]Iris, turn them back, and see that they do not come near me, for

[008-339]if we come to fighting there will be mischief. This is what I

[008-340]say, and this is what I mean to do. I will lame their horses for

[008-341]them; I will hurl them from their chariot, and will break it in

[008-342]pieces. It will take them all ten years to heal the wounds my

[008-343]lightning shall inflict upon them; my grey-eyed daughter will

[008-344]then learn what quarrelling with her father means. I am less

[008-345]surprised and angry with Juno, for whatever I say she always

[008-346]contradicts me.”

[008-347]With this Iris went her way, fleet as the wind, from the heights

[008-348]of Ida to the lofty summits of Olympus. She met the goddesses at

[008-349]the outer gates of its many valleys and gave them her message.

[008-350]“What,” said she, “are you about? Are you mad? The son of Saturn

[008-351]forbids going. This is what he says, and this is what he means to

[008-352]do, he will lame your horses for you, he will hurl you from your

[008-353]chariot, and will break it in pieces. It will take you all ten

[008-354]years to heal the wounds his lightning will inflict upon you,

[008-355]that you may learn, grey-eyed goddess, what quarrelling with your

[008-356]father means. He is less hurt and angry with Juno, for whatever
