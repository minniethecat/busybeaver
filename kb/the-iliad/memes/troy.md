# Troy (Ilios)

Troy — also called Ilios, Ilium, and Pergamus — is the great walled city of the Troad, home to King [[priam|Priam]], [[hector|Hector]], and the Trojan people. For ten years it has withstood the Achaean siege, protected by its divine walls (built by Poseidon and Apollo) and by the favor of the gods. The *Iliad* takes place on the plain before these walls, and every battle moves toward or away from them. The city is the central object of the war: the Achaeans fight to destroy it, the Trojans to preserve it, and the tension between its fated fall and its present resistance gives the entire poem its structural and emotional gravity.

## Geography

Troy occupies a strategic position near the Hellespont (modern Dardanelles), controlling the maritime approach to the Black Sea. The city itself is situated on a hill overlooking the plain of the [[scamander|Scamander River (Xanthus)]]. Its key features in the epic include:

| Feature | Description | Significance in the Epic |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Scaean Gates** | The main gates of the city | Hector's final stand; Andromache's desperate run (Book 22) |
| **The Great Tower** | Overlooks the battlefield | Where Helen stands with Priam in Book 3 to identify the Achaean heroes |
| **The Walls** | Divine construction by Poseidon and Apollo | Physical and symbolic boundary between the living and the doomed |
| **Priam's Palace** | The royal residence | Site of the domestic scenes that humanize the Trojan royal family |
| **The Plain** | The battlefield between the ships and the walls | Where all the epic's combat takes place |

The walls themselves are both physical fortification and symbolic boundary between the world of the living and the world of the dead. Built by gods, they are fated to fall — a paradox that captures Troy's tragic condition.

## Inhabitants and Structure

Troy is home to a complex royal household and a diverse population:

| Role | Key Figures |
|------|------------|
| Royal Family | [[priam|Priam]] (king), Hecuba (queen), [[hector|Hector]] (crown prince), [[paris-alexander|Paris]] (prince), [[helen|Helen]] |

## Significance

Troy is more than a setting — it is the epic's central object of desire and destruction:

| Perspective | What Troy Represents |
|------------|---------------------|
| For Hector | Family, home, and the future embodied in his son Astyanax |
| For Priam | A dynasty, a civilization, everything worth defending |
| For Achilles | The stage on which he will win immortal *kleos* |
| For the Achaeans | The prize that ten years of war must finally yield |
| For the Audience | The doomed city — we know it falls, even if the *Iliad* ends before that moment |

The *Iliad* ends without the city's fall, but the knowledge of that coming destruction — already fated, already sung — hangs over every line of the poem. Troy's walls still stand at the epic's close, but Hector is dead, and the city's fate is sealed.

## Defensive Features

Troy's walls are its defining feature and the reason the siege has lasted ten years:

- **Divine construction**: Built by Poseidon and Apollo as punishment for King Laomedon's treachery
- **Impregnability**: No Achaean assault breaches them during the *Iliad*
- **The Scaean Gate**: The focal point of battle — Hector's death, Andromache's grief, and Priam's departure for Achilles' tent all pass through these gates
- **Symbolic function**: The walls represent the boundary between civilization (within) and chaos (without)
