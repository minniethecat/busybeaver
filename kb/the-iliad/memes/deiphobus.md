# Deiphobus

Deiphobus, son of Priam and Hecuba, is a Trojan prince who fights prominently in the middle books of the Iliad. He is one of the many sons of Priam who defend Troy, but he is distinguished from his brothers by his role in the poem's climactic deception: in Book 22, Athena assumes the form of Deiphobus to lure [[hector|Hector]] into facing [[achilles|Achilles]], convincing him that with a brother beside him he can triumph. When Hector turns for a second spear and calls for Deiphobus, the god has vanished, and Hector speaks his devastating line: "The gods have called me to my death" (022-260). This single narrative function — being the brother Hector trusted most — gives Deiphobus a tragic significance disproportionate to his relatively limited appearances as a fighter.

## Identity

Deiphobus is one of the many sons of Priam, king of Troy, and Hecuba. Along with his brothers [[hector|Hector]], [[paris|Paris]] (Alexander), Helenus, and others, he belongs to the sprawling Priamid dynasty that dominates the Trojan leadership. In the Catalogue of Trojan forces, Deiphobus and Helenus are listed together as commanders of a division (012-079).

Unlike Hector, who is defined by martial prowess and tragic responsibility, or Paris, who is defined by beauty and moral ambiguity, Deiphobus is defined by his relationship to Hector. He is the trusted brother, the reliable second — which is precisely why Athena chooses his shape for the final deception. The trust between Hector and Deiphobus is the emotional core of the Book 22 deception: Hector faces Achilles believing his brother stands beside him.

## Role in the Epic

Deiphobus appears in Books 12, 13, and 22, with his most sustained fighting in Book 13.

- **Book 13 — Battlefield Prowess:** Deiphobus fights extensively in Book 13, where his combat sequence is one of the longest for a non-Hector Trojan. He rallies the Trojans at a moment of wavering (013-146), engages in a duel with Idomeneus (013-356), and kills several named Achaeans. When his spear-throw fails to kill Idomeneus, Deiphobus "vaunted over him and cried with a loud voice" (013-365), a standard gesture of battlefield triumph. He withdraws briefly to consult with Paris and Agenor (013-431), showing tactical awareness, then returns to the fray. He wounds Meriones' body and strips a fallen Achaean's helmet (013-465), only to be counter-attacked.

- **Book 12 — Command Role:** Deiphobus and Helenus are listed as the third division of Trojan assault, alongside Asius (012-079). The three divisions attack different sections of the Achaean wall, with Deiphobus and Helenus assaulting the centre. This command position establishes him as a figure of sufficient authority to be credible as Hector's trusted lieutenant.

- **Book 22 — The Fatal Deception:** Deiphobus' most significant contribution to the Iliad is involuntary. As Achilles pursues Hector around the walls of Troy, Athena descends from Olympus and "took the form and voice of Deiphobus" (022-200). She appears at Hector's side, speaking as his brother: "Dear brother, truly fleet Achilles is giving you rough work, chasing you round the city of Priam. But come, let us make a stand together and beat him back" (022-204). Hector, heartened, agrees: "Deiphobus, you have always been dearest to me of all my brothers" (022-260).

    The two approach Achilles together. Hector throws his spear and it rebounds from Achilles' shield. He turns to Deiphobus for a second spear — and Deiphobus has vanished. Hector's recognition is instant and devastating: "Alas! The gods have called me to my death. I deemed that the hero Deiphobus was near me, but he is within the walls, and Athena has deceived me" (022-262). The deception works because Deiphobus is the brother Hector trusted to stand with him; the betrayal by the gods is made more bitter by the absence of the mortal brother whose shape they borrowed.

## Notable Speeches

> "Deiphobus, you have always been dearest to me of all my brothers, sons of Priam and Hecuba. Now I shall hold you in even greater honour, for you have dared to come outside the walls for my sake when you saw me in distress, while the others remain inside."
> — Hector to Athena-as-Deiphobus, moments before his death (022-260)

> "Alas! The gods have called me to my death. I deemed that the hero Deiphobus was near me, but he is within the walls, and Athena has deceived me. Death is now indeed near at hand."
> — Hector, realising the deception (022-262)

## Relationships

- **Hector (beloved brother):** Deiphobus' most defining relationship is with Hector. Hector's words — "Deiphobus, you have always been dearest to me of all my brothers" (022-260) — establish a depth of fraternal trust that makes Athena's deception both plausible and devastating. Deiphobus never learns, within the poem, that his likeness was used to lure Hector to his death.

- **Priam (father):** Deiphobus is one of Priam's many sons; in the later catalogue of Priam's surviving sons, Deiphobus is listed among those still living (024-222). He inherits something of his father's torment, though at a lesser register than Hector.

- **Helenus (brother and co-commander):** Helenus and Deiphobus share command of a Trojan division (012-079). In later tradition (not Homeric), Deiphobus marries Helen after Paris' death — a fate that the Iliad does not anticipate but which retroactively deepens his connection to the war's origins.

- **Idomeneus (battlefield rival):** Deiphobus' most detailed combat sequence is against Idomeneus in Book 13 (013-356). Their inconclusive duel — neither kills the other — places Deiphobus among the capable but not supreme warriors of the Trojan force.

## Death or Fate

The Iliad does not narrate Deiphobus' death. He survives the poem and is listed among Priam's surviving sons in Book 24 (024-222). Later tradition, however, records a grim fate: after Paris' death, Deiphobus marries Helen, and when Troy falls, he is betrayed by her to Menelaus, who mutilates his corpse. This post-Homeric tradition — most vivid in Virgil's Aeneid, where Aeneas encounters Deiphobus' shade in the underworld — retroactively casts the Book 22 deception as a foreshadowing: Deiphobus is twice used as an instrument of others' fates, his own death obscured.

## Scholarly Notes

Deiphobus' name (Δηΐφοβος) means "rout of the enemy" or "terrifier of the foe" — an ironic name for a man whose most famous contribution to the poem is being impersonated in a deception that leads to his brother's death.

The Athena-as-Deiphobus deception in Book 22 is structurally parallel to other Iliadic scenes of divine impersonation (Athena as Pandarus' companion in Book 4, Apollo as Agenor in Book 21). But the Deiphobus deception carries unique emotional weight because of the fraternal bond invoked. Hector's trust in Deiphobus, expressed in his last hopeful words before the deception is revealed, is one of the poem's most concentrated moments of pathos.

In later Greek art and literature, Deiphobus becomes a more prominent figure. The Ilioupersis (Sack of Troy) tradition makes him Helen's third Trojan husband, and vase paintings depict his death at Menelaus' hands. The contrast between his relatively modest role in the Iliad and his expanded role in later tradition suggests that the name "Deiphobus" carried strong narrative associations for ancient audiences.
