## Identity

Anchises is a Trojan nobleman of the royal house, son of Capys and descendant of Assaracus, the second son of Tros. He belongs to a junior branch of the Trojan dynasty — the line that runs from Dardanus through Tros, Assaracus, Capys, to Anchises himself (020-207). While Priam's line (through Ilus, Tros's eldest son) holds the throne, Anchises' line commands its own prestige, rooted in his singular distinction: he is the mortal lover of Aphrodite and father of the hero Aeneas.

The *Iliad* repeatedly identifies Aeneas as "the son of Anchises" or "the valiant son of Anchises" (005-216, 012-083, 017-406, 020-148), and Aeneas proudly declares: "I have noble Anchises for my father, and Venus for my mother" (020-183). Anchises' union with the goddess occurred "upon the mountain slopes of Ida" (002-667), where "she, goddess though she was, had lain with him" (002-667). This divine encounter — a mortal man sleeping with a goddess — places Anchises in the select company of mortals loved by immortals.

## Role in the Epic

Anchises does not appear in person in the *Iliad* — he is too old to fight — but his presence pervades the epic through his son Aeneas and through the stories told about him. He is a figure of memory and inheritance, his legacy carried forward by his descendants and his horses.

**The Divine Horses (Book V):** One of the most revealing episodes involving Anchises concerns the immortal horses of Aeneas. When Diomedes and Sthenelus see Aeneas' chariot, Sthenelus urges Diomedes to seize the horses, explaining their extraordinary provenance: "They are of the stock that great Jove gave to Tros in payment for his son Ganymede, and are the finest that live and move under the sun" (005-229). The crucial detail follows: "King Anchises stole the blood by putting his mares to them without Laomedon's knowledge, and they bore him six foals" (005-231). Anchises kept four and gave two to Aeneas (005-233). This act of daring — secretly breeding his mares with horses given by Zeus himself — reveals Anchises as a bold, resourceful figure willing to risk royal displeasure for his son's advantage.

**The Lineage of Aeneas (Book XX):** Before his duel with Achilles, Aeneas delivers a lengthy genealogy tracing his lineage from Dardanus, son of Zeus, through Erichthonius, Tros, Assaracus, Capys, to "Anchises, who was my father" (020-207). He contrasts his line with Hector's: "Hector is son to Priam" (020-207), while his own descent runs through Assaracus rather than Ilus. This speech establishes Anchises as the genealogical link between Aeneas and the divine origins of the Trojan people — a lineage that, in later tradition, will become the foundation myth of Rome.

**Anchises' Family (Book XIII):** Anchises' eldest daughter Hippodameia married Alcathous, son of Aesyetes — a man who "excelled all her generation in beauty, accomplishments, and understanding" (013-381). Alcathous falls in battle to Idomeneus, "laid low by the hand of Idomeneus" (013-383) with Neptune's aid. This detail anchors Anchises in the web of Trojan kinship, his line extending through marriage into the broader aristocracy.

**The Wealth of Anchises (Book XXIII):** At Patroclus' funeral games, Menelaus races with a mare bred from the stock of Anchises — a horse that "Jove had endowed with great wealth" (023-265) in Sicyon. This passing reference confirms Anchises' legendary prosperity and the dispersal of his prized horses across the Greek world.

## Notable Speeches

Anchises himself speaks no direct words in the *Iliad*. His voice is rendered through others — through Aeneas' genealogical recitation (020-188-207) and through Sthenelus' account of the divine horses (005-229-234). This narrative strategy, in which Anchises' story is told by those who inherit his legacy, is appropriate to a character defined by what he bequeaths rather than what he does within the poem's temporal frame.

## Relationships

**Aeneas:** Anchises' defining relationship is with his son Aeneas, born of his union with Aphrodite "when he was tending cattle on the slopes of Ida" (as the Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite elaborates). Aeneas is called "son of Anchises" throughout the epic (005-216, 012-083, 020-148, 020-183), and his divine lineage — mortal father, divine mother — is the source of both his heroic status and the protection Aphrodite extends to him in battle (005-268).

**Aphrodite (Venus):** Anchises' affair with Aphrodite on Mount Ida (002-667) is among the most famous divine-mortal unions in Greek myth. Within the *Iliad*, this relationship is not narrated in detail — the epic assumes its audience knows the story — but its consequences structure Aeneas' entire role. Aphrodite's intervention to save Aeneas from Diomedes (005-268) is motivated by her love for her mortal son and, implicitly, her memory of Anchises.

**Priam:** Anchises belongs to a cadet branch of the Trojan royal house. Aeneas' genealogy (020-207) carefully distinguishes his line (through Assaracus) from Priam's (through Ilus). There is no direct interaction described between Anchises and Priam, but the two branches coexist within the epic's Trojan hierarchy.

**Laomedon:** Anchises' theft of the divine horses' bloodline occurred "without Laomedon's knowledge" (005-231), the former king of Troy. This act of secret daring positions Anchises as a figure operating at the edge of royal authority — bold, resourceful, and willing to risk transgression for his son's benefit.

**Hippodameia:** Anchises' eldest daughter, "the darling of her father and mother" (013-380), who married Alcathous. Her husband's death in battle at the hands of Idomeneus (013-383) represents the cost of war borne by Anchises' family.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Anchises' death; he is an off-stage figure, too old for war. Later tradition, most famously Virgil's *Aeneid*, depicts Aeneas carrying the aged Anchises from the burning ruins of Troy on his shoulders — an image that has become one of the most enduring in Western art and literature. Within the *Iliad*, the mention that Anchises' horses were given to Aeneas because Anchises was too old to go to Troy himself — "he might not have to follow him to Ilius, but might stay at home and take his ease" (023-264) — confirms his absence from the battlefield and his survival into old age in comfort and wealth.

## Scholarly Notes

Anchises occupies a unique position in the *Iliad*: he is a character who does not appear yet whose presence is felt throughout the epic. His story exists in what narratologists call the "extra-diegetic" space — the world of the poem's alluded past — and his significance is transmitted through the characters who invoke his memory (Aeneas, Sthenelus) and through the physical tokens of his legacy (the immortal horses).

The union of Anchises and Aphrodite is one of the few divine-mortal love affairs referenced in the *Iliad* but not narrated within it. The story was well known to Homer's audience and is preserved in the Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite, where the goddess, punished by Zeus for making other gods fall in love with mortals, is herself made to desire Anchises. She approaches him disguised as a mortal maiden, and after their union reveals her true identity, warning him never to boast of having slept with a goddess — a warning Anchises, in some versions, fails to heed.

The theft of the immortal horses (005-231) is a remarkable detail. Anchises' decision to secretly breed his mares with Zeus-gifted stallions "without Laomedon's knowledge" parallels the divine thefts and deceptions that run through the epic (Prometheus, Hermes). It also establishes a pattern of daring and transgression that Aeneas inherits — the son who will dare to face Achilles himself (Book XX), protected by his divine mother but armed with his mortal father's boldness.

Anchises' genealogical position — as the link between the divine Dardanus and the mortal Aeneas — made him a crucial figure in later Greek and Roman mythography. The Julian family of Rome claimed descent from Aeneas and, through him, from Anchises and Venus, making Anchises a foundational ancestor of the Roman people. The *Iliad* itself does not hint at this future significance, but the care with which Aeneas recites his genealogy (020-188-207) suggests that the poet was aware of a tradition that traced a special destiny to Anchises' line.
