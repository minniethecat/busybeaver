## Identity

Laomedon is the former king of Troy, son of Ilus — the founder of Ilium — and father of Priam, who rules Troy during the events of the *Iliad*. He occupies a pivotal position in Trojan genealogy: "Ilus begat Laomedon, and Laomedon begat Tithonus, Priam, Lampus, Clytius, and Hiketaon of the stock of Mars" (020-204-206). His name is a constant presence in the epic — invoked by characters on both sides as a point of reference, a source of ancestral glory, and a cautionary example of royal faithlessness.

The Trojans are often called "the people of Laomedon" or addressed through his lineage. When the herald Idaeus approaches Priam, he calls him "Son of Laomedon" (003-198), and the horses of Laomedon's royal stables are celebrated as "the noblest in this country" (023-306). His reign represents the previous generation of Trojan power, and his legacy — both glorious and tainted — shapes the city's fate.

## Role in the Epic

Laomedon does not appear in person in the *Iliad* — he belongs to the generation before the war — but his actions, particularly his betrayal of the gods, are repeatedly invoked as a defining element of Troy's history.

**The Breach of Faith with the Gods (Book XXI):** The most significant story about Laomedon is told by Poseidon himself during the Battle of the Gods. Poseidon reminds Apollo of how they served Laomedon: "Jove's house and worked for Laomedon a whole year at a stated wage and he gave us his orders. I built the Trojans the wall about their city, so wide and fair that it might be impregnable, while you, Apollo, herded cattle for him in the dales of many-valleyed Ida" (021-386-389). But when the time came for payment, "mighty Laomedon robbed us of all our hire and sent us off with nothing but abuse. He threatened to bind us hand and foot and sell us over into some distant island" (021-391-393). This betrayal — cheating two Olympian gods of their wages — is the original sin that places Troy under a permanent divine shadow, contributing to the enmity of Poseidon, who becomes Troy's most implacable divine enemy.

**The Story Retold by Tlepolemus (Book V):** The Heraclid Tlepolemus, son of Heracles, gives a parallel account when confronting Sarpedon. Heracles, he recounts, sacked Troy with only six ships "for the horses of Laomedon" (005-535), and he "overthrew Ilius by reason of Laomedon's folly in refusing payment to one who had served him well" (005-542). Laomedon had also cheated Heracles of the immortal horses promised for rescuing his daughter Hesione from a sea monster. Tlepolemus contrasts his ancestor Heracles' valour with Sarpedon's, implying that Laomedon's faithlessness is the common thread connecting Troy's past humiliations.

**The Royal Horses (Book V):** Anchises, a member of the junior branch of the Trojan royal house, secretly bred his mares with the immortal stallions given by Zeus to Tros — the horses of Laomedon's royal line — "without Laomedon's knowledge" (005-232). The offspring, "the finest that live and move under the sun" (005-230), were kept partly by Anchises and partly given to his son Aeneas. This clandestine appropriation of the royal bloodline underscores Laomedon's authority even in his absence and reveals the internal dynamics of the Trojan ruling house.

**Laomedon's Sons (Book VI and XV):** Laomedon fathered many notable children. Bucolion, "eldest son to Laomedon, but he was a bastard" (006-023), fathered Aesepus and Pedasus, who were killed by Euryalus. Lampus, "son of Laomedon and noted for his valour" (015-446), fathered Dolops, who fell to Meges. Through these scattered genealogical references, Laomedon's descendants — both legitimate and illegitimate — populate the ranks of Troy's warriors, and their deaths at the hands of Greek heroes are a recurring motif.

## Relationships

**Priam (son):** Laomedon's most important relationship is with his son and successor Priam, who is repeatedly addressed as "Son of Laomedon" (003-198, 007-362). Priam inherited both the throne and the divine animosities that Laomedon's faithlessness had engendered. The epithet links Priam's authority to his father's line while carrying the weight of his father's transgressions.

**Apollo and Poseidon:** Laomedon's betrayal of Apollo and Poseidon (021-386-393) is the most consequential relationship of his reign. Poseidon, who built Troy's walls, becomes the city's bitterest divine enemy; Apollo, who herded his cattle, remains more ambivalent — a protector of Troy who nevertheless allowed Laomedon's city to be sacked by Heracles. This dual divine relationship shapes the theological landscape of the *Iliad*, where Poseidon fights for the Greeks and Apollo for the Trojans.

**Heracles:** Laomedon's cheating of Heracles — refusing the promised reward of immortal horses for rescuing Hesione — led to the first Greek sack of Troy, a generation before the Trojan War. Tlepolemus recounts this history to Sarpedon (005-535-542), drawing a direct line from Laomedon's faithlessness to Troy's vulnerability to Greek attack. Heracles' sack of Troy is the precedent and foreshadowing of the greater destruction to come.

**Anchises:** Laomedon's relationship with Anchises, a prince of the junior royal line, is defined by Anchises' secret theft of the immortal horses' bloodline (005-231-233). Anchises' bold transgression — breeding his mares with the royal stallions "without Laomedon's knowledge" — suggests the internal tensions within Troy's ruling house and the ambition of its cadet branches.

**Tithonus, Lampus, Clytius, Hiketaon (sons):** Laomedon's legitimate sons, listed in Aeneas' genealogy (020-205-206), form the core of the Trojan aristocracy. Through them, Laomedon's line extends into every corner of Trojan society — the royal house (Priam), the warrior class (Lampus and his son Dolops), and the elders who counsel Priam on the walls of Troy.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Laomedon's death, and later traditions are contradictory. Some accounts say he was killed by Heracles during the first sack of Troy; others that he survived into Priam's reign and died of old age. Within the epic, Laomedon exists as a genealogical anchor and a moral exemplum — his death matters less than the legacy of faithlessness he bequeathed to his city. The repeated phrase "by reason of Laomedon's folly" (005-542) encapsulates his role: a king whose breach of trust with the gods created a debt that his descendants must pay.

## Scholarly Notes

Laomedon is one of the *Iliad*'s most important "off-stage" characters. Though he never appears, his actions structure the epic's theological and political landscape in three crucial ways. First, his betrayal of Poseidon and Apollo (Book XXI) provides the mythological explanation for Poseidon's implacable hostility to Troy — a divine enmity that shapes the course of the war. Second, his cheating of Heracles (Book V) establishes the precedent of Greek military success against Troy, foreshadowing the city's ultimate fall. Third, his genealogy provides the framework for Trojan aristocratic identity, with every major Trojan character traceable to his line.

The story of Laomedon's betrayal is part of a broader mythological tradition about the building of Troy's walls. Poseidon and Apollo, punished by Zeus for rebellion, were compelled to serve Laomedon for a year. Poseidon built the walls, Apollo tended the cattle, and Laomedon — true to form — refused to pay. The walls built by Poseidon become a central symbol in the *Iliad*: divinely constructed and therefore formidable, yet tainted by the faithlessness of the king who commissioned them.

The parallel between Laomedon's cheating of the gods and Paris' abduction of Helen — both acts of *hybris* against those to whom obligations are owed — is implicit but powerful. Laomedon's folly establishes a pattern of Trojan transgression that the epic's audience would recognize as the deeper cause of the city's destruction, beyond the immediate cause of the Trojan War.

In the Homeric Hymn to Aphrodite, Laomedon appears as a negative contrast to Anchises, whose piety the goddess rewards. This tradition reinforces the *Iliad*'s characterization of Laomedon as the archetypal bad king — one whose personal failings have catastrophic communal consequences.
