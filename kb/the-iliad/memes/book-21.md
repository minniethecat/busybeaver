# Book XXI

[021-001]BOOK XXI.

[021-002]The fight between [[achilles|Achilles]] and the river Scamander—The gods fight

[021-003]among themselves—Achilles drives the Trojans within their gates.

[021-004]Now when they came to the ford of the full-flowing river [[scamander|Xanthus]],

[021-005]begotten of immortal Jove, Achilles cut their forces in two: one

[021-006]half he chased over the plain towards the city by the same way

[021-007]that the Achaeans had taken when flying panic-stricken on the

[021-008]preceding day with [[hector|Hector]] in full triumph; this way did they fly

[021-009]pell-mell, and Juno sent down a thick mist in front of them to

[021-010]stay them. The other half were hemmed in by the deep

[021-011]silver-eddying stream, and fell into it with a great uproar. The

[021-012]waters resounded, and the banks rang again, as they swam hither

[021-013]and thither with loud cries amid the whirling eddies. As locusts

[021-014]flying to a river before the blast of a grass fire—the flame

[021-015]comes on and on till at last it overtakes them and they huddle

[021-016]into the water—even so was the eddying stream of Xanthus filled

[021-017]with the uproar of men and horses, all struggling in confusion

[021-018]before Achilles.

[021-019]Forthwith the hero left his spear upon the bank, leaning it

[021-020]against a tamarisk bush, and plunged into the river like a god,

[021-021]armed with his sword only. Fell was his purpose as he hewed the

[021-022]Trojans down on every side. Their dying groans rose hideous as

[021-023]the sword smote them, and the river ran red with blood. As when

[021-024]fish fly scared before a huge dolphin, and fill every nook and

[021-025]corner of some fair haven—for he is sure to eat all he can

[021-026]catch—even so did the Trojans cower under the banks of the mighty

[021-027]river, and when Achilles’ arms grew weary with killing them, he

[021-028]drew twelve youths alive out of the water, to sacrifice in

[021-029]revenge for [[patroclus|Patroclus]] son of Menoetius. He drew them out like

[021-030]dazed fawns, bound their hands behind them with the girdles of

[021-031]their own shirts, and gave them over to his men to take back to

[021-032]the [[achaean-camp|ships]]. Then he sprang into the river, thirsting for still

[021-033]further blood.

[021-034]There he found Lycaon, son of [[priam|Priam]] seed of Dardanus, as he was

[021-035]escaping out of the water; he it was whom he had once taken

[021-036]prisoner when he was in his father’s vineyard, having set upon

[021-037]him by night, as he was cutting young shoots from a wild fig-tree

[021-038]to make the wicker sides of a chariot. Achilles then caught him

[021-039]to his [[grief|sorrow]] unawares, and sent him by sea to Lemnos, where the

[021-040]son of Jason bought him. But a guest-friend, Eetion of Imbros,

[021-041]freed him with a great sum, and sent him to Arisbe, whence he had

[021-042]escaped and returned to his father’s house. He had spent eleven

[021-043]days happily with his friends after he had come from Lemnos, but

[021-044]on the twelfth heaven again delivered him into the hands of

[021-045]Achilles, who was to send him to the house of Hades sorely

[021-046]against his will. He was unarmed when Achilles caught sight of

[021-047]him, and had neither helmet nor shield; nor yet had he any spear,

[021-048]for he had thrown all his armour from him on to the bank, and was

[021-049]sweating with his struggles to get out of the river, so that his

[021-050]strength was now failing him.

[021-051]Then Achilles said to himself in his surprise, “What marvel do I

[021-052]see here? If this man can come back alive after having been sold

[021-053]over into Lemnos, I shall have the Trojans also whom I have slain

[021-054]rising from the world below. Could not even the waters of the

[021-055]grey sea imprison him, as they do many another whether he will or

[021-056]no? This time let him taste my spear, that I may know for certain

[021-057]whether mother earth who can keep even a strong man down, will be

[021-058]able to hold him, or whether thence too he will return.”

[021-059]Thus did he pause and ponder. But Lycaon came up to him dazed and

[021-060]trying hard to embrace his knees, for he would fain live, not

[021-061]die. Achilles thrust at him with his spear, meaning to kill him,

[021-062]but Lycaon ran crouching up to him and caught his knees, whereby

[021-063]the spear passed over his back, and stuck in the ground,

[021-064]hungering though it was for blood. With one hand he caught

[021-065]Achilles’ knees as he besought him, and with the other he

[021-066]clutched the spear and would not let it go. Then he said,

[021-067]“Achilles, have mercy upon me and spare me, for I am your

[021-068][[guest-friendship|suppliant]]. It was in your tents that I first broke bread on the

[021-069]day when you took me prisoner in the vineyard; after which you

[021-070]sold me away to Lemnos far from my father and my friends, and I

[021-071]brought you the price of a hundred oxen. I have paid three times

[021-072]as much to gain my freedom; it is but twelve days that I have

[021-073]come to Ilius after much suffering, and now cruel fate has again

[021-074]thrown me into your hands. Surely father Jove must hate me, that

[021-075]he has given me over to you a second time. Short of life indeed

[021-076]did my mother Laothoe bear me, daughter of aged Altes—of Altes

[021-077]who reigns over the warlike Lelegae and holds steep Pedasus on

[021-078]the river Satnioeis. Priam married his daughter along with many

[021-079]other women and two sons were born of her, both of whom you will

[021-080]have slain. Your spear slew noble Polydorus as he was fighting in

[021-081]the front ranks, and now evil will here befall me, for I fear

[021-082]that I shall not escape you since heaven has delivered me over to

[021-083]you. Furthermore I say, and lay my saying to your heart, spare

[021-084]me, for I am not of the same womb as Hector who slew your brave

[021-085]and noble comrade.”

[021-086]With such words did the princely son of Priam [[supplication|beseech]] Achilles;

[021-087]but Achilles answered him sternly. “Idiot,” said he, “talk not to

[021-088]me of ransom. Until Patroclus fell I preferred to give the

[021-089]Trojans quarter, and sold beyond the sea many of those whom I had

[021-090]taken alive; but now not a man shall live of those whom heaven

[021-091]delivers into my hands before the city of Ilius—and of all

[021-092]Trojans it shall fare hardest with the sons of Priam. Therefore,

[021-093]my friend, you too shall die. Why should you whine in this way?

[021-094]Patroclus fell, and he was a better man than you are. I too—see

[021-095]you not how I am great and goodly? I am son to a noble father,

[021-096]and have a goddess for my mother, but the hands of [[fate|doom]] and death

[021-097]overshadow me all as surely. The day will come, either at dawn or

[021-098]dark, or at the noontide, when one shall take my life also in

[021-099]battle, either with his spear, or with an arrow sped from his

[021-100]bow.”

[021-101]Thus did he speak, and Lycaon’s heart sank within him. He loosed

[021-102]his hold of the spear, and held out both hands before him; but

[021-103]Achilles drew his keen blade, and struck him by the collar-bone

[021-104]on his neck; he plunged his two-edged sword into him to the very

[021-105]hilt, whereon he lay at full length on the ground, with the dark

[021-106]blood welling from him till the earth was soaked. Then Achilles

[021-107]caught him by the foot and flung him into the river to go down

[021-108]stream, vaunting over him the while, and saying, “Lie there among

[021-109]the fishes, who will lick the blood from your wound and gloat

[021-110]over it; your mother shall not lay you on any bier to mourn you,

[021-111]but the eddies of Scamander shall bear you into the broad bosom

[021-112]of the sea. There shall the fishes feed on the fat of Lycaon as

[021-113]they dart under the dark ripple of the waters—so perish all of

[021-114]you till we reach the citadel of strong Ilius—you in flight, and

[021-115]I following after to destroy you. The river with its broad silver

[021-116]stream shall serve you in no stead, for all the bulls you offered

[021-117]him and all the horses that you flung living into his waters.

[021-118]None the less miserably shall you perish till there is not a man

[021-119]of you but has paid in full for the death of Patroclus and the

[021-120]havoc you wrought among the Achaeans whom you have slain while I

[021-121]held aloof from battle.”

[021-122]So spoke Achilles, but the river grew more and more angry, and

[021-123]pondered within himself how he should stay the hand of Achilles

[021-124]and save the Trojans from disaster. Meanwhile the son of Peleus,

[021-125]spear in hand, sprang upon Asteropaeus son of Pelegon to kill

[021-126]him. He was son to the broad river Axius and Periboea eldest

[021-127]daughter of Acessamenus; for the river had lain with her.

[021-128]Asteropaeus stood up out of the water to face him with a spear in

[021-129]either hand, and Xanthus filled him with courage, being angry for

[021-130]the death of the youths whom Achilles was slaying ruthlessly

[021-131]within his waters. When they were close up with one another

[021-132]Achilles was first to speak. “Who and whence are you,” said he,

[021-133]“who dare to face me? Woe to the parents whose son stands up

[021-134]against me.” And the son of Pelegon answered, “Great son of

[021-135]Peleus, why should you ask my lineage. I am from the fertile land

[021-136]of far Paeonia, captain of the Paeonians, and it is now eleven

[021-137]days that I am at Ilius. I am of the blood of the river Axius—of

[021-138]Axius that is the fairest of all rivers that run. He begot the

[021-139]famed warrior Pelegon, whose son men call me. Let us now fight,

[021-140]Achilles.”

[021-141]Thus did he defy him, and Achilles raised his spear of Pelian

[021-142]ash. Asteropaeus failed with both his spears, for he could use

[021-143]both hands alike; with the one spear he struck Achilles’ shield,

[021-144]but did not pierce it, for the layer of gold, gift of the god,

[021-145]stayed the point; with the other spear he grazed the elbow of

[021-146]Achilles’ right arm drawing dark blood, but the spear itself went

[021-147]by him and fixed itself in the ground, foiled of its bloody

[021-148]banquet. Then Achilles, fain to kill him, hurled his spear at

[021-149]Asteropaeus, but failed to hit him and struck the steep bank of

[021-150]the river, driving the spear half its length into the earth. The

[021-151]son of Peleus then drew his sword and sprang furiously upon him.

[021-152]Asteropaeus vainly tried to draw Achilles’ spear out of the bank

[021-153]by main force; thrice did he tug at it, trying with all his might

[021-154]to draw it out, and thrice he had to leave off trying; the fourth

[021-155]time he tried to bend and break it, but ere he could do so

[021-156]Achilles smote him with his sword and killed him. He struck him

[021-157]in the belly near the navel, so that all his bowels came gushing

[021-158]out on to the ground, and the darkness of death came over him as

[021-159]he lay gasping. Then Achilles set his foot on his chest and

[021-160]spoiled him of his armour, vaunting over him and saying, “Lie

[021-161]there—begotten of a river though you be, it is hard for you to

[021-162]strive with the offspring of Saturn’s son. You declare yourself

[021-163]sprung from the blood of a broad river, but I am of the seed of

[021-164]mighty Jove. My father is Peleus, son of Aeacus ruler over the

[021-165]many Myrmidons, and Aeacus was the son of Jove. Therefore as Jove

[021-166]is mightier than any river that flows into the sea, so are his

[021-167]children stronger than those of any river whatsoever. Moreover

[021-168]you have a great river hard by if he can be of any use to you,

[021-169]but there is no fighting against Jove the son of Saturn, with

[021-170]whom not even King Achelous can compare, nor the mighty stream of

[021-171]deep-flowing Oceanus, from whom all rivers and seas with all

[021-172]springs and deep wells proceed; even Oceanus fears the lightnings

[021-173]of great Jove, and his thunder that comes crashing out of

[021-174]heaven.”

[021-175]With this he drew his bronze spear out of the bank, and now that

[021-176]he had killed Asteropaeus, he let him lie where he was on the

[021-177]sand, with the dark water flowing over him and the eels and

[021-178]fishes busy nibbling and gnawing the fat that was about his

[021-179]kidneys. Then he went in chase of the Paeonians, who were flying

[021-180]along the bank of the river in panic when they saw their leader

[021-181]slain by the hands of the son of Peleus. Therein he slew

[021-182]Thersilochus, Mydon, Astypylus, Mnesus, Thrasius, Oeneus, and

[021-183]Ophelestes, and he would have slain yet others, had not the river

[021-184]in [[wrath|anger]] taken human form, and spoken to him from out the deep

[021-185]waters saying, “Achilles, if you excel all in strength, so do you

[021-186]also in wickedness, for the gods are ever with you to protect

[021-187]you: if, then, the son of Saturn has vouchsafed it to you to

[021-188]destroy all the Trojans, at any rate drive them out of my stream,

[021-189]and do your grim work on land. My fair waters are now filled with

[021-190]corpses, nor can I find any channel by which I may pour myself

[021-191]into the sea for I am choked with dead, and yet you go on

[021-192]mercilessly slaying. I am in despair, therefore, O captain of

[021-193]your host, trouble me no further.”

[021-194]Achilles answered, “So be it, Scamander, Jove-descended; but I

[021-195]will never cease dealing out death among the Trojans, till I have

[021-196]pent them up in their city, and made trial of Hector face to

[021-197]face, that I may learn whether he is to vanquish me, or I him.”

[021-198]As he spoke he set upon the Trojans with a fury like that of the

[021-199]gods. But the river said to Apollo, “Surely, son of Jove, lord of

[021-200]the silver bow, you are not obeying the commands of Jove who

[021-201]charged you straitly that you should stand by the Trojans and

[021-202]defend them, till twilight fades, and darkness is over the

[021-203]earth.”

[021-204]Meanwhile Achilles sprang from the bank into mid-stream, whereon

[021-205]the river raised a high wave and attacked him. He swelled his

[021-206]stream into a torrent, and swept away the many dead whom Achilles

[021-207]had slain and left within his waters. These he cast out on to the

[021-208]land, bellowing like a bull the while, but the living he saved

[021-209]alive, hiding them in his mighty eddies. The great and terrible

[021-210]wave gathered about Achilles, falling upon him and beating on his

[021-211]shield, so that he could not keep his feet; he caught hold of a

[021-212]great elm-tree, but it came up by the roots, and tore away the

[021-213]bank, damming the stream with its thick branches and bridging it

[021-214]all across; whereby Achilles struggled out of the stream, and

[021-215]fled full speed over the plain, for he was afraid.

[021-216]But the mighty god ceased not in his pursuit, and sprang upon him

[021-217]with a dark-crested wave, to stay his hands and save the Trojans

[021-218]from destruction. The son of Peleus darted away a spear’s throw

[021-219]from him; swift as the swoop of a black hunter-eagle which is the

[021-220]strongest and fleetest of all birds, even so did he spring

[021-221]forward, and the armour rang loudly about his breast. He fled on

[021-222]in front, but the river with a loud roar came tearing after. As

[021-223]one who would water his garden leads a stream from some fountain

[021-224]over his plants, and all his ground—spade in hand he clears away

[021-225]the dams to free the channels, and the little stones run rolling

[021-226]round and round with the water as it goes merrily down the bank

[021-227]faster than the man can follow—even so did the river keep

[021-228]catching up with Achilles albeit he was a fleet runner, for the

[021-229]gods are stronger than men. As often as he would strive to stand

[021-230]his ground, and see whether or no all the gods in heaven were in

[021-231]league against him, so often would the mighty wave come beating

[021-232]down upon his shoulders, and he would have to keep flying on and

[021-233]on in great dismay; for the angry flood was tiring him out as it

[021-234]flowed past him and ate the ground from under his feet.

[021-235]Then the son of Peleus lifted up his voice to heaven saying,

[021-236]“Father Jove, is there none of the gods who will take pity upon

[021-237]me, and save me from the river? I do not care what may happen to

[021-238]me afterwards. I blame none of the other dwellers on [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] so

[021-239]severely as I do my dear mother, who has beguiled and tricked me.

[021-240]She told me I was to fall under the walls of Troy by the flying

[021-241]arrows of Apollo; would that Hector, the best man among the

[021-242]Trojans, might there slay me; then should I fall a hero by the

[021-243]hand of a hero; whereas now it seems that I shall come to a most

[021-244]pitiable end, trapped in this river as though I were some

[021-245]swineherd’s boy, who gets carried down a torrent while trying to

[021-246]cross it during a storm.”

[021-247]As soon as he had spoken thus, Neptune and Minerva came up to him

[021-248]in the likeness of two men, and took him by the hand to reassure

[021-249]him. Neptune spoke first. “Son of Peleus,” said he, “be not so

[021-250]exceeding fearful; we are two gods, come with Jove’s sanction to

[021-251]assist you, I, and Pallas Minerva. It is not your fate to perish

[021-252]in this river; he will abate presently as you will see; moreover

[021-253]we strongly advise you, if you will be guided by us, not to stay

[021-254]your hand from fighting till you have pent the Trojan host within

[021-255]the famed walls of Ilius—as many of them as may escape. Then kill

[021-256]Hector and go back to the ships, for we will vouchsafe you a

[021-257]triumph over him.”

[021-258]When they had so said they went back to the other immortals, but

[021-259]Achilles strove onward over the plain, encouraged by the charge

[021-260]the gods had laid upon him. All was now covered with the flood of

[021-261]waters, and much goodly armour of the youths that had been slain

[021-262]was rifting about, as also many corpses, but he forced his way

[021-263]against the stream, speeding right onwards, nor could the broad

[021-264]waters stay him, for Minerva had endowed him with great strength.

[021-265]Nevertheless Scamander did not slacken in his pursuit, but was

[021-266]still more furious with the son of Peleus. He lifted his waters

[021-267]into a high crest and cried aloud to Simois saying, “Dear

[021-268]brother, let the two of us unite to save this man, or he will

[021-269]sack the mighty city of King Priam, and the Trojans will not hold

[021-270]out against him. Help me at once; fill your streams with water

[021-271]from their sources, rouse all your torrents to a fury; raise your

[021-272]wave on high, and let snags and stones come thundering down you

[021-273]that we may make an end of this savage creature who is now

[021-274]lording it as though he were a god. Nothing shall serve him

[021-275]longer, not strength nor comeliness, nor his fine armour, which

[021-276]forsooth shall soon be lying low in the deep waters covered over

[021-277]with mud. I will wrap him in sand, and pour tons of shingle round

[021-278]him, so that the Achaeans shall not know how to gather his bones

[021-279]for the silt in which I shall have hidden him, and when they

[021-280]celebrate his funeral they need build no barrow.”

[021-281]On this he upraised his tumultuous flood high against Achilles,

[021-282]seething as it was with foam and blood and the bodies of the

[021-283]dead. The dark waters of the river stood upright and would have

[021-284]overwhelmed the son of Peleus, but Juno, trembling lest Achilles

[021-285]should be swept away in the mighty torrent, lifted her voice on

[021-286]high and called out to Vulcan her son. “Crook-foot,” she cried,

[021-287]“my child, be up and doing, for I deem it is with you that

[021-288]Xanthus is fain to fight; help us at once, kindle a fierce fire;

[021-289]I will then bring up the west and the white south wind in a

[021-290]mighty hurricane from the sea, that shall bear the flames against

[021-291]the heads and armour of the Trojans and consume them, while you

[021-292]go along the banks of Xanthus burning his trees and wrapping him

[021-293]round with fire. Let him not turn you back neither by fair words

[021-294]nor foul, and slacken not till I shout and tell you. Then you may

[021-295]stay your flames.”

[021-296]On this Vulcan kindled a fierce fire, which broke out first upon

[021-297]the plain and burned the many dead whom Achilles had killed and

[021-298]whose bodies were lying about in great numbers; by this means the

[021-299]plain was dried and the flood stayed. As the north wind, blowing

[021-300]on an orchard that has been sodden with autumn rain, soon dries

[021-301]it, and the heart of the owner is glad—even so the whole plain

[021-302]was dried and the dead bodies were consumed. Then he turned

[021-303]tongues of fire on to the river. He burned the elms the willows

[021-304]and the tamarisks, the lotus also, with the rushes and marshy

[021-305]herbage that grew abundantly by the banks of the river. The eels

[021-306]and fishes that go darting about everywhere in the water, these,

[021-307]too, were sorely harassed by the flames that cunning Vulcan had

[021-308]kindled, and the river himself was scalded, so that he spoke

[021-309]saying, “Vulcan, there is no god can hold his own against you. I

[021-310]cannot fight you when you flare out your flames in this way;

[021-311]strive with me no longer. Let Achilles drive the Trojans out of

[021-312]their city immediately. What have I to do with quarrelling and

[021-313]helping people?”

[021-314]He was boiling as he spoke, and all his waters were seething. As

[021-315]a cauldron upon a large fire boils when it is melting the lard of

[021-316]some fatted hog, and the lard keeps bubbling up all over when the

[021-317]dry faggots blaze under it—even so were the goodly waters of

[021-318]Xanthus heated with the fire till they were boiling. He could

[021-319]flow no longer but stayed his stream, so afflicted was he by the

[021-320]blasts of fire which cunning Vulcan had raised. Then he prayed to

[021-321]Juno and besought her saying, “Juno, why should your son vex my

[021-322]stream with such especial fury? I am not so much to blame as all

[021-323]the others are who have been helping the Trojans. I will leave

[021-324]off, since you so desire it, and let your son leave off also.

[021-325]Furthermore I [[oath|swear]] never again will I do anything to save the

[021-326]Trojans from destruction, not even when all Troy is burning in

[021-327]the flames which the Achaeans will kindle.”

[021-328]As soon as Juno heard this she said to her son Vulcan, “Son

[021-329]Vulcan, hold now your flames; we ought not to use such violence

[021-330]against a god for the sake of mortals.”

[021-331]When she had thus spoken Vulcan quenched his flames, and the

[021-332]river went back once more into his own fair bed.

[021-333]Xanthus was now beaten, so these two left off fighting, for Juno

[021-334]stayed them though she was still angry; but a furious quarrel

[021-335]broke out among the other gods, for they were of divided

[021-336]counsels. They fell on one another with a mighty uproar—earth

[021-337]groaned, and the spacious firmament rang out as with a blare of

[021-338]trumpets. Jove heard as he was sitting on Olympus, and laughed

[021-339]for joy when he saw the gods coming to blows among themselves.

[021-340]They were not long about beginning, and Mars piercer of shields

[021-341]opened the battle. Sword in hand he sprang at once upon Minerva

[021-342]and reviled her. “Why, vixen,” said he, “have you again set the

[021-343]gods by the ears in the pride and haughtiness of your heart? Have

[021-344]you forgotten how you set Diomed son of Tydeus on to wound me,

[021-345]and yourself took visible spear and drove it into me to the hurt

[021-346]of my fair body? You shall now suffer for what you then did to

[021-347]me.”

[021-348]As he spoke he struck her on the terrible tasselled aegis—so

[021-349]terrible that not even can Jove’s lightning pierce it. Here did

[021-350]murderous Mars strike her with his great spear. She drew back and

[021-351]with her strong hand seized a stone that was lying on the

[021-352]plain—great and rugged and black—which men of old had set for the

[021-353]boundary of a field. With this she struck Mars on the neck, and

[021-354]brought him down. Nine roods did he cover in his fall, and his

[021-355]hair was all soiled in the dust, while his armour rang rattling

[021-356]round him. But Minerva laughed and vaunted over him saying,

[021-357]“Idiot, have you not learned how far stronger I am than you, but

[021-358]you must still match yourself against me? Thus do your mother’s

[021-359]curses now roost upon you, for she is angry and would do you

[021-360]mischief because you have deserted the Achaeans and are helping
