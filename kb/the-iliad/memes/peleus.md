# Peleus

King of the Myrmidons in Phthia, father of Achilles by the sea-goddess Thetis, and a former Argonaut — Peleus himself never appears on the battlefield of Troy, but his shadow falls across the entire epic through the figure of his son.

## Identity

Peleus is the son of Aeacus, king of Aegina, and thus belongs to the Aeacid line of heroes. He is consistently referred to in the *Iliad* by the patronymic formula "son of Peleus" (*Pēlēïádēs* or *Pēlḗïos huios*), which becomes Achilles' most common epithet — a constant reminder that Achilles' identity is inseparable from his father's legacy.

In the Catalogue of Ships, the Myrmidon contingent is introduced not by Achilles' name but by reference to Peleus' son (002-545). Elsewhere, Peleus is remembered as "counsellor and orator among the Myrmidons" (007-099), a man valued for wisdom as well as martial prowess. These scattered references construct an image of Peleus as the ideal Homeric king: pious — the gods themselves attended his wedding to Thetis — wealthy in flocks and land, and a father who shaped his son's character through instruction.

His marriage to Thetis is the mythological fulcrum of the Trojan War tradition. According to the *Cypria*, it was at this wedding that Eris threw the golden apple inscribed "to the fairest," leading to the Judgment of Paris. The *Iliad* suppresses this direct chain of causation, but the marriage remains central: Thetis' ongoing intervention on Achilles' behalf — pleading with Zeus in Book I, commissioning new armour from Hephaestus in Book XVIII — is motivated entirely by her love for her mortal son and her grief that he was born to a father who must die.

## Role in the Epic

Though absent from Troy, Peleus is a constant presence in the epic's memory, appearing in three distinct dimensions: as the father who sent a son to war, as the measure of the wealth and home that war has taken from Achilles, and as the generous host who received exiles into his house.

**The Father Who Sent a Son to War:** In Book IX, Phoenix reminds Achilles of the day "your father Peleus sent you from Phthia to Agamemnon" (009-216), recalling that Peleus charged Phoenix with the boy's education: "He sent me to teach you to be a speaker of words and a doer of deeds" (009-340). Achilles himself recalls that Peleus "bade me go with you when he sent you as a mere lad from Phthia to Agamemnon" (009-361), and reflects that his father "charged me to be ever the best and to outvie my peers" — a paternal injunction that becomes the motto of Achilles' heroic career and, paradoxically, the source of his fatal pride.

**The Measure of Wealth and Home:** Achilles twice contrasts the wealth of Peleus' house with the value of his own life. In the embassy to Achilles, he declares that not all the riches of "my old father Peleus" (009-335) — not Orchomenus, not Egyptian Thebes with its hundred gates — can compensate for the loss of life. Later, in Book XXIV, Achilles reflects on his father with a new tenderness: "Peleus, my father, had no other sons to care for him in his old age" (024-459). The thought of Peleus waiting at home for a son who will never return is one of the epic's most poignant motifs — and it is this very image that Priam deploys in his supplication of Achilles, asking him to "think of your own father" (024-440).

**The Host of Exiles:** Peleus is remembered as a generous king who took in men driven from their own lands. Phoenix, exiled from his father's house after a family catastrophe, recalls: "I came to Phthia, mother of sheep, and to King Peleus, who made me welcome" (009-392). Patroclus, too, was received by Peleus as a boy after accidentally killing a playmate. This quality — the capacity to receive and honour the displaced — is the foundation of the loyalty that Phoenix and Patroclus both feel toward Achilles' house, a loyalty that costs Patroclus his life.

**The Armour and the Spear:** Peleus' legacy is also embodied in physical objects. The great Pelian ash spear, cut by Chiron and given to Peleus, passes to Achilles — a weapon so massive that "no other man among the Achaeans could wield it" (016-127). And it is the divine armour that Peleus received as a wedding gift from the gods — forged by Hephaestus — that Thetis commissions anew for Achilles after Hector strips the old set from Patroclus' body.

## Notable Speeches

Peleus himself speaks no words in the *Iliad*, but his words are recalled by others, and these recollections give him an almost elegiac quality — a figure from a nobler, earlier age.

> "My good friend, when your father Peleus sent you from Phthia to Agamemnon, you were still a mere lad, with no knowledge of war or of the arts of the assembly. He sent me with you to teach you to be a speaker of words and a doer of deeds." — Phoenix recalling Peleus' paternal charge (009-340)

> "I came to Phthia, mother of sheep, and to King Peleus, who made me welcome. He gave me many gifts and made me ruler over the Dolopians, who live on the borders of Phthia." — Phoenix's memory of exile and refuge (009-392)

> "Achilles answered, 'Ulysses, noble son of Laertes... the riches of my old father Peleus are beyond measure — cattle, sheep, tripods, and gold — but all the wealth of Orchomenus or of Egyptian Thebes could not buy back my life.'" (009-335-340)

> In Book XI, Nestor remembers visiting Peleus' house during a recruiting expedition: "Peleus, the old horseman, was burning the fat of oxen to Jove in his courtyard, holding a golden cup and pouring wine upon the offering" (011-670-672). This image — Peleus at prayer, golden cup in hand, offering sacrifice — distills the piety and prosperity of the old king's household in a single visual moment.

## Relationships

**Thetis (wife):** The sea-goddess, married to Peleus in a wedding attended by all the gods. Their marriage, a union of mortal and divine, makes Achilles semi-divine — and therefore fated for either a long, obscure life or a short, glorious one. Thetis' grief, her pleading with Zeus, and her commissioning of new armour are all ultimately expressions of her love for Peleus' son.

**Achilles (son):** The relationship between Peleus and his absent son defines the *Iliad*'s emotional register. Achilles is forever "the son of Peleus," and his tears by the shore in Book I are answered by a mother, not a father — Peleus is too far away, an old man waiting in an empty hall. The paternal bond, stretched across the Aegean, is one of the invisible threads that holds the epic together.

**Phoenix (follower and tutor):** An exiled prince whom Peleus welcomed and made rich. Phoenix's loyalty to Achilles is loyalty to Peleus' house — a debt of honour that binds the generations. His speech in Book IX, recalling Peleus' trust and generosity, is one of the epic's most sustained acts of recollection.

**Patroclus (follower):** Another exile received by Peleus. Patroclus, like Phoenix, owes his place in the world to Peleus' generosity, and repays it with his life at Achilles' side. The pattern — Peleus receives the outcast, the outcast serves Peleus' son — is one of the poem's most consistent ethical threads.

**Hector (son's adversary):** Peleus and Hector never meet, but the whole trajectory of the poem — from Achilles' withdrawal to Hector's death — is, in a sense, Peleus' tragedy too: the loss of the son whose return he awaits. When Achilles gazes at Priam in Book XXIV, he sees his own father — and this moment of recognition, achieved through the mirror of Priam's grief, is the closest the poem comes to bringing Peleus onto the stage.

**Priam (mirror-father):** The parallel between Peleus and Priam — two aging kings who lose their greatest sons — is explicitly drawn in Book XXIV, when Priam's supplication forces Achilles to think of his own father. This mirroring makes the two absent/present fathers into a structural pair, each illuminating the other's loss.

## Death or Fate

Peleus does not die in the *Iliad*. He waits in Phthia, an aging king whose sole heir fights a distant war. The pathos of Peleus' situation is drawn out by Achilles himself: "Peleus, my father, had no other sons to care for him in his old age" (024-459) — an old man with a single son, and that son far away, and likely never to return. Later tradition records that Peleus was eventually driven from Phthia after Achilles' death and died in exile, but the *Iliad* holds him suspended in waiting — a permanent, unresolved absence that is itself the poem's most sustained image of what war costs those who stay behind.

## Scholarly Notes

Peleus' role in the *Iliad* exemplifies a distinctively Homeric technique: the construction of a character through absence and recollection. He is evoked more than fifty times, almost always through the patronymic formula applied to Achilles, yet never appears. This pattern — the father who exists only as his son's identifier — has been read by scholars as a structural device that foregrounds the *Iliad*'s central concern with mortality and succession. Every time Achilles is called "son of Peleus," the epithet carries a double meaning: it identifies the hero and reminds the audience that Peleus will lose him.

The marriage of Peleus and Thetis is the mythological hinge between the age of gods and the age of heroes. In the *Cypria*, it was the catalyst for the Trojan War; in the *Iliad*, it is the source of Thetis' ongoing grief and intervention. The poem's suppression of the direct causal chain — Eris, the apple, the Judgment — has been much discussed. One influential reading holds that the *Iliad* deliberately backgrounds the mythological apparatus of the war's origin to foreground its human consequences: the divine wedding matters not because it triggered the war but because it produced Achilles, the poem's subject.

The parallel between Peleus and Priam in Book XXIV is one of the most celebrated structural features of the *Iliad*. Both are aging fathers, both have lost (or will lose) their greatest son, and both are defined by their paternal love. When Priam kisses the hand of the man who killed his son, Achilles sees his own father — and weeps. This moment of recognition across enemy lines is the poem's moral climax, and Peleus, though absent, is its catalyst.
