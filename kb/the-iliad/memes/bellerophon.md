## Identity

Bellerophon is a Corinthian hero of an earlier generation, introduced in the *Iliad* through the genealogy of Glaucus the Lycian. Glaucus identifies himself as the son of Hippolochus, who was the son of Bellerophon (006-132), establishing Bellerophon as grandfather to one of Troy's most prominent allied commanders. He is described as a man "whom heaven endowed with the most surpassing comeliness and beauty" (006-132), and his homeland is Ephyra (Corinth), "the land of the Argives, over which Jove had made [Proetus] ruler" (006-136).

Bellerophon's lineage traces back through Glaucus to Sisyphus, the craftiest of mankind: "He was the son of Aeolus, and had a son named Glaucus, who was father to Bellerophon" (006-130-132). This descent from Sisyphus through Glaucus to Bellerophon and then to Hippolochus and Glaucus the Lycian connects the Trojan ally to a distinguished Corinthian heroic line.

## Role in the Epic

Bellerophon does not appear in person in the *Iliad* — he belongs to the generation before the Trojan War — but his story occupies one of the longest embedded narratives in the poem, recounted by his grandson Glaucus during his encounter with Diomedes in Book VI.

**The Encounter with Diomedes (Book VI):** When Diomedes and Glaucus meet in battle, Diomedes asks Glaucus to identify himself. Glaucus responds with a lengthy account of his ancestry, centered on the story of Bellerophon (006-130-187). This narrative — the "Story of Bellerophon" — is introduced by the chapter heading and serves a dual purpose: it establishes Glaucus' heroic lineage and provides the basis for Diomedes' recognition that their grandfathers were guest-friends, leading to their famous exchange of armour.

**The Chimaera and the Labours:** Bellerophon's story follows the classic pattern of the hero who must prove himself through impossible tasks. After the wife of Proetus falsely accused him of attempting to seduce her, Proetus sent Bellerophon to Lycia with a sealed letter commanding his death. The Lycian king set Bellerophon three tasks: to kill the Chimaera — "who was not a human being, but a goddess, for she had the head of a lion and the tail of a serpent, while her body was that of a goat, and she breathed forth flames of fire" (006-148-152); to fight the Solymi, which he called "the hardest of all his battles" (006-156); and to defeat the Amazons, "women who were the peers of men" (006-157). Bellerophon accomplished all three, "guided by signs from heaven" (006-155).

**The Ambush and Recognition:** When the king set an ambush of Lycia's bravest warriors, "not a man ever came back, for Bellerophon killed every one of them" (006-161). Only then did the king recognize Bellerophon's divine heritage, giving him his daughter in marriage and half his kingdom (006-163).

> "The king's daughter bore Bellerophon three children, Isander, Hippolochus, and Laodameia. Jove, the lord of counsel, lay with Laodameia, and she bore him noble Sarpedon." (006-168-171)

## Relationships

**Glaucus (grandson):** Bellerophon's defining relationship in the *Iliad* is with his grandson Glaucus, who recounts his story to Diomedes. Glaucus inherits Bellerophon's guest-friendship with the house of Oeneus, which saves his life in battle and leads to the exchange of golden armour for bronze (006-188-190).

**Diomedes:** Through the guest-friendship between Bellerophon and Oeneus, Diomedes — Oeneus' grandson — recognizes Glaucus as a hereditary friend. "Great Oeneus once entertained Bellerophon for twenty days, and the two exchanged presents" (006-184-185). This ancient bond halts their combat and transforms enemies into allies.

**Proetus and Antea:** Bellerophon's troubles originate at the court of Proetus, king of Argos, whose wife Antea "lusted after him" (006-136). When Bellerophon rejected her advances, she falsely accused him, setting in motion the chain of events that led to his exile and heroic labours.

**Hippolochus (son):** Bellerophon's son Hippolochus is the father of Glaucus, forming the genealogical bridge between Bellerophon's heroic generation and the Trojan War. Hippolochus sent Glaucus to Troy with the command "to fight ever among the foremost and outvie my peers" (006-180).

**Laodameia (daughter):** Bellerophon's daughter Laodameia bore Sarpedon to Zeus, making Bellerophon the mortal grandfather of one of Troy's greatest allied commanders: "Jove, the lord of counsel, lay with Laodameia, and she bore him noble Sarpedon" (006-170-171).

## Death or Fate

Bellerophon's end is one of the most haunting passages in the *Iliad*. After his heroic triumphs, "when Bellerophon came to be hated by all the gods, he wandered all desolate and dismayed upon the Alean plain, gnawing at his own heart, and shunning the path of man" (006-170-173). His son Isander was killed by Ares "insatiate of battle" (006-173). This trajectory — from divine favour and heroic achievement to divine hatred and lonely despair — makes Bellerophon a paradigm of the Greek conception of heroic tragedy: the hero who rises too high and falls into the gods' displeasure.

## Scholarly Notes

Bellerophon's story is one of the earliest and most complete "hero pattern" narratives in Greek literature. It follows the structure that would later be formalized in myths of Heracles, Theseus, and Perseus: a false accusation, impossible labours imposed by a hostile king, divine assistance, triumph, recognition, marriage to a king's daughter, and eventual downfall. The narrative is embedded within Glaucus' battlefield self-identification — a common Homeric technique in which a hero's genealogy serves as both introduction and battlefield rhetoric.

The Chimaera, described in vivid hybrid terms, is one of the earliest monster descriptions in Western literature. Its triple nature — lion, goat, serpent — has parallels in Near Eastern iconography, and Bellerophon's slaying of it (mounted on the winged horse Pegasus, though the *Iliad* does not mention Pegasus by name) became one of the most popular subjects in Greek art.

The guest-friendship between Bellerophon and Oeneus is the hinge on which the Diomedes-Glaucus encounter turns. The exchange of presents — "Oeneus gave a belt rich with purple, and Bellerophon a double cup" (006-186-187) — creates an obligation of hospitality that survives across three generations, preventing Diomedes and Glaucus from fighting. Their exchange of armour — Glaucus' golden armour (worth a hundred oxen) for Diomedes' bronze (worth nine) — is one of the most famous and puzzling gestures in the epic, interpreted variously as an act of extraordinary generosity, a testament to the binding power of guest-friendship, or a moment of near-comic disproportion.

In later Greek tradition, Bellerophon attempts to ride Pegasus to Olympus, only to be thrown down by Zeus — a detail absent from the *Iliad* but preserved in Pindar and later sources. The *Iliad*'s version, in which Bellerophon simply becomes hated by the gods and wanders alone, is notably more restrained and psychologically complex.
