# Corslet of Diomed

The Corslet of Diomed is the bronze body-armour worn by the son of Tydeus during the most remarkable single day of warfare in the *Iliad* — his *[[battle-fury|aristeia]]* in Book 5. Unlike the divine panoply of [[achilles|Achilles]] or the golden armour of [[glaucus|Glaucus]], Diomed's corslet is the work of mortal hands. Yet under its protection, and with the goddess [[athena|Athena]] pouring strength into his limbs, Diomed accomplishes what no other mortal in the epic achieves: he wounds Aphrodite in the wrist and drives Ares himself from the battlefield. The corslet is a testament to what mortal craft, combined with divine favour, can withstand.

## Description

The corslet (θώρηξ) is a bronze breastplate covering the torso from shoulders to waist, fastened over a leather under-girdle. Homer refers to it as "gleaming" and notes its solid construction — it is not the ornate, divinely wrought armour of a god-born hero, but the practical battle-gear of an Argive captain.

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Material | Bronze plate, probably hammered and fitted |
| Cove[[wrath|rage]] | Torso — chest, belly, and sides |
| Under-girdle | A leather belt worn beneath the bronze plate (011-209) |
| Distinguishing marks | None described — a functional piece, not an heirloom |
| Condition in battle | Withstood multiple strikes; cracked but not broken in Book 13 |

The corslet is several times pierced or struck in combat — at one point an arrow from [[bow-of-pandarus|Pandarus' bow]] passes through it into Diomed's shoulder (005-097). Yet the armour does not fail catastrophically; Diomed fights on through the wound, a testament both to his endurance and to the corslet's soundness.

## Origin and Ownership

The corslet's origin is not mythologized. It is not a gift from Cheiron like the [[spear-of-achilles|Pelian spear]], nor the work of Hephaestus like the [[shield-of-achilles|Shield of Achilles]]. It belongs to the world of mortal warfare — a piece of gear from the Argive armoury, brought to Troy among the eighty [[achaean-camp|ships]] Diomed led from Argos and Tiryns (002-455).

| Order | Owner | Context |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Unknown Argive smith | Forged in Argos or Tiryns, cities under Diomed's command |
| 2 | Diomed son of Tydeus | Worn throughout the Trojan campaign; his primary body-armour |

The absence of a divine or heroic origin is itself [[prophecy|sign]]ificant. Diomed is the *Iliad*'s supreme example of mortal [[aristeia|excellence]] unaided by divine blood. His armour, like his [[aristeia|prowess]], is the product of human effort operating at its highest pitch — amplified, but not created, by Athena's favour.

## Role in the Epic

The corslet's defining m[[prophecy|omen]]ts come in three Books:

- **Book 4**: Diomed enters the battle in full armour. When Pandarus' arrow strikes, it passes through the corslet's shoulder-piece and draws blood. Diomed's response — praying to Athena, who grants him the power to distinguish gods from mortals on the battlefield — transforms the wound into the prelude to his greatest day (004-314).

- **Book 5 — The Aristeia** (005-001–005-907): Wearing the same corslet, Diomed [[wrath|rage]]s through the Trojan ranks. He kills Pandarus, wounds Aeneas, and drives his spear into Aphrodite's wrist when she tries to rescue her son. Later he attacks Apollo himself (and is rebuffed), then, with Athena standing beside him in his chariot, drives his spear into Ares' belly (005-745). Throughout this long catalogue of violence, the corslet holds.

- **Book 13**: A Trojan spear strikes the corslet's plate but does not pierce through; the bronze cracks but saves Diomed's life (013-445). The m[[prophecy|omen]]t encapsulates the corslet's nature: it can be damaged, but it does not fail its wearer.

## Symbolism

Diomed's corslet embodies a distinct Homeric theme:

- **Mortal Worth Without Divine Pedigree**: Diomed is not the son of a god, and his armour is not divine handiwork. His achievement — wounding two immortals — is the more extraordinary for being accomplished with mortal equipment. The corslet represents the upper limit of human capability: good but not invincible, strong but not unbreakable.

- **The Armour That Bears the Scar**: Every dent and scratch on the corslet is a record of combat survived. Unlike the inviolate [[armour-of-achilles|Armour of Achilles]], which deflects all blows, Diomed's corslet receives them and testifies to its wearer's endurance.

- **Athena's Partnership**: The goddess empowers the man but does not replace his equipment. Diomed fights in his own bronze, with his own strength — augmented, not supplanted, by divine will.

## Notable Passages

The m[[prophecy|omen]]t when Pandarus' arrow strikes is one of the *Iliad*'s most vivid wound-descriptions:

> "He aimed and drew the string to his shoulder, and the sharp arrow flew; it struck the right shoulder of Diomed through the plate of his corslet, and the barbs passed through, so that the blood ran down and stained the corslet." (005-097)

The detail that matters is not the failure of the armour but Diomed's reaction: he does not retreat. He prays. And Athena answers.
