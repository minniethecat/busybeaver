# Book XVII

[017-001]BOOK XVII.

[017-002]The fight around the body of [[patroclus|Patroclus]].

[017-003]Brave Menelaus son of Atreus now came to know that Patroclus had

[017-004]fallen, and made his way through the front ranks clad in full

[017-005]armour to bestride him. As a cow stands lowing over her first

[017-006]calf, even so did yellow-haired Menelaus bestride Patroclus. He

[017-007]held his round shield and his spear in front of him, resolute to

[017-008]kill any who should dare face him. But the son of Panthous had

[017-009]also noted the body, and came up to Menelaus saying, “Menelaus,

[017-010]son of Atreus, draw back, leave the body, and let the

[017-011]blood-stained spoils be. I was first of the Trojans and their

[017-012]brave allies to drive my spear into Patroclus, let me, therefore,

[017-013]have my full [[honor|glory]] among the Trojans, or I will take aim and kill

[017-014]you.”

[017-015]To this Menelaus answered in great [[wrath|anger]] “By father Jove,

[017-016]boasting is an ill thing. The pard is not more bold, nor the lion

[017-017]nor savage wild boar, which is fiercest and most dauntless of all

[017-018]creatures, than are the proud sons of Panthous. Yet Hyperenor did

[017-019]not see out the days of his youth when he made light of me and

[017-020]withstood me, deeming me the meanest soldier among the Danaans.

[017-021]His own feet never bore him back to gladden his wife and parents.

[017-022]Even so shall I make an end of you too, if you withstand me; get

[017-023]you back into the crowd and do not face me, or it shall be worse

[017-024]for you. Even a fool may be wise after the event.”

[017-025]Euphorbus would not listen, and said, “Now indeed, Menelaus,

[017-026]shall you pay for the death of my brother over whom you vaunted,

[017-027]and whose wife you widowed in her bridal chamber, while you

[017-028]brought grief unspeakable on his parents. I shall comfort these

[017-029]poor people if I bring your head and armour and place them in the

[017-030]hands of Panthous and noble Phrontis. The time is come when this

[017-031]matter shall be fought out and settled, for me or against me.”

[017-032]As he spoke he struck Menelaus full on the shield, but the spear

[017-033]did not go through, for the shield turned its point. Menelaus

[017-034]then took aim, praying to father Jove as he did so; Euphorbus was

[017-035]drawing back, and Menelaus struck him about the roots of his

[017-036]throat, leaning his whole weight on the spear, so as to drive it

[017-037]home. The point went clean through his neck, and his armour rang

[017-038]rattling round him as he fell heavily to the ground. His hair

[017-039]which was like that of the Graces, and his locks so deftly bound

[017-040]in bands of silver and gold, were all bedrabbled with blood. As

[017-041]one who has grown a fine young olive tree in a clear space where

[017-042]there is abundance of water—the plant is full of promise, and

[017-043]though the winds beat upon it from every quarter it puts forth

[017-044]its white blossoms till the blasts of some fierce hurricane sweep

[017-045]down upon it and level it with the ground—even so did Menelaus

[017-046]strip the fair youth Euphorbus of his armour after he had slain

[017-047]him. Or as some fierce lion upon the mountains in the pride of

[017-048]his strength fastens on the finest heifer in a herd as it is

[017-049]feeding—first he breaks her neck with his strong jaws, and then

[017-050]gorges on her blood and entrails; dogs and shepherds raise a hue

[017-051]and cry against him, but they stand aloof and will not come close

[017-052]to him, for they are pale with fear—even so no one had the

[017-053]courage to face valiant Menelaus. The son of Atreus would have

[017-054]then carried off the armour of the son of Panthous with ease, had

[017-055]not Phoebus Apollo been angry, and in the guise of Mentes chief

[017-056]of the Cicons incited [[hector|Hector]] to attack him. “Hector,” said he,

[017-057]“you are now going after the horses of the noble son of Aeacus,

[017-058]but you will not take them; they cannot be kept in hand and

[017-059]driven by mortal man, save only by [[achilles|Achilles]], who is son to an

[017-060]immortal mother. Meanwhile Menelaus son of Atreus has bestridden

[017-061]the body of Patroclus and killed the noblest of the Trojans,

[017-062]Euphorbus son of Panthous, so that he can fight no more.”

[017-063]The god then went back into the toil and turmoil, but the soul of

[017-064]Hector was darkened with a cloud of grief; he looked along the

[017-065]ranks and saw Euphorbus lying on the ground with the blood still

[017-066]flowing from his wound, and Menelaus stripping him of his armour.

[017-067]On this he made his way to the front like a flame of fire, clad

[017-068]in his gleaming armour, and crying with a loud voice. When the

[017-069]son of Atreus heard him, he said to himself in his dismay, “Alas!

[017-070]what shall I do? I may not let the Trojans take the armour of

[017-071]Patroclus who has fallen fighting on my behalf, lest some Danaan

[017-072]who sees me should cry shame upon me. Still if for my honour’s

[017-073]sake I fight Hector and the Trojans single-handed, they will

[017-074]prove too many for me, for Hector is bringing them up in force.

[017-075]Why, however, should I thus hesitate? When a man fights in

[017-076]despite of heaven with one whom a god befriends, he will soon rue

[017-077]it. Let no Danaan think ill of me if I give place to Hector, for

[017-078]the hand of heaven is with him. Yet, if I could find Ajax, the

[017-079]two of us would fight Hector and heaven too, if we might only

[017-080]save the body of Patroclus for Achilles son of Peleus. This, of

[017-081]many evils would be the least.”

[017-082]While he was thus in two minds, the Trojans came up to him with

[017-083]Hector at their head; he therefore drew back and left the body,

[017-084]turning about like some bearded lion who is being chased by dogs

[017-085]and men from a stockyard with spears and hue and cry, whereon he

[017-086]is daunted and slinks sulkily off—even so did Menelaus son of

[017-087]Atreus turn and leave the body of Patroclus. When among the body

[017-088]of his men, he looked around for mighty Ajax son of Telamon, and

[017-089]presently saw him on the extreme left of the fight, cheering on

[017-090]his men and exhorting them to keep on fighting, for Phoebus

[017-091]Apollo had spread a great panic among them. He ran up to him and

[017-092]said, “Ajax, my good friend, come with me at once to dead

[017-093]Patroclus, if so be that we may take the body to Achilles—as for

[017-094]his armour, Hector already has it.”

[017-095]These words stirred the heart of Ajax, and he made his way among

[017-096]the front ranks, Menelaus going with him. Hector had stripped

[017-097]Patroclus of his armour, and was dragging him away to cut off his

[017-098]head and take the body to fling before the dogs of Troy. But Ajax

[017-099]came up with his shield like wall before him, on which Hector

[017-100]withdrew under shelter of his men, and sprang on to his chariot,

[017-101]giving the armour over to the Trojans to take to the city, as a

[017-102]great trophy for himself; Ajax, therefore, covered the body of

[017-103]Patroclus with his broad shield and bestrode him; as a lion

[017-104]stands over his whelps if hunters have come upon him in a forest

[017-105]when he is with his little ones—in the pride and fierceness of

[017-106]his strength he draws his knit brows down till they cover his

[017-107]eyes—even so did Ajax bestride the body of Patroclus, and by his

[017-108]side stood Menelaus son of Atreus, nursing great [[grief|sorrow]] in his

[017-109]heart.

[017-110]Then Glaucus son of Hippolochus looked fiercely at Hector and

[017-111]rebuked him sternly. “Hector,” said he, “you make a brave show,

[017-112]but in fight you are sadly wanting. A runaway like yourself has

[017-113]no claim to so great a reputation. Think how you may now save

[017-114]your town and citadel by the hands of your own people born in

[017-115]Ilius; for you will get no Lycians to fight for you, seeing what

[017-116]thanks they have had for their incessant hardships. Are you

[017-117]likely, sir, to do anything to help a man of less note, after

[017-118]leaving Sarpedon, who was at once your guest and comrade in arms,

[017-119]to be the [[prize|spoil]] and prey of the Danaans? So long as he lived he

[017-120]did good service both to your city and yourself; yet you had no

[017-121]stomach to save his body from the dogs. If the Lycians will

[017-122]listen to me, they will go home and leave Troy to its fate. If

[017-123]the Trojans had any of that daring fearless spirit which lays

[017-124]hold of men who are fighting for their country and harassing

[017-125]those who would attack it, we should soon bear off Patroclus into

[017-126]Ilius. Could we get this dead man away and bring him into the

[017-127]city of [[priam|Priam]], the Argives would readily give up the armour of

[017-128]Sarpedon, and we should get his body to boot. For he whose squire

[017-129]has been now killed is the foremost man at the [[achaean-camp|ships]] of the

[017-130]Achaeans—he and his close fighting followers. Nevertheless you

[017-131]dared not make a stand against Ajax, nor face him, eye to eye,

[017-132]with battle all round you, for he is a braver man than you are.”

[017-133]Hector scowled at him and answered, “Glaucus, you should know

[017-134]better. I have held you so far as a man of more understanding

[017-135]than any in all Lycia, but now I despise you for saying that I am

[017-136]afraid of Ajax. I fear neither battle nor the din of chariots,

[017-137]but Jove’s will is stronger than ours; Jove at one time makes

[017-138]even a strong man draw back and snatches victory from his grasp,

[017-139]while at another he will set him on to fight. Come hither then,

[017-140]my friend, stand by me and see indeed whether I shall play the

[017-141]coward the whole day through as you say, or whether I shall not

[017-142]stay some even of the boldest Danaans from fighting round the

[017-143]body of Patroclus.”

[017-144]As he spoke he called loudly on the Trojans saying, “Trojans,

[017-145]Lycians, and Dardanians, fighters in close combat, be men, my

[017-146]friends, and fight might and main, while I put on the goodly

[017-147]armour of Achilles, which I took when I killed Patroclus.”

## Section 50

[017-148]With this Hector left the fight, and ran full speed after his men

[017-149]who were taking the armour of Achilles to Troy, but had not yet

[017-150]got far. Standing for a while apart from the woeful fight, he

[017-151]changed his armour. His own he sent to the strong city of Ilius

[017-152]and to the Trojans, while he put on the immortal armour of the

[017-153]son of Peleus, which the gods had given to Peleus, who in his age

[017-154]gave it to his son; but the son did not grow old in his father’s

[017-155]armour.

[017-156]When Jove, lord of the storm-cloud, saw Hector standing aloof and

[017-157]arming himself in the armour of the son of Peleus, he wagged his

[017-158]head and muttered to himself saying, “A! poor wretch, you arm in

[017-159]the armour of a hero, before whom many another trembles, and you

[017-160]reck nothing of the [[fate|doom]] that is already close upon you. You have

[017-161]killed his comrade so brave and strong, but it was not well that

[017-162]you should strip the armour from his head and shoulders. I do

[017-163]indeed endow you with great might now, but as against this you

[017-164]shall not return from battle to lay the armour of the son of

[017-165]Peleus before Andromache.”

[017-166]The son of Saturn bowed his portentous brows, and Hector fitted

[017-167]the armour to his body, while terrible Mars entered into him, and

[017-168]filled his whole body with might and valour. With a shout he

[017-169]strode in among the allies, and his armour flashed about him so

[017-170]that he seemed to all of them like the great son of Peleus

[017-171]himself. He went about among them and cheered them on—Mesthles,

[017-172]Glaucus, Medon, Thersilochus, Asteropaeus, Deisenor and

[017-173]Hippothous, Phorcys, Chromius and Ennomus the augur. All these

[017-174]did he exhort saying, “Hear me, allies from other cities who are

[017-175]here in your thousands, it was not in order to have a crowd about

[017-176]me that I called you hither each from his several city, but that

[017-177]with heart and soul you might defend the wives and little ones of

[017-178]the Trojans from the fierce Achaeans. For this do I oppress my

[017-179]people with your food and the presents that make you rich.

[017-180]Therefore turn, and charge at the foe, to stand or fall as is the

[017-181]game of war; whoever shall bring Patroclus, dead though he be,

[017-182]into the hands of the Trojans, and shall make Ajax give way

[017-183]before him, I will give him one half of the spoils while I keep

[017-184]the other. He will thus share like honour with myself.”

[017-185]When he had thus spoken they charged full weight upon the Danaans

[017-186]with their spears held out before them, and the hopes of each ran

[017-187]high that he should force Ajax son of Telamon to yield up the

[017-188]body—fools that they were, for he was about to take the lives of

[017-189]many. Then Ajax said to Menelaus, “My good friend Menelaus, you

[017-190]and I shall hardly come out of this fight alive. I am less

[017-191]concerned for the body of Patroclus, who will shortly become meat

[017-192]for the dogs and vultures of Troy, than for the safety of my own

[017-193]head and yours. Hector has wrapped us round in a storm of battle

[017-194]from every quarter, and our destruction seems now certain. Call

[017-195]then upon the princes of the Danaans if there is any who can hear

[017-196]us.”

[017-197]Menelaus did as he said, and shouted to the Danaans for help at

[017-198]the top of his voice. “My friends,” he cried, “princes and

[017-199]counsellors of the Argives, all you who with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and

[017-200]Menelaus drink at the public cost, and give orders each to his

[017-201]own people as Jove vouchsafes him power and glory, the fight is

[017-202]so thick about me that I cannot distinguish you severally; come

[017-203]on, therefore, every man unbidden, and think it shame that

[017-204]Patroclus should become meat and morsel for Trojan hounds.”

[017-205]Fleet Ajax son of Oileus heard him and was first to force his way

[017-206]through the fight and run to help him. Next came Idomeneus and

[017-207]Meriones his esquire, peer of murderous Mars. As for the others

[017-208]that came into the fight after these, who of his own self could

[017-209]name them?

[017-210]The Trojans with Hector at their head charged in a body. As a

[017-211]great wave that comes thundering in at the mouth of some

[017-212]heaven-born river, and the rocks that jut into the sea ring with

[017-213]the roar of the breakers that beat and buffet them—even with such

[017-214]a roar did the Trojans come on; but the Achaeans in singleness of

[017-215]heart stood firm about the son of Menoetius, and fenced him with

[017-216]their bronze shields. Jove, moreover, hid the brightness of their

[017-217]helmets in a thick cloud, for he had borne no grudge against the

[017-218]son of Menoetius while he was still alive and squire to the

[017-219]descendant of Aeacus; therefore he was loth to let him fall a

[017-220]prey to the dogs of his foes the Trojans, and urged his comrades

[017-221]on to defend him.

[017-222]At first the Trojans drove the Achaeans back, and they withdrew

[017-223]from the dead man daunted. The Trojans did not succeed in killing

[017-224]any one, nevertheless they drew the body away. But the Achaeans

[017-225]did not lose it long, for Ajax, foremost of all the Danaans after

[017-226]the son of Peleus alike in stature and [[aristeia|prowess]], quickly rallied

[017-227]them and made towards the front like a wild boar upon the

[017-228]mountains when he stands at bay in the forest glades and routs

[017-229]the hounds and lusty youths that have attacked him—even so did

[017-230]Ajax son of Telamon passing easily in among the phalanxes of the

[017-231]Trojans, disperse those who had bestridden Patroclus and were

[017-232]most bent on winning glory by dragging him off to their city. At

[017-233]this moment Hippothous brave son of the Pelasgian Lethus, in his

[017-234]zeal for Hector and the Trojans, was dragging the body off by the

[017-235]foot through the press of the fight, having bound a strap round

[017-236]the sinews near the ancle; but a mischief soon befell him from

[017-237]which none of those could save him who would have gladly done so,

[017-238]for the son of Telamon sprang forward and smote him on his

[017-239]bronze-cheeked helmet. The plumed headpiece broke about the point

[017-240]of the weapon, struck at once by the spear and by the strong hand

[017-241]of Ajax, so that the bloody brain came oozing out through the

[017-242]crest-socket. His strength then failed him and he let Patroclus’

[017-243]foot drop from his hand, as he fell full length dead upon the

[017-244]body; thus he died far from the fertile land of Larissa, and

[017-245]never repaid his parents the cost of bringing him up, for his

[017-246]life was cut short early by the spear of mighty Ajax. Hector then

[017-247]took aim at Ajax with a spear, but he saw it coming and just

[017-248]managed to avoid it; the spear passed on and struck Schedius son

[017-249]of noble Iphitus, captain of the Phoceans, who dwelt in famed

[017-250]Panopeus and reigned over much people; it struck him under the

[017-251]middle of the collar-bone the bronze point went right through

[017-252]him, coming out at the bottom of his shoulder-blade, and his

[017-253]armour rang rattling round him as he fell heavily to the ground.

[017-254]Ajax in his turn struck noble Phorcys son of Phaenops in the

[017-255]middle of the belly as he was bestriding Hippothous, and broke

[017-256]the plate of his cuirass; whereon the spear tore out his entrails

[017-257]and he clutched the ground in his palm as he fell to earth.

[017-258]Hector and those who were in the front rank then gave ground,

[017-259]while the Argives raised a loud cry of triumph, and drew off the

[017-260]bodies of Phorcys and Hippothous which they stripped presently of

[017-261]their armour.

[017-262]The Trojans would now have been worsted by the brave Achaeans and

[017-263]driven back to Ilius through their own cowardice, while the

[017-264]Argives, so great was their courage and endurance, would have

[017-265]achieved a triumph even against the will of Jove, if Apollo had

[017-266]not roused Aeneas, in the likeness of Periphas son of Epytus, an

[017-267]attendant who had grown old in the service of Aeneas’ aged

[017-268]father, and was at all times devoted to him. In his likeness,

[017-269]then, Apollo said, “Aeneas, can you not manage, even though

[017-270]heaven be against us, to save high Ilius? I have known men, whose

[017-271]numbers, courage, and self-reliance have saved their people in

[017-272]spite of Jove, whereas in this case he would much rather give

[017-273]victory to us than to the Danaans, if you would only fight

[017-274]instead of being so terribly afraid.”

[017-275]Aeneas knew Apollo when he looked straight at him, and shouted to

[017-276]Hector saying, “Hector and all other Trojans and allies, shame on

[017-277]us if we are beaten by the Achaeans and driven back to Ilius

[017-278]through our own cowardice. A god has just come up to me and told

[017-279]me that Jove the supreme disposer will be with us. Therefore let

[017-280]us make for the Danaans, that it may go hard with them ere they

[017-281]bear away dead Patroclus to the ships.”

[017-282]As he spoke he sprang out far in front of the others, who then

[017-283]rallied and again faced the Achaeans. Aeneas speared Leiocritus

[017-284]son of Arisbas, a valiant follower of Lycomedes, and Lycomedes

[017-285]was moved with pity as he saw him fall; he therefore went close

[017-286]up, and speared Apisaon son of Hippasus shepherd of his people in

[017-287]the liver under the midriff, so that he died; he had come from

[017-288]fertile Paeonia and was the best man of them all after

[017-289]Asteropaeus. Asteropaeus flew forward to avenge him and attack

[017-290]the Danaans, but this might no longer be, inasmuch as those about

[017-291]Patroclus were well covered by their shields, and held their

[017-292]spears in front of them, for Ajax had given them strict orders

[017-293]that no man was either to give ground, or to stand out before the

[017-294]others, but all were to hold well together about the body and

[017-295]fight hand to hand. Thus did huge Ajax bid them, and the earth

[017-296]ran red with blood as the corpses fell thick on one another alike

[017-297]on the side of the Trojans and allies, and on that of the

[017-298]Danaans; for these last, too, fought no bloodless fight though

[017-299]many fewer of them perished, through the care they took to defend

[017-300]and stand by one another.

[017-301]Thus did they fight as it were a flaming fire; it seemed as

[017-302]though it had gone hard even with the sun and moon, for they were

[017-303]hidden over all that part where the bravest heroes were fighting

[017-304]about the dead son of Menoetius, whereas the other Danaans and

[017-305]Achaeans fought at their ease in full daylight with brilliant

[017-306]sunshine all round them, and there was not a cloud to be seen

[017-307]neither on plain nor mountain. These last moreover would rest for

[017-308]a while and leave off fighting, for they were some distance apart

[017-309]and beyond the range of one another’s weapons, whereas those who

[017-310]were in the thick of the fray suffered both from battle and

[017-311]darkness. All the best of them were being worn out by the great

[017-312]weight of their armour, but the two valiant heroes, Thrasymedes

[017-313]and Antilochus, had not yet heard of the death of Patroclus, and

[017-314]believed him to be still alive and leading the van against the

[017-315]Trojans; they were keeping themselves in reserve against the

[017-316]death or rout of their own comrades, for so [[nestor|Nestor]] had ordered

[017-317]when he sent them from the ships into battle.

[017-318]Thus through the livelong day did they wage fierce war, and the

[017-319]sweat of their toil rained ever on their legs under them, and on

[017-320]their hands and eyes, as they fought over the squire of the fleet

[017-321]son of Peleus. It was as when a man gives a great ox-hide all

[017-322]drenched in fat to his men, and bids them stretch it; whereon

[017-323]they stand round it in a ring and tug till the moisture leaves

[017-324]it, and the fat soaks in for the many that pull at it, and it is

[017-325]well stretched—even so did the two sides tug the dead body hither

[017-326]and thither within the compass of but a little space—the Trojans

[017-327]steadfastly set on dragging it into Ilius, while the Achaeans

[017-328]were no less so on taking it to their ships; and fierce was the

[017-329]fight between them. Not Mars himself the lord of hosts, nor yet

[017-330]Minerva, even in their fullest fury could make light of such a

[017-331]battle.

## Section 51

[017-332]Such fearful turmoil of men and horses did Jove on that day

[017-333]ordain round the body of Patroclus. Meanwhile Achilles did not

[017-334]know that he had fallen, for the fight was under the wall of Troy

[017-335]a long way off the ships. He had no idea, therefore, that

[017-336]Patroclus was dead, and deemed that he would return alive as soon

[017-337]as he had gone close up to the gates. He knew that he was not to

[017-338]sack the city neither with nor without himself, for his mother

[017-339]had often told him this when he had sat alone with her, and she

[017-340]had informed him of the counsels of great Jove. Now, however, she

[017-341]had not told him how great a disaster had befallen him in the

[017-342]death of the one who was far dearest to him of all his comrades.

[017-343]The others still kept on charging one another round the body with

[017-344]their pointed spears and killing each other. Then would one say,

[017-345]“My friends, we can never again show our faces at the

[017-346]ships—better, and greatly better, that earth should open and

[017-347]swallow us here in this place, than that we should let the

[017-348]Trojans have the triumph of bearing off Patroclus to their city.”

[017-349]The Trojans also on their part spoke to one another saying,

[017-350]“Friends, though we fall to a man beside this body, let none

[017-351]shrink from fighting.” With such words did they exhort each

[017-352]other. They fought and fought, and an iron clank rose through the

[017-353]void air to the brazen vault of heaven. The horses of the

[017-354]descendant of Aeacus stood out of the fight and wept when they

[017-355]heard that their driver had been laid low by the hand of

[017-356]murderous Hector. Automedon, valiant son of Diores, lashed them

[017-357]again and again; many a time did he speak kindly to them, and

[017-358]many a time did he upbraid them, but they would neither go back

[017-359]to the ships by the waters of the broad Hellespont, nor yet into
