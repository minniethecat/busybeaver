# Thracians

The Thracians (Θρῇκες) are a Trojan-allied contingent from Thrace, a region to the north of the Hellespont. They are led by Peirous, son of Imbrasus, and later by Acamas, and are described as "both brave and of great stature" (006-011). The Thracians feature prominently in the Trojan Catalogue and in the night raid of Book 10, where their king Rhesus and twelve of his men are slain by Diomedes and Odysseus.

## Identity

The Thracians are introduced in the Trojan Catalogue: "Acamas and the warrior Peirous commanded the Thracians" (002-689). Their homeland of Thrace, across the Hellespont from Troy, positioned them as the nearest and most accessible ally of the Trojans among the northern contingents. The poet emphasizes their physical prowess, describing them as "both brave and of great stature" (006-011), and later in Book 10, the Thracians are described as arriving "newly come" as reinforcements, led by their king Rhesus, "the handsomest man I have ever set eyes upon" (010-004).

## Leadership

The Thracian leadership structure is complex. In the Catalogue, two commanders are named: Acamas (described as "fleet Acamas chief of the Thracians" at 005-391) and Peirous, son of Imbrasus. Peirous is killed in Book 4 by Diores: "it was Peirous, son of Imbrasus, captain of the Thracians, who struck him with a jagged stone" (004-419). Later, in Book 10, King Rhesus arrives with a fresh Thracian contingent as a late reinforcement, and Hector chastises the Thracians for not fighting as they should (010-004). Rhesus and twelve of his men are killed in Odysseus and Diomedes's night raid (010-404).

## Role in the War

- **Book 2**: Listed in the Trojan Catalogue under Acamas and Peirous (002-689).
- **Book 4**: Peirous kills Diores, an Achaean captain, and is himself killed — "captain of the Thracians and the other of the Epeans; and many Trojans and Achaeans fell" (004-419).
- **Book 5**: Acamas, "fleet Acamas chief of the Thracians," rouses the Trojans to battle (005-391).
- **Book 6**: The Thracians are noted as "both brave and of great stature" among the Trojan allies fighting on the plain (006-011).
- **Book 10**: The night raid — Rhesus, king of the Thracians, arrives with horses "whiter than snow and fleet as the winds" (010-004), and his camp is attacked by Odysseus and Diomedes. Diomedes "set upon the Thracian soldiers till he had killed twelve" (010-404), including Rhesus. This episode is one of the most self-contained narrative arcs in the *Iliad*.
- **Book 13**: Helenus strikes Deipyrus "with a great Thracian sword" (013-510), suggesting Thracian weapons were distinguished by their size and quality.

## Warfare Style

The Thracians are primarily characterized as horsemen and close-combat infantry. The mention of "Thracian horsemen" (014-195) and the capture of Rhesus's magnificent horses indicate their association with cavalry excellence. The recurring epithet "great" applied to Thracian weapons — "a great Thracian sword" — suggests their armament was distinctive. Their tall stature is noted twice in the text, a Homeric marker of martial prowess.

## The Rhesus Episode

The most extensive Thracian narrative occurs in Book 10 (the *Doloneia*), where Rhesus, the newly arrived Thracian king, camps apart from the main Trojan force. After capturing and executing the Trojan spy Dolon, Odysseus and Diomedes infiltrate the Thracian camp, kill Rhesus and twelve of his soldiers, and steal his magnificent horses — an act of stealth warfare that deprives the Trojans of a potentially decisive reinforcement.

## Notable Members

- Peirous — son of Imbrasus, captain of the Thracians, killed in Book 4
- Acamas — "fleet Acamas chief of the Thracians"
- Rhesus — king of the Thracians, killed in the night raid of Book 10
