# Book X

[010-001]BOOK X.

[010-002][[odysseus|Ulysses]] and Diomed go out as spies, and meet Dolon, who gives

[010-003]them information: they then kill him, and profiting by what he

[010-004]had told them, kill Rhesus king of the Thracians and take his

[010-005]horses.

[010-006]Now the other princes of the Achaeans slept soundly the whole

[010-007]night through, but [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] son of Atreus was troubled, so that

[010-008]he could get no rest. As when fair Juno’s lord flashes his

[010-009]lightning in token of great rain or hail or snow when the

[010-010]snow-flakes whiten the ground, or again as a [[prophecy|sign]] that he will

[010-011]open the wide jaws of hungry war, even so did Agamemnon heave

[010-012]many a heavy sigh, for his soul trembled within him. When he

[010-013]looked upon the plain of Troy he marvelled at the many watchfires

[010-014]burning in front of Ilius, and at the sound of pipes and flutes

[010-015]and of the hum of men, but when presently he turned towards the

[010-016][[achaean-camp|ships]] and hosts of the Achaeans, he tore his hair by handfuls

[010-017]before Jove on high, and groaned aloud for the very disquietness

[010-018]of his soul. In the end he deemed it best to go at once to [[nestor|Nestor]]

[010-019]son of Neleus, and see if between them they could find any way of

[010-020]the Achaeans from destruction. He therefore rose, put on his

[010-021]shirt, bound his sandals about his comely feet, flung the skin of

[010-022]a huge tawny lion over his shoulders—a skin that reached his

[010-023]feet—and took his spear in his hand.

[010-024]Neither could Menelaus sleep, for he, too, boded ill for the

[010-025]Argives who for his sake had sailed from far over the seas to

[010-026]fight the Trojans. He covered his broad back with the skin of a

[010-027]spotted panther, put a casque of bronze upon his head, and took

[010-028]his spear in his brawny hand. Then he went to rouse his brother,

[010-029]who was by far the most powerful of the Achaeans, and was

[010-030]honoured by the people as though he were a god. He found him by

[010-031]the stern of his ship already putting his goodly array about his

[010-032]shoulders, and right glad was he that his brother had come.

[010-033]Menelaus spoke first. “Why,” said he, “my dear brother, are you

[010-034]thus arming? Are you going to send any of our comrades to exploit

[010-035]the Trojans? I greatly fear that no one will do you this service,

[010-036]and spy upon the enemy alone in the dead of night. It will be a

[010-037]deed of great daring.”

[010-038]And King Agamemnon answered, “Menelaus, we both of us need shrewd

[010-039]counsel to save the Argives and our ships, for Jove has changed

[010-040]his mind, and inclines towards [[hector|Hector]]’s sacrifices rather than

[010-041]ours. I never saw nor heard tell of any man as having wrought

[010-042]such ruin in one day as Hector has now wrought against the sons

[010-043]of the Achaeans—and that too of his own unaided self, for he is

[010-044]son neither to god nor goddess. The Argives will rue it long and

[010-045]deeply. Run, therefore, with all speed by the line of the ships,

[010-046]and call Ajax and Idomeneus. Meanwhile I will go to Nestor, and

[010-047]bid him rise and go about among the companies of our sentinels to

[010-048]give them their instructions; they will listen to him sooner than

[010-049]to any man, for his own son, and Meriones brother in arms to

[010-050]Idomeneus, are captains over them. It was to them more

[010-051]particularly that we gave this charge.”

[010-052]Menelaus replied, “How do I take your meaning? Am I to stay with

[010-053]them and wait your coming, or shall I return here as soon as I

[010-054]have given your orders?” “Wait,” answered King Agamemnon, “for

[010-055]there are so many paths about the camp that we might miss one

[010-056]another. Call every man on your way, and bid him be stirring;

[010-057]name him by his lineage and by his father’s name, give each all

[010-058]titular observance, and stand not too much upon your own dignity;

[010-059]we must take our full share of toil, for at our birth Jove laid

[010-060]this heavy burden upon us.”

[010-061]With these instructions he sent his brother on his way, and went

[010-062]on to Nestor shepherd of his people. He found him sleeping in his

[010-063]tent hard by his own ship; his goodly armour lay beside him—his

[010-064]shield, his two spears and his helmet; beside him also lay the

[010-065]gleaming girdle with which the old man girded himself when he

[010-066]armed to lead his people into battle—for his age stayed him not.

[010-067]He raised himself on his elbow and looked up at Agamemnon. “Who

[010-068]is it,” said he, “that goes thus about the [[guest-friendship|host]] and the ships

[010-069]alone and in the dead of night, when men are sleeping? Are you

[010-070]looking for one of your mules or for some comrade? Do not stand

[010-071]there and say nothing, but speak. What is your business?”

[010-072]And Agamemnon answered, “Nestor, son of Neleus, [[honor|honour]] to the

[010-073]Achaean name, it is I, Agamemnon son of Atreus, on whom Jove has

[010-074]laid labour and [[grief|sorrow]] so long as there is breath in my body and

[010-075]my limbs carry me. I am thus abroad because sleep sits not upon

[010-076]my eyelids, but my heart is big with war and with the jeopardy of

[010-077]the Achaeans. I am in great fear for the Danaans. I am at sea,

[010-078]and without sure counsel; my heart beats as though it would leap

[010-079]out of my body, and my limbs fail me. If then you can do

[010-080]anything—for you too cannot sleep—let us go the round of the

[010-081]watch, and see whether they are drowsy with toil and sleeping to

[010-082]the neglect of their duty. The enemy is encamped hard and we know

[010-083]not but he may attack us by night.”

[010-084]Nestor replied, “Most noble son of Atreus, king of men,

[010-085]Agamemnon, Jove will not do all for Hector that Hector thinks he

[010-086]will; he will have troubles yet in plenty if [[achilles|Achilles]] will lay

[010-087]aside his [[wrath|anger]]. I will go with you, and we will rouse others,

[010-088]either the son of Tydeus, or Ulysses, or fleet Ajax and the

[010-089]valiant son of Phyleus. Some one had also better go and call Ajax

[010-090]and King Idomeneus, for their ships are not near at hand but the

[010-091]farthest of all. I cannot however refrain from blaming Menelaus,

[010-092]much as I love him and [[shame|respect]] him—and I will say so plainly,

[010-093]even at the risk of offending you—for sleeping and leaving all

[010-094]this trouble to yourself. He ought to be going about imploring

[010-095]aid from all the princes of the Achaeans, for we are in extreme

[010-096]danger.”

[010-097]And Agamemnon answered, “Sir, you may sometimes blame him justly,

[010-098]for he is often remiss and unwilling to exert himself—not indeed

[010-099]from sloth, nor yet heedlessness, but because he looks to me and

[010-100]expects me to take the lead. On this occasion, however, he was

[010-101]awake before I was, and came to me of his own accord. I have

[010-102]already sent him to call the very men whom you have named. And

[010-103]now let us be going. We shall find them with the watch outside

[010-104]the gates, for it was there I said that we would meet them.”

[010-105]“In that case,” answered Nestor, “the Argives will not blame him

[010-106]nor disobey his orders when he urges them to fight or gives them

[010-107]instructions.”

[010-108]With this he put on his shirt, and bound his sandals about his

[010-109]comely feet. He buckled on his purple coat, of two thicknesses,

[010-110]large, and of a rough shaggy texture, grasped his redoubtable

[010-111]bronze-shod spear, and wended his way along the line of the

[010-112]Achaean ships. First he called loudly to Ulysses peer of gods in

[010-113]counsel and woke him, for he was soon roused by the sound of the

[010-114]battle-cry. He came outside his tent and said, “Why do you go

[010-115]thus alone about the host, and along the line of the ships in the

[010-116]stillness of the night? What is it that you find so urgent?” And

[010-117]Nestor knight of Gerene answered, “Ulysses, noble son of Laertes,

[010-118]take it not amiss, for the Achaeans are in great straits. Come

[010-119]with me and let us wake some other, who may advise well with us

[010-120]whether we shall fight or fly.”

[010-121]On this Ulysses went at once into his tent, put his shield about

[010-122]his shoulders and came out with them. First they went to Diomed

[010-123]son of Tydeus, and found him outside his tent clad in his armour

[010-124]with his comrades sleeping round him and using their shields as

[010-125]pillows; as for their spears, they stood upright on the spikes of

[010-126]their butts that were driven into the ground, and the burnished

[010-127]bronze flashed afar like the lightning of father Jove. The hero

[010-128]was sleeping upon the skin of an ox, with a piece of fine carpet

[010-129]under his head; Nestor went up to him and stirred him with his

[010-130]heel to rouse him, upbraiding him and urging him to bestir

[010-131]himself. “Wake up,” he exclaimed, “son of Tydeus. How can you

[010-132]sleep on in this way? Can you not see that the Trojans are

[010-133]encamped on the brow of the plain hard by our ships, with but a

[010-134]little space between us and them?”

[010-135]On these words Diomed leaped up instantly and said, “Old man,

[010-136]your heart is of iron; you rest not one moment from your labours.

[010-137]Are there no younger men among the Achaeans who could go about to

[010-138]rouse the princes? There is no tiring you.”

[010-139]And Nestor knight of Gerene made answer, “My son, all that you

[010-140]have said is true. I have good sons, and also much people who

[010-141]might call the chieftains, but the Achaeans are in the gravest

[010-142]danger; life and death are balanced as it were on the edge of a

[010-143]razor. Go then, for you are younger than I, and of your courtesy

[010-144]rouse Ajax and the fleet son of Phyleus.”

[010-145]Diomed threw the skin of a great tawny lion about his shoulders—a

[010-146]skin that reached his feet—and grasped his spear. When he had

[010-147]roused the heroes, he brought them back with him; they then went

[010-148]the round of those who were on guard, and found the captains not

[010-149]sleeping at their posts but wakeful and sitting with their arms

[010-150]about them. As sheep dogs that watch their flocks when they are

[010-151]yarded, and hear a wild beast coming through the mountain forest

[010-152]towards them—forthwith there is a hue and cry of dogs and men,

[010-153]and slumber is broken—even so was sleep chased from the eyes of

[010-154]the Achaeans as they kept the watches of the wicked night, for

[010-155]they turned constantly towards the plain whenever they heard any

[010-156]stir among the Trojans. The old man was glad and bade them be of

[010-157]good cheer. “Watch on, my children,” said he, “and let not sleep

[010-158]get hold upon you, lest our enemies triumph over us.”

[010-159]With this he passed the trench, and with him the other chiefs of

[010-160]the Achaeans who had been called to the council. Meriones and the

[010-161]brave son of Nestor went also, for the princes bade them. When

[010-162]they were beyond the trench that was dug round the wall they held

[010-163]their meeting on the open ground where there was a space clear of

[010-164]corpses, for it was here that when night fell Hector had turned

[010-165]back from his onslaught on the Argives. They sat down, therefore,

[010-166]and held [[counsel|debate]] with one another.

[010-167]Nestor spoke first. “My friends,” said he, “is there any man bold

[010-168]enough to venture among the Trojans, and cut off some straggler,

[010-169]or bring us news of what the enemy mean to do whether they will

[010-170]stay here by the ships away from the city, or whether, now that

[010-171]they have worsted the Achaeans, they will retire within their

[010-172]walls. If he could learn all this and come back safely here, his

[010-173][[glory|fame]] would be high as heaven in the mouths of all men, and he

[010-174]would be rewarded richly; for the chiefs from all our ships would

[010-175]each of them give him a black ewe with her lamb—which is a

[010-176]present of surpassing value—and he would be asked as a guest to

[010-177]all feasts and clan-gatherings.”

[010-178]They all held their peace, but Diomed of the loud war-cry spoke

[010-179]saying, “Nestor, gladly will I visit the host of the Trojans over

[010-180]against us, but if another will go with me I shall do so in

[010-181]greater confidence and comfort. When two men are together, one of

[010-182]them may see some opportunity which the other has not caught

[010-183]sight of; if a man is alone he is less full of resource, and his

[010-184]wit is weaker.”

[010-185]On this several offered to go with Diomed. The two Ajaxes,

[010-186]servants of Mars, Meriones, and the son of Nestor all wanted to

[010-187]go, so did Menelaus son of Atreus; Ulysses also wished to go

[010-188]among the host of the Trojans, for he was ever full of daring,

[010-189]and thereon Agamemnon king of men spoke thus: “Diomed,” said he,

[010-190]“son of Tydeus, man after my own heart, choose your comrade for

[010-191]yourself—take the best man of those that have offered, for many

[010-192]would now go with you. Do not through delicacy reject the better

[010-193]man, and take the worst out of respect for his lineage, because

[010-194]he is of more royal blood.”

[010-195]He said this because he feared for Menelaus. Diomed answered, “If

[010-196]you bid me take the man of my own choice, how in that case can I

[010-197]fail to think of Ulysses, than whom there is no man more eager to

[010-198]face all kinds of danger—and Pallas Minerva loves him well? If he

[010-199]were to go with me we should pass safely through fire itself, for

[010-200]he is quick to see and understand.”

[010-201]“Son of Tydeus,” replied Ulysses, “say neither good nor ill about

[010-202]me, for you are among Argives who know me well. Let us be going,

[010-203]for the night wanes and dawn is at hand. The stars have gone

[010-204]forward, two-thirds of the night are already spent, and the third

[010-205]is alone left us.”

[010-206]They then put on their armour. Brave Thrasymedes provided the son

[010-207]of Tydeus with a sword and a shield (for he had left his own at

[010-208]his ship) and on his head he set a helmet of bull’s hide without

[010-209]either peak or crest; it is called a skull-cap and is a common

[010-210]headgear. Meriones found a bow and quiver for Ulysses, and on his

[010-211]head he set a leathern helmet that was lined with a strong

[010-212]plaiting of leathern thongs, while on the outside it was thickly

[010-213]studded with boar’s teeth, well and skilfully set into it; next

[010-214]the head there was an inner lining of felt. This helmet had been

[010-215]stolen by Autolycus out of Eleon when he broke into the house of

[010-216]Amyntor son of Ormenus. He gave it to Amphidamas of Cythera to

[010-217]take to Scandea, and Amphidamas gave it as a guest-gift to Molus,

[010-218]who gave it to his son Meriones; and now it was set upon the head

[010-219]of Ulysses.

[010-220]When the pair had armed, they set out, and left the other

[010-221]chieftains behind them. Pallas Minerva sent them a heron by the

[010-222]wayside upon their right hands; they could not see it for the

[010-223]darkness, but they heard its cry. Ulysses was glad when he heard

[010-224]it and prayed to Minerva: “Hear me,” he cried, “daughter of

[010-225]aegis-bearing Jove, you who spy out all my ways and who are with

[010-226]me in all my hardships; befriend me in this mine hour, and grant

[010-227]that we may return to the ships covered with glory after having

[010-228]achieved some mighty exploit that shall bring sorrow to the

[010-229]Trojans.”

[010-230]Then Diomed of the loud war-cry also prayed: “Hear me too,” said

[010-231]he, “daughter of Jove, unweariable; be with me even as you were

[010-232]with my noble father Tydeus when he went to Thebes as envoy sent

[010-233]by the Achaeans. He left the Achaeans by the banks of the river

[010-234]Aesopus, and went to the city bearing a message of peace to the

[010-235]Cadmeians; on his return thence, with your help, goddess, he did

[010-236]great deeds of daring, for you were his ready helper. Even so

[010-237]guide me and guard me now, and in return I will offer you in

[010-238]sacrifice a broad-browed heifer of a year old, unbroken, and

[010-239]never yet brought by man under the yoke. I will gild her horns

[010-240]and will offer her up to you in sacrifice.”

[010-241]Thus they prayed, and Pallas Minerva heard their prayer. When

[010-242]they had done praying to the daughter of great Jove, they went

[010-243]their way like two lions prowling by night amid the armour and

[010-244]blood-stained bodies of them that had fallen.

[010-245]Neither again did Hector let the Trojans sleep; for he too called

[010-246]the princes and councillors of the Trojans that he might set his

[010-247]counsel before them. “Is there one,” said he, “who for a great

[010-248][[prize|reward]] will do me the service of which I will tell you? He shall

[010-249]be well paid if he will. I will give him a chariot and a couple

[010-250]of horses, the fleetest that can be found at the ships of the

[010-251]Achaeans, if he will dare this thing; and he will win infinite

[010-252]honour to boot; he must go to the ships and find out whether they

[010-253]are still guarded as heretofore, or whether now that we have

[010-254]beaten them the Achaeans design to fly, and through sheer

[010-255]exhaustion are neglecting to keep their watches.”

[010-256]They all held their peace; but there was among the Trojans a

[010-257]certain man named Dolon, son of Eumedes, the famous herald—a man

[010-258]rich in gold and bronze. He was ill-favoured, but a good runner,

[010-259]and was an only son among five sisters. He it was that now

[010-260]addressed the Trojans. “I, Hector,” said he, “Will to the ships

[010-261]and will exploit them. But first hold up your sceptre and [[oath|swear]]

[010-262]that you will give me the chariot, bedight with bronze, and the

[010-263]horses that now carry the noble son of Peleus. I will make you a

[010-264]good scout, and will not fail you. I will go through the host

[010-265]from one end to the other till I come to the ship of Agamemnon,

[010-266]where I take it the princes of the Achaeans are now consulting

[010-267]whether they shall fight or fly.”

[010-268]When he had done speaking Hector held up his sceptre, and swore

[010-269]him his oath saying, “May Jove the thundering husband of Juno

[010-270]bear witness that no other Trojan but yourself shall mount those

[010-271]steeds, and that you shall have your will with them for ever.”

[010-272]The oath he swore was bootless, but it made Dolon more keen on

[010-273]going. He hung his bow over his shoulder, and as an overall he

[010-274]wore the skin of a grey wolf, while on his head he set a cap of

[010-275]ferret skin. Then he took a pointed javelin, and left the camp

[010-276]for the ships, but he was not to return with any news for Hector.

[010-277]When he had left the horses and the troops behind him, he made

[010-278]all speed on his way, but Ulysses perceived his coming and said

[010-279]to Diomed, “Diomed, here is some one from the camp; I am not sure

[010-280]whether he is a spy, or whether it is some thief who would

[010-281]plunder the bodies of the dead; let him get a little past us, we

[010-282]can then spring upon him and take him. If, however, he is too

[010-283]quick for us, go after him with your spear and hem him in towards

[010-284]the ships away from the Trojan camp, to prevent his getting back

[010-285]to the town.”

[010-286]With this they turned out of their way and lay down among the

[010-287]corpses. Dolon suspected nothing and soon passed them, but when

[010-288]he had got about as far as the distance by which a mule-plowed

[010-289]furrow exceeds one that has been ploughed by oxen (for mules can

[010-290]plow fallow land quicker than oxen) they ran after him, and when

[010-291]he heard their footsteps he stood still, for he made sure they

[010-292]were friends from the Trojan camp come by Hector’s orders to bid

[010-293]him return; when, however, they were only a spear’s cast, or

[010-294]less, away from him, he saw that they were enemies and ran as

[010-295]fast as his legs could take him. The others gave chase at once,

[010-296]and as a couple of well-trained hounds press forward after a doe

[010-297]or hare that runs screaming in front of them, even so did the son

[010-298]of Tydeus and Ulysses pursue Dolon and cut him off from his own

[010-299]people. But when he had fled so far towards the ships that he

[010-300]would soon have fallen in with the outposts, Minerva infused

[010-301]fresh strength into the son of Tydeus for fear some other of the

[010-302]Achaeans might have the glory of being first to hit him, and he

[010-303]might himself be only second; he therefore sprang forward with

[010-304]his spear and said, “Stand, or I shall throw my spear, and in

[010-305]that case I shall soon make an end of you.”

[010-306]He threw as he spoke, but missed his aim on purpose. The dart

[010-307]flew over the man’s right shoulder, and then stuck in the ground.

[010-308]He stood stock still, trembling and in great fear; his teeth

[010-309]chattered, and he turned pale with fear. The two came breathless

[010-310]up to him and seized his hands, whereon he began to weep and

[010-311]said, “Take me alive; I will ransom myself; we have great store

[010-312]of gold, bronze, and wrought iron, and from this my father will

[010-313]satisfy you with a very large ransom, should he hear of my being

[010-314]alive at the ships of the Achaeans.”

[010-315]“Fear not,” replied Ulysses, “let no thought of death be in your

[010-316]mind; but tell me, and tell me true, why are you thus going about

[010-317]alone in the dead of night away from your camp and towards the

[010-318]ships, while other men are sleeping? Is it to plunder the bodies

[010-319]of the slain, or did Hector send you to spy out what was going on

[010-321]Dolon answered, his limbs trembling beneath him: “Hector, with

[010-322]his vain flattering promises, lured me from my better judgement.

[010-323]He said he would give me the horses of the noble son of Peleus

[010-324]and his bronze-bedizened chariot; he bade me go through the

[010-325]darkness of the flying night, get close to the enemy, and find

[010-326]out whether the ships are still guarded as heretofore, or

[010-327]whether, now that we have beaten them, the Achaeans design to

[010-328]fly, and through sheer exhaustion are neglecting to keep their

[010-329]watches.”

[010-330]Ulysses smiled at him and answered, “You had indeed set your

[010-331]heart upon a great reward, but the horses of the descendant of

[010-332]Aeacus are hardly to be kept in hand or driven by any other

[010-343]which you ask me, there is no chosen watch to keep guard over the

[010-345]have them; they, therefore, are awake and keep each other to

[010-346]their duty as sentinels; but the allies who have come from other

[010-347]places are asleep and leave it to the Trojans to keep guard, for

[010-348]their wives and children are not here.”

[010-349]Ulysses then said, “Now tell me; are they sleeping among the

[010-350]Trojan troops, or do they lie apart? Explain this that I may

[010-351]understand it.”

[010-359]the camp; and they have Rhesus son of Eioneus for their king. His

[010-360]horses are the finest and strongest that I have ever seen, they
