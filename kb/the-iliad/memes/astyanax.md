## Identity

Astyanax is the infant son of Hector and Andromache, the heir to Troy's royal house and the epic's most powerful symbol of what war destroys beyond the battlefield. His father Hector named him Scamandrius, after the river Scamander that flows through the Trojan plain, but "the people called him Astyanax, for his father stood alone as chief guardian of Ilius" (006-333). The name Astyanax means "lord of the city" — an epithet that encodes both the hope invested in this child and the tragedy of his situation: the city whose lord he is named for will not survive to see him rule.

He is a "mere babe" (006-331), carried in his nurse's arms, too young to understand the war that surrounds him. His age makes him unique among the *Iliad*'s characters — he is the only person who appears in the epic not as an agent of action but purely as an object of love, hope, and grief.

## Role in the Epic

Astyanax appears in two of the *Iliad*'s most celebrated scenes: the farewell between Hector and Andromache in Book VI, and Andromache's lament for her husband and her son's future in Book XXII.

**The Farewell at the Scaean Gates (Book VI):** When Hector returns to Troy to arrange offerings to Athena, he seeks out Andromache. She "came to meet him with a nurse who carried his little child in her bosom" (006-330). What follows is one of the most intimate scenes in all of ancient literature. Andromache pleads with Hector to stay within the walls, invoking their son: "think on your infant son, and on my hapless self who ere long shall be your widow" (006-337). She begs: "make not your child fatherless, and your wife a widow" (006-355).

Hector reaches for his son, but "the boy cried and nestled in his nurse's bosom, scared at the sight of his father's armour, and at the horse-hair plume that nodded fiercely from his helmet" (006-382). The child does not recognise his father in battle gear — an image of startling psychological realism that transforms the epic's grandest hero into something frightening to his own son. "His father and mother laughed to see him" (006-385), and Hector removes his helmet, laying it "all gleaming upon the ground" (006-386). He takes his child, kisses him, and prays to Zeus:

> "Jove, grant that this my child may be even as myself, chief among the Trojans; let him be not less excellent in strength, and let him rule Ilius with his might. Then may one say of him as he comes from battle, 'The son is far better than the father.'" (006-389-393)

The prayer is one of the most painful ironies in the epic. Hector envisions a future for Astyanax — strength, rule, surpassing his father — that the audience knows will never come. The child whose name means "lord of the city" will never rule; the son who is to be "far better than the father" will be thrown from the walls of Troy while still a child.

Hector then lays the child "again in the arms of his wife, who took him to her own soft bosom, smiling through her tears" (006-395). The image of Andromache holding her son, smiling and weeping simultaneously, is one of the epic's definitive moments — the domestic world that war both protects and destroys.

**Andromache's Lament (Book XXII):** After Hector's death, Andromache speaks a lament that centres on Astyanax's devastated future. Her vision of what awaits the fatherless child is mercilessly detailed:

> "he will go crying back to his widowed mother — he, Astyanax, who erewhile would sit upon his father's knees, and have none but the daintiest and choicest morsels set before him" (022-446)

The contrast between Astyanax's past — privileged, loved, seated on his father's knees — and his future — hungry, humiliated, weeping — is the *Iliad*'s most concentrated expression of what war costs those who never chose to fight. Andromache continues: "whereas now that he has lost his father his lot will be full of hardship — he, whom the Trojans name Astyanax, because you, O Hector, were the only defence of their gates and battlements" (022-451). The name that was once a title of honour becomes an accusation: the city whose lord he was named for has fallen, and its only defender lies dead.

## Notable Speeches

Astyanax himself has no speech — he is an infant, and his voice in the epic is only a cry. But two of the *Iliad*'s most powerful speeches are spoken over him or about him:

> "Jove, grant that this my child may be even as myself, chief among the Trojans; let him be not less excellent in strength, and let him rule Ilius with his might. Then may one say of him as he comes from battle, 'The son is far better than the father.'" (006-389-393)

Hector's prayer over Astyanax is the epic's most heartbreaking statement of paternal hope — made all the more devastating by the audience's knowledge that none of it will come true.

> "he will go crying back to his widowed mother — he, Astyanax, who erewhile would sit upon his father's knees, and have none but the daintiest and choicest morsels set before him." (022-446)

Andromache's vision of Astyanax's future is the *Iliad*'s definitive image of the orphan's fate in a world without fathers.

## Relationships

**Hector:** Astyanax's relationship with his father is the emotional core of Book VI. Hector reaches for his son, removes his helmet when the child is frightened, kisses him, and prays for his future. The bond between them — the warrior-father and the infant-son — is the epic's fullest image of what heroism protects and what war destroys. Hector's death in Book XXII makes Astyanax an orphan, and the child's fate is left suspended in Andromache's lament.

**Andromache:** Astyanax is "carried in his nurse's arms" when Andromache meets Hector (006-330), and after Hector's prayer, Andromache receives him back to "her own soft bosom, smiling through her tears" (006-396). In Book XXII, her lament for Hector is inseparable from her lament for Astyanax's future — mother and son are bound in a shared fate of loss.

**Priam and Hecuba:** As the grandson of Priam and Hecuba, Astyanax represents the third generation of Troy's royal house. Priam's grief for Hector in Book XXIV is also, implicitly, grief for the line that Hector's death has severed — a line embodied in Astyanax.

**The Nurse:** Astyanax's nurse is an unnamed figure who carries him throughout Book VI. Her presence — the infant's dependence on her care — underscores Astyanax's extreme youth and vulnerability.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Astyanax's death, but his fate is vividly foreseen. Andromache's lament in Book XXII imagines a life of hardship and social humiliation (022-446-451). In post-Homeric tradition — the *Sack of Troy* and later Euripides' *Trojan Women* — Astyanax is thrown from the walls of Troy by the Greeks (sometimes by Odysseus, sometimes by Neoptolemus), who fear that Hector's son, if allowed to live, would grow to avenge his father and rebuild Troy. The death of Astyanax is one of the most notorious atrocities of the city's fall, and its brutality — the killing of a child to extinguish a line — makes explicit what the *Iliad* implies: that war does not distinguish between soldiers and children, and that the destruction of a city means the destruction of its future.

Within the *Iliad*, the final image of Astyanax is Andromache's vision of him weeping, hungry, and fatherless — a future that the poem's closing funeral rites cannot erase. The pyre of Hector burns, but Astyanax remains, the living consequence of everything the epic has described.

## Scholarly Notes

Astyanax is one of the *Iliad*'s smallest roles in terms of narrative space — he appears in perhaps forty lines — and one of its largest in terms of emotional and symbolic weight. His name, meaning "lord of the city," has been the subject of extensive scholarly analysis. The name is an ironic commentary on the epic's central theme: the city (astu) that Astyanax is named to rule will fall, and the father (anax) who alone defended it is dead. The double name — Scamandrius (given by Hector, connecting the child to the Trojan landscape) and Astyanax (given by the people, connecting him to the city's political existence) — reflects the dual identity of all Trojan figures: natural and civic, personal and public.

The helmet scene (006-382-386) is one of the most discussed passages in Homeric criticism. The child's fear of his father's armour, Hector's removal of the helmet, the parents' laughter — this sequence has been read as a miniature drama of the tension between the domestic and martial spheres that structures the entire epic. The helmet, the supreme symbol of the warrior, must be laid aside before the father can hold his son. The moment when Hector lays the gleaming helmet on the ground has been called one of the great gestures of renunciation in literature — a warrior laying down his identity to become, for a moment, simply a father.

The contrast between Hector's prayer (006-389-393) and Andromache's lament (022-446-452) is the structural climax of the Astyanax narrative. Hector prays for his son to surpass him; Andromache foresees him reduced to beggary. The two visions are incompatible, and the epic leaves unresolved which — if either — represents the child's actual destiny, though post-Homeric tradition decisively sided with Andromache's darker vision.
