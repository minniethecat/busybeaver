# Oath (Orkos)

Oath-taking is the closest thing the Homeric world has to law. In the absence of written codes or impartial judges, the sworn oath — witnessed by the gods and enforced by divine retribution — is the mechanism that binds warriors to their words. When oaths are honored, temporary order emerges from chaos; when they are broken, as when the [[trojans|Trojans]] violate the truce in Book IV, cosmic punishment follows.

## Definition

*Orkos* (ὅρκος) is both the oath itself and the divine power that enforces it. An oath in Homer involves: (1) a verbal decl[[curse|ara]]tion of the terms, (2) the calling of gods as witnesses (typically Zeus, Helios who sees all, the Rivers, and the Erinyes who punish perjurers), and (3) often a sacrificial [[sacrifice|ritual]] in which the slaughter of an animal symbolizes the fate awaiting the oath-breaker. The oath creates a binding obligation that even the gods must [[shame|respect]] — Zeus himself cannot casually disregard an oath sworn in his name.

## Manifestations in the Epic

The most consequential oath in the *Iliad* is the truce sworn before the duel between Paris and Menelaus in Book III. Both sides agree that the winner shall take [[helen|Helen]] and the war shall end. A sacrifice is performed, wine is poured, and the oath is solemnized with the words: "Whoever shall be first to break this oath, may their brains be poured out on the ground as this wine is poured." When Pandarus, urged by Athena, shoots an arrow at Menelaus during the truce, the violation triggers divine [[wrath|fury]] and escalates the war to new levels of savagery.

[[achilles|Achilles]]' oath in Book I — swearing by the scepter that he will not return to battle — is another pivotal oath. The scepter, a physical object invested with authority, serves as the visible token of the oath's binding power. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] had sworn by this same scepter when he took command of the expedition.

## Greek Terminology

**ὅρκος (orkos)** — the oath, both as verbal act and as the divine power that enforces it. **ὅρκια (horkia)** — the oath-sacrifice, the [[sacrifice|ritual]] that accompanies oath-taking. **ἐπιορκέω (epiorkeō)** — to swear falsely, to perjure oneself. The Erinyes (Furies) are the divine enforcers of oaths, pursuing perjurers even beyond death.

## Exemplary Passages

> Whoever shall be first to break this oath, may their brains be poured out on the ground as this wine is poured. (Book III)

## Contrasts and Tensions

Oaths in the *Iliad* exist in tension with the chaotic passions that drive the narrative. The truce-oath of Book III should have ended the war; instead, its violation deepens it. This pattern — oath made, oath broken, consequences amplified — suggests a pessimistic view of the oath's capacity to restrain violence. The gods themselves subvert oaths: Athena, at Zeus's direction, persuades Pandarus to break the truce, raising the question of whether mortals can be held accountable for divinely instigated perjury.

The binding power of oaths also creates tragic dilemmas. [[hector|Hector]] knows the [[trojans|Trojans]] are in the wrong after the truce violation, yet he cannot abandon his city. [[achilles|Achilles]]' oath to stay out of battle, sworn in the heat of justified [[wrath|anger]], later prevents him from responding to his comrades' desperate need.
