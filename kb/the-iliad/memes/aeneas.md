# Aeneas

Aeneas is the son of the goddess [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]] (Venus in Butler's translation) and the mortal Anchises, and the commander of the Dardanian contingent allied to Troy. He is the second greatest warrior on the Trojan side after [[hector|Hector]], and his destiny — to survive the fall of Troy and lead its remnant to a new homeland — extends far beyond the *Iliad* into Virgil's *Aeneid* and the foundation myth of Rome. Within the *Iliad*, however, Aeneas is portrayed as a brave but vulnerable hero, grievously wounded by [[diomedes|Diomedes]] in Book 5 and rescued from certain death by his divine mother and Apollo.

## Identity

Aeneas is the son of Anchises, a member of the junior branch of the Trojan royal house, and [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]], the goddess of love. His epithet "brave Aeneas" appears in the Catalogue of Ships, where he is named as the leader of the Dardanians alongside the sons of Antenor (002-666). His dual heritage — mortal royalty on his father's side, divine beauty on his mother's — places him in the highest echelon of the Trojan aristocracy, second only to Hector and the sons of Priam. He is intimately connected to Mount Ida, where Aphrodite bore him to Anchises, and where his own son Ascanius will later rule.

## Role in the Epic

Aeneas is a significant presence in the early and middle books of the *Iliad*, with his most dramatic episode occurring in Book 5.

The Book 5 episode is Aeneas's Iliadic centerpiece. When Diomedes rampages through the Trojan ranks, Aeneas seeks out Pandarus and together they attack the Greek champion. The encounter is catastrophic for the Trojans: Diomedes kills Pandarus and then strikes Aeneas on the groin with a massive stone, shattering his hip joint (005-261). As Diomedes moves in for the kill, Aphrodite descends to shield her son:

> The son of Tydeus sprang upon Aeneas, though he knew the goddess was shielding him, so set was he on killing Aeneas and stripping him of his armour.
> — Diomedes attacking Aeneas despite Aphrodite's protection (005-365, 005-367)

Aphrodite is wounded in the rescue — Diomedes slashes her wrist — and Apollo must take over, spiriting Aeneas away to his temple on the Pergamus, where he is healed and restored (005-376, 005-380). The episode establishes the pattern of Aeneas's life: a hero of great promise who requires divine rescue to fulfill a destiny that lies beyond the battlefield.

## Notable Speeches

Aeneas speaks with the directness of a warrior who knows his worth and his vulnerabilities. When Pandarus identifies Diomedes, his reply reveals a pragmatic recognition of the Greek champion's prowess:

> Aeneas answered, "Say no more. Things are not as you think."
> — Aeneas to Pandarus before their fatal attack on Diomedes (005-196)

His words carry the weight of a man who has faced death and been snatched back:

> "Hector and Aeneas, you two are the foremost of the Trojans in battle and in counsel."
> — A Trojan elder singling out Aeneas alongside Hector (006-065)

## Relationships

**Aphrodite (Venus):** The mother-son bond between Aphrodite and Aeneas is the most dramatically realized divine-parent relationship in the *Iliad* after that of Zeus and Sarpedon. Aphrodite's intervention to save Aeneas in Book 5 is an act of maternal love that costs her a wound — one of the few times a god suffers physical pain in the poem (005-322). Her care for Aeneas humanizes the goddess of love and gives Aeneas a tangible connection to the divine.

**Hector:** Aeneas fights alongside Hector as one of Troy's two foremost champions. He is named alongside Hector as the best of the Trojans "in battle and in counsel" (006-065), a distinction that places him in the inner circle of Trojan leadership. Their relationship is one of mutual respect rather than intimacy.

**Priam's House:** Aeneas's relationship with the royal family is complex. He belongs to a cadet branch of the Dardanian royal line, not the direct line of Priam. Post-Homeric tradition develops a rivalry between Aeneas and the sons of Priam, but within the *Iliad*, he fights loyally for the Trojan cause.

## Death or Fate

Aeneas does not die in the *Iliad*. He is rescued from certain death twice — first by Aphrodite and Apollo in Book 5, and later by [[poseidon|Poseidon]] in Book 20 when he confronts Achilles. Poseidon's intervention is explicitly motivated by fate: Aeneas is destined to survive the fall of Troy and continue the Dardanian line, and the gods will not permit him to die before his time.

This destiny, only hinted at in the *Iliad*, becomes the subject of Virgil's *Aeneid*, in which Aeneas leads the Trojan survivors from the burning city, journeys across the Mediterranean, and founds the settlement in Italy that will eventually give rise to Rome. The *Iliad* itself is notably restrained about this future — it suggests only that Aeneas is spared for a purpose — but the tradition that developed around him made him one of the most consequential figures in classical mythology.

## Scholarly Notes

Aeneas presents an interesting case of a character whose post-Homeric fame far exceeds his Iliadic role. Within the *Iliad*, he is a brave but secondary champion, rescued from death by divine intervention in his most prominent episode. His destiny — to rule over the remnant of the Trojan people — is mentioned only obliquely, in Poseidon's prophecy in Book 20. Samuel Butler's translation, using "Aeneas" consistently and "Venus" for his mother, conveys the character's nobility without the weight of Virgilian association.

Scholars have debated whether Aeneas's Iliadic portrayal reflects an independent tradition of Aeneas-worship in the Troad, predating the Homeric poems. The fact that he is given a unique destiny — and that Poseidon, a partisan of the Greeks, nevertheless saves him — suggests that the character carried special significance for the poem's original audience. The *Iliad*'s Aeneas is a hero in waiting: his true story begins where the epic ends.
