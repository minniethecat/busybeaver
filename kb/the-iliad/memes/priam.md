## Identity

Priam is the aged king of Troy, son of Laomedon, and descendant of Dardanus (hence the epithet "Dardanides"). He is the father of fifty sons, most notably Hector and Paris, and a host of daughters. His name evokes both royal authority and paternal vulnerability. He is described as "descendant of Dardanus" (003-241), tracing his lineage back to Zeus and the founding of the Trojan royal house.

By the time of the *Iliad*, Priam is an old man who has ruled Troy for many years — too old to fight, but still the symbolic heart of the city. He views the Achaean forces from the walls of Troy alongside the elders (003-003). Despite his age, he ventures to the battlefield to swear the oath for the truce between Paris and Menelaus (003-087), demonstrating his active role as king and his willingness to face danger for his city.

## Role in the Epic

Priam appears throughout the epic as the symbolic centre of Troy. The Greeks repeatedly speak of sacking "the city of Priam" (002-032, 002-093, 002-128) — the king's name is synonymous with the city itself. His authority is such that Agamemnon taunts the army with the prospect of "leaving Priam and the Trojans the glory" (002-140). Priam is not merely the ruler of Troy; he is its embodiment.

**The Teichoscopia (Book III):** Priam's first significant appearance is on the walls of Troy, where he sits among the elders and watches the Greek army assembled on the plain. When Helen approaches, he bids her draw near with unexpected kindness: "I do not blame you; it is the gods who have brought about this terrible war with the Achaeans" (003-131). He asks her to identify the Greek heroes — Agamemnon, Odysseus, Ajax — and his questions, full of genuine curiosity, reveal a king who is more interested in understanding his enemies than in hating them.

**The Truce (Book III):** Priam descends to the battlefield to swear the oath that will bind the single combat between Paris and Menelaus. His willingness to leave the safety of the walls — an old man venturing among armed warriors — is a testament to his courage and his commitment to ending the war. When the truce is broken by Pandarus' arrow, Priam's hopes of a peaceful resolution die with it.

**Watching Hector's Death (Book XXII):** As Achilles chases Hector around the walls, Priam stands on the rampart and sees his son's doom approaching. His anguish is described in harrowing detail. He begs Hector to come inside the walls: "Hector, my dear son, do not await this man alone. Have pity on me — an old man, still sentient, whom Zeus will destroy at the end of my life when I have seen my sons slain and my daughters dragged away" (022-042). When Hector falls, Priam is restrained from rushing onto the battlefield by his remaining sons, and his cry of grief echoes across the plain.

**The Embassy to Achilles (Book XXIV):** Priam's defining act is his night journey to the Greek camp to ransom Hector's body. Instructed by Zeus through Iris, he prepares a waggon loaded with treasure — "ten talents of gold, two tripods, four cauldrons, and a cup of rare workmanship" (024-232) — and sets out, guided by Hermes in disguise. The journey across the Trojan plain, through the Greek sentries, and into the very tent of Achilles is an act of extraordinary courage: an old, unarmed king walking alone into the heart of his enemy's camp.

When Priam reaches Achilles' tent, he falls at the killer's feet and kisses "the hand of him who slew my son" (024-445). He appeals to Achilles by invoking the memory of Peleus, Achilles' own father: "Think of your father, O Achilles, who is even such as I am — on the threshold of old age. Perhaps his neighbours are troubling him, and there is no one to defend him. Yet he rejoices in hearing that you are alive, and hopes day by day to see his dear son returning from Troy. But I — I had fifty sons when the Achaeans came, and of them all, Hector alone was left to me, the guardian of the city and its people." (024-440-456)

Achilles, moved to tears by the old man's grief, grants Priam's request and returns Hector's body, washed and wrapped in fine linen. The two enemies share a meal together — the first food Achilles has eaten since Patroclus' death — and Achilles grants Priam an eleven-day truce for Hector's funeral. The epic closes with the funeral of Hector (024-703), which Priam oversees as father and king.

## Notable Speeches

Priam's speeches in Book XXIV are among the most powerful in the *Iliad*. His supplication of Achilles is a masterpiece of emotional rhetoric — he does not threaten or bargain, but appeals to the universal experience of fatherhood and grief.

> "Hector, my dear son, do not await this man alone. Have pity on me — an old man, still sentient, whom Zeus will destroy at the end of my life when I have seen my sons slain and my daughters dragged away, my bridal chambers ransacked, and innocent children dashed to the ground in the terror of battle." (022-042-046)

> "Think of your father, O Achilles, who is even such as I am — on the threshold of old age. Perhaps his neighbours are troubling him, and there is no one to defend him. Yet he rejoices in hearing that you are alive, and hopes day by day to see his dear son returning from Troy. But I — I had fifty sons when the Achaeans came, and of them all, Hector alone was left to me, the guardian of the city and its people." (024-440-456)

> "I have endured what no other mortal on the face of the earth has ever done — I have kissed the hand of the man who killed my son." (024-445)

> Earlier in the epic, Priam's speech to Helen on the walls of Troy is notably kind: "My child, I do not blame you; it is the gods who have brought about this terrible war with the Achaeans." (003-130-131)

His wife Hecuba warns him against the embassy, but Priam is resolute: "Bring me not comfort in my grief, but let me go. If it is my fate to die by the ships of the Achaeans, so be it. Let Achilles kill me, so long as I may hold my son in my arms once more" (024-184-187). Priam's determination — to risk death for the chance to bury his son properly — is one of the epic's supreme acts of paternal devotion.

## Relationships

**Hector:** Priam's relationship with Hector is the emotional core of Books XXII and XXIV. Watching Hector's death from the walls, Priam is inconsolable, tearing his hair and crying out. His assertion that "there is none left bravest in all Troy for my sons" (024-434) is a father's anguished tribute. The recovery of Hector's body and the eleven-day truce for his funeral are Priam's final acts as a father — and they restore to Hector, even in death, the honour that Achilles' desecration had denied him.

**Achilles:** The encounter in Book XXIV transforms their relationship from pure enmity to mutual recognition of sorrow. Achilles sees his own father Peleus in Priam, and Priam sees in Achilles the son he has lost. Their shared meal — bread, meat, and wine, consumed in the tent that until moments before had held Hector's body — is a ritual of reconciliation that transcends the war.

**Helen:** Unusually, Priam treats Helen with kindness. At the Scaean gate, he bids her draw near and questions her about the Greek heroes without blame: "I do not blame you; it is the gods who have brought about this terrible war" (003-130-131). This moment of generosity toward the woman whose presence has brought war to his city reveals Priam's magnanimity. Helen's lament for Hector in Book XXIV reciprocates this kindness: "Priam was as kind to me as though he were my own father."

**Hecuba (wife):** The queen of Troy shares Priam's grief and warns him against the embassy to Achilles: "Your heart was ever as iron" (024-178). Yet Priam overrules her objections, and she ultimately supports his mission, giving him a libation cup and advising him to pour an offering to Zeus before departing.

**Paris:** Priam's relationship with Paris is complex. Though Paris is the cause of the war, Priam never reproaches him directly in the poem. When Paris is spirited away from the duel with Menelaus, it is Priam who announces that the terms of the truce — Helen to the victor — have been fulfilled, though the text leaves ambiguous whether Priam himself believes this. He seems to accept Paris with a kind of resigned paternal affection, unable to condemn the son whose actions have doomed his city.

**Other Sons:** Beyond Hector and Paris, Priam's sons include Deiphobus, Polites, Helenus the seer, and Troilus (killed before the action of the poem). His daughters include Cassandra the prophetess and Laodice, "the fairest of Priam's daughters" (003-103). These children populate the Trojan ranks and the city's inner chambers — the vast family that makes Priam both a king and a father.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Priam's death, but his fate is frequently alluded to. In later tradition — the *Sack of Troy* and Virgil's *Aeneid* — Priam is killed by Neoptolemus (Achilles' son) at the altar of Zeus Herkeios during the city's fall. He is cut down as he clings to the altar, his blood staining the sacred precinct. This brutal death — an old king, unarmed, slaughtered at the household altar — is the ultimate image of the destruction of a city and a family. The *Iliad*'s foreshadowing makes clear that this fate is inevitable, and the poem's closing scenes — the funeral of Hector, with Priam presiding — gain their emotional power precisely because the audience knows that Priam's own death, and the death of his city, will follow.

## Scholarly Notes

Priam is one of the most fully realised characters in the *Iliad* despite his limited appearances. Scholars have noted the parallel between Priam and Peleus — both aging fathers who lose their greatest son — which Achilles himself recognises in Book XXIV. The embassy scene is widely regarded as the poem's moral and emotional climax, demonstrating the *Iliad*'s capacity for empathy across enemy lines.

Priam's name may be connected to the Greek verb *priamai* ("to buy, to ransom"), fitting his defining act — the ransoming of Hector's body. The epithet "Dardanides" connects him to Dardanus, the mythical founder of the Trojan race and son of Zeus, reinforcing his dynastic legitimacy and his city's claim to divine favour.

His courage in entering the Greek camp alone — an old, unarmed king among his enemies — has been praised since antiquity as one of the supreme acts of heroism in the poem, a heroism defined not by martial prowess but by paternal love. The structure of Book XXIV — journey, supplication, recognition, meal, return — has been compared to the pattern of *xenia* (guest-friendship), inverted: the suppliant becomes the guest, the killer becomes the host, and the shared meal seals a temporary peace between enemies who both know the war will resume.

The mirroring of Priam and Peleus has also been read in psychoanalytic terms: Achilles, confronted with the image of his own father in the form of his enemy's father, achieves a moment of emotional integration. His tears for Priam are also tears for Peleus — and, ultimately, tears for himself, the son who will never return.
