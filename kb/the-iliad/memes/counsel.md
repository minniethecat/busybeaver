# Counsel (Boulē)

The Homeric world values [[aristeia|excellence]] in counsel as highly as [[aristeia|excellence]] in battle. The ideal hero — like [[odysseus|Odysseus]] or [[nestor|Nestor]] — is "excellent in fight and counsel" alike (001-045). The failure of counsel in Book I, when [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[achilles|Achilles]] cannot reach agreement, is the political failure that drives the entire epic.

## Definition

*Boulē* (βουλή) in Homer denotes both the act of deliberation and the institutional council where it occurs. It is distinct from the *agorē* (assembly) of the full army: the *boulē* is the council of chiefs and elders whose collective wisdom should guide the community. Zeus himself is "Olympian lord of counsel" (001-249), and divine *boulē* — the plans of the gods — shapes mortal affairs just as human councils shape battle strategy.

## Manifestations in the Epic

The epic's first assembly, called by [[achilles|Achilles]] in Book I, should be a m[[prophecy|omen]]t of communal deliberation about the plague. Instead it becomes the arena for the quarrel, demonstrating how *boulē* fails when personal honor overrides collective deliberation (001-081). [[nestor|Nestor]] repeatedly attempts to restore good counsel, urging both kings to end their quarrel: "I am older than either of you; therefore be advised by me" (001-048). The council of the gods mirrors human deliberation — Zeus presides, gods debate, but ultimately submit to his will (001-282).

## Greek Terminology

**βουλή (boulē)** — counsel, plan, or the council itself. **μῆτις (mētis)** — cunning intelligence ([[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s hallmark). **εὐβουλία (euboulia)** — good counsel, the quality [[nestor|Nestor]] embodies. **ἀγορή (agorē)** — the full assembly of the army.

## Exemplary Passages

> I am older than either of you; therefore be advised by me. Do not disregard my counsels. (001-048)

> You are excellent in fight and counsel. (001-045)

> Olympian lord of counsel, grant victory to the Trojans. (001-249)

## Contrasts and Tensions

The epic presents a tension between deliberative counsel and autocratic command. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] believes his authority entitles him to act without consultation; [[achilles|Achilles]] contests this model. The *Iliad* does not resolve this tension but dramatizes its consequences. The embassy to [[achilles|Achilles]] in Book IX further demonstrates the limits of counsel: despite [[odysseus|Odysseus]]'s cunning, Phoenix's emotion, and Ajax's bluntness, words cannot heal a wound to honor that only blood can address.
