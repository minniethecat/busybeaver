# Poseidon (Neptune)

Poseidon, called Neptune in Samuel Butler's translation, is the god of the sea, earthquakes, and horses — the "earth-shaker" (*ennosigaios*) and "earth-encircler" (*gaieochos*) whose power extends over the oceans and the ground beneath men's feet. He is the brother of [[zeus|Zeus]] (Jove) and Hades (Pluto), with whom he divided the cosmos: Zeus received the sky, Hades the underworld, and Poseidon the sea. In the Iliad, Poseidon is the most powerful divine champion of the Achaean cause after Hera and Athena. His great intervention in Books 13–14 — defying Zeus' express prohibition — turns the battle at a moment when the Trojans are on the verge of burning the Achaean ships, and constitutes the poem's most sustained depiction of a god fighting among mortals.

## Identity

Poseidon is the second-born son of Cronus (Saturn) and Rhea, and the brother of Zeus, Hades, [[hera|Hera]], Demeter, and Hestia. His domain, established by lot after the overthrow of Cronus, is the sea (015-025). But in the Iliad he exercises authority over more than the waters: as "lord of the earthquake" (007-355) he can shake the very foundations of the earth, and his anger, once roused, is formidable.

Butler's "Neptune" follows the Roman convention, though the Latin Neptune (originally a freshwater god) lacks the pronounced chthonic and equine associations of the Greek Poseidon. In the Iliad, Poseidon's most distinctive epithets — *ennosigaios* ("earth-shaker") and *gaieochos* ("earth-holding" or "earth-encircling") — emphasise his dominion over the physical world beneath and around the battlefield. He is a god of immense, barely contained force; even Zeus treats him with a respect that borders on wariness.

Poseidon's alignment with the Achaeans stems from an old grudge: he and Apollo were once compelled to serve Laomedon, king of Troy, building the city's walls for a promised wage that Laomedon refused to pay (012-017). This ancient betrayal fuels his enduring hatred of Troy.

## Role in the Epic

Poseidon's role in the Iliad is concentrated in the central books, where his defiance of Zeus constitutes the poem's most significant divine rebellion and its most spectacular display of divine warfare.

- **Books 13–14 — The Great Intervention:** Poseidon's intervention is the Iliad's longest sustained account of a god fighting on the battlefield. Stung by Zeus' prohibition against divine participation (008-174), Poseidon watches the Trojans advance toward the Achaean ships with growing fury. Seizing the moment when Zeus' attention is diverted, he descends from his observation post on Samothrace and arms himself in a scene that parallels a hero's arming: he yokes his bronze-shod horses, takes his great golden whip, and drives across the waves to Troy (013-012). The sea creatures recognise their lord and gambol around his chariot as he passes.

    He first appears to the two Ajaxes in the form of Calchas the seer, urging them to hold the line (013-045). Then, in his own voice — "loud as nine or ten thousand men crying out in battle" (013-045) — he roars encouragement to the entire Achaean army. His presence transforms the battle: the Achaeans, who moments before were on the verge of collapse, rally and drive the Trojans back. Throughout Books 13 and 14, Poseidon moves among the Achaean ranks, sometimes visible, sometimes disguised, his "mighty hand" tipping the balance in countless individual encounters.

- **Book 7 — The Wall Dispute:** When the Achaeans build a wall and ditch to protect their ships without offering sacrifice to the gods, Poseidon is outraged. He complains to Zeus that the Achaean wall will eclipse the fame of the wall he and Apollo once built for Laomedon at Troy (007-355). Zeus grants him permission to destroy the wall after the war — a prophetic moment that looks beyond the Iliad's timeframe to the eventual obliteration of all trace of the Achaean camp.

- **Book 15 — Withdrawal:** When Zeus awakens from Hera's seduction and sees Poseidon leading the Achaeans, his fury is immediate. He sends Iris to order Poseidon off the battlefield. Poseidon's initial response is defiant: "What, shall I, his own brother, bow before him? We three brothers, sons of Cronus, drew lots for our domains — he took the sky, I the sea, and Hades the underworld. The earth and Olympus are common to all" (015-025). But Iris counsels him to yield, reminding him that Zeus is the elder and stronger. Poseidon withdraws, but not before issuing a warning: if Troy is spared, he and Zeus will be "at war forever." This reluctant submission confirms both Poseidon's immense pride and the hierarchy of Olympus that Zeus, ultimately, enforces.

- **Book 20 — Saving Aeneas:** In a surprising turn, Poseidon — the Achaean partisan — rescues Aeneas from Achilles during the theomachy (020-101). When Apollo urges Aeneas to face Achilles, Poseidon objects, citing Aeneas' piety and noting that Aeneas is destined to survive the war and rule over the Trojans in a later age. This moment of divine mercy toward an enemy hero is one of the few instances where fate overrides partisan allegiance among the gods.

## Notable Speeches

> "What now, wide ruling lord of the earthquake? Are you, too, troubled?"
> — Zeus to Poseidon, noting his brother's anger at the Achaean wall (008-174)

> "We three brothers, sons of Cronus, drew lots for our domains — he took the sky, I the sea, and Hades the underworld. The earth and Olympus are common to all. I will not bow to the will of Zeus."
> — Poseidon defying Zeus' order to withdraw, Book 15 (015-025)

> "Hear me, all gods and goddesses: it is fate that Aeneas shall not perish. Zeus himself would be angry if Achilles slew him, for he is destined to escape, that the race of Dardanus may not perish without seed."
> — Poseidon advocating for Aeneas' survival, Book 20 (020-101)

## Relationships

- **Zeus (brother and sovereign):** The tension between Poseidon and Zeus is the Iliad's central fraternal dynamic among the gods. Poseidon is the younger brother who disputes the elder's authority, asserting the equality of their lot-determined domains. Zeus, for his part, handles Poseidon with more caution than he shows toward other gods — Poseidon is too powerful to be threatened with the same violence Zeus uses against Hera. Their dynamic is one of mutual wariness: Zeus is supreme, but Poseidon is not easily subdued.

- **Hera (ally in rebellion):** Hera and Poseidon share both a commitment to the Achaean cause and a willingness to defy Zeus' express commands. Hera's seduction of Zeus in Book 14 is designed precisely to give Poseidon a free hand on the battlefield. Their alliance is tactical rather than affectionate, but it is effective.

- **Apollo (fellow wall-builder):** Poseidon and Apollo share the grievance of having built Troy's walls for Laomedon without payment (012-017). But where Poseidon's grievance fuels hatred of Troy, Apollo's does not — the two gods part ways on the issue, and in Book 20 they nearly come to blows.

- **Athena (fellow Achaean partisan):** Athena and Poseidon work in parallel throughout Books 13–15, each supporting the Achaeans through different means — Athena through strategic counsel, Poseidon through direct martial intervention.

- **Aeneas (rescued enemy):** Poseidon's rescue of Aeneas in Book 20 is one of the poem's most striking acts of divine mercy. Despite his hatred of Troy, Poseidon respects fate: Aeneas is destined to survive, and Poseidon will not oppose destiny.

## Death or Fate

As a god, Poseidon is immortal, but the Iliad explores the limits of his power within the Olympian hierarchy. His defiance of Zeus in Books 13–15 is the most sustained act of divine rebellion in the poem, yet it ends in submission. The fraternal tension between Zeus and Poseidon mirrors the human conflicts of the epic — the younger brother who must yield to the elder, the powerful subordinate who chafes under authority. Poseidon's domain (the sea) is a constant presence at the edges of the poem: the Achaean ships, the shore, the walls that will one day be washed away. His sphere is the element that will ultimately erase all trace of the war.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler's "Neptune" for Poseidon follows the standard 19th-century convention of Latinising Greek divine names. The Greek *Poseidōn* (Ποσειδῶν) is of uncertain etymology — possibly "husband of the earth" (*posis* + *da*) or "lord of the waters." The epithet *ennosigaios* (ἐννοσίγαιος, "earth-shaker") and *gaieochos* (γαιήοχος, "earth-holding") encode Poseidon's dual nature as both the shaker and the stabiliser of the physical world.

Poseidon's arming scene in Book 13 (013-012) is modelled on the heroic arming type-scene, transferring the formulaic elements of mortal warriors (chariot, horses, whip) to a god. The detail that the sea creatures recognise and gambol around him as he passes is a rare moment of cosmic delight in a poem otherwise dominated by suffering.

The passage in Book 15 where Poseidon describes the three-way division of the cosmos among Zeus, himself, and Hades (015-025) is an important piece of early Greek cosmology, providing one of the poem's rare glimpses of the world's structure beyond the battlefield.
