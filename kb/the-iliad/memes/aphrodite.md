# Aphrodite (Venus)

Aphrodite, called Venus in Samuel Butler's translation, is the goddess of love, beauty, and desire. She is the daughter of Zeus and Dione, and the mother of [[aeneas|Aeneas]] by the mortal Anchises. In the Iliad she fights on the Trojan side — both to protect her mortal son Aeneas and because Paris (Alexander) awarded her the golden apple, choosing her beauty over Hera and Athena. Yet the poem consistently presents her as unfit for the battlefield: her interventions in combat are ineffectual, and when she is wounded by Diomedes in Book 5, the war-god realm rejects her. Her true power lies not in arms but in the irresistible force of desire — the "charm of love" that even Zeus cannot resist (014-181). Butler's "laughter-loving Venus" (from Homeric *philommeidēs Aphroditē*) captures both her delight in pleasure and her distance from the grim work of war.

## Identity

Aphrodite is the goddess who governs the realm of love, desire, and sexual attraction. In the Iliad's divine economy, she represents a force that is both universally powerful and unsuited to the heroic battlefield. Her Homeric epithet *philommeidēs* — "laughter-loving" or "smile-loving" — signals her remove from the world of mortal suffering. Butler consistently translates this as "laughter-loving Venus," preserving the epithet's ironic lightness even in the poem's darkest moments.

Her parentage in the Iliad follows the Homeric tradition: she is the daughter of Zeus and Dione (005-294). This differs from Hesiod's account, in which Aphrodite is born from the sea foam after Cronus castrates Uranus. The Iliad's genealogy places her firmly within the Olympian family as Zeus' daughter, a status that shapes her interactions — particularly when she flees to Dione's lap after being wounded (005-294).

Her association with Troy and the Trojans stems from two sources: she is the mother of Aeneas by the mortal Anchises (002-666), and she was chosen by Paris as the most beautiful goddess, earning his lifelong favour. Both ties draw her into the conflict on the Trojan side, though the poem makes clear that this is not her natural arena.

## Role in the Epic

Aphrodite appears in four major episodes spanning the Iliad, each illustrating a different facet of her power and its limitations in a war fought by warriors.

- **Book 3 — The Rescue of Paris:** When Menelaus is on the verge of killing Paris in their judicial duel, Aphrodite intervenes. She "snatched him up in a moment" and transported him through divine mist to his perfumed chamber in Troy (003-291). She then appears to Helen in the likeness of an aged spinning-woman and compels her — against Helen's sharp resistance — to go to Paris' bed (003-320). When Helen rebukes her, Venus answers with sudden menace: "Bold woman, do not provoke me. If you anger me, I will abandon you" (003-320). The scene encapsulates Aphrodite's two modes: protective of those she favours, coercive toward those who resist her domain.

- **Book 5 — The Wounding:** In the midst of Diomedes' aristeia, Athena grants him the power to distinguish gods from mortals on the battlefield and directs him to attack Aphrodite (005-121). Diomedes pursues her as she carries the wounded Aeneas from the field and drives his spear through her wrist (005-268). "The spear tore the skin, and the immortal blood — ichor — flowed from the wound" (005-294). Aphrodite "screamed aloud, and let her son fall" (005-294). She flees to Olympus, where Dione comforts her on her lap. This episode is the Iliad's most pointed statement about Aphrodite's incompatibility with warfare: the battlefield belongs to Athena and Ares; the goddess of love has no place on it.

- **Book 5 — The Complaint and Rebuke:** On Olympus, Aphrodite falls at Dione's knees and complains that Diomedes has wounded her (005-294). Dione comforts her, cataloguing other gods who have suffered at mortal hands — Ares bound by Otus and Ephialtes, Hera shot by Heracles, Hades wounded by the same hero (005-316). Zeus then calls her to his side and says, with characteristic gentleness toward her: "My child, the works of war are not for you. You shall concern yourself with the tender duties of marriage, and leave all feats of war to Athena and to Ares" (005-361). This divine division of labour is the Iliad's theological settlement: each god has a proper sphere, and Aphrodite's is not the battlefield.

- **Book 14 — The Deception of Zeus:** Aphrodite's most consequential contribution to the epic's plot comes indirectly. Hera, plotting to seduce Zeus so that Poseidon can aid the Achaeans unchecked, approaches Aphrodite and asks to borrow "the charm of love by which you subdue gods and men alike" (014-162). Aphrodite, deceived by Hera's fabricated story, takes from her bosom "the embroidered girdle into which all her charms had been wrought — love, desire, and the sweet whispers that steal away the judgment even of the wise" and gives it to Hera (014-181). The charm proves irresistible, and Zeus falls into the deepest sleep of his immortal life, allowing Poseidon to turn the battle in the Achaeans' favour. Aphrodite's unwitting role in this divine deception reveals her power's scope: even the supreme god is subject to desire.

## Relationships

- **Aeneas (son):** Aeneas is Aphrodite's mortal son by Anchises (002-666). Her maternal love drives her most dramatic battlefield intervention — when she shields Aeneas from Diomedes' attack and is herself wounded in the process (005-216). This wounding is the poem's most visceral illustration that love and war are fundamentally incompatible domains.

- **Paris (protégé):** Paris awarded Aphrodite the prize of beauty over Hera and Athena, and she remains his divine patron throughout the epic. She rescues him from death at Menelaus' hands in Book 3 (003-291), and her favour toward him aligns her with the Trojan cause.

- **Zeus (father):** Zeus' treatment of Aphrodite is notably gentle compared to his interactions with other gods. When she comes to him wounded, he does not rebuke her harshly but directs her away from war and toward her proper sphere (005-361). Their dynamic is one of paternal tenderness — unusual in the often-fraught relations of Olympus.

- **Hera (dupe):** Hera's manipulation of Aphrodite in Book 14 reveals both Aphrodite's power and her political naivety among the gods. Hera approaches her with a false story and obtains the charm of desire, using Aphrodite's own power against her husband. Aphrodite never learns of the deception, and her easy compliance underscores her distance from the strategic calculations that drive the other Olympians.

- **Diomedes (antagonist):** Diomedes, empowered by Athena, is the only mortal to wound a god in the Iliad, and Aphrodite is his first divine victim (005-268). The encounter establishes a hierarchy among divine powers: Athena-enabled martial prowess defeats Aphrodite's protective instinct every time.

## Death or Fate

As a goddess, Aphrodite is immortal. The Iliad does not threaten her with death but with something subtler: irrelevance. Her wounding by Diomedes (005-294) and Zeus' gentle dismissal of her from war (005-361) together establish that the battlefield — the poem's central arena of meaning — is not her domain. Where Ares can fight and be defeated, Aphrodite cannot fight at all. Her power operates elsewhere: in the bedchamber, in the seduction, in the irresistible pull of desire that governs even the king of the gods. The Iliad's treatment of Aphrodite is a meditation on the limits of love in a world organised around honour and violence.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler consistently renders Aphrodite as "Venus," following the Roman convention standard in 19th-century English translations. The Greek name *Aphroditē* (Ἀφροδίτη) is of uncertain etymology; the ancients associated it with *aphros* ("foam"), referencing Hesiod's alternative birth myth, but modern scholarship finds this dubious.

The epithet *philommeidēs* (φιλομμειδής), which Butler renders as "laughter-loving," is one of the most debated words in Homeric philology. It may mean "smile-loving" or "fond of laughter," but some scholars argue it derives from *meidiaō* ("to smile") rather than *gelōs* ("laughter"), and carries a subtly different connotation — not mirth but the alluring smile of seduction. The epithet "golden" (*chrysē*) applied to Aphrodite (003-055) is one of several Homeric colour-terms that scholars believe may refer to radiance or divine beauty rather than literal gold.

The wounding of Aphrodite in Book 5 is a unique episode in epic — no other goddess is wounded in combat. The motif of a god bleeding *ichor* (immortal blood, distinct from mortal blood) appears elsewhere (Ares, Hades), but Aphrodite's wounding carries particular ideological weight: it expels the erotic from the martial sphere in the most literal way possible.
