# Fate (Moira)

In the Homeric universe, fate is not an abstract philosophical principle but a concrete apportionment: each mortal receives their *moira* — a measured portion of life, honor, and ultimately death. Zeus himself, though supreme among the gods, cannot override fate without cosmic consequences (002-126).

## Definition

The Greek *moira* (μοῖρα) literally means "portion" or "share." In Homer, fate is the as[[prophecy|sign]]ed lot that no mortal or god can permanently evade. It is not predestination in the later philosophical sense but a boundary: a warrior may die "before his fated time" through recklessness, but cannot live beyond his appointed span. When a warrior is about to die, Homer often notes that "fate fell upon him" or "fate lured him to destruction" (002-680) (004-417).

## Manifestations in the Epic

Fate's presence is felt most acutely in m[[prophecy|omen]]ts of mortal peril. When Sarpedon's death is fated, Zeus contemplates saving him but is warned by Hera that overturning fate would unravel divine order. When [[achilles|Achilles]] must choose between a long obscure life and a short glorious one, his mother Thetis has known his fate since birth — he is "doomed to live but for a little season" (001-119). The fate of Troy itself hangs over the epic: the city is fated to fall, yet the poem ends before that fall, leaving destruction as an ever-present future (005-028).

## Greek Terminology

**μοῖρα (moira)** — one's portion or lot, especially in death. **αἶσα (aisa)** — a near-synonym, also meaning "portion." **κήρ (kēr)** — the doom or death-spirit that claims each warrior. **ὑπὲρ μόρον (hyper moron)** — "beyond fate," the possibility of dying before one's appointed time through folly.

## Exemplary Passages

> Fate lured them to destruction. (002-680)

> Then fate fell upon Diores, son of Amarynceus, for he was struck by a jagged stone. (004-417)

> You bore me doomed to live but for a little season. (001-119)

## Contrasts and Tensions

The *Iliad* holds two seemingly contradictory views in tension: fate is fixed and immutable, yet ch[[curse|ara]]cters make choices that affect outcomes. This ambiguity reflects the epic's deeper exploration of divine determination versus human responsibility. The concept also intersects with honor: a warrior's *moira* includes not only his death but his due share of [[honor|glory]] and [[prize|spoil]]s during life (005-082).
