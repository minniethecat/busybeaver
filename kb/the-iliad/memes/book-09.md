# Book IX

[009-001]BOOK IX.

[009-002][[the-embassy-to-achilles|The Embassy to Achilles]].

[009-003]Thus did the Trojans watch. But Panic, comrade of blood-stained

[009-004]Rout, had taken fast hold of the Achaeans, and their princes were

[009-005]all of them in despair. As when the two winds that blow from

[009-006]Thrace—the north and the northwest—spring up of a sudden and

[009-007]rouse the [[wrath|fury]] of the main—in a moment the dark waves uprear

[009-008]their heads and scatter their sea-wrack in all directions—even

[009-009]thus troubled were the hearts of the Achaeans.

## Section 23

[009-010]The son of Atreus in dismay bade the heralds call the people to a

[009-011]council man by man, but not to cry the matter aloud; he made

[009-012]haste also himself to call them, and they sat sorry at heart in

[009-013]their [[counsel|assembly]]. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] shed tears as it were a running stream

[009-014]or cataract on the side of some sheer cliff; and thus, with many

[009-015]a heavy sigh he spoke to the Achaeans. “My friends,” said he,

[009-016]“princes and councillors of the Argives, the hand of heaven has

[009-017]been laid heavily upon me. Cruel Jove gave me his solemn promise

[009-018]that I should sack the city of Troy before returning, but he has

[009-019]played me false, and is now bidding me go ingloriously back to

[009-020]Argos with the loss of much people. Such is the will of Jove, who

[009-021]has laid many a proud city in the dust as he will yet lay others,

[009-022]for his power is above all. Now, therefore, let us all do as I

[009-023]say and sail back to our own country, for we shall not take

[009-024]Troy.”

[009-025]Thus he spoke, and the sons of the Achaeans for a long while sat

[009-026]sorrowful there, but they all held their peace, till at last

[009-027]Diomed of the loud battle-cry made answer saying, “Son of Atreus,

[009-028]I will chide your folly, as is my right in council. Be not then

[009-029]aggrieved that I should do so. In the first place you attacked me

[009-030]before all the Danaans and said that I was a coward and no

[009-031]soldier. The Argives young and old know that you did so. But the

[009-032]son of scheming Saturn endowed you by halves only. He gave you

[009-033][[honor|honour]] as the chief ruler over us, but valour, which is the

[009-034]highest both right and might he did not give you. Sir, think you

[009-035]that the sons of the Achaeans are indeed as unwarlike and

[009-036]cowardly as you say they are? If your own mind is set upon going

[009-037]home—go—the way is open to you; the many [[achaean-camp|ships]] that followed you

[009-038]from Mycene stand ranged upon the sea-shore; but the rest of us

[009-039]stay here till we have sacked Troy. Nay though these too should

[009-040]turn homeward with their ships, Sthenelus and myself will still

[009-041]fight on till we reach the goal of Ilius, for heaven was with us

[009-042]when we came.”

[009-043]The sons of the Achaeans shouted applause at the words of Diomed,

[009-044]and presently [[nestor|Nestor]] rose to speak. “Son of Tydeus,” said he, “in

[009-045]war your [[aristeia|prowess]] is beyond question, and in council you excel all

[009-046]who are of your own years; no one of the Achaeans can make light

[009-047]of what you say nor gainsay it, but you have not yet come to the

[009-048]end of the whole matter. You are still young—you might be the

[009-049]youngest of my own children—still you have spoken wisely and have

[009-050]counselled the chief of the Achaeans not without discretion;

[009-051]nevertheless I am older than you and I will tell you everything;

[009-052]therefore let no man, not even King Agamemnon, disregard my

[009-053]saying, for he that foments civil discord is a clanless,

[009-054]hearthless outlaw.

[009-055]“Now, however, let us obey the behests of night and get our

[009-056]suppers, but let the sentinels every man of them camp by the

[009-057]trench that is without the wall. I am giving these instructions

[009-058]to the young men; when they have been attended to, do you, son of

[009-059]Atreus, give your orders, for you are the most royal among us

[009-060]all. Prepare a feast for your councillors; it is right and

[009-061]reasonable that you should do so; there is abundance of wine in

[009-062]your tents, which the ships of the Achaeans bring from Thrace

[009-063]daily. You have everything at your disposal wherewith to

[009-064]entertain guests, and you have many subjects. When many are got

[009-065]together, you can be guided by him whose counsel is wisest—and

[009-066]sorely do we need shrewd and prudent counsel, for the foe has lit

[009-067]his watchfires hard by our ships. Who can be other than dismayed?

[009-068]This night will either be the ruin of our [[guest-friendship|host]], or save it.”

[009-069]Thus did he speak, and they did even as he had said. The

[009-070]sentinels went out in their armour under command of Nestor’s son

[009-071]Thrasymedes, a captain of the host, and of the bold warriors

[009-072]Ascalaphus and Ialmenus: there were also Meriones, Aphareus and

[009-073]Deipyrus, and the son of Creion, noble Lycomedes. There were

[009-074]seven captains of the sentinels, and with each there went a

[009-075]hundred youths armed with long spears: they took their places

[009-076]midway between the trench and the wall, and when they had done so

[009-077]they lit their fires and got every man his supper.

[009-078]The son of Atreus then bade many councillors of the Achaeans to

[009-079]his quarters and prepared a great feast in their honour. They

[009-080]laid their hands on the good things that were before them, and as

[009-081]soon as they had enough to eat and drink, old Nestor, whose

[009-082]counsel was ever truest, was the first to lay his mind before

[009-083]them. He, therefore, with all sincerity and goodwill addressed

[009-084]them thus.

[009-085]“With yourself, most noble son of Atreus, king of men, Agamemnon,

[009-086]will I both begin my speech and end it, for you are king over

[009-087]much people. Jove, moreover, has vouchsafed you to wield the

[009-088]sceptre and to uphold righteousness, that you may take thought

[009-089]for your people under you; therefore it behooves you above all

[009-090]others both to speak and to give ear, and to out the counsel of

[009-091]another who shall have been minded to speak wisely. All turns on

[009-092]you and on your commands, therefore I will say what I think will

[009-093]be best. No man will be of a truer mind than that which has been

[009-094]mine from the hour when you, sir, angered [[achilles|Achilles]] by taking the

[009-095]girl Briseis from his tent against my judgment. I urged you not

[009-096]to do so, but you yielded to your own pride, and dishonoured a

[009-097]hero whom heaven itself had honoured—for you still hold the prize

[009-098]that had been awarded to him. Now, however, let us think how we

[009-099]may appease him, both with presents and fair speeches that may

[009-100]conciliate him.”

[009-101]And King Agamemnon answered, “Sir, you have reproved my folly

[009-102]justly. I was wrong. I own it. One whom heaven befriends is in

[009-103]himself a host, and Jove has shown that he befriends this man by

[009-104]destroying much people of the Achaeans. I was blinded with

[009-105]passion and yielded to my worser mind; therefore I will make

[009-106]amends, and will give him great gifts by way of atonement. I will

[009-107]tell them in the presence of you all. I will give him seven

[009-108]tripods that have never yet been on the fire, and ten talents of

[009-109]gold. I will give him twenty iron cauldrons and twelve strong

[009-110]horses that have won races and carried off prizes. Rich, indeed,

[009-111]both in land and gold is he that has as many prizes as my horses

[009-112]have won me. I will give him seven excellent workwomen, Lesbians,

[009-113]whom I chose for myself when he took Lesbos—all of surpassing

[009-114]beauty. I will give him these, and with them her whom I erewhile

[009-115]took from him, the daughter of Briseus; and I [[oath|swear]] a great oath

[009-116]that I never went up into her couch, nor have been with her after

[009-117]the manner of men and women.

[009-118]“All these things will I give him now, and if hereafter the gods

[009-119]vouchsafe me to sack the city of [[priam|Priam]], let him come when we

[009-120]Achaeans are dividing the [[prize|spoil]], and load his ship with gold and

[009-121]bronze to his liking; furthermore let him take twenty Trojan

[009-122]women, the loveliest after [[helen|Helen]] herself. Then, when we reach

[009-123]Achaean Argos, wealthiest of all lands, he shall be my son-in-law

[009-124]and I will show him like honour with my own dear son Orestes, who

[009-125]is being nurtured in all abundance. I have three daughters,

[009-126]Chrysothemis, Laodice, and Iphianassa, let him take the one of

[009-127]his choice, freely and without gifts of wooing, to the house of

[009-128]Peleus; I will add such dower to boot as no man ever yet gave his

[009-129]daughter, and will give him seven well-established cities,

[009-130]Cardamyle, Enope, and Hire, where there is grass; holy Pherae and

[009-131]the rich meadows of Anthea; Aepea also, and the vine-clad slopes

[009-132]of Pedasus, all near the sea, and on the borders of sandy Pylos.

[009-133]The men that dwell there are rich in cattle and sheep; they will

[009-134]honour him with gifts as though he were a god, and be obedient to

[009-135]his comfortable ordinances. All this will I do if he will now

[009-136]forgo his anger. Let him then yield; it is only Hades who is

[009-137]utterly ruthless and unyielding—and hence he is of all gods the

[009-138]one most hateful to mankind. Moreover I am older and more royal

[009-139]than himself. Therefore, let him now obey me.”

[009-140]Then Nestor answered, “Most noble son of Atreus, king of men,

[009-141]Agamemnon. The gifts you offer are no small ones, let us then

[009-142]send chosen messengers, who may go to the tent of Achilles son of

[009-143]Peleus without delay. Let those go whom I shall name. Let

[009-144]Phoenix, dear to Jove, lead the way; let Ajax and [[odysseus|Ulysses]] follow,

[009-145]and let the heralds Odius and Eurybates go with them. Now bring

[009-146]water for our hands, and bid all keep silence while we pray to

[009-147]Jove the son of Saturn, if so be that he may have mercy upon us.”

[009-148]Thus did he speak, and his saying pleased them well. Men-servants

[009-149]poured water over the hands of the guests, while pages filled the

[009-150]mixing-bowls with wine and water, and handed it round after

[009-151]giving every man his drink-[[sacrifice|offering]]; then, when they had made

[009-152]their offerings, and had drunk each as much as he was minded, the

[009-153]envoys set out from the tent of Agamemnon son of Atreus; and

[009-154]Nestor, looking first to one and then to another, but most

[009-155]especially at Ulysses, was instant with them that they should

[009-156]prevail with the noble son of Peleus.

[009-157]They went their way by the shore of the sounding sea, and prayed

[009-158]earnestly to earth-encircling Neptune that the high spirit of the

[009-159]son of Aeacus might incline favourably towards them. When they

[009-160]reached the ships and tents of the Myrmidons, they found Achilles

[009-161]playing on a lyre, fair, of cunning workmanship, and its

[009-162]cross-bar was of silver. It was part of the spoils which he had

[009-163]taken when he sacked the city of Eetion, and he was now diverting

[009-164]himself with it and singing the feats of heroes. He was alone

[009-165]with [[patroclus|Patroclus]], who sat opposite to him and said nothing, waiting

[009-166]till he should cease singing. Ulysses and Ajax now came

[009-167]in—Ulysses leading the way—and stood before him. Achilles sprang

[009-168]from his seat with the lyre still in his hand, and Patroclus,

[009-169]when he saw the strangers, rose also. Achilles then greeted them

[009-170]saying, “All hail and welcome—you must come upon some great

[009-171]matter, you, who for all my anger are still dearest to me of the

[009-172]Achaeans.”

[009-173]With this he led them forward, and bade them sit on seats covered

[009-174]with purple rugs; then he said to Patroclus who was close by him,

[009-175]“Son of Menoetius, set a larger bowl upon the table, mix less

[009-176]water with the wine, and give every man his cup, for these are

[009-177]very dear friends, who are now under my roof.”

[009-178]Patroclus did as his comrade bade him; he set the chopping-block

[009-179]in front of the fire, and on it he laid the loin of a sheep, the

[009-180]loin also of a goat, and the chine of a fat hog. Automedon held

[009-181]the meat while Achilles chopped it; he then sliced the pieces and

[009-182]put them on spits while the son of Menoetius made the fire burn

[009-183]high. When the flame had died down, he spread the embers, laid

[009-184]the spits on top of them, lifting them up and setting them upon

[009-185]the spit-racks; and he sprinkled them with salt. When the meat

[009-186]was roasted, he set it on platters, and handed bread round the

[009-187]table in fair baskets, while Achilles dealt them their portions.

[009-188]Then Achilles took his seat facing Ulysses against the opposite

[009-189]wall, and bade his comrade Patroclus offer sacrifice to the gods;

[009-190]so he cast the offerings into the fire, and they laid their hands

[009-191]upon the good things that were before them. As soon as they had

[009-192]had enough to eat and drink, Ajax made a [[prophecy|sign]] to Phoenix, and

[009-193]when he saw this, Ulysses filled his cup with wine and pledged

[009-194]Achilles.

## Section 24

[009-195]“Hail,” said he, “Achilles, we have had no scant of good cheer,

[009-196]neither in the tent of Agamemnon, nor yet here; there has been

[009-197]plenty to eat and drink, but our thought turns upon no such

[009-198]matter. Sir, we are in the face of great disaster, and without

[009-199]your help know not whether we shall save our fleet or lose it.

[009-200]The Trojans and their allies have camped hard by our ships and by

[009-201]the wall; they have lit watchfires throughout their host and deem

[009-202]that nothing can now prevent them from falling on our fleet.

[009-203]Jove, moreover, has sent his lightnings on their right; [[hector|Hector]],

[009-204]in all his glory, rages like a maniac; confident that Jove is

[009-205]with him he fears neither god nor man, but is gone raving mad,

[009-206]and prays for the approach of day. He vows that he will hew the

[009-207]high sterns of our ships in pieces, set fire to their hulls, and

[009-208]make havoc of the Achaeans while they are dazed and smothered in

[009-209]smoke; I much fear that heaven will make good his boasting, and

[009-210]it will prove our lot to perish at Troy far from our home in

[009-211]Argos. Up, then, and late though it be, save the sons of the

[009-212]Achaeans who faint before the fury of the Trojans. You will

[009-213]repent bitterly hereafter if you do not, for when the harm is

[009-214]done there will be no curing it; consider ere it be too late, and

[009-215]save the Danaans from destruction.

[009-216]“My good friend, when your father Peleus sent you from Phthia to

[009-217]Agamemnon, did he not charge you saying, ‘Son, Minerva and Juno

[009-218]will make you strong if they choose, but check your high temper,

[009-219]for the better part is in goodwill. Eschew vain quarrelling, and

[009-220]the Achaeans old and young will [[shame|respect]] you more for doing so.’

[009-221]These were his words, but you have forgotten them. Even now,

[009-222]however, be appeased, and put away your anger from you. Agamemnon

[009-223]will make you great amends if you will forgive him; listen, and I

[009-224]will tell you what he has said in his tent that he will give you.

[009-225]He will give you seven tripods that have never yet been on the

[009-226]fire, and ten talents of gold; twenty iron cauldrons, and twelve

[009-227]strong horses that have won races and carried off prizes. Rich

[009-228]indeed both in land and gold is he who has as many prizes as

[009-229]these horses have won for Agamemnon. Moreover he will give you

[009-230]seven excellent workwomen, Lesbians, whom he chose for himself,

[009-231]when you took Lesbos—all of surpassing beauty. He will give you

[009-232]these, and with them her whom he erewhile took from you, the

[009-233]daughter of Briseus, and he will swear a great oath, he has never

[009-234]gone up into her couch nor been with her after the manner of men

[009-235]and women. All these things will he give you now down, and if

[009-236]hereafter the gods vouchsafe him to sack the city of Priam, you

[009-237]can come when we Achaeans are dividing the spoil, and load your

[009-238]ship with gold and bronze to your liking. You can take twenty

[009-239]Trojan women, the loveliest after Helen herself. Then, when we

[009-240]reach Achaean Argos, wealthiest of all lands, you shall be his

[009-241]son-in-law, and he will show you like honour with his own dear

[009-242]son Orestes, who is being nurtured in all abundance. Agamemnon

[009-243]has three daughters, Chrysothemis, Laodice, and Iphianassa; you

[009-244]may take the one of your choice, freely and without gifts of

[009-245]wooing, to the house of Peleus; he will add such dower to boot as

[009-246]no man ever yet gave his daughter, and will give you seven

[009-247]well-established cities, Cardamyle, Enope, and Hire where there

[009-248]is grass; holy Pherae and the rich meadows of Anthea; Aepea also,

[009-249]and the vine-clad slopes of Pedasus, all near the sea, and on the

[009-250]borders of sandy Pylos. The men that dwell there are rich in

[009-251]cattle and sheep; they will honour you with gifts as though were

[009-252]a god, and be obedient to your comfortable ordinances. All this

[009-253]will he do if you will now forgo your anger. Moreover, though you

[009-254]hate both him and his gifts with all your heart, yet pity the

[009-255]rest of the Achaeans who are being harassed in all their host;

[009-256]they will honour you as a god, and you will earn great glory at

[009-257]their hands. You might even kill Hector; he will come within your

[009-258]reach, for he is infatuated, and declares that not a Danaan whom

[009-259]the ships have brought can hold his own against him.”

[009-260]Achilles answered, “Ulysses, noble son of Laertes, I should give

[009-261]you formal notice plainly and in all fixity of purpose that there

[009-262]be no more of this cajoling, from whatsoever quarter it may come.

[009-263]Him do I hate even as the gates of hell who says one thing while

[009-264]he hides another in his heart; therefore I will say what I mean.

[009-265]I will be appeased neither by Agamemnon son of Atreus nor by any

[009-266]other of the Danaans, for I see that I have no thanks for all my

[009-267]fighting. He that fights fares no better than he that does not;

[009-268]coward and hero are held in equal honour, and death deals like

[009-269]measure to him who works and him who is idle. I have taken

[009-270]nothing by all my hardships—with my life ever in my hand; as a

[009-271]bird when she has found a morsel takes it to her nestlings, and

[009-272]herself fares hardly, even so many a long night have I been

[009-273]wakeful, and many a bloody battle have I waged by day against

[009-274]those who were fighting for their women. With my ships I have

[009-275]taken twelve cities, and eleven round about Troy have I stormed

[009-276]with my men by land; I took great store of wealth from every one

[009-277]of them, but I gave all up to Agamemnon son of Atreus. He stayed

[009-278]where he was by his ships, yet of what came to him he gave

[009-279]little, and kept much himself.

[009-280]“Nevertheless he did distribute some meeds of honour among the

[009-281]chieftains and kings, and these have them still; from me alone of

[009-282]the Achaeans did he take the woman in whom I delighted—let him

[009-283]keep her and sleep with her. Why, pray, must the Argives needs

[009-284]fight the Trojans? What made the son of Atreus gather the host

[009-285]and bring them? Was it not for the sake of Helen? Are the sons of

[009-286]Atreus the only men in the world who love their wives? Any man of

[009-287]common right feeling will love and cherish her who is his own, as

[009-288]I this woman, with my whole heart, though she was but a fruitling

[009-289]of my spear. Agamemnon has taken her from me; he has played me

[009-290]false; I know him; let him tempt me no further, for he shall not

[009-291]move me. Let him look to you, Ulysses, and to the other princes

[009-292]to save his ships from burning. He has done much without me

[009-293]already. He has built a wall; he has dug a trench deep and wide

[009-294]all round it, and he has planted it within with stakes; but even

[009-295]so he stays not the murderous might of Hector. So long as I

[009-296]fought the Achaeans Hector suffered not the battle range far from

[009-297]the city walls; he would come to the Scaean gates and to the oak

[009-298]tree, but no further. Once he stayed to meet me and hardly did he

[009-299]escape my onset: now, however, since I am in no mood to fight

[009-300]him, I will to-morrow offer sacrifice to Jove and to all the

[009-301]gods; I will draw my ships into the water and then victual them

[009-302]duly; to-morrow morning, if you care to look, you will see my

[009-303]ships on the Hellespont, and my men rowing out to sea with might

[009-304]and main. If great Neptune vouchsafes me a fair passage, in three

[009-305]days I shall be in Phthia. I have much there that I left behind

[009-306]me when I came here to my [[grief|sorrow]], and I shall bring back still

[009-307]further store of gold, of red copper, of fair women, and of iron,

[009-308]my share of the spoils that we have taken; but one prize, he who

[009-309]gave has insolently taken away. Tell him all as I now bid you,

[009-310]and tell him in public that the Achaeans may hate him and beware

[009-311]of him should he think that he can yet dupe others for his

[009-312]effrontery never fails him.

[009-313]“As for me, hound that he is, he dares not look me in the face. I

[009-314]will take no counsel with him, and will undertake nothing in

[009-315]common with him. He has wronged me and deceived me enough, he

[009-316]shall not cozen me further; let him go his own way, for Jove has

[009-317]robbed him of his reason. I loathe his presents, and for himself

[009-318]care not one straw. He may offer me ten or even twenty times what

[009-319]he has now done, nay—not though it be all that he has in the

[009-320]world, both now or ever shall have; he may promise me the wealth

[009-321]of Orchomenus or of Egyptian Thebes, which is the richest city in

[009-322]the whole world, for it has a hundred gates through each of which

[009-323]two hundred men may drive at once with their chariots and horses;

[009-324]he may offer me gifts as the sands of the sea or the dust of the

[009-325]plain in multitude, but even so he shall not move me till I have

[009-326]been revenged in full for the bitter wrong he has done me. I will

[009-327]not marry his daughter; she may be fair as Venus, and skilful as

[009-328]Minerva, but I will have none of her: let another take her, who

[009-329]may be a good match for her and who rules a larger kingdom. If

[009-330]the gods spare me to return home, Peleus will find me a wife;

[009-331]there are Achaean women in Hellas and Phthia, daughters of kings

[009-332]that have cities under them; of these I can take whom I will and

[009-333]marry her. Many a time was I minded when at home in Phthia to woo

[009-334]and wed a woman who would make me a suitable wife, and to enjoy

[009-335]the riches of my old father Peleus. My life is more to me than

[009-336]all the wealth of Ilius while it was yet at peace before the

[009-337]Achaeans went there, or than all the treasure that lies on the

[009-338]stone floor of Apollo’s temple beneath the cliffs of Pytho.

[009-339]Cattle and sheep are to be had for harrying, and a man buy both

[009-340]tripods and horses if he wants them, but when his life has once

[009-341]left him it can neither be bought nor harried back again.

[009-342]“My mother Thetis tells me that there are two ways in which I may

[009-343]meet my end. If I stay here and fight, I shall not return alive

[009-344]but my name will live for ever: whereas if I go home my name will

[009-345]die, but it will be long ere death shall take me. To the rest of

[009-346]you, then, I say, ‘Go home, for you will not take Ilius.’ Jove

[009-347]has held his hand over her to protect her, and her people have

[009-348]taken heart. Go, therefore, as in duty bound, and tell the

[009-349]princes of the Achaeans the message that I have sent them; tell

[009-350]them to find some other plan for the saving of their ships and

[009-351]people, for so long as my displeasure lasts the one that they

[009-352]have now hit upon may not be. As for Phoenix, let him sleep here
