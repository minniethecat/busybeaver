# Book XI

[011-001]BOOK XI.

[011-002]In the forenoon the fight is equal, but [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] turns the

[011-003]fortune of the day towards the Achaeans until he gets wounded and

[011-004]leaves the field—[[hector|Hector]] then drives everything before him till he

[011-005]is wounded by Diomed—Paris wounds Diomed—[[odysseus|Ulysses]], [[nestor|Nestor]], and

[011-006]Idomeneus perform prodigies of valour—Machaon is wounded—Nestor

[011-007]drives him off in his chariot—[[achilles|Achilles]] sees the pair driving

[011-008]towards the camp and sends [[patroclus|Patroclus]] to ask who it is that is

[011-009]wounded—This is the beginning of evil for Patroclus—Nestor makes

[011-010]a long speech.

[011-011]And now as Dawn rose from her couch beside Tithonus, harbinger of

[011-012]light alike to mortals and immortals, Jove sent fierce Discord

[011-013]with the ensign of war in her hands to the [[achaean-camp|ships]] of the Achaeans.

[011-014]She took her stand by the huge black hull of Ulysses’ ship which

[011-015]was middlemost of all, so that her voice might carry farthest on

[011-016]either side, on the one hand towards the tents of Ajax son of

[011-017]Telamon, and on the other towards those of Achilles—for these two

[011-018]heroes, well assured of their own strength, had valorously drawn

[011-019]up their ships at the two ends of the line. There she took her

[011-020]stand, and raised a cry both loud and shrill that filled the

[011-021]Achaeans with courage, giving them heart to fight resolutely and

[011-022]with all their might, so that they had rather stay there and do

[011-023]battle than go home in their ships.

[011-024]The son of Atreus shouted aloud and bade the Argives gird

[011-025]themselves for battle while he put on his armour. First he girded

[011-026]his goodly greaves about his legs, making them fast with

[011-027]ankle-clasps of silver; and about his chest he set the

[011-028]breastplate which Cinyras had once given him as a guest-gift. It

[011-029]had been noised abroad as far as Cyprus that the Achaeans were

[011-030]about to sail for Troy, and therefore he gave it to the king. It

[011-031]had ten courses of dark cyanus, twelve of gold, and ten of tin.

[011-032]There were serpents of cyanus that reared themselves up towards

[011-033]the neck, three upon either side, like the rainbows which the son

[011-034]of Saturn has set in heaven as a [[prophecy|sign]] to mortal men. [[About]] his

[011-035]shoulders he threw his sword, studded with bosses of gold; and

[011-036]the scabbard was of silver with a chain of gold wherewith to hang

[011-037]it. He took moreover the richly-dight shield that covered his

[011-038]body when he was in battle—fair to see, with ten circles of

[011-039]bronze running all round it. On the body of the shield there were

[011-040]twenty bosses of white tin, with another of dark cyanus in the

[011-041]middle: this last was made to show a Gorgon’s head, fierce and

[011-042]grim, with Rout and Panic on either side. The band for the arm to

[011-043]go through was of silver, on which there was a writhing snake of

[011-044]cyanus with three heads that sprang from a single neck, and went

[011-045]in and out among one another. On his head Agamemnon set a helmet,

[011-046]with a peak before and behind, and four plumes of horse-hair that

[011-047]nodded menacingly above it; then he grasped two redoubtable

[011-048]bronze-shod spears, and the gleam of his armour shot from him as

[011-049]a flame into the firmament, while Juno and Minerva thundered in

[011-050][[honor|honour]] of the king of rich Mycene.

## Section 29

[011-051]Every man now left his horses in charge of his charioteer to hold

[011-052]them in readiness by the trench, while he went into battle on

[011-053]foot clad in full armour, and a mighty uproar rose on high into

[011-054]the dawning. The chiefs were armed and at the trench before the

[011-055]horses got there, but these came up presently. The son of Saturn

[011-056]sent a portent of evil sound about their [[guest-friendship|host]], and the dew fell

[011-057]red with blood, for he was about to send many a brave man

[011-058]hurrying down to Hades.

[011-059]The Trojans, on the other side upon the rising slope of the

[011-060]plain, were gathered round great Hector, noble Polydamas, Aeneas

[011-061]who was honoured by the Trojans like an immortal, and the three

[011-062]sons of Antenor, Polybus, Agenor, and young Acamas beauteous as a

[011-063]god. Hector’s round shield showed in the front rank, and as some

[011-064]baneful star that shines for a moment through a rent in the

[011-065]clouds and is again hidden beneath them; even so was Hector now

[011-066]seen in the front ranks and now again in the hindermost, and his

[011-067]bronze armour gleamed like the lightning of aegis-bearing Jove.

[011-068]And now as a band of reapers mow swathes of wheat or barley upon

[011-069]a rich man’s land, and the sheaves fall thick before them, even

[011-070]so did the Trojans and Achaeans fall upon one another; they were

[011-071]in no mood for yielding but fought like wolves, and neither side

[011-072]got the better of the other. Discord was glad as she beheld them,

[011-073]for she was the only god that went among them; the others were

[011-074]not there, but stayed quietly each in his own home among the

[011-075]dells and valleys of [[mount-olympus|Olympus]]. All of them blamed the son of

[011-076]Saturn for wanting to give victory to the Trojans, but father

[011-077]Jove heeded them not: he held aloof from all, and sat apart in

[011-078]his all-glorious majesty, looking down upon the city of the

[011-079]Trojans, the ships of the Achaeans, the gleam of bronze, and

[011-080]alike upon the slayers and on the slain.

[011-081]Now so long as the day waxed and it was still morning, their

[011-082]darts rained thick on one another and the people perished, but as

[011-083]the hour drew nigh when a woodman working in some mountain forest

[011-084]will get his midday meal—for he has felled till his hands are

[011-085]weary; he is tired out, and must now have food—then the Danaans

[011-086]with a cry that rang through all their ranks, broke the

[011-087]battalions of the enemy. Agamemnon led them on, and slew first

[011-088]Bienor, a leader of his people, and afterwards his comrade and

[011-089]charioteer Oileus, who sprang from his chariot and was coming

[011-090]full towards him; but Agamemnon struck him on the forehead with

[011-091]his spear; his bronze visor was of no avail against the weapon,

[011-092]which pierced both bronze and bone, so that his brains were

[011-093]battered in and he was killed in full fight.

[011-094]Agamemnon stripped their shirts from off them and left them with

[011-095]their breasts all bare to lie where they had fallen. He then went

[011-096]on to kill Isus and Antiphus two sons of [[priam|Priam]], the one a

[011-097]bastard, the other born in wedlock; they were in the same

[011-098]chariot—the bastard driving, while noble Antiphus fought beside

[011-099]him. Achilles had once taken both of them prisoners in the glades

[011-100]of [[mount-ida|Ida]], and had bound them with fresh withes as they were

[011-101]shepherding, but he had taken a ransom for them; now, however,

[011-102]Agamemnon son of Atreus smote Isus in the chest above the nipple

[011-103]with his spear, while he struck Antiphus hard by the ear and

[011-104]threw him from his chariot. Forthwith he stripped their goodly

[011-105]armour from off them and recognized them, for he had already seen

[011-106]them at ships when Achilles brought them in from Ida. As a lion

[011-107]fastens on the fawns of a hind and crushes them in his great

[011-108]jaws, robbing them of their tender life while he on his way back

[011-109]to his lair—the hind can do nothing for them even though she be

[011-110]close by, for she is in an agony of fear, and flies through the

[011-111]thick forest, sweating, and at her utmost speed before the mighty

[011-112]monster—so, no man of the Trojans could help Isus and Antiphus,

[011-113]for they were themselves flying in panic before the Argives.

[011-114]Then King Agamemnon took the two sons of Antimachus, Pisander and

[011-115]brave Hippolochus. It was Antimachus who had been foremost in

[011-116]preventing [[helen|Helen]]’s being restored to Menelaus, for he was largely

[011-117]bribed by [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]]; and now Agamemnon took his two sons, both

[011-118]in the same chariot, trying to bring their horses to a stand—for

[011-119]they had lost hold of the reins and the horses were mad with

[011-120]fear. The son of Atreus sprang upon them like a lion, and the

[011-121]pair besought him from their chariot. “Take us alive,” they

[011-122]cried, “son of Atreus, and you shall receive a great ransom for

[011-123]us. Our father Antimachus has great store of gold, bronze, and

[011-124]wrought iron, and from this he will satisfy you with a very large

[011-125]ransom should he hear of our being alive at the ships of the

[011-126]Achaeans.”

[011-127]With such piteous words and tears did they [[supplication|beseech]] the king, but

[011-128]they heard no pitiful answer in return. “If,” said Agamemnon,

[011-129]“you are sons of Antimachus, who once at a council of Trojans

[011-130]proposed that Menelaus and Ulysses, who had come to you as

[011-131]envoys, should be killed and not suffered to return, you shall

[011-132]now pay for the foul iniquity of your father.”

[011-133]As he spoke he felled Pisander from his chariot to the earth,

[011-134]smiting him on the chest with his spear, so that he lay face

[011-135]uppermost upon the ground. Hippolochus fled, but him too did

[011-136]Agamemnon smite; he cut off his hands and his head—which he sent

[011-137]rolling in among the crowd as though it were a ball. There he let

[011-138]them both lie, and wherever the ranks were thickest thither he

[011-139]flew, while the other Achaeans followed. Foot soldiers drove the

[011-140]foot soldiers of the foe in rout before them, and slew them;

[011-141]horsemen did the like by horsemen, and the thundering tramp of

[011-142]the horses raised a cloud of dust from off the plain. King

[011-143]Agamemnon followed after, ever slaying them and cheering on the

[011-144]Achaeans. As when some mighty forest is all ablaze—the eddying

[011-145]gusts whirl fire in all directions till the thickets shrivel and

[011-146]are consumed before the blast of the flame—even so fell the heads

[011-147]of the flying Trojans before Agamemnon son of Atreus, and many a

[011-148]noble pair of steeds drew an empty chariot along the highways of

[011-149]war, for lack of drivers who were lying on the plain, more useful

[011-150]now to vultures than to their wives.

[011-151]Jove drew Hector away from the darts and dust, with the carnage

[011-152]and din of battle; but the son of Atreus sped onwards, calling

[011-153]out lustily to the Danaans. They flew on by the tomb of old Ilus,

[011-154]son of Dardanus, in the middle of the plain, and past the place

[011-155]of the wild fig-tree making always for the city—the son of Atreus

[011-156]still shouting, and with hands all bedrabbled in gore; but when

[011-157]they had reached the Scaean gates and the oak tree, there they

[011-158]halted and waited for the others to come up. Meanwhile the

[011-159]Trojans kept on flying over the middle of the plain like a herd

[011-160]of cows maddened with fright when a lion has attacked them in the

[011-161]dead of night—he springs on one of them, seizes her neck in the

[011-162]grip of his strong teeth and then laps up her blood and gorges

[011-163]himself upon her entrails—even so did King Agamemnon son of

[011-164]Atreus pursue the foe, ever slaughtering the hindmost as they

[011-165]fled pell-mell before him. Many a man was flung headlong from his

[011-166]chariot by the hand of the son of Atreus, for he wielded his

[011-167]spear with [[wrath|fury]].

[011-168]But when he was just about to reach the high wall and the city,

[011-169]the father of gods and men came down from heaven and took his

[011-170]seat, thunderbolt in hand, upon the crest of many-fountained Ida.

[011-171]He then told Iris of the golden wings to carry a message for him.

[011-172]“Go,” said he, “fleet Iris, and speak thus to Hector—say that so

[011-173]long as he sees Agamemnon heading his men and making havoc of the

[011-174]Trojan ranks, he is to keep aloof and bid the others bear the

[011-175]brunt of the battle, but when Agamemnon is wounded either by

[011-176]spear or arrow, and takes to his chariot, then will I vouchsafe

[011-177]him strength to slay till he reach the ships and night falls at

[011-178]the going down of the sun.”

[011-179]Iris hearkened and obeyed. Down she went to strong Ilius from the

[011-180]crests of Ida, and found Hector son of Priam standing by his

[011-181]chariot and horses. Then she said, “Hector son of Priam, peer of

[011-182]gods in counsel, father Jove has sent me to bear you this

[011-183]message—so long as you see Agamemnon heading his men and making

[011-184]havoc of the Trojan ranks, you are to keep aloof and bid the

[011-185]others bear the brunt of the battle, but when Agamemnon is

[011-186]wounded either by spear or arrow, and takes to his chariot, then

[011-187]will Jove vouchsafe you strength to slay till you reach the

[011-188]ships, and till night falls at the going down of the sun.”

[011-189]When she had thus spoken Iris left him, and Hector sprang full

[011-190]armed from his chariot to the ground, brandishing his spear as he

[011-191]went about everywhere among the host, cheering his men on to

[011-192]fight, and stirring the dread strife of battle. The Trojans then

[011-193]wheeled round, and again met the Achaeans, while the Argives on

[011-194]their part strengthened their battalions. The battle was now in

[011-195]array and they stood face to face with one another, Agamemnon

[011-196]ever pressing forward in his eagerness to be ahead of all others.

[011-197]Tell me now ye Muses that dwell in the mansions of Olympus, who,

[011-198]whether of the Trojans or of their allies, was first to face

[011-199]Agamemnon? It was Iphidamas son of Antenor, a man both brave and

[011-200]of great stature, who was brought up in fertile Thrace, the

[011-201]mother of sheep. Cisses, his mother’s father, brought him up in

[011-202]his own house when he was a child—Cisses, father to fair Theano.

[011-203]When he reached manhood, Cisses would have kept him there, and

[011-204]was for giving him his daughter in marriage, but as soon as he

[011-205]had married he set out to fight the Achaeans with twelve ships

[011-206]that followed him: these he had left at Percote and had come on

[011-207]by land to Ilius. He it was that now met Agamemnon son of Atreus.

[011-208]When they were close up with one another, the son of Atreus

[011-209]missed his aim, and Iphidamas hit him on the girdle below the

[011-210]cuirass and then flung himself upon him, trusting to his strength

[011-211]of arm; the girdle, however, was not pierced, nor nearly so, for

[011-212]the point of the spear struck against the silver and was turned

[011-213]aside as though it had been lead: King Agamemnon caught it from

[011-214]his hand, and drew it towards him with the fury of a lion; he

[011-215]then drew his sword, and killed Iphidamas by striking him on the

[011-216]neck. So there the poor fellow lay, sleeping a sleep as it were

[011-217]of bronze, killed in the defence of his fellow-citizens, far from

[011-218]his wedded wife, of whom he had had no joy though he had given

[011-219]much for her: he had given a hundred head of cattle down, and had

[011-220]promised later on to give a thousand sheep and goats mixed, from

[011-221]the countless flocks of which he was possessed. Agamemnon son of

[011-222]Atreus then despoiled him, and carried off his armour into the

[011-223]host of the Achaeans.

[011-224]When noble Coon, Antenor’s eldest son, saw this, sore indeed were

[011-225]his eyes at the sight of his fallen brother. Unseen by Agamemnon

[011-226]he got beside him, spear in hand, and wounded him in the middle

[011-227]of his arm below the elbow, the point of the spear going right

[011-228]through the arm. Agamemnon was convulsed with pain, but still not

[011-229]even for this did he leave off struggling and fighting, but

[011-230]grasped his spear that flew as fleet as the wind, and sprang upon

[011-231]Coon who was trying to drag off the body of his brother—his

[011-232]father’s son—by the foot, and was crying for help to all the

[011-233]bravest of his comrades; but Agamemnon struck him with a

[011-234]bronze-shod spear and killed him as he was dragging the dead body

[011-235]through the press of men under cover of his shield: he then cut

[011-236]off his head, standing over the body of Iphidamas. Thus did the

[011-237]sons of Antenor meet their fate at the hands of the son of

[011-238]Atreus, and go down into the house of Hades.

## Section 30

[011-239]As long as the blood still welled warm from his wound Agamemnon

[011-240]went about attacking the ranks of the enemy with spear and sword

[011-241]and with great handfuls of stone, but when the blood had ceased

[011-242]to flow and the wound grew dry, the pain became great. As the

[011-243]sharp pangs which the Eilithuiae, goddesses of childbirth,

[011-244]daughters of Juno and dispensers of cruel pain, send upon a woman

[011-245]when she is in labour—even so sharp were the pangs of the son of

[011-246]Atreus. He sprang on to his chariot, and bade his charioteer

[011-247]drive to the ships, for he was in great agony. With a loud clear

[011-248]voice he shouted to the Danaans, “My friends, princes and

[011-249]counsellors of the Argives, defend the ships yourselves, for Jove

[011-250]has not suffered me to fight the whole day through against the

[011-251]Trojans.”

[011-252]With this the charioteer turned his horses towards the ships, and

[011-253]they flew forward nothing loth. Their chests were white with foam

[011-254]and their bellies with dust, as they drew the wounded king out of

[011-255]the battle.

[011-256]When Hector saw Agamemnon quit the field, he shouted to the

[011-257]Trojans and Lycians saying, “Trojans, Lycians, and Dardanian

[011-258]warriors, be men, my friends, and acquit yourselves in battle

[011-259]bravely; their best man has left them, and Jove has vouchsafed me

[011-260]a great triumph; charge the foe with your chariots that you may

[011-261]win still greater glory.”

[011-262]With these words he put heart and soul into them all, and as a

[011-263]huntsman hounds his dogs on against a lion or wild boar, even so

[011-264]did Hector, peer of Mars, hound the proud Trojans on against the

[011-265]Achaeans. Full of hope he plunged in among the foremost, and fell

[011-266]on the fight like some fierce tempest that swoops down upon the

[011-267]sea, and lashes its deep blue waters into fury.

[011-268]What, then is the full tale of those whom Hector son of Priam

[011-269]killed in the hour of triumph which Jove then vouchsafed him?

[011-270]First Asaeus, Autonous, and Opites; Dolops son of Clytius,

[011-271]Opheltius and Agelaus; Aesymnus, Orus and Hipponous steadfast in

[011-272]battle; these chieftains of the Achaeans did Hector slay, and

[011-273]then he fell upon the rank and file. As when the west wind

[011-274]hustles the clouds of the white south and beats them down with

[011-275]the fierceness of its fury—the waves of the sea roll high, and

[011-276]the spray is flung aloft in the rage of the wandering wind—even

[011-277]so thick were the heads of them that fell by the hand of Hector.

[011-278]All had then been lost and no help for it, and the Achaeans would

[011-279]have fled pell-mell to their ships, had not Ulysses cried out to

[011-280]Diomed, “Son of Tydeus, what has happened to us that we thus

[011-281]forget our [[aristeia|prowess]]? Come, my good fellow, stand by my side and

[011-282]help me, we shall be shamed for ever if Hector takes the ships.”

[011-283]And Diomed answered, “Come what may, I will stand firm; but we

[011-284]shall have scant joy of it, for Jove is minded to give victory to

[011-285]the Trojans rather than to us.”

[011-286]With these words he struck Thymbraeus from his chariot to the

[011-287]ground, smiting him in the left breast with his spear, while

[011-288]Ulysses killed Molion who was his squire. These they let lie, now

[011-289]that they had stopped their fighting; the two heroes then went on

[011-290]playing havoc with the foe, like two wild boars that turn in fury

[011-291]and rend the hounds that hunt them. Thus did they turn upon the

[011-292]Trojans and slay them, and the Achaeans were thankful to have

[011-293]breathing time in their flight from Hector.

[011-294]They then took two princes with their chariot, the two sons of

[011-295]Merops of Percote, who excelled all others in the arts of

[011-296]divination. He had forbidden his sons to go to the war, but they

[011-297]would not obey him, for fate lured them to their fall. Diomed son

[011-298]of Tydeus slew them both and stripped them of their armour, while

[011-299]Ulysses killed Hippodamus and Hypeirochus.

[011-300]And now the son of Saturn as he looked down from Ida ordained

[011-301]that neither side should have the advantage, and they kept on

[011-302]killing one another. The son of Tydeus speared Agastrophus son of

[011-303]Paeon in the hip-joint with his spear. His chariot was not at

[011-304]hand for him to fly with, so blindly confident had he been. His

[011-305]squire was in charge of it at some distance and he was fighting

[011-306]on foot among the foremost until he lost his life. Hector soon

[011-307]marked the havoc Diomed and Ulysses were making, and bore down

[011-308]upon them with a loud cry, followed by the Trojan ranks; brave

[011-309]Diomed was dismayed when he saw them, and said to Ulysses who was

[011-310]beside him, “Great Hector is bearing down upon us and we shall be

[011-311]undone; let us stand firm and wait his onset.”

[011-312]He poised his spear as he spoke and hurled it, nor did he miss

[011-313]his mark. He had aimed at Hector’s head near the top of his

[011-314]helmet, but bronze was turned by bronze, and Hector was

[011-315]untouched, for the spear was stayed by the visored helm made with

[011-316]three plates of metal, which Phoebus Apollo had given him. Hector

[011-317]sprang back with a great bound under cover of the ranks; he fell

[011-318]on his knees and propped himself with his brawny hand leaning on

[011-319]the ground, for darkness had fallen on his eyes. The son of

[011-320]Tydeus having thrown his spear dashed in among the foremost

[011-321]fighters, to the place where he had seen it strike the ground;

[011-322]meanwhile Hector recovered himself and springing back into his

[011-323]chariot mingled with the crowd, by which means he saved his life.

[011-324]But Diomed made at him with his spear and said, “Dog, you have

[011-325]again got away though death was close on your heels. Phoebus

[011-326]Apollo, to whom I ween you pray ere you go into battle, has again

[011-327]saved you, nevertheless I will meet you and make an end of you

[011-328]hereafter, if there is any god who will stand by me too and be my

[011-329]helper. For the present I must pursue those I can lay hands on.”

[011-330]As he spoke he began stripping the spoils from the son of Paeon,

[011-331]but Alexandrus husband of lovely Helen aimed an arrow at him,

[011-332]leaning against a pillar of the monument which men had raised to

[011-333]Ilus son of Dardanus, a ruler in days of old. Diomed had taken

[011-334]the cuirass from off the breast of Agastrophus, his heavy helmet

[011-335]also, and the shield from off his shoulders, when Paris drew his

[011-336]bow and let fly an arrow that sped not from his hand in vain, but

[011-337]pierced the flat of Diomed’s right foot, going right through it

[011-338]and fixing itself in the ground. Thereon Paris with a hearty

[011-339]laugh sprang forward from his hiding-place, and taunted him

[011-340]saying, “You are wounded—my arrow has not been shot in vain;

[011-341]would that it had hit you in the belly and killed you, for thus

[011-342]the Trojans, who fear you as goats fear a lion, would have had a

[011-343][[oath|truce]] from evil.”

[011-344]Diomed all undaunted answered, “Archer, you who without your bow

[011-345]are nothing, slanderer and seducer, if you were to be tried in

[011-346]single combat fighting in full armour, your bow and your arrows

[011-347]would serve you in little stead. Vain is your boast in that you

[011-348]have scratched the sole of my foot. I care no more than if a girl

[011-349]or some silly boy had hit me. A worthless coward can inflict but

[011-350]a light wound; when I wound a man though I but graze his skin it

[011-351]is another matter, for my weapon will lay him low. His wife will

[011-352]tear her cheeks for grief and his children will be fatherless:

[011-353]there will he rot, reddening the earth with his blood, and
