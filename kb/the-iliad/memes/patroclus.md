# Patroclus

Patroclus is the moral center of the *Iliad* — the one character whose death changes everything. Gentler than Achilles, more compassionate than the other Greek commanders, he is killed wearing Achilles' armor in an attempt to save the Achaean ships. His death transforms Achilles' passive wrath into active vengeance and sets in motion the events that lead to Hector's death and, ultimately, Achilles' own.

## Identity

Son of Menoetius, Patroclus was sent to Peleus's court as a youth after accidentally killing a playmate. There he became Achilles' closest companion — a relationship that Homer describes with an intimacy deeper than comradeship. Throughout the epic, Patroclus serves as Achilles' moral compass, the one person who can reach him when his pride isolates him from the rest of the army.

In the tent, it is Patroclus who attends to the practical rituals of hospitality and friendship. When the embassy arrives in Book IX, "Patroclus did as his dear comrade had bidden him" — preparing the meal, placing the meat on the spits, setting out the bread in a basket (009-178, 009-189). These acts of service, performed without a word of complaint, define Patroclus's character: gentle, loyal, and without the consuming pride that isolates Achilles.

## Role in the Epic

Patroclus's role crystallizes in three movements, each building toward the catastrophe of Book XVI.

**The Embassy (Book IX):** During the embassy scene, Patroclus sits silently while Achilles plays the lyre and sings of "the glories of heroes" — the only scene in the *Iliad* where a character sings (009-165-168). Patroclus waits for Achilles to finish before attending to the practical work of hospitality. This domestic tableau — Achilles singing, Patroclus listening — is the poem's most intimate portrait of their relationship, a moment of peace before the storm.

**Nestor's Intervention (Book XI):** When Achilles sends Patroclus to inquire who has been wounded, Patroclus "did as his dear comrade had bidden him" (011-540) and goes to Nestor's tent. There Nestor delivers a long, manipulative speech — recounting his own youthful exploits, shaming Achilles' inaction, and planting the idea that Patroclus could turn the battle by wearing Achilles' armor. "The words moved the heart of Patroclus, who set off at a run" (011-702) back to Achilles. This is "the beginning of evil for Patroclus" (011-009) — Nestor's persuasion is the proximate cause of Patroclus's death.

**The Aristeia and Death (Book XVI):** Patroclus's role reaches its climax in Book XVI, which opens with him weeping before Achilles. Achilles' question — "Why, Patroclus, do you stand there weeping like some silly child that runs after its mother?" (016-009-011) — is both tender and cruel, and Patroclus's reply is the most eloquent speech of his career: he catalogs the wounded Greek heroes and indicts Achilles' pitilessness: "You are too full of pride, Achilles, and will not yield" (016-022-048).

Achilles relents. He allows Patroclus to lead the Myrmidons into battle wearing his armour, with one condition: drive the Trojans from the ships, but do not pursue them to the walls of Troy. Patroclus dons the divine armour — "first he put on the goodly greaves fitted with ancle-clasps" (016-117) — and the Myrmidons pour out like wolves (016-226-232).

What follows is Patroclus's *aristeia*, his finest and final hour. He kills Pyraechmes, leader of the Paeonians, with a spear thrust (016-256), and the Trojans flee in panic. He kills Areilycus, Pronous, Thestor, Erymas, Amphoterus, Epaltes, Tlepolemus, Echius, Pyris, Ipheus, Euippus, and Polymelus in a cascade of violence. Most notably, he kills Sarpedon, the son of Zeus himself — driving a spear into the Lycian king's chest (016-372-386). Zeus, watching, debates whether to save his son but yields to Hera's counsel and allows him to die; Sarpedon's death is marked by a shower of bloody rain, the grief of heaven made visible (016-399-418).

But in his battle fury, Patroclus pushes too far. He assaults the walls of Troy — three times he climbs them, and three times Apollo hurls him back (016-612). On the fourth attempt, the god strikes him from behind, "striking the helmet from his head" (016-653), disarming and disorienting him. Euphorbus wounds him from behind with a spear, and Hector delivers the killing blow — "with a spear-thrust he struck him in the lower part of the belly" (016-675). As he dies, Patroclus prophesies Hector's own approaching death at Achilles' hands: "You yourself shall die soon after at the hand of Achilles" (016-704).

## Notable Speeches

Patroclus's speeches are fewer and less elaborate than those of Odysseus or Nestor, but they are marked by moral clarity and emotional directness.

> "Then, O knight Patroclus, with a deep sigh you answered, 'Achilles, son of Peleus, noblest of all the Achaeans, be not angry with me for what I shall say. The bravest of our people are lying wounded at the ships. Diomedes, Ulysses, Agamemnon, and Eurypylus have all been hit by arrows or spears. A physician must heal them; but you, Achilles, are implacable. May I never be like you. I would rather help our people than nurse my wrath.'" (016-022-030)

> "He that has fallen is Sarpedon, leader of the Lycians and son to Jove. If you will not save your son, let me at least send Sleep and Death to bear his body home to Lycia, where his people may bury him with mound and pillar, as befits the dead." (from Book XVI)

> As he died, Patroclus spoke: "You vaunt loudly, Hector, for you have slain me with the help of Jove and Apollo — an easy task. Had twenty such as you confronted me, I should have slain them all. But cruel fate and the son of Leto have slain me, and of men it is Euphorbus; you are only my third slayer. One thing more I say to you and bid you lay it to heart: you yourself shall not live long — death and the day of your doom are close upon you, and they shall strike you down at the hand of Achilles." (016-704-711)

## Relationships

**Achilles:** Beloved companion — *philos hetairos* — whose relationship with Patroclus is the deepest emotional bond in the poem. Patroclus is the only person who can challenge Achilles' pride, and his death transforms Achilles' wrath from a private grievance into a cosmic force of destruction. Achilles' grief for Patroclus is described in language of unprecedented intensity: he pours ashes on his head, tears his hair, and lies stretched in the dust, and his cry of grief is so terrible that his mother Thetis hears it from the depths of the sea.

**Nestor:** The aged counselor who plants the fatal idea in Patroclus's mind. Nestor's speech to Patroclus (011-535-702) is a masterpiece of manipulation disguised as reminiscence — he tells his own youthful exploits, shames Achilles' inaction, and suggests that Patroclus could be the army's saviour. His words "moved the heart of Patroclus" (011-702), and the narrator marks this as "the beginning of evil for Patroclus" (011-009).

**Hector:** Patroclus's killer, who boasts over his body — only to hear Patroclus's dying prophecy that Achilles will soon avenge him. Hector takes Achilles' armour from Patroclus's body, an act that seals his own doom.

**Sarpedon:** Zeus's son, the most significant victim of Patroclus's *aristeia*. His death (016-372-418) is one of the most elaborately narrated killings in the poem, marked by Zeus's anguished deliberation and the portent of bloody rain.

**Briseis:** The captive woman over whom Achilles and Agamemnon quarreled. In a poignant detail, Briseis mourns Patroclus's death in Book XIX, recalling how he was always kind to her and promised that Achilles would make her his wedded wife — a rare glimpse of Patroclus's gentleness from a perspective outside the warrior circle.

## Death or Fate

Patroclus's death in Book XVI is the epic's central turning point. Killed by Hector with Apollo's aid, his body is fought over in a day-long battle until Achilles retrieves it. His funeral in Book XXIII — complete with games, sacrifices, and the slaughter of twelve Trojan captives — is the most elaborate ritual in the poem. His shade appears to Achilles in a dream, requesting a proper burial so he may enter the house of Hades: "Bury me with all speed, that I may pass the gates of Hades. The spirits keep me at a distance, the ghosts of the buried dead, and will not let me cross the river to join them" (023-053-056).

The ghost's request is charged with pathos: in death, as in life, Patroclus is separated from the community he belongs to — kept at the threshold of Hades, unable to join the other dead, just as in life he was always at the threshold of Achilles' glory, unable to claim it for himself.

## Scholarly Notes

Butler's translation preserves the emotional intensity of Achilles' grief for Patroclus. The relationship between the two men has been interpreted variously as deep friendship, mentorship, or romantic love — Butler's Victorian translation is characteristically understated. Ancient Greek tradition was divided: Aeschylus, in the lost *Myrmidons*, treated the relationship as explicitly erotic, while Xenophon's Socrates argued for a purely spiritual bond. The *Iliad* itself does not specify, but the language of intimacy — "dear comrade," the sharing of a tent, the intensity of Achilles' grief — is unmistakable.

Patroclus's *aristeia* is structurally a mirror of Achilles' own, demonstrating both his excellence and his fatal limitation: he lacks Achilles' semi-divine immunity to consequence. The sequence in which he dons Achilles' armour, routs the Trojans, and is then struck down by a god is a miniature of the heroic pattern — rise, triumph, and fall — that governs the entire epic. The difference is that Patroclus cannot transcend the pattern; he is mortal all the way through, and his death is final.

The role of Apollo in Patroclus's death is theologically significant. The god strikes him from behind, knocking off his helmet and breaking his spear — a death that is neither heroic nor fair. This divine intervention underscores the *Iliad*'s insistence that human excellence, no matter how great, is always subject to forces beyond human control. Patroclus, who showed compassion to everyone, receives none from the gods.
