# Danaans

The Danaans (Δαναοί) are one of the three primary poetic names used by Homer to refer to the combined Greek forces besieging Troy, alongside "Achaeans" and "Argives." The term appears frequently throughout the *Iliad* as a collective designation for the Greek host under Agamemnon's command.

## Identity

The name "Danaans" derives from Danaus, a mythical king of Argos, and is used by Homer as an ethnically inclusive term for all Greeks regardless of their specific city-state or regional origin (001-194). The term functions synonymously with "Achaeans" in most contexts, though scholars note it carries a slightly more archaic and poetic register. Homer uses all three names — Danaans, Achaeans, and Argives — to refer to the same coalition force, varying the term primarily for metrical convenience within the dactylic hexameter line (002-207).

## Leadership

The Danaans fight under the supreme command of Agamemnon, son of Atreus, who is described as the ruler of the most numerous contingent and "king of men" (002-394). The collective leadership includes the "chiefs and princes of the Danaans" — a council of kings and commanders that includes Achilles (leader of the Myrmidons), Odysseus (leader of the Cephallenians), Diomed (leader of the Argive contingent), Nestor, and Ajax (002-618).

## Role in the War

The Danaans form the invading force that has besieged Troy for nine years prior to the events of the *Iliad*. Throughout the poem, they appear in every major battle sequence:

- **Books 1–2**: The Danaans suffer from Apollo's plague, sent in answer to Chryses' prayer (001-194). Agamemnon tests the army's morale by suggesting retreat, and the Danaans rush to their ships before being stopped by Odysseus (002-207).
- **Books 4–5**: The Danaans march in "serried phalanxes" against the Trojan forces (004-334). Diomed, fighting on behalf of the Danaans, enjoys extraordinary success until Apollo intervenes, sending Aeneas forth and rousing the Trojans (005-431). The Danaans "stood firm" like a storm-cloud against Trojan assaults (005-442).
- **Book 6**: Hector returns to Troy and urges the Trojans to "stand firm here and fight the Danaans however hard they press" (006-071).
- **Book 7**: Athena intervenes to grant "victory in favour of the Danaans" (007-028), and Hector challenges "some one of the Danaans" to single combat, a challenge eventually accepted by Ajax (007-036, 007-077).

The Danaans' fortunes oscillate dramatically — from near-victory to near-defeat — reflecting the ebb and flow of divine intervention and the absence or presence of their greatest warrior, Achilles.

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

The Danaans as a collective are the subject of the entire Catalogue of Ships (Book 2, lines 002-484 through 002-877), which enumerates the twenty-nine contingents that make up the Greek expedition. The Catalogue names each leader, the number of ships they brought, and the cities or regions from which their forces were drawn. As a collective name, "Danaans" encompasses all contingents listed in the Catalogue.
