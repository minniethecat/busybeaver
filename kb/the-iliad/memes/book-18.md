# Book XVIII

[018-001]BOOK XVIII.

[018-002]The grief of [[achilles|Achilles]] over [[patroclus|Patroclus]]—The visit of Thetis to

[018-003]Vulcan and the armour that he made for Achilles.

[018-004]Thus then did they fight as it were a flaming fire. Meanwhile the

[018-005]fleet runner Antilochus, who had been sent as messenger, reached

[018-006]Achilles, and found him sitting by his tall [[achaean-camp|ships]] and boding that

[018-007]which was indeed too surely true. “Alas,” said he to himself in

[018-008]the heaviness of his heart, “why are the Achaeans again scouring

[018-009]the plain and flocking towards the ships? Heaven grant the gods

[018-010]be not now bringing that [[grief|sorrow]] upon me of which my mother Thetis

[018-011]spoke, saying that while I was yet alive the bravest of the

[018-012]Myrmidons should fall before the Trojans, and see the light of

[018-013]the sun no longer. I fear the brave son of Menoetius has fallen

[018-014]through his own daring and yet I bade him return to the ships as

[018-015]soon as he had driven back those that were bringing fire against

[018-016]them, and not join battle with [[hector|Hector]].”

[018-017]As he was thus pondering, the son of [[nestor|Nestor]] came up to him and

[018-018]told his sad tale, weeping bitterly the while. “Alas,” he cried,

[018-019]“son of noble Peleus, I bring you bad tidings, would indeed that

[018-020]they were untrue. Patroclus has fallen, and a fight is raging

[018-021]about his naked body—for Hector holds his armour.”

[018-022]A dark cloud of grief fell upon Achilles as he listened. He

[018-023]filled both hands with dust from off the ground, and poured it

[018-024]over his head, disfiguring his comely face, and letting the

[018-025]refuse settle over his shirt so fair and new. He flung himself

[018-026]down all huge and hugely at full length, and tore his hair with

[018-027]his hands. The bondswomen whom Achilles and Patroclus had taken

[018-028]captive screamed aloud for grief, beating their breasts, and with

[018-029]their limbs failing them for sorrow. Antilochus bent over him the

[018-030]while, weeping and holding both his hands as he lay groaning for

[018-031]he feared that he might plunge a knife into his own throat. Then

[018-032]Achilles gave a loud cry and his mother heard him as she was

[018-033]sitting in the depths of the sea by the old man her father,

[018-034]whereon she screamed, and all the goddesses daughters of Nereus

[018-035]that dwelt at the bottom of the sea, came gathering round her.

[018-036]There were Glauce, Thalia and Cymodoce, Nesaia, Speo, Thoe and

[018-037]dark-eyed Halie, Cymothoe, Actaea and Limnorea, Melite, Iaera,

[018-038]Amphithoe and Agave, Doto and Proto, Pherusa and Dynamene,

[018-039]Dexamene, Amphinome and Callianeira, Doris, Panope, and the

[018-040]famous sea-nymph Galatea, Nemertes, Apseudes and Callianassa.

[018-041]There were also Clymene, Ianeira and Ianassa, Maera, Oreithuia

[018-042]and Amatheia of the lovely locks, with other Nereids who dwell in

[018-043]the depths of the sea. The crystal cave was filled with their

[018-044]multitude and they all beat their breasts while Thetis led them

[018-045]in their lament.

[018-046]“Listen,” she cried, “sisters, daughters of Nereus, that you may

[018-047]hear the burden of my sorrows. Alas, woe is me, woe in that I

[018-048]have borne the most glorious of offspring. I bore him fair and

[018-049]strong, hero among heroes, and he shot up as a sapling; I tended

[018-050]him as a plant in a goodly garden, and sent him with his ships to

[018-051]Ilius to fight the Trojans, but never shall I welcome him back to

[018-052]the house of Peleus. So long as he lives to look upon the light

[018-053]of the sun he is in heaviness, and though I go to him I cannot

[018-054]help him. Nevertheless I will go, that I may see my dear son and

[018-055]learn what sorrow has befallen him though he is still holding

[018-056]aloof from battle.”

[018-057]She left the cave as she spoke, while the others followed weeping

[018-058]after, and the waves opened a path before them. When they reached

[018-059]the rich plain of Troy, they came up out of the sea in a long

[018-060]line on to the sands, at the place where the ships of the

[018-061]Myrmidons were drawn up in close order round the tents of

[018-062]Achilles. His mother went up to him as he lay groaning; she laid

[018-063]her hand upon his head and spoke piteously, saying, “My son, why

[018-064]are you thus weeping? What sorrow has now befallen you? Tell me;

[018-065]hide it not from me. Surely Jove has granted you the prayer you

[018-066]made him, when you lifted up your hands and besought him that the

[018-067]Achaeans might all of them be pent up at their ships, and rue it

[018-068]bitterly in that you were no longer with them.”

## Section 53

[018-069]Achilles groaned and answered, “Mother, Olympian Jove has indeed

[018-070]vouchsafed me the fulfilment of my prayer, but what boots it to

[018-071]me, seeing that my dear comrade Patroclus has fallen—he whom I

[018-072]valued more than all others, and loved as dearly as my own life?

[018-073]I have lost him; aye, and Hector when he had killed him stripped

[018-074]the wondrous armour, so glorious to behold, which the gods gave

[018-075]to Peleus when they laid you in the couch of a mortal man. Would

[018-076]that you were still dwelling among the immortal sea-nymphs, and

[018-077]that Peleus had taken to himself some mortal bride. For now you

[018-078]shall have grief infinite by reason of the death of that son whom

[018-079]you can never welcome home—nay, I will not live nor go about

[018-080]among mankind unless Hector fall by my spear, and thus pay me for

[018-081]having slain Patroclus son of Menoetius.”

[018-082]Thetis wept and answered, “Then, my son, is your end near at

[018-083]hand—for your own death awaits you full soon after that of

[018-084]Hector.”

[018-085]Then said Achilles in his great grief, “I would die here and now,

[018-086]in that I could not save my comrade. He has fallen far from home,

[018-087]and in his hour of need my hand was not there to help him. What

[018-088]is there for me? Return to my own land I shall not, and I have

[018-089]brought no saving neither to Patroclus nor to my other comrades

[018-090]of whom so many have been slain by mighty Hector; I stay here by

[018-091]my ships a bootless burden upon the earth, I, who in fight have

[018-092]no peer among the Achaeans, though in council there are better

[018-093]than I. Therefore, perish strife both from among gods and men,

[018-094]and [[wrath|anger]], wherein even a righteous man will harden his

[018-095]heart—which rises up in the soul of a man like smoke, and the

[018-096]taste thereof is sweeter than drops of honey. Even so has

[018-097][[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] angered me. And yet—so be it, for it is over; I will

[018-098]force my soul into subjection as I needs must; I will go; I will

[018-099]pursue Hector who has slain him whom I loved so dearly, and will

[018-100]then abide my [[fate|doom]] when it may please Jove and the other gods to

[018-101]send it. Even Hercules, the best beloved of Jove—even he could

[018-102]not escape the hand of death, but fate and Juno’s fierce anger

[018-103]laid him low, as I too shall lie when I am dead if a like doom

[018-104]awaits me. Till then I will win [[glory|fame]], and will bid Trojan and

[018-105]Dardanian women wring tears from their tender cheeks with both

[018-106]their hands in the grievousness of their great sorrow; thus shall

[018-107]they know that he who has held aloof so long will hold aloof no

[018-108]longer. Hold me not back, therefore, in the love you bear me, for

[018-109]you shall not move me.”

[018-110]Then silver-footed Thetis answered, “My son, what you have said

[018-111]is true. It is well to save your comrades from destruction, but

[018-112]your armour is in the hands of the Trojans; Hector bears it in

[018-113]triumph upon his own shoulders. Full well I know that his vaunt

[018-114]shall not be lasting, for his end is close at hand; go not,

[018-115]however, into the press of battle till you see me return hither;

[018-116]to-morrow at break of day I shall be here, and will bring you

[018-117]goodly armour from King Vulcan.”

[018-118]On this she left her brave son, and as she turned away she said

[018-119]to the sea-nymphs her sisters, “Dive into the bosom of the sea

[018-120]and go to the house of the old sea-god my father. Tell him

[018-121]everything; as for me, I will go to the cunning workman Vulcan on

[018-122]high [[mount-olympus|Olympus]], and ask him to provide my son with a suit of

[018-123]splendid armour.”

[018-124]When she had so said, they dived forthwith beneath the waves,

[018-125]while silver-footed Thetis went her way that she might bring the

[018-126]armour for her son.

[018-127]Thus, then, did her feet bear the goddess to Olympus, and

[018-128]meanwhile the Achaeans were flying with loud cries before

[018-129]murderous Hector till they reached the ships and the Hellespont,

[018-130]and they could not draw the body of Mars’s servant Patroclus out

[018-131]of reach of the weapons that were showered upon him, for Hector

[018-132]son of [[priam|Priam]] with his [[guest-friendship|host]] and horsemen had again caught up to

[018-133]him like the flame of a fiery furnace; thrice did brave Hector

[018-134]seize him by the feet, striving with might and main to draw him

[018-135]away and calling loudly on the Trojans, and thrice did the two

[018-136]Ajaxes, clothed in valour as with a garment, beat him from off

[018-137]the body; but all undaunted he would now charge into the thick of

[018-138]the fight, and now again he would stand still and cry aloud, but

[018-139]he would give no ground. As upland shepherds that cannot chase

[018-140]some famished lion from a carcase, even so could not the two

[018-141]Ajaxes scare Hector son of Priam from the body of Patroclus.

[018-142]And now he would even have dragged it off and have won

[018-143]imperishable [[honor|glory]], had not Iris fleet as the wind, winged her

[018-144]way as messenger from Olympus to the son of Peleus and bidden him

[018-145]arm. She came secretly without the knowledge of Jove and of the

[018-146]other gods, for Juno sent her, and when she had got close to him

[018-147]she said, “Up, son of Peleus, mightiest of all mankind; rescue

[018-148]Patroclus about whom this fearful fight is now raging by the

[018-149]ships. Men are killing one another, the Danaans in defence of the

[018-150]dead body, while the Trojans are trying to hale it away, and take

[018-151]it to windy Ilius: Hector is the most furious of them all; he is

[018-152]for cutting the head from the body and fixing it on the stakes of

[018-153]the wall. Up, then, and bide here no longer; shrink from the

[018-154]thought that Patroclus may become meat for the dogs of Troy.

[018-155]Shame on you, should his body suffer any kind of outrage.”

[018-156]And Achilles said, “Iris, which of the gods was it that sent you

[018-157]to me?”

[018-158]Iris answered, “It was Juno the royal spouse of Jove, but the son

[018-159]of Saturn does not know of my coming, nor yet does any other of

[018-160]the immortals who dwell on the snowy summits of Olympus.”

[018-161]Then fleet Achilles answered her saying, “How can I go up into

[018-162]the battle? They have my armour. My mother forbade me to arm till

[018-163]I should see her come, for she promised to bring me goodly armour

[018-164]from Vulcan; I know no man whose arms I can put on, save only the

[018-165]shield of Ajax son of Telamon, and he surely must be fighting in

[018-166]the front rank and wielding his spear about the body of dead

[018-167]Patroclus.”

[018-168]Iris said, “We know that your armour has been taken, but go as

[018-169]you are; go to the deep trench and show yourself before the

[018-170]Trojans, that they may fear you and cease fighting. Thus will the

[018-171]fainting sons of the Achaeans gain some brief breathing time,

[018-172]which in battle may hardly be.”

[018-173]Iris left him when she had so spoken. But Achilles dear to Jove

[018-174]arose, and Minerva flung her tasselled aegis round his strong

[018-175]shoulders; she crowned his head with a halo of golden cloud from

[018-176]which she kindled a glow of gleaming fire. As the smoke that goes

[018-177]up into heaven from some city that is being beleaguered on an

[018-178]island far out at sea—all day long do men sally from the city and

[018-179]fight their hardest, and at the going down of the sun the line of

[018-180]beacon-fires blazes forth, flaring high for those that dwell near

[018-181]them to behold, if so be that they may come with their ships and

[018-182]succour them—even so did the light flare from the head of

[018-183]Achilles, as he stood by the trench, going beyond the wall—but he

[018-184]did not join the Achaeans for he heeded the charge which his

[018-185]mother laid upon him.

[018-186]There did he stand and shout aloud. Minerva also raised her voice

[018-187]from afar, and spread terror unspeakable among the Trojans.

[018-188]Ringing as the note of a trumpet that sounds alarm then the foe

[018-189]is at the gates of a city, even so brazen was the voice of the

[018-190]son of Aeacus, and when the Trojans heard its clarion tones they

[018-191]were dismayed; the horses turned back with their chariots for

[018-192]they boded mischief, and their drivers were awe-struck by the

[018-193]steady flame which the grey-eyed goddess had kindled above the

[018-194]head of the great son of Peleus.

[018-195]Thrice did Achilles raise his loud cry as he stood by the trench,

[018-196]and thrice were the Trojans and their brave allies thrown into

[018-197]confusion; whereon twelve of their noblest champions fell beneath

[018-198]the wheels of their chariots and perished by their own spears.

[018-199]The Achaeans to their great joy then drew Patroclus out of reach

[018-200]of the weapons, and laid him on a litter: his comrades stood

[018-201]mourning round him, and among them fleet Achilles who wept

[018-202]bitterly as he saw his true comrade lying dead upon his bier. He

[018-203]had sent him out with horses and chariots into battle, but his

[018-204]return he was not to welcome.

[018-205]Then Juno sent the busy sun, loth though he was, into the waters

[018-206]of Oceanus; so he set, and the Achaeans had rest from the tug and

[018-207]turmoil of war.

[018-208]Now the Trojans when they had come out of the fight, unyoked

[018-209]their horses and gathered in [[counsel|assembly]] before preparing their

[018-210]supper. They kept their feet, nor would any dare to sit down, for

[018-211]fear had fallen upon them all because Achilles had shown himself

[018-212]after having held aloof so long from battle. Polydamas son of

[018-213]Panthous was first to speak, a man of judgement, who alone among

[018-214]them could look both before and after. He was comrade to Hector,

[018-215]and they had been born upon the same night; with all sincerity

[018-216]and goodwill, therefore, he addressed them thus:—

[018-217]“Look to it well, my friends; I would urge you to go back now to

[018-218]your city and not wait here by the ships till morning, for we are

[018-219]far from our walls. So long as this man was at enmity with

[018-220]Agamemnon the Achaeans were easier to deal with, and I would have

[018-221]gladly camped by the ships in the hope of taking them; but now I

[018-222]go in great fear of the fleet son of Peleus; he is so daring that

[018-223]he will never bide here on the plain whereon the Trojans and

[018-224]Achaeans fight with equal valour, but he will try to storm our

[018-225]city and carry off our women. Do then as I say, and let us

[018-226]retreat. For this is what will happen. The darkness of night will

[018-227]for a time stay the son of Peleus, but if he find us here in the

[018-228]morning when he sallies forth in full armour, we shall have

[018-229]knowledge of him in good earnest. Glad indeed will he be who can

[018-230]escape and get back to Ilius, and many a Trojan will become meat

[018-231]for dogs and vultures may I never live to hear it. If we do as I

[018-232]say, little though we may like it, we shall have strength in

[018-233]counsel during the night, and the great gates with the doors that

[018-234]close them will protect the city. At dawn we can arm and take our

[018-235]stand on the walls; he will then rue it if he sallies from the

[018-236]ships to fight us. He will go back when he has given his horses

[018-237]their fill of being driven all whithers under our walls, and will

[018-238]be in no mind to try and force his way into the city. Neither

[018-239]will he ever sack it, dogs shall devour him ere he do so.”

[018-240]Hector looked fiercely at him and answered, “Polydamas, your

[018-241]words are not to my liking in that you bid us go back and be pent

[018-242]within the city. Have you not had enough of being cooped up

[018-243]behind walls? In the old-days the city of Priam was famous the

[018-244]whole world over for its wealth of gold and bronze, but our

[018-245]treasures are wasted out of our houses, and much goods have been

[018-246]sold away to Phrygia and fair Meonia, for the hand of Jove has

[018-247]been laid heavily upon us. Now, therefore, that the son of

[018-248]scheming Saturn has vouchsafed me to win glory here and to hem

[018-249]the Achaeans in at their ships, prate no more in this fool’s wise

[018-250]among the people. You will have no man with you; it shall not be;

[018-251]do all of you as I now say;—take your suppers in your companies

[018-252]throughout the host, and keep your watches and be wakeful every

[018-253]man of you. If any Trojan is uneasy about his possessions, let

[018-254]him gather them and give them out among the people. Better let

[018-255]these, rather than the Achaeans, have them. At daybreak we will

[018-256]arm and fight about the ships; granted that Achilles has again

[018-257]come forward to defend them, let it be as he will, but it shall

[018-258]go hard with him. I shall not shun him, but will fight him, to

[018-259]fall or conquer. The god of war deals out like measure to all,

[018-260]and the slayer may yet be slain.”

## Section 54

[018-261]Thus spoke Hector; and the Trojans, fools that they were, shouted

[018-262]in applause, for Pallas Minerva had robbed them of their

[018-263]understanding. They gave ear to Hector with his evil counsel, but

[018-264]the wise words of Polydamas no man would heed. They took their

[018-265]supper throughout the host, and meanwhile through the whole night

[018-266]the Achaeans mourned Patroclus, and the son of Peleus led them in

[018-267]their lament. He laid his murderous hands upon the breast of his

[018-268]comrade, groaning again and again as a bearded lion when a man

[018-269]who was chasing deer has robbed him of his young in some dense

[018-270]forest; when the lion comes back he is furious, and searches

[018-271]dingle and dell to track the hunter if he can find him, for he is

[018-272]mad with rage—even so with many a sigh did Achilles speak among

[018-273]the Myrmidons saying, “Alas! vain were the words with which I

[018-274]cheered the hero Menoetius in his own house; I said that I would

[018-275]bring his brave son back again to Opoeis after he had sacked

[018-276]Ilius and taken his share of the spoils—but Jove does not give

[018-277]all men their heart’s desire. The same soil shall be reddened

[018-278]here at Troy by the blood of us both, for I too shall never be

[018-279]welcomed home by the old knight Peleus, nor by my mother Thetis,

[018-280]but even in this place shall the earth cover me. Nevertheless, O

[018-281]Patroclus, now that I am left behind you, I will not bury you,

[018-282]till I have brought hither the head and armour of mighty Hector

[018-283]who has slain you. Twelve noble sons of Trojans will I behead

[018-284]before your bier to avenge you; till I have done so you shall lie

[018-285]as you are by the ships, and fair women of Troy and Dardanus,

[018-286]whom we have taken with spear and strength of arm when we sacked

[018-287]men’s goodly cities, shall weep over you both night and day.”

[018-288]Then Achilles told his men to set a large tripod upon the fire

[018-289]that they might wash the clotted gore from off Patroclus. Thereon

[018-290]they set a tripod full of bath water on to a clear fire: they

[018-291]threw sticks on to it to make it blaze, and the water became hot

[018-292]as the flame played about the belly of the tripod. When the water

[018-293]in the cauldron was boiling they washed the body, anointed it

[018-294]with oil, and closed its wounds with ointment that had been kept

[018-295]nine years. Then they laid it on a bier and covered it with a

[018-296]linen cloth from head to foot, and over this they laid a fair

[018-297]white robe. Thus all night long did the Myrmidons gather round

[018-298]Achilles to mourn Patroclus.

[018-299]Then Jove said to Juno his sister-wife, “So, Queen Juno, you have

[018-300]gained your end, and have roused fleet Achilles. One would think

[018-301]that the Achaeans were of your own flesh and blood.”

[018-302]And Juno answered, “Dread son of Saturn, why should you say this

[018-303]thing? May not a man though he be only mortal and knows less than

[018-304]we do, do what he can for another person? And shall not

[018-305]I—foremost of all goddesses both by descent and as wife to you

[018-306]who reign in heaven—devise evil for the Trojans if I am angry

[018-307]with them?”

[018-308]Thus did they converse. Meanwhile Thetis came to the house of

[018-309]Vulcan, imperishable, star-bespangled, fairest of the abodes in

[018-310]heaven, a house of bronze wrought by the lame god’s own hands.

[018-311]She found him busy with his bellows, sweating and hard at work,

[018-312]for he was making twenty tripods that were to stand by the wall

[018-313]of his house, and he set wheels of gold under them all that they

[018-314]might go of their own selves to the assemblies of the gods, and

[018-315]come back again—marvels indeed to see. They were finished all but

[018-316]the ears of cunning workmanship which yet remained to be fixed to

[018-317]them: these he was now fixing, and he was hammering at the

[018-318]rivets. While he was thus at work silver-footed Thetis came to

[018-319]the house. Charis, of graceful head-dress, wife to the far-famed

[018-320]lame god, came towards her as soon as she saw her, and took her

[018-321]hand in her own, saying, “Why have you come to our house, Thetis,

[018-322]honoured and ever welcome—for you do not visit us often? Come

[018-323]inside and let me set refreshment before you.”

[018-324]The goddess led the way as she spoke, and bade Thetis sit on a

[018-325]richly decorated seat inlaid with silver; there was a footstool

[018-326]also under her feet. Then she called Vulcan and said, “Vulcan,

[018-327]come here, Thetis wants you”; and the far-famed lame god

[018-328]answered, “Then it is indeed an august and honoured goddess who

[018-329]has come here; she it was that took care of me when I was

[018-330]suffering from the heavy fall which I had through my cruel

[018-331]mother’s anger—for she would have got rid of me because I was

[018-332]lame. It would have gone hardly with me had not Eurynome,

[018-333]daughter of the ever-encircling waters of Oceanus, and Thetis,

[018-334]taken me to their bosom. Nine years did I stay with them, and

[018-335]many beautiful works in bronze, brooches, spiral armlets, cups,

[018-336]and chains, did I make for them in their cave, with the roaring

[018-337]waters of Oceanus foaming as they rushed ever past it; and no one

[018-338]knew, neither of gods nor men, save only Thetis and Eurynome who

[018-339]took care of me. If, then, Thetis has come to my house I must

[018-340]make her due requital for having saved me; entertain her,

[018-341]therefore, with all hospitality, while I put by my bellows and

[018-342]all my tools.”

[018-343]On this the mighty monster hobbled off from his anvil, his thin

[018-344]legs plying lustily under him. He set the bellows away from the

[018-345]fire, and gathered his tools into a silver chest. Then he took a

[018-346]sponge and washed his face and hands, his shaggy chest and brawny

[018-347]neck; he donned his shirt, grasped his strong staff, and limped

[018-348]towards the door. There were golden handmaids also who worked for

[018-349]him, and were like real young women, with sense and reason, voice

[018-350]also and strength, and all the learning of the immortals; these

[018-351]busied themselves as the king bade them, while he drew near to

[018-352]Thetis, seated her upon a goodly seat, and took her hand in his

[018-353]own, saying, “Why have you come to our house, Thetis honoured and

[018-354]ever welcome—for you do not visit us often? Say what you want,

[018-355]and I will do it for you at once if I can, and if it can be done

[018-356]at all.”

[018-357]Thetis wept and answered, “Vulcan, is there another goddess in
