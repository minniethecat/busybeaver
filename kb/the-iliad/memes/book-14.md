# Book XIV

[014-001]BOOK XIV.

[014-002][[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] proposes that the Achaeans should sail home, and is

[014-003]rebuked by [[odysseus|Ulysses]]—Juno beguiles Jupiter—[[hector|Hector]] is wounded.

[014-004][[nestor|Nestor]] was sitting over his wine, but the cry of battle did not

[014-005]escape him, and he said to the son of Aesculapius, “What, noble

[014-006]Machaon, is the meaning of all this? The shouts of men fighting

[014-007]by our [[achaean-camp|ships]] grow stronger and stronger; stay here, therefore,

[014-008]and sit over your wine, while fair Hecamede heats you a bath and

[014-009]washes the clotted blood from off you. I will go at once to the

[014-010]look-out station and see what it is all about.”

[014-011]As he spoke he took up the shield of his son Thrasymedes that was

[014-012]lying in his tent, all gleaming with bronze, for Thrasymedes had

[014-013]taken his father’s shield; he grasped his redoubtable bronze-shod

[014-014]spear, and as soon as he was outside saw the disastrous rout of

[014-015]the Achaeans who, now that their wall was overthrown, were flying

[014-016]pell-mell before the Trojans. As when there is a heavy swell upon

[014-017]the sea, but the waves are dumb—they keep their eyes on the watch

[014-018]for the quarter whence the fierce winds may spring upon them, but

[014-019]they stay where they are and set neither this way nor that, till

[014-020]some particular wind sweeps down from heaven to determine

[014-021]them—even so did the old man ponder whether to make for the crowd

[014-022]of Danaans, or go in search of Agamemnon. In the end he deemed it

[014-023]best to go to the son of Atreus; but meanwhile the hosts were

[014-024]fighting and killing one another, and the hard bronze rattled on

[014-025]their bodies, as they thrust at one another with their swords and

[014-026]spears.

[014-027]The wounded kings, the son of Tydeus, Ulysses, and Agamemnon son

[014-028]of Atreus, fell in with Nestor as they were coming up from their

[014-029]ships—for theirs were drawn up some way from where the fighting

[014-030]was going on, being on the shore itself inasmuch as they had been

[014-031]beached first, while the wall had been built behind the

[014-032]hindermost. The stretch of the shore, wide though it was, did not

[014-033]afford room for all the ships, and the [[guest-friendship|host]] was cramped for

[014-034]space, therefore they had placed the ships in rows one behind the

[014-035]other, and had filled the whole opening of the bay between the

[014-036]two points that formed it. The kings, leaning on their spears,

[014-037]were coming out to survey the fight, being in great anxiety, and

[014-038]when old Nestor met them they were filled with dismay. Then King

[014-039]Agamemnon said to him, “Nestor son of Neleus, [[honor|honour]] to the

[014-040]Achaean name, why have you left the battle to come hither? I fear

[014-041]that what dread Hector said will come true, when he vaunted among

[014-042]the Trojans saying that he would not return to Ilius till he had

[014-043]fired our ships and killed us; this is what he said, and now it

[014-044]is all coming true. Alas! others of the Achaeans, like [[achilles|Achilles]],

[014-045]are in such [[wrath|anger]] with me that they refuse to fight by the sterns

[014-046]of our ships.”

[014-047]Then Nestor knight of Gerene, answered, “It is indeed as you say;

[014-048]it is all coming true at this moment, and even Jove who thunders

[014-049]from on high cannot prevent it. Fallen is the wall on which we

[014-050]relied as an impregnable bulwark both for us and our fleet. The

[014-051]Trojans are fighting stubbornly and without ceasing at the ships;

[014-052]look where you may you cannot see from what quarter the rout of

[014-053]the Achaeans is coming; they are being killed in a confused mass

[014-054]and the battle-cry ascends to heaven; let us think, if counsel

[014-055]can be of any use, what we had better do; but I do not advise our

[014-056]going into battle ourselves, for a man cannot fight when he is

[014-057]wounded.”

[014-058]And King Agamemnon answered, “Nestor, if the Trojans are indeed

[014-059]fighting at the rear of our ships, and neither the wall nor the

[014-060]trench has served us—over which the Danaans toiled so hard, and

[014-061]which they deemed would be an impregnable bulwark both for us and

[014-062]our fleet—I see it must be the will of Jove that the Achaeans

[014-063]should perish ingloriously here, far from Argos. I knew when Jove

[014-064]was willing to defend us, and I know now that he is raising the

[014-065]Trojans to like honour with the gods, while us, on the other

[014-066]hand, he has bound hand and foot. Now, therefore, let us all do

[014-067]as I say; let us bring down the ships that are on the beach and

[014-068]draw them into the water; let us make them fast to their

[014-069]mooring-stones a little way out, against the fall of night—if

[014-070]even by night the Trojans will desist from fighting; we may then

[014-071]draw down the rest of the fleet. There is nothing wrong in flying

[014-072]ruin even by night. It is better for a man that he should fly and

[014-073]be saved than be caught and killed.”

[014-074]Ulysses looked fiercely at him and said, “Son of Atreus, what are

[014-075]you talking about? Wretch, you should have commanded some other

[014-076]and baser army, and not been ruler over us to whom Jove has

[014-077]allotted a life of hard fighting from youth to old age, till we

[014-078]every one of us perish. Is it thus that you would quit the city

[014-079]of Troy, to win which we have suffered so much hardship? Hold

[014-080]your peace, lest some other of the Achaeans hear you say what no

[014-081]man who knows how to give good counsel, no king over so great a

[014-082]host as that of the Argives should ever have let fall from his

[014-083]lips. I despise your judgement utterly for what you have been

[014-084]saying. Would you, then, have us draw down our ships into the

[014-085]water while the battle is raging, and thus play further into the

[014-086]hands of the conquering Trojans? It would be ruin; the Achaeans

[014-087]will not go on fighting when they see the ships being drawn into

[014-088]the water, but will cease attacking and keep turning their eyes

[014-089]towards them; your counsel, therefore, sir captain, would be our

[014-090]destruction.”

[014-091]Agamemnon answered, “Ulysses, your rebuke has stung me to the

[014-092]heart. I am not, however, ordering the Achaeans to draw their

[014-093]ships into the sea whether they will or no. Someone, it may be,

[014-094]old or young, can offer us better counsel which I shall rejoice

[014-095]to hear.”

[014-096]Then said Diomed, “Such an one is at hand; he is not far to seek,

[014-097]if you will listen to me and not resent my speaking though I am

[014-098]younger than any of you. I am by lineage son to a noble sire,

[014-099]Tydeus, who lies buried at Thebes. For Portheus had three noble

[014-100]sons, two of whom, Agrius and Melas, abode in Pleuron and rocky

[014-101]Calydon. The third was the knight Oeneus, my father’s father, and

[014-102]he was the most valiant of them all. Oeneus remained in his own

[014-103]country, but my father (as Jove and the other gods ordained it)

[014-104]migrated to Argos. He married into the family of Adrastus, and

[014-105]his house was one of great abundance, for he had large estates of

[014-106]rich corn-growing land, with much orchard ground as well, and he

[014-107]had many sheep; moreover he excelled all the Argives in the use

[014-108]of the spear. You must yourselves have heard whether these things

[014-109]are true or no; therefore when I say well despise not my words as

[014-110]though I were a coward or of ignoble birth. I say, then, let us

[014-111]go to the fight as we needs must, wounded though we be. When

[014-112]there, we may keep out of the battle and beyond the range of the

[014-113]spears lest we get fresh wounds in addition to what we have

[014-114]already, but we can spur on others, who have been indulging their

[014-115]spleen and holding aloof from battle hitherto.”

[014-116]Thus did he speak; whereon they did even as he had said and set

[014-117]out, King Agamemnon leading the way.

[014-118]Meanwhile Neptune had kept no blind look-out, and came up to them

[014-119]in the semblance of an old man. He took Agamemnon’s right hand in

[014-120]his own and said, “Son of Atreus, I take it Achilles is glad now

[014-121]that he sees the Achaeans routed and slain, for he is utterly

[014-122]without remorse—may he come to a bad end and heaven confound him.

[014-123]As for yourself, the blessed gods are not yet so bitterly angry

[014-124]with you but that the princes and counsellors of the Trojans

[014-125]shall again raise the dust upon the plain, and you shall see them

[014-126]flying from the ships and tents towards their city.”

[014-127]With this he raised a mighty cry of battle, and sped forward to

[014-128]the plain. The voice that came from his deep chest was as that of

[014-129]nine or ten thousand men when they are shouting in the thick of a

[014-130]fight, and it put fresh courage into the hearts of the Achaeans

[014-131]to wage war and do battle without ceasing.

[014-132]Juno of the golden throne looked down as she stood upon a peak of

[014-133][[mount-olympus|Olympus]] and her heart was gladdened at the sight of him who was

[014-134]at once her brother and her brother-in-law, hurrying hither and

[014-135]thither amid the fighting. Then she turned her eyes to Jove as he

[014-136]sat on the topmost crests of many-fountained [[mount-ida|Ida]], and loathed

[014-137]him. She set herself to think how she might hoodwink him, and in

[014-138]the end she deemed that it would be best for her to go to Ida and

[014-139]array herself in rich attire, in the hope that Jove might become

[014-140]enamoured of her, and wish to embrace her. While he was thus

[014-141]engaged a sweet and careless sleep might be made to steal over

[014-142]his eyes and senses.

[014-143]She went, therefore, to the room which her son Vulcan had made

[014-164]refuse me because you are angry at my being on the Danaan side,

[014-165]while you are on the Trojan?”

[014-166]Jove’s daughter Venus answered, “Juno, august queen of goddesses,

[014-167]daughter of mighty Saturn, say what you want, and I will do it

[014-168]for you at once, if I can, and if it can be done at all.”

[014-171]all things mortal and immortal to your feet. I am going to the

[014-172]world’s end to visit Oceanus (from whom all we gods proceed) and

[014-181]Thereon laughter-loving Venus said, “I cannot and must not refuse

[014-183]As she spoke she loosed from her bosom the curiously embroidered

[014-190]When she heard this Juno smiled, and still smiling she laid the

[014-207]Then Sleep answered, “Juno, great queen of goddesses, daughter of

[014-208]mighty Saturn, I would lull any other of the gods to sleep

## Section 41

[014-383]When the Argives saw Hector leaving the field, they took heart

[014-384]and set upon the Trojans yet more furiously. Ajax fleet son of

[014-385]Oileus began by springing on Satnius son of Enops, and wounding

[014-386]him with his spear: a fair naiad nymph had borne him to Enops as

[014-387]he was herding cattle by the banks of the river Satnioeis. The

[014-388]son of Oileus came up to him and struck him in the flank so that

[014-389]he fell, and a fierce fight between Trojans and Danaans raged

[014-390]round his body. Polydamas son of Panthous drew near to avenge

[014-391]him, and wounded Prothoenor son of Areilycus on the right

[014-392]shoulder; the terrible spear went right through his shoulder, and

[014-393]he clutched the earth as he fell in the dust. Polydamas vaunted

[014-394]loudly over him saying, “Again I take it that the spear has not

[014-395]sped in vain from the strong hand of the son of Panthous; an

[014-396]Argive has caught it in his body, and it will serve him for a

[014-397]staff as he goes down into the house of Hades.”

[014-398]The Argives were maddened by this boasting. Ajax son of Telamon

[014-399]was more angry than any, for the man had fallen close beside him;

[014-400]so he aimed at Polydamas as he was retreating, but Polydamas

[014-401]saved himself by swerving aside and the spear struck Archelochus

[014-402]son of Antenor, for heaven counselled his destruction; it struck

[014-403]him where the head springs from the neck at the top joint of the

[014-404]spine, and severed both the tendons at the back of the head. His

[014-405]head, mouth, and nostrils reached the ground long before his legs

[014-406]and knees could do so, and Ajax shouted to Polydamas saying,

[014-407]“Think, Polydamas, and tell me truly whether this man is not as

[014-408]well worth killing as Prothoenor was: he seems rich, and of rich

[014-409]family, a brother, it may be, or son of the knight Antenor, for

[014-410]he is very like him.”

[014-411]But he knew well who it was, and the Trojans were greatly

[014-412]angered. Acamas then bestrode his brother’s body and wounded

[014-413]Promachus the Boeotian with his spear, for he was trying to drag

[014-414]his brother’s body away. Acamas vaunted loudly over him saying,

[014-415]“Argive archers, braggarts that you are, toil and suffering shall

[014-416]not be for us only, but some of you too shall fall here as well

[014-417]as ourselves. See how Promachus now sleeps, vanquished by my

[014-418]spear; payment for my brother’s blood has not been long delayed;

[014-419]a man, therefore, may well be thankful if he leaves a kinsman in

[014-420]his house behind him to avenge his fall.”

[014-421]His taunts infuriated the Argives, and Peneleos was more enraged

[014-422]than any of them. He sprang towards Acamas, but Acamas did not

[014-423]stand his ground, and he killed Ilioneus son of the rich

[014-424]flock-master Phorbas, whom Mercury had favoured and endowed with

[014-425]greater wealth than any other of the Trojans. Ilioneus was his

[014-426]only son, and Peneleos now wounded him in the eye under his

[014-427]eyebrows, tearing the eye-ball from its socket: the spear went

[014-428]right through the eye into the nape of the neck, and he fell,

[014-429]stretching out both hands before him. Peneleos then drew his

[014-430]sword and smote him on the neck, so that both head and helmet

[014-431]came tumbling down to the ground with the spear still sticking in

[014-432]the eye; he then held up the head, as though it had been a

[014-433]poppy-head, and showed it to the Trojans, vaunting over them as

[014-434]he did so. “Trojans,” he cried, “bid the father and mother of

[014-435]noble Ilioneus make moan for him in their house, for the wife

[014-436]also of Promachus son of Alegenor will never be gladdened by the

[014-437]coming of her dear husband—when we Argives return with our ships

[014-438]from Troy.”

[014-439]As he spoke fear fell upon them, and every man looked round about

[014-440]to see whither he might fly for safety.
