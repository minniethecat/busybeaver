# Pelasgians

The Pelasgians (Πελασγοί) are a contingent of spearmen in the Trojan alliance, led by Hippothous, son of Lethus. They are described as dwelling in "deep-soiled Larissa" and fight on the Trojan side throughout the *Iliad*, with their leader Hippothous meeting his death in Book 17 while attempting to drag away Patroclus's body (017-233).

## Identity

The Pelasgians are introduced in the Trojan Catalogue as a distinct fighting force: "Hippothous led the tribes of Pelasgian spearsmen, who dwelt in deep-soiled Larissa" (002-686). The epithet "Pelasgian" carries an ancient resonance — the Pelasgians were traditionally regarded as the prehistoric inhabitants of Greece, but here they appear as a specific contingent from Thessalian Larissa. Their mention alongside "Pelasgic Argos" in the Achaean section of the Catalogue (002-551) suggests a split population, with some Pelasgian communities allied to the Achaeans and others to the Trojans.

They are grouped with the "noble Pelasgi" alongside Carians, Leleges, Cauconians, Lycians, and Mysians in the enumeration of Trojan allies (010-354), marking them as part of the broader Anatolian and peripheral-Greek coalition defending Troy.

## Leadership

Hippothous, "brave son of the Pelasgian Lethus, son of Teutamus" (017-233), is the sole named commander of the Pelasgian contingent. His genealogy traces through Lethus to Teutamus, establishing a three-generation heroic lineage. Hippothous is the most prominent Pelasgian warrior in the narrative: he is killed in Book 17 by Ajax while attempting to drag Patroclus's body away from the Achaeans, an act that underscores both his courage and the fierce struggle over the fallen companion of Achilles (017-234).

## Role in the War

The Pelasgians participate in several key episodes:

- **Book 2**: Listed in the Trojan Catalogue under Hippothous, with their contingent described as "tribes of Pelasgian spearsmen" dwelling in Larissa (002-686).
- **Book 10**: Enumerated among the Trojan allies — "the noble Pelasgi" appear alongside Carians, Paeonian bowmen, Leleges, Cauconians, Lycians, and Mysians in the full roll-call of allied forces (010-354).
- **Book 16**: Achilles, in his prayer to Zeus, refers to "Pelasgi, who dwellest afar," associating the Pelasgians with the far-distant regions from which the Trojan allies come (016-210).
- **Book 17**: Hippothous is killed while attempting to seize Patroclus's body, marking the Pelasgians' most narratively significant moment. "Hippothous, brave son of the Pelasgian Lethus," falls in the desperate fighting around the corpse (017-233).

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

In the Trojan Catalogue, the Pelasgians are described as "tribes of Pelasgian spearsmen, who dwelt in deep-soiled Larissa" led by "Hippothous and Pylaeus of the stock of Mars, sons of the Pelasgian Lethus, son of Teutamus" (002-686). The description of their homeland as "deep-soiled" (ἐριβώλακι) is a standard Homeric formula for fertile territory, applied also to Larissa in the Achaean Catalogue.

## Notable Members

- Hippothous — son of Lethus, leader of the Pelasgians, killed in Book 17
- Pylaeus — son of Lethus, co-commander mentioned in the Catalogue
