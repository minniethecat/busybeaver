# Lycians

The Lycians (Λύκιοι) are a contingent of Trojan allies from Lycia, a distant land in south-western Anatolia "by the eddying waters of the Xanthus." Led by Sarpedon, a son of Zeus, and Glaucus, son of Hippolochus, they are among the most distinguished and formidable of the allied forces fighting alongside Troy (002-719).

## Identity

The Lycians hail from a fertile and honoured territory — Glaucus recounts how "the Lycians gave him a piece of land, the fairest in all Lycia, with orchards and wheatfields" (006-165). Their distant origin underscores the breadth of Priam's alliance network. Despite fighting far from home for a cause not their own, the Lycians are consistently portrayed as fierce and loyal warriors. Sarpedon's famous speech to Glaucus articulates the noblesse oblige that drives Lycian aristocratic warriors: their privileged status at home compels them to fight in the forefront of battle (012-274).

## Leadership

Sarpedon, son of Zeus and Laodamia, is the "captain of the Lycians" (005-541). He is the most prominent Trojan ally in the poem — a warrior of near-equal standing to Hector, whose death at the hands of Patroclus in Book 16 is one of the *Iliad's* most consequential events. Glaucus, son of Hippolochus, serves as Sarpedon's second-in-command and closest companion; their meeting with Diomed in Book 6, where they discover ancestral guest-friendship and exchange armour, is one of the poem's most celebrated episodes of chivalric honour (006-165).

## Role in the War

The Lycians appear prominently from the Catalogue of Ships through the battle for the Achaean wall:

- **Book 2**: "Sarpedon and Glaucus led the Lycians from their distant land, by the eddying waters of the Xanthus" (002-719).
- **Book 5**: Sarpedon confronts Tlepolemus in single combat. Though wounded, he rallies the Lycians, rebuking them: "Lycians, why should you come skulking here? I mean to stand firm" (005-531). Sarpedon kills Tlepolemus but is gravely wounded and carried from the field.
- **Book 6**: Glaucus meets Diomed between the armies; they exchange genealogies, discover their grandfathers' guest-friendship, and trade armour — Glaucus's gold for Diomed's bronze — in a gesture that transcends the battle lines (006-165).
- **Book 12**: The Lycians play the leading role in the assault on the Achaean wall. Sarpedon, "standing at the head of all the Lycians," spearheads the attack (012-274). When the Lycians falter, Sarpedon rebukes them: "Lycians, why do you thus fail me? For hard as the struggle is, I will surely break through" (012-353). "The Lycians, shamed by his rebuke, pressed on with redoubled fury" (012-357). The Achaeans cannot "drive the Lycians from the wall now that they had gained a footing" (012-362).

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

The Lycian contingent is listed in the Trojan Catalogue (Book 2): "Sarpedon and peerless Glaucus led the Lycians from distant Lycia, from beside the eddying Xanthus" (002-719). No ship count is given for the allied contingents, unlike the Achaean Catalogue.
