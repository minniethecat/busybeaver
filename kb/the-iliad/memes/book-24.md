# Book XXIV

[024-001]BOOK XXIV.

[024-002][[priam|Priam]] ransoms the body of [[hector|Hector]]—Hector’s funeral.

[024-003]The [[counsel|assembly]] now broke up and the people went their ways each to

[024-004]his own ship. There they made ready their supper, and then

[024-005]bethought them of the blessed boon of sleep; but [[achilles|Achilles]] still

[024-006]wept for thinking of his dear comrade, and sleep, before whom all

[024-007]things bow, could take no hold upon him. This way and that did he

[024-008]turn as he yearned after the might and manfulness of [[patroclus|Patroclus]];

[024-009]he thought of all they had done together, and all they had gone

[024-010]through both on the field of battle and on the waves of the weary

[024-011]sea. As he dwelt on these things he wept bitterly and lay now on

[024-012]his side, now on his back, and now face downwards, till at last

[024-013]he rose and went out as one distraught to wander upon the

[024-014]sea-[[achaean-camp|shore]]. Then, when he saw dawn breaking over beach and sea, he

[024-015]yoked his horses to his chariot, and bound the body of Hector

[024-016]behind it that he might drag it about. Thrice did he drag it

[024-017]round the tomb of the son of Menoetius, and then went back into

[024-018]his tent, leaving the body on the ground full length and with its

[024-019]face downwards. But Apollo would not suffer it to be disfigured,

[024-020]for he pitied the man, dead though he now was; therefore he

[024-021]shielded him with his golden aegis continually, that he might

[024-022]take no hurt while Achilles was dragging him.

[024-023]Thus shamefully did Achilles in his [[wrath|fury]] [[honor|dishonour]] Hector; but

[024-024]the blessed gods looked down in pity from heaven, and urged

[024-025]Mercury, slayer of Argus, to steal the body. All were of this

[024-026]mind save only Juno, Neptune, and Jove’s grey-eyed daughter, who

[024-027]persisted in the hate which they had ever borne towards Ilius

[024-028]with Priam and his people; for they forgave not the wrong done

[024-029]them by [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] in disdaining the goddesses who came to him

[024-030]when he was in his sheepyards, and preferring her who had offered

[024-031]him a wanton to his ruin.

[024-032]When, therefore, the morning of the twelfth day had now come,

[024-033]Phoebus Apollo spoke among the immortals saying, “You gods ought

[024-034]to be ashamed of yourselves; you are cruel and hard-hearted. Did

[024-035]not Hector burn you thigh-bones of heifers and of unblemished

[024-036]goats? And now dare you not rescue even his dead body, for his

[024-037]wife to look upon, with his mother and child, his father Priam,

[024-038]and his people, who would forthwith commit him to the flames, and

[024-039]give him his due funeral rites? So, then, you would all be on the

[024-040]side of mad Achilles, who knows neither right nor ruth? He is

[024-041]like some savage lion that in the pride of his great strength and

[024-042]daring springs upon men’s flocks and gorges on them. Even so has

[024-043]Achilles flung aside all pity, and all that conscience which at

[024-044]once so greatly banes yet greatly boons him that will heed it. A

[024-045]man may lose one far dearer than Achilles has lost—a son, it may

[024-046]be, or a brother born from his own mother’s womb; yet when he has

[024-047]mourned him and wept over him he will let him bide, for it takes

[024-048]much [[grief|sorrow]] to kill a man; whereas Achilles, now that he has

[024-049]slain noble Hector, drags him behind his chariot round the tomb

[024-050]of his comrade. It were better of him, and for him, that he

[024-051]should not do so, for brave though he be we gods may take it ill

[024-052]that he should vent his fury upon dead clay.”

[024-053]Juno spoke up in a rage. “This were well,” she cried, “O lord of

[024-054]the silver bow, if you would give like honour to Hector and to

[024-055]Achilles; but Hector was mortal and suckled at a woman’s breast,

[024-056]whereas Achilles is the offspring of a goddess whom I myself

[024-057]reared and brought up. I married her to Peleus, who is above

[024-058]measure dear to the immortals; you gods came all of you to her

[024-059]wedding; you feasted along with them yourself and brought your

[024-060]lyre—false, and fond of low company, that you have ever been.”

[024-061]Then said Jove, “Juno, be not so bitter. Their honour shall not

[024-062]be equal, but of all that dwell in Ilius, Hector was dearest to

[024-063]the gods, as also to myself, for his offerings never failed me.

[024-064]Never was my [[sacrifice|altar]] stinted of its dues, nor of the

[024-065]drink-offerings and savour of sacrifice which we claim of right.

[024-066]I shall therefore permit the body of mighty Hector to be stolen;

[024-067]and yet this may hardly be without Achilles coming to know it,

[024-068]for his mother keeps night and day beside him. Let some one of

[024-069]you, therefore, send Thetis to me, and I will impart my counsel

[024-070]to her, namely that Achilles is to accept a ransom from Priam,

[024-071]and give up the body.”

[024-072]On this Iris fleet as the wind went forth to carry his message.

[024-073]Down she plunged into the dark sea midway between Samos and rocky

[024-074]Imbrus; the waters hissed as they closed over her, and she sank

[024-075]into the bottom as the lead at the end of an ox-horn, that is

[024-076]sped to carry death to fishes. She found Thetis sitting in a

[024-077]great cave with the other sea-goddesses gathered round her; there

[024-078]she sat in the midst of them weeping for her noble son who was to

[024-079]fall far from his own land, on the rich plains of Troy. Iris went

[024-080]up to her and said, “Rise Thetis; Jove, whose counsels fail not,

[024-081]bids you come to him.” And Thetis answered, “Why does the mighty

[024-082]god so bid me? I am in great grief, and shrink from going in and

[024-083]out among the immortals. Still, I will go, and the word that he

[024-084]may speak shall not be spoken in vain.”

[024-085]The goddess took her dark veil, than which there can be no robe

[024-086]more sombre, and went forth with fleet Iris leading the way

[024-087]before her. The waves of the sea opened them a path, and when

[024-088]they reached the shore they flew up into the heavens, where they

[024-089]found the all-seeing son of Saturn with the blessed gods that

[024-090]live for ever assembled near him. Minerva gave up her seat to

[024-091]her, and she sat down by the side of father Jove. Juno then

[024-092]placed a fair golden cup in her hand, and spoke to her in words

[024-093]of comfort, whereon Thetis drank and gave her back the cup; and

[024-094]the sire of gods and men was the first to speak.

[024-095]“So, goddess,” said he, “for all your sorrow, and the grief that

[024-096]I well know reigns ever in your heart, you have come hither to

[024-097][[mount-olympus|Olympus]], and I will tell you why I have sent for you. This nine

[024-098]days past the immortals have been quarrelling about Achilles

[024-099]waster of cities and the body of Hector. The gods would have

[024-100]Mercury slayer of Argus steal the body, but in furtherance of our

[024-101]peace and amity henceforward, I will concede such honour to your

[024-102]son as I will now tell you. Go, then, to the [[guest-friendship|host]] and lay these

[024-103]commands upon him; say that the gods are angry with him, and that

[024-104]I am myself more angry than them all, in that he keeps Hector at

[024-105]the ships and will not give him up. He may thus fear me and let

[024-106]the body go. At the same time I will send Iris to great Priam to

[024-107]bid him go to the ships of the Achaeans, and ransom his son,

[024-108]taking with him such gifts for Achilles as may give him

[024-109]satisfaction.”

[024-110]Silver-footed Thetis did as the god had told her, and forthwith

[024-111]down she darted from the topmost summits of Olympus. She went to

[024-112]her son’s tents where she found him grieving bitterly, while his

[024-113]trusty comrades round him were busy preparing their morning meal,

[024-114]for which they had killed a great woolly sheep. His mother sat

[024-115]down beside him and caressed him with her hand saying, “My son,

[024-116]how long will you keep on thus grieving and making moan? You are

[024-117]gnawing at your own heart, and think neither of food nor of

[024-118]woman’s embraces; and yet these too were well, for you have no

[024-119]long time to live, and death with the strong hand of fate are

[024-120]already close beside you. Now, therefore, heed what I say, for I

[024-121]come as a messenger from Jove; he says that the gods are angry

[024-122]with you, and himself more angry than them all, in that you keep

[024-123]Hector at the ships and will not give him up. Therefore let him

[024-124]go, and accept a ransom for his body.”

[024-125]And Achilles answered, “So be it. If Olympian Jove of his own

[024-126]motion thus commands me, let him that brings the ransom bear the

[024-127]body away.”

[024-128]Thus did mother and son talk together at the ships in long

[024-129]discourse with one another. Meanwhile the son of Saturn sent Iris

[024-130]to the strong city of Ilius. “Go,” said he, “fleet Iris, from the

[024-131]mansions of Olympus, and tell King Priam in Ilius, that he is to

[024-132]go to the ships of the Achaeans and free the body of his dear

[024-133]son. He is to take such gifts with him as shall give satisfaction

[024-134]to Achilles, and he is to go alone, with no other Trojan, save

[024-135]only some honoured servant who may drive his mules and waggon,

[024-136]and bring back the body of him whom noble Achilles has slain. Let

[024-137]him have no thought nor fear of death in his heart, for we will

[024-138]send the slayer of Argus to escort him, and bring him within the

[024-139]tent of Achilles. Achilles will not kill him nor let another do

[024-140]so, for he will take heed to his ways and sin not, and he will

[024-141]entreat a [[supplication|suppliant]] with all honourable courtesy.”

[024-142]On this Iris, fleet as the wind, sped forth to deliver her

[024-143]message. She went to Priam’s house, and found weeping and

[024-144]lamentation therein. His sons were seated round their father in

[024-145]the outer courtyard, and their raiment was wet with tears: the

[024-146]old man sat in the midst of them with his mantle wrapped close

[024-147]about his body, and his head and neck all covered with the filth

[024-148]which he had clutched as he lay grovelling in the mire. His

[024-149]daughters and his sons’ wives went wailing about the house, as

[024-150]they thought of the many and brave men who lay dead, slain by the

[024-151]Argives. The messenger of Jove stood by Priam and spoke softly to

[024-152]him, but fear fell upon him as she did so. “Take heart,” she

[024-153]said, “Priam offspring of Dardanus, take heart and fear not. I

[024-154]bring no evil tidings, but am minded well towards you. I come as

[024-155]a messenger from Jove, who though he be not near, takes thought

[024-156]for you and pities you. The lord of Olympus bids you go and

[024-157]ransom noble Hector, and take with you such gifts as shall give

[024-158]satisfaction to Achilles. You are to go alone, with no Trojan,

[024-159]save only some honoured servant who may drive your mules and

[024-160]waggon, and bring back to the city the body of him whom noble

[024-161]Achilles has slain. You are to have no thought, nor fear of

[024-162]death, for Jove will send the slayer of Argus to escort you. When

[024-163]he has brought you within Achilles’ tent, Achilles will not kill

[024-164]you nor let another do so, for he will take heed to his ways and

[024-165]sin not, and he will entreat a suppliant with all honourable

[024-166]courtesy.”

[024-167]Iris went her way when she had thus spoken, and Priam told his

[024-168]sons to get a mule-waggon ready, and to make the body of the

[024-169]waggon fast upon the top of its bed. Then he went down into his

[024-170]fragrant store-room, high-vaulted, and made of cedar-wood, where

[024-171]his many treasures were kept, and he called Hecuba his wife.

[024-172]“Wife,” said he, “a messenger has come to me from Olympus, and

[024-173]has told me to go to the ships of the Achaeans to ransom my dear

[024-174]son, taking with me such gifts as shall give satisfaction to

[024-175]Achilles. What think you of this matter? for my own part I am

[024-176]greatly moved to pass through the camps of the Achaeans and go to

[024-177]their ships.”

[024-178]His wife cried aloud as she heard him, and said, “Alas, what has

[024-179]become of that judgement for which you have been ever famous both

[024-180]among strangers and your own people? How can you venture alone to

[024-181]the ships of the Achaeans, and look into the face of him who has

[024-182]slain so many of your brave sons? You must have iron courage, for

[024-183]if the cruel savage sees you and lays hold on you, he will know

[024-184]neither [[shame|respect]] nor pity. Let us then weep Hector from afar here

[024-185]in our own house, for when I gave him birth the threads of

[024-186]overruling fate were spun for him that dogs should eat his flesh

[024-187]far from his parents, in the house of that terrible man on whose

[024-188]liver I would fain fasten and devour it. Thus would I avenge my

[024-189]son, who showed no cowardice when Achilles slew him, and thought

[024-190]neither of flight nor of avoiding battle as he stood in defence

[024-191]of Trojan men and Trojan women.”

[024-192]Then Priam said, “I would go, do not therefore stay me nor be as

[024-193]a bird of ill [[prophecy|omen]] in my house, for you will not move me. Had it

[024-194]been some mortal man who had sent me some prophet or priest who

[024-195]divines from sacrifice—I should have deemed him false and have

[024-196]given him no heed; but now I have heard the goddess and seen her

[024-197]face to face, therefore I will go and her saying shall not be in

[024-198]vain. If it be my fate to die at the ships of the Achaeans even

[024-199]so would I have it; let Achilles slay me, if I may but first have

[024-200]taken my son in my arms and mourned him to my heart’s

[024-201]comforting.”

[024-202]So saying he lifted the lids of his chests, and took out twelve

[024-203]goodly vestments. He took also twelve cloaks of single fold,

[024-204]twelve rugs, twelve fair mantles, and an equal number of shirts.

[024-205]He weighed out ten talents of gold, and brought moreover two

[024-206]burnished tripods, four cauldrons, and a very beautiful cup which

[024-207]the Thracians had given him when he had gone to them on an

[024-208]embassy; it was very precious, but he grudged not even this, so

[024-209]eager was he to ransom the body of his son. Then he chased all

[024-210]the Trojans from the court and rebuked them with words of anger.

[024-211]“Out,” he cried, “shame and disgrace to me that you are. Have you

[024-212]no grief in your own homes that you are come to plague me here?

[024-213]Is it a small thing, think you, that the son of Saturn has sent

[024-214]this sorrow upon me, to lose the bravest of my sons? Nay, you

[024-215]shall prove it in person, for now he is gone the Achaeans will

[024-216]have easier work in killing you. As for me, let me go down within

[024-217]the house of Hades, ere mine eyes behold the sacking and wasting

[024-218]of the city.”

[024-219]He drove the men away with his staff, and they went forth as the

[024-220]old man sped them. Then he called to his sons, upbraiding

[024-221]Helenus, Paris, noble Agathon, Pammon, Antiphonus, Polites of the

[024-222]loud battle-cry, Deiphobus, Hippothous, and Dius. These nine did

[024-223]the old man call near him. “Come to me at once,” he cried,

[024-224]“worthless sons who do me shame; would that you had all been

[024-225]killed at the ships rather than Hector. Miserable man that I am,

[024-226]I have had the bravest sons in all Troy—noble [[nestor|Nestor]], Troilus the

[024-227]dauntless charioteer, and Hector who was a god among men, so that

[024-228]one would have thought he was son to an immortal—yet there is not

[024-229]one of them left. Mars has slain them and those of whom I am

[024-230]ashamed are alone left me. Liars, and light of foot, heroes of

[024-231]the dance, robbers of lambs and kids from your own people, why do

[024-232]you not get a waggon ready for me at once, and put all these

[024-233]things upon it that I may set out on my way?”

[024-234]Thus did he speak, and they feared the rebuke of their father.

[024-235]They brought out a strong mule-waggon, newly made, and set the

[024-236]body of the waggon fast on its bed. They took the mule-yoke from

[024-237]the peg on which it hung, a yoke of boxwood with a knob on the

[024-238]top of it and rings for the reins to go through. Then they

[024-239]brought a yoke-band eleven cubits long, to bind the yoke to the

[024-240]pole; they bound it on at the far end of the pole, and put the

[024-241]ring over the upright pin making it fast with three turns of the

[024-242]band on either side the knob, and bending the thong of the yoke

[024-243]beneath it. This done, they brought from the store-chamber the

[024-244]rich ransom that was to purchase the body of Hector, and they set

[024-245]it all orderly on the waggon; then they yoked the strong

[024-246]harness-mules which the Mysians had on a time given as a goodly

[024-247]present to Priam; but for Priam himself they yoked horses which

[024-248]the old king had bred, and kept for his own use.

[024-249]Thus heedfully did Priam and his servant see to the yolking of

[024-250]their cars at the palace. Then Hecuba came to them all sorrowful,

[024-251]with a golden goblet of wine in her right hand, that they might

[024-252]make a drink-offering before they set out. She stood in front of

[024-253]the horses and said, “Take this, make a drink-offering to father

[024-254]Jove, and since you are minded to go to the ships in spite of me,

[024-255]pray that you may come safely back from the hands of your

[024-256]enemies. Pray to the son of Saturn lord of the whirlwind, who

[024-257]sits on [[mount-ida|Ida]] and looks down over all Troy, pray him to send his

[024-258]swift messenger on your right hand, the bird of omen which is

[024-259]strongest and most dear to him of all birds, that you may see it

[024-260]with your own eyes and trust it as you go forth to the ships of

[024-261]the Danaans. If all-seeing Jove will not send you this messenger,

[024-262]however set upon it you may be, I would not have you go to the

[024-263]ships of the Argives.”

[024-264]And Priam answered, “Wife, I will do as you desire me; it is well

[024-265]to lift hands in prayer to Jove, if so be he may have mercy upon

[024-266]me.”

[024-267]With this the old man bade the serving-woman pour pure water over

[024-268]his hands, and the woman came, bearing the water in a bowl. He

[024-269]washed his hands and took the cup from his wife; then he made the

[024-270]drink-offering and prayed, standing in the middle of the

[024-271]courtyard and turning his eyes to heaven. “Father Jove,” he said,

[024-272]“that rulest from Ida, most glorious and most great, grant that I

[024-273]may be received kindly and compassionately in the tents of

[024-274]Achilles; and send your swift messenger upon my right hand, the

[024-275]bird of omen which is strongest and most dear to you of all

[024-276]birds, that I may see it with my own eyes and trust it as I go

[024-277]forth to the ships of the Danaans.”

[024-278]So did he pray, and Jove the lord of counsel heard his prayer.

[024-279]Forthwith he sent an eagle, the most unerring portent of all

[024-302]Thus he spoke and Mercury, guide and guardian, slayer of Argus,

[024-309]beauty with the down just coming upon his face.

[024-310]Now when Priam and Idaeus had driven past the great tomb of

[024-311]Ilius, they stayed their mules and horses that they might drink

[024-312]in the river, for the shades of night were falling, when,

[024-314]Priam, “Take heed, descendant of Dardanus; here is matter which

[024-315]demands consideration. I see a man who I think will presently

[024-316]fall upon us; let us fly with our horses, or at least embrace his

[024-317]knees and implore him to take compassion upon us?”

[024-320]stood on end over his whole body; but the bringer of good luck

[024-321]came up to him and took him by the hand, saying, “Whither,

[024-322]father, are you thus driving your mules and horses in the dead of

[024-323]night when other men are asleep? Are you not afraid of the fierce

[024-324]Achaeans who are hard by you, so cruel and relentless? Should

[024-325]some one of them see you bearing so much treasure through the

[024-342]And Priam said, “Who are you, my friend, and who are your
