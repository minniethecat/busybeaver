# Cauldron

The cauldron (λέβης) appears throughout the *Iliad* not as a single named artifact but as a recurring object of material value — a large metal vessel used for boiling water, heating baths, and, most importantly, serving as a unit of exchange in the epic's gift economy. In [[achilles|Achilles]]' reconciliation with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in Book 9, cauldrons are proffered alongside tripods, horses, and gold as measures of [[honor|honour]] restored. In Book 23, they are brought from the [[achaean-camp|ships]] as prizes for the funeral games of [[patroclus|Patroclus]]. The cauldron is an artifact of the domestic sphere pressed into heroic service: it feeds men, heats their baths, and, when offered as a gift or prize, measures their standing among their peers.

## Description

Homer mentions cauldrons by material and feature, never lingering on a single example but evoking a class of objects:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Material | Iron or bronze; "twenty iron cauldrons" (009-109) suggests iron was standard for larger vessels |
| Form | Deep, broad-mouthed; sometimes three-legged (023-235) or with ear-handles (023-443) |
| Decoration | One prize cauldron is "engraved with flowers" (023-235), marking it as finer than ordinary cookware |
| Size | Large enough to boil water for a bath (018-293); heavy enough to be carried by a team of men |
| Value | A single goodly cauldron could be worth an ox or more in the gift economy |

The three-legged cauldron with floral engraving is singled out in Book 23 as a prize of particular beauty — "a three-legged cauldron that had never yet been on the fire" (023-235), its newness and ornament marking it as a prestige object rather than utilitarian kitchenware.

## Origin and Craftsmanship

Cauldrons are the work of mortal smiths, not divine craftsmen. They belong to the world of the *demos* — the products of workshops in the cities of Achaean Greece and the wider Aegean world. Their value lies not in supernatural provenance but in the labour and material invested in their making.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Maker | Unknown smiths; possibly from workshops in Crete, Cyprus, or the Achaean mainland |
| New vs. used | "Never yet been on the fire" is a mark of exceptional value — an unused cauldron is a pristine gift |
| Floral engraving | The decorated cauldron of Book 23 represents the high end of bronze-age metalwork |

The cauldron's value is partly aesthetic (the engraving) and partly functional (its capacity and material). An iron cauldron represents a [[prophecy|sign]]ificant investment of both ore and smithing time.

## Role in the Epic

Cauldrons appear at key m[[prophecy|omen]]ts in the *Iliad*'s exchanges of [[honor|honour]] and wealth:

- **Book 9 — [[the-embassy-to-achilles|The Embassy to Achilles]]** (009-109, 009-225): [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s offer of reconciliation to [[achilles|Achilles]] includes "twenty iron cauldrons and twelve strong horses" among the material gifts. The cauldrons here function as a quantifiable unit of [[honor|honour]] — the more cauldrons, the greater the gesture of reparation.

- **Book 18 — [[patroclus|Patroclus]]' Body Prepared** (018-293): A cauldron is set on the fire to heat water for washing [[patroclus|Patroclus]]' corpse. Here the cauldron transitions from prize-object to instrument of [[sacrifice|ritual]], participating in the funeral preparations that will culminate in the games.

- **Book 23 — The Funeral Games** (023-231–023-443): [[achilles|Achilles]] brings prizes from the [[achaean-camp|ships]] — "cauldrons, tripods, horses, mules, strong oxen, and fair-girdled w[[prophecy|omen]]" (023-231). Specific cauldrons are awarded across the contests: a goodly cauldron for the wrestlers, an ear-handled cauldron for the foot-race. The prizes convert athletic [[aristeia|excellence]] into material wealth, closing the circuit of [[honor|honour]] that began with [[patroclus|Patroclus]]' death.

- **Book 21 — The Simile of Battle** (021-315): The [[scamander|River Scamander]], boiling with corpses and blood, is compared to a cauldron bubbling over a fire. The domestic object becomes a figure for the violence that has overflowed all boundaries.

## Symbolism

The cauldron occupies a distinctive position in the *Iliad*'s symbolic landscape:

- **Convertible Value**: The cauldron is never used as a weapon, never worn as armour, never wielded in battle. Its entire meaning is economic and social — it measures, transfers, and stores [[honor|honour]] in material form. It is the *Iliad*'s closest equivalent to currency.

- **The Domestic in the Heroic**: Cauldrons come from kitchens and bath-houses. Their presence at the funeral games reminds us that the heroic world is sustained by a domestic economy — smiths, workshops, trade routes — that the poem mostly keeps out of sight.

- **Newness and Honour**: The "cauldron that had never yet been on the fire" is the gift-economy equivalent of a medal struck for the occasion. Its virginity guarantees that this is not a hand-me-down but an object made specifically to be given.

## Notable Passages

The prize-list from Book 23 captures the cauldron's place in the epic's economy of [[honor|honour]]:

> "Achilles brought prizes from the ships — cauldrons, tripods, horses, mules, strong oxen, and fair-girdled women, and grey iron." (023-231)

And the prized cauldron:

> "For the winner, a goodly cauldron that had never yet been on the fire, worth an ox, engraved with flowers." (023-235/023-239)

The detail of the floral engraving is rare in Homer — a m[[prophecy|omen]]t when the poet notices not just the value but the beauty of a crafted object.
