# Book XIII

[013-001]BOOK XIII.

[013-002]Neptune helps the Achaeans—The feats of Idomeneus—[[hector|Hector]] at the

[013-003][[achaean-camp|ships]].

[013-004]Now when Jove had thus brought Hector and the Trojans to the

[013-005]ships, he left them to their never-ending toil, and turned his

[013-006]keen eyes away, looking elsewhither towards the horse-breeders of

[013-007]Thrace, the Mysians, fighters at close quarters, the noble

[013-008]Hippemolgi, who live on milk, and the Abians, justest of mankind.

[013-009]He no longer turned so much as a glance towards Troy, for he did

[013-010]not think that any of the immortals would go and help either

[013-011]Trojans or Danaans.

[013-012]But King Neptune had kept no blind look-out; he had been looking

[013-013]admiringly on the battle from his seat on the topmost crests of

[013-014]wooded Samothrace, whence he could see all [[mount-ida|Ida]], with the city of

[013-015][[priam|Priam]] and the ships of the Achaeans. He had come from under the

[013-016]sea and taken his place here, for he pitied the Achaeans who were

[013-017]being overcome by the Trojans; and he was furiously angry with

[013-018]Jove.

[013-019]Presently he came down from his post on the mountain top, and as

[013-020]he strode swiftly onwards the high hills and the forest quaked

[013-021]beneath the tread of his immortal feet. Three strides he took,

[013-022]and with the fourth he reached his goal—Aegae, where is his

[013-023]glittering golden palace, imperishable, in the depths of the sea.

[013-024]When he got there, he yoked his fleet brazen-footed steeds with

[013-025]their manes of gold all flying in the wind; he clothed himself in

[013-026]raiment of gold, grasped his gold whip, and took his stand upon

[013-027]his chariot. As he went his way over the waves the sea-monsters

[013-028]left their lairs, for they knew their lord, and came gambolling

[013-029]round him from every quarter of the deep, while the sea in her

[013-030]gladness opened a path before his chariot. So lightly did the

[013-031]horses fly that the bronze axle of the car was not even wet

[013-032]beneath it; and thus his bounding steeds took him to the ships of

[013-033]the Achaeans.

## Section 35

[013-034]Now there is a certain huge cavern in the depths of the sea

[013-035]midway between Tenedos and rocky Imbrus; here Neptune lord of the

[013-036]earthquake stayed his horses, unyoked them, and set before them

[013-037]their ambrosial forage. He hobbled their feet with hobbles of

[013-038]gold which none could either unloose or break, so that they might

[013-039]stay there in that place until their lord should return. This

[013-040]done he went his way to the [[guest-friendship|host]] of the Achaeans.

[013-041]Now the Trojans followed Hector son of Priam in close array like

[013-042]a storm-cloud or flame of fire, fighting with might and main and

[013-043]raising the cry battle; for they deemed that they should take the

[013-044]ships of the Achaeans and kill all their chiefest heroes then and

[013-045]there. Meanwhile earth-encircling Neptune lord of the earthquake

[013-046]cheered on the Argives, for he had come up out of the sea and had

[013-047]assumed the form and voice of Calchas.

[013-048]First he spoke to the two Ajaxes, who were doing their best

[013-049]already, and said, “Ajaxes, you two can be the saving of the

[013-050]Achaeans if you will put out all your strength and not let

[013-051]yourselves be daunted. I am not afraid that the Trojans, who have

[013-052]got over the wall in force, will be victorious in any other part,

[013-053]for the Achaeans can hold all of them in check, but I much fear

[013-054]that some evil will befall us here where furious Hector, who

[013-055]boasts himself the son of great Jove himself, is leading them on

[013-056]like a pillar of flame. May some god, then, put it into your

[013-057]hearts to make a firm stand here, and to incite others to do the

[013-058]like. In this case you will drive him from the ships even though

[013-059]he be inspired by Jove himself.”

[013-060]As he spoke the earth-encircling lord of the earthquake struck

[013-061]both of them with his sceptre and filled their hearts with

[013-062]daring. He made their legs light and active, as also their hands

[013-063]and their feet. Then, as the soaring falcon poises on the wing

[013-064]high above some sheer rock, and presently swoops down to chase

[013-065]some bird over the plain, even so did Neptune lord of the

[013-066]earthquake wing his flight into the air and leave them. Of the

[013-067]two, swift Ajax son of Oileus was the first to know who it was

[013-068]that had been speaking with them, and said to Ajax son of

[013-069]Telamon, “Ajax, this is one of the gods that dwell on [[mount-olympus|Olympus]],

[013-070]who in the likeness of the prophet is bidding us fight hard by

[013-071]our ships. It was not Calchas the [[prophecy|seer]] and diviner of omens; I

[013-072]knew him at once by his feet and knees as he turned away, for the

[013-073]gods are soon recognised. Moreover I feel the lust of battle burn

[013-074]more fiercely within me, while my hands and my feet under me are

[013-075]more eager for the fray.”

[013-076]And Ajax son of Telamon answered, “I too feel my hands grasp my

[013-077]spear more firmly; my strength is greater, and my feet more

[013-078]nimble; I long, moreover, to meet furious Hector son of Priam,

[013-079]even in single combat.”

[013-080]Thus did they converse, exulting in the hunger after battle with

[013-081]which the god had filled them. Meanwhile the earth-encircler

[013-082]roused the Achaeans, who were resting in the rear by the ships

[013-083]overcome at once by hard fighting and by grief at seeing that the

[013-084]Trojans had got over the wall in force. Tears began falling from

[013-085]their eyes as they beheld them, for they made sure that they

[013-086]should not escape destruction; but the lord of the earthquake

[013-087]passed lightly about among them and urged their battalions to the

[013-088]front.

[013-089]First he went up to Teucer and Leitus, the hero Peneleos, and

[013-090]Thoas and Deipyrus; Meriones also and Antilochus, valiant

[013-091]warriors; all did he exhort. “Shame on you young Argives,” he

[013-092]cried, “it was on your [[aristeia|prowess]] I relied for the saving of our

[013-093]ships; if you fight not with might and main, this very day will

[013-094]see us overcome by the Trojans. Of a truth my eyes behold a great

[013-095]and terrible portent which I had never thought to see—the Trojans

[013-096]at our ships—they, who were heretofore like panic-stricken hinds,

[013-097]the prey of jackals and wolves in a forest, with no strength but

[013-098]in flight for they cannot defend themselves. Hitherto the Trojans

[013-099]dared not for one moment face the attack of the Achaeans, but now

[013-100]they have sallied far from their city and are fighting at our

[013-101]very ships through the cowardice of our leader and the

[013-102]disaffection of the people themselves, who in their discontent

[013-103]care not to fight in defence of the ships but are being

[013-104]slaughtered near them. True, King [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] son of Atreus is the

[013-105]cause of our disaster by having insulted the son of Peleus, still

[013-106]this is no reason why we should leave off fighting. Let us be

[013-107]quick to heal, for the hearts of the brave heal quickly. You do

[013-108]ill to be thus remiss, you, who are the finest soldiers in our

[013-109]whole army. I blame no man for keeping out of battle if he is a

[013-110]weakling, but I am indignant with such men as you are. My good

[013-111]friends, matters will soon become even worse through this

[013-112]slackness; think, each one of you, of his own [[honor|honour]] and credit,

[013-113]for the hazard of the fight is extreme. Great Hector is now

[013-114]fighting at our ships; he has broken through the gates and the

[013-115]strong bolt that held them.”

[013-116]Thus did the earth-encircler address the Achaeans and urge them

[013-117]on. Thereon round the two Ajaxes there gathered strong bands of

[013-118]men, of whom not even Mars nor Minerva, marshaller of hosts could

[013-119]make light if they went among them, for they were the picked men

[013-120]of all those who were now awaiting the onset of Hector and the

[013-121]Trojans. They made a living fence, spear to spear, shield to

[013-122]shield, buckler to buckler, helmet to helmet, and man to man. The

[013-123]horse-hair crests on their gleaming helmets touched one another

[013-124]as they nodded forward, so closely serried were they; the spears

[013-125]they brandished in their strong hands were interlaced, and their

[013-126]hearts were set on battle.

[013-127]The Trojans advanced in a dense body, with Hector at their head

[013-128]pressing right on as a rock that comes thundering down the side

[013-129]of some mountain from whose brow the winter torrents have torn

[013-130]it; the foundations of the dull thing have been loosened by

[013-131]floods of rain, and as it bounds headlong on its way it sets the

[013-132]whole forest in an uproar; it swerves neither to right nor left

[013-133]till it reaches level ground, but then for all its [[wrath|fury]] it can go

[013-134]no further—even so easily did Hector for a while seem as though

[013-135]he would career through the tents and ships of the Achaeans till

[013-136]he had reached the sea in his murderous course; but the closely

[013-137]serried battalions stayed him when he reached them, for the sons

[013-138]of the Achaeans thrust at him with swords and spears pointed at

[013-139]both ends, and drove him from them so that he staggered and gave

[013-140]ground; thereon he shouted to the Trojans, “Trojans, Lycians, and

[013-141]Dardanians, fighters in close combat, stand firm: the Achaeans

[013-142]have set themselves as a wall against me, but they will not check

[013-143]me for long; they will give ground before me if the mightiest of

[013-144]the gods, the thundering spouse of Juno, has indeed inspired my

[013-145]onset.”

[013-146]With these words he put heart and soul into them all. Deiphobus

[013-147]son of Priam went about among them intent on deeds of daring with

[013-148]his round shield before him, under cover of which he strode

[013-149]quickly forward. Meriones took aim at him with a spear, nor did

[013-150]he fail to hit the broad orb of ox-hide; but he was far from

[013-151]piercing it for the spear broke in two pieces long ere he could

[013-152]do so; moreover Deiphobus had seen it coming and had held his

[013-153]shield well away from him. Meriones drew back under cover of his

[013-154]comrades, angry alike at having failed to vanquish Deiphobus, and

[013-155]having broken his spear. He turned therefore towards the ships

[013-156]and tents to fetch a spear which he had left behind in his tent.

[013-157]The others continued fighting, and the cry of battle rose up into

[013-158]the heavens. Teucer son of Telamon was the first to kill his man,

[013-159]to wit, the warrior Imbrius, son of Mentor, rich in horses. Until

[013-160]the Achaeans came he had lived in Pedaeum, and had married

[013-161]Medesicaste, a bastard daughter of Priam; but on the arrival of

[013-162]the Danaan fleet he had gone back to Ilius, and was a great man

[013-163]among the Trojans, dwelling near Priam himself, who gave him like

[013-164]honour with his own sons. The son of Telamon now struck him under

[013-165]the ear with a spear which he then drew back again, and Imbrius

[013-166]fell headlong as an ash-tree when it is felled on the crest of

[013-167]some high mountain beacon, and its delicate green foliage comes

[013-168]toppling down to the ground. Thus did he fall with his

[013-169]bronze-dight armour ringing harshly round him, and Teucer sprang

[013-170]forward with intent to strip him of his armour; but as he was

[013-171]doing so, Hector took aim at him with a spear. Teucer saw the

[013-172]spear coming and swerved aside, whereon it hit Amphimachus, son

[013-173]of Cteatus son of Actor, in the chest as he was coming into

[013-174]battle, and his armour rang rattling round him as he fell heavily

[013-175]to the ground. Hector sprang forward to take Amphimachus’s helmet

[013-176]from off his temples, and in a moment Ajax threw a spear at him,

[013-177]but did not wound him, for he was encased all over in his

[013-178]terrible armour; nevertheless the spear struck the boss of his

[013-179]shield with such force as to drive him back from the two corpses,

[013-180]which the Achaeans then drew off. Stichius and Menestheus,

[013-181]captains of the Athenians, bore away Amphimachus to the host of

[013-182]the Achaeans, while the two brave and impetuous Ajaxes did the

[013-183]like by Imbrius. As two lions snatch a goat from the hounds that

[013-184]have it in their fangs, and bear it through thick brushwood high

[013-185]above the ground in their jaws, thus did the Ajaxes bear aloft

[013-186]the body of Imbrius, and strip it of its armour. Then the son of

[013-187]Oileus severed the head from the neck in revenge for the death of

[013-188]Amphimachus, and sent it whirling over the crowd as though it had

[013-189]been a ball, till it fell in the dust at Hector’s feet.

[013-190]Neptune was exceedingly angry that his grandson Amphimachus

[013-191]should have fallen; he therefore went to the tents and ships of

[013-192]the Achaeans to urge the Danaans still further, and to devise

[013-193]evil for the Trojans. Idomeneus met him, as he was taking leave

[013-194]of a comrade, who had just come to him from the fight, wounded in

[013-195]the knee. His fellow-soldiers bore him off the field, and

[013-196]Idomeneus having given orders to the physicians went on to his

[013-197]tent, for he was still thirsting for battle. Neptune spoke in the

[013-198]likeness and with the voice of Thoas son of Andraemon who ruled

[013-199]the Aetolians of all Pleuron and high Calydon, and was honoured

[013-200]among his people as though he were a god. “Idomeneus,” said he,

[013-201]“lawgiver to the Cretans, what has now become of the threats with

[013-202]which the sons of the Achaeans used to threaten the Trojans?”

[013-203]And Idomeneus chief among the Cretans answered, “Thoas, no one,

[013-204]so far as I know, is in fault, for we can all fight. None are

[013-205]held back neither by fear nor slackness, but it seems to be the

[013-206]will of almighty Jove that the Achaeans should perish

[013-207]ingloriously here far from Argos: you, Thoas, have been always

[013-208]staunch, and you keep others in heart if you see any fail in

[013-209]duty; be not then remiss now, but exhort all to do their utmost.”

## Section 36

[013-210]To this Neptune lord of the earthquake made answer, “Idomeneus,

[013-211]may he never return from Troy, but remain here for dogs to batten

[013-212]upon, who is this day wilfully slack in fighting. Get your armour

[013-213]and go, we must make all haste together if we may be of any use,

[013-214]though we are only two. Even cowards gain courage from

[013-215]companionship, and we two can hold our own with the bravest.”

[013-216]Therewith the god went back into the thick of the fight, and

[013-217]Idomeneus when he had reached his tent donned his armour, grasped

[013-218]his two spears, and sallied forth. As the lightning which the son

[013-219]of Saturn brandishes from bright Olympus when he would show a

[013-220]sign to mortals, and its gleam flashes far and wide—even so did

[013-221]his armour gleam about him as he ran. Meriones his sturdy squire

[013-222]met him while he was still near his tent (for he was going to

[013-223]fetch his spear) and Idomeneus said:

[013-224]“Meriones, fleet son of Molus, best of comrades, why have you

[013-225]left the field? Are you wounded, and is the point of the weapon

[013-226]hurting you? or have you been sent to fetch me? I want no

[013-227]fetching; I had far rather fight than stay in my tent.”

[013-228]“Idomeneus,” answered Meriones, “I come for a spear, if I can

[013-229]find one in my tent; I have broken the one I had, in throwing it

[013-230]at the shield of Deiphobus.”

[013-231]And Idomeneus captain of the Cretans answered, “You will find one

[013-232]spear, or twenty if you so please, standing up against the end

[013-233]wall of my tent. I have taken them from Trojans whom I have

[013-234]killed, for I am not one to keep my enemy at arm’s length;

[013-235]therefore I have spears, bossed shields, helmets, and burnished

[013-236]corslets.”

[013-237]Then Meriones said, “I too in my tent and at my ship have spoils

[013-238]taken from the Trojans, but they are not at hand. I have been at

[013-239]all times valorous, and wherever there has been hard fighting

[013-240]have held my own among the foremost. There may be those among the

[013-241]Achaeans who do not know how I fight, but you know it well enough

[013-242]yourself.”

[013-243]Idomeneus answered, “I know you for a brave man: you need not

[013-244]tell me. If the best men at the ships were being chosen to go on

[013-245]an ambush—and there is nothing like this for showing what a man

[013-246]is made of; it comes out then who is cowardly and who brave; the

[013-247]coward will change colour at every touch and turn; he is full of

[013-248]fears, and keeps shifting his weight first on one knee and then

[013-249]on the other; his heart beats fast as he thinks of death, and one

[013-250]can hear the chattering of his teeth; whereas the brave man will

[013-251]not change colour nor be frightened on finding himself in ambush,

[013-252]but is all the time longing to go into action—if the best men

[013-253]were being chosen for such a service, no one could make light of

[013-254]your courage nor feats of arms. If you were struck by a dart or

[013-255]smitten in close combat, it would not be from behind, in your

[013-256]neck nor back, but the weapon would hit you in the chest or belly

[013-257]as you were pressing forward to a place in the front ranks. But

[013-258]let us no longer stay here talking like children, lest we be ill

[013-259]spoken of; go, fetch your spear from the tent at once.”

[013-260]On this Meriones, peer of Mars, went to the tent and got himself

[013-261]a spear of bronze. He then followed after Idomeneus, big with

[013-262]great deeds of valour. As when baneful Mars sallies forth to

[013-263]battle, and his son Panic so strong and dauntless goes with him,

[013-264]to strike terror even into the heart of a hero—the pair have gone

[013-265]from Thrace to arm themselves among the Ephyri or the brave

[013-266]Phlegyans, but they will not listen to both the contending hosts,

[013-267]and will give victory to one side or to the other—even so did

[013-268]Meriones and Idomeneus, captains of men, go out to battle clad in

[013-269]their bronze armour. Meriones was first to speak. “Son of

[013-270]Deucalion,” said he, “where would you have us begin fighting? On

[013-271]the right wing of the host, in the centre, or on the left wing,

[013-272]where I take it the Achaeans will be weakest?”

[013-273]Idomeneus answered, “There are others to defend the centre—the

[013-274]two Ajaxes and Teucer, who is the finest archer of all the

[013-275]Achaeans, and is good also in a hand-to-hand fight. These will

[013-276]give Hector son of Priam enough to do; fight as he may, he will

[013-277]find it hard to vanquish their indomitable fury, and fire the

[013-278]ships, unless the son of Saturn fling a firebrand upon them with

[013-279]his own hand. [[ajax-the-greater|Great Ajax]] son of Telamon will yield to no man who

[013-280]is in mortal mould and eats the grain of Ceres, if bronze and

[013-281]great stones can overthrow him. He would not yield even to

[013-282][[achilles|Achilles]] in hand-to-hand fight, and in fleetness of foot there is

[013-283]none to beat him; let us turn therefore towards the left wing,

[013-284]that we may know forthwith whether we are to give glory to some

[013-285]other, or he to us.”

[013-286]Meriones, peer of fleet Mars, then led the way till they came to

[013-287]the part of the host which Idomeneus had named.

[013-288]Now when the Trojans saw Idomeneus coming on like a flame of

[013-289]fire, him and his squire clad in their richly wrought armour,

[013-290]they shouted and made towards him all in a body, and a furious

[013-291]hand-to-hand fight raged under the ships’ sterns. Fierce as the

[013-292]shrill winds that whistle upon a day when dust lies deep on the

[013-293]roads, and the gusts raise it into a thick cloud—even such was

[013-294]the fury of the combat, and might and main did they hack at each

[013-295]other with spear and sword throughout the host. The field

[013-296]bristled with the long and deadly spears which they bore.

[013-297]Dazzling was the sheen of their gleaming helmets, their

[013-298]fresh-burnished breastplates, and glittering shields as they

[013-299]joined battle with one another. Iron indeed must be his courage

[013-300]who could take pleasure in the sight of such a turmoil, and look

[013-301]on it without being dismayed.

[013-302]Thus did the two mighty sons of Saturn devise evil for mortal

[013-303]heroes. Jove was minded to give victory to the Trojans and to

[013-304]Hector, so as to do honour to fleet Achilles, nevertheless he did

[013-305]not mean to utterly overthrow the Achaean host before Ilius, and

[013-306]only wanted to glorify Thetis and her valiant son. Neptune on the

[013-307]other hand went about among the Argives to incite them, having

[013-308]come up from the grey sea in secret, for he was grieved at seeing

[013-309]them vanquished by the Trojans, and was furiously angry with

[013-310]Jove. Both were of the same race and country, but Jove was elder

[013-311]born and knew more, therefore Neptune feared to defend the

[013-312]Argives openly, but in the likeness of man, he kept on

[013-313]encouraging them throughout their host. Thus, then, did these two

[013-314]devise a knot of war and battle, that none could unloose or

[013-315]break, and set both sides tugging at it, to the failing of men’s

[013-316]knees beneath them.

[013-317]And now Idomeneus, though his hair was already flecked with grey,

[013-318]called loud on the Danaans and spread panic among the Trojans as

[013-319]he leaped in among them. He slew Othryoneus from Cabesus, a

[013-320]sojourner, who had but lately come to take part in the war. He

[013-321]sought Cassandra, the fairest of Priam’s daughters, in marriage,

[013-322]but offered no gifts of wooing, for he promised a great thing, to

[013-323]wit, that he would drive the sons of the Achaeans willy nilly

[013-324]from Troy; old King Priam had given his consent and promised her

[013-325]to him, whereon he fought on the strength of the promises thus

[013-326]made to him. Idomeneus aimed a spear, and hit him as he came

[013-327]striding on. His cuirass of bronze did not protect him, and the

[013-328]spear stuck in his belly, so that he fell heavily to the ground.

[013-329]Then Idomeneus vaunted over him saying, “Othryoneus, there is no

[013-330]one in the world whom I shall admire more than I do you, if you

[013-331]indeed perform what you have promised Priam son of Dardanus in

[013-332]return for his daughter. We too will make you an offer; we will

[013-333]give you the loveliest daughter of the son of Atreus, and will

[013-334]bring her from Argos for you to marry, if you will sack the

[013-335]goodly city of Ilius in company with ourselves; so come along

[013-336]with me, that we may make a [[oath|covenant]] at the ships about the

[013-337]marriage, and we will not be hard upon you about gifts of

[013-338]wooing.”

[013-339]With this Idomeneus began dragging him by the foot through the

[013-340]thick of the fight, but Asius came up to protect the body, on

[013-341]foot, in front of his horses which his esquire drove so close

[013-342]behind him that he could feel their breath upon his shoulder. He

[013-343]was longing to strike down Idomeneus, but ere he could do so

[013-344]Idomeneus smote him with his spear in the throat under the chin,

[013-345]and the bronze point went clean through it. He fell as an oak, or

[013-346]poplar, or pine which shipwrights have felled for ship’s timber

[013-347]upon the mountains with whetted axes—even thus did he lie full

[013-348]length in front of his chariot and horses, grinding his teeth and

[013-349]clutching at the blood-stained dust. His charioteer was struck

[013-350]with panic and did not dare turn his horses round and escape:

[013-351]thereupon Antilochus hit him in the middle of his body with a

[013-352]spear; his cuirass of bronze did not protect him, and the spear

[013-353]stuck in his belly. He fell gasping from his chariot and

[013-354]Antilochus, great [[nestor|Nestor]]’s son, drove his horses from the Trojans

[013-355]to the Achaeans.

[013-356]Deiphobus then came close up to Idomeneus to avenge Asius, and

[013-357]took aim at him with a spear, but Idomeneus was on the look-out

[013-358]and avoided it, for he was covered by the round shield he always

[013-359]bore—a shield of ox-hide and bronze with two arm-rods on the
