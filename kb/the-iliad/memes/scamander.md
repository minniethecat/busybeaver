# Scamander River (Xanthus)

The Scamander (called Xanthus by the gods) is the principal river of the Trojan plain, flowing past the city and into the Hellespont. In the *Iliad*, it is both a geographical feature and a divine person — the river god Scamander, who rises from his waters in Book XXI to fight Achilles when the hero's slaughter of Trojans chokes the river with corpses.

## Geography

The Scamander flows from Mount Ida across the Trojan plain, past the city of Troy, to the Hellespont. Its waters mark the boundary between the Achaean camp and the city, and much of the epic's battle action takes place on the plain between the Scamander and its tributary, the Simois. The river is both a source of life for the Trojan agricultural plain and, in Book XXI, a lethal obstacle that nearly kills Achilles.

## The Battle with Achilles (Book XXI)

The Scamander's most dramatic moment comes in Book XXI. Enraged that Achilles has filled his "fair streams" with the corpses of slaughtered Trojans, the river god rises up and attacks Achilles, pursuing him across the plain with massive waves. Achilles flees — the greatest warrior in the world, running from a river — until Hephaestus, at Hera's command, sends a wall of fire that boils the river back into its banks. This episode, in which nature itself rises up against human violence, is one of the epic's most surreal and powerful passages.
