# Achilles

Achilles is the central figure of the Iliad, the son of the mortal king Peleus and the sea-goddess Thetis. He is the leader of the Myrmidons, a contingent of warriors from Phthia, and is renowned as the greatest fighter among the Achaeans. His consuming [[wrath|Wrath (Mēnis)]] against [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sets the epic in motion, and his [[grief|Grief (Akhos)]] for [[patroclus|Patroclus]] transforms that wrath into world-shattering vengeance. His common epithets include "swift-footed" (*podas okus*), "son of Peleus" (Pelides), and "descendant of Aeacus" (Aeacides).

## Identity

Achilles is defined by his semi-divine heritage, his matchless prowess in battle, and his consuming passions. He is the son of Peleus, king of the Myrmidons, and Thetis, a sea-nymph of the Nereids. His dual nature — mortal through his father, touched by divinity through his mother — places him at the boundary between human and divine. His *menis* (divine wrath) is the first word of the poem and its thematic engine (001-001). Achilles' identity is inseparable from his awareness of his own fate: he knows he will die young at Troy but chooses everlasting glory (*kleos aphthiton*) over a long, obscure life (001-293). His mother Thetis foretold this destiny to him before the war began, and the tension between his mortality and his longing for immortal renown defines every choice he makes.

## Role in the Epic

Achilles' presence or absence defines the structure of the Iliad, and his actions drive every major turning point in the narrative.

- **Book 1 — The Quarrel:** When Agamemnon seizes Briseis, Achilles' prize of honor, he withdraws from the fighting. Athena intervenes to restrain his impulse to kill Agamemnon on the spot (001-008). After surrendering Briseis, he withdraws to his tent (001-100) and prays to his mother Thetis by the shore (001-116), begging her to persuade Zeus to grant victory to the Trojans so the Achaeans will feel his absence.

- **Books 2–8 — Wrath and Absence:** Achilles abides at his ships and nurses his wrath (001-234), refusing all appeals to return to battle. His absence allows Hector and the Trojans to drive the Achaeans back to their ships. The Achaean plight worsens with each passing book, and the reader is made to feel the enormity of Achilles' withdrawal.

- **Book 9 — The Embassy:** Agamemnon sends Odysseus, Ajax, and Phoenix with generous gifts to persuade Achilles to return. Achilles rejects them in a speech of extraordinary eloquence (009-077), declaring that no wealth can compensate for the slight to his honor. This scene represents a profound critique of the material value-system of heroic culture.

- **Books 16–18 — Loss of Patroclus:** When Patroclus falls to Hector, Achilles' grief is boundless. His cry of anguish reaches Thetis beneath the sea (018-001). He receives divine armor forged by Hephaestus at Thetis' request, including the magnificent Shield of Achilles depicting the entire cosmos (018-200). He reconciles with Agamemnon, and his wrath shifts from the Achaean commander to Hector and the Trojans.

- **Books 20–22 — Aristeia:** Achilles returns to battle with terrible fury, slaughtering Trojans in such numbers that the river Scamander itself rises against him (021-001). His *aristeia* (moment of supreme excellence) culminates in the death of Hector outside the walls of Troy (022-250). He drags Hector's body behind his chariot around the walls of Troy, an act of desecration that shocks gods and mortals alike (022-375).

- **Book 24 — Resolution:** Under divine command, Achilles receives Priam in his tent. Moved by the old king's courage and grief, he returns Hector's body for burial (024-400). This act of compassion restores his humanity and brings the epic to its transcendent close. As Priam kissed the hands that had killed his son, "Achilles wept for his own father" (024-420) — the poem's most profound moment of shared mortal sorrow.

> — Achilles after Briseis is taken from him (001-100)

> — Achilles' grief upon learning of Patroclus' death (018-001)

## Relationships

- **Thetis (mother):** Achilles' closest divine ally. She intercedes with Zeus on his behalf (001-116), commissions Hephaestus to forge his new armor (018-200), and consoles him throughout the epic. Her own grief for her mortal son's inevitable death permeates her every appearance.

- **Patroclus (companion):** The defining relationship of Achilles' life. Patroclus' death transforms Achilles' wrath from passive resentment into active, world-shattering vengeance. Achilles' lament for Patroclus is one of the most powerful expressions of grief in ancient literature (018-001–018-050). His funeral games in Book 23 are a monumental act of honor.

- **Agamemnon (antagonist):** The conflict over Briseis and honor drives the first half of the epic. Their quarrel in Book 1 (001-001–001-234) exposes the fragility of the Achaean coalition and the centrality of honor (*timē*) to heroic identity. Their reconciliation in Book 19 is a necessary precondition for Achilles' return.

- **Hector (foil):** The Trojan champion whose death at Achilles' hands (022-250) marks the climax of the poem. Their final encounter outside Troy is one of the most famous duels in literature. Hector is Achilles' mirror — a warrior who fights to defend his city and family, while Achilles fights for personal glory and vengeance.

- **Priam (suppliant):** In Book 24, Priam's courage in coming to Achilles' tent to ransom Hector's body moves Achilles to compassion (024-400). As they weep together — Achilles for Patroclus and his own father, Priam for Hector — the epic reaches its moral culmination.

- **Briseis (prize):** The woman whose seizure by Agamemnon triggers Achilles' withdrawal and the entire chain of events. Though a captive, Briseis' lament for Patroclus in Book 19 reveals her genuine affection for Achilles' household (019-050).

## Death and Fate

Achilles is defined throughout the epic by his awareness of his own fate — that he will die young at Troy but achieve everlasting glory (*kleos aphthiton*). His mother Thetis has told him of two possible destinies: a long, obscure life at home, or a short, glorious death at Troy (001-293). His choice of the latter is the foundational moral fact of his character. The Iliad does not narrate Achilles' death, but it is foretold multiple times: he will fall to Paris and Apollo at the Scaean Gate after killing Hector. His burial mound on the shore of the Hellespont becomes a landmark visible to sailors for generations.

## Scholarly Notes

Achilles embodies the Homeric heroic code in its most extreme form. His *menis* (divine wrath) is the first word of the poem and its thematic engine. Scholars have long debated whether Achilles undergoes genuine moral development — from the petulant, honor-obsessed warrior of Book 1 to the figure of compassion who receives Priam in Book 24. His rejection of the embassy's gifts in Book 9 represents a profound critique of the material value-system of heroic culture, anticipating philosophical questions about the commensurability of honor and life. Samuel Butler's translation captures the directness and emotional intensity of Achilles' speeches with particular vividness, rendering the hero's voice in plain, forceful English that preserves the psychological immediacy of Homer's Greek.
