# Book XXII

[022-001]BOOK XXII.

[022-002]The death of [[hector|Hector]].

[022-003]Thus the Trojans in the city, scared like fawns, wiped the sweat

[022-004]from off them and drank to quench their thirst, leaning against

[022-005]the goodly battlements, while the Achaeans with their shields

[022-006]laid upon their shoulders drew close up to the walls. But stern

[022-007]fate bade Hector stay where he was before Ilius and the Scaean

[022-008]gates. Then Phoebus Apollo spoke to the son of Peleus saying,

[022-009]“Why, son of Peleus, do you, who are but man, give chase to me

[022-010]who am immortal? Have you not yet found out that it is a god whom

[022-011]you pursue so furiously? You did not harass the Trojans whom you

[022-012]had routed, and now they are within their walls, while you have

[022-013]been decoyed hither away from them. Me you cannot kill, for death

[022-014]can take no hold upon me.”

[022-015][[achilles|Achilles]] was greatly angered and said, “You have baulked me,

[022-016]Far-Darter, most malicious of all gods, and have drawn me away

[022-017]from the wall, where many another man would have bitten the dust

[022-018]ere he got within Ilius; you have robbed me of great [[honor|glory]] and

[022-019]have saved the Trojans at no risk to yourself, for you have

[022-020]nothing to fear, but I would indeed have my revenge if it were in

[022-021]my power to do so.”

[022-022]On this, with fell intent he made towards the city, and as the

[022-023]winning horse in a chariot race strains every nerve when he is

[022-024]flying over the plain, even so fast and furiously did the limbs

[022-025]of Achilles bear him onwards. King [[priam|Priam]] was first to note him as

[022-026]he scoured the plain, all radiant as the star which men call

[022-027]Orion’s Hound, and whose beams blaze forth in time of harvest

[022-028]more brilliantly than those of any other that shines by night;

[022-029]brightest of them all though he be, he yet bodes ill for mortals,

[022-030]for he brings fire and fever in his train—even so did Achilles’

[022-031]armour gleam on his breast as he sped onwards. Priam raised a cry

[022-032]and beat his head with his hands as he lifted them up and shouted

[022-033]out to his dear son, imploring him to return; but Hector still

[022-034]stayed before the gates, for his heart was set upon doing battle

[022-035]with Achilles. The old man reached out his arms towards him and

[022-036]bade him for pity’s sake come within the walls. “Hector,” he

[022-037]cried, “my son, stay not to face this man alone and unsupported,

[022-038]or you will meet death at the hands of the son of Peleus, for he

[022-039]is mightier than you. Monster that he is; would indeed that the

[022-040]gods loved him no better than I do, for so, dogs and vultures

[022-041]would soon devour him as he lay stretched on earth, and a load of

[022-042]grief would be lifted from my heart, for many a brave son has he

[022-043]reft from me, either by killing them or selling them away in the

[022-044]islands that are beyond the sea: even now I miss two sons from

[022-045]among the Trojans who have thronged within the city, Lycaon and

[022-046]Polydorus, whom Laothoe peeress among women bore me. Should they

[022-047]be still alive and in the hands of the Achaeans, we will ransom

[022-048]them with gold and bronze, of which we have store, for the old

[022-049]man Altes endowed his daughter richly; but if they are already

[022-050]dead and in the house of Hades, [[grief|sorrow]] will it be to us two who

[022-051]were their parents; albeit the grief of others will be more

[022-052]short-lived unless you too perish at the hands of Achilles. Come,

[022-053]then, my son, within the city, to be the guardian of Trojan men

[022-054]and Trojan women, or you will both lose your own life and afford

[022-055]a mighty triumph to the son of Peleus. Have pity also on your

[022-056]unhappy father while life yet remains to him—on me, whom the son

[022-057]of Saturn will destroy by a terrible [[fate|doom]] on the threshold of old

[022-058]age, after I have seen my sons slain and my daughters haled away

[022-059]as captives, my bridal chambers pillaged, little children dashed

[022-060]to earth amid the [[wrath|rage]] of battle, and my sons’ wives dragged away

[022-061]by the cruel hands of the Achaeans; in the end fierce hounds will

[022-062]tear me in pieces at my own gates after some one has beaten the

[022-063]life out of my body with sword or spear-hounds that I myself

[022-064]reared and fed at my own table to guard my gates, but who will

[022-065]yet lap my blood and then lie all distraught at my doors. When a

[022-066]young man falls by the sword in battle, he may lie where he is

[022-067]and there is nothing unseemly; let what will be seen, all is

[022-068]honourable in death, but when an old man is slain there is

[022-069]nothing in this world more pitiable than that dogs should defile

[022-070]his grey hair and beard and all that men hide for shame.”

## Section 63

[022-071]The old man tore his grey hair as he spoke, but he moved not the

[022-072]heart of Hector. His mother hard by wept and moaned aloud as she

[022-073]bared her bosom and pointed to the breast which had suckled him.

[022-074]“Hector,” she cried, weeping bitterly the while, “Hector, my son,

[022-075]spurn not this breast, but have pity upon me too: if I have ever

[022-076]given you comfort from my own bosom, think on it now, dear son,

[022-077]and come within the wall to protect us from this man; stand not

[022-078]without to meet him. Should the wretch kill you, neither I nor

[022-079]your richly dowered wife shall ever weep, dear offshoot of

[022-080]myself, over the bed on which you lie, for dogs will devour you

[022-081]at the [[achaean-camp|ships]] of the Achaeans.”

[022-082]Thus did the two with many tears implore their son, but they

[022-083]moved not the heart of Hector, and he stood his ground awaiting

[022-084]huge Achilles as he drew nearer towards him. As a serpent in its

[022-085]den upon the mountains, full fed with deadly poisons, waits for

[022-086]the approach of man—he is filled with fury and his eyes glare

[022-087]terribly as he goes writhing round his den—even so Hector leaned

[022-088]his shield against a tower that jutted out from the wall and

[022-089]stood where he was, undaunted.

[022-090]“Alas,” said he to himself in the heaviness of his heart, “if I

[022-091]go within the gates, Polydamas will be the first to heap reproach

[022-092]upon me, for it was he that urged me to lead the Trojans back to

[022-093]the city on that awful night when Achilles again came forth

[022-094]against us. I would not listen, but it would have been indeed

[022-095]better if I had done so. Now that my folly has destroyed the

[022-096][[guest-friendship|host]], I dare not look Trojan men and Trojan women in the face,

[022-097]lest a worse man should say, ‘Hector has ruined us by his

[022-098]self-confidence.’ Surely it would be better for me to return

[022-099]after having fought Achilles and slain him, or to die gloriously

[022-100]here before the city. What, again, if I were to lay down my

[022-101]shield and helmet, lean my spear against the wall and go straight

[022-102]up to noble Achilles? What if I were to promise to give up [[helen|Helen]],

[022-103]who was the fountainhead of all this war, and all the treasure

[022-104]that [[paris-alexander|Alexandrus]] brought with him in his ships to Troy, aye, and

[022-105]to let the Achaeans divide the half of everything that the city

[022-106]contains among themselves? I might make the Trojans, by the

[022-107]mouths of their princes, take a solemn oath that they would hide

[022-108]nothing, but would divide into two shares all that is within the

[022-109]city—but why argue with myself in this way? Were I to go up to

[022-110]him he would show me no kind of mercy; he would kill me then and

[022-111]there as easily as though I were a woman, when I had off my

[022-112]armour. There is no parleying with him from some rock or oak tree

[022-113]as young men and maidens prattle with one another. Better fight

[022-114]him at once, and learn to which of us Jove will vouchsafe

[022-115]victory.”

[022-116]Thus did he stand and ponder, but Achilles came up to him as it

[022-117]were Mars himself, plumed lord of battle. From his right shoulder

[022-118]he brandished his terrible spear of Pelian ash, and the bronze

[022-119]gleamed around him like flashing fire or the rays of the rising

[022-120]sun. Fear fell upon Hector as he beheld him, and he dared not

[022-121]stay longer where he was but fled in dismay from before the

[022-122]gates, while Achilles darted after him at his utmost speed. As a

[022-123]mountain falcon, swiftest of all birds, swoops down upon some

[022-124]cowering dove—the dove flies before him but the falcon with a

[022-125]shrill scream follows close after, resolved to have her—even so

[022-126]did Achilles make straight for Hector with all his might, while

[022-127]Hector fled under the Trojan wall as fast as his limbs could take

[022-128]him.

[022-129]On they flew along the waggon-road that ran hard by under the

[022-130]wall, past the look-out station, and past the weather-beaten wild

[022-131]fig-tree, till they came to two fair springs which feed the river

[022-132]Scamander. One of these two springs is warm, and steam rises from

[022-133]it as smoke from a burning fire, but the other even in summer is

[022-134]as cold as hail or snow, or the ice that forms on water. Here,

[022-135]hard by the springs, are the goodly washing-troughs of stone,

[022-136]where in the time of peace before the coming of the Achaeans the

[022-137]wives and fair daughters of the Trojans used to wash their

[022-138]clothes. Past these did they fly, the one in front and the other

[022-139]giving chase behind him: good was the man that fled, but better

[022-140]far was he that followed after, and swiftly indeed did they run,

[022-141]for the prize was no mere beast for sacrifice or bullock’s hide,

[022-142]as it might be for a common foot-race, but they ran for the life

[022-143]of Hector. As horses in a chariot race speed round the

[022-144]turning-posts when they are running for some great prize—a tripod

[022-145]or woman—at the games in honour of some dead hero, so did these

[022-146]two run full speed three times round the city of Priam. All the

[022-147]gods watched them, and the sire of gods and men was the first to

[022-148]speak.

[022-149]“Alas,” said he, “my eyes behold a man who is dear to me being

[022-150]pursued round the walls of Troy; my heart is full of pity for

[022-151]Hector, who has burned the thigh-bones of many a heifer in my

[022-152]honour, one while on the crests of [[mount-ida|many-valleyed Ida]], and again

[022-153]on the citadel of Troy; and now I see noble Achilles in full

[022-154]pursuit of him round the city of Priam. What say you? Consider

[022-155]among yourselves and decide whether we shall now save him or let

[022-156]him fall, valiant though he be, before Achilles, son of Peleus.”

[022-157]Then Minerva said, “Father, wielder of the lightning, lord of

[022-158]cloud and storm, what mean you? Would you pluck this mortal whose

[022-159]doom has long been decreed out of the jaws of death? Do as you

[022-160]will, but we others shall not be of a mind with you.”

[022-161]And Jove answered, “My child, Trito-born, take heart. I did not

[022-162]speak in full earnest, and I will let you have your way. Do

[022-163]without let or hindrance as you are minded.”

[022-164]Thus did he urge Minerva who was already eager, and down she

[022-165]darted from the topmost summits of [[mount-olympus|Olympus]].

[022-166]Achilles was still in full pursuit of Hector, as a hound chasing

[022-167]a fawn which he has started from its covert on the mountains, and

[022-168]hunts through glade and thicket. The fawn may try to elude him by

[022-169]crouching under cover of a bush, but he will scent her out and

[022-170]follow her up until he gets her—even so there was no escape for

[022-171]Hector from the fleet son of Peleus. Whenever he made a set to

[022-172]get near the Dardanian gates and under the walls, that his people

[022-173]might help him by showering down weapons from above, Achilles

[022-174]would gain on him and head him back towards the plain, keeping

[022-175]himself always on the city side. As a man in a dream who fails to

[022-176]lay hands upon another whom he is pursuing—the one cannot escape

[022-177]nor the other overtake—even so neither could Achilles come up

[022-178]with Hector, nor Hector break away from Achilles; nevertheless he

[022-179]might even yet have escaped death had not the time come when

[022-180]Apollo, who thus far had sustained his strength and nerved his

[022-181]running, was now no longer to stay by him. Achilles made signs to

[022-182]the Achaean host, and shook his head to show that no man was to

[022-183]aim a dart at Hector, lest another might win the glory of having

[022-184]hit him and he might himself come in second. Then, at last, as

[022-185]they were nearing the fountains for the fourth time, the father

[022-186]of all balanced his golden scales and placed a doom in each of

[022-187]them, one for Achilles and the other for Hector. As he held the

[022-188]scales by the middle, the doom of Hector fell down deep into the

[022-189]house of Hades—and then Phoebus Apollo left him. Thereon Minerva

[022-190]went close up to the son of Peleus and said, “Noble Achilles,

[022-191]favoured of heaven, we two shall surely take back to the ships a

[022-192]triumph for the Achaeans by slaying Hector, for all his lust of

[022-193]battle. Do what Apollo may as he lies grovelling before his

[022-194]father, aegis-bearing Jove, Hector cannot escape us longer. Stay

[022-195]here and take breath, while I go up to him and persuade him to

[022-196]make a stand and fight you.”

[022-197]Thus spoke Minerva. Achilles obeyed her gladly, and stood still,

[022-198]leaning on his bronze-pointed ashen spear, while Minerva left him

[022-199]and went after Hector in the form and with the voice of

[022-200]Deiphobus. She came close up to him and said, “Dear brother, I

[022-201]see you are hard pressed by Achilles who is chasing you at full

[022-202]speed round the city of Priam, let us await his onset and stand

[022-203]on our defence.”

[022-204]And Hector answered, “Deiphobus, you have always been dearest to

[022-205]me of all my brothers, children of Hecuba and Priam, but

[022-206]henceforth I shall rate you yet more highly, inasmuch as you have

[022-207]ventured outside the wall for my sake when all the others remain

[022-208]inside.”

[022-209]Then Minerva said, “Dear brother, my father and mother went down

[022-210]on their knees and implored me, as did all my comrades, to remain

[022-211]inside, so great a fear has fallen upon them all; but I was in an

[022-212]agony of grief when I beheld you; now, therefore, let us two make

[022-213]a stand and fight, and let there be no keeping our spears in

[022-214]reserve, that we may learn whether Achilles shall kill us and

[022-215]bear off our spoils to the ships, or whether he shall fall before

[022-216]you.”

[022-217]Thus did Minerva inveigle him by her cunning, and when the two

[022-218]were now close to one another great Hector was first to speak. “I

[022-219]will no longer fly you, son of Peleus,” said he, “as I have been

[022-220]doing hitherto. Three times have I fled round the mighty city of

[022-221]Priam, without daring to withstand you, but now, let me either

[022-222]slay or be slain, for I am in the mind to face you. Let us, then,

[022-223]give pledges to one another by our gods, who are the fittest

[022-224]witnesses and guardians of all covenants; let it be agreed

[022-225]between us that if Jove vouchsafes me the longer stay and I take

[022-226]your life, I am not to treat your dead body in any unseemly

[022-227]fashion, but when I have stripped you of your armour, I am to

[022-228]give up your body to the Achaeans. And do you likewise.”

[022-229]Achilles glared at him and answered, “Fool, prate not to me about

[022-230]covenants. There can be no covenants between men and lions,

[022-231]wolves and lambs can never be of one mind, but hate each other

[022-232]out and out all through. Therefore there can be no understanding

[022-233]between you and me, nor may there be any covenants between us,

[022-234]till one or other shall fall and glut grim Mars with his life’s

[022-235]blood. Put forth all your strength; you have need now to prove

[022-236]yourself indeed a bold soldier and man of war. You have no more

[022-237]chance, and Pallas Minerva will forthwith vanquish you by my

[022-238]spear: you shall now pay me in full for the grief you have caused

[022-239]me on account of my comrades whom you have killed in battle.”

[022-240]He poised his spear as he spoke and hurled it. Hector saw it

[022-241]coming and avoided it; he watched it and crouched down so that it

[022-242]flew over his head and stuck in the ground beyond; Minerva then

[022-243]snatched it up and gave it back to Achilles without Hector’s

[022-244]seeing her; Hector thereon said to the son of Peleus, “You have

[022-245]missed your aim, Achilles, peer of the gods, and Jove has not yet

[022-246]revealed to you the hour of my doom, though you made sure that he

[022-247]had done so. You were a false-tongued liar when you deemed that I

[022-248]should forget my valour and quail before you. You shall not drive

[022-249]your spear into the back of a runaway—drive it, should heaven so

[022-250]grant you power, drive it into me as I make straight towards you;

[022-251]and now for your own part avoid my spear if you can—would that

[022-252]you might receive the whole of it into your body; if you were

[022-253]once dead the Trojans would find the war an easier matter, for it

[022-254]is you who have harmed them most.”

## Section 64

[022-255]He poised his spear as he spoke and hurled it. His aim was true

[022-256]for he hit the middle of Achilles’ shield, but the spear

[022-257]rebounded from it, and did not pierce it. Hector was angry when

[022-258]he saw that the weapon had sped from his hand in vain, and stood

[022-259]there in dismay for he had no second spear. With a loud cry he

[022-260]called Deiphobus and asked him for one, but there was no man;

[022-261]then he saw the truth and said to himself, “Alas! the gods have

[022-262]lured me on to my destruction. I deemed that the hero Deiphobus

[022-263]was by my side, but he is within the wall, and Minerva has

[022-264]inveigled me; death is now indeed exceedingly near at hand and

[022-265]there is no way out of it—for so Jove and his son Apollo the

[022-266]far-darter have willed it, though heretofore they have been ever

[022-267]ready to protect me. My doom has come upon me; let me not then

[022-268]die ingloriously and without a struggle, but let me first do some

[022-269]great thing that shall be told among men hereafter.”

[022-270]As he spoke he drew the keen blade that hung so great and strong

[022-271]by his side, and gathering himself together be sprang on Achilles

[022-272]like a soaring eagle which swoops down from the clouds on to some

[022-273]lamb or timid hare—even so did Hector brandish his sword and

[022-274]spring upon Achilles. Achilles mad with rage darted towards him,

[022-275]with his wondrous shield before his breast, and his gleaming

[022-276]helmet, made with four layers of metal, nodding fiercely forward.

[022-277]The thick tresses of gold with which Vulcan had crested the

[022-278]helmet floated round it, and as the evening star that shines

[022-279]brighter than all others through the stillness of night, even

[022-280]such was the gleam of the spear which Achilles poised in his

[022-281]right hand, fraught with the death of noble Hector. He eyed his

[022-282]fair flesh over and over to see where he could best wound it, but

[022-283]all was protected by the goodly armour of which Hector had

[022-284]spoiled [[patroclus|Patroclus]] after he had slain him, save only the throat

[022-285]where the collar-bones divide the neck from the shoulders, and

[022-286]this is a most deadly place: here then did Achilles strike him as

[022-287]he was coming on towards him, and the point of his spear went

[022-288]right through the fleshy part of the neck, but it did not sever

[022-289]his windpipe so that he could still speak. Hector fell headlong,

[022-290]and Achilles vaunted over him saying, “Hector, you deemed that

[022-291]you should come off scatheless when you were spoiling Patroclus,

[022-292]and recked not of myself who was not with him. Fool that you

[022-293]were: for I, his comrade, mightier far than he, was still left

[022-294]behind him at the ships, and now I have laid you low. The

[022-295]Achaeans shall give him all due funeral rites, while dogs and

[022-296]vultures shall work their will upon yourself.”

[022-297]Then Hector said, as the life ebbed out of him, “I pray you by

[022-298]your life and knees, and by your parents, let not dogs devour me

[022-299]at the ships of the Achaeans, but accept the rich treasure of

[022-300]gold and bronze which my father and mother will offer you, and

[022-301]send my body home, that the Trojans and their wives may give me

[022-302]my dues of fire when I am dead.”

[022-303]Achilles glared at him and answered, “Dog, talk not to me neither

[022-304]of knees nor parents; would that I could be as sure of being able

[022-305]to cut your flesh into pieces and eat it raw, for the ill you

[022-306]have done me, as I am that nothing shall save you from the

[022-307]dogs—it shall not be, though they bring ten or twenty-fold ransom

[022-308]and weigh it out for me on the spot, with promise of yet more

[022-309]hereafter. Though Priam son of Dardanus should bid them offer me

[022-310]your weight in gold, even so your mother shall never lay you out

[022-311]and make lament over the son she bore, but dogs and vultures

[022-312]shall eat you utterly up.”

[022-313]Hector with his dying breath then said, “I know you what you are,

[022-314]and was sure that I should not move you, for your heart is hard

[022-315]as iron; look to it that I bring not heaven’s anger upon you on

[022-316]the day when Paris and Phoebus Apollo, valiant though you be,

[022-317]shall slay you at the Scaean gates.”

[022-318]When he had thus said the shrouds of death enfolded him, whereon

[022-319]his soul went out of him and flew down to the house of Hades,

[022-320]lamenting its sad fate that it should enjoy youth and strength no

[022-321]longer. But Achilles said, speaking to the dead body, “Die; for

[022-322]my part I will accept my fate whensoever Jove and the other gods

[022-323]see fit to send it.”

[022-324]As he spoke he drew his spear from the body and set it on one

[022-325]side; then he stripped the blood-stained armour from Hector’s

[022-326]shoulders while the other Achaeans came running up to view his

[022-327]wondrous strength and beauty; and no one came near him without

[022-328]giving him a fresh wound. Then would one turn to his neighbour

[022-329]and say, “It is easier to handle Hector now than when he was

[022-330]flinging fire on to our ships”—and as he spoke he would thrust

[022-331]his spear into him anew.

[022-332]When Achilles had done spoiling Hector of his armour, he stood

[022-333]among the Argives and said, “My friends, princes and counsellors

[022-334]of the Argives, now that heaven has vouchsafed us to overcome

[022-335]this man, who has done us more hurt than all the others together,

[022-336]consider whether we should not attack the city in force, and

[022-337]discover in what mind the Trojans may be. We should thus learn

[022-338]whether they will desert their city now that Hector has fallen,

[022-339]or will still hold out even though he is no longer living. But

[022-340]why argue with myself in this way, while Patroclus is still lying

[022-341]at the ships unburied, and unmourned—he whom I can never forget

[022-342]so long as I am alive and my strength fails not? Though men

[022-343]forget their dead when once they are within the house of Hades,

[022-344]yet not even there will I forget the comrade whom I have lost.

[022-345]Now, therefore, Achaean youths, let us raise the song of victory

[022-346]and go back to the ships taking this man along with us; for we

[022-347]have achieved a mighty triumph and have slain noble Hector to

[022-348]whom the Trojans prayed throughout their city as though he were a

[022-349]god.”

[022-350]On this he treated the body of Hector with contumely: he pierced

[022-351]the sinews at the back of both his feet from heel to ancle and

[022-352]passed thongs of ox-hide through the slits he had made: thus he

[022-353]made the body fast to his chariot, letting the head trail upon

[022-354]the ground. Then when he had put the goodly armour on the chariot

[022-355]and had himself mounted, he lashed his horses on and they flew

[022-356]forward nothing loth. The dust rose from Hector as he was being

[022-357]dragged along, his dark hair flew all abroad, and his head once

[022-358]so comely was laid low on earth, for Jove had now delivered him

[022-359]into the hands of his foes to do him outrage in his own land.

[022-360]Thus was the head of Hector being dishonoured in the dust. His

[022-361]mother tore her hair, and flung her veil from her with a loud cry

[022-362]as she looked upon her son. His father made piteous moan, and

[022-364]was as though the whole of frowning Ilius was being smirched with
