## Identity

Acamas is a Trojan warrior of the Dardanian contingent, son of the wise counsellor Antenor and his wife Theano, priestess of Athena. He is paired with his brother Archelochus throughout the *Iliad*: "the two sons of Antenor, Archelochus and Acamas, both skilled in all the arts of war" (002-669-670). Together they lead the Dardanians — the people of Aeneas — alongside Aeneas himself, forming one of Troy's most capable command triads.

The *Iliad* describes Acamas as "young Acamas beauteous as a god" (011-062), a phrase that marks both his youth and his physical distinction. His lineage through Antenor places him at the centre of Trojan politics: Antenor is one of Priam's most trusted counsellors, famous for having hosted Odysseus and Menelaus during their embassy to Troy before the war. Acamas and Archelochus are repeatedly characterized as "men well versed in all the arts of war" (012-084), a formulaic phrase that establishes their martial competence.

Note: A second Acamas appears in the *Iliad* — "Acamas son of Eussorus, the best man among the Thracians" (006-010), killed by Ajax son of Telamon. This is a distinct character, a Thracian ally, not to be confused with Antenor's son.

## Role in the Epic

Acamas appears across multiple books of the *Iliad*, consistently in the company of his brother Archelochus and the Dardanian contingent.

**The Catalogue of Ships (Book II):** Acamas and Archelochus are introduced as commanders of the Dardanians: "the two sons of Antenor, Archelochus and Acamas, both skilled in all the arts of war" (002-669-670). The Dardanians are distinct from the main Trojan force — they are the people of Aeneas, settled in the foothills of Mount Ida, and their commanders reflect the alliance between the house of Antenor and the house of Anchises.

**The Trojan Marshalling (Book XII):** When the Trojans organize their assault on the Greek wall, Acamas and Archelochus are listed among the five captains who divide the attacking force: "Antenor, Archelochus and Acamas, men well versed in all the arts of war" (012-084). They command a division alongside the Lycian captains Sarpedon and Glaucus, the Trojan heroes Hector, Paris, and Agenor, and the allied leaders Asius and Polydamas — a testament to their standing in the Trojan high command.

**The Death of Archelochus and Acamas' Vengeance (Book XIV):** Acamas' most dramatic moment comes when his brother Archelochus is killed by Ajax son of Telamon. The spear strikes Archelochus "at the joining of the head and neck, and cut through both the tendons" (014-411-412), a clean and fatal blow. Acamas, seeing his brother fall, immediately moves to protect the body: "Acamas then bestrode his brother's body and wounded Promachus the Boeotian with his spear, for he was trying to drag his brother's body away" (014-414-416). He then delivers one of the epic's most pointed battlefield taunts:

> "Argive archers, braggarts that you are, toil and suffering shall not be for us only, but some of you too shall fall here as well as ourselves. See how Promachus now sleeps, vanquished by my spear; payment for my brother's blood has not been long delayed; a man, therefore, may well be thankful if he leaves a kinsman in his house behind him to avenge his fall." (014-417-421)

This speech — which directly echoes the taunt Ajax delivered after killing Archelochus — transforms personal grief into a statement of the heroic code: a kinsman's death demands swift vengeance, and a man who leaves a brother behind him has ensured that his blood will not go unavenged. Peneleos, enraged by the taunt, rushes at Acamas, but "Acamas did not stand his ground" (014-422), and Peneleos kills Ilioneus instead.

**The Pursuit by Meriones (Book XVI):** In the fighting over Patroclus' body, Acamas appears briefly: "Meriones gave chase to Acamas on foot and caught him up just as he was about to mount his chariot" (016-310). The outcome of this chase is not narrated, but the detail illustrates Acamas' role as a mobile, engaged warrior who fights from his chariot and retreats to it when pressed.

## Relationships

**Archelochus (brother):** Acamas' most defining relationship is with his brother Archelochus, with whom he shares command of the Dardanians. Their pairing is formulaic — they are always named together — and Acamas' most significant battlefield action is his defence of Archelochus' body and his avenging of his brother's death (014-414-421). The bond between the two Antenorid brothers is one of the *Iliad*'s most memorable examples of fraternal loyalty in war.

**Antenor (father):** Acamas' father Antenor is one of Troy's most respected elders, a man who counselled the return of Helen to the Greeks before the war. Antenor's wisdom and diplomatic instincts are not inherited by his sons in any obvious way — Acamas and Archelochus are warriors, not counsellors — but their standing in the Trojan command structure reflects their father's political importance.

**Ajax son of Telamon:** Ajax kills Archelochus with a spear-thrust to the neck (014-411-412) and taunts the Trojans: "I ween that you too are no coward, Archelochus, who have thus come to your end" (014-415). Acamas' subsequent taunt over Promachus is a direct response to Ajax's words, making the exchange between Ajax and Acamas a finely crafted example of Homeric battlefield rhetoric: death, taunt, vengeance, counter-taunt.

**Peneleos:** The Boeotian captain, enraged by Acamas' killing of Promachus and his taunting speech, rushes to attack Acamas. Acamas refuses to stand and fight, and Peneleos kills Ilioneus instead — an implicit admission of Peneleos' superior prowess or, alternatively, a prudent tactical withdrawal by a warrior who has already achieved his objective of avenging his brother.

**Meriones:** The Cretan warrior pursues Acamas as he attempts to mount his chariot (016-310-311), in a brief engagement that highlights Acamas' role as a chariot-fighter — mobile, dangerous when mounted, vulnerable when caught on foot.

## Death or Fate

The *Iliad* does not narrate Acamas' death. He survives the events of the epic, though he is pursued by Meriones in Book XVI (016-310). In later tradition — reflected in Quintus Smyrnaeus' *Posthomerica* — Acamas survives the war. His father Antenor, according to some accounts, survived the sack of Troy and went on to found cities in Italy (Padua) or northern Greece, and his sons may have accompanied him. Within the *Iliad*, Acamas' ultimate fate remains unresolved — an open narrative thread in the tangled web of Trojan aristocratic survival.

## Scholarly Notes

Acamas exemplifies a particular type of Homeric character: the paired brother-commander. Like the Atreidae (Agamemnon and Menelaus) on the Greek side, the Antenoridae (Archelochus and Acamas) on the Trojan side fight as a unit, and the death of one brother becomes the catalyst for the other's most dramatic battlefield moment. The structure — Archelochus killed by Ajax, Ajax taunts, Acamas kills Promachus in vengeance, Acamas taunts, Peneleos attacks — is a miniature of the epic's larger pattern of death and reprisal.

The scholarly name for Acamas' taunt over Promachus is a "vaunt" or *euchos* — a formalized battlefield boast that accompanies the killing of an enemy. The content of Acamas' vaunt is notable for its reflection on kinship and vengeance: the sentiment that a man with a brother may die more easily, knowing his blood will be avenged, is a variation on the epic's pervasive theme of heroic reciprocity.

The dual Acamas problem — two characters with the same name, one Trojan (son of Antenor) and one Thracian (son of Eussorus) — has generated scholarly discussion about Homeric naming conventions. The name "Acamas" means "untiring" or "indefatigable" (from Greek *a-* "not" + *kamnein* "to tire"), a fitting name for a warrior. The presence of two Acamases on the Trojan side may reflect a historical reality of recurring names in Anatolian aristocratic families, or it may be a simple case of oral-formulaic composition, where a stock name attached to different patronymics generates distinct characters.
