# Supplication (Hikesia)

Supplication is the most powerful non-violent act in the Homeric world. When a [[guest-friendship|suppliant]] grasps another's knees, lowers their eyes, and speaks the [[sacrifice|ritual]] words of entreaty, they invoke divine protection that the stronger party can refuse only at grave risk. The *Iliad* dramatizes both the power and the limits of supplication: accepted supplications save lives, but refused ones — as when [[achilles|Achilles]] rejects Lycaon's plea — mark the darkest m[[prophecy|omen]]ts of the epic.

## Definition

*Hikesia* (ἱκεσία) is the [[sacrifice|ritual]] of supplication, involving specific physical gestures (grasping the knees or chin, sitting at the hearth) and verbal formulas. Zeus Hiketesios (Zeus of Suppliants) protects those who perform the [[sacrifice|ritual]] correctly. To harm a [[guest-friendship|suppliant]] is to commit sacrilege; to accept a supplication creates a bond of obligation. The [[sacrifice|ritual]] operates even between enemies — it is a universal language of entreaty that transcends the divisions of war.

## Manifestations in the Epic

The epic's most poignant supplication scenes are those that fail. In Book XXI, Lycaon, a son of [[priam|Priam]] whom [[achilles|Achilles]] had previously captured and ransomed, meets [[achilles|Achilles]] again in battle. He grasps [[achilles|Achilles]]' knees and begs for mercy, [[sacrifice|offering]] ransom. [[achilles|Achilles]], in the grip of his *[[wrath|mēnis]]* after [[the-death-of-patroclus|Patroclus's death]], refuses: "Die, friend, and make no [[grief|lamentation]]. [[patroclus|Patroclus]] died, who was a better man than you." The rejection of supplication marks [[achilles|Achilles]]' descent into inhuman [[wrath|rage]].

The epic's culminating supplication — [[priam|Priam]] before [[achilles|Achilles]] in [[priam-ransoms-hector|Book XXIV]] — succeeds where Lycaon's failed. [[priam|Priam]] kneels, kisses the hands that killed his son, and appeals to [[achilles|Achilles]]' memory of his own father. [[achilles|Achilles]], moved to tears, accepts the supplication and returns [[hector|Hector]]'s body. This acceptance restores the moral order that the earlier rejection had shattered.

## Greek Terminology

**ἱκεσία (hikesia)** — the act of supplication. **ἱκέτης (hiketēs)** — the [[guest-friendship|suppliant]], one who comes as a protected petitioner. **Ζεὺς Ἱκετήσιος (Zeus Hiketesios)** — Zeus in his aspect as protector of [[guest-friendship|suppliant]]s. **γουνάζομαι (gounazomai)** — to clasp the knees, the defining physical gesture of supplication.
