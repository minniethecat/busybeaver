# Paris

Paris (also called Alexandrus or Alexander) is a son of [[priam|Priam]] and Hecuba, and the younger brother of [[hector|Hector]]. His abduction of Helen from [[menelaus|Menelaus]] is the proximate cause of the Trojan War, and throughout the Iliad he embodies a distinctive and troubling heroic type: beautiful, favoured by the gods, skilled with the bow, yet lacking the steadfast courage expected of a Trojan prince. Butler renders his double name transparently — "Alexandrus, also called Paris" (003-002) — reflecting the Homeric tradition that the character carries both a Trojan name (Paris) and a Greek one (Alexandrus, meaning "defender of men"). Where Hector fights in the front ranks, Paris fights from a distance; where Hector takes responsibility for his city's fate, Paris deflects blame with charm and divine favouritism.

## Identity

Paris is the son of Priam, king of Troy, and Hecuba, the queen. He is brother to Hector — Troy's greatest warrior — as well as to Deiphobus, Helenus, Cassandra, and many other children of Priam's vast household. Before his birth, Hecuba dreamed she gave birth to a flaming torch that consumed Troy, and the seers interpreted this to mean the child would bring destruction to the city. Priam ordered the infant exposed, but he was rescued and raised by shepherds on Mount Ida, returning to Troy only as a young man when his royal identity was discovered. This backstory, though not narrated directly in the Iliad, colours the poem's persistent tension: Paris is both a prince of Troy and the agent of its doom.

His defining epithets in the Greek tradition mark his beauty. He is "godlike Alexandrus" (*theoeidēs*) and the possessor of "lovely locks," an attribute associated with youth, seduction, and the favour of [[aphrodite|Aphrodite]]. Unlike his brother Hector, whose epithet "of the gleaming helmet" emphasizes martial prowess, Paris' epithets point toward the bedchamber rather than the battlefield.

## Role in the Epic

Paris appears most prominently in Books 3, 6, 7, 11, and 13, his narrative arc moving from near-death and divine rescue through shame and belated return to combat.

- **Book 3 — The Duel:** When the armies agree to settle the war by single combat between Paris and Menelaus, Paris steps forward in his leopard-skin and fine armour. But when Menelaus advances "fierce as a lion," Paris' courage fails: "when Alexandrus saw Menelaus come forward, his heart failed him and he shrank back into the ranks of his comrades" (003-002). Hector rebukes him savagely: "Paris, evil-hearted Paris, fair to see but woman-mad and false" (003-034). Paris then agrees to fight, but when Menelaus overpowers him and begins dragging him by the helmet, Aphrodite breaks the chin-strap and whisks him away in a mist to his perfumed bedchamber — preserving his life but utterly destroying his honour.

- **Book 3 — The Bedchamber:** Aphrodite compels Helen to return to Paris, and she confronts him with bitter contempt: "Would that you had fallen there, mastered by a better man" (003-338). Paris answers without shame: "Wife, do not vex me with your reproaches... let us take our joy, couched together in love" (003-338). The scene establishes Paris' defining trait: immunity to shame, shielded by Aphrodite's favour.

- **Book 6 — Hector's Visit:** Hector finds Paris in his chamber, polishing his armour while the battle rages outside (006-236). Hector's rebuke is gentler than in Book 3 but carries the weight of the city's desperation. Paris acknowledges the justice of the reproach and prepares to return to the fighting, emerging "like a spirited horse" (006-420). This is the moment Paris comes closest to heroic action — yet even here, he follows rather than leads.

- **Book 11 — Wounding Diomed:** Paris' greatest martial achievement comes from the bow, not the spear. Hiding behind a pillar on the tomb of Ilus, he shoots [[diomed|Diomed]] through the right foot (011-335). In the Greek text, Paris "laughed aloud and sprang up" in triumph — a rare moment of battlefield success for a character who normally fights at a distance.

- **Book 22 — Prophecy of Achilles' Death:** Though Paris does not appear directly, the dying Hector prophesies that "Paris and Phoebus Apollo" will kill Achilles at the Scaean Gate (022-316), a reminder that Paris' bow — and Apollo's guidance — will ultimately claim the greatest Greek warrior.

## Notable Speeches

> "Paris, evil-hearted Paris, fair to see but woman-mad and false, would that you had never been born, or that you had died unwed."
> — Hector rebuking Paris after he shrinks from Menelaus (003-034)

> "Wife, do not vex me with your reproaches. This time Menelaus with the help of Minerva has beaten me; another time I may myself be victor, for I too have gods that are on my side."
> — Paris to Helen after Aphrodite saves him (003-338)

> "I will go with you; I know that the battle is going hard, and I will fight."
> — Paris' response to Hector's rebuke, Book 6 (006-413)

## Relationships

- **Hector (brother):** Paris' relationship with his elder brother defines his character. Hector rebukes Paris with brutal honesty — "evil-hearted Paris, fair to see but woman-mad" (003-034) — yet never disowns him. Paris acknowledges Hector's superiority but never reforms. Their final interaction in Book 6, where Paris prepares to rejoin the battle after Hector's visit to the city, is the closest the brothers come to solidarity.

- **Helen (lover/wife):** Paris' union with Helen is the cause of the war, yet the Iliad depicts it as poisoned by shame. Helen despises Paris for his cowardice and wishes Menelaus had killed him (003-338), yet Aphrodite compels her to return to his bed. Paris himself shows no guilt — only an easy sensuality that renders him immune to the moral weight of the destruction he has caused.

- **Aphrodite (divine patron):** Paris owes his survival and his very identity to Aphrodite, who chose him as the most beautiful of mortals in the Judgment of Paris and repays him with continuous protection — snatching him from death at Menelaus' hands (003-256) and compelling Helen's compliance. Paris is less a free agent than Aphrodite's favourite, and his immunity from consequence is the direct expression of divine favouritism.

- **Menelaus (rival):** Paris wronged Menelaus by abducting his wife while a guest in his house — a violation of *xenia* (guest-friendship) that outrages gods and mortals alike. Their duel in Book 3 is a contest not of equals but of a warrior against a seducer with divine protection; Menelaus dominates, but Aphrodite denies him justice.

## Death or Fate

Paris does not die within the Iliad, but his death is foretold. Hector's dying prophecy — that "Paris and Phoebus Apollo" will kill Achilles at the Scaean Gate (022-316) — reminds the audience that Paris' bow, guided by the god of archery, will accomplish what the entire Trojan army could not. In the post-Iliadic tradition, Paris kills Achilles with an arrow to the heel, only to die later from a wound inflicted by Philoctetes' bow. Paris' death, like his life, is defined by the bow — the weapon of distance, detachment, and divine intervention — rather than the spear of the front-rank warrior. He is both the destroyer of Troy (through his abduction of Helen) and the slayer of its greatest enemy, a paradox that defines his tragic role.

## Scholarly Notes

Paris' double name — Alexandrus/Paris — has generated extensive scholarly discussion. "Alexandrus" (Ἀλέξανδρος) means "defender of men" in Greek, an ironic name for the man who brought destruction upon his city. "Paris" may be of non-Greek (possibly Luwian) origin, reflecting the historical contact between the Hittite world of Anatolia and the Homeric tradition. That the Iliad uses both names interchangeably suggests an awareness of this dual identity.

Paris represents a distinctive Homeric type: the warrior whose excellence lies outside the traditional heroic code. Where Hector and Achilles win *kleos* (glory) through spear-combat, Paris' skill with the bow — a weapon sometimes coded as unmanly in Homeric epic — marks him as different. His beauty, his eroticism, and his reliance on Aphrodite rather than Ares all distance him from the heroic ideal, yet he is indispensable to the plot: without Paris, there is no war, and without Paris, there is no death of Achilles.

Butler's translation consistently uses "Alexandrus" for the character's Greek name and "Paris" for his Trojan name, preserving the Homeric convention. He renders Hector's rebuke — "evil-hearted Paris" — with a force that captures the Greek *Dyspari* ("ill-starred Paris"), a pun on the character's name that the original audience would have recognized.
