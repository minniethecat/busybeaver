# Phthians

The Phthians are the people of Phthia (Φθίη), a region in southern Thessaly described by Homer as the homeland of Peleus, his son Achilles, and the Myrmidon contingent. While the Myrmidons are the military force that Achilles leads at Troy, "Phthians" denotes the broader regional identity of these warriors and their homeland (002-552, 013-618).

## Identity

Phthia is characterised in the *Iliad* by the formulaic epithet "mother of sheep," evoking a pastoral landscape of fertile plains (009-392). Alongside Hellas — a region in the Spercheius valley — Phthia forms the territorial base of Peleus's kingdom. Achilles refers to "the Achaean women in Hellas and Phthia, daughters of kings" as potential brides, indicating the region's wealth and noble standing among the Greek polities (009-331). In the Catalogue of Ships, Homer records that "those of Phthia and Hellas the land of fair women" sailed under Achilles' command, noting that the contingent were "called Myrmidons, Hellenes, and Achaeans" (002-552–553).

## Leadership

The Phthians' military contingent, the Myrmidons, is commanded by Achilles, son of Peleus (001-100). Achilles' connection to Phthia is central to his identity and his grievance with Agamemnon: he repeatedly speaks of returning to Phthia, contrasting his peaceful homeland with the inglorious struggle at Troy (009-305). Phoenix, Achilles' tutor, recalls how Peleus "sent you from Phthia to Agamemnon" when Achilles was still young (009-362), and Nestor echoes this memory: "we came to the house of Peleus, I and Ulysses, and he sent you from Phthia to Agamemnon" (011-668).

In Achilles' absence from battle, command of the Phthian forces falls to Patroclus, who leads the Myrmidons in Achilles' armour (016-039). Other commanders include Medon, the bastard son of Oileus, who "stood in the van of the Phthians" alongside Podarces after Philoctetes was abandoned on Lemnos (013-607, 013-618).

## Role in the War

The Phthian contingent, fighting as the Myrmidons, plays a decisive role in the *Iliad's* narrative despite entering the main battle only in Book 16:

- **Books 1–15**: The Myrmidons remain encamped by their ships, following Achilles' withdrawal from the fighting after his quarrel with Agamemnon. Achilles sits idle "by the tents and ships of the Myrmidons" (009-160), and the Achaeans suffer grievously in their absence.
- **Book 9**: The Embassy to Achilles sees Odysseus, Phoenix, and Ajax come to the Myrmidon camp to persuade Achilles to return. Achilles refuses Agamemnon's gifts, famously declaring that he will "be in Phthia" within days (009-305), and that Peleus established him "on the borders of Phthia where I was chief ruler" (009-395).
- **Book 16**: Patroclus begs Achilles: "send me and the Myrmidons with me, if I may bring deliverance to the Danaans" (016-039). Achilles consents, and the Myrmidons enter the battle, turning the tide against the Trojans until Patroclus's death.
- **Book 19**: After reconciling with Agamemnon, Achilles speaks of his father Peleus, "who is now in Phthia weeping for his son" (019-256), and imagines bringing Neoptolemus back "to Phthia" after the war (019-235).

## Catalogue of Ships Reference

The Phthian contingent is listed in the Catalogue of Ships: "those of Phthia and Hellas the land of fair women, who were called Myrmidons, Hellenes, and Achaeans — of these Achilles was captain, with fifty ships" (002-552–553). The three names — Myrmidons, Hellenes, and Achaeans — applied to a single contingent suggest a layered regional identity within the broader Greek coalition.
