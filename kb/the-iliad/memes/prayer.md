# Prayer (Eukhē)

Prayer in the *Iliad* is not spontaneous outpouring but structured [[sacrifice|ritual]]. The Homeric prayer follows a three-part formula: the [[guest-friendship|suppliant]] invokes the god by name and epithet, reminds the god of past services rendered (sacrifices, temple-building), and then — and only then — makes the specific request. This structure is so consistent that it can be mapped across every book of the epic, from Chryses' appeal to Apollo in Book 1 to [[priam|Priam]]'s desperate [[supplication|entreaty]] before setting out to ransom [[hector|Hector]]'s body in Book 24. Prayer is a transaction: the mortal offers a claim of past piety, and the god is expected to reciprocate.

## Definition

*Eukhē* (εὐχή) in Homer denotes both the prayer itself and the [[oath|vow]] or boast that often accompanies it. The term bridges sacred and secular speech — a prayer is a form of *eukhē*, but so is a warrior's boast on the battlefield. What distinguishes prayer proper is its addressee (a god), its [[sacrifice|ritual]] form, and its expectation of reciprocity. The underlying logic is captured in the phrase that recurs throughout: "if ever I did you service in word or deed among the immortals, hear my prayer" (001-246). The god's favorable response is not grace freely given but a return on prior investment.

The prayer formula is consistent: (1) invocation by name and cult title, often with a gesture of reaching toward the god's dwelling place; (2) reminder of past *kharis* (favor, relationship) established through sacrifices and [[sacrifice|offering]]s; (3) the specific [[supplication|petition]]. When Chryses prays to Apollo in Book 1, he begins "Hear me, O god of the silver bow, that protectest Chryse and holy Cilla" — the invocation — then reminds Apollo of his faithful service, and finally asks for vengeance against the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] (001-200–001-204).

The poem opens and closes with answered prayer. In Book 1, the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] are being decimated by Apollo's plague because [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] dishonored Chryses (001-143). The remedy is prayer and sacrifice: Chryses prays for the plague to lift after his daughter is returned, and Apollo hears him (001-206). The [[achaeans|Achaeans]] follow with purification rites and [[sacrifice|hecatomb]]s — a communal act of prayer-through-sacrifice (001-206–001-207). Prayer is the mechanism by which the cosmic order, disrupted by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s arrogance, is restored.

[[achilles|Achilles]]' prayer to his mother is one of the epic's pivotal m[[prophecy|omen]]ts. Alone by the sea, weeping at his dishonor, he "raised his hands in prayer to his immortal mother" (001-118). Thetis answers, promising to [[supplication|petition]] Zeus. Her prayer to Zeus follows the classic form: she reminds him of her past service (the time she saved him from the other gods' conspiracy, 001-157), and then asks that the [[trojans|Trojans]] be granted victory until the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] honor her son (001-249). Zeus's nod — the solemn inclination of his head that shakes [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] (001-266) — is the divine ratification of a mortal prayer.

## Contrasts and Tensions

Prayer and [[supplication|Supplication (Hikesia)]] are closely related but distinct. Supplication involves physical contact — grasping the knees, touching the chin — and is directed at mortals as well as gods. Prayer, by contrast, uses only words and gesture (raised hands) and is directed exclusively at gods. A [[guest-friendship|suppliant]] may pray while performing supplication, as Thetis does when she clasps Zeus's knees and simultaneously makes her [[supplication|petition]] (001-253), but the two [[sacrifice|ritual]]s remain conceptually sep[[curse|ara]]te.

A deeper tension surrounds the reliability of prayer. The Homeric gods are not obligated to answer — Zeus almost refuses Thetis's prayer because granting it would provoke conflict with Hera (001-257). Prayer is necessary but not sufficient. Yet when a god has received sacrifices and honors from a mortal, the failure to answer is a breach of the divine economy. This transactional logic makes prayer a form of moral pressure on the gods — the [[achaeans|Achaeans]] expect Apollo to lift the plague because they have offered proper sacrifice (001-206).
