# Bow of Pandarus

The Bow of Pandarus is the instrument that reopens the Trojan War. In Book 4 of the *Iliad*, a [[oath|truce]] has been sworn: [[menelaus|Menelaus]] and [[paris|Alexandrus]] have fought their single combat, and the war appears on the verge of ending. Then [[athena|Athena]], acting on [[hera|Hera]]'s implacable hatred of Troy, descends to the plain and persuades Pandarus, son of Lycaon, to loose an arrow at Menelaus. The arrow flies, the [[oath|truce]] shatters, and the war that will consume countless lives roars back to life. More than any sword or spear in the epic, the bow of Pandarus is the weapon of a single decision — a single shot — that changes everything.

## Origin and Craftsmanship

The bow is described in unusual detail in Book 4, and its making is a small masterpiece of Homeric material culture:

| Component | Material | Detail |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Arms | Wild ibex horn | Pandarus himself killed the ibex, striking it from ambush on a crag; the horns measured sixteen palms in length |
| Joint | Polished and fitted | A master craftsman in horn-work shaped and joined the horns |
| Tips | Gold | Gold tips were fitted to each end for the bowstring |
| String | Ox-gut | The string was freshly fitted when Pandarus prepared to shoot |

The bow is a personal weapon in the deepest sense: Pandarus killed the ibex with his own hands, commissioned the bow from a skilled artisan, and strings it himself before the fateful shot. Every detail of its making is recounted by Homer, as if to emphasize that a weapon so carefully crafted should not have been used so recklessly.

## The Truce-Breaking Shot

The shooting of Menelaus in Book 4 is one of the most narratively consequential actions in the *Iliad*, and the bow is its agent. The sequence unfolds with grim precision:

1. **Athena's Persuasion**: Athena appears to Pandarus in the form of a Trojan spearman and tempts him with [[honor|glory]], promising that Paris will [[prize|reward]] him richly if he kills Menelaus (004-071)
2. **The Stringing**: Pandarus takes the bow from its case, strings it, and lays it carefully on the ground while his companions shield him with their shields (004-082, 004-088)
3. **The Shot**: Pandarus chooses an arrow, prays to [[apollo|Apollo]] (the archer-god), and lets fly. The arrow pierces Menelaus's belt, corslet, and skin — but Athena deflects it from a mortal wound (004-087)
4. **The Consequence**: Blood flows down Menelaus's thighs. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sees his wounded brother and knows the [[oath|truce]] is broken. The armies clash again, and the war resumes its full [[wrath|fury]].

The bow shot achieves its tactical purpose — Menelaus is wounded — but its strategic purpose fails: Menelaus lives, and the war that Pandarus hoped to end with one arrow instead consumes him. Pandarus will die himself in Book 5, killed by [[diomed|Diomedes]], the arrow's promise of [[honor|glory]] proving hollow.

## Significance

The Bow of Pandarus embodies several Homeric themes:

- **Individual Choice and Cosmic Consequence**: A single archer, a single shot, a single m[[prophecy|omen]]t of persuasion — and the entire course of the war changes. The bow reminds us that great events in the *Iliad* often turn on small, personal decisions.
- **Divine Manipulation**: Pandarus is not acting freely. Athena has disguised herself, flattered him, and promised [[prize|reward]]s she cannot deliver. The bow is a tool of divine will as much as human ambition — Pandarus pulls the string, but Athena guides the arrow.
- **The Fragility of Peace**: The [[oath|truce]] in Book 3 was solemnly sworn, with sacrifices to Zeus and oaths before both armies. One arrow is enough to destroy it. The bow represents the ease with which violence can overwhelm law, oaths, and the best intentions of mortals.

## Notable Passages

> "Then Pandarus son of Lycaon, the redoubtable spearman, was persuaded, and he took his bow from its case. This bow was made from the horns of a wild ibex which he had killed by a shot from ambush as it was leaping from a rock; the horns measured sixteen palms, and a worker in horn bound them together, polishing them well down and fitting them with golden tips."
> — The making of the bow described in detail, Book 4 (004-082)

> "Pandarus had strung his bow, he laid it carefully on the ground, and his brave followers held their shields before him lest the Achaeans should set upon him before he could shoot Menelaus."
> — Pandarus prepares the shot under cover, Book 4 (004-088)

> "Pandarus, where is now your bow, your winged arrows, and your fame as an archer? You are the best archer in all Troy, but the arrow cannot save you now."
> — Diomedes taunts the dying Pandarus, Book 5 (005-157)
