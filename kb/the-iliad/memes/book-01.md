# Book I

## Section 2

[001-001]And Minerva said, “I come from heaven, if you will hear me, to

[001-002]bid you stay your [[wrath|anger]]. Juno has sent me, who cares for both of

[001-003]you alike. Cease, then, this brawling, and do not draw your

[001-004]sword; rail at him if you will, and your railing will not be

[001-005]vain, for I tell you—and it shall surely be—that you shall

[001-006]hereafter receive gifts three times as splendid by reason of this

[001-007]present insult. Hold, therefore, and obey.”

[001-008]“Goddess,” answered [[achilles|Achilles]], “however angry a man may be, he

[001-009]must do as you two command him. This will be best, for the gods

[001-010]ever hear the prayers of him who has obeyed them.”

[001-011]He stayed his hand on the silver hilt of his sword, and thrust it

[001-012]back into the scabbard as Minerva bade him. Then she went back to

[001-013][[mount-olympus|Olympus]] among the other gods, and to the house of aegis-bearing

[001-014]Jove.

[001-015]But the son of Peleus again began railing at the son of Atreus,

[001-016]for he was still in a rage. “Wine-bibber,” he cried, “with the

[001-017]face of a dog and the heart of a hind, you never dare to go out

[001-018]with the [[guest-friendship|host]] in fight, nor yet with our chosen men in ambuscade.

[001-019]You shun this as you do death itself. You had rather go round and

[001-020]rob his prizes from any man who contradicts you. You devour your

[001-021]people, for you are king over a feeble folk; otherwise, son of

[001-022]Atreus, henceforward you would insult no man. Therefore I say,

[001-023]and [[oath|swear]] it with a great oath—nay, by this my sceptre which

[001-024]shalt sprout neither leaf nor shoot, nor bud anew from the day on

[001-025]which it left its parent stem upon the mountains—for the axe

[001-026]stripped it of leaf and bark, and now the sons of the Achaeans

[001-027]bear it as judges and guardians of the decrees of heaven—so

[001-028]surely and solemnly do I swear that hereafter they shall look

[001-029]fondly for Achilles and shall not find him. In the day of your

[001-030]distress, when your men fall dying by the murderous hand of

[001-031][[hector|Hector]], you shall not know how to help them, and shall rend your

[001-032]heart with rage for the hour when you offered insult to the

[001-033]bravest of the Achaeans.”

[001-034]With this the son of Peleus dashed his gold-bestudded sceptre on

[001-035]the ground and took his seat, while the son of Atreus was

[001-036]beginning fiercely from his place upon the other side. Then

[001-037]uprose smooth-tongued [[nestor|Nestor]], the facile speaker of the Pylians,

[001-038]and the words fell from his lips sweeter than honey. Two

[001-039]generations of men born and bred in Pylos had passed away under

[001-040]his rule, and he was now reigning over the third. With all

[001-041]sincerity and goodwill, therefore, he addressed them thus:—

[001-042]“Of a truth,” he said, “a great [[grief|sorrow]] has befallen the Achaean

[001-043]land. Surely [[priam|Priam]] with his sons would rejoice, and the Trojans

[001-044]be glad at heart if they could hear this quarrel between you two,

[001-045]who are so excellent in fight and counsel. I am older than either

[001-046]of you; therefore be guided by me. Moreover I have been the

[001-047]familiar friend of men even greater than you are, and they did

[001-048]not disregard my counsels. Never again can I behold such men as

[001-049]Pirithous and Dryas shepherd of his people, or as Caeneus,

[001-050]Exadius, godlike Polyphemus, and Theseus son of Aegeus, peer of

[001-051]the immortals. These were the mightiest men ever born upon this

[001-052]earth: mightiest were they, and when they fought the fiercest

[001-053]tribes of mountain savages they utterly overthrew them. I came

[001-054]from distant Pylos, and went about among them, for they would

[001-055]have me come, and I fought as it was in me to do. Not a man now

[001-056]living could withstand them, but they heard my words, and were

[001-057]persuaded by them. So be it also with yourselves, for this is the

[001-058]more excellent way. Therefore, [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], though you be strong,

[001-059]take not this girl away, for the sons of the Achaeans have

[001-060]already given her to Achilles; and you, Achilles, strive not

[001-061]further with the king, for no man who by the grace of Jove wields

[001-062]a sceptre has like [[honor|honour]] with Agamemnon. You are strong, and

[001-063]have a goddess for your mother; but Agamemnon is stronger than

[001-064]you, for he has more people under him. Son of Atreus, check your

[001-065]anger, I implore you; end this quarrel with Achilles, who in the

[001-066]day of battle is a tower of strength to the Achaeans.”

[001-067]And Agamemnon answered, “Sir, all that you have said is true, but

[001-068]this fellow must needs become our lord and master: he must be

[001-069]lord of all, king of all, and captain of all, and this shall

[001-070]hardly be. Granted that the gods have made him a great warrior,

[001-071]have they also given him the right to speak with railing?”

[001-072]Achilles interrupted him. “I should be a mean coward,” he cried,

[001-073]“were I to give in to you in all things. Order other people

[001-074]about, not me, for I shall obey no longer. Furthermore I say—and

[001-075]lay my saying to your heart—I shall fight neither you nor any man

[001-076]about this girl, for those that take were those also that gave.

[001-077]But of all else that is at my ship you shall carry away nothing

[001-078]by force. Try, that others may see; if you do, my spear shall be

[001-079]reddened with your blood.”

[001-080]When they had quarrelled thus angrily, they rose, and broke up

[001-081]the [[counsel|assembly]] at the [[achaean-camp|ships]] of the Achaeans. The son of Peleus went

[001-082]back to his tents and ships with the son of Menoetius and his

[001-083]company, while Agamemnon drew a vessel into the water and chose a

[001-084]crew of twenty oarsmen. He escorted Chryseis on board and sent

[001-085]moreover a [[sacrifice|hecatomb]] for the god. And [[odysseus|Ulysses]] went as captain.

[001-086]These, then, went on board and sailed their ways over the sea.

[001-087]But the son of Atreus bade the people purify themselves; so they

[001-088]purified themselves and cast their filth into the sea. Then they

[001-089]offered hecatombs of bulls and goats without blemish on the

[001-090]sea-shore, and the smoke with the savour of their sacrifice rose

[001-091]curling up towards heaven.

[001-092]Thus did they busy themselves throughout the host. But Agamemnon

[001-093]did not forget the threat that he had made Achilles, and called

[001-094]his trusty messengers and squires Talthybius and Eurybates. “Go,”

[001-095]said he, “to the tent of Achilles, son of Peleus; take Briseis by

[001-096]the hand and bring her hither; if he will not give her I shall

[001-097]come with others and take her—which will press him harder.”

[001-098]He charged them straightly further and dismissed them, whereon

[001-099]they went their way sorrowfully by the seaside, till they came to

[001-100]the tents and ships of the Myrmidons. They found Achilles sitting

[001-101]by his tent and his ships, and ill-pleased he was when he beheld

[001-102]them. They stood fearfully and reverently before him, and never a

[001-103]word did they speak, but he knew them and said, “Welcome,

[001-104]heralds, messengers of gods and men; draw near; my quarrel is not

[001-105]with you but with Agamemnon who has sent you for the girl

[001-106]Briseis. Therefore, [[patroclus|Patroclus]], bring her and give her to them,

[001-107]but let them be witnesses by the blessed gods, by mortal men, and

[001-108]by the fierceness of Agamemnon’s anger, that if ever again there

[001-109]be need of me to save the people from ruin, they shall seek and

[001-110]they shall not find. Agamemnon is mad with rage and knows not how

[001-111]to look before and after that the Achaeans may fight by their

[001-112]ships in safety.”

[001-113]Patroclus did as his dear comrade had bidden him. He brought

[001-114]Briseis from the tent and gave her over to the heralds, who took

[001-115]her with them to the ships of the Achaeans—and the woman was loth

[001-116]to go. Then Achilles went all alone by the side of the hoar sea,

[001-117]weeping and looking out upon the boundless waste of waters. He

[001-118]raised his hands in prayer to his immortal mother, “Mother,” he

[001-119]cried, “you bore me doomed to live but for a little season;

[001-120]surely Jove, who thunders from Olympus, might have made that

[001-121]little glorious. It is not so. Agamemnon, son of Atreus, has done

[001-122]me [[shame|dishonour]], and has robbed me of my prize by force.”

[001-123]As he spoke he wept aloud, and his mother heard him where she was

[001-124]sitting in the depths of the sea hard by the old man her father.

[001-125]Forthwith she rose as it were a grey mist out of the waves, sat

[001-126]down before him as he stood weeping, caressed him with her hand,

[001-127]and said, “My son, why are you weeping? What is it that grieves

[001-128]you? Keep it not from me, but tell me, that we may know it

[001-129]together.”

[001-130]Achilles drew a deep sigh and said, “You know it; why tell you

[001-131]what you know well already? We went to Thebe the strong city of

[001-132]Eetion, sacked it, and brought hither the [[prize|spoil]]. The sons of the

[001-133]Achaeans shared it duly among themselves, and chose lovely

[001-134]Chryseis as the meed of Agamemnon; but Chryses, priest of Apollo,

[001-135]came to the ships of the Achaeans to free his daughter, and

[001-136]brought with him a great ransom: moreover he bore in his hand the

[001-137]sceptre of Apollo, wreathed with a [[supplication|suppliant]]’s wreath, and he

[001-138]besought the Achaeans, but most of all the two sons of Atreus who

[001-139]were their chiefs.

[001-140]“On this the rest of the Achaeans with one voice were for

[001-141]respecting the priest and taking the ransom that he offered; but

[001-142]not so Agamemnon, who spoke fiercely to him and sent him roughly

[001-143]away. So he went back in anger, and Apollo, who loved him dearly,

[001-144]heard his prayer. Then the god sent a deadly dart upon the

[001-145]Argives, and the people died thick on one another, for the arrows

[001-146]went everywhither among the wide host of the Achaeans. At last a

[001-147][[prophecy|seer]] in the fulness of his knowledge declared to us the oracles

[001-148]of Apollo, and I was myself first to say that we should appease

[001-149]him. Whereon the son of Atreus rose in anger, and threatened that

[001-150]which he has since done. The Achaeans are now taking the girl in

[001-151]a ship to Chryse, and sending gifts of sacrifice to the god; but

[001-152]the heralds have just taken from my tent the daughter of Briseus,

[001-153]whom the Achaeans had awarded to myself.

[001-154]“Help your brave son, therefore, if you are able. Go to Olympus,

[001-155]and if you have ever done him service in word or deed, implore

[001-156]the aid of Jove. Ofttimes in my father’s house have I heard you

[001-157]glory in that you alone of the immortals saved the son of Saturn

[001-158]from ruin, when the others, with Juno, Neptune, and Pallas

[001-159]Minerva would have put him in bonds. It was you, goddess, who

[001-160]delivered him by calling to Olympus the hundred-handed monster

[001-161]whom gods call Briareus, but men Aegaeon, for he is stronger even

[001-162]than his father; when therefore he took his seat all-glorious

[001-163]beside the son of Saturn, the other gods were afraid, and did not

[001-164]bind him. Go, then, to him, remind him of all this, clasp his

[001-165]knees, and bid him give succour to the Trojans. Let the Achaeans

[001-166]be hemmed in at the sterns of their ships, and perish on the

[001-167]sea-shore, that they may reap what joy they may of their king,

[001-168]and that Agamemnon may rue his blindness in offering insult to

[001-169]the foremost of the Achaeans.”

[001-170]Thetis wept and answered, “My son, woe is me that I should have

[001-171]borne or suckled you. Would indeed that you had lived your span

[001-172]free from all sorrow at your ships, for it is all too brief;

[001-173]alas, that you should be at once short of life and long of sorrow

[001-174]above your peers: woe, therefore, was the hour in which I bore

[001-175]you; nevertheless I will go to the snowy heights of Olympus, and

[001-176]tell this tale to Jove, if he will hear our prayer: meanwhile

[001-177]stay where you are with your ships, nurse your anger against the

[001-178]Achaeans, and hold aloof from fight. For Jove went yesterday to

[001-179]Oceanus, to a feast among the Ethiopians, and the other gods went

[001-180]with him. He will return to Olympus twelve days hence; I will

[001-181]then go to his mansion paved with bronze and will beseech him;

[001-182]nor do I doubt that I shall be able to persuade him.”

[001-183]On this she left him, still furious at the loss of her that had

[001-184]been taken from him. Meanwhile Ulysses reached Chryse with the

[001-185]hecatomb. When they had come inside the harbour they furled the

[001-186]sails and laid them in the ship’s hold; they slackened the

[001-187]forestays, lowered the mast into its place, and rowed the ship to

[001-188]the place where they would have her lie; there they cast out

[001-189]their mooring-stones and made fast the hawsers. They then got out

[001-190]upon the sea-shore and landed the hecatomb for Apollo; Chryseis

[001-191]also left the ship, and Ulysses led her to the altar to deliver

[001-192]her into the hands of her father. “Chryses,” said he, “King

[001-193]Agamemnon has sent me to bring you back your child, and to offer

[001-194]sacrifice to Apollo on behalf of the Danaans, that we may

[001-195]propitiate the god, who has now brought sorrow upon the Argives.”

[001-196]So saying he gave the girl over to her father, who received her

[001-197]gladly, and they ranged the holy hecatomb all orderly round the

[001-198]altar of the god. They washed their hands and took up the

[001-199]barley-meal to sprinkle over the victims, while Chryses lifted up

[001-200]his hands and prayed aloud on their behalf. “Hear me,” he cried,

[001-201]“O god of the silver bow, that protectest Chryse and holy Cilla,

[001-202]and rulest Tenedos with thy might. Even as thou didst hear me

[001-203]aforetime when I prayed, and didst press hardly upon the

[001-204]Achaeans, so hear me yet again, and stay this fearful pestilence

[001-205]from the Danaans.”

[001-206]Thus did he pray, and Apollo heard his prayer. When they had done

[001-207]praying and sprinkling the barley-meal, they drew back the heads

[001-208]of the victims and killed and flayed them. They cut out the

[001-209]thigh-bones, wrapped them round in two layers of fat, set some

[001-210]pieces of raw meat on the top of them, and then Chryses laid them

[001-211]on the wood fire and poured wine over them, while the young men

[001-212]stood near him with five-pronged spits in their hands. When the

[001-213]thigh-bones were burned and they had tasted the inward meats,

[001-214]they cut the rest up small, put the pieces upon the spits,

[001-215]roasted them till they were done, and drew them off: then, when

[001-216]they had finished their work and the feast was ready, they ate

[001-217]it, and every man had his full share, so that all were satisfied.

[001-218]As soon as they had had enough to eat and drink, pages filled the

[001-219]mixing-bowl with wine and water and handed it round, after giving

[001-220]every man his drink-offering.

[001-221]Thus all day long the young men worshipped the god with song,

[001-222]hymning him and chaunting the joyous paean, and the god took

[001-223]pleasure in their voices; but when the sun went down, and it came

[001-224]on dark, they laid themselves down to sleep by the stern cables

[001-225]of the ship, and when the child of morning, rosy-fingered Dawn,

[001-226]appeared they again set sail for the host of the Achaeans. Apollo

[001-227]sent them a fair wind, so they raised their mast and hoisted

[001-228]their white sails aloft. As the sail bellied with the wind the

[001-229]ship flew through the deep blue water, and the foam hissed

[001-230]against her bows as she sped onward. When they reached the

[001-231]wide-stretching host of the Achaeans, they drew the vessel

[001-232]ashore, high and dry upon the sands, set her strong props beneath

[001-233]her, and went their ways to their own tents and ships.

[001-234]But Achilles abode at his ships and nursed his anger. He went not

[001-235]to the honourable assembly, and sallied not forth to fight, but

[001-236]gnawed at his own heart, pining for battle and the war-cry.

[001-237]Now after twelve days the immortal gods came back in a body to

[001-238]Olympus, and Jove led the way. Thetis was not unmindful of the

[001-239]charge her son had laid upon her, so she rose from under the sea

[001-240]and went through great heaven with early morning to Olympus,

[001-241]where she found the mighty son of Saturn sitting all alone upon

[001-242]its topmost ridges. She sat herself down before him, and with her

[001-243]left hand seized his knees, while with her right she caught him

[001-244]under the chin, and besought him, saying:—

[001-245]“Father Jove, if I ever did you service in word or deed among the

[001-246]immortals, hear my prayer, and do honour to my son, whose life is

[001-247]to be cut short so early. King Agamemnon has dishonoured him by

[001-248]taking his prize and keeping her. Honour him then yourself,

[001-249]Olympian lord of counsel, and grant victory to the Trojans, till

[001-250]the Achaeans give my son his due and load him with riches in

[001-251]requital.”

[001-252]Jove sat for a while silent, and without a word, but Thetis still

[001-253]kept firm hold of his knees, and besought him a second time.

[001-254]“Incline your head,” said she, “and promise me surely, or else

[001-255]deny me—for you have nothing to fear—that I may learn how greatly

[001-256]you disdain me.”

[001-257]At this Jove was much troubled and answered, “I shall have

[001-258]trouble if you set me quarrelling with Juno, for she will provoke

[001-259]me with her taunting speeches; even now she is always railing at

[001-260]me before the other gods and accusing me of giving aid to the

[001-261]Trojans. Go back now, lest she should find out. I will consider

[001-262]the matter, and will bring it about as you wish. See, I incline

[001-263]my head that you may believe me. This is the most solemn promise

[001-264]that I can give to any god. I never recall my word, or deceive,

[001-265]or fail to do what I say, when I have nodded my head.”

[001-266]As he spoke the son of Saturn bowed his dark brows, and the

[001-267]ambrosial locks swayed on his immortal head, till vast Olympus

[001-268]reeled.

[001-269]When the pair had thus laid their plans, they parted—Jove to his

[001-270]house, while the goddess quitted the splendour of Olympus, and

[001-271]plunged into the depths of the sea. The gods rose from their

[001-272]seats, before the coming of their sire. Not one of them dared to

[001-273]remain sitting, but all stood up as he came among them. There,

[001-274]then, he took his seat. But Juno, when she saw him, knew that he

[001-275]and the old merman’s daughter, silver-footed Thetis, had been

[001-276]hatching mischief, so she at once began to upbraid him.

[001-277]“Trickster,” she cried, “which of the gods have you been taking

[001-278]into your counsels now? You are always settling matters in secret

[001-279]behind my back, and have never yet told me, if you could help it,

[001-280]one word of your intentions.”

[001-281]“Juno,” replied the sire of gods and men, “you must not expect to

[001-282]be informed of all my counsels. You are my wife, but you would

[001-283]find it hard to understand them. When it is proper for you to

[001-284]hear, there is no one, god or man, who will be told sooner, but

[001-285]when I mean to keep a matter to myself, you must not pry nor ask

[001-286]questions.”

[001-287]“Dread son of Saturn,” answered Juno, “what are you talking

[001-288]about? I? Pry and ask questions? Never. I let you have your own

[001-289]way in everything. Still, I have a strong misgiving that the old

[001-290]merman’s daughter Thetis has been talking you over, for she was

[001-291]with you and had hold of your knees this self-same morning. I

[001-292]believe, therefore, that you have been promising her to give

[001-293]glory to Achilles, and to kill much people at the ships of the

[001-294]Achaeans.”

[001-295]“Wife,” said Jove, “I can do nothing but you suspect me and find

[001-296]it out. You will take nothing by it, for I shall only dislike you

[001-297]the more, and it will go harder with you. Granted that it is as

[001-298]you say; I mean to have it so; sit down and hold your tongue as I

[001-299]bid you for if I once begin to lay my hands about you, though all

[001-300]heaven were on your side it would profit you nothing.”

[001-301]On this Juno was frightened, so she curbed her stubborn will and

[001-302]sat down in silence. But the heavenly beings were disquieted

[001-303]throughout the house of Jove, till the cunning workman Vulcan

[001-304]began to try and pacify his mother Juno. “It will be

[001-305]intolerable,” said he, “if you two fall to wrangling and setting

[001-306]heaven in an uproar about a pack of mortals. If such ill counsels

[001-307]are to prevail, we shall have no pleasure at our banquet. Let me

[001-308]then advise my mother—and she must herself know that it will be

[001-309]better—to make friends with my dear father Jove, lest he again

[001-310]scold her and disturb our feast. If the Olympian Thunderer wants

[001-311]to hurl us all from our seats, he can do so, for he is far the

[001-312]strongest, so give him fair words, and he will then soon be in a

[001-313]good humour with us.”

[001-314]As he spoke, he took a double cup of nectar, and placed it in his

[001-315]mother’s hand. “Cheer up, my dear mother,” said he, “and make the

[001-316]best of it. I love you dearly, and should be very sorry to see

[001-317]you get a thrashing; however grieved I might be, I could not

[001-318]help, for there is no standing against Jove. Once before when I

[001-319]was trying to help you, he caught me by the foot and flung me

[001-320]from the heavenly threshold. All day long from morn till eve, was

[001-321]I falling, till at sunset I came to ground in the island of

[001-322]Lemnos, and there I lay, with very little life left in me, till

[001-323]the Sintians came and tended me.”

[001-324]Juno smiled at this, and as she smiled she took the cup from her

[001-325]son’s hands. Then Vulcan drew sweet nectar from the mixing-bowl,

[001-326]and served it round among the gods, going from left to right; and

[001-327]the blessed gods laughed out a loud applause as they saw him

[001-328]bustling about the heavenly mansion.

[001-329]Thus through the livelong day to the going down of the sun they

[001-330]feasted, and every one had his full share, so that all were

[001-331]satisfied. Apollo struck his lyre, and the Muses lifted up their

[001-332]sweet voices, calling and answering one another. But when the

[001-333]sun’s glorious light had faded, they went home to bed, each in

[001-334]his own abode, which lame Vulcan with his consummate skill had

[001-335]fashioned for them. So Jove, the Olympian Lord of Thunder, hied

[001-336]him to the bed in which he always slept; and when he had got on

[001-337]to it he went to sleep, with Juno of the golden throne by his

[001-338]side.
