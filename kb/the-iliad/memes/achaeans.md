# Achaeans

The Achaeans (Ἀχαιοί) — also called Argives, Danaans, and Hellenes in Homer's formulaic language — are the Greek coalition that has besieged [[troy|Troy]] for nine years before the *Iliad* begins. They are not a unified nation but a loose federation of independent kingdoms, each ruled by its own king (basileus), bound together by oath to recover [[helen|Helen]] and sack Troy. This coalition is the poem's laboratory for political life: every crisis in the *Iliad* — from [[the-quarrel|Achilles' quarrel with Agamemnon]] to the near-rout of Book 15 — turns on the tension between individual aristocratic honour and the collective good of the expedition.

## Composition

The Achaean force is catalogued in detail in Book 2 (the "Catalogue of Ships"), which lists twenty-nine contingents from across the Greek world:

| Region / Kingdom | Leader | Ships | Notable Warriors |
|-----------------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Mycenae / Argos | Agamemnon | 100 | Agamemnon |
| Phthia / Hellas | Achilles | 50 | Achilles, Patroclus, the Myrmidons |
| Sparta | Menelaus | 60 | Menelaus |
| Pylos | Nestor | 90 | Nestor, Antilochus |
| Crete | Idomeneus | 80 | Idomeneus, Meriones |
| Ithaca | Odysseus | 12 | Odysseus |
| Salamis | Ajax the Greater | 12 | Ajax, Teucer |
| Argos (Peloponnesian) | Diomedes | 80 | Diomedes, Sthenelus |

The total Achaean force numbered over 100,000 men in 1,186 ships — a scale that Homer emphasizes to establish the magnitude of the war and the commensurate glory of those who fight in it.

## Leadership and Command Structure

The Achaean command structure is the poem's central political problem:

| Role | Holder | Authority Basis |
|------|--------|----------------|
| Supreme Commander | Agamemnon | Commands the largest contingent; holds the ancestral sceptre |
| Best Warrior | Achilles | Personal excellence (arete); commands the elite Myrmidons |
| Elder Statesman | Nestor | Age, wisdom, and rhetorical skill — commands by counsel, not force |
| Tactical Genius | Odysseus | Cunning (metis); trusted with the most delicate missions |
| Strongest after Achilles | Ajax the Greater | Sheer physical strength; the defensive bulwark of the army |
| Premier Young Warrior | Diomedes | Excellence in battle; distinguished in Books 5–6 |

The conflict between Agamemnon's positional authority and Achilles' merit-based claim to honour is the *Iliad*'s animating political tension. Agamemnon commands by rank; Achilles excels by nature. The poem refuses to resolve which claim is superior — instead, it shows the catastrophic cost of their collision.

## Role in the Epic

The Achaeans as a collective body serve several functions:

- **The Political Arena**: The assembly (agora) is where political speech, deliberation, and conflict occur. The Achaean camp is a polity in microcosm.
- **The Measure of Excellence**: Individual warriors achieve glory (kleos) by their performance in Achaean battle lines. The army is the audience before which heroic excellence is performed.
- **The Object of Divine Attention**: The gods intervene on behalf of or against the Achaeans throughout the poem — the army is the field on which divine wills compete.

## Notable Passages

> "Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus, that brought countless ills upon the Achaeans. Many a brave soul did it send hurrying down to Hades, and many a hero did it yield a prey to dogs and vultures..."
> — The poem's opening invocation, naming the Achaeans as the collective victim of Achilles' wrath, Book 1 (001-001)

> "And now the sons of the Achaeans..."
> — The formulaic phrase that recurs hundreds of times, binding individual actions into a collective identity
