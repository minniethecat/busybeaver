# Book XX

[020-001]BOOK XX.

[020-002]The gods hold a council and determine to watch the fight, from

[020-003]the hill Callicolone, and the barrow of Hercules—A fight between

[020-004][[achilles|Achilles]] and AEneas is interrupted by Neptune, who saves

[020-005]AEneas—Achilles kills many Trojans.

[020-006]Thus, then, did the Achaeans arm by their [[achaean-camp|ships]] round you, O son

[020-007]of Peleus, who were hungering for battle; while the Trojans over

[020-008]against them armed upon the rise of the plain.

[020-009]Meanwhile Jove from the top of many-delled [[mount-olympus|Olympus]], bade Themis

[020-010]gather the gods in council, whereon she went about and called

[020-011]them to the house of Jove. There was not a river absent except

[020-012]Oceanus, nor a single one of the nymphs that haunt fair groves,

[020-013]or springs of rivers and meadows of green grass. When they

[020-014]reached the house of cloud-compelling Jove, they took their seats

[020-015]in the arcades of polished marble which Vulcan with his

[020-016]consummate skill had made for father Jove.

[020-017]In such wise, therefore, did they gather in the house of Jove.

[020-018]Neptune also, lord of the earthquake, obeyed the call of the

[020-019]goddess, and came up out of the sea to join them. There, sitting

[020-020]in the midst of them, he asked what Jove’s purpose might be.

[020-021]“Why,” said he, “wielder of the lightning, have you called the

[020-022]gods in council? Are you considering some matter that concerns

[020-023]the Trojans and Achaeans—for the blaze of battle is on the point

[020-024]of being kindled between them?”

[020-025]And Jove answered, “You know my purpose, shaker of earth, and

[020-026]wherefore I have called you hither. I take thought for them even

[020-027]in their destruction. For my own part I shall stay here seated on

[020-028]Mt. Olympus and look on in peace, but do you others go about

[020-029]among Trojans and Achaeans, and help either side as you may be

[020-030]severally disposed. If Achilles fights the Trojans without

[020-031]hindrance they will make no stand against him; they have ever

[020-032]trembled at the sight of him, and now that he is roused to such

[020-033][[wrath|fury]] about his comrade, he will override fate itself and storm

[020-034]their city.”

[020-035]Thus spoke Jove and gave the word for war, whereon the gods took

[020-036]their several sides and went into battle. Juno, Pallas Minerva,

[020-037]earth-encircling Neptune, Mercury bringer of good luck and

[020-038]excellent in all cunning—all these joined the [[guest-friendship|host]] that came from

[020-039]the ships; with them also came Vulcan in all his [[honor|glory]], limping,

[020-040]but yet with his thin legs plying lustily under him. Mars of

[020-041]gleaming helmet joined the Trojans, and with him Apollo of locks

[020-042]unshorn, and the archer goddess Diana, Leto, [[scamander|Xanthus]], and

[020-043]laughter-loving Venus.

[020-044]So long as the gods held themselves aloof from mortal warriors

[020-045]the Achaeans were triumphant, for Achilles who had long refused

[020-046]to fight was now with them. There was not a Trojan but his limbs

[020-047]failed him for fear as he beheld the fleet son of Peleus all

[020-048]glorious in his armour, and looking like Mars himself. When,

[020-049]however, the Olympians came to take their part among men,

[020-050]forthwith uprose strong Strife, rouser of hosts, and Minerva

[020-051]raised her loud voice, now standing by the deep trench that ran

[020-052]outside the wall, and now shouting with all her might upon the

[020-053]shore of the sounding sea. Mars also bellowed out upon the other

[020-054]side, dark as some black thunder-cloud, and called on the Trojans

[020-055]at the top of his voice, now from the acropolis, and now speeding

[020-056]up the side of the river Simois till he came to the hill

[020-057]Callicolone.

[020-058]Thus did the gods spur on both hosts to fight, and rouse fierce

[020-059]contention also among themselves. The sire of gods and men

[020-060]thundered from heaven above, while from beneath Neptune shook the

[020-061]vast earth, and bade the high hills tremble. The spurs and crests

[020-062]of many-fountained [[mount-ida|Ida]] quaked, as also the city of the Trojans

[020-063]and the ships of the Achaeans. Hades, king of the realms below,

[020-064]was struck with fear; he sprang panic-stricken from his throne

[020-065]and cried aloud in terror lest Neptune, lord of the earthquake,

[020-066]should crack the ground over his head, and lay bare his mouldy

[020-067]mansions to the sight of mortals and immortals—mansions so

[020-068]ghastly grim that even the gods shudder to think of them. Such

[020-069]was the uproar as the gods came together in battle. Apollo with

[020-070]his arrows took his stand to face King Neptune, while Minerva

[020-071]took hers against the god of war; the archer goddess Diana with

[020-072]her golden arrows, sister of far-darting Apollo, stood to face

[020-073]Juno; Mercury the lusty bringer of good luck faced Leto, while

[020-074]the mighty eddying river whom men can Scamander, but gods

[020-075]Xanthus, matched himself against Vulcan.

[020-076]The gods, then, were thus ranged against one another. But the

[020-077]heart of Achilles was set on meeting [[hector|Hector]] son of [[priam|Priam]], for it

[020-078]was with his blood that he longed above all things else to glut

[020-079]the stubborn lord of battle. Meanwhile Apollo set Aeneas on to

[020-080]attack the son of Peleus, and put courage into his heart,

[020-081]speaking with the voice of Lycaon son of Priam. In his likeness

[020-082]therefore, he said to Aeneas, “Aeneas, counsellor of the Trojans,

[020-083]where are now the brave words with which you vaunted over your

[020-084]wine before the Trojan princes, saying that you would fight

[020-085]Achilles son of Peleus in single combat?”

[020-086]And Aeneas answered, “Why do you thus bid me fight the proud son

[020-087]of Peleus, when I am in no mind to do so? Were I to face him now,

[020-088]it would not be for the first time. His spear has already put me

[020-089]to flight from Ida, when he attacked our cattle and sacked

[020-090]Lyrnessus and Pedasus; Jove indeed saved me in that he vouchsafed

[020-091]me strength to fly, else had I fallen by the hands of Achilles

[020-092]and Minerva, who went before him to protect him and urged him to

[020-093]fall upon the Lelegae and Trojans. No man may fight Achilles, for

[020-094]one of the gods is always with him as his guardian angel, and

[020-095]even were it not so, his weapon flies ever straight, and fails

[020-096]not to pierce the flesh of him who is against him; if heaven

[020-097]would let me fight him on even terms he should not soon overcome

[020-098]me, though he boasts that he is made of bronze.”

[020-099]Then said King Apollo, son to Jove, “Nay, hero, pray to the

[020-100]ever-living gods, for men say that you were born of Jove’s

[020-101]daughter Venus, whereas Achilles is son to a goddess of inferior

[020-102]rank. Venus is child to Jove, while Thetis is but daughter to the

[020-103]old man of the sea. Bring, therefore, your spear to bear upon

[020-104]him, and let him not scare you with his taunts and menaces.”

[020-105]As he spoke he put courage into the heart of the shepherd of his

[020-106]people, and he strode in full armour among the ranks of the

[020-107]foremost fighters. Nor did the son of Anchises escape the notice

[020-108]of white-armed Juno, as he went forth into the throng to meet

[020-109]Achilles. She called the gods about her, and said, “Look to it,

[020-110]you two, Neptune and Minerva, and consider how this shall be;

[020-111]Phoebus Apollo has been sending Aeneas clad in full armour to

[020-112]fight Achilles. Shall we turn him back at once, or shall one of

[020-113]us stand by Achilles and endow him with strength so that his

[020-114]heart fail not, and he may learn that the chiefs of the immortals

[020-115]are on his side, while the others who have all along been

[020-116]defending the Trojans are but vain helpers? Let us all come down

[020-117]from Olympus and join in the fight, that this day he may take no

[020-118]hurt at the hands of the Trojans. Hereafter let him suffer

[020-119]whatever fate may have spun out for him when he was begotten and

[020-120]his mother bore him. If Achilles be not thus assured by the voice

[020-121]of a god, he may come to fear presently when one of us meets him

[020-122]in battle, for the gods are terrible if they are seen face to

[020-123]face.”

[020-124]Neptune lord of the earthquake answered her saying, “Juno,

[020-125]restrain your fury; it is not well; I am not in favour of forcing

[020-126]the other gods to fight us, for the advantage is too greatly on

[020-127]our own side; let us take our places on some hill out of the

[020-128]beaten track, and let mortals fight it out among themselves. If

[020-129]Mars or Phoebus Apollo begin fighting, or keep Achilles in check

[020-130]so that he cannot fight, we too, will at once raise the cry of

[020-131]battle, and in that case they will soon leave the field and go

[020-132]back vanquished to Olympus among the other gods.”

[020-133]With these words the dark-haired god led the way to the high

[020-134]earth-barrow of Hercules, built round solid masonry, and made by

[020-135]the Trojans and Pallas Minerva for him to fly to when the

[020-136]sea-monster was chasing him from the shore on to the plain. Here

[020-137]Neptune and those that were with him took their seats, wrapped in

[020-138]a thick cloud of darkness; but the other gods seated themselves

[020-139]on the brow of Callicolone round you, O Phoebus, and Mars the

[020-140]waster of cities.

[020-141]Thus did the gods sit apart and form their plans, but neither

[020-142]side was willing to begin battle with the other, and Jove from

[020-143]his seat on high was in command over them all. Meanwhile the

[020-144]whole plain was alive with men and horses, and blazing with the

[020-145]gleam of armour. The earth rang again under the tramp of their

[020-146]feet as they rushed towards each other, and two champions, by far

[020-147]the foremost of them all, met between the hosts to fight—to wit,

[020-148]Aeneas son of Anchises, and noble Achilles.

[020-149]Aeneas was first to stride forward in attack, his doughty helmet

[020-150]tossing defiance as he came on. He held his strong shield before

[020-151]his breast, and brandished his bronze spear. The son of Peleus

[020-152]from the other side sprang forth to meet him, like some fierce

[020-153]lion that the whole country-side has met to hunt and kill—at

[020-154]first he bodes no ill, but when some daring youth has struck him

[020-155]with a spear, he crouches openmouthed, his jaws foam, he roars

[020-156]with fury, he lashes his tail from side to side about his ribs

[020-157]and loins, and glares as he springs straight before him, to find

[020-158]out whether he is to slay, or be slain among the foremost of his

[020-159]foes—even with such fury did Achilles burn to spring upon Aeneas.

[020-160]When they were now close up with one another Achilles was first

[020-161]to speak. “Aeneas,” said he, “why do you stand thus out before

[020-162]the host to fight me? Is it that you hope to reign over the

[020-163]Trojans in the seat of Priam? Nay, though you kill me Priam will

[020-164]not hand his kingdom over to you. He is a man of sound judgement,

[020-165]and he has sons of his own. Or have the Trojans been allotting

[020-166]you a demesne of passing richness, fair with orchard lawns and

[020-167]corn lands, if you should slay me? This you shall hardly do. I

[020-168]have discomfited you once already. Have you forgotten how when

[020-169]you were alone I chased you from your herds helter-skelter down

[020-170]the slopes of Ida? You did not turn round to look behind you; you

[020-171]took refuge in Lyrnessus, but I attacked the city, and with the

[020-172]help of Minerva and father Jove I sacked it and carried its women

[020-173]into captivity, though Jove and the other gods rescued you. You

[020-174]think they will protect you now, but they will not do so;

[020-175]therefore I say go back into the host, and do not face me, or you

[020-176]will rue it. Even a fool may be wise after the event.”

[020-177]Then Aeneas answered, “Son of Peleus, think not that your words

[020-178]can scare me as though I were a child. I too, if I will, can brag

[020-179]and talk unseemly. We know one another’s race and parentage as

[020-180]matters of common [[glory|fame]], though neither have you ever seen my

[020-181]parents nor I yours. Men say that you are son to noble Peleus,

[020-182]and that your mother is Thetis, fair-haired daughter of the sea.

[020-183]I have noble Anchises for my father, and Venus for my mother; the

[020-184]parents of one or other of us shall this day mourn a son, for it

[020-185]will be more than silly talk that shall part us when the fight is

[020-186]over. Learn, then, my lineage if you will—and it is known to

[020-187]many.

[020-188]“In the beginning Dardanus was the son of Jove, and founded

[020-189]Dardania, for Ilius was not yet stablished on the plain for men

[020-190]to dwell in, and her people still abode on the spurs of

[020-191]many-fountained Ida. Dardanus had a son, king Erichthonius, who

[020-192]was wealthiest of all men living; he had three thousand mares

[020-193]that fed by the water-meadows, they and their foals with them.

[020-194]Boreas was enamoured of them as they were feeding, and covered

[020-195]them in the semblance of a dark-maned stallion. Twelve filly

[020-196]foals did they conceive and bear him, and these, as they sped

[020-197]over the rich plain, would go bounding on over the ripe ears of

[020-198]corn and not break them; or again when they would disport

[020-199]themselves on the broad back of Ocean they could gallop on the

[020-200]crest of a breaker. Erichthonius begat Tros, king of the Trojans,

[020-201]and Tros had three noble sons, Ilus, Assaracus, and Ganymede who

[020-202]was comeliest of mortal men; wherefore the gods carried him off

[020-203]to be Jove’s cup-bearer, for his beauty’s sake, that he might

[020-204]dwell among the immortals. Ilus begat Laomedon, and Laomedon

[020-205]begat Tithonus, Priam, Lampus, Clytius, and Hiketaon of the stock

[020-206]of Mars. But Assaracus was father to Capys, and Capys to

[020-207]Anchises, who was my father, while Hector is son to Priam.

[020-208]“Such do I declare my blood and lineage, but as for valour, Jove

[020-209]gives it or takes it as he will, for he is lord of all. And now

[020-210]let there be no more of this prating in mid-battle as though we

[020-211]were children. We could fling taunts without end at one another;

[020-212]a hundred-oared galley would not hold them. The tongue can run

[020-213]all whithers and talk all wise; it can go here and there, and as

[020-214]a man says, so shall he be gainsaid. What is the use of our

[020-215]bandying hard like women who when they fall foul of one another

[020-216]go out and wrangle in the streets, one half true and the other

[020-217]lies, as rage inspires them? No words of yours shall turn me now

[020-218]that I am fain to fight—therefore let us make trial of one

[020-219]another with our spears.”

[020-220]As he spoke he drove his spear at the great and terrible shield

[020-221]of Achilles, which rang out as the point struck it. The son of

[020-222]Peleus held the shield before him with his strong hand, and he

[020-223]was afraid, for he deemed that Aeneas’s spear would go through it

[020-224]quite easily, not reflecting that the god’s glorious gifts were

[020-225]little likely to yield before the blows of mortal men; and indeed

[020-226]Aeneas’s spear did not pierce the shield, for the layer of gold,

[020-227]gift of the god, stayed the point. It went through two layers,

[020-228]but the god had made the shield in five, two of bronze, the two

[020-229]innermost ones of tin, and one of gold; it was in this that the

[020-230]spear was stayed.

[020-231]Achilles in his turn threw, and struck the round shield of Aeneas

[020-232]at the very edge, where the bronze was thinnest; the spear of

[020-233]Pelian ash went clean through, and the shield rang under the

[020-234]blow; Aeneas was afraid, and crouched backwards, holding the

[020-235]shield away from him; the spear, however, flew over his back, and

[020-236]stuck quivering in the ground, after having gone through both

[020-237]circles of the sheltering shield. Aeneas though he had avoided

[020-238]the spear, stood still, blinded with fear and grief because the

[020-239]weapon had gone so near him; then Achilles sprang furiously upon

[020-240]him, with a cry as of death and with his keen blade drawn, and

[020-241]Aeneas seized a great stone, so huge that two men, as men now

[020-242]are, would be unable to lift it, but Aeneas wielded it quite

[020-243]easily.

[020-244]Aeneas would then have struck Achilles as he was springing

[020-245]towards him, either on the helmet, or on the shield that covered

[020-246]him, and Achilles would have closed with him and despatched him

[020-247]with his sword, had not Neptune lord of the earthquake been quick

[020-248]to mark, and said forthwith to the immortals, “Alas, I am sorry

[020-249]for great Aeneas, who will now go down to the house of Hades,

[020-250]vanquished by the son of Peleus. Fool that he was to give ear to

[020-251]the counsel of Apollo. Apollo will never save him from

[020-252]destruction. Why should this man suffer when he is guiltless, to

[020-253]no purpose, and in another’s quarrel? Has he not at all times

[020-254]offered acceptable sacrifice to the gods that dwell in heaven?

[020-255]Let us then snatch him from death’s jaws, lest the son of Saturn

[020-256]be angry should Achilles slay him. It is fated, moreover, that he

[020-257]should escape, and that the race of Dardanus, whom Jove loved

[020-258]above all the sons born to him of mortal women, shall not perish

[020-259]utterly without seed or [[prophecy|sign]]. For now indeed has Jove hated the

[020-260]blood of Priam, while Aeneas shall reign over the Trojans, he and

[020-261]his children’s children that shall be born hereafter.”

[020-262]Then answered Juno, “Earth-shaker, look to this matter yourself,

[020-263]and consider concerning Aeneas, whether you will save him, or

[020-264]suffer him, brave though he be, to fall by the hand of Achilles

[020-265]son of Peleus. For of a truth we two, I and Pallas Minerva, have

[020-266]sworn full many a time before all the immortals, that never would

[020-267]we shield Trojans from destruction, not even when all Troy is

[020-268]burning in the flames that the Achaeans shall kindle.”

[020-269]When earth-encircling Neptune heard this he went into the battle

[020-270]amid the clash of spears, and came to the place where Achilles

[020-271]and Aeneas were. Forthwith he shed a darkness before the eyes of

[020-272]the son of Peleus, drew the bronze-headed ashen spear from the

[020-273]shield of Aeneas, and laid it at the feet of Achilles. Then he

[020-274]lifted Aeneas on high from off the earth and hurried him away.

[020-275]Over the heads of many a band of warriors both horse and foot did

[020-276]he soar as the god’s hand sped him, till he came to the very

[020-277]fringe of the battle where the Cauconians were arming themselves

[020-278]for fight. Neptune, shaker of the earth, then came near to him

[020-279]and said, “Aeneas, what god has egged you on to this folly in

[020-280]fighting the son of Peleus, who is both a mightier man of valour

[020-281]and more beloved of heaven than you are? Give way before him

[020-282]whensoever you meet him, lest you go down to the house of Hades

[020-283]even though fate would have it otherwise. When Achilles is dead

[020-284]you may then fight among the foremost undaunted, for none other

[020-285]of the Achaeans shall slay you.”

[020-286]The god left him when he had given him these instructions, and at

[020-287]once removed the darkness from before the eyes of Achilles, who

[020-288]opened them wide indeed and said in great anger, “Alas! what

[020-289]marvel am I now beholding? Here is my spear upon the ground, but

[020-290]I see not him whom I meant to kill when I hurled it. Of a truth

[020-291]Aeneas also must be under heaven’s protection, although I had

[020-292]thought his boasting was idle. Let him go hang; he will be in no

[020-293]mood to fight me further, seeing how narrowly he has missed being

[020-294]killed. I will now give my orders to the Danaans and attack some

[020-295]other of the Trojans.”

[020-296]He sprang forward along the line and cheered his men on as he did

[020-297]so. “Let not the Trojans,” he cried, “keep you at arm’s length,

[020-298]Achaeans, but go for them and fight them man for man. However

[020-299]valiant I may be, I cannot give chase to so many and fight all of

[020-300]them. Even Mars, who is an immortal, or Minerva, would shrink

[020-301]from flinging himself into the jaws of such a fight and laying

[020-302]about him; nevertheless, so far as in me lies I will show no

[020-303]slackness of hand or foot nor want of endurance, not even for a

[020-304]moment; I will utterly break their ranks, and woe to the Trojan

[020-305]who shall venture within reach of my spear.”

[020-306]Thus did he exhort them. Meanwhile Hector called upon the Trojans

[020-307]and declared that he would fight Achilles. “Be not afraid, proud

[020-308]Trojans,” said he, “to face the son of Peleus; I could fight gods

[020-309]myself if the battle were one of words only, but they would be

[020-310]more than a match for me, if we had to use our spears. Even so

[020-311]the deed of Achilles will fall somewhat short of his word; he

[020-312]will do in part, and the other part he will clip short. I will go

[020-313]up against him though his hands be as fire—though his hands be

[020-314]fire and his strength iron.”

[020-315]Thus urged the Trojans lifted up their spears against the

[020-316]Achaeans, and raised the cry of battle as they flung themselves

[020-317]into the midst of their ranks. But Phoebus Apollo came up to

[020-318]Hector and said, “Hector, on no account must you challenge

[020-319]Achilles to single combat; keep a look-out for him while you are

[020-320]under cover of the others and away from the thick of the fight,

[020-321]otherwise he will either hit you with a spear or cut you down at

[020-322]close quarters.”

[020-323]Thus he spoke, and Hector drew back within the crowd, for he was

[020-324]afraid when he heard what the god had said to him. Achilles then

[020-325]sprang upon the Trojans with a terrible cry, clothed in valour as

[020-326]with a garment. First he killed Iphition son of Otrynteus, a

[020-327]leader of much people whom a naiad nymph had borne to Otrynteus

[020-328]waster of cities, in the land of Hyde under the snowy heights of

[020-329]Mt. Tmolus. Achilles struck him full on the head as he was coming

[020-330]on towards him, and split it clean in two; whereon he fell

[020-332]be low, son of Otrynteus, mighty hero; your death is here, but

[020-333]your lineage is on the Gygaean lake where your father’s estate

[020-334]lies, by Hyllus, rich in fish, and the eddying waters of Hermus.”

[020-335]Thus did he vaunt, but darkness closed the eyes of the other. The

[020-336]chariots of the Achaeans cut him up as their wheels passed over

[020-337]him in the front of the battle, and after him Achilles killed

[020-338]Demoleon, a valiant man of war and son to Antenor. He struck him

[020-339]on the temple through his bronze-cheeked helmet. The helmet did

[020-340]not stay the spear, but it went right on, crushing the bone so

[020-341]that the brain inside was shed in all directions, and his lust of

[020-342]fighting was ended. Then he struck Hippodamas in the midriff as

[020-343]he was springing down from his chariot in front of him, and

[020-344]trying to escape. He breathed his last, bellowing like a bull

[020-345]bellows when young men are dragging him to offer him in sacrifice

[020-346]to the King of Helice, and the heart of the earth-shaker is glad;

[020-347]even so did he bellow as he lay dying. Achilles then went in

[020-348]pursuit of Polydorus son of Priam, whom his father had always

[020-349]forbidden to fight because he was the youngest of his sons, the

[020-350]one he loved best, and the fastest runner. He, in his folly and

[020-351]showing off the fleetness of his feet, was rushing about among

[020-352]front ranks until he lost his life, for Achilles struck him in

[020-353]the middle of the back as he was darting past him: he struck him

[020-354]just at the golden fastenings of his belt and where the two

[020-355]pieces of the double breastplate overlapped. The point of the

[020-356]spear pierced him through and came out by the navel, whereon he

[020-357]fell groaning on to his knees and a cloud of darkness

[020-359]When Hector saw his brother Polydorus with his entrails in his

[020-360]hands and sinking down upon the ground, a mist came over his

[020-361]eyes, and he could not bear to keep longer at a distance; he

[020-362]therefore poised his spear and darted towards Achilles like a

[020-363]flame of fire. When Achilles saw him he bounded forward and

[020-364]vaunted saying, “This is he that has wounded my heart most deeply

[020-365]and has slain my beloved comrade. Not for long shall we two quail

[020-366]before one another on the highways of war.”

[020-367]He looked fiercely on Hector and said, “Draw near, that you may

[020-368]meet your [[fate|doom]] the sooner.” Hector feared him not and answered,

[020-369]“Son of Peleus, think not that your words can scare me as though

[020-370]I were a child; I too if I will can brag and talk unseemly; I

[020-371]know that you are a mighty warrior, mightier by far than I,

[020-372]nevertheless the issue lies in the lap of heaven whether I, worse

[020-373]man though I be, may not slay you with my spear, for this too has

[020-374]been found keen ere now.”

[020-375]He hurled his spear as he spoke, but Minerva breathed upon it,

[020-376]and though she breathed but very lightly she turned it back from

[020-377]going towards Achilles, so that it returned to Hector and lay at

[020-378]his feet in front of him. Achilles then sprang furiously on him

[020-379]with a loud cry, bent on killing him, but Apollo caught him up

[020-380]easily as a god can, and hid him in a thick darkness. Thrice did

[020-381]Achilles spring towards him spear in hand, and thrice did he

[020-382]waste his blow upon the air. When he rushed forward for the

[020-383]fourth time as though he were a god, he shouted aloud saying,

[020-384]“Hound, this time too you have escaped death—but of a truth it

[020-385]came exceedingly near you. Phoebus Apollo, to whom it seems you

[020-386]pray before you go into battle, has again saved you; but if I too

[020-387]have any friend among the gods I will surely make an end of you

[020-388]when I come across you at some other time. Now, however, I will

[020-389]pursue and overtake other Trojans.”

[020-390]On this he struck Dryops with his spear, about the middle of his

[020-391]neck, and he fell headlong at his feet. There he let him lie and

[020-392]stayed Demouchus son of Philetor, a man both brave and of great

[020-393]stature, by hitting him on the knee with a spear; then he smote

[020-394]him with his sword and killed him. After this he sprang on

[020-395]Laogonus and Dardanus, sons of Bias, and threw them from their

[020-396]chariot, the one with a blow from a thrown spear, while the other

[020-397]he cut down in hand-to-hand fight. There was also Tros the son of

[020-398]Alastor—he came up to Achilles and clasped his knees in the hope

[020-399]that he would spare him and not kill him but let him go, because

[020-400]they were both of the same age. Fool, he might have known that he

[020-401]should not prevail with him, for the man was in no mood for pity

[020-402]or forbearance but was in grim earnest. Therefore when Tros laid

[020-403]hold of his knees and sought a hearing for his prayers, Achilles

[020-404]drove his sword into his liver, and the liver came rolling out,

[020-405]while his bosom was all covered with the black blood that welled

[020-406]from the wound. Thus did death close his eyes as he lay lifeless.

[020-407]Achilles then went up to Mulius and struck him on the ear with a

[020-408]spear, and the bronze spear-head came right out at the other ear.

[020-409]He also struck Echeclus son of Agenor on the head with his sword,

[020-410]which became warm with the blood, while death and stern fate

[020-411]closed the eyes of Echeclus. Next in order the bronze point of

[020-412]his spear wounded Deucalion in the fore-arm where the sinews of

[020-413]the elbow are united, whereon he waited Achilles’ onset with his

[020-414]arm hanging down and death staring him in the face. Achilles cut

[020-415]his head off with a blow from his sword and flung it helmet and

[020-416]all away from him, and the marrow came oozing out of his backbone

[020-417]as he lay. He then went in pursuit of Rhigmus, noble son of

[020-418]Peires, who had come from fertile Thrace, and struck him through

[020-419]the middle with a spear which fixed itself in his belly, so that

[020-420]he fell headlong from his chariot. He also speared Areithous

[020-421]squire to Rhigmus in the back as he was turning his horses in

[020-422]flight, and thrust him from his chariot, while the horses were

[020-423]struck with panic.

[020-424]As a fire raging in some mountain glen after long drought—and the

[020-425]dense forest is in a blaze, while the wind carries great tongues

[020-426]of fire in every direction—even so furiously did Achilles rage,

[020-427]wielding his spear as though he were a god, and giving chase to

[020-428]those whom he would slay, till the dark earth ran with blood. Or

[020-429]as one who yokes broad-browed oxen that they may tread barley in

[020-430]a threshing-floor—and it is soon bruised small under the feet of

[020-431]the lowing cattle—even so did the horses of Achilles trample on

[020-432]the shields and bodies of the slain. The axle underneath and the

[020-433]railing that ran round the car were bespattered with clots of

[020-434]blood thrown up by the horses’ hoofs, and from the tyres of the

[020-435]wheels; but the son of Peleus pressed on to win still further

[020-436]glory, and his hands were bedrabbled with gore.
