# Book XVI

[016-001]BOOK XVI.

[016-002]Fire being now thrown on the ship of Protesilaus, [[patroclus|Patroclus]]

[016-003]fights in the armour of [[achilles|Achilles]]—He drives the Trojans back, but

[016-004]is in the end killed by Euphorbus and [[hector|Hector]].

[016-005]Thus did they fight about the ship of Protesilaus. Then Patroclus

[016-006]drew near to Achilles with tears welling from his eyes, as from

[016-007]some spring whose crystal stream falls over the ledges of a high

[016-008]precipice. When Achilles saw him thus weeping he was sorry for

[016-009]him and said, “Why, Patroclus, do you stand there weeping like

[016-010]some silly child that comes running to her mother, and begs to be

[016-011]taken up and carried—she catches hold of her mother’s dress to

[016-012]stay her though she is in a hurry, and looks tearfully up until

[016-013]her mother carries her—even such tears, Patroclus, are you now

[016-014]shedding. Have you anything to say to the Myrmidons or to myself?

[016-015]or have you had news from Phthia which you alone know? They tell

[016-016]me Menoetius son of Actor is still alive, as also Peleus son of

[016-017]Aeacus, among the Myrmidons—men whose loss we two should bitterly

[016-018]deplore; or are you grieving about the Argives and the way in

[016-019]which they are being killed at the [[achaean-camp|ships]], through their own

[016-020]high-handed doings? Do not hide anything from me but tell me that

[016-021]both of us may know about it.”

## Section 45

[016-022]Then, O knight Patroclus, with a deep sigh you answered,

[016-023]“Achilles, son of Peleus, foremost champion of the Achaeans, do

[016-024]not be angry, but I weep for the disaster that has now befallen

[016-025]the Argives. All those who have been their champions so far are

[016-026]lying at the ships, wounded by sword or spear. Brave Diomed son

[016-027]of Tydeus has been hit with a spear, while famed [[odysseus|Ulysses]] and

[016-028][[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] have received sword-wounds; Eurypylus again has been

[016-029]struck with an arrow in the thigh; skilled apothecaries are

[016-030]attending to these heroes, and healing them of their wounds; are

[016-031]you still, O Achilles, so inexorable? May it never be my lot to

[016-032]nurse such a passion as you have done, to the baning of your own

[016-033]good name. Who in future story will speak well of you unless you

[016-034]now save the Argives from ruin? You know no pity; knight Peleus

[016-035]was not your father nor Thetis your mother, but the grey sea bore

[016-036]you and the sheer cliffs begot you, so cruel and remorseless are

[016-037]you. If however you are kept back through knowledge of some

[016-038]oracle, or if your mother Thetis has told you something from the

[016-039]mouth of Jove, at least send me and the Myrmidons with me, if I

[016-040]may bring deliverance to the Danaans. Let me moreover wear your

[016-041]armour; the Trojans may thus mistake me for you and quit the

[016-042]field, so that the hard-pressed sons of the Achaeans may have

[016-043]breathing time—which while they are fighting may hardly be. We

[016-044]who are fresh might soon drive tired men back from our ships and

[016-045]tents to their own city.”

[016-046]He knew not what he was asking, nor that he was suing for his own

[016-047]destruction. Achilles was deeply moved and answered, “What, noble

[016-048]Patroclus, are you saying? I know no prophesyings which I am

[016-049]heeding, nor has my mother told me anything from the mouth of

[016-050]Jove, but I am cut to the very heart that one of my own rank

[016-051]should dare to rob me because he is more powerful than I am.

[016-052]This, after all that I have gone through, is more than I can

[016-053]endure. The girl whom the sons of the Achaeans chose for me, whom

[016-054]I won as the fruit of my spear on having sacked a city—her has

[016-055]King Agamemnon taken from me as though I were some common

[016-056]vagrant. Still, let bygones be bygones: no man may keep his [[wrath|anger]]

[016-057]for ever; I said I would not relent till battle and the cry of

[016-058]war had reached my own ships; nevertheless, now gird my armour

[016-059]about your shoulders, and lead the Myrmidons to battle, for the

[016-060]dark cloud of Trojans has burst furiously over our fleet; the

[016-061]Argives are driven back on to the beach, cooped within a narrow

[016-062]space, and the whole people of Troy has taken heart to sally out

[016-063]against them, because they see not the visor of my helmet

[016-064]gleaming near them. Had they seen this, there would not have been

[016-065]a creek nor grip that had not been filled with their dead as they

[016-066]fled back again. And so it would have been, if only King

[016-067]Agamemnon had dealt fairly by me. As it is the Trojans have beset

[016-068]our [[guest-friendship|host]]. Diomed son of Tydeus no longer wields his spear to

[016-069]defend the Danaans, neither have I heard the voice of the son of

[016-070]Atreus coming from his hated head, whereas that of murderous

[016-071]Hector rings in my cars as he gives orders to the Trojans, who

[016-072]triumph over the Achaeans and fill the whole plain with their cry

[016-073]of battle. But even so, Patroclus, fall upon them and save the

[016-074]fleet, lest the Trojans fire it and prevent us from being able to

[016-075]return. Do, however, as I now bid you, that you may win me great

[016-076][[honor|honour]] from all the Danaans, and that they may restore the girl

[016-077]to me again and give me rich gifts into the bargain. When you

[016-078]have driven the Trojans from the ships, come back again. Though

[016-079]Juno’s thundering husband should put triumph within your reach,

[016-080]do not fight the Trojans further in my absence, or you will rob

[016-081]me of glory that should be mine. And do not for lust of battle go

[016-082]on killing the Trojans nor lead the Achaeans on to Ilius, lest

[016-083]one of the ever-living gods from [[mount-olympus|Olympus]] attack you—for Phoebus

[016-084]Apollo loves them well: return when you have freed the ships from

[016-085]peril, and let others wage war upon the plain. Would, by father

[016-086]Jove, Minerva, and Apollo, that not a single man of all the

[016-087]Trojans might be left alive, nor yet of the Argives, but that we

[016-088]two might be alone left to tear aside the mantle that veils the

[016-089]brow of Troy.”

[016-090]Thus did they converse. But Ajax could no longer hold his ground

[016-091]for the shower of darts that rained upon him; the will of Jove

[016-092]and the javelins of the Trojans were too much for him; the helmet

[016-093]that gleamed about his temples rang with the continuous clatter

[016-094]of the missiles that kept pouring on to it and on to the

[016-095]cheek-pieces that protected his face. Moreover his left shoulder

[016-096]was tired with having held his shield so long, yet for all this,

[016-097]let fly at him as they would, they could not make him give

[016-098]ground. He could hardly draw his breath, the sweat rained from

[016-099]every pore of his body, he had not a moment’s respite, and on all

[016-100]sides he was beset by danger upon danger.

[016-101]And now, tell me, O Muses that hold your mansions on Olympus, how

[016-102]fire was thrown upon the ships of the Achaeans. Hector came close

[016-103]up and let drive with his great sword at the ashen spear of Ajax.

[016-104]He cut it clean in two just behind where the point was fastened

[016-105]on to the shaft of the spear. Ajax, therefore, had now nothing

[016-106]but a headless spear, while the bronze point flew some way off

[016-107]and came ringing down on to the ground. Ajax knew the hand of

[016-108]heaven in this, and was dismayed at seeing that Jove had now left

[016-109]him utterly defenceless and was willing victory for the Trojans.

[016-110]Therefore he drew back, and the Trojans flung fire upon the ship

[016-111]which was at once wrapped in flame.

[016-112]The fire was now flaring about the ship’s stern, whereon Achilles

[016-113]smote his two thighs and said to Patroclus, “Up, noble knight,

[016-114]for I see the glare of hostile fire at our fleet; up, lest they

[016-115]destroy our ships, and there be no way by which we may retreat.

[016-116]Gird on your armour at once while I call our people together.”

[016-117]As he spoke Patroclus put on his armour. First he greaved his

[016-118]legs with greaves of good make, and fitted with ancle-clasps of

[016-119]silver; after this he donned the cuirass of the son of Aeacus,

[016-120]richly inlaid and studded. He hung his silver-studded sword of

[016-121]bronze about his shoulders, and then his mighty shield. On his

[016-122]comely head he set his helmet, well wrought, with a crest of

[016-123]horse-hair that nodded menacingly above it. He grasped two

[016-124]redoubtable spears that suited his hands, but he did not take the

[016-125]spear of noble Achilles, so stout and strong, for none other of

[016-126]the Achaeans could wield it, though Achilles could do so easily.

[016-127]This was the ashen spear from Mount Pelion, which Chiron had cut

[016-128]upon a mountain top and had given to Peleus, wherewith to deal

[016-129]out death among heroes. He bade Automedon yoke his horses with

[016-130]all speed, for he was the man whom he held in honour next after

[016-131]Achilles, and on whose support in battle he could rely most

[016-132]firmly. Automedon therefore yoked the fleet horses [[scamander|Xanthus]] and

[016-133]Balius, steeds that could fly like the wind: these were they whom

[016-134]the harpy Podarge bore to the west wind, as she was grazing in a

[016-135]meadow by the waters of the river Oceanus. In the side traces he

[016-136]set the noble horse Pedasus, whom Achilles had brought away with

[016-137]him when he sacked the city of Eetion, and who, mortal steed

[016-138]though he was, could take his place along with those that were

[016-139]immortal.

[016-140]Meanwhile Achilles went about everywhere among the tents, and

[016-141]bade his Myrmidons put on their armour. Even as fierce ravening

[016-142]wolves that are feasting upon a homed stag which they have killed

[016-143]upon the mountains, and their jaws are red with blood—they go in

[016-144]a pack to lap water from the clear spring with their long thin

[016-145]tongues; and they reek of blood and slaughter; they know not what

[016-146]fear is, for it is hunger drives them—even so did the leaders and

[016-147]counsellors of the Myrmidons gather round the good squire of the

[016-148]fleet descendant of Aeacus, and among them stood Achilles himself

[016-149]cheering on both men and horses.

[016-150]Fifty ships had noble Achilles brought to Troy, and in each there

[016-151]was a crew of fifty oarsmen. Over these he set five captains whom

[016-152]he could trust, while he was himself commander over them all.

[016-153]Menesthius of the gleaming corslet, son to the river Spercheius

[016-154]that streams from heaven, was captain of the first company. Fair

[016-155]Polydora daughter of Peleus bore him to ever-flowing Spercheius—a

[016-156]woman mated with a god—but he was called son of Borus son of

[016-157]Perieres, with whom his mother was living as his wedded wife, and

[016-158]who gave great wealth to gain her. The second company was led by

[016-159]noble Eudorus, son to an unwedded woman. Polymele, daughter of

[016-160]Phylas the graceful dancer, bore him; the mighty slayer of Argos

[016-161]was enamoured of her as he saw her among the singing women at a

[016-162]dance held in honour of Diana the rushing huntress of the golden

[016-163]arrows; he therefore—Mercury, giver of all good—went with her

[016-164]into an upper chamber, and lay with her in secret, whereon she

[016-165]bore him a noble son Eudorus, singularly fleet of foot and in

[016-166]fight valiant. When Ilithuia goddess of the pains of childbirth

[016-167]brought him to the light of day, and he saw the face of the sun,

[016-168]mighty Echecles son of Actor took the mother to wife, and gave

[016-169]great wealth to gain her, but her father Phylas brought the child

[016-170]up, and took care of him, doting as fondly upon him as though he

[016-171]were his own son. The third company was led by Pisander son of

[016-172]Maemalus, the finest spearman among all the Myrmidons next to

[016-173]Achilles’ own comrade Patroclus. The old knight Phoenix was

[016-174]captain of the fourth company, and Alcimedon, noble son of

[016-175]Laerceus of the fifth.

[016-176]When Achilles had chosen his men and had stationed them all with

[016-177]their captains, he charged them straitly saying, “Myrmidons,

[016-178]remember your threats against the Trojans while you were at the

[016-179]ships in the time of my anger, and you were all complaining of

[016-180]me. ‘Cruel son of Peleus,’ you would say, ‘your mother must have

[016-181]suckled you on gall, so ruthless are you. You keep us here at the

[016-182]ships against our will; if you are so relentless it were better

[016-183]we went home over the sea.’ Often have you gathered and thus

[016-184]chided with me. The hour is now come for those high feats of arms

[016-185]that you have so long been pining for, therefore keep high hearts

[016-186]each one of you to do battle with the Trojans.”

[016-187]With these words he put heart and soul into them all, and they

[016-188]serried their companies yet more closely when they heard the

[016-189]words of their king. As the stones which a builder sets in the

[016-190]wall of some high house which is to give shelter from the

[016-191]winds—even so closely were the helmets and bossed shields set

[016-192]against one another. Shield pressed on shield, helm on helm, and

[016-193]man on man; so close were they that the horse-hair plumes on the

[016-194]gleaming ridges of their helmets touched each other as they bent

[016-195]their heads.

[016-196]In front of them all two men put on their armour—Patroclus and

[016-197]Automedon—two men, with but one mind to lead the Myrmidons. Then

[016-198]Achilles went inside his tent and opened the lid of the strong

[016-199]chest which silver-footed Thetis had given him to take on board

[016-200]ship, and which she had filled with shirts, cloaks to keep out

[016-201]the cold, and good thick rugs. In this chest he had a cup of rare

[016-202]workmanship, from which no man but himself might drink, nor would

[016-203]he make [[sacrifice|offering]] from it to any other god save only to father

[016-204]Jove. He took the cup from the chest and cleansed it with

[016-205]sulphur; this done he rinsed it clean water, and after he had

[016-206]washed his hands he drew wine. Then he stood in the middle of the

[016-207]court and prayed, looking towards heaven, and making his

[016-208]drink-offering of wine; nor was he unseen of Jove whose joy is in

[016-209]thunder. “King Jove,” he cried, “lord of Dodona, god of the

[016-210]Pelasgi, who dwellest afar, you who hold wintry Dodona in your

[016-211]sway, where your prophets the Selli dwell around you with their

[016-212]feet unwashed and their couches made upon the ground—if you heard

[016-213]me when I prayed to you aforetime, and did me honour while you

[016-214]sent disaster on the Achaeans, vouchsafe me now the fulfilment of

[016-215]yet this further prayer. I shall stay here where my ships are

[016-216]lying, but I shall send my comrade into battle at the head of

[016-217]many Myrmidons. Grant, O all-seeing Jove, that victory may go

[016-218]with him; put your courage into his heart that Hector may learn

[016-219]whether my squire is man enough to fight alone, or whether his

[016-220]might is only then so indomitable when I myself enter the turmoil

[016-221]of war. Afterwards when he has chased the fight and the cry of

[016-222]battle from the ships, grant that he may return unharmed, with

[016-223]his armour and his comrades, fighters in close combat.”

[016-224]Thus did he pray, and all-counselling Jove heard his prayer. Part

[016-225]of it he did indeed vouchsafe him—but not the whole. He granted

[016-226]that Patroclus should thrust back war and battle from the ships,

[016-227]but refused to let him come safely out of the fight.

[016-228]When he had made his drink-offering and had thus prayed, Achilles

[016-229]went inside his tent and put back the cup into his chest.

[016-230]Then he again came out, for he still loved to look upon the

[016-231]fierce fight that raged between the Trojans and Achaeans.

[016-232]Meanwhile the armed band that was about Patroclus marched on till

[016-233]they sprang high in hope upon the Trojans. They came swarming out

[016-234]like wasps whose nests are by the roadside, and whom silly

[016-235]children love to tease, whereon any one who happens to be passing

[016-236]may get stung—or again, if a wayfarer going along the road vexes

[016-237]them by accident, every wasp will come flying out in a fury to

[016-238]defend his little ones—even with such rage and courage did the

[016-239]Myrmidons swarm from their ships, and their cry of battle rose

[016-240]heavenwards. Patroclus called out to his men at the top of his

[016-241]voice, “Myrmidons, followers of Achilles son of Peleus, be men my

[016-242]friends, fight with might and with main, that we may win glory

[016-243]for the son of Peleus, who is far the foremost man at the ships

[016-244]of the Argives—he, and his close fighting followers. The son of

[016-245]Atreus King Agamemnon will thus learn his folly in showing no

[016-246][[shame|respect]] to the bravest of the Achaeans.”

[016-247]With these words he put heart and soul into them all, and they

[016-248]fell in a body upon the Trojans. The ships rang again with the

[016-249]cry which the Achaeans raised, and when the Trojans saw the brave

[016-250]son of Menoetius and his squire all gleaming in their armour,

[016-251]they were daunted and their battalions were thrown into

[016-252]confusion, for they thought the fleet son of Peleus must now have

[016-253]put aside his anger, and have been reconciled to Agamemnon; every

[016-254]one, therefore, looked round about to see whither he might fly

[016-255]for safety.

[016-256]Patroclus first aimed a spear into the middle of the press where

[016-257]men were packed most closely, by the stern of the ship of

[016-258]Protesilaus. He hit Pyraechmes who had led his Paeonian horsemen

[016-259]from the Amydon and the broad waters of the river Axius; the

[016-260]spear struck him on the right shoulder, and with a groan he fell

[016-261]backwards in the dust; on this his men were thrown into

[016-262]confusion, for by killing their leader, who was the finest

[016-263]soldier among them, Patroclus struck panic into them all. He thus

[016-264]drove them from the ship and quenched the fire that was then

[016-265]blazing—leaving the half-burnt ship to lie where it was. The

[016-266]Trojans were now driven back with a shout that rent the skies,

[016-267]while the Danaans poured after them from their ships, shouting

[016-268]also without ceasing. As when Jove, gatherer of the

[016-269]thunder-cloud, spreads a dense canopy on the top of some lofty

[016-270]mountain, and all the peaks, the jutting headlands, and forest

[016-271]glades show out in the great light that flashes from the bursting

[016-272]heavens, even so when the Danaans had now driven back the fire

[016-273]from their ships, they took breath for a little while; but the

[016-274]fury of the fight was not yet over, for the Trojans were not

[016-275]driven back in utter rout, but still gave battle, and were ousted

[016-276]from their ground only by sheer fighting.

[016-277]The fight then became more scattered, and the chieftains killed

[016-278]one another when and how they could. The valiant son of Menoetius

[016-279]first drove his spear into the thigh of Areilycus just as he was

[016-280]turning round; the point went clean through, and broke the bone

[016-281]so that he fell forward. Meanwhile Menelaus struck Thoas in the

[016-282]chest, where it was exposed near the rim of his shield, and he

[016-283]fell dead. The son of Phyleus saw Amphiclus about to attack him,

[016-284]and ere he could do so took aim at the upper part of his thigh,

[016-285]where the muscles are thicker than in any other part; the spear

[016-286]tore through all the sinews of the leg, and his eyes were closed

[016-287]in darkness. Of the sons of [[nestor|Nestor]] one, Antilochus, speared

[016-288]Atymnius, driving the point of the spear through his throat, and

[016-289]down he fell. Maris then sprang on Antilochus in hand-to-hand

[016-290]fight to avenge his brother, and bestrode the body spear in hand;

[016-291]but valiant Thrasymedes was too quick for him, and in a moment

[016-292]had struck him in the shoulder ere he could deal his blow; his

[016-293]aim was true, and the spear severed all the muscles at the root

[016-294]of his arm, and tore them right down to the bone, so he fell

[016-295]heavily to the ground and his eyes were closed in darkness. Thus

[016-296]did these two noble comrades of Sarpedon go down to Erebus slain

[016-297]by the two sons of Nestor; they were the warrior sons of

[016-298]Amisodorus, who had reared the invincible Chimaera, to the bane

[016-299]of many. Ajax son of Oileus sprang on Cleobulus and took him

[016-300]alive as he was entangled in the crush; but he killed him then

[016-301]and there by a sword-blow on the neck. The sword reeked with his

[016-302]blood, while dark death and the strong hand of fate gripped him

[016-303]and closed his eyes.

[016-304]Peneleos and Lycon now met in close fight, for they had missed

[016-305]each other with their spears. They had both thrown without

[016-306]effect, so now they drew their swords. Lycon struck the plumed

[016-307]crest of Peneleos’ helmet but his sword broke at the hilt, while

[016-308]Peneleos smote Lycon on the neck under the ear. The blade sank so

[016-309]deep that the head was held on by nothing but the skin, and there

[016-310]was no more life left in him. Meriones gave chase to Acamas on

[016-311]foot and caught him up just as he was about to mount his chariot;

[016-312]he drove a spear through his right shoulder so that he fell

[016-313]headlong from the car, and his eyes were closed in darkness.

[016-314]Idomeneus speared Erymas in the mouth; the bronze point of the

[016-315]spear went clean through it beneath the brain, crashing in among

[016-316]the white bones and smashing them up. His teeth were all of them

[016-317]knocked out and the blood came gushing in a stream from both his

[016-318]eyes; it also came gurgling up from his mouth and nostrils, and

[016-319]the darkness of death enfolded him round about.

[016-320]Thus did these chieftains of the Danaans each of them kill his

[016-321]man. As ravening wolves seize on kids or lambs, fastening on them

[016-322]when they are alone on the hillsides and have strayed from the

[016-323]main flock through the carelessness of the shepherd—and when the

[016-324]wolves see this they pounce upon them at once because they cannot

[016-325]defend themselves—even so did the Danaans now fall on the

[016-326]Trojans, who fled with ill-omened cries in their panic and had no

[016-327]more fight left in them.

[016-328]Meanwhile great Ajax kept on trying to drive a spear into Hector,

[016-329]but Hector was so skilful that he held his broad shoulders well

[016-330]under cover of his ox-hide shield, ever on the look-out for the

[016-331]whizzing of the arrows and the heavy thud of the spears. He well

[016-332]knew that the fortunes of the day had changed, but still stood

[016-333]his ground and tried to protect his comrades.

[016-334]As when a cloud goes up into heaven from Olympus, rising out of a

[016-335]clear sky when Jove is brewing a gale—even with such panic

[016-336]stricken rout did the Trojans now fly, and there was no order in

[016-337]their going. Hector’s fleet horses bore him and his armour out of

[016-338]the fight, and he left the Trojan host penned in by the deep

[016-339]trench against their will. Many a yoke of horses snapped the pole

[016-340]of their chariots in the trench and left their master’s car

[016-341]behind them. Patroclus gave chase, calling impetuously on the

[016-342]Danaans and full of fury against the Trojans, who, being now no

[016-343]longer in a body, filled all the ways with their cries of panic

[016-344]and rout; the air was darkened with the clouds of dust they

[016-345]raised, and the horses strained every nerve in their flight from

[016-346]the tents and ships towards the city.

[016-347]Patroclus kept on heading his horses wherever he saw most men

[016-348]flying in confusion, cheering on his men the while. Chariots were

[016-349]being smashed in all directions, and many a man came tumbling

[016-350]down from his own car to fall beneath the wheels of that of

[016-351]Patroclus, whose immortal steeds, given by the gods to Peleus,

[016-352]sprang over the trench at a bound as they sped onward. He was

[016-353]intent on trying to get near Hector, for he had set his heart on

[016-354]spearing him, but Hector’s horses were now hurrying him away. As

[016-355]the whole dark earth bows before some tempest on an autumn day

[016-356]when Jove rains his hardest to punish men for giving crooked

[016-357]judgement in their courts, and driving justice therefrom without

[016-358]heed to the decrees of heaven—all the rivers run full and the

## Section 49

[016-750]Then, as the life ebbed out of you, you answered, O knight

[016-751]Patroclus: “Hector, vaunt as you will, for Jove the son of Saturn

[016-752]and Apollo have vouchsafed you victory; it is they who have

[016-753]vanquished me so easily, and they who have stripped the armour

[016-754]from my shoulders; had twenty such men as you attacked me, all of

[016-755]them would have fallen before my spear. Fate and the son of Leto

[016-756]have overpowered me, and among mortal men Euphorbus; you are

[016-757]yourself third only in the killing of me. I say further, and lay

[016-758]my saying to your heart, you too shall live but for a little

[016-759]season; death and the day of your doom are close upon you, and

[016-760]they will lay you low by the hand of Achilles son of Aeacus.”

[016-761]When he had thus spoken his eyes were closed in death, his soul

[016-762]left his body and flitted down to the house of Hades, [[grief|mourning]]

[016-763]its sad fate and bidding farewell to the youth and vigor of its

[016-764]manhood. Dead though he was, Hector still spoke to him saying,

[016-765]“Patroclus, why should you thus foretell my doom? Who knows but

[016-766]Achilles, son of lovely Thetis, may be smitten by my spear and

[016-767]die before me?”

[016-768]As he spoke he drew the bronze spear from the wound, planting his

[016-769]foot upon the body, which he thrust off and let lie on its back.

[016-770]He then went spear in hand after Automedon, squire of the fleet

[016-771]descendant of Aeacus, for he longed to lay him low, but the

[016-772]immortal steeds which the gods had given as a rich gift to Peleus

[016-773]bore him swiftly from the field.
