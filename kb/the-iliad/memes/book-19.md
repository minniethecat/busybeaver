# Book XIX

[019-001]BOOK XIX.

[019-002][[achilles|Achilles]] is reconciled with [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], puts on the armour which

[019-003]Vulcan had made him, and goes out to fight.

[019-004]Now when Dawn in robe of saffron was hasting from the streams of

[019-005]Oceanus, to bring light to mortals and immortals, Thetis reached

[019-006]the [[achaean-camp|ships]] with the armour that the god had given her. She found

[019-007]her son fallen about the body of [[patroclus|Patroclus]] and weeping bitterly.

[019-008]Many also of his followers were weeping round him, but when the

[019-009]goddess came among them she clasped his hand in her own, saying,

[019-010]“My son, grieve as we may we must let this man lie, for it is by

[019-011]heaven’s will that he has fallen; now, therefore, accept from

[019-012]Vulcan this rich and goodly armour, which no man has ever yet

[019-013]borne upon his shoulders.”

[019-014]As she spoke she set the armour before Achilles, and it rang out

[019-015]bravely as she did so. The Myrmidons were struck with awe, and

[019-016]none dared look full at it, for they were afraid; but Achilles

[019-017]was roused to still greater [[wrath|fury]], and his eyes gleamed with a

[019-018]fierce light, for he was glad when he handled the splendid

[019-019]present which the god had made him. Then, as soon as he had

[019-020]satisfied himself with looking at it, he said to his mother,

[019-021]“Mother, the god has given me armour, meet handiwork for an

[019-022]immortal and such as no-one living could have fashioned; I will

[019-023]now arm, but I much fear that flies will settle upon the son of

[019-024]Menoetius and breed worms about his wounds, so that his body, now

[019-025]he is dead, will be disfigured and the flesh will rot.”

[019-026]Silver-footed Thetis answered, “My son, be not disquieted about

[019-027]this matter. I will find means to protect him from the swarms of

[019-028]noisome flies that prey on the bodies of men who have been killed

[019-029]in battle. He may lie for a whole year, and his flesh shall still

[019-030]be as sound as ever, or even sounder. Call, therefore, the

[019-031]Achaean heroes in [[counsel|assembly]]; unsay your anger against Agamemnon;

[019-032]arm at once, and fight with might and main.”

[019-033]As she spoke she put strength and courage into his heart, and she

[019-034]then dropped ambrosia and red nectar into the wounds of

[019-035]Patroclus, that his body might suffer no change.

[019-036]Then Achilles went out upon the sea-shore, and with a loud cry

[019-037]called on the Achaean heroes. On this even those who as yet had

[019-038]stayed always at the ships, the pilots and helmsmen, and even the

[019-039]stewards who were about the ships and served out rations, all

[019-040]came to the place of assembly because Achilles had shown himself

[019-041]after having held aloof so long from fighting. Two sons of Mars,

[019-042][[odysseus|Ulysses]] and the son of Tydeus, came limping, for their wounds

[019-043]still pained them; nevertheless they came, and took their seats

[019-044]in the front row of the assembly. Last of all came Agamemnon,

[019-045]king of men, he too wounded, for Coon son of Antenor had struck

[019-046]him with a spear in battle.

[019-047]When the Achaeans were got together Achilles rose and said, “Son

[019-048]of Atreus, surely it would have been better alike for both you

[019-049]and me, when we two were in such high anger about Briseis, surely

[019-050]it would have been better, had Diana’s arrow slain her at the

[019-051]ships on the day when I took her after having sacked Lyrnessus.

[019-052]For so, many an Achaean the less would have bitten dust before

[019-053]the foe in the days of my anger. It has been well for [[hector|Hector]] and

[019-054]the Trojans, but the Achaeans will long indeed remember our

[019-055]quarrel. Now, however, let it be, for it is over. If we have been

[019-056]angry, necessity has schooled our anger. I put it from me: I dare

[019-057]not nurse it for ever; therefore, bid the Achaeans arm forthwith

[019-058]that I may go out against the Trojans, and learn whether they

[019-059]will be in a mind to sleep by the ships or no. Glad, I ween, will

[019-060]he be to rest his knees who may fly my spear when I wield it.”

[019-061]Thus did he speak, and the Achaeans rejoiced in that he had put

[019-062]away his anger.

[019-063]Then Agamemnon spoke, rising in his place, and not going into the

[019-064]middle of the assembly. “Danaan heroes,” said he, “servants of

[019-065]Mars, it is well to listen when a man stands up to speak, and it

[019-066]is not seemly to interrupt him, or it will go hard even with a

[019-067]practised speaker. Who can either hear or speak in an uproar?

[019-068]Even the finest orator will be disconcerted by it. I will expound

[019-069]to the son of Peleus, and do you other Achaeans heed me and mark

[019-070]me well. Often have the Achaeans spoken to me of this matter and

[019-071]upbraided me, but it was not I that did it: Jove, and Fate, and

[019-072]Erinys that walks in darkness struck me mad when we were

[019-073]assembled on the day that I took from Achilles the [[prize|meed]] that had

[019-074]been awarded to him. What could I do? All things are in the hand

[019-075]of heaven, and Folly, eldest of Jove’s daughters, shuts men’s

[019-076]eyes to their destruction. She walks delicately, not on the solid

[019-077]earth, but hovers over the heads of men to make them stumble or

[019-078]to ensnare them.

[019-079]“Time was when she fooled Jove himself, who they say is greatest

[019-080]whether of gods or men; for Juno, woman though she was, beguiled

[019-081]him on the day when Alcmena was to bring forth mighty Hercules in

[019-082]the fair city of Thebes. He told it out among the gods saying,

[019-083]‘Hear me, all gods and goddesses, that I may speak even as I am

[019-084]minded; this day shall an Ilithuia, helper of women who are in

[019-085]labour, bring a man child into the world who shall be lord over

[019-086]all that dwell about him who are of my blood and lineage.’ Then

[019-087]said Juno all crafty and full of guile, ‘You will play false, and

[019-088]will not hold to your word. Swear me, O Olympian, [[oath|swear]] me a

[019-089]great oath, that he who shall this day fall between the feet of a

[019-090]woman, shall be lord over all that dwell about him who are of

[019-091]your blood and lineage.’

[019-092]“Thus she spoke, and Jove suspected her not, but swore the great

[019-093]oath, to his much ruing thereafter. For Juno darted down from the

[019-094]high summit of [[mount-olympus|Olympus]], and went in haste to Achaean Argos where

[019-095]she knew that the noble wife of Sthenelus son of Perseus then

[019-096]was. She being with child and in her seventh month, Juno brought

[019-097]the child to birth though there was a month still wanting, but

[019-098]she stayed the offspring of Alcmena, and kept back the Ilithuiae.

[019-099]Then she went to tell Jove the son of Saturn, and said, ‘Father

[019-100]Jove, lord of the lightning—I have a word for your ear. There is

[019-101]a fine child born this day, Eurystheus, son to Sthenelus the son

[019-102]of Perseus; he is of your lineage; it is well, therefore, that he

[019-103]should reign over the Argives.’

[019-104]“On this Jove was stung to the very quick, and in his rage he

[019-105]caught Folly by the hair, and swore a great oath that never

[019-106]should she again invade starry heaven and Olympus, for she was

[019-107]the bane of all. Then he whirled her round with a twist of his

[019-108]hand, and flung her down from heaven so that she fell on to the

[019-109]fields of mortal men; and he was ever angry with her when he saw

[019-110]his son groaning under the cruel labours that Eurystheus laid

[019-111]upon him. Even so did I grieve when mighty Hector was killing the

[019-112]Argives at their ships, and all the time I kept thinking of Folly

[019-113]who had so baned me. I was blind, and Jove robbed me of my

[019-114]reason; I will now make atonement, and will add much treasure by

[019-115]way of amends. Go, therefore, into battle, you and your people

[019-116]with you. I will give you all that Ulysses offered you yesterday

[019-117]in your tents: or if it so please you, wait, though you would

[019-118]fain fight at once, and my squires shall bring the gifts from my

[019-119]ship, that you may see whether what I give you is enough.”

[019-120]And Achilles answered, “Son of Atreus, king of men Agamemnon, you

[019-121]can give such gifts as you think proper, or you can withhold

[019-122]them: it is in your own hands. Let us now set battle in array; it

[019-123]is not well to tarry talking about trifles, for there is a deed

[019-124]which is as yet to do. Achilles shall again be seen fighting

[019-125]among the foremost, and laying low the ranks of the Trojans: bear

[019-126]this in mind each one of you when he is fighting.”

[019-127]Then Ulysses said, “Achilles, godlike and brave, send not the

[019-128]Achaeans thus against Ilius to fight the Trojans fasting, for the

[019-129]battle will be no brief one, when it is once begun, and heaven

[019-130]has filled both sides with fury; bid them first take food both

[019-131]bread and wine by the ships, for in this there is strength and

[019-132]stay. No man can do battle the livelong day to the going down of

[019-133]the sun if he is without food; however much he may want to fight

[019-134]his strength will fail him before he knows it; hunger and thirst

[019-135]will find him out, and his limbs will grow weary under him. But a

[019-136]man can fight all day if he is full fed with meat and wine; his

[019-137]heart beats high, and his strength will stay till he has routed

[019-138]all his foes; therefore, send the people away and bid them

[019-139]prepare their meal; King Agamemnon will bring out the gifts in

[019-140]presence of the assembly, that all may see them and you may be

[019-141]satisfied. Moreover let him swear an oath before the Argives that

[019-142]he has never gone up into the couch of Briseis, nor been with her

[019-143]after the manner of men and women; and do you, too, show yourself

[019-144]of a gracious mind; let Agamemnon entertain you in his tents with

[019-145]a feast of reconciliation, that so you may have had your dues in

[019-146]full. As for you, son of Atreus, treat people more righteously in

[019-147]future; it is no [[shame|disgrace]] even to a king that he should make

[019-148]amends if he was wrong in the first instance.”

[019-149]And King Agamemnon answered, “Son of Laertes, your words please

[019-150]me well, for throughout you have spoken wisely. I will swear as

[019-151]you would have me do; I do so of my own free will, neither shall

[019-152]I take the name of heaven in vain. Let, then, Achilles wait,

[019-153]though he would fain fight at once, and do you others wait also,

[019-154]till the gifts come from my tent and we ratify the oath with

[019-155]sacrifice. Thus, then, do I charge you: take some noble young

[019-156]Achaeans with you, and bring from my tents the gifts that I

[019-157]promised yesterday to Achilles, and bring the women also;

[019-158]furthermore let Talthybius find me a boar from those that are

[019-159]with the [[guest-friendship|host]], and make it ready for sacrifice to Jove and to the

[019-160]sun.”

[019-161]Then said Achilles, “Son of Atreus, king of men Agamemnon, see to

[019-162]these matters at some other season, when there is breathing time

[019-163]and when I am calmer. Would you have men eat while the bodies of

[019-164]those whom Hector son of [[priam|Priam]] slew are still lying mangled upon

[019-165]the plain? Let the sons of the Achaeans, say I, fight fasting and

[019-166]without food, till we have avenged them; afterwards at the going

[019-167]down of the sun let them eat their fill. As for me, Patroclus is

[019-168]lying dead in my tent, all hacked and hewn, with his feet to the

[019-169]door, and his comrades are [[grief|mourning]] round him. Therefore I can

[019-170]take thought of nothing save only slaughter and blood and the

[019-171]rattle in the throat of the dying.”

[019-172]Ulysses answered, “Achilles, son of Peleus, mightiest of all the

[019-173]Achaeans, in battle you are better than I, and that more than a

[019-174]little, but in counsel I am much before you, for I am older and

[019-175]of greater knowledge. Therefore be patient under my words.

[019-176]Fighting is a thing of which men soon surfeit, and when Jove, who

[019-177]is war’s steward, weighs the upshot, it may well prove that the

[019-178]straw which our sickles have reaped is far heavier than the

[019-179]grain. It may not be that the Achaeans should mourn the dead with

[019-180]their bellies; day by day men fall thick and threefold

[019-181]continually; when should we have respite from our sorrow? Let us

[019-182]mourn our dead for a day and bury them out of sight and mind, but

[019-183]let those of us who are left eat and drink that we may arm and

[019-184]fight our foes more fiercely. In that hour let no man hold back,

[019-185]waiting for a second summons; such summons shall bode ill for him

[019-186]who is found lagging behind at our ships; let us rather sally as

[019-187]one man and loose the fury of war upon the Trojans.”

[019-188]When he had thus spoken he took with him the sons of [[nestor|Nestor]], with

[019-189]Meges son of Phyleus, Thoas, Meriones, Lycomedes son of Creontes,

[019-190]and Melanippus, and went to the tent of Agamemnon son of Atreus.

[019-191]The word was not sooner said than the deed was done: they brought

[019-192]out the seven tripods which Agamemnon had promised, with the

[019-193]twenty metal cauldrons and the twelve horses; they also brought

[019-194]the women skilled in useful arts, seven in number, with Briseis,

[019-195]which made eight. Ulysses weighed out the ten talents of gold and

[019-196]then led the way back, while the young Achaeans brought the rest

[019-197]of the gifts, and laid them in the middle of the assembly.

[019-198]Agamemnon then rose, and Talthybius whose voice was like that of

[019-199]a god came to him with the boar. The son of Atreus drew the knife

[019-200]which he wore by the scabbard of his mighty sword, and began by

[019-201]cutting off some bristles from the boar, lifting up his hands in

[019-202]prayer as he did so. The other Achaeans sat where they were all

[019-203]silent and orderly to hear the king, and Agamemnon looked into

[019-204]the vault of heaven and prayed saying, “I call Jove the first and

[019-205]mightiest of all gods to witness, I call also Earth and Sun and

[019-206]the Erinyes who dwell below and take vengeance on him who shall

[019-207]swear falsely, that I have laid no hand upon the girl Briseis,

[019-208]neither to take her to my bed nor otherwise, but that she has

[019-209]remained in my tents inviolate. If I swear falsely may heaven

[019-210]visit me with all the penalties which it metes out to those who

[019-211]perjure themselves.”

[019-212]He cut the boar’s throat as he spoke, whereon Talthybius whirled

[019-213]it round his head, and flung it into the wide sea to feed the

[019-214]fishes. Then Achilles also rose and said to the Argives, “Father

[019-215]Jove, of a truth you blind men’s eyes and bane them. The son of

[019-216]Atreus had not else stirred me to so fierce an anger, nor so

[019-217]stubbornly taken Briseis from me against my will. Surely Jove

[019-218]must have counselled the destruction of many an Argive. Go, now,

[019-219]and take your food that we may begin fighting.”

[019-220]On this he broke up the assembly, and every man went back to his

[019-221]own ship. The Myrmidons attended to the presents and took them

[019-222]away to the ship of Achilles. They placed them in his tents,

[019-223]while the stable-men drove the horses in among the others.

[019-224]Briseis, fair as Venus, when she saw the mangled body of

[019-225]Patroclus, flung herself upon it and cried aloud, tearing her

[019-226]breast, her neck, and her lovely face with both her hands.

[019-227]Beautiful as a goddess she wept and said, “Patroclus, dearest

[019-228]friend, when I went hence I left you living; I return, O prince,

[019-229]to find you dead; thus do fresh sorrows multiply upon me one

[019-230]after the other. I saw him to whom my father and mother married

[019-231]me, cut down before our city, and my three own dear brothers

[019-232]perished with him on the self-same day; but you, Patroclus, even

[019-233]when Achilles slew my husband and sacked the city of noble Mynes,

[019-234]told me that I was not to weep, for you said you would make

[019-235]Achilles marry me, and take me back with him to Phthia, we should

[019-236]have a wedding feast among the Myrmidons. You were always kind to

[019-237]me and I shall never cease to grieve for you.”

[019-238]She wept as she spoke, and the women joined in her lament-making

[019-239]as though their tears were for Patroclus, but in truth each was

[019-240]weeping for her own sorrows. The elders of the Achaeans gathered

[019-241]round Achilles and prayed him to take food, but he groaned and

[019-242]would not do so. “I pray you,” said he, “if any comrade will hear

[019-243]me, bid me neither eat nor drink, for I am in great heaviness,

[019-244]and will stay fasting even to the going down of the sun.”

[019-245]On this he sent the other princes away, save only the two sons of

[019-246]Atreus and Ulysses, Nestor, Idomeneus, and the knight Phoenix,

[019-247]who stayed behind and tried to comfort him in the bitterness of

[019-248]his sorrow: but he would not be comforted till he should have

[019-249]flung himself into the jaws of battle, and he fetched sigh on

[019-250]sigh, thinking ever of Patroclus. Then he said—

[019-251]“Hapless and dearest comrade, you it was who would get a good

[019-252]dinner ready for me at once and without delay when the Achaeans

[019-253]were hasting to fight the Trojans; now, therefore, though I have

[019-254]meat and drink in my tents, yet will I fast for sorrow. Grief

[019-255]greater than this I could not know, not even though I were to

[019-256]hear of the death of my father, who is now in Phthia weeping for

[019-257]the loss of me his son, who am here fighting the Trojans in a

[019-258]strange land for the accursed sake of [[helen|Helen]], nor yet though I

[019-259]should hear that my son is no more—he who is being brought up in

[019-260]Scyros—if indeed Neoptolemus is still living. Till now I made

[019-261]sure that I alone was to fall here at Troy away from Argos, while

[019-262]you were to return to Phthia, bring back my son with you in your

[019-263]own ship, and show him all my property, my bondsmen, and the

[019-264]greatness of my house—for Peleus must surely be either dead, or

[019-265]what little life remains to him is oppressed alike with the

[019-266]infirmities of age and ever present fear lest he should hear the

[019-267]sad tidings of my death.”

[019-268]He wept as he spoke, and the elders sighed in concert as each

[019-269]thought on what he had left at home behind him. The son of Saturn

[019-270]looked down with pity upon them, and said presently to Minerva,

[019-271]“My child, you have quite deserted your hero; is he then gone so

[019-272]clean out of your recollection? There he sits by the ships all

[019-273]desolate for the loss of his dear comrade, and though the others

[019-274]are gone to their dinner he will neither eat nor drink. Go then

[019-275]and drop nectar and ambrosia into his breast, that he may know no

[019-276]hunger.”

[019-277]With these words he urged Minerva, who was already of the same

[019-278]mind. She darted down from heaven into the air like some falcon

[019-279]sailing on his broad wings and screaming. Meanwhile the Achaeans

[019-280]were arming throughout the host, and when Minerva had dropped

[019-281]nectar and ambrosia into Achilles so that no cruel hunger should

[019-282]cause his limbs to fail him, she went back to the house of her

[019-283]mighty father. Thick as the chill snow-flakes shed from the hand

[019-284]of Jove and borne on the keen blasts of the north wind, even so

[019-285]thick did the gleaming helmets, the bossed shields, the strongly

[019-286]plated breastplates, and the ashen spears stream from the ships.

[019-287]The sheen pierced the sky, the whole land was radiant with their

[019-288]flashing armour, and the sound of the tramp of their treading

[019-289]rose from under their feet. In the midst of them all Achilles put

[019-290]on his armour; he gnashed his teeth, his eyes gleamed like fire,

[019-291]for his grief was greater than he could bear. Thus, then, full of

[019-292]fury against the Trojans, did he don the gift of the god, the

[019-293]armour that Vulcan had made him.

[019-294]First he put on the goodly greaves fitted with ancle-clasps, and

[019-295]next he did on the breastplate about his chest. He slung the

[019-296]silver-studded sword of bronze about his shoulders, and then took

[019-297]up the shield so great and strong that shone afar with a

[019-298]splendour as of the moon. As the light seen by sailors from out

[019-299]at sea, when men have lit a fire in their homestead high up among

[019-300]the mountains, but the sailors are carried out to sea by wind and

[019-301]storm far from the haven where they would be—even so did the

[019-302]gleam of Achilles’ wondrous shield strike up into the heavens. He

[019-303]lifted the redoubtable helmet, and set it upon his head, from

[019-304]whence it shone like a star, and the golden plumes which Vulcan

[019-305]had set thick about the ridge of the helmet, waved all around it.

[019-306]Then Achilles made trial of himself in his armour to see whether

[019-307]it fitted him, so that his limbs could play freely under it, and

[019-308]it seemed to buoy him up as though it had been wings.

[019-309]He also drew his father’s spear out of the spear-stand, a spear

[019-310]so great and heavy and strong that none of the Achaeans save only

[019-311]Achilles had strength to wield it; this was the spear of Pelian

[019-312]ash from the topmost ridges of Mt. Pelion, which Chiron had once

[019-313]given to Peleus, fraught with the death of heroes. Automedon and

[019-314]Alcimus busied themselves with the harnessing of his horses; they

[019-315]made the bands fast about them, and put the bit in their mouths,

[019-316]drawing the reins back towards the chariot. Automedon, whip in

[019-317]hand, sprang up behind the horses, and after him Achilles mounted

[019-318]in full armour, resplendent as the sun-god Hyperion. Then with a

[019-319]loud voice he chided with his father’s horses saying, “[[scamander|Xanthus]]

[019-320]and Balius, famed offspring of Podarge—this time when we have

[019-321]done fighting be sure and bring your driver safely back to the

[019-322]host of the Achaeans, and do not leave him dead on the plain as

[019-323]you did Patroclus.”

[019-324]Then fleet Xanthus answered under the yoke—for white-armed Juno

[019-325]had endowed him with human speech—and he bowed his head till his

[019-326]mane touched the ground as it hung down from under the yoke-band.

[019-327]“Dread Achilles,” said he, “we will indeed save you now, but the

[019-328]day of your death is near, and the blame will not be ours, for it

[019-329]will be heaven and stern fate that will destroy you. Neither was

[019-330]it through any sloth or slackness on our part that the Trojans

[019-331]stripped Patroclus of his armour; it was the mighty god whom

[019-332]lovely Leto bore that slew him as he fought among the foremost,

[019-333]and vouchsafed a triumph to Hector. We two can fly as swiftly as

[019-334]Zephyrus who they say is fleetest of all winds; nevertheless it

[019-335]is your [[fate|doom]] to fall by the hand of a man and of a god.”

[019-336]When he had thus said the Erinyes stayed his speech, and Achilles

[019-337]answered him in great sadness, saying, “Why, O Xanthus, do you

[019-338]thus foretell my death? You need not do so, for I well know that

[019-339]I am to fall here, far from my dear father and mother; none the

[019-340]more, however, shall I stay my hand till I have given the Trojans

[019-341]their fill of fighting.”

[019-342]So saying, with a loud cry he drove his horses to the front.
