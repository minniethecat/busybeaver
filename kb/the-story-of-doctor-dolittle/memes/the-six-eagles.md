## Overview

The Six Eagles are summoned by [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]] through a swallow messenger to help search for the missing red-haired [[the-fisherman|fisherman]]. They represent the pinnacle of avian visual ability — yet their failure to find the man underscores the book's theme that different animals have different gifts.

## The Summons

After the porpoises confirm the [[the-fisherman|fisherman]] was not drowned, [[dab-dab|Dab-Dab]] suggests enlisting the eagles: "No living creature can see better than an eagle. When they are miles high in the air they can count the ants crawling on the ground" (018-003). The Doctor dispatches a swallow, and within an hour "the little bird came back with six different kinds of eagles: a Black Eagle, a Bald Eagle, a Fish Eagle, a Golden Eagle, an Eagle-Vulture, and a White-tailed Sea Eagle" (018-005).

## Appearance

The eagles are imposing: "Twice as high as the boy they were, each one of them. And they stood on the rail of the [[voyage-and-sailing|ship]], like round-shouldered soldiers all in a row, stern and still and stiff; while their great, gleaming, black eyes shot darting glances here and there and everywhere" (018-005).

[[gub-gub|Gub-Gub]] is terrified of them and hides behind a barrel, "He said he felt as though those terrible eyes were looking right inside of him to see what he had stolen for lunch" (018-006).

## The Search

The eagles are laconic: "Eagles do not talk very much. And all they answered in their husky voices was, 'You may be sure that we will do our best — for [[john-dolittle|John Dolittle]]'" (018-009–018-010).

They ascend until they appear as "tiny grains of black sand creeping across the wide, blue sky" and "parted company and started going off all different ways — North, East, South and West" (018-011). Their search is exhaustive: "We have searched all the seas and all the countries and all the islands and all the cities and all the villages in this half of the world" (018-015).

## Failure

Despite their incredible vision, the eagles fail. The closest they come is finding "three red hairs lying on a wheelbarrow before a baker's door" in Gibraltar — "but they were not the hairs of a man — they were the hairs out of a fur-coat" (018-015). Their final report is both proud and defeated: "Nowhere, on land or water, could we see any sign of this boy's uncle. And if we could not see him, then he is not to be seen... For [[john-dolittle|John Dolittle]] — we have done our best" (018-015).

## Significance

The eagles' failure is narratively important: their magnificent eyesight is insufficient because the [[the-fisherman|fisherman]] is hidden in a cave on a rock. It is [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s nose, not the eagles' eyes, that ultimately finds the man. This creates a thematic contrast between visual and olfactory perception, and reinforces the book's message that even the most extraordinary abilities have their limits.
