# The Circus Tour

## Summary

After his return to England, [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]] did not immediately go home to [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]]. Instead, he [[travel-and-exploration|traveled]] through the land with the pushmi-pullyu in a gipsy-wagon, stopping at country fairs and charging sixpence for admission to see the "Marvelous Two-Headed Animal from the Jungles of [[africa-expedition|Africa]]" (021-002).

## Context

Having returned from his [[africa-expedition|African voyage]], [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] needed [[money-and-wealth|money]]. The pushmi-pullyu, the rarest animal in the world, drew enormous crowds. The Doctor set up his wagon alongside acrobats and Punch-and-Judy shows at country fairs (021-002).

The timing of the circus tour was seasonal. "March winds had come and gone; April's showers were over; May's buds had opened into flower; and the June sun was shining on the pleasant fields, when [[john-dolittle|John Dolittle]] at last got back to his own country" (021-001). The tour thus took place in the high summer months, when country fairs were at their busiest and the roads were dry enough for a gipsy-wagon.

## Narrative

The pushmi-pullyu stayed inside the wagon while the other animals rested underneath. The Doctor sat in a chair at the front, "taking the sixpences and smiling on the people as they went in" (021-003). [[dab-dab|Dab-Dab the duck]] was kept busy scolding him "because he would let the children in for nothing when she wasn't looking" (021-003).

Menagerie-keepers and circus-men approached [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] offering "a tremendous lot of [[money-and-wealth|money]]" for the strange creature, but [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] always refused:

> "No. The pushmi-pullyu shall never be shut up in a cage. He shall be free always to come and go, like you and me."
>
> (021-005)

Although the wandering life seemed interesting at first, "after a few weeks they all got dreadfully tired of it and [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] and all of them were longing to go home" (021-006). So many people paid to see the pushmi-pullyu that "very soon [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] was able to give up being a showman" (021-007).

## The Doctor's Philosophy on Freedom

The repeated offers from circus-men and menagerie-keepers tested a core principle: [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s refusal to treat the pushmi-pullyu as property. His response — that the creature "shall never be shut up in a cage" — echoes his earlier defense of the crocodile, whom he refused to return to the circus because "he doesn't like the circus" (003-018).

Yet the irony of the situation was not lost on anyone. The pushmi-pullyu was, in fact, being put on display — albeit in a wagon, not a cage — to earn [[money-and-wealth|money]] by being stared at. The Doctor balanced this tension by keeping the display voluntary and temporary, ending the tour the moment enough [[money-and-wealth|money]] was earned (021-007). The principle was not that animals should never be seen by humans, but that they should never be imprisoned by them.

## The Wagon as a Traveling Community

The gipsy-wagon was more than a business venture — it was a mobile extension of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s household. The other animals "would lie about underneath" the wagon, maintaining the informal, domestic atmosphere that characterized [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s life even while [[travel-and-exploration|traveling]] (021-003).

"Many curious sights and happenings they saw in this wandering life; but they all seemed quite ordinary after the great things they had seen and done in foreign lands" (021-008). The Doctor and his animals had crossed oceans, survived [[voyage-and-sailing|shipwrecks]], cured plagues, and escaped dungeons. Beside those [[travel-and-exploration|adventures]], the country fairs of England — with their acrobats and Punch-and-Judy shows — were gentle entertainment.

## Participants

- **[[john-dolittle|John Dolittle]]** — the showman collecting sixpences
- **The [[pushmi-pullyu|Pushmi-Pullyu]]** — the two-headed attraction
- **[[dab-dab|Dab-Dab]]** — [[dab-dab|the duck]] who managed admissions and scolded [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] for his generosity

## Outcome & Consequences

The circus tour earned [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] enough [[money-and-wealth|money]] to return to [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]] a rich man. He paid his debts to the sailor who had lent him the boat, bought the sailor two new [[voyage-and-sailing|ships]] and a rubber doll for his baby, and paid the grocer for the food borrowed for the [[africa-expedition|Africa]] [[travel-and-exploration|journey]] (021-011). Even after filling his old [[money-and-wealth|money-box]], he still "had to get three more [[money-and-wealth|money-boxes]], just as big, to put the rest in" (021-012).

The tour also marked a symbolic closure of the [[africa-expedition|African]] [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]. What [[chee-chee|the monkeys]] had given as a gift of gratitude became, through [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s ingenuity, the means of settling every debt incurred on the [[travel-and-exploration|journey]]. The pushmi-pullyu — the rarest animal in the world, which no white man had ever seen — briefly became the most famous attraction on the English fair circuit, and then quietly retired to [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s garden in [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]].

## The Homecoming

When the hollyhocks were in full bloom, [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] "came back to [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]] a rich man, to live in the little house with the big garden" (021-009). The old lame horse was glad to see him; [[the-swallow-messenger|the swallows]] who had nested under his eaves had young ones; [[dab-dab|Dab-Dab]] returned to a house full of cobwebs and dust (021-009). [[jip-the-dog|Jip]] ran round the garden chasing rats and digging for buried bones, while [[gub-gub|Gub-Gub]] found the horseradish that "had grown three feet high in the corner by the garden-wall" (021-010).

The Doctor's final remark — "Money is a terrible nuisance. But it's nice not to have to worry" (021-013) — captured the deep ambivalence of his relation[[voyage-and-sailing|ship]] with [[money-and-wealth|wealth]]. He had earned it, settled his debts, and was content to let the rest sit in [[money-and-wealth|money-boxes]] while he returned to the life that mattered: reading to his animals by the fire.
