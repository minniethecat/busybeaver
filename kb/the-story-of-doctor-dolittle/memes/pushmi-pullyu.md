# Pushmi-Pullyu

> "Doctor," said Chee-Chee, "is the pushmi-pullyu — the rarest animal of the African jungles, the only two-headed beast in the world." No White Man had ever seen one, yet the monkeys captured it as a gift — a creature so gentle it would eat out of the Doctor's hand, and so strange it would draw crowds from miles around.

## Definition

The **pushmi-pullyu** is a fictional animal species in *The Story of [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]]* — a gazelle-like creature with a head at each end of its body, allowing it to watch in both directions simultaneously. Described as the rarest animal in all of [[africa-expedition|Africa]], it is notable not only for its singular anatomy but for its gentle, cooperative nature.

## In the Story

The pushmi-pullyu is introduced during [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s [[africa-expedition|Africa]]n [[travel-and-exploration|expedition]]. After Dolittle cures the epidemic among [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s, [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]] announces that [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s wish to give [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] a gift of thanks — the rarest animal in the jungle:

> Chee-Chee told the Doctor that the monkeys wanted to give him the pushmi-pullyu, which had never been captured by any White Man before. (010-017)

Dolittle and his animal companions track the creature through the jungle. The two-headed nature of the pushmi-pullyu makes it nearly impossible to catch — it can watch both the pursuer and the path ahead. When cornered, [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] speaks to it gently, and the animal, recognizing his kindness, agrees to accompany him willingly:

> "Yes, I'll go," said the pushmi-pullyu who saw at once, from the Doctor's quiet way, that he was a man to be trusted. (010-023)

Back in England, the pushmi-pullyu becomes the centerpiece of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s [[travel-and-exploration|travel]]ing show. Its gentle temperament and extraordinary appearance attract enormous interest, providing [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] with the funds he needs to continue his work:

> The pushmi-pullyu would stay inside the wagon, and the Doctor would charge sixpence for grown-ups and threepence for children to come inside and see it. (021-005)

## Significance

Within the book's framework, the pushmi-pullyu serves three narrative functions:

1. **A symbol of reciprocity.** The monkeys' gift of their rarest animal mirrors Dolittle's gift of healing — it is the tangible proof that kindness to animals is repaid in kind. No [[money-and-wealth|money]] changed hands; the exchange was one of mutual respect.

2. **A practical solution.** The pushmi-pullyu's rarity generates the income Dolittle needs to sustain his practice. The Doctor, who has never charged animals for treatment, can now charge people to see an animal — converting wonder into livelihood without compromising his principles.

3. **A world-building element.** Like the [[animal-language|animal language]] and Dolittle's [[medicine-and-magic|unique medicine]], the pushmi-pullyu enriches the book's natural-history tone. It is presented not as [[medicine-and-magic|magic]] but as a biological curiosity — one that happens to exist in the deep jungles where no European has ventured, reinforcing the idea that the world still holds wonders unknown to conventional science.
