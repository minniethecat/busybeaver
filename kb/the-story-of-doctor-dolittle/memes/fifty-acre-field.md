# Fifty-Acre Field

The Fifty-Acre Field is a modest agricultural location near [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby-on-the-Marsh]], mentioned in the story as the workplace of [[the-plow-horse]] and the setting for one of the earliest demonstrations of [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]]'s practical care for animals.

## Location and Description

The field is a working farm field near [[doctors-garden|Puddleby]], large enough to require a plow-horse. Its name suggests a substantial acreage by English standards — fifty acres is a sizeable holding for a single field, indicating the agricultural character of the countryside surrounding [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]].

Its defining characteristic in the story is its sun exposure. When the [[the-plow-horse|plow-horse]] visits [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] about his failing eyesight, he specifies the field as the source of his problem:

> "I would like a pair like yours — only green. They'll keep the sun out of my eyes while I'm plowing the Fifty-Acre Field." ((002-029))

The request reveals that plowing the field involves long hours under direct sunlight — a practical detail that grounds [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s fantastical animal clinic in the realities of working animal life.

## Story Significance

The Fifty-Acre Field appears only in this single passage, but it serves two narrative purposes. First, it establishes [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s clientele extends to working farm animals — not only pampered pets — and that his practice serves the agricultural economy of the region. Second, it prompts one of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s first successful animal treatments:

> The Doctor got a fine, big pair of green spectacles; and the plow-horse stopped going blind in one eye and could see as well as ever. ((002-039))

The outcome — a simple, practical solution to a practical problem — typifies [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s approach to animal [[medicine-and-magic|medicine]] and contributes to his growing reputation among the animals of the district.
