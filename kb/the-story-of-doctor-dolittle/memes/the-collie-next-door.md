# The Collie Next-Door

The Collie Next-Door is an unseen but recurring presence in the story — a neighborhood dog in [[puddleby-on-the-marsh|Puddleby]] whose rivalry with [[jip-the-dog|Jip the Dog]] provides a comic thread of domestic competition that runs beneath [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s grand [[africa-expedition|Africa]]n [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]].

## An Offstage Antagonist

The Collie Next-Door never appears directly in the narrative. Like [[theodosia]], she exists only through the words of another character — in this case, [[jip-the-dog|Jip]], who mentions her three times across the story, always with competitive disdain. Her sole described trait is her conceitedness, which [[jip-the-dog|Jip]] invokes whenever he is doing something brave or impressive.

## Jip's Measure of Achievement

The Collie Next-Door functions as [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s personal benchmark for glory. When the [[the-barbary-dragon|Barbary Dragon]] threatens [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s party during their return [[voyage-and-sailing|voyage]], [[jip-the-dog|Jip]] responds not with fear but with competitive anticipation:

> "I'd love to tell that collie next door, when we get home, that I had bitten a real pirate." ((015-009))

The imagined conversation — [[jip-the-dog|Jip]] recounting his heroism to his rival — is clearly more satisfying to him than the actual fighting. The Collie's imagined reaction provides a domestic yardstick against which [[jip-the-dog|Jip]] measures the grandeur of his [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]s.

Later, when the [[the-six-eagles|eagles]] fail to locate the [[the-fishermans-nephew|fisherman's nephew]], [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s frustration takes the same form:

> "They're just conceited — like that collie in Puddleby." ((018-019))

The comparison elevates the Collie from a mere neighbor to an archetype: she represents the general category of "conceited creatures who overstate their abilities." In [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s taxonomy of character, the eagles and the Collie Next-Door belong to the same infuriating species.

## The Triumphant Return

The Collie's narrative arc culminates in [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s homecoming:

> And after Jip had gone and shown his golden collar to the conceited collie next-door, he came back and began running round the garden like a crazy thing, looking for the bones he had buried long ago. ((021-010))

This is the Collie's finest moment — not because she does anything, but because her very existence gives [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s triumph meaning. The "[[money-and-wealth|gold]]en collar" — a gift from a grateful [[the-fishermans-nephew|fisherman]] — is not merely an ornament; it is ammunition in a neighborhood feud. [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s subsequent joy — running "like a crazy thing" — is the joy of vindication made complete by the opportunity to display it before his rival.

## Narrative Significance

The Collie Next-Door is a minor comic device, but she serves a genuine narrative purpose. By grounding [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s motivations in a petty neighborhood rivalry, the story reminds readers that [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s animal companions are not merely heroic [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]rs but also creatures of ordinary vanities and small-town grudges. [[jip-the-dog|Jip]]'s courage in [[africa-expedition|Africa]] is real, but so is his desire to rub it in the Collie's face — and the story finds both motivations equally valid, equally animal.
