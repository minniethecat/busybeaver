# King of the Jolliginki

The King of the Jolliginki is the primary antagonist of [[doctor-dolittle-m-d|Doctor Dolittle]]'s [[africa-expedition|African]] chapters. Ruling a fictional kingdom somewhere in [[africa-expedition|Africa]], the King embodies the political and racial barriers [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] must overcome — yet his motivations, rooted in a past betrayal by a white visitor, add a layer of tragic complexity to his antagonism.

## First Encounter

The Doctor and his animals are summoned to the King's court upon entering [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] territory:

> "You must all come before the King," said the black man. (005-037)

> "The King of the Jolliginki," the man announced. (005-039)

The palace is described with vivid detail that emphasizes both its grandeur and its otherness:

> …they saw the King's palace which was made of mud… (006-001)

## The King's Grievance

The King's hostility is not arbitrary. He explains his motivation directly to [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]:

> "Many years ago a white man came to these shores…" (006-004)

This backstory transforms the King from a simple villain into a wounded ruler acting on historical grievance. His refusal to let [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] [[travel-and-exploration|travel]] through his lands is a political act, not mere caprice:

> "You may not travel through my lands," said the King. (006-004)

| Motivation | Quote |
|------------|-------|
| Past betrayal | "Many years ago a white man…" |
| Protective instinct | Refuses passage through the kingdom |
| Distrust of strangers | Imprisons [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] regardless of his mission |

## Imprisonment

The King orders [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] imprisoned, and the narrative treats this imprisonment as a test of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s resourcefulness:

> Then the King turned to some of the black men and said… (006-005)

The imprisonment — and the subsequent escape — is the central conflict of the [[africa-expedition|African]] chapters. [[polynesia|Polynesia]] [[polynesia|the parrot]] plays a decisive role:

> …while the King was busy talking, I hid in the throne… (006-013)

> "We'll soon find a way to make the King let us all out of prison." (006-015)

## The Queen and Domestic Life

The King lives with his Queen, Ermintrude, and their son [[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]]:

> This was where the King lived with his Queen, Ermintrude… (006-002)

The presence of a family humanizes the King — he is not merely a tyrant but a husband and father, his harshness tempered by domestic attachments that eventually facilitate [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s escape.

## Significance

The King of the Jolliginki serves several narrative functions:

1. **Antagonist**: He creates the central obstacle of the [[africa-expedition|African]] chapters
2. **Foil to [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]**: Where [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] trusts strangers and helps freely, the King rules by suspicion and imprisonment
3. **Political commentary**: His grievance against white visitors introduces a note of colonial critique into the children's [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]
4. **Father of [[prince-bumpo|Bumpo]]**: His relation[[voyage-and-sailing|ship]] with [[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]] sets up the prince's redemptive arc
