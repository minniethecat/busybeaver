# Puddleby-on-the-Marsh

> A small town in the West Country of England, unremarkable in every way — except that it is home to the most famous animal-doctor in the world. Puddleby is where Dolittle's journey begins and where, after crossing oceans and jungles, he always intends to return.

## Description

Puddleby-on-the-Marsh is introduced in the very first chapter as a "little town" where everyone knows everyone else. Dolittle lives in a small house on the edge of the town — close enough to walk to, far enough that the noise of his many animals does not disturb the neighbors. The house has a garden where Dolittle keeps his flower-pots, a kitchen where [[polynesia|Polynesia]] gives language lessons, and a parlor once filled with human patients, now filled with animal ones.

> He lived in a little town called Puddleby-on-the-Marsh. (001-002)

## Location & Geography

Puddleby is located in [[west-country|the West Country of England]], near the coast. Its proximity to the sea is important — a sailor living nearby provides Dolittle with a boat and directions to [[africa-expedition|Africa]]. The town's name ("Puddleby-by-the-Marsh") suggests a damp, marshy landscape typical of southwestern England.

Once Dolittle becomes famous as an animal-doctor, word of his skill spreads from Puddleby outward:

> …miles and miles got to know about John Dolittle, M.D. And the birds who flew to other countries told the animals. (002-044)

## Life in Puddleby

Life in Puddleby before the [[africa-expedition|African voyage]] is a study in gentle decline. The Doctor, once "the best known doctor in the [[west-country|West Country]]" (001-014), watches his human practice evaporate as his household fills with animals. [[sarah-dolittle|Sarah Dolittle]], his housekeeper-sister, complains bitterly: she counts a hedgehog in the cellar, a cow in the pantry, and white mice in the piano. When a [[the-crocodile-incident|crocodile]] arrives, she leaves for good. (001-007)(003-014)

Yet the animals reorganize the household into a functioning commune. [[dab-dab|Dab-Dab the duck]] becomes housekeeper, [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee the monkey]] takes on cooking and mending, and [[jip-the-dog|Jip the dog]] sweeps the kitchen floor with his tail. (003-019)(003-020) [[the-cats-meatman|The Cat's-meatman]] — one of the few humans who remains loyal — supplies meat for the menagerie and becomes a regular visitor. The household is chaotic, impoverished, and utterly unlike any other in the [[west-country|West Country]] — and it is also, in its strange way, perfectly functional.

After [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s triumphant return from [[africa-expedition|Africa]] with the [[pushmi-pullyu|pushmi-pullyu]], Puddleby becomes a destination in its own right. Curious visitors come to see the two-headed creature, and [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s financial troubles are at last resolved. The little house on the edge of the marsh, once a source of local embarrassment, becomes a place of wonder.

## Story Significance

Puddleby represents **home and normalcy** — the place Dolittle leaves and returns to. His desire to get back to Puddleby drives the later chapters: he must pay the butcher's bill, tend to the sailor who lent him a boat, and settle his debts:

> "I really must get back to Puddleby. That poor sailor will think I've stolen his ship." (011-014)

The town also represents the contrast between English domesticity and [[africa-expedition|African]] [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]. Dolittle owes [[money-and-wealth|money]] in Puddleby (009-010); in [[africa-expedition|Africa]], he owes nothing but earns everything — including the pushmi-pullyu and the gratitude of countless monkeys.
