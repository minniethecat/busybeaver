# Queen Ermintrude

Queen Ermintrude is the Queen of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]], married to [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] and mother of [[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]]. She appears in two chapters and plays a critical role in the plot: her accidental discovery of [[polynesia|Polynesia]] escaping through the pantry window exposes [[polynesia|the parrot]]'s trick, undoing [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s escape and triggering the kingdom-wide jungle pursuit.

## Overview

Queen Ermintrude is a figure of quiet authority who, in a few brief appearances, shapes the course of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s [[africa-expedition|Africa]]n [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]]. Unlike her volatile husband [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]], or her fairy-tale-obsessed son [[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]], the Queen is defined by practicality and sharp observation. She is not an antagonist by disposition — she is simply the person who happens to see what others miss, and whose honesty compels her to report it.

## First Appearance

The Queen is introduced when [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] and his animals arrive at [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]'s palace in the Land of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]]. She is described sitting with [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] "under an umbrella before the palace door" — and she is asleep ((006-002)). When [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] tells [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] his business, [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] — remembering a previous white man who had exploited his lands — orders [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] and all his animals locked in the strongest prison ((006-004)–(006-006)).

The image of the Queen asleep under an umbrella is the reader's first impression of her: a figure of leisure and detachment, not yet engaged with the action. Her sleep contrasts with her later wakefulness — it is when she is awake and returning home that she becomes dangerous to [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s plans.

## The Dance and the Discovery

On the night [[polynesia|Polynesia]] executes her ruse, the Queen is "away at a dance that night at her cousin's" ((006-020)). The parrot slips into [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]'s bedroom, hides under the bed, and imitates [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]]'s voice, tricking the terrified King into ordering the prison doors opened ((006-021)–(006-031)).

The Queen returns at precisely the wrong moment. "The Queen, who was just letting herself in at the backdoor with a latch-key, saw [[polynesia|the parrot]] getting out through the broken glass" ((006-033)). She tells [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] what she saw — and "then [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] understood that he had been tricked, and he was dreadfully angry" ((006-034)).

The detail of the latch-key is telling. The Queen lets herself in like an ordinary householder, not with a royal herald or ceremonial entry. It humanizes her, making her discovery of [[polynesia|Polynesia]] feel less like a plot contrivance and more like the bad luck of being in the wrong place at the right time. She is not hunting for evidence; she is simply coming home from a party.

## The Pursuit

The Queen's discovery transforms [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]'s rage. "Queen Ermintrude had never in her life seen her husband so terrible as he got that night" ((007-001)). In his fury, [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] conscripts everyone — "even the Queen, who was tired from dancing in a pair of tight shoes, was packed off to help the soldiers in their search" ((007-001)).

> Queen Ermintrude had never in her life seen her husband so terrible as he got that night.
>
> (007-001)

The detail of the tight shoes is a small masterpiece of characterization. While [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] rages with operatic fury, the Queen's discomfort is mundane and physical — her feet hurt. She has been dancing all night, and now she must tramp through the jungle in formal shoes. Her participation in the search is not ideological but dutiful: she is helping because her husband demands it, not because she shares his anger.

Despite her discomfort, the Queen participates actively. After an exhausting night searching the jungle, it is Queen Ermintrude who finally declares defeat: "in a very tired voice that it was no use looking any more — that they might as well go back and get some sleep" ((007-008)). Her decision to call off the search allows [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] to emerge from hiding and continue toward the [[land-of-the-monkeys|Land of the Monkeys]].

## The Queen and the King: A Study in Contrasts

The dynamic between Queen Ermintrude and [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] is one of the story's more subtle character pairings. The King is loud, impulsive, easily enraged and easily manipulated — [[polynesia|Polynesia]]'s trick works precisely because he is susceptible to fear. The Queen, by contrast, is observant, practical, and calm. She sees what [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] does not; she ends the search that [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]] would prolong indefinitely.

Their marriage appears functional but asymmetrical. The King gives orders; the Queen follows them, but with a weariness that suggests long experience with his temper. She does not challenge his authority, but her quiet declaration of defeat after the fruitless search effectively overrules his rage — not by opposing it head-on, but by simply stating the obvious.

## In the Eyes of Others

### Prince Bumpo's Perspective

The Queen is [[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]]'s mother, though their relation[[voyage-and-sailing|ship]] is barely sketched in the narrative. [[prince-bumpo|Bumpo]]'s fairy-tale aspirations — his desire to be a handsome prince — suggest a domestic life in which storytelling and imagination are valued. Whether these come from the Queen or from other sources is not stated, but the Queen's own attendance at dances and social events suggests a household that participates in the courtly rituals of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] society.

### The King's Perspective

For [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]], the Queen is both partner and subordinate. He involves her in the pursuit without consulting her, treating her as an extension of his authority rather than an independent agent. Yet it is her observation, not his, that proves decisive — and her exhaustion, not his rage, that finally ends the search. The King's power is performative; the Queen's influence is practical.

## Significance

Queen Ermintrude is a minor but pivotal character. Her single action — seeing [[polynesia|Polynesia]] at the pantry window — undoes [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s clever escape and drives the entire pursuit sequence of Chapter 7. She is one of the few named female characters in the book, and her pragmatism ("no use looking any more") contrasts with her husband's ungovernable rage.

Her role also serves a narrative function: she is the complication that prevents the escape from succeeding too easily. Without her, [[polynesia|Polynesia]]'s trick would have worked perfectly, [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] would have slipped away into the jungle, and the story would have lost one of its most tense and comedic sequences. The Queen is, in a sense, the story's reality principle — the person who sees through the illusion and restores the obstacle that makes the [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]] worth reading.
