# Gub-Gub

Gub-Gub the baby pig is the comic heart of [[doctor-dolittle-m-d|Doctor Dolittle]]'s animal household. His endless appetite, theatrical emotions, and innocent bewilderment at the world make him both a source of trouble and a beloved companion. Where [[polynesia]] schemes and [[dab-dab]] dives, Gub-Gub provides the story's most reliable laughter.

## The Doctor's Pet

Gub-Gub is introduced as one of [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s inner circle:

> Gub-Gub the baby pig… (001-004)

He joins the select group chosen for the [[africa-expedition|African voyage]]:

> …with Jip the dog, Dab-Dab the duck, Gub-Gub the pig and the owl, Too-Too. (004-018)

## Shipboard Life

Gub-Gub's first encounter with [[voyage-and-sailing|shipboard]] accommodations is characteristically comic:

> As soon as they were on the ship, Gub-Gub, the pig, asked where the beds were. (004-030)

His confusion about nautical beds leads to one of the book's funniest exchanges:

> "Why, that isn't a bed!" cried Gub-Gub. "That's a shelf!" (004-031)

And his excitement prevents sleep:

> "I don't think I'll go to bed yet," said Gub-Gub. "I'm too excited." (004-033)

|-------|---------|
| Gluttony | Constant concern with food |
| Emotional | Cries easily |

## Anxiety and Tears

When the animals face imprisonment by the [[king-of-the-jolliginki|King of the Jolliginki]], Gub-Gub's reaction is immediate and emotional:

> …they all grew very sad; and Gub-Gub, the pig, began to cry. (006-007)

His tears are both comic and touching — Gub-Gub feels things deeply but expresses them with a child's lack of restraint:

> "Oh, what can you do?" said Gub-Gub, turning up his nose and beginning to cry again. (006-016)

## The Non-Swimmer

Gub-Gub's limitations are highlighted when the animals face water:

> But Chee-Chee and Gub-Gub did not know how to swim. (005-019)

This detail — a pig who cannot swim — is both practical (pigs are not ducks) and gently funny, reinforcing Gub-Gub's role as the animal least suited to [[travel-and-exploration|adventure]].

## Gub-Gub's Development Arc

While Gub-Gub is primarily comic relief, he undergoes a subtle but meaningful development over the course of the story. At the outset, he is defined entirely by his appetites — food, sleep, and comfort. The [[voyage-and-sailing|voyage]] to [[africa-expedition|Africa]] forces him into situations where comfort is impossible, and he must cope with genuine fear.

The most telling moment in Gub-Gub's arc occurs during the [[voyage-and-sailing|ship]]wreck. Confronted with actual danger, he does not freeze or panic uncontrollably — he follows the lifeline [[polynesia]] has devised:

> …climbed along the rope to safety… (005-021)

This is remarkable: the baby pig who asked where the beds were, who cried when told they were shelves, navigates a rope in a storm to reach shore. He does not become brave — that would violate his character — but he becomes *capable of being brave when it is needed*.

His emotional vulnerability also deepens over the course of the [[travel-and-exploration|expedition]]. His tears in [[jolliginki]] are not merely comic — they represent a real response to injustice and danger. Gub-Gub cries not because he is a crybaby but because he processes fear honestly, without the stoic defenses that characters like [[polynesia]] and [[jip-the-dog]] maintain. In a story about communication across species, Gub-Gub's transparency is itself a form of honesty that the narrative values.

## Significance

Gub-Gub is the story's emotional barometer. When Gub-Gub cries, the reader knows things are bad. When Gub-Gub asks about dinner, normalcy is returning. He is not brave or clever or useful — he is loved, and that is enough.
