# The Captain of the Army

The Captain of the Army is a minor antagonist in the [[the-great-journey|Jolliginki]] sequence of the story. He commands the [[king-of-the-jolliginki|King of the Jolliginki]]'s soldiers in their pursuit of [[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]] and the escaped animals, and his failure — caused by his own anatomy — provides one of the book's most vivid comic scenes.

## The Pursuit

When [[queen-ermintrude|Queen Ermintrude]] exposes [[polynesia|Polynesia]]'s trick and the [[king-of-the-jolliginki|King]] discovers [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] has escaped, the monarch responds with fury:

> He rushed round in his nightshirt and woke up all his army and sent them into the jungle to catch the Doctor. ((007-001))

The Captain leads this pursuit. The [[king-of-the-jolliginki|King]] is confident of success — after all, [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] is "in a strange land and would not know his way" ((007-004)) — but the army fails to find their quarry and is sent out again with orders to "stay in the jungle till they caught him" ((007-015)).

## The Chase and the Long Ears

When the Captain finally spots the escaping party, he pursues with determination:

> The big monkey carrying Gub-Gub was coming along behind slowly, and he saw the Captain of the army sneaking through the trees. So he hurried after the Doctor and told him to run. ((007-020))

The chase becomes frantic — "they all ran harder than they had ever run in their lives; and [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]'s men, coming after them, began to run too; and the Captain ran hardest of all" ((007-021)) — and nearly succeeds. [[john-dolittle|The Doctor]] trips over his [[medicine-and-magic|medicine]]-bag and falls "down in the mud, and the Captain thought he would surely catch him this time" ((007-022)).

What follows is Lofting's most perfectly timed piece of physical comedy:

> But the Captain had very long ears — though his hair was very short. And as he sprang forward to take hold of the Doctor, one of his ears caught fast in a tree; and the rest of the army had to stop and help him. ((007-023))

The contrast — short hair concealing dangerously long ears, the lethal spring transformed into absurd entanglement — is delivered with deadpan economy. The entire army, poised to capture the escaped prisoners, must instead perform an undignified rescue of their suspended commander.

## The Escape

The moment's delay is decisive. [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]] seizes the opportunity to organize the [[the-bridge-of-apes|monkey bridge]]:

> "Boys — a bridge! Quick! — Make a bridge! We've only a minute to do it. They've got the Captain loose, and he's coming on like a deer." ((007-031))

The simian bridge forms just in time, and [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] escapes across the cliff — the Captain reduced to a frustrated spectator of his own failure.

## Narrative Significance

The Captain of the Army is a classic Lofting creation: an authority figure whose dignity is punctured by the physical world. His long ears function as the story's practical joke on official power — the same anatomical feature that makes him recognizable as a military commander also makes him ridiculous. He appears in the story for only a few paragraphs, but the image of a decorated captain dangling from a tree by one ear is among the book's most enduring visual moments.

Like the [[king-of-the-jolliginki|King]] himself, the Captain is not a villain in the traditional sense — he is a functionary doing his job. His humiliation is not punishment but comedy, and the story treats it with the same gentle absurdity it applies to all human pretensions.
