# Land of the Monkeys

The Land of the Monkeys is a remote jungle region in [[africa-expedition|Africa]], set apart from [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]dom of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] by a natural boundary — a steep cliff with a river flowing below. This geographical isolation protects [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s from human intrusion, making it accessible only through the famous "Bridge of Apes."

## Geography

The land lies across a river gorge from [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]]: "they came to a steep cliff with a river flowing below. This was the end of [[king-of-the-jolliginki|the King]]dom of [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]]; and the Land of the Monkeys was on the other side — across the river" ((007-026)). The approach to the land is through "the very thickest part of the forest — a place where no man had ever been before" ((007-004)).

The [[travel-and-exploration|journey]] to reach it requires [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]]'s guidance through trackless jungle. The monkey knows "all the paths through the jungle — paths that no one else knew, not even the other monkeys" ((007-007)). [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]] leads the party through terrain so dense and untouched that "no man had ever been there before" ((007-004)).

## The Journey to the Gorge

The Doctor's party makes a grueling [[travel-and-exploration|journey]] from [[jolliginki|Jolliginki]] to reach the gorge. Along the way, [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]]'s knowledge proves indispensable: "[[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]] knew all the paths through the jungle — paths that no one else knew, not even the other monkeys" ((007-007)). The party includes [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]]'s cousin and other monkeys who hadn't yet fallen ill, all eager to guide [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] to their homeland ((007-017)). The monkeys' oral tradition preserves the story of their land through storytelling rather than written records: "[[chee-chee|the monkey]]s had no history-books of their own, but they kept their history alive by telling stories from one generation of monkeys to another" ((007-014)).

## The Bridge of Apes

The sole access route is the Bridge of Apes, a living bridge formed by monkeys holding hands and feet across the gorge. The bridge is not merely functional — it is a legendary feat that humans have long tried and failed to witness:

> "Many great explorers and gray-bearded naturalists have lain long weeks hidden in the jungle waiting to see the monkeys do that trick. But we never let a white man get a glimpse of it before." ((007-039))

[[john-dolittle|Doctor Dolittle]] is the first white man to witness and cross this bridge. The bridge forms spontaneously: the larger monkeys join hands and feet across the chasm, creating a living chain that spans the gorge. The Doctor and his animals cross on this bridge of living bodies — a moment of absolute trust between species.

## The Monkey Sickness

When [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] arrives, [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s are suffering from a terrible epidemic. The crisis has been building since before the [[voyage-and-sailing|voyage]] began:

> "There is a terrible sickness among the monkeys out there. They are all catching it — and they are dying in hundreds." ((004-004))

The monkeys had sent [[the-swallow-messenger|the swallow]] as a messenger, and they grew increasingly anxious waiting: "[[chee-chee|the monkey]]s in [[africa-expedition|Africa]] were getting worried because he was so long coming" ((005-005)). During the [[travel-and-exploration|journey]], [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]]'s urgency is driven by this knowledge: "[[prince-bumpo|Prince Bumpo]]... told him he must not come back — that all his people were sick like [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s. For I can make people well" ((006-029)).

Upon arrival, [[john-dolittle|the Doctor]] discovers [[chee-chee|Chee-Chee]]'s cousin "and a lot of other monkeys, who had not yet got sick, sitting about in the trees, looking very anxious" ((007-017)). The Doctor spends many days nursing the sick monkeys back to health, staying in a little house of grass built for him by [[chee-chee|the monkey]]s themselves.

## The Stone Monument

At the farewell feast, the Grand Gorilla "rolled a great rock up to the head of the table and said, 'This stone for all time shall mark the spot'" ((010-034)). The monument endures in monkey oral tradition:

> "Even to this day, in the heart of the jungle, that stone still is there. And monkey-mothers, passing through the forest with their families, still point down at it from the branches and whisper to their children, 'Sh! There it is — look — where the Good White Man sat and ate food with us in the Year of the Great Sickness!'" ((010-035))
