## Definition

In Chesterfield's vocabulary, esteem is the earned respect and favorable regard of others — a social currency more valuable than mere popularity or affection. Unlike love, which he considered fickle, esteem is built on demonstrated merit, learning, and consistent good conduct. He distinguishes carefully between "the general esteem of all" and "the particular friendship of a few" (032-001), recognizing esteem as the broader, more reliable form of social approval.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield returns to the theme of esteem throughout the correspondence, increasingly emphasizing its importance as his son matures. In the early letters, esteem is presented as a goal to be pursued through learning and accomplishment. By the middle period, Chesterfield refines the concept: esteem without affection is hollow, but affection without esteem is shallow. The ideal is to be both "admired and esteemed" (053-008). In the political letters, esteem takes on a professional dimension — it is necessary for advancement and the exercise of influence (056-005). The late letters reflect a mature appreciation: Chesterfield values his godson for his "parts, learning, and more than all, your solid good sense" (082-003), and speaks of esteem as something to be given sincerely, not simulated.

## Related Concepts

Esteem is closely connected to [[merit]], which is its foundation — one must possess genuine worth to earn lasting esteem. [[reputation]] is esteem made public and durable. [[character]] provides the substance that esteem recognizes. [[flattery]] is the counterfeit of esteem, offering the appearance without the substance. [[contempt]] is esteem's opposite. [[prudence]] guides one in distinguishing between those who deserve esteem and those who merely seek it.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers several practical recommendations regarding esteem:

- **Earn it, don't demand it**: Esteem must be built through demonstrated merit and conduct over time (032-001)
- **Distinguish general from particular**: Seek the broad esteem of society, but cultivate deeper relationships with a select few (032-001)
- **Don't mistake flattery for esteem**: Those who lavish praise may not truly respect you; look to actions rather than words (133-002)
- **Learning commands esteem**: Intellectual substance earns more lasting regard than mere social polish, though both are needed (082-003, 132-008)
- **Avoid anything that cheapens you**: Conduct that is "awkward, and gauche" may still allow you to be "esteemed" if you possess great inner worth, but social grace amplifies esteem (054-022)

## Classical References

The classical distinction between *honor* (external recognition) and *virtus* (inner worth) underlies Chesterfield's treatment of esteem. Cicero's *[[de-officiis]]* addresses the proper basis for earning the respect of others, and Chesterfield follows this tradition in insisting that esteem must be grounded in genuine merit rather than artifice. The concept also reflects the Roman ideal of *dignitas* — the standing one earns through public service and personal conduct.
