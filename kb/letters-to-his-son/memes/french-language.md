# French Language
## Definition
For Lord Chesterfield, mastery of the **French language** was not a mere accomplishment but a practical necessity. French was the lingua franca of 18th-century European diplomacy, courts, and polite society. To be ignorant of French was, in Chesterfield's view, to be excluded from the best company and the most important affairs.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's emphasis on French appears in several contexts across the correspondence:
1. **Practical necessity (1746–1749)** — French is required for diplomatic service and conversation in every European court; without it one is "a very cripple" (040-002)
2. **Social grace (1749–1755)** — French literature and theater cultivate taste; French conversation teaches the *entregent* (easy manner) that Chesterfield prizes
3. **Continued practice (1755–1767)** — Even after proficiency is gained, Chesterfield urges his son to maintain French through reading and correspondence
## Key Passages
> "We may, reciprocally, keep up our French, which, for want of practice, we might forget." (005-001)
> "You should be perfectly master of the German language; which you may very soon do there, if you please." (012-002)
> "The best company there will improve you in the German language; and, as they come from different parts of Germany, you will learn the different dialects." (024-003)
> "Write a page of German every day, and send it to me, that I may correct it. Let what you write be on some subject." (024-008)
> "I would by no means have you neglect Italian, or French; for a man who is ignorant of those two languages is a very cripple in the world." (040-002)
> "Do you make yourself master of that language? And what French books do you read for your amusement?" (044-003)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's method for mastering French:
- Begin early and practice constantly — languages rust without use (005-001)
- Read French books for both instruction and amusement — he recommends specific French texts on history and politics (026-005)
- Attend the French theater and comedies to absorb the tone and manner of cultivated French society (044-006)
- Write regularly in the language and submit to correction (024-008)
- Learn what the French call *les attentions* — the small courtesies that distinguish good breeding (044-001)
## Classical References
- Chesterfield recommends French translations of classical authors as a bridge between ancient wisdom and modern language
- The French 17th-century classics (Corneille, Racine, Molière) are held up as models of taste and style
- Chesterfield notes that the French have refined the art of social intercourse to a degree unmatched by other nations
