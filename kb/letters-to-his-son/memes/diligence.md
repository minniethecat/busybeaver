## Definition

Diligence, for Chesterfield, is not mere busyness. It is the disciplined, persistent application of effort to a defined end — the quality that converts talent into achievement and opportunity into success. He often pairs it with "industry" as a near-synonym and with "method" and "discretion" as its necessary companions: diligence without method is wasted motion; method without diligence is empty form. Together, the three "will carry a man through the world much better than great parts without them" (153-007).

## In the Letters

Diligence appears as a recurring exhortation throughout the correspondence. In the early letters, Chesterfield urges diligence in study — particularly in mastering modern languages and classical authors, where steady application is the only reliable path. The middle letters shift the emphasis to career: a young diplomat must "serve up" to his opportunities with diligence (104-003), counterworking rivals "with diligence and dexterity" (178-003). The quality is so central to Chesterfield's conception of success that he treats reports of his son's diligence as the most encouraging news he can receive (206-005, 211-001, 214-002). The message is consistent: diligence is not optional. It is the price of entry to any serious pursuit.

## Key Passages

> "He redoubles his industry and his diligence; he perseveres, and infallibly succeeds, or at least is sure to deserve success." (071-002)

> "You must serve up to your opportunities with diligence. Never put off till tomorrow what you can do today." (104-003)

> "Method, diligence, and discretion, will carry a man through the world much better than great parts without them." (153-007)

> "Counterwork your rivals with diligence and dexterity, but at the same time with all possible personal civility to them." (178-003)

> "Go on so, with diligence, and you will be, what I began to think you would be four or five years ago, an honour to me and to your country." (211-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on diligence is pragmatic. First, **never defer**: "Never put off till tomorrow what you can do today" (104-003) is his most succinct formulation. Second, **pair diligence with method**: a diligent man without a system is like a horse without a rider — much motion, little progress. Third, **be visibly diligent**: in a diplomatic or political career, the reputation for diligence is itself an asset, attracting mentors and opportunities. Fourth, **counterwork rivals with diligence** — but always with civility, so that diligence is seen as virtue rather than aggression.

## Classical References

Chesterfield does not anchor diligence in a specific classical text, but the concept is thoroughly Stoic in character. The emphasis on steady, unglamorous effort as the path to achievement echoes Seneca's counsel on the discipline of daily study and Cicero's insistence on industria as a Roman virtue. Chesterfield's framing — that diligence with method and discretion surpasses "great parts" alone — is also consonant with the Aristotelian view that natural gifts must be cultivated through habit and practice to become true excellences.
