# Voltaire
Voltaire is one of the most frequently mentioned contemporary figures in the [[letters-to-his-son]]. Chesterfield, who corresponded with Voltaire directly, treats the French philosopher with a characteristic blend of genuine admiration and sharp critical discernment — making Voltaire a case study in his [[education|educational method]] of learning from great figures without uncritical reverence.
## Life and Career
François-Marie Arouet, known universally as Voltaire, was the dominant literary figure of the French Enlightenment. By the time Chesterfield was writing his letters (1746–1767), Voltaire had already produced many of his major works and was living in exile from Paris, first at the court of Frederick the Great in Berlin, and later at Ferney near Geneva.
Chesterfield's letters capture Voltaire at the height of his European fame — the author of *Zaïre*, *La Henriade*, and the *Lettres philosophiques*, and the acknowledged master of French verse and prose.
## In the Letters
### Literary Admiration
Chesterfield's praise of Voltaire's literary achievement is unstinting. He eagerly follows Voltaire's new publications:
> "I long to read Voltaire's 'Rome Sauvée', which, by the very faults that your friend finds in it, I am sure I shall like." [162-008]
He rates Voltaire as the supreme poet of his age, declaring that "Voltaire undoubtedly exceeds all the French poets in the harmony of the verse" [180-004]. The range of Voltaire's works mentioned — tragedies (*Rome Sauvée*), historical essays (*Le Siècle de Louis XIV*), verse tales (*Zélis dans le Bain*), and epic poetry (*La Henriade*) — demonstrates Chesterfield's extensive engagement with his corpus.
### Critical Distance
Chesterfield's admiration, however, is tempered by his characteristic insistence on separating the work from the man:
> "This is an affectation below Voltaire; who, I am not ashamed to say, I admire and delight in; but who, I thank God, I am not the dupe of." [164-004]
He takes Voltaire as an object lesson in the difference between literary genius and personal character — a distinction he considers essential to his son's [[education|education in the world]].
### Voltaire and Berlin
A significant subplot in the letters concerns Voltaire's relationship with Frederick the Great's court in Berlin. Chesterfield follows Voltaire's time in Prussia with keen interest, using it to illustrate the dangers intellectuals face when they enter the orbit of absolute power:
> "His history is a comment upon those lines of Horace: 'Principibus placuisse viris non ultima laus est.'" [172-006]

## In Chesterfield's Educational Program
Voltaire serves a dual role in the letters. Positively, he is evidence that modern literature can rival the classics — proof that a contemporary author can achieve the stylistic excellence Chesterfield urges his son to pursue. Negatively, he is a cautionary example of how literary genius can coexist with personal vanity and affectation — a reminder that style without [[virtue|character]] is incomplete.
This dual treatment exemplifies Chesterfield's pedagogical method: no model, however brilliant, is exempt from critical scrutiny.
