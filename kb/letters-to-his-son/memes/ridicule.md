# Ridicule

## Definition

**Ridicule**, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is the act of treating persons or opinions with contemptuous laughter — a weapon he regards as both unjust and imprudent. Its injustice lies in its indifference to truth: sincere error deserves pity, not mockery. Its imprudence lies in its consequence: ridicule makes enemies more effectively than almost any other social offense. Chesterfield explicitly challenges Lord Shaftesbury's famous maxim that "ridicule is the best test of truth," arguing instead that wit and humor can make even sound truths appear absurd, and that the pleasure of mockery often overwhelms the pursuit of accuracy.

> "Errors and mistakes, however gross, in matters of opinion, if they are sincere, are to be pitied, but not punished nor laughed at." (014-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of ridicule is unusually philosophical for a manual of manners. He engages directly with a major intellectual proposition of his age — Shaftesbury's claim that ridicule is the test of truth — and disputes it on both logical and psychological grounds.

The moral foundation is laid early. In Letter XIV, he warns his son about the credulity of Catholic pilgrims at Einsiedlen, then arrests himself: "Remember, at the same time, that errors and mistakes, however gross, in matters of opinion, if they are sincere, are to be pitied, but not punished nor laughed at" (014-001). Sincerity immunizes error against ridicule — a remarkably tolerant principle for an eighteenth-century aristocrat.

The pragmatic warning follows in Letter XXIV: "Take care not to express any contempt, or throw out any ridicule; which I can assure you, is not soon forgiven" (024-003). The context is Philip's fourteen commensauls at Leipzig — dull companions, perhaps, but people whose goodwill matters. Ridicule may gratify the moment but wounds permanently.

The intellectual engagement with Shaftesbury comes in a later passage where Chesterfield considers whether "ridicule is the best test of truth; for that it will not stick where it is not just." His reply is firm: "I deny it. A truth learned in a certain light, and attacked in certain words, by men of wit and humor, may, and often doth, become ridiculous, at least so far that the truth is only remembered and repeated for the sake of the ridicule." The example is telling — Marie de' Medici's near-drowning is remembered only because a court wit quipped "la Reine boit" (the Queen drinks). "Pleasure or malignity often gives ridicule a weight which it does not deserve."

Chesterfield also notes the social dynamics of ridicule as a weapon of desperation: those who fail in reasoned argument "have recourse to ridicule, which is harder for one of your age to answer than for one of mine" (070-001). The young are especially vulnerable to being silenced by mockery, because they lack the authority and experience to deflect it.

In a confessional moment, Chesterfield admits that fear of ridicule shaped his own early behavior: "The fear of exposing myself to the ridicule of those whom I considered as my superiors" (064-003) led him to excessive caution. Even the master of social strategy was not immune.

> "Honest error is to be pitied, not ridiculed." (029-005)

> "When they fail in argument, they have recourse to ridicule, which is harder for one of your age to answer than for one of mine." (070-001)

## Practical Advice

- Never ridicule sincere error — pity it (014-001, 029-005)
- Understand that ridicule is never forgotten — it creates permanent enemies (024-003)
- Resist the Shaftesburian temptation to test truth with laughter — wit can make any truth look absurd
- When faced with ridicule yourself, do not retreat — it is a weapon of the intellectually desperate (070-001)
- Guard against the fear of ridicule in your own conduct — it can paralyze ambition (064-003)
