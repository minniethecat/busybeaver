# Prejudice

## Definition

**Prejudice**, for Chesterfield, is the disposition to judge before examining — a failure of reason caused by the inertia of habit, the distortions of education, and the comfort of received opinion. He treats it less as a moral failing than as an intellectual one: prejudice is what happens when the mind, "instead of being guided by reason" (064-001), is hurried along by unexamined assumptions. The antidote is analytic: "Examine carefully, and reconsider all your notions of things; analyze them, and discover their component parts, and see if habit and prejudice are not the principal ones" (050-005).

> "It is not to be conceived how many people, capable of reasoning, if they would, live and die in a thousand errors, from laziness." (050-005)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's campaign against prejudice runs through the middle letters with particular intensity. His target is threefold: the prejudices of formal education, the prejudices of social first impressions, and the deeper intellectual laziness that makes prejudice comfortable.

The critique of education is sharpest. He observes how scholars, "from the prejudices of a school education, where they hear of nothing else, are always talking of the ancients, as something more than men, and of the moderns, as something less" (030-003). Such pedants carry classics in their pockets and "will show you, plainly, that no improvement has been made, in any one art or science, these last seventeen hundred years" (030-003). The prejudice for antiquity is not learning but its parody.

He extends the attack to those who "draw all their maxims, both for public and private life, from what they call parallel cases in the ancient authors; without considering, that, in the first place, there never were, since the creation of the world, two cases exactly parallel" (030-004). The remedy is to "reason upon the case itself, and the several circumstances that attend it, and act accordingly; but not from the authority of ancient poets, or historians" (030-004). This is Enlightenment rationalism in its purest form — the demand that each question be decided on its own evidence.

Chesterfield also addresses the social dimension of prejudice: first impressions create fixed judgments that resist correction. "The different effects of the same things, said or done, when accompanied or abandoned by the Graces, is almost inconceivable" (032-001). An awkward manner "prejudices you against him, though for aught you know, he may have great intrinsic sense and merit" (032-001). The graceful man enjoys the opposite effect: his graces prepossess observers in his favor before he has spoken a word.

The deepest analysis appears in Letter L: "Examine carefully, and reconsider all your notions of things; analyze them, and discover their component parts, and see if habit and prejudice are not the principal ones; weigh the matter upon which you are to form your opinion, in the equal and impartial scales of reason" (050-005). Chesterfield here anticipates modern cognitive psychology: most of what we call opinion is inherited prejudice dressed in the language of conviction. The tragedy is that people are capable of reasoning but choose not to: "It is not to be conceived how many people, capable of reasoning, if they would, live and die in a thousand errors, from laziness; they will rather adopt the prejudices of others, than give themselves the trouble of forming opinions of their own" (050-005).

In a rare confessional moment, Chesterfield admits to his own prejudices: "My first prejudice (for I do not mention the prejudices of boyhood) was my classical enthusiasm" (064-002), and acknowledges being "hurried away by prejudices, instead of being guided by reason" (064-001). This self-knowledge lends weight to his warnings.

## Practical Advice

- Examine every opinion you hold — ask whether it is reasoned or merely inherited (050-005)
- Distrust the authority of precedent; no two cases are exactly alike (030-004)
- Be aware that first impressions are often prejudices in embryo — the awkward man may have great merit (032-001)
- Resist intellectual laziness — prejudice is the refuge of those who will not think for themselves
- Recognize that formal education implants as many prejudices as it removes — be especially vigilant about classicism (030-003)
