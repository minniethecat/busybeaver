# Praise

## Definition

**Praise**, for Chesterfield, is the great motivator and the great test of character. He approaches it with the same analytical precision he brings to flattery — but where flattery is interested, praise (when deserved) is just. His central distinction is between the desire for praise from anyone (which is mere vanity) and the desire for praise from the praiseworthy (which is the engine of excellence). "Desirous of praise from the praiseworthy" (060-003) — this single phrase encapsulates Chesterfield's entire moral psychology of ambition.

> "While you deserve praise, it is reasonable you should be sensible of it and receive it." (079-008)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of praise begins with his own experience as a father wielding it deliberately. In Letter XXIII, he reports that a third party's letter "is full of your praises; and he answers for you, that you will continue to deserve them" (023-001). This is the first instance of a recurring pattern: praise is relayed through intermediaries to maximize its motivational effect. Chesterfield understands that praise heard indirectly is more powerful than praise given directly — the same mechanism he later codifies in his advice on flattery (071-001).

He is attentive to the psychology of receiving praise. In Letter XLVII, he warns of its seductive danger: "reading your own praises, I am sure that it must naturally tend to give you a very good opinion of yourself" (047-002). The risk is real — praise can inflate vanity as easily as it can encourage virtue. The safeguard is not to reject praise but to receive it from the right sources.

The ethical core of his theory appears in Letter LX: "It pleases me that you are desirous of praise from the praiseworthy. This is a principle of true honor" (060-003). The desire for praise is not itself corrupt — it becomes corrupt only when directed at the wrong audience. To want the approval of those who themselves deserve approval is, for Chesterfield, the very definition of honor. He immediately extends the point: "I would have you extend your desire of praise a little beyond the strictly praiseworthy" (060-004) — because in the world one must deal with people of all qualities, and their good opinion too has practical value.

Chesterfield is equally precise about the mechanisms of giving praise. In Letter LIV, he warns against those who openly angle for it: "One often sees people angling for praise, where, admitting all they say to be true, no just praise is to be caught" (054-011). The metaphor is exact: the man who fishes for compliments exposes his need and earns contempt. Praise must be bestowed, not solicited. In Letter LV, he offers a subtler technique: "Expatiate in the praise of some virtue, which some in company notoriously want" (055-003). This is praise used not as flattery but as moral instruction — commending courage in the presence of the cowardly, or temperance before the intemperate.

The most psychologically acute passage comes in Letter LXXI, where he describes the mechanism of indirect praise: "Repeat and even amplify the praise to the party concerned. This is, of all flattery, the most pleasing" (071-001). The distinction between praise and flattery collapses at the level of mechanism — both work best when the target overhears them rather than receives them directly.

In the later letters, Chesterfield extends the analysis to include the praise of the world. In Letter LXXV: "Continue to deserve the praises of those who deserve praises themselves" (075-007). This is the social dimension: a man's reputation is built on the good opinion of good judges. And in Letter XCVIII, he acknowledges that merit "will extort praise; but will, at the same time, excite envy" (098-001). Praise has a cost — it attracts rivals as surely as it attracts admirers.

The tactical dimension appears in Letter CXLVII: "Everything should either directly or indirectly tend to their praise, and even not be liable to a contrary interpretation" (147-003) — advice for the courtier and diplomat. And in his final letters, he is blunt: "You must be lavish in your praises" (188-002). Praise, skillfully deployed, is the cheapest and most effective instrument of social influence.

## Practical Advice

- Desire praise from the praiseworthy — this is not vanity but the engine of excellence and true honor (060-003)
- Never angle openly for praise; the man who fishes for compliments catches only contempt (054-011)
- Give praise indirectly whenever possible — relayed through a third party, it carries far greater weight (071-001)
- Lavish praise on those whose good opinion you need, but ensure it is deserved (188-002)
- Accept that merit extorts praise but also excites envy — the two travel together (098-001)
