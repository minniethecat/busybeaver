# The Grand Tour
## Overview
The Grand Tour was the extended educational journey through continental Europe that formed the capstone of an 18th-century English gentleman's education. Philip Stanhope's Tour, supervised at every stage by his father's detailed letters and the physical presence of his tutor Mr. Harte, provides one of the most thoroughly documented examples of this institution in English literature.
## Philip Stanhope's Itinerary
Philip's Tour began in the mid-1740s, when he was in his early teens, and continued for several years. His route followed the classic Grand Tour circuit:
1. **Switzerland (Lausanne)**: His base of operations for early studies—"You are by this time (I suppose) quite settled and at home at Lausanne" (002-001)
2. **Germany (Heidelberg, Leipzig, Schaffhausen)**: For language study and exposure to German courts
3. **Italy (Rome, Venice, Turin)**: For classical antiquities and the refinements of Italian society
4. **France (Paris, Montpelier)**: The final and most important stage—the school of manners and social polish
## Chesterfield's Supervision
Unlike many fathers who sent their sons abroad with minimal guidance, Chesterfield directed every aspect of Philip's Tour through his letters:
- **Academic program**: Daily study of history, geography, languages, and classical literature
- **Social training**: Instructions on how to behave in each court and company
- **Moral guidance**: Warnings against the vices that ensnared unwary young travellers
- **Practical logistics**: Advice on money, travel arrangements, and letters of introduction
## The Role of Mr. Harte
The Reverend Walter Harte accompanied Philip as tutor and chaperone. Chesterfield's letters frequently refer to Harte's reports on Philip's progress, and Harte himself appears as a figure of trust and authority. Chesterfield used Harte as both an informant and an intermediary, writing separately to him with instructions he wished conveyed more formally.
## Key Passages
> "Your distresses in your journey from Heidelberg to Schaffhausen, your lying upon straw, your black bread, and your broken 'berline,' are proper seasonings for the greater fatigues and distresses which you must expect in the course of your travels." (001-002)
## Significance
The Grand Tour was intended to transform a schoolboy into a man of the world. Chesterfield's letters document both the ideal and the reality: the classical education, the linguistic immersion, the social apprenticeship—and the constant anxiety that his son was not absorbing the lessons as thoroughly as he should.
