# Cicero
Cicero is the classical author to whom Chesterfield accords the highest place in his pantheon of models. Throughout the [[letters-to-his-son]], Cicero's works — particularly *[[de-officiis|De Officiis]]* and *De Oratore* — are recommended not merely as fine specimens of Latin prose but as essential instruments in the formation of a gentleman's character and public capacity.
## The Man and His Context
Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was the greatest orator of the Roman Republic and one of its most influential philosophical writers. A "new man" (*novus homo*) who rose to the consulship through talent rather than ancestry, Cicero embodied the ideal of the self-made public figure that resonated powerfully with Chesterfield's vision of advancement through [[education|cultivation]] and merit.
Chesterfield's admiration for Cicero is both stylistic and substantive. Cicero's prose — periodic, balanced, and sonorous — is the acknowledged summit of classical Latin eloquence. His philosophical works, particularly those on moral duties and the art of persuasion, provide the intellectual framework for Chesterfield's own teachings.
## In the Letters
### De Officiis: The Moral Foundation
Chesterfield singles out Cicero's *De Officiis* (*On Duties*) for the highest praise, calling an unnamed chapter "that incomparable chapter in Cicero's 'Offices'" [078-009]. The work — a treatise on moral obligations addressed by Cicero to his own son Marcus — provided Chesterfield with both a philosophical framework and a rhetorical precedent for his own paternal correspondence.
The parallel is striking: both works are letters from a distinguished public man to a son at a formative age, offering instruction in the duties and conduct proper to a life of public distinction. Chesterfield was conscious of this parallel and deliberately positioned himself as a modern Cicero.
### De Oratore: The Art of Persuasion
For the cultivation of eloquence, Chesterfield recommends Cicero's *De Oratore* as unsurpassed:
> "Pray read 'Cicero de Oratore', the best book in the world to form an orator." [091-010]
This recommendation is of a piece with Chesterfield's consistent emphasis on oratory as the supreme skill for a public man. *De Oratore* — a dialogue in three books on the ideal orator — treats eloquence not as a technical skill but as the expression of comprehensive humanistic cultivation. The orator must know philosophy, history, law, and human nature. This is precisely Chesterfield's own vision of the complete gentleman.
### A Personal Connection
Chesterfield's engagement with Cicero extended beyond literary admiration. He owned a bust of Cicero, which he describes with evident satisfaction:
> "My bust of Cicero is a very fine one, and well preserved." [045-004]
This physical possession — a piece of classical sculpture in his London residence — symbolizes the intimacy of Chesterfield's relationship with the Roman author. Cicero was not merely read but lived with.

## Cicero and Chesterfield's Educational Program
Cicero occupies a unique position in Chesterfield's scheme because he is at once a stylistic model (the supreme master of Latin prose), a moral authority (the philosopher of duty and decorum), and a biographical exemplar (the *novus homo* who rose by talent alone). No other figure in the letters combines these three roles so completely.
When Chesterfield urges his son to study Cicero, he is not prescribing a course of reading but a program of self-formation. To know Cicero is to know what a cultivated mind, a disciplined character, and a persuasive tongue can achieve — and that is precisely what Chesterfield wants his son to become.
