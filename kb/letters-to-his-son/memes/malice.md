## Definition

Malice, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is the disposition of active ill-will — the desire to injure another not for any advantage to oneself but for the satisfaction of seeing another harmed. He diagnoses its psychological origins with clinical precision: "it is the production either of malice, cowardice, or vanity" (014-001). Malice is not the same as resentment or just anger; it is the ungenerous cherishing of grievances, the "bearing malice, which is always ungenerous" (133-005). Chesterfield's personal declaration — "I have not malice enough in my nature, to wish any one the greatest of all human evils, that of getting the better of himself" (099-002) — reveals his understanding of malice as self-destructive: the malicious person is consumed by his own poison. He classifies malice alongside "lying, fraud, envy, detraction" as vices that every parent should teach a child to abhor (113-001).

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of malice is consistent and emphatic. In an early letter, he anatomizes slander: "it is the production either of malice, cowardice, or vanity; and generally of all three together" (014-001) — malice is the motive, cowardice the means (attacking the absent), and vanity the gratification. His most personal reflection comes in a middle-period letter: "I have not malice enough in my nature, to wish any one the greatest of all human evils, that of getting the better of himself" (099-002). The phrasing is significant — Chesterfield frames the absence of malice not as saintly virtue but as a matter of nature and constitution; he is simply not made that way. In enumerating the vices a child must be taught to detest, he groups malice with "lying, fraud, envy, detraction, etc." — the whole catalogue of social poisons (113-001). The crucial distinction between resentment and malice appears in his counsel on bearing injuries: "there is a great difference between bearing malice, which is always ungenerous, and a becoming resentment of an injury, which is always justifiable" (133-005). Resentment is the proportionate response to wrong; malice is the lingering, ungenerous cherishing of the wound. In the later letters, he warns against "captiousness, sullenness, and that ill-nature which either shackles or exposes their malice" (200-001) — malice, when unconcealed, makes its bearer socially insupportable.

## Key Passages

> "…it is the production either of malice, cowardice, or vanity; and generally of all three together. Malice prompts the slander, cowardice makes the slanderer attack the absent and defenseless, and vanity is gratified by the attention paid to the scandal." (014-001)

> "I have not malice enough in my nature, to wish any one the greatest of all human evils, that of getting the better of himself; and yet such is my opinion of you, that I am sure you will never give me occasion to feel that malice." (099-002)

> "…such as lying, fraud, envy, malice, detraction, etc., and I do not know one of them that I would not rather my son should be guilty of than you." (113-001)

> "…at difference between bearing malice, which is always ungenerous, and a becoming resentment of an injury, which is always justifiable." (133-005)

> "…ither shackle or expose their malice. Besides, captiousness, sullenness, and that ill-nature which either shackles or exposes their malice, will make a man insupportable in company." (200-001)

## Related Concepts

Malice is the dark counterpart of [[generosity]]: where generosity inclines a person to think well of others, malice inclines to think ill. It is closely associated with [[envy]], [[detraction]], and [[slander]] — the social vices that share malice's root in ill-will (014-001, 113-001). [[Resentment]], properly understood, is distinct from malice: "a becoming resentment of an injury" is justifiable, whereas "bearing malice" is "always ungenerous" (133-005). [[Vanity]] and [[cowardice]] are malice's frequent companions — together they form the "three together" that produce slander (014-001). Malice is the opposite of [[good-nature]], [[humanity]], and [[benevolence]]. [[Captiousness]] and [[sullenness]] are malice's social expressions (200-001).

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on malice is firm and unambiguous:

- **Renounce malice absolutely**: Malice is "always ungenerous" (133-005); it has no legitimate place in the conduct of a gentleman
- **Distinguish resentment from malice**: It is permissible to feel "a becoming resentment of an injury" — resentment is the natural and justifiable response to wrong, while malice is the ungenerous refusal to let go (133-005)
- **Detect malice in slander**: When you hear slander, recognize it as "the production either of malice, cowardice, or vanity" (014-001) — understanding its source helps you discount its content
- **Do not expose your malice**: If you must harbor ill-will, at least do not let it "shackle or expose" you (200-001) — but far better not to harbor it at all
- **Teach children to abhor it**: Malice stands alongside lying and fraud as vices that must be instilled against from the earliest age (113-001)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of malice draws on the classical distinction between *ira* (anger, which can be righteous) and *malevolentia* (ill-will, which is always vicious). Seneca's *De Ira* makes precisely Chesterfield's distinction: anger may be justified as a response to injury, but the nursing of grudges — *malevolentia* — is the mark of a small soul. Cicero in the *Tusculan Disputations* classifies *malitia* (malice) among the perturbations of the soul that the wise man must extirpate. The Christian tradition Chesterfield inherited added the theological dimension: malice is one of the capital sins, the deliberate willing of evil to another that corrupts the soul of the malicious more than it harms its object. Chesterfield's personal declaration — "I have not malice enough in my nature" (099-002) — echoes the classical ideal of the magnanimous man who is too great of soul to remember injuries, a theme developed by Aristotle in his portrait of the *megalopsychos* who "does not bear grudges."
