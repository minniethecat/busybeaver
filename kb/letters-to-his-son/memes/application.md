# Application
## Definition
In Chesterfield's lexicon, **application** denotes the steady, purposeful direction of one's mental energies toward a defined goal — be it the mastery of a language, the study of history, or the cultivation of social graces. It is the practical realization of *attention*, transforming passive awareness into active exertion. Chesterfield distinguishes application from mere intelligence, arguing that natural gifts are worthless without the discipline to apply them.
## In the Letters
The theme of application runs through the correspondence from the earliest letters (1746) to the later years (1760s), evolving in emphasis:
1. **Early letters (1746–1748)** — Application is framed as the companion to "care" in the schoolroom: languages, geography, history. Chesterfield insists that common abilities, when wedded to common application, reliably surpass genius without industry.
2. **Middle letters (1748–1754)** — As Philip Stanhope enters society, the concept broadens to encompass social application: the effort of adapting to company, reading the temper of others, and shaping one's own manners. Chesterfield urges that time spent in idle pleasure be balanced by "uninterrupted application to serious studies."
3. **Later letters (1757–1765)** — Application becomes a marker of settled character. Chesterfield begins to praise his son for having "resumed that application" at Leipzig and having become an "example of application and temperance."
## Key Passages
> "If care and application are necessary to the acquiring of those qualifications, without which you can never be considerable, nor make a figure in the world, they are not less necessary to the keeping of them." (001-004)
> "In truth, common sense (which, in truth, is very uncommon) is the best sense I know of: abide by it; it will counsel you best. … Common sense … pointed out the art of pleasing, and the utility of being pleased; and this, with common application, will carry a man but a very little way." (001-003)
> "If, by the application which I recommend to you, you acquire a great share of knowledge, it will be of the greatest use to you." (020-002)
> "We are in general, in England, more distinguished by our parts than by our learning … But you have parts; and, with application, may make a very considerable figure." (027-003)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's guidance on cultivating application:
- Set aside fixed hours each day for uninterrupted study, treating them as sacred
- Apply yourself most vigorously to the subjects you find least congenial — these are often the ones you most need
- Do not mistake busy-ness for application: many trifling occupations consume time without producing improvement
- Let your hours of relaxation be as deliberately chosen as your hours of labor
- Remember that application, like a muscle, strengthens with use and atrophies with neglect
## Classical References
Chesterfield invokes the classical ideal of *studium* (zealous devotion) and the Stoic principle that *aretē* (excellence) requires *askēsis* (disciplined practice). He quotes Juvenal's maxim — *"Nullum numen abest, si sit prudentia"* (No god is absent if prudence is present) — as an endorsement of systematic effort over reliance on natural talent.
