# Flattery

## Definition

**Flattery**, in Chesterfield's lexicon, is neither a simple vice nor a casual compliment — it is a precise social technology whose value depends entirely on its calibration. He draws a bright line between two species: **abject flattery**, which debases both giver and receiver, and **delicate, indirect flattery**, which is simply the art of making others pleased with themselves. "No flattery is either too high or too low for them" (049-004) — this is the cynical premise from which all his advice proceeds: people are insatiably hungry for praise, and the man who feeds that hunger skillfully holds the key to every door.

> "Abject flattery and indiscriminate assentation degrade the giver as much as they debase the receiver." (078-005)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield develops his theory of flattery from the earliest letters, transforming what might appear mere cynicism into a coherent social doctrine. The key insight — presented as early as Letter XVII — is that different people are susceptible to flattery upon different heads: "Cardinal Richelieu... was little open to flattery upon that head; for he was in no respect a fine gentleman" (017-004). But women, he notes, are almost universally vulnerable to flattery upon their beauty: "scarce any flattery is too gross for them to swallow" (017-005). The point is analytical, not moralistic: identify the target's ruling vanity, and you have found the key to their will.

He is careful to distinguish his teaching from the counsel of a sycophant. Immediately after the analysis above, he warns: "I do not recommend to you abject and criminal flattery: no; flatter nobody's vices or crimes" (017-006). What he recommends instead is a subtler art. The most effective flattery, he argues in Letter LXXI, is that which reaches its target indirectly — "repeat and even amplify the praise to the party concerned. This is, of all flattery, the most pleasing, and consequently the most effectual" (071-001). The mechanism is simple: direct praise may be dismissed as interested; praise relayed through a third party appears disinterested and therefore sincere.

Throughout the middle and late letters, Chesterfield refines this instrument. In Letter XXXVIII, he notes that some mistake "abject flattery" for the real thing, "having no opinion of one's own, and adopting the opinions of the company you frequent" (038-006) — a subtle point: unthinking agreement is a form of flattery, and a particularly cheap one. In Letter XLIX, he extends the analysis to the flatterer's own character: "No flattery is either too high or too low for them. They will listen with eager credulity to the grossest exaggerations of their own merit" (049-004). The man who understands this can manage anyone.

His later letters become more tactical. "Is he vain, and open to flattery? An artful, flattering favorite will make him believe that all his schemes are practicable" (097-002) — here flattery merges with statecraft. The final distillation appears in Letter CLXIII: "There is something to be gained by every sort of flattery, and every man by one sort or another" (163-001). This is Chesterfield at his most pragmatic: flattery is not a weakness to be condemned but a universal human appetite to be understood and, when necessary, supplied.

By the closing letters, Chesterfield reveals that he practiced what he preached: "I was sure would please them; flattery, gallantry, and love" (182-002) — he deployed the full arsenal in his own diplomatic career.

## Practical Advice

- Never flatter vices or crimes — this crosses from social skill into moral corruption (017-006)
- Identify the ruling vanity before approaching anyone; flattery must be tailored to the object
- Prefer indirect flattery — praise relayed through a third party appears disinterested and is far more potent (071-001)
- Avoid "abject and indiscriminate assentation"; it degrades everyone involved (078-005)
- Unthinking agreement with the opinions of one's company is a cheap form of flattery — have opinions of your own (038-006)
- Do not let flattery make you suspect your own judgment: a man who needs constant praise is a man without self-knowledge (133-005)
