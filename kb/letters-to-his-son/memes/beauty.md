# Beauty

## Definition

**Beauty**, in Chesterfield's usage, operates on two registers. The first is literal — physical attractiveness, which he treats as a real but unreliable social asset. The second is metaphorical — moral and intellectual beauty, which he considers the true adornment of the gentleman. "Even virtue, which is moral beauty, wants some of its charms if it be not accompanied by the Graces" (056-002). This layered treatment reveals a mind that sees beauty not as an end but as a means: it opens doors, but character must walk through them.

> "Even virtue, which is moral beauty, wants some of its charms if it be not accompanied by the Graces." (056-002)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of physical beauty is coolly realistic. He acknowledges its power — "scarce any flattery is too gross for them to swallow" (017-005), speaking of beautiful women — but he never places it above more durable qualities. Beauty is an advantage, not an achievement, and those who rely on it alone are eventually disappointed. "Their beauty neglected or controverted, their best resolutions flag" (049-004) — the dependence on external validation that beauty fosters is, for Chesterfield, a structural weakness.

The concept extends naturally into aesthetics. When discussing classical literature, Chesterfield draws attention to "the happiness of his diction, and the beauty of his poetry" (009-001), connecting literary beauty to the cultivation of taste. The gentleman must be capable of appreciating beauty in art as much as he is cautioned against being seduced by beauty in persons.

The architectural metaphor appears tellingly: a mind, properly cultivated, rises "gradually with all their beauty, proportions, and ornaments" (090-004), like a classical edifice. This is beauty as order, harmony, and proportion — the aesthetic of the Enlightenment, far removed from romantic passion.

The practical counsel is characteristically shrewd: "Make love to the most impertinent beauty of condition that you meet with" (096-001). Here beauty is simply a fact of court life — something to be managed, flattered, and strategically engaged. Chesterfield's cynicism about beautiful people is of a piece with his cynicism about all people who mistake their natural gifts for personal merit.

## Practical Advice

- Value moral beauty above physical beauty — the latter fades, the former endures (056-002)
- Recognize that beauty confers social power, but dependence on it is a vulnerability (049-004)
- Cultivate an appreciation for beauty in art and poetry — it refines taste and sharpens judgment (009-001)
- Compose your conduct as an architect composes a building — each part contributes to the beauty of the whole (070-004)
- Flatter beauty where you encounter it in society, but never mistake it for substance (017-005)
