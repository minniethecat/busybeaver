# Classical Education

## Definition

In Chesterfield's educational scheme, **classical education** refers to the thorough grounding in Greek and Latin literature, language, and history that he considered the bedrock of a gentleman's intellectual and moral formation. It is not merely ornamental learning but a practical discipline that sharpens judgment, furnishes the mind with historical exemplars, and provides the common cultural vocabulary of European polite society.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's insistence on classical education is relentless and spans the entire correspondence. His prescriptions form a coherent program:

1. **Daily study**: He insists that Philip devote mornings to classical and "severer" studies before the social engagements of the afternoon and evening—"pursue your classical and other studies every morning" (066-004)
2. **Greek maintenance**: Even as Philip advances in his travels and diplomatic apprenticeship, Chesterfield urges him to keep up Greek "to keep up your classical learning, which will be an ornament to you" (130-005)
3. **Classical travel**: He instructs Philip to view Italy's antiquities "with a classical spirit" (096-001), and praises him for having "traveled, classically, critically, and da virtuoso" (114-001)
4. **Lifelong value**: The classical authors are not schoolroom drudgery but enduring companions—"those which I call classical works, in every language" (161-005)

Chesterfield is aware that classical education can become pedantic. He warns against the scholar who "looks upon the best classical books, as books for school-boys only" (052-001), insisting that the classics reward lifelong rereading.

## Key Passages

> "He looks upon the best classical books, as books for school-boys only, and therefore reads only the modern trifles of the day." (052-001)

> "First, that you pursue your classical and other studies every morning, while you are abroad, as diligently as you would at home." (066-004)

> "You have your classical and severer studies to continue, and your exercises to perform." (069-001)

> "Consider it classically and politically, and do not view it only through the narrow spectacle of a French antiquarian." (083-006)

> "I would have you know those which I call classical works, in every language; such as Boileau, Corneille, Racine, Molière in French; Milton, Dryden, Pope, Swift in English; and the three great tragic poets of antiquity in Greek and Latin." (161-005)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical guidance on classical education includes:

- Study the classics in the morning when the mind is fresh; reserve evenings for modern languages and society
- Read classical authors in the original language — translations lose the style that is half the value
- Combine classical reading with travel: visit the sites described in ancient texts
- Avoid pedantic display of classical learning in conversation; wear it lightly
- Know the "classical works" of every major European language, not just Greek and Latin

## Classical References

The classical education Chesterfield prescribes draws primarily on:

- **Cicero** — the supreme model of Latin prose and ethical reflection; Chesterfield calls *De Officiis* essential reading
- **Horace** — quoted throughout the letters for his urbane wisdom and poetic craft
- **Homer and Virgil** — the epic foundations of classical culture
- **The Greek tragedians** — Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides as the "three great tragic poets of antiquity"
- **Classical historians** — Livy, Tacitus, Thucydides for political and moral instruction
