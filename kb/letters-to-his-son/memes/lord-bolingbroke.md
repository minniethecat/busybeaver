# Lord Bolingbroke
Henry St John, 1st Viscount Bolingbroke, appears throughout the [[letters-to-his-son]] not merely as a historical figure but as Lord Chesterfield's supreme exemplar of eloquence, style, and worldly accomplishment. Chesterfield's repeated invocations of Bolingbroke constitute one of the most sustained pieces of literary portraiture in the correspondence.
## Life and Career
Bolingbroke was a dominant figure in early 18th-century English politics. A leader of the Tory party under Queen Anne, he served as Secretary of State and helped negotiate the Treaty of Utrecht (1713). After the Hanoverian succession, he was attainted for treason and fled to France, where he lived in exile before being permitted to return to England in 1725.
Though barred from the House of Lords, Bolingbroke remained politically active through his writings — most notably *The Idea of a Patriot King* and *Letters on the Study and Use of History* — which exerted considerable influence on the "country party" opposition to Sir Robert Walpole.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield knew Bolingbroke personally and regarded him with deep, if sometimes critical, admiration. His references to Bolingbroke in the letters serve three distinct functions:
### Model of Eloquence
Chesterfield's praise of Bolingbroke's style is unambiguous:
> "Having mentioned Lord Bolingbroke's style, which is, undoubtedly, infinitely superior to anybody's, I would have you read his works, which you have, over and over again, with particular attention to his style." [135-003]
He declares that he would "much rather that you had Lord Bolingbroke's style and eloquence in speaking and writing, than all the learning of the Academy of Sciences" [135-002].
### Model of Social Grace
Beyond style, Bolingbroke exemplified the union of intellect and manners that Chesterfield prized above all:
> "Lord Bolingbroke joined all the politeness, the manners, and the graces of a courtier, to the solidity of a statesman, and the learning of a pedant." [160-002]
### Caution About Character
Chesterfield's portrait is not wholly uncritical. He acknowledges the historical controversies surrounding Bolingbroke's conduct and urges his son to read him with "great attention" — distinguishing between the man's stylistic mastery and his more questionable political decisions [128-018].
| Role | How Chesterfield Uses Him |
|------|--------------------------|
| Stylistic exemplar | Supreme model of English prose and oratory |
| Social ideal | Union of courtly grace with intellectual substance |
| Political cautionary | Brilliant but flawed — a lesson in separating style from character |
| Personal friend | Chesterfield's own acquaintance, adding intimacy to his portrait |
## Major Works Referenced
Chesterfield specifically recommends several of Bolingbroke's works to his son:
- **Letters on the Study and Use of History** — praised for its style and recommended for careful reading [095-004]
- **The Idea of a Patriot King** — described as "a short book" published under the pseudonym Sir John Oldcastle [127-002]
- **On the Spirit of Patriotism** — cited for its political philosophy [128-018]
## In Chesterfield's Educational Program
Bolingbroke occupies a unique position in Chesterfield's [[education|educational scheme]]. Unlike [[cicero|Cicero]] or [[horace|Horace]], who are classical models from the distant past, Bolingbroke was a contemporary — a living (and recently dead) demonstration that the union of eloquence, learning, and social grace was attainable in the modern world. He is, in effect, the proof that Chesterfield's educational ideal is not an antiquarian fantasy but a practical program for distinction in 18th-century public life.
