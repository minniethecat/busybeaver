# Letters

## Definition

**Letters**, for Chesterfield, are not merely a medium of communication but a distinct literary and social art — one that a young gentleman must master to succeed in both private and public life. His own letters to his son constitute an extended practical demonstration: they are at once instruction in the art and a living example of it. Chesterfield treats letter-writing as a discipline with distinct genres (the business letter, the friendly letter, the amusing letter), each with its own models and rules, and each essential to the conduct of a life lived in society.

> "The best models that you can form yourself upon are, Cicero, Cardinal d'Ossat, Madame Sevigne, and Comte Bussy Rebutin." (011-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's most concentrated advice on letter-writing appears early in the correspondence, when he provides his son with a reading list of epistolary masters (011-001). The passage is a small treatise in itself, prescribing a different model for each register:

**Cicero's Epistles to Atticus** are "the best examples that you can imitate, in the friendly and the familiar style." They teach the informal warmth and directness appropriate to letters between intimates.

**Cardinal d'Ossat's letters** are the model for business correspondence: "no affected turns, no attempts at wit, obscure or perplex his matter; which is always plainly and clearly stated, as business always should be." The business letter is an exercise in clarity and concision — ornament is a defect here.

**Comte Bussy and Madame de Sevigne** provide the model for "gay and amusing letters, for 'enjouement and badinage'" — the playful, witty letters that are "so natural, that they seem to be the extempore conversations of two people of wit, rather than letters which are commonly studied, though they ought not to be so" (011-001).

Chesterfield also gives practical instruction on content. He urges his son to make his letters "a sort of journal of your daily life" (023-006), transforming correspondence from a duty into a record of observation and self-cultivation. His own "long and frequent letters" (001-006) model the ideal: copious, attentive, and tirelessly instructive.

## Key Passages

> "A propos of letter-writing, the best models that you can form yourself upon are, Cicero, Cardinal d'Ossat, Madame Sevigne, and Comte Bussy Rebutin." (011-001)

> "The simplicity and the clearness of Cardinal d'Ossat's letters show how letters of business ought to be written; no affected turns, no attempts at wit, obscure or perplex his matter; which is always plainly and clearly stated, as business always should be." (011-001)

> "For gay and amusing letters, for 'enjouement and badinage,' there are none that equal Comte Bussy's and Madame Sevigne's. They are so natural, that they seem to be the extempore conversations of two people of wit." (011-001)

> "I would now rather that your letters should be a sort of journal of your daily life." (023-006)

> "My long and frequent letters, which I send you, in great doubt of their success." (001-006)

> "My Lord Bacon says, that a pleasing figure is a perpetual letter of recommendation." (012-002)

## Practical Advice

- Model friendly letters on Cicero's Epistles to Atticus — informal, warm, and direct (011-001)
- Model business letters on Cardinal d'Ossat — clear, plain, and free of ornament (011-001)
- Model amusing letters on Madame de Sevigne and Comte Bussy — natural, witty, conversation-like (011-001)
- Make your letters a journal of daily life — they become a discipline of observation as well as a record (023-006)
- Write copiously — Chesterfield's own model is the "long and frequent" letter (001-006)
