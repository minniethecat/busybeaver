## Definition

Familiarity, in Chesterfield's social vocabulary, is the most delicate of the social arts — the calibrated ease that puts others at their ease without overstepping the invisible boundary at which ease becomes presumption. He draws the distinction with precision: there is "easy without too much familiarity" (023-005), and there is "indiscriminate familiarity" that "will sink both merit and knowledge into contempt" (078-003). The difference is one of judgment and gradation: the accomplished man knows "the several gradations of familiarity or respect" (132-009) and adjusts his manner accordingly. At its best, familiarity is "that interior domestic familiarity with people of fashion, that is of all things the most necessary to give a young fellow an air of the world" (144-005) — it is the quality that distinguishes the insider from the outsider, the man who belongs from the man who merely visits.

## In the Letters

Familiarity appears across the full chronological span of the correspondence, growing in sophistication as Chesterfield's son matures. In the early letters, he warns against the excess: "waggery, and indiscriminate familiarity, will sink both merit and knowledge into contempt" (078-003) — familiarity without judgment is self-destructive. The middle letters develop the concept of gradation: the well-bred man understands "the several gradations of familiarity or respect" and "is exactly the same person to everybody, but not in the same manner" (132-009). This is the crucial distinction — familiarity is not a fixed register but a sliding scale calibrated to the relationship and the occasion. Chesterfield urges his son to establish "an easy, domestic, but polite familiarity and intimacy in the several French houses" where he is received (136-002), treating familiarity as the passport to good company. He identifies "that interior domestic familiarity with people of fashion" as "of all things the most necessary to give a young fellow an air of the world" (144-005). The boundary is clear: "it is rude and ill-bred, unless where the familiarity is extreme" (146-002) — certain liberties are permitted only at the highest degree of intimacy. In the later letters, he warns against contracting "a familiarity at any place" that would make it difficult to leave (188-002), and celebrates the "confidence, familiarity, and connection" that prudence can establish with people of merit (215-005).

## Related Concepts

Familiarity is the most finely calibrated expression of [[good-breeding]]: breeding provides the rules, familiarity the warmth that makes those rules human. It is closely allied to [[intimacy]] (136-002) but distinct from it — intimacy is the relationship, familiarity the manner that expresses it. [[Respect]] is familiarity's necessary counterweight: the accomplished man calibrates "the several gradations of familiarity or respect" according to the person and the occasion (132-009). Familiarity stands opposed to [[stiffness]] and [[formality]] on one side, and to [[presumption]] and [[impertinence]] on the other. [[Ease]] is familiarity's medium — "easy without too much familiarity" (023-005). The degenerate form is "coarse familiarity" (088-005) or "indiscriminate familiarity" (078-003) — familiarity without judgment that breeds contempt.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on familiarity is notably precise:

- **Calibrate the gradation**: Understand "the several gradations of familiarity or respect" and adjust your manner accordingly — "exactly the same person to everybody, but not in the same manner" (132-009)
- **Seek domestic familiarity with the fashionable**: "That interior domestic familiarity with people of fashion" is "of all things the most necessary to give a young fellow an air of the world" (144-005)
- **Never descend into coarse familiarity**: "Indiscriminate familiarity will sink both merit and knowledge into contempt" (078-003); familiarity must remain "polite" and "decent" (128-017, 136-002)
- **Be easy without being too familiar**: The ideal is "easy without too much familiarity, genteel without affectation" (023-005)
- **Respect the boundary**: Certain liberties are permitted "only where the familiarity is extreme" (146-002); the man of fashion knows where that boundary lies
- **Don't contract familiarity that traps you**: "Contracting a familiarity at any place" can make it difficult to leave when circumstances require (188-002)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of familiarity draws on the classical distinction between *familiaritas* (the ease of equals) and *adulatio* (the false familiarity of flatterers). Cicero in *De Amicitia* treats familiarity as the natural expression of true friendship but warns that it must be governed by *verecundia* — the sense of propriety that prevents ease from becoming offense. The Aristotelian mean between obsequiousness and surliness — what Aristotle calls "friendliness" in the *Nicomachean Ethics* — is precisely Chesterfield's "decent familiarity": the disposition to associate with others in the right way, neither flattering to please nor standing aloof to preserve dignity. The French concept of *aisance* (ease) that Chesterfield admired in the salons of Paris had developed elaborate unwritten rules about the gradations of familiarity — rules that Chesterfield's son was expected to absorb through "interior domestic familiarity with people of fashion" (144-005).
