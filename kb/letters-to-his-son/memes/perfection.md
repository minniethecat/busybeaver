## Definition

Chesterfield uses "perfection" not as a fixed endpoint but as a **directional ideal** — a standard against which all effort is measured and toward which all self-cultivation strives. He is acutely aware that perfection is unattainable in any literal sense, yet he insists that aiming for it is the only reliable way to achieve excellence. In his lexicon, perfection is less a destination than a *method*: the habit of never settling for adequacy when improvement is possible.

> "The breeding of a gentleman, the learning of a scholar, and the knowledge of the world — joined to the well-doing, the well-speaking, and the well-thinking of his country, is the perfection of human nature."
> (006-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of perfection spans the entire correspondence and reveals a distinctive educational philosophy:

**Early letters (1746–1748)**: Perfection is established as the explicit goal of the son's education. Chesterfield does not pretend the boy can actually attain it, but he frames every subject — languages, dancing, history, manners — as a step toward this ideal. The tone is exhortatory: "I would have you as near perfection as possible" (029-007).

**Middle period (1749–1754)**: The concept gains nuance. Chesterfield acknowledges the danger of mistaking mere defects for perfections — a form of self-deception common among the unreflective (053-008). He also connects perfection to classical models: the "Attic salt" of Athenian wit (008-003), the perfection of Greek sculpture (041-008). Perfection in this period is increasingly tied to *taste* — the cultivated judgment that distinguishes genuine excellence from its counterfeits.

**Late letters (1755–1767)**: The emphasis shifts from exhortation to anxiety. "I never think I have said enough" (056-006) reveals Chesterfield's own perfectionism as a father. The ideal of perfection becomes a burden as much as an inspiration — the son must bear the weight of his father's impossibly high standard.

> "Whatever you do at all, do it to the utmost perfection, ought to be your aim at this time of your life."
> (044-003)

## Key Passages

> "I would have you as near perfection as possible. I know nobody in a fairer way to obtain it than yourself, if you please."
> (029-007)

The conditional "if you please" is characteristically Chesterfieldian: perfection is available but requires the will to pursue it.

> "Without them, no knowledge, no perfection whatever, is seen in its best light."
> (016-004)

The "them" refers to the graces — manners, address, deportment. Chesterfield insists that knowledge without social grace is invisible perfection, which is no perfection at all.

> "Mistaking their defects for their perfections, and thinking that they owe their success to those very things which, in truth, are the greatest obstacles to it."
> (053-008)

The danger of self-deception: what you think is your strength may be your weakness.

> "And, as I aimed at perfection, I adopted gaming as a necessary part of that character."
> (053-011)

A revealing autobiographical aside: Chesterfield himself once adopted vices *instrumentally*, believing (in his youth) that a complete gentleman must be versed even in fashionable dissipation.

> "With a cool constitution, is the perfection of human nature."
> (061-003)

The Stoic ideal resurfaces: perfection of character requires self-command, the ability to remain "cool" under provocation.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on perfection is practical rather than philosophical:

1. **Aim for perfection, accept excellence**. The ideal serves as a compass; you will not reach it, but without it you will drift. "Whatever you do at all, do it to the utmost perfection" (044-003) is a rule of thumb, not a literal demand.

2. **Perfection requires the graces, not just the substance**. Knowledge without manners is "not seen in its best light" (016-004). The perfection of a gentleman is social as well as intellectual.

3. **Beware the self-deception of mistaking defects for perfections** (053-008). The unreflective person attributes success to the very traits that most limit him. Honest self-assessment is the prerequisite of genuine improvement.

4. **Perfection of one's character requires a "cool constitution"** (061-003) — the Stoic capacity to govern one's passions. Heat and haste are enemies of excellence.

5. **Do not confuse the perfection of particular skills with the perfection of the whole person**. Chesterfield wants his son to be a complete gentleman — learned, graceful, politically adept, morally sound — not merely a specialist in any one domain.

> "My desire, my anxiety for your perfection, that I never think I have said enough."
> (056-006)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of perfection draws on several classical traditions:

- **Aristotle's *telos***: The idea that every being has an end or perfection toward which it naturally tends is Aristotelian in origin. Chesterfield secularizes this: the perfection of a gentleman is a this-worldly ideal achieved through education and practice, not through divine grace.

- **Cicero's *perfectus orator***: Cicero's ideal of the perfectly accomplished orator — learned, eloquent, morally serious — is the closest classical model for Chesterfield's ideal gentleman. Both are synthetic ideals that combine intellectual, moral, and aesthetic excellence.

- **Stoic *apatheia***: The "cool constitution" (061-003) echoes the Stoic ideal of emotional self-mastery. But Chesterfield's perfection is warmer than the Stoic sage: it includes social warmth, wit, and grace that the austere Stoic would distrust.

- **The Renaissance *uomo universale***: Chesterfield's perfection is also indebted to the Renaissance ideal of the universal man, accomplished in letters, arts, arms, and society. The eighteenth-century gentleman is the Enlightenment descendant of Castiglione's courtier.

Chesterfield's originality lies in synthesizing these traditions into a practical educational program: perfection is not a gift of nature or grace but the product of methodical cultivation, beginning in youth and continuing throughout life.
