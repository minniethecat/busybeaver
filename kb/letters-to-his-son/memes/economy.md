# Economy

## Definition

In Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, **economy** is the rational governance of resources — not merely money, but time, attention, and effort. It occupies the golden mean between the vices of profusion (wasteful extravagance) and avarice (miserly hoarding). Chesterfield treats economy as an expression of the Stoic virtue of temperance, applied to the practical management of a gentleman's life.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's teaching on economy grows more pointed as Philip matures and assumes financial independence:

1. **The mean between extremes**: He warns that "profuseness often runs into profusion, economy into avarice" (030-001), insisting that the gentleman must steer between both
2. **False economy**: "Never, from a mistaken economy, buy a thing you do not want, because it is cheap; or from a silly splendor, buy a thing you do want, because it is dear" (062-003) — a maxim that has passed into common wisdom
3. **Economy of time**: Chesterfield is more urgent about the economy of time than of money. "A prudent economy in both would make them rich in knowledge and pleasure" (168-004), he writes, linking temporal and intellectual economy
4. **Domestic economy**: He urges Philip to study "the rules, and the order and economy in the inside" of well-managed households (063-003), seeing domestic order as a model for life-management
5. **Economy as independence**: Financial economy is ultimately about independence — a gentleman who lives within his means is free; one who does not is enslaved to his creditors

Chesterfield's concept of economy is notably modern in its extension beyond mere money management to encompass what we would now call "life management."

## Key Passages

> "Profuseness often runs into profusion, economy into avarice, courage into rashness, and caution into timidity." (030-001)

> "Never, from a mistaken economy, buy a thing you do not want, because it is cheap; or from a silly splendor, buy a thing you do want, because it is dear." (062-003)

> "A prudent economy in both would make them rich in knowledge and pleasure." (168-004)

> "I look back with regret upon that time, which I might have employed so much better, and which is now gone forever." (168-004)

> "You will, I hope, take notice of the rules, and the order and economy in the inside." (063-003)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical teaching on economy includes:

- Keep exact accounts of income and expenditure — ignorance of one's finances is the first step to ruin
- Never borrow money except in genuine emergency; debt is the enemy of independence
- Distinguish between economy (wise management) and avarice (miserly refusal to spend on what is proper)
- Apply economy to time as rigorously as to money — lost time cannot be recovered
- A well-regulated economy permits generosity; a disordered one makes even small expenses painful

## Classical References

- **Cicero** — *De Officiis* discusses *frugalitas* as a subdivision of temperance, the virtue of restraining appetites
- **Horace** — The *Satires* celebrate the *aurea mediocritas* (golden mean) in all things, including expenditure
- **Seneca** — *De Brevitate Vitae* on the economy of time: "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste much of it"
