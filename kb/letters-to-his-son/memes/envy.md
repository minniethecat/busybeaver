# Envy

## Definition

**Envy** is, in Chesterfield's moral taxonomy, one of the "dark" passions — a resentful awareness of another's advantage that corrodes the soul and alienates society. Unlike ambition (which looks upward and strives) or emulation (which seeks to equal), envy looks sideways with ill will and wishes to pull down. Chesterfield treats it as both a moral failing and a practical liability: the envious man is unhappy, untrustworthy, and ultimately self-defeating. The gentleman neither indulges envy himself nor provokes it in others — indeed, much of the art of pleasing consists in avoiding the appearance of superiority that would excite envy in one's companions.

> "Low people, in good circumstances, either treat their equals with insolent contempt, or with low envy and jealousy." (055-009)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's earliest mention of envy, in Letter XX, is personal and generous: "Be your fortune what it may be, I neither can nor shall envy you them, as old people are sometimes suspected to do" (020-003). A father disclaims the generational envy that the old are sometimes thought to feel toward the young. This establishes envy as something one must actively disclaim — the imputation is so damaging that it requires explicit denial.

His political analysis appears in Letter XXXVII, discussing Brutus: "Such as pride, envy, personal pique, and disappointment, are often the real motives" (037-005). Even the noblest-seeming actions — the assassination of Caesar — may have base motives at their root. Envy, Chesterfield suggests, is one of the hidden springs of history, moving men to actions they later justify with high principle.

The social mechanics of envy are laid out in Letter LV, with characteristic precision: "Low people, in good circumstances, either treat their equals with insolent contempt, or with low envy and jealousy" (055-009). Envy is the mark of low birth and poor breeding. The gentleman neither feels it nor expresses it. The phrase "low envy" is doubly damning — envy is not merely a passion but a vulgar one, characteristic of those who have risen without acquiring the manners of their new station.

Chesterfield warns his son against exciting envy in Letter XCVIII: "At the same time, excite both fear and envy; two sentiments absolutely incompatible with love and friendship" (098-001). The desire to be admired, to be thought superior, is dangerous — it provokes sentiments that destroy rather than attract. The wise man conceals his superiority, or at least wears it lightly enough that it does not prick the self-love of others.

In the moral catalogue of Letter CXIII, envy is listed among the cardinal vices: "The black and ill-natured passions of the heart, such as lying, fraud, envy, malice, detraction, etc." (113-001). Envy is "black" — dark, malignant, a poison of the heart. It belongs with lying and malice in the lowest circle of Chesterfield's moral universe.

The practical utility of others' envy is noted in Letter CXXXIV: "Hatred, jealousy, or envy, make, most people attentive to discover the least flaws" (134-004). Envy makes people watchful — your envious rivals will find your weaknesses faster than your friends. This is not a recommendation but a warning: guard yourself, because the envious are always watching.

Chesterfield dismisses envy of simpler lives in Letter CXLV: "Let them show me a cottage, where there are not the same passions of envy" (145-002). The pastoral fantasy — that the poor are free from the vices of the rich — is false. Envy is universal, infecting cottage and court alike. It is not a product of circumstance but of human nature.

The highest wisdom on the subject comes in Letter CLII: "They commonly envy or hate, but most certainly do not love" (152-001). This is the bottom line: the envious person cannot love. Envy and affection are mutually exclusive. To be envied is to be isolated; to envy is to cut oneself off from the possibility of genuine connection.

A practical lesson follows in Letter CLXXXI: "The wise man will rise by those arts which please the highest with the least envy; and fall, if he does fall, the least" (181-003). The successful man must calibrate his rise to minimize the envy it provokes. This is the highest social art — to advance without exciting the resentment that would pull you back down.

## Practical Advice

- Never show envy of another's fortune or success — it betrays low breeding and makes you "absolutely incompatible with love and friendship" (098-001)
- Conceal your advantages rather than display them — superiority provokes envy, and envy isolates (098-001)
- Remember that envy is universal: "even a cottage" has its envious passions — do not romanticize the simplicity of the poor (145-002)
- Rise "by those arts which please the highest with the least envy" — advance quietly, without provoking the resentment of those you pass (181-003)
- Use the envious attention of rivals to your advantage — they will find your flaws before your friends do, and you can correct them first (134-004)
- Classify envy among the "black" passions that must be extirpated from the heart, alongside lying, fraud, and malice (113-001)

## Related Concepts

- [[Jealousy]] is envy's close cousin — Chesterfield often pairs them, and both spring from the same root of insecurity and ill-nature (055-009)
- [[Pride]] and envy are linked in Chesterfield's analysis of Brutus — pride makes a man resent another's superiority, and envy supplies the motive to destroy it (037-005)
- [[Ambition]] is the virtuous alternative to envy — ambition looks upward and strives to rise; envy looks sideways and wishes to pull down
- [[Good-breeding]] is the social art that prevents one from exciting envy in others — the well-bred man never makes his superiority felt (098-001)
- [[Self-love]] explains why envy is so painful — another's advantage wounds our self-love, and envy is the defensive reaction (152-001)
- [[Friendship]] cannot coexist with envy — "they commonly envy or hate, but most certainly do not love" (152-001)
