# Cambridge University
## Overview
The University of Cambridge, specifically Trinity Hall, was where Philip Dormer Stanhope received the classical education that would shape his entire intellectual life. He entered Trinity Hall in 1712, and though he left without taking a degree (as was common for young aristocrats), the classical curriculum—Latin, Greek, rhetoric, philosophy, and history—provided the foundation for his later career as statesman and man of letters.
## In the Letters
Cambridge appears in the Letters not as an institution described in detail, but as the implicit standard of gentlemanly learning. Chesterfield's constant references to Cicero, Horace, and the classical canon reflect the education he absorbed at Cambridge. When he urges his son to study history, languages, and philosophy, he is prescribing the Cambridge curriculum in portable form.
The university also represents the English educational tradition that Chesterfield valued above all others. Though he admired French polish and German scholarship, his intellectual framework remained that of an English university man.
## Chesterfield's Education
At Trinity Hall, Chesterfield studied:
- **Classical languages**: Latin and Greek, the foundation of all gentlemanly learning
- **Rhetoric**: The art of persuasion, which he would practice as a statesman and perfect as a correspondent
- **History**: Ancient and modern, which he considered the best school of virtue and prudence
- **Moral philosophy**: The Ciceronian ethics that inform his letters at every turn
## Significance
Chesterfield's Cambridge education shaped not only his knowledge but his values. His belief in the primacy of classical learning, his respect for eloquence, his conviction that history is the best teacher of worldly wisdom—all these are the products of an 18th-century Cambridge education.
