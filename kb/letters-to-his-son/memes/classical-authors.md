# Classical Authors
## Definition
In Chesterfield's educational system, the **classical authors** — principally Cicero, Horace, Virgil, and the Greek and Roman historians — constitute the foundation of all sound learning. They are not schoolroom texts to be outgrown but lifelong companions whose wisdom deepens with the reader's own experience.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's references to classical authors span the entire correspondence:
1. **Early education (1746–1750)** — Classical studies form the morning's work; Greek and Latin are the gateways to all serious knowledge
2. **The Grand Tour (1750–1755)** — Classical reading is now applied to the actual sites of Italy and Greece; Chesterfield urges his son to view ruins and landscapes through a "classical spirit" (096-001)
3. **Mature reflection (1755–1767)** — Classical authors become models of style and wisdom; Chesterfield recommends reading them in every language's best translations (161-005)
## Key Passages
> "He looks upon the best classical books, as books for school-boys, and never reads them afterward. Never was a more mistaken notion." (052-001)
> "Criticism is short and easy, I will classically end and poetically advise you to read." (056-001)
> "My classical enthusiasm, which I received from my tutor, was one of the most useful and pleasing circumstances of my education." (064-002)
> "Mr. Harte will complete your classical studies. You will likewise have the best masters for modern languages." (066-003)
> "Pursue your classical and other studies every morning, and pass your afternoons and evenings in the best company." (066-004)
> "You have your classical and severer studies to continue with Mr. Harte in the mornings." (069-001)
> "See the remains of antiquity with a classical spirit; and they will clear up to you many passages of the classical authors." (096-001)
> "You should know those which I call classical works, in every language; such as Boileau, Corneille, Racine, Molière in French; Milton, Dryden, Pope, Swift in English." (161-005)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's counsel on engaging with the classics:
- Never abandon classical reading after school — the best books grow with the reader and yield more at forty than at fourteen (052-001)
- Read the classics in the morning when the mind is fresh; reserve lighter reading for evening (066-004)
- When traveling in Italy, view antiquities through a classical lens — let Virgil and Cicero guide your eye (096-001)
- Extend the category of "classical" to include the best authors in every modern language (161-005)
- A classical education is not pedantry but an ornament that distinguishes the gentleman from the mere man of business
## Classical References
- **Cicero** — Model of eloquence and moral philosophy; Chesterfield recommends *De Officiis* and the orations
- **Horace** — Master of urbane wisdom; the *Epistles* and *Satires* are repeatedly cited
- **Virgil** — The *Aeneid* as the poetic companion to Roman history and topography
- **The Greek historians** (Thucydides, Xenophon) — Models of political and military narrative
- Chesterfield expands the classical canon to include French and English classics: Boileau, Corneille, Racine, Molière, Milton, Dryden, Pope, Swift (161-005)
