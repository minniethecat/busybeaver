# Friendship

## Definition

Chesterfield approaches **friendship** with a dual perspective that reflects his broader philosophy: genuine warmth moderated by worldly prudence. He acknowledges the reality and value of true friendship — he writes movingly of his own closest friendship with his brother (058-001) — but he is equally direct about the dangers of simulated or imprudent friendships. For a young man entering the world, the ability to distinguish between friends and flatterers, between genuine regard and self-interested association, is a critical social skill.

> "True friendship requires certain proportions of age, rank, and situation." (020-004)

## In the Letters

Friendship is treated across the entire correspondence, evolving as Chesterfield's son matures. In the early letters, the emphasis is on the transition from a tutorial relationship to a friendship between father and son: "Friendship shall be our mutual and only tie" (066-004). This remarkable declaration signals Chesterfield's hope that their relationship will mature beyond authority and obedience.

In the middle and later letters, the focus shifts to the son's peer friendships. Chesterfield is acutely aware of the dangers of indiscriminate friendships, warning against the "impudent" people who claim friendship with persons of rank for their own advantage (070-001). He provides a taxonomy of useful versus dangerous associations, always returning to the principle that a friend's character determines the value — and risk — of the friendship.

## Key Passages

> "There is that profession of simulated friendship, with an indiscreet and unbounded professions of eternal fidelity, which is always suspicious." (016-001)

> "Particularly gain the affection and friendship of particular people, whether men or women." (017-004)

> "True friendship requires certain proportions of age, rank, and situation; and there must be a mutual and reciprocal pleasure." (020-004)

> "I have had the ease, freedom, and friendship of that correspondence; and you will find it a most useful one." (052-006)

> "We had always lived in the closest friendship. My brother John died last Friday." (058-001)

> "I shall have no authority over you, and friendship shall be our mutual and only tie." (066-004)

> "They make what they impudently call friendships with these people; which are always suspected, and often proved to be connections of interest." (070-001)

> "The same thing equally creates and dissolves friendship, produces and reconciles enmity: interest." (079-002)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on friendship is notably practical:

- Distinguish between simulated and genuine friendship — unbounded professions of eternal fidelity are "always suspicious" (016-001)
- Recognize that true friendship requires compatibility in "age, rank, and situation" — it cannot be forced across incompatible circumstances (020-004)
- Actively cultivate the friendship of people whose character and position can be of genuine value to you (017-004, 060-005)
- Avoid "impudent" claims of friendship with persons of rank — these are transparently self-interested (070-001)
- Be aware that "interest" can create and dissolve friendships with equal speed; do not mistake convenience for loyalty (079-002)
- Genuine friendships require "attention" and "regard" to sustain — neglect destroys them (075-002)
