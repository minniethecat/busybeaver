# Judgment

## Definition

**Judgment** is, for Chesterfield, the faculty of accurate discrimination — the ability to weigh evidence, assess character, and arrive at sound conclusions. It is the intellectual virtue that governs all practical activity, from the choice of friends to the conduct of public affairs. Chesterfield distinguishes judgment from mere learning or cleverness: a man may be learned without judgment, in which case his learning is useless lumber; a man may be witty without judgment, in which case his wit makes him ridiculous.

> "There is more judgment required for the proper conduct of our domestic affairs, and the management of our private interests." (030-001)

The centrality of judgment in Chesterfield's thought is signaled by his insistence that it must be cultivated actively, not received passively — "let no authority impose upon your understanding" (064-004).

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of judgment develops from early exhortations to exercise independent thought to later reflections on the consequences of its absence. In the early letters, he warns against "reading at the expense of judgment" (052-001) — the scholar who absorbs without evaluating. The middle period emphasizes practical judgment in social settings: knowing "that degree of attention and civility which is due" (058-004) to each person according to their station.

> "Read with discernment and judgment, the real perfections of the best authors." (053-012)

In the later letters, judgment becomes the faculty that distinguishes the statesman from the mere politician. Chesterfield notes approvingly that some possess "superior judgment and distinguishing eye" (139-001) that enables them to see beyond immediate appearances to underlying realities. He is sharply critical of those who lack it: "people have ears, but few have judgment; tickle those ears, and depend upon it you catch their judgments" (094-003).

> "One whose superior judgment and distinguishing eye hinder him from being dazzled." (139-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on cultivating judgment is pointed and practical:

- **Exercise your own judgment** — never defer to authority without independent evaluation (064-004)
- **Read with discernment** — do not absorb books passively; "read with discernment and judgment" (053-012)
- **Observe before deciding** — Chesterfield prefers "ocular and auricular evidence" (092-001) to hearsay or reputation
- **Distinguish degrees of merit** — good judgment knows exactly how much attention each person or subject deserves (058-004)
- **Beware the expense of judgment** — excessive reading or specialization can come at the expense of judgment (052-001)
- **Trust evidence over authority** — form your judgment on direct observation, not received opinion

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of judgment corresponds to the classical *judicium* — the discriminating faculty that Cicero, in *De Oratore*, identifies as the distinguishing mark of the truly eloquent man. The warning against letting "authority impose upon your understanding" (064-004) echoes the skeptical tradition of Montaigne and the Enlightenment imperative of *sapere aude* — dare to know, dare to judge for yourself. The pairing of "plain understanding with sound judgment" (056-004) recalls Locke's distinction between wit and judgment in the *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*.
