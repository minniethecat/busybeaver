# Opinion

## Definition

In Chesterfield's usage, **opinion** straddles the private and the public: it is both the considered judgment one forms in one's own mind and the reputation one holds in the minds of others. This dual meaning shapes his advice — one must cultivate independent opinions while remaining acutely sensitive to how those opinions (and every other action) shape the opinions others form of you. The danger lies at two extremes: the rigidity that refuses to reconsider any view, and the pliability that has no views of its own.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of opinion unfolds along two tracks:

1. **Opinion as social currency** — "People...form their opinion of you, upon that which they see of your behavior" (016-001). A man's reputation is the aggregate of others' opinions of him, and every word, gesture, and acquaintance contributes to it. This is the world of appearances, where managing others' opinions is a practical necessity.

2. **Opinion as intellectual independence** — "Have a will and an opinion of your own, and adhere to them steadily" (055-006). Chesterfield wants his son to think for himself, to form judgments based on evidence rather than fashion, and to hold his ground against contrary pressure. This is the world of conviction.

The tension between these two tracks — conforming to others' expectations while maintaining intellectual independence — is never fully resolved. Chesterfield's solution is pragmatic: form your opinions privately through careful study, express them modestly in company, and never confuse a matter of opinion with a matter of fact.

## Key Passages

> "However gross, in matters of opinion, if they are sincere, are to be respected" (014-001)

> "Give your opinion modestly and coolly, which is the only way to convince" (017-001)

> "Have a will and an opinion of your own, and adhere to them steadily, without the least affectation of singularity or obstinacy" (055-006)

> "Matters of opinion do not depend upon the will; and it is as absurd to be angry with a man for not thinking as you do, as for not being as tall as you are" (064-002)

> "It is very difficult to distinguish early in life between habits and opinions; for many errors, which from long habit, have perhaps grown into real opinions" (064-001)

> "Form your opinion upon the greater or lesser degree of probability" (050-005)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's rules for managing opinion:

- **Form opinions on evidence, not fashion** — Examine the facts and weigh probabilities before reaching a conclusion (050-005).
- **Express opinions modestly** — Forceful dogmatism alienates others and rarely persuades; a cool, reasoned delivery is more effective (017-001).
- **Respect the opinions of others** — Sincere opinions deserve respect even when they are wrong (014-001).
- **Distinguish opinion from fact** — "Matters of opinion do not depend upon the will," and it is irrational to resent someone for holding a different view (064-002).
- **Be aware of your own intellectual habits** — What feels like a settled opinion may be nothing more than "long habit" masquerading as conviction (064-001).

## Classical References

Chesterfield's epistemology of opinion reflects:
- The classical rhetorical tradition (Cicero, Quintilian) on the distinction between *doxa* (opinion) and *episteme* (knowledge)
- The skeptical tradition's emphasis on probability as the guide where certainty is unavailable
- The Stoic discipline of examining one's impressions before assenting to them
