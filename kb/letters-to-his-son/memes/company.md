# Company

## Definition

In Chesterfield's vocabulary, **company** denotes the social circles a person frequents — the companions, acquaintances, and assemblies that constitute one's social world. It is simultaneously a school, a theater, and a test: one learns from good company, performs in it, and is judged by the company one keeps. Chesterfield distinguishes sharply between *good company* (people of rank, sense, and polished manners) and *low company* (the vulgar, the dissipated, and the merely fashionable without substance).

## In the Letters

The discourse on company runs through the entire correspondence and intensifies during the son's continental travels (1746–1750), when choosing the right company becomes a daily practical decision.

Chesterfield elaborates a taxonomy of company:

1. **Good company of the first sort** — "which is called the beau monde, and consists of people of the first quality, with a mixture of people of distinguished merit" (016-003). These are the people whose manners, taste, and conversation set the standard.

2. **Good company of the second sort** — "people of sense and learning" (007-008), whose value lies in intellectual exchange rather than social cachet.

3. **Low company** — those whose manners, morals, or intellectual level make them dangerous associates. "Young people are apt to think that being in low company shows a generous disregard of rank; it really shows a low education, and a habit of low company" (083-001).

The son is urged to be strategic: "Endeavor, as much as you can, to keep company with people above you" (016-002), because one inevitably takes the tone of one's companions.

## Key Passages

> "There are two sorts of good company; one, which is called the beau monde, and consists of people of the first quality... The other sort of good company is that of people of distinguished merit and character." (016-003)

> "Endeavor, as much as you can, to keep company with people above you: there you rise, as much as you sink with people below you." (016-002)

> "Take the tone of the company that you are in, and do not pretend to give it; be serious, gay, or even trifling, as the present humor of the company requires." (017-001)

> "The company of women of fashion will improve your manners, and give you a certain tournure that men alone cannot give you." (023-004)

> "They are made, by education and company, from fifteen to five-and-twenty." (035-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical counsel on company includes:

- **Choose your company deliberately** — "the choice of your company" is second only to the choice of one's friends (016-002).
- **Adapt to the tone** — One must blend with whatever company one is in, never imposing one's own mood (017-001).
- **Avoid low company entirely** — It lapses into a habit that marks a man permanently (083-001).
- **Seek women's company** — The company of women of fashion polishes manners in a way male company cannot (023-004).
- **Observation in company** — Merely being present is insufficient; one must actively observe and learn from the behavior of polished people (020-005).

## Classical References

The distinction between good and bad company draws on a long tradition of classical moral philosophy:
- Cicero's *De Amicitia* — on the moral character of one's associates
- Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* — the shaping power of habitual environment on character
- The Renaissance courtly tradition (Castiglione, Gracián) — the art of navigating social hierarchies
