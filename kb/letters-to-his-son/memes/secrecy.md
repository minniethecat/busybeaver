## Definition

Secrecy, for Chesterfield, is the disciplined practice of keeping confidences — the ability to receive, hold, and protect sensitive information without disclosure. He distinguishes it sharply from its absence: the "retailer" of secrets (055-005) who cannot resist passing on what he has heard is a social menace. In Chesterfield's most striking formulation, "dissimulation is a shield, as secrecy is armor" (071-001) — secrecy is the stronger, more essential defense, the armor without which a man in public life is dangerously exposed.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces secrecy early in the correspondence, advising his son to choose companions "with choice, delicacy, and secrecy" (007-009). The concept gains urgency as the son approaches manhood and a diplomatic career. Chesterfield offers his own confidence as a model: "I promise you secrecy" (052-006) he writes, establishing the father-son correspondence as a space of inviolable trust. He is severe on those who breach secrecy — "a retailer of this kind is set down" (055-005) as unreliable and contemptible. The metaphor of secrecy as armor appears in the context of a wider discussion of prudence and dissimulation (071-001), where Chesterfield argues that concealment of one's thoughts is not merely permissible but necessary. He advises his son to make "discretion and secrecy be known parts of your character" (111-005) — that is, to cultivate a reputation for trustworthiness that precedes any specific confidence. Secrecy is particularly important in diplomatic correspondence: "matters of the utmost secrecy, not fit to be trusted to the common post" (129-002) require special handling. He extends the principle to ordinary letter-writing: "use yourself to secrecy as to the letters you either write or receive" (132-014), making discretion a habit rather than a situational response. Near the end of his active correspondence, Chesterfield confesses something "under the secrecy of confession" (182-002), showing that even the father uses the language of secrecy to frame intimate disclosure.

## Related Concepts

Secrecy is the stronger counterpart to [[dissimulation]] — where dissimulation is a shield (passive defense), secrecy is armor (active protection) (071-001). [[discretion]] is the general quality of judgment that secrecy puts into specific practice (111-005). [[prudence]] governs when and with whom secrecy should be exercised. [[trust]] is the bond that secrecy both creates and depends upon: to share a secret is to trust; to keep it is to prove worthy of trust. [[reserve]] is the manner of self-containment that signals a capacity for secrecy. The "retailer" of secrets (055-005) is the antithesis of this virtue, related to the vice of [[indiscretion]].

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical instruction on secrecy includes:

- **Make secrecy a known part of your character**: Let your reputation for discretion precede you, so that your discretion is "known" (111-005) before any specific test
- **Never retail a secret**: The man who passes on confidences "is set down" (055-005) as untrustworthy; a single breach destroys the reputation for secrecy permanently
- **Choose companions with secrecy in mind**: Select friends who possess the quality, and practice it yourself (007-009)
- **In business, secrecy is non-negotiable**: Men of business "know the importance of secrecy, and will observe it" (050-006); the diplomatic profession, especially, demands it (129-002)
- **Extend secrecy to ordinary correspondence**: "Use yourself to secrecy as to the letters you either write or receive" (132-014) — make discretion habitual, not situational

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of secrecy echoes the Stoic and Ciceronian ideal of *taciturnitas* — the disciplined silence of the wise man who speaks only when speech serves a purpose. In the classical rhetorical tradition, the ability to withhold as well as to disclose was a mark of the complete orator. The metaphor of secrecy as "armor" (071-001) draws on military imagery common in classical moral philosophy, where virtues are often figured as weapons or defenses against the assaults of fortune and malice. The confessional model Chesterfield invokes — "under the secrecy of confession" (182-002) — has roots in both the Catholic sacrament and the classical tradition of intimate philosophical correspondence (Seneca's letters to Lucilius).
