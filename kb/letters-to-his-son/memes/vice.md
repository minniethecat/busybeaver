# Vice
## Definition
In Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, **vice** is not a single failing but a genus of corruptions that he classifies into two distinct orders: *vices of the heart* — the deep moral defects such as malignity, envy, perfidy, and cruelty — and *vices of character* — the behavioral failings such as idleness, extravagance, and dissimulation. The distinction is crucial to his pedagogy: vices of the heart are fatal to moral worth and render a man incapable of genuine esteem; vices of character, while damaging, are remediable through discipline and self-correction. Chesterfield's own rhetorical strategy in the letters is to present himself as an "enemy to vice" who warns without condemning.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's treatment of vice follows the arc of his son's maturation:
1. **Early warnings (1746–1748)** — The earliest letters adopt a tone of cautious reassurance: "I have hitherto not discovered any vice of the heart" (Letter I). The father surveys the moral landscape, mapping the territory of youthful temptation — drinking, gambling, sexual excess — and distinguishing between the pardonable errors of inexperience and the damning habits of settled depravity.
2. **Confessional turn (1748–1750)** — Chesterfield makes a remarkable rhetorical move in Letter VII, confessing that "the vices of my youth proceeded much more from my being ashamed to refuse than from any real disposition to vice." This admission transforms the advice from precept to testimony, positioning the father not as a moral scold but as a fellow traveler who learned through error.
3. **Social dimension (1750–1754)** — Vice becomes a subject of social analysis in Letters XVI–XVII: the flatterer who "flatters every vice and every folly you have," the false friend who encourages indulgence to secure influence. Chesterfield warns that society will not oppose your vices — it will applaud and deepen them — and that the only defense is a declared enmity to vice in all company.
## Key Passages
> "I have hitherto not discovered any vice of the heart, or any peculiar weakness of the head; on the contrary, I think I perceive good sense well-improved, and a right way of thinking." (001-003)
> "The vices of my youth proceeded much more from my being ashamed to refuse, than from any real disposition to vice." (007-002)
> "A man of the best parts … will be a declared enemy to their vices and follies, without being marked as their enemy." (016-001)
> "No; flatter nobody's vices or crimes: on the contrary, abhor and discourage them. But there is no living in the world without a complaisant indulgence for people's weaknesses, and innocent, though ridiculous vanities." (017-006)
> "When a man goes much beyond [the bounds of pleasure], and exceeds them, it becomes low vice, brutal passion, debauchery, and destruction of health, fortune, and reputation." (005-004)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's counsel on avoiding vice:
- Distinguish between vices of the heart and vices of character; guard against the former with absolute vigilance, and correct the latter with steady discipline
- Be a declared enemy to vice in all company, but do so with such politeness and good humor that you are not marked as censorious
- Recognize that the greatest danger to virtue is not open temptation but the flattery of false friends who excuse and encourage your failings
- Never affect vices you do not have in order to fit in — "a man of sense … neither borrows nor affects vices"
- If you have the misfortune to fall into vice, do not compound the error by being ashamed to acknowledge and correct it
## Classical References
Chesterfield's moral taxonomy echoes the classical distinction between *vitium* (vice as settled disposition) and *peccatum* (error as single act), developed in Stoic ethics and refined by Cicero in *De Officiis*. The warning against flatterers who encourage vice draws on Plutarch's essay "How to Tell a Flatterer from a Friend." His confessional strategy — admitting his own youthful vices to disarm resistance — is a rhetorical device with roots in Horace's *Satires* and the tradition of moral counsel through self-disclosure.
