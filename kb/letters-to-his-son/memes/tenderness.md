# Tenderness
## Definition
Chesterfield uses **tenderness** in a specific and revealing sense: it is the warm parental affection he bears for his son, Philip Stanhope — an affection he freely acknowledges yet deliberately disciplines into measured expression. Tenderness is, for Chesterfield, the emotional foundation of the entire correspondence, but he is acutely aware that unrestrained tenderness can weaken the authority of instruction and embarrass the recipient. The result is a distinctive rhetorical stance: tenderness constantly felt, carefully modulated.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's negotiation with his own tenderness evolves across the correspondence:
1. **The preface and early letters (P01, 1746–1748)** — The "Special Introduction" to the published collection observes that in his private life Chesterfield "disclosed a welcome tenderness, a touch of the divine." In the early letters to his young son, tenderness is expressed through anxious solicitude about health, studies, and moral formation.
2. **Middle letters (1749–1754)** — As Philip matures, Chesterfield frames his advice as flowing "from that tenderness and affection" which has no interest but the son's welfare. He appeals to his son's knowledge of "my tenderness" as a warrant for intrusive counsel. The word increasingly carries a rhetorical weight — it justifies the very act of letter-writing.
3. **Later letters (1757–1765)** — The tone shifts subtly. Chesterfield speaks of "inflicting my tenderness" — a striking phrase that reveals his awareness that parental affection can burden. He increasingly substitutes action for sentiment, preferring to serve his son's interests quietly rather than "inflict" emotion upon him.
## Key Passages
> "All the information and advice I give you, is the result of my own dear-bought experience, and flows from that tenderness and affection with which, while I live, I shall be prompted to guide and guard you." (040-004)
> "My true tenderness for you makes me think more of your future interest than of your present inclinations." (099-002)
> "You know my tenderness, I know your affection. My only object is, and always will be, your interest and your happiness." (152-001)
> "Instead of inflicting my tenderness upon you, I have taken all possible pains to inform myself of your affairs." (164-006)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's implicit guidance on tenderness, drawn from his own practice:
- Let tenderness be the motive of your care for others, but do not make it the subject of your discourse
- Express affection through useful action rather than through protestation
- When giving counsel to those you love, ground it in experience and benefit, not in sentiment
- Beware of "inflicting" tenderness — affection that demands acknowledgment becomes a burden
- The father who truly loves his son will risk the son's temporary displeasure to secure his lasting welfare
## Classical References
Chesterfield's treatment of tenderness draws implicitly on the Stoic paradox of the sage's affections: the wise man loves, but without the perturbation that ungoverned emotion produces. The Horatian paternal voice — particularly in the *Epistles* — provides a classical model for the father who instructs through affectionate letters. Chesterfield's "jubeo te bene valere" (I bid you farewell) echoes the Roman epistolary convention of closing with a blessing that conveys warmth without effusion.
