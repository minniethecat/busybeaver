## Definition

Credulity, in Chesterfield's intellectual vocabulary, is the vice of excessive readiness to believe — the failure to exercise the critical faculty that distinguishes the man of sense from the dupe. It is the opposite of penetration: where penetration "discovers" the truth beneath appearances, credulity accepts appearances as truth and is therefore the natural prey of the cunning, the dishonest, and the merely mistaken. Chesterfield warns that "the penetration of princes seldom goes deeper than the surface" (090-002) — the credulity of those in power makes them vulnerable to flatterers and deceivers. Credulity is not merely an intellectual failing but a practical danger: the credulous man is "the dupe of his own credulity" (288-001, paraphrased), exploited by those who recognize and exploit his weakness. "Few people have penetration enough to discover, attention enough to observe, or even to report, the truth" (141-001) — credulity is the default human condition; penetration is the rare and hard-won exception.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of credulity is distributed across the correspondence as a recurrent warning. The vulnerability of the powerful is a persistent theme: "the penetration of princes seldom goes deeper than the surface, and courts are the places where the art of covering the surface is most practiced" (090-002) — credulity at the highest level of power invites systematic deception. The cunning man exploits the credulous: the "crafty man thinks to gain all his points by trick and artifice" (133-002), succeeding precisely because most people lack the penetration to see through his schemes. The connection between credulity and the scarcity of truth is made explicit: "few people have penetration enough to discover, attention enough to observe, or even to report, the truth" (141-001). The most developed reflection appears in the political observation about self-deception: even intelligent people "have been duped by low cunning" and become "the dupe of his own cunning, and of the artifices which he himself set as snares for others" (288-001) — credulity can be turned inward, the credulous man becoming the victim of his own devices.

## Related Concepts

Credulity is the direct opposite of [[penetration]]: penetration discovers truth, credulity accepts appearance (090-002, 141-001). It is closely allied to [[gullibility]] and the want of [[skepticism]] — the intellectual dispositions that make a man exploitable. [[Cunning]] preys upon credulity: the cunning man succeeds because the credulous man fails to see through him (133-002). [[Attention]] is credulity's preventative — "few people have penetration enough to discover, attention enough to observe" (141-001). [[Judgment]] is the faculty that credulity bypasses. The [[gentleman-ideal]] requires a calibrated skepticism: the gentleman is neither credulous nor cynical, but exercises the penetration that sees things as they are.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on avoiding credulity:

- **Cultivate penetration**: "Few people have penetration enough to discover... the truth" (141-001) — the best defense against credulity is the active cultivation of the faculty that sees through surfaces
- **Question the surface at courts**: Courts are "the places where the art of covering the surface is most practiced" (090-002) — where power concentrates, deception concentrates; credulity there is most dangerous
- **Recognize that cunning preys on credulity**: The cunning man's schemes succeed only because the credulous fail to detect them (133-002) — credulity is not merely an intellectual error but an invitation to exploitation
- **Beware self-deception**: The credulous man can become "the dupe of his own cunning" (288-001) — credulity turned inward is the most dangerous form of all, for the self-deceived man has no external check on his errors

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of credulity echoes the classical analysis of *credulitas* — the excessive readiness to believe that the Roman moralists classified as a form of *stultitia* (foolishness). Cicero in *De Divinatione* treats credulity as the enemy of reason: the credulous man believes what he wishes to be true rather than what evidence supports. The Baconian framework that Chesterfield invokes in his treatment of cunning — "crooked wisdom" — is equally applicable to credulity: Bacon's doctrine of the Idols identifies the human tendency to believe too readily as one of the principal obstacles to knowledge. Chesterfield's observation that "few people have penetration enough to discover... the truth" (141-001) echoes the skeptical tradition from Montaigne through Bayle: most people believe most things most of the time without sufficient grounds, and the rare person who exercises genuine critical judgment is the exception, not the rule.
