# Grace

## Definition

**The graces** are, in Chesterfield's system, the set of exterior qualities — ease of manner, charm of address, elegance of movement and speech — that make a person pleasing in company. They are distinguished from solid virtues and accomplishments: the graces do not substitute for merit, but merit without the graces is, for Chesterfield, a treasure locked in a chest to which no one has the key. The graces are the medium through which all other qualities become effective.

> "To do all these things in the most correct and elegant manner, and with that ease and grace, are certainly to be acquired by observation and imitation of the best models." (001-003)

Chesterfield's almost obsessive insistence on "the graces, the graces, the graces" (P01-001) is the signature theme of the entire correspondence — he returns to it in the preface and never abandons it.

## In the Letters

The graces appear from the earliest letters as Chesterfield's central educational concern. In the preface, the editor notes that Chesterfield "cared not a fig for 'the graces, the graces, the graces'" (P01-003) — meaning he cared enormously, to the exclusion of almost everything else. The first letter proper establishes the program: "a genteel carriage, and graceful, engaging address" (001-003) are to be acquired by studying the best models.

> "That engaging manner, and those graces, which seduce and prepossess people in your favor at first sight." (023-005)

The middle letters develop the distinction between the graces and solid merit. Chesterfield insists that the graces are *acquired*, not innate — they come from "observation and imitation" (001-003), not birth. The late letters show the graces operating in professional contexts: "the graceful manner of doing all these things" (032-001) is what distinguishes the successful courtier from the merely competent one.

> "The graceful manner of doing all these things is as necessary as the things themselves." (032-001)

> "Good God! if this ungraceful and disagreeable manner of speaking were the only way to Heaven, I would not go there." (042-001)

> "It is full as necessary to speak gracefully and distinctly upon them." (044-005)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's advice on acquiring the graces is intensely practical:

- **Study the best models** — observe graceful people and imitate their manner, not slavishly but analytically (001-003)
- **Cultivate ease** — grace is the opposite of affectation; forced elegance is no elegance at all
- **Mind your exterior** — "exterior graces of person and address" (043-003) are improvable and indispensable
- **Speak gracefully** — Chesterfield ranks graceful speaking second only to graceful carriage (042-002)
- **Avoid ungraceful habits** — awkwardness, slouching, dirty appearance "disgrace and foul themselves" (027-005)
- **Remember the graces are acquired** — they are not gifts of birth but products of "observation and imitation" (001-003)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's "graces" are a translation of the Roman concept of *decorum* — the fitting, graceful, appropriate conduct that Cicero treats at length in *De Officiis*. The tripartite emphasis on "the graces, the graces, the graces" (P01-001) echoes the classical triad of *venustas* (charm), *dignitas* (dignity), and *suavitas* (sweetness of manner) that together compose the ideal social presentation. Chesterfield's insistence that the graces are acquired rather than innate also reflects the Stoic doctrine that virtue, including social virtue, is teachable.
