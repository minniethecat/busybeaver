# London
## In the Letters
London is the gravitational center of Chesterfield's world. From his town house—Chesterfield House, built with architectural elements purchased from the Duke of Chandos's estate at Cannons—he conducted his political career, received society, and wrote the majority of his letters to his son abroad.
The city appears in the letters not as a described place but as the implicit standard against which all other places are measured. When Chesterfield writes of "the best company" or "people of fashion," he means London society. The city is the destination to which Philip Stanhope must eventually return, properly polished and prepared.
## Chesterfield House
Chesterfield's London residence was furnished with a celebrated collection of art and antiquities. His purchase of the hall pillars, floor, and staircase from the Duke of Chandos's magnificent house at Cannons (about eight miles from London) is mentioned in the letters: "I am very glad that Mr. Lyttelton approves of my new house, and particularly of my CANONICAL pillars" (045-004). A fine bust of Cicero held the place of honor in his library.
## Political and Social Life
As a peer of the realm, Secretary of State, and later Lord-Lieutenant of Ireland, Chesterfield moved in the highest circles of London politics. The letters reflect the preoccupations of a man who understood that London was where reputations were made and unmade: "People in general take characters, as they do most things, upon trust... and the decisions of four or five fashionable people, in every place, are final."
