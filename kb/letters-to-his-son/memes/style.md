## Definition

Chesterfield's concept of style is comprehensive: it covers writing, speech, deportment, and even architecture. Style is the *how* that makes the *what* effective. A just argument delivered in a "homely, coarse, and vulgar" style fails (091-003); a mediocre argument elegantly expressed may succeed. Style, in Chesterfield's view, is not decoration applied after thought is complete — it is integral to thought itself, because unclear expression reflects unclear thinking, and graceful expression wins the hearing that substance alone cannot command.

> "Let them be ever so just, if your style is homely, coarse, and vulgar, they will be rejected."
> (091-003)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's attention to style intensifies as his son matures:

**Early letters (1746–1748)**: Style is introduced as a component of classical education. The son is urged to study the best authors — Cicero, Horace, Demosthenes — not just for their content but for their *manner*. Chesterfield distinguishes between the "friendly and the familiar style" (011-001), noting that different occasions demand different registers.

**Middle period (1749–1754)**: Style becomes a central preoccupation. Chesterfield devotes long passages to the mechanics of elegant expression: purity of language, elegance of diction, harmony of periods, the turn of a sentence. He recommends the best English stylists — Dryden, Atterbury, Swift — as models. The letters on style (especially Letter 091) are among the most technically detailed in the entire correspondence.

**Late letters (1755–1767)**: Style is increasingly discussed in architectural and aesthetic contexts. Palladio's "taste and style of buildings" (077-002) illustrates that style is a universal category: the principles of proportion, harmony, and elegance apply to buildings, sentences, and manners alike.

> "The ornaments and the graces of style, elocution, and action. Sense and argument, though ever so just, will not do alone."
> (093-003)

## Key Passages

> "Let them be ever so just, if your style is homely, coarse, and vulgar, they will be rejected."
> (091-003)

The core Chesterfieldian thesis on style: substance without elegance is politically and socially useless. This is not cynicism but realism — the world judges by appearances, and the wise man dresses his thoughts as carefully as his person.

> "The simplicity and the clearness of the friendly and the familiar style."
> (011-001)

Chesterfield distinguishes registers of style. Not all occasions call for elevated oratory; the familiar letter requires a different style — simpler, clearer, warmer — than the Parliamentary speech.

> "A pure and elegant English style: it requires nothing but application."
> (091-010)

Style is acquirable, not inborn. Chesterfield insists that any young man of ordinary abilities can develop an elegant style through study of the best models and diligent practice.

> "The elegance of the style, and the turn of the periods, make the chief merit of an orator."
> (094-003)

In the classical rhetorical tradition, *elocutio* (style) was one of the five canons of rhetoric. Chesterfield echoes this: style is not incidental to eloquence but constitutive of it.

> "With particular attention to the style, and to all those beauties of composition."
> (095-004)

Chesterfield recommends reading the best authors with an eye to their technique — not passively absorbing but actively analyzing *how* they achieve their effects.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on style is among his most detailed:

1. **Style is not optional**. "Sense and argument, though ever so just, will not do alone" (093-003). A man who neglects style neglects the instrument of his own effectiveness.

2. **Study the best models**. Chesterfield names English stylists — Dryden, Atterbury, Swift, Bolingbroke — and urges his son to read them with analytical attention. "Particular attention to the style, and to all those beauties of composition" (095-004).

3. **Purity first, elegance second**. Before attempting ornate periods, master correctness: grammar, diction, clarity. "A pure and elegant English style: it requires nothing but application" (091-010). Purity is the foundation; elegance is the superstructure.

4. **Match style to occasion**. The "friendly and the familiar style" (011-001) suits letters and conversation; the elevated style suits Parliament and formal occasions. A man of judgment knows which register to deploy.

5. **Style is acquirable through practice**. Chesterfield insists that elegant style "requires nothing but application" (091-010). It is not a gift of genius but the product of methodical effort — reading, imitating, writing, and revising.

6. **Vulgar style wounds substance**. An argument that would be persuasive if elegantly expressed "will be rejected" (091-003) if the style is coarse. This is the harsh reality of social life; the wise man adapts to it rather than protesting it.

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of style is deeply grounded in the classical rhetorical tradition:

- **Cicero's *Orator***: Cicero's three levels of style (*genus tenue*, *genus medium*, *genus grande*) and his ideal of the accomplished orator who commands all three is the foundation of Chesterfield's teaching. The son is being trained as a Ciceronian orator adapted for eighteenth-century politics.

- **Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria***: Quintilian's comprehensive treatment of style — from grammar and usage to the figures of speech and the rhythm of periods — provides the technical framework. Chesterfield's attention to "purity of diction" and "elegance of style" (093-002) echoes Quintilian's categories.

- **Longinus's *On the Sublime***: The ideal of stylistic elevation that transports the hearer is present in Chesterfield's insistence on the "harmony of periods" and "the turn of the periods" (094-003). Great style, for Chesterfield as for Longinus, is not merely correct but *moving*.

- **Palladio and Vitruvius**: Chesterfield's extension of "style" to architecture (077-002) reflects the classical tradition, inherited through the Renaissance, that sees proportion, harmony, and decorum as universal principles governing all arts.

Chesterfield's distinctiveness is his insistence on the *social* function of style. Classical rhetoric treated style as an art of persuasion; Chesterfield treats it as an art of *reputation*. Elegant style is not merely what makes an argument persuasive; it is what makes a man welcome in the best company, trusted in politics, and remembered by posterity.
