# Truth

## Definition

Chesterfield addresses **truth** on two distinct levels: as a moral absolute — the obligation of truthfulness that every gentleman must uphold — and as a social practice that requires nuance and judgment. He insists on the fundamental duty of truthfulness, calling it an obligation binding "in all things" (P01-004), yet he also recognizes that the world does not always reward unvarnished honesty. The art, as he presents it, lies in being fundamentally truthful while possessing the "dexterity enough to conceal a truth without telling a lie" (025-002) — a formulation that encapsulates his worldly approach to a moral ideal.

> "The obligation of truthfulness in all things, and of honor in dealing with the world." (P01-004)

## In the Letters

Truth appears throughout the correspondence as both a moral requirement and a practical consideration. In the early letters, Chesterfield establishes truthfulness as a non-negotiable foundation of character. By the middle letters, he refines this into a more sophisticated treatment: a gentleman must be truthful in substance but need not always be candid in expression. He distinguishes sharply between the diplomatic concealment of truth (which is permissible and often wise) and the telling of a lie (which is never acceptable).

Chesterfield also deploys the phrase "to tell you the plain truth" as a rhetorical device in his own letters, signaling moments of particular candor or criticism — as when he admits doubts about his son's progress (028-002).

## Key Passages

> "The obligation of truthfulness in all things, and of honor in dealing with the world. His preface insists on these." (P01-004)

> "In truth, whatever is worth doing at all, is worth doing well." (001-004)

> "Every man seeks for truth; but God only knows who has found it." (014-001)

> "There is nothing more engaging than beauty. This truth is evident, from the studied ornaments of every age and country." (017-005)

> "You have dexterity enough to conceal a truth without telling a lie; sagacity enough to perceive that truth itself may be ill-timed." (025-002)

> "Which, to tell you the plain truth, were what I doubted of the most." (028-002)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's advice on truth is notably more nuanced than his treatment of other virtues:

- Uphold the fundamental obligation of truthfulness — this is non-negotiable (P01-004)
- Learn to "conceal a truth without telling a lie" — diplomatic silence is a skill, not a vice (025-002)
- Recognize that "truth itself may be ill-timed" — timing and context matter (025-002)
- Do not confuse social discretion with dishonesty; the former is a necessary art of living in the world
