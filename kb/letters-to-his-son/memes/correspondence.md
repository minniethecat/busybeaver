## Definition

Correspondence in Chesterfield's world is far more than the mechanical exchange of letters — it is the primary medium through which he educates his son, maintains political and social connections across Europe, and exercises the art of epistolary persuasion. His own letters constitute a sustained pedagogical correspondence designed to transmit not only information but character. As he writes, "I like your correspondence better than that of all the kings and princes in Europe" (027-002), elevating the father-son epistolary bond above even diplomatic communication.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of correspondence evolves with the rhythm of his son's travels. In the early letters, he eagerly solicits regular correspondence as proof of filial affection and a channel for instruction. The quality of his son's letters becomes a barometer of his progress: when they are frequent and detailed, Chesterfield is pleased; when they lapse, he reproaches. During his son's time at Hanover, Chesterfield complains that "your correspondence has been both unfrequent and laconic" (176-001), treating epistolary neglect as a failure of duty and affection. He distinguishes between different registers of correspondence — the "confidential correspondence" (052-006) of political intimacy, the "literary correspondence" (127-007) with his son's tutor, and the "secret correspondences" (151-001) of diplomatic intelligence. In the later letters, with his health declining, he reflects that in the "long course of our correspondence" (220-001) he has exhausted all he has to teach. The metaphor of correspondence as financial exchange recurs: being "in arrears in my correspondence" (239-001) is a debt to be discharged.

## Related Concepts

Correspondence is interwoven with Chesterfield's core educational project. [[education]] is the purpose of the father-son correspondence; the letters are a curriculum in epistolary form. [[duty]] is the obligation that binds son to father in regular written communication. [[secrecy]] and [[discretion]] govern the handling of confidential political correspondence (132-014, 151-001). [[attention]] is what Chesterfield demands of his son as a reader and what he himself lavishes as a writer. [[knowledge]] flows through correspondence; it is the conduit by which political intelligence travels across Europe (135-001). The decline of correspondence signals the decline of the relationship itself.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical counsel on correspondence includes:

- **Write regularly and at length**: A short, infrequent letter is a mark of negligence; a regular, detailed letter demonstrates affection and attention (176-001, 027-002)
- **Match your register to your correspondent**: A "confidential correspondence" differs from a "literary" or diplomatic one; know what tone and content each relationship requires (052-006, 127-007)
- **Do not claim exhaustion prematurely**: Chesterfield declares at one point that he has exhausted all he can supply (145-001), yet finds more to teach — correspondence is a renewable resource
- **Treat correspondence as a form of presence**: In the absence of physical proximity, letters maintain the relationship; they are a duty and a bond (239-001, 267-008)
- **Preserve discretion in political letters**: Ciphers were used for diplomatic correspondence; never commit sensitive matter to an ordinary letter (132-014, 135-001)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's epistolary practice belongs to a long tradition of letter-writing as an art form — from Cicero's *Epistulae ad Familiares* to Pliny the Younger's carefully composed literary letters, to the eighteenth-century culture of letter-writing that produced the great correspondences of Madame de Sévigné and Lord Bolingbroke. Cicero's letters to his son Marcus, though Chesterfield does not directly cite them as a model, provide the classical archetype of paternal epistolary instruction. The use of letters as vehicles for political intelligence (135-001) reflects the early modern development of diplomatic correspondence as a formal practice.
