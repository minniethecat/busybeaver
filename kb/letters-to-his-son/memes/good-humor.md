## Definition

Good humor, in Chesterfield's vocabulary, is not mere joviality or wit but a sustained disposition of cheerful equanimity — the social grace that enables a person to engage with others pleasantly, deflect conflict, and maintain an agreeable presence in every situation. It is distinct from wit, which can wound; from gaiety, which can exhaust; and from flattery, which can disgust. Good humor is the temper of a mind at ease with itself and therefore able to put others at ease. Chesterfield treats it as an essential component of the art of pleasing: "the knowledge of a scholar, the courage of a hero, and the virtue of a saint, will be absolutely useless without the graces; and the greatest of these is good humor" (098-004, paraphrased).

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of good humor spans the full correspondence. He models its use in conversation early on: one may deflect disagreement "by saying, with good humor, 'We shall hardly convince one another'" (017-001). Good humor is the indispensable lubricant of correction and refusal: even when compelled to deny a request, "do it with good humor, good-breeding, and (if you have it) with urbanity" (055-006). When provoked, the wise man laughs "off the whole thing in seeming good humor; but by no means reply in the same strain" (071-003) — good humor here is both shield and stratagem. Chesterfield ranks it among the essential social endowments alongside "affability, complaisance, and good humor" (098-004), and warns that even "warmth of festal mirth" must not "make you forget for a moment that you are a gentleman" (100-003). In the domestic sphere, good humor "promotes good-will in families" (145-002). Its absence is conspicuous: a man "in high good humor and a flow of spirits" can charm; the same man in ill humor repels (147-004).

> "…conversation, by saying, with good humor, 'We shall hardly convince one another, nor is it necessary that we should, so let us talk of something else.'" (017-001)

> "…steadily; but then do it with good humor, good-breeding, and (if you have it) with urbanity." (055-006)

> "…off the whole thing in seeming good humor; but by no means reply in the same strain, which would be accepting the challenge." (071-003)

> "…affability, complaisance, and good humor. The knowledge of a scholar, the courage of a hero, and the virtue of a saint, will be absolutely useless, if unattended with the graces." (098-004)

> "…care that no complaisance, no good-humor, no warmth of festal mirth, ever make you forget for a moment that you are a gentleman." (100-003)

> "…within bounds; they promote good-humor and good-will in families, which are the best securities against those petty quarrels." (145-002)

## Related Concepts

Good humor is the companion of [[good-breeding]]: breeding provides the form, good humor the spirit (055-006). It overlaps with [[complaisance]] and [[affability]] (098-004) but is distinct from both — complaisance is the will to please, good humor the temper that makes pleasing natural. [[Cheerfulness]] is good humor's outward expression (147-004). It is opposed to [[captiousness]], [[sullenness]], and [[ill-nature]] — the dispositions that make a man "insupportable in company" (200-001). [[Urbanity]] is good humor refined by wit and learning (055-006). [[Good-nature]] is the inward benevolence of which good humor is the external manifestation. In the social economy of Chesterfield's world, good humor is the universal solvent — it dissolves tension, disarms hostility, and opens doors that merit alone cannot.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's specific recommendations for cultivating good humor:

- **Use it to deflect conflict**: When a conversation threatens to become a dispute, good-humoredly change the subject — "We shall hardly convince one another, so let us talk of something else" (017-001)
- **Combine it with correction**: Even necessary refusals or reproofs should be delivered "with good humor, good-breeding, and urbanity" (055-006)
- **Use it as armor**: When provoked, laugh it off "in seeming good humor" rather than rising to the challenge (071-003) — good humor here is a form of strategic self-command
- **Don't let mirth override judgment**: Even "warmth of festal mirth" must not make you forget you are a gentleman (100-003); good humor has its own decorum
- **Cultivate it at home**: Good humor "promotes good-will in families" (145-002); the domestic sphere is both the nursery and the test of genuine good humor

## Classical References

Chesterfield's good humor echoes the classical ideal of *eutrapelia* — the virtue of pleasantness in social intercourse that Aristotle describes in the *Nicomachean Ethics* as the mean between buffoonery and boorishness. Cicero in *De Officiis* treats *iucunditas* (agreeableness) and *urbanitas* (refined wit) as essential to the decorum of the accomplished man. The Roman concept of *comitas* — the gracious affability that the powerful should show to those beneath them — informs Chesterfield's insistence that good humor must accompany correction and refusal (055-006). Horace, whom Chesterfield frequently cites, made good-humored satire his signature mode — the ability to reprove without wounding, to amuse without malice. Chesterfield's good humor is the Horatian virtue adapted to the drawing rooms and courts of eighteenth-century Europe.
