# Philip Stanhope
## Biography
Philip Stanhope (1732 – 16 November 1768) was the illegitimate son of Philip Dormer Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield, and a French governess. He was the recipient of over 400 letters from his father, collectively known as *Letters to His Son*, which constitute one of the most famous correspondences in English literature.
Philip was educated under the rigorous supervision of his father, who engaged tutors—notably the Reverend Walter Harte—to accompany him on a European Grand Tour. He studied at Lausanne, Leipzig, and other continental centres, acquiring fluency in French, German, Italian, and the classical languages. Despite this intensive education, Philip never quite fulfilled his father's hopes of becoming a polished courtier and statesman.
He served in minor diplomatic posts, including a position at the British embassy in Paris, and was elected to Parliament. His career, however, was unremarkable. In 1768, at the age of thirty-six, he died in the south of France. After his death, it was discovered that he had been secretly married for several years to Eugenia Stanhope and had two sons. Lord Chesterfield, who had known nothing of this marriage, accepted the situation with stoic dignity and provided for his grandsons.
## In the Letters
Philip appears in the letters as "Dear Boy"—the perpetual student, forever being instructed, admonished, and encouraged. Chesterfield's tone ranges from genuine affection to exasperated disappointment. The letters trace Philip's journey from a schoolboy in Lausanne to a young diplomat in Paris, Rome, and Germany.
Key themes in Chesterfield's advice to Philip include:
1. **The cultivation of "the graces"**: Philip's greatest failing, in his father's eyes, was his neglect of manners, address, and social charm.
2. **Intellectual discipline**: Chesterfield urged systematic study of history, geography, and languages.
3. **Moral conduct**: Though Chesterfield's moral philosophy was worldly rather than pious, he consistently insisted on truthfulness and honor.
4. **Social advancement**: Every letter contains practical advice on how to ingratiate oneself with people of rank and influence.
## Key Attributes
A Venetian lady's portrait of the young Philip Stanhope, commissioned by Chesterfield, provides the most vivid contemporary description:
> "His face is pleasing, his countenance sensible, and his look clever. His figure is at present rather too square; but if he shoots up, which he has matter and years for, he will then be of a good size. He has, undoubtedly, a great fund of acquired knowledge... As for French, I know he speaks it perfectly, and, I am told, German as well. The questions he asks are judicious; and denote a thirst after knowledge. I cannot say that he appears equally desirous of pleasing, for he seems to neglect attentions and the graces." (092-002)
Chesterfield himself acknowledged Philip's intellectual strengths while lamenting his social shortcomings: "If little Stanhope acquires the graces, I promise you he will make his way; if not, he will be stopped in a course, the goal of which he might attain with honor."
