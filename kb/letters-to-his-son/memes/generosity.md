## Definition

Chesterfield's treatment of generosity is economical but pointed. He distinguishes between two forms: genuine generosity, which flows from good-nature and is directed by sound judgment, and spurious generosity, which is merely prodigality dressed in the language of virtue — "commonly called generosity and magnanimity, but is, in truth, nothing but the want of economy and order" (133-005). The distinction is characteristic of Chesterfield's larger moral framework: a virtue is not defined by its appearance but by its source and its consequences.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's direct discussions of generosity are brief, but they encode a broader principle that runs through the letters: character is revealed not by grand gestures but by the steady, unostentatious practice of virtue. True generosity, in this view, is not the spectacular gift but the consistent disposition to give — of money, time, attention, and forbearance — without calculation of return.

The negative case is instructive. When Chesterfield describes a man as having "more good-nature and generosity than parts" (083-007), he is offering measured praise: generosity is a real virtue, but without judgment to guide it, it is insufficient for high office. The man with generosity but without intellect may be beloved in private life but is a liability in public affairs. The complementary warning — that what passes for generosity may be mere disorder — completes the picture: generosity must be both genuine in motive and prudent in execution.

## Key Passages

> "He has more good-nature and generosity than parts. However, I will send you to him as well recommended as I can." (083-007)

> "This is commonly called generosity and magnanimity, but is, in truth, nothing but the want of economy and order, and a facility of disposition, which makes people incapable of resisting the impulse of their present passions, or of denying themselves anything that they think will please." (133-005)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's implicit advice on generosity can be reconstructed from his framing. **Give from good-nature, not from impulse**: true generosity is a settled disposition, not a spasmodic response to the desire to please. **Let judgment direct liberality**: a generous act that impoverishes the giver or enables the recipient's vices is not virtuous. **Beware the reputation of generosity**: since false generosity is widely admired, the temptation to perform rather than practice the virtue is strong. The gentleman who spends beyond his means to appear generous has achieved neither generosity nor prudence.

## Classical References

Chesterfield does not cite classical sources directly on generosity, but his distinction between genuine and spurious generosity echoes Aristotle's treatment of liberality (eleutheriotēs) as the mean between prodigality and stinginess, as well as the Stoic emphasis on intention over appearance. The critique of generosity-as-impulse in 133-005 is particularly close to the Aristotelian view that virtue is a settled disposition (hexis), not a momentary passion.
