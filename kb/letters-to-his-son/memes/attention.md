# Attention
## Definition
In Lord Chesterfield's educational philosophy, **attention** is the deliberate application of one's mental faculties to the task at hand — whether study, conversation, or social observation. It is contrasted with *inattention*, *laziness*, and *absence of mind*, which Chesterfield considers among the most damning faults in a young man.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield returns to the theme of attention throughout the correspondence (1746–1767), making it one of the most frequently invoked concepts. It appears in three distinct contexts:
1. **Intellectual attention** — the focused study of history, geography, languages, and other subjects necessary for a diplomat's education
2. **Social attention** — the art of observing and adapting to others in company, flattering their vanity through small acts of consideration
3. **Moral attention** — the scrupulous care for one's own character and reputation
## Key Passages
> "In truth, whatever is worth doing at all, is worth doing well; and nothing can be done well without attention: I therefore carry the necessity of attention down to the lowest things, even to dancing and dress." (001-004)
> "Ancient and modern history are, by attention, easily attainable. Geography and chronology the same, none of them requiring any uncommon share of genius or invention." (001-003)
> "Remember to have that constant attention about you which flatters every man's little vanity; and the want of which, by mortifying his pride, never fails to excite his resentment, or at least his ill will." (001-005)
> "Your moral character must be not only pure, but, like Caesar's wife, unsuspected. The least speck or blemish upon it is fatal." (100-002)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's advice on cultivating attention includes:
- Examine, improve, and strengthen your understanding every day (001-002)
- Never show inattention in company — it signals contempt
- Take the tone of whatever company you are in, rather than displaying your boredom
- Remember and act upon others' likes and aversions
- Apply attention even to seemingly trivial accomplishments (dancing, dress)
## Classical References
Chesterfield frames attention through the Stoic emphasis on self-mastery, quoting classical maxims:
- *"Nullum numen abest, si sit prudentia"* — "No divine power is absent if prudence is present" (001-003)
- Caesar's *"Nil actum reputans, si quid superesset agendum"* — "Thinking nothing done while anything remains to be done" (001-003)
