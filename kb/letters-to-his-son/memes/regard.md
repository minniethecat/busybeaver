# Regard

## Definition

**Regard**, in Chesterfield's vocabulary, denotes the respectful attention, consideration, and esteem that one person shows to another. It occupies a middle ground between cold politeness and warm affection — more considered than mere civility, less intimate than love. Chesterfield treats regard as a currency of social exchange: it is what one offers to secure goodwill, what one earns through merit, and what one must never neglect to display if one wishes to rise in the world. Expressions of regard "flatter the self-love of those they are applied to" and are thus among the most practical tools of social advancement (032-001).

> "Expressions of regard and esteem; by complaisance, by attentions, by every thing that shows a desire of pleasing." (032-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield distinguishes regard from affection in a passage that maps the emotional geography of human relationships: "affection on one side, and regard on the other, make up the duties of the several relations of life" (020-004). Affection belongs to the intimate sphere; regard governs the broader social world of acquaintance, profession, and public life. A gentleman must be fluent in both languages.

The display of regard is a practical necessity. Chesterfield urges his son to show "unquestionable proofs of the regard and consideration which we have for people" (017-007), for without such demonstrations, regard exists only in one's own mind and does no social work. The point is characteristic: a virtue unexpressed is a virtue wasted. Regard must be made visible through attentions, complaisance, and every mark of consideration.

Chesterfield also notes the difficulty of maintaining regard for those who show none for themselves: it requires "complaisance to those who have no regard to their own" (007-007). This is a test of social discipline — the ability to treat others with consideration even when they do not deserve it. The alternative is to be "unregarded" (035-001), ignored and forgotten in the social scramble.

## Key Passages

> "Unquestionable proofs of the regard and consideration which we have for people." (017-007)

> "Affection on one side, and regard on the other, make up the duties of the several relations of life." (020-004)

> "Expressions of regard and esteem; by complaisance, by attentions, by every thing that shows a desire of pleasing, and a yielding to the opinions and humors of others." (032-001)

> "It requires a great share of patience and complaisance to those who have no regard to their own." (007-007)

> "Many a very good speech [is] unregarded, upon account of the disagreeable manner in which it was delivered." (035-001)

## Practical Advice

- Make regard visible — "expressions of regard and esteem" must be shown through attentions and complaisance, not merely felt (032-001)
- Distinguish affection from regard — the former is for intimates, the latter governs all social relations (020-004)
- Offer "unquestionable proofs" of the consideration you hold for others — unexpressed respect is socially useless (017-007)
- Maintain regard even for those who lack self-regard — it is a discipline of character and good breeding (007-007)
- Guard against being "unregarded" — neglect of manner can render even excellent substance invisible (035-001)
