# Negligence

## Definition

**Negligence**, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is not mere forgetfulness but a form of moral and social failure. He introduces it in the very first letter as a theme of central importance, linking negligence of dress, speech, and conduct to a deeper negligence of character. Chesterfield's concept of negligence encompasses both the passive failure to attend to one's duties and the active carelessness that signals indifference to the opinions of others. In his taxonomy of faults, negligence sits between idleness (not doing) and positive vice (doing wrong), but is no less damaging to a young gentleman's prospects.

> "A propos of negligence: I must say something to you upon that subject." (001-003)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield returns to the theme of negligence with remarkable frequency, from the very first letter through the mature correspondence. The opening letter frames negligence as the opposite of the attention he so prizes: while attention opens doors and wins friends, negligence lets opportunity slip away (001-005).

In the middle letters, the concept sharpens into specific domains. Negligence in speech — habits that "either by your negligence or mine, become habitual to you" (042-001) — is treated as a social liability that can alienate others as surely as active rudeness. Negligence of dress is "very alienating; and a total negligence of dress and air is an impertinent insult upon the company" (042-002). Chesterfield is careful to distinguish negligence from the opposite extreme of foppish vanity, preferring to be thought "a fop than a sloven" (082-004).

Chesterfield also draws a moral economy around negligence: it is wasteful, "the idle lavishness of negligence and laziness" that squanders time and money alike (062-003). Security and abundance, he warns, "commonly produces negligence and indolence" (069-001), suggesting that negligence thrives in comfort and must be actively guarded against.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on avoiding negligence is characteristically specific:

- Attend to dress not from vanity but from respect for others — negligence of appearance insults the company you keep (042-002)
- Guard against the negligence that comfort breeds — prosperity and security make negligence easy; vigilance must be constant (069-001)
- Treat negligence as a form of waste — time, money, and opportunity are all lost through negligence as surely as through active vice (062-003)
- Correct negligent habits in speech before they become fixed — what begins as carelessness hardens into a permanent defect of character (042-001)
- Prefer even excessive care to none at all — "I would rather a fop than a sloven" (082-004)
