# Folly

## Definition

**Folly**, for Chesterfield, is not the absence of intelligence but the corruption of judgment — and its consequences are practical, not philosophical. He is not interested in folly as a theological category (the "fool hath said in his heart" of the Psalmist) but as a social liability: the man who acts against his own interest through vanity, impatience, or self-deception. Folly is what makes a man lose a friend for a jest, stake his fortune on a speculative gamble, or suppose that flatterers are friends. Chesterfield's treatment of folly is relentless because its cost is real: "It is a decided folly to lose a friend for a jest" (071-003).

> "Loud laughter is the characteristic of folly and ill manners; it is the manner in which the mob express their silly joy at silly things." (032-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's earliest reference to folly is autobiographical: he confesses to having affected a certain manner "but this folly I soon laid aside, upon finding that it was an improper one" (007-004). This sets the pattern for all his subsequent treatments: folly is an error that can and should be corrected by observation and reflection. The young man who persists in folly after being warned is not unlucky — he is willful.

In Letter XVI, he warns against the folly of mistaking a social circle of indulgence for genuine friendship: young men surround themselves with "idle sauntering people" who "have the impudence and folly to call this confederacy a friendship" (016-001). These false friends "flatter every vice and every folly you have, in order to engage you to continue that connection" (016-003). The mechanism is clear: folly attracts flatterers, who reinforce the folly, creating a downward spiral.

Chesterfield frequently pairs folly with its cousin, **vice**, but the distinction is important. In Letter LXXXIV he defines legitimate raillery as "that which has neither vice nor folly for its object" (084-003), and reiterates in LXXXVI: "it is, if neither vice nor folly are the objects of it" (086-002). Vice and folly are the two things that justify criticism — everything else should be borne with tolerance.

By the middle letters, folly becomes a diagnostic category. "Knavery and folly have often the same symptoms" (097-006) — a disturbing observation that complicates moral judgment: the knave and the fool may look identical from the outside, but one is culpable and the other pitiable. Chesterfield does not always distinguish them: in Letter XCVIII, he declares that a man's suffering was "justly merited: yours, by your folly; his, by his wisdom. The arrogance of some men can contrive, the folly of others receive" (098-005, 085-002). Folly is not merely passive — it collaborates with villainy.

The most philosophically developed treatment appears in Letter CIV: "I am a severe censor of vice and folly, I am a friend and advocate for every error of the understanding" (104-006). This is Chesterfield's clearest statement of his moral epistemology: errors of understanding (simple mistakes) deserve compassion; folly (persistent misjudgment rooted in vanity or self-love) deserves censure. The difference is not in the act but in its source.

In the later letters, he broadens the lens: folly encompasses gambling (110-002), affectation (113-001), and self-deception (160-002). The thread connecting them all is the refusal to see things as they are — what Chesterfield would call a failure of "knowledge of the world."

## Key Passages

> "I was of an age and disposition to imitate even the faults of those I liked and esteemed; and affected a certain manner of speaking, writing, conversation, and application, which I thought very refined; but this folly I soon laid aside, upon finding that it was an improper one." (007-004)

> "They have the impudence and folly to call this confederacy a friendship." (016-001)

> "Loud laughter is the characteristic of folly and ill manners; it is the manner in which the mob express their silly joy at silly things." (032-001)

> "It is a decided folly to lose a friend for a jest; but, in my mind, it is not a much less degree of folly, to make a friend of him whom we cannot esteem." (071-003)

> "Knavery and folly have often the same symptoms." (097-006)

> "I am a severe censor of vice and folly, I am a friend and advocate for every error of the understanding." (104-006)

> "From a consciousness of its folly, as well as of its guilt; such modesty and tenderness, such an utter abhorrence of every kind of indecency, that a woman cannot, without blushing, hear it named." (113-001)

> "I will point out to you the weakness and the folly of this reasoning, if it deserves the name of reasoning." (160-002)

## Practical Advice

- Treat folly as an error to be corrected by observation, not a fixed trait — Chesterfield himself "soon laid aside" his early follies (007-004)
- Distinguish errors of understanding (deserving compassion) from persistent folly rooted in vanity (deserving censure) — the difference lies in the source (104-006)
- Beware the social circle that flatters your follies rather than correcting them — such people are not friends (016-001, 016-003)
- Never lose a friend for a jest; folly in jest is still folly in its consequences (071-003)
- Knavery and folly look alike — do not confuse one for the other when judging character (097-006)
