# Temperance
## Definition
In Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, **temperance** is the rational regulation of the appetites — particularly those for food, drink, and pleasure. It is a classical virtue with direct practical consequences: health, reputation, and worldly success all depend upon it.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield invokes temperance in two distinct registers:
1. **Physical temperance** — The moderation of eating and drinking as a condition of health; at a young age, excess may not show immediate effects but will exact its price later
2. **Moral temperance** — A broader self-governance that extends to all passions and desires; the temperate person is master of himself
## Key Passages
> "Your health will continue, while your temperance continues; and at your age nature always mends by temperance." (035-001)
> "I begin to believe that, as you grow older, you grow wiser; your letter gives me a good example of application and temperance." (047-004)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's counsel on temperance includes:
- Guard your health through moderation — youth is no protection against the consequences of excess (035-001)
- Let temperance become habitual; nature itself rewards the temperate with continued health
- Apply temperance not only to physical appetites but to all passions, including ambition and the desire for pleasure
- Temperance in speech and behavior is a mark of the well-bred gentleman
- The temperate person is never at the mercy of his own impulses
## Classical References
- **Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*** — Temperance (*sophrosyne*) as the mean between self-indulgence and insensibility
- **Cicero's *De Officiis*** — Temperance as one of the four cardinal virtues, along with wisdom, justice, and courage
- **Stoicism** — The temperate man is the free man; the intemperate man is a slave to his passions
- Chesterfield follows the classical tradition in treating temperance not as deprivation but as the condition of genuine enjoyment
