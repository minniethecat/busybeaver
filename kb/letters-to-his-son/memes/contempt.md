## Definition

Chesterfield defines contempt as the outward expression of regarding others as beneath one's notice or esteem. Unlike mere private disdain, contempt in his usage is always **visible** — a look, a tone, a silence — that signals to its object that they are not worth serious engagement. He treats it as one of the most socially destructive vices because it wounds *amour-propre* (self-love) more deeply than open hostility.

> "There is nothing that people will not revenge, and with the greater indignation and contempt."
> (008-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of contempt evolves across the correspondence:

**Early letters (1746–1748)**: Contempt is introduced as a cardinal social error. The young son is warned that showing contempt for others' tastes, manners, or opinions is the surest way to make permanent enemies. The logic is pragmatic rather than moral: people will forgive injury more readily than they will forgive being treated as insignificant.

**Middle period (1749–1754)**: The theme deepens. Chesterfield connects contempt to the broader architecture of social negotiation — showing contempt forecloses the possibility of persuasion, because the contemned party will resist even reasonable proposals out of wounded pride. The concept is linked to the classical distinction between *contemptus mundi* (contempt for the world, a Stoic ideal) and *contemptus hominum* (contempt for fellow humans, which Chesterfield condemns).

**Late letters (1755–1767)**: Contempt reappears in discussions of political and court life, where the temptation to disdain lesser figures is strongest. Chesterfield advises that even those who genuinely deserve contempt should be treated with outward civility, because the political cost of visible contempt always exceeds its satisfaction.

> "Speak of the moderns without contempt, and of the ancients without idolatry; judge them all by their merits, but not by their ages."
> (030-003)

## Key Passages

> "Instead of manifesting your contempt for them. There is nothing throws a man more out of doors, and makes him appear more contemptible, than a supercilious air."
> (001-005)

Chesterfield establishes contempt as self-defeating: the person who shows contempt renders *themselves* contemptible in the eyes of discerning observers.

> "Remember, then, that to avoid doing an injury is not sufficient; you must also carefully avoid the appearance of contempt, which is what they never forgive."
> (049-004)

The distinction between overt injury (which may be forgiven) and perceived contempt (which never is) runs throughout the letters.

> "Be convinced, that there are but very few people who deserve either too much attention to everybody, and too much contempt to many."
> (043-002)

Chesterfield's characteristic moderation: the wise man avoids both extremes of social judgment.

> "Take care not to express any contempt, or throw out any ridicule; which, as it never yet made a friend, has often made an enemy."
> (024-003)

> "The inside of books, and due contempt for the outside, is the proper object of a scholar's attention."
> (062-002)

The single instance where Chesterfield *endorses* contempt: directed at the superficial trappings of learning (fine bindings and editions) rather than at people.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on contempt is strikingly practical:

1. **Never show contempt for another's profession, taste, or opinion**, however inferior it may appear. The wound to self-love is permanent and the cost to your interests is certain.

2. **Distinguish between private judgment and public expression**. You may privately think a man a fool; you must never let that judgment show in your countenance or manner.

3. **The contempt you show reflects on you, not its target**. Observers will judge *your* character — as arrogant, insecure, or ill-bred — more harshly than they judge the supposed inadequacy of the person you contemn.

4. **The one legitimate object of contempt is superficial display**. Chesterfield permits intellectual contempt only when directed at the vanity of fine book-bindings, ostentatious but empty learning, and similar pretenses (062-002).

5. **In political and court life, suppress contempt even more rigorously**. The powerful are surrounded by those who deserve contempt; those who show it do not remain powerful long.

> "Those low minds, is in the utmost contempt with great ones. It is the lowest and most contemptible of all vices."
> (054-018)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of contempt draws on multiple classical sources:

- **Cicero**, *De Officiis*: The distinction between legitimate moral contempt for vice and socially destructive contempt for persons is Ciceronian in origin. Chesterfield follows Cicero in treating *contemptio* as properly directed at moral failings, not at persons of lower rank or different taste.

- **Seneca and the Stoics**: The ideal of *contemptus mundi* — philosophical indifference to worldly goods and status — is present in Chesterfield's counsel to value the "inside of books" over their "outside." But he sharply departs from Stoic indifference in his insistence on the practical social cost of visible contempt.

- **Lord Chesterfield's own synthesis**: He adapts the classical framework into a distinctively modern, sociable ethic. Contempt is wrong not primarily because it offends virtue, but because it is *imprudent* — it wounds the self-love of others and damages your own interests. This pragmatic turn is characteristic of Chesterfield's Enlightenment sensibility.

Chesterfield's position ultimately echoes La Rochefoucauld's maxim: "We may forgive those who have injured us, but never those whom we have injured" — the psychology of contempt is such that the contemner is perceived as having injured the contemned, and that injury is never forgotten.
