# Affectation

## Definition

**Affectation**, for Chesterfield, is the cardinal sin of social performance — the attempt to impress by displaying qualities one does not genuinely possess. He treats it as fundamentally self-defeating: affectation is always detected, and detection always produces contempt. The affected person seeks admiration but earns only ridicule. Chesterfield contrasts affectation with genuine ease and naturalness, which he considers the hallmark of the true gentleman: one should be "genteel without affectation" (023-005).

> "Nothing is more unreasonable in a young fellow than affectation." (042-002)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of affectation is sharp and unforgiving. "Nothing is more unreasonable in a young fellow than affectation," he declares, for "it shows an offensive indifference for the opinions of those one converses with" (042-002). The affected person is guilty of a double fault: he lacks the qualities he pretends to, and he underestimates the discernment of those he tries to deceive.

The positive ideal is set in opposition: true social grace is "familiarity, genteel without affectation, and insinuating without any design" (023-005). Affectation, by contrast, is always designed — it is the product of calculation rather than character, and calculation in social life is always eventually exposed.

Chesterfield extends the concept even to the minor "affectations of the company" that a young man should learn to tolerate (055-007), suggesting that affectation is not only to be avoided in oneself but patiently endured in others — a mark of the social flexibility he so values.

## Key Passages

> "Familiarity, genteel without affectation, and insinuating without any design." (023-005)

> "Nothing is more unreasonable in a young fellow than affectation. It shows an offensive indifference for the opinions of those one converses with." (042-002)

> "The idle but innocent affectations of the company, but even to bear with good nature." (055-007)

## Practical Advice

- Cultivate genuine ease rather than studied effect — the gentleman is "genteel without affectation" (023-005)
- Understand that affectation is always detected — those you try to impress are more perceptive than you assume (042-002)
- Distinguish natural manner from affected performance — the former flows from character, the latter from calculation
- Tolerate the minor affectations of others with good humor — social grace includes the ability to overlook faults in company (055-007)
