# Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)
## Overview
The Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle, signed on 18 October 1748, ended the War of the Austrian Succession (1740–1748). It was negotiated during Lord Chesterfield's tenure as one of His Majesty's Secretaries of State, placing him at the center of British diplomatic decision-making during this period.
## Historical Context
The War of the Austrian Succession had embroiled most of Europe in conflict over the question of whether Maria Theresa could succeed to the Habsburg throne. Britain, aligned with Austria, fought against France and Prussia. The treaty largely restored the status quo ante bellum, leaving many issues unresolved—foreshadowing the Seven Years' War that would erupt less than a decade later.
## Chesterfield's Role
As Secretary of State (1746–1748), Chesterfield was intimately involved in the diplomacy surrounding the peace. His letters to his son from this period reflect the preoccupations of a man managing affairs of state. In letter 003, he notes: "I have not, in my present situation—[His Lordship was, in the year 1746, appointed one of his Majesty's secretaries of state.]—time to write to you, either so much or so often as I used, while I was in a place of much more leisure and profit."
## In the Letters
The treaty and its diplomatic context appear in the letters primarily as background: Chesterfield's demanding official duties explain the frequency and length of his correspondence. The political experience he was acquiring, however, infused his advice to his son with hard-won knowledge of how the world actually worked.
## Aftermath
The peace proved fragile. The unresolved colonial rivalry between Britain and France, and the unresolved struggle for supremacy in Germany between Austria and Prussia, ensured that the treaty was merely a truce. Chesterfield's later letters reflect the hardening of Anglo-French rivalry that would lead to the Seven Years' War (1756–1763).
