# Society

## Definition

For Chesterfield, **society** is the ordered world of human relations — not the abstraction of "society" as a sociological category, but the concrete network of drawing rooms, courts, assemblies, and diplomatic circles in which a gentleman must learn to move. It is simultaneously a *school* (where manners and morals are refined by example), a *stage* (where reputation is performed and judged), and a *fabric* (whose threads are held together by shared virtues and decencies). To fail in society is to fail in life; to master it is to unlock every door.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's concept of society operates at three scales:

1. **The great world** — "Your initiation in the great society of the world" (017-008). This is the highest level: the international aristocracy, the courts of Europe, the diplomatic corps. Chesterfield's son is being prepared for this sphere, and every lesson in manners, languages, and history is calibrated to it.

2. **Polite society** — The everyday social circles of well-bred people, governed by "the decency and peace of society" (179-003). Here the emphasis is on the small graces: conversation, deportment, the ability to please. "Les manieres nobles" and the social arts give "society all its charms" (176-002).

3. **Society as moral order** — In his most philosophical moments, Chesterfield treats society as a moral community: "virtues are the foundation of society in general, and of friendship in particular" (075-002); "what good morals are to society in general, [manners are] their cement and support" (088-002).

Chesterfield also analyzes organized "societies" — most notably the Jesuits, "whose society I look upon to be the most able and best governed society in the world" (085-001), and the Sorbonne (156-002). These serve as object lessons in organization and influence.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's social imperatives:

- **Know the society you are entering** — Each society has its own tone, customs, and expectations; observe before you act (017-008).
- **Be useful to society** — A gentleman's ambition should be directed "with utility to society" (107-001), not merely toward private advantage.
- **Respect the decencies** — "The decency and peace of society" depend on shared norms; violating them is an offense against the social order itself (179-003).
- **Understand institutions** — Chesterfield urges his son to study the policy and practice of powerful organizations like the Jesuits to learn how influence is wielded (096-001).

## Classical References

Chesterfield's social philosophy draws on:
- The Aristotelian conception of man as a political animal — "it is not so much what draws us toward society, as what distinguishes us from the brutes" (116-004)
- Cicero's *De Officiis* — the duties of a citizen toward the social order
- The Renaissance ideal of the *uomo universale* — the man who can move gracefully through every level of society

Chesterfield also engages critically with the Jesuits as an organizational model (085-001, 096-001), admiring their discipline and influence while deploring their moral principles.
