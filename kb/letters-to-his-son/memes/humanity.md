## Definition

Humanity, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is the benevolent disposition that recognizes in every person a fellow creature deserving of kindness and respect. It is the foundation upon which the social virtues are built: "good-breeding, and good-nature, do incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures" (044-001). Chesterfield distinguishes humanity from mere politeness — the former springs from genuine fellow-feeling, the latter may be mere performance. Humanity is what transforms justice into something warmer: the favor of others "must be captivated by a more pleasing address, and more engaging manners, than those of mere justice and humanity" (133-003) — significantly, humanity is here the baseline below which no gentleman may fall, the floor rather than the ceiling. In his historical reflections, Chesterfield contrasts the "humanity of former days" with the cruelties of his own era (205-002), treating humanity as a mark of civilization itself.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of humanity runs from moral philosophy to practical counsel. In an early letter, he establishes humanity as the natural consequence of good breeding and good nature combined: these qualities "do incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures" (044-001). In the middle letters, his pragmatism sharpens: winning favor requires more than "mere justice and humanity" (133-003) — humanity is necessary but not sufficient in a world where grace and address are the true currency of social success. The domestic application appears in his advice on refusing a request: one should be "very sorry to refuse the one, and refuses the other with humanity" (147-007) — humanity softens the necessary harshness of denial. His admiration for the magnanimous man centers on the marriage of power with tenderness: "who had more pleasure in bestowing than in receiving, and who gave with the most generous and social sentiments of humanity" (180-005). In a darker key, he observes that "there is a strange but never-failing succession of cruelties committed by madmen and fools, against humanity" (185-004), and contrasts the barbarism of modern warfare — where prisoners are killed "by a rape" — with "the humanity of former days" when prisoners were spared (205-002).

## Related Concepts

Humanity is the root of [[good-nature]] and [[good-breeding]]: both "incline us to humanity and benevolence" (044-001). It is closely allied to [[benevolence]] and [[compassion]] — the active expressions of the disposition toward kindness. [[Justice]] is humanity's necessary complement: "mere justice and humanity" is the baseline (133-003), but the social arts must build upon it. [[Generosity]] is humanity in action — "who gave with the most generous and social sentiments of humanity" (180-005). Humanity stands opposed to [[cruelty]] (185-004), [[barbarism]] (205-002), and the coldness of mere [[formality]]. In Chesterfield's scheme, humanity is to conduct what [[virtue]] is to character: the moral foundation without which all graces are hollow.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on humanity emphasizes its integration with social skill:

- **Let humanity incline you to kindness**: "Good-breeding and good-nature do incline us to humanity and benevolence" (044-001) — cultivate the internal disposition, and the external expression will follow naturally
- **Exceed mere justice**: Winning favor requires more than "mere justice and humanity" (133-003); humanity is the floor, not the ceiling — add grace to kindness
- **Refuse with humanity**: Even necessary refusals should be delivered "with humanity" (147-007); the manner of a refusal matters as much as its substance
- **Remember the example of the magnanimous**: The great man "had more pleasure in bestowing than in receiving" (180-005) — humanity takes joy in giving
- **Guard humanity against the age's cruelty**: Chesterfield saw his era as one in which "madmen and fools" committed cruelties "against humanity" (185-004); the gentleman's humanity is a conscious resistance to the barbarism of the times

## Classical References

Chesterfield's humanity draws on the Roman concept of *humanitas* — a term that Cicero and Aulus Gellius developed to mean not merely kindness but the full cultivation of what is distinctively human: learning, refinement, and benevolent disposition fused into a single ideal. Seneca's *De Clementia* treats *humanitas* as the virtue that restrains power: the ruler who possesses *humanitas* will spare the vanquished and temper justice with mercy — precisely the contrast Chesterfield draws between "the humanity of former days" and the cruelties of modern warfare (205-002). The Stoic doctrine of *oikeiosis* — the natural progression from self-concern to concern for all humanity — underlies Chesterfield's claim that good breeding and good nature "incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures" (044-001). The Enlightenment language of *humanité* that Chesterfield would have encountered in French thought — particularly in Montesquieu and the *philosophes* — reinforced the classical ideal with a modern emphasis on the social and political benefits of benevolent disposition.
