## Definition

Method, for Chesterfield, is the systematic and orderly approach to managing one's affairs, time, and studies. It is less a philosophical concept than a practical discipline — a habit of mind that brings efficiency and clarity to every undertaking. "Without care and method," he warns, "the largest fortune will not" suffice (062-003). Method is the organizing principle that transforms scattered effort into sustained achievement, and scattered knowledge into useful understanding.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces method as a theme in his earliest letters, often coupling it with care and attention. In Letter 15, he notes that "they will take very different methods to gratify it" (015-001), recognizing that method is instrumental to achieving one's ends. By Letter 54, he promises his instruction "will now be more regular and methodical" (054-001), modeling the very quality he preaches. The middle letters emphasize method's practical benefits: it aids memory (099-005), accelerates work (104-004), and preserves financial resources (062-003). His most forceful endorsement comes in Letter 104: "Nothing contributes more to dispatch than method. Lay down a method for everything" (104-004). He preemptively dismisses the objection that order and method are "very troublesome, only fit" for tedious minds (104-005), insisting they are essential for anyone who wishes to achieve anything of consequence.

## Related Concepts

Method is essential to several practical virtues in Chesterfield's system. [[diligence]] sustains method over time. [[time-management]] is the specific application of method to one's hours and days. [[order]] is the broader principle of which method is the practical expression. [[prudence]] guides the selection of the right method for each task. The absence of method manifests as [[idleness]] or [[negligence]]. Method also supports [[learning]], as systematic study retains more than haphazard reading.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical counsel on method includes:

- **Establish a routine**: Chesterfield credits his own success partly to a fixed method — attending Parliament by day and society by night (099-005)
- **Take systematic notes**: When traveling or studying, keep organized records; "a little care and method in taking notes" preserves what you learn (063-003)
- **Apply method to finances**: Even a large fortune will not survive carelessness; method in accounts is essential (062-003)
- **Don't dismiss method as tedious**: The objection that order and method suit only dull minds is self-defeating; effective people in every field rely on system (104-005)
- **Adapt method to subject**: Different pursuits require different methods — what works for study may not work for pleasure; choose the right approach for each domain (162-003)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's emphasis on method reflects the classical rhetorical tradition, particularly the canon of *dispositio* (arrangement), which held that the ordering of material was as important as the material itself. His practical approach also echoes Seneca's advice on the productive use of time in *De Brevitate Vitae*, and the Roman administrative ideal of *ratio* (systematic reason) applied to daily life.
