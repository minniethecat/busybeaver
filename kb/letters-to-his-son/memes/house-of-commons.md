# House of Commons
The House of Commons occupies a unique place in the [[letters-to-his-son]] as both the destination of Chesterfield's [[education|educational program]] and the arena in which all his lessons — in history, languages, [[manners|social grace]], and moral judgment — would be put to the ultimate test.
## The Destination
From early in the correspondence, Chesterfield makes it clear that a seat in the Commons awaits [[philip-stanhope|his son]]:
> "You will be of the House of Commons as soon as you are of age; and you must make a figure there, if you would make a figure in your country." [093-003]
This is not presented as an option but as a certainty — and with that certainty comes the expectation of distinction. Chesterfield's educational program is calibrated to produce not merely a competent member of Parliament but a commanding one.
## Oratory as the Key
Chesterfield's advice about the Commons focuses overwhelmingly on the art of speaking. He treats parliamentary oratory as the supreme test of a gentleman's cultivation:
> "In the House of Commons you can never make a figure without a certain degree of eloquence." [140-001]
He repeatedly warns that the Commons is a uniquely demanding audience. Unlike a courtroom or a pulpit, where a speaker faces a passive or deferential audience, the Commons is filled with equals and rivals who will judge every word with partisan severity:
> "Oratory in the House of Commons at home. What figure can you make without it?" [091-003]
## Preparation
Chesterfield's method of preparing his son for the Commons is characteristic: historical study, imitation of models, and practical exercise. He recommends:
1. **Reading classical orators**: [[cicero|Cicero]]'s *De Oratore* is singled out as "the best book in the world to form an orator" [091-010]
2. **Studying contemporary models**: [[lord-bolingbroke|Lord Bolingbroke]]'s published speeches and writings provide a modern standard of eloquence [135-002]
3. **Understanding the audience**: Chesterfield insists that effective speaking requires knowing the temper and interests of the House [094-002]
## Comparative Analysis
In Letter CLIV, Chesterfield draws an illuminating comparison between the House of Commons and the French *Tiers État* (Third Estate), noting their structural similarity as representative bodies:
> "The Tiers Etat was exactly our House of Commons, that is, the people, represented by their deputies." [154-009]
This comparative perspective is typical of Chesterfield's method: by understanding how institutions function in different countries, his son would develop the analytical sophistication essential to political judgment.
## The Stakes
For Chesterfield, parliamentary success is not merely a career advantage but the very measure of a public man's worth. The letters convey an almost anxious urgency about the Commons — a recognition that all the classical learning, continental polish, and social cultivation would count for nothing if they could not be deployed effectively in the chamber where power actually resided.
