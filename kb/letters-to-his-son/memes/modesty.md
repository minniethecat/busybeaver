# Modesty
## Definition
Chesterfield's **modesty** is a social virtue of a distinctly strategic character. He defines it not as genuine self-depreciation but as "exterior modesty and *seeming* diffidence" — a manner of presenting one's merits that disarms envy and secures goodwill. The emphasis on *seeming* is deliberate and recurrent: modesty for Chesterfield is performative, a social technique rather than a state of the soul. He distinguishes it sharply from "awkward bashfulness," which he diagnoses as a form of self-absorption — the bashful man is too conscious of himself to attend to others. True Chesterfieldian modesty, by contrast, is a gift to the audience: it creates space for others to shine while quietly advancing one's own interests.
## In the Letters
The concept of modesty evolves across the correspondence:
1. **Early formulation (1748–1751)** — Letter XXX introduces modesty as the instrument of social flattery: "modesty is the surest way of gratifying every man's vanity." The insight is psychological: people resent being outshone, and the modest man lowers his apparent brightness so that others may glow by comparison.
2. **Mature refinement (1751–1754)** — Letters XCIV and CXIII elaborate the theory of "seeming modesty." Chesterfield now speaks of "modesty unembarrassed" — a poise that combines deference to superiors with ease in their presence. The paradox is explicit: one must be "intrepid, with exterior modesty."
3. **Late synthesis (1757–1765)** — Letters CXX and CXXVII present modesty as a comprehensive social strategy. The image is martial: "under the white banner of seeming modesty, clear the way for merit." Modesty becomes the vanguard of ambition, not its restraint. Chesterfield is now fully candid about the instrumental nature of the virtue he recommends.
## Key Passages
> "Modesty is the surest way of gratifying every man's vanity." (030-002)
> "By this modesty I do not mean timidity and awkward bashfulness. On the contrary, be inwardly firm and steady, know your own value, and maintain it." (113-002)
> "What we call modesty is awkward bashfulness and *mauvaise honte*. … Under the white banner of seeming modesty, clear the way for merit, that it may act with greater security." (120-007)
> "The happy assemblage of modesty unembarrassed, respect without timidity, and a certain dignity without the least tincture of pride." (102-002)
> "He must be intrepid, with exterior modesty and *seeming* diffidence." (114-009)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's instructions for practicing modesty:
- Never speak of your own accomplishments directly; let others discover them, or let them emerge naturally in conversation
- When praised, receive the compliment with a brief acknowledgment and turn the subject — do not amplify or contradict the praise
- Adopt the outward air of modesty even when inwardly confident: "seeming diffidence" protects you from envy while costing you nothing
- Distinguish true modesty (strategic self-presentation) from bashfulness (paralyzing self-consciousness) — cultivate the first, cure the second
- In disputation, yield on small points and let others have the last word; the appearance of modesty in argument wins more allies than being right
## Classical References
Chesterfield's instrumental modesty has more in common with the rhetorical tradition — particularly Quintilian's advice on the *captatio benevolentiae* (the orator's gesture of humility before the audience) — than with Christian humility. The classical reference is reinforced by his citation of an unnamed French maxim: *"Les manières nobles et aisées, la politesse, les égards, la modestie, relèvent infiniment le mérite"* (Noble and easy manners, politeness, consideration, modesty, infinitely elevate merit). The Stoic distinction between *modestia* (temperance in self-estimation) and *humilitas* (abject self-abasement) underwrites his insistence that modesty must coexist with a firm knowledge of one's own worth.
