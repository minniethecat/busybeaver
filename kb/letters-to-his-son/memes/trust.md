# Trust

## Definition

In Chesterfield's social philosophy, **trust** is a calibrated commodity — never to be given indiscriminately, but essential to genuine relationships when earned. He distinguishes between the "general tacit trust in conversation" that oils social discourse (055-004) and the deeper trust of confidences and serious matters, which must be earned through long observation. His treatment of trust reflects the worldly prudence of a diplomat who has seen the consequences of misplaced confidence.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's counsel on trust unfolds across several dimensions:

1. **Trust and judgment**: He laments that most people "take it upon trust" rather than consulting their own reason and experience (007-002) — a habit he warns Philip against
2. **Trust as a signal of character**: Those who are not trusted with serious matters have been judged and found wanting — "he neither consults them about, nor trusts them with serious matters" (049-004)
3. **The danger of betrayed trust**: "There is nothing so dangerous as to have been trusted with a secret and to have revealed it" (052-007); the betrayer of confidence is permanently excluded from serious society
4. **Degree of trust**: Chesterfield advises Philip to vary the degree of trust according to the person and the matter — "keep the key yourself, and trust it with no man living" when it comes to one's most intimate affairs (071-001)
5. **Distrust as prudence**: He candidly admits "I have long distrusted you" (082-003) when Philip has failed to meet expectations, using conditional trust as a pedagogical tool

Chesterfield's treatment is characteristically unsentimental. Trust is not a virtue to be practiced universally but a reward to be dispensed judiciously.

## Key Passages

> "They generally take it upon trust; and instead of consulting their own reason, and forming their own judgment, they lazily adopt the opinions of others." (007-002)

> "He neither consults them about, nor trusts them with serious matters; though they may possibly be a little informed about such trifles as the furniture of a ballroom, or the dress of a woman of fashion." (049-004)

> "There is nothing so dangerous as to have been trusted with a secret and to have revealed it; for the person whose secret has been betrayed will never forgive the betrayer." (052-007)

> "Besides, there is a general tacit trust in conversation, by which a man is believed upon his own assertion, unless he has been detected in a falsehood." (055-004)

> "Keep the key yourself, and trust it with no man living. The lady is not to be trusted with it." (071-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical teaching on trust includes:

- Never trust a new acquaintance with anything of consequence until long observation has proved them worthy
- Distinguish between conversational trust (the default presumption of honesty in polite society) and confidential trust (rarely given, never presumed)
- If you must distrust someone, do not let them see it — visible distrust creates an enemy
- A gentleman's word, once given, must be inviolable — this is the foundation on which others' trust is built
- Be especially cautious of those who eagerly offer confidences; they are likely to betray yours as freely
