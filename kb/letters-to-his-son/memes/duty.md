## Definition

Duty, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is the binding obligation that arises from one's station, relationships, and nature as a rational and social being. It is the framework within which he conducts his entire pedagogical enterprise: the letters themselves are an act of paternal duty — "it is not only my right, but my duty to tell you of" (001-003). Chesterfield's conception of duty is capacious, spanning filial obligation, professional responsibility, historical truthfulness, and civil allegiance. It is not the cold duty of Kantian imperative but the warm duty of relationship: the father who writes "you owe her not only duty, but likewise great obligations" (039-002) recognizes that duty is the expression of gratitude, not merely the discharge of an abstract requirement. Chesterfield's invocation of duty is always personal and relational — duty to a particular person, in a particular station, for a particular reason.

## In the Letters

Duty pervades the correspondence from the preface to the final letters. Chesterfield opens by declaring the letters "the less so for being common duty" (P01-003) — even the publication and preservation of the letters is framed as an act of obligation. His pedagogy is itself a duty: "it is not only my right, but my duty to tell you of; and it is your duty to correct" (001-003). The reciprocity is crucial — duty binds both parties, not merely the subordinate. He frames filial obligation in terms of gratitude: "you owe her not only duty, but likewise great obligations" (039-002), recognizing a mother's claims. He finds "a satisfaction and a pleasure which amply repay me for the care and anxiety" of his paternal duty (049-002). The duty to country appears in the example of a man who "thought it a duty which he owed to his country, to return to his post" (050-004). Even social obligations are duties: "it is, in truth, a sort of polite duty" to pay respects to sovereigns (055-007). The duty of the historian is "the only one that can dignify the character — that of telling the truth" (175-004). And duty extends to all human obligations: "a religious, moral, or civil duty" (189-001). Even in political controversy, duty is the frame: the Stamp Act is a "Stamp-duty" (283-002) — duty as tax, duty as obligation, duty as the fundamental unit of social order.

## Related Concepts

Duty is the moral framework within which [[honor]], [[virtue]], and [[obligation]] operate. It is closely allied to [[gratitude]] — duty arises from benefits received as much as from station occupied (039-002). [[Truth]] is the historian's first duty (175-004). [[Religion]], [[morality]], and [[law]] are the three sources of duty Chesterfield recognizes (189-001). [[Politeness]] itself has its duties — "a sort of polite duty" to observe the forms of respect (055-007). The [[gentleman-ideal]] is in part defined by the faithful discharge of duty in every relation. Duty stands opposed to [[negligence]], [[ingratitude]], and the selfishness that recognizes no claims beyond one's own interest. [[Patriotism]] is duty to country (050-004); [[filial piety]] is duty to parents (001-003).

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on duty is woven throughout the letters as example rather than precept:

- **Recognize duty as reciprocal**: "It is not only my right, but my duty to tell you of; and it is your duty to correct" (001-003) — duty binds both parties in a relationship, each according to their station
- **Discharge duty with warmth, not merely correctness**: Chesterfield's own performance of paternal duty is suffused with affection — duty without warmth is mere form
- **Acknowledge duties of gratitude**: "You owe her not only duty, but likewise great obligations" (039-002) — duty arises from benefits received, not merely from abstract rules
- **Fulfill duties to country**: The man who "thought it a duty which he owed to his country" to return to his post (050-004) is the model of civic obligation
- **Observe the duties of politeness**: Social forms are "a sort of polite duty" (055-007) — not optional courtesies but binding obligations of the well-bred
- **Tell the truth as a duty**: "The first duty of an historian" and of every truthful person "is that of telling the truth" (175-004)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's conception of duty draws directly on Cicero's *De Officiis* (On Duties), the text he most frequently recommends to his son. Cicero derives *officium* (duty) from the four cardinal virtues — wisdom, justice, courage, and temperance — and applies it to every sphere of life: the duties of the magistrate, the citizen, the father, the son. Chesterfield's framing of the letters as paternal duty (P01-003) echoes Cicero's dedication of *De Officiis* to his own son Marcus, written as an act of fatherly instruction from a statesman at the end of his career. The Stoic doctrine of *kathekonta* (appropriate acts) — actions that reason recommends as fitting to one's nature and station — underlies Chesterfield's capacious understanding of duty as spanning religious, moral, and civil obligations (189-001). The Roman concept of *pietas* — the dutiful respect owed to gods, country, and parents — is the classical ancestor of Chesterfield's insistence that duty is personal and relational, not abstract and universal.
