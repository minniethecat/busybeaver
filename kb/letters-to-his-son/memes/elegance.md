# Elegance

## Definition

**Elegance** in Chesterfield's usage is the quality of refined correctness — in language, in manners, in the whole presentation of self — that distinguishes the cultivated gentleman from the merely competent one. It is not ornamentation but precision raised to the level of art: "great purity and elegance of style, with a graceful eloquence" (091-009). Chesterfield treats elegance as an indispensable quality, not an optional grace note; the preface notes his "rigid rule requiring absolute elegance of expression at all times" (P01-005).

> "A man will be forgiven even great errors, if he speaks with elegance and purity." (091-006)

Elegance in Chesterfield's system is the visible proof of cultivation — the quality that persuades others of one's merit before they have even examined the substance.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's campaign for elegance runs through the entire correspondence. The early letters focus on elegance of manner and person; the middle and later letters shift toward elegance of language and style. He urges his son to study "the purity of diction, the elegance of style, the harmony of periods" (093-002) in the best authors, and warns that without "correctness and elegance in your own language" (093-003), no amount of foreign accomplishment will compensate.

> "The purity, correctness, and elegance of your diction; the clearness and gracefulness of your utterance." (126-005)

A distinctive feature of Chesterfield's treatment is his insistence that elegance applies equally to conversation and writing. The French and Italians, he notes, attend "very much to the purity and elegance of their style, even in common conversation" (095-003) — a model he urges his son to emulate. The "little elegances of manners which distinguish a gentleman" (128-001) are the social equivalent of stylistic polish in writing.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's recommendations for cultivating elegance are specific and demanding:

- **Read the best authors aloud** — this trains the ear in "the purity of diction, the elegance of style, the harmony of periods" (093-002)
- **Attend to your native language first** — correctness and elegance in one's own tongue is the foundation of all polite accomplishment (093-003)
- **Study the French model** — the French attention to elegance "even in common conversation" (095-003) is the standard to emulate
- **Practice the little elegances** — these "little elegances of manners" (128-001) accumulate into the overall impression of a gentleman
- **Pursue correctness before ornament** — elegance is precision first, decoration second
- **Apply elegance to everything** — Chesterfield's "rigid rule requiring absolute elegance of expression at all times" (P01-005) leaves no exemptions

## Classical References

Chesterfield's ideal of elegance draws on the classical rhetorical tradition, particularly Cicero's *elegantia* — the quality of refined, correct, tasteful expression that distinguishes the orator from the mere speaker. Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria* similarly treats *elegantia* as a product of *latinitas* (purity of language), *perspicuitas* (clarity), and *ornatus* (appropriate ornament). Chesterfield's insistence on elegance in conversation as well as writing reflects the Renaissance ideal of *sprezzatura* — studied effortlessness — as articulated by Castiglione in *The Book of the Courtier*.
