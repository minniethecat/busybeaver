## Definition

Barbarism, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is not merely the absence of civilization but its active negation — the condition in which the restraints of humanity, decency, and law are abandoned and men act as brutes rather than as rational and social beings. He invokes the concept primarily in two contexts: the cruelties of warfare and the general moral regression of his age. Barbarism is what happens when "humanity" — the benevolent disposition toward one's fellow creatures — is extinguished. Chesterfield measures his own era against the standards of earlier ages and finds it wanting: "there is a strange but never-failing succession of cruelties committed by madmen and fools, against humanity" (185-004). The concept serves as the dark background against which the civilized virtues — humanity, good breeding, decency — shine more brightly.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of barbarism appears primarily in the later letters where historical and political reflection deepens. The most striking passage contrasts the conduct of modern warfare with "the humanity of former days": "Whereas (such was the humanity of former days) prisoners were spared and exchanged" (205-002) — the implication is that modern warfare has regressed to a barbarism that earlier ages had transcended. This is not merely a military observation but a civilizational judgment: the treatment of prisoners is the measure of a society's humanity, and by that measure Chesterfield's age has fallen. The general principle appears in his observation that "there is a strange but never-failing succession of cruelties committed by madmen and fools, against humanity" (185-004) — barbarism is not an isolated phenomenon but a recurring feature of human affairs, requiring constant vigilance. The foundation of his opposition to barbarism lies in the cultivation of the benevolent disposition: "good-breeding, and good-nature, do incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures" (044-001) — civilization is the work of these inclinations; barbarism is their extinction.

## Related Concepts

Barbarism is the negation of [[humanity]] — the benevolent disposition that "inclines us to benevolence towards our fellow creatures" (044-001). It is closely associated with [[cruelty]] (185-004) — cruelty is barbarism's active expression. [[Civilization]] is the state barbarism destroys. [[Good-breeding]] and [[good-nature]] are the antibodies against barbarism: they "incline us to humanity" (044-001) and make barbarism unthinkable. [[Reason]] and [[religion]] are the only forces that can "effectually oppose" the succession of cruelties (185-004). The [[gentleman-ideal]] is defined in part by his distance from barbarism — the gentleman is the civilized man par excellence.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel regarding barbarism is primarily diagnostic and cautionary:

- **Recognize barbarism's recurrence**: "There is a strange but never-failing succession of cruelties" (185-004) — barbarism is not a relic of the past but a permanent possibility that must be vigilantly resisted
- **Contrast past and present honestly**: The "humanity of former days" that spared prisoners contrasts with modern warfare's cold-blooded murder (205-002) — the assumption of moral progress is not always warranted
- **Cultivate the antibodies**: "Good-breeding and good-nature do incline us to humanity" (044-001) — the best defense against barbarism is the cultivation of the social and moral virtues that make cruelty unthinkable
- **Support reason and religion**: These are "the only forces that can effectually oppose" barbarism's advance (185-004)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's contrast between civilization and barbarism draws on the classical distinction between *humanitas* and *feritas* (savagery). Cicero develops this opposition in *De Officiis*: the civilized man is governed by reason, law, and the sense of common humanity; the barbarian is governed by appetite and force. The Roman concept of *ius gentium* (the law of nations) — the rules that ought to govern even warfare between enemies — informs Chesterfield's outrage at the modern treatment of prisoners. Tacitus, in the *Germania*, had used the Germanic tribes as a mirror to reflect Rome's own barbarism — the barbarian outside reveals the barbarism within. Chesterfield's observation that "there is a strange but never-failing succession of cruelties" (185-004) echoes the Tacitean conviction that barbarism is not a stage to be passed through but a permanent possibility of human nature, requiring the constant counterforce of civilization.
