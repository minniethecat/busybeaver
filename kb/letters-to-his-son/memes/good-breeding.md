# Good-Breeding

## Definition

**Good-breeding** is, for Chesterfield, the social art of making others comfortable in one's presence through graceful, attentive, and considerate behavior. He distinguishes it sharply from mere ceremonial formality, defining it as the happy medium between awkward reserve and troublesome familiarity. It is the practical expression of respect for others, rendered with ease and without ostentation.

> "Respect is the perfection of good-breeding, which nothing but superior genius can arrive at." (065-001)

## In the Letters

Good-breeding is one of the most extensively discussed concepts in the correspondence, with Chesterfield devoting entire letters to its nuances. He draws a crucial distinction between the substance and the outward forms: genuine good-breeding springs from a desire to please, while mere ceremony is empty formality.

> "In manners, this line is good-breeding; beyond it, is troublesome ceremony; short of it, is unpoliteness." (062-003)

He observes that good-breeding varies across nations but that its underlying principle — consideration for others — is universal. What the French call *les manieres* and *les graces* are merely local expressions of this universal social law.

## Key Passages

> "I would have you act, in the world, according to the laws of good-breeding." (001-005)

> "An insinuating manner, an easy good-breeding, a genteel behavior and address, are of infinite advantage." (006-001)

> "Merit and good-breeding will make their way everywhere." (016-004)

> "Good-breeding and good-nature do incline people to be civil and officious." (044-001)

> "If you would particularly gain a man's affection, find out his prevailing merit, and either praise it or flatter it." (055-006)

> "A well-bred man will converse with his equals with ease, with his superiors with respect, and with his inferiors with good-nature." (039-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical advice on good-breeding is remarkably specific:

- Adapt your behavior to the company you are in — take its tone (001-005)
- Never show inattention, which mortifies pride and excites resentment (001-005)
- Render small services and attentions freely — they are "the free-will offerings of good-breeding and good nature" (044-001)
- Find the line between polite ceremony and troublesome formality (062-003)
- Associate with the best models of good-breeding and form yourself upon them (068-003)
- Remember that a man who is not perfectly well-bred is unfit for both good company and business (088-008, 088-009)
