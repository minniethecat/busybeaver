# Integrity

## Definition

**Integrity**, in Chesterfield's framework, is the cornerstone of character — the quality of being morally whole, consistent, and trustworthy. He pairs it with "good manners" as the twin pillars upon which a gentleman's reputation must be built (113-001). Integrity is not, for Chesterfield, a merely private virtue; it is the public foundation of credibility. A man of integrity may have many faults, but a man without it can have no genuine virtues, for every other quality is rendered suspect by the absence of this fundamental soundness.

> "Establishing a character of integrity and good manners; without these, no man can make a figure in the world." (113-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's references to integrity, though fewer in number than his treatments of other virtues, carry disproportionate weight. They appear at structurally significant moments — the early advice establishing what matters most, and the late letters summing up what has been achieved.

The key formulation comes early: his son must focus on "establishing a character of integrity and good manners" (113-001). The pairing is significant — integrity and manners are not alternatives but complements. Manners without integrity are empty form; integrity without manners is bluntness that fails to persuade. Together, they constitute the complete gentleman.

The late recurrence of the same theme — "establishing a character of integrity and good manners" (320-162) — suggests Chesterfield considered this formula definitive. It bookends the correspondence, appearing at the beginning of the son's public life and again at its culmination.

When Chesterfield assesses others, integrity is a primary criterion. Of an acquaintance he writes: "I believe integrity; I dare answer for him" (269-002). The phrasing reveals the evidentiary standard: integrity is not claimed but demonstrated, and those who possess it inspire a confidence that goes beyond mere opinion to something like certainty.

## Key Passages

> "Establishing a character of integrity and good manners; without these, no man can make a figure in the world." (113-001)

> "I believe integrity; I dare answer for him, that he will do nothing dishonest." (269-002)

> "Establishing a character of integrity and good manners." (320-162)

## Practical Advice

- Build integrity as a "character" — not a collection of honest acts but a settled disposition that others can rely upon (113-001)
- Pair integrity with good manners — moral soundness without social grace is incomplete; social grace without moral soundness is hollow (113-001, 320-162)
- Let integrity be demonstrated rather than declared — Chesterfield "believes" integrity based on conduct, not protestations (269-002)
- Understand integrity as the foundation of all other virtues — without it, no man "can make a figure in the world" (113-001)
