# Dignity
## Definition
Chesterfield defines **dignity** as an exterior quality of manners that reflects inner self-respect. It is "a certain dignity of manners absolutely necessary to make even the most valuable character respected" — a social signal that one knows one's own worth without needing to assert it. Dignity for Chesterfield is not pompousness nor stiffness; it is the poised midpoint between familiarity and haughtiness, between the levity that invites contempt and the gravity that invites ridicule. It must be "unprostituted" — never cheapened by association with base conduct — but also never so rigid as to exclude wit and ease.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield develops the theme of dignity across three phases:
1. **Early definition (1749–1751)** — Dignity is introduced as a component of good-breeding, associated with the conduct of established public figures. Letter LVI praises men who maintain "decent mirth" alongside "unprostituted dignity," establishing the tension between conviviality and self-command.
2. **Mature elaboration (1752–1754)** — Letter LXXVIII provides the fullest treatment: a passage of several paragraphs analyzing the "certain dignity of manners" without which "every virtue is diminished." Chesterfield extends the concept from personal carriage to intellectual deportment: the way one presents opinions, receives correction, and engages in debate.
3. **Integration with character (1757–1765)** — In Letter C, dignity becomes inseparable from moral purity: "without this purity, you can have no dignity of character." The concept matures from mere social performance to a state of being that unites inner virtue with outer manner.
## Key Passages
> "There is a certain dignity of manners absolutely necessary, to make even the most valuable character either respected or respectable." (078-002)
> "This dignity of manners, which I recommend so much to you, is not only as it is in itself, a material and an elegant ornament, but also as it is a guard and a protector to virtue." (078-004)
> "Mind their decent mirth, their discreet frankness, and that inoffensive, unreserved, but yet unprostituted dignity." (056-003)
> "Without this purity, you can have no dignity of character; and without dignity of character, it is impossible to be respected." (100-008)
> "There is a certain dignity to be kept up in pleasures, as well as a dignity to be supported in business." (104-007)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's recommendations for cultivating dignity:
- Carry yourself with a composed gravity in public, reserving ease and levity for intimate company
- Never let your dignity appear artificial or stiff — it should be the natural expression of self-respect, not a costume
- Preserve dignity even in pleasures: a man who loses composure in his amusements loses authority in his affairs
- Avoid the two extremes: the "unprostituted dignity" of the coxcomb who trades on borrowed importance, and the careless familiarity that invites disrespect
- Let your speech be measured without being ponderous; let your silence be attentive without being sullen
## Classical References
Chesterfield's conception of dignity draws on the Roman ideal of *gravitas* — the weightiness of character that distinguished the senatorial class. He particularly admires Lord Bolingbroke as a modern exemplar who possessed "all the dignity and good-breeding which a man of quality should have." The Stoic principle that external poise must be grounded in inner virtue (*decorum* as the visible face of *honestum*) underpins his insistence that dignity without character is mere acting.
