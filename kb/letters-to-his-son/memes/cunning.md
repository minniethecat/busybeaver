## Definition

Cunning, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is not intelligence but its corrupt imitation — the narrow, dishonest cleverness that seeks advantage through deception and manipulation. He draws on Lord Bacon's distinction between wisdom and cunning, quoting approvingly: "that is the cunning which Lord Bacon calls crooked wisdom" (071-001). Cunning is the weapon of the "crafty man" who "thinks to gain all his points by trick and artifice" (133-002), in contrast to the man of true ability who succeeds through merit and straightforward conduct. Chesterfield's verdict is absolute: "Those who violate it may be cunning, but they are not able" (165-002) — cunning is a confession of weakness, not a demonstration of strength.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of cunning appears across the full span of the correspondence, from early moral instruction to late political reflection. The key passage draws on Bacon: cunning is "crooked wisdom" (071-001), the perversion of intelligence that substitutes manipulation for understanding. He contrasts the man of "sense and knowledge" with the merely crafty — the former "may gain his ends by fair means," while the latter resorts to cunning precisely because he lacks genuine ability (133-002). Chesterfield's psychological insight is that cunning is self-defeating: the cunning man's schemes are eventually discovered, and "the moment he is found out, he is ruined" (133-002). The political dimension appears in his observation that "dupes, even of their own making, are very numerous" — even intelligent people "have been duped by low cunning" (288-001). He treats cunning as distinct from the permissible "cunning and craft" of diplomacy "when joined with the want of power" (116-008) — a concession that reveals his awareness of the ambiguous moral status of cunning in statecraft. Yet his final teaching is clear: "Lies and cunning are the resort of the weak" (165-002), and truthfulness is the mark of genuine ability.

## Related Concepts

Cunning is the corrupt inverse of [[wisdom]]: where wisdom proceeds by understanding and virtue, cunning proceeds by deception and self-interest (071-001). It is closely associated with [[deceit]] and [[falsehood]] — "lies and cunning are the resort of the weak" (165-002). [[Prudence]], by contrast, is the legitimate exercise of foresight and judgment, distinguished from cunning by its commitment to truth. [[Craft]] and [[artifice]] are cunning's instruments (133-002, 288-001). The "cunning man" is the opposite of the man of [[merit]] and [[ability]] (133-002). [[Duplicity]] and [[dissimulation]] are cunning's social expressions.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on cunning is consistent and emphatic:

- **Renounce cunning absolutely**: Cunning is "so low and narrow a thing" that it cannot be practiced long without discovery, and "the moment he is found out, he is ruined" (071-001)
- **Trust to ability, not trick**: The cunning man "thinks to gain all his points by trick and artifice" but "generally fails" (133-002); the able man succeeds by merit and straightforward conduct
- **Recognize cunning as weakness**: "Lies and cunning are the resort of the weak; they require no talents, and can only impose upon fools" (165-002) — cunning advertises its practitioner's incapacity
- **Distinguish diplomatic craft from personal cunning**: Chesterfield concedes a limited place for "cunning and craft" in statecraft "when joined with the want of power" (116-008), but never endorses it in personal conduct
- **Beware the cunning of others**: Even able people "have been duped by low cunning" (288-001); vigilance against the cunning of others is a necessary worldly precaution

## Classical References

Chesterfield's distinction between wisdom and cunning explicitly invokes Francis Bacon's essay "Of Cunning," where Bacon characterizes cunning as "crooked wisdom" — a "left-handed" or "sinister" wisdom that is to true wisdom what a dishonest tradesman is to an honorable merchant. Cicero in *De Officiis* treats *calliditas* (cunning) as the opposite of *sapientia* (wisdom): the cunning man uses intelligence to deceive, the wise man to discern truth. The figure of the *versutus* (the crafty man) in Roman moral philosophy is the type Chesterfield deploys: clever but crooked, agile but dishonest, ultimately self-defeating. The Stoic tradition holds that the cunning man is not merely immoral but irrational — he misunderstands his own interest, for the discovery of his cunning destroys the reputation that genuine merit would have secured.
