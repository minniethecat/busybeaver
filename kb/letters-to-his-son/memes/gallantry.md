## Definition

Gallantry in Chesterfield's vocabulary is the polished, courteous attention a gentleman pays to women in society — a blend of politeness, charm, and romantic address that operates within strict social bounds. It is emphatically not debauchery; Chesterfield draws a clear line between "gallantry without debauchery" (077-004) and the "infamous mean debauchery" (070-001) of lower company. Properly understood, gallantry is a social art that enhances a gentleman's reputation, provides entrance to fashionable circles, and demonstrates mastery of the graces.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces gallantry early in the correspondence as one of the "graces" his son must acquire. He praises his own father for having "a polite and happy turn to gallantry" (017-004), establishing it as a family tradition of social excellence. His treatment of gallantry is consistently framed as a matter of degree and setting: in high life, gallantry is an elegant accomplishment; in low life, the same impulses degenerate into vulgarity. He notes that in Paris particularly, "gallantry is both the profession and the practice of every woman of fashion" (121-003), making competence in gallant address essential for social integration there. In later letters he grows more direct, recommending his son "have some decent object of gallantry in view at some places" (162-004), treating it as part of the social equipment of a young diplomat. Chesterfield also distinguishes between gallantry as social performance and genuine attachment — one may practice the forms of gallantry without deep romantic investment.

## Related Concepts

Gallantry is closely connected to [[good-breeding]] — it is a specialized application of the general principle of pleasing others. [[manners]] provide the framework within which gallant conduct is judged appropriate or excessive. [[pleasing]] is the end gallantry serves; a turn to gallantry makes a young man agreeable to women, and through them, to fashionable society as a whole. [[decency]] sets the lower bound — gallantry must not descend into [[indiscretion]] or debauchery. The concepts of [[vanity]] and [[flattery]] are its frequent companions: gallantry often involves "flattery, gallantry, and love" (182-002) in combination.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical guidance on gallantry includes:

- **Keep gallantry decent**: Distinguish between the "gallantry of high life" and the "infamous mean debauchery" of low company (070-001, 107-002)
- **Have an object of gallantry in view**: At each social stop, identify a respectable woman to pay gallant attention to — it gives "a certain 'éclat'" to a young man's reputation (162-004)
- **Use gallantry as a social passport**: In places like Paris, where gallantry is the "profession and the practice" (121-003), competence in gallant address opens doors that would otherwise remain closed
- **Prefer gallantry without gaming**: Chesterfield pairs these two social activities — both can enhance a gentleman's reputation, but both must be practiced with moderation and style (077-004)
- **Wear gallantry lightly**: As with all graces, gallantry should appear effortless; the mark of mastery is that it seems natural rather than studied (136-002)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's conception of gallantry draws on the French tradition of *galanterie* — a seventeenth- and eighteenth-century ideal of refined, courteous attentiveness codified in the salons of Paris. The distinction between *l'honnête homme* (the gentleman of polished manners) and the debauched libertine runs through French moral literature of the period and shapes Chesterfield's careful separation of gallantry from debauchery. The chivalric tradition of courtly love, though not directly invoked, provides the distant cultural foundation for the gallant ideal Chesterfield recommends.
