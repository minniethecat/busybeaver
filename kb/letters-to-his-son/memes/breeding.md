# Breeding

Breeding, in Lord Chesterfield's vocabulary, denotes far more than mere politeness or good manners. It is the cultivated art of social grace — the external expression of inward merit that distinguishes the true gentleman from the merely well-born. Chesterfield returns to this theme obsessively throughout the [[letters-to-his-son]], treating good breeding as the indispensable passport to distinction in public life.

## Definition

Chesterfield draws a clear distinction between two concepts often conflated:

1. **Good breeding** (external) — the polished manners, easy address, and graceful deportment that make a person agreeable in company. This is an acquired skill, not an innate quality [015-001].
2. **Good nature** (internal) — the benevolent disposition that inclines a person toward kindness. Chesterfield notes that good breeding and good nature "do incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures" [044-001], but insists that good nature alone, without the polish of breeding, is socially ineffective.

> "The externals of good breeding cannot be over-estimated, if we consider how much the world is governed by appearances." [P01-004]

Chesterfield's conception is thoroughly pragmatic: breeding is not an ornament but a tool — a means of winning favour, avoiding offence, and advancing one's interests without arousing resentment.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of breeding evolves across the correspondence:

| Letter | Date | Theme |
|--------|------|-------|
| Letter I | 1746 | Good breeding as the foundation — "according to the laws of good-breeding" [001-005] |
| Letter VI | 1746 | Breeding combined with an insinuating manner and genteel address [006-001] |
| Letter XIII | 1747 | Breeding with ease and respect at court [013-001] |
| Letter XV | 1747 | Good breeding as the universal language of every country [015-001] |
| Letter XVI | 1747 | Merit and good breeding together make their way everywhere [016-004] |
| Letter XXIII | 1747 | Breeding adorned with engaging manner and those graces [023-005] |
| Letter XXXII | 1748 | Breeding as what people of sense should show themselves above vulgarity [032-001] |
| Letter XXXVIII | 1748 | Breeding and circumspection necessary in courts [038-006] |
| Letter LVI | 1749 | Good breeding as what Chesterfield calls "very truly" essential [056-003] |
| Letter LXI | 1750 | Manners, good breeding, and the Graces as recurrent themes [061-003] |

A notable shift occurs in the middle letters: where the early correspondence emphasizes the rules and externals of breeding, the later letters increasingly stress the marriage of breeding with good nature — the internal disposition without which outward polish is merely deceitful.

## Key Passages

> "Merit and good-breeding will make their way everywhere. Knowledge will introduce them, and good-breeding will endear them to all the world." [016-004]

> "What is called good-breeding... is the result of much good sense, some good nature, and a little self-denial for the sake of others." [015-001]

> "Good-breeding, and good-nature, do incline us to humanity and benevolence towards our fellow creatures." [044-001]

> "People of sense and breeding should show themselves above such petulancy of manners." [032-001]

> "I have marked all the parts of good-breeding... that you may distinguish it from that formal, stiff, ceremonious politeness which is the characteristic of a fool." [056-003]

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's specific recommendations for cultivating good breeding include:

- **Observe the best models**: Study the manners of the most polished people in every company and imitate their ease without aping their mannerisms [052-007]
- **Practice self-denial**: Good breeding requires suppressing one's own inclinations when they would inconvenience others — "a little self-denial for the sake of others" [015-001]
- **Distinguish breeding from ceremony**: True breeding is easy and natural, never stiff or formal. Ceremonious politeness without ease is "the characteristic of a fool" [056-003]
- **Adapt to the company**: Breeding varies by context — the behaviour appropriate at court differs from that in private society [038-006]
- **Combine with good humour**: Even correction or refusal can be delivered with breeding — "do it with good humor, good-breeding, and (if you have it) with urbanity" [055-006]
