# Self-Love

## Definition

**Self-love** is, for Chesterfield, the master key to human psychology. It is not a sin to be condemned but a permanent feature of human nature to be acknowledged and managed — in oneself through rigorous self-examination, and in others through the strategic arts of pleasing. He returns to the theme across the full arc of the correspondence with remarkable consistency, treating it as the hidden spring behind nearly every social behavior. Self-love explains why flattery works, why criticism wounds, why people deceive themselves, and why the honest friend is the rarest of treasures.

> "Our own self-love draws a thick veil between us and our faults." (042-003)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces self-love early, as a warning. "Do not let your vanity and self-love make you suppose that people become your friends upon so short an acquaintance" (016-001). Self-love distorts perception: it makes a young man imagine that casual politeness is genuine friendship. This is the foundational insight — self-love is not wickedness but blindness. It "draws a thick veil between us and our faults" (042-003), preventing the self-knowledge that is the prerequisite of all improvement.

He establishes self-love as a universal constant in Letter XVII: "That degree of pride and self-love, which is inseparable from human nature" (017-007). The phrasing is precise — self-love is "inseparable" from human nature, not a removable defect. The wise man does not try to eliminate his self-love (an impossible project) but learns to see around it. The mechanism of this seeing-around is friendship: "The lover's best friend is self-love. Do not then be afraid; behave with prudence and address, and self-love will do the rest" (144-004). Self-love can be an ally, if channeled correctly.

The social application is the heart of his teaching. "They flatter the self-love of those to whom they are shown" (044-001) — the pleasing address, the attentive manner, every gesture of politeness is, at bottom, a tribute to the other person's self-love. This is not cynical; it is a description of how sociability works. The man who understand this can navigate any company. "Flatter that degree of self-love and vanity from which no man is free" (090-002). The phrase "from which no man is free" is crucial — self-love is democratic, affecting princes and paupers alike.

Chesterfield is particularly alert to self-love's negative manifestations. In Letter XLII, he notes that people are reluctant to confess their weaknesses because "the discovery is too mortifying to our self-love, either to tell another, or to own it even to ourselves" (043-002). Self-love is the censor that edits our internal narrative, deleting evidence of our own inadequacy. Similarly, in Letter XCII: "Too mortifying to that self-love and vanity, which are inseparable from human nature, ever to own it" (092-001) — the inability to admit error is self-love's most stubborn product.

The antidote is the honest friend. In Letter CLII, Chesterfield describes the unique value of a friend who will tell uncomfortable truths: this friend provides "assistance against his own self-love" (149-003). The friend who flatters your self-love is no friend at all; the friend who helps you see past it is invaluable. He expands this in Letter CLII proper: friendship serves "to authorize that unreserved freedom of communication, which can only show the weaknesses, the oddnesses, and the blindnesses of self-love" (152-001).

The most personal testimony comes in Letter CLXVI: "I know that this will touch the sorest place in every breast where any degree of self-love dwells; and I am sure I never yet met with that breast where there was not a great deal" (166-008). Chesterfield has looked for a person without self-love and never found one. This is the empirical foundation of his entire social philosophy.

In the later letters, self-love explains even the smallest social frictions. "Our self-love is mortified when we think our opinion neglected, though in the merest trifles" (188-002) — the slight that would be trivial on its face wounds because self-love magnifies every apparent disregard. And in Letter CCXI, he notes the persistence of the problem: "People's self-love is very apt to make them think themselves the objects of everybody's attention" (211-003) — self-love distorts not only self-perception but social perception, making every man the hero of a drama in which he is, at most, a minor character.

## Practical Advice

- Acknowledge that self-love is "inseparable from human nature" — do not try to eliminate it, learn to see around it (017-007)
- Cultivate at least one honest friend who can provide "assistance against his own self-love" by telling uncomfortable truths (149-003)
- Remember that self-love "draws a thick veil between us and our faults" — the most dangerous errors are those we cannot see (042-003)
- In company, flatter the self-love of others — this is not deception but the foundation of pleasing address (090-002)
- Guard against the tendency to think yourself the object of everyone's attention — self-love magnifies slights that are not intended (211-003)
- Do not "mortify people's vanity and self-love; on the contrary, you will please them by appearing to think well of them" (102-002)

## Related Concepts

- [[Vanity]] is self-love's social manifestation — the need for others to confirm the high opinion we already hold of ourselves
- [[Flattery]] works because it feeds self-love — "of all flattery, the most pleasing" is that which confirms a person's own estimation
- [[Pride]] is the degree of self-love that asserts superiority — "inseparable from human nature" but dangerous when ungoverned (017-007)
- [[Self-knowledge]] is what self-love prevents — "I see a great deal of self-love in the world, but very little self-knowledge" (049-002)
- [[Friendship]] is the only reliable counterweight to self-love — the honest friend shows us what self-love conceals (152-001)
- [[Prudence]] counsels the management of other people's self-love — never mortify it, always flatter it indirectly
