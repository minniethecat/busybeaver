# Extravagance

## Definition

For Chesterfield, **extravagance** is the failure to govern one's desires and expenditures within the bounds of reason and circumstances. He applies the term broadly — to financial recklessness, to excessive emotional display, to the rhetorical excesses of the "Oriental ravings" in literature — but always with the same core meaning: the absence of that measured self-command without which no gentleman is complete. Extravagance, in his view, is a form of vanity made visible: it seeks to impress but succeeds only in exposing the weakness of judgment beneath.

> "Vanity draws people into follies and extravagances." (054-012)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of extravagance is woven through his broader counsel on prudence and self-governance. Unlike some vices he addresses at length in single letters, extravagance surfaces episodically — always as a cautionary counterpoint to the measured conduct he advocates.

In the early middle letters, he warns of "an insensible gradation of extravagances" (050-004), suggesting that extravagance rarely arrives all at once. Rather, it creeps in through small concessions, each one seeming reasonable in isolation until the cumulative departure from prudence is catastrophic. The metaphor is spatial: extravagance is a slope, not a cliff, and those who slide down it rarely notice until they have fallen too far.

The social psychology of extravagance interests Chesterfield particularly. He traces it to vanity: "the follies and extravagances, which vanity draws people into" (054-012). The extravagant person is not merely imprudent but fundamentally insecure, seeking through display what can only be earned through substance. This is why extravagance, for all its noise, impresses nobody of judgment.

Chesterfield also deploys the term aesthetically, criticizing the "Oriental ravings and extravagances of the Arabian Nights" (104-002) as a failure of literary taste. The same principle — excess without discipline — applies whether the currency is money, emotion, or language: extravagance in any domain betrays a mind that has not learned to govern itself.

## Key Passages

> "By an insensible gradation of extravagances from the same quarter." (050-004)

> "The follies and extravagances, which vanity draws people into, are innumerable." (054-012)

> "Impetuosity, excess, and almost extravagance, characterized not only his public but his private life." (095-005)

> "The Oriental ravings and extravagances of the Arabian Nights." (104-002)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on avoiding extravagance is rooted in his broader philosophy of self-command:

- Guard against the gradual creep of extravagance — it arrives by "insensible gradation," each small excess normalizing the next (050-004)
- Recognize vanity as the root of extravagance — you overspend, overdisclose, and overact because you seek admiration, not because the expenditure is wise (054-012)
- Distinguish becoming liberality from extravagance — a gentleman spends appropriately on his station; he does not spend to impress
- Apply the principle of measure across all domains — extravagance of emotion and language is as damaging to character as extravagance of purse (095-005, 104-002)
- Study those ruined by extravagance as cautionary examples — Chesterfield points to public figures whose excess destroyed both career and character (095-005)
