## Definition

Chesterfield uses "conduct" to encompass the entirety of a gentleman's outward behavior — not merely moral rectitude but the artful management of one's appearance, speech, and social interactions. Conduct is the visible expression of inner virtue, shaped by prudence and polished by practice. He places it as a "ruling principle" (P01-004) alongside religion and virtue, treating it as foundational to success in both public and private life.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of conduct evolves across the correspondence. In the early letters (1746–1748), he frames conduct in terms of general principles — the importance of being well-mannered and avoiding offense. By the middle period (1749–1754), his advice becomes more tactical, focusing on the conduct appropriate to specific settings: courts, salons, and diplomatic circles. He observes that "contradictions in our conduct" (037-005) are common, and that few people achieve consistency between their principles and their behavior. In the later letters (1757 onward), he acknowledges his son's maturity and declares he will "interfere no longer in your conduct" (110-003), trusting that his lessons have been absorbed.

## Related Concepts

Conduct is inextricably linked to several other Chesterfieldian concepts. [[good-breeding]] provides the social framework within which proper conduct operates. [[prudence]] governs the judgment required to adapt conduct to circumstances. [[manners]] are the specific practices that constitute good conduct. [[character]] is the inner substance that conduct outwardly expresses. [[decency]] sets the minimum standard below which conduct must not fall. The absence of proper conduct results in [[negligence]] or [[indiscretion]].

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical recommendations for conduct include:

- **Observe before acting**: Study the manners and expectations of any company before determining your own conduct (054-001)
- **Adapt to circumstances**: What is proper conduct in one setting may be inappropriate in another; flexibility is essential (085-001)
- **Consistency is key**: Avoid contradictions between your stated principles and your behavior (037-005)
- **Let experience guide**: Chesterfield repeatedly offers his own experience as a model, urging his son to learn from his observations (054-001, 121-004)
- **Be judicious**: Make conscious choices about conduct rather than drifting with impulse; judgment should precede action (111-001)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's conception of conduct draws on the classical tradition of decorum, particularly as articulated by Cicero in *[[de-officiis]]*, which treats appropriate behavior as a moral category. The emphasis on consistency between inner virtue and outer action echoes Stoic philosophy. The practical, adaptive quality Chesterfield recommends reflects the Roman ideal of *prudentia* applied to social life.
