# Politeness

## Definition

Chesterfield elevates **politeness** far above mere etiquette, treating it as the practical expression of benevolence and the essential lubricant of all social interaction. He coins the phrase "politeness of the heart" (P01-004) to capture his conviction that true politeness originates in genuine goodwill, not in mechanical rule-following. For a young man entering the world, politeness is not optional decoration — it is "absolutely necessary" (016-004) for making knowledge, talent, and virtue effective in society.

> "Politeness of the heart, which finds expression in a constant attention to the feelings, the wishes, and the convenience of others." (P01-004)

## In the Letters

Politeness is one of Chesterfield's most frequently recurring themes, appearing in almost every phase of the correspondence. In the early letters, it is introduced as a fundamental social skill alongside attention and good-breeding. By the middle letters, politeness becomes the specific quality Chesterfield most fears his son lacks — he repeatedly urges him to study and imitate the polished manners of the French court and aristocracy.

The later letters maintain the emphasis, with Chesterfield insisting that politeness creates the conditions in which a man's "parts and knowledge" can be effective (074-002). He is careful to distinguish between the superficial politeness of ceremony and the deeper politeness of genuine regard for others.

## Key Passages

> "That 'politesse de coeur,' the politeness of the heart, which finds expression in a constant attention to the feelings, the wishes, and the convenience of others." (P01-004)

> "Do you behave yourself with politeness enough to make them desire your company again?" (004-003)

> "Politeness and good-breeding are absolutely necessary to adorn any, or all other good qualities or talents." (016-004)

> "Complaisance and politeness, which are so useful in men's dealings with one another." (023-004)

> "I mean that easy politeness, genteel and graceful address, and those engaging manners, which are the characteristic of a gentleman." (052-007)

> "I mean manners, politeness, and the Graces; in which Sir Charles Williams was extremely refined." (067-002)

> "Shining manners; distinguished politeness, an almost irresistible address; he had all these." (068-003)

> "If your good-breeding and politeness made way for your parts and knowledge, your parts and knowledge would secure you the approbation of the best company." (074-002)

> "They are, unquestionably, the seats of politeness and good-breeding; were they not so, they would be the seats of slaughter." (079-003)

## Practical Advice

- Cultivate "politeness of the heart" — genuine consideration for others, not merely formal courtesy (P01-004)
- Use politeness as the vehicle that delivers your knowledge and talent to the world (016-004, 074-002)
- Study the manners of the most polished societies — Chesterfield specifically recommends French company as the model (079-003)
- Distinguish between easy, natural politeness and stiff ceremony — the former attracts, the latter repels (052-007)
- Let your conduct in company prompt others to desire your return — this is the practical test of politeness (004-003)
