## Definition

Laughter, in Chesterfield's social vocabulary, is not a spontaneous expression but a carefully calibrated social signal. He draws a sharp line between the uncontrolled "frequent and loud laughter" that is "the characteristic of folly and ill manners" (032-001) and the refined wit that distinguishes the gentleman. Laughter at its worst is the "mirth of the mob" (054-021); at its best, when governed by taste and judgment, it is the evidence of a cultivated mind. Chesterfield's regulation of laughter is one of his most distinctive and frequently repeated social prescriptions.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of laughter is among the most emphatic and consistent themes in the correspondence. He returns to the injunction against loud laughter at least three times across the letters (032-001, 054-021, 147-005), each time with increasing force. The earliest statement sets the rule: "Frequent and loud laughter is the characteristic of folly and ill manners" (032-001) — it is the signal that distinguishes the vulgar from the well-bred. He later sharpens this into a memorable aphorism: "Loud laughter is the mirth of the mob" (054-021), linking uncontrolled mirth to low social station. The full case against excessive laughter appears in his catalog of vulgarities: "frequent and loud fits of laughter, jokes, waggery, and indiscriminate familiarity" (078-003) are all marks of ill-breeding. He distinguishes between the wit that provokes genuine intellectual pleasure and the "trite jokes and loud laughter" that "reduce him to a buffoon" (104-007). Yet Chesterfield does not condemn laughter entirely. He acknowledges that certain things naturally "excite your indignation or your laughter" (131-001) — the key is whether one controls the expression. His final word on the subject reiterates that loud laughter is "extremely inconsistent with" the graces of the gentleman (147-005).

## Related Concepts

Laughter is conceptually paired with [[ridicule]] — Chesterfield warns against making others "objects of laughter and ridicule" (029-005), which wounds [[pride]] and breeds resentment. [[Wit]] is the refined alternative to crude laughter: intellectual amusement rather than physical mirth. [[Good-breeding]] teaches the restraint of laughter (032-001); its absence is a primary sign of [[ill-breeding]]. [[Politeness]] is "extremely inconsistent with" loud laughter (147-005). The "buffoon" (104-007) is the cautionary figure — the man whose uncontrolled jocularity has destroyed his claim to seriousness. [[Cheerfulness]] and [[good-humor]] are the acceptable alternatives to boisterous mirth.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's recommendations on laughter are among his most unambiguous:

- **Suppress loud laughter absolutely**: "Frequent and loud laughter is the characteristic of folly and ill manners" (032-001); the gentleman never laughs loudly in company
- **Distinguish wit from buffoonery**: "Trite jokes and loud laughter reduce him to a buffoon" (104-007); genuine wit is quiet, controlled, and intellectual
- **Do not laugh at others' expense**: Making others "objects of laughter and ridicule" (029-005) is not merely rude but dangerous — it creates enemies
- **Let genuine amusement be expressed with restraint**: Chesterfield does not forbid all laughter; he forbids its loud and frequent expression — a smile, a quiet appreciation, is the mark of breeding
- **Remember that laughter signals station**: "Loud laughter is the mirth of the mob" (054-021); control of mirth is a class marker

## Classical References

Chesterfield's strictures on laughter echo a long classical tradition. Aristotle in the *Nicomachean Ethics* treats wit (*eutrapelia*) as a virtue — the mean between buffoonery and boorishness — and Chesterfield's position is essentially Aristotelian: laughter is permissible within limits. Cicero in *De Officiis* advises that the gentleman should avoid excessive mirth as incompatible with *gravitas*. The Stoic tradition is more severe: Epictetus counsels against laughter altogether as a disturbance of equanimity. Chesterfield's position is closer to the Aristotelian mean than to Stoic severity — he permits laughter but strictly regulates its volume, frequency, and occasion.
