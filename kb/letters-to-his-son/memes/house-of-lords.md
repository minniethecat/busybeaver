# House of Lords
## Overview
The House of Lords was the upper chamber of the Parliament of Great Britain, composed of the lords spiritual (bishops) and lords temporal (peers of the realm). Lord Chesterfield took his seat in the Lords upon succeeding to the earldom in 1726 and remained an active member throughout his political career, participating in debates and serving in ministerial offices.
## In the Letters
The House of Lords appears in the Letters primarily as background—the arena in which Chesterfield exercised his political ambitions. His letters to his son rarely discuss parliamentary business in detail, but the assumptions of aristocratic governance pervade every page. When Chesterfield writes of "the world," he means the world of men who sit in the Lords and Commons, who staff the ministries and embassies, who dispose of patronage and power.
## Chesterfield's Career
Chesterfield's career in the Lords included:
- **1726**: Succeeded to the earldom and took his seat
- **1728**: Appointed ambassador to The Hague
- **1745–1746**: Lord-Lieutenant of Ireland during the Jacobite rising
- **1746–1748**: Secretary of State (Northern Department)
- **1750s**: Withdrawal from active politics due to deafness and declining health
His political life was marked by eloquence rather than great achievements. He was respected as a speaker but never attained the highest offices. The letters to his son reflect a man who understood power intimately but held it only intermittently.
## The Peerage System
Chesterfield was a product of the Whig aristocracy that dominated 18th-century Britain. His title, his wealth, and his connections all derived from the peerage system, and his advice to his son assumes that advancement in the world means advancement within this aristocratic framework. Yet he knew that title alone was insufficient—a peer without address and ability was, in his view, contemptible.
