## Definition

Jealousy, in Chesterfield's moral psychology, is the resentful awareness of another's advantage — the "ever watchful" passion (145-002) that "not only discovers, but magnifies" the excellences of others in order to discredit them. He distinguishes jealousy from mere envy by its active dimension: jealousy does not merely wish the other ill but watches, schemes, and works to undermine. It operates in personal relations ("this sowed the seeds of that jealousy and enmity," 033-007), in social competition ("low people, in good circumstances, are always jealous," 055-009), and in political affairs ("there was a jealousy and alienation between the King and his ministers," 225-003). Chesterfield's insight is that jealousy is self-wounding: the jealous man damages his own peace of mind more than his target's reputation.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield traces jealousy across three domains. In personal relations, he observes how "this sowed the seeds of that jealousy and enmity, which have flourished ever since" (033-007) — a single act of favoritism can generate lasting resentment. In social competition, he notes the class dimension: "low people, in good circumstances, are always the most jealous of their rank and dignity" (055-009) — the insecure are the most watchful of perceived slights. The political dimension appears in his analysis of European power: "the temporal powers, but to the jealousy of each other" (085-001) — the Pope's authority is checked not by principle but by the mutual suspicion of rival sovereigns. The psychological analysis is acute: "jealousy, ever watchful, not only discovers but magnifies every little fault" (145-002) — jealousy is not passive observation but active distortion. Chesterfield's practical counsel focuses on managing the jealousy of others: "his talents may excite the jealousy of some, but his engaging arts will disarm the rest" (163-001). The remedy for others' jealousy is personal charm — the "engaging arts" that make a man impossible to resent. The political consequences of jealousy are severe: "there was a jealousy and alienation between the King and his ministers that proved a public misfortune" (225-003) — jealousy between leaders damages not only themselves but the state.

## Related Concepts

Jealousy is closely paired with [[envy]] — Chesterfield often uses them together, as in "low envy and jealousy" (055-009) and "hatred, jealousy, or envy" (134-004). [[Hatred]] is jealousy's active form — the jealous man "discovers and exaggerates" faults (134-004) as hatred's instrument. [[Watchfulness]] and [[suspicion]] are jealousy's cognitive modes: "jealousy, ever watchful" (145-002). [[Ambition]] when unchecked breeds jealousy among rivals (163-001). The "engaging arts" — [[address]], [[politeness]], [[affability]] — are the antidote to jealousy (163-001). [[Emulation]] is the virtuous alternative to jealousy: where jealousy resents another's excellence, emulation seeks to equal it. [[Security]] and [[confidence]] are the opposites of jealousy in the social domain (055-009).

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on jealousy addresses both how to avoid it in oneself and how to manage it in others:

- **Recognize jealousy as a mark of low station**: "Low people, in good circumstances, are always the most jealous of their rank and dignity" (055-009) — jealousy betrays insecurity, not superiority
- **Do not be the object of jealousy through arrogance**: Talents "may excite the jealousy of some" (163-001), but "engaging arts" — modesty, affability, good manners — can disarm the jealous
- **Understand that jealousy distorts perception**: Jealousy "not only discovers, but magnifies every little fault, and often makes crimes of what are only follies" (145-002) — the jealous person is not a fair judge
- **Guard against jealousy in political and professional life**: The jealousy "between the King and his ministers" (225-003) proved a "public misfortune"; institutional jealousy damages everyone, not just the rivals
- **Distinguish jealousy from legitimate vigilance**: Not all watchfulness is jealousy — the statesman must be "ever watchful" for the public good; jealousy is watchfulness in the service of resentment

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of jealousy draws on the classical analysis of *invidia* (envy) and *zelotypia* (jealousy) in Roman moral philosophy. Seneca in *De Ira* treats envy as a form of anger — the resentment of another's good fortune that dissolves the envious man's own peace. Plutarch's essay "On Envy and Hatred" distinguishes envy from hatred: hatred wishes the other harm, envy merely resents the other's advantage. Chesterfield's insight that jealousy "magnifies" faults (145-002) echoes Bacon's essay "Of Envy," where envy is described as a "glass" that distorts what it reflects. The classical remedy for jealousy — cultivated indifference to the goods of fortune — is Stoic; Chesterfield's remedy — the "engaging arts" (163-001) that disarm the jealous — is distinctively modern, emphasizing social skill over philosophical detachment.
