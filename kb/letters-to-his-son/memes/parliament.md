# Parliament
Parliament is the gravitational center of Chesterfield's political vision in the [[letters-to-his-son]]. It is the institution for which his entire [[education|educational program]] was designed to prepare [[philip-stanhope|Philip Stanhope]], and it serves in the letters as the ultimate test of whether a young man has acquired the knowledge, eloquence, and judgment that Chesterfield considers essential to public life.
## Structure and Function
In the mid-18th century, the Parliament of Great Britain consisted of two houses:
- **The House of Commons** — the elected lower house, where political careers were made through oratory and debate. Chesterfield was preparing his son for entry here [093-003]
- **The House of Lords** — the hereditary and episcopal upper house, where Chesterfield himself sat and to which his son might eventually be elevated
Chesterfield's references to Parliament reflect the institution's actual workings: the management of majorities, the importance of parliamentary oratory, and the complex relationship between the legislature and the Crown.
## In the Letters
### The Arena of Oratory
For Chesterfield, Parliament is primarily a theater of eloquence. He repeatedly emphasizes the centrality of speaking ability to parliamentary success:
> "When you come into the House of Commons, if you imagine that speaking in that House is a trifle, you will find yourself most egregiously mistaken." [135-002]
He contrasts effective parliamentary speech with the shallow rhetoric of academic disputation, insisting that the House of Commons tests a speaker against the most demanding audience in the kingdom:
> "The man who speaks in the House of Commons, speaks in that House, and to that audience." [094-002]
### A Source of Metaphor
Chesterfield's own political experience colors his use of parliamentary language even when discussing non-political matters. He borrows the formula "as they say in acts of Parliament" to lend mock-legal precision to his arguments [146-003]. This habitual reference reveals how deeply parliamentary culture shaped his thinking.
### The Son's Future Seat
Chesterfield makes it clear from early in the correspondence that a seat in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] awaits his son as soon as he comes of age [093-003]. The entire continental education — the courts, the languages, the historical reading, the cultivation of [[manners|social grace]] — is framed as preparation for this parliamentary destiny.
## Chesterfield's Parliamentary Career
Chesterfield himself was a significant parliamentary figure. He served in the House of Commons before succeeding to the earldom and entering the House of Lords. His experience as a debater and manager of parliamentary business informs every piece of political advice in the letters.
## Political Context
The letters span a period of intense parliamentary activity: the fall of Walpole (1742), the rise of the Pelhams, the Duke of Newcastle's administration, and the emergence of William Pitt. Chesterfield comments on these developments with the insider's authority of a man who has himself negotiated the shifting allegiances of 18th-century parliamentary politics.
