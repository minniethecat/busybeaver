## Definition

Employment in Chesterfield's lexicon has a double meaning: the specific sense of holding a public office or professional position, and the broader sense of usefully occupying one's time. Both meanings are united by the conviction that idleness is degrading and that the proper "employment of time" (029-004) is a moral obligation. Chesterfield regards employment not merely as a means of earning a livelihood but as essential to character formation, social standing, and personal fulfillment.

## In the Letters

The theme of employment runs throughout the correspondence, adapting to his son's changing circumstances. In the early letters, Chesterfield addresses employment in the broadest sense — the "employment of time" (029-004) through study, travel, and self-improvement. He distinguishes between "the frivolous employment" (038-006) of mere amusement and productive activity. As his son enters diplomatic service, the meaning narrows to public office: Chesterfield discusses the value of "the best employments" (148-006), the politics of securing positions, and the social capital required to succeed in them. In the later letters, he balances the two meanings — professional employment must be pursued, but the broader employment of time in useful activity remains paramount. He observes that employments are both a means to influence and a liberty to be valued (116-023).

## Related Concepts

Employment is opposed to [[idleness]], which Chesterfield condemns as the root of vice and mediocrity. It is closely allied with [[diligence]] and [[industry]], the personal qualities that make employment fruitful. [[time-management]] is the practical discipline that ensures one's hours are usefully employed. [[ambition]] provides the motivation to seek and excel in employments. The choice of employment is guided by [[prudence]], and success in it depends on [[pleasing]] and [[good-breeding]].

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers concrete guidance on employment:

- **Employ time, don't kill it**: The difference between active pleasures and passive time-wasting is crucial; seek employments that engage the mind (162-002, 162-006)
- **Be versatile**: Prepare for multiple possible employments rather than betting on a single path; an "uncertain and precarious employment" is risky (200-002)
- **Use employment to build character**: The right employments develop discipline, knowledge, and social skills simultaneously (029-004)
- **Understand the politics of office**: Securing and keeping employments requires not just competence but the art of [[pleasing]] superiors and cultivating [[friendship]] (148-006, 145-002)
- **Balance breadth with usefulness**: Chesterfield's own life modeled the ideal — public office, intellectual pursuits, and social duties all found their proper place (092-001, 099-005)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's ideal of useful employment draws on the Ciceronian concept of *otium cum dignitate* (leisure with dignity) — the notion that even time away from public duty should be spent in worthy pursuits. The Roman *cursus honorum* (sequence of public offices) also informs his treatment of professional employment as a ladder of increasing responsibility and honor. Seneca's warnings against the waste of time in trivial pursuits echo throughout Chesterfield's advice on the employment of hours and days.
