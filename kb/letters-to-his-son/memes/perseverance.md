# Perseverance

## Definition

**Perseverance** is, for Chesterfield, the quality that separates those who merely wish from those who achieve. It is the refusal to abandon a course of action in the face of difficulty, tedium, or slow progress. Chesterfield contrasts perseverance with two opposing faults: the *impatience* that abandons a project too soon, and the *madness* that pursues the impossible. True perseverance occupies the rational middle ground — it presses forward against difficulties that are real but surmountable.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of perseverance is concentrated in a few letters but carries extraordinary weight. The concept appears at critical junctures — when Philip faces the long grind of diplomatic apprenticeship, when he must master public speaking, when the path to distinction seems endless.

The most sustained treatment appears in **Letter CXV** (1752), where Chesterfield directly links perseverance to the boundary between sanity and folly:

> "None but madmen attempt impossibilities; but whatever is only difficult, spirit and perseverance will get the better of the latter." (115-002)

This is the core Chesterfieldian formula: ambition must be tempered by realism, but within the realm of the possible, perseverance is decisive. The contrast with *madmen* is telling — Chesterfield wants his son to be daring without being delusional.

In **Letter CXXXIII** (1753), perseverance appears in a social context — the tenacity required to maintain one's position in negotiation:

> "On the other hand, you must, by a steady perseverance and decent tenaciousness, show that you are not to be shaken." (133-003)

Here perseverance becomes a tool of diplomacy: the appearance of immovability, sustained long enough, becomes the reality.

In **Letter CCII** (1761), Chesterfield returns to perseverance as the key to parliamentary oratory — perhaps the most demanding skill he urged upon his son:

> "With speaking in parliament, perseverance will most certainly prevail sooner or later." (202-001)

The phrase "sooner or later" captures the essence of perseverance: it does not promise quick results, only eventual ones. The question is whether one can endure the interval.

## Key Passages

> "Nothing but resolution, and an unwearied perseverance. None but madmen attempt impossibilities; but whatever is only difficult, and spirit and perseverance will get the better of the latter." (115-002)

This double citation from the same paragraph shows Chesterfield weaving perseverance and spirit together — perseverance is the sustained application of the spirit's initial impulse. Resolution begins a project; perseverance finishes it.

> "You must, by a steady perseverance and decent tenaciousness, show that you are not to be shaken from what you have a right to insist upon." (133-003)

The qualifier "decent" is characteristically Chesterfieldian: tenacity must be clothed in politeness. One may be immovable, but one must never be rude about it.

## Related Concepts

- [[patience]] — The passive dimension of endurance; perseverance is patience in action, persistence directed toward a goal.
- [[diligence]] — The daily expression of perseverance; diligence is perseverance applied to ordinary tasks.
- [[ambition]] — The motive force that perseverance serves; without ambition, perseverance has no direction.
- [[resolution]] — The initial act of will; perseverance is resolution sustained over time.
- [[indolence]] — The vice that perseverance directly opposes; indolence is the failure to persist.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on perseverance is practical and domain-specific:

1. **In learning**: Language acquisition, historical study, and political knowledge all require sustained application over years. There is no shortcut; perseverance is the only method.

2. **In public speaking**: Parliamentary eloquence, Chesterfield insists, is not a gift but an acquisition. Those who persevere in practice and in speaking "sooner or later" prevail.

3. **In diplomacy**: Negotiation rewards the party who can sustain a position longer. "Decent tenaciousness" — the refusal to be shifted combined with the refusal to offend — is the diplomat's form of perseverance.

4. **In self-improvement**: Chesterfield's entire educational program for his son assumes perseverance. The graces, the knowledge, the connections — none can be acquired quickly.

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of perseverance draws on the Stoic virtue of *constantia* — the steadiness of the wise man who is not shaken by fortune. [[cicero]]'s *De Officiis*, which Chesterfield recommended to his son repeatedly, treats perseverance (*perseverantia*) as a subdivision of courage (*fortitudo*): the courage not of the battlefield but of sustained effort in civil life.

The distinction between attempting the impossible and persevering through the difficult also echoes Aristotle's doctrine of the mean. Perseverance is the mean between the deficiency of giving up too soon and the excess of stubbornly pursuing the unattainable.
