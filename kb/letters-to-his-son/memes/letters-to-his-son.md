# Letters to His Son
## Overview
*Letters to His Son on the Art of Becoming a Man of the World and a Gentleman* is a collection of over 400 letters written by Philip Dormer Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield, to his illegitimate son Philip Stanhope between approximately 1746 and 1768. Published in 1774 by Philip's widow Eugenia Stanhope, the letters became an immediate sensation and have remained in print for over 250 years.
## Content and Structure
The letters cover an extraordinary range of subjects, all directed toward a single purpose: the making of a gentleman. Their content can be grouped into several major themes:
1. **Education**: Systematic advice on the study of history, geography, modern languages, and classical literature
2. **Manners**: Detailed instruction on address, conversation, dress, and the thousand small arts of pleasing
3. **Morality**: A Ciceronian ethics of honor, prudence, and self-mastery
4. **Worldly wisdom**: Practical knowledge of courts, diplomacy, and the management of ambition
5. **Personal affairs**: News of family, health, politics, and the events of Chesterfield's own life
## Literary Significance
The Letters occupy a unique position in English literature. They are at once:
- **An educational treatise**: A complete curriculum for the formation of a gentleman
- **An epistolary novel avant la lettre**: The gradual unfolding of a father-son relationship over decades
- **A philosophical work**: A sustained reflection on virtue, manners, and the good life
- **A historical document**: An insider's view of 18th-century aristocratic society and politics
## Reception
The publication of the Letters provoked intense controversy. Samuel Johnson's verdict—that they "teach the morals of a whore, and the manners of a dancing master"—set the terms of a debate that has never been resolved. Critics have condemned Chesterfield's worldliness, his apparent cynicism, his reduction of virtue to calculation. Defenders have praised his psychological acuity, his stylistic brilliance, and the genuine affection that infuses even his most pragmatic advice.
The Letters have influenced generations of readers on both sides of the question. They were admired by Voltaire, who called them the best book on education ever written, and condemned by George Washington, who found their morality suspect. Their influence can be traced in the educational philosophy of the 18th and 19th centuries, and they remain a touchstone in discussions of manners and morality.
