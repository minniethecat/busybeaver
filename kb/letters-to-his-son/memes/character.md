# Character

## Definition

For Chesterfield, **character** is the integrated whole of a person's moral, intellectual, and social qualities — the complete picture that others form of who a man is. It is distinct from reputation (which is what others say) and from virtue (which is one component), though it depends on both. Character is built over time through consistent conduct and is the ultimate asset a gentleman can possess: more durable than fortune, more convincing than eloquence, and more powerful than rank.

> "Those who deserve a good character, ought to have the satisfaction of knowing that they have it." (006-001)

## In the Letters

Character is a persistent theme from the earliest letters. Chesterfield is intensely aware that he is shaping his son's character through the correspondence itself — each letter is an act of character formation. He warns repeatedly against the character types that young men fall into: the frivolous, the pedantic, the dissipated. His own youthful admission that he aimed at "the shining character" (007-004) and found it wanting serves as both caution and instruction.

In the middle letters, character becomes the standard by which travel, education, and social conduct are evaluated. Chesterfield urges his son to study "with the greatest care, into the characters of those whom you converse with" (015-001) — character analysis is a critical skill for navigating the world.

## Key Passages

> "Those who deserve a good character, ought to have the satisfaction of knowing that they have it." (006-001)

> "The character which most young men first aim at, is that of a man of great spirit and fire." (007-002)

> "I hoped that those would contribute to forming and completing the shining character which I affected; but this forward, pragmatical temper proved the greatest obstacle to it." (007-004)

> "Search, with the greatest care, into the characters of those whom you converse with; and discover, if possible, their real sentiments." (015-001)

> "Nothing in the world lets down a character quicker than that wrong turn." (016-003)

> "The particular characters, the habits, the cant of one country, will be very different from those of another." (017-003)

> "Those alone travel with advantage, who attend to their several manners and characters." (018-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on character formation:

- Deserve a good character, and seek the satisfaction of knowing you have it — not merely the reputation of it (006-001)
- Avoid the "shining character" pursued for appearance rather than substance — it proves the "greatest obstacle" to real development (007-004)
- Study the characters of others with great care — this is essential knowledge for navigating society (015-001)
- Recognize that "nothing in the world lets down a character quicker" than wrong turns in conduct (016-003)
- Travel and social experience should be used to understand "the particular characters" of different countries and people (017-003)
