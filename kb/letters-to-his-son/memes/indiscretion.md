# Indiscretion

## Definition

**Indiscretion**, in Chesterfield's vocabulary, is the failure to govern one's speech and actions with the prudence that social life demands. It is not a positive vice — not malice, not deceit — but a want of judgment that can damage reputation as surely as any deliberate wrongdoing. Chesterfield's ideal gentleman is "frank without indiscretion" (127-004), able to speak openly while knowing precisely where openness must yield to reserve.

> "Frank without indiscretion, and secret without mysteriousness." (127-004)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield treats indiscretion as a peculiarly dangerous fault because it so often accompanies otherwise admirable qualities. Frankness, openness, and candor are virtues — but without the governing restraint of discretion, they become liabilities. The formula he repeats is "frank, but without indiscretion" (320-192, 320-193), a balancing act that defines the social competence he seeks to instill.

Indiscretion's consequences are illustrated through negative examples. Sir C. W. is said "by his indiscretions, to have caused the disgrace" of his party (220-010), showing that indiscretion in public life can have consequences far beyond personal embarrassment. The lesson is clear: a single unguarded word can undo years of careful reputation-building.

The late letters distill the principle to its essence: "you must be frank, but without indiscretion; and close, without being costive" (172-001). The gentleman is neither a compulsive confessor nor an impenetrable mystery — he is precisely as open as the occasion warrants and no more.

## Practical Advice

- Cultivate frankness tempered by judgment — openness is a virtue; openness without limits is indiscretion (127-004)
- Learn from the indiscretions of others — observe how public figures damage themselves through unguarded speech (220-010)
- Practice the dual art: be frank without indiscretion, and reserved without mysteriousness (172-001)
- Recognize that indiscretion is not a moral failing but a failure of judgment — it can be corrected through attention and practice
- Remember that what is said cannot be unsaid — the indiscreet word travels farther and lasts longer than any retraction
