# Wisdom

## Definition

For Chesterfield, **wisdom** is the faculty that guides a gentleman's conduct across all domains of life — intellectual, social, and moral. He is careful to distinguish between genuine wisdom, which aligns with virtue and sound judgment, and what he calls "crooked or left-handed wisdom" (071-001), a cunning self-interest that masquerades as cleverness but ultimately damages one's character. True wisdom, in his view, is inseparable from knowledge of the world and must be worn lightly, without pedantry or affectation.

> "There is a certain kind of wisdom, which Mr. Locke very properly calls crooked or left-handed wisdom, and which is never made use of but by those who have not true wisdom." (071-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield develops the theme of wisdom throughout the correspondence, with particular intensity in the middle and later letters. Early on, wisdom is paired with learning as the goal of education; by the middle period (1750s), it becomes the lens through which his son should evaluate political actors and court behavior. In the late letters, wisdom is what Chesterfield hopes his accumulated advice will distill into.

He repeatedly contrasts the "arrogant pedant" with the truly wise man, arguing that wisdom manifests not in displays of learning but in temperate conduct and sound judgment (098-005). Political wisdom receives special attention in the later letters, where Chesterfield urges his son to study the "arts and wisdom of government" through direct observation rather than books alone (172-006).

## Key Passages

> "You have sense and judgment enough to distinguish, and you must therefore have virtue enough to detest, that sort of wisdom." (071-001)

> "The arrogant pedant does not communicate his knowledge; let us be communicative, but with modesty." (098-005)

> "They were trusted, as they justly are, the wisdom of their political principles and conduct." (156-006)

> "Be silent, and seem to think, rather than talk, upon those subjects; and you will pass for a wise man." (172-001)

> "Live without the affectation of wisdom, which does not become the age you are of." (172-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers specific guidance on cultivating and displaying wisdom:

- Distinguish true wisdom from cunning by its ends — wisdom serves virtue, cunning serves self-interest (071-001)
- Avoid the affectation of wisdom, particularly in youth; silent attention passes for wisdom more readily than eager talk (172-001)
- Study government and politics directly to acquire practical, not merely theoretical, wisdom (172-006)
- Communicate knowledge with modesty; the pedant hoards, the wise man shares gracefully (098-005)
