## Definition

Ill nature, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is the settled disposition of sourness, ungenerosity, and malevolence that renders a person incapable of pleasing and unwelcome in every company. He distinguishes it from justified resentment: ill nature is not the proportionate response to injury but the chronic condition of a mind inclined to think ill of others and to express that ill-thinking in manners and speech. Chesterfield's analysis is precise: "captiousness, sullenness, and that ill-nature which either shackles or exposes their malice, will make a man insupportable in company" (200-001). Ill nature is self-punishing: the ill-natured man suffers from his own disposition more than anyone else does, for he lives perpetually in the prison of his own sourness. Chesterfield's personal declaration — "I have not malice enough in my nature" (099-002) — is a repudiation of ill nature by self-description: he is simply not constituted that way.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of ill nature is consistent and emphatic. The foundation is laid in the contrast with good nature: "good-breeding, and good-nature, do incline us to humanity and benevolence" (044-001) — ill nature is the privation of these inclinations, the absence of the benevolent disposition that makes social life possible. His most personal reflection appears in the middle letters: "I have not malice enough in my nature, to wish any one the greatest of all human evils" (099-002) — the phrasing implies that malice, and the ill nature from which it springs, is a constitutional defect as much as a moral failure. The crucial distinction between resentment and ill nature appears in the counsel on bearing injuries: "there is a great difference between bearing malice, which is always ungenerous, and a becoming resentment of an injury" (133-005) — resentment is just and proportionate; ill nature is the chronic disposition that transforms every slight into a grievance. In the later letters, the social consequences are made explicit: ill nature "will make a man insupportable in company" (200-001).

## Related Concepts

Ill nature is the direct opposite of [[good-nature]] — the benevolent disposition that "inclines us to humanity" (044-001). It is closely allied to [[malice]], [[sullenness]], and [[captiousness]] (200-001). [[Resentment]], properly understood, is distinct from ill nature: resentment is the just response to injury, while ill nature is the chronic disposition that transforms every slight into a grievance (133-005). [[Good-humor]] is ill nature's antidote. The ill-natured man is the opposite of the [[gentleman-ideal]]: where the gentleman pleases by benevolence and grace, the ill-natured man repels by sourness and fault-finding.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on avoiding ill nature:

- **Cultivate good nature instead**: "Good-breeding and good-nature do incline us to humanity and benevolence" (044-001) — the positive cultivation of good nature is the best defense against ill nature
- **Distinguish resentment from ill nature**: It is permissible to feel "a becoming resentment of an injury," but "bearing malice" is "always ungenerous" (133-005) — let resentment be proportionate and temporary, never chronic
- **Guard against the companions of ill nature**: "Captiousness, sullenness, and that ill-nature" travel together (200-001) — if you find yourself becoming captious or sullen, recognize these as the early symptoms of ill nature
- **Remember the social cost**: Ill nature "will make a man insupportable in company" (200-001) — whatever satisfaction it provides in the moment is repaid a hundredfold in social isolation

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of ill nature echoes the Aristotelian analysis of *duskolia* (sourness, ill-temper) — the vice of deficiency in the sphere of social pleasantness, the disposition to cause pain rather than pleasure in social intercourse. Theophrastus's *Characters* includes the portrait of the "ill-natured man" (*authades*) who takes pleasure in contradicting, criticizing, and finding fault — a type Chesterfield would have recognized immediately. Seneca's *De Ira* develops the Stoic distinction between anger (which may be justified by injury) and *iracundia* (the habitual disposition to anger, which is always vicious) — precisely Chesterfield's distinction between "becoming resentment" and the chronic ill nature that "shackles or exposes" its possessor (133-005, 200-001).
