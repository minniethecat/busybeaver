## Definition

Scandal, in Chesterfield's usage, is the public report of private misconduct — the defamation that circulates through society, damaging reputations and poisoning social relations. He distinguishes between private scandal (personal defamation) and public scandal (notorious misconduct that reflects on institutions). His position is unequivocal: "Neither retail nor receive scandal willingly" (054-017) — the gentleman must be neither its source nor its audience.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield addresses scandal primarily as a moral prohibition. His most famous formulation is among his most quoted maxims: "Neither retail nor receive scandal willingly; defamation of others may for the present gratify the malignity of the heart, but the reflection on cool thoughts will make the hearer detest the reporter" (054-017, 320-383). The passage is characteristic of Chesterfield's moral psychology: he condemns scandal not from abstract principle but from a shrewd understanding of its effects — the hearer ultimately comes to "detest the reporter" of scandal, not merely the subject. He applies the same logic to historical scandal: the conduct of the Elector of Hanover, he notes, "consequently prevented no public scandal" (183-001) — the damage was done regardless of prevention. He warns his son explicitly to be "ignorant... of all matters of private scandal and defamation, though you should be well informed of all public affairs" (186-001), drawing a clear line between the private vice of scandal-mongering and the legitimate knowledge required of a public man. Chesterfield extends his condemnation to "scurrilous and scandalous" publications that "betray private conversation" (289-002), treating the betrayal of confidence as a form of scandal.

## Related Concepts

Scandal is the corrupt form of [[conversation]] — where conversation builds social bonds, scandal destroys them. It is closely related to [[defamation]], the active dimension of scandal (054-017). [[Reputation]] is scandal's target: scandal seeks to damage what reputation has built. [[Discretion]] is the virtue that prevents scandal — the man who guards his own speech and refuses to entertain scandal in others. [[Secrecy]] is scandal's opposite: where secrecy protects confidences, scandal betrays them (289-002). [[Malice]] and [[envy]] are the psychological roots of scandal-mongering (054-017). [[Calumny]] is scandal's more vicious cousin — deliberate falsehood rather than mere circulation of truth.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on scandal is terse and absolute:

- **Neither retail nor receive scandal**: The injunction is two-sided — do not spread scandal, and do not willingly listen to it (054-017); both acts are morally compromising
- **Understand the psychology of scandal**: The hearer of scandal "will make the hearer detest the reporter" (054-017) upon reflection — scandal damages the scandalmonger's reputation as much as the target's
- **Remain ignorant of private scandal**: Be "ignorant... of all matters of private scandal and defamation" (186-001) — knowledge of private vice is not useful and may be harmful
- **Distinguish private scandal from public knowledge**: While remaining ignorant of private scandal, "be well informed of all public affairs" (186-001) — the statesman must know what is publicly relevant
- **Never betray private conversation**: The "scurrilous and scandalous" betrayal of private talk (289-002) is a breach of the fundamental social contract of conversation

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of scandal draws on the classical condemnation of *maledicentia* (evil-speaking) in Cicero's *De Officiis*, where the gentleman is advised to speak well of others or remain silent. The Stoic tradition treats gossip and defamation as disturbances of the rational soul — impulses to be mastered by reason. Chesterfield's psychological insight that the hearer of scandal comes to "detest the reporter" echoes Plutarch's essay "On Curiosity" (*De Curiositate*), which argues that the busybody who seeks out others' secrets ends by making himself odious. The distinction between legitimate knowledge of public affairs and illegitimate curiosity about private scandal has roots in the classical conception of the *vir bonus* (good man) whose speech serves the common good rather than private malice.
