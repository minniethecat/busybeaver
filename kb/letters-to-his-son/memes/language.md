# Language

Language, in Lord Chesterfield's educational scheme, is not merely a tool of communication but a fundamental accomplishment of the polished gentleman. His insistence that his son master modern languages — German, French, Italian — reflects the practical demands of eighteenth-century diplomacy and the broader belief that linguistic precision is inseparable from clarity of thought.

## Definition

Chesterfield's conception of language encompasses three distinct but interrelated competencies:

1. **Grammatical correctness** — the ability to speak and write foreign languages without errors in syntax, gender, or case. Chesterfield considers this the bare minimum, not an achievement [004-001].
2. **Stylistic purity** — the cultivation of an elegant, unaffected style in both speech and writing. Chesterfield distinguishes this sharply from pedantic ostentation [045-003].
3. **Social adaptation** — the skill of matching one's language to the company: "Speak the language of the company that you are in... speak it purely, and unlarded with any other" [030-005].

> "Writing and speaking the modern languages correctly... is an essential part of a gentleman's education." [004-001]

A striking feature of Chesterfield's approach is his emphasis on **living languages** over dead ones. While he values Latin and Greek as foundations, he repeatedly warns that a gentleman ignorant of French and German is ill-prepared for public life — "without speaking any one foreign language" is a mark of the provincial Englishman he despises [040-003].

## Key Passages

> "Writing and speaking the modern languages correctly; the knowledge of the laws of nations... these are the objects of a liberal education." [004-001]

> "Speak the language of the company that you are in; speak it purely, and unlarded with any other." [030-005]

> "A man who is ignorant of those two languages [French and German] is, in this age, a disgrace to his country." [040-002]

> "I do not mean... you should be content with the common runs of language... but I mean, that you should so far perfectly master the purity, the delicacy, and the idiom of it." [045-003]

> "I am told, [Voltaire] spoke his language very distinctly and gracefully." [043-003]

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical recommendations for language acquisition:

- **Immersion, not books**: Read newspapers and literature in the target language, write the German character, and converse with native speakers — never rely solely on grammars [024-003, 024-008]
- **Speak it purely**: When in German company, speak German — not French or Latin; to mix languages marks one as a pedant or a provincial [030-005]
- **Distinguish correctness from pedantry**: Master the language thoroughly, but avoid displaying learned ostentation — "Speak the language of the company that you are in... and never quote Latin or French" when among those who don't understand [030-005]
- **Model on the best**: Chesterfield points to Voltaire as an exemplar of graceful French speech [043-003]
- **Practice continuously**: Language is a skill that atrophies without use; Chesterfield's repeated urgings suggest he suspected his son was neglecting his German studies

## Classical References

Though Chesterfield emphasizes modern over ancient languages, his linguistic philosophy draws on classical rhetoric:
- **Cicero**'s ideal of the orator who combines grammatical precision with stylistic elegance
- **Quintilian**'s *Institutio Oratoria*, which treats language mastery as the foundation of all liberal education
- The Renaissance ideal of the *uomo universale*, fluent in multiple tongues
