# Habit

## Definition

In Chesterfield's moral psychology, **habit** is the mechanism by which repeated actions become settled dispositions — "a second nature," as the classical formulation has it. Habits operate in two directions: they can solidify virtue into instinctive excellence, or they can entrench vice until it becomes invisible to the one who practices it. Chesterfield's concern is overwhelmingly practical: since youth is the season when habits are formed, the wise young man must consciously cultivate good habits before bad ones take root.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of habit intensifies in the middle and later letters (1748–1750s), as the son enters adulthood and the window for easy habit-formation begins to close. The theme appears in three registers:

1. **Preventive (early letters)** — "Do not get a habit of neglecting" (042-002). The emphasis is on avoiding the formation of bad habits while the character is still plastic. Chesterfield warns against habits of negligence, inattention, and dissipation.

2. **Formative (middle letters)** — "You cannot make them too habitual and familiar to you" (055-009). The positive program: drill good manners, elegant expression, and attentive observation until they become second nature.

3. **Corrective (later letters)** — Chesterfield acknowledges that some faults "from long habit, have perhaps grown into real opinions" (064-001), and that correcting ingrained habits requires painful self-examination.

A key metaphor is the contrast between nature and habit: "education, custom, and habit" together shape a man, giving his natural temperament its final form (015-001).

> "A man may as surely get a habit of letting his reason prevail, as of letting his passions prevail" (071-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical program for habit-formation:

- **Begin early** — Habits formed in youth are the most durable; delay makes correction exponentially harder (042-001).
- **Cultivate small habits first** — Attention to dress, punctuality, and minor courtesies generalizes to larger virtues (046-002).
- **Practice good manners until they become automatic** — The social graces must be so deeply ingrained that they survive fatigue, ill-humor, and distraction (055-009).
- **Audit your own habits periodically** — Chesterfield urges self-examination to discover faults that habit has rendered invisible (064-001).
- **Avoid gaming and dissipation** — These "give you a habit of futility" from which it is nearly impossible to recover (044-002).

## Classical References

Chesterfield's theory of habit draws on:
- Aristotle's doctrine in the *Nicomachean Ethics* that virtue is a *hexis* (habit/disposition) acquired through repeated action — "we become just by doing just acts"
- The Stoic emphasis on *askesis* (training) as the path to virtue
- The Renaissance educational tradition (Erasmus, Montaigne) that treats early habituation as more important than later reasoning
