# Confidence

## Definition

**Confidence**, in Chesterfield's usage, is a double-edged concept. On one edge, it is the dangerous impulse to confide in others — a temptation he regards as among the most perilous a young man faces. "Indiscreet and unbounded confidence" leads "always to their loss, often to their ruin" (016-001). On the other edge, it is the invaluable social skill of inspiring trust in others — the ability to draw people into confiding their secrets, interests, and vulnerabilities. The gentleman must master the second while scrupulously avoiding the first.

> "Can you ever get into the confidence and the secrets of the courts where you reside?" (090-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's warnings about misplaced confidence are among the most emphatic in the entire correspondence. The young are particularly susceptible: "indiscreet and unguarded confidences, made in tender moments, to the objects of their passion" (097-002) — the erotic dimension of the warning is characteristically frank. Confidence is dangerous precisely because it feels virtuous: one confides out of trust, openness, and affection, and is punished for those very qualities.

The strategic alternative is carefully elaborated. The gentleman must study "the means of making people confide in you" (071-004) — not through manipulation but through demonstrated trustworthiness. "A seeming confidence in their moral virtues turns their heads" (071-004) — the key word is "seeming." Chesterfield does not advocate genuine openness; he advocates the appearance of it, deployed as a tool of social intelligence.

This apparent cynicism is grounded in hard experience. "All that passes, either by the confidences made him, or by the carelessness of people in general" (052-007) — the world is full of careless talk, and the wise man collects it without contributing to it. "Deserve attention better than confidence; to hold by them is a very prudent maxim" (079-007) — better to earn respect than to receive secrets.

Yet Chesterfield also speaks of confidence in a more positive register when discussing his son's judgment. "I have great confidence in it," he writes of Philip's discretion, "and am convinced that you will not make a bad use of the confidence I repose in you" (122-002). The father's own confidence in his son is the goal toward which the entire educational project strives — a trust earned through demonstrated prudence.

## Practical Advice

- Guard your own secrets with extreme care — confidences made in moments of emotional warmth are the most dangerous (016-001)
- Inspire confidence in others by appearing to trust them, without actually placing your interests in their hands (071-004)
- Observe what people reveal through carelessness — most information is given, not extracted (052-007)
- Cultivate a reputation for deserving attention rather than confidence — the former is safer and more durable (079-007)
- Earn a father's confidence through demonstrated discretion — trust is the final reward of a well-conducted youth (122-002)
