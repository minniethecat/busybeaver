# Social Grace

## Definition

The **graces** (*les grâces*) are the central obsession of Chesterfield's educational philosophy — the indefinable quality of ease, charm, and pleasing manner that makes every virtue attractive and every talent effective. Without the graces, he repeatedly insists, learning is pedantry, wit is impertinence, and virtue itself is unlovable. The graces are the polish on the diamond: they add nothing to its substance but everything to its value.

## In the Letters

The graces appear so frequently in Chesterfield's letters that they amount to a leitmotif. His treatment can be traced through several recurring themes:

1. **The graces as doctrine**: The preface to the published letters famously describes Chesterfield as "a preacher of the gospel of not grace, but — 'the graces, the graces, the graces'" (P01-001). This is no exaggeration — the graces are his gospel
2. **Grace as the differentiator**: Chesterfield insists that the graces distinguish the truly accomplished gentleman from the merely learned one. "An awkward address, a slovenly figure, an ungraceful manner of speaking, will prejudice people against the most solid merit" (032-001)
3. **Acquirable through practice**: The graces are not inborn — they can and must be acquired. "To speak correctly, and with ease and grace, are certainly to be acquired by reading the best authors and conversing with the best company" (001-003)
4. **Seduction and prepossession**: "That engaging manner, and those graces, which seduce and prepossess people in your favor at first sight" (023-005) — the graces operate before reason can intervene, winning affection before judgment
5. **Grace as social lubricant**: The graceful manner "of doing all these things" — the small courtesies of daily life — makes ordinary interactions pleasant rather than merely transactional (032-001)

The cumulative effect of Chesterfield's teaching is to make gracefulness not an optional ornament but the essential medium through which all other qualities must be expressed.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical guidance on acquiring the graces includes:

- Observe the most graceful people in every company and imitate their manner — not slavishly, but analytically
- Practice speaking before a mirror to correct ungraceful habits of expression and gesture
- Dancing is essential — it teaches bodily grace and ease of movement
- Never appear hurried or flustered; grace is incompatible with haste
- The graces must become habitual — they must be "naturalized" so thoroughly that they appear effortless

## Classical References

Chesterfield's doctrine of the graces draws on:

- **The Greek Charites** — the three Graces of classical mythology (Aglaia, Euphrosyne, Thalia), goddesses of charm, beauty, and creativity
- **Cicero** — *De Oratore* discusses the importance of *actio* (delivery) in oratory — the graceful embodiment of argument
- **Castiglione** — *The Book of the Courtier* and its concept of *sprezzatura* (studied nonchalance) is the Renaissance precursor to Chesterfield's graces
- **French moralists** — La Rochefoucauld and La Bruyère on the social arts; Chesterfield's "je ne sais quoi" comes from this tradition
