# Nature

## Definition

**Nature** is, for Chesterfield, the most frequently invoked authority in the letters — more than the classics, more than religion, more than any individual mentor. It serves simultaneously as explanation and prescription. "Human nature" describes the permanent features of human character that the wise man must acknowledge and work with: self-love, pride, the desire for esteem. "The nature of things" describes the objective structure of the world that determines what is and is not possible. And "good-nature" describes a quality of character — benevolence, good humor, the disposition to please and be pleased. Chesterfield's repeated counsel to "follow nature" is not a Romantic invitation to spontaneity but a Stoic injunction to align one's conduct with reality.

> "Follow nature and not fashion: weigh the present, and the future, in their proper scales." (007-006)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's first use of "nature" is characteristically practical, in Letter I: "Dress is of the same nature; you must dress; therefore attend to it" (001-004). The "nature" of dress — what it is, essentially — is that it is unavoidable. One must dress; therefore one should dress well. This is the logic of "the nature of things" applied to daily life: understand the reality, then act accordingly.

In Letter II, Chesterfield instructs his son to "inform yourself daily of the nature of the government and constitution of the country where you are" (002-001). Nature here means the essential character, the animating principles. A diplomat must understand not merely the surface forms of a foreign government but its "nature" — how it actually works, where power truly resides.

The perfectionist ideal appears in Letter VI: "Good-breeding, and good-nature, are the perfection of human nature" (006-001). This is one of Chesterfield's most important declarations. Good-breeding and good-nature together constitute not just a social accomplishment but the highest realization of what it means to be human. The phrase elevates manners to anthropology — politeness is not mere convention but the fulfillment of our nature.

Letter VII delivers the methodological principle: "Follow nature and not fashion: weigh the present, and the future, in their proper scales" (007-006). Nature is set against fashion — the permanent against the ephemeral, the real against the conventional. To follow nature is to make decisions based on the enduring structure of things rather than the passing preferences of the moment. The parenthetical instruction to "weigh the present, and the future" is the practical application — fashion considers only the present; nature considers both.

In Letter XV, the study of human nature becomes a project: "Characters resort, and human nature is seen in all the various shapes and forms which it can assume" (015-001). The great world — courts, cities, assemblies — is a laboratory of human nature. The young man should observe it as a naturalist observes specimens, cataloguing the varieties of character and motive.

The most philosophically dense formulation appears in Letter XVII: "That degree of pride and self-love, which is inseparable from human nature" (017-007). Some things are "inseparable from human nature" — they cannot be removed because they are constitutive. Pride and self-love are not defects to be cured but features to be managed. This is the Stoic strain in Chesterfield: the wise man does not fight against human nature but works with it.

Letter XX introduces the normative force of nature: "The nature of things (as I have often told you) is the truest and best rule of conduct" (020-005). This is Chesterfield's epistemology in a sentence. The "nature of things" — objective reality, properly understood — is the standard against which all conduct should be measured. Not custom, not fashion, not the opinions of others, but "the nature of things." A man who understands human nature understands how to succeed; a man who understands the nature of things understands how to live.

In Letter XXII, Chesterfield clarifies his own paternal affection: "But do not mistake the nature of that affection, and think it to be a weakness" (022-002). A father's love is not sentimental indulgence but a calculated investment in his son's improvement. He warns against thinking that he depends "upon any weakness in my nature, for a reconciliation, as children do upon their parents" (022-003). Nature, in this context, is not an excuse for softness — it is a standard to which one must rise.

Letter XXVI lists the ingredients of social success: "Good sense, and good nature, are the principal ingredients of all human society" (026-005). Good sense provides judgment; good nature provides benevolence. Together they constitute the minimal equipment of a social being. Without good nature, good sense becomes cunning; without good sense, good nature becomes foolishness.

In Letter XXXV, Chesterfield acknowledges the power of natural endowment over education: "At your age nature takes sufficient care of the body" (035-001). Nature provides; education supplements. The body knows what it needs; the mind must be taught. This is a recurring theme — nature gives the foundation, art builds the superstructure.

The political application comes in Letter XXXVIII: "Ministers are both men; their nature and passions the same, the motives of their actions the same" (038-002). The diplomat dealing with foreign ministers should remember that they are, beneath their offices, men like any others — driven by the same self-love, vanity, and ambition that drive all men. Understanding "the nature of things" means understanding that all politics is, at bottom, human nature in action.

Letter XLIV completes the circle by linking nature back to breeding: "Good-breeding, and good-nature, are the source of all that is agreeable in society" (044-001). The phrase "good-nature" here means benevolence, the disposition to please rather than offend. It is a natural endowment that breeding refines, just as good sense is a natural endowment that education develops.

> "Good-breeding, and good-nature, are the perfection of human nature." (006-001)

## Practical Advice

- Study "the nature of things" as the foundation of all good judgment — it is "the truest and best rule of conduct," more reliable than fashion, custom, or popular opinion (020-005)
- Follow nature, not fashion — "weigh the present, and the future, in their proper scales" rather than being swayed by what is merely current (007-006)
- Cultivate "good-nature" alongside good-breeding — together they are "the perfection of human nature" and "the source of all that is agreeable in society" (006-001, 044-001)
- Accept what is "inseparable from human nature" rather than fighting it — pride and self-love cannot be eliminated, only managed (017-007)
- Observe human nature as a naturalist observes specimens — "human nature is seen in all the various shapes and forms" in courts, cities, and assemblies (015-001)
- Remember that all men, however elevated, share the same nature — "ministers are both men; their nature and passions the same" (038-002)
