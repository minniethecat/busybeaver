# Rank

## Definition

**Rank**, for Chesterfield, is a practical fact of social life rather than a source of pride — a position that carries both advantages and obligations, and that must be navigated with skill rather than assumed as a substitute for merit. His treatment of rank is characteristically pragmatic: high birth opens doors, but it cannot hold them open; one must earn what rank merely lends. He teaches his son to respect rank in others without being "dazzled" by it (039-001), and to use his own rank as a supplement to personal accomplishment, never as a replacement.

> "Your own rank and fortune will not assist you; your merit and your manners can alone raise you." (032-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield writes from a position of genuine aristocratic standing — himself an earl, a former Secretary of State, and a man whose son was, by birth, entitled to move in the highest circles. Yet his counsel on rank is notably cautious: rank is an asset but an insufficient one. "Your own rank and fortune will not assist you; your merit and your manners can alone raise you" (032-001). The message is blunt — a young man who relies on his birth to carry him will find himself carried nowhere.

The same caution appears when he acknowledges his son's disadvantage: "you have not the advantage of rank or fortune to bear you up; I have no doubt but you will make your own way" (040-001). Here rank is framed not as a source of entitlement but as a practical tool the young man must compensate for lacking.

Chesterfield also insists on the social intelligence to calibrate conduct by rank: "there is a certain manner of behavior to be used to people of all ranks; which is a point so material" (049-001). One must neither fawn before superiors nor condescend to inferiors. True gentlemen "are not dazzled by superior rank: they know, and pay all the regard that is due to it, but without being awed or abashed" (039-001).

The spectrum of rank runs from "people of considerable birth, rank, and character" (053-002) to those who are "low indeed; low in rank, low in parts, low in manners" (053-006). Chesterfield's ideal is a man who can move comfortably across this entire range, adapting his manner while maintaining his dignity.

## Key Passages

> "Your own rank and fortune will not assist you; your merit and your manners can alone raise you to the figure you ought to make in the world." (032-001)

> "Well-bred people are not dazzled by superior rank: they know, and pay all the regard that is due to it, but without being awed or abashed." (039-001)

> "You have not the advantage of rank or fortune to bear you up; I have no doubt but you will make your own way." (040-001)

> "It is a sort of defensive conduct never to be accused of pride for showing respect to superior rank." (044-001)

> "There is a certain manner of behavior to be used to people of all ranks; which is a point so material." (049-001)

> "People of considerable birth, rank, and character; for people of no birth, rank, or character." (053-002)

> "Low indeed; low in rank, low in parts, low in manners, and consequently low in company and in life." (053-006)

## Practical Advice

- Never rely on rank alone — "your merit and your manners can alone raise you" (032-001)
- Pay due respect to superior rank without servility — well-bred people "are not dazzled" (039-001)
- Adapt your manner to the rank of your company — there is "a certain manner to be used to people of all ranks" (049-001)
- If you lack the advantage of high birth, compensate with merit and accomplishment (040-001)
- Avoid the "low" condition that combines low rank, low parts, and low manners into a comprehensive social failure (053-006)
