# Virtue
## Definition
In Chesterfield's educational philosophy, **virtue** is the moral foundation upon which all other accomplishments must rest. He distinguishes it sharply from mere external graces: virtue is the substance, while manners and wit are the ornament. Without virtue, he insists, no man can truly be called a gentleman.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's treatment of virtue is thoroughly classical in character, drawing on Stoic and Ciceronian ethics rather than Christian piety. His conception of virtue encompasses:
1. **Honor**: Truthfulness in word and deed—"Your moral character must be not only pure, but, like Caesar's wife, unsuspected" (100-002)
2. **Prudence**: The practical wisdom to navigate the world without compromising one's principles
3. **Self-mastery**: The discipline to govern one's passions and appetites
4. **Integrity**: Consistency between public conduct and private character
Chesterfield's moral philosophy is worldly, not saintly. He expects a gentleman to be virtuous enough to earn respect, but not so rigid as to be impractical. This has provoked centuries of debate about the sincerity and depth of his ethical teaching.
## Key Passages
> "Your moral character must be not only pure, but, like Caesar's wife, unsuspected. The least speck or blemish upon it is fatal." (100-002)
> "Pleasure is the rock which most young people split upon: they launch out with crowded sails in quest of it, but without a compass to direct their course, or reason sufficient to steer the vessel; for want of which, pain and shame, instead of pleasure, are the returns of their voyage." (007-001)
> "Nullum numen abest, si sit prudentia" — No divine power is absent if prudence is present (001-003)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's practical teaching on virtue includes:
- Honor must be the governing principle of all actions
- A gentleman's word must be his bond—never promise what you cannot perform
- Avoid even the appearance of vice, for reputation is as important as reality
- Study history to learn from the virtuous and the vicious alike
- Distinguish between true virtue and its counterfeit (hypocrisy, self-righteousness)
## Classical References
Chesterfield grounds his conception of virtue in classical sources:
- Cicero's *De Officiis* — the foundational text for Chesterfield's ethics of gentlemanly conduct
- Stoic philosophy — the emphasis on self-mastery and indifference to fortune
- Horace — quoted frequently on the golden mean and the follies of excess
