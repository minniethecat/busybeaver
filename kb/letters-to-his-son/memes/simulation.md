## Definition

Chesterfield distinguishes carefully between simulation and dissimulation — a distinction he takes from political philosophy. Simulation is the active feigning of qualities, sentiments, or intentions that one does not possess: appearing pleased when one is indifferent, professing warmth when one feels coldness, performing virtue one does not practice. Dissimulation, by contrast, is the concealment or suppression of what one truly is — hiding one's cards rather than playing false ones. Chesterfield regards dissimulation as necessary and generally defensible; simulation he treats as a more morally ambiguous instrument, to be used sparingly and never with "ill intent."

## In the Letters

Chesterfield first raises the topic in the context of courtly conduct. Citing historical examples, he notes that rulers who mastered dissimulation "found it more effectual than violence" (079-003). The distinction between the two arts is made explicit around 1749: simulation and dissimulation are different things, and while both are tools of the courtier and diplomat, simulation carries the greater risk of corruption.

By the middle letters, Chesterfield's advice becomes more granular. He acknowledges that "no simulation of passion is too gross" when one seeks favor at court (097-004), but he also warns that simulation, if detected, destroys reputation more thoroughly than honest opposition ever could. The language of simulation and dissimulation, he observes, is "very hard" to master (116-028) — it is a skill acquired only through long practice and careful observation of those more experienced.

The moral frame is clearest in the late letters. Chesterfield insists that simulation is not perfidy "if not used with an ill intention" (141-001), and that a gentleman may deploy it defensively without becoming false. But he also records the downward arc of those who rely on it too heavily: the man "long the dupe of his simulation and dissimulation" eventually loses the trust of all parties (267-003).

## Key Passages

> "Do not think that I am now recommending dissimulation to you; I both own and justify it in some cases. But simulation I recommend to you still more rarely, and with still greater caution." (071-001)

> "He who knew the passions of men, and the various workings of those passions at courts, found dissimulation more effectual than violence; and simulation sometimes necessary, but always dangerous." (079-003)

> "Lord Bacon makes a distinction between simulation and dissimulation; and allows the latter, while he more or less condemns the former." (100-006)

> "At courts there is a language, a set of phrases, expressions, and habits; the language of simulation and dissimulation: very hard, and requiring much attention and observation to acquire." (116-028)

> "There is a wide difference between simulation and dissimulation. Dissimulation is only to hide our own cards; simulation is to put on a false appearance, with regard to other people." (141-001)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers three rules for simulation. First, **prefer dissimulation**: it is safer to conceal your cards than to play false ones. Second, **never simulate with ill intent**: simulation used to harm others is perfidy; simulation used to protect oneself or smooth social relations is legitimate. Third, **master the language gradually**: the vocabulary and gestures of simulation — the courtly smile, the feigned interest, the diplomatic silence — must be learned by observation, not from books. A novice who simulates clumsily is worse off than one who never attempts it.

## Classical References

Chesterfield explicitly cites Francis Bacon's distinction between simulation and dissimulation (100-006), grounding his own advice in a philosophical tradition that treats political prudence as a legitimate art. The broader framework — that some degree of concealment and even feigning is necessary to navigate a world of competing interests — owes much to Machiavelli, though Chesterfield is careful to frame his advice within the language of gentlemanly honor rather than ruthless statecraft. The distinction between defensive dissimulation (hiding one's cards) and offensive simulation (playing a false hand) is also traceable to classical rhetorical training, where the orator's management of his own persona was a recognized skill.
