## Definition

Custom, in Chesterfield's social philosophy, is the accumulated practice of a society — the "established customs" (082-008) and "forms and modes" (015-001) that govern behavior in particular places and times. He distinguishes between two kinds of custom: that which regulates mere ceremony and outward form, and that which touches on moral truth. In the first domain, custom is sovereign — the prudent man "conforms to custom" (007-007) and treats its violation as an "impertinent insult upon custom and fashion" (042-002). In the second, custom is strictly subordinate to moral law: "custom can never alter the nature of good and ill" (052-003). Chesterfield's teaching on custom thus balances two imperatives: conform where convention governs, but never mistake convention for conscience.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces custom early in his educational program, instructing his son to study "the customs, and the manners of the several parts of Europe" (001-003) alongside their history and constitutions. Custom is not merely a matter of etiquette but of practical intelligence — the man who knows the customs of a country knows how to conduct himself there. He counsels conformity: "I would conform to custom" (007-007) in matters of amusement and ceremony, treating resistance to harmless convention as a form of vanity. The most vivid illustration is his anecdote about Mr. — who refused to kneel at Communion in a Catholic country: Chesterfield considers this "an impertinent insult upon custom and fashion" (042-002), not a principled stand. The theoretical foundation appears in his meditation on the mutability of custom: "modes and customs vary often, but human nature is still the same" (064-002). Custom is the surface, human nature the constant depth. His clearest statement of the limits of custom's authority is moral: "custom can never alter the nature of good and ill" (052-003) — the gentleman conforms to convention but never confuses it with virtue. The social law of custom Chesterfield traces to the pragmatic: "this is therefore established by custom, and certainly necessary" (071-004) — custom solves coordination problems without requiring explicit legislation. In religion and ceremony, he advocates compliance: "I would comply with an established custom" (082-008) rather than draw attention through conspicuous refusal.

## Key Passages

> "Your destination is the great and busy world; your immediate object is the affairs, the interests, the history, the constitutions, the customs, and the manners of the several parts of Europe." (001-003)

> "I would conform to custom, but I would take care not to be led by it into any custom that would hurt either my health, my fortune, or my character." (007-007)

> "The world is a country which nobody ever yet knew by description; one must travel through it oneself to be acquainted with it. The knowledge of the world is a knowledge of the manners and customs of men." (015-001)

> "This is an impertinent insult upon custom and fashion. You remember Mr. —, who would never kneel — and who was always making himself ridiculous by such unnecessary and ill-timed deviations from established custom." (042-002)

> "Custom can never alter the nature of good and ill; and though it may make some things appear less criminal, it can never make them less so." (052-003)

> "Modes and customs vary often, but human nature is still the same." (064-002)

> "This is therefore established by custom, and certainly necessary. But there are other decencies of behavior, which though established by custom, are however not necessary." (071-004)

> "I would comply with an established custom, rather than draw the eyes of the whole company upon me by a ridiculous and affected singularity." (082-008)

## Related Concepts

Custom is closely related to [[manners]], which are the specific behavioral rules that custom establishes in a given society (015-001). [[Fashion]] is the transient form of custom — "fashion and custom only give the different shape" (089-001) to the same underlying impulses. [[Conformity]] is the practical response to custom's demands: the prudent man "conforms to custom" (007-007) in harmless matters. [[Singularity]] and [[affectation]] are the vices of the man who defies custom for personal vanity (042-002, 082-008). [[Human nature]] is the constant beneath custom's variations (064-002). [[Religion]] and [[ceremony]] are domains where custom's authority is strongest, and defiance most imprudent (082-008).

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on custom is nuanced and practical:

- **Study the customs of every country you enter**: Customs are as essential to "the knowledge of the world" as history and politics (001-003, 015-001)
- **Conform to harmless customs**: In matters of "amusement" and ceremony, "conform to custom" (007-007) — resistance to convention for its own sake is vanity
- **Never violate established custom for personal distinction**: The man who refuses to kneel or otherwise deviates from "established custom" makes himself "ridiculous" and "impertinent" (042-002, 082-008)
- **Distinguish necessary from optional customs**: Some customs are "established by custom, and certainly necessary" (071-004); others are merely conventional and may be observed or not as circumstances permit
- **Never let custom override morality**: "Custom can never alter the nature of good and ill" (052-003); the gentleman conforms in ceremony but never compromises on virtue
- **Remember that custom is the surface, not the substance**: "Modes and customs vary often, but human nature is still the same" (064-002); study human nature first, customs second

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of custom draws on the ancient distinction between *nomos* (convention, law, custom) and *physis* (nature), a central theme of Greek philosophy from the Sophists through Aristotle. His insistence that "custom can never alter the nature of good and ill" (052-003) echoes Cicero's argument in *De Legibus* that moral law is grounded in nature, not in the variable decrees of peoples. The pragmatic counsel to conform to local custom while maintaining inner moral independence has roots in the Stoic concept of *decorum* — the appropriate adaptation of behavior to circumstance without compromising virtue. Montaigne's essays on custom ("Of Custom, and That We Should Not Easily Change a Law Received") provide the early modern framework: custom is the "second nature" that shapes behavior so profoundly that men mistake it for truth itself. Chesterfield's distinction between necessary customs (071-004) and merely conventional ones reflects the Enlightenment project of subjecting all received practice to rational scrutiny while maintaining outward compliance for the sake of social peace.
