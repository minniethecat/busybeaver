# Justice
## Definition
Chesterfield treats **justice** not as an abstract legal principle but as a practical moral rule governing all social and personal conduct. His definition is strikingly simple: "To do as you would be done by" — the Golden Rule. He explicitly rejects the sophistical refinements of casuists who would carve exceptions into this plain standard, insisting that justice is a matter of common sense available to every reasonable person. For Chesterfield, justice is inseparable from character; a man suspected of injustice is a man whose reputation is fundamentally compromised.
## In the Letters
Justice surfaces in the letters in three distinct registers:
1. **Moral justice** — The obligation to give others their due: credit where credit is earned, fair judgment where judgment is rendered. Chesterfield himself models this in Letter VI when he declares himself "obliged, in justice both to them and to you," to relay favorable reports about his son.
2. **Historical justice** — The perversion of justice by power and ideology. In his historical survey of the Reformation (Letter XXXVII), Chesterfield observes how the language of religious dispute became "the specious covering of injustice and ambition," a warning against allowing noble words to mask self-serving ends.
3. **Social justice** — The psychological dimension. Chesterfield's penetrating observation that "men will much sooner forgive an injustice than an insult" (Letter XLIX) reflects his conviction that pride, more than fairness, governs human reactions. The slight to dignity stings more sharply than the breach of right.
## Key Passages
> "To do as you would be done by, is the plain, sure, and undisputed rule of morality and justice. Stick to that; and be convinced that whatever breaks into it, in any degree, however speciously it may be turned … is, notwithstanding, false in itself, unjust, and criminal." (052-004)
> "I thought myself obliged, in justice both to them and, to you, to let you know it. Those who deserve a good character, ought to have the satisfaction of knowing that they have it, both as a reward and as an encouragement." (006-001)
> "Men … will much sooner forgive an injustice than an insult. Every man is not ambitious, or courteous, or passionate; but every man has pride enough in his composition to feel and resent the least slight and contempt." (049-004)
> "Should you be suspected of injustice, malignity, perfidy, lying, envy, etc., all the parts and knowledge in the world will never procure you esteem, friendship, or respect." (100-004)
> "The Reformation … became the specious covering of injustice and ambition." (037-003)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's counsel on justice emphasizes:
- Adhere to the plain rule of doing unto others as you would have them do unto you, and resist clever arguments that would justify exceptions
- Give people the credit they deserve, openly — it is a requirement of justice, not merely generosity
- Beware of ideologies and "refinements" that dress injustice in respectable language
- Never let pride or the desire to wound override the obligation to be fair
- If you must choose between wounding a man's pride and doing him injustice, know that the first may cost you a friend, but the second will cost you your character
## Classical References
Chesterfield draws on the natural-law tradition, citing the Golden Rule as a principle of right reason. He critiques the Jesuit casuists (particularly Escobar's twenty-four collected cases) as exemplars of how plausible reasoning can corrupt plain moral truth. His position aligns with the Stoic conviction that virtue is accessible to every rational being through common sense — *recta ratio* (right reason) — without the need for elaborate philosophical apparatus.
