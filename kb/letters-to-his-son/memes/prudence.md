# Prudence

## Definition

**Prudence** is, in Chesterfield's lexicon, the sovereign virtue of practical life — the ability to govern one's actions by reason, calculate consequences, and choose the course most likely to secure one's interests. It is distinct from mere caution or timidity; Chesterfield's ideal of prudence is an active, strategic intelligence that knows when to advance and when to retreat, what to reveal and what to conceal.

> "In any point which prudence bids you pursue, and which a manifestly private interest does not oppose, pursue it steadily." (071-002)

Chesterfield pairs prudence frequently with "reason" and "abilities," treating it as the executive faculty that translates good judgment into effective action. Without prudence, even the most brilliant talents lead to ruin.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces prudence early as a practical necessity in social and professional life. In the middle letters, he deepens the concept by contrasting prudence with its opposites — "imprudence" and "carelessness" — which he treats not as intellectual failures but as character defects in "men of the best sense" (054-010). The later letters show prudence operating in the highest spheres of diplomacy and statecraft, where "consummate skill and prudence" (139-001) determine the fate of nations.

> "The prudence and necessity of often concealing the truth, in short, the whole system of good-breeding, was, in his judgment, comprehended in that single article." (100-005)

A recurring theme is the tension between prudence and passion. Chesterfield warns repeatedly that even wise men are undone when passion overrides prudence: "though they know all the imprudence of it" (054-010), they cannot help themselves. The remedy, he suggests, is to make prudence habitual rather than relying on moment-by-moment self-control.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical counsel on prudence can be distilled into several maxims:

- **Pursue steadily what prudence dictates** — once a course is chosen by reason, follow through without wavering (071-002)
- **Conceal when necessary** — prudence often requires withholding information, not from dishonesty but from strategic necessity (100-005)
- **Retreat gently** — if prudence demands abandoning a pursuit, do so by degrees, not abruptly (133-005)
- **Check passion with prudence** — the vivacity of youth is a dangerous ally; prudence must govern it (179-001)
- **Let prudence govern love** — even intimate relationships benefit from prudent management; "loving with prudence" prevents irreconcilable quarrels (179-003)
- **Prefer prudence to pettishness** — peevish conduct is not only ineffective but contemptible; prudence keeps the temper in check (200-001)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's conception of prudence draws heavily on the classical virtue of *prudentia* — one of the four cardinal virtues in both Platonic and Ciceronian ethics. Cicero, in *De Officiis*, defines *prudentia* as the knowledge of what is good, what is bad, and what is neither — exactly the calculating, consequentialist intelligence Chesterfield advocates. The pairing of "prudence and sound policy" (168-001) echoes Machiavelli's *prudenza*, the political virtue of the successful prince, though Chesterfield wraps it in the language of honor and religion.
