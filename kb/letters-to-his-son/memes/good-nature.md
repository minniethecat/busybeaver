## Definition

Good-nature, for Chesterfield, is the fundamental disposition of benevolence — the inclination to think and act kindly toward others. It is, he writes, "the mixed result of good-nature and a vanity by no means blameable" (149-002) that produces the desire to please. Unlike acquired graces such as [[good-breeding]] or [[politeness]], good-nature is partly innate — a quality of character rather than a skill. Yet Chesterfield insists that good-nature alone is insufficient: without the refinement of manners, it remains "ineffectual and ungraceful" (089-001), like a jewel in the rough.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield treats good-nature as a foundation that requires cultivation. In the early letters he pairs it with good-breeding: "Good-breeding, and good-nature, do incline us rather to raise and help people" (044-001). The two concepts are complementary — good-nature supplies the benevolent intent, while good-breeding supplies the form that makes benevolence effective. He applies this lens to the people he observes: "They tell me that he is good-natured, and does not want parts" (060-005) — praise that is qualified by the implication that parts and good-nature may not be enough. Of another acquaintance he notes "He has more good-nature and generosity than parts" (083-007), again drawing the distinction between natural benevolence and developed ability. Chesterfield is keenly aware of the limits of good-nature in a competitive world: "People in high life are hardened to the wants and distresses of mankind, as surgeons are to their bodily pains" (133-003), warning that one cannot always "hope for from their justice or good-nature." He also notes the social deception around good-nature — "good-breeding passes for good-nature" (179-003), meaning that polished manners can simulate a benevolence one does not genuinely feel.

## Related Concepts

Good-nature forms the moral foundation for several Chesterfieldian social virtues. [[good-breeding]] is the polish applied to the raw material of good-nature; without breeding, good-nature is ineffectual (089-001). [[civility]] is the outward expression of good-nature in social interaction (128-001). [[generosity]] is good-nature extended to material assistance (083-007). [[pleasing]] — the desire to be agreeable — arises from the combination of good-nature and a "vanity by no means blameable" (149-002). Good-nature must be guarded by [[prudence]], lest the "very good-natured" man be thought "a very silly man" (110-002) who cannot refuse any request. [[humanity]] is good-nature raised to a general principle.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical observations on good-nature include:

- **Let good-nature incline, let interest enforce**: Good-nature provides the initial impulse to act well; self-interest provides the sustaining motive (088-004, 090-006)
- **Do not let good-nature become weakness**: The man who is merely good-natured "is thought a very silly man" (110-002) — benevolence must be paired with judgment to avoid exploitation
- **Distinguish good-nature from good-breeding**: Good-breeding can pass for good-nature (179-003), but genuine good-nature is a character trait, not a performance
- **Good sense + good-nature = civility**: The combination of sound judgment and benevolent disposition naturally produces civil behavior (128-001)
- **Do not rely on the good-nature of the powerful**: In high life, people are "hardened to the wants and distresses of mankind" (133-003); expect nothing from their benevolence that self-interest does not also command

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of good-nature draws on the classical virtue of *benevolentia* — goodwill toward others — as articulated by Cicero in *De Officiis*, where it is treated as a natural human inclination that must be cultivated by reason and directed by judgment. The Stoic tradition of *philanthropia* (love of humankind) provides a philosophical grounding for good-nature as a universal disposition. Chesterfield's insistence that good-nature without polish is ineffectual echoes Aristotle's teaching in the *Nicomachean Ethics* that natural virtue must be completed by practical wisdom (*phronesis*) to become full virtue.
