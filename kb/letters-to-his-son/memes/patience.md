# Patience

## Definition

**Patience**, for Chesterfield, is not a passive resignation but an active discipline of the will. It is the cultivated ability to govern one's own impatience — to listen when one wishes to speak, to wait when one wishes to act, and to endure discomfort when one wishes to escape it. Chesterfield treats patience as a social instrument: without it, a man cannot be agreeable in company, cannot profit from instruction, and cannot navigate the inevitable delays and frustrations of public life.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of patience evolves across the correspondence. In the **early letters** (1746–1750), patience appears primarily as a discipline the young Philip must learn — the patience to listen to tedious speakers, to endure his tutor's instruction, to wait for letters and replies. Chesterfield repeatedly confesses his own *impatience* for news of his son's progress, modeling the very impatience he urges his son to overcome.

> "I wait with impatience for your accurate history of the Court of Dresden." (051-001)

In the **middle letters** (1750–1760), patience shifts into a social virtue. Chesterfield warns that nobody will hear a man with patience if he does not first learn to hear others with patience. The social dimension is paramount: patience is the price of admission to conversation, and those who lack it are shut out.

> "Nobody heard him with patience; and the young fellows used to joke upon it, and called him the impatient hearer." (093-002)

In the **late letters** (1760–1767), patience takes on a more somber register. Chesterfield's deafness, which cuts him off from society, becomes a trial of patience he must endure alone. The virtue he once prescribed for social success is now required merely to bear his own condition.

> "My deafness, I own, has tried my patience; as it has cut me off from so much social pleasure." (177-007)

## Key Passages

> "Endeavor to be always master of yourself, and never give way to passion, upon any account; patience, to hear frivolous, impertinent, and even unreasonable things, without expressing any uneasiness or contempt." (025-002)

This passage establishes patience as a form of self-command — not feeling no irritation, but refusing to show it. Chesterfield links patience directly to the Stoic ideal of mastering one's own passions.

> "If a man lays hold of you, hear him with patience (and at least seeming attention), if he is ever so frivolous or impertinent." (054-005)

The parenthetical "(and at least seeming attention)" is characteristically Chesterfieldian: patience need not be sincere, only convincing. The performance of patience is as valuable as patience itself.

> "There is nothing which nobody will hear you with patience: this everybody may acquire, who will but take the necessary pains." (102-006)

Chesterfield insists that patient attention is universally attainable — it is a matter of effort, not natural temperament.

> "Nothing but resolution, and an unwearied perseverance. None but madmen attempt impossibilities; but whatever is only difficult, and spirit and perseverance will get the better of the latter." (115-002)

Here patience merges with perseverance: sustained effort over time is the practical expression of patience in the pursuit of ambitious goals.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical counsel on patience clusters around three domains:

1. **In conversation**: Never interrupt. Hear even the most tedious speaker with an appearance of attention. The reputation for being a good listener is earned entirely through patience.

2. **In waiting**: Whether waiting for letters, for advancement, or for a desired outcome, learn to occupy the interval productively. Impatience, expressed, never accelerates the awaited event and always damages one's standing.

3. **In adversity**: Physical afflictions (Chesterfield's own deafness) and social disappointments must be borne without complaint. The man who complains of what cannot be changed advertises his weakness.

The thread connecting all three: patience is less about what one feels than about what one *shows*. The internal experience of impatience is natural; its external expression is a choice, and a damaging one.

## Classical References

Chesterfield's treatment of patience draws heavily on Stoic philosophy, particularly the works of [[cicero]] and Seneca. The Stoic doctrine of distinguishing between what is within one's control (one's own responses) and what is not (external events, other people's behavior) is the philosophical foundation of the Chesterfieldian patience. When Chesterfield urges his son to hear tedious speakers "without expressing any uneasiness or contempt," he is echoing Epictetus's teaching that it is not things that disturb us but our judgments about things.

The classical ideal of *constantia* — steadiness of character under pressure — also informs Chesterfield's conception. Patience is not passivity but the active maintenance of one's composure and purposes despite obstacles.
