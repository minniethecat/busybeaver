## Definition

Greatness, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is not a matter of station or wealth but of character — an inward elevation of mind and soul that distinguishes the truly superior man from the merely fortunate one. He distinguishes sharply between "real" greatness and "borrowed greatness" (116-015): the former is the greatness of soul (*magnanimité*) that makes a man worthy of admiration, while the latter is the reflected glory of office or title that vanishes when the external prop is removed. Chesterfield's model is the Duke of Marlborough, whose "greatness and riches" he traces not to accident but to "those graces" of character and conduct (056-004). Greatness, properly understood, is the earned consequence of merit, not the inherited accident of birth.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of greatness spans the full correspondence. Early in the letters, he warns that "affectation cannot stand the rays of greatness; they are frightened out of their wits when they are in company with real merit" (039-001) — genuine greatness exposes pretense by its mere presence. In the middle period, he uses Marlborough as the exemplar: the Duke's greatness was not a gift but an achievement, the product of graces cultivated over a lifetime (056-004). Chesterfield's political realism emerges in his acknowledgment that "cunning and craft" may be permissible in statecraft "when joined with the want of power," yet even this concession is framed against "the notion of greatness of soul or importance of affairs" (116-008). The distinction between real and borrowed greatness is his central teaching: "quitting a real for a borrowed greatness" is the error of those who mistake the trappings of office for the substance of merit (116-015). In the final letters, he reflects that kings "with good reason" see their "greatness and glory singly depend upon" the prosperity of their people (287-001) — greatness, even at the highest station, is earned by service.

## Key Passages

> "…affectation cannot stand the rays of greatness; they are frightened out of their wits when they are in company with real merit." (039-001)

> "…of the Duke of Marlborough's greatness and riches to those graces. He was not only a great general, but a great man in every respect." (056-004)

> "It allows of cunning and craft, when joined with the want of power, to supply the place of strength; and, joined with the notion of greatness of soul or importance of affairs, to supply the place of abilities." (116-008)

> "…quitting a real for a borrowed greatness. The latter pleases the court and the populace, but the former only satisfies the man of sense." (116-015)

> "…and with good reason, as their greatness and glory singly depend upon the happiness and prosperity of their people." (287-001)

## Related Concepts

Greatness is the crown of [[merit]]: where merit is the substance, greatness is the recognition it commands (039-001). It is closely allied to [[virtue]] — greatness without virtue is merely "borrowed greatness" (116-015). [[Honor]] and [[glory]] are greatness's public manifestations (287-001). The concept stands opposed to [[affectation]] (039-001), [[vanity]], and the petty arts of [[cunning]] (116-008). The [[gentleman-ideal]] is the social embodiment of greatness in Chesterfield's scheme: the man whose inward superiority expresses itself in outward grace. [[Ambition]], properly directed, is the engine of greatness — but ambition without merit produces only the counterfeit.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on greatness is directed toward its attainment through merit rather than its pursuit through intrigue:

- **Seek real, not borrowed greatness**: The greatness that comes from office or title is borrowed and transient; only greatness of soul endures (116-015)
- **Cultivate the graces**: Marlborough's greatness was the product not of genius alone but of "those graces" — the social and moral qualities that made him "a great man in every respect" (056-004)
- **Let affectation flee from greatness**: Genuine merit exposes pretense; the surest way to appear great is to be worthy, not to seem so (039-001)
- **Greatness is service**: Even kings find their greatness "singly dependent" on the prosperity of those they govern (287-001); greatness is measured by what one contributes, not by what one accumulates
- **Avoid the courtier's trap**: "Borrowed greatness pleases the court and the populace, but the former only satisfies the man of sense" (116-015) — flattery and favour may confer the appearance of greatness, but never its substance

## Classical References

Chesterfield's conception of greatness draws on the classical tradition of *magnanimitas* — the greatness of soul that Aristotle describes in the *Nicomachean Ethics* as the crown of the virtues, the disposition of the man who knows himself worthy of great things and is worthy of them. Cicero in *De Officiis* treats *magnitudo animi* (greatness of spirit) as one of the four cardinal virtues, distinguished by its contempt for external goods and its orientation toward honour and service. The Stoic tradition, particularly Seneca's *De Clementia*, develops the idea that true greatness belongs to the ruler who governs for the benefit of the governed — a theme Chesterfield echoes in his reflection on royal greatness (287-001). Marlborough, Chesterfield's exemplar, was himself compared by contemporaries to the great commanders of antiquity, and Chesterfield's portrait of him as "not only a great general, but a great man in every respect" (056-004) consciously echoes Plutarch's parallel lives.
