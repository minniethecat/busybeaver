# Indolence

## Definition

**Indolence**, in Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, is not mere relaxation but a settled disposition toward inaction — a "love of ease" that gradually consumes the energies a young man should direct toward self-improvement. He distinguishes it from the occasional idleness that everyone experiences; indolence is idleness hardened into character, a habitual preference for doing nothing that becomes increasingly difficult to reverse. Chesterfield pairs it repeatedly with negligence, treating the two as companion vices that reinforce each other.

> "Listlessness and indolence are always blameable, but, at your age, they are unpardonable." (018-002)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's warnings about indolence grow more urgent as the correspondence progresses. Early on, he frames it as a matter of age-appropriate conduct: "listlessness and indolence are always blameable, but, at your age, they are unpardonable" (018-002). The young man who is indolent wastes not merely time but the irrecoverable advantage of youth itself.

The middle letters develop the economic metaphor. Indolence, Chesterfield warns, makes a man easy prey: "tradesmen conspire with his own indolence to cheat him" (062-003). The indolent person does not merely fail to advance — he actively invites exploitation. Security and comfort breed indolence: "security, which commonly produces negligence and indolence" (069-001). Prosperity, paradoxically, is indolence's most fertile soil.

The political dimension appears late: indolent populations are vulnerable to tyranny, as rulers may seek "to sink the people into indolence" as a method of control (116-027). And in a striking formulation, Chesterfield warns against those who "from despair, negligence, or indolence, leave to chance the work of their own fortune" (160-001). Indolence is not passive — it is an active surrender of agency.

## Key Passages

> "Listlessness and indolence are always blameable, but, at your age, they are unpardonable." (018-002)

> "Tradesmen conspire with his own indolence to cheat him; and, in a very little time, he is undone." (062-003)

> "Security, which commonly produces negligence and indolence." (069-001)

> "To sink the people into indolence, the other into poverty — consequently, both into slavery." (116-027)

> "From despair, negligence, or indolence, leave to chance the work of their own fortune." (160-001)

## Practical Advice

- Recognize that indolence is most dangerous in youth — it is "always blameable" but "unpardonable" when one is young and has the most to gain from effort (018-002)
- Guard against the indolence that comfort breeds — security and ease are its natural habitat (069-001)
- Understand that indolence invites exploitation — tradesmen, rivals, and circumstances will all take advantage of the indolent man (062-003)
- Treat indolence as a surrender of agency — those who "leave to chance the work of their own fortune" have only themselves to blame (160-001)
