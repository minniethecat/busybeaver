# Delicacy
## Definition
In Chesterfield's understanding, **delicacy** denotes a refined subtlety of thought, expression, and manner that transforms mere correctness into grace. It is the quality that distinguishes the gentleman of true polish from the merely well-instructed: a "peculiar quickness" of perception, a fineness of touch, an elegance of phrase that cannot be reduced to rules. Where decency marks the floor, delicacy marks the ceiling of social accomplishment.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield returns to delicacy as a theme particularly in the later letters, when his son has mastered the rudiments and must now achieve distinction:
1. **Literature and taste (1747–1750)** — The earliest references connect delicacy to literary discernment. Chesterfield praises the "Attic salt" of classical authors and urges his son to cultivate a palate for the finest writers. Delicacy in this period is primarily a matter of intellectual refinement.
2. **Manners as art (1751–1755)** — In the middle period, Chesterfield increasingly applies delicacy to social conduct. He speaks of a "certain turn and delicacy of manners" that must be acquired through observation and practice, not learned from books. The "delicacy of taste in everything" that prevails at court becomes the model.
3. **Expression and style (1757–1765)** — The later letters return insistently to delicacy of expression. Chesterfield laments his son's early lack of "elegance and delicacy of expression" and prescribes reading of the best French authors — particularly Voltaire and the King of Prussia — as models. The goal is that "solidity and delicacy of thought" be united.
## Key Passages
> "At court we find a delicacy of taste in everything; a delicacy of the senses, a delicacy of the mind, a delicacy of expression, and a delicacy of manners, which are nowhere else to be met with, at least in the same degree." (116-016)
> "There was a peculiar quickness and delicacy. That same Attic salt seasoned all that he said." (008-003)
> "You want an elegance and delicacy of expression, necessary to adorn and set off the best and most solid matter." (144-007)
> "Solidity and delicacy of thought must be given us: it is the gift of nature, and not of education." (143-002)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's program for cultivating delicacy:
- Read the finest authors — not for information alone but for style; attend to how they express as much as what they say
- Observe the manner of the best company; delicacy is caught, not taught
- Avoid "brusquerie" and roughness of address — the opposite of delicacy, which Chesterfield calls "indelicacy"
- Let your pleasures be chosen with delicacy and enjoyed with secrecy
- In writing, attend to elegance and orthography as marks of a cultivated mind
- Remember that delicacy of thought is partly a gift of nature, but delicacy of expression can be acquired through study
## Classical References
Chesterfield invokes the "Attic salt" (sal Atticum) — the quality of wit and refinement characteristic of Athenian discourse — as the classical model of delicacy. The concept draws on the rhetorical tradition of *subtilitas* (subtlety of argument and style) as opposed to *copia* (abundance). The Horatian ideal of *curiosa felicitas* (studied spontaneity) also underlies Chesterfield's conception of delicacy as effort that conceals itself.
