# Vanity

## Definition

**Vanity** is, in Chesterfield's moral psychology, the universal human desire for the good opinion of others. He treats it as an ineradicable feature of human nature — "that degree of self-love and vanity from which no man living is free" (090-002). While vanity can lead to ridiculous excesses, it is also, when properly directed, a powerful stimulus to virtuous and praiseworthy action.

> "Such is the natural pride and vanity of our hearts, that it perpetually dictates to us the most improbable conduct." (054-008)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of vanity is remarkably nuanced. He identifies two distinct forms: the ridiculous vanity that leads men into "follies and extravagances" (054-012), and the laudable vanity that drives men to excel. The crucial distinction lies in the object — whether one seeks praise for genuine merit or for empty show.

> "This principle of vanity and pride is so strong in human nature, that it descends even to the lowest objects." (054-011)

He also distinguishes vanity from ambition, noting that while both seek distinction, vanity is concerned with the appearance of worth, whereas ambition pursues actual power and achievement. Yet the two are often intertwined: "Vanity and love are the universal passions" (097-004) that govern human conduct.

## Key Passages

> "Remember to have that constant attention about you which flatters every man's little vanity; and the want of which, by mortifying his pride, never fails to excite his resentment, or at least his ill will." (001-005)

> "Do not let your vanity and self-love make you suppose that people are as attentive to you as you are to yourself." (016-001)

> "The surest way of gratifying your vanity is to conceal it." (030-002)

> "If the love of praise, which is generally a motive of doing well, can be called vanity, it is a happy vanity." (060-002)

> "Vanity and ignorance, as peremptorily as they dare, decide in favor of the former." (064-002)

> "There is that degree of self-love and vanity from which no man living is free." (090-002)

> "Vanity and love are the universal passions of women." (097-004)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel regarding vanity reveals his deep understanding of human nature:

- Flatter others' vanity through constant attention and small acts of consideration (001-005)
- Conceal your own vanity — displaying it defeats its own purpose (030-002)
- Distinguish between the vanity that leads to folly and the love of praise that inspires excellence (060-002)
- Never assume others are as interested in you as you are in yourself (016-001)
- Use your knowledge of vanity in others as a tool of social navigation (054-011)
- The greatest secret: make your vanity serve your genuine improvement rather than mere appearance

## Classical References

Chesterfield's analysis of vanity echoes the Stoic observation that the desire for reputation (*kenodoxia*) is one of the most powerful — and dangerous — human passions. Cicero's discussion of *gloria* (glory) in *De Officiis* similarly distinguishes between the love of true honor and the appetite for empty praise, a distinction Chesterfield adapts to the social world of the eighteenth-century gentleman.
