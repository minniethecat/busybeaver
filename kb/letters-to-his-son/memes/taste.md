# Taste

## Definition

**Taste**, for Chesterfield, is the refined faculty of judgment in matters of art, literature, dress, manners, and pleasure. It is partly innate — "a discerning taste" (005-003) — but largely acquired through careful cultivation and exposure to the best models. Taste is what elevates a gentleman from mere correctness to genuine elegance; it is the difference between dressing finely and dressing with style, between reading widely and reading with discrimination.

> "I know of no book that will form your taste better." (026-005)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield returns to taste across the full span of the correspondence, treating it as a unifying principle that bridges art, fashion, and social conduct. In the early letters, he frames taste as the cure for blindly following fashion — young men should consult their "own taste and inclinations" rather than adopt whatever their companions pursue (007-002). By the middle letters, taste has become a diagnostic category: a man without it is vulgar, whatever his rank; a man with it commands respect, whatever his circumstances.

He is particularly interested in French taste, which he considers the European gold standard. The "Temple of Taste" established at the Hôtel de Rambouillet under Louis XIV represents, in his view, both the potential and the pitfalls of institutionalized refinement — taste must be "judiciously refined," not merely fashionable (125-004). Parisian conversation, he tells his son, "both forms and improves the taste, and whets the judgment" (157-001).

## Key Passages

> "Instead of consulting their own taste and inclinations, they blindly adopt whatever those with whom they chiefly converse are pleased to call by the name of pleasure." (007-002)

> "Pleasures of a gentleman are those which are marked by reason, and a discerning taste." (005-003)

> "Toward the end of Cardinal de Richelieu's reign... the Temple of Taste was established at the Hotel of Rambouillet; but that taste was not judiciously refined." (125-004)

> "Such conversations, that both form and improve the taste, and whet the judgment; are surely preferable to the conversations of our mixed companies here." (157-001)

> "A fine suit ill-made, and slatternly worn, is worse than a plain suit perfectly well made and well put on." (123-002)

## Practical Advice

- Cultivate taste actively: read the best authors not just for knowledge but to "form your taste" and develop a sense of excellent style (009-001)
- In dress, taste consists in "fitness, and manner of wearing your clothes," not in finery or expense — a plain suit well-made defeats a fine suit ill-worn (123-002)
- Seek out conversation with people of taste, particularly in Paris, where such discourse "forms and improves" one's own judgment (157-001)
- Taste must be "judiciously refined" — mere fashionability is not taste, and the most fashionable circle may have the worst judgment (125-004)
- The pleasures of a gentleman are those "marked by reason, and a discerning taste" — taste is what distinguishes cultivation from indulgence (005-003)
