# Gentleman Ideal
## Definition
The **Chesterfieldian gentleman** is the central figure of the *Letters to His Son*—a composite ideal of learning, manners, morality, and worldly wisdom that Chesterfield spent nearly thirty years trying to cultivate in his son. This ideal is not merely a code of conduct but a complete philosophy of life, grounded in classical ethics and refined by 18th-century aristocratic culture.
## In the Letters
Every letter Chesterfield wrote is, in some sense, a lesson in becoming this ideal gentleman. The education he prescribes has four pillars:
1. **Classical Learning**: History, geography, languages, and philosophy—the intellectual foundation
2. **The Graces**: Manners, address, conversation, and the thousand small arts of pleasing
3. **Moral Integrity**: Honor, truthfulness, and the unsullied character proper to a gentleman
4. **Worldly Wisdom**: Knowledge of men and affairs, the ability to read character and navigate ambition
## Components
### The Graces
Chesterfield's most famous—and most controversial—insistence is on the primacy of "the graces." He believed that learning without manners was useless in the world, and that a gentleman's first duty was to please:
> "The proud Lord Chesterfield would have turned in his grave had he known that he was to go down to posterity as a teacher and preacher of the gospel of not grace, but—'the graces, the graces, the graces.'"
### Les Attentions
The small, voluntary acts of consideration that distinguish the well-bred man:
> "The constant practice of what the French call 'les Attentions', is a most necessary ingredient in the art of pleasing; they flatter the self-love of those to whom they are shown; they engage, they captivate, more than things of much greater importance." (092-002)
### Dissimulation
Chesterfield's most controversial teaching: the art of concealing one's true sentiments without falsifying them. He urged his son to keep his own counsel, observe others closely, and never let his feelings betray him.
## Criticism
Samuel Johnson's famous condemnation—that Chesterfield's letters "teach the morals of a whore, and the manners of a dancing master"—has shadowed the Letters since their publication. Critics argue that Chesterfield's gentleman is a hollow figure, all surface and no substance. Defenders reply that Chesterfield never separated manners from morals, insisting that true courtesy must spring from a virtuous character.
## Classical References
- Cicero's ideal orator-statesman is the classical model for Chesterfield's gentleman
- Castiglione's *Book of the Courtier* provides the Renaissance template of sprezzatura (studied nonchalance)
- The Stoic sage—self-sufficient, emotionally controlled, indifferent to fortune
