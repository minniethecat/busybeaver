## Definition

Chesterfield uses "fortune" in two distinct but related senses: (1) the material and social position a person holds — their wealth, rank, and worldly circumstances; and (2) the favorable or unfavorable turn of events — what we would call "luck" or "fortune" in the classical sense of *Fortuna*. The two meanings are connected by Chesterfield's conviction that, while fortune's gifts are unevenly distributed, a young man of sense can substantially *make* his own fortune through conduct, connections, and prudent management.

> "You, who have your fortune to make, should more particularly apply yourself to the art of pleasing."
> (032-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of fortune develops across three phases:

**Early letters (1746–1749)**: Fortune is presented as a practical project. The young son is repeatedly reminded that he has "his fortune to make" — unlike his father, who inherited rank, the son must earn his place through merit and address. This is the most urgent and personal theme in the early correspondence.

**Middle period (1750–1755)**: Fortune becomes a test of character. Chesterfield distinguishes between the man who *has* fortune and the man who *deserves* it. He warns that inherited fortune without merit produces idleness and contempt; earned fortune without grace produces envy and resentment. The ideal is fortune acquired through merit and borne with modesty.

**Late letters (1756–1767)**: Fortune is increasingly discussed in terms of legacy. Chesterfield reflects on his own fortune — both financial and reputational — and urges his son to consolidate what has been built. The emphasis shifts from acquisition to stewardship.

> "Your future fortune, character, and figure in the world, depend upon the next two years."
> (040-003)

## Key Passages

> "I am sorry that, by the accidents of my life, I lost real ones; and my fortune impaired, and my constitution shattered."
> (007-005)

Chesterfield reflects on the personal cost of his own career — a rare moment of vulnerability that reveals fortune's fragility.

> "Every man's reason is, and must be, his guide; and I may as well expect that every man should be of my size and complexion, as that he should reason just as I do, when the same misfortune happens to him."
> (014-001)

Fortune's misfortunes are filtered through individual reason — what one man bears lightly, another finds crushing.

> "I have frequently known a man's fortune decided for ever by his first appearance in the world."
> (042-001)

The decisive importance of first impressions: fortune is not merely accumulated over time but can be made or lost in a moment.

> "The foundation of your future fortune and character; for I never meant to confine you to the dry and unsocial knowledge of books."
> (043-002)

Fortune and character are inseparable in Chesterfield's scheme — one cannot build lasting fortune without a solid foundation of character.

> "By knowing yourself, your character and your fortune will be immovably fixed."
> (057-004)

Self-knowledge as the ultimate guarantor of fortune's stability.

> "Without care and method, the largest fortune will not, and with them, almost the smallest will, supply all the expenses of the heart."
> (062-003)

The paradox of fortune management: method and prudence matter more than the absolute quantity of wealth.

## Practical Advice

1. **If you must make your fortune, make it through merit and address, not through scheming**. Chesterfield insists that the art of pleasing — cultivated manners, genuine knowledge, and social grace — is the most reliable path to fortune for a young man without inherited advantages (032-001).

2. **First impressions are disproportionately decisive for fortune**. A young man's first appearance in society can "decide his fortune forever" (042-001). Preparation before debut is not vanity but prudence.

3. **Fortune without character is a liability**. Wealth that outruns the moral and practical capacity to manage it leads to ruin. The foundation of fortune is self-knowledge and steady conduct (057-004).

4. **Method and economy matter more than the size of fortune**. A small income well managed supports a more dignified life than a large one squandered (062-003).

5. **Misfortune is an individual experience**. What crushes one man barely touches another; Chesterfield counsels his son to develop the stoic resilience that robs misfortune of its sting (014-001).

6. **Be sensible of your good fortune in having mentors**. Gratitude for favorable circumstances — including the guidance of a concerned father — is itself a mark of character (042-003).

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of fortune draws on a rich classical tradition:

- **Fortuna in Roman thought**: The goddess Fortuna, famously fickle, is the backdrop. Chesterfield follows the Roman moralists in arguing that while fortune is beyond human control, the *response* to fortune is entirely within our power.

- **Machiavelli's *virtù* vs. *fortuna***: Chesterfield's insistence that a young man can "make his fortune" through skill and address echoes Machiavelli's concept of *virtù* — the active, shaping power that counters fortune's caprice. The Renaissance prince and the 18th-century gentleman share the conviction that fortune favors the prepared and the bold.

- **Stoic indifference to fortune**: Chesterfield partially adopts the Stoic counsel of indifference to fortune's gifts and blows, but he is too practical to recommend genuine indifference. Instead, he recommends *appearing* indifferent while actively and prudently pursuing fortune's goods. This subtle hypocrisy is characteristically Chesterfieldian.

- **La Rochefoucauld**: The French moralist's observation that "fortune never appears more blind than when she chooses her favorites" lurks behind Chesterfield's insistence that fortune, to be stable, must be grounded in genuine merit rather than mere luck.
