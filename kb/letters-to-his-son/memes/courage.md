# Courage
## Definition
For Chesterfield, **courage** is not merely physical bravery but a compound virtue encompassing moral resolution, intellectual firmness, and the refusal to be discouraged by opposition. He draws a sharp distinction between true courage and its counterfeits — rashness (courage without judgment), bluster (courage without substance), and pride (courage misdirected). Courage in Chesterfield's usage is the steady disposition that both initiates worthy action and sustains it against obstacles.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield addresses courage less frequently than the social virtues, but when he does, he treats it with particular gravity:
1. **Distinguishing courage from rashness (1748–1750)** — In the early moral instruction, Chesterfield warns against the corruption of virtues into vices: "courage into rashness, caution into timidity." The point is that every virtue requires the guidance of judgment to remain virtuous.
2. **Moral courage and self-interest (1751–1754)** — Chesterfield makes the striking argument that acknowledging self-interest requires courage: "I have had the courage to own that I do so." The honest admission of motive is itself a species of fortitude.
3. **Courage and character (1757–1760)** — In the later letters, Chesterfield links courage to the settled character of the gentleman. He contrasts "true courage" with vain pretension and insists that a man "blasted by vices and debauchery" can no more claim courage than a kicked dog.
## Key Passages
> "Economy into avarice, courage into rashness, caution into timidity. … Virtues, carried to excess, degenerate into vices." (030-001)
> "I have had the courage to own that I do so, you cannot but be both pleased and improved by the confidence I place in you." (064-001)
> "True courage is from blustering, or true wit from punning." (078-004)
> "Reflection will always give courage in a good cause; and the courage which arises from reflection is of a superior nature to that which is merely constitutional." (095-001)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's guidance on cultivating courage:
- Distinguish courage from rashness: the former is guided by judgment, the latter by impulse
- Let your courage be founded on reflection — a considered cause is a firm cause
- Do not mistake bluster for bravery; the loudest pretender is often the most fearful
- Cultivate the moral courage to acknowledge your own interests and motives honestly
- Do not be discouraged by first difficulties — perseverance in a good cause is the truest courage
- A man "blasted by vices" forfeits his claim to courage, for courage requires a clean conscience
## Classical References
Chesterfield's treatment of courage draws on the Aristotelian doctrine of the mean, in which courage (*andreia*) is the midpoint between cowardice and rashness. The Stoic concept of *fortitudo* — firmness of mind in adversity — also underlies Chesterfield's emphasis on courage as sustained resolution rather than momentary daring. The classical pairing of courage with prudence (*fortitudo* with *prudentia*) informs Chesterfield's insistence that courage without reflection is mere animal impulse.
