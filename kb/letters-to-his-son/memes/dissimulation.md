# Dissimulation

## Definition

**Dissimulation**, as Chesterfield teaches it, is the art of concealing one's thoughts and feelings when openness would be disadvantageous. He is careful to distinguish it from its darker twin, simulation — the active pretense of sentiments one does not feel — and from outright falsehood. Drawing on classical sources, Chesterfield frames dissimulation as a defensive social skill: the gentleman does not deceive, but he does not lay all his cards upon the table either.

> "Certainly the ablest men that ever were, have all had an openness and frankness of dealing... but then, they were like horses well managed; for they could tell, passing well, when to stop or turn." (100-006)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield returns to dissimulation throughout the correspondence, treating it as an indispensable tool of courtly and diplomatic life. Early references establish the philosophical foundation — he quotes Locke and Bacon to authorize a practice that might otherwise seem morally dubious. In the middle letters, dissimulation becomes a practical strategy for navigating the competitive world of courts and politics. By the late letters, Chesterfield is blunt: the language of simulation and dissimulation is "very hard, but very necessary" (116-028) for anyone who wishes to succeed in public life.

The crucial distinction, which he insists upon repeatedly, is that dissimulation is *passive* concealment, not active deception. One may withhold information or feeling, but must never make professions of friendship or esteem that one does not genuinely hold (179-003).

## Key Passages

> "The ablest men that ever were… could tell, passing well, when to stop or turn; and at such times, when they thought the case indeed required some dissimulation." (100-006)

> "Those two arts of dissimulation and secrecy are like the alloy mingled with pure ore: a little is necessary, and will not debase the coin below its proper standard." (071-001)

> "Here dissimulation is very often necessary, and even simulation sometimes allowable; which, as it pleases them, may be useful to you, and is injurious to nobody." (071-004)

> "Ambition and avarice, the two prevailing passions at courts, found dissimulation more effectual than violence; and dissimulation introduced that exterior civility we call politeness." (079-003)

> "This is not perfidy nor dissimulation; it would be so if you were, at the same time, to make professions of esteem and friendship to this man." (179-003)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers a graduated guide to practicing dissimulation:

- Distinguish dissimulation (passive concealment) from simulation (active pretense); the former is often necessary, the latter rarely so (071-001)
- Never profess sentiments you do not feel — that crosses the line from prudence to perfidy (179-003)
- In courts and public life, learn the "language of simulation and dissimulation" as you would any foreign tongue — difficult, but essential (116-028)
- A little dissimulation, like alloy in coin, preserves value; too much debases character (071-001)
- Hold your tongue when candor would harm you, but never use your tongue to deceive
