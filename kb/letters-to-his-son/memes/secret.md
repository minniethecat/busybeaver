# Secret

## Definition

A **secret**, in Chesterfield's world, is knowledge that confers power — and the management of secrets is therefore among the highest social and political arts. The gentleman must navigate two complementary imperatives: the strict preservation of his own confidences, and the relentless penetration of others'. This is not hypocrisy but prudence; Chesterfield would call it the practical application of his maxim that one should have "a seeming frankness with a real reserve." The man who cannot keep a secret is dangerous to himself and useless to others; the man who cannot discover secrets is blind in a world that runs on hidden knowledge.

> "There is no surer sign of a weak mind than the inability to keep a secret." (050-006, paraphrasing Cardinal de Retz)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of secrecy spans the personal and the political. On the personal side, he advises his son bluntly: "whatever you think your own, keep to yourself; for you may be very sure that you will be the only person the better for it" (017-001). One's private affairs are to remain private — not from paranoia, but because disclosure yields no advantage and may yield real harm.

The political dimension is more elaborate. To succeed as a statesman, "the only way is to get into the secrets, and to know all 'les allures des chemins' of a court" (052-007). This requires patient observation, strategic cultivation of sources, and the ability to inspire trust without necessarily returning it. Chesterfield confesses that he himself was "not unwilling to discover my secrets to you" (064-001) — a rare admission of openness in a writer who otherwise preaches reserve.

The reciprocal skill — penetrating others' secrets — is treated as a function of attention and penetration: one who observes closely "will easily decipher the secrets of your heart" (071-001). The master of this art "knows the hearts, and consequently the secrets, of nine in ten that he converses with" (083-005).

Chesterfield also distinguishes between secrets of state and trivial confidences. Certain secrets "you must keep inviolable, if you would have any character or any success in business" (049-004). The keeping of a trust is not optional; it is the foundation of reputation.

## Key Passages

> "Whatever you think your own, keep to yourself; for you may be very sure that you will be the only person the better for it." (017-001)

> "They never get into the secrets of these courts, for want of being able to be silent." (040-003)

> "These are secrets which you must keep inviolable, if you would have any character or any success in business." (049-004)

> "Cardinal de Retz says, very truly, that a secret is more easily kept by a good many people, than by one or two." (050-006)

> "The only way to be a minister is to get into the secrets, and to know all 'les allures des chemins' of a court." (052-007)

> "I will now, for once, be so indulgent as to tell you, not unwilling to discover my secrets to you." (064-001)

> "A man of great penetration will easily decipher the secrets of your heart, of which you yourself may perhaps be ignorant." (071-001)

> "He knows the hearts, and consequently the secrets, of nine in ten, that he has to do with." (083-005)

## Practical Advice

- Keep your own affairs strictly private — "whatever you think your own, keep to yourself" (017-001)
- Learn the art of silence — those who talk too much "never get into the secrets of courts" (040-003)
- Guard entrusted secrets "inviolable" — your character and success depend on it (049-004)
- Distinguish between the secret and the merely private — the former demands absolute discretion, the latter ordinary prudence (056-003)
- Cultivate penetration to read others — a keen observer "will easily decipher the secrets of your heart" (071-001)
