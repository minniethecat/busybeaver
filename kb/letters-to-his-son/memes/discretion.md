# Discretion
## Definition
Chesterfield treats **discretion** as the sovereign social virtue — the faculty that governs all others in their outward expression. It is the practiced judgment that tells a man what to say and what to leave unsaid, when to speak and when to be silent, whom to trust and whom to watch. Discretion is not mere secrecy (though it includes it) nor mere prudence (though it requires it); it is the comprehensive art of navigating a world in which every word and action has consequences.
## In the Letters
Discretion is a theme from the earliest letters and grows in urgency as Philip Stanhope approaches his diplomatic career:
1. **Early letters (1747–1750)** — Chesterfield introduces discretion as a general principle of conduct: keep within "certain bounds of discretion and decency." The emphasis is on avoiding excess — in speech, in pleasure, in expenditure.
2. **Middle letters (1751–1755)** — As Philip enters society independently, the theme intensifies. Chesterfield warns that "one slip is dangerous" and that "sense and discretion must accompany you at your first setting out." The reputation for discretion, he argues, is self-reinforcing: it "gets him others" — confidence begets confidence.
3. **The diplomatic imperative (1757–1765)** — The later letters introduce the language of statecraft. Chesterfield recommends "the utmost discretion, and the profoundest silence" in political matters. Discretion becomes the defining quality of the diplomat and the statesman.
## Key Passages
> "Sense and discretion must accompany you at your first setting out in the great world, or you are ruined. One slip is dangerous." (078-010)
> "A man has a reputation of discretion, and that reputation gets him others. It is just so with a man's character." (111-004)
> "Let discretion and secrecy be known parts of your character. Be frank without indiscretion, and secret without mysteriousness." (127-004)
> "I recommend to you the utmost discretion, and the profoundest silence. … Consider all that is said to you as a secret, and never repeat it." (128-004)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's rules for discretion:
- Let discretion and secrecy be known parts of your character — the reputation for keeping confidences invites them
- Be frank without indiscretion: openness need not mean imprudence
- Be close without being mysterious: reserve need not invite suspicion
- Never repeat what is told to you in confidence, however trifling it may seem
- In political and diplomatic matters, observe the "profoundest silence" — a word once spoken cannot be recalled
- At table, a man may with decency have a distinguishing palate, but not a voracious one
- Method, diligence, and discretion together will carry a man of good sense further than genius alone
## Classical References
Chesterfield's doctrine of discretion draws on the Ciceronian concept of *prudentia* — practical wisdom in the conduct of affairs — and the Roman ideal of *gravitas* (weightiness of character that commands confidence). The classical distinction between *dicenda* and *tacenda* (things to be said and things to be kept silent) underlies Chesterfield's repeated insistence on knowing what not to say. Tacitus's praise of men who possessed *silentium* (the capacity for strategic silence) provides a further classical model.
