# Complaisance

## Definition

**Complaisance**, in Chesterfield's vocabulary, is the social virtue of pleasing accommodation — the willingness to adapt one's manners, conversation, and even expressed opinions to suit the company one keeps. It is a concept he handles with unusual care, for complaisance occupies the delicate boundary between social grace and moral abdication. His central distinction is categorical: there is "a necessary complaisance; for it relates only to manners and not to morals" (054-019), and there is a "foolish, and often criminal complaisance" that "flows from a foolish cause" (055-006). The difference lies in whether one adapts the form or the substance — the manner or the matter.

> "A man of the world must, like the chameleon, be able to take every different hue; which is by no means a criminal or abject, but a necessary complaisance." (054-019)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield introduces the concept early and refines it throughout the correspondence. By Letter XVII, he frames it as central to "the art of pleasing," which he calls "a very necessary one to possess; but a very difficult one to acquire. It can hardly be reduced to rules" (017-001). Complaisance is not formulaic; it requires the exercise of good sense and observation in real time.

His most extended treatment appears in Letter LIV, where he deploys the chameleon metaphor: "A man of the world must, like the chameleon, be able to take every different hue; which is by no means a criminal or abject, but a necessary complaisance; for it relates only to manners and not to morals" (054-019). The example is conversational: one would not "talk upon the same subject, and in the same manner, to a minister of state, a bishop, a philosopher, a captain, and a woman" (054-019). This is not hypocrisy — it is the elementary courtesy of considering one's audience.

The crucial distinction follows immediately in Letter LV. Chesterfield identifies the dangerous counterfeit: a complaisance that "flows from a foolish cause — the want of spirit, the fear of offending, the inability to refuse" (055-006). This false complaisance produces the "very good-natured fellows" — people who "having no will of their own, readily assent to, concur in, and applaud, whatever is said or done in the company" (055-006). The true gentleman is complaisant without being spineless.

He then draws the bright line: "All other kinds of complaisance are not only blameless, but necessary" (055-007). These include enduring "the idle but innocent affectations of the company" with good nature, adapting to local customs, and showing deference where custom requires.

Chesterfield provides practical applications across social contexts. To women: "A general complaisance and attention to that sex is... advantageous to you" (071-004). To fools: "Some complaisance and attention to fools is prudent, and not ill-bred" (060-004). To foreign customs: "It is a complaisance due to the custom of the place" (082-008). In each case, the governing principle is the same — adapt the manner without compromising the morals.

He also notes that complaisance is best learned in the company of women: "that complaisance and politeness, which are so useful in men's company, can only be acquired in women's" (023-004). The drawing room, not the library, is the school of social grace.

## Practical Advice

- Adapt your conversation to your audience — the subject and manner should vary with the hearer (054-019)
- Be complaisant in manners but firm in morals — the distinction is categorical (054-019)
- Avoid the false complaisance of the "very good-natured fellow" — assent without thought is not virtue but weakness (055-006)
- Cultivate complaisance in the company of women, where it is most naturally learned (023-004)
- Observe local customs and defer to them — it is complaisance "due to the custom of the place" (082-008)
- Extend a prudent complaisance even to fools — it costs nothing and avoids unnecessary offense (060-004)
