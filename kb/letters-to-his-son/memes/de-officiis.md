# De Officiis
## Overview
*De Officiis* (On Duties) is a philosophical treatise by Marcus Tullius Cicero, written in 44 BC and addressed to his son Marcus. It is the foundational text for Lord Chesterfield's ethical system, providing the classical framework within which he understood virtue, honor, and the duties of a gentleman.
## In the Letters
Cicero's work is the silent partner in Chesterfield's educational project. The structure of *De Officiis*—a father writing to an absent son, instructing him in the duties proper to a gentleman—precisely mirrors Chesterfield's own enterprise. Chesterfield does not always name *De Officiis* explicitly, but its principles pervade his letters:
1. **The Honorable (*honestum*)**: That which is morally right in itself—corresponding to Chesterfield's insistence on virtue and honor
2. **The Useful (*utile*)**: That which advances one's interests—corresponding to Chesterfield's worldly pragmatism
3. **The Conflict**: When the honorable and the useful appear to conflict—the central ethical problem Chesterfield navigates throughout the letters
## Key Ideas
### The Four Cardinal Virtues
Cicero's framework of wisdom, justice, courage, and temperance underlies Chesterfield's moral teaching. Chesterfield adapted these for an 18th-century aristocrat:
- **Wisdom**: Knowledge of history, men, and affairs
- **Justice**: Honor and truthfulness in all dealings
- **Courage**: The self-mastery to withstand fortune's blows
- **Temperance**: Moderation in pleasures, control of the passions
### Propriety (*decorum*)
Cicero's concept of *decorum*—the fitness of conduct to person, place, and occasion—is the classical antecedent of Chesterfield's "graces." Both insist that virtue is not enough; it must be displayed with appropriate style.
## Chesterfield's Use
Chesterfield does not merely quote Cicero as ornament. He internalizes Ciceronian ethics and applies them to the practical problems of an 18th-century gentleman's life. When he advises his son to govern his passions, to keep his word, to study the characters of others, he is translating Cicero for a modern audience.
## Significance
The *De Officiis* connection illuminates the deepest structure of Chesterfield's thought. Despite the surface worldliness—the emphasis on manners, the pragmatic advice on advancement—Chesterfield's ethical framework is firmly classical. He is, in his own way, as much a moral philosopher as a man of the world.
