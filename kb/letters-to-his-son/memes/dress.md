# Dress

## Definition

In Chesterfield's system of worldly education, **dress** is far more than mere fashion — it is a primary component of the exterior graces that predispose others in one's favor before a word is spoken. A gentleman's dress signals his respect for the company he keeps, his attention to detail, and his understanding that the world judges first by appearances. Chesterfield elevates dress from a trivial concern to a moral and social duty.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's attention to dress is unremitting, appearing from the earliest letters to the latest. His doctrine has several distinct layers:

1. **Dress as social obligation**: To neglect one's dress is to insult one's company — "a total negligence of dress and air is an impertinent insult upon the company one keeps" (042-002). Dress is not vanity but courtesy
2. **Cleanliness first**: "Take care to be very clean, well dressed, and genteel" (012-002). Cleanliness is the foundation; elegance builds upon it
3. **Adaptation to company**: Dress should suit the occasion and the company — neither overdressed (which suggests vanity) nor underdressed (which suggests contempt)
4. **Avoiding extremes**: Fashion should be followed but not led; a gentleman dresses well without being remarked upon for his dress
5. **Dress as self-respect**: Chesterfield connects dress to the broader cultivation of the self — "a spruceness of dress is also very proper and becoming" (044-003)

Chesterfield includes dress alongside dancing, address, and conversation as one of the essential exterior graces, devoting as much attention to it as to classical education.

## Key Passages

> "Take care to be very clean, well dressed, and genteel; to have no disagreeable attitudes, nor awkward gestures." (012-002)

> "A total negligence of dress and air is an impertinent insult upon the company one keeps, and upon the master or mistress of the house one frequents." (042-002)

> "An awkward address, a slovenly figure, an ungraceful manner of speaking, will prejudice people against the most solid merit." (032-001)

> "A spruceness of dress is also very proper and becoming; and will naturally incline people to think you have a proper value for yourself." (044-003)

> "Custom has made dancing sometimes necessary for a young man; therefore you must learn to dance. Custom has likewise made it decent for a gentleman to dress well; therefore you must dress well." (001-004)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical guidance on dress includes:

- Dress as well as the best-dressed people in your company, but never better — to outshine is to offend
- Prefer simplicity and quality over ostentation; a well-cut plain coat is worth more than a gaudy one
- Cleanliness is non-negotiable: clean linen, clean hands, clean person — every day, without exception
- Adapt your dress to your age and station — what suits a young man does not suit an older one
- Never talk about your dress; let it speak for itself, silently

## Classical References

- **Ovid** — *Ars Amatoria* treats personal adornment as an art of pleasing; Chesterfield secularizes this ancient counsel
- **Castiglione** — *The Book of the Courtier* (though Renaissance, not classical) provides the concept of *sprezzatura* — studied carelessness in dress and manner — that Chesterfield adapts
- The classical tradition of *decorum* (propriety, fitness to circumstance) underlies Chesterfield's entire doctrine of dress
