# Decency
## Definition
In Chesterfield's moral vocabulary, **decency** designates the outward observance of propriety — the visible, minimal standard of conduct that distinguishes the civilized man from the barbarian. It is not synonymous with virtue (a man may be outwardly decent yet inwardly vicious), but Chesterfield treats it as the indispensable floor beneath which no gentleman may sink without forfeiting his claim to good company. Decency governs speech, dress, table manners, and financial conduct, serving as the social translation of inward morality.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield returns to decency across the full span of the correspondence, consistently positioning it as a necessary — though not sufficient — condition of gentlemanly character:
1. **As a social threshold (1746–1749)** — In the early letters, decency is presented as the first lesson of company: "cheerfulness and decency are united" in well-bred gatherings. Chesterfield warns his son against the "indecency and turpitude" of loose companions at school, emphasizing that the company one keeps reflects upon one's own decency.
2. **Financial and moral decency (1750–1754)** — The middle letters broaden the concept to include financial probity. Chesterfield insists that a gentleman "neither borrows nor lends" and that gaming beyond what "decency warrants" is a species of dishonor. The "indecency" of debt becomes a recurring admonition.
3. **Decency as character armor (1757–1765)** — In the later letters, Chesterfield frames "exterior decency and dignity of manners" as a shield: even a man of damaged reputation may retain social standing if he preserves the outward forms. He urges his son to speak of women "with great decency" and to observe "common decency" in all physical habits.
## Key Passages
> "There is a certain dignity of manners absolutely necessary, to make even the most valuable character either respected or respectable. … An exterior decency and dignity of manners will even keep a man longer in the world's good opinion, than perhaps he deserves." (078-009)
> "Keep within certain bounds of discretion and decency. These bounds are the point which the French call *le point*, and which is so easily transgressed that a man had better stop a little short of it than go a very little beyond it." (055-002)
> "Be more than ordinarily attentive to your person, your linen, your hands, your teeth, and your nails. This is but common decency, in order not to offend people." (074-003)
> "Of all things, I beg of you to speak of women with great decency, and to avoid all double entendres." (096-001)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's practical directives on decency:
- Keep your clothing, linen, hands, teeth, and nails immaculately clean — visible neglect offends others and signals inward disorder
- Neither borrow money nor lend it habitually; the former is indecency, the latter folly
- When gaming, stop well short of the point where you could not pay what you lose
- Speak of women with great decency and avoid all double entendres
- In company, join cheerfulness with decency — wit without propriety is buffoonery
- Treat religious subjects with decency, whatever your private opinions
## Classical References
Chesterfield draws upon the classical concept of *decorum* (πρέπον), which Cicero treats in *De Officiis* as the fittingness of action to circumstance, character, and station. The distinction between outward decency and inward virtue echoes the Stoic teaching that external conduct should reflect internal order, even when the inward state is imperfect.
