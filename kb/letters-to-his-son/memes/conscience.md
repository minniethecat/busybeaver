# Conscience

## Definition

**Conscience**, for Chesterfield, is the private tribunal before which every gentleman must ultimately answer. It is not a theological abstraction but a practical instrument of self-governance — the faculty that allows one to "go through the world" with honor "unwounded" (014-001). Chesterfield treats conscience as a companion to reason rather than a substitute for it: a well-formed conscience is the product of reflection and experience, not mere sentiment. Significantly, he acknowledges its limits — conscience can be satisfied "to a reasonable man" (241-003) without demanding perfection — and he recognizes its susceptibility to manipulation by those who would "govern the consciences and the councils" of others (156-006).

> "Go through the world, with either your conscience or your honor unwounded." (014-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield invokes conscience sparingly but with precision, always in contexts where personal judgment must navigate between external rules and internal conviction. The earliest mention (014-001) sets the tone: conscience and honor are paired as twin guardians, suggesting that the social and the moral are not opposed but complementary. A well-lived life wounds neither.

Several passages reveal Chesterfield's awareness that conscience can be co-opted. He observes how Cardinal Alberoni "governed the consciences and the councils of all the princes in Christendom" (156-006), a remark that treats conscience as a lever of political control — something to be influenced, directed, even exploited. This wariness runs through the letters: conscience is a faculty that must remain one's own, not surrendered to a confessor, a director, or a faction.

In his later letters, Chesterfield grows more personal. He acknowledges a debt of conscience — "in conscience I think fifty pounds is too little" (229-003) — and reflects that a man may satisfy "a reasonable" conscience without satisfying every scruple (241-003). The implication is clear: conscience is a guide, not a tyrant. It should be consulted, not obeyed blindly; it should be formed, not merely followed.

## Practical Advice

- Preserve both honor and conscience intact — neither should be sacrificed to worldly advantage (014-001)
- Earn your leisure honestly: a morning of diligent application entitles you to an evening of diversion with a "safe conscience" (020-005)
- Consult your conscience regularly, but do not become its prisoner — a reasonable conscience, not a scrupulous one, is the goal (241-003)
- Be vigilant against those who would govern your conscience — whether directors, confessors, or political operators — for conscience governed by another is no conscience at all (156-006)
- When unsure, invite the honest assessment of a trusted mentor; Chesterfield asks his son "how far, in your conscience, you think it like" (092-002)
