# Wit

## Definition

**Wit**, for Chesterfield, is the capacity for brilliant, incisive verbal expression — a quality he respects but approaches with pronounced ambivalence. He carefully distinguishes **true wit**, which pleases the mind and gives cheerfulness to the countenance, from its vulgar counterfeits: buffoonery, joking, and the "common topics of witlings and coxcombs" (038-003). True wit is an adornment; false wit is a liability. His most striking formulation is that wit makes admirers but not friends — it dazzles but scorches (173-011).

> "True wit, or sense, never yet made anybody laugh; they are above it: They please the mind, and give a cheerfulness to the countenance." (032-001)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield develops his theory of wit across the full span of the correspondence, from the early instructional letters to the mature reflections of his later years.

In the early letters, he draws a sharp line between wit and laughter. "Loud laughter is the mirth of the mob, who are only pleased with silly things; for true wit or good sense never excited a laugh since the creation of the world" (054-021). The man of parts and fashion smiles; he does not laugh. This is not mere fastidiousness — it reflects Chesterfield's conviction that wit belongs to the higher faculties of mind, while laughter is a bodily eruption closer to folly.

He extends the distinction by contrasting wit with its debased relatives. "A joker is near akin to a buffoon; and neither of them is the least related to wit" (078-003). In the same passage, he draws a parallel: "This dignity of manners... is not only as different from pride, as true courage is from blustering, or true wit from joking" (078-004). Wit, properly understood, belongs to the same family as courage and dignity — it is a quality of substance, not surface.

The most personally revealing passage appears in a late letter, where Chesterfield turns the analysis on himself: "That ready wit; which you so partially allow me, and so justly Sir Charles Williams, may create many admirers; but, take my word for it, it makes few friends. It shines and dazzles like the noon-day sun, but, like that too, is very apt to scorch; and therefore is always feared" (173-011). This is the voice of experience — a man who knows his gift and has paid its price. He follows with a prescription: "Never seek for wit; if it presents itself, well and good; but, even in that case, let your judgment interpose; and take care that it be not at the expense of anybody" (173-011).

Chesterfield also warns against false claimants to wit — the "witlings" who traffic in commonplace observations: "They are the common topics of witlings and coxcombs; those, who really have wit, have the utmost contempt for them" (038-003). A century before Wilde, Chesterfield understood that the truly witty despise the merely clever.

By the later letters (1760s), Chesterfield broadens his survey: "true wit, sound taste, and good sense, are now, as it were, engrossed by France and England" (105-023) — a rare concession to national pride, linking wit to a broader cultural achievement.

## Practical Advice

- Let wit arise naturally; never force or seek it — "if it presents itself, well and good" (173-011)
- Always subordinate wit to judgment — unchecked wit scorches and creates enemies
- Distinguish true wit (which pleases the mind) from mere joking (which excites laughter)
- A gentleman smiles, but is never heard to laugh — the man of wit controls his expression (054-021)
- Avoid the "common topics of witlings" — reheated cleverness is worse than none at all (038-003)
