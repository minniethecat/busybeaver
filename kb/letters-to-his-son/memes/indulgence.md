## Definition

Chesterfield uses "indulgence" in two distinct senses. In its social sense, indulgence is a form of complaisance — a generous tolerance of the weaknesses and foibles of others, essential to smooth social and political relations. In its parental sense, indulgence is a dangerous vice: the blindness that leads parents to overlook or excuse their children's faults, thereby enabling their ruin. The same word thus names both a social virtue and a moral hazard, depending entirely on context and object.

## In the Letters

The social sense of indulgence appears early and consistently. Chesterfield counsels his son that one cannot move through the world "without a complaisant indulgence for people's weaknesses" (017-006). This is not mere politeness but a form of strategic tolerance — the recognition that everyone has flaws, and pointing them out serves no purpose.

The parental sense carries a darker weight. Chesterfield reflects on his own experience of being indulged as a young man and on the danger of "mistaken indulgence" (092-001) by those who love too blindly. He acknowledges that his own affection for his son makes him vulnerable to this error, and he struggles to balance warmth with honest critique. By the later letters, indulgence takes on a political dimension: the Administration's "indulgence and forbearance" toward rebellious factions becomes a question of statecraft (283-002).

## Key Passages

> "One cannot move through the world without a complaisant indulgence for people's weaknesses, and a blindness to their faults." (017-006)

> "The audience, from a natural partiality to their own abilities, and from a just allowance of the difficulties of the undertaking, show great indulgence to a new actor." (061-001)

> "Parents are generally apt to spoil those they love by a mistaken indulgence, by a blindness, nay, often by a weakness towards their faults." (092-001)

> "I believe a Stoic would blame my indulgence; but I am yet no Stoic, though I am grown a good deal more severe than I was." (107-002)

> "More indulgence too, in my mind, should be shown to those faults which proceed from the head than to those which proceed from the heart." (157-004)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's practical guidance on indulgence is contextual. In society: **indulge others' weaknesses generously** — you will gain more by overlooking faults than by correcting them. In parenting: **resist the temptation of indulgence** — the kindest thing you can do for a child is to see their faults clearly and name them honestly. In politics: **indulgence has limits** — forbearance toward factions can be wisdom or weakness depending on whether it preserves or endangers the state.

## Classical References

Chesterfield's reference to the Stoics (107-002) is telling: he knows the Stoic position would condemn his indulgence outright, but he permits himself this human failing. This tension between philosophical severity and humane flexibility runs through the letters. The distinction between faults of the head and faults of the heart (157-004) echoes classical and Enlightenment debates about moral responsibility and the relationship between intellect and character.
