## Definition

Conformity, in Chesterfield's vocabulary, is not the surrender of principle but the strategic adaptation of manners — the art of making oneself agreeable by fitting one's behavior to the customs, tone, and expectations of whatever company one enters. He defines it precisely: "a conformity and flexibility of manners is necessary in the course of the world; that is, a versatility of manners which can adapt itself to all kind of people and all kind of companies" (015-001). This is not hypocrisy or servility; it is the social intelligence that recognizes that different contexts require different registers of behavior. Chesterfield contrasts conformity with the stiffness of the man who "always preserves his own character and manners, in all places and in all companies" — such a man, he warns, will find himself unwelcome everywhere except among people exactly like himself (187-001).

## In the Letters

Conformity appears across the full span of the correspondence as one of Chesterfield's most consistently urged social skills. In the early letters, he presents it as essential to travel: one must adopt "an easy and genteel conformity" to the manners of whatever country one visits, "or rather the assuming of them, for the time one is there" (020-005). The distinction is important — conformity is temporary and strategic, not a permanent alteration of character. In the middle period, he applies the principle to domestic life: "a conformity of expense to that of the company one keeps" is necessary to avoid the twin errors of ostentation and meanness (062-003), and "a strict conformity to the hours and rules of the place" is the first duty of a guest (066-004). He traces even his own dress choices to "conformity to custom" — demonstrating that the principle applies to the most mundane details (151-007). The later letters develop the psychological dimension: "a cheerful and easy conformity to people's particular manners and particular foibles" is the secret of being welcome everywhere (187-001). His summary is definitive: "use ourselves to a ready conformity to whatever is neither criminal nor dishonorable" (188-002).

## Related Concepts

Conformity is the practical expression of [[versatility]] — the capacity to shift registers that Chesterfield ranks among the highest social attainments (015-001, 166-006). It is closely allied to [[complaisance]] and [[good-breeding]]: breeding provides the form, conformity the flexibility. [[Manners]] are the specific rules to which one must conform; conformity is the meta-skill of adapting those manners to context. It stands opposed to [[stiffness]], [[singularity]], and the rigidity of the man who "always preserves his own character and manners" regardless of company (187-001). [[Custom]] is the external standard to which conformity responds — "conformity to custom" governs dress, expense, and hours (151-007). The [[gentleman-ideal]] requires conformity without servility: adaptability without loss of principle.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's specific recommendations for cultivating conformity:

- **Adapt manners to company**: "A conformity and flexibility of manners is necessary in the course of the world" — the man who cannot adapt his register to his audience will be welcome only among those exactly like himself (015-001, 187-001)
- **Conform in externals, not in principles**: Conformity extends to manners, dress, expense, and hours — "whatever is neither criminal nor dishonorable" (188-002) — but never to moral or intellectual principle
- **Assume, don't affect**: In foreign travel, adopt local manners "for the time one is there, without any affectation of preferring your own" (020-005) — conformity is temporary and strategic, not a permanent surrender of identity
- **Conform in expense**: Match your spending to "that of the company one keeps" (062-003), avoiding the extremes that provoke either envy or contempt
- **Practice cheerful conformity**: The key is not merely to conform but to do so "cheerfully and easily" — visible reluctance defeats the purpose (187-001)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's concept of conformity echoes the Aristotelian idea of *to prepon* (the appropriate, the fitting) — the virtue of saying and doing what suits the occasion, the audience, and the speaker's own character. Cicero in *De Officiis* develops the concept of *decorum*: the art of adapting one's conduct to circumstances while maintaining consistency of character. The tension Chesterfield navigates — between adaptability and integrity — is the central problem of Cicero's discussion: how to be versatile without being false. The French tradition of *honnêteté* that Chesterfield admired — particularly in the writings of the Chevalier de Méré — made "conformity to the company" one of the cardinal rules of the *honnête homme*. Chesterfield's insistence that conformity extends only to what is "neither criminal nor dishonorable" (188-002) is the Stoic boundary condition: the wise man adapts his manner but never his virtue.
