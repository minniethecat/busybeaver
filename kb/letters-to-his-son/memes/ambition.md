# Ambition

## Definition

**Ambition** is, for Chesterfield, the passionate desire to excel and to distinguish oneself in the world. He presents it as an inherently neutral force — capable of producing either magnificent achievement or moral ruin depending on the direction it takes. The key distinction is between *right ambition*, which drives a man to deserve genuine praise through real merit, and *unbounded ambition*, which sacrifices everything to the mere pursuit of power.

> "This is a right and generous ambition; and without which, I fear, few men can be great, and none good." (060-003)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's treatment of ambition evolves across the correspondence. In the early letters, he encourages his son's ambition as the necessary spur to application and study. In the middle period, he warns against its excesses, observing that "ambition and avarice, the two prevailing passions" (079-003) have corrupted even the most capable men. In the later letters, he reflects on the fate of those whose ambition outstripped their judgment, such as the princes and statesmen who brought ruin upon themselves through "unbounded ambition and impetuous passions" (090-008).

> "The passion of ambition, for instance, is the same in every country; but good-breeding, as it is called, which is the mode of showing that passion, differs in every country." (015-001)

He also warns against the dangerous combination of ambition with avarice, noting that these two passions act as "clogs upon each other" (097-003) — the ambitious man spends freely to advance himself, while the avaricious man hoards.

## Key Passages

> "A constant attention and ambition to excel in it, with a careful observation of the best models." (061-001)

> "An ambition of pleasing and shining in the best company." (070-001)

> "Unbounded ambition and impetuous passions, but a good heart." (090-008)

> "Interrupted but by a stronger ambition. The former impaired both his fortune and his character." (095-005)

> "I will suppose ambition to be (as it commonly is) the ruling passion." (097-002)

> "I am sure you have too much right ambition to prefer low and disgraceful pleasures." (122-007)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's counsel on ambition is characteristically pragmatic:

- Direct your ambition toward genuine excellence rather than mere advancement (060-003)
- Cultivate a "constant attention and ambition to excel" in your chosen field (061-001)
- Let your ambition be the desire "of pleasing and shining in the best company" (070-001)
- Beware of combining ambition with avarice — they are incompatible masters (097-003)
- Regulate ambition with morality, lest it become "unbounded" and destructive (090-008)
- Prefer right ambition to base pleasures — the former ennobles, the latter degrades (122-007)

## Classical References

Chesterfield's understanding of ambition is deeply rooted in classical thought. The distinction between *ambitio* (praiseworthy striving for honor) and *ambitio mala* (corrupt lust for power) comes from Roman moral philosophy, particularly Cicero's *De Officiis*. The warning against "unbounded ambition" echoes the Stoic critique of passions that overwhelm reason, while the praise of "right ambition" reflects the aristocratic ideal of *virtus* — excellence displayed in public service.
