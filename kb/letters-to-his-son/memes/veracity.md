# Veracity

## Definition

**Veracity**, for Chesterfield, is the habit of truth-telling that establishes a man's reputation for reliability. His treatment of the concept is characteristically dual: veracity is both a moral obligation and a practical instrument. A man known for veracity possesses a currency — his word — that others will accept without question. A man known for falsehood, by contrast, forfeits the power to persuade, because nothing he says will be believed, even when it happens to be true.

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's most sustained discussion of veracity appears in **Letter C** (1750), a remarkable passage that treats truth-telling as a matter of reputation engineering:

> "It is most certain, that the reputation of veracity is a very great one, and that a man who has once forfeited it is not to be believed, even when he speaks the truth." (100-007)

The argument is pragmatic, not principled: tell the truth not because lying is wrong, but because being *known* as a liar destroys one's effectiveness. Chesterfield traces this insight to an anecdote about a "famous liar of antiquity" who, having told the truth once, found that nobody believed him — a reference to the classical fable of the boy who cried wolf.

> "I remember a very remarkable instance of this kind, in a gentleman who was famous for lying." (100-006)

The gentleman in question, Chesterfield recounts, had so thoroughly ruined his own credibility that even his true statements were met with disbelief. The lesson is clear: veracity is like a reputation credit account — once overdrawn, it is nearly impossible to restore.

In **Letter CXVI** (1752), Chesterfield adds a physiological dimension to veracity:

> "A person habituated to veracity has an air of boldness and freedom, which is very becoming and agreeable." (116-010)

Truth-telling, in this view, is not only morally and strategically sound but aesthetically pleasing. The truthful man *looks* better — his countenance, his bearing, his freedom from the anxiety of being caught in a lie — all contribute to a more agreeable presence.

## Key Passages

> "Endeavor to establish such a character of veracity, that people may conceive an opinion of your veracity, of which opinion you may afterwards make your advantage." (100-005)

This is perhaps the most nakedly instrumental statement in all of Chesterfield's letters about truth-telling. Veracity is not an end in itself but a *character* — a reputation — that one "may afterwards make advantage of." The honesty is in the candor with which he reveals his own calculus.

> "A person habituated to veracity has an air of boldness and freedom, which is very becoming and agreeable; whereas a person accustomed to lying has always a certain air of constraint, suspicion, and fear." (116-010)

The contrast between *freedom* and *constraint* is philosophically significant: Chesterfield presents the liar as paradoxically unfree — trapped by his own fabrications — while the truthful man moves through the world with the liberty of having nothing to hide.

## Related Concepts

- [[honor]] — Veracity is one of the pillars of honor; a man without veracity cannot be a man of honor.
- [[reputation]] — The broader concept of which veracity is a component; reputation is the accumulation of observed veracity over time.
- [[sincerity]] — A gentler, more social form of veracity; sincerity is truthfulness in matters of sentiment and praise.
- [[lying]] — The direct opposite; Chesterfield treats lying as not merely wrong but foolish — it impoverishes the liar.
- [[discretion]] — The virtue that limits veracity; truthfulness does not require revealing everything one knows.

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield's guidance on veracity is remarkably nuanced for a virtue so often treated in absolute terms:

1. **Never lie outright**: A direct falsehood, once discovered, destroys credibility permanently. The cost of a single detected lie far exceeds any momentary advantage.

2. **Veracity does not require full disclosure**: One may be truthful without being indiscreet. Silence, evasion, and redirection are all compatible with veracity.

3. **Cultivate the appearance of veracity**: The manner of the truthful man — bold, free, unconstrained — can be cultivated even before the habit is fully formed. Manner and substance reinforce each other.

4. **Guard against even small lies**: Chesterfield warns that minor falsehoods, told for convenience or politeness, accumulate and erode the reputation for veracity just as surely as large ones.

## Classical References

Chesterfield's instrumental approach to veracity reflects the influence of Cicero's *De Officiis*, which treats truthfulness (*veritas*) as a foundation of justice but also discusses its role in establishing *fides* — the trustworthiness that makes civil society possible. The anecdote of the famous liar echoes Aesop's fable of the shepherd boy and the wolf, a story Chesterfield would have known from his classical education.

The Stoic tradition also informs Chesterfield's emphasis on the *freedom* of the truthful man. For the Stoics, the sage is free precisely because he has nothing to hide — his life is transparent, his actions consistent with his principles. Chesterfield secularizes this ideal: the truthful man enjoys social freedom, not spiritual liberation, but the mechanism is the same.
