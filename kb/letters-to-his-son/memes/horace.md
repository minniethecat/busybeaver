# Horace
Horace is, alongside [[cicero|Cicero]], the classical author most frequently invoked in the [[letters-to-his-son]]. Chesterfield's references to Horace span the full range of the Roman poet's œuvre — the Odes, the Satires, the Epistles, and the Ars Poetica — and serve purposes at once literary, moral, and social.
## The Poet and His World
Quintus Horatius Flaccus (65–8 BCE) was the leading lyric poet of Augustan Rome. A former republican soldier who became a client of Maecenas and a friend of the Emperor Augustus, Horace occupied a position at the center of Rome's literary and political world — a position that gave his poetry its characteristic blend of independence and accommodation, wit and wisdom.
Chesterfield clearly recognized in Horace a kindred spirit: a man of cultivated sensibility who moved easily between literary refinement and worldly engagement, and who expressed his observations on human nature with a concision and urbanity that Chesterfield considered the peak of gentlemanly style.
## In the Letters
### As Literary Model
Chesterfield recommends Horace as a standard of literary taste from the very first letters. He urges his son to read Horace not merely for the Latin but for the quality of mind the poetry reveals:
> "When you read Horace, attend to the justness of his thoughts, the happiness of his expressions, and the beauty of his numbers." [009-001]
This triple emphasis — thought, expression, and form — is characteristic of Chesterfield's approach to all literary models. Horace is not a museum piece but a living exemplar of how a civilized mind expresses itself.
### As Moral Guide
Horace's ethical content — particularly in the Epistles and Satires — provides Chesterfield with a classical vocabulary for discussing conduct:
> "Remember, that what Horace says of good writing is justly applicable to those who would succeed in the world." [038-006]
Chesterfield repeatedly adapts Horatian maxims to his own pedagogical purposes, drawing on the poet's authority to reinforce lessons about moderation, self-knowledge, and social adaptability.
### As Social Capital
In a revealing passage, Chesterfield tells his son that a well-timed Horatian quotation distinguishes a gentleman from a pedant:
> "A common edition of Horace, of which he tore off gradually pages as he read them." [021-002]
The anecdote — about a man who literally consumed Horace page by page — is both an endorsement of Horace's wisdom and a warning against the wrong way to relate to classical texts. The gentleman carries Horace in his mind, not his hands.

## Horace and the Gentleman-Ideal
Horace's particular value in Chesterfield's [[education|educational program]] lies in his combination of qualities that the 18th-century gentleman was meant to embody: learning without pedantry, wit without malice, wisdom without solemnity, and style without affectation. Horace is, in effect, the classical ancestor of Chesterfield's own ideal — and his presence throughout the letters is a reminder that the educational program they describe is not a modern invention but the latest expression of a tradition reaching back to Augustan Rome.
