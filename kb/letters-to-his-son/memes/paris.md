# Paris
## In the Letters
For Chesterfield, Paris was not merely a city but the supreme school of manners. He believed that no young man's education was complete without a sojourn in Parisian society, where the art of pleasing had been raised to its highest refinement. Philip Stanhope spent considerable time in Paris during his diplomatic career, and his father's letters from this period are filled with advice on how to navigate French society.
Chesterfield's attitude toward Paris reflects the anglophile's mixture of admiration and reserve: he respected French polish while remaining firmly English in his political loyalties. He urged his son to learn everything the French could teach about social grace, while never forgetting that he was an Englishman.
## Diplomatic and Social Life
Paris was home to the British embassy where Philip served, and to Lord Albemarle, the British ambassador whose patronage Chesterfield cultivated for his son's advancement. The city's salons, theatres, and court provided the arena in which a young diplomat could display—or fail to display—the graces his father had spent a lifetime inculcating.
## The French Model
Chesterfield frequently held up French manners as the model his son should emulate:
- **Les attentions**: The small, voluntary acts of consideration that "flatter the self-love of those to whom they are shown"
- **Les graces**: The ease and elegance of manner that distinguished the French aristocracy
- **Conversation**: The art of adapting one's tone to the company, a skill Chesterfield believed the French had perfected
## Key Passages
