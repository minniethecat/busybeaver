# Lord Chesterfield
## Biography
Philip Dormer Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield (22 September 1694 – 24 March 1773), was a British statesman, diplomat, and man of letters. He served as Lord-Lieutenant of Ireland from 1745 to 1746 and was one of His Majesty's Secretaries of State. A protégé of the Whig grandees, he combined political ambition with a classical education and a reputation as one of the finest wits of his age.
Chesterfield was born into the aristocracy and educated at Trinity Hall, Cambridge. He entered the House of Commons in 1715 and succeeded to the earldom in 1726, taking his seat in the House of Lords. His diplomatic career included an embassy to The Hague (1728) and the Lord-Lieutenancy of Ireland—a post he held during the Jacobite rising of 1745. In his later years, deafness and gout forced his withdrawal from public life.
His enduring legacy, however, rests not on his political achievements but on the letters he wrote almost daily to his illegitimate son, Philip Stanhope (1732–1768), over a period of nearly thirty years. These letters, never intended for publication, were published by his son's widow in 1774 and have remained in print ever since.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's letters to his son reveal a man of immense learning, worldly wisdom, and relentless ambition for his child's success. Writing from London, Bath, and his country estates, he instructed "Dear Boy" in everything from classical history to the art of conversation. His letters blend parental affection with a systematic philosophy of self-improvement.
The letters were written in English, French, and occasionally Latin, reflecting Chesterfield's fluency in multiple languages. They cover the years 1746 to 1768, the final letter being written shortly before Philip Stanhope's death at age thirty-six.
Chesterfield often referred to himself in the third person in editorial annotations within the letters, and his correspondents included his wife Lady Chesterfield, his son's tutor Mr. Harte, and various European acquaintances.
## Key Attributes
Chesterfield's character, as revealed in the letters, is marked by several dominant traits:
- **Wit and urbanity**: He was celebrated as the finest conversationalist of his age. Samuel Johnson grudgingly acknowledged his charm while condemning his morals, famously declaring that Chesterfield's letters "teach the morals of a whore, and the manners of a dancing master."
- **Pragmatic worldliness**: He believed that "the graces, the graces, the graces" mattered more than mere learning in social advancement.
- **Classical erudition**: His letters are studded with quotations from Cicero, Horace, and other classical authors, reflecting his Cambridge education.
- **Paternal devotion**: Despite his illegitimate son's failure to meet his exacting standards, Chesterfield's devotion never wavered. After Philip's death, he provided for his son's widow and two grandsons.
