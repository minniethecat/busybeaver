# Eloquence

## Definition

**Eloquence**, in Chesterfield's lexicon, is the power to persuade through language — in speech and in writing. He distinguishes sharply between what he calls "holiday eloquence" — the formal, set-piece oratory displayed once or twice a parliamentary session — and the "every-day, ready, and habitual eloquence" that a gentleman must command at all times (200-002). The latter is not about theatrical performance; it is about being clear, graceful, and persuasive in every conversation and every letter.

> "I would not give a farthing for a mere holiday eloquence, displayed once or twice in a session, in a set declamation, but I want an every-day, ready, and habitual eloquence." (200-002)

## In the Letters

Chesterfield's advocacy for eloquence grows more urgent as his son approaches the age for a parliamentary career. In the early letters, it is one accomplishment among many; by the 1750s, it becomes a fixation. He holds up Lord Bolingbroke as the supreme model — a man whose "manner of speaking in private conversation is full as elegant as his writings" and who "adorns with the most splendid eloquence" whatever subject he touches (095-004).

The English constitution, Chesterfield argues, makes eloquence "more useful, and more necessary, here than in any other country in Europe" (093-002), because Parliament is the arena where careers are made. He urges his son to study Cicero's rhetorical treatises, to practice declaiming scenes from tragedies, and to read parliamentary debates aloud to develop cadence and fluency.

## Key Passages

> "His address pre-engages, his eloquence persuades, and his knowledge informs." (090-008) — on Lord Bolingbroke

> "The nature of our constitution makes eloquence more useful, and more necessary, here than in any other country in Europe." (093-002)

> "Lord Bolingbroke has both a tongue and a pen to persuade... whatever subject he either speaks or writes upon, he adorns with the most splendid eloquence." (095-004)

> "Cicero, conscious that he was at the top of his profession (for in his time eloquence was a profession), in order to set himself off, defines an orator to be such a man as never was." (094-004)

> "If to his knowledge he joins eloquence and elocution, he must necessarily trample down all opposition." (128-013)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers a comprehensive program for cultivating eloquence:

- Aim for habitual, everyday eloquence, not occasional rhetorical fireworks — it is a constant tool, not a holiday ornament (200-002)
- Study the best models: Bolingbroke for English prose, Cicero for rhetorical theory, French parliamentary speeches for clarity and structure (095-004, 094-004)
- Practice reading aloud: recite scenes from tragedies, declaim passages of oratory, and pay attention to cadence, pause, and emphasis (117-002)
- Eloquence without knowledge is empty noise; knowledge without eloquence is buried treasure — the two must be cultivated together (128-013)
- In conversation, let your eloquence be natural and unforced; studied eloquence is worse than none
