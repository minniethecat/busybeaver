# Reputation

## Definition

For Chesterfield, **reputation** is the mirror in which the world sees a man's character. It is not identical to character — a man may be better or worse than his reputation — but it is the currency in which social and professional advancement is transacted. Chesterfield treats reputation as a tangible asset, something that can be "acquired," "established," "destroyed," and "lost." He distinguishes between a reputation for ability (which can be rebuilt) and a reputation for character (which, once damaged, is nearly impossible to restore).

> "Of what use a good reputation is, and how swift and advantageous a currency it gives a man." (060-003)

## In the Letters

Reputation becomes a major theme in the middle and later letters, as Chesterfield's son approaches the age when he must enter public and professional life. Chesterfield chronicles how reputation is built — through "parts," "learning," and consistent conduct (053-008) — and how it is destroyed, often in a single moment of indiscretion or poor company.

He is particularly attentive to the gender asymmetry of reputation, noting that "the reputation of chastity is not so necessary for a man as for a woman" (100-007), but that a man's reputation for honor and integrity is every bit as fragile and valuable. The letter writer also provides cautionary examples, such as the man who "destroyed both his fortune and his reputation" (095-005).

## Key Passages

> "Those people have acquired their reputation by their parts, their learning, and their good conduct." (053-008)

> "Of what use a good reputation is, and how swift and advantageous a currency it gives a man." (060-003)

> "You establish or destroy every man's reputation of good-breeding; you must, therefore, be very attentive and careful." (090-002)

> "Be neither jealous nor envious of your reputation or fortune, which I must be both of, in your behalf." (092-001)

> "It is most certain, that the reputation of chastity is not so necessary for a man as for a woman." (100-007)

> "Your character as a man of morality, truth, and honor — you must have the reputation of them also." (113-001)

> "In either case he destroys the reputation he wants to get. Some flatterers, for instance, make it their business to destroy a man's reputation." (113-002)

## Practical Advice

Chesterfield offers specific guidance for managing reputation:

- Recognize that reputation serves as "currency" — it accelerates every transaction and opens doors that would otherwise remain closed (060-003)
- Reputation for good-breeding can be established or destroyed by small things; attention to detail in social conduct is essential (090-002)
- Do not merely possess virtue, truth, and honor — you must have the "reputation of them also" (113-001)
- Avoid flatterers, who "make it their business to destroy a man's reputation" (113-002)
- Guard against jealousy of others' reputation, which is corrosive and unworthy (092-001)
