# Time Management
## Definition
In Chesterfield's educational system, **time management** is the art of employing every hour to its proper purpose. He regards the wise use of time as the single most reliable indicator of a person's character and future success, observing that few people truly employ their whole time.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield addresses the use of time throughout the correspondence:
1. **Early discipline (1746–1748)** — Emphasis on filling the day with study, dividing time between classical learning and modern languages
2. **The gentleman's schedule (1749–1755)** — Time divided between studies and pleasures, but always with method; idleness is the enemy
3. **Career years (1756–1767)** — The pressure of public life makes time management essential; the man who wastes time is lost
## Key Passages
> "Though I have very little time, and though I write by this post to the secretary of state, yet I cannot omit writing to you." (004-001)
> "I hope you employ your whole time, which few people do; and that you put every moment to the account of some useful or agreeable end." (004-002)
> "I would pass some of my time in reading, and the rest in the company of people of sense and knowledge." (007-008)
> "You have but little time to lose; for you are now at that critical period of life when you must either make or mar your fortune for the remainder of your days." (009-001)
> "Instead of sitting idle and yawning, as most young travelers do, divide your day between studies and pleasures." (018-002)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's method for managing time:
- Account for every hour — treat time as a finite resource that must be audited (004-002)
- Divide the day systematically between intellectual work (morning) and social cultivation (afternoon/evening)
- Avoid the idleness that afflicts most young people, particularly travelers (018-002)
- Recognize that youth is the critical period when habits are formed and fortunes are made or lost (009-001)
- Even leisure should be active — reading, improving conversation, exercise — not passive idleness
## Classical References
- Seneca's *De Brevitate Vitae* (On the Shortness of Life) — the classical source for the idea that life is long enough if well employed
- Chesterfield echoes the Stoic maxim that we complain of the shortness of time while wasting most of it
- The Horatian principle of *carpe diem* underlies Chesterfield's urgency about youthful application
