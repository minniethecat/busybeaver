# Happiness
## Definition
In Lord Chesterfield's philosophy, **happiness** is not a matter of chance or external fortune but the product of rational self-direction. He defines its solid foundation as virtue and knowledge, contrasting it with the illusory happiness sought through riches, power, rank, or sensual pleasure.
## In the Letters
Chesterfield's treatment of happiness evolves across the correspondence (1746–1767):
1. **Early letters (1746–1750)** — Happiness is tied to the cultivation of the mind through study, languages, and history
2. **Middle letters (1750–1760)** — Emphasis shifts to the social dimension: the happiness derived from pleasing others and succeeding in the world
3. **Late letters (1760–1767)** — A more reflective, almost stoic tone: happiness depends on inner resources rather than external circumstances
## Key Passages
> "I am truly your friend, who sincerely wishes your happiness, and desires to promote your pleasure." (026-003)
> "It is certain that we seek our own happiness in everything we do; and it is as certain that we can only find it in doing well, and in the consciousness of doing so." (049-002)
> "Virtue and knowledge are the only solid foundation of all happiness; for riches, power, rank, or whatever else the world calls good, are often the instruments of misery." (099-002)
> "A man who has not the resources of his own mind is the most dependent of all creatures; draw back those external advantages, and he is nothing." (177-005)
> "The chances are greatly against happiness." (243-004)
## Practical Advice
Chesterfield's advice on securing happiness:
- Cultivate the resources of your own mind through study and reflection — these cannot be taken from you (099-002, 177-005)
- Seek happiness through the consciousness of doing well, not through external acquisitions (049-002)
- Be useful to others and please in company — a source of steady satisfaction (026-003)
- Do not rely on fortune or external circumstances, which are fickle and may be withdrawn at any moment (177-005)
- Recognize that pleasure pursued without virtue leads to pain and shame
## Classical References
Chesterfield's conception of happiness draws on Stoic philosophy:
- The Stoic principle that virtue alone suffices for happiness (*eudaimonia*)
- Cicero's *De Officiis*, which Chesterfield repeatedly recommends, argues that moral worth (*honestum*) is the only true good
- The Horatian ideal of the self-sufficient man who carries his happiness within
