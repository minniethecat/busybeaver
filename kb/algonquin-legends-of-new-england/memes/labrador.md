# Labrador

**Labrador** is the vast subarctic peninsula of northeastern North America, bounded by the Labrador Sea to the east, Hudson Bay to the west, and the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the south. In the context of *Algonquin Legends of New England*, Labrador represents the northern frontier of the Wabanaki world — a region where Algonquian oral traditions met and intermingled with Eskimo (Inuit) mythology. Charles Godfrey Leland identifies Labrador as the geographic link connecting Wabanaki stories to the broader circumpolar narrative tradition stretching as far as Greenland.

## Overview

Leland situates Labrador within the geographic range of the Algonquian peoples whose legends he collected:

> "This people ranged from Labrador..." (002-094).

This positioning is significant: it establishes the Wabanaki oral tradition not as an isolated phenomenon but as part of a vast northern narrative network. Labrador appears in Leland's analysis as a cultural bridge — a region where Algonquian and Eskimo peoples interacted, and where their respective oral traditions influenced each other.

Leland explicitly draws this connection:

> "intercourse between Greenland and Labrador" (003-080).

This claim, while perhaps overstated in terms of direct cultural contact, reflects Leland's genuine insight that the mythological systems of the northern peoples — from Greenland through Labrador to the Maritimes — shared structural features that suggested either common origin or sustained cultural exchange.

## Geography

The Labrador Peninsula encompasses approximately 1.4 million square kilometers of boreal forest, tundra, and rugged coastline. Its southern shore, along the Gulf of St. Lawrence, was the traditional territory of the Innu (Montagnais-Naskapi) people, an Algonquian-speaking group whose territory abutted that of the Mi'kmaq to the south.

Leland notes that Labrador was "a country often traveled by" the Wabanaki (037-019), suggesting that the peninsula was not a remote or unknown territory but a familiar part of the Wabanaki geographic imagination. The travel routes between the Maritimes and Labrador — following the coastline or traversing the interior via river systems — facilitated the exchange of both goods and stories.

## Role in Legends

### The Eskimo Connection

Labrador's most significant role in the legends is as the zone of contact between Wabanaki and Eskimo traditions. Leland observes that certain mythological motifs are:

> "common to Greenland and Labrador" (035-094).

This observation is part of Leland's broader argument for cultural diffusion across the North. He notes that the Eskimo of Greenland and Labrador shared stories with the Wabanaki:

> "Rink found in Greenland and Labrador that the Eskimo, as I have shown, have many legends in common with the Algonquins" (045-089).

The Eskimo (Inuit) of Labrador thus serve as cultural intermediaries in Leland's framework — carriers of narrative traditions that span the entire northern hemisphere.

### The Northern Limit of Wabanaki Territory

Labrador also functions in the legends as a marker of geographic extent. When Leland describes the Wabanaki as ranging "from Labrador to the Kennebec" (003-105), he is defining the northern and southern boundaries of the Algonquian cultural world he documents. The reference to:

> "the Eskimo to the Wabanaki, of Labrador, New Brunswick, and Maine" (003-105)

maps a continuous cultural geography from the subarctic through the Maritimes to northern New England.

## Historical Context

At the time of Leland's writing in the 1880s, Labrador was sparsely populated by European settlers and remained largely the domain of its Indigenous inhabitants — the Innu in the interior and the Inuit along the coast. The region's remoteness meant that it preserved traditional lifeways and oral traditions longer than the more heavily colonized Maritimes to the south.

Leland's references to Labrador reflect the 19th-century European fascination with the Arctic and subarctic as zones of cultural "purity" — places where Indigenous traditions survived in forms closer to their pre-contact origins. While this framing carries obvious colonial assumptions, it also motivated Leland to document the connections between Labrador traditions and those of the Wabanaki, producing a record of cultural geography that remains valuable.

The reference to Hans Rink, the Danish geologist who collected Greenlandic and Labrador Inuit traditions, situates Leland's work within a broader 19th-century scholarly network interested in comparing Indigenous oral traditions across the North. This comparative approach was innovative for its time and anticipates later developments in folklore studies.
