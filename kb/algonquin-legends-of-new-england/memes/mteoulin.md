# M'teoulin

## Overview

The M'teoulin (also rendered as *m'teoulin* or *m'teoulinism*) is the Wabanaki term for the magician, shaman, or spiritual practitioner of the Northeastern Algonquin peoples. The M'teoulin tradition represents the indigenous spiritual technology of the Micmac, Passamaquoddy, and Penobscot tribes, and embodies a sophisticated animistic worldview in which every created thing — animate or inanimate — was understood to possess an indwelling spirit.

## Description

The M'teoulin distinctly taught that whatever had an *idea* had a soul. This doctrine extended personhood and spiritual agency to the entire created order: animals, plants, rocks, rivers, and celestial phenomena were all understood to be inhabited by conscious spirits. Leland observed that this teaching made the Wabanaki mythology "strangely like that of the Rosicrucians," suggesting parallels between indigenous Algonquin animism and European esoteric philosophy (002-137, 002-139).

The magician's power derived from direct relationship with these indwelling spirits. Unlike the hierarchical priesthoods of European religion, the M'teoulin operated through personal alliance and communication with specific spirit beings. This shamanic tradition preceded the later introduction of Christianity among the Wabanaki and represented what Leland termed "the black sorcery which preceded Shamanism" in the region (002-133).

## Key Concepts

### Animistic Doctrine

The core teaching of the M'teoulin held that the world was densely populated by conscious, volitional spirits. Every rock, river, and ancient hill in New England had "a fairy, an elf, a naiad, or a hero" associated with it (002-150). This worldview did not distinguish sharply between natural and supernatural, between material and spiritual — the two domains were understood to interpenetrate continuously.

### Spirit Hierarchy

Wabanaki mythology as mediated through the M'teoulin tradition included multiple orders of spirit beings: strange giants — literal Jotuns of stone and ice, sorcerers who could become giants at will, the terrible Chenoo with an icy-stone heart, witches and demons, and the minor mythology of dwarfs and fairies (002-130, 002-133). This hierarchy of beings constituted both a cosmology and a practical map of spiritual dangers and allies.

### The Indian Puck

Among the most distinctive figures in the M'teoulin tradition was Mikumwess, the Indian Puck or Robin Good-Fellow. A frontispiece illustration in Leland's original 1884 edition, taken from a scraping on birch bark by Tomah Josephs, Indian Governor at Peter Dana's Point, Maine, depicts Mikumwess wearing a red cap "like the Norse Goblin" — one of many details that suggested to Leland the possibility of historical contact between Norse and Algonquin mythological traditions (frontispiece).

## Cultural Significance

The M'teoulin tradition is significant for several reasons. First, it represents one of the best-documented examples of indigenous North American animistic philosophy, recorded directly from practicing informants in the late nineteenth century. Second, the parallels Leland identified between M'teoulin doctrine and both Rosicrucian philosophy and Norse mythology raise intriguing questions about cultural transmission across the North Atlantic — questions that continue to be debated by scholars of comparative religion and ethnology.

The M'teoulin worldview also provides essential context for understanding the narrative content of Wabanaki legends. The constant presence of spirit beings, transformations, and magical operations in these stories is not merely decorative but reflects a coherent metaphysical system in which such events were understood as real possibilities within the fabric of the world.
