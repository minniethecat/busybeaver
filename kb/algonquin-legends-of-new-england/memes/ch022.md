---[022-001] Now he had learned from the boys that they were of a _Cullo_

[022-002] family; and the Culloos are certain monstrous birds, exceeding fierce.

[022-003] But Master Lox, having seen in the cabin plenty of fine meat, desired

[022-004] greatly to become one of the family, and having been much about in life

[022-005] knew something of the ways of every one. So putting on the Culloo

[022-006] style, he, seeing a babe, began to sing with the most natural air in

[022-007] the world a Culloo nursery-song:--

[022-008] "Agoo ge abeol,

[022-009] Wetkusanabeol."

[022-010] [Footnote: Micmac.]

[022-011] A seal-skin strap,

[022-012] A shoulder-strap.

[022-013] Now it costs very little to fall into the humor of a man; but this the

[022-014] woman would not do, and told him plainly that he could not deceive her.

[022-015] On hearing which Master Lox, in a great rage, seized his tomahawk and

[022-016] slew her. Then seeing a kettle boiling on the fire, he cut off her head

[022-017] and put it into the pot, hiding the body. And this was a merry jest

[022-018] after his own heart, so that it greatly solaced him. But after a time,

[022-019] the two boys, returning, missed their mother, and looking into the

[022-020] kettle, found her head. Then they knew well who had done this. And,

[022-021] being fearless, they pursued him, but having no bows they could do him

[022-022] no harm; however, they took from him his gloves, and with these they

[022-023] returned.

[022-024] And anon there came also an uncle of the boys, or _Kah-kah-goos_

[022-025] (P.), the Crow. So he gave chase to Lox, yet all that he could do was

[022-026] to snatch away his cap as he ran. Yet without shame he cried aloud,

[022-027] "Well, my head was getting warm, and now I am cooler. Thank you!"

[022-028] [Illustration: LOX CARRIED OFF BY CULLOO]

[022-029] Then came another relative, _Kitpoo_, the Eagle (M.). And he,

[022-030] pursuing Lox, took from him his coat. Yet all unabashed he replied,

[022-031] "Thanks unto you also; for I was just wishing that my younger brother

[022-032] were here to carry my coat for me." But he who now arrived, hearing of

[022-033] the deadly deed, was the great Culloo himself, the most terrible of all

[022-034] created creatures, and he, pursuing Lox, caught him up, and carrying

[022-035] him in his claws, even to the summit of the sky itself, let him drop,

[022-036] and he was a whole day in falling; even from the first dawn unto sunset

[022-037] he went down ere he touched the earth. But before he was let drop, and

[022-038] when on high, he burst into a mocking song on what he saw, and the

[022-039] words were as follows:--

[022-040] "Kumut kenovek,

[022-041] Telap tumun ek,

[022-042] Stugach' kesenagasikel,

[022-043] Yog wa egen'

[022-044] Yog wa egeno

[022-045] Telap tumen ek

[022-046] Kumut ken ooik'

[022-047] Stuga 'mkudomoos koon."

[022-048] Our country all lost

[022-049] Seems clearly to us

[022-050] As though it were all spread with boughs.

[022-051] Heigh ho, hay hum!

[022-052] Heigh ho, hay hum!

[022-053] Our country now lost

[022-054] Seems now unto us

[022-055] To be blue like the clear blue sky.

[022-056] Hum, hum--tol de rol!

[022-057] And when let fall, this graceless jackanapes in nowise ceased his

[022-058] ribaldry; for while pretending to flap with his arms as if they were

[022-059] wings, he imitated with his mouth, mockingly, the _wish! wish!_ of

[022-060] the wide wings of the Culloo. Yet ere he touched the earth he uttered

[022-061] one little magic spell, "Oh, spare my poor backbone!" And with that all

[022-062] the trouble of all the birds went for nothing. Truly he was mashed to a

[022-063] batter, and his blood and brains flew in every direction, like

[022-064] raspberry pudding; but among the remains his backbone lay whole, and

[022-065] this was his life.

[022-066] And in a few days after his younger brother came by, who, seeing the

[022-067] dire mess, exclaimed, "Hey, what is all this?" [Footnote: The dead body

[022-068] of a sorcerer must lie until addressed by some human being. Then it

[022-069] revives. This is suggestive of vampirism, which is well known to the

[022-070] Indians. There is something strangely ghastly in the idea of the Voice

[022-071] calling separately to each dead limb to come to it. The Culloo is an

[022-072] emblem of the cloud, and Lox let fall from one probably signified fire,

[022-073] or the lightning.] Whereupon a Voice came from the bone, crying,

[022-074] "_Nuloogoon, ba ho_!" "Ho, my leg, come hither!" and a leg came

[022-075] unto the spine. Then the Voice cried," _N'petunagum, ba ho_!" "Ho, my

[022-076] arm, come hither!" And when the last fragment had come he arose, the

[022-077] same indomitable Lox as ever, even the Indian Devil, or Wolverine, who

[022-078] never says Die, and whom nothing can kill, and who is hard to put away.

[022-079] Now the two brothers went on till they came to the top of a high

[022-080] mountain, where there lay a very great round rock, or a mighty boulder.

[022-081] And being full of fun, they turned it over with great sticks, saying to

[022-082] it, "Now let us run a race!" Then it rolled downhill till it stopped at

[022-083] the foot, they rushing along by it all the time. And when it rested

[022-084] they jeered it, and bade it race with them again, when it so listed.

[022-085] And truly they had not long to wait, for soon after, as they sat

[022-086] cooking their food, they heard a mighty commotion as of something

[022-087] coming with dreadful speed through the forest. And lo! it was the stone

[022-088] in dire wrath, which, having rested a little while, came rushing

[022-089] through the forest, crashing the mighty trees like grass, with a roar

[022-090] like thunder, leaving a smooth road behind it in the roughest

[022-091] wilderness. Up and after the sorcerers flew the stone, and the younger

[022-092] slipped aside like a snake, but the elder had scarcely time to utter

[022-093] his magic charm, "_Noo-goon ooskudeskuch_!" "Let my backbone

[022-094] remain uninjured!" ere the awful rock rolled down upon him, crushing

[022-095] his bones and mashing his flesh. Yet the spine was unhurt; it remained

[022-096] sound as ever.

[022-097] And the stone went on and ever on, till the sound of its roar died away

[022-098] in the breeze and afar in the wilderness.

[022-099] Then the younger brother turned to the Backbone and said, "_Cagooee

[022-100] wejismook' tumun_?" (M.) "Why are you lying there?" And hearing this

[022-101] charm the Bone called aloud, "_Ntenin ba ho_!" "My body, ho!" and

[022-102] "_Nuloogoon ba ho_!" "My leg, ho!" and so with the rest of the

[022-103] members as before, until he that was decomposed was now recomposed;

[022-104] yes, and composed perfectly. And then he that was dead, but was now

[022-105] alive, arose, and said as one awaking, "What have I been doing?" So his

[022-106] brother told him all.

[022-107] Then he was greatly angered, and when the Wolverine is angry it is not

[022-108] a little. And he said in his wrath, "Shall I that am the devil of the

[022-109] woods himself be slain by birds and stones, and not be revenged?" So

[022-110] they went onwards through the woods till they found the Great Rock:

[022-111] they followed in the path of the broken trees; even by the trees did

[022-112] they track it. Which having found, they built a fire around it; with

[022-113] great stones for hammers they broke it, and ever more and still

[022-114] smaller, till it was all mere dust, for their souls were sore for

[022-115] revenge.

[022-116] When lo, a great wonder! For the Spirit of the Old Rock, even that

[022-117] which was itself, turned all the dust to black flies, into the stinging

[022-118] and evil things which drive men and beasts mad, so that its hatred and

[022-119] spite might be carried out on all living creatures unto the end of

[022-120] time.

[022-121] And having had their ill-will of the Rock and seen it become Flies, the

[022-122] two went through the forest, and so on till they came to a village of

[022-123] good, honest folk; and knowing what manner of men they were, Lox

[022-124] resolved to forthwith play them an evil trick, for in all life there

[022-125] was nothing half so dear to him as to make mischief, the worse the

[022-126] better.

[022-127] And this time it came into his head that it would be a fine piece of

[022-128] wit to go into the town as a gay girl and get married, and see what

[022-129] would come of it, trusting to luck to fashion a sad fool out of

[022-130] somebody. So having made himself into a delicate young beauty, richly

[022-131] attired, he entered the place; and truly the town was soon agog over

[022-132] the new guests. And the young chief of the tribe, wanting her, won her

[022-133] without waste of time. Truly there lieth herein some mystery. I know

[022-134] not what, only this I know: that there are in all towns certain folk

[022-135] who, by means of magic or meddling, always find out everything about

[022-136] everybody, and then tittle-tattle thereof. Now, albeit Lox had utterly

[022-137] abjured all the sinfulness of manhood, and had made a new departure in

[022-138] an utterly new direction, saying not a word thereof to any one, yet in

[022-139] a brief measure of time, one here, another there, Jack in a corner and

[022-140] Jane by the bush, began to whisper of a strange thing, and hint that

[022-141] all was not as it should be, and, whatever the chief might think, that

[022-142] in their minds matters were going wrong in his wigwam.

[022-143] Now Lox, knowing all this thread as soon as it was spun, began to think

[022-144] it high time to show his hand in the game. And what was the amazement

[022-145] of all the town to hear, one fine evening, that the chief's wife would

[022-146] soon be a mother. And when the time came Dame Lox informed her husband

[022-147] that, according to the custom of her people, she must be left utterly

[022-148] alone till he was a father and the babe born. And when in due time the

[022-149] cry of a small child was heard in the lodge the women waiting ran in,

[022-150] and received from the mother the little one, abundantly rolled in many

[022-151] wrappers, which they took to the chief. But what was his amazement,

[022-152] when having unrolled the package, he found under one skin after

[022-153] another, tied up hard, yet another sewed up, and yet again, as the

[022-154] inmost kernel of this nut, the little withered, wizened, dead, and

[022-155] dried shrivelment of an unborn moose calf. Which pleased the chief so

[022-156] much that, dashing Master Moose into the fire, he seized his tomahawk

[022-157] and ran to his lodge to make his first morning call on the mother.

[022-158] But Master Lox was now a man again, and expecting this call, and not

[022-159] wishing to see visitors, had with his brother fled to the woods, and

[022-160] that rapidly. And in the rush he came to a river, and, seeing a very

[022-161] high waterfall, thought of a rare device whereby he might elude

[022-162] pursuit. For he with his brother soon built a dam across the top with

[022-163] trees and earth, so that but little water went below. And lying in a

[022-164] cave, concealed with care, he imitated the _boo-oo-oo_ of a

[022-165] falling stream with quaint and wondrous skill. And there he lay, and no

[022-166] man wist thereof.

[022-167] But verily the wicked one is caught in his own snare, and even so it

[022-168] befell Master Lox. For as he hid, the water above, having gathered to a

[022-169] great lake, burst the dam, so that it all came down upon him at once

[022-170] and drowned him; nor was there any great weeping for him that ever I

[022-171] heard of. So here he passes out of this story, and does not come into

[022-172] it again. But whether he went for good and all out of this life is

[022-173] doubtful, since I find him living again in so many rare, strange

[022-174] histories that it has become a proverb that Lox never dies.

[022-175] Now the tale returns to the two little Weasels, or Ermines, or

[022-176] Water-Maids, poor souls, who had such a hard life! And it happened

[022-177] that, fleeing from Master Lox, they came at evening to a deserted

[022-178] village, and entered a wigwam to pass the night. But the elder, being

[022-179] the wiser, and somewhat of a witch in the bud, mistrusted the place,

[022-180] deeming it not so empty as it seemed. And beholding by the door, lying

[022-181] on the ground, the Neckbone of a man or some other animal, she warned

[022-182] her sister that she should in nowise offend it or treat it lightly, to

[022-183] which the younger replied by giving it a kick which sent it flying, and

[022-184] by otherwise treating it with scorn and disdain.
