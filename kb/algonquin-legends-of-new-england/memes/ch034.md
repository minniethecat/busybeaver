---[034-001] Another time an Indian village was visited by a Kewahqu', but he was

[034-002] driven away by magic. The people marked _crosses_ on the trees

[034-003] where they expected the Kewahqu' to come. There was a great excitement

[034-004] among the Indians, expecting to hear their strange visitor with his

[034-005] frightful noises. It was the old people who gave the advice to mark

[034-006] crosses on the trees.

[034-007] Another time an Indian of either the Passamaquoddy or Mareschite tribe

[034-008] was turned to a Kewahqu'. The last time he was seen was by a party of

[034-009] Indian hunters, who recognized him. He had only small strips of

[034-010] clothing. "This country,"' he said," is too warm for me. I am going to

[034-011] a colder one."

[034-012] This story from the Passamaquoddy Anglo-Indian, manuscript of Mitchell

[034-013] supplies some very important deficiencies in the preceding Micmac

[034-014] version. We are told that the _heart_ of the Chenoo is of ice in

[034-015] human figure. This human figure is that of the Kewahqu' himself, or

[034-016] rather his very self, or microcosm. It is this, and not the liver,

[034-017] which is swallowed by the victor, who thus adds another frozen "soul"

[034-018] to his own. Of the three vomited by the Kewahqu', two were the hearts

[034-019] of enemies whom he had conquered. He could not give up his own,

[034-020] however. It is much more according to common sense that the woman

[034-021] should have given the cannibal the magic medicine which made him yield

[034-022] his heart than that he should voluntarily have purged himself. In the

[034-023] Micmac tale he merely relieves his stomach; in the Passamaquoddy

[034-024] version he, by woman's influence, loses his icy _heart_. It is

[034-025] interesting to observe that the use of the Christian cross is in the

[034-026] additional anecdote described as _magic_.

[034-027] It is the main point in the Chenoo stories that this horrible being,

[034-028] this most devilish of devils, is at first human; perhaps an unusually

[034-029] good girl, or youth. From having the heart once chilled, she or he goes

[034-030] on in cruelty, until at last the sufferer eats the heart of another

[034-031] Chenoo, especially a female's. Then utter wickedness ensues. It is more

[034-032] than probable that this leads us back to some dark and terrible Shaman

[034-033] superstition, older than we can now fathom. There is a passage in the

[034-034] Edda which its translator, Thorpe, thinks can never be explained. "I

[034-035] believe," he writes, "the difficulty is beyond help." The lines are as

[034-036] follows:--

[034-037] "Loki scorched up [Footnote: The _Edda_, p. 112.]

[034-038] In his heart's affections,

[034-039] Had found a half-burnt

[034-040] Woman's heart.

[034-041] Loki became guileful

[034-042] from that wicked woman:

[034-043] thence in the world

[034-044] are all giantesses come."

[034-045] Of which Thorpe writes, "The sense of this and the following line is

[034-046] not apparent. They stand thus in the original: _Loki of hiarta lyrdi

[034-047] brendu, fann hann halfsvidthin hugstein konu_, for which Grimm

[034-048] (Myth. Vorrede 37) would read _Loki at hiarta lundi brenda_, etc.,

[034-049] _Lokius comedit cor in nemore assum, invenit semiustum mentis lapidem

[034-050] mulieris_." Whatever obscurity exists here, it is evident that it

[034-051] means that Loki, having become bad, grew worse after having got the

[034-052] half-burnt stone of a woman's soul. That is, his own heart, half

[034-053] ruined, became utterly so after he had added to it the demoralized

[034-054] _hugstein_, soul-stone, thought-stone, or _heart_ of a woman.

[034-055] If we assume that stone and heart are the same, the difficulty

[034-056] vanishes. And they are one in the Chenoo, who, like Loki, illustrates

[034-057] or symbolizes the passage from good to evil, which a German writer

[034-058] declares is quicker than thought, or that very same _Ilugi_ which

[034-059] the Norse myth puts forwards as swiftest of all runners. Loki, not as

[034-060] yet lost, gets the stone heart of a giantess, and becomes an utter

[034-061] devil at once. The Chenoo becomes an utter devil when he has swallowed

[034-062] the _thought-stone_ of a giantess, and so does Loki.

[034-063] _The Girl-Chenoo._

[034-064] (Micmac.)

[034-065] Of the old time. Far up the Saguenay River a branch turns off to the

[034-066] north, running back into the land of ice and snow. Ten families went up

[034-067] this stream one autumn in their canoes, to be gone all winter on a

[034-068] hunt. Among them was a beautiful girl, twenty years of age. A young man

[034-069] in the band wished her to become his wife, but she flatly refused him.

[034-070] Perhaps she did it in such a way as to wound his pride; certainly she

[034-071] roused all that was savage in him, and he gave up all his mind to

[034-072] revenge. He was skilled in medicine, or in magic, so he went into the

[034-073] woods and gathered an herb which makes people insensible. Then stealing

[034-074] into the lodge when all were asleep, he held it to the girl's face,

[034-075] until she had inhaled the odor and could not be easily awakened. Going

[034-076] out he made a ball of snow, and returning placed it in the hollow of

[034-077] her neck, in front, just below the throat. Then he retired without

[034-078] being discovered. So she could not awake, while the chill went to her

[034-079] heart. [Footnote: The Eskimo Shamans and the Indian _boo-oin_ are

[034-080] familiar with many very ingenious and singular ways of producing

[034-081] prolonged illness and death. There is one known to a very few old

[034-082] gypsies, of gradually inducing insanity and death, which I have never

[034-083] seen noted in any work on toxicology. In a work which I lately read, it

[034-084] was positively denied that there was any such thing as a "lingering

[034-085] poison"!]

[034-086] When she awoke she was chilly, shivering, and sick. She refused to eat.

[034-087] This lasted long, and her parents became alarmed. They inquired what

[034-088] ailed her. She was ill-tempered; she said that nothing was the matter.

[034-089] One day, having been sent to the spring for water, she remained absent

[034-090] so long that her mother went to seek her. Approaching unseen, she

[034-091] observed her greedily eating snow. And asking her what it meant, the

[034-092] daughter explained that she felt within a burning sensation, which the

[034-093] snow relieved. More than that, she craved the snow; the taste of it was

[034-094] pleasant to her.

[034-095] After a few days she began to grow fierce, as though she wished to kill

[034-096] some one. At last she begged her parents to kill her. Hitherto she had

[034-097] loved them very much. Now she told them that unless they killed her she

[034-098] would certainly be their death. Her whole nature was being changed.

[034-099] "How can we kill you?" her mother asked.

[034-100] "You must shoot at me," she replied, "with seven arrows. [Footnote: The

[034-101] Micmac version gives _guns_. But the Chenoo stories are evidently

[034-102] very ancient, and refer to terrors of the olden time.] And if you can

[034-103] kill me with seven shots, all will be well. But if you cannot, I shall

[034-104] kill you."

[034-105] Seven men shot at her, as she sat in the wigwam. She was not bound.

[034-106] Every arrow struck her in the breast, but she sat firm and unmoved.

[034-107] Forty-nine times they pierced her; from time to time she looked up with

[034-108] an encouraging smile. When the last arrow struck she fell dead.

[034-109] Then they burned the body, as she had directed. It was soon reduced to

[034-110] ashes, with the exception of the heart, which was of the hardest ice.

[034-111] This required much time to melt and break. At last all was over.

[034-112] She had been brought under the power of an evil spirit; she was rapidly

[034-113] being changed into a Chenoo a wild, fierce, unconquerable being. But

[034-114] she knew it all the while, and it was against her will. So she begged

[034-115] that she might be killed.

[034-116] The Indians left the place; since that day none have ever returned to

[034-117] it. They feared lest some small part of the body might have remained

[034-118] unconsumed, and that from it another Chenoo would rise, capable of

[034-119] killing all whom she met. [Footnote: Mr. Rand (manuscript) gives a

[034-120] detailed account of an Indian who went mad during the winter, ran away

[034-121] naked into the wilderness among the snows, and was unanimously declared

[034-122] to have turned into a Chenoo. I agree with Mr. Rand that "the

[034-123] historical basis of these tales, if they have any, may be the same,--a

[034-124] case of lunacy; fiction and figure adding the incredible details."]

[034-125] THUNDER STORIES

[034-126] _Of the Girl who married Mount Katahdin, and how all the Indians

[034-127] brought about their own Ruin._

[034-128] (Penobscot.)

[034-129] Of the old time. There was once an Indian girl gathering blueberries on

[034-130] Mount Katahdin. And, being lonely, she said, "I would that I had a

[034-131] husband!" And seeing the great mountain in all its glory rising on

[034-132] high, with the red sunlight on the top, she added, "I wish Katahdin

[034-133] were a man, and would marry me!"

[034-134] All this she was heard to say ere she went onward and up the mountain,

[034-135] but for three years she was never seen again. Then she reappeared,

[034-136] bearing a babe, a beautiful child, but his little eyebrows were of

[034-137] stone. For the Spirit of the Mountain had taken her to himself; and

[034-138] when she greatly desired to return to her own people, he told her to go

[034-139] in peace, but forbade her to tell any man who had married her.

[034-140] Now the boy had strange gifts, and the wise men said that he was born

[034-141] to become a mighty magician. For when he did but point his finger at a

[034-142] moose, or anything which ran, it would drop dead; and when in a canoe,

[034-143] if he pointed at the flocks of wild ducks or swans, then the water was

[034-144] at once covered with the floating game, and they gathered them in as

[034-145] they listed, and through that boy his mother and every one had food and

[034-146] to spare.

[034-147] Now this was the truth, and it was great wonder, that Katahdin had

[034-148] wedded this girl, thinking with himself and his wife to bring up a

[034-149] child who should build up his nation, and make of the Wabanaki a mighty

[034-150] race. And he said, "Declare unto these people that they are not to

[034-151] inquire of thee who is the father of thy child; truly they will all

[034-152] know it by seeing him, for they shall not grieve thee with

[034-153] impertinence." Now the woman had made it known that she would not be

[034-154] questioned, and she gave them all what they needed; yet, for all this,

[034-155] they could not refrain nor restrain themselves from talking to her on

[034-156] what they well knew she would fain be silent. And one day when they had

[034-157] angered her, she thought, "Truly Katahdin was right; these people are

[034-158] in nowise worthy of my son, neither shall he serve them; he shall not

[034-159] lead them to victory; they are not of those who make a great nation."

[034-160] And being still further teased and tormented, she spake and said, "Ye

[034-161] fools, who by your own folly will kill yourselves; ye mud-wasps, who

[034-162] sting the fingers which would pick ye out of the water, why will ye

[034-163] ever trouble me to tell you what you well know? Can you not see who was

[034-164] the father of my boy? Behold his eyebrows; do ye not know Katahdin by

[034-165] them? But it shall be to your exceeding great sorrow that ever ye

[034-166] inquired. From this day ye may feed yourselves and find your own

[034-167] venison, for this child shall do so no more for you."

[034-168] And she arose and went her way into the woods and up the mountain, and

[034-169] was seen on earth no more. And since that day the Indians, who should

[034-170] have been great, have become a little people. Truly it would have been

[034-171] wise and well for those of early times if they could have held their

[034-172] tongues.

[034-173] This remarkable legend was related to me by Mrs. Marie Sakis, a

[034-174] Penobscot, a very clever story-teller. It gives the Fall of Man from a

[034-175] purely Indian standpoint. Nothing is so contemptible in Indian eyes as

[034-176] a want of dignity and idle, loquacious teasing; therefore it is made in

[034-177] the myth the sin which destroyed their race. The tendency of the lower

[034-178] class of Americans, especially in New England, to raise and emphasize

[034-179] the voice, to speak continually in italics and small and large

[034-180] capitals, with a wide display, and the constant disposition to chaff

[034-181] and tease, have contributed more than any other cause to destroy

[034-182] confidence and respect for them among the Indians.
