---[009-001] So they plotted to kill Turtle, and his nephew, who was about to leave,

[009-002] told him how it would be. "First of all, they will build a mighty fire

[009-003] and throw you in it. But do thou, O uncle, go cheerfully, for by my

[009-004] power thou wilt in nowise suffer. Then they will speak of drowning, but

[009-005] thou must beg and pray that this may not be; and then they will the

[009-006] more seek to do so, and thou shalt fight them to the bitter end, and

[009-007] yet it shall be."

[009-008] And as he said, so it came to pass; and Mikchich, being of good cheer,

[009-009] bade farewell to his nephew. [Footnote: This is amusingly, though not

[009-010] very clearly, set forth in the Indian manuscript as follows: "Make

[009-011] believe but you dond want be trown. So he shaken hands witt is nuncel

[009-012] kick hororch good by do him. Tell is uncle you--I shall not be kill and

[009-013] I am going Lever (to live)--we may meet again."] And they seized him

[009-014] and threw him into a great fire, but he turned over and went to sleep

[009-015] in it, being very lazy; and when the fire had burnt out he awoke, and

[009-016] called for more wood, because it was a cold night.

[009-017] Then they seized him yet again, and spoke of drowning. But, hearing

[009-018] this, he, as if he were in mortal dread, begged them not to do this

[009-019] thing. And he said they might cut him to pieces, or burn him, as they

[009-020] would, but not to throw him into the water. [Footnote: This in the

[009-021] original is extremely like Brer Rabbit's prayer not to be thrown into

[009-022] the brier-bush. As this legend is one of the oldest of the Algonquin,

[009-023] and certainly antedating the coming of the whites, I give it the

[009-024] priority over the negro.] Therefore they resolved to do so, and dragged

[009-025] him on. Then he screamed horribly and fought lustily, and tore up trees

[009-026] and roots and rocks like a madman; but they took him into a canoe and

[009-027] paddled out into the middle of the lake (or to the sea), and, throwing

[009-028] him in, watched him sink as he vanished far down below. So they thought

[009-029] him dead, and returned rejoicing.

[009-030] Now the next day at noon there was a hot sunshine, and something was

[009-031] seen basking on a great rock, about a mile out in the lake. So two

[009-032] young men took a canoe and went forth to see what this might be. And

[009-033] when they came to the edge of the rock, which was about a foot high,

[009-034] there lay Mikchich sunning himself; but seeing them coming to take him,

[009-035] he only said, "Good-by," and rolled over plump into the water, where he

[009-036] is living to this day. In memory whereof all turtles, when they see any

[009-037] one coming, tip-tilt themselves over into the water at once.

[009-038] And Turtle lived happily with his wife, and she had a babe. New it

[009-039] happened in after-days that Glooskap came to see his uncle, and the

[009-040] child cried. "Dost thou know what he says?" exclaimed the Master.

[009-041] "Truly, not I," answered Mikchich, "unless it be the language of the

[009-042] Mu-se-gisk (P., Spirits of the Air), which no man knoweth." "Well," replied

[009-043] Glooskap, "he is talking of eggs, for he says '_Hoo-wah! hoo-wah_!'

[009-044] which methinks is much the same as '_Waw-wun, waw-wun_.' And

[009-045] this in Passamaquoddy means egg." "But where are there any?" asked

[009-046] Mikchich. Then Glooskap bade him seek in the sand, and he found many,

[009-047] and admired and marveled over them greatly; and in memory of this, and

[009-048] to glorify this jest of Glooskap, the Turtle layeth eggs even to this

[009-049] day.

[009-050] * * * * *

[009-051] The great Glooskap was a right valiant smoker; in all the world was no

[009-052] man who loved a pipe of good tobacco so much as he. In those days the

[009-053] summers were longer in the land of the Wabanaki, the sun was warmer,

[009-054] and the Indians raised _tomawe_ (tobacco, P.), and solaced

[009-055] themselves mightily therewith. [Footnote: I have met with an old Indian

[009-056] woman in New Brunswick who told me that her grandmother remembered to

[009-057] have seen tobacco raised there by the Passamaquoddy.] And there came to

[009-058] Glooskap a certain evil-minded magician, who sought to take his life,

[009-059] as the Master very well knew, for he read the hearts of men as if they

[009-060] had been strings of wampum. And this _m'teoulin_ (P., magician),

[009-061] believing himself to be greatest in all things, thought to appall

[009-062] Glooskap by outdoing him at first in something at which he excelled;

[009-063] for a fish is frightened when another swims faster, but not till then.

[009-064] And the man sat down to smoke with an exceeding long pipe with a great

[009-065] bowl, but that of Glooskap grew to be much greater. Then, having filled

[009-066] his pipe, the sorcerer exhausted and burnt it out at one pull, and then

[009-067] blew all the smoke out of his nose at one puff. So he sat and looked at

[009-068] the Master. But Glooskap, whose pipe held ten times as much tobacco,

[009-069] did the same, and blowing it out split the rocky ground, so that a

[009-070] great chasm opened before them. Then they were silent awhile, till the

[009-071] Master said, "If you can do that you may kill me." But he could not,

[009-072] and so went back with shame to those who had sent him. [Footnote: In

[009-073] this "tale of tobacco," told me by John Gabriel, the evil-minded

[009-074] magician is described as a Black Cat. This is probably an error, as

[009-075] Glooskap himself appears as chief of the Black Cats in another tale. It

[009-076] may be, however, that this was Pook-jin-skwess in disguise.]

[009-077] _How Glooskap sailed through the great Cavern of Darkness._

[009-078] (Micmac.)

[009-079] Now it is told in another tradition--and men tell even this

[009-080] differently--that _pitche_, in these old times (P.) Glooskap's

[009-081] seven neighbors, who were all so many different animals, took away his

[009-082] family, and that he followed them, even as it has been written, unto

[009-083] Newfoundland. And when he came there it was night, and, finding Marten

[009-084] alone, he took him forth into the forest to seek food, putting his belt

[009-085] on the boy, which gave him such power that he hunted well and got much

[009-086] meat.

[009-087] So it came to pass that the next morning Dame Kah-kah-gooch, the

[009-088] Crow, [Footnote: _Kah-kah-gooch_, Micmac, _Kah-kah-goos_,

[009-089] Passamaquoddy. The Crow is represented in several stories as always

[009-090] peeping, spying, begging, pilfering, and tale-bearing about a town. The

[009-091] Passamaquoddy Indians hare peculiar superstitions as regards killing

[009-092] the crow.] observed that Marten was drying meat on his wigwam. And this

[009-093] she spread abroad. But when the people learned that the child had done

[009-094] this, a great fear came upon them all, and they sat every man in his

[009-095] lodge and awaited death, for they knew that the Master had come.

[009-096] And he indeed came; but when he saw them all as frightened as rabbits

[009-097] before the wild-cat, he laughed aloud and forgave them, for he was

[009-098] noble and generous. And as they were hungry--for he had come in hard

[009-099] times--he gave them much venison, and sorrow departed from their

[009-100] wigwams. But as they had left him of old, he now left them. When they

[009-101] knew him not they left him to die; now that they knew him they feared

[009-102] lest they should perish without him. But he turned his steps towards

[009-103] other paths.

[009-104] Now having made a canoe, the Master, with Marten and Dame Bear, went

[009-105] upon a mighty river. As the story says, it was broad and beautiful at

[009-106] first, and so they sailed away down towards its mouth. Then they came

[009-107] to great cliffs, which gathered round and closed over them. But the

[009-108] river ran on beneath these, and ever on far underground, deeper and

[009-109] deeper in the earth, till it dashed headlong into rapids, among rocks

[009-110] and ravines, and under cataracts which were so horrible that death

[009-111] seemed to come and go with every plunge of the canoe. And the water

[009-112] grew narrower and the current more dreadful, and fear came upon Marten

[009-113] and the woman, so that they died. But the Master sat with silent soul,

[009-114] though he sang the songs of magic, and so passed into the night, but

[009-115] came forth again into sunlight. And there was a lonely wigwam on the

[009-116] bank, into which he bore Marten and the grandmother, and saying,

[009-117] "_Numchahse_! arise!" lo, they arose, and deemed they had only

[009-118] slept. And now Glooskap had gained the greatest power. [Footnote: This

[009-119] incident of the passage underground is deeply suggestive of Wabeno

[009-120] mystery and initiation. It will strike every student of classic lore as

[009-121] almost identical with much that he has read. If it has not the same

[009-122] symbolical meaning here, it has apparently none whatever.]

[009-123] This incident of passing through darkness, on a roaring stream in a

[009-124] frail bark, before emerging to sunlight or illumination, was not only

[009-125] in the ancient heathen myths. We are reminded of it by the storm

[009-126] through which Jesus passed with the disciples. That it made a great

[009-127] impression upon the Indians is shown by its being told of Pulewech, the

[009-128] Partridge, who is a type of Glooskap, and who, like him, makes war on

[009-129] the powers of evil, set forth in the Porcupines. The Indians, who

[009-130] imagined and selected so many wild and terrible tests to form the

[009-131] Shaman, or sorcerer, as well as the warrior, would hardly neglect that

[009-132] of _de profundis clamari_, the storm, the waves, darkness, and the

[009-133] roaring flood.

[009-134] If there is really any Norse influence in this tale, this river must be

[009-135] the one mentioned in the Vafthrudnismal,--

[009-136] "Ifing the stream is called

[009-137] which earth divides between

[009-138] the Jotuns and the gods.

[009-139] Open it shall run

[009-140] throughout all time.

[009-141] On that stream no ice shall he."

[009-142] It will be observed that, having gone down or across this stream,

[009-143] Pulewech finds himself in the country of the Evil sorcerers; that is,

[009-144] Jotunheim. To conquer a river among the Norse, in a dream, was a sign

[009-145] of victory; to be carried away by one was a terrible omen.

[009-146] "Methought a river ran

[009-147] Through the whole house,

[009-148] that it roared violently,

[009-149] rushed over the benches,

[009-150] brake the feet of yon

[009-151] brothers twain;

[009-152] Nothing the water spared;

[009-153] Something that will portend."

[009-154] (Atlamal, in Groenlewzku, 25.)

[009-155] _Of the Great Works which Glooskap made in the Land._

[009-156] (Micmac, Passamaquoddy, Penobscot.)

[009-157] Over all the Land of the Wabanaki there is no place which was not

[009-158] marked by the hand of the Master. And it is to be seen on hills and

[009-159] rivers and great roads, as well as mighty rocks, which were in their

[009-160] day living monsters.

[009-161] For there is a very wonderful highway from Cwesowra legek [Footnote:

[009-162] Hardwood Point, Fort Cumberland.] to Parrsborough, running parallel

[009-163] with the river now called Hebert, and this road is called by Indians

[009-164] Ou-wokun, the Causeway, but by white men, or the Iglesmani, the Boar's

[009-165] Back. For it is said that he meant to visit Partridge Island and Cape

[009-166] Blomidon, but they who were with him had got tired of the sea, and

[009-167] wished to cross over by land. And while they were resting and getting

[009-168] ready for their trip across, the Master, raising his magic power to a

[009-169] great deed to be spoken of forever, went away a little time, and cast

[009-170] up a great and beautiful level ridge, throwing it over bogs and

[009-171] streams; and on this they traveled, rejoicing, and, having reached the

[009-172] island, awaited him.

[009-173] And yet again the Master did a mighty deed. It came to pass in those

[009-174] days that the Beavers had built a dam across from Utkoguncheek, or Cape

[009-175] Blomidon, to the opposite shore, and thereby made a pond that filled

[009-176] all the valley of Annapolis. Now in those times the Beavers were

[009-177] monstrous beasts, and the Master, though kind of heart, seems to have

[009-178] had but little love for them ever since the day when Qwah-beetsis, the

[009-179] son of the Great Beaver, tempted Malsum to slay his brother. Now the

[009-180] bones of these Beavers may be found to this day, and many there are on

[009-181] Oonamahgik, and their teeth are six inches across, and there are no

[009-182] such _qwah-beet_ to-day. [Footnote: Both Mr. Rand and myself have

[009-183] been solemnly assured by Indians who had seen these antediluvian

[009-184] remains that they are the petrified relics of Glooskap's victims.] And

[009-185] these are the remains of the Beavers who built the dam at Cape Blomidon

[009-186] and forded the Annapolis Valley. Now Glooskap would have a hunt and do

[009-187] a deed which should equal the great whale-fishing of Kit-pooseeog-unow.

[009-188] So he cut the great dam near the shore, and bade Marten watch; for he

[009-189] said, "I mistrust that there is a little Beaver hiding hereabouts." And

[009-190] when the dam was cut from where it joined the shore there was a mighty

[009-191] rush of many waters, so that it swung round to the westward, yet it did

[009-192] not break away from the other shore. Therefore the end of it lodged

[009-193] with a great split therein when the flood had found a free course, and

[009-194] the whole may be seen there still, even to this day, and may be seen by

[009-195] all of those who pass up the bay; and this point, or Cape Split, is

[009-196] called by the Micmacs Pleegun, which, being interpreted, means the

[009-197] opening of a beaver dam.
