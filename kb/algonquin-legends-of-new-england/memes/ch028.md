---[028-001] Here he rested. He remained for a hundred moons observing the customs

[028-002] of the people and learning their language. One morning the Great Chief

[028-003] told him that he must return to his own people. He disliked to do this,

[028-004] for he was very happy in the new place. The Chief said, "These are the

[028-005] happy hunting grounds. We have admitted you that you may know how and

[028-006] what to teach your people, that they may get here. Go, and if you do

[028-007] what I tell you, you may return to remain forever. You have not been

[028-008] allowed to come here to remain, but only to observe. When you come

[028-009] again, you shall join us in all things. You shall hunt and fish then,

[028-010] and have whatever you wish. But return now, and teach what you have

[028-011] learned here."

[028-012] A cloud of smoke in the form of a great eagle came to him, and, seated

[028-013] on its back, he was borne down to the top of the tree from which he had

[028-014] risen. He opened his eyes. The sun was shining. His pursuers had gone

[028-015] away. He descended and traveled on. His mind was filled with what he

[028-016] had seen. He said, "I will no longer play tricks, but tell the people

[028-017] about what I learned in the happy hunting-grounds."

[028-018] After a long journey he drew near a village. He gave the common signal.

[028-019] Runners came to meet him. The head chief and all the people came to

[028-020] hear. He was asked, "What news do you bring us?"

[028-021] He said, "I that was the Mischief Maker am the Peace Maker now. The

[028-022] Great Spirit took me to the happy hunting-grounds, and I am sent back

[028-023] to tell you how to get there." Then the Peace Maker described all he

[028-024] had seen. The people built a great fire and danced around it, and

[028-025] shouted as they had never done before. Then he said, "This is the

[028-026] message I bring you."

[028-027] So the people sat in a great circle round the fire and listened. He

[028-028] spoke:--

[028-029] "The Great Spirit is unseen, but he is about us. He will not forsake us.

[028-030] He rules all things for us. He will take care of us. He told me that we

[028-031] should return thanks to him, for he changes the seasons, and makes corn

[028-032] and beans and squashes grow for us. He is displeased when we kill our

[028-033] brothers. He hopes that we will not forget him. He will never die. His

[028-034] name is _Ha-wen-ni-yu_,--the Ruler. He bids us keep away from his

[028-035] wicked brother, whose name is _Ha-ne-go-ate-geh_, the Evil-Minded.

[028-036] He is very bad. He brings pestilence and fevers, and lizards and poisonous

[028-037] weeds. He destroys peace, and brings war. Ha-wen-ni-yu will care for us

[028-038] if we trust in him. Obey his words, and Ha-ne-go-ate-geh will never

[028-039] harm us.

[028-040] The Great Spirit, has messengers, who aid him in his work. They watch

[028-041] over the people. They take care of the mother and her new-born babe,

[028-042] that they receive no harm; they watch over those whom the Evil-Minded

[028-043] has troubled with disease. The Evil-Minded has messengers who do his

[028-044] work. They scatter pestilence, and whisper in our ears, and tell us to

[028-045] go against Ha-wen-ni-yu.

[028-046] The Great Spirit has messengers. Heno has a pouch filled with

[028-047] thunderbolts. Heno gathers the clouds and sends the rain. He is a

[028-048] friend to the corn and beans and squashes. He also punishes witches and

[028-049] evil persons. Pray to Heno when you plant, and thank him when you

[028-050] gather your crop. Pray also to Ha-wen-ni-yu, who will send Heno to care

[028-051] for you. Let Heno be called Grandfather.

[028-052] Ga-oh is the Spirit of the Winds. He moves the winds, but he is chained

[028-053] to a rock. The winds trouble him, and he tries very hard to get free.

[028-054] When he struggles the winds are forced away from him, and they blow

[028-055] upon the earth. Sometimes he suffers terrible pain, and then his

[028-056] struggles are violent. This makes the winds wild, and they do damage on

[028-057] the earth. Then he feels better and goes to sleep, and the winds become

[028-058] quiet also.

[028-059] There is a spirit for the corn, another for beans, another for squashes.

[028-060] They are sisters, and are very kind to each other. They dwell together,

[028-061] and live in the fields. They shall be known as _De-o-ha-ka_,--the

[028-062] keepers of our life.

[028-063] There are spirits in the water, in fire, in all the trees and berries,

[028-064] in herbs and in tobacco, in the grass. They assist the Great Spirit.

[028-065] Always return thanks to _Ho-noh-che-noh-keh_, the Guardian Spirits.

[028-066] _Ha-ne-go-ate-geh_ has messengers. These are the spirits of disease,

[028-067] of fever, of witches, weeds, and murder. But the Great Spirit will keep

[028-068] them away from his children.

[028-069] This is the message I bring from the happy hunting-grounds. Obey these

[028-070] words, and the Great Spirit will give you a place there."

[028-071] So Peace Maker taught the people. They threw tobacco on the fire,

[028-072] according to his instructions, and on the column of its smoke he was

[028-073] borne away to the happy hunting-grounds. And the people danced and sang

[028-074] around the dying embers of the council fire.

[028-075] This is probably an ancient legend with a modern moral. The idea of an

[028-076] Indian Tyl Eulenspiegel going about the country making mischief recalls

[028-077] a great part of the adventures of Hiawatha or Manobozho; in fact, it

[028-078] could not fail to suggest itself to a believer in Shamanism, or pow-wow,

[028-079] according to which evil spirits and men like them are continually

[028-080] teasing mankind, out of sheer malice. The reform of the wicked man,

[028-081] under the influence of the "Great Spirit," is of later days. I do not

[028-082] believe that the idea of a Great Spirit, in the sense in which it is

[028-083] generally used by Indians, or is attributed to them, was ever known

[028-084] till learned from the whites. Nothing is more natural than that during

[028-085] the two hundred years past intelligent Indians, who felt that there

[028-086] were many evils in the old barbaric state, yet who were still under the

[028-087] influence of its myths and poetry, should have made up legends like

[028-088] this purporting to be revelations. There is one of the kind given in

[028-089] the Hiawatha Legend, as "Eroneniera, an Indian visit to the Great

[028-090] Spirit," which bears on its face every mark of modern manufacture for a

[028-091] purpose. For these very reasons, however, the tale here given is of

[028-092] great interest to the impartial historian. I am indebted for it to the

[028-093] kindness of Colonel T. Wentworth Higginson. This is the only story in

[028-094] my collection of which I cannot give the name and residence of the

[028-095] original Indian narrator.

[028-096] In the first part we have in the _Mischief Maker_ the same

[028-097] character or principle who appears as Lox, the Wolverine, the Raccoon,

[028-098] and Badger among the Wabanaki. The setting the blind women together by

[028-099] the ears, and the dashing of hot pudding, soup, or water in their

[028-100] faces, is another form of a Lox story, which occurs again in the

[028-101] Kalevala. But the entire spirit of the tricks is that of Lox, as those

[028-102] of Lox are like those of Loki. The Rev. D. Moncure Conway once said to

[028-103] me, as Miss E. Robins has also said in an article in the Atlantic

[028-104] Monthly, that it is only in the Norse mythology that the Evil One, or

[028-105] devil, is represented as growing up from or inspired solely by reckless

[028-106] wanton _mischief_,--the mischief of a bad boy or a monkey. But the

[028-107] very same is as true of so much of a devil as there is in the Wabanaki

[028-108] mythology. It is as a grotesque shadow of Loki, but still it is his.

[028-109] The Germans say the devil is God's ape; the Indian Lox is the Norse

[028-110] devil's.

[028-111] _How Lox told a Lie._

[028-112] (Passamaquoddy.)

[028-113] Lox had a brother, who had married a red squaw. When she was touched

[028-114] the red color rubbed off. The brother kept this wife in a box.

[028-115] One day, returning, the brother saw that Lox had red fingers. "Aha!" he

[028-116] cried, in a rage, "you have taken my wife out of the box." But Lox

[028-117] denied it, so that his brother believed him.

[028-118] The next time the husband returned, Lox's fingers were again red. And

[028-119] again he was accused, and once more he denied it. But as he swore with

[028-120] all his might that he was innocent, something, as if on the floor,

[028-121] laughed, and said, "You lie. I was with you; I helped you."

[028-122] Lox thought it was his right foot. So he cut off the toes, and then the

[028-123] foot, but the accusation continued. Thinking it was the other foot, he

[028-124] cut that off; yet as the testimony was continued, he found that it was

[028-125] _Taloose_, even he himself, the bodily offender in person,

[028-126] testifying against his lying soul. So in a rage he struck himself such

[028-127] a blow with his war-club that he fell dead. I cannot give in full all

[028-128] the adventures of Lox. I may, however, observe one thing of great

[028-129] importance. Lox, in these tales, is the Evil Principle, that is, a

[028-130] giant by birth. His two feet in this story are male and female; they

[028-131] talk as if they were human. In the Edda, a giant's two feet beget

[028-132] together a six-headed son (Vafthrudnismal):--

[028-133] "Foot with foot begot

[028-134] Of that wise Jotun,

[028-135] A six-headed son."

[028-136] This six-headed son reappears as a demon in the Passamaquoddy tale of

[028-137] the Three Strong Men.

[028-138] _Tuloose_, literally translated, is the phallus. The red squaw

[028-139] refers to the Newfoundland Indians, covered with red ochre. They are

[028-140] believed to be now extinct.

[028-141] THE AMAZING ADVENTURES OF MASTER RABBIT

[028-142] WITH THE OTTER, THE WOODPECKER GIRLS, AND MOOIN THE BEAR

[028-143] ALSO A FULL ACCOUNT OF THE FAMOUS CHASE, IN WHICH HE FOOLED LUSIFEE,

[028-144] THE WILD CAT

[028-145] _I. How Master Rabbit sought to rival Keeoony, the Otter_.

[028-146] Of old times, _Mahtigwess_, the Rabbit, who is called in the

[028-147] Micmac tongue _Ableegumooch_, lived with his grandmother, waiting

[028-148] for better times; and truly he found it a hard matter in midwinter,

[028-149] when ice was on the river and snow was on the plain, to provide even

[028-150] for his small household. And running through the forest one day he

[028-151] found a lonely wigwam, and he that dwelt therein was _Keeoony_,

[028-152] the Otter. The lodge was on the bank of a river, and a smooth road of

[028-153] ice slanted from the door down to the water. And the Otter made him

[028-154] welcome, and directed his housekeeper to get ready to cook; saying

[028-155] which, he took the hooks on which he was wont to string fish when he

[028-156] had them, and went to fetch a mess for dinner. Placing himself on the

[028-157] top of the slide, he coasted in and under the water, and then came out

[028-158] with a great bunch of eels, which were soon cooked, and on which they

[028-159] dined.

[028-160] "By my life," thought Master Rabbit, "but that is an easy way of

[028-161] getting a living! Truly these fishing-folk have fine fare, and cheap!

[028-162] Cannot I, who am so clever, do as well as this mere Otter? Of course I

[028-163] can. Why not?" Thereupon he grew so confident of himself as to invite

[028-164] the Otter to dine with him--_adamadusk ketkewop_--on the third day

[028-165] after that, and so went home.

[028-166] "Come on!" he said to his grandmother the next morning; "let us remove

[028-167] our wigwam down to the lake." So they removed; and he selected a site

[028-168] such as the Otter had chosen for his home, and the weather being cold

[028-169] he made a road of ice, or a coast, down from his door to the water, and

[028-170] all was well. Then the guest came at the time set, and Rabbit, calling

[028-171] his grandmother, bade her get ready to cook a dinner. "But what am I to

[028-172] cook, grandson?" inquired the old dame.

[028-173] "Truly I will see to that," said he, and made him a _nabogun_, or

[028-174] stick to string eels. Then going to the ice path, he tried to slide

[028-175] like one skilled in the art, but indeed with little luck, for be went

[028-176] first to the right side, then to the left, and so hitched and jumped

[028-177] till he came to the water, where he went in with a bob backwards. And

[028-178] this bad beginning had no better ending, since of all swimmers and

[028-179] divers the Rabbit is the very worst, and this one was no better than

[028-180] his brothers. The water was cold, he lost his breath, he struggled, and

[028-181] was well-nigh drowned.
