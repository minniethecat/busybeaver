---[026-001] Now the Raccoon is bare or has little fur where he scratched himself,

[026-002] to this very day. This story is at an end.

[026-003] This story is from the Passamaquoddy Indian-English collection made for

[026-004] me by Louis Mitchell. In the original, the same incident of boiling the

[026-005] hero in a kettle and of his springing out of it occurs as in the tale

[026-006] of Mrs. Bear and the Raccoon. This I have here omitted. The

[026-007] Mephistophelian and mocking character of Lox is strongly shown when he

[026-008] says, "Nothing but a cat-tail or bulrush can kill me," this being

[026-009] evidently an allusion to Glooskap. This is to an Indian much like

[026-010] blasphemy. Lox, or Raccoon, or Badger,--for they are all the same,--in

[026-011] his journeyings after mere mischief reminds us of an Indian Tyl

[026-012] Eulenspiegel. But the atrocious nature of his jokes is like nothing

[026-013] else, unless it be indeed the homicide Punch. It is the indomitable

[026-014] nature of both which commends them respectively to the Englishman and

[026-015] to the Red Indian. In this tale Lox appears as the spirit of fire by

[026-016] drawing a bag from it. The itching or pricking from which he suffers is

[026-017] also significant of that element, as appears, according to Keary, in

[026-018] many Norse, etc., legends.

[026-019] In the Seneca tale of the Mischief Maker, the Berries are distinctly

[026-020] declared to have souls.

[026-021] _How Lox deceived the Ducks, cheated the Chief, and beguiled the

[026-022] Bear._

[026-023] (Micmac and Passamaquoddy.)

[026-024] Somewhere in the forest lived Lox, with a small boy, his brother. When

[026-025] winter came they went far into the woods to hunt. And going on, they

[026-026] reached at last a very large and beautiful lake. It was covered with

[026-027] water-fowl. There were wild geese and brant, black ducks and wood-ducks,

[026-028] and all the smaller kinds down to teal and whistlers.

[026-029] The small boy was delighted to see so much game. He eagerly asked his

[026-030] brother how he meant to catch them. He answered, "We must first go to

[026-031] work and build a large wigwam. It must be very strong, with a heavy,

[026-032] solid door." This was done; and Lox, being a great magician, thus

[026-033] arranged his plans for taking the wild-fowl. He sent the boy out to a

[026-034] point of land, where he was to cry to the birds and tell them that his

[026-035] brother wished to give them a kingly reception. (_Nakamit_, to act

[026-036] the king.) He told them their king had come. Then Lox, arraying himself

[026-037] grandly, sat with dignity next the door, with his eyes closed, as if in

[026-038] great state. Then the little boy shouted that they might enter and hear

[026-039] what the great sagamore had to say. They flocked in, and took their

[026-040] seats in the order of their size. The Wild Geese came nearest and sat

[026-041] down, then the Ducks, and so on to the smallest, who sat nearest the

[026-042] door. Last of all came the boy, who entering also sat down by the door,

[026-043] closed it, and held it fast. So the little birds, _altumabedajik_

[026-044] (M.), sat next to him.

[026-045] Then they were all told "_Spegwedajik_!" "Shut your eyes!" and

[026-046] were directed to keep them closed for their very lives, until directed

[026-047] to open them again. Unless they did this first, their eyes would be

[026-048] blinded forever when they beheld their king in all his magnificence. So

[026-049] they sat in silence. Then the sorcerer, stepping softly, took them one

[026-050] by one, grasping each tightly by the wings, and ere the bird knew what

[026-051] he was about it had its head crushed between his teeth. And so without

[026-052] noise or fluttering he killed all the Wild Geese and Brant and Black

[026-053] Ducks. Then the little boy began to pity the poor small wild-fowl. He

[026-054] thought it was a shame to kill so many, having already more than they

[026-055] needed. So stooping down, he whispered to a very little bird to open

[026-056] its eyes. It did so, but very cautiously indeed, for fear of being

[026-057] blinded.

[026-058] Great was his horror to see what Lox was doing! He screamed,

[026-059] "_Kedumeds'lk_!" "We are all being killed!" Then they opened their

[026-060] eyes, and flew about in the utmost confusion, screaming loudly in

[026-061] terror. The little boy dropped down as if he had been knocked over in

[026-062] the confusion, so that the door flew wide open, and the birds, rushing

[026-063] over him, began to, escape, while Lox in a rage continued to seize them

[026-064] and kill them with his teeth. Then the little boy, to avoid suspicion,

[026-065] grasped the last fugitive by the legs and held him fast. But he was

[026-066] suspected all the same by the wily sorcerer, who caught him up roughly,

[026-067] and would have beaten him cruelly but that he earnestly protested that

[026-068] the birds knocked him down and forced the door open, and that he could

[026-069] by no means help it: which being somewhat slowly believed, he was

[026-070] forgiven, and they began to pluck and dress the game. The giblets were

[026-071] preserved, the fowls sliced and dried and laid by for the winter's

[026-072] store.

[026-073] Then having plenty of provisions, Lox gave a feast. Among the guests

[026-074] were Marten and Mahtigwess, the Rabbit, who talked together for a long

[026-075] time in the most confidential manner, the Rabbit confiding and the

[026-076] Marten attending to him.

[026-077] Now while this conversation had been going on, Lox, who was deeply

[026-078] addicted to all kinds of roguery and mischief, had listened to it with

[026-079] interest. And when the two little guests had ceased he asked them where

[026-080] their village was, and who lived in it. Then he was told that all the

[026-081] largest animals had their homes there: the bear, caribou or reindeer,

[026-082] deer, wolf, wild cat, to say nothing of squirrels and mice. And having

[026-083] got them to show him the way, he some time after turned himself into a

[026-084] young woman of great beauty, or at least disguised himself like one,

[026-085] and going to the village married the young chief. And having left

[026-086] little Marten alone in a hollow tree outside the village, the boy,

[026-087] getting hungry, began to howl for food; which the villagers hearing

[026-088] were in a great fright. But the young chiefs wife, or the magician

[026-089] Lox, soon explained to them what it meant. "It is," she-he said,

[026-090] "_Owoolakumooejit_, the Spirit of Famine. He is grim and gaunt;

[026-091] hear how he howls for food! Woe be unto you, should he reach this

[026-092] village! Ah, I remember only too well what happened when he once

[026-093] came among us. Horror! starvation!"

[026-094] "Can you drive him back?" cried all the villagers.

[026-095] "Yes, 'tis in my power. Do but give me the well-tanned hide of a

[026-096] yearling moose and a good supply of moose-tallow, [Footnote: A great

[026-097] delicacy among these semi-Arctic Indians.] then the noise will cease."

[026-098] And seizing it, and howling furiously the name of his brother after a

[026-099] fashion which no one could understand,--_Aa-chowwa'n_!--and

[026-100] bidding him begone, he rushed out into the night, until he came to

[026-101] Marten, to whom he gave the food, and, wrapping him up well in the

[026-102] moose-skin, bade him wait a while. And the villagers thought the

[026-103] chief's wife was indeed a very great conjurer.

[026-104] And then she-he announced that a child would soon be born. And when the

[026-105] day came Badger handed out a bundle, and said that the babe was in it.

[026-106] "_Noolmusugakelaimadijul_," "They kiss it outside the blanket."

[026-107] But when the chief opened it what he found therein was the dried,

[026-108] withered embryo of a moose-calf. In a great rage he flung it into the

[026-109] fire, and all rushed headlong in a furious pack to catch Badger. They

[026-110] saw him and Marten rushing to the lake. They pursued him, but when he

[026-111] reached the bank the wily sorcerer cast in a stick; it turned into a

[026-112] canoe, and long ere the infuriated villagers could reach them they were

[026-113] on the opposite shore and in the woods.

[026-114] Now it came to pass one day that as Lox sat on a log a bear came by,

[026-115] who, being a sociable fellow, sat down by him and smoked a pipe. While

[026-116] they were talking a gull flew over, and inadvertently offered to Lox

[026-117] what he considered, or affected to consider, as a great insult. And

[026-118] wiping the insult off, Lox cried to the Gull, "Oh, ungrateful and

[026-119] insolent creature, is this the way you reward me for having made you

[026-120] white!"

[026-121] Now the Bear would always be white if he could, for the White Bear

[026-122] (_wabeyu mooin_) is the aristocrat of Beardom. So he eagerly

[026-123] cried, "Ha! did you make the Gull white?"

[026-124] "Indeed I did," replied Lox. "And this is what I get for it."

[026-125] "Could you, my dear friend,--could you make _me_ white?"

[026-126] Then Lox saw his way, and replied that he could indeed, but that it

[026-127] would be a long and agonizing process; Mooin might die of it. To be

[026-128] sure the Gull stood it, but could a Bear?

[026-129] Now the Bear, who had a frame as hard as a rock, felt sure that he

[026-130] could endure anything that a gull could, especially to become a white

[026-131] bear. So, with much ceremony, the Great Enchanter went to work. He

[026-132] built a strong wigwam, three feet high, of stones, and having put the

[026-133] Bear into it he cast in red-hot stones, and poured water on them

[026-134] through a small hole in the roof. Erelong the Bear was in a terrible

[026-135] steam.

[026-136] "Ah, Doctor Lox," he cried, "this is awfully hot! I fear I am dying!"

[026-137] "Courage," said Lox; "this is nothing. The Gull had it twice as hot."

[026-138] "Can't stand it any more, doctor. _O-o-o-oh_!"

[026-139] Doctor Lox threw in more hot stones and poured more water on them. The

[026-140] Bear yelled.

[026-141] "Let me out! _O-o-h_! let me out! _O-o-o-oh_!"

[026-142] So he came bursting through the door. The doctor examined him

[026-143] critically.

[026-144] Now there is on an old bear a small white or light spot on his upper

[026-145] breast, which he cannot see. [Footnote: This is very white on the

[026-146] Japanese bears.] And Doctor Lox, looking at this, said,--

[026-147] "What a pity! You came out just as you were beginning to turn white.

[026-148] Here is the first spot. Five minutes more and you'd have been a white

[026-149] bear. Ah, you haven't the pluck of a gull; that I can see."

[026-150] Now the Bear was mortified and disappointed. He had not seen the spot,

[026-151] so he asked Lox if it was really there.

[026-152] "Wait a minute," said the doctor. He led the Bear to a pool and made

[026-153] him look in. Sure enough, the spot was there. Then he asked if they

[026-154] could not begin again.

[026-155] "Certainly we can," replied the doctor. "But it will be much hotter and

[026-156] harder and longer this time. Don't try it if you feel afraid, and don't

[026-157] blame _me_ if you die of it."

[026-158] The Bear went in again, but he never came out alive. The doctor had

[026-159] roast bear meat all that winter, and much bear's oil. He gave some of

[026-160] the oil to his younger brother. The boy took it in a measure. Going

[026-161] along the creek, he saw a Muskrat (_Keuchus_, Pass.). He said to

[026-162] the Muskrat, "If you can harden this oil for me, I will give you half."

[026-163] The Muskrat made it as hard as ice. The boy said, "If my brother comes

[026-164] and asks you to do this for him, do you keep it all." And, returning,

[026-165] he showed the oil thus hardened to his brother, who, taking a large

[026-166] measure of it, went to the Muskrat and asked him to harden it. The

[026-167] Muskrat indeed took the dish and swam away with it, and never returned.

[026-168] Then the elder, vexed with the younger, and remembering the ducks in

[026-169] the wigwam, and believing now that he had indeed been cheated, slew

[026-170] him.

[026-172] This confused and strange story is manifestly pieced together out of

[026-173] several others, each of which have incidents in common. A part of it

[026-174] is very ancient. Firstly, the inveigling the ducks into the wigwam is

[026-175] found in the Eskimo tale of Avurungnak (Rink, p. 177). The Eskimo is

[026-176] told by a sorcerer to let the sea-birds into the tent, and not to

[026-177] begin to kill them till the tent is full. He disobeys, and a part of

[026-178] them escape. In Schoolcraft's Hiawatha Legends, Manobozho gets the

[026-179] mysterious oil which ends the foregoing story from a fish. He fattens

[026-180] all the animals in the world with it, and the amount which they consume

[026-181] is the present measure of their fatness. When this ceremony is over, he

[026-182] inveigles all the birds into his power by telling them to shut their

[026-183] eyes. At last a small duck, the diver, suspecting something, opens one

[026-184] eye, and gives the alarm.
