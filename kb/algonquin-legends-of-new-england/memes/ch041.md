---[041-001] When their father returned home the next evening he brought with him

[041-002] many of the pretty little shells from which _weiopeskool_ (M.),

[041-003] or wampum, was made, [Footnote: In Passamaquoddy wampum is called

[041-004] _waw-bap_. It is said that a single bead required a full day's work

[041-005] to make and finish it. It is not many years since it was made much more

[041-006] expeditiously in certain New York villages.] and they were soon engaged

[041-007] _napawejik_ (in stringing them). That day poor little

[041-008] Oochigeaskw', the burnt-faced girl, who had always run barefoot, got a

[041-009] pair of her father's old moccasins, and put them into water that they

[041-010] might become flexible to wear. And begging her sisters for a few wampum

[041-011] shells, the eldest did but call her "a lying little pest," but the

[041-012] other gave her a few. And having no clothes beyond a few paltry rags,

[041-013] the poor creature went forth and got herself from the woods a few

[041-014] sheets of birch bark, of which she made a dress, putting some figures

[041-015] on the bark. [Footnote: Probably by scraping. Birch bark

[041-016] (_moskwe_) peeled in winter can have the thin dark brown coat

[041-017] scraped away, leaving a very light yellowish-brown ground. Tornah

[041-018] Josephs and his niece Susan, of Princeton, Maine, are experts at this

[041-019] work.] And this dress she shaped like those worn of old. [Footnote:

[041-020] This remark indicates the lateness of the Micmac version of this very

[041-021] old myth.] So she made a petticoat and a loose gown, a cap, leggins,

[041-022] and handkerchief, and, having put on her father's great old moccasins,--

[041-023] which came nearly up to her knees,--she went forth to try her luck.

[041-024] For even this little thing would see the Invisible One in the great

[041-025] wigwam at the end of the village.

[041-026] Truly her luck had a most inauspicious beginning, for there was one

[041-027] long storm of ridicule and hisses, yells and hoots, from her own door

[041-028] to that which she went to seek. Her sisters tried to shame her, and

[041-029] bade her stay at home, but she would not obey; and all the idlers,

[041-030] seeing this strange little creature in her odd array, cried "Shame!"

[041-031] But she went on, for she was greatly resolved; it may be that some

[041-032] spirit had inspired her.

[041-033] Now this poor small wretch in her mad attire, with her hair singed off

[041-034] and her little face as full of burns and scars as there are holes in a

[041-035] sieve, was, for all this, most kindly received by the sister of the

[041-036] Invisible One; for this noble girl knew more than the mere outside of

[041-037] things as the world knows them. And as the brown of the evening sky

[041-038] became black, she took her down to the lake. And erelong the girls knew

[041-039] that He had come. Then the sister said, "Do you see him?" And the other

[041-040] replied with awe, "Truly I do,--and He is wonderful." "And what is his

[041-041] sled-string?" "It is," she replied, "the Rainbow." And great fear was

[041-042] on her. "But, my sister," said the other, "what is his bow-string?"

[041-043] "His bowstring is _Ketaksoowowcht_" (the Spirits' Road, the Milky

[041-044] Way). [Footnote: The Spirits' or Ghosts' Road, so called because it is

[041-045] believed to be the highway by which spirits pass to and from the earth.

[041-046] The Micmac version, belittled and reduced in every way, limits this

[041-047] reply to "a _piece_ of a rainbow." There is a grandeur of

[041-048] conception in the Passamaquoddy myth which recalls the most stupendous

[041-049] similes in Scripture.]

[041-050] "Thou hast seen him," said the sister. And, taking the girl home, she

[041-051] bathed her, and as she washed all the scars disappeared from face and

[041-052] body. Her hair grew again; it was very long, and like a blackbird's

[041-053] wing. Her eyes were like stars. In all the world was no such beauty.

[041-054] Then from her treasures she gave her a wedding garment, and adorned

[041-055] her. Under the comb, as she combed her, her hair grew. It was a great

[041-056] marvel to behold.

[041-057] Then, having done this, she bade her take the _wife's seat_ in the

[041-058] wigwam,--that by which her brother sat, the seat next the door. And

[041-059] when He entered, terrible and beautiful, he smiled and said,

[041-060] "_Wajoolkoos_!" "So we are found out!" "_Alajulaa_." "Yes,"

[041-061] was her reply. So she became his wife. [Footnote: This is the true end of

[041-062] this Indian Cupid and Psyche legend. But the Micmacs having, for no

[041-063] apparent reason, made the Stupendous Deity of the Heavens a moose

[041-064] (Team), have added to it another for the sake of the name, and which I

[041-065] give in due succession simply as an illustration of the manner in which

[041-066] tales are tacked together. I have very little doubt that the story as

[041-067] here given is an old solar myth, worked up, perhaps, with the story of

[041-068] Cinderella, derived from a Canadian-French source. There are enough of

[041-069] these French-Indian stories in my possession alone to form what would

[041-070] make one of the most interesting volumes of the series of the _Contes

[041-071] Populaires_. The Passamaquoddy version is to this effect: "There was

[041-072] a great being, a mighty hunter, who had a wife, of wonderful magic

[041-073] gifts, and a boy; and the child became blind. After a long time his

[041-074] sight returned, and he said so; but his mother was suspicious, and did

[041-075] not believe him." It is evident that she suspected that he saw by

[041-076] _clairvoyance_, not by literal vision. "So one day she bade her

[041-077] husband put on certain things which no one could behold who did not see

[041-078] them in truth. Then she asked the boy, 'What has your father for a

[041-079] sled-string?' (literally for a moose-runner haul). And he replied, 'The

[041-080] rainbow to haul by.' Then she asked him yet again, 'What has he for a

[041-081] bow-string?' And he answered, '_Ke'taksoo wowcht_;' 'The Spirits'

[041-082] or Ghosts' Road.' And once more she inquired, 'What has he on his

[041-083] sled?' To which he said, 'A beaver.' Then she knew that he could indeed

[041-084] see." (T. Josephs.)

[041-085] We can perceive by shreds and patches such as these the _all but_

[041-086] loss of an early and grand mythology which has undergone the usual

[041-087] transmutation into romantic and nursery legends. By great exertion we

[041-088] might recover it, but the old Indians who retain its fragments are

[041-089] passing away rapidly, and no subject attracts so little interest among

[041-090] our _literati_. A few hundred dollars expended annually in each

[041-091] State would result in the collection of all that is extant of this

[041-092] folk-lore; and a hundred years hence some few will, perhaps, regret

[041-093] that it was not done.

[041-094] It may be observed that in the Edda the rainbow is the heavenly road

[041-095] over which the gods pass. The rainbow is not the Milky Way, but it may

[041-096] be observed that in this tale the two are curiously compared, or almost

[041-097] identified. But according to Charles Francis Keary (_Mythology of the

[041-098] Eddas_, London, 1882), "there is small hint in the Edda of the use

[041-099] of the _rainbow_ as a path for souls, save where Helgi says to his

[041-100] wife,--

[041-101] "''Tis time for me to ride the ruddy road,

[041-102] And on my horse to tread the path of flight,'"

[041-103] which is more applicable to the Milky Way than the rainbow. "We owe,"

[041-104] he says, "to the learned Adalbert Kohn some researches which have

[041-105] traced the path of the Milky Way as a bridge of souls from its first

[041-106] appearance in Eastern creeds to its later appearance in mediaeval

[041-107] German tradition." (_Zeitschrift f. v. Sp.l.c._) In the Vedas the

[041-108] Milky Way is called the Gods' Path. The American Indians firmly believe

[041-109] that the Spirits' Road is one of their very earliest traditions, and I

[041-110] believe with them that they had it long before Columbus discovered this

[041-111] country.

[041-112] Since the foregoing remarks were written, Mrs. W. Wallace Brown has

[041-113] obtained the following fragment, which was given as a song, and

[041-114] declared to be very ancient:--

[041-115] "Then was woman, long, long ago:

[041-116] She came out of a hole.

[041-117] In it dead people were buried.

[041-118] She made her house in a tree;

[041-119] She was dressed in leaves,

[041-120] All long ago.

[041-121] When she walked among the dry leaves

[041-122] Her feet were so covered

[041-123] The feet were invisible.

[041-124] She walked through the woods,

[041-125] Singing all the time,

[041-126] 'I want company; I'm lonesome!'

[041-127] A wild man heard her:

[041-128] From afar over the lakes and mountains

[041-129] He came to her.

[041-130] She saw him; she was afraid;

[041-131] She tried to flee away,

[041-132] For he was covered with the rainbow;

[041-133] Color and light were his garments.

[041-134] She ran, and he pursued rapidly;

[041-135] He chased her to the foot of a mountain.

[041-136] He spoke in a strange language,

[041-137] She could not understand him at first.

[041-138] He would make her tell where she dwelt.

[041-139] They married, they had two children.

[041-140] One of them was a boy,

[041-141] He was blind from his birth,

[041-142] But he frightened his mother by his sight.

[041-143] He could tell her what was coming,

[041-144] What was coming from afar

[041-145] What was near he could not see.

[041-146] He could see the bear and the moose

[041-147] Far away beyond the mountains,

[041-148] He could see through everything."

[041-149] The old Indian woman ended this story by saying abruptly, "Don't know

[041-150] any more. Guess they all eat up by _mooin_" (the bear). She said

[041-151] that it was only a fragment. "If you could have heard her repeat this,"

[041-152] adds Mrs. Brown, "in pieces, stopping to explain what the characters

[041-153] said, and describing how they looked, and anon singing it again, you

[041-154] would have got the _inner sense_ of a wonderfully weird tale. The

[041-155] woman's feet covering and the man's dress _like_ a rainbow, yet

[041-156] not one, which made their bodies invisible, seemed to exercise her

[041-157] imagination strangely; and these were to her the most important part of

[041-158] the story." The fragment is part of a very old myth; I regret to say a

[041-159] very obscure one.]

[041-160] STORY OF THE THREE STRONG MEN.

[041-161] (Micmac.)

[041-162] There was a chieftain in the days of yore. He had a great desire for a

[041-163] poor girl who was a servant, and who worked for him. To win this girl

[041-164] he first I most lose his wife. He took his wife afar into the woods to

[041-165] gather spruce-gum, and then left her there.

[041-166] She soon found out that she had lost her way, and, wandering, she lost

[041-167] it more and more for many days, until she came at last to a bear's den,

[041-168] where, going in, she found the Chief of all the bears, who welcomed

[041-169] her, provided for her wants, and furnished her with pleasant food; but

[041-170] as the meat was raw he went into a neighboring town for fire. And as

[041-171] she lived with him she was to him in all things as he wished, and as a

[041-172] wife.

[041-173] So that it came to pass, as time went on, that a new-comer was

[041-174] expected, and she bade the Bear provide the baby's clothes. And when

[041-175] the long-expected infant came it was a boy, large, beautiful, and

[041-176] strong; he was in everything beyond all other boys.

[041-177] And as the child was born in a strange way, he very soon displayed a

[041-178] magic power. No baby ever grew so rapidly: when four months old he

[041-179] wrestled with the Bear and threw him easily upon the floor. And so the

[041-180] mother saw that he would be a warrior, and the chief of other men.

[041-181] She loathed the life she led, and wished to leave, and live as she had

[041-182] done in days of old. To this the Bear would in nowise consent, and as

[041-183] her son was human, like herself, he loved his mother best, and thought

[041-184] with her.

[041-185] One day he said, "Now I can wrestle well and throw the Bear as often as

[041-186] I choose. When I next time cast him upon the ground, catch up a club;

[041-187] the rest remains for you!"

[041-188] They waited yet a while till he had grown so strong that the Bear was

[041-189] nothing in his grasp. One day they wrestled as they ever did, and then

[041-190] the woman, with a vigorous blow, strengthened by hate and famishing

[041-191] desire of freedom and a better human life, laid him in death upon the

[041-192] mossy floor.
