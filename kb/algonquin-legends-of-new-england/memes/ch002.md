[002-092] INTRODUCTION

[002-093] Among the six chief divisions of the red Indians of North America the

[002-094] most widely extended is the Algonquin. This people ranged from Labrador

[002-095] to the far South, from Newfoundland to the Rocky Mountains, speaking

[002-096] forty dialects, as the Hon. J. H. Trumbull has shown in his valuable

[002-097] work on the subject. Belonging to this division are the Micmacs of New

[002-098] Brunswick and the Passamaquoddy and Penobscot tribes of Maine, who with

[002-099] the St. Francis Indians of Canada and some smaller clans call

[002-100] themselves the Wabanaki, a word derived from a root signifying white or

[002-101] light, intimating that they live nearest to the rising sun or the east.

[002-102] In fact, the French-speaking St. Francis family, who are known _par

[002-103] eminence_ as "the Abenaki," translate the term by _point du

[002-104] jour_.

[002-105] The Wabanaki have in common the traditions of a grand mythology, the

[002-106] central figure of which is a demigod or hero, who, while he is always

[002-107] great, consistent, and benevolent, and never devoid of dignity,

[002-108] presents traits which are very much more like those of Odin and Thor,

[002-109] with not a little of Pantagruel, than anything in the characters of the

[002-110] Chippewa Manobozho, or the Iroquois Hiawatha. The name of this divinity

[002-111] is Glooskap, meaning, strangely enough, the Liar, because it is said

[002-112] that when he left earth, like King Arthur, for Fairyland, he promised

[002-113] to return, and has never done so. It is characteristic of the Norse

[002-114] gods that while they are grand they are manly, and combine with this a

[002-115] peculiarly domestic humanity. Glooskap is the Norse god intensified. He

[002-116] is, however, more of a giant; he grows to a more appalling greatness

[002-117] than Thor or Odin in his battles; when a _Kiawaqu'_, or Jotun,

[002-118] rises to the clouds to oppose him, Glooskap's head touches the stars,

[002-119] and scorning to slay so mean a foe like an equal, he kills him

[002-120] contemptuously with a light tap of his bow. But in the family circle he

[002-121] is the most benevolent of gentle heroes, and has his oft-repeated

[002-122] little standard jokes. Yet he never, like the Manobozho-Hiawatha of the

[002-123] Chippewas, becomes silly, cruel, or fantastic. He has his roaring revel

[002-124] with a brother giant, even as Thor went fishing in fierce fun with the

[002-125] frost god, but he is never low or feeble.

[002-126] Around Glooskap, who is by far the grandest and most Aryan-like

[002-127] character ever evolved from a savage mind, and who is more congenial to

[002-128] a reader of Shakespeare and Rabelais than any deity ever imagined out

[002-129] of Europe, there are found strange giants: some literal Jotuns of stone

[002-130] and ice, sorcerers who become giants like Glooskap, at will; the

[002-131] terrible Chenoo, a human being with an icy-stone heart, who has sunk to

[002-132] a cannibal and ghoul; all the weird monsters and horrors of the Eskimo

[002-133] mythology, witches and demons, inherited from the terribly black

[002-134] sorcery which preceded Shamanism, and compared to which the latter was

[002-135] like an advanced religion, and all the minor mythology of dwarfs and

[002-136] fairies. The Indian _m'teoulin_, or magician, distinctly taught

[002-137] that every created thing, animate or inanimate, had its indwelling

[002-138] spirit. Whatever had an _idea_ had a soul. Therefore the Wabanaki

[002-139] mythology is strangely like that of the Rosicrucians. But it created

[002-140] spirits for the terrible Arctic winters of the north, for the icebergs

[002-141] and frozen wastes, for the Northern Lights and polar bears. It made, in

[002-142] short, a mythology such as would be perfectly congenial to any one who

[002-143] has read and understood the Edda, Beowulf, and the Kalevala, with the

[002-144] wildest and oldest Norse sagas. But it is, as regards spirit and

[002-145] meaning, utterly and entirely unlike anything else that is American. It

[002-146] is not like the Mexican pantheon; it has not the same sounds, colors,

[002-147] or feelings; and though many of its incidents or tales are the same as

[002-148] those of the Chippewas, or other tribes, we still feel that there is an

[002-149] incredible difference in the spirit. Its ways are not as their ways.

[002-150] This Wabanaki mythology, which was that which gave a fairy, an elf, a

[002-151] naiad, or a hero to every rock and river and ancient hill in New

[002-152] England, is just the one of all others which is least known to the New

[002-153] Englanders. When the last Indian shall be in his grave, those who come

[002-154] after us will ask in wonder why we had no curiosity as to the romance

[002-155] of our country, and so much as to that of every other land on earth.

[002-156] Much is allowed to poets and painters, and no fault was found with Mr.

[002-157] Longfellow for attributing to the Iroquois Hiawatha the choice exploits

[002-158] of the Chippewa demi-devil Manobozho. It was "all Indian" to the

[002-159] multitude, and one name answered as well in poetry as another, at a

[002-160] time when there was very little attention paid to ethnology. So that a

[002-161] good poem resulted, it was of little consequence that the plot was a

[002-162] _melange_ of very different characters, and characteristics. And

[002-163] when, in connection with this, Mr. Longfellow spoke of the Chippewa

[002-164] tales as forming an Indian Edda, the term was doubtless in a poetic and

[002-165] very general sense permissible. But its want of literal truth seems to

[002-166] have deeply impressed the not generally over particular or accurate

[002-167] Schoolcraft, since his first remarks in the Introduction to the

[002-168] Hiawatha Legends are as follows:--

[002-169] "Where analogies are so general, there is a constant liability to

[002-170] mistakes. Of these foreign analogies of myth-lore, the least tangible,

[002-171] it is believed, is that which has been suggested with the Scandinavian

[002-172] mythology. That mythology is of so marked and peculiar a character that

[002-173] it has not been distinctly traced out of the great circle of tribes of

[002-174] the Indo-Germanic family. Odin and his terrific pantheon of war gods

[002-175] and social deities could only exist in the dreary latitudes of storms

[002-176] and fire which produce a Hecla and a Maelstrom. These latitudes have

[002-177] invariably produced nations whose influence has been felt in an

[002-178] elevating power over the world. From such a source the Indian could

[002-179] have derived none of him vague symbolisms and mental idiosyncrasies

[002-180] which have left him as he is found to-day, without a government and

[002-181] without a god."

[002-182] This is all perfectly true of the myths of Hiawat'ha-Manobozho. Nothing

[002-183] on earth could be more unlike the Norse legends than the "Indian Edda"

[002-184] of the Chippewas and Ottawas. But it was not known to this writer that

[002-185] there already existed in Northeastern America a stupendous mythology,

[002-186] derived from a land of storms and fire more terrible and wonderful than

[002-187] Iceland; nay, so terrible that Icelanders themselves were appalled by

[002-188] it. "This country," says the Abbe Morillot, "is the one most suggestive

[002-189] of superstition. Everything there, sea, earth, or heaven, is strange."

[002-190] The wild cries which rise from the depths of the caverned ice-hills,

[002-191] and are reechoed by the rocks, icebergs, or waves, were dreadful to

[002-192] Egbert Olafson in the seventeenth century. The interior is a desert

[002-193] without parallel for desolation. A frozen Sahara seen by Northern

[002-194] lightning and midnight suns is but a suggestion of this land. The sober

[002-195] Moravian missionary Crantz once only in his life rose to poetry, when

[002-196] more than a century ago he spoke of its scenery. Here then was the

[002-197] latitude of storm and fire required by Schoolcraft to produce something

[002-198] wilder and grander than he had ever found among Indians. And here

[002-199] indeed there existed all the time a cycle of mythological legends or

[002-200] poems such as he declared Indians incapable of producing. But strangest

[002-201] of all, this American mythology of the North, which has been the very

[002-202] last to become known to American readers, is literally so nearly like

[002-203] the Edda itself that as this work fully proves, there is hardly a song

[002-204] in the Norse collection which does not contain an incident found in the

[002-205] Indian poem-legends, while in several there are many such coincidences.

[002-206] Thus, in the Edda we are told that the first birth on earth was that of

[002-207] a giant girl and boy, begotten by the feet of a giant and born from his

[002-208] armpit. In the Wabanaki legends, the first birth was of Glooskap, the

[002-209] Good principle, and Malsum the Wolf, or Evil principle. The Wolf was

[002-210] born from his mother's armpit. He is sometimes male and sometimes

[002-211] female. His feet are male and female, and converse. We pass on only

[002-212] twelve lines in the Edda (Vafthrudnismal, 36) to be told that the wind

[002-213] is caused by a giant in eagle's plumage, who sits on a rock far in the

[002-214] north "at the end of heaven." This is simply and literally the

[002-215] _Wochowsen_ or Windblower of the Wabanaki word for word,--not the

[002-216] "Thunder-Bird" of the Western Indians. The second birth on earth,

[002-217] according to the Edda, was that of man. Odin found Ash and Elm "nearly

[002-218] powerless," and gave them sense. This was the first man and woman.

[002-219] According to the Indians of Maine, Glooskap made the first men from the

[002-220] _ash_-tree. They lived or were in it, "devoid of sense" till he

[002-221] gave it to them. It is to be observed that primevally among the Norse

[002-222] the _ash_ alone stood for man. So it goes on through the whole

[002-223] Edda, of which all the main incidents are to be found among the sagas

[002-224] of the Wabanaki. The most striking of these are the coincidences

[002-225] between _Lox_ (lynx, wolf, wolverine, badger, or raccoon, and

[002-226] sometimes man) and Loki. It is very remarkable indeed that the only two

[002-227] religions in the world which possess a devil in whom _mischief_

[002-228] predominates should also give to each the same adventures, if both did

[002-229] not come from the same source. In the Hymiskvida of the Edda, two

[002-230] giants go to fish for whales, and then have a contest which is actually

[002-231] one of heat against cold. This is so like a Micmac legend in every

[002-232] detail that about twenty lines are word for word the same in the Norse

[002-233] and Indian. The Micmac giants end their whale fishing by trying to

[002-234] freeze one another to death.
