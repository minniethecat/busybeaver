# The Great Dance of the Mikumwess

## Narrative

When a young man, aided by the Mikumwess and the magic pipe Glooskap had lent him, won the daughter of a distant chief by triumphing in a race against Wey-ad-esk (the Northern Lights) and in a diving contest against an Ukchigumooech (the Sea Duck), a wedding feast was held in his honor.

At the great dance which was held that evening at the wedding, the Mikumwess astonished all who beheld him. As he danced around the circle upon the very hard beaten floor, they saw his feet sink deeper at every step, and ever deeper as the dance went on — ploughing the ground up into high, uneven ridges, forming a trench as he went, until at length only his head was to be seen (013-035).

This ended the dancing for that night, since the ground was no longer to be danced upon by anybody except wizards and witches (013-050). The ordinary mortals, awed by this display, withdrew from the circle, recognizing that they had witnessed something beyond the natural order.

## The Magical Context

The Mikumwess' dance is not merely a feat of strength but a demonstration of _m'teoulin_ — the magical power that distinguishes sorcerers and spirit-beings from ordinary humans. The ability to walk through solid ground as if through snow is a recognized sign of the _angakok_, or sorcerer, in both Algonquin and Eskimo folklore (013-042). Among the Passamaquoddy, it is still told that there is a living _m'teoulin_ who can walk up to his knees in a floor or paved street (013-047).

The dance caps a series of supernatural trials. Before the feast, the young man had already raced the Northern Lights around the world — the Chain Lightning returned ere noon, not out of breath, having circled the entire earth, while the Northern Lights trembled and quivered with fatigue, having turned back before completing the circuit (013-016). The Sea Duck rose for breath after diving, but the Loon remained submerged for hours before surfacing victorious (013-029). These trials, and the great dance that followed, were all, as Glooskap later revealed, orchestrated by the Master himself — "in all they had experienced he had been busy, and in all their triumphs he had had a hand" (013-097).

## Participants

- **The Mikumwess** — The forest elf who performed the trench-making dance
- **The Young Man** — The bridegroom, who won his wife through supernatural trials
- **Wey-ad-esk (Northern Lights)** — The defeated racer
- **Ukchigumooech (Sea Duck)** — The defeated diver
- **The Old Chief** — Father of the bride, who set the trials
- **[[glooskap|Glooskap]]** — Who later revealed that he had guided all events

## Significance

The great dance episode illustrates the Algonquin understanding of magical power as something tangible and physically manifest — the Mikumwess does not merely claim to have power; the earth itself yields before him. The detail that the dancing must end because the ground is now "no longer to be danced upon by anybody except wizards and witches" (013-050) establishes a boundary between the mundane and the magical that persists after the event. The physical transformation of the dance floor serves as a permanent marker: this place has been touched by _m'teoulin_.

The dance also functions within the narrative as the climactic display before the departure. After the dance, the young man, his wife, and the Mikumwess sail home, encountering further magical trials — a sorcerous storm, the giant Beaver Quahbeet, and the giant Skunk A-bekk-thee-lo — all of which the Mikumwess defeats before Glooskap reveals his guiding hand (013-066).
