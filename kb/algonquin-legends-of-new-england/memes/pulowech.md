**Pulowech** — the Partridge in the Micmac tongue, called _Mitchihess_ by the Passamaquoddy — is a hero-figure in Algonquin mythology who is explicitly identified as "a type of Glooskap" and, like him, "makes war on wicked sorcerers" (009-127). His adventures are recorded in two major story cycles: "The Adventures of the Great Hero Pulowech, or the Partridge" and a series of shorter tales collected by Leland. He embodies the complex Algonquin hero type — a mixture of benevolence, sorcerous power, and a harsh, wolverine-like possessiveness.

## Overview

Pulowech is a Micmac culture hero who shares the fundamental characteristics of [[Glooskap]]: he battles cannibal sorcerers, commands magical powers including divination and transformation, and serves as a protector figure. His name is toponymically linked to Pulewech Munegoo (Partridge Island), the site of the encounter between Glooskap and [[Kitpooseagunow]]. The Passamaquoddy name Mitchihess confirms the figure's broad distribution across Wabanaki tribal traditions.

Leland notes that the story of the sorceress who tests her suitor's wit is told of Pulewech, "who is a type of Glooskap" (009-127) — a direct assertion that Pulowech represents a regional or narrative variant of the culture hero complex. His adventures range from romantic comedy (capturing a sea-maiden wife) to grim revenge tragedy (avenging her murder by cannibal sorcerers) to magical contests of wit and transformation.

The name Pulowech is attested in both Micmac and Passamaquoddy sources (037-147). The place-name Pulewech Munegoo (Partridge Island) preserves his mythological association with that location, where Glooskap met Kitpooseagunow (011-053).

### Character and Temperament

Pulowech is a figure of marked contradictions. He is a protector and hero, yet Leland describes him as one who "held to his own in all things like a wolverine" and was "the cruel Pulowech, the hard-hearted" (038-105). This duality — benevolent toward his dependents, ruthless toward enemies — is characteristic of the Algonquin hero type, which does not demand moral perfection of its protagonists.

His sorcerous abilities include divination by water-filled dish (the _woltes_ of divination), transformation, and control over animals. He is described as "being himself addicted to sorcery" (038-029), a trait that connects him to the broader Algonquin tradition of _m'teoulin_ (spiritual power) wielded by [[Glooskap]] and other major figures.

## Role in Legends

### The Capture of the Sea-Maiden

Living in a wigwam by a beautiful lake with his companion Wejek (the Tree Partridge), Pulowech one winter day spies three sea-maidens — fairy beings who dwell in the water — sitting on the ice braiding their hair (037-150). These beings, Leland notes, "were plentier of old than they are now — to our sorrow be it said, for they were good company for the one who could get them" (037-155).

Pulowech's first attempt to catch them by stealth fails when one cries the warning: "_Ne miha skedap_!" — "I see a man!" — and they plunge into the icy water of the Bay of Fundy (037-163). Undeterred, he adopts a hunter's stratagem:

> "Though Pulowech had never hunted for sea-girls, yet he had fished for seals, who are greatly akin unto them, being almost as slippery; and wotting well that no man hath the mitten till he is refused thirty times and many more, he went about it in another wise" (037-163).

He strews fir boughs about as if blown by the wind, hides behind them, and makes a sudden dart. This time he catches the hair-string of the fairest maiden — her _sakultobee_, or magical charm — without which she cannot live (038-029). Taking the string home and tying it above his sleeping place, he waits. Before long the maiden comes to him and remains as his wife.

### The Cannibal Sorcerers

Pulowech, knowing the woods to be prowled by "knaves of the same stamp" — robbers, ravishers, and cannibals — warns his bride to keep within doors and open to none (038-029). But a wicked _boo-oin_ (pow-wow), subtle and crafty, comes at midnight and imitates the voices of her family:

> "'O sister, we have come from afar!' they cried. 'We missed you, and have followed you. Let us in!' And yet again she heard a sad and very earnest voice, and it was that of her old mother, crying, '_N'toos', n'toos', pantahdooe!_' — 'My daughter! my daughter! open unto me!'" (038-066).

When the voice of her "dear old father" cries that he is cold, she can resist no longer. Opening the door, the sorcerers "springing on her like mad wolves, dragged her away and devoured her. They did not leave two of her little bones one with another" (038-074).

Wejek, the Tree Partridge, rashly pursues and is himself slain. Pulowech, returning last, uses magical divination — filling a _woltes_ (wooden dish) with water, charming it with a spell, and leaving it overnight — to learn the truth: in the morning the dish is half full of blood (038-079).

### The Magical Contests

The Pulowech cycle includes several magical contests that demonstrate the hero's sorcerous power:

**The Squirrel**: When a rude man repeatedly offers Pulowech a dish of food only to snatch it back, the hero summons _Meeko_ the squirrel, which he transforms into a dog. Tapping it on the back, Pulowech causes it to grow to the size of "the largest of all beasts that ever were seen" — a creature that in another tale is described as large as a small whale (038-090).

**The Evil Sorceress**: In a tale told of Pulewech (009-127), the hero finds himself in the country of evil sorcerers — the land of the Northern Lights — where he must match wits with a sorceress who tests his intelligence. The story parallels Glooskap's own adventures among the Northern sorcerers, further confirming Pulowech's status as a variant of the culture hero.

## Key Stories

### The Adventures of the Great Hero Pulowech

> **Source**: Micmac, collected by Leland (037-142 through 038-105)

Pulowech lives with Wejek the Tree Partridge by a beautiful lake. In winter he spies three sea-maidens on the ice and, after two attempts, captures the hair-string of the fairest — her life-charm. She comes to him as his wife. But evil cannibal sorcerers, imitating the voices of her family at midnight, trick her into opening the door, then devour her. Wejek pursues and is slain. Pulowech discovers the truth through water-divination — his dish fills with blood overnight — and avenges the murders through a series of magical contests, including the transformation of a squirrel into a giant beast.

## Cultural Significance

Pulowech exemplifies the Algonquin hero as a figure who is not morally simplified but complex and contradictory. He loves his wife and avenges her — yet he is also "the cruel Pulowech, the hard-hearted" (038-105). This duality reflects a mythological sensibility that does not demand that heroes be saints. The hero's power is morally neutral; what matters is how it is deployed.

The sea-maiden episode belongs to a widespread international tale type (the Swan Maiden / Selkie complex) in which a mortal man captures a supernatural wife by seizing her magical garment or charm. The Algonquin version adds the uniquely North American element of the _boo-oin_ — the cannibal sorcerer — as antagonist, connecting romantic narrative to the culture hero's larger war against supernatural predation.

The explicit identification of Pulowech as "a type of Glooskap" (009-127) suggests that the Partridge represents a regional Micmac variant of the culture hero, adapted to local geography (Partridge Island) and the specific fauna of the Acadian forest. Like [[Glooskap]], he defeats sorcerers who devour the innocent, commands magical transformation, and embodies the paradoxical combination of protector and trickster.
