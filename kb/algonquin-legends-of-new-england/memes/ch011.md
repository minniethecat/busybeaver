---[011-001] Now the third went merrily onward alone, when all at once it flashed

[011-002] upon his mind that Glooskap had given him a present, and without the

[011-003] least heed to the injunction that he was to wait till he had reached

[011-004] his home drew out the root and ate it; and scarce had he done this ere

[011-005] he realized that he possessed the power of uttering the weird and

[011-006] mystic sound to absolute perfection. And as it rang o'er many a hill

[011-007] and dale, and woke the echoes of the distant hills, until it was

[011-008] answered by the solemn owl, he felt that it was indeed wonderful. So he

[011-009] walked on gayly, trumpeting as he went, over hill and vale, happy as a

[011-010] bird.

[011-011] But by and by he began to weary of himself. Seeing a deer he drew an

[011-012] arrow and stealing silently to the game was just about to shoot, when

[011-013] despite himself the wild, unearthly sound broke forth like a demon's

[011-014] warble. The deer bounded away, and the young man cursed! And when he

[011-015] reached Old Town, half dead with hanger, he was worth little to make

[011-016] laughter, though the honest Indians at first did not fail to do so, and

[011-017] thereby somewhat cheered his heart. But as the days went on they

[011-018] wearied of him, and, life becoming a burden, he went into the woods and

[011-019] slew himself. And the evil spirit of the night-air even Bumole,

[011-020] [Footnote: For an account of Bumole, or Pamola, see the chapter on

[011-021] Supernatural Beings. Bumole seems to have been the personification

[011-022] of the night-hawk.] or Pamola, from whom came the gift, swooped down

[011-023] from the clouds and bore him away to 'Lahmkekqu', the dwelling place of

[011-024] darkness, and he was no more heard of among men.

[011-025] As regards the destruction of the giants by Glooskap, it may be

[011-026] observed that the same tradition exists among the Six Nations. Cusick

[011-027] tells us that about 1250 years before Columbus discovered America a

[011-028] powerful tribe called Otne-yar-heh, that is, Stone Giants, who were

[011-029] ravenous cannibals, overran the country, and nearly exterminated the

[011-030] inhabitants. These Stone Giants practiced, themselves in rolling on the

[011-031] sand; by this means their bodies became hard. Then Tas-enyawa-gen, the

[011-032] Holder of the Heavens, came to earth as a giant, and being made their

[011-033] chief, led them into a hollow, where he overwhelmed them with rocks.

[011-034] Only one escaped to the far North. The reader will recognize in these

[011-035] the Chenoos, or Kewahqu', who cover themselves with pitch and roll on

[011-036] the ground. But no one can deny that, while that which Cusick narrates

[011-037] has much in common with the mythology of the Wabanaki, it is much less

[011-038] like that of the Edda; that Indian grotesqueness has in it greatly

[011-039] perverted an original: and finally, that it certainly occupies a

[011-040] position midway between the mythology of the Northeastern Algonquins

[011-041] and that of the Chippewas, Ottawas, and other Western tribes.

[011-042] Examination shows this in every story. Thus the Wabanaki warrior makes

[011-043] his bow infallible in aim by stringing it with a cord made of his

[011-044] sister's hair. This is Norse, as it was of old Latin. But in the

[011-045] Iroquois the young man "adorns his arms with the hairs of his sister."

[011-046] Here the tradition has begun to weaken.

[011-047] It may be interesting to visitors to Niagara to know that the army of

[011-048] Stone Giants crossed the river during their journey just below the

[011-049] Falls.

[011-050] _How Glooskap had a great Frolic with Kitpooseagunow, a Mighty Giant

[011-051] who caught a Whale._

[011-052] (Micmac.)

[011-053] _N'kah-nee-oo_. In the old time (P.) Glooskap came to Pulewech

[011-054] Munegoo (M., Partridge Island), and here he met with Kitpooseagunow,

[011-055] [Footnote: _Kitpooseagunow_, "one born after his mother's death," is

[011-056] a magician-giant, who plays in the Algonquin mythology a part only inferior

[011-057] to that of Glooskap, whom he in every way resembles. Both are benevolent,

[011-058] both make war on wicked sorcerers and evil wild beasts, and both, finally,

[011-059] are much like Gargantua and Pantagruel in their sense of humor. They are

[011-060] sometimes made the heroes of the same adventure in different stories. The

[011-061] true origin of the name, according to Mr. Rand, is as follows: "After a

[011-062] cow moose or caribou has been killed, her calf is sometimes taken out

[011-063] alive, and reared by hand. As may be supposed, the calf is very easily

[011-064] tamed. The animal thus born is called _Kitpooseagunow_, and from

[011-065] this a verb is formed which denotes the act."--_Legends of the Mic Macs,

[011-066] Old Dominion Monthly_, 1871.

[011-067] This giant was also called the Protector of the Oppressed. He probably

[011-068] represents the Glooskap myth in another form.] whose mother had been

[011-069] slain by a fearful cannibal giant. And it was against these that he

[011-070] made war all his life long, as did Glooskap. Whence it came to pass

[011-071] that they loved one another, which did not at all hinder them from

[011-072] having a hearty and merry encounter, in which they missed but little of

[011-073] killing one or the other, and all in the best natured way in the world.

[011-074] [Illustration: Glooskap and Keanke spearing the whale]

[011-075] Now, having come to Pulewech Munegoo, the lord of men and beasts was

[011-076] entertained by Kitpooseagunow. And when the night came, he who was born

[011-077] after his mother's death said to his guest, "Let us go on the sea in a

[011-078] canoe and catch whales by torchlight;" to which Glooskap, nothing loath,

[011-079] consented, for he was a mighty fisherman, as are all the Wabanaki of

[011-080] the seacoast. [Footnote: Glooskap would seem to have been the prototype

[011-081] of the giant fisher so well known in song:--

[011-082] "His rod was made of a sturdy oak,

[011-083] His line, a cable, in storms ne'er broke;

[011-084] He baited his hook with a dragon's tail,

[011-085] And sat on a rock and bobbed for whale."

[011-086] A fabulous monster, apparently identical with the dragon, is common in

[011-087] Micmac stories.]

[011-088] Now when they came to the beach there were only great rocks, lying here

[011-089] and there; but Kitpooseagunow, lifting the largest of these, put it on

[011-090] his head, and it became a canoe. And picking up another, it turned to a

[011-091] paddle, while a long splinter which he split from a ledge seemed to be

[011-092] a spear. Then Glooskap asked, "Who shall sit in the stern and paddle,

[011-093] and who will take the spear?" Kitpooseagunow said "That will I." So

[011-094] Glooskap paddled, and soon the canoe passed over a mighty whale; in all

[011-095] the great sea there was not his like; but he who held the spear sent it

[011-096] like a thunderbolt down into the waters, and as the handle rose again

[011-097] to sight he snatched it up, and the great fish was caught. And as

[011-098] Kitpooseagunow whirled it on high, the whale, roaring, touched the

[011-099] clouds. Then taking him from the point, the fisher tossed him into the

[011-100] bark as if he had been a trout. And the giants laughed; the sound of

[011-101] their laughter was heard all over the land of the Wabanaki. And being

[011-102] at home, the host took a stone knife and split the whale, and threw one

[011-103] half to the guest Glooskap, and they roasted each his piece over the

[011-104] fire and ate it.

[011-105] Now the Master, having marked the light, which was long in the heaven

[011-106] after the sun went down, said, "The sky is red; we shall have a cold

[011-107] night." And his host understood him well, and saw that he would make it

[011-108] cold by magic. So he bade Marten bring in all the fuel he could find,

[011-109] and all there was of the oil of a porpoise; and this oil he so

[011-110] multiplied by magic that there was ten times more of it. And they sat

[011-111] them down and smoked, and told tales of old times; but it grew ever

[011-112] colder and colder. And at midnight, when all was burnt out, Marten

[011-113] froze to death, and then the grandmother, but the two giants smoked on,

[011-114] and laughed and talked. Then the rocks out-of-doors split with the

[011-115] cold, the great trees in the forest split; the sound thereof was as

[011-116] thunder, but the Master and he who was born after his mother's death

[011-117] laughed even louder. And so they sat until the sun rose. Then Glooskap

[011-118] said to the dead woman, "_Noogume, numchahse_!" (M.) "Grandmother,

[011-119] arise!" and to his boy, "_Abistanooch numchahse_!" "Marten,

[011-120] arise!" and they arose, and went about their work.

[011-121] And the morning being bright, they went forth far into the forest to

[011-122] find game. But they got very little, for they caught only one small

[011-123] beaver, and Glooskap gave up his share of this to Kitpooseagunow. And

[011-124] he, taking the skin, fastened it to his garter, whence it dangled like

[011-125] the skin of a mouse at the knee of a tall man. But as he went on

[011-126] through the woods the skin grew larger and larger and larger, till it

[011-127] broke away by its own weight. Then the giant twisted a mighty sapling

[011-128] into a withe, and fastened it around his waist. But it still grew apace

[011-129] as he went on, till, trailing after, it tore down all the forest,

[011-130] pulling away the trees, so that Kitpooseagunow left a clean, fair road

[011-131] behind him. [Footnote: Many of these stories have received later

[011-132] additions, which can be detected by their occurring only in single

[011-133] versions of them. In the story of Kitpooseagunow (Rand's manuscript)

[011-134] the giants arrive at a "large town," and go to a "store," where they

[011-135] sell the skin for all the money, goods, houses, and lands which, the

[011-136] merchant possesses. "And the skin was so heavy that it took the greater

[011-137] part of the day to weigh it."]

[011-138] And when the night came on they fished again, as they had done before;

[011-139] and again it was said, but this time by the host, "The sky is red; we

[011-140] shall have a cold night." So they heaped up wood more than the first

[011-141] time, but now it was far colder. And soon the boy was dead, and the

[011-142] grandmother also lay frozen. But when the sun rose the Master brought

[011-143] them back to life, and, bidding good-by to Kitpooseagunow, went his

[011-144] way. [Footnote: It is possible that there is a version of this story in

[011-145] which Glooskap kills his friend with frost, and then revives him. In

[011-146] one story it is a _frozen stream_, incarnate as a man, which

[011-147] attempts in vain to freeze Glooskap. The extraordinary manner in which

[011-148] host and guest, or even intimate friends, endeavor to _kill_ one

[011-149] another in the most good-natured rivalry, is of constant occurrence in

[011-150] the Eskimo legends. It is not infrequent among our own backwoods or

[011-151] frontier-men.

[011-152] The stone-canoe occurs in Eskimo legends (_vide_ Rink), as it does

[011-153] in those of all American Indians.]

[011-154] The most striking feature, however, of this legend is its Norse-like

[011-155] breadth or grandeur and its genial humor, which are very remarkable

[011-156] characteristics for the fictions of savages. Its resemblance to the

[011-157] Scandinavian tales is, if accidental, very remarkable. The two heroes

[011-158] are, like Thor and Odin, giant heroes who make war on Jotuns and

[011-159] Trolls; that is, giant-like sorcerers. It is their profession; they

[011-160] live in it. No one can read Beowulf or the Eddas without being struck

[011-161] by the great resemblance between Grendel, the hideous, semi-human night

[011-162] prowler, and the Kewahqu', a precisely similar monster, who rises from

[011-163] the depths of waters to wantonly murder man. I do not recall any two

[011-164] beings in any other two disconnected mythologies so strangely similar.

[011-165] The fishing for the whale recalls that which is told in the Older Edda

[011-166] (Hymiskrida, 21), where Hymir succeeds in hooking two of these fish:--

[011-167] "Then he and Hymir rowed out to sea. Thor rowed oft with two oars, and

[011-168] so powerfully that the giant was obliged to acknowledge they were

[011-169] speeding very fast. _He himself rowed at the prow_."

[011-170] If the reader will compare this account of the Edda with the Micmac

[011-171] story, he cannot fail to be struck with the great resemblance between

[011-172] them. It is even specified in both that the hero, though a guest,

[011-173] paddles. And in both instances the host catches a whale. Now compare

[011-174] with this the legend of Manobozho-Hiawatha, who merely catches the

[011-175] great sunfish, and is swallowed by it. Does it not seem as if the

[011-176] Western Indians had here borrowed from the Micmacs, and the Micmacs

[011-177] from the Norse? Whether this was done directly or through the Eskimo is

[011-178] as yet a problem. It may also be noted that both in the Edda and in the

[011-179] Micmac story, it is declared that one of the giants picked up the boat

[011-180] and carried it.
