# Oochigeaskw

**Oochigeaskw** (Passamaquoddy for "the rough-faced girl" or "burnt-faced girl") is the heroine of a Micmac legend recorded by Charles Leland under the title "The Invisible One." The tale, set in a village on the shore of a lake called _Nameskeek' oodun Kuspemku_, is a Wabanaki parallel to the European Cinderella story, with elements Leland identified as an "old solar myth."

## Identity and Attributes

Oochigeaskw is the youngest of three daughters of a widower. Unlike her sisters, she is "very small, weak, and often ill." Her eldest sister subjects her to systematic abuse:

> "the other would burn her hands and face with hot coals; yes, her whole body was scarred with the marks made by torture, so that people called her _Oochigeaskw_ (the rough-faced girl)" [040-153]

When her father inquires about her injuries, the elder sister lies, claiming the girl disobeyed and "fallen in" the fire. The abuse extends to deprivation: Oochigeaskw "had always run barefoot" [041-008], owning nothing but rags.

## Role in Narratives

### The Invisible One

At the end of the village stands a lodge in which dwells "a being who was always invisible." His sister attends him, and it is known that any girl who can truly **see** him may marry him. Many try; all fail—they claim to see him but cannot answer the sister's test questions correctly, revealing their pretense.

Oochigeaskw's two elder sisters "clad themselves in their finest" and attempt the trial, but they too lie and are dismissed.

### The Heroine's Preparation

Undeterred by poverty and ridicule, Oochigeaskw prepares herself:

> "having no clothes beyond a few paltry rags, the poor creature went forth and got herself from the woods a few sheets of birch bark, of which she made a dress, putting some figures on the bark" [041-008]

She fashions a complete outfit—petticoat, loose gown, cap, leggings, and handkerchief—from birch bark, and dons her father's oversized moccasins. Her journey to the Invisible One's lodge is met with "one long storm of ridicule and hisses, yells and hoots," but:

> "she went on, for she was greatly resolved; it may be that some spirit had inspired her."

### The Seeing

The sister of the Invisible One "knew more than the mere outside of things" and receives Oochigeaskw kindly. At the lake, when asked if she sees the Invisible One, Oochigeaskw answers:

> "Truly I do,—and He is wonderful."

When asked "What is his sled-string?"—the question that had exposed all previous pretenders—she replies:

> "It is the Rainbow."

And to "What is his bow-string?":

> "His bowstring is _Ketaksoowowcht_" (the Spirits' Road, the Milky Way).

Leland notes the grandeur of this reply: the Micmac version reduces it to "a _piece_ of a rainbow," but the Passamaquoddy original elevates it to cosmic scale.

### Transformation and Marriage

The sister bathes Oochigeaskw:

> "as she washed all the scars disappeared from face and body. Her hair grew again; it was very long, and like a blackbird's wing. Her eyes were like stars. In all the world was no such beauty."

She is given a wedding garment, seated in the wife's place, and when the Invisible One enters, he smiles and says: "_Wajoolkoos!_ So we are found out!" She becomes his wife.

## Cultural Context

Leland identifies the tale as "a very old myth"—an "Indian Cupid and Psyche legend" combining a solar myth with the Cinderella motif, possibly influenced by "a Canadian-French source." The Passamaquoddy variant preserves grander cosmic imagery (the Rainbow, the Milky Way as the Spirits' Road) than the Micmac retelling, which "belittled and reduced in every way" the celestial scale.

The Invisible One's tutelary animal is the Moose (_Team_), and the Passamaquoddy variant diverges significantly from the Micmac: in one version, the story concerns "a great being, a mighty hunter, who had a wife, of wonderful magic gifts, and a boy; and the child became blind."
