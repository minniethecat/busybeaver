# How Glooskap Strode with the Mighty Wasis

## Narrative

Now it came to pass when Glooskap had conquered all his enemies — even the Kewahqu', who were giants and sorcerers, and the m'teoulin, and the Medecolin, and Pamola, and the Chenoo — he began to grow proud and boasted that he had subdued everything in the world (017-096).

But one day a woman laughed when she heard him speak thus. "Are you quite sure, Master," she asked, "that you have conquered everything?" Glooskap replied that he was certain. "There is one thing," she said, "that remains untouched by your power." Glooskap demanded to know what this thing might be. "It is the mighty Wasis," she replied, "and there he sits; and I assure you, Master, you cannot make him do anything" (017-107).

Now Wasis was the Baby. And he sat on the floor sucking a piece of maple-sugar, utterly indifferent to the great being who stood before him (017-109). Glooskap approached the child and, in his most commanding voice, ordered Wasis to come to him. But Wasis sat still and sucked his maple-sugar (017-117).

Then the Master frowned and spoke terribly, and ordered Wasis to come to him. But Wasis only smiled and sucked his maple-sugar, and did not move. Glooskap grew angry. He drew himself up to his full height, and his face became terrible to behold. He shouted the great songs of power that could raise the dead and scare away the devils. And Wasis sat and looked on admiringly, and gurgled, and sucked his maple-sugar, but he would not come (017-118, 017-123).

Then Glooskap tried every art he knew. He danced the terrible dances, he sang the songs that made the mountains tremble, he called upon all the powers of the earth and sky. But Wasis only laughed and kicked his little feet and reached for more maple-sugar. Finally, utterly exhausted and humiliated, Glooskap gave it up in despair. And Wasis, sitting on the floor, cried "Goo-goo!" — which means, in the language of babies, that he had won (017-126).

> "It is the mighty _Wasis_," she replied, "and there he sits; and I assure you, Master, you cannot make him do anything." (017-107)

And so it is that the greatest power in the universe must sometimes bow to the smallest will. To this day, when a baby cries "Goo-goo!" and will not be comforted, the Passamaquoddy remember that even Glooskap could not conquer the mighty Wasis.

## Significance

The Wasis episode occupies a unique position in the Glooskap cycle as the only narrative in which the Master suffers unequivocal defeat. Its humor — the cosmic hero laid low by an infant sucking maple-sugar — does not diminish its philosophical depth. The story teaches a profound lesson about the limits of power: that will, even when smallest, can resist the greatest force. It functions as a caution against hubris, coming immediately after the narrator observes that Glooskap had conquered all his enemies and was growing proud (017-096). The episode also humanizes Glooskap, making him relatable to his worshippers — if even the Master cannot command a baby, no parent need feel ashamed when their own child defies them.
