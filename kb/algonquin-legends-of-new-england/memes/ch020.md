---[020-001] (Micmac and Passamaquoddy.)

[020-002] _Wee-zig-yik-keseyook_. "Of old times." Far back in the forest, by

[020-003] a brook, dwelt two young men, Abistanooch, the Marten, and Team, the

[020-004] Moose. Of these each had a wigwam, and therewith a grandmother who kept

[020-005] house. And Team hunted and worked industriously, but Master Marten was

[020-006] greatly _moalet_ (M.), which signifies one who liveth upon his

[020-007] neighbors, depending on their good nature, even as he that planteth

[020-008] corn and beans depends upon the pleasant smiles of the sun; whence it

[020-009] came to pass that wherever victuals were in store there too his presence

[020-010] did greatly abound.

[020-011] Now it happened that one day Team, the Moose, had killed a bear, and

[020-012] brought home a single load of the meat, leaving the rest to be looked

[020-013] after anon. And being thrifty, and not caring to feed those who fed him

[020-014] not, neither did they thank, he said unto himself, and also to his

[020-015] grandmother, "Truly, the eyes of Marten shall not see this thing, his

[020-016] nose shall not smell thereof, neither shall his tongue taste it; so let

[020-017] not the tidings of our good luck go forth from the wigwam." "Yes,"

[020-018] replied the old woman, "and well and wisely thou speakest, my son. But

[020-019] we have this day broken our kettle, while Marten has brought in a new

[020-020] one. Behold, I will go and borrow it, and having cooked in it I will

[020-021] wash and wipe it, so that there shall be no sign of what we did

[020-022] therewith, and so return it."

[020-023] Now, this was done, but he who is _moalet_ and a haunter of feasts

[020-024] is like a hunter of beasts: he knows well from a small sign where there

[020-025] is a large load, and the borrowing of kettles means the boiling of

[020-026] victuals therein. So having in him somewhat of sorcery, he did but step

[020-027] to his friend's wigwam, and, peeping through a crevice, saw a great

[020-028] store of bear's meat. And when the grandmother of Moose came unto him

[020-029] to return the kettle, just as she entered the lodge there arose from it

[020-030] a savory steam, and looking in it was full of well-cooked food. And

[020-031] Marten thanked her greatly, yet she, being put to shame, fled to her

[020-032] own home. But Moose said it was no matter, so the next day they went to

[020-033] the woods together, and all was well.

[020-034] Now it befell Marten, as it might have befallen any other man, that one

[020-035] day he came to a distant and lonely lake in the mountains. Yet there,

[020-036] stepping softly as a cat behind the rocks bung with grapevines, he

[020-037] heard laughing and splashing, and a pleasant sound as of girls' voices.

[020-038] So, peeping carefully, he saw many maids merrily bathing in the lake:

[020-039] and these were of the fairy race, who dwell in deep waters and dark

[020-040] caves, and keep away from mankind. And seeing their garments lying on

[020-041] the shore, and beholding among the damsels one whom he desired to

[020-042] obtain, [Footnote: There are many of these stories which indicate

[020-043] passionate and deeply seated attachment, but I never once heard a real

[020-044] Indian say that man or woman loved, though they have words which fully

[020-045] express it. "He wanted her" is the nearest approach to tenderness which

[020-046] I have ever heard from them. This is not the result of a want of

[020-047] feeling, but of the suppression of all manifestation of it, to which

[020-048] every red man is trained from earliest infancy.] Marten quietly slipped

[020-049] along unseen, as all of his species can do, till he had the clothes in

[020-050] his hands. For being tinctured with magic and learned in the lore of

[020-051] all kind of goblins, elves, and witches, Master Marten knew that when

[020-052] Naiads are naked and a man has their garments he holds them at his

[020-053] mercy. For in the apparel lies their fairy power; and if you doubt it,

[020-054] do but give it a trial and see for yourself!

[020-055] And having done this, the merry fellow ran inland with a brave whoop,

[020-056] which the fairies hearing, they in a great rage ran after the ravisher

[020-057] of their robes. But she whom he desired outstripped the rest, and when

[020-058] she approached him he did but tap her lightly on the head with a small

[020-059] stick, according to a certain ancient prescription followed in Fairy-land,

[020-060] which makes of a woman a wife; whereupon she, according to the

[020-061] antique rite, being astonished to find herself so, suddenly married,

[020-062] fainted dead away, and was carried off in peace. And as for the clothes

[020-063] of the others, the Marten gave them back without taking fee or rewards.

[020-064] Then Team, the Moose, who was a good soul, but not wise above all the

[020-065] world, coming home and finding Marten married, wished also for a wife.

[020-066] And having heard all the tale, he said, "Well, if it is no harder than

[020-067] that, 'tis as easy as sucking a honeysuckle, and I am as good as

[020-068] married." And going to the pond in the mountains, among the rocks and

[020-069] behind the grapevines, he too beheld the virgins jumping, flapping,

[020-070] splashing, and mischieving merrily, like mad minxes, in the water;

[020-071] whereat he, being all of a rage, as it were, caught up the clothes of

[020-072] these, poor maids and ran; she whom he most admired catching up with

[020-073] him. And being resolved to do the thing thoroughly, he grappled up a

[020-074] great club and gave her a bang on her small head, which stunned her

[020-075] indeed, and that forever, inasmuch as she was slain outright. So the

[020-076] Moose remained unmarried.

[020-077] Now Team was one of the kind not uncommon, in this world, who hold that

[020-078] if any other man has or gets more than they have, then they are deeply

[020-079] wronged. And it had come to pass that Master Marten, finding that his

[020-080] wife yearned greatly for the society of her sisters, offered to take

[020-081] yet another of them in marriage, merely to oblige his wife; for in such

[020-082] a kind of benevolence he was one of the best souls that ever lived, and

[020-083] rather than have trouble in the family he would have wedded all the

[020-084] pretty girls in the country. So going as before to the pond in the

[020-085] mountains, among the rocks and behind the grapevines, he, by the same

[020-086] device, captured yet another fairy, whom, taking home, he wedded.

[020-087] Yet Team took this sadly to heart, and willed that Marten should give

[020-088] him this last spouse, to which Marten would in nowise agree. Truly,

[020-089] Team argued earnestly that as he had no wife, and no wisdom wherewith

[020-090] to win one, of course he must have one of Marten's, or that Marten

[020-091] should go and get him one. To which Marten replied that Moose might

[020-092] skin his own skunks, and fish for his own minnows, and also paddle his

[020-093] own canoe to the devil, if it so pleased him,--all of these being

[020-094] approved Indian sayings of high and racy antiquity. Whereupon Team

[020-095] sought to persuade Marten with a club, who gave a soft answer by

[020-096] shooting a flint-headed arrow through Team's scalp-lock; and this

[020-097] friendship they continued for many days, passing their evenings in

[020-098] manufacturing missiles, and the mornings in sending them one at the

[020-099] other.

[020-100] Now the fairy water-wives, not being accustomed to this kind of intimacy,

[020-101] sought to subtract themselves from it. So one morning, when Marten and

[020-102] Team were most industriously endeavoring to effect mutual murder, the

[020-103] two wives of the former fled afar to seek fortune, and succeeded therein

[020-104] to perfection. And it came to pass when the sun had set and the voice of

[020-105] Bumole, the Spirit of Night, was heard afar on high, and Nibauchset (P.),

[020-106] the Night-Walker, shone over all, that the two brides lay in an oak

[020-107] opening of the forest, and looked at P'ses'muk, the Stars, and talked

[020-108] about them even as children might do. And one said to the other, "If

[020-109] those Stars be men, which would you have for a husband?" "By my faith,"

[020-110] replied the other," it should be that little red, twinkling fellow, for

[020-111] I like the little stars best." "And I," said the other, "will wed the

[020-112] Wisawaioo P'ses'm (P.), the Great Yellow Star, for I love the large

[020-113] stars." And, saying this in jest, they fell asleep.

[020-114] But many a word spoken in jest is recalled in earnest, as these brides

[020-115] learned when they awoke, and found themselves married again in the

[020-116] Indian manner, at only a word. For she who had wished for the Great

[020-117] Yellow Shining Star, as she opened her eyes, heard a man's voice say,

[020-118] "Take care, or you will upset my war-paint!" [Footnote: Sekroon (red

[020-119] ochre).] And lo, there lay by her side a great and handsome man, very

[020-120] noble, with large and lustrous eyes. [Footnote: In the Passamaquoddy

[020-121] version of this tale, given me by Tomah Josephs, the brides awake in

[020-122] Star-Land. The husbands are both elderly men, and he who is the Yellow

[020-123] Star has bright yellow corners to his eyes, while the other has red. In

[020-124] another the Yellow Star is called Wobeyu, the White. While they are all

[020-125] distinctly forms of one tale, the three differ so much that I have had

[020-126] great difficulty in reconstituting what appears to be the Original

[020-127] legend.] Then the other, as she awoke and stirred, heard a little feeble,

[020-128] cracked voice crying, "Take care, or you will spill my eye-water!"

[020-129] [Footnote: _Nebijegwode_ (eye medicine, M.)] And by her was the

[020-130] smaller star, whom she had chosen; but he was a weak-looking old fellow,

[020-131] with little red, twinkling eyes. And as they had chosen so it came unto

[020-132] them.

[020-133] But yellow or red, young or old, in a few days they both grew a-weary

[020-134] of the star country to which they were taken, and wished to return to

[020-135] the earth. And then that came to pass which made them yearn with

[020-136] tenfold longing; for their husbands, who were absent all day hunting,

[020-137] had pointed out to them a large flat stone, which they were on no

[020-138] account to lift; which they obeyed in this wise, that they did not both

[020-139] lift the stone, but only the younger, who, as soon as the Stars had

[020-140] gone to the greenwood, rushed to the slab, and, lifting it up, gazed

[020-141] greedily down into the hole beneath. And what she saw was wonderful,

[020-142] for it was the sky itself, and directly under them was the world in

[020-143] which they had lived, and specially in sight was the home of their

[020-144] childhood, with all its woods and rivers. And then the elder having

[020-145] looked, both almost broke their hearts with weeping.

[020-146] Now the Stars were by no means such evil-minded men as you may have

[020-147] deemed; for having perceived by magic that their wives had looked

[020-148] through the hole in the sky, and knowing that they were lying when

[020-149] they denied it, they gave them leave to go back to earth. Yet there

[020-150] were conditions, and those not easy to such fidgety damsels as these;

[020-151] for they said, "Ye shall lie together all this night, and in the

[020-152] morning when ye awake ye shall be in no haste to open your eyes or

[020-153] to uncover your faces. Wait until ye shall have heard the song of the

[020-154] _Ktsee-gee-gil-laxsis_ (P.), or chick-a-dee-dee. And even then ye

[020-155] shall not arise, but be quiet until the song of the red squirrel shall

[020-156] be heard. And even then ye must wait and keep your faces covered and

[020-157] your eyes closed until ye hear the striped squirrel sing. And then ye

[020-158] may leave your bed and look around."

[020-159] Now the younger wife was ever impatient, and when the chick-a-dee-dee

[020-160] sang she would have leaped up at once, but the elder restrained her.

[020-161] "Wait," she said, "my sister, until we hear the _Abalkakmooech_."

[020-162] [Footnote: Ground squirrel] And she lay still till the _Adoo-doo-dech_

[020-163] [Footnote: Red squirrel] began his early chatter and his morning's work.

[020-164] Then, without waiting, she jumped up, as did the elder, when they found

[020-165] themselves indeed on earth, but in the summit of a tall, spreading

[020-166] hemlock-tree, and that in such a manner that they could not descend

[020-167] without assistance. And it had come to pass in this wise: for as each

[020-168] song was sung by the bird and the squirrels, they had come nearer and

[020-169] nearer to the earth; even as the light of day drew near, but as they

[020-170] could not delay they had been deserted. [Footnote: A want of patience

[020-171] or of dignity, and restlessness, are more scorned by every Indian than

[020-172] any other fault. This is not the only story in which people are represented

[020-173] as being punished for being unable to bide their time. Glooskap was

[020-174] specially severe on all such sinners.]
