---[044-001] He had with him an elder brother, a great brave, a very fierce man. He,

[044-002] not being able to do aught else, did the most desperate thing a

[044-003] Wabanaki Indian can do. He went down to the river, and sang the song

[044-004] which calls the _Weewillmekq'_.

[044-005] "We que moh wee will l'mick,

[044-006] We que moh m'cha micso,

[044-007] Som'awo wee will l'mick!

[044-008] Cardup ke su m'so wo Sawo!"

[044-009] I call on the Wee-will-l'mick!

[044-010] I call on the Terrible One!

[044-011] On the One with the Horns!

[044-012] I dare him to appear!

[044-013] It came to him in all its terrors. Its eyes were like fire; its horns

[044-014] rose. It asked him what he wanted. He said that he wished his brother

[044-015] to be in his right mind again.

[044-016] "I will give you what you want," said the Weewillmekq', "if you are not

[044-017] afraid."

[044-018] "I am not afraid of anything," said the Indian.

[044-019] "Not of me?"

[044-020] "Not of you nor of Mitche-hant, the devil himself."

[044-021] "If you dare take me by my horns and scrape somewhat from one of them

[044-022] with your knife," said the monster, "you may have your wish."

[044-023] Now this Indian was indeed as savage and brave as the devil; and he had

[044-024] need to be so to do this, for the Weewillmekq' looked his very worst.

[044-025] But the man drew his knife and scraped from the horn till he was told

[044-026] that he had enough.

[044-027] "Go to your camp," said the Worm. "Put half the scrapings into a cup of

[044-028] water. Make your brother drink it."

[044-029] "And the other half?" asked the Indian.

[044-030] "Give it to the girl who made all this trouble. She needs medicine,

[044-031] too."

[044-032] He returned to camp, and gave the drink to his brother, who recovered.

[044-033] When the hunt was at an end they went home.

[044-034] They arrived at night. There was an immense lodge in the town, and a

[044-035] dance was going on. The younger brother had prepared a cool drink,--

[044-036] sweet with maple-sugar, fragrant with herbs,--and in it was the powder

[044-037] of the horn of the Weewillmekq'. The witch, warm and very thirsty from

[044-038] dancing, came to the door. He offered her the cup. Without heeding who

[044-039] gave it, she drank it dry, and, turning to her partner, went on in the

[044-040] dance.

[044-041] And then a strange thing happened. For at every turn of the dance she

[044-042] grew a year older. She began as a young girl; when at the end of the

[044-043] room she was fifty years of age; and when she got back to the door

[044-044] whence she started she fell dead on the floor, at the feet of him who

[044-045] gave her the drink, a little, wrinkled, wizened-up old squaw of a

[044-046] hundred years.

[044-047] _Aha, yes? wood enit atokhahyen, muggoh mah't adem_. This is the

[044-048] story of the Dance of Old Age. But you may call it _Sektegah_, the

[044-049] Dance of Death, if you like it better. [Footnote: This extraordinary

[044-050] story was related to me by Noel Joseph, at Campobello, August 26, 1883.

[044-051] I am indebted to Mrs. W. Wallace Brown for the incantation song. The

[044-052] Weewillmekq' has, as it appears in several tales, an extraordinary

[044-053] resemblance to the Norse dragon. It cures mental diseases. It seems to

[044-054] be the same with the _Chepitchcalm_.]

[044-055] _III. Another Version of the Dance of Old Age._

[044-056] (Passamaquoddy.)

[044-057] It was in the autumn, the time when Indians go up the rivers to their

[044-058] hunting-grounds, that two young men left home. They ascended the

[044-059] stream; they came to a branch, where they parted: one going alone,

[044-060] another with his married brother. This latter, with the brother, had

[044-061] left in the village a female friend, a witch, who had forbidden him to

[044-062] go hunting, but he had not obeyed her.

[044-063] And she had cause to keep him at home, for, when he was afar in the

[044-064] woods, and alone, he met one day with a very beautiful girl, who

[044-065] fascinated him, and gave herself to him. And when he said that he did

[044-066] not know how to conceal her from his friends she told him that she was

[044-067] a fairy, and could make herself as small as a newly born squirrel, and

[044-068] that all he need do was to wrap her up in a handkerchief and carry her

[044-069] in his pocket. When alone, he could take her out, enjoy her company,

[044-070] and then reduce and fold her up and put her away again.

[044-071] He did so, but from that hour, while he carried the fairy near his

[044-072] heart, he began to be wicked and strange. This was not caused by her,

[044-073] but by the girl at home. He was entirely changed; he grew devilish; he

[044-074] refused to eat, and never spoke. His sister-in-law began to fear him.

[044-075] When she offered him food he cried out, "Unless I can devour one of

[044-076] your children I will have nothing!"

[044-077] When his brother returned and heard all this, he, too, offered him

[044-078] meat, but met with a refusal and the reply, "Give me one of your little

[044-079] children." To which he answered, "The child is so small that it will

[044-080] not satisfy you. Let me go and get a larger one." Then he ran to the

[044-081] village and informed his friends of what had come over the brother. And

[044-082] as they knew that he was about to become a _kewahqu'_

[044-083] (_chenoo_) they resolved to kill him.

[044-084] But there was a young man there, a friend of the sufferer, who said

[044-085] that he could save him. So all who were assembled bade him try.

[044-086] And when night came he went apart, and began to sing his

[044-087] _m'teoulin_, or magic song. When it ended there was a loud sound

[044-088] as of some heavy body falling and striking the earth, which fairly

[044-089] shook. The next morning he called all his friends and the married

[044-090] brother, and showed them a human corpse. "Now leave me," he said. "Go

[044-091] to my friend and tell him that I have food for him." The Indians did

[044-092] so, and in horror left the two cannibals to devour their disgusting

[044-093] meal. When the insane youth was satisfied, his friend asked, "Have you

[044-094] had enough?" He replied that he had. [Footnote: The human body which

[044-095] supplied the meal was probably in reality a deer, or some such animal.]

[044-096] Then the magician said, "You are bewitched by the girl who forbade you

[044-097] to go hunting; she knew you would find a maid better than she is. Now

[044-098] come with me."

[044-099] They went to a small lake; they sat down by its side; the sorcerer

[044-100] began his magic song. And as he sang the waters opened; from the

[044-101] disturbed waves rose a huge Weewillmekq', a creature like an alligator,

[044-102] with horns. And, as the terrible being came ashore, the magician said,

[044-103] "Go and scrape somewhat from his horn and bring it here!" The young man

[044-104] had become fearless; he went and did as he was bid: he scraped the

[044-105] horn, and brought the scraping.

[044-106] "Now, my friend," said the magician, "let us try this on a tree." There

[044-107] was a large green beech growing by them. It was simply touched with the

[044-108] fragment from the horn when another color spread all over the bark as

[044-109] rapidly as the eye could follow it: in an instant it was dead, and in a

[044-110] few minutes more it fell to the ground, utterly rotten, as if it were a

[044-111] century old.

[044-112] "Now," said the sorcerer, "we will experiment with this on the witch

[044-113] who wishes to destroy you." So as it was night they went to the

[044-114] village. A dance was being held, and the beautiful tall witch having

[044-115] paused to rest, the two men approached her. The young man placed his

[044-116] hand on her head; he held in it a scraping of the horn of the

[044-117] _weewillmekq'_. As he did so she grew older in an instant,--she

[044-118] became very old; a pale color rippled all over her; she fell, looking a

[044-119] hundred years, dead on the floor, shriveled, dried, and dropped to

[044-120] powder.

[044-121] "She will not trouble you any more," said the sorcerer. "Her dance is

[044-122] over."

[044-123] This is the same story as the preceding; but I give it to show now

[044-124] differently a tale may be told by neighbors. In one it is the _spretae

[044-125] injuria formae_, the wrath of rejected love, which inspires the witch

[044-126] to revenge; in the other it is jealousy. In one she inflicts madness; in

[044-127] the other she turns him into a cannibal demon, as Loki, when only half

[044-128] bad, was made utterly so by getting the "thought-stone" or heart of a

[044-129] witch. This legend was sent to me by Louis Mitchell. It is written not

[044-130] by him, but by some other Passamaquoddy, in Indian-English.

[044-131] TALES OF MAGIC.

[044-132] _M'teoulin, or Indian Magic_.

[044-133] The study of magic as it is believed in or understood by the Indians of

[044-134] America is extremely interesting, for it involves that of all

[044-135] supernaturalism or of all religion whatever. But if we, declining all

[044-136] question as to the origin of monotheism, limit ourselves definitely to

[044-137] what is known of Shamanism alone, we shall still have before us an

[044-138] immense field for investigation. Shamanism is the belief that

[044-139] _all_ the events and accidents of life are caused or influenced by

[044-140] spirits, and as fear of suffering is in all men, but particularly the

[044-141] savage, the strongest moral emotion, the natural consequence is a

[044-142] greater fear of _evil_ invisible beings. The result of it is a

[044-143] faith that everything which is obscure or invisible is supposed to be

[044-144] the work of mysterious agents, generally evil. Thus all disease

[044-145] whatever, all suffering, pain, loss, or disaster, or bad weather, is at

[044-146] once attributed either to a spirit or to some enemy who practices

[044-147] witchcraft. The Shaman is the priest or doctor, who professes to be

[044-148] able, by his counter-charms, to counteract or neutralize this devil's

[044-149] work.

[044-150] It will be long ere the scholar definitely determines whether Shamanism

[044-151] as it now exists originated spontaneously in different countries where

[044-152] the same causes were to be found, or whether it is _historical_;

[044-153] that is, derived from a single source. I believe that while darkness,

[044-154] hunger, fear, and similar causes could not fail to create a rude

[044-155] religion anywhere, as Moncure Conway has shown, yet that the derivation

[044-156] from one beginning, or at least later modifications from it, has been

[044-157] very great indeed. Investigation indicates that it was in Assyria, at a

[044-158] very remote age, that Shamanism had, if not its origin, at least its

[044-159] fullest development. The reader who will consult Lenormant's work on

[044-160] Chaldean magic will learn from it that the fear of devils and the art

[044-161] of neutralizing their power were never carried to such an extent

[044-162] elsewhere as in the Land of Bel. Now as Shamanism has at the present

[044-163] day its stronghold among the Turanian races of Central Asia, it may

[044-164] greatly strengthen the theory, somewhat doubted of late, of the early

[044-165] Accadian predecessors of the Chaldeans and their Turanian origin, if we

[044-166] can only prove that their magical religion was the same as that of the

[044-167] Tartars. So far as my reading has aided me, I am inclined to believe

[044-168] that they are identical. "Magic" went so far among the former that,

[044-169] while they discovered natural remedies for natural ills, they never

[044-170] doubted that one was as much the result of sorcery as the other. This

[044-171] theory spread everywhere.

[044-172] Shamanism, or a vague fear of invisible evils and the sorcerer, may

[044-173] indeed have sprung up independently in Tartary, Central Africa,

[044-174] Finland, and North America. But it is almost incredible that the use of

[044-175] a drum inscribed with magical figures, the spirit flight of the angakok

[044-176] or Shaman, and twenty other characteristics of the art should have

[044-177] become, without transmission, common to all these countries. Shamanism

[044-178] has probably been at the root of all religions; there was a great deal

[044-179] of it in all those of the Semitic races, and, admitting this, it is not

[044-180] difficult to see how from Chaldea and Babylon it may have found its way

[044-181] into Africa, where black savages, who would have rejected a higher

[044-182] religion, would grasp greedily at what they sympathized with. The only

[044-183] real difference between the Voodoo and Pow-wow practices is that the

[044-184] former is, so to speak, the _blacker_ and more revolting. This is

[044-185] because a low state of culture has induced the believers in it to

[044-186] retain more of the coarse witchcraft on which Shamanism was based, or

[044-187] out of which it grew.
