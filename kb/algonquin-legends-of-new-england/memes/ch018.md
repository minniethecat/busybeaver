---[018-001] This family was at _Samgadihawk_, or Saco, on the sandy field

[018-002] which is in the Intervale or the summer bed of the Saco River, in the

[018-003] El-now-e-bit, the White Mountains, between Geh-sit-wah-zuch [Footnote:

[018-004] _Geh-sit-wah-zuch_, "many mountains" (Pen.). Mount Kearsarge, so

[018-005] called from the several lesser peaks around it.] and K'tchee penahbesk,

[018-006] [Footnote: _K'tchee penabesk_, "the great rock," a much more

[018-007] sensible and appropriate name than that of "Cathedral Rocks," which has

[018-008] been bestowed upon it; also _chee penabsk_.] and near Oonahgemessuk

[018-009] weegeet, the Home of the Water Fairies. [Footnote: Also called from a

[018-010] legend, _Oonahgemessuk k'tubbee_, the Water Fairies' Spring. This

[018-011] appropriate and beautiful name has been rejected in favor of the

[018-012] ridiculously rococo term "Diana's Bath." As there is a "Diana's Bath"

[018-013] at almost every summer watering place in America, North Conway must of

[018-014] course have one. The absolute antipathy which the majority of Americans

[018-015] manifest for the aboriginal names, even in a translation, is really

[018-016] remarkable.] Now the old man, the father of the evil magicians and his

[018-017] adopted father, had only one eye, and was half gray. [Footnote: This would

[018-018] directly connect him with the beings which are half stone, like the

[018-019] Oonahgemessuk, or water-goblins, the dwellers in Katahdin, and the Eskimo

[018-020] elves. This will be referred to again.] And Glooskap made himself like

[018-021] him,--there was not between them the difference of a hair; and having

[018-022] this form, he entered the wigwam and sat down by the old man. And the

[018-023] brothers, who killed everybody, not sparing one living soul, hearing a

[018-024] talking, looked in slyly, and seeing the new-comer, so like their father

[018-025] that they knew not which was which, said, "This is a great magician. But

[018-026] he shall be tried ere he goes, and that bitterly."

[018-027] Then the sister took the tail of a whale, and cooked it for the

[018-028] stranger to eat. But as it lay before him, on the platter and on his

[018-029] knees, the elder brother entered, and saying rudely, "This is too good

[018-030] for a beggar like you," took it away to his own wigwam. Then Glooskap

[018-031] spoke: "That which was given to me was mine; therefore I take it

[018-032] again." And sitting still he simply _wished_ for it, and it came

[018-033] flying into the platter where it was before. So he ate it.

[018-034] Then the brothers said, "Indeed, he is a great magician. But he shall

[018-035] be tried ere he goes, and that bitterly."

[018-036] When he had eaten, they brought in a mighty bone, the jaw of a whale,

[018-037] and the eldest brother, with great ado, and using both his arms and all

[018-038] his strength, bent it a little. Then he handed it to Glooskap, who with

[018-039] his thumb and fingers, snapped it like a pipe-stem. And the brothers

[018-040] said again, "Truly, this is a great magician. But he shall for all that

[018-041] be tried ere he goes, and that bitterly."

[018-042] Then they brought a great pipe full of the strongest tobacco; no man

[018-043] not a magician could have smoked it. And it was passed round: every one

[018-044] smoked; the brothers blew the smoke through their nostrils. But

[018-045] Glooskap filled it full, and, lighting it, burnt all the tobacco to

[018-046] ashes at one pull, and blew all the smoke through his nostrils at one

[018-047] puff. Then the brothers said again in anger, "This is indeed a great

[018-048] magician. Yet he shall be tried again ere he goes, and that bitterly."

[018-049] But they never said it again.

[018-050] And they still tried to smoke with him, and the wigwam was closed; they

[018-051] hoped to smother him in smoke, but he sat and puffed away as if he had

[018-052] been on a mountain-top, till they could bear it no longer. And one

[018-053] said, "This is idle; let us go and play at ball." The place where they

[018-054] were to play was on the sandy plain of Samgadihawk, or Saco, on the

[018-055] bend of the river. [Footnote: I have an Indian stone pestle, or hominy

[018-056] pounder, which I picked up on the site of this ball-play.] And the game

[018-057] begun; but Glooskap found that the ball with which they played was a

[018-058] hideous skull; it was alive and snapped at his heels, and had he been

[018-059] as other men and it had bitten him, it would have taken his foot off.

[018-060] Then Glooskap laughed, and said, "So this is the game you play. Good,

[018-061] but let us all play with our own balls." So he stepped up to a tree on

[018-062] the edge of the river-bed and broke off the end of a bough, and it

[018-063] turned into a skull ten times more terrible than the other. And the

[018-064] magicians ran before it as it chased them as a lynx chases rabbits;

[018-065] they were entirely beaten. Then Glooskap stamped on the sand, and the

[018-066] waters rose and came rushing fearfully from the mountains adown the

[018-067] river-bed; the whole land rang with their roar. Now Glooskap sang a

[018-068] magic song, which changes all beings, and the three brothers and their

[018-069] father became the _chinahmess_, a fish which is as long and large

[018-070] as a man, and they went headlong down on the flood, to the deep sea, to

[018-071] dwell there forever. And the magicians had on, each of them, a wampum

[018-072] collar; wherefore the _chinahmess_ has beneath its head, as one

[018-073] may say, round its neck, the wampum collar, as may be seen to this day.

[018-074] And they were mighty _m'teoulin_ in their time; but they were

[018-075] tried before they went, and that bitterly.

[018-076] Yes, _seewass_, my brother, this is a true story. For Glus-gah-be

[018-077] was a great man in his day, and the day will come when I shall go to

[018-078] him and see him. [Footnote: This legend is from a single authority,

[018-079] Maria Saksis.]

[018-080] _How Glooskap went to England and France, and was the first to make

[018-081] America known to the Europeans_.

[018-082] (Passamaquoddy.)

[018-083] There was an Indian woman: she was a Woodchuck (Mon-in-kwess, P.). She

[018-084] had lost a boy; she always thought of him. Once there came to her a

[018-085] strange boy; he called her mother.

[018-086] He had a pipe with which he could call all the animals. He said,

[018-087] "Mother, if you let any one have this pipe we shall starve."

[018-088] "Where did you get it?"

[018-089] "A stranger gave it to me."

[018-090] One day the boy was making a canoe. The woman took the pipe and blew

[018-091] it. There came a deer and a _qwah-beet_,--a beaver. They came

[018-092] running; the deer came first, the beaver next. The beaver had a stick

[018-093] in his mouth; he gave it to her, and said, "Whenever you wish to kill

[018-094] anything, though it were half a mile off, point this stick at it." She

[018-095] pointed it at the deer; it fell dead.

[018-096] The boy was Glooskap. He was building a stone canoe. Every morning he

[018-097] went forth, and was gone all day. He worked a year at it. The mother

[018-098] had killed many animals. When the great canoe was finished he took his

[018-099] (adopted) mother to see it. He said that he would make sails for it.

[018-100] She asked him, "Of what will you make them?" He answered, "Of leaves."

[018-101] She replied, "Let the leaves alone. I have something better." She had

[018-102] many buffalo skins already tanned, and said, "Take as many as you

[018-103] need."

[018-104] He took his pipe. He piped for moose; he piped for elk and for bear:

[018-105] they came. He pointed his stick at them: they were slain. He dried

[018-106] their meat, and so provisioned his great canoe. To carry water he

[018-107] killed many seals; he filled their bladders with water.

[018-108] So they sailed across the sea. This was before the white people had

[018-109] ever heard of America. The white men did not discover this country

[018-110] first at all. Glooskap discovered England, and told them about it. He

[018-111] got to London. The people had never seen a canoe before. They came

[018-112] flocking down to look at it.

[018-113] The Woodchuck had lost her boy. This boy it was, who first discovered

[018-114] America (England?). This boy could walk on the water and fly up to the

[018-115] sky. [Footnote: This tale was taken down in very strange and confused

[018-116] English. The first part is in my notes almost unintelligible.] He took

[018-117] his mother to England. They offered him a large ship for his stone

[018-118] canoe. He refused it. He feared lest the ship should burn. They offered

[018-119] him servants. He refused them. They gave him presents which almost

[018-120] overloaded the canoe. They gave him an anchor and an English flag.

[018-121] He and his mother went to France. The French people fired cannon at him

[018-122] till the afternoon. They could not hurt the stone canoe. In the night

[018-123] Glooskap drew all their men-of-war ashore. Next morning the French saw

[018-124] this. They said, "Who did this?" He answered, "I did it."

[018-125] They took him prisoner. They put him into a great cannon and fired it

[018-126] off. They looked into the cannon, and there he sat smoking his stone

[018-127] pipe, knocking the ashes out.

[018-128] The king heard how they had treated him. He said it was wrong. He who

[018-129] could do such deeds must be a great man. He sent for Glooskap, who

[018-130] replied, "I do not want to see your king. I came to this country to

[018-131] have my mother baptized as a Catholic." They sent boats, they sent a

[018-132] coach; he was taken to the king, who put many questions to him.

[018-133] He wished to have his mother christened. It was done. They called her

[018-134] Molly. [Footnote: The Indians pronounce the word Marie Mahli or Molly.

[018-135] Mahlinskwess, "Miss Molly," sounds like Mon-in-kwess, a woodchuck.

[018-136] Hence this very poor pun.] Therefore to this day all woodchucks are

[018-137] called Molly. They went down to the shore; to please the king Glooskap

[018-138] drew all the ships into the sea again. So the king gave him what he

[018-139] wanted, and he returned home. Since that time white men have come to

[018-140] America.

[018-141] * * * * *

[018-142] This is an old Eskimo tale, greatly modernized and altered. The Eskimo

[018-143] believe in a kind of sorcerers or spirits, who have instruments which

[018-144] they merely point at people or animals, to kill them. I think that the

[018-145] Indian who told me this story (P.) was aware of its feebleness, and was

[018-146] ashamed to attribute such nonsense to Glooskap, and therefore made the

[018-147] hero an Indian named Woodchuck. But among Mr. Rand's Micmac tales it

[018-148] figures as a later tribute to the memory of the great hero.

[018-149] One version of this story was given to me by Tomah Josephs, another by

[018-150] Mrs. W. Wallace Brown. In the latter Glooskap's canoe is a great ship,

[018-151] with all kinds of birds for sailors. In the Shawnee legend of the

[018-152] Celestial Sisters (Hiawatha Legends), a youth who goes to the sky must

[018-153] take with him one of every kind of bird. This indicates that the

[018-154] Glooskap voyage meant a trip to heaven.

[018-155] _How Glooskap is making Arrows, and preparing for a Great Battle. The

[018-156] Twilight of the Indian Gods._

[018-157] (Passamaquoddy.)

[018-158] "_Is Glooskap living yet_?" "Yes, far away; no one knows where.

[018-159] Some say he sailed away in his stone canoe beyond the sea, to the east,

[018-160] but he will return in it one day; others, that he went to the west. One

[018-161] story tells that while he was alive those who went to him and found him

[018-162] could have their wishes given to them. But there is a story that if one

[018-163] travels long, and is not afraid, he may still find the great sagamore

[018-164] (_sogmo_). Yes. He lives in a very great, a very long wigwam. He

[018-165] always making arrows. One side of the lodge is full of arrows now. They

[018-166] so thick as that. When it is all quite full, he will come forth and

[018-167] make war. He never allows any one to enter the wigwam while he is

[018-168] making these arrows."

[018-169] "_And on whom will he make war_?" "He will make war on all, kill

[018-170] all; there will be no more world,--world all gone. Dunno how quick,--

[018-171] mebbe long time; all be dead then, mebbe,--guess it will be long time."
