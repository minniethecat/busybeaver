**Bumole** — also called _Pamola_ — is the Spirit of Night in Passamaquoddy mythology. Described as "the spirit of the night-air" (011-019), Bumole appears in multiple Algonquin narratives as a presence associated with darkness, the nocturnal world, and the boundary between the human and supernatural realms. Leland devotes a chapter section to Bumole among the "Supernatural Beings" of the Wabanaki cosmos.

## Overview

Bumole occupies a distinctive position in the Algonquin supernatural hierarchy. Unlike the cannibal monsters ([[chenoo]], [[kookwess]]) who threaten physical destruction, or the culture hero [[glooskap]] who protects humanity, Bumole is an ambient spiritual force — the personification of night itself. In the tale of [[abistanooch]], it is Bumole whose voice signals the transition from day to the supernatural time of darkness: "when the sun had set and the voice of Bumole, the Spirit of Night, was heard afar on high" (020-105).

The alternate name Pamola connects Bumole to a broader Algonquin spiritual complex. Leland notes that "Pamola (that is, Bumole), who is the evil spirit of the night air" appears in Abenaki as well as Passamaquoddy tradition (035-004). The name Pamola is better known in some contexts as the storm-bird spirit of Mount Katahdin — a connection that suggests Bumole/Pamola may represent a complex of night-and-storm spiritual forces rather than a single clearly defined entity.

## Identity and Attributes

### Name and Etymology

The name appears in two forms: _Bumole_ (Passamaquoddy) and _Pamola_ (Abenaki). Leland uses both interchangeably: "Bumole, or Pamola" (011-020). The equivalence is confirmed by the chapter reference: "For an account of Bumole, or Pamola, see the chapter on Supernatural Beings" (011-020).

The linguistic relationship between the two forms suggests dialectal variation within the Eastern Algonquian language continuum. Both names refer to a spirit associated with night and the night air.

### Nature and Domain

Bumole is characterized primarily as a nocturnal presence. In the Abistanooch narrative, his voice marks the onset of night as a palpable event:

> "It came to pass when the sun had set and the voice of Bumole, the Spirit of Night, was heard afar on high, and Nibauchset (P.), the Night-Walker, shone over all, that the two brides lay in an oak opening of the forest" (020-105).

This passage establishes Bumole as an auditory phenomenon — a "voice" heard "afar on high" — rather than a visually embodied being. He is the sound of nightfall, the acoustic signature of the transition from day-world to spirit-world.

Leland describes Bumole as an "evil spirit" in one passage (035-004), though this characterization may reflect Christian-influenced dualism rather than traditional Algonquin cosmology. In the broader mythological context, night-spirits are not necessarily malevolent; they are powerful, dangerous if approached without proper knowledge, but integral to the cosmic order.

## Role in Legends

### The Voice at Dusk

Bumole's primary narrative function is as a temporal marker — his voice signals the passage from day to night, the moment when the ordinary world recedes and the supernatural becomes active. In the Abistanooch tale, Bumole's voice directly precedes the fairy brides' star-gazing conversation, in which they jest about marrying celestial bodies — a moment of supernatural intimacy made possible by the night Bumole has inaugurated.

### Cosmological Position

Bumole is paired in the narrative with _Nibauchset_, the Night-Walker (the moon or a lunar spirit), who "shone over all" after Bumole's voice announces the darkness (020-105). Together, Bumole and Nibauchset constitute a nocturnal dyad: the voice of night and the light of night, the sound and the vision of the dark hours.

### Relation to Pamola

The Pamola variant connects to the better-known figure of the storm-bird of Katahdin — a powerful spirit associated with Mount Katahdin in Maine, often described as having the head of a moose and the body and wings of an eagle. Whether the Bumole of the night air and the Pamola of the mountain storm are the same being or related beings within a shared spiritual complex remains an open question in the sources. Leland's chapter on "Supernatural Beings" appears to treat them as a single entity.

## Cultural Significance

Bumole exemplifies the Algonquin practice of personifying natural phenomena as spiritual beings with agency and voice. The night is not merely an absence of light but a presence — Bumole — whose arrival is an event, whose voice can be heard, and whose domain must be navigated with care.

This personification serves both cosmological and practical functions. Cosmologically, it integrates the diurnal cycle into the spiritual order: every sunset is Bumole's arrival, every night his dominion. Practically, it encodes a warning: the night is not empty; it is inhabited by powers that demand respect. The fairy brides' conversation under Bumole's aegis — their jest about marrying stars — takes place in a liminal space where such speech may have consequences beyond the ordinary.
