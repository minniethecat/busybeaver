**Wejek** — the Tree Partridge in the Micmac tongue — is the companion and fellow hunter of [[Pulowech]] the Partridge in Micmac mythology. He appears in "The Adventures of the Great Hero Pulowech," where his loyal but rash pursuit of the cannibal sorcerers who murdered Pulowech's wife leads to his own death.

## Overview

Wejek lives with Pulowech "in one wigwam in the woods, on the border of a beautiful lake" (037-147). The two are differentiated by their species-names: Pulowech is the Partridge (_Mitchihess_ in Passamaquoddy) while Wejek is the Tree Partridge — a distinction that may reflect different grouse species or simply narrative differentiation of two companion figures.

When Pulowech's sea-maiden wife is devoured by cannibal sorcerers, Wejek acts before thinking: "without waiting, he set forth to seek her. And this was not wisely done, since, falling among them, he was himself slain" (038-026). His death is the catalyst for Pulowech's revenge quest, discovered through the blood-filled divination dish.

## Identity and Attributes

### Name

The name _Wejek_ is glossed by Leland as the Micmac term for the Tree Partridge (037-147), distinguishing him from Pulowech, the ground-dwelling Partridge. This species-level distinction may encode ecological knowledge — the ruffed grouse (ground) versus the spruce grouse (tree) — or may serve purely narrative purposes.

### Character

Wejek is characterized by loyalty and impulsiveness — qualities that make him a sympathetic figure whose death carries emotional weight. Unlike the cunning Pulowech, who uses divination to learn the truth before acting, Wejek rushes forward on instinct alone. His fate serves as a narrative lesson in the value of patience and magical knowledge over raw courage.

## Role in Legends

### The Death of Wejek

When Wejek returns to the wigwam and finds Pulowech's wife gone, "he was so angry that, without waiting, he set forth to seek her" (038-026). He falls among the cannibal sorcerers and is slain — a death confirmed when Pulowech, using his water-divination dish, finds it "half full of blood" in the morning, signifying that "the twain had been murdered" (038-079).
