---[048-001] This legend is little more than an enumeration of the recipes popularly

[048-002] employed to obtain certain powers. It may be observed that it is

[048-003] limited to all that a real Indian requires. It is very different from

[048-004] what a white man or an Asiatic savage would have wanted; and there is

[048-005] just enough truth and common sense in the methods recommended to make

[048-006] the whole plausible. The reader will observe that the magic hair-string

[048-007] and locks of hair play the same important part in _m'teoulin_ that

[048-008] they did in Old World magic. This is hardly one of the coincidences

[048-009] which can be attributed to spontaneous development from similar causes.

[048-010] It may be such, but there may be also an Eskimo sidegate through which

[048-011] it entered from the other side.

[048-012] Another magic means was the influencing high and mysterious powers. Of

[048-013] this the following is an admirable illustration:--

[048-014] _Tumilkoontaoo, or the Broken Wing._

[048-015] (Micmac.)

[048-016] An Indian family lived on the sea-shore. They had two sons; the eldest

[048-017] of these was married, and had many small children. They lived by

[048-018] fishing; they chiefly caught eels.

[048-019] It came to pass that the weather was so stormy that they could not

[048-020] fish. The wind blew terribly night and day; the waves were like dancing

[048-021] hills. Hunger made them fierce. One day the father told his boys to

[048-022] walk along the shore and see if no fish had been cast on the beach.

[048-023] A young man went; he went far along; and as he went the wind was ever

[048-024] worse; it blew so fiercely that he could hardly stand. It seemed to

[048-025] come from a point of land. He resolved to pass it, and when there he

[048-026] saw the cause of the tempest. Upon a _kwesopskeak'_--a high and

[048-027] rocky ledge, a bold cliff, but surrounded by the water--sat the

[048-028] Wind-Bird, or storm-sagamore himself, flapping his wings, and thereby

[048-029] raising all the wind.

[048-030] Then the young man, who was brave and wise, resolved to outwit the

[048-031] wind-god. And approaching him and addressing him as _Nikskamich_,

[048-032] "My grandfather," he inquired, "are you cold!" And he answered, "Nay;"

[048-033] but the young man insisted that he must be suffering, and offered to

[048-034] carry him on his back to the main-land. [Footnote: It would appear that

[048-035] while the bird flapped his wings he did not fly. I believe this was the

[048-036] same with the Norse Hrosvelgar.] And the offer being accepted, he

[048-037] carried the mighty bird from one weedy, slippery rock to another, up

[048-038] and down, jumping anon, and wading through the pools. But at the last

[048-039] rock he, with full intention, stumbled and fell as if by accident, yet

[048-040] managed it so well as to break one of the wings of the eagle, as he

[048-041] indeed meant to do. Yet he made great show of being very sorry, and,

[048-042] having set the wing, bade the bird keep quiet, and not move his wings

[048-043] for many days; not till the wound was healed should he stir them. "Sit

[048-044] still, _Nikskamich_," he said, "and I will bring you food; I will

[048-045] be attentive; you shall want nothing." And the god sat still: there was

[048-046] a calm on the water; no leaves moved in the forest; there was no wind

[048-047] in all the world.

[048-048] The young man went home; there was not a breeze, the canoe went

[048-049] smoothly over the sea, the eels could be seen in the depths, the

[048-050] Indians caught fish by thousands; never before had they caught so many.

[048-051] And the sagamore of the birds sat still; the Wind-Bird waited to get

[048-052] well; the young man fed him every day.

[048-053] There can be too much of what is good; good turns to evil, sweet to

[048-054] sour. After many days of quiet calm the sea was covered with

[048-055] _Ogokpegeak_, a scum which is caused by sickness among the fish,

[048-056] and which is thrown off by them, for they suffer in still water. Then

[048-057] the fisherman can no longer look down into the sea; then he cannot use

[048-058] the spear.

[048-059] Then the young man, examining the wing of the storm-bird, said,

[048-060] "Grandfather, it is much better; move it but a little now, that I may

[048-061] see!" So he moved it; he gave a flap, and lo! a slight ripple passed

[048-062] over the surface of the sleeping sea. And striking lightly with his

[048-063] wings, again there came a breeze, and the _Ogokpegeak_, or the

[048-064] scum, was blown away, and the Indians fished again, and all was well.

[048-065] So they had the Wind-Bird for a friend, and the sea was smooth or

[048-066] stormy as they willed. But these Indians wished for more than they

[048-067] could manage. They grew tired of catching small fish; they wanted

[048-068] whales. "Let us go and catch the Bootup!" said the elder brother. "How

[048-069] will you take him?" asked the younger. "I will entice him with the

[048-070] _peepoogwokan_," said the elder, "with my pipe." So he sat by the

[048-071] sea; he played on the pipe; he played, but no whale came. So they went

[048-072] back to their small fishery.

[048-073] This is manifestly the beginning and end of a very ancient Indian mythical

[048-074] tale. The Micmacs have tacked on to it a ridiculous fragment of an

[048-075] indifferent French nursery tale, without an end and without any connection

[048-076] with the Indian beginning. The tradition is probably entirely Eskimo.

[048-077] Among the Greenlanders there is a caste of whale-fishers, separate and

[048-078] apart, and this story, in its second stage, was applied to teach, _Ne

[048-079] sutor ultra crepidam_,--that all should stick to their trades, and that

[048-080] though a sorcerer might rule the winds it did not follow that he could win

[048-081] the whales.

[048-082] I have spoken before of the curious identity of the Indian storm-king,

[048-083] or Wind-Bird, with that of the Norse Hrosvelgar. When among the

[048-084] Chippewas, west of Lake Superior, I met with a white man who had

[048-085] received the name of Thunder-Bird from the Indians still further west.

[048-086] The magicians of all countries, be they of Africa, Asia, or North

[048-087] America, are invariably represented by travelers as holding their flock

[048-088] in subjection, and never being doubted as to power or skill. But there

[048-089] are skeptics or Agnostics among the men of the woods as well as among

[048-090] those of civilized cities. There are shrewd fellows who cannot only

[048-091] detect impostors, but turn their tricks to their own advantage. An

[048-092] amusing illustration of this is given in the following story:

[048-093] _Fish-Hawk and Scapegrace_. [Footnote: Wiskumagwasoo and Mahgwis.

[048-094] The Mahgwis, or "Scapegrace," is a kind of sea-gull.]

[048-095] (Micmac.)

[048-096] Two men met and talked: one was Fish-Hawk, the other was Scapegrace.

[048-097] Now the Fish-Hawk can fly higher than any other ocean bird, and he is

[048-098] proud and particular as to his food; he is only beaten by the eagle.

[048-099] When he dives and takes a fish the eagle pursues him; he lets it drop;

[048-100] the great sagamore of the birds catches it; but to less than the chief

[048-101] he yields nothing. But the Scapegrace will eat anything he is heavy in

[048-102] flying; he is slow and of low degree.

[048-103] So when the Scapegrace proposed to the Fish-Hawk that they should

[048-104] become partners the proud bird was angry in his heart, but said

[048-105] nothing, as he was crafty, and as it occurred to him that he could

[048-106] punish the other; and this he was the more willing to do because the

[048-107] Scapegrace actually proposed to fly a race with him! So he said, "Let

[048-108] us go together to a certain Indian village." And they went off

[048-109] together.

[048-110] The Fish-Hawk arrived there far before the other. And on arriving he

[048-111] said, "Beware of him who will come after me. You will know him by these

[048-112] signs: he is ugly and heavy; he will bring with him his own food. It is

[048-113] coarse and common; in fact it is poison. He wishes to kill you; he will

[048-114] offer it. Do not eat of it, or you will die."

[048-115] Then having been very well entertained himself, he took his departure.

[048-116] Scapegrace soon appeared, but was treated with great reserve. He

[048-117] offered his food, and the people pretended to eat it, but took good

[048-118] care to quietly throw it away. Then he told the chief that he was

[048-119] seeking a wife, and asked if there were girls to marry in the town. To

[048-120] which the chief replied, "Yes, there is a mother with several

[048-121] daughters, of the _Amalchoogwech'_ or Raccoon tribe."

[048-122] He went to see the girls. A bad name had gone before him. One of them

[048-123] stood before the lodge. She saw him, and cried, "_Mahgwis

[048-124] wechooveet_!" "Scapegrace is coming!" They received him as if he had

[048-125] been Sickness. He was welcomed like filth on fine clothes. They cried

[048-126] out, "_Ulummeye_!" "Go home!" He asked the mother if she had

[048-127] daughters. She answered, "Yes." He asked her if she would give him one.

[048-128] She replied, "I will not." So he went his way.

[048-129] Now when he had gone Fish-Hawk came again, and asked if Scapegrace had

[048-130] been there. He inquired if all had passed as he predicted. They said it

[048-131] had. Then it occurred to him to pass himself off for a great prophet, a

[048-132] wise magician, well knowing that he could make much of it. So he said,

[048-133] "It is well. Remember that you would have all died but for my

[048-134] foresight. That wizard would have poisoned you all. But have no fear.

[048-135] In future I will watch over you."

[048-136] Then, he said to a man of the people that if at any time he should see

[048-137] a large bird flying over the village it would be an omen of great

[048-138] coming danger. "Then," he said, "think of me; call on me, and I will

[048-139] come." So he departed.

[048-140] The man thought it all over for a long time. He was shrewd and wise.

[048-141] "He foretold the coming of Scapegrace," he reflected. "Now he pretends

[048-142] to be a very great sorcerer. We shall see!"

[048-143] Sure enough, in a few days he saw a bird flying on high. "That," said

[048-144] he, "must be the _Wis-kuma-gwasoo_." He called him, and he came.

[048-145] "You spoke," he said, "of danger to our town. What is it?"

[048-146] "There is great danger. In a few days your town will be attacked by a

[048-147] Kookwes. [Footnote: In Passamaquoddy _Kewahqu'_, a cannibal giant,

[048-148] who is also a sorcerer.] Unless you save yourselves you will all be

[048-149] devoured."

[048-150] "What shall we do to be saved?" asked the man. "When will he come?"

[048-151] "In seven days," replied the Fish-Hawk. "Before that time you must take

[048-152] to your canoes and flee afar. You may get beyond his reach, but you

[048-153] cannot before that time get beyond the horrible roar of his voice. And

[048-154] all who hear it will drop dead."

[048-155] "How can we escape this second danger?" asked the man.

[048-156] "You must all close your ears, so that you can hear nothing. When the

[048-157] time is over you may return."

[048-158] The man's name was Oscoon. [Footnote: Oscoon (M.): the Liver.] He led

[048-159] the people away. He closed their ears; he did not close his own. Once

[048-160] he heard-a far-away whoop. It was not very terrible. But he said

[048-161] nothing. After a time, the scouts who were sent out returned. They

[048-162] reported that the Kookwes had departed. They had not even seen him. It

[048-163] was a great escape.

[048-164] The people thought much of Oscoon. They made him their chief. In a few

[048-165] days the Fish-Hawk returned. He spoke to Oscoon: "Did the giant come?"

[048-166] "He did." "You escaped?" "By following your advice, we did." "And in

[048-167] which direction did he go?" [Footnote: Here the Fish-Hawk inadvertently

[048-168] betrays himself. In the Edda, Loki changes himself into a falcon and

[048-169] flies to Jotunheim to make mischief, as usual. Odin also changes

[048-170] himself to a hawk or eagle when he is chased by the giant Suttung.

[048-171] There is a strong Norse color to all this tale. The Fish-Hawk is very

[048-172] Loki-like and tricky.] "Surely you, who know so much about him, must

[048-173] know that better than we do." Then the Fish-Hawk saw that he was found

[048-174] out. He flew away, and never returned to the town to play the prophet.
