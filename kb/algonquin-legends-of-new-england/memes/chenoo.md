# Chenoo

The **Chenoo** (also *Chenook*, *Kewahqu'*) is one of the most distinctive supernatural beings in Wabanaki mythology: a human transformed by the act of cannibalism into a towering ice-giant whose heart has turned to stone. Unlike the trickster [[Lox]] or the noble [[Glooskap]], the Chenoo represents a profound psychological concept — the irreversible corruption of the human soul through an unforgivable act, and the possibility (however rare) of redemption. The story of the Great Chenoo, told by the [[Passamaquoddy]] and occupying an entire chapter of Leland's collection, is among the longest and most fully realized narratives in *Algonquin Legends of New England*.

## Overview

The Chenoo embodies the Algonquian understanding of cannibalism as the ultimate moral transgression — an act so terrible that it physically transforms the perpetrator. Leland describes the Chenoo as:

> "a human being with an icy-stone heart, who has become a cannibal" (002-131).

The Chenoo's body temperature is so low that it freezes everything it touches; its heart has become a block of ice encased in stone. Yet, uniquely among monsters, the Chenoo retains a vestige of its former humanity. As the Passamaquoddy story demonstrates, a Chenoo can be cured — if the ice within its body can be expelled through heat, dance, and medicinal treatment, the human being may be restored.

## Identity and Attributes

The Chenoo is primarily associated with the far North, a region of existential cold that mirrors its frozen interior:

> "the dreadful Chenoo of the North" (010-072).

Physically, the Chenoo is described as gigantic, with ice-cold flesh and a heart encased in stone. Its voice produces a "terrible war-whoop" that is "death to mortals" (032-125). The creature's hunger for human flesh is insatiable, yet it can be temporarily sated by other meat.

The Chenoo's most distinctive attribute is the presence of **ice within its body** — physical ice that can be purged through intense heat. Leland's footnote provides context:

> "The Chenoo is not only a cannibal, but a being in whose heart a block of ice is firmly pressed in large blocks" (032-062).

The related beings called *Kewahqu'* (or *Kewokqu*) are sometimes distinguished from Chenoos as wind-hungry giants rather than cannibals, though the terms overlap in usage:

> "the Chenoos, or Kewahqu', who cover themselves with their hair" (011-035).

## Key Stories

### The Great Chenoo: A Story of Redemption

The definitive Chenoo narrative occupies Chapter 32 of Leland's collection and is identified as Passamaquoddy in origin. An Indian man and his wife encounter a Chenoo in the forest. Rather than fleeing, the woman treats the monster with unexpected kindness:

> "The Chenoo came to her. He said, 'Give me something to eat'" (032-035).

This simple act of feeding the monster initiates a remarkable transformation. The husband builds a sweat lodge and, through intense heat, begins to melt the ice within the Chenoo's body:

> "and it became warm, the Chenoo asked that a screen should be placed before him, to shield him from the heat" (032-052).

The process culminates in a ritual dance during which the Chenoo vomits up the ice and stone from its body:

> "Then the Chenoo stripped himself, and danced... The Chenoo danced faster and wilder" (032-089).

Following this purging, the Chenoo is transformed — it aids the family against another attacking Chenoo, becoming their protector:

> "the Chenoo, with softened soul" (033-053).

### The Girl-Chenoo

A separate tale, *The Girl-Chenoo* (002-040), concerns a female variant of the monster. This story explores the gendered dimension of the cannibal transformation and offers a different narrative resolution from the redemption arc of the Great Chenoo.

| Origin | Human transformed by cannibalism |
| Physical Form | Giant, icy flesh, stone-encased heart |
| Habitat | The far North, frozen wilderness |
| Behavior | Cannibalistic, terrifying war-whoop |

## Relationships

The Chenoo's most significant relationship is with the **human family** that redeems it — an Indian man and his wife whose courage and compassion reverse the monster's corruption. This relationship subverts the expected predator-prey dynamic and instead offers a model of healing through kindness and ritual.

The Chenoo also exists in relation to other supernatural beings in the Wabanaki cosmos. It is sometimes conflated with the **Kewokqu** (Giant Magicians), though Leland distinguishes between the cannibal Chenoo of the North and the wind-hungry Kewokqu who fight Glooskap. The Chenoo's adversaries include other Chenoos — the narrative features a climactic battle between the redeemed male Chenoo and a hostile female Chenoo:

> "An enemy, a Chenoo, a woman, even his own sister, was coming to destroy them" (032-119).

The Chenoo's connection to **[[Glooskap]]** is indirect. While Glooskap battles the Kewokqu, the Chenoo operates in a different mythic register — not as Glooskap's opponent but as a figure whose defeat or redemption occurs through human agency rather than divine intervention.

## Cultural Significance

The Chenoo story is remarkable for several reasons. First, it articulates a sophisticated psychology of moral corruption — the idea that a single transgressive act (cannibalism) can fundamentally alter a person's nature, and that this alteration has physical as well as spiritual dimensions. The ice in the Chenoo's heart literalizes the concept of "cold-heartedness" in a way that is both concrete and metaphorically powerful.

Second, the redemption narrative offers a rare example of a monster being cured rather than killed. The sweat lodge, dance, and herbal medicine that purge the Chenoo's ice draw on real Wabanaki healing practices, suggesting that the story encodes traditional knowledge about both physical and spiritual healing.

Third, the Chenoo represents a uniquely northeastern Algonquian concept. While other Indigenous traditions feature cannibal giants (such as the Algonquian *Windigo*), the Chenoo's specific attributes — the ice heart, the sweat-lodge cure, the female variant — are distinctively Wabanaki. The story's prominence in Leland's collection reflects its importance to Passamaquoddy oral tradition.
