---[004-001] It came to pass in after-days that Kwah-beet-a-sis, the son of the

[004-002] Great Beaver, or, as others say, Miko the Squirrel, or else the evil

[004-003] which was in himself, tempted Malsumsis to kill Glooskap; for in those

[004-004] days all men were wicked. So taking his bow he shot Ko-ko-khas the Owl,

[004-005] and with one of his feathers he struck Glooskap while sleeping. Then he

[004-006] awoke in anger, yet craftily said that it was not by an owl's feather,

[004-007] but by a blow from a pine-root, that his life would end.

[004-008] [Illustration: Glooskap killing his brother the wolf]

[004-009] Then the false man led his brother another day far into the forest to

[004-010] hunt, and, while he again slept, smote him on the head with a pine-root.

[004-011] But Glooskap arose unharmed, drove Malsumsis away into the woods,

[004-012] sat down by the brook-side, and thinking aver all that had happened,

[004-013] said, "Nothing but a flowering rush can kill me." But the Beaver, who

[004-014] was hidden among the reeds, heard this, and hastening to Malsumsis told

[004-015] him the secret of his brother's life. For this Malsumsis promised to

[004-016] bestow on Beaver whatever he should ask; but when the latter wished for

[004-017] wings like a pigeon, the warrior laughed, and scornfully said, "Get

[004-018] thee hence; thou with a tail like a file, what need hast thou of

[004-019] wings?"

[004-020] Then the Beaver was angry, and went forth to the camp of Glooskap, to

[004-021] whom he told what he had done. Therefore Glooskap arose in sorrow and

[004-022] in anger, took a fern-root, sought Malsumsis in the deep, dark forest,

[004-023] and smote him so that he fell down dead. And Glooskap sang a song over

[004-024] him and lamented.

[004-025] The Beaver and the Owl and the Squirrel, for what they did and as they

[004-026] did it, all come again into these stories; but Malsumsis, being dead,

[004-027] was turned into the Shick-shoe mountains in the Gaspe peninsula.

[004-028] For this chapter and parts of others I am indebted to the narrative of

[004-029] a Micmac Indian, taken down by Mr. Edward Jock; also to another version

[004-030] in the Rand MS. The story is, in the main-points, similar to that given

[004-031] by David Cusick in his History of the Six Nations, of Enigorio the Good

[004-032] Mind, and Enigonhahetgea, Bad Mind, to which I shall refer anon.

[004-033] It is very evident that in this tradition Glooskap represents the Good

[004-034] principle, and Malsumsis, the little wolf,--that is the Wolf who is the

[004-035] Younger, rather than little or small,--the Evil one. Malsum typifies

[004-036] destruction and sin in several of these tales. He will arise at the

[004-037] last day, when Glooskap is to do battle with all the giants and evil

[004-038] beasts of olden time, and will be the great destroyer. Malsum is the

[004-039] Wolf Fenris of this the true Indian Edda.

[004-040] For a further comment on this birth of the twins and its resemblance to

[004-041] a passage in the Edda, the reader is referred to the notes on the next

[004-042] chapter.

[004-043] _How Glooskap made the Elves and Fairies, and then Man of an Ash

[004-044] Tree, and last of all, Beasts, and of his Coming at the Last Day._

[004-045] (Passamaquoddy.)

[004-046] Glooskap came first of all into this country, into Nova Scotia, Maine,

[004-047] Canada, into the land of the Wabanaki, next to sunrise. There were no

[004-048] Indians here then (only wild Indians very far to the west).

[004-049] First born were the Mikumwess, the Oonabgemessuk, the small Elves,

[004-050] little men, dwellers in rocks.

[004-051] And in this way he made Man: He took his bow and arrows and shot at

[004-052] trees, the basket-trees, the Ash. Then Indians came out of the bark of

[004-053] the Ash-trees. And then the Mikumwees said ... called tree-man....

[004-054] [Footnote: The relater, an old woman, was quite unintelligible at this

[004-055] point.]

[004-056] Glooskap made all the animals. He made them at first very large. Then

[004-057] he said to Moose, the great Moose who was as tall as Ketawkqu's,

[004-058] [Footnote: A giant, high as the tallest pines, or as the clouds.] "What

[004-059] would you do should you see an Indian coming?" Moose replied, "I would

[004-060] tear down the trees on him." Then Glooskap saw that the Moose was too

[004-061] strong, and made him smaller, so that Indians could kill him.

[004-062] Then he said to the Squirrel, who was of the size of a Wolf, "What

[004-063] would you do if you should meet an Indian?" And the Squirrel answered,

[004-064] "I would scratch down trees on him." Then Glooskap said, "You also are

[004-065] too strong," and he made him little. [Footnote: Another account states

[004-066] that Glooskap took the Squirrel in his hands and smoothed him down.]

[004-067] Then he asked the great White Bear what he would do if he met an

[004-068] Indian; and the Bear said, "Eat him." And the Master bade him go and

[004-069] live among rocks and ice, where he would see no Indians.

[004-070] So he questioned all the beasts, changing their size or allotting their

[004-071] lives according to their answers.

[004-072] He took the Loon for his dog; but the Loon absented himself so much

[004-073] that he chose for this service two wolves,--one black and one white,

[004-074] [Footnote: Dogs are used for beasts of burden, to draw sledges, in the

[004-075] North.] But the Loons are always his tale-bearers. Many years ago a man

[004-076] very far to the North wished to cross a bay, a great distance, from one

[004-077] point to another. As he was stepping into his canoe he saw a man with

[004-078] two dogs,--one black and one white,--who asked to be set across. The

[004-079] Indian said, "You may go, but what will become of your dogs?" Then the

[004-080] stranger replied, "Let them go round by land." "Nay," replied the

[004-081] Indian, "that is much too far." But the stranger saying nothing, he put

[004-082] him across. And as they reached the landing place there stood the dogs.

[004-083] But when he turned his head to address the man, he was gone. So he said

[004-084] to himself, "I have seen Glooskap."

[004-085] Yet again,--but this was not so many years ago,--far in the North there

[004-086] were at a certain place many Indians assembled. And there was a

[004-087] frightful commotion, caused by the ground heaving and rumbling; the

[004-088] rocks shook and fell, they were greatly alarmed, and lo! Glooskap stood

[004-089] before them, and said, "I go away now, but I shall return again; when

[004-090] you feel the ground tremble, then know it is I." So they will know when

[004-091] the last great war is to be, for then Glooskap will make the ground

[004-092] shake with an awful noise.

[004-093] Glooskap was no friend of the Beavers; he slew many of them. Up on the

[004-094] Tobaic are two salt-water rocks (that is, rocks by the ocean-side, near

[004-095] a freshwater stream). The Great Beaver, standing there one day, was

[004-096] seen by Glooskap miles away, who had forbidden him that place. Then

[004-097] picking up a large rock where he stood by the shore, he threw it all

[004-098] that distance at the Beaver, who indeed dodged it; but when another

[004-099] came, the beast ran into a mountain, and has never come forth to this

[004-100] day. But the rocks which the master threw are yet to be seen.

[004-101] This very interesting tradition was taken down by Mrs. W. Wallace Brown

[004-102] from a very old Passamaquoddy Indian woman named Molly Sepsis, who

[004-103] could not speak a word of English, with the aid of another younger

[004-104] woman named Sarah.

[004-105] It will be observed that it is said in the beginning that Glooskap

[004-106] produced the first human beings from, the ash-tree. Ash and Elm in the

[004-107] Edda were the Adam and Eve of the human race. There were no intelligent

[004-108] men on earth--

[004-109] "Until there came three

[004-110] mighty and benevolent

[004-111] Aesir to the world

[004-112] from their assembly

[004-113] nearly powerless,

[004-114] Ash and Embla (Ash and Elm),

[004-115] void of destiny.

[004-116] "Spirit they possessed not,

[004-117] sense they had not,

[004-118] blood nor motive powers,

[004-119] nor goodly color.

[004-120] Spirit gave Odin,

[004-121] sense gave Hoenir,

[004-122] blood gave Lodur,

[004-123] and good color."

[004-124] [Footnote: _The Edda of Saemund_, translated by Benjamin Thorpe.

[004-125] London: Trubner & Co. 1866. Voluspa, v. 17, 18.]

[004-126] It is certain, however, that the _ash_ was the typic tree of all

[004-127] life, since the next verse of the Voluspa is devoted to Yggdrasil, the

[004-128] tree of existence, or of the world itself. It may be observed that in

[004-129] the Finnish poem of Kalevala it is by the destruction of the great oak

[004-130] that Wainamoien, aided by the hero of the sea, causes all things to

[004-131] grow. The early clearing away of trees, as a first step towards

[004-132] culture, may be symbolized in the shooting of arrows at the ash.

[004-133] The wolf, as a beast for the deity to ride, is strongly Eddaic.

[004-134] "Magic songs they sung,

[004-135] rode on wolves,

[004-136] the god (Odin) and gods."

[004-137] [Footnote: _Rognnir og regin._ Odin and the Powers. Note by B.

[004-138] Thorpe to the _Hrafnagalar Odins_, in Edda, p.30.]

[004-139] We have here within a few lines, accordingly, the elm as the parent of

[004-140] mankind, and wolves as the beasts of transport for the supreme deity,

[004-141] both in the Indian legend and in the Edda.

[004-142] As Glooskap is directly declared in one tradition to keep by him as an

[004-143] attendant a being who is the course of the sun and of the seasons, it

[004-144] may be assumed that the black and white wolf represent day and night.

[004-145] Again, great stress is laid in the Glooskap legend upon the fact that

[004-146] the last great day of battle with Malsum the Wolf and the frost-giants,

[004-147] stone-giants, and other powers of evil, shall be announced by an

[004-148] earthquake.

[004-149] "Trembles Yggdrasil's

[004-150] Ash yet standing,

[004-151] groans that aged tree....

[004-152] and the Wolf runs....

[004-153] The monster's kin goes

[004-154] all with the Wolf....

[004-155] The stony hills are dashed together,

[004-156] The giantesses totter.

[004-157] Then arises Hlin's second grief

[004-158] When Odin goes

[004-159] with the wolf to fight."

[004-160] Word for word, ash-tree, giantesses, the supreme god fighting with a

[004-161] wolf, and falling hills, are given in the Indian myth. This is not the

[004-162] Christian Day of Judgment, but the Norse.

[004-163] In this myth Glooskap has two wolves, one black and the other white.

[004-164] This is an indication of day and night, since he is distinctly stated

[004-165] to have as an attendant Kulpejotei, who typifies the course of the

[004-166] seasons. In the Eddas (Ragnarok) we are told that one wolf now follows

[004-167] the sun, another the moon; one Fenris, the other Moongarm:--

[004-168] "The moon's devourer

[004-169] In a troll's disguise."

[004-170] The magic arrows of Glooskap are of course worldwide, and date from the

[004-171] shafts of Abaris and those used among the ancient Jews for divination.

[004-172] But it may be observed that those of the Indian hero are like the "Guse

[004-173] arrows," described in Oervarodd's Saga, which always hit their mark and

[004-174] return to the one who shoots them. [Footnote: _The Primitive

[004-175] Inhabitants of Scandinavia._ By Svent Nilsson. Edited by Sir John

[004-176] Lubbock, 1868.]

[004-177] It is important here to compare this _old_ Algonquin account of

[004-178] the Creation with that of the Iroquois, or Six Nations, as given by

[004-179] David Cusick, himself an Indian:--

[004-180] "There was a woman who was with child, with twins. She descended from

[004-181] the higher world, and was received on the turtle. While she was in the

[004-182] distress of travail, one of the infants in her womb was moved by an

[004-183] evil desire, and determined to pass out under the side of the parent's

[004-184] arm, and the other infant endeavored in vain to prevent his design.

[004-185] They entered the dark world by compulsion, and their mother expired in

[004-186] a few minutes. One of them possessed a gentle disposition, and was

[004-187] named Enigorio, the Good Mind. The other possessed an insolence of

[004-188] character, and was called Enigonhahetgea; that is, the Bad Mind. The

[004-189] Good Mind was not content to remain in a dark situation, and was

[004-190] desirous to create a great light in the dark world; but the Bad Mind

[004-191] was desirous that the world should remain in its original state. The

[004-192] Good Mind, determined to prosecute his design, began the work of

[004-193] creation. Of his mother's head he made the sun, of her body the moon.

[004-194] After he had made creeks and rivers, animals and fishes, he formed two

[004-195] images of the dust of the ground in his own likeness, male and female,

[004-196] and by his breathing into their nostrils he gave them living souls, and

[004-197] named them _ea gwe howe_, that is a real people; and he gave the

[004-198] Great Island all the animals--of game for the inheritance of the

[004-199] people.... The Bad Mind, while his brother was making the universe,

[004-200] went through the island, and made numerous high mountains and falls of

[004-201] water and great steeps, and also created reptiles which would be

[004-202] injurious to mankind; but the Good Mind restored the island to its

[004-203] former condition. The Bad Mind made two images of clay in the form of

[004-204] mankind, but while he was giving them existence they became

[004-205] _apes_. The Good Mind discovered his brother's contrivances, and

[004-206] aided in giving them living souls.
