# How Glooskap Bound Wuchowsen, the Great Wind-Bird

## Narrative

In the old time, there was a great being called Wuchowsen — meaning Wind-Blow or the Wind-Blower — who lived far in the North. Wuchowsen was an immense bird, a giant among winged creatures, and when he moved his wings he caused the wind to blow across the entire world (016-077).

But Wuchowsen had no measure in his flapping. He beat his great wings without rest, and the winds grew into terrible gales that uprooted trees, scattered the lodges of the people, and made the waters of the lakes and the sea so rough that no canoe could safely travel upon them. The people suffered greatly, unable to hunt or fish, and they cried out to Glooskap for deliverance.

Glooskap heard their prayers and resolved to act. "Wuchowsen, the Great Bird, has done this!" he declared (016-084). He journeyed far to the North, across frozen tundra and through mountains of ice, until he came to the remote place where Wuchowsen dwelt. There he found the giant bird perched upon a crag, and with every stroke of his wings a new tempest was born.

Using his great m'teoulin power, Glooskap seized the Great Bird-giant Wuchowsen as though he were a duck, and tied both his wings tightly against his body (016-094). And instantly — the wind ceased. Everywhere across the world, the air grew still. The trees stood motionless. The lakes became like glass. The sea lay flat as a mirror.

At first the people rejoiced. But as the stillness continued day after day, they discovered a new misery. Without wind, the waters grew stagnant and foul. Ponds became covered with green scum, and the creatures of the water began to sicken and die (016-098). The air itself grew heavy and oppressive. And so the people returned to Glooskap and begged him to restore the wind — but in measure.

Glooskap considered this and saw that they spoke wisely. He returned to Wuchowsen, and as he had left him he found him, for Wuchowsen is immortal (016-101). The Master loosened the bindings, but only partially — allowing Wuchowsen to move one wing, so that a gentle and steady wind might blow across the world, enough to keep the waters fresh, the air clean, and the people's canoes moving, but not so much as to cause destruction.

> "Wuchowsen, the Great Bird, has done this!" (016-084)

And so it is that the Wind blows to this day — sometimes gently, sometimes with greater force when Wuchowsen stirs in his ancient bonds. No one who knows the Edda, Leland observes, will deny that Wuchowsen, the Wind-Blower, is the incarnation of the Wind — a being corresponding to the great elemental forces found in mythologies across the world (016-110). The Passamaquoddy name Wochowsen, or Wuchowsen, means Wind-Blower, and he remains one of the enduring figures of the Algonquin elemental pantheon (035-185).

## Significance

The binding of Wuchowsen is a sophisticated parable about balance and the dangers of extremes. It teaches that natural forces — even those that seem purely destructive — serve a necessary function in the order of the world. Glooskap's initial binding of the Wind-Bird represents the human desire to eliminate all hardship, but the resulting stagnation reveals the wisdom of measure. His partial release of Wuchowsen models the ideal of moderation. The narrative also belongs to a broader pattern in the Glooskap cycle: the hero's mastery over the elements — having bound the Wind, Glooskap demonstrates lordship over air itself, complementing his feats over water, earth, and fire. Leland's comparison to the Norse Edda (016-110) situates this Algonquin elemental being within the universal mythology of wind-giants and storm-birds, from the Thunderbird to Hraesvelg.
