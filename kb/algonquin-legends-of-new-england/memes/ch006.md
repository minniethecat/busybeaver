---[006-001] Then the lord of men and beasts sang the song again, and there came the

[006-002] largest, a mighty female, and she bore him well and easily over to

[006-003] Kes-poog-itk. But she was greatly afraid of getting into shoal water,

[006-004] or of running ashore, and this was what Glooskap wished her to do that he

[006-005] might not wet his feet. So as she approached she asked him if land were

[006-006] in sight. But he lied, and said "No." So she went on rapidly.

[006-007] However, she saw shells below, and soon the water grew so shoal that

[006-008] she said in fear, "_Moon-as-taba-kan-kari-jean-nook_? (M.) Does

[006-009] not the land show itself like a bow-string?" And he said, "We are still

[006-010] far from land."

[006-011] Then the water grew so shoal that she heard the song of the Clams as

[006-012] they lay under the sand, singing to her that she should throw him off

[006-013] and drown him. For these Clams were his deadly enemies. But Bootup the

[006-014] Whale did not understand their language, so she asked her rider--for he

[006-015] knew Clam--what they were chanting to her. And he replied in a song:--

[006-016] "They tell you to hurry (_cussal_) (M),

[006-017] To hurry, to hurry him along,

[006-018] Over the water,

[006-019] Away as fast as you can!"

[006-020] Then the Whale went like lightning, and suddenly found herself high on

[006-021] the shore. Then she lamented and sang:--

[006-022] "Alas, my grandchild (_noojeech_),

[006-023] Ah, you have been my death;

[006-024] I can never leave the land,

[006-025] I shall swim in the sea no more."

[006-026] But Glooskap sang:--

[006-027] "Have no fear, _noogumee_,

[006-028] You shall not suffer,

[006-029] You shall swim in the sea once more."

[006-030] [Illustration: GLOOSKAP LOOKING AT THE WHALE SMOKING HIS PIPE]

[006-031] Then with a push of his bow against her head he sent her off into deep

[006-032] water. And the Whale rejoiced greatly. But ere she went she said, "Oh,

[006-033] my grandson, _K'teen pehabskwass n'aga tomawe_?" (P.). "Hast thou

[006-034] not such a thing as an old pipe and some tobacco?" He replied,--

[006-035] "Ah yes.

[006-036] You want tobacco,

[006-037] I behold you."

[006-038] So he gave her a short pipe and some tobacco, and thereunto a light.

[006-039] And the Whale, being of good cheer, sailed away, smoking as she went,

[006-040] while Glooskap, standing silent on the shore, and ever leaning on his

[006-041] maple bow, beheld the long low cloud which followed her until she

[006-042] vanished in the far away.

[006-043] In a Passamaquoddy tale of Pook-jin-skwess the Witch, the Clams sing a

[006-044] song deriding the hero. The words are:--

[006-045] "Mow chow nut-pess sell

[006-046] Peri marm-hole wett."

[006-047] These words are not Indian, but they are said to mean,--

[006-048] You look very funny with your long hair streaming in the wind,

[006-049] And sailing on a snail's horn.

[006-050] The large Clams sing this in a bass voice, the small ones in falsetto.

[006-051] The gypsies say that a Snail, when put on a pie, utters four cries, or

[006-052] squeaks; hence in Germany the Romany call it _Stargoli_: that is,

[006-053] _shtor-godli_, four cries.

[006-054] _Of the Dreadful Deeds of the Evil Pitcher, who was both Man and

[006-055] Woman, and how she fell in love with Glooskap, and, being scorned,

[006-056] became his Enemy. Of the Toads and Porcupines, and the Awful Battle of

[006-057] the Giants._

[006-058] (Passamaquoddy.)

[006-059] When Glooskap came into the world it abounded in giants, monsters,

[006-060] sorcerers and witches, fiends and devils. Among the witches there was,

[006-061] one whom the Passamaquoddy call Pook-jin-skwess, or the Pitcher.

[006-062] [Footnote: It is not impossible that this well-known Indian witch gave

[006-063] her name to Moll Pitcher, the famous fortune-teller of Lynn.] And they

[006-064] have a legend that she once fell in love with Glooskap when he was

[006-065] young and had not gained the power of his riper age. He fled before

[006-066] her, and she pursued him. It was a dreadful flight, since to make rapid

[006-067] steps both took the form of giants by their _m'-te-oulin_ (P.), or

[006-068] magic power. It was like an awful storm in winter, the wind chasing the

[006-069] cloud; it was like a frightful tempest in summer, the lightning chasing

[006-070] the thunder. As the snow lay deep, both had snow-shoes on. When they

[006-071] came to the shore Glooskap leaped from the main-land to the island of

[006-072] Grand Manan, [Footnote: A leap of about nine miles.] and so escaped

[006-073] her. Now the snow-shoes of Glooskap were _sams'ook_ (P.), or

[006-074] round, while those of Pook-jin-skwess were long and pointed, [Footnote:

[006-075] The Penobscots give the long shoes to Glooskap.] and the marks of them

[006-076] as they jumped are to be seen deep in the rocks to this day.

[006-077] When Glooskap came to the camp, which was at Ogumkegeak (M.), now called

[006-078] Liverpool, he found no one. But there lay the _witch-kwed-lakun-cheech_

[006-079] (M.), or birch-bark dish of Martin, and from it, or, as another legend

[006-080] states, from an old man and woman who dwelt hard by, he learned that

[006-081] Win-pe and the families had been gone for seven years, along a road

[006-082] guarded by wicked and horrible beings, placed by Win-pe to prevent the

[006-083] Great Master from finding him. For it was a great triumph for him to

[006-084] keep Glooskap's friends as slaves, and all the land spoke thereof.

[006-085] And these monsters were Pook-jin-skwess, or the Evil Pitcher herself,

[006-086] in many forms; for she could be man or woman, [Footnote: In the

[006-087] _Tales and Traditions_ of the Eskimo, we are told that a woman

[006-088] named Arnakuak, being apparently gifted by magic with the ability to

[006-089] change her sex, had her daughter-in-law; Ukuamak, for a wife, and,

[006-090] having eloped with her, was followed and killed by her own son. As this

[006-091] is almost immediately followed by a story of a man who gave birth to a

[006-092] child, it would appear that the idea was common to both Eskimo and

[006-093] Indians. Only the wicked magicians in Indian tales change their sex,

[006-094] like Loki in the Edda.] or many of them, and also several girls, when

[006-095] she willed it. Now it is a great part of Indian _m'teoulin_ (P.)

[006-096] to know what one's enemies are planning and plotting, and all their

[006-097] tricks and darkened paths; and in this Glooskap went beyond them all,

[006-098] for before his time every one went his own way, even in wickedness. But

[006-099] Glooskap first of all threw out his soul unto others.

[006-100] And when he came to Ogumkeok he found a hut, and in it, seated over a

[006-101] fire, the ugliest old hag he had ever seen, trembling in every limb, as

[006-102] if near death, dirty, ragged, and loathsome in all ways. Looking up at

[006-103] him with bleared eyes, she begged him to gather her a little firewood,

[006-104] which he did. And then she prayed him to free her from the _wah

[006-105] gook_(M.), or vermin, with which she was covered, and which were

[006-106] maddening her with their bites. These were all devils in disguise, the

[006-107] spirits of foul poison, such as she deemed must kill even the Master.

[006-108] Now Glooskap, foreseeing all this, had taken with him, as he came, from

[006-109] a bog many cranberries. And bidding Pook-jin-skwess bend over, he began

[006-110] to take from her hair the hideous vermin, and each, as he took it,

[006-111] became a horrid porcupine or toad. [Footnote: In the Eskimo mythology,

[006-112] _Arnarkuagsak_, the old woman of the sea, is tormented by vermin

[006-113] about her head. These are really the souls of still-born or murdered

[006-114] infants, who have become imps. The first thing which the _angakok_

[006-115] or sorcerer, who visits her must do is to free her from these pests.

[006-116] The descent of the sorcerer to this mother of all the monsters of the

[006-117] sea, who are at the same time _giants_, when they choose to assume

[006-118] the human form, recalls that of Odin to Hela. Both make this journey to

[006-119] hell, not for themselves, but in the interests of mankind.] Then the

[006-120] hag asked, "Have you found one?" "I have," replied the Master.

[006-121] "_Basp_!" (M.) "Crush it!" was her answer, and Glooskap crushed a

[006-122] cranberry; and she, hearing the noise, thought that he had done as she

[006-123] bid, and that the poison on his fingers would penetrate to his life.

[006-124] But he put the imps, one by one, under the wooden platter, which lay

[006-125] before him. As this went on he put the witch to sleep. When she awoke

[006-126] he was gone. The foul porcupines and toads were swarming all over the

[006-127] ground, having upset their hive. And filled with fury at being made a

[006-128] jest of, since it was a great despite that he had not even found it

[006-129] worth while to kill her when asleep, she burst out into her own form,

[006-130] which was beautiful as sin, wild as the devil, and gathering up all her

[006-131] imps, and making herself far more magical by fiercer will, went onward

[006-132] to encounter him again.

[006-133] Then Glooskap came to a narrow pass in the hills. Here were two

[006-134] terrible beasts, as one story has it, or two monstrous dogs, [Footnote:

[006-135] The Indians had dogs before the coming of the whites. They were

[006-136] wolf-like.] as it is told in another. And they attacked him; but he set

[006-137] his own at them, and they, growing to tremendous size, killed the others.

[006-138] His dogs were so trained that when called to come off they went on, and

[006-139] the more they were bid to be quiet the more they bit.

[006-140] Soon he came to the top of a high hill, and looking thence over all the

[006-141] land saw afar off a large wigwam, and knew in his heart that an enemy

[006-142] dwelt therein. And coming to it he found an old man and his two

[006-143] daughters. [Footnote: In another account, an old sorceress and her

[006-144] daughters; also an old man and his wife and daughters. According to two

[006-145] versions, these are all separate wizards, but the whole spirit of the

[006-146] Passamaquoddy legends make them Pook-jin-skwess alone.] Now the girls

[006-147] came out greeting [Footnote: In the story of the Rabbit and Lusifee the

[006-148] sorcerer singly twice assumes the form of an old man and his two

[006-149] daughters. There is yet another story, in which a magician thus triples

[006-150] himself with two daughters. It is, I believe, Eskimo, but I cannot

[006-151] distinctly remember as to this.] him with very pleasant glances, wooing

[006-152] softly and sweetly; they offered him a string of sausages, such as the

[006-153] Indians make from the entrails of the bear by only turning them inside

[006-154] out. For the fat, which clings to the outside, fills the skin. When

[006-155] these are washed and dried and smoked, many deem them delicious. But

[006-156] these which the girls offered, as girls do, to show their love, by

[006-157] casting the string round the neck of the favored youth, were enchanted,

[006-158] and had they once put the necklace upon him he would have been

[006-159] overpowered. However, they knew not of this new magic which the Master

[006-160] had brought into the land, by which one can read the heart; so, as they

[006-161] sidled up unto him with smiles and blandishments, waving in the wind as

[006-162] they danced their garlands of enchanted sausages, he looked as if he

[006-163] wanted to be won. And when his dogs growled at them he cried,

[006-164] "_Cuss_!" (M.), which means _Stop_! but which the dogs only

[006-165] knew as "Hie, at them!" So they flew at the witches, and these flashed

[006-166] up like fire into their own dreadful forms of female fiends. Then there

[006-167] was a terrible tumult, for never before in the land of the Wabanaki had

[006-168] there been such a battle. All the earth and rocks around were torn up.

[006-169] All the while the Master cried to the dogs, "Stop! These are my

[006-170] sisters. Come off, ye evil beasts! Let them alone! Cease, oh cease!"

[006-171] Yet the more he exhorted them to peace the more they inclined to war,

[006-172] and the more fiercely they fought, until the witches fled.

[006-173] [Illustration: GLOOSKAP SETTING HIS DOGS ON THE WITCHES]

[006-174] Then he entered the wigwam where the old sorcerer sat, waiting for him

[006-175] as food. And the Master said, "Are you hungry? Or do you love sausages?

[006-176] Here they are!" Instantly casting the links around his neck, he was

[006-177] taken, and Glooskap slew him with one blow.
