---[036-001] I may here mention that I am well acquainted with old Peter Sabattis,

[036-002] the possessor of the "thunder-bullet."] one of the Passamaquoddy tribe.

[036-003] He has it yet. He found it in a crotch-root of a spruce-tree at Head

[036-004] Harbor, on the island of Campobello. This stone is a sign of good-luck

[036-005] to him who finds it.

[036-006] The thunder is the sound of the wings of the men who fly above. The

[036-007] lightning we see is the fire and smoke of their pipes.

[036-008] _Of the Woman who married the Thunder, and of their Boy._

[036-009] (Passamaquoddy.)

[036-010] Once a woman went to the edge of a lake [Footnote: It is impossible to

[036-011] distinguish in any Indian story between lake and sea.] and lay down to

[036-012] sleep. As she awoke, she saw a great serpent, with glittering eyes,

[036-013] crawl from the water, and stealthily approach her. She had no power to

[036-014] resist his embrace. After her return to her people her condition

[036-015] betrayed itself, and she was much persecuted; they pursued her with

[036-016] sticks and stones, howling abuse.

[036-017] She fled from the village; she went afar into wild places, and, sitting

[036-018] down on the grass, wept, wishing that she were dead. As she sat and

[036-019] wailed, a very beautiful girl, dressed in silver and gold, [Footnote:

[036-020] Both silver and gold were known in pre-Columbian, times to the Indians.

[036-021] I had a cousin who once found a very old stone pipe in which a small

[036-022] piece of gold had been set. Particles of gold are found in many

[036-023] mountain-streams in New England.] appeared, and after listening to her

[036-024] sad story said, "Follow me!"

[036-025] Then they went up on high into a mountain, through three rocks, until

[036-026] they came into a pleasant wigwam with a very smooth floor. An old man,

[036-027] so old that he was all white, came to meet them. Then he, taking a

[036-028] short stick, bade her dance. He began to sing, and as he sang she gave

[036-029] birth, one by one, to twelve serpents. These the old man killed in

[036-030] succession with his stick as they were born. Then she had become thin

[036-031] again, and was in her natural form.

[036-032] The old man had a son, Badawk, the Thunder, and a daughter,

[036-033] _Psawk-tankapic_, the Lightning, and when Thunder returned he

[036-034] offered to take her back to her own people, but she refused to go.

[036-035] Then the old man, said to his son, "Take her for your wife and be

[036-036] good to her." So they were married.

[036-037] In time she bore a son. When the boy could stand, the old man, who

[036-038] never leaves the mountain, called him to stand before him, while be

[036-039] fastened wings to the child. He was soon able, with these wings, to

[036-040] make a noise, which greatly pleased the grandfather. When a storm is

[036-041] approaching, the distant rumbling is the muttering thunder made by the

[036-042] child, but it is _Badawk_, his father, who comes in the dark cloud

[036-043] and makes the roaring crash, while _Psawk-tankapic_ flashes her

[036-044] lightnings.

[036-045] In after days, when the woman visited her people, she told them that

[036-046] they never need fear the thunder or lightning.

[036-047] AT-O-SIS, THE SERPENT

[036-048] _How Two Girls were changed to Water-Snakes, and of Two Others that

[036-049] became Mermaids._

[036-050] (Passamaquoddy.)

[036-051] Pocumkwess, or Thoroughfare, is sixty-five miles from Campobello. There

[036-052] was an Indian village there in the old times. Two young Indian girls

[036-053] had a strange habit of absenting themselves all day every Sunday. No

[036-054] one knew for a long time where they went or what they did. But this was

[036-055] how they passed their time. They would take a canoe and go six miles

[036-056] down the Grand Lake, where, at the north end, is a great ledge of rock

[036-057] and sixty feet of water. There they stayed. All day long they ran about

[036-058] naked or swam; they were wanton, witch-like girls, liking eccentric and

[036-059] forbidden ways.

[036-060] They kept this up for a long time. Once, while they were in the water,

[036-061] an Indian who was hunting spied them. He came nearer and nearer,

[036-062] unseen. He saw them come out of the water and sit on the shore, and

[036-063] then go in again; but as he looked they grew longer and longer, until

[036-064] they became snakes.

[036-065] He went home and told this. (But now they had been seen by a man they

[036-066] must keep the serpent form.) Men of the village, in four or five

[036-067] canoes, went to find them. They found the canoe and clothes of the

[036-068] girls; nothing more. A few days after, two men on Grand Lake saw the

[036-069] snake-girls on shore, showing their heads over the bushes. One began to

[036-070] sing.

[036-071] "N'ktieh ieben iut,

[036-072] Qu'spen ma ke owse."

[036-073] We are going to stay in this lake

[036-074] A few days, and then go down the river.

[036-075] Bid adieu to our friends for us;

[036-076] We are going to the great salt water.

[036-077] After singing this they sank into the water. They had very long hair.

[036-078] A picture of the man looking at the snake-girls was scraped for me by

[036-079] the Indian who told me this story. The pair were represented as snakes

[036-080] with female heads. When I first heard this tale, I promptly set it down

[036-081] as nothing else but the Melusina story derived from a Canadian French

[036-082] source. But I have since found that it is so widely spread, and is told

[036-083] in so many different forms, and is so deeply connected with tribal

[036-084] traditions and totems, that there is now no doubt in my mind that it is

[036-085] at least pre-Columbian.

[036-086] Another and a very curious version of this story was obtained by Mrs.

[036-087] W. Wallace Brown, who has been the chief discoverer of curious Indian

[036-088] lore among the Passamaquoddies. It is called:

[036-089] _Ne Hwas, the Mermaid._

[036-090] A long time ago there was an Indian, with his wife and two daughters.

[036-091] They lived by a great lake, or the sea, and the mother told her girls

[036-092] never to go into the water there, for that, if they did, something

[036-093] would happen to them.

[036-094] They, however, deceived her repeatedly. When swimming is prohibited it

[036-095] becomes delightful. The shore of this lake _sands_ away out or

[036-096] slopes to an island. One day they went to it, leaving their clothes on

[036-097] the beach. The parents missed them.

[036-098] The father went to seek them. He saw them swimming far out, and called

[036-099] to them. The girls swam up to the sand, but could get no further. Their

[036-100] father asked them why they could not. They cried that they had grown to

[036-101] be so heavy that it was impossible. They were all slimy; they grew to

[036-102] be snakes from below the waist. After sinking a few times in this

[036-103] strange slime they became very handsome, with long black hair and

[036-104] large, bright black eyes, with silver bands on their neck and arms.

[036-105] When their father went to get their clothes, they began to sing in the

[036-106] most exquisite tones:--

[036-107] "Leave them there!

[036-108] Do not touch them!

[036-109] Leave them there!"

[036-110] Hearing this, their mother began to weep, but the girls kept on:--

[036-111] "It is all our own fault,

[036-112] But do not blame us;

[036-113] 'T will be none the worse for you.

[036-114] When you go in your canoe,

[036-115] Then you need not paddle;

[036-116] We shall carry it along!"

[036-117] And so it was: when their parents went in the canoe, the girls carried

[036-118] it safely on everywhere.

[036-119] One day some Indians saw the girls' clothes on the beach, and so looked

[036-120] out for the wearers. They found them in the water, and pursued them,

[036-121] and tried to capture them, but they were so slimy that it was

[036-122] impossible to take them, till one, catching hold of a mermaid by her

[036-123] long black hair, cut it off.

[036-124] Then the girl began to rock the canoe, and threatened to upset it

[036-125] unless her hair was given to her again. The fellow who had played the

[036-126] trick at first refused, but as the mermaids, or snake-maids, promised

[036-127] that they should all be drowned unless this was done, the locks were

[036-128] restored. And the next day they were heard singing and were seen, and

[036-129] on her who had lost her hair it was all growing as long as ever.

[036-130] We may very easily detect the hand of Lox, the Mischief Maker, in this

[036-131] last incident. It was the same trick which Loki played on Sif, the wife

[036-132] of Odin. That both Lox and Loki were compelled to replace the hair and

[036-133] make it grow again--the one on the snake-maid, the other on the

[036-134] goddess--is, if a coincidence, at least a very remarkable one. It is a

[036-135] rule with little exception that where we have to deal with myths which

[036-136] have passed into romances or tales, that which was originally one

[036-137] character becomes many, just as the king who has but one name and one

[036-138] appearance at court assumes a score when he descends to disguise of low

[036-139] degree and goes among the people. But when, in addition to

[036-140] characteristic traits, we have even a single anecdote or attribute in

[036-141] common, the identification is very far advanced. When not one, but

[036-142] many, of these coincidences occur, we are in all probability at the

[036-143] truth. Thus we find in the mythology of the Wabanaki, as in the Edda,

[036-144] the chief evil being indulging in mere wanton, comic mischief, to an

[036-145] extent not to be found in the devil of any other race whatever. Here,

[036-146] in a mythical tale, the same mischief maker steals a snake-girl's hair,

[036-147] and is compelled to replace it. In the Edda, the corresponding mischief

[036-148] maker steals the hair of a goddess, and is also forced to make

[036-149] restitution. Yet this is only one of many such resemblances in these

[036-150] tales. It will be observed that in both cases the hair of the loser is

[036-151] made to grow again. But while the incident has in the Edda a meaning,

[036-152] as appears from its context, it has none in the Indian tale. All that

[036-153] we can conclude from this is that the Wabanaki tale is subsequent to

[036-154] the Norse, or taken from it. The incidents of tales are often

[036-155] remembered when the plot is lost. It is certainly very remarkable that,

[036-156] wherever the mischief maker occurs in these Indian tales, he in every

[036-157] narrative does something in common with his Norse prototype.

[036-158] _Of the Woman who loved a Serpent who lived in a Lake_.

[036-159] (Passamaquoddy.)

[036-160] Of old times. There was a very beautiful woman. She turned the heads of

[036-161] all the men. She married, and her husband died very soon after, but she

[036-162] immediately took another. Within a single year she had five husbands,

[036-163] and these were the cleverest and handsomest and bravest in the tribe.

[036-164] And then she married again.

[036-165] This, the sixth, was such a silent man that he passed for a fool. But

[036-166] he was wiser than people thought. He came to believe, by thinking it

[036-167] over, that this woman had some strange secret. He resolved to find it

[036-168] out. So he watched her all the time. He kept his eye on her by night

[036-169] and by day.

[036-170] It was summer, and she proposed to go into the woods to pick berries,

[036-171] and to camp there. By and by, when they were in the forest, she

[036-172] suggested that he should go on to the spot where they intended to

[036-173] remain and build a wigwam. He said that he would do so. But he went a

[036-174] little way into the woods and watched her.

[036-175] As soon as she believed that he was gone, she rose and walked rapidly

[036-176] onwards. He followed her, unseen. She went on, till, in a deep, wild

[036-177] place among the rocks, she came to a pond. She sat down and sang a

[036-178] song. A great foam, or froth, rose to the surface of the water. Then in

[036-179] the foam appeared the tail of a serpent. The creature was of immense

[036-180] size. The woman, who had laid aside all her garments, embraced the

[036-181] serpent, which twined around her, enveloping all her limbs and body in

[036-182] his folds. The husband watched it all. He now understood that, the

[036-183] venom of the serpent having entered the woman, she had saved her life

[036-184] by transferring it to others, who died.
