---[032-001] THE CHENOO LEGENDS.

[032-002] _I. The Chenoo, or the Story of a Cannibal with an Icy Heart._

[032-003] (Micmac and Passamaquoddy.)

[032-004] Of the old time. An Indian, with his wife and their little boy, went

[032-005] one autumn far away to hunt in the northwest. And having found a fit

[032-006] place to pass the winter, they built a wigwam. The man brought home the

[032-007] game, the woman dressed and dried the meat, the small boy played about

[032-008] shooting birds with bow and arrow; in Indian-wise all went well.

[032-009] One afternoon, when the man was away and the wife gathering wood, she

[032-010] heard a rustling in the bushes, as though some beast were brushing

[032-011] through them, and, looking up, she saw with horror something worse than

[032-012] the worst she had feared. It was an awful face glaring at her,--a

[032-013] something made of devil, man, and beast in their most dreadful, forms.

[032-014] It was like a haggard old man, with wolfish eyes; he was stark naked;

[032-015] his shoulders and lips were gnawed away, as if, when mad with hunger,

[032-016] he had eaten his own flesh. He carried a bundle on big back. The woman

[032-017] had heard of the terrible Chenoo, the being who comes from the far, icy

[032-018] north, a creature who is a man grown to be both devil and cannibal, and

[032-019] saw at once that this was one of them.

[032-020] Truly she was in trouble; but dire need gives quick wit, as it was with

[032-021] this woman, who, instead of showing fear, ran up and addressed him with

[032-022] fair words, as "My dear father," pretending surprise and joy, and,

[032-023] telling him how glad her heart was, asked where he had been so long.

[032-024] The Chenoo was amazed beyond measure at such a greeting where he

[032-025] expected yells and prayers, and in mute wonder let himself be led into

[032-026] the wigwam.

[032-027] She was a wise and good woman. She took him in; she said she was sorry

[032-028] to see him so woe-begone; she pitied his sad state; she brought a suit

[032-029] of her husband's clothes; she told him to dress himself and be cleaned.

[032-030] He did as she bade. He sat by the side of the wigwam, and looked surly

[032-031] and sad, but kept quiet. It was all a new thing to him.

[032-032] She arose and went out. She kept gathering sticks. The Chenoo rose and

[032-033] followed her. She was in great fear. "Now," she thought, "my death is

[032-034] near; now he will kill and devour me."

[032-035] The Chenoo came to her. He said, "Give me the axe!" She gave it, and he

[032-036] began to cut down the trees. Man never saw such chopping! The great

[032-037] pines fell right and left, like summer saplings; the boughs were hewed

[032-038] and split as if by a tempest. She cried out, "_Noo, tabeagul

[032-039] boohsoogul_!" "My father, there is enough!" [Footnote: The

[032-040] tremendous pine chopper is a character in another Indian tale.] He laid

[032-041] down the axe; he walked into the wigwam and sat down, always in grim

[032-042] silence. The woman gathered her wood, and remained as silent on the

[032-043] opposite side.

[032-044] She heard her husband coming. She ran out and told him all. She asked

[032-045] him to do as she was doing. He thought it well. He went in and spoke

[032-046] kindly. He said, "_N'chilch_," "My father-in-law," and asked where

[032-047] he had been so long. The Chenoo stared in amazement, but when he heard

[032-048] the man talk of all that had happened for years his fierce face grew

[032-049] gentler.

[032-050] They had their meal; they offered him food, but he hardly touched it.

[032-051] He lay down to sleep. The man and his wife kept awake in terror. When

[032-052] the fire burned up, and it became warm, the Chenoo asked that a screen

[032-053] should be placed before him. He was from the ice; he could not endure

[032-054] heat.

[032-055] For three days he stayed in the wigwam; for three days he was sullen

[032-056] and grim; he hardly ate. Then he seemed to change. He spoke to the

[032-057] woman; he asked her if she had any tallow. She told him they had much.

[032-058] He filled a large kettle; there was a gallon of it. He put it on the

[032-059] fire. When it was scalding hot he drank it all off at a draught.

[032-060] He became sick; he grew pale. He cast up all the horrors and

[032-061] abominations of earth, things appalling to every sense. When all was

[032-062] over he seemed changed. [Footnote: The Chenoo is not only a cannibal,

[032-063] but a ghoul. He preys on nameless horrors. In this case, "having

[032-064] yielded to the power of kindness, he has made up his mind to partake of

[032-065] the food and hospitality of his hosts,"" to change his life; but to

[032-066] adapt his system to the new regimen, he must thoroughly clear it of the

[032-067] old."--Rand manuscript. This is a very _naive_ and curious Indian

[032-068] conception of moral reformation. It appears to be a very ancient Eskimo

[032-069] tale, recast in modern time by some zealous recent Christian convert.]

[032-070] He lay down and slept. When he awoke he asked for food, and ate much.

[032-071] From that time he was kind and good. They feared him no more.

[032-072] They lived on meat such as Indians prepare. [Footnote: That is, cured,

[032-073] dried, smoked, and then packed and pressed in large blocks.] The Chenoo

[032-074] was tired of it. One day he said, "_N'toos_" (my daughter), "have

[032-075] you no _pela weoos_?" (fresh meat). She said, "No." When her husband

[032-076] returned the Chenoo saw that there was black mud on his snow-shoes. He

[032-077] asked him if there was a spring of water near. The friend said there was

[032-078] one half a day's journey distant. "We must go there to-morrow," said the

[032-079] Chenoo.

[032-080] And they went together, very early. The Indian was fleet in such running.

[032-081] But the old man, who seemed so wasted and worn, went on his snow-shoes

[032-082] like the wind. They came to the spring. [Footnote: "The Micmacs have two

[032-083] words for a spring of water: one for summer, _utkuboh_, which means

[032-084] that the water is cool; the other for winter, _keesoobok_, indicating

[032-085] that it is warm."--S.T. Rand.] It was large and beautiful; the snow was

[032-086] all melted away around it; the border was flat and green. [Footnote: Not

[032-087] uncommon round warm springs even in midwinter, and among ice and snow.]

[032-088] [Illustration: THE CHENOO AND THE LIZARD.]

[032-089] Then the Chenoo stripped himself, and danced around the spring his

[032-090] magic dance; and soon the water began to foam, and anon to rise and

[032-091] fall, as if some monster below were heaving in accord with the steps

[032-092] and the song. The Chenoo danced faster and wilder; then the head of an

[032-093] immense _Taktalok_, or lizard, rose above the surface. The old man

[032-094] killed it with a blow of his hatchet. Dragging it out he began again to

[032-095] dance. He brought out another, the female, not so large, but still

[032-096] heavy as an elk. They were small spring lizards, but the Chenook had

[032-097] conjured them; by his magic they were made into monsters.

[032-098] He dressed the game; he cut it up. He took the heads and feet and tails

[032-099] and all that he did not want, and cast them back into the spring. "They

[032-100] will grow again into many lizards," he said. When the meat was trimmed

[032-101] it looked like that of the bear. He bound it together with withes; he

[032-102] took it on his shoulders; he ran like the wind; his load was nothing.

[032-103] The Indian was a great runner; in all the land was not his like; but

[032-104] now he lagged far behind. "Can you go no faster than that?" asked the

[032-105] Chenoo. "The sun is setting; the red will be black anon. At this rate

[032-106] it will be dark ere we get home. Get on my shoulders."

[032-107] The Indian mounted on the load. The Chenoo bade him hold his head low,

[032-108] so that he could not be knocked off by the branches. "Brace your feet,"

[032-109] he said, "so as to be steady." Then the old man flew like the wind,--

[032-110] _ne[original illegible] sokano'v'jal samastukteskugul chel wegwasumug

[032-111] wegul_; the bushes whistled as they flew past them. They got home

[032-112] before sunset.

[032-113] The wife was afraid to touch such meat. [Footnote: "The Indians are

[032-114] much less particular than white men as to food, but they avoid

[032-115] _choojeeck_, or reptiles."--Rand manuscript.] But her husband was

[032-116] persuaded to eat of it. It was like bear's meat. The Chenoo fed on it.

[032-117] So they all lived as friends.

[032-118] Then the spring was at hand. One day the Chenoo told them that

[032-119] something terrible would soon come to pass. An enemy, a Chenoo, a woman

[032-120] was coming like wind, yes--on the wind--from the north to kill him.

[032-121] There could be no escape from the battle. She would be far more

[032-122] furious, mad, and cruel than any male, even one of his own cruel race,

[032-123] could be. He knew not how the battle would end; but the man and his

[032-124] wife must be put in a place of safety. To keep from hearing the

[032-125] terrible war-whoops of the Chenoo, which is death to mortals, their

[032-126] ears must be closed. They must hide themselves in a cave.

[032-127] Then he sent the woman for the bundle which he had brought with him,

[032-128] and which had hung untouched on a branch of a tree since he had been

[032-129] with them. And he said if she found aught in it offensive to her to

[032-130] throw it away, but to certainly bring him a smaller bundle which was

[032-131] within the other. So she went and opened it, and that which she found

[032-132] therein was a pair of human legs and feet, the remains of some earlier

[032-133] horrid meal. She threw them far away. The small bundle she brought to

[032-134] him.

[032-135] The Chenoo opened it and took from it a pair of horns,--horns of the

[032-136] _chepitchcalm_, or dragon. One of them has two branches; the other

[032-137] is straight and smooth. [Footnote: In the winter of 1882-1883, Tomah

[032-138] Josephs killed a deer whose horns were precisely like those of the

[032-139] chepitchcalm as regarded shape.] They were golden-bright. He gave the

[032-140] straight horn to the Indian; he kept the other. He said that these were

[032-141] magical weapons, and the only ones of any use in the coming fight. So

[032-142] they waited for the foe.

[032-143] And the third day came. The Chenoo was fierce and bold; he listened; he

[032-144] had no fear. He heard the long and awful scream--like nothing of earth--of

[032-145] the enemy, as she sped through the air far away in the icy north,

[032-146] long ere the others could hear it. And the manner of it was this: that

[032-147] if they without harm should live after hearing the first deadly yell of

[032-148] the enemy they could take no harm, and if they did but hear the

[032-149] answering shout of their friend all would be well with them. [Footnote:

[032-150] In all this we clearly perceive the horrible scream of the

[032-151] _angakok_, or Eskimo Shaman, trained through years and generations

[032-152] to utter sounds which terrify even brave men.] But he said, "Should you

[032-153] hear me call for help, then hasten with the horn, and you may save my

[032-154] life."

[032-155] They did as he bade: they stopped their ears; they hid in a deep hole

[032-156] dug in the ground. All at once the cry of the foe burst on them like

[032-157] screaming thunder; their ears rang with pain: they were well-nigh

[032-158] killed, for all the care they had taken. But then they heard the

[032-159] answering cry of their friend, and were no longer in danger from mere

[032-160] noise.

[032-161] The battle begun, the fight was fearful. The monsters, by their magic

[032-162] with their rage, rose to the size of mountains. The tall pines were

[032-163] torn up, the ground trembled as in an earthquake, rocks crashed upon

[032-164] rocks, the conflict deepened and darkened; no tempest was ever so

[032-165] terrible. Then the male Chenoo was heard crying: "_N'loosook!

[032-166] choogooye! abog unumooe!_" "My son-in-law, come and help me!"
