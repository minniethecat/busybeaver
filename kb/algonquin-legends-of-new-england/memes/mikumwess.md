# Mikumwess

## Identity and Attributes

Mikumwess (also rendered as Mik Um Wess or Mikumwesu) is the Indian Puck or Robin Good-Fellow of Wabanaki folklore. He belongs to what Leland calls "the minor mythology of dwarfs and fairies" (002-133) — the innumerable nature spirits that populated every rock, river, and ancient hill in the Wabanaki world. His most distinctive attribute, recorded in both oral tradition and visual art, is his red cap.

## Iconography

A frontispiece illustration in Leland's original 1884 edition of *The Algonquin Legends of New England* depicts Mikumwess "from a scraping on birch bark by Tomah Josephs, Indian Governor at Peter Dana's Point, Maine." The illustration shows Mikumwess wearing a red cap, a detail that Leland noted was "like the Norse Goblin" — one of the many parallels between Wabanaki and Norse folklore that suggested to him the possibility of historical cultural transmission (frontispiece).

## Relationships

### With the M'teoulin Tradition

Mikumwess belongs to the broader spiritual ecology of the M'teoulin worldview, in which every natural feature possessed its indwelling spirit (002-137). As a forest elf, Mikumwess represents one of the more approachable and folkloric spirit types — less terrifying than the Chenoo or the stone giants, and more integrated into the everyday experience of the Wabanaki people.

### Norse Parallels

Leland drew explicit comparisons between Mikumwess and the Norse goblin or *puck*, noting the shared red cap, the forest dwelling, and the trickster nature. These parallels are part of a larger pattern of resemblances between Wabanaki and Norse mythology that Leland documented throughout his work. Whether these similarities represent independent invention, ancient cultural contact across the North Atlantic, or transmission via the Eskimo (who ranged as far south as Massachusetts before the fourteenth century), remains a subject of scholarly discussion.

## Cultural Significance

Mikumwess represents the enduring vitality of Wabanaki folk belief into the nineteenth century. The fact that Tomah Josephs, an Indian Governor, produced a birch-bark scraping of Mikumwess in the 1880s testifies to the living continuity of this tradition. Unlike the grand mythological figures of Glooskap and Malsum, Mikumwess belongs to the everyday supernatural — the kind of being one might encounter at the edge of the forest or in the flicker of firelight.

The figure also provides evidence for the visual tradition of Wabanaki mythology. The birch-bark scraping medium itself is significant: it represents an indigenous art form used to record and transmit mythological imagery, parallel to the oral tradition that preserved the narratives.
