## Overview

**Cape Blomidon** is a dramatic red sandstone headland rising from the shores of the Bay of Fundy in present-day Nova Scotia. In Mi'kmaq legend, it is one of the most sacred landscapes associated with **[[glooskap|Glooskap]]**, the great culture hero. The cape and its distinctive agates appear in multiple traditions recorded by Leland, marking Blomidon as a central landmark in the mythological geography of the Wabanaki peoples.

## Geography

Cape Blomidon stands at the head of the Bay of Fundy, at the eastern end of the Annapolis Valley [019-155]. The cape's red sandstone cliffs, shaped by the world's highest tides, are visible from across the bay. The agates found along its shores—semi-precious stones formed in the region's volcanic basalt—feature prominently in Glooskap traditions [019-144].

## Role in Legends

### The Great Dam

One of the most significant Glooskap stories associated with Blomidon tells of how the culture hero and his companions built a great dam across the waters at Cape Blomidon [009-185]. This dam was part of Glooskap's larger efforts to shape the landscape and control the waters for the benefit of his people. The story describes how those who were with Glooskap constructed the dam at Blomidon, and afterwards Glooskap traveled to the opposite shore [009-166] [009-175].

### The Agates of Blomidon

The agates found at Cape Blomidon carry a special tradition in Mi'kmaq oral history. Leland notes an "allusion to the agates of Cape Blomidon" that "refers to a tradition" concerning their origin and supernatural significance [019-144]. These stones were believed to hold magical properties connected to Glooskap's presence at the cape.

### Glooskap's Vigil

Another tradition places Glooskap standing watch from the crest of Blomidon, surveying the lands and waters of his domain [019-184]. From this vantage point, the culture hero could observe the Bay of Fundy and the surrounding territory, maintaining his watch over the Wabanaki peoples.

## Historical Context

Cape Blomidon forms part of the traditional territory of the Mi'kmaq people, who have inhabited the region for thousands of years. The name "Blomidon" is believed to derive from the Mi'kmaq word for the cape. At the time of Leland's collection, the area was part of Kings County, Nova Scotia, and the Mi'kmaq oral traditions concerning Glooskap's presence at Blomidon remained active among local storytellers.
