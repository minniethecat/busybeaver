# Campobello

## Geography

Campobello Island is located in the Bay of Fundy, in the Canadian province of New Brunswick, near the border with Maine. It lies within the traditional territory of the Passamaquoddy people, one of the principal Wabanaki tribes. The island's location — at the meeting point of land and sea, of Canadian and American territory, of Passamaquoddy and broader Wabanaki cultural space — made it a natural site for the transmission and preservation of oral traditions.

## Historical Context

### Leland's Fieldwork

In the summer of 1882, Charles G. Leland began collecting Passamaquoddy traditions and folk-lore at Campobello. He initially expected to find very little — the Indians were few in number, surrounded by white settlements, and thoroughly converted to Roman Catholicism (001-016). Instead, he discovered day by day "a far grander mythology than that which has been made known to us by either the Chippewa or Iroquois Hiawatha Legends, and that this was illustrated by an incredible number of tales" (001-020).

### Discovery of the Oral Epic

At Campobello, Leland made the crucial discovery that many of these stories had once been sung as poems. The old people declared they had heard from their progenitors that all of these stories were once sung; they themselves remembered when many of them were poems (001-024). This was later proved when Leland received a long Micmac tale that had been sung by an Indian, confirming the existence of a poetic epic tradition underlying the prose narratives.

## Cultural Significance

Campobello holds a place in the history of American ethnology comparable to the significance of specific fieldwork sites in other traditions. It was here that the rich oral literature of the Wabanaki was first systematically documented, preserving a mythology that Leland considered "utterly and entirely unlike anything else that is American" (002-145). Without the collecting work done at Campobello, the grand cycle of Glooskap legends — which Leland argued was far more extensive than the better-known Hiawatha cycle — might have been lost to scholarship.

The island remains within Passamaquoddy traditional territory, and its name in indigenous memory is tied not only to the nineteenth-century collecting work but to the deeper history of the people who have inhabited the region for millennia.
