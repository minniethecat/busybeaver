## Overview

The **Gaspé Peninsula** (French: _Gaspésie_) is a large peninsula in eastern Quebec, Canada, extending into the Gulf of St. Lawrence. In the Algonquin legends collected by Leland, Gaspé appears in connection with the **Shick-shoe Mountains**, a range said to have been created by Glooskap's transformative acts during the mythic age, and with the mysterious figure or entity known as the **Gaspereanx**.

## Geography

The Gaspé Peninsula lies north of New Brunswick and east of the St. Lawrence River. Its rugged terrain includes the Chic-Choc Mountains (the "Shick-shoe" of the legends), which run along the peninsula's spine. The name appears in the legends as the site where Glooskap turned something—or someone—"into the Shick-shoe mountains in the Gaspe peninsula" (004-027), a transformation story typical of the culture hero's landscape-shaping mythology.

## Role in Legends

### The Shick-shoe Mountains

The creation of the Shick-shoe Mountains is attributed to Glooskap's power of transformation. According to the legend, a being or object was transformed "into the Shick-shoe mountains in the Gaspe peninsula" (004-027), joining other landscape features that Glooskap shaped during his passage through the Wabanaki world. Like [[blomidon|Cape Blomidon]], the Shick-shoe range stands as a permanent marker of the mythic age.

### The Gaspereanx

A mysterious figure or entity called **Gaspereanx** is mentioned in connection with the region. The text records "with wondrous beauty the Gaspereanx" (019-158), suggesting a legendary being or perhaps a personification of the Gaspé landscape itself. The nature of the Gaspereanx is not fully detailed in the surviving legends, leaving it as one of the more enigmatic references in the corpus.

## Historical Context

The Gaspé Peninsula was historically inhabited by the Mi'kmaq people, who referred to the region as _Gespe'gewa'gi_ ("the last land"). The peninsula marked the northern boundary of Mi'kmaq traditional territory. French explorer Jacques Cartier landed at Gaspé in 1534 and claimed the region for France. At the time of Leland's collection, the peninsula was part of the province of Quebec and known for its fishing and lumber industries.
