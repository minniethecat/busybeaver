---[023-001] Then they laid them down to sleep; but before their slumber came they

[023-002] heard a doleful, bitter voice chanting aloud and shouting, and it was

[023-003] _Chamach keg wech_, or the Neckbone, bewailing the scorn that had

[023-004] been put upon him, and reviling them with all manner of curses. Then

[023-005] the elder said, "There, truly, I said it. I knew you would be our death

[023-006] if you did not mind me:" it being in all cases an esteemed solace for

[023-007] every woman and most men to say, "I told you so!" But the younger,

[023-008] being well-nigh frightened to a corpse, in a soft whisper implored the

[023-009] elder to let her hide herself in her roll of hair, [Footnote: That is,

[023-010] the elder should retain the human form, and the younger become a

[023-011] weasel.] which the Voice, mocking her, repeated; adding thereto all the

[023-012] reviling and railing that Mitche-hant, the devil, himself ever yet

[023-013] invented, and abusing her so for her past life, and exhorting her so

[023-014] for all the sins, slips, and slaps therein (of which there were many),

[023-015] that even the impenitent little Weasel repented and wept bitterly.

[023-016] Howbeit no further harm came to them beyond this, so that the next

[023-017] morning they went their way in peace; and I warrant you Master Neckbone

[023-018] got no kicks that day from them, departing. [Footnote: This incident of

[023-019] the Neckbone is very much like the common nursery tale of Teeny Tiny,

[023-020] in which an old woman takes home a human bone and puts it in the

[023-021] cupboard. It torments her all night by its cries.]

[023-022] Then, coming to a river, they saw on the other side a handsome young

[023-023] man holding a bow, and to him they called, making their usual offer to

[023-024] become his wives, and all for no greater thing than to carry them over

[023-025] the ferry. And this man's name was See-witch, [Footnote: A kind of

[023-026] small sea-duck.] and to please them he did indeed pass them over in his

[023-027] canoe; but as for taking them home, he said that he had housekeepers in

[023-028] store, and as many as he needed just then, and that of a kind who kept

[023-029] him very busy. So they went their way onwards.

[023-030] And coming anon to the great sea, they beheld yet another canoe with

[023-031] two men therein, and these were Kwe-moo, the Loon, and Mahgwis, the

[023-032] Scapegrace. And embarking with them, Loon soon began to admire the

[023-033] girls greatly. And saying many sweet things, he told them that he dwelt

[023-034] in the Wigem territory, or in the land of the Owealkesk, [Footnote: A

[023-035] very beautiful species of sea-duck.] of which he himself was one. But

[023-036] the Mahgwis whispered to them aside that they should put little trust

[023-037] in what he told them, for Loon was a great liar. Now when they came to

[023-038] the land of the Owealkesk, they were amazed at the beauty of the

[023-039] people, and saw that all in that land was lovely, nor did they

[023-040] themselves seem less marvelously fair to the men therein. Indeed, the

[023-041] poor little Weasels began to see the end of their sorrows, for, being

[023-042] water-fairies, these sea-birds were nigh akin to them. And there was a

[023-043] great feast, a great dance, and great games held in honor of their

[023-044] arrival, and the two finest young Sea-Duck men, utterly unheeding the

[023-045] old Loon, who believed indeed that they were his own wives, carried

[023-046] them off, and nothing loath wedded them.

[023-047] And it was in this wise. There was a canoe-race, and Kwee-moo, being

[023-048] bitterly angry that he was held of so little account in the Sea-Duck

[023-049] land, went forth with the rest, and, paddling far outside, upset his

[023-050] canoe, and making as if he were drowning called to the Weasels to come

[023-051] and save him. But the Sea-Ducks laughed, and said, "Let him alone.

[023-052] Truly he will never drown. We know him." And the race ended they went

[023-053] ashore in peace. [Footnote: Here the Micmac narrative ends. The rest is

[023-054] as it was given to me by Noel Josephs, or _Chi gatch gok_, the

[023-055] Raven, a Passamaquoddy. It would not be a complete Indian tale if a man

[023-056] having received a slight or injury did not take a bloody revenge for

[023-057] it.]

[023-058] And that night they danced late, and the Weasels, being better pleased

[023-059] with the two handsome Sea-Ducks than with Loon, forthwith divorced

[023-060] themselves out of hand, and at once married them, going to where their

[023-061] canoe lay, to pass the bridal night. Now Loon had not gone to the

[023-062] dance, but sat at home nursing his vengeance till he was well-nigh mad.

[023-063] And as the Weasels did not return, he went forth and sought them; and

[023-064] this he did so carefully that at last he found all four by the sea,

[023-065] sound asleep. Whereupon he, with his knife, slew the young men, and

[023-066] being in great fear of their friends took his canoe and went down the

[023-067] river to kill a deer. But not daring to return, and being mad for loss

[023-068] of the Weasels, and fearing to fall into the hands of the enemy, he in

[023-069] despair took his knife and killed himself.

[023-070] Yet the Weasels, who had seen the deed done, did not betray him, for

[023-071] there was at least so much truth left in them. And they lived with the

[023-072] Sea-Ducks, and I doubt me not went on marrying and mischief-making

[023-073] after their wont even unto the end of their days. And their kind are

[023-074] not dead as yet in any land.

[023-075] * * * * *

[023-076] This is a fair specimen of many Indian legends. So much of it as is

[023-077] Micmac was told to Mr. Rand by a highly intelligent Indian, named

[023-078] Benjamin Brooks, who was certain that the story was of great antiquity.

[023-079] As I at first heard it, it was limited to the adventure with the Stars,

[023-080] but I was told that this formed only a part of an extremely long

[023-081] narrative. It consists, in fact, of different parts of other tales

[023-082] connected, and I doubt not that there is much more of it. It cannot

[023-083] escape the reader versed in fairy-lore that the incident of the

[023-084] water-maiden captured by her clothes is common to all European nations,

[023-085] but that it is especially Norse; while the adventures of the Wolverine,

[023-086] and indeed his whole character, are strangely suggestive of Loki, the

[023-087] Spirit of mere Mischief, who becomes evil. The fact that both Loki and

[023-088] Lox end their earthly career at a waterfall is very curious. The two

[023-089] also become, in wizard fashion, women at will. But it is chiefly in the

[023-090] extreme and wanton devilishness of their tricks that they are alike.

[023-091] Many other resemblances will suggest themselves to those who know the

[023-092] Eddas.

[023-093] In the Passamaquoddy version of this tale, it is Seewitch, and not the

[023-094] Loon, who plays the part of the jealous husband at the end. The career

[023-095] of the Weasels _seems_ to set forth the adventures of a couple of

[023-096] Indian Becky Sharps, very much in the spirit of an Indian Thackeray.

[023-097] The immorality of these damsels, the sponging of Marten, the deviltry

[023-098] of Lox, the servile follies and ferocious vindictiveness of the Loon,

[023-099] all seem to impress the composer of the tale as so many bubbles rising

[023-100] and falling on the sea of life, only remarkable for the sun-gleam of

[023-101] humor which they reflect. Outside these tales I know of nothing which

[023-102] so resembles the inner spirit of Aristophanes, Rabelais, and

[023-103] Shakespeare. I do not say that the genius of these great masters is in

[023-104] them, but their manner of seeing humor and wickedness combined. The

[023-105] cause of this lies in the cultivated stoicism with which every Indian

[023-106] trains himself to regard life. The inevitable result of such culture is

[023-107] always in some way a kind of humor, either grim or gay.

[023-108] A re-perusal of the Eddas has impressed me with the remarkable

[023-109] resemblance of Lox, the Wolverine, to Loki. The story begins with the

[023-110] incident of a bird maiden caught by a trick, and married. This is

[023-111] distinctly Scandinavian. It is known in all lands, but the Norse made

[023-112] the most of it. Then the two girls sit and choose the kind of stars

[023-113] they will have. In the Eskimo (Rink, No. 8), two girls sitting on a

[023-114] beach, talking in the same way, seeing eagles' and whales' bones by

[023-115] them, declare that they would like to marry, the one an eagle, the

[023-116] other a whale, and both get their wishes. In the Norse legends stars

[023-117] are like human beings. Lox is pursued by a giant bird; Loki is chased

[023-118] by Thiassi, the giant, in eagle plumage. Again, in the Edda a giant

[023-119] eagle drags and trails Loki over woods and mountains, till he screams

[023-120] for pity. The Wolverine's race with a stone giant also recalls this

[023-121] race, the eagle being really one of the Jotuns, who were also all

[023-122] mountains and rocks. The Wolverine wizard becomes a girl, merely to

[023-123] make mischief. Loki took the form of a woman in Fensal, where he

[023-124] schemed to kill Balder. This is certainly a strange coincidence; for as

[023-125] in the Edda, Loki's becoming a woman led to all the subsequent tragedy

[023-126] and to his own doom, so in the Indian tale the very same thing caused

[023-127] the Wolverine to be chased to the high waterfall, where, owing to his

[023-128] own tricks, he perished, just as Loki came to grief in Franangursfors,

[023-129] the bright and glistening cataract. But the most remarkable point is

[023-130] that the general immoral character of the Lox, [Footnote: The

[023-131] coincidence of name amounts to nothing, as Lox is not, I believe, an

[023-132] Indian word.] or Wolverine, is so much like that of Loki, consisting of

[023-133] evil or mischief of the worst kind, always tempered by humor, which

[023-134] provokes a laugh. Now to find a similar and very singular character

[023-135] supported by several coincidences of incident is, if nothing more, at

[023-136] least very remarkable.

[023-137] Loki is fire, and Lox, when killed in another tale, is revived by heat.

[023-138] He is carried off by the Culloo, or cloud, and let fall, typifying fire

[023-139] or lightning coming from a cloud. Again, in another story he dies for

[023-140] want of fire. And he twice dies by drowning; that is, the fire is

[023-141] quenched by water.

[023-142] In one of the Passamaquoddy versions of this tale, which is, though

[023-143] less detailed, far superior in humor to the Micmac, the Loon is cheated

[023-144] by his two nephews, the _Assoops_, a species of loon, who steal

[023-145] the Weasels from him. He revenges himself, not by murdering, but by

[023-146] merely frightening them. He fills a bladder with blood, puts it under

[023-147] his shirt, and then stabs himself. They, thinking he is killed, lament,

[023-148] when he grandly comes to life, and is regarded as a great magician.

[023-149] _Of the Wolverine and the Wolves, or how Master Lox Froze to

[023-150] Death._

[023-151] (Passamaquoddy.)

[023-152] Of old times it came to pass that Master Lox, the Wolverine, or Indian

[023-153] Devil, he who was slain many times and as often rose from the dead, found

[023-154] himself deeply down in luck; for he was crossing a wide and dismal heath

[023-155] in winter-time, being but poorly provided in any way for travel. The wind

[023-156] blew like knives; the snow fell; sleet, frost, hail, and rain seemed to

[023-157] come all together in bad company, and still Lox was not happy, although

[023-158] he had no blanket or fur coat beyond his own. Yet this evil-minded jolly

[023-159] companion with every vice had one virtue, and that was that of all the

[023-160] beasts of the forest or devils in _P'lamkik'_ he was the hardest

[023-161] hearted, toughest, and most unconquerable, being ever the first to fight

[023-162] and the last to give in, which even then he did not, never having done

[023-163] it and never intending to; whence it happened that he was greatly admired

[023-164] and made much of by all the blackguardly beasts of the backwoods,--wherein

[023-165] they differed but little from many among men.
