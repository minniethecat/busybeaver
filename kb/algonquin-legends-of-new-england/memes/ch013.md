---[013-001] Yet the young man had more to do; for the chief said, "There is here a

[013-002] man who has never been beaten in running, and thou must strive with him

[013-003] in that and overcome him, to win thy wife." And the race was appointed;

[013-004] but ere it came off he that was Mikumwess lent to his friend the magic

[013-005] pipe to give him power. [Footnote: It may be observed that Indian magic

[013-006] depends on fetich, or objects having innate power. Glooskap himself

[013-007] relies on his belt, and when he lends it to Marten, the boy becomes

[013-008] "manitoo," as the more Western Indians term it. There is in the early

[013-009] red Indian mythology really no God; only more or less powerful

[013-010] magicians.] And when he that was the racer of the village met the young

[013-011] man, the youth said, "Who art thou?" and he replied, "I am Wey-ad-esk"

[013-012] (the Northern Lights, M.); "but who art thou?" And he answered, "I am

[013-013] Wosogwodesk" (the Chain Lightning). And they ran. In an instant they

[013-014] were no longer in sight; they were far away over the most distant

[013-015] hills. Then all sat and waited, and ere it was noon he that was the

[013-016] Chain Lightning returned, and he was not out of breath, nor weary, and

[013-017] he had gone round the world. And at evening they saw the Northern

[013-018] Lights return, and he trembled and quivered with fatigue; yet for all

[013-019] that he had not been round the world, but had turned back. And the old

[013-020] chief, seeing him beaten, exclaimed, "This time I shall lose my child!"

[013-021] And yet there was another trial of the young man ere he could win her

[013-022] whom he wanted. For the chief had a man whom no one could overcome in

[013-023] swimming and diving, and it was chiefly in this last thing that he

[013-024] excelled. And the young man must strive with him. And when they met he

[013-025] asked the man of the village his name, and he replied, "I am an

[013-026] _Ukchigumooech_" (a Sea Duck, M.); "but who are you?" And he

[013-027] answered, "I am a _Kweemoo_" (a Loon, M.). So they dived, and

[013-028] after a time the Sea Duck rose again for breath, but those who waited

[013-029] waited long indeed ere they saw the Loon. And an hour passed, and he

[013-030] came not, and yet another ere they beheld him; but when he at last rose

[013-031] the old chief said, "This is the end of all our weary work, for this

[013-032] time truly I have lost my child."

[013-033] Yet it was not the end of the wonderful deeds which were done in that

[013-034] village by the power of the great Glooskap. For the Mikumwess, at the

[013-035] great dance which was held that evening at the wedding, astonished all

[013-036] who beheld him. As he danced around the circle, upon the very hard

[013-037] beaten floor, they saw his feet sink deeper at every step, and ever

[013-038] deeper as the dance went on; ploughing the ground up into high, uneven

[013-039] ridges, forming a trench as he went, until at length only his head was

[013-040] to be seen. [Footnote: This is very characteristic of the true

[013-041] magician, both in the Algonquin and Eskimo folk-lore. "The

[013-042] _angakok_," or sorcerer of Greenland, "after meeting with

[013-043] _tomarsuk_, or guardian spirits, sometimes manifested it by his

[013-044] feet sinking into the rocky ground _just as if in snow_." (Rink.)

[013-045] This phrase indicates the Northern origin of the idea, which occurs in

[013-046] many Indian stories. I have been assured in all faith that there is a

[013-047] Passamaquoddy _m'teoulin_, or sorcerer, now living, who can walk

[013-048] up to his knees in a floor or in the paved street, and an honest and

[013-049] trustworthy Indian assured me that he had seen him do it.]

[013-050] And this ended the dancing for that night, since the ground was no

[013-051] longer to be danced upon by anybody except wizards and witches.

[013-052] Then the young man and his wife and the Mikumwess entered their canoe

[013-053] and sailed _boosijk_ (homewards, M.). And yet their trials were

[013-054] not over. [Footnote: These subsequent trials were not inflicted by the

[013-055] old chief, but were, as appears by comparison with other legends,

[013-056] simply jokes played by the incorrigible Glooskap. It is most probable

[013-057] that in its original form this remarkable myth was all _maya_,

[013-058] or illusion, and the whole a series of illusions, caused by the

[013-059] arch-conjurer, typifying natural phenomena.] For they had not gone

[013-060] far ere they saw an awful storm coming to meet them; and he that had

[013-061] the Elfin spells knew that it was raised by _boo-oin_, or sorcery,

[013-062] since these storms are the worst of all. Then, without fear, he rose,

[013-063] and, filling his lungs and puffing his cheeks, he blew against the

[013-064] tempest, wind against wind, until he blew the wind away, and the great

[013-065] water was '_aoobuneak_', as calm and smooth as before.

[013-066] So they sailed on over the sunlit sea, but it was not long before the

[013-067] Elf-gifted saw rising among the waves far before them a dark mass,

[013-068] which soon proved to be a tremendous Beast coming to attack them. And

[013-069] as he drew near they saw it was Quahbeet, the giant beaver, and his eyes

[013-070] were angry. [Footnote: From the beginning, when _Quahbeetsis_,

[013-071] the son of the Beaver, inspired Malsumsis with hatred of Glooskap, this

[013-072] quadruped appears as an enemy.] But the Mikumwess, seeing this, steered

[013-073] straight to meet the monster, and, coming to him, said, "I am the great

[013-074] hunter of beavers; lo, I am their butcher; many a one has fallen by my

[013-075] hand." [Footnote: This is oddly like the speech of the beaver-killer in

[013-076] _The Hunting of the Snark_.] Now the Beaver had placed himself

[013-077] under water, with his tail out of it and rising upwards, that he might

[013-078] sink the canoe with a blow thereof; for the Beaver strikes mightily in

[013-079] such wise, as is his wont. But he of the magic power, with one blow of

[013-080] his tomahawk, cut the tail from the body, and sailed onward.

[013-081] Yet they had not gone far ere, on rounding a point, they saw before

[013-082] them another animal of giant size, who likewise had his tail in the

[013-083] air, waiting to overcome them, and this was A-bekk-thee-lo (M.), the

[013-084] Skunk. Yet ere he made his hideous attack the Mikumwess, ever on the

[013-085] watch, caught up his spear, and, hurling it, pierced A-bekk-thee-lo,

[013-086] who did but kick two or three times ere he died. And, stepping ashore,

[013-087] he who had slain him took a pole, a long dead pine, which lay upon the

[013-088] sand, and, transfixing the Skunk, lifted him high in air, and, planting

[013-089] the tree on the ground, left him, saying scornfully, as he left,

[013-090] "_Lik cho je nain_!" which, being interpreted, meaneth, "And now

[013-091] show your tail there!" [Footnote: The Skunk is here a parody on the

[013-092] Beaver.]

[013-093] So they returned safely. And Glooskap met them at the landing, and his

[013-094] first words were, "Well, my friends, I see that you have brought back

[013-095] my canoe." And they answered, "We have, indeed." Then he inquired," Has

[013-096] all gone well with ye?" And they replied that it had. Then Glooskap,

[013-097] laughing, let them know that in all they had experienced he had been

[013-098] busy, and that in all their triumphs he had had a hand. And to the

[013-099] Mikumwess he said, "Go now thy ways, thou and these, and ever lead

[013-100] happy lives: thou amid the Elfin, they among mankind. And be sure of

[013-101] this, that if danger or trouble should come to you, you have but to

[013-102] think of me, and verily aid will come." So they rose and went to their

[013-103] wigwams. [Footnote: In its earlier form this must have been a very

[013-104] remarkable narrative, or poem. That the two combatants in the race were

[013-105] originally the personified Northern Lights and Lightning, and that

[013-106] these were _not_ merely names assumed for boasting, is shown by

[013-107] the incident that the Lightning actually _passed round the world_,

[013-108] while the Aurora Borealis only covered a portion of it. The diving is

[013-109] either a later addition, or it represents the same stupendous spirits

[013-110] taking on the appearance of mastering the element of water as well as

[013-111] that of fire. Without carrying the Solar myth theory to extremes, it

[013-112] cannot be denied that Glooskap appears in several of these stories as

[013-113] Spring, or as the melter of ice, the conqueror of the frozen stream and

[013-114] of the iceberg. In this narrative he is active and creative Nature

[013-115] itself, directing and sporting with the warring elements. His vast

[013-116] practical joking cannot fail to remind the reader yet again of the

[013-117] Norse deities and their jovial household godhood.

[013-118] This tradition is Micmac, and taken almost entirely from Mr. Rand's

[013-119] manuscript. It should be borne in mind that it is not from a single

[013-120] story of this collection, but from a careful analysis and comparison of

[013-121] them all, that their entire value is to be ascertained.

[013-122] Certain incidents in this tale deserve special attention. The young men

[013-123] go to a land of evil sorcerers, of _boo-oin_. When one is required

[013-124] to run a race he conquers because he is really the Lightning. When Thor

[013-125] visits Utgard Loki, there is also a race, in which Hugi wins, because

[013-126] he is _Thought_ disguised as a man. Glooskap has a canoe, which is

[013-127] sometimes immensely large, but which at other times shrinks to a very

[013-128] small size. In the Edda, Odin is said to have had made for him by the

[013-129] dwarfs a boat, Skidbladnir, which, like Glooskap's bark, expanded or

[013-130] diminished. Sigurd, in the New Edda, is obliged to kill a dragon, and

[013-131] it is very remarkable that he does it by a special previous

[013-132] preparation. That is to say, he digs a little ditch, and when the

[013-133] dragon crawls over it the hero pierces him with his sword. In this

[013-134] story the Indian lays a log over the dragon's hole, to enable him to

[013-135] chop his head off. The dragon, or horned snake, is an old-time

[013-136] tradition in America, or pre-Columbian.]

[013-137] _How a Certain Wicked Witch sought to cajole the Great and Good

[013-138] Glooskap, and of her Punishment._

[013-139] (Micmac.)

[013-140] _N'karnayoo_, of old time. Once it came to pass that Glooskap met

[013-141] with an evil witch, and she had made herself like unto a fair young

[013-142] girl, and believed that he could not know who she was. And she asked

[013-143] him to take her with him in his canoe. So they sailed out over a summer

[013-144] sea: and as they went the witch sought to beguile him with sweet words;

[013-145] but he answered naught, for he wist well what kind of passenger he had

[013-146] on board. And as they went on she played her cajoleries, but he

[013-147] remained grim as a bear. Then she, being angry, showed it, and there

[013-148] arose a great storm. The wind howled over the waves as they rose and

[013-149] fell, like white wolves jumping while they run, the first lightnings

[013-150] flashed, and the sky grew dark as night. The Master was angered that so

[013-151] mean a creature dared to play him such tricks, and, paddling the canoe

[013-152] to the beach, he leaped ashore. Then giving the bark, with the witch in

[013-153] it, a push out to sea, he cried to her, "Sail thou with the devil! But

[013-154] never be in human form again, O she-beast!"

[013-155] Then she, being frightened, said, "Master, what wilt thou that I

[013-156] become?" And he replied, "Whatever thou wilt; that grace alone I give

[013-157] thee." And in despair she plunged into the waters, and became a

[013-158] _keegunibe_, a ferocious fish, which has upon its back a great

[013-159] fin, which it shows like a sail when swimming through the water. So the

[013-160] canoe and the witch became one in the evil fish, and the Indians to

[013-161] this day when they see it, cry, "See the witch, who was punished by the

[013-162] great Master!"

[013-163] Now of sinful men, evil beasts, foul sorcerers, witches, and giants,

[013-164] there were in those days many who sought to do great harm to Glooskap;

[013-165] but of them all there did not escape any; verily, no, not one.

[013-166] [Footnote: A Micmac story, from the Rand manuscript. I believe that the

[013-167] fish here spoken of is a shark.]
