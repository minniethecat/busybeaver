---[003-001] It is to the Rev. Silas T. Rand that the credit belongs of having

[003-002] discovered Glooskap, and of having first published in the Dominion

[003-003] Monthly several of these Northern legends. After I had collected nearly

[003-004] a hundred among the Passamaquoddy and Penobscot Indians, this

[003-005] gentleman, with unexampled kindness, lent me a manuscript of eighty-four

[003-006] Micmac tales, making in all nine hundred folio pages. Many were

[003-007] similar to others in my collection, but I have never yet received a

[003-008] duplicate which did not contain something essential to the whole.

[003-009] Though the old Indians all declare that most of their lore has

[003-010] perished, especially the more recondite mythic poems, I am confident

[003-011] that much more remains to be gathered than I have given in this work.

[003-012] As it is, I have omitted many tales simply because they were evidently

[003-013] Canadian French stories. Yet all of these, without exception, are half

[003-014] Indian, and it may be old Norse modified; for a French story is

[003-015] sometimes the same with one in the Eddas. Again, for want of room I

[003-016] have not given any Indian tales or chronicles of the wars with the

[003-017] Mohawks. Of these I have enough to make a very curious volume.

[003-018] These legends belong to all New England. Many of them exist as yet

[003-019] among the scattered fragments of Indian tribes here and there. The

[003-020] Penobscots of Oldtown, Maine, still possess many. In fact, there is not

[003-021] an old Indian, male or female, in New England or Canada who does not

[003-022] retain stories and songs of the greatest interest. I sincerely trust

[003-023] that this work may have the effect of stimulating collection. Let every

[003-024] reader remember that everything thus taken down, and deposited in a

[003-025] local historical society, or sent to the Ethnological Bureau at

[003-026] Washington, will forever transmit the name of its recorder to

[003-027] posterity. Archaeology is as yet in its very beginning; when the

[003-028] Indians shall have departed it will grow to giant-like proportions, and

[003-029] every scrap of information relative to them will be eagerly

[003-030] investigated. And the man does not live who knows what may be made of

[003-031] it all. I need not say that I should be grateful for such Indian lore

[003-032] of any kind whatever which may be transmitted to me.

[003-033] It may very naturally be asked by many how it came to pass that the

[003-034] Indians of Maine and of the farther north have so much of the Edda in

[003-035] their sagas; or, if it was derived through the Eskimo tribes, how these

[003-036] got it from Norsemen, who were professedly Christians. I do not think

[003-037] that the time has come for fully answering the first question. There is

[003-038] some great mystery of mythology, as yet unsolved, regarding the origin

[003-039] of the Edda and its relations with the faiths and folk-lore of the

[003-040] elder Shamanic beliefs, such as Lapp, Finn, Samoyed, Eskimo, and

[003-041] Tartar. This was the world's first religion; it is found in the

[003-042] so-called Accadian Turanian beginning of Babylon, whence it possibly

[003-043] came from the West. But what we have here to consider is whether the

[003-044] Norsemen did directly influence the Eskimo and Indians. Let us first

[003-045] consider that these latter were passionately fond of stories, and that

[003-046] they had attained to a very high standard of culture as regards both

[003-047] appreciation and invention. They were as fond of recitations as any

[003-048] white man is of reading. Their memories were in this respect very

[003-049] remarkable indeed. They have taken into their repertory during the past

[003-050] two hundred years many French fairy tales, through the Canadians. Is it

[003-051] not likely that they listened to the Northmen?

[003-052] It is not generally noted among our learned men how long the Icelanders

[003-053] remained in Greenland, how many stories are still told of them by the

[003-054] Eskimo, or to what extent the Indians continue to mingle with the

[003-055] latter. During the eleventh, twelfth, and thirteenth centuries, says

[003-056] the Abbe Morillot, "there were in Greenland, after Archbishop Adalbert,

[003-057] more than twenty bishops, and in the colony were many churches and

[003-058] monasteries. In the Oestrbugd, one of the two inhabited portions of the

[003-059] vast island, were one hundred and ninety villages, with twelve

[003-060] churches. In Julianshaab, one may to-day see the ruins of eight

[003-061] churches and of many monasteries." In the fifteenth century all these

[003-062] buildings were in ruins, and the colony was exterminated by the

[003-063] pestilence or the natives. But among the latter there remained many

[003-064] traditions of the Scandinavians associated with the ruins. Such is the

[003-065] story of Oren'gortok, given by the Abbe Morillot, and several are to be

[003-066] found in Rink's Legends. When we learn that the Norsemen, during their

[003-067] three centuries of occupation of Greenland, brought away many of the

[003-068] marvelous tales of the Eskimo, it is not credible that they left none

[003-069] of their own. Thus we are told in the Floamanna Saga how a hero,

[003-070] abandoned on the icy coast of Greenland, met with two giant witches

[003-071] (Troldkoner), and cut the band from one of them. An old Icelandic work,

[003-072] called the Konungs Skuggsjo (Danish, Kongespeilet), has much to say of

[003-073] the marvels of Greenland and its monsters of the sea. On the other

[003-074] hand, Morillot declares that the belief in ghosts was brought to

[003-075] Greenland by the Icelanders and Scandinavians. The sagas have not been

[003-076] as yet much studied with a view to establishing how much social

[003-077] intercourse there was between the natives and the colonists, but common

[003-078] experience would teach that during three centuries it must have been

[003-079] something.

[003-080] There has always been intercourse between Greenland and Labrador, and

[003-081] in this latter country we find the first Algonquin Indians. Even at the

[003-082] present day there are men among the Micmacs and Passamaquoddies who

[003-083] have gone on their hunting excursions even to the Eskimo. I myself know

[003-084] one of the latter who has done so, and the Rev. S. T. Rand, in answer

[003-085] to a question on the subject, writes to me as follows:--

[003-086] "Nancy Jeddore, a Micmac woman, assures me that her father, now dead,

[003-087] used to go as far as the wild (heathen) Eskimo, and remained once for

[003-088] three years among the more civilized. She has so correctly described

[003-089] their habits that I am satisfied that her statements are correct."

[003-090] [Footnote: The word _Eskimo_ is Algonquin, meaning to eat raw

[003-091] fish, _Eskumoga_ in Micmac, and people who eat raw flesh, or

[003-092] _Eskimook_, that is, _eski_, raw, and _moo-uk_, people.

[003-093] This word recalls _in-noo-uk_, people, and spirits, in Eskimo,

[003-094] _Innue_, which has the same double meaning. This was all suggested

[003-095] to me by an Indian.]

[003-096] These Eskimo brought from the Old World that primeval gloomy Shaman

[003-097] religion, or sorcery, such as is practiced yet by Laplanders and

[003-098] Tartars, such as formed the basis of the old Accadian Babylonian

[003-099] cultus, and such as is now in vogue among all our own red Indians. I

[003-100] believe that it was from the Eskimo that this American Shamanism all

[003-101] came. In Greenland this faith assumed its strangest form; it made for

[003-102] itself a new mythology. The Indians, their neighbors, borrowed from

[003-103] this, but also added new elements of an only _semi_-Arctic

[003-104] character. Thus there is a series of steps, but every one different,

[003-105] from the Eskimo to the Wabanaki, of Labrador, New Brunswick, and Maine,

[003-106] from the Wabanaki to the Iroquois, and from the Iroquois to the more

[003-107] western Indians. And while they all have incidents in common, the

[003-108] character of each is radically different.

[003-109] It may be specially noted that while there is hardly an important point

[003-110] in the Edda which may not be found, as I have just shown, in Wabanaki

[003-111] legends, there is very little else in the latter which is in common

[003-112] with such Old World mythology as might have come to the Indians since

[003-113] the discovery by Columbus. Excluding French Canadian fairy tales, what

[003-114] we have left is chiefly Eskimo and Eddaic, and the proportion of the

[003-115] latter is simply surprising. There are actually more incidents taken

[003-116] from the Edda than there are from lower sources. I can only account for

[003-117] this by the fact that, as the Indians tell me, all these tales were

[003-118] once _poems_, handed down from generation to generation, and

[003-119] always sung. Once they were religious. Now they are in a condition

[003-120] analogous to that of the German Heldenbuch. They have been cast into a

[003-121] new form, but they are not as yet quite degraded to the nursery tale.

[003-122] It may be objected that if the Norsemen in Greenland were Christians it

[003-123] is most unlikely that they would have taught the legends of the Edda to

[003-124] the heathen; to which I reply that some scholar a few centuries hence

[003-125] may declare it was a most improbable thing that Christian Roman

[003-126] Catholic Indians should have taught me the tales of Glooskap and Lox.

[003-127] But the truth is, we really know very little as to how soon wandering

[003-128] Vikings went to America, or how many were here.

[003-129] I would say in conclusion that, while these legends of the Wabanaki are

[003-130] fragmentary and incomplete, they still read like the fragments of a

[003-131] book whose subject was once broadly and coherently treated by a man of

[003-132] genius. They are handled in the same bold and artistic manner as the

[003-133] Norse. There is nothing like them in any other North American Indian

[003-134] records. They are, especially those which are from the Passamaquoddy

[003-135] and Penobscot, inspired with a genial cosmopolite humor. While Glooskap

[003-136] is always a gentleman, Lox ranges from Punch to Satan; passing through

[003-137] the stages of an Indian Mephistopheles and the Norse Loki, who appears

[003-138] to have been his true progenitor. But neither is quite like anything to

[003-139] be found among really savage races. When it is borne in mind that the

[003-140] most ancient and mythic of these legends have been taken down from the

[003-141] trembling memories of old squaws who never understood their inner

[003-142] meaning, or from ordinary _senaps_ who had not thought of them

[003-143] since boyhood, it will be seen that the preservation of a mass of prose

[003-144] poems, equal in bulk to the Kalevala or Heldenbuch, is indeed almost

[003-145] miraculous.

[003-146] THE ALGONQUIN LEGENDS OF NEW ENGLAND.

[003-147] GLOOSKAP THE DIVINITY.

[003-148] _Of Glooskap's Birth, and of his Brother Malsum the Wolf._

[003-149] Now the great lord Glooskap, who was worshiped in after-days by all the

[003-150] Wabanaki, or children of light, was a twin with a brother. As he was

[003-151] good, this brother, whose name was Malsumsis, or Wolf the younger, was

[003-152] bad. Before they were born, the babes consulted to consider how they

[003-153] had best enter the world. And Glooskap said, "I will be born as others

[003-154] are." But the evil Malsumsis thought himself too great to be brought

[003-155] forth in such a manner, and declared that he would burst through his

[003-156] mother's side. [Footnote: The reader of Rabelais cannot fail to recall

[003-157] here the remarks of the author as to the extraordinary manner in which

[003-158] it pleased the giant Gargantua to come into the world. The Armenians

[003-159] believe that Christ was born through the right side of the Virgin. The

[003-160] Buddhists say the same of Buddha's birth. (Heth and Moab, London,

[003-161] 1883.) Another and as I believe the correct account declares that

[003-162] Malsum the Wolf was born from his mother's armpit.] And as they planned

[003-163] it so it came to pass. Glooskap as first came quietly to light, while

[003-164] Malsumsis kept his word, killing his mother.

[003-165] The two grew up together, and one day the younger, who knew that both

[003-166] had charmed lives, asked the elder what would kill him, Glooskap. Now

[003-167] each had his own secret as to this, and Glooskap, remembering how

[003-168] wantonly Malsumsis had slain their mother, thought it would be

[003-169] misplaced confidence to trust his life to one so fond of death, while

[003-170] it might prove to be well to know the bane of the other. So they agreed

[003-171] to exchange secrets, and Glooskap, to test his brother, told him that

[003-172] the only way in which he himself could be slain was by the stroke of an

[003-173] owl's feather, [Footnote: There are different readings of this

[003-174] incident. In Mr. Band's manuscript the alleged means of Glooskap's

[003-175] death is described as being a cat-tail flag (_haw-kwee-usqu'_,

[003-176] Passamaquoddy), while a handful of bird's down is the bane of Malsum

[003-177] the Wolf. The termination _sis_ is a diminutive, here meaning the

[003-178] younger.] though this was not true. And Malsumsis said, "I can only die

[003-179] by a blow from a fern-root."
