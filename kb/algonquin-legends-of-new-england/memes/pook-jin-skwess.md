# Glooskap and Pook-jin-skwess the Evil Pitcher

## Narrative

In a Passamaquoddy tale of Pook-jin-skwess the Witch, it is told that she was a monstrous being who haunted the forests of the Wabanaki country (006-043). The Passamaquoddy called her Pook-jin-skwess, or the Pitcher — a name derived from her peculiar method of drawing water in enormous quantities (006-061). She was distinguishable from ordinary women by her feet: a human woman's footprints are round, while those of Pook-jin-skwess were long and pointed (006-074).

Glooskap encountered Pook-jin-skwess while she was gathering cranberries. He bade her bend over, pretending to help, and while she was thus occupied he thrust his spear into her (006-109). But the witch was not so easily slain. She pursued Glooskap relentlessly, tracking him from place to place, until in her rage and shame she fled through the woods "like a wild wolf" (008-006).

At Bar Harbor (Pes'sonkqu'), the defeated witch sat down upon a log and, with her heart full of bitterness and malice, declared: "I would that I could become something which should torment all men" (008-008). As she spoke these words, she was transformed into a mosquito (T'sis-o), and so it came to pass that mosquitoes were made (008-010).

## Nature of the Witch

Pook-jin-skwess was not merely a solitary monster but the matriarch of an evil lineage. She had children begotten by sorcerers and giants and monsters; but as they were all ugly, she stole the fairest babes from Indian women and raised them as her own, so that she might not be entirely put to shame because of her children (008-024). Her descendants were said to be the 'Nmmok-skwess — a race of dark, malevolent spirits (008-038). Another account traces her lineage differently, suggesting that the descendants of Pook-jin-skwess became a distinct tribe of supernatural beings feared throughout the Wabanaki territory (008-038).

## Variants

One narrative states that Pook-jin-skwess, having pursued young men all her life, changed into a mosquito that she might continue to prey on them (008-021). Another Passamaquoddy version relates that she pursued a moose to Bar Harbor, where, having killed him and drawn out the entrails, she petrified him — creating the moose rock formation still pointed out by Penobscot storytellers (008-015). A further variation hints that Pook-jin-skwess may have reappeared in disguise in later episodes of the Glooskap cycle (009-076).

## Significance

The Pook-jin-skwess tale serves multiple functions in Algonquin mythology. As an etiological myth, it explains the origin of the mosquito — a creature that has tormented the inhabitants of the northeastern forests since time immemorial. As a hero narrative, it demonstrates Glooskap's role as the defender of humanity against monstrous forces. The witch's ability to transform herself and escape final destruction — becoming a mosquito rather than being annihilated — reflects a characteristic theme in Wabanaki legend: evil, once introduced into the world, cannot be entirely eradicated but only contained or transmuted into a lesser form.
