## Overview

**New Brunswick** is one of Canada's Maritime provinces, situated between Nova Scotia, Quebec, and the U.S. state of Maine. At the time Charles Godfrey Leland collected his _Algonquin Legends of New England_ (1884), New Brunswick was the homeland of three major Wabanaki peoples: the **Mi'kmaq** (Micmacs), the **Maliseet** (Wolastoqiyik), and the **Passamaquoddy**. The legends gathered at Campobello Island and from informants throughout the province preserve the rich oral tradition of these nations.

## Geography

New Brunswick borders the Bay of Fundy to the south, the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the east, Quebec to the north, and Maine to the west. In the legends, several key locations are referenced: Campobello Island (the site of Leland's primary fieldwork), the St. John River valley (Maliseet territory), and Fredericton (the provincial capital) [001-017] [001-043] [045-037].

## Role in Legends

### Campobello and the Passamaquoddy

Leland's collection began with the Passamaquoddy Indians at **[[campobello|Campobello]]**, New Brunswick, where he gathered "their traditions and folk-lore" [001-017]. The Passamaquoddy, whose traditional territory straddles the border between New Brunswick and Maine, provided the foundation stories of Glooskap and other culture heroes.

### Mi'kmaq Territory

The Mi'kmaq (Micmacs) of New Brunswick contributed a substantial body of legends to the collection [001-043]. Their traditional territory spans the eastern part of the province, extending into Nova Scotia and Prince Edward Island. The Mi'kmaq stories in Leland's work include tales of Glooskap, the _chenoo_ (ice giants), and various animal spirits.

### Wabanaki Homeland

New Brunswick formed a central part of the **[[wabanaki|Wabanaki]]** confederacy, the alliance of Algonquian-speaking peoples described as extending "from Labrador to New Brunswick and Maine" [003-105]. The legends reference the broader Wabanaki territory that included parts of New England and the Canadian Maritimes [045-037].

### Informants

Several key informants for Leland's collection were based in New Brunswick. These included Mrs. W. Wallace Brown, who collected Passamaquoddy stories at Campobello, and Louis Mitchell, an Indian member of the provincial legislature who provided written manuscripts [001-017]. An elderly Mi'kmaq woman in New Brunswick also contributed a tale about her grandmother's encounter with a supernatural being [009-056].

## Historical Context

At the time of the legends' collection (1880s), New Brunswick was a province of the Dominion of Canada (confederated 1867). The Indigenous peoples of the region had experienced significant displacement from European settlement, but oral traditions remained vibrant. The Passamaquoddy at Campobello, the Maliseet along the St. John River, and the Mi'kmaq throughout the eastern portion of the province all maintained active storytelling cultures that Leland documented.
