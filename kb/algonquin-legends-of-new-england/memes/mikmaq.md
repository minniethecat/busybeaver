# Mi'kmaq

The **Mi'kmaq** (also spelled *Micmac*, historically *Mi'kmaw*) are an Algonquian-speaking Indigenous nation whose traditional territory encompasses present-day Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, and the Gaspé Peninsula of Quebec. Along with the [[Passamaquoddy]] and [[Penobscot]], the Mi'kmaq are one of the three principal tribal sources whose oral traditions were collected by Charles Godfrey Leland in *Algonquin Legends of New England* (1884). The Mi'kmaq contributed the largest single corpus of tales to the collection — approximately eighty-five narratives recorded by the missionary **Silas Tertius Rand**, supplemented by Leland's own fieldwork.

## Overview

The Mi'kmaq are the easternmost member of the Wabanaki Confederacy, their territory spanning the Maritime provinces of Canada. Their name is believed to derive from *nikmaq*, meaning "my kin-friends" in their own language. In Leland's book, they are consistently referred to as "Micmac," reflecting the 19th-century orthographic convention.

Unlike the Passamaquoddy narratives, which Leland collected firsthand during his 1882 field season at Campobello Island, the Mi'kmaq stories came primarily through written sources. The most important of these was the manuscript collection of Reverend Silas Tertius Rand, a Baptist missionary who had lived among the Mi'kmaq for decades and recorded their oral traditions in the original language before translating them.

> "Rand's manuscript collection of eighty-five Micmac tales, making in all nine hundred pages" (003-006).

## Description

### Historical Context

The Mi'kmaq were among the first Indigenous peoples of northeastern North America to encounter European colonists, beginning with Norse explorers around 1000 CE and intensifying after John Cabot's voyage of 1497. By the 19th century, when Leland and Rand were collecting their narratives, the Mi'kmaq had experienced nearly four centuries of contact, yet their oral tradition remained remarkably intact.

Leland's fieldwork also included direct Mi'kmaq sources. He credits several named informants, including:

> "Nancy Jeddore, a Micmac woman, assures me that her father..." (003-086).

This direct testimony, combined with Rand's extensive manuscript collection, gave Leland access to what was likely the most comprehensive corpus of Mi'kmaq oral literature available in English at the time.

### Language and Terminology

The Mi'kmaq language belongs to the Eastern Algonquian branch. Leland frequently includes Mi'kmaq words in his text, often providing translations. For example, the crow is called:

> "*Kah-kah-gooch*, Micmac, *Kah-kah-goos*, Passamaquoddy" (009-088).

The giant magician is named in Mi'kmaq as *Pleegun*:

> "called by the Micmacs Pleegun, which, being interpreted, means the one who is enticed" (009-196).

## Key Narratives

### Glooskap Cycle

The Mi'kmaq version of the Glooskap cycle is extensive. The culture hero's birth, his creation of the Elves and Fairies, his naming of the animals, and his final departure are all recorded in Mi'kmaq variants. Nine Mi'kmaq legends alone were published by Rand in *The Dominion Illustrated* for 1871.

### Six-Headed Demon

A distinctive Mi'kmaq narrative features a six-headed demon not found in the Passamaquoddy corpus:

> "The six-headed demon appears in a Micmac tale" (005-060).

This figure represents a uniquely Mi'kmaq elaboration of the giant adversary motif also found in the Kewokqu and Chenoo stories of other Wabanaki traditions.

### Narrative Style

Leland observes a stylistic difference between Mi'kmaq and Passamaquoddy storytelling:

> "The Micmac tales are generally more spirited and humorous than the Micmac" (008-155).

This comment, while garbled in transcription, points to a recognized distinction: the Mi'kmaq storytellers favored a livelier, more entertaining style compared to the extended, detailed Passamaquoddy manner.

## Cultural Significance

The Mi'kmaq oral tradition preserved by Rand and Leland represents one of the most extensive early records of Eastern Algonquian mythology. Unlike many 19th-century collectors who freely embellished their sources, Rand's training as a linguist and his long residence among the Mi'kmaq lent unusual fidelity to his transcriptions. Leland's decision to incorporate Rand's corpus alongside his own Passamaquoddy fieldwork created a uniquely broad picture of Wabanaki oral literature.

The Mi'kmaq supernatural world shares key elements with other Wabanaki traditions: the culture hero Glooskap, the trickster [[Lox]], the magic power *[[mteoulin]]*, and the various giant beings. Yet Mi'kmaq narratives also display distinctive features, including the six-headed demon, the specific character of *Noogumee* ("my grandmother," a recurring helper figure), and stylistic preferences for shorter, more humorous tales compared to the extended Passamaquoddy narration.

The concept of *Eskumoga* in Mi'kmaq tradition — referring to those who eat raw flesh — connects to the broader Wabanaki theme of transformation through diet central to the [[Chenoo]] legend:

> "fish, *Eskumoga* in Micmac, and people who eat raw flesh..." (003-091).
