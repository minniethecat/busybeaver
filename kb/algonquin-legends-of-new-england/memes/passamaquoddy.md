# Passamaquoddy

The **Passamaquoddy** (*Peskotomuhkati*) are an Algonquian-speaking Indigenous people whose traditional territory spans the coastal region between present-day Maine and New Brunswick. Along with the [[Micmac]], [[Penobscot]], and [[Wabanaki]] tribes, the Passamaquoddy provided the oral narratives that form the foundation of Charles Godfrey Leland's *Algonquin Legends of New England* (1884). Their storytellers — particularly **Tomah Josephs**, **Molly Sepsis**, and **John Gabriel** — preserved a rich mythological corpus centered on the culture hero [[Glooskap]], the trickster [[Lox]], and the cannibal ice-giant [[Chenoo]].

## Overview

The Passamaquoddy belong to the Wabanaki Confederacy, an alliance of Algonquian-speaking nations that also includes the Penobscot, Maliseet, and Mi'kmaq. Their name derives from *Peskotomuhkati*, meaning "those who spear pollock" in their own language, reflecting the centrality of fishing to their traditional economy. In Leland's collection, the Passamaquoddy are identified as one of the three principal tribal sources alongside the Micmac and Penobscot.

The Passamaquoddy oral tradition is notable for its narrative vividness and detail. As Leland observed:

> "The Passamaquoddy narrations are invariably more detailed and extended than those of the Micmac" (008-154).

This narrative richness made Passamaquoddy storytellers indispensable to Leland's fieldwork during the summer of 1882, when he collected myths among the Passamaquoddy at Campobello Island and Point Pleasant.

## Description

### Territory and Demographics

The Passamaquoddy traditional homeland centers on the St. Croix River watershed and Passamaquoddy Bay, an area of dense forests and dramatic tides. At the time of Leland's research in the 1880s, Passamaquoddy communities persisted at Point Pleasant, Maine, and Peter Dana Point (Indian Township), Maine.

Leland's collection documents approximately one hundred narratives collected "among the Passamaquoddy and Penobscot Indians" (003-004). The Passamaquoddy contributions came primarily through three key informants whose names appear repeatedly in the book's footnotes and attributions.

### Language

The Passamaquoddy language belongs to the Eastern Algonquian branch and is closely related to Maliseet and Penobscot. Leland recorded numerous Passamaquoddy words in his text, often providing etymological notes. For example, the Giant Magician figure known as the Kewokqu is described through Passamaquoddy terminology:

> "The word *ark* which occurs in this tale is the Passamaquoddy name for a giant magician" (005-090).

Another instance of linguistic documentation appears in Leland's note that the name of the Frog is "in Passamaquoddy *Chick-we-notchk*, meaning the one who is enticed" (008-080).

## Key Narratives

The Passamaquoddy contributed some of the most distinctive stories in Leland's collection, several of which appear only in their tribal version.

### Pook-jin-skwess: The Witch Woman

One of the most prominent Passamaquoddy-specific figures is **Pook-jin-skwess**, a witch-woman who appears in multiple tales:

> "In a Passamaquoddy tale of Pook-jin-skwess the Witch-Woman..." (006-043).

Leland notes that "the Passamaquoddy call Pook-jin-skwess, or the Witch-Woman" and that "Passamaquoddy legends make them Pook-jin-skwess" (006-061, 006-146). This figure embodies the darker aspects of Algonquian supernatural belief and has no direct counterpart in the Micmac tradition.

### The Chenoo: Cannibal Ice-Giant

The Chenoo story, one of the longest and most detailed in the collection, is explicitly identified as Passamaquoddy in origin:

> "The Story of the Great Chenoo, as told by the Passamaquoddy Indians" (002-039).

The story describes a human transformed by cannibalism into a freezing, stone-skinned monster — a uniquely northeastern Algonquian concept that differs from the wind-hungry Kewokqu.

### Glooskap and the Giant Magicians

The Passamaquoddy version of Glooskap's battle with the Giant Magicians is cited at multiple points. Leland records a narrative he collected:

> "from a very old Passamaquoddy Indian woman named Molly Sepsis... who remembered when the Passamaquoddy used flint and steel" (004-102).

This tale describes Glooskap's journey to confront the Kewokqu who had been oppressing the people, and his use of magic (*m'teoulin*) to overcome them.

## Cultural Significance

The Passamaquoddy narratives preserved by Leland represent one of the earliest systematic English-language collections of Wabanaki oral literature. The stories reflect a worldview in which humans, animals, and supernatural beings interact within a morally charged cosmos, mediated by the culture hero Glooskap.

A distinctive feature of Passamaquoddy storytelling, noted by Leland, is the integration of shamanic concepts. The Passamaquodmy *m'teoulin* — a sorcerer or shaman — appears throughout the narratives as a figure of power who can transform shape and communicate with spirits:

> "Passamaquoddy *m'teoulin*, or sorcerer, now..." (013-047).

The Passamaquoddy concept of *m'teoulin* is central to understanding Algonquian spiritual practice. Unlike the European concept of magic as illusion, *m'teoulin* represents genuine supernatural power wielded by specialists who have undergone rigorous training and initiation — a theme that recurs across the legends of [[Glooskap]]'s battles and transformations.

The collection also documents the material culture of 19th-century Passamaquoddy life. Tomah Josephs carved a powder horn "as was used by his Passamaquoddy ancestors" (008-069), and his narration of the Giant Magicians story included detailed descriptions of traditional implements and dwellings.
