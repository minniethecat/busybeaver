# Keekwahjoo

**Keekwahjoo** (also spelled _Keekwajoo_) is the Badger in Mi'kmaq and Passamaquoddy oral tradition. He occupies a dual role in the surviving narratives: as an elder pilgrim in a northward migration story, and as a rogue figure within the Lox-Wolverine-Raccoon trickster cycle.

## Identity and Attributes

In the Micmac version of the migration narrative, Keekwahjoo is explicitly identified as the elder leader:

> "the elder of these pilgrims was Keekwahjoo, the Badger" [012-083]

This positions him as a figure of seniority and guidance—the one who leads a band of travelers. The Passamaquoddy variant substitutes a different animal for the same role, but Leland notes the Micmac identification is consistent across tellings.

## Relationships

- **Glooskap**: Keekwahjoo's pilgrimage is conducted under Glooskap's broader cosmological framework, the migration forming part of the post-Glooskap reordering of the world.
- **Lox (the Wolverine/Raccoon)**: In the trickster cycle, Keekwahjoo is explicitly equated with Lox and Raccoon as interchangeable rogue figures:
  > "Lox, or Raccoon, or Badger,—for they are all the same" [026-010]
- **Mooin (the Bear)**: Badger and Bear appear together in a shared adventure with the Ducks [031-045].

## Role in Narratives

### The Elder Pilgrim

Keekwahjoo leads a band of animal-pilgrims northward in a narrative that Leland interprets as a mythological memory of Wabanaki migration. The elder Badger guides the company through hardship, embodying the wisdom of age.

### The Trickster Cycle

In the Mi'kmaq telling, the Badger takes the role elsewhere assigned to Lox or Raccoon. Leland's footnote makes the equivalence explicit:

> "In the Micmac it is the Badger, Keekwajoo, who is the rogue" [021-035]

In one episode, Badger "handed out a bundle" [026-105], triggering a pursuit:

> "came Badger handed out a bundle, and said…" [026-105]

The furious pack that "came to catch Badger" [026-109] mirrors the chase sequences characteristic of Lox narratives, where the trickster's schemes inevitably backfire.

### The Shared Cycle

Leland observes the interchangeability of these animal tricksters across Algonquin dialects:

> "While the hero is decidedly a Badger in the Micmac, I regard" [021-167]

The Wolverine, Badger, and Raccoon share "adventures" [025-004] that are "attributed to the Wolverine, Badger, and Raccoon" [025-069] across different tribal tellings, suggesting a common trickster archetype that localizes to different animals by region.

## Cultural Context

The Badger's roguish character in Mi'kmaq tradition parallels the Wolverine (Lox) in Passamaquoddy and Maliseet versions. This interchangeability reflects the fluidity of oral tradition across Wabanaki peoples—a shared narrative repertoire mapped onto locally significant fauna.
