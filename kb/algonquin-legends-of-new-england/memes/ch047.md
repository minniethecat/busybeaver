---[047-001] A very important part of _m'teoulin_ is the materials employed. In

[047-002] Old World magic these are exclusively objects which startle or disgust,

[047-003] parts of the human body, dead reptiles, or things singular and rare.

[047-004] Among the Indians, very commonplace articles are employed indifferently

[047-005] with those of the former kind. The magic consists not in them, but in

[047-006] the magician and his methods. He has had, let us say, his dreams, or

[047-007] received, while alone in the forest, his inspirations, which have told

[047-008] him what to do. He takes the objects suggested, and with them performs

[047-009] his wonder works. Sometimes he tells others to do the same with the

[047-010] same things, but in this case he is still the motive force; it is

[047-011] _his_ enchantment. In illustration of this I give the following

[047-012] legend:--

[047-013] Far in the woods was an Indian town; near it lived two old people, who

[047-014] had two beautiful daughters, and no son. The girls were very shy. They

[047-015] seldom let themselves be seen. They would not listen to the young men.

[047-016] The chief of the tribe had a fine son, a great hunter, and skilled in

[047-017] mysteries. [Footnote: In Passamaquoddy, _N'paowlin_: a man learned

[047-018] in mysteries, a scholar. This is my own Indian name. It is apparently

[047-019] the same with: _boo-oin_; that is, pow-wow man.] The young man

[047-020] wanted one of the girls. His father went to their parents and obtained

[047-021] their consent, but the girls refused to be married.

[047-022] There lived in the village a young man who was neither strong,

[047-023] handsome, nor clever at any kind of work. Hearing that the chief's son

[047-024] had failed to get one of the shy or proud girls, he said--but all in

[047-025] jest, for he had but a poor opinion of himself--that he was the right

[047-026] kind of a man to get them. "If they had, for example, only seen

[047-027] _me_, now," he exclaimed, "they would have wished to be married at

[047-028] once!" Then they all laughed, and proposed that they should go that

[047-029] night and try to see the girls, and how they would receive the plain

[047-030] looking youth.

[047-031] So they went quietly, about supper-time, and entered so suddenly that

[047-032] the girls had not time to hide behind the curtain, and so were obliged

[047-033] to receive the visitors. After supper they engaged in playing

[047-034] _Mingwadokadjik_. In this game a ring is hidden in the ashes or

[047-035] sand, and each player, with a pointed stick, makes a plunge until the

[047-036] ring is hit, and brought out. (This is Indian _poker_.--T. B.)

[047-037] So the evening passed, and nothing was said of marriage; and at last

[047-038] the guests went away, and for some time the young man made a jest of

[047-039] his having gone courting. One day he was far and alone in the woods,

[047-040] when he met an old woman of very strange appearance. She was wrinkled

[047-041] and bent with extreme age, and her head was braided up with a very

[047-042] great number of _sakalobeek_, or hair-strings, which hung down to

[047-043] her heels. After greeting him civilly, she asked him if he was really

[047-044] anxious to marry one of the beauties whom he had visited. "O

[047-045] _Nugumee_" (grandmother), he replied, "I do not care about it."

[047-046] "Only if you did," she replied, "I can give you the one you want, if

[047-047] you will only say so."

[047-048] Now the young man saw that the old woman was in earnest, and he replied

[047-049] that in fact he would be very glad to get one of the girls, but that no

[047-050] girl worth having would look at him. Then the old dame, taking one of

[047-051] her hair-strings, said, "Roll this up, and carry it in your pouch for a

[047-052] while; [Footnote: One of the infallible ancient methods to make

[047-053] anything into a fetich, or amulet, is to carry it a long time about the

[047-054] person. Familiarity, as Heine observes (_Reisebilder_), gives a

[047-055] silent life, or apparent sympathy, to even old clothes. Thus domestic

[047-056] well-known objects become fairies, and thus they talk to children.] and

[047-057] then go, and, catching an opportunity, toss the cord upon her back. But

[047-058] take care that she does not know that you have done this, and let it be

[047-059] indeed a secret to all."

[047-060] So he took the _sakalobe_, and, visiting the girls once again,

[047-061] threw it on one of them, more hopeful of success this time. And the

[047-062] cast succeeded, though she said nothing then. But the next day, alone

[047-063] in the woods, he met her, for she had followed him. And she said,

[047-064] "_Tamealeen_?" "Where are you going?" "I am going hunting," he

[047-065] replied. "But, if you have not lost your way, what are you doing here?"

[047-066] "I am not lost in the woods," she replied, but said no more. Then he,

[047-067] seeing how it was, said, "It would be better, though, if I returned

[047-068] with you to your parents, and told them that I found you lost, and

[047-069] showed you the way home." And having done this, the girl's father,

[047-070] noting that she liked the young man, asked him if he wished to marry

[047-071] her; and as both were willing, and something more, the wedding feast

[047-072] was soon ready, the friends invited, and the couple settled down.

[047-073] Some days after, the husband, seeing his wife wearing the magic

[047-074] hair-string, asked her, "Where did you get that pretty _sakalobe_?"

[047-075] "I found it," she replied, "in my _'ntuboonk_" (usual sitting place

[047-076] in the wigwam). This caused the young man to reflect how kindly he had

[047-077] been treated by the old fairy or witch, and how easily he, without any

[047-078] merit, had won his wife, and then to think of the deserving young

[047-079] chiefs son who had failed. So, taking him into the woods, they found

[047-080] the old woman, who, kind as ever, did for the chief's son what she had

[047-081] already done for his friend, and gave him also a magic hair-string. And

[047-082] using it in the same way he in like manner won the other sister; and it

[047-083] was indeed well, for she was the one whom he wanted most. And the two

[047-084] men whose wives were sisters (_wechoosjik_), were on the best of

[047-085] terms and much together.

[047-086] Now the young chief reflected that his brother-in-law had been very

[047-087] kind to him, for little cause, and thought how he could repay him. So

[047-088] he asked him one day if he would like to be a swift runner. "Truly I

[047-089] would," replied the other. "Then go and gather some feathers, and let

[047-090] them blow when the wind is high, and chase them. You will soon be able

[047-091] to outstrip the wind, and when the art comes it will never depart from

[047-092] you." Then he did this, and became so swift that no man or beast could

[047-093] escape him.

[047-094] Yet again the chiefs son said, "Would you like to become strong and

[047-095] very active?" And as he of course said "Yes," the friend replied,

[047-096] "Dress yourself in the worst and raggedest garments, and attack the

[047-097] first man you find. He will catch you by the clothes; but do you slip

[047-098] out of them and run." This he did; the first man whom he met was a

[047-099] lunatic, who gladly grappled for a fight. So he slipped out of the

[047-100] clothes and ran; but the madman thought the apparel made the man, and

[047-101] beat it a long time, and left it for dead. But after he had done this

[047-102] with many men he indeed became strong and active.

[047-103] Then the chief's son said, "I will teach you quickness of sight, so

[047-104] that you may perceive animals while hunting, though other men may not.

[047-105] Take a handful of moose's hairs; hold them firmly in a roll between

[047-106] your thumb and finger; hold them up in a high wind and let them go. So

[047-107] you will be able to perceive, in time, all the moose. And to see deer,

[047-108] or any other animal, you must take their hair and treat it in the same

[047-109] way." So he did; and by means of this magic became so keen of sight

[047-110] that he beheld every beast.

[047-111] Yet again the chief's son said, "Would you see birds where no other men

[047-112] can?" And he, assenting, was told to strip the feathery part from a

[047-113] bird's quills (_chekakadega_), and, blowing it into the air, look

[047-114] carefully in the direction in which it flew. And having practiced this

[047-115] also, he became very perfect in the art. [Footnote: The secret of these

[047-116] spells is very apparent. But the teacher would make the pupil believe

[047-117] that the successful result would greatly depend on the color and kind

[047-118] of the fur or feathers employed. It is curious to observe how, in the

[047-119] over-refinement of "sport" among gentlemen, the idea that this or that

[047-120] is "good form" and "the correct thing," which _must_ be done, has

[047-121] had the effect of establishing much which is mere fetich. A fox in

[047-122] England and a bear in Canada must be killed in a certain way by men of

[047-123] caste.]

[047-124] Now, having learned all these things, he asked the chief's son how he

[047-125] could learn to see the fishes of the sea. And being told that he must

[047-126] collect all kinds of fishes' bones, and burn them and pound them to

[047-127] dust, he did so; and, having blown them up into the wind, he could see

[047-128] all manner of fish and call them to him.

[047-129] This young man went afar in his thoughts; for reflecting that the

[047-130] whales were giant-like in power, he wondered what might be done by

[047-131] magic with them. And his friend said that it was true that the whales

[047-132] could give to man unearthly power and exceeding long life. "For," said

[047-133] he, "they never die till they are killed, and by their aid one may live

[047-134] on till life borders on immortality." So burning a piece of whale-bone

[047-135] (_pootup-awigun_), he pounded it to powder, and, standing on a

[047-136] rock that jutted out into the sea, the sorcerer blew the dust seawards.

[047-137] And erelong he saw dark spots far away, and as they grew to be more

[047-138] numerous they became larger, and yet more numerous anon, and for every

[047-139] grain of dust which he blew there came a whale; and yet he blew again

[047-140] seven times. Then the whole school of immense creatures came towards

[047-141] him; and he that was largest, or the sagamore of the whales, swimming

[047-142] close to the man on the rock, said, "Why hast thou called me?" And he

[047-143] replied, "Make me strong."

[047-144] And the Whale answered, "It is well. Put thy hand in my mouth!" And,

[047-145] doing this, he found and took out a golden key. [Footnote: This is a

[047-146] manifestly modern addition. There is every indication that the story

[047-147] itself is ancient, probably Eskimo.] "Keep that," said the Whale.

[047-148] "While you have it you will be safe against man, beast, or illness. The

[047-149] foe shall not harm you; the spirits which haunt the wilderness shall

[047-150] pass you by; hunger and pain shall not know you; death shall not be in

[047-151] your road."

[047-152] So the young man thanked the great magician, and went home; and as it

[047-153] had been promised it came to pass. All was ever well with him; trouble

[047-154] and trial were with him no more. Those who were, in his village never

[047-155] knew hunger; the wild game abounded, and came to them when called; no

[047-156] enemy attacked them; the sun and moon smiled on them; they sang the

[047-157] songs of the olden time, and played the flute in peace.

[047-158] In time the old chief drew near the end of his life, and his son asked

[047-159] the friend if his father's days could not be prolonged. But the

[047-160] magician thought it best to let him pass in peace; and he did so. Then

[047-161] the young chief offered his place and power to his brother-in-law

[047-162] (_wechoosul_); but he refused it, and passed his, life in aiding

[047-163] his friend in every way by his power and wisdom. _Kespeahdvoksit_

[047-164] (here the story ends).
