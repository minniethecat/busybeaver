---[030-001] "Rabbits! Rabbits!" replied the young man. "Why, there are hundreds

[030-002] racing about in the cedar swamps near this place, and you can have as

[030-003] many as you want." "Ah!" replied Wild Cat, "but they are not what I

[030-004] seek. Mine is an entirely different kind." The other said that he knew

[030-005] of no sort save the wild wood-rabbits, but that perhaps their Governor,

[030-006] or Chief, who was very wise, could tell him all about them. Then the

[030-007] Governor, or Sagamore, came up. Like the preacher, he was very

[030-008] remarkable and gray, with the long locks standing up one on either side

[030-009] of his head. And he invited the stranger to his house, where his two

[030-010] very beautiful daughters cooked him a fine supper. And when he wished

[030-011] to retire they brought out blankets and a beautiful _white bear's

[030-012] skin_, and made up a bed for him by the fire. Truly, his eyes were

[030-013] closed as soon as he lay down, but when he awoke there had been a great

[030-014] change. For now he was in a wet cedar swamp, the wind blowing ten times

[030-015] worse than ever, and his supper and sleep had done him little good, for

[030-016] they were all a delusion. All around him were rabbits' tracks and

[030-017] broken twigs, but nothing more.

[030-018] Yet he sprang up, more enraged than ever, and swearing more terribly by

[030-019] his tail, teeth, and claws that he would be revenged. So he ran on all

[030-020] day, and at night, when he came to another large village, he was so

[030-021] weary that he could just gasp, "Have--you--seen a Rab--bit run this

[030-022] way?" With much concern and kindness they all asked him what was the

[030-023] matter. So he told them all this story, and they pitied him very much;

[030-024] yea, one gray old man,--and this was the Chief,--with two beautiful

[030-025] daughters, shed tears and comforted him, and advised him to stay with

[030-026] them. So they took him to a large hall, where there was a great fire

[030-027] burning in the middle thereof. And over it hung two pots with soup and

[030-028] meat, and two Indians stood by and gave food to all the people. And he

[030-029] had his share with the rest, and all feasted gayly.

[030-030] Now, when they had done eating, the old Governor, who was very gray,

[030-031] and from either side of whose head rose two very venerable, long white

[030-032] feathers, rose to welcome the stranger, and in a long speech said it

[030-033] was, indeed, the custom of their village to entertain guests, but that

[030-034] they expected from them a song. Then Wild Cat, who was vain of his

[030-035] voice, uplifted it in vengeance against the Rabbits:--

[030-036] "Oh, how I hate them!

[030-037] How I despise them!

[030-038] How I laugh at them!

[030-039] May I scalp them all!"

[030-040] Then he said that he thought the Governor should sing. And to this the

[030-041] Chief consented, but declared that all who were present should bow

[030-042] their heads while seated, and shut their eyes, which they did. Then

[030-043] Chief Rabbit, at one bound, cleared the heads of his guests, and

[030-044] drawing his _timheyen_, or tomahawk, as he jumped, gave Wild Cat a

[030-045] wound which cut deeply into his head, and only fell short of killing

[030-046] him by entirely stunning him. When he recovered, he was again in snow,

[030-047] slush, and filth, more starved than ever, his head bleeding from a

[030-048] dreadful blow, and he himself almost dead. Yet, with all that, the

[030-049] Indian devil was stronger in him than ever, for every new disgrace did

[030-050] but bring more resolve to be revenged, and he swore it by his tail,

[030-051] claws, teeth, and eyes.

[030-052] So he tottered along, though he could hardly walk; nor could he,

[030-053] indeed, go very far that day. And when, almost broken down with pain

[030-054] and weariness, he came about noon to two good wigwams. Looking into

[030-055] one, he saw a gray-haired old man, and in the other a young girl,

[030-056] apparently his daughter. And they received him kindly, and listened to

[030-057] his story, saying it was very sad, the old man declaring that he must

[030-058] really remain there, and that he would get him a doctor, since, unless

[030-059] he were well cared for at once, he would die. Then he went forth as if

[030-060] in great concern, leaving his daughter to nurse the weary, wounded

[030-061] stranger.

[030-062] Now, when the Doctor came, he, too, was an old gray man, with a

[030-063] scalp-lock strangely divided like two horns. But the Wild Cat had

[030-064] become a little suspicious, having been so often deceived, for much

[030-065] abuse will cease to amuse even the most innocent; and truly he was

[030-066] none of these. And, looking grimly at the Doctor, [Footnote: This

[030-067] cross-examination of the Doctor is taken from an Abenaki version,

[030-068] narrated by a St. Francis Indian to Miss Alger. This Indian is the

[030-069] well-known Josep Cappino.] he said: "I was asking if any Rabbits are

[030-070] here, and truly you look very much like one yourself. How did you get

[030-071] that split nose?" "Oh, that is very simple," replied the old man. "Once

[030-072] I was hammering wampum beads, and the stone on which I beat them broke

[030-073] in halves, and one piece flew up, and, as you see, split my nose."

[030-074] "But," persisted the Wild Cat, "why are the soles of your feet so yellow,

[030-075] even like a Rabbit's?" "Ah, that is because I have been preparing some

[030-076] tobacco, and I had to hold it down with my feet, for, truly, I needed

[030-077] both my hands to work with. So the tobacco stained them yellow." Then

[030-078] the Wild Cat suspected no more, and the Doctor put salve on his wound,

[030-079] so that he felt much better, and, ere he departed, put by him a platter

[030-080] of very delicate little round biscuits, or rolls, and a beautiful pitcher

[030-081] full of nice wine, and bade him refresh himself from these during the

[030-082] night, and so, stealing away softly, he departed.

[030-083] But oh, the wretchedness of the awaking in the morning! For then Wild

[030-084] Cat found himself indeed in the extreme of misery. His head was swollen

[030-085] and aching to an incredible degree, and the horrible wound, which was

[030-086] gaping wide, had been stuffed with hemlock needles and pine splinters,

[030-087] and this was the cool salve which the Doctor had applied. And as a last

[030-088] touch to his rage and shame, thinking in his deadly thirst of the wine,

[030-089] he beheld on the ground, still left in the snow, a last summer's

[030-090] pitcher-plant, half full of what might indeed pass for wine by the mere

[030-091] sight thereof, though hardly to the taste. While seeking for the

[030-092] biscuits on a platter, he found only certain small pellets, such as

[030-093] abound about a rabbit warren. And then he swore by all his body and

[030-094] soul that he would slay the next being he met, Rabbit or Indian. Verily

[030-095] this time he would be utterly revenged.

[030-096] Now Mahtigwess, the Rabbit, had almost come to an end of his

[030-097] _m'teoulin_, or wizard power, for that time, yet he had still

[030-098] enough left for one more great effort. And, coming to a lake, he picked

[030-099] up a very large chip, and having seamed it with sorcery and magnified

[030-100] it by magic threw it into the water, where it at once seemed to be a

[030-101] great ship, such as white men build. And when the Wild Cat came up he

[030-102] saw it, with sails spread and flags flying, and the captain stood so

[030-103] stately on the deck, with folded arms, and he was a fine, gray-haired,

[030-104] dignified man, with a cocked hat, the two points of which were like

[030-105] grand and stately horns. But the Wild Cat had sworn, and he was mindful

[030-106] of his great oath; so he cried, "You cannot escape me this time,

[030-107] Rabbit! I have you now!" Saying this he plunged in, and tried to swim

[030-108] to the ship. And the captain, seeing a Wild Cat in the water, being

[030-109] engaged in musket drill, ordered his men to fire at it, which they did

[030-110] with a bang! Now this was caused by a party of night-hawks overhead,

[030-111] who swooped down with a sudden cry like a shot; at least it seemed so

[030-112] to Wild Cat, who, deceived and appalled by this volley, deeming that he

[030-113] had verily made a mistake this time, turned, tail and swam ashore into

[030-114] the dark old forest, where, if he is not dead, he is running still.

[030-115] [Footnote: This expression, very common among the Indians, appears to

[030-116] have been taken from the Canadians, _Il court encore_ ends many of

[030-117] their stories. This was related to me by Tomah Josephs, September 2,

[030-118] 1882\. I have four versions of it. In one, the Chippewa, given by

[030-119] Schoolcraft, the wretched efforts to rival the woodpeckers and bear are

[030-120] attributed to a no less personage than Hiawatha, or Manobozho, himself,

[030-121] when under a cloud. But Hiawatha as a poem deals only with the better

[030-122] part of the hero's character. In the Rand manuscript, the most amusing

[030-123] portion of the adventures of the Rabbit, or those with the Wild Cat,

[030-124] are much abbreviated. Tomah's tale supplies this missing portion, but

[030-125] consists of nothing else. The Abenaki tale is slightly different in its

[030-126] beginning: "Rabbit was making maple-sugar in the woods, but he was very

[030-127] pious, and rested on the Sabbath. While praying on this day by his

[030-128] hearth, there came a great black fierce man, who glared at him, but

[030-129] Mahtigwess kept saying 'Peace! peace! peace!' for that is the way the

[030-130] Rabbit prays. Then the stranger was angry because he would not cease

[030-131] praying and talk to him, but the Rabbit said, 'Would you have me break

[030-132] the Sabbath?' Then he went and brought the stranger, who was a Wild

[030-133] Cat, refreshments." These refreshments were the same as those given by

[030-134] the Doctor. Here the chase begins.

[030-135] There is probably much more of this story.]

[030-136] _V. How Master Rabbit went to a Wedding and won the Bride._

[030-137] (Passamaquoddy.)

[030-138] Chee mahtigwess, or the Great Rabbit, was once very stout or large of

[030-139] body, having a very long tail. And one day in the old times, as he sat

[030-140] on the rock, with his fine long tail trailing afar into the bushes, an

[030-141] old man came by who asked the way. And Master Rabbit, being as usual

[030-142] obliging, offered to show it to him. So they talked together and grew

[030-143] intimate, but as the old man went very slowly, while Rabbit was always

[030-144] running, he said, "Go on before, and I will follow." So the guide was

[030-145] soon out of sight, and then the old man, hurrying without heeding, fell

[030-146] down into a deep pit or chasm, where he cried out aloud for help, but

[030-147] was not heard. After a time, Rabbit, missing his follower, turned back

[030-148] and tracked him till he found the pit. Yet they could not between them

[030-149] manage to bring the traveler up again, until Rabbit said, "Catch hold

[030-150] of my tail;" and when this was done he gave a jump, but alas! the fine

[030-151] tail broke off short within an inch of the root.

[030-152] One would think that by this time Master Rabbit must have had enough of

[030-153] helping, but all the stories of him show that he never gave up anything

[030-154] which he had once begun. So he simply said to the old man, "Catch hold

[030-155] of me round the waist;" and when this was done he gave another leap,

[030-156] and brought the prisoner out. But the man, being heavy, had slipped

[030-157] down, and almost broken Rabbit's back. So it came to pass that since

[030-158] that day Master Rabbit has had a very short tail and a slender waist.

[030-159] The old man was on his way to marry a young girl. But she was in love

[030-160] with Mikumwess, the forest fairy. However, the old man married her, and

[030-161] invited Master Rabbit to the dance, which in old times made the

[030-162] ceremony. And the guest dressed for the occasion by putting ear-rings

[030-163] on his heels--for Rabbits or Hares dance on their tip-toes--and a

[030-164] beautiful bangle round his neck, and he danced opposite the bride. Now

[030-165] the bride had on only a very short skirt, and in crossing a brook it

[030-166] had got wet. So that as she danced, it began to shrink and shrink,

[030-167] until Master Rabbit, pitying the poor girl, ran out and got a deer-skin,

[030-168] and hastily twisted a cord to tie it with. But it seemed as if Master

[030-169] Rabbit's efforts to oblige people always got him into trouble, for he

[030-170] twisted this string so rapidly and earnestly, holding one end of it in

[030-171] his teeth as he did so, that he cut his upper lip through to the nose,

[030-172] for which reason his descendants all have hare-lips to this day.
