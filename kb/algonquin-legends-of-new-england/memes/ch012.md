---[012-001] It may be observed that most of these Indian traditions were originally

[012-002] poems. It is probable that all were sung, while they still retained the

[012-003] character of serious mythical or sacred narrative. Now they are in the

[012-004] transition state of heroic tales. But they unquestionably still retain

[012-005] many passages of very great antiquity, and it is not impossible that

[012-006] Eskimo and even Norse songs are still preserved in them. In this tale

[012-007] the following coincidences with passages in the Elder Edda (Hymiskrida)

[012-008] are remarkable. In both the host asks his guest to go with him to catch

[012-009] whales, to which the latter assents.

[012-010] "'We three to-morrow night

[012-011] Shall be compelled

[012-012] On what we catch to live.'

[012-013] Thor said he would

[012-014] On the sea row."

[012-015] Kitpooseagunow picks up the heavy canoe, with its oars and a spear, and

[012-016] carries them.

[012-017] "Thor went,

[012-018] grasped the prow

[012-019] quickly with its hold-water,

[012-020] lifted the boat

[012-021] together with its oars

[012-022] and scoop;

[012-023] bore to the dwelling

[012-024] the curved vessel."

[012-025] Glooskap asks which of the two shall take the paddle, and which sit in

[012-026] the stern. Hymir inquires,--

[012-027] "Wilt thou do

[012-028] half the work with me?

[012-029] either the whales

[012-030] home to the dwelling bear,

[012-031] Or the boat

[012-032] fast bind?"

[012-033] Kitpooseagunow drew up a whale.

[012-034] "The mighty Hymir,

[012-035] He alone

[012-036] two whales drew

[012-037] up with his hook."

[012-038] After this whale-fishing, the Scandinavian giants at home have a trial

[012-039] of strength and endurance. Thor throws a cup at Hymir. This cup can

[012-040] only be broken on Hymir's head, which is of ice, and intensely hard.

[012-041] "That is harder

[012-042] than any cup."

[012-043] This is therefore an effort on the part of Thor to overcome Cold. Hymir

[012-044] is the incarnation of Cold itself.

[012-045] "The icebergs resounded

[012-046] as the churl approached;

[012-047] the thicket on his cheeks

[012-048] was frozen.

[012-049] In shivers flew the pillars

[012-050] At the Jotun's glance."

[012-051] That is, the frost cracks the stones and rocks. In the Indian tale the

[012-052] two giants try to see which can freeze the other. In both there is

[012-053] distinctly a contest. In the Norse tale Strength or Heat fights Frost;

[012-054] in the American, Frost is battled with by Frost as a rival.

[012-055] It may be observed that the Indian tale is far from being perfect, and

[012-056] that in all probability the whole of it includes a fishing for the

[012-057] sea-serpent.

[012-058] It is plainly set forth in the Edda that Cold may be overcome by a

[012-059] magic spell. Thus Groa (Grougaldr, 12) promises her son a rune to

[012-060] effect this:--

[012-061] "A seventh (charm) I will sing thee:

[012-062] If on a mountain high

[012-063] frost should assail thee,

[012-064] deadly cold shall not

[012-065] thy body injure,

[012-066] nor draw it to thy limbs."

[012-067] _How Glooskap made a Magician of a Young Man, who aided another to

[012-068] win a Wife and do Wonderful Deeds_.

[012-069] (Micmac.)

[012-070] It is well known unto all Indians who still keep the true faith of the

[012-071] olden time that there are wondrous dwellers in the lonely woods, such

[012-072] as elves and fairies, called by the Micmacs _Mikumwessos_, and by

[012-073] the Passamaquoddies _Oonahgawessos_. And these can work great

[012-074] wonders, and also sing so as to charm the wildest beasts. From them

[012-075] alone come the magic pipes or flutes, which sometimes pass into

[012-076] possession of noted sorcerers and great warriors; and when these are

[012-077] played upon, the woman who hears the melody is bewitched with love, and

[012-078] the moose and caribou follow the sound even to their death. And when

[012-079] the _Megumawessos_ are pleased with a mortal they make him a

[012-080] fairy, even like themselves.

[012-081] _N'Karnayoo_. In old times there was an Indian village, and in it

[012-082] were two young men, [Footnote: According to another Micmac version of

[012-083] this legend, the elder of these pilgrims was Keekwahjoo, the Badger,

[012-084] and the younger Caktoogwasees, or Little Thunder.] who had heard that

[012-085] Glooskap, ere he left the world, would bestow on those who came to him

[012-086] whatever they wanted. So they went their way, an exceeding long

[012-087] pilgrimage, until they came to a great island, where he dwelt. And there

[012-088] they first met with Dame Bear and Marten, and next with the Master

[012-089] himself. Then they all, sitting down to supper, had placed before them

[012-090] only one extremely small dish, and on this there was a tiny bit of

[012-091] meat, and nothing more. But being a bold and jolly fellow, the first of

[012-092] the pilgrims, thinking himself mocked for sport, cut off a great part

[012-093] of the meat, and ate it, when that which was in the dish grew in a

[012-094] twinkling to its former size; and so this went on all through the

[012-095] supper, every one eating his fill, the dish at the end being as full as

[012-096] ever.

[012-097] Of these two, one wished to become a Mikum-wess, and the other to win a

[012-098] very beautiful girl, the daughter of a great chief, who imposed such

[012-099] cruel tasks on all who came for her, that they died in attempting them.

[012-100] And the first was taken by Glooskap; and after he had by a merry trick

[012-101] covered him with filth and put him to great shame, he took him to the

[012-102] river, and after washing him clean and combing his hair gave him a

[012-103] change of raiment and a hair string of exceeding great magic virtue,

[012-104] since when he had bound it on he became a Mikumwess, having all the

[012-105] power of the elfin-world. And also because he desired to excel in

[012-106] singing and music, the Master gave him a small pipe, and it was that

[012-107] which charmed all living beings; [Footnote: The identity of these

[012-108] incidents with those of "classic" times is worth noting. There is a

[012-109] lustration and the clothing the neophyte in a new garment, and he

[012-110] receives the magic fillet, as in the Mysteries of the old world. Nor is

[012-111] the resemblance of the pipe to that of Orpheus less striking. In many

[012-112] respects this is the most remarkable old Indian myth I have ever met

[012-113] with.] and then singing a song bade him join in with him. And doing

[012-114] this he found that he could sing, and ever after had a wondrous voice.

[012-115] Now to seek the beautiful girl it was necessary to sail afar over the sea;

[012-116] and during this adventure the Mikumwess was charged to take care of the

[012-117] younger pilgrim. So he begged the Master to lend him his canoe. And

[012-118] Glooskap answered, "Yes, I will do this for thee, if thou wilt honestly

[012-119] return it when thou needest it no more. Yet in very truth I did never

[012-120] yet lend it to mortal man but that I had to go after it myself."

[012-121] [Footnote: One of the traits of _bonhomie_ and common humanity

[012-122] which continually occur in the Glooskap tales, even in the most serious

[012-123] situations and solemn myths. In this respect the resemblance of the

[012-124] Northwest Algonquin tales to the Norse is truly striking. The canoe is

[012-125] among all Indians, even in Central America, exactly what the umbrella

[012-126] is in civilized society. With all his immense originality Glooskap had

[012-127] a number of "old Joes," of which he never seems to have tired. One was

[012-128] the inexhaustible dish, and another the giant skunk set upon end to salute

[012-129] his visitors, and this of the canoe was probably the commonest of all.

[012-130] He is a true Indian divinity, shining like the lightning and striking

[012-131] only when there is a storm, but appearing like the Aurora Borealis, or

[012-132] even the Robin Goodfellow-Will-o'-the-Wisp at others.]

[012-133] Thereupon the young man promised most faithfully that he would indeed

[012-134] return the canoe, and with this they got them ready for the journey.

[012-135] But when they came to the bay there was no canoe, and they knew not

[012-136] what was to be done. But Glooskap pointed to a small island of granite

[012-137] which rose amid the waves, and it was covered with tall pine-trees.

[012-138] "There is my canoe!" said he; [Footnote: Another standard "piece of

[012-139] witt" with the incorrigible joker. Glooskap's "floating island" was

[012-140] served up as a dessert to all guests, and I doubt not that if the

[012-141] double meaning of the word had been known to him, they would have had

[012-142] that too.] and when he had taken them unto it, it became a real canoe,

[012-143] with masts, and they set sail on it, rejoicing.

[012-144] So they came in time to a very large island, where they drew up the

[012-145] canoe and hid it in the bushes. Then they went forward to seek for

[012-146] people, and found a village in which dwelt the chief who had the

[012-147] beautiful daughter, in seeking whom so many had lost their lives.

[012-148] And having found him, they went into his wigwam, and were placed on the

[012-149] seat of honor. Now when an Indian seeks a wife, he or his mutual friend

[012-150] makes no great ado about it, but utters two words, which tell the whole

[012-151] story. And these are _Sewin-coadoo-gwah-loogwet_', which mean--in

[012-152] Micmac, "I am tired of living alone." And the chief, hearing this,

[012-153] consented that the young man should marry her whom he sought; but on

[012-154] one condition: and this was that he should slay and bring unto him the

[012-155] head of a certain horned dragon, called in Micmac _Chepichealm_.

[012-156] [Footnote: Vide "Supernatural Beings." The _Chepichealm_ (M.) is

[012-157] an immense horned serpent or wingless dragon. It is probably identical

[012-158] with the Wiwillmekq' (P. and Pen.), which is a singular horned worm

[012-159] found on trees or by water. It is believed to be capable of assuming a

[012-160] vast size and to be gifted with supernatural powers.] So this was

[012-161] agreed upon, and the two strangers went to the wigwam which was

[012-162] assigned them.

[012-163] Now in the night he that was Mikumwess arose and went alone and afar

[012-164] until he came to the den of the dragon, and this was a great hole in

[012-165] the ground. And over this he laid a mighty log, and then began the

[012-166] magic dance around the den. So the serpent, or the great Chepichcalm,

[012-167] hearing the call, came forth, putting out his head after the manner of

[012-168] snakes, waving it all about in every way and looking round him. While

[012-169] doing this he rested his neck upon the log, when the Indian with a blow

[012-170] of his hatchet severed it. Then taking the head by one of the shining

[012-171] yellow horns he bore it to his friend, who in the morning gave it to

[012-172] the chief. And the old man said to himself, "This time I fear me I

[012-173] shall lose my child."

[012-174] Yet the young man had more to do; for the chief said, "I would fain see

[012-175] my son coast down yonder hill on hand-sled." Now this lull was an

[012-176] exceeding high mountain; the sides thereof were ragged with rocks and

[012-177] terrible with trees and ice. Then two toboggins [Footnote: Toboggin: a

[012-178] sled or sledge.] were brought out, one of them for the two strangers,

[012-179] and this he that was Mikumwess was to direct. And on the other were two

[012-180] powerful men, and these were both _boo-oinak_, [Footnote:

[012-181] Magicians, the original of _pow-wow-in_. It is apparently the same

[012-182] in meaning as the _angakok_ of the neighboring Eskimo.] who hoped

[012-183] to see the former soon fall out, and then to run over them. And at the

[012-184] word they went flying fearfully down the mountain, and yet ever faster,

[012-185] as if to death. And soon he that sought the girl went whirling headlong

[012-186] from the sled, and the two _boo-oinak_ gave a loud hurrah; for

[012-187] they knew not that this had been done with intent by the Mikumwess,

[012-188] that he might get them before him. So he put forth his hand, and,

[012-189] seizing the younger man, turned a little aside, but in an instant went

[012-190] on after; and erelong the sled of the _boo-oinak_ stopped, but the

[012-191] other, bounding upwards from a mighty wall of ice, flew far over their

[012-192] heads onwards; nor did it stop in the valley, but, running with

[012-193] tremendous speed up the opposite hill and into the village, struck the

[012-194] side of the chief's wigwam, ripping it up from end to end ere it

[012-195] stopped. And the old man, seeing this, said, "This time I have lost my

[012-196] daughter!"
