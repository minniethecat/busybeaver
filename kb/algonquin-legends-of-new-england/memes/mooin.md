# Mooin

**Mooin** (also rendered _Mooinarkw_) is the Bear in Passamaquoddy and Mi'kmaq oral tradition. He features prominently in the Glooskap saga as the subject of a dedicated adventure tale—the third of Glooskap's exploits—and appears across several episodes in the Lox cycle.

## Identity and Attributes

Mooin is characterized as a resourceful provider, capable of feeding his household through remarkable means. After placing a pot on the fire, he would:

> "having put a vessel on to boil, he bound a bark-dish over his head, and, with this for a drum, he sang a song" [029-054]

The drumming and song transformed into a supply of food for his family, demonstrating a form of hunting magic associated with bear spirits in Wabanaki cosmology.

## Relationships

Mooin appears in connection with several other animal characters:

- **Glooskap**: The culture-hero's third adventure specifically involves Mooin, suggesting the Bear held a significant place in the oral cycle.
- **Lox (the Wolverine/Raccoon)**: In one episode, an unnamed antagonist dismisses Mooin's dwelling as "too small," prompting a confrontation [025-081].
- **Badger and Ducks**: Mooin appears alongside Badger and the Ducks in a shared adventure narrative [031-045].

## Role in Narratives

### The Adventure with Mooin

The third tale in Glooskap's cycle centers on Mooin. Though the chapter heading preserves the title—"Of the Adventure with Mooin, the Bear; it being the Third of Glooskap's Exploits" [002-029]—the surviving narrative fragments suggest a story of cunning and survival.

### The Healing Episode

In a darker episode within the Lox-Badger-Raccoon cycle, Mooin faces a prolonged ordeal:

> "a long and agonizing process; Mooin might die of it" [026-127]

This passage situates Mooin within the trickster cycle, where animal characters endure physical trials at the hands of Lox or his analogues.

## Cultural Context

The bear holds profound significance across Algonquin-speaking cultures as a symbol of strength, healing, and spiritual power. Mr. Rand's footnote preserves the variant form _Mooinarkw_ [005-166], reflecting dialectal variation among Passamaquoddy and Mi'kmaq speakers.
