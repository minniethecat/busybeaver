## Overview

Partridge Island (Mi'kmaq: **Munegoo**) is an island located at the mouth of the Bay of Fundy. In the Algonquin legend cycle, the island is associated with Glooskap's travels along the Fundy coast and his encounters with the woodland spirits of the region. Its Mi'kmaq name, Munegoo, directly connects it to the partridge—a bird that plays a significant role in Wabanaki mythology through the figure of Kitpooseagunow.

## Geography

Partridge Island lies "somewhat to the eastward" of the Bay of Fundy's mouth (010-002), near the entrance to the bay where the cold Atlantic waters meet the tidal basin. The island is a small but notable landmark along the Fundy coastline, visible from both the Nova Scotia and New Brunswick shores. Its location makes it a natural reference point in legends that trace Glooskap's journeys along the Fundy seaboard.

## Role in Legends

### Glooskap's Visit

The legends record that Glooskap "meant to visit Partridge Island and Cape Blomidon" during his travels along the Fundy coast (009-165). This itinerary places Partridge Island among the significant landmarks that Glooskap touched during his mythic shaping of the Wabanaki landscape, alongside [[blomidon|Cape Blomidon]].

### Kitpooseagunow

The island's Mi'kmaq name, **Munegoo**, links it to the partridge. At Munegoo, Glooskap met with **Kitpooseagunow**, a powerful woodland spirit associated with the partridge (011-054). Kitpooseagunow was one of the supernatural beings whose encounters with Glooskap form the core of the Algonquin myth cycle. He appears as a benevolent figure who aids the culture hero, and his connection to Partridge Island marks the location as a place of spiritual significance.

### The Partridge in Wabanaki Tradition

The partridge (or ruffed grouse) holds cultural significance throughout Wabanaki tradition, appearing in multiple legends. The bird is associated with [[mitchihess|Mitchihess]], a partridge figure who features as a great builder in Passamaquoddy legend, and with [[wejek|Wejek]], another partridge character in the oral tradition.

## Historical Context

Partridge Island has served various functions in colonial and post-colonial history, including as a quarantine station for immigrants arriving at Saint John. In the Algonquin legends, however, it appears in its pre-colonial context as a landmark in the sacred geography of the Mi'kmaq and Maliseet peoples, where every significant landform carried a story and a spiritual association.
