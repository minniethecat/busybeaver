---[033-001] He ran to the fight. What he saw was terrible! The Chenoos, who upright

[033-002] would have risen far above the clouds as giants of hideous form, were

[033-003] struggling on the ground. The female seemed to be the conqueror. She

[033-004] was holding her foe down, she knelt on him, she was doing all she could

[033-005] to thrust her dragon's horn into his ear. And he, to avoid death, was

[033-006] moving his head rapidly from side to side, while she, mocking his cries,

[033-007] said, "You have no son-in-law to help you." _Neen nabujjeole_,

[033-008] "I'll take your cursed life, [Footnote: It is generally said that there

[033-009] can be no swearing in Indian, but Mr. Rand corrects this gross error.

[033-010] "It is a mistake," he writes, "to suppose that the red man cannot swear

[033-011] in his own tongue." It cannot, of course, be expected that simple savages

[033-012] can swear like cultivated Christians, but they do the best they can. They

[033-013] introduce the venom into their speech by inserting an extra syllable.

[033-014] Thus _nabole_ or _nabol'_ means, "I will kill you," but

[033-015] _nabujeol'_ is the equivalent of "I'll take your cursed life,"

[033-016] though it has not that literal meaning. Having only one small syllable

[033-017] to swear with, the Indians are, however, not so profuse and wasteful of

[033-018] profanity as their more gifted and pious white brethren.] and, eat your

[033-019] liver."

[033-020] The Indian was so small by these giants that the stranger did not

[033-021] notice him. "Now," said his friend, "thrust the horn into her ear!" He

[033-022] did this with a well-directed blow; he struck hard; the point entered

[033-023] her head. At the touch it sprouted quick as a flash of lightning, it

[033-024] darted through the head, it came out of the other ear, it had become

[033-025] like a long pole. It touched the ground, it struck downward, it took

[033-026] deep and firm root.

[033-027] The male Chenoo bade him raise the other end of the horn and place it

[033-028] against a large tree. He did so. It coiled itself round the tree like a

[033-029] snake, it grew rapidly; the enemy was held hard and fast. Then the two

[033-030] began to dispatch her. It was long and weary work. Such a being, to be

[033-031] killed at all, must be hewed into small pieces; flesh and bones must

[033-032] all be utterly consumed by fire. Should the least fragment remain

[033-033] unburnt, from it would spring a grown Chenoo, with all the force and

[033-034] fire of the first. [Footnote: The idea is common to both Eskimo and

[033-035] Indian that so long as a fragment of a body remains unburned, the

[033-036] being, man or beast, may, by magic, be revived from it. It was probably

[033-037] suggested by observing the great vitality and power of self-production

[033-038] inherent in many lower forms of life, and may have given rise to the

[033-039] belief in vampires.]

[033-040] The fury of battle past, the Chenoos had become of their usual size.

[033-041] The victor hewed the enemy to small pieces, to be revenged for the

[033-042] insult and threat as to eating his liver. He, having roasted that part

[033-043] of his captive, ate it before her; while she was yet alive he did this.

[033-044] He told her she was served as she would have served him.

[033-045] But the hardest task of all was to come. It was to burn or melt the

[033-046] heart. It was of ice, and more than ice: as much colder as ice is

[033-047] colder than fire, as much harder as ice is harder than water. When

[033-048] placed in the fire it put out the flame, yet by long burning it melted

[033-049] slowly, until they at last broke it to fragments with a hatchet, and

[033-050] then melted these. So they returned to the camp.

[033-051] Spring came. The snows of winter, as water, ran down the rivers to the

[033-052] sea; the ice and snow which had encamped on the inland hills sought the

[033-053] shore. So did the Indian and his wife; the Chenoo, with softened soul,

[033-054] went with them. Now he was becoming a man like other men. Before going

[033-055] they built a canoe for the old man: they did not cover it with birch bark;

[033-056] they made it of moose-skin. [Footnote: "The Indians have several names

[033-057] for a canoe: _Kwedun_ (M.); _A'kweden_ (P.); _N'tooal_

[033-058] (M.), my canoe or my water-craft of any kind; _Mooseoolk_, a canoe

[033-059] covered with moose-skin (M.); _Skogumoolk_ (M..), a new canoe;

[033-060] _N'canoolk_ (M.), an old canoe."--Rand manuscript. To these may

[033-061] be added the different patterns of canoes peculiar to different tribes,

[033-062] as for instance the Mohawk, which is broad, with peculiar ends, etc.]

[033-063] In it they placed a part of their venison and skins. The Chenoo took

[033-064] his place in it; they took the lead, he followed.

[033-065] And after winding on with the river, down rapids and under forest-boughs,

[033-066] they came out into the sunshine, on a broad, beautiful lake. But

[033-067] suddenly, when midway in the water, the Chenoo laid flat in the canoe,

[033-068] as if to hide himself. And to explain this he said that be had just then

[033-069] been discovered by another Chenoo, who was standing on the top of a

[033-070] mountain, whose dim blue outline could just be seen stretching far away

[033-071] to the north. "He has seen me," he said, "but he cannot see you. Nor can

[033-072] he behold me now; but should he discover me again, his wrath will be

[033-073] roused. Then he will attack me; I know not who might conquer. I prefer

[033-074] peace."

[033-075] So he lay hidden, and they took his canoe in tow. But when they had

[033-076] crossed the lake and come to the river again, the Chenoo said that he

[033-077] could not travel further by water. He would walk the woods, but sail

[033-078] on streams no more. So they told him where they meant to camp that

[033-079] night. He started over mountains and through woods and up rocks, a far,

[033-080] round-about journey. And the man and his wife went down the river in a

[033-081] spring freshet, headlong with the rapids. [Footnote: One should be

[033-082] familiar with the almost impassable forests of Maine and Canada, even

[033-083] as they are at the present day, to properly appreciate the Chenook's

[033-084] journey. As for the speed of the canoe, I have myself gone down the

[033-085] Kenawha River (Va.), in a dug-out, at the rate of one hundred miles

[033-086] in a day.] But when they had paddled round the point where they meant

[033-087] to pass the night, they saw smoke rising among the trees, and on landing

[033-088] they found the Chenoo sleeping soundly by the fire which had been built

[033-089] for them.

[033-090] This he repeated for several days. But as they went south a great

[033-091] change came over him. He was a being of the north. Ice and snow had no

[033-092] effect on him, but he could not endure the soft airs of summer. He grew

[033-093] weaker and weaker; when they had reached their village he had to be

[033-094] carried like a little child. He had grown gentle. His fierce and

[033-095] formidable face was now like that of a man. His wounds had healed; his

[033-096] teeth no longer grinned wildly all the time. The people gathered round

[033-097] him in wonder.

[033-098] He was dying. This was after the white men had come. They sent for a

[033-099] priest. He found the Chenoo as ignorant of all religion as a wild

[033-100] beast. At first he would repel the father in anger. Then he listened

[033-101] and learned the truth. So the old heathen's heart changed; he was

[033-102] deeply moved. He asked to be baptized, and as the first tear which he

[033-103] had ever shed in all his life came to his eyes he died. [Footnote: This

[033-104] strange and touching tale was told to Mr. Rand by a Micmac Indian,

[033-105] Louis Brooks, who heard it from his grandfather, Samuel Paul, a chief,

[033-106] who died in 1843, at the age of eighty. He was a living chronicle of

[033-107] ancient traditions. The Chenoo can be directly identified with the

[033-108] so-called Inlander of the Greenland Eskimo. He is a cannibal, a giant,

[033-109] a mysterious being who haunts the horrible and almost unexplored

[033-110] interior. He assumes different forms; in one shape he is supposed to be

[033-111] a man who has become a recluse and a misanthrope. But no such being as

[033-112] a Chenoo could ever have been imagined out of an arctic country. The

[033-113] conception of the heart of hardest ice and the gradual civilization of

[033-114] the savage by kindness; the tact with which this is done, as only a

[033-115] woman could do it; the indication of the old nature, as shown by eating

[033-116] the liver of his conquered foe, and his final conversion, display a

[033-117] genius which is greatly heightened by the simplicity of the narrative.]

[033-118] As there is actually a tribe of Indians in the Northwest called Chenoo,

[033-119] there can be little doubt as to the derivation of the name. Such a

[033-120] character could have originated, as I have said, only in the icy north;

[033-121] it could never have grown in the milder regions of the west and south.

[033-122] But the Chenoo, the monstrous, ferocious cannibal giant, with an icy

[033-123] heart, is the central figure of the evil supernatural beings of the

[033-124] north. The Schoolcraft traditions and Hiawatha have little to say of

[033-125] Titans whose heads top the clouds, who tear up forests and rend rocks,

[033-126] and change the whole face of Nature in their hideous battles or

[033-127] horrible revels. But such scenes are continually described by the

[033-128] Passamaquoddy and Micmac story-tellers, and they would be natural

[033-129] enough to Greenlanders, familiar with whales, icebergs, frozen wastes,

[033-130] long winter nights, and all the frozen desolation of the north.

[033-131] There is a mystery connected with the _eating of the liver_, which

[033-132] is to be explained, like many other Indian mysteries, by having

[033-133] recourse to the Eskimo Shamanism. "In Greenland a man who has been

[033-134] murdered can revenge himself by _rushing into_ him," that is,

[033-135] entering his soul, "which can only be prevented by eating a piece of

[033-136] his liver." (Rink, T. and T. of the Eskimo, page 45.) The Chenoo is in

[033-137] all essentials identical with the _Kivigtok_ of Greenland, "a man

[033-138] who has fled mankind, and acquired extraordinary mental and physical

[033-139] powers." The story which I have here given is probably that of the

[033-140] Eskimo tale of the Blind Man who recovered his sight (Rink, page 99),

[033-141] in which a _Kivigtok_, after becoming incredibly old, returns to

[033-142] mankind to seek a Shaman priest and repent. In both stories there is a

[033-143] "Chenoo," and in both there is atonement with mankind and the higher

[033-144] powers.

[033-145] It may be observed that while the Chenoo is a giant with a heart of ice

[033-146] as hard as stone, the giant Hrungnir, of the Edda, has a heart of

[033-147] stone. The Chenoo agrees with the Jotuns in many respects.

[033-148] _The Story of the Great Chenoo, as told by the Passamaquoddies._

[033-149] (Passamaquoddy.)

[033-150] What the Micmacs call a Chenoo is known to the Passamaquoddies as a

[033-151] _Kewahqu'_ or _Kewoqu'_. And this is their origin. When the

[033-152] _k'tchi m'teoulin_, or Great Big Witch, [Footnote: When legends

[033-153] from the Anglo-Indian manuscript collection of Mitchell are given, many

[033-154] of the phrases or words in the original are retained, without regard to

[033-155] style or correctness. Wizard is here placed for witch.] is conquered by

[033-156] the smaller witches, or _M'teoulinssisk_, they can kill him or

[033-157] turn him into a _Kewahqu'_. He still fights, however, with the

[033-158] other _Kewaquiyck_. When they get ready to fight, they suddenly

[033-159] become as tall as the highest trees; their weapons are the trees

[033-160] themselves, which they uproot with great strength. And this strength

[033-161] depends upon the quantity or size of the piece of ice which makes the

[033-162] heart of the _Kewahqu'_. This piece of ice is like distance.

[033-163] "There is a great female Kewahqu' coming to fight me. In the struggle I

[033-164] may not know you, and may hurt you." So they went away as fast and as

[033-165] far as they could, but they heard the fighting, the most frightful

[033-166] noises, howls, yells, thundering and crashing of wood and rocks. After

[033-167] a time the man determined to see the fight. When he got to the place he

[033-168] saw a horrible sight: big trees uprooted, the giants in a deadly

[033-169] struggle. Then the Indian, who was very brave, and who was afraid that

[033-170] his father-in-law would be killed, came up and helped as much as he

[033-171] could, and in fact so much that between them they killed the enemy. The

[033-172] old Kewahqu' was badly but not fatally hurt, and the woman was very

[033-173] glad her father came off victorious. She had always heard that a

[033-174] Kewahqu' had a piece of ice for a heart. If this can be taken out, the

[033-175] Kewahqu' can be tamed and cured. So she made a preparation or medicine,

[033-176] and offered it to him. He did not know what it was, nor its strength,

[033-177] so he swallowed it, and it gave him a vomit. She saw something drop, so

[033-178] quietly picked it up: it was the figure of a man of ice; it was the

[033-179] Kewahqu's heart. She, not being seen or noticed, put it in the fire,

[033-180] when he cried," Daughter, you are killing me now; you destroy my

[033-181] strength." Yet she made him take more of the medicine, and a second

[033-182] heart came out. This she also put on the fire. But when a third came he

[033-183] grabbed it from her hand, and swallowed it. However, he was almost

[033-184] entirely cured.
