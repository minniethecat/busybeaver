## Overview

The St. John River (referred to in the legends as "St. John's River" or by its Indigenous place-name **Menogwes**) is a major waterway flowing through present-day New Brunswick. It is the traditional territory of the Maliseet (Wolastoqiyik) people—"the people of the beautiful river"—and appears throughout the Algonquin legends collected by Charles Godfrey Leland as a backdrop for encounters with _m'teoulin_ (shamans), hunting expeditions, and supernatural events.

## Geography

The St. John River flows from its headwaters in Maine through western New Brunswick to the Bay of Fundy at the city of Saint John. In the legends, the river is referenced as a familiar landmark for the Maliseet storytellers who contributed to Leland's collection. The Indigenous name "Menogwes" is recorded as a specific location along the river [005-163].

## Role in Legends

### Maliseet Storytellers

Several legends in the collection were contributed by a Maliseet informant from St. John, along with two Penobscot collaborators [010-124]. These informants provided tales of Glooskap, the _m'teoulin_, and other supernatural beings central to Wabanaki oral tradition.

### Hunting and Supernatural Encounters

The St. John River serves as the setting for a story of a hunter who traveled "far up the St. John River with his traps" to seek revenge through magical means [045-148]. In another account, a family living near the river St. John's experienced a supernatural visitation involving their children [037-043].

### M'teoulin Magic

The St. John area was known as a place where _m'teoulin_ (shamans or magicians) were active. One account describes a deaf boy who was treated by a _m'teoulin_ from St. John's, a man of about thirty-five years old who attempted to cure him through traditional healing practices [045-096] [045-097] [045-099]. The text notes that "many at St. John and Sebayk are still _m'teoulin_" at the time of the collection [045-096].

## Historical Context

The St. John River valley has been inhabited by the Maliseet people for thousands of years. At the time of Leland's writing (1884), the area was part of the province of New Brunswick, Canada. The river served as a major transportation route and the Maliseet who lived along its banks preserved a rich tradition of oral storytelling, from which several of the legends in Leland's collection were drawn.
