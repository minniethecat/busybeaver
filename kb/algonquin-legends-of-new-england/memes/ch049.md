---[049-001] He who would cheat must watch his words well.

[049-002] As in the preceding tradition, there has been tacked to this a fragment

[049-003] of a very poor French tale about a king, a great city, a royal

[049-004] carriage, and the forest of wild beasts, borrowed from so many old

[049-005] European romances. But what is here given is apparently really Indian,

[049-006] and it shows with spirit and humor how men tricked one another and rose

[049-007] in life by trickery, in the days of old.

[049-008] There are naturally contradictory opinions on such a subject as to what

[049-009] constitutes the morality of magic. The old Shaman or Manitou regarded

[049-010] witchcraft as wicked. The Roman Catholic has taught the Indian that all

[049-011] sorceries and spells except his own are of the devil. Hence it came

[049-012] that I got from two Passamaquoddy Indians, next-door neighbors, the

[049-013] following opinions:--

[049-014] _Tomah_.--"There was once a man who hated another. So he prayed

[049-015] until he became a snake," etc.

[049-016] _Another Indian_.--"If a man wanted to be _m'teoulin_ he must

[049-017] go without food, or sleep, or saying his prayers, for seven days. Yes,

[049-018] that certainly. He must go far into the woods. He must go again when

[049-019] his power was used up."

[049-020] The faith in and fondness for magic were so great among the Algonquins

[049-021] that there is not one even of their most serious histories into which

[049-022] it has not been introduced. The Passamaquoddies will narrate an

[049-023] incident of their wars with the Mohawks. The first time it will all be

[049-024] probable enough; but hear it again, when the story-teller has become

[049-025] more trustful, and some of the actors in it or the scene will be sure

[049-026] to end like a Christmas pantomime in fairy-land. With them

[049-027] _m'teoulin_ covered everything; it entered into every detail of

[049-028] life. I do not think that it was so deeply felt even by the ancient

[049-029] Babylonians or the modern Arabs and Hindoos as by our red men. It is no

[049-030] wonder they prefer the Catholic religion to the Protestant.

[049-031] There is a Micmac legend which is so magical and mystical, so inspired

[049-032] with Eskimo Shamanism, that it would not be remarkable if it had been

[049-033] originally a sacred song. This is

[049-034] _The Giant Magicians._

[049-035] There was once a man and his wife who lived by the sea, far away from

[049-036] other people. They had many children, and they were very poor. One day

[049-037] this couple were in their canoe, far from land. There came up a dense

[049-038] fog; they were quite lost.

[049-039] They heard a noise as of paddles and voices. It drew nearer. They saw

[049-040] dimly a monstrous canoe filled with giants, who greeted the little folk

[049-041] like friends. "_Uch keen, tahmee wejeaok_?" "My little brother,"

[049-042] said the leader, "where are you going?" "I am lost in the fog," said

[049-043] the poor Indian, very sadly. "Ah, come with us to our camp," said the

[049-044] giant, who seemed to be a good fellow, if there ever was one. "Truly,

[049-045] ye will be well treated, my small friends, for my father is the chief;

[049-046] so be of good cheer!" And they, being much amazed at this gentleness,

[049-047] sat still in awe, while two of the giants, each putting a tip of his

[049-048] paddle under their bark, lifted it up and put it into their own, as if

[049-049] it had been a chip. And truly the giants seemed to be as much pleased

[049-050] with the little folk as a boy would be who had found a flying squirrel.

[049-051] [Footnote: A story like this of giants in a canoe would very naturally

[049-052] originate about the Bay of Fundy, where, in the dense and frequent

[049-053] fogs, all objects assume greatly exaggerated apparent dimensions. One

[049-054] often beholds there, on the shore, "men as trees walking."]

[049-055] And as they drew near the beach, lo! they beheld three wigwams, high as

[049-056] mountains, in size according to that of the giants. And coming to meet

[049-057] them was the chief, who was taller than the rest.

[049-058] "Ha!" he cried. "Son, what have you there? Where did you pick up that

[049-059] little brother?" "_Noo_, my father, I found him lost in the fog."

[049-060] "Well, bring him home to the lodge, my son!" So the giant took the

[049-061] small canoe in the palm of his hand, the man and his wife sitting

[049-062] therein, and carried them home. Then they were taken into the wigwam,

[049-063] and the canoe was laid carefully in the eaves, but within easy reach,

[049-064] about a hundred and fifty yards from the ground.

[049-065] Then an abundant meal was set before them, but the benevolent host,

[049-066] mindful of their small size, did not give them more to eat than they

[049-067] would have needed for about ten years to come, and informed them in a

[049-068] subdued whisper, which could hardly have been heard a hundred miles

[049-069] off, that his name was Oscoon. [Footnote: Mr. Rand suggests that this

[049-070] may indicate the dark color of his tribe. Eskimo legends speak of

[049-071] people among them who were black.]

[049-072] Now it came to pass, a few days after, that a company of these well-grown

[049-073] people went hunting, and when they returned the guests must needs pity

[049-074] them that they had no game in their land which answered to their size;

[049-075] for they came in with strings of such small affairs as two or three

[049-076] dozen caribou hanging in their belts, as a Micmac would carry a string

[049-077] of squirrels, and swinging one or two moose in their hands like rabbits.

[049-078] Yet, what with these and many deer, bears, and beavers, they made up in

[049-079] the weight of their game what it lacked in size, and of what they had

[049-080] they were generous.

[049-081] Now the giants became very fond of the small folk, and would not for

[049-082] the world that they should in any way come to harm. And it came to pass

[049-083] that one morning the chief told them that they were to have a grand

[049-084] battle, since they expected in three days to be attacked by a Chenoo.

[049-085] Therefore the Micmac saw that in all things it was even with the giants

[049-086] as with his own people at home, they having their troubles with the

[049-087] wicked, and the chiefs their share in being obliged to keep up their

[049-088] magic and know all that was going on in the world. Yea, for he would be

[049-089] a poor _powwow_ and a necromancer worth nothing who could not

[049-090] foretell such a trifle as the day and hour when an enemy would be on

[049-091] them!

[049-092] But this time the Sakumow (M.), or sagamore, was forewarned, and bade

[049-093] his little guests stop their ears and bind up their heads, and roll

[049-094] themselves in many folds of dressed skins, lest they should hear the

[049-095] deadly war-scream of the Chenoo. And with all their care they hardly

[049-096] survived it; but the second scream hurt them less; and after the third

[049-097] the chief came to them with a cheerful countenance, and bade them arise

[049-098] and unpack themselves, for the monster was slain, and though his four

[049-099] sons, with two other giants, had been sorely tried, yet they had

[049-100] conquered.

[049-101] But the sorrows of the good are never at an end, and so it was with

[049-102] these honest giants, who were always being pestered with some kind of

[049-103] scurvy knaves or others who would not leave them in peace. For anon the

[049-104] chief announced that this time a Kookwes--a burly, beastly villain, not

[049-105] two points better than his cousin the Chenoo--was coming to play at

[049-106] rough murder with them. And, verily, by this time the Micmac began to

[049-107] believe, without bating an ace on it, that all of these tall people

[049-108] were like the wolves, who, meeting with nobody else, bite one another.

[049-109] So they were bound and bundled up as before, and put to bed like dolls.

[049-110] And again they heard the horrible shout, the moderate shout, and the

[049-111] smaller shout, until _sooel moonoodooahdigool_, which, being

[049-112] interpreted, meaneth that they hardly heard him at all.

[049-113] Then the warriors, returning, gave proof that they had indeed done

[049-114] something more than kick the wind, for they were covered with blood,

[049-115] and their legs were stuck full of large pines, with here and there an

[049-116] oak or hemlock, for the fight had been in a forest; so that they had

[049-117] been as much troubled as men would be with thistles, nettles, and pine

[049-118] splinters, which is truly often a great trouble. But this was their

[049-119] least trial, for, as they told their chief, the enemy had well-nigh

[049-120] made Jack Drum's entertainment for them, and led them the devil's

[049-121] dance, had not one of them, by good luck, opened his eye for him with a

[049-122] rock which drove it into his brain. And as it was, the chiefs youngest

[049-123] son had been so mauled that, coming home, he fell dead just before his

[049-124] father's door. Truly this might have been deemed almost an accident in

[049-125] some families; but lo! what a good thing it is to have an enchanter in

[049-126] the house, especially one who knows his business, as did the old chief,

[049-127] who, going out, asked the young man why he was lying there. To which he

[049-128] replying that it was because he was dead, his father bade him rise and

[049-129] walk, which he did straight to the supper table, and ate none the less

[049-130] for it.

[049-131] Now the old chief, thinking that perhaps, his dear little people found

[049-132] life dull and devoid of incident with him, asked them if they were

[049-133] aweary of him. They, with golden truth indeed, answered that they had

[049-134] never been so merry, but that they were anxious as to their children at

[049-135] home. He answered that they were indeed right, and that the next

[049-136] morning they might depart. So their canoe was reached down for them,

[049-137] and packed full of the finest furs and best meat, when they were told

[049-138] to _tebah'-dikw'_, or get in. Then a small dog was put in, and

[049-139] this dog was solemnly charged that he should take the people home,

[049-140] while the people were told to paddle in the direction in which the dog

[049-141] should point. [Footnote: Strange as it may seem, there is not the least

[049-142] exaggeration in this. Lieutenant-Colonel Barclay Kennan told me that

[049-143] when surveying in the far North Pacific he had an Eskimo dog which, in

[049-144] the thickest fog, would scent the land at a great distance, and

[049-145] continually point to it.] And to the Micmac he said, "Seven years hence

[049-146] you will be reminded of me." And then _tokooboosijik_ (off they

[049-147] went). The man sat in the stern, his wife in the prow, and the dog in

[049-148] the middle of the canoe. The dog pointed, the Indian paddled, the water

[049-149] was smooth. They soon reached home; the children with joy ran to meet

[049-150] them; the dog as joyfully ran to see the children, wagging his tail

[049-151] with great glee, just as if he had been like any other dog, and not a

[049-152] fairy. For, having made acquaintance, he without delay turned tail and

[049-153] trotted off for home again, running over the ocean surface as if it had

[049-154] been hard ice; which might, indeed, have once astonished the good man

[049-155] and his wife, but they had of late days seen so many wonders that they

[049-156] were past marveling.

[049-157] Now this Indian, who had in the past been always poor, seemed to have

[049-158] quite recovered from that complaint. When he let down his lines the

[049-159] biggest fish bit; all his sprats were salmon; he prayed for goslings,

[049-160] and got geese; moose were as mice to him now; yea, he had the best in

[049-161] the land, with all the fatness thereof. So seven years passed away, and

[049-162] then, as he slept, there came unto him divers dreams, and in them he

[049-163] went back to the Land of the Giants, and saw all those who had been so

[049-164] kind to him. And yet again he dreamed one night that he was standing by

[049-165] his wigwam near the sea, and that a great whale swam up to him and

[049-166] began to sing, and that the singing was the sweetest he had ever heard.
