---[035-001] Since writing the foregoing paragraph, I have read The Abnakis, by Rev.

[035-002] Eugene Vetromile. In his chapter on the Religion and Superstition of

[035-003] these Indians he gives this story, but, as I think, in a corrupted

[035-004] form. Firstly, he states that Pamola (that is, Bumole), who is the evil

[035-005] spirit of the night air, was the Spirit of Mount Katahdin. Now these

[035-006] are certainly _at present_ two very distinct beings, which are

[035-007] described as being personally quite unlike. Secondly, in Vetromile's

[035-008] story the mother and child disappear in consequence of the child having

[035-009] _inadvertently_ killed an Indian by pointing at him. It will be

[035-010] seen that this feeble, impotent conclusion utterly spoils the manifest

[035-011] meaning of the whole legend.

[035-012] Of this story Vetromile remarks that "it is, of course, a superstitious

[035-013] tale, made up by the prolific imagination of some Indians, yet we can

[035-014] perceive in it some vestiges of the fall of the first man in having

[035-015] transgressed the command of God, and how it could be repaired only by

[035-016] God. We can also trace some ideas of the mystery of the Incarnation of

[035-017] the Son of God in the womb of the Blessed Virgin Mary, mixed with

[035-018] fables, superstitions, and pagan errors. The appearance of God to Moses

[035-019] in the Burning Bush may be glimpsed in Pamole appearing to the Indian

[035-020] on Mount Katahdin, and so forth."

[035-021] The pilgrims in Rabelais did not point out scriptural coincidences with

[035-022] greater ingenuity than this. It is deeply to be regretted that the

[035-023] reverend father's entire knowledge of the mythology of the Abenakis was

[035-024] limited to this single story. (Vide Bumole, in chapter on Supernatural

[035-025] Beings.) It may be, however, observed, that if the name Bumole or

[035-026] Pamola really means "he curses on the mountain," or curse on mountain,

[035-027] it was natural that the evil spirit should be supposed to be on the

[035-028] mountain. Pamola was perhaps at an early period the spirit of

[035-029] lightning, and might thus be very easily confused with Katahdin.

[035-030] _How a hunter visited the Thunder Spirits who dwell in Mount

[035-031] Katahdin._

[035-032] (Passamaquoddy.)

[035-033] _N'karnayoo_. Of old times. Once an Indian went forth to hunt. And

[035-034] he departed from the east branch of the Penobscot, and came to the head

[035-035] of another branch that leads into the east branch, and this he followed

[035-036] even to the foot of Mount Katahdin. [Footnote: This minuteness of

[035-037] needless detail is very characteristic of Indian tales. I do not think

[035-038] that it is introduced for the sake of local color, or to give an air of

[035-039] truthful seeming, because the Indian simply believes the whole, as it

[035-040] is. I think the reason may be that, owing to their love of adventure,

[035-041] they enjoy the mere recitation of topographical details.] And there he

[035-042] hunted many a day alone, and met none, till one morning in midwinter he

[035-043] found the track of snow-shoes. So he returned to his camp; but the next

[035-044] day he met with it again in a far-distant place. And thus it was that,

[035-045] wherever he went, this track came to him every day. Then noting this,

[035-046] as a sign to be observed, he followed it, and it went up the mountain,

[035-047] Katahdin, which, being interpreted, means "the great mountain," until

[035-048] at last it was lost in a hard snow-shoe road made by many travelers.

[035-049] And since it was hard and even, he took off his _agahmook_ (P.),

[035-050] or snow-shoes, and went ever on and up with the road; and it was a

[035-051] strange path and strange was its ending, for it stopped just before a

[035-052] high ledge, like an immense wall, on a platform at its foot. And there

[035-053] were many signs there, as of many people, yet he saw no one. And as he

[035-054] stayed it seemed to grow stranger and stranger. At last he heard a

[035-055] sound as of footsteps coming, yet within the wall, when lo! a girl

[035-056] stepped directly out of the precipice upon the platform. But though she

[035-057] was beautiful beyond belief, he was afraid. And to his every thought

[035-058] she answered in words, and that so sweetly and kindly and cleverly that

[035-059] he was soon without fear, though he saw that she had powerful

[035-060] _m'teoulin_, or great magic power. And they being soon pleased one

[035-061] with the other, and wanting each other, she bade him accompany her, and

[035-062] that by walking directly through the rock. "Have no fear," said she,

[035-063] "but, advance boldly!" So he obeyed, and lo! the rock was as the air,

[035-064] and it gave way as he went on. And ever as they went the maiden talked

[035-065] to him, answering his thoughts, so that he spoke not aloud.

[035-066] And anon they came to a great cavern far within, and there was an old

[035-067] man seated by a fire, and the old man welcomed him. And he was very

[035-068] kindly treated by the strange pair all day: in all his life he had

[035-069] never been so happy. Now as the night drew near, the old man said to

[035-070] his daughter, "Can you hear aught of your brothers?" Then she went out

[035-071] to the terrace, and, returning, said, "No." Then anon he asked her

[035-072] again, and she, going and returning as before, replied, "Now I hear

[035-073] them coming." Then they listened, when lo! there came, as at the door

[035-074] without, a crash of thunder with a flash of lightning, and out of the

[035-075] light stepped two young men of great beauty, but like giants,

[035-076] stupendous and of awful mien. And, like their father, their eyebrows

[035-077] were of stone, while their cheeks were as rocks.

[035-078] And the hunter was told by their sister that when they went forth,

[035-079] which was every few days, their father said to them, "Sons, arise! it

[035-080] is time now for you to go forth over the world and save our friends. Go

[035-081] not too near the trees, but if you see aught that is harmful to those

[035-082] whom we love, strike, and spare not!" Then when they went forth they

[035-083] flew on high among the clouds; and thus it is that the Thunder and

[035-084] Lightning, whose home is in the mighty Katahdin, are made. And when the

[035-085] thunder strikes, the brothers are shooting at the enemies of their

[035-086] friends.

[035-087] Now when the day was done the hunter returned to his home, and when

[035-088] there, found he had been gone seven years. All this I have heard from

[035-089] the old people who are dead and gone.

[035-090] This tale was told me by Tomah Josephs (P.). It seems to have nothing

[035-091] in common with the very widely spread myth that the thunder is the

[035-092] flapping of the wings of a giant bird, and the lightning the flashes of

[035-093] its eyes. The tradition is probably of Eskimo origin, supernatural

[035-094] beings partially of stone being common to Greenland and Labrador. There

[035-095] is a strange but entirely accidental resemblance between this story

[035-096] and Rip Van Winkle, as in the distant sound of the nine-pins like

[035-097] low-muttered thunder, the hospitable entertainment, and finally the

[035-098] seven years as one day. Apparent resemblances are very deceptive. In

[035-099] the Eskimo mythology the _mersugat_ or _kutadlit_, who are

[035-100] the higher or benevolent spirits, protecting mortals, are distinguished

[035-101] from the evil ones by dwelling in cliffs, to which there are invisible

[035-102] entrances.

[035-103] There is a remarkable resemblance between Katahdin and Hrungnir of the

[035-104] Edda. Hrungnir has a face of stone; he is unquestionably a mountain

[035-105] personified, as Miss Larned declares: "His stony head pierces the blue

[035-106] sky." [Footnote: _Tales of the Elder Edda_, p. 235.] Both giants

[035-107] are the typical great mountain of their respective countries. Hrungnir

[035-108] has also very great affinity with the Chenoo giant. He has a _stony

[035-109] heart_, an insatiable appetite, and is cruel and brutal.

[035-110] The Iroquois have the very stone giants--or, as Schoolcraft calls them,

[035-111] the stonish giants--themselves, and a very curious picture of them has

[035-112] been preserved. [Footnote: Vide Cusick's _Five Nations_, 2d

[035-113] edition, and Schoolcraft's _Indian Tribes_, vol. i p: 429.] Of

[035-114] them he remarks, "Who the giants are intended to symbolize is

[035-115] uncertain. They are represented as impenetrable by darts." The

[035-116] connection between the stone giants of the Indians, the Eskimo, and the

[035-117] Norsemen, if not historical, is at least identical in this, that they

[035-118] all typify the mountains.

[035-119] _The Thunder and Lightning Men._

[035-120] (Passamaquoddy.)

[035-121] This is truly an old Indian story of old time. Once an Indian was

[035-122] whirled up by the roaring wind: he was taken up in a thunder-storm, and

[035-123] set down again in the village of the Thunders. [Footnote: This tale is

[035-124] transcribed, with very little alteration, from a manuscript collection

[035-125] of tales written in Indian-English by an Indian. I retain the word

[035-126] _thunders_ as expressive of the beings in question. It has for

[035-127] title, _A Story called "An Indian transformed into a Thunder!"_]

[035-128] In after-times he described them as very like human beings: they used

[035-129] bows and arrows (_tah-bokque_), and had wings.

[035-130] But these wings can be laid aside, and kept for use. And from time to

[035-131] time their chief gives these Thunders orders to put them on, and tells

[035-132] them where to go. He also tells them how long they are to be gone, and

[035-133] warns them not to go too low, for it is sure death for them to be

[035-134] caught in the crotch of a tree.

[035-135] The great chief of the Thunders, hearing of the stranger's arrival,

[035-136] sent for him, and received him very kindly, and told him that he would

[035-137] do well to become one of them. To which the man being willing, the

[035-138] chief soon after called all his people together to see the ceremony of

[035-139] thunderifying [Footnote: This word is one of the Indian author's own,

[035-140] but as I know of no synonym for it I retain it. It is certainly not

[035-141] worse than "Native-Americanizing."] the Indian.

[035-142] Then they bade him go into a square thing, or box, and while in it he

[035-143] lost his senses and became a Thunder. Then they brought him a pair of

[035-144] wings, and he put them on. So he flew about like the rest of the

[035-145] Thunders; he became quite like them, and followed all their ways. And

[035-146] he said that they always flew towards the _sou' n' snook_, or,

[035-147] south, and that the roar and crash of the thunder was the sound of

[035-148] their wings. Their great amusement is to play at ball across the sky.

[035-149] [Footnote: The Eskimo say that the lightning of the Northern Lights is

[035-150] caused by spirits playing at ball with the head of a walrus.] When they

[035-151] return they carefully put away their wings for their next flight. There

[035-152] is a big bird in the south, and this they are always trying to kill,

[035-153] but never succeed in doing so.

[035-154] They made long journeys, and always took him with them. So it went on

[035-155] for a long time, but it came to pass that the Indian began to tire of

[035-156] his strange friends. Then he told the chief that he wished to see his

[035-157] family on earth, and the sagamore listened to him and was very kind.

[035-158] Then he called all his people together, and said that their brother

[035-159] from the other world was very lonesome, and wished to return. They were

[035-160] all very sorry indeed to lose him, but because they loved him they let

[035-161] him have his own way, and decided to carry him back again. So bidding

[035-162] him close his eyes till he should be on earth, they carried him down.

[035-163] The Indians saw a great thunder-storm drawing near; they heard such

[035-164] thunder as they never knew before, and then something in the shape of a

[035-165] human being coming down with lightning; then they ran to the spot where

[035-166] he sat, and it was their long-lost brother, who had been gone seven

[035-167] years.

[035-168] He had been in the Thunder-world. He told them how he had been playing

[035-169] ball with the Thunder-boys: yes, how he had been turned into a real

[035-170] Thunder himself.

[035-171] This is why the Indians to this very day have a firm belief that the

[035-172] thunder and lightning we hear and see are caused by (beings or spirits)

[035-173] (called) in Indian _Bed-dag yek_ (or thunder), [Footnote: The

[035-174] manuscript is here difficult to understand, but this is apparently the

[035-175] real meaning of it.] because they see them, and have, moreover,

[035-176] actually picked up the _bed-dags k'chisousan_, or thunder-bullet.

[035-177] [Footnote: Thunderbolt.] It is of many different kinds of stone, but

[035-178] always of the same shape. The last was picked up by Peter Sabattis,

[035-179] [Footnote: I heard of the existence of this legend a long time before I

[035-180] found it in the manuscript collection obtained for me by Louis

[035-181] Mitchell. It is very curious as being unquestionably of Eskimo origin,

[035-182] or common to the Eskimo; also because it speaks of the Thunders as

[035-183] always endeavoring to kill a great bird in the south. This is probably

[035-184] the thunder or storm bird, called by the Passamaquoddy Indians

[035-185] _Wochowsen_ or _Wuchowsen_, that is, Wind-Blower. Another

[035-186] legend makes Thunder and Lightning the sons of Mount Katahdin.
