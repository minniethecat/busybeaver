**Oscoon** — whose name means "the Liver" in Micmac (048-158) — is a figure in Passamaquoddy mythology who appears in the tale of the [[Kookwess]]. When warned by the Fish-Hawk of an impending attack by the cannibal giant, Oscoon alone keeps his ears open to the deadly sound while leading his people to safety. For his courage and leadership, he is elevated to the position of chief.

## Overview

Oscoon embodies the Algonquin ideal of leadership under supernatural threat. Unlike the heroic figures who confront monsters directly ([[Glooskap]], [[Kitpooseagunow]]), Oscoon's heroism lies in prudent action and the willingness to bear danger personally while protecting others. He closes the people's ears against the Kookwess's deadly roar but leaves his own open — hearing the giant's distant whoop, judging its threat, and saying nothing to alarm his followers.

His name — "the Liver" — may carry symbolic weight in Algonquin anatomical metaphor, though Leland provides no direct gloss on its significance. In many Indigenous North American traditions, the liver is associated with courage, vitality, or the seat of certain emotions.

## Identity and Attributes

### Name

Leland's footnote provides the literal meaning: "Oscoon (M.): the Liver" (048-158). The name is Micmac in linguistic origin, though the story is attributed to Passamaquoddy tradition — a common pattern in the cross-tribal circulation of Wabanaki narratives.

### Character

Oscoon's defining trait is his combination of protective leadership and personal courage. When the Fish-Hawk delivers his warning, Oscoon acts decisively:

> "He led the people away. He closed their ears; he did not close his own. Once he heard — a far-away whoop. It was not very terrible. But he said nothing" (048-164).

This silence is the mark of his leadership: he absorbs the terror personally so that his people need not carry it. When he later exposes the Fish-Hawk's duplicity with the sharp retort — "Surely you, who know so much about him, must know that better than we do" — he demonstrates the discernment that makes him more than merely brave (048-165).

## Role in Legends

### The Kookwess Escape

Oscoon's sole narrative appearance is in the tale of the Kookwess and the Fish-Hawk. When the Fish-Hawk warns of the giant's approach in seven days, Oscoon organizes the evacuation, implementing the two-part strategy of flight and sensory denial. The people escape in canoes; the Kookwess departs unseen. In the aftermath, "the people thought much of Oscoon. They made him their chief" (048-164).

The story concludes with a second act: the Fish-Hawk's return and Oscoon's exposure of his duplicity. Leland notes the Norse parallel — "very Loki-like and tricky" — but Oscoon's role is that of the clear-sighted leader who sees through supernatural deception (048-165).

## Cultural Significance

The Oscoon story models a distinct form of Algonquin heroism: leadership through prudence rather than combat. Unlike Glooskap, who defeats the Kookwess directly through his labors, Oscoon survives the giant through strategic withdrawal. This is not a lesser heroism but a different one — the heroism of the chief who preserves his people rather than seeking personal glory in battle.

The story also exemplifies the Algonquin narrative motif of the ambiguous supernatural messenger. The Fish-Hawk is neither ally nor enemy but a third thing — a being whose information is valuable but whose motives are suspect. Oscoon's ability to accept the warning while seeing through the messenger marks him as a figure of unusual discernment, the quality that earns him the chieftainship.
