## Overview

**Piktook** (recorded by Leland as the Mi'kmaq name for present-day **Pictou**, Nova Scotia) appears in one of the Glooskap legends as the setting for a feast and a contest for the hand of the chief's daughter. The name "Piktook" means "the place of the rising bubbles" or "explosive place" in Mi'kmaq, referring to the bubbling waters of Pictou Harbour [008-075].

## Geography

Piktook is located on the Northumberland Strait coast of present-day Nova Scotia, on the traditional territory of the Mi'kmaq people. The settlement, situated at the mouth of Pictou Harbour, was a significant gathering place for the Mi'kmaq long before European contact.

## Role in Legends

### Glooskap at Piktook

The legend describes how **[[glooskap|Glooskap]]** traveled to Piktook, where he found a village of "above a hundred" people gathered for a feast [008-075] [008-088]. The chief of Piktook had three beautiful daughters, and all the young men of the village desired the hand of the eldest [008-115].

### The Contest

According to the tradition, Glooskap arrived at Piktook and joined the gathering. The account records that all the young men in Piktook "desired this girl, and would" compete for her hand [008-118]. The chief's three daughters became central figures in the events that unfolded during Glooskap's visit, highlighting the importance of marriage alliances and social gatherings in Mi'kmaq oral tradition.

### Cultural Significance

The Piktook story illustrates several themes common to Glooskap legends: the culture hero's travels among his people, his participation in social ceremonies, and his interactions with local chiefs and their families. Unlike the grander cosmological tales of Glooskap shaping the landscape, the Piktook episode presents a more intimate portrait of the culture hero moving among ordinary village life.

## Historical Context

Pictou (Piktook) is one of the oldest European settlement sites in Nova Scotia, with the first Scottish settlers arriving in 1773. The Mi'kmaq had inhabited the area for millennia before European contact, and the name "Piktook" preserves the Indigenous toponymy of the region. At the time of Leland's collection, the Mi'kmaq oral traditions concerning Glooskap's visit to Piktook were still recounted among local storytellers.
