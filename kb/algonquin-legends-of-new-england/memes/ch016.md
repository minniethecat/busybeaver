---[016-001] It came to pass that the Turtle waxed mighty in his own conceit, and

[016-002] thought that he could take Glooskap's place and reign in his stead. So

[016-003] he held a council of all the animals to find out how he could be slain.

[016-004] The Lord of Men and Beasts laughed at this. Little did he care for

[016-005] them!

[016-006] And knowing all that was in their hearts, he put on the shape of an old

[016-007] squaw and went into the council-house. And he sat down by two witches:

[016-008] one was the Porcupine, the other the Toad; as women they sat there. Of

[016-009] them the Master asked humbly how they expected to kill him. And the

[016-010] Toad answered savagely, "What is that to thee, and what hast thou to do

[016-011] with this thing?" "Truly," he replied, "I meant no harm," and saying

[016-012] this he softly touched the tips of their noses, and rising went his

[016-013] way. But the two, witches, looking one at the other, saw presently that

[016-014] their noses were both gone, and they screamed aloud in terror, but

[016-015] their faces were none the less flat. And so it came that the Toad and

[016-016] the Porcupine both lost their noses and have none to this day.

[016-017] Glooskap had two dogs. One was the Loon (Kwemoo), the other the Wolf

[016-018] (Malsum). Of old all animals were as men; the Master gave them the

[016-019] shapes which they now bear. But the Wolf and the Loon loved Glooskap so

[016-020] greatly that since he left them they howl and wail. He who hears their

[016-021] cries over the still sound and lonely lake, by the streams where no

[016-022] dwellers are, or afar at night in the forests and hollows, hears them

[016-023] sorrowing for the Master.

[016-024] I am indebted for this legend to Mr. Edward Jack, of Fredericton, N. B.

[016-025] "I give it to you," he writes, "just as it came from an Indian's lips,

[016-026] as he sat before the fire in my room this evening, smoking his tobacco

[016-027] mixed with willow bark. He has an endless store of Indian lore." It may

[016-028] be observed that this story gives a far more ingenious reason for

[016-029] Glooskap's telling his brother what would be his bane than appears in

[016-030] the other version. For he tells him what would indeed deprive him of

[016-031] life, but not forever.

[016-032] No one can compare the story of Glooskap with that of Manobozho-Hiawatha

[016-033] and the like, as given by Schoolcraft or Cusick, and not decide that the

[016-034] latter seems to be a second-hand version of the former. In one we have

[016-035] the _root_ of the bulrush,--not the light, feathery rush itself. In

[016-036] this story, as in that of Balder and Loki, it is the very apparent

[016-037] harmlessness of the bane which points the incident. Manobozho's father

[016-038] _says_ that a black rock will kill him; but it does not, although

[016-039] he flies before it. Glooskap declares that a handful of down will cause

[016-040] his death. The _double entendre_ of the swoon is entirely wanting

[016-041] in the Western tale, as is the apparent harmlessness of the medium of

[016-042] death. In the Edda the mistletoe, the softest, and apparently the least

[016-043] injurious, of plants, kills Balder; in the Wabanaki tale it is a ball of

[016-044] down or a rush. The Chippewas change it, like savages, to a substantial

[016-045] root and a black rock, thereby manifesting an insensibility to the point

[016-046] of the original, which is that the most trifling thing may be the cause

[016-047] of the most terrible events.

[016-048] _How Glooskap changed Certain Saucy Indians into Rattlesnakes_

[016-049] (Passamaquoddy.)

[016-050] You know At-o-sis, the Snake? Well, the worst of all is Rattlesnake.

[016-051] Long time ago the Rattlesnakes were saucy Indians. They were very

[016-052] saucy. They had too much face. They could not be put down by much, and

[016-053] they got up for very little.

[016-054] When the great Flood was coming Glooskap told them about it. They said

[016-055] they did not care. He told them the water would come over their heads.

[016-056] They said that would be very wet. He told them to be good and quiet,

[016-057] and pray. Then those Indians hurrahed. He said, "A great Flood is

[016-058] coming." Then they gave three cheers for the great Flood. He said, "The

[016-059] Flood will come and drown you all." Then these Indians hurrahed again,

[016-060] and got their rattles, made of turtle-shells, in the old fashion,

[016-061] fastened together, filled with pebbles, and rattled them and had a

[016-062] grand dance. Afterwards, when the white men brought cows and oxen into

[016-063] the country, they made rattles of horns.

[016-064] Yes, they had a great dance. The rain began to fall, but they danced.

[016-065] The thunder roared, and they shook their rattles and yelled at it. Then

[016-066] Glooskap was angry. He did not drown them in the Flood, however, but he

[016-067] changed them into rattlesnakes. Nowadays, when they see a man coming,

[016-068] they lift up their heads and move them about. That's the way snakes

[016-069] dance. And they shake the rattles in their tails just as Indians shake

[016-070] their rattles when they dance. How do you like such music?

[016-071] A Passamaquoddy tale related by an old woman to Mrs. W. Wallace Brown.

[016-072] These Indians still keep up a very curious snake-dance.

[016-073] _How Glooskap bound Wuchowsen, the Great Wind-Bird, and made all the

[016-074] Waters in all the World Stagnant._

[016-075] (Passamaquoddy.)

[016-076] The Indians believe in a great bird called by them _Wochowsen_ or

[016-077] _Wuchowsen_, meaning Wind-Blow or the Wind-Blower, who lives far

[016-078] to the North, and sits upon a great rock at the end of the sky. And it

[016-079] is because whenever he moves his wings the wind blows they of old times

[016-080] called him that.

[016-081] When Glooskap was among men he often went out in his canoe with bow and

[016-082] arrows to kill sea-fowl. At one time it was every day very windy; it

[016-083] grew worse; at last it blew a tempest, and he could not go out at all.

[016-084] Then he said, "Wuchowsen, the Great Bird, has done this!"

[016-085] He went to find him; it was long ere he reached his abode. He found

[016-086] sitting on a high rock a large white Bird.

[016-087] "Grandfather," said Glooskap, "you take no compassion on your

[016-088] _Koosesek_, your grandchildren. You have caused this wind and

[016-089] storm; it is too much. Be easier with your wings!"

[016-090] The Giant Bird replied, "I have been here since ancient times; in the

[016-091] earliest days, ere aught else spoke, I first moved my wings; mine was

[016-092] the first voice,--and I will ever move my wings as I will."

[016-093] Then Glooskap rose in his might; he rose to the clouds; he took the

[016-094] Great Bird-giant Wuchowsen as though he were a duck, and tied both his

[016-095] wings, and threw him down into a chasm between deep rocks, and left him

[016-096] lying there.

[016-097] The Indians could now go out in their canoes all day long, for there

[016-098] was a dead calm for many weeks and months. And with that all the waters

[016-099] became stagnant. They were so thick that Glooskap could not paddle his

[016-100] canoe. Then he thought of the Great Bird, and went to see him.

[016-101] As he had left him he found him, for Wuchowsen is immortal. So, raising

[016-102] him, he put him on his rock again, and untied one of his wings. Since

[016-103] then the winds have never been so terrible as in the old time. The

[016-104] reader will find the main incident of this story repeated in

[016-105] "Tumilkoontaoo, the Broken Wing," from the Micmac, in which there is no

[016-106] mention of Glooskap. This of _Wuchowsen_ is from the Passamaquoddy

[016-107] manuscript collection by Louis Mitchell. It is unquestionably the

[016-108] original. Glooskap, as the greatest magician, most appropriately

[016-109] subdues the giant eagle of the North, the terrible god of the storm.

[016-110] No one who knows the Edda will deny that Wuchowsen, or the Wind-blower,

[016-111] as he appears in the Passamaquoddy tale, is far more like the same bird

[016-112] of the Norsemen than the grotesque Thunder Bird of the Western tribes.

[016-113] He is distinctly spoken of by the Indians of Maine as a giant and a

[016-114] bird in one, sitting on a high cliff at the end of the sky, the wind--not

[016-115] thunder--coming from his pinions:--

[016-116] "Tell me ninthly,

[016-117] Since thou art called wise,

[016-118] Whence the wind comes,

[016-119] That over ocean passes,

[016-120] Itself invisible to man.

[016-121] "Hraesvelg he is called

[016-122] Who at the end of heaven sits,

[016-123] A Jotun (giant) in eagle's plumage:

[016-124] From his wings comes,

[016-125] It is said, the wind.

[016-126] That over all men passes."

[016-127] (The Lay of Vafthrudnir. The Edda, trans. by B. Thorpe.)

[016-128] _How Glooskcap conquered the Great Bull-Frog, and in what Manner all

[016-129] the Pollywogs, Crabs, Leeches, and other Water Creatures were

[016-130] created._

[016-131] (Passamaquoddy and Micmac.)

[016-132] _N'karnayoo_, of old times, there was an Indian village far away

[016-133] among the mountains, little known to other men. And the dwellers

[016-134] therein were very comfortable: the men hunted every day, the women did

[016-135] the work at home, and all went well in all things save in this. The

[016-136] town was by a brook, and except in it there was not a drop of water in

[016-137] all the country round, unless in a few rain-puddles. No one there had

[016-138] ever found even a spring.

[016-139] Now these Indians were very fond of good water. The brook was of a

[016-140] superior quality, and they became dainty over it.

[016-141] But after a time they began to observe that the brook was beginning to

[016-142] run low, and that not in the summer time, but in autumn, even after the

[016-143] rains. And day by day it diminished, until its bed was as dry as a dead

[016-144] bone in the ashes of a warm fire.

[016-145] Now it was said that far away up in the land where none had ever been

[016-146] there was on this very stream another Indian village; but what manner

[016-147] of men dwelt therein no one knew. And thinking that these people of the

[016-148] upper country might be in some way concerned in the drought, they sent

[016-149] one of their number to go and see into the matter.

[016-150] And after he had traveled three days he came to the place; and there he

[016-151] found that a dam had been raised across the rivulet, so that no water

[016-152] could pass, for it was all kept in a pond. Then asking them why they

[016-153] had made this mischief, since the dam was of no use to them, they bade

[016-154] him go and see their chief, by whose order this had been built.

[016-155] And when he came to him, lo, there lay lazily in the mud a creature who

[016-156] was more of a monster than a man, though he had a human form. For he

[016-157] was immense to measure, like a giant, fat, bloated, and brutal to

[016-158] behold. His great yellow eyes stuck from his head like pine-knots, his

[016-159] mouth went almost from ear to ear, and he had broad, skinny feet with

[016-160] long toes, exceeding marvelous.

[016-161] The messenger complained to this monster, who at first said nothing,

[016-162] and then croaked, and finally replied in a loud bellow,--

[016-163] "Do as you choose,

[016-164] Do as you choose,

[016-165] Do as you choose.

[016-166] "What do I care?

[016-167] What do I care?

[016-168] What do I care?

[016-169] "If you want water,

[016-170] If you want water,

[016-171] If you want water,

[016-172] Go somewhere else."

[016-173] Then the messenger remonstrated, and described the suffering of the

[016-174] people, who were dying of thirst. And this seemed to please the

[016-175] monster, who grinned. At last he got up, and, making a single spring to

[016-176] the dam, took an arrow and bored a hole in it, so that a little water

[016-177] trickled out, and then he bellowed,--

[016-178] "Up and begone!

[016-179] Up and begone!

[016-180] Up and begone!"

[016-181] So the man departed, little comforted. He came to his home, and for a

[016-182] few days there was a little water in the stream; but this soon stopped,

[016-183] and there was great suffering again.

[016-184] Now these Indians, who were the honestest fellows in all the world, and

[016-185] never did harm to any one save their enemies, were in a sorry pickle.

[016-186] For it is a bad thing to have nothing but water to drink, but to want

[016-187] that is to be mightily dry. And the great Glooskap, who knew all that

[016-188] was passing in the hearts of men and beasts, took note of this, and

[016-189] when he willed it he was among them; for he ever came as the wind

[016-190] comes, and no man wist how.
