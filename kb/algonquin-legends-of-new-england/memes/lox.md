# Lox

**Lox**, also known as **Master Lox** or **the Great Indian Devil**, is the principal trickster-antagonist of Wabanaki mythology. Unlike the noble culture hero [[Glooskap]], Lox embodies chaos, deception, and malevolent magic. Leland describes him as ranging "from Punch to Satan" in character (003-136), and his exploits — spanning theft, murder, shape-shifting, and cosmic mischief — constitute one of the largest story cycles in *Algonquin Legends of New England*. Appearing in multiple animal forms (wolverine, wolf, lynx, badger, raccoon), Lox is a shape-shifter whose greed and cunning invariably lead to either spectacular triumph or humiliating defeat.

## Overview

Lox occupies a unique position in Algonquian oral tradition: he is simultaneously feared as a devil and celebrated as a clever trickster. His narrative cycle includes at least eight distinct tales in Leland's collection, each illustrating a different facet of his character — from the cosmic to the absurd. Leland identifies him as "the Great Indian Devil, who is the coeval of Glooskap" (002-013), indicating that in Wabanaki cosmology, Lox represents a primordial force of disorder co-existing with the creative power of Glooskap.

The ambiguity of Lox's nature is captured in Leland's characterization:

> "Glooskap is always a gentleman, Lox ranges from Punch to Satan; presenting a series of pictures of amazing power and infinite variety" (003-136).

## Identity and Attributes

Lox is fundamentally a shape-shifter, and his physical form varies across tales. He appears most commonly as a **wolverine** — a fierce, solitary predator of the northern forests whose reputation for cunning and ferocity made it a natural embodiment of the trickster principle. Leland notes the range of forms:

> "between Lox (lynx, wolf, wolverine, badger, and raccoon) there is such confusion that it is difficult to determine" (002-225).

The Passamaquoddy identify him specifically as "Lox the Great Wolverine" (017-016), and Leland observes that his character is "preeminently characteristic of the Lox or Wolverine of the Passamaquoddy" (021-172).

Lox's magical powers are formidable. He is described as "a great magician at certain times" (021-164), capable of dismembering himself and reassembling his body, deceiving entire villages, and transforming into different animals at will. His power, however, is inconsistent — a trait that distinguishes him from the reliable magic (*[[mteoulin]]*) of Glooskap and makes his stories unpredictable.

## Key Stories

### How Lox deceived the Ducks and cheated the Chief

In one characteristic tale, Lox uses trickery to exploit a village of ducks. Pretending friendship, he systematically deceives them, cheats their chief, and escapes with their resources. The story exemplifies Lox's modus operandi: using charm and false friendship as weapons against the unsuspecting.

### How Master Lox played a Trick on Mrs. Bear

Lox's encounter with Mrs. Bear showcases his predatory cleverness directed at a powerful adversary. Rather than confronting Bear directly, Lox employs psychological manipulation, a hallmark of the trickster who wins through deceit what he could never take by force.

### How Master Lox froze to Death

This tale demonstrates the self-destructive potential of Lox's trickery:

> "How Master Lox froze to Death" (002-015).

The story follows Lox's plots against the Wolves and culminates in his own demise through the very forces of nature he sought to exploit. Yet, like all great tricksters, Lox's death is never final — he reappears in subsequent tales, embodying the principle that chaos, once introduced into the world, cannot be permanently eliminated.

### How Lox told a Lie

A brief but significant story:

> "How Lox told a Lie" (002-024).

This tale serves an etiological function, explaining through Lox's action how falsehood entered the human world. The story positions Lox not merely as a troublesome figure but as a cosmogonic agent whose actions shaped the moral landscape of human existence.

## Relationships

Lox's primary relationship is with **[[Glooskap]]**, their opposition forming the central moral axis of Wabanaki mythology. Where Glooskap creates, teaches, and protects, Lox destroys, deceives, and exploits. Yet they are not simple opposites — Leland suggests they are "coeval" (002-013), co-eternal principles whose tension drives the narrative universe.

Lox's relationship with humans is adversarial. He appears as a threat to villages and individuals, though his malevolence is tempered by his incompetence — his schemes frequently backfire, making him as often a figure of comedy as of terror. In the Passamaquoddy variant, the "Mohawk race found the Lox lying dead on a rock" (021-175) after one of his failed schemes, only for him to resurrect.

His interactions with animal characters — **Bear**, **Wolf**, **Raccoon**, **Duck** — are invariably predatory, though the prey sometimes outwits the predator.

## Cultural Significance

Lox represents a complex figure in Wabanaki thought: he is the explanation for why the world contains suffering, deception, and disorder. Unlike the Christian devil, Lox is not purely evil — his trickster nature carries elements of the culture hero, and his defeats often serve to reinforce social and moral norms.

Leland's collection is notable for preserving the full spectrum of Lox's character. While many 19th-century collectors sanitized Indigenous trickster figures out of Victorian propriety, Leland's Lox retains his subversive edge. The tales of his deceptions, shape-shiftings, and humiliating defeats survive in a form that conveys both their entertainment value and their moral function.

The Lox cycle also provides valuable evidence of pre-contact narrative traditions. The figure of the trickster-wolverine appears across the subarctic and northeastern woodlands, and the Wabanaki Lox shares features with the Cree **Wisakedjak** and the Ojibwe **Nanabozho**, suggesting an ancient pan-Algonquian trickster tradition.
