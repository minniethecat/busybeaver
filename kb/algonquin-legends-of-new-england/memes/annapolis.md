## Overview

The **Annapolis Valley** is a fertile valley in western Nova Scotia, running parallel to the Bay of Fundy. In the Algonquin legends, the valley is the setting for one of the tales in the Glooskap cycle, in which the culture hero caused a great transformation of the landscape involving the beaver and the creation of geographical features visible to later generations.

## Geography

The Annapolis Valley stretches approximately 130 kilometers from Digby on the Bay of Fundy eastward to Wolfville and beyond. It lies between two parallel mountain ranges: North Mountain along the Fundy shore and South Mountain to the south. In the legends, the valley is described as being shaped by Glooskap's passage—he "forded the Annapolis Valley" (009-186) during his mythic journey, and the entire valley served as the stage for a significant transformation story (009-176).

## Role in Legends

### The Glooskap-Beaver Cycle

The Annapolis Valley features in the Glooskap-Beaver cycle, one of the culture hero's great transformation stories. The text records that "all the valley of Annapolis" was involved in an episode where Glooskap confronted Beaver (009-176). In this tale, Glooskap pursued Beaver across the landscape, and the pursuit itself reshaped the geography. The beaver's attempt to dam the waters of the Annapolis region connects this valley to the broader pattern of Glooskap stories about water, dams, and landscape transformation seen at other locations such as the [[bay-of-fundy|Bay of Fundy]].

### Fording the Valley

Glooskap "forded the Annapolis Valley" (009-186) during his mythic passage through the region. The act of fording—crossing a body of water—emphasizes the valley's character as a water-shaped landscape and Glooskap's mastery over natural barriers. This theme of the culture hero traversing and transforming waterways appears throughout the Algonquin legend cycle.

## Historical Context

The Annapolis Valley was historically inhabited by the Mi'kmaq people before European settlement. The French established Port-Royal in the valley in 1605, one of the earliest European settlements in North America. The valley's fertile soil made it Nova Scotia's agricultural heartland. At the time of Leland's collection in the 1880s, the valley was well-settled by European-descended farmers, though Mi'kmaq communities continued to maintain their presence and oral traditions in the region.
