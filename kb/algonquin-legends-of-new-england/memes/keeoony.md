# Keeoony

**Keeoony** (also rendered _Kecoony_ in some sources) is the Otter in Passamaquoddy oral tradition. He appears in a single well-preserved narrative in which Master Rabbit attempts to rival his skills, forming one episode in the broader Master Rabbit cycle.

## Identity and Attributes

Keeoony is defined by craftsmanship and aquatic competence. His lodge—built on a riverbank with a sliding entrance leading directly to the water—is described as an ideal dwelling:

> "he that dwelt therein was Keeoony" [028-151]

The lodge's design reflects the Otter's mastery over his environment:

> "The lodge was on the bank of… the Otter made him" [028-152]

A slide running "down to the water" [028-153] allows Keeoony effortless access to his fishing grounds—a detail that becomes central to Master Rabbit's envy.

## Role in Narratives

### Master Rabbit's Rivalry

The core Keeoony narrative opens with Master Rabbit observing the Otter's prosperity:

> "Master Rabbit sought to rival Keeoony, the Otter" [028-145]

Rabbit, surveying Keeoony's well-constructed lodge and abundant fish supply, determines to equal or surpass him:

> "do as well as this mere Otter?" [028-162]

Rabbit invites Keeoony to dine—"_adamadusk_" [028-164]—and attempts to replicate the Otter's lodge design slavishly:

> "such as the Otter had chosen for his home" [028-168]

His imitation proves disastrous. The Otter's slide, which Keeoony navigates with practiced ease, cannot be mastered by Rabbit. When Keeoony calls out:

> "come out of that now," cried the Otter, "and hand me your" [029-005]

Rabbit's humiliation is complete. The Otter plunges into the water [029-008]—his natural element—while Rabbit flounders, the episode serving as a cautionary tale against envying another creature's nature-given gifts.

## Cultural Context

The Otter (Passamaquoddy _keeoony_, variant _kecoony_ [002-026]) occupies a modest but distinct place in Wabanaki animal lore. Unlike the Bear or Moose, whose narratives are bound up with hunting and subsistence, the Otter's story teaches through contrast—the futility of imitation when the imitator lacks the imitated's inherent capacities. The chapter title preserves this moral: "Master Rabbit sought to rival Kecoony, the Otter" [002-026].
