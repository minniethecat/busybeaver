---[045-001] For wherever Shamanism exists, there is to be found, in company with

[045-002] it, an older sorcery, or witchcraft, which it professes to despise, and

[045-003] against which it does battle. As the Catholic priest, by Bible

[045-004] incantations or scriptural magic, exorcises devils and charms cattle or

[045-005] sore throats, disowning the darker magic of older days, so the Shaman

[045-006] acts against the real _wizard_. Rink tells us that among the

[045-007] heathen Eskimo the Shaman is sacred, and witchcraft a deadly crime, but

[045-008] that the latter is the secret survival of a more ancient religion.

[045-009] _Voodoo_, whether practiced, as it is to-day, in Philadelphia, New

[045-010] York, Havana, or Senegambia, deals with alleged devils, poisons,

[045-011] chicken bones, the ivory root, unnatural orgies,--all, in short, that

[045-012] can startle and astonish ignorant natures; it is the combination of the

[045-013] oldest faith with its successor. Far higher forms are those of the

[045-014] magic of the black _Takowri_ whom one meets divining about the

[045-015] streets of Cairo, or of the Arab proper, which brings us fairly to the

[045-016] Cabala and the Jew, Cornelius Agrippa and Eliphas Levi.

[045-017] It is not difficult to understand how Shamanism with its drums and

[045-018] darkened rooms, its conjuring of evil-doers and extraction of diseases

[045-019] in tangible forms, should have spread from Central Asia to the

[045-020] Laplanders and Eskimo, and thence to the red Indians. Very little

[045-021] attention has been paid to the intercourse actually existing at the

[045-022] present day between these races. I have met with a Passamaquoddy Indian

[045-023] who spoke French well, who had been educated at a mission school, and

[045-024] who had been among the Eskimo. As regards legends and folk-lore, no one

[045-025] can read the Eskimo tales and those of this volume and not feel that

[045-026] the Algonquin is to the man of the icy north what the gypsy is to the

[045-027] Hindoo. As regards the early religion of both races, it is simply

[045-028] _identical_, and it is far too peculiar in its many similar

[045-029] details to have simply sprung up, as many might assume, from the common

[045-030] likeness in customs of all savages. For there is in both a great deal

[045-031] of "literary" culture, especially in the Algonquin, and it would be

[045-032] little less than miraculous that this too should have assimilated by

[045-033] chance. It does not help the "opposition" to point out that Algonquin

[045-034] legends, declare that their ancestors came from the west. Even so, they

[045-035] came from the Pacific coast, where Eskimo Shamanism exists in its most

[045-036] decided forms. But in any case it cannot be denied that in the red

[045-037] Indian mythology of New England, and of Canada and New Brunswick, we

[045-038] have a collection of vigorous, icy, powerful legends, like those of a

[045-039] strong northern race, while those of the middle continent, or Chippewa,

[045-040] are far feebler and gentler. Hiawatha-Manobozho is to Glooskap as a

[045-041] flute to a war trumpet.

[045-042] It is absurd to laugh at or pity the Indian for believing in his magic.

[045-043] Living as he does in the woods, becoming familiar with animals, and

[045-044] learning how much more intelligent and allied to man they are than

[045-045] civilized man supposes, he believes they have souls, and were perhaps

[045-046] originally human. Balaam's ass spoke once for every Christian; every

[045-047] animal spoke once for the Indian. If a child can be put to sleep by

[045-048] singing to it, why cannot insensibility to pain or a cure be caused by

[045-049] the same process? He is told that the wafer becomes the body of Christ;

[045-050] this may confirm his belief that the Indian god Manobozho turned bits

[045-051] of his own flesh or his wife's into raccoons, for food. If it is

[045-052] difficult for any educated or cultivated man to conceive how, if any

[045-053] condition or phase of supernaturalism be admitted, any other can be

[045-054] denied, how can the Indian be logically blamed for believing anything?

[045-055] But the greatest cause of all for a faith in magic is one which the

[045-056] white man talks about without feeling, and which the Indian feels

[045-057] without talking about it. I mean the poetry of nature, with all its

[045-058] quaint and beautiful superstitions. To every Algonquin a rotten log by

[045-059] the road, covered with moss, suggests the wild legend of the log-demon;

[045-060] the Indian corn and sweet flag in the swamp are the descendants of

[045-061] beautiful spirits who still live in them; Meeko, the squirrel, has the

[045-062] power of becoming a giant monster; flowers, beasts, trees, have all

[045-063] loved and talked and sung, and can even now do so, should the magician

[045-064] only come to speak the spell. And there are such magicians. Why should

[045-065] he doubt it? If the squirrel once yielded to such a power in man, it

[045-066] follows that some man may still have the power, or that he himself may

[045-067] acquire it. And how much of this feeling of the real poetry of nature

[045-068] does the white man or woman possess, who pities the poor ignorant

[045-069] Indian? A few second-hand scraps of Byron and Tupper, Tennyson and

[045-070] Longfellow, the jingle of a few rhymes and a few similes, and a little

[045-071] second-hand supernaturalism, more "accepted" than felt, and that

[045-072] derived from far foreign sources, does not give the white man what the

[045-073] Indian _feels_. Joe, or Noel, or Sabattis may seem to the American

[045-074] Philistine to be a ragged, miserable, ignorant Indian; but to the

[045-075] _scholar_ he is by far the Philistine's superior in that which

[045-076] life is _best_ worth living for.

[045-077] The magic of the Passamaquoddy and Penobscot, like the magician himself,

[045-078] is called _meteowlin_, _m'deoolin_ or _m'teoulin_. It is

[045-079] the same effectively as _meda_, which is from the same root. It is

[045-080] a power, but opinions differ as to how it is acquired. It is certain,

[045-081] as I was told by an old Passamaquoddy Indian, of Sebayk, near Campobello,

[045-082] that some children are born _m'teoulin_. They manifest it, even

[045-083] while babes, by being capricious, eccentric, and malicious. Others

[045-084] acquire the art as they grow older. From all that I have heard I infer

[045-085] that _m'teoulin_ takes two forms,--one of witchcraft, the other of

[045-086] magic. The former is innate, or may be acquired; the latter, for aught

[045-087] I know, may be sometimes inborn, but is generally acquired by fasting,

[045-088] abstinence of other kinds, and ceremonies. The two are distinctly

[045-089] different. Rink found in Greenland and Labrador that the Eskimo, as I

[045-090] have said, made this difference.

[045-091] I will now give, word for word, the remarks of certain Indians on this

[045-092] subject, beginning with those of an intelligent and prosperous old man,

[045-093] who is certainly enlightened and Christianized very much beyond the

[045-094] average, of his race. I had asked him if there were any

[045-095] _m'teoulin_, or magicians, living. He replied:--

[045-096] "There are. Many at St. John and Sebayk are still _m'teoulin_. I

[045-097] saw this myself thirty-five years ago at St. John's. There was a deaf

[045-098] Indian there. The white men were abusing him. They spat on him. By and

[045-099] by a _m'teoulin_ from St. John's came, a man of thirty-five or

[045-100] forty. I saw this. The _m'teoulin_ asked them not to abuse the

[045-101] deaf and dumb Indian. They turned on the _m'teoulin_. Then he

[045-102] screamed so horribly, so awfully, and looked so like a devil that the

[045-103] men were frightened. They fell on their knees, and could not move. They

[045-104] let the man go."

[045-105] This is precisely what is narrated by many writers of the Shaman

[045-106] screaming and distorting of the features. Very few people know of what

[045-107] the human, voice is capable. It can not only be trained to divine song,

[045-108] but to such demoniacal howling as to deafen and appall even the

[045-109] guardians of a lunatic asylum. In Lapland, Central Asia, or on Nootka

[045-110] Sound the initiated are trained in remote solitudes to these

[045-111] utterances, to which no one can listen without terror. My informant

[045-112] continued:--

[045-113] "Two or three weeks after I was in another place. We spoke of the

[045-114] _m'teoulin_. The white folks ridiculed them. I said there was one

[045-115] in Fredericton, and I said I would bet ten dollars that he would get

[045-116] the better of them. And they bet that no Indian could do more than they

[045-117] could. So the _m'teoulin_ came. And first of all he screamed so

[045-118] that no one could move. It was dreadful. Then he took seven steps

[045-119] through the ground up to his ankles, _just as if it had been light

[045-120] snow_. When I asked for the ten dollars, the white men paid. I gave

[045-121] it to the _m'teoulin_."

[045-122] Among the Greenland Eskimo the sorcerer, writes Rink, "after meeting

[045-123] with _tomassuk_, or guardian spirits, sometimes manifests it by

[045-124] his feet sinking into the rocky ground _just as if in snow_." He

[045-125] uses the very words of the Indian who described the same thing to me.

[045-126] And very recently in Philadelphia, in fact while I was writing the

[045-127] preceding remarks, a spiritualist named Gordon performed the very same

[045-128] trick. Having been detected, a full account of the manner of action

[045-129] appeared in the Press of that city. It was done by a peculiar method of

[045-130] stooping, and of concealing the stoop behind a skirt. It was a very odd

[045-131] coincidence that the explanation should thus present itself while I was

[045-132] seeking it.

[045-133] This Shaman Eskimo trick was known to the Norsemen. In the Saga of

[045-134] Thorstein it is said that Ogantun, a noted sorcerer, when stabbed at,

[045-135] "thrust himself down into the ground, so that only the soles of his

[045-136] feet could be seen;" and of Kol it was said that "he could pass through

[045-137] the earth as well as walk upon it."

[045-138] "Women are sometimes _m'teoulin_. There is one at Psesuk (Bar

[045-139] Harbor) now, this summer. You have met her. She is ---'s wife.

[045-140] [Footnote: I am acquainted with all the parties, but for obvious

[045-141] reasons suppress their names.] If you offend her she can hurt you in

[045-142] strange ways.

[045-143] "She is a good doctor. Once she cured a man. When he got well he could

[045-144] not pay her for the medicine. His name is Louis ---. She asked for her

[045-145] money; she asked many times; she could not get it. He was going to the

[045-146] woods, far away, to trap; he said he would pay her when he returned,

[045-147] but she wanted it then. She said, 'I will never forget this; I will be

[045-148] revenged.' He went far up the St. John River with his traps; he set

[045-149] them in the stream for beaver. All that he caught that winter was

[045-150] sticks, and sometimes an eel. Then at the end of the day he would say

[045-151] to his man, 'It is of no use.' And then they could hear the witch

[045-152] laughing behind the bushes, and tittering when he came home. So it went

[045-153] on long. Then he was sorry, and said, 'I wish I had paid that woman

[045-154] what I owed her.' And at once they heard a voice from the bushes, or

[045-155] rocks, say, 'Louis, that will do. It is enough.' And the next day they

[045-156] caught two beaver, and every day two, and so on, till the season was

[045-157] over.

[045-158] "This happened in 1872, in Miramichi Waters."

[045-159] There does not appear to be any single approved method of acquiring

[045-160] _m'teoulin_. Some, as I have said, are born to it, but they appear

[045-161] to be wizards or witches. Others are formally trained from boyhood by

[045-162] the experienced magicians. Others acquire certain gifts by certain

[045-163] ceremonies or penances. Of this kind was the power obtained in the

[045-164] manner narrated in the following story, which I heard from an old

[045-165] Passamaquoddy:--

[045-166] "There was once a young man who wished to become a very wise and brave

[045-167] warrior, like his father. And his father said to him, 'I get all my

[045-168] luck of every kind from my dreams. You can have such dreams; any man

[045-169] can, if he will do a certain thing; but that thing is not easy for a

[045-170] young man like you. You must sleep seven nights with a virgin, and

[045-171] never touch her.'

[045-172] "The young man thought this over for a few days, and then asked his

[045-173] father how it could be arranged or managed.
