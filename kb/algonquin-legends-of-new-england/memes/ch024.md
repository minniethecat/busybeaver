---[024-001] Now as of all rowdies and rascals the wolves are the worst, we may well

[024-002] believe that it was with great joy Lox heard, as the darkness was

[024-003] coming on, a long, sad howl, far away, betokening the coming of a pack

[024-004] of these pleasant people; to which he raised his own voice in the wolf

[024-005] tongue,--for he was learned in many languages,--and soon was surrounded

[024-006] by some fifteen or sixteen lupine land loafers, who danced, rolling

[024-007] over, barking and biting one another, all for very joy at meeting with

[024-008] him. And the elder, he who was captain, or the sogmo, [Footnote:

[024-009] _Sogmo_, sagamore, a chief; the word corrupted into sachem.] said,

[024-010] "Peradventure thou wilt encamp with us this night, for it is ill for a

[024-011] gentleman to be alone, where he might encounter vulgar fellows." And

[024-012] Lox thanked him as if he were doing him a favor, and accepted the best

[024-013] of their dried meat, and took the highest place by their fire, and

[024-014] smoked the chief's choicest _tomawe_ out of his best pipe, and all

[024-015] that with such vast condescension that the wolves grinned with delight.

[024-016] And when they laid them down to sleep he that was the eldest, or the

[024-017] sogmo, bade the younger cover their guest Lox over very carefully. Now

[024-018] the tail of the wolf has broad-spreading, shaggy hair, and Lox, being

[024-019] sleepy, really thought it was a fur blanket that they spread, and

[024-020] though the night was cold enough to crack the rocks he threw the

[024-021] covering off; twice he did this, and the chief who looked after him,

[024-022] with all the rest, admired him greatly because he cared so little for

[024-023] the cold or for their care.

[024-024] And having eaten after they arose, when in the morning they would wend

[024-025] away, the Wolf Chief said unto Lox, "Uncle, thou hast yet three days'

[024-026] hard travel before thee in a land where there is neither home, house,

[024-027] nor hearth, and it will be ill camping without a fire. Now I have a

[024-028] most approved and excellent charm, or spell, by which I can give thee

[024-029] three fires, but no more; yet will they suffice, one for each night,

[024-030] until thou gettest to thy journey's end. And this is the manner thereof:

[024-031] that thou shalt take unto thee dry wood, even such as men commonly burn,

[024-032] and thou shalt put them together, even as boys build little wigwams for

[024-033] sport, and then thou shalt jump over it. And truly, uncle, this is an

[024-034] approved and excellent charm of ripe antiquity, kept as a solemn secret

[024-035] among the wolves, and thou art the first not of our holy nation to whom

[024-036] it hath been given." So they parted.

[024-037] Now Lox trudged on, and as he went westwards kept thinking of this

[024-038] great secret of the pious and peculiar people, and wondering if it were

[024-039] even as the Wolf said, or only a deceit; for however kindly he was

[024-040] treated by people, he always suspected that they mocked him to scorn,

[024-041] or were preparing to do so; for as he ever did this thing himself to

[024-042] every condition of mankind or beasts, he constantly awaited to have it

[024-043] done to him. And being curious withal, and anxious to see some new

[024-044] thing, he had not walked half an hour ere he said, "Tush! let me try

[024-045] it. Yea, and I will!" So building up the sticks, he jumped over them,

[024-046] and at once they caught fire and blazed up, and it came to pass even as

[024-047] the Wolf had prophesied.

[024-048] Now having solaced himself by the heat, Lox went on. And anon it grew

[024-049] cold again, and he began to think how pleasant it was to be warm; and

[024-050] being, like most evil people, wanting in a corner of wisdom, he at once

[024-051] put the sticks together again and jumped over them, and as before there

[024-052] rose a blaze, and he was happy. And this was the second fire, and he

[024-053] had still three cold nights before him before he could reach his home.

[024-054] And yet this Wolverine, who was so wise in all wickedness and witty in

[024-055] evil-doing, had not walked into the afternoon before he began to think

[024-056] of the third fire. "Truly," he said to himself, "who knows but the

[024-057] weather may take a turn to a thaw, and give us a warm night? Hum! ha!

[024-058] methinks by the look of the clouds the wind will soon be southwesterly.

[024-059] Have I not heard my grandmother say that such a color, even the red,

[024-060] meant something?--I forget what, but it might be a warm change. Luck be

[024-061] on me, I will risk the odds." And, saying this, he set up the sticks

[024-062] again; and this was the last fire, though it was not even the first

[024-063] night.

[024-064] And when he came after dark to the first camping place it grew cold in

[024-065] earnest. Howbeit Lox, thinking that what was good for once must be good

[024-066] forever, made him his little pile of sticks and jumped over them. It

[024-067] was of no avail. Finally, when he had jumped twenty or thirty times

[024-068] more, there arose a little smoke, and, having his heart cheered by

[024-069] this, he kept on jumping. Now it is said that there can be no smoke

[024-070] without fire, but this time it went not beyond smoke. Then Lox jumped

[024-071] again, and this time the Indian Devil came up within him, and he swore

[024-072] by it that he would jump till it blazed or burst. So he kept on, and

[024-073] yet there came no comfort, not even a spark; and being at last aweary

[024-074] he fell down in a swoon, and so froze to death. And so the Devil was

[024-075] dead, and that was the last of him for that turn; but I think he got

[024-076] over it, for he has been seen many a time since.

[024-077] In two stories Lox (once as the _loup cervier_) is intimate with

[024-078] the wolves. Loki was the father of the wolves. Loki is fire: here Lox

[024-079] dies for want of fire. Since I wrote the foregoing, Mrs. W. Wallace

[024-080] Brown has learned that Lox is definitely the king or chief of the

[024-081] wolves, and that many Indians deny that he is really an animal at all,

[024-082] though he assumes the forms of certain animals. He is a spirit, and the

[024-083] Mischief Maker. It will be admitted that this brings the Lox much

[024-084] nearer to Loki.

[024-085] It is said that when Glooskap left the world, as he took away with him

[024-086] the kings of all the animals, Lox went with him as king of the Wolves.

[024-087] This is an identification of him with Malsum, the Wolf, himself.

[024-088] _How Master Lox played a Trick on Mrs. Bear, who lost her eyesight

[024-089] and had her eyes opened._

[024-090] (Micmac.)

[024-091] Don't live with mean people if you can help it. They will turn your

[024-092] greatest sorrow to their own account if they can. Bad habit gets to be

[024-093] devilish second nature. One dead herring is not much, but one by one

[024-094] you may make such a heap of them as to stink out a whole village.

[024-095] As it happened to old Mrs. Bear, who was easy as regarded people,

[024-096] And thought well of everybody, and trusted all. So she took in for

[024-097] A house-mate another old woman. Their wigwam was all by itself, and

[024-098] the next neighbor was so far off that he was not their neighbor at

[024-099] all, but that of some other folks.

[024-100] One night the old women made up a fire, and lay down and went to sleep

[024-101] Indian-fashion,--_witkusoodijik_,--heads and points, so that both

[024-102] could lie with their back to the fire.

[024-103] Now while they were sound asleep, Lox, the Wolverine, or Indian Devil,

[024-104] came prowling round. Some people say it was Hespuns, the Raccoon; and

[024-105] it is a fact that Master Coon can play a very close game of deviltry on

[024-106] his own account. However, this time it must have been Lox, as you can

[024-107] see by the tracks.

[024-108] While they were both sound asleep Lox looked in. He found the old women

[024-109] asleep, heads and points, and at once saw his way to a neat little bit

[024-110] of mischief. So, going into the woods, he cut a fine long sapling-pole

[024-111] of _ow-bo-goos_, and poked one end of it into the fire till it was

[024-112] a burning coal. Then he touched the soles of Mrs. Bear; and she,

[024-113] waking, cried out to the other, "Take care! you are burning me!" which

[024-114] the other denied like a thunder-clap.

[024-115] Then Master Lox carefully applied the end of the hot pole to the feet

[024-116] of the other woman. First she dreamed that she was walking on hot sand

[024-117] and roasting rocks in summer-time, and then that the Mohawks were

[024-118] cooking her at the death-fire; and then she woke up, and, seeing where

[024-119] she was, began to blame Mrs. Bear for it all, just as if she were a

[024-120] Mohawk.

[024-121] Ah, yes. Well, Master Lox, seeing them fighting in a great rage, burst

[024-122] out laughing, so that he actually burst himself, and fell down dead

[024-123] with delight. It was a regular side-splitter. When my grandfather said

[024-124] _that_ we _always_ laughed.

[024-125] In the morning, when the women came out, there lay a dead devil at the

[024-126] door. He must indeed have looked like a Raccoon this time; but whatever

[024-127] he was, they took him, skinned him, and dressed him for breakfast. Then

[024-128] the kettle was hung and the water boiled, and they popped him in. But

[024-129] as soon as it began to scald he began to come to life. In a minute he

[024-130] was all together again, alive and well, and with one good leap went

[024-131] clear of the kettle. Rushing out of the lodge, he grabbed his skin,

[024-132] which hung on a bush outside, put it on, and in ten seconds was safe in

[024-133] the greenwood. He just saved himself with a whole skin.

[024-134] Now Master Lox had precious little time, you will say, to do any more

[024-135] mischief between his coming to life and running away; yet, short as the

[024-136] allowance was, he made a great deal of it. For even while jumping out

[024-137] his wits for wickedness came to him, and he just kicked the edge of the

[024-138] pot, so that it spilled all the scalding hot water into the fire, and

[024-139] threw up the ashes with a great splutter. They flew into the eyes of

[024-140] Dame Bear and blinded her.

[024-141] Now this was hard on the old lady. She could not go out hunting, or set

[024-142] traps, or fish any more; and her partner, being mean, kept all the nice

[024-143] morsels for herself. Mrs. Bear only got the leanest and poorest of the

[024-144] meat, though there was plenty of the best. As my grandfather used to

[024-145] say, Mrs. Bear might have fared better if she had used her eyes earlier.

[024-146] One day, when she was sitting alone in the wigwam, Mrs. Bear began to

[024-147] remember all she had ever heard about eyes, and it came into her head

[024-148] that sometimes they were closed up in such a way that clever folk could

[024-149] cut them open again. So she got her knife and sharpened it, and,

[024-150] carefully cutting a little, saw the light of day. Then she was glad

[024-151] indeed, and with a little more cutting found that she could see as

[024-152] well as ever. And as good luck does nut come single, the very first

[024-153] thing she beheld was an abundance of beautiful fat venison, fish, and

[024-154] maple-sugar hung up overhead.

[024-155] Dame Bear said nothing about her having recovered her eyesight. She

[024-156] watched all the cooking going on, and saw the daintiest dinner, which

[024-157] all went into one platter, and a very poor lot of bones and scraps

[024-158] placed in another. Then, when she was called to eat, she simply said to

[024-159] the other woman, who kept the best, "Well, you have done well for

[024-160] yourself!"

[024-161] The other saw that Mrs. Bear had recovered her sight. She was

[024-162] frightened, for Dame Bear was by far the better man of the two. So she

[024-163] cried out, "Bless me! what a mistake I've made! Why, I gave you the

[024-164] wrong dish. You know, my dear sister, that I always give you the best

[024-165] because you are blind."
