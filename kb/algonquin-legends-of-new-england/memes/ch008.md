[008-002] Stay on a distant island,

[008-003] He is stuck by the chief

[008-004] Fast with his back to a tree."

[008-005] Now Pitcher the Witch, being mad with shame and spite, fled from the

[008-006] face of man, and ran through the woods like a wild wolf. And so she

[008-007] came to Bar Harbor (Pes'sonkqu', P.), and sat down on a log, and said,

[008-008] with her heart full of bitterness and malice, "I would that I could

[008-009] become something which should torment all men." And as she said this

[008-010] she became a mosquito (T'sis-o, P.), and so it came to pass that

[008-011] mosquitoes were made. And to this day men see that wherever the Black

[008-012] Cat is, there too is the Sable not far away. [Footnote: The

[008-013] Passamaquoddy version relates that Pitcher in her flight pursued a

[008-014] moose to Bar Harbor, where, having killed him and drawn out the

[008-015] entrails, she petrified him. A Penobscot woman told me she had often

[008-016] seen the moose rock there, and the "inments." But she attributed the

[008-017] deed to Glooskap, to whom it properly belongs, his petrified moose and

[008-018] dogs and the print of his bow, etc., being still shown in Nova Scotia;

[008-019] and it is also said that it was at Freshwater, after returning from Bar

[008-020] Harbor (Maine), that Pitcher was changed into a mosquito. Another story

[008-021] states that Pook-jin-skwess, having pursued young men all her life,

[008-022] changed into a mosquito that she might continue to prey on them.]

[008-023] Of this Pook-jin-skwess it was said that she had children of her own,

[008-024] begotten by sorcerers and giants and monsters; but as they were all

[008-025] ugly she stole from the Indian women their fairest babes, and brought

[008-026] them up as if they were her own, that she might not be entirely put to

[008-027] shame because of her children. And once she had thus stolen a boy, and

[008-028] when he grew up some one said to him that he should not believe that

[008-029] she was his mother, but should question her as to it. Now the youth,

[008-030] reflecting on this, observed that his brothers and sisters were all as

[008-031] ugly as evil beasts and no better behaved, while he himself was comely

[008-032] and good. Then he asked her what this might mean. And she replied,

[008-033] laughing, "Because they were all begotten (or born) in the night-time,

[008-034] but you are a child of the day and of light." [Footnote: There is

[008-035] probably an allusion in this to the Wabanaki, or Children of Light;

[008-036] that is, the Algonquin. This story was told me by Noel Josephs, a

[008-037] Passamaquoddy. I have been told by an old Passamaquoddy woman that the

[008-038] descendants of Pook-jin-skwess were the 'Nmmok-skwess. This stealing

[008-039] the white boy is related in another tale more folly. It may refer to

[008-040] the early dark Eskimo.]

[008-041] _How Glooskap became friendly to the Loons, and made them his

[008-042] Messengers._

[008-043] (Micmac.)

[008-044] When Glooskap was pursuing Win-pe, he one day on Uktukamkw saw from

[008-045] afar flying over water the Kwe-moo (M.), or Loons. And thrice did their

[008-046] chief make the circle of the lake, coming near to the land of men and

[008-047] beasts every time, as if he would fain seek somewhat. Then Glooskap

[008-048] asking him what he wanted, Kwe-moo replied that he would be his servant

[008-049] and friend. So Glooskap taught him a strange long cry like the howl of

[008-050] a dog, and when the loons were in need of him or would pray to him they

[008-051] were to utter this cry.

[008-052] And it came to pass that when he was in Newfoundland he came to an

[008-053] Indian town, and they who dwelt therein were all Kwee-moo-uk, or Loons.

[008-054] And they, as men, were exceeding glad to see their lord, who had

[008-055] blessed them as birds, and did their best to please him. So he made

[008-056] them his huntsmen and messengers, and in all the tales of Glooskap the

[008-057] Kweemoo ever appears as faithful to him. Whence to this day, when the

[008-058] Indians hear the cry of the Loon, they say, "_Kwemoo el-komik-too-ajul

[008-059] Gloocapal_" (He is calling upon Glooskap).

[008-060] _How Glooskap made his Uncle Mikchich the Turtle into a Great Man,

[008-061] and got him a Wife. [Footnote: This legend of the tortoise is carefully

[008-062] compiled from six different versions: the narration of Tomah Josephs, a

[008-063] Passamaquoddy; the Anglo-Indian manuscript, already cited; two accounts

[008-064] in the Rand manuscript; the author quoted without credit in _The

[008-065] Maritime Provinces_; and one by Mrs. W. Wallace Brown. As the totem

[008-066] of the Tortoise was of the highest rank among the Algonquins, this

[008-067] account of its origin is of corresponding interest. Having employed an

[008-068] old Indian to carve the handle of a war or scalping knife for me, such

[008-069] as was used by his Passamaquoddy ancestors, he carved on it a tortoise.

[008-070] It was especially the totem of the Lenni-Lenape, called by the

[008-071] Passamaquoddies _Lel-le-mabe_, "the people."] Of Turtles' Eggs,

[008-072] and how Glooskap vanquished a Sorcerer by smoking Tobacco._

[008-073] (Micmac and Passamaquoddy.)

[008-074] Now when Glooskap left Uktukamkw, or Newfoundland, it was in a canoe,

[008-075] and he came to Piktook (M. for Pictou), which means the bubbling up of

[008-076] air, because there is much bubbling in the water near that place. And

[008-077] here there was an Indian village, and in that place the Master met with

[008-078] a man whom he loved all his life.

[008-079] And this was not because this man, whose name in Micmac is Mikchich and

[008-080] in Passamaquoddy Chick-we-notchk, meaning the Turtle, was great, or

[008-081] well favored, or rich. For truly he was none of these, being very poor

[008-082] and lazy, no longer young, and not very clever or wise in any way. It

[008-083] is said that he was indeed Glooskap's uncle, but others think that this

[008-084] was by adoption. However, this old fellow bore all his wants with such

[008-085] good nature that the Master, taking him in great affection, resolved to

[008-086] make of him a mighty man. Which came to pass, and that in a strange

[008-087] manner, as we shall see.

[008-088] For coming to Piktook, where there were above a hundred wigwams,

[008-089] Glooskap, being a very handsome, stately man, with the manner of a

[008-090] great chief, was much admired, and that not a little by all the women,

[008-091] so that every one wished to have him in the house. Yet he gave them all

[008-092] the go-by, and dwelt with his old uncle, in whose quaint ways and

[008-093] old-time stories he took great delight. And there was to be a great

[008-094] feast with games, but Glooskap did not care to go, either as a guest

[008-095] or a performer in the play.

[008-096] Still he inquired of Mikchich if he would not take part in it, telling

[008-097] him that all the maidens would be there, and asking him why he had

[008-098] never married, and saying that he should not live alone. Then the uncle

[008-099] said: "Poor and old and plain am I; I have not even garments fit for a

[008-100] feast; better were it for me to smoke my pipe at home." "Truly, if that

[008-101] be all, uncle," replied Glooskap, "I trow I can turn tailor and fit you

[008-102] to a turn; and have no care as to your outside or your face, for to him

[008-103] who knows how, 't is as easy to make a man over as a suit of clothes."

[008-104] "Yes; but, nephew," said Mikchich, "how say you as to making over the

[008-105] inside of a mortal?" "By the great Beaver!" answered the Master, "that

[008-106] is something harder to do, else I were not so long at work in this

[008-107] world. But before I leave this town I shall do that also for you; and

[008-108] as for this present sport, do but put on my belt." And when he had done

[008-109] that, Mikchich became so young and handsome that no man or woman ever

[008-110] saw the like. And then Glooskap dressed him in his own best clothes,

[008-111] and promised him that to the end of his days, whenever he should be a

[008-112] man, he would be the comeliest of men; and because he was patient and

[008-113] tough, he should, as an animal, become the hardest to kill of all

[008-114] creatures on the face of the earth, as it came to pass.

[008-115] So Mikchich went to the feast. Now the chief of Piktook had three

[008-116] beautiful daughters, and the youngest was the loveliest in the land.

[008-117] And on her he cast his eyes, and returning said, "I have seen one whom

[008-118] I want." Now all the young men in Piktook desired this girl, and would

[008-119] kill any one who would win her.

[008-120] So the next day Glooskap, taking a bunch of _wawbap_ (P., wampum),

[008-121] went, to the chief and proposed for Mikchich, [Footnote: All invitations

[008-122] to festivals, or formal ceremonies, proposals of marriage, etc., were

[008-123] preceded among these tribes by a gift of wampum.] and the mother at

[008-124] once said "Yes." So the girl made up a bed of fresh twigs and covered

[008-125] it with a great white bear-skin, and went to Mikchich, and they

[008-126] returned and had dried meat for supper. So they were married.

[008-127] Now Turtle seemed to be very lazy; and when others hunted he lounged at

[008-128] home. One day his young wife said to him that if this went on thus they

[008-129] must soon starve. So he put on his snow-shoes and went forth, and she

[008-130] followed him to see what he would do. And he had not gone far ere he

[008-131] tripped and fell down, and the girl, returning, told her mother that he

[008-132] was worthless. But the mother said, "He will do something yet. Be

[008-133] patient."

[008-134] One day it came to pass that Glooskap said to Mikchich, "To-morrow

[008-135] there will be a great game at ball, and you must play. But because you

[008-136] have made yourself enemies of all the young men here, they will seek to

[008-137] slay you, by crowding all together and trampling upon you. And when

[008-138] they do this it will be by your father-in-law's lodge, and to escape

[008-139] them I give you the power to jump high over it. This you may do twice,

[008-140] but the third time will be terrible for you, and yet it must be."

[008-141] All this happened as he foretold; for the young men indeed tried to

[008-142] take his life, and to escape them Mikchich jumped over the lodge, so

[008-143] that he seemed like a bird flying. But the third time he did this he

[008-144] was caught on the top of the tent-poles, and hung there dangling in the

[008-145] smoke which rose from below.

[008-146] [Illustration: THE MUD TURTLE JUMPING OVER THE WIGWAM OF HIS

[008-147] FATHER-IN-LAW.]

[008-148] Then Glooskap, who was seated in the tent, said, "Uncle, I will now

[008-149] make you the _sogmo_, or great chief of the Tortoises, and you

[008-150] shall bear up a great nation." Then he smoked Mikchich [Footnote: In a

[008-151] verbal Passamaquoddy narrative (John Gabriel), and in one given in

[008-152] _The Maritime Provinces_, this was effected by Glooskap with

[008-153] tobacco-smoke from his pipe. In Mr. Rand's manuscript it is the smoke

[008-154] of the tent-fire. The Passamaquoddy narrations are invariably more

[008-155] spirited and _humorous_ than the Micmac.] so long that his skin

[008-156] became a hard shell, and the marks of the smoke may be seen thereon to

[008-157] this day. And removing his entrails he destroyed them, so that but one

[008-158] short one was left. And he cried aloud, "_Milooks_! (M.) My

[008-159] nephew, you will kill me!" But the nephew replied, "Not so. I am giving

[008-160] you great life. From this time you may roll through a flame and never

[008-161] feel it, and live on land or in the water. And though your head be cut

[008-162] off, it will live for nine days, and your heart, even, shall beat as

[008-163] long when taken from your body." So Mikchich rejoiced greatly.

[008-164] And this came betimes, for he soon had need of it all. For the next day

[008-165] all the men went on a hunt, and the Master warned him that they would

[008-166] seek to slay him. Now the young men went on before, and Turtle lingered

[008-167] behind; but all at once he made a magic flight far over their heads,

[008-168] unseen, and deep in the forest he slew a moose. Then he drew this to

[008-169] the snow-shoe track or road, and when his foes came up there he sat

[008-170] upon the moose, smoking, and waiting for them. Now Glooskap had told

[008-171] them that they would see some one come out ahead of them all that day,

[008-172] and when this came to pass they were more angered in their hearts than

[008-173] ever.
