---[019-001] "_Are any to be saved by any one_?" "Dunno. _Me hear_ how

[019-002] some say world all burn up some day, water all boil all fire; some good

[019-003] ones be taken up in good heavens, but me dunno,--me just _hear_

[019-004] that. Only hear so."

[019-005] It was owing to a mere chance question that this account of the Last

[019-006] Day was obtained from an Indian. It was related to Mrs. W. Wallace

[019-007] Brown, of Calais, Maine, by Mrs. Le Cool, an old Passamaquoddy Indian.

[019-008] It casts a great light on the myth of Glooskap, since it appears that a

[019-009] day is to come when, like Arthur, Barbarossa, and other heroes in

[019-010] retreat, he is to come forth at a new twilight of the gods, exterminate

[019-011] the _Iglesmani_, and establish an eternal happy hunting-ground.

[019-012] This preparing for a great final battle is more suggestive of Norse or

[019-013] Scandinavian influence than of aught else. It is certainly not of a

[019-014] late date, or Christian, but it is very much like the Edda and

[019-015] Ragnarok. Heine does not observe, in the Twilight of the Gods, that

[019-016] Jupiter or Mars intend to return and conquer the world. But the

[019-017] Norsemen expected such a fight, when arrows would fly like hail, and

[019-018] Glooskap is supposed to be deliberately preparing for it.

[019-019] A very curious point remains to be noted in this narration. When the

[019-020] Indians speak of Christian, or white, or civilized teachings, they say,

[019-021] "I heard," or, "I have been told." This they never do as regards their

[019-022] own ancient traditions. When Mrs. Le Cool said that she "had heard"

[019-023] that some were to be taken up into _good_ heavens, she declared,

[019-024] in her way, that this was what Christians said, but that she was not so

[019-025] sure of it. The Northeastern Algonquin always distinguish very accurately

[019-026] between their ancient lore and that derived from the whites. I have often

[019-027] heard French fairy tales and Aesop's fables Indianized to perfection, but

[019-028] the narrator always knew that they were not _N'Karnayoo_, "of the

[019-029] old time."

[019-030] Glooskap is now living in a Norse-like Asa-heim; but there is to come a

[019-031] day when the arrows will be ready, and he will go forth and slay all

[019-032] the wicked. Malsum the Wolf, his twin brother, the typical colossal

[019-033] type of all Evil, will come to life, with all the giant cannibals,

[019-034] witches, and wild devils slain of old; but the champion will gird on

[019-035] his magic belt, and the arrows will fly in a rain as at Ragnarok: the

[019-036] hero will come sailing in his wonderful canoe, which expands to hold an

[019-037] army. Thus it will be on

[019-038] "That day of wrath, that dreadful day,

[019-039] When heaven and earth shall pass away,"

[019-040] with all things, in blood and death and fire. Then there will come the

[019-041] eternal happy hunting-grounds.

[019-042] If this was derived from Christian priests, it must be admitted that it

[019-043] has changed wonderfully on the way. It is to me very heathen, grimly

[019-044] archaic, and with the strong stamp of an original. Its resemblance to

[019-045] the Norse is striking. Either the Norsemen told it to the Eskimo and

[019-046] the Indians, or the latter to the Norsemen. None know, after all, what

[019-047] was going on for ages in the early time, up about Jotunheim, in the

[019-048] North Atlantic! Vessels came to Newfoundland to fish for cod since

[019-049] unknown antiquity, and, returning, reported that they had been to

[019-050] Tartary.

[019-051] It may be assumed at once that this Indian Last Battle of the Giants,

[019-052] or of the good hero giants against the Evil, led by the Malsum-Fenris

[019-053] Wolf, was not derived from the Canadian French. The influence of, the

[019-054] latter is to be found even among the Chippewas, but they never dealt in

[019-055] myths like this.

[019-056] It is very remarkable indeed that the one great principle of the Norse

[019-057] mythology is identical with that of the Indian. So long as man shall

[019-058] make war and heroism his standard, just so long his hero god exists.

[019-059] But there will come a day when mankind can war no more,--when higher

[019-060] civilization must prevail. Then there will be a great final war, and

[019-061] death of the heroes, and death of their foes, and after all a new

[019-062] world.

[019-063] "Then shall another come

[019-064] yet mightier,

[019-065] although I dare not

[019-066] his name declare.

[019-067] Few may see

[019-068] further forth

[019-069] than when Odin

[019-070] meets the Wolf."

[019-071] (Hyndluloid, 42.)

[019-072] The Norsemen may have drawn this from a Christian source; but the

[019-073] Indian, to judge by form, spirit, and expression, would seem to have

[019-074] taken it from the Norse.

[019-075] _How Glooskap found the Summer._

[019-076] In the long ago time when people lived always in the early red morning,

[019-077] before sunrise, before the _Squid to neck_ was peopled as to-day,

[019-078] Glooskap went very far north, where all was ice.

[019-079] He came to a wigwam. Therein he found a giant, a great giant, for he

[019-080] was Winter. Glooskap entered; he sat down. Then Winter gave him a pipe;

[019-081] he smoked, and the giant told tales of the old times.

[019-082] The charm was on him; it was the Frost. The giant talked on and froze,

[019-083] and Glooskap fell asleep. He slept for six months, like a toad. Then

[019-084] the charm fled, and he awoke. He went his way home; he went to the

[019-085] south, and at every step it grew warmer, and the flowers began to come

[019-086] up and talk to him.

[019-087] He came to where there were many little ones dancing in the forest;

[019-088] their queen was Summer. I am singing the truth: it was Summer, the

[019-089] inmost beautiful one ever born. He caught her up; he kept her by a

[019-090] crafty trick. The Master cut a moose-hide into a long cord; as he ran

[019-091] away with Summer he let the end trail behind him.

[019-092] They, the fairies of Light, pulled at the cord, but as Glooskap ran,

[019-093] the cord ran out, and though they pulled he left them far away. So he

[019-094] came to the lodge of Winter, but now he had Summer in his bosom; and

[019-095] Winter welcomed him, for he hoped to freeze him again to sleep. I am

[019-096] singing the song of Summer.

[019-097] But this time the Master did the talking. This time his _m'teoulin_

[019-098] was the strongest. And ere long the sweat ran down Winter's face, and

[019-099] then he melted more and quite away, as did the wigwam. Then every thing

[019-100] awoke; the grass grew, the fairies came out, and the snow ran down the

[019-101] rivers, carrying away the dead leaves. Then Glooskap left Summer with

[019-102] them, and went home.

[019-103] This poem--for it is such--was related to Mrs. W. Wallace Brown by an

[019-104] Indian named Neptune. It appears to be the completer form of the

[019-105] beautiful allegory of Winter and Spring given in the Hiawatha Legends

[019-106] as Peboan and Seegwum (Odjibwa). The struggle between Spring and

[019-107] Winter, Summer and Winter, or Heat and Cold, represented as incarnate

[019-108] human or mythic beings, forms the subject of several Indian legends, as

[019-109] it does a part of the Hymiskrida, in the Edda. The German J. B.

[019-110] Friedreich (Symbolik der Natur, Wurzburg, 1859) remarks that in the

[019-111] Bible, Job xxxviii. 28, and in the Song of the Three in the Fiery

[019-112] Furnace, Ice and Snow are spoken of as intelligences.

[019-113] Heat and cold, in classic times, were supposed to be united, yet in

[019-114] conflict, in the lightning and hail (Virgil, Aen, VIII. 429), the symbol

[019-115] for this being a twisted horn. In the legend of the _Culloo_ the

[019-116] frost giantess can only be killed by a crooked horn thrust into her

[019-117] ear. The horn darts out at once into incredible, irregular length, and

[019-118] evidently means lightning. In the Edda the he-goat is, on account of

[019-119] his horns, the symbol of lightning and storm. (Schwenk, Sinnbilden der

[019-120] alten Volker.) The Giala-horn of the Edda (Nyer up. Dict Scan. Mythol.)

[019-121] is the thunder which summons the Elves. "Miolner, the hammer of Thor,

[019-122] with which he kills frost giants, is the lightning." (Kirchner, Thor's

[019-123] Donnerkeil, Neu Strelitz, 1853, p. 60.) The coincidence of the symbols

[019-124] in the Edda with that of the lightning horn in the Indian legend is

[019-125] very curious, if nothing more.

[019-126] The cord which Glooskap unrolls, and with which he deceives the

[019-127] fairies, who think they have him fast, while he is escaping, means

[019-128] delusive speech or plausible talk. To "talk like paying out rope" is an

[019-129] old simile.

[019-130] "Speech runes thou must know,

[019-131] If thou wilt that no one

[019-132] for injury with hate requite thee.

[019-133] Those thou must wind,

[019-134] Those thou must wrap round (thee),

[019-135] Those thou must altogether place

[019-136] in the assembly,

[019-137] where people have

[019-138] into full court to go."

[019-139] (Sigrdrifumal.)

[019-140] This is a merely accidental coincidence, but it illustrates the meaning

[019-141] of the myth. In both cases it is "wound or wrapped around" and rapidly

[019-142] unrolled, and the same simile.

[019-143] The following poem on Glooskap may be appropriately placed in this

[019-144] work. The allusion to the agates of Cape Blomidon refers to a tradition

[019-145] given by S. T. Rand, which states that when Glooskap would make his

[019-146] adopted grandmother young again he created the brilliant stones, which

[019-147] are still found at that place, to adorn her. [Footnote: _Youth's

[019-148] Companion_.]

[019-149] THE LEGEND OF GLOOSKAP.

[019-150] Bathed in the sunshine still as of yore

[019-151] Stretches the peaceful Acadian shore;

[019-152] Fertile meadows and fields of grain

[019-153] Smile as they drink the summer rain.

[019-154] There like a sentinel, grim and gray,

[019-155] Blomidon stands at the head of the bay,

[019-156] And the famous Fundy tides, at will,

[019-157] Sweep into Minas Basin still.

[019-158] With wondrous beauty the Gaspereanx

[019-159] Winds its way to the sea below,

[019-160] And the old Acadian Grand Pre

[019-161] Is the home of prosperous men to-day.

[019-162] The place where Basil the blacksmith wrought,

[019-163] In the glow of his forge, is a classic spot,

[019-164] And every summer tourists are seen

[019-165] In the fairy haunts of Evangeline.

[019-166] But the old Acadian woods and shores,

[019-167] Rich in beautiful legend stores,

[019-168] Were once the home of an older race,

[019-169] Who wore their epics with untaught grace.

[019-170] Long ere the dikes that guard for aye

[019-171] From the merciless tides the old Grand Pre,

[019-172] Built by the Frenchman's tireless hands,

[019-173] Grew round the rich Acadian lands.

[019-174] The Micmac sailed in his birch canoe

[019-175] Over the Basin, calm and blue;

[019-176] Speared the salmon, his heart's desire,

[019-177] Danced and slept by his wigwam fire;

[019-178] Far in the depth of the forest gray

[019-179] Hunted the moose the livelong day,

[019-180] While the mother sang to her Micmac child

[019-181] Songs of the forest, weird and wild.

[019-182] Over the tribe, with jealous eye,

[019-183] Watched the Great Spirit from on high,

[019-184] While on the crest of Blomidon

[019-185] Glooskap, the God-man, dwelt alone.

[019-186] No matter how far his feet might stray

[019-187] From the favorite haunts of his tribe away,

[019-188] Glooskap could hear the Indian's prayer,

[019-189] And send some message of comfort there.

[019-190] Glooskap it was who taught the use

[019-191] Of the bow and the spear, and sent the moose

[019-192] Into the Indian banter's hands;

[019-193] Glooskap who strewed the shining sands

[019-194] Of the tide-swept beach of the stormy bay

[019-195] With amethysts purple and agates gray,

[019-196] And brought to each newly wedded pair

[019-197] The Great Spirit's benediction fair.

[019-198] But the white man came, and with ruthless hand

[019-199] Cleared the forests and sowed the land,

[019-200] And drove from their haunts by the sunny shore

[019-201] Micmac and moose, forevermore.

[019-202] And Glooskap, saddened and sore distressed,

[019-203] Took his way to the unknown West,

[019-204] And the Micmac kindled his wigwam fire

[019-205] Far from the grave of his child and his sire;

[019-206] Where now, as he weaves his basket gay,

[019-207] And paddles his birch canoe away,

[019-208] He dreams of the happy time for men

[019-209] When Glooskap shall come to his tribe again.
