# How Glooskap is Making Arrows for a Great Battle

## Overview

The Twilight of the Indian Gods is among the most striking eschatological traditions in the Algonquin legendary corpus. Glooskap dwells in a vast wigwam in a far land, ceaselessly fashioning arrows. When the lodge is filled, he will return for a final war that ends the world. The tradition parallels the Norse Ragnarök.

## Narrative

When asked whether Glooskap yet lives, the Passamaquoddy answer that he dwells far away. No one knows where. Some say he sailed away in his stone canoe beyond the sea to the east, and will return in it one day. Others say he went to the west (018-158).

Glooskap inhabits a very great and very long wigwam. Within it, he is always making arrows. One side of the lodge is already full of them, so thick that one could not pass between. He permits no one to enter while he is at this work (018-165).

When the wigwam is entirely filled with arrows, he will come forth and make war. He will make war on all, and kill all. There will be no more world — the world will all be gone (018-168).

> "He always making arrows. One side of the lodge is full of arrows now. They so thick as that. When it is all quite full, he will come forth and make war." (018-165)

An old Passamaquoddy woman, pressed for more, offered a syncretic account. Some say the world will all burn up, the water will boil with fire, and good ones will be taken up into good heavens. She qualified this: "Me just *hear* that. Only hear so" (019-025).

## Significance

Leland recognized this tradition as exceptionally significant. The preparing for a great final battle is more suggestive of Norse influence than aught else. It resembles the Edda and Ragnarök, yet is not of late or Christian origin (019-015).

The Norsemen expected a final fight in which arrows would fly like hail. Glooskap is deliberately preparing for just such a conflict (019-035). His magic arrows are worldwide in distribution — Leland compares them to Örvar-Oddr's Saga, where they always hit their mark and returned. The shooting of arrows into the ash-tree was the very means by which Glooskap created humankind (004-170).

## Variants

The tradition exists in multiple versions. Tomah Josephs provided one account to Leland; Mrs. W. Wallace Brown collected another from Mrs. Le Cool. In some tellings the arrows fill only one side of the wigwam. The destination of Glooskap varies — some place him beyond the eastern sea, others to the far west. What remains constant is the certainty of his return and the finality of the battle.
