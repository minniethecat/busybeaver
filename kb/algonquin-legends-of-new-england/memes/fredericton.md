## Overview

**Fredericton**, the capital city of the province of New Brunswick, Canada, appears in the Algonquin legends not as a mythological setting but as the residence of one of Charles Godfrey Leland's most important informants: Mr. **Edward Jack**. Jack contributed several significant Mi'kmaq legends to the collection, serving as a crucial bridge between the oral traditions of the Wabanaki peoples and their written preservation.

## Geography

Fredericton is located on the banks of the **[[st-john|St. John River]]** (Wolastoq) in central New Brunswick, within the traditional territory of the Maliseet (Wolastoqiyik) people. The city sits approximately 135 kilometers inland from the Bay of Fundy, along a stretch of the river that has been inhabited for thousands of years.

## Role in Legends

### Edward Jack, Informant

Edward Jack of Fredericton is credited with providing Leland "several Micmac legends" including the story of **[[tumilkoontaoo|Tumilkoontaoo, or the Broken Wing]]** [001-131] [016-024]. Jack's contributions are noted alongside those of Louis Mitchell, the Indian member of the provincial legislature who provided the Passamaquoddy manuscript collection [001-102].

### The Broken Wing

The legend of Tumilkoontaoo—"the Broken Wing"—was sent to Leland by Edward Jack from Fredericton. This tale, from the Mi'kmaq tradition, involves supernatural transformation and has parallels to other Algonquin stories of broken or injured beings who possess magical powers [016-024]. Jack's written account of this legend was incorporated directly into Leland's collection.

### Personal Anecdote

Leland also records a personal encounter with Jack in Fredericton, noting: "I said I would bet ten dollars" in the context of a discussion about supernatural beliefs, illustrating the ongoing debate between traditional belief and skepticism that framed the collection of these legends [045-115].

## Historical Context

At the time of Leland's writing in the 1880s, Fredericton was a small but established provincial capital with a population of several thousand. The city was a center for legal and governmental affairs in New Brunswick, and educated residents like Edward Jack served as intermediaries between Indigenous oral traditions and the emerging field of folklore studies. Jack's role as a collector and transcriber of Mi'kmaq legends reflects the complex network of informants—both Indigenous and settler—through which Leland assembled his collection.
