---[025-001] My grandfather said that after this Mrs. Bear kept her eyes open on

[025-002] people in two ways. And it always made us laugh, _that_ did.

[025-003] The Spirit of Mischief in these stories is sometimes Lox, the

[025-004] Wolverine; at others the Raccoon, or the Badger. Their adventures are

[025-005] interchangeable. But the character is always the same, and it is much

[025-006] like that of Loki. Now Loki is Fire; and it may be observed in this

[025-007] legend that the wolverine or raccoon comes to life when thrown into

[025-008] scalding water, and that in another narrative Lox dies for want of

[025-009] fire; in another he is pricked by thorns and stung by ants. "We must,"

[025-010] says C. F. Keary, in his Mythology of the Eddas, "admit that the

[025-011] constant appearance of thorn-hedges, pricking with a sleep-thorn (Lox's

[025-012] thorns are his bed), in German and Norse legends, is a mythical way of

[025-013] expressing the idea of the funeral _fire_."

[025-014] The first thing that the Lox-Raccoon does in this tale, on coming to

[025-015] life, is to upset a pot into the ashes for mischief's sake. And the

[025-016] very first exploit of the magic deer, made by the evil spirits and

[025-017] sorcerers in the Kalevala (Runes XIII.), is thus set forth:--

[025-018] "Then the Husi stag went bounding,

[025-019] Bounding to the land of Pohja,

[025-020] Till he reached the fields of Lapland.

[025-021] Passing there before a cabin (_goatte_),

[025-022] With a single kick while running

[025-023] He upset the boiling kettle,

[025-024] So that all the meat went rolling,

[025-025] Rolling ruined in the ashes,

[025-026] And the soup upon the hearth-stone."

[025-027] This is, in both cases, the very first act of an animal, created and

[025-028] living only for mischief, on coming to a magic or artificial life.

[025-029] The legends of Finland and Lapland are as important as the Norse to

[025-030] explain the origin of our Indian mythology.

[025-031] _How Lox came to Grief by trying to catch a Salmon._

[025-032] (Passamaquoddy.)

[025-033] Kusk, the Crane, had two brothers. One of these was Lox, the Wolverine,

[025-034] or Indian Devil. And his other brother was Koskomines, the Blue Jay.

[025-035] Kusk was very lazy, and one day, being hungry, thought he would go and

[025-036] get a dinner from Lox. Lox served him a kind of pudding-soup in a

[025-037] broad, flat platter. Poor Kusk could hardly get a mouthful, while Lox

[025-038] hipped it all up with ease.

[025-039] Soon after, Kusk made a fine soup, and invited Lox to dinner. This he

[025-040] served up in a jug, a long cylinder. None of it had Lox. Kusk ate it

[025-041] all.

[025-042] The next day the pair went to dine with Blue Jay. Blue Jay said, "Wait

[025-043] till I get our food." Then he ran out on a bough of a tree which spread

[025-044] over a river, and in a minute fished out a large salmon. "Truly,"

[025-045] thought Lox, "that is easy to do, and I can do it."

[025-046] So the next day he invited the Blue Jay and Crane to feed with him.

[025-047] Then he, too, ran down to the river and out on a tree, and, seeing a

[025-048] fine salmon, caught at it with his claws. But he had not learned the

[025-049] art, and so fell into the river, and was swept away by the rushing

[025-050] current.

[025-051] This is one of AEsop's fables Indianized and oddly eked out with a

[025-052] fragment from a myth attributed to both Manobozho and the Wabanaki

[025-053] Rabbit. As the Wolverine has a great resemblance to Loki, it may be

[025-054] here observed that, while he dies in trying to catch a salmon, "Loki,

[025-055] in the likeness of a salmon, cast himself into the waterfall of

[025-056] Franangr." which was effectively his last act in life before being

[025-057] captured by the gods, as told in the Edda. Otter, in the Edda, caught a

[025-058] salmon, and was then caught by Loki. There is, of course, great

[025-059] confusion here, but the Indian tale is a mere fragment, carelessly

[025-060] pieced and indifferently told. Lox is, like Loki, _fire_ and

[025-061] perishes by water.

[025-062] _How Master Lox as a Raccoon killed the Bear and the Black Cats and

[025-063] performed other Notable Feats of Skill, all to his Great Discredit._

[025-064] (Passamaquoddy.)

[025-065] Now of old time there is a tale of Hespuns, the Raccoon, according to

[025-066] the Passamaquoddy Indians, but by another record it is Master Lox, to

[025-067] whom all Indian deviltry truly belongs. And this is the story. One fine

[025-068] morning Master Lox started off as a Raccoon; [Footnote: The same

[025-069] stories are attributed to the Wolverine, Badger, and Raccoon.] for he

[025-070] walked the earth in divers disguises, to take his usual roundabouts,

[025-071] and as he went he saw a huge bear, as the manuscript reads, "right

[025-072] straight ahead of him."

[025-073] Now the old Bear was very glad, to see the Raccoon, for he had made up

[025-074] his mind to kill him at once if he could: firstly, to punish him for

[025-075] his sins; and secondly, to eat him for breakfast. Then the Raccoon ran

[025-076] into a hollow tree, the Bear following, and beginning to root it up.

[025-077] Now the Coon saw that in a few minutes the tree would go and he be

[025-078] gone. But he began to sing as if he did not care a bean, and said, "All

[025-079] the digging and pushing this tree will never catch me. Push your way in

[025-080] backwards, and then I must yield and die. But that you cannot do, since

[025-081] the hole is too small for you." Then Mooin, the Bruin, hearing this,

[025-082] believed it, but saw that he could easily enlarge the hole, which he

[025-083] did, and so put himself in arrear; upon which the Raccoon seized him,

[025-084] and held on till he was slain. [Footnote: As Reynard, the Fox, won the

[025-085] victory in the famous tale versified by Goethe. Vide _Reinecke

[025-086] Fuchs_.]

[025-087] Then he crawled out of the tree, and, having made himself a fine pair

[025-088] Of mittens out of the Bear's skin, started off again, and soon saw a

[025-089] wigwam from which rose a smoke, and, walking in, he found a family of

[025-090] _Begemkessisek_, or Black Cats. So, greeting them, he said,

[025-091] "Young folks, comb me down and make me nice, and I will give you these

[025-092] beautiful bear-skin mittens." So the little Black Cats combed him down,

[025-093] and parted his hair, and brushed his tail, and while they were doing this

[025-094] he fell asleep; and they, being very hungry, took the fresh bear-skin

[025-095] mitts, and scraped them all up, and cooked and ate them. Then the Coon,

[025-096] waking up, looked very angry at them, and said in an awful voice,

[025-097] "Where are my bear-skin mitts?" And they, in great fear, replied,

[025-098] "Please, sir, we cooked and ate them." Then the Coon flew at them and

[025-099] strangled them every one, all except the youngest, who, since he could

[025-100] not speak as yet, the Raccoon, or Lox, thought could not tell of him.

[025-101] Then, for a great joke, he took all the little dead creatures and set

[025-102] them up by the road-side in a row; as it was a cold day they all froze

[025-103] stiff, and then he put a stick across their jaws, so that the little

[025-104] Black Cats looked as if they were laughing for joy. Then he made off at

[025-105] full speed.

[025-106] Soon the father, the old Black Cat, came home, and, seeing his children

[025-107] all grinning at him, he said, "How glad the dear little things are to

[025-108] see me." But as none moved he saw that something was wrong, and his joy

[025-109] soon changed to sorrow. [Footnote: This trick is so precisely in the

[025-110] style of Lox that it seems a gross mistake to attribute it to the

[025-111] Raccoon. Those who have seen a wild cat grin will appreciate the humor

[025-112] of Lox on this occasion.]

[025-113] Then the youngest Black Cat, the baby, came out of some hole where he

[025-114] had hid himself. Now the baby was too young to speak, but he was very

[025-115] clever, and, picking up a piece of charcoal, he made a mark from the

[025-116] end of his mouth around his cheek. [Footnote: The reader cannot fail to

[025-117] recall the peculiar mustache of the Raccoon so well indicated by the

[025-118] infant artist.] Then the father cried, "Ah, _now_ I know who it

[025-119] was,--the Raccoon, as sure as I live!" And he started after him in hot

[025-120] pursuit.

[025-121] Soon the Raccoon saw the fierce Black Cat, as an Indian, coming after

[025-122] him with a club. And, looking at him, he said, "No club can kill me;

[025-123] nothing but a bulrush or cat-tail can take my life." Then the Black

[025-124] Cat, who knew where to get one, galloped off to a swamp, and, having

[025-125] got a large cat-tail, came to the Coon and hit him hard with it. It

[025-126] burst and spread all over the Raccoon's head, and, being wet, the fuzz

[025-127] stuck to him. And the Black Cat, thinking it was the Coon's brains and

[025-128] all out, went his way.

[025-129] The Raccoon lay quite still till his foe was gone, and then went on his

[025-130] travels. Now he was a great magician, though little to other folks'

[025-131] good. And he came to a place where there were many women nursing their

[025-132] babes, and said, "This is but a slow way you have of raising children."

[025-133] To which the good women replied, "How else should we raise them?" Then

[025-134] he answered, "I will show you how we do in our country. When we want

[025-135] them to grow fast, we dip them into cold water over night. Just lend me

[025-136] one, and I will show you how to raise them in a hurry." They gave him

[025-137] one: he took it to the river, and, cutting a hole in the ice, put the

[025-138] child into it. The next morning he went to the place, and took out a

[025-139] full-grown man, alive and well. The women were indeed astonished at

[025-140] this. All hastened to put their babes that night under the ice, and

[025-141] then the Raccoon rushed away. So they all died.

[025-142] Then he came to another camp, where many women with fine stuff and furs

[025-143] were making bags. "That is a very slow way you have of working," he

[025-144] said to the goodwives. "In our country we cook them under the ashes.

[025-145] Let me see the stuff and show you how!" They gave him a piece: he put

[025-146] it under the hot coals and ashes, and in a few minutes drew out from

[025-147] them a beautiful bag. Then they all hurried to put their cloth under

[025-148] the fire. Just then he left in haste. And when they drew the stuff out

[025-149] it was scorched or burned, and all spoiled.

[025-150] Then he came to a great river, and did not know how to get across. He

[025-151] saw on the bank an old _Wiwillmekq'_, a strange worm which is like

[025-152] a horned, alligator; but he was blind. "Grandfather," said the Raccoon,

[025-153] "carry me over the lake." "Yes, my grandson," said the Wiwillmekq', and

[025-154] away he swam; the Ravens and Crows above began to ridicule them. "What

[025-155] are those birds saying?" inquired the Old One. "Oh, they are crying to

[025-156] you to hurry, hurry, for your life, with that Raccoon!" So the

[025-157] Wiwillmekq', not seeing land ahead, hurried with such speed that the

[025-158] Raccoon made him run his head and half his body into the bank, and then

[025-159] jumped off and left him. But whether the Wiwillmekq' ever got out again

[025-160] is more than he ever troubled himself to know.

[025-161] So he went on till he came to some Black Berries, and said, "Berries,

[025-162] how would you agree with me if I should eat you?" "Badly indeed, Master

[025-163] Coon," they replied, "for we are Choke-berries." "Choke-berries,

[025-164] indeed! Then I will have none of you." And then further he found on

[025-165] some bushes, Rice-berries. "Berries," he cried, "how would you agree

[025-166] with me if I should eat you?" "We should make you itch, for we are

[025-167] Itch-berries." "Ah, that is what I like," he replied, and so ate his

[025-168] fill. Then as he went on he felt very uneasy: he seemed to be tormented

[025-169] with prickles, he scratched and scratched, but it did not help or cure.

[025-170] So he rubbed himself on a ragged rock; he slid up and down it till the

[025-171] hair came off.
