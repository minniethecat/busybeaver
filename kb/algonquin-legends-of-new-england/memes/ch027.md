---[027-001] The sorcerer's passing himself off for a woman and the trick of the

[027-002] moose abortion occurs in three tales, but it is most completely given

[027-003] in this. To this point the narrative follows the Micmac, Passamaquoddy,

[027-004] and Chippewa versions. After the tale of the chief is at an end it is

[027-005] entirely Passamaquoddy; but of the latter I have two versions, one from

[027-006] Tomah Josephs and one from Mrs. W. Wallace Brown.

[027-007] I can see no sense in the account of the bear's oil hardened by ice,

[027-008] but that oil is an essential part of the duck story appears from the

[027-009] Chippewa legend (Hiawatha L. p. 30). In the latter it is represented as

[027-010] giving size to those who partake of it.

[027-011] _The Mischief Maker. A Tradition of the Origin of the Mythology of

[027-012] the Senecas. A Lox Legend._

[027-013] (Seneca.)

[027-014] An Indian mischief maker was once roving about. He saw that he was

[027-015] approaching a village, and said, "How can I attract attention?"

[027-016] Seeing two girls coming from the wigwams, he pulled up a wild plum-bush

[027-017] and placed it upon his head, the roots clasping about his chin.

[027-018] It will be strange to see a plum-tree on my head, bearing ripe fruit.

[027-019] These girls will want trees also. So he thought. The tree shook as he

[027-020] walked, and many plums fell to the ground.

[027-021] The girls wondered greatly at the strange man with the tree. They

[027-022] admired it, and said they, too, would like to be always supplied with

[027-023] fruit in such a manner.

[027-024] "I can manage that," he replied. So he pulled up a bush for each, and

[027-025] planted them on their heads. The plums were delicious, and grew as fast

[027-026] as they were plucked; and the girls stepped along proudly, for they had

[027-027] something which certainly no girls ever had before.

[027-028] The Mischief Maker went on to the village. On the way he reflected,

[027-029] "There is no such thing in the world as a plum-tree growing on a man's

[027-030] head. I will take this off." He did so, and, on entering the village,

[027-031] gave a loud signal (a whoop). All the people listened, and the chiefs

[027-032] sent messengers to inquire what news he brought.

[027-033] He said, "I have seen a very strange sight. As I was coming hither I

[027-034] saw two girls walking. Trees grew on their heads; the boughs were

[027-035] covered with plums, and the roots, which came through their hair, were

[027-036] fastened about their necks. They were beautiful, and seemed to be very

[027-037] happy."

[027-038] "We will go and see them!" cried the women.

[027-039] They had not gone far before they saw one of the girls lying on the

[027-040] ground, while the other pulled at the tree on her head. The roots gave

[027-041] way and the tree came out, but all the hair came with it also. Then the

[027-042] other lay down, and her friend in turn pulled the tree from her head.

[027-043] They were very angry, and said, "If we meet with the man who played us

[027-044] this trick we will punish him."

[027-045] When the women who had gathered round them learned how the trees had

[027-046] been fastened by magic upon the girls' heads, they returned to the

[027-047] village, resolved to chastise the man who had played the trick. But

[027-048] when they reached home he was gone.

[027-049] Gone far and away to another town. Before reaching it he sat down, and

[027-050] said, "Now I will show these people also what I can do." He went a

[027-051] little distance into the woods, where he found a wigwam. A woman with a

[027-052] bucket in her hand came from it. He saw that as she passed along she

[027-053] reached high with one hand, and felt her way by a thong which ran from

[027-054] tree to tree till it ended at a spring of cold water. She went on,

[027-055] filled her bucket, and so returned. Then another woman after her did

[027-056] the same.

[027-057] "They must be blind," said the Mischief Maker. "I will have some fun

[027-058] with them." And so it was. There lived in that wigwam five blind

[027-059] sisters.

[027-060] Then he untied the thong from the tree near the spring and fastened it

[027-061] to another, where there was no water. Then a third blind woman came

[027-062] with a bucket, and followed the line to the end, but found no water.

[027-063] She returned to the wigwam, and said, "The spring is dried up."

[027-064] "No, it isn't," replied one of the sisters, who was stirring pudding

[027-065] over the fire. "You say that because you are too lazy to bring water;

[027-066] you never work. Here, do you stir the pudding, and let me go for

[027-067] water."

[027-068] The Mischief Maker heard all this, and made haste to tie the end of the

[027-069] thong where it belonged. The blind woman filled her bucket, and when

[027-070] she returned said to her sister, "There, you lazy creature, I found the

[027-071] water!"

[027-072] By this time the Mischief Maker was in the house, and slipping quietly

[027-073] up to the fire he dipped out some of the pudding and threw it, scalding

[027-074] hot, into the face of the scolding woman, who cried in a rage,--

[027-075] "You throw hot pudding at me, do you?"

[027-076] "No, I did not throw any at you," replied the sister.

[027-077] Then the Mischief Maker threw some into _her_ face. She screamed,

[027-078] being very angry.

[027-079] "You mean thing! You threw hot pudding at me, when I did you no harm."

[027-080] "I didn't throw any!" said the other, in a rage.

[027-081] "Yes, you _did_, you mean thing!"

[027-082] "Stop! stop!" cried the others. Just then hot pudding flew in all their

[027-083] faces; they had a terrible quarrel, and the Mischief Maker left them to

[027-084] settle it among themselves as they could.

[027-085] He entered the village near by, and gave the usual signal for news. The

[027-086] runners came out and met him; the chiefs and all the people assembled,

[027-087] lining the path on both sides for a long way. They asked, "What news do

[027-088] you bring?"

[027-089] He replied, "I come from at village where there is great distress. A

[027-090] pestilence visited the people. The medicine man could not cure the

[027-091] sick; till I came there was no remedy; the tribe was becoming very

[027-092] small. But I told them the remedy, and now they are getting well. I

[027-093] have come to tell you to prepare for the pestilence: it will soon be

[027-094] here; it is flying like the wind, and there is only one remedy."

[027-095] "What is it? what is it? what is it?" interrupted the people.

[027-096] He answered, "Every man must embrace the woman who is next to him at

[027-097] this very instant; kiss her, quick, immediately!"

[027-098] They all did so on the spot, he with the rest.

[027-099] As he was leaving them an elderly man came to him and whispered, "Are

[027-100] you going to do this thing again at the next village? If you are I

[027-101] should like to be on hand. I didn't get any girl myself here. The woman

[027-102] I went for dodged me, and said she had rather have the pestilence, and

[027-103] death too, than have me kiss her. Is the operation to be repeated?"

[027-104] The Mischief Maker said that it certainly would be, about the middle of

[027-105] the morrow forenoon.

[027-106] "Then I will start now," said the middle-aged man, "for I am lame, and

[027-107] it will take me all night to get there."

[027-108] So he hurried on, and at daylight entered the village. He found a

[027-109] wigwam, by which several beautiful Indian girls were pounding corn in a

[027-110] great wooden mortar. He sat down by them. He could hardly take his eyes

[027-111] from them, they were so charming, and they wondered at his strange

[027-112] behavior.

[027-113] He talked with them, and said, "My eyelids quiver, and by that I know

[027-114] that some great and strange news will soon be brought to this tribe.

[027-115] Hark!"--here he moved up towards the one whom he most admired,--"did

[027-116] you not hear a signal?"

[027-117] "No," they replied.

[027-118] The middle-aged man became very uneasy. Suddenly the girls gave a cry,

[027-119] and dropped their corn pestles. A voice was heard afar; the runners

[027-120] leaped and flew, the chiefs and people went forth. With them went the

[027-121] girls and the middle-aged man, who took great pains to keep very near

[027-122] his chosen one, so as to lose no time in applying the remedy for the

[027-123] pestilence when the Mischief Maker should give the signal. He was

[027-124] determined that a life should not be lost if he could prevent it.

[027-125] The Stranger went through his story as at the other village. The people

[027-126] became very much excited. They cried, out to know the remedy, and the

[027-127] old bachelor drew nearer to the pretty girl.

[027-128] "The only remedy for the pestilence is for every woman _to knock down

[027-129] the man who is nearest her_."

[027-130] The women began to knock down, and the first to fall was the too

[027-131] familiar old bachelor. So the Mischief Maker waited no longer than to

[027-132] see the whole town in one general and bitter fight, tooth and nail,

[027-133] tomahawk and scalper, and then ran at the top of his speed far away and

[027-134] fleet, to find another village. Then the people, finding they had been

[027-135] tricked, said, as people generally do on such occasions, "If we had

[027-136] that fellow here, wouldn't we pay him up for this?"

[027-137] The Mischief Maker was greatly pleased at his success. It was nearly

[027-138] dark when he stopped, and said, "I will not enter the next village

[027-139] to-night; I will camp here in the woods." So he had piled up logs for a

[027-140] fire, and was just about to strike a light, when he saw a stranger

[027-141] approaching. "Camp with me here over night," said the Mischief Maker,

[027-142] "and we will go to the village in the morning."

[027-143] So they ate and smoked their pipes, and told stories till it was very

[027-144] late. But the stranger did not seem to tire; nay, he even proposed to

[027-145] tell stories all night long. The Mischief Maker looked at him aslant.

[027-146] "My friend," he said, "can you tell me of what wood my back-log is?"

[027-147] "Hickory?" inquired the stranger.

[027-148] "No, not hickory."

[027-149] "Maple?"

[027-150] "No, not maple."

[027-151] "White oak?"

[027-152] "No, not white oak."

[027-153] "Black walnut?"

[027-154] "No, not black walnut."

[027-155] "Moosewood?"

[027-156] "No, not moosewood."

[027-157] "Ash?"

[027-158] "No, not ash."

[027-159] "Pine?"

[027-160] "No, not pine."

[027-161] "Cedar?"

[027-162] "No, not cedar."

[027-163] "Birch?"

[027-164] The stranger began to yawn, but he kept on guessing. Then his head

[027-165] nodded. By the time he had found out that it was slippery elm he was

[027-166] sound asleep.

[027-167] "This fellow deserves punishment," remarked the Mischief Maker. "He is

[027-168] an enemy to mankind." Here he adroitly put some sticky clay on the

[027-169] sleeper's eyes, and departed. When the stranger awoke he thought

[027-170] himself still fast asleep in darkness, and then that he was blind.

[027-171] "If ever I meet with that fellow again," he said, "I'll punish him!"

[027-172] The Mischief Maker played so many pranks that all the tribes sent out

[027-173] runners to catch him. He heard their whoops in every forest. He knew

[027-174] that he was being hunted down. He hurried on, and once at night hid in

[027-175] a cave under a rock. The runners did not quite overtake him, but they

[027-176] saw that his tracks were fresh, and thought they might catch him in the

[027-177] morning. In the morning he was up and far away long before they awoke.

[027-178] The next night he hid again in a hollow log. In the middle of the

[027-179] afternoon of the next day he heard the whoops of the pursuers very

[027-180] near, and knew that they were gaining fast on him. He climbed a thickly

[027-181] limbed tree, and hid in the top. Here the runners lost his track,

[027-182] because he had broken the weeds and bushes down beyond the tree, as if

[027-183] he had gone further on. They ran for a long distance. Then they

[027-184] returned, and camped and built a fire under the tree.

[027-185] The smoke crept up among the branches and curled above, and rose in a

[027-186] straight column to the sky. The fugitive sailed away on the smoke,

[027-187] going up and up,--past beautiful lakes and hunting-grounds stocked with

[027-188] deer, large fields of corn and beans, tobacco and squashes; past great

[027-189] companies of handsome Indians, whose wigwams were hung full of dried

[027-190] venison and bear's meat. And so he went on and up to the wig-wam of the

[027-191] Great Chief.
