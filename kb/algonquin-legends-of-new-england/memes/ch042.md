---[042-001] They went their way back to the chieftain's town, and found him married

[042-002] to the servant-girl. The mother only spoke, and the wild boy tore down

[042-003] the wigwam of the Indian chief just with a blow, and then he called

[042-004] aloud unto the Lightning in the sky above, "Come down to me and help me

[042-005] in my need! Build a grand wigwam such as man ne'er saw! Build it, I

[042-006] say, and for my mother here!"

[042-007] The Lightning came, and with a single flash built such a home as man

[042-008] had never seen.

[042-009] And then he said, "Mother, I mean to go and travel everywhere, until I

[042-010] find another man who is as strong as I. When he is found I will return

[042-011] to thee."

[042-012] So on he went afar until he saw a man who lifted up a vast canoe with

[042-013] many people in it. This he did, raising it in the water; but the boy

[042-014] bore it ashore, and lifted it on land.

[042-015] And so the two agreed that they would go on together until they found a

[042-016] third equal to them in strength, if such a man were living anywhere in

[042-017] all the world.

[042-018] So traveling by hill and lake, they went, until one day, far in a

[042-019] lonely land, they saw a man rolling a mighty rock, large as the largest

[042-020] wigwam, up a hill. But the Bear's son, lifting the stone with ease,

[042-021] threw it afar over the mountain-top,--threw it afar beyond the rocky

[042-022] range; they heard it thunder down the depths below.

[042-023] Then the three strong men went to hunt the moose. He who had tossed the

[042-024] ship remained in camp to do the cooking, while the others went with bow

[042-025] and spear afar to find their game.

[042-026] Now when the sun was at the edge of noon, just balancing to fall, there

[042-027] came a boy, a little wretched, elfish-looking child, as sad and sickly

[042-028] as a boy could be, who asked the man for food. He answered him, "Poor

[042-029] little fellow! there, the pot is full of venison, so go and eat your

[042-030] fill."

[042-031] He ate, indeed, the dinner for the three. When he had done he did not

[042-032] leave a scrap; then walked into the stony mountain-side, as any man

[042-033] might walk into the fog, and in a second he was seen no more.

[042-034] Now when the two returned and heard the tale they were right angry,

[042-035] being hungry men. The man; who rolled the stone stayed next in turn,

[042-036] but when the I little fellow came to him he seemed so famished and he

[042-037] shed such tears that this one also gave him leave to eat. Then, in a

[042-038] single swallow, as it seemed, he bolted all the food, and yelled aloud

[042-039] with an insulting laugh. The man, enraged, grappled him by the throat,

[042-040] but the strange boy flung him away as one would throw a not, and

[042-041] vanished in the mountain as before.

[042-042] On the third day the mighty man himself remained at home, and soon the

[042-043] starveling child came and began to beg, with tears, for food. "Eat,"

[042-044] said the chief, "as other people eat, and no more tricks, or I will

[042-045] deal with you." But as it was with him the day before, so it went now;

[042-046] he swallowed all the meat with the same jeering yell Then the strong

[042-047] man closed with the boy. It was an awful strife; they fought together

[042-048] from the early morn until the sun went down, and then the Elf--for elf

[042-049] he was--cried out, "I now give in!" So both his arms were tightly bound

[042-050] behind, and with a long, tough cord of plaited hide the strong man kept

[042-051] his prey, the lariat fast noosed about his neck. The child went on, the

[042-052] strong man ever following behind, holding the cord well twisted round

[042-053] his hand.

[042-054] And so they went into the mountain-side, and ever on, a long and

[042-055] winding way, down a deep cavern, on for many a mile,--the light of

[042-056] sorcery shining from the elf made it all clear,--until at last the

[042-057] guide stopped in ins course, and said:--

[042-058] "Now list to me. I am the servant of a frightful fiend, a seven-beaded

[042-059] devil, whom I deemed no man could ever conquer, he and I being of equal

[042-060] strength; but I believe that thou mayst conquer him, since I have

[042-061] found, by bitter proof, that thou canst conquer me. Here is a staff,

[042-062] the only thing on earth that man may smite him with and give him pain.

[042-063] Now, do your best; it is all one to me which of you gains, so one of

[042-064] you be slain, for well I wot 't will be a roaring fight."

[042-065] In came the evil being with a scream, and clutched the Indian with

[042-066] teeth and claws. There, in the magic cavern, many a mile from the sun's

[042-067] rays, they fought for seven days, the stubborn devil and the stubborn

[042-068] man, whose savage temper gave him fresher strength with every fresh

[042-069] wound; the more his blood ran from his body all the more his heart grew

[042-070] harder with the love of fight, until he beat away the monster's seven

[042-071] heads. And so he slew him, and the watching elf burst into laughter at

[042-072] the victory.

[042-073] "Now," said the Elf, "I have a gift for thee. I have three sisters: all

[042-074] are beautiful, and all shall be thine own if thou wilt but unbind my

[042-075] hands." The strong man set him free. And so he led the man to another

[042-076] cave, and there he saw three girls so strangely fair they seemed to be

[042-077] a dream. The first, indeed, was very beautiful, and yet as plump as she

[042-078] was lovely; then the second maid was tall, superb, and most

[042-079] magnificent, in rarest furs, with richest wampum bands, the very

[042-080] picture of a perfect bride; bet fairer than them both, as much more

[042-081] fair as swans outrival ducks, the youngest smiled. And the young

[042-082] chieftain chose her for his own.

[042-083] With the three girls he went into the day. Far on the rocks above him

[042-084] he could see his two companions, and a sudden thought came to his mind,

[042-085] for he was quick to think, and so he called, "I say, let down a rope; I

[042-086] have three girls here, and they cannot climb." And so the two strong

[042-087] men let down a cord: then the first fairy-maid went up by it, and then

[042-088] the second. Now the chief cried out, "It is my turn; now you must pull

[042-089] on me!" And saying this, he tied a heavy stone, just his own weight,

[042-090] unto the long rope's end, then bid them haul. It rose, but as it came

[042-091] just to the top the traitors let it fall, as he supposed they would, to

[042-092] murder him.

[042-093] And then the chieftain said unto the elf, "You know the mountain and

[042-094] its winding ways: bear me upon thy back, and that in haste, to where

[042-095] those fellows are!" The goblin flew, and in an instant he was by their

[042-096] side.

[042-097] He found the villains in a deadly fight, quarreling for the maids; but

[042-098] seeing him they ceased to wrestle, upon which he said, "I risked my

[042-099] life to bring away these girls; I would have given each of you a wife:

[042-100] for doing this you would have murdered me. Now I could kill you, and

[042-101] you both deserve death at the stake, vile serpents that you are; but

[042-102] take your lives,--you are too low for me,--and with them take these

[042-103] women, if they wish to wed with such incarnate brutes as you!"

[042-104] They went their way; the women followed them along the forest paths,

[042-105] and ever on. Into this story they return no more.

[042-106] And then the strong man said to his young bride, "I must return unto my

[042-107] village; then I'll come again to you; await me here." But she, as one

[042-108] to elfin magic born, replied, "I warn you of a single thing. When you

[042-109] again are at your wigwam door a small black dog will leap to lick your

[042-110] hand. Beware, I say; if he succeed in it, you surely will forget me

[042-111] utterly." As she predicted so it came to pass.

[042-112] And so she waited in the lonely wood beside the mountain till a month

[042-113] was gone, and then arose and went to seek her love. All in the early

[042-114] dawn she reached the town, and found the wigwam of the sagamore. She

[042-115] sought a neighboring hiding-place, where she might watch unseen, and

[042-116] found a tree, a broad old ash, which spread its stooping boughs over

[042-117] the surface of a silent pool.

[042-118] An old black Indian had a hut hard by. His daughter, coming, looked

[042-119] into the spring, and saw a lovely face. The simple girl thought it was

[042-120] hers, her own grown beautiful by sorcery which hung about the place.

[042-121] She flung away her pail, and said, "Aha! I'll work no more; some chief

[042-122] shall marry me!" and so she went to smile among the men.

[042-123] Then came the mother, who beheld the same sweet, smiling, also girlish

[042-124] face. She said, "Now I am young and beautiful again; I'll seek another

[042-125] husband, and at once." She threw her pail afar and went away, losing no

[042-126] time to smile among the men.

[042-127] And then in turn the old black Indian came, and looking in the spring

[042-128] beheld the face. He knew right well that it was not his own, for in his

[042-129] youth he never had been fair. So looking up above he saw the bride, and

[042-130] bade her come to him; and then he said, "My wife has gone away; my

[042-131] daughter, too. You were the cause of it; it is but right that you

[042-132] should take the place my wife has left. Therefore remain with me and be

[042-133] my own."

[042-134] He fares but ill who weds unwilling witch. When night came on they laid

[042-135] them down to sleep, and then the bride murmured a magic prayer, begging

[042-136] the awful Spirit of the Wind, the giant Eagle of the wilderness, to do

[042-137] his worst. A fearful tempest blew, and all night long the old black

[042-138] Indian was out-of-doors, working with all his power to keep the lodge

[042-139] from being blown away. As soon as he had pinned one sheet of bark into

[042-140] its place another blew away, and then a tent pole rattling in the rain

[042-141] bounded afar. It was a weary work, but all night long the young bride

[042-142] slept in peace, until the morning came, and then he slept.

[042-143] Then she arose, and, walking to the wood, sat down beside a stream and

[042-144] sang a song:--

[042-145] "There are many men in the world,

[042-146] But only one is dear to me.

[042-147] He is good and brave and strong.

[042-148] He swore to love none but me;

[042-149] He has forgotten me.

[042-150] It was a bad spirit that changed him,

[042-151] But I will love none but him."

[042-152] And as she sat and sang, the sagamore her husband, paddling by in his

[042-153] canoe, heard the sweet song intoned in magic style, [Footnote: Not only

[042-154] the words, but the peculiar intonations of them, were essential to produce

[042-155] the proper effect of a magic song. An intelligent white man has left it

[042-156] on record that it required two years to learn one of these incantations

[042-157] of only a few lines.] and all at once recalled what had been lost,--the

[042-158] two strong giants, the cavern and the elf, the seven-headed monster and

[042-159] the fight, the sisters and the evil-minded men, and the black dog who

[042-160] leaped to lick his hand: it flashed upon him like some early dream brought

[042-161] out by sorcery. He saw her sit beside the stream, and still he heard her

[042-162] song, soft as a magic flute. He went to her, and in a minute he was won

[042-163] again.

[042-164] And then she said, "This world is ever false. I know another, let us go

[042-165] to it." So then again she sang a magic spell, and as she sang they saw

[042-166] the great Culloo, the giant bird, broad as a thunder cloud, winging his

[042-167] way towards them. Then he came; they stepped upon him, and he soared

[042-168] away. But to this earth they never came again.
