**Lusifee** — the Loup-Cervier, or Wild Cat (Canada Lynx, _Lynx canadensis_) — is the natural predator of Master Rabbit (_Mahtigwess_ / _Ableegumooch_) in Micmac mythology. He appears in the narrative cycle "The Amazing Adventures of Master Rabbit," in which his relentless, single-minded pursuit of the Rabbit forms a comic counterpoint to the prey's magical cleverness. Leland describes him as "a kind of wild cat, none being more obstinate" (029-099).

## Overview

Lusifee occupies the role of the determined but ultimately outwitted predator in the Master Rabbit cycle. The ecological relationship between the Canada Lynx and the Snowshoe Hare — one of the most tightly coupled predator-prey dynamics in the North American boreal forest — is transformed in Micmac narrative into a contest of wits in which the physically weaker but magically gifted Rabbit repeatedly escapes and humiliates his pursuer.

The Lusifee stories belong to the trickster-prey tradition found across Algonquin oral literature, in which small, clever animals (Rabbit, Marten, Squirrel) defeat larger, stronger adversaries through magical knowledge and quick thinking. Lusifee's defining trait — his obstinacy — makes him at once formidable (he will never stop hunting) and vulnerable (he can be led into traps by his own fixation).

## Identity and Attributes

### Species

The name _Lusifee_ is the Micmac term for the Loup-Cervier — the Canada Lynx. Leland glosses it as "a kind of wild cat" (029-099). The French term _Loup-Cervier_ (literally "deer-wolf") was the common colonial name for the lynx, reflecting its size and predatory nature.

### Character

Lusifee is defined by a single overwhelming trait: **obstinacy**. He is "none being more obstinate" (029-099), swearing "by his tail" that he will catch Rabbit "if he had to hunt forever and run himself to death" (029-099). This monomania makes him a dangerous predator — unlike the easily discouraged Wolves who abandon the hunt — but also the perfect foil for a trickster who understands that the most determined pursuer is the easiest to deceive.

His secondary trait is **vanity**: as leader of the wolf pack, he "had made them great promises and acted as chief" (029-099), and his hunt for Rabbit is as much about salvaging his wounded pride as satisfying his hunger.

## Role in Legends

### The Hunt for Master Rabbit

Lusifee's primary narrative is the famous chase in which he is fooled by Master Rabbit — "a full account of the famous chase, in which he fooled Lusifee, the Wild Cat" (029-099).

The story begins with Lusifee leading a gang of Wolves on a hunt. Having made "great promises and acted as chief," he is humiliated when they find nothing. To salvage his authority, he promises them Rabbit, and leads them to Rabbit's wigwam — but Rabbit is out. The Wolves, "vexed and starved," revile Lusifee and abandon him (029-099).

Undeterred, Lusifee swears to hunt Rabbit alone. But Rabbit, who "is _m'teoulin_" — possessed of magical power — deploys a charm of evasion:

> "He picked up a handful of chips, and threw one as far as possible, then jumped to it — for he had a charm for a long jump; and then threw another, and so on, for a great distance. This was to make no tracks" (029-099).

This technique — throwing chips and jumping to them to leave no scent trail — is a classic Algonquin magical evasion. Lusifee, employing the hunter's spiral-search method (described by the Passamaquoddy storyteller Tomah Josephs as an expanding volute), eventually finds the track and pursues in "hot haste."

The chase continues through a series of magical transformations and deceptions — Rabbit disguising himself as a medicine man, Rabbit standing on a spruce twig, Rabbit appearing as a great horned creature on a ship — each time evading the single-minded Wild Cat through superior cleverness and magical power.

### The Death of Lusifee

The chase culminates in Lusifee's destruction. When Rabbit, disguised, draws his _timheyen_ (tomahawk) and strikes, the Wild Cat's scalp-lock is "strangely divided like two horns" — the distinctive ear-tufts of the Canada Lynx explained through narrative etiology (029-099).

## Key Stories

### How Master Rabbit Fooled Lusifee the Wild Cat

> **Source**: Micmac, collected by Leland (029-099 through 030-043)

Lusifee leads a wolf pack on a hunt, promising them success, but finds nothing. To redeem himself, he takes them to Rabbit's wigwam — empty. The Wolves abandon him, and Lusifee swears to catch Rabbit alone. But Rabbit uses magical chip-throwing (long-jumping without leaving tracks) to evade the Wild Cat's spiral-search. What follows is an extended chase in which Rabbit deploys multiple magical disguises to humiliate his pursuer, culminating in Lusifee's defeat.

## Cultural Significance

Lusifee exemplifies a narrative pattern found throughout Algonquin animal tales: the powerful but cognitively limited predator who is undone by the cleverness of his prey. This pattern serves both as entertainment and as encoded ecological knowledge — the real Canada Lynx's hunting success depends almost entirely on snowshoe hare populations, and the lynx's famous "obstinacy" (its extreme dietary specialization) makes it vulnerable to hare population cycles in ways that more adaptable predators are not.

The story also models a cultural value: persistence without intelligence is self-defeating. Lusifee's sworn oath — to hunt "forever" if necessary — is made ridiculous by Rabbit's effortless magical evasions. The Wild Cat's single-mindedness, which initially seems a strength, becomes the very mechanism of his humiliation.

In the broader mythological context, Lusifee belongs to the class of adversaries — alongside the [[Chenoo]], the [[Kookwess]], and the cannibal sorcerers — whom the heroes of Algonquin legend must overcome. But unlike those cosmic-scale monsters, Lusifee operates at the level of the animal fable, his defeat comic rather than heroic, his role that of the buffoon rather than the demon.
