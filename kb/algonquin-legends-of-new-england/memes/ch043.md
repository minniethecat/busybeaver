---[043-001] This very singular legend was obtained for me by Mrs. W. Wallace Brown.

[043-002] It is from the Micmac, and is in the original from beginning to end a

[043-003] song, or poem. For this reason I have given it a plain metrical form,

[043-004] neither prose nor poetry, such being quite the character of the

[043-005] original. But I, have not introduced anything not in the original.

[043-006] This story consists of a very old Indian legend mingled with a European

[043-007] fairy tale drawn through a French-Canadian source. The incident of the

[043-008] Elf who eats the food of three men is to be found in another tale. In

[043-009] one version, the bride, finding that her husband, though utterly

[043-010] deprived by magic of his memory, has married again, sails away on the

[043-011] great bird, leaving him forever. I have naturally rejected this

[043-012] senseless termination in favor of one found in another form.

[043-013] The calling on the Lightning to build a wigwam is probably a mistake.

[043-014] It is more likely that it was summoned to destroy the chiefs wigwam,

[043-015] but the narrator, confused with the subject of the hero's strength,

[043-016] changed the original. The invocations of Lightning, and subsequently of

[043-017] the Storm Bird are probably entirely Indian, though there are Norse

[043-018] invocations to Hroesvelgar, or the Eagle of the Northwest, as we read

[043-019] in Scott's Pirate.

[043-020] The black whelp or small black dog is in this tale ominous of evil. It

[043-021] causes oblivion. In the Edda to dream of the same thing is the most

[043-022] evil of all Atli's bad dreams (vide the second lay of Gudrun, 41):--

[043-023] "Seemed to me from my hand

[043-024] Whelps I let slip.

[043-025] Lacking cause of joy;"

[043-026] and in the very same song (24) be takes a potion which causes oblivion.

[043-027] But there is even a third point in the Atlamal in Groenlenzku, which

[043-028] resembles one in the Indian tale. It is where the half enchantress

[043-029] Kostbera warns Hogni against leaving her:

[043-030] "From home thou art going:

[043-031] Give ear to counsel;

[043-032] Few are fully prudent;

[043-033] Go another time."

[043-034] In the Norse lay we are told that to dream of a white bear indicates a

[043-035] storm, but here it means a strange and terrible event. Long before I

[043-036] met with this, I observed that the introduction, or mention, of a white

[043-037] bear-skin in these Indian stories invariably intimates some strange

[043-038] magical change.

[043-039] But it is most remarkable of all, that, while the poems of the Edda

[043-040] have nothing but a very few incidents in common with the traditions of

[043-041] the western tribes, they are inspired throughout with a strange and

[043-042] mysterious sentiment or _manner_ wonderfully like that of the

[043-043] Wabanaki. As regards literal resemblance the following coincidences may

[043-044] here be noted.

[043-045] In a widely spread Norse tale a very small goblin sustains a long and

[043-046] obstinate contest with an immense white bear.

[043-047] The Norsemen invoked the Eagle Giant of the Winds, as Scott has shown

[043-048] in his song of the Reimkennar. The same being is invoked in this

[043-049] legend.

[043-050] The whelp, as an omen of evil, is mentioned in the Edda. In this tale

[043-051] he causes forgetfulness. A potion of oblivion is also mentioned in the

[043-052] Norse poem in close connection with the omen of the dog.

[043-053] If we accept the termination of this tale as given in the Micmac poem

[043-054] it amounts to this: A certain woman causes the whelp to lick the hero's

[043-055] hand. This causes forgetfulness. The hero marries her, and thereby

[043-056] loses his first wife. In the Edda, Brynhild, who has morally the first

[043-057] claim to Sigurd, says of Crymhild, "She presented to Sigurd the

[043-058] pernicious drink, so that he no more remembers me." In the saga of

[043-059] Thorstein, Viking's son the hero, is made by the witch Dis to utterly

[043-060] forget his bride Hunoor.

[043-061] The Kalmuk tale of How the Schimm-Khan was Slain contains striking

[043-062] analogies to this of the Three Strong Men. [Footnote: _Sagas from He

[043-063] Far East_, London, 1873.] In it the hero associates with three men,

[043-064] who take turns to cook. Their food is devoured, as in this tale, every

[043-065] day by a little old witch who is very strong. He overcomes her by

[043-066] craft. His companions, instead of drawing him up by the rope, as agreed

[043-067] on, leave him to perish, in order to possess themselves of a treasure.

[043-068] There can be no doubt as to the Hindoo origin of this and many more

[043-069] plots found among the red Indians. But a careful study of the Norse

[043-070] story convinces me that the tale did not come to the Wabanaki through

[043-071] any other than a Norse source.

[043-072] Since writing out the foregoing poem, with the comment, I have received

[043-073] from Louis Mitchell the Penobscot version of it. It is about twice as

[043-074] long as the Micmac story, and differs from it very materially. In it

[043-075] the hero conquers the goblin by getting possession of his red cap. In

[043-076] the Norse tales the same incident occurs in different forms. He then

[043-077] fights with a copper demon; also with one of silver and another of

[043-078] gold. Each devil, while he is sharpening his sword, exclaims, "Hurry!

[043-079] hurry! I am hungry!" The last of the three, the _Kche mitche-hant_,

[043-080] or great devil, has three heads, which replace themselves when cut off;

[043-081] but the hero summons a lion (_pee'tahlo_) and an eagle, who devour

[043-082] each a head, when the demon, to save the last, surrenders. There are old

[043-083] "aboriginal" incidents in this Passamaquoddy tale, but the European

[043-084] elements predominate to such an extent as to call for the following

[043-085] remark from the Indian writer:--

[043-086] "This story is ended. When Indians in it, as they do in many others,

[043-087] speak of kings and queens or ships and ivory, I think they got it all

[043-088] from Europe. But perhaps when the Indians came here from Asia they

[043-089] brought these stories with them. Thus they very often mention ivory,

[043-090] calling it white bone. They also mention cities. But these things are

[043-091] not new, for they were handed down from one generation to another."

[043-092] I have to add that, while the story agrees with an universally spread

[043-093] Aryan fairy tale, it is very remarkable that it should add to these,

[043-094] several strictly Eddaic details, such as the white bear.

[043-095] THE WEEWILLMEKQ'.

[043-096] _I. How a Woman Lost a Gun for Fear of the Weewillmekq'_.

[043-097] There was a man and his wife who had got together all they had for the

[043-098] fall hunt. They went up the St. John's River; they left the village of

[043-099] Foxerbica; they went twenty-five miles beyond it. They passed the falls

[043-100] on the upper side to get some game. They cooked and ate. They got ready

[043-101] to start again; they launched the canoe. [Footnote: This story and the

[043-102] preceding are taken _word for word_ from the Indian narration. The

[043-103] singular precision of minute details is very characteristic of many of

[043-104] these legends.] They shoved the canoe twenty-five feet from the shore.

[043-105] The woman turned, and upset it. It went like lightning down the rapids.

[043-106] They had hard work to get ashore, and lost their gun, traps, kettle,

[043-107] and everything. They escaped with great trouble; they had trouble to

[043-108] save their canoe.

[043-109] The man was in great grief at the loss of his gun. He sat down and

[043-110] sang:--

[043-111] "Nici sigi psach ke-yin,

[043-112] Dich m'djel mieol wagh nuch'."

[043-113] I am sorry,

[043-114] I am in great trouble.

[043-115] There came two Indians down to the portage where the man and his wife

[043-116] sat. They asked him why he was so sad. He told them all. One of them

[043-117] was a _m'teoulin_. He asked of them, "Could you tell your gun if

[043-118] you saw it?" The woman cried quickly, "I could!" He was not pleased at

[043-119] her forwardness, but put the question again; when she as pertly

[043-120] answered, "Yes," for her husband. He looked sternly at her, and said,

[043-121] "Are you sure?" To which she cried, "Yes, yes!" Then he said, "If you

[043-122] are very bold, and not afraid of anything, you may get it again." And

[043-123] this, too, she took on herself, saying, "Oh, yes, _I'm_ not

[043-124] afraid; _I'll_ get it," making no account of her husband.

[043-125] Then, by the order of the man, she went to a ledge just below the

[043-126] falls, where they are seventy-five feet high. There was a little

[043-127] projecting rock on which she could just sit,--a horrible place. Below

[043-128] it was a dreadful eddy, in which nothing could live. He helped her down

[043-129] to it, and she was in mortal terror, as such glib-tongued women

[043-130] generally are when there is the least danger. Then the man went away.

[043-131] And as she sat there, trembling and half dead with fright, she saw

[043-132] Something come up out of the eddy,--even out of the worst of it. It

[043-133] rose; it was an awful sight,--a kind of monstrous head, with great

[043-134] forked horns and terrible eyes. She was stiff as a stone with fear. The

[043-135] lost gun lay crosswise on the prongs of the horns. It moved slowly on

[043-136] through the eddy, glaring at her. It came nearer and nearer; the gun

[043-137] was within her reach, but she was too frightened to touch it. Then the

[043-138] monster passed by and sank into the water, and was seen no more, nor

[043-139] was the gun.

[043-140] They got her back with trouble from the place where she sat. The

[043-141] _m'teoulin_ was furious with rage at her, that he had taken such

[043-142] pains for nothing. He said, "This serves you right for your impudence

[043-143] and forwardness. Learn your proper place, and never undertake to do

[043-144] what is none of your business." He then condoled with the husband, but

[043-145] said, "If you could give me all you could think of, I could never get

[043-146] your gun again."

[043-147] By this women may learn not to speak too quickly, or propose to do

[043-148] men's duties, "_Hu 'sami n'zama wiuch wee lel n'aga samee n'gamma

[043-149] wiool petin'l._" (P. "Too quick with the tongue, slow with the hands.")

[043-150] [Footnote: Though the Weewillmekq' is a worm inhabiting the forest and

[043-151] found in dry wood, it is certainly identified, or confused, by the

[043-152] Passamaquoddy Indians with the alligator, or some kind of a horrible

[043-153] water-goblin, which appears to have many points in common with the

[043-154] _Chepitchcalm,_ or dragon of the Micmacs. This story was related to

[043-155] me by Tomah Josephs, now Indian governor at Princeton, Maine.

[043-156] Among various notes I find the following:--

[043-157] "The weewillmekq' becomes human at times, even now."

[043-158] "Six years ago," said T. J., "I was in the woods collecting boughs, and

[043-159] I saw a _weewillmekq'_ on a tree. The thunder kept approaching the

[043-160] tree on which it was, and finally struck it. It seemed to me as if the

[043-161] worm had attracted the lightning." (August 26, 1883.)

[043-162] "The Weewillmekq' is a small worm, sometimes two or three inches long.

[043-163] It is seen sometimes in the water as large as a horse. Then it has

[043-164] horns. It is a very horrible-looking little worm."]

[043-165] _II. Muggahmaht'adem, the Dance of Old Age, or the Magic of the

[043-166] Weewillmekq'._ [Footnote: This mysterious being is called

[043-167] _Wee-wil-li-ah-mek_ in Penobscot The correct pronounciation is very

[043-168] nearly _Wee-wil-'l-mekqu'_ for both Penobscot and Passamaquoddy,

[043-169] but this would be a difficult utterence for any one who has never

[043-170] listened to the Algonquin soft gutturals.

[043-171] Mrs. W. Wallace Brown informs me that "the _Weewillmekqu'_ is a

[043-172] snail." This would account for its being thought to inhabit both land

[043-173] and water.]

[043-174] (Passamaquoddy.)

[043-175] Of old times. There lived in a village many Indians. Among them was a

[043-176] handsome young man, very brave, a great hunter. And there was a

[043-177] beautiful girl. What was her name? Mahli-hahn-sqwess, or Kaliwahdazi,--

[043-178] I don't remember which. But she was proud and high-tempered, and, what

[043-179] was worse, a great witch, but nobody knew it. She wanted the young man

[043-180] to marry her, but he was very busy getting ready for the fall and

[043-181] winter hunt, and had no time to attend to such a thing; and told her so

[043-182] very plainly.

[043-183] Yes, he must have been very plain with her, for she was very angry, and

[043-184] said to him, "You may go; but you will never return as you went." She

[043-185] meant that, he would be ill or changed. He gave no heed to her words;

[043-186] he did not care for her nor fear her. But far away in the woods, far in

[043-187] the north, in midwinter, he went raging mad. The witch had struck him,

[043-188] when far away, with her magic.
