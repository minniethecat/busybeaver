---[031-001] Now having dressed the bride, she was so grateful to Rabbit that she

[031-002] danced with him all the night. The old man, seeing this, was so angry

[031-003] at her fickleness that, without saying a word, he walked away, and left

[031-004] her to Mahtigwess, with whom she lived very happily until she ran away

[031-005] with Mikumwess; with whom, if she has not run away again, she is living

[031-006] yet. This story is at an end.

[031-007] _VI. How Master Rabbit gave himself Airs._

[031-008] (Micmac.)

[031-009] It happened once that Lox was living in great luxury. He had a wigwam

[031-010] full of hundreds of dried sea-ducks, moose meat, maple sugar, and corn.

[031-011] He gave a dinner, and among the guests invited Marten and Mahtigwess,

[031-012] the Rabbit.

[031-013] Now it is a great weakness of Master Rabbit that he is much given to

[031-014] hinting at one minute, and saying pretty plainly the next, that he has

[031-015] been in better society than that around him, and has lived among great

[031-016] people, and no one was quicker than the Marten to find out that wherein

[031-017] any one was foolish or feeble. So when Master Rabbit, smoothing down

[031-018] his white fur, said it was the only kind of a coat worn by the

[031-019] aristocracy, Marten humbly inquired, "if that were so, how he came by

[031-020] it."

[031-021] "It shows," replied Master Rabbit, "that I have habitually kept company

[031-022] with gentlemen."

[031-023] "How did you get that slit in your lip?" inquired Marten, who knew very

[031-024] well what this Indian really was.

[031-025] "Ah!" replied the Rabbit, "where _I_ live they use knives and

[031-026] forks. And one day, while eating with some great sagamores, my knife

[031-027] slipped, and I cut my lip."

[031-028] "And why are your mouth and whiskers always going when you are still?

[031-029] Is that high style?"

[031-030] "Yes; I am meditating, planning, combining great affairs; talking to

[031-031] myself, you see. That's the way _we_ do."

[031-032] "But why do you always hop? Why don't you sometimes walk, like other

[031-033] people?"

[031-034] "Ah, that's _our_ style. We gentlemen don't run, like the vulgar.

[031-035] _We_ have a gait of our own, don't you know?"

[031-036] "Indeed! Well, if you don't mind a question, I would like to know why

[031-037] you always scamper away so suddenly, and jump so far and so rapidly

[031-038] when you run."

[031-039] "Aw! don't you know? I used to be employed in very genteel business;

[031-040] public service,--in fact, diplomatic. I carried dispatches

[031-041] (_weegadigunn_, Micmac; _wighiggin_, Pass.)--books, letters,

[031-042] papers, and so I got in the way of moving nimbly. Now it comes

[031-043] naturally to me. One of my old aristocratic habits." [Footnote: This

[031-044] droll dialogue occurs in the middle of the Micmac story of Lox, or

[031-045] Badger, and the Ducks and Bear, where it evidently does not belong, or

[031-046] has been interpolated to make length. In the original, Marten carries

[031-047] his inquiries much further into certain physiological details, all of

[031-048] which Master Rabbit naively explains as the result of the delicate diet

[031-049] and the wine to which he as a gentleman had been accustomed.]

[031-050] Upon this Marten gave it up. He had seen something of good society

[031-051] himself, as he lived habitually with Glooskap, but Master Rabbit was

[031-052] too much for him.

[031-053] _VII. The Young Man who was Saved by a Rabbit and a Fox._

[031-054] (Passamaquoddy.)

[031-055] There dwelt a couple in the woods, far away from other people,--a man

[031-056] and his wife. They had one boy, who grew up strong and clever. One day

[031-057] he said, "Father and mother, let me go and see other men and women."

[031-058] They grieved, but let him go.

[031-059] He went afar. All night he lay on the ground. In the morning he heard

[031-060] something coming. He rose and saw it was a Rabbit, who said, "Ha,

[031-061] friend, where go you?" The boy answered, "To find people." "That is

[031-062] what I want," replied the Rabbit. "Let us go together."

[031-063] So they went on for a long time, till they heard voices far off, and

[031-064] walking quietly came to a village. "Now," said the Rabbit, "steal up

[031-065] unseen, and listen to them!" The boy did so, and heard the people

[031-066] saying that a _kewahqu'_, a cannibal monster, was to come the next

[031-067] day to devour the daughter of their sagamore. And having returned and

[031-068] reported this to the Rabbit, the latter said to the boy, "Have no fear;

[031-069] go to the people and tell them that you can save her." He did so, but

[031-070] it was long before they would listen to him. Yet at last it came to the

[031-071] ears of the old chief that a strange young man insisted that he could

[031-072] save the girl; so the chief sent for him, and said, "They tell me that

[031-073] you think you can deliver my daughter from death. Do so, and she shall

[031-074] be yours."

[031-075] Then he returned to the Rabbit, who said, "They did not send the girl

[031-076] far away because they know that the demon can follow any track. But I

[031-077] hope to make a track which he cannot follow. Now do you, as soon as it

[031-078] shall be dark, bring her to this place." The young man did so, and the

[031-079] Rabbit was there with a sled, and in his hand he had two squirrels.

[031-080] These he smoothed down, and as he did so they grew to be as large as

[031-081] the largest sled-dogs. Then all three went headlong, like the wind,

[031-082] till they came to another village.

[031-083] The Rabbit looked about till he found a certain wigwam, and then peered

[031-084] through a crevice into it. "This is the place," he said. "Enter." They

[031-085] did so; then the Rabbit ran away. They found in the cabin an old woman,

[031-086] who was very kind, but who, on seeing them, burst into tears. "Ah, my

[031-087] dear grandchildren," [Footnote: The terms grandchildren, grandmother,

[031-088] etc., do not here signify actual relationship, but only friendship

[031-089] between elderly and young people.] she cried, "your death is following

[031-090] you rapidly, for the kewahqu' is on your track, and will soon be here.

[031-091] But run down to the river, where you will find your grandfather

[031-092] camping."

[031-093] They went, and were joined by the Rabbit, who had spent the time in

[031-094] making many divergent tracks in the ground. The kewahqu' came. The

[031-095] tracks delayed him a long time, but at last he found the right one.

[031-096] Meanwhile the young couple went on, and found an old man by the river.

[031-097] He said, "Truly you are in great danger, for the kewahqu' is coming.

[031-098] But I will help you." Saying this, he threw himself into the water,

[031-099] where he floated with outstretched limbs, and said, "Now, my children,

[031-100] get on me." The girl feared lest she should fall off, but being

[031-101] reassured mounted, when he turned into a canoe, which carried them

[031-102] safely across. But when they turned to look at him, lo! he was no

[031-103] longer a canoe, but an old Duck. "Now, my dear children," he said,

[031-104] "hasten to the top of yonder old mountain, high among the gray rocks.

[031-105] There you will find your friend." They fled, to the old gray mountain.

[031-106] The kewahqu' came raging and roaring in a fury, but however he pursued

[031-107] they were at the foot of the precipice before him.

[031-108] There stood the Rabbit. He was holding up a very long pole; no pine was

[031-109] ever longer. "Climb this," he said. And, as they climbed, it

[031-110] lengthened, till they left it for the hill, and then scrambled up the

[031-111] rocks. Then the kewahqu' came yelling and howling horribly. Seeing the

[031-112] fugitives far above, he swarmed up the pole. With him, too, it grew,

[031-113] and grew rapidly, till it seemed to be half a mile high. Now the

[031-114] kewahqu' was no such sorcerer that he could fly; neither had he wings;

[031-115] he must remain on the pole; and when he came to the top the young man

[031-116] pushed it afar. It fell, and the monster was killed by the fall

[031-117] thereof.

[031-118] They went with the squirrel-sledge; they flew through the woods on the

[031-119] snow by the moonlight; they were very glad. And at last they came to

[031-120] the girl's village, when the Rabbit said, "Now, friend, good-by. Yet

[031-121] there is more trouble coming, and when it is with you I and mine will

[031-122] aid you. So farewell." And when they were home again it all appeared

[031-123] like a dream. Then the wedding feast was held, and all seemed well.

[031-124] But the young men of the village hated the youth, and desired to kill

[031-125] him, that they might take his wife. They persuaded him to go with them

[031-126] fishing on the sea. Then they raised a cry, and said, "A whale is

[031-127] chasing us! he is under the canoe!" and suddenly they knocked him

[031-128] overboard, and paddled away like an arrow in flight.

[031-129] The young man called for help. A Crow came, and said, "Swim or float as

[031-130] long as you can. I will bring you aid." He floated a long time. The

[031-131] Crow returned with a strong cord; the Crow made himself very large; he

[031-132] threw one end of the cord to the youth; by the other he towed him to a

[031-133] small island. "I can do no more," he said; "but there is another

[031-134] friend." So as the youth sat there, starving and freezing, there came

[031-135] to him a Fox. "Ha, friend," he said, "are you here?" "Yes," replied the

[031-136] youth, "and dying of hunger." The Fox reflected an instant, and said,

[031-137] "Truly I have no meat; and yet there is a way." So he picked from the

[031-138] ground a blade of dry grass, and bade the youth eat it. He did so, and

[031-139] found himself a moose (or a _horse_). Then he fed richly on the

[031-140] young grass till he had enough, when the Fox gave him a second straw,

[031-141] and he became a man again. "Friend," said the Fox, "there is an Indian

[031-142] village on the main-land, where there is to be a great feast, a grand

[031-143] dance. Would you like to be there?" "Indeed I would," replied the

[031-144] youth. "Then wait till dark, and I will take you there," said the Fox.

[031-145] And when night came he bade the youth close his eyes and enter the

[031-146] river, and take hold of the end of his tail, while he should draw. So

[031-147] in the tossing sea they went on for hours. _Thought_ the youth,

[031-148] "We shall never get there." _Said_ the Fox, "Yes, we will, but

[031-149] keep your eyes shut." So it went on for another hour, when the youth

[031-150] _thought_ again, "We shall never reach land." _Said_ the Fox,

[031-151] "Yes, we shall." However, after a time he opened his eyes, when they

[031-152] were only ten feet from the shore, and this cost them more time and

[031-153] trouble than all the previous swim ere they had the beach under foot.

[031-154] It was his own village. The festival was for the marriage of his own

[031-155] wife to one of the young men who had pushed him overboard. Great was

[031-156] his magic power, great was his anger; he became strong as death. Then

[031-157] he went to his own wigwam, and his wife, seeing him, cried aloud for

[031-158] joy, and kissed him and wept all at once. He said, "Be glad, but the

[031-159] hour of punishment for the men who made these tears is come." So he

[031-160] went to the sagamore and told him all.

[031-161] The old chief called for the young men. "Slay them all as you choose,"

[031-162] he said to his son-in-law; "scalp them." But the youth refused. He

[031-163] called to the Fox, and got the straws which gave the power to transform

[031-164] men to beasts. He changed his enemies into bad animals,--one into a

[031-165] porcupine, one into a hog,--and they were driven into the woods. Thus

[031-166] it was that the first hog and the first porcupine came into the world.

[031-167] This story, narrated by Tomah Josephs, is partly old Indian and partly

[031-168] European, but whether the latter element was derived from a French

[031-169] Canadian or a Norse source I cannot tell, since it is common to both.

[031-170] The mention of the horse and the hog, or of cattle, does not prove that

[031-171] a story is not pre-Columbian. The Norsemen had brought cattle of

[031-172] various descriptions even to New England. It is to be very much

[031-173] regretted that the first settlers in New England took no pains to

[031-174] ascertain what the Indians knew of the white men who had preceded them.

[031-175] But modern material may have easily been added to an old legend.
