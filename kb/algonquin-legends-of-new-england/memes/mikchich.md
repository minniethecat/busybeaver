# Mikchich

**Mikchich** — the Turtle (*Chick-we-notchk* in Passamaquoddy) — is the uncle of the culture hero [[Glooskap]] and the central figure of one of the most widely attested origin stories in the Wabanaki corpus: how Glooskap transformed his aged, unremarkable kinsman into a great man and secured him a wife. The story, compiled by Charles Godfrey Leland from six distinct versions, establishes the origin of the Turtle totem — "of the highest rank among the Algonquins" (008-060) — and illustrates the culture hero's power to elevate the humble through affection and magic.

## Overview

Mikchich appears in a single, extensively narrated episode within the Glooskap cycle, set at the village of Piktook (present-day Pictou, Nova Scotia). When Glooskap arrives there after departing Newfoundland, he meets Mikchich — a poor, lazy, middle-aged man distinguished only by his unfailing good nature. Despite Mikchich's utter lack of status or ability, Glooskap forms a deep attachment to him, chooses to lodge in his humble dwelling, and ultimately uses his *m'teoulin* (spiritual power) to remake his uncle into a young, handsome, and socially successful figure — in the process securing him a wife of high standing and establishing the Turtle as a paramount totem.

The story is notable for its compilation from six independent sources — including the narration of the renowned Passamaquoddy storyteller **Tomah Josephs**, the Rand manuscript, and the Anglo-Indian manuscript — making it one of the best-documented single narratives in Leland's collection (008-063).

## Identity and Attributes

### Names and Tribal Attribution

In the Micmac language the figure is called **Mikchich**, while in Passamaquoddy he is **Chick-we-notchk** — both forms translating to "the Turtle" (008-079). This dual naming across the two primary tribal sources of Leland's collection reflects the figure's broad geographical distribution within Wabanaki oral tradition. The story is explicitly attributed to "Micmac and Passamaquoddy" sources (008-079).

### Character and Status Before Transformation

Mikchich's defining trait is his amiability in the face of poverty and obscurity:

> "Truly he was none of these, being very poor and lazy, no longer young, and not very clever or wise in any way" (008-096).

It is precisely this lack of distinction — combined with "good nature" and the charm of his "quaint ways and old-time stories" (008-104) — that endears him to Glooskap. The Master's choice to dwell with Mikchich rather than accept the invitations of the village's more prestigious households is a deliberate inversion of social expectation. Leland notes:

> "It is said that he was indeed Glooskap's uncle, but others think that this was by adoption" (008-092).

This genealogical ambiguity is typical of Algonquian kinship narration, where the distinction between biological and adoptive relationship often blurs in the service of narrative meaning.

### Physical Transformation

Glooskap's *m'teoulin* works a comprehensive physical renewal upon Mikchich:

> "Mikchich became so young and handsome that no man or woman ever saw such a splendid creature" (008-109).

The transformation is not merely cosmetic — it enables Mikchich to participate in the social world of feasts and courtship from which his previous condition excluded him. The motif of magical rejuvenation by a culture hero is a recurring pattern in the Glooskap cycle, but the Mikchich story is unique in its focus on the transformation of an ordinary figure rather than a monster or adversary.

## Role in Legends

### The Uncle in the Glooskap Cycle

Mikchich occupies a distinctive position in the Glooskap cycle as the recipient of unearned favor. Unlike [[Lox]] the trickster, who earns Glooskap's tolerance through cleverness, or the [[Chenoo]], who requires exorcism and conversion, Mikchich is chosen simply because Glooskap loves him. The relationship models the culture hero's role as a bestower of blessings upon the humble and the overlooked.

The story serves a cosmogonic function: it explains the origin and prestige of the Turtle totem, which Leland describes as "of the highest rank among the Algonquins" (008-063). The transformation of Mikchich is thus not only a personal narrative but an etiology of a major social institution — the Turtle clan.

### The Feast at Piktook

The narrative unfolds against the backdrop of a great feast at Piktook (Pictou), where Glooskap, admired by all the village women for his "handsome, stately" appearance, deliberately snubs their invitations and instead resides with his uncle (008-104). When Glooskap urges Mikchich to attend the feast, the old man protests his unsuitability — prompting the magical transformation that enables his participation.

At the feast, the chief of Piktook's three daughters become objects of courtship. Through Glooskap's intervention, Mikchich is presented as an eligible suitor. Leland records the ceremonial details:

> "All invitations to a feast, or _n'kquai'gu_, were given by the host to the guest by a messenger bearing a quill or stick" (008-121).

The social ritual of invitation and the gifting of a white bear-skin underscore the story's function as a charter for proper marriage protocols among the Wabanaki.

### The Smoking of Mikchich

The narrative concludes with a distinctive ritual act:

> "Then he smoked Mikchich" (008-150).

Leland's footnote explains that this refers to a ritual smoking — a ceremonial act of purification or consecration — that finalizes Mikchich's transformation and invests him with the spiritual authority of the Turtle totem. The passage establishes a direct connection between the narrative and the physical practice of smoking as a sacred act:

> "I will now smoke you, that you shall be a great Sagamore, whose seed shall bear up a great nation" (008-150).

### Escape and Afterlife

The story includes a vivid escape episode in which Mikchich, pursued by enemies who seek to take his life, demonstrates the agility granted by his transformation:

> "And to escape them Mikchich jumped over the lodge, so that they gave up the chase" (008-142).

After his escape, Mikchich rejoices "greatly" at being told that he will "live as long as the world stands" — the Turtle's legendary longevity now encoded as a spiritual gift from Glooskap (008-163).

## Key Stories

### How Glooskap Made His Uncle into a Great Man

> **Source**: Micmac and Passamaquoddy, compiled from six versions (008-060 through 009-046)

Glooskap arrives at Piktook after departing Newfoundland and meets Mikchich, his old uncle — a man of no wealth, beauty, or wit, distinguished only by his good humor. Rather than accept the invitations of the village's prominent families, Glooskap chooses to lodge with Mikchich. When a great feast is announced, Glooskap urges his uncle to attend; upon Mikchich's protest that he is too old and obscure, Glooskap uses *m'teoulin* to transform him into a young and extraordinarily handsome man.

At the feast, Glooskap arranges for Mikchich to be proposed to the chief of Piktook's daughters, presenting him with a white bear-skin as a ceremonial gift. The marriage is secured, and Glooskap later summons a whale for a feast, demonstrating his power before the assembled people. When enemies later pursue Mikchich to take his life, he leaps over the lodge to safety. Glooskap then smokes him in a ritual act, declaring that Mikchich and his descendants shall "bear up a great nation" and live as long as the world endures — thus establishing the Turtle totem.

## Cultural Significance

The Mikchich narrative occupies a structurally unique position in the Wabanaki mythological corpus. Unlike the majority of Glooskap-cycle stories, which involve combat with monsters (the [[Chenoo]], the Kewokqu), pursuit of adversaries ([[Win-pe]]), or acts of world-shaping, the Mikchich story is a domestic narrative of affection, social elevation, and totemic foundation.

Its compilation from six independent versions — the most extensively cross-referenced story in Leland's collection — indicates both the narrative's centrality to Wabanaki oral tradition and its function as a charter for the Turtle clan's preeminence. The story's emphasis on ritual smoking as a transformative act connects the mythological past to living ceremonial practice among the Micmac and Passamaquoddy.

The tale also exemplifies a core thematic pattern of the Glooskap cycle: the hero's power is expressed not through conquest alone but through acts of generous transformation. By remaking the obscure Mikchich into a founding ancestor, Glooskap models the relationship between the divine and the human — one of elevation through affection rather than through merit.
