---[039-001] In this legend the hero passes the mysterious river which separates in

[039-002] several Indian tales the ordinary world from that where the evil

[039-003] giants, Jotuns, sorcerers, or witches live. It appears to correspond

[039-004] exactly to "the stream called Ifing, which divides the earth between

[039-005] the Jotuns and the Gods." (Edda, Vafthrudnismal, 16.) The attempt by

[039-006] the Porcupine host to roast the guest alive and its failure bears

[039-007] marked likeness to the scene in the Grimnismal, in which King Geirrod

[039-008] vainly strives to roast his guest, Odin, and is himself slain.

[039-009] "Fire, thou art hot,

[039-010] and much too great;

[039-011] flame, let us separate."

[039-012] The grandeur of Odin and the behavior of the Indian are set forth in a

[039-013] strikingly similar manner in both narratives. If any modern poet had

[039-014] depicted this incident in so like a style, every critic would have

[039-015] cried out plagiarism!

[039-016] _The Story of a Partridge and his Wonderful Wigwam._

[039-017] Once a man was traveling through the woods, and he heard afar off a

[039-018] sound as of footsteps beating the ground. So he sought to find the

[039-019] people that made it, and went on for a full week ere he came to them.

[039-020] And it was a man and his wife dancing about a tree, in the top of which

[039-021] was a Raccoon. They had, by their constant treading, worn a trench in

[039-022] the ground; indeed, they were in it up to their waists. [Footnote: To

[039-023] dance away the ground, or walk knee-deep in it, was characteristic of

[039-024] wizards. So was the hearing of any sound at an apparently incredible

[039-025] distance. To an Indian mind this tale is weird and wonderful from the

[039-026] first words thereof.] Then, being asked why they did this strange

[039-027] thing, they answered that, being hungry, they were trying to dance down

[039-028] the tree to catch the Raccoon.

[039-029] Then the man who had come said, "Truly there is a newer and better way

[039-030] of felling trees, which has lately come into the land." As they wished

[039-031] to know what this might be, he showed them how to cut it down, and did

[039-032] so; making it a condition that if they got the game they might have the

[039-033] meat and he should get the skin. So when the tree fell they caught the

[039-034] animal, and the woman, having tanned the skin, gave it to the man, and

[039-035] he went his way.

[039-036] And being afar, in a path in the forest, he met another man, and was

[039-037] greatly amazed at him because he was bearing on his head a house, or a

[039-038] large birch wigwam of many rooms. He was frightened at first at such a

[039-039] sight, but the man, putting down his house, shook hands with him, and

[039-040] seemed to be a right honest good fellow. Then while they smoked and

[039-041] talked, the Man of the House, seeing the skin of _Hespuns_, or

[039-042] that of the Raccoon, in the other's belt, said, "Well, that is a fine

[039-043] pelt! Where did you get it, brother?" And he, answering, told all the

[039-044] story of the Dancing Man and Wife; whereupon he of the House became

[039-045] mightily anxious to buy it, offering one thing after another for it,

[039-046] and at last the House, which was accepted. And, examining it, the buyer

[039-047] was amazed to find how many rooms it contained, and how full it was of

[039-048] good furniture. "Truly," said he, "I can never carry this as you do!"

[039-049] "Yes, you can," replied the _Pil-wee-mon-soo-in_ (P., one who

[039-050] belongs somewhere else,--a stranger). "Do but try it!" So he essayed

[039-051] and lifted it easily, for he found it as light as any _bassinode_

[039-052] or basket.

[039-053] So they parted and he went on carrying his cabin till night-fall, when

[039-054] coming to a hard-wood ridge, near a good spring of water, he resolved

[039-055] to settle there. [Footnote: A hard-wood ridge; that is, where there is

[039-056] plenty of birch, ash, and such trees as are necessary for baskets,

[039-057] dishes, canoes, and other Indian wants. Hence it is mentioned in many

[039-058] tales as a desirable place to live.] And, searching, he found a room in

[039-059] which there was a very fine bed, covered with a _white bear-skin_.

[039-060] [Footnote: A sure indication of sorcery.] And as it was very soft, and

[039-061] he was very weary, he slept well.

[039-062] In the morning, when he awoke, what was his astonishment and delight to

[039-063] see above him, hanging to the beams, all kinds of nice provisions,--

[039-064] venison, hams, ducks, baskets of berries and of maple-sugar, with many

[039-065] ears of Indian corn. And as he, in his joy, stretched out his arms and

[039-066] made a jump towards all these dainties, behold the white bear-skin

[039-067] melted and ran away, for it was the snow of winter; and his arms spread

[039-068] forth into wings, and he flew up to the food, which was the early buds

[039-069] of the birch, on which they hung. [Footnote: Birch-buds are the food of

[039-070] the partridge. The unexpected ending of this tale signifies the sudden

[039-071] return of spring. As told by an Indian, it is very effective. This tale

[039-072] was told me by Tomah Josephs.] And he was a Partridge, who after the

[039-073] manner of his kind had been wintering under a snow-drift, and now came

[039-074] forth to greet the pleasant spring.

[039-075] _How the Partridge built Good Canoes for all the Birds, and a Bad One

[039-076] for Himself._

[039-077] When a partridge beats upon a hollow log he makes a noise like an

[039-078] Indian at work upon a canoe, and when an Indian taps at a canoe it

[039-079] sounds afar off like the drumming of a partridge, even of

[039-080] _Mitchihess_. And this comes because that _N'karnayoo_, of

[039-081] ancient days, the Partridge, was the canoe builder for all the other

[039-082] birds. Yes, for all at once.

[039-083] And on a certain day they every one assembled, and each got into his bark,

[039-084] and truly it was a brave sight to see. First of all _Kicheeplagon_,

[039-085] the Eagle, entered his great shell and paddled off, using the ends of his

[039-086] wings; and then came _Ko-ko-kas_, the Owl, doing the same; and,

[039-087] _Kosqu'_, the Crane, _Wee-sow-wee-hessis_, the Bluebird,

[039-088] _Tjidge-is-skwess_, the Snipe, and _Meg-sweit-tchip-sis_,

[039-089] the Blackbird, all came sailing proudly after. Even the tiny

[039-090] _A-la-Mussit_, the Humming-Bird, had a dear little boat, and for

[039-091] him the good Partridge had made a pretty little paddle, only that

[039-092] some thought it rather large, for it was almost an inch long. And

[039-093] _Ishmegwess_, the Fish-Hawk, who lived on the wing, cried in

[039-094] amazement, "_Akweden skouje_!" "A canoe is coming!" when he

[039-095] beheld this beautiful squadron standing out to sea.

[039-096] But when _Mitchihess_, the great builder, was asked why he had not

[039-097] built a canoe for himself, he merely looked mysterious and drummed. And

[039-098] being further questioned by the birds, he shook his head, and at last

[039-099] hinted that when he built a canoe unto himself it would be indeed a

[039-100] marvel; yea, a wonder such as even birds' eyes had never beheld,--an

[039-101] entire novelty, and something to dream of. And this went on for many

[039-102] days.

[039-103] But in due time it was noised abroad that the wonderful canoe had at

[039-104] last been really built, and would soon be shown. And at an appointed

[039-105] time all the birds assembled on the banks to behold this new thing. Now

[039-106] the Partridge had reasoned that if a boat having two ends could be

[039-107] rowed in two ways, one which was all ends, all round, could be rowed in

[039-108] every way. So he had made a canoe which was exactly like a nest, or

[039-109] perfectly round. And this idea had greatly amazed the honest feathered

[039-110] folk, who were astonished that so simple a thing had not occurred to

[039-111] all of them.

[039-112] But what was their wonder when Partridge, having entered his canoe and

[039-113] proceeded to paddle, made no headway at all; for it simply turned round

[039-114] and round, and ever and again the same way, let him work it as he

[039-115] would. And after wearying himself and all in vain, he went ashore, and,

[039-116] flying far inland, hid himself for very shame under the low bushes, on

[039-117] the earth, where he yet remains. This is the reason why he never seeks

[039-118] the sea or rivers, and has ever since remained an inland bird.

[039-119] [Footnote: Having met Mr. Louis Mitchell, the Indian member of the

[039-120] legislature in Maine, one day in Eastport, I asked him to occupy the

[039-121] few minutes which would pass before I should take the steamboat for

[039-122] Calais by telling me a story. He complied by narrating the foregoing.

[039-123] It is very remarkable that the Indian story-tellers of ancient days

[039-124] should have taken it into their heads to satirize an idea which has

[039-125] been of late carried out completely by the Russian Admiral Popoff, in

[039-126] his celebrated circular war steamer. The story and all the Indian words

[039-127] in it are Passamaquoddy.]

[039-128] _The Mournful Mystery of the Partridge-Witch; setting forth how a

[039-129] Young Man died from Love._

[039-130] Of the olden time. Two brothers went hunting in the autumn, and that as

[039-131] far as the head waters of the Penobscot, where they remained all

[039-132] winter. But in March their snow-shoes (_agahmook_, P.) gave out,

[039-133] as did their moccasins, and they wished that a woman were there to mend

[039-134] them.

[039-135] When the younger brother returned first to the lodge, the next day,--

[039-136] which he generally did, to get it ready for the elder,--he was

[039-137] astonished to find that some one had been there before him, and that,

[039-138] too, in the housekeeping. For garments had been mended, the place

[039-139] cleaned and swept, a fire built, and the pot was boiling. He said

[039-140] nothing of this to his brother; but returning the next day at the same

[039-141] time, found that all had been attended to, as at first. And again he

[039-142] said nothing; but in the morning, when he went forth to hunt, he did

[039-143] but go a little way, and, returning, watched, from a hidden place, the

[039-144] door. And there came a beautiful and graceful girl, well attired, who

[039-145] entered the wigwam. And he, stepping softly, looking through a hole in

[039-146] the hut, saw her very busy with his housekeeping.

[039-147] Then he entered, and she seemed to be greatly alarmed and confused; but

[039-148] he calmed her, and they soon became good friends, sporting together

[039-149] very happily all day long like children, for indeed they were both

[039-150] young.

[039-151] When the sun's height was little and his shadows long, the girl said,

[039-152] "I must go now. I hear your brother coming, and I fear him. But I will

[039-153] return to-morrow. _Addio_!" So she went, and the elder brother knew

[039-154] nothing of what had happened. The next day she came again, and once

[039-155] more they played in sunshine and shadow until evening; but ere she went

[039-156] he sought to persuade her to remain always. And she, as if in doubt,

[039-157] answered, "Tell thy brother all, and it may be that I will stay and

[039-158] serve ye both. For I can make the snow-shoes and moccasins which ye so

[039-159] much need, and also canoes." Then she departed with the day, and the

[039-160] elder, returning, heard from his brother all that had happened, and

[039-161] said, "Truly I should be glad to have some one here to take care of the

[039-162] wigwam and make snow-shoes." So she came in the morning, and hearing

[039-163] from the younger that his brother had consented to her coming was very

[039-164] glad, and went away, as in haste. But she returned about noon, drawing

[039-165] a _toboggin_ (sled) piled up with garments and arms, for she was a

[039-166] huntress. Indeed, she could do all things as few women could, whether

[039-167] it were cooking, needle-work, or making all that men need. And the

[039-168] winter passed very pleasantly, until the snow grew soft, and it was

[039-169] time for them to return. Till she came they had little luck in hunting,

[039-170] but since her coming all had gone well with them, and they now had a

[039-171] wonderful quantity of furs.

[039-172] Then they returned in a canoe, going down the river to their village.

[039-173] But as they came near it the girl grew sad, for she had thrown out her

[039-174] soul to their home, though they knew it not, by _meelahbi-give_.

[039-175] [Footnote: Passamaquoddy: Clairvoyance, or state of vision.] And

[039-176] suddenly she said, as they came to a point of land, "Here I must leave.

[039-177] I can go no further. Say nothing of me to your parents, for your father

[039-178] would have but little love for me." And the young men sought to

[039-179] persuade her, but she only answered sorrowfully, "It cannot be." So

[039-180] they came home with their furs, and the elder was so proud of their

[039-181] luck and their strange adventure that he could not hold his peace, but

[039-182] told all.
