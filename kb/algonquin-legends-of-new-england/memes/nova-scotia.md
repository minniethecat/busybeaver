## Overview

Nova Scotia (known in Mi'kmaq as **Mi'kma'ki**) is a Maritime province on the Atlantic coast of Canada. It is the traditional territory of the Mi'kmaq (Micmac) people and served as the geographic backdrop for many of the Algonquin legends collected in the late 19th century. The province's coastline, forests, and river valleys feature prominently in the tales of Glooskap and other Wabanaki culture heroes.

## Geography

Nova Scotia comprises a peninsula connected to New Brunswick by the Isthmus of Chignecto, along with Cape Breton Island to the northeast. The Mi'kmaq name for Cape Breton is **Oona-mahgik**, recorded in the legends as a landmark along the southern coast (005-179). The province's landscape of rocky shores, dense forests, and tidal bays—including the Bay of Fundy and the Annapolis Valley—forms the physical world in which the Algonquin mythic cycle unfolds. One legend describes Glooskap traveling "all into this country, into Nova Scotia, Maine" as he shaped the landscape (004-046).

## Role in Legends

### The Collectors

Nova Scotia was home to two key collectors of Algonquin oral tradition. The Rev. **Silas T. Rand**, of Hantsport, Nova Scotia (001-096), was a Baptist missionary and linguist who devoted decades to recording Mi'kmaq language and folklore. Leland acknowledges Rand's "great manuscript collection of Micmac legends" (001-113) as a source for some of the tales in his own volume. These manuscripts, gathered across Nova Scotia Mi'kmaq communities, preserve stories that might otherwise have been lost.

### Landscape and Legend

Several geographic features in Nova Scotia are tied to Glooskap legends. The text notes that "Glooskap's bath-tub and slide, his seat, his flock of birds which became the Shick-shoe mountains in the Gaspe peninsula" are among the landmarks "still shown in Nova Scotia" (008-018). The Annapolis Valley functions as a setting for the Glooskap-Beaver cycle, where "all the valley of Annapolis" was the stage for a great transformation story (009-176).

## Historical Context

At the time of Leland's writing (1884), Nova Scotia was a province of the Dominion of Canada. The Mi'kmaq people had inhabited the region for over 10,000 years before European contact. The name "Nova Scotia" is Latin for "New Scotland," reflecting British colonial settlement. Hantsport, where Rand lived and worked, was a shipbuilding and trading center on the Minas Basin, part of the upper Bay of Fundy.
