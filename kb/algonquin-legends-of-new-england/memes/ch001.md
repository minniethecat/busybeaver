[001-009] BY CHARLES G. LELAND

[001-015] PREFACE.

[001-016] When I began, in the summer of 1882, to collect among the Passamaquoddy

[001-017] Indians at Campobello, New Brunswick, their traditions and folk-lore, I

[001-018] expected to find very little indeed. These Indians, few in number,

[001-019] surrounded by white people, and thoroughly converted to Roman

[001-020] Catholicism, promised but scanty remains of heathenism. What was my

[001-021] amazement, however, at discovering, day by day, that there existed

[001-022] among them, entirely by oral tradition, a far grander mythology than

[001-023] that which has been made known to us by either the Chippewa or Iroquois

[001-024] Hiawatha Legends, and that this was illustrated by an incredible number

[001-025] of tales. I soon ascertained that these were very ancient. The old

[001-026] people declared that they had heard from their progenitors that all of

[001-027] these stories were once sung; that they themselves remembered when many

[001-028] of them were poems. This was fully proved by discovering manifest

[001-029] traces of poetry in many, and finally by receiving a long Micmac tale

[001-030] which had been sung by an Indian. I found that all the relaters of this

[001-031] lore were positive as to the antiquity of the narratives, and

[001-032] distinguished accurately between what was or was not pre-Columbian. In

[001-033] fact, I came in time to the opinion that the original stock of all the

[001-034] Algonquin myths, and perhaps of many more, still existed, not far away

[001-035] in the West, but at our very doors; that is to say, in Maine and New

[001-036] Brunswick. It is at least certain, as the reader may convince himself,

[001-037] that these Wabanaki, or Northeastern Algonquin, legends give, with few

[001-038] exceptions, in full and coherently, many tales which have only reached

[001-039] us in a broken, imperfect form, from other sources.

[001-040] This work, then, contains a collection of the myths, legends, and

[001-041] folk-lore of the principal Wabanaki, or Northeastern Algonquin, Indians;

[001-042] that is to say, of the Passamaquoddies and Penobscots of Maine, and of

[001-043] the Micmacs of New Brunswick. All of this material was gathered

[001-044] directly from Indian narrators, the greater part by myself, the rest by

[001-045] a few friends; in fact, I can give the name of the aboriginal authority

[001-046] for every tale except one. As my chief object has been simply to

[001-047] collect and preserve valuable material, I have said little of the

[001-048] labors of such critical writers as Brinton, Hale, Trumbull, Powers,

[001-049] Morgan, Bancroft, and the many more who have so ably studied and set

[001-050] forth red Indian ethnology. If I have rarely ventured on their field,

[001-051] it is because I believe that when the Indian shall have passed away

[001-052] there will come far better ethnologists than I am, who will be much

[001-053] more obliged to me for collecting raw material than for cooking it.

[001-054] Two or three subjects have, it is true, tempted me into occasional

[001-055] commenting. The manifest, I may say the undeniable, affinity between

[001-056] the myths and legends of the Northeastern Indians and those of the

[001-057] Eskimo could hardly be passed over, nor at the same time the identity

[001-058] of the latter and of the Shaman religion with those of the Finns,

[001-059] Laplanders, and Samoyedes. I believe that I have contributed material

[001-060] not devoid of value to those who are interested in the study of the

[001-061] relations of the aborigines of America with the Mongoloid races of the

[001-062] Old World. This is a subject which has been very little studied through

[001-063] the relations of these Wabanaki with the Eskimo.

[001-064] A far more hazardous venture has been the indicating points of

[001-065] similarity between the myths or tales of the Algonquins and those of

[001-066] the Norsemen, as set forth in the Eddas, the Sagas, and popular tales

[001-067] of Scandinavia. When we, however, remember that the Eskimo once ranged

[001-068] as far south as Massachusetts, that they did not reach Greenland till

[001-069] the fourteenth century, that they had for three centuries intimate

[001-070] relations with Scandinavians, that they were very fond of legends, and

[001-071] that the Wabanaki even now mingle with them, the marvel would be that

[001-072] the Norsemen had not left among them traces of their tales or of their

[001-073] religion. But I do not say that this was positively the case; I simply

[001-074] set forth in this book a great number of curious coincidences, from

[001-075] which others may draw their own conclusions. I confess that I cannot

[001-076] account for these resemblances save by the so-called "historical

[001-077] theory" of direct transmission; but if any one can otherwise explain

[001-078] them I should welcome the solution of what still seems to be, in many

[001-079] respects, a problem.

[001-080] I am, in fact, of the opinion that what is given in this work confirms

[001-081] what was conjectured by David Crantz, and which is thus expressed in

[001-082] his History of Greenland (London, 1767): "If we read the accounts which

[001-083] have been given of the most northerly American Indians and Asiatic

[001-084] Tartars, we find a pretty great resemblance between their manner of

[001-085] life, morals, usages, and notions and what has been said in this book

[001-086] of the Greenlanders, only with this difference: that the farther the

[001-087] savage nations wandered towards the North, the fewer they retained of

[001-088] their ancient conceptions and customs. As for the Greenlanders, if it

[001-089] be true, as is supposed, that a remnant of the old Norway Christians

[001-090] incorporated themselves and became one people with them, the

[001-091] Greenlanders may thence have heard and adopted some of their notions,

[001-092] which they may have new-modeled in the coarse mould of their own

[001-093] brain."

[001-094] Among those who have greatly aided me in preparing this work I deem it

[001-095] to be a duty to mention MISS ABBY ALGER, of Boston, to whom it is

[001-096] cordially dedicated; the REV. SILAS T. RAND, of Hantsport, Nova Scotia,

[001-097] who lent me a manuscript collection of eighty-five Micmac tales, and

[001-098] communicated to me, with zealous kindness, much information by letter;

[001-099] and MRS. W. WALLACE BROWN, of Calais, Maine. It was through this lady

[001-100] that I derived a great proportion of the most curious folk-lore of the

[001-101] Passamaquoddies, especially such parts as coincided with the Edda. With

[001-102] these I would include MR. E. JACK, of Fredericton, New Brunswick. When

[001-103] it is remembered that there are only forty-two of the Hiawatha Legends

[001-104] of Schoolcraft, out of which five books have been made by other

[001-105] authors, and that I have collected more than two hundred, it will be

[001-106] seen how these friends must have worked to aid me.
