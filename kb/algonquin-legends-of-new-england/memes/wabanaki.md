# Wabanaki

## Overview

The Wabanaki, a word derived from a root signifying white or light, designates the Northeastern Algonquin peoples who live nearest to the rising sun or the east. They include the Micmacs of New Brunswick, the Passamaquoddy and Penobscot tribes of Maine, and the St. Francis Indians of Canada, among smaller clans (002-100). The French-speaking St. Francis family translate the term by *point du jour* — the break of day (002-103).

## Description

The Algonquin language family is one of the most widely extended in North America, ranging from Labrador to the far South and from Newfoundland to the Rocky Mountains, speaking forty dialects (002-094). Within this vast family, the Wabanaki hold a distinctive place: they share the traditions of a grand mythology whose central figure is the demigod Glooskap (002-105).

Leland observes that Glooskap, while always great, consistent, and benevolent, presents traits more like those of Odin and Thor, with not a little of Pantagruel, than anything in the characters of the Chippewa Manobozho or the Iroquois Hiawatha (002-108). He describes Glooskap as "the Norse god intensified" — more of a giant who grows to appalling greatness in his battles, yet in the family circle is the most benevolent of gentle heroes (002-116, 002-121).

The Wabanaki mythology populated every rock, river, and ancient hill in New England with a fairy, an elf, a naiad, or a hero (002-150). It created spirits for the terrible Arctic winters, for the icebergs and frozen wastes, for the Northern Lights and polar bears — a mythology congenial to any reader of the Edda, Beowulf, and the Kalevala (002-141, 002-143).

## Key Narratives

### The Wabanaki Creation Story

According to the Wabanaki, Glooskap was born in the land of the Wabanaki, which is nearest to the rising sun (005-083). The creation story involves Glooskap and his evil twin brother Malsum the Wolf, who represent the Good and Evil principles respectively (002-208).

### The Shared Mythology

The Wabanaki mythology includes strange giants — literal Jotuns of stone and ice, sorcerers who become giants at will, the terrible Chenoo with an icy-stone heart, witches and demons inherited from the black sorcery which preceded Shamanism, and the minor mythology of dwarfs and fairies (002-130, 002-133).

### The Indian *M'teoulin*

The Wabanaki *m'teoulin*, or magician, distinctly taught that every created thing, animate or inanimate, had its indwelling spirit. Whatever had an *idea* had a soul. This makes the Wabanaki mythology strangely like that of the Rosicrucians (002-137, 002-139).

## Cultural Significance

As regards spirit and meaning, Leland argues that Wabanaki mythology is "utterly and entirely unlike anything else that is American" (002-145). It is not like the Mexican pantheon; though many of its incidents are the same as those of the Chippewas or other tribes, "there is an incredible difference in the spirit. Its ways are not as their ways" (002-148).

This mythology, which Leland considers the one of all others least known to New Englanders, represents a literary and cultural heritage of extraordinary richness. When the last Indian shall be in his grave, those who come after will ask why there was no curiosity as to the romance of our own country (002-153, 002-155).
