**Kookwess** — _Kewahqu'_ in Passamaquoddy — is a cannibal giant and sorcerer who stands among the monstrous adversaries of [[Glooskap]] alongside the [[Chenoo]]. His defining attribute is a voice of such supernatural power that "all who hear it will drop dead" (050-063), making him a threat that cannot be faced through ordinary means of combat but must be survived through cunning and evasion.

## Overview

The Kookwess appears in a Passamaquoddy narrative collected by Leland in which the Fish-Hawk warns the people of a coming giant attack. Unlike the [[Chenoo]], who can be confronted and converted by Glooskap's power, the Kookwess is an implacable threat whose approach requires strategic flight. His name appears alongside the Chenoo in the summary of Glooskap's accomplishments, confirming his status as one of the major monster-types that the culture hero has overcome: "with the Chenoo and Kookwess; he has, like Hercules, fulfilled his labors" (050-063).

## Identity and Attributes

### Name and Etymology

Leland's footnote provides the linguistic context: "In Passamaquoddy _Kewahqu'_, a cannibal giant, who is also a sorcerer" (050-063). The variant spellings — Kookwess, Kookwes, Kewahqu', Kewokqu — reflect the transcription of an oral term across different recording contexts. The figure is explicitly Passamaquoddy in tribal attribution.

### Nature and Powers

The Kookwess possesses two defining attributes:

**Cannibalism**: Like the [[Chenoo]] and the unnamed giant who slew [[kitpooseagunow]]'s mother, the Kookwess is a devourer of humans. The warning given to the people is stark: "Unless you save yourselves you will all be devoured" (050-063).

**The Deadly Voice**: The Kookwess's most distinctive power is his roar — a sonic weapon of supernatural potency. The Fish-Hawk warns: "Before that time you must take to your canoes and flee afar. You may get beyond his reach, but you cannot before that time get beyond the horrible roar of his voice. And all who hear it will drop dead" (050-063). This power — to kill through sound alone — distinguishes the Kookwess from other Algonquin monsters, who typically kill through physical violence or consumption.

**Sorcery**: Leland's gloss identifies the Kookwess as a sorcerer as well as a giant, suggesting magical abilities beyond mere physical monstrosity.

## Role in Legends

### The Warning of the Fish-Hawk

The primary Kookwess narrative is embedded in a Passamaquoddy tale of prophecy and survival:

The Fish-Hawk, a supernatural messenger of ambiguous loyalty, arrives at a town and warns a man named Oscoon (M.: "the Liver") that a Kookwess will attack in seven days. The Fish-Hawk advises two countermeasures: flight beyond the giant's reach, and the closing of ears against the deadly roar. Oscoon leads the people away, closing their ears but leaving his own open. He hears a "far-away whoop" — not yet terrible — but says nothing. When scouts return, they report the Kookwess has departed without being seen. "It was a great escape" (050-063).

The Fish-Hawk, returning, inadvertently betrays his own ambiguous nature when he asks which direction the giant went. Oscoon's sharp reply — "Surely you, who know so much about him, must know that better than we do" — exposes the messenger's duplicity. The Fish-Hawk, "found out," flies away and never returns (050-063).

### Glooskap's Victory

In the summary passage that concludes the Glooskap cycle, the Kookwess is listed among the monsters the culture hero has overcome. Leland writes that Glooskap "has, like Hercules, fulfilled his labors" against the Chenoo and the Kookwess (050-063), explicitly framing these victories as labors in the classical sense — defining trials that establish the hero's stature.

## Key Stories

### The Kookwess and the Fish-Hawk

> **Source**: Passamaquoddy (050-063)

The Fish-Hawk warns Oscoon that a Kookwess — a cannibal giant whose roar kills all who hear it — will attack in seven days. Oscoon leads the people away in canoes, closing their ears against the deadly sound. He alone hears the giant's distant whoop. Scouts later report the Kookwess has departed unseen — a narrow escape. When the Fish-Hawk returns and inadvertently reveals his own complicity by asking the giant's direction, Oscoon's sharp retort exposes him, and the Fish-Hawk flees, never to return.

Leland notes that the Fish-Hawk in this tale is "very Loki-like and tricky" (050-063), comparing the narrative to Norse mythology in which Loki changes into a falcon to make mischief. The European parallel suggests a possible overlay on an originally Algonquin monster-tale.

## Cultural Significance

The Kookwess represents a distinct category of Algonquin monster — the threat that cannot be fought but must be survived. Unlike the [[Chenoo]], whose ice-heart can be melted and whose humanity can be restored, the Kookwess offers no possibility of redemption or confrontation. His power is absolute within its domain: to hear his voice is to die. The only viable response is strategic withdrawal and sensory denial — closing the ears against the world-destroying sound.

This pattern — the monster as implacable force of nature rather than moral adversary — recurs across Algonquin mythology. The Kookwess is not evil in a theological sense; he simply is what he is, as a hurricane or a plague is. Human survival depends not on heroism but on the wisdom to recognize limits and the prudence to withdraw beyond reach.

The Fish-Hawk's ambiguous role — simultaneously warning and colluding — adds a layer of narrative sophistication. Like the trickster figures who populate Algonquin tales ([[Lox]], [[abistanooch]]), the Fish-Hawk serves his own interests while incidentally benefiting others. The tale thus becomes as much about the discernment of true from false counsel as about the monster itself.
