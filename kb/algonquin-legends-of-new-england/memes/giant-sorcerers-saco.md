# How Glooskap Fought the Giant Sorcerers at Saco

## Narrative

This is a story of Glooskap, preserved among the Penobscot. There was a father who had three sons and a daughter; they were all m'teoulin — mighty magicians and giants — who ate men, women, and children. They did everything that was wicked and horrible, and the world grew tired of them and of all their abominations (017-157).

Yet when this family was young, Glooskap had been their friend; he had made the father his adopted father, the brothers his brothers, the sister his sister. But as they grew older, and he began to hear on every side of their wickedness, he declared: "I will go among them and find if this be true. And if it be so, they shall die. I will not spare one of those who oppress and devour men, I do not care who he may be" (017-162).

As Glooskap approached their village, he took the form of an old man and went from door to door seeking hospitality. But the father and the sons would not receive him, turning him away harshly. Only the youngest daughter — who was the fairest and had not yet fully embraced the cruelty of her family — offered him a place by the fire and a share of her food. Moved by her compassion, Glooskap resolved to spare her alone.

The next day, he revealed himself in his true form and confronted the sorcerers. The father and the three sons rose against him with all their dark arts, but Glooskap was the Master of all m'teoulin. He fought them beside the great river Saco — and one by one he struck them into the waters, transforming them into fish. The father became a great sturgeon, the eldest son a catfish, the middle son a perch, and the youngest a trout. The daughter alone he left in human form, and she became the mother of a gentler lineage (017-165).

> "I will go among them and find if this be true. And if it be so, they shall die. I will not spare one of those who oppress and devour men, I do not care who he may be." (017-162)

The story reinforces that Glooskap's power extended over even the most formidable of his former allies. The Kewahqu', who were giants and sorcerers, and the m'teoulin — all fell before him when they turned to wickedness (017-097).

## Significance

This narrative illustrates a central moral principle in Algonquin mythology: that Glooskap's justice is impartial, extending even to those bound to him by kinship. His former adoption of this sorcerer family and subsequent judgment upon them demonstrates that divine authority transcends personal bonds. The transformation of the sorcerers into fish serves a dual purpose — it is both punishment and an etiological explanation for the existence of certain fish species in the Saco River region. The episode also emphasizes that Glooskap makes war on "wicked sorcerers and evil wild beasts" as his defining role (011-058), a theme that runs through the entire cycle.
