**Kitpooseagunow** — "one born after his mother's death" in the Micmac language — is a magician-giant who occupies a position in Algonquin mythology "only inferior to that of Glooskap, whom he in every way resembles" (011-064). Like the Master, he is benevolent, wages war on wicked sorcerers and evil wild beasts, and possesses a sense of humor comparable to Gargantua and Pantagruel (011-073). His most famous appearance is in the Micmac tale "How Glooskap had a Great Frolic with Kitpooseagunow," in which the two giants engage in a friendly contest of whale-catching and cold-making that tests their magical powers to the limit.

## Overview

Kitpooseagunow is encountered by [[Glooskap]] at Pulewech Munegoo (Partridge Island), where the two giants — who love one another despite their rivalry — engage in a series of prodigious feats: transforming rocks into a canoe, spearing a whale of colossal size, and enduring two nights of supernatural cold that freezes their companions solid. The story, drawn from Rand's manuscript of Micmac legends, presents Kitpooseagunow as a parallel or alternate form of the Glooskap myth itself — a figure whose mother was slain by a cannibal giant, and who thereafter dedicated his life to protecting the oppressed (011-088).

The name derives from the practice of taking a moose or caribou calf alive from its dead mother and rearing it by hand; the animal thus born is called _Kitpooseagunow_, and from this a verb is formed denoting the act of posthumous birth (011-077). The etymology connects the giant's identity to themes of survival, resilience, and the acquisition of power through ordeal.

## Identity and Attributes

### Name and Etymology

The name _Kitpooseagunow_ is a Micmac term whose literal meaning — "one born after his mother's death" — is recorded in Rand's _Legends of the Mic Macs_ (011-077). Leland provides the folk-etymology in full:

> "After a cow moose or caribou has been killed, her calf is sometimes taken out alive, and reared by hand. As may be supposed, the calf is very easily tamed. The animal thus born is called _Kitpooseagunow_, and from this a verb is formed which denotes the act" (011-077).

### Nature and Character

Kitpooseagunow is a benevolent giant who, like [[Glooskap]], makes war on wicked sorcerers and evil wild beasts. Leland characterizes him in terms that explicitly parallel the culture hero:

> "Both are benevolent, both make war on wicked sorcerers and evil wild beasts, and both, finally, are much like Gargantua and Pantagruel in their sense of humor" (011-071).

His alternate title — "the Protector of the Oppressed" (011-088) — reinforces his role as a guardian figure. His mother had been slain by a "fearful cannibal giant" (011-091), a personal tragedy that motivated his lifelong war against such beings.

### Relationship to Glooskap

The two giants are described as loving one another, "which did not at all hinder them from having a hearty and merry encounter, in which they missed but little of killing one or the other, and all in the best natured way in the world" (011-097). This friendly rivalry — testing strength, magical power, and endurance — is characteristic of the Algonquin mythological mode, in which contests between powerful beings serve to establish cosmic balance rather than enmity.

## Role in Legends

### The Great Frolic

The central Kitpooseagunow narrative is a Micmac story collected by Rand and published in Leland's collection under the title "How Glooskap had a Great Frolic with Kitpooseagunow, a Mighty Giant who caught a Whale" (011-050). The story unfolds in two symmetrical episodes — a whale hunt and a freezing contest — each demonstrating the giants' supernatural powers and their good-natured rivalry.

#### The Whale Hunt

Arriving at Pulewech Munegoo (Partridge Island), Glooskap is entertained by Kitpooseagunow, who proposes a nocturnal whale hunt by torchlight (011-104). The episode is marked by prodigious feats of transformation.

> "Now when they came to the beach there were only great rocks, lying here and there; but Kitpooseagunow, lifting the largest of these, put it on his head, and it became a canoe" (011-032).

A second rock becomes a paddle, and a long splinter split from a ledge serves as a spear (011-033). Glooskap paddles while Kitpooseagunow takes the harpoon. The whale they encounter is without equal in all the sea:

> "He who held the spear sent it like a thunderbolt down into the waters, and as the handle rose again to sight he snatched it up, and the great fish was caught. And as Kitpooseagunow whirled it on high, the whale, roaring, touched the clouds" (011-045).

The giant tosses the whale into the canoe as casually as if it were a trout, and the laughter of the two giants "was heard all over the land of the Wabanaki" (011-055). They split the whale with a stone knife, roast their halves over a fire, and feast.

#### The Freezing Contest

After the feast, Glooskap remarks that the red sky portends a cold night. Kitpooseagunow understands that Glooskap intends to make it cold by magic, and responds by having Marten gather all available fuel and multiplying the porpoise oil tenfold by his own magic (011-070). What follows is a contest of magical endurance:

> "At midnight, when all was burnt out, Marten froze to death, and then the grandmother, but the two giants smoked on, and laughed and talked" (011-079).

The cold is so severe that rocks split and great trees crack with sounds like thunder — yet the two giants only laugh louder. They sit until sunrise, at which point Glooskap revives the dead with a command: "_Noogume, numchahse!_" — "Grandmother, arise!" — and "_Abistanooch numchahse!_" — "Marten, arise!" (011-090).

A second night of cold follows the next evening, still more severe, and again the companions freeze and are revived at dawn. Having matched powers with his host, Glooskap bids farewell and departs.

### The Growing Beaver Skin

A comic interlude in the narrative concerns a beaver caught during the day's hunting. Glooskap yields his share to Kitpooseagunow, who fastens the tiny skin to his garter:

> "But as he went on through the woods the skin grew larger and larger and larger, till it broke away by its own weight. Then the giant twisted a mighty sapling into a withe, and fastened it around his waist. But it still grew apace as he went on, till, trailing after, it tore down all the forest, pulling away the trees, so that Kitpooseagunow left a clean, fair road behind him" (011-105).

This episode — the uncontrollable growth of a magical object — is a motif that appears in multiple Algonquin tales, here serving to underscore the giants' power over the natural world even in mundane circumstances.

## Key Stories

### How Glooskap had a Great Frolic with Kitpooseagunow

> **Source**: Micmac, from Rand's manuscript, published in Leland (011-050 through 012-045)

Glooskap arrives at Pulewech Munegoo and is entertained by Kitpooseagunow, a magician-giant whose mother was slain by a cannibal giant. The two giants, who love one another despite their rivalry, embark on a torchlight whale hunt: Kitpooseagunow transforms rocks into a canoe, paddle, and spear; spears a whale of such magnitude that it touches the clouds when lifted; and tosses it into the boat with the laughter of the two shaking the Wabanaki land.

After the feast, Glooskap makes the night supernaturally cold. Kitpooseagunow matches him, multiplying fuel by magic. The cold freezes Marten and the grandmother solid, but the giants smoke and laugh through the night. At sunrise Glooskap revives the dead with a word. A second night of still-greater cold follows, and again Glooskap restores the frozen. A comic episode intervenes: a tiny beaver skin, fastened to Kitpooseagunow's garter, swells and grows until it tears down the forest, leaving a clean road. Glooskap departs, having met his equal.

## Cultural Significance

Kitpooseagunow represents what Leland recognized as a probable "Glooskap myth in another form" (011-088). The parallels are exhaustive: both are born of violent maternal death (Glooskap's mother dies at his birth; Kitpooseagunow's is slain by a cannibal), both protect the weak, and both wield transformative magic over objects and weather. The story of their "frolic" functions as a recognition scene — the culture hero meeting his double — and establishes a mythological framework in which power is tested and affirmed through friendly contest rather than conquest.

The narrative's structure — symmetrical episodes of whale-catching and cold-endurance, each followed by resurrection — suggests a ritual pattern, possibly connected to seasonal cycles or initiation ceremonies. The growing beaver skin, which clears a road through the forest, echoes the culture hero's role as a landscape-shaper and path-maker for humanity.

The figure's alternate title, "the Protector of the Oppressed," has led some scholars to interpret Kitpooseagunow as a specifically social-justice aspect of the Glooskap complex, embodying the hero's role as defender of the vulnerable against supernatural predation. The cannibal giant who slew his mother may represent the same class of man-eating monsters — [[chenoo]], kewokqu, and their kin — that Glooskap himself battles throughout the cycle.
