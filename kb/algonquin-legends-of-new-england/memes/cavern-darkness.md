# How Glooskap Sailed Through the Great Cavern of Darkness

## Narrative

In a Micmac tradition, it is told that Glooskap's seven neighbors — who were all different animals — took away his family, and he followed them as far as Newfoundland (009-077). Arriving there at night, he found only Marten alone. Glooskap took the boy into the forest to seek food, and placing his magic belt upon Marten, the child gained such power that he hunted well and obtained much meat (009-080).

The next morning, Dame Kah-kah-gooch the Crow observed Marten drying meat on his wigwam and spread the news abroad (009-082). When the people learned what the child had done, a great fear came upon them all, for they knew the Master had come. Glooskap indeed arrived, but when he saw them all as frightened as rabbits before the wild-cat, he laughed aloud and forgave them, for he was noble and generous (009-085).

Having made a canoe, the Master set out upon a mighty river with Marten and Dame Bear. The river began broad and beautiful, but then came great cliffs that gathered round and closed over them (009-106). The river ran on beneath, deeper and deeper into the earth, until it dashed headlong into rapids among rocks and ravines, and under cataracts so horrible that death seemed to come and go with every plunge of the canoe (009-110). The water narrowed, the current grew more dreadful, and fear overcame Marten and the woman so that they died. But Glooskap sat with silent soul, singing the songs of magic, and so passed into the night, emerging again into sunlight (009-115).

> "But the Master sat with silent soul, though he sang the songs of magic, and so passed into the night, but came forth again into sunlight." (009-115)

Glooskap bore Marten and the grandmother into a lonely wigwam on the bank, and saying "Numchahse! arise!" — they awoke, deeming they had only slept. And now Glooskap had gained the greatest power (009-118). Leland notes that this incident of passing through darkness, on a roaring stream in a frail canoe, is deeply suggestive of Wabeno mystery and initiation (009-123).

## Significance

The Cavern of Darkness journey represents one of the most profound episodes in the Glooskap cycle. As an underworld voyage, it bears striking parallels to classical and Norse motifs — the hero's descent into darkness, the death of companions, and emergence into sunlight with greater power. Leland himself notes that "every student of classic lore" will recognize the archetype (009-123). Within the Algonquin context, the episode is deeply connected to Wabeno mystery initiation, where the initiate undergoes a symbolic death and rebirth. The fact that Glooskap revives his companions with the word "Numchahse" (arise) reinforces the shamanic dimension of the narrative.
