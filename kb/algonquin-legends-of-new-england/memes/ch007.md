---[007-001] Then, going on, he reached the Strait of Camsoke [Footnote: Camsoke

[007-002] means, "There is a high bluff on the opposite side of the river."--S.

[007-003] T. Rand.] (M.), or Canso, and to cross over again sang the song which

[007-004] wins the whales, and one of these rising, carried him to the opposite

[007-005] shore. Thence he made the circle of Oona-mah-gik, keeping round by the

[007-006] southern coast, and coming to the old camps where his enemy had been.

[007-007] From the _witch-kwed-lakun-cheech_, or birch-bark dish, left by

[007-008] Martin, he learned how long they had been gone. [Footnote: As the gypsy

[007-009] leaves his _patteran_, or sign, so the Indian makes marks which

[007-010] set forth clearly enough how long he has camped at any place, and how

[007-011] many were in the party, etc. It may be supposed that Martin, not daring

[007-012] to attract Win-pe's attention, effected this by a few secret scratches.

[007-013] Thus three lines and a crescent or moon would mean three nights.] When

[007-014] he came to Uk-tu-tun (M., Cape North) he found they had rowed to

[007-015] Uk-tuk-amqw (M., Newfoundland), and had left three days before.

[007-016] Then again he sang, and once more a whale carried him over. And now he

[007-017] knew that he was indeed coming to what he sought, for in the deserted

[007-018] camp he found the embers of a fire, still smoking. Advancing rapidly,

[007-019] he saw near the next camp Martin, seeking wood to burn. The youth and

[007-020] the old Dame Bear had been most cruelly treated by Win-pe, and they

[007-021] were nearly starved, but Martin's clothes were good. [Footnote: There

[007-022] is a reason for this singular detail. Nancy Jeddore, the Indian from

[007-023] whom Mr. Rand learned one version of this legend, informed him that the

[007-024] Martin, thin at all times, always has a fine fur, however starved he

[007-025] may be. Dying with hunger, he is always well dressed.] And Martin was

[007-026] so sunk in sorrow that he did not hear Glooskap call him, and not till

[007-027] the Master threw a small stick at him did he look up, and even then he

[007-028] thought it had fallen from a tree. Then, seeing him, he cried out with

[007-029] joy; but Glooskap, who was hiding in the woods, bade him be silent.

[007-030] "Wait till it is dark," he said, "and I will go to your wigwam. Now you

[007-031] may go home and tell your grandmother."

[007-032] In the other story (M.) it is narrated that as Martin with the

[007-033] grandmother were on the road, and Dame Bear bore him as almost a babe

[007-034] on her back, he turned his head and saw Glooskap following them, and

[007-035] cried out,--

[007-036] "Where, oh where,

[007-037] Where is my brother?

[007-038] He who fed me often

[007-039] On the marrow of the moose!"

[007-040] And she replied,--

[007-041] "Alas for thee, boy!

[007-042] He is far, far away;

[007-043] You will see him no more."

[007-044] But the little fellow, seeing him again, sang as before, and Dame Bear,

[007-045] turning her head and beholding her Master, was so moved that she

[007-046] fainted and fell to the ground. Then Glooskap raised her in his arms,

[007-047] and when she had recovered she related how cruelly they had been

[007-048] treated by Win-pe. And Glooskap said, "Bear with him yet a little

[007-049] while, for I will soon pay him in full for what he has done."

[007-050] Then the Master bade the old woman go back to the camp with Martin, and

[007-051] say nothing. It was the youth's duty to go for water and tend the baby

[007-052] in its swinging cot. And Glooskap told him all that he should do. When

[007-053] he should bring water he must mix with it the worst filth, and so offer

[007-054] it to Win-pe, the sorcerer.

[007-055] And even as he ordered it was done, and Martin meekly offered the foul

[007-056] drink to the evil man, who at the smell of it cried aloud, "_Uk

[007-057] say_!" (M., Oh, horror!) and bade him bring a cleaner cup. But

[007-058] Martin, bearing the babe, threw it into the fire, and, running to the

[007-059] spot where Glooskap hid, cried out, "_Nse-sako! nse-sako_!" (M.,

[007-060] My brother! my brother!) Win-pe, pursuing him, said, "Cry out to him;

[007-061] your brother cannot help you now. He is far away from here, on the

[007-062] island where I left him. Cry out well, for now you must die!" All this

[007-063] had been done that Win-pe's power might be put to sleep by anger, and

[007-064] his mind drawn to other things. And the Master rose before him in all

[007-065] his might, and stepped forward, while Win-pe drew backward a pace to

[007-066] recover his strength. And with great will the Master roused all the

[007-067] magic within him, and, as it came, he rose till his head was above the

[007-068] tallest pine; and truly in those days trees were giants beyond those of

[007-069] this time. But the lord of men and beasts laughed as he grew till his

[007-070] head was far above the clouds and reached the stars, and ever higher,

[007-071] till Win-pe was as a child at his feet. And holding the man in scorn,

[007-072] and disdaining to use a nobler weapon, he tapped the sorcerer lightly

[007-073] with the end of his bow, like a small dog, and he fell dead.

[007-074] _How the Story of Glooskap and Pook-jin-skwess, the Evil Pitcher, is

[007-075] told by the Passamaquoddy Indians_. [Footnote: In this story

[007-076] Glooskap is called Pogumk, the Black Cat or Fisher, that is, a species

[007-077] of wild cat, while Martin is a N'mockswess, sable. There seems to be no

[007-078] settled idea as to what was the _totem_ or innate animal nature of

[007-079] the lord of men and beasts. I have a series of pictures scraped on

[007-080] birch-bark illustrating these myths, executed by a Passamaquoddy, in

[007-081] which Glooskap and the adopted grandmother in the stone canoe are

[007-082] represented as wood-chucks, or ground-hogs. (Mon-in-kwess, P.)]

[007-083] (Passamaquoddy.)

[007-084] There was a village of Indians who were all Black Cats, or Po'gum'k.

[007-085] One of them, the cleverest and bravest, went forth every day with bow

[007-086] and arrow, tomahawk and knife, and killed moose and bear, and sent meat

[007-087] to the poor, and so he fed them all. When he returned they came to him

[007-088] to know where his game lay, and when he had told them they went forth

[007-089] with toboggins [Footnote: Toboggin, a sled made very simply by turning

[007-090] up the ends of one or more pieces of wood to prevent them from catching

[007-091] in the snow.] and returned with them loaded with meat. And the chief of

[007-092] the Black Cats was by his mother the son of a bear. [Footnote: A

[007-093] confused but important point in all these myths.]

[007-094] Pook-jin-skwess, the Witch, was a Black Cat. She was a woman or a man

[007-095] as she willed to be; but in these days she was a man. And she, being

[007-096] evil, hated the chief, and thought long how she could kill or remove

[007-097] him, and take his place. Now, one day when all the camp had packed what

[007-098] they had, being about to travel, Pitcher asked the chief to go with

[007-099] him, or with her, as you may will, down to the water-side to gather

[007-100] gulls' eggs. And then they went far out in a canoe, and very far, and

[007-101] still farther, till they came to an island, and there they landed, and

[007-102] while Pogumk (who was Glooskap) sought for eggs, the false-hearted

[007-103] Pitcher stole away in the _akweden_ (P., canoe), and as she

[007-104] paddled she sang a song--

[007-105] "Nikhed-ha Pogumk min nekuk,

[007-106] Netswil sagamawin!" (P)

[007-107] "I have left the Black Cat on an island,

[007-108] I shall be the chief of the Fishers now!"

[007-109] So she came to the village, and the next day they all departed through

[007-110] the woods; there was not one of them left save the one who was worth

[007-111] them all. And at night they camped, expecting every day that the chief

[007-112] would come to them, and till then Pitcher was in his place.

[007-113] Now on the thirtieth day the chief remembered his friend the Fox, who

[007-114] had _m'teoulin_ (P.), or magic power. And he sang a song, and the

[007-115] Fox heard it, although he was miles away, beyond forests and mountains.

[007-116] And thus knowing all, he went to the shore and swam to the island,

[007-117] where he found the chief. At this time the Black Cat could not swim

[007-118] such a distance, [Footnote: The most powerful _manitous_, or

[007-119] magicians, in the Chippeway tales, as well as in all others of the

[007-120] Indians, may exhaust their power and be forced to depend on that of

[007-121] inferiors in the great art. In this tale Glooskap is decidedly under a

[007-122] cloud.] but the Fox offered to take him to the mainland. Then they

[007-123] waded into the water, and the Fox said, "Close thine eyes and hold fast

[007-124] to my tail as tightly as thou canst, and be of good faith, oh, my elder

[007-125] brother, and we shall soon gain the shore." Saying this, he swam away

[007-126] and his friend followed. And it went well with them, but the chief grew

[007-127] weary, and he opened one eye a little, and saw that they were not ten

[007-128] feet from the shore. And being of little faith he thought, for he spoke

[007-129] not aloud, "We shall never get to land." But the Fox replied, "Do not

[007-130] believe it." But the journey lasted long, for what seemed to Pogumk to

[007-131] be ten feet was ten miles, and the wind was high and the waters were

[007-132] wild, for Pitcher had called forth a storm. So they swam all day and

[007-133] all the evening before they landed. "And now, my elder brother," said

[007-134] the Fox, "you may go your way." And he went to the camp of the Black

[007-135] Cats.

[007-136] When he came to the camp it was cold, and there were only ashes, for

[007-137] the people had gone on. So he followed them, and in one day came near

[007-138] them. And the first whom he overtook was his mother, bearing his

[007-139] younger brother Sable ('Nmmok-swess, P.) on her back, so that while she

[007-140] looked forward he looked behind. And as Pogumk peeped out from among

[007-141] the leaves, Sable saw him, and said, "Here comes my brother!" And she

[007-142] turned, but saw nothing, for the chief suddenly hid himself behind a

[007-143] tree. Then they went on, and Sable cried again, "Indeed, mother, I

[007-144] behold my elder brother!" And this time the mother, glancing quickly,

[007-145] caught him, and they all laughed for joy, and she threw Sable down in

[007-146] the leaves, like a stick. Then the chief bade Sable run to the camp.

[007-147] "And when you are there," he said, "build up a great fire of hemlock

[007-148] bark, and take Pitcher's babe, even the babe which she loves, and which

[007-149] you tend, and throw it into the fire, and run to me as fast as you can,

[007-150] for verily thou wilt be in dire need to do so."

[007-151] And as he commanded it was done; and when the fire was hot, Sable threw

[007-152] the babe into it, and it was burned to death. And Pitcher, being, as

[007-153] one may well believe, maddened at such a sight, pursued him as a

[007-154] starving wolf pursues a rabbit. Then Sable, in great fear, cried aloud,

[007-155] "Oh, my elder brother, my brother!" And Pitcher screamed, "Call aloud

[007-156] to him, for you must run as far as the island where Pogumk is, to save

[007-157] yourself!" And at that word Pogumk stepped forward and confronted her,

[007-158] and said, "Truly, she need not run so far."

[007-159] And seeing him and hearing this, fear came upon her; but she laughed

[007-160] aloud to hide it, and said, "I did but chase him in sport, for I love

[007-161] Sable." But Pogumk replied grimly, "I know thee and thy tricks, thou

[007-162] the evil one." Then, as his magic had come to him, he used his power,

[007-163] and put Pitcher with her back against a tree; and there she stayed,

[007-164] stuck to it, unable to get away. But the chief and Sable went to the

[007-165] camp. Now Pitcher had a hatchet and wedge, and with much ado she cut

[007-166] herself away, and the Black Cats heard her pounding and chopping all

[007-167] night long. And in the morning she came to them, and there was a great

[007-168] piece of wood sticking to her back, and they laughed her to scorn, and

[007-169] sang at her,--
