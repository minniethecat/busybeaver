---[037-001] He went on to the camping ground and built a wigwam. He made up two

[037-002] beds; he built a fire. His wife came. She was earnest that there should

[037-003] be only a single bed. He sternly bade her lie by herself. She was

[037-004] afraid of him. She laid down, and went to sleep. He arose three times

[037-005] during the night to replenish the fire. Every time he called her, and

[037-006] there was no answer. In the morning he shook her. She was dead. She had

[037-007] died by the poison of the serpent. They sunk her in the pond where the

[037-008] snake lived.

[037-009] [Illustration: THE WOMAN AND THE SERPENT]

[037-010] I do not omit this ghastly and repulsive legend for the following reasons:

[037-011] One might hastily conclude, from its resemblance to the old legend of the

[037-012] origin of the Merovingian family, that this idea of the woman with the

[037-013] horrible water spirit for a lover was of Canadian French origin. But a

[037-014] story like it in the main detail is told by the Indians of Guiana, and

[037-015] that of the Faithless Wife, given in Rink's Tales and Traditions of the

[037-016] Eskimo (p. 143), is almost the same. But in the latter the husband

[037-017] revenges himself by stuffing the woman full of poisonous vermin. Rink

[037-018] says that he had five different versions of this tale, and that one was

[037-019] from Labrador, a country often traveled by the Micmacs, and even by the

[037-020] Penobscots and Passamaquoddies; I myself knowing one of the latter who

[037-021] has been there. I conjecture that this tale sets forth the aboriginal

[037-022] idea of the origin of a certain disease supposed to have come from

[037-023] America. It is popularly believed among the vulgar that this disease can

[037-024] be transferred to another person, thereby removing it from the first. Of

[037-025] this the Rev. Thistleton Dyer, in his Folk Lore of Shakespeare, says,

[037-026] "According to an old but erroneous belief, infection communicated to

[037-027] another left the infecter free; in allusion to which Timon of Athens

[037-028] (Act IV. 3) says,--

[037-029] "'I will not kiss thee; then the rot returns

[037-030] To thy own lips again.'"

[037-031] Bonifacius, Historia Ludicra, has collected all the instances known to

[037-032] classical antiquity of women who had serpent lovers. The kings of the

[037-033] early races of Central America laid great stress on the fact that they

[037-034] were descendants of serpents. One could fill a volume with all the

[037-035] Arab, Hindoo, and other Oriental tales belonging to the beloved of

[037-036] "ophitic monsters."

[037-037] I am indebted for this very curious and ancient tale to Governor Tomah

[037-038] Josephs, of Peter Dana's Point, Maine.

[037-039] _The Mother of Serpents._

[037-040] (Passamaquoddy.)

[037-041] There was once a couple well advanced in years. They were powerful and

[037-042] rich in the Indian fashion, but they were unhappy because they had no

[037-043] children. This was near the river St. John's, on the shore of a small

[037-044] lake.

[037-045] After the woman had gone in vain to all the medicine men and

[037-046] _m'teoulin_, she heard of an old doctress, or witch, who lived not

[037-047] very far off. And though hope was almost dead, the witch was consulted.

[037-048] She gave the wife some herbs, and bade her steep them in a pot

[037-049] out-of-doors, and then let them boil. When the vessel should dance

[037-050] over the flame, the propitious moment would be at hand.

[037-051] Everything succeeded according to the witch's prediction. A few days

[037-052] after she appeared in the town. The mother, who was a very proud woman,

[037-053] had in advance hung up an Indian cradle with very fine ornaments. The

[037-054] old woman was very dirty, poor, and squalid. The proud woman was

[037-055] furious at the visit, which mortified her in every way. She drove the

[037-056] witch away with bitter words, bidding her begone with her rags. The old

[037-057] woman went away muttering, "That woman--too proud--too ugly proud--I'll

[037-058] see." [Footnote: The story was narrated in Indian-English.]

[037-059] What she saw was bad for the mother. She took some more herbs from her

[037-060] box and threw them in the fire, crying with a loud voice, "_At-o-sis!

[037-061] At-o-sis!_" and imitated the motions of a snake.

[037-062] When the proud woman was confined, she gave birth to two large

[037-063] serpents. They had each a white ring round the neck and red stripes

[037-064] down the sides. As soon as they were born they went rapidly to the

[037-065] lake, and disappeared in its water. They have been seen there, now and

[037-066] then, ever since.

[037-067] She who gave birth, to them was a Mohawk, and she is called the Mother

[037-068] of Serpents.

[037-069] Another Passamaquoddy tale gives the following account of the origin of

[037-070] the Serpent-race.

[037-071] Once there was an Indian sorcerer came to a wigwam where there was a

[037-072] man who had a very handsome daughter.

[037-073] The magician wished to win the girl; the father made up his mind that

[037-074] he should not have her.

[037-075] The magician told them that he was very wealthy, and had a great lodge

[037-076] filled with furs and wampum. It was of no use.

[037-077] Then he told the father that if he would go and cast his lines in a

[037-078] certain place he would catch as many of the finest fish as he wanted.

[037-079] The old man went, but took his daughter with him.

[037-080] When they returned, loaded with fish, the magician, smiling, said to

[037-081] the girl with great mystery, "When you have cooked _these_ fish,

[037-082] always throw away the tail, and begin by eating the head first."

[037-083] He knew very well that her curiosity and perversity would make her

[037-084] disobey him. She waited with impatience till the man had left, when she

[037-085] hurried to cook and eat the fish. Thereby she became a mother, and the

[037-086] magician had his revenge.

[037-087] _Origin of the Black Snakes._

[037-088] (Passamaquoddy.)

[037-089] Far away, very far in the north, there dwelt by the border of a great

[037-090] lake a man and his wife. They had no children, and the woman was very

[037-091] beautiful and passionate.

[037-092] The lake was frozen over during the greater part of the year. One day

[037-093] when the woman cut away the ice, she saw in the water a bright pair of

[037-094] large eyes looking steadily at her. They charmed her so that she could

[037-095] not move. Then she distinguished a handsome face; it was that of a fine

[037-096] slender young man. He came out of the water. His eyes seemed brighter

[037-097] and more fascinating than ever; he glittered from head to foot; on his

[037-098] breast was a large shining silvery plate.

[037-099] The woman learned that this was At-o-sis, the Serpent, but she returned

[037-100] his embraces and held conversation with him, and was so charmed with

[037-101] her lover that she not only met him more than once every day, but even

[037-102] went forth to see him in the night.

[037-103] Her husband, noticing these frequent absences asked her why she went

[037-104] forth so frequently. She replied, "To get the fresh air."

[037-105] The weather grew warmer; the ice left the lake; grass and leaves were

[037-106] growing. Then the woman waited till her husband slept, and stole out

[037-107] from the man whom she kissed no more, to the lover whom she fondled and

[037-108] kissed more than ever.

[037-109] At last the husband's suspicions being fairly aroused, he resolved to

[037-110] watch her. To do this he said that he would be absent for three days.

[037-111] But he returned at the end of the first day, and found that she was

[037-112] absent. As she came in he observed something like silvery scales on the

[037-113] logs. He asked what they were. She replied, _Brooches_. [Footnote:

[037-114] _Nskmahn'l_: coins of all sizes hammered out by the Indians and

[037-115] made into pin-brooches.]

[037-116] He was still dissatisfied, and said that he would be gone for one day.

[037-117] He went to the top of a hill not far distant, whence he watched her.

[037-118] She went to the shore, and sat there. By and by there rose up out of

[037-119] the lake, at a distance, what seemed to be a brightly shining piece of

[037-120] ice. It came to the strand and rose from the water. It was a very tall

[037-121] and very handsome man, dressed in silver. His wife clasped the bright

[037-122] stranger in her arms, kissing him again and again.

[037-123] The husband was awed by this strange event. He went home, and tried to

[037-124] persuade his wife to leave the place and to return to her people. This

[037-125] she refused to do. He departed; he left her forever. But her father and

[037-126] mother came to find her. They found her there; they dwelt with her.

[037-127] Every day she brought to them furs and meat. They asked her whence she

[037-128] got them. "I have another husband," she replied; "one who suits me. The

[037-129] one I had was bad, and did not use me well. This one brings all the

[037-130] animals to me." Then she sent them away with many presents, telling

[037-131] them not to return until the ice had formed; that was in the autumn.

[037-132] When they returned she had become white. She was with young, and soon

[037-133] gave birth to her offspring. It consisted of many serpents. The parents

[037-134] went home. As they departed she said to them, "When you come again you

[037-135] may see me, but you will not know me."

[037-136] Years after some hunters, roaming that way, remembered the tale, and

[037-137] looked for the wigwam. It was there, but no one was in it. But all the

[037-138] woods about the place were full of great black snakes, which would rise

[037-139] up like a human being and look one in the face, then glide away without

[037-140] doing any harm.

[037-141] THE PARTRIDGE

[037-142] _The Adventures of the Great Hero Pulowech, or the Partridge._

[037-143] (Micmac.)

[037-144] _Wee-yig-yik-keseyook_. A tale of old times. Two men once lived

[037-145] together in one wigwam in the woods, on the border of a beautiful lake.

[037-146] Many hard-wood trees made their pictures in it. One of these Indians

[037-147] was Pulowech, the Partridge in the Micmac tongue, but who is called by

[037-148] the Passamaquoddy Mitchihess; but the other was Wejek (M.), the Tree

[037-149] Partridge.

[037-150] Now it befell that one day Pulowech was walking along the shore, when

[037-151] it was winter, and he beheld three girls, fair and fine, with flowing

[037-152] hair, sitting on the ice braiding their locks. Then he knew that they

[037-153] were of the fairy kind, who dwell in the water; and, verily, these were

[037-154] plentier of old than they are now,--to our sorrow be it said, for they

[037-155] were good company for the one who could get them. And Pulowech, knowing

[037-156] this, said, "I will essay this thing, and perchance I may catch one or

[037-157] two of them; which will be a great comfort, for a pretty girl is a nice

[037-158] thing to have about the wigwam." So he sought to secure them by

[037-159] stealing softly along; but one cried, "_Ne miha skedap_!" "I see a

[037-160] man!" P., and they all went head over heels, first best time, into the

[037-161] water; and verily that was a cold duck for December in the Bay of

[037-162] Fundy.

[037-163] But though Pulowech had never hunted for sea-girls, yet he had fished

[037-164] for seals, who are greatly akin unto them, being almost as slippery;

[037-165] and wotting well that no man hath the mitten till he is refused thirty

[037-166] times and many more, he went about it in another wise. For this time he

[037-167] gat many fir boughs, strewing them about as if blown by the wind, and

[037-168] hiding himself behind them, again came up and made a sudden dart. Then

[037-169] the maids, crying as before, "_Ne miha skedap_!" "I see a, man!"

[037-170] went with a dive into the deep. But this time he caught, if not the

[037-171] hair, at least the hair-string, of the fairest, which remained in his

[037-172] hand. And, gazing on this, it came into his mind that he had got that

[037-173] which was her charm, or life, and that she could not live without it,

[037-174] [Footnote: The magic hair-string plays a part in many of these tales.

[037-175] It belongs to the sorcery of all the world in all ages.] or her

[037-176] cherished _sakultobee_ (M.). And taking it home, he tied it to the

[037-177] place in the wigwam above that wherein he slept. Nor had he waited long

[037-178] before she came, and, with little ado, remained with him as his wife.
