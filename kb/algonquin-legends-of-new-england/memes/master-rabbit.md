# The Adventures of Master Rabbit

The **Adventures of Master Rabbit** (*Mahtigwess* in Passamaquoddy) constitute one of the longest and most carefully preserved trickster cycles in *Algonquin Legends of New England*. Spanning at least six distinct tales across multiple chapters, the cycle follows the vain, foolish, yet strangely endearing Master Rabbit through a series of failed attempts to imitate other beings, gain social status, and escape the consequences of his own foolishness. Unlike the malevolent trickster [[Lox]], Master Rabbit is a comic figure — his schemes always fail, yet he never learns, making him one of the most recognizably human characters in Wabanaki oral literature.

## Overview

The Master Rabbit cycle is structured as a series of episodes, each illustrating a different facet of the character's vanity and foolishness. Leland collected the tales from multiple Passamaquoddy informants, notably **Tomah Josephs**, whose narratives form the backbone of the cycle as it appears in the book.

The cycle begins with Master Rabbit's attempt to rival **Keeoony the Snowy Owl**:

> "How Master Rabbit sought to rival Keeoony, the Snowy Owl" (002-026).

This opening establishes the pattern that defines the entire cycle: Master Rabbit observes another being's ability (the Owl's power to produce light from his eyes), assumes it must be easy, attempts to imitate it, and fails spectacularly.

## Narrative

### Rivaling Keeoony the Snowy Owl

The first tale establishes Master Rabbit's defining flaw — his inability to accept his natural limitations. Seeing Keeoony's eyes glow with light, Master Rabbit decides he too can produce illumination:

> "'By my life,' thought Master Rabbit, 'but that is an easy way of making light'" (028-160).

Master Rabbit's attempt to produce light from his own eyes predictably fails, and he retreats humiliated. Yet, the pattern of foolish imitation followed by humiliation proves to be the engine that drives the entire cycle.

### Playing Woodpecker and Making a Fool of Himself

Undeterred by his failure with Keeoony, Master Rabbit next attempts to imitate woodpeckers he observes pecking at trees:

> "Now Master Rabbit, though disappointed, was not discouraged" (029-014).

He climbs a tree and attempts to peck at the bark with his nose, only to fall and injure himself:

> "Master Rabbit undertook to play woodpecker. He climbed a high tree and tried to peck with his nose" (029-033).

The tale culminates in what Leland titles "The Last Time that Master Rabbit made a Fool of Himself" (029-048), a phrase that captures both the cycle's comic tone and its cyclical nature — there will always be a "last time" that turns out not to be the last.

### Going to a Wedding and Winning the Bride

In perhaps the cycle's most elaborate tale, Master Rabbit attends a wedding and, through a combination of luck and the foolishness of others, actually wins the bride:

> "How Master Rabbit went to a Wedding and won the Bride" (030-136).

This rare "victory" proves short-lived, as Master Rabbit's inherent foolishness soon undermines his success. The tale ends with Master Rabbit losing his long tail — an etiological detail explaining why rabbits have short tails:

> "that day Master Rabbit has had a very short tail" (030-158).

### Giving Himself Airs

The final tale in the cycle shows Master Rabbit attempting to claim status he has not earned:

> "Now it is a great weakness of Master Rabbit that he is much given to giving himself airs" (031-013).

When he claims that his ragged appearance proves "that I have habitually kept company with Glooskap" (031-048), the absurdity of the boast is apparent to all — yet Master Rabbit, true to his nature, remains oblivious.

| Master Rabbit Stories | Lesson |
|----------------------|--------|
| Rivaling Keeoony | Accept natural limitations |
| Playing Woodpecker | Imitation without skill leads to injury |
| Going to a Wedding | Even success is temporary for the foolish |
| Giving Himself Airs | Pretended status fools no one |

## Participants

- **Master Rabbit** (*Mahtigwess*) — The vain and foolish protagonist, a trickster who is always tricked
- **Keeoony** — The Snowy Owl whose glowing eyes Master Rabbit envies
- **Woodpeckers** — Birds Master Rabbit attempts to imitate
- **Wolves** — Predators who threaten Master Rabbit's household
- **[[Glooskap]]** — The culture hero whom Master Rabbit falsely claims as his associate

## Significance

The Master Rabbit cycle serves multiple functions in Wabanaki oral tradition. As entertainment, the stories provide comedy through the protagonist's serial humiliations. As moral instruction, they warn against vanity, false ambition, and social pretension — vices embodied by Master Rabbit in exaggerated form.

The cycle also provides an important contrast to the [[Lox]] trickster stories. Where Lox is genuinely dangerous — a malevolent magician whose tricks cause real harm — Master Rabbit is merely foolish. His schemes threaten no one but himself, and his humiliations are private rather than cosmic. This distinction between the "dangerous trickster" and the "foolish trickster" is a significant feature of Wabanaki oral taxonomy.

The etiological elements of the cycle — explaining why rabbits have short tails, why they fear wolves — connect the stories to the broader body of Wabanaki "why" tales that explain features of the natural world through narrative. In this, Master Rabbit shares a function with the [[Glooskap]] stories, though where Glooskap's transformations of the world are acts of creative power, Master Rabbit's are merely the unintended consequences of his foolishness.
