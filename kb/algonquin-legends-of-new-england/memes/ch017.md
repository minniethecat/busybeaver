---[017-001] And just before he came all of these good fellows had resolved in

[017-002] council that they would send the boldest man among them to certain

[017-003] death, even to the village which built the dam that kept the water

[017-004] which filled the brook that quenched their thirst, whenever it was not

[017-005] empty. And when there he was either to obtain that they should cut the

[017-006] dam, or do something desperate, and to this intent he should go armed,

[017-007] and sing his death-song as he went. And they were all agog.

[017-008] Then Glooskap, who was much pleased with all this, for he loved a brave

[017-009] man, came among them looking terribly ferocious; in all the land there

[017-010] was not one who seemed half so horrible. For he appeared ten feet high,

[017-011] with a hundred red and black feathers in his scalp-lock, his face

[017-012] painted like fresh blood with green rings round his eyes, a large

[017-013] clam-shell hanging from each ear, a spread eagle, very awful to behold,

[017-014] flapping its wings from the back of his neck, so that as he strode into

[017-015] the village all hearts quaked. Being but simple Indians, they accounted

[017-016] that this must be, if not Lox the Great Wolverine, at least Mitche-hant,

[017-017] the devil himself in person, turned Wabanaki; and they admired

[017-018] him greatly, and the squaws said they had never seen aught so lovely.

[017-019] Then Glooskap, having heard the whole story, bade them be of good

[017-020] cheer, declaring that he would soon set all to rights. And he without

[017-021] delay departed up the bed of the brook; and coming to the town, sat

[017-022] down and bade a boy bring him water to drink. To which the boy replied

[017-023] that no water could be had in that town unless it were given out by the

[017-024] chief. "Go then to your chief," said the Master, "and bid him hurry,

[017-025] or, verily, I will know the reason why." And this being told, Glooskap

[017-026] received no reply for more than an hour, during which time he sat on a

[017-027] log and smoked his pipe. Then the boy returned with a small cup, and

[017-028] this not half full, of very dirty water.

[017-029] So he arose, and said to the boy, "I will go and see your chief, and I

[017-030] think he will soon give me better water than this." And having come to

[017-031] the monster, he said, "Give me to drink, and that of the best, at once,

[017-032] thou Thing of Mud!" But the chief reviled him, and said, "Get thee

[017-033] hence, to find water where thou canst." Then Glooskap thrust a spear

[017-034] into his belly, and lo! there gushed forth a mighty river; even all the

[017-035] water which should have run on while in the rivulet, for he had made it

[017-036] into himself. And Glooskap, rising high as a giant pine, caught the

[017-037] chief in his hand and crumpled in his back with a mighty grip. And lo!

[017-038] it was the Bull-Frog. So he hurled him with contempt into the stream,

[017-039] to follow the current.

[017-040] And ever since that time the Bull-Frog's back has crumpled wrinkles in

[017-041] the lower part, showing the prints of Glooskap's awful squeeze.

[017-042] Then he returned to the village; but there he found no people,--no, not

[017-043] one. For a marvelous thing had come to pass during his absence, which

[017-044] shall be heard in every Indian's speech through all the ages. For the

[017-045] men, being, as I said, simple, honest folk, did as boys do when they

[017-046] are hungry, and say unto one another, "What would you like to have, and

[017-047] what you?" "Truly, I would be pleased with a slice of hot venison

[017-048] dipped in maple-sugar and bear's oil." "Nay, give me for my share

[017-049] succotash and honey." Even so these villagers had said, "Suppose

[017-050] _you_ had all the nice cold, fresh, sparkling, delicious water

[017-051] there is in the world, what would _you_ do?"

[017-052] And one said that he would live in the soft mud, and always be wet and

[017-053] cool.

[017-054] And another, that he would plunge from the rocks, and take headers,

[017-055] diving into the deep, cold water, drinking as he dived.

[017-056] And the third, that he would be washed up and down with the rippling

[017-057] waves, living on the land, yet ever in the water.

[017-058] Then the fourth said, "Verily, you know not how to wish, and I will

[017-059] teach you. I would live in the water all the time, and swim about in it

[017-060] forever."

[017-061] Now it chanced that these things were said in the hour which, when it

[017-062] passes over the world, all the wishes uttered by men are granted. And

[017-063] so it was with these Indians. For the first became a Leech, the second

[017-064] a Spotted Frog, the third a Crab, which is washed up and down with the

[017-065] tide, and the fourth a Fish. Ere this there had been in all the world

[017-066] none of the creatures which dwell in the water, and now they were

[017-067] there, and of all kinds. And the river came rushing and roaring on, and

[017-068] they all went headlong down to the sea, to be washed into many lands

[017-069] over all the world. [Footnote: This was told by Tomah Josephs. It is

[017-070] given much more imperfectly in the tale of Kitpooseagunow in the Rand

[017-071] manuscript, and in the Anglo-Indian "Storey of Glooscap." I have taken

[017-072] very great pains in this, as in all the tales written down from verbal

[017-073] narration, to be accurate in details, and to convey as well as I could

[017-074] the quaint manner and dry humor which characterized the style of the

[017-075] narrator. Even white men do not tell the same story in the same way to

[017-076] everybody; and if Tomahquah and others fully expressed their feelings

[017-077] to me, it was because they had never before met with a white man who

[017-078] listened to them with such sympathy. It may be observed that the

[017-079] Indians commonly say that wherever the bull-frog is to be found in

[017-080] summer there is always water. It is not to be understood, in this tale,

[017-081] that the bull-frog is supposed to have merely drunk up the river. It is

[017-082] the river which has become incarnate in him. It is the ice of winter

[017-083] penetrated by the spear of the sun; that is, Glooskap. Thus, in another

[017-084] tale, a frozen river tries, as a man, to destroy the hero, but is

[017-085] melted by him. The conception of _the hour_ when all wishes are

[017-086] granted, and the abrupt termination of the whole in a grand

[017-087] transformation scene, are both very striking. There is something like

[017-088] the former in Rabelais, in his narrative of the golden hatchet; as

[017-089] regards the latter, it is like the ending of a Christmas pantomime.

[017-090] Indeed, the entire tale is perfectly adapted to such a "dramatization."

[017-091] I have been told by an old Passamaquoddy woman that the name of the

[017-092] monster who swallowed the stream was _Hahk-lee-be-mo_.]

[017-093] _How the Lord of Men and Beasts strove with the Mighty Wasis, and was

[017-094] shamefully defeated._

[017-095] (Penobscot.)

[017-096] Now it came to pass when Glooskap had conquered all his enemies, even the

[017-097] _Kewahqu'_, who were giants and sorcerers, and the _m'teoulin_,

[017-098] who were magicians, and the _Pamola_, who is the evil spirit of the

[017-099] night air, and all manner of ghosts, witches, devils, cannibals, and

[017-100] goblins, that he thought upon what he had done, and wondered if his

[017-101] work was at an end.

[017-102] And he said this to a certain woman. But she replied, "Not so fast,

[017-103] Master, for there yet remains One whom no one has ever conquered or got

[017-104] the better of in any way, and who will remain unconquered to the end of

[017-105] time."

[017-106] "And who is he?" inquired the Master.

[017-107] "It is the mighty _Wasis_," she replied, "and there he sits; and I

[017-108] warn you that if you meddle with him you will be in sore trouble."

[017-109] Now _Wasis_ was the Baby. And he sat on the floor sucking a piece

[017-110] of maple-sugar, greatly contented, troubling no one.

[017-111] As the Lord of Men and Beasts had never married or had a child, he knew

[017-112] naught of the way of managing children. Therefore he was quite certain,

[017-113] as is the wont of such people, that he knew all about it. So he turned

[017-114] to Baby with a bewitching smile and bade him come to him.

[017-115] Then Baby smiled again, but did not budge. And the Master spake sweetly

[017-116] and made his voice like that of the summer bird, but it was of no

[017-117] avail, for Wasis sat still and sucked his maple-sugar.

[017-118] Then the Master frowned and spoke terribly, and ordered Wasis to come

[017-119] crawling to him immediately. And Baby burst out into crying and

[017-120] yelling, but did not move for all that.

[017-121] Then, since he could do but one thing more, the Master had recourse to

[017-122] magic. He used his most awful spells, and sang the songs which raise

[017-123] the dead and scare the devils. And Wasis sat and looked on admiringly,

[017-124] and seemed to find it very interesting, but all the same he never moved

[017-125] an inch.

[017-126] So Glooskap gave it up in despair, and Wasis, sitting on the floor in

[017-127] the sunshine, went _goo! goo!_ and crowed.

[017-128] And to this day when you see a babe well contented, going _goo!

[017-129] goo!_ and crowing, and no one can tell why, know that it is because

[017-130] he remembers the time when he overcame the Master who had conquered all

[017-131] the world. For of all the beings that have ever been since the

[017-132] beginning, Baby is alone the only invincible one. [Footnote: I am

[017-133] indebted for this "marchen" to Maria Saksis, a very intelligent

[017-134] Penobscot woman, a widow of a former governor, whom I met at North

[017-135] Conway, in the White Mountains, N. H. In her dialect Glooskap is

[017-136] invariably called _Glus-gah-be_. She told it with that admirable

[017-137] dry drollery, characteristic of a good story-teller in a race where

[017-138] there are no bad ones. The exquisite humor and humanity of this little

[017-139] legend, placed as a pendant to the stupendous successes of the giant

[017-140] hero, are such as to entitle its Indian author to rank as a genius. I

[017-141] have frequently asserted that these Wabanaki or Northeastern Algonquin

[017-142] tales bore to those of the West the apparent relation of originals to

[017-143] poor copies. Let the reader compare this, which is given as nearly word

[017-144] for word as was possible from the Indian narrative, with that of

[017-145] Manobozho-Hiawatha's effort to compete with a baby. The Cherokee

[017-146] account is that, seeing an infant sucking its own toe, he tried to do

[017-147] the same, and failed. It is in accounting for the unaccountable crowing

[017-148] of Baby that the point of the Penobscot story lies. Of this there is no

[017-149] mention made in the Western tale, which is utterly wanting in any

[017-150] feeling as to the power of childhood or its charm over the strongest. A

[017-151] real Indian tale may always be assumed to be ancient when it is told to

[017-152] set forth an _origin_. This gives the origin of a baby's crowing.]

[017-153] _How the great Glooskap fought the Giant Sorcerers at Saco, and

[017-154] turned them into Fish._

[017-155] (Penobscot.)

[017-156] _N'karnayoo_, of old times: _Woodenit atok hagen_

[017-157] _Glusgahbe_. This is a story of Glooskap (P.) There was a father

[017-158] who had three sons and a daughter: they were _m'teoulin_, or

[017-159] mighty magicians; they were giants; they ate men, women, and children;

[017-160] they did everything that was wicked and horrible; and the world grew

[017-161] tired of them and of all their abominations. Yet when this family was

[017-162] young, Glooskap had been their friend; he had made the father his

[017-163] adopted father, the brothers his brothers, the sister his sister.

[017-164] [Footnote: The Indians make formal adoptions of relatives of every

[017-165] grade, and in addition to this use all the terms of relationship as

[017-166] friendly greetings. This is in fact made apparent in all the stories in

[017-167] this collection.] Yet as they grew older, and he began to hear on every

[017-168] side of their wickedness, he said: "I will go among them and find if

[017-169] this be true. And if it be so, they shall die. I will not spare one of

[017-170] those who oppress and devour men, I do not care who he may be."
