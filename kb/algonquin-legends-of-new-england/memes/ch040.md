---[040-001] Then his father was very angry, and said, "All my life have I feared

[040-002] this. Know that this woman was a devil of the woods, a witch of the

[040-003] Mitche-hant, a sister of the _Oonahgamess_ [Footnote: P. Goblins

[040-004] and ghosts.] and of the _Ke'tahks_." And he spoke so earnestly and

[040-005] so long of this thing that they were afraid, and the elder, being

[040-006] persuaded by the sire, went forth to slay her, and the younger followed

[040-007] him afar. So they sought her by the stream, and found her bathing, and,

[040-008] seeing them, she ran up a little hill. And, as she ran, the elder shot

[040-009] an arrow at her. Then there was a strange flurry about her, a

[040-010] fluttering of scattered feathers, and they saw her fly away as a

[040-011] partridge. Returning, they told all this to their father, who said,

[040-012] "You did well. I know all about these female devils who seek to destroy

[040-013] men. Verily this was a she Mikumwess." [Footnote: P. The Mikumwess is a

[040-014] Robin Goodfellow, who plays pranks on people, or treats them kindly,

[040-015] according to his caprice.]

[040-016] But the younger could not forget her, and longed to see her again; so

[040-017] one day he went into the woods, and there he indeed found her, and she

[040-018] was as kind as before. Then he said, "Truly it was not by my goodwill

[040-019] that my brother shot at you." And she answered, "Well do I know that,

[040-020] and that it was all by your father; yet I blame him not, for this is an

[040-021] affair of _N'karnayoo_, the days of old; and even yet it is not at

[040-022] an end, and the greatest is to come. But let the day be only a day unto

[040-023] itself; the things of to-morrow are for to-morrow, and those of

[040-024] yesterday are departed." So they forgot their troubles, and played

[040-025] together merrily all day long in the woods and in the open places, and

[040-026] told stories of old times till sunset. And as, the _Kah-kah-goos_,

[040-027] or Crow, went to his tree, the boy said, "I must return;" and she

[040-028] replied, "Whenever you would see me, come to the woods. And remember

[040-029] what I say. Do not marry any one else. For your father wishes you to do

[040-030] so, and he will speak of it to you, and that soon. Yet it is for your

[040-031] sake only that I say this." Then she told him word by word all that his

[040-032] father had said; but he was not astonished, for now he knew that she

[040-033] was not as other women; but he cared not. And he grew brave and bold,

[040-034] and then he was above all things. And when she told him that if he

[040-035] should marry another he would surely die, it was as nothing to him.

[040-036] Then returning, the first thing his father said was, "My son, I have

[040-037] provided a wife for you, and the wedding must be at once." And he said,

[040-038] "It is well. Let it be so." Then the bride came. For four days they

[040-039] held the wedding dance; four days they feasted. But on the last day he

[040-040] said, "This is the end of it all," and he laid him down on a white

[040-041] bear-skin, and a great sickness came upon him, and when they brought

[040-042] the bride to him he was dead.

[040-043] Truly the father knew what ailed him, and more withal, of which he said

[040-044] nothing. But he liked the place no longer, and he and his went away

[040-045] therefrom, and scattered far and wide.

[040-046] This strange story recalls the Undine of La Motte Fouque. There is in

[040-047] it an element of mystery and destiny, equal in every way to anything in

[040-048] German literature. The family secret, touched on but never explained,

[040-049] which ends in such a death, is, speaking from an artistic point of

[040-050] view, very skillfully managed. It must be borne in mind that in this,

[040-051] as in most of these tales, there are associations and chords which make

[040-052] as gold to an Indian that which is only copper, or at best silver, to

[040-053] the civilized reader of my translations.

[040-054] There is a characteristic feature of this story superior to anything in

[040-055] Undine. It is the growth in the hero, when he knows the worst to come,

[040-056] of that will, or stoicism, or complete indifference to fate, which the

[040-057] Indians regard as equivalent to attaining _m'teoulin_, or magic

[040-058] power. When a man has in him such courage that nothing earthly can do

[040-059] more than increase it, he has attained to what is in one sense at least

[040-060] _Nirvana_. From an Algonquin point of view the plot is perfect.

[040-061] I have given this story accurately as it was told to me by Tomah

[040-062] Josephs, a Passamaquoddy Indian.

[040-063] _How one of the Partridge's Wives became a Sheldrake Duck, and why

[040-064] her Feet and Feathers are Red._

[040-065] _N'karnayoo_, of the old time, there was a hunter who lived in the

[040-066] woods. He had a brother, [Footnote: The word brother is so generally

[040-067] applied in adoption or friendship that it cannot here be taken in a

[040-068] literal sense. The brother in this case seems to have been a goblin or

[040-069] spirit.] who was so small that he kept him in a box, and when he went

[040-070] forth he closed this very carefully, for fear lest an evil spirit

[040-071] (Mitche-hant) should get him.

[040-072] One day this hunter, returning, saw a very beautiful girl sitting on a

[040-073] rock by a river, making a moccasin. And being in a canoe he paddled up

[040-074] softly and silently to capture her; but she, seeing him coming, jumped

[040-075] into the water and disappeared. On returning to her mother, who lived

[040-076] at the bottom of the river, she was told to go back to the hunter and

[040-077] be his wife; "for now," said the mother, "you belong to that man."

[040-078] The hunter's name was Mitchihess, the Partridge. When she came to his

[040-079] lodge he was absent. So she arranged everything for his return, making

[040-080] a bed of boughs. At night he came back with one beaver. This he

[040-081] divided; cooked one half for supper and laid by the other half. In the

[040-082] morning when she awoke he was gone, and the other half of the beaver

[040-083] had also disappeared. That night he returned with another beaver, and

[040-084] the same thing took place again. Then she resolved to spy and find out

[040-085] what all this meant.

[040-086] So she laid down and went to sleep, wide awake, with one eye open. Then

[040-087] he quietly rose and cooked the half of the beaver, and taking a key

[040-088] (_Apkwosgehegan_, P.) unlocked a box, and took out a little red

[040-089] dwarf and fed him. Replacing the elf, he locked him up again, and lay

[040-090] down to sleep. And the small creature had eaten the whole half beaver.

[040-091] But ere he put him in his box he washed him and combed his hair, which

[040-092] seemed to delight him.

[040-093] The next morning, when her husband had gone for the day, the wife

[040-094] sought for the key, and having found it opened the box and called to

[040-095] the little fellow to come out. This he refused to do for a long time,

[040-096] though she promised to wash and comb him. Being at length persuaded, he

[040-097] peeped out, when she pulled him forth. But whenever she touched him her

[040-098] hands became red, though of this she took no heed, thinking she could

[040-099] wash it off at will. But lo! while combing him, there entered a hideous

[040-100] being, an awful devil, who caught the small elf from her and ran away.

[040-101] Then she was terribly frightened. And trying to wash her hands, the red

[040-102] stain remained. When her husband returned that night he had no game;

[040-103] when he saw the red stain he knew all that had happened; when he knew

[040-104] what had happened he seized his bow to beat her; when she saw him seize

[040-105] his bow to beat her she ran down to the river, and jumped in to escape

[040-106] death at his hands, though it should be by drowning. But as she fell

[040-107] into the water she became a sheldrake duck. And to this day the marks

[040-108] of the red stain are to be seen on her feet and feathers. [Footnote:

[040-109] Related to me by Noel Josephs, a Passamaquoddy. Notwithstanding its

[040-110] resemblance to Blue Beard, it is probably in every detail a very old

[040-111] Indian tradition. It bears a slight resemblance to several far western

[040-112] legends, which refer to peculiarities in the duck. It is partly

[040-113] repeated in a Lox legend.]

[040-114] THE INVISIBLE ONE.

[040-115] (Micmac.)

[040-116] There was once a large Indian village situated on the border of a

[040-117] lake,--_Nameskeek' oodun Kuspemku_ (M.). At the end of the place

[040-118] was a lodge, in which dwelt a being who was always invisible.

[040-119] [Footnote: In this Micmac tale, which is manifestly corrupted in many

[040-120] ways, the hero is said to be "a youth whose _teeomul_ (or tutelary

[040-121] animal) was the moose," whence he took his name. In the Passamaquoddy

[040-122] version nothing is said about a moose. A detailed account of the

[040-123] difficulty attending the proper analysis of this tradition will be

[040-124] found at the end of this chapter.] He had a sister who attended to his

[040-125] wants, and it was known that any girl who could see him might marry

[040-126] him. Therefore there were indeed few who did not make the trial, but it

[040-127] was long ere one succeeded:

[040-128] And it passed in this wise. Towards evening, when the Invisible One was

[040-129] supposed to be returning home, his sister would walk with any girls who

[040-130] came down to the shore of the lake. She indeed could see her brother,

[040-131] since to her he was always visible, and beholding him she would say to

[040-132] her companions, "Do you see my brother?" And then they would mostly

[040-133] answer, "Yes," though some said, "Nay,"--_alt telovejich, aa

[040-134] alttelooejik_. And then the sister would say, "_Cogoowa'

[040-135] wiskobooksich_?" "Of what is his shoulder-strap made?" But as some

[040-136] tell the tale, she would, inquire other things, such as, "What is his

[040-137] moose-runner's haul?" or, "With what does he draw his sled?" And they

[040-138] would reply, "A strip of rawhide," or "A green withe," or something of

[040-139] the kind. And then she, knowing they had not told the truth, would

[040-140] reply quietly, "Very well, let us return to the wigwam!"

[040-141] And when they entered the place she would bid them not to take a

[040-142] certain seat, for it was his. And after they had helped to cook the

[040-143] supper they would wait with great curiosity to see Him eat. Truly he

[040-144] gave proof that he was a real person, for as he took off his moccasins

[040-145] they became visible, and his sister hung them up; but beyond this they

[040-146] beheld nothing not even when they remained all night, as many did.

[040-147] There dwelt in the village an old man, a widower, with three daughters.

[040-148] The youngest of these was very small, weak, and often ill, which did

[040-149] not prevent her sisters, especially the eldest, treating her with great

[040-150] cruelty. The second daughter was kinder, and sometimes took the part of

[040-151] the poor abused little girl, but the other would burn her lands and

[040-152] face with hot coals; yes, her whole body was scarred with the marks

[040-153] made by torture, so that people called her _Oochigeaskw_ (the

[040-154] rough-faced girl). And when her father, coming home, asked what it

[040-155] meant that the child was so disfigured, her sister would promptly say

[040-156] that it was the fault of the girl, herself, for that, having been

[040-157] forbidden to go near the fire, she had disobeyed and fallen in.

[040-158] Now it came to pass that it entered the heads of the two elder sisters

[040-159] of this poor girl that they would go and try their fortune at seeing

[040-160] the Invisible One. So they clad themselves in their finest and strove

[040-161] to look their fairest; and finding his sister at home went with her to

[040-162] take the wonted walk down to the water. Then when He came, being asked

[040-163] if they saw him, they said, "Certainly," and also replied to the

[040-164] question of the shoulder-strap or sled cord, "A piece of rawhide." In

[040-165] saying which, they lied, like the rest, for they had seen nothing, and

[040-166] got nothing for their pains.
