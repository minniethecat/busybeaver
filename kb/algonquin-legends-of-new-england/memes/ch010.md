---[010-001] Then, to frighten the Beaver, Glooskap threw at it a few handfuls of

[010-002] earth, and these, falling somewhat to the eastward of Partridge Island,

[010-003] became the Five Islands. And the pond which was left was the Basin of

[010-004] Minas.

[010-005] And yet another tradition tells that, after cutting the dam, Glooskap

[010-006] sat and watched, but no beaver came out; [Footnote: This is the

[010-007] Anglo-Indian manuscript, already referred to.] for _Qwah-beet_

[010-008] had gone out of a back door. So he took a rock and threw it afar,

[010-009] [Footnote: "He took Rock tructed 150 miles ip River to sker beaber

[010-010] bock down, but beaber has gone ober granfalls."]--one hundred and

[010-011] fifty miles,--to scare the Beaver back again; but the Beaver had gone

[010-012] over the Grand Falls, and the stone remaineth there even to this day.

[010-013] _The Story of Glooskap as told in a few Words by a Woman of the

[010-014] Penobscots._

[010-015] "Glus-gahbe gave names to everything. He made men and gave them life,

[010-016] and made the winds to make the waters move. The Turtle was his uncle;

[010-017] the Mink, _Uk-see-meezel_, his adopted son; and _Monin-kwessos_,

[010-018] the Woodchuck, his grandmother. The Beaver built a great dam, and

[010-019] Glus-gahbe turned it away and killed the Beaver. At Moose-tchick he

[010-020] killed a moose; the bones may be seen at Bar Harbor turned to stone.

[010-021] He threw the entrails of the Moose across the bay to his dogs, and they,

[010-022] too, may be seen there to this day, as I myself have seen them; and there,

[010-023] too, in the rock are the prints of his bow and arrow." [Footnote: Many a

[010-024] place is pointed out as the locality of the same legend. In addition to

[010-025] those in New Brunswick and Bar Harbor, Thoreau found another in Maine,

[010-026] which he thus describes:--

[010-027] "While we were crossing this bay" (that is, the mouth of Moose River),

[010-028] "where Mount Kineo rose dark before us, within two or three miles, the

[010-029] Indian repeated the tradition respecting this mountain's having been

[010-030] anciently a cow-moose,--how a mighty Indian hunter, whose name I

[010-031] forget, succeeded in killing this queen of the moose tribe with great

[010-032] difficulty, while her calf was killed somewhere among the islands in

[010-033] Penobscot Bay; and to his eyes this mountain had still the form of the

[010-034] moose in a reclining posture, its precipitous side presenting the

[010-035] outline of her head. He told this at some length, though it did not

[010-036] amount to much, and with apparent good faith, and asked us how we

[010-037] supposed the hunter could have killed such a mighty moose as that; how

[010-038] we could do it. Whereupon a man-of-war to fire broadsides into her was

[010-039] suggested, etc. An Indian tells such a story as if he thought it

[010-040] deserved to have a good deal said about it, only he has not got it to

[010-041] say; and so he makes up for the deficiency by a drawling tone,

[010-042] long-windedness, and a dumb wonder which he hopes will be contagious."

[010-043] This concluding criticism is indeed singularly characteristic of Mr.

[010-044] Thoreau's own nasal stories about Nature, but it is as utterly untrue

[010-045] as ridiculous when applied to any Indian storytelling to which I have

[010-046] ever listened, and I have known the near relatives of the Indians of

[010-047] whom he speaks, and heard many of them tell their tales. This writer

[010-048] passed months in Maine, choosing Penobscot guides expressly to study

[010-049] them, to read Indian feelings and get at Indian secrets, and this

[010-050] account of Glooskap, whose name he forgets, is a fair specimen of what

[010-051] he learned. Yet he could in the same book write as follows: "The

[010-052] Anglo-American can indeed cut down and grub up all this waving forest,

[010-053] And make a stump and vote for Buchanan on its ruins; but he cannot

[010-054] Converse with the spirit of the tree he fells, he cannot read the

[010-055] poetry and mythology which retires as he advances."

[010-056] If Mr. Thoreau had known the Indian legend of the spirit of the fallen

[010-057] tree--and his guide knew it well--he might have been credited with

[010-058] speaking wisely of the poetry and _mythology_ which he ridicules

[010-059] the poor rural Yankees for not possessing.

[010-060] Such a writer can, indeed, peep and botanize on the grave of Mother

[010-061] Nature, but never evoke _her_ spirit.

[010-062] The moving the island is evidently of Eskimo origin, since Crantz

[010-063] (_History of Greenland_) heard nearly the same story of some

[010-064] magician-giant. It was probably suggested by the very common floating

[010-065] away of ice-islands.]

[010-066] _How Glooskap, leaving the World, all the Animals mourned for him,

[010-067] and how, ere he departed, he gave Gifts to Men._

[010-068] (Micmac.)

[010-069] Now Glooskap had freed the world from all the mighty monsters of an

[010-070] early time: the giants wandered no longer in the wilderness; the

[010-071] _cullo_ terrified man no more, as it spread its wings like the

[010-072] cloud between him and the sun; the dreadful Chenoo of the North

[010-073] devoured him not; no evil beasts, devils, and serpents were to be found

[010-074] near his home. And the Master had, moreover, taught men the arts which

[010-075] made them happier; but they were not grateful to him, and though they

[010-076] worshiped him they were not the less wicked.

[010-077] "Now when the ways of men and beasts waxed evil they greatly vexed

[010-078] Glooskap, and at length he could no longer endure them, and he made a

[010-079] rich feast by the shore of the great Lake Minas. All the beasts came to

[010-080] it, and when the feast was over he got into a great canoe, and the

[010-081] beasts looked after him till they saw him no more. And after they

[010-082] ceased to see him, they still heard his voice as he sang; but the

[010-083] sounds grew fainter and fainter in the distance, and at last they

[010-084] wholly died away; and then deep silence fell on them all, and a great

[010-085] marvel came to pass, and the beasts, who had till now spoken but one

[010-086] language, were no longer able to understand each other, and they fled

[010-087] away, each his own way, and never again have they met together in

[010-088] council. Until the day when Glooskap shall return to restore the Golden

[010-089] Age, and make men and animals dwell once more together in amity and

[010-090] peace, all Nature mourns. And tradition says that on his departure from

[010-091] Acadia the Great Snowy Owl retired to the deep forests, to return no

[010-092] more until he could come to welcome Glooskap; and in those sylvan

[010-093] depths the owls even yet repeat to the night _Koo-koo-skoos_!

[010-094] which is to say in the Indian tongue, 'Oh, I am sorry! Oh, I am sorry!'

[010-095] And the Loons, who had been the huntsmen of Glooskap, go restlessly up

[010-096] and down through the world, seeking vainly for their master, whom they

[010-097] cannot find, and wailing sadly because they find him not." [Footnote:

[010-098] This passage is one of seven on the subject of Glooskap, cited in

[010-099] Osgood's _Maritime Provinces_, without giving either the name of

[010-100] the author or the book from which they were taken.]

[010-101] But ere the Master went away from life, or ceased to wander in the ways

[010-102] of men, he bade it be made known by the Loons, his faithful messengers,

[010-103] that before his departure years would pass, and that whoever would seek

[010-104] him might have one wish granted, whatever that wish might be. Now,

[010-105] though the journey was long and the trials were terrible which those

[010-106] must endure who would find Glooskap, there were still many men who

[010-107] adventured them. [Footnote: There is a great embarrassment of riches,

[010-108] or rather a great wealth of embarrassment, as regards this chapter. In

[010-109] the Rand manuscript there are three histories of the adventures of the

[010-110] pilgrims who sought Glooskap. Another and very different was given to

[010-111] me by John Gabriel. In one account there are three travelers, in

[010-112] another four; others speak of seven and twelve. Finally, there are many

[010-113] incidents which apparently belong to this part of the Glooskap cycle,

[010-114] scattered here and there in different disconnected legends.

[010-115] Mrs. W. Wallace Brown was told by the Passamaquoddy Indians that when

[010-116] Glooskap departed he took with him the king of each of the different

[010-117] kinds of animals; so that the wolves, loons, etc., mourn not only for

[010-118] the lord, but for their masters.]

[010-119] Now ye shall hear who some of these were and what happened to them. And

[010-120] this is the first tale as it was told me in the tent of John Gabriel,

[010-121] the Passamaquoddy.

[010-122] When all men had heard that Glooskap would grant a wish to any one who

[010-123] would come to him, three Indians resolved to try this thing; and one

[010-124] was a Maliseet from St. John, and the other two were Penobscots from

[010-125] Old Town. And the path was long and the way was hard, and they suffered

[010-126] much, and they were seven years on it ere they came to him. But while

[010-127] they were yet three months' journey from his dwelling, they heard the

[010-128] barking of his dogs, and as they drew nearer, day by day, it was

[010-129] louder. And so, after great trials, they found the lord of men and

[010-130] beasts, and he made them welcome and entertained them.

[010-131] But, ere they went, he asked them what they wanted. And the eldest, who

[010-132] was an honest, simple man, and of but little account among his people,

[010-133] because he was a bad hunter, asked that he might excel in the killing

[010-134] and catching of game. Then the Master gave him a flute, or the magic

[010-135] pipe, which pleases every ear, and has the power of persuading every

[010-136] animal to follow him who plays it. And he thanked the lord, and left.

[010-137] Now the second Indian, being asked what he would have, replied, 'The

[010-138] love of many women.' And when Glooskap asked how many, he said, "I care

[010-139] not how many; so that there are but enough of them, and more than

[010-140] enough." At hearing this the Master seemed displeased, but, smiling

[010-141] anon, he gave him a bag which was tightly tied, and told him not to

[010-142] open it until he had reached his home. So he thanked the lord, and

[010-143] left.

[010-144] Now the third Indian was a gay and handsome but foolish young fellow,

[010-145] whose whole heart was set on making people laugh, and on winning a

[010-146] welcome at every merry-making. And he, being asked what he would have

[010-147] or what he chiefly wanted, said that it would please him most to be

[010-148] able to make a certain quaint and marvelous sound or noise, [Footnote:

[010-149] Pedere, crepitare.] which was frequent in those primitive times among

[010-150] all the Wabanaki, and which it is said may even yet be heard in a few

[010-151] sequestered wigwams far in the wilderness, away from men; there being

[010-152] still here and there a deep magician, or man of mystery, who knows the

[010-153] art of producing it. And the property of this wondrous sound is such

[010-154] that they who hear it must needs burst into a laugh; whence it is the

[010-155] cause that the men of these our modern times are so sorrowful, since

[010-156] that sound is no more heard in the land. And to him Glooskap was also

[010-157] affable, sending Marten into the woods to seek a certain mystical and

[010-158] magic root, which when eaten would make the miracle the young man

[010-159] sought. But he warned him not to touch the root ere he got to his home,

[010-160] or it would be the worse for him. And so he kindly thanked the lord,

[010-161] and left.

[010-162] It had taken seven years to come, but seven days were all that was

[010-163] required to tread the path returning to their home, that is, for him

[010-164] who got there. Only one of all the three beheld his lodge again. This

[010-165] was the hunter, who, with his pipe in his pocket, and not a care in his

[010-166] heart, trudged through the woods, satisfied that so long as he should

[010-167] live, there would always be venison in the larder.

[010-168] But he who loved women, and had never won even a wife, was filled with

[010-169] anxious wishfulness. And he had, not gone very far into the woods

[010-170] before he opened the bag. And there flew out by hundreds, like white

[010-171] doves, swarming all about him, beautiful girls, with black burning eyes

[010-172] and flowing hair. And wild with passion the winsome witches threw their

[010-173] arms about him, and kissed him as he responded to their embraces; but

[010-174] they came ever more and more, wilder and more passionate. And he bade

[010-175] them give way, but they would not, and he sought to escape, but he

[010-176] could not; and so panting, crying for breath, smothered, he perished.

[010-177] And those who came that way found him dead, but what became of the

[010-178] girls no man knows.
