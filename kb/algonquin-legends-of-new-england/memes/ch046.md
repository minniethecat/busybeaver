---[046-001] "'I will tell you,' replied the old man. 'Find a girl; the more

[046-002] beautiful she is and the more you want her, the stronger the magic will

[046-003] be. Go to the parents for their daughter as a wife. Cheat them so.

[046-004] Before you marry get seven bear-skins, and let no man except one know

[046-005] anything about it. Make him clean them. One skin should be cleaned

[046-006] every twenty-four hours. Seven days must pass so.'

[046-007] "The young man was accepted by the parents; he sent the seven bear-skins

[046-008] to the young woman; they were married; they went to their wigwam. He lay

[046-009] on the bear-skins; he directed his wife to make another bed and sleep on

[046-010] it. They lay apart. The bride thought this was strange; she told her

[046-011] mother of it. The mother said, 'Never mind. By and by it will be all

[046-012] right.' The wife thought it was all wrong. When seven nights had passed

[046-013] the bridegroom disappeared. He was not seen in his village for twenty-five

[046-014] or thirty years. Then he returned to his father. He could divine all

[046-015] things by dreams. He had but to take the magic bear-skin and sleep on

[046-016] it, and dream. He could tell where to find good hunting or fishing. He

[046-017] _foredreamed_ war with the Mohawks. Can any man do this? They say

[046-018] so, and I have known many who tried it in vain. They could not pass the

[046-019] trial successfully."

[046-020] "There are stones in the forest with names on them. They give great

[046-021] power to dream. I have seen in my dreams the _m'teoulin_ of

[046-022] ancient times,--the magicians, my father told me, of long ago. I have

[046-023] seen them diving under the waters from one island to another. I have

[046-024] seen them dive ten miles.

[046-025] "When I was, young, J. N., who was a great _m'teoulin_, offered to

[046-026] teach me the art. I could have become one, but I would not. I did not

[046-027] think it was right.

[046-028] "Once old J. N. and my grandfather hunted in the woods. It was near

[046-029] Katahdin, the Great Mountain. [Footnote: Katahdin, like the Intervale

[046-030] near North Conway, is haunted and enchanted ground, abounding in

[046-031] fairies and other marvelous beings. But there is not a mile square of

[046-032] New England which has not its legends.] And they wanted everything.

[046-033] They had got out of everything. One night old N. said, 'I can bear this

[046-034] no longer. Would you like a nice pipe of tobacco? We have had nothing

[046-035] but meat for four weeks.' So he went away for a short time; perhaps it

[046-036] was an hour. He returned with a box. There was in it three pounds of

[046-037] tobacco; there was cheese, rice, and sugar; there was fifty pounds of

[046-038] provision in all."

[046-039] This famous _m'teoulin_ was long a popular governor of the

[046-040] Passamaquoddies. I have a curious old brass candlestick, said to be one

[046-041] hundred and fifty years old, which he owned all his life. The following

[046-042] remarkable reminiscences of this very clever old sagamore were given to

[046-043] me by Marie Sakis, a Penobscot:

[046-044] "The old governor was a great _m'teoulin_. He had got it among the

[046-045] Chippewas. He said that it would come to pass that he would die before

[046-046] the next snow-storm. No, he did not care himself, but my husband's

[046-047] mother did, when she heard this, and she cried. Then he said, 'Well, I

[046-048] will try to live, or else die in a month; but it will be a hard fight.'

[046-049] So he made him a bow, and strung it with his wife's hair; [Footnote: In

[046-050] a Chippewa legend a boy confers magic power on a bow by stringing it

[046-051] with his sister's hair.] and having done this, he shot an arrow through

[046-052] the smoke-hole of his wigwam. [Footnote: This is also mentioned in a

[046-053] legend where it is said that every arrow killed a supernatural enemy.]

[046-054] All this was at Nessaik, near Eastport. Then he said to his wife, 'Take

[046-055] one of your leggins and put it on my head.' She did so. Then he took

[046-056] medicine. A rainbow appeared in the sky, and a great horse-fly came out

[046-057] of his mouth, and then a large grasshopper. He cried to his wife, 'Do

[046-058] not kill it!' And then came a stone spear-head. [Footnote: This is all

[046-059] in detail perfectly Shamanic. The smell of the fresh fish after such a

[046-060] fight is the same in an Eskimo legend. The horse-fly (_gan_) is

[046-061] Lapp.]

[046-062] 'Now,' said the governor, 'this is all right so far, but the great

[046-063] struggle is yet to come. It is a _wee-wilmekq'_ who has done

[046-064] this.' (You know what that is: the Passamaquoddies call it

[046-065] _weewilmekq'_. It is a worm an inch long, which can make itself

[046-066] into a horrid monster as large as a deer; yes, and much larger. It is

[046-067] _m'teoulin_; yes, it is a great magician.) 'I am going to fight

[046-068] it. You must come with a small stick to hit it once, and only a mere

[046-069] tap.' [Footnote: In the legend of Partridge, a mere tap stuns the

[046-070] water-fairy.] But she would not go. So he went and fought with the

[046-071] Weewillmekq'. He killed it. It was a frightful battle. When he returned

[046-072] he smelt like fresh fish. His wife bade him go and wash himself; but

[046-073] let him bathe as much as he could, the smell remained for days. The

[046-074] pond where he fought has been muddy, and foul ever since.

[046-075] The governor could with a gimlet bore a hole in any tree in the woods,

[046-076] and draw from it as he pleased; any kind of wine or other liquor. Once

[046-077] he was far in the forest with some white gentlemen; he wished to

[046-078] entertain them. He did this, to their astonishment. He produced tobacco

[046-079] in a miraculous manner when it was wanted. Then, returning to Eastport,

[046-080] he went to Mr. Pearce, who kept a store, and showed him that a certain

[046-081] amount of wine had disappeared from his barrels, and paid him for it.

[046-082] He never drank wine or spirits himself.

[046-083] He once went hunting. He took his wife with him; she was

[046-084] _enceinte_. It was in midwinter. She had a great yearning for

[046-085] green corn. He put a dish on the ground, and there fell from above ears

[046-086] of fresh-boiled green corn into it. 'There,' said he, 'as I promised,

[046-087] you have it.'

[046-088] She had a silver cross and beads. One day she lost it, and grieved

[046-089] very much. He said, 'Put that wooden dish upside down, near the fire.'

[046-090] It was done, and when she turned it up the cross was under the dish.

[046-091] And he said the Ketawks, or Spirits, had brought it."

[046-092] The following legend, told me by Tomah Josephs, sets forth another

[046-093] manner by which _m'teoulin_ may be acquired.

[046-094] "There were two Indian families camped away at some distance from the

[046-095] main village. In one lived a young man, and every night he would go to

[046-096] the other wigwams to see some girls. His mother warned him that he

[046-097] would come to harm, for there was danger abroad, but he never minded

[046-098] her.

[046-099] Now, one night at the end of winter, when the ground was bare of snow,

[046-100] as he was walking along he heard something come after. It had a very

[046-101] heavy, steady tramp. He stopped, and saw a long figure, white, but

[046-102] without arms or legs. It looked like a corpse rolled up. He was

[046-103] horribly frightened, but when it attacked him he grew angry. The

[046-104] object, though it had no arms, fought madly. It twined round him; it

[046-105] struck itself against him, and thrashed itself, bending like a fish all

[046-106] about. And he, too, fought as if he was crazy. He was one of those

[046-107] whose blood and courage go up, but never down; he could die, but never

[046-108] give in till dead. Before daylight the Ghost suggested a rest, or

[046-109] peace; the Indian would not hear of it, but fought on. The Ghost began

[046-110] to implore mercy, but the youth just then saw in the north _Kival lo

[046-111] kesso_, the break of day. Then he knew that if he could but endure

[046-112] the battle a little longer he should indeed get a great victory.

[046-113] Then the Ghost implored him, saying, 'Let me go, and whatever you may want

[046-114] you shall get, and good luck all your life.' Yet for all this he would not

[046-115] yield, for he knew that by conquering he would win all the Spirit had to

[046-116] give. And as the first sun-ray shone on him he became insensible, and when

[046-117] he awoke it was as from a sleep. But by his side lay a large, old, decayed

[046-118] log, covered with moss. He remembered that during the fight he had seemed

[046-119] once to plunge his fist, by a violent blow, completely into the enemy up

[046-120] to his elbow, and there was a hole in it corresponding to this wound. He

[046-121] had torn away the other's scalp-lock, stripping the skin down to the

[046-122] waist; he found a long, hairy-looking piece of moss ripped from the end

[046-123] of the log to the middle. And all about lay pieces of moss and locks of

[046-124] his own hair, testifying to the fury of the fight.

[046-125] He was terribly bruised and torn, but that he did not heed, for now he

[046-126] was another man, and a terrible one. His mother said, 'I warned you of

[046-127] danger:' but he had conquered the danger. He had all the strength of

[046-128] five strong men, and all the might and magic of the Spirit; yes, the

[046-129] Spirit itself was now in him. After this he could do anything, and find

[046-130] game where no one else could. To conquer a ghost gives power."

[046-131] To conquer the dead, or to fight terrible spirits, to thereby absorb

[046-132] their power, and finally to keep them in a struggle until the day

[046-133] shines on them, is both Norse and Celtic, if not, indeed, world-wide.

[046-134] But the grim spirit of this narrative is Norse; it is that of the hero

[046-135] wresting from a corpse's hold the sword of victory.

[046-136] "Farewell, daughter!

[046-137] Fleet give I thee,

[046-138] Five men's bane,

[046-139] If thou it believe."

[046-140] But the great element or chief cause of magic power among, the Indians

[046-141] is that of Will. It manifests itself in many forms, mere courage being

[046-142] one. Thus the _Weewillmekq'_ confers supernatural ability or other

[046-143] favors only on those who are not afraid of it. The demon Log, as we

[046-144] have just seen, gives strength and prosperity to a man for simply

[046-145] fighting like a bull-dog. Beyond courage, pluck or bottom is with these

[046-146] Indians as nearly allied to magic as poetry was among the Greeks, or

[046-147] with an Eschenwaya. When the true magician "gets mad," and continues to

[046-148] get madder till the end, he is invincible. Allied to this is

[046-149] perseverance. The Rabbit is rewarded with skill as an enchanter merely

[046-150] for continuing to try. His very failures have this in them, that he

[046-151] keeps on resolutely, though in a wrong road. No one can fail to be

[046-152] struck, in these legends of the Northeast Algonquins, how often a boy,

[046-153] or adult, when asked if he can do a difficult thing, replies, "I can

[046-154] try." All of this apotheosis of pluck, perseverance, and patience is

[046-155] _far_ more developed among these legends than in those of the

[046-156] Chippewas or other western and southern tribes, at least so far as I am

[046-157] familiar with them. It exists wherever there are red Indians, but the

[046-158] Eastern Algonquin seems to have thought it out more and made more of it

[046-159] than others have done. Therefore his cycle of myths, or his Edda,

[046-160] occupies a higher place. It is less chaotic; it is more consistent; it

[046-161] is a chorus in which every voice is trained to respond to or correspond

[046-162] with the leader. In this respect it has a remarkable resemblance to the

[046-163] Scandinavian myths and poems. In its theory that magic power may be

[046-164] obtained by "penitence,"--I do not mean here "repentance,"--that is by

[046-165] self-inflicted pain, it agrees with the Hindoo, and in fact more or

[046-166] less with all religions. But it is only, I believe, in the red Indian

[046-167] and Hindoo creeds that it is distinctly admitted that man can attain

[046-168] the power to do both good and evil, or whatever he pleases, if he will

[046-169] only pay for it by suffering. The doctrine of power through penance is

[046-170] so simple and obvious in its origin that it would long precede

[046-171] monotheism. A man exercises himself with great exertion in lifting

[046-172] stones, as in an Eskimo tale, till he is strong; he practices shooting

[046-173] arrows and running after them, as in the story of the Chief's Son, till

[046-174] he can outrun them. Then the secret of such marvelous deeds is supposed

[046-175] to exist in the bow, and it becomes a fetich.
