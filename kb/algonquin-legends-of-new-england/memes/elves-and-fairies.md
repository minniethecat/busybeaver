# How Glooskap Made the Elves and Fairies

## Narrative

Glooskap came first of all into this country — into Nova Scotia, Maine, Canada, into the land of the Wabanaki, next to sunrise. There were no Indians here then, only wild Indians very far to the west (004-046).

The first beings Glooskap brought forth were the **Mikumwess** — the Oonabgemessuk, the small Elves, little men, dwellers in rocks (004-049). These Mikumwess were the earliest race, beings of the forest and stone, neither fully spirit nor fully flesh. What gave a fairy, an elf, its nature — this was among the first mysteries that Glooskap established in the world (002-150). The water fairies, too, were of the fairy race, who dwell in deep water (020-039). Among these were the Oonahgemessuk who inhabited Katahdin and the spring called Oonahgemessuk k'tubbee — the Water Fairies' Spring (018-009).

After making the Elves and Fairies, Glooskap created Man. He took his bow and arrows and shot at trees — the basket-trees, the Ash. Then Indians came out of the bark of the Ash-trees (004-052). This is why the Ash is sacred to the Wabanaki: from its bark humanity itself was born.

Then Glooskap made all the animals. He made them at first very large — so large that the Moose was as tall as Ketawkqu's, a giant high as the tallest pines or as the clouds (004-057). When Glooskap asked the Moose what he would do should he see an Indian coming, Moose replied, "I would tear down the trees on him." Then Glooskap saw that the Moose was too strong, and made him smaller, so that Indians could kill him (004-061). Thus Glooskap ordered the world, adjusting the size and power of each creature until a balance was struck between men and beasts.

## The Nature of the Elves

The Mikumwess are not merely diminutive humans — they possess magical powers that set them apart. They can lend their magic to worthy mortals, as when the Mikumwess gave his magic pipe to a young man seeking a wife, enabling him to overcome supernatural trials (013-004). They appear as helpers throughout the Glooskap cycle, often aiding the hero or testing mortals. The water fairies, meanwhile, dwell beneath the surface of lakes and springs, and their fairy power resides in their apparel — if one can capture a water fairy's garment, one may compel her to wed (020-086).

## Cultural Context

Excluding French Canadian fairy tales, which entered the tradition through colonial contact, the indigenous Algonquin fairy lore represents a deeply rooted element of the Northeastern woodland cosmology (003-113). The Mikumwess occupy a middle ground between the great culture hero Glooskap and ordinary humans — they are the "little people" of the forest, analogous to similar beings in European folklore yet distinctly Algonquin in character.

The sequence of creation — Elves first, then Man from the Ash, then the beasts — establishes an ontological hierarchy. The spirit-like beings precede material humanity, and the animals, made last, are subject to human dominion after Glooskap's adjustments. This cosmogony places humanity at the center of creation while acknowledging older, more elemental races that continue to inhabit the margins of the world.
