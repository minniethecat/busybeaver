---[005-001] Finding that his brother continually thwarted him, the Good Mind

[005-002] admonished him to behave better. The Bad Mind then offered a challenge

[005-003] to his brother, on condition that the victor should rule the universe.

[005-004] The Good Mind was willing. He falsely mentioned that whipping with

[005-005] flags [bulrushes] would destroy his _temporal_ life, and earnestly

[005-006] solicited his brother to observe the instrument of death, saying that

[005-007] by using deer-horns he would expire. [This is very obscure in Cusick's

[005-008] Indian-English.] On the day appointed the battle began; it lasted for

[005-009] two days; they tore up the trees and mountains; at last the Good Mind

[005-010] gained the victory by using the horns. The last words uttered by the

[005-011] Bad Mind were that he would have equal power over the souls of mankind

[005-012] after their death, and so sank down to eternal doom and became the Evil

[005-013] Spirit."

[005-014] Contrasted with this hardly heathen cosmogony, which shows recent Bible

[005-015] influence throughout, the Algonquin narrative reads like a song from

[005-016] the Edda. That the latter is the original and the older there can be no

[005-017] doubt. Between the "Good Mind," making man "from the dust of the

[005-018] earth," and Glooskap, rousing him by magic arrows from the ash-tree,

[005-019] there is a great difference. It may be observed that the fight with

[005-020] horns is explained in another legend in this book, called the Chenoo,

[005-021] and that these horns are the magic horns of the Chepitch calm, or Great

[005-022] Serpent, who is somewhat like the dragon.

[005-023] In the Algonquin story, two Loons are Glooskap's "tale-bearers," which

[005-024] occasion him great anxiety by their prolonged absences. This is

[005-025] distinctly stated in the Indian legend, as it is of Odin's birds in the

[005-026] Edda. Odin has, as news-bringers, two ravens.

[005-027] "Hugin and Munin

[005-028] Fly each day

[005-029] over the spacious earth.

[005-030] I fear for Hugin

[005-031] that he comes not back,

[005-032] yet more anxious am I

[005-033] for Munin."

[005-034] The Loons, indeed, occasioned Glooskap so much trouble by absences that

[005-035] he took wolves in their place. The ravens of the Edda are probably of

[005-036] biblical origin. But it is a most extraordinary coincidence that the

[005-037] Indians have a corresponding perversion of Scripture, for they say that

[005-038] Glooskap, when he was in the ark, that is as Noah, sent out a white

[005-039] dove, which returned to him colored black, and became a raven. This is

[005-040] not, however, related as part of the myth.

[005-041] The Ancient History of the Six Nations, by David Cusick, gives us in

[005-042] one particular a strange coincidence with the Edda. It tells us that

[005-043] the Bad Mind, the principle of Evil, forced himself out into life, as

[005-044] Cusick expresses it in his broken Indian-English, "under the side of

[005-045] the parent's arm;" that is, through the armpit. In the Edda

[005-046] (Vafthrudnismal, 33) we are told of the first beings born on earth that

[005-047] they were twins, begotten by the two feet of a giant, and born out of

[005-048] his armpit.

[005-049] "Under the armpit grew,

[005-050] 't is said of the Hrimthurs,

[005-051] a girl and boy together;

[005-052] foot with foot begat,

[005-053] of that wise Jotun,

[005-054] a six-headed son."

[005-055] There are in these six lines six coincidences with red Indian

[005-056] mythology: (1.) The Evil principle as a Jotun's first-born in the one

[005-057] and the Bad Mind in the other are born of the mother's armpit. (2.) In

[005-058] one of the tales of Lox, the Indian devil, also a giant, we are told

[005-059] that his feet are male and female. (3.) In both faiths this is the

[005-060] first birth on earth. (4.) The six-headed demon appears in a Micmac

[005-061] tale. (5.) There is in both the Eddaic and the Wabanaki account a very

[005-062] remarkable coincidence in this: that there is a Titanic or giant birth

[005-063] of twins on earth, followed by the creation of man from the ash-tree.

[005-064] (6.) The Evil principle, whether it be the Wolf-Lox, in the Wabanaki

[005-065] myths, or Loki in the Norse, often turns himself into a woman. Thus the

[005-066] male and female sex of the first-born twins is identified.

[005-067] According to the Edda, the order of births on earth was as follows:--

[005-068] First, two giants were born from the mother's armpit.

[005-069] Secondly, the dwarfs were created.

[005-070] Thirdly, man was made from the ash-tree.

[005-071] According to the Wabanaki, this was the order:--

[005-072] First, two giants were born, _one_ from his mother's armpit.

[005-073] Secondly, the dwarfs (Mikumwessuk) were created from the bark of the

[005-074] ash-tree.

[005-075] Thirdly, man was made from the _trunk_ of the ash.

[005-076] The account of the creation of the dwarfs is wanting in the present

[005-077] manuscript.

[005-078] _Of the Great Deeds which Glooskap did for Men; how he named the

[005-079] Animals, and who they were that formed his Family._

[005-080] (Passamaquoddy.)

[005-081] _Woodenit atbk-hagen Gloosekap_: [Footnote: Passamaquoddy.] this

[005-082] is a story of Glooskap. It is told in traditions of the old time that

[005-083] Glooskap was born in the land of the Wabanaki, which is nearest to the

[005-084] sunrise; but another story says that he came over the sea in a great

[005-085] stone canoe, and that this canoe was an island of granite covered with

[005-086] trees. When the great man, of all men and beasts chief ruler, had come

[005-087] down from this ark, he went among the Wabanaki. [Footnote: This part of

[005-088] the legend is from a very singular and I may add almost unintelligible

[005-089] manuscript, _Storey about Glooscap_, written in English by a

[005-090] Passamaquoddy Indian. The word _ark_ which occurs in it reminds me

[005-091] that the Indian from whom I obtained it once asked me if I did not

[005-092] think that Glooskap was the same as Noah. This sentence is as follows

[005-093] in the Indian-English of the original: "Gloosecap hat left from ark

[005-094] come crosse even wiht wabnocelel."] And calling all the animals he gave

[005-095] them each a name: unto the Bear, _mooin_; and asked him what he

[005-096] would do if he should meet with a man. The Bear said, "I fear him, and

[005-097] I should run." Now in those days the Squirrel (_mi-ko_) was

[005-098] greater than the Bear. Then Glooskap took him in his hands, and

[005-099] smoothing him down he grew smaller and smaller, till he became as we

[005-100] see him now. In after-days the Squirrel was Glooskap's dog, and when he

[005-101] so willed, grew large again and slew his enemies, however fierce they

[005-102] might be. But this time, when asked what he would do should he meet

[005-103] with a man, Mi-ko replied, "I should run up a tree."

[005-104] Then the Moose, being questioned, answered, standing still and looking

[005-105] down, "I should run through the woods." And so it was with Kwah-beet

[005-106] the Beaver, [Footnote: This is very obscure in the original manuscript.

[005-107] It reads "Herask beaber did do anything just look behager."] and

[005-108] Glooskap saw that of all created beings the first and greatest was Man.

[005-109] Before men were instructed by him, they lived in darkness; it was so

[005-110] dark that they could not even see to slay their enemies. [Footnote:

[005-111] This was read to me by an Indian from a wampum record, now kept at

[005-112] Sebayk. I do not think I am mistaken in the phrase. It probably refers

[005-113] to ignorance of warlike weapons.] Glooskap taught them how to hunt, and

[005-114] to build huts and canoes and weirs for fish. Before he came they knew

[005-115] not how to make weapons or nets. He the Great Master showed them the

[005-116] hidden virtues of plants, roots, and barks, and pointed out to them

[005-117] such vegetables as might be used for food, as well as what kinds of

[005-118] animals, birds, and fish were to be eaten. And when this was done he

[005-119] taught them the names of all the stars. He loved mankind, and wherever

[005-120] he might be in the wilderness he was never very far from any of the

[005-121] Indians. He dwelt in a lonely land, but whenever they sought him they

[005-122] found him. [Footnote: This is from the Rand manuscript. The writer

[005-123] remarks that these expressions were the very words of a Micmac Indian

[005-124] named Stephen Flood, "who had no idea that he was using almost the

[005-125] identical expressions of Holy Writ with reference to God."] He traveled

[005-126] far and wide: there is no place in all the land of the Wabanaki where

[005-127] he left not his name; hills, rocks and rivers, lakes and islands, bear

[005-128] witness to him.

[005-129] Glooskap was never married, yet as he lived like other men he lived not

[005-130] alone. There dwelt with him an old woman, who kept his lodge; he called

[005-131] her Noogumee, "my grandmother." (Micmac.) With her was a youth named

[005-132] Abistariaooch, or the Martin. (M.) And Martin could change himself to a

[005-133] baby or a little boy, a youth or a young man, as befitted the time in

[005-134] which he was to act; for all things about Glooskap were very wonderful.

[005-135] This Martin ate always from a small birch-bark dish, called

[005-136] _witch-kwed-lakun-cheech_ (M.), and when he left this anywhere

[005-137] Glooskap was sure to find it, and could tell from its appearance all that

[005-138] had befallen his family. And Martin was called by Glooskap Uch-keen (M.),

[005-139] "my younger brother." The Lord of men and beasts had a belt which gave

[005-140] him magical power and endless strength. And when he lent this to

[005-141] Martin, the younger brother could also do great deeds, such as were

[005-142] only done in old times.

[005-143] Martin lived much with the Mikumwess or Elves, or Fairies, and is said

[005-144] to have been one of them.

[005-145] _How Win-pe the Sorcerer, having stolen Glooskap's Family, was by him

[005-146] pursued, and how, Glooskap for a Merry Jest cheated the Whale. Of the

[005-147] Song of the Clams, and how the Whale smoked a Pipe._

[005-148] (Micmac.)

[005-149] _N'kah-ne-oo_. In old times (P.), in the beginning of things, men

[005-150] were as animals and animals as men; how this was, no one knows. But it

[005-151] is told that all were at first men, and as they gave themselves up to

[005-152] this and that desire, and to naught else, they became beasts. But

[005-153] before this came to pass, they could change to one or the other form;

[005-154] yet even as men there was always something which showed what they were.

[005-155] Now Glooskap lived on an island named Aja-lig-un-mechk, and with him

[005-156] were many Indians with the names and natures of animals and birds.

[005-157] These men, but most of all Pulowech, the Partridge, having acquired

[005-158] power themselves, became jealous of Glooskap, and made up their minds

[005-159] to depart when he was away, taking with them Martin and the

[005-160] grandmother. For they had great hope that Glooskap, being left alone on

[005-161] the island, would perish, because they knew not his power. There is

[005-162] another story which says that he was living at the mouth of the

[005-163] Oolostook, at a place called Menogwes (St. John, N. B.), and went away

[005-164] into the forest as far as Goolwahgik (Juan), and had been gone six

[005-165] weeks, when he returned home and found the old woman, whose name was

[005-166] Mooinarkw, [Footnote: Mr. Rand translates this Micmac word as Mrs.

[005-167] Bear.] and Martin had been taken away. Following their tracks to the

[005-168] shore he saw one of his greatest enemies, a terrible sorcerer named

[005-169] Win-pe, just pushing off in his canoe. And with him were his wife and

[005-170] child and Dame Bear and Martin. They were still within call, and

[005-171] Glooskap cried from the shore to the grandmother to send back his dogs,

[005-172] which were not larger than mice, and, as some stories tell us, were

[005-173] squirrels. So she took a _woltes-takun_, which is a small wooden

[005-174] platter, and on such Indian dice are tossed. This she put in the water,

[005-175] and placed the dogs on it, and it floated to the shore, and Glooskap

[005-176] took it up. Win-pe with his family and prisoners pushed on to

[005-177] Passamoogwaddy (M.), and thence to Grand Manan; and after remaining

[005-178] there a while he crossed over to Kes-poog-itk (Yarmouth), and so went

[005-179] slowly along the southern coast through Oona-mahgik (Cape Breton), and

[005-180] over to Uktukkamkw (Newfoundland), where he was slain.

[005-181] Now whether it was to gain magical power, or to weaken that of Win-pe,

[005-182] or to chasten the others by suffering, who knows? But Glooskap rested

[005-183] seven years alone before he pursued the enemy, though some say it was

[005-184] seven months. And when the time had come, he took his dogs and went to

[005-185] the shore, and looked far out to sea over the waves, and sang the magic

[005-186] song which the whales obey. [Footnote: In the _Tales and Traditions

[005-187] of the Eskimo_, by Dr. Henry Rink, we are told in the story of

[005-188] Akigsiak that an old man taught the hero a magic lay for luring a whale

[005-189] to him. In another, Katersparsuk sings such a song to the walrus.] Soon

[005-190] there rose in the distance a small whale, who had heard the call, and

[005-191] came to Glooskap; but he was then very great, and he put one foot on

[005-192] the whale to test his weight, and the fish sank under him. So he sent

[005-193] it away.
