---[029-001] "But what on earth ails the fellow?" said the Otter to the grandmother,

[029-002] who was looking on in amazement.

[029-003] "Well, he has seen somebody do something, and is trying to do likewise,"

[029-004] replied the old lady.

[029-005] "Ho! come out of that now," cried the Otter, "and hand me your

[029-006] _nabogun_!" And the poor Rabbit, shivering with cold, and almost

[029-007] frozen, came from the water and limped into the lodge. And there he

[029-008] required much nursing from his grandmother, while the Otter, plunging

[029-009] into the stream, soon returned with a load of fish. But, disgusted at

[029-010] the Rabbit for attempting what he could not perform, he threw them down

[029-011] as a gift, and went home without tasting the meal.

[029-012] _II. How Mahtigwess, the Rabbit dined with the Woodpecker Girls, and

[029-013] was again humbled by trying to rival them._

[029-014] Now Master Rabbit, though disappointed, was not discouraged, for this

[029-015] one virtue he had, that he never gave up. [Footnote: It will be seen in

[029-016] the end that this great Indian virtue of never giving in eventually

[029-017] raised Rabbit to power and prosperity. _Il y a de morale ici_.]

[029-018] And wandering one day in the wilderness, he found a wigwam well filled

[029-019] with young women, all wearing red head-dresses; and no wonder, for they

[029-020] were Woodpeckers. Now, Master Rabbit was a well-bred Indian, who made

[029-021] himself as a melody to all voices, and so he was cheerfully bidden to

[029-022] bide to dinner, which he did. Then one of the red-polled pretty girls,

[029-023] taking a _woltes_, or wooden dish, lightly climbed a tree, so that

[029-024] she seemed to run; and while ascending, stopping here and there and

[029-025] tapping now and then, took from this place and that many of those

[029-026] insects called by the Indians _apchel-moal-timpkawal_, or rice,

[029-027] because they so much resemble it. And note that this rice is a dainty

[029-028] dish for those who like it. And when it was boiled, and they had dined,

[029-029] Master Rabbit again reflected, "La! how easily some folks live! What is

[029-030] to hinder me from doing the same? Ho, you girls! come over and dine

[029-031] with me the day after to-morrow!"

[029-032] And having accepted this invitation, all the guests came on the day

[029-033] set, when Master Rabbit undertook to play woodpecker. So having taken

[029-034] the head of an eel-spear and fastened it to his nose to make a bill, he

[029-035] climbed as well as he could--and bad was the best--up a tree, and tried

[029-036] to get his harvest of rice. Truly he got none; only in this did he

[029-037] succeed in resembling a Woodpecker, that he had a red poll; for his

[029-038] pate was all torn and bleeding, bruised by the fishing-point. And the

[029-039] pretty birds all looked and laughed, and wondered what the Rabbit was

[029-040] about.

[029-041] "Ah!" said his grandmother, "I suppose he is trying again to do

[029-042] something which he has seen some one do. 'T is just like him."

[029-043] "Oh, come down there!" cried Miss Woodpecker, as well as she could for

[029-044] laughing. "Give me your dish!" And having got it she scampered up the

[029-045] trunk, and soon brought down a dinner. But it was long ere Master

[029-046] Rabbit heard the last of it from these gay tree-tappers.

[029-047] _III. Of the Adventure with Mooin, the Bear; it being the Third and

[029-048] Last Time that Master Rabbit made a Fool of himself._

[029-049] Now, truly, one would think that after all that had befallen Master

[029-050] _Mahtigwess_, the Rabbit, that he would have had enough of trying

[029-051] other people's trades; but his nature was such that, having once set

[029-052] his mighty mind to a thing, little short of sudden death would cure

[029-053] him. And being one day with the Bear in his cave, he beheld with great

[029-054] wonder how _Mooin_ fed his folk. For, having put a great pot on

[029-055] the fire, he did but cut a little slice from his own foot and drop it

[029-056] into the boiling water, when it spread and grew into a mess of meat

[029-057] which served for all. [Footnote: Mr. Rand observes that this is

[029-058] evidently an allusion to the bear's being supposed to live during the

[029-059] winter by sucking his own paws.] Nay, there was a great piece given to

[029-060] Rabbit to take home to feed his family.

[029-061] "Now, truly," he said, "this is a thing which I can indeed do. Is it

[029-062] not recorded in the family wampum that whatever a Bear can do well a

[029-063] Rabbit can do better? "So, in fine, he invited his friend to come and

[029-064] dine with him, _Ketkewopk'_, the day after to-morrow.

[029-065] And the Bear being there, Rabbit did but say, "_Noogume' kuesawal'

[029-066] wohu_!" "Grandmother, set your pot to boiling!" And, whetting his

[029-067] knife on a stone, he tried to do as the Bear had done; but little did

[029-068] he get from his small, thin soles, though he cut himself madly and

[029-069] sadly.

[029-070] "What can he be trying to do?" growled the guest.

[029-071] "Ah!" sighed the grandmother, "something which he has seen some one

[029-072] else do."

[029-073] "Ho! I say there! Give me the knife," quoth Bruin. And, getting it, he

[029-074] took a slice from his sole, which did him no harm, and then, what with

[029-075] magic and fire, gave them a good dinner. But Master Rabbit was in sad

[029-076] case, and it was many a day ere he got well.

[029-077] _IV. Relating how the Rabbit became Wise by being Original, and of

[029-078] the Terrible Tricks which he by Magic played Loup-Cervier, the Wicked

[029-079] Wild Cat._

[029-080] There are men who are bad at copying, yet are good originals, and of

[029-081] this kind was Master Rabbit, who, when he gave up trying to do as

[029-082] others did, succeeded very well. And, having found out his foible, he

[029-083] applied himself to become able in good earnest, and studied

[029-084] _m'teoulin_, or magic, so severely that in time he grew to be an

[029-085] awful conjurer, so that he could raise ghosts, crops, storms, or devils

[029-086] whenever he wanted them. [Footnote: The three previous chapters of the

[029-087] Rabbit legend are from the Micmac. The rest is Passamaquoddy, as told

[029-088] by Tomah Josephs, who in his narration not only often interpolated

[029-089] jocose remarks, but was wont to ejaculate "By Jolly!" especially in the

[029-090] most striking scenes. I think that with him the interjection had become

[029-091] refined and dignified.] For he had perseverance, and out of this may

[029-092] come anything, if it be only brought into the right road.

[029-093] Now it came to pass that Master Rabbit got into great trouble. The

[029-094] records of the Micmacs say that it was from his stealing a string of

[029-095] fish from the Otter, who pursued him; but the Passamaquoddies declare

[029-096] that he was innocent of this evil deed, probably because they make

[029-097] great account of him as their ancestor and as the father of the

[029-098] Wabanaki. Howbeit, this is the way in which they tell the tale.

[029-099] Now the Rabbit is the natural prey of the Loup-Cervier, or Lusifee, who

[029-100] is a kind of wild cat, none being more obstinate. And this Wild Cat

[029-101] once went hunting with a gang of wolves, and they got nothing. Then

[029-102] Wild Cat, who had made them great promises and acted as chief, became

[029-103] angry, and, thinking of the Rabbit, promised them that this time they

[029-104] should indeed get their dinner. So he took them to Rabbit's wigwam; but

[029-105] he was out, and the Wolves, being vexed and starved, reviled Wild Cat,

[029-106] and then rushed off howling through the woods.

[029-107] Now I think that the Rabbit is _m'teoulin_. Yes, he must be, for

[029-108] when Wild Cat started to hunt him alone, he determined with all his

[029-109] soul not to be caught, and made himself as magical as he could. So he

[029-110] picked up a handful of chips, and threw one as far as possible, then

[029-111] jumped to it,--for he had a charm for a long jump; and then threw

[029-112] another, and so on, for a great distance. This was to make no tracks,

[029-113] and when he thought he had got out of scent and sight and sound he

[029-114] scampered away like the wind.

[029-115] [Illustration: THE RABBIT MAGICIAN.]

[029-116] Now, as I said, when the wolves got to Master Rabbit's house and found

[029-117] nothing, they smelt about and left Wild Cat, who swore by his tail that

[029-118] he would catch Rabbit, if he had to hunt forever and run himself to

[029-119] death. So, taking the house for a centre, he kept going round and round

[029-120] it, all the time a little further, and so more around and still further.

[029-121] [Footnote: While telling this, Tomah described a spiral line. It is

[029-122] evident that if the volute were only continued long enough it must

[029-123] inevitably end in finding any trail, if the point of departure be only

[029-124] known. This device is familiar to all Indians, and it is mentioned in

[029-125] other stories.] Then at last having found the track, he went in hot haste

[029-126] after Mr. Rabbit. And both ran hard, till, night coming on, Rabbit, to

[029-127] protect himself, had only just time _to trample down the snow a little,

[029-128] and stick up a spruce twig on end and sit on it_. But when Wild Cat

[029-129] came up he found there a fine wigwam, and put his head in. All that he

[029-130] saw was an old man of very grave and dignified appearance, whose hair

[029-131] was gray, and whose majestic (_sogmoye_) appearance was heightened

[029-132] by a pair of long and venerable ears. And of him Wild Cat asked in a

[029-133] gasping hurry if he had seen a Rabbit running that way.

[029-134] "Rabbits!" replied the old man. "Why, of course I have seen many. They

[029-135] abound in the woods about here. I see dozens of them every day." With

[029-136] this he said kindly to Wild Cat that he had better tarry with him for a

[029-137] time. "I am an old man," he remarked with solemnity,--"an old man,

[029-138] living alone, and a respectable guest, like you, sir, comes to me like

[029-139] a blessing." And the Cat, greatly impressed, remained. After a good

[029-140] supper he lay down by the fire, and, having run all day, was at once

[029-141] asleep, and made but one nap of it till morning. But how astonished,

[029-142] and oh, how miserable he was, when he awoke, to find himself on the

[029-143] open heath in the snow and almost starved! The wind blew as if it had a

[029-144] keen will to kill him; it seemed to go all through his body. Then he

[029-145] saw that he had been a fool and cheated by magic, and in a rage swore

[029-146] again by his teeth, as well as his tail, that the Rabbit should die.

[029-147] There was no hut now, only the trampled snow and a spruce twig, and yet

[029-148] out of this little, Rabbit had conjured up so great a delusion.

[029-149] Then he ran again all day. And when night came, Master Rabbit, having a

[029-150] little more time than before, again trampled down the snow, but for a

[029-151] greater space, and strewed many branches all about, for now a huge

[029-152] effort was to be made. And when Wild Cat got there he found a great

[029-153] Indian village, with crowds of people going to and fro. The first

[029-154] building he saw was a church, in which service was being held. And he,

[029-155] entering, said hastily to the first person he saw, "Ha! ho! have you

[029-156] seen a Rabbit running by here?"

[029-157] "Hush--sh, sh!" replied the man. "You must wait till meeting is over

[029-158] before asking such questions." [Footnote: Though this story is very

[029-159] old, the incident of the church (_sogmoye wigwam_, or chief house)

[029-160] is manifestly modern.] Then a young man beckoned to him to come in, and

[029-161] he listened till the end to a long sermon on the wickedness of being

[029-162] vindictive and rapacious; and the preacher was a gray ancient, and his

[029-163] ears stood up over his little cap like the two handles of a pitcher,

[029-164] yet for all that the Wild Cat's heart was not moved one whit. And when

[029-165] it was all at an end he said to the obliging young man, "But

[029-166] _have_ you seen a Rabbit running by?"
