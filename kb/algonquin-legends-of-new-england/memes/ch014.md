---[014-001] _Of other Men who went to Glooskap for Gifts_.

[014-002] (Micmac.)

[014-003] _N'karnayoo: wood-enit-atokhagen Glooskap_. Of the old times: this

[014-004] is a story of Glooskap. Now there went forth many men unto Glooskap,

[014-005] hearing that they could win the desires of their hearts; and all got

[014-006] what they asked for, in any case; but as for having what they wanted,

[014-007] that depended on the wisdom with which they wished or acted.

[014-008] The good Glooskap liked it not that when he had told any one evenly and

[014-009] plainly what to do, that man should then act otherwise, or double with

[014-010] him. And it came to pass that a certain fool, of the kind who can do

[014-011] nothing unless it be in his own way, made a long journey to the Master.

[014-012] And his trials were indeed many. For he came to an exceeding high

[014-013] mountain in a dark and lonely land, where he heard no sound. And the

[014-014] ascent thereof was like a smooth pole, and the descent on the other

[014-015] side far worse, for it hung over the bottom. Yet it was worse beyond,

[014-016] for there the road lay between the heads of two huge serpents, almost

[014-017] touching each other, who darted their terrible tongues at those who

[014-018] went between. And yet again the path passed under the Wall of Death.

[014-019] Now this wall hung like an awful cloud over a plain, rising and falling

[014-020] at times, yet no man knew when. And when it fell it struck the ground,

[014-021] and that so as to crush all that was beneath it.

[014-022] But the young man escaped all these trials, and came to the island of

[014-023] the Great Master. And when he had dwelt there a certain time, and was

[014-024] asked what he would have, he replied, "If my lord will, let him give me

[014-025] a medicine which will cure all disease." More than this he asked not.

[014-026] So the Master gave him a certain small package, and said, "Herein is

[014-027] that which thou seekest; but I charge thee that thou lettest not thine

[014-028] eyes behold it until thou shalt reach thy home." So he thanked the

[014-029] Master, and left.

[014-030] But he was not far away ere he desired to open the package and test the

[014-031] medicine, and, yet more, the truth of the Master. And he said to

[014-032] himself, "Truly, if this be but a deceit it was shrewdly devised to bid

[014-033] me not open it till I returned. For he knew well that once so far I

[014-034] would make no second journey to him. Tush! if the medicine avail aught

[014-035] it cannot change in aught." So he opened it, when that which was

[014-036] therein fell to the ground, and spread itself like water everywhere,

[014-037] and then dried away like a mist. And when he returned and told his

[014-038] tale, men mocked him.

[014-039] Then again there were three brothers, who, having adventured, made

[014-040] known their wishes. Now the first was very tall, far above all his

[014-041] fellows, and vain of his comeliness. For he was of those who put bark

[014-042] or fur into their moccasins, that they may be looked up to by the

[014-043] little folk and be loved by the squaws; and his hair was plastered to

[014-044] stand up on high, and on the summit of it was a very long turkey-tail

[014-045] feather. And this man asked to become taller than any Indian in all the

[014-046] land. [Footnote: This story has been told to me in three different

[014-047] forms. I have here given it with great care in what I conceive to be

[014-048] the original. In one version it is the pine, in another the cedar-tree.]

[014-049] And the second wished that he might ever remain where he was to behold

[014-050] the land and the beauty of it, and to do naught else.

[014-051] And the third wished to live to an exceeding old age, and ever to be in

[014-052] good health.

[014-053] Now the three, when they came to the island, had found there three

[014-054] wigwams, and in two of these were dwellers, not spoken of in other

[014-055] traditions. In one lived _Cool-puj-ot_, a very strange man. For he

[014-056] has no bones, and cannot move himself, but every spring and autumn he

[014-057] is _rolled over with handspikes_ by the order of Glooskap, and

[014-058] this is what his name means in the Micmac tongue. And in the autumn he

[014-059] is turned towards the west, but in the spring towards the east, and

[014-060] this is a figure of speech denoting the revolving seasons of the year.

[014-061] With his breath he can sweep down whole armies, and with his looks

[014-062] alone he can work great wonders, and all this means this weather,--

[014-063] frost, snow, ice, and sunshine. [Footnote: Mr. Rand (manuscript, p.

[014-064] 471) says that all of this explanation was given _verbatim_ by a

[014-065] Micmac named Stephen Flood, who was a "very intelligent and reliable

[014-066] Indian." Cool-puj-ot is almost identical with Shawandasee, the guardian

[014-067] of the South. "He is represented as an affluent, plethoric old man, who

[014-068] has grown unwieldy from repletion, and seldom moves. He keeps his eyes

[014-069] steadfastly fixed on the north. When he sighs in autumn, we have those

[014-070] balmy southern airs, which communicate warmth and delight over the

[014-071] northern hemisphere, and make the Indian summer." The "affluence" and

[014-072] "grown unwieldy from repletion," in this account, are probably due to

[014-073] Schoolcraft's florid style. (_Hiawatha Legends_.) Shawandasee is

[014-074] identical with Svasud of the Edda. (Vafthrudnisnal, 27.)]

[014-075] And in the other wigwam dwelt _Cuhkw_ (M.), which means

[014-076] Earthquake. And this mighty man can pass along under the ground, and

[014-077] make all things shake and tremble by his power.

[014-078] Now when Glooskap had heard what these visitors wished for, he called

[014-079] Earthquake, and bid him take them all three and put them with their

[014-080] feet in the ground. And he did so, when they at once became three

[014-081] trees: as one tradition declares, pines; and another, cedars.

[014-082] So that he that would be tall became exceeding tall, for his head rose

[014-083] above the forest; and even the turkey-feather at the top thereof is not

[014-084] forgotten, since to this day it is seen waving in the wind. And he who

[014-085] will listen in a pine-wood may hear the tree murmuring all day long in

[014-086] the Indian tongue of the olden time,--

[014-087] "Ee nil Etuchi nek m'kilaskitopp

[014-088] Ee nil Etuche wiski nek n'kil ooskedjin."

[014-089] [Footnote: Passamaquoddy.]

[014-090] Oh, I am such a great man!

[014-091] Oh, I am such a great Indian!

[014-092] And the second, who would remain in the land, remains there; for while

[014-093] his roots are in the ground he cannot depart from it.

[014-094] And the third, who would live long in health, unless men have cut him

[014-095] down, is standing as of yore. [Footnote: In another version of this

[014-096] tale, Glooskap transformed him into an old gnarled and twisted cedar,

[014-097] with limbs growing out rough and ugly all the way from the bottom.

[014-098] "There," he said to the cedar-tree, "I cannot say how long you will

[014-099] live; only the Great Spirit above can tell that; but you will not be

[014-100] disturbed for a good while, as no one can have any object in cutting

[014-101] you down. You are yourself unfit for any earthly purpose, and the land

[014-102] around you is useless for cultivation. I think you will stand there for

[014-103] a long while." (Rand manuscript.)

[014-104] It should be added that in one version we are told that the seeds from

[014-105] these cedars or pines were blown by the wind, and so spread forth all

[014-106] over the earth. The planting of the cedar by Earthquake possibly

[014-107] indicates the storms by which seeds are blown afar.]

[014-108] _Of Glooskap and the Three Other Seekers_.

[014-109] (Micmac.)

[014-110] Of old time. Now when it was noised abroad that whoever besought

[014-111] Glooskap could obtain the desire of his heart, there were three men who

[014-112] said among themselves, "Let us seek the Master." So they left their

[014-113] home in the early spring when the bluebird first sang, and walked till

[014-114] the fall frosts, and then into winter, and ever on till the next

[014-115] midsummer. And having come to a small path in a great forest, they

[014-116] followed it, till they came out by a very beautiful river; so fair a

[014-117] sight they had never seen, and so went onward till it grew to be a

[014-118] great lake. And so they kept to the path which, when untrodden, was

[014-119] marked by blazed trees, the bark having been removed, in Indian

[014-120] fashion, on the side of the trunk which is _opposite_ the place

[014-121] where the wigwam or village lies towards which it turns. So the mark

[014-122] can be seen as the traveler goes towards the goal, but not while

[014-123] leaving it.

[014-124] [Illustration: GLOOSKAP TURNING A MAN INTO A CEDAR-TREE]

[014-125] Then after a time they came to a long point of land running out into

[014-126] the lake, and, having ascended a high hill, they saw in the distance a

[014-127] smoke, which guided them to a large, well-built wigwam. And, entering,

[014-128] they found seated on the right side a handsome, healthy man of middle

[014-129] age, and by the other a woman so decrepit that she seemed to be a

[014-130] hundred years old. Opposite the door, and on the left side, was a mat,

[014-131] which seemed to show that a third person had there a seat.

[014-132] And the man made them welcome, and spoke as if he were _weleda'asit

[014-133] kesegvou_ (M.)--well pleased to see them, but did not ask them

[014-134] whence they came or whither they were going, as is wont among Indians

[014-135] when strangers come to their homes or are met in travel. Erelong they

[014-136] heard the sound of a paddle, and then the noise of a canoe being drawn

[014-137] ashore. And there came in a youth of fine form and features and well

[014-138] clad, bearing weapons as if from hunting who addressed the old woman as

[014-139] _Kejoo_, or mother, and told her that he had brought game. And

[014-140] with sore ado--for she was feeble--the old dame tottered out and

[014-141] brought in four beavers; but she was so much troubled to cut them up

[014-142] that the elder, saying to the younger man _Uoh-keen_! (M.), "My

[014-143] brother," bade him do the work. And they supped on beaver. So they

[014-144] remained for a week, resting themselves, for they were sadly worn with

[014-145] their wearisome journey, and also utterly ragged. And then a wondrous

[014-146] thing came to pass, which first taught them that they were in an

[014-147] enchanted land. For one morning the elder man bade the younger wash

[014-148] their mother's face. And as he did this all her wrinkles vanished, and

[014-149] she became young and very beautiful; in all their lives the travelers

[014-150] had never seen so lovely a woman. Her hair, which had been white and

[014-151] scanty, now hung to her feet, dark and glossy as a blackbird's breast.

[014-152] Then, having been clad in fine array, she showed a tall, lithe, and

[014-153] graceful form at its best.

[014-154] And the travelers said to themselves, "Truly this man is a great

[014-155] magician!" They all walked forth to see the place. Never was sunshine

[014-156] so pleasantly tempered by a soft breeze; for all in that land was fair,

[014-157] and it grew fairer day by day to all who dwelt there. Tall trees with

[014-158] rich foliage and fragrant flowers, but without lower limbs or

[014-159] underbrush, grew as in a grove, wide as a forest, yet so far apart that

[014-160] the eye could pierce the distance in every direction.

[014-161] Now when they felt for the first time that they were in a new life and

[014-162] a magic land, he that was host asked them whence they came and what

[014-163] they sought. So they said that they sought Glooskap. And the host

[014-164] replied, "Lo, I am he!" And they were awed by his presence, for a great

[014-165] glory and majesty now sat upon him. As the woman had changed, so had

[014-166] he, for all in that place was wonderful.

[014-167] Then the first, telling what he wanted, said, "I am a wicked man, and I

[014-168] have a bad temper. I am prone to wrath and reviling, yet I would fain

[014-169] be pious, meek, and holy."
