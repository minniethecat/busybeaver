# How Glooskap Went to England and France

## Overview

This remarkable Passamaquoddy tradition recounts how Glooskap sailed across the Atlantic in a stone canoe before Europeans knew of America. He discovered England, visited France, survived cannon fire and imprisonment, and had his Woodchuck mother baptized as a Catholic. Leland recognized the tale as an ancient Eskimo narrative modernized by contact, yet it preserves core mythological elements of the culture hero's overseas journey.

## Narrative

There was an Indian woman, a Woodchuck (Mon-in-kwess), who had lost a boy and always thought of him. One day a strange boy came to her and called her mother. He had a magic pipe with which he could summon all animals (018-089).

The boy was Glooskap. He built a stone canoe, working on it for a year. When it was finished, he provisioned it with meat from moose, elk, and bear, and carried water in seal bladders. Then they sailed across the sea, before the white people had ever heard of America (018-096).

> "The white men did not discover this country first at all. Glooskap discovered England, and told them about it." (018-109)

They reached London, where crowds flocked to see the strange canoe. The English offered Glooskap a large ship in exchange, but he refused, fearing it would burn. They offered servants; he refused them too. They gave him presents, an anchor, and an English flag (018-111).

Next he and his mother sailed to France. The French fired cannon at the stone canoe all afternoon but could not harm it. That night Glooskap drew all their men-of-war ashore. When the French discovered this, they took him prisoner (018-122). They put him into a great cannon and fired it off — yet when they looked inside, there sat Glooskap calmly smoking his stone pipe and knocking out the ashes (018-126).

The king heard of these deeds and declared that such a man must be great. He sent for Glooskap, who replied: "I do not want to see your king. I came to this country to have my mother baptized as a Catholic." Nevertheless he was taken to the king, and his mother was christened. They called her Molly — and to this day all woodchucks are called Molly (018-130).

## Significance

Leland identified this tale as an old Eskimo story greatly modernized and altered by contact (019-001). The Eskimo believe in sorcerers who possess instruments that kill by merely pointing — and this motif survives in the magic stick that slays animals at a distance. The tale was later attributed to Glooskap as a tribute to the great hero's memory.

The stone canoe is a recurring motif in Glooskap mythology. It appears also in the tradition of his departure from the world (005-085), and Leland notes that Tomah Josephs and Mrs. W. Wallace Brown each gave different versions. In one variant, Glooskap's canoe is a great ship crewed by birds — suggesting, Leland argues, that the voyage originally meant a trip to heaven.

The baptism episode represents a clear post-contact adaptation. Yet even here, Glooskap retains his autonomy: he does not seek the king's favor but demands the baptism on his own terms. The pun connecting Molly (Marie) to Mon-in-kwess (woodchuck) illustrates the playful syncretism characteristic of Wabanaki oral tradition.

## Variants

Tomah Josephs told one version to Leland; Mrs. W. Wallace Brown provided another. In the latter, Glooskap's canoe is a great ship with all kinds of birds for sailors. Leland connects this to the Shawnee legend of the Celestial Sisters, where a youth traveling to the sky must take one of every kind of bird — evidence that the Glooskap voyage originally represented a celestial journey.
