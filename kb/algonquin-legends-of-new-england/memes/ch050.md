---[050-001] Then he remembered that the giant had told him he would think of him in

[050-002] seven years; and it came clearly before him what it all meant, and that

[050-003] he was erelong to have magical power given to him, and that he should

[050-004] become a _Megumoowessoo_. This he told his wife, who, not being

[050-005] learned in darksome lore, would fain know more nearly what kind of a

[050-006] being he expected to be, and whether a spirit or a man, good or bad;

[050-007] which was, indeed, not easy to explain, nor is it clearly set down in

[050-008] the chronicles beyond this,--that, whatever it might be, it was all for

[050-009] the best, and that there was a great deal of magic in it.

[050-010] That day they saw a great shark cruising about in their bay, chasing

[050-011] fish, and this they held for an evil omen. But, soon after, there came

[050-012] trotting towards them over the sea the same small dog who had been

[050-013] their pilot from the Land of the Giants. So he, full of joy, as before,

[050-014] at seeing them and the children, wagged his tail and danced for glee,

[050-015] and then looked earnestly at the man as if for some message. And to him

[050-016] the man said, "It is well. In three years' time I will make you a

[050-017] visit. I will look to the southwest." Then the dog licked the hands and

[050-018] the ears and the eyes of the man, and went home as before over the sea,

[050-019] running on the water.

[050-020] And when the three years had passed the Indian entered his canoe, and,

[050-021] paddling without fear, found his way to the Land of the Giants. He saw

[050-022] the wigwams standing on the beach; the immense canoes were drawn up on

[050-023] the water's edge; from afar he beheld the old giant coming down to

[050-024] welcome him. But he was alone. And when he had been welcomed, and was

[050-025] in the wigwam, he learned that all the sons were dead. They had died

[050-026] three years before, when the shark, the great sorcerer, had been seen.

[050-027] They had gone, and the old man had but lingered a little longer. They

[050-028] had made the magic change, they had departed, and he would soon join

[050-029] them _in his own kingdom_. But ere he went he would leave their

[050-030] great inheritance, their magic, to the man.

[050-031] Therewith the giant brought out his son's clothes, and bade the Indian

[050-032] put them on. Truly this was as if he had been asked to clothe himself

[050-033] with a great house, since the smallest fold in them would have been to

[050-034] him as a cavern. But he stepped in, and as he did this he rose to great

[050-035] size; he filled out the garments till they fitted; he was a giant, of

[050-036] Giant-Land. With the clothes came the wisdom, the _m'teoulin_, the

[050-037] _manitou_ power of the greatest and wisest of the olden time. He

[050-038] was indeed _Megumoowessoo_, and had attained to the Mystery.

[050-039] This very remarkable and evidently ancient tale is one of that kind

[050-040] which the keepers of tribe chronicles among the pagan Indians do not

[050-041] tell to the world, and which they conceal from white men. It is not a

[050-042] fragment, nor is it unfinished, as some readers may suppose. Its plot

[050-043] is of a much higher nature than a novel, which ends in a marriage. To

[050-044] an Indian, whose ideas of earthly happiness were not in money, houses,

[050-045] and lands, personal power was the one thing to be most desired. As a

[050-046] Passamaquoddy said once to me, "To be rich in those days meant to be a

[050-047] great hunter and always have plenty of meat for everybody." Hence the

[050-048] desire to be great and strong, to be able to entice wild animals, to

[050-049] run like the wind, to be crafty in all things, especially in making

[050-050] war; hence to have prophetic dreams. All of this was to be attained by

[050-051] _m'teoulin_, or magic. The highest ambition of an Indian was to

[050-052] become a _Megumoowessoo_, a mystical being, which is explained

[050-053] differently as fairy, faun, sylvan deity, but which means one who

[050-054] enjoys all the highest privileges of humanity allied to the

[050-055] supernatural. This is what the hero of this story gets by favor of the

[050-056] giant.

[050-057] It may be observed that in this tale the Indian cannot explain to his

[050-058] wife what he nevertheless perfectly understands; that is, the exact

[050-059] nature of a _Megumoowessoo_. The giant, by speaking of his own

[050-060] kingdom, gives the true key of the whole mystery. He has attained magic

[050-061] power so far as one can exercise it in this life. Like Glooskap he can

[050-062] be, or unlike him prefers, to be habitually, a giant. He has battled

[050-063] with the Chenoo and Kookwess; he has, like Hercules, fulfilled his

[050-064] mission; and now he departs for his own realm, that of the

[050-065] _Megumoowessoo_, as Arthur went to Fairy-Land, as Buddha to the

[050-066] unknown Nirvana,--that is, to something beyond the conception of poet

[050-067] or theosophist.

[050-068] I suspect that the period of seven years, and again of three years, had

[050-069] been employed by the Indian in preparing himself by penance for

[050-070] _m'teoulin_. The respect of the Indians for the number

[050-071] _seven_ is so remarkable, that if it be true that _Deus

[050-072] imparibus numeris gaudet_, they are in that respect, at least, like

[050-073] deities. Whenever _seven_ or a white bear's skin occurs in these

[050-074] tales, there always lies hidden a magical mystery.

[050-075] It is not the least remarkable feature of this tale that it abounds in

[050-076] that quiet small humor which recalls the adventures of Captain Lemuel

[050-077] Gulliver. The Indian, like, the Norseman, was such an _implicit_

[050-078] believer in his own myths, and he had evolved them so entirely from

[050-079] himself without borrowing,--since we may regard him as one in this

[050-080] respect with the Eskimo,--that no human characteristic detracted from

[050-081] the dignity of the Manitou.

[050-082] There is a strong suggestion in this story that the giants were whales.

[050-083] This and the incident of their inhabiting a mysterious country beyond

[050-084] the sea and the fog would identify them with the enchanted land of the

[050-085] Eskimo, visited by the Angakok in their trances, and by others in

[050-086] _kayaks_. This country was named _Akilinek_, "a fabulous land

[050-087] beyond the sea." The whole story of Malaise, the man who traveled to

[050-088] Akilinek, is in every detail extremely like an Indian tale. (Rink, page

[050-089] 169.) It has also a Norse affinity. The land of the giants was supposed

[050-090] by both Icelanders and Indians to be in the North Atlantic. There is a

[050-091] Norse tale of a man changed to a whale which indicates a common origin

[050-092] with the one here given.

[050-093] It is believed that the _m'teoulin_ can, when speaking, make

[050-094] themselves heard to whom they will, at any distance. They, can confer

[050-095] with one another secretly when miles away, or make themselves known to

[050-096] many. I was informed by an Indian in all faith that an old witch who

[050-097] died in 1876, twelve miles from Pleasant Point, was heard to speak in

[050-098] the latter place when at her last. A very intelligent Passamaquoddy

[050-099] told me that when Osalik (Sarah) Hequin died he himself heard all she

[050-100] said, though sixty-five miles distant. I am certain that he firmly

[050-101] believed this. This woman died a strange death, for she was found

[050-102] standing up, dead, in the snow, with her arms extended and "hands

[050-103] sticking out." It is generally believed that she was killed by other

[050-104] _m'teoulin_.

[050-105] There are really very few ideas in modern mesmerism not known to Eskimo

[050-106] or Indian Shamans. Clairvoyance is called by the Passamaquoddies

[050-107] _Meelah bi give he_.

[050-122] THE SONG OF THE STARS.

[050-123] We are the stars which sing,

[050-124] We sing with our light;

[050-125] We are the birds of fire,

[050-126] We fly over the sky.

[050-127] Our light is a voice;

[050-128] We make a road for spirits,

[050-129] For the spirits to pass over.

[050-130] Among us are three hunters

[050-131] Who chase a bear;

[050-132] There never was a time

[050-133] When they were not hunting.

[050-134] We look down on the mountains.

[050-135] This is the Song of the Stars.
