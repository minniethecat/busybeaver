**Abistanooch** — the Marten — is a figure who appears in two distinct roles within the Algonquin mythological corpus: as the boy-companion and servant of [[Glooskap]] in the culture hero's adventures, and as an independent trickster-hero in the Micmac and Passamaquoddy tale of Abistanooch and Team the Moose. His name, meaning "Marten" (a weasel-like forest animal), reflects the Algonquin tradition of animal-people who embody the characteristics of their species — in Abistanooch's case, stealth, cleverness, and a certain predatory charm.

## Overview

Abistanooch occupies a liminal position in Algonquin mythology. As Glooskap's servant, he appears in several of the culture hero's major adventures: bearing the hero's mystical belt, gathering fuel in the great cold-making contest with [[Kitpooseagunow]], and assisting at the breaking of the giant beaver dam. In his independent story, he is a _moalet_ — "one who liveth upon his neighbors" (020-003) — whose cleverness and magical knowledge allow him to capture two fairy wives while his earnest but foolish companion Team the Moose fails utterly.

The two portrayals are consistent: whether serving Glooskap or pursuing his own ends, Abistanooch is defined by quickness, stealth, and a pragmatic relationship to rules and obligations. He is a trickster in the classic Algonquin mold — not malevolent, but guided by appetite and opportunity rather than principle.

## Identity and Attributes

### Name and Species

The name _Abistanooch_ is the Micmac term for the marten (_Martes americana_), a forest-dwelling mustelid known for its agility and stealth. Leland identifies him explicitly: "Evidently no other than Marten, or the Abistanooch of the Micmacs" (015-079). The marten's natural characteristics — silent movement, keen observation, opportunistic feeding — are directly translated into the character's personality and methods.

### Character Traits

Abistanooch is described as "greatly _moalet_ (M.), which signifies one who liveth upon his neighbors, depending on their good nature, even as he that planteth corn and beans depends upon the pleasant smiles of the sun" (020-003). This characterization — the charming freeloader — is central to his identity. Yet he is also "tinctured with magic and learned in the lore of all kind of goblins, elves, and witches" (020-003), possessing the sorcerous knowledge that distinguishes major figures in the Algonquin mythological world.

His "merry" disposition, quick wit, and ability to turn situations to his advantage make him one of the more purely entertaining figures in the corpus. Unlike the grandiose contests of [[Glooskap]] and [[Kitpooseagunow]], Abistanooch's adventures operate at a domestic, almost comic scale — borrowing kettles, stealing fairy garments, and feuding with his neighbor.

## Role in Legends

### Companion to Glooskap

Abistanooch serves as Glooskap's trusted attendant in several adventures:

**The Cold-Making Contest**: When Glooskap and Kitpooseagunow engage in their supernatural freezing contest, it is Marten who gathers fuel and tends the fire — only to freeze to death at midnight, requiring Glooskap's resurrection command: "_Abistanooch numchahse!_" — "Marten, arise!" (011-119).

**The Giant Beaver Dam**: At the great dam, Glooskap "bade Marten watch" as he cuts the structure near the shore, relying on his servant's vigilance (015-079).

**The Mystical Belt**: Glooskap's power belt, when lent to Marten, makes "the boy becomes" supernaturally strong — a detail that confirms the intimate trust between master and servant (015-079).

### The Tale of Abistanooch and Team the Moose

Abistanooch lives by a forest brook with his grandmother, alongside his neighbor Team the Moose. While Team "hunted and worked industriously," Master Marten is _moalet_ — living off the labor of others (020-003).

#### The Borrowed Kettle

When Team kills a bear and tries to hide the meat from his freeloading neighbor, Marten's instincts prove sharper than Team's cunning. Team borrows Marten's new kettle, planning to cook the bear meat in secret and return the pot washed clean. But "he who is _moalet_ and a haunter of feasts is like a hunter of beasts: he knows well from a small sign where there is a large load" (020-003). Peeping through a crevice, Marten sees the meat; through sorcery, he causes the returned kettle to be magically filled with well-cooked food:

> "Just as she entered the lodge there arose from it a savory steam, and looking in it was full of well-cooked food" (020-003).

The grandmother flees in shame, but Team accepts the situation philosophically, and the next day they hunt together — "and all was well."

#### The Fairy Wives

The story's centerpiece is Marten's capture of a supernatural wife. Coming to a distant mountain lake, he hears laughing and splashing, and spies fairy maidens bathing — "of the fairy race, who dwell in deep waters and dark caves" (020-003). Knowing that "when Naiads are naked and a man has their garments he holds them at their mercy," he steals the clothes of the one he desires:

> "Marten quietly slipped along unseen, as all of his species can do, till he had the clothes in his hands" (020-003).

He taps her lightly on the head with a stick — "according to a certain ancient prescription followed in Fairy-land, which makes of a woman a wife" — and carries her off. Team, learning of this success, attempts the same method but misapplies it fatally: seizing "a great club" he "gave her a bang on her small head, which stunned her indeed, and that forever, inasmuch as she was slain outright" (020-003).

Marten later captures a second fairy wife to please his first wife's yearning for her sisters' company — "in such a kind of benevolence he was one of the best souls that ever lived" (020-003). This triggers a feud with the envious Team, who demands one of Marten's wives, and the two spend their days "passing their evenings in manufacturing missiles, and the mornings in sending them one at the other."

## Key Stories

### Abistanooch and Team the Moose

> **Source**: Micmac and Passamaquoddy (020-003 through 021-028)

Abistanooch the Marten and Team the Moose live as neighbors by a forest brook, each with a grandmother keeping house. When Team kills a bear and attempts to hide the meat, Marten's sorcerous knowledge reveals the secret, and he humiliates Team's grandmother by magically filling the returned kettle with food.

Marten later discovers fairy maidens bathing in a mountain lake and, knowing the lore of such beings, steals one's garments — gaining power over her — and makes her his wife with a light tap of a stick. Team attempts the same but kills his intended bride with an overzealous club-blow. Marten, ever the good-natured opportunist, captures a second fairy wife for his household's harmony. The envious Team demands one of them, leading to a protracted feud of arrows and clubs. The fairy wives, unaccustomed to such violence, flee into the forest, and the story concludes with them gazing at the stars, jesting about which celestial body they would wed.

## Cultural Significance

Abistanooch represents a distinct register of Algonquin mythological narrative — the domestic trickster tale. Unlike the cosmic-scale contests of [[Glooskap]] and [[Kitpooseagunow]], or the grim revenge drama of [[Pulowech]], the Marten stories operate in a world of borrowed kettles, neighborly feuds, and fairy wives. This register — comic, intimate, concerned with the management of appetite and obligation — is as integral to the Algonquin narrative tradition as the heroic cycle.

The figure also illustrates the fluid boundary between animal and human identity in Algonquin thought. Abistanooch is simultaneously a marten (stealthy, opportunistic) and a man (with a wigwam, a grandmother, and magical knowledge). This duality is not presented as metamorphosis but as a natural condition: the animal-people simply are who they are, their natures continuous with their species.

The fairy-wife episode belongs to the international Swan Maiden tale type (ATU 400), adapted here with characteristically Algonquin humor and pragmatism. The contrast between Marten's deft, knowing approach and Team's catastrophic clumsiness enacts a folk pedagogy: magical power requires knowledge and subtlety, not mere force.
