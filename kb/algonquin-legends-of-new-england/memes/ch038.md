---[038-001] Now Pulowech, being himself addicted to sorcery, knew that there were

[038-002] divers knaves of the same stamp prowling about the woods, who would

[038-003] make short work of a wife if they could find a plump young one in the

[038-004] way,--they being robbers, ravishers, and cannibals withal. Therefore he

[038-005] warned his bride to keep well within doors when he was away, and to

[038-006] open to none, which she, poor soul, meant to obey with all her might.

[038-007] But being alone at midnight, and hearing a call outside, even

[038-008] "_Pantahdooe_!" M., "Open the door to me!" she wondered greatly who

[038-009] it might be. And it was a very wicked wizard, a _boo-oin_, or

[038-010] pow-wow; and he, being subtle and crafty, and knowing of her family,

[038-011] so imitated the voices of her brothers and sisters; beseeching her to let

[038-012] them in, that her very heart ached. "O sister, we have come from afar!"

[038-013] they cried. "We missed you, and have followed you. Let us in!" And yet

[038-014] again she heard a sad and very earnest voice, and it was that of her

[038-015] old mother, crying, "_N'toos', n'toos', pantahdooe_!" M., "My

[038-016] daughter! my daughter! open unto me!" and she verily wist that it must

[038-017] be so. But when she heard the voice of her dear old father, shaking and

[038-018] saying, "_Pantahdooe loke cyowchee_!" "Open the door, for I am very

[038-019] cold!" she could resist no more, and, springing up, opened it to those

[038-020] who were without. And then the evil sorcerers, springing on her like

[038-021] mad wolves, dragged her away and devoured her. They did not leave two

[038-022] of her little bones one with another. [Footnote: This Indian Little Red

[038-023] Riding-Hood story is very effective. The wolfish sorcerers bursting in

[038-024] at midnight are even more terrible, from a nursery melodramatic point,

[038-025] than the old wolf in bed.]

[038-026] Now when _Wejek_, the Tree Partridge, came in and found his

[038-027] friend's wife gone, he was so angry that, without waiting, he set forth

[038-028] to seek her. And this was not wisely done, since, falling among them,

[038-029] he was himself slain. Then Pulowech, returning last of all, and finding

[038-030] no one, sought by means of magic to know where friend and wife might

[038-031] be. For taking a _woltes_, or a wooden dish, he filled it with

[038-032] water, and charmed it with a spell, and placed it in the back part of

[038-033] his wigwam, just opposite the door. So he laid him down to sleep, and

[038-034] in the morning when he arose he looked upon the dish,--even the dish of

[038-035] divination,--and lo! it was half full of blood. Then he knew that the

[038-036] twain had been murdered.

[038-037] Then gathering all his arms, he went forth for revenge, and passed many

[038-038] days on the path, tracking the _boo-oin_; and having the eyesight

[038-039] of sorcery, he one day beheld very far away, upon an exceeding high

[038-040] cliff, the knee of a man sticking out of the stone, and knew that a

[038-041] sorcerer had hidden himself in the solid rock, even as a child might

[038-042] hide itself in a pile of feathers. Then throwing his tomahawk he cut

[038-043] away the knee, and the _boo-oin_, his spell broken, remained hard

[038-044] and fast forever in the ledge. And yet, anon, a little further on, he

[038-045] saw a foot projecting from a wall, and this he likewise cut off, and

[038-046] with that he had slain two.

[038-047] And as he went further he found by the way a poor little squirrel, even

[038-048] _Meeko_, who was crawling along, half dead, in sorry plight. And

[038-049] taking her up he made her well, and placing her in his bosom, said,

[038-050] "Rest there yet a while, _Meeko_, for thou must fight to-day, and

[038-051] that fiercely. Yet fear not, for I will stand by thee, and when I tap

[038-052] thy back, then shalt thou bring forth thy young!"

[038-053] Then going ever on, he saw from the mountains far in a lake below a

[038-054] flock of wild geese sporting merrily, even the _Senum-kwak'_. But

[038-055] he wist right well that these also were of the _boo-oin_, whom he

[038-056] sought, and placing a spell on his bow, and singing a charm over his

[038-057] arrows that they should not miss, he slew the wild fowl one by one, and

[038-058] tying their heads together, he carried them in a bunch upon his back.

[038-059] And truly he deemed it a good bag of game for one day.

[038-060] And yet further on he came to a wigwam, and entering it saw a man there

[038-061] seated, whom he knew at once was of the enemy. For he who sat there

[038-062] glared at him grimly; he did not say to him, "_'Kutakumoogwal!_" "Come

[038-063] higher up!" as they do who are hospitable. But having cooked some meat,

[038-064] and given it in a dish to Pulowech's hand, he snatched it back again,

[038-065] and said he would sooner give it to his dog. And this he did more than

[038-066] once, saying the same thing. But Pulowech kept quiet. Then the rude man

[038-067] said, "Hast thou met with aught to-day, thou knave?" And the guest

[038-068] replied, "Truly I saw a fellow's knee sticking out of a stone, and I

[038-069] cut it off. And yet, anon, I saw a foot coming from a rock, and this I

[038-070] also chopped. And further on there was a flock of wild geese, and them

[038-071] I slew; there was not one left,--no, not one. And if you will look

[038-072] without there you may see them all dead, and much good may it do you!"

[038-073] Then the savage sorcerer burst forth in all his rage: "Come on, then,

[038-074] our dogs must fight this out!" "Thou sayest well," replied Pulowech;

[038-075] "truly I am fond of a good dog-fight, so bring out thy pup!" And that

[038-076] which the man brought forth was terrible; for it was no dog, but a

[038-077] hideous savage beast, known to Micmacs as the _Weisum_. [Footnote:

[038-078] The _Amarok_ of the Eskimo.]

[038-079] But that which Pulowech produced was quite as different from a dog as

[038-080] was the _Weisum_; for it was only _Meeko_, a poor little

[038-081] squirrel, and half dead at that, which he laid carefully before the

[038-082] fire that it might revive. [Footnote: In another version of this story,

[038-083] the savage stranger puts up a real dog against the squirrel; and in the

[038-084] story of Glooskap, it is that great man who makes the squirrel great or

[038-085] small.] But anon it began to revive, and grew until it was well-nigh as

[038-086] great as the _Weisum_. And then there was indeed a battle as of

[038-087] devils and witches; he who had been a hundred miles away might have

[038-088] heard it.

[038-089] But anon it seemed that the _Weisum_ was getting the better of

[038-090] _Meeko_. Then Pulowech did but tap the squirrel on the back, when

[038-091] lo! she brought forth two other squirrels, and these grew in an instant

[038-092] to be as large as their mother, and the three were soon too many for

[038-093] the beast. "Ho! call off your dogs!" cried the _boo-oin_; "you

[038-094] have beaten. But spare mine, since, indeed, he does not belong to me,

[038-095] but to my grandmother, who is very fond of him." [Footnote: This

[038-096] trivial episode of begging a call-off seems to have deeply impressed

[038-097] the Indians, who are generally sporting-men, since I find it in both

[038-098] the Passamaquoddy and Micmac versions of the legend.]

[038-099] Pulowech, who held to his own in all things like a wolverine, was the

[038-100] last man alive to think of, and he encouraged the squirrels until they

[038-101] had torn the _Weisum_ to rags.

[038-102] Then he who had staked it, bitterly lamented, saying, "Alack, my poor

[038-103] grandmother! Alas, how she will wail when she hears that her

[038-104] _Weisum_ is dead! Woe the day that ever I did put him up! Alas, my

[038-105] grandmother!" For all which the cruel Pulowech, the hard-hearted,

[038-106] impenitent Partridge, did not care the hair of a dead musk-rat.

[038-107] Now the host, who had thus suddenly grown so tender-hearted, said, "Let

[038-108] us sail forth upon the river in a canoe." Then they were soon on the

[038-109] stream, and rushing down a rapid like a dart. And anon they came to a

[038-110] terribly high cliff, in which there was a narrow cavern into which the

[038-111] river ran. And on it, thundering through this door of death, borne on a

[038-112] boiling surge, the bark was forced furiously into darkness. And

[038-113] Pulowech sat firmly in his seat, and steered the boat with steady,

[038-114] certain hand; but just as he entered the horrible hole, glancing

[038-115] around, he saw the sorcerer leap ashore. For the evil man, believing

[038-116] that no one had ever come alive out of the cavern, had betrayed him

[038-117] into it.

[038-118] Yet ever cool and calm the mighty man went on, for danger now was

[038-119] bringing out all the force of his magic; [Footnote: It is very

[038-120] characteristic of the heroes of these Indian tales that they gradually

[038-121] unfold or develop from small characteristics to very great ones. There

[038-122] is a lesson in this, and it has been perfectly appreciated by poets and

[038-123] similar sorcerers.] and soon the stream grew smoother, the rocks

[038-124] disappeared from its bed, and then from afar there was a brightness,

[038-125] and he was soon in the daylight and sunshine on a beautiful stream,

[038-126] and by the banks thereof there grew the _wabeyu-beskwan_, or

[038-127] water-lilies, and very pleasant it was to him to feel the wind again.

[038-128] So using his paddle he saw a smoke rising from a cave in the rocks,

[038-129] And landing and softly stepping up heard talking within.

[038-130] Nor had he listened long ere he knew the voice of the man who had lured

[038-131] him into the canoe, and he was telling his grandmother how, one after

[038-132] the other, all the best _boo-oin_ of their band had been slain by

[038-133] a mighty sorcerer. But when she heard from him how her beloved, or the

[038-134] one who had inspired the _Weisum_, had been beaten, her wrath

[038-135] burst forth in a storm, like the raving of devils, like a mad wind on

[038-136] the waves. And she said, "If Pulowech were but before me, were he but

[038-137] alive, I would roast him." The man, hearing this, cried, "Aye; but he

[038-138] is _not_ alive, for I sent him afloat down into the dark cavern!"

[038-139] And then Pulowech, stepping in before them, said, "And yet I am alive.

[038-140] And do thou, woman, _bak sok bok sooc_!" (roast me to death). Then

[038-141] she scowled horribly at him, but said naught; and he, sitting down,

[038-142] looked at them.

[038-143] This woman was of the Porcupines, who are never long without raising

[038-144] their quills, and they are fond of heat. Now there was in the cave much

[038-145] hemlock bark, and this she began to heap on the fire. Then it blazed,

[038-146] it crackled and roared; but Pulowech sat still, and said naught,

[038-147] neither did his eyes change. And he called unto himself all his might,

[038-148] the might of his magic did he awaken, and the spirit came unto him very

[038-149] terribly, so that all the _boo-oin_, with their vile black

[038-150] witchcraft, were but as worms before him, the Great and Terrible One.

[038-151] And when the fire had burned low he brought in by his will great store

[038-152] of bark, so that the whole cave was filled, and closing the door he

[038-153] lighted the fuel. Then the Porcupines, who were those who had slain his

[038-154] wife and friend, howled for mercy, but he was deaf as a stone to their

[038-155] cries. Then the roof and sides of the cavern cracked with the heat, the

[038-156] red-hot stones fell in heavy blocks, the red flames rose in the

[038-157] thickest smoke, but Pulowech sat and sang his song until the witch and

[038-158] wizard were burned to cinders; yea, till their white bones crumbled to

[038-159] ashes beneath his feet. And then he arose and went unto his home.

[038-160] [Footnote: In this Micmac legend, which is plainly a poem, there is one

[038-161] very striking and original element in the art with which the great

[038-162] knowledge and power of Pulowech are kept out of sight until towards the

[038-163] final unfolding. When he picks up the Squirrel it is with a full

[038-164] comprehension that he will be confronted with the _Weisum_. From

[038-165] the beginning to the end, he is master of the situation; all goes on

[038-166] with him like the unfolding of Fate in a Greek tragedy, until the end,

[038-167] when, stern and unpitying, he sits in the cavern of fire and sees his

[038-168] enemies roasted alive before him.--From the Rand Manuscript.]
