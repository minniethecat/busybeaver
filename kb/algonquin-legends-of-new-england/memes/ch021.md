---[021-001] And as they sat there and day dawned, men of the different Indian

[021-002] families went by, and unto all of these they cried for help. It is true

[021-003] that their star husbands had made for them in the tree, a bed of moss,

[021-004] but they cared not to rest in the hemlock, for all that. [Footnote: In

[021-005] another very full version of this legend (M.), the water-wives are

[021-006] called Weasels (_Uskoolsh_), "from their great whiteness." This,

[021-007] however, indicates supernatural fairness or beauty. In the same story

[021-008] the tree is a pine, not a hemlock. Insignificant as these differences

[021-009] may appear, they are of primary importance in the elucidation of a

[021-010] myth.] And of all the beasts of the forest or men of the clearing, who

[021-011] should be the first to appear but Team, or Master Moose, himself. And

[021-012] to him they cried, "_N'sesenen-apkwahlin, n'sesenen_!" "Oh, our

[021-013] elder brother, let us free; take us down, and we will be your two dear

[021-014] little wives, and go home with you." But he, looking up scornfully,

[021-015] said, "I was married this autumn." And so he went his way.

[021-016] And he who next came was the shaggy Bear, or _mooin_, to whom they

[021-017] made the same request, offering themselves for no higher price than to

[021-018] be taken down safely out of their nest. But he growled out that he had

[021-019] been married in the spring, and that one wife was enough for any man.

[021-020] So he went his way. [Footnote: N. B.--There is a joke here. The animals

[021-021] who pass by the tree each mate at the season of the year when they

[021-022] declare that they were married. The White Ladies, weasels or ermines,

[021-023] therefore, came at the wrong time. The fickle, variable nature ascribed

[021-024] to woman, _varium et mutabile semper femina_, is supposed to be

[021-025] most decidedly expressed by such slender, slippery, active little

[021-026] animals.]

[021-027] And then who should come along but Marten himself, even the

[021-028] Abistanooch, whom they had deserted! And they cried out for joy,

[021-029] begging him to take them back. But he, behaving as if they were utter

[021-030] strangers, replied that he had been married in the early spring to one

[021-031] of his own tribe, and unto a damsel whose name was Marten, and that it

[021-032] was not seemly for animals to wed out of their own land. So he

[021-033] scampered off, leaving the little Weasels all alone.

[021-034] And last of all came Lox, whom hunters call the Indian Devil,

[021-035] [Footnote: In the Micmac it is the Badger, Keekwajoo, who is the rogue

[021-036] and teaser of the tale. But in the Passamaquoddy versions it is the

[021-037] dreaded and mysterious Lox, who appears to be a species of Lynx or

[021-038] Wolverine. The Lox is said, by trustworthy white travelers as well as

[021-039] Indians, to follow hunting parties for weeks, inspired apparently only

[021-040] by an incredible mania for mischief, much like that of a monkey or a

[021-041] revengeful savage, but guided by remarkable intelligence. He will find

[021-042] his way into a camp and destroy every object made by the hand of man

[021-043] with a thoroughness akin to genius, and what he cannot destroy he will

[021-044] carry to a great distance and carefully conceal. As his ferocity is

[021-045] equal to his craftiness, he is very appropriately termed the Indian

[021-046] Devil.] and others the Wolverine, who is exceeding subtle above the

[021-047] beasts of the forest, and who is gifted with more evil mischief than

[021-048] all of them in one. And when the Weasels called to him for help he

[021-049] tarried, for it came into his heart that he might in some way torment

[021-050] and tease them. But verily he had to deal with those who were not much

[021-051] more virtuous than himself, and quite as cunning, for what with

[021-052] traveling from the earth to the heavens and changing husbands, these

[021-053] fair minevers were learning wisdom rapidly. So the elder sister, who

[021-054] had not the least idea of keeping her promise unless it suited her

[021-055] fancy, played a trick, and that quickly anon. For she at once took off

[021-056] her hair-string [Footnote: The Hair-String, _Saggalobee_ (M),

[021-057] occurs very often in Indian legends, generally as gifted with magic.

[021-058] The Indian women allowed their hair to grow long, then doubled it upon

[021-059] the back of the head, often making additions of something to enlarge

[021-060] the roll. It was then bound in a bunch with the string.] and tied it

[021-061] into a few less than a hundred knots among the twigs of the trees,

[021-062] tangling it so that you would have deemed it a week's work before a man

[021-063] could loosen it again without injury.

[021-064] Now Master Lox, having taken down the younger sister with all the

[021-065] politeness in the world, came for the other, and aided her also to

[021-066] descend. And when on the ground she indeed said, "_Willcr-oon_" "I

[021-067] thank you" (P.), but begged him to go up the tree again and bring down

[021-068] a great treasure which she had left there, her hair-string: beseeching

[021-069] him for all their lives not to break or injure it in any way, but to

[021-070] most carefully untie every knot, for thus doing it would bring untold

[021-071] felicity on them all; and that they, the Weasels, would meantime build

[021-072] a beautiful bridal bower, or a wigwam, and that so furnished as he had

[021-073] never seen the like before,--in which verily they kept their word.

[021-074] For they speedily built the wigwam, but the furniture thereof was of

[021-075] this rare kind. The Weasels had, it seems, certain sworn friends,--for

[021-076] birds of a feather flock together,--and these were not far to seek, as

[021-077] they were the Thorns, Burrs, and Briers of all kinds, Hornets and other

[021-078] winged and stinged insects, besides the Ants. And they were, moreover,

[021-079] intimate with all the sharp-edged Flints in the land, which was a

[021-080] goodly company. So when the bower was built it had therein a hornet's

[021-081] nest for a bridal bed, thorns for a carpet, flints for a floor, and an

[021-082] ant's nest for a seat, which for a bare-footed and bare-breeched Indian

[021-083] is indeed a sore essay. Now it had taken Master Lox the entire day to

[021-084] untie the hair-string, so when he came down it was dark, and he was

[021-085] glad when he saw the hut and thought of resting therein.

[021-086] But, as he entered, he ran among the Thorns, which pierced his nose,

[021-087] and Flints, which cut his feet, so that he roared aloud. Then he heard

[021-088] a voice, which seemed to be that of the younger Miss Weasel, crying

[021-089] "_Names-cole_" (M.), "Go to my sister, yonder!" So he went, and trod

[021-090] in an ant-hill, and this was worse than the Briers. And then he heard

[021-091] another voice on that side which cried, laughing, "_N'kwech-kale_!"

[021-092] (M.), "Go to my sister, who is younger than I." And plunging furiously

[021-093] through the darkness, he fell on the hornet's nest; and verily the last

[021-094] state of that Indian was worst of all. Thus, seeing himself mocked, he

[021-095] became furious; so that he who has by nature the very worst temper of

[021-096] all beasts or men was never so angry before, and, seeking the tracks of

[021-097] the Weasels, he pursued them as they fled in the night and through the

[021-098] thick forest.

[021-099] Now it came to pass that by daybreak the two girls, even the Misses

[021-100] Weasel, had come to a broad river which they could not cross. But

[021-101] In The edge of the water stood a large Crane, motionless, or the

[021-102] _Tum-gwo-lig-unach_, who was the ferryman. Now truly this is

[021-103] esteemed to be the least beautiful of all the birds, for which cause

[021-104] he is greedy of good words and fondest of flattery. And of all beings

[021-105] there were none who had more bear's oil ready to anoint every one's hair

[021-106] with--that is to say, more compliments ready for everybody--than the

[021-107] Weasels. So, seeing the Crane, they sang:--

[021-108] "Wa wela quis kip pat kasqu',

[021-109] Wa wela quis kip pat kasqu'." (P.)

[021-110] The Crane has a very beautiful long neck,

[021-111] The Crane has a very beautiful long neck.

[021-112] This charmed the old ferryman very much, and when they said, "Please,

[021-113] grandfather, hurry along," he came quickly. Seeing this, they began to

[021-114] chant in chorus, sweetly as the Seven Stars themselves:--

[021-115] "Wa wela quig nat kasqu',

[021-116] Wa wela quig nat kasqu'." (P.)

[021-117] The Crane has very beautiful long legs,

[021-118] The Crane has very beautiful long legs.

[021-119] Hearing this, the good Crane wanted more; so when they asked him to

[021-120] give them a lift across, he answered slowly that to do so he must be

[021-121] well paid, but that good praise would answer as well. Now they who had

[021-122] abundance of this and to spare for everybody were these very girls.

[021-123] "Have I not a beautiful form?" he inquired; and they both cried aloud,

[021-124] "Oh, uncle, it is indeed beautiful!" "And my feathers?" "Ah,

[021-125] _pegeakopchu_" (M.), "Beautiful and straight feathers indeed!"

[021-126] "And have I not a charming long, straight neck?" "Truly our uncle has

[021-127] it straight and long." "And will ye not acknowledge, oh, maidens, that

[021-128] my legs are fine?" "Fine! oh, uncle, they are perfection. Never in this

[021-129] life did we see such legs!" So being well pleased, the Crane put them

[021-130] across, and then the two little Weasels scampered like mice into the

[021-131] bush.

[021-132] And scarcely were they concealed, or the Crane well again in his place,

[021-133] ere Master Lox appeared. And being in no good temper he called to Uncle

[021-134] Crane to set him across, and that speedily. Now the Crane had been made

[021-135] mightily pleased and proud by the winsome words of the Weasels, and was

[021-136] but little inclined to be rudely addressed. So he said to Lox, "I will

[021-137] bear thee over the river if thou wilt bear witness to my beauty. Are

[021-138] not my legs straight?" "Yea" replied the Lox, "and beautifully painted,

[021-139] too." Now the color thereof was little pleasing to poor Uncle Crane.

[021-140] "Are not my feathers very smooth and fine?" "Yea, smooth and fine; what

[021-141] a pity, though, that they are mildewed and dusty!" "And my straight

[021-142] neck?" "Yes, wonderfully straight,--straight as _this_" said Lox

[021-143] to himself, taking up a crooked stick. And then he sang:--

[021-144] "Mecha guiskipat kasqu',

[021-145] Mecha quig nat kasqu'."

[021-146] The Crane has a very ugly neck,

[021-147] The Crane has dirty, ugly legs.

[021-148] "Come, _mooso me_ (grandfather), hurry up!"

[021-149] Oh, the Crane has a very ugly neck,

[021-150] The Crane has dirty, ugly legs.

[021-151] "I wish you to be quick, _mooso me_. Hurry up, I say!" [Footnote:

[021-152] This dialogue, including the songs, is from a very curious

[021-153] Passamaquoddy version of the tale, sent to me by Louis Mitchell. As in

[021-154] all such cases, there is far more humor in the Passamaquoddy narratives

[021-155] than in the Micmac or Eskimo.]

[021-156] And all of this ill-temper and insincerity was deeply and inwardly

[021-157] detected by Uncle Crane, but he said not a word, and only meekly bent

[021-158] him down to take the traveler on his back. But when in the stream, and

[021-159] where it was deepest and most dangerous, he gave himself a shake, and

[021-160] in another instant Lox was whirling round and round like a chip in the

[021-161] rapids. And yet a little time he was dashed against the rocks, and then

[021-162] anon was thrown high and dry on the shore, but dead as a seven-year-old

[021-163] cedar cone.

[021-164] Now the Lox is a great magician at certain times and seasons, albeit

[021-165] his power fails at others. [Footnote: From this point of the legend

[021-166] onward there is an inextricable confusion as regards the four different

[021-167] versions. While the hero is decidedly a Badger in the Micmac, I regard

[021-168] the great ferocity, craft, and above all the vitality which he displays

[021-169] as far more characteristic of the Lox or Wolverine of the Passamaquoddy.

[021-170] What is almost decisively in favor of the latter theory is that in all

[021-171] the stories, despite his craft and power, he is always getting himself

[021-172] into trouble through them. This is eminently characteristic of the Lox,

[021-173] much less so of the Badger.] And he is one of those who rise from the

[021-174] dead. Now it came to pass that some days after two boys of the Kwedech

[021-175] or Mohawk race found the Lox lying dead on a rock in the sunshine, and

[021-176] the worms were crawling from him. But when they touched him he arose as

[021-177] if from sleep, and stood before them as a proud and fierce warrior. But

[021-178] he was scarce alive ere he sought to do them who had roused him to life

[021-179] a mischief; for having noted that they had fine bows, he got them into

[021-180] his hands, and broke them, yet all as if he meant it not. [Footnote: In

[021-181] the Passamaquoddy version of this tale, when Lox is thus dismembered,

[021-182] the ants, pitying him, bring his scattered members together. As soon as

[021-183] he recovers, the Wolverine, with characteristic ingratitude, amuses

[021-184] himself by trampling his benefactors to death beneath his feet.] And

[021-185] then by magic making a sound as of many children at play, afar off

[021-186] across the next point of land by the river, he bade them run and join

[021-187] the pleasant games. And when he had got them a space onward, lo, the

[021-188] sound seemed ever farther on, mingled with the murmur of the stream;

[021-189] and so they went without him, seeking it, and yet it wandered ever

[021-190] far away.
