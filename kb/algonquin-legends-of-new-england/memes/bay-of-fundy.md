# Bay of Fundy

The **Bay of Fundy** is a vast inlet of the Atlantic Ocean separating the Canadian provinces of New Brunswick and Nova Scotia. Renowned for having the highest tidal range in the world — exceeding 16 meters — the Bay of Fundy forms the maritime heartland of Wabanaki traditional territory. Its shores, islands, and extreme tidal rhythms feature prominently in several Algonquin legends, most notably as the location of [[Campobello]] Island where Charles Godfrey Leland collected Passamaquoddy oral narratives during the summer of 1882.

## Overview

The Bay of Fundy's unique geography — a funnel-shaped bay amplifying Atlantic tides — creates a landscape of daily transformation. Massive mudflats are exposed at low tide, then submerged beneath surging waters hours later. This dynamic environment shaped Wabanaki cosmology, providing both literal and metaphorical material for stories of transformation, disappearance, and return.

Leland identifies the Bay of Fundy region as the epicenter of the legends he collected:

> "originate about the Bay of Fundy, where, in the dense and frequent fogs..." (049-052).

The bay's famous mists and fog banks, produced by the collision of cold Fundy waters with warm air, appear in multiple narratives as settings for supernatural encounters and shape-shifting episodes.

## Geography

The Bay of Fundy extends approximately 270 kilometers from its mouth at the Gulf of Maine to its northeastern extremities: Chignecto Bay and the Minas Basin. The bay's most distinctive feature — its tides — is directly referenced in the legends:

> "And the famous Fundy tides, at will..." (019-156).

This passing mention in the narrative of Glooskap suggests that the tides were understood not merely as a natural phenomenon but as an expression of supernatural agency — the "will" that moves waters being connected to the culture hero's power over the natural world.

The Passamaquoddy traditional territory encompasses the western shore of the bay, including Point Pleasant, Maine, and the islands of [[Campobello]] and Grand Manan. The Micmac ranged along the eastern and northern shores, including the Minas Basin and Chignecto regions.

## Role in Legends

### Campobello: Leland's Field Station

Campobello Island, situated at the mouth of Passamaquoddy Bay (an arm of the Bay of Fundy), served as Leland's primary field station. It was here, during the summer of 1882, that he collected the majority of his Passamaquoddy narratives:

> "to collect among the Passamaquoddy Indians at Campobello, New Brunswick, their traditional stories" (001-017).

The island provided access to multiple Passamaquoddy informants, particularly Tomah Josephs, whose narratives make up a significant portion of the collection. Noel Joseph, another informant, related a story to Leland "at Campobello, August 26, 1883" (044-050).

### The Tidal World in Legend

The Bay of Fundy's tidal extremes create a world where the boundary between land and sea is perpetually shifting. This liminal quality resonates with Wabanaki stories of transformation — beings who shift between human and animal forms, between this world and the spirit world. The fog banks that roll in from the bay become, in the legends, the veil through which supernatural beings pass.

## Historical Context

At the time of Leland's research in the 1880s, the Bay of Fundy region was undergoing rapid transformation. The traditional Wabanaki economy — based on fishing, hunting, and seasonal movement — was being displaced by settler agriculture and commercial fishing. Leland's informants, including Tomah Josephs and Molly Sepsis, represented the last generation to have lived fully within the pre-contact seasonal round.

The "stone" at Indian Harbor on Campobello Island, described as "a sign of good luck" (036-004), exemplifies the continuity of sacred geography within the bay. Such landmarks anchored oral traditions to specific places, making the Bay of Fundy not merely a backdrop for Wabanaki mythology but an active participant in it.
