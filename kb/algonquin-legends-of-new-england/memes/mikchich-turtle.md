# How Glooskap Made His Uncle Mikchich the Turtle into a Great Man

## Narrative

Coming to Piktook (Pictou), Glooskap met with a man whom he loved all his life. This man, whose name in Micmac is Mikchich and in Passamaquoddy Chick-we-notchk, meaning the Turtle, was poor, lazy, no longer young, and not very clever or wise in any way (008-079). Some say he was Glooskap's uncle, though others think this was by adoption (008-083). Yet the old fellow bore all his wants with such good nature that the Master, taking him in great affection, resolved to make of him a mighty man (008-085).

When a great feast with games was to be held in Piktook, Mikchich despaired of attending, saying: "Poor and old and plain am I; I have not even garments fit for a feast; better were it for me to smoke my pipe at home" (008-100). But Glooskap bade him put on his belt, and when he had done that, Mikchich became so young and handsome that no man or woman ever saw the like (008-109). Glooskap dressed him in his own best clothes and promised that whenever he should be a man, he would be the comeliest of men; and because he was patient and tough, he should, as an animal, become the hardest to kill of all creatures on the face of the earth (008-112).

Thus transformed, Mikchich went to the feast and cast his eyes upon the youngest daughter of the chief of Piktook — the loveliest maiden in the land. Glooskap, taking a bunch of wampum, proposed for Mikchich, and the mother at once consented (008-121). The girl made up a bed of fresh twigs covered with a great white bear-skin, and they were married (008-125).

But danger awaited. The young men of Piktook, jealous of the newcomer, sought to slay him during a great game at ball. Glooskap forewarned his uncle and gave him the power to leap high over the lodge to escape them (008-135). Mikchich did this twice, seeming like a bird flying; but the third time he was caught on the top of the tent-poles, dangling in the smoke (008-144). Then Glooskap declared: "Uncle, I will now make you the _sogmo_, or great chief of the Tortoises, and you shall bear up a great nation" (008-149). He smoked Mikchich so long with his pipe that his skin became a hard shell, and the marks of the smoke may be seen on the tortoise to this day (008-155). Removing his entrails, he destroyed them so that but one short one was left, and declared that though his head be cut off, it would live for nine days, and his heart would beat as long when taken from his body (008-161). So Mikchich rejoiced greatly.

## Significance

This legend explains the origin of the Turtle totem, which was of the highest rank among the Algonquins — especially the Lenni-Lenape, called by the Passamaquoddies _Lel-le-mabe_, "the people" (008-067). The narrative encodes several cultural values: the power of good nature to earn great favor, the belief that outward form can be transformed by supernatural intervention, and the sacred origin of the turtle as a being blessed with extraordinary resilience. The smoking transformation — where Glooskap's pipe smoke literally changes Mikchich's skin into shell — is a distinctly Algonquin motif linking tobacco, a sacred substance, with acts of creation and metamorphosis.

## Variants

The legend has been compiled from six different versions: the narration of Tomah Josephs, a Passamaquoddy; the Anglo-Indian manuscript; two accounts in the Rand manuscript; an account in _The Maritime Provinces_; and one by Mrs. W. Wallace Brown (008-062). This multiplicity of sources attests to the tale's wide currency across Mi'kmaq and Passamaquoddy communities. In Mr. Rand's manuscript, the transformation is effected by the smoke of the tent-fire rather than by Glooskap's pipe — a significant variation reflecting different storytelling traditions (008-151).
