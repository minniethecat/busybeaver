---[015-001] And the next said, "I am very poor, and my life is hard. I toil, but

[015-002] can barely make my living. I would fain be rich."

[015-003] Now the third replied, "I am of low estate, being despised and hated by

[015-004] all my people, and I wish to be loved and respected." And to all these

[015-005] the Master made answer, "So shall it be!"

[015-006] And taking his medicine-bag (_Upsakumoode_, M.) he gave unto each

[015-007] a small box, and bade them keep it closed until they should be once

[015-008] more at home. [Footnote: In this version (Rand manuscript) there is a

[015-009] fourth Indian introduced,--he who would fain be tall and long-lived,

[015-010] and is changed to a tree. As it is precisely the same tale as that of

[015-011] the three who became cypresses or pines, I have not repeated it.] And

[015-012] on returning to the wigwam he also, gave to each of them new garments;

[015-013] in all their lives they had never seen or heard of such rich apparel or

[015-014] such ornaments as they now had. Then when it was time to depart, as

[015-015] they knew not the way to their home, he arose and went with them. Now

[015-016] they had been more than a year in coming. But he, having put on his

[015-017] belt, went forth, and they followed, till in the forenoon he led them

[015-018] to the top of a high mountain, from which in the distance they beheld

[015-019] yet another, the blue outline of which could just be seen above the

[015-020] horizon. And having been told that their way was unto it, they thought

[015-021] it would be a week's journey to reach it. But they went on, and in the

[015-022] middle of the afternoon of the same day they were there, on the summit

[015-023] of the second mountain. And looking from this afar, all was familiar to

[015-024] them--hill and river, and wood and lakes; all was in their memory. "And

[015-025] there," said the Master, pointing unto it,--"there is your own village!"

[015-026] So he left them alone, and they went on their way, and before the sun had

[015-027] set were safe at home.

[015-028] Yet when they came no one knew them, because of the great change in

[015-029] their appearance and their fine attire, the like of which had never

[015-030] been seen by man in those days. But having made themselves known to

[015-031] their friends, all that were there of old and young gathered together

[015-032] to gaze upon and hear what they had to say. And they were amazed.

[015-033] Then each of them, having opened his box, found therein an unguent,

[015-034] rich and fragrant, and with this they rubbed their bodies completely.

[015-035] And they were ever after so fragrant from the divine anointing that all

[015-036] sought to be near them. Happy were they who could but sniff at the

[015-037] blessed smell which came from them.

[015-038] Now he who had been despised for his deformity and weakness and

[015-039] meanness became beautiful and strong and stately as a pine-tree. There

[015-040] was no man in all the land so graceful or of such good behavior.

[015-041] And he who had desired abundance had it, in all fullness, his wish. For

[015-042] the moose and caribou came to him in the forest, the fish leaped into

[015-043] his nets, all men gave unto him, and he gave unto all freely, to the

[015-044] end.

[015-045] And he that had been wicked and of evil mind, hasty and cruel, became

[015-046] meek and patient, good and gentle, and he made others like himself. And

[015-047] he had his reward, for there was a blessing upon him as upon all those

[015-048] who had wished wisely even unto the end of their days. [Footnote: This

[015-049] beautiful story, in its original simplicity, reminds one of the

[015-050] tenderest biblical narratives. There is in it nothing reflected or

[015-051] second-hand; it is a very ancient or truly aboriginal tale. I can but

[015-052] sincerely regret my utter inability to do justice to it. The pen of a

[015-053] great master would be required to describe the fairyland freshness and

[015-054] light of Glooskap's home as it is _felt_ in the original by men

[015-055] far more familiar with the forest in all its loveliness at all seasons

[015-056] than any white writer can be. The _naivete_ or simplicity of the

[015-057] pilgrims is as striking as that of the narrator or poet, to whom fine

[015-058] clothes--a Homeric trait--are as wonderful as all the deeds of magic

[015-059] which he describes.

[015-060] In this and other tales a man is represented as being punished by being

[015-061] turned into a tree, so that he can never leave a certain spot. This is

[015-062] a kind of imprisonment. In the Edda the Ash Yggdrasil is the prison of

[015-063] Iduna.

[015-064] "She ill brooked

[015-065] her descent

[015-066] under the hoar tree's

[015-067] trunk confined."

[015-068] (_Hrofnagaldr Odins_, 7.)

[015-069] It is to keep a man or a woman in a certain place, as prisoner, that

[015-070] the characters described in the Indian and Norse myths are put into

[015-071] trees.

[015-072] This was related to Mr. Rand by Benjamin Brooks, a Micmac.]

[015-073] _Of Glooskap and the Sinful Serpent._

[015-074] (Passamaquoddy.)

[015-075] Of old time it befell that Glooskap had an enemy, an evil man, a sinful

[015-076] beast, a great sorcerer. And this man, after trying many things, made

[015-077] himself a great serpent, hoping so to slay the Master.

[015-078] Of old time Glooskap met a boy whose name was _'Nmmokswess_, the

[015-079] Sable. [Footnote: Evidently no other than Marten, or the Abistanooch of

[015-080] the Micmac mythology.] And the boy had a flute: whoever played on it

[015-081] could entice unto him all the animals. And once, when the Master was

[015-082] afar, the boy broke the flute, and in his great sorrow he would not

[015-083] return home, but wandered away into the wilderness. Now Glooskap knew

[015-084] in his heart that the flute was broken: he who is a magician knows at

[015-085] once of a great evil. And coming home, he asked of the grandmother

[015-086] where the boy was, and she could only weep. Then the Master said,

[015-087] "Though I roam forever, yet will I find the boy." So he went forth, and

[015-088] he tracked him in the snow for three days; and on the third night he

[015-089] heard some one singing in a hollow; and it was a magic song, that which

[015-090] the _m'teoulin_ sings when he is in dire need and death is near.

[015-091] And making a circle round about the place, Glooskap looked down and saw

[015-092] a wigwam, and heard the voice more distinctly as he drew nearer; and it

[015-093] was the voice of the boy, and he was singing a song against all of the

[015-094] snake kind. And he was wandering about the wigwam, seeking a straight

[015-095] stick.

[015-096] Then Glooskap understood all the thing, and how the boy had been

[015-097] enticed into the wilderness by the evil arts of At-o-sis, the Snake,

[015-098] and that the Great Serpent was in the wigwam, and had sent him out to

[015-099] seek a straight stick. Then Glooskap, singing again softly, bade him

[015-100] get a very crooked one, and told what more to do. So the boy got an

[015-101] exceedingly crooked one; and when he entered, the Snake, seeing it,

[015-102] said, "Why hast thou got such a bad stick?" And the boy, answering,

[015-103] said, "Truly, it is very crooked, but that which is crookedest may be

[015-104] made straightest, and I know a charm whereby this can be done; for I

[015-105] will but heat this stick in the fire, and, then I will make it quite

[015-106] straight, as you shall see." Now At-o-sis was very anxious to behold

[015-107] this wonderful thing, and he looked closely; but the boy, as soon as

[015-108] the end of the stick was red-hot, thrust it into his eyes and blinded

[015-109] him, and ran forth. Yet the Snake followed him; but when he was without

[015-110] the wigwam he met the Master, who slew him out of hand. [Footnote: This

[015-111] curious legend is suggestive of Ulysses and the Cyclops. The enemies of

[015-112] Glooskap are all cannibals; the boy is sent out for a straight stick to

[015-113] serve as a spit to roast him on. It is not impossible that the Snake,

[015-114] in some perfect version of the tale, has but a single eye since many of

[015-115] the evil creatures of red Indian mythology are half stone lengthwise.

[015-116] But the whole story is full of strange hints. It was told me by Tomah

[015-117] Josephs, at Campobello, N. B.]

[015-118] Of old times. This is an end of the story.

[015-119] _The Tale of Glooskap as told by another Indian. Showing how the Toad

[015-120] and Porcupine lost their Noses._

[015-121] (Micmac.)

[015-122] In the old time. Far before men knew themselves, in the light before

[015-123] the sun, Glooskap and his brother were as yet unborn; they waited for

[015-124] the day to appear. Then they talked together, and the youngest said,

[015-125] "Why should I wait? I will go into the world and begin my life at

[015-126] once." Then the elder said, "Not so, for this were a great evil." But

[015-127] the younger gave no heed to any wisdom: in his wickedness he broke

[015-128] through his mother's side, he rent the wall; his beginning of life was

[015-129] his mother's death.

[015-130] Now, in after years, the younger brother would learn in what lay the

[015-131] secret of the elder's death. And Glooskap, being crafty, told the truth

[015-132] and yet lied; for his name was the Liar, yet did he never lie for evil

[015-133] or aught to harm. So he told his brother that the blow of a ball, or

[015-134] handful of the down of feathers, would take away his life; and this was

[015-135] true, for it would stun him, but it would not prevent his returning to

[015-136] life. Then Glooskap asked the younger for his own secret. And he, being

[015-137] determined to give the elder no time, answered truly and fearlessly, "I

[015-138] can only be slain by the stroke of a cat-tail or bulrush."

[015-139] And then the younger, having gathered the down of bird's feathers,

[015-140] struck the elder, so that he fell dead, and therein he told the truth.

[015-141] But he soon recovered, and in that was his deceit. Howbeit it was well

[015-142] for the world and well for him that he then gathered bulrushes and

[015-143] smote his younger brother, so that he died. But the plant never grew

[015-144] that could harm the Master, wherefore he is alive to this day.

[015-145] Who was his mother? The female Turtle was his mother.

[015-146] The Master was the Lord of Men and Beasts. Beasts and Men, one as the

[015-147] other, he ruled them all Great was his army, his tribe was All. In it

[015-148] the Great Golden Eagle was a chief; he married a female Caribou. The

[015-149] Turtle was Glooskap's uncle; he married a daughter of the Golden Eagle

[015-150] and Caribou. Of all these things there are many and long traditions.

[015-151] Our people tell them in the winter by the fire: the old people know

[015-152] them; the young forget them and the wisdom which is in them.

[015-153] When the Turtle married, the Master bade him make a feast, and wished

[015-154] that the banquet should be a mighty one. To do this he gave him great

[015-155] power. He bade him go down to a point of rocks by the sea, where many

[015-156] whales were always to be found. He bade him bring one; he gave him

[015-157] power to do so, but he set a mark, or an appointed space, and bade him

[015-158] not go an inch beyond it. So the Turtle went down to the sea; he caught

[015-159] a great whale, he bore it to camp; it seemed to him easy to do this.

[015-160] But like all men there was in him vain curiosity; the falsehood of

[015-161] disobedience was in him, and to try the Master he went beyond the mark;

[015-162] and as he did this he lost his magic strength; he became as a man; even

[015-163] as a common mortal his nerves weakened, and he fell, crushed flat

[015-164] beneath the weight of the great fish.

[015-165] Then men ran to Glooskap, saying that Turtle was dead. But the Master

[015-166] answered, "Cut up the Whale; he who is now dead will revive." So they

[015-167] cut it up; (and when the feast was ready) Turtle came in yawning, and

[015-168] stretching out his leg he cried, "How tired I am! Truly, I must have

[015-169] overslept myself." Now from this time all men greatly feared Glooskap,

[015-170] for they saw that he was a spirit.
