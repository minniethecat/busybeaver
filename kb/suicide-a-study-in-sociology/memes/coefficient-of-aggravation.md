# Coefficient of Aggravation

## Overview

Throughout *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim employs a distinctive
statistical instrument — the **coefficient of aggravation**
(*coefficient d'aggravation*) — to measure the degree to which
specific social conditions increase the probability of suicide. The
coefficient is typically expressed as a ratio: the suicide rate of the
exposed group divided by that of the reference group. A coefficient of
2.0 means the condition doubles the suicide risk; a coefficient of
1.0 means no effect. (doc_final.md:6052, 6633, 8316)

> Il y a donc une aggravation qui tient au célibat. Mais elle est
> beaucoup plus marquée pour les hommes que pour les femmes.
> (doc_final.md:5898)

## The Logic of Measurement

### Constructing the Coefficient

The coefficient of aggravation is constructed by comparing the suicide
rate of a group exposed to a putative aggravating condition with the
rate of a comparable group not so exposed:

```
Coefficient d'aggravation = Rate(exposed group) / Rate(reference group)
```

A coefficient > 1.0 indicates aggravation; a coefficient < 1.0
indicates preservation (protection). The further the coefficient
departs from 1.0, the stronger the effect. (doc_final.md:6052, 8316)

### The Symmetry with Preservation

The coefficient of aggravation is the **conceptual twin** of the
*coefficient of preservation* (*coefficient de préservation*).
Mathematically, they are reciprocals: if a condition has a
preservation coefficient of 0.67 (reducing suicide risk by one-third),
it has an aggravation coefficient of 1/0.67 ≈ 1.5 for the absence of
that condition. Durkheim uses both measures depending on whether the
emphasis is on the **protective** or the **aggravating** aspect of a
social condition. (doc_final.md:6549, 6633)

## Key Empirical Findings

### Celibacy and Widowhood

The most extensively analysed case of aggravation concerns marital
status. Durkheim calculates that:

- **Celibacy** (never-married status) carries an aggravation
  coefficient relative to the married, particularly for men
- **Widowhood** produces an even greater aggravation than celibacy:
  the coefficient for widowers is approximately 1.6, while for
  widows it is much lower

(doc_final.md:5898, 5933, 5958, 6317)

The magnitude of these coefficients varies by sex: the aggravation
of celibacy is substantially greater for men than for women, a
finding that Durkheim uses to argue that marriage provides different
functions for men (regulation) and women (protection through
integration). (doc_final.md:6052, 6116)

> …l'importance de cette aggravation qu'une idée imparfaitement
> exacte. En réalité, en un coefficient d'aggravation.
> (doc_final.md:5958, 6052)

### The Age-Structured Aggravation of Widowhood

Durkheim's analysis is unusually precise in disaggregating the
aggravation of widowhood by **age at bereavement**:

| Age Group | Coefficient of Aggravation (Men) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Young widowers | Highest (early widowhood is most destabilising) |
| Middle-aged widowers | Intermediate |
| Elderly widowers | Lowest (approaches 1.0) |

(doc_final.md:6758, 6633)

The declining aggravation with age suggests that widowhood's
destabilising effect is mediated by the life-course: older persons
have more crystallised habits and attachments that buffer the shock.
(doc_final.md:6549, 6753-6758)

### Military Suicide

One of Durkheim's most striking applications of the aggravation
coefficient concerns **military suicide**. Across European armies,
soldiers kill themselves at rates dramatically higher than civilian
males of the same age:

| Comparison | Coefficient of Aggravation |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| French army vs. civilian males (same age) | ~1.6 |
| Prussian army vs. civilian males | ~1.94 (approaching 4.0 for NCOs) |
| Elite corps vs. line regiments | Up to 2.15 |

(doc_final.md:8316, 8351, 8365, 8390-8400)

These coefficients are central to Durkheim's argument that military
suicide exemplifies **altruistic suicide** — the aggravation cannot
be explained by the hardships of military life (Prussian soldiers
live better than Prussian civilians) but by the **excessive
integration** of military society, which devalues individual life.
(doc_final.md:8351, 8452)

### The Decreasing Aggravation with Length of Service

Durkheim makes a crucial refinement: the aggravation of military
suicide **decreases with length of service**. Recruits and junior
soldiers have the highest coefficients; veterans and senior NCOs
approach civilian rates:

> …d'aggravation diminuer à mesure qu'ils sont depuis plus longtemps
> sous les drapeaux. (doc_final.md:8452)

This temporal gradient is theoretically significant. It suggests
that the military *penchant* for suicide is not a selection effect
(men with suicidal dispositions choosing the army) but a
**socialisation effect** — the military milieu actively produces the
altruistic disposition, and its intensity diminishes as the soldier
matures within the institution. (doc_final.md:8452, 8485)

## The Theoretical Status of the Coefficient

### Beyond Description: Causal Inference

The coefficient of aggravation is not merely descriptive. Durkheim
uses it as a tool of **causal inference** through the method of
concomitant variation. If the coefficient:
1. Varies systematically with the intensity of the hypothesised
   social cause, and
2. Does not vary with plausible confounds (age, climate, race),

— then the social cause is confirmed. The military coefficients are
a model of this inferential logic: the aggravation is highest where
the *esprit militaire* is strongest, regardless of material
conditions. (doc_final.md:5187, 8351, 8377)

### The Coefficient as a Social Fact

It is important to recognise the coefficient as itself a **social
fact** — a statistical regularity that expresses a collective force.
The coefficient summarises thousands of individual acts into a single
number that characterises the **collective disposition** of a group.
In this sense, it is the operationalisation of the *penchant
collectif*. (doc_final.md:6052, 8316)

> …ce qui fait un coefficient d'aggravation… nous pourrons être assurés
> non seulement qu'il est réel, mais encore qu'il est dû au service
> militaire lui-même. (doc_final.md:8385)
