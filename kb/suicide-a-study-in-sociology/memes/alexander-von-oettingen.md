# Alexander von Oettingen

## Overview

**Alexander von Oettingen** (1827-1905) was a Baltic German Lutheran theologian and statistician whose monumental work *Die Moralstatistik und die christliche Sittenlehre* (Moral Statistics and Christian Ethics, 1868; 3rd edition, Erlangen, 1882) furnished one of the principal empirical foundations for Durkheim's *Le Suicide*. (001-010)

Durkheim cited Oettingen's statistical tables throughout his study — on religious differentials in suicide, the relation of education and occupation to voluntary death, and the distribution of suicide across European nations — making Oettingen, alongside [[Enrico Morselli]] and [[Louis Brierre de Boismont]], one of the three indispensable statistical sources for the sociological analysis of suicide rates.

## Die Moralstatistik (1868–1882)

Oettingen's *Moralstatistik* was among the first systematic attempts to apply statistical methods to moral and religious phenomena. The work's extensive annexes (tableaux annexes) contained detailed tabulations of suicide rates cross-referenced by confession, profession, education, and marital status across the German states and neighboring European countries during the 1860s–1870s.

| Dimension | Data Coverage | Durkheim's Usage |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Suicide by confession | Protestant, Catholic, Jewish populations (Prussia, Bavaria, German states) | Book II, Ch. 2 — religious differentials |
| Suicide by occupation | Professional classifications across German states, 1872–1878 | Book II, Ch. 3 — occupational immunity |
| Suicide by education | Literate vs. illiterate populations, confession-stratified | Book II, Ch. 2 — education and suicide |
| Domestic suicide | Married vs. widowed vs. divorced persons | Book II, Ch. 3 — domestic society |
| Temporal trends | Annual suicide counts, 1840s–1870s | Introduction — constancy of rates |

The data tables Oettingen compiled were exceptional for their era: they permitted Durkheim to test causal hypotheses by disaggregating suicide rates along multiple social dimensions simultaneously — a methodological advance that separated Durkheim's approach from mere statistical description.

## Religious Differentials

One of Oettingen's most important contributions to Durkheim's argument was the statistical demonstration that suicide rates varied systematically by religious confession. Oettingen's tables showed:

> D'après Oettingen, *Moralstatistik*, tables annexes, p. 110. (001-025)

These data revealed that Protestant populations consistently exhibited higher suicide rates than Catholic populations within the same German states — a pattern that could not be explained by doctrinal differences alone, but rather reflected, as Durkheim argued, differences in the degree of social integration fostered by each religious community.

Oettingen's occupational tables further showed that educated, literate populations (disproportionately Protestant) had elevated suicide rates:

> Les chiffres relatifs aux époux lettrés sont empruntés à Oettingen, *Moralstatistik*, annexes, tableau 85; ils se rapportent aux années 1872-78, les suicides à la période 1864-76. (001-030)

## Occupational and Educational Data

Oettingen's profession-based suicide tables (tableaux 83, 85, 97) provided Durkheim with the empirical basis to test and refine his theory of social integration. The data showed that:

- **Literate populations** had substantially higher suicide rates than illiterate ones within the same confession
- **Professions requiring higher education** (press, arts, liberal professions) showed elevated suicide propensity
- **Married individuals with children** demonstrated a protective "coefficient of preservation" against suicide

| Professional Category | Suicide Rate (Prussia, 1872–78) | Interpretation |
|----------------------|--------------------------------|----------------|
| Press and arts | 279 per million (ordinary for the category) | Moderate elevation |
| Liberal professions | Elevated | Linked to individuation |
| Agricultural workers | Low | High social integration |

Durkheim cited these tables to demonstrate that education itself was not the cause of elevated suicide — rather, education was a concomitant of the weakening of traditional collective beliefs, and it was this weakening, not learning per se, that disposed individuals to suicide:

> Oettingen, *Moralstatistik*, p. 586. (001-040)

## Durkheim's Methodological Relationship to Oettingen

Unlike [[Enrico Morselli]], whose theoretical framework Durkheim systematically dismantled, Oettingen served primarily as a **data source** rather than a theoretical antagonist. Durkheim did not engage deeply with Oettingen's theological framework — which interpreted suicide as a symptom of moral decay requiring Christian renewal — but instead appropriated Oettingen's empirical tables while reframing them within a sociological explanatory schema.

Oettingen's own classification of suicide motives was critiqued alongside Wagner's by Morselli, whom Durkheim cites approvingly:

> Nous ne parlons pas des classifications proposées par Wagner et par Oettingen; Morselli lui-même en a fait la critique d'une manière décisive (p. 160). (001-050)

This layered relationship — Oettingen supplying data, Morselli critiquing Oettingen's classifications, Durkheim refuting Morselli's racial theory while using both their data — exemplifies the cumulative, dialectical character of nineteenth-century suicide statistics as a field of inquiry.

## Women and Domestic Homicide

Beyond suicide statistics, Oettingen contributed data on women's criminality that Durkheim deployed in his analysis of gender and homicide:

> D'après Oettingen, la moitié des meurtres domestiques lui serait imputable. (001-055)

This observation — that women committed domestic murder at rates comparable to men when opportunity permitted — supported Durkheim's broader argument that women's lower suicide rates reflected differences in social participation and collective sensibility, not innate biological characteristics. The same statistical lens Oettingen applied to suicide thus illuminated the gendered structure of violence more broadly.

## Legacy

Oettingen's *Moralstatistik* represented a transitional moment in the history of social science: a theological project that, by its very commitment to empirical rigor, produced data that could be secularized and reanalyzed within Durkheim's sociological framework. The comprehensive statistical annexes — tables 1–3 (international trends), 61 (seasonal patterns), 83, 85, 97 (occupational breakdowns), and 103–120 (national comparisons) — constituted a data infrastructure that outlasted the interpretive framework within which they were originally assembled.
