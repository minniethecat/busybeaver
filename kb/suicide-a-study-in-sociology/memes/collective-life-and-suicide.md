# Collective Life and Suicide

## Overview

**Collective life** (*vie collective*) is the shared social activity — beliefs, practices, exchanges, and sentiments — through which a society maintains its moral unity. In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim demonstrated that the intensity of collective life is the primary determinant of a society's suicide rate: the more vigorous the collective life, the more strongly individuals feel its protective and regulative influence. (002-015)

This principle explains both the protective effect of social integration (egoistic suicide) and the seasonal and diurnal rhythms of suicide.

## Mechanism

Durkheim argued that collective life operates through two interrelated channels:

| Channel | Effect | Suicide Type Affected |
|---------|--------|-----------------------|
| **Moral density** | Strengthens shared beliefs and norms that bind individuals to life | Egoistic suicide |
| **Social regulation** | Imposes limits on individual desires and expectations | Anomic suicide |

When collective life is intense — during periods of national crisis, within tightly integrated religious communities, or within dense family structures — the individual feels less isolated and more meaningfully connected to the group. Conversely, when collective life weakens, the individual is thrown back upon their own resources, and suicide rates rise. (002-012)

## Seasonal and Diurnal Variation

One of Durkheim's most striking empirical findings was that suicide rates follow the rhythm of collective life:

- **Seasonal pattern**: Suicide rises from winter (minimum) to summer (maximum), tracking not temperature but the intensification of social activity — as days lengthen, the "career of collective life" expands, opening more space for its effects to manifest.
- **Urban-rural difference**: The seasonal amplitude is far greater in the countryside (52–68% between minimum and maximum) than in large cities (20–25%), because rural social life is more strongly seasonal, while urban collective life continues more evenly year-round.

(002-015)

## Relationship to Suicide Types

Durkheim used collective life to distinguish his two primary suicide etiologies:
- **Weakened collective life** → The individual is insufficiently bound to the group → [[Egoistic Suicide]]
- **Excessive collective life** → The individual is so thoroughly absorbed by the group that their own existence counts for little → [[Altruistic Suicide]]

As Durkheim summarized: "For the individual to hold so little place in collective life, they must be almost totally absorbed in the group." (002-019)
