# Egoism

## Overview

In the conceptual architecture of *Le Suicide* (1897), **egoism** (égoïsme) occupies a central position as the social-psychological condition that generates the most characteristic form of modern suicide. Durkheim defines egoism not as ordinary selfishness — the pursuit of personal advantage — but as a structural state of the relation between individual and society: it is the condition in which **the individual self (moi individuel) affirms itself with excess against the social self (moi social)**, and collective purposes lose their power to regulate individual conduct. (doc_final.md:7589-7591)

This concept is the theoretical bridge between Durkheim's empirical findings — the Protestant–Catholic suicide differential, the protective effect of marriage and family, the decline of suicide during wars — and his typology of suicide. Each empirical pattern reflects a variation in the degree of **social integration**, and egoism is the name for the pathological pole of that continuum.

## Egoism as a Collective State, Not an Individual Trait

Durkheim is emphatic that egoism, as he uses the term, is not a psychological disposition of particular individuals. It is a **property of the social group** — a state of collective existence that can be more or less pronounced in a given society, class, or historical period:

> Nous appelons égoïsme cet état où le moi individuel s'affirme avec excès en face du moi social et aux dépens de ce dernier. (doc_final.md:7589-7591)

Individual egoism — the selfishness of a particular person — is merely the manifestation in individual psychology of this underlying collective condition. The causes are social; the expression is individual. (doc_final.md:7822-7830)

## The Mechanism: Insufficient Social Integration

Egoism arises when the bonds attaching the individual to collective life are too weak. Durkheim identifies several structural sources:

| Source of Under-Integration | Mechanism | Empirical Evidence |
|---------------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Religious individualism (Protestantism) | Fewer shared beliefs and practices; free inquiry dissolves collective dogma | Higher suicide rates in Protestant regions |
| Family dissolution (celibacy, widowhood) | Thinner domestic collective life; fewer daily interactions | Higher suicide among unmarried and widowed |
| Political individualism | Weak patriotic or civic attachment; private life dominates | Decline of suicide during national wars |
| Intellectual over-development | Excessive reflection detaches the individual from collective sentiments | Higher suicide among educated classes |

## Egoism and Egoistic Suicide

Egoism is the generating cause of [[egoistic-suicide]]. When collective purposes lose their grip on the individual, life itself loses meaning:

> L'égoïste est triste parce qu'il ne voit rien de réel au monde que l'individu... La tristesse de l'égoïste est une tristesse sans cause, une mélancolie sans objet. (doc_final.md:8186-8190)

Durkheim's theory is a radical inversion of common sense: suicide, which appears the most individual of acts, is in fact produced by a deficiency of *individuality* in the sociological sense — a failure of the group to implant in the individual reasons for living that transcend the self. The egoist does not suffer from thinking too much of himself; he suffers because there is nothing *beyond* himself worth caring about.

## Against Psychological Reductionism

Throughout his analysis, Durkheim insists that egoism cannot be reduced to a psychological trait. Even the observation that children are "naturally egoistic" yet rarely commit suicide — because their egoism is directed outward, toward objects and activities, not inward toward existential emptiness — supports the sociological interpretation: suicide requires not just self-preoccupation but a specifically social form of detachment. (doc_final.md:7647-7671)
