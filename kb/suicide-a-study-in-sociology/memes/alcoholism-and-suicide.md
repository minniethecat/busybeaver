# Alcoholism and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim subjects the widely held belief that alcoholism
is a major cause of suicide to rigorous empirical scrutiny. At the time of his
writing, alcoholism was habitually blamed for nearly all the ills of modern
civilisation — the progress of insanity, pauperism, and criminality — and the
question naturally arose whether it also influenced the development of suicide.
Durkheim concludes, on the basis of systematic geographic and statistical
comparison, that **alcoholism has no definite influence on the social suicide
rate**. (doc_final.md:1761-1763)

The *a priori* implausibility of the alcoholism hypothesis is noted at the
outset: suicide claims its greatest number of victims among the most cultivated
and affluent classes, precisely the milieux in which alcoholism has its fewest
clients. But Durkheim insists that nothing can prevail against the facts; the
question must be settled empirically. (doc_final.md:1765-1769)

## Geographic Comparison: France

Durkheim's first line of evidence is cartographic. He compares the French map
of suicides with the map of prosecutions for abuse of alcoholic beverages and
finds **almost no correspondence** between them:

> Si l'on compare la carte française des suicides avec celle des poursuites
> pour abus de boissons, on n'aperçoit entre elles presque aucun rapport.
> (doc_final.md:1774-1776)

The suicide map is characterised by two major centres of contamination: one in
the Île-de-France extending eastward, the other on the Mediterranean coast from
Marseille to Nice. The alcoholism map shows a completely different distribution,
with three principal centres in Normandy (particularly Seine-Inférieure),
Brittany (Finistère and surrounding departments), and the Rhône region. At the
point of suicide, the Rhône is not above average, most Norman departments are
below average, and Brittany is almost entirely free of suicide. The geographies
of the two phenomena are too different for alcoholism to be assigned an
important role in the production of suicide. (doc_final.md:1777-1786)

## Alcoholic Insanity and Suicide

Durkheim extends the comparison from prosecutions for drunkenness to mental
illnesses caused by alcoholism. Grouping French departments into eight classes
according to their suicide rates, he examines the average number of cases of
insanity of alcoholic origin in each class, using data from Dr. Lunier:

| Suicide rate (per 100,000) | Alcoholic insanity (per 100 admissions) |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Below 50 (5 departments) | 11.45 |
| 51–75 (18 departments) | 12.07 |
| 76–100 (15 departments) | 11.92 |
| 101–150 (20 departments) | 13.42 |
| 151–200 (10 departments) | 14.57 |
| 201–250 (9 departments) | 13.26 |
| 251–300 (4 departments) | 16.32 |
| Above 300 (5 departments) | 13.47 |

The two columns do not correspond. While suicides increase from simple to
sextuple and beyond, the proportion of alcoholic insanities increases by barely
a few units, and the increase is irregular. If alcoholism acts on suicide as a
psychopathic state, it can only do so through the mental disturbances it
determines — and these show no systematic relationship to suicide rates.
(doc_final.md:1788-1828)

## Alcohol Consumption and Suicide: Cross-National Evidence

A closer relationship initially appears between the quantity of alcohol consumed
and the tendency to suicide, at least within France. The northern departments
consume the most alcohol and also suffer the most suicide. However, the
configurations of the two maps differ in critical respects: the maximum of
alcohol consumption is in Normandy and the North, degrading toward Paris, while
the maximum of suicide is in the Seine and surrounding departments, already less
intense in Normandy and not reaching the North at all. (doc_final.md:1851-1866)

The cross-national evidence is decisive. Moving northward from France, alcohol
consumption increases almost regularly while suicide does not develop
correspondingly:

| Country | Alcohol consumption (litres per capita) |
|---------|---------------------------------------|
| France (1873) | 2.84 |
| Belgium (1870) | 8.56 |
| England (1870–71) | 9.07 |

Despite far higher alcohol consumption in Belgium and England, their suicide
rates do not match the pattern that the alcoholism hypothesis would predict.
(doc_final.md:1867-1870)

## Durkheim's Conclusion

Durkheim's final position is nuanced. He does not deny that alcoholic suicides
exist — they are evidently numerous — but insists that alcoholism is not among
the factors on which the **social suicide rate** depends. Even in France, where
the most influence is sometimes attributed to alcoholism, only one-tenth of
suicide cases would be imputable to it. The social suicide rate is determined by
structural social causes, not by the distribution of individual psychopathology.
(doc_final.md:4729-4731, 8419-8424)
