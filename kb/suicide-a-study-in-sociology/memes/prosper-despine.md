# Prosper Despine

## Overview

**Prosper Despine** (1812–1892) was a French psychiatrist who made significant contributions to the study of moral contagion and the statistical relationship between suicide and violent crime. His works *De la contagion morale* (*On Moral Contagion*, 1870) and *Psychologie naturelle* (*Natural Psychology*, 1840) bridged psychiatry and moral statistics at a time when the boundaries between these fields were still fluid. Alongside [[André-Michel Guerry]], Despine was among the earliest researchers to document the inverse geographical distribution of suicide and crimes against persons in France — a finding that became foundational to the Italian criminological school's theory of a necessary antagonism between homicide and suicide.

## The Geographical Discovery

In Livre II, Chapitre II of *Le Suicide*, Durkheim traced the lineage of the homicide-suicide antagonism thesis. The empirical observation originated with [[André-Michel Guerry]], who noted in 1833 that crimes against persons were twice as numerous in the southern departments of France as in the northern ones, while the inverse held for suicide. (12994–12996)

Despine extended and quantified Guerry's observation. He calculated that in the 14 departments where blood crimes were most frequent, there were only 30 suicides per million inhabitants, whereas in 14 other departments where the same crimes were much rarer, the suicide rate reached 82 per million. (12996–12998) Despine further noted that in the Seine department, only 17 out of 100 prosecutions involved crimes against persons, while the suicide rate averaged 427 per million; in Corsica, by contrast, 83 percent of prosecutions were for violent crimes while suicide stood at only 18 per million. (12999–13001)

Durkheim acknowledged the descriptive accuracy of Despine's figures but rejected the causal inference that the Italian school — [[Enrico Ferri]] and [[Enrico Morselli]] — drew from them. For Durkheim, the inverse geographical distribution did not prove a direct psychological antagonism between homicide and suicide. The real explanatory variable was social structure: the same low level of social integration that elevated suicide in the urbanized, individualistic North also depressed violent crime, while the intense collective life of the South simultaneously raised homicide and lowered suicide.

## Moral Contagion and Imitation

Despine's *De la contagion morale* addressed the broader question of whether immoral acts — including suicide, murder, and other crimes — could spread through imitation. This placed Despine within the same intellectual field as [[Gabriel Tarde]], whose *Les lois de l'imitation* (1890) made imitation the central mechanism of social life, and [[Paul Moreau de Tours]], whose *De la contagion du suicide* (1871) argued that suicidal contagion required organic predisposition.

Durkheim's treatment of the imitation question in Livre I, Chapitre IV engaged with this French psychiatric tradition, including Despine's work. While Durkheim acknowledged that imitation could explain the local transmission of individual suicidal acts, he insisted that it could not explain the stability of national suicide rates — a stability that required the action of genuine social causes.
