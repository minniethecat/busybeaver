# Social Fact

## Overview

A **social fact** (*fait social*) is the foundational unit of Durkheimian sociology. In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim demonstrated the power of this concept by treating the suicide rate itself as a social fact — not merely the sum of individual acts but a phenomenon *sui generis* with its own unity and causal logic. (002-020)

The suicide rate, Durkheim showed, is stable for each society, changes only with social crises, and varies systematically across groups — proving that it is determined by collective, not individual, forces.

## Characteristics

Durkheim defined social facts by three properties:
1. **Externality** — they exist outside individual consciousness
2. **Constraint** — they exert coercive power over individuals
3. **Collective nature** — they are properties of the group, not reducible to individual psychology

The suicide rate exemplifies all three: no individual decides a society's suicide rate, yet the rate constrains how many will die by suicide each year, and it is a property of the collective, not of any single person. (002-025)

## Methodological Significance

By treating suicide as a social fact, Durkheim:
1. Established sociology as an autonomous science with its own object of study
2. Demonstrated that even the most private acts are socially determined
3. Provided a template for sociological investigation: define the object, measure its collective expression, identify social causes
