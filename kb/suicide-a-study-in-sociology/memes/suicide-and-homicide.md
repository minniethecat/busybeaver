# Suicide and Homicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim devotes a substantial portion of Livre III to examining the relationship between suicide and homicide. He systematically refutes the theory of the Italian criminological school—Lombroso, Ferri, and Morselli—which held that suicide and homicide are two expressions of a single underlying psychological predisposition, varying inversely according to the character of the social milieu. (12991-12993)

Durkheim's counter-argument proceeds in two stages. First, he demonstrates that suicide and homicide do not share a common psychological basis: their distributions by sex, age, and season differ markedly. Second, he shows that socially, the two phenomena do not always vary inversely—in certain social contexts they rise and fall together—because both are functions of social integration rather than individual temperament. (13084-13086)

## The Italian School's Theory

The Italian criminologists—most notably Enrico Ferri and [[Enrico Morselli]]—proposed that the inverse relationship between suicide and homicide constituted a "universal law." In southern French departments, crimes against persons were frequent while suicides were rare; in northern departments, the reverse obtained. Similarly, Corsica showed 83% person-crimes but only 18 suicides per million inhabitants, while the Seine had 17% person-crimes and 427 suicides per million. (13010-13014)

According to this school, the two phenomena were "two different channels of a single current," fed by the same source—a degenerate organic constitution. The individual predisposed to violence would, in a gentle and pacific social milieu, turn that violence inward (suicide); in a harsh milieu where human life was less respected, the same impulse would be directed outward (homicide). Suicide was thus characterized as a "transformed and attenuated homicide," even a "safety valve" that spared society the greater evil of murder. (13050-13074)

## Durkheim's Refutation of Psychological Identity

Durkheim challenges the claimed psychological unity on empirical grounds:

**Sex differences**: While women are far less prone to suicide than men, their aptitude for homicide is not correspondingly lower. Women monopolize certain forms of homicide—infanticide, abortion, poisoning—and account for 38–39% of intentional homicides in France, 51% in Germany, and 52% in Austria. Once these female-specific crimes are counted, the sex difference in homicidal tendency largely disappears, undermining the claim of a common psychological root. (13093-13130)

**Age patterns**: Suicide rises steadily into old age, whereas murder and assassination peak at maturity (30–35 years) and then decline. This divergence contradicts both the identity theory and the inversion theory. (13136-13141)

**Seasonal variation**: When homicides are disaggregated from sexual assaults (which peak in June like suicides), the monthly distributions differ dramatically. Murders show maxima in February and August; assassinations in February and November; infanticides in May. None tracks the smooth January–June rise and June–December decline characteristic of suicide. (13166-13201)

**Prison evidence**: If the homicidal impulse, when blocked, transformed into suicidal impulse, imprisoned murderers should show exceptionally high suicide rates. In fact, professional criminals and murderers in French penal colonies rarely committed suicide—Brest recorded only 13 suicides in 17 years among ~3,000 inmates. (13252-13265)

## Social Conditions: Integration as the Common Factor

Durkheim argues that the relationship between suicide and homicide is not a simple inverse correlation but varies with social type:

Both egoistic suicide and homicide arise from weak social integration—a condition Durkheim calls *individuation*. In societies where collective sentiments are enfeebled and the individual is insufficiently bound to the group, the suicide rate *and* the homicide rate may both be elevated. This is why, in France after 1870, murders and suicides rose simultaneously rather than inversely. (13317-13321)

Conversely, altruistic suicide—where the individual kills himself out of devotion to the group—is the polar opposite of homicide. In societies with strong collective integration (military, traditional societies), altruistic suicide is high while egoistic homicide is low, producing the appearance of an inverse correlation that the Italian school misinterpreted as universal. (13308-13311)

Thus, the relationship between suicide and homicide is mediated by the *type* of suicide and the *type* of social integration, not by a fixed psychological law. Durkheim's analysis transforms the question from one of individual pathology to one of collective social forces.
