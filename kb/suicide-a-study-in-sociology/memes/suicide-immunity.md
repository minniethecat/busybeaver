# Suicide Immunity

## Overview

Throughout *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim systematically analyses the
**immunity** (*immunité*) that certain social groups confer against
suicide. The term is not used metaphorically but with precise analytical
intent: just as biological immunity protects an organism against
pathogens, social immunity protects the individual against the suicidal
current. The key empirical finding is that married persons, Catholics,
and citizens during periods of political crisis all exhibit markedly
lower suicide rates than their comparison groups — and the common
mechanism is **social integration**. (doc_final.md:5250, 13471)

> Il y a donc, dans les groupes sociaux, une sorte d'immunité contre le
> suicide. (doc_final.md:13471)

## The Marital Immunity and Its Explanation

The most extensively analysed case of suicide immunity is that of
**married persons** relative to the unmarried. Bertillon's original
calculations showed that married individuals of both sexes enjoy
substantial protection against suicide — a finding that Durkheim
subjects to careful causal analysis. (doc_final.md:15444-15468)

### Two Competing Hypotheses

Durkheim identifies two possible explanations for the marital immunity:

| Hypothesis | Mechanism | Key Advocate |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Domestic milieu** | The family, through its collective life, neutralises the suicidal impulse | Durkheim's preferred explanation |
| **Matrimonial selection** | Marriage selects the healthiest individuals; the unmarried contain "human waste" (*déchet humain*) | Letourneau, partially Bertillon |

(doc_final.md:6240-6272)

Durkheim rejects the selection hypothesis on multiple grounds. First,
the poor do not marry less than the wealthy — they typically have more
children. Second, the neurasthenic temperament that predisposes to
suicide is not socially disqualifying for marriage; in refined
societies, nervousness is almost a mark of distinction. Third and most
decisively, only the most severely insane are excluded from marriage —
too small a group to explain the magnitude of the immunity observed.
(doc_final.md:6273-6294)

### The Family, Not the Conjugal Bond

Durkheim's most important analytical refinement is to distinguish the
protective effect of the **family** (the domestic group including
children) from that of the **conjugal society** (the married couple
alone). Through a series of comparisons — married persons without
children, widowed persons with and without children — he demonstrates
that the conjugal bond alone accounts for only a small fraction of the
immunity:

> La société conjugale n'est donc que pour une faible part dans
> l'immunité dont jouissent les gens mariés. (doc_final.md:6473)

The overwhelming source of protection is the **family** — specifically,
the presence of children, which increases the density and duration of
collective life. Widowers without children lose almost all immunity;
widowers with children retain a substantial portion. (doc_final.md:6510-6516)

> Ainsi l'immunité que présentent les gens mariés en général est due,
> tout entière ou presque tout entière, à l'action de la famille.
> (doc_final.md:6574-6575)

## Religious Immunity

The immunity conferred by religious groups follows a parallel logic.
Catholics enjoy markedly greater protection against suicide than
Protestants — and Durkheim attributes this not to doctrine (both
religions prohibit suicide with equal severity) but to the **degree of
integration** of the religious community:

| Religious Group | Relative Suicide Rate | Degree of Integration |
|----------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Jews | Lowest | Highest (minority solidarity + strong collective life) |
| Catholics | Intermediate | High (hierarchical authority, invariant tradition) |
| Protestants | Highest | Lowest (free inquiry, individual judgment) |

The Jewish case provides the most striking illustration: their
"exceptional immunity" cannot be explained by doctrinal prohibition
alone, since they share the same Biblical prohibition as Christians.
Rather, it is the intense collective life of a minority group
subject to external hostility that produces the protective effect.
(doc_final.md:5240-5250, 15418-15425)

The Catholic immunity, likewise, is not due to minority status — it
persists even in regions where Catholics constitute 92–96% of the
population, such as the Upper Palatinate and Upper Bavaria.
(doc_final.md:5260-5268)

## Political Immunity: The War Effect

A third form of immunity is observed during periods of **political
crisis**, most dramatically during wars. Durkheim documents that suicide
rates drop sharply during wars across all belligerent nations — an
effect he attributes to the intensification of collective sentiments
and the integration of individuals into a shared national purpose:

> Les grandes crises politiques... produisent les mêmes effets [que la
> guerre]. (doc_final.md:7572)

The political society, like the family and the religious community,
confers immunity when it is strongly integrated and actively engaged
with its members. The protective mechanism is the same across all three
domains: the individual, absorbed into a collective life larger than the
self, is shielded from the egoistic current that produces suicide.

## The Generality of the Mechanism

Durkheim's concept of immunity unifies a diverse set of empirical
findings under a single theoretical principle. Whether the protective
group is the family (with children as the critical integrating factor),
the Church (with its hierarchical authority and invariant tradition),
or the nation (during moments of collective mobilisation), the mechanism
is the same: **social integration neutralises the suicidal current**.

The analytical power of the immunity concept lies in its sociological
character. Immunity is not an attribute of individuals but of *groups*.
It varies not with the personal qualities of members but with the
structural properties of the collective — its density, its permanence,
its capacity to absorb the individual into a shared life.
