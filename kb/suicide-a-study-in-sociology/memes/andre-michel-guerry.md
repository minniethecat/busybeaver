# André-Michel Guerry

## Overview

**André-Michel Guerry** (1802–1866) was a French lawyer turned statistician who, alongside [[Adolphe Quetelet]], pioneered the field of *statistique morale* — the quantitative study of moral and social phenomena. Guerry's principal works, *Essai sur la statistique morale de la France* (1833) and *Statistique morale comparée de la France et de l'Angleterre* (1864), provided the first systematic geographic and temporal mapping of suicide, crime, and other social behaviors across French départements.

Guerry's discovery that suicide rates remained remarkably stable over time — while crime rates varied — was a foundational finding for the positivist study of suicide. Durkheim cited Guerry's data extensively in *Le Suicide* (1897), drawing on his tables for diurnal and weekly suicide distributions, geographic concentration patterns, and the inverse north-south relationship between crimes against persons and suicide.

## Geographic and Temporal Patterns of Suicide

Guerry's most influential contribution was demonstrating that suicide was spatially structured. His maps of French départements revealed that suicide rates increased steadily as one moved from the periphery toward Paris — a finding Durkheim invoked to argue that suicide was governed by social density and civilizational concentration rather than individual pathology. Guerry observed that "if one starts from any point on the periphery of the country (Marseille excepted) moving toward the capital, one sees suicides multiply more and more the closer one approaches."

As early as 1833, Guerry identified a striking inverse geographic relationship: crimes against persons were twice as numerous in the South of France as in the North, while suicide displayed the opposite pattern. This divergence between violence directed outward (homicide) and violence directed inward (suicide) became a cornerstone of Durkheim's argument that suicide was not a variant of criminality but a distinct social phenomenon governed by different collective forces.

Guerry also pioneered the study of temporal rhythms in suicide. Analyzing 6,587 cases by day of the week, he established that suicide peaked on certain days and declined on others — patterns Durkheim reproduced in *Le Suicide* to demonstrate the influence of social rhythm on individual acts. Together with Brierre de Boismont's Paris data, Guerry's national sample provided the empirical basis for Durkheim's analysis of diurnal suicide variation.

## Legacy

Guerry's *statistique morale* established the empirical foundation upon which Durkheim's sociological theory of suicide was built. While Guerry himself interpreted statistical regularities primarily as descriptive patterns, Durkheim transformed them into evidence of *suicidogenic currents* — collective forces irreducible to individual psychology. Guerry's work, together with that of [[Adolphe Quetelet]] and [[Adolph Wagner]], constituted the quantitative tradition that made *Le Suicide* possible as a work of empirical sociology.
