# Religion and Suicide

## Overview

One of Émile Durkheim's most famous and influential findings in *Le Suicide* (1897) is the systematic variation of suicide rates by religious confession. Across nineteenth-century Europe, Protestant regions consistently recorded higher suicide rates than Catholic regions, with Jewish communities showing the lowest rates of all. Durkheim argues that this variation is not attributable to differences in doctrine, theology, or the degree of condemnation of suicide, but rather to differences in the degree of **social integration** that each religious community provides. (book.md:4987-5005)

This finding is the empirical foundation of Durkheim's concept of [[egoistic-suicide]]: suicide that results from insufficient integration of the individual into the collective life of a social group. Religion serves as a protective factor not because of what it teaches about suicide, but because of how tightly it binds the individual to a moral community.

## The European Pattern

Durkheim presents comparative data showing the stark religious gradient in suicide rates:

| Religious Character of State | Suicides per Million Inhabitants |
|-----------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Protestant states | 190 |
| Mixed states (Protestant and Catholic) | 96 |
| Catholic states | 96 |
| Greek Catholic states | 40 |

> Observons d'abord la manière dont les différentes confessions religieuses agissent sur le suicide. Si l'on jette un coup d'œil sur la carte des suicides en Europe, on reconnaît à première vue que dans les pays purement catholiques, comme l'Espagne, le Portugal, l'Italie, le suicide est très peu développé, tandis qu'il est à son maximum dans les pays protestants, en Prusse, en Saxe, en Danemark. (book.md:4997-5005)

The pattern is consistent: Protestant territories show roughly double the suicide rate of Catholic territories. The Greek Catholic (Eastern Orthodox) states show even lower rates, though Durkheim cautions that this may reflect lower levels of general civilization rather than specifically religious effects. (book.md:5024-5028)

## Within-Country Confirmation: Switzerland

To eliminate the confounding effect of national culture, Durkheim turns to Switzerland, where Catholic and Protestant populations coexist within the same political boundaries:

| Canton Type | Population | Suicides | Suicides per Million |
|-------------|-----------|----------|---------------------|
| Catholic cantons | 970,000 | 457 | ~47 |
| Protestant cantons | 1,793,000 | 2,597 | ~145 |
| Mixed cantons (both confessions) | 760,000 | 832 | ~110 |

The Protestant suicide rate is approximately three times the Catholic rate within the same country. (book.md:5042-5075)

> Ce ne sont pas seulement les moyennes générales qui traduisent cette relation; tous les cantons, sans exception, la vérifient. (book.md:5074-5075)

Crucially, Durkheim demonstrates that this relationship holds regardless of the national-linguistic character of the canton:

| Cantons | Confession | Suicide Rate |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| German Catholic | Catholic | Low |
| German Protestant | Protestant | High |
| French Catholic | Catholic | Low |
| French Protestant | Protestant | High |

> L'action du culte est donc si puissante qu'elle domine toutes les autres. (book.md:5149)

The religious effect overrides linguistic and national-cultural differences entirely.

## The Jewish Exception

Jewish communities present the most striking case. Despite facing social marginalization and persecution, Jews consistently record the lowest suicide rates of any religious group:

| Confession | Relative Suicide Rate |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Protestants | Highest (baseline) |
| Catholics | ~50% lower |
| Jews | Lowest (often below Catholic rate) |

Durkheim argues that this is precisely what his integration theory predicts. Jews, as a minority group facing external hostility, develop an exceptionally tight internal solidarity. The very forces that should, on a psychological reading, increase suicide (persecution, marginalization) are counteracted by the compensatory integration of the minority community. (book.md:1527-1535)

> Nous montrerons même plus loin que c'est la religion où il y a le moins de suicides, quoique la folie y soit le plus répandue. (book.md:1531-1532)

This finding is methodologically crucial: it shows that mental illness rates and suicide rates can move in opposite directions, definitively severing the link between psychopathology and suicide that Esquirol and other alienists had asserted.

## The Mechanism: Integration, Not Doctrine

Durkheim argues that the Protestant-Catholic differential is not a product of theological differences regarding suicide. Both confessions condemn suicide with equal severity. The difference lies in the structure of religious life:

| Dimension | Protestantism | Catholicism |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| Free inquiry (*libre examen*) | Encouraged — individual conscience is the final authority | Restricted — interpretation is mediated by Church hierarchy |
| Common beliefs | Fewer shared dogmas; more doctrinal diversity | Unified doctrine enforced by ecclesiastical authority |
| Ritual practice | Minimal; individual devotion emphasized | Rich; collective liturgy and sacraments |
| Clergy role | Pastor as teacher; no sacerdotal mediation | Priest as intermediary; sacramental authority |
| Community density | Loose, voluntary association | Dense, obligatory membership with shared practice |

Durkheim's argument is that Protestantism, by emancipating individual conscience from collective tradition, produces a higher degree of [[individuation]]. The Protestant is more alone before God—and, by extension, more alone before life. The Catholic, by contrast, is embedded in a dense web of shared beliefs, collective rituals, and hierarchical authority that integrates the individual into a moral community. (book.md:4427-4448)

> Le protestantisme est une religion moins fortement intégrée que le catholicisme. [...] C'est que le protestantisme est une Église dont le degré d'intégration est moindre; tout le prouve. (book.md:4427-4448)

## The Generalization: Religion as Social Integration

The religion finding is not ultimately about religion per se. Durkheim's theoretical move is to generalize from the religious case to a broader principle:

> Si la religion protège l'homme contre le désir de se détruire, ce n'est pas parce qu'elle lui prêche, avec des arguments *sui generis*, le respect de sa personne; c'est parce qu'elle est une société. Ce qui constitue cette société, c'est l'existence d'un certain nombre de croyances et de pratiques communes à tous les fidèles, traditionnelles et, par suite, obligatoires. (book.md:1527-1535)

What matters is not the *content* of religion but its *form* — specifically, the degree to which it constitutes a **society**: a group of individuals united by common beliefs and practices that are obligatory and traditional. Any social group with these characteristics—a family, a political community, a professional group—should exercise an analogous protective function. This is the theoretical bridge from the religion chapter to Durkheim's analyses of [[family-and-suicide|family integration]], [[celibacy-and-suicide|marriage]], and [[professional-groups-and-suicide|professional groups]].

## Egoistic Suicide: The Concept

The religion chapter introduces Durkheim's first and most developed suicide type: **egoistic suicide**. This form of suicide results from excessive individuation—the weakening of the bonds that attach the individual to the collective:

> Le suicide égoïste vient de ce que les hommes ne tiennent plus à la vie. Il vient de ce que la société, dans ce qu'elle a d'essentiel, a perdu de sa cohésion. [...] En un mot, le suicide égoïste résulte de ce que la société n'est pas suffisamment intégrée pour retenir tous ses membres sous sa dépendance. (book.md:4165)

The religious gradient is the paradigmatic case of egoistic suicide:

| Group | Integration Level | Suicide Rate |
|-------|------------------|-------------|
| Jews | Highest (minority solidarity) | Lowest |
| Catholics | High (collective ritual, hierarchy) | Moderate |
| Protestants | Lowest (free inquiry, individualism) | Highest |

## Alternative Explanations Rejected

Durkheim systematically rejects competing explanations for the religious gradient:

| Alternative | Durkheim's Refutation |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Protestantism condemns suicide less | Both confessions condemn suicide equally; doctrinal difference is minimal |
| Catholics underreport suicide | Suicide is easier to hide in Protestant regions where ecclesiastical surveillance is weaker; data bias would go the wrong direction |
| Education differences produce the gradient | Education is an effect of the same individuation process, not an independent cause |
| Minority status protects (Catholics in Protestant regions) | The pattern holds regardless of majority/minority status |
