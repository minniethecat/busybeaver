# Mental Alienation and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim opens his causal analysis (Livre I, Chapitre I) by examining the most prominent extra-social explanation of suicide: that it is a manifestation of mental illness (*aliénation mentale*). This thesis, championed by leading French psychiatrists including Esquirol and Falret, held that "l'homme n'attente à ses jours que lorsqu'il est dans le délire et les suicidés sont aliénés." (791-830)

Durkheim refutes this claim through a two-pronged argument: first, he demonstrates that suicide cannot be a distinct mental disease (a *monomanie-suicide*); second, he shows that even if suicide occurs frequently among the mentally ill, this does not prove it is *always* pathological. His ultimate weapon is statistical: suicide rates vary systematically by social structure in ways that rates of mental illness do not.

## Refutation of the Monomania-Suicide Thesis

The doctrine that suicide is a distinct mental illness—a *monomanie-suicide*—rested on the broader psychiatric concept of monomania: a partial delusion localized to a single idea or impulse, leaving the rest of the mind intact. Durkheim deploys the emerging consensus of modern psychiatry against this concept:

Monomanias do not exist. Clinical experience has never identified a morbid mental tendency in true isolation. Whenever one faculty appears affected, others are always involved; earlier alienists simply failed to observe the concomitant disturbances. As Falret himself acknowledged, a patient seemingly possessed only by religious ideas also exhibits pride, or humility, or fearful tendencies on closer examination. (928-970)

The theoretical foundation of monomania—the "faculty psychology" that treats the mind as composed of separate, independently acting forces—had been abandoned by contemporary science. Mental functions are not discrete organs that can fall ill one by one; they are "solidaires," intimately interpenetrating. A lesion in one inevitably reverberates through all. The brain's regions are not sufficiently specialized for isolated pathology. (982-998)

If monomanias do not exist, there can be no *monomanie-suicide*. Suicide is not a distinct mental disease.

## Suicide Within Mental Illness: The Statistical Refutation

But Durkheim must also address the weaker claim: that suicide, while not a distinct disease, occurs *only* in a state of mental illness—as a syndrome appearing within various forms of *vésanie*. Here he deploys statistical reasoning:

If suicide were exclusively a product of mental illness, the geographic and demographic distribution of suicide should parallel that of insanity. It does not:

**Sex**: Women are universally recognized as more prone to mental illness than men. In French asylums, women outnumber men (55 to 45 per 100 admissions). Yet women commit suicide at roughly *one-quarter* the rate of men. If suicide were a function of mental alienation, this ratio would be impossible. (1004-1030)

**Religion**: Jews exhibit the highest rates of mental illness among European religious groups—a finding Durkheim accepts from the psychiatric literature. Yet Jews have the *lowest* suicide rates. Conversely, Protestants have lower insanity rates but the highest suicide rates. These divergences are incompatible with the psychiatric thesis.

**Age**: Mental illness increases steadily with age, peaking in maturity and old age. Suicide also rises with age, but not in the same proportion or pattern. Moreover, the age curve of suicide varies between societies in ways the age curve of insanity does not.

**Nationality**: Countries with similar levels of mental illness have widely divergent suicide rates. Denmark and Norway have comparable insanity rates, but Denmark's suicide rate is far higher.

Durkheim concludes that suicide and mental illness are independent variables. The fact that some suicides occur among the mentally ill no more proves that all suicide is pathological than the fact that some fevers occur among the insane proves that all fever is a form of madness.

## Methodological Significance

The refutation of the mental-alienation thesis serves a foundational methodological purpose in *Le Suicide*. By eliminating the most prominent individual-level explanation, Durkheim clears the ground for his sociological analysis. The chapter models the argumentative structure he repeats throughout Livre I: define the competing (psychological) explanation rigorously, test it against comparative statistics, and demonstrate its inadequacy before proposing a social alternative.

This chapter also introduces Durkheim's key epistemological principle: a social fact—the suicide rate—must be explained by other social facts, not by individual pathology. The stability and systematic variation of suicide rates across societies points to collective, not individual, causation.
