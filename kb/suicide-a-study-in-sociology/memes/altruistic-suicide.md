# Altruistic Suicide

## Overview

**Altruistic suicide** (*suicide altruiste*) is Durkheim's second type, representing the opposite pole of social integration from egoistic suicide. Where egoistic suicide occurs because the individual is too detached from society, altruistic suicide occurs because the individual is **too strongly integrated** — the self is absorbed into the collective to the point where individual life has no value independent of the group. (004-025)

Durkheim identified three sub-types: obligatory, optional, and acute (mystical) altruistic suicide.

## Forms

| Sub-type | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Obligatory** | Society requires self-sacrifice | Widow suicide in traditional India; elderly suicide in some societies |
| **Optional** | Society honors but does not require it | Warrior death before dishonor; religious asceticism |
| **Acute (Mystical)** | Individual seeks to escape individuality itself | Religious martyrs seeking union with the divine |

## Mechanism

In altruistic suicide, the individual's personality is not sufficiently differentiated from the group. The collective identity overwhelms personal identity. Death is not seen as a tragic individual loss but as the fulfillment of a social role. (004-030)

This type is more common in **traditional and pre-modern societies** where collective identity is strong. In modern societies, Durkheim noted its persistence primarily in the military, where soldiers' suicide rates exceeded civilian rates due to the military's intense collective discipline.
