# Suicide Rate

## Overview

The **suicide rate** (*taux de suicide* or *taux social des suicides*) is the key empirical measure in Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897). For each society, the annual suicide count per million (or 100,000) inhabitants is remarkably stable over time, demonstrating that suicide is not merely an aggregate of individual acts but a **social fact** with its own causal logic. (002-025)

## Constancy

Durkheim documented in *Tableau I* that France, Prussia, England, Saxony, Bavaria, and Denmark maintained near-constant suicide counts through the 1840s–1860s. Variability appeared only during social crises — wars, political upheavals, economic disruptions — confirming that the rate responds to collective, not individual, forces. (002-027)

## Analytical Role

The suicide rate allowed Durkheim to:
1. Compare societies quantitatively
2. Correlate rate variations with social structural variables (religion, family structure, economic conditions)
3. Establish causal relationships between social integration/regulation and suicide propensity
