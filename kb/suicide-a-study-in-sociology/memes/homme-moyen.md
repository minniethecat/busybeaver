# Average Man (Homme Moyen)

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim engages with the concept of the **average man**
(*homme moyen*), a statistical construct developed by the Belgian statistician
and astronomer [[adolphe-quetelet]]. Quételet proposed that for any human
population, the distribution of physical and moral characteristics clusters
around a central "average type" that represents the ideal form of the group.
Durkheim's engagement with this concept is critical to his larger methodological
argument that **social facts are irreducible to individual averages** and possess
their own distinctive reality. (doc_final.md:265-280)

## Quételet's Average Man

Quételet's theory held that just as physical measurements (height, weight)
distribute around a mean in any population, so too do moral and social
characteristics. The "average man" was not merely a statistical abstraction but,
in Quételet's view, the type toward which nature tended — a kind of ideal
norm embedded in the population itself. This concept was enormously influential
in mid-nineteenth-century social science and informed early attempts to
establish statistical regularities in human behaviour.

## Durkheim's Critique

Durkheim's use of statistical averages is fundamentally different from
Quételet's. For Durkheim, the stability of the social suicide rate — its
constancy across years and its variation across societies — demonstrates that
there exists a **collective inclination** that is not merely the average of
individual inclinations but a *sui generis* social reality:

> Ce qui importe, ce n'est pas d'exprimer avec un peu de précision la notion
> que la moyenne des intelligences s'est faite du suicide, mais c'est de
> constituer une catégorie d'objets qui... soit fondée objectivement,
> c'est-à-dire corresponde à une nature déterminée de choses.
> (doc_final.md:269-273)

The average is not the cause; it is an effect of deeper social forces. Durkheim
moves beyond Quételet's descriptive averaging to a genuinely explanatory
sociology in which collective states — what he calls *courants suicidogènes* —
possess causal power independent of the individuals through whom they are
expressed.

## The Social Suicide Rate as a Distinct Reality

The empirical demonstration of this point lies in the behaviour of suicide
statistics themselves. The suicide rate of a given society is **more stable
from year to year than its mortality rate** and varies systematically with
social structure — religion, family integration, political crisis — in ways
that cannot be reduced to the summation of individual psychopathologies.
The "average" suicide rate is, in Durkheim's hands, transformed from a
descriptive statistic into an indicator of a collective reality:

| Approach | Quételet | Durkheim |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Average man | Descriptive ideal type | — |
| Social suicide rate | — | Causal collective force |
| Method | Statistical averaging | Comparative analysis of rates |
| Ontology | Individual-level | Social-level (*sui generis*) |

## Methodological Significance

The critique of the average man is a specific instance of Durkheim's general
methodological rule, first articulated in *Les Règles de la méthode
sociologique* (1895): **social facts must be treated as things**. The suicide
rate is not an average of individual acts but a property of the social group —
a collective phenomenon with its own causes, its own regularities, and its own
explanatory logic. The average man, by reducing the social to the statistical,
obscures precisely what is most sociologically significant. (doc_final.md:269-273)
