# Paul Moreau de Tours

## Overview

**Paul Moreau de Tours** (1844–1908) was a French psychiatrist and alienist who wrote on the psychiatric theory of suicide and the phenomenon of suicidal contagion. His 1871 work *De la contagion du suicide* (*On the Contagion of Suicide*) examined cases where suicide appeared to spread through imitation or social proximity, a topic of intense debate in late-nineteenth-century French psychiatry. Moreau de Tours was one of several alienists, alongside [[Jean-Étienne Esquirol]], [[Jean-Pierre Falret]], and Jousset, who maintained that suicide was fundamentally a manifestation of mental alienation. (0823–0824)

Durkheim engaged with Moreau de Tours at two strategic points in *Le Suicide* (1897): first, as a representative of the psychiatric orthodoxy that equated suicide with insanity; and second, as an authority on the limits of imitation — a position Durkheim turned against the imitation theory itself.

## The Psychiatric Theory of Suicide

In Livre I, Chapitre I of *Le Suicide*, Durkheim situated Moreau de Tours within the dominant psychiatric tradition. Alongside Esquirol and Falret, Moreau de Tours held that suicide "offers all the characters of mental alienation" and that "man only attempts his life when he is in a state of delirium." (0823–0824) Durkheim noted, however, that Moreau de Tours himself introduced a qualification that rendered the thesis suspect. In the very passage where he endorsed the psychiatric doctrine, Moreau de Tours asked: "Should suicide be regarded in all cases as the result of mental alienation? Without wishing to resolve this difficult question here, let us say as a general thesis that one is instinctively inclined toward the affirmative the more deeply one has studied insanity, the more experience one has acquired, and the more patients one has seen." (0824–0830)

For Durkheim, this hesitation was telling: if the psychiatric theory of suicide were truly self-evident, it would not require the circular reasoning that equated clinical experience with confirming evidence. Moreau de Tours' own ambivalence exposed the gap between clinical impression and empirical demonstration.

Moreau de Tours also contributed, with Jousset, to the four-part classification of suicides among the insane: manic suicide (driven by hallucinations or delusions), melancholic suicide, obsessive suicide, and impulsive suicide. (1037) This nosological schema reflected the alienist ambition to bring all suicidal acts within the diagnostic framework of psychiatry. Durkheim also cited a clinical case reported by Moreau de Tours of a family where multiple brothers died by suicide, illustrating the psychiatric argument for hereditary transmission of suicidal tendencies. (2656–2658)

## The Contagion Debate

In Livre I, Chapitre IV's discussion of imitation, Durkheim cited Moreau de Tours as a witness against the contagion hypothesis. Moreau de Tours had argued, based on personal clinical observation, that "contagious suicide is never encountered except in individuals who are strongly predisposed." (4499–4501) That is, imitation did not create suicidal tendencies in previously healthy individuals; it merely activated a latent organic vulnerability.

Durkheim deployed this finding for his own purposes: if even an alienist who believed in organic predisposition acknowledged that contagion presupposed such predisposition, then the alleged epidemic spread of suicide through imitation could not explain population-level suicide rates. Suicide clusters in barracks and prisons — which Moreau de Tours attributed to the chance accumulation of constitutionally vulnerable individuals — were better explained, Durkheim argued, by the action of a shared social environment. (4502–4512) The prison and the regiment did not gather the predisposed; they generated the conditions for suicide.
