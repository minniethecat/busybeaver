# Race and Suicide

## Overview

In Livre I, Chapter II of *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim undertakes a systematic refutation of the thesis that racial constitution determines suicide rates. Several contemporary suicidologists had argued that different races possess distinct and stable propensities to suicide. Durkheim demonstrates through comparative European data that the concept of race is too vague to serve as a scientific variable, and that what appear to be racial differences dissolve under closer examination into effects of civilization, social structure, and national context. (book.md:1972-1990)

## The Problem of Defining Race

Durkheim opens with a fundamental methodological question:

> Et d'abord, qu'est-ce qu'une race? Il est d'autant plus nécessaire d'en donner une définition que non seulement le vulgaire, mais les anthropologistes eux-mêmes emploient ce mot dans des acceptions très différentes. (book.md:1993-1995)

He distinguishes four competing conceptions of race:

| Conception | Definition | Example | Scientific Utility |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Anthropological (Quatrefages) | Hereditary type fixed through generations | Aryan, Semitic | None — pure original races no longer exist |
| Historical/cultural | Peoples with shared civilization and history | Latin, Anglo-Saxon, Germanic | Limited — blends with nationality |
| Linguistic | Language-group aggregates | Indo-European groups | Weak — language ≠ biology |
| Craniometric | Skull-shape-based grouping | Dolichocephalic, brachycephalic | Arbitrary — continuous variation |

Durkheim concludes that none of these conceptions provides a reliable basis for empirical analysis. The notion of race, he argues, has become so elastic that it "no longer corresponds to anything definite" from a scientific standpoint. (book.md:2100-2110)

## The Germanic Race Hypothesis

The most influential racial theory of Durkheim's time held that the Germanic peoples possessed an elevated suicide propensity. Wagner and other race-theorists pointed to the high suicide rates of German-speaking regions. Durkheim challenges this by examining areas where the Germanic "race" should be most pronounced:

| Region | Germanic character | Suicide rate |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Bavaria | Purest Germanic population | Very low (similar to Mediterranean levels) |
| Prussia | Mixed Germanic-Slavic | High |
| German Austria | Germanic | Moderate |
| German Switzerland | Germanic | Very high |
| Denmark | Purest Germanic (per Wagner) | Moderate |

> Il serait bien difficile d'attribuer le taux des suicides danois à la race, puisque, dans les deux pays où cette race est le plus pure, elle produit des effets contraires. (book.md:2157-2159)

If the purest Germanic populations exhibit opposite suicide rates, race cannot be the determining factor. The variation is better explained by religious composition, degree of [[social-integration]], and the state of civilization in each country. (book.md:2160-2170)

## The Latin-Germanic Comparison in Switzerland

Durkheim presents Switzerland as a natural experiment, where Germanic and Latin populations co-exist under the same political institutions:

> En Suisse, nous trouvons ces deux races en présence. Quinze cantons sont allemands; ils avaient, en 1876, une population de 1,793,502 habitants et 2,597 suicides. Cinq cantons sont français. (book.md:2271-2275)

When comparing the suicide rates across the two linguistic-ethnic groups, Durkheim finds the differences are not attributable to race but to the higher degree of civilization and urban development in the German cantons. When French cantons reach similar levels of development, their suicide rates converge toward German levels. (book.md:2297-2305)

## The Celtic and Cymric Case

Durkheim examines the case of the two main racial groups of the British Isles—the Celts and the Cymri (Kymris)—who show different suicide rates:

> D'un côté, il n'y a pas d'écart sensible entre les deux races; de l'autre, la légère différence observée peut s'expliquer sans faire intervenir la race. (book.md:2297-2299)

The Celto-Cymric comparison becomes another case study in why racial explanations fail: geographic distribution, economic development, and religious tradition account for the variation more parsimoniously than racial typology.

## Morselli's Racial Statistics: A Critical Examination

Durkheim engages extensively with the work of [[enrico-morselli|Enrico Morselli]], the Italian psychiatrist and statistician who had compiled the most comprehensive cross-national suicide data of the era. Morselli himself, despite compiling data organized by "race," found no consistent evidence of racial determination:

> D'après Morselli lui-même, on ne trouve pas la moindre trace de l'influence allemande. La race n'est donc pour rien dans la production du phénomène. (book.md:2240-2242)

Durkheim cites Morselli's own conclusion that when "circumstances are otherwise, the effect does not hold"—a finding that undermines the race thesis entirely. If race were a genuine cause, its effects should be invariant across different social contexts. The fact that they are not demonstrates that social, not biological, mechanisms are at work. (book.md:2256-2268)

## The Slavs and the "Inferior Races" Argument

The race theory was often deployed in defense of European superiority: "inferior races" supposedly had weaker suicidal tendencies because they lacked the refinement of civilization. Durkheim turns this argument on its head:

| Group | Suicide Rate | Durkheim's Interpretation |
|-------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Slavs | Lower than Germans | Less "civilized" = less individuation |
| Latins | Intermediate | Intermediate social development |
| Germans | Highest | Most advanced individuation |

The Slavic case illustrates Durkheim's counter-argument: lower suicide rates among Slavs reflect not racial inferiority but a lower degree of [[individuation]], which is a social, not a biological, condition. As Slavic societies modernize and individuation increases, their suicide rates rise—confirming the social rather than racial character of the phenomenon.

## Methodological Significance

The race chapter exemplifies Durkheim's broader sociological method:

1. **Conceptual clarification** — Before testing a hypothesis, define the terms precisely. The word "race" is shown to be scientifically unusable without rigorous definition.

2. **Comparative control** — Hold constant what the theory claims is constant (race) and vary the social context. If suicide rates vary while race is constant, race cannot be the cause.

3. **Parsimony** — The same variation explained by race can be explained more parsimoniously by known social factors (religion, education, urbanization, family structure).

4. **The social as sui generis** — The chapter reinforces the central Durkheimian principle that [[social-fact|social facts]] must be explained by other social facts, not by biological or psychological reduction.

## Relation to Other Refutations

The race chapter forms the middle link in Durkheim's chain of refutations of extra-social causes of suicide:

| Chapter | Target | Key Argument |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| I | [[mental-alienation-and-suicide|Psychopathological factors]] | No consistent correlation between mental illness rates and suicide rates |
| **II** | **Race and heredity** | **Racial categories are scientifically unusable; comparative data refute racial determinism** |
| III | [[temperature-and-suicide|Cosmic factors]] | Seasonal variation reflects social rhythm, not climate |
| IV | [[imitation]] | Imitation affects distribution, not overall rate |

Together, these refutations clear the ground for Durkheim's positivist sociology of suicide, which locates causation in [[social-integration]] and [[social-regulation]].
