# Age and Suicide

## Overview

One of the most consistent empirical regularities documented in
*Le Suicide* (1897) is the continuous increase of suicide rates with
age. From childhood through the most advanced old age, the propensity
to suicide rises in every society Durkheim examines — France, Prussia,
Saxony, Italy, and Denmark. This cross-national uniformity serves a
critical argumentative function: it is deployed as evidence against both
the hereditarian theory of suicide and the psychopathological
explanation. (doc_final.md:1519-1529)

> Dans tous les pays, la tendance au suicide croît régulièrement depuis
> l'enfance jusqu'à la vieillesse la plus avancée. Si, parfois, elle
> régresse après 70 ou 80 ans, le recul est très léger; elle reste
> toujours à cette période de la vie deux et trois fois plus forte qu'à
> l'époque de la maturité.
> (doc_final.md:1520-1524)

## The Empirical Pattern

Durkheim presents a comprehensive comparative table (Tableau IX)
showing suicide rates by age group across five European countries:

| Age Group | France (1835–44) M | Prussia (1873–75) M | Saxony (1847–58) M | Italy (1872–76) M | Denmark (1845–56) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|--------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Under 16 | 2.2 | 10.5 | 9.6 | 3.2 | 113 |
| 16–20 | 56.5 | 122.0 | 210 | 32.3 | 272 |
| 20–30 | 130.5 | 231.1 | 396 | 77.0 | 307 |
| 30–40 | 155.6 | 235.1 | 551 | 72.3 | 426 |
| 40–50 | 204.7 | 347.0 | — | 102.3 | 576 |
| 50–60 | 217.9 | — | 906 | 140.0 | 702 |
| 60–70 | 317.3 | 529.0 | — | 147.8 | — |
| 70–80 | 317.3 | — | 917 | 124.3 | 783 |
| 80+ | 345.1 | — | — | 103.8 | 642 |

(doc_final.md:2778-2807)

With minor nuances, the pattern is the same in every country: suicide
increases continuously with age, reaching its maximum in the final or
penultimate period of life. The slight decline sometimes observed after
80 years is not universal and, where it occurs, is very small — the rate
among octogenarians remains well above that of younger adults.
(doc_final.md:2809-2819)

Sweden is the only exception, with a maximum falling between 40 and 50
years, though Durkheim acknowledges this anomalous case without
elaboration. (doc_final.md:2810-2811)

## Argumentative Function: Refuting Heredity

The age gradient serves a precise polemical purpose in Durkheim's
architecture. If suicide were caused by hereditary predisposition, one
would expect it to manifest at roughly the same age across generations —
just as hereditary illnesses (insanity, phtisis) typically appear at
predictable life stages. Instead, suicide increases continuously with
age, never stabilising at any period:

> Comment, dès lors, attribuer à l'hérédité une tendance qui, chez
> l'enfant, est à peu près nulle, qui progresse ensuite d'âge en âge,
> régulièrement et d'un mouvement continu, et qui n'arrive à son
> apogée que chez le vieillard?
> (doc_final.md:2819-2824)

The continuous increase is incompatible with the hereditarian model:
hereditary traits typically appear at a fixed age and then may recede;
suicide only ever accumulates. (doc_final.md:2828-2830)

## Argumentative Function: Refuting Psychopathology

The age pattern also contradicts the psychopathological explanation of
suicide, which would attribute it to mental illness. Durkheim points out
that the two phenomena follow opposite trajectories. Mental illness
(insanity) reaches its maximum frequency around age 30–35 and then
declines, being at its lowest in old age. Suicide, conversely, is
rarest in youth and most frequent in extreme old age:

> C'est vers la trentaine que le danger [de la folie] est le plus grand;
> au delà il diminue, et c'est pendant la vieillesse qu'il est, et de
> beaucoup, le plus faible. Un tel antagonisme serait inexplicable si
> les causes qui font varier le suicide et celles qui déterminent les
> troubles mentaux n'étaient pas de nature différente.
> (doc_final.md:1525-1529)

This *antagonism* between the age curves of suicide and insanity is, for
Durkheim, decisive: if both were products of the same underlying
psychopathology, they could not exhibit opposite age distributions.

## The Social Interpretation

What does the age gradient positively demonstrate? The progressive
accumulation of suicide risk with age is, for Durkheim, the cumulative
effect of **social causes** operating on the individual over the life
course. The longer one lives in society, the more one is exposed to the
social currents (*courants sociaux*) that determine suicide rates.

Even the suicide of children, though rare, is cited as evidence for the
social interpretation: children's suicides vary by social environment
and are most frequent in large cities, where social life begins earliest
and most intensely for the child. (doc_final.md:2760-2770)

> Ils ne sont nulle part aussi nombreux que dans les grandes villes.
> C'est que, nulle part aussi, la vie sociale ne commence aussitôt pour
> l'enfant, comme le prouve la précocité qui distingue le petit citadin.
> (doc_final.md:2763-2767)
