# Penchant (Social Propensity)

## Overview

The term *penchant* (propensity, tendency, inclination) appears
throughout *Le Suicide* (1897) — not as a casual metaphor but as a
precisely defined methodological instrument. When Durkheim writes of
the *penchant au suicide* (propensity to suicide), he deliberately
reconstructs what common sense takes to be an individual psychological
disposition as a **collective property** of social groups. This
semantic shift — from individual psychology to collective sociology —
is one of the most consequential theoretical moves in the book.
(doc_final.md:740, 1226, 1453)

> …le penchant collectif au suicide… (doc_final.md:1226)

> …un penchant défini, qui est même constant pour chaque milieu social.
> (doc_final.md:1453)

## From Individual Inclination to Social Fact

### The Common-Sense View and Its Refutation

Nineteenth-century psychiatry and moral statistics typically
conceptualised suicide as the expression of an **individual
inclination** — a *penchant* rooted in personal psychology,
temperament, or pathology. Durkheim does not deny that individuals
have inclinations, but he argues that these individual variations
cannot explain the **constancy** of suicide rates across time:

> …le penchant au suicide revient avec plus de force et les
> individus… (doc_final.md:1082)

The problem is simple: if suicide were merely the sum of independent
individual decisions, the rate should fluctuate randomly from year to
year. The fact that it does not — that each society has a
characteristic, stable suicide rate — implies the existence of a
**collective force** that imposes itself upon individuals. This
collective force is what Durkheim calls the *penchant collectif*.
(doc_final.md:740, 868, 1453)

### The Penchant as a Social Fact

Durkheim's *penchant collectif* satisfies the criteria of a **social
fact** (*fait social*) as defined in *Les Règles de la méthode
sociologique* (1895):

1. **Exteriority**: the *penchant* exists outside any particular
   individual; an individual is born into a society that already has a
   characteristic suicide rate
2. **Constraint**: the *penchant* imposes itself on individuals; it is
   not chosen but *suffered*
3. **Collective diffusion**: the *penchant* is a property of the group,
   not reducible to the sum of individual dispositions

(doc_final.md:763, 1453)

> …les sociétés n'auraient pas, en matière de suicide, le même penchant
> plus ou moins intense. (doc_final.md:763)

> …un penchant, d'intensité variable selon les pays, et qui entraînât…
> (doc_final.md:800)

## Penchant and the Critique of Psychopathology

The concept of *penchant collectif* is central to Durkheim's critique
of psychiatric explanations of suicide in Livre I. The psychiatrists
— Esquirol, Falret, and others — had argued that suicide is always
the manifestation of mental illness, a *penchant* rooted in individual
pathology. Durkheim's response is not to deny that some suicides are
committed by the mentally ill, but to show that the **magnitude and
constancy** of the collective phenomenon cannot be explained by
individual pathology:

> …l'aliénation mentale ne peuvent rendre compte du penchant collectif
> au suicide. (doc_final.md:1226)

Even granting the highest estimates of mental illness among suicide
victims, the statistical regularity of suicide rates — their stability
across years, their systematic variation across social groups — far
exceeds what individual pathology can explain. The *penchant collectif*
is a phenomenon at a different level of reality. (doc_final.md:1140, 1226)

## Penchant and Race

Durkheim deploys the same argument against racial theories of suicide.
If suicide propensity were inherited — a *penchant* fixed in the
biological constitution of racial groups — then it should be stable
across social conditions. But the data show precisely the opposite:

> La théorie qui fait de la race un facteur important du penchant au
> suicide… (doc_final.md:2456)

The suicide rates of German-speaking populations vary dramatically
depending on which *social milieu* they inhabit, not on which racial
stock they descend from. The *penchant* varies with social structure,
not with biology. (doc_final.md:1502, 2146, 2150)

## Penchant and Heredity

The heredity argument — that the suicidal *penchant* is transmitted
genetically — receives particularly careful treatment. Durkheim
examines cases of suicide running in families and concludes that what
appears to be hereditary transmission is actually **social contagion**
within the family milieu:

> …plutôt combien grande est l'influence contagieuse qu'exercent sur
> les individus les cas de suicide. (doc_final.md:2622)

The family is a social environment, and the apparent heritability of
suicide reflects the social influence of family members upon one
another, not biological inheritance. (doc_final.md:2539, 2560, 2570, 2591-2630)

## Penchant, Tendance, and Courant: A Conceptual Triad

Durkheim uses three interrelated terms to describe the social
determination of suicide, and understanding their relationship is
essential:

| Term | Translation | Analytical Level |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| *Penchant collectif* | Collective propensity | The **stable disposition** of a social group toward suicide; a structural property |
| *Tendance au suicide* | Suicidal tendency | The **directional force** that produces the observed rate; the "pull" toward suicide |
| *Courant suicidogène* | Suicidogenic current | The **dynamic movement** of suicide through a society; the realisation of tendency in events |

(doc_final.md:740, 868, 1226, 1453)

The *penchant* is the most abstract of the three — it designates the
**underlying disposition** of a social milieu, prior to its
manifestation in any particular suicide. The *tendance* is the
**operative force** derived from the *penchant*, measurable through
statistical rates. The *courant* is the **concrete flow** of suicidal
acts through time and space.

## Methodological Significance

The concept of *penchant collectif* performs indispensable
**methodological work** in Durkheim's argument:

1. It **shifts the analytical level** from the individual to the
   collective, making sociological explanation possible
2. It **operationalises** the suicide rate — what the statistician
   measures as a rate, the sociologist explains as a *penchant*
3. It **unifies diverse phenomena** — different types of suicide
   (egoistic, altruistic, anomic) are all expressions of different
   collective *penchants*, rooted in different states of social
   integration and regulation

Without the concept of *penchant collectif*, Durkheim would have no
way to move from the statistical observation that suicide rates are
stable to the theoretical claim that they are *caused* by social
forces. The *penchant* is the bridge between measurement and
explanation. (doc_final.md:740, 868, 1226, 1453)
