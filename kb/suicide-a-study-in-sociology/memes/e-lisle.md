# E. Lisle

## Overview

**E. Lisle** (often cited as *Docteur Lisle*) was a French physician who produced one of the most comprehensive nineteenth-century statistical surveys of suicide. His major work, *Du suicide, statistique, médecine, histoire et législation* (1856), synthesized medical, statistical, historical, and legal perspectives on self-destruction, making it a key reference for subsequent researchers including [[Enrico Morselli]] and Émile Durkheim.

Lisle's significance to Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897) lies primarily in his empirical data. Durkheim cited Lisle at multiple points — on prison suicide rates, age-specific suicide statistics, suicide in ancient civilizations, and moral-philosophical reflections — reflecting the encyclopedic scope of Lisle's treatise. His data frequently served as the factual backbone against which Durkheim tested competing theories.

## Prison Suicide and Institutional Discipline

Lisle's most famous empirical finding concerned suicide within the French prison system. From data covering 1816 to 1837, he reported that among 9,320 deaths in the *bagnes* (penal colonies), only 6 were suicides. This extraordinarily low rate — in a population composed almost entirely of adult males, the demographic group with the highest suicide risk in the general population — presented a striking anomaly that Durkheim exploited in his argument against psychological and biological theories of suicide.

If suicide were a direct expression of individual misery or organic predisposition, the brutal conditions of the French penal system should have produced elevated rates. Instead, the near-absence of suicide in the *bagnes* suggested that intense collective discipline, even when harsh, exercised a protective function. Durkheim interpreted this finding as evidence that social integration — however coercive — counteracted the tendency to suicide, a key element in his theory of egoistic suicide.

Lisle also contributed data on age-specific suicide patterns, compiled from French national statistics. Durkheim drew on Lisle's age tables to establish that suicide rates increased progressively with age — a demographic regularity that, like the prison finding, pointed toward social rather than purely individual determinants of suicide.

## Legacy

Like [[Jean-Baptiste Cazauvieilh]] and [[André-Michel Guerry]], Lisle belonged to the generation of empirical researchers whose painstaking data collection made Durkheim's sociological synthesis possible. Without endorsing Durkheim's sociological conclusions, Lisle's comprehensive statistical compendium provided the factual substrate that *Le Suicide* transformed into a theoretical argument. His prison suicide finding remains one of the most cited pieces of evidence in the Durkheimian tradition for the social determination of suicide rates.
