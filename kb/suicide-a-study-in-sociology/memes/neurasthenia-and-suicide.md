# Neurasthenia and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim examines **neurasthenia** (*neurasthénie*) as
a potential causal factor in the determination of suicide rates, occupying the
middle ground between the insanity hypothesis (which he has already refuted)
and the fully social explanation he will develop. Neurasthenia, in the medical
discourse of the late nineteenth century, designated a broad spectrum of nervous
disorders — a "rudimentary form of insanity" — that was believed to be
increasingly widespread in modern societies. Durkheim's analysis confirms that
while neurasthenia may predispose *individuals* to suicide, it cannot explain
the **social suicide rate**. (doc_final.md:1228-1243)

## The Conceptual Space Between Insanity and Health

Durkheim situates neurasthenia in the conceptual space between two poles:
full-blown insanity (*aliénation mentale*), which he has already shown cannot
account for the collective inclination to suicide, and the perfect equilibrium
of intelligence. Between these extremes lies a whole series of intermediate
states — the anomalies gathered under the common name of neurasthenia.
(doc_final.md:1228-1233)

> La neurasthénie est une sorte de folie rudimentaire; elle doit donc avoir,
> en partie, les mêmes effets. Or elle est un état beaucoup plus répandu que
> la vésanie; elle va même de plus en plus en se généralisant.
> (doc_final.md:1237-1240)

Because neurasthenia is far more widespread than full insanity — and appears
to be becoming ever more so — it presents a more plausible candidate for
explaining variations in the suicide rate across societies and over time.

## The Mechanism of Predisposition

Durkheim acknowledges a plausible physiological mechanism linking neurasthenia
to suicidal behaviour. Neurasthenics are, by their temperament, predestined to
suffering: pain results from an excessive disturbance of the nervous system,
and the threshold beyond which stimulation becomes painful is lower in those
whose nerves are less resistant. For the neuropath, every impression is a
source of discomfort, every movement a fatigue; the nerves, as if on the
surface of the skin, are irritated at the slightest contact. (doc_final.md:1244-1255)

This lowered threshold for suffering makes the neurasthenic more vulnerable to
the kinds of despair that can lead to suicide. Yet this individual-level
mechanism, Durkheim argues, cannot explain why **societies** have different
suicide rates or why those rates change in regular patterns.

## The Indeterminacy of Neurasthenia

Durkheim's central objection is that neurasthenia is too **indeterminate** in
its effects to account for the regularity of social suicide rates. The same
nervous condition can produce widely different outcomes depending on the social
context — it may lead to suicide in one setting, to artistic creativity in
another, to religious mysticism in a third. Today, Durkheim notes, neurasthenia
passes rather for a mark of **civilisation and intellectual distinction** than
for a pathology that inevitably leads to self-destruction. (doc_final.md:6288-6289)

> Il n'y a pas de rapport immédiat et régulier entre la neurasthénie et le
> taux social des suicides. (doc_final.md:11428-11430)

There is no immediate and regular relationship between neurasthenia and the
social suicide rate. The nervous condition may be a *facilitating* factor at
the individual level, but the **direction** it takes — toward suicide or away
from it — is determined by social forces.

## From Individual Predisposition to Social Causation

This analysis is a crucial step in Durkheim's larger argumentative strategy. By
systematically eliminating each individual-level explanation — insanity, race,
heredity, climate, imitation, alcoholism, and neurasthenia — he clears the
ground for the genuinely sociological explanation that occupies Livre II. The
failure of neurasthenia to explain suicide rates is not a negative result; it
is the positive demonstration that **social facts require social explanations**.
(doc_final.md:2476)
