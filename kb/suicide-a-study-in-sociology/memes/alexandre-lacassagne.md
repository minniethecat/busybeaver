# Alexandre Lacassagne

## Overview

**Alexandre Lacassagne** (1843–1924) was a French criminologist and forensic physician, best known as the founder of the Lyon School of criminology — the French sociological rival to the Italian positivist school of [[Cesare Lombroso]]. Lacassagne emphasized social environment over biological determinism in the etiology of crime, a position that aligned him with Durkheim's broader sociological project. However, his specific thesis on the relationship between suicide and crime — that suicide and property crime varied inversely — became a target of Durkheim's most sustained statistical critique in *Le Suicide* (1897).

## The Suicide-Crime Antagonism Thesis

In Livre II, Chapitre II of *Le Suicide*, Durkheim examined the relationship between suicide and other immoral acts, particularly crimes and delicts. He introduced Lacassagne's thesis as follows: "According to M. Lacassagne, there would be a regularly inverse relationship between the movement of suicides and that of crimes against property (qualified thefts, arson, fraudulent bankruptcies, etc.)." (12948–12951) This thesis had been advanced on Lacassagne's behalf by his student, Dr. Chaussinand, in a work titled *Contribution à l'étude de la statistique criminelle*.

Durkheim's refutation was meticulous. He demonstrated that the apparent inverse relationship between suicide and property crime from 1854 onward was an artifact of judicial accounting: around that date, French courts began the practice of *correctionnalisation* — reclassifying certain crimes as delicts to transfer them from the jurisdiction of assize courts to correctional tribunals. A significant number of property offenses thus disappeared from the crime column only to reappear in the delict column. (12958–12965) The apparent decline in property crime was, Durkheim argued, "exclusively due to an artifice of bookkeeping."

Furthermore, Durkheim showed that even if the post-1854 decline were real, the period from 1826 to 1854 revealed the two curves moving in the same direction, not in opposition. From 1831 to 1835, an average of 5,095 defendants were charged annually with property crimes; this number rose to 5,732 in the following period, and stood at 4,918 in 1841–45 — a decline of only 2 percent from 1830 levels. (12970–12973) The configuration of the two curves, Durkheim concluded, "excludes any idea of comparison": property crime was erratic and accident-driven, while suicide rose regularly and uniformly. "Between two phenomena whose development is so little comparable, there cannot exist any link whatsoever." (12978–12979)

## Lacassagne and the Homicide-Suicide Debate

Durkheim noted that Lacassagne "appears, moreover, to have remained isolated in his opinion." (12988) However, a different thesis — that suicide stood in inverse relation to crimes against persons, especially homicide — enjoyed far wider support, counting [[Enrico Ferri]] and [[Enrico Morselli]] among its defenders. This homicide-suicide antagonism, rather than Lacassagne's property-crime thesis, became the focus of Durkheim's extended analysis in the remainder of the chapter. Lacassagne later claimed paternity of the homicide-suicide theory at the 1887 Congress of Criminology in Rome. (17410)

## Legacy

Lacassagne's broader criminological legacy — emphasizing social causes over biological determinants — proved far more durable than his specific thesis on suicide and property crime. As founder of the Lyon School, Lacassagne trained a generation of French criminologists in sociological methods. Durkheim's engagement with him, though critical, reflected the intellectual proximity between the Lyon School of criminology and the emerging discipline of sociology: both sought to explain social facts by other social facts, rejecting the reduction of complex social phenomena to individual biology or psychology.
