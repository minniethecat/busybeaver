# Anomic Suicide

## Overview

Anomic suicide is one of the four types of suicide identified by [[Emile Durkheim]] in *Le Suicide* (1897). It results from a **rupture of normative regulation** — a state of *[[Anomie]]* in which the individual's desires, no longer constrained by collective moral authority, outrun any possibility of satisfaction. While [[Egoistic Suicide|egoistic suicide]] stems from insufficient [[Social Integration|social integration]] (the individual is detached from collective life), anomic suicide stems from insufficient [[Social Regulation|social regulation]] (collective norms fail to set limits on individual passions).

Durkheim defines the type in explicit distinction from egoistic and altruistic suicide:

> Nous donnerons à cette dernière espèce le nom de *suicide anomique*. [...] Dans le suicide anomique, c'est aux passions proprement individuelles qu'elle manque, les laissant ainsi sans frein qui les règle. (009, approx.)

The distinction is fundamental to Durkheim's typological framework: egoistic suicide arises when society is insufficiently present in collective activity, leaving it without object and meaning; anomic suicide arises when society fails to regulate individual passions, leaving them without restraint.

| Suicide Type | Axis | Social Deficit | Key Symptom |
|-------------|------|---------------|-------------|
| [[Egoistic Suicide]] | Integration (low) | Excessive individuation | Melancholy, apathy |
| [[Altruistic Suicide]] | Integration (high) | Insufficient individuation | Calm sense of duty |
| **Anomic Suicide** | **Regulation (low)** | **Normative deregulation** | **Irritation, exasperation** |
| [[Fatalistic Suicide]] | Regulation (high) | Excessive regulation | Oppression, hopelessness |

## Mechanism

Anomie — literally "absence of law" (from Greek *a-* + *nomos*) — is a state in which the normative framework that ordinarily limits human desires breaks down. Durkheim argued that human needs, unlike animal needs, are not self-regulating: beyond the physical minimum, desires are inherently unlimited and require an external moral authority to set boundaries (009, approx.).

When this regulatory force weakens — whether through economic crisis, rapid prosperity, or institutional change — **individual aspirations outrun any possibility of satisfaction**. The resulting gap between desire and fulfillment produces a state of chronic frustration and disorientation:

> L'anomie est donc, dans nos sociétés modernes, un facteur régulier et spécifique de suicides; elle est une des sources auxquelles s'alimente le contingent annuel. (009, approx.)

Economic crises (both busts and booms) produce sharp increases in suicide precisely because they disturb the normative equilibrium that keeps aspirations in check. In a bust, individuals are thrown below their accustomed condition; in a boom, the rapid increase in wealth removes the ceiling on desire before new norms can crystallize. Both directions of change produce the same anomic effect.

## Varieties

### Economic Anomie

The most extensively documented form. Durkheim analyzed suicide statistics across European nations and found that industrial and commercial regions consistently showed higher suicide rates than agricultural ones. The Vienna financial crisis of 1873–1874 produced a 51% increase in suicides, concentrated in the four months when the crisis was most acute (009, approx.).

| Context | Suicide Rate | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Agricultural regions | Lower | Traditional regulatory powers (guilds, custom) still operative |
| Industrial/commercial regions | Higher | Fever of business, deregulation of economic life |
| Rentier population | Highest (720/million) | Those with "nothing but void above them" are most vulnerable |

### Domestic and Conjugal Anomie

Economic anomie is not the only variety. Durkheim identified **domestic anomie** produced by changes in family structure — particularly widowhood and divorce. The disruption of marital regulation, especially for men, removes a key normative constraint on desire:

> L'anomie domestique [...] résulte du veuvage ou du divorce. (009, approx.)

The statistical evidence showed that divorced men had significantly elevated suicide rates compared to married men, while the effect was less pronounced for women — a finding Durkheim interpreted as reflecting the different regulatory function of marriage for each sex in late 19th-century European society.

## Psychological Form

Durkheim distinguished the psychological profile of anomic suicide from that of egoistic suicide. While the egoistic suicide is marked by melancholy, apathy, and withdrawal from the world, the anomic suicide is characterized by **irritation and exasperation** — a violent reaction against the frustration of unbounded desire:

> Qu'elle soit progressive ou régressive, l'anomie, en affranchissant les besoins de la mesure qui convient, ouvre la porte aux illusions et, par suite, aux déceptions. (009, approx.)

When the individual attributes their ruin to external causes, the suicide may be preceded by homicide; when they blame themselves, the violence is turned inward. In both cases, the emotional register is one of active passion rather than passive despair.

## Relationship to Other Types

Anomic suicide and egoistic suicide share a family resemblance — both involve society being insufficiently present to individuals — but operate in different spheres:

- **Egoistic suicide**: society fails in the sphere of *collective activity* → detachment, meaninglessness
- **Anomic suicide**: society fails in the sphere of *individual regulation* → unbound passion, frustration

They can combine: Durkheim described the **ego-anomic suicide** as a mixed type found in modern societies where excessive individuation and normative deregulation reinforce each other. In fact, Durkheim argued that egoism and anomie are the two principal pathological currents in modern society, often acting together.
