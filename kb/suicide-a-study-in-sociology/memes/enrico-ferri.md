# Enrico Ferri

## Overview

**Enrico Ferri** (1856–1929) was an Italian criminologist, sociologist, and politician who, together with Cesare Lombroso, shaped the Italian school of positivist criminology. His 1884 work *L'Omicidio-suicidio* (Homicide-Suicide) advanced a comprehensive theory linking suicide and homicide as expressions of the same underlying psychological constitution — a thesis that Durkheim engaged with extensively and refuted across multiple chapters of *Le Suicide*.

Durkheim used Ferri's data on suicide prosecutions and his age-based analyses while systematically dismantling Ferri's central claim that suicide and homicide share a common causal mechanism rooted in individual pathology. Ferri thus served as one of Durkheim's most important theoretical adversaries.

## L'Omicidio-suicidio (1884) and Durkheim's Critique

| Element | Ferri's Position | Durkheim's Refutation |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Suicide-homicide relationship | Both spring from the same individual psychopathic constitution | Geographic and demographic patterns show divergence, not convergence |
| Temperature and suicide | Heat directly stimulates the nervous system, provoking suicide | The seasonal distribution of suicides is identical across European regions with vastly different temperatures — the real factor is day-length and social intensity |
| Age and suicide | Both suicide and homicide increase with age | Suicide increases regularly with age; homicide peaks in young adulthood and declines — opposite trajectories |
| Mental pathology | Suicide and homicide are two expressions of the same degenerative condition | Statistical correlations between mental illness and suicide disappear when more complete data are used |

Durkheim demonstrated that Ferri's own data, when properly analyzed, contradicted the individual-pathology model. Ferri and Morselli, following Lombroso, had attempted to define a single criminal-suicidal temperament characterized by organic degeneration — a construct that Durkheim showed could not explain the stable, society-specific suicide rates documented across Europe.

## Statistical and Policy Contributions

Ferri compiled extensive data on legal prosecutions for suicide across European jurisdictions, documenting that even where suicide had been decriminalized, residual penal procedures persisted. According to Ferri, in 1889 there were still 106 prosecutions for attempted suicide and 84 convictions in England alone.

His comparative framework, while methodologically flawed in Durkheim's assessment, provided valuable cross-national data that Durkheim re-purposed for his own sociological argument. Ferri's observation that the suicide rate accelerated with age — which Durkheim confirmed — became one element of Durkheim's broader demonstration that suicide varies systematically with social integration and regulation rather than with individual predisposition.
