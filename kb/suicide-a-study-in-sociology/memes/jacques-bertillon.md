# Jacques Bertillon

## Overview

**Jacques Bertillon** (1851–1922) was a French statistician and demographer who served as chief of the Municipal Statistical Service of Paris. Alongside his father Louis-Adolphe Bertillon, Jacques Bertillon produced some of the most influential statistical studies of marriage, divorce, and mortality in late 19th-century France — work that Durkheim drew upon extensively in *Le Suicide*, particularly in his analysis of egoistic suicide and the protective effect of marriage.

Bertillon was the first to publish a rigorous statistical demonstration of the correlation between divorce rates and suicide rates across European nations, a finding that became central to Durkheim's theory of anomic suicide. His methodological innovations in controlling for age effects when comparing married and unmarried populations were directly adopted and refined by Durkheim.

## Key Statistical Contributions

### The Marriage Protection Coefficient

Bertillon was the first to calculate the "coefficient of preservation" (coefficient de préservation) that married persons enjoyed relative to unmarried persons with respect to suicide. He determined an initial figure of 1.6, which Durkheim later showed was a minimum rather than an average — the true protective effect of marriage varied systematically by age and sex.

### Divorce and Suicide

In a landmark paper published in the *Annales de démographie internationale* (September 1882), Bertillon established the proposition that across all of Europe, the number of suicides varied in parallel with the number of divorces and legal separations. He proposed that both phenomena depended on a common factor: the greater or lesser frequency of "mal équilibrés" (unbalanced individuals) in a population — an explanation that Durkheim challenged by demonstrating that the correlation persisted even when controlling for individual characteristics, pointing instead to the weakening of marital regulation as the causal mechanism.

### Method of Age-Adjusted Comparison

Bertillon pioneered the statistical method of separating the effects of age from those of marital status — a technique that Durkheim acknowledged as essential and applied throughout his analysis. By comparing suicide rates within specific age bands rather than across whole populations, Bertillon made it possible to isolate the independent effect of marriage on suicide propensity.
