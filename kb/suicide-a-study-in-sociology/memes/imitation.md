# Imitation

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim devotes an entire chapter (Livre I, Chapitre IV) to examining the role of imitation in suicide. He identifies three distinct phenomena commonly conflated under the term "imitation" and argues that only one—automatic reproduction without intellectual mediation—properly deserves the name. His purpose is methodological: if imitation were the primary driver of suicide rates, suicide would depend on individual psychological causes rather than social ones. (3784-3794)

Durkheim acknowledges the reality of suicide contagion—cases where exposure to one suicide triggers others—but demonstrates that such contagion affects only the geographic *distribution* of suicides, not their overall *rate*. The social suicide rate, he concludes, is determined by collective forces, not by the imitative propagation of individual acts. (4110-4120)

## Durkheim's Three Categories of "Imitation"

Durkheim observes that sociologists habitually use the word "imitation" without defining it, allowing it to slide between three fundamentally different phenomena:

**1. Collective fusion (synthèse collective)**. When individuals in a crowd are affected by the same circumstance, their shared emotional states combine and intensify into a new collective sentiment. Durkheim insists this is not imitation but *creation*: "Au lieu d'imitation, c'est bien plutôt création qu'il faudrait dire, puisque de cette composition de forces résulte quelque chose de nouveau." The product is a collective state, not a copy. (3837-3860)

**2. Conformity to social norms**. When we follow customs, fashions, or moral rules, we are not imitating—we are obeying social authority. The act is reproduced not because we witnessed it, but because it bears the "estampille sociale" (social stamp) and carries obligation. As Durkheim puts it: "Agir par respect ou par crainte de l'opinion, ce n'est pas agir par imitation." (3929-3972)

**3. Automatic reproduction (contagion)**. This alone—the mechanical copying of an act solely because one witnessed it, without any intervening judgment—is what Durkheim reserves for "imitation." He defines it rigorously: *"Il y a imitation quand un acte a pour antécédent immédiat la représentation d'un acte semblable, antérieurement accompli par autrui, sans que, entre cette représentation et l'exécution, ne s'intercale aucune opération intellectuelle."* (4042-4052)

## Evidence of Suicide Contagion

Durkheim does not deny the reality of contagious suicide. He catalogs numerous cases: the famous sentry-box at Boulogne where several soldiers hanged themselves in succession; the village of Saint-Pierre-Monjau where a woman's suicide by hanging triggered copycat deaths; the priest near Étampes whose suicide was followed by two clerical and several lay imitations; and Lord Castlereagh's companions who followed him into Vesuvius. (4111-4130)

He also discusses "obsidional suicides" (mass suicides under siege), such as the forty Jews in Jerusalem who resolved to die collectively rather than surrender to the Romans. But Durkheim classifies these differently: they represent a *consensus social*, a collective resolution elaborated by the group as a whole, not a chain of individual contagions. (4132-4160)

## Imitation Does Not Determine the Suicide Rate

Durkheim's central argument is that while imitation affects the geographic *distribution* of suicides—concentrating them in particular locations or moments—it does not explain the overall social suicide *rate*. If imitation were causal, suicide rates would show epidemic patterns, with each outbreak propagating endlessly. Instead, societies display relatively stable suicide rates that vary systematically with social structure, not with exposure to individual cases.

The chapter thus serves a critical function in Durkheim's larger argument: by eliminating imitation as a primary cause, he clears the ground for the sociological analysis of suicide that occupies Livres II and III. Suicide rates are not driven by psychological contagion but by **social integration** and **social regulation**—the twin axes of his typology.
