# Monomania

## Overview

In the opening chapter of *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim devotes sustained critical attention to the doctrine of **monomania** (*monomanie*) — the 19th-century psychiatric theory that certain forms of insanity could be localized to a single delusional idea, leaving the rest of the mind intact. Within this framework, suicide was classified as a species of monomania: the patient was said to be afflicted by an irresistible impulse toward self-destruction, operating in isolation from any broader mental disturbance. Durkheim's refutation of monomania is the pivot on which his entire sociological argument turns, for if suicide were a form of monomania, it would by definition be an individual pathology — a matter for psychiatry, not sociology. (doc_final.md:874-910)

Durkheim's critique operates on two levels. First, he marshals the empirical evidence from contemporary psychiatry showing that monomania as a clinical entity does not exist. Second, he demonstrates that even if monomania did exist, it could not explain the statistical regularities of [[suicide-rate|suicide rates]] — the stable, society-level patterns that demand a social explanation. The demolition of the monomania doctrine is thus the negative foundation on which Durkheim's positive sociological theory is built.

## The Doctrine of Monomania

The concept of monomania emerged from the work of early 19th-century French alienists, particularly **Jean-Étienne Esquirol**, who classified mental disorders into general insanities (affecting the whole mind) and partial insanities (affecting only a single faculty or idea). In this nosology:

> On appelle monomanies ces délires restreints. Le sujet, sous tous les autres rapports, pense et agit comme les autres hommes; seulement, il est affecté d'un délire partiel. (doc_final.md:874-876)

If suicide were understood as a form of madness, it could only be a monomania — a localized delusion focused on self-destruction — since the person who kills himself typically appears rational in all other respects. The alternative, that suicide were a manifestation of general mania, was contradicted by the observed composure and methodical behavior of many suicidal individuals. (doc_final.md:880-884)

The monomania doctrine had enormous institutional and intellectual consequences. By classifying suicide as a species of insanity, it placed the phenomenon squarely within the jurisdiction of psychiatry. The social, moral, and statistical dimensions of suicide were rendered secondary — mere epiphenomena of an underlying pathology that was fundamentally individual and medical. This was the intellectual edifice that Durkheim set out to dismantle.

## Durkheim's Refutation: The Non-Existence of Monomania

Durkheim's first line of attack is clinical. He reports that by the 1880s, the very existence of monomanias had come under sustained challenge within psychiatry itself:

> Pendant longtemps, leur existence n'a pas été contestée... Mais il est établi qu'on n'en peut pas distinguer d'espèces définies, parce qu'il n'existe pas de maladies mentales où une idée délirante existe à l'état isolé. (doc_final.md:907-932)

The decisive empirical finding was that when a delusional idea appears to exist in isolation, closer examination invariably reveals broader disturbances. The delusion is never truly local; it is always accompanied by other symptoms — disturbances of affect, cognition, or behavior — that indicate a more general pathology:

> L'idée de monomanies est en contradiction avec les données actuelles de la science. Il n'y a pas, il ne peut pas y avoir de monomanies proprement dites. Les troubles partiels que l'on désignait ainsi ne sont jamais que des accidents de troubles beaucoup plus généraux. (doc_final.md:968-998)

The logical conclusion follows directly: if there are no monomanias, there can be no monomania-suicide. And if suicide is not a monomania, it cannot be considered a form of insanity in the strict sense:

> Si donc il n'y a pas de monomanies, il ne saurait y avoir une monomanie-suicide et, par conséquent, le suicide n'est pas une folie distincte. (doc_final.md:998-1000)

Durkheim is careful to note that this does not mean suicidal individuals are never mentally ill. Many are. The point is that suicide itself — the act of self-destruction — is not a *species* of mental illness. It is a behavior that can occur in the context of many different mental conditions, or in none at all. The psychiatric classification confuses a symptom with a disease.

## From Clinical to Statistical Refutation

Having shown that monomania lacks clinical validity, Durkheim escalates to a more fundamental argument. Even if monomania existed, it could not explain the **social patterning** of suicide — the stable, differentiated rates that characterize different societies, religious groups, family structures, and historical periods.

The argument is statistical: if suicide were caused by monomania — an individual pathology distributed randomly or according to biological predisposition — then:

1. Suicide rates should be relatively uniform across different social groups within the same society.
2. Changes in suicide rates over time should be slow and linked to changes in the prevalence of mental illness.
3. The demographic patterns of suicide (by age, sex, religion, marital status) should follow the demographic patterns of insanity.

None of these predictions holds. Suicide rates vary dramatically and systematically by religion (Protestants much higher than Catholics), by family structure (celibates higher than married persons), by economic conditions (rising both in prosperity and in crisis), and by season and time of day. These patterns are stable over decades, resistant to short-term events, and specific to particular societies — the hallmarks of social facts, not individual pathologies. (doc_final.md:15265-15269)

The statistical argument is the bridge from Durkheim's negative critique (suicide is not monomania) to his positive theory (suicide is a social phenomenon caused by collective currents). By eliminating the psychiatric alternative, Durkheim creates the logical space for a sociological explanation.

## The Broader Significance: Durkheim's Method of Argument

The monomania critique exemplifies a characteristic Durkheimian method: **argument by elimination of alternatives**. Before presenting his own theory, Durkheim systematically examines and refutes every competing explanation. Book I of *Le Suicide* dispatches extra-social factors one by one:

- **Psychopathic states** (Chapter I): monomania, general insanity, neurasthenia, alcoholism — none can account for the social patterning of suicide rates.
- **Race and heredity** (Chapter II): anthropological theories of racial predisposition fail the same statistical tests.
- **Cosmic factors** (Chapter III): climate, temperature, season — Durkheim shows these operate only insofar as they affect the rhythm of social life.
- **Imitation** (Chapter IV): [[gabriel-tarde|Gabriel Tarde]]'s theory that suicide spreads by imitation is refuted through the geographical distribution of suicide rates.

Each refutation follows the same structure: the competing theory makes predictions about the statistical distribution of suicide, and these predictions are falsified by the data. The monomania doctrine is the first and most important target because it represents the dominant medical-psychological framework that Durkheim's sociology must displace.

The ultimate target of this method is not any particular theory but a whole mode of explanation: the reduction of social phenomena to individual psychology. By demonstrating that even something as apparently individual as self-destruction cannot be explained at the individual level, Durkheim establishes the autonomy of sociology as a science.

## Historical Context

The monomania doctrine was not merely a scientific error; it was embedded in the institutional and legal structures of 19th-century France. The diagnosis of monomania had been used in criminal trials to argue for diminished responsibility — if a defendant committed a crime under the influence of a localized delusion, he could not be held fully accountable. The doctrine thus had practical consequences for the administration of justice and the treatment of the mentally ill. (doc_final.md:15790-15791)

Durkheim's refutation was part of a broader transformation in French psychiatry in the late 19th century, as the Esquirolian nosology gave way to more dynamic and holistic conceptions of mental disorder. The concept of monomania, once the cornerstone of forensic psychiatry, was progressively abandoned — a development that Durkheim both documented and accelerated.
