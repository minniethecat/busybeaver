# Geiger

## Overview

**Geiger** was a German classical scholar of the late nineteenth century whose work *Der Selbstmord im klassischen Altertum* (*Self-murder in Classical Antiquity*, published in Augsburg) served as Durkheim's principal reference on suicide in ancient Greece and Rome. Throughout the historical sections of *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim repeatedly cited Geiger for specific legal provisions, burial customs, and the evolving moral status of suicide in classical civilization.

Although Geiger himself is not named in Durkheim's main text — his citations appear exclusively in the footnotes — his compilation of primary sources provided the empirical foundation for Durkheim's historical argument that suicide was not universally condemned but varied in its moral and legal treatment across societies. Geiger's work enabled Durkheim to demonstrate that the modern European horror of suicide was a historically specific social fact, not a natural or eternal moral intuition.

## Classical Suicide Law and Practice

Durkheim's discussion of ancient suicide legislation in Livre II, Chapitre III drew extensively on Geiger's scholarship. Three specific footnotes illustrate the range of Geiger's contribution.

First, Geiger documented the Roman practice of denying burial to those who hanged themselves — a sanction preserved in the pontifical books and enforced by religious confraternities such as the one at Lanuvium. (17385) This burial prohibition was not framed as a punishment for moral wrongdoing but as a religious purification measure: the corpse of a suicide was ritually polluted (*sacer*), and its exposure threatened the community's relationship with the gods.

Second, Geiger traced the gradual relaxation of sanctions against suicide during the late Republic and early Empire — a period when Stoic philosophy, which regarded rational self-destruction as a legitimate exercise of personal liberty, gained cultural ascendancy. (17400) By the time of the early Empire, suicide had become, in Durkheim's formulation drawing on Geiger, "an act essentially religious" — a voluntary sacrifice to the gods or a demonstration of philosophical conviction rather than a crime.

Third, Geiger's evidence showed that even during periods of relative tolerance, Roman law continued to penalize suicide when it was committed to escape criminal prosecution, since in that case the suicide defrauded the public treasury of confiscation penalties. (17405) This selective penalization revealed that the legal treatment of suicide was driven not by a consistent moral principle but by practical interests — a finding that supported Durkheim's broader thesis that suicide's moral status was socially constructed.

## Durkheim's Use of Geiger

Durkheim's reliance on Geiger illustrates the division of labor between classical philology and sociology at the end of the nineteenth century. Geiger collected and organized the primary evidence; Durkheim drew the comparative and theoretical conclusions. By demonstrating that suicide was treated differently in different legal and religious regimes — from the burial prohibitions of archaic Rome to the permissive attitudes of the Stoic-influenced Empire — Geiger's documentation allowed Durkheim to argue against the universalistic claims of both psychiatric and moralistic theories of suicide.
