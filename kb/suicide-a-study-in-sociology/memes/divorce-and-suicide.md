# Divorce and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim examines the statistical relationship between divorce rates and suicide rates across European countries, finding a striking positive correlation. He argues that this correlation does not reflect individual psychopathology—as Bertillon had suggested—but rather the effect of the institution of divorce itself on the moral regulation of marriage. Divorce weakens the regulatory power of matrimonial society, producing what Durkheim calls *anomie conjugale* (conjugal anomie). (9744-9760)

The divorced suffer the highest suicide rates of any civil-status group—three to four times higher than the married—not because divorce is psychologically traumatic, but because the predisposition was already present during the marriage itself. The anomie produced by weak marital regulation persists even after legal dissolution. (9940-10050)

## Comparative Data: Divorce and Suicide Across Europe

Durkheim compiles data from European states, grouping them by divorce frequency:

| Group | Avg. divorces per 1,000 marriages | Avg. suicides per million |
|-------|----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| I. Rare divorce (Norway, Russia, England, Italy, Finland) | 2.07 | 46.5 |
| II. Moderate divorce (Bavaria, Belgium, France, Prussia, etc.) | 6.4 | 109.6 |
| III. Frequent divorce (Saxony, Denmark, Switzerland) | 37.3 | 257.0 |

The pattern is unmistakable: as divorce frequency increases, the suicide rate rises proportionally. Switzerland, with 47 divorces per 1,000 marriages, records 216 suicides per million inhabitants; Italy, with only 3 divorces, records just 31. (9744-9800)

## Refutation of Bertillon's Psychopathological Explanation

Bertillon had proposed that both divorce and suicide derive from a common cause: the prevalence of mentally unbalanced individuals. Societies with more "insupportable spouses" would have both more divorces and more suicides, with no causal link between the two institutions. Durkheim rejects this argument as "arbitrary and unproven": it would require Switzerland to have 15 times more unstable individuals than Italy, and 6–7 times more than France—a claim without empirical foundation. (9950-9970)

## The Divorced: Highest Suicide Risk of Any Group

Durkheim presents data showing that divorced persons of both sexes kill themselves at three to four times the rate of married persons, despite being younger (average age 40 vs. 46 for married persons) and significantly more than widows and widowers, despite the latter's advanced age. In Saxony (1876), divorced men produced 3,252 suicides per million—compared to 821 for married men. (9975-10010)

This is paradoxical: widowhood is an objectively more painful disruption of life—it was not desired, whereas divorce is often a deliverance. Yet the divorced kill themselves far more. Durkheim argues the cause lies not in the trauma of divorce but in the character of the marriage that preceded it.

## Conjugal Anomie: The Causal Mechanism

Durkheim's key theoretical move is to link the divorced person's elevated suicide rate to the nature of the marriage that has ended. Drawing on his earlier finding that the widowed retain, in attenuated form, the suicide tendency of their married state, he proposes the same logic for divorce:

> "L'aggravation dont souffrent les divorcés est une conséquence, non du divorce, mais du mariage auquel il a mis fin. Elle doit tenir à une certaine constitution matrimoniale dont les époux continuent à subir l'influence, alors même qu'ils sont séparés." (10033-10050)

In societies where divorce is common and easy, marriage itself is less strictly regulated. The institution loses its power to constrain passions and fix desires. This produces a state of *anomie conjugale*—a normative deregulation of marital life—that affects married persons even before any divorce occurs, and persists after separation.

Durkheim finds that the effect is asymmetrical by sex. In countries with high divorce rates, married *men* lose their immunity to suicide more than married women. The institution of marriage, when weakened by the availability of divorce, provides less regulatory benefit to husbands—for whom marital regulation is, in Durkheim's framework, a more important protective factor.
