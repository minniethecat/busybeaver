# Social Integration

## Overview

**Social integration** (*intégration sociale*) is the degree to which an individual is connected to, and regulated by, the collective life of their society. In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim demonstrated empirically that the suicide rate varies inversely with the degree of social integration of the groups to which individuals belong. (004-002)

This finding was revolutionary: suicide, the most private and individual of acts, was shown to be governed by the strength of collective bonds.

## Dimensions

Durkheim analyzed integration across three primary social domains:

| Domain | Indicator | Finding |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Religious** | Denomination | Protestants > Catholics > Jews (due to differing degrees of collective belief and practice) |
| **Domestic** | Marital/parental status | Unmarried > Married > Married with children |
| **Political** | National crises | Peacetime > Wartime (collective sentiment intensifies during war) |

Each domain operates through the same mechanism: stronger collective bonds provide individuals with purpose, meaning, and restraint that protect against the impulse to self-destruction. (004-010)

## Relationship to Suicide Types

Social integration is the primary axis for two of Durkheim's four suicide types:
- **Too little integration** → [[Egoistic Suicide]]
- **Too much integration** → [[Altruistic Suicide]]

The protective optimum lies in a balanced integration where the individual is neither isolated from nor absorbed by the collective.
