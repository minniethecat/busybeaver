# Professional Groups and Suicide

## Overview

In the concluding chapters of *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim proposes that the only institution capable of curbing the rising tide of suicide in modern societies is the professional group or corporation (*groupe professionnel*, *corporation*). Having demonstrated that suicide rates vary inversely with social integration and regulation, and having shown that traditional integrative institutions—religion, family, the State—are weakening, Durkheim argues that occupational associations must be revived to provide the moral framework that modern individuals lack. (14670-14690)

The professional group is uniquely suited to this role, Durkheim argues, because it is *omnipresent*—accompanying the worker everywhere and at all times, unlike the family (which one leaves daily) or the State (which is remote and intermittent). "Comme la vie professionnelle, c'est presque toute la vie, l'action corporative se fait sentir sur tout le détail de nos occupations." (14690-14710)

## The Problem: Anomie in Modern Economic Life

Durkheim's diagnosis of modern society is stark. Religion has lost its regulatory power. The State is too distant and clumsy—"une lourde machine qui n'est faite que pour des besognes générales et simples"—to regulate the infinite variety of professional life. Its uniform action is necessarily "compressive et niveleuse," crushing diversity rather than channeling it. (14726-14740)

Meanwhile, the medieval guilds and corporations that once regulated economic activity have been abolished, and nothing has replaced them. The result is chronic anomie: desires, liberated from all constraint, become insatiable. "Du haut en bas de l'échelle, les convoitises sont soulevées sans qu'elles sachent où se poser définitivement." The industrial and commercial world exists in a "state of crisis and anomie" that has become normal. (9600-9620)

This deregulation of economic life directly produces **anomic suicide**—the suicide of unregulated desire. But the weakening of occupational groups also contributes to **egoistic suicide**, by depriving individuals of the collective attachment that integrates them into society.

## The Corporation as Moral Milieu

Durkheim's proposed solution is the revival of the corporation—not as the medieval guild it once was, but as a modern public institution. The corporation brings together all workers in the same function; their shared interests, common culture, and identical occupations make it the richest terrain for the formation of "idées et de sentiments sociaux." (14687-14692)

He identifies three unique advantages of the professional group over other integrative institutions:

**1. Temporal omnipresence**. Unlike the State, which acts only intermittently, the professional group is in constant contact with individuals because the function it organizes is always in operation. It is "de tous les instants." (14705)

**2. Spatial omnipresence**. Unlike the family, which is left behind when one goes to work, the professional group follows workers wherever they go. "En quelque point qu'ils soient, ils le retrouvent qui les entoure, les rappelle à leurs devoirs, les soutient à l'occasion." (14705-14708)

**3. Life-wide scope**. Professional life constitutes nearly the entirety of waking existence. Corporate action thus extends to "tout le détail de nos occupations," orienting daily activity toward collective purposes. (14708-14710)

## Proposed Functions of the Modern Corporation

Durkheim envisions the revived corporations taking on concrete regulatory functions:

- Administering insurance, assistance, and pension funds—tasks that the State is too clumsy to manage effectively
- Regulating conflicts between branches of the same profession
- Setting the conditions for fair contracts, varying by type of enterprise
- Preventing the strong from exploiting the weak "au nom de l'intérêt commun"
- Developing and maintaining professional ethics specific to each occupation

As the division of labor increases, Durkheim argues, law and morality differentiate by function. Each profession requires its own "discipline spéciale," and only an organ composed of the workers themselves can apply and maintain it. (14750-14760)

## Historical Justification

Durkheim defends the corporation against the charge that it is a medieval relic. He points out that corporations existed not only in the Middle Ages but in Greco-Roman antiquity as well—their persistence across civilizations and millennia suggests they fulfill an essential social function. Their abolition during the French Revolution, he argues, was a historical error born of their temporary corruption under the *ancien régime*, not a judgment on the institution itself.

The fact that modern corporations are weak—their members treat each other as rivals rather than cooperators—does not disprove their potential. Durkheim argues that once workers share substantial common interests, "des sentiments de solidarité naîtraient qui sont encore presque inconnus." The moral temperature of professional life would rise of its own accord. (14770-14790)
