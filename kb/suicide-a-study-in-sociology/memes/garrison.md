# Garrison

## Overview

**Garrison** was a French legal historian whose 1883 monograph *Le suicide en droit romain et en droit français* (*Suicide in Roman Law and French Law*) provided Durkheim with the detailed legal-historical evidence for his discussion of the punishment of suicide in medieval and early modern Europe. Published in Toulouse, Garrison's work traced the evolution of suicide legislation from Roman antecedents through the customary law of medieval France to the nineteenth-century debates over decriminalization.

Although Garrison, like [[Geiger]], appears only in the footnotes of *Le Suicide* (1897), his compilation of legal sources enabled Durkheim to argue that the ferocious punishments visited upon suicides in medieval Europe were not expressions of timeless moral truths but historically specific social practices — practices that lost their rationale as the collective conscience evolved.

## Medieval Suicide Law

In Livre II, Chapitre III, Durkheim documented the harsh treatment of suicide under medieval French customary law. Drawing on Garrison, he described a legal regime where suicide was treated as a crime against the sovereign, punishable by confiscation of property and desecration of the corpse. "At Bordeaux, the corpse was hung by the feet; at Abbeville, it was dragged on a hurdle through the streets; at Lille, if it was a man, the corpse, dragged to the gallows, was hung; if it was a woman, it was burned." (12506–12509) Insanity was not always considered a sufficient defense: the corpse of a person who died by suicide could still be subjected to posthumous punishment even if the act had been committed during mental illness.

Garrison's documentation also covered the legal treatment of complicity in suicide. French customary law, like Roman law before it, punished those who assisted or encouraged a suicide — a provision that survived into the modern penal codes Durkheim critiqued. (12574)

## From Roman to French Law

Garrison's work spanned both Roman and French legal traditions, allowing Durkheim to trace the continuity and transformation of suicide legislation across the great historical divide of the French Revolution. The same Garrison who documented the Roman *Digest*'s provisions on suicide (confiscation of property for those who died by suicide while facing criminal charges, exemption for those whose suicide was motivated by *taedium vitae* — weariness of life) also documented the survival of these Roman principles in the customary law of medieval France. (17352, 17358)

This legal-historical continuity demonstrated, for Durkheim, that legal institutions possessed a social inertia independent of their original rationale. The medieval punishments of suicide, inherited from Roman law and intensified by the religious fervor of the Christian Middle Ages, persisted long after the social conditions that had produced them had disappeared.

## Durkheim's Use of Garrison

Durkheim's citations of Garrison serve a specific argumentative purpose: to establish that the legal treatment of suicide was a social fact, varying systematically across historical periods and legal systems. By showing that what one society punished as a crime another tolerated as a right, Garrison's evidence undermined the claim — common in Durkheim's time — that the prohibition of suicide expressed a natural or universal moral intuition. The ferocity of medieval suicide law was not a response to the intrinsic gravity of the act but a symptom of the intensity of the medieval collective conscience.
