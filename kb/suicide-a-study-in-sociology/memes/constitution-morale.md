# Moral Constitution (Constitution Morale)

## Overview

Throughout *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim employs the concept of
**moral constitution** (*constitution morale*) to designate the
characteristic moral configuration of a social group — the ensemble of
collective habits, shared sentiments, and normative dispositions that
distinguish one social milieu from another and determine its specific
mode of acting upon the individual. The moral constitution is not a
psychological attribute of persons but a **property of the group**, and
it varies systematically with social structure. (doc_final.md:4310,
10516)

The concept serves a crucial analytical function: it bridges Durkheim's
structural analysis (the degree of integration or regulation of a group)
and the observed behavioural outcome (the suicide rate). The moral
constitution is the *mechanism* through which social structure produces
its effects on individuals.

## The Concept in Durkheim's Architecture

Durkheim uses *constitution morale* in several distinct but related
contexts, each illuminating a different dimension of the concept:

### 1. The Moral Constitution of Genders

One of the most striking applications concerns the differential suicide
rates of men and women. Durkheim attributes the lower suicide rate of
women not to biological difference but to a different **moral
constitution** produced by their different mode of participation in
social life:

> Ce qui affecte différemment la constitution morale des deux sexes,
> c'est que l'un et l'autre ne sont pas également engagés dans la vie
> collective. (doc_final.md:6391-6393)

Women's moral constitution — more traditional, less individualised, more
closely governed by collective sentiment — is the product of their
lesser integration into the individuating currents of modern social
life, not of innate female psychology.

### 2. The Moral Constitution of the Widowed

The differential suicide rates of widows and widowers are traced to the
different effects of widowhood on the moral constitution of each sex:

> La vérité, c'est que la constitution morale des veufs et des veuves
> n'est pas la même... (doc_final.md:6979-6980)

The widower's moral constitution is more profoundly disturbed by the
loss of the spouse because, for men, the conjugal bond is a more
important source of moral regulation than it is for women, whose moral
constitution is shaped more by the family as a whole than by the
marriage relation alone.

### 3. The Moral Constitution of the Military

The army provides the clearest example of a group whose moral
constitution directly produces a specific suicide type:

> Puisque donc cette constitution morale est le terrain naturel du
> suicide altruiste, il y a tout lieu de supposer que le suicide
> militaire a ce même caractère et provient de la même origine.
> (doc_final.md:8580-8582)

The military moral constitution — defined by impersonality, abnegation,
and the displacement of the principle of conduct outside the self — is
the "natural terrain" of altruistic suicide. It is not an individual
pathology but a collective moral state that, when sufficiently intense,
inclines individuals to sacrifice their lives.

### 4. The Moral Constitution and the Division of Labour

In Livre III, Durkheim extends the concept to characterise entire types
of social organisation. The moral constitution generated by the modern
division of labour is one of **moral individualism** — a cult of the
individual person that, paradoxically, is itself a collective
representation:

> ...d'une constitution morale *sui generis* qui a elle-même pour cause
> un certain état de la division du travail social. (doc_final.md:10516-10517)

This moral constitution of modern societies — individualistic, valuing
personal autonomy — is the social-psychological condition that makes
egoistic suicide possible. It is not that individuals *choose* to be
egoistic; rather, the moral constitution of their society has made
individualism a collective imperative.

## Theoretical Function

The *constitution morale* concept performs several theoretical functions
in Durkheim's argument:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Mediation** | Links social structure (integration/regulation levels) to individual behaviour (suicide) |
| **Typification** | Distinguishes the characteristic moral profiles of different social groups and suicide types |
| **Anti-psychologism** | Insists that the determinants of suicide are collective, not individual-psychological |
| **Dynamism** | Allows for historical change in the moral character of societies (e.g., the rise of moral individualism) |

The concept is central to Durkheim's methodological project: to
demonstrate that even the most apparently personal act — suicide — is
shaped by collective moral forces that have the same externality and
constraint as physical facts.

## The Moral Constitution as Collective Fact

Crucially, the moral constitution is not reducible to the sum of
individual psychologies. It is a **collective fact** that pre-exists
each individual, is transmitted through socialisation, and persists
beyond any particular member. When Durkheim speaks of a group's "moral
constitution inclining powerfully toward suicide," he is describing a
property of the *group as a whole* that exerts a sui generis pressure on
its members:

> ...cette constitution morale qui incline puissamment l'homme à se
> défaire de la vie. (doc_final.md:8828-8829)

This is the core of Durkheim's sociological realism: moral
constitutions, though immaterial, are as real and as causally
efficacious as material structures. They are the collective soul whose
variations are written in suicide statistics.
