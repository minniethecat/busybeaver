# Coefficient of Preservation

## Overview

The **coefficient of preservation** (*coefficient de préservation*) is one of Émile Durkheim's key methodological innovations in *Le Suicide* (1897). It is a statistical metric designed to quantify the protective effect that membership in a social group — particularly the domestic group of marriage and family — exercises against suicide. Durkheim defines it with characteristic precision:

> Par ce mot, nous désignons le nombre qui indique combien, dans un groupe, on se tue de fois moins que dans un autre considéré au même âge. (doc_final.md:6044-6046)

When the coefficient falls below 1, it becomes a **coefficient of aggravation** (*coefficient d'aggravation*), indicating that the group faces an elevated suicide risk relative to the reference population. The concept is central to Durkheim's empirical demonstration that the protective effect of the domestic group is a genuine social fact — irreducible to individual selection effects, psychological predisposition, or economic advantage.

The coefficient of preservation is deployed extensively in Book II, Chapters II–III, where Durkheim analyzes the differential suicide rates of celibates, married persons, and widowed persons across multiple European countries, controlling for both age and sex.

## Definition and Calculation

The coefficient of preservation expresses the relative immunity of one group compared to another. When Durkheim states that the coefficient of preservation of married men aged 25 relative to celibates of the same age is 3, he means:

> Si l'on représente par 1 la tendance au suicide des époux à ce moment de la vie, il faudra représenter par 3 celle des célibataires à la même période. (doc_final.md:6047-6049)

The calculation requires **age-specific suicide rates** for each civil status group, since suicide propensity varies systematically with age. Without age control, the coefficient is meaningless — as Durkheim demonstrated in his critique of the raw numbers that appeared to show celibates had lower suicide rates than married persons. Once age is controlled, the apparent immunity of celibates inverts, revealing the protective effect of marriage. (doc_final.md:5883-5962)

## Key Empirical Findings

### The Protective Effect of Marriage

Durkheim's analysis of Oldenburg data (1871–85) and French statistics yields four major findings:

**1. From age 20 onward, married persons of both sexes benefit from a coefficient of preservation relative to celibates.** The figure of 1.6 calculated by [[jacques-bertillon|Bertillon]] is "plutôt un minimum qu'une moyenne" — a minimum rather than an average. (doc_final.md:6190-6193)

**2. The coefficient evolves with age.** It rises rapidly to a maximum between ages 25–30 (France) or 30–40 (Oldenburg), then declines gradually until the final period of life, where a slight recovery sometimes occurs. (doc_final.md:6196-6198)

**3. The coefficient varies by sex — and the direction of sex difference varies by society.** In France, married men are substantially more protected than married women (average coefficient 2.73 vs. 1.56, a gap of 43%). But in Oldenburg, the pattern is reversed: married women have the higher coefficient (2.16 vs. 1.83). Durkheim formulates this as a general law: *the sex most favored in marriage varies across societies, and the magnitude of the gap between the sexes varies according to which sex is favored*. (doc_final.md:6201-6210)

**4. Widowhood diminishes but does not eliminate the coefficient.** Widowed persons kill themselves more than married persons but generally less than celibates. The coefficient of widowed persons sometimes reaches 1.60–1.66, retaining — in attenuated form — the protective benefit marriage had conferred. (doc_final.md:6221-6235)

The following table summarizes Durkheim's Oldenburg data (doc_final.md:6063-6127):

| Group | Coefficient of Preservation (vs. Celibates) |
|-------|-------------------------------------------|
| Married men (France, mean) | 2.73 |
| Married women (France, mean) | 1.56 |
| Married men (Oldenburg, mean) | 1.83 |
| Married women (Oldenburg, mean) | 2.16 |
| Widowers (France, mean) | 1.32 |
| Widows (France, mean) | 0.84 (aggravation) |

### The Selection Hypothesis Refuted

A natural objection to Durkheim's interpretation is that married persons might simply be a biologically or psychologically superior group — that marriage "selects" the healthiest individuals, and their lower suicide rate reflects this pre-existing superiority rather than any protective effect of the domestic group itself. Durkheim calls this the **sélection matrimoniale** hypothesis. (doc_final.md:6238-6242)

Durkheim refutes the selection hypothesis through the age-profile of the coefficient:

- If innate superiority explained the protection, the coefficient should be **highest** at later ages, when selection has had time to concentrate the fittest into the married population. Instead, the coefficient reaches its **maximum almost immediately** (age 25–30), when the vast majority of the eventual married population is still classified as celibate — precisely when selection has operated *least*. (doc_final.md:6315-6337)

- Between ages 30–40, when the separation of the married and celibate populations is most complete (those who will never marry have been fully sifted out), the coefficient **declines sharply** — from 3.20 to 2.77 for men, and from 4.53 to 2.22 for women. If selection were the mechanism, the coefficient should peak here, not fall. (doc_final.md:6337-6341)

- If selection operated equally on both sexes, the coefficient should be symmetric. But in France, married men are protected at 2.73 while married women at only 1.56 — a 43% gap. Selection cannot explain why the same filtering process would produce such different outcomes by sex. (doc_final.md:6351-6357)

### The Primacy of the Family over the Conjugal Pair

The most decisive evidence for the social (rather than selective) nature of the protective effect comes from the analysis of **children**. When Durkheim separates married persons with children from those without, the results are striking:

| Group | Coefficient of Preservation |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Married men without children | 1.5 |
| Married men with children | nearly double (≈3.0) |
| Married women without children | **0.67** (aggravation — they kill themselves *more* than celibates) |
| Married women with children | 1.89 |

(doc_final.md:6548-6565)

The finding that childless married women have a coefficient *below* 1 — meaning marriage without children actually **aggravates** their suicide tendency — is one of Durkheim's most striking results. It demonstrates that:

1. The protective effect is primarily attributable to the **family society** (*société familiale*), not the conjugal society alone.
2. The conjugal relationship, by itself, may even be detrimental to women — a finding Durkheim links to his broader analysis of [[divorce-and-suicide|conjugal anomie]] and the differential impact of marital regulation on the sexes.
3. The presence of children provides a measurable additional protection that cannot be reduced to individual psychology or economic advantage. (doc_final.md:6570-6590)

## Theoretical Significance

The coefficient of preservation is far more than a descriptive statistic. It is Durkheim's principal evidentiary tool for establishing three core theoretical claims:

1. **The domestic group is a sui generis social reality.** Its protective effect against suicide is a collective property that cannot be disaggregated into the sum of individual benefits (companionship, sexual gratification, economic support). The coefficient measures the force of a *social* bond, not a psychological predisposition.

2. **Integration and regulation are analytically distinct.** The coefficient captures the integrative function of the family (the density of relations binding the individual to a group). The regulatory function of marriage — which Durkheim analyzes separately through [[divorce-and-suicide]] — operates through a different mechanism. The fact that marriage without children aggravates women's suicide risk while protecting men's reveals the complex interplay of these two dimensions.

3. **Moral statistics require rigorous age-control.** The coefficient of preservation is a methodological lesson in the analysis of [[suicide-rate|suicide rates]]. Raw comparisons across civil status groups are systematically misleading because of confounding by age, sex, and the presence of children. The coefficient formalizes the necessity of multivariate control that Durkheim insists upon throughout *Le Suicide*.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The coefficient of preservation is methodologically and theoretically linked to several other Durkheimian concepts:
- It is the primary quantitative evidence for **[[social-integration]]** — the force binding individuals to collective life
- Its sex-specific patterns connect directly to **[[divorce-and-suicide|conjugal anomie]]** and the differential regulation of the sexes
- The discovery that childless marriage aggravates women's suicide risk anticipates Durkheim's analysis of **[[anomic-suicide]]**
- The age-profile of the coefficient refutes the selection hypothesis that [[jacques-bertillon|Bertillon]] had advanced
- It exemplifies Durkheim's broader approach to **[[suicide-rate|moral statistics]]** as the empirical foundation of sociological theory
