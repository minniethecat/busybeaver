# Biological Determinism

## Overview

Before presenting his own sociological theory, Durkheim devotes a substantial portion of Livre I of *Le Suicide* (1897) to the systematic refutation of extra-social explanations of suicide. Among these, **biological determinism** — the claim that suicide propensity is rooted in race, heredity, or constitutional organic factors — receives particularly detailed scrutiny. Durkheim's procedure is consistent: he accepts the empirical data cited by biological theorists, then demonstrates that the same data are better explained by social causes. (doc_final.md:1943-1946)

This refutation is not merely polemical. By eliminating the biological candidate explanations, Durkheim carves out the logical space for his sociological account: if suicide rates are not determined by organic constitution, they must be determined by the constitution of society.

## Race as a Putative Cause

The claim that different racial groups possess inherently different propensities to suicide is examined through cross-national suicide statistics. Germany and the German-speaking lands show markedly higher suicide rates than Latin, Slavic, or Anglo-Saxon peoples, a fact sometimes attributed to a racial disposition of the Germanic type. (doc_final.md:2130-2144)

Durkheim concedes that this correlation exists at the level of nations — but correlation with nationality is not correlation with race. A nationality comprises a shared civilization, shared institutions, and a shared moral environment. To isolate race, one must examine whether the alleged propensity persists when the social environment changes:

> Mais il faudrait avoir démontré que ce penchant est lié à un état héréditaire de l'organisme allemand, que c'est un trait permanent du type, qui subsiste alors même que le milieu social est changé. (doc_final.md:2150-2154)

### The Austria Test

Austria provides a natural experiment. Its population includes both Germans and non-Germans living under the same political and legal institutions. If the German "racial" propensity were genuine, German Austrians should show higher suicide rates than their Czech, Polish, or Italian co-citizens. The data show otherwise: when the social environment is held constant, the "racial" difference largely disappears. What matters is not German blood but the specific character of German *civilization* — its religious composition, its family structures, its degree of social integration. (doc_final.md:2157-2190)

## Heredity

Durkheim next examines the claim that a specific hereditary disposition to suicide exists — that suicide "runs in families" through biological transmission rather than social contagion or shared environment.

His treatment is characteristically epidemiological. If suicide were transmitted biologically, its distribution across age groups should follow the pattern of hereditary traits: early onset, stability across the life course. Instead, suicide rates increase monotonically with age — the precise opposite of what a hereditary model would predict. The age curve of suicide is social, not biological.

Moreover, families that show multiple suicides across generations are also families that share a common moral atmosphere, common beliefs, and common patterns of social integration. Disentangling biological heredity from social heredity is impossible in family-level data, and the cross-national patterns already examined speak against the biological interpretation.

## The Sociological Alternative

Durkheim's refutation of biological determinism is not nihilistic — it does not deny the reality of the observed patterns. Rather, it relocates their cause. The stability of national suicide rates over decades (doc_final.md:687-696) — which biological theorists cited as evidence of constitutional causes — is equally consistent with the stability of social structures. The suicide rate is a property of the *social group*, not of the biological organism.

This argument establishes a methodological principle that runs through the entire work: **social facts must be explained by other social facts**. The suicide rate is a collective phenomenon, and its causes must be sought in the structure of collective life — in integration and regulation — not in the bodies or brains of individuals.
