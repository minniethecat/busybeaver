# Military Spirit (Esprit Militaire)

## Overview

In Livre II, Chapitre IV of *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim identifies the
army as the one milieu in contemporary Europe where **altruistic
suicide** persists as a chronic phenomenon. The proximate cause is not
the hardship of military life, the absence of liberty, or alcoholism,
but rather a specific moral constitution — the **military spirit**
(*esprit militaire*) — characterised by the systematic weakening of
individual personality and the displacement of the principle of conduct
outside the self. (doc_final.md:8298-8300, 8560-8564)

> La première qualité du soldat est une sorte d'impersonnalité que l'on
> ne rencontre nulle part, au même degré, dans la vie civile. Il faut
> qu'il soit exercé à faire peu de cas de sa personne, puisqu'il doit
> être prêt à en faire le sacrifice dès qu'il en a reçu l'ordre.
> (doc_final.md:8564-8568)

## The Magnitude of Military Suicide

Durkheim presents a striking comparative table (Tableau XXIII) showing
that in every European country, soldiers die by suicide at a higher rate
than civilians of the same age:

| Country | Soldiers per million | Civilians per million (same age) | Coefficient of Aggravation |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Austria (1876–90) | 1,253 | 122 | 10.0 |
| United States (1870–84) | 680 | 80 | 8.5 |
| Italy (1876–90) | 407 | 77 | 5.2 |
| England (1876–90) | 209 | 79 | 2.6 |
| Wurtemberg (1846–58) | 320 | 170 | 1.92 |
| Saxony (1847–58) | 640 | 369 | 1.77 |
| Prussia (1876–90) | 607 | 394 | 1.50 |
| France (1876–90) | 333 | 265 | 1.25 |

(doc_final.md:8308-8338)

The gap is even wider when controlling for marital status. French
soldiers aged 20–25 had 380 suicides per million versus 237 for
unmarried civilian males of the same age — a coefficient of 1.6
independent of celibacy. For non-commissioned officers, the coefficient
rose to nearly 1.94 (and by Durkheim's correction, close to 4).
Officers, despite being mostly married, still had a coefficient of 2.15
relative to all civilian males of the same age. (doc_final.md:8362-8401)

## Rejected Explanations

### Celibacy

Soldiers are unmarried, and celibacy is associated with higher suicide
rates. But the soldier is *not* an isolated individual — he is a member
of a strongly constituted society that partially substitutes for the
family. Moreover, the excess persists even when soldiers are compared to
unmarried civilians of the same age. (doc_final.md:8353-8366)

### Alcoholism

Alcoholism is said to be more prevalent in the army. But Durkheim has
already demonstrated that alcoholism has no definite influence on
suicide rates in general. Furthermore, the few years of military service
(three in France, two and a half in Prussia) are insufficient to produce
enough inveterate alcoholics to explain the enormous excess. Even the
observers most favourable to the alcoholism hypothesis attribute at most
one tenth of cases to it. (doc_final.md:8414-8428)

### Disgust with Service

The most common explanation — that the rigours of discipline, absence of
liberty, and privations of barracks life make service intolerable — is
contradicted by the facts. Non-commissioned officers, who have chosen to
remain in the army beyond obligatory service, have *higher* suicide
rates than conscripts. The coefficient increases with duration of
service and with rank up to the level of sous-officier: the more one is
integrated into military life, the greater the risk. (doc_final.md:8430-8440,
8584-8600)

## The Positive Explanation: Impersonality as Altruism

The military spirit is defined by a systematic **impersonality**
(*impersonnalité*). The soldier is trained to hold his person in low
esteem because he must be ready to sacrifice it on command. Even in
peacetime, discipline requires obedience without discussion and,
sometimes, without understanding — an intellectual abnegation
incompatible with individualism:

> Il faut ne tenir que faiblement à son individualité pour se conformer
> aussi docilement à des impulsions extérieures. En un mot, le soldat a
> le principe de sa conduite en dehors de lui-même; ce qui est la
> caractéristique de l'état d'altruisme.
> (doc_final.md:8572-8576)

The army, Durkheim observes, is the part of modern societies that most
closely resembles the structure of **lower societies** — a massive,
compact group that tightly frames the individual and prevents autonomous
movement. This moral constitution (*constitution morale*) is the natural
terrain of altruistic suicide. (doc_final.md:8576-8582)

## The Hierarchy of Impersonality

The suicide rates within the military follow a precise hierarchy that
confirms Durkheim's interpretation:

| Rank | Relative Suicide Rate | Degree of Impersonality |
|------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Conscripts (simple soldats) | Lowest | Basic training in submission |
| Re-enlisted (rengagés) | Higher | Longer exposure to military spirit |
| Non-commissioned officers (sous-officiers) | Highest (~4× civilian) | Maximum submission and passivity required |
| Officers (officiers) | Intermediate (~2.15×) | Some initiative required; more developed individuality |

(doc_final.md:8584-8600)

Non-commissioned officers have the highest suicide rate because no
function demands the habit of submission and passivity to the same
degree. The officer, however disciplined, must retain some capacity for
initiative — he has a wider field of action and therefore a more
developed individuality. The conditions favourable to altruistic suicide
are thus less completely realised in him than in the sous-officier.
(doc_final.md:8592-8600)

## The Inverse Relationship with Civilian Suicide

A striking cross-national pattern further confirms the interpretation:
the military coefficient of aggravation is *highest* where the civilian
suicide rate is *lowest*, and vice versa. Denmark, the "classic land of
suicide," is the only country where soldiers do not kill themselves
more than civilians. This inverse relationship suggests that military
and civilian suicides belong to different types — altruistic and
egoistic respectively — and that the social conditions favouring one are
inimical to the other. (doc_final.md:8606-8609)
