# Celibacy and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim presents a rigorous statistical analysis of the relationship between celibacy (the unmarried state) and suicide rates. At first glance, raw suicide statistics appear to show that celibates kill themselves less than married people—a finding consistent with the popular belief that marriage, with its responsibilities and burdens, produces despair. Durkheim systematically dismantles this interpretation by demonstrating that the apparent immunity of celibates is an artifact of age distribution. Once age is properly controlled, celibacy is revealed as an aggravating factor: unmarried persons are approximately 1.6 times more likely to die by suicide than married persons of the same age. (book.md:5883-5962)

This finding forms the foundation of Durkheim's broader theory of [[social-integration|social integration]] and the protective function of the domestic group. The celibate, lacking the regulatory and integrative force of conjugal society, is more exposed to the [[suicidogenic-current|suicidogenic currents]] that traverse modern societies.

## The Statistical Illusion: Raw Numbers vs. Age-Adjusted Rates

Durkheim begins by confronting the raw numbers. In France during the period 1873–78, there were 16,264 suicides of married persons against 14,709 of celibates—a ratio of 100:132. Similar proportions were observed in other periods and countries, leading many authors to conclude that marriage itself was a cause of suicide. (book.md:5883-5890)

> Cette opinion a pour elle toutes les vraisemblances. Le célibataire, en effet, a la vie plus facile que l'homme marié. Le mariage n'apporte-t-il pas avec lui toute sorte de charges et de responsabilités? (book.md:5891-5895)

Durkheim identifies a critical confounding variable: a very large number of celibates are under 16 years of age, whereas all married persons are older. Since the suicide tendency before age 16 is extremely weak, the presence of children in the celibate group artificially depresses its suicide rate. (book.md:5903-5911)

When celibates under 16 are eliminated from the comparison, the relationship inverts:

| Group | Suicides per million (1863–68, France) |
|-------|---------------------------------------|
| Celibates over 16 | 173 |
| Married persons | 154.5 |
| Ratio | 112 : 100 |

> Il y a donc une aggravation qui tient au célibat. (book.md:5924)

## The Age Structure Refinement

Durkheim further refines the analysis by noting that even among those over 16, celibates and married persons do not have the same average age. In France, 58% of unmarried men are concentrated in the youngest age bracket, while married men are distributed across older ages. Since suicide propensity increases with age, a proper comparison must control for age simultaneously with civil status. (book.md:5926-5940)

Durkheim proposes the **coefficient of preservation** (*coefficient de préservation*) as the key metric:

> Par ce mot, nous désignons le nombre qui indique combien, dans un groupe, on se tue de fois moins que dans un autre considéré au même âge. (book.md:6070-6072)

When the coefficient falls below 1, it becomes a **coefficient of aggravation**—indicating that the group faces an elevated suicide risk relative to the reference group.

## Quantifying the Aggravation

Using data from 1848–57, Durkheim calculates that if age alone operated, the suicide aptitude of celibates could not exceed 97.9, while that of married persons would be approximately 140—a ratio of 100:69 in favor of celibates. But in reality, celibates kill themselves *more* than married persons. The family life thus reverses the expected age relationship:

| Scenario | Celibate rate (relative) | Married rate (relative) |
|----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Age effect alone (expected) | 69 | 100 |
| Observed (actual) | 112 | 100 |

Durkheim concludes that marriage reduces the danger of suicide by approximately half. The aggravation attributable to celibacy is expressed by the ratio 112/69 = **1.6**. (book.md:5947-5962)

> On peut dire, par conséquent, que l'état de mariage diminue de moitié environ le danger du suicide; ou, pour parler avec plus de précision, il résulte du célibat une aggravation qui est exprimée par le rapport 112/69 = 1,6. (book.md:5957-5961)

The same ratio is confirmed in Italy (1873–77), where the celibate-to-married suicide ratio is also 1.6. Comparable findings are reported across different countries, including Württemberg (1846–60) where the ratio reaches 143:100. (book.md:5964-5975)

## Celibacy and Sex

Durkheim's analysis reveals that the protective effect of marriage operates differently by sex. In France, married men benefit more from marriage than married women. The coefficient of preservation for married men relative to celibates is consistently higher than that for married women. This asymmetry becomes central to Durkheim's later analysis of [[divorce-and-suicide|conjugal anomie]] and the differential impact of marital regulation on the sexes. (book.md:6205-6240)

## Widowhood as Comparative Evidence

The comparison with widowhood provides further confirmation. Widowed persons, despite being older (which naturally increases suicide risk), consistently show higher suicide rates than married persons of the same age, but—critically—lower rates than celibates. This intermediate position supports Durkheim's thesis:

> Le veuvage diminue le coefficient des époux des deux sexes, mais, le plus souvent, il ne le supprime pas complètement. (book.md:6241-6243)

The widowed retain, in attenuated form, the protective benefit that marriage had conferred. The family bond, once established, leaves a lasting imprint on the individual's integration into society, even after the spouse's death. This finding is further explored in the analysis of [[widowhood-and-suicide]].

## The Presence of Children

Durkheim goes beyond the simple married/celibate binary to examine the role of children in the protective effect. Widowed persons with children show a better coefficient of preservation than widowed persons without children—despite being older on average. The protective function of the domestic group is therefore not reducible to the conjugal tie alone; it extends to the density of family relations more broadly. (book.md:6521-6544)

| Group | Suicides per million |
|-------|---------------------|
| Widowed with children | 766 |
| Widowed without children | 1,004 |

The presence of children provides a measurable additional protection beyond that of marriage itself.

## Theoretical Significance

The celibacy finding is one of the pillars of Durkheim's theory of [[social-integration]]. It demonstrates that:

1. **The social group exercises a genuine protective function** — suicide rates vary systematically with the degree of integration into domestic society, independent of individual psychology or economic conditions.

2. **Statistical artifacts must be rigorously controlled** — the apparent immunity of celibates is entirely explained by age composition; the true effect is the opposite of what raw numbers suggest. This methodological lesson about [[suicide-rate|suicide rate]] analysis is central to the entire work.

3. **The domestic group is a sui generis social reality** — its protective effect cannot be reduced to the sum of individual benefits (sexual gratification, economic support, companionship). It operates as a collective force that integrates the individual into a moral community.

## Relation to Other Suicide Types

Celibacy-induced suicide fits within Durkheim's typology primarily as a manifestation of **[[egoistic-suicide]]**. The unmarried individual, less integrated into the collective life of the domestic group, is more exposed to the currents of egoistic suicide that result from excessive [[individuation]]. However, the analysis of [[divorce-and-suicide|divorce]] introduces a second mechanism: the weakening of [[social-regulation|marital regulation]] produces [[anomic-suicide]], particularly affecting men in societies with high divorce rates. Celibacy thus involves both a deficit of integration (egoism) and, for those who have never been married, the absence of the regulatory framework that marriage provides.
