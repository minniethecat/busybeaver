# Georg von Mayr

## Overview

**Georg von Mayr** (1841–1897) was a German statistician and political economist whose work on moral statistics (*Moralstatistik*) provided Durkheim with detailed empirical evidence on suicide trends in Bavaria and beyond. As a leading figure in the German statistical tradition associated with the *Handwörterbuch der Staatswissenschaften*, Mayr assembled and analyzed long-run data series on suicide, enabling the kind of cross-temporal comparison that was central to Durkheim's sociological method.

Durkheim cited Mayr at several critical junctures in *Le Suicide*: on the sex ratio of mental illness, on the Scandinavian anomaly in the relationship between Protestantism and suicide, and most extensively on the inverse relationship between grain prices and suicide rates in Bavaria. In each case, Mayr's data served as a test of competing explanatory frameworks.

## Suicide Statistics and Moralstatistik

Mayr's article *Selbstmordstatistik* (Suicide Statistics), published in 1895 in the *Handwörterbuch der Staatswissenschaften*, was a comprehensive synthesis of nineteenth-century suicide data across European countries. This reference work placed Mayr at the center of the German *Moralstatistik* tradition — an approach to social statistics that sought to identify regularities in apparently voluntary human behavior, including suicide, marriage, and crime.

Mayr's earlier monograph *Die Gesetzmässigkeit im Gesellschaftsleben* (The Regularity of Social Life, 1877) had already articulated the philosophical foundation of this approach: that aggregate human behavior displayed statistical regularities as stable as those of the physical world, implying the operation of supra-individual social forces. This premise was fundamental to Durkheim's argument that the suicide rate constituted a *social fact* irreducible to individual psychology.

## The Bavarian Grain Price Curve

Durkheim's most extensive use of Mayr's data appears in his discussion of the relationship between economic conditions and suicide. Drawing on "a curve constructed by Mayr for the period 1835–61," Durkheim showed that the years of lowest rye prices in Bavaria — 1857–58 and 1858–59 — coincided with marked increases in suicide: from 286 suicides in 1857 to 329 in 1858 and 387 in 1859. (8986)

This pattern, Durkheim argued, was paradoxical if suicide were simply a response to economic hardship. Mayr's data showed that improving material conditions — falling grain prices — were associated with rising, not falling, suicide rates. Durkheim used this finding to support his theory of *anomie*: that economic prosperity, no less than economic crisis, could disturb the regulatory function of social norms and thereby increase suicide.

## The Scandinavian Challenge

When Durkheim presented his finding that Protestant regions consistently showed higher suicide rates than Catholic ones, Mayr's work provided the most prominent counterexample. Mayr had noted that Norway and Sweden — both predominantly Protestant — had relatively low suicide rates, a fact that seemed to contradict Durkheim's thesis about the suicidogenic effects of Protestantism. (5171)

Durkheim addressed this challenge directly, arguing that international comparisons of this kind were inconclusive when limited to only two countries, and that Norway and Sweden's suicide rates, while low in absolute terms, were relatively high given their modest level of civilizational development. Durkheim concluded that the Scandinavian case not only failed to refute the confessional-suicide relationship but actually tended to confirm it.

## Sex and Mental Illness

In his discussion of the sex ratio of suicide, Durkheim also drew on Mayr's data. Koch's census of the mentally ill across eleven German states had found 1.18 male and 1.30 female cases of insanity per 1,000 inhabitants — a female excess that might seem to explain the lower female suicide rate. Durkheim noted that "Mayr, for his part, found analogous figures," reinforcing the claim that the sex difference in suicide could not be attributed to differential rates of mental illness. (1387)
