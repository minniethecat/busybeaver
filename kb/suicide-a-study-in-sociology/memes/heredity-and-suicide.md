Durkheim's analysis of **heredity and suicide** constitutes a foundational [[social-fact|sociological]] critique of [[biological-determinism]], appearing in Livre I, Chapter IV of *Le Suicide* (1897). Building on his refutation of psychopathological and racial explanations, Durkheim argues that the transmission of suicidal tendencies through genetic inheritance cannot account for the systematic variation observed in [[suicide-rate|suicide rates]] across societies.

## Durkheim's Definitional Precision

Durkheim begins by distinguishing two meanings of "hereditary suicide" [lines 2467–2471]:

> When one says that suicide is hereditary, does one simply mean that the children of suicides, having inherited their parents' temperament, are inclined to behave as they did in similar circumstances? In these terms, the proposition is incontestable, but without significance, for it is not then suicide that is hereditary; what is transmitted is merely a certain general temperament.

The second, stronger meaning — which Durkheim subjects to empirical scrutiny — is the claim that the tendency to suicide passes "directly and integrally from parents to children" as a quasi-autonomous psychological mechanism akin to [[mental-alienation-and-suicide|monomania]] [lines 2479–2486].

## The Empirical Case Against Heredity

Durkheim marshals three lines of evidence against the heredity hypothesis:

### Insufficient Statistical Evidence

The available data, Durkheim argues, is anecdotal rather than systematic [lines 2524–2541]. He surveys the existing studies:

| Researcher | Sample | Heredity Rate | Durkheim's Critique |
|------------|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| Dr Luys | 39 patients with suicidal tendencies | 2.6% (1 case) | No family history in vast majority |
| [[louis-brierre-de-boismont|Brierre de Boismont]] | 265 patients | 4% (11 cases) | Very low prevalence |
| Cazauvieilh | 60 patients | 28% (13 cases) | Small sample, unrepresentative |
| Bavarian statistics | population-level (1857–66) | ~13% | Highest estimate, still modest |

For Durkheim, even the most favorable estimate (13%) fails to establish a causal relationship [lines 2548–2554].

### The Confound of Mental Illness

Durkheim's second argument is methodological: most heredity studies were conducted by alienists on asylum populations [lines 2558–2567]:

> One may therefore ask whether it is the inclination to suicide that is hereditary, or whether it is rather mental alienation, of which suicide is a frequent but nevertheless contingent symptom.

Since [[mental-alienation-and-suicide|mental illness]] itself shows strong heritability, the apparent transmission of suicidal behavior may simply reflect the inheritance of underlying psychopathology.

### Alternative Explanations: Social Contagion

Durkheim proposes that cases of suicide clustering within families can be explained by [[imitation|social contagion]] and shared environment rather than biological inheritance [lines 2567+]. Family members share not only genes but also social circumstances, values, and exposure to common stressors.

## Integration with Durkheim's Sociological Method

The heredity critique exemplifies Durkheim's broader methodological strategy in *Le Suicide*:

1. **Isolate a putatively individual cause** (here, biological inheritance)
2. **Confront it with comparative statistics** (cross-national suicide rates by sex, religion, etc.)
3. **Demonstrate that the individual-level factor cannot explain the social-level variation**
4. **Introduce a properly sociological explanation** ([[social-integration]] and [[social-regulation]])

The refutation of heredity thus clears the ground for Durkheim's positive thesis: suicide rates are determined by **social facts**, not by the organic constitution of individuals.

## Durkheim's Concession

Durkheim does not deny that individual temperament may create a predisposition [lines 2471–2478]. His claim is more limited and more precise: such predisposition, even if hereditary, cannot explain *why* suicide rates vary systematically across societies, religious groups, and historical periods. The hereditary temperament is, at most, a "terrain" favorable to suicide — never its sufficient cause.
