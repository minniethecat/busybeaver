Durkheim's analysis of **crime and suicide** appears in Livre III, Chapter II of *Le Suicide* (1897), where he examines whether these two forms of deviance share common causes or stand in systematic opposition. Engaging critically with the Italian criminological school — particularly [[enrico-morselli|Enrico Morselli]] and Enrico Ferri — Durkheim argues that crime and suicide are manifestations of **distinct social currents**, not two expressions of a single underlying cause.

## The Italian School's Thesis

The Italian criminologists, particularly Ferri and Morselli, proposed that suicide and homicide stand in a relationship of **antagonism** [lines 12994–13005]:

> According to them, the antagonism between suicide and homicide would be an absolutely general law. Whether in their geographical distribution or their evolution over time, one would everywhere see them develop in inverse relation to each other.

The empirical basis was a geographic observation: southern French and Italian departments showed high homicide rates and low suicide rates, while northern departments showed the reverse pattern [lines 12994–13001]. Guerry (1833) and Despine had earlier noted the same inverse relationship.

### Two Possible Interpretations

Durkheim identifies two ways to interpret this inverse relationship [lines 13001–13010]:

1. **Opposite currents**: Suicide and homicide are genuinely antagonistic social forces — as one rises, the other must fall.
2. **Common source, divergent expression**: Both are manifestations of a single underlying cause (a "constitutional" predisposition), expressed sometimes as homicide and sometimes as suicide, depending on circumstances.

The Italian school adopted the second interpretation, arguing that individual-level factors (age, sex, temperature) affect both suicide and homicide similarly, pointing to a shared organic basis [lines 13010–13040].

## Durkheim's Critique

Durkheim subjects the Italian thesis to systematic empirical scrutiny.

### Crime Against Property: No Relationship

Durkheim first examines the claim by Lacassagne and Chaussinand that suicide and property crime vary inversely [lines 12949–12990]. He finds the evidence wanting:

> In reality, it is impossible to perceive between them any relationship, whether direct or inverse. ... The general configuration of the two curves excludes any idea of correspondence. The property-crime curve is highly erratic; one sees it, from one year to the next, make abrupt jumps. ... By contrast, the suicide curve rises regularly in a uniform movement.

The statistical artifact of "correctionnalisation" (reclassifying crimes to lower courts) further undermines the claimed inverse relationship [lines 12958–12972].

### Crime Against Persons: A Spurious Antagonism

For crimes against persons (including homicide), Durkheim acknowledges the geographic inverse pattern [lines 12990–13005] but argues it has a sociological rather than psychological explanation:

The inverse relationship arises because the **social conditions** that produce high homicide rates (strong collective passions, traditional social structures) are precisely those that produce low suicide rates (high [[social-integration]]), and vice versa. The antagonism is between **types of social organization**, not between individual psychological dispositions.

### Individual-Level Parallelisms Explained

Durkheim addresses the Italian school's claim that individual-level factors (age, sex, temperature) affect both phenomena similarly:

| Factor | Italian Claim | Durkheim's Response |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| Age | Both increase with age | True, but this is a demographic regularity, not evidence of common causation |
| Sex | Both more frequent in men | True of many behaviors; reflects differential social position, not biology |
| Temperature | Both peak in warm months | Seasonal patterns reflect [[social-fact|social]] rhythms (intensified collective life), not thermometric effects |

For Durkheim, these parallelisms arise from the **social** organization of age, sex, and seasonal activity — not from shared organic causes.

## Crime, Suicide, and Social Types

Durkheim's conclusion integrates the crime-suicide relationship into his broader typology:

| Social Type | Suicide Pattern | Crime Pattern | Mechanism |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Low integration (egoism) | High [[egoistic-suicide]] | Variable property crime | Individuation weakens moral restraint |
| High integration (altruism) | High [[altruistic-suicide]] | Low crime against persons | Strong collective conscience suppresses individual violence |
| Low regulation (anomie) | High [[anomic-suicide]] | Higher property crime | Weakened normative regulation |
| High regulation (fatalism) | High [[fatalistic-suicide]] | Context-dependent | Excessive regulation |

The relationship between suicide and crime is thus not a direct causal link but a function of the **underlying state of [[social-integration]] and [[social-regulation]]** in a given society.

## Relation to Suicide and Homicide

Durkheim's analysis here should be distinguished from his later treatment of [[suicide-and-homicide]] in Livre III, where he argues that suicide and homicide, far from being psychological alternatives, can rise together under conditions of [[anomie]], challenging the Italian school's antagonism thesis on its own terms.
