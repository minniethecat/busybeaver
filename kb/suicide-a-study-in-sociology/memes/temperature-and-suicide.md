# Temperature and Suicide

## Overview

Among the extra-social factors proposed as causes of suicide, Émile Durkheim examines the role of climate and seasonal temperature in *Le Suicide* (1897). The hypothesis — advanced most notably by Enrico Morselli — holds that the temperate zone of Europe is the "privileged region" of suicide, and that the summer months, with their higher temperatures, precipitate suicidal acts. Durkheim subjects both claims to rigorous empirical scrutiny and concludes that **temperature and climate have no direct causal relationship with suicide**. The apparent correlations are artifacts of the geographic distribution of civilization and the seasonal rhythm of social life. (doc_final.md:2896-2942)

This argument exemplifies Durkheim's broader methodology: apparent cosmic or biological causes of suicide dissolve under sociological analysis, revealing the primacy of collective forces.

## The Illusion of Climatic Causation

Morselli observed that the suicide rate in Europe rises from south to north, peaking between the 47th and 57th parallels — the temperate zone — before declining again toward the far north. The temperate zone coincides with the centers of European civilization: Île-de-France and Saxony-Prussia. (doc_final.md:2907-2942)

> La tache qu'elle forme sur la carte n'est pas constituée par une seule bande... mais par deux taches distinctes: l'une qui a pour centre l'Île-de-France et les départements circonvoisins, l'autre la Saxe et la Prusse. Elles coïncident donc, non avec une région climatérique nettement définie, mais avec les deux principaux foyers de la civilisation européenne. (doc_final.md:2936-2942)

Durkheim advances three arguments against climatic causation:

1. **Historical variability**: Suicide flourished in Imperial Rome under a Mediterranean climate that today shows low rates; conversely, it was highly developed in certain periods under the burning sky of India (doc_final.md:2927-2934)
2. **Non-contiguous distribution**: The high-suicide zone is not a continuous climatic band but two disconnected spots centered on the intellectual capitals of Europe (doc_final.md:2936-2942)
3. **Within-country reversals**: In Italy, the north–south suicide gradient reversed between 1870 and the 1880s, while the climate remained unchanged — the only thing that changed was the diffusion of social currents from northern Europe (doc_final.md:2946-2968)

## Seasonal Temperature: A Real but Non-Causal Correlation

The seasonal data presents a genuine puzzle: suicide rates peak in the summer months (May–July) and reach their minimum in winter. This pattern is robust across countries. A naive interpretation would attribute the summer peak to heat, but Durkheim identifies a crucial confound: **the length of the day**. (doc_final.md:3005-3036)

| Factor | Correlation with Suicide | Causal? |
|--------|------------------------|---------|
| Ambient temperature | Positive (summer peak) | No — confounded by day length |
| Day length / social activity | Positive | Yes — drives social life intensity |
| Season of maximum social density | Summer | Yes — collective life is most active |

> L'homme quitte de préférence la vie au moment où elle est le plus active, non au moment où le soleil est le plus chaud. (doc_final.md:3025-3026)

The mechanism is sociological, not thermometric: longer days mean more intense collective life, which in turn means more occasions for the social currents that produce suicide to exert their influence on vulnerable individuals.
