# Bourdin

## Overview

**Bourdin** (often cited as *Docteur Bourdin*) was a French physician whose 1845 pamphlet *Du suicide considéré comme maladie* (*Suicide Considered as a Disease*) created significant debate in French medical circles upon its publication. Bourdin advanced the most extreme version of the psychiatric theory of suicide: that suicide was not merely associated with or symptomatic of mental illness but was itself a distinct disease entity (*une maladie*) with its own etiology and clinical course.

This thesis made Bourdin the ideal opponent for Durkheim's opening argument in *Le Suicide* (1897). By selecting the strongest and most explicit formulation of the pathological theory of suicide, Durkheim could demonstrate that even its most thorough defense collapsed under empirical scrutiny. Bourdin's work thus occupies a strategic position in the architecture of *Le Suicide*: it is the thesis that must be defeated for the sociological argument to begin.

## The Disease Thesis and Durkheim's Refutation

Bourdin's central claim was uncompromising: suicide was always and everywhere a disease. Unlike [[Jean-Étienne Esquirol]], who viewed suicide as a symptom that could appear in various forms of mental alienation — a "syndrome" rather than a distinct disease — Bourdin insisted that suicide constituted its own specific pathological entity. This position represented what Durkheim described as the thesis that suicide was "a disease *sui generis*, a special form of insanity."

Durkheim's refutation of Bourdin proceeded through statistical demonstration. If suicide were a disease, its distribution across populations should correlate with other disease patterns — yet suicide rates varied with social factors (religion, marital status, economic conditions) that bore no relationship to disease prevalence. Furthermore, if it were a disease, it should not exhibit the extraordinary temporal stability that statisticians from [[André-Michel Guerry]] to [[Enrico Morselli]] had documented. Diseases fluctuated with seasons, epidemics, and medical interventions; suicide rates remained constant year after year within the same society.

Bourdin also extended an exception — like Esquirol and [[Jean-Pierre Falret]] before him — to suicides motivated by religious faith or patriotic devotion. Durkheim seized on this concession: if self-destruction for faith or country was not pathological, then suicide could not be inherently a disease. The very act of carving out exceptions revealed that what distinguished "pathological" from "noble" suicide was not clinical symptomatology but social judgment.

## Legacy

Bourdin's pamphlet, though now largely forgotten outside Durkheim scholarship, played an essential role in the history of suicidology. By formulating the disease theory of suicide in its purest form, Bourdin gave Durkheim the clearest possible target. The thoroughness of Durkheim's refutation of Bourdin — occupying much of the opening chapter of *Le Suicide* — established the inadequacy of purely medical explanations and cleared the ground for the sociological theory that followed. Bourdin is thus remembered less for his own contributions than for the quality of the rebuttal he provoked.
