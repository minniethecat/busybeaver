# Corre

## Overview

**Corre** was a French naval physician and criminologist whose statistical investigations into the relationship between crime and suicide provided Durkheim with important empirical evidence for *Le Suicide* (1897). Writing from the dual perspective of naval medicine and the emerging field of criminal anthropology, Corre published detailed studies of suicide in the military port city of Brest and comparative data on crime and suicide across French departments.

Durkheim cited Corre's work at multiple junctures in *Le Suicide*, drawing on his departmental statistics to examine whether suicide and crime varied together across regions — a key test of the prevailing Lombrosian assumption that suicide and criminality shared a common psychopathological root. Corre's evidence helped Durkheim demonstrate that the two phenomena were, in fact, inversely related.

## Crime and Suicide

In *Crime et suicide* (Paris, 1891), Corre assembled statistical tables comparing the rates of suicide and criminal homicide across French departments. Durkheim drew on this data to address the claim, common in late nineteenth-century criminal anthropology, that suicide was a form of attenuated criminality directed against the self rather than others. If this hypothesis were correct, suicide and homicide should be positively correlated; regions with high homicide rates should also display high suicide rates.

Corre's data showed the opposite pattern. Durkheim cited Corre alongside [[Cesare Lombroso|Lombroso]], [[Enrico Ferri|Ferri]], and [[Enrico Morselli|Morselli]] in his discussion of the inverse relationship between homicide and suicide, noting that the southern departments of France — which had the highest homicide rates — consistently showed the lowest suicide rates. (16493) This geographical divergence was one of Durkheim's central arguments against the psychopathological theory of suicide.

## Suicide in Brest

Corre's study *Le délit et le suicide à Brest* (1890), published in the *Archives d'anthropologie criminelle*, examined suicide in a concentrated military and naval population. Brest, as a major French naval base, offered Corre a distinctive demographic setting: a young, predominantly male population subject to the disciplinary regime of military life but also exposed to the social disruptions characteristic of port cities.

Durkheim cited this study in the context of his broader discussion of the relationship between social milieu and suicide rates, using Corre's data as evidence that even within a single city, the incidence of suicide varied systematically according to social position, occupation, and civil status.

## Colonial Criminology

Beyond metropolitan France, Corre also published on crime in colonial settings. His study *Le crime en pays créoles* examined criminality in France's Creole territories, providing comparative data on the social determination of crime across different civilizational contexts. Durkheim referred to this work in his discussion of *anomie* and the *suicide fataliste*, noting that the fatalistic acceptance of social regulation in traditional and colonial societies stood in contrast to the anomic deregulation characteristic of modern European societies. (17142)

## Durkheim's Use of Corre

Corre's significance for Durkheim lay primarily in the quality and geographical detail of his statistical data. Unlike the more theoretically committed criminal anthropologists such as Lombroso and Ferri, who interpreted crime and suicide data through the lens of biological determinism, Corre's work was primarily empirical and descriptive. This made his data usable by Durkheim even when Corre's own interpretive framework differed from the sociological one.

In the footnotes of *Le Suicide*, Corre appears alongside [[Gabriel Tarde|Tarde]], [[Jacques Bertillon|Bertillon]], and [[Alexandre Lacassagne|Lacassagne]] — a constellation of French scholars whose work at the intersection of criminology, statistics, and social science provided the empirical foundation on which Durkheim built his sociological theory of suicide.
