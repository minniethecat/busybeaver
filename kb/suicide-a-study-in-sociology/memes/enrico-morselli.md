# Enrico Morselli

## Overview

**Enrico Morselli** (1852-1929), also referred to as Henry Morselli in French sources, was an Italian psychiatrist, anthropologist, and statistician whose 1879 work *Il Suicidio: Saggio di statistica morale comparata* (Suicide: An Essay in Comparative Moral Statistics) was one of the most comprehensive statistical treatments of suicide before Durkheim. (001-020)

Durkheim used Morselli's data extensively throughout *Le Suicide* — citing his suicide rate tables, his racial classification of European peoples, and his prophylactic recommendations — while systematically refuting Morselli's key theoretical conclusions. Morselli thus served as both a crucial empirical resource and a principal theoretical foil for Durkheim's sociological argument.

## Il Suicidio (1879)

| Contribution | Durkheim's Assessment |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Comprehensive European suicide statistics (1860s–1870s) | Used extensively as empirical basis |
| Racial-ethnic theory of suicide (Germanic, Celto-Roman, Slavic, Uralo-Altaic types) | Systematically refuted — variation is social, not racial |
| Correlation between mental illness and suicide | Refuted — idiots and the mentally ill do not show parallelism with suicide rates |
| Prophylactic treatment of suicide | Discussed and critiqued in Conclusion |

Morselli distinguished four European racial types with distinct suicide propensities: the Germanic type (German, Scandinavian, Anglo-Saxon, Flemish), the Celto-Roman type (Belgian, French, Italian, Spanish), the Slavic type, and the Uralo-Altaic type. (007-012) Durkheim demonstrated that the supposed racial patterns dissolved under sociological analysis — regional variations within nations correlated with social organization, not ethnic composition.

## Statistical Legacy

Morselli's data collection was formidable for its time. His tables of suicide rates across European nations and Italian provinces provided the raw material that Durkheim reanalyzed to demonstrate the constancy of suicide rates for each society — the empirical foundation for treating the suicide rate as a [[Social Fact]]. (001-025)

However, as Durkheim showed by substituting Koch's more rigorous mental health statistics for Morselli's, the correlation between mental alienation and suicide disappeared when more complete data was used. (001-030) This methodological refutation was foundational to Durkheim's argument that suicide is a social, not psychological, phenomenon.
