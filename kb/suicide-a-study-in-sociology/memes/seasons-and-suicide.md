# Seasons and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim examines the seasonal distribution of suicides as part of his systematic refutation of cosmic and biological explanations. The data reveal a robust pattern: **suicide rates rise through the spring, peak in summer (May–July), and decline through autumn to a winter minimum**. Roughly 600 suicides occur during the "beautiful season" for every 400 during the winter. (doc_final.md:3025-3036)

The question is *why*. A naive cosmic interpretation — that heat or light directly stimulates the suicidal impulse — is at odds with the pattern: the peak precedes the hottest months (July–August), and the monthly distribution tracks not the temperature curve but the curve of **day length**, which governs the intensity of collective social life.

## The Monthly Pattern

Durkheim presents data from multiple European countries showing the seasonal suicide rhythm:

| Period | Suicide Frequency | Social Life Intensity |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| December–January | Minimum | Minimum (short days, indoor life) |
| February–April | Rising | Accelerating |
| May–July | Maximum | Maximum (long days, outdoor life) |
| August–October | Declining | Decelerating |
| November | Low | Low |

> L'homme quitte de préférence la vie au moment où elle est le plus active. (doc_final.md:3025-3026)

The summer peak is counter-intuitive: one might expect suicide, as an act of despair, to cluster in the dark and cold months. Durkheim's inversion — suicide peaks when life is *most* active — is a decisive move in his argument that suicide is a social phenomenon, not a psychological or cosmic one.

## Day Length, Not Temperature

Durkheim distinguishes his interpretation from the temperature hypothesis through several observations. First, if heat were the mechanism, the peak should fall in July–August, the hottest months; instead it falls in June. Second, the monthly suicide curve more closely follows the **length of the working day** than the temperature curve. Third, the effect is largely independent of occupation: those who work outdoors and those who work indoors show the same seasonal rhythm, suggesting the mechanism operates through the general intensity of social interaction rather than direct exposure to the elements. (doc_final.md:3036-3060)

## The Sociological Mechanism

Longer days → more hours of collective activity → greater social density → stronger currents of collective sentiment → more individuals swept into suicidal acts. The same social forces that protect against suicide when they bind the individual to a group (as in marriage or religion) become dangerous when they merely increase the *volume* of collective life without simultaneously integrating the individual more tightly into it.

This argument complements Durkheim's analysis of [[temperature-and-suicide]] by demonstrating that even the most seemingly "natural" rhythms of suicide — the turn of the seasons — are in fact expressions of the rhythm of collective existence.
