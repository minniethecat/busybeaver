# Cesare Lombroso

## Overview

**Cesare Lombroso** (1835–1909) was an Italian criminologist, physician, and founder of the Italian school of criminal anthropology (*scuola positiva*). His theory of the "born criminal" (*delinquente nato*) — the idea that criminality was determined by inherited biological traits identifiable through physical stigmata — was among the most influential and controversial doctrines of late nineteenth-century social science. In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim engaged Lombroso's work critically, using the failure of biological-determinist explanations of suicide as a demonstration case for the necessity of sociological analysis.

Lombroso's principal works include *L'Uomo delinquente* (1876) and *La Donna delinquente* (1893, with Guglielmo Ferrero). His theory extended beyond crime to encompass suicide, which he and his followers — notably [[Enrico Ferri]] and Enrico Morselli — treated as expressions of the same underlying biological degeneracy that produced criminal behavior.

## The Italian School and Durkheim's Refutation

The core claim of Lombroso's school regarding suicide was that both homicide and suicide were manifestations of a single biological predisposition — a constitutional degeneracy that rendered the individual incapable of productive social life. The murderer and the suicide, in this view, were both "degenerates and weaklings" (*dégénérés et des impuissants*), equally destined to be defeated in the struggle for existence. The only difference was the direction in which the destructive impulse was turned: outward in homicide, inward in suicide.

Durkheim identified multiple empirical failures in this theory. First, if suicide and homicide shared a biological root, their geographic distributions should correlate — yet they were inversely related across Europe. Southern Italy had high homicide rates but low suicide, while northern France showed the opposite pattern. Second, the seasonal distribution of suicide — peaking with increasing day length rather than increasing heat — contradicted Lombroso's climatic-biological model. Third, the stable, society-specific suicide rates that Wagner and [[Adolphe Quetelet]] had documented could not be explained by individual biological variation.

Lombroso and Ferri attempted to salvage their theory by shifting the causal emphasis from absolute temperature to the *contrast* between cold and the arrival of warm weather — a modification Durkheim dismissed as having "no foundation whatsoever" when checked against the empirical data. Durkheim's alternative explanation — that suicide rates tracked the intensity of social life, which in turn followed the length of the working day — demonstrated the explanatory superiority of sociological over biological reductionism.

## Legacy

Lombroso's biological determinism has been thoroughly discredited in contemporary criminology and sociology. However, his historical importance is considerable: by formulating a systematic, natural-scientific theory of deviance, Lombroso provided the foil against which Durkheim developed sociology's distinctive claim — that social facts require social explanations. The confrontation between Lombroso's Italian school and Durkheim's French sociology in the pages of *Le Suicide* marks one of the founding debates of the sociological discipline.
