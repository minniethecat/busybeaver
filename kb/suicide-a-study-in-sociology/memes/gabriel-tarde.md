# Gabriel Tarde

## Overview

**Gabriel Tarde** (1843–1904) was a French sociologist, criminologist, and magistrate who served as chief of the judicial statistics service at the French Ministry of Justice. He was Durkheim's most significant intellectual rival in fin-de-siècle French sociology, advocating an individualistic, imitation-based theory of social phenomena against Durkheim's collective, constraint-based model.

In *Le Suicide*, Durkheim acknowledged Tarde's assistance in providing unpublished judicial statistics, but sharply contested Tarde's methodological approach to criminal statistics and his broader sociological theory. Tarde's works — including *Les lois de l'imitation* (1890), *La criminalité comparée* (1886), and *La sociologie élémentaire* — formed the backdrop against which Durkheim articulated his own conception of [[Social Fact]].

## Theoretical Disputes with Durkheim

| Issue | Tarde's Position | Durkheim's Position |
|-------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Nature of the social | Society is the product of inter-individual imitation and invention | Society is a reality sui generis, irreducible to individuals |
| Suicide rate constancy | Can be explained by imitative propagation of suicidal ideas | Constancy reflects collective forces, not individual contagion |
| Homicide trends in France | The apparent decline of homicide was an artifact of unprosecuted cases | Long-term trends are real and reveal social-structural changes |
| Statistical method | Comparisons of a few data points can establish trends | Systematic, long-period observation is required to establish laws |

Durkheim's most pointed methodological critique of Tarde concerned the latter's claim that the decline of homicide in France was merely apparent, resulting from cases classified without follow-up (*classées sans suite*) by prosecutors. Durkheim showed that a proper longitudinal analysis of these administrative classifications revealed patterns contrary to Tarde's interpretation.

## Key Works

Tarde's major publications that Durkheim engaged with include:

- **Les lois de l'imitation** (1890) — Advanced the theory that all social phenomena propagate through imitation, from individual to individual. Durkheim contested this by demonstrating that suicide rates remained stable even as individual suicidal acts varied, making imitation an insufficient explanation.
- **La criminalité comparée** (1886) — Comparative study of crime statistics that Durkheim cited in his discussion of the suicide-homicide relationship.
- **La sociologie élémentaire** — Published in the *Annales de l'Institut international de sociologie*, where Tarde continued to develop his individualistic framework.
