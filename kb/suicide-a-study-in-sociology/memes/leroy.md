# Dr. Leroy

## Overview

**Dr. Leroy** was a nineteenth-century French physician whose monograph *Étude sur le suicide et les maladies mentales dans le département de Seine-et-Marne* (1870) provided some of the most granular suicide statistics available to Durkheim. Leroy's study was methodologically distinctive: rather than working with national-level data, he analyzed suicide rates commune by commune within a single French department, revealing patterns invisible at higher levels of aggregation. Durkheim cited Leroy's findings extensively in *Le Suicide* (1897), particularly in his arguments about the social (rather than climatic or racial) determination of suicide rates.

Leroy occupies a unique position in the *Le Suicide* source base. Unlike the major theorists Durkheim engaged with critically — [[Jean-Étienne Esquirol]], [[Cesare Lombroso]], [[Adolphe Quetelet]] — Leroy was primarily an empirical researcher whose local data Durkheim used affirmatively as evidence for sociological explanations of suicide.

## The Seine-et-Marne Study

Leroy's central contribution was his commune-level classification of suicide rates across the Seine-et-Marne department. After ranking all communes by their suicide rate, he made the striking observation that the major urban centers did not necessarily top the list: La Ferté-sous-Jouarre (population 4,482) ranked 124th, Meaux (population 10,762) ranked 130th, Provins (population 7,547) ranked 135th, and Coulommiers (population 4,628) ranked 438th. This finding challenged the simple assumption that suicide was primarily an urban phenomenon.

Equally important was Leroy's observation regarding the relationship between mental illness and suicide. His data suggested that while localities with high rates of mental illness often also had high suicide rates, the two *maxima* could be completely separated — some areas had mental illness without suicide, others suicide without mental illness. Durkheim seized on this finding as direct empirical evidence against the psychiatric thesis that suicide was simply a manifestation of mental pathology.

Leroy also documented an intriguing institutional pattern: he noted that the prison at Melun appeared to have a suppressive effect on suicide in the surrounding area. Durkheim cited this as evidence that tightly regulated social environments could reduce suicidal tendencies — an observation consistent with his theory of [[Social Integration]].

## Legacy

Though Leroy is less known than the grand theorists of nineteenth-century suicidology, his empirical work exemplifies the local, data-driven tradition that made Durkheim's grand synthesis possible. By demonstrating that suicide rates varied systematically at the commune level — below the resolution of national statistics — Leroy provided evidence that suicide was sensitive to local social conditions, supporting Durkheim's core argument that suicide rates expressed collective social forces rather than individual dispositions or broad climatic factors.
