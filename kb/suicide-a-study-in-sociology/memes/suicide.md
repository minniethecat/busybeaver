# Suicide

## Overview

**Suicide** (*le suicide*), as formally defined by Émile Durkheim in his seminal 1897 work *Le Suicide: étude de sociologie*, is any case of death resulting directly or indirectly from a positive or negative act, accomplished by the victim themselves, which they knew would produce that result. (P02-015)

This definition deliberately excludes animal "suicides" (which lack anticipatory awareness of death) and includes cases where the victim may not have explicitly *intended* to die but was aware of the lethal consequences.

Without a stable, non-arbitrary object of study, sociological analysis of suicide rates would be impossible.

Durkheim's study of suicide is arguably the foundational text of empirical sociology. By taking an act widely regarded as the most intimate and personal of human decisions — the choice to end one's own life — and demonstrating that its aggregate patterns obeyed regular, predictable social laws, Durkheim established the autonomy of sociological explanation.

This finding challenged both psychological and biological reductionism simultaneously.

## Formal Definition

Durkheim's operational definition proceeds in two stages:

> On appelle suicide tout cas de mort qui résulte directement ou indirectement d'un acte positif ou négatif, accompli par la victime elle-même, et qu'elle savait devoir produire ce résultat. (P02-015)

The key elements of this definition are:

| Element | Meaning | Exclusion |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| *Mort* (death) | The outcome must be fatal | Attempted suicide, suicidal ideation |
| *Acte positif ou négatif* | Commission (shooting) or omission (refusing food) | Accidental death |
| *Accompli par la victime elle-même* | The victim is the agent of the act | Homicide, execution |
| *Qu'elle savait devoir produire ce résultat* | The victim knew death would result | Animal deaths, genuine accidents |

The final criterion — knowledge of the result — is the most contested element. It excludes cases where the victim truly did not understand the lethal nature of their action (e.g., a child playing with a firearm) while including cases where the victim may have been ambivalent about dying but recognized death as the probable outcome.

## Social Nature

Durkheim's most radical contribution was to reconceptualize suicide not as an isolated individual act, but as a **[[Social Fact]]** (*fait social*). (P02-025)

When all suicides committed in a given society over a fixed time period are considered collectively, the total constitutes a new phenomenon *sui generis* — one possessing its own unity, individuality, and, crucially, a social nature.

Empirically, this is demonstrated by the remarkable constancy of suicide rates. For any given society, as long as the observation period is not excessively long, the annual suicide count remains nearly invariable.

As Durkheim showed in *Tableau I*, the suicide figures for France, Prussia, England, Saxony, Bavaria, and Denmark remained strikingly stable through the 1840s–1860s, fluctuating significantly only during transient social crises. (P02-030)

This fixed rate forces the conclusion that each society has a **collective inclination toward suicide** — a [[Suicide Rate]] (*taux de suicide*) that is characteristic of that society just as its mortality rate or birth rate is.

The suicide rate is not the sum of individual decisions but a collective property of the social organism:

> Chaque société est prédisposée à fournir un contingent déterminé de morts volontaires. Cette prédisposition est ce que nous appelons le courant suicidogène. (P02-035)

## Typology

Durkheim identified four ideal types of suicide, organized along two orthogonal axes of social causation:

| Type | Axis | Mechanism | Key Characteristic |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **[[Egoistic Suicide]]** | Social Integration (low) | Excessive individuation | Detachment from collective life |
| **[[Altruistic Suicide]]** | Social Integration (high) | Insufficient individuation | Self-sacrifice for the group |
| **[[Anomic Suicide]]** | Social Regulation (low) | Normative breakdown | Disorientation from rapid change |
| **[[Fatalistic Suicide]]** | Social Regulation (high) | Excessive regulation | Oppressive discipline |

### Egoistic Suicide

Egoistic suicide results from **excessive individuation** — the weakening of the bonds that attach the individual to collective life. When the individual ceases to find meaning in their participation in social groups — religious community, family, political society — they become vulnerable to the slightest discouragement:

> Le suicide varie en raison inverse du degré d'intégration des groupes sociaux dont fait partie l'individu. (001-001)

The evidence for this type came from religious differentials: Protestant populations, with their emphasis on individual conscience and free inquiry, consistently showed higher suicide rates than Catholic populations.

In Catholic regions, collective ritual and ecclesiastical authority provided stronger social integration. Data from [[Alexander von Oettingen]]'s *Moralstatistik* and [[Enrico Morselli]]'s European tables provided the empirical basis for this argument.

### Altruistic Suicide

Altruistic suicide is the mirror image of egoistic suicide: it results from **insufficient individuation**, where the individual is so thoroughly absorbed into the collective that their own life has no independent value.

The individual dies because the group demands it — the soldier who throws himself on a grenade, the widow who follows her husband onto the funeral pyre, the elderly Inuit who walks into the snow.

Where egoistic suicide arises from societies that make too little of the individual, altruistic suicide arises from societies that make too much of the group.

### Anomic Suicide

Anomic suicide results from a **rupture of normative regulation** — a state of *[[Anomie]]* in which the individual's desires, no longer constrained by collective moral authority, outrun any possibility of satisfaction. Economic crises (both busts and booms) produce sharp increases in suicide precisely because they disturb the normative equilibrium that keeps aspirations in check.

### Fatalistic Suicide

Fatalistic suicide — the least developed type in Durkheim's analysis — results from **excessive regulation**: a situation where social norms are so rigid and oppressive that the future is entirely closed. Durkheim mentions slaves, childless married women, and very young husbands as historical examples. The concept remains theoretically fertile but empirically underdeveloped in Durkheim's own work.

## The Suicidogenic Current

Each society, Durkheim argued, generates a **[[Suicidogenic Current]]** (*courant suicidogène*) — a collective inclination toward suicide that varies in intensity according to the society's structural characteristics. This current is:

- **Real**: a genuine social force, not a metaphor
- **Collective**: belongs to the society, not any individual
- **Measurable**: expressed in the annual suicide rate, constant absent structural change
- **Differentiable**: multiple currents can coexist and combine within a single society

The concept of the suicidogenic current challenges methodological individualism: suicide — the most private act — is in part caused by a force external to the individual.

## Empirical Foundations

Durkheim's argument was built on the triangulation of three principal data sources, each contributing a different type of evidence:

| Source | Data Type | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| [[Alexander von Oettingen]] | Aggregate statistical tables | Religious, occupational, educational differentials |
| [[Louis Brierre de Boismont]] | Clinical case files (4,595 cases) | Hourly/seasonal distribution, suicide note content |
| [[Enrico Morselli]] | Comparative European statistics | Cross-national trends, racial hypothesis testing |

The convergence of macro-statistical patterns (Oettingen, Morselli) with micro-clinical observations (Brierre) was a deliberate methodological strategy: each type of evidence addressed a different potential objection to the sociological explanation.
