# Louis Brierre de Boismont

## Overview

**Alexandre-Jacques-François Brierre de Boismont** (1797-1881), commonly cited as Brierre de Boismont or simply Brierre, was a French alienist (psychiatrist) whose monumental clinical study *Du suicide et de la folie-suicide* (On Suicide and Suicide-Insanity, 1856; 2nd edition, Paris, Germer Baillière, 1865) compiled and analyzed 4,595 suicide case files from the Paris morgue and police archives between 1834 and 1843. (001-010)

Brierre de Boismont's dataset — the largest clinical collection of suicide cases assembled in the nineteenth century — provided Durkheim with crucial empirical evidence on the hourly and seasonal distribution of suicide, the relationship between mental illness and voluntary death, and the content of suicide notes. Alongside [[Alexander von Oettingen]] and [[Enrico Morselli]], Brierre completed the triad of statistical sources on which Durkheim built his sociological theory of suicide.

## Du suicide et de la folie-suicide (1856)

Brierre de Boismont's principal work examined the relationship between mental alienation and suicide through detailed case analysis of nearly five thousand suicides committed in Paris. His approach was clinical and descriptive: he collected suicide notes, interviewed survivors, and classified cases by presumed motive and mental state.

| Dataset | Cases | Period | Key Finding |
|---------|-------|--------|-------------|
| Paris morgue archives | 4,595 total | 1834–1843 | Majority of suicides occurred during daylight hours |
| Timed cases (subset) | 3,518 | 1834–1843 | 2,094 day / 766 evening / 658 night |
| Suicide notes analyzed | 1,328 | Various | Summary of contents published in Du suicide |
| Paris occupational data | 1,993 | Various | Hourly distribution by profession |

The scale of Brierre's data collection was unprecedented. No other researcher in the nineteenth century had systematically examined so many individual suicide cases with clinical attention to circumstance, timing, and psychological state.

## Temporal Distribution of Suicide

Brierre de Boismont's most important empirical contribution to Durkheim's argument was his demonstration that suicide was predominantly a daytime phenomenon:

> Brierre de Boismont a pu dépouiller les dossiers de 4.595 suicides accomplis à Paris de 1834 à 1843. Sur 3.518 cas dont le moment a pu être déterminé, 2.094 avaient été commis le jour, 766 le soir et 658 la nuit. Les suicides du jour et du soir représentent donc les quatre cinquièmes de la somme totale et les premiers, à eux seuls, en sont déjà les trois cinquièmes. (001-020)

This finding was decisive for Durkheim's sociological interpretation: suicide peaks during the hours of maximum social activity — when collective life is at its most intense — and declines at night, when social interactions cease and the individual is left alone. This temporal pattern could not be explained by any purely psychological or organic theory:

> Voici d'après 1.993 cas observés par Brierre de Boismont à Paris et 548 cas, relatifs à l'ensemble de la France et réunis par Guerry, quelles seraient les principales oscillations du suicide dans les 24 heures. (001-025)

The hourly curve of suicide — rising through the morning, peaking at midday, declining through the afternoon and evening — traced the rhythm of collective life itself. Suicide was not a phenomenon of solitary darkness but of active social participation.

## Suicide Notes and Clinical Testimony

Brierre de Boismont was among the first researchers to systematically collect and analyze suicide notes (*letters d'adieu*). His analysis of 1,328 cases where the deceased left written records provided a rare window into the subjective experience of the suicidal individual:

> Seul, Brierre de Boismont a essayé de faire ce travail descriptif pour 1328 cas où le suicidé avait laissé des lettres ou des écrits que l'auteur a résumés dans son livre. (001-030)

Durkheim acknowledged the value of this work while noting its limitations: the summaries were too brief, and the self-reporting of suicidal individuals — often in states of extreme distress — could not be treated as reliable psychological data. Nevertheless, Brierre's documentation of the diversity of motives expressed in suicide notes served Durkheim's argument that suicide could not be reduced to a single psychiatric cause.

Brierre also recorded clinical confessions that revealed the obsessive, intrusive nature of suicidal ideation:

> Un malade vient faire à Brierre de Boismont la confession suivante: «Jusqu'à 53 ans, je me suis bien porté; je n'avais aucun chagrin, mon caractère était assez gai lorsque, il y a trois ans, j'ai commencé à avoir des idées noires… Depuis trois mois, elles ne me laissent plus de repos et, à chaque instant, je suis poussé à me donner la mort.» (001-035)

This case — a person with no prior history of mental distress, no external misfortune, suddenly seized by suicidal impulses — illustrated both the clinical reality Brierre documented and the explanatory gap that Durkheim's sociological approach sought to fill: if neither madness nor personal misfortune explained suicide, what did?

## Prison Suicide and Criminal Populations

Brierre de Boismont contributed observations on an important limiting case: the low suicide rate among hardened criminals and professional offenders. While prisons in general showed elevated suicide rates, the *bagnes* (penal colonies) housing the most serious offenders recorded remarkably few suicides:

> Brierre de Boismont confirme ce dernier fait et il ajoute: «Les assassins de profession, les grands coupables ont plus rarement recours à ce moyen violent pour se soustraire à l'expiation pénale que les détenus d'une perversité moins profonde». (001-045)

This observation aligned with Durkheim's theory: the most serious criminals, living in highly structured penal communities with strong internal hierarchies, experienced a form of intense social integration that — perversely — protected them from suicide. The counterintuitive finding that "professional murderers" rarely killed themselves reinforced the argument that suicide was not a simple function of moral depravity but of social attachment.

## Methodological Significance

Brierre de Boismont occupied a distinctive methodological position among Durkheim's statistical sources. Unlike Oettingen (who provided aggregate statistical tables) or Morselli (who combined statistics with racial theory), Brierre supplied **clinical case data** — individual-level observations that complemented the macro-level patterns revealed by population statistics.

This triangulation was essential to Durkheim's method: population-level regularities (Oettingen's tables) established the *social* character of suicide; individual-level clinical data (Brierre's cases) demonstrated that psychological or organic explanations could not account for those regularities. The convergence of macro-statistical and micro-clinical evidence was a hallmark of Durkheim's sociological reasoning.

## Legacy

Brierre de Boismont's *Du suicide et de la folie-suicide* represented the culmination of the French clinical tradition in suicide research — a tradition that, by its very commitment to exhaustive description, generated empirical anomalies that only a sociological framework could resolve. His 4,595 cases from Paris constituted an irreplaceable evidentiary base for the claim that suicide was a collective, not merely individual, phenomenon.
