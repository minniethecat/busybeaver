# War and Suicide

## Overview

One of the most striking empirical regularities documented in *Le Suicide* (1897) is the sharp decline in suicide rates during wars and major political upheavals. Émile Durkheim presents data from multiple European conflicts — the Austro-Italian War of 1866, the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71, the Danish-Prussian War of 1864, and the revolutions of 1848 — each showing the same pattern: **suicide drops when collective existence is intensified by external threat**. The effect is visible among both men and women, among victors and vanquished, and persists for months after hostilities end. (doc_final.md:7388-7476)

This phenomenon provides a crucial empirical pillar for Durkheim's theory of [[egoistic-suicide]]: if insufficient social integration increases suicide, then a sudden intensification of collective sentiment — such as that produced by national crisis — should temporarily suppress the suicide rate. The war data confirm this prediction dramatically.

## The Austro-Italian War of 1866

When war broke out between Austria and Italy in 1866, suicide rates fell by 14% in both countries:

| Country | 1865 | 1866 | 1867 |
|---------|------|------|------|
| Italy | 678 | 588 | 657 |
| Austria | 1,464 | 1,265 | 1,407 |

The decline is symmetrical — affecting both eventual victor and vanquished — demonstrating that the mechanism is not military success or material well-being but the intensification of collective sentiment itself. (doc_final.md:7393-7416)

## The Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71

The larger conflict between France and the German states produced an even more pronounced effect:

| Country | 1869 | 1870 | 1871 | 1872 |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| Prussia | 3,186 | 2,963 | 2,723 | 2,950 |
| Saxony | 710 | 657 | 653 | 687 |
| France | 5,114 | 4,157 | 4,490 | 5,275 |

> La guerre de 1870-71 eut les mêmes conséquences en France et en Allemagne. (doc_final.md:7416-7433)

Crucially, the decline cannot be explained by young men being conscripted and their suicides going unrecorded on the battlefield. Durkheim notes that women — excluded from military service — show the same proportional decline: female suicides in Italy fell from 130 (1864) to 117 (1866); in Saxony from 133 (1863) to 120 (1864) to 114 (1865), a drop of 15%. (doc_final.md:7433-7445)

## Small Wars and the "Professional" Distinction

An important boundary condition strengthens Durkheim's sociological interpretation. While large national wars reliably suppress suicide, **small professional wars** — those fought by mercenary or career armies without broad popular mobilization — produce no such effect. The Prussian wars of 1864 and 1866, fought by professional armies against Denmark and Austria respectively, showed no suicide decline, unlike the fully national mobilization against France in 1870. (doc_final.md:7476-7496)

> C'est que ces guerres étaient dues tout entières à l'initiative des gouvernements... et ne remuèrent pas profondément la masse de la population comme celle de 1870. (doc_final.md:7482-7496)

This differential effect demonstrates that the protective mechanism is not noise or administrative artifact but a genuine sociological phenomenon: only when collective sentiments are broadly aroused does the suicide rate respond.

## Mechanism: Collective Sentiment and Social Integration

The war effect is a natural experiment in social integration. Under external threat:

1. **Collective sentiments are intensified** — patriotic feeling, mutual obligation, shared purpose
2. **Individual preoccupations recede** — private sorrows and personal calculations lose their salience
3. **Social density increases** — mobilization, rationing, shared sacrifice, common conversation

Together, these temporarily pull the individual out of the egoistic isolation that Durkheim identifies as the fundamental cause of modern suicide.
