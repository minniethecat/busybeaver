# Contagion (Suicide)

## Overview

The hypothesis that suicide spreads by **contagion** — that one
suicide provokes others through a kind of moral infection — was widely
accepted in Durkheim's intellectual milieu. From dramatic newspaper
accounts of suicide epidemics to the psychiatric concept of
*contagion mentale*, the idea that suicide is transmitted from person
to person through imitation enjoyed substantial scientific and popular
authority. In *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim does not dismiss contagion
outright but subjects it to a rigorous examination, ultimately
concluding that its explanatory power is limited to isolated,
sporadic phenomena and cannot account for the stable, society-level
patterns that constitute the proper object of sociological analysis.
(doc_final.md:3994, 3996-4009, 2630-2639)

## The Contagion Hypothesis in Nineteenth-Century Thought

### Psychiatric and Popular Sources

The contagion hypothesis drew support from two distinct sources.
First, **alienists** (early psychiatrists) had documented cases where
exposure to a suicide appeared to trigger imitative acts, particularly
in institutional settings — hospitals, prisons, barracks. Second, the
**popular press** regularly reported on *épidémies de suicide*
(suicide epidemics), creating a cultural narrative in which suicide
was understood as a communicable phenomenon. (doc_final.md:2633, 4093, 4105)

The most famous case was the **"suicide corridor"** at a Parisian
barracks, where fifteen soldiers hanged themselves in succession from
the same hook — a case that seemed to demonstrate contagion in its
purest form:

> Nous avons déjà parlé de ce couloir où quinze invalides se pendirent
> successivement. (doc_final.md:4093)

### Tarde's Imitation Theory

Behind the contagion concept lay Gabriel Tarde's broader theory of
**imitation** as the fundamental mechanism of social life. For Tarde,
society itself was constituted by the imitative propagation of
beliefs, desires, and actions from individuals to masses. Suicide was
merely one instance of this general law. Durkheim's critique of
contagion is thus simultaneously a critique of Tarde's sociological
programme — an argument that **social facts** cannot be reduced to
interpersonal imitation. (doc_final.md:3994, 4059, 4021)

## Durkheim's Critique

### Definitional Rigour: What Contagion Is and Is Not

Durkheim begins by insisting on precise definitions. A phenomenon
qualifies as **contagion** only when three conditions are met:

1. The idea of the act is **caused by** observation of another's act
2. This idea **directly produces** a similar act
3. The act would **not have occurred** without the prior observation

(doc_final.md:3996-4009)

This definitional rigour excludes many phenomena commonly called
contagious. When members of the same social group share a common
disposition toward suicide (a *penchant collectif*), their acts may
appear contagious but are actually independent expressions of a shared
social cause. True contagion requires **direct interpersonal
transmission** of the suicidal impulse. (doc_final.md:4001-4015)

> …qu'y a-t-il de contagieux dans le fait d'accomplir un précepte de
> la religion à laquelle on appartient? (doc_final.md:3996-4001)

### The Empirical Problem: Scale

The most decisive argument against contagion as a major cause of
suicide is one of **scale**. The cases of genuine contagion that
Durkheim acknowledges — the barracks corridor, the fifteen invalids,
the forty Jews in the underground refuge — involve small numbers of
individuals in closed, tightly bounded settings. These episodes do not
generate sustained, society-wide effects:

> …plutôt que d'une simple propagation contagieuse. L'idée ne naît pas
> chez les individus par une sorte de génération spontanée…
> (doc_final.md:4124)

If contagion were a significant cause of suicide, we should observe
**epidemic patterns** in the statistical data — sharp spikes followed
by rapid declines, clustering in space and time. What we actually
observe is the opposite: suicide rates are remarkably **stable** from
year to year, varying smoothly and predictably with large-scale social
conditions (economic cycles, wars, political crises). (doc_final.md:2630-2639)

### Where Contagion Should Be Most Visible

Durkheim turns the contagion hypothesis against itself by asking:
where should contagion be most intense if it really operates? The
obvious answer is **cities** — the places of greatest population
density, most frequent interpersonal contact, and most rapid
circulation of news:

> C'est dans les capitales et les grandes villes que les phénomènes de
> contagion doivent être le plus fréquents… (doc_final.md:4221)

If contagion drove suicide rates, cities should show **episodic
spikes** corresponding to widely publicised suicides. But Durkheim
finds that urban suicide rates, while higher than rural ones, are
smooth and stable — not episodic. The urban-rural differential is
attributable to the **intensity of collective life** (social density),
not to contagion. (doc_final.md:4225, 4249)

### The Obliteration of the Imitative Suicide

In a famous passage, Durkheim argues that imitative suicides, even
if they occur, are **obliterated** (*effacés*) at the scale of
sociological analysis:

> …les suicides par imitation sont trop peu nombreux et trop
> sporadiques pour affecter sensiblement le taux social des suicides.
> (doc_final.md:2630-2639)

The word "obliterated" captures the statistical logic: individual
contagion events are **noise** relative to the **signal** of social
causation. They do not reproduce themselves; they do not accumulate;
they leave no trace in the aggregate data. What the sociologist
studies — the stable, society-level suicide rate — is determined by
social-structural forces that operate independently of interpersonal
contagion.

## Recognised Cases: The Limits of Contagion

Durkheim does not deny that contagion occurs. He acknowledges three
settings where it has been documented:

### Institutional Contagion

Closed institutions — prisons, barracks, hospitals — can incubate
genuine contagion chains, as the barracks corridor case illustrates.
But these are **ecologically bounded**: the contagion does not escape
the institution because the conditions that sustain it (constant
proximity, shared milieu, isolation from wider society) do not
generalise. (doc_final.md:4093, 4105)

### Historical Contagion: The Jews at Masada

Durkheim cites Josephus's account of the forty Jews who, during the
siege of Jerusalem, decided collectively to kill themselves rather
than be captured. This is a case of contagion in a **limit situation**
— extreme external threat producing collective resolution — not a
model for ordinary social life. (doc_final.md:4112-4115)

### Localised Epidemics

Durkheim mentions contemporary reports of suicide epidemics linked to
specific locations or events — the crochet (hook) epidemic, a tree
used for hanging, a hotel — where the removal of the "contaminated"
object terminated the epidemic. These episodes are real but trivial in
magnitude relative to the total suicide rate:

> …le crochet enlevé, l'épidémie prit fin. (doc_final.md:2636-2639)

## The Broader Argument: Against Imitation as Social Causation

Durkheim's critique of contagion is a **case study in sociological
method**. It demonstrates the difference between:

- **Interpersonal causation** (contagion, imitation): events
  transmitted from individual to individual through direct contact
- **Social-structural causation**: forces that operate at the level
  of the collective, imposing themselves on individuals regardless of
  their personal contacts

The contagion debate is thus not merely about suicide — it is about
**what kind of thing society is**. For Tarde, society is an aggregate
of individual imitations; for Durkheim, society is a reality *sui
generis* whose properties cannot be derived from interpersonal
processes. The failure of contagion to explain suicide rates is, for
Durkheim, a demonstration that Tarde's sociology is fundamentally
inadequate. (doc_final.md:3994, 4059-4093, 4249)
