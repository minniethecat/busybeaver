# Jean-Étienne Esquirol

## Overview

**Jean-Étienne Dominique Esquirol** (1772–1840) was a French psychiatrist and a leading figure of the early nineteenth-century alienist tradition. A student of Philippe Pinel, Esquirol advanced the thesis that suicide was invariably the result of mental alienation (*aliénation mentale*), stating that "suicide offers all the characteristics of mental alienation" and that "man attempts his life only when he is in delirium, and suicides are alienated." This position made Esquirol the central intellectual opponent against whom Durkheim constructed his sociological theory of suicide in *Le Suicide* (1897).

Esquirol's major work, *Des maladies mentales considérées sous les rapports médical, hygiénique et médico-légal* (1838), devoted substantial attention to suicide as a symptom of mental pathology. His influence extended beyond medicine into jurisprudence: arguing that suicide was involuntary, Esquirol concluded it should not be punished by law — a progressive legal position that nevertheless rested on a premise Durkheim would systematically dismantle.

## The Psychiatric Thesis and Durkheim's Refutation

Esquirol's core claim was that suicide was always a manifestation of mental illness, specifically of monomania — a partial delirium focused on a single object or idea. According to this view, the suicidal individual was not morally responsible for their act; they were sick, and their sickness explained the act entirely.

Durkheim's refutation proceeded on multiple fronts. First, he demonstrated statistically that the geographic distribution of suicide did not correlate with that of mental illness — countries and regions with high rates of insanity did not necessarily have high suicide rates. Second, he showed that suicide rates varied systematically with social factors (religion, marital status, economic conditions) that could not be reduced to individual psychopathology. Third, he argued that if suicide were purely a mental illness, it would not exhibit the stable, predictable patterns across time and place that Wagner, [[Adolphe Quetelet]], and other statisticians had documented.

Esquirol shared with [[Jean-Pierre Falret]] the view that celebrated historical suicides — such as Cato's death — were not truly suicides, because they were acts of honor rather than pathology. Durkheim saw in this exception an implicit admission that social meaning, not individual sickness, determined the classification of self-destructive acts.

## Legacy

Esquirol's importance to the sociology of suicide is paradoxical: he provided the most systematic formulation of the psychiatric theory that Durkheim needed to refute in order to establish sociology's claim to the phenomenon. His clinical observations — including cases of hereditary suicide and family suicide clusters — supplied data that Durkheim reinterpreted through a sociological lens. The very thoroughness of Esquirol's psychiatric argument made it the ideal foil for Durkheim's demonstration that suicide was, fundamentally, a social fact.
