# Mixed Suicide Types

## Overview

A central methodological insight of Émile Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897) is that the pure types of suicide — [[egoistic-suicide|egoistic]], [[altruistic-suicide|altruistic]], and [[anomic-suicide|anomic]] — are **analytical constructs** rather than empirical descriptions. In actual cases, these types rarely appear in isolation. The different social causes of suicide can act simultaneously on the same individual, mixing their effects and producing compound or mixed forms (*formes mixtes*):

> Il arrive très souvent qu'elles se combinent entre elles de manière à donner naissance à des espèces composées; des caractères appartenant à plusieurs d'entre elles se retrouvent conjointement dans un même suicide. La raison en est que les différentes causes sociales du suicide peuvent elles-mêmes agir simultanément sur un même individu et mêler en lui leurs effets. (doc_final.md:11042-11048)

This concept of mixed types is not a secondary refinement but a **logical necessity** of Durkheim's sociological method. If suicide is caused by social currents, and multiple currents can traverse the same society — and indeed the same individual — then the resulting suicides will inevitably bear the marks of more than one current. The classification of mixed types thus serves both a theoretical purpose (preserving the purity of the analytical categories) and an empirical one (accounting for the complexity of actual cases).

## The Three Pure Types and Their Affinities

Before analyzing the mixtures, Durkheim establishes the psychological profile of each pure type (doc_final.md:11323-11355):

| Pure Type | Fundamental Character | Secondary Varieties |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Egoistic** | Apathy | Languid melancholy with self-complacency; Disillusioned sang-froid of the sceptic |
| **Altruistic** | Passionate or voluntary energy | Calm sense of duty; Mystical enthusiasm; Peaceful courage |
| **Anomic** | Irritation, disgust | Violent recriminations against life in general; Violent recriminations against a particular person (homicide-suicide) |

These three fundamental characters — apathy, energy, and irritation — are the primary colors from which the mixed types are composed. The mixtures arise because the underlying social currents have structural affinities for one another.

## Ego-Anomic Suicide

The most important mixed type, and the one Durkheim analyzes in greatest detail, is the **ego-anomic** combination. Egoism and anomie have what Durkheim calls "une affinité spéciale" — a special affinity — because they are generally two different aspects of the same social state. It is almost inevitable that the egoist should have some aptitude for deregulation, since his detachment from society means society lacks the grip on him necessary to regulate his desires. (doc_final.md:11063-11069)

The dynamics of this mixture are subtle. The egoist, turned inward upon himself, normally has languishing passions — the external world does not attract him. But he may seek new sensations to fill the void he feels within:

> Pour combler le vide qu'il sent en lui, il recherche des sensations nouvelles; il y met, il est vrai, moins de fougue que le passionné proprement dit, mais aussi il se lasse plus vite et cette lassitude le rejette à nouveau sur lui-même et renforce sa mélancolie première. (doc_final.md:11066-11071)

The result is a characteristic **oscillation**: the individual alternates between the restless pursuit of new experiences (anomie) and the melancholic retreat into the self (egoism). Each phase reinforces the other — satiety drives him back inward, and the emptiness he finds there drives him outward again:

> Ainsi se produisent des suicides mixtes où l'abattement alterne avec l'agitation, le rêve avec l'action, les emportements du désir avec les méditations du mélancolique. (doc_final.md:11080-11082)

Conversely, anomie itself contains a germ of egoism, for one could not be rebellious against all social restraint unless one were already incompletely socialized. When anomie is intense, this germ cannot develop — the agitation throws the individual outside himself, preventing self-isolation. But when anomie is less intense, it may allow egoism to produce some of its effects: the obstacle against which the insatiable desire collides can drive the individual to retreat inward, seeking in interior life a diversion from disappointed passions. Finding nothing there to which he can attach himself, the sadness of this spectacle can only drive him to flee himself once more, increasing his restlessness and discontent. (doc_final.md:11072-11080)

## Anomic-Altruistic Suicide

Anomie can also combine with [[altruistic-suicide|altruism]]. A single crisis can simultaneously disrupt the equilibrium between an individual and his environment (producing anomie) and activate altruistic dispositions that incline him toward self-destruction (producing altruism). Durkheim's primary example is the **obsidional suicide** — the mass suicides that occur when a community is under siege:

> Si les Juifs, par exemple, se tuèrent en masse au moment de la prise de Jérusalem, c'est à la fois parce que la victoire des Romains, en faisant d'eux des sujets et des tributaires de Rome, menaçaient de transformer le genre de vie auquel ils étaient faits, et parce qu'ils aimaient trop leur ville et leur culte pour survivre à l'anéantissement probable de l'un et de l'autre. (doc_final.md:11087-11093)

Similarly, a ruined man may kill himself both because he does not wish to live with diminished circumstances (anomie) and to spare his name and family the shame of bankruptcy (altruism). Military officers who commit suicide upon forced retirement are caught between the sudden disruption of their way of life and their general predisposition to count their lives as nothing in service of a higher cause. The two causes act in the same direction, producing suicides in which "l'exaltation passionnelle soit la fermeté courageuse du suicide altruiste s'allient à l'affolement exaspéré que produit l'anomie." (doc_final.md:11095-11104)

## Ego-Altruistic Suicide: The Stoic Case

The most paradoxical mixture is that of egoism and altruism — two contrary tendencies that would seem to cancel each other out. Durkheim's analysis of how they combine is one of the most philosophically sophisticated passages in *Le Suicide*.

At certain historical moments, when a disintegrated society can no longer serve as an objective for individual activities, there nevertheless exist individuals who, while suffering the influence of this general state of egoism, still aspire to something beyond themselves. Sensing that it is a poor means of escaping oneself to go endlessly from egoistic pleasure to egoistic pleasure, they seek a durable object to which they can attach themselves with constancy and which gives meaning to their life. But since there is nothing real to which they are attached, they can only satisfy this need by constructing an ideal reality — an imaginary being whose servants they become, to which they devote themselves all the more exclusively because they are detached from everything else, including themselves. (doc_final.md:11110-11125)

> Ils vivent ainsi d'une existence double et contradictoire: individualistes pour tout ce qui regarde le monde réel, ils sont d'un altruisme immodéré pour tout ce qui concerne cet objet idéal. Or l'une et l'autre disposition mènent au suicide. (doc_final.md:11126-11128)

The archetype of this mixture is the **Stoic suicide**. The Stoic professes absolute indifference to everything beyond the enclosure of individual personality — radical moral [[individualism-and-suicide|individualism]]. Yet at the same time, he places himself in a state of strict dependence on universal reason, reducing himself to being merely the instrument through which reason realizes itself. The Stoic thus "combine donc ces deux conceptions antagonistes: l'individualisme moral le plus radical et un panthéisme intempérant." (doc_final.md:11134-11137)

The Stoic suicide is consequently:
- **Apathetic** like the egoist's — a calm detachment from the world
- **Dutiful** like the altruist's — accomplished as an obligation
- Marked by both the **melancholy** of egoism and the **active energy** of altruism

Durkheim uses this to distinguish two forms of mysticism. The mysticism of young, formative peoples results from collective élan sweeping individual wills in a common direction, from the self-abnegation of citizens collaborating in a common work. The mysticism of decadent epochs — of which the Stoic is an instance — is only "un égoïsme conscient de soi-même et de son néant, qui s'efforce de se dépasser, mais n'y parvient qu'en apparence et artificiellement." (doc_final.md:11142-11148)

## The Classification Table

Durkheim formalizes his complete typology in a synoptic table (doc_final.md:11323-11355):

```
Elementary Types:
├── Egoistic suicide (Apathy)
│   ├── Languid melancholy with self-complacency
│   └── Disillusioned sang-froid of the sceptic
├── Altruistic suicide (Passionate/voluntary energy)
│   ├── With calm sense of duty
│   ├── With mystical enthusiasm
│   └── With peaceful courage
└── Anomic suicide (Irritation, disgust)
    ├── Violent recriminations against life in general
    └── Violent recriminations against a particular person

Mixed Types:
├── Ego-anomic suicide → Mixture of agitation and apathy, action and reverie
├── Anomic-altruistic suicide → Exasperated effervescence
└── Ego-altruistic suicide → Melancholy tempered by moral firmness
```

## The Fourth Type: Fatalistic Suicide

Durkheim briefly acknowledges a fourth pure type — **fatalistic suicide** — arising from excessive regulation rather than insufficient regulation. This is the suicide of persons whose future is pitilessly walled in, whose passions are violently compressed by oppressive discipline: the too-young husband, the married woman without children. However, Durkheim considers it of such minor contemporary importance that he declines to give it a full treatment, noting only that it stands opposed to anomic suicide as egoistic and altruistic suicide stand opposed to each other. (doc_final.md:17121-17128)

This acknowledgment is significant because it suggests that mixed types involving fatalism are also theoretically possible — though Durkheim does not analyze them, given the type's marginal empirical importance in modern societies.

## Theoretical Significance

The analysis of mixed types serves several crucial functions in Durkheim's argument:

1. **Preserving the purity of the ideal types.** By acknowledging that empirical cases are almost always mixtures, Durkheim protects his analytical categories from falsification by counterexample. A suicide that does not perfectly match the egoistic profile is not a refutation — it is likely an ego-anomic or ego-altruistic mixture.

2. **Explaining psychological complexity.** The mixed types bridge the gap between macroscopic social causes and microscopic individual experience. The alternation of agitation and apathy, dream and action, that characterizes the ego-anomic suicide is a recognizable psychological pattern that resists reduction to a single cause.

3. **Demonstrating the causal independence of the three currents.** The fact that the currents can combine in all logically possible permutations — egoism+anomie, anomie+altruism, egoism+altruism — demonstrates that they are genuinely distinct causal forces, not different names for the same thing.

4. **Historicizing the typology.** The ego-altruistic mixture is specifically characteristic of decadent epochs — periods when social disintegration has progressed far enough to produce widespread egoism, but the human need for attachment to something beyond the self persists. The Stoic suicide of antiquity and the mystical suicide of fin-de-siècle Europe are structurally homologous.
