# Melancholy and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim engages extensively with melancholy (*mélancolie*) as both a clinical category inherited from 19th-century psychiatry and a sociological phenomenon that reveals the emotional structure of [[egoistic-suicide]]. Nineteenth-century alienists, following Esquirol, classified *suicide mélancolique* as one of the major psychiatric forms of self-destruction — a suicide arising from a generalized state of extreme depression, sadness, and fixed despair. Durkheim does not reject this clinical description; rather, he reinterprets it sociologically, demonstrating that the melancholy characteristic of egoistic suicide is not an individual pathology but the psychological expression of a specific form of defective social integration. (doc_final.md:1064-1089)

The concept of melancholy thus occupies a pivotal position in Durkheim's argument: it is the bridge between the psychiatric classification he critiques in Book I and the sociological typology he constructs in Book II. By showing that melancholic detachment is the individual manifestation of excessive [[individuation]] — the weakening of the ties binding the individual to collective life — Durkheim transforms melancholy from a clinical symptom into a social fact.

## Melancholy in 19th-Century Psychiatric Classification

Before Durkheim, the dominant framework for understanding suicide was psychiatric. Esquirol and his successors classified suicides according to the mental state presumed to precede them. In this nosology, melancholic suicide (*suicide mélancolique*) was defined by its link to a generalized condition of extreme depression and exaggerated sadness:

> Le suicide mélancolique est lié à un état général d'extrême dépression, de tristesse exagérée qui fait que le malade n'apprécie plus sainement les rapports qu'ont avec lui les personnes et les choses qui l'entourent. Les plaisirs n'ont pour lui aucun attrait; il voit tout en noir. (doc_final.md:1064-1069)

Durkheim presents the clinical case of a young woman who, after leaving her childhood countryside for the city, conceives an "inexprimable ennui" and an unshakeable desire to die. She remains motionless for hours, eyes fixed on the ground, seeking the most isolated places to drown herself — a picture of melancholic fixation that returns with renewed force even after temporary remission. (doc_final.md:1072-1082)

Crucially, Durkheim distinguishes melancholic suicide from the manic and obsessive varieties. Unlike the manic suicide, where delusional ideas are mobile and transient, the melancholic's hallucinations and self-reproaches are **fixed** and **chronic** — constant as the underlying depressive state from which they derive. The melancholic patient prepares means of execution with calm, persistence, and sometimes astonishing cunning, utterly unlike the perpetual instability of the manic. (doc_final.md:1084-1098)

## The Sociological Reinterpretation

Durkheim's decisive move is to reinterpret the clinical picture of melancholy as the **individual expression of egoistic suicide**. The calm, detached, intellectual quality of the melancholic's final act — the patient who chooses his hour, meditates his plan far in advance, and even finds a "mélancolie calme et qui, parfois, n'est pas sans douceur" in his last moments — is not a psychiatric curiosity but the logical consequence of a specific form of social disintegration. (doc_final.md:10787-10792)

Durkheim provides vivid examples: a merchant who withdraws into a forest and lets himself die of hunger over three weeks, keeping a journal of his impressions; another who asphyxiates himself while recording his sensations "au fur et à mesure." These are not acts of passion but of **meditative detachment**:

> Cette paresse à l'action, ce détachement mélancolique résultent de cet état d'individuation exagérée par lequel nous avons défini ce type de suicide. Si l'individu s'isole, c'est que les liens qui l'unissaient aux autres êtres sont détendus ou brisés, c'est que la société, sur les points où il est en contact avec elle, n'est pas assez fortement intégrée. (doc_final.md:10805-10809)

The melancholy of the egoistic suicide is thus not a primary cause but a **symptom** — the affective register of [[social-integration|social disintegration]]. The voids that separate individual consciousnesses, rendering them foreign to one another, are the product of a relaxed social fabric. The intellectual and meditative character of these suicides follows directly: in a society where reflective consciousness is compelled to extend its domain, it is also more exposed to exceeding the normal limits beyond which it destroys itself. (doc_final.md:10810-10820)

## Melancholy vs. Anomic Agitation: Two Forms of the "Mal de l'Infini"

Durkheim draws a crucial distinction between the melancholy of the egoistic suicide and the restless agitation of the [[anomic-suicide|anomic]]. Both types suffer from what has been called the *mal de l'infini*, but the sickness takes radically different forms:

- In **egoistic melancholy**, it is reflective intelligence that is afflicted and hypertrophied beyond measure — thought, by folding back upon itself, loses all object.
- In **anomic agitation**, it is sensibility that is overexcited and deregulated — passion, recognizing no limits, loses all goal.

> Le premier se perd dans l'infini du rêve, le second, dans l'infini du désir. (doc_final.md:11025-11030)

The literary archetypes Durkheim marshals are revealing: **Raphaël** (the meditative egoist absorbed in himself) versus **René** (Chateaubriand's insatiable, restless protagonist). René cries out: "On m'accuse d'avoir des goûts inconstants, de ne pouvoir jouir longtemps de la même chimère… Est-ce ma faute si je trouve partout les bornes, si ce qui est fini n'a pour moi aucune valeur?" This is not melancholy but **lassitude born of satiety** — the exhaustion that follows from chasing desire without limit. (doc_final.md:11011-11022)

The distinction matters because it reveals the **different social causes** operating through apparently similar psychological states. The melancholy egoist suffers from a deficit of integration; the agitated anomic suffers from a deficit of regulation.

## Mixed Types: Melancholy Combined

One of Durkheim's most sophisticated insights is that pure types rarely appear in reality. Egoism and anomie — the two great suicidal currents — have a special affinity for each other, and their combination produces characteristic mixed forms in which melancholy alternates with agitation:

> Ainsi se produisent des suicides mixtes où l'abattement alterne avec l'agitation, le rêve avec l'action, les emportements du désir avec les méditations du mélancolique. (doc_final.md:11080-11082)

Durkheim tabulates these combinations in his formal classification (doc_final.md:11323-11355):

| Type | Secondary Varieties |
|------|-------------------|
| Egoistic suicide (apathy) | Languid melancholy with self-complacency; Disillusioned sang-froid of the sceptic |
| Ego-anomic (mixed) | Mixture of agitation and apathy, action and reverie |
| Ego-altruistic (mixed) | Melancholy tempered by a certain moral firmness |

The ego-altruistic mixture is particularly instructive. Durkheim notes that certain philosophical suicides — notably the Stoic — combine the apathetic self-absorption of the egoist with the sense of duty characteristic of the altruist. The result is a suicide that is "at once apathetic like that of the egoist and accomplished as a duty like that of the altruist," in which "one finds both the melancholy of the one and the active energy of the other; egoism is mingled with mysticism." (doc_final.md:11134-11140)

## The Normal and the Pathological

Durkheim closes his analysis of melancholy with a characteristically nuanced reflection. Melancholy is not, in itself, pathological. A certain capacity for sadness is necessary for both individual and collective health:

> C'est, en effet, une erreur de croire que la joie sans mélange soit l'état normal de la sensibilité. L'homme ne pourrait pas vivre s'il était entièrement réfractaire à la tristesse. Il y a bien des douleurs auxquelles on ne peut s'adapter qu'en les aimant, et le plaisir qu'on y trouve a nécessairement quelque chose de mélancolique. (doc_final.md:14162-14166)

Melancholy becomes **morbid** only when it occupies too large a place in life — but it is equally morbid for it to be entirely absent. A morality too smiling is a lax morality, suited only to peoples in decadence. Every society requires a pessimistic current to contain the optimistic one. (doc_final.md:14166-14182)

Durkheim goes further, noting that the great religions of the most civilized peoples are more deeply impregnated with sadness than the simpler beliefs of earlier societies. The pessimistic current does not lose ground; it appears destined to persist and even develop as social types ascend the evolutionary scale. (doc_final.md:14182-14190)

This normal melancholy, however, becomes **pathological** when the social organism is disturbed. Durkheim diagnoses his own era as suffering from an abnormal intensification of collective melancholy — visible not only in the official pessimism of Schopenhauer and Hartmann but in the shared "haine ou de dégoût pour ce qui est" that unites the anarchist, the aesthete, the mystic, and the revolutionary socialist. When collective melancholy invades consciousness to this degree, it produces an epidemic of suicide. (doc_final.md:14335-14345)

## Theoretical Significance

The analysis of melancholy illuminates three core Durkheimian principles:

1. **The irreducibility of the social to the psychological.** What psychiatry calls "melancholy" is, at the sociological level, the affective signature of a specific defect in [[social-integration]]. The individual's sadness is real, but its cause is collective.

2. **The necessity of classificatory precision.** By distinguishing the melancholy of egoism from the agitation of anomie and the mixed forms that combine both, Durkheim demonstrates that "sadness" is not a unitary phenomenon. Different social causes produce different kinds of suffering, and conflating them obscures the causal structure.

3. **The normal-pathological distinction.** Melancholy has a legitimate social function — containing excessive optimism, registering the harsh and disappointing dimensions of existence. It becomes suicidogenic only when social disturbances amplify it beyond its normal intensity.
