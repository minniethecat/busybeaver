# Corporation (Professional Group)

## Overview

In the concluding chapters of *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim turns from
diagnosis to remedy, proposing the **corporation** (*groupe professionnel* or
*corporation*) as the institutional form uniquely capable of addressing the
pathological conditions — egoism and anomie — that generate the high suicide
rates of modern societies. The corporation is the occupational group that
brings together all workers engaged in the same function, providing them with a
**moral milieu** intermediate between the isolated individual and the distant
State. (doc_final.md:14670-14690)

> En dehors de la société confessionnelle, familiale, politique, il en est une
> autre... c'est celle que forment, par leur association, tous les
> travailleurs du même ordre, tous les coopérateurs de la même fonction, c'est
> le groupe professionnel ou la corporation. (doc_final.md:14676-14680)

## Why the Corporation?

Durkheim's argument for the corporation rests on its unique structural
advantages over other social groups. The religious society, the family, and the
political society all protect against egoistic suicide because they are
**integrated societies**. The corporation can play the same role — and in some
respects, a superior one:

| Group | Advantage | Limitation |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Religion | Shared beliefs and practices | Declining in modern secular societies |
| Family | Intense daily collective life | Confined to domestic sphere; shrinking |
| Political society | Broadest collective identity | Intermittent contact; distant from daily life |
| **Corporation** | Constant, ubiquitous, pervasive | Currently under-organised and atomised |

The corporation has three decisive advantages: it is **of all times** (its
action on the individual is continuous, not intermittent like the political
society), **of all places** (it follows workers wherever they go, unlike the
family), and its empire **extends to the greatest part of existence** — since
professional life is almost the whole of life. (doc_final.md:14695-14710)

## The Corporation as Moral Power

Durkheim envisions the corporation not merely as an economic association but as
a **moral power** — a collective personality capable of exercising authority
over its members:

> La corporation a témoigné dans le passé qu'elle était susceptible d'être une
> personnalité collective, jalouse, même à l'excès, de son autonomie et de son
> autorité sur ses membres; il n'est donc pas douteux qu'elle ne puisse être
> pour eux un milieu moral. (doc_final.md:14688-14693)

The corporation provides what the individual most needs in modern society: a
group close enough to feel its influence constantly, authoritative enough to
impose discipline, and meaningful enough to invest life with purpose beyond
the self. It answers to both the **integration deficit** (egoism) and the
**regulation deficit** (anomie) that Durkheim's empirical analysis has
identified as the twin pathologies of modern suicide.

## The Historical Precedent and Its Reform

Durkheim draws on the historical precedent of the medieval and early-modern
corporations while acknowledging their defects. The old corporation was
narrowly local, closed to external influence, and had become an obstacle to
progress. But the remedy was not abolition — it was **reform and expansion**:

> Si, au lieu de rester isolées, les corporations similaires des différentes
> localités avaient été reliées les unes aux autres de manière à former un
> même système, si tous ces systèmes avaient été soumis à l'action générale
> de l'État... le despotisme de la routine et l'égoïsme professionnel se
> seraient renfermés dans de justes limites. (doc_final.md:14809-14816)

The reformed corporation would be national in scope, interconnected across
localities, and subordinated to the general action of the State — a
decentralised but coordinated system of occupational governance capable of
regulating working conditions, administering welfare, and most importantly,
constituting a moral environment for its members. (doc_final.md:14750-14773)

## The Corporation and the Division of Labour

The need for the corporation grows with the **division of labour**. As
occupational functions become more specialised, the law and morality common to
all citizens become insufficient to regulate the specific ethical problems of
each profession. Each function requires its own discipline, and each discipline
requires its own specialised organ to apply and maintain it. The corporation is
that organ. (doc_final.md:14761-14773)

This links the argument of *Le Suicide* to Durkheim's earlier work, *De la
division du travail social* (1893), where the corporation was already
identified as the institutional form appropriate to organic solidarity. In
*Le Suicide*, the corporation gains an additional justification: it is the
remedy for the pathological forms of suicide that organic solidarity, in its
currently under-regulated form, generates.
