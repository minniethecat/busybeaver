Durkheim's analysis of **sex and suicide** serves as a pivotal argumentative lever in *Le Suicide* (1897). The observation that men die by suicide at approximately four times the rate of women — a pattern he documents across multiple European nations — provides Durkheim with decisive evidence against [[mental-alienation-and-suicide|psychopathological]] and [[heredity-and-suicide|biological]] explanations, while opening the way to a properly sociological account grounded in [[social-integration]] and marriage institutions.

## The Statistical Pattern

Durkheim presents Tableau IV of *Le Suicide*, documenting the sex distribution of suicide across European nations [lines 1392–1452]:

| Country (Period) | Male % | Female % | M:F Ratio |
|------------------|--------|----------|-----------|
| Austria (1873–77) | 82.1 | 17.9 | 4.6:1 |
| Prussia (1871–76) | 81.5 | 18.5 | 4.4:1 |
| Italy (1872–77) | 80.0 | 20.0 | 4.0:1 |
| Saxony (1871–76) | 80.7 | 19.3 | 4.2:1 |
| France (1871–76) | 78.7 | 21.3 | 3.7:1 |
| Denmark (1870–76) | 76.9 | 23.1 | 3.3:1 |
| England (1863–67) | 73.3 | 26.7 | 2.7:1 |

The consistency is striking: across different nations, legal systems, and religious contexts, suicide everywhere appears as a "predominantly masculine phenomenon" [line 1452].

## Refutation of the Neurasthenia Hypothesis

Durkheim deploys the sex differential to refute the claim that suicide is caused by [[mental-alienation-and-suicide|neurasthenia]] (nervous predisposition) [lines 1439–1452]:

> If there were a causal relationship between the suicide rate and neurasthenia, women should kill themselves more than men, or at least as much. ... Yet, far from their aptitude for voluntary death being superior or equivalent to that of men, suicide turns out to be an essentially masculine manifestation. For one woman who kills herself, there are on average four men who do so.

The logic is devastating: women show **higher** rates of diagnosed mental illness and nervous disorders, yet **lower** suicide rates. The psychopathological theory predicts the opposite of what the data show.

## Durkheim's Sociological Explanation

Having eliminated individual-level explanations, Durkheim proposes that sex differences in suicide stem from differential modes of **social existence** [lines 2696–2740]:

### Marriage and Family Integration

Durkheim argues that marriage and family life affect men and women differently. Men derive greater [[social-integration]] from the conjugal institution, while women's social integration — in the social conditions of late 19th-century Europe — is less dependent on marriage for its structure [lines 2696–2705].

### Divorce and Conjugal Anomie

The sex differential also interacts with [[divorce-and-suicide|divorce rates]]: in societies where divorce is accessible, the protective effect of marriage for men weakens ([[anomie|conjugal anomie]]), while women's suicide rates are less affected — a pattern Durkheim attributes to the differential social functions of marriage for each sex [lines 6202–6233].

### Education and Social Complexity

Durkheim observes that suicide rates correlate with educational attainment differently by sex, reflecting broader patterns of [[individuation]] and social complexity that affect men and women asymmetrically.

## Consistency Across the Typology

The sex differential holds across all four types of suicide:

| Suicide Type | Sex Pattern | Mechanism |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| [[egoistic-suicide]] | Higher in men | Men more individuated, less integrated into family |
| [[altruistic-suicide]] | Higher in men (historically) | Military/service contexts predominantly male |
| [[anomic-suicide]] | Higher in men | Men more exposed to economic and conjugal anomie |
| [[fatalistic-suicide]] | Context-dependent | Oppressive regulation may affect both sexes |

## Methodological Significance

The sex analysis exemplifies Durkheim's method of **concomitant variation**: a stable cross-national pattern (the sex ratio in suicide) is correlated with a stable cross-national difference (differential social integration by sex), and the former is explained by the latter. The argument simultaneously refutes rival theories and builds positive sociological explanation.
