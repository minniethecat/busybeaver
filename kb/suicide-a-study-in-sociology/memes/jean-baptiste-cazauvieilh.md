# Jean-Baptiste Cazauvieilh

## Overview

**Jean-Baptiste Cazauvieilh** was a French physician active in the 1830s–1840s, whose monograph *Du suicide et de l'aliénation mentale* (1840) — and the subsequent comparative study *Du suicide, de l'aliénation mentale et des crimes contre les personnes, comparés dans leurs rapports réciproques* — represented one of the earliest systematic investigations of the relationship between suicide and mental illness. Working within the alienist tradition that dominated early nineteenth-century French psychiatry, Cazauvieilh collected clinical data from asylums and prisons, seeking to establish empirical correlations between mental alienation and self-destructive behavior.

Durkheim cited Cazauvieilh's work at multiple critical junctures in *Le Suicide* (1897), both as a source of statistical data and as a representative of the psychiatric theory he sought to refute. Cazauvieilh's findings — particularly his elevated estimate of hereditary antecedents among suicides — became exhibits in Durkheim's demonstration that the psychiatric explanation of suicide was empirically inadequate.

## Clinical Data and Durkheim's Critique

Cazauvieilh's most striking finding concerned heredity: among 60 suicides he studied, 13 — or 28% — had hereditary antecedents of mental illness or suicide in their families. This figure was substantially higher than the 4% reported by [[Louis Brierre de Boismont]], who found only 11 cases of familial suicide among 265 mentally ill patients. Durkheim cited this discrepancy to highlight the unreliability of clinical suicide statistics and the wide variation that resulted from different sampling methods and institutional settings.

Cazauvieilh also collected data on suicide within the French prison system. Surveying physicians at various penal institutions (*bagnes*), he found remarkably low suicide rates among convicts — a counterintuitive result that Durkheim used to challenge the assumption that social misery and individual pathology directly caused suicide. At Rochefort prison, only one suicide had been recorded in thirty years, a finding Durkheim interpreted as evidence that intense collective discipline, however harsh, could suppress suicidal tendencies.

His Charenton asylum admissions data provided further material for Durkheim's analysis of seasonal variation in suicide. Cazauvieilh's statistics showed the following seasonal distribution per 1,000 annual admissions: winter 222, spring 283, summer 261, autumn 231. Durkheim compared these figures with seasonal insanity admissions to demonstrate that the rhythms of suicide and mental illness did not coincide — undermining the claim that suicide was simply a symptom of mental pathology.

## Legacy

Cazauvieilh exemplifies the empirical tradition within early psychiatry that, paradoxically, supplied Durkheim with the very data he needed to dismantle psychiatric explanations of suicide. His careful collection of institutional statistics — from asylums, prisons, and hospital admission records — provided a clinical evidence base that Durkheim reinterpreted through a sociological lens. Where Cazauvieilh saw correlations between mental illness and suicide as evidence of a causal link, Durkheim saw the same data as demonstrating that social organization, not individual psychopathology, determined suicide rates.
