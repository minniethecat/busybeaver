# Adolphe Quetelet

## Overview

**Lambert Adolphe Jacques Quetelet** (1796–1874) was a Belgian astronomer, mathematician, and statistician who is widely recognized as the founder of moral statistics — the quantitative study of human behavior. His concept of the *homme moyen* (average man) and his demonstration that social phenomena such as marriage, crime, and suicide followed regular statistical patterns were foundational to the development of sociology as a scientific discipline. However, Durkheim offered a sustained critique of Quetelet's theoretical framework, arguing that statistical regularity reflected collective social forces rather than the properties of an aggregated average individual.

Quetelet's major works — *Sur l'homme et le développement de ses facultés, ou Essai de physique sociale* (1835) and *Du système social et des lois qui le régissent* (1848) — established the empirical baseline that Durkheim would later reinterpret through the lens of [[Social Fact]].

## The Theory of the Average Man (Homme Moyen)

| Aspect | Quetelet's Theory | Durkheim's Critique |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| Statistical regularity | Constancy of social rates reflects the stability of the *type moyen* | Constancy reflects collective forces, not averaged individuals |
| The "average man" | The arithmetic mean of individual traits represents the social norm | The average is a statistical artifact; social facts exist at the collective level, not in averaged individuals |
| Normal and abnormal | The average defines the normal; deviations are anomalies | The normal can be determined by the abnormal — the pathological illuminates the normal |
| Suicide rate | The suicide rate expresses the average propensity of individuals in a society | The suicide rate expresses the intensity of *suicidogenic currents* — collective forces irreducible to individual psychology |

Durkheim's core objection was epistemological: Quetelet had confused the statistical expression of a social fact with its causal substrate. The constancy of suicide rates was real and important, but attributing it to the stability of some averaged "typical individual" explained nothing — it merely restated the observation in different terms. What needed explanation was why *this* society, with *this* degree of integration and regulation, produced *this* suicide rate.

## Legacy and Influence

Despite his theoretical disagreements, Durkheim acknowledged Quetelet's pioneering role. Quetelet was the first to attempt a scientific explanation of the regularity of social phenomena, though he was not the first to observe it — that honor, Durkheim noted, belonged to the German pastor Johann Peter Süssmilch (1707–1767) and his work *Die göttliche Ordnung* (1741).

Quetelet's method of calculating the probability that an individual belonging to a given social group would commit suicide within a year was, in Durkheim's view, statistically valid but theoretically barren: it told us *how likely* suicide was, not *why*. The transformation of Quetelet's descriptive moral statistics into Durkheim's explanatory sociology of [[Suicide]] marked a decisive moment in the emergence of sociology as an autonomous discipline.
