# Education and Suicide

## Overview

In Livre II, Chapitre II of *Le Suicide* (1897), Durkheim addresses a
puzzling empirical correlation: regions and religious groups with higher
levels of education also exhibit higher suicide rates. Rather than
concluding that education *causes* suicide, Durkheim argues that both
phenomena are parallel effects of a single underlying social cause — the
weakening of traditional collective beliefs and the concomitant rise of
**free inquiry** (*libre examen*). The taste for instruction, like the
propensity to suicide, grows as the hold of tradition weakens.
(doc_final.md:5457-5480)

> Le goût du libre examen ne peut pas s'éveiller sans être accompagné du
> goût de l'instruction. La science, en effet, est le seul moyen dont la
> libre réflexion dispose pour arriver à ses fins... Au fond, ces deux
> penchants n'en font qu'un et ils résultent de la même cause.
> (doc_final.md:5457-5463)

## Education as an Effect, Not a Cause

Durkheim's central methodological move is to reframe education from a
*determining cause* to an *epiphenomenon*. The statistical association
between higher literacy and higher suicide rates is real — but the
causal arrow runs from a deeper social fact to both simultaneously:

1. **Traditional beliefs weaken** → the individual is thrown back upon
   their own judgment
2. **Free inquiry awakens** → the individual seeks knowledge to fill
   the void left by crumbling tradition
3. **Suicide rises** → the same loss of collective regulation that
   stimulates the desire for learning also removes the social
   integration that protects against suicide

> En général, les hommes n'aspirent à s'instruire que dans la mesure où
> ils sont affranchis du joug de la tradition; car tant que celle-ci est
> maîtresse des intelligences, elle suffit à tout et ne tolère pas
> facilement de puissance rivale. (doc_final.md:5463-5467)

Thus education — far from being a prophylactic against suicide — is a
symptom of the same social disorganisation that generates it. This is
why Durkheim calls the taste for instruction and the taste for suicide
"two facets of the same coin." (doc_final.md:5462-5463)

## The Protestantism–Education–Suicide Nexus

The link between education and suicide is most clearly visible through
the lens of religious affiliation. Protestantism, by granting every
believer the right to interpret the Bible individually, makes education
a practical necessity and simultaneously weakens the collective
authority of tradition:

| Confession | School Attendance (per 1,000 children, 1877–78) | Suicide Rate |
|-----------|------------------------------------------------|-------------|
| Protestant nations (Saxe, Norwège, Suède, Bade, Danemark, Prusse) | 957 avg | High |
| Catholic nations (France, Autriche-Hongrie, Espagne, Italie) | 667 avg | Low |

Durkheim compiles data showing that Protestant nations had 43% higher
school attendance than Catholic nations in the late nineteenth century,
while also exhibiting markedly higher suicide rates.
(doc_final.md:5496-5506)

Within Germany, the pattern holds at the sub-national level: Bavaria,
the most Catholic province, had the highest illiteracy rate (15% of
conscripts unable to read or write in the Upper Palatinate in 1871),
while Prussia counted only 66 illiterate persons per 1,000 Protestants
versus 152 per 1,000 Catholics. (doc_final.md:5506-5514)

Crucially, the Protestant nations attach importance to elementary
instruction precisely because every individual must be capable of
interpreting the Bible — a requirement that does not exist in the
Catholic system, where an organised hierarchy of authorities renders
tradition invariable. (doc_final.md:5531-5534, 5286-5299)

## The Italian Verification

Durkheim provides further evidence from Italy — an entirely Catholic
country where the religious variable is held constant. Even within a
single confession, the correlation between instruction and suicide
persists (Tableau XIX):

| Province Group | Literacy (% of marriage contracts where both spouses can read) | Suicides per million |
|---------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Group 1 (Piémont, Lombardie, Ligurie, etc.) | 39.09% avg | 41.1 avg |
| Group 2 (Venise, Émilie, Ombrie, etc.) | 15.23% avg | 32.5 avg |

(doc_final.md:5561-5595)

The provinces where both spouses are more frequently literate also have
higher suicide rates, even when religion is identical across all
provinces. This demonstrates that the education–suicide correlation is
not an artifact of the Protestant–Catholic comparison but a general
sociological regularity.

## Distinguishing Elite from Popular Education

Durkheim draws a careful distinction between **elite scientific
production** and **popular instruction**. The former — the achievements
of great scientists and scholars — reveals only what happens in a
restricted portion of society. The latter — the diffusion of basic
literacy to the masses — is a far more reliable indicator of how
intensely a people, taken as a whole, feels the *need* for knowledge:

> À ce point de vue spécial, l'état du haut enseignement et de la
> production proprement scientifique serait un mauvais critère; car il
> nous révélerait seulement ce qui se passe dans une portion restreinte
> de la société. L'enseignement populaire et général est un indice plus
> sûr. (doc_final.md:5537-5540)

What matters sociologically is not the *quantity* of knowledge produced
but the *intensity of the collective need* for education — the degree to
which a society feels compelled to extend learning to its lowest strata.
When a society goes so far as to legally proscribe ignorance, it
testifies to the depth of this need, which is itself a measure of how
far the disintegration of traditional beliefs has progressed.
(doc_final.md:5522-5529)
