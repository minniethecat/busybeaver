# Alfred Legoyt

## Overview

**Alfred Legoyt** (1812–1885) was a French statistician and demographer who directed the Bureau de la statistique générale de France. His 1881 work *Le suicide ancien et moderne* was one of the most comprehensive 19th-century compilations of suicide statistics, covering data from France, Saxony, Baden, and other European states across multiple decades. Durkheim cited Legoyt's data extensively throughout *Le Suicide* — on childhood suicide, professional differentials, marital status effects, and divorce-related suicide rates.

Legoyt's compilations served as a crucial empirical bridge between the early moral statisticians (Quételet, Guerry) and Durkheim's own sociological synthesis. While Legoyt himself offered primarily descriptive accounts rather than systematic theoretical explanations, his meticulous data gathering gave Durkheim the raw material to construct a sociological theory of suicide.

## Le suicide ancien et moderne (1881)

Legoyt's major work compiled and analyzed suicide statistics across multiple dimensions:

| Data Domain | Key Findings Cited by Durkheim |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Childhood suicide | In France (1861–75), only 4.3 suicides per million boys under 16 and 1.8 per million girls — demonstrating suicide's extreme rarity in childhood |
| Professional differentials | Agricultural workers showed markedly different suicide rates than liberal professions, undermining purely occupation-based explanations |
| Marital status and suicide | Suicide rates among divorced persons in Saxony (1847–56) reached 1,400 per million, compared to 176 for the separated — a dramatic differential |
| International comparisons | Compiled comparative tables for Baden, showing suicide rates by marital status across different time periods |

Durkheim used Legoyt's Saxon data on divorced persons to demonstrate that the exceptionally high suicide rate among the divorced could not be explained by individual selection effects alone, but pointed to the breakdown of marital regulation — a key pillar of the theory of [[Anomic Suicide]].

## Methodological Role in Durkheim's Argument

Legoyt's data served several crucial functions in *Le Suicide*:

1. **Childhood suicide rarity** — Legoyt's figures showed that suicide was virtually nonexistent before age 16, supporting Durkheim's argument that suicide propensity is socially acquired, not biologically innate.

2. **Professional comparisons** — Legoyt demonstrated that suicide rates varied dramatically by occupation, which Durkheim interpreted as evidence of differential social integration rather than occupational hazard.

3. **Marital dissolution effects** — Legoyt's comparison of divorced versus separated individuals in Saxony provided some of the earliest quantitative evidence that the dissolution of the marital bond, independent of individual characteristics, elevated suicide risk.
