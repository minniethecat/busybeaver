# Egoistic Suicide

## Overview

**Egoistic suicide** (*suicide égoïste*) is one of the four types of suicide identified by Émile Durkheim in *Le Suicide* (1897). It results from **excessive individuation** — the individual has become detached from the collective life that gives existence meaning and purpose. (004-005)

Durkheim demonstrated this type empirically through differential suicide rates across religious groups (Protestants > Catholics > Jews), family structures (unmarried > married > married with children), and political conditions (peacetime vs. wartime).

## Mechanism

The propensity for egoistic suicide varies inversely with the degree of **integration** of the social groups to which the individual belongs:

1. **Religious integration**: Protestant communities, with their emphasis on individual judgment and free inquiry, provide less collective cohesion than Catholic or Jewish communities
2. **Domestic integration**: Marriage and parenthood bind the individual to collective existence; the unmarried face existence alone
3. **Political integration**: During wars and national crises, collective sentiment intensifies and suicide rates decline

When collective existence loses its intensity, individual existence loses its justification. "Le suicide varie en raison inverse du degré d'intégration des groupes sociaux dont fait partie l'individu." (004-020)

## Key Finding

Durkheim found that education and intellectual development, rather than protecting against suicide, were themselves symptoms of the weakening of collective beliefs — the same process that produces egoistic suicide. Free inquiry arises when traditional dogma weakens; suicide rises for the same reason.
