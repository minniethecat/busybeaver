# Lunier

## Overview

**Lunier** (1822–1885) was a French physician and psychiatrist whose statistical research on alcohol consumption and its pathological consequences provided Durkheim with the data needed to critically examine the role of alcoholism in suicide. As a contributor to the *Annales médico-psychologiques* and the *Journal de la Société de statistique*, Lunier assembled comprehensive departmental data on admissions for alcohol-related mental illness across France.

Durkheim cited Lunier in *Le Suicide* (1897) as part of his argument that alcoholism, contrary to widespread contemporary belief, was not a sufficient explanation for the geographical distribution of suicide in France. Lunier's own data, Durkheim showed, failed to correlate with suicide rates in the way the alcohol-suicide thesis would predict. This was a characteristically Durkheimian move: using a scholar's own evidence to refute the interpretive framework within which it was typically deployed.

## Alcoholism and Mental Illness

Lunier's principal work, *De la production et de la consommation des boissons alcooliques en France* (On the Production and Consumption of Alcoholic Beverages in France, Paris, 1877), compiled statistics on alcohol production, consumption, and the incidence of alcohol-related mental disorders across French departments. His data covered admissions to French asylums for "folie de cause alcoolique" (insanity of alcoholic origin) during the periods 1867–69 and 1874–76.

Durkheim reproduced Lunier's departmental breakdown in *Le Suicide*, grouping the eighty-six French departments into eight classes according to their suicide rates and calculating the mean proportion of alcohol-related insanity admissions for each class. The resulting table revealed a striking absence of correlation. (1791)

## Durkheim's Test

Using Lunier's own figures, Durkheim constructed a table comparing suicide rates per 100,000 inhabitants (1872–76) with the proportion of alcohol-related insanity among total asylum admissions (1867–69 and 1874–76), grouped by department into eight classes ranging from fewer than 50 suicides per 100,000 to more than 300. (1791)

The result was decisive: while suicide rates varied from the lowest to the highest class by a factor of more than six, the proportion of alcohol-related insanity admissions increased by only a few percentage points and the increase was irregular. The second class had a higher proportion of alcohol-related cases than the third; the fifth exceeded the sixth; the seventh exceeded the eighth. As Durkheim summarized: "The two columns do not correspond. While suicides range from single to sextuple and beyond, the proportion of alcoholic insanities barely increases by several units, and the increase is not regular."

This disjunction, Durkheim argued, demonstrated that if alcoholism acted upon suicide it could only be through the mental disorders it produced — and since those disorders did not vary geographically with suicide rates, alcoholism could not be the primary cause of suicide.

## The Wider Alcohol-Suicide Debate

Lunier's work must be understood in the context of the intense late nineteenth-century French debate over alcoholism as a social pathology. Figures such as Lunier, who documented the spread of alcohol-related mental illness, provided the empirical basis for this debate. But by showing that alcohol-related admissions did not track suicide rates geographically, Durkheim used Lunier's data to challenge the reduction of suicide to a consequence of individual pathology — whether alcoholic, hereditary, or mental.
