# Marriage and Suicide

## Overview

In *Le Suicide* (1897), Émile Durkheim demonstrates that marital status exercises a measurable protective effect against suicide. The crude statistics appear to show that the unmarried (célibataires) die by suicide *less* often than the married; however, once age composition is corrected — a large proportion of the unmarried are under 16 and thus at minimal suicide risk — the pattern reverses decisively. The domestic society, Durkheim argues, acts as a buffer against [[egoistic-suicide]] by binding the individual to a collective life that transcends mere self-interest. (doc_final.md:5857-5895)

This finding is central to Durkheim's broader theory of social integration: the family, like [[religion-and-suicide|religion]], protects against suicide not through doctrine or material advantage, but through the density of collective existence it imposes on its members.

## The Statistical Correction

The appearance that the unmarried are less suicide-prone is an artifact of age distribution. Many of the unmarried are children under 16, for whom the suicide rate is negligible (1–2 per million). Once this cohort is removed, the unmarried aged 16+ show 173 suicides per million compared to 154.5 for the married — a ratio of 112:100. (doc_final.md:5878-5898)

> Si l'on ne consulte que les chiffres absolus, les célibataires paraissent se tuer moins que les gens mariés... Cependant, si évident qu'il paraisse, ce raisonnement *a priori* est entièrement faux et les faits ne lui donnent une apparence de raison que pour avoir été mal analysés. (doc_final.md:5857-5877)

A further correction is needed: the married are on average older (40–45 years) than the unmarried (26–28 years), and suicide risk rises with age. If age alone operated, the married should have *higher* suicide rates than the unmarried — approximately 140 vs. 97.9 per million. In fact, the reverse holds, proving that domestic life actively suppresses the suicide tendency. (doc_final.md:5900-5930)

## Marriage as Domestic Society

Durkheim argues that the protective mechanism is not material comfort — the unmarried have fewer burdens — but the **density of collective life** (densité de la vie collective) within the conjugal family. The density hypothesis predicts that:

1. **Married people with children should be more protected than childless married people**, since children increase the volume and intensity of collective interactions
2. **The protective effect should weaken with the dissolution of the domestic bond**, i.e., widowhood

Both predictions are confirmed by the statistical record. (doc_final.md:115-184)

| Marital Status | Relative Suicide Risk |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Married (corrected for age) | 100 (baseline) |
| Unmarried (bachelor, 16+) | 112 |
| Widowed | Significantly elevated |

## Sexual Dimorphism in the Protective Effect

A striking feature of Durkheim's data is that marriage protects men far more than women. In fact, married women in some populations show *higher* suicide rates than unmarried women. Durkheim interprets this asymmetry through the lens of differential benefit from collective regulation: men gain more from the discipline of domestic life, while women may experience the constraints of marriage as burdensome. This finding anticipates later work on gender and social regulation. (doc_final.md:10580-10600)
