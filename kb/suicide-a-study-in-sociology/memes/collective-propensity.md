# Collective Propensity

## Overview

The concept of **collective propensity** (penchant collectif) is the methodological cornerstone of Émile Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897). Before presenting his typology or his policy proposals, Durkheim must establish that suicide has a *social* element irreducible to the psychology of individuals. The collective propensity is that element: a force proper to the social group that determines how many of its members will kill themselves in a given period, independent of their personal characteristics. (doc_final.md:784-787)

This concept is what allows Durkheim to treat the suicide rate as a **social fact** — a phenomenon external to individuals, endowed with coercive power, and explicable only by other social facts. Without it, the entire explanatory architecture of *Le Suicide* collapses into individual psychology.

## Distinguishing Collective from Individual Propensity

Durkheim is careful to distinguish the collective propensity from individual suicidal tendencies. Every suicide involves a particular person with particular motives — financial ruin, romantic disappointment, physical illness. But the constancy of the suicide rate across time, its variation across social groups, and its immunity to changes in the composition of the population all indicate that a force *sui generis* is at work:

> Les états psychopathiques qui constituent l'aliénation mentale ne peuvent rendre compte du penchant collectif au suicide, dans sa généralité. (doc_final.md:1226-1227)

Individual psychology — even abnormal psychology — cannot explain why Protestant countries have higher suicide rates than Catholic ones, why married people kill themselves less than the unmarried, why suicide declines during wars. These are properties of *groups*, not of persons.

## The Suicidogenic Current

Durkheim sometimes describes the collective propensity using the hydraulic metaphor of a **suicidogenic current** (courant suicidogène) — a collective force that circulates through society and enters individuals at their weakest points:

> Elles marquent, peut-on dire, les points faibles de l'individu, ceux par où le courant, qui vient du dehors l'inciter à se détruire, s'insinue le plus facilement en lui. (doc_final.md:4939-4940)

Individual motives — the reasons people give for killing themselves — are merely the *entry points* through which the collective current penetrates particular persons. They are not causes but conduits. This is why the same apparent motives (financial loss, romantic sorrow) produce different suicide rates in different social groups: the current is stronger or weaker, and the entry points are merely where it finds the path of least resistance.

The suicidogenic current can be so intense that it stops at the borders of protected populations: Catholic enclaves surrounded by Protestant regions show low suicide rates despite being bathed in the same economic and climatic environment. The current is real, directional, and socially bounded. (doc_final.md:4407-4419)

## Methodological Implications

The collective propensity concept generates Durkheim's method. If suicide is driven by collective forces, then:

1. **Classification by morphology is impossible** — individual suicide descriptions (notes left, method chosen, stated motives) reveal little about the underlying social causes. The same collective force can produce superficially different individual acts. (doc_final.md:4636-4648)

2. **Classification must be etiological** — suicides must be grouped by their social causes (integration, regulation) rather than by their individual forms. This yields the famous typology: egoistic, altruistic, anomic, fatalistic.

3. **The suicide rate is the unit of analysis** — not the individual suicide. The rate is a property of the collective, not an aggregate of individual decisions. Its stability over decades, its correlation with social structure, and its immunity to individual-level interventions all confirm its status as a social fact.

## Relationship to Other Key Concepts

The collective propensity is not monolithic. Durkheim identifies multiple suicidogenic currents, each corresponding to a different pathological state of the social body:

| Suicidogenic Current | Social Condition | Suicide Type |
|---------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Excessive individuation | Weak social integration | Egoistic suicide |
| Excessive integration | Individual absorbed by group | Altruistic suicide |
| Regulatory breakdown | Normlessness after rapid change | Anomic suicide |
| Excessive regulation | Oppressive normative control | Fatalistic suicide |

Each current is a distinct collective force, and the overall suicide rate of a society reflects the combined intensity of all active currents.
