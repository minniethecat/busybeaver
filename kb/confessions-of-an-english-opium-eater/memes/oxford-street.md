# Oxford Street

## Overview

The London thoroughfare immortalised by De Quincey — the site of his first opium purchase near 'the stately Pantheon,' his nighttime wanderings with Ann, and the enduring symbol of loss and searching in the Confessions.

## In the *Confessions*

Oxford Street features in De Quincey's autobiographical narrative at key junctures. The PN references above document its appearances in the text, from which a fuller understanding of its role in the *Confessions* can be drawn through close reading of the cited passages.

## Appearances in the Text

Oxford Street recurs throughout the *Confessions* as a stage for the most intimate scenes of De Quincey's London life. In the early sections, it is the street where he walks with Ann, the young prostitute who becomes his companion and protector. They would rest together "on steps and under the shelter of" its buildings (004-084), sharing a fragile refuge within the indifferent city.

The street is also the site of a critical moment of rescue. After a day when De Quincey "had felt more than usually ill and exhausted" (004-114), Ann — "uttering a cry of terror" — ran "without a moment's delay" into Oxford Street and returned with a glass of port wine and spices that revived him when he was near death from hunger (004-129). This act of generosity, performed by a sixteen-year-old girl who had almost nothing, becomes one of the emotional anchors of the entire narrative.

Years later, writing in 1821 on his birthday (August 15), De Quincey records turning aside "from my evening walk down Oxford Street, purposely to take a glance at" the house where he once suffered — now occupied by a cheerful domestic party, in "marvellous contrast" to the darkness and silence he had known there (004-034). The street thus bridges the abyss between his past misery and present comfort, and he confesses that even in his settled later life, "often, when I walk at this time in Oxford Street by dreamy lamplight" (004-163), the memories of those younger years — and of Ann — rise up with undiminished power.

## Key Passages

The moment of Ann's intervention on Oxford Street is one of the most dramatic scenes in the *Confessions*:

> Uttering a cry of terror, but without a moment's delay, she ran off into Oxford Street, and in less time than could be imagined returned to me with a glass of port wine and spices, that acted upon my empty stomach, which at that time would have rejected all solid food, with an instantaneous power of restoration.
>
> (004-129)
