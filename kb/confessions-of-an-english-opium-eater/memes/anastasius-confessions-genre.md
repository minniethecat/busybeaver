# Anastasius and the Confessional Mode

## Overview

[[anastasius|Thomas Hope's *Anastasius*]] (1819) occupies a unique position in the *Confessions*: it is simultaneously a source of Orientalist reference, a stylistic precursor, and — in an odd twist — the book from which De Quincey drew his reference to the drug "madjoon." De Quincey's engagement with Hope's novel reveals how the confessional mode of the *Confessions* emerged from a broader literary context in which fiction and autobiography, Orientalism and drug narrative, were fluid categories.

## Anastasius in the *Confessions*

De Quincey references *Anastasius* several times. The most significant citation concerns the drug "madjoon" — a Turkish confection of opium and spices: De Quincey notes that he took the reference to madjoon "from *Anastasius*" (010-127), acknowledging his debt to Hope's novel for ethnographic detail. Elsewhere, he credits the novel's accuracy on medical matters, citing it as a source for pharmacological information (009-198).

Later in the *Confessions*, De Quincey returns to *Anastasius* with a more critical eye. He recounts an anecdote about a gentleman "whose wit was much admired" — a character from Hope's novel — and subjects the novel's claims to ironic scrutiny (017-041, 017-053). He concludes that, "considered as a hoax on *Anastasius*, it reads excellently" (017-059) — a playful suggestion that Hope's Orientalist expertise may itself be a kind of literary performance.

## Genre Intersection

The relationship between *Anastasius* and the *Confessions* illuminates several generic tensions:

1. **Fiction vs. confession**: *Anastasius* is a novel — the fictional autobiography of a Greek renegade in the Ottoman Empire. The *Confessions* presents itself as genuine autobiography, yet De Quincey borrows from fiction to authenticate his drug narrative.

2. **Orientalism and the self**: Hope's novel provided De Quincey with a ready-made vocabulary of Orientalist tropes — the exotic East, the languorous opium-den — which the *Confessions* then repurposes for the exploration of De Quincey's own psyche.

3. **The intertextual confessional**: De Quincey's self-conscious citation of his sources blurs the line between confession and literary criticism, creating an intertextual mode in which the confessing self is constructed from prior texts.

## Significance

The *Anastasius* connection reveals the *Confessions* not as a naive outpouring of personal experience but as a sophisticated literary artifact, built from a network of intertextual references. De Quincey's confessional mode is neither purely autobiographical nor purely fictional — it operates in the hybrid space between, where "hoax" and "confession" are not mutually exclusive (017-059).

## Key Passages

De Quincey's most direct engagement with *Anastasius* comes in a passage that blends literary criticism with ironic commentary:

> I must caution my readers specially against the brilliant author of _Anastasius_. This gentleman, whose wit would lead one to presume him an opium-eater, has made it impossible to consider him in that character, from the grievous misrepresentation which he gives of its effects at pp. 215-17 of vol. i.
>
> (017-041)
