# English Opium Trade

The **English opium trade** of the early nineteenth century provided the material foundation for Thomas De Quincey's opium consumption. Opium was legally and readily available in Britain during De Quincey's lifetime, sold by druggists and apothecaries without prescription (007-117), and imported in large quantities — primarily from Turkey and India (007-177).

## Availability

One of the striking features of the *Confessions* for modern readers is the casual availability of opium. De Quincey buys it from a Soho druggist as easily as one might buy tea or coffee. The price was modest — "for five guineas a year," he notes, one could maintain a significant habit. This availability reflected Britain's position at the centre of a global opium trade that would, within decades, lead to the Opium Wars with China (1839–1842, 1856–1860).

The casual nature of opium procurement is underscored by De Quincey's own admission that he could not even track his consumption:

> How much I was at that time taking I cannot say, for the opium which I used had been purchased for me by a friend, who afterwards refused to let me pay him; so that I could not ascertain even what quantity I had used within the year.
>
> (014-171)

## Medical and Recreational Use

In De Quincey's time, opium was used both medicinally (for pain, insomnia, diarrhoea, and nervous complaints) and recreationally. The boundary between these uses was poorly defined, and De Quincey exploits this ambiguity throughout the *Confessions* — presenting his consumption alternately as medical necessity and philosophical experiment.

## Empire and Opium

De Quincey's personal opium consumption was inextricable from the larger structures of British imperial trade. The opium that fuelled his dreams was the same commodity that fuelled Britain's commercial expansion into Asia — a connection that later critics, from Edward Said onward, have explored in depth.
