# Twelfth Night

## Overview

*Twelfth Night, or What You Will* (c. 1601) is one of William Shakespeare's great comedies. De Quincey cites it in the *Confessions* for its treatment of music — a subject of intense personal significance in the opium-eater's experience.

## Shakespeare on Music

In one of the most celebrated digressions of the *Confessions* — the meditation on the powers of music under opium's influence — De Quincey surveys the treatment of music across all literature. He finds most accounts inadequate, making two significant exceptions: a passage in Sir Thomas Browne's *Religio Medici* and "the fine extravaganza on that subject in 'Twelfth Night.'" (008-173)(008-174)

The reference is to Duke Orsino's famous opening speech ("If music be the food of love, play on..."), which De Quincey singles out as capturing something essential about music's emotional power. Though De Quincey does not quote Shakespeare directly, the allusion positions his own reflections on music — which he calls "the sensuous expression of the beautiful" — within a literary tradition stretching back to the Renaissance.

## In the Context of the *Confessions*

Shakespeare's presence in the *Confessions* is subtle but pervasive. De Quincey, like many Romantic writers, regarded Shakespeare as the supreme English literary authority. By invoking *Twelfth Night* alongside Browne's *Religio Medici*, De Quincey triangulates his own position between Renaissance comedy and Baroque spiritual meditation — a characteristically eclectic pairing that mirrors the *Confessions*' own blend of the comic and the sublime.
