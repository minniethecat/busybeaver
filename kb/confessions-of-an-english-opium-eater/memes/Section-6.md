[006-001]Lord of my learning, and no land beside—

[006-002]were, like my friend Lord ——, heir by general repute to £70,000 per

[006-003]annum, what a panic should I be under at this moment about my throat!

[006-004]Indeed, it was not likely that Lord —— should ever be in my situation.

[006-005]But nevertheless, the spirit of the remark remains true—that vast power

[006-006]and possessions make a man shamefully afraid of dying; and I am

[006-007]convinced that many of the most intrepid adventurers, who, by

[006-008]fortunately being poor, enjoy the full use of their natural courage,

[006-009]would, if at the very instant of going into action news were brought to

[006-010]them that they had unexpectedly succeeded to an estate in [[england|England]] of

[006-011]£50,000 a-year, feel their dislike to bullets considerably sharpened,

[006-012]{6} and their efforts at perfect equanimity and self-possession

[006-013]proportionably difficult. So true it is, in the language of a wise man

[006-014]whose own experience had made him acquainted with both fortunes, that

[006-015]riches are better fitted

[006-016]To slacken virtue, and abate her edge,

[006-017]Than tempt her to do ought may merit praise.

[006-018]_[[paradise-regained|Paradise Regained]]_.

[006-019]I dally with my subject because, to myself, the [[memory|remembrance]] of these

[006-020]times is profoundly interesting. But my reader shall not have any

[006-021]further cause to complain, for I now hasten to its close. In the road

[006-022]between Slough and [[eton|Eton]] I fell asleep, and just as the morning began to

[006-023]dawn I was awakened by the voice of a man standing over me and

[006-024]surveying me. I know not what he was: he was an ill-looking fellow, but

[006-025]not therefore of necessity an ill-meaning fellow; or, if he were, I

[006-026]suppose he thought that no person sleeping out-of-doors in winter could

[006-027]be worth robbing. In which conclusion, however, as it regarded myself,

[006-028]I beg to assure him, if he should be among my readers, that he was

[006-029]mistaken. After a slight remark he passed on; and I was not sorry at

[006-030]his disturbance, as it enabled me to pass through Eton before people

[006-031]were generally up. The night had been heavy and lowering, but towards

[006-032]the morning it had changed to a slight frost, and the ground and the

[006-033]trees were now covered with rime. I slipped through Eton unobserved;

[006-034]washed myself, and as far as possible adjusted my dress, at a little

[006-035]public-house in Windsor; and about eight o’clock went down towards

[006-036]Pote’s. On my road I met some junior boys, of whom I made inquiries. An

[006-037]Etonian is always a gentleman; and, in spite of my shabby habiliments,

[006-038]they answered me civilly. My friend Lord —— was gone to the University

[006-039]of ——. “Ibi omnis effusus labor!” I had, however, other friends at

[006-040]Eton; but it is not to all that wear that name in prosperity that a man

[006-041]is willing to present himself in distress. On recollecting myself,

[006-042]however, I asked for the Earl of D——, to whom (though my acquaintance

[006-043]with him was not so intimate as with some others) I should not have

[006-044]shrunk from presenting myself under any circumstances. He was still at

[006-045]Eton, though I believe on the wing for [[cambridge|Cambridge]]. I called, was

[006-046]received kindly, and asked to breakfast.

[006-047]Here let me stop for a moment to check my reader from any erroneous

[006-048]conclusions. Because I have had occasion incidentally to speak of

[006-049]various patrician friends, it must not be supposed that I have myself

[006-050]any pretension to rank and high blood. I thank God that I have not. I

[006-051]am the son of a plain English merchant, esteemed during his life for

[006-052]his great integrity, and strongly attached to literary pursuits

[006-053](indeed, he was himself, anonymously, an author). If he had lived it

[006-054]was expected that he would have been very rich; but dying prematurely,

[006-055]he left no more than about £30,000 amongst seven different claimants.

[006-056]My mother I may mention with honour, as still more highly gifted; for

[006-057]though unpretending to the name and honours of a _literary_ woman, I

[006-058]shall presume to call her (what many literary women are not) an

[006-059]_intellectual_ woman; and I believe that if ever her letters should be

[006-060]collected and published, they would be thought generally to exhibit as

[006-061]much strong and masculine sense, delivered in as pure “mother English,”

[006-062]racy and fresh with idiomatic graces, as any in our language—hardly

[006-063]excepting those of Lady M. W. Montague. These are my honours of

[006-064]descent, I have no other; and I have thanked God sincerely that I have

[006-065]not, because, in my judgment, a station which raises a man too

[006-066]eminently above the level of his fellow-creatures is not the most

[006-067]favourable to moral or to intellectual qualities.

[006-068]Lord D—— placed before me a most magnificent breakfast. It was really

[006-069]so; but in my eyes it seemed trebly magnificent, from being the first

[006-070]regular meal, the first “good man’s table,” that I had sate down to for

[006-071]months. Strange to say, however, I could scarce eat anything. On the

[006-072]day when I first received my £10 bank-note I had gone to a baker’s shop

[006-073]and bought a couple of rolls; this very shop I had two months or six

[006-074]weeks before surveyed with an eagerness of desire which it was almost

[006-075]humiliating to me to recollect. I remembered the story about Otway, and

[006-076]feared that there might be danger in eating too rapidly. But I had no

[006-077]need for alarm; my appetite was quite sunk, and I became sick before I

[006-078]had eaten half of what I had bought. This effect from eating what

[006-079]approached to a meal I continued to feel for weeks; or, when I did not

[006-080]experience any nausea, part of what I ate was rejected, sometimes with

[006-081]acidity, sometimes immediately and without any acidity. On the present

[006-082]occasion, at Lord D-’s table, I found myself not at all better than

[006-083]usual, and in the midst of luxuries I had no appetite. I had, however,

[006-084]unfortunately, at all times a craving for wine; I explained my

[006-085]situation, therefore, to Lord D——, and gave him a short account of my

[006-086]late sufferings, at which he expressed great compassion, and called for

[006-087]wine. This gave me a momentary relief and pleasure; and on all

[006-088]occasions when I had an opportunity I never failed to drink wine, which

[006-089]I worshipped then as I have since worshipped opium. I am convinced,

[006-090]however, that this indulgence in wine contributed to strengthen my

[006-091]malady, for the tone of my stomach was apparently quite sunk, and by a

[006-092]better regimen it might sooner, and perhaps effectually, have been

[006-093]revived. I hope that it was not from this love of wine that I lingered

[006-094]in the neighbourhood of my Eton friends; I persuaded myself then that

[006-095]it was from reluctance to ask of Lord D——, on whom I was conscious I

[006-096]had not sufficient claims, the particular service in quest of which I

[006-097]had come down to Eton. I was, however unwilling to lose my journey,

[006-098]and—I asked it. Lord D——, whose good nature was unbounded, and which,

[006-099]in regard to myself, had been measured rather by his compassion perhaps

[006-100]for my condition, and his knowledge of my intimacy with some of his

[006-101]relatives, than by an over-rigorous inquiry into the extent of my own

[006-102]direct claims, faltered, nevertheless, at this request. He acknowledged

[006-103]that he did not like to have any dealings with money-lenders, and

[006-104]feared lest such a transaction might come to the ears of his

[006-105]connexions. Moreover, he doubted whether _his_ signature, whose

[006-106]expectations were so much more bounded than those of ——, would avail

[006-107]with my unchristian friends. However, he did not wish, as it seemed, to

[006-108]mortify me by an absolute refusal; for after a little consideration he

[006-109]promised, under certain conditions which he pointed out, to give his

[006-110]security. Lord D—— was at this time not eighteen years of age; but I

[006-111]have often doubted, on recollecting since the good sense and prudence

[006-112]which on this occasion he mingled with so much urbanity of manner (an

[006-113]urbanity which in him wore the grace of youthful sincerity), whether

[006-114]any statesman—the oldest and the most accomplished in diplomacy—could

[006-115]have acquitted himself better under the same circumstances. Most

[006-116]people, indeed, cannot be addressed on such a business without

[006-117]surveying you with looks as austere and unpropitious as those of a

[006-118]Saracen’s head.

[006-119]Recomforted by this promise, which was not quite equal to the best but

[006-120]far above the worst that I had pictured to myself as possible, I

[006-121]returned in a Windsor coach to [[london|London]] three days after I had quitted

[006-122]it. And now I come to the end of my story. The Jews did not approve of

[006-123]Lord D——’s terms; whether they would in the end have acceded to them,

[006-124]and were only seeking time for making due inquiries, I know not; but

[006-125]many delays were made, time passed on, the small fragment of my

[006-126]bank-note had just melted away, and before any conclusion could have

[006-127]been put to the business I must have relapsed into my former state of

[006-128]wretchedness. Suddenly, however, at this crisis, an opening was made,

[006-129]almost by accident, for reconciliation with my friends; I quitted

[006-130]London in haste for a remote part of England; after some time I

[006-131]proceeded to the university, and it was not until many months had

[006-132]passed away that I had it in my power again to revisit the ground which

[006-133]had become so interesting to me, and to this day remains so, as the

[006-134]chief scene of my youthful sufferings.

[006-135]Meantime, what had become of poor [[ann|Ann]]? For her I have reserved my

[006-136]concluding words. According to our agreement, I sought her daily, and

[006-137]waited for her every night, so long as I stayed in London, at the

[006-138]corner of Titchfield Street. I inquired for her of every one who was

[006-139]likely to know her, and during the last hours of my stay in London I

[006-140]put into activity every means of tracing her that my knowledge of

[006-141]London suggested and the limited extent of my power made possible. The

[006-142]street where she had lodged I knew, but not the house; and I remembered

[006-143]at last some account which she had given me of ill-treatment from her

[006-144]landlord, which made it probable that she had quitted those lodgings

[006-145]before we parted. She had few acquaintances; most people, besides,

[006-146]thought that the earnestness of my inquiries arose from motives which

[006-147]moved their laughter or their slight regard; and others, thinking I was

[006-148]in chase of a girl who had robbed me of some trifles, were naturally

[006-149]and excusably indisposed to give me any clue to her, if indeed they had

[006-150]any to give. Finally as my despairing resource, on the day I left

[006-151]London I put into the hands of the only person who (I was sure) must

[006-152]know Ann by sight, from having been in company with us once or twice,

[006-153]an address to ——, in ——shire, at that time the residence of my family.

[006-154]But to this hour I have never heard a syllable about her. This, amongst

[006-155]such troubles as most men meet with in this life, has been my heaviest

[006-156][[suffering|affliction]]. If she lived, doubtless we must have been some time in

[006-157]search of each other, at the very same moment, through the mighty

[006-158]labyrinths of London; perhaps even within a few feet of each other—a

[006-159]barrier no wider than a London street often amounting in the end to a

[006-160]separation for eternity! During some years I hoped that she _did_ live;

[006-161]and I suppose that, in the literal and unrhetorical use of the word

[006-162]_myriad_, I may say that on my different visits to London I have looked

[006-163]into many, many myriads of female faces, in the hope of meeting her. I

[006-164]should know her again amongst a thousand, if I saw her for a moment;

[006-165]for though not handsome, she had a sweet expression of countenance and

[006-166]a peculiar and graceful carriage of the head. I sought her, I have

[006-167]said, in hope. So it was for years; but now I should fear to see her;

[006-168]and her cough, which grieved me when I parted with her, is now my

[006-169]consolation. I now wish to see her no longer; but think of her, more

[006-170]gladly, as one long since laid in the grave—in the grave, I would hope,

[006-171]of a Magdalen; taken away, before injuries and cruelty had blotted out

[006-172]and transfigured her ingenuous nature, or the brutalities of ruffians

[006-173]had completed the ruin they had begun.
