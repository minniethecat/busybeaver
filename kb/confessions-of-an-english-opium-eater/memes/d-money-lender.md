# D——, the Jewish Money-Lender

## Identity

The money-lender designated only as "D——" is one of the anonymous figures of De Quincey's London period. De Quincey deliberately conceals the man's full name, recording him only by an initial followed by a long dash — a convention he also applies to other acquaintances (Lord D——, Lady ——) whose identities he wished to protect or whose full names he had forgotten. The money-lender belonged to the network of Jewish financiers who advertised money-lending services in late eighteenth- and early nineteenth-century London, catering especially to young gentlemen with expectations of future inheritance.

## Role in the Confessions

D—— first appears when De Quincey, hard-pressed for funds, seeks out money-lenders willing to advance cash against his future prospects:

> amongst other persons I applied to a Jew named D—— [005-052]

De Quincey presented his case to D—— and other lenders, offering an account of his expectations. The money-lenders were initially receptive, but a crucial doubt lingered: was De Quincey truly who he claimed to be — a young gentleman of genuine prospects — or merely an impostor?

The negotiations advanced to the point where D—— proposed that De Quincey travel to Eton to secure a guarantee from a young nobleman friend:

> In pursuance of this proposal on the part of the Jew, about eight or nine days after I had received the £10, I prepared to go down to Eton. [005-088]

The transaction was further complicated by Lord D——'s terms, which the Jewish lenders ultimately found unsatisfactory.

## Reappearance

Eighteen months later, De Quincey returned to D—— for a second loan:

> To this same Jew, by the way, some eighteen months afterwards, I applied again on the same business [016-161]

This repeat transaction suggests a relationship of some continuity — D—— was apparently satisfied enough with the first engagement to entertain a second. De Quincey describes the terms as "regular," noting the standard rate of seventeen and a half per cent — a detail that grounds the episode in the real economics of Regency London's informal credit markets.

## Narrative Function

D—— and his fellow money-lenders serve a dual function in the *Confessions*. On the narrative level, they are agents of plot: the loan negotiations drive De Quincey from London toward Eton and back, generating the geographic movement that structures the early chapters. On the thematic level, they embody the precariousness of De Quincey's social position — suspended between gentlemanly expectations and the humiliating realities of debt, reliant on men whose confidence he must constantly negotiate.
