# Confession as Genre

## Definition

The title *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* signals De Quincey's choice of a specific literary genre with deep roots in Western tradition. The **confession** — from Augustine's *Confessions* through Rousseau's — is a first-person narrative that simultaneously admits to transgression and claims authority through that very admission. De Quincey exploits this double movement masterfully: he confesses to excess ("indulged in it to an excess not yet *recorded* of any other man," 001-057) while immediately claiming the moral high ground of self-conquest ("have untwisted, almost to its final links, the accursed chain which fettered me," 001-061). The confession form allows him to be simultaneously sinner and saint, patient and physician, subject and author.

## In the *Confessions*

### The Framing Device

De Quincey distinguishes his "confessions" from juridical or religious confession: they are "spontaneous and extra-judicial" (001-018) — offered freely, not extracted under duress. This voluntary character is essential to the rhetorical strategy: the author is not compelled to speak but chooses to do so for "the service which I may thereby render to the whole class of opium-eaters" (001-070). The confession is thus also a public service — an act of altruistic self-exposure.

### Guilt and Innocence

The confession form forces De Quincey to negotiate the question of guilt explicitly. "Guilt, therefore, I do not acknowledge" (001-068). Yet he admits that "these preliminary confessions, or introductory narrative of the youthful adventures which first led me to opium" may "amount to a confession of guilt" (001-042) in the reader's eyes. This ambiguity — confessing without conceding guilt — is the genre's central dynamic in De Quincey's hands.

### A New Kind of Confession

De Quincey's innovation is to apply the confessional form not to sins of sex or violence (the traditional territory) but to the intimate experience of a psychoactive substance. By doing so, he created a template that would influence drug literature from Baudelaire to Burroughs. The "matter of the confessions" (001-143) is not moral turpitude but phenomenological precision — the texture of altered consciousness itself.

## Key Quotations

> These confessions (that is, spontaneous and extra-judicial) I make for the benefit of my countrymen. (001-018)

> Guilt, therefore, I do not acknowledge; and if I did, it is possible that I might still resolve on the present act of confession in consideration of the service which I may thereby render to the whole class of opium-eaters. (001-068)
