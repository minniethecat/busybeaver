# Confessions of an English Opium-Eater

## Overview

*Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* is Thomas De Quincey's most famous work, first published in two instalments in the [[london-magazine|London Magazine]] — the first part in September 1821 and the second in October 1821 (001-005, 007-004). It was republished as a single volume in 1822 and substantially revised for De Quincey's collected works in 1856. The text draws on De Quincey's own experience as an opium-eater, a habit that began at [[oxford|Oxford]] in 1804 and continued throughout his life.

The work is divided into two principal parts: "The Pleasures of Opium" and "The Pains of Opium," preceded by the "Preliminary Confessions" — an autobiographical account of the author's youth that De Quincey considered essential context for understanding his opium narrative. He explicitly frames these preliminaries as integral: "These preliminary confessions, or introductory narrative of the youthful incidents which first led me into the use of opium" (001-127), arguing that they "cannot fail to render the confessions themselves more interesting" (001-144).

## Publication History

The work first appeared in the [[london-magazine|London Magazine]]:
- **Part I** (September 1821): The "Preliminary Confessions" and "The Pleasures of Opium" (001-005)
- **Part II** (October 1821): "The Pains of Opium" and an appendix (007-004)
- **Part III** (December 1822): A promised continuation, later incorporated into the expanded edition (015-043, 015-056)

The 1822 book edition added considerable material. The 1856 collected-works edition represents De Quincey's final text, with extensive revisions — some passages expanded, others cut. The 1856 text is generally considered the definitive version.

## Structure and Content

### Preliminary Confessions

De Quincey opens by defining his project as a confessional narrative in the tradition of Rousseau and St. Augustine — though he reserves the right to omit certain details: he addresses "the reader" directly, explaining that his confessional mode is "spontaneous and extra-judicial" (001-018). The Preliminary Confessions recount his unhappy childhood in Manchester, his flight from school, his wanderings through [[wales|Wales]], and his descent into poverty in [[london|London]] — where he met [[ann|Ann]], the young prostitute who became a defining figure in his emotional life.

### The Pleasures of Opium

This section describes the early, euphoric phase of opium use — the heightened perception, intellectual stimulation, and relief from physical pain that De Quincey experienced. He distinguishes his refined, intellectual opium-eating from the crude consumption of "Turkish opium-eaters" (008-131). The pleasures are presented not merely as physical sensations but as a key to expanded consciousness and aesthetic appreciation — particularly of music and urban spectacle.

### The Pains of Opium

The second major section describes the dark side of long-term opium use: nightmares, bodily deterioration, and psychological torment (012-137). The famous opium dreams — of Piranesi's endless staircases, of Asiatic landscapes, of Ann's face lost in crowds — belong to this section. De Quincey's account of withdrawal and suffering became the template for countless later addiction narratives.

## Literary Significance

The *Confessions* established the "drug confessional" as a literary genre. Its influence extends to:
- [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], whose tales of altered consciousness echo De Quincey's dream-prose
- Charles Baudelaire's *Les Paradis Artificiels* (1860), a partial translation and commentary
- The modern addiction memoir (William Burroughs, Thomas De Quincey's own *Suspiria de Profundis*)
- The Romantic tradition of autobiographical confession

Within De Quincey's own corpus, the *Confessions* is the centerpiece — referenced and elaborated in his later works *Suspiria de Profundis* (1845) and *The English Mail-Coach* (1849), both of which extend the dream-writing mode pioneered here.

## In the *Confessions*

De Quincey's relationship to his own text is characteristically self-conscious. He refers to the *Confessions* throughout the work as a living project, addressing the reader directly and commenting on his own compositional choices. The "main subject of these latter confessions" (012-137) — the pains of opium — is presented as the inevitable counterweight to the pleasures, a structural symmetry that mirrors the drug's own dual nature.
