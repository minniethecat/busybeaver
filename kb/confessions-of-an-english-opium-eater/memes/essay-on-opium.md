# Essay on the Effects of Opium

## Overview

Essay on the Effects of Opium (1763) by Awsiter. Awsiter's medical treatise (1763) — cited and challenged by De Quincey as representative of the medical establishment's ignorance about opium, a foil against which he positions his own experiential authority.

## In the *Confessions*

Referenced in the passages cited. Its role in De Quincey's text is documented through these PN-cited references.

## De Quincey's Citation

Awsiter's *Essay on the Effects of Opium* enters the *Confessions* at a crucial early moment, as De Quincey is establishing the stakes of his confessional project. Having acknowledged that "the fascinating powers of opium are admitted even by medical writers, who are its greatest enemies," De Quincey turns to the testimony of an unwilling expert witness — Awsiter, apothecary to Greenwich Hospital:

> Thus, for instance, Awsiter, apothecary to Greenwich Hospital, in his "Essay on the Effects of Opium" (published in the year 1763), when attempting to explain why Mead had not been sufficiently explicit on the properties, counteragents, &c., of this drug, expresses himself in the following mysterious terms (φωναντα συνετοισι): "Perhaps he thought the subject of too delicate a nature to be made common; and as many people might then indiscriminately use it, it would take from that necessary fear and caution which should prevent their experiencing the extensive power of this drug, *for there are many properties in it, if universally known, that would habituate the use, and make it more in request with us than with Turks themselves*; the result of which knowledge," he adds, "must prove a general misfortune." (001-110)

The Greek phrase De Quincey inserts — φωναντα συνετοισι ("speaking to those who understand") — is a wink to the learned reader. It signals that Awsiter's warning is about to be turned against itself. The medical establishment's greatest fear — that knowledge of opium's pleasures would spread — becomes, in De Quincey's hands, the most compelling advertisement for the drug. If even its enemies concede that opium has "many properties" whose universal knowledge "would habituate the use," then the reader has already been told everything worth knowing.

## De Quincey's Rebuttal

Having quoted Awsiter's dire warning, De Quincey demurs: "In the necessity of this conclusion I do not altogether concur" (001-123). The demurral is, of course, ironic. De Quincey has just spent several pages establishing the scale of opium consumption in England — from the "three respectable London druggists" who confirm "immense" amateur use to the Manchester cotton-workers whose Saturday night opium purchases litter the druggists' counters. Awsiter's fear, De Quincey implies, has already been realised: the knowledge *has* spread, and the English, far from being protected by "necessary fear and caution," are consuming opium in quantities that would alarm even the Turks. The medical writer's warning has become, retroactively, a prophecy — and De Quincey, by quoting it verbatim, enlists his enemy as his best witness.

This passage exemplifies De Quincey's rhetorical mastery of the hostile citation. By reproducing Awsiter's words at length, he grants the medical establishment an apparently fair hearing; by framing them with his own commentary, he transforms their warning into an endorsement. The *Essay on the Effects of Opium* — a forgotten medical pamphlet — survives in literary history only because De Quincey chose to quote it, and to subvert it, in the *Confessions*.
