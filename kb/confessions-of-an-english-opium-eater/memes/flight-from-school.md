# Flight from School

In the summer of 1802, at the age of sixteen, [[thomas-de-quincey|Thomas De Quincey]] fled [[manchester|Manchester]] Grammar School, where he had been a brilliant but unhappy pupil. This dramatic and impulsive act marked the beginning of his years of wandering — first through North [[wales|Wales]], then to [[london|London]] — and formed the autobiographical prelude to the opium narrative of the *[[confessions-of-an-english-opium-eater|Confessions]]*.

## The Escape

De Quincey had been placed at Manchester Grammar School by his guardians after his father's death. The school was one of the best in England, and De Quincey excelled there, particularly in Greek and Latin. But he chafed under the institutional discipline and felt increasingly stifled. After a failed attempt to persuade his guardians to let him leave, he simply walked out — with a small amount of money and a volume of [[euripides]] in his pocket.

## Wales and London

After leaving Manchester, De Quincey wandered through North Wales, staying in cottages and living cheaply among the Welsh peasantry. When his money ran low, he made his way to London, where he entered the period of extreme poverty that would later form the most harrowing section of the *Confessions* — his time on the streets, his friendship with [[ann|Ann]], and his descent into near-starvation.

De Quincey describes his departure in characteristically vivid detail:

> ..."with Providence my guide," I set off on foot, carrying a small parcel with some articles of dress under my arm; a favourite English poet in one pocket, and a small 12mo volume, containing about nine plays of Euripides, in the other.
>
> (002-180)

## Significance

The flight from school is the origin point of De Quincey's narrative of self-determination and suffering. It established the pattern — intellectual precocity combined with social alienation — that would characterize his entire life, and that [[opium]] would both soothe and exacerbate.
