## Appearances in the Text

Liverpool appears in the *Confessions* as a brief but revealing detail within De Quincey's account of his Welsh subsistence. During the period when he was living on blackberries, hips, haws, and casual hospitality, he earned his keep by writing letters for the local cottagers: "Sometimes I wrote letters of business for cottagers who happened to have relatives in Liverpool or in London" (003-101). He also wrote love-letters for young women who had worked as servants in Shrewsbury and other English border towns.

The pairing of Liverpool with London in this passage is significant. These two cities — the great Atlantic port and the imperial capital — represent the poles of the commercial geography from which De Quincey had temporarily exiled himself. The Welsh cottagers' relatives in Liverpool were likely engaged in the maritime trade that made the city one of the busiest ports in the world; their relatives in London belonged to the metropolitan world De Quincey would soon re-enter. By serving as an amanuensis for these provincial families, the young scholar occupied a liminal position: literate enough to bridge the gap between rural Wales and urban England, but reduced to writing other people's letters for his supper.

In the broader architecture of the *Confessions*, Liverpool is a footnote — a name glimpsed in passing — but it belongs to the same network of English cities (Manchester, Birmingham, Bristol, Bath) that form the commercial and geographic backdrop against which De Quincey's personal drama unfolds. Each city represents a node in the economic system that the young runaway had, for a time, escaped.
