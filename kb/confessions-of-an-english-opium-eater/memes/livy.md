# Livy

**Titus Livius** (59 BCE – 17 CE), known in English as Livy, was a Roman historian who wrote *Ab Urbe Condita* (From the Founding of the City), a monumental history of Rome from its legendary origins to the reign of Augustus.

## References in the *Confessions*

De Quincey twice invokes Livy in [Section 13](Section-13) in connection with his opium dreams. He describes himself as a long-time admirer of the Roman historian, and reflects on the solemn, almost musical power of certain Latin phrases in Livy's prose:

> I had been in youth, and even since, for occasional amusement, a great reader of Livy, whom I confess that I prefer, both for style and matter, to any other of the Roman historians; and I had often felt as most solemn and appalling sounds, and most emphatically representative of the majesty of the Roman people, the two words so often occurring in Livy—*Consul Romanus*, especially when the consul is introduced in his military character.
>
> (013-050)

De Quincey compares the scale and emotional weight of his dream-visions to Livy's descriptions of Rome's greatest crises, particularly the Second Punic War and the terror Hannibal inspired. For De Quincey, Livy's history represents the ultimate standard of tragic national catastrophe — a register of collective suffering against which individual pain must be measured.

## Livy and De Quincey's Dream-Aesthetics

De Quincey's preference for Livy over other Roman historians is revealing. He does not choose Tacitus, with his compressed cynicism, nor Sallust, with his moralising brevity. He chooses Livy — the historian of vast scale, of "solemn and appalling sounds," of the *Consul Romanus* in his military majesty. What De Quincey values in Livy is not accuracy or analysis but grandeur: the sheer rhetorical weight of Roman power rendered in prose. This preference maps directly onto De Quincey's own dream-aesthetics in the *Confessions*, where architectural vastness — Piranesi's infinite staircases, the "vast processions" that "passed along in mournful pomp" — substitutes for narrative logic. Livy is the historian who writes history as spectacle, and it is precisely this quality that makes him the aptest classical analogue for De Quincey's dream-prose.

The reader of Livy, De Quincey confesses, is someone "whom I confess that I prefer" — and the word "confess" here is no accident. In a book of confessions, the confession of literary taste is itself a confession of sensibility. To prefer Livy is to prefer the sublime over the merely accurate, the majestic over the merely true.
