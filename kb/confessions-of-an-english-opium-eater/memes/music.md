# Music

## Definition

De Quincey devotes a striking philosophical digression in Section 8 to the nature of **music** and its effects on the listener. This passage, though ostensibly a tangent, is central to his argument about opium's effects: music exemplifies the kind of intellectual pleasure that opium enhances — an active engagement of the mind rather than passive sensory reception. "For music is an intellectual or a sensual pleasure according to the temperament of him who hears it" (008-171). By positioning music as an *intellectual* pleasure, De Quincey aligns it with the kind of experience that opium intensifies, reinforcing his claim that the drug sharpens rather than dulls the faculties.

## De Quincey's Theory of Music

### The Active Mind

De Quincey challenges the common assumption "that it is by the ear they communicate with music, and therefore that they are purely passive to its effects. But this is not so; it is by the reaction of the mind upon the notices of the ear (the *matter* coming by the senses, the *form* from the mind) that the pleasure is constructed" (008-179). This Kantian formulation — matter from sensation, form from the understanding — elevates musical experience from mere sensory titillation to an act of intellectual construction.

### Sir Thomas Browne and Literary Precedent

De Quincey acknowledges the rarity of serious writing on music: "with the exception of the fine extravaganza on that subject in 'Twelfth Night,' I do not recollect more than one thing said adequately on the subject of music in all literature; it is a passage in the *Religio Medici* of Sir T. Brown" (008-175). Browne's passage, he notes, "though chiefly remarkable for its sublimity, has also a philosophic value, inasmuch as it points to the true theory of musical effects" (008-177).

### Ideas Without Concepts

When a friend objects that a "succession of musical sounds is to me like a collection of Arabic characters; I can attach no ideas to them," De Quincey retorts: "Ideas! my good sir? There is no occasion for them; all that class of ideas which can be available in such a case has a language of representative feelings" (008-189). This insight — that music communicates through feeling rather than conceptual content — anticipates 19th-century musical aesthetics.
