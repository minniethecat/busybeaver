# Childhood

**Childhood** occupies a central place in Thomas De Quincey's *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* as both a biographical period and a psychological concept. De Quincey presents his early years — the death of his father (002-001), the loss of his sister Elizabeth, his solitude at school — as experiences that shaped his later sensibility and his relationship with opium.

In the Romantic tradition, De Quincey treats childhood as a period of heightened perception and vulnerability. His autobiographical narrative traces a direct line from childhood grief to adult addiction, establishing a psychological framework that anticipates modern understandings of trauma and dependency.

## In the Confessions

De Quincey's recollections of childhood in the *Confessions* focus particularly on the period following his father's death, when he was sent to live with guardians. The emotional deprivation and intellectual precocity of these years form what he later called "the deep, deep tragedies of infancy."
## Childhood Phantoms

De Quincey describes a visual faculty originating in childhood that was later reawakened by opium — the ability to project vivid images upon the darkness of closed eyes:

> I know not whether my reader is aware that many children, perhaps most, have a power of painting, as it were upon the darkness, all sorts of phantoms. In some that power is simply a mechanical affection of the eye; others have a voluntary or semi-voluntary power to dismiss or to summon them; or, as a child once said to me when I questioned him on this matter, "I can tell them to go, and they go ——, but sometimes they come when I don't tell them to come."
>
> (012-142)

This faculty, which De Quincey initially treats as a curiosity of childhood physiology, becomes, under the influence of opium, a source of terror and sublimity.
