## Appearances in the Text

Manchester enters the *Confessions* not as a setting for narrative events but as a piece of documentary evidence in De Quincey's sociological argument about the spread of opium-eating. In the opening section, he challenges the assumption that opium use is confined to London, citing firsthand testimony from the industrial north: "some years ago, on passing through Manchester, I was informed by several cotton manufacturers that their workpeople were rapidly getting into the practice of opium-eating; so much so, that on a Saturday afternoon the counters of the druggists were strewed with pills of one, two, or three grains, in preparation for the known demand" (001-096).

This passage is remarkable on several counts. It provides one of the earliest documented observations of recreational opiate use among the English industrial working class — a phenomenon that would later become the subject of significant social concern. It also reveals the economic geography of opium distribution: Manchester's cotton workers, earning wages in the new industrial economy, had both the means and the motive to purchase the drug that De Quincey himself consumed. The detail of druggists preparing pills in advance for the Saturday pay-day rush paints a vivid picture of a demand-side market that was regular, predictable, and entirely legal.

De Quincey deploys this Manchester evidence strategically within his broader argument that opium-eating was far more widespread than his readers might suppose (001-089), undermining the notion that his own consumption was exceptional or eccentric. In the rhetorical economy of the *Confessions*, Manchester serves as the provincial corroboration of a London truth: that England, unbeknownst to itself, was already a nation of opium-eaters.
