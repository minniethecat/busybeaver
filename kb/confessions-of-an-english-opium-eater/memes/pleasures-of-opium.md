# Pleasures of Opium

## Definition

The **pleasures of opium** form the first half of De Quincey's dialectical account, explicitly set against the later pains. Far from the lurid sensationalism that a modern reader might expect, De Quincey's description of opium's pleasures emphasises intellectual clarity, emotional serenity, and aesthetic enhancement — the drug as a tool for thought and appreciation rather than escape. The pleasures are "of a grave and solemn complexion" (007-152); the opium-eater "speaks and thinks as becomes *Il Penseroso*" (007-155). This gravitas is essential to De Quincey's polemical purpose: to rescue opium-eating from the category of mere debauchery.

## In the *Confessions*

### The First Revelation

De Quincey's first experience of opium was transformative. Within an hour of taking the tincture: "what a revulsion! what an upheaving, from its lowest depths, of inner spirit! what an apocalypse of the world within me!" (007-140). The language is explicitly religious — *apocalypse* as revelation — positioning opium not as a narcotic stupor but as an instrument of expanded consciousness. "Here was a panacea, a φαρμακον for all human woes; here was the secret of happiness, about which philosophers had disputed for so many ages, at once discovered" (007-145).

### Intellectual Enhancement

A recurring theme in De Quincey's description of opium's pleasures is their compatibility with intellectual activity. Under opium's influence, he "read Kant again, and again I understood him" (010-078). The drug does not stupefy but clarifies; it "introduces amongst [the mental faculties] the most exquisite order, legislation, and harmony" (008-021). This claim — that opium is a nootropic, not a narcotic — is perhaps the most radical element of De Quincey's defence.

### The Steady Pleasure

Crucially, opium's pleasure is not acute and transient like wine's but "stationary for eight or ten hours" (008-016). It is "a steady and equable glow" (008-019), not a flame. De Quincey emphasises this stability to distinguish the opium-eater from the drunkard: the opium-eater's pleasure is consistent, dignified, and compatible with productive life — at least in the early stages.

## Key Quotations

> I took it — and in an hour — oh, heavens! what a revulsion! what an upheaving, from its lowest depths, of inner spirit! what an apocalypse of the world within me! (007-140)

> Here was a panacea, a φαρμακον for all human woes; here was the secret of happiness, about which philosophers had disputed for so many ages, at once discovered: happiness might now be bought for a penny, and carried in the waistcoat pocket. (007-145)

> [Opium's] pleasures even are of a grave and solemn complexion, and in his happiest state the opium-eater cannot present himself in the character of *L'Allegro*: even then he speaks and thinks as becomes *Il Penseroso*. (007-152)
