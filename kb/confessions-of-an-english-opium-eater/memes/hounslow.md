# Hounslow

## Geographic Identity

Hounslow is a town approximately ten miles west of central London, situated on the Bath Road — one of the principal coaching routes out of the capital. In the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, Hounslow Heath (the extensive common land adjoining the town) held a notorious reputation as a haunt of highwaymen and footpads, making it a byword for the dangers of travel in the metropolitan hinterland.

## Role in the Confessions

Hounslow appears in the *Confessions* during De Quincey's account of his early financial negotiations and wanderings. After receiving money from the Jewish money-lender D——, De Quincey travelled toward Eton — but his body, weakened by privation and opium, could not sustain the journey. Hounslow marks the point at which physical exhaustion overtook him:

> Hounslow (for in spite of my wishes and my efforts I had fallen asleep on the coach) was the place at which I was compelled to stop. (005-181)

He was not permitted to continue because his body — and the effects of opium upon it — had reached their limit. The passage records a kind of threshold experience: the narrator drifting between sleeping and waking states, only partially aware of his surroundings. He records that "the next time after leaving Hounslow that I fully awoke was upon a summer morning, lying upon the step of a door in Soho Square." (005-189)

## Hounslow Heath

De Quincey explicitly invokes the Heath's dark reputation in connection with the perils that surrounded him. He notes the constant threat of violence: the danger of being "before a murder committed on or near Hounslow Heath." (005-204) The heath serves as the geographical correlative of his psychological state — a place of exposure and vulnerability where the civilized protections of London have fallen away and the narrator is reduced to his most elemental condition.

## Narrative Significance

Hounslow functions in the *Confessions* as a liminal space — neither London nor the countryside, neither safety nor confirmed danger. It is the geographical expression of De Quincey's suspended existence during his early opium period: caught between destinations, between social identities, between consciousness and the oblivion that opium alternately promises and threatens. (005-181)(005-189)(005-204)
