# London

**London** is the gravitational centre of the *Confessions* — the city where [[Thomas De Quincey]]'s [[Opium|opium]] narrative begins, intensifies, and reaches its most harrowing depths. It is simultaneously a real, mappable metropolis of early 19th-century England and a psychological labyrinth that mirrors the architecture of the [[Opium Dreams|opium dreamscape]]. Here De Quincey first purchased laudanum from a druggist on [[Oxford Street]]; here he wandered the streets in hunger and poverty as a sixteen-year-old runaway; here he met and lost [[Ann]], the young prostitute whose disappearance haunts the entire work. London in the *Confessions* is not the glittering Regency city of high society but a nocturnal, marginal London experienced from the streets.

## In the *Confessions*

### The City of First Opium

De Quincey's introduction to opium took place in London in the autumn of 1804, during a visit from [[Oxford]]: "I was in London, having come thither for the first time since my entrance at college" (007-094). On a rainy Sunday afternoon, he purchased a tincture of opium from a druggist's shop near the Pantheon on Oxford Street — a transaction he elevates to cosmic significance:

> The druggist, unconscious minister of celestial pleasures! — as if in sympathy with the rainy Sunday, looked dull and stupid, just as any mortal druggist might be expected to look on a Sunday. (007-125)

London druggists recur as authorities on opium-eating's prevalence. De Quincey cites "three respectable London druggists, in widely remote quarters of London" (001-088).

### Oxford Street and Ann

Oxford Street is the emotional epicentre. It is where De Quincey "paced the terraces of Oxford Street by night" (004-034) during his early poverty, where he searched for the lost [[Ann]] "on the steps of the house in Oxford Street" (004-084), and where the geometry of grief was written into the city's map:

> If she lived, doubtless we must have been sometimes in search of each other, at the very same moment, through the mighty labyrinths of London; perhaps even within a few feet of each other — a barrier no wider than a London street, often amounting in the end to a separation for eternity! (004-125)

### The Labyrinth

London functions as a labyrinth — a space of wandering and disorientation that prefigures the architecture of the opium dreams. The city's vastness maps onto the Piranesian dreamscapes of Sections 12–14. London is the waking rehearsal for the opium nightmare.
