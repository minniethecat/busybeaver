# Suffering

## Definition

**Suffering** is the experiential bedrock of the *Confessions*. De Quincey's narrative is not merely *about* suffering — it is structured *by* it. Physical pain (rheumatism, gastric distress) provided the medical rationale for his initial opium use; psychological suffering (melancholy, nightmare, the loss of Ann) deepened his dependence; and the redemptive arc of the confession depends on the reader's recognition that the author has endured exceptional torment. "I have brought upon myself all the sufferings which" opium can inflict (001-170), he acknowledges — but this very acknowledgement is the ground of his authority.

## In the *Confessions*

### Physical Pain as Gateway

De Quincey's first use of opium was in response to "excruciating rheumatic pains of the head and face" (007-102). He is emphatic that "it was not for the purpose of creating pleasure, but of mitigating pain in the severest degree, that I first began to use opium as an article of daily diet" (001-180). The physical "sufferings which first produced this desperate turn" (001-190) transformed an occasional indulgence into a medical necessity — a distinction crucial to De Quincey's moral defence.

### The Stages of Suffering

The narrative traces a progression through increasingly severe stages. De Quincey describes entering "a fiercer stage of my long sufferings" (003-164) as his dependence deepened. He distinguishes "the former part of my sufferings" (003-176) from their later intensification, when "from the length of my sufferings" (003-181) the experience became qualitatively different. This clinical precision in documenting the trajectory of suffering is part of what makes the *Confessions* a pioneering text in the literature of addiction.

### Suffering as Narrative Structure

The arc from suffering-through-pain to suffering-through-dependence to suffering-through-dreams gives the *Confessions* its three-part architecture. Suffering is not merely described but *demonstrated* through the progressive darkening of the narrative tone — from the relatively buoyant early chapters to the nightmarish visions of the later sections.

## Key Quotations

> I have brought upon myself all the sufferings which I detail. (001-170)

> [I entered] a fiercer stage of my long sufferings. (003-164)

> During the former part of my sufferings... from the length of my sufferings, I began to experience a new and more terrible species of torment. (003-176)
