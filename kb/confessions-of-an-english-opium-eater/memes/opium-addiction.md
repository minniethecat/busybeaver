# Opium Addiction

**Opium addiction** — or the "opium habit," as De Quincey called it — is the central subject of the *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater*. De Quincey's account is one of the earliest and most influential first-person narratives of substance dependence in Western literature.

## De Quincey's Account

De Quincey describes the trajectory of his opium use in three phases:

- **The Pleasures**: Initial use, characterized by euphoria, intellectual stimulation, and aesthetic enhancement. De Quincey insists that opium does not intoxicate like alcohol but instead returns the user to a natural state of equilibrium and well-being.
- **The Habit**: Gradual escalation from occasional to daily use, driven partly by physical pain (rheumatism, neuralgia) and partly by psychological craving. The "habit" becomes self-perpetuating.
- **The Pains**: The later stages, marked by diminishing returns, increasing dosage, horrific dreams, and the agonizing prospect of withdrawal.

De Quincey stresses the gravity of opium's pleasures, distinguishing them sharply from the levity of alcohol:

> ...nobody will laugh long who deals much with opium: its pleasures even are of a grave and solemn complexion, and in his happiest state the opium-eater cannot present himself in the character of *L'Allegro*: even then he speaks and thinks as becomes *Il Penseroso*.
>
> (007-152)

## Withdrawal and Tolerance

De Quincey provides detailed descriptions of the physical misery of opium withdrawal — sweating, trembling, gastric distress, and profound depression. He also notes the development of tolerance, requiring ever-larger doses to achieve the same effect, while acknowledging that total abstinence was for him an impossibility.

## Historical Significance

The *Confessions* was among the first works to present addiction as a psychological and physiological condition worthy of serious literary treatment. Its influence can be traced through Baudelaire, Coleridge (another opium user), and into modern addiction narratives and medical discourse.
## Pain and Dependence

De Quincey is explicit that his descent into daily opium use was driven by physical suffering rather than the pursuit of pleasure:

> It was not for the purpose of creating pleasure, but of mitigating pain in the severest degree, that I first began to use opium as an article of daily diet. In the twenty-eighth year of my age a most painful affection of the stomach, which I had first experienced about ten years before, attacked me in great strength.
>
> (001-180)

This distinction — between recreational and medicinal use — was central to De Quincey's self-presentation. By framing his addiction as a response to genuine physical affliction, he sought to distinguish himself from the merely dissipated and to claim a measure of sympathy from his readers.
