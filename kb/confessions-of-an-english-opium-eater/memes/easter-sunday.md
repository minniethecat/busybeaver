# Easter Sunday

## Definition

**Easter Sunday** appears in Section 14 of the *Confessions* not as a theological argument but as a temporal and emotional anchor — a specific date around which De Quincey's dream-memories crystallise. The day carries multiple resonances: the Christian festival of resurrection (echoing the opium-eater's hope of self-conquest and rebirth), the association with springtime and childhood, and the personal grief De Quincey attaches to it. Its appearance in the dream-text is characteristically oblique — the reader must piece together the significance from scattered references — yet it functions as one of the few fixed temporal points in the otherwise fluid chronology of the opium visions.

## In the *Confessions*

### The Dream Context

In Section 14, De Quincey describes a dream-sequence set on "a Sunday morning in May, that it was Easter Sunday" (014-057). The specificity of the date — Easter Sunday in May — creates an impossible combination (Easter cannot fall in May), signalling that we are in dream-logic rather than calendar-logic. The dream superimposes temporalities: "Easter Sunday; and that is the day of" (014-072) — a sentence that trails off, its completion deferred by the associative structure of the dream.

### Resurrection and Grief

Easter's theological meaning — resurrection — resonates with De Quincey's narrative of self-conquest. Just as Christ rises from death, the author rises from his "accursed chain" of opium dependence. But the resurrection is incomplete, shadowed by grief: "there also it was Easter Sunday" (014-080) in a different dream-context, suggesting the day's recurrence is not redemptive but obsessive. The resurrection promise is inseparable from the loss that necessitated it.

### A Temporal Anchor

In the dream-sections, time is typically fluid — years contract to moments, moments expand to eons. Easter Sunday is one of the few specific temporal references, grounding the otherwise boundless dream-world in the calendar of ordinary life. This anchoring function is characteristic of De Quincey's method: the sublime is always tethered to the specific.

## Key Quotations

> [It was] a Sunday morning in May, that it was Easter Sunday. (014-057)

> Easter Sunday; and that is the day of... (014-072)

> [T]here also it was Easter Sunday. (014-080)
