[007-003]PART II

[007-004]From the [[london-magazine|London Magazine]] for October 1821.

[007-005]So then, [[oxford-street|Oxford Street]], stony-hearted step-mother! thou that listenest

[007-006]to the sighs of orphans and drinkest the tears of children, at length I

[007-007]was dismissed from thee; the time was come at last that I no more

[007-008]should pace in anguish thy never-ending terraces, no more should dream

[007-009]and wake in captivity to the pangs of hunger. Successors too many, to

[007-010]myself and [[ann|Ann]], have doubtless since then trodden in our footsteps,

[007-011]inheritors of our calamities; other orphans than Ann have sighed; tears

[007-012]have been shed by other children; and thou, [[oxford|Oxford]] Street, hast since

[007-013]doubtless echoed to the groans of innumerable hearts. For myself,

[007-014]however, the storm which I had outlived seemed to have been the pledge

[007-015]of a long fair-weather—the premature sufferings which I had paid down

[007-016]to have been accepted as a ransom for many years to come, as a price of

[007-017]long immunity from sorrow; and if again I walked in [[london|London]] a solitary

[007-018]and contemplative man (as oftentimes I did), I walked for the most part

[007-019]in serenity and peace of mind. And although it is true that the

[007-020]calamities of my noviciate in London had struck root so deeply in my

[007-021]bodily constitution, that afterwards they shot up and flourished

[007-022]afresh, and grew into a noxious umbrage that has overshadowed and

[007-023]darkened my latter years, yet these second assaults of suffering were

[007-024]met with a fortitude more confirmed, with the resources of a maturer

[007-025]intellect, and with alleviations from sympathising affection—how deep

[007-026]and tender!

[007-027]Thus, however, with whatsoever alleviations, years that were far

[007-028]asunder were bound together by subtle links of suffering derived from a

[007-029]common root. And herein I notice an instance of the short-sightedness

[007-030]of human desires, that oftentimes on moonlight nights, during my first

[007-031]mournful abode in London, my consolation was (if such it could be

[007-032]thought) to gaze from Oxford Street up every avenue in succession which

[007-033]pierces through the heart of Marylebone to the fields and the woods;

[007-034]for _that_, said I, travelling with my eyes up the long vistas which

[007-035]lay part in light and part in shade, “_that_ is the road to the North,

[007-036]and therefore to, and if I had the wings of a dove, _that_ way I would

[007-037]fly for comfort.” Thus I said, and thus I wished, in my blindness. Yet

[007-038]even in that very northern region it was, even in that very valley,

[007-039]nay, in that very house to which my erroneous wishes pointed, that this

[007-040]second birth of my sufferings began, and that they again threatened to

[007-041]besiege the citadel of life and hope. There it was that for years I was

[007-042]persecuted by visions as ugly, and as ghastly phantoms as ever haunted

[007-043]the couch of an Orestes; and in this unhappier than he, that sleep,

[007-044]which comes to all as a respite and a restoration, and to him

[007-045]especially as a blessed {7} balm for his wounded heart and his haunted

[007-046]brain, visited me as my bitterest scourge. Thus blind was I in my

[007-047]desires; yet if a veil interposes between the dim-sightedness of man

[007-048]and his future calamities, the same veil hides from him their

[007-049]alleviations, and a grief which had not been feared is met by

[007-050]consolations which had not been hoped. I therefore, who participated,

[007-051]as it were, in the troubles of Orestes (excepting only in his agitated

[007-052]conscience), participated no less in all his supports. My Eumenides,

[007-053]like his, were at my bed-feet, and stared in upon me through the

[007-054]curtains; but watching by my pillow, or defrauding herself of sleep to

[007-055]bear me company through the heavy watches of the night, sate my

[007-056]Electra; for thou, beloved M., dear companion of my later years, thou

[007-057]wast my Electra! and neither in nobility of mind nor in long-suffering

[007-058]affection wouldst permit that a Grecian sister should excel an English

[007-059]wife. For thou thoughtest not much to stoop to humble offices of

[007-060]kindness and to servile {8} ministrations of tenderest affection—to

[007-061]wipe away for years the unwholesome dews upon the forehead, or to

[007-062]refresh the lips when parched and baked with fever; nor even when thy

[007-063]own peaceful slumbers had by long sympathy become infected with the

[007-064]spectacle of my dread contest with phantoms and shadowy enemies that

[007-065]oftentimes bade me “sleep no more!”—not even then didst thou utter a

[007-066]complaint or any murmur, nor withdraw thy angelic smiles, nor shrink

[007-067]from thy service of love, more than Electra did of old. For she too,

[007-068]though she was a Grecian woman, and the daughter of the king {9} of

[007-069]men, yet wept sometimes, and hid her face {10} in her robe.

[007-070]But these troubles are past; and thou wilt read records of a period so

[007-071]dolorous to us both as the legend of some hideous dream that can return

[007-072]no more. Meantime, I am again in London, and again I pace the terraces

[007-073]of Oxford Street by night; and oftentimes, when I am oppressed by

[007-074]anxieties that demand all my philosophy and the comfort of thy presence

[007-075]to support, and yet remember that I am separated from thee by three

[007-076]hundred miles and the length of three dreary months, I look up the

[007-077]streets that run northwards from Oxford Street, upon moonlight nights,

[007-078]and recollect my youthful ejaculation of anguish; and remembering that

[007-079]thou art sitting alone in that same valley, and mistress of that very

[007-080]house to which my heart turned in its blindness nineteen years ago, I

[007-081]think that, though blind indeed, and scattered to the winds of late,

[007-082]the promptings of my heart may yet have had reference to a remoter

[007-083]time, and may be justified if read in another meaning; and if I could

[007-084]allow myself to descend again to the impotent wishes of childhood, I

[007-085]should again say to myself, as I look to the North, “Oh, that I had the

[007-086]wings of a dove—” and with how just a confidence in thy good and

[007-087]gracious nature might I add the other half of my early ejaculation—“And

[007-088]_that_ way I would fly for comfort!”

[007-089]THE PLEASURES OF OPIUM

[007-090]It is so long since I first took opium that if it had been a trifling

[007-091]incident in my life I might have forgotten its date; but cardinal

[007-092]events are not to be forgotten, and from circumstances connected with

[007-093]it I remember that it must be referred to the autumn of 1804. During

[007-094]that season I was in London, having come thither for the first time

[007-095]since my entrance at college. And my introduction to opium arose in the

[007-096]following way. From an early age I had been accustomed to wash my head

[007-097]in cold water at least once a day: being suddenly seized with

[007-098]toothache, I attributed it to some relaxation caused by an accidental

[007-099]intermission of that practice, jumped out of bed, plunged my head into

[007-100]a basin of cold water, and with hair thus wetted went to sleep. The

[007-101]next morning, as I need hardly say, I awoke with excruciating rheumatic

[007-102]pains of the head and face, from which I had hardly any respite for

[007-103]about twenty days. On the twenty-first day I think it was, and on a

[007-104]Sunday, that I went out into the streets, rather to run away, if

[007-105]possible, from my torments, than with any distinct purpose. By accident

[007-106]I met a college acquaintance, who recommended opium. [[opium|Opium]]! dread agent

[007-107]of unimaginable pleasure and [[suffering|pain]]! I had heard of it as I had of manna

[007-108]or of ambrosia, but no further. How unmeaning a sound was it at that

[007-109]time: what solemn chords does it now strike upon my heart! what

[007-110]heart-quaking vibrations of sad and happy remembrances! Reverting for a

[007-111]moment to these, I feel a mystic importance attached to the minutest

[007-112]circumstances connected with the place and the time and the man (if man

[007-113]he was) that first laid open to me the Paradise of Opium-eaters. It was

[007-114]a Sunday afternoon, wet and cheerless: and a duller spectacle this

[007-115]earth of ours has not to show than a rainy Sunday in London. My road

[007-116]homewards lay through Oxford Street; and near “the stately Pantheon”

[007-117](as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it) I saw a druggist’s shop.

[007-118]The druggist—unconscious minister of celestial pleasures!—as if in

[007-119]sympathy with the rainy Sunday, looked dull and stupid, just as any

[007-120]mortal druggist might be expected to look on a Sunday; and when I asked

[007-121]for the [[laudanum|tincture of opium]], he gave it to me as any other man might do,

[007-122]and furthermore, out of my shilling returned me what seemed to be real

[007-123]copper halfpence, taken out of a real wooden drawer. Nevertheless, in

[007-124]spite of such indications of humanity, he has ever since existed in my

[007-125]mind as the beatific vision of an immortal druggist, sent down to earth

[007-126]on a special mission to myself. And it confirms me in this way of

[007-127]considering him, that when I next came up to London I sought him near

[007-128]the stately Pantheon, and found him not; and thus to me, who knew not

[007-129]his name (if indeed he had one), he seemed rather to have vanished from

[007-130]Oxford Street than to have removed in any bodily fashion. The reader

[007-131]may choose to think of him as possibly no more than a sublunary

[007-132]druggist; it may be so, but my faith is better—I believe him to have

[007-133]evanesced, {11} or evaporated. So unwillingly would I connect any

[007-134]mortal remembrances with that hour, and place, and creature, that first

[007-135]brought me acquainted with the celestial drug.

[007-136]Arrived at my lodgings, it may be supposed that I lost not a moment in

[007-137]taking the quantity prescribed. I was necessarily ignorant of the whole

[007-138]art and mystery of opium-taking, and what I took I took under every

[007-139]disadvantage. But I took it—and in an hour—oh, heavens! what a

[007-140]revulsion! what an upheaving, from its lowest depths, of inner spirit!

[007-141]what an apocalypse of the world within me! That my pains had vanished

[007-142]was now a trifle in my eyes: this negative effect was swallowed up in

[007-143]the immensity of those [[pleasures-of-opium|positive effects]] which had opened before me—in

[007-144]the abyss of divine enjoyment thus suddenly revealed. Here was a

[007-145]panacea, a φαρμακον for all human woes; here was the secret of

[007-146]happiness, about which philosophers had disputed for so many ages, at

[007-147]once discovered: happiness might now be bought for a penny, and carried

[007-148]in the waistcoat pocket; portable ecstacies might be had corked up in a

[007-149]pint bottle, and peace of mind could be sent down in gallons by the

[007-150]mail-coach. But if I talk in this way the reader will think I am

[007-151]laughing, and I can assure him that nobody will laugh long who deals

[007-152]much with opium: its pleasures even are of a grave and solemn

[007-153]complexion, and in his happiest state the [[opium-eating|opium-eater]] cannot present

[007-154]himself in the character of _L’Allegro_: even then he speaks and thinks

[007-155]as becomes _Il Penseroso_. Nevertheless, I have a very reprehensible

[007-156]way of jesting at times in the midst of my own misery; and unless when

[007-157]I am checked by some more powerful feelings, I am afraid I shall be

[007-158]guilty of this indecent practice even in these annals of suffering or

[007-159]enjoyment. The reader must allow a little to my infirm nature in this

[007-160]respect; and with a few indulgences of that sort I shall endeavour to

[007-161]be as grave, if not drowsy, as fits a theme like opium, so

[007-162]anti-mercurial as it really is, and so drowsy as it is falsely reputed.

[007-163]And first, one word with respect to its bodily effects; for upon all

[007-164]that has been hitherto written on the subject of opium, whether by

[007-165]travellers in Turkey (who may plead their privilege of lying as an old

[007-166]immemorial right), or by professors of medicine, writing _ex cathedra_,

[007-167]I have but one emphatic criticism to pronounce—Lies! lies! lies! I

[007-168]remember once, in passing a book-stall, to have caught these words from

[007-169]a page of some satiric author: “By this time I became convinced that

[007-170]the London newspapers spoke truth at least twice a week, viz., on

[007-171]Tuesday and Saturday, and might safely be depended upon for—the list of

[007-172]bankrupts.” In like manner, I do by no means deny that some truths have

[007-173]been delivered to the world in regard to opium. Thus it has been

[007-174]repeatedly affirmed by the learned that opium is a dusky brown in

[007-175]colour; and this, take notice, I grant. Secondly, that it is rather

[007-176]dear, which also I grant, for in my time East Indian opium has been

[007-177]three guineas a pound, and Turkey eight. And thirdly, that if you eat a

[007-178]good deal of it, most probably you must do what is particularly

[007-179]disagreeable to any man of regular habits, viz., die. {12} These

[007-180]weighty propositions are, all and singular, true: I cannot gainsay

[007-181]them, and truth ever was, and will be, commendable. But in these three

[007-182]theorems I believe we have exhausted the stock of knowledge as yet

[007-183]accumulated by men on the subject of opium.
