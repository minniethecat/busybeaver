[005-001]Soon after the period of the last incident I have recorded I met in

[005-002]Albemarle Street a gentleman of his late Majesty’s household. This

[005-003]gentleman had received hospitalities on different occasions from my

[005-004]family, and he challenged me upon the strength of my family likeness. I

[005-005]did not attempt any disguise; I answered his questions ingenuously,

[005-006]and, on his pledging his word of honour that he would not betray me to

[005-007]my guardians, I gave him an address to my friend the attorney’s. The

[005-008]next day I received from him a £10 bank-note. The letter enclosing it

[005-009]was delivered with other letters of business to the attorney, but

[005-010]though his look and manner informed me that he suspected its contents,

[005-011]he gave it up to me honourably and without demur.

[005-012]This present, from the particular service to which it was applied,

[005-013]leads me naturally to speak of the purpose which had allured me up to

[005-014][[london|London]], and which I had been (to use a forensic word) soliciting from

[005-015]the first day of my arrival in London to that of my final departure.

[005-016]In so mighty a world as London it will surprise my readers that I

[005-017]should not have found some means of starving off the last extremities

[005-018]of penury; and it will strike them that two resources at least must

[005-019]have been open to me—viz., either to seek assistance from the friends

[005-020]of my family, or to turn my youthful talents and attainments into some

[005-021]channel of pecuniary emolument. As to the first course, I may observe

[005-022]generally, that what I dreaded beyond all other evils was the chance of

[005-023]being reclaimed by my guardians; not doubting that whatever power the

[005-024]law gave them would have been enforced against me to the utmost—that

[005-025]is, to the extremity of forcibly restoring me to the school which I had

[005-026]quitted, a restoration which, as it would in my eyes have been a

[005-027]dishonour, even if submitted to voluntarily, could not fail, when

[005-028]extorted from me in contempt and defiance of my own wishes and efforts,

[005-029]to have been a humiliation worse to me than death, and which would

[005-030]indeed have terminated in death. I was therefore shy enough of applying

[005-031]for assistance even in those quarters where I was sure of receiving it,

[005-032]at the risk of furnishing my guardians with any clue of recovering me.

[005-033]But as to London in particular, though doubtless my father had in his

[005-034]lifetime had many friends there, yet (as ten years had passed since his

[005-035]death) I remembered few of them even by name; and never having seen

[005-036]London before, except once for a few hours, I knew not the address of

[005-037]even those few. To this mode of gaining help, therefore, in part the

[005-038]difficulty, but much more the paramount fear which I have mentioned,

[005-039]habitually indisposed me. In regard to the other mode, I now feel half

[005-040]inclined to join my reader in wondering that I should have overlooked

[005-041]it. As a corrector of Greek proofs (if in no other way) I might

[005-042]doubtless have gained enough for my slender wants. Such an office as

[005-043]this I could have discharged with an exemplary and punctual accuracy

[005-044]that would soon have gained me the confidence of my employers. But it

[005-045]must not be forgotten that, even for such an office as this, it was

[005-046]necessary that I should first of all have an introduction to some

[005-047]respectable publisher, and this I had no means of obtaining. To say the

[005-048]truth, however, it had never once occurred to me to think of literary

[005-049]labours as a source of profit. No mode sufficiently speedy of obtaining

[005-050]money had ever occurred to me but that of borrowing it on the strength

[005-051]of my future claims and expectations. This mode I sought by every

[005-052]avenue to compass; and amongst other persons I applied to a Jew named

[005-053]D—— {4}

[005-054]To this Jew, and to other advertising money-lenders (some of whom were,

[005-055]I believe, also Jews), I had introduced myself with an account of my

[005-056]expectations; which account, on examining my father’s will at Doctors’

[005-057]Commons, they had ascertained to be correct. The person there mentioned

[005-058]as the second son of —— was found to have all the claims (or more than

[005-059]all) that I had stated; but one question still remained, which the

[005-060]faces of the Jews pretty significantly suggested—was _I_ that person?

[005-061]This doubt had never occurred to me as a possible one; I had rather

[005-062]feared, whenever my Jewish friends scrutinised me keenly, that I might

[005-063]be too well known to be that person, and that some scheme might be

[005-064]passing in their minds for entrapping me and selling me to my

[005-065]guardians. It was strange to me to find my own self _materialiter_

[005-066]considered (so I expressed it, for I doated on logical accuracy of

[005-067]distinctions), accused, or at least suspected, of counterfeiting my own

[005-068]self _formaliter_ considered. However, to satisfy their scruples, I

[005-069]took the only course in my power. Whilst I was in [[wales|Wales]] I had received

[005-070]various letters from young friends; these I produced, for I carried

[005-071]them constantly in my pocket, being, indeed, by this time almost the

[005-072]only relics of my personal encumbrances (excepting the clothes I wore)

[005-073]which I had not in one way or other disposed of. Most of these letters

[005-074]were from the Earl of ——, who was at that time my chief (or rather

[005-075]only) confidential friend. These letters were dated from [[eton|Eton]]. I had

[005-076]also some from the Marquis of ——, his father, who, though absorbed in

[005-077]agricultural pursuits, yet having been an Etonian himself, and as good

[005-078]a scholar as a nobleman needs to be, still retained an affection for

[005-079]classical studies and for youthful scholars. He had accordingly, from

[005-080]the time that I was fifteen, corresponded with me; sometimes upon the

[005-081]great improvements which he had made or was meditating in the counties

[005-082]of M—— and Sl—— since I had been there, sometimes upon the merits of a

[005-083]Latin poet, and at other times suggesting subjects to me on which he

[005-084]wished me to write verses.

[005-085]On reading the letters, one of my Jewish friends agreed to furnish me

[005-086]with two or three hundred pounds on my personal security, provided I

[005-087]could persuade the young Earl —— who was, by the way, not older than

[005-088]myself—to guarantee the payment on our coming of age; the Jew’s final

[005-089]object being, as I now suppose, not the trifling profit he could expect

[005-090]to make by me, but the prospect of establishing a connection with my

[005-091]noble friend, whose immense expectations were well known to him. In

[005-092]pursuance of this proposal on the part of the Jew, about eight or nine

[005-093]days after I had received the £10, I prepared to go down to Eton.

[005-094]Nearly £3 of the money I had given to my money-lending friend, on his

[005-095]alleging that the stamps must be bought, in order that the writings

[005-096]might be preparing whilst I was away from London. I thought in my heart

[005-097]that he was lying; but I did not wish to give him any excuse for

[005-098]charging his own delays upon me. A smaller sum I had given to my friend

[005-099]the attorney (who was connected with the money-lenders as their

[005-100]lawyer), to which, indeed, he was entitled for his unfurnished

[005-101]lodgings. [[About]] fifteen shillings I had employed in re-establishing

[005-102](though in a very humble way) my dress. Of the remainder I gave one

[005-103]quarter to [[ann|Ann]], meaning on my return to have divided with her whatever

[005-104]might remain. These arrangements made, soon after six o’clock on a dark

[005-105]winter evening I set off, accompanied by Ann, towards Piccadilly; for

[005-106]it was my intention to go down as far as Salthill on the [[bath|Bath]] or

[005-107][[bristol|Bristol]] mail. Our course lay through a part of the town which has now

[005-108]all disappeared, so that I can no longer retrace its ancient

[005-109]boundaries—Swallow Street, I think it was called. Having time enough

[005-110]before us, however, we bore away to the left until we came into Golden

[005-111]Square; there, near the corner of Sherrard Street, we sat down, not

[005-112]wishing to part in the tumult and blaze of Piccadilly. I had told her

[005-113]of my plans some time before, and I now assured her again that she

[005-114]should share in my good fortune, if I met with any, and that I would

[005-115]never forsake her as soon as I had power to protect her. This I fully

[005-116]intended, as much from inclination as from a sense of duty; for setting

[005-117]aside gratitude, which in any case must have made me her debtor for

[005-118]life, I loved her as affectionately as if she had been my sister; and

[005-119]at this moment with sevenfold tenderness, from pity at witnessing her

[005-120]extreme dejection. I had apparently most reason for dejection, because

[005-121]I was leaving the saviour of my life; yet I, considering the shock my

[005-122]health had received, was cheerful and full of hope. She, on the

[005-123]contrary, who was parting with one who had had little means of serving

[005-124]her, except by kindness and brotherly treatment, was overcome by

[005-125]sorrow; so that, when I kissed her at our final farewell, she put her

[005-126]arms about my neck and wept without speaking a word. I hoped to return

[005-127]in a week at farthest, and I agreed with her that on the fifth night

[005-128]from that, and every night afterwards, she would wait for me at six

[005-129]o’clock near the bottom of Great Titchfield Street, which had been our

[005-130]customary haven, as it were, of rendezvous, to prevent our missing each

[005-131]other in the great Mediterranean of [[oxford-street|Oxford Street]]. This and other

[005-132]measures of precaution I took; one only I forgot. She had either never

[005-133]told me, or (as a matter of no great interest) I had forgotten her

[005-134]surname. It is a general practice, indeed, with girls of humble rank in

[005-135]her unhappy condition, not (as novel-reading women of higher

[005-136]pretensions) to style themselves _Miss Douglas_, _Miss Montague_, &c.,

[005-137]but simply by their Christian names—_Mary_, _Jane_, _Frances_, &c. Her

[005-138]surname, as the surest means of tracing her hereafter, I ought now to

[005-139]have inquired; but the truth is, having no reason to think that our

[005-140]meeting could, in consequence of a short interruption, be more

[005-141]difficult or uncertain than it had been for so many weeks, I had

[005-142]scarcely for a moment adverted to it as necessary, or placed it amongst

[005-143]my memoranda against this parting interview; and my final anxieties

[005-144]being spent in comforting her with hopes, and in pressing upon her the

[005-145]necessity of getting some medicines for a violent cough and hoarseness

[005-146]with which she was troubled, I wholly forgot it until it was too late

[005-147]to recall her.

[005-148]It was past eight o’clock when I reached the Gloucester Coffee-house,

[005-149]and the Bristol mail being on the point of going off, I mounted on the

[005-150]outside. The fine fluent motion {5} of this mail soon laid me asleep:

[005-151]it is somewhat remarkable that the first easy or refreshing sleep which

[005-152]I had enjoyed for some months, was on the outside of a mail-coach—a bed

[005-153]which at this day I find rather an uneasy one. Connected with this

[005-154]sleep was a little incident which served, as hundreds of others did at

[005-155]that time, to convince me how easily a man who has never been in any

[005-156]great distress may pass through life without knowing, in his own person

[005-157]at least, anything of the possible goodness of the human heart—or, as I

[005-158]must add with a sigh, of its possible vileness. So thick a curtain of

[005-159]_manners_ is drawn over the features and expression of men’s _natures_,

[005-160]that to the ordinary observer the two extremities, and the infinite

[005-161]field of varieties which lie between them, are all confounded; the vast

[005-162]and multitudinous compass of their several harmonies reduced to the

[005-163]meagre outline of differences expressed in the gamut or alphabet of

[005-164]elementary sounds. The case was this: for the first four or five miles

[005-165]from London I annoyed my fellow-passenger on the roof by occasionally

[005-166]falling against him when the coach gave a lurch to his side: and

[005-167]indeed, if the road had been less smooth and level than it is, I should

[005-168]have fallen off from weakness. Of this annoyance he complained heavily,

[005-169]as perhaps, in the same circumstances, most people would; he expressed

[005-170]his complaint, however, more morosely than the occasion seemed to

[005-171]warrant, and if I had parted with him at that moment I should have

[005-172]thought of him (if I had considered it worth while to think of him at

[005-173]all) as a surly and almost brutal fellow. However, I was conscious that

[005-174]I had given him some cause for complaint, and therefore I apologized to

[005-175]him, and assured him I would do what I could to avoid falling asleep

[005-176]for the future; and at the same time, in as few words as possible, I

[005-177]explained to him that I was ill and in a weak state from long

[005-178]suffering, and that I could not afford at that time to take an inside

[005-179]place. This man’s manner changed, upon hearing this explanation, in an

[005-180]instant; and when I next woke for a minute from the noise and lights of

[005-181]Hounslow (for in spite of my wishes and efforts I had fallen asleep

[005-182]again within two minutes from the time I had spoken to him) I found

[005-183]that he had put his arm round me to protect me from falling off, and

[005-184]for the rest of my journey he behaved to me with the gentleness of a

[005-185]woman, so that at length I almost lay in his arms; and this was the

[005-186]more kind, as he could not have known that I was not going the whole

[005-187]way to Bath or Bristol. Unfortunately, indeed, I _did_ go rather

[005-188]farther than I intended, for so genial and so refreshing was my sleep,

[005-189]that the next time after leaving Hounslow that I fully awoke was upon

[005-190]the sudden pulling up of the mail (possibly at a post-office), and on

[005-191]inquiry I found that we had reached Maidenhead—six or seven miles, I

[005-192]think, ahead of Salthill. Here I alighted, and for the half-minute that

[005-193]the mail stopped I was entreated by my friendly companion (who, from

[005-194]the transient glimpse I had had of him in Piccadilly, seemed to me to

[005-195]be a gentleman’s butler, or person of that rank) to go to bed without

[005-196]delay. This I promised, though with no intention of doing so; and in

[005-197]fact I immediately set forward, or rather backward, on foot. It must

[005-198]then have been nearly midnight, but so slowly did I creep along that I

[005-199]heard a clock in a cottage strike four before I turned down the lane

[005-200]from Slough to Eton. The air and the sleep had both refreshed me; but I

[005-201]was weary nevertheless. I remember a thought (obvious enough, and which

[005-202]has been prettily expressed by a Roman poet) which gave me some

[005-203]consolation at that moment under my poverty. There had been some time

[005-204]before a murder committed on or near Hounslow Heath. I think I cannot

[005-205]be mistaken when I say that the name of the murdered person was

[005-206]_Steele_, and that he was the owner of a lavender plantation in that

[005-207]neighbourhood. Every step of my progress was bringing me nearer to the

[005-208]Heath, and it naturally occurred to me that I and the accused murderer,

[005-209]if he were that night abroad, might at every instant be unconsciously

[005-210]approaching each other through the darkness; in which case, said

[005-211]I—supposing I, instead of being (as indeed I am) little better than an

[005-212]outcast—
