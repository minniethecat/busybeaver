# Collected Works of De Quincey

## Overview

Collected Works of De Quincey (1853) by Thomas De Quincey. De Quincey's broader oeuvre beyond the *Confessions* — his essays on philosophy, political economy, and literary criticism that form the intellectual context for his most famous work.

## In the *Confessions*

This work is referenced in the *Confessions*. Its connection to De Quincey's text is established through the cited passages.

## The *Confessions* in Context

The *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* first appeared in the *London Magazine* in two instalments — September and October 1821 (001-005). At the time of its publication, De Quincey was already an established magazine writer, known for essays on subjects ranging from Kantian philosophy to the aesthetics of murder. The *Confessions* was, in its original context, a magazine piece — sensational, confessional, and serialised — rather than the unified literary work it later became. De Quincey substantially revised and expanded the text for the first collected edition of his writings, published by James Hogg in 1853, adding the dream-visions and the extended reflections on opium's intellectual effects that give the later *Confessions* its architectural grandeur.

The collected edition transformed the *Confessions* from a periodical confection into the centrepiece of a philosophical *oeuvre*. Read alongside De Quincey's essays on political economy (in which he positions himself as a disciple of Ricardo), his literary criticism (the essays on Wordsworth, Coleridge, and Shakespeare), and his philosophical speculations (the unfinished *De Emendatione Humani Intellectus*), the *Confessions* reveals itself as more than drug memoir: it is the autobiographical keystone of a sprawling intellectual project, a life's work in which the opium-eater and the philosopher are the same man.

## Key Works in the Collected Edition

Beyond the *Confessions*, De Quincey's collected writings include:

- *On the Knocking at the Gate in Macbeth* (1823) — a celebrated piece of Shakespeare criticism
- *On Murder Considered as One of the Fine Arts* (1827) — blackly comic essays that established a new genre of ironic true-crime writing
- *Suspiria de Profundis* (1845) — a sequel to the *Confessions*, extending the dream-prose into more explicitly visionary territory
- *The English Mail-Coach* (1849) — a meditation on speed, empire, and the technological sublime
- *Prolegomena to all Future Systems of Political Economy* — the unfinished work De Quincey describes in the *Confessions* as his great abandoned project
