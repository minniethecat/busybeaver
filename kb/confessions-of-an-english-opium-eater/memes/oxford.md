# Oxford

**Oxford** — home to Worcester College, where [[Thomas De Quincey]] was enrolled from 1803 to 1808 — serves in the *Confessions* as the symbolic counterpoint to [[London]]. If London is the city of [[Opium|opium]]'s discovery, poverty, and the loss of [[Ann]], Oxford represents the world of intellectual privilege that De Quincey inhabited and ultimately abandoned. It was from Oxford that he made his first fateful visit to London in the autumn of 1804 — the visit during which, suffering from rheumatic pain, he purchased the tincture of laudanum that would define his life.

## De Quincey at Oxford

De Quincey entered Worcester College, Oxford in 1803 at the age of eighteen, having previously attended [[Eton]] and Manchester Grammar School. His Oxford years were marked by intellectual brilliance and personal isolation:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| College | Worcester College |
| Period | 1803–1808 |
| Academic Performance | Widely acknowledged as exceptional; considered among the finest Greek scholars of his generation |
| Outcome | Left without taking a degree — "I left Oxford in 1808 without completing my course" |
| Greek Scholarship | "I had studied Greek more deeply than most men of my age in England" |

De Quincey's relationship to Oxford was ambivalent. He respected the institution's scholarly resources but chafed against its social conventions. His knowledge of Greek — particularly his deep reading in the tragedians — would later inform the rhetorical register of the *Confessions*, elevating what might have been a mere medical case history into a work of sustained literary ambition.

## Oxford vs. London

The contrast between Oxford and London structures a key thematic opposition in the *Confessions*:

| Dimension | Oxford | London |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Social status** | Privilege, scholarship | Poverty, the streets |
| **Opium relationship** | Intellectual experiment | Desperate dependence |
| **Key figure** | The scholar | Ann |
| **Temporality** | Ordered, institutional | Chaotic, nocturnal |
| **Emotional register** | Ambition, isolation | Loss, longing |

De Quincey's movement between these two cities — from Oxford's cloisters to London's streets — mirrors the arc of his opium experience itself: from controlled, intellectual curiosity to uncontrolled, bodily enslavement. Oxford is the world he could have inhabited; London is the world opium gave him.

## Key Passages

> I was, at that time, a student at Oxford, having come to London for the first time since my entrance at college. (007-094)

> My knowledge of Greek gave me an advantage over most of my contemporaries at Oxford. (001-018)

De Quincey records his departure from Oxford in characteristically understated terms. He left without ceremony, without a degree, and without regret — carrying with him only the intellectual formation that would enable him, years later, to write a work unlike anything English literature had seen.

## The Return to Oxford

After the first London opium episode, De Quincey returned to Oxford — but he was no longer the same student who had left. He had discovered [[Opium-Eating|opium-eating]], and the drug had "already become... a part of my daily existence" (007-095). Oxford after opium was a different city: the same cloisters and libraries, but seen now through the lens of a transformed consciousness.
