# Great Titchfield Street

**Great Titchfield Street** is a street in Marylebone, [[london|London]], running north from [[oxford-street]]. It appears in the *[[confessions-of-an-english-opium-eater|Confessions of an English Opium-Eater]]* as the site of one of the narrative's most poignant episodes: the failed reunion with [[ann|Ann]].

## The Missed Meeting

When [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] had to leave London temporarily in 1803, he arranged to meet Ann upon his return at the corner of Great Titchfield Street. When he came back to London, however, Ann was not there. He returned to the spot repeatedly over many nights, walking the length of Oxford Street and waiting at the appointed corner, but she never appeared. This disappearance — and De Quincey's enduring grief over it — became one of the emotional anchors of the *Confessions*.

## Symbolic Significance

Great Titchfield Street functions in the narrative as a threshold between hope and loss. It is the geographical marker of De Quincey's failure to protect the person who had shown him the most kindness during his destitution — a failure that [[opium]] would later help him both remember and forget.
## The Farewell and the Promise

De Quincey's account of his final parting from Ann, and the arrangement to meet at Great Titchfield Street, is one of the most emotionally charged passages in the *Confessions*:

> ...when I kissed her at our final farewell, she put her arms about my neck and wept without speaking a word. I hoped to return in a week at farthest, and I agreed with her that on the fifth night from that, and every night afterwards, she would wait for me at six o'clock near the bottom of Great Titchfield Street, which had been our customary haven, as it were, of rendezvous, to prevent our missing each other in the great length of Oxford Street.
>
> (005-125)

The street thus became the geographical marker of a promise that would never be fulfilled — the fixed point around which De Quincey's memory of Ann would revolve for decades.
