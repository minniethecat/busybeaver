# Opium-Eating

## Definition

**Opium-eating** was the term De Quincey used to describe the habitual oral consumption of opium — a practice he sharply distinguished from both medicinal use and mere intoxication. In early 19th-century England, opium-eating was a recognised cultural phenomenon, particularly among the educated classes. De Quincey opens the *Confessions* with a direct admission: "If opium-eating be a sensual pleasure, and if I am bound to confess that I have indulged in it to an excess not yet *recorded* of any other man" (001-056). Throughout the text, he constructs the identity of the "opium-eater" as a distinct human type — neither a drunkard nor a mere patient, but a figure defined by a unique relationship between will, pleasure, and suffering.

## In the *Confessions*

### A Social Phenomenon

De Quincey insists that opium-eating was far more widespread than his contemporaries acknowledged. He estimates "the number of those in one small class of English society (the class of men distinguished for talents)" who practised it (001-071), and describes becoming "convinced some years ago by computing at that time the number" of opium-eaters in this demographic (001-073). The practice, he argues, arose from diverse motivations, with some driven to it by pain and others by "the excitement of positive pleasure" (001-067).

### The Author's Own Initiation

De Quincey's initiation into opium-eating was gradual. He describes it as a progression: "for ten years I did occasionally take opium for the sake of the exquisite pleasure it gave me" (001-174), but it was not until later that he "first came to be a regular opium-eater" (001-168). The turning point came through the mitigation of physical pain — "it was not for the purpose of creating pleasure, but of mitigating pain in the severest degree, that I first began to use opium as an article of daily diet" (001-180).

### Defence of the Practice

De Quincey emphatically denies that opium-eating is merely a form of debauchery. "As to the torpor supposed to accompany the practice of opium-eating, I deny that also" (008-123). He challenges the reader's expectations: "You will find my health after all this opium-eating? In short, how do I do? Why, pretty well" (009-188). This defence is part of a larger polemic against what he saw as medical ignorance and moral panic surrounding the drug.

## Key Quotations

> If opium-eating be a sensual pleasure, and if I am bound to confess that I have indulged in it to an excess not yet *recorded* of any other man, it is no less true that I have struggled against this fascinating enthralment with a religious zeal. (001-056)

> It was not for the purpose of creating pleasure, but of mitigating pain in the severest degree, that I first began to use opium as an article of daily diet. (001-180)

> I deny that it is possible for a human being to persist in the practice of opium-eating through a course of years. Every man who eat opium will renounce it before his death. (009-231)


## Literary and Cultural Context

Opium-eating occupied a distinctive place in Romantic-era literary culture. While De Quincey's *Confessions* is the most famous account, he was not alone in his experimentation. Samuel Taylor Coleridge's "Kubla Khan" was famously composed under the influence of opium, and the drug was widely available in the form of laudanum — a tincture of opium in alcohol — throughout the nineteenth century.

De Quincey's innovation was to treat opium-eating not merely as a medical or moral issue but as a gateway to profound psychological experience. His distinction between the "pleasures" and "pains" of opium established a narrative framework that influenced countless later writers, from Baudelaire to Burroughs.
