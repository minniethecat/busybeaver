# Lake District

The **Lake District**, also known as the Lakes, is a mountainous region in Cumbria, northwest England. In the early nineteenth century it became the geographical and symbolic centre of English Romanticism, associated above all with William Wordsworth (001-035), Samuel Taylor Coleridge, and Robert Southey — the so-called "Lake Poets." Thomas De Quincey joined this circle when he took up residence at Dove Cottage in Grasmere.

De Quincey's Lake District cottage became the unlikely setting for one of the *Confessions*' most memorable episodes:

> One day a Malay knocked at my door. What business a Malay could have to transact amongst English mountains I cannot conjecture; but possibly he was on his road to a seaport about forty miles distant.
>
> (010-088)

## De Quincey and the Lakes

De Quincey's relationship with the Lake District was complex. He loved the landscape with a passion that matched Wordsworth's, and his descriptions of the fells, lakes, and winter nights at Dove Cottage are among the most evocative passages in the *Confessions*. Yet his presence in the Lakes was also shadowed by his growing opium habit, his uneasy relationship with Wordsworth (whom he admired but who eventually distanced himself from De Quincey), and the scandal of his private life.

## Literary Landscape

The Lake District in De Quincey's writing is never merely scenery. It is a psychological landscape — the mountains and lakes function as external correlatives for interior states: peace and grandeur, isolation and expansion, the sublime and the terrifying. This treatment of landscape as a map of consciousness is one of De Quincey's most significant contributions to Romantic prose.
