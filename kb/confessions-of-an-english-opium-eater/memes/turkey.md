# Turkey

## Overview

In the *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater*, Turkey appears primarily as a source region for opium — one of the two great supply chains, alongside East India, that fed the British opium market of the early nineteenth century. De Quincey invokes Turkey in connection with travellers, trade routes, and the economics of opium procurement.

## Turkey as an Opium Source

De Quincey draws a commercial distinction between Turkey opium and East Indian opium, noting differences in price and quality:

> ...three guineas a pound, and Turkey eight. (007-177)

The mention appears in the context of the economics of opium — De Quincey is explaining the cost structure that shaped his own consumption habits. Turkey opium, being more expensive than the East Indian variety, occupied a specific niche in the market hierarchy.

## In the *Confessions*

Turkey is referenced in three distinct passages across the *Confessions*:

1. **Travellers in Turkey** (007-165): De Quincey mentions travellers in Turkey who might plead certain privileges — an ethnographic aside that situates Turkey within the broader orientalist imagination of the period.

2. **Opium pricing** (007-177): The explicit comparison between East Indian and Turkey opium prices, anchoring De Quincey's personal economics in the material realities of the global drug trade.

3. **Supply networks** (010-167): De Quincey references "both East India and Turkey" as the commercial channels through which his opium supply was conducted — placing Turkey at the center of the material infrastructure of his addiction.

Together these references establish Turkey not as a dream-space (unlike China and Asia in the later visions) but as a node in the real-world geography of the opium trade — a geography that underlies and enables the entire confessional narrative.

## The Geopolitics of Opium

By distinguishing between Turkey opium and East India opium, De Quincey inadvertently documents the geography of an early nineteenth-century global commodity chain. Turkish opium, cultivated in Anatolia and exported through the Ottoman Empire, reached the British market through Mediterranean trade routes. East India opium, by contrast, was largely a product of the British East India Company's monopoly — cultivated in Bengal and shipped under imperial auspices. The price difference De Quincey notes (three guineas vs. eight guineas per pound) reflects not merely quality but the political economy of empire: the East India Company's opium was cheaper because it was produced under colonial conditions.

De Quincey himself shows no interest in these geopolitical realities — his concern is purely with the drug's effect on his own sensorium. But the *Confessions*, in its documentary aspect, preserves a snapshot of the global drug trade at a pivotal moment, just decades before the Opium Wars would transform China's relationship with the substance and reshape the geopolitics of the drug for the next century.
