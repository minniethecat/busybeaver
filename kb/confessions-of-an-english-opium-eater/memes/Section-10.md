[010-001]This, then, let me repeat, I postulate—that at the time I began to take

[010-002]opium daily I could not have done otherwise. Whether, indeed,

[010-003]afterwards I might not have succeeded in breaking off the habit, even

[010-004]when it seemed to me that all efforts would be unavailing, and whether

[010-005]many of the innumerable efforts which I did make might not have been

[010-006]carried much further, and my gradual reconquests of ground lost might

[010-007]not have been followed up much more energetically—these are questions

[010-008]which I must decline. Perhaps I might make out a case of palliation;

[010-009]but shall I speak ingenuously? I confess it, as a besetting infirmity

[010-010]of mine, that I am too much of an Eudæmonist; I hanker too much after a

[010-011]state of happiness, both for myself and others; I cannot face misery,

[010-012]whether my own or not, with an eye of sufficient firmness, and am

[010-013]little capable of encountering present [[suffering|pain]] for the sake of any

[010-014]reversionary benefit. On some other matters I can agree with the

[010-015]gentlemen in the cotton trade {15} at [[manchester|Manchester]] in affecting the Stoic

[010-016]philosophy, but not in this. Here I take the liberty of an Eclectic

[010-017]philosopher, and I look out for some courteous and considerate sect

[010-018]that will condescend more to the infirm condition of an [[opium-eating|opium-eater]];

[010-019]that are “sweet men,” as Chaucer says, “to give absolution,” and will

[010-020]show some conscience in the penances they inflict, and the efforts of

[010-021]abstinence they exact from poor sinners like myself. An inhuman

[010-022]moralist I can no more endure in my nervous state than opium that has

[010-023]not been boiled. At any rate, he who summons me to send out a large

[010-024]freight of self-denial and mortification upon any cruising voyage of

[010-025]moral improvement, must make it clear to my understanding that the

[010-026]concern is a hopeful one. At my time of life (six-and-thirty years of

[010-027]age) it cannot be supposed that I have much energy to spare; in fact, I

[010-028]find it all little enough for the intellectual labours I have on my

[010-029]hands, and therefore let no man expect to frighten me by a few hard

[010-030]words into embarking any part of it upon desperate adventures of

[010-031]morality.

[010-032]Whether desperate or not, however, the issue of the struggle in 1813

[010-033]was what I have mentioned, and from this date the reader is to consider

[010-034]me as a regular and confirmed opium-eater, of whom to ask whether on

[010-035]any particular day he had or had not taken opium, would be to ask

[010-036]whether his lungs had performed respiration, or the heart fulfilled its

[010-037]functions. You understand now, reader, what I am, and you are by this

[010-038]time aware that no old gentleman “with a snow-white beard” will have

[010-039]any chance of persuading me to surrender “the little golden receptacle

[010-040]of the pernicious drug.” No; I give notice to all, whether moralists or

[010-041]surgeons, that whatever be their pretensions and skill in their

[010-042]respective lines of practice, they must not hope for any countenance

[010-043]from me, if they think to begin by any savage proposition for a Lent or

[010-044]a Ramadan of abstinence from opium. This, then, being all fully

[010-045]understood between us, we shall in future sail before the wind. Now

[010-046]then, reader, from 1813, where all this time we have been sitting down

[010-047]and loitering, rise up, if you please, and walk forward about three

[010-048]years more. Now draw up the curtain, and you shall see me in a new

[010-049]character.

[010-050]If any man, poor or rich, were to say that he would tell us what had

[010-051]been the happiest day in his life, and the why and the wherefore, I

[010-052]suppose that we should all cry out—Hear him! Hear him! As to the

[010-053]happiest _day_, that must be very difficult for any wise man to name,

[010-054]because any event that could occupy so distinguished a place in a man’s

[010-055]retrospect of his life, or be entitled to have shed a special felicity

[010-056]on any one day, ought to be of such an enduring character as that

[010-057](accidents apart) it should have continued to shed the same felicity,

[010-058]or one not distinguishably less, on many years together. To the

[010-059]happiest _lustrum_, however, or even to the happiest _year_, it may be

[010-060]allowed to any man to point without discountenance from wisdom. This

[010-061]year, in my case, reader, was the one which we have now reached; though

[010-062]it stood, I confess, as a parenthesis between years of a gloomier

[010-063]character. It was a year of brilliant water (to speak after the manner

[010-064]of jewellers), set as it were, and insulated, in the [[melancholy|gloom]] and cloudy

[010-065]melancholy of opium. Strange as it may sound, I had a little before

[010-066]this time descended suddenly, and without any considerable effort, from

[010-067]320 grains of opium (_i.e_. eight {16} thousand drops of [[opium|laudanum]]) per

[010-068]day, to forty grains, or one-eighth part. Instantaneously, and as if by

[010-069]magic, the cloud of profoundest melancholy which rested upon my brain,

[010-070]like some black vapours that I have seen roll away from the summits of

[010-071]mountains, drew off in one day (νυχθημερον); passed off with its murky

[010-072]banners as simultaneously as a ship that has been stranded, and is

[010-073]floated off by a spring tide—

[010-074]That moveth altogether, if it move at all.

[010-075]Now, then, I was again happy; I now took only 1000 drops of laudanum

[010-076]per day; and what was that? A latter spring had come to close up the

[010-077]season of youth; my brain performed its functions as healthily as ever

[010-078]before; I read Kant again, and again I understood him, or fancied that

[010-079]I did. Again my feelings of pleasure expanded themselves to all around

[010-080]me; and if any man from [[oxford|Oxford]] or [[cambridge|Cambridge]], or from neither, had been

[010-081]announced to me in my unpretending cottage, I should have welcomed him

[010-082]with as sumptuous a reception as so poor a man could offer. Whatever

[010-083]else was wanting to a wise man’s happiness, of laudanum I would have

[010-084]given him as much as he wished, and in a golden cup. And, by the way,

[010-085]now that I speak of giving laudanum away, I remember about this time a

[010-086]little incident, which I mention because, trifling as it was, the

[010-087]reader will soon meet it again in my [[opium-dreams|dreams]], which it influenced more

[010-088]fearfully than could be imagined. One day a Malay knocked at my door.

[010-089]What business a Malay could have to transact amongst English mountains

[010-090]I cannot conjecture; but possibly he was on his road to a seaport about

[010-091]forty miles distant.

[010-092]The servant who opened the door to him was a young girl, born and bred

[010-093]amongst the mountains, who had never seen an Asiatic dress of any sort;

[010-094]his turban therefore confounded her not a little; and as it turned out

[010-095]that his attainments in English were exactly of the same extent as hers

[010-096]in [[malay-incident|the Malay]], there seemed to be an impassable gulf fixed between all

[010-097]communication of ideas, if either party had happened to possess any. In

[010-098]this dilemma, the girl, recollecting the reputed learning of her master

[010-099](and doubtless giving me credit for a knowledge of all the languages of

[010-100]the earth besides perhaps a few of the lunar ones), came and gave me to

[010-101]understand that there was a sort of demon below, whom she clearly

[010-102]imagined that my art could exorcise from the house. I did not

[010-103]immediately go down, but when I did, the group which presented itself,

[010-104]arranged as it was by accident, though not very elaborate, took hold of

[010-105]my fancy and my eye in a way that none of the statuesque attitudes

[010-106]exhibited in the ballets at the Opera-house, though so ostentatiously

[010-107]complex, had ever done. In a cottage kitchen, but panelled on the wall

[010-108]with dark wood that from age and rubbing resembled oak, and looking

[010-109]more like a rustic hall of entrance than a kitchen, stood the Malay—his

[010-110]turban and loose trousers of dingy white relieved upon the dark

[010-111]panelling. He had placed himself nearer to the girl than she seemed to

[010-112]relish, though her native spirit of mountain intrepidity contended with

[010-113]the feeling of simple awe which her countenance expressed as she gazed

[010-114]upon the tiger-cat before her. And a more striking picture there could

[010-115]not be imagined than the beautiful English face of the girl, and its

[010-116]exquisite fairness, together with her erect and independent attitude,

[010-117]contrasted with the sallow and bilious skin of the Malay, enamelled or

[010-118]veneered with mahogany by marine air, his small, fierce, restless eyes,

[010-119]thin lips, slavish gestures and adorations. Half-hidden by the

[010-120]ferocious-looking Malay was a little child from a neighbouring cottage

[010-121]who had crept in after him, and was now in the act of reverting its

[010-122]head and gazing upwards at the turban and the fiery eyes beneath it,

[010-123]whilst with one hand he caught at the dress of the young woman for

[010-124]protection. My knowledge of the Oriental tongues is not remarkably

[010-125]extensive, being indeed confined to two words—the Arabic word for

[010-126]barley and the Turkish for opium (madjoon), which I have learned from

[010-127]_[[anastasius|Anastasius]]_; and as I had neither a Malay dictionary nor even

[010-128]Adelung’s _Mithridates_, which might have helped me to a few words, I

[010-129]addressed him in some lines from the Iliad, considering that, of such

[010-130]languages as I possessed, Greek, in point of longitude, came

[010-131]geographically nearest to an Oriental one. He worshipped me in a most

[010-132]devout manner, and replied in what I suppose was Malay. In this way I

[010-133]saved my reputation with my neighbours, for the Malay had no means of

[010-134]betraying the secret. He lay down upon the floor for about an hour, and

[010-135]then pursued his journey. On his departure I presented him with a piece

[010-136]of opium. To him, as an Orientalist, I concluded that opium must be

[010-137]familiar; and the expression of his face convinced me that it was.

[010-138]Nevertheless, I was struck with some little consternation when I saw

[010-139]him suddenly raise his hand to his mouth, and, to use the schoolboy

[010-140]phrase, bolt the whole, divided into three pieces, at one mouthful. The

[010-141]quantity was enough to kill three dragoons and their horses, and I felt

[010-142]some alarm for the poor creature; but what could be done? I had given

[010-143]him the opium in compassion for his solitary life, on recollecting that

[010-144]if he had travelled on foot from [[london|London]] it must be nearly three weeks

[010-145]since he could have exchanged a thought with any human being. I could

[010-146]not think of violating the laws of hospitality by having him seized and

[010-147]drenched with an emetic, and thus frightening him into a notion that we

[010-148]were going to sacrifice him to some English idol. No: there was clearly

[010-149]no help for it. He took his leave, and for some days I felt anxious,

[010-150]but as I never heard of any Malay being found dead, I became convinced

[010-151]that he was used {17} to opium; and that I must have done him the

[010-152]service I designed by giving him one night of respite from the pains of

[010-153]wandering.

[010-154]This incident I have digressed to mention, because this Malay (partly

[010-155]from the picturesque exhibition he assisted to frame, partly from the

[010-156]anxiety I connected with his image for some days) fastened afterwards

[010-157]upon my dreams, and brought other Malays with him, worse than himself,

[010-158]that ran “a-muck” {18} at me, and led me into a world of troubles. But

[010-159]to quit this episode, and to return to my intercalary year of

[010-160]happiness. I have said already, that on a subject so important to us

[010-161]all as happiness, we should listen with pleasure to any man’s

[010-162]experience or experiments, even though he were but a plough-boy, who

[010-163]cannot be supposed to have ploughed very deep into such an intractable

[010-164]soil as that of human pains and pleasures, or to have conducted his

[010-165]researches upon any very enlightened principles. But I who have taken

[010-166]happiness both in a solid and liquid shape, both boiled and unboiled,

[010-167]both East India and Turkey—who have conducted my experiments upon this

[010-168]interesting subject with a sort of galvanic battery, and have, for the

[010-169]general benefit of the world, inoculated myself, as it were, with the

[010-170]poison of 8000 drops of laudanum per day (just for the same reason as a

[010-171]French surgeon inoculated himself lately with cancer, an English one

[010-172]twenty years ago with plague, and a third, I know not of what nation,

[010-173]with hydrophobia), I (it will be admitted) must surely know what

[010-174]happiness is, if anybody does. And therefore I will here lay down an

[010-175]analysis of happiness; and as the most interesting mode of

[010-176]communicating it, I will give it, not didactically, but wrapped up and

[010-177]involved in a picture of one evening, as I spent every evening during

[010-178]the intercalary year when laudanum, though taken daily, was to me no

[010-179]more than the elixir of pleasure. This done, I shall quit the subject

[010-180]of happiness altogether, and pass to a very different one—_the pains of

[010-181]opium_.
