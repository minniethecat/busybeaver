# Worcester College, Oxford

**Worcester College** is one of the constituent colleges of the University of Oxford, founded in 1714. It was the college where Thomas De Quincey matriculated in 1803, though in the *Confessions* he refers to it only obliquely as "—— College, Oxford" — a discretion typical of the era's literary conventions for institutions and individuals still living.

De Quincey encountered the Oxford academic establishment through its institutional machinery:

> ...a respectable scholar at the head of a great school on an ancient foundation. This man had been appointed to his situation by —— College, Oxford, and was a sound, well-built scholar...
>
> (002-023)

## De Quincey at Worcester

De Quincey's time at Worcester College was marked by intellectual brilliance but also by increasing opium use. He was regarded as one of the most gifted classicists of his generation, with a command of Greek that astonished his tutors. However, he left Oxford in 1808 without taking a degree, driven partly by his escalating opium consumption and partly by a growing sense of alienation from academic life.

The college appears in the *Confessions* primarily as the backdrop to De Quincey's first significant opium experiences and his reflections on the relationship between scholarly discipline and the seductions of the drug.
