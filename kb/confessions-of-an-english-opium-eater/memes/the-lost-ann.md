# The Lost Ann

**The Lost Ann** is one of the most emotionally charged episodes in the *[[confessions-of-an-english-opium-eater|Confessions of an English Opium-Eater]]*. After befriending the young prostitute [[ann|Ann]] during his period of extreme poverty in [[london|London]], [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] recounts their separation and his subsequent, fruitless attempts to find her again on the streets of the metropolis.

## The Narrative

Ann was a sixteen-year-old street-walker who showed De Quincey extraordinary kindness during his darkest months. When he fell ill and collapsed on [[oxford-street]], she spent what little money she had to buy him wine and refreshments. The two formed a bond of genuine affection amid their shared destitution.

De Quincey planned to leave London temporarily and arranged to meet Ann at the corner of [[great-titchfield-street]]. When he returned, she was gone. For months afterwards, he walked the length of Oxford Street at night, scanning every face, hoping to find her.

De Quincey recounts their departure in sombre detail:

> ...soon after six o'clock on a dark winter evening I set off, accompanied by Ann, towards Piccadilly; for it was my intention to go down as far as Salthill on the Bath or Bristol mail.
>
> (005-105)

## Significance

The episode is the emotional core of the *Confessions*, grounding the abstract philosophy of [[opium]] in concrete human loss. De Quincey's enduring grief over Ann — and his belief that opium helped him bear it — forms a central thread in the work's meditation on suffering, memory, and consolation.
