# Principles of Political Economy

## Overview

David Ricardo's *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817) is one of the foundational texts of classical economics. De Quincey engages with the work extensively in the economic digressions of the *Confessions*, making it a surprisingly prominent intertext in what is ostensibly an addiction memoir.

## De Quincey's Engagement

De Quincey describes his intellectual relationship with political economy as a restorative counterpoint to the mental torpor induced by opium. He states that "political economy offers this advantage — that it presents very few distractions to the mind" and allowed him to concentrate despite his condition. (012-048)(012-050)

He took up Ricardo's book and "recurring to my own prophetic anticipation of the advent of some legislator for this science," found his expectations confirmed by Ricardo's deductive method. (012-068)(012-069) De Quincey praises Ricardo's approach: "Mr. Ricardo had deduced *à priori* from the understanding itself laws which first gave a ray of light into the unwieldy mass of materials." (012-080)(012-081)

## The Prolegomena

De Quincey composed a set of "Prolegomena to all Future Systems of Political Economy" that he intended to dedicate to Ricardo. He planned "to make a splendid one, to Mr. Ricardo," but "found myself quite unable to accomplish all this" due to organizational challenges. (012-090)(012-091)(012-105)(012-106) The Prolegomena, he notes wryly, "became of Political Economy, must have gone into darkness from that day." (012-118)

## Significance

The presence of Ricardo's *Principles* in the *Confessions* demonstrates De Quincey's intellectual range beyond the confessional mode. The economic digressions serve as evidence for his argument that opium did not destroy his intellectual powers — if he could engage seriously with Ricardo's treatise, his mind could not have been ruined by the drug.

## Key Passages

De Quincey describes how opium-induced mental lethargy led him, paradoxically, to the study of political economy:

> In this state of imbecility I had, for amusement, turned my attention to political economy; my understanding, which formerly had been as active and restless as a hyæna, could not, I suppose (so long as I lived at all) sink into utter lethargy; and political economy offers this advantage to a person in my state, that though it is eminently an organic science (no part, that is to say, but what acts on the whole as that whole acts on the part), it frequently becomes more and more interesting as one advances in years.
>
> (012-048)
