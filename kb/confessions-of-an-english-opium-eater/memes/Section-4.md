[004-001]But who and what, meantime, was the master of the house himself?

[004-002]Reader, he was one of those anomalous practitioners in lower

[004-003]departments of the law who—what shall I say?—who on prudential reasons,

[004-004]or from necessity, deny themselves all indulgence in the luxury of too

[004-005]delicate a conscience, (a periphrasis which might be abridged

[004-006]considerably, but _that_ I leave to the reader’s taste): in many walks

[004-007]of life a conscience is a more expensive encumbrance than a wife or a

[004-008]carriage; and just as people talk of “laying down” their carriages, so

[004-009]I suppose my friend Mr. —— had “laid down” his conscience for a time,

[004-010]meaning, doubtless, to resume it as soon as he could afford it. The

[004-011]inner economy of such a man’s daily life would present a most strange

[004-012]picture, if I could allow myself to amuse the reader at his expense.

[004-013]Even with my limited opportunities for observing what went on, I saw

[004-014]many scenes of [[london|London]] intrigues and complex chicanery, “cycle and

[004-015]epicycle, orb in orb,” at which I sometimes smile to this day, and at

[004-016]which I smiled then, in spite of my misery. My situation, however, at

[004-017]that time gave me little experience in my own person of any qualities

[004-018]in Mr. ——’s character but such as did him honour; and of his whole

[004-019]strange composition I must forget everything but that towards me he was

[004-020]obliging, and to the extent of his power, generous.

[004-021]That power was not, indeed, very extensive; however, in common with the

[004-022]rats, I sate rent free; and as Dr. Johnson has recorded that he never

[004-023]but once in his life had as much wall-fruit as he could eat, so let me

[004-024]be grateful that on that single occasion I had as large a choice of

[004-025]apartments in a London mansion as I could possibly desire. Except the

[004-026]Bluebeard room, which the poor child believed to be haunted, all

[004-027]others, from the attics to the cellars, were at our service; “the world

[004-028]was all before us,” and we pitched our tent for the night in any spot

[004-029]we chose. This house I have already described as a large one; it stands

[004-030]in a conspicuous situation and in a well-known part of London. Many of

[004-031]my readers will have passed it, I doubt not, within a few hours of

[004-032]reading this. For myself, I never fail to visit it when business draws

[004-033]me to London; about ten o’clock this very night, August 15, 1821—being

[004-034]my birthday—I turned aside from my evening walk down [[oxford-street|Oxford Street]],

[004-035]purposely to take a glance at it; it is now occupied by a respectable

[004-036]family, and by the lights in the front drawing-room I observed a

[004-037]domestic party assembled, perhaps at tea, and apparently cheerful and

[004-038]gay. Marvellous contrast, in my eyes, to the darkness, cold, silence,

[004-039]and desolation of that same house eighteen years ago, when its nightly

[004-040]occupants were one famishing scholar and a neglected child. Her,

[004-041]by-the-bye, in after-years I vainly endeavoured to trace. Apart from

[004-042]her situation, she was not what would be called an interesting child;

[004-043]she was neither pretty, nor quick in understanding, nor remarkably

[004-044]pleasing in manners. But, thank God! even in those years I needed not

[004-045]the embellishments of novel accessories to conciliate my affections:

[004-046]plain human nature, in its humblest and most homely apparel, was enough

[004-047]for me, and I loved the child because she was my partner in

[004-048]wretchedness. If she is now living she is probably a mother, with

[004-049]children of her own; but, as I have said, I could never trace her.

[004-050]This I regret; but another person there was at that time whom I have

[004-051]since sought to trace with far deeper earnestness, and with far deeper

[004-052]sorrow at my failure. This person was a young woman, and one of that

[004-053]unhappy class who subsist upon the wages of prostitution. I feel no

[004-054]shame, nor have any reason to feel it, in avowing that I was then on

[004-055]familiar and friendly terms with many women in that unfortunate

[004-056]condition. The reader needs neither smile at this avowal nor frown;

[004-057]for, not to remind my classical readers of the old Latin proverb,

[004-058]“_Sine cerere_,” &c., it may well be supposed that in the existing

[004-059]state of my purse my connection with such women could not have been an

[004-060]impure one. But the truth is, that at no time of my life have I been a

[004-061]person to hold myself polluted by the touch or approach of any creature

[004-062]that wore a human shape; on the contrary, from my very earliest youth

[004-063]it has been my pride to converse familiarly, _more Socratio_, with all

[004-064]human beings, man, woman, and child, that chance might fling in my way;

[004-065]a practice which is friendly to the knowledge of human nature, to good

[004-066]feelings, and to that frankness of address which becomes a man who

[004-067]would be thought a philosopher. For a philosopher should not see with

[004-068]the eyes of the poor limitary creature calling himself a man of the

[004-069]world, and filled with narrow and self-regarding prejudices of birth

[004-070]and education, but should look upon himself as a catholic creature, and

[004-071]as standing in equal relation to high and low, to educated and

[004-072]uneducated, to the guilty and the innocent. Being myself at that time

[004-073]of necessity a peripatetic, or a walker of the streets, I naturally

[004-074]fell in more frequently with those female peripatetics who are

[004-075]technically called street-walkers. Many of these women had occasionally

[004-076]taken my part against watchmen who wished to drive me off the steps of

[004-077]houses where I was sitting. But one amongst them, the one on whose

[004-078]account I have at all introduced this subject—yet no! let me not class

[004-079]the, oh! noble-minded [[ann|Ann]]—with that order of women. Let me find, if it

[004-080]be possible, some gentler name to designate the condition of her to

[004-081]whose bounty and compassion, ministering to my necessities when all the

[004-082]world had forsaken me, I owe it that I am at this time alive. For many

[004-083]weeks I had walked at nights with this poor friendless girl up and down

[004-084][[oxford|Oxford]] Street, or had rested with her on steps and under the shelter of

[004-085]porticoes. She could not be so old as myself; she told me, indeed, that

[004-086]she had not completed her sixteenth year. By such questions as my

[004-087]interest about her prompted I had gradually drawn forth her simple

[004-088]history. Hers was a case of ordinary occurrence (as I have since had

[004-089]reason to think), and one in which, if London beneficence had better

[004-090]adapted its arrangements to meet it, the power of the law might oftener

[004-091]be interposed to protect and to avenge. But the stream of London

[004-092]charity flows in a channel which, though deep and mighty, is yet

[004-093]noiseless and underground; not obvious or readily accessible to poor

[004-094]houseless wanderers; and it cannot be denied that the outside air and

[004-095]framework of London society is harsh, cruel, and repulsive. In any

[004-096]case, however, I saw that part of her injuries might easily have been

[004-097]redressed, and I urged her often and earnestly to lay her complaint

[004-098]before a magistrate. Friendless as she was, I assured her that she

[004-099]would meet with immediate attention, and that English justice, which

[004-100]was no respecter of persons, would speedily and amply avenge her on the

[004-101]brutal ruffian who had plundered her little property. She promised me

[004-102]often that she would, but she delayed taking the steps I pointed out

[004-103]from time to time, for she was timid and dejected to a degree which

[004-104]showed how deeply sorrow had taken hold of her young heart; and perhaps

[004-105]she thought justly that the most upright judge and the most righteous

[004-106]tribunals could do nothing to repair her heaviest wrongs. Something,

[004-107]however, would perhaps have been done, for it had been settled between

[004-108]us at length, but unhappily on the very last time but one that I was

[004-109]ever to see her, that in a day or two we should go together before a

[004-110]magistrate, and that I should speak on her behalf. This little service

[004-111]it was destined, however, that I should never realise. Meantime, that

[004-112]which she rendered to me, and which was greater than I could ever have

[004-113]repaid her, was this:—One night, when we were pacing slowly along

[004-114]Oxford Street, and after a day when I had felt more than usually ill

[004-115]and faint, I requested her to turn off with me into Soho Square.

[004-116]Thither we went, and we sat down on the steps of a house, which to this

[004-117]hour I never pass without a pang of grief and an inner act of homage to

[004-118]the spirit of that unhappy girl, in memory of the noble action which

[004-119]she there performed. Suddenly, as we sate, I grew much worse. I had

[004-120]been leaning my head against her bosom, and all at once I sank from her

[004-121]arms and fell backwards on the steps. From the sensations I then had, I

[004-122]felt an inner conviction of the liveliest kind, that without some

[004-123]powerful and reviving stimulus I should either have died on the spot,

[004-124]or should at least have sunk to a point of exhaustion from which all

[004-125]reäscent under my friendless circumstances would soon have become

[004-126]hopeless. Then it was, at this crisis of my fate, that my poor orphan

[004-127]companion, who had herself met with little but injuries in this world,

[004-128]stretched out a saving hand to me. Uttering a cry of terror, but

[004-129]without a moment’s delay, she ran off into Oxford Street, and in less

[004-130]time than could be imagined returned to me with a glass of port wine

[004-131]and spices, that acted upon my empty stomach, which at that time would

[004-132]have rejected all solid food, with an instantaneous power of

[004-133]restoration; and for this glass the generous girl without a murmur paid

[004-134]out of her humble purse at a time—be it remembered!—when she had

[004-135]scarcely wherewithal to purchase the bare necessaries of life, and when

[004-136]she could have no reason to expect that I should ever be able to

[004-137]reimburse her.

[004-138]Oh, youthful benefactress! how often in succeeding years, standing in

[004-139]solitary places, and thinking of thee with grief of heart and perfect

[004-140]love—how often have I wished that, as in ancient times, the curse of a

[004-141]father was believed to have a supernatural power, and to pursue its

[004-142]object with a fatal necessity of self-fulfilment; even so the

[004-143]benediction of a heart oppressed with gratitude might have a like

[004-144]prerogative, might have power given to it from above to chase, to

[004-145]haunt, to waylay, to overtake, to pursue thee into the central darkness

[004-146]of a London brothel, or (if it were possible) into the darkness of the

[004-147]grave, there to awaken thee with an authentic message of peace and

[004-148]forgiveness, and of final reconciliation!

[004-149]I do not often weep: for not only do my thoughts on subjects connected

[004-150]with the chief interests of man daily, nay hourly, descend a thousand

[004-151]fathoms “too deep for tears;” not only does the sternness of my habits

[004-152]of thought present an antagonism to the feelings which prompt

[004-153]tears—wanting of necessity to those who, being protected usually by

[004-154]their levity from any tendency to meditative sorrow, would by that same

[004-155]levity be made incapable of resisting it on any casual access of such

[004-156]feelings; but also, I believe that all minds which have contemplated

[004-157]such objects as deeply as I have done, must, for their own protection

[004-158]from utter despondency, have early encouraged and cherished some

[004-159]tranquillising belief as to the future balances and the hieroglyphic

[004-160]meanings of human sufferings. On these accounts I am cheerful to this

[004-161]hour, and, as I have said, I do not often weep. Yet some feelings,

[004-162]though not deeper or more passionate, are more tender than others; and

[004-163]often, when I walk at this time in Oxford Street by dreamy lamplight,

[004-164]and hear those airs played on a barrel-organ which years ago solaced me

[004-165]and my dear companion (as I must always call her), I shed tears, and

[004-166]muse with myself at the mysterious dispensation which so suddenly and

[004-167]so critically separated us for ever. How it happened the reader will

[004-168]understand from what remains of this introductory narration.
