[001-001]CONFESSIONS OF AN ENGLISH OPIUM-EATER:

[001-002]BEING AN EXTRACT FROM THE

[001-003]LIFE OF A SCHOLAR.

[001-004]by [[thomas-de-quincey|Thomas De Quincey]]

[001-005]_From the “[[london-magazine|London Magazine]]” for September_ 1821.

[001-006]TO THE READER

[001-007]I here present you, courteous reader, with the record of a remarkable

[001-008]period in my life: according to my application of it, I trust that it

[001-009]will prove not merely an interesting record, but in a considerable

[001-010]degree useful and instructive. In _that_ hope it is that I have drawn

[001-011]it up; and _that_ must be my apology for breaking through that delicate

[001-012]and honourable reserve which, for the most part, restrains us from the

[001-013]public exposure of our own errors and infirmities. Nothing, indeed, is

[001-014]more revolting to English feelings than the spectacle of a human being

[001-015]obtruding on our notice his moral ulcers or scars, and tearing away

[001-016]that “decent drapery” which time or indulgence to human frailty may

[001-017]have drawn over them; accordingly, the greater part of _our_

[001-018][[confession-genre|confessions]] (that is, spontaneous and extra-judicial confessions)

[001-019]proceed from demireps, adventurers, or swindlers: and for any such acts

[001-020]of gratuitous self-humiliation from those who can be supposed in

[001-021]sympathy with the decent and self-respecting part of society, we must

[001-022]look to French literature, or to that part of the German which is

[001-023]tainted with the spurious and defective sensibility of the French. All

[001-024]this I feel so forcibly, and so nervously am I alive to reproach of

[001-025]this tendency, that I have for many months hesitated about the

[001-026]propriety of allowing this or any part of my narrative to come before

[001-027]the public eye until after my death (when, for many reasons, the whole

[001-028]will be published); and it is not without an anxious review of the

[001-029]reasons for and against this step that I have at last concluded on

[001-030]taking it.

[001-031]Guilt and misery shrink, by a natural instinct, from public notice:

[001-032]they court [[solitude|privacy]] and solitude: and even in their choice of a grave

[001-033]will sometimes sequester themselves from the general population of the

[001-034]churchyard, as if declining to claim fellowship with the great family

[001-035]of man, and wishing (in the affecting language of Mr. Wordsworth)

[001-036]“—Humbly to express

[001-037]A penitential loneliness.”

[001-038]It is well, upon the whole, and for the interest of us all, that it

[001-039]should be so: nor would I willingly in my own person manifest a

[001-040]disregard of such salutary feelings, nor in act or word do anything to

[001-041]weaken them; but, on the one hand, as my self-accusation does not

[001-042]amount to a confession of guilt, so, on the other, it is possible that,

[001-043]if it _did_, the benefit resulting to others from the record of an

[001-044]experience purchased at so heavy a price might compensate, by a vast

[001-045]overbalance, for any violence done to the feelings I have noticed, and

[001-046]justify a breach of the general rule. Infirmity and misery do not of

[001-047]necessity imply guilt. They approach or recede from shades of that dark

[001-048]alliance, in proportion to the probable motives and prospects of the

[001-049]offender, and the palliations, known or secret, of the offence; in

[001-050]proportion as the temptations to it were potent from the first, and the

[001-051]resistance to it, in act or in effort, was earnest to the last. For my

[001-052]own part, without breach of truth or modesty, I may affirm that my life

[001-053]has been, on the whole, the life of a philosopher: from my birth I was

[001-054]made an intellectual creature, and intellectual in the highest sense my

[001-055]pursuits and pleasures have been, even from my schoolboy days. If

[001-056][[opium|opium-eating]] be a sensual pleasure, and if I am bound to confess that I

[001-057]have indulged in it to an excess not yet _recorded_ {1} of any other

[001-058]man, it is no less true that I have struggled against this fascinating

[001-059]enthralment with a religious zeal, and have at length accomplished what

[001-060]I never yet heard attributed to any other man—have untwisted, almost to

[001-061]its final links, the accursed chain which fettered me. Such a

[001-062]self-conquest may reasonably be set off in counterbalance to any kind

[001-063]or degree of self-indulgence. Not to insist that in my case the

[001-064]self-conquest was unquestionable, the self-indulgence open to doubts of

[001-065]casuistry, according as that name shall be extended to acts aiming at

[001-066]the bare relief of [[suffering|pain]], or shall be restricted to such as aim at the

[001-067]excitement of positive pleasure.

[001-068]Guilt, therefore, I do not acknowledge; and if I did, it is possible

[001-069]that I might still resolve on the present act of confession in

[001-070]consideration of the service which I may thereby render to the whole

[001-071]class of opium-eaters. But who are they? Reader, I am sorry to say a

[001-072]very numerous class indeed. Of this I became convinced some years ago

[001-073]by computing at that time the number of those in one small class of

[001-074]English society (the class of men distinguished for talents, or of

[001-075]eminent station) who were known to me, directly or indirectly, as

[001-076]opium-eaters; such, for instance, as the eloquent and benevolent ——,

[001-077]the late Dean of ——, Lord ——, Mr. —— the philosopher, a late

[001-078]Under-Secretary of State (who described to me the sensation which first

[001-079]drove him to the use of opium in the very same words as the Dean of ——,

[001-080]viz., “that he felt as though rats were gnawing and abrading the coats

[001-081]of his stomach”), Mr. ——, and many others hardly less known, whom it

[001-082]would be tedious to mention. Now, if one class, comparatively so

[001-083]limited, could furnish so many scores of cases (and _that_ within the

[001-084]knowledge of one single inquirer), it was a natural inference that the

[001-085]entire population of [[england|England]] would furnish a proportionable number. The

[001-086]soundness of this inference, however, I doubted, until some facts

[001-087]became known to me which satisfied me that it was not incorrect. I will

[001-088]mention two. (1) Three respectable [[london|London]] druggists, in widely remote

[001-089]quarters of London, from whom I happened lately to be purchasing small

[001-090]quantities of opium, assured me that the number of _amateur_

[001-091]opium-eaters (as I may term them) was at this time immense; and that

[001-092]the difficulty of distinguishing those persons to whom habit had

[001-093]rendered opium necessary from such as were purchasing it with a view to

[001-094]suicide, occasioned them daily trouble and disputes. This evidence

[001-095]respected London only. But (2)—which will possibly surprise the reader

[001-096]more—some years ago, on passing through [[manchester|Manchester]], I was informed by

[001-097]several cotton manufacturers that their workpeople were rapidly getting

[001-098]into the practice of opium-eating; so much so, that on a Saturday

[001-099]afternoon the counters of the druggists were strewed with pills of one,

[001-100]two, or three grains, in preparation for the known demand of the

[001-101]evening. The immediate occasion of this practice was the lowness of

[001-102]wages, which at that time would not allow them to indulge in ale or

[001-103]spirits, and wages rising, it may be thought that this practice would

[001-104]cease; but as I do not readily believe that any man having once tasted

[001-105]the divine luxuries of opium will afterwards descend to the gross and

[001-106]mortal enjoyments of alcohol, I take it for granted

[001-107]That those eat now who never ate before;

[001-108]And those who always ate, now eat the more.

[001-109]Indeed, the fascinating powers of opium are admitted even by medical

[001-110]writers, who are its greatest enemies. Thus, for instance, Awsiter,

[001-111]apothecary to Greenwich Hospital, in his “Essay on the Effects of

[001-112]Opium” (published in the year 1763), when attempting to explain why

[001-113]Mead had not been sufficiently explicit on the properties,

[001-114]counteragents, &c., of this drug, expresses himself in the following

[001-115]mysterious terms (φωναντα συνετοισι): “Perhaps he thought the subject

[001-116]of too delicate a nature to be made common; and as many people might

[001-117]then indiscriminately use it, it would take from that necessary fear

[001-118]and caution which should prevent their experiencing the extensive power

[001-119]of this drug, _for there are many properties in it, if universally

[001-120]known, that would habituate the use, and make it more in request with

[001-121]us than with Turks themselves_; the result of which knowledge,” he

[001-122]adds, “must prove a general misfortune.” In the necessity of this

[001-123]conclusion I do not altogether concur; but upon that point I shall have

[001-124]occasion to speak at the close of my Confessions, where I shall present

[001-125]the reader with the _moral_ of my narrative.

[001-126]PRELIMINARY CONFESSIONS

[001-127]These preliminary confessions, or introductory narrative of the

[001-128]youthful adventures which laid the foundation of the writer’s habit of

[001-129]opium-eating in after-life, it has been judged proper to premise, for

[001-130]three several reasons:

[001-131]1. As forestalling that question, and giving it a satisfactory answer,

[001-132]which else would painfully obtrude itself in the course of the Opium

[001-133]Confessions—“How came any reasonable being to subject himself to such a

[001-134]yoke of misery; voluntarily to incur a captivity so servile, and

[001-135]knowingly to fetter himself with such a sevenfold chain?”—a question

[001-136]which, if not somewhere plausibly resolved, could hardly fail, by the

[001-137]indignation which it would be apt to raise as against an act of wanton

[001-138]folly, to interfere with that degree of sympathy which is necessary in

[001-139]any case to an author’s purposes.

[001-140]2. As furnishing a key to some parts of that tremendous scenery which

[001-141]afterwards peopled the [[opium-dreams|dreams]] of the Opium-eater.

[001-142]3. As creating some previous interest of a personal sort in the

[001-143]confessing subject, apart from the matter of the confessions, which

[001-144]cannot fail to render the confessions themselves more interesting. If a

[001-145]man “whose talk is of oxen” should become an [[opium-eating|opium-eater]], the

[001-146]probability is that (if he is not too dull to dream at all) he will

[001-147]dream about oxen; whereas, in the case before him, the reader will find

[001-148]that the Opium-eater boasteth himself to be a philosopher; and

[001-149]accordingly, that the phantasmagoria of _his_ dreams (waking or

[001-150]sleeping, day-dreams or night-dreams) is suitable to one who in that

[001-151]character

[001-152]Humani nihil a se alienum putat.

[001-153]For amongst the conditions which he deems indispensable to the

[001-154]sustaining of any claim to the title of philosopher is not merely the

[001-155]possession of a superb intellect in its _analytic_ functions (in which

[001-156]part of the pretensions, however, England can for some generations show

[001-157]but few claimants; at least, he is not aware of any known candidate for

[001-158]this honour who can be styled emphatically _a subtle thinker_, with the

[001-159]exception of _[[samuel-taylor-coleridge|Samuel Taylor Coleridge]]_, and in a narrower department of

[001-160]thought with the recent illustrious exception {2} of _[[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]]_)

[001-161]but also on such a constitution of the _moral_ faculties as shall give

[001-162]him an inner eye and power of intuition for the vision and the

[001-163]mysteries of our human nature: _that_ constitution of faculties, in

[001-164]short, which (amongst all the generations of men that from the

[001-165]beginning of time have deployed into life, as it were, upon this

[001-166]planet) our English poets have possessed in the highest degree, and

[001-167]Scottish professors {3} in the lowest.

[001-168]I have often been asked how I first came to be a regular opium-eater,

[001-169]and have suffered, very unjustly, in the opinion of my acquaintance

[001-170]from being reputed to have brought upon myself all the sufferings which

[001-171]I shall have to record, by a long course of indulgence in this practice

[001-172]purely for the sake of creating an artificial state of pleasurable

[001-173]excitement. This, however, is a misrepresentation of my case. True it

[001-174]is that for nearly ten years I did occasionally take opium for the sake

[001-175]of the exquisite pleasure it gave me; but so long as I took it with

[001-176]this view I was effectually protected from all material bad

[001-177]consequences by the necessity of interposing long intervals between the

[001-178]several acts of indulgence, in order to renew the pleasurable

[001-179]sensations. It was not for the purpose of creating pleasure, but of

[001-180]mitigating pain in the severest degree, that I first began to use opium

[001-181]as an article of daily diet. In the twenty-eighth year of my age a most

[001-182]painful affection of the stomach, which I had first experienced about

[001-183]ten years before, attacked me in great strength. This affection had

[001-184]originally been caused by extremities of hunger, suffered in my boyish

[001-185]days. During the season of hope and redundant happiness which succeeded

[001-186](that is, from eighteen to twenty-four) it had slumbered; for the three

[001-187]following years it had revived at intervals; and now, under

[001-188]unfavourable circumstances, from [[melancholy|depression of spirits]], it attacked me

[001-189]with a violence that yielded to no remedies but opium. As the youthful

[001-190]sufferings which first produced this derangement of the stomach were

[001-191]interesting in themselves, and in the circumstances that attended them,

[001-192]I shall here briefly retrace them.
