# Dr. Samuel Johnson

## Biography

Dr. Samuel Johnson (1709–1784) was a central figure of 18th-century English letters — lexicographer, critic, poet, and essayist. De Quincey invokes Johnson at several strategic moments in the *Confessions* as a figure of authority and a benchmark of English literary judgment.

## Johnson as Critical Authority

De Quincey invokes Dr. Johnson's critical judgment to support his own assertions. He notes: "It is a just remark of Dr. Johnson's (and, what cannot often be said of his remarks, it is a very happy one)." (002-074) This characteristically double-edged compliment — praising the remark while parenthetically criticizing Johnson's general critical accuracy — illustrates De Quincey's complex relationship with the literary predecessors he simultaneously reveres and challenges.

> It is a just remark of Dr. Johnson's (and, what cannot often be said of his remarks, it is a very feeling one), that we never do anything consciously for the last time (of things, that is, which we have long been in the habit of doing) without sadness of heart. This truth I felt deeply when I came to leave ——, a place which I did not love, and where I had not been happy.
>
> (002-074)(002-079)

## Johnson and Poverty

De Quincey aligns himself with Johnson through the shared experience of financial struggle. He notes that "as Dr. Johnson has recorded that he never but once aspired to the possession of a clean shirt," De Quincey himself lived in comparable privation during his London wanderings. (004-022) This identification with Johnson as a fellow sufferer of genteel poverty lends literary pedigree to De Quincey's own hardships.

## Johnson and Opium

De Quincey invokes Johnson's combative spirit in the context of opium: "I would have joined Dr. Johnson in a *bellum internecinum* against opium." (011-054) The Latin phrase (*bellum internecinum* — a war of extermination) and the invocation of Johnson together frame the struggle with opium in heroic, almost epic terms, borrowing Johnson's authority to elevate the personal to the universal.

## Narrative Role

Johnson serves in the *Confessions* as what might be called a "prestige marker" — his name lends cultural weight to De Quincey's arguments, whether about critical judgment, poverty, or the struggle with addiction. The references to Johnson help position the *Confessions* within the English essay tradition that Johnson himself helped establish.
