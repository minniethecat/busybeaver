# Paradise Regained

## Overview

*Paradise Regained* (1671) is John Milton's epic poem — the sequel to *Paradise Lost*. De Quincey references the work as a literary touchstone at multiple points in the *Confessions*, drawing on its language and themes to enrich his own narrative of suffering and possible redemption.

## De Quincey's Engagement

De Quincey's engagement with Milton runs throughout the *Confessions*. He explicitly names *Paradise Regained* in the early sections of the work, invoking Miltonic language as part of the literary texture of his memoir. (006-018)

Most strikingly, De Quincey describes the power of reading Milton aloud:

> The Satanic speeches in Paradise Regained, when read aloud by myself...
>
> (012-018)

The ellipsis is De Quincey's own — the sentence breaks off into a meditation on the relationship between the reader's voice and the text's power. The Miltonic Satan, as rendered through De Quincey's own voice, becomes a figure of tremendous imaginative authority, resonating with the confessional narrator's own struggles with temptation and will.

## The Paradise Motif

*Paradise Regained* participates in a broader "paradise" motif that runs through the *Confessions*. De Quincey repeatedly invokes paradisal language — the "Paradise of Opium-eaters" (007-113)(008-168), the druggist who holds "the keys of Paradise" (009-130) — that echoes and ironizes Milton's theological framework. Where Milton's Christ regains Paradise through resistance to temptation, De Quincey's narrator finds and loses his paradise through surrender to opium, creating a dark inversion of the Miltonic pattern.

## In the Literary Context of the *Confessions*

The *Confessions* is saturated with Miltonic allusion. *Paradise Regained* joins *Paradise Lost*, *Samson Agonistes*, and the figure of Milton himself as part of the literary constellation that De Quincey draws upon to elevate his personal narrative to epic dimensions. The confessional mode borrows from the Miltonic sublime — transforming the private struggles of an opium-eater into a drama of cosmic proportions.
