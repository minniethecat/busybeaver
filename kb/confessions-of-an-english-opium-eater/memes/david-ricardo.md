# David Ricardo

## Biography

David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a British political economist, one of the most influential of the classical economists. De Quincey singles him out in the *Confessions* as a "recent illustrious exception" among modern thinkers. (001-160)

## De Quincey's Engagement with Ricardo

De Quincey's relationship with Ricardo's work is one of the more unexpected intellectual dimensions of the *Confessions*. In a digression from the opium narrative, he describes how he took up "Ricardo's book" during his intellectual recovery and "recurring to my own prophetic anticipation of the advent of some legislator for this science," found his predictions confirmed. (012-068)(012-069)

De Quincey praises Ricardo's deductive method, noting that Ricardo "had deduced *à priori* from the understanding itself laws which first gave a ray of light into the unwieldy mass of materials." (012-080)(012-081) This admiration for Ricardo's theoretical approach — deriving truths from pure reason rather than empirical accumulation — aligns with De Quincey's own intellectual temperament.

> Ricardo's book; and recurring to my own prophetic anticipation of the advent of some legislator for this science, I said, before I had finished the first chapter, "Thou art the man!" Wonder and curiosity were emotions that had long been dead in me. Yet I wondered once more: I wondered at myself that I could once again be stimulated to the effort of reading, and much more I wondered at the book.
>
> (012-068)(012-073)

## The Prolegomena

De Quincey wrote a set of "Prolegomena to all Future Systems of Political Economy" which he intended to dedicate to Ricardo. He planned "to make a splendid one, to Mr. Ricardo," but "found myself quite unable to accomplish all this," as the organizational challenges exceeded his resources at the time. (012-090)(012-091)(012-105)(012-106)

## Significance in the *Confessions*

Ricardo's presence in the *Confessions* demonstrates the breadth of De Quincey's intellectual interests beyond the confessional and the literary. The economic digressions — however tangential they may seem — serve as evidence for his claim that opium did not destroy his intellectual faculties. If he could engage seriously with Ricardo's political economy, the argument goes, his mind could not have been ruined.
