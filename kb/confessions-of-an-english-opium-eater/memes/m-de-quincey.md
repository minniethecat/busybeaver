# M. (De Quincey's Wife)

## Biography

Margaret De Quincey, the author's wife, referred to in the text as "beloved M." and compared to Electra — the devoted companion who "defrauded herself of sleep" to nurse him through the worst of his opium sufferings. Little biographical detail is provided in the *Confessions* beyond her role as caregiver; she remains a figure defined by her acts of devotion rather than by independent biographical facts.

## The Electra Comparison

De Quincey's most elaborate tribute to his wife appears in the "Pains of Opium" section, where he invokes the classical figure of Electra to describe her devotion. The passage is one of the most emotionally direct moments in the entire *Confessions*:

> For thou, beloved M., dear companion of my later years, thou wast my Electra! and neither in nobility of mind nor in long-suffering affection wouldst permit that a Grecian sister should excel an English wife.
>
> (007-056)(007-057)(007-058)(007-059)

## The Service of Love

De Quincey catalogues the physical toll of his opium sufferings on his wife with harrowing specificity. She stooped "to humble offices of kindness and to servile ministrations of tenderest affection — to wipe away for years the unwholesome dews upon the forehead, or to refresh the lips when parched and baked with fever." (007-059)(007-060)(007-061)(007-062)

Her own sleep became infected with "the spectacle of my dread contest with phantoms and shadowy enemies that oftentimes bade me 'sleep no more!'" Yet, De Quincey attests, "not even then didst thou utter a complaint or any murmur, nor withdraw thy angelic smiles, nor shrink from thy service of love, more than Electra did of old." (007-063)(007-064)(007-065)(007-066)(007-067)

## Narrative Role

M. appears in the *Confessions* not as a character with agency but as a witness and a measure — her suffering serves to authenticate the severity of De Quincey's opium torments, and her devotion provides the emotional counterweight to the clinical descriptions of addiction. She is the silent auditor whose presence makes the confession possible.
