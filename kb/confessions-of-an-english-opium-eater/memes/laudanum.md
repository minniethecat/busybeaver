# Laudanum

## Definition

**Laudanum** was the common 19th-century name for *tincture of opium* — a solution of opium dissolved in alcohol (proof spirit). Unlike crude opium, which De Quincey insists "is incapable of producing any state of body at all resembling that which is produced by alcohol" (008-011), laudanum could intoxicate "if a man could bear to take enough of it" — but only "because it contains so much proof spirit, and not because it contains so much opium" (008-008). De Quincey maintained this distinction between the two forms throughout the *Confessions*, using "laudanum" when referring specifically to the liquid preparation he purchased from London druggists.

## In the *Confessions*

### The Druggist's Purchase

De Quincey's first encounter with opium was in fact an encounter with laudanum. On a rainy Sunday in 1804, suffering from rheumatic pains, he entered a druggist's shop near Oxford Street and "asked for the tincture of opium" (007-121). The druggist — whom the author later mythologised as "the beatific vision of an immortal druggist" (007-125) — dispensed the preparation with mundane indifference, unaware that he was initiating one of literature's most famous substance dependencies.

### Consumption and Dosage

De Quincey's daily consumption of laudanum, measured in drops, reached staggering proportions. At his peak, he reports taking "eight thousand drops of laudanum" per day (010-067) — the equivalent of roughly 320 grains of solid opium. His remarkable reduction to "only 1000 drops of laudanum per day" (010-075) was accompanied by a dramatic restoration of mental clarity and happiness. In one passage he describes an acquaintance who allegedly took "five-and-twenty *ounces* of laudanum at once" (017-035), though De Quincey treats this claim with characteristic scepticism. He provides the reader with a helpful conversion: "reckon twenty-five drops of laudanum as equivalent to one" grain of opium (017-069).

### The Negus Episode

In Section 8, De Quincey recounts a moment of domestic comfort involving the preparation: "afterwards, for 'a glass of laudanum negus, warm, and without sugar'" (008-151) — a detail that underscores how thoroughly the drug was integrated into the fabric of his daily life. He notes that he "seldom drank laudanum, at that time, more than" moderate quantities (008-152), presenting his early use as controlled and even genteel.

### Laudanum as Poison

In Section 10, De Quincey reflects darkly on the quantities involved: "I have often administered to others... the poison of 8000 drops of laudanum per day" (010-170). He observes an "intercalary year when laudanum, though taken daily, was to me" something different — a period when the drug's hold temporarily loosened (010-178). Even in recounting these extremes, De Quincey's prose maintains a clinical precision that underscores the confessional purpose of the text.

## Key Quotations

> As to the tincture of opium (commonly called laudanum) *that* might certainly intoxicate if a man could bear to take enough of it; but why? Because it contains so much proof spirit, and not because it contains so much opium. (008-008)

> I had a little before this time descended suddenly, and without any considerable effort, from 320 grains of opium (*i.e*. eight thousand drops of laudanum) per day, to forty grains, or one-eighth part. (010-067)

> I now took only 1000 drops of laudanum per day; and what was that? A latter spring had come to close up the season of youth; my brain performed its functions as healthily as ever before. (010-075)
