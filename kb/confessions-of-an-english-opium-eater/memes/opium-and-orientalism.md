# Opium and Orientalism

## Definition

In the *Confessions*, opium is inseparable from the Orient. The drug itself comes from the East — Turkey, India, the Levant — and De Quincey's descriptions of its effects are saturated with Orientalist imagery: "Asiatic" landscapes, "Turkish opium-eaters," and the explosive encounter with the [[malay-visitor|Malay]]. This intersection of drug culture and British Orientalism places the *Confessions* at a key node in 19th-century cultural exchange — and cultural misunderstanding.

## The Malay Visitor Episode

The Malay visitor appears at De Quincey's cottage in the [[lakes|Lake District]], an anomalous figure in the English countryside: "his sallow and bilious skin... enamelled or veneered with the weathers of the tropics" (010-117). De Quincey's description oscillates between genuine curiosity and racial caricature — the "ferocious-looking Malay" turns out to be "a little child" in demeanour (010-120).

The linguistic impasse that follows — De Quincey has "neither a Malay dictionary nor even a Malay grammar" (010-127) — becomes a darkly comic set piece. He gives the Malay a large piece of opium, which the visitor swallows whole, to De Quincey's horror and subsequent relief when the man departs unharmed. The episode encapsulates the power asymmetry of empire: De Quincey, the English intellectual, administers the Oriental substance to the Oriental body, with unpredictable results.

## Opium and Imperial Trade

Opium was not only a personal drug but a commodity of empire:
- British opium trade with China (the Opium Wars, 1839–1842, 1856–1860)
- The East India Company's opium monopoly in Bengal
- [[laudanum]] as a mass-market medicinal product — made possible by colonial supply chains

De Quincey rarely addresses the political economy of opium directly, but his casual references to "Turkish opium-eaters" (008-131) and his Orientalist framing of the drug's effects are inseparable from Britain's imperial relationship with Asia.

## The *Anastasius* Connection

De Quincey cites [[anastasius|Thomas Hope's *Anastasius*]] (1819) as a literary source for Orientalist detail (010-127). Hope's sprawling novel of the Levant provided De Quincey with ready-made Orientalist tropes — reinforcing the circular relationship between literary representation and imperial attitudes.

## Key Passages

The Malay episode provides the most vivid intersection of Orientalism and opium in the *Confessions*, as De Quincey attempts communication with a silent visitor:

> I addressed him in some lines from the Iliad, considering that, of such languages as I possessed, Greek, in point of longitude, came geographically nearest to an Oriental one. He worshipped me in a most devout manner, and replied in what I suppose was Malay.
>
> (010-131)
