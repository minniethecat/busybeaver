# Religio Medici

## Overview

*Religio Medici* (The Religion of a Physician, 1643) is Sir Thomas Browne's spiritual autobiography and philosophical meditation. The work explores themes of faith, reason, mortality, and wonder — making it a natural touchstone for De Quincey's own confessional project.

## De Quincey's Citation

De Quincey invokes *Religio Medici* in his celebrated digression on the powers of music under opium's influence. He singles it out as containing one of the few truly adequate passages on the subject of music in all literature:

> It is a passage in the *Religio Medici* of Sir T. Brown, and though chiefly remarkable for its sublimity, it is most instructive as a physiological comment upon the occult powers of music.
>
> (008-175)(008-176)

De Quincey's choice of Browne is significant: the *Religio Medici*, like the *Confessions*, is a first-person narrative that uses personal experience as a lens for philosophical inquiry. Browne's combination of scientific curiosity and religious wonder provides a model for De Quincey's own fusion of clinical observation and visionary experience.

## The Confessional-Meditative Tradition

*Religio Medici* belongs to a tradition of confessional-meditative prose that De Quincey consciously inherits and transforms. Where Browne's confessions move toward religious faith, De Quincey's move toward psychological complexity; where Browne finds wonder in the natural order, De Quincey finds it in the chemical alteration of consciousness. The *Confessions* secularizes the spiritual autobiography even as it borrows its forms.
