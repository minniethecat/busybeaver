**German metaphysics**—or German Idealism—refers to the tradition of post-Kantian philosophy that De Quincey immersed himself in during the period of his life described in the *Confessions*. His engagement with the works of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], Fichte, and Schelling formed a crucial part of his self-presentation as a philosopher and scholar, even as he descended deeper into opium addiction. (009-169)

## De Quincey's Engagement with German Philosophy

By 1812, De Quincey had withdrawn to a cottage in the mountains and was devoting himself to the study of German metaphysics:

> in 1812, the year we are now arrived at, as well as for some years previous, I have been chiefly studying German metaphysics in the writings of Kant, Fichte, Schelling, &c. (009-169)

This was no casual interest. De Quincey was one of the earliest English interpreters of Kant and German Idealism, and he took pride in his ability to engage with these difficult texts. His intellectual confidence is evident in his remark that upon reducing his opium dose from 320 grains to 40 grains per day, he experienced a revival of his philosophical faculties:

> I read Kant again, and again I understood him, or fancied that I did. Again my feelings of pleasure expanded themselves to all around me. (010-078)

## German Metaphysics and the Opium-Eater's Identity

German metaphysics was integral to De Quincey's self-image as a scholar and gentleman. In his celebrated description of the winter cottage scene—the iconic portrait of the opium-eater at home—he places the book of German metaphysics alongside the laudanum decanter as the two essential props that define him:

> Into this you may put a quart of ruby-coloured laudanum; that, and a book of German Metaphysics placed by its side, will sufficiently attest my being in the neighbourhood. (011-094)

The juxtaposition is deliberate and revealing: opium and German philosophy are twin pillars of De Quincey's identity. The one represents his physical enslavement, the other his intellectual distinction. Together they embody the central paradox of the *Confessions*—a man of formidable intellect simultaneously in thrall to a debilitating drug.
