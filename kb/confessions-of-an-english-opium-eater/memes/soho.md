# Soho

**Soho** is a district in the West End of London, known in De Quincey's time as a neighbourhood of immigrants, artists, and tradesmen. In the *Confessions*, Soho appears as the location of the "druggist" from whom De Quincey purchased his opium — a figure who becomes, in the narrative, a silent witness to the author's escalating consumption.

## The Druggist

De Quincey's unnamed Soho druggist is one of the memorable minor characters of the *Confessions*. He sells opium with "an air of officious benevolence" and seems ever-ready to supply ever-larger quantities. De Quincey's prose, characteristically, hovers between gratitude and irony in describing this purveyor of his "daily poison."

## Soho in the Nineteenth Century

In the early 1800s, Soho was a mixed residential and commercial area, home to Huguenot silk-weavers, continental refugees, and a growing number of shops and trades. Its reputation as a slightly raffish, cosmopolitan quarter — neither the poverty of the East End nor the gentility of Mayfair — suited De Quincey, who moved between social worlds as easily as he moved between states of consciousness.
