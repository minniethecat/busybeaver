[009-001]These were my opera pleasures; but another pleasure I had which, as it

[009-002]could be had only on a Saturday night, occasionally struggled with my

[009-003]love of the Opera; for at that time Tuesday and Saturday were the

[009-004]regular opera nights. On this subject I am afraid I shall be rather

[009-005]obscure, but I can assure the reader not at all more so than Marinus in

[009-006]his Life of Proclus, or many other biographers and autobiographers of

[009-007]fair reputation. This pleasure, I have said, was to be had only on a

[009-008]Saturday night. What, then, was Saturday night to me more than any

[009-009]other night? I had no labours that I rested from, no wages to receive;

[009-010]what needed I to care for Saturday night, more than as it was a summons

[009-011]to hear Grassini? True, most logical reader; what you say is

[009-012]unanswerable. And yet so it was and is, that whereas different men

[009-013]throw their feelings into different channels, and most are apt to show

[009-014]their interest in the concerns of the poor chiefly by sympathy,

[009-015]expressed in some shape or other, with their distresses and sorrows, I

[009-016]at that time was disposed to express my interest by sympathising with

[009-017]their pleasures. The pains of poverty I had lately seen too much of,

[009-018]more than I wished to remember; but the pleasures of the poor, their

[009-019]consolations of spirit, and their reposes from bodily toil, can never

[009-020]become oppressive to contemplate. Now Saturday night is the season for

[009-021]the chief, regular, and periodic return of rest of the poor; in this

[009-022]point the most hostile sects unite, and acknowledge a common link of

[009-023]brotherhood; almost all Christendom rests from its labours. It is a

[009-024]rest introductory to another rest, and divided by a whole day and two

[009-025]nights from the renewal of toil. On this account I feel always, on a

[009-026]Saturday night, as though I also were released from some yoke of

[009-027]labour, had some wages to receive, and some luxury of repose to enjoy.

[009-028]For the sake, therefore, of witnessing, upon as large a scale as

[009-029]possible, a spectacle with which my sympathy was so entire, I used

[009-030]often on Saturday nights, after I had taken opium, to wander forth,

[009-031]without much regarding the direction or the distance, to all the

[009-032]markets and other parts of [[london|London]] to which the poor resort of a

[009-033]Saturday night, for laying out their wages. Many a family party,

[009-034]consisting of a man, his wife, and sometimes one or two of his

[009-035]children, have I listened to, as they stood consulting on their ways

[009-036]and means, or the strength of their exchequer, or the price of

[009-037]household articles. Gradually I became familiar with their wishes,

[009-038]their difficulties, and their opinions. Sometimes there might be heard

[009-039]murmurs of discontent, but far oftener expressions on the countenance,

[009-040]or uttered in words, of patience, hope, and tranquillity. And taken

[009-041]generally, I must say that, in this point at least, the poor are more

[009-042]philosophic than the rich—that they show a more ready and cheerful

[009-043]submission to what they consider as irremediable evils or irreparable

[009-044]losses. Whenever I saw occasion, or could do it without appearing to be

[009-045]intrusive, I joined their parties, and gave my opinion upon the matter

[009-046]in discussion, which, if not always judicious, was always received

[009-047]indulgently. If wages were a little higher or expected to be so, or the

[009-048]quartern loaf a little lower, or it was reported that onions and butter

[009-049]were expected to fall, I was glad; yet, if the contrary were true, I

[009-050]drew from opium some means of consoling myself. For opium (like the

[009-051]bee, that extracts its materials indiscriminately from roses and from

[009-052]the soot of chimneys) can overrule all feelings into compliance with

[009-053]the master-key. Some of these rambles led me to great distances, for an

[009-054][[opium-eating|opium-eater]] is too happy to observe the motion of time; and sometimes

[009-055]in my attempts to steer homewards, upon nautical principles, by fixing

[009-056]my eye on the pole-star, and seeking ambitiously for a north-west

[009-057]passage, instead of circumnavigating all the capes and head-lands I had

[009-058]doubled in my outward voyage, I came suddenly upon such knotty problems

[009-059]of alleys, such enigmatical entries, and such sphynx’s riddles of

[009-060]streets without thoroughfares, as must, I conceive, baffle the audacity

[009-061]of porters and confound the intellects of hackney-coachmen. I could

[009-062]almost have believed at times that I must be the first discoverer of

[009-063]some of these _terræ incognitæ_, and doubted whether they had yet been

[009-064]laid down in the modern charts of London. For all this, however, I paid

[009-065]a heavy price in distant years, when the human face tyrannised over my

[009-066][[opium-dreams|dreams]], and the perplexities of my steps in London came back and

[009-067]haunted my sleep, with the feeling of perplexities, moral and

[009-068]intellectual, that brought confusion to the reason, or anguish and

[009-069]remorse to the conscience.

[009-070]Thus I have shown that opium does not of necessity produce inactivity

[009-071]or torpor, but that, on the contrary, it often led me into markets and

[009-072]theatres. Yet, in candour, I will admit that markets and theatres are

[009-073]not the appropriate haunts of the opium-eater when in the divinest

[009-074]state incident to his enjoyment. In that state, crowds become an

[009-075]oppression to him; music even, too sensual and gross. He naturally

[009-076]seeks solitude and silence, as indispensable conditions of those

[009-077]trances, or profoundest reveries, which are the crown and consummation

[009-078]of what opium can do for human nature. I, whose disease it was to

[009-079]meditate too much and to observe too little, and who upon my first

[009-080]entrance at college was nearly falling into a deep melancholy, from

[009-081]brooding too much on the sufferings which I had witnessed in London,

[009-082]was sufficiently aware of the tendencies of my own thoughts to do all I

[009-083]could to counteract them. I was, indeed, like a person who, according

[009-084]to the old legend, had entered the cave of Trophonius; and the remedies

[009-085]I sought were to force myself into society, and to keep my

[009-086]understanding in continual activity upon matters of science. But for

[009-087]these remedies I should certainly have become hypochondriacally

[009-088]melancholy. In after years, however, when my cheerfulness was more

[009-089]fully re-established, I yielded to my natural inclination for a

[009-090]solitary life. And at that time I often fell into these reveries upon

[009-091]taking opium; and more than once it has happened to me, on a summer

[009-092]night, when I have been at an open window, in a room from which I could

[009-093]overlook the sea at a mile below me, and could command a view of the

[009-094]great town of L——, at about the same distance, that I have sate from

[009-095]sunset to sunrise, motionless, and without wishing to move.

[009-096]I shall be charged with mysticism, Behmenism, quietism, &c., but _that_

[009-097]shall not alarm me. Sir H. Vane, the younger, was one of our wisest

[009-098]men; and let my reader see if he, in his philosophical works, be half

[009-099]as unmystical as I am. I say, then, that it has often struck me that

[009-100]the scene itself was somewhat typical of what took place in such a

[009-101]reverie. The town of L—— represented the earth, with its sorrows and

[009-102]its graves left behind, yet not out of sight, nor wholly forgotten. The

[009-103]ocean, in everlasting but gentle agitation, and brooded over by a

[009-104]dove-like calm, might not unfitly typify the mind and the mood which

[009-105]then swayed it. For it seemed to me as if then first I stood at a

[009-106]distance and aloof from the uproar of life; as if the tumult, the

[009-107]fever, and the strife were suspended; a respite granted from the secret

[009-108]burthens of the heart; a sabbath of repose; a resting from human

[009-109]labours. Here were the hopes which blossom in the paths of life

[009-110]reconciled with the peace which is in the grave; motions of the

[009-111]intellect as unwearied as the heavens, yet for all anxieties a halcyon

[009-112]calm; a tranquillity that seemed no product of inertia, but as if

[009-113]resulting from mighty and equal antagonisms; infinite activities,

[009-114]infinite repose.

[009-115]Oh, just, subtle, and mighty opium! that to the hearts of poor and rich

[009-116]alike, for the wounds that will never heal, and for “the pangs that

[009-117]tempt the spirit to rebel,” bringest an assuaging balm; eloquent opium!

[009-118]that with thy potent rhetoric stealest away the purposes of wrath; and

[009-119]to the guilty man for one night givest back the hopes of his youth, and

[009-120]hands washed pure from blood; and to the proud man a brief oblivion for

[009-121]“Wrongs undress’d and insults unavenged;” that summonest to the

[009-122]chancery of dreams, for the triumphs of suffering innocence, false

[009-123]witnesses; and confoundest perjury, and dost reverse the sentences of

[009-124]unrighteous judges;—thou buildest upon the bosom of darkness, out of

[009-125]the fantastic imagery of the brain, cities and temples beyond the art

[009-126]of Phidias and Praxiteles—beyond the splendour of Babylon and

[009-127]Hekatómpylos, and “from the anarchy of dreaming sleep” callest into

[009-128]sunny light the faces of long-buried beauties and the blessed household

[009-129]countenances cleansed from the “dishonours of the grave.” Thou only

[009-130]givest these gifts to man; and thou hast the keys of Paradise, oh,

[009-131]just, subtle, and mighty opium!

[009-132]INTRODUCTION TO THE PAINS OF OPIUM

[009-133]Courteous, and I hope indulgent, reader (for all _my_ readers must be

[009-134]indulgent ones, or else I fear I shall shock them too much to count on

[009-135]their courtesy), having accompanied me thus far, now let me request you

[009-136]to move onwards for about eight years; that is to say, from 1804 (when

[009-137]I have said that my acquaintance with opium first began) to 1812. The

[009-138]years of academic life are now over and gone—almost forgotten; the

[009-139]student’s cap no longer presses my temples; if my cap exist at all, it

[009-140]presses those of some youthful scholar, I trust, as happy as myself,

[009-141]and as passionate a lover of knowledge. My gown is by this time, I dare

[009-142]say, in the same condition with many thousand excellent books in the

[009-143]Bodleian, viz., diligently perused by certain studious moths and worms;

[009-144]or departed, however (which is all that I know of his fate), to that

[009-145]great reservoir of _somewhere_ to which all the tea-cups, tea-caddies,

[009-146]tea-pots, tea-kettles, &c., have departed (not to speak of still

[009-147]frailer vessels, such as glasses, decanters, bed-makers, &c.), which

[009-148]occasional resemblances in the present generation of tea-cups, &c.,

[009-149]remind me of having once possessed, but of whose departure and final

[009-150]fate I, in common with most gownsmen of either university, could give,

[009-151]I suspect, but an obscure and conjectural history. The persecutions of

[009-152]the chapel-bell, sounding its unwelcome summons to six o’clock matins,

[009-153]interrupts my slumbers no longer, the porter who rang it, upon whose

[009-154]beautiful nose (bronze, inlaid with copper) I wrote, in retaliation so

[009-155]many Greek epigrams whilst I was dressing, is dead, and has ceased to

[009-156]disturb anybody; and I, and many others who suffered much from his

[009-157]tintinnabulous propensities, have now agreed to overlook his errors,

[009-158]and have forgiven him. Even with the bell I am now in charity; it

[009-159]rings, I suppose, as formerly, thrice a-day, and cruelly annoys, I

[009-160]doubt not, many worthy gentlemen, and disturbs their peace of mind; but

[009-161]as to me, in this year 1812, I regard its treacherous voice no longer

[009-162](treacherous I call it, for, by some refinement of malice, it spoke in

[009-163]as sweet and silvery tones as if it had been inviting one to a party);

[009-164]its tones have no longer, indeed, power to reach me, let the wind sit

[009-165]as favourable as the malice of the bell itself could wish, for I am 250

[009-166]miles away from it, and buried in the depth of mountains. And what am I

[009-167]doing among the mountains? Taking opium. Yes; but what else? Why

[009-168]reader, in 1812, the year we are now arrived at, as well as for some

[009-169]years previous, I have been chiefly studying German metaphysics in the

[009-170]writings of Kant, Fichte, Schelling, &c. And how and in what manner do

[009-171]I live?—in short, what class or description of men do I belong to? I am

[009-172]at this period—viz. in 1812—living in a cottage and with a single

[009-173]female servant (_honi soit qui mal y pense_), who amongst my neighbours

[009-174]passes by the name of my “housekeeper.” And as a scholar and a man of

[009-175]learned education, and in that sense a gentleman, I may presume to

[009-176]class myself as an unworthy member of that indefinite body called

[009-177]_gentlemen_. Partly on the ground I have assigned perhaps, partly

[009-178]because from my having no visible calling or business, it is rightly

[009-179]judged that I must be living on my private fortune; I am so classed by

[009-180]my neighbours; and by the courtesy of modern [[england|England]] I am usually

[009-181]addressed on letters, &c., “Esquire,” though having, I fear, in the

[009-182]rigorous construction of heralds, but slender pretensions to that

[009-183]distinguished honour; yet in popular estimation I am X. Y. Z., Esquire,

[009-184]but not justice of the Peace nor Custos Rotulorum. Am I married? Not

[009-185]yet. And I still take opium? On Saturday nights. And perhaps have taken

[009-186]it unblushingly ever since “the rainy Sunday,” and “the stately

[009-187]Pantheon,” and “the beatific druggist” of 1804? Even so. And how do I

[009-188]find my health after all this [[opium|opium-eating]]? In short, how do I do? Why,

[009-189]pretty well, I thank you, reader; in the phrase of ladies in the straw,

[009-190]“as well as can be expected.” In fact, if I dared to say the real and

[009-191]simple truth, though, to satisfy the theories of medical men, I _ought_

[009-192]to be ill, I never was better in my life than in the spring of 1812;

[009-193]and I hope sincerely that the quantity of claret, port, or “particular

[009-194]Madeira,” which in all probability you, good reader, have taken, and

[009-195]design to take for every term of eight years during your natural life,

[009-196]may as little disorder your health as mine was disordered by the opium

[009-197]I had taken for eight years, between 1804 and 1812. Hence you may see

[009-198]again the danger of taking any medical advice from _[[anastasius|Anastasius]]_; in

[009-199]divinity, for aught I know, or law, he may be a safe counsellor; but

[009-200]not in medicine. No; it is far better to consult Dr. Buchan, as I did;

[009-201]for I never forgot that worthy man’s excellent suggestion, and I was

[009-202]“particularly careful not to take above five-and-twenty ounces of

[009-203]laudanum.” To this moderation and temperate use of the article I may

[009-204]ascribe it, I suppose, that as yet, at least (_i.e_. in 1812), I am

[009-205]ignorant and unsuspicious of the avenging terrors which opium has in

[009-206]store for those who abuse its lenity. At the same time, it must not be

[009-207]forgotten that hitherto I have been only a dilettante eater of opium;

[009-208]eight years’ practice even, with a single precaution of allowing

[009-209]sufficient intervals between every indulgence, has not been sufficient

[009-210]to make opium necessary to me as an article of daily diet. But now

[009-211]comes a different era. Move on, if you please, reader, to 1813. In the

[009-212]summer of the year we have just quitted I have suffered much in bodily

[009-213]health from distress of mind connected with a very melancholy event.

[009-214]This event being no ways related to the subject now before me, further

[009-215]than through the bodily illness which it produced, I need not more

[009-216]particularly notice. Whether this illness of 1812 had any share in that

[009-217]of 1813 I know not; but so it was, that in the latter year I was

[009-218]attacked by a most appalling irritation of the stomach, in all respects

[009-219]the same as that which had caused me so much suffering in youth, and

[009-220]accompanied by a revival of all the old dreams. This is the point of my

[009-221]narrative on which, as respects my own self-justification, the whole of

[009-222]what follows may be said to hinge. And here I find myself in a

[009-223]perplexing dilemma. Either, on the one hand, I must exhaust the

[009-224]reader’s patience by such a detail of my malady, or of my struggles

[009-225]with it, as might suffice to establish the fact of my inability to

[009-226]wrestle any longer with irritation and constant suffering; or, on the

[009-227]other hand, by passing lightly over this critical part of my story, I

[009-228]must forego the benefit of a stronger impression left on the mind of

[009-229]the reader, and must lay myself open to the misconstruction of having

[009-230]slipped, by the easy and gradual steps of self-indulging persons, from

[009-231]the first to the final stage of opium-eating (a misconstruction to

[009-232]which there will be a lurking predisposition in most readers, from my

[009-233]previous acknowledgements). This is the dilemma, the first horn of

[009-234]which would be sufficient to toss and gore any column of patient

[009-235]readers, though drawn up sixteen deep and constantly relieved by fresh

[009-236]men; consequently that is not to be thought of. It remains, then, that

[009-237]I _postulate_ so much as is necessary for my purpose. And let me take

[009-238]as full credit for what I postulate as if I had demonstrated it, good

[009-239]reader, at the expense of your patience and my own. Be not so

[009-240]ungenerous as to let me suffer in your good opinion through my own

[009-241]forbearance and regard for your comfort. No; believe all that I ask of

[009-242]you—viz., that I could resist no longer; believe it liberally and as an

[009-243]act of grace, or else in mere prudence; for if not, then in the next

[009-244]edition of my Opium Confessions, revised and enlarged, I will make you

[009-245]believe and tremble; and _à force d’ennuyer_, by mere dint of

[009-246]pandiculation I will terrify all readers of mine from ever again

[009-247]questioning any postulate that I shall think fit to make.
