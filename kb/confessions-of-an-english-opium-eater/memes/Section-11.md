[011-001]Let there be a cottage standing in a valley, eighteen miles from any

[011-002]town—no spacious valley, but about two miles long by three-quarters of

[011-003]a mile in average width; the benefit of which provision is that all the

[011-004]family resident within its circuit will compose, as it were, one larger

[011-005]household, personally familiar to your eye, and more or less

[011-006]interesting to your affections. Let the mountains be real mountains,

[011-007]between 3,000 and 4,000 feet high, and the cottage a real cottage, not

[011-008](as a witty author has it) “a cottage with a double coach-house;” let

[011-009]it be, in fact (for I must abide by the actual scene), a white cottage,

[011-010]embowered with flowering shrubs, so chosen as to unfold a succession of

[011-011]flowers upon the walls and clustering round the windows through all the

[011-012]months of spring, summer, and autumn—beginning, in fact, with May

[011-013]roses, and ending with jasmine. Let it, however, _not_ be spring, nor

[011-014]summer, nor autumn, but winter in his sternest shape. This is a most

[011-015]important point in the science of happiness. And I am surprised to see

[011-016]people overlook it, and think it matter of congratulation that winter

[011-017]is going, or, if coming, is not likely to be a severe one. On the

[011-018]contrary, I put up a petition annually for as much snow, hail, frost,

[011-019]or storm, of one kind or other, as the skies can possibly afford us.

[011-020]Surely everybody is aware of the divine pleasures which attend a winter

[011-021]fireside, candles at four o’clock, warm hearth-rugs, tea, a fair

[011-022]tea-maker, shutters closed, curtains flowing in ample draperies on the

[011-023]floor, whilst the wind and rain are raging audibly without,

[011-024]And at the doors and windows seem to call,

[011-025]As heav’n and earth they would together mell;

[011-026]Yet the least entrance find they none at all;

[011-027]Whence sweeter grows our rest secure in massy hall.

[011-028]_Castle of Indolence_.

[011-029]All these are items in the description of a winter evening which must

[011-030]surely be familiar to everybody born in a high latitude. And it is

[011-031]evident that most of these delicacies, like ice-cream, require a very

[011-032]low temperature of the atmosphere to produce them; they are fruits

[011-033]which cannot be ripened without weather stormy or inclement in some way

[011-034]or other. I am not “_particular_,” as people say, whether it be snow,

[011-035]or black frost, or wind so strong that (as Mr. —— says) “you may lean

[011-036]your back against it like a post.” I can put up even with rain,

[011-037]provided it rains cats and dogs; but something of the sort I must have,

[011-038]and if I have it not, I think myself in a manner ill-used; for why am I

[011-039]called on to pay so heavily for winter, in coals and candles, and

[011-040]various privations that will occur even to gentlemen, if I am not to

[011-041]have the article good of its kind? No, a Canadian winter for my money,

[011-042]or a Russian one, where every man is but a co-proprietor with the north

[011-043]wind in the fee-simple of his own ears. Indeed, so great an epicure am

[011-044]I in this matter that I cannot relish a winter night fully if it be

[011-045]much past St. Thomas’s day, and have degenerated into disgusting

[011-046]tendencies to vernal appearances. No, it must be divided by a thick

[011-047]wall of dark nights from all return of light and sunshine. From the

[011-048]latter weeks of October to Christmas Eve, therefore, is the period

[011-049]during which happiness is in season, which, in my judgment, enters the

[011-050]room with the tea-tray; for tea, though ridiculed by those who are

[011-051]naturally of coarse nerves, or are become so from wine-drinking, and

[011-052]are not susceptible of influence from so refined a stimulant, will

[011-053]always be the favourite beverage of the intellectual; and, for my part,

[011-054]I would have joined Dr. Johnson in a _bellum internecinum_ against

[011-055]Jonas Hanway, or any other impious person, who should presume to

[011-056]disparage it. But here, to save myself the trouble of too much verbal

[011-057]description, I will introduce a painter, and give him directions for

[011-058]the rest of the picture. Painters do not like white cottages, unless a

[011-059]good deal weather-stained; but as the reader now understands that it is

[011-060]a winter night, his services will not be required except for the inside

[011-061]of the house.

[011-062]Paint me, then, a room seventeen feet by twelve, and not more than

[011-063]seven and a half feet high. This, reader, is somewhat ambitiously

[011-064]styled in my family the drawing-room; but being contrived “a double

[011-065]debt to pay,” it is also, and more justly, termed the library, for it

[011-066]happens that books are the only article of property in which I am

[011-067]richer than my neighbours. Of these I have about five thousand,

[011-068]collected gradually since my eighteenth year. Therefore, painter, put

[011-069]as many as you can into this room. Make it populous with books, and,

[011-070]furthermore, paint me a good fire, and furniture plain and modest,

[011-071]befitting the unpretending cottage of a scholar. And near the fire

[011-072]paint me a tea-table, and (as it is clear that no creature can come to

[011-073]see one such a stormy night) place only two cups and saucers on the

[011-074]tea-tray; and, if you know how to paint such a thing symbolically or

[011-075]otherwise, paint me an eternal tea-pot—eternal _à parte ante_ and _à

[011-076]parte post_—for I usually drink tea from eight o’clock at night to four

[011-077]o’clock in the morning. And as it is very unpleasant to make tea or to

[011-078]pour it out for oneself, paint me a lovely young woman sitting at the

[011-079]table. Paint her arms like Aurora’s and her smiles like Hebe’s. But no,

[011-080]dear M., not even in jest let me insinuate that thy power to illuminate

[011-081]my cottage rests upon a tenure so perishable as mere personal beauty,

[011-082]or that the witchcraft of angelic smiles lies within the empire of any

[011-083]earthly pencil. Pass then, my good painter, to something more within

[011-084]its power; and the next article brought forward should naturally be

[011-085]myself—a picture of the [[opium|Opium]]-eater, with his “little golden receptacle

[011-086]of the pernicious drug” lying beside him on the table. As to the opium,

[011-087]I have no objection to see a picture of _that_, though I would rather

[011-088]see the original. You may paint it if you choose, but I apprise you

[011-089]that no “little” receptacle would, even in 1816, answer _my_ purpose,

[011-090]who was at a distance from the “stately Pantheon,” and all druggists

[011-091](mortal or otherwise). No, you may as well paint the real receptacle,

[011-092]which was not of gold, but of glass, and as much like a wine-decanter

[011-093]as possible. Into this you may put a quart of ruby-coloured laudanum;

[011-094]that, and a book of German Metaphysics placed by its side, will

[011-095]sufficiently attest my being in the neighbourhood. But as to

[011-096]myself—there I demur. I admit that, naturally, I ought to occupy the

[011-097]foreground of the picture; that being the hero of the piece, or (if you

[011-098]choose) the criminal at the bar, my body should be had into court. This

[011-099]seems reasonable; but why should I confess on this point to a painter?

[011-100]or why confess at all? If the public (into whose private ear I am

[011-101]confidentially whispering my [[confession-genre|confessions]], and not into any painter’s)

[011-102]should chance to have framed some agreeable picture for itself of the

[011-103]Opium-eater’s exterior, should have ascribed to him, romantically an

[011-104]elegant person or a handsome face, why should I barbarously tear from

[011-105]it so pleasing a delusion—pleasing both to the public and to me? No;

[011-106]paint me, if at all, according to your own fancy, and as a painter’s

[011-107]fancy should teem with beautiful creations, I cannot fail in that way

[011-108]to be a gainer. And now, reader, we have run through all the ten

[011-109]categories of my condition as it stood about 1816-17, up to the middle

[011-110]of which latter year I judge myself to have been a happy man, and the

[011-111]elements of that happiness I have endeavoured to place before you in

[011-112]the above sketch of the interior of a scholar’s library, in a cottage

[011-113]among the mountains, on a stormy winter evening.

[011-114]But now, farewell—a long farewell—to happiness, winter or summer!

[011-115]Farewell to smiles and laughter! Farewell to peace of mind! Farewell to

[011-116]hope and to tranquil [[opium-dreams|dreams]], and to the blessed consolations of sleep.

[011-117]For more than three years and a half I am summoned away from these. I

[011-118]am now arrived at an Iliad of woes, for I have now to record

[011-119]THE PAINS OF OPIUM

[011-120]—as when some great painter dips

[011-121]His pencil in the [[melancholy|gloom]] of earthquake and eclipse.

[011-122]SHELLEY’S _Revolt of Islam_.

[011-123]Reader, who have thus far accompanied me, I must request your attention

[011-124]to a brief explanatory note on three points:

[011-125]1. For several reasons I have not been able to compose the notes for

[011-126]this part of my narrative into any regular and connected shape. I give

[011-127]the notes disjointed as I find them, or have now drawn them up from

[011-128]memory. Some of them point to their own date, some I have dated, and

[011-129]some are undated. Whenever it could answer my purpose to transplant

[011-130]them from the natural or chronological order, I have not scrupled to do

[011-131]so. Sometimes I speak in the present, sometimes in the past tense. Few

[011-132]of the notes, perhaps, were written exactly at the period of time to

[011-133]which they relate; but this can little affect their accuracy, as the

[011-134]impressions were such that they can never fade from my mind. Much has

[011-135]been omitted. I could not, without effort, constrain myself to the task

[011-136]of either recalling, or constructing into a regular narrative, the

[011-137]whole burthen of horrors which lies upon my brain. This feeling partly

[011-138]I plead in excuse, and partly that I am now in [[london|London]], and am a

[011-139]helpless sort of person, who cannot even arrange his own papers without

[011-140]assistance; and I am separated from the hands which are wont to perform

[011-141]for me the offices of an amanuensis.

[011-142]2. You will think perhaps that I am too confidential and communicative

[011-143]of my own private history. It may be so. But my way of writing is

[011-144]rather to think aloud, and follow my own humours, than much to consider

[011-145]who is listening to me; and if I stop to consider what is proper to be

[011-146]said to this or that person, I shall soon come to doubt whether any

[011-147]part at all is proper. The fact is, I place myself at a distance of

[011-148]fifteen or twenty years ahead of this time, and suppose myself writing

[011-149]to those who will be interested about me hereafter; and wishing to have

[011-150]some record of time, the entire history of which no one can know but

[011-151]myself, I do it as fully as I am able with the efforts I am now capable

[011-152]of making, because I know not whether I can ever find time to do it

[011-153]again.

[011-154]3. It will occur to you often to ask, why did I not release myself from

[011-155]the horrors of opium by leaving it off or diminishing it? To this I

[011-156]must answer briefly: it might be supposed that I yielded to the

[011-157]fascinations of opium too easily; it cannot be supposed that any man

[011-158]can be charmed by its terrors. The reader may be sure, therefore, that

[011-159]I made attempts innumerable to reduce the quantity. I add, that those

[011-160]who witnessed the agonies of those attempts, and not myself, were the

[011-161]first to beg me to desist. But could not have I reduced it a drop a

[011-162]day, or, by adding water, have bisected or trisected a drop? A thousand

[011-163]drops bisected would thus have taken nearly six years to reduce, and

[011-164]that way would certainly not have answered. But this is a common

[011-165]mistake of those who know nothing of opium experimentally; I appeal to

[011-166]those who do, whether it is not always found that down to a certain

[011-167]point it can be reduced with ease and even pleasure, but that after

[011-168]that point further reduction causes intense suffering. Yes, say many

[011-169]thoughtless persons, who know not what they are talking of, you will

[011-170]suffer a little low spirits and dejection for a few days. I answer, no;

[011-171]there is nothing like low spirits; on the contrary, the mere animal

[011-172]spirits are uncommonly raised: the pulse is improved: the health is

[011-173]better. It is not there that the suffering lies. It has no resemblance

[011-174]to the sufferings caused by renouncing wine. It is a state of

[011-175]unutterable irritation of stomach (which surely is not much like

[011-176]dejection), accompanied by intense perspirations, and feelings such as

[011-177]I shall not attempt to describe without more space at my command.
