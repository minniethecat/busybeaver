# Malay Incident

## Overview

The **Malay incident** is one of the most vivid and frequently discussed episodes in the *Confessions*. While living in a cottage in the English mountains, De Quincey received an unexpected visitor — a Malay traveller whose appearance, language barrier, and startling consumption of opium created a scene of surreal theatricality. The episode occupies only a few paragraphs of Section 10, yet its resonance extends throughout the remainder of the book, as the Malay becomes a recurrent figure in the author's opium dreams, "a fearful enemy... upon my dreams" who "brought other Malays with him, worse than himself" (010-154).

## The Encounter

### Arrival

"One day a Malay knocked at my door. What business a Malay could have to transact amongst English mountains I cannot conjecture" (010-088). De Quincey's opening to the episode acknowledges its intrinsic strangeness — the intrusion of the Orient into a remote corner of England. The servant girl who answered the door, "born and bred amongst the mountains, who had never seen an Asiatic dress of any sort," was confounded by his turban (010-092). With "his attainments in English... exactly of the same extent as hers in the Malay, there seemed to be an impassable gulf fixed between all communication of ideas" (010-096).

### The Tableau

De Quincey presents the scene as a painterly composition: "In a cottage kitchen, but panelled on the wall with dark wood that from age and rubbing resembled oak... stood the Malay — his turban and loose trousers of dingy white relieved upon the dark panelling" (010-109). The visual contrast between "the beautiful English face of the girl, and its exquisite fairness" and "the sallow and bilious skin of the Malay, enamelled or veneered with mahogany by marine air" (010-117) creates what De Quincey calls "a more striking picture... than any of the statuesque attitudes exhibited in the ballets at the Opera-house" (010-115).

### Communication and the Gift of Opium

Unable to speak Malay, De Quincey "addressed him in some lines from the Iliad, considering that, of such languages as I possessed, Greek, in point of longitude, came geographically nearest to an Oriental one" (010-129). The Malay "worshipped me in a most devout manner, and replied in what I suppose was Malay" (010-132). Before the visitor departed, De Quincey "presented him with a piece of opium" (010-136), reasoning that "to him, as an Orientalist... opium must be familiar" (010-136). To the author's consternation, the Malay "bolt the whole, divided into three pieces, at one mouthful" — "the quantity was enough to kill three dragoons and their horses" (010-141). Yet De Quincey "never heard of any Malay being found dead," and "became convinced that he must have been a habitué of opium" (010-150).

## Aftermath: The Malay in Dreams

The Malay did not remain a mere anecdote. De Quincey explains that the visitor "partly from the picturesque exhibition he assisted to frame, partly from the anxiety I connected with his opium" became a permanent inhabitant of his nightmares, "and brought other Malays with him, worse than himself, that ran 'a-muck' through my nightly visions" (010-157). This transformation — from quotidian encounter to nightmarish archetype — exemplifies the mechanism of the opium dream described in later sections.

## Key Quotations

> One day a Malay knocked at my door. What business a Malay could have to transact amongst English mountains I cannot conjecture. (010-088)

> The quantity was enough to kill three dragoons and their horses, and I felt some alarm for the poor creature; but what could be done? I had given him the opium in compassion for his solitary life. (010-141)

> This Malay... became a fearful enemy, I need not say a fiend, upon my dreams, and brought other Malays with him, worse than himself, that ran 'a-muck' through my nightly visions. (010-154)
