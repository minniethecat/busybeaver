# Samson Agonistes

## Overview

Samson Agonistes (1671) by John Milton. Milton's tragic closet drama (1671) — published in the same volume as *Paradise Regained* and referenced by De Quincey as part of the Miltonic literary universe that informs the *Confessions*.

## In the *Confessions*

Referenced in the passages cited. Its role in De Quincey's text is documented through these PN-cited references.

## De Quincey's Reading

The reference to *Samson Agonistes* occurs in one of the most candid passages of the later *Confessions*, where De Quincey describes the intellectual paralysis brought on by prolonged opium use. His studies, he confesses, have been "long interrupted" — he cannot read to himself "with any pleasure, hardly with a moment's endurance." Yet one faculty remains: reading aloud for the pleasure of others. And it is in this context that Milton's work appears:

> Of late, if I have felt moved by anything it has been by the grand lamentations of Samson Agonistes, or the great harmonies of the Satanic speeches in Paradise Regained, when read aloud by myself. (012-017)

The juxtaposition is revealing. Samson's lament — the blinded hero bound to the Philistine mill, bereft of his God-given strength — mirrors De Quincey's own condition: a man of once-prodigious intellectual power now "powerless as an infant," unable even to attempt the work he had planned. The "Satanic speeches" of *Paradise Regained*, by contrast, offer a counterpoint of defiant eloquence — the voice of a fallen being who has not yet succumbed to despair. Between lamentation and defiance, De Quincey locates his own state.

## Significance in the Narrative

The *Samson Agonistes* passage does more than illustrate De Quincey's literary taste. It anchors his suffering in a Miltonic framework of heroic affliction and spiritual trial. Samson's blindness — like the "phantoms" De Quincey sees painted on the darkness of his own eyelids — becomes a figure for the opium-eater's condition: a man who sees everything and can act on nothing. The "grand lamentations" are not simply something De Quincey reads; they are something he performs, his voice giving body to Milton's verse. In this act of reading aloud, the boundary between confessor and literary precursor dissolves: De Quincey becomes Samson, and the *Confessions* becomes his own lamentation.

The pairing of *Samson Agonistes* with *Paradise Regained* is also significant. Both were published together in 1671; both treat of trial, temptation, and ultimate restoration. In placing his own suffering within this Miltonic dyad, De Quincey implicitly claims for his *Confessions* the dignity of a spiritual autobiography — a narrative not merely of degradation but of possible redemption.
