[002-001]My father died when I was about seven years old, and left me to the

[002-002]care of four guardians. I was sent to various schools, great and small;

[002-003]and was very early distinguished for my classical attainments,

[002-004]especially for my knowledge of Greek. At thirteen I wrote Greek with

[002-005]ease; and at fifteen my command of that language was so great that I

[002-006]not only composed Greek verses in lyric metres, but could converse in

[002-007]Greek fluently and without embarrassment—an accomplishment which I have

[002-008]not since met with in any scholar of my times, and which in my case was

[002-009]owing to the practice of daily reading off the newspapers into the best

[002-010]Greek I could furnish _extempore_; for the necessity of ransacking my

[002-011]memory and invention for all sorts and combinations of periphrastic

[002-012]expressions as equivalents for modern ideas, images, relations of

[002-013]things, &c., gave me a compass of diction which would never have been

[002-014]called out by a dull translation of moral essays, &c. “That boy,” said

[002-015]one of my masters, pointing the attention of a stranger to me, “that

[002-016]boy could harangue an Athenian mob better than you and I could address

[002-017]an English one.” He who honoured me with this eulogy was a scholar,

[002-018]“and a ripe and a good one,” and of all my tutors was the only one whom

[002-019]I loved or reverenced. Unfortunately for me (and, as I afterwards

[002-020]learned, to this worthy man’s great indignation), I was transferred to

[002-021]the care, first of a blockhead, who was in a perpetual panic lest I

[002-022]should expose his ignorance; and finally to that of a respectable

[002-023]scholar at the head of a great school on an ancient foundation. This

[002-024]man had been appointed to his situation by —— College, [[oxford|Oxford]], and was

[002-025]a sound, well-built scholar, but (like most men whom I have known from

[002-026]that college) coarse, clumsy, and inelegant. A miserable contrast he

[002-027]presented, in my eyes, to the Etonian brilliancy of my favourite

[002-028]master; and beside, he could not disguise from my hourly notice the

[002-029]poverty and meagreness of his understanding. It is a bad thing for a

[002-030]boy to be and to know himself far beyond his tutors, whether in

[002-031]knowledge or in power of mind. This was the case, so far as regarded

[002-032]knowledge at least, not with myself only, for the two boys, who jointly

[002-033]with myself composed the first form, were better Grecians than the

[002-034]head-master, though not more elegant scholars, nor at all more

[002-035]accustomed to sacrifice to the Graces. When I first entered I remember

[002-036]that we read Sophocles; and it was a constant matter of triumph to us,

[002-037]the learned triumvirate of the first form, to see our “Archididascalus”

[002-038](as he loved to be called) conning our lessons before we went up, and

[002-039]laying a regular train, with lexicon and grammar, for blowing up and

[002-040]blasting (as it were) any difficulties he found in the choruses; whilst

[002-041]_we_ never condescended to open our books until the moment of going up,

[002-042]and were generally employed in writing epigrams upon his wig or some

[002-043]such important matter. My two class-fellows were poor, and dependent

[002-044]for their future prospects at the university on the recommendation of

[002-045]the head-master; but I, who had a small patrimonial property, the

[002-046]income of which was sufficient to support me at college, wished to be

[002-047]sent thither immediately. I made earnest representations on the subject

[002-048]to my guardians, but all to no purpose. One, who was more reasonable

[002-049]and had more knowledge of the world than the rest, lived at a distance;

[002-050]two of the other three resigned all their authority into the hands of

[002-051]the fourth; and this fourth, with whom I had to negotiate, was a worthy

[002-052]man in his way, but haughty, obstinate, and intolerant of all

[002-053]opposition to his will. After a certain number of letters and personal

[002-054]interviews, I found that I had nothing to hope for, not even a

[002-055]compromise of the matter, from my guardian. Unconditional submission

[002-056]was what he demanded, and I prepared myself, therefore, for other

[002-057]measures. Summer was now coming on with hasty steps, and my seventeenth

[002-058]birthday was fast approaching, after which day I had sworn within

[002-059]myself that I would no longer be numbered amongst schoolboys. Money

[002-060]being what I chiefly wanted, I wrote to a woman of high rank, who,

[002-061]though young herself, had known me from a child, and had latterly

[002-062]treated me with great distinction, requesting that she would “lend” me

[002-063]five guineas. For upwards of a week no answer came, and I was beginning

[002-064]to despond, when at length a servant put into my hands a double letter

[002-065]with a coronet on the seal. The letter was kind and obliging. The fair

[002-066]writer was on the sea-coast, and in that way the delay had arisen; she

[002-067]enclosed double of what I had asked, and good-naturedly hinted that if

[002-068]I should _never_ repay her, it would not absolutely ruin her. Now,

[002-069]then, I was prepared for my scheme. Ten guineas, added to about two

[002-070]which I had remaining from my pocket-money, seemed to me sufficient for

[002-071]an indefinite length of time; and at that happy age, if no _definite_

[002-072]boundary can be assigned to one’s power, the spirit of hope and

[002-073]pleasure makes it virtually infinite.

[002-074]It is a just remark of Dr. Johnson’s (and, what cannot often be said of

[002-075]his remarks, it is a very feeling one), that we never do anything

[002-076]consciously for the last time (of things, that is, which we have long

[002-077]been in the habit of doing) without sadness of heart. This truth I felt

[002-078]deeply when I came to leave ——, a place which I did not love, and where

[002-079]I had not been happy. On the evening before I left —— for ever, I

[002-080]grieved when the ancient and lofty schoolroom resounded with the

[002-081]evening service, performed for the last time in my hearing; and at

[002-082]night, when the muster-roll of names was called over, and mine (as

[002-083]usual) was called first, I stepped forward, and passing the

[002-084]head-master, who was standing by, I bowed to him, and looked earnestly

[002-085]in his face, thinking to myself, “He is old and infirm, and in this

[002-086]world I shall not see him again.” I was right; I never _did_ see him

[002-087]again, nor ever shall. He looked at me complacently, smiled

[002-088]good-naturedly, returned my salutation (or rather my valediction), and

[002-089]we parted (though he knew it not) for ever. I could not reverence him

[002-090]intellectually, but he had been uniformly kind to me, and had allowed

[002-091]me many indulgences; and I grieved at the thought of the mortification

[002-092]I should inflict upon him.

[002-093]The morning came which was to launch me into the world, and from which

[002-094]my whole succeeding life has in many important points taken its

[002-095]colouring. I lodged in the head-master’s house, and had been allowed

[002-096]from my first entrance the indulgence of a private room, which I used

[002-097]both as a sleeping-room and as a study. At half after three I rose, and

[002-098]gazed with deep emotion at the ancient towers of ——, “drest in earliest

[002-099]light,” and beginning to crimson with the radiant lustre of a cloudless

[002-100]July morning. I was firm and immovable in my purpose; but yet agitated

[002-101]by anticipation of uncertain danger and troubles; and if I could have

[002-102]foreseen the hurricane and perfect hail-storm of [[suffering|affliction]] which soon

[002-103]fell upon me, well might I have been agitated. To this agitation the

[002-104]deep peace of the morning presented an affecting contrast, and in some

[002-105]degree a medicine. The silence was more profound than that of midnight;

[002-106]and to me the silence of a summer morning is more touching than all

[002-107]other silence, because, the light being broad and strong as that of

[002-108]noonday at other seasons of the year, it seems to differ from perfect

[002-109]day chiefly because man is not yet abroad; and thus the peace of nature

[002-110]and of the innocent creatures of God seems to be secure and deep only

[002-111]so long as the presence of man and his restless and unquiet spirit are

[002-112]not there to trouble its sanctity. I dressed myself, took my hat and

[002-113]gloves, and lingered a little in the room. For the last year and a half

[002-114]this room had been my “pensive citadel”: here I had read and studied

[002-115]through all the hours of night, and though true it was that for the

[002-116]latter part of this time I, who was framed for love and gentle

[002-117]affections, had lost my gaiety and happiness during the strife and

[002-118]fever of contention with my guardian, yet, on the other hand, as a boy

[002-119]so passionately fond of books, and dedicated to intellectual pursuits,

[002-120]I could not fail to have enjoyed many happy hours in the midst of

[002-121]general dejection. I wept as I looked round on the chair, hearth,

[002-122]writing-table, and other familiar objects, knowing too certainly that I

[002-123]looked upon them for the last time. Whilst I write this it is eighteen

[002-124]years ago, and yet at this moment I see distinctly, as if it were

[002-125]yesterday, the lineaments and expression of the object on which I fixed

[002-126]my parting gaze. It was a picture of the lovely ——, which hung over the

[002-127]mantelpiece, the eyes and mouth of which were so beautiful, and the

[002-128]whole countenance so radiant with benignity and divine tranquillity,

[002-129]that I had a thousand times laid down my pen or my book to gather

[002-130]consolation from it, as a devotee from his patron saint. Whilst I was

[002-131]yet gazing upon it the deep tones of —— clock proclaimed that it was

[002-132]four o’clock. I went up to the picture, kissed it, and then gently

[002-133]walked out and closed the door for ever!

[002-134]So blended and intertwisted in this life are occasions of laughter and

[002-135]of tears, that I cannot yet recall without smiling an incident which

[002-136]occurred at that time, and which had nearly put a stop to the immediate

[002-137]execution of my plan. I had a trunk of immense weight, for, besides my

[002-138]clothes, it contained nearly all my library. The difficulty was to get

[002-139]this removed to a carrier’s: my room was at an aërial elevation in the

[002-140]house, and (what was worse) the staircase which communicated with this

[002-141]angle of the building was accessible only by a gallery, which passed

[002-142]the head-master’s chamber door. I was a favourite with all the

[002-143]servants, and knowing that any of them would screen me and act

[002-144]confidentially, I communicated my embarrassment to a groom of the

[002-145]head-master’s. The groom swore he would do anything I wished, and when

[002-146]the time arrived went upstairs to bring the trunk down. This I feared

[002-147]was beyond the strength of any one man; however, the groom was a man

[002-148]Of Atlantean shoulders, fit to bear

[002-149]The weight of mightiest monarchies;

[002-150]and had a back as spacious as Salisbury Plain. Accordingly he persisted

[002-151]in bringing down the trunk alone, whilst I stood waiting at the foot of

[002-152]the last flight in anxiety for the event. For some time I heard him

[002-153]descending with slow and firm steps; but unfortunately, from his

[002-154]trepidation, as he drew near the dangerous quarter, within a few steps

[002-155]of the gallery, his foot slipped, and the mighty burden falling from

[002-156]his shoulders, gained such increase of impetus at each step of the

[002-157]descent, that on reaching the bottom it trundled, or rather leaped,

[002-158]right across, with the noise of twenty devils, against the very bedroom

[002-159]door of the Archididascalus. My first thought was that all was lost,

[002-160]and that my only chance for executing a retreat was to sacrifice my

[002-161]baggage. However, on reflection I determined to abide the issue. The

[002-162]groom was in the utmost alarm, both on his own account and on mine,

[002-163]but, in spite of this, so irresistibly had the sense of the ludicrous

[002-164]in this unhappy _contretemps_ taken possession of his fancy, that he

[002-165]sang out a long, loud, and canorous peal of laughter, that might have

[002-166]wakened the Seven Sleepers. At the sound of this resonant merriment,

[002-167]within the very ears of insulted authority, I could not myself forbear

[002-168]joining in it; subdued to this, not so much by the unhappy _étourderie_

[002-169]of the trunk, as by the effect it had upon the groom. We both expected,

[002-170]as a matter of course, that Dr. —— would sally, out of his room, for in

[002-171]general, if but a mouse stirred, he sprang out like a mastiff from his

[002-172]kennel. Strange to say, however, on this occasion, when the noise of

[002-173]laughter had ceased, no sound, or rustling even, was to be heard in the

[002-174]bedroom. Dr. —— had a painful complaint, which, sometimes keeping him

[002-175]awake, made his sleep perhaps, when it did come, the deeper. Gathering

[002-176]courage from the silence, the groom hoisted his burden again, and

[002-177]accomplished the remainder of his descent without accident. I waited

[002-178]until I saw the trunk placed on a wheelbarrow and on its road to the

[002-179]carrier’s; then, “with Providence my guide,” I set off on foot,

[002-180]carrying a small parcel with some articles of dress under my arm; a

[002-181]favourite English poet in one pocket, and a small 12mo volume,

[002-182]containing about nine plays of Euripides, in the other.
