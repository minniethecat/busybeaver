# The Iliad

## Overview

The *Iliad* is Homer's epic poem of the Trojan War, the foundational text of the Western literary tradition. De Quincey references it at two significant moments in the *Confessions*, drawing on its cultural authority for both comic and tragic effect.

## Quoting Homer to the Malay

In one of the most darkly comic scenes in the *Confessions*, De Quincey attempts to communicate with the visiting Malay by quoting from the *Iliad* in the original Greek. The Malay, who speaks no English, responds in Malay — and De Quincey, with characteristic irony, notes that his "Greek had the same effect upon the Malay which his Malay had upon me." (010-129) The episode underscores the comic futility of classical learning when confronted with the actual East that De Quincey's opium dreams would later transform into nightmare.

## An Iliad of Woes

De Quincey deploys the *Iliad* as a metaphor for his own suffering in a second passage, writing: "I am now arrived at an Iliad of woes, for I have now to record the period of my worst sufferings." (011-118) The invocation of the *Iliad* as a synonym for catastrophe elevates De Quincey's personal ordeal to epic dimensions — the private pains of the opium-eater likened to the founding violence of Western literature.

## Classical Allusion as Rhetorical Strategy

Throughout the *Confessions*, De Quincey uses classical allusion — Homer, Euripides, the Greek tragedians — to lend epic gravity to his personal narrative. The *Iliad* serves this function par excellence: by casting his sufferings as Homeric, De Quincey transforms the sordid details of opium addiction into material worthy of the highest literary tradition.
