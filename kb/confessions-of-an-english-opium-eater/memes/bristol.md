## Appearances in the Text

Bristol enters the *Confessions* not as a destination but as a direction — the western terminus of the mail coach route that structures one of the narrative's most emotionally charged episodes. On a dark winter evening, having made arrangements for his departure from London, De Quincey set off "accompanied by Ann, towards Piccadilly; for it was my intention to go down as far as Salthill on the Bath or Bristol mail" (005-107). The two walked together through streets that have since vanished — Swallow Street, Golden Square, Sherrard Street — in what would prove to be their final hours together.

The Bristol mail reappears later in the same section as a marker of De Quincey's physical disorientation. Having fallen into an exhausted sleep on the coach, he travelled farther than intended: "Unfortunately, indeed, I *did* go rather farther than I intended, for so genial and so refreshing was my sleep, that the next time after leaving Hounslow that I fully awoke was upon the sudden pulling up of the mail" (005-187) — at Maidenhead, well past his planned stop. The coach continued toward Bath and Bristol, carrying its unconscious passenger beyond the point of return.

In the geography of the *Confessions*, Bristol represents the western limit — the direction of departure, the road not fully taken. It is never reached in the narrative, existing only as a name on the coach-board and a point on the horizon. This absence is itself significant: Bristol is the journey De Quincey began but never completed, just as his farewell to Ann was a parting whose finality neither of them could have anticipated.

The departure for Bristol with Ann is one of the most emotionally charged scenes in the *Confessions*:

> one winter evening I set off, accompanied by Ann, towards Piccadilly; for it was my intention to go down as far as Salthill on the Bath or Bristol mail. Our course lay through a part of the town which has now all disappeared, so that I can no longer retrace its ancient boundaries—Swallow Street, I think it was called.
>
> (005-107)
