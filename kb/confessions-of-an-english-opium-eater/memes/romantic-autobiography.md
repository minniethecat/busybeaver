# Romantic Autobiography

**Romantic autobiography** is the mode of literary self-examination and confession that emerged as a major genre during the Romantic period (c. 1780–1830). It is characterized by an intense focus on interior experience, the dramatization of the self, and the belief that personal narrative can illuminate universal human truths.

De Quincey grounds his confessional authority in a claim to philosophical seriousness:

> ...my life has been, on the whole, the life of a philosopher: from my birth I was made an intellectual creature, and intellectual in the highest sense my pursuits and pleasures have been, even from my schoolboy days.
>
> (001-053)

## De Quincey's Contribution

The *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* (1821) is one of the foundational texts of Romantic autobiography. It fuses several sub-genres:

- **The Confession**: Following Rousseau and St. Augustine, De Quincey presents his life as a moral drama of error, suffering, and (partial) redemption.
- **The Medical Case History**: De Quincey writes with the precision of a clinician, documenting doses, symptoms, and physiological effects with scientific detachment.
- **The Dream Vision**: The later sections of the *Confessions* adopt the mode of visionary literature — at once archaic (Dante, Milton) and thoroughly modern in their psychological complexity.

## To the Reader

De Quincey's preface "To the Reader" is itself a manifesto for Romantic autobiography. He acknowledges the English aversion to public self-exposure — "nothing, indeed, is more revolting to English feelings than the spectacle of a human being obtruding on our notice his moral ulcers or scars (001-015)" — but insists that his narrative serves a useful, instructive purpose. The paradox of Romantic autobiography is here laid bare: the claim to instructive utility masks (and enables) a profound impulse toward self-display.
