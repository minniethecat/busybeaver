# Eton

## Overview

The elite school whose 'Etonian brilliancy' De Quincey contrasts with his own wandering youth — an educational institution that represents both privilege and the path not taken.

## In the *Confessions*

Eton features in De Quincey's autobiographical narrative at key junctures. The PN references above document its appearances in the text, from which a fuller understanding of its role in the *Confessions* can be drawn through close reading of the cited passages.

## Appearances in the Text

Eton enters the *Confessions* first as a symbol of privileged culture rather than a physical place. De Quincey invokes "Etonian brilliancy" (002-027) to describe the dazzling intellectual polish of one of his favourite authors — a quality he contrasts, implicitly, with his own irregular and self-directed education.

The school becomes a concrete destination during the episode of the Jewish money-lender D——. Having received £10 from the lender, De Quincey "prepared to go down to Eton" (005-093) to secure the guarantee of a young nobleman friend who was then a student there. The plan was conceived by the money-lender himself, who saw in De Quincey's connection to an Etonian aristocrat the prospect of a profitable long-term relationship (005-077). Letters from this friend "were dated from Eton" (005-075), confirming his presence at the school during the period of these negotiations.

De Quincey's journey took him "from Slough to Eton," and he records that "the air and the sleep had both refreshed me" upon arrival (005-200) — a rare moment of physical well-being in a narrative otherwise dominated by privation and narcotic stupor. In the broader architecture of the *Confessions*, Eton represents the world of established privilege and institutional belonging from which De Quincey, by temperament and circumstance, was forever excluded.

## Key Passages

De Quincey records his arrival at Eton in a passage that underscores both his shabby appearance and the civility of its students:

> On my road I met some junior boys, of whom I made inquiries. An Etonian is always a gentleman; and, in spite of my shabby habiliments, they answered me civilly.
