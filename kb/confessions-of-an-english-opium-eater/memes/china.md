# China

## Geographic Identity

China in De Quincey's *Confessions* is less a real geographic entity than a symbolic locus within his opium-induced dream topography. It stands alongside "Indostan" (India) as one of the two great Asiatic spaces that populate his visions — realms of antiquity, vastness, and cultural otherness that both fascinate and terrify the dreaming narrator.

## Role in the Confessions

China enters the *Confessions* in the pivotal dream-sections, where De Quincey describes the architectural and demographic hallucinations that opium produces. He imagines fleeing England entirely for China — a fantasy of geographical escape that is also a flight from self:

> I thought I had done with England, and I entered a little boat, and sailed away from her shores. I was going to live in China. (013-167)

China functions as the polar opposite of England: where England is personal history, debt, and the memory of Ann, China is anonymity, immersion, and dissolution of identity. The passage from one to the other mirrors the psychological transit that opium itself effects.

## Orientalist Dream-Imagery

De Quincey's China is saturated with the orientalist imagery characteristic of early nineteenth-century European writing about Asia. He describes the terror of encountering a world fundamentally alien to European sensibilities:

> In China, over and above what it has in common with the rest of Southern Asia, I was terrified by the modes of life, by the manners, by the barrier of utter abhorrence placed between myself and them, by counter-sympathies deeper than I can analyse. (013-190)

The dream-logic of the *Confessions* assembles all of Asia into a single overwhelming composite: De Quincey describes how his visions "assembled them together in China or Indostan" — the two great Asiatic civilizations merging into one monstrous dreamscape. (013-201)

## The Mythological Dimension

The Asiatic dreams escalate from geographical terror to mythological persecution. De Quincey writes that "Vishnu hated me: Seeva laid wait for me" — the Hindu gods themselves becoming agents of psychological torment. (013-207) This transformation of cultural otherness into cosmic persecution represents the furthest reach of De Quincey's opium-induced paranoia, where the entirety of Asian civilization — its gods, its populations, its antiquity — becomes a single hostile force arrayed against the solitary dreamer.

## In the Arc of the *Confessions*

China's role in the *Confessions* is structural: it marks the extreme point of De Quincey's psychological dislocation. Where the early sections of the work are anchored in specific London streets and Welsh mountains, the later sections dissolve into the borderless geography of dream, where China and India merge, where centuries collapse, and where the individual self is threatened with absorption into an alien immensity. (013-190)(013-201)(013-207)
