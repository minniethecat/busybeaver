[017-001]{5} The [[bristol|Bristol]] mail is the best appointed in the Kingdom, owing to the

[017-002]double advantages of an unusually good road and of an extra sum for the

[017-003]expenses subscribed by the Bristol merchants.

[017-004]{6} It will be objected that many men, of the highest rank and wealth,

[017-005]have in our own day, as well as throughout our history, been amongst

[017-006]the foremost in courting danger in battle. True; but this is not the

[017-007]case supposed; long familiarity with power has to them deadened its

[017-008]effect and its attractions.

[017-009]{7} Φιλον υπνη θελyητρον επικουρον νοσον.

[017-010]{8} ηδυ δουλευμα. EURIP. Orest.

[017-011]{9} αναξανδρων ’Αyαμεμνων.

[017-012]{10} ομμα θεισ’ ειτω πεπλων. The scholar will know that throughout this

[017-013]passage I refer to the early scenes of the Orestes; one of the most

[017-014]beautiful exhibitions of the domestic affections which even the dramas

[017-015]of Euripides can furnish. To the English reader it may be necessary to

[017-016]say that the situation at the opening of the drama is that of a brother

[017-017]attended only by his sister during the demoniacal possession of a

[017-018]suffering conscience (or, in the mythology of the play, haunted by the

[017-019]Furies), and in circumstances of immediate danger from enemies, and of

[017-020]desertion or cold regard from nominal friends.

[017-021]{11} _Evanesced_: this way of going off the stage of life appears to

[017-022]have been well known in the 17th century, but at that time to have been

[017-023]considered a peculiar privilege of blood-royal, and by no means to be

[017-024]allowed to druggists. For about the year 1686 a poet of rather ominous

[017-025]name (and who, by-the-bye, did ample justice to his name), viz., Mr.

[017-026]_Flat-man_, in speaking of the death of Charles II. expresses his

[017-027]surprise that any prince should commit so absurd an act as dying,

[017-028]because, says he,

[017-029]“Kings should disdain to die, and only _disappear_.”

[017-030]They should _abscond_, that is, into the other world.

[017-031]{12} Of this, however, the learned appear latterly to have doubted; for

[017-032]in a pirated edition of Buchan’s _Domestic Medicine_, which I once saw

[017-033]in the hands of a farmer’s wife, who was studying it for the benefit of

[017-034]her health, the Doctor was made to say—“Be particularly careful never

[017-035]to take above five-and-twenty _ounces_ of [[opium|laudanum]] at once;” the true

[017-036]reading being probably five-and-twenty _drops_, which are held equal to

[017-037]about one grain of crude opium.

[017-038]{13} Amongst the great herd of travellers, &c., who show sufficiently

[017-039]by their stupidity that they never held any intercourse with opium, I

[017-040]must caution my readers specially against the brilliant author of

[017-041]_[[anastasius|Anastasius]]_. This gentleman, whose wit would lead one to presume him

[017-042]an [[opium-eating|opium-eater]], has made it impossible to consider him in that

[017-043]character, from the grievous misrepresentation which he gives of its

[017-044]effects at pp. 215-17 of vol. i. Upon consideration it must appear such

[017-045]to the author himself, for, waiving the errors I have insisted on in

[017-046]the text, which (and others) are adopted in the fullest manner, he will

[017-047]himself admit that an old gentleman “with a snow-white beard,” who eats

[017-048]“ample doses of opium,” and is yet able to deliver what is meant and

[017-049]received as very weighty counsel on the bad effects of that practice,

[017-050]is but an indifferent evidence that opium either kills people

[017-051]prematurely or sends them into a madhouse. But for my part, I see into

[017-052]this old gentleman and his motives: the fact is, he was enamoured of

[017-053]“the little golden receptacle of the pernicious drug” which Anastasius

[017-054]carried about him; and no way of obtaining it so safe and so feasible

[017-055]occurred as that of frightening its owner out of his wits (which, by

[017-056]the bye, are none of the strongest). This commentary throws a new light

[017-057]upon the case, and greatly improves it as a story; for the old

[017-058]gentleman’s speech, considered as a lecture on pharmacy, is highly

[017-059]absurd; but considered as a hoax on Anastasius, it reads excellently.

[017-060]{14} I have not the book at this moment to consult; but I think the

[017-061]passage begins—“And even that tavern music, which makes one man merry,

[017-062]another mad, in me strikes a deep fit of devotion,” &c.

[017-063]{15} A handsome newsroom, of which I was very politely made free in

[017-064]passing through [[manchester|Manchester]] by several gentlemen of that place, is

[017-065]called, I think, _The Porch_; whence I, who am a stranger in

[017-066]Manchester, inferred that the subscribers meant to profess themselves

[017-067]followers of Zeno. But I have been since assured that this is a

[017-068]mistake.

[017-069]{16} I here reckon twenty-five drops of laudanum as equivalent to one

[017-070]grain of opium, which, I believe, is the common estimate. However, as

[017-071]both may be considered variable quantities (the crude opium varying

[017-072]much in strength, and the tincture still more), I suppose that no

[017-073]infinitesimal accuracy can be had in such a calculation. Teaspoons vary

[017-074]as much in size as opium in strength. Small ones hold about 100 drops;

[017-075]so that 8,000 drops are about eighty times a teaspoonful. The reader

[017-076]sees how much I kept within Dr. Buchan’s indulgent allowance.

[017-077]{17} This, however, is not a necessary conclusion; the varieties of

[017-078]effect produced by opium on different constitutions are infinite. A

[017-079][[london|London]] magistrate (Harriott’s _Struggles through Life_, vol. iii. p.

[017-080]391, third edition) has recorded that, on the first occasion of his

[017-081]trying laudanum for the gout he took _forty_ drops, the next night

[017-082]_sixty_, and on the fifth night _eighty_, without any effect whatever;

[017-083]and this at an advanced age. I have an anecdote from a country surgeon,

[017-084]however, which sinks Mr. Harriott’s case into a trifle; and in my

[017-085]projected medical treatise on opium, which I will publish provided the

[017-086]College of Surgeons will pay me for enlightening their benighted

[017-087]understandings upon this subject, I will relate it; but it is far too

[017-088]good a story to be published gratis.

[017-089]{18} See the common accounts in any Eastern traveller or voyager of the

[017-090]frantic excesses committed by Malays who have taken opium, or are

[017-091]reduced to desperation by ill-luck at gambling.

[017-092]{19} The reader must remember what I here mean by _thinking_, because

[017-093]else this would be a very presumptuous expression. [[england|England]], of late,

[017-094]has been rich to excess in fine thinkers, in the departments of

[017-095]creative and combining thought; but there is a sad dearth of masculine

[017-096]thinkers in any analytic path. A Scotchman of eminent name has lately

[017-097]told us that he is obliged to quit even mathematics for want of

[017-098]encouragement.

[017-099]{20} William Lithgow. His book (Travels, &c.) is ill and pedantically

[017-100]written; but the account of his own sufferings on the rack at Malaga is

[017-101]overpoweringly affecting.

[017-102]{21} In saying this I mean no disrespect to the individual house, as

[017-103]the reader will understand when I tell him that, with the exception of

[017-104]one or two princely mansions, and some few inferior ones that have been

[017-105]coated with Roman cement, I am not acquainted with any house in this

[017-106]mountainous district which is wholly waterproof. The architecture of

[017-107]books, I flatter myself, is conducted on just principles in this

[017-108]country; but for any other architecture, it is in a barbarous state,

[017-109]and what is worse, in a retrograde state.

[017-140]What mean these abrupt relapses, the reader will ask perhaps, to such

[017-141]numbers as 300, 350, &c.? The _impulse_ to these relapses was mere

[017-142]infirmity of purpose; the _motive_, where any motive blended with this

[017-143]impulse, was either the principle, of “_reculer pour mieux sauter_;”

[017-144](for under the torpor of a large dose, which lasted for a day or two, a

[017-145]less quantity satisfied the stomach, which on awakening found itself

[017-146]partly accustomed to this new ration); or else it was this

[017-147]principle—that of sufferings otherwise equal, those will be borne best

[017-148]which meet with a mood of anger. Now, whenever I ascended to my large

[017-149]dose I was furiously incensed on the following day, and could then have

[017-150]borne anything.
