# Thomas De Quincey

## Biography

Thomas De Quincey (1785–1859) was born in Manchester and educated at Manchester Grammar School, Eton, and Worcester College, Oxford — though he left Oxford without taking a degree. He first used opium in 1804 while at Oxford, to relieve severe facial neuralgia, an event he recounts in memorable detail (001-168). This medicinal use gradually became habitual, and by the time of the *Confessions*' publication in 1821, he had been an opium-eater for nearly two decades.

De Quincey's early life was marked by the death of his father (a wealthy linen merchant) when Thomas was seven, a period of wandering in Wales and London as a teenager (1802–1803), and his formative friendship with the young prostitute [[ann|Ann]] — an episode that haunts the later opium dreams. His guardians eventually placed him at [[oxford|Oxford]], where his academic promise was undermined by the deepening opium habit.

In 1809 he moved to the [[lakes|Lake District]], where he became a close associate of [[william-wordsworth|William Wordsworth]], [[samuel-taylor-coleridge|Samuel Taylor Coleridge]], and [[robert-southey|Robert Southey]] — the inner circle of English Romanticism. He lived at Dove Cottage (Wordsworth's former home) from 1809 to 1820, supporting himself through literary journalism.

The *Confessions* was first published in the [[london-magazine|London Magazine]] in two parts (September and October 1821), and later expanded into a book in 1822. It was an immediate sensation — earning De Quincey both fame as a prose stylist and notoriety as a self-confessed addict. The work influenced [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]], [[charles-baudelaire|Charles Baudelaire]], and generations of confessional writers.

De Quincey spent his later years in Edinburgh, producing a vast body of essays, memoirs, and literary criticism. His other major works include *Suspiria de Profundis* (1845) and *The English Mail-Coach* (1849). He died in Edinburgh in 1859, aged 74.

## De Quincey as Narrator of the *Confessions*

The *Confessions* is remarkable for De Quincey's dual role: he is simultaneously the suffering subject and the analytical observer. He describes his own condition with clinical detachment, classifying himself among the "class of opium-eaters" whose "happiness" is a "false and treacherous tranquillity" (001-071). The narrative voice shifts between intimate confession — "I first came to be a regular opium-eater" (001-168) — and philosophical reflection on the nature of pleasure, pain, and memory.

De Quincey explicitly frames his work within the confessional tradition: "These preliminary confessions, or introductory narrative of the youthful incidents which first led me into the use of opium" (001-127). He is self-conscious about the genre, insisting that the autobiographical frame "cannot fail to render the confessions themselves more interesting" (001-144).

## Opium and Identity

The opium-eater identity is central to De Quincey's self-presentation. He distinguishes the "regular opium-eater" from casual users, describing the physical and psychological transformations that long-term use produces (007-153, 008-061). Yet he also critiques his fellow opium-eaters — "Turkish opium-eaters, it seems, are absurd enough to sit, like so many equestrian statues, on logs of wood" (008-131) — positioning himself as a more refined, intellectual consumer of the drug.

For De Quincey, opium is not merely an addiction but a key to expanded consciousness: the "pains of opium" (008-122) become inseparable from its "pleasures," and the drug's effect on dreaming and memory is a central theme of his literary output.

## Key Passages

> Nothing, indeed, is more revolting to English feelings than the spectacle of a human being obtruding on our notice his moral ulcers or scars (001-018)

> It is so long since I first took opium that if it had been a trifling incident in my life I might have forgotten its date; but cardinal events are not to be forgotten (001-168)

## Literary Influence

The *Confessions* established a new literary mode — the drug confessional — and directly influenced:
- [[edgar-allan-poe|Edgar Allan Poe]]'s tales of altered consciousness
- Baudelaire's *Les Paradis Artificiels* (1860), a partial translation and commentary
- The modern addiction memoir as a genre
- 20th-century explorations of drug-induced creativity
