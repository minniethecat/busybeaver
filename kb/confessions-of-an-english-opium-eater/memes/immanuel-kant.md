# Immanuel Kant

## Biography

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German philosopher whose works form a cornerstone of modern philosophy. The German philosopher whose works De Quincey reports reading and understanding under opium's influence — a figure representing the intellectual ambition that opium allegedly supported rather than undermined.

## In De Quincey's *Confessions*

De Quincey invokes Kant at several key points in the *Confessions*. During the period of his deepest opium consumption, he reports having been "chiefly studying German metaphysics in the writings of Kant, Fichte, Schelling, &c." (009-169)(009-170) This intellectual engagement while under opium's influence forms part of his argument that the drug, far from destroying mental capacity, could enhance it.

De Quincey makes the striking claim: "I read Kant again, and again I understood him, or fancied that I did." (010-078) He describes his feelings of pleasure expanding "to all around" upon this renewed engagement with Kant's philosophy. (010-079)

> I read Kant again, and again I understood him, or fancied that I did. Again my feelings of pleasure expanded themselves to all around me; and if any man from Oxford or Cambridge, or from neither, had been announced to me in my unpretending cottage, I should have welcomed him with as sumptuous a reception as so poor a man could offer.
>
> (010-078)(010-082)

Kant also appears in a self-deprecating passage where De Quincey reflects on a critic: "neither has he read Kant in his manhood (which is his misfortune)." (016-157) The parenthetical aside reveals De Quincey's regard for Kant as an essential mark of intellectual maturity.

## Relationship to De Quincey

Kant represents, in the rhetorical strategy of the *Confessions*, the intellectual heights that opium supposedly enabled De Quincey to reach — a counter-argument to the prevailing view that opium use necessarily degraded the mind. The German metaphysical tradition, with Kant at its apex, serves as evidence for the claim that the opium-eater could remain a serious thinker.
