# Anastasius

**_Anastasius, or Memoirs of a Greek_** (1819) — [[Thomas Hope]]'s sprawling Orientalist novel — makes a single, vivid appearance in the *Confessions*, but that appearance is telling. During the [[Malay Visitor|Malay incident]] in Section 10, De Quincey records that he knew the Turkish word for [[Opium|opium]] ("madjoon") because he had encountered it "in the common novel of 'Anastasius'" (010-127). This casual reference — "the common novel" — captures both the book's popularity in Regency Britain and De Quincey's characteristic blend of erudition and self-deprecation.

## The "Madjoon" Reference

The single citation in the *Confessions* occurs during the Malay's visit to De Quincey's cottage. After giving the Malay a dangerously large piece of opium, De Quincey attempts to communicate:

> I knew from "Anastasius" that the Turks used opium under the name of "madjoon." (010-127)

The reference is characteristic of De Quincey's method: a novel — a work of fiction — is treated as a source of ethnographic knowledge, on par with travel writing or medical literature. This blurring of informational and imaginative sources is central to the *Confessions*' epistemology. De Quincey does not distinguish between learning about opium from a druggist, a surgeon, a philosopher, or a novelist — all are valid informants in his synthetic method.

## About the Novel

Thomas Hope's *Anastasius* (published anonymously in 1819) was one of the most successful Orientalist novels of the Regency period. Its key characteristics illuminate why De Quincey might have reached for it:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Author | Thomas Hope (1769–1831), Dutch-British collector and writer |
| Publication | 1819, John Murray (also Byron's publisher) |
| Form | Fictional memoir of a Greek adventurer across the Ottoman Empire |
| Reception | Enormously popular; attributed by some to Byron |
| Oriental Content | Richly detailed accounts of Turkish customs, drug use, and daily life |

The novel's detailed ethnographic passages about Ottoman life — including drug culture — made it a plausible source for De Quincey's pharmacological vocabulary. That he calls it "the common novel" suggests both its wide circulation and his slightly superior attitude toward it as mere popular fiction, beneath the dignity of serious scholarship but useful all the same.

## Significance in the *Confessions*

The *Anastasius* reference matters for three reasons:

1. **It reveals De Quincey's source ecology.** His knowledge of opium came not only from personal experience and medical literature but from novels, travelogues, and conversation — a heterogeneous information environment typical of the Romantic period.

2. **It anchors the Malay incident in its cultural moment.** The Malay's visit is made possible, narratively, by De Quincey's access to Orientalist knowledge through popular fiction. Without *Anastasius*, De Quincey cannot communicate the word "madjoon" — and the entire episode's comedy of cross-cultural miscommunication would be diminished.

3. **It exemplifies De Quincey's citational practice.** His willingness to cite a "common novel" alongside medical authorities and classical philosophers reflects the *Confessions*' democratic approach to sources — anything that illuminates the opium experience is admissible.
