# Wales

## Overview

The region where De Quincey's early wanderings took place — a space of poverty, subsistence, and the pre-opium phase of his life that the Confessions' introductory narrative recounts.

## Appearances in the Text

Wales is the destination of De Quincey's first flight from school and the landscape of his earliest independent wanderings. Although he had "originally intended to proceed to Westmoreland," accident "gave a different direction to my wanderings, and I bent my steps towards North Wales" (003-004). There he wandered through Denbighshire, Merionethshire, and Carnarvonshire before taking lodgings in a small house in B——, where provisions were cheap and he might have stayed in comfort.

His life in Wales was one of extreme simplicity and frequent privation. "So long as I remained in Wales," he records, "I subsisted either on" the barest means (003-097), and he notes that this period of subsistence — "generally in Wales, and always for the first two months" (003-177) — defined the economic condition of his wandering youth. While still in Wales, he "had received" letters that shaped his next decisions (005-069), setting in motion the journey that would eventually carry him to London.

In the geography of the *Confessions*, Wales represents the pre-opium, pre-London phase of De Quincey's life — a pastoral interlude of solitary walking, cheap lodgings, and the radical independence of a boy who had chosen vagrancy over the institutional comforts of school and guardianship. It is the last phase of his life before the encounter with opium and with Ann that would transform everything.

De Quincey's account of subsistence in Wales captures the radical simplicity of his wandering life:

> afterwards, so long as I remained in Wales, I subsisted either on blackberries, hips, haws, &c., or on the casual hospitalities which I now and then received in return for such little services as I had an opportunity of rendering.

> (003-097)
