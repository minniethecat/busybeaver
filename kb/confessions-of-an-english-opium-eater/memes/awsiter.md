# Awsiter (Apothecary)

## Biography

Awsiter (Apothecary) (unknown–unknown) was English. The apothecary to Greenwich Hospital whose 1763 'Essay on the Effects of Opium' De Quincey cites as an example of medical ignorance — a representative figure in De Quincey's polemic against the medical establishment.

## Relationship to De Quincey

Referenced in the *Confessions* in connection with De Quincey's narrative of opium experience.

## De Quincey's Citation

Awsiter appears in the opening pages of the *Confessions* as a specimen of medical authority De Quincey means to subvert. Having established that "the fascinating powers of opium are admitted even by medical writers, who are its greatest enemies," De Quincey summons Awsiter as his first expert witness for the prosecution — only to turn the testimony inside out:

> Thus, for instance, Awsiter, apothecary to Greenwich Hospital, in his "Essay on the Effects of Opium" (published in the year 1763), when attempting to explain why Mead had not been sufficiently explicit on the properties, counteragents, &c., of this drug, expresses himself in the following mysterious terms. (001-110)

The adjective "mysterious" is a barbed compliment. Awsiter had argued that the full properties of opium must be kept secret lest knowledge of its pleasures spread uncontrollably — a position De Quincey quotes at length only to demolish. By the time De Quincey finishes his rebuttal, Awsiter has become an unwitting ally: the medical writer who warned against opium's seductive power has inadvertently produced the most eloquent advertisement for the drug in the *Confessions*.

## Role in the Confessions

Awsiter's function in De Quincey's rhetoric is precise. He stands for everything De Quincey opposes — medical authority that presumes to know opium from the outside, through professional observation rather than lived experience. That Awsiter was apothecary to Greenwich Hospital — a respected institutional position — only sharpens the irony. De Quincey, the anonymous magazine writer and confessed addict, claims a deeper knowledge of opium than the credentialed expert. The confrontation between the apothecary and the opium-eater is, in miniature, the epistemological argument of the entire *Confessions*: experience trumps authority, and the best witness is the one who has suffered what he describes.
