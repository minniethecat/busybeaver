# Mithridates (Adelung)

## Overview

Mithridates (Adelung) (1806) by Johann Christoph Adelung. Johann Christoph Adelung's linguistic reference work (1806) — mentioned by De Quincey in a comic aside during the Malay incident as a book that 'might have helped me to a few words' of Malay.

## In the *Confessions*

This work is referenced in the *Confessions*. Its connection to De Quincey's text is established through the cited passages.

## The Malay Incident

The reference to Adelung's *Mithridates* occurs during one of the most celebrated set-pieces in the *Confessions*: the visit of an unexpected Malay to De Quincey's cottage in the English mountains. The episode begins with a comic collision of worlds. A Malay traveller — turbaned, "sallow and bilious," eyes "small, fierce, restless" — appears at the door of a remote cottage, where a young English servant girl, who has "never seen an Asiatic dress of any sort," takes him for "a sort of demon." She appeals to her master, the scholar, to exorcise the visitor. De Quincey descends to find a scene worthy of an opera-house tableau.

The comedy turns on language — or rather, on its complete absence. De Quincey's "knowledge of the Oriental tongues" proves decidedly thin:

> My knowledge of the Oriental tongues is not remarkably extensive, being indeed confined to two words — the Arabic word for barley and the Turkish for opium (madjoon), which I have learned from *Anastasius*; and as I had neither a Malay dictionary nor even Adelung's *Mithridates*, which might have helped me to a few words, I addressed him in some lines from the Iliad, considering that, of such languages as I possessed, Greek, in point of longitude, came geographically nearest to an Oriental one. (010-128)

The logic is lunatic and magnificent: lacking Malay, lacking even Adelung's universal language catalogue, De Quincey deploys Homeric Greek — on the geographical theory that Greece lies between England and the East. The Malay, magnificently, "worshipped me in a most devout manner, and replied in what I suppose was Malay."

## Adelung's Work and Its Comic Function

Johann Christoph Adelung's *Mithridates, oder allgemeine Sprachenkunde* (1806) was a monumental survey of the world's languages — precisely the kind of Enlightenment reference work that a scholar in De Quincey's position would be expected to own. Its title invoked Mithridates VI of Pontus, the ancient king said to have spoken twenty-two languages. By confessing that he possesses neither this universal key to tongues nor even a simple Malay dictionary, De Quincey performs a double mockery: of his own scholarly pretensions, and of the Enlightenment fantasy that knowledge could be catalogued and mastered. Adelung's *Mithridates* — the book that might have bridged the gulf between the English scholar and the Malay traveller — stands in the *Confessions* as the absent book, the unfilled shelf, the piece of learned apparatus that would have made communication rational but whose absence makes the scene sublime.

## The Dream-Sequel

The Malay incident, trivial as it seems, becomes one of the generative images of De Quincey's opium dreams. The Malay "fastened afterwards upon my dreams, and brought other Malays with him, worse than himself, that ran 'a-muck' at me, and led me into a world of troubles" (010-157). Thus Adelung's *Mithridates* — a dry work of Enlightenment philology — becomes, by its very absence, the unwitting midwife of one of the most famous dream-sequences in English literature.
