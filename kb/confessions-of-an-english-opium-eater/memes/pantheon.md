# Pantheon (Oxford Street)

## Overview

The London landmark near which De Quincey purchased his first opium — "the stately Pantheon" as Wordsworth called it, a specific architectural anchor in the geography of the *Confessions*. The Pantheon on Oxford Street was a well-known London entertainment venue in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries.

## The First Opium Purchase

The Pantheon is immortalized in the *Confessions* as the landmark beside which De Quincey made his epochal first opium purchase. Walking home through Oxford Street, he came upon the druggist's shop:

> My way homewards lay through Oxford Street; and near "the stately Pantheon" (as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it) I saw a druggist's shop.
>
> (007-116)(007-117)

The druggist, whom De Quincey later calls "the beatific druggist," dispensed the opium that would define the rest of his life. (009-187)

## A Landmark of Memory

The Pantheon becomes a recurring reference point in De Quincey's mental geography. When he returns to London and seeks out the druggist, he goes "to the stately Pantheon, and found him not." (007-128) The druggist's disappearance — he had "evaporated" — transforms the Pantheon from a landmark of physical geography into one of memory and loss. De Quincey's grief at this discovery is rendered with characteristic hyperbole: "to me, who knew not his name, he seemed a god."

Later, reflecting on his opium history, De Quincey contrasts a distant apothecary — "who was at a distance from the 'stately Pantheon'" — with the original druggist "in the neighbourhood of" that same landmark. (011-090) The Pantheon thus serves as the fixed point in a shifting urban geography of addiction.

## In London's Geography

The Pantheon stood on Oxford Street, one of London's principal thoroughfares. Its proximity to De Quincey's route home made the fateful encounter with the druggist all but inevitable — a collision of urban geography and personal destiny that structures the *Confessions*' narrative of origins.
