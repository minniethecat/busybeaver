## Appearances in the Text

Cambridge appears in the *Confessions* as a single glancing reference, but one loaded with significance for understanding De Quincey's relationship to the English class system. Describing his visit to the Earl of D——, one of his "patrician friends," De Quincey notes that the young nobleman "was still at Eton, though I believe on the wing for Cambridge" (006-045). The phrase "on the wing" — suggesting a bird in flight, already in motion toward its destination — captures the smooth trajectory from Eton to Cambridge that was the established path for young men of rank.

The contrast with De Quincey's own educational trajectory could hardly be sharper. While the Earl progressed from Eton to Cambridge along the well-oiled tracks of privilege, De Quincey had fled Manchester Grammar School, wandered through Wales, and was now in London, sleeping in an empty house and surviving on the margins. Cambridge represents the institutional path not taken — the world of formal scholarship, collegiate belonging, and social legitimacy from which De Quincey, by a combination of temperament and circumstance, had permanently diverged.

Yet the relationship between De Quincey and his aristocratic friend was genuine. The Earl received him kindly and asked him to breakfast, and De Quincey is at pains to insist that his acquaintance with such figures was not mere name-dropping but real connection. Cambridge, in this light, is less a place than a symbol: the door that stood open to others but remained closed to the Opium-Eater.
