# Royal College of Surgeons

The **Royal College of Surgeons** (and the associated **London College of Physicians**) serves a single, rhetorically devastating function in the *Confessions*: as the embodiment of institutional medical authority against which [[Thomas De Quincey]] arrays his personal testimony. The College's "official historian" of opium receives De Quincey's most famous polemical broadside — a dismissal so comprehensive that it has echoed through two centuries of drug literature.

## Role in De Quincey's Polemic

De Quincey's relationship to the medical establishment of his day was adversarial from the start. He positioned himself not as a patient seeking treatment but as an expert witness whose years of firsthand opium experience gave him an authority that no physician could match. The College of Surgeons, as the institutional gatekeeper of medical knowledge in early 19th-century Britain, was the natural target of this claim:

> Upon all that has been hitherto written on the subject of opium, whether by travellers in Turkey (who may plead their privilege of lying as an old immemorial right), or by professors of medicine writing *ex cathedrâ*, I have but one emphatic criticism to pronounce — Lies! lies! lies! (007-164)

The Latin phrase *ex cathedrâ* — "from the chair," denoting authoritative papal pronouncement — is pointed. De Quincey frames the College's pronouncements not as science but as dogma, and himself as the reformer who will replace institutional falsehood with experiential truth.

## The "Official Historian"

The specific target of De Quincey's ire appears to be the body of medical literature on opium that the College had sanctioned or produced. While he does not name a particular physician, his complaint is structural: the medical profession, in his view, had never taken opium itself seriously as an object of study, relying instead on secondhand reports and moralistic condemnation:

> I have been assured that several of the most distinguished surgeons in London are daily in the habit of taking opium themselves. (001-074)

This claim — that the very surgeons who condemned opium were secret consumers of it — is a characteristic De Quincey move: using insider knowledge to undermine institutional authority.

## In the *Confessions*

The College appears primarily as a rhetorical foil. De Quincey's project — to write a truthful account of opium from the inside — depends on discrediting the medical literature that preceded it. The College of Surgeons, as the institutional centre of that literature, must be refuted before De Quincey's personal testimony can be accepted.

## Historical Context

The Royal College of Surgeons of England received its royal charter in 1800, just a few years before De Quincey's first opium experience. The early 19th century was a period of professionalization for British medicine, as the old tripartite division (physicians, surgeons, apothecaries) gave way to more modern forms of medical authority. De Quincey's attack on the College should be understood in this context: he was not merely quarrelling with a particular claim about opium but challenging the very idea that institutional medicine had privileged access to truth about the body and its experiences.
