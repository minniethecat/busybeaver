# Pains of Opium

## Definition

The **pains of opium** form the second half of De Quincey's confessional structure, explicitly counterbalancing the rapturous description of the drug's pleasures in Sections 7–8. The phrase is De Quincey's own — a section heading that announces the descent from paradise into hell. The pains are not merely physical (though the body certainly suffers) but encompass psychological torment, spiritual desolation, and the peculiar horror of the opium dreams. Together they constitute one of the earliest and most psychologically sophisticated accounts of substance dependence and withdrawal in Western literature.

## In the *Confessions*

### The Structure of Confession

De Quincey frames the entire narrative in terms of struggle and self-conquest. The opening section establishes the stakes: "I have struggled against this fascinating enthralment with a religious zeal, and have at length accomplished what I never yet heard attributed to any other man — have untwisted, almost to its final links, the accursed chain which fettered me" (001-059). The metaphor of the "accursed chain" (001-061) runs throughout the text, positioning opium as a form of bondage from which the author has miraculously freed himself.

### Physical and Mental Torment

The pains include physical suffering — the bodily dependence that drove De Quincey to ever-increasing doses. At his peak, he consumed "320 grains of opium (*i.e*. eight thousand drops of laudanum) per day" (010-067), a dose he himself describes as "the poison of 8000 drops of laudanum per day" (010-170). The reduction to forty grains brought temporary relief but also demonstrated the depth of physiological adaptation. The mental torments are more terrible still: nightmares of "chasms and sunless abysses," temporal distortions where moments stretched into millennia, and the invasion of Oriental imagery into his dream-life.

### The Paradox of Pain as Pleasure's Shadow

Crucially, De Quincey's pains are not simply the consequences of abuse but the inseparable shadow of opium's pleasures. The very intensity of the initial experiences — "an apocalypse of the world within me" (007-141) — makes their withdrawal proportionally devastating. The text's structure mirrors this: the "Pleasures" section is relatively brief and ecstatic; the "Pains" section dominates the later chapters with its dark majesty.

## Key Quotations

> I have struggled against this fascinating enthralment with a religious zeal, and have at length accomplished what I never yet heard attributed to any other man — have untwisted, almost to its final links, the accursed chain which fettered me. (001-059)

> The poison of 8000 drops of laudanum per day (just for the same reason as a man would take a glass of wine). (010-170)
