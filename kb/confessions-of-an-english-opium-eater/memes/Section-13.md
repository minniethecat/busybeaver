[013-001]2. For this and all other changes in my [[opium-dreams|dreams]] were accompanied by

[013-002]deep-seated anxiety and gloomy melancholy, such as are wholly

[013-003]incommunicable by words. I seemed every night to descend, not

[013-004]metaphorically, but literally to descend, into chasms and sunless

[013-005]abysses, depths below depths, from which it seemed hopeless that I

[013-006]could ever reascend. Nor did I, by waking, feel that I _had_

[013-007]reascended. This I do not dwell upon; because the state of [[melancholy|gloom]] which

[013-008]attended these gorgeous spectacles, amounting at last to utter

[013-009]darkness, as of some suicidal despondency, cannot be approached by

[013-010]words.

[013-011]3. The sense of space, and in the end the sense of time, were both

[013-012]powerfully affected. Buildings, landscapes, &c., were exhibited in

[013-013]proportions so vast as the bodily eye is not fitted to receive. Space

[013-014]swelled, and was amplified to an extent of unutterable infinity. This,

[013-015]however, did not disturb me so much as the vast expansion of time; I

[013-016]sometimes seemed to have lived for 70 or 100 years in one night—nay,

[013-017]sometimes had feelings representative of a millennium passed in that

[013-018]time, or, however, of a duration far beyond the limits of any human

[013-019]experience.

[013-020]4. The minutest incidents of childhood, or forgotten scenes of later

[013-021]years, were often revived: I could not be said to recollect them, for

[013-022]if I had been told of them when waking, I should not have been able to

[013-023]acknowledge them as parts of my past experience. But placed as they

[013-024]were before me, in dreams like intuitions, and clothed in all their

[013-025]evanescent circumstances and accompanying feelings, I _recognised_ them

[013-026]instantaneously. I was once told by a near relative of mine, that

[013-027]having in her childhood fallen into a river, and being on the very

[013-028]verge of death but for the critical assistance which reached her, she

[013-029]saw in a moment her whole life, in its minutest incidents, arrayed

[013-030]before her simultaneously as in a mirror; and she had a faculty

[013-031]developed as suddenly for comprehending the whole and every part. This,

[013-032]from some opium experiences of mine, I can believe; I have indeed seen

[013-033]the same thing asserted twice in modern books, and accompanied by a

[013-034]remark which I am convinced is true; viz., that the dread book of

[013-035]account which the Scriptures speak of is in fact the mind itself of

[013-036]each individual. Of this at least I feel assured, that there is no such

[013-037]thing as _forgetting_ possible to the mind; a thousand accidents may

[013-038]and will interpose a veil between our present consciousness and the

[013-039]secret inscriptions on the mind; accidents of the same sort will also

[013-040]rend away this veil; but alike, whether veiled or unveiled, the

[013-041]inscription remains for ever, just as the stars seem to withdraw before

[013-042]the common light of day, whereas in fact we all know that it is the

[013-043]light which is drawn over them as a veil, and that they are waiting to

[013-044]be revealed when the obscuring daylight shall have withdrawn.

[013-045]Having noticed these four facts as memorably distinguishing my dreams

[013-046]from those of health, I shall now cite a case illustrative of the first

[013-047]fact, and shall then cite any others that I remember, either in their

[013-048]chronological order, or any other that may give them more effect as

[013-049]pictures to the reader.

[013-050]I had been in youth, and even since, for occasional amusement, a great

[013-051]reader of Livy, whom I confess that I prefer, both for style and

[013-052]matter, to any other of the Roman historians; and I had often felt as

[013-053]most solemn and appalling sounds, and most emphatically representative

[013-054]of the majesty of the Roman people, the two words so often occurring in

[013-055]Livy—_Consul Romanus_, especially when the consul is introduced in his

[013-056]military character. I mean to say that the words king, sultan, regent,

[013-057]&c., or any other titles of those who embody in their own persons the

[013-058]collective majesty of a great people, had less power over my

[013-059]reverential feelings. I had also, though no great reader of history,

[013-060]made myself minutely and critically familiar with one period of English

[013-061]history, viz., the period of the Parliamentary War, having been

[013-062]attracted by the moral grandeur of some who figured in that day, and by

[013-063]the many interesting memoirs which survive those unquiet times. Both

[013-064]these parts of my lighter reading, having furnished me often with

[013-065]matter of reflection, now furnished me with matter for my dreams. Often

[013-066]I used to see, after painting upon the blank darkness a sort of

[013-067]rehearsal whilst waking, a crowd of ladies, and perhaps a festival and

[013-068]dances. And I heard it said, or I said to myself, “These are English

[013-069]ladies from the unhappy times of Charles I. These are the wives and the

[013-070]daughters of those who met in peace, and sate at the same table, and

[013-071]were allied by marriage or by blood; and yet, after a certain day in

[013-072]August 1642, never smiled upon each other again, nor met but in the

[013-073]field of battle; and at Marston Moor, at Newbury, or at Naseby, cut

[013-074]asunder all ties of love by the cruel sabre, and washed away in blood

[013-075]the memory of ancient friendship.” The ladies danced, and looked as

[013-076]lovely as the court of George IV. Yet I knew, even in my dream, that

[013-077]they had been in the grave for nearly two centuries. This pageant would

[013-078]suddenly dissolve; and at a clapping of hands would be heard the

[013-079]heart-quaking sound _of Consul Romanus_; and immediately came “sweeping

[013-080]by,” in gorgeous paludaments, Paulus or Marius, girt round by a company

[013-081]of centurions, with the crimson tunic hoisted on a spear, and followed

[013-082]by the _alalagmos_ of the Roman legions.

[013-083]Many years ago, when I was looking over Piranesi’s Antiquities of Rome,

[013-084]Mr. Coleridge, who was standing by, described to me a set of plates by

[013-085]that artist, called his _Dreams_, and which record the scenery of his

[013-086]own visions during the delirium of a fever. Some of them (I describe

[013-087]only from memory of Mr. Coleridge’s account) represented vast Gothic

[013-088]halls, on the floor of which stood all sorts of engines and machinery,

[013-089]wheels, cables, pulleys, levers, catapults, &c. &c., expressive of

[013-090]enormous power put forth and resistance overcome. Creeping along the

[013-091]sides of the walls you perceived a staircase; and upon it, groping his

[013-092]way upwards, was Piranesi himself: follow the stairs a little further

[013-093]and you perceive it come to a sudden and abrupt termination without any

[013-094]balustrade, and allowing no step onwards to him who had reached the

[013-095]extremity except into the depths below. Whatever is to become of poor

[013-096]Piranesi, you suppose at least that his labours must in some way

[013-097]terminate here. But raise your eyes, and behold a second flight of

[013-098]stairs still higher, on which again Piranesi is perceived, but this

[013-099]time standing on the very brink of the abyss. Again elevate your eye,

[013-100]and a still more aërial flight of stairs is beheld, and again is poor

[013-101]Piranesi busy on his aspiring labours; and so on, until the unfinished

[013-102]stairs and Piranesi both are lost in the upper gloom of the hall. With

[013-103]the same power of endless growth and self-reproduction did my

[013-104]architecture proceed in dreams. In the early stage of my malady the

[013-105]splendours of my dreams were indeed chiefly architectural; and I beheld

[013-106]such pomp of cities and palaces as was never yet beheld by the waking

[013-107]eye unless in the clouds. From a great modern poet I cite part of a

[013-108]passage which describes, as an appearance actually beheld in the

[013-109]clouds, what in many of its circumstances I saw frequently in sleep:

[013-110]The appearance, instantaneously disclosed,

[013-111]Was of a mighty city—boldly say

[013-112]A wilderness of building, sinking far

[013-113]And self-withdrawn into a wondrous depth,

[013-114]Far sinking into splendour—without end!

[013-115]Fabric it seem’d of diamond, and of gold,

[013-116]With alabaster domes, and silver spires,

[013-117]And blazing terrace upon terrace, high

[013-118]Uplifted; here, serene pavilions bright

[013-119]In avenues disposed; there towers begirt

[013-120]With battlements that on their restless fronts

[013-121]Bore stars—illumination of all gems!

[013-122]By earthly nature had the effect been wrought

[013-123]Upon the dark materials of the storm

[013-124]Now pacified; on them, and on the coves,

[013-125]And mountain-steeps and summits, whereunto

[013-126]The vapours had receded,—taking there

[013-127]Their station under a Cerulean sky. &c. &c.

[013-128]The sublime circumstance, “battlements that on their _restless_ fronts

[013-129]bore stars,” might have been copied from my architectural dreams, for

[013-130]it often occurred. We hear it reported of Dryden and of Fuseli, in

[013-131]modern times, that they thought proper to eat raw meat for the sake of

[013-132]obtaining splendid dreams: how much better for such a purpose to have

[013-133]eaten opium, which yet I do not remember that any poet is recorded to

[013-134]have done, except the dramatist Shadwell; and in ancient days Homer is

[013-135]I think rightly reputed to have known the virtues of opium.

[013-136]To my architecture succeeded dreams of lakes and silvery expanses of

[013-137]water: these haunted me so much that I feared (though possibly it will

[013-138]appear ludicrous to a medical man) that some dropsical state or

[013-139]tendency of the brain might thus be making itself (to use a

[013-140]metaphysical word) _objective_; and the sentient organ _project_ itself

[013-141]as its own object. For two months I suffered greatly in my head, a part

[013-142]of my bodily structure which had hitherto been so clear from all touch

[013-143]or taint of weakness (physically I mean) that I used to say of it, as

[013-144]the last Lord Orford said of his stomach, that it seemed likely to

[013-145]survive the rest of my person. Till now I had never felt a headache

[013-146]even, or any the slightest [[suffering|pain]], except rheumatic pains caused by my

[013-147]own folly. However, I got over this attack, though it must have been

[013-148]verging on something very dangerous.

[013-149]The waters now changed their character—from translucent lakes shining

[013-150]like mirrors they now became seas and oceans. And now came a tremendous

[013-151]change, which, unfolding itself slowly like a scroll through many

[013-152]months, promised an abiding torment; and in fact it never left me until

[013-153]the winding up of my case. Hitherto the human face had mixed often in

[013-154]my dreams, but not despotically nor with any special power of

[013-155]tormenting. But now that which I have called the tyranny of the human

[013-156]face began to unfold itself. Perhaps some part of my [[london|London]] life might

[013-157]be answerable for this. Be that as it may, now it was that upon the

[013-158]rocking waters of the ocean the human face began to appear; the sea

[013-159]appeared paved with innumerable faces upturned to the heavens—faces

[013-160]imploring, wrathful, despairing, surged upwards by thousands, by

[013-161]myriads, by generations, by centuries: my agitation was infinite; my

[013-162]mind tossed and surged with the ocean.

[013-163]_May_, 1818

[013-164]The Malay has been a fearful enemy for months. I have been every night,

[013-165]through his means, transported into Asiatic scenes. I know not whether

[013-166]others share in my feelings on this point; but I have often thought

[013-167]that if I were compelled to forego [[england|England]], and to live in China, and

[013-168]among Chinese manners and modes of life and scenery, I should go mad.

[013-169]The causes of my horror lie deep, and some of them must be common to

[013-170]others. Southern [[asia|Asia]] in general is the seat of awful images and

[013-171]associations. As the cradle of the human race, it would alone have a

[013-172]dim and reverential feeling connected with it. But there are other

[013-173]reasons. No man can pretend that the wild, barbarous, and capricious

[013-174]superstitions of Africa, or of savage tribes elsewhere, affect him in

[013-175]the way that he is affected by the ancient, monumental, cruel, and

[013-176]elaborate religions of Indostan, &c. The mere antiquity of Asiatic

[013-177]things, of their institutions, histories, modes of faith, &c., is so

[013-178]impressive, that to me the vast age of the race and name overpowers the

[013-179]sense of youth in the individual. A young Chinese seems to me an

[013-180]antediluvian man renewed. Even Englishmen, though not bred in any

[013-181]knowledge of such institutions, cannot but shudder at the mystic

[013-182]sublimity of _castes_ that have flowed apart, and refused to mix,

[013-183]through such immemorial tracts of time; nor can any man fail to be awed

[013-184]by the names of the Ganges or the Euphrates. It contributes much to

[013-185]these feelings that southern Asia is, and has been for thousands of

[013-186]years, the part of the earth most swarming with human life, the great

[013-187]_officina gentium_. Man is a weed in those regions. The vast empires

[013-188]also in which the enormous population of Asia has always been cast,

[013-189]give a further sublimity to the feelings associated with all Oriental

[013-190]names or images. In China, over and above what it has in common with

[013-191]the rest of southern Asia, I am terrified by the modes of life, by the

[013-192]manners, and the barrier of utter abhorrence and want of sympathy

[013-193]placed between us by feelings deeper than I can analyse. I could sooner

[013-194]live with lunatics or brute animals. All this, and much more than I can

[013-195]say or have time to say, the reader must enter into before he can

[013-196]comprehend the unimaginable horror which these dreams of Oriental

[013-197]imagery and mythological tortures impressed upon me. Under the

[013-198]connecting feeling of tropical heat and vertical sunlights I brought

[013-199]together all creatures, birds, beasts, reptiles, all trees and plants,

[013-200]usages and appearances, that are found in all tropical regions, and

[013-201]assembled them together in China or Indostan. From kindred feelings, I

[013-202]soon brought Egypt and all her gods under the same law. I was stared

[013-203]at, hooted at, grinned at, chattered at, by monkeys, by parroquets, by

[013-204]cockatoos. I ran into pagodas, and was fixed for centuries at the

[013-205]summit or in secret rooms: I was the idol; I was the priest; I was

[013-206]worshipped; I was sacrificed. I fled from the wrath of Brama through

[013-207]all the forests of Asia: Vishnu hated me: Seeva laid wait for me. I

[013-208]came suddenly upon Isis and Osiris: I had done a deed, they said, which

[013-209]the ibis and the crocodile trembled at. I was buried for a thousand

[013-210]years in stone coffins, with mummies and sphynxes, in narrow chambers

[013-211]at the heart of eternal pyramids. I was kissed, with cancerous kisses,

[013-212]by crocodiles; and laid, confounded with all unutterable slimy things,

[013-213]amongst reeds and Nilotic mud.
