[015-001]I triumphed. But think not, reader, that therefore my sufferings were

[015-002]ended, nor think of me as of one sitting in a _dejected_ state. Think

[015-003]of me as one, even when four months had passed, still agitated,

[015-004]writhing, throbbing, palpitating, shattered, and much perhaps in the

[015-005]situation of him who has been racked, as I collect the torments of that

[015-006]state from the affecting account of them left by a most innocent

[015-007]sufferer {20} of the times of James I. Meantime, I derived no benefit

[015-008]from any medicine, except one prescribed to me by an Edinburgh surgeon

[015-009]of great eminence, viz., ammoniated tincture of valerian. Medical

[015-010]account, therefore, of my emancipation I have not much to give, and

[015-011]even that little, as managed by a man so ignorant of medicine as

[015-012]myself, would probably tend only to mislead. At all events, it would be

[015-013]misplaced in this situation. The moral of the narrative is addressed to

[015-014]the [[opium-eating|opium-eater]], and therefore of necessity limited in its application.

[015-015]If he is taught to fear and tremble, enough has been effected. But he

[015-016]may say that the issue of my case is at least a proof that opium, after

[015-017]a seventeen years’ use and an eight years’ abuse of its powers, may

[015-018]still be renounced, and that _he_ may chance to bring to the task

[015-019]greater energy than I did, or that with a stronger constitution than

[015-020]mine he may obtain the same results with less. This may be true. I

[015-021]would not presume to measure the efforts of other men by my own. I

[015-022]heartily wish him more energy. I wish him the same success.

[015-023]Nevertheless, I had motives external to myself which he may

[015-024]unfortunately want, and these supplied me with conscientious supports

[015-025]which mere personal interests might fail to supply to a mind

[015-026]debilitated by opium.

[015-027][[jeremy-taylor|Jeremy Taylor]] conjectures that it may be as painful to be born as to

[015-028]die. I think it probable; and during the whole period of diminishing

[015-029]the opium I had the torments of a man passing out of one mode of

[015-030]existence into another. The issue was not death, but a sort of physical

[015-031]regeneration; and I may add that ever since, at intervals, I have had a

[015-032]restoration of more than youthful spirits, though under the pressure of

[015-033]difficulties which in a less happy state of mind I should have called

[015-034]misfortunes.

[015-035]One memorial of my former condition still remains—my [[opium-dreams|dreams]] are not yet

[015-036]perfectly calm; the dread swell and agitation of the storm have not

[015-037]wholly subsided; the legions that encamped in them are drawing off, but

[015-038]not all departed; my sleep is still tumultuous, and, like the gates of

[015-039]Paradise to our first parents when looking back from afar, it is still

[015-040](in the tremendous line of Milton)

[015-041]With dreadful faces throng’d, and fiery arms.

[015-042]APPENDIX

[015-043]From the “[[london-magazine|London Magazine]]” for December 1822.

[015-044]The interest excited by the two papers bearing this title, in our

[015-045]numbers for September and October 1821, will have kept our promise of a

[015-046]Third Part fresh in the [[memory|remembrance]] of our readers. That we are still

[015-047]unable to fulfil our engagement in its original meaning will, we, are

[015-048]sure, be matter of regret to them as to ourselves, especially when they

[015-049]have perused the following affecting narrative. It was composed for the

[015-050]purpose of being appended to an edition of the Confessions in a

[015-051]separate volume, which is already before the public, and we have

[015-052]reprinted it entire, that our subscribers may be in possession of the

[015-053]whole of this extraordinary history.

[015-054]The proprietors of this little work having determined on reprinting it,

[015-055]some explanation seems called for, to account for the non-appearance of

[015-056]a third part promised in the _[[london|London]] Magazine_ of December last; and

[015-057]the more so because the proprietors, under whose guarantee that promise

[015-058]was issued, might otherwise be implicated in the blame—little or

[015-059]much—attached to its non-fulfilment. This blame, in mere justice, the

[015-060]author takes wholly upon himself. What may be the exact amount of the

[015-061]guilt which he thus appropriates is a very dark question to his own

[015-062]judgment, and not much illuminated by any of the masters in casuistry

[015-063]whom he has consulted on the occasion. On the one hand it seems

[015-064]generally agreed that a promise is binding in the inverse ratio of the

[015-065]numbers to whom it is made; for which reason it is that we see many

[015-066]persons break promises without scruple that are made to a whole nation,

[015-067]who keep their faith religiously in all private engagements, breaches

[015-068]of promise towards the stronger party being committed at a man’s own

[015-069]peril; on the other hand, the only parties interested in the promises

[015-070]of an author are his readers, and these it is a point of modesty in any

[015-071]author to believe as few as possible—or perhaps only one, in which case

[015-072]any promise imposes a sanctity of moral obligation which it is shocking

[015-073]to think of. Casuistry dismissed, however, the author throws himself on

[015-074]the indulgent consideration of all who may conceive themselves

[015-075]aggrieved by his delay, in the following account of his own condition

[015-076]from the end of last year, when the engagement was made, up nearly to

[015-077]the present time. For any purpose of self-excuse it might be sufficient

[015-078]to say that intolerable bodily suffering had totally disabled him for

[015-079]almost any exertion of mind, more especially for such as demands and

[015-080]presupposes a pleasurable and genial state of feeling; but, as a case

[015-081]that may by possibility contribute a trifle to the medical history of

[015-082]opium, in a further stage of its action than can often have been

[015-083]brought under the notice of professional men, he has judged that it

[015-084]might be acceptable to some readers to have it described more at

[015-085]length. _Fiat experimentum in corpore vili_ is a just rule where there

[015-086]is any reasonable presumption of benefit to arise on a large scale.

[015-087]What the benefit may be will admit of a doubt, but there can be none as

[015-088]to the value of the body; for a more worthless body than his own the

[015-089]author is free to confess cannot be. It is his pride to believe that it

[015-090]is the very ideal of a base, crazy, despicable human system, that

[015-091]hardly ever could have been meant to be seaworthy for two days under

[015-092]the ordinary storms and wear and tear of life; and indeed, if that were

[015-093]the creditable way of disposing of human bodies, he must own that he

[015-094]should almost be ashamed to bequeath his wretched structure to any

[015-095]respectable dog. But now to the case, which, for the sake of avoiding

[015-096]the constant recurrence of a cumbersome periphrasis, the author will

[015-097]take the liberty of giving in the first person.

[015-098]Those who have read the Confessions will have closed them with the

[015-099]impression that I had wholly renounced the use of opium. This

[015-100]impression I meant to convey, and that for two reasons: first, because

[015-101]the very act of deliberately recording such a state of suffering

[015-102]necessarily presumes in the recorder a power of surveying his own case

[015-103]as a cool spectator, and a degree of spirits for adequately describing

[015-104]it which it would be inconsistent to suppose in any person speaking

[015-105]from the station of an actual sufferer; secondly, because I, who had

[015-106]descended from so large a quantity as 8,000 drops to so small a one

[015-107](comparatively speaking) as a quantity ranging between 300 and 160

[015-108]drops, might well suppose that the victory was in effect achieved. In

[015-109]suffering my readers, therefore, to think of me as of a reformed

[015-110]opium-eater, I left no impression but what I shared myself; and, as may

[015-111]be seen, even this impression was left to be collected from the general

[015-112]tone of the conclusion, and not from any specific words, which are in

[015-113]no instance at variance with the literal truth. In no long time after

[015-114]that paper was written I became sensible that the effort which remained

[015-115]would cost me far more energy than I had anticipated, and the necessity

[015-116]for making it was more apparent every month. In particular I became

[015-117]aware of an increasing callousness or defect of sensibility in the

[015-118]stomach, and this I imagined might imply a scirrhous state of that

[015-119]organ, either formed or forming. An eminent physician, to whose

[015-120]kindness I was at that time deeply indebted, informed me that such a

[015-121]termination of my case was not impossible, though likely to be

[015-122]forestalled by a different termination in the event of my continuing

[015-123]the use of opium. [[opium|Opium]] therefore I resolved wholly to abjure as soon

[015-124]as I should find myself at liberty to bend my undivided attention and

[015-125]energy to this purpose. It was not, however, until the 24th of June

[015-126]last that any tolerable concurrence of facilities for such an attempt

[015-127]arrived. On that day I began my experiment, having previously settled

[015-128]in my own mind that I would not flinch, but would “stand up to the

[015-129]scratch” under any possible “punishment.” I must premise that about 170

[015-130]or 180 drops had been my ordinary allowance for many months;

[015-131]occasionally I had run up as high as 500, and once nearly to 700; in

[015-132]repeated preludes to my final experiment I had also gone as low as 100

[015-133]drops; but had found it impossible to stand it beyond the fourth

[015-134]day—which, by the way, I have always found more difficult to get over

[015-135]than any of the preceding three. I went off under easy sail—130 drops a

[015-136]day for three days; on the fourth I plunged at once to 80. The misery

[015-137]which I now suffered “took the conceit” out of me at once, and for

[015-138]about a month I continued off and on about this mark; then I sunk to

[015-139]60, and the next day to—none at all. This was the first day for nearly

[015-140]ten years that I had existed without opium. I persevered in my

[015-141]abstinence for ninety hours; i.e., upwards of half a week. Then I

[015-142]took—ask me not how much; say, ye severest, what would ye have done?

[015-143]Then I abstained again—then took about 25 drops then abstained; and so

[015-144]on.

[015-145]Meantime the symptoms which attended my case for the first six weeks of

[015-146]my experiment were these: enormous irritability and excitement of the

[015-147]whole system; the stomach in particular restored to a full feeling of

[015-148]vitality and sensibility, but often in great [[suffering|pain]]; unceasing

[015-149]restlessness night and day; sleep—I scarcely knew what it was; three

[015-150]hours out of the twenty-four was the utmost I had, and that so agitated

[015-151]and shallow that I heard every sound that was near me. Lower jaw

[015-152]constantly swelling, mouth ulcerated, and many other distressing

[015-153]symptoms that would be tedious to repeat; amongst which, however, I

[015-154]must mention one, because it had never failed to accompany any attempt

[015-155]to renounce opium—viz., violent sternutation. This now became

[015-156]exceedingly troublesome, sometimes lasting for two hours at once, and

[015-157]recurring at least twice or three times a day. I was not much surprised

[015-158]at this on recollecting what I had somewhere heard or read, that the

[015-159]membrane which lines the nostrils is a prolongation of that which lines

[015-160]the stomach; whence, I believe, are explained the inflammatory

[015-161]appearances about the nostrils of dram drinkers. The sudden restoration

[015-162]of its original sensibility to the stomach expressed itself, I suppose,

[015-163]in this way. It is remarkable also that during the whole period of

[015-164]years through which I had taken opium I had never once caught cold (as

[015-165]the phrase is), nor even the slightest cough. But now a violent cold

[015-166]attacked me, and a cough soon after. In an unfinished fragment of a

[015-167]letter begun about this time to ——, I find these words: “You ask me to

[015-168]write the ——. Do you know Beaumont and Fletcher’s play of “Thierry and

[015-169]Theodore”? There you will see my case as to sleep; nor is it much of an

[015-170]exaggeration in other features. I protest to you that I have a greater

[015-171]influx of thoughts in one hour at present than in a whole year under

[015-172]the reign of opium. It seems as though all the thoughts which had been

[015-173]frozen up for a decade of years by opium had now, according to the old

[015-174]fable, been thawed at once—such a multitude stream in upon me from all

[015-175]quarters. Yet such is my impatience and hideous irritability that for

[015-176]one which I detain and write down fifty escape me: in spite of my

[015-177]weariness from suffering and want of sleep, I cannot stand still or sit

[015-178]for two minutes together. ‘I nunc, et versus tecum meditare canoros.’”
