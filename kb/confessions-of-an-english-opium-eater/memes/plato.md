# Plato

**Plato** (c. 428–348 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher, a student of Socrates and teacher of Aristotle, and the founder of the Academy in Athens. His dialogues — including *The Republic*, *Phaedo*, and *Phaedrus* — are foundational texts of Western philosophy.

## Reference in the *Confessions*

De Quincey invokes Plato in [Section 16](Section-16) in connection with the doctrine of *anamnesis* (recollection) — the Platonic idea that all learning is the recovery of knowledge the soul possessed before birth. De Quincey draws an analogy between Platonic recollection and the action of opium upon memory: opium, he suggests, does not create new sensations but recovers and amplifies buried impressions — the forgotten experiences of childhood, the "deep memorable graves" of the past that rise again in opium visions.

This use of Platonic philosophy to explain the phenomenology of opium is characteristic of De Quincey's method: classical learning marshalled to illuminate radically modern states of consciousness.
