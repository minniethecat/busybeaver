# Grasmere

**Grasmere** is a village in the Lake District of Cumbria, England, famous for its association with the Romantic poets, particularly William Wordsworth and, subsequently, Thomas De Quincey. Nestled beside Grasmere lake and surrounded by fells, the village became a focal point of English Romanticism in the early nineteenth century.

## De Quincey at Dove Cottage

After William Wordsworth moved out of Dove Cottage in 1808, De Quincey became its tenant, living there from 1809. It was at Dove Cottage that the famous episode of [the Malay's visit](malay-visit) took place — De Quincey's encounter with a Malay traveller who, to De Quincey's horror, swallowed an entire lump of opium in one dose.

Dove Cottage had previously been Wordsworth's home (1799–1808), where he wrote much of his greatest poetry. De Quincey's occupancy created a literal and symbolic continuity between the two writers, though their relationship later soured.

## Literary Significance

Grasmere represented for De Quincey both the pastoral ideal (the tranquility of the Lakes) and the encroachment of his opium habit upon that ideal. His descriptions of the cottage, its library lined with books, and the surrounding landscape, form a counterpoint to the urban nightmares of his London years.
