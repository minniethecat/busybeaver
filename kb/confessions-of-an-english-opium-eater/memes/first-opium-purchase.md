# First Opium Purchase

The **first opium purchase** — on a rainy Sunday in the autumn of 1804 — is the origin event of the *Confessions*. [[Thomas De Quincey]], then a student at [[Oxford]], was visiting [[London]] when he was struck by excruciating rheumatic pains. A college acquaintance recommended [[Opium|opium]]. De Quincey made his way to a druggist's shop near the [[Pantheon]] on [[Oxford Street]], purchased a tincture of [[Laudanum|laudanum]], took it, and experienced what he would later describe as "an apocalypse of the world within me" (007-140). From this single transaction — a few pence exchanged for a pint bottle — everything that followed in the *Confessions* would unfold.

## Background

### De Quincey's Condition

In the autumn of 1804, De Quincey was eighteen years old and enrolled at Worcester College, [[Oxford]]. He had come to London "for the first time since my entrance at college" (007-094). The immediate precipitating cause of the opium purchase was physical pain:

> From an early age I had been subject to a peculiar form of rheumatic pain in the head and face, which returned in paroxysms of about twenty minutes' duration. (007-106)

On this particular occasion, the pain was especially severe — the consequence, De Quincey believed, of sleeping with wet hair. "I was driven," he writes, "into the streets, on a rainy Sunday" (007-111).

### The Recommendation

In the streets of London, De Quincey encountered a college acquaintance who "recommended opium" (007-106). De Quincey's response to the word itself is rendered in a passage of extraordinary retrospective intensity:

> Opium! dread agent of unimaginable pleasure and pain! I had heard of it as I had of manna or of ambrosia, but no further. How unmeaning a sound was it at that time: what solemn chords does it now strike upon my heart! what heart-quaking vibrations of sad and happy remembrances! (007-107)

## The Purchase

The druggist's shop was located near "the stately Pantheon" on [[Oxford Street]] (007-125). De Quincey's description of the transaction is one of the most famous passages in the book:

> The druggist, unconscious minister of celestial pleasures! — as if in sympathy with the rainy Sunday, looked dull and stupid, just as any mortal druggist might be expected to look on a Sunday. (007-125)

The druggist — anonymous, unremarkable, entirely unaware of the cosmic significance of the transaction he was facilitating — is elevated by De Quincey's retrospect into "the beatific vision of an immortal druggist, sent down to earth on a special mission to myself" (007-125).

## The Experience

De Quincey took the opium immediately upon returning to his lodgings. The effect was instantaneous and overwhelming:

> I took it — and in an hour — oh, heavens! what a revulsion! what an upheaving, from its lowest depths, of inner spirit! what an apocalypse of the world within me! (007-140)

The language is religious — "apocalypse" in its root sense of revelation, an unveiling. What was unveiled was not merely relief from pain but an entirely new register of consciousness:

> Here was the secret of happiness, about which philosophers had disputed for so many ages, at once discovered: happiness might now be bought for a penny, and carried in the waistcoat pocket; portable ecstacies might be had corked up in a pint bottle. (007-146)

## Consequences

The first opium purchase inaugurated what would become nearly two decades of opium-eating. De Quincey's initial use was occasional — "once in about three weeks" during his Oxford years (007-095) — but the trajectory was set. The pleasures of that first Sunday would lead, by inexorable gradations, to the pains of Sections 12–14: the architectural nightmares, the temporal distortions, the multiplying Malays, and the "dark interminable ocean" of guilt and anxiety.

The episode's retrospective narration — De Quincey writing in 1821, looking back across seventeen years of opium use — is saturated with the knowledge of what followed. The "immortal druggist" is a figure of comedy and pathos precisely because De Quincey knows, as the druggist did not, what that pint bottle contained.
