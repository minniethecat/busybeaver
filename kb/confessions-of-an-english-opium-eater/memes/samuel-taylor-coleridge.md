# Samuel Taylor Coleridge

## Biography

Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834) was English. The Lake Poet and fellow opium-user whose experience with the drug De Quincey references as both parallel and contrast to his own — a figure who represents the intersection of opium, creativity, and suffering in Romantic literature.

## Appearances in the Text

Coleridge occupies a unique position in the *Confessions* as the only contemporary figure De Quincey acknowledges as a comparable opium-eater. In the opening section, De Quincey grants a conspicuous "exception of *Samuel Taylor Coleridge*" (001-159) to his claim that most opium-eaters are obscure and unknown — placing Coleridge in a category of his own among literary consumers of the drug.

The most vivid Coleridge episode occurs not around opium but around architecture and dream. Many years before writing the *Confessions*, De Quincey was looking over Piranesi's *Antiquities of Rome* when "Mr. Coleridge, who was standing by, described to me a set of plates by that artist, called his *Dreams*, which record the scenery of his own visions during the delirium of a fever" (013-084). Coleridge's account — delivered "only from memory" — represented "vast Gothic halls, on the floor of which stood all sorts of engines and machinery" (013-087), images that would later merge with De Quincey's own opium visions of Piranesian staircases and endless architectural spaces.

> Mr. Coleridge, who was standing by, described to me a set of plates by that artist, called his *Dreams*, and which record the scenery of his own visions during the delirium of a fever. Some of them (I describe only from memory of Mr. Coleridge's account) represented vast Gothic halls, on the floor of which stood all sorts of engines and machinery, wheels, cables, pulleys, levers, catapults, &c. &c., expressive of enormous power put forth and resistance overcome.
>
> (013-084)(013-090)

This convergence of Coleridge, Piranesi, and the fever-dream aesthetic is one of the most important intertextual moments in the *Confessions*. Coleridge serves not merely as a fellow opium-eater but as a transmitter of the visual and architectural vocabulary — the "Gothic halls" and impossible spaces — that would become the signature imagery of De Quincey's drug-induced dreamscapes.
