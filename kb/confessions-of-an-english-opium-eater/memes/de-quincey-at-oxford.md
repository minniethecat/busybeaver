# De Quincey at Oxford

Thomas De Quincey matriculated at Worcester College, Oxford in 1803 (002-024), shortly after his period of destitution in London and his reconciliation with his guardians. His years at Oxford, spanning 1803 to 1808, were marked by extraordinary intellectual achievement and deepening opium dependence.

## The Scholar

At Oxford, De Quincey's reputation as a Greek scholar became legendary. He was said to have read more Greek than any man in England, and his ability to converse in classical Greek astonished his contemporaries. He contemplated writing a major work of philosophy — a project that would gestate for decades without ever reaching completion.

## The Opium-Eater

It was at Oxford that De Quincey first began using opium regularly, initially for the relief of rheumatic facial pain — the same neuralgic condition that would later justify his opium consumption in medical terms. The transition from medicinal to habitual use occurred gradually during these years. In the *Confessions*, De Quincey describes his Oxford opium-taking as a controlled, even elegant practice — buying opium from a London druggist on his weekly visits, taking it in measured doses before attending the opera or engaging in solitary philosophical meditation.

De Quincey later reflected on the long arc of his opium use from this early Oxford period:

> ...opium, after a seventeen years' use and an eight years' abuse of its powers, may still be renounced...
>
> (015-017)

## Departure

Despite his intellectual achievements, De Quincey left Oxford in 1808 without taking a degree. The reasons were complex: disillusionment with the examination system, increasing absorption in opium and solitary study, and perhaps a fear that he could not live up to the impossibly high expectations his tutors held for him.
