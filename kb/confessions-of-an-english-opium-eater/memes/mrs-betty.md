# Mrs. Betty

## Identity

Mrs. Betty was a Welsh landlady from whose household De Quincey rented lodgings during his youthful wandering in Wales. She had formerly served as a domestic in the household of the Bishop of Bangor and maintained connections with the episcopal palace. Her full name and life outside these episodes are not recorded; she is known solely through De Quincey's narrative of their brief and tumultuous landlord–tenant relationship.

## Role in the Confessions

Mrs. Betty appears in a single extended episode in the early part of the *Confessions*, but the consequences of that episode ripple through the entire subsequent narrative. After taking lodgings with her, De Quincey learned through Mrs. Betty herself that the Bishop of Bangor — on hearing she had let her apartments — had cautioned her about the risk of harbouring disreputable travellers:

> "You must recollect, Betty, that this place is in the high road to the Head; so that multitudes of Irish swindlers running away from their debts into England, and of English swindlers running away from their debts to the Isle of Man, are likely to take this place in their route." (003-060)

Mrs. Betty, rather than keeping the bishop's counsel private, reported it directly to De Quincey — an act he attributed not to malice but to "coarseness of mind." The disclosure wounded his pride deeply. He notes that the bishop's words were "rather fitted to be stored up for Mrs. Betty's private meditations than for communication to the party concerned." (003-065)

When the landlady attempted conciliation, a contemptuous remark De Quincey directed at the bishop himself made reconciliation impossible:

> The same coarseness of mind which had led Mrs. Betty to repeat the advice at all, might have coloured it in a way more agreeable to her own style of thinking than to the actual expressions of the worthy bishop. (003-086)

## Narrative Significance

Mrs. Betty's episode is a pivot-point in the narrative economy of the *Confessions*. De Quincey left her lodgings the same hour and was thereafter compelled to live at inns, where his money drained rapidly. The breach with Mrs. Betty thus initiates the chain of events that leads directly to his descent into London poverty — making an otherwise minor character the unwitting catalyst of the memoir's central crisis. (003-060)(003-065)(003-086)
