[014-001]I thus give the reader some slight abstraction of my Oriental [[opium-dreams|dreams]],

[014-002]which always filled me with such amazement at the monstrous scenery

[014-003]that horror seemed absorbed for a while in sheer astonishment. Sooner

[014-004]or later came a reflux of feeling that swallowed up the astonishment,

[014-005]and left me not so much in terror as in hatred and abomination of what

[014-006]I saw. Over every form, and threat, and punishment, and dim sightless

[014-007]incarceration, brooded a sense of eternity and infinity that drove me

[014-008]into an oppression as of madness. Into these dreams only it was, with

[014-009]one or two slight exceptions, that any circumstances of physical horror

[014-010]entered. All before had been moral and spiritual terrors. But here the

[014-011]main agents were ugly birds, or snakes, or crocodiles; especially the

[014-012]last. The cursed crocodile became to me the object of more horror than

[014-013]almost all the rest. I was compelled to live with him, and (as was

[014-014]always the case almost in my dreams) for centuries. I escaped

[014-015]sometimes, and found myself in Chinese houses, with cane tables, &c.

[014-016]All the feet of the tables, sofas, &c., soon became instinct with life:

[014-017]the abominable head of the crocodile, and his leering eyes, looked out

[014-018]at me, multiplied into a thousand repetitions; and I stood loathing and

[014-019]fascinated. And so often did this hideous reptile haunt my dreams that

[014-020]many times the very same dream was broken up in the very same way: I

[014-021]heard gentle voices speaking to me (I hear everything when I am

[014-022]sleeping), and instantly I awoke. It was broad noon, and my children

[014-023]were standing, hand in hand, at my bedside—come to show me their

[014-024]coloured shoes, or new frocks, or to let me see them dressed for going

[014-025]out. I protest that so awful was the transition from the damned

[014-026]crocodile, and the other unutterable monsters and abortions of my

[014-027]dreams, to the sight of innocent _human_ natures and of infancy, that

[014-028]in the mighty and sudden revulsion of mind I wept, and could not

[014-029]forbear it, as I kissed their faces.

[014-030]June 1819

[014-031]I have had occasion to remark, at various periods of my life, that the

[014-032]deaths of those whom we love, and indeed the contemplation of death

[014-033]generally, is (_cæteris paribus_) more affecting in summer than in any

[014-034]other season of the year. And the reasons are these three, I think:

[014-035]first, that the visible heavens in summer appear far higher, more

[014-036]distant, and (if such a solecism may be excused) more infinite; the

[014-037]clouds, by which chiefly the eye expounds the distance of the blue

[014-038]pavilion stretched over our heads, are in summer more voluminous,

[014-039]massed and accumulated in far grander and more towering piles.

[014-040]Secondly, the light and the appearances of the declining and the

[014-041]setting sun are much more fitted to be types and characters of the

[014-042]Infinite. And thirdly (which is the main reason), the exuberant and

[014-043]riotous prodigality of life naturally forces the mind more powerfully

[014-044]upon the antagonist thought of death, and the wintry sterility of the

[014-045]grave. For it may be observed generally, that wherever two thoughts

[014-046]stand related to each other by a law of antagonism, and exist, as it

[014-047]were, by mutual repulsion, they are apt to suggest each other. On these

[014-048]accounts it is that I find it impossible to banish the thought of death

[014-049]when I am walking alone in the endless days of summer; and any

[014-050]particular death, if not more affecting, at least haunts my mind more

[014-051]obstinately and besiegingly in that season. Perhaps this cause, and a

[014-052]slight incident which I omit, might have been the immediate occasions

[014-053]of the following dream, to which, however, a predisposition must always

[014-054]have existed in my mind; but having been once roused it never left me,

[014-055]and split into a thousand fantastic varieties, which often suddenly

[014-056]reunited, and composed again the original dream.

[014-057]I thought that it was a Sunday morning in May, that it was [[easter-sunday|Easter]]

[014-058]Sunday, and as yet very early in the morning. I was standing, as it

[014-059]seemed to me, at the door of my own cottage. Right before me lay the

[014-060]very scene which could really be commanded from that situation, but

[014-061]exalted, as was usual, and solemnised by the power of dreams. There

[014-062]were the same mountains, and the same lovely valley at their feet; but

[014-063]the mountains were raised to more than Alpine height, and there was

[014-064]interspace far larger between them of meadows and forest lawns; the

[014-065]hedges were rich with white roses; and no living creature was to be

[014-066]seen, excepting that in the green churchyard there were cattle

[014-067]tranquilly reposing upon the verdant graves, and particularly round

[014-068]about the grave of a child whom I had tenderly loved, just as I had

[014-069]really beheld them, a little before sunrise in the same summer, when

[014-070]that child died. I gazed upon the well-known scene, and I said aloud

[014-071](as I thought) to myself, “It yet wants much of sunrise, and it is

[014-072]Easter Sunday; and that is the day on which they celebrate the first

[014-073]fruits of resurrection. I will walk abroad; old griefs shall be

[014-074]forgotten to-day; for the air is cool and still, and the hills are high

[014-075]and stretch away to heaven; and the forest glades are as quiet as the

[014-076]churchyard, and with the dew I can wash the fever from my forehead, and

[014-077]then I shall be unhappy no longer.” And I turned as if to open my

[014-078]garden gate, and immediately I saw upon the left a scene far different,

[014-079]but which yet the power of dreams had reconciled into harmony with the

[014-080]other. The scene was an Oriental one, and there also it was Easter

[014-081]Sunday, and very early in the morning. And at a vast distance were

[014-082]visible, as a stain upon the horizon, the domes and cupolas of a great

[014-083]city—an image or faint abstraction, caught perhaps in childhood from

[014-084]some picture of Jerusalem. And not a bow-shot from me, upon a stone and

[014-085]shaded by Judean palms, there sat a woman, and I looked, and it

[014-086]was—[[ann|Ann]]! She fixed her eyes upon me earnestly, and I said to her at

[014-087]length: “So, then, I have found you at last.” I waited, but she

[014-088]answered me not a word. Her face was the same as when I saw it last,

[014-089]and yet again how different! Seventeen years ago, when the lamplight

[014-090]fell upon her face, as for the last time I kissed her lips (lips, Ann,

[014-091]that to me were not polluted), her eyes were streaming with tears: the

[014-092]tears were now wiped away; she seemed more beautiful than she was at

[014-093]that time, but in all other points the same, and not older. Her looks

[014-094]were tranquil, but with unusual solemnity of expression, and I now

[014-095]gazed upon her with some awe; but suddenly her countenance grew dim,

[014-096]and turning to the mountains I perceived vapours rolling between us. In

[014-097]a moment all had vanished, thick darkness came on, and in the twinkling

[014-098]of an eye I was far away from mountains, and by lamplight in [[oxford|Oxford]]

[014-099]Street, walking again with Ann—just as we walked seventeen years

[014-100]before, when we were both children.

[014-101]As a final specimen, I cite one of a different character, from 1820.

[014-102]The dream commenced with a music which now I often heard in dreams—a

[014-103]music of preparation and of awakening suspense, a music like the

[014-104]opening of the Coronation Anthem, and which, like _that_, gave the

[014-105]feeling of a vast march, of infinite cavalcades filing off, and the

[014-106]tread of innumerable armies. The morning was come of a mighty day—a day

[014-107]of crisis and of final hope for human nature, then suffering some

[014-108]mysterious eclipse, and labouring in some dread extremity. Somewhere, I

[014-109]knew not where—somehow, I knew not how—by some beings, I knew not

[014-110]whom—a battle, a strife, an agony, was conducting, was evolving like a

[014-111]great drama or piece of music, with which my sympathy was the more

[014-112]insupportable from my confusion as to its place, its cause, its nature,

[014-113]and its possible issue. I, as is usual in dreams (where of necessity we

[014-114]make ourselves central to every movement), had the power, and yet had

[014-115]not the power, to decide it. I had the power, if I could raise myself

[014-116]to will it, and yet again had not the power, for the weight of twenty

[014-117]Atlantics was upon me, or the oppression of inexpiable guilt. “Deeper

[014-118]than ever plummet sounded,” I lay inactive. Then like a chorus the

[014-119]passion deepened. Some greater interest was at stake, some mightier

[014-120]cause than ever yet the sword had pleaded, or trumpet had proclaimed.

[014-121]Then came sudden alarms, hurryings to and fro, trepidations of

[014-122]innumerable fugitives—I knew not whether from the good cause or the

[014-123]bad, darkness and lights, tempest and human faces, and at last, with

[014-124]the sense that all was lost, female forms, and the features that were

[014-125]worth all the world to me, and but a moment allowed—and clasped hands,

[014-126]and heart-breaking partings, and then—everlasting farewells! And with a

[014-127]sigh, such as the caves of Hell sighed when the incestuous mother

[014-128]uttered the abhorred name of death, the sound was

[014-129]reverberated—everlasting farewells! And again and yet again

[014-130]reverberated—everlasting farewells!

[014-131]And I awoke in struggles, and cried aloud—“I will sleep no more.”

[014-132]But I am now called upon to wind up a narrative which has already

[014-133]extended to an unreasonable length. Within more spacious limits the

[014-134]materials which I have used might have been better unfolded, and much

[014-135]which I have not used might have been added with effect. Perhaps,

[014-136]however, enough has been given. It now remains that I should say

[014-137]something of the way in which this conflict of horrors was finally

[014-138]brought to a crisis. The reader is already aware (from a passage near

[014-139]the beginning of the introduction to the first part) that the

[014-140][[opium|Opium]]-eater has, in some way or other, “unwound almost to its final

[014-141]links the accursed chain which bound him.” By what means? To have

[014-142]narrated this according to the original intention would have far

[014-143]exceeded the space which can now be allowed. It is fortunate, as such a

[014-144]cogent reason exists for abridging it, that I should, on a maturer view

[014-145]of the case, have been exceedingly unwilling to injure, by any such

[014-146]unaffecting details, the impression of the history itself, as an appeal

[014-147]to the prudence and the conscience of the yet unconfirmed

[014-148][[opium-eating|opium-eater]]—or even (though a very inferior consideration) to injure

[014-149]its effect as a composition. The interest of the judicious reader will

[014-150]not attach itself chiefly to the subject of the fascinating spells, but

[014-151]to the fascinating power. Not the Opium-eater, but the opium, is the

[014-152]true hero of the tale, and the legitimate centre on which the interest

[014-153]revolves. The object was to display the marvellous agency of opium,

[014-154]whether for pleasure or for [[suffering|pain]]: if that is done, the action of the

[014-155]piece has closed.

[014-156]However, as some people, in spite of all laws to the contrary, will

[014-157]persist in asking what became of the Opium-eater, and in what state he

[014-158]now is, I answer for him thus: The reader is aware that opium had long

[014-159]ceased to found its empire on spells of pleasure; it was solely by the

[014-160]tortures connected with the attempt to abjure it that it kept its hold.

[014-161]Yet, as other tortures, no less it may be thought, attended the

[014-162]non-abjuration of such a tyrant, a choice only of evils was left; and

[014-163]_that_ might as well have been adopted which, however terrific in

[014-164]itself, held out a prospect of final restoration to happiness. This

[014-165]appears true; but good logic gave the author no strength to act upon

[014-166]it. However, a crisis arrived for the author’s life, and a crisis for

[014-167]other objects still dearer to him—and which will always be far dearer

[014-168]to him than his life, even now that it is again a happy one. I saw that

[014-169]I must die if I continued the opium. I determined, therefore, if that

[014-170]should be required, to die in throwing it off. How much I was at that

[014-171]time taking I cannot say, for the opium which I used had been purchased

[014-172]for me by a friend, who afterwards refused to let me pay him; so that I

[014-173]could not ascertain even what quantity I had used within the year. I

[014-174]apprehend, however, that I took it very irregularly, and that I varied

[014-175]from about fifty or sixty grains to 150 a day. My first task was to

[014-176]reduce it to forty, to thirty, and as fast as I could to twelve grains.
