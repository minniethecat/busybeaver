# Opium and Wine

## Definition

The **opium-and-wine comparison** is not merely a passing analogy in the *Confessions* but a sustained rhetorical strategy spanning multiple sections. De Quincey systematically contrasts the two substances across every relevant dimension — physiological, psychological, moral, and social — to construct opium as the gentleman's drug, the philosopher's aid, and the productive citizen's comfort, as opposed to wine's association with coarseness, loss of control, and social degradation. This comparison was strategically necessary in an era when opium-eaters risked being classed with drunkards.

## In the *Confessions*

### Temporal Structure

Wine produces an acute, transient pleasure: "the pleasure given by wine is always mounting and tending to a crisis, after which it declines" (008-015). Opium's pleasure is fundamentally different in temporal structure: "when once generated, is stationary for eight or ten hours" (008-016). This distinction — "the one is a flame, the other a steady and equable glow" (008-019) — maps pleasure onto a value hierarchy where duration and stability are virtues.

### Mental Effects

The most damning contrast concerns mental faculties. "Wine robs a man of his self-possession; opium greatly invigorates it. Wine unsettles and clouds the judgement... opium, on the contrary, communicates serenity and equipoise to all the faculties" (008-023). Where wine produces "a preternatural brightness and a vivid exaltation to the contempts and the admirations, the loves and the hatreds of the drinker" (008-025), opium "gives simply that sort of vital warmth which is approved by the judgment" (008-029).

### Benevolent Affections

Even the social effects differ. "Opium, like wine, gives an expansion to the heart and the benevolent affections; but then, with this remarkable difference, that in the sudden development of kind-heartedness which accompanies inebriation there is always more or less of a maudlin character, which exposes it to the contempt of the bystander" (008-032). Opium's benevolence is genuine and dignified — "no febrile access, but a healthy restoration" (008-039).

### The Author's Own Experience with Wine

De Quincey's comparison is not purely theoretical. He describes his own relationship with wine: "I had, at all times, a craving for wine" (006-087), and admits that "this indulgence in wine contributed to strengthen my" attachment to it (006-090). His authority in comparing the two substances derives from personal experience — he is, by his own account, qualified to judge both.

## Key Quotations

> The pleasure given by wine is always mounting and tending to a crisis, after which it declines; that from opium, when once generated, is stationary for eight or ten hours: the first... is a case of acute — the second, the chronic pleasure; the one is a flame, the other a steady and equable glow. (008-016)

> Wine robs a man of his self-possession; opium greatly invigorates it. (008-023)

> Opium, on the contrary, communicates serenity and equipoise to all the faculties, active or passive. (008-027)
