# Melancholy

## Definition

In the *Confessions*, **melancholy** is not merely a passing mood but a structural condition of De Quincey's psyche — a "profoundest melancholy" that "rested upon my brain, like some black vapours that I have seen roll away from the summits of mountains" (010-069). It is both the cause and the consequence of opium-eating: De Quincey turned to opium to relieve physical pain, but the drug also addressed an underlying disposition toward gloom; yet prolonged use deepened that very melancholy into a near-catatonic depression. The term, in early 19th-century usage, carried resonances of both medical pathology and Romantic sensibility — De Quincey exploits both registers.

## In the *Confessions*

### The Initial Descent

De Quincey describes his early adult years as a period when he "was nearly falling into a deep melancholy" (009-080). The phrasing is significant: melancholy is something one *falls into*, a pit or abyss, not a state one chooses. The causes are multiple — poverty, isolation, the loss of the beloved Ann — but opium initially offered relief from this emotional landscape.

### Opium's Paradox

The paradox of opium and melancholy is central to the *Confessions*. Opium lifts the initial melancholy but produces a deeper one in its wake. De Quincey describes the "gloom and cloudy melancholy of opium" (010-065) as the permanent atmosphere of his addicted years — interrupted only by the miraculous period when he reduced his dose and felt the cloud "drew off in one day" (010-071). This temporary clearing proves the exception; the rule is the "melancholy event" (009-213) that haunts his narrative.

### Melancholy and Dreams

The opium-induced melancholy feeds directly into the dream-state. The "melancholy" De Quincey describes in Section 9 prefigures the architectural nightmares of later sections — vast, oppressive spaces that mirror the internal landscape of depression. The connection between melancholy and creativity is a Romantic commonplace, but De Quincey gives it a pharmacological twist: the drug that produces the melancholy also produces the visions.

## Key Quotations

> I was nearly falling into a deep melancholy. (009-080)

> Strange as it may sound, I had a little before this time descended suddenly, and without any considerable effort, from 320 grains of opium... to forty grains... Instantaneously, and as if by magic, the cloud of profoundest melancholy which rested upon my brain... drew off in one day. (010-067)

> A year of brilliant water... set as it were, and insulated, in the gloom and cloudy melancholy of opium. (010-065)
