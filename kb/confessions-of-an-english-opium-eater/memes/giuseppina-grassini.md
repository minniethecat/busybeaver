**Giuseppina Grassini** (1773–1850) was an Italian contralto who performed at the London Opera during the early nineteenth century. She is celebrated in the "Pleasures of Opium" section of De Quincey's *Confessions*, where her singing, amplified by the effects of opium, provided what De Quincey considered the summit of musical enjoyment. (008-154)

## Role in the Confessions

During the period from 1804 to 1812, De Quincey regularly attended the opera on Tuesday and Saturday nights, timing these excursions to follow his periodic indulgence in opium. (008-149)(008-154) Grassini was the principal attraction:

> In those days Grassini sang at the Opera, and her voice was delightful to me beyond all that I had ever heard. (008-155)

De Quincey would sit in the gallery, which cost five shillings, and immerse himself in the orchestral music and choruses. (008-159) The experience reached its peak when Grassini performed dramatic interludes:

> when Grassini appeared in some interlude, as she often did, and poured forth her passionate soul as Andromache at the tomb of Hector, &c., I question whether any Turk, of all that ever entered the Paradise of Opium-eaters, can have had half the pleasure I had. (008-165)

For De Quincey, Grassini's voice represented the highest form of intellectual pleasure attainable through music—a pleasure that opium, by "greatly increasing the activity of the mind," intensified and refined. (008-184)

## Opium, Music, and the Opera

De Quincey's attendance at Grassini's performances was part of a carefully orchestrated ritual. He fixed his opium indulgences to coincide with opera nights—Tuesdays and Saturdays—and the anticipation of hearing Grassini was itself a structuring element of his early opium years. (008-149)(009-011) The combination of opium and music produced what De Quincey described as an almost mystical synthesis: the music would unfold his entire past life before him, "not as if recalled by an act of memory, but as if present and incarnated in the music." (008-195)

This ritual belonged to the period De Quincey later called the "Pleasures of Opium," before his use escalated from intermittent indulgence to daily necessity in 1813. (009-211)
