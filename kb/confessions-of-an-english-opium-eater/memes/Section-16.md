[016-001]At this stage of my experiment I sent to a neighbouring surgeon,

[016-002]requesting that he would come over to see me. In the evening he came;

[016-003]and after briefly stating the case to him, I asked this question;

[016-004]Whether he did not think that the opium might have acted as a stimulus

[016-005]to the digestive organs, and that the present state of suffering in the

[016-006]stomach, which manifestly was the cause of the inability to sleep,

[016-007]might arise from indigestion? His answer was; No; on the contrary, he

[016-008]thought that the suffering was caused by digestion itself, which should

[016-009]naturally go on below the consciousness, but which from the unnatural

[016-010]state of the stomach, vitiated by so long a use of opium, was become

[016-011]distinctly perceptible. This opinion was plausible; and the

[016-012]unintermitting nature of the suffering disposes me to think that it was

[016-013]true, for if it had been any mere _irregular_ affection of the stomach,

[016-014]it should naturally have intermitted occasionally, and constantly

[016-015]fluctuated as to degree. The intention of nature, as manifested in the

[016-016]healthy state, obviously is to withdraw from our notice all the vital

[016-017]motions, such as the circulation of the blood, the expansion and

[016-018]contraction of the lungs, the peristaltic action of the stomach, &c.,

[016-019]and opium, it seems, is able in this, as in other instances, to

[016-020]counteract her purposes. By the advice of the surgeon I tried

[016-021]_bitters_. For a short time these greatly mitigated the feelings under

[016-022]which I laboured, but about the forty-second day of the experiment the

[016-023]symptoms already noticed began to retire, and new ones to arise of a

[016-024]different and far more tormenting class; under these, but with a few

[016-025]intervals of remission, I have since continued to suffer. But I dismiss

[016-026]them undescribed for two reasons: first, because the mind revolts from

[016-027]retracing circumstantially any sufferings from which it is removed by

[016-028]too short or by no interval. To do this with minuteness enough to make

[016-029]the review of any use would be indeed _infandum renovare dolorem_, and

[016-030]possibly without a sufficient motive; for secondly, I doubt whether

[016-031]this latter state be anyway referable to opium—positively considered,

[016-032]or even negatively; that is, whether it is to be numbered amongst the

[016-033]last evils from the direct action of opium, or even amongst the

[016-034]earliest evils consequent upon a _want_ of opium in a system long

[016-035]deranged by its use. Certainly one part of the symptoms might be

[016-036]accounted for from the time of year (August), for though the summer was

[016-037]not a hot one, yet in any case the sum of all the heat _funded_ (if one

[016-038]may say so) during the previous months, added to the existing heat of

[016-039]that month, naturally renders August in its better half the hottest

[016-040]part of the year; and it so happened that—the excessive perspiration

[016-041]which even at Christmas attends any great reduction in the daily

[016-042]quantum of opium—and which in July was so violent as to oblige me to

[016-043]use a bath five or six times a day—had about the setting-in of the

[016-044]hottest season wholly retired, on which account any bad effect of the

[016-045]heat might be the more unmitigated. Another symptom—viz., what in my

[016-046]ignorance I call internal rheumatism (sometimes affecting the

[016-047]shoulders, &c., but more often appearing to be seated in the

[016-048]stomach)—seemed again less probably attributable to the opium, or the

[016-049]want of opium, than to the dampness of the house {21} which I inhabit,

[016-050]which had about this time attained its maximum, July having been, as

[016-051]usual, a month of incessant rain in our most rainy part of [[england|England]].

[016-052]Under these reasons for doubting whether opium had any connexion with

[016-053]the latter stage of my bodily wretchedness—except, indeed, as an

[016-054]occasional cause, as having left the body weaker and more crazy, and

[016-055]thus predisposed to any mal-influence whatever—I willingly spare my

[016-056]reader all description of it; let it perish to him, and would that I

[016-057]could as easily say let it perish to my own remembrances, that any

[016-058]future hours of tranquillity may not be disturbed by too vivid an ideal

[016-059]of possible human misery!

[016-060]So much for the sequel of my experiment. As to the former stage, in

[016-061]which probably lies the experiment and its application to other cases,

[016-062]I must request my reader not to forget the reasons for which I have

[016-063]recorded it. These were two: First, a belief that I might add some

[016-064]trifle to the history of opium as a medical agent. In this I am aware

[016-065]that I have not at all fulfilled my own intentions, in consequence of

[016-066]the torpor of mind, [[suffering|pain]] of body, and extreme disgust to the subject

[016-067]which besieged me whilst writing that part of my paper; which part

[016-068]being immediately sent off to the press (distant about five degrees of

[016-069]latitude), cannot be corrected or improved. But from this account,

[016-070]rambling as it may be, it is evident that thus much of benefit may

[016-071]arise to the persons most interested in such a history of opium, viz.,

[016-072]to opium-eaters in general, that it establishes, for their consolation

[016-073]and encouragement, the fact that opium may be renounced, and without

[016-074]greater sufferings than an ordinary resolution may support, and by a

[016-075]pretty rapid course {22} of descent.

[016-076]To communicate this result of my experiment was my foremost purpose.

[016-077]Secondly, as a purpose collateral to this, I wished to explain how it

[016-078]had become impossible for me to compose a Third Part in time to

[016-079]accompany this republication; for during the time of this experiment

[016-080]the proof-sheets of this reprint were sent to me from [[london|London]], and such

[016-081]was my inability to expand or to improve them, that I could not even

[016-082]bear to read them over with attention enough to notice the press errors

[016-083]or to correct any verbal inaccuracies. These were my reasons for

[016-084]troubling my reader with any record, long or short, of experiments

[016-085]relating to so truly base a subject as my own body; and I am earnest

[016-086]with the reader that he will not forget them, or so far misapprehend me

[016-087]as to believe it possible that I would condescend to so rascally a

[016-088]subject for its own sake, or indeed for any less object than that of

[016-089]general benefit to others. Such an animal as the self-observing

[016-090]valetudinarian I know there is; I have met him myself occasionally, and

[016-091]I know that he is the worst imaginable _heautontimoroumenos_;

[016-092]aggravating and sustaining, by calling into distinct consciousness,

[016-093]every symptom that would else perhaps, under a different direction

[016-094]given to the thoughts, become evanescent. But as to myself, so profound

[016-095]is my contempt for this undignified and selfish habit, that I could as

[016-096]little condescend to it as I could to spend my time in watching a poor

[016-097]servant girl, to whom at this moment I hear some lad or other making

[016-098]love at the back of my house. Is it for a Transcendental Philosopher to

[016-099]feel any curiosity on such an occasion? Or can I, whose life is worth

[016-100]only eight and a half years’ purchase, be supposed to have leisure for

[016-101]such trivial employments? However, to put this out of question, I shall

[016-102]say one thing, which will perhaps shock some readers, but I am sure it

[016-103]ought not to do so, considering the motives on which I say it. No man,

[016-104]I suppose, employs much of his time on the phenomena of his own body

[016-105]without some regard for it; whereas the reader sees that, so far from

[016-106]looking upon mine with any complacency or regard, I hate it, and make

[016-107]it the object of my bitter ridicule and contempt; and I should not be

[016-108]displeased to know that the last indignities which the law inflicts

[016-109]upon the bodies of the worst malefactors might hereafter fall upon it.

[016-110]And, in testification of my sincerity in saying this, I shall make the

[016-111]following offer. Like other men, I have particular fancies about the

[016-112]place of my burial; having lived chiefly in a mountainous region, I

[016-113]rather cleave to the conceit, that a grave in a green churchyard

[016-114]amongst the ancient and solitary hills will be a sublimer and more

[016-115]tranquil place of repose for a philosopher than any in the hideous

[016-116]Golgothas of London. Yet if the gentlemen of Surgeons’ Hall think that

[016-117]any benefit can redound to their science from inspecting the

[016-118]appearances in the body of an [[opium-eating|opium-eater]], let them speak but a word,

[016-119]and I will take care that mine shall be legally secured to them—i.e.,

[016-120]as soon as I have done with it myself. Let them not hesitate to express

[016-121]their wishes upon any scruples of false delicacy and consideration for

[016-122]my feelings; I assure them they will do me too much honour by

[016-123]“demonstrating” on such a crazy body as mine, and it will give me

[016-124]pleasure to anticipate this posthumous revenge and insult inflicted

[016-125]upon that which has caused me so much suffering in this life. Such

[016-126]bequests are not common; reversionary benefits contingent upon the

[016-127]death of the testator are indeed dangerous to announce in many cases:

[016-128]of this we have a remarkable instance in the habits of a Roman prince,

[016-129]who used, upon any notification made to him by rich persons that they

[016-130]had left him a handsome estate in their wills, to express his entire

[016-131]satisfaction at such arrangements and his gracious acceptance of those

[016-132]loyal legacies; but then, if the testators neglected to give him

[016-133]immediate possession of the property, if they traitorously “persisted

[016-134]in living” (_si vivere perseverarent_, as Suetonius expresses it), he

[016-135]was highly provoked, and took his measures accordingly. In those times,

[016-136]and from one of the worst of the Cæsars, we might expect such conduct;

[016-137]but I am sure that from English surgeons at this day I need look for no

[016-138]expressions of impatience, or of any other feelings but such as are

[016-139]answerable to that pure love of science and all its interests which

[016-140]induces me to make such an offer.

[016-141]Sept 30, 1822
