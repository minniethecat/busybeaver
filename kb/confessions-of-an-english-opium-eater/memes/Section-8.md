[008-001]And therefore, worthy doctors, as there seems to be room for further

[008-002]discoveries, stand aside, and allow me to come forward and lecture on

[008-003]this matter.

[008-004]First, then, it is not so much affirmed as taken for granted, by all

[008-005]who ever mention opium, formally or incidentally, that it does or can

[008-006]produce intoxication. Now, reader, assure yourself, _meo perieulo_,

[008-007]that no quantity of opium ever did or could intoxicate. As to the

[008-008][[laudanum|tincture of opium]] (commonly called [[opium|laudanum]]) _that_ might certainly

[008-009]intoxicate if a man could bear to take enough of it; but why? Because

[008-010]it contains so much proof spirit, and not because it contains so much

[008-011]opium. But crude opium, I affirm peremptorily, is incapable of

[008-012]producing any state of body at all resembling that which is produced by

[008-013]alcohol, and not in _degree_ only incapable, but even in _kind_: it is

[008-014]not in the quantity of its effects merely, but in the quality, that it

[008-015]differs altogether. The pleasure given by wine is always mounting and

[008-016]tending to a crisis, after which it declines; that from opium, when

[008-017]once generated, is stationary for eight or ten hours: the first, to

[008-018]borrow a technical distinction from medicine, is a case of acute—the

[008-019]second, the chronic pleasure; the one is a flame, the other a steady

[008-020]and equable glow. But the main distinction lies in this, that whereas

[008-021]wine disorders the mental faculties, opium, on the contrary (if taken

[008-022]in a proper manner), introduces amongst them the most exquisite order,

[008-023]legislation, and harmony. Wine robs a man of his self-possession; opium

[008-024]greatly invigorates it. Wine unsettles and clouds the judgement, and

[008-025]gives a preternatural brightness and a vivid exaltation to the

[008-026]contempts and the admirations, the loves and the hatreds of the

[008-027]drinker; opium, on the contrary, communicates serenity and equipoise to

[008-028]all the faculties, active or passive, and with respect to the temper

[008-029]and moral feelings in general it gives simply that sort of vital warmth

[008-030]which is approved by the judgment, and which would probably always

[008-031]accompany a bodily constitution of primeval or antediluvian health.

[008-032]Thus, for instance, opium, like wine, gives an expansion to the heart

[008-033]and the benevolent affections; but then, with this remarkable

[008-034]difference, that in the sudden development of kind-heartedness which

[008-035]accompanies [[intoxication|inebriation]] there is always more or less of a maudlin

[008-036]character, which exposes it to the contempt of the bystander. Men shake

[008-037]hands, swear eternal friendship, and shed tears, no mortal knows why;

[008-038]and the sensual creature is clearly uppermost. But the expansion of the

[008-039]benigner feelings incident to opium is no febrile access, but a healthy

[008-040]restoration to that state which the mind would naturally recover upon

[008-041]the removal of any deep-seated irritation of [[suffering|pain]] that had disturbed

[008-042]and quarrelled with the impulses of a heart originally just and good.

[008-043]True it is that even wine, up to a certain point and with certain men,

[008-044]rather tends to exalt and to steady the intellect; I myself, who have

[008-045]never been a great wine-drinker, used to find that half-a-dozen glasses

[008-046]of wine advantageously affected the faculties—brightened and

[008-047]intensified the consciousness, and gave to the mind a feeling of being

[008-048]“ponderibus librata suis;” and certainly it is most absurdly said, in

[008-049]popular language, of any man that he is _disguised_ in liquor; for, on

[008-050]the contrary, most men are disguised by sobriety, and it is when they

[008-051]are drinking (as some old gentleman says in Athenæus), that men εαυτους

[008-052]εμφανιζουσιν οιτινες εισιν—display themselves in their true complexion

[008-053]of character, which surely is not disguising themselves. But still,

[008-054]wine constantly leads a man to the brink of absurdity and extravagance,

[008-055]and beyond a certain point it is sure to volatilise and to disperse the

[008-056]intellectual energies: whereas opium always seems to compose what had

[008-057]been agitated, and to concentrate what had been distracted. In short,

[008-058]to sum up all in one word, a man who is inebriated, or tending to

[008-059]inebriation, is, and feels that he is, in a condition which calls up

[008-060]into supremacy the merely human, too often the brutal part of his

[008-061]nature; but the [[opium-eating|opium-eater]] (I speak of him who is not suffering from

[008-062]any disease or other remote effects of opium) feels that the diviner

[008-063]part of his nature is paramount; that is, the moral affections are in a

[008-064]state of cloudless serenity, and over all is the great light of the

[008-065]majestic intellect.

[008-066]This is the doctrine of the true church on the subject of opium: of

[008-067]which church I acknowledge myself to be the only member—the alpha and

[008-068]the omega: but then it is to be recollected that I speak from the

[008-069]ground of a large and profound personal experience: whereas most of the

[008-070]unscientific {13} authors who have at all treated of opium, and even of

[008-071]those who have written expressly on the materia medica, make it

[008-072]evident, from the horror they express of it, that their experimental

[008-073]knowledge of its action is none at all. I will, however, candidly

[008-074]acknowledge that I have met with one person who bore evidence to its

[008-075]intoxicating power, such as staggered my own incredulity; for he was a

[008-076]surgeon, and had himself taken opium largely. I happened to say to him

[008-077]that his enemies (as I had heard) charged him with talking nonsense on

[008-078]politics, and that his friends apologized for him by suggesting that he

[008-079]was constantly in a state of intoxication from opium. Now the

[008-080]accusation, said I, is not _prima facie_ and of necessity an absurd

[008-081]one; but the defence _is_. To my surprise, however, he insisted that

[008-082]both his enemies and his friends were in the right. “I will maintain,”

[008-083]said he, “that I _do_ talk nonsense; and secondly, I will maintain that

[008-084]I do not talk nonsense upon principle, or with any view to profit, but

[008-085]solely and simply, said he, solely and simply—solely and simply

[008-086](repeating it three times over), because I am drunk with opium, and

[008-087]_that_ daily.” I replied that, as to the allegation of his enemies, as

[008-088]it seemed to be established upon such respectable testimony, seeing

[008-089]that the three parties concerned all agree in it, it did not become me

[008-090]to question it; but the defence set up I must demur to. He proceeded to

[008-091]discuss the matter, and to lay down his reasons; but it seemed to me so

[008-092]impolite to pursue an argument which must have presumed a man mistaken

[008-093]in a point belonging to his own profession, that I did not press him

[008-094]even when his course of argument seemed open to objection; not to

[008-095]mention that a man who talks nonsense, even though “with no view to

[008-096]profit,” is not altogether the most agreeable partner in a dispute,

[008-097]whether as opponent or respondent. I confess, however, that the

[008-098]authority of a surgeon, and one who was reputed a good one, may seem a

[008-099]weighty one to my prejudice; but still I must plead my experience,

[008-100]which was greater than his greatest by 7,000 drops a-day; and though it

[008-101]was not possible to suppose a medical man unacquainted with the

[008-102]characteristic symptoms of vinous intoxication, it yet struck me that

[008-103]he might proceed on a logical error of using the word intoxication with

[008-104]too great latitude, and extending it generically to all modes of

[008-105]nervous excitement, instead of restricting it as the expression for a

[008-106]specific sort of excitement connected with certain diagnostics. Some

[008-107]people have maintained in my hearing that they had been drunk upon

[008-108]green tea; and a medical student in [[london|London]], for whose knowledge in his

[008-109]profession I have reason to feel great respect, assured me the other

[008-110]day that a patient in recovering from an illness had got drunk on a

[008-111]beef-steak.

[008-112]Having dwelt so much on this first and leading error in respect to

[008-113]opium, I shall notice very briefly a second and a third, which are,

[008-114]that the elevation of spirits produced by opium is necessarily followed

[008-115]by a proportionate depression, and that the natural and even immediate

[008-116]consequence of opium is torpor and stagnation, animal and mental. The

[008-117]first of these errors I shall content myself with simply denying;

[008-118]assuring my reader that for ten years, during which I took opium at

[008-119]intervals, the day succeeding to that on which I allowed myself this

[008-120]luxury was always a day of unusually good spirits.

[008-121]With respect to the torpor supposed to follow, or rather (if we were to

[008-122]credit the numerous pictures of Turkish opium-eaters) to accompany the

[008-123]practice of opium-eating, I deny that also. Certainly opium is classed

[008-124]under the head of narcotics, and some such effect it may produce in the

[008-125]end; but the primary effects of opium are always, and in the highest

[008-126]degree, to excite and stimulate the system. This first stage of its

[008-127]action always lasted with me, during my noviciate, for upwards of eight

[008-128]hours; so that it must be the fault of the opium-eater himself if he

[008-129]does not so time his exhibition of the dose (to speak medically) as

[008-130]that the whole weight of its narcotic influence may descend upon his

[008-131]sleep. Turkish opium-eaters, it seems, are absurd enough to sit, like

[008-132]so many equestrian statues, on logs of wood as stupid as themselves.

[008-133]But that the reader may judge of the degree in which opium is likely to

[008-134]stupefy the faculties of an Englishman, I shall (by way of treating the

[008-135]question illustratively, rather than argumentatively) describe the way

[008-136]in which I myself often passed an opium evening in London during the

[008-137]period between 1804-1812. It will be seen that at least opium did not

[008-138]move me to seek solitude, and much less to seek inactivity, or the

[008-139]torpid state of self-involution ascribed to the Turks. I give this

[008-140]account at the risk of being pronounced a crazy enthusiast or

[008-141]visionary; but I regard _that_ little. I must desire my reader to bear

[008-142]in mind that I was a hard student, and at severe studies for all the

[008-143]rest of my time; and certainly I had a right occasionally to

[008-144]relaxations as well as other people. These, however, I allowed myself

[008-145]but seldom.

[008-146]The late Duke of —— used to say, “Next Friday, by the blessing of

[008-147]heaven, I purpose to be drunk;” and in like manner I used to fix

[008-148]beforehand how often within a given time, and when, I would commit a

[008-149]debauch of opium. This was seldom more than once in three weeks, for at

[008-150]that time I could not have ventured to call every day, as I did

[008-151]afterwards, for “_a glass of laudanum negus, warm, and without sugar_.”

[008-152]No, as I have said, I seldom drank laudanum, at that time, more than

[008-153]once in three weeks: This was usually on a Tuesday or a Saturday night;

[008-154]my reason for which was this. In those days Grassini sang at the Opera,

[008-155]and her voice was delightful to me beyond all that I had ever heard. I

[008-156]know not what may be the state of the Opera-house now, having never

[008-157]been within its walls for seven or eight years, but at that time it was

[008-158]by much the most pleasant place of public resort in London for passing

[008-159]an evening. Five shillings admitted one to the gallery, which was

[008-160]subject to far less annoyance than the pit of the theatres; the

[008-161]orchestra was distinguished by its sweet and melodious grandeur from

[008-162]all English orchestras, the composition of which, I confess, is not

[008-163]acceptable to my ear, from the predominance of the clamorous

[008-164]instruments and the absolute tyranny of the violin. The choruses were

[008-165]divine to hear, and when Grassini appeared in some interlude, as she

[008-166]often did, and poured forth her passionate soul as Andromache at the

[008-167]tomb of Hector, &c., I question whether any Turk, of all that ever

[008-168]entered the Paradise of Opium-eaters, can have had half the pleasure I

[008-169]had. But, indeed, I honour the barbarians too much by supposing them

[008-170]capable of any pleasures approaching to the intellectual ones of an

[008-171]Englishman. For music is an intellectual or a sensual pleasure

[008-172]according to the temperament of him who hears it. And, by-the-bye, with

[008-173]the exception of the fine extravaganza on that subject in “Twelfth

[008-174]Night,” I do not recollect more than one thing said adequately on the

[008-175]subject of music in all literature; it is a passage in the _Religio

[008-176]Medici_ {14} of Sir T. Brown, and though chiefly remarkable for its

[008-177]sublimity, has also a philosophic value, inasmuch as it points to the

[008-178]true theory of [[music|musical effects]]. The mistake of most people is to

[008-179]suppose that it is by the ear they communicate with music, and

[008-180]therefore that they are purely passive to its effects. But this is not

[008-181]so; it is by the reaction of the mind upon the notices of the ear (the

[008-182]_matter_ coming by the senses, the _form_ from the mind) that the

[008-183]pleasure is constructed, and therefore it is that people of equally

[008-184]good ear differ so much in this point from one another. Now, opium, by

[008-185]greatly increasing the activity of the mind, generally increases, of

[008-186]necessity, that particular mode of its activity by which we are able to

[008-187]construct out of the raw material of organic sound an elaborate

[008-188]intellectual pleasure. But, says a friend, a succession of musical

[008-189]sounds is to me like a collection of Arabic characters; I can attach no

[008-190]ideas to them. Ideas! my good sir? There is no occasion for them; all

[008-191]that class of ideas which can be available in such a case has a

[008-192]language of representative feelings. But this is a subject foreign to

[008-193]my present purposes; it is sufficient to say that a chorus, &c., of

[008-194]elaborate harmony displayed before me, as in a piece of arras work, the

[008-195]whole of my past life—not as if recalled by an act of memory, but as if

[008-196]present and incarnated in the music; no longer painful to dwell upon;

[008-197]but the detail of its incidents removed or blended in some hazy

[008-198]abstraction, and its passions exalted, spiritualized, and sublimed. All

[008-199]this was to be had for five shillings. And over and above the music of

[008-200]the stage and the orchestra, I had all around me, in the intervals of

[008-201]the performance, the music of the Italian language talked by Italian

[008-202]women—for the gallery was usually crowded with Italians—and I listened

[008-203]with a pleasure such as that with which Weld the traveller lay and

[008-204]listened, in Canada, to the sweet laughter of Indian women; for the

[008-205]less you understand of a language, the more sensible you are to the

[008-206]melody or harshness of its sounds. For such a purpose, therefore, it

[008-207]was an advantage to me that I was a poor Italian scholar, reading it

[008-208]but little, and not speaking it at all, nor understanding a tenth part

[008-209]of what I heard spoken.
