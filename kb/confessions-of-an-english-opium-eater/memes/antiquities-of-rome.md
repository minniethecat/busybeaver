# Antiquities of Rome (Piranesi)

## Overview

Piranesi's *Antiquities of Rome* (*Antichità Romane*) is a series of etched views of Roman ruins and monuments, published from 1748 onward. The work established Piranesi's reputation as the preeminent engraver of classical antiquities and profoundly influenced European perceptions of ancient Rome.

## In De Quincey's *Confessions*

The *Antiquities of Rome* enters the *Confessions* at a pivotal moment in De Quincey's description of his opium dreams. He writes:

> Many years ago, when I was looking over Piranesi's Antiquities of Rome, I noticed that Piranesi himself was standing on the staircases — follow the stairs a little further and you perceive Piranesi again, standing on a still higher platform; and so on, until the unfinished stairs and Piranesi both die away together in the upper gloom.
>
> (013-083)(013-092)(013-096)(013-098)(013-101)

The passage uses the *Antiquities* as a springboard for one of the *Confessions*' most memorable visions: the artist trapped within his own creation, endlessly climbing staircases that he himself engraved. This becomes a metaphor for the opium-eater's condition — caught in architectural spaces of the mind that expand without limit.

## The Antiquities and the Romantic Imagination

Piranesi's *Antiquities* exercised a powerful influence on Romantic writers beyond De Quincey. The engravings' combination of archaeological precision and sublime scale — vast ruins dwarfing human figures, monumental remnants of a vanished civilization — resonated with the Romantic fascination with ruins, mortality, and the passage of empires.
