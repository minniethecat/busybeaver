# Bath

## Overview

## Appearances in the Text

Bath appears twice in the *Confessions*, both times in close association with Bristol and both within the same pivotal episode — De Quincey's departure from London and his final hours with Ann. On a dark winter evening, having pawned his books and dressed himself as respectably as his means allowed, De Quincey set out "accompanied by Ann, towards Piccadilly; for it was my intention to go down as far as Salthill on the Bath or Bristol mail" (005-106). The two walked through the vanished streets of Georgian London — Swallow Street, Golden Square, Sherrard Street — sharing their last moments together before the coach carried him westward.

The journey did not go as planned. De Quincey's exhausted body betrayed his intentions: he fell into a deep sleep on the coach and travelled well beyond his intended stop. "Unfortunately, indeed, I *did* go rather farther than I intended, for so genial and so refreshing was my sleep, that the next time after leaving Hounslow that I fully awoke was upon the sudden pulling up of the mail" (005-187) — at Maidenhead, miles past Salthill and further along the road that led toward Bath and Bristol.

Bath itself — the elegant Georgian spa city, Austen's territory of polite society and medicinal waters — is never actually reached in the narrative. It exists only as a destination on the coach-board, a name that marks the westward direction of De Quincey's flight. This elusiveness is characteristic of the *Confessions*' treatment of English geography: places are often named as directions rather than destinations, as points on a compass of longing and departure rather than sites of arrival. Bath, in this sense, is the road not fully travelled — like Ann, something De Quincey set out toward but never quite reached.
