# Dejection: An Ode

## Overview

Samuel Taylor Coleridge's "Dejection: An Ode" (1802) is one of the central confessional poems of English Romanticism. Written after the first version of the *Immortality Ode* by Wordsworth, Coleridge's poem charts the loss of creative joy — "My genial spirits fail" — and the poet's sense that his imaginative power has deserted him. The poem's original version was addressed to "Sara" (Sara Hutchinson), making it an intimate verse-letter before Coleridge revised it for publication. Its themes of melancholy, creative sterility, and the search for the source of imaginative vitality place it in direct dialogue with De Quincey's own confessional project.

## In the *Confessions*

Samuel Taylor Coleridge appears in the *Confessions* early and prominently, as De Quincey establishes his own intellectual credentials. Listing the preconditions of a true philosophical intelligence, De Quincey distinguishes between the analytic and the moral-imaginative faculties. England, he argues, can boast few "subtle thinkers" in the analytic line — with one towering exception:

> England can for some generations show but few claimants; at least, he is not aware of any known candidate for this honour who can be styled emphatically *a subtle thinker*, with the exception of *Samuel Taylor Coleridge*, and in a narrower department of thought with the recent illustrious exception of *David Ricardo*. (001-159)

By naming Coleridge as the pre-eminent English thinker, De Quincey does more than flatter a contemporary. He aligns his own *Confessions* with the Lake School's project of psychological introspection and philosophical seriousness. Coleridge's "Dejection: An Ode" — the poem that most explicitly charts the collapse of imaginative power — haunts De Quincey's own narrative of opium's intellectual devastation. The *Confessions* may be read, in part, as a prose counterpart to Coleridge's ode: both are studies in the loss of creative joy, both trace that loss to its sources in bodily and spiritual derangement, and both seek to convert private suffering into public meaning.

## Thematic Resonance

The word "dejection" itself — though De Quincey uses it in its ordinary sense to describe his emotional state during periods of suffering — echoes throughout the *Confessions* with Coleridgean overtones. De Quincey speaks of "general dejection" during his wanderings (002-121), of "extreme dejection" during his London privations (005-120), and of the "low spirits and dejection" that he insists are not the necessary consequence of opium (011-170). These recurrences of the term do not necessarily reference Coleridge's poem directly, but they establish a shared vocabulary of Romantic melancholy that binds the prose confessional to its poetic predecessor. Both Coleridge and De Quincey anatomize the condition of creative paralysis — the state in which the "genial spirits" fail and the intellect, though intact, cannot bring its powers to bear upon the world.
