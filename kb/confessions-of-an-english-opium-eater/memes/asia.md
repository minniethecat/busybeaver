# Asia

## Overview

A vast geographical imagination-space in the *Confessions* — not a specific place visited but a dream-region populated by Oriental imagery that haunts De Quincey's opium visions in the later sections.

## The Malay as Catalyst

Asia first enters the *Confessions* in embodied form through the Malay visitor who arrives at De Quincey's cottage amongst the English mountains. (010-088) This incident, De Quincey notes, "brought other Malays with him, worse than himself, that ran 'a-muck' at me, and led me into a world of troubles." (010-154)(010-157) The single visitor becomes a gateway through which all of Asia enters his dream-life.

## Southern Asia as Dream-Region

Through opium, De Quincey reports being "transported into Asiatic scenes." (013-165) Southern Asia in particular becomes the locus of terror and awe in his visions:

> Southern Asia in general is the seat of awful images and associations. As the cradle of the human race, it would alone have a dim and reverential feeling connected with it.
>
> (013-170)(013-171)

The antiquity of Asiatic civilizations — "the mere antiquity of Asiatic things, of their institutions, histories, modes of faith, &c. is so impressive" — contributes to the overwhelming nature of these dream-images. (013-176)

## The Terror of the East

De Quincey describes being terrified "by the modes of life, by the manners, by the barrier of utter abhorrence placed between myself and the rest of southern Asia." (013-185)(013-191) This escalates to mythological persecution: "Vishnu hated me: Seeva laid wait for me." (013-207)

## Narrative Significance

Asia in the *Confessions* functions not as geography but as psychogeography — a landscape of the mind where Orientalist dread, the weight of ancient civilizations, and opium-induced hallucination converge. The fact that southern Asia "has been for thousands of years the part of the earth most swarming with human life" compounds the oppressive sense of multitudes pressing upon the dreaming consciousness. (013-185)(013-188)
