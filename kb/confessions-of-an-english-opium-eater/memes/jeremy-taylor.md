# Jeremy Taylor

## Biography

Jeremy Taylor (1613–1667) was an Anglican divine and author, known for his elaborately wrought prose style and devotional works including *Holy Living* and *Holy Dying*. De Quincey references Taylor in the *Confessions* as part of the literary and intellectual tradition within which he positions his own writing.

## In De Quincey's *Confessions*

De Quincey groups Jeremy Taylor with Samuel Taylor Coleridge as exceptional prose stylists, noting him as a figure of literary significance alongside "the recent illustrious exception of David Ricardo" — placing Taylor within a diverse intellectual lineage that spans theology, poetry, and political economy. (001-159)(001-160)

## Taylor's Conjecture on Birth

De Quincey invokes Taylor specifically for a striking theological and psychological observation: "Jeremy Taylor conjectures that it may be as painful to be born as to die." (015-027) This conjecture — that the trauma of birth might equal the trauma of death — appears in the later sections of the *Confessions* where De Quincey meditates on suffering, mortality, and the human condition under opium's influence.

> Jeremy Taylor conjectures that it may be as painful to be born as to die. I think it probable; and during the whole period of diminishing the opium I had the torments of a man passing out of one mode of existence into another. The issue was not death, but a sort of physical regeneration.
>
> (015-027)(015-031)

## Literary Significance

Taylor's presence in the *Confessions* reflects De Quincey's deep engagement with 17th-century English prose. Taylor's elaborate, periodic style — with its intricate clauses and rhetorical amplitude — influenced De Quincey's own prose, which shares Taylor's tendency toward expansive, oratorical sentence structures.

The confessional mode itself, though secularized in De Quincey's hands, draws on the tradition of spiritual autobiography that Taylor's devotional works helped establish. This literary genealogy positions the *Confessions* within a recognizably Christian framework of self-examination.
