# Dr. William Buchan

## Biography

William Buchan (1729–1805) was a Scottish physician best known as the author of *Domestic Medicine; or, The Family Physician* (first published in 1769). The book was an enormously popular household medical guide in the English-speaking world, running through numerous editions and translations well into the nineteenth century. It offered practical advice on the prevention and home treatment of common ailments, intended for families who could not readily access a physician. Buchan's approach emphasised hygiene, fresh air, and moderation — principles that made him a natural reference point for De Quincey's ironies about medical authority.

## Relationship to De Quincey

De Quincey invokes Dr. Buchan at two key moments in the *Confessions*, both in a spirit of comic self-justification regarding his opium consumption. In the first instance, De Quincey contrasts Buchan favourably against the unreliable medical advice found in the novel *Anastasius*:

> No; it is far better to consult Dr. Buchan, as I did; for I never forgot that worthy man's excellent suggestion, and I was "particularly careful not to take above five-and-twenty ounces of laudanum." [009-200]

The joke — as De Quincey later clarifies — is that the figure of "five-and-twenty ounces" is itself a textual corruption. In a pirated edition of *Domestic Medicine*, which De Quincey claims to have seen in the hands of a farmer's wife, the typesetter had substituted "ounces" for "drops":

> in a pirated edition of Buchan's *Domestic Medicine*, which I once saw in the hands of a farmer's wife, who was studying it for the benefit of her health, the Doctor was made to say — "Be particularly careful never to take above five-and-twenty *ounces* of laudanum at once"; the true reading being probably five-and-twenty *drops*, which are held equal to about one grain of crude opium. [017-032]

De Quincey then performs a mathematical sleight-of-hand: calculating that twenty-five ounces of laudanum equals approximately eight thousand drops, he concludes — with deadpan irony — that his own consumption was safely within limits:

> The reader sees how much I kept within Dr. Buchan's indulgent allowance. [017-076]

## Comic Function

The Buchan passages exemplify a characteristic De Quincey rhetorical strategy: the deployment of pseudo-learned medical citation to create ironic distance from the horror of his own addiction. By pretending to take seriously a manifest printing error, De Quincey simultaneously mocks the pretensions of domestic medical manuals, satirises his own rationalisations, and demonstrates the literary sophistication that distinguishes the *Confessions* from mere confessional tract. Buchan — entirely innocent of De Quincey's designs — becomes an unwitting straight man in a dark comedy about addiction and self-deception.
