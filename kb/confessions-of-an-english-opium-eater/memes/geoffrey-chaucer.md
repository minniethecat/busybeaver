# Geoffrey Chaucer

## Biography

Geoffrey Chaucer (1343–1400) was English. The medieval English poet (c.1343–1400) referenced by De Quincey as a foundational figure in English literature — representing the longue durée of the literary tradition De Quincey positions himself within.

## Relationship to De Quincey

Geoffrey Chaucer is referenced in the *Confessions*. Their connection to De Quincey's narrative and intellectual world is documented in the text.

## In the *Confessions*

De Quincey invokes Chaucer at a moment of characteristic self-deprecation in the *Confessions*. Having confessed that he is "too much of an Eudæmonist" — too attached to happiness and too averse to present pain — he looks for a lenient moral philosophy, one that accommodates human frailty rather than demanding heroic self-denial. In this spirit, he appeals to Chaucer's authority:

> I take the liberty of an Eclectic philosopher, and I look out for some courteous and considerate sect that will condescend more to the infirm condition of an opium-eater; that are "sweet men," as Chaucer says, "to give absolution," and will show some conscience in the penances they inflict, and the efforts of abstinence they exact from poor sinners like myself. (010-019)

The allusion is to the Prologue of *The Canterbury Tales*, where Chaucer describes the Friar as "a ful pleasant man," adding that "sweetly herde he confessioun, / And plesaunt was his absolucioun." De Quincey adapts this satirical portrait of an indulgent confessor to his own purposes, casting himself as a penitent who deserves a gentle penance — and the reader as a moralist whose severity he cannot, in his "nervous state," endure.

## The Chaucer Allusion

De Quincey's use of Chaucer is not merely ornamental. The reference accomplishes three things at once. First, it places the *Confessions* within the English literary tradition, aligning De Quincey's confessional prose with a lineage that reaches back to the fourteenth century. Second, the comic misapplication of Chaucer's satire — the Friar's easy penances were a mark of corruption, not compassion — creates an ironic distance between De Quincey and his own rationalisations; the reader is invited to see through the self-exculpation even as De Quincey performs it. Third, the quotation establishes a tone of learned playfulness that distinguishes the *Confessions* from the sensational drug confessions that would later proliferate in the nineteenth century. Chaucer serves, in short, as a warrant of literary respectability for what might otherwise appear a merely scandalous narrative.
