[003-001]It had been my intention originally to proceed to Westmoreland, both

[003-002]from the love I bore to that country and on other personal accounts.

[003-003]Accident, however, gave a different direction to my wanderings, and I

[003-004]bent my steps towards North [[wales|Wales]].

[003-005]After wandering about for some time in Denbighshire, Merionethshire,

[003-006]and Carnarvonshire, I took lodgings in a small neat house in B——. Here

[003-007]I might have stayed with great comfort for many weeks, for provisions

[003-008]were cheap at B——, from the scarcity of other markets for the surplus

[003-009]produce of a wide agricultural district. An accident, however, in which

[003-010]perhaps no offence was designed, drove me out to wander again. I know

[003-011]not whether my reader may have remarked, but I have often remarked,

[003-012]that the proudest class of people in [[england|England]] (or at any rate the class

[003-013]whose pride is most apparent) are the families of bishops. Noblemen and

[003-014]their children carry about with them, in their very titles, a

[003-015]sufficient notification of their rank. Nay, their very names (and this

[003-016]applies also to the children of many untitled houses) are often, to the

[003-017]English ear, adequate exponents of high birth or descent. Sackville,

[003-018]Manners, Fitzroy, Paulet, Cavendish, and scores of others, tell their

[003-019]own tale. Such persons, therefore, find everywhere a due sense of their

[003-020]claims already established, except among those who are ignorant of the

[003-021]world by virtue of their own obscurity: “Not to know _them_, argues

[003-022]one’s self unknown.” Their manners take a suitable tone and colouring,

[003-023]and for once they find it necessary to impress a sense of their

[003-024]consequence upon others, they meet with a thousand occasions for

[003-025]moderating and tempering this sense by acts of courteous condescension.

[003-026]With the families of bishops it is otherwise: with them, it is all

[003-027]uphill work to make known their pretensions; for the proportion of the

[003-028]episcopal bench taken from noble families is not at any time very

[003-029]large, and the succession to these dignities is so rapid that the

[003-030]public ear seldom has time to become familiar with them, unless where

[003-031]they are connected with some literary reputation. Hence it is that the

[003-032]children of bishops carry about with them an austere and repulsive air,

[003-033]indicative of claims not generally acknowledged, a sort of _noli me

[003-034]tangere_ manner, nervously apprehensive of too familiar approach, and

[003-035]shrinking with the sensitiveness of a gouty man from all contact with

[003-036]the οι πολλοι. Doubtless, a powerful understanding, or unusual goodness

[003-037]of nature, will preserve a man from such weakness, but in general the

[003-038]truth of my representation will be acknowledged; pride, if not of

[003-039]deeper root in such families, appears at least more upon the surface of

[003-040]their manners. This spirit of manners naturally communicates itself to

[003-041]their domestics and other dependants. Now, my landlady had been a

[003-042]lady’s maid or a nurse in the family of the Bishop of ——, and had but

[003-043]lately married away and “settled” (as such people express it) for life.

[003-044]In a little town like B——, merely to have lived in the bishop’s family

[003-045]conferred some distinction; and my good landlady had rather more than

[003-046]her share of the pride I have noticed on that score. What “my lord”

[003-047]said and what “my lord” did, how useful he was in Parliament and how

[003-048]indispensable at [[oxford|Oxford]], formed the daily burden of her talk. All this

[003-049]I bore very well, for I was too good-natured to laugh in anybody’s

[003-050]face, and I could make an ample allowance for the garrulity of an old

[003-051]servant. Of necessity, however, I must have appeared in her eyes very

[003-052]inadequately impressed with the bishop’s importance, and, perhaps to

[003-053]punish me for my indifference, or possibly by accident, she one day

[003-054]repeated to me a conversation in which I was indirectly a party

[003-055]concerned. She had been to the palace to pay her respects to the

[003-056]family, and, dinner being over, was summoned into the dining-room. In

[003-057]giving an account of her household economy she happened to mention that

[003-058]she had let her apartments. Thereupon the good bishop (it seemed) had

[003-059]taken occasion to caution her as to her selection of inmates, “for,”

[003-060]said he, “you must recollect, Betty, that this place is in the high

[003-061]road to the Head; so that multitudes of Irish swindlers running away

[003-062]from their debts into England, and of English swindlers running away

[003-063]from their debts to the Isle of Man, are likely to take this place in

[003-064]their route.” This advice certainly was not without reasonable grounds,

[003-065]but rather fitted to be stored up for Mrs. Betty’s private meditations

[003-066]than specially reported to me. What followed, however, was somewhat

[003-067]worse. “Oh, my lord,” answered my landlady (according to her own

[003-068]representation of the matter), “I really don’t think this young

[003-069]gentleman is a swindler, because ——” “You don’t _think_ me a swindler?”

[003-070]said I, interrupting her, in a tumult of indignation: “for the future I

[003-071]shall spare you the trouble of thinking about it.” And without delay I

[003-072]prepared for my departure. Some concessions the good woman seemed

[003-073]disposed to make; but a harsh and contemptuous expression, which I fear

[003-074]that I applied to the learned dignitary himself, roused her indignation

[003-075]in turn, and reconciliation then became impossible. I was indeed

[003-076]greatly irritated at the bishop’s having suggested any grounds of

[003-077]suspicion, however remotely, against a person whom he had never seen;

[003-078]and I thought of letting him know my mind in Greek, which, at the same

[003-079]time that it would furnish some presumption that I was no swindler,

[003-080]would also (I hoped) compel the bishop to reply in the same language;

[003-081]in which case I doubted not to make it appear that if I was not so rich

[003-082]as his lordship, I was a far better Grecian. Calmer thoughts, however,

[003-083]drove this boyish design out of my mind; for I considered that the

[003-084]bishop was in the right to counsel an old servant; that he could not

[003-085]have designed that his advice should be reported to me; and that the

[003-086]same coarseness of mind which had led Mrs. Betty to repeat the advice

[003-087]at all, might have coloured it in a way more agreeable to her own style

[003-088]of thinking than to the actual expressions of the worthy bishop.

[003-089]I left the lodgings the very same hour, and this turned out a very

[003-090]unfortunate occurrence for me, because, living henceforward at inns, I

[003-091]was drained of my money very rapidly. In a fortnight I was reduced to

[003-092]short allowance; that is, I could allow myself only one meal a day.

[003-093]From the keen appetite produced by constant exercise and mountain air,

[003-094]acting on a youthful stomach, I soon began to suffer greatly on this

[003-095]slender regimen, for the single meal which I could venture to order was

[003-096]coffee or tea. Even this, however, was at length withdrawn; and

[003-097]afterwards, so long as I remained in Wales, I subsisted either on

[003-098]blackberries, hips, haws, &c., or on the casual hospitalities which I

[003-099]now and then received in return for such little services as I had an

[003-100]opportunity of rendering. Sometimes I wrote letters of business for

[003-101]cottagers who happened to have relatives in [[liverpool|Liverpool]] or in [[london|London]];

[003-102]more often I wrote love-letters to their sweethearts for young women

[003-103]who had lived as servants at Shrewsbury or other towns on the English

[003-104]border. On all such occasions I gave great satisfaction to my humble

[003-105]friends, and was generally treated with hospitality; and once in

[003-106]particular, near the village of Llan-y-styndw (or some such name), in a

[003-107]sequestered part of Merionethshire, I was entertained for upwards of

[003-108]three days by a family of young people with an affectionate and

[003-109]fraternal kindness that left an impression upon my heart not yet

[003-110]impaired. The family consisted at that time of four sisters and three

[003-111]brothers, all grown up, and all remarkable for elegance and delicacy of

[003-112]manners. So much beauty, and so much native good breeding and

[003-113]refinement, I do not remember to have seen before or since in any

[003-114]cottage, except once or twice in Westmoreland and Devonshire. They

[003-115]spoke English, an accomplishment not often met with in so many members

[003-116]of one family, especially in villages remote from the high road. Here I

[003-117]wrote, on my first introduction, a letter about prize-money, for one of

[003-118]the brothers, who had served on board an English man-of-war; and, more

[003-119]privately, two love-letters for two of the sisters. They were both

[003-120]interesting-looking girls, and one of uncommon loveliness. In the midst

[003-121]of their confusion and blushes, whilst dictating, or rather giving me

[003-122]general instructions, it did not require any great penetration to

[003-123]discover that what they wished was that their letters should be as kind

[003-124]as was consistent with proper maidenly pride. I contrived so to temper

[003-125]my expressions as to reconcile the gratification of both feelings; and

[003-126]they were as much pleased with the way in which I had expressed their

[003-127]thoughts as (in their simplicity) they were astonished at my having so

[003-128]readily discovered them. The reception one meets with from the women of

[003-129]a family generally determines the tenor of one’s whole entertainment.

[003-130]In this case I had discharged my confidential duties as secretary so

[003-131]much to the general satisfaction, perhaps also amusing them with my

[003-132]conversation, that I was pressed to stay with a cordiality which I had

[003-133]little inclination to resist. I slept with the brothers, the only

[003-134]unoccupied bed standing in the apartment of the young women; but in all

[003-135]other points they treated me with a respect not usually paid to purses

[003-136]as light as mine—as if my scholarship were sufficient evidence that I

[003-137]was of “gentle blood.” Thus I lived with them for three days and great

[003-138]part of a fourth; and, from the undiminished kindness which they

[003-139]continued to show me, I believe I might have stayed with them up to

[003-140]this time, if their power had corresponded with their wishes. On the

[003-141]last morning, however, I perceived upon their countenances, as they

[003-142]sate at breakfast, the expression of some unpleasant communication

[003-143]which was at hand; and soon after, one of the brothers explained to me

[003-144]that their parents had gone, the day before my arrival, to an annual

[003-145]meeting of Methodists, held at Carnarvon, and were that day expected to

[003-146]return; “and if they should not be so civil as they ought to be,” he

[003-147]begged, on the part of all the young people, that I would not take it

[003-148]amiss. The parents returned with churlish faces, and “_Dym Sassenach_”

[003-149](_no English_) in answer to all my addresses. I saw how matters stood;

[003-150]and so, taking an affectionate leave of my kind and interesting young

[003-151]hosts, I went my way; for, though they spoke warmly to their parents in

[003-152]my behalf, and often excused the manner of the old people by saying it

[003-153]was “only their way,” yet I easily understood that my talent for

[003-154]writing love-letters would do as little to recommend me with two grave

[003-155]sexagenarian Welsh Methodists as my Greek sapphics or alcaics; and what

[003-156]had been hospitality when offered to me with the gracious courtesy of

[003-157]my young friends, would become charity when connected with the harsh

[003-158]demeanour of these old people. Certainly, Mr. Shelley is right in his

[003-159]notions about old age: unless powerfully counteracted by all sorts of

[003-160]opposite agencies, it is a miserable corrupter and blighter to the

[003-161]genial charities of the human heart.

[003-162]Soon after this I contrived, by means which I must omit for want of

[003-163]room, to transfer myself to London. And now began the latter and

[003-164]fiercer stage of my long sufferings; without using a disproportionate

[003-165]expression I might say, of my agony. For I now suffered, for upwards of

[003-166]sixteen weeks, the physical anguish of hunger in various degrees of

[003-167]intensity; but as bitter, perhaps, as ever any human being can have

[003-168]suffered who has survived it. I would not needlessly harass my reader’s

[003-169]feelings by a detail of all that I endured; for extremities such as

[003-170]these, under any circumstances of heaviest misconduct or guilt, cannot

[003-171]be contemplated, even in description, without a rueful pity that is

[003-172]painful to the natural goodness of the human heart. Let it suffice, at

[003-173]least on this occasion, to say that a few fragments of bread from the

[003-174]breakfast-table of one individual (who supposed me to be ill, but did

[003-175]not know of my being in utter want), and these at uncertain intervals,

[003-176]constituted my whole support. During the former part of my sufferings

[003-177](that is, generally in Wales, and always for the first two months in

[003-178]London) I was houseless, and very seldom slept under a roof. To this

[003-179]constant exposure to the open air I ascribe it mainly that I did not

[003-180]sink under my torments. Latterly, however, when colder and more

[003-181]inclement weather came on, and when, from the length of my sufferings,

[003-182]I had begun to sink into a more languishing condition, it was no doubt

[003-183]fortunate for me that the same person to whose breakfast-table I had

[003-184]access, allowed me to sleep in a large unoccupied house of which he was

[003-185]tenant. Unoccupied I call it, for there was no household or

[003-186]establishment in it; nor any furniture, indeed, except a table and a

[003-187]few chairs. But I found, on taking possession of my new quarters, that

[003-188]the house already contained one single inmate, a poor friendless child,

[003-189]apparently ten years old; but she seemed hunger-bitten, and sufferings

[003-190]of that sort often make children look older than they are. From this

[003-191]forlorn child I learned that she had slept and lived there alone for

[003-192]some time before I came; and great joy the poor creature expressed when

[003-193]she found that I was in future to be her companion through the hours of

[003-194]darkness. The house was large, and, from the want of furniture, the

[003-195]noise of the rats made a prodigious echoing on the spacious staircase

[003-196]and hall; and amidst the real fleshly ills of cold and, I fear, hunger,

[003-197]the forsaken child had found leisure to suffer still more (it appeared)

[003-198]from the self-created one of ghosts. I promised her protection against

[003-199]all ghosts whatsoever, but alas! I could offer her no other assistance.

[003-200]We lay upon the floor, with a bundle of cursed law papers for a pillow,

[003-201]but with no other covering than a sort of large horseman’s cloak;

[003-202]afterwards, however, we discovered in a garret an old sofa-cover, a

[003-203]small piece of rug, and some fragments of other articles, which added a

[003-204]little to our warmth. The poor child crept close to me for warmth, and

[003-205]for security against her ghostly enemies. When I was not more than

[003-206]usually ill I took her into my arms, so that in general she was

[003-207]tolerably warm, and often slept when I could not, for during the last

[003-208]two months of my sufferings I slept much in daytime, and was apt to

[003-209]fall into transient dosings at all hours. But my sleep distressed me

[003-210]more than my watching, for beside the tumultuousness of my [[opium-dreams|dreams]]

[003-211](which were only not so awful as those which I shall have to describe

[003-212]hereafter as produced by opium), my sleep was never more than what is

[003-213]called _dog-sleep_; so that I could hear myself moaning, and was often,

[003-214]as it seemed to me, awakened suddenly by my own voice; and about this

[003-215]time a hideous sensation began to haunt me as soon as I fell into a

[003-216]slumber, which has since returned upon me at different periods of my

[003-217]life—viz., a sort of twitching (I know not where, but apparently about

[003-218]the region of the stomach) which compelled me violently to throw out my

[003-219]feet for the sake of relieving it. This sensation coming on as soon as

[003-220]I began to sleep, and the effort to relieve it constantly awaking me,

[003-221]at length I slept only from exhaustion; and from increasing weakness

[003-222](as I said before) I was constantly falling asleep and constantly

[003-223]awaking. Meantime, the master of the house sometimes came in upon us

[003-224]suddenly, and very early; sometimes not till ten o’clock, sometimes not

[003-225]at all. He was in constant fear of bailiffs. Improving on the plan of

[003-226]Cromwell, every night he slept in a different quarter of London; and I

[003-227]observed that he never failed to examine through a private window the

[003-228]appearance of those who knocked at the door before he would allow it to

[003-229]be opened. He breaksfasted alone; indeed, his tea equipage would hardly

[003-230]have admitted of his hazarding an invitation to a second person, any

[003-231]more than the quantity of esculent _matériel_, which for the most part

[003-232]was little more than a roll or a few biscuits which he had bought on

[003-233]his road from the place where he had slept. Or, if he _had_ asked a

[003-234]party—as I once learnedly and facetiously observed to him—the several

[003-235]members of it must have _stood_ in the relation to each other (not

[003-236]_sate_ in any relation whatever) of succession, as the metaphysicians

[003-237]have it, and not of a coexistence; in the relation of the parts of

[003-238]time, and not of the parts of space. During his breakfast I generally

[003-239]contrived a reason for lounging in, and, with an air of as much

[003-240]indifference as I could assume, took up such fragments as he had left;

[003-241]sometimes, indeed, there were none at all. In doing this I committed no

[003-242]robbery except upon the man himself, who was thus obliged (I believe)

[003-243]now and then to send out at noon for an extra biscuit; for as to the

[003-244]poor child, _she_ was never admitted into his study (if I may give that

[003-245]name to his chief depository of parchments, law writings, &c.); that

[003-246]room was to her the Bluebeard room of the house, being regularly locked

[003-247]on his departure to dinner, about six o’clock, which usually was his

[003-248]final departure for the night. Whether this child were an illegitimate

[003-249]daughter of Mr. ——, or only a servant, I could not ascertain; she did

[003-250]not herself know; but certainly she was treated altogether as a menial

[003-251]servant. No sooner did Mr. —— make his appearance than she went below

[003-252]stairs, brushed his shoes, coat, &c.; and, except when she was summoned

[003-253]to run an errand, she never emerged from the dismal Tartarus of the

[003-254]kitchen, &c., to the upper air until my welcome knock at night called

[003-255]up her little trembling footsteps to the front door. Of her life during

[003-256]the daytime, however, I knew little but what I gathered from her own

[003-257]account at night, for as soon as the hours of business commenced I saw

[003-258]that my absence would be acceptable, and in general, therefore, I went

[003-259]off and sate in the parks or elsewhere until nightfall.
