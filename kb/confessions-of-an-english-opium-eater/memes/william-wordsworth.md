# William Wordsworth

## Biography

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) was English. The Lake Poet and neighbour whose description of London ('the stately Pantheon') De Quincey quotes — a towering literary figure whose presence shadows the *Confessions*.

## Relationship to De Quincey

William Wordsworth appears in the *Confessions* in the passages cited below. Their connection to De Quincey and role in the narrative is documented through these PN-referenced sections.

## Key Passages

De Quincey invokes Wordsworth at two pivotal moments in the *Confessions*. Early in the narrative, he borrows the poet's language to characterize his own confessional posture:

> of man, and wishing (in the affecting language of Mr. Wordsworth)
>
> "—Humbly to express
> A penitential loneliness."
>
> (001-035)

The second passage marks the pharmacological turning-point of the entire narrative — De Quincey's first purchase of opium:

> homewards lay through Oxford Street; and near "the stately Pantheon"
> (as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it) I saw a druggist's shop.
>
> (007-117)

De Quincey's parenthetical attribution — "as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it" — is at once deferential and ironic, acknowledging the poet's descriptive authority while gently mocking the grandiosity of the phrase.

## Appearances in the Text

Wordsworth enters the *Confessions* at two pivotal moments, each marked by De Quincey's appropriation of a Wordsworthian phrase. The first is early in the narrative, when De Quincey frames his project with "the affecting language of Mr. Wordsworth" (001-035), invoking the poet's sensibility to dignify his own confessional mode.

The second and more consequential appearance occurs at the narrative's pharmacological turning point: De Quincey's first purchase of opium. Walking home through London on a rainy Sunday afternoon, his route lay through Oxford Street, and "near 'the stately Pantheon' (as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it) I saw a druggist's shop" (007-117). The parenthetical attribution — "as Mr. Wordsworth has obligingly called it" — is vintage De Quincey: at once deferential and ironic, acknowledging the poet's descriptive authority while gently mocking the grandiosity of the phrase.

The druggist in that shop, an "unconscious minister of celestial pleasures," sold De Quincey the tincture of opium that inaugurated his decades-long relationship with the drug. That Wordsworth's name should be woven into this originary moment — the poet's phrase providing the geographic marker for the site of De Quincey's fall — is one of the *Confessions*' most elegant literary gestures, binding the Lakeland neighbour to the London street, the sublime poet to the squalid transaction, the natural visionary to the artificial paradise.
