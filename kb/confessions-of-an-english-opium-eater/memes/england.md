# England

## Overview

The national frame of the entire *Confessions* — the cultural, legal, and social context within which De Quincey's opium-eating takes place and against which he measures the exoticism of Oriental opium culture. The essay first appeared in the *London Magazine* for September 1821, anchoring it firmly in English literary culture. (001-005)

## English Society and Opium

De Quincey addresses the social dimension of opium-eating in England directly. He acknowledges the existence of a "class of opium-eaters" but with characteristic irony notes that the reader might be "sorry to say a very numerous one." (001-071) This rhetorical move — simultaneously confessing and deflecting — is typical of the *Confessions*' complex negotiation with English respectability.

He observes the "proudest class of people in England (or at any rate the class with the proudest pretensions)" as part of the social landscape against which his own financial decline is measured. (003-012) The theme of English swindlers "running away from their debts into England" underscores the moral ambiguity that surrounds money and class throughout the narrative. (003-062)

## Geography and Movement

England provides the physical stage for De Quincey's wanderings. He describes travelling "in haste for a remote part of England" during one episode, and the English mountains of the North serve as the setting for the Malay incident. (006-010)(010-089) The contrast between the familiar English landscape and the exotic Oriental intrusions — whether a Malay visitor or opium-induced Asiatic dreams — is a recurring structural device.

## Literary Context

De Quincey positions his work within a distinctly English literary tradition. He notes "by the courtesy of modern England I am usually designated" in a certain manner, reflecting on English readership and critical reception. (009-180) The essay itself, published in a London periodical, participates in the English essay tradition while transgressing its boundaries of subject matter.

## Key Passages

De Quincey's observations on English social hierarchy are characteristically sharp:

> I have often remarked, that the proudest class of people in England (or at any rate the class whose pride is most apparent) are the families of bishops. Noblemen and their children carry about with them, in their very titles, a sufficient notification of their rank.
>
> (003-012)
