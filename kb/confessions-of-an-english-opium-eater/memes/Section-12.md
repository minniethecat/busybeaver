[012-001]I shall now enter _in medias res_, and shall anticipate, from a time

[012-002]when my [[pains-of-opium|opium pains]] might be said to be at their _acmé_, an account of

[012-003]their palsying effects on the intellectual faculties.

[012-004]My studies have now been long interrupted. I cannot read to myself with

[012-005]any pleasure, hardly with a moment’s endurance. Yet I read aloud

[012-006]sometimes for the pleasure of others, because reading is an

[012-007]accomplishment of mine, and, in the slang use of the word

[012-008]“accomplishment” as a superficial and ornamental attainment, almost the

[012-009]only one I possess; and formerly, if I had any vanity at all connected

[012-010]with any endowment or attainment of mine, it was with this, for I had

[012-011]observed that no accomplishment was so rare. Players are the worst

[012-012]readers of all: —— reads vilely; and Mrs. ——, who is so celebrated, can

[012-013]read nothing well but dramatic compositions: Milton she cannot read

[012-014]sufferably. People in general either read poetry without any passion at

[012-015]all, or else overstep the modesty of nature, and read not like

[012-016]scholars. Of late, if I have felt moved by anything it has been by the

[012-017]grand lamentations of [[samson-agonistes|Samson Agonistes]], or the great harmonies of the

[012-018]Satanic speeches in [[paradise-regained|Paradise Regained]], when read aloud by myself. A

[012-019]young lady sometimes comes and drinks tea with us: at her request and

[012-020]M.’s, I now and then read W-’s poems to them. (W., by-the-bye is the

[012-021]only poet I ever met who could read his own verses: often indeed he

[012-022]reads admirably.)

[012-023]For nearly two years I believe that I read no book, but one; and I owe

[012-024]it to the author, in discharge of a great debt of gratitude, to mention

[012-025]what that was. The sublimer and more passionate poets I still read, as

[012-026]I have said, by snatches, and occasionally. But my proper vocation, as

[012-027]I well know, was the exercise of the analytic understanding. Now, for

[012-028]the most part analytic studies are continuous, and not to be pursued by

[012-029]fits and starts, or fragmentary efforts. Mathematics, for instance,

[012-030]intellectual philosophy, &c, were all become insupportable to me; I

[012-031]shrunk from them with a sense of powerless and infantine feebleness

[012-032]that gave me an anguish the greater from remembering the time when I

[012-033]grappled with them to my own hourly delight; and for this further

[012-034]reason, because I had devoted the labour of my whole life, and had

[012-035]dedicated my intellect, blossoms and fruits, to the slow and elaborate

[012-036]toil of constructing one single work, to which I had presumed to give

[012-037]the title of an unfinished work of Spinosa’s—viz., _De Emendatione

[012-038]Humani Intellectus_. This was now lying locked up, as by frost, like

[012-039]any Spanish bridge or aqueduct, begun upon too great a scale for the

[012-040]resources of the architect; and instead of reviving me as a monument of

[012-041]wishes at least, and aspirations, and a life of labour dedicated to the

[012-042]exaltation of human nature in that way in which God had best fitted me

[012-043]to promote so great an object, it was likely to stand a memorial to my

[012-044]children of hopes defeated, of baffled efforts, of materials uselessly

[012-045]accumulated, of foundations laid that were never to support a

[012-046]super-structure—of the grief and the ruin of the architect. In this

[012-047]state of imbecility I had, for amusement, turned my attention to

[012-048]political economy; my understanding, which formerly had been as active

[012-049]and restless as a hyæna, could not, I suppose (so long as I lived at

[012-050]all) sink into utter lethargy; and political economy offers this

[012-051]advantage to a person in my state, that though it is eminently an

[012-052]organic science (no part, that is to say, but what acts on the whole as

[012-053]the whole again reacts on each part), yet the several parts may be

[012-054]detached and contemplated singly. Great as was the prostration of my

[012-055]powers at this time, yet I could not forget my knowledge; and my

[012-056]understanding had been for too many years intimate with severe

[012-057]thinkers, with logic, and the great masters of knowledge, not to be

[012-058]aware of the utter feebleness of the main herd of modern economists. I

[012-059]had been led in 1811 to look into loads of books and pamphlets on many

[012-060]branches of economy; and, at my desire, M. sometimes read to me

[012-061]chapters from more recent works, or parts of parliamentary debates. I

[012-062]saw that these were generally the very dregs and rinsings of the human

[012-063]intellect; and that any man of sound head, and practised in wielding

[012-064]logic with a scholastic adroitness, might take up the whole academy of

[012-065]modern economists, and throttle them between heaven and earth with his

[012-066]finger and thumb, or bray their fungus-heads to powder with a lady’s

[012-067]fan. At length, in 1819, a friend in Edinburgh sent me down Mr.

[012-068]Ricardo’s book; and recurring to my own prophetic anticipation of the

[012-069]advent of some legislator for this science, I said, before I had

[012-070]finished the first chapter, “Thou art the man!” Wonder and curiosity

[012-071]were emotions that had long been dead in me. Yet I wondered once more:

[012-072]I wondered at myself that I could once again be stimulated to the

[012-073]effort of reading, and much more I wondered at the book. Had this

[012-074]profound work been really written in [[england|England]] during the nineteenth

[012-075]century? Was it possible? I supposed thinking {19} had been extinct in

[012-076]England. Could it be that an Englishman, and he not in academic bowers,

[012-077]but oppressed by mercantile and senatorial cares, had accomplished what

[012-078]all the universities of Europe and a century of thought had failed even

[012-079]to advance by one hair’s breadth? All other writers had been crushed

[012-080]and overlaid by the enormous weight of facts and documents. Mr. Ricardo

[012-081]had deduced _à priori_ from the understanding itself laws which first

[012-082]gave a ray of light into the unwieldy chaos of materials, and had

[012-083]constructed what had been but a collection of tentative discussions

[012-084]into a science of regular proportions, now first standing on an eternal

[012-085]basis.

[012-086]Thus did one single work of a profound understanding avail to give me a

[012-087]pleasure and an activity which I had not known for years. It roused me

[012-088]even to write, or at least to dictate what M. wrote for me. It seemed

[012-089]to me that some important truths had escaped even “the inevitable eye”

[012-090]of Mr. Ricardo; and as these were for the most part of such a nature

[012-091]that I could express or illustrate them more briefly and elegantly by

[012-092]algebraic symbols than in the usual clumsy and loitering diction of

[012-093]economists, the whole would not have filled a pocket-book; and being so

[012-094]brief, with M. for my amanuensis, even at this time, incapable as I was

[012-095]of all general exertion, I drew up my _Prolegomena to all future

[012-096]Systems of Political Economy_. I hope it will not be found redolent of

[012-097]opium; though, indeed, to most people the subject is a sufficient

[012-098]opiate.

[012-099]This exertion, however, was but a temporary flash, as the sequel

[012-100]showed; for I designed to publish my work. Arrangements were made at a

[012-101]provincial press, about eighteen miles distant, for printing it. An

[012-102]additional compositor was retained for some days on this account. The

[012-103]work was even twice advertised, and I was in a manner pledged to the

[012-104]fulfilment of my intention. But I had a preface to write, and a

[012-105]dedication, which I wished to make a splendid one, to Mr. Ricardo. I

[012-106]found myself quite unable to accomplish all this. The arrangements were

[012-107]countermanded, the compositor dismissed, and my “Prolegomena” rested

[012-108]peacefully by the side of its elder and more dignified brother.

[012-109]I have thus described and illustrated my intellectual torpor in terms

[012-110]that apply more or less to every part of the four years during which I

[012-111]was under the Circean spells of opium. But for misery and suffering, I

[012-112]might indeed be said to have existed in a dormant state. I seldom could

[012-113]prevail on myself to write a letter; an answer of a few words to any

[012-114]that I received was the utmost that I could accomplish, and often

[012-115]_that_ not until the letter had lain weeks or even months on my

[012-116]writing-table. Without the aid of M. all records of bills paid or _to

[012-117]be_ paid must have perished, and my whole domestic economy, whatever

[012-118]became of Political Economy, must have gone into irretrievable

[012-119]confusion. I shall not afterwards allude to this part of the case. It

[012-120]is one, however, which the [[opium-eating|opium-eater]] will find, in the end, as

[012-121]oppressive and tormenting as any other, from the sense of incapacity

[012-122]and feebleness, from the direct embarrassments incident to the neglect

[012-123]or procrastination of each day’s appropriate duties, and from the

[012-124]remorse which must often exasperate the stings of these evils to a

[012-125]reflective and conscientious mind. The opium-eater loses none of his

[012-126]moral sensibilities or aspirations. He wishes and longs as earnestly as

[012-127]ever to realize what he believes possible, and feels to be exacted by

[012-128]duty; but his intellectual apprehension of what is possible infinitely

[012-129]outruns his power, not of execution only, but even of power to attempt.

[012-130]He lies under the weight of incubus and nightmare; he lies in sight of

[012-131]all that he would fain perform, just as a man forcibly confined to his

[012-132]bed by the mortal languor of a relaxing disease, who is compelled to

[012-133]witness injury or outrage offered to some object of his tenderest love:

[012-134]he curses the spells which chain him down from motion; he would lay

[012-135]down his life if he might but get up and walk; but he is powerless as

[012-136]an infant, and cannot even attempt to rise.

[012-137]I now pass to what is the main subject of these latter [[confession-genre|confessions]], to

[012-138]the history and journal of what took place in my [[opium-dreams|dreams]], for these were

[012-139]the immediate and proximate cause of my acutest suffering.

[012-140]The first notice I had of any important change going on in this part of

[012-141]my physical economy was from the reawakening of a state of eye

[012-142]generally incident to childhood, or exalted states of irritability. I

[012-143]know not whether my reader is aware that many children, perhaps most,

[012-144]have a power of painting, as it were upon the darkness, all sorts of

[012-145]phantoms. In some that power is simply a mechanical affection of the

[012-146]eye; others have a voluntary or semi-voluntary power to dismiss or to

[012-147]summon them; or, as a child once said to me when I questioned him on

[012-148]this matter, “I can tell them to go, and they go ——, but sometimes they

[012-149]come when I don’t tell them to come.” Whereupon I told him that he had

[012-150]almost as unlimited a command over apparitions as a Roman centurion

[012-151]over his soldiers.—In the middle of 1817, I think it was, that this

[012-152]faculty became positively distressing to me: at night, when I lay awake

[012-153]in bed, vast processions passed along in mournful pomp; friezes of

[012-154]never-ending stories, that to my feelings were as sad and solemn as if

[012-155]they were stories drawn from times before Œdipus or Priam, before Tyre,

[012-156]before Memphis. And at the same time a corresponding change took place

[012-157]in my dreams; a theatre seemed suddenly opened and lighted up within my

[012-158]brain, which presented nightly spectacles of more than earthly

[012-159]splendour. And the four following facts may be mentioned as noticeable

[012-160]at this time:

[012-161]1. That as the creative state of the eye increased, a sympathy seemed

[012-162]to arise between the waking and the dreaming states of the brain in one

[012-163]point—that whatsoever I happened to call up and to trace by a voluntary

[012-164]act upon the darkness was very apt to transfer itself to my dreams, so

[012-165]that I feared to exercise this faculty; for, as Midas turned all things

[012-166]to gold that yet baffled his hopes and defrauded his human desires, so

[012-167]whatsoever things capable of being visually represented I did but think

[012-168]of in the darkness, immediately shaped themselves into phantoms of the

[012-169]eye; and by a process apparently no less inevitable, when thus once

[012-170]traced in faint and visionary colours, like writings in sympathetic

[012-171]ink, they were drawn out by the fierce chemistry of my dreams into

[012-172]insufferable splendour that fretted my heart.
