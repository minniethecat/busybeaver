# Euripides

**Euripides** (c. 480–406 BCE) was one of the three great tragedians of classical Athens, alongside Aeschylus and [[sophocles]]. His plays — including *Medea*, *The Bacchae*, *Hippolytus*, and *The Trojan Women* — are noted for their psychological depth, their unflinching portrayal of human suffering, and their sympathetic treatment of marginalized figures.

## References in the *Confessions*

[[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] twice cites Euripides in the *[[confessions-of-an-english-opium-eater|Confessions]]*, drawing on the tragedian's authority in representing extreme states of human agony. In [Section 2](Section-2), he invokes a passage from Euripides to illustrate his argument about the philosophical dignity of suffering. The reference returns in [Section 17](Section-17) in the context of De Quincey's discussion of bodily pain and the limits of endurance.

Euripides serves De Quincey as a classical precedent for the literary examination of suffering — a tradition in which De Quincey positions his own confessional narrative.

When De Quincey fled Manchester Grammar School, Euripides was his chosen companion:

> ...a favourite English poet in one pocket, and a small 12mo volume, containing about nine plays of Euripides, in the other.
>
> (002-182)
