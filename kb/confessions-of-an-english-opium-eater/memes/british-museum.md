# British Museum

The **British Museum** — founded in 1753 and opened to the public in [[London]] in 1759 — appears in the *Confessions* as a cultural landmark housing the artistic works that shaped [[Thomas De Quincey]]'s imaginative vocabulary. Its most significant role in the text is as the repository of [[piranesi|Piranesi]]'s engravings, particularly the *[[Carceri d'Invenzione]]*, which provided the visual lexicon for De Quincey's architectural [[Opium Dreams|opium nightmares]].

## Role in De Quincey's World

The British Museum, as a public institution of the early 19th century, represented the Enlightenment ideal of accessible knowledge — a collection of the world's art and antiquities available to any citizen. De Quincey likely visited the Museum's print room during his [[London]] years, where he would have encountered Piranesi's vast prison etchings:

> Many years ago, when I was looking over Piranesi's Antiquities of Rome, in the British Museum, Mr. Coleridge... described to me a set of plates by that artist... which record the scenery of his own visions during the delirium of a fever. (001-143)

This passage — recording [[Samuel Taylor Coleridge]]'s description of the *Carceri* — is the seed from which De Quincey's own architectural dream imagery would grow.

## Key Figures

- **[[piranesi|Giovanni Battista Piranesi]]** — the Venetian artist whose engravings, housed at the Museum, inspired the visual vocabulary of De Quincey's opium dreams
- **[[Samuel Taylor Coleridge]]** — who described Piranesi's plates to De Quincey at the Museum
- **[[Thomas De Quincey]]** — who visited the Museum and absorbed Piranesi's imagery

## In the *Confessions*

The Museum's role is brief but consequential. It is the site of transmission — Coleridge to De Quincey — of the Piranesian visual vocabulary that would later structure the most famous passages of the *Confessions*:

> Creeping along the sides of the walls, you perceived a staircase; and upon it, groping his way upwards, was Piranesi himself: follow the stairs a little further, and you perceive it come to a sudden abrupt termination, without any other balustrade. (001-143)

This image — the endless staircase terminating in the abyss — is one of the controlling metaphors of the opium dreams.

## Historical Context

When De Quincey visited, the British Museum was housed in Montagu House in Bloomsbury. The present-day iconic building (designed by Robert Smirke) would not be completed until the 1850s. The Museum's collection of prints and drawings, however, was already among the finest in Europe, making it a natural destination for anyone interested in Italian engraving.
