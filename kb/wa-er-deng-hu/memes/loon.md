# Loon

## Definition

The *loon* (*Colymbus glacialis*) occupies a singular place in *Walden*: it is the one wild creature Thoreau describes in extended, dramatic, almost novelistic detail, and the one that most fully resists his powers of observation and comprehension. The loon is not a companion, like the mice or the robin; it is an adversary in a game of hide-and-seek played across the surface of the pond, a being whose "demoniac laughter" and preternatural diving ability make it the emblem of nature's untamable intelligence.

## In Walden

The great loon encounter occupies a long passage in "Brute Neighbors" and is unlike anything else in *Walden*. Thoreau launches his boat onto the pond in pursuit of a loon, and what follows is a comic-epic chase: "In the fall the loon (*Colymbus glacialis*) came, as usual, to moult and bathe in the pond, making the woods ring with his wild laughter before I had risen" (012-016). Thoreau rows after it, and the loon dives — surfacing unpredictably, watching the boat with what Thoreau imagines as mocking intelligence.

The game is one-sided. Thoreau, for all his observational skill, cannot predict where the loon will surface: "when I was on the alert to hear him, he would suddenly throw himself, and with a single long-drawn peal of unearthly laughter, plunge down and come up a great way off" (012-016). The loon's laughter — "demoniac" and "unearthly" — is the sound of a creature that cannot be caught, studied, or domesticated. Thoreau admires the loon precisely because it resists him.

Beyond the chase scene, the loon is a recurring presence in Walden's soundscape. Thoreau associates its cry with the approach of winter: "perhaps I shall hear a solitary loon laugh as he dives and plumes himself in the pond" (016-025), he muses, imagining the pond in the grip of ice. In spring, the loon's return signals the pond's renewal: "on the third or fourth of May I saw a loon in the pond" (017-045), a date as precise and significant as any in Thoreau's seasonal calendar.

> [012-016] In the fall the loon (*Colymbus glacialis*) came, as usual, to moult and bathe in the pond, making the woods ring with his wild laughter before I had risen. At rumor of his arrival all the Mill-dam sportsmen are on the alert… But now, on a calm October morning, I was rowing in the pond with my eyes shut, when suddenly a loon started up, and gave one glance back toward me, and laughed, and so I knew him.

> [012-017] …when I had spent the most pains and come the nearest to him, he dashed down and I lost him. It was surprising, how quickly he made up his mind and put his resolve into execution. He said to himself, it is time to be off, and he was off, I knew not whither. It was as if he had made up a whole new system of tactics for this single occasion.

> [017-045] On the third or fourth of May I saw a loon in the pond, and during the first week of the month I heard the whippoorwill, the brown-thrasher, the veery, the wood-pewee, the chewink, and other birds.

## Thoreau's Perspective

The loon embodies for Thoreau a quality of wildness that the more domesticable animals — the mice, the robin, even the fox — do not. The loon cannot be approached, only pursued; it cannot be understood, only admired. Thoreau's chase across the pond is not a hunt (he carries no weapon) but a form of philosophical inquiry: he wants to know the loon, and the loon's genius is to remain unknown. The chase teaches Thoreau something about the limits of knowledge — that some beings exist to remind us that the world exceeds our grasp.

## Historical Context

The common loon was well known to nineteenth-century New Englanders, who encountered it on northern lakes and ponds during migration. Thoreau's contemporaries would have recognized the loon's cry as a familiar sound of wilderness. But Thoreau differs from the natural historians of his time — and anticipates later ecological writing — by treating the loon not as a specimen to be classified but as a character in a drama, an intelligent agent with its own purposes. His description prefigures the modern understanding of animal consciousness while remaining rooted in direct, embodied encounter.
