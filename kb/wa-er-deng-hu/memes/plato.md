# Plato

Ancient Greek philosopher (428–348 BCE), founder of the Academy, referenced in Walden as a model of philosophical depth and the examined life.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau references Plato in the Reading and Visitors chapters, contrasting genuine philosophical engagement with the superficial intellectual culture of Concord. Plato stands, alongside Homer, as an exemplar of the classical education Thoreau values and fears his contemporaries are abandoning.

> [003-010] Or shall I hear the name of Plato and never read his book? As if Plato were my townsman and I never saw him—my next neighbor and I never heard him speak or attended to the wisdom of his words.

> [006-022] At another time, hearing Plato's definition of a man,—a biped without feathers,—having pulled a cock in the yard and brought it into the lecture room, he said, "Here is Plato's man."

## Biography

Plato (428–348 BCE) was a student of Socrates and teacher of Aristotle, and the founder of the Academy in Athens—the first institution of higher learning in the Western world. His dialogues, including the *Republic*, *Phaedo*, and *Symposium*, are foundational texts of Western philosophy. Thoreau would have read Plato in Greek during his Harvard education.

## Significance

For Thoreau, Plato represents the value of serious philosophical reading. The rhetorical question "shall I hear the name of Plato and never read his book?" encapsulates Thoreau's broader argument in the Reading chapter: that one ought to engage directly with the great works rather than merely knowing their authors' names. Plato's definition of man, humorously subverted by Diogenes in Thoreau's second reference, also ties into Walden's recurring theme of questioning conventional definitions.
