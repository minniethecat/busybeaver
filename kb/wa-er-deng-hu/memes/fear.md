# Fear

## Definition

Fear, in Walden, is the primary obstacle to freedom. It is not the acute fear of physical danger — Thoreau faces that calmly enough — but the chronic, diffuse fear that prevents people from departing from convention: fear of poverty, fear of solitude, fear of what the neighbors will think. Thoreau's entire experiment is an answer to fear: a demonstration that the things people are most afraid of are, upon examination, harmless.

## In Walden

Thoreau's relationship with fear is most directly explored in his accounts of solitude. He acknowledges the instinctive unease of being alone in the woods at night:

> [005-004] I was at the same time conscious of a slight insanity in my mood, and seemed to foresee my recovery.

This "slight insanity" — a brush with irrational fear — passes when examined. Fear dissipates under attention, which is Thoreau's consistent strategy: look directly at what frightens you, and it shrinks.

The social dimension of fear is even more pervasive. People remain in jobs they hate, in towns they despise, performing roles they never chose — all from fear of the alternative:

> [001-005] By a seeming fate, commonly called necessity, they are employed, as it says in an old book, laying up treasures which moth and rust will corrupt.

This "seeming fate" is actually fear in disguise — fear of poverty, of social disapproval, of the unknown. Thoreau's "Economy" chapter is, at bottom, an argument that these fears are unfounded: one can live simply and freely on very little.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau treats fear as a form of delusion — a phantasm generated by the "diseased imagination" (005-017) that feeds on itself when not challenged by direct experience. The cure for fear is exposure: go into the woods at night, sit alone in the dark, discover that nothing terrible happens. This is therapeutic in the most literal sense — a practice of desensitization through voluntary exposure.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's analysis of fear draws on Stoic philosophy (Epictetus: "It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things") and Emerson's call for self-reliance. But it is also a response to the specific anxieties of antebellum America: the fear of financial ruin in a volatile market economy, the fear of social ostracism in tightly-knit communities, and the fear of the wilderness that still haunted the New England imagination.
