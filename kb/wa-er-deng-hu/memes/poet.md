# Poet

## Definition

In *Walden*, the poet is an archetype of authentic existence. Thoreau distinguishes the true poet from the mere writer of verse: the poet is one who perceives the world directly, without the mediation of social convention, and whose life itself is the primary work of art. "I have frequently seen a poet withdraw, having enjoyed the most valuable part of a farm" (002-005) — the poet's wealth lies in perception, not possession.

## In Walden

Thoreau returns to the figure of the poet throughout the book as a marker of the highest human possibility. In the celebrated "Hermit and Poet" dialogue of Chapter 12 ("Brute Neighbors"), the Poet appears as a companion and interlocutor. "See those clouds; how they hang!" (012-003) — the Poet's speech is exclamatory, attuned to beauty, indifferent to practical industry. The Hermit, by contrast, is preoccupied with inner contemplation. Their dialogue stages a productive tension between two modes of the examined life.

> [012-003] Poet. See those clouds; how they hang! That's the greatest northern lights I ever saw.

The Poet in this dialogue represents the outward-facing, celebratory aspect of the Walden project — the capacity to be delighted by the visible world. To the Hermit's question "How do you like the world today?" the Poet answers with pure attention to the sky, unburdened by the Hermit's self-conscious introspection.

> [012-002] Hermit. I wonder what the world is doing now. ... Eh, Mr. Poet, is it you? How do you like the world today?

For Thoreau, poetry is not confined to verse. "When the reign of poetry commences here, its leaves and blossoms will be the noblest works of art" (013-001) — the poet's true medium is life itself, and the poet's role is to herald a transformed relationship between humanity and nature. Thoreau also quotes the Persian poet Mîr Camar Uddîn Mast (003-002), signaling that the poet archetype transcends Western literary tradition.

> [013-001] When the reign of poetry commences here, its leaves and blossoms will be the noblest works of art.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau positions the poet alongside the philosopher and the hero as one of humanity's exemplary types, yet he is alive to the tension between poetic sensibility and practical life. "I took a poet to board for a fortnight about that time, and he caused me considerable trouble" (013-005) — a wry acknowledgment that the poetic temperament, for all its nobility, does not always accommodate itself gracefully to the requirements of a house in the woods. The poet is an ideal, not always a convenient housemate. Still, Thoreau insists that "why should not a poet's cat be winged as well as his horse?" (012-015) — the poet's transformative vision should, in principle, extend to every creature and object in his orbit.
