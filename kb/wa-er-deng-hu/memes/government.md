# Government

## Definition

In *Walden*, government appears primarily as an object of Thoreau's critique — an external authority whose claims on the individual are at best a distraction from the more urgent work of self-governance. Thoreau does not propose political programs but rather questions whether any government that demands the surrender of individual conscience can be called just.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most direct engagement with government in Walden comes in the final chapter, where he frames political authority against the sovereignty of individual perception. He writes of the "government ship, with five hundred men and boys, one of which is captain" (018-007), an image that reduces the grandeur of the state to a single vessel — impressive but fundamentally limited compared to the "shoreless ocean" of truth that the individual can navigate alone.

> [018-013] Is there not a sort of bloodshed when the conscience is wounded?

This question, posed in the context of opposition to "a just government," captures Thoreau's central political claim: the state's violence is not only physical but moral, and the individual who cooperates with an unjust government wounds his own conscience. His solution is withdrawal — not into apathy but into a higher allegiance.

Thoreau's model of governance is natural rather than institutional: "The government of the world I live in was not framed, like that of Great Britain, in after-dinner conversations over the wine" (018-027). The natural order, for Thoreau, is already well-governed; human governments are late, artificial, and usually inferior additions.

Earlier in the book, Thoreau references the governor of Plymouth Colony — "When Winslow, afterward governor of the Plymouth Colony, went with a companion..." (006-010) — as a figure from early American history. But the reference is anthropological rather than political; Winslow appears as a traveler, not an administrator.

The critique of institutional authority extends to those "who govern public affairs" (008-008). Thoreau asks what need such governors have for the labor of ordinary men, suggesting that the machinery of state is maintained at the expense of individual freedom. The "governor and his council" faintly remind the citizen of their existence (011-007), but for Thoreau this reminder is an intrusion, not a reassurance.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's perspective on government in Walden is consistent with his better-known essay "Civil Disobedience," though it is expressed more obliquely here. Government is a human construction that, at best, does no harm; at worst, it demands that the individual violate conscience for the sake of collective expediency. Thoreau's response is not revolution but withdrawal — the building of a life so self-sufficient that the state becomes irrelevant. His cabin at Walden is itself a political argument: a demonstration that one can live well without the apparatus of civic authority. The vote, the tax, the law — all are weaker than a life so simplified that it needs nothing from the state.

## Historical Context

Thoreau wrote Walden in the 1850s, a period of intensifying political crisis over slavery. His own act of tax resistance — for which he spent a night in the Concord jail in 1846 — occurred during his Walden sojourn. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 and the looming Civil War gave urgency to questions of individual conscience versus state authority. Thoreau's critique of government in Walden, though less polemical than "Civil Disobedience," draws on the same conviction: that the individual who obeys an unjust law surrenders his humanity.
