# Vision

## Definition

Vision in *Walden* is the faculty Thoreau both relies upon and distrusts. Physical sight registers surfaces; spiritual vision, which Thoreau calls for throughout the book, penetrates to essences. "Our vision does not penetrate the surface of things" (002-028), he writes, and the statement is both a diagnosis and a program: we see surfaces because we have not trained ourselves to see deeper. Vision, in Thoreau's understanding, is not passive reception but active construction — the eye sees what the soul is prepared to receive. To change what one sees, one must change who one is.

## In Walden

Thoreau's practice at Walden is a discipline of vision. He trains himself to see the pond not as a single body of water but as a changing surface — now blue, now green, now gray, now transparent — and the training is transferable: the pond teaches him to see everything with the same attention to mutability. "Looking with divided vision, so as to see the reflection" (009-005), he describes his method of observing the pond, and the "divided vision" becomes a model for all perception — the ability to see the thing and its reflection simultaneously, the surface and the depth in a single glance.

The most important vision in *Walden* is the vision that occurs in solitude. "He in me that now reviews the vision" (003-001), Thoreau writes, describing a state of self-consciousness in which the observer and the observed are the same. This reflexive vision — seeing oneself seeing — is Thoreau's version of transcendental perception: not a vision of another world but a vision of this world from a vantage point that includes the seer. No dust has settled on that vision, he claims, because it occurs in the clear medium of solitude, where the usual obscuring particles of social life have fallen away.

Thoreau is also attentive to the failures and distortions of vision. "Relieved by the grotesque visions with which, owing to bodily" (005-017) illness or exhaustion, the mind populates the darkness. Vision can deceive; the eye can see what is not there, or fail to see what is. Thoreau's honesty about this is part of his method: the reliable seer is not the one who never errs but the one who acknowledges error and corrects for it. His Walden Pond, "beheld this vision of serenity and purity once and" (009-036), is both a real place and an ideal image, and he knows the difference.

The night brings its own visions. Thoreau recounts a neighbor's "terrible dream or vision which he had during his confinement" (010-002) — a reminder that vision is not always benign, that what the mind sees in extremity can terrify. Yet even the terrifying vision has value: it reveals what the daylight mind conceals. The "midnight vision" (013-022) is Thoreau's poetic figure for the deepest seeing, the sight that comes when ordinary vision is suspended.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's philosophy of vision is a philosophy of attention. The problem is not that we lack eyes but that we do not use them. "The question is not what you look at, but what you see," he might have said; in fact he says something close: the Walden experiment is in large part an experiment in seeing — seeing the pond, seeing the seasons, seeing the self — with a clarity unavailable in the distraction of the village.

Vision for Thoreau is also communal. The seer who reports his vision makes it available to others; *Walden* itself is the record of one person's seeing, offered as an aid to the seeing of others. Thoreau does not claim unique perceptual gifts; he claims only that he took the time to look, and that anyone who took the same time might see something comparable. Vision is democratic: the capacity is universal, the cultivation is optional, and the results are transformative.
