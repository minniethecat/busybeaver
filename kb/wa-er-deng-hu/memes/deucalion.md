# Deucalion

Figure from Greek mythology who, with Pyrrha, repopulated the earth after a great flood; briefly referenced by Thoreau in Walden's Economy chapter as an archetype of human renewal.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau invokes Deucalion in the opening pages of Economy, drawing a parallel between the mythological repopulation of the earth and the human capacity for self-renewal through deliberate living. The reference sets the mythic tone for Thoreau's experiment—suggesting that going to Walden is itself an act of world-making, a throwing of stones over one's shoulder from which a new life may grow.

> [001-005] It is said that Deucalion and Pyrrha created men by throwing stones over their heads behind them:—Inde genus durum sumus, experiensque laborum, Et documenta damus qua simus origine nati. Or, as Raleigh rhymes it in his sonorous way,—"From thence our kind hard-hearted is, enduring pain and care, Approving that our bodies of a stony nature are."

## Significance

Thoreau's use of the Deucalion myth at the outset of Walden is programmatic. The myth—in which stones thrown behind the back become a new race of humans—becomes a metaphor for Thoreau's own project: by turning away from society and toward the natural world, he hopes to create a new kind of life. The Latin tag he quotes ("hence we are a hard race, experienced in toil, and we give proof of the origin from which we are sprung") also introduces the theme of labor and hardship that runs throughout Walden. Deucalion thus serves as the mythological key to Thoreau's central argument: that human beings can remake themselves through deliberate, principled action.
