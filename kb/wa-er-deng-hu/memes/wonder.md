# Wonder / Awe

## Definition

Wonder in *Walden* is not a passing emotion but a philosophical discipline. For Thoreau, the capacity to be astonished — by the transparency of ice, the geometry of a pond, the persistence of a single bean sprout — is the defining characteristic of the awakened mind. Wonder is the opposite of the "quiet desperation" (001-015) that afflicts those who have ceased to see what is before them. To wonder is to perceive the "miracle which is taking place every instant" (001-021), and this perception is, for Thoreau, the beginning of both wisdom and genuine religion.

## In Walden

Thoreau establishes wonder as a philosophical principle in the opening pages of "Economy." Surveying the "thousand remarkable ways" in which his neighbors do penance, he confesses himself astonished — not by exotic spectacles but by the ordinary madness of ordinary life. "I sometimes wonder," he writes, "that we can be so frivolous, I may almost say, as to attend to the gross but somewhat foreign form of servitude called Negro Slavery, there are so many keen and subtle masters that enslave both north and south" (001-014). This is wonder as moral clarity: the capacity to be astonished by what convention has rendered invisible.

The same wonder, turned outward toward nature, becomes a mode of perception. Walking the railroad causeway, Thoreau reports: "I used to wonder at the halo of light around my shadow, and would fain fancy myself one of the elect" (010-002). The halo is an optical phenomenon — explicable, mundane — but Thoreau's wonder transforms it into a sign of election, a small apocalypse. He does not need miracles; the ordinary, seen with sufficient attention, is already miraculous. "All change is a miracle to contemplate," he insists, "but it is a miracle which is taking place every instant" (001-021).

The pond itself is a primary object of wonder. "At first you wonder if the Indians could have formed them on the ice for any purpose," Thoreau writes of the mysterious ridges on Walden's floor, "but they are too regular and some of them obviously too deep" (009-015). The wondering mind proposes hypotheses, tests them against evidence, and remains open to mystery — this is the scientific attitude and the religious one, fused. Thoreau measures the pond's depth not to demystify it but to wonder more precisely.

Wonder reaches its highest pitch in "Spring," where the thawing sand and clay of the railroad cut become a vision of creation itself. Thoreau watches "the wonderful and varied spectacle of this universe" (016-001) unfold in miniature — the sand foliage that seems to recapitulate the forms of all living things, a "leaf" that is the prototype of all organic form. This is wonder as theophany: the perception of the divine through and within the material.

In "Solitude," wonder is the antidote to loneliness. Standing before a lightning-blasted pine, Thoreau writes: "I passed it again the other day, and was struck with awe on looking up and beholding that mark, now more distinct than ever, where a terrific and resistless bolt came down out of the harmless sky" (005-008). Awe — the more intense, more fearful cousin of wonder — here brings not terror but companionship. The blasted tree is a "kindred" presence, scarred by forces larger than itself.

The "Conclusion" returns wonder to its moral function. "No wonder that Alexander carried the Iliad with him on his expeditions in a precious casket" (003-005), Thoreau had earlier remarked of the hunger for great texts. But the true wonder is not the book but the world the book describes, and the world the reader inhabits. Thoreau's final exhortation — to "explore your own higher latitudes" (018-021) — is an invitation to wonder at one's own unexplored interior, as vast and as worthy of astonishment as any foreign shore.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's wonder is distinctive in its democracy. He does not reserve astonishment for the sublime — the mountain peak, the storm at sea — but directs it at the proximate: a pond, a woodchuck, the sound of the wind in the telegraph wire. This is wonder as a practice, a daily discipline of attention. It requires no special equipment and no exotic travel; it requires only the willingness to see what is already there. "The stars are the apexes of what wonderful triangles!" (001-019) — but so is the thawing sandbank, so is the bean sprout, so is the reflection of clouds on still water. Wonder, in Thoreau's hands, becomes a form of resistance: against the deadening force of habit, against the "stereotyped but unconscious despair" (001-015) that passes for normal life.
