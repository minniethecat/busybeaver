# Hawk

## Definition

In *Walden*, hawks are creatures of the air — circling, soaring, diving in patterns that Thoreau reads as the visible form of freedom. Unlike the owl's dark wisdom or the frog's choral persistence, the hawk embodies pure motion, a wildness that cannot be captured or domesticated. Its flight is nature's calligraphy, written on the sky and erased in the same instant.

## In Walden

The hawk first appears in "Sounds" as part of the view from Thoreau's doorway on a summer afternoon: "hawks are circling about my clearing" (004-005). The circling motion is significant — the hawk does not travel in a straight line from point A to point B but describes circles, a geometry of attention rather than destination. Thoreau's own days at Walden follow a similar pattern: not linear progress but sustained, circling contemplation.

Elsewhere in the same chapter, the hawk's cry enters the soundscape: "it was a new sound, sounding like the scream of a hawk sailing over some farmer's yard" (004-010). Even when heard indirectly — a simile for another sound — the hawk's scream carries connotations of wildness intruding on domestic space.

The richest hawk passage appears at the end of "Sounds," where Thoreau writes his celebrated declaration: "I rejoice that there are owls." The sentence continues beyond the owl, however, to encompass "small hawks circulate above, and the chickadee lisps amid the evergreens" (004-034). The hawk is part of the "vast and undeveloped nature which men have not recognized," the twilight world that the daylight economy ignores.

In "The Bean-Field," the hawk punctuates Thoreau's agricultural labor: "The night-hawk circled overhead in the sunny afternoons" (007-006). Even while hoeing beans, Thoreau keeps one eye on the sky. The hawk's presence collapses the distinction between work and observation — to farm at Walden is to be perpetually distracted by the sublime.

"The Ponds" chapter records a more direct encounter: "I have sometimes disturbed a fish hawk sitting on a white-pine over the water" (009-014). The verb "disturbed" acknowledges that Thoreau is the intruder here; the hawk was there first, and the observer's presence momentarily disrupts the wild order.

The most detailed hawk description comes in "Spring," where Thoreau witnesses an aerial display of stunning precision: "I observed a very slight and graceful hawk, like a night-hawk, alternately soaring like a ripple and tumbling a rod or two over and over, showing the underside of its wings, which gleamed like a satin ribbon" (017-042). The simile chain — ripple, tumble, satin ribbon — translates flight into a sequence of visual impressions, each more surprising than the last. The hawk does not merely fly; it performs a "slight and graceful" ballet for an audience of one.

Earlier in "Spring," the marsh-hawk appears as another seasonal signal: "The marsh-hawk sailing low over the meadow is already looking for the first sluggish life" (017-013). The hawk hunts; it is not merely a symbol but a predator, and Thoreau's naturalism refuses to prettify this fact.

> [004-034] I rejoice that there are owls. Let them do the idiotic and maniacal hooting for men. It is a sound admirably suited to swamps and twilight woods which no day illustrates, suggesting a vast and undeveloped nature which men have not recognized. All day the sun has shone on the surface of some savage swamp, where the single spruce stands hung with usnea lichens, and small hawks circulate above, and the chickadee lisps amid the evergreens.

## Thoreau's Perspective

The hawk, for Thoreau, is the visible form of the transcendentalist aspiration — to rise above the particular and see the whole. Yet Thoreau's hawk is never merely allegorical. He observes its actual flight patterns, its hunting behavior, its seasonal comings and goings with a naturalist's fidelity. The hawk is both symbol and creature, and Thoreau's genius is to refuse to choose between these registers. The hawk soars; the hawk hunts; both facts are true, and both illuminate the life Thoreau is trying to live at the pond's edge.

## Historical Context

By the mid-nineteenth century, many hawk species in New England were under pressure from deforestation and hunting. Thoreau's detailed records of species and behaviors have proven valuable to modern ecologists reconstructing baseline wildlife populations. His hawks are not generic "birds of prey" but specific observed individuals — the night-hawk, the marsh-hawk, the fish hawk — each with its own habits and habitats.
