# White Pond

A clear, forty-acre pond about a mile and a half beyond Fair Haven, described by Thoreau as the "lesser twin of Walden" and the gem of the Concord woods.

## Location and Geography

White Pond is located about a mile and a half southwest of Fair Haven, in the Nine Acre Corner area of Concord. It covers approximately forty acres and, like Walden, is a kettle pond of remarkable clarity and depth. Thoreau describes it as "a lesser twin of Walden," so similar in character that "you would say they must be connected under ground."

## In Walden

White Pond appears in "The Ponds" chapter as Walden's matching gem—less famous but, in Thoreau's view, perhaps even more beautiful after Walden had been "profaned" by woodcutters, the railroad, and Thoreau himself. The pond's waters are of the same "misty bluish-green or glaucous color" as Walden's.

> [009-042] Since the woodcutters, and the railroad, and I myself have profaned Walden, perhaps the most attractive, if not the most beautiful, of all our lakes, the gem of the woods, is White Pond;—a poor name from its commonness, whether derived from the remarkable purity of its waters or the color of its sands.

> [009-044] White Pond and Walden are great crystals on the surface of the earth, Lakes of Light.

## Ecological Features

White Pond shares Walden's stony shore and remarkable water clarity. Thoreau records the famous account of the sunken yellow-pine tree that stood upright on the pond bottom for decades, its branches pointing downward—a local curiosity that drew visitors. The pond is less frequented than Walden, preserving a wilder character.

## Significance

White Pond represents the unspoiled counterpart to Walden—what Walden was before human attention "profaned" it. Thoreau's preference for White Pond in the later pages of "The Ponds" echoes his ambivalence about his own role in making Walden known: the very act of living at and writing about Walden changes it.
