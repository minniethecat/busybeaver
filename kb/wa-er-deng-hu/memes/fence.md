# Fence

## Definition

The fence in *Walden* is the visible inscription of property on the landscape. It marks where one person's claim ends and another's begins, transforming open land into owned parcels. Thoreau treats the fence as the primary architectural expression of a society organized around possession rather than experience. To leap a fence — which he does repeatedly — is to refuse the legitimacy of these artificial divisions. The fence is not morally neutral: it fences nature *out* as much as it fences property *in*, and in doing so, it impoverishes both the land and the human relationship to it.

## In Walden

Thoreau's critique of fences runs throughout the text, sometimes explicit, sometimes enacted through his own movements. He confesses that he has frequently "had an eye to the advantages of getting a living by the exercise of skill, and jumping fences" (001-035) — phrasing that frames fence-jumping as an alternative economy, a mode of mobility that bypasses the legal geography of ownership. The fence is an obstacle only to those who accept its authority.

The architectural contrast is most vivid when Thoreau describes his own house as having "No yard! but unfenced Nature reaching up to your very sills" (004-036). The absence of a fence becomes the defining feature of the Walden dwelling — the cabin does not exclude the woods but opens directly onto them. A fence, by contrast, "puts such an interval between the house and the barn" (002-006) that the simple adjacency of dwelling and work is broken. The rail-fence is a symptom of a worldview that partitions life into discrete, fenced-off compartments.

Thoreau also notes that Walden Pond itself "needs no fence. Nations come and go without defiling it" (009-019). The pond's boundary is natural, self-maintaining, and indifferent to human claims. It requires no enclosure because its integrity is intrinsic, not legal — the model for how a place should relate to its inhabitants.

> [004-036] …in the yard. No yard! but unfenced Nature reaching up to your very sills. A young forest growing up under your windows, and wild sumachs and blackberry vines breaking through into your cellar…

> [009-019] Sky water. It needs no fence. Nations come and go without defiling it.

> [001-035] …I have looked after the wild stock of the town, which give a faithful herdsman a good deal of trouble by leaping fences; and I have had an eye to the advantages of getting a living by the exercise of skill, and jumping fences.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, fences are not merely practical structures — they are physical manifestations of a possessive individualism that he rejects. The fence says "mine" where nature says "ours." His repeated acts of crossing fences, of moving through gaps without hesitation (008-001), are acts of philosophical trespass: the assertion that the walker's right of passage precedes the landowner's right of exclusion. The fence is the original sin of civil society, the first mark on the land that transforms earth into real estate.

Yet Thoreau is not entirely without pragmatism on the subject. He notes that the old forest fence "which had seen its best days" (013-012) was repurposed as fuel for his hearth, a material recycling that undoes the fence's original function. The fence that once divided becomes the fire that warms — a small redemption through use.

## Historical Context

In nineteenth-century New England, stone walls and rail-fences were ubiquitous features of the agricultural landscape, marking field boundaries, pastures, and woodlots. The region's rocky soil produced an abundance of fieldstone that farmers stacked into walls — many of which still run through New England forests today, remnants of farms long abandoned. Thoreau was writing in an era when enclosure was still an active process, with common lands being increasingly privatized. His critique of fences participates in a broader Romantic and Transcendentalist suspicion of property regimes, shared by figures like Emerson and the English Romantics before them.
