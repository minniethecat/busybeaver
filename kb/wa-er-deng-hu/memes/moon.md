# Moon

## Definition

The moon in *Walden* is an alternative sun — a gentler, more contemplative source of illumination that reveals a different Walden than the one visible by day. Where the morning sun represents awakening, clarity, and the deliberate life, the moon represents mystery, reflection, and the life of the night. Thoreau's moon is a companion to solitude: it does not intrude upon the darkness so much as share it, making the night navigable without dispelling its essential quality. The moonlit walk and the midnight fishing expedition are lunar practices, attuned to a rhythm older and slower than the solar day.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most sustained engagement with moonlight occurs on the pond itself. Fishing "from a boat by moonlight, serenaded by owls and foxes" (009-004), he experiences the pond as a different world — the same water, the same surrounding woods, but transformed by lunar illumination into something stranger and more intimate. The moonlight turns the pond into a mirror of the sky, and the fisherman, suspended between two darknesses — the water below and the night above — becomes a creature of the moon, his activity timed to lunar rather than solar rhythms.

The moon also figures in Thoreau's sense of the pond as a complete world. "My own sun and moon and stars, and a little world all to myself" (005-003), he writes of his solitude at Walden, claiming the celestial bodies as personal possessions, the furnishings of his cosmic home. The claim is playful but philosophically serious: the moon belongs to whoever sees it, and Thoreau, by virtue of his attention, sees it more fully than most. The moon that rises over Walden Pond is not the same moon that rises over Concord village; it is a wilder moon, a moon restored to its original significance.

The moon is also a timekeeper. Thoreau notes his visitor John Field "at other times, and even by moonlight" (010-002) coming to fish, the moonlight extending the working day beyond its solar limit. For Thoreau, the moonlit hours are not an extension of labor but a sanctuary from it — the time when the demands of the bean-field and the woodpile are suspended by darkness, and the only possible activity is contemplation.

Winter moonlight has its own character. "Ranged over the snow crust, in moonlight nights, in search of a" (015-004) quarry, the fox and the hunter share the lunar landscape, the snow reflecting the moonlight until the night is almost as bright as day but entirely different in quality. "At midnight, when there was a moon, I sometimes met with hounds" (015-012) in the woods, Thoreau writes, and the encounter — human and animal meeting in the moonlit forest — has the quality of myth, as if the moonlight dissolved the boundary between species along with the boundary between day and night.

The moon's decline is as significant as its presence. "Day and night, sun down, moon down, and at last earth down too" (018-012), Thoreau writes in his concluding chapter, enumerating the downward movements of celestial bodies as a figure for the inevitability of endings. Yet the moon rises again, and Thoreau's final image is of a vantage point "where there I could best see the moonlight amid the mountains" (018-014) — the moon as the light by which one takes one's bearings for the journey ahead.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's moon is a teacher of subtlety. Solar philosophy is about clarity, definition, and the casting out of shadows; lunar philosophy is about ambiguity, gradation, and the acceptance of shadow as an inextricable part of sight. The moon does not banish the darkness; it softens it, makes it habitable. Thoreau, who valued both the solar clarity of morning and the lunar mystery of night, understood that a complete life requires both kinds of light.

The moon also represents what Thoreau would call the "wild" in perception. Moonlight seeing is not the seeing of the surveyor or the scientist; it is the seeing of the poet, the lover, the solitary. It reveals not the outlines of things but their atmospheres. To walk by moonlight is to walk in a world made strange — the same trees, the same path, but transformed by a light that leaves room for imagination.
