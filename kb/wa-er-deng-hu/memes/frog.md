# Frog

## Definition

In *Walden*, frogs — especially bullfrogs — are the bass note of the pond's soundscape: loud, ancient-sounding, and gloriously unashamed. Thoreau hears in their trumpeting not merely animal noise but the voice of an older, wilder earth, a choral assertion of life that human propriety cannot domesticate.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most vivid frog passage comes at dusk in "Sounds," when the pond's shoreline becomes a theater of amphibian music. As evening deepens and the sounds of wagons and dogs fade into the distance, "all the shore rang with the trump of bullfrogs, the sturdy spirits of ancient wine-bibbers and wassailers, still unrepentant, trying to sing a catch in their Stygian lake" (004-035). The mock-heroic register — bullfrogs as unrepentant revelers, the pond as the river Styx — is affectionate rather than dismissive. Thoreau grants these creatures a mythology; they are not merely noisy but cosmically, defiantly alive.

The frog chorus returns in "Solitude" as a companionable presence rather than a disturbance. "The bullfrogs trump to usher in the night" (005-001), and the verb is precise: they *usher*, performing a ceremonial office. On a "delicious evening, when the whole body is one sense," the frog-song becomes part of Thoreau's expanded sensorium — not an external stimulus but a texture of the evening itself.

In "The Village," Thoreau registers a subtler frog presence: "the peeping of frogs" (008-001) accompanies his walk through the woods, a smaller, more delicate amphibian music than the bullfrogs' trumpet. This auditory gradient — from the peeping of tree frogs to the trump of bullfrogs — maps the pond's acoustic ecology with a naturalist's precision.

"The Ponds" inventory of Walden's fauna includes "a clean race of frogs and tortoises, and a few mussels" (009-014). The adjective "clean" is characteristically Thoreauvian — the pond's frogs are not muddy or slimy but purified by the water they inhabit. Walden's clarity extends to its creatures.

In "Spring," the frog becomes a seasonal marker. Thoreau notes that "in almost all climes the tortoise and the frog are among the precursors and heralds of this season" (017-017). The frog's reappearance signals the earth's awakening, its voice the first syllable of the year's renewal.

> [004-035] In the mean while all the shore rang with the trump of bullfrogs, the sturdy spirits of ancient wine-bibbers and wassailers, still unrepentant, trying to sing a catch in their Stygian lake.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's frogs embody a quality he values throughout *Walden*: unapologetic self-expression. The bullfrog does not modulate its volume to avoid disturbing the neighbors; it trumpets because it must. This is the same principle Thoreau applies to his own writing — a "simple and sincere account" of a life, offered without apology. The frog is nature's autobiographer, singing its own existence into the night.

## Historical Context

In classical literature, frogs were associated with the underworld (the Stygian reference is deliberate) and with poetic inspiration — Aristophanes' *Frogs* stages a contest of poets in Hades. Thoreau's "Stygian lake" allusion activates this literary heritage while grounding it in close observation of a real Massachusetts pond. The bullfrog's croak is both a biological fact and a mythic echo.
