# Fame

## Definition

Fame in *Walden* is the public shadow of private worth — a shadow that Thoreau consistently treats as thinner and less nourishing than the substance it mimics. "Fame won by his own deeds" (001-014) may sound noble, but in Thoreau's usage the phrase describes the man who is "the slave and prisoner of his own opinion of himself," driven not by inner conviction but by the need for external validation. Against the trumpeted fame of markets and battlefields, Thoreau sets a quieter standard: the mosquito's hum that is "more than any trumpet that ever sang of fame" (002-021), and the pickerel whose beauty far exceeds the "fame trumpeted in our streets" (016-005). True worth, he insists, does not need a trumpet.

## In Walden

Thoreau's critique of fame begins in the opening pages of "Economy," where he anatomizes the self-enslaved man: "the slave and prisoner of his own opinion of himself, a fame won by his own deeds" (001-014). The formulation cuts deep — even deeds that win the world's applause can become a prison if the doer is beholden to reputation. Public opinion, Thoreau continues, "is a weak tyrant compared with our own private opinion," implying that the pursuit of fame is ultimately a pursuit of self-approval by proxy, a confession that one's own judgment is insufficient.

The counterpoint arrives in "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For." Describing the dawn chorus of his Walden mornings, Thoreau writes that he was "as much affected by the faint hum of a mosquito making its invisible and unimaginable tour through my apartment at earliest dawn... as I could be by any trumpet that ever sang of fame" (002-021). The mosquito's hum is "Homer's requiem; itself an Iliad and Odyssey in the air." The inversion is deliberate: the smallest, most ephemeral sound in nature outweighs all the trumpets of military glory. Fame is not just overrated; it is dwarfed by the cosmic significance of the merely real. A mosquito, fully attended to, contains more epic than a battlefield.

Thoreau also invokes Concord's "only field known to fame, Concord Battle Ground" (002-012), the site of the first battle of the American Revolution. His tone is dismissive — the battlefield is the town's claim to fame, but Thoreau's own Walden Pond, two miles south, is "so low in the woods" that it escapes notice entirely. The obscurity is the point: what is famous is not thereby valuable, and what is valuable may be utterly obscure.

In "Reading," Thoreau approaches fame from the perspective of mortality. "In accumulating property for ourselves or our posterity, in founding a family or a state, or acquiring fame even, we are mortal; but in dealing with truth we are immortal" (003-001). Fame, like property and dynastic ambition, belongs to the world of death. Only truth confers anything like permanence, and truth does not require a public. The reader who deals with truth in solitude is more "immortal" than the conqueror whose name echoes through a thousand trumpets.

The "trumpet that sings of fame" appears again in "The Bean-Field," this time heard from a distance. A military band playing in the village reaches Thoreau's field as a faint strain, and "I felt as if I could spit a Mexican with a good relish" (007-009) — the martial music inflames a mock-heroic aggression that Thoreau immediately undercuts with self-mockery. Fame's trumpets, he suggests, make one want to shoot something; the bean-field's silence makes one want to grow something.

In "The Ponds," Thoreau reduces fame to its most literal form: the weight of a fish. "The weight of a fish is commonly its only title to fame" (009-014), he writes, cataloguing the pickerel and perch of Walden. A fish's fame is its poundage — a measure as crude and uninteresting as the fame of men measured in dollars or titles. The pond's inhabitants have a different kind of value, "much cleaner, handsomer, and firmer fleshed" than their river counterparts, a value that requires no weighing to be appreciated.

The image recurs in "The Pond in Winter," where the pickerel's "dazzling and transcendent beauty" separates them "by a wide interval from the cadaverous cod and haddock whose fame is trumpeted in our streets" (016-005). The cod's fame is commercial — it is the fish that is bought and sold, whose weight is recorded in ledgers. The pickerel has no fame and no market; it has, instead, "rare colors, like flowers and precious stones." Fame, in the Thoreauvian economy, is what things have when they lack intrinsic beauty.

The final and most famous word on fame comes in "Conclusion": "Rather than love, than money, than fame, give me truth" (018-025). Thoreau recounts sitting at a rich man's table where "they talked to me of the age of the wine and the fame of the vintage," but he went away hungry because "sincerity and truth were not." The fame of the vintage is a story told to justify a price; truth is the wine itself, which these men cannot offer because they do not possess it. The triplet — love, money, fame — names the three great objects of human striving, and Thoreau dismisses all three in favor of the one thing that cannot be accumulated, traded, or celebrated: truth.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of fame is radical not in rejecting it — many moralists have done that — but in proposing a scale by which the mosquito's hum outweighs the trumpet of Achilles. Fame, for Thoreau, is a category error: it confuses the report of a thing with the thing itself. The man who seeks fame is like someone who would rather read a review of a book than the book, or rather admire a label than drink the wine. The cure for the love of fame is not modesty but attention — the deliberate, unhurried perception of what is actually before one, which reveals that the mosquito, the pickerel, and the morning are already more wonderful than any reputation could be.
