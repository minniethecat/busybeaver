# Pasture

## Definition

The pasture is a landscape of intermediate character — neither wild forest nor cultivated field, but ground that has been cleared for grazing and then, in Thoreau's New England, often abandoned to the advance of young woods. In *Walden*, the pasture is Thoreau's own symbolic territory: "There was pasture enough for my imagination" (002-014). It is the landscape of the walker, the berry-picker, the contemplative who needs open ground in which to wander without destination. The pasture is low and unheroic — Thoreau calls it his "lowest" ground — but it is also the ground from which all his higher flights begin.

## In Walden

The pasture makes its first appearance in the opening paragraphs, in Thoreau's famous image of young men who have inherited farms and would be better off "if they had been born in the open pasture and suckled by a wolf" (001-004). The open pasture, opposed to the inherited farm, represents freedom from property — a place where one might see "with clearer eyes what field they were called to labor in."

In "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," Thoreau makes the pasture his own. Surveying the landscape around his prospective house, he writes: "There was pasture enough for my imagination. The low shrub-oak plateau to which the opposite shore arose... was the pasture I had dreamed of" (002-014). The pasture is not grand; it is "low" and unremarkable. But its very lack of drama makes it available to the imagination in a way that a more spectacular landscape would not be. Thoreau does not need a mountain; he needs a pasture — open, undemanding, sufficient.

In "The Bean-Field," the pasture becomes a site of natural wonder. Thoreau notes that "in the woods and pastures and swamps grows a rich and various crop, unreaped by man" (007-004). The wild crops — berries, herbs, the "perennial root" of truth — grow where human agriculture does not reach. The pasture is a middle ground where the cultivated and the wild overlap, and Thoreau's attention moves freely between them.

The pasture also marks the edge of the village. Walking through "retired pastures" (014-013), Thoreau finds "new-rising bushes" and reads the landscape as a palimpsest of human use and natural reclamation. What was once grazing ground is returning to wood — the pasture is a stage in the succession that Thoreau observes with patient attention.

> [002-014] There was pasture enough for my imagination. The low shrub-oak plateau to which the opposite shore arose, stretching away toward the prairies of the West and the steppes of Tartary, afforded ample room for all the roving families of men.

> [004-019] Their flocks, all but the mountain pastures, whirled along like leaves blown from the mountains by the October winds.

> [009-001] ...to fresh woods and pastures new...

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's preference for the pasture over the mountain is characteristic. The pasture does not demand ascent or heroic effort; it asks only that one walk through it with attention. It is the landscape of the everyday sublime — the ordinary ground that, when seen with the right eyes, opens onto "the prairies of the West and the steppes of Tartary" (002-014). The pasture is Thoreau's version of the infinite: not the vastness of the ocean or the height of the peak, but the open field that imagination can fill. "Pasture enough" is a modest phrase for an immodest claim: that the world, rightly seen, is always sufficient.
