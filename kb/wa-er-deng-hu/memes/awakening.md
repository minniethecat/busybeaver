# Awakening

## Definition

Awakening is one of the central imperatives of Walden. Thoreau uses "awake" and "awakening" to describe the condition of full consciousness — a state that must be actively cultivated against the constant pull of habit, convention, and spiritual sleep. To be awake is to perceive reality directly, unmediated by social consensus or inherited belief.

## In Walden

The theme is established early in the book as a moral summons:

> [002-022] We must learn to reawaken and keep ourselves awake, not by mechanical aids, but by an infinite expectation of the dawn.

Thoreau frames morning itself as the archetypal awakening hour:

> [002-021] The most memorable season of the day is the awakening hour. Then there is least somnolence.

This awakening extends beyond the human to encompass all of nature:

> [004-034] A different race of creatures awakes to express the meaning of Nature.

Thoreau contrasts deliberate awakening with the passive "dreaming awake" state that characterizes conventional life:

> [009-023] I sat in my sunny doorway from sunrise till noon, rapt in a revery, amidst the pines and hickories, in undisturbed solitude and stillness, while the birds sang around or flitted through the house, until I was aroused by the sun falling on my western window.

The ultimate statement of Thoreau's philosophy of awakening closes the book:

> [018-029] Only that day dawns to which we are awake. There is more day to dawn. The sun is but a morning star.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, awakening is not a one-time event but a perpetual discipline. The natural human tendency is toward somnolence — toward living by rote, accepting inherited opinions, and mistaking conventional wisdom for truth. The "infinite expectation of the dawn" is an orientation toward perpetual renewal, a refusal to let any day pass unexamined. This connects awakening to Thoreau's broader project of deliberate living: one goes to Walden not to sleep more soundly but to wake more fully.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's language of awakening draws on multiple traditions: the Protestant Great Awakening's emphasis on personal spiritual renewal, Emerson's call in "Nature" for an "original relation to the universe," and Eastern meditation practices (referenced throughout Walden) that cultivate mindful awareness. What distinguishes Thoreau is his naturalization of awakening — it happens not in church or through scripture but through direct contact with morning light, birdsong, and the physical discipline of early rising.
