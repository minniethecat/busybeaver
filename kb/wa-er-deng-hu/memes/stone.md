# Stone

## Definition

Stone in *Walden* functions as the elemental symbol of permanence and foundation. In Thoreau's landscape of seasonal transformation — ice that melts, fields that yield, houses that crumble — stone alone endures. It is the material substrate beneath the organic world, the relic of former inhabitants, and the witness against which human transience is measured. "What is a house," Thoreau asks implicitly throughout his narrative, "but a *sedes*" — a seat carved from the enduring earth, anchored by stone.

## In Walden

Stone appears at every register of Thoreau's experience, from the mythic to the mundane. In "Economy," he invokes the ancient fable that "Deucalion and Pyrrha created men by throwing stones over their heads behind them" (001-005), casting stone as the primordial human substance — humanity born of the mineral world, not separate from it. The same passage recurs later: "The thundering oracle, throwing the stones over their heads behind them, saw them gradually assume 'the human face divine'" (001-011). Stone is not dead matter but latent life.

On the practical level, stone is the literal foundation of Thoreau's experiment. His cellar, two feet deep and six feet square, was dug into the hillside and "stoned" for stability. He speaks of "bringing two cartloads of stones up the hill from the pond in my arms" (001-081), hauling not lumber but the earth's own weight to anchor his habitation. The act is emblematic: the deliberate life must be grounded in something as durable as stone.

Walden Pond itself owes its clarity to stone. Thoreau observes "a belt of smooth rounded white stones like paving stones" (009-007) on the pond floor, and records the local tradition that "when the hill shook these stones rolled down its side and became the bottom of the pond" (009-012). The pond's perfect transparency is a function of this mineral floor — no mud, no murk, only stone polished by the water's perpetual attention. "Every leaf and twig and stone and cobweb sparkles now at midday," he writes of the pond's surface, "set round with stones as precious to my eye as if fewer or more" (009-019).

In "House-Warming," Thoreau uses pond stones to build his chimney foundation: "I built the chimney after my hoeing in the fall, bringing stones from the pond shore, and also built the hearth" (013-005). Stone mediates between the cold earth and the warm hearth — the fireplace, like the life it sustains, must be founded on something that does not burn. He speculates: "I might have got good limestone within a mile or two and burned it myself, had I wanted" (013-010), but prefers the unprocessed pond stones, as he prefers the unprocessed life.

The most haunting stone images in *Walden* appear in "Former Inhabitants and Winter Visitors." Walking the abandoned roads that cut through Walden Woods, Thoreau finds "buried cellar stones" (014-012) — all that remains of the homes of freed slaves, laborers, and outcasts who once lived on this land. "Still," he writes, "these ruins underlie the present dusty highway" (014-001). The stones are tombstones without inscription, mute testimony to lives the village has forgotten. Thoreau remembers them, and in remembering, insists that stone is not only foundation but memorial.

On the pond in winter, stone aids Thoreau's scientific curiosity. He measures the pond's depth using "a cod-line and a stone weighing about a pound and a half" (016-007), the stone as instrument of exact knowledge. Even here, the stone's weight, its pull toward the bottom, makes it the right tool for plumbing what is hidden — a fitting metaphor for Thoreau's own method of sounding the spiritual depths through material attention.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's attitude toward stone is characteristically double: he values it for its utility — foundation, tool, heat-bank — but also for what it refuses to be. Stone cannot be consumed, possessed, or reduced to exchange value. "I had three pieces of limestone on my desk," he confesses, "but I was terrified to find that they required to be dusted daily, while the furniture of my mind was all undusted still" (001-063). The stone reproaches him: its permanence exposes the neglect of the spiritual life. To attend to outward things while the inward crumbles is the error Thoreau diagnoses everywhere around him. Stone, in its silent durability, is the corrective — the witness that asks what, in us, will outlast the cellar hole.
