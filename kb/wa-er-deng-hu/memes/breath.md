# Breath

## Definition

In Walden, breath is both a physical fact and a philosophical metaphor. Thoreau attends to breath in its literal form — the chilled breath of workers on a winter morning (004-014), the "beneficent breath of the morning" that awakens the pond in spring (017-029).

He elevates breath to a figure for vitality itself. To breathe deliberately is to live deliberately; the shallow, hurried breath of the city dweller stands for a life "gasping for breath" (001-013) under the weight of unnecessary labor.

## In Walden

Breath is the invisible medium through which Thoreau registers the quality of his environment. The morning air at the pond is "intoxicating" — he writes of the pure air he breathes (011-011). The contrast is with the "malaria" breathed by those who ride in "the fancy car of an excursion train" (001-064).

The most dramatic breath in Walden belongs to the railroad locomotive. Thoreau describes the iron horse "breathing fire and smoke from his nostrils" (004-011), a fearsome parody of natural respiration. Yet he does not simply condemn it; he listens as attentively as he listens to the wind.

Breath also marks the boundary between life and death. The chimney's breath melting a hole in the snowdrift signals life within — "so relenting was the breath of the chimney" (014-016). In winter, breath becomes visible, a sign of presence.

The fog-bank of "chilled breath" around the laborer (004-014) is proof of exertion. The person who "breathes his own breath over again" (010-034), recirculating stale thoughts, is dying by inches.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau treats breath as the fundamental transaction between self and world. To breathe Walden's air is to take the place into oneself. This is why he insists on the quality of his atmosphere: "to be intoxicated by the air he breathes" (011-011) is both a literal experience and a spiritual ideal.

The rhythm of breath provides a model for the rhythm of life Thoreau advocates. Just as breathing alternates between taking in and releasing, so deliberate living alternates between solitude and society, labor and leisure.
