# Fitchburg Railroad

The railroad line that passed near Walden Pond, representing for Thoreau the forces of industrialization and commerce encroaching on the natural world.

## In Walden

The Fitchburg Railroad ran along the western shore of Walden Pond. Thoreau devotes an extended passage in Sounds to the train's daily passage, transforming its mechanical rhythms into a kind of modern epic. The railroad embodies the tension at the heart of Walden: the natural world and the industrial world occupy the same space, and Thoreau must reckon with both.

> [004-010] The Fitchburg Railroad touches the pond about a hundred rods south of where I dwell. I usually go to the village along its causeway, and am, as it were, related to society by this link.

> [004-011] When I hear the iron horse make the hills echo with his snort like thunder, shaking the earth with his feet, and breathing fire and smoke from his nostrils, it seems as if the earth had got a race now worthy to inhabit it.

## Significance

Thoreau's treatment of the railroad is characteristically ambivalent. He hears the train as a disturbance of the natural soundscape but also finds a kind of poetry in its regular passage. The railroad is "commerce" made audible—the sound of goods and people moving through a landscape that was once silent. By incorporating the train into his natural description rather than simply condemning it, Thoreau models the complex response to industrialization that makes Walden more than simple pastoral nostalgia.
