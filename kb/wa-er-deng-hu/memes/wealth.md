# Wealth

## Definition

In *Walden*, wealth is inverted from its conventional meaning. Thoreau systematically dismantles the 19th-century equation of prosperity with possessions, arguing instead that **true wealth consists in the freedom to live deliberately** — to have time for thought, nature, and self-culture. Material accumulation, in his view, is more often a form of impoverishment: "that seemingly wealthy, but most terribly impoverished class of all" (001-027).

## In Walden

The critique of wealth runs through the entire text, beginning with *Economy*, where Thoreau diagnoses the spiritual cost of materialism. He observes that the so-called "wealthy" are, in truth, trapped:

> [001-027] …that seemingly wealthy, but most terribly impoverished class of all, who have accumulated dross, but know not how to use it, or get rid of it, and thus have forged their own golden or silver fetters.

For Thoreau, the problem is not money itself but the **enslavement** to its pursuit. He contrasts the farmer who owns his land with the land that owns the farmer — a man who spends his life working to pay for a house he barely lives in. The true cost of anything, in Thoreau's calculus, is "the amount of what I will call life which is required to be exchanged for it."

Inherited wealth fares no better in his judgment. In *Economy*, he recounts a cautionary tale:

> [001-037] …arguments, and by some magic, wealth and standing followed, he had been cheated of his birthright, which was to live.

The "birthright" here is the capacity for authentic experience — lost when ease and status eliminate the need for genuine effort. Thoreau's own experiment at Walden Pond was a deliberate rejection of this bargain: he would live cheaply and thereby buy time, the only non-renewable resource.

Yet Thoreau is no simple ascetic. In *Reading*, he speaks of books as a form of genuine wealth:

> [003-005] Books are the treasured wealth of the world and the fit inheritance of generations and nations.

This is wealth of a different order — wealth that **grows** with use, that cannot be spent down, that passes from generation to generation without diminishing. It is intellectual and spiritual capital, the antithesis of the "dross" accumulated by the materially rich.

In *The Ponds*, Thoreau extends the metaphor to the natural landscape. Observing the farmers around Walden, he remarks:

> [009-039] …the poverty that enjoys true wealth. Farmers are respectable and interesting to me in proportion as they are poor, — poor farmers.

The paradox is deliberate: the poor farmer, who works the land with his own hands and knows it intimately, possesses a wealth of experience that the gentleman farmer, directing laborers from a distance, can never access.

The final chapter, *Conclusion*, brings the critique to its climax:

> [018-023] …there were bestowed on us the wealth of Crœsus, our aims must still be the same, and our means essentially the same.

Even if one possessed all the gold of Croesus, Thoreau insists, the work of living well would remain unchanged. The means — simplicity, attention, moral effort — are independent of the bank balance.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's position on wealth is more radical than simple anti-materialism. He does not merely argue that money cannot buy happiness; he argues that the **pursuit of money actively prevents** the pursuit of happiness, because both consume the same finite resource: a human life. His economic philosophy, laid out in the opening pages of *Economy*, is that one should calculate the true cost of any possession in hours of life exchanged — and that most purchases, by this measure, are catastrophic bargains.

> [001-041] …the accidental possession of wealth, and its manifestation in dress and equipage, is a mistake.

The word "accidental" is key: for Thoreau, wealth is not earned through merit but acquired through contingency — inheritance, luck, or exploitation. It confers no moral distinction. What *does* confer distinction is the deliberate cultivation of the self, which requires not wealth but time, and time requires simplicity.

He drives the point home with wry observation in *Sounds*:

> [004-036] …became unspeakably healthy, wealthy, and wise?

The echo of Ben Franklin's maxim — "early to bed and early to rise makes a man healthy, wealthy, and wise" — is ironic. Thoreau's "wealth" is health and wisdom; the third term of Franklin's triad has been subsumed into the other two.

## Historical Context

Thoreau wrote during the **Market Revolution** (1820s–1850s), when New England was transforming from an agrarian economy of subsistence farming into a commercial-industrial order of factories, railroads, and wage labor. Concord itself was bisected by the Fitchburg Railroad, whose morning train Thoreau watched from his doorway. The new economy generated fortunes — and also produced the first American literature of critique, from Thoreau's *Walden* to Melville's "Bartleby the Scrivener."

The **Transcendentalist** circle to which Thoreau belonged was uniformly skeptical of commerce. Emerson's "Self-Reliance" (1841) urged readers to trust inner conviction over social convention; Bronson Alcott's failed utopian community, Fruitlands, was an experiment in living without markets. Thoreau's contribution was the most practical: a detailed cost-accounting of how little one actually needs to live well.
