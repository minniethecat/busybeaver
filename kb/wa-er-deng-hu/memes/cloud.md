# Cloud

## Definition

Clouds in *Walden* are the sky's most mutable inhabitants — formations that appear, transform, and dissolve, demonstrating impermanence on a grand scale. Thoreau watches clouds not as a meteorologist but as a contemplative, finding in their drift and dissolution a model for the life of the mind: thoughts, like clouds, form and pass, and wisdom consists partly in letting them pass without clinging. The cloud is the visible form of change; to watch clouds is to practice acceptance of transience.

## In Walden

Thoreau's sky at Walden is never empty. Clouds shape the light, the mood, and the meaning of each day. "Though it is cool as well as cloudy and windy, and I see nothing special" (005-001), he writes on an unpromising morning, and the observation is significant: a cloudy day at Walden is not a disappointment but simply a condition, worthy of description in its own right. The pond, "full of light and reflections" when clear, becomes "shallow and darkened by clouds" (002-013) when the sky closes over. The clouds pattern the water with their passage, making the pond a moving canvas of light and shadow.

The railroad introduces a new kind of cloud into Thoreau's landscape: the steam cloud. The iron horse travels "with its steam cloud like a banner streaming behind in golden and silver wreaths" (004-011), and "their train of clouds stretching far behind and rising higher and higher" (004-012) becomes a new feature of the Walden sky. Thoreau is ambivalent — the steam cloud is both beautiful and industrial, a banner of commerce imposed on a pastoral scene. But he grants it its aesthetic due: even mechanized cloud-making cannot entirely escape the sky's capacity to transfigure.

Clouds are also agents of precipitation and renewal. "The clouds rain tears" (005-020), Thoreau writes in a moment of anthropomorphic sympathy, as if the sky mourned with the earth. Walden Pond has no inlet or outlet "except by the clouds and evaporation" (009-005): the clouds are the pond's only source of replenishment, the cycle of evaporation and rainfall that keeps the water level steady across seasons. The pond "sends its own to float as clouds high above its surface" (009-019), a vertical circulation that ties the depths to the heights.

The cloud-reflecting surface of Walden becomes Thoreau's most disorienting and sublime experience. "Reflecting the clouds, I seemed to be floating through the air as in a balloon" (009-021), he writes, suspended between water and sky, the boundary dissolved. The cloud is the agent of this dissolution: by occupying both the sky above and its reflection below, it collapses the distance between the celestial and the terrestrial, making the pond a threshold between worlds.

Not all clouds are benign. Thoreau knows the sudden storms of New England: "If suddenly in the shadow of a cloud, and the thunder began to rumble" (010-010), the sky turns threatening. The cloud-shadow is a warning, and Thoreau, always alert to nature's shifts, reads it instantly. "Take shelter under the cloud, while they flee to carts and sheds" (010-018), he advises — the cloud itself becomes shelter, its dark bulk a roof against the storm it brings.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, clouds are teachers of impermanence. They form from nothing, take shape for a moment, and dissolve back into the blue — and nothing is lost. The mind that can regard its own thoughts with the same equanimity, watching them form and pass without attachment, has attained something like wisdom. Cloud-watching is a form of meditation, and Thoreau practices it without naming it.

Clouds also serve a critical function in Thoreau's epistemology. Just as a pond without reflections would be invisible — pure transparency reveals nothing — so a mind without clouds, without the transient formations of thought and feeling, would be a blank. The clouds are what make the sky visible; thoughts are what make the mind aware of itself. The goal is not a cloudless sky but the right relationship to the clouds: neither ignoring them nor mistaking them for permanent structures.
