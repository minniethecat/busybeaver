# Dream

## Definition

Thoreau uses "dream" in Walden with extraordinary range: it can mean the night-dreams of sleep, the waking reverie of solitary contemplation, the visionary aspiration toward a higher life, and — critically — the social illusion from which one must awaken. Dreaming is not escapism but an alternate mode of cognition, a way of accessing truths unavailable to the calculating, practical mind.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most sustained meditation on dreaming occurs in "Solitude," where he describes sitting in his doorway in a state of receptive trance:

> [009-023] I sat in my sunny doorway from sunrise till noon, rapt in a revery, amidst the pines and hickories, in undisturbed solitude and stillness, until I was aroused by the sun falling on my western window.

This "dreaming awake" is not idleness but a deliberate practice of open attention — the mind receptive rather than directive. Elsewhere, Thoreau contrasts the shallowness of social dreaming with the depth of solitary vision:

> [002-025] After a night's sleep the news is as indispensable as the breakfast. "Pray tell me anything new that has happened to a man anywhere on this globe" — and he reads it over his coffee and rolls, that a man has had his eyes gouged out this morning on the Wachito River; never dreaming the while that he lives in the dark unfathomed mammoth cave of this world.

The famous closing of Walden returns to the imperative of living one's dreams in waking life:

> [018-015] If one advances confidently in the direction of his dreams, and endeavors to live the life which he has imagined, he will meet with a success unexpected in common hours.

Thoreau also records literal dreams, treating them as significant communications from the deeper self:

> [010-002] After a certain terrible dream or vision which he had during the night.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau positions dreaming on a spectrum: at one end is the "dream" of conventional life — the unexamined assumptions and inherited beliefs that most people mistake for reality. At the other end is the visionary dream — the deliberate cultivation of imagination and aspiration that enables one to "live the life which he has imagined" (018-015). Between these poles lies the waking dream of reverie, which Thoreau practices as a spiritual discipline, sitting motionless for hours while the mind ranges freely.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's treatment of dreaming reflects the Romantic rehabilitation of the dream-state (Coleridge's "Kubla Khan," De Quincey's *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater*) as a source of creative vision. But Thoreau's emphasis on "dreaming awake" — maintaining consciousness within the dream-state — owes more to Eastern meditation practices, which he encountered through his reading of Hindu and Buddhist texts.
