# Purity

## Definition

Purity in *Walden* operates on two interconnected levels. On the physical level, it describes the crystalline clarity of Walden Pond's water, which Thoreau celebrates as a visible manifestation of natural perfection. On the moral and spiritual level, purity signifies the state of self-discipline, temperance, and chastity that Thoreau argues is prerequisite for a genuinely higher life. The pond's transparency becomes a metaphor for the soul's condition: just as Walden's water reveals its depth, so a person's conduct reveals their inner purity.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most extended meditation on purity occurs in "The Ponds" (009), where he devotes considerable attention to the exceptional clarity of Walden's water. He contrasts it with neighboring ponds whose waters are comparatively impure:

> [009-005] The scenery of Walden is on a humble scale, and, though very beautiful, does not approach to grandeur, nor can it much concern one who has not long frequented it or lived by its shore; yet this pond is so remarkable for its depth and purity as to merit a particular description. It is a clear and deep green well, half a mile long and a mile and three quarters in circumference, and contains about sixty-one and a half acres; a perennial spring, with no visible inlet nor outlet.

The physical purity of the pond's water becomes a daily fact of Thoreau's life. He drinks from it directly, using it as his well:

> [009-013] The pond was my well ready dug. For four months in the year its water is as cold as it is pure at all times; and I think that it is then as good as any, if not the best, in the town.

In "Higher Laws" (011), Thoreau shifts from physical to moral purity, arguing that sensual indulgence compromises the spiritual life. He asserts the unity of all purity and warns against the contamination of character:

> [011-023] All sensuality is one, though it takes many forms; all purity is one. It is the same whether a man eat, or drink, or cohabit, or sleep sensually. They are but one appetite, and we only need to see a person do any one of these things to know how great a sensualist he is. The impure can neither stand nor sit with purity.

He extends this meditation into a broader reflection on the animal and spiritual natures within every person, insisting that purity requires active vigilance:

> [011-014] We are conscious of an animal in us, which awakens in proportion as our higher nature slumbers. It is reptile and sensual, and perhaps cannot be wholly expelled; like the worms which, even in life and health, occupy our bodies. Possibly we may withdraw from it, but never change its nature. I fear that it may enjoy a certain health of its own; that we may be well, yet not pure.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of purity is distinctively non-dogmatic. He does not invoke religious doctrine or social convention; rather, he grounds purity in direct observation of nature and in personal experience. Walden Pond's physical purity — its transparency, its cold clarity, its freedom from contamination — serves as his tangible proof that purity is a real and attainable condition. He extends this natural model to the moral realm: just as Flint's Pond's "comparatively impure waters" (009-037) disappoint the observer, so an impure life fails to sustain genuine fulfillment. For Thoreau, purity is not repression but an elevation — a deliberate shedding of excess sensuality that clears the way for higher perception and creative energy.
