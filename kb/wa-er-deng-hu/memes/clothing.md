# Clothing

## Definition

In Walden, clothing represents one of the fundamental necessities of human life that Thoreau believes society has inflated beyond its true purpose.

Clothing should serve two primary functions: retaining body heat and maintaining modesty. Thoreau argues that beyond these basic functions, clothing becomes a vehicle for vanity, social conformity, and economic waste — a "false necessity" that enslaves people to labor for appearances rather than substance.

## In Walden

Thoreau develops his critique of clothing primarily in "Economy," where he systematically deconstructs society's attachment to fashion. He observes that people are more concerned with being well-dressed than with being well-lived.

> [001-041] recollect that the object of clothing is, first, to retain the vital heat, and secondly, in this state of society, to cover nakedness.

Thoreau mocks the fashion industry's power over individual judgment. People wear clothes not for function but to conform to others' expectations.

> [001-043] as three thin ones, and cheap clothing can be obtained at prices reasonable.

His broader argument ties clothing to industrial exploitation, observing how garment production entraps workers in cycles of poverty.

> [001-047] est mode by which men may get clothing. The condition of the operatives is becoming every day more like that of the English.

Even the pond provides Thoreau with metaphors for clothing. In "Where I Lived," he watches morning mist lift from the water as the pond sheds its nightly garments.

> [002-012] it throwing off its nightly clothing of mist, and here and there, by degrees, its soft ripples or its smooth reflecting surface revealed.

Thoreau's personal practice embodies his philosophy: he wears simple, durable clothing and mends his own garments, refusing to participate in the fashion economy. Through winter, thick boots and stout clothing become genuine necessities rather than vanities (010-015), confirming his critique is of excess, not genuine protection.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's view on clothing is radically functional and anti-consumerist. He identifies a deep irrationality in how people prioritize new clothes over inner development. For Thoreau, clothing is a symptom of a broader spiritual sickness — the tendency to value external appearances over internal character.

He draws an analogy between clothing and shelter, arguing both have been perverted from necessities into status symbols. The "civilized" man spends more on his wardrobe than a "savage" spends on his entire dwelling, yet is no warmer or more dignified.

His remedy is deliberate simplicity: wear only what is functionally necessary, repair rather than replace, and redirect the time and money saved toward intellectual and spiritual pursuits. The act of rejecting fashion becomes a form of civil disobedience against the economic system that exploits garment workers.

## Historical Context

In the 1840s, New England was experiencing the early stages of industrial textile production. The mills of Lowell employed thousands of young women in cotton and woolen manufacturing. Thoreau's critique targeted both the exploitative production system and the consumer culture it enabled.
