# Mystery

## Definition

In *Walden*, mystery is not a problem to be solved but a quality to be preserved. It is the dimension of nature that resists explanation, the "pleasing mystery" (009-015) that lingers at the bottom of the pond and at the edge of human understanding.

Thoreau values mystery not as ignorance to be dispelled by knowledge but as a permanent feature of a world that exceeds our capacity to fully comprehend it. Mystery is the sign that nature is larger than our theories about it.

## In Walden

Mystery makes its most memorable appearance in "The Ponds," where Thoreau describes the circular heaps of small stones on Walden's sandy bottom. After considering and dismissing explanations — Indian structures on winter ice? fish nests? — he arrives at a deliberate suspension of inquiry: "These lend a pleasing mystery to the bottom" (009-015).

The adjective "pleasing" is essential: the point is not to solve the mystery but to enjoy it. The pond's depth becomes a reservoir of the unknown, a presence that keeps the observer humble.

> [009-015] Perhaps they are the nests of the chivin. These lend a pleasing mystery to the bottom.

Earlier in the same chapter, Thoreau reflects on the Indian legend of the pond's origin — a hill that sank during a profane pow-wow, its stones rolling down to form the present shore. Having entertained the geological and the mythological explanations side by side, he concludes with a confession that is also a manifesto: "I detect the paver" (009-012).

The statement is ironic, for the "paver" he detects is no supernatural agent but the natural process of frost and wave action. Yet his detection does not dispel the mystery; it relocates it. Knowing how the stones were placed does not exhaust the wonder that they *were* placed, that the shore took this particular form, that the pond exists at all.

> [009-012] I detect the paver. If the name was not derived from that of some English locality — Saffron Walden, for instance — one might suppose that it was called originally Walled-in Pond.

Mystery also attends the encounter with wild creatures. In "Winter Animals," the old hunter's tale of the fox hunt culminates in a scene of revelation that is also a scene of mystery's end: "Then the hunter came forward and stood in their midst, and the mystery was solved" (015-010).

The hounds, who had been pursuing the fox by scent alone, confront its dead body and are "struck dumb with amazement." The resolution of their mystery — what they were chasing — is also a kind of bereavement. Thoreau presents this as a parable: the solving of a mystery, while satisfying to the hunter, silences the hounds' ecstatic cry. Some mysteries are better pursued than resolved.

Elsewhere, Thoreau invokes mystery in a more personal register. He speaks of answering nature "with equal mystery" (001-044), refusing to reduce the world's enigmatic address to a simple reply. The stance is characteristic: Thoreau meets the world's depth with his own, preserving a symmetry of unknowing that is the condition of genuine encounter.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of mystery is a quiet rebellion against the Enlightenment drive to explain everything. He does not reject knowledge — he is, after all, the man who sounded every inch of the pond and mapped its depths — but he insists that knowledge has a limit, and that beyond that limit lies not emptiness but a different kind of richness.

The "pleasing mystery" is pleasing precisely because it reminds us that the world is not our construct. It precedes us, exceeds us, and will outlast us.

For Thoreau, this is not a cause for despair but for a particular kind of joy — the joy of knowing that there is always more to know, that the pond will always have another secret, that the bottom will always lend a pleasing mystery to those who look.
