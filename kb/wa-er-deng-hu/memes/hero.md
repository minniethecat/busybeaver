# Hero

## Definition

The hero, in Thoreau's *Walden*, is not the soldier on the battlefield but the person who lives with deliberate attention. Thoreau strips the concept of its martial connotations and reinvests it with moral and intellectual meaning: the hero is the one who reads Homer in the original Greek, who rises early to bathe in the pond, who refuses to be "employed" by the trivial demands of the village. Heroism is a quality of *attention* — to books, to morning, to the self — rather than of action alone. "Old shoes will serve a hero longer than they have served his valet" (001-042): the hero is defined by what he does not need, not by what he displays.

## In Walden

Thoreau's discussion of the heroic unfolds across the early chapters of *Walden*, interwoven with his arguments about reading, labor, and the conduct of life. In "Economy," he offers the image of the hero's old shoes — worn garments that testify to a life devoted to something other than appearance. "Old shoes will serve a hero longer than they have served his valet" (001-042): the sentence turns on the contrast between the hero, who wears shoes in the service of his own purpose, and the valet, who wears them in the service of another.

In "Reading," Thoreau makes his most sustained case for a new kind of heroism. To read the classics in the original languages is to "in some measure emulate their heroes, and consecrate morning hours to their pages" (003-003). The act of reading Homer or Aeschylus in Greek becomes a heroic discipline — a training of the mind and spirit that parallels the physical training of the ancient warrior. "The heroic books, even if printed in the character of our mother tongue," Thoreau insists, "will always be in a language dead to degenerate times" (003-003). The heroism of the reader lies in the effort to recover that dead language, to make the ancient texts live again through sustained attention.

The morning itself becomes the theater of the heroic. "Morning brings back the heroic ages" (002-021), Thoreau writes, and his daily ritual of bathing in the pond is a "religious exercise" that renews the world each dawn. The heroic is not remote or historical; it is available every morning to anyone willing to rise and meet it.

In "Sounds," Thoreau contrasts the heroic with the merely commercial. The railroad cars pass his house with "planetary motion," and he asks: "If the enterprise were as heroic and commanding as it is protracted and unwearied..." (004-012). Commerce, for all its energy, lacks the heroic because it lacks purpose beyond accumulation. The hero does not merely persist; the hero strives toward something worthy.

> [001-042] A man who has at length found something to do will not need to get a new suit to do it in; for him the old will do, that has lain dusty in the garret for an indeterminate period. Old shoes will serve a hero longer than they have served his valet.

> [003-003] The student may read Homer or Æschylus in the Greek without danger of dissipation or luxuriousness, for it implies that he in some measure emulate their heroes, and consecrate morning hours to their pages.

> [002-021] Morning brings back the heroic ages. I was as much affected by the faint hum of a mosquito making its invisible and unimaginable tour through my apartment at earliest dawn, when I was sitting with door and windows open, as I could be by any trumpet that ever sang of fame.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's heroism is democratic and domestic. It requires no battlefield, no public stage — only a pond, a book, and the discipline to rise early. By relocating heroism from martial glory to intellectual and spiritual practice, Thoreau proposes that every person has access to the heroic life. The heroism he values is the heroism of integrity: doing one's own work, reading one's own books, living one's own life, regardless of what the village expects. "I am less affected by their heroism who stood up for half an hour in the front of the battle of Bunker Hill" (004-014), he writes, redirecting admiration from the famous dead to the anonymous living — the man who goes about his daily business "with more or less courage and content."
