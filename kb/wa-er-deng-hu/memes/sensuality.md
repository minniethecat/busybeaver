# Sensuality

## Definition

In *Walden*, sensuality is Thoreau's term for the animal principle in human nature — the bodily appetites that, though they take many forms (eating, drinking, sleeping, cohabiting), are fundamentally one. "All sensuality is one, though it takes many forms; all purity is one" (011-023).

Sensuality is not a particular act but an underlying disposition, a "sluggish habit of mind" (011-023) that opposes the active, striving, spiritual dimension of human life. It is the reptile within, the force that awakens "in proportion as our higher nature slumbers" (011-014).

## In Walden

Thoreau's treatment of sensuality is concentrated in "Higher Laws," the chapter where he most directly confronts the tension between animal instinct and spiritual aspiration.

He opens the analysis with a confession of internal division: "We are conscious of an animal in us, which awakens in proportion as our higher nature slumbers. It is reptile and sensual, and perhaps cannot be wholly expelled" (011-014).

The animal within is not an external temptation but a permanent resident, as ineradicable as the parasitic worms that "even in life and health, occupy our bodies" (011-014).

> [011-014] We are conscious of an animal in us, which awakens in proportion as our higher nature slumbers. It is reptile and sensual, and perhaps cannot be wholly expelled.

The key insight comes in the declaration that unifies all forms of sensual indulgence: "All sensuality is one, though it takes many forms" (011-023). Whether a person eats too much, drinks too much, sleeps too much, or indulges sexually, "They are but one appetite, and we only need to see a person do any one of these things to know how great a sensualist he is" (011-023).

This is Thoreau at his most radically reductive — and his most psychologically acute. The specific form of indulgence is secondary; what matters is the underlying disposition toward passive consumption rather than active creation.

> [011-023] All sensuality is one, though it takes many forms; all purity is one. It is the same whether a man eat, or drink, or cohabit, or sleep sensually. They are but one appetite.

The remedy is not repression but redirection. "From exertion come wisdom and purity; from sloth ignorance and sensuality" (011-023).

Thoreau's prescription is disarmingly practical: "If you would avoid uncleanness, and all the sins, work earnestly, though it be at cleaning a stable" (011-023).

The antidote to sensuality is not prayer or fasting but exertion — any exertion, even the humblest. Sensuality, in this analysis, is less a moral failing than an energy problem: the same generative force that "when we are loose, dissipates and makes us unclean, when we are continent invigorates and inspires us" (011-014).

Thoreau also sketches the animal principle from a gentler angle in "Visitors," in his portrait of the Canadian woodchopper — a man in whom "the animal man chiefly was developed" (006-020). The woodchopper is no sensualist; his animality expresses itself as exuberance, contentment, and an almost prelapsarian innocence.

Yet his thinking "was so primitive and immersed in his animal life" (006-023) that it could never ripen into full consciousness.

This is the animal principle without the corruption of sensuality — the brute nature unperverted, content with its portion. The contrast sharpens Thoreau's taxonomy: the animal within is not inherently degrading; it becomes sensuality only when the higher nature slumbers and the lower appetite usurps the whole person.

Thoreau is also honest about the difficulty of speaking of sensuality without participating in it: "I hesitate to say these things, but it is not because of the subject — I care not how obscene my words are — but because I cannot speak of them without betraying my impurity" (011-024). This is a remarkable moment of self-awareness.

He observes that "we discourse freely without shame of one form of sensuality, and are silent about another" (011-024) — a critique of Victorian hypocrisy that remains pointed. The body itself bears the marks of its ruling disposition: "Any nobleness begins at once to refine a man's features, any meanness or sensuality to imbrute them" (011-025).

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of sensuality is unusual for its refusal to separate the moral from the physiological. He does not condemn the body — he insists that "every man is the builder of a temple, called his body, to the god he worships" (011-025) — but he demands that the body be built well.

Sensuality is the cheap construction, the temple thrown up without care for the god it will house.

His unified theory of appetite ("all sensuality is one") is both a diagnosis and a promise: if all forms of sensual indulgence stem from a single source, then addressing that source — through exertion, through deliberate living, through the cultivation of purity — can transform the whole person at once.
