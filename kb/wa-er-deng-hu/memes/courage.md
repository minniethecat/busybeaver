# Courage

## Definition

Courage in *Walden* is less the martial virtue celebrated by history than the quiet, persistent resolve to live by one's own lights. Thoreau distinguishes between the courage of "the front line at Buena Vista" and "the steady and cheerful valor of the men who inhabit the snow-plough for their winter quarters" (004-014). True courage is not episodic heroism but the daily discipline of maintaining oneself "in whatever attitude he find himself through obedience to the laws of his being" (018-013).

## In Walden

The word "courage" and its cognates appear sparingly in *Walden*, but the concept pervades the book. In "Economy," Thoreau positions himself as a reluctant exhorter, declining to "prescribe rules to strong and valiant natures" (001-027). Instead he addresses "the mass of men who are discontented" — and the implicit argument is that the courage to change one's life is available to all who will exercise it. The pioneers who first settled Concord modeled a courage Thoreau admires: they burrowed into hillsides and "did not provide them houses till the earth, by the Lord's blessing, brought forth bread" (001-066).

The richest meditation on courage comes in "Sounds," where Thoreau contrasts military heroism with the courage of labor. Commerce, he writes, wins his admiration "not because it clasps its hands and prays to Jupiter" but because its practitioners "go about their business with more or less courage and content" (004-014). The snow-plough men — anonymous, unsung — possess what Bonaparte called "the three-o'-clock in the morning courage," and they possess it not once but every day.

> [004-014] I am less affected by their heroism who stood up for half an hour in the front line at Buena Vista, than by the steady and cheerful valor of the men who inhabit the snow-plough for their winter quarters; who have not merely the three-o'-clock in the morning courage, which Bonaparte thought was the rarest, but whose courage does not go to rest so early, who go to sleep only when the storm sleeps or the sinews of their iron steed are frozen.

The counter-example appears later in the same chapter, where the hooting owl embodies the opposite of courage: "a mind which has reached the gelatinous mildewy stage in the mortification of all healthy and courageous thought" (004-033). Courage, for Thoreau, is a quality of *aliveness* — the owl's moan is the sound of thought that has given up.

In "Conclusion," Thoreau delivers his most pointed redefinition. He recounts that Mirabeau took to highway robbery "to ascertain what degree of resolution was necessary in order to place one's self in formal opposition to the most sacred laws of society" (018-013). Mirabeau's conclusion — "a soldier who fights in the ranks does not require half so much courage as a foot-pad" — is "manly, as the world goes; and yet it was idle, if not desperate." Thoreau's alternative is more radical: true courage is not opposition to society but fidelity to oneself. "It is not for a man to put himself in such an attitude to society, but to maintain himself in whatever attitude he find himself through obedience to the laws of his being" (018-013). Courage is alignment, not defiance.

> [018-013] A saner man would have found himself often enough "in formal opposition" to what are deemed "the most sacred laws of society," through obedience to yet more sacred laws, and so have tested his resolution without going out of his way.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau democratizes courage, relocating it from the battlefield to the workbench, the cabin, and the inward life. The courage he values is steady, not spectacular; it is the courage that keeps a fire lit through the winter, that refuses the collective delusion, that "does not go to rest so early." He is skeptical of courage that seeks opposition for its own sake — Mirabeau's experiment is "idle, if not desperate" — because true courage need not go looking for a fight. It emerges naturally from the attempt to live by "yet more sacred laws" than those of society.
