# Comfort

## Definition

In *Walden*, comfort is a contested term. Thoreau distinguishes between the natural comfort of warmth, shelter, and sufficiency—which he values—and the artificial "comforts" of modern civilization, which he argues are "not only not indispensable, but positive hindrances to the elevation of mankind" (001-025). The concept is central to his economic and spiritual argument: what society calls comfort is often a trap.

## In Walden

Thoreau opens his critique early in "Economy." Surveying the houses and possessions of his neighbors, he concludes that "many of the so called comforts of life, are not only not indispensable, but positive hindrances" (001-025). The "comfortable house" becomes a "rude and hardy race's" burden: "A comfortable house for a rude and hardy race, that lived mostly out of doors, was once made here almost entirely of such materials as Nature furnished" (001-050). The more civilized we become, the more we suffer to maintain our comforts.

> [001-056] He was a man of a new and vigorous breed, who with a hair spring to catch comfort and independence, and then, as it were, a turning-lathe to round off his fortune.

The "hair spring to catch comfort" is for Thoreau an image of the delicacy and fragility of the pursuit. Comfort, sought directly, eludes; it is a by-product of living well, not a goal to be purchased. He mocks the notion that "attaining gross necessaries and comforts merely" (001-061) could be the purpose of a human life, asking "why should he have a better dwelling than the former?" (001-061) if mere comfort is the standard.

> [001-025] Of a life of luxury the fruit is luxury, whether in agriculture, or commerce, or literature, or art.

The logic is circular: luxury begets only the need for more luxury. Thoreau's alternative is what he calls "voluntary poverty" (001-025)—the deliberate renunciation of comfort not as deprivation but as liberation. When a visitor challenges him, "I find it hard to give up so many of the comforts of life," Thoreau answers simply: "I am convinced, both by faith and experience, that to maintain one's self on this earth is not a hardship but a pastime" (005-008).

Yet Thoreau is no ascetic absolutist. He confesses that in winter "it was more comfortable" to have a plastered and shingled wall than his initial open construction (013-006). He observes that "even the wildest animals love comfort and warmth as well as man" (013-028), naturalizing the desire while still warning against its excess. The distinction is between comfort that supports life and comfort that encumbers it.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's position on comfort is more nuanced than simple renunciation. He recognizes three tiers: **necessity** (warmth, food, shelter — all of which he secures with minimal labor), **genuine comfort** (a tight-fitting suit, a well-built chimney, a clear view of the pond — achieved through craftsmanship, not purchase), and **luxury** (the "gross necessaries and comforts" that require a lifetime of labor to maintain and ultimately own their owner).

The key insight is that comfort-seeking is self-defeating beyond a certain threshold. The farmer who works a lifetime to afford a "comfortable" house has spent that lifetime not living in it. Thoreau's cabin, by contrast, cost $28.12½ and freed him to spend his days walking, reading, and observing.

## Historical Context

Thoreau wrote during the early stages of American consumer culture. The Industrial Revolution was producing an unprecedented flood of manufactured goods marketed as "comforts." The middle-class home was becoming filled with objects—carpets, curtains, tableware, decorations—that earlier generations had done without. Thoreau's *Walden* is among the earliest and most sustained critiques of consumerism in American literature, anticipating by over a century the "voluntary simplicity" movement.
