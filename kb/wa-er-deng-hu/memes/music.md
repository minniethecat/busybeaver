# Music

## Definition

In Walden, music extends far beyond human performance to encompass the entire soundscape of nature. Thoreau hears music in the wind through the pines, the hooting of owls, the rumble of the Fitchburg Railroad, the church bells across the bay, and most profoundly in the silence of the pond itself. For Thoreau, natural sound is not merely pleasant — it is a form of spiritual communication that cultivated music can only approximate. The wild, untutored sounds of the earth are, he suggests, closer to truth than the most refined symphony.

## In Walden

Thoreau devotes the chapter "Sounds" to a sustained meditation on the auditory dimension of his life at the pond. He describes the experience of sitting in his doorway on a summer morning, "rapt in a revery," listening to the layered sounds of birds, wind, and distant human activity.

> [004-031] the night, and were again as musical as ever just before and about dawn. The note of the cock was the most remarkable of any of these sounds.

The hooting of owls becomes, in Thoreau's rendering, a mournful and philosophically suggestive music:

> [004-032] de; reminding me sometimes of music and singing birds; as if it were the dark and tearful side of music, the regrets and sighs that would fain be sung.

Even the railroad's whistle, heard across the pond, takes on musical qualities when received with the right attention. Thoreau refuses the conventional distinction between "music" and "noise":

> [004-030] ed into the cheap and natural music of the cow. I do not mean to be satirical, but to express my appreciation of those youthful drivers.

The wind through the telegraph wire — an unintended Æolian harp — becomes a metaphor for nature's invisible music:

> [005-004] uch a storm but it was Æolian music to a healthy and innocent ear. There is nothing so charming as the note of the flute heard at night in the open air.

Thoreau's own flute-playing at the pond participates in this natural orchestra. His music does not compete with the birds and wind but answers them, joining a conversation that predates and will outlast him.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's musical philosophy is radically democratic — the best music is free, ubiquitous, and requires no special training to appreciate. He is skeptical of cultivated music, which he sees as another expensive substitute for what nature already provides abundantly. Why pay for a concert when the wind and the birds perform daily without charge?

This does not mean Thoreau rejects human music. He owns a flute and plays it at the pond. But his playing is integrated into the natural soundscape, not set apart from it. The ideal music, for Thoreau, is dialogical — the human voice or flute answering the owl, the wind, the pond's lapping waves. "We will consider what kind of music they are like," he writes of the sounds around him (002-029).

Behind this aesthetic lies a spiritual conviction: the universe is fundamentally harmonious, and all genuine sound participates in that harmony. Disharmony — the "noise" of the factory, the clamor of commerce — is a symptom of spiritual disorder. To restore one's hearing to natural music is to restore one's soul to its proper attunement.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's musical philosophy drew on several currents in early nineteenth-century thought. The Romantic poets (particularly Wordsworth and Coleridge) had celebrated the music of nature as a source of spiritual insight. In New England, the Transcendentalists saw art as a form of divine communication — Emerson's essay "Art" paralleled Thoreau's musical reflections. The Æolian harp, a stringed instrument played by the wind, was a popular Romantic symbol for the receptive soul, appearing in poetry by Coleridge, Shelley, and Emerson. Thoreau's innovation was to hear the Æolian principle everywhere — in telegraph wires, pine needles, and the lapping of water — democratizing the sublime.
