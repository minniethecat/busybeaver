# Faith

## Definition

Faith in *Walden* operates on two levels: the religious faith that Thoreau inherited from his Puritan New England culture and critiqued, and the existential faith — trust in one's own experience and in the fundamental order of the universe — that he sought to cultivate. Thoreau is "determined not to live by faith if we can avoid it" (001-021), preferring the solid ground of direct experience, yet he repeatedly acknowledges that some residue of faith is inescapable in any life worth living.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most direct statement on faith comes early: "I for one am determined not to live by faith if we can avoid it; all the day long I am on the alert to get some evidence to support my faith" (001-021). The sentence is characteristically dual — a rejection of blind faith paired with an acknowledgment that evidence *supports* faith, that faith is not abolished by empiricism but refined by it. Thoreau is not an atheist but an empiricist of the spirit, demanding that belief earn its keep.

> [001-021] I have not been able to find in my experience any solid ground on which to stand, except the point of a needle. I for one am determined not to live by faith if we can avoid it; all the day long I am on the alert to get some evidence to support my faith.

The bean-field chapter contains Thoreau's most personal confession of faith: "I said to myself, I will not plant beans with another to pick them, nor waste my time in dressing in which I had entire faith" (007-005). Here faith becomes operational — faith is what you plant, what you dress yourself in each morning, the commitments you make with your labor. Faith is not a proposition to be believed but a life to be lived.

Thoreau's treatment of the "Hermit" (a figure he partly identifies with) suggests that "the man of the most faith of any alive" (014-021) is not the most doctrinaire but the most attentive — the one who trusts the world enough to live in it without intermediaries. "His words and actions have to stand for those of all men" (014-021): faith makes one representative, universal.

> [014-021] He must be the man of the most faith of any alive. His words and actions have to stand for those of all men.

The chapter on higher laws invokes faith in a more traditionally religious register: "He who has true faith in the Omnipresent Supreme Being" (011-011) — a rare explicit reference to deity in Thoreau's otherwise-naturalistic vocabulary. But even here, faith is quickly connected to conduct: "as he grows more resolute and faithful, his road lies" (011-010). Faith opens a path; unfaith closes it.

In the conclusion, Thoreau writes that "the words which express our faith and piety are not definite" (018-016) — faith resists precise articulation. It is felt rather than formulated. And his final image of faith is startlingly physical: "Who does not feel his faith in a resurrection and immortality strengthened by hearing the notes of the wood-thrush?" (018-028). Faith comes not from scripture but from birdsong — nature itself as the primary revelation.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's faith is Emersonian self-reliance applied to religion. He inherits the vocabulary of New England Protestantism — "faith," "piety," "resurrection," "God" — but empties these terms of their institutional content and refills them with direct experience. Faith is not membership in a church but "entire faith" (007-005) in one's own chosen way of living.

He is particularly skeptical of those "who spoke of God as if they enjoyed a monopoly of the subject" (006-028). Institutional religion, for Thoreau, is faith that has stopped being faith and become property — something owned, controlled, and doled out. True faith cannot be monopolized because it cannot be possessed; it is always a relation, never an asset.

Yet Thoreau is not a simple skeptic. His acknowledgment that "we may have some faith left which belies that experience" (001-016) suggests that faith exceeds what we can verify — that there is something in us that trusts beyond the evidence. This is not a weakness to be overcome but a feature of the human condition that Thoreau, for all his empiricism, respects.
