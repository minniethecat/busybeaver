# Genius

In *Walden*, genius represents the highest reach of human intellect and creative spirit. Thoreau discusses genius primarily in "Reading," where he contrasts the works of genius with the mediocrity of popular culture.

## Definition

Genius for Thoreau is the rare capacity for original insight that transcends ordinary understanding:

> The works of the great poets have never yet been read by mankind, for only great poets can read them. (003-006)

## In Walden

Thoreau emphasizes that reading works of genius requires effort equal to their creation:

> ...to read the works of genius written in those languages... (003-003)

He laments that most people never engage with true intellectual greatness:

> ...the best books are not read even by those who are called good readers. (003-005)

Genius is listed among the highest human attainments:

> ...genius—learning—wit—books—paintings—statuary—music—philosophical instruments, and the like... (003-012)

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau urges readers to trust their own inner genius rather than conform to external expectations:

> Follow your genius closely enough, and it will not fail to show you a fresh prospect every hour. (004-003)
