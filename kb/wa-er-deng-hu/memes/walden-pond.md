# Walden Pond

The central geographic feature of Thoreau's *Walden* — a deep, clear glacial kettle pond in Concord, Massachusetts, where Thoreau lived from 1845 to 1847.

## Location and Geography

Walden Pond is located about a mile and a half south of Concord village, in Middlesex County, Massachusetts. It is a kettle pond formed by retreating glaciers approximately 12,000 years ago. The pond covers about 61 acres with a maximum depth of approximately 102 feet. Thoreau carefully surveyed the pond himself, recording its dimensions with characteristic precision.

## In Walden

Walden Pond is both the literal setting and the symbolic center of Thoreau's book. He describes its water, its colors, its seasonal changes, its fish and wildlife, and its place in the landscape with a naturalist's precision and a poet's reverence. The pond serves as Thoreau's metaphor for depth, clarity, and self-knowledge throughout the work.

> [009-008] Successive nations perchance have drank at, admired, and fathomed it, and passed away, and still its water is green and pellucid as ever. Not an intermitting spring! Perhaps on that spring morning when Adam and Eve were driven out of Eden Walden Pond was already in existence, and even then breaking up in a gentle spring rain accompanied with mist and a southerly wind... It is a gem of the first water which Concord wears in her coronet.

> [009-044] White Pond and Walden are great crystals on the surface of the earth, Lakes of Light. If they were permanently congealed, and small enough to be clutched, they would, perchance, be carried off by slaves, like precious stones, to adorn the heads of emperors; but being liquid, and ample, and secured to us and our successors forever, we disregard them, and run after the diamond of Kohinoor.

## Ecological Features

Thoreau records the pond's fish species (pickerel, perch, pouts, breams, eels), its turtles and frogs, the ducks and geese that visit, and the loon that appears on its surface each year. The pond's remarkable clarity—"its water is green and pellucid as ever"—and its mysterious fluctuations in water level are subjects of extended observation in the chapter "The Ponds."

> [009-014] The shiners, pouts, and perch also, and indeed all the fishes which inhabit this pond, are much cleaner, handsomer, and firmer fleshed than those in the river and most other ponds, as the water is purer.
