# The Ice Harvest

The annual winter harvest of ice from Walden Pond, described by Thoreau in the House-Warming and The Pond in Winter chapters as a metaphor for the commercialization of nature.

## In Walden

During the winters of 1846 and 1847, Thoreau watched a crew of Irish laborers cut and harvest blocks of ice from the frozen surface of Walden Pond. The ice was shipped to warmer climates—Thoreau imagines it reaching India. He uses the ice harvest to meditate on the relationship between nature and commerce, purity and commodification.

> [016-019] Thus it appears that the sweltering inhabitants of Charleston and New Orleans, of Madras and Bombay and Calcutta, drink at my well. In the morning I bathe my intellect in the stupendous and cosmogonal philosophy of the Bhagvat Geeta, since whose composition years of the gods have elapsed, and in comparison with which our modern world and its literature seem puny and trivial.

> [016-020] The ice in the pond at length begins to be honeycombed, and I can set my heel in it as I walk. The water, which had been still and dark, now becomes translucent, and a thousand bubbles rise.

## The Ice Harvesters

Thoreau describes a crew of one hundred Irish laborers who come to harvest the ice. Their work transforms the pond into a scene of industry, complete with sleds, horses, and specialized tools. Thoreau notes the irony that Walden's pure water would soon be mingled with the Ganges, connecting Concord to the wider world through commerce.

## Significance

The ice harvest is Thoreau's most sustained treatment of the intersection between nature and commerce. He does not condemn the ice harvesters—indeed, he finds their work fascinating and purchases their leftover ice for his own use—but he uses the event to illustrate his larger argument about how human industry transforms the natural world without truly understanding or appreciating it.
