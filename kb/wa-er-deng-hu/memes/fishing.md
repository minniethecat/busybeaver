# Fishing

## Definition

In *Walden*, fishing occupies an ambiguous middle ground between the primal hunting instinct and the contemplative life. Thoreau presents it as the most defensible form of animal pursuit—less violent than hunting, yet still rooted in the ancient human relationship with wild food. Fishing is, for him, a thread connecting the solitary philosopher to the village economy and to deeper reflections on time and eternity.

## In Walden

Fishing appears throughout the Walden narrative as a regular activity. Thoreau describes his "patient companion who had been fishing on the pond since morning, as silent as a mute" (009-002), and his own practice of spending "the hours of midnight fishing from a boat by moonlight" (009-004). The pond yields pouts, perch, and pickerel, and Thoreau describes catching "pouts with a bunch of worms" dropped into the water (009-003).

Fishing is also how Thoreau connects with visitors and neighbors. The two young men "who had been fishing in the pond" (008-002) represent the village's use of Walden as a resource. Thoreau's own companions—"sometimes I had a companion in my fishing, who came through the village to my house" (012-001)—bridge his solitude and the social world.

> [002-030] Time is but the stream I go a-fishing in. I drink at it; but while I drink I see the sandy bottom and detect how shallow it is.

This celebrated passage uses fishing as the governing metaphor for Thoreau's relationship with time itself. Time is not a river carrying him away but a stream he fishes in—active, wading, choosing where to cast. The "sandy bottom" he sees suggests that his philosophical probing has revealed time's shallowness: it is not the inexorable, deep current others fear.

> [011-001] The ease and adventure that are in fishing still recommended it to me.

Thoreau acknowledges the pull of fishing even as his philosophy moves him away from animal pursuits. He "had long felt differently about fishing" (011-003), recognizing that "the solitary amusements of hunting fishing and the like have not yet given place to higher pursuits" (011-002). Yet he never entirely renounces it. The question—"Shall I go to heaven or a-fishing?" (012-005)—captures the unresolved tension between transcendence and earthly pleasure.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's view of fishing evolves across *Walden* from acceptance to qualified critique. In the early chapters, fishing is a natural, even noble way of procuring food. By the "Higher Laws" chapter (011), he begins to question whether any killing is compatible with spiritual aspiration. He proposes that "every boy" should pass through a hunting and fishing phase, but that maturity means outgrowing it: "No humane being, past the thoughtless age of boyhood, will wantonly murder any creature which holds its life by the same tenure that he does" (011-006).

Yet Thoreau is no dogmatist. His own practice of occasional fishing—even in winter, when "men come with fishing reels and slender lunch" to the frozen pond (016-003)—persists. Fishing is the last animal pursuit he gives up, and he gives it up gradually, without self-righteousness.

## Historical Context

In 19th-century New England, fishing was both a subsistence practice and a growing recreational pastime. The transcendentalist circle, including Emerson and Bronson Alcott, tended toward vegetarianism and animal-rights sentiment—Alcott's Fruitlands experiment was strictly vegan. Thoreau's nuanced position—acknowledging the value of fishing while recognizing its moral limitation—reflects his independence from even his closest intellectual companions.
