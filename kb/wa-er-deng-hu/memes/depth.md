# Depth

## Definition

In *Walden*, *depth* operates simultaneously as physical fact and philosophical metaphor. Thoreau's painstaking measurement of Walden Pond's depths — sounding it with a cod-line and anchor — is one of the book's most sustained acts of empirical attention, yet it opens continuously onto the invisible: the depth of a pond becomes "the beholder's measure of the depth of his own nature" (009-017). Depth is what resists the surface glance, what must be sought with patience and precision, and what reveals itself only to the one who is willing to drop a line into the unknown.

## In Walden

Thoreau devotes a remarkable portion of "The Ponds" to the question of depth. Having heard rumors that Walden was bottomless, he sets out to disprove them with method: "I could easily discern, with a cod-line, the depth of the pond, which is so remarkable for its depth and purity as to merit a particular description" (009-005). He finds the greatest depth to be one hundred and two feet — a number that fascinates him because it is finite yet profound, measurable yet deeply resistant to casual perception.

The act of sounding becomes, for Thoreau, a figure for self-knowledge. "A lake is the landscape's most beautiful and expressive feature," he writes. "It is earth's eye; looking into which the beholder measures the depth of his own nature. The fluviatile trees next the shore are the slender eyelashes which fringe it" (009-017). The pond does not merely have depth; it bestows the concept of depth upon the one who contemplates it, turning outward observation into inward inquiry.

Thoreau extends the metaphor to human character. In a memorable passage he suggests that "the height or depth of his character" (016-018) may be plotted like the contours of a pond bottom — a moral bathymetry in which what is deepest is also what is truest. He also notes the practical consequence of Walden's greater depth in spring: the ice melts later there than on neighboring ponds, "on account both of its greater depth and its having no stream passing through it" (017-001), making depth a property that resists the haste of seasonal change.

> [009-005] As I was desirous to recover the long-lost bottom of Walden Pond, I surveyed it carefully, before the ice broke up, early in '46, with compass and chain and sounding-line. There have been many stories told about the bottom, or rather no bottom, of this pond, which certainly had no foundation for themselves. It is remarkable how long men will believe in the bottomlessness of a pond without taking the trouble to sound it.

> [009-017] A lake is the landscape's most beautiful and expressive feature. It is earth's eye; looking into which the beholder measures the depth of his own nature. The fluviatile trees next the shore are the slender eyelashes which fringe it, and the wooded hills and cliffs around are its overhanging brows.

> [016-018] What I have observed of the pond is no less true in ethics. It is the law of average. Such a rule of the two diameters not only guides us toward the sun in the system and the heart in man, but draws lines through the length and breadth of the aggregate of a man's particular daily behaviors and waves of life into his coves and inlets, and where they intersect will be the height or depth of his character.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Walden's depth, for Thoreau, is the antidote to a culture of surfaces. The world of buying and selling, of inherited farms and unexamined customs, is a world that never sounds its own depths. To measure the pond is to perform an act of attention that the marketplace never demands. Thoreau's precision — noting that the depth "did not vary more than one foot in a hundred feet" (016-013) along an arbitrary line — is itself a philosophical stance: the truth is in the details, and the details reward the patient hand.

## Historical Context

The nineteenth century was an age of great hydrographic surveys — the charting of ocean depths, the mapping of lakes and rivers. Thoreau's private survey of Walden Pond participates in this scientific spirit but transforms it: his sounding line is also a philosophical instrument. The "factory owner" who hears of Thoreau's measurement and "thought that it could not be true" (016-008) represents the skepticism of practical men toward knowledge that serves no commercial end — a skepticism Thoreau answers not with argument but with the quiet authority of measured fact.
