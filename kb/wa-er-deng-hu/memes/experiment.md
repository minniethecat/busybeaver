# Experiment

## Definition

The concept of experiment in *Walden* is the organizing principle of Thoreau's entire project. His two years at the pond were not a retreat but a controlled trial: a deliberate reduction of life to its essentials to determine what is actually necessary. "Here is life, an experiment to a great extent untried by me" (001-016), he writes, framing his undertaking not as a prescription for others but as a personal empirical inquiry. The experiment is at once scientific in method and spiritual in aim: measure the minimum, discover the essential.

## In Walden

Thoreau announces his experiment explicitly in the opening chapter. "But to make haste to my own experiment" (001-068), he writes, impatient with preliminary justifications. The experiment is narrative as well as philosophical: the book *Walden* is the record of the trial, the data set, the proof that a person can live on very little and gain very much. His bean-field is an experiment in the most literal sense — he plants, tends, harvests, and calculates profit and loss, discovering that the true yield is not in bushels but in "tropes and figures" (007-012).

The experiment extends to shelter, clothing, food, and fuel. "The present was my next experiment of this kind" (002-009), he writes of building his house, and the progression is methodical: shelter first, then warmth, then sustenance. Each element of life is tested for necessity, and much is found wanting. "I have learned this, at least, by my experiment," he concludes, "that if one advances confidently in the direction of his dreams, and endeavors to live the life which he has imagined, he will meet with a success unexpected in common hours" (018-015). The experiment, in other words, succeeded — not in proving a thesis but in demonstrating a possibility.

The empirical stance also gives Thoreau critical distance from the world he has temporarily left. He reports "chiefly the expensive experiments of gentlemen farmers" (007-012) with the irony of one whose own experiment cost twenty-eight dollars and twelve and a half cents. The gentleman farmers experiment with new methods of extraction; Thoreau experiments with a new method of living, and his results are more interesting. In winter, he notes that "in the experiment at Cambridge to freeze water under glass" (017-001) produced results inferior to nature's own ice-making at Walden Pond — another instance where the natural experiment surpasses the artificial.

Thoreau concludes his experiment with the observation that "youthful philosophers and experimentalists we are" (018-026), including himself in a category that is both humble and ambitious. The experimenter is always a beginner, always trying something untested, and Thoreau embraces this provisional identity. The experiment at Walden was not meant to be permanent; it was meant to demonstrate what was possible, and having done so, he moved on.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's notion of experiment is a deliberate fusion of Enlightenment empiricism and transcendentalist intuition. He weighs, measures, and calculates — but he also listens, watches, and receives. The experiment is both quantitative (how many dollars? how many bushels?) and qualitative (what does it feel like to wake before dawn and bathe in the pond?). This dual method is uniquely Thoreauvian: he insists that the most important data are the ones that cannot be tabulated.

The experiment also has a moral dimension. "We are the subjects of an experiment which is not a little interesting to me" (005-013), Thoreau writes, dissolving the boundary between experimenter and subject. To live is to be experimented upon — by nature, by society, by the passage of time. The only question is whether one will be a passive subject or an active participant in the experiment. Thoreau's choice is clear: he will design his own trial, set his own conditions, and record his own results.
