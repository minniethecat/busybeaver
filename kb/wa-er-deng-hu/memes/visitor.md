# Visitor

## Definition

The visitor in *Walden* is a philosophical figure — not merely a person who stops by but a test of Thoreau's experiment and a mirror of his values. Thoreau did not go to the woods to escape human company; "I had more visitors while I lived in the woods than at any other period of my life" (006-011). But the visitors who came to Walden came on different terms than those who might call at a Concord parlor. The distance they traveled, the simplicity of the accommodations, and the absence of the usual social props — tea service, comfortable chairs, a door that could close out the weather — all functioned as a filter, admitting some and discouraging others. The visitor, in this economy, is a natural experiment in human connection.

## In Walden

Thoreau's chapter "Visitors" is a taxonomy of the people who made their way to his cabin. At one extreme is the "most wise and humorous friend, whom I love much" (005-019) — the ideal visitor whose presence deepens solitude rather than disrupting it. At the other are the "half-witted men from the almshouse" (006-024) who come not to converse but to be fed, and whose needs Thoreau meets with a mixture of charity and irritation. Between these poles falls a range of humanity: the Canadian woodchopper, a "true Homeric or Paphlagonian man" (006-015) whose simplicity Thoreau admires; the "self-styled reformers" who come to lecture Thoreau on his duties; the "young men, who had ceased to be young" (006-025), already defeated by lives they had not chosen.

Thoreau's visitor protocol is revealing. He has "three chairs in my house; one for solitude, two for friendship, three for society" (006-002). When more than three visitors arrive, they must stand — and typically they do not stay long. The chair count is a deliberate constraint, a way of keeping social life within bounds. "When visitors came in larger and unexpected numbers there was but the third chair for them all, but they generally economized the room by standing up" (006-002). Thoreau's hospitality is real but bounded; he will share his space but not surrender it.

The visitor also serves as a mirror. "I require of a visitor that he be not actually starving, though he may have the very best appetite in the world" (006-025) — the requirement is spiritual as much as physical. A visitor should come already nourished, bringing something of his own rather than seeking to extract from the host. This is Thoreauvian hospitality inverted: the host does not fill the guest but expects the guest to arrive already full.

One visitor proposes "a book in which visitors should write their names, as at the White Mountains" (006-027) — a guestbook, the apparatus of conventional hospitality. Thoreau's amused refusal is characteristic: Walden is not a tourist destination, and the visitor who thinks it is has misunderstood what kind of place this is.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of visitors reveals the tension at the heart of his solitude. He has removed himself from society, yet society keeps arriving — in the form of the woodchopper, the reformer, the runaway slave, the curious neighbor. His response to each is a lesson in the art of bounded connection: how to be present to others without being absorbed by them, how to offer hospitality without sacrificing solitude, how to receive the visitor as a gift rather than an intrusion. The visitor, in Thoreau's hands, becomes the occasion for practicing exactly the kind of deliberate relationship that mass society renders impossible.
