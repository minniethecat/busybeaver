# Habit / Custom

## Definition

Habit in *Walden* is nearly always a term of critique. Thoreau uses it to name the unexamined, automatic patterns through which people conduct their lives — patterns inherited from society, reinforced by custom, and rarely subjected to conscious scrutiny. Habit is the mechanism by which "the mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation" (001-015): not because they choose desperation but because habit has made any other way of living unimaginable. To awaken from habit — to live deliberately — is Thoreau's fundamental project.

## In Walden

Thoreau introduces the problem of habit early in "Economy," where he diagnoses the "thousand remarkable ways" in which his Concord neighbors perform unconscious penance, driven by customs they have never questioned. "It is never too late to give up our prejudices," he insists. "No way of thinking or doing, however ancient, can be trusted without proof" (001-016). This is a direct assault on habit as an epistemological principle: the fact that something has always been done is, for Thoreau, precisely no reason to continue doing it.

The economic dimension of habit appears in Thoreau's analysis of clothing, shelter, and food. People wear what fashion dictates, build houses larger than they need, and eat what custom prescribes — not because these choices serve life but because "the customs of the day" (003-003) have usurped the place of individual judgment. "Comparatively, tattooing is not the hideous custom which it is called" (001-046), Thoreau observes with characteristic inversion: what one culture calls barbaric, another calls convention, and neither has examined its own premises.

In "Reading," Thoreau extends the critique to intellectual habits: "To read well, that is, to read true books in a true spirit, is a noble exercise, and one that will task the reader more than any exercise which the customs of the day esteem" (003-003). The customs of the day esteem light reading, news, and entertainment; genuine reading — the kind that transforms — requires breaking the habit of passive consumption.

The deepest treatment of habit appears in "Higher Laws," where Thoreau confesses his own habitual pull toward "a primitive rank and savage one" (011-001) — an instinctual life that resists the discipline of spiritual aspiration. But here habit is not merely social convention but something more fundamental: the inertia of the body itself, its tendency toward comfort and repetition. "This was habit" (011-003), Thoreau writes of his fishing impulse, acknowledging that even the alert mind contends with its own ingrained patterns.

The remedy for habit is never simple willpower but the cultivation of conscious attention. In "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," Thoreau articulates the counter-principle: "I wanted to live deep and suck out all the marrow of life" (002-023). This deliberate living — the antithesis of habit — requires constant vigilance, a daily renewal of intention. The seasonal cycle of *Walden* itself models this: each season demands a new way of being, a shedding of the previous season's habits.

In the "Conclusion," Thoreau returns to the theme with his famous injunction: "If you have built castles in the air, your work need not be lost; that is where they should be. Now put the foundations under them" (018-013). The habit of dreaming without building, of aspiring without acting, is perhaps the most insidious habit of all — and the one Thoreau's entire experiment was designed to break.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's critique of habit is radical because it refuses to distinguish between "good" and "bad" habits at the level of form. The problem with habit is not its content but its structure: any pattern of behavior adopted without conscious examination is, for Thoreau, a form of slavery — even if the behavior itself appears virtuous. This is why he insists that "old deeds for old people, and new deeds for new" (001-016). Each generation, each person, must discover their own way of living; the transmission of custom across generations is not wisdom but a failure of nerve. Thoreau's own "strict business habits" (001-039) — the disciplined routine of his life at Walden — are not a contradiction of this principle but its embodiment: habits consciously chosen and continually re-examined are not habits at all, but practices.
