# Senses

## Definition

The senses in *Walden* are the instruments of Thoreau's radical empiricism. He insists on the body's direct apprehension of the world — "when the whole body is one sense, and imbibes delight through every pore" (005-001) — as the foundation of all higher knowledge. The senses are not obstacles to spiritual life but its necessary medium. To have one's "senses still" is to be alive; to lose them is to die inwardly long before the body fails.

## In Walden

Thoreau's philosophy of the senses begins in "Economy" with a diagnosis of sensory loss. "Our lives are domestic in more senses than we think," he writes, tracing the human journey from the cave to the shingled house (001-049). Shelter, which began as protection, has become separation — "from the hearth to the field is a great distance." The roof that shields us from the elements also severs us from the stars. "It would be well perhaps if we were to spend more of our days and nights without any obstruction between us and the celestial bodies" (001-049).

> [001-049] At last, we know not what it is to live in the open air, and our lives are domestic in more senses than we think. From the hearth to the field is a great distance.

In "Solitude," the argument reaches its apex. "There can be no very black melancholy to him who lives in the midst of Nature and has his senses still" (005-004). The senses, when kept open and responsive, are a guarantor of sanity. Thoreau's famous claim that he "never felt lonesome" rests on this sensory saturation — the pine needles that "expanded and swelled with sympathy," the pattering rain that became "sweet and beneficent society."

The senses are also the gateway to awakening. "The place where that may occur is always the same, and indescribably pleasant to all our senses" (005-009). Awakening — Thoreau's master metaphor — is not an escape from the body but a heightening of its capacities. The senses, fully alive, become indistinguishable from spiritual receptivity.

In "Higher Laws," Thoreau confronts the dark side of sensory experience. "A command over our passions, and over the external senses of the body, and good acts, are declared by the Ved to be indispensable in the mind's approximation to God" (011-014). The senses must be governed, not indulged. The animal in us awakens "in proportion as our higher nature slumbers" (011-014), and the discipline of purity is precisely the discipline of the senses — channeling their energy upward rather than dissipating it.

> [011-014] Yet the spirit can for the time pervade and control every member and function of the body, and transmute what in form is the grossest sensuality into purity and devotion.

In "Former Inhabitants," Thoreau offers a comic parable of sensory self-deception, rushing to a fire with his neighbors while "rejecting the evidence of our senses, until at a turn in the road we heard the crackling and actually felt the heat" (014-007). Idealism, he wryly notes, can become a refusal to see what is plainly burning.

The final word comes in "Conclusion," where Thoreau mocks the demand for a single, dull interpretation: "the verses of Kabir have four different senses; illusion, spirit, intellect, and the exoteric doctrine of the Vedas" (018-017). The multiplicity of senses — both meanings and perceptions — is a richness, not a defect. "While England endeavors to cure the potato-rot, will not any endeavor to cure the brain-rot, which prevails so much more widely and fatally?" (018-017).

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, the senses are the foundation of all real knowledge, but they must be disciplined, not indulged. The ideal is not ascetic denial — the owl's "gelatinous mildewy" moan of a mind that has severed itself from the body — but the "whole body" that "is one sense," fully alive and fully governed. The senses, kept still in Nature, become the instruments of spiritual perception. To lose them is to lose the world; to govern them is to enter it more deeply.
