# Hunting

## Definition

Hunting in *Walden* is not merely a recreational activity or a means of procuring food; it is, for Thoreau, a developmental stage in the individual's and the species' progression toward higher consciousness. Hunting represents humanity's primitive heritage — a genuine, instinctual engagement with wildness — but Thoreau insists that true maturity requires transcending it in favor of more refined pursuits such as poetry, natural science, and spiritual contemplation.

## In Walden

Thoreau opens "Higher Laws" (011) with a candid confession of his own hunting impulse, framing it as a vestigial instinct shared by all who live close to nature:

> [011-001] As I came home through the woods with my string of fish, trailing my pole, it being now quite dark, I caught a glimpse of a woodchuck stealing across my path, and felt a strange thrill of savage delight, and was strongly tempted to seize and devour him raw; not that I was hungry then, except for that wildness which he represented.

He acknowledges that hunting and fishing are among the "more primitive but solitary amusements" that persist in American life, noting that these activities have not yet been displaced by organized games and public holidays as they have in England (011-002). Yet Thoreau is clear that hunting is, at best, an introduction to the forest — a gateway to deeper engagement that must eventually be left behind:

> [011-007] He goes thither at first as a hunter and fisher, until at last, if he has the seeds of a better life in him, he distinguishes his proper objects, as a poet or naturalist it may be, and leaves the gun and fish-pole behind.

Thoreau's neighbor, described in "Visitors" (006), embodies the hunter's simple, unreflective relationship with nature. This man gathers woodchucks and bark with innocent pragmatism, but Thoreau observes that "the intellectual and what is called spiritual man in him were slumbering" (006-020). The hunter represents a wholesome beginning, not an endpoint.

Thoreau's most succinct formulation of hunting's proper role appears in his answer to parents who ask whether they should let their sons hunt: "yes,—remembering that it was one of the best parts of my education,—make them hunters, though only as sportsmen" (011-003). He invokes the medieval maxim approvingly while also insisting on its limits:

> [011-005] That saith that hunters ben not holy men.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's perspective on hunting is developmental and evolutionary. He sees the hunting instinct as authentic and valuable — far preferable to the artificiality of urban amusements — but fundamentally incomplete. The truly cultivated person passes through the hunting stage and emerges as a "poet or naturalist," one who observes and celebrates nature without needing to kill it. This progression mirrors Thoreau's larger philosophy: we must honor our animal nature while striving toward a higher, spiritual existence. He is neither a sentimental opponent of hunting nor an uncritical advocate; he recognizes its educative power while insisting that education must have a graduation.
