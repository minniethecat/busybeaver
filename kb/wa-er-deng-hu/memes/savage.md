# Savage / The Savage State

## Definition

The "savage" in Thoreau's *Walden* is less an ethnographic category than a philosophical device: the primitive state of humankind serves as a benchmark for what is truly necessary. By comparing civilized life with the savage condition, Thoreau exposes the artificial wants that accumulate under civilization and recovers a vision of life stripped to its essentials. The savage is not an ideal to return to but a mirror in which civilization sees its own superfluities.

## In Walden

Thoreau invokes the savage throughout *Walden*, most densely in the opening chapter "Economy," where he builds the case for simplicity. He observes that "in the savage state every family owns a shelter as good as the best" (001-051), drawing the uncomfortable conclusion that civilized housing, for all its expense, may represent no real advance. The comparison cuts deeper: "the civilized man" is merely "a more experienced and wiser savage" (001-068), not a different order of being.

The distinction between savage and civilized is often, in Thoreau's telling, a distinction of ornament rather than substance. He notes how "the childish and savage taste of men and women for new patterns keeps how many shaking and squinting through kaleidoscopes" (001-046), locating in consumer desire a primitive instinct that civilization has not overcome but merely dressed in finer cloth. Tattooing, he argues, is "not the hideous custom which it is called" when compared with the fashions of Boston (001-046).

A pivotal passage in "Higher Laws" records Thoreau's own brush with savage instinct: while fishing, he "felt a strange thrill of savage delight" and "was strongly tempted to seize and devour a woodchuck raw" (011-001). The confession is honest rather than self-congratulatory. He recognizes that the "savage" is not an external other but a live possibility within himself — "whose instincts are more boundless even than those of a savage" (011-002). The spiritual life, for Thoreau, is not a denial of savage impulse but its deliberate transcendence.

In his own bean-field, Thoreau finds the land itself speaking of gradations between wild and cultivated, "some half-civilized, and others savage or barbarous" (007-004). The "savage swamp" into which the sun shines (004-034) and "forlorn and savage places" where the dandelion appears (010-018) extend the category into the landscape itself — Walden's wildness is a standing rebuke to the overly domesticated.

## Perspective

Thoreau's use of "savage" is strategically ambiguous. When he writes that his neighbors' pursuits "are no worthier than the savage's, if he is employed the greater part of his life in obtaining gross necessaries" (001-061), the comparison cuts both ways: it elevates the savage to parity with the civilized while simultaneously downgrading the pretensions of civilization. The savage provides what the Concord farmer has lost — a direct relationship between labor and need, uncluttered by the "thousand savage comforts" (001-056) that civilization mistakes for progress.

## Historical Context

Thoreau wrote in the shadow of the "noble savage" tradition running from Montaigne through Rousseau, but also in an America actively displacing Native peoples. His references to "the Indian" as a figure of practical wisdom (001-051, 001-068) coexist uneasily with the language of "savageness" — a tension characteristic of Transcendentalist thought, which sought universal spiritual truth in particular cultural forms it did not always fully respect.
