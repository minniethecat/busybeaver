# Zilpha

A woman of color who lived near Walden Pond before Thoreau's time, briefly recorded in the Former Inhabitants chapter as one of the land's previous dwellers.

## Connection to Walden

Zilpha is one of the former inhabitants Thoreau lists in his survey of those who lived on the land around Walden Pond before him. Her brief mention—a single sentence—is nonetheless significant as one of the few records of a woman of color in antebellum Concord's rural margins.

> [014-003] Down the road, on the right hand, a field, still nearer to town, Zilpha, a colored woman, had her little house, where she spun linen for the townsfolk, and made the Walden Woods ring with her singing, for she had a loud and notable voice.

## Biography

Almost nothing is known of Zilpha beyond Thoreau's single sentence. She was a woman of color who lived in a small house near Walden Pond, supporting herself by spinning linen. Her singing voice was powerful enough to carry through the Walden woods. Thoreau does not record her last name, her origins, or the circumstances of her life—the single detail of her "loud and notable voice" is the entirety of her surviving memorial.

## Significance

Zilpha's inclusion in Walden is remarkable precisely because of how little Thoreau tells us. She is the only named woman of color among the former inhabitants Thoreau records. The detail of her singing—making the woods "ring"—is Thoreau at his most evocative, conjuring a whole human presence from a single sensory image. Like Brister Freeman and Hugh Quoil, Zilpha belongs to the invisible history Thoreau attempts to recover, however fragmentarily, in the Former Inhabitants chapter. Her story is also a reminder of how much is lost: a life reduced to one sentence, preserved only because a writer happened to pass that way.
