# Reason

## Definition

Reason in *Walden* occupies an uneasy middle ground between instinct and spiritual intuition. Thoreau is an heir of the Enlightenment, committed to the proposition that the mind can understand the world through its own powers. Yet he is also a Romantic, suspicious of reason's tendency to reduce the living to the mechanical, the mysterious to the merely explicable. "For what reason have I this vast range and circuit, some square miles of unfrequented forest, for my privacy, abandoned to me by men?" (005-003) — the question is both rational (it asks for a cause) and transcendent (it points toward a gift that exceeds any cause). Reason can ask the question; it cannot, by itself, answer it.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most direct engagement with reason appears in his critique of the division between intellectual and manual labor. His neighbors, he observes, are proud of their rationality — their ability to calculate, to plan, to optimize — yet this rationality has led them into lives of "quiet desperation." "This is the reason he is poor," Thoreau says of the farmer whose rational pursuit of profit has impoverished his spirit, "and for a similar reason we are all poor in respect to a thousand savage comforts" (001-056). Reason, when it serves only material ends, becomes a form of poverty — the poverty of the person who can calculate everything except the value of a life.

The limitations of reason are most visible in Thoreau's treatment of philanthropy. "If I knew for a certainty that a man was coming to my house with the conscious design of doing me good, I should run for my life" (001-011). The philanthropist's rational calculation of what others need is, for Thoreau, a form of violence — the imposition of one person's reason upon another's life. True help is not rational but intuitive, arising from a shared life rather than a calculated program.

Yet Thoreau does not reject reason outright. His own method — measuring the pond, keeping accounts, testing hypotheses about bread, ice, and soil — is deeply rational. "I have been willing to omit the unusual advantages of going through a course of scientific study" (011-003), he admits, but the admission itself reveals his respect for scientific method. What he rejects is not reason but the imperialism of reason — the claim that rational thought is the only valid way of knowing. The pond can be measured, but its meaning cannot be calculated; the bean-field can be accounted for, but the joy of cultivation cannot be entered in a ledger.

The most profound statement about reason in *Walden* may be its absence from the book's most ecstatic passages. When Thoreau describes the thawing sandbank in "Spring," reason falls silent; what speaks is perception, wonder, and a joy that does not need to explain itself. "For the same reason that I prefer the natural sky to an opium-eater's heaven," Thoreau writes (011-011), preferring the actual to the imagined — but the "reason" here is not a logical argument but a declaration of temperament, a preference that reason can articulate but not produce.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's ambivalence toward reason reflects the tension within transcendentalism itself. Emerson's "Reason" (with a capital R) was the faculty that perceives universal truth directly, bypassing the senses; Thoreau, more embodied, more practical, is less confident that any single faculty can deliver the whole truth. His reason is a tool among tools — valuable when used in its proper sphere, dangerous when it claims sovereignty over all others. The deliberate life requires reason, but it is not constituted by reason alone. Instinct, intuition, perception, and love each have their own claims, and the wise person knows when to let each speak.
