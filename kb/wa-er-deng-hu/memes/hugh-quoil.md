# Hugh Quoil

An Irish immigrant and former soldier who lived near Walden Pond as a squatter, briefly memorialized by Thoreau in the Former Inhabitants chapter.

## Connection to Walden

Hugh Quoil was one of the last inhabitants of the Walden woods before Thoreau's experiment. Thoreau describes him occupying Wyman's abandoned tenement, a figure on the margins of Concord society. Quoil's story represents the final layer of human habitation before Thoreau's own arrival.

> [014-011] Here, by the very corner of my field, still nearer to town, an Irishman, Hugh Quoil (if I have spelt his name with coil enough,) who occupied Wyman's tenement,—Col. Quoil, he was called. He was said to have been a soldier at Waterloo. He lived to be an old man, a drunkard, and was said to have lain out for many weeks together in the woods, in a hollow tree.

## Biography

Little is known of Hugh Quoil beyond Thoreau's brief account. He was an Irish immigrant who claimed to have fought at the Battle of Waterloo (1815). By the 1840s, he was living as a squatter in Wyman the potter's abandoned house near Walden Pond. Thoreau records his death shortly before the Walden experiment began, noting the grotesque detail of his distorted corpse.

## Significance

Hugh Quoil exemplifies the marginalized figures Thoreau excavates from the Walden landscape. As an Irish immigrant, a drunkard, and a squatter, Quoil represents the lowest rung of Concord's social order—yet Thoreau grants him a place in the record. The name "Quoil" itself (which Thoreau suspects he has misspelled) suggests the coiled, compressed life of a man who had no other chronicler. In Thoreau's hands, even a brief paragraph can serve as a memorial.
