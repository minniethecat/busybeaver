# Cellar

## Definition

In *Walden*, the cellar carries a double significance: it is both a practical necessity of shelter (food storage, frost protection) and an archaeological record of human habitation. Thoreau uses cellar-holes — the sunken depressions left by collapsed or abandoned houses — as evidence of former lives, reading them as texts that reveal the transience of human settlement against the permanence of nature.

## In Walden

Thoreau dug his own cellar early in the construction of his house, describing the process in practical, almost geological terms: "I dug my cellar in the side of a hill sloping to the south" (001-080). He excavated "six or seven feet deep, cellar fashion" (001-066), reaching soil suitable for a foundation. This act of digging connected him to the countless cellar-diggers before him — "the cellar was a sort of dust hole two feet deep" (001-078) in the house he dismantled for boards, a hint of how casually earlier builders had treated what Thoreau made deliberate.

> [001-080] I dug my cellar in the side of a hill sloping to the south, where a woodchuck had formerly dug his burrow, down through sumach and blackberry roots, and the lowest stain of vegetation, six feet square by seven deep, to a fine sand where potatoes would not freeze in any winter.

His cellar became a living space for other creatures as well: "The moles nested in my cellar, nibbling every third potato" (013-028). This cohabitation — moles in the cellar, woodchuck at the door — expressed the porous boundary between human dwelling and animal habitat that Thoreau cultivated.

But it is the cellar-holes of former inhabitants that most occupy Thoreau's imagination. In "Former Inhabitants," he catalogs the remains of previous Walden Woods residents: "Cato's half-obliterated cellar hole still remains, though known to few" (014-002). These depressions in the earth are the only surviving markers of houses, families, and whole lives — "with buried cellar stones, and strawberries, raspberries" (014-012) — nature reclaiming human construction. The cellar-hole is Thoreau's memento mori: what remains when all above-ground structures collapse and rot.

> [014-002] Cato's half-obliterated cellar hole still remains, though known to few, being concealed from the traveller by a fringe of pines.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, the cellar is an image of foundation in both senses — the literal base of a house and the metaphorical ground of a life. He critiques those who build elaborate superstructures on shallow cellars: "a man is rich in proportion to the number of things which he can afford to let alone" (002-020). The cellar represents what lies beneath appearances — the honest excavation versus the showy facade. Thoreau's own cellar was modest but sufficient, dug by hand to the exact depth required, no more. This practical minimalism was his answer to the grand "cellars for the storage of wines and other munitions of peace" (006-002) that burdened Concord's propertied class.

## Historical Context

In nineteenth-century New England, the root cellar was essential infrastructure — the only means of preserving vegetables through the winter. Thoreau describes how one must "sprout potatoes in a cellar Sundays, in order to keep away from meeting" (014-007), humorously linking cellar-work to Sabbath avoidance. The cellar was also a marker of permanence: a dug and stone-lined cellar indicated intent to stay, while its absence marked temporary or impoverished settlement. The abandoned cellar-holes Thoreau cataloged were the archaeological record of Walden Woods' earlier African American and Irish residents, whose histories were otherwise unrecorded.
