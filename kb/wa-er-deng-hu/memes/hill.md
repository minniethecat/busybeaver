# Hill

## Definition

A hill in *Walden* is more than a topographical feature. It is a place of vantage, a site for dwelling and cultivation, and a symbol of the perspective that elevation affords. Thoreau's house stands on a hillside, his bean-field lies on a hillslope, and his gaze repeatedly rises to the hill-tops that surround Walden Pond. The hill is a middle ground between the sunken pond and the open sky — a place where the earth rises toward the heavens without leaving the ground.

## In Walden

Thoreau chose a hillside for his dwelling. "My house was on the side of a hill, immediately on the edge of the larger wood" (004-004), he writes, and the location is deliberate. The slope provides drainage for the cellar and a south-facing exposure for warmth. "I dug my cellar in the side of a hill sloping to the south, where a woodchuck had formerly dug his burrow" (001-080). The hillside dwelling follows the land's own shape, borrowing the hill's shelter as the woodchuck borrows its burrow — a natural adaptation, not an imposition.

The hill is also a place of labor. Thoreau describes his bean-field on "a pleasant hillside where I worked, covered with pine woods, through which I looked down on the pond" (001-069). The slope gives the work a view; hoeing beans becomes a contemplative act when the pond lies below and the sky above. The stones he carries "up the hill from the pond in my arms" (001-081) are the literal weight of cultivation, but the upward movement — from water to height — enacts a spiritual ascent.

From the hill-top, Thoreau gains perspective on the world below. "From a hill top near by, where the wood had been recently cut off, there was a pleasing vista southward" (002-013), he reports, and the vista is not merely scenic but philosophical: the elevated view reveals the pattern of woods and fields, the course of the river, the relation of parts to the whole. The hill-top is where the mind, like the eye, can see farthest.

The hills also carry and transform sound. The railroad's "iron horse" makes "the hills echo with his snort like thunder" (004-011), the echo translating the mechanical into the natural, the intrusive train into a voice of the landscape. The pine logs of Thoreau's house, "hewn on far northern hills" (004-016), carry the memory of their origin — the hills are present in the dwelling even as sawn timber. "The cattle of a thousand hills" (004-019) feed the railroad cars, and the biblical echo (Psalm 50:10) dignifies the commerce it describes.

Walden Pond itself is hidden from most approaches. It is "visible from no place but the hill-tops within half a mile of my dwelling" (005-003), which makes the hill a condition of seeing the pond at all. Elevation reveals what the flat ground conceals: the hill is not an escape from the world but a better view of it.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's hills are as much moral as physical. The ascent to a hill-top is an act of self-overcoming — the effort of climbing is rewarded with a widened horizon. The "down hill" path he warns against is both literal and spiritual: "the rest of the way is down hill" (002-029) describes the ease of following the worn path, the rut of habit that requires no effort and affords no view. To live deliberately is to climb — to seek the vantage from which one's life becomes visible in its true proportions.

The hills around Walden are modest by mountain standards, but Thoreau values them precisely for this modesty. They are accessible elevations, hills one can walk up in an afternoon, not remote peaks requiring expedition. The democracy of the hill — available to any walker — suits Thoreau's democratic philosophy: wisdom is not reserved for specialists on distant summits but available to anyone who will climb the local rise.
