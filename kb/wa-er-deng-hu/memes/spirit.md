# Spirit

## Definition

In Walden, the spirit is the divine element within the human — that which instinctively reaches toward "a higher, or, as it is named, spiritual life" (011-001). Thoreau distinguishes the spirit from the animal body (which hungers and sleeps), from the intellect (which analyzes but does not aspire), and from the soul (the individual self). The spirit is the upward tendency, the gravitational pull toward what is noblest in human nature.

## In Walden

Spirit is not an abstraction in Walden but a felt presence, most accessible in solitude. "I have felt my nearest approach to the spiritual world through the solitude of the woods," Thoreau writes, describing how the pond, the trees, and the sky together become a sanctuary more potent than any church. The spirit is awakened by nature, not by sermons: "A field of water betrays the spirit that is in the air" (009-020). The pond's surface becomes a medium of spiritual perception, revealing the invisible currents that move through the visible world.

Yet Thoreau is candid about the difficulty of sustaining spiritual life. "We are all sculptors and painters, and our material is our own flesh and blood and bones," he acknowledges (011-025), framing spiritual cultivation as an artistic labor performed on the self. The body is the temple, but the spirit is the god worshipped within it — and the worshipper must build the temple first. This labor is ongoing, never complete. "I fear that we are such gods or demigods only as fauns and satyrs," he warns (011-014), suggesting that the spirit is always at risk of being dragged down by animal appetite.

Thoreau finds spirit even in the brute world. The "exuberance of animal spirits" in the Canadian woodchopper (006-019) and the "sturdy spirits of ancient wine-bibbers" in the bullfrogs (004-035) suggest that vitality itself is a kind of spiritual quality. The distinction between the animal and the spiritual is not absolute but a matter of degree — the "vital spirits" that plants draw from the air (007-011) are continuous with the spiritual aspiration of the human.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's concept of spirit is radically this-worldly. Unlike the Puritan theology of his New England inheritance, he does not oppose spirit to flesh or spirit to nature. The "spiritual life" he advocates (011-001) is not a rejection of the body but its refinement — eating simply, rising early, bathing in the pond. Spiritual discipline and physical discipline are one practice.

Thoreau is also skeptical of those who claim spiritual superiority while neglecting earthly obligations. "There are nowadays professors of philosophy, but not philosophers," he observes (001-012), drawing a line between those who merely talk about spiritual matters and those who embody spirit in conduct. The true test of spirit is not doctrinal correctness but the quality of one's daily life — how one treats neighbors, how one spends one's mornings, how one meets "the essential facts of life" (001-002).
