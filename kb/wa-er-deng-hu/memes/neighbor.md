# Neighbor / Neighborliness

## Definition

The concept of the neighbor in *Walden* undergoes a radical expansion in Thoreau's hands. A neighbor is not simply the nearest human inhabitant but any being — human, animal, or even natural feature — with whom one shares proximity and from whom one can learn. Thoreau reimagines neighborliness as a relationship defined not by physical closeness but by mutual respect, and he finds his truest neighbors in the birds, the pond, and the woods rather than in the farmers of Concord.

## In Walden

Neighbor and neighborhood are among the most recurrent terms in *Walden*, appearing from the opening sentence — "a mile from any neighbor" (001-001) — to the closing meditations. The word carries the full weight of Thoreau's social philosophy, marking both what he left behind and what he found anew at the pond.

The Concord neighborhood from which Thoreau retreats is defined by economic comparison and social conformity. "The greater part of what my neighbors call good I believe in my soul to be bad," he declares in "Economy" (001-020), establishing the moral independence that will govern all his neighborly relations. His neighbors' undertakings — building larger houses, accumulating property — strike him not as achievements but as burdens: enterprises "which they have undertaken" that weigh upon their lives (001-003).

Yet Thoreau is no misanthrope. His critique of neighborly convention is paired with genuine curiosity about individual neighbors. He catalogs his Concord neighbors — the farmers, the Irish laborers, the lawyer — with ethnographic precision. "When I consider my neighbors, the farmers of Concord," he writes (001-055), the observation is at once critical and attentive. He knows these men; his distance from them is philosophical, not indifferent.

The physical neighborhood around Walden Pond becomes a laboratory for reimagining neighborly relations. "My nearest neighbor is a mile distant," Thoreau notes in "Solitude" (005-003), and this distance is not deprivation but liberation — it frees him to choose his neighbors rather than have them imposed. He doubts momentarily whether "the near neighborhood of man was not essential to a serene and healthy life" (005-004), but the doubt dissolves as he discovers that the pond, the pines, and the birds provide a neighborliness more nourishing than human society.

This discovery culminates in one of *Walden*'s most striking declarations: "I found myself suddenly neighbor to the birds; not by having imprisoned one, but by having caged myself near them" (002-011). The inversion is characteristic Thoreau — he becomes a neighbor not by capturing or possessing but by placing himself within reach. The birds are neighbors because he has made himself available to them.

The pond itself takes on the role of neighbor. Walden Pond was "of most value as a neighbor in the intervals of a gentle rain" (002-013), Thoreau writes, attributing to a body of water the qualities of companionship and constancy. The pond as neighbor neither judges nor demands; it simply abides, and in its abiding teaches.

In "Reading," Thoreau extends the neighbor concept to books and their authors. He laments of a classical text: "I never saw him — my next neighbor and I never heard him speak" (003-010). The great authors of antiquity are neighbors we have neglected, and reading is an act of neighborly attention across time. Conversely, he praises the writer who "treated his neighbors accordingly" (003-011) — with the respect due to equals.

"Brute Neighbors" makes explicit what the earlier chapters imply: that animals are the true neighbors at Walden. Thoreau's mice, his loon, his ants — these are fellow inhabitants whose presence shapes the quality of his days more profoundly than any human visitor. The chapter title itself is a philosophical claim: brutes *are* neighbors.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's philosophy of neighborliness inverts the conventional understanding. Where society treats proximity as obligation — the neighbor as someone you must tolerate, borrow from, or compete with — Thoreau treats proximity as opportunity for mutual enhancement. The best neighbor is not the nearest but the one whose presence teaches. This is why the pond qualifies as neighbor while many Concordians do not: the pond enriches Thoreau's life; the gossiping villager diminishes it. Thoreau's neighborliness is elective, merit-based, and grounded in the conviction that "it must of necessity have neighbors" (005-013) — that relationship is essential to life, but the choice of whom to relate to is the individual's sovereign right.
