# Evil

## Definition

In *Walden*, evil is understood less as a theological category than as a condition of estrangement — from nature, from conscience, and from the deliberate life. Thoreau inherits the moral vocabulary of New England Calvinism but empties it of doctrinal content: evil is real, but it resides in avoidable human arrangements and failures of attention rather than in original sin or demonic agency. "Would the savage have been wise to exchange his wigwam for a palace on these terms?" (001-051) — the "doubtful choice of evils" is between forms of civilized deprivation.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most direct treatment of evil appears in his critique of social institutions. Slavery — "the state which buys and sells men, women, and children, like cattle at the door of its senate-house" — is the unambiguous evil against which he measures all other social arrangements. But evil also takes subtler forms: the "evil genius" (009-006) that seems to direct the ice-cutters, the "devilish Iron Horse" (009-024) whose noise violates the pond's quiet, and the "evil which one does in the interval" (017-029) — the cumulative moral damage of inattention and conformity.

> [009-006] ...as if some evil genius had directed it, it slid into the water where it was never seen again.

The railroad recurs as an emblem of systemic evil — not because transportation is inherently wicked, but because the railroad's power is deployed without regard for the human and natural communities it disrupts. "That devilish Iron Horse, whose ear-rending neigh is heard throughout the town" (009-024) personifies industrial force as a malevolent agent. The echo of "blue devils" in "it has not the blue devils, but the blue angels in it" (005-018) suggests that evil is a quality of the perceiving mind as much as of the object perceived — the pond's water reflects heaven, not hell.

> [009-024] That devilish Iron Horse, whose ear-rending neigh is heard throughout the town.

Thoreau also gestures toward the way evil propagates through time: "the evil which one does in the interval of a second may be felt for a thousand years" (017-029). This is not karma in the Eastern sense but a recognition that moral consequences, like the rings of a felled tree, extend far beyond their moment of origin.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's evil is structural rather than metaphysical. He shows little interest in personified evil (Satan, demons) and much in the evil that ordinary people do when they defer moral responsibility to institutions. The man who works for the railroad or votes for a pro-slavery government is not, in Thoreau's view, personally wicked in the melodramatic sense; he has merely failed to wake up. This is what makes evil in *Walden* simultaneously less dramatic and more pervasive than in conventional religious discourse — it is not a foreign invader but a default condition from which one must actively rouse oneself. The antidote to evil is not exorcism but deliberation: the deliberate life, by refusing to sleepwalk through moral choices, drains evil of its power.
