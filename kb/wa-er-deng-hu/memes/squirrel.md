# Squirrel

## Definition

The red squirrel (*Sciurus Hudsonius*) is the most intimately observed wild animal in *Walden*. Unlike the loon or the fox, which Thoreau encounters occasionally, the squirrel is a daily presence — it lives on his roof, under his floor, and at the edge of his bean-field. Thoreau's portrait of the red squirrel is affectionate but unsentimental: the squirrel is "impudent" and "chattering," a creature of boundless energy and comic self-importance. In the economy of Walden's animal life, the squirrel occupies the middle ground between the wild and the domestic — it is not a pet, but neither is it wholly other; it coexists with Thoreau in a condition of mutual toleration that borders on companionship.

## In Walden

The squirrel enters *Walden* unobtrusively, in a list of the creatures that share Thoreau's dwelling. His roof and floor, he notes, were "never baited in,—only squirrels on the roof and under the floor" (004-036). The observation is offhand but significant: the squirrel is a co-tenant, not an intruder. Thoreau has built his house in the squirrels' territory, and they have accepted his presence with the same equanimity he extends to theirs.

The red squirrel becomes a central character in "Brute Neighbors." Thoreau describes it "coursing down the nearest bough" with the jerky, impulsive motion that is the squirrel's signature: "he would advance by short impulses, like a squirrel, which it resembled in its motions, and then pause, and then advance again" (012-009, 012-011). The squirrel's movement is a kind of punctuation in the stillness of the woods — bursts of activity followed by sudden stillness, a rhythm that Thoreau finds endlessly watchable.

The most sustained squirrel passage occurs in "Winter Animals." Thoreau describes waking to the sound of the red squirrel on his roof: "Usually the red squirrel (Sciurus Hudsonius) waked me in the dawn, coursing over the roof and up and down the sides of the house, as if sent out of the woods for this purpose" (015-005). The squirrel as alarm clock — sent, Thoreau half-seriously suggests, by the woods themselves to ensure the human inhabitant does not sleep through the morning. The squirrel's energy is prodigal: "he would start up a chestnut tree, and scold at the harmless spectator, — what, as if he were the sole possessor of the woods" (015-004). The squirrel's chattering is a claim of ownership, a declaration that the woods belong to him and that Thoreau is there on sufferance.

In the bean-field, Thoreau rises "before yet any woodchuck or squirrel had run across the road" (007-004), marking the squirrel's appearance as the boundary between the pre-dawn solitude and the day's fuller activity. The squirrel's first crossing of the road is, for Thoreau, the official commencement of the day. In autumn, he notes "the squirrels manifest no concern whether the woods will bear chestnuts this year or not" (007-040) — the squirrel's freedom from anxiety is a lesson in living in the present, an example Thoreau holds up to his own tendency toward worry.

> [015-005] Usually the red squirrel (Sciurus Hudsonius) waked me in the dawn, coursing over the roof and up and down the sides of the house, as if sent out of the woods for this purpose.

> [012-011] Or sometimes I heard the red squirrel, coursing down the nearest bough, with a ludicrous deliberation and noise.

> [007-040] I cease from anxiety, as the squirrels manifest no concern whether the woods will bear chestnuts this year or no.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's squirrel is never merely a squirrel. It is, by turns, a clock (waking him at dawn), a comic (its "ludicrous deliberation"), a proprietor (scolding "as if he were the sole possessor of the woods"), and a philosopher (manifesting no concern for the future). The squirrel's lesson is the lesson of the wild: to live wholly in the present, to take what the day offers without anxiety for what the season may or may not bring. Thoreau's affection for the squirrel is rooted in this quality of absolute presence. The squirrel does not deliberate; it acts. It does not worry; it gathers. In a book so much concerned with the perils of over-thinking, the squirrel is an emblem of unreflective vitality — a mode of being that Thoreau admires even as he recognizes that his own nature inclines him toward reflection.
