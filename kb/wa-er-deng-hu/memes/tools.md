# Tools

## Definition

Tools are the implements by which human beings shape the material world — ploughs, axes, hammers, saws — and in Thoreau's lexicon they are a test of human freedom. A tool, rightly used, extends the hand and liberates the spirit; wrongly used, it becomes a master. The central aphorism of *Walden* on this subject is among Thoreau's most compressed and devastating judgments: "men have become the tools of their tools" (001-065). In seven words it reverses the presumed hierarchy between person and implement, diagnosing a civilization that has forgotten what its instruments are for.

## In Walden

The theme of tools appears early in *Walden*, in Thoreau's diagnosis of his Concord neighbors. He observes young men who have "inherited farms, houses, barns, cattle, and farming tools; for these are more easily acquired than got rid of" (001-004). Inheritance, which should be a liberation, becomes a trap — the tools and property come with obligations that bind a man to a life he did not choose. The tools are not neutral; they carry the weight of the system that produced them.

Thoreau's most famous pronouncement on the subject arrives in the first chapter's sustained critique of labor: "men have become the tools of their tools" (001-065). The man who plows the field is himself plowed under — his labor serves not his own flourishing but the maintenance of the implements and property that supposedly serve him. The inversion is complete: the tool, which should be an extension of human will, has reduced the human to its extension.

Thoreau's own practice offers a counter-example. Building his house at Walden, he tells us: "Each stick was carefully mortised or tenoned by its stump, for I had borrowed other tools by this time" (001-077). He does not own the tools; he borrows them. By keeping his tool-set minimal and temporary, he prevents them from accumulating into a burden. His days in the woods "were not very long ones," yet he accomplished what he needed — because the tools remained tools, and he remained their master.

In the winter chapter, the ice-harvesters arrive with "many car-loads of ungainly-looking farming tools, sleds, ploughs, drill-barrows, turf-knives, spades, saws, rakes" (016-022). These hundred men of "Hyperborean extraction" come to strip Walden of its ice, and their tools are described as ungainly — disproportionate, almost grotesque. The catalogue of implements underscores the industrial scale of the operation, a far cry from Thoreau's borrowed axe and mortising chisel.

> [001-065] But lo! men have become the tools of their tools. The man who independently plucked the fruits when he was hungry is become a farmer; and he who stood under a tree for shelter, a housekeeper.

> [001-077] Each stick was carefully mortised or tenoned by its stump, for I had borrowed other tools by this time. My days in the woods were not very long ones; yet I usually carried my dinner of bread and butter, and read the newspaper in which it was wrapped, at noon, sitting amid the green pine boughs which I had cut off.

> [018-016] It is a ridiculous demand which England and America make, that you shall speak so that they can understand you. Neither men nor toad-stools grow so.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's critique of tools is not a primitivist rejection of technology. He uses tools constantly — axe, hammer, saw, chisel, spade — and describes his work with them in precise, appreciative detail. What he rejects is the *accumulation* of tools beyond genuine need, and the social system that compels such accumulation. The farmer "ploughs the field" but "does not pluck the fruits" — his tools intervene between him and direct sustenance. Thoreau's ideal is the tool that disappears into its use: borrowed, returned, forgotten. "Simplify, simplify" applies to the toolbox as much as to the wardrobe.
