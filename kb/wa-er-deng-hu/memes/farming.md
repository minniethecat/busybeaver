# Farming

## Definition

Farming in *Walden* encompasses both the conventional agricultural labor of Concord's farmers and Thoreau's own experimental cultivation of beans at Walden Pond. Thoreau distinguishes between farming as economic drudgery — a life of "quiet desperation" — and cultivation as a deliberate, philosophical engagement with the earth that yields spiritual as well as material sustenance.

## In Walden

Thoreau devotes an entire chapter, "The Bean-Field" (007), to his experiment in cultivation. He plants approximately two and a half acres of beans, not primarily for profit but as a means of understanding the relationship between labor, land, and self-reliance.

> [007-001] Meanwhile my beans, the length of whose rows, added together, was seven miles already planted, were impatient to be hoed, for the earliest had grown considerably before the latest were in the ground.

His critique of conventional farming runs throughout the book. In "Economy" (001), he portrays the farmer as enslaved by his own possessions and routines:

> [001-056] The farmer is endeavoring to solve the problem of a livelihood by a formula more complicated than the problem itself. To get his shoestrings he speculates in herds of cattle.

Thoreau's own approach to cultivation is deliberately modest. He keeps careful accounts of his bean-field expenses and yields (007-025), yet sees the true value of his labor as something beyond ledger entries:

> [007-006] It was no longer beans that I hoed, nor I that hoed beans; and I remembered with as much pity as pride, if I remembered at all, my acquaintances who had gone to the city to attend the oratorios.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau reimagines farming not as a commercial enterprise but as a sacred art with classical roots. He invokes the Roman tradition of agricultural festivals and suggests that farming has lost its spiritual dimension in the modern age:

> [007-039] Ancient poetry and mythology suggest, at least, that husbandry was once a sacred art; but it is pursued with irreverent haste and heedlessness by us, our object being to have large farms and large crops merely.

He sees his bean-field as a means of cultivating himself as much as the soil, arguing that the true harvest is the character and wisdom gained through deliberate labor in nature.
