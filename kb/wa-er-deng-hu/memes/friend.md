# Friend / Friendship

## Definition

Friendship in *Walden* is not a casual social category but a demanding spiritual discipline. Thoreau distinguishes sharply between the neighbor — who is owed courtesy and practical aid — and the friend, who is owed truth. The friend is the one before whom one can think aloud, the companion whose presence deepens rather than distracts from solitude. "I had three chairs in my house," Thoreau writes, "one for solitude, two for friendship, three for society" (006-002). The arithmetic is deliberate: friendship occupies the middle ground between the self's necessary aloneness and the crowd's dissipation, and it is, of the three, the most precarious — easy to degrade into mere society, difficult to maintain at the pitch of genuine encounter.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most concentrated reflection on friendship appears in "Visitors," where the distinction between the visitor and the friend is drawn with characteristic precision. The visitor comes with expectations, with social scripts, with the need to be entertained; the friend comes to be present, and presence is enough. Thoreau describes receiving "a most wise and humorous friend, whom I love much, who keeps a lonely cottage" (005-019) — the friend is himself a solitary, which is why his presence does not violate Thoreau's own solitude but completes it. Two solitudes, meeting, become friendship without ceasing to be solitude.

The friend also appears as a moral category. "A true friend of man; almost the only friend of human progress" (014-023) — this is Thoreau's description of the kind of person who serves humanity not through philanthropy but through the example of a life lived deliberately. The friend is one who helps others not by doing things for them but by being what they might become. This is friendship as inspiration rather than assistance, and it sets a standard that few actual social relations can meet.

Thoreau is clear-eyed about the difficulty of true friendship. "Some of my friends spoke as if I was coming to the woods on purpose to freeze myself" (013-028), he reports — the friends who misunderstand, who worry, who project their own fears onto his experiment. These are friends in the social sense but not in the Thoreauvian sense, and the gap between the two senses is the space of Thoreau's implicit critique. Most friendship, he suggests, is merely the mutual reassurance of shared habits; genuine friendship is the risky encounter of two minds that may disagree.

The friend is also, crucially, a witness. In "Economy," Thoreau contrasts his own practices with those of his neighbors, and the implied reader — the friend who understands — is the audience for whom the entire Walden experiment is performed. "I do not propose to write an ode to dejection," he says elsewhere, "but to brag as lustily as chanticleer in the morning" (002-022). The bragging requires a listener, and that listener is the friend: the one who can hear Thoreau's self-assertion not as arrogance but as testimony.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's concept of friendship is influenced by Emerson's essay "Friendship" but departs from it in significant ways. Emerson's friend is a spiritual ideal, almost Platonic — the friend one never quite meets in any actual person. Thoreau's friend is more embodied, more demanding, and more disappointing. He expects friends to be truthful, to challenge him, to resist the slide into comfortable silence. "We never need to go out of the way to meet a friend," he might say, but the friend who appears without effort is also the friend most easily taken for granted. Thoreauvian friendship is work — the work of attention, of honesty, of refusing to let the relationship become mere habit.

This demanding quality makes Thoreau's friendship ethic both admirable and difficult. He lost friends over it — Emerson among them, at times — because the standard was so high that almost no one could meet it consistently. But the standard itself is the point: friendship, like life, should be deliberate rather than default.
