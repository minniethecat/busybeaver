# River

## Definition

The river in *Walden* is the pond's opposite and complement. Where Walden Pond is still, self-contained, and essentially vertical — its depth the register of its character — the Concord River is horizontal, flowing, connective. The pond is being; the river is becoming. Thoreau does not spend as many pages on the river as on the pond, but the river is a constant presence at the edges of his attention — the waterway that links Concord to the sea, the current that carries trade goods and spring freshets, the ecosystem that supports a different community of creatures than the pond's clear depths. To understand Thoreau's water philosophy, one must see the river and the pond together, as a single hydrological and symbolic system.

## In Walden

The Concord River appears first in "Economy" as a boundary — "its bounding on the river" (002-006) is one of the features that attracted Thoreau to the Hollowell farm he almost bought. The river as boundary is the river as limit: it defines the edge of a property, the end of one jurisdiction and the beginning of another. But for Thoreau, boundaries are always also invitations. The river that marks the edge of a farm also marks the beginning of a journey, and Thoreau's imagination flows downstream as readily as the water itself.

The river's water is qualitatively different from the pond's. "I have seen our river, when, the landscape being covered with snow, the water was of a pale greenish hue" (009-005) — Thoreau notes the river's color with the same precision he brings to the pond, but the river's color is a function of its movement. The pond's color comes from its depth and its purity; the river's color comes from what it carries — sediment, vegetation, the dissolved substance of the land it drains. The river is murkier than the pond, but its murkiness is not a defect; it is the sign of its connectedness to the larger landscape.

Thoreau's most lyrical river passage appears in "The Ponds," where he describes the fish of Walden in contrast to those of the river. "A bright golden kind, with greenish reflections, and remarkably deep-bodied" (009-014) — the pond's pickerel are distinct from the river's fish, shaped by still water rather than flowing. The difference between pond fish and river fish is a parable of the difference between two modes of being: the contemplative and the active, the deep and the broad, the self-contained and the connected.

The river also carries the moral weight of commerce. The railroad follows the river's course, as railroads tend to do, and the river thus becomes a corridor of trade. Thoreau's ambivalence about commerce attaches to the river as well: it is the artery through which the world reaches Concord, and reaching Concord, reaches Walden. The river connects; connection is both gift and threat.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of the river reveals a dimension of his thought that the pond, for all its depth, cannot fully express. The pond models the life of withdrawal, of depth, of self-sufficiency; the river models the life of engagement, of movement, of relationship to what lies beyond the self. Thoreau clearly prefers the pond — he built his house beside it, not beside the river — but he does not dismiss the river. The deliberate life, at its fullest, knows when to be still and when to flow, when to deepen and when to connect.

This river-pond dialectic is one of Thoreau's most original contributions to American nature writing. The pond is not a retreat from the world but a vantage point from which the world — flowing past in the river, rumbling past on the railroad — can be seen more clearly. The river brings the world to the pond's edge; the pond receives it, reflects it, and lets it pass.
