# Slavery

## Definition

Slavery in *Walden* is a layered concept. On the surface, it refers to the institution of chattel slavery in the antebellum United States, which Thoreau unequivocally condemned. Beneath this literal meaning, slavery serves as Thoreau's governing metaphor for the condition of most people in modern society — enslaved not by a master with a whip but by their own possessions, habits, and social conformity. The workers who built the pyramids, the Irish laborers who laid the railroad sleepers, and the farmer "harassed to death" by his mortgage are all, in Thoreau's analysis, living in servitude. True freedom, he argues, requires emancipation on both levels: political and spiritual.

## In Walden

Thoreau addresses slavery explicitly in "Economy" (001), where he compares the condition of wage laborers in the North unfavorably with chattel slavery in the South — arguing that the difference is one of form rather than substance:

> [001-014] It is hard to have a Southern overseer; it is worse to have a Northern one; but worst of all when you are the slave-driver of yourself. Talk of a divinity in man! Look at the teamster on the highway, wending to market by day or night; does any divinity stir within him?

He extends this analysis to encompass not only economic bondage but the subtler forms of internalized servitude — the "keen and subtle masters that enslave both north and south" (001-014). The body of the laborer becomes a " staple production" exported by the South, making human beings into commodities.

In "Visitors" (006), Thoreau records actual encounters with fugitive slaves who passed through Concord on the Underground Railroad. These are not abstractions but real people whom Thoreau assisted:

> [006-027] One real runaway slave, among the rest, whom I helped to forward toward the north star.

He also notes the presence of "runaway slaves with plantation manners, who listened from time to time, like the fox in the fable, as if they heard the hounds a-baying on their track, and looked at me beseechingly, as much as to say, — 'O Christian, will you send me back?'" (006-025). These encounters ground his political critique in lived experience. In "Baker Farm" (010), Thoreau explicitly links slavery to war, referring to "the slavery and war and other superfluous expenses" (010-015) that the state compels citizens to sustain — a premonition of the Civil War that would begin less than a decade after *Walden*'s publication.

The landscape around Walden Pond itself bears the memory of slavery. In "Former Inhabitants" (014), Thoreau records the names of Black residents — Cato Ingraham, "slave of Duncan Ingraham, Esquire" (014-002), and Brister Freeman, "'a handy Negro,' slave of Squire Cummings once" (014-004) — whose freedom came late and whose lives left only cellar-holes. By naming them, Thoreau performs an act of historical recovery that refuses to let slavery's victims disappear into the landscape.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's anti-slavery stance was not merely political but existential. He saw the institution of slavery as the most extreme expression of a principle that operated everywhere in American life: the reduction of persons to instruments, of lives to labor, of time to money. His famous essay "Civil Disobedience" (published 1849, during the *Walden* period) made the case for individual resistance to an unjust state, and *Walden* itself is that philosophy lived out — a demonstration that freedom is possible, that one can step outside the system of compulsory labor and consumption. For Thoreau, the abolition of chattel slavery was necessary but not sufficient; the deeper emancipation required each person to examine their own chains — the unnecessary wants, the conformist opinions, the fear of simplicity.
