# Wall

Walls in *Walden* appear as both physical structures and philosophical metaphors. Thoreau examines the walls of his house as exercises in economy, the walls of society as constraints on freedom, and the impulse to "wall in" one's world as a fundamental human gesture of delimitation and self-definition.

## The House Walls

Thoreau built his Walden house with walls of rough, weather-stained boards, rejecting the elaborate plaster and ornament of conventional dwellings. He describes the simple construction with precision:

> ...without plastering or chimney, the walls being of rough, weather-stained boards... (002-010)

The interior walls were given careful attention — Thoreau lined them with wood and covered the wood with a backing of brown paper:

> ...inside with wood all round the wall, and line the wood with the brown paper... (001-066)

This methodical description of the house walls reflects Thoreau's philosophy that every detail of shelter should be deliberate and economical. Even the walls themselves participate in the "vital heat" principle — keeping warmth within the dwelling:

> ...the inside of the roof and the walls, and also that the board floor... (001-078)

## Walls of Society and Custom

Thoreau extends the metaphor of walls to describe the boundaries imposed by civilization and convention. The village walls — "the city's walls" — represent the accumulated structures of settled life that Thoreau sought to escape:

> ...an hour against the city's walls, and chairs enough to seat a... (004-010)

He observes that men too often seek to narrow their world, walling themselves into confined spaces that match their diminished aspirations:

> ...seek to narrow his world, and wall in a space such as fitted him... (001-048)

The walls of custom, Thoreau suggests, are self-imposed — built not from necessity but from habit and conformity. The traveller who could not "get over walls" represents those who cannot transcend the boundaries that convention erects:

> ...the traveller could get over walls or turn aside into cow paths... (008-001)

## Walls Against Nature

Thoreau is acutely aware that walls stand in tension with nature. The beautiful objects of the natural world resist enclosure:

> ...with beautiful objects the walls must be stripped, and our lives... (001-065)

The walls of civilization are temporary assertions against a nature that will eventually reclaim them. Thoreau notes the sand-bank by the railroad as an instance where human construction — walls of piled stone — are themselves made from and subject to the earth:

> ...obliged to pile them up in walls on both sides of the railroad... (009-012)

Even within his house, Thoreau notes the absence of rats "in the wall" — a sign that the walls were stripped of the accumulated detritus of long habitation. Nature had not yet been invited in:

> ...Not even rats in the wall, for they were starved out... (004-036)

## Boundary and Delimitation

For Thoreau, the act of wall-building is tied to architecture, both personal and communal. He notes that a man might "found a wall or a state" — linking the physical boundary of a wall to the political boundary of a state:

> ...place where you might found a wall or a state, or set a lamp-post... (002-029)

The wall thus becomes a symbol of primary human activity: marking space, claiming territory, defining inside against outside. Thoreau's own experiment at Walden was an act of walling — but lightly, provisionally, with the consciousness that all walls are temporary and that the highest life requires the walls to be "stripped" to reveal what lies beyond them.
