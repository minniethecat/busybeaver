# Boston

## Location

Boston lies approximately **18 miles southeast of Concord, Massachusetts**, connected by the Fitchburg Railroad, whose tracks passed within sight of Thoreau's cabin at Walden Pond. In Thoreau's day (1845–1847), Boston was a city of roughly 130,000 souls — the cultural and commercial capital of New England, and the center of the Transcendentalist movement of which Thoreau was a reluctant member.

## Role in Walden

Boston functions in *Walden* primarily as an **absent presence** — the place Thoreau has left, the gravitational center from which he has escaped, and the destination toward which the morning train perpetually rushes. It represents everything his experiment repudiates: commerce, fashion, gossip, and the unexamined busyness that he calls "lives of quiet desperation."

The train that passes Walden Pond each morning embodies this tension:

> [004-005] …conveying travellers from Boston to the country. For I did not ride on the railroad; it was the railroad that rode on us.

Thoreau's pun is serious: the railroad, built on the backs of laborers (many of them Irish immigrants like John Field), symbolizes an economic order that consumes human lives as fuel. Boston is the destination — the market that demands and receives the sacrifice.

> [004-012] …while the cars are going to Boston, conceals the sun for a minute, and makes the landscape a picture for city eyes.

The morning train hides the sun — a metaphor Thoreau deploys deliberately: the pursuit of Boston (commerce, society, advancement) literally **eclipses** the natural world that is the true source of illumination. The passengers see the landscape as "a picture for city eyes" — aestheticized, distanced, consumed rather than inhabited.

In *The Bean-Field*, Thoreau draws a direct contrast between Boston's relationship to the land and his own:

> [009-001] A huckleberry never reaches Boston; they have not been known there since they grew on the hills. The ambrosial and essential part of the fruit is lost with the bloom which is rubbed off on the journey.

The huckleberry is a synecdoche for all that Boston's market economy destroys: the delicate, the local, the perishable — the things that cannot survive the journey from field to counting-house.

Yet Thoreau's relationship to Boston is not simply hostile. In *Reading*, he invokes it as a measure of provincial narrowness:

> [003-012] …why not skip the gossip of Boston and take the best newspaper in the world, the classics?

The injunction is characteristic: Boston's "gossip" — its newspapers, its social whirl, its literary fashions — is a distraction from the permanent things. But the very need to say this acknowledges Boston's cultural authority. Thoreau is not indifferent to Boston; he is in **reaction** against it, and reaction is itself a form of engagement.

Late in the book, Thoreau's tone shifts. Boston becomes, improbably, a site of hope:

> [018-026] …in Boston and London and Paris and Rome, thinking and speaking, and creating new worlds.

The city that once represented distraction now appears in a chain of civilization's great capitals. Thoreau's deepest conviction is that the work of awakening can happen anywhere — even in Boston — if one carries the "morning" within.

## Description

In the 1840s, Boston was a city of brick and cobblestone, its harbor crowded with merchant vessels, its streets lined with bookshops and lecture halls. The Massachusetts State House, designed by Charles Bulfinch, dominated Beacon Hill. The city's intellectual life was concentrated in the publishing houses of Cornhill, the Athenaeum library, and the parlors where Emerson, Fuller, and Alcott held their "Conversations."

For Thoreau, who had lived in Boston briefly as a young man and visited regularly to see Emerson and to lecture, the city was both familiar and alienating. Its noise, its pace, its social demands — all stood in stark contrast to the silence of Walden Pond. When Thoreau writes of "the factory bell" and "the newsboy's cry," he is writing of Boston even when he does not name it.
