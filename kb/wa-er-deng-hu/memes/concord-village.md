# Concord Village

## Overview

Concord, Massachusetts, in the 1840s was a town of approximately **2,000 inhabitants**, situated at the confluence of the Sudbury and Assabet Rivers, which form the Concord River. Settled in 1635 as the first inland Puritan town, Concord had by Thoreau's time become a center of intellectual and reformist energy — home to Ralph Waldo Emerson, the Alcott family, the abolitionist Thoreau family, and the gathering place of the Transcendentalist circle. It was also, and simultaneously, a working agricultural community of farmers, shopkeepers, and laborers.

Thoreau's relationship to the village was complex: he was born there, lived most of his life there, and died there — yet *Walden* is, in large part, a **sustained critique** of the village's values and assumptions.

## Role in Walden

The village appears in *Walden* primarily as the place Thoreau has left — the "civilized" space against which the woods define themselves. Yet he never severs the connection. He walks into the village regularly, visits his family, hears the gossip, and returns to the pond with his sense of the village's smallness renewed.

The most sustained treatment of the village as an institution appears in Chapter 8, *The Village*, where Thoreau describes his evening walks:

> [008-002] …two young men who had been fishing in the pond. They lived about a mile off, and I often saw them pass. I think that they were attracted thither by the fishing chiefly.

But the chapter quickly shifts from these benign observations to a darker topic: Thoreau's arrest for non-payment of the poll tax, an act of civil disobedience against a government that supported slavery and the Mexican-American War. The village, in this episode, reveals its coercive face:

The village jail, the constable, the tax-gatherer — these are the institutions of Concord as **state apparatus**, and Thoreau's night in jail becomes a defining moment in the history of civil disobedience. The village that nurtured him also imprisoned him.

Thoreau's economic critique of the village is no less sharp. In *Economy*, he itemizes the costs that trap his townsmen in lives of "quiet desperation":

> [001-066] The wealthy and principal men in New England, in the country, are the farmers who own their farms, and they are commonly poorer than the day-laborers who work for them.

The village's economy, built on land ownership and inheritance, produces a class of nominally wealthy farmers who are, in Thoreau's analysis, the poorest of all — because their property owns them. He contrasts this with his own experiment: a cabin that cost $28.12½ to build, leaving him free to think.

Yet the village is not merely a target. Thoreau's tone toward it is often **affectionate**, even tender. He knows every path, every cellar-hole, every inhabitant. The village is his home, and his critique is the critique of a native, not a tourist.

## Structure

Concord's institutional structure in the 1840s included:

- **Town Meeting**: The traditional New England form of direct democracy, in which all adult male property-holders could vote on town affairs. Thoreau participated in Town Meeting and respected its deliberative character even as he chafed at majority rule.
- **First Parish Church**: The Unitarian congregation where Emerson had served as minister (1829–1832) and where Thoreau's mother was a member. Thoreau himself stopped attending services, a decision that scandalized some of his neighbors.
- **Concord Lyceum**: The lecture society where Thoreau, Emerson, and many visiting speakers addressed the town on topics ranging from natural history to abolition. Thoreau's early drafts of *Walden* were tested as Lyceum lectures.
- **The Town Farm / Poor House**: An institution Thoreau mentions in *Economy* as evidence of the village's failure to provide for its own.
