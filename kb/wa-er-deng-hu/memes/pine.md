# Pine

## Definition

The pine is the dominant tree of the Walden woods and the most frequently named tree species in Thoreau's text. Two varieties appear: the **white pine** (*Pinus strobus*), tall and arrowy, prized as timber; and the **pitch pine** (*Pinus rigida*), sturdier and more gnarled, capable of sending new shoots from a burned stump. Together they form the sylvan architecture of Thoreau's world — the pines are the walls of his outdoor chamber, the fuel of his hearth, and the companions of his solitude. More than botanical specimens, the pines are characters in the Walden narrative: they creak, rub, hum, and stand as silent witnesses to the human drama unfolding beneath their boughs.

## In Walden

The pines announce themselves early in *Walden*, in the catalogue of Concord's native products: "much ice and pine timber and a little granite" (001-039). Pine timber is the town's economic reality, but for Thoreau the pine is more than a commodity. When he describes "some tall, arrowy white pines, still in their youth, for timber" (001-069), the adjective *arrowy* captures the white pine's distinctive form — it ascends straight and swift, a living shaft pointing skyward, already marked for the sawyer's blade even as Thoreau admires its form.

Building his house, Thoreau works among the pines. He sits "at noon, sitting amid the green pine boughs which I had cut off" (001-077), and his dwelling is nestled into a young forest "of pitch pines and hickories" (004-004). The pines are his nearest neighbors. A thrasher perches "restless on the white-pine boughs behind my house" (004-005), and the "sturdy pitch pines" rub and creak "against the shingles for want of room, their roots reaching quite under the house" (004-036). The pines are not merely around the house — they press against it, their roots reaching beneath the foundation, their boughs brushing the roof. The boundary between dwelling and woods is permeable.

The pine woods behind Thoreau's house serve as his reception room for visitors. "Thither in summer days, when distinguished guests came, I took them" (006-004). The pine grove is a place of hospitality more generous than any parlor — a shaded chamber where "the sun rarely fell" and where conversation could unfold without the constraint of walls.

Thoreau's regard for the pine deepens into reverence in "The Ponds," where he describes Walden as "fringed with pine woods" (005-019). The pines are the pond's setting, the dark green frame around its blue eye. In several passages, the pine needles become instruments of sound: "a certain vibratory hum, as if the pine needles in the horizon were the strings of a harp which it swept" (004-029). The wind across the pine needles produces a music that Thoreau hears as nature's own accompaniment to his work — a sound that blurs the line between instrument and landscape.

> [001-039] The principal native products of the town, as far as I can learn, are much ice and pine timber and a little granite, always in native hands.

> [001-077] I was so much pleased with the place that I frequently rested at noon, sitting amid the green pine boughs which I had cut off, and read my paper.

> [004-004] I could look upward through the hoops to a roof composed of the smoke of burning stumps, the vapor of boiling sap, and the mist of a young forest of pitch pines and hickories, and half a dozen rods from its door a kettle of water was beginning to sing over a fire of stumps and logs.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's feeling for the pine is both aesthetic and philosophical. The pine's verticality — the arrowy white pine pointing skyward — embodies aspiration, the upward reach of the spirit. But the pitch pine's stubbornness — its ability to regenerate from a burned stump, to persist where other trees would die — embodies endurance, the quiet resistance of the wild to all attempts at domestication. "The pines still stand here older than I," Thoreau writes (007-002), acknowledging the trees as a presence that predates and will outlast his own tenancy. The pine is a teacher of patience: it grows slowly, stands silently, and asks nothing of the human observer except attention.
