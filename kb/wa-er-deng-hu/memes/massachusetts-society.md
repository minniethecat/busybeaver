# Massachusetts Society

## Overview

"Massachusetts Society" in *Walden* is not a formal organization but the **totality of social arrangements** that Thoreau subjects to critique: the market economy, the state with its taxes and wars, the church with its Sabbath decorum, the press with its gossip and trivia, and the unspoken code of respectability that governs — and, in Thoreau's view, imprisons — the lives of his neighbors. It is, in short, the 19th-century New England social order, examined and found wanting.

Thoreau's opening salvo in *Economy* establishes the stakes:

> [001-057] "The false society of men—"

The fragment, drawn from a verse Thoreau quotes, announces a theme that will run throughout the book: the society into which we are born is not a given, not a neutral medium, but a **fabrication** — and a false one at that — whose claims on our loyalty must be examined rather than assumed.

## Role in Walden

Thoreau's critique of society operates on multiple fronts:

**Economic.** The market economy, centered in Boston but reaching into every Concord household, demands that men spend their lives working to acquire things they do not need:

> [001-041] …in this state of society, to cover nakedness, and he must be clothed, not with the skin of animals merely, but with the woven garments of distant nations.

The phrase "in this state of society" is crucial: Thoreau insists that our needs are not natural but socially constructed. A human being "needs" clothing, but only "in this state of society" does he need the particular clothing — fashionable, imported, expensive — that the market insists upon.

**Political.** The State, represented by the tax-gatherer and the constable, demands allegiance and payment. Thoreau's night in the Concord jail for refusing the poll tax is his most famous act of civil disobedience, and it crystallizes his position: the individual conscience outranks the state's demand:

> [008-003] It is true, I might have resisted forcibly with more or less effect, might have run "amok" against society; but I preferred that society should run "amok" against me, it being the desperate party.

The sarcasm is sharp — "I preferred that society should run amok against me" — and the logic is radical: the individual who refuses to participate in an unjust system is not the aggressor; the system that coerces him is.

**Social.** The web of visiting, gossip, and social expectation that binds Concord together is, for Thoreau, a form of **emotional taxation**. In *Solitude*, he writes:

> [005-013] Can we not do without the society of our gossips a little while, under the pretense of needing their society?

The question is rhetorical. Thoreau's answer is that we can — and must — learn to do without the constant presence of others, because solitude is the condition of self-knowledge.

Yet, characteristically, Thoreau complicates his own position. In *Visitors*, he acknowledges:

> [006-001] I think that I love society as much as most, and am ready enough to fasten myself like a bloodsucker for the time to any full-blooded man that comes in my way.

The confession is startling in its intensity — Thoreau "loves society as much as most" and clings to visitors "like a bloodsucker." The hermit of Walden Pond is also a man who craves human connection. His critique of society is not a rejection of human warmth but a rejection of the **forms** — the conventions, the small talk, the obligatory visiting — that substitute for genuine encounter.

**Intellectual.** The press, the lecture circuit, the "gossip of Boston" — these constitute what Thoreau calls, in *Reading*, the "trivial" culture that distracts from great books and permanent things:

> [003-012] …why not skip the gossip of Boston and take the best newspaper in the world, the classics?

The "best newspaper in the world" is Homer, not the *Boston Daily Advertiser*. Thoreau's point is that we have limited attention, and the society around us is engineered to capture and waste it.

## Significance

Thoreau's critique of Massachusetts society is the **negative pole** of his philosophy — the diagnosis that makes his positive vision of deliberate living necessary. Without "the false society of men," there would be no need to go to the woods. Walden Pond is not an escape *from* society but an experiment in whether a different way of living is possible — and the answer, for Thoreau, is yes.
