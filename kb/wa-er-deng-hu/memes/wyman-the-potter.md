# Wyman the Potter

A potter who lived near Walden Pond, recorded by Thoreau in the Former Inhabitants chapter as one of the previous occupants of the land.

## Connection to Walden

Wyman was one of the former inhabitants whose traces Thoreau encountered in the woods around Walden Pond. His abandoned tenement later housed Hugh Quoil, and the site of his dwelling was near enough to Thoreau's bean-field to be part of his daily awareness. Wyman represents the layer of artisan-squatters who occupied the Walden margins before Thoreau's experiment.

> [014-010] Farther up the hill, on the road which approaches nearest to the pond, Wyman the potter squatted, and furnished the townsfolk with earthenware, and left descendants to succeed him. They were not rich in worldly goods, and while the land was in their possession they held it by sufferance.

> [014-011] Here, by the very corner of my field, still nearer to town, an Irishman, Hugh Quoil (if I have spelt his name with coil enough,) who occupied Wyman's tenement,—Col. Quoil, he was called.

## Significance

Wyman the potter belongs to the economic underclass of Concord—those who "held [land] by sufferance" rather than by deed. As a potter, he provided useful goods to the community yet remained marginal to it. The detail that he "left descendants to succeed him" adds a generational dimension to Thoreau's archaeology of the Walden landscape—Wyman is not isolated but part of a family line, albeit one that has since vanished from the land. His tenement's reuse by Hugh Quoil illustrates Thoreau's theme of transient habitation: one person's abandoned home becomes another's shelter, until the forest reclaims all.
