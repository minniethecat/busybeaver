# Worship

## Definition

Worship in *Walden* undergoes a radical redefinition. For Thoreau, worship is not the Sunday-morning ritual conducted in a wooden meetinghouse but the continuous orientation of the self toward what is highest — in nature, in consciousness, in the moral life. The woods are the temple; the pond is the baptismal font; and the act of living deliberately, with full attention to the present moment, is the liturgy. To worship, in Thoreau's lexicon, is to stand in right relation to the sacred, and the sacred is everywhere — in the bean-field, in the thawing sandbank, in the "morning star" of one's own awakening consciousness.

## In Walden

Thoreau's redefinition of worship is announced quietly in "Economy." Describing the essentials of life — food, shelter, clothing, fuel — he remarks with characteristic dryness: "I am sure, that if my coat and shoes are fit to worship God in, they will do" (001-042). The comment is double-edged: it mocks the sartorial anxieties of the churchgoer while affirming that true worship requires no special garment. A coat that keeps out the cold is a coat fit for encountering the divine. The sacred requires no costume, only presence.

The same passage expands the critique: "We worship not the Graces, nor the Parcae, but Fashion. She spins and weaves and cuts with full authority" (001-044). The conventional church-goer who worries about a suitable coat is not worshipping God at all — he is worshipping Fashion, the false goddess who dictates what must be worn and owned. Thoreau's catalog of the householder's burdens is, at bottom, an exposé of idolatry: the village worships at the altar of respectable appearance, while the one thing needful — the awakened soul — goes unattended.

The positive alternative emerges in "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," where Thoreau declares: "I have been as sincere a worshipper of Aurora as the Greeks" (002-021). Aurora — the dawn — is the object of genuine worship, and Thoreau's devotion to the morning is not metaphorical. He rises early, bathes in the pond, and greets the sun as the Greeks greeted their goddess. This is worship without priest or scripture, grounded in direct experience of natural beauty. The dawn does not demand belief; it demands attention, which is the truer form of reverence.

In "Baker Farm," Thoreau imagines a religion so naturalized that the groves themselves become sacred architecture: "Men would have forsaken their oaks to worship in them" (010-001). The sentence inverts the expected order: the oaks are not the objects of worship but its architecture, the pillars and vaults of a living cathedral. Thoreau envisions a worship so integrated with the natural world that no special building is needed — the pine grove is as holy as any consecrated nave.

The most personal account of worship appears in "Higher Laws." Thoreau describes the angler who, "after a style purely his own," offers his body as sacrifice "to the god he worships" (011-025). The fisherman's devotion — his absorption in the act, his attunement to water and weather — is a form of worship more authentic than any recited creed. Thoreau does not fish often (he gave it up as he grew older, finding it inconsistent with his evolving sense of the sanctity of animal life), but he recognizes the fisherman's piety for what it is: a state of total presence to the moment, which is the essence of worship.

Throughout *Walden*, Thoreau practices what might be called the worship of attention. "We must learn to reawaken and keep ourselves awake," he writes, "not by mechanical aids, but by an infinite expectation of the dawn" (002-021). The expectation of dawn — the daily discipline of looking for light — is worship reduced to its simplest, most portable form. It requires no institution, no creed, no special garment, no special day. It requires only the willingness to be present, to pay attention, to be astonished by what is already there.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau was steeped in the religious language of his New England upbringing — his prose is full of biblical cadences and allusions — but his worship is post-ecclesiastical. He does not reject religion; he relocates it. The meetinghouse is exchanged for the pine grove, the sermon for the song of the wood thrush, the communion wafer for the unleavened bread baked in the ashes of his own hearth. This is not secularism — Thoreau's world is saturated with the sacred — but a sacramentalism so thorough that it dissolves the distinction between the religious and the ordinary. Every act, performed with full attention, becomes an act of worship. Every morning, greeted with expectation, becomes a liturgy.
