## Definition

Ancestor in Walden carries a dual meaning: on one hand, the literal forebears whose customs, houses, and trades are inherited as unexamined obligation; on the other, the "primitive ancestor" — a, pre-civilized self that still survives within each person and represents instinctual vitality. Thoreau seeks neither to revere nor to reject ancestors wholesale, but to distinguish what is living from what is dead in the inheritance they bequeath.

## In Walden

Thoreau's treatment of ancestors appears most forcefully in "Economy," where the question of tradition becomes the question of freedom itself. "How many a poor immortal soul have I met well nigh crushed and smothered" under the weight of inherited circumstances — the farm, the house, the barn, the cattle — the "inherited encumbrances" that ancestors pass down as a curse disguised as a gift (001-049). The "portionless" are fortunate precisely because they "struggle with no such unnecessary inherited encumbrances" — they begin life unburdened by ancestral expectation (001-052).

Yet Thoreau does not simply discard ancestors. In a striking formulation, he speaks of "that portion of our most primitive ancestor which still survived in us" (001-049) — the wild, unencumbered self that civilization has not yet tamed. This primitive ancestor is not a burden but a resource: the instinctual wisdom that knows how to live simply, build shelter, find food, and attend to the present moment without anxiety for the future.

Later, in "Solitude," Thoreau extends the ancestral metaphor to the natural world. The pond itself has ancestors: "our Saxon and Norman ancestors" who knew a wilder landscape, an older forest (013-014). Even the fish that ancient settlers fought for connect the present to a deeper past (012-012). Ancestors appear not as biography but as geology — layers beneath the present surface. And in "The Bean-Field," Thoreau notes dryly that "sacrifices and oblations to their ancestors" were an "ocean of subtile intelligence" — the ancient world's wisdom practices, however strange to modern eyes (005-012).

## Perspective

Thoreau's innovation is to treat ancestry as a choice, not a fate. The "primitive ancestor" within can be cultivated or suppressed; ancestral custom can be questioned and discarded. "Why should we be in such desperate haste to succeed and in such desperate enterprises?" he asks — because ancestors did? His answer is a quiet refusal. The true ancestor worth honoring is not the one who accumulated property but the one who lived deliberately, who "fronted the essentials of life." That ancestor Thoreau claims as his own — not by blood but by spiritual election.
