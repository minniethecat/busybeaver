# Jesus Christ

Central figure of Christianity, referenced by Thoreau in Walden as an example of moral and spiritual authority, alongside other ancient wisdom traditions.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau references Christ and Christianity several times in Walden, typically in a comparative context—placing Christian teachings alongside those of the Vedas, Zoroaster, and the classical philosophers. His tone is respectful but syncretic; he treats Christ as one of the great moral teachers of humanity rather than a uniquely divine figure, reflecting the Transcendentalist tendency to seek universal spiritual truths across traditions.

> [003-011] It is true, we are such poor historians that we think it necessary to make a free-stone monument of our hero, and a New England village of the next generation of men. Every child begins the world again, to some extent, and loves to stay out of doors, even in wet and cold. It plays house, as well as horse, having an instinct for it. Who does not remember the interest with which, when young, he looked at shelving rocks, or any approach to a cave? It was the natural yearning of that portion of our most primitive ancestor which still survived in us. I see not but we might make as good a use of the materials which the earth affords without the formality of a deed. None can be an impartial or wise observer of the human race but from the vantage ground of what *we* should call involuntary poverty. I would not have any one adopt my mode of living on any account; for, beside that before he has fairly learned it I may have found out another for myself, I desire that there may be as many different persons in the world as possible; but I would have each one be very careful to find out and pursue *his own* way, and not his father's or his mother's or his neighbor's instead. The youth may build or plant or sail, only let him not be hindered from doing that which he tells me he would like to do.

> [005-003] But the only true America is that country where you are at liberty to pursue such a mode of life as may enable you to do without these, and where the state does not endeavor to compel you to sustain the slavery and war and other superfluous expenses which directly or indirectly result from the use of such things. For I purposely talk to him as if he were a philosopher, or desired to be one. It was his peculiar doctrine that a man had a perfect right to live by the work of his hands, and he was bound to give an account of himself to nobody.

## Biography

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BCE–c. 30 CE) was a first-century Jewish preacher and religious leader whose teachings form the basis of Christianity. The Gospels of the New Testament record his sayings and parables. In Thoreau's New England, Christianity was the dominant religious framework, but Thoreau—like other Transcendentalists—sought spiritual wisdom beyond Christian orthodoxy, turning also to Hindu, Buddhist, and classical sources.

## Significance

Christ appears in Walden not as a dogmatic figure but as part of Thoreau's broader argument for deliberate, morally examined living. Thoreau's references to Christ and Christianity are often tinged with critique of institutional religion, which he saw as having departed from the simplicity and directness of original spiritual insight. The "church" Thoreau imagines is one that would welcome "all the worthies, with Jesus Christ himself," alongside Zoroaster and other sages.
