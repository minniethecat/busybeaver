# Simplicity

Simplicity is the central philosophical theme of Henry David Thoreau's *Walden* (1854). Thoreau advocates for reducing life to its essentials as a path to spiritual clarity and genuine freedom.

## Core Philosophy

Thoreau presents simplicity not as deprivation but as a deliberate choice to focus on what is essential. By stripping away material complexity, one gains access to deeper truths.

> I wanted to live deep and suck out all the marrow of life, to live so sturdily and Spartan-like as to put to rout all that was not life... (002-021)

The pursuit of simplicity is intimately connected to Thoreau's experiment at Walden Pond, where he lived alone for two years in a self-built cabin.

## Key Passages

### The Call to Simplify

Thoreau's most famous exhortation on simplicity comes in "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For":

> Simplicity, simplicity, simplicity! I say, let your affairs be as two or three, and not a hundred or a thousand; instead of a million count half a dozen, and keep your accounts on your thumb-nail. (002-024)

### Simplicity as Innocence

Thoreau links simplicity with moral and spiritual qualities:

> ...to make my life of equal simplicity, and I may say innocence, with Nature herself. (002-021)

### The Nakedness of Life

In the opening chapter "Economy," Thoreau reflects on the inherent simplicity of human existence:

> The very simplicity and nakedness of man's life in the primitive ages may imply this advantage, at least, that they left him still but a sojourner in nature. (001-065)

## Related Virtues

Thoreau groups simplicity with other cardinal virtues throughout the text. In "The Bean-Field," he lists:

> ...sincerity, truth, simplicity, faith, innocence, and the like... (007-035)
