# News / Media

## Definition

News and media in *Walden* represent the stream of trivial, ephemeral information that Thoreau believes distracts individuals from meaningful reflection and genuine knowledge. He targets the newspaper, the post-office, and the rapid circulation of gossip as symptoms of a culture that mistakes novelty for wisdom.

For Thoreau, the relentless consumption of news is a form of intellectual dissipation that leaves no room for engagement with the permanent and the profound.

## In Walden

Thoreau's critique of news is concentrated in "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For" (002), where he argues that the hurry to consume information is a symptom of spiritual poverty:

> [002-025] Why should we live with such hurry and waste of life? We are determined to be starved before we are hungry. Men say that a stitch in time saves nine, and so they take a thousand stitches to-day to save nine to-morrow. As for work, we haven't any of any consequence.

He goes on to indict the post-office and the newspaper as institutions that deliver almost nothing of genuine importance:

> [002-026] For my part, I could easily do without the post-office. I think that there are very few important communications made through it. To speak critically, I never received more than one or two letters in my life — I wrote this some years ago — that were worth the postage. The penny-post is, commonly, an institution through which you seriously offer a man that penny for his thoughts which is so often safely offered in jest.

In "Reading" (003), Thoreau extends his critique to the reading habits of his contemporaries, lamenting that people consume newspapers rather than classical literature. He argues that reading should be a "noble intellectual exercise" (003-007), not a passive consumption of the day's incidents:

> [003-008] Most men are satisfied if they read or hear read, and perchance have been convicted by the wisdom of one good book, the Bible, and for the rest of their lives vegetate and dissipate their faculties in what is called easy reading.

Thoreau also satirizes the hunger for news in "The Village" (008), noting how gossip circulates "from mouth to mouth, or from newspaper to newspaper" (008-001), forming a ceaseless circulation of insignificant detail.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's dismissal of news is not anti-intellectual but radically selective. He believes that human life is too short to be spent on information forgotten within a day.

His alternative is "true books in a true spirit" (003-003) — classical works that address permanent questions of existence. News actively competes with and displaces the serious reading that genuine wisdom requires. His stance anticipates modern critiques of information overload, rooted in a transcendentalist conviction that truth is timeless, not time-bound.
