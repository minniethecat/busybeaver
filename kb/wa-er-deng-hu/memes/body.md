# Body

## Definition

The body in *Walden* is the irreducible ground of Thoreau's philosophy. Before any question of spirit or soul, there is the body that hungers, works, bathes, and "imbibes delight through every pore" (005-001). Thoreau refuses the transcendentalist temptation to bypass the physical: "every man is the builder of a temple, called his body," and the quality of the temple reflects the quality of the builder. The body is not the prison of the soul but its material expression — the stove that must be properly fueled, the house that must be properly tended.

## In Walden

Thoreau opens his inquiry into the body in "Economy" with a striking scientific metaphor. Citing the chemist Liebig, he writes that "man's body is a stove, and food the fuel which keeps up the internal combustion in the lungs" (001-023). This is not reductive materialism — Thoreau immediately distinguishes "vital heat" from mere fire — but it establishes the body as a system of exchanges with the material world. Food, shelter, clothing, and fuel are "the necessaries of life" precisely because the body requires them. To neglect the body's economy is to be unable to "entertain the true problems of life with freedom."

> [001-023] According to Liebig, man's body is a stove, and food the fuel which keeps up the internal combustion in the lungs. In cold weather we eat more, in warm less. The animal heat is the result of a slow combustion, and disease and death take place when this is too rapid; or for want of fuel, or from some defect in the draught, the fire goes out.

"Solitude" opens with the body at its most exalted. "This is a delicious evening, when the whole body is one sense, and imbibes delight through every pore" (005-001). The body is not a burden to be transcended but a sensorium to be awakened. Walking the stony shore of the pond in his shirt sleeves on a cool, cloudy evening, Thoreau experiences "a strange liberty in Nature, a part of herself." The body, properly attuned, dissolves the boundary between self and world.

In "Visitors," Thoreau gives us his most detailed portrait of a human body — the Canadian woodchopper, whose "stout but sluggish body, yet gracefully carried" (006-017) embodies a kind of pre-philosophical contentment. The woodchopper is described almost entirely in physical terms: "a thick sunburnt neck, dark bushy hair, and dull sleepy blue eyes, which were occasionally lit up with expression" (006-017). His body is his philosophy — he "was never tired in his life" — and his limitation. "In him the animal man chiefly was developed" (006-020); the intellectual and spiritual man "were slumbering as in an infant." Nature "gave him a strong body and contentment for his portion" (006-020), but no hunger for what lies beyond contentment.

> [006-020] When Nature made him, she gave him a strong body and contentment for his portion, and propped him on every side with reverence and reliance, that he might live out his threescore years and ten a child.

The body's strange autonomy appears in "The Village," where Thoreau describes walking home in the dark: "I have thought that perhaps my body would find its way home if its master should forsake it, as the hand finds its way to the mouth without assistance" (008-002). The body knows paths the mind cannot remember — an observation that is at once practical and deeply philosophical. Consciousness is not the body's only guide.

In "Higher Laws," Thoreau confronts the body's moral dimension. The body must be fed, but "this, I think, is to be fed when we feed the body; they should both sit down at the same table" (011-009). The body and the spirit cannot be nourished separately. The carnivorous appetite troubles Thoreau not because the body is sinful but because it is capable of refinement: "it is a part of the destiny of the human race, in its gradual improvement, to leave off eating animals" (011-009). The body is a project, not a fixed fact.

> [011-009] It is hard to provide and cook so simple and clean a diet as will not offend the imagination; but this, I think, is to be fed when we feed the body; they should both sit down at the same table.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's philosophy of the body is integrative: the body is not to be mortified or transcended but disciplined and awakened. From the "stove" of Liebig's physiology to the "temple" of spiritual metaphor, the body is a site of continuous transformation. The goal is neither the woodchopper's unreflective animal contentment nor the ascetic's denial of the flesh, but the "whole body" that "is one sense" — fully alive, fully governed, fully present to the world it inhabits.
