# Woodchuck

## Definition

The woodchuck (*Marmota monax*) is one of the most symbolically charged animals in *Walden*. Thoreau encounters it not as a distant observer but as a fellow inhabitant of the wooded margins — the woodchuck digs its burrow in the same hillside where Thoreau digs his cellar, raids his bean-field, and crosses his path at twilight. The animal embodies the wild, uncultivated nature that Thoreau both admires and, in moments of "savage delight," is tempted to consume. The woodchuck thus becomes a test case for Thoreau's philosophy of the relationship between the human animal and its wild kin.

## In Walden

The woodchuck's presence is established at the very foundation of Thoreau's dwelling. Digging his cellar, Thoreau notes: "I dug my cellar in the side of a hill sloping to the south, where a woodchuck had formerly dug his burrow" (001-080). The human habitation is built upon — and in continuity with — the animal's prior claim to the site. Thoreau and the woodchuck are, in a sense, neighbors who share the same hillside, each burrowing into the earth for shelter. The cellar, descended through "sumach and blackberry roots, and the lowest stain of vegetation, to a fine sand where potatoes would not freeze in any winter," follows the woodchuck's own instinct for insulation and depth.

In the bean-field, the woodchuck appears as a rival. Thoreau rises before dawn to hoe his beans, noting that he begins "before yet any woodchuck or squirrel had run across the road" (007-004). The woodchuck's emergence marks the boundary between Thoreau's pre-dawn solitude and the day's fuller animal activity. The bean-field raid — implicit in Thoreau's mention of the woodchuck's presence near his crops — casts the animal as a competitor for the harvest, yet Thoreau never describes killing or driving it away. His tolerance of the woodchuck's depredations is consistent with his broader philosophy of minimal intervention.

The most dramatic woodchuck encounter occurs in *Higher Laws*. Returning home through the woods at dusk, Thoreau confesses: "I caught a glimpse of a woodchuck stealing across my path, and felt a strange thrill of savage delight, and was strongly tempted to seize and devour him raw; not that I was hungry then, except for that wildness which he represented" (011-001). This moment crystallizes Thoreau's ambivalence toward wild nature. The urge to consume the woodchuck is not hunger but a desire to incorporate wildness itself — to take into the self what the animal embodies. The "savage delight" is a recognition of kinship across the species boundary, a pull toward the pre-civilized state that Thoreau's philosophy simultaneously admires and seeks to transcend.

The woodchuck also figures in the broader ecology of the Walden woods. Thoreau observes village dogs "ineffectually smelled at old fox burrows and woodchucks' holes" (012-015), the domesticated animal failing to penetrate the wild creature's subterranean refuge. The woodchuck's burrow, like Thoreau's cellar, is a place of withdrawal and safety — a space that civilization's representatives cannot enter.

> [001-080] I dug my cellar in the side of a hill sloping to the south, where a woodchuck had formerly dug his burrow, down through sumach and blackberry roots, and the lowest stain of vegetation, six feet square by seven deep, to a fine sand where potatoes would not freeze in any winter.

> [007-004] Before yet any woodchuck or squirrel had run across the road, or the sun had got above the shrub oaks, while all the dew was on, though the farmers warned me against it,—I would advise you to do all your work if possible while the dew is on,—I began to level the ranks of haughty weeds in my bean-field.

> [011-001] As I came home through the woods with my string of fish, trailing my pole, it being now quite dark, I caught a glimpse of a woodchuck stealing across my path, and felt a strange thrill of savage delight, and was strongly tempted to seize and devour him raw; not that I was hungry then, except for that wildness which he represented.

## Thoreau's Perspective

The woodchuck encounter in *Higher Laws* is the most visceral expression of Thoreau's philosophy of the "wild" and the "higher." The "strange thrill of savage delight" is an honest admission that the human animal still responds to wildness with predatory instinct. Thoreau does not deny this instinct or claim to have overcome it. Instead, he acknowledges it as a component of human nature — the "animal in us, which awakens in proportion as our higher nature slumbers" (011-014). The woodchuck becomes a mirror: to see it is to see one's own wildness, and the temptation to devour it is the temptation to reclaim a lost animal vitality. Thoreau's restraint — he does not actually seize and eat the woodchuck — marks the distance between instinct and philosophy, between the "savage" and the "deliberate."

## Historical Context

The woodchuck was a familiar presence in nineteenth-century New England farmland, widely regarded as a pest by farmers whose crops it damaged. Thoreau's refusal to treat the woodchuck as vermin — his willingness instead to see it as a fellow creature worthy of philosophical reflection — distinguishes his writing from the agricultural manuals and practical literature of his time. His woodchuck is not a problem to be solved but a neighbor to be contemplated.
