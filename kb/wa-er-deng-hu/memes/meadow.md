# Meadow

## Definition

The meadow in *Walden* is a landscape of transition. Neither forest nor field, neither water nor dry land, it occupies the fertile margin where the Concord River floods and recedes. Thoreau's meadows — the Sudbury meadows visible from his hill-top, the cranberry meadows raked by commerce, the river meadows where muskrats colonize — are places of abundance and ambiguity. They yield berries and hay but also conceal bog-holes; they flood in spring and crack with frost in winter. The meadow is Thoreau's most liminal landscape: productive but not domesticated, wet but not aquatic, a corridor between the human and the wild.

## In Walden

Thoreau's view from his hill-top takes in the meadows to the south. "From a hill top near by, where the wood had been recently cut off, there was a pleasing vista southward toward the Sudbury meadows, which in time of flood I distinguished as a higher water" (002-013). The meadows, when flooded, become a second lake — an inland sea that blurs the distinction between land and water, field and pond. The flood is the meadow's seasonal transformation, a reminder that the solid ground is only provisionally solid.

The meadow is also Thoreau's foraging ground. In autumn he goes "a-graping to the river meadows, and loaded myself with clusters more precious for their beauty and fragrance than for food" (013-001), the wild grapes of the meadow yielding not sustenance but delight. The cranberry meadows are stripped by commerce: "All the Indian huckleberry hills are stripped, all the cranberry meadows are raked into the city" (004-010), Thoreau laments. The city's appetite reaches even here, to the boggy margins where wild fruit grows. The meadow is not remote enough to escape the market.

Yet the meadow also resists domestication. A colony of muskrats inhabits "the river meadows" (008-001), building their lodges and going about their lives indifferent to human claims of ownership. The meadow mouse, Thoreau notes, lives "as snug as a meadow mouse" (014-016), a model of domestic comfort achieved without architecture, without mortgage, without the burdens of human housekeeping. The meadow is home to creatures who have solved the problem of dwelling more elegantly than we have.

Thoreau's own relationship to the meadow is practical as well as contemplative. He observes a farmer "turning up a meadow with a spade or bog hoe" (010-015), and the image is of labor that transforms landscape — turning meadow into cropland, wild into cultivated. But the meadow resists: "there was nothing to redeem it but the fact that the pond had risen" (009-039), Thoreau writes of an area near Walden. The pond's rise reclaims the meadow for water, undoing the farmer's work. The cycle of flood and recession is older than agriculture and will outlast it.

The meadow in winter becomes something else entirely. The frost works on it — "when the frost comes out in the spring, and even in a thawing day in the winter, the sand begins to flow down the slopes like lava" (017-006) — and the meadow's surface cracks and heaves. The meadow, like the pond, is seasonally re-made, its contours softened and re-sculpted by frost and thaw.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau values the meadow precisely for what it is not: it is not the dark, enclosed forest; it is not the open, exposed field; it is not the deep, reflective pond. It is a landscape of partial illumination, of half-wild and half-tame, and this ambiguity suits Thoreau's own sensibility. He is drawn to edges and margins, to places that resist clear classification, and the meadow — sometimes water, sometimes land, sometimes pasture, sometimes wilderness — is the landscape that best embodies this resistance.

The meadow also embodies Thoreau's economy of attention. It yields wild fruit (grapes, cranberries) that are free for the taking, and the taking is a form of harvest that requires no planting, no weeding, no ownership. The meadow's abundance is a gift, and Thoreau receives it as such — a model of the right relationship to nature's provision.
