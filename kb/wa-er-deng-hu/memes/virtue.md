# Virtue

Virtue is an important moral concept in *Walden*. Thoreau treats virtue not as social conformity but as authentic goodness rooted in simplicity and truth.

## Definition

Thoreau sees virtue as inseparable from simplicity. True virtue needs no ornament:

> Our best virtue has for its occasion a superfluous and evitable wretchedness. (002-024)

## In Walden

Honesty and related virtues are qualities Thoreau admires in simple, unpretentious people:

> He believed in honesty and the like virtues. (006-022)

Thoreau uses agricultural metaphor to describe how virtue enriches character:

> ...the salt, power, or virtue (call it either) which gives them their savor... (007-011)

Virtue is closely linked with civic life:

> Love virtue, and the people will be virtuous. (008-008)

## Historical Context

Thoreau's concept of virtue draws from both classical and Transcendentalist traditions. Like Emerson, he emphasizes self-trust and inner moral conviction over external authority.
