# Virgil

Roman poet (70–19 BCE), author of the Aeneid and Georgics, cited by Thoreau in Walden alongside Homer as an exemplar of classical literary achievement.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau mentions Virgil twice in Walden. In the Reading chapter, Virgil appears alongside Homer and Æschylus as one of the classical writers whose works have never been adequately translated into English—a claim Thoreau uses to argue for reading the classics in their original languages. In the Bean-Field chapter, Thoreau invokes Virgil's *Georgics*, the classical poem of agriculture, humorously applying Virgil's advice to his own bean cultivation.

> [003-006] Homer has never yet been printed in English, nor Æschylus, nor Virgil even—works as refined, as solidly done, and as beautiful almost as the morning itself.

> [007-007] I came to love my beans, though so many more than I wanted. They attached me to the earth, and so I got strength like Antæus. But why should I raise them? Only Heaven knows. This was my curious labor all summer—to make this portion of the earth's surface, which had yielded only cinquefoil, blackberries, johnswort, and the like, before, sweet wild fruits and pleasant flowers, produce instead this pulse. What shall I learn of beans or beans of me? I cherish them, I hoe them, early and late I have an eye to them; and this is my day's work. It is a fine broad leaf to look on. My auxiliaries are the dews and rains which water this dry soil, and what fertility is in the soil itself, which for the most part is lean and effete. My enemies are worms, cool days, and most of all woodchucks. The last have nibbled for me a quarter of an acre clean. But what right had I to oust johnswort and the rest, and break up their ancient herb garden? Soon, however, the remaining beans will be too tough for them, and go forward to meet new foes. When I was four years old, as I remember, I was brought from Boston to this my native town, through these very woods and this field, to the pond. That woodland vision for a long time made the drapery of my dreams, and my most distinct remembrance yet dates from that earliest look at the country. Here is a myrtle, a citrus—a black birch, yellow birch, beech, and hornbeam. I know the aspect of each of these trees, and I know the taste of the juneberries and the checkerberries. I love to see the woodchucks' half-eaten beans on the ground after a less quiet night than usual.

## Biography

Publius Vergilius Maro (70–19 BCE) was the greatest poet of Augustan Rome. His three major works—the *Eclogues*, *Georgics*, and *Aeneid*—established him as the central figure of Latin literature. The *Georgics*, a poem about agriculture and rural life, was particularly relevant to Thoreau's own agricultural experiments at Walden. Virgil was part of Thoreau's classical education at Harvard, where students read his works in the original Latin.

## Significance

Virgil represents for Thoreau the classical ideal of literature that repays a lifetime of study. The claim that Virgil has "never yet been printed in English" is characteristic Thoreauvian hyperbole—his point being that translation can never capture what makes a classic work great. The reference to Virgil's agricultural advice in the Bean-Field chapter playfully connects Thoreau's bean-growing experiment to the grand classical tradition of agricultural poetry, elevating humble labor through literary allusion.
