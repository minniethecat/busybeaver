# Morning

## Definition

In *Walden*, morning is far more than a time of day — it is the supreme metaphor for **spiritual awakening**, the daily opportunity to renew one's conscious participation in life. Thoreau treats morning not as a clock hour but as an interior state: "Morning is when I am awake, and there is a dawn in me" (002-021). To be truly awake is to be morally and spiritually alive; to sleep through morning is to miss the very purpose of existence.

## In Walden

Morning pervades the text as both literal experience and philosophical symbol. Thoreau rises early at Walden Pond, bathes in the pond at dawn, and frames his entire experiment in deliberate living around the discipline of early rising.

His most famous articulation of morning as metaphor appears in *Where I Lived, and What I Lived For*:

> [002-021] Every morning was a cheerful invitation to make my life of equal simplicity, and I may say innocence, with Nature herself. I have been as sincere a worshipper of Aurora as the Greeks. I got up early and bathed in the pond; that was a religious exercise, and one of the best things which I did.

The morning bath in Walden Pond is itself a ritual of purification. The physical act mirrors the spiritual: to wash off the night's dullness is to prepare for conscious life:

> [004-002] Sometimes, in a summer morning, having taken my accustomed bath, I sat in my sunny doorway from sunrise till noon, rapt in a revery, amidst the pines and hickories and sumachs, in undisturbed solitude and stillness, while the birds sang around or flitted noiseless through the house, until by the sun falling in at my west window, or the noise of some traveller's wagon on the distant highway, I was reminded of the lapse of time.

Morning also appears as Thoreau's benchmark of moral courage. He invokes an arresting image:

> [004-014] …the three-o'-clock in the morning courage, which Bonaparte thought was the rarest.

This "morning courage" — the willingness to act at the darkest, coldest hour before dawn — becomes the standard by which Thoreau measures true resolve. It is not the courage of battlefield heroics but the quieter courage of living by principle when the world still sleeps.

The passage of the morning train, an intrusion of commerce into the natural soundscape, provokes a different kind of morning reflection:

> [004-012] I watch the passage of the morning cars with the same feeling that I do the rising of the sun, which is hardly more regular. Their train-band of attendants, or smoke, goes before them, and, conforming to the same laws, stretches far behind, and reminds me of the steam which rises from a horse in the morning, both being equally a part of the day's history.

Morning also appears in the context of intellectual life. In *Reading*, Thoreau urges:

> [003-003] …their heroes, and consecrate morning hours to their pages. The hero of the book, who is its author, is as dear a hero as any other, and morning hours are the heroic hours of the day.

Thoreau explicitly links morning to spring itself — both are seasons of beginning:

> [003-011] …would be more salutary than the morning or the spring to our lives, and would put a new aspect on the face of things.

The image of the sun dispersing mist closes a morning meditation in *Sounds*:

> [004-035] …until the sun disperses the morning mist, and only the patriarch of the village is left standing, the rest having long since dispersed to their several tasks.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau radically redefines morning. For him, it is **not a given** — the sun rises, but one may sleep through it in the deeper sense. His famous passage in *Where I Lived* makes this explicit:

> [002-021] Morning is when I am awake, and there is a dawn in me. Moral reform is the effort to throw off sleep. … To be awake is to be alive. I have never yet met a man who was quite awake. How could I have looked him in the face?

This is Thoreau's most radical claim: that most people, most of the time, are **not truly awake**. They go through the motions of life — working, eating, conversing — but their moral and spiritual faculties slumber. Morning, in Thoreau's lexicon, is the *effort* to wake up, not the automatic consequence of sunrise.

He pushes the metaphor further by linking morning to **creative work** and **vigor**:

> [002-029] With unrelaxed nerves, with morning vigor, sail by it, looking another way, tied to the mast like Ulysses.

The allusion to Ulysses binding himself to the mast to resist the Sirens reinforces Thoreau's point: morning vigor is the strength to resist the seductions of conventional life — wealth, status, comfort — and to steer toward one's true purpose.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's veneration of morning belongs to a broader **Romantic and Transcendentalist** tradition. The 19th century saw a cultural shift toward nature as a source of spiritual truth, and the early morning — uncorrupted by commerce and social convention — became a privileged site of authentic experience. Ralph Waldo Emerson, Thoreau's mentor, wrote in *Nature* (1836) that "the sun illuminates only the eye of the man, but shines into the eye and the heart of the child." Thoreau extends this: the morning sun illuminates only those who have cultivated the *inner* capacity to see.

The industrial revolution, underway in New England during Thoreau's lifetime, gave morning a second, competing meaning: the morning whistle, the factory shift, the commuter train. Thoreau's morning is, in part, a deliberate **reclamation** of dawn from industrial time — a refusal to let the factory bell define when the day begins.
