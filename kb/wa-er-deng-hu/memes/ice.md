# Ice

## Definition

Ice in *Walden* operates on two registers: as a natural phenomenon that Thoreau studies with scientific curiosity, observing its crystalline structure, transparency, and seasonal transformation; and as a commodity extracted from the pond by the ice trade, connecting Walden's pristine waters to the luxuries of Boston, Charleston, and even India. The intersection of these two registers reveals Thoreau's ambivalent view of nature transformed into commerce.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most sustained treatment of ice appears in "The Pond in Winter" (016), where he watches a crew of Irish laborers cut and harvest blocks from the frozen surface. He is fascinated by the ice as a substance — its clarity, its grain, its color — even as he notes with irony that Walden's water will soon cool the drinks of distant merchants.

> [016-024] Ice is an interesting subject for contemplation. They told me that they had some in the ice-houses at Fresh Pond five years old which was as good as ever. Why is it that a bucket of water soon becomes putrid, but frozen remains sweet forever? It is commonly said that this is the difference between the affections and the intellect.

The ice trade appears earlier in "Economy" (001) as part of Thoreau's catalog of New England's commercial enterprise:

> [001-039] The products of the country are purely native products, much ice and pine timber and a little granite, which are exported.

In "Spring" (017), Thoreau observes the thaw, seeing in melting ice the same organizational principle that shapes leaves, feathers, and living tissue:

> [017-001] Ice has its grain as well as wood, and when a cake begins to rot or "comb," that is, assume the appearance of honeycomb, whatever may be its position, the air cells are at right angles with the plane of the surface where it originally froze.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau treats the ice harvesters with characteristic detachment — they are part of the landscape, their labor neither condemned nor celebrated. But the fact that Walden's ice travels to India becomes for him a symbol of the unexpected connections between his solitary pond and the global economy. The ice cutter is an ambivalent figure: he unlocks the pond's winter treasury, yet Thoreau notes that "the pure Walden water is mingled with the sacred water of the Ganges" — a union achieved not through spiritual pilgrimage but through commerce.
