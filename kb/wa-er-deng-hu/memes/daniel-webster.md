# Daniel Webster

American statesman and orator (1782–1852), U.S. Senator from Massachusetts, referenced in Walden in connection with the Fugitive-Slave Bill and the rhetoric of American politics.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau references Daniel Webster in two contexts that illuminate his critique of American political life. In the Former Inhabitants chapter, Webster's 1850 Fugitive-Slave Bill—which required the return of escaped slaves—serves as a temporal marker and a moral indictment. In the Conclusion, Webster appears as the archetype of the political orator whose words Thoreau weighs and finds wanting against the deeper truths of the examined life.

> [012-014] It was a few years before the passage of Webster's Fugitive-Slave Bill that I was surveying the woods.

> [018-024] The president of the day, and Webster is his orator. I love to weigh, to settle, to gravitate toward that which most strongly and rightfully attracts me—that is not in the statute-book.

## Biography

Daniel Webster (1782–1852) was a leading American statesman during the antebellum period. He served as a U.S. Representative and Senator from Massachusetts and as Secretary of State under three presidents. Renowned as one of the greatest orators in American history, Webster's support for the Compromise of 1850 and the Fugitive-Slave Act placed him at odds with abolitionists, including many in Thoreau's Concord circle. Thoreau's references to Webster carry the weight of this political disappointment.

## Significance

Webster represents for Thoreau the failure of political eloquence to translate into moral courage. By juxtaposing Webster's oratory against the simple, lived truths of the Walden experiment, Thoreau argues that political rhetoric—however skilled—cannot substitute for individual conscience. The reference to Webster's Fugitive-Slave Bill ties Thoreau's personal experiment in living to the urgent moral question of his time: slavery. Thoreau's refusal to pay his poll tax and his night in jail (recorded in "Civil Disobedience") were direct responses to the same political context that Webster's bill represented.
