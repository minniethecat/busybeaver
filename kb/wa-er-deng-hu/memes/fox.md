# Fox

## Definition

The fox in *Walden* is a creature of the margins — heard more often than seen, a presence inferred from tracks and distant barks rather than directly encountered. Thoreau's fox belongs to the winter landscape, where its range over the snow crust is one of the few signs of animal life in the frozen woods. The fox is a predator, kin to the wolf and the hunting dog, and its wildness is of a different order from the squirrel's or the partridge's — it is not comic or companionable but elusive, intelligent, and slightly dangerous. In Thoreau's imagination, the fox represents the wild intelligence that civilization has not managed to domesticate.

## In Walden

The fox first appears in *Walden* as an absence to be desired. Surveying the features of his location, Thoreau lists what the site offers: "a fox to bark in the night" (004-036). The fox's bark is not an intrusion but an amenity — part of the "pastoral life" that Thoreau has chosen. The phrasing is important: Thoreau does not want a fox for a pet or a trophy. He wants a fox to bark in the night, to remind him that wildness persists in the darkness beyond his hearth-light. The bark is the sound of the untamed.

The winter landscape brings the fox into sharper focus. Thoreau describes the night woods in December: "the fox, and skunk, and rabbit, now range over the snow crust without obstruction" (005-001). Winter erases the boundaries that contain predators in summer — the snow crust bears the fox's weight while it hunts, and the frozen world is more legible (every track is recorded) and more mysterious (the track-maker is seldom seen). In "Winter Animals," Thoreau devotes an extended passage to the fox's voice: "Sometimes I heard the foxes as they ranged over the snow crust, in moonlight nights, in search of a partridge or other game, barking raggedly and demoniacally like forest dogs, as if laboring with some anxiety, or seeking expression, struggling for light" (015-004). The fox's bark is "ragged" and "demoniacal" — a sound that unsettles as much as it announces. The comparison to "forest dogs" domesticates the fox only to make the gap clearer: these are dogs that have never known a master.

The most dramatic fox passage occurs at the conclusion of the winter fox sequence: "and yet no fox bursts forth on to the open level, nor following the hounds, as if they were the fathers of their race" (015-009). The fox remains unseen — its bark fills the night, but the creature itself stays hidden. The following paragraph breaks the tension: "ere long a fox leaped the wall into the road, and at a sudden impulse of his flight, whirled this way and that, snapping at his tail, and giving a quick, scornful glance toward the house" (015-010). The fox's leap is sudden, its flight erratic, its glance "scornful." Thoreau's fox is not afraid of the human dwelling; it is disdainful of it.

Earlier, in "Sounds," Thoreau associates the fox with the arrival of the railroad, which "scares the owl and fox" (004-013) as it cuts through the woods. The train is an intrusion into the fox's territory — a machine whose noise drives the wild creatures deeper into the remaining forest. The fox and the owl are the original inhabitants, displaced by the iron horse.

> [015-004] Sometimes I heard the foxes as they ranged over the snow crust, in moonlight nights, in search of a partridge or other game, barking raggedly and demoniacally like forest dogs, as if laboring with some anxiety, or seeking expression, struggling for light.

> [004-036] My pinetrees, some of them, were so old and tall that a fox might bark in the night without my hearing him.

> [015-010] Ere long a fox leaped the wall into the road, and at a sudden impulse of his flight, whirled this way and that, snapping at his tail, and giving a quick, scornful glance toward the house.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's fox carries a specific philosophical charge: it is the wild that refuses to be seen. Unlike the squirrel, which scolds Thoreau from a bough, or the partridge, which leads its chicks across his wood-pile, the fox stays hidden. Its bark is a voice without a body, a sign of presence that withholds the presence itself. This elusiveness is, for Thoreau, the essence of wildness. The fox is not a pet; it is not a companion; it is not even, strictly speaking, a neighbor. It is a fellow inhabitant of the Walden woods who shares the territory but withholds the acquaintance. Thoreau's respect for this withholding — his willingness to let the fox remain unseen — is a quiet demonstration of his larger philosophy: to know nature is not to possess it, and sometimes the truest form of knowledge is the recognition of what remains unknown.
