# Reform

Reform is a concept that Thoreau approaches with characteristic independence, redirecting the nineteenth-century fervor for social improvement away from institutions and toward the sovereign self. For Thoreau, the only reform that matters is the reform of one's own life.

## Definition

Thoreau distinguishes between two orders of reform: the external reform pursued by societies, movements, and legislation, and the internal reform — what he calls "moral reform" — that an individual undertakes within the soul. The former, he suggests, is often futile or even counterproductive; the latter is the true lever of change. His famous declaration that "there is a dawn in me" (002-021) positions reform not as a social project but as a daily inward renewal.

## In Walden

Thoreau's commentary on reform appears early and with uncompromising clarity. In Chapter 1, he aligns the wisest philosophers of antiquity with a radical simplicity that renders conventional reform superfluous:

> [001-025] Most of the luxuries, and many of the so called comforts of life, are not only not indispensable, but positive hindrances to the elevation of mankind. With respect to luxuries and comforts, the wisest have ever lived a more simple and meagre life than the poor.

This passage sets the terms: elevation of mankind is the goal, but it is achieved through subtraction, not addition — through personal renunciation rather than institutional restructuring.

In "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," Thoreau crystallizes his philosophy of reform in a single resonant sentence:

> [002-021] Every morning was a cheerful invitation to make my life of equal simplicity, and I may say innocence, with Nature herself. ... "Renew thyself completely each day; do it again, and again, and forever again."

Reform, in this light, is a daily practice — a ritual of renewal akin to bathing in the pond — not a program to be enacted once and for all.

## Encounters with Reformers

Thoreau's skepticism toward organized reform is vividly dramatized in his account of visitors to Walden. The Canadian woodchopper, asked whether he wanted the world to be changed, replies with baffled indifference:

> [006-021] I heard that a distinguished wise man and reformer asked him if he did not want the world to be changed; but he answered with a chuckle of surprise ... "No, I like it well enough."

Thoreau presents this response not as ignorance but as a deeper wisdom — the woodchopper is content, and contentment, Thoreau implies, is a more radical reform than any political program.

Thoreau further notes the woodchopper's simple, practical intelligence when discussing the issues of the day:

> [006-022] I loved to sound him on the various reforms of the day, and he never failed to look at them in the most simple and practical light.

This admiration for the unlettered man's clarity stands in implicit contrast to the self-styled reformers who also visited Walden:

> [006-028] there were the self-styled reformers, the greatest bores of all, who thought that I was forever singing, — "This is the house that I built; / This is the man that lives in the house that I built."

The reformers Thoreau satirizes are interested in Thoreau as a specimen rather than in Thoreau's actual life; they fail to see that his experiment is the reform, not a platform for advocating it.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's position on reform reflects his broader commitment to individual sovereignty. He is not opposed to social improvement; he is opposed to the displacement of responsibility from the self to the collective. The reformer who agitates for temperance while remaining intemperate in his own soul has, in Thoreau's view, missed the point. Reform begins and ends with the individual conscience.
