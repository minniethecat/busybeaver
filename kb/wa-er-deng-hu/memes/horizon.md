# Horizon

## Definition

For Thoreau, the horizon is both a literal feature of the Walden landscape and a philosophical metaphor for the limits of perception and knowledge. It represents the boundary between the known and the unknown — a line that recedes as one advances, embodying the Transcendentalist conviction that spiritual and intellectual growth is an endless pursuit.

## In Walden

The horizon appears early in Walden as Thoreau orients himself within his new environment. From his house-site, the horizon is defined by the surrounding woods:

> [002-012] The wooded hills that surround the pond were my most distant horizon.

But this near horizon soon expands as Thoreau's perception deepens:

> [002-013] I could see distant and higher ones in the horizon, tinged with blue.

Thoreau explicitly connects the horizon to intellectual freedom, quoting Eastern philosophy:

> [002-014] "Beings who enjoy freely a vast horizon," — said Damodara, when his herds required new and larger pastures.

The horizon also functions as a sensory amplifier. Distant sounds — the lowing of a cow, the strings of a pine-needle harp — seem to emerge from it:

> [004-029] It was as if the pine needles in the horizon were the strings of a harp which the wind swept.

Thoreau insists on the necessity of open space for the mind, linking physical and mental horizon:

> [005-003] Sufficient space about us. Our horizon is never quite at our elbows.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau treats the horizon not as a fixed line but as a dynamic index of personal growth. A narrow horizon — like that of the "poor man" who, "with his horizon all his own, yet he a poor man" (010-035) — represents spiritual poverty despite material possession. True wealth lies in an expanding horizon, in constant reach toward what lies beyond. This aligns with Thoreau's broader critique of settlement and stasis: just as the farmer who never leaves his fields is impoverished, the mind that never questions its horizons is unfree.

## Historical Context

The horizon carried particular resonance in nineteenth-century America, an era of westward expansion and geographical discovery. Thoreau inverts the frontier mentality: his horizon is not territorial but philosophical. The "vast horizon" of Damodara's herds becomes the inner horizon of self-knowledge. This reflects the Transcendentalist adaptation of Romantic landscape aesthetics, where external nature mirrors internal states of consciousness.
