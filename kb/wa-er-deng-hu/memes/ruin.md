# Ruin

## Definition

Ruin in *Walden* is the visible record of time's erosion — the "ruinous state of the house and barn" (002-006), the cellar-holes of former inhabitants, the crumbling materials of a civilization that outlasts its builders. Thoreau reads ruins not as monuments to past greatness but as warnings: what was built with great labor and expense is now "a hoary ruin" (018-021), and the lesson is not to build more durably but to build less — to invest in what time cannot erode.

## In Walden

Thoreau's first encounter with ruin is the house he almost bought: "the gray color and ruinous state of the house and barn" (002-006) appealed to him precisely because the house was already partly ruined — there was nothing left to lose. A new house is a hostage to fortune; a ruined house is free. This is Thoreau's ruin-aesthetic in miniature: the attraction of the already-decayed, which has passed beyond the anxiety of preservation.

> [002-006] The house was unfit for winter use: the gray color and ruinous state of the house and barn, and the dilapidated fences, which put such an interval between me and the last occupant.

The former inhabitants chapter (014) is Walden's ruin-catalogue. Thoreau walks the woods and reads the landscape for signs of past habitation — cellar-holes, overgrown wells, the "ruins" of homes that have returned to forest. "The city, whose materials are ruins, whose gardens cemeteries" (014-015) is his grim summary of human settlement: every city is built on the ruins of something else, and every city will become ruins in turn.

> [014-015] The city, whose materials are ruins, whose gardens cemeteries. The soil is not soil but ground up and digested city.

The chapter on the ponds imagines Walden itself as a ruin: "a venerable moss-grown and hoary ruin, built of azure-tinted marble" (016-023). The pond is ancient — older than any human construction, older than human time — and its "ruined" state is paradoxically a condition of perfect beauty. Ruin, in nature, is not decay but transformation: the moss that grows on the "ruin" is life feeding on life.

> [016-023] A venerable moss-grown and hoary ruin, built of azure-tinted marble, — the tomb of a hero.

The parable of the artist of Kouroo in the conclusion gives ruin its most philosophical treatment. The artist spends eons perfecting a single staff, and by the time he finishes, "the city of Kouroo was a hoary ruin, and he sat on one of its mounds to pare his stick" (018-021). The city that was once the center of the world is now rubble, but the artist's work survives — not because it was built to last but because it was made with such complete attention that time could not touch it. Ruin is the fate of all things made for the world; the things made for eternity escape ruin.

> [018-021] The city of Kouroo was a hoary ruin, and he sat on one of its mounds to pare his stick. Before he gave it the last touch, the dynasty of the Candahars was at an end, and he wrote the name of the last of that race on a pebble.

Thoreau also uses ruin as a critique of luxury: the man who is "ruined by luxury and heedless expense" (002-024) suffers a spiritual ruin that mirrors the physical ruin of his overbuilt house. "What if it threaten ruin to farmers' crops?" he asks of the railroad (010-018) — the answer is that true ruin is not economic loss but spiritual bankruptcy, the life "ruined" not by poverty but by misdirected effort.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's ruin is counter-Romantic. Where the European Romantics saw ruins as sublime — the picturesque decay of abbeys and castles — Thoreau sees ruins as instructive. The "ruinous state" of a house is a text to be read: it tells you what happens to those who build more than they need, who mortgage their lives to maintain structures that time will claim regardless.

His deepest insight about ruin is that it is not an ending but a return. The cellar-hole becomes a garden; the "ruined" house returns its materials to the soil. "The soil is not soil but ground up and digested city" (014-015) — ruin is digestion, the earth reclaiming what was briefly extracted from it. Thoreau finds this process consoling rather than tragic. If everything built must become ruin, then the wise person builds light — a cabin that can be returned to the earth without protest, a life that leaves no permanent scar.

The artist of Kouroo represents ruin-transcended. By attending so completely to his work that he loses track of time, the artist produces something that ruin cannot claim. This is Thoreau's ultimate answer to the problem of ruin: not to build fortresses against time but to make work so true to itself that time becomes irrelevant.
