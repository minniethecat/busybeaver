# Pickerel

## Definition

The pickerel (*Esox americanus*) is a small predatory fish native to the ponds and slow streams of eastern North America. In Thoreau's *Walden*, the pickerel is more than a fish: it is the pond's secret self, a creature of "rare beauty" and "fabulous" appearance that seems to belong to another world. When Thoreau sees a pickerel lying on the ice or swimming beneath the transparent surface, he sees the wild heart of Walden — something foreign, untamable, and utterly unlike the domesticated landscapes of Concord.

## In Walden

The pickerel first appears in "The Ponds," where Thoreau describes fishing through the ice. The water of Walden is so transparent that "you may see, many feet beneath the surface, the schools of perch and shiners" (009-006), and among them the pickerel — a predator distinguished by its solitary, deliberate motion. Thoreau notes that pickerel weighing seven and even eight pounds have been caught in Walden (009-014), a fact that testifies to the pond's hidden abundance.

In "Baker Farm," Thoreau recalls wading through retired meadows and bog-holes in pursuit of pickerel. His "haste to catch pickerel, wading in retired meadows, in sloughs and bog-holes, in forlorn and savage places" (010-018) appears, for an instant, trivial to him — a man who has "been sent to school and college." Yet the impulse to fish, to seek the wild creature in its wild place, is precisely what draws him away from the schoolroom and toward the pond. The pickerel is the lure that pulls him back into the savage.

The most sustained and lyrical treatment of the pickerel comes in "The Pond in Winter." Thoreau describes men who come "early in the morning, while all things are crisp with frost," letting "down their fine lines through the snowy field to take pickerel and perch" (016-003). These fishermen are "wild men, who instinctively follow other fashions and trust other authorities than their townsmen." The pickerel fisherman is a kind of hero — someone who pursues a knowledge and a pleasure that the village does not sanction.

And then Thoreau delivers his most famous passage on the fish:

> [016-005] Ah, the pickerel of Walden! when I see them lying on the ice, or in the well which the fisherman cuts in the ice, making a little hole to admit the water, I am always surprised by their rare beauty, as if they were fabulous fishes, they are so foreign to the streets, even to the woods, foreign as Arabia to our Concord life.

The pickerel's "rare beauty" is the beauty of the foreign — it belongs to an order of reality that the streets and shops of Concord cannot contain. "Foreign as Arabia to our Concord life" is a remarkable phrase: it suggests that the wild is not distant but adjacent, lying just beneath the ice, accessible through a hole cut by a fisherman.

> [009-006] The water is so transparent that the bottom can easily be discerned at the depth of twenty-five or thirty feet. Paddling over it, you may see, many feet beneath the surface, the schools of perch and shiners... and you think that they must be ascetic fish that find a subsistence there.

> [009-014] There have been caught in Walden pickerel, one weighing seven pounds, to say nothing of another which carried off a reel with great velocity, which the fisherman safely set down at eight pounds because he did not see him.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's pickerel is an emissary from the wild, a proof that Walden is not merely a pond but a *world* — complete, self-contained, and inhabited by creatures of fabulous beauty. The pickerel embodies what Thoreau values most: the irreducible strangeness of nature, its refusal to be domesticated or fully understood. When he describes the pickerel as "foreign as Arabia," he is asserting that the wild is not elsewhere — not in distant continents — but right here, beneath the ice of a Massachusetts pond, waiting to surprise anyone who takes the trouble to look.
