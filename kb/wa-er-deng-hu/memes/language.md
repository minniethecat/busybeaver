# Language

## Definition

Language in *Walden* is understood on two planes. On the lower plane, it is the vernacular of commerce, gossip, and newspapers — a degraded instrument worn smooth by careless repetition. On the higher plane, language is a conduit for immortal truth: the written word of classical authors and the deliberate speech of those who have thought deeply. Thoreau insists that genuine language is not merely communicative but transformative — it does not describe reality so much as disclose it.

## In Walden

Thoreau's fullest treatment of language appears in "Reading" (003), where he argues that the classical languages are not dead but rather represent a more durable form of expression than the fleeting spoken word:

> [003-003] The works of the great poets have never yet been read by mankind, for only great poets can read them. They have only been read as the multitude read the stars, at most astrologically, not astronomically.

He draws a sharp distinction between spoken language and written language, asserting that the written word alone can carry truth across time:

> [003-004] A written word is the choicest of relics. It is something at once more intimate with us and more universal than any other work of art. It is the work of art nearest to life itself. It may be translated into every language, and not only be read but actually breathed from all human lips; — not be represented on canvas or in marble only, but be carved out of the breath of life itself.

For Thoreau, the difficulty of classical languages is not an obstacle but a test — a discipline that separates the serious reader from the dilettante:

> [003-006] The student may read Homer or Æschylus in the Greek without danger of dissipation or luxuriousness, for it implies that he in some measure emulate their heroes, and consecrate morning hours to their pages.

In "Sounds" (004), Thoreau extends this reflection to consider reading itself as a form of translation. He notes that most people "read only particular written languages, which are themselves but dialects and provincial" (004-001), suggesting that the deepest truths require a literacy beyond mere alphabet — a capacity to read nature, experience, and the self. In "House-Warming" (013), language appears in a domestic metaphor: "It would seem as if the very language of our parlors would lose all its nerve and degenerate into *parlaver* wholly" (013-008), linking linguistic decline to the softening effects of comfort and conventional society.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau approaches language with an almost religious reverence, treating the act of reading and writing as a spiritual discipline. His own prose models the standard he advocates: every sentence is worked and reworked, dense with metaphor and etymological play. He is deeply influenced by the Transcendentalist conviction that words are not arbitrary signs but natural symbols — that "the symbol of the writer's faith and the natural fact are one" (as Emerson put it). For Thoreau, the decline of language into newspaper prose and parlor chatter is a symptom of a broader spiritual decline: when people cease to think deeply, their words lose their edge. To read the classics in the original is therefore not a pedantic exercise but a moral act — a deliberate resistance to the trivialization of the mind.
