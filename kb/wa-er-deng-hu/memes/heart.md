# Heart

## Definition

The heart in *Walden* is the organ of inward truth. Thoreau uses it to name what lies beneath calculation, custom, and the "external senses" — the core of the self that feels the "sweet edge" of reality "dividing you through the heart and marrow" (002-029). It is at once a physical presence (the heart of the pine log, the heart-leaf on the pond) and a spiritual metaphor, the seat of sincerity, courage, and the affections that bind us to others and to Nature.

## In Walden

The heart appears first in "Economy" in a characteristic Thoreauvian double move. Having described his careful household accounts, he admits with gentle irony: "I have not set my heart on that" (001-036). The accounts are kept faithfully, but they do not engage the heart. The distinction between the economic and the heartfelt is drawn in a single line — Thoreau will record his expenditures, but his treasure is elsewhere.

The heart's deepest register sounds in "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For." In the celebrated passage on reality, Thoreau imagines standing "right fronting and face to face to a fact" — and then, with a violence that startles, that fact becomes a blade: "you will see the sun glimmer on both its surfaces, as if it were a cimeter, and feel its sweet edge dividing you through the heart and marrow, and so you will happily conclude your mortal career" (002-029). The heart is what must be cut open for reality to enter. "Be it life or death, we crave only reality" — and the heart is the organ that knows the difference.

> [002-029] If you stand right fronting and face to face to a fact, you will see the sun glimmer on both its surfaces, as if it were a cimeter, and feel its sweet edge dividing you through the heart and marrow, and so you will happily conclude your mortal career.

In "Solitude," Thoreau quotes from an Eastern scripture: "They cause that in all the universe men purify and sanctify their hearts, and clothe themselves in their holiday garments to offer sacrifices and oblations to their ancestors" (005-012). The heart requires purification — it is soiled by the world and must be made clean. This is not a Christian mortification but a universal spiritual discipline Thoreau finds confirmed across traditions.

The social dimension of the heart appears in Thoreau's critique of too-frequent company. "Certainly less frequency would suffice for all important and hearty communications" (005-016). The heart's communications are deep, not frequent; they require space and silence, not the constant "old musty cheese" of social routine. Thoreau's preference for solitude is not misanthropy but a theory of the heart's economy: it has limited warmth to give, and must not be dissipated.

> [005-016] Society is commonly too cheap. We meet at very short intervals, not having had time to acquire any new value for each other. ... Certainly less frequency would suffice for all important and hearty communications.

In "Baker Farm," the heart migrates outward into the landscape. Thoreau's "Good Genius" speaks to him as he runs toward the pond: "rest thee by many brooks and hearth-sides without misgiving" (010-018). The hearth — the heart's architectural twin — is wherever warmth is offered and received. The Irishman's grudging hospitality yields "the heartiest draught I could" (010-017), a small kindness that Thoreau receives without squeamishness.

"House-Warming" brings the heart back to the material. Thoreau describes his chimney foundation, the fireplace at the center of the cabin: the fire-dogs "keep the wood from the hearth, and it did me good to see the soot form on the back of the chimney which I had built" (013-006). The hearth is the cabin's heart, and tending it is an act of devotion. Later, in the same chapter, he writes of "prospecting" for pine roots: "stumps thirty or forty years old, at least, will still be sound at the core, though the sapwood has all become vegetable mould, as appears by the scales of the thick bark forming a ring level with the earth four or five inches distant from the heart" (013-016). The heart of the pine endures while the outer rings decay — an image of what Thoreau seeks in himself.

> [013-016] Stumps thirty or forty years old, at least, will still be sound at the core, though the sapwood has all become vegetable mould, as appears by the scales of the thick bark forming a ring level with the earth four or five inches distant from the heart.

The most melancholy heart-image appears in "Former Inhabitants," where Thoreau visits the abandoned cabin of Hugh Quoil. "His pipe lay broken on the hearth, instead of a bowl broken at the fountain" (014-011). The broken pipe on the cold hearth is the heart's death — the warmth gone out, the ritual extinguished. "Soiled cards, kings of diamonds spades and hearts, were scattered over the floor" — the heart, reduced to a playing-card emblem among the debris of a life that has burned out.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, the heart is what remains when the accounts are balanced and the opinions are silenced. It is the seat of sincerity — what the "Good Genius" speaks to, what the "cimeter" of reality cuts through to, what the hearth-fire warms. The heart must be purified ("sanctify their hearts"), protected from the dissipation of cheap society, and kept burning through the long winter. It is, finally, the one thing that outlasts the sapwood — the "sound core" that remains when all the outer rings have turned to mould.
