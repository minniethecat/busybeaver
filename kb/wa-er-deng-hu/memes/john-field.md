# John Field

An Irish laborer who appears in Walden's Baker Farm chapter, representing the struggles of the working poor under the economic system Thoreau critiques.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau encounters John Field and his family while taking shelter from a rainstorm at Baker Farm. Field, an Irish immigrant, works tirelessly to support his family through "bogging" (digging peat) but remains poor. Thoreau uses this encounter to contrast his own philosophy of simple, deliberate living with the grinding labor that traps Field in poverty.

> [010-015] But I was at the same time taking shelter from a drenching rain under the roof of a house at Baker Farm. Therein, as I found, dwelt now John Field, an Irishman, and his wife, and several children.

> [010-016] John Field had not improved his condition by coming to America. He was an honest, hard-working, but shiftless man; his wife was bravely stirring to keep the house in order.

> [010-035] I tried to help him with my experience, telling him that I came as near to living the whole time in the woods as he. But John heaved a sigh at this, and said that it was hard to get a living.

## Biography

John Field was an Irish immigrant who lived with his family at Baker Farm near Walden Pond in the 1840s. He supported his family by "bogging"—digging meadow mud to sell as fertilizer—earning about ten dollars per acre. Thoreau's account of Field is one of the most extended portraits of a neighbor in Walden, notable for both its sympathy and its didactic intent.

## Significance

John Field is Walden's most fully realized example of what Thoreau calls "lives of quiet desperation." He is not lazy—he works hard—but he is trapped by convention, unable to imagine a different way of living. Thoreau presents him not with contempt but as a cautionary example of what happens when one never questions the terms of one's labor. Field embodies the economic critique at the heart of Walden.
