# Grace

## Definition

In Thoreau's *Walden*, grace is the quality of effortless beauty that inheres in natural forms and movements. It is not a social accomplishment — Thoreau pointedly distances himself from conventional gracefulness — but a property of the wild: the arc of a hawk in flight, the curve of a pond's shoreline, the unstudied elegance of a muskrat swimming. Grace is what nature achieves without deliberation and what human artifice, for all its striving, can only approximate.

## In Walden

Thoreau first encounters grace in the natural transformation of the landscape. Observing the sand foliage in "Spring," he describes how the earth's materials "moulded themselves as by magic into graceful green and tender boughs" (004-004). Grace here is spontaneous — not imposed from without but arising from the inherent tendencies of matter toward beautiful form.

In "The Bean-Field," he describes his rows of beans as having "a stout but sluggish body, yet gracefully carried" (006-017), attributing a bodily grace to agricultural plants that human laborers might envy. Grace belongs to the natural world before it belongs to the parlor.

Water, Walden's central metaphor, exemplifies grace in motion. Thoreau finds the pond's ripples "graceful and slender like ripples caught in a net" (007-006), while a neighboring pond has a "more graceful construction" (009-022) in its shoreline. Grace in the landscape is a matter of contour and proportion — the pond knows how to take a curve.

The living creatures of Walden embody grace most vividly. Thoreau describes the partridge "exposing and disgracing this intruder" (015-002) with its protective display, and a serpent "lifting its body and its limbs into graceful length" (015-014) as it recovers from torpor. The hawk he observes in the Conclusion performs "a very slight and graceful" (017-042) aerial maneuver — an ethereal grace that human observers can only admire.

Thoreau himself disclaims the social version of this quality: "I did not stand much about gracefulness, and never hesitated at a gap in a fence" (008-001). His rejection of social grace clears space for a wilder, truer grace — the grace of fitness rather than fashion.

> [004-004] Innumerable little streams overlap and interlace one another, exhibiting a sort of hybrid product, which obeys half way the law of currents, and half way the law of vegetation. As it flows it takes the forms of sappy leaves or vines, making heaps of pulpy sprays a foot or more in depth, and resembling, as you look down on them, the laciniated, lobed, and imbricated thalluses of some lichens; or you are reminded of coral, of leopards' paws or birds' feet, of brains or lungs or bowels, and excrements of all kinds. It is a truly grotesque vegetation, whose forms and color we see imitated in bronze, a sort of architectural foliage more ancient and typical than acanthus, chiccory, ivy, vine, or any vegetable leaves; destined perhaps, under some circumstances, to become a puzzle to future geologists.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's understanding of grace is fundamentally anti-aristocratic. Where European tradition associated grace with courtly refinement and social polish, Thoreau relocates it in the wild, the unkempt, and the spontaneous. Grace in *Walden* is not what one learns in the drawing room but what one witnesses in the pond, the forest, and the sky. His dismissal of personal gracefulness — the "gap in a fence" passage — is a declaration of allegiance: better to move awkwardly through the world with authentic purpose than to glide through it with empty elegance. This inversion places Thoreau in the Romantic tradition that finds the sublime and the beautiful not in culture but in nature.
