# Spiritual Life

## Definition

The spiritual life in *Walden* is Thoreau's term for the deliberate cultivation of one's higher nature — an existence oriented not toward material accumulation or sensual gratification but toward moral clarity, intellectual aspiration, and communion with the eternal. It is not a life removed from the world but a life lived within it with conscious purpose, where every action, from eating to reading to labor, is judged by its contribution to spiritual growth rather than by social convention or immediate pleasure.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most sustained treatment of the spiritual life appears in "Higher Laws" (011), where he confesses his own internal struggle between animal impulse and spiritual aspiration. He opens the chapter with a dramatic admission of the primitive instinct that still stirs within him:

> [011-001] As I came home through the woods with my string of fish, trailing my pole, it being now quite dark, I caught a glimpse of a woodchuck stealing across my path, and felt a strange thrill of savage delight, and was strongly tempted to seize and devour him raw; not that I was hungry then, except for that wildness which he represented.

This tension between animal nature and spiritual ambition is the central drama of the chapter. Thoreau does not deny the animal within; he acknowledges it frankly and argues that spiritual life consists precisely in the effort to subordinate it to higher ends. He depicts the spiritual dimension as something that must be actively chosen and vigilantly maintained:

> [011-014] We are conscious of an animal in us, which awakens in proportion as our higher nature slumbers. It is reptile and sensual, and perhaps cannot be wholly expelled; like the worms which, even in life and health, occupy our bodies.

The spiritual life, for Thoreau, is fundamentally a matter of diet, discipline, and daily practice. His vegetarianism, his abstention from alcohol, and his preference for water over wine are all expressions of a spiritual program that seeks purity through physical temperance. He sees the body not as an enemy but as the instrument through which spiritual aspiration must be realized, and therefore insists on its careful regulation.

In "Reading" (003), Thoreau links the spiritual life to the study of sacred and classical texts. Reading Homer or the Vedas in their original languages becomes a spiritual exercise — a means of transcending the narrow present and participating in the accumulated wisdom of the ages. He describes this as entering "the region of the spiritual world" (003-002), suggesting that literacy and spirituality are, for him, deeply intertwined.

> [011-011] I am glad to have drunk water so long, for the same reason that I prefer the natural sky to an opium-eater's heaven. I would fain keep sober always; and there are infinite degrees of drunkenness.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's spiritual life is distinctively non-institutional. He invokes neither church doctrine nor clerical authority; his spiritual practice is solitary, experimental, and grounded in direct experience of nature and self-examination. He borrows freely from Hindu, Greek, and Christian sources — the Vedas, Homer, and the New Testament coexist in his spiritual vocabulary — but always subordinates them to his own lived experiment at Walden Pond. For Thoreau, the spiritual life is not a destination but a direction: an endless process of refining one's perceptions, restraining one's appetites, and orienting one's attention toward what is permanent and true. It is, in the deepest sense, what the entire Walden experiment was about.
