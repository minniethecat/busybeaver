# Hope

## Definition

Hope, in Walden, is not the airy optimism Thoreau's critics sometimes attribute to him. It is a specific, earned disposition: the conviction, grounded in observation of nature's cycles, that renewal is possible. "By such a pile we may hope to scale heaven at last" (003-006) — the "pile" is the accumulated wisdom of the classics, and the hope is proportional to the effort invested. Thoreauvian hope is conditional on work; it is what remains when effort has been made and the outcome is not yet visible.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most explicit discussion of hope appears in "Reading." Gazing on the accumulated treasure of the world's great books, he writes: "By such a pile we may hope to scale heaven at last" (003-006). The image is architectural: hope is not a feeling but a structure built book by book, insight by insight. The conditional "may" is crucial — hope does not guarantee ascent, but it makes the attempt conceivable.

The counterpoint to hope in Walden is the explicitly hopeless. In "Sounds," Thoreau describes "the relic of mortality who has left hope behind, and howls like an animal" (004-033). This figure — someone who has abandoned the human capacity for forward-looking aspiration — is presented as a warning. To lose hope is to lose the specifically human; it is to regress to the merely animal. Thoreau does not sentimentalize this state; he records it with the same clinical attention he gives to natural phenomena, but the judgment is clear: a life without hope is a life below the human threshold.

In "The Bean-Field," Thoreau offers what amounts to a definition of hope's function. Referring to the Latin etymology of "Ceres," he notes that "hope should not be the only hope of the race" (007-040) — the pun on "hope" (the virtue) and "hope" (agricultural expectation) suggests that spiritual hope must complement material hope. To hope only for a good harvest is to reduce hope to calculation; genuine hope reaches beyond the calculable.

The most lyrical expression of hope comes, fittingly, in "Spring." Thoreau describes "the year beginning with younger hope than ever" (017-013) as the ice melts and the pond stirs back to life. The sand foliage flowing down the railroad bank in thaw is "the beginning of vegetation" — nature's annual performance of rebirth that constitutes, for Thoreau, the strongest argument for hope. "The pond already calm and full of hope as in a summer evening" (017-015) is not a metaphor; for Thoreau, the pond literally embodies hope by demonstrating, every spring, that apparent death is followed by renewed life.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's hope is thoroughly naturalized. It is not a theological virtue (faith, hope, and charity) but a biological rhythm. Spring teaches hope more effectively than any sermon because spring is evidence. The person who has witnessed one winter at Walden dissolve into one spring has all the data needed to hope.

This grounding in observation distinguishes Thoreau's hope from the shallow optimism he despised — the "hope" of the speculator, the "hope" of the farmer who expects a bumper crop. Those are bets on outcomes. Thoreau's hope is confidence in process: the ice will break, the pond will thaw, the sand will flow, the birds will return. What humans make of this renewal is up to them, but the renewal itself is reliable.
