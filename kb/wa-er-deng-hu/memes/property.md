## Definition

Property in Walden is Thoreau's term for the material accumulations that, in his analysis, imprison rather than liberate. Far from being a measure of success, property is the weight that anchors a person to a life of quiet desperation — "our houses are such unwieldy property that we are often imprisoned rather than housed in them" (001-060). Thoreau's radical proposition is that freedom begins with dispossession: the "portionless, who struggle with no such unnecessary inherited encumbrances," are the truly wealthy (001-052).

## In Walden

Thoreau develops his critique of property across Walden's opening chapters, building from economic analysis to moral philosophy. The core observation is that property ownership reverses the proper relationship between person and possession: the owner becomes the owned. A house, rather than sheltering, traps its inhabitant in a lifelong mortgage of labor. "In accumulating property for ourselves or our posterity," Thoreau writes, "we may possibly meet with some disappointment" — the accumulation itself being the disappointment, since the time spent accumulating is time not spent living (003-001).

Thoreau's alternative is not poverty but deliberate simplicity — owning only what one can carry, both physically and psychologically. He reports with satisfaction that he has "never got a fair view" of what is called "a handsome property" — the phrase itself foreign to his vocabulary of value (005-008). The farmer who "regards the soil as property, or the means of acquiring property" has already lost the land's true gift: it is a world to inhabit, not a commodity to extract (007-039).

Property also distorts social relations. Thoreau's neighbor, asked about pecuniary matters, illustrates how property thinking colonizes all transactions: "If an ox were his property, and he wished to get needles and thread, he found it inconvenient to exchange" — the logic of ownership making every exchange more complicated than it need be (006-022). And yet society organizes itself around the protection of property: the fire bell rings "not mainly to save property from the flames, but, if we will confess the truth, to save ourselves from the inconvenience" — a dark joke on the priority of things over lives (002-025).

## Perspective

Thoreau's property critique is not socialist — he is not arguing for collective ownership — but existential. The problem with property is not that it is unequally distributed but that it is, in any quantity beyond the barest necessity, a spiritual liability. "The cost of a thing is the amount of what I will call life which is required to be exchanged for it" applies doubly to property: not only does it cost life to acquire, but it costs additional life to maintain, protect, and worry over. The "portionless" are free not because they have been cheated but because they have escaped the trap.
