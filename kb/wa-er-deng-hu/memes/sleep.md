# Sleep

## Definition

Sleep in Walden operates on two levels: as the bodily necessity that Thoreau observes with minimal accommodation (a simple bed, early rising), and as the master metaphor for the spiritual torpor from which humanity must awaken. To "sleep" in Thoreau's figurative sense is to live by convention, habit, and received opinion — to move through life without ever being truly conscious.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most urgent moral imperative is to "reawaken and keep ourselves awake" — a call issued against the gravitational pull of spiritual sleep:

> [002-022] We must learn to reawaken and keep ourselves awake, not by mechanical aids, but by an infinite expectation of the dawn.

Sleep is not condemned but contextualized: it is necessary, even sweet, but must not become a way of life. Thoreau rises early, observing that "the most memorable season of the day is the awakening hour" when "there is least somnolence" (002-021). Sleep is the daily rehearsal of death — a temporary surrender of consciousness that makes morning's return to awareness all the more precious.

Thoreau records the literal conditions of his sleep at Walden with characteristic honesty:

> [013-007] I sometimes dream of a larger and more populous house, standing in a golden age.

And he notes how the body's sleep is penetrated by nature's sounds:

> [015-003] I was waked by the cracking of the ground by the frost.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Sleep, for Thoreau, is not the enemy — habitual sleep is. The problem is not that we sleep at night but that we sleep through the day, performing the motions of life without consciousness. His famous declaration that "only that day dawns to which we are awake" (018-029) makes clear that wakefulness is not a given but an achievement — something that must be won anew each morning against the tremendous inertia of habit and comfort.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's treatment of sleep reflects multiple traditions: the Stoic practice of morning meditation (Marcus Aurelius's reflections on rising), the Protestant emphasis on spiritual vigilance, and the Romantic fascination with the borderland between sleep and waking (Coleridge, De Quincey). His innovation is to make sleep-and-waking not a theological issue but a practice of daily life — something determined by when you rise, how you spend your morning, and whether you meet the day deliberately.
