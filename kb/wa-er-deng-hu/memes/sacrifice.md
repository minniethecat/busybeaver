# Sacrifice

## Definition

Sacrifice in *Walden* names the hidden cost of the civilized order. Thoreau uses the word in two registers: the concrete sacrifices demanded by a system that absorbs "the life of the individual" into "the life of a civilized people" (001-052), and the more intimate sacrifice of "the bloom of the present moment" (004-002) to work, wealth, and worry. In every case, sacrifice is the metric of what we give up — and Thoreau's central question is whether the exchange is worth it.

## In Walden

Thoreau introduces sacrifice as the dark underside of civilization's advantages. In "Economy," he declares his intent "to show at what a sacrifice this advantage is at present obtained" (001-052), pointing to a social order that preserves the race by absorbing the individual. The civilized man, he suggests, sacrifices his autonomy to institutions that demand his submission. The solution Thoreau proposes is radical: "we may possibly so live as to secure all the advantage without suffering any of the disadvantage" (001-052), a life that refuses the terms of the civilizational bargain.

The word returns with a different valence in "Sounds." Having set aside books for the bean-field, Thoreau confesses that "there were times when I could not afford to sacrifice the bloom of the present moment to any work, whether of the head or hands" (004-002). Here sacrifice is reframed as a positive refusal — not the loss of something, but the protection of the present against the claims of productivity. The "broad margin" he loves is precisely what is sacrificed in a life governed by clocks and calendars.

In "The Bean-Field," sacrifice takes an economic and religious turn. Thoreau laments that the modern farmer "sacrifices not to Ceres and the Terrestrial Jove, but to the infernal Plutus rather" (007-039). Husbandry, once a sacred art, has become a profane calculation. The farmer's sacrifice to the god of wealth deforms both the landscape and the soul. What should be a relation of piety becomes one of plunder — "he knows Nature but as a robber" (007-039).

> [007-039] By avarice and selfishness, and a grovelling habit, from which none of us is free, of regarding the soil as property, or the means of acquiring property chiefly, the landscape is deformed, husbandry is degraded with us, and the farmer leads the meanest of lives.

The winter woods offer a practical, almost playful image of sacrifice. An old forest fence, "past serving the god Terminus," becomes fuel: "I sacrificed it to Vulcan, for it was past serving the god Terminus" (013-012). The fence that marked a boundary becomes the fire that sustains life. Thoreau's Latin pun — Terminus (boundaries) to Vulcan (fire) — makes sacrifice a form of transformation, not loss.

> [013-012] An old forest fence which had seen its best days was a great haul for me. I sacrificed it to Vulcan, for it was past serving the god Terminus.

In "Spring," the scope widens to the cosmic. "I love to see that Nature is so rife with life that myriads can be afforded to be sacrificed and suffered to prey on one another" (017-044). Nature's prodigality reframes sacrifice as abundance rather than scarcity. The tadpole swallowed by the heron is not a tragedy but evidence of "the strong appetite and inviolable health of Nature."

The final and most searing usage appears in "Conclusion." Thoreau indicts those "who have no self-respect, and sacrifice the greater to the less" (018-007). Patriotism, love of the soil that makes one's grave, is a maggot in the head if it sacrifices the spirit to the clay. The passage gathers all the earlier meanings: sacrifice is always a question of proportion — are we giving the greater for the lesser, or the lesser for the greater?

> [018-007] Yet some can be patriotic who have no self-respect, and sacrifice the greater to the less. They love the soil which makes their graves, but have no sympathy with the spirit which may still animate their clay.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, sacrifice is not a virtue to be cultivated but a cost to be interrogated. His critique is not that we sacrifice too little but that we sacrifice the wrong things — the present moment to the future, the individual self to the social machine, the sacred to the pecuniary. The deliberate life he advocates is one that refuses the economy of sacrifice, insisting instead on what he calls "a broad margin" — time, attention, and spirit protected from the logic of exchange. To sacrifice nothing essential is the true discipline.
