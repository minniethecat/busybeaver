# Conformity

## Definition

In *Walden*, conformity names the unreflective adherence to inherited patterns of life that Thoreau sees as the primary obstacle to deliberate living.

It is the social and psychological force that makes people "make shift to live merely by conformity, practically as their fathers did" (001-025), reproducing the lives of previous generations without ever asking whether those lives were worth living.

Conformity is not merely social pressure; it is a failure of consciousness. It is a sleepwalk through existence on paths others have worn.

## In Walden

Thoreau's critique of conformity runs through the whole of *Walden*, surfacing in contexts as varied as philosophy, topography, and the farewell to the woods.

In "Economy," he diagnoses the intellectual class itself as afflicted: great scholars and thinkers "make shift to live merely by conformity, practically as their fathers did, and are in no sense the progenitors of a nobler race of men" (001-025).

The indictment is precise — even the learned fail to originate anything, merely reproducing the intellectual habits they inherited. Conformity is not a failing of the uneducated alone; it is the default condition of civilized life.

In "Higher Laws," conformity takes on a positive valence, revealing the concept's doubleness in Thoreau's thought. He speaks of "a life in conformity to higher principles" (011-010) — the conformity of the individual will to moral law, not to social custom.

The phrase is carefully chosen: the same structural act of aligning one's life to an external standard can be degrading or elevating depending on what one conforms *to*. Conformity to genius, to higher laws, to the "faintest but constant suggestions" of one's own nature is the path of spiritual integrity.

> [011-010] Though the result were bodily weakness, yet perhaps no one can say that the consequences were to be regretted, for these were a life in conformity to higher principles.

The pond itself becomes a metaphor for conformity's geological expression. Sounding Walden's frozen surface, Thoreau notes "the regularity of the bottom and its conformity to the shores and the range of the neighboring hills were so perfect" (016-013).

Nature's conformity — the way depths mirror heights, the way the basin answers the surrounding landscape — is a harmony, not a submission. The pond conforms without losing its identity; it reflects the hills without becoming them.

In the final chapter, conformity appears as the deepest rut in the road of life: "How worn and dusty, then, must be the highways of the world, how deep the ruts of tradition and conformity!" (018-014).

Thoreau's exit from Walden is framed as a refusal of these ruts — he "did not wish to take a cabin passage, but rather to go before the mast and on the deck of the world" (018-014). Conformity is the cabin passage: comfortable, sheltered, and blind to the moonlight on the mountains.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau sees conformity not as a moral failing but as a failure of attention. It is what happens when people stop observing their own lives and instead accept the ready-made patterns handed to them.

His solution is not rebellion for its own sake but the substitution of one conformity for another: instead of conforming to "what their fathers did," conform to "higher principles," to the "different drummer" each person hears (018-020).

The individual who marches to their own rhythm is still marching — still conforming, in a sense, to an internal standard. Thoreau's radicalism lies not in abolishing conformity but in relocating its authority from the social to the spiritual, from the inherited to the discovered.
