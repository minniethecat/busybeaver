# Hospitality

## Definition

Hospitality in *Walden* is Thoreau's art of receiving visitors within the constraints of his small cabin and his larger philosophical commitments. With only three chairs — "one for solitude, two for friendship, three for society" — he develops a practice of hospitality that privileges meaningful exchange over material provision. His approach challenges the nineteenth-century conventions of entertaining, replacing lavish tables and spare bedrooms with conversation, simplicity, and the natural setting of the pond itself.

## In Walden

Thoreau devotes the chapter "Visitors" (006) to his philosophy and practice of hospitality, recounting the diverse guests who found their way to his door. He distinguishes between those who come for genuine society and those who are merely curious, offering each what the situation requires.

> [006-002] I had three chairs in my house; one for solitude, two for friendship, three for society. When visitors came in larger and unexpected numbers there was but the third chair for them all, but they generally economized the room by standing up.

His hospitality is defined by what it excludes as much as by what it provides. He refuses to let the conventions of entertaining disrupt his work or his solitude:

> [006-025] I require of a visitor that he be not actually starving, though he may have the very best appetite, and the best digestion, whatever food he gets. Not that I would not, if necessity required, put my hand in the fire for a guest — but for the guest who is not a necessity.

The visitors Thoreau most values are those who come without pretension: the Canadian woodchopper (006-017), the simple-minded men from the almshouse (006-024), and children who bring "cheer" rather than demands:

> [006-035] I had more cheering visitors than the last. Children come a-berrying, railroad men taking a Sunday morning walk in clean shirts, fishermen and hunters, poets and philosophers; in short, all honest pilgrims, who came out to the woods for freedom's sake, and really left the village behind.

Yet for all his openness, Thoreau insists on distance. When conversation with a guest grows too intimate or prolonged, he retreats:

> [006-003] I had a sufficient distance from my guest when we began to utter the big thoughts in big words.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's hospitality is an extension of his philosophy of simplicity. Just as he reduces his material possessions to the essentials, he reduces social intercourse to its essential element: genuine human contact. The three chairs are not a limitation but a deliberate choice — an architecture of intentional relationship. By refusing to expand his house to accommodate more guests, he preserves the quality of each encounter and protects the solitude that makes his creative and philosophical life possible. In winter, when visitors dwindle to almost none (014-016), he welcomes the deepened solitude not as deprivation but as a different form of society — with the pond, the animals, and himself.
