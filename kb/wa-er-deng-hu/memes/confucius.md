# Confucius

Chinese philosopher (551–479 BCE) whose teachings on virtue, wisdom, and self-knowledge are cited by Thoreau in Walden.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau quotes Confucius twice in Walden, drawing on the Analects to support his arguments about self-knowledge and virtue. These references reveal Thoreau's engagement with Eastern philosophy as a source of moral authority parallel to Western classical tradition.

> [001-021] Confucius said, "To know that we know what we know, and that we do not know what we do not know, that is true knowledge."

> [005-013] Confucius says truly, "Virtue does not remain as an abandoned orphan; it must of necessity have neighbors."

## Biography

Confucius (Kong Qiu, 551–479 BCE) was a Chinese teacher, philosopher, and political theorist of the Spring and Autumn period. His teachings, preserved in the *Analects*, emphasize personal morality, social harmony, and the cultivation of virtue through ritual and education. By Thoreau's time, Confucius was known in the West through Jesuit translations and the Transcendentalists' interest in Eastern thought.

## Significance

Thoreau's inclusion of Confucius alongside Western authorities like Cato and Plato demonstrates his universalist approach to wisdom. He treats Confucian sayings not as exotic curiosities but as practical ethical guidance, integrating Eastern thought into his argument for deliberate, virtuous living.
