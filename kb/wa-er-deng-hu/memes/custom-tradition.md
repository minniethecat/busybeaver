# Custom / Tradition

## Definition

Custom and tradition, in Thoreau's *Walden*, name the accumulated weight of inherited practice — the ways of living, thinking, and judging that people absorb from their society without ever consciously choosing them. Custom is what "all tongues" conform to (018-012); tradition is what passes unquestioned from one generation to the next. For Thoreau, the unexamined life is not merely an individual failure but a cultural one: custom is the mechanism by which whole societies sleepwalk through their own existence. To wake up is, first and always, to interrogate the customs one has inherited.

## In Walden

Thoreau's attack on custom runs through *Walden* from the opening pages. In "Economy," he observes how people "are accustomed to consider every spot as the possible site of a house" (002-001), treating the inherited desire for property as a law of nature rather than a custom that could be set aside. The irony is that what we call "custom" has the force of compulsion: it "requireth more than any exercise which the customs of the day esteem" to resist (003-003).

The language of habituation pervades Thoreau's descriptions of his own life at Walden, but with an important inversion. He speaks of "having taken my accustomed bath" in the pond each morning (004-002), of being "accustomed to make an irruption into some houses" (008-001), and of being "accustomed to fish from a boat" on the pond (009-010). These are customs he has deliberately chosen — rituals he has built for himself rather than inherited from the village. The difference between his accustomed bath and the villager's accustomed mortgage is the difference between a chosen practice and an unexamined inheritance.

The most pointed critique appears in the context of the "Higher Laws" chapter, where Thoreau writes that "no man ever followed his genius till it misled him" — but that "we are accustomed" to hear the opposite counsel (011-010). The passive construction is telling: custom speaks in the passive voice, in "it is said" and "one does" and "they have always done." Against this, Thoreau sets the active voice of individual conscience.

In the concluding chapter, Thoreau advises the reader to "shake off" custom with the same violence as shaking off "the village" (018-012). The advice is in the imperative: "If you would speak all tongues and conform to the customs of all nations" (018-012), you lose the only thing worth keeping — your distinctive self. Custom is not merely inconvenient; it is a kind of spiritual death by a thousand small conformities.

Thoreau extends this critique even to the perception of nature. Scenes "which we are accustomed to call wild and dreary" (005-004) are, on closer attention, rich with life — the custom of dismissing them as "dreary" prevents us from seeing what is actually there. Similarly, the pond whose depth seems terrifying is only so because "some are accustomed to speak of deep and dangerous ponds" (016-013). Custom distorts perception.

## Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of custom places him in a long line of radical individualists — from Socrates to Emerson — who saw social conformity as the primary obstacle to the examined life. But Thoreau adds something distinctive: a materialist's attention to the cost of custom. Conforming to the village's way of farming, housing, clothing, and eating is not just spiritually deadening; it is also economically crippling. The man who follows custom works a lifetime to pay for a house he did not truly choose. Custom is not free; its price is a life.

## Historical Context

Nineteenth-century New England was a society in tension between inherited Puritan custom and the emerging forces of individualism, transcendentalism, and market capitalism. Thoreau's friend Emerson had declared in "Self-Reliance" (1841) that "society everywhere is in conspiracy against the manhood of every one of its members." Thoreau's attack on custom extends Emerson's into the practical details of daily life — what to wear, what to eat, how to build, when to work. The Transcendentalist project was, at bottom, a project of custom-breaking.
