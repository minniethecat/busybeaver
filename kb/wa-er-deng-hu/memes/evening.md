# Evening

## Definition

In Walden, evening is more than a time of day — it is a state of heightened sensory awareness and reflective tranquility. Thoreau treats evening as the daily threshold between the active labor of daylight and the inward contemplation of night, a moment when the body becomes "one sense" and the mind opens to deeper thought.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most vivid celebration of evening occurs in "Solitude," where he declares it a moment of pure embodied presence:

> [005-001] This is a delicious evening, when the whole body is one sense, and imbibes delight through every pore.

The serenity of evening extends beyond the pond itself. Thoreau notes how the late afternoon already carries evening's quality:

> [002-013] The afternoon had all the serenity of evening, and the wood-thrush sang around, and was heard from shore to shore.

Evening is also a time of distant sounds — the lowing of cows, the rumble of the train, the whip-poor-will's song — each marking the transition from work to rest:

> [004-030] At evening, the distant lowing of some cow in the horizon beyond the woods sounded sweet and melodious.

In winter, evening becomes a time of fireside hospitality and inward reflection:

> [014-001] I spent some cheerful winter evenings by my fire-side, while the snow whirled without.

The evening train's departure signals the close of the working day and the beginning of Thoreau's solitary hours:

> [004-031] For the rest of the summer, after the evening train had gone by, the whip-poor-will sang as usual for a half-hour.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau sees evening not as the day's end but as its fulfillment. Where morning represents awakening and labor, evening represents absorption and wisdom — the moment when impressions gathered during the day settle into insight. He consistently portrays evening as the time when nature's "different race of creatures awakes" (004-034), hinting at a nocturnal order invisible to the daylight mind. This inversion — evening as awakening — is characteristic of Thoreau's tendency to overturn conventional temporal values.

## Historical Context

In nineteenth-century New England, evening marked the close of agricultural and domestic labor, a time for family gathering, reading aloud, and fireside conversation. Thoreau inherits this cultural pattern but transforms it: his evening is solitary rather than social, oriented toward nature rather than the hearth. This shift reflects the Transcendentalist emphasis on individual communion with the natural world as a source of spiritual renewal.
