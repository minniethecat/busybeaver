# The Encounter with the Loon

Thoreau's extended account of a playful autumn encounter with a loon on Walden Pond, one of the most celebrated narrative set-pieces in the book.

## In Walden

In the "Brute Neighbors" chapter, Thoreau describes an elaborate game of hide-and-seek with a loon on Walden Pond. The loon repeatedly surfaces, calls, and dives again, each time appearing in an unexpected place. Thoreau pursues it in his boat but is consistently outwitted, and the encounter becomes a meditation on wildness, intelligence, and the limits of human understanding of the natural world.

> [012-017] In the fall the loon (Colymbus glacialis) came, as usual, to moult and bathe in the pond, making the woods ring with his wild laughter before I had risen. At rumor of his arrival all the Mill-dam sportsmen are on the alert, in gigs and on foot, two by two and three by three, with patent rifles and conical balls and spy-glasses. They come rustling through the woods like autumn leaves, at least ten men to one loon.

> [012-018] It was a pretty game, played on the smooth surface of the pond, a man against a loon. Suddenly your adversary's checker disappears beneath the board, and the problem is to place yours nearest to where his will appear again.

## Significance

The loon encounter is the most purely playful of all Thoreau's nature descriptions. It has been interpreted as a parable of the relationship between the conscious mind (Thoreau in his boat) and the wild, instinctive, unknowable part of nature (the diving loon). The loon's wild laughter—which Thoreau compares to the call of a "demonic" presence—represents nature's irreducible otherness.
