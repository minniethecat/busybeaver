# The Hunter

## Connection to Walden

The hunter appears throughout *Walden* not as a single named individual but as a **recurring human type** — the man who enters the woods for game but may, if he remains open, be transformed by the woods into something higher. Thoreau treats the hunter with a mixture of respect and gentle condescension: the hunter is closer to nature than the townsman, but he has not yet arrived at the contemplative relationship Thoreau himself embodies.

The most vivid portrait appears in *Higher Laws*, where Thoreau devotes sustained attention to the hunter's place in the moral order:

> [011-001] Fishermen, hunters, woodchoppers, and others, spending their lives in the fields and woods, in a peculiar sense a part of Nature themselves, are often in a more favorable mood for observing her, in the intervals of their pursuits, than philosophers or poets even, who approach her with expectation.

Thoreau grants the hunter a privileged observational intimacy with nature — he is *in* the landscape, not merely *spectating*. But Thoreau also insists that hunting is, or should be, a **stage** rather than a destination:

> [011-007] He goes thither at first as a hunter and fisher, until at last, if he has the seeds of a better life in him, he distinguishes his proper objects, as a poet or naturalist it may be, and leaves the gun and fish-pole behind.

The trajectory is clear: hunting is the gateway; contemplation is the goal. The hunter who never lays down his gun remains, in Thoreau's taxonomy, developmentally arrested.

Thoreau also records specific encounters. One old hunter, a man of few words but deep woods-knowledge, earns his grudging admiration:

> [015-010] One old hunter who has a dry tongue, who used to come to bathe in Walden once every year when the water was warmest, and at such times looked at it, and said that it was a good place.

The old hunter's annual pilgrimage to bathe in Walden Pond echoes Thoreau's own morning ritual — the hunter and the philosopher share a common recognition of the pond's sanctity, even if they express it differently.

In winter, the hunters' presence diminishes, but their traces remain:

> [014-017] …my hands and knees, when the hunters had gone into winter quarters.

> [015-009] …perhaps at evening I see the hunters returning with a single brush of feathers, the spoils of the day, which they have taken for the museum.

Thoreau as a young man had himself been a hunter, and this autobiographical thread gives his reflections unusual authority:

> [011-003] …parts of my education,—make them hunters, though sportsmen only at first, if possible, mighty hunters at last, so that they shall feel the same interest in the pursuit of any noble aim, as in the chase of a fox or a deer.

The progression is explicit: the boy hunts animals, and if well educated, graduates to hunting nobler quarry — truth, beauty, wisdom. The hunter's skills — patience, alertness, knowledge of terrain — are transferable to the spiritual life.

## Biography

The hunter in Walden is a composite figure drawn from Thoreau's encounters in the Concord woods. The "old hunter" of *Winter Animals* (Chapter 15) appears to be a specific local character — older, taciturn, steeped in woods lore but unsentimental about it. Another hunter appears in the opening of *Higher Laws*, where Thoreau describes taking his gun into the woods as a boy and feeling the "savage" thrill of the chase.

Thoreau's own hunting career was brief. In *Higher Laws*, he confesses that he gave it up because he felt it coarsened his sympathies — that the hunter's necessary indifference to animal suffering was incompatible with the moral refinement he sought. This autobiographical pivot gives the hunter figure its philosophical weight in Walden: the hunter is the self Thoreau **outgrew**, and his portrait of hunters is therefore a portrait of his own past.

## Significance

The hunter serves three functions in Walden's philosophical architecture:

1. **The hunter as threshold figure.** He is halfway between civilization and wilderness, commerce and contemplation. Unlike the farmer, who transforms nature into commodity, the hunter enters nature on its own terms. He is therefore closer to the truth than most men — but he has not yet articulated what he intuits.

2. **The hunter as moral test case.** In *Higher Laws*, Thoreau uses hunting to explore the tension between our "wild" and "spiritual" natures. The question is not whether hunting is wrong, but whether one can **transcend** it — whether the boy who kills can grow into the man who contemplates.

3. **The hunter as vanishing type.** Walden was written at a moment when the New England frontier had closed. The hunter belonged to an older economic order — subsistence, not commerce — and Thoreau records him with the awareness that he is documenting something disappearing.
