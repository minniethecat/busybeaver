# Instinct

## Definition

Instinct in *Walden* is Thoreau's term for the inborn impulse toward elevation: the untaught, uncoerced turning of the human being toward what is higher.

He locates this drive not in doctrine or culture but in the body and its intuitions. "I find in myself, and still find, an instinct toward a higher, or, as it is named, spiritual life" (011-001), he writes.

Instinct is nature speaking from within; to follow it is to be true to one's own constitution. Thoreau's instinct is at once animal and divine.

## In Walden

Thoreau grounds his concept of instinct in careful observation of animals. The hound "yielding to the instinct of the chase" (012-002) demonstrates a fidelity to innate impulse that Thoreau admires.

The young partridges, "obedient to their mother and their instinct" (012-010), squat motionless to avoid detection. The hunting dog "unable to resist the instinct of the chase" (015-009) shows the same unthinking commitment.

Thoreau seeks to emulate this animal fidelity in the spiritual realm. The animal does not question its instinct; it obeys.

The human being has an analogous instinct toward higher things, but has learned to override it. "Many of my fellows," Thoreau observes, retain "a certain instinct for it, which revives from time to time" (011-008).

Thoreau extends the concept to practical wisdom. "My instinct tells me that my head is an organ for burrowing" (002-030), he writes, comparing human intellectual labor to the mole's excavation.

The intellect has its own instinct, its own direction of effort. Thought that follows this instinct is more productive than thought imposed by external demand.

The instinct toward higher life separates the human from the merely animal. Yet "a step or two beyond instinct" (013-028) is Thoreau's definition of the human.

Thoreau's instinct is ultimately aligned with the seasons. "Like a new-born instinct, and for a short hour the soul" (017-028) awakens with the spring thaw.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, instinct is a corrective to the over-intellectualization of spiritual life. The churches and reform societies of his day offered elaborate doctrines; Thoreau offers instinct instead.

This is radical democratic theology: every person has direct access to the higher by virtue of being alive and attentive.

Instinct also bridges the natural and the supernatural without requiring a leap of faith. The same faculty that guides the migratory bird guides the aspiring soul.

Thoreau dismantles the hierarchy that places spirit above nature. To be spiritual, in his account, is simply to be fully natural.
