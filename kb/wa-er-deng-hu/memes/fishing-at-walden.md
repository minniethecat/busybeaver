# Fishing at Walden

## Narrative

Fishing threads through *Walden* as a **recurring ritual** — not a single dramatic event but a practice repeated across seasons, alone and with companions, in daylight and by moonlight. Thoreau fishes Walden Pond, the Concord River, Fair Haven Bay, and the surrounding brooks with a patience that blurs the line between pursuit and meditation.

The most famous fishing passage opens *Where I Lived, and What I Lived For*:

> [002-030] Time is but the stream I go a-fishing in. I drink at it; but while I drink I see the sandy bottom and detect how shallow it is. Its thin current slides away, but eternity remains. I would drink deeper; fish in the sky, whose bottom is pebbly with stars.

This is fishing as **metaphysics**: the stream is Time itself, and the fisherman who "drinks" from it is the contemplative who perceives the eternal beneath the temporal. Thoreau's pun on "drink deeper" — to fish more profoundly, to live more fully — sets the register for every subsequent fishing scene in the book.

A more literal but equally luminous scene occurs at night on Walden Pond:

> [009-004] …spent the hours of midnight fishing from a boat by moonlight, serenaded by owls and foxes, and not knowing but the next moment some larger fish might take my bait.

The night-fisherman is alone on the dark water, suspended between the owl's cry and the fox's bark, waiting for a tug on the line that may be a pickerel or may be something stranger. Thoreau captures the **expectancy** of fishing — the way time dilates and the senses sharpen.

Fishing is also social. Thoreau records outings with a companion who walks the railroad track to reach the pond:

> [012-001] Sometimes I had a companion in my fishing, who came through the village to the pond by the railroad track, and we angled together from opposite sides of the boat, and talked of the olden time, when men were simpler and wiser.

The companion — likely William Ellery Channing, Thoreau's closest friend and frequent walking partner — adds a dimension of friendship to the solitary practice. Fishing together, they talk; talking, they fish. The line between the two activities dissolves.

In deeper winter, a different kind of fishing appears — men boring through the ice of Walden Pond to take pickerel:

> [016-003] …men come with fishing reels and slender lunch, and let down their fine lines through the snowy field to take pickerel and perch.

These winter fishermen are practical men, not philosophers. But Thoreau, characteristically, finds in their practice a metaphor: to fish through ice is to trust that life persists beneath the frozen surface — an act of faith as much as of skill.

Thoreau's own relationship to fishing evolves over the course of the book. In *Higher Laws*, he reflects on having given it up, or nearly so:

> [011-001] I cannot fish without feeling a little compunction. I have skill at it, and, like many of my fellows, a certain instinct for it, which revives from time to time, but always when I have done I feel that it would have been better if I had not fished.

The "compunction" is a moral unease — fishing requires the taking of life, and Thoreau is no longer confident that this is justified. He compares fishing to hunting: both are stages in the development of the soul, necessary perhaps in youth, but to be outgrown:

> [011-003] I speak of fishing only now, for I had long felt differently about fowling, and sold my gun before I went to the woods. Not that I am less humane than others, but I did not perceive that my feelings were much affected. I did not pity the fishes nor the worms.

This is Thoreau at his most honest — he does not pretend to a compassion he does not feel; he simply notices that fishing no longer "feels right," and acts on that intuition. The trajectory is clear: from hunter to fisherman to vegetarian to — perhaps — something beyond, where even the taking of vegetable life gives pause.

## Context

Fishing appears in nearly every chapter of Walden that touches on the pond: *Where I Lived* (Ch. 2), *The Ponds* (Ch. 9), *Baker Farm* (Ch. 10), *Higher Laws* (Ch. 11), *Brute Neighbors* (Ch. 12), *The Pond in Winter* (Ch. 16), and *Spring* (Ch. 17). No other activity — not even reading or farming — is so persistently revisited. Fishing is, for Thoreau, the **ur-activity**: the thing humans have always done at the edge of water, the practice that connects him to the Penobscot Indians, to the first inhabitants of the pond, to the "olden time when men were simpler and wiser."

## Significance

Fishing serves three functions in Walden:

1. **Subsistence.** Thoreau genuinely depends on fish for food, especially in his first year at the pond. He is not a recreational angler but a man who eats what he catches.

2. **Meditation.** The stillness required for fishing — the long watch, the suspended attention — is indistinguishable, in Thoreau's practice, from the stillness required for thought. To fish is to think in a particular way: alert, patient, receptive.

3. **Moral test case.** Fishing allows Thoreau to explore the ethics of taking life without the charge that surrounds hunting. Fish are "lower" than mammals, and Thoreau's ambivalence about killing them is correspondingly more nuanced — and more interesting.
