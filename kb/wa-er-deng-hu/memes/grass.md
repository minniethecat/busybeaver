# Grass

## Definition

In *Walden*, grass is the humble, universal ground-cover that Thoreau treats as a symbol of nature's quiet abundance and impartial generosity. Unlike cultivated crops that require human labor and ownership, grass grows freely for all creatures — the bison of the prairie, the mole in its burrow, and the philosopher in his retreat. It represents both the material foundation of life and a spiritual metaphor for what sustains without ostentation.

## In Walden

Thoreau opens his discussion of life's necessities with grass as the most elemental food. Reflecting on what a creature truly needs, he writes: "To the bison of the prairie it is a few inches of palatable grass, with water to drink; unless he seeks the Shelter of the forest or the mountain's shadow" (001-023). Grass is the baseline of subsistence — the original food that requires no cooking, no cultivation, no commerce. It is nature's gift, not man's product.

The same image appears in the domestic sphere, where Thoreau observes that even our most intimate shelters borrow from this humble material: "as the mole has its bed of grass and leaves at the end of its burrow" (001-024). The mole's bed is both literal and figurative — a reminder that warmth and comfort, like food, ultimately trace back to the grass and leaves that cover the earth.

When Thoreau dismantles his first small house and moves it to the pond, he finds the grass itself participating in his experiment: "spreading the boards on the grass there to bleach and warp back again in the sun" (001-079). The grass becomes a work-surface, a partner in the building process, receiving the boards and, with the sun, reshaping them. This quiet collaboration with the grass exemplifies the non-exploitative relationship with nature that Thoreau seeks.

The Bean-Field chapter transforms grass from passive ground-cover into an active teacher. Thoreau's battle against weeds is also a grudging admiration for the grasses that resist his cultivation: "There the sun looked on all the labor of the virtuous, turning the earth into various crops of weeds and grasses, as if it had a spite against the husbandman" (007-004). The grasses are the earth's own crop, growing without permission, mocking the farmer's pretense of control.

Thoreau ultimately concludes that the true crop of his bean-field was not beans at all but the spiritual seeds planted alongside them: "I said to myself, I will not plant beans and corn with so much industry another summer, but such seeds, if the seed is not lost, as sincerity, truth, simplicity, faith, innocence, and the like, and see if they will not grow in this soil" (007-035). The grass crop — what emerges naturally from the earth without human striving — becomes a metaphor for virtues that flourish without forced cultivation.

> [005-004] There can be no very black melancholy to him who lives in the midst of Nature and has his senses still. There was never yet such a storm but it was Æolian music to a healthy and innocent ear.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, grass embodies the principle of sufficiency — enough for all, without competition or accumulation. Where his neighbors see a lawn to be trimmed or a pasture to be fenced, Thoreau sees a democratic carpet that serves bison, mole, and philosopher alike. Grass is what grows when human ambition stops, and its lesson is that the earth already provides what is necessary. His "grass crop" is the spiritual harvest that comes from attention rather than extraction, from dwelling rather than exploiting.

## Historical Context

In nineteenth-century New England agriculture, grass was primarily valued as hay — winter fodder for livestock — or as pasture for grazing. The clearing of forests for grassland was a mark of civilization and productive land use. Thoreau inverts this hierarchy: for him, the forest is not an obstacle to grass but its necessary companion, and the wild grasses of the meadow teach more than the cultivated hayfield. This perspective aligns with the Romantic revaluation of wild nature against agricultural improvement.
