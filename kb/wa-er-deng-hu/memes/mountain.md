# Mountain

## Definition

The mountain in Thoreau's *Walden* appears less as a physical feature of the Concord landscape — which is notably flat — than as an emblem of elevation, distance, and spiritual aspiration. Thoreau invokes mountains as points of reference against which the scale of a human life can be measured: climbing them, seeing them on the horizon, or imagining the view from their summits. The mountain is the opposite of the village: it is high, remote, and indifferent to human commerce. To think of mountains is to think beyond the plain of ordinary existence.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most concentrated meditation on mountains appears early in "Economy," in a passage that moves from the primitive to the modern. In the primitive ages, man "dwelt, as it were, in a tent in this world, and was either threading the valleys, or crossing the plains, or climbing the mountain tops" (001-065). The mountain top is the terminus of a journey — the place from which the whole landscape becomes visible. But this freedom has been lost: "men have become the tools of their tools" (001-065), and the mountain, once climbed, is now merely looked at from a distance.

In "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," Thoreau describes a house he once considered buying, recalling "a certain house on a mountain which I had visited the year before" (002-010). He imagines living above a "tarn high up on the side of a mountain, its bottom far above the surface of other lakes" (002-012), and from his imagined doorway he can see "still bluer and more distant mountain ranges in the north-west" (002-013). The mountain dwelling is a metaphor for the elevated perspective Thoreau seeks: to live above the plain of ordinary concerns and see farther than one's neighbors.

The mountain also appears as a counterpoint to the pond. In "The Pond in Winter," Thoreau reflects that "the rule also for the height of mountains, regarded as the opposite of valleys, may be applied to the depth of the pond" (016-014). Walden's depth is a kind of inverted mountain — where the mountain rises into the sky, the pond plunges into the earth. Both are measures of vertical extremity, and both invite the imagination beyond the horizontal plane of daily life.

> [001-065] He dwelt, as it were, in a tent in this world, and was either threading the valleys, or crossing the plains, or climbing the mountain tops. But lo! men have become the tools of their tools.

> [002-013] It suggested that there was a finer, still bluer and more distant mountain ranges in the north-west, those true sierras which the eye of the oldest inhabitant had not seen.

> [016-017] As the traveler, a mountain outline varies with every step, and it has an infinite number of profiles, though absolutely but one form.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, the mountain is primarily a figure of thought. He does not climb mountains as a naturalist cataloguing flora; he imagines them as stations of perspective from which the ordinary world resolves into pattern and meaning. The "mountain outline" that "varies with every step" (016-017) is a lesson in the relativity of all perspectives: truth, like the mountain's profile, changes as the observer moves. Yet behind the shifting appearances there is "absolutely but one form" — a single reality that the mountain embodies and that the disciplined mind, moving through many perspectives, may eventually apprehend.
