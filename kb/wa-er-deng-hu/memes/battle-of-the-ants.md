# Battle of the Ants

## Narrative

One afternoon in the summer of 1845, Thoreau stepped outside his cabin to his wood-pile and found himself witness to a war. Two species of ants — one red, one black — were locked in a combat of such ferocity that it recalled, in miniature, the great battles of human history:

> [012-012] It was the only battle which I have ever witnessed, the battle-field before me — the red republicans on the one hand, and the black imperialists on the other. On every side they were engaged in deadly combat, yet without any noise that I could hear, and never soldiers fought so resolutely.

The silence of the battle is what first arrests Thoreau's attention: a struggle for life and death conducted without the noise of gunpowder or the cries of the wounded — purer, in some sense, than human warfare, because more elemental.

He describes individual combatants with the vividness of an epic poet:

> [012-013] I watched a couple that were fast locked in each other's embraces, in a little sunny valley amid the chips, now at high noon prepared to fight till the sun went down, or life went out.

The passage is remarkable for its **scale-shifting**: Thoreau moves between the microscopic (the "little sunny valley amid the chips") and the cosmic (the sun, life, death) without ever breaking the spell. The ants are both themselves and emblems of something larger. When he picks up a wood chip bearing three combatants and carries it indoors to observe under a microscope, the gesture is both scientific and literary — he wants to see more clearly, but he also wants to *tell* what he saw.

The battle has no obvious cause and no decisive outcome. Thoreau does not record which side "won." The point is elsewhere: in the sheer **intensity** of the struggle, which he reads as evidence that the will to fight — the "internecine" impulse — is as fundamental to life as the will to eat or reproduce. The ants fight because fighting is what ants — and men — do.

The episode concludes with Thoreau's reflection on the universality of war, drawing explicit parallels between the ant battle and the great human conflicts recorded in classical literature:

> [012-014] Kirby and Spence tell us that the battles of ants have long been celebrated and the date of them recorded, though it is said that Huber is the only modern author who appears to have witnessed them. "Æneas Sylvius," say they, "after giving a very circumstantial account of a battle between two species of ants, one large and the other small, adds that this battle was fought 'Pontificatu Eugenii Quarti, in the sight of Nicholas Pistoriensis, an eminent lawyer, who related the whole history of the battle with the greatest fidelity.'" A similar engagement between great and small ants is recorded by Olaus Magnus, in which the small ants, being victorious, are said to have buried the bodies of their own soldiers, but left those of their giant enemies a prey to the birds. This event happened previous to the expulsion of the tyrant Christiern the Second from Sweden.

The scholarly apparatus — the citations of Kirby, Spence, Huber, Æneas Sylvius, Olaus Magnus — is only half-serious. Thoreau is both elevating his ant battle to the dignity of recorded history and gently mocking the pretension of doing so. The tonal balance is exquisitely Thoreauvian: earnest about the *meaning* of what he saw, ironic about the *form* in which he reports it.

## Context

The Battle of the Ants occurs in Chapter 12, *Brute Neighbors*, which also includes the famous passage on the loon and Thoreau's encounter with the Canadian woodchopper. The chapter explores the boundary between the human and the animal — and the ant battle, with its explicit comparison to human warfare, is the chapter's most direct meditation on what the two realms share.

Thoreau's description of the ants as "red republicans" and "black imperialists" is, of course, a political allegory. The "republicans" evoke the democratic ideals of the American Revolution; the "imperialists" suggest the monarchies of Europe. But Thoreau does not press the allegory, and the ants resist reduction to simple political symbols. They are, finally, ants — and their inscrutable, instinct-driven violence is perhaps more disturbing than any political reading could capture.

## Significance

The Battle of the Ants is one of the most anthologized passages in Walden, and for good reason. It showcases Thoreau's characteristic gifts: precise natural observation, wit, literary allusion, and the ability to find cosmic significance in the smallest things. It also embodies his philosophical method — the conviction that truth is best approached **obliquely**, through the concrete and the particular, rather than through abstract assertion.
