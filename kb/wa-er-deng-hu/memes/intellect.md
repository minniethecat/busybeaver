# Intellect

## Definition

In *Walden*, intellect is the analytic and discerning power of the mind — "a cleaver; it discerns and rifts its way into the secret of things" (002-030). Thoreau distinguishes sharply between intellectual activity and the deeper wisdom that comes from spiritual awakening and direct experience. The intellect is a tool, not an end — necessary for the examined life but insufficient for the *good* life. Cultivation of the intellect alone, without corresponding moral and spiritual growth, produces what Thoreau calls "intellectual dwarfs" (018-019).

## In Walden

Thoreau opens the book with a question that frames his ambivalence toward intellect: "What is the value of the intellectualness of the civilized man compared with the instinctive wisdom of the savage?" (001-023). He does not answer definitively, but the comparison structures much of *Walden*'s argument. The civilized man has refined his intellect at the cost of his instincts; the savage possesses a wholeness that civilization has fragmented.

> [001-023] What does this assertion of the sacredness of human life and the infinite value of the human soul amount to when half the life of the civilized world, — I mean the intellectualness of the civilized man, — is spent in sharpening the instruments of destruction?

The reading chapter (003) is Thoreau's most concentrated treatment of intellect. He distinguishes between mere literacy — which "a child might read" — and true reading as "a noble intellectual exercise" (003-007): "To read well, that is, to read true books in a true spirit, is a noble exercise" (003-008). But he also warns that "we are underbred and low-lived and illiterate" in this deeper sense, that "the best books are not read even by those who are called good readers" (003-006). Intellectual culture, properly understood, is rare.

Thoreau's neighbor — the unnamed Canadian woodchopper — becomes a case study in the limits of intellect. The woodchopper is described as "deficient in intellect" (006-024) yet possesses a natural wisdom and contentment that Thoreau clearly admires. "He was so genuine and unsophisticated that no introduction would serve to introduce him" (006-024). The woodchopper's deficiency is intellectual; his *fullness* is something else — a wholeness of being that the over-intellectualized lack.

> [006-024] He had been instructed only in that innocent and ineffectual way in which the Catholic priests teach the aborigines, — by which the pupil is taught merely to read and write, but never to think. His intellect was not cultivated, but his moral and animal nature were sound.

The chapter on higher laws (011) presents the most striking image of intellectual cultivation: "He sat down to re-create his intellectual man" (011-026). Thoreau himself, after physical labor, sits to read or think — the intellect as a "man" within the man, requiring its own nourishment and exercise. In the morning, he writes, "I bathe my intellect in the stupendous and cosmogonal philosophy of the Bhagavad-Geeta" (016-026), treating intellectual activity as a kind of ablution.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's view of intellect is fundamentally Transcendentalist: intellect is valuable but partial. "The intellect is a cleaver; it discerns and rifts its way into the secret of things" (002-030) — this is a description of analytic power, but also of violence. The intellect *cleaves*; it divides. The spiritual life, by contrast, unifies. Thoreau wants both — the precision of intellect and the wholeness of spirit — but he is clear about which takes priority: "To be awake is to be alive. I have never yet met a man who was quite awake. How could I have looked him in the face?" (002-022).

His final word on intellect in Walden's conclusion is cautionary: "The moderns generally, are intellectual dwarfs compared with the ancients" (018-019). This is not a dismissal of intellect but a call for its proper scale — intellect in service of character, not as a substitute for it. The "intellectual centipede" (006-027) — a man made entirely of ideas, with no legs to stand on — is Thoreau's image of intellect run amok.
