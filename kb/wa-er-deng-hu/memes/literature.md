# Literature

## Definition

In Walden, literature represents the "noblest recorded thoughts of man" — the enduring written works of genius that transcend their age. Thoreau distinguishes between true literature (the classics and scriptures of all nations) and the cheap, ephemeral reading that occupies most of his contemporaries. For Thoreau, literature is not entertainment but a spiritual discipline, demanding the same deliberate attention that one brings to living.

## In Walden

Thoreau devotes the entire chapter "Reading" to the subject of literature and its neglect. He argues that the written word is "the choicest of relics," more intimate and universal than any other work of art (003-006). While his neighbors pride themselves on literacy, Thoreau observes that they read only "Easy Reading" and storybooks, leaving the classics unread. "Most men are satisfied if they read or hear read... one good book, the Bible, and for the rest of their lives vegetate and dissipate their faculties in what is called easy reading" (003-012).

Thoreau measures Concord's culture and finds it wanting: "There is in this town, with a very few exceptions, no taste for the best or for very good books even in English literature, whose words all can read and spell" (003-015). Even the college-educated have "really little or no acquaintance with the English classics" (003-015). This cultural poverty stands in sharp contrast to the spiritual riches available in books — the "treasured wealth of the world and the fit inheritance of generations and nations" (003-006).

In a striking later passage, Thoreau describes bathing his intellect in the Bhagavad Gita and finding "our modern world and its literature seem puny and trivial" by comparison (016-008). Literature, for Thoreau, connects the solitary dweller at Walden Pond to the sages of India, the poets of Greece, and the wisdom of all ages — the pond water mingling with the sacred water of the Ganges.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's view of literature is radically elitist in the best sense: he insists that the best books exist for everyone, not just the educated few, yet he observes that almost no one avails themselves of them. The "heroic books" require heroic readers. "To read well, that is, to read true books in a true spirit, is a noble exercise, and one that will task the reader more than any exercise which the customs of the day esteem" (003-007). Reading is not passive reception but active, lifelong training — "a training such as the athletes underwent, the steady intention almost of the whole life to this object" (003-007).

Crucially, Thoreau does not separate literature from life. He warns against being merely "a reader, a student merely, or a seer" (003-029), insisting that books must lead outward to direct experience of the world. The purpose of reading is not to accumulate secondhand knowledge but to awaken — to become, as he says, "born again" into the language of truth.

## Historical Context

In nineteenth-century New England, literacy rates were high by global standards, and the proliferation of cheap printing presses, lending libraries, and popular novels created what Thoreau saw as a crisis of discernment. The "modern cheap and fertile press, with all its translations, has done little to bring us nearer to the heroic writers of antiquity" (003-007). Thoreau wrote against the grain of a culture that prided itself on progress in "art and science and literature" (018-010) while neglecting the foundational texts of world civilization. His stance reflects the Transcendentalist conviction that each individual must encounter truth directly, whether through scripture, nature, or the classics — and that institutions of learning had failed to cultivate this direct engagement.
