# Arrival at Walden Pond

Thoreau's move to Walden Pond on July 4, 1845, the symbolic beginning of his experiment in simple living that became the subject of *Walden*.

## Date and Context

Thoreau chose Independence Day—July 4, 1845—to begin his experiment at Walden Pond. The date was not accidental: moving to the woods on the anniversary of American independence represented Thoreau's declaration of independence from the conventions and expectations of society.

> [001-086] When I first took up my abode in the woods, that is, began to spend my nights as well as days there, which, by accident, was on Independence Day, or the Fourth of July, 1845, my house was not done for winter, but was merely a defence against the rain.

## The First Days

Thoreau describes the early days at Walden with a sense of wonder and discovery. His partially-built house was "merely a defence against the rain," with no plastering or chimney, yet he found it "a clean and airy house." The first weeks were spent completing the house and planting his bean field, integrating labor and contemplation from the start.

## Significance

The choice of July 4th is the single most potent symbolic detail in Walden. By beginning his experiment on Independence Day, Thoreau positions his personal withdrawal from society as an act of political and philosophical independence. The simple act of moving to a pond becomes, in Thoreau's telling, a reenactment of the American founding—not of a nation, but of a self.
