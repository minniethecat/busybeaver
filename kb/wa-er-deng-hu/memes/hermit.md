# Hermit

## Definition

In *Walden*, the hermit is a complex figure — at once a persona Thoreau inhabits, a literary archetype he borrows from classical and Eastern traditions, and a social identity he both claims and complicates. The hermit is defined not by mere physical isolation but by a deliberate orientation of attention away from the social world and toward the inner life and the natural order.

## In Walden

Thoreau opens the question of his hermetic identity with a characteristic qualification. In "Visitors," he declares: "I am naturally no hermit, but might possibly sit out the sturdiest frequenter of the bar-room if my business called me thither" (006-001). This denial is itself a performance — Thoreau insists he is no misanthrope, yet his capacity for solitude exceeds that of any ordinary person.

The most sustained treatment of the hermit appears in "Brute Neighbors," which opens with the celebrated "Hermit-Poet" dialogue (012-002 through 012-007). The Hermit speaks first, wondering "what the world is doing now" but confessing he has heard nothing — "I wonder what the world is doing" — before being drawn back into meditation on a knot in his table and a recollection of Asia's ancient contemplatives. When the Poet arrives to summon him fishing, the Hermit protests:

> [012-004] Hermit. I cannot resist. My brown bread will soon be gone.

The dialogue captures the essential tension of the hermit life: the pull between the contemplative call ("Let me see; where was I? Methinks I was in this frame of mind...") and the persistent demands of the body and the social world. The Hermit-Poet exchange is both comic and profound — two modes of being negotiating a shared afternoon, each making space for the other.

Thoreau extends the hermit metaphor beyond the human. Walden Pond itself is described as "reserved and austere, like a hermit in the woods, so long, it has acquired a wonderful purity" (009-037). The pond, in Thoreau's rendering, is a better hermit than he is — more constant, more pure, more indifferent to human attention. By likening the pond to a hermit, Thoreau naturalizes the archetype: solitude is not a human invention but a property of the landscape itself.

In "Former Inhabitants," the hermit appears as a historical figure, not just a literary one: "Ah! such discourse we had, hermit and philosopher, and the old settler I have spoken of — we three" (014-024). This passage, from the allegorical "old settler" section, casts the hermit as one of three eternal types — hermit, philosopher, settler — who converse across time. The hermit here is not Thoreau himself but a companion spirit, a figure from the deep past who validates Thoreau's own solitary practice.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's hermit is never simple. By denying that he is "naturally" a hermit, he distinguishes his solitude from pathology. His hermitage is chosen, provisional, and in dialogue with the social world — he receives visitors, walks to the village, and reads the classics. The hermit for Thoreau is not one who flees society but one who has learned to be alone without loneliness. The Hermit-Poet dialogue dramatizes this: the hermit and the poet need each other, and their fishing trip is a collaboration between two modes of being, not the victory of one over the other.

## Historical Context

The hermit archetype has deep roots in both Western and Eastern traditions. Thoreau read widely in Hindu and Buddhist texts, where the forest-dwelling hermit (vanaprastha) represents a recognized stage of life. In the Western tradition, the Desert Fathers of early Christianity and the Romantic poets' solitary wanderers provided ready models. Thoreau adapts both traditions to the specific conditions of 19th-century New England, creating a hermit who chops wood, grows beans, and reads Homer — a practical mystic whose solitude is a form of citizenship.
