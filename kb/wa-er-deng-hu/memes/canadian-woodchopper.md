# The Canadian Woodchopper

An unnamed Canadian woodchopper and post-maker who visits Thoreau at Walden, portrayed as a model of natural contentment and unconscious wisdom in the Visitors chapter.

## Connection to Walden

The Canadian woodchopper is the most fully realized visitor in Walden. Thoreau devotes several pages to describing him—a Homeric figure in simple guise, a man whose contentment and good humor represent a kind of natural philosophy achieved without books or formal learning. Thoreau's portrait is both affectionate and philosophical, using the woodchopper to explore the relationship between labor, wisdom, and happiness.

> [006-012] Who should come to my lodge this morning but a true Homeric or Paphlagonian man,—he had so suitable and poetic a name that I am sorry I cannot print it here,—a Canadian, a woodchopper and post-maker, who can hole fifty posts in a day, who made his last supper on a woodchuck which his dog caught.

> [006-019] He interested me because he was so quiet and solitary and so happy withal; a well of good humor and contentment which overflowed at his eyes. His mirth was without alloy.

> [006-021] I did not know whether he was as wise as Shakespeare or as simply ignorant as a child, whether to suspect him of a fine poetic consciousness or of stupidity. A townsman told me that when he met him sauntering through the village in his small close-fitting cap, and whistling to himself, he reminded him of a prince in disguise.

## Significance

The Canadian woodchopper embodies a central Walden paradox: that true wisdom may be found in those without book-learning, and that contentment arises not from philosophy studied but from life lived. Thoreau presents him as a kind of natural Homeric hero—a man who works with his hands, lives entirely in the present, and possesses a happiness that eludes the educated and ambitious. The woodchopper's response to the reformer who asks if he wants the world changed—"No, I like it well enough"—is one of the most resonant moments in Walden, a quiet rebuke to the restless dissatisfaction Thoreau diagnoses in modern life.
