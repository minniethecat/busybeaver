# Homer

Ancient Greek epic poet, author of the Iliad and Odyssey, frequently referenced in Walden as the archetype of poetic genius and ancient wisdom.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau kept Homer's *Iliad* on his table at Walden Pond and references the epic poet throughout the book. For Thoreau, Homer represents the highest form of literary achievement—works that repay endless rereading across millennia, in contrast to the ephemeral reading material of his contemporaries.

> [001-043] The student may read Homer or Æschylus in the Greek without danger of dissipation or luxuriousness, for it implies that he in some measure emulate their heroes, and consecrate morning hours to their pages.

> [003-002] I kept Homer's Iliad on my table through the summer, though I looked at his page only now and then. Incessant labor with my hands, at first, for I had my house to finish and my beans to hoe at the same time, made it impossible.

> [003-003] The heroic books, even if printed in the character of our mother tongue, will always be in a language dead to degenerate times; and we must laboriously seek the meaning of each word and line.

## Biography

Homer (c. 8th century BCE) is the presumed author of the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*, the two foundational epics of ancient Greek literature. Little is known of his life; tradition depicts him as a blind bard from Ionia. His works were central to Greek education and remained widely read in Thoreau's classical curriculum at Harvard.

## Significance

Homer embodies Thoreau's ideal of timeless literature. The contrast Thoreau draws between reading Homer in the original Greek and consuming trivial modern publications is central to his argument in the Reading chapter. Homer represents the "heroic books" that demand and reward serious engagement—a standard against which Thoreau measures all reading.
