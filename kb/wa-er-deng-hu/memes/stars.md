# Stars

## Definition

Stars in *Walden* serve Thoreau as symbols of the sublime — distant, innumerable, and indifferent to human categories. They are "the apexes of what wonderful triangles" (001-019), points of light that organize the darkness without dispelling it. Unlike the sun, which insists on visibility, the stars are visible only to those who look up in darkness, and their light is ancient by the time it reaches the pond. For Thoreau, the stars represent the limits of counting and cataloguing: "I cannot count one. I know not the first letter of the alphabet" (002-030). They are the visible face of the infinite, and the proper response to them is not measurement but wonder.

## In Walden

Thoreau's stars appear first in "Economy," where he describes hoeing his bean-field by starlight: "The stars are the apexes of what wonderful triangles" (001-019). The image is geometric but the geometry is "wonderful" — not cold but awe-struck. The stars are not alien to his labor; they participate in it, forming triangles whose base is the earth and whose apex is somewhere in the dark above the bean-rows. Farming by starlight is farming within a cosmos, not merely on a plot of ground.

In "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For," Thoreau deepens the image. The morning, he writes, "is the awakening hour. Then there is least somnolence in us," and yet his mind turns to the pond whose "bottom is pebbly with stars" (002-030). The pond reflects the night sky so perfectly that the stars seem to lie on its bed, pebbles of light in dark water. Thoreau confesses he "cannot count one" — the stars defeat enumeration, and this is their gift. They resist the impulse to inventory, to reduce the world to a catalogue of known quantities.

In "Reading," Thoreau uses the stars as a metaphor for how most people read: "as the multitude read the stars, at most astrologically, not astronomically" (003-007). The distinction is crucial to his educational philosophy. To read "astrologically" is to read for personal application — What does this mean for me? — without understanding the underlying structures. True reading, like true astronomy, requires a grasp of the laws that govern the visible. The stars are the same for all; the difference lies in the depth of attention brought to them.

The stars also serve as a measure of self-sufficiency. Thoreau writes of a farmer who rises "by the light of the stars amid the mountains, to fodder his cattle" (004-012), connecting starlight to the dignity of labor performed before dawn. Later, in "Solitude," he declares that his little house contained "my own sun and moon and stars, and a little world all to myself" (005-003). The stars are not diminished by being "his own"; rather, the claim is that true solitude gives one access to the cosmos on personal terms, not mediated by society or convention.

In "Higher Laws," Thoreau invokes the stars as witnesses to moral possibility. "Those same stars twinkle over other fields than these" (011-026), he writes, reminding the reader that the celestial order is not confined to Walden, that the same light that falls on the bean-field falls on every field, and with it the same moral demands. The stars, in their impartiality, are emblems of the higher law — universal, indifferent to local custom, visible to anyone who looks up.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's stars differ from the Romantic stars of Shelley or Keats in their epistemological humility. The Romantics often used stars as symbols of transcendent truth or immortal beauty; Thoreau uses them as symbols of what cannot be known. "I cannot count one" is not a confession of failure but a statement of philosophical position: there are things that exceed enumeration, and the wise person acknowledges the limit rather than pretending to surpass it. The stars teach negative capability — the capacity to remain in uncertainty without irritable reaching after fact.
