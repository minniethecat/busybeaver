# Clock

## Definition

In *Walden*, the clock represents artificial, mechanical time — the measured, regimented schedule of civilized society that Thoreau rejects in favor of natural rhythms. The clock is not merely a timekeeping device but an emblem of the hurried, economically-driven life that divorces human beings from the seasons, the sun, and their own inner pace.

## In Walden

Thoreau systematically replaces clock-time with nature-time throughout his Walden experiment. He describes his morning routine not by the hour but by the sunrise: "Every morning was a cheerful invitation to make my life of equal simplicity, and I may say innocence, with Nature herself" (002-021). Rather than being governed by a timepiece, he bathes in the pond as a "religious exercise," aligning his day with natural cycles rather than mechanical ones.

The railroad serves as the new clock of industrial society, its schedule dictating the village's daily rhythm. Thoreau observes that "the startings and arrivals of the cars are now the epochs in the village day. They go and come with such regularity and precision" (004-013). The train's whistle replaces the church bell as the community's time signal, yet Thoreau treats this with irony — the railroad's "precision" mimics nature's own regularity without its deeper meaning.

Thoreau finds a superior clock in the whippoorwills, which "would begin to sing almost with as much precision as a clock, within five minutes of a particular time, referred to the setting of the sun, every evening" (004-031). The birds keep time not by mechanism but by their attunement to the sun's position — a living clock embedded in the cosmos rather than imposed upon it.

Even Thoreau's labor follows this principle. Working his bean-field, he notes: "I used to hoe from five o'clock in the morning till noon, and commonly spent the rest of the day about other affairs" (007-010). The hours are mentioned, but they mark the sun's position — early morning to noon — rather than a schedule imposed by an employer or social convention. The work ends when the sun reaches its zenith, not when a clock says to stop.

> [004-002] Sometimes, in a summer morning, having taken my accustomed bath, I sat in my sunny doorway from sunrise till noon, rapt in a revery, amidst the pines and hickories and sumachs, in undisturbed solitude and stillness, while the birds sing around or flitted noiseless through the house.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's rejection of the clock is part of his broader critique of the division of labor and the industrialization of time. In the famous passage "Time is but the stream I go a-fishing in," he suggests that time is not a resource to be measured and spent but a medium to be immersed in. The clock, by dividing the day into abstract, exchangeable units, turns lived experience into a commodity. Thoreau's alternative is to let natural phenomena — the sun, the seasons, the birds, the pond's freezing and thawing — mark the passage of time. His "broad margin" to life (004-002) is precisely the space outside clock-time where contemplation and genuine experience become possible.

## Historical Context

In mid-nineteenth-century New England, the clock was becoming a central tool of industrial discipline. The factory system required workers to arrive, labor, and leave by the clock; the railroad demanded standardized time across towns. Thoreau wrote *Walden* in the same decade that mechanical clock production was rapidly expanding in Connecticut's clock-making industry. His resistance to clock-time was not mere pastoral nostalgia but a specific critique of how industrial capitalism was reshaping human experience of time itself.
