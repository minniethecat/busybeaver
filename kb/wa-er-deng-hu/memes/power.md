# Power

## Definition

In *Walden*, power operates on multiple registers: the creative power of nature that "fashions their being" (005-009), the coercive power of social institutions, and the inward power of the individual who lives deliberately. Thoreau is skeptical of institutional power — government, railroad corporations, the press — but reverent toward the "subtle powers of Heaven and of Earth" (005-010) that shape life from within rather than imposing upon it from without.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most concentrated reflection on power appears in "Solitude" (Chapter 005), where he distinguishes between the noisy force of human institutions and the silent, pervasive influence of natural processes. "Nearest to all things is that power which fashions their being" (005-009) — this immanent creative power is the opposite of the railroad's external, violent intrusion. The railroad, Walden's emblem of industrial force, is described as requiring "a powerful press first, to squeeze the old Roman people out" (001-044) — institutional power flattens and homogenizes.

> [005-009] Nearest to all things is that power which fashions their being. Next to us the grandest laws are being enacted and administered.

The individual's relation to power is not passive. Thoreau insists on a form of power that cannot be overcome: "that on which no power can resist me" (018-024). This is the power of deliberate living — the freedom to set one's own terms, independent of the state's claims. His night in jail (described in Chapter 008) dramatizes this: the state can imprison the body but cannot reach the mind that has chosen its own allegiance.

> [018-024] ...that on which no power can resist me. It affords me no satisfaction to commence to spring an arch before I have got a foundation.

Nature's power is, for Thoreau, gentler and more effective than human coercion. "His gentle persuasion is more powerful than Thor with his hammer" (017-010) — the thawing of the pond in spring, accomplished not by force but by the gradual influence of warmth, becomes a model of effective power. "The same sweet and powerful song as of yore" (017-015) locates power in continuity and sweetness rather than in domination.

> [017-010] His gentle persuasion is more powerful than Thor with his hammer. That melts the icebergs and the ice in the pond.

The power of the "salt" that fertilizes the bean-field (007-011) — "the salt, power, or virtue (call it either) which draws the moisture" — suggests that Thoreau sees power as akin to a natural property, an inherent virtue that attracts and nourishes, not a possession to be wielded.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of power reflects his anarchist leanings and his Transcendentalist faith in inner authority. Institutional power — whether political ("the state which buys and sells men, women, and children") or economic (the railroad, the bank) — is suspect precisely because it substitutes external compulsion for the individual's direct access to moral truth. True power, for Thoreau, is indistinguishable from virtue: it persuades rather than compels, and it operates through affinity and attraction rather than through force. This is why he can claim, paradoxically, that the deliberately lived life is immune to all earthly power — not because it is physically invulnerable, but because it has ceased to recognize the legitimacy of external coercion.
