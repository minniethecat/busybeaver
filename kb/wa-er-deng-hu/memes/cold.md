# Cold

## Definition

Cold in *Walden* is both a physical reality — the New England winter is long and bitter — and a philosophical condition. Thoreau treats cold not as an enemy to be defeated by stoves and overcoats but as a discipline, a clarifying agent that sharpens perception and strips away softness. To be cold, in Thoreau's understanding, is to be honest; warmth, by contrast, can be a form of self-deception, a comfort that dulls the senses and encourages complacency. Cold is the season of truth.

## In Walden

Thoreau's Walden winter is measured in cold. The pond water "is as cold as it is pure at all times" (009-013), and the purity is linked to the cold: what is cold resists corruption. The ice-cutters of winter harvest cold itself, sawing blocks from the frozen pond as if cold were a crop, a commodity to be shipped to warmer latitudes. But Thoreau sees the deeper truth: cold cannot really be exported. It remains the condition of Walden Pond, the permanent quality beneath the summer's warm surface.

The house, simple and unplastered, offers little refuge. On a winter morning, "when they were numbed with cold, I swept some of them out" (013-003), Thoreau writes of the wasps that shared his dwelling. The cold is democratic: it affects man and insect alike. His solution is not to fight the cold with greater fires but to accept it, to let the body learn its limits and then discover that those limits are wider than supposed. "A little colder Friday, or greater snow, would put a period to man's existence on the globe" (013-028), he reflects, and the thought is not morbid but bracing — an honest accounting of human fragility.

The cold is also a social filter. In warm weather, visitors multiply; in cold, only the serious remain. "In cold weather we eat more, in warm less" (001-023), Thoreau observes, and the logic extends beyond diet: cold weather demands more of everything — more food, more fuel, more resolve. Those who cannot meet the demand stay away, and the winter solitude at Walden is correspondingly deeper, more productive.

Thoreau's own relationship to cold is ambivalent. He criticizes those who have "a long habit of shrinking from cold and misery" (001-062), arguing that the shrinking is itself the misery — a habit of avoidance that weakens more than any frost. "There is no more use to complain that this is a cold world" (001-024), he writes, because the cold is not an injustice but a fact, and facts are to be met, not lamented. The same cold that "chills men's blood" (004-014) also invigorates, waking the sleeper from his comfortable stupor.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's philosophy of cold is rooted in his broader ethics of simplicity. Cold strips away the unnecessary; it is nature's own form of asceticism. The man who can endure cold needs less — less clothing, less fuel, less house — and needing less is the Thoreauvian definition of wealth. "To cold, no less than to heat, we owe a debt" (001-024), he writes, and the debt is this: cold teaches us what we actually require to live, as opposed to what we merely desire.

The endurance of cold is also a form of attention. When one is cold, one notices the world with a particular sharpness — every draft, every patch of sun, every small shelter. Cold compels presence; it is difficult to be absent-minded when one's body is insistently reminding one of its condition. Thoreau values this bodily honesty as a counter to the abstracted, disembodied life of the mind that he, as a writer and thinker, is always at risk of falling into.
