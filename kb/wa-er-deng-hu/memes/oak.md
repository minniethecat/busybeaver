# Oak

## Definition

The oak appears in *Walden* in several distinct forms, each carrying a different symbolic weight: the **shrub oak** (*Quercus ilicifolia*), low and tenacious, covering the plateau opposite Thoreau's dwelling; the **white oak** (*Quercus alba*), whose bark is gathered for tanning and medicine; and the solitary, gnarled oak that survives where human habitation has failed. Together these oaks form a counterpoint to the pines — the oak is deciduous, hard-wooded, slower-growing, and mythologically associated with strength and endurance. In Thoreau's landscape, the oak is both a practical resource (bark, fuel, timber) and a philosophical marker: to stand beneath an oak is to stand in the presence of time.

## In Walden

The first oak in *Walden* appears as an object of deliberation. Walking his newly purchased hollowell farm before the owner's wife changed her mind, Thoreau pauses at the property line "to decide what fine oaks or pines should be left to stand before the house" (002-001). The decision — which trees to spare — is a miniature of Thoreau's larger philosophy: living deliberately means choosing what to preserve and what to cut, in the landscape as in the soul.

The most sustained oak presence is the shrub-oak plateau. Thoreau looks out from his house across the pond to "the low shrub-oak plateau to which the opposite shore rises" (002-014). This plateau is not majestic — shrub oaks are low, scrubby, unprepossessing — but it is Thoreau's horizon, the limit of his daily visual world. In the bean-field chapter, he works "before the sun had got above the shrub oaks, while all the dew was on" (007-004). The shrub oaks mark the threshold between night and day: when the sun clears them, the dew evaporates and the work of the morning is done.

In "Brute Neighbors," Thoreau describes a hawk "perched on some new perch on the gray oaks" (004-032), the bird using the oak as a hunting platform. The oak serves as infrastructure for the wild community — a perch, a shelter, a larder of acorns. Elsewhere, the oak is associated with human failure. Walking the site of a former homestead, Thoreau notes "some pitch-pine or gnarled oak occupies what was the chimney nook" (014-012). The house is gone, its inhabitants forgotten, but the oak has rooted in the cellar hole and grown where the hearth once was. The oak is a witness to transience — it outlasts the human structures it absorbs.

The white oak appears in practical context: Thoreau's neighbor "has a great bundle of white-oak bark under his arm for a sick man" (006-017). Oak bark was a standard nineteenth-century astringent and febrifuge, part of the herbal pharmacopoeia that Thoreau knew well. The oak is medicine, not just metaphor.

> [002-001] I had been a sojourner in the hollowell farm, and had got a right of preemption to it, and had even begun to lay out grounds, and to decide what fine oaks or pines should be left to stand before the house.

> [007-004] Before yet any woodchuck or squirrel had run across the road, or the sun had got above the shrub oaks, while all the dew was on, though the farmers warned me against it,—I would advise you to do all your work if possible while the dew is on,—I began to level the ranks of haughty weeds in my bean-field.

> [014-012] Some pitch-pine or gnarled oak occupies what was the chimney nook, and the sweet-scented black birch where the door-stone was.

## Thoreau's Perspective

The oak serves Thoreau as a memento mori in the landscape. Where pines suggest aspiration (the arrowy upward reach), oaks suggest rootedness — the gnarled oak that grows in the cellar hole is a monument to what has passed, a living gravestone. Thoreau's interest in the "former inhabitants" of Walden Woods is mediated through these oaks: the tree is what remains when all else is gone. Yet the oak is not mournful. It is simply durable. Thoreau's prose around the oak tends toward the matter-of-fact: he does not sermonize over the gnarled oak in the chimney nook; he notes it, and the notation itself is the philosophy.
