# God

## Definition

Thoreau's God is not the stern Calvinist judge of his New England inheritance but a presence diffused through the natural world. Throughout Walden, "God" and "the gods" appear interchangeably — Thoreau draws as readily on classical pantheism as on Christian monotheism. His God is encountered at the pond's edge, in the bean-field, and in the "travelling demigod" of the railroad locomotive (004-011), not in the meetinghouse. For Thoreau, the divine is wherever attention and reverence meet.

## In Walden

Thoreau invokes God and the gods with a distinctive blend of reverence and familiarity. The pond is "sacred to some god" (013-013), not because a church has consecrated it but because its beauty compels worship. The Roman "made an expiatory offering" to the god Terminus at boundary stones — Thoreau treats the pond's shores as similarly sacred precincts. The locomotive becomes a "travelling demigod, this cloud-compeller" (004-011), its smoke and thunder likened to Zeus. Thoreau's pantheon is populated by the entities he encounters daily: the pond, the train, the hawk, the loon.

Thoreau is particularly attentive to the way people miss the divine by looking past the world. "Who goes to market for his god as it is?" he asks of a farmer whose land holds Walden Pond but who sees only real estate (009-039). The question is devastating in its simplicity: God is present where one stands, but the commercial eye cannot perceive it. "On whose farm nothing grows free," Thoreau continues, suggesting that the divine is precisely what cannot be commodified — the wild, the unearned, the gratuitously beautiful.

The gods are also witnesses. Thoreau speaks of being "more favored by the gods than they" (005-004) — the "they" being farmers who own their land but do not see it. Divine favor, in this lexicon, is not luck or election but attentiveness. The "gods of Troy" removed by a later hand (001-079) serve as a warning: gods can be displaced, sacred things profaned, when people cease to honor what is before them.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's theology is deliberately unorthodox. He mixes classical and Christian references freely — "the god of loons" (012-017), the "swamp gods" whose round tables cover the ground (010-001), and "the removal of the gods of Troy" (001-079) sit comfortably alongside his references to Christian scripture. This syncretism is not confusion but method: Thoreau treats all mythologies as human attempts to name the ineffable, and he insists that Walden itself is the primary scripture.

Crucially, Thoreau's God does not replace human agency. "I know of no more encouraging fact than the unquestionable ability of man to elevate his life by a conscious endeavor," he writes (001-010). The divine enables but does not substitute for deliberate living. "We are all sculptors and painters, and our material is our own flesh and blood and bones" — each person builds the temple and chooses the god worshipped within (011-025). This is Thoreau's most radical theological claim: the divine is real, but it requires human collaboration to become manifest.
