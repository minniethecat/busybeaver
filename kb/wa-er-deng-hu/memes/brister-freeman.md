# Brister Freeman

A formerly enslaved man who lived on Brister's Hill near Walden Pond, memorialized by Thoreau in the Former Inhabitants chapter of Walden.

## Connection to Walden

Brister Freeman was one of the former inhabitants of the woods around Walden Pond whose story Thoreau preserves in the "Former Inhabitants; and Winter Visitors" chapter. Brister's Hill, named after him, was part of Thoreau's daily landscape, and Thoreau records his story as part of the layered human history of the Walden woods. Freeman represents the marginalized figures whose lives, though largely forgotten by official history, shaped the land Thoreau inhabited.

> [014-004] Down the road, on the right hand, on Brister's Hill, lived Brister Freeman, "a handy Negro," slave of Squire Cummings once—there where grow still the apple trees which Brister planted and tended; large old trees now, but their fruit still wild and ciderish to my taste.

> [014-005] Brister Freeman was the owner of a considerable piece of land, which he had fenced and cultivated. He had been a soldier in the Revolution, and had fought at Bunker Hill.

## Biography

Brister Freeman (c. 1744–c. 1822) was born enslaved in Massachusetts and belonged to Squire Cummings of Concord. He gained his freedom—the origin of his surname—and served as a soldier in the American Revolution, fighting at the Battle of Bunker Hill. After the war, he settled on land near Walden Pond that became known as Brister's Hill, where he planted an orchard of apple trees. Thoreau notes that Brister's apple trees were still growing on the hillside decades after his death, a living memorial to the man who cultivated them.

## Significance

Brister Freeman embodies the theme of invisible histories that runs through Walden. Thoreau excavates the stories of those who lived on the land before him—the formerly enslaved, the poor, the outcast—and in doing so, challenges the official narratives of Concord's past. Freeman's story is all the more powerful for its brevity: a few paragraphs in Walden preserve what would otherwise have been entirely lost. The apple trees that "still" grow on Brister's Hill serve as Thoreau's metaphor for how the past persists into the present, visible to those who take the time to look.
