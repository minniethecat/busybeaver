# Darkness

## Definition

Darkness in *Walden* functions on two planes: the literal darkness of the New England woods at night, through which Thoreau learns to move without fear, and the metaphorical darkness of spiritual ignorance — the unexamined life that "puts out our eyes" (018-029). Thoreau treats darkness not as a deficiency of light to be remedied but as a condition that reveals what daylight conceals. Humility, he writes, "like darkness reveals the heavenly lights" (018-023); the stars are only visible when the greater light is withdrawn.

## In Walden

Thoreau's relationship to darkness is practical before it is philosophical. He recounts walking the village streets "when the darkness was so thick that you could cut it with a knife" (008-002), navigating by touch and memory rather than sight. This is not a complaint but an observation: darkness is a fact of the New England night, and the person who would live deliberately must learn its textures. He crosses the pond in darkness, "whistling a tune" (009-003), and the detail is important — the whistle is evidence that he is not afraid, that darkness has become a familiar medium rather than an obstacle.

The woods at night held a particular darkness for Thoreau, one he associated with the primeval. "The earth and did the deeds of darkness" (004-032) carries the weight of ancient forests, the pre-colonial wilderness that still lingered in the deeper woods. This darkness is not evil but old — older than the cleared fields and white-painted houses of Concord, older than the moral categories that would condemn it. Thoreau is drawn to it precisely because it resists domestication.

Darkness also bears fruit. The phrase "the darkness bear its fruit, and prove its" (002-021) links darkness to fertility — the seed germinates in the dark soil, the night gives way to the morning, and spiritual growth often happens in obscurity, invisible to the watching world. Thoreau, who spent two years in the woods "to live deliberately," understood that most of that living happened unobserved, in the dark soil of solitude.

His visitors came "through snow and rain and darkness, till he saw my lamp through the trees" (014-021), the lamp a small beacon in the vast darkness of the Walden woods. The image is Thoreau's own: the hermit-philosopher, his single lamp visible through the trees, a point of light that does not dispel the darkness so much as define it. Darkness is the condition in which a single lamp becomes significant.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's darkness is Emersonian in its foundations but more experiential in its texture. Emerson wrote of the "transparent eyeball" that sees all; Thoreau writes of the darkness that cannot be seen through, that must be felt. His is a philosophy of opacity — the acknowledgement that some things resist illumination, and that the attempt to illuminate everything is itself a form of violence against mystery.

Darkness, for Thoreau, is also the precondition of dawn. "Only that day dawns to which we are awake" (018-029), he writes, and the corollary is that those who have not known darkness cannot recognize dawn. The darkness of the unexamined life — the life of "quiet desperation" — is the darkness from which awakening is possible. Without darkness, awakening would have no meaning. Thoreau's darkness is thus ultimately hopeful: it is the night that makes morning precious.
