# Zoroaster

Ancient Persian prophet and founder of Zoroastrianism, cited by Thoreau in Walden's Reading chapter alongside other ancient sages.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau invokes Zoroaster in the Reading chapter as part of his argument for the universality of ancient wisdom. Zoroaster appears alongside figures from multiple wisdom traditions—Greek, Christian, Hindu—suggesting Thoreau's syncretic vision of spiritual knowledge that transcends any single tradition.

> [003-011] It is true, we are such poor historians that we think it necessary to make a free-stone monument of our hero, and a New England village of the next generation of men. But Zoroaster, thousands of years ago, travelled the same road and had the same experience; but he, being wise, knew it to be universal, and treated his neighbors accordingly, and is even said to have invented the art of making a free-stone monument.

## Biography

Zoroaster (also known as Zarathustra) was an ancient Persian prophet, traditionally dated to around 1500 BCE. He founded Zoroastrianism, one of the world's oldest monotheistic religions, which emphasized the cosmic struggle between truth (Asha) and falsehood (Druj). Zoroastrian thought influenced later Persian, Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions. Thoreau would have encountered Zoroaster through classical sources (Herodotus, Plutarch) and the Orientalist scholarship of his time.

## Significance

Zoroaster's appearance in Walden is part of Thoreau's strategy of building a universal chorus of ancient wisdom. By placing Zoroaster alongside Christ, the Vedas, and the Greek philosophers, Thoreau constructs a trans-cultural canon that supports his arguments for simplicity, self-knowledge, and deliberate living. The reference is characteristically brief but strategically placed—Zoroaster is invoked not as an exotic curiosity but as a co-equal authority.
