# Concord, Massachusetts

The historic Massachusetts town where Thoreau lived, wrote, and situated his Walden experiment — the social and economic world he both inhabited and critiqued.

## Location

Concord is located about eighteen miles northwest of Boston, Massachusetts. Walden Pond itself lies approximately a mile and a half south of the village center.

Thoreau situates Concord within the regional geography of Middlesex County, noting its relation to surrounding towns such as Lincoln, Acton, and Bedford whose church bells could be heard from Walden Pond when the wind was favorable.

> [004-029] I have heard the bells of Lincoln, Acton, Bedford, or Concord bell, when the wind was favorable, a faint, sweet, and, as it were, natural melody, worth importing into the wilderness.

## In Walden

Thoreau's relationship to Concord is central to the structure of Walden. The town is the world he partially withdraws from yet remains connected to.

He walks there regularly, does business there, observes its inhabitants, and uses it as a foil for his critique of 19th-century American life. The opening chapter, Economy, is largely a critique of his Concord neighbors and their economic assumptions.

> [001-003] I have travelled a good deal in Concord; and everywhere, in shops, and offices, and fields, the inhabitants have appeared to me to be doing penance in a thousand remarkable ways.

> [001-055] My neighbors, the farmers of Concord, who are at least as well off as the other classes, I find that for the most part they have been toiling twenty, thirty, or forty years, that they may become the real owners of their farms.

## History

Founded in 1635, Concord was one of the earliest English settlements in Massachusetts. By Thoreau's time it was an established farming community with a strong intellectual culture.

It was home to Ralph Waldo Emerson and the Transcendentalist circle, and had already played a role in the American Revolution. Thoreau's Walden is simultaneously rooted in this specific New England place and engaged with timeless philosophical questions.

## Natural Features

Thoreau describes Concord's waters as defining features of the landscape. Walden, Flint's, White Pond, Fair Haven, Goose Pond, and the Concord River form what he calls his "lake country."

> [009-008] A lake is the landscape's most beautiful and expressive feature. It is earth's eye; looking into which the beholder measures the depth of his own nature. The fluviatile trees next the shore are the slender eyelashes which fringe it, and the wooded hills and cliffs around are its overhanging brows.
