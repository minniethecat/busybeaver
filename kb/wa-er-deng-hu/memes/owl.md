# Owl

## Definition

In *Walden*, the owl is the voice of midnight truth — a creature whose eerie, mournful cry embodies the wildness that civilized society suppresses. Thoreau embraces the owl's darker qualities rather than domesticating it into conventional wisdom. Its hooting is nature's honest acknowledgment of mortality, solitude, and the unexamined depths of both forest and human soul.

## In Walden

Thoreau introduces the owl in the "Sounds" chapter as the night counterpart to the daytime chorus of birds, trains, and church bells. When diurnal birds fall silent, "the screech owls take up the strain, like mourning women their ancient u-lu-lu" (004-032). The comparison to professional mourners casts the owl's cry as a ritual acknowledgment of death.

Thoreau calls them "wise midnight hags," reclaiming the pejorative as a term of respect for those who speak truths the daylight world avoids.

The hooting owl deepens the association with mortality. Thoreau describes its sound as nature's deliberate memorial: "as if she meant by this to stereotype and make permanent in her choir the dying moans of a human being" (004-033). The voice is "made more awful by a certain gurgling melodiousness," crossing the boundary between human anguish and animal instinct.

Thoreau's most direct declaration comes in a single sentence: "I rejoice that there are owls" (004-034). He values them because they do what human society cannot — "Let them do the idiotic and maniacal hooting for men." The owl gives voice to the "unsatisfied thoughts which all have," the dark regions as real and necessary as the sunlit surface.

The chapter closes with a comic contrast: the rooster's crow, Thoreau imagines, would "surpass the clangor of the goose and the hooting of the owl" (004-036). The comparison is affectionate — the owl's hooting has become for Thoreau the baseline of wild music, the standard against which other natural sounds are measured.

The owl reappears in "Winter Animals," where Thoreau hears it as the defining sound of winter nights: "I heard the forlorn but melodious note of a hooting owl indefinitely far" (015-002). He calls it "the very *lingua vernacula* of Walden Wood," the native tongue of the place. In this winter setting, the owl is no longer a distant serenader but an intimate presence heard every evening.

The most dramatic winter encounter occurs when a "cat-owl" responds to a passing goose with "the most harsh and tremendous voice I ever heard from any inhabitant of the woods" (015-002). Thoreau ventriloquizes the owl's indignation: "What do you mean by alarming the citadel at this time of night consecrated to me?" (015-002).

The scene transforms the owl from a distant symbol into a living, territorial creature — jealous of its domain, proud of its voice, indifferent to human standards of beauty.

> [004-034] I rejoice that there are owls. Let them do the idiotic and maniacal hooting for men. It is a sound admirably suited to swamps and twilight woods which no day illustrates, suggesting a vast and undeveloped nature which men have not recognized. They represent the stark twilight and unsatisfied thoughts which all have.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's owl is a figure for the philosopher who tells uncomfortable truths. Just as the owl's cry disturbs the sleeper's peace, the genuine thinker disturbs society's complacency. The owl does not sing to please; it hoots because it must.

Thoreau's admiration for this refusal to prettify reality is central to his own authorial voice. He too wishes to be a "wise midnight hag," sounding notes the daylight world would prefer not to hear. The owl teaches that wisdom and melancholy are inseparable — the truest music may be the least conventionally beautiful.

## Historical Context

In classical mythology, the owl was sacred to Athena and symbolized wisdom. In European folklore, however, the owl's nocturnal habits and eerie cry associated it with death and witchcraft.

Thoreau draws on both traditions: he retains the classical link between owl and wisdom but restores the folkloric darkness that conventional symbolism had sanitized. The result is a distinctively Romantic owl — wise not despite its darkness but because of it.
