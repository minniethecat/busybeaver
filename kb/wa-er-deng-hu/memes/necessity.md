# Necessity

## Definition

The concept of necessity anchors the philosophical argument of Walden's opening chapter, "Economy." Thoreau undertakes to determine, through experiment rather than speculation, what is actually necessary for human life — as opposed to what convention, fashion, and anxiety have persuaded people they need. The distinction between necessity and luxury is the conceptual engine of Thoreau's entire critique of nineteenth-century American life.

## In Walden

Thoreau announces his project with characteristic directness, identifying the four irreducible necessities:

> [001-024] The grand necessity, then, for our bodies, is to keep warm, to keep the vital heat in us.

From this single physiological principle, Thoreau derives the four necessities — Food, Shelter, Clothing, and Fuel — and proceeds to show how simply and directly each can be obtained. The problem is not that necessities are hard to meet but that people have been persuaded to mistake luxuries for necessities:

> [001-005] By a seeming fate, commonly called necessity, they are employed, as it says in an old book, laying up treasures which moth and rust will corrupt.

Thoreau's most radical claim is that much of what passes for necessity is in fact a form of voluntary servitude. The farmer who works himself to death to buy more land has confused necessity with accumulation:

> [001-037] I studied rather how to avoid the necessity of selling them. The life which men praise and regard as successful is but one kind.

Even neighborly assistance, Thoreau suggests, should arise from genuine goodwill rather than the "necessity" of reciprocal obligation:

> [001-081] Rather from neighborliness than from any necessity, I set up the frame of my house.

Thoreau's own fishing, too, is driven not by economic need but by philosophical curiosity:

> [011-003] I fished from the same kind of necessity that the first fishers did.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's concept of necessity is deliberately minimalist — he wants to discover the absolute floor below which life becomes impossible, and then live just above it. This is not asceticism for its own sake but a strategic reduction: by minimizing necessary labor, he maximizes time for what he considers truly necessary to the soul — reading, thinking, walking, and writing. The "grand necessity" of keeping warm becomes a principle of liberation, not deprivation.

## Historical Context

Thoreau's analysis of necessity participates in a long philosophical tradition — from Epicurus's classification of desires (natural and necessary, natural and unnecessary, vain) to the Stoic distinction between what is within our control and what is not. More immediately, Thoreau responds to the economic anxieties of antebellum New England, where the market revolution had transformed subsistence farmers into debt-burdened producers of commodities, convincing them that "necessities" now included store-bought goods their grandparents had never known.
