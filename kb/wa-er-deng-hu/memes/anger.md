# Anger

## Definition

Anger in Walden is never mere personal irritation. Thoreau consistently elevates it to classical or cosmic scale. The mosquito's hum is "an Iliad and Odyssey in the air, singing its own wrath and wanderings" (002-021); the ant is "some Achilles, who had nourished his wrath apart" (012-012); the loon's god is "angry with me" (012-017). Anger is the energy that drives conflict, the spark that ignites both war and moral reform, and — in Thoreau's hands — a force to be understood rather than repressed.

## In Walden

Thoreau's first treatment of anger is unexpectedly musical. In "Where I Lived," the mosquito's dawn hum becomes "Homer's requiem; itself an Iliad and Odyssey in the air, singing its own wrath and wanderings" (002-021). The mosquito is the epic poet of the morning, and its subject is wrath — the same μῆνις (mēnis) that opens the Iliad. Thoreau is not being whimsical. He is claiming that anger, the oldest subject of Western literature, is present even in the smallest creature, at the quietest hour. Wrath is woven into the fabric of being.

The most sustained treatment of anger comes in the ant-war passage of "Brute Neighbors" (Chapter 012). Thoreau describes a single red ant arriving late to the battle: "Or perchance he was some Achilles, who had nourished his wrath apart, and had now come to avenge or rescue his Patroclus" (012-012). The Homeric parallel is exact: Achilles' wrath — his μῆνις — was nursed in solitude, away from the fighting, until the death of Patroclus gave it a target. Thoreau sees in the ant the same sequence: withdrawal, brooding anger, return to combat.

In the same chapter, during the loon episode, Thoreau records an experience of perceived divine anger. After pursuing the loon across the pond for an hour, a wind suddenly rises: "I was impressed as if it were the prayer of the loon answered, and his god was angry with me" (012-017). The anger is cosmic, not personal — the loon's god, like Achilles' Apollo, intervenes on behalf of its favorite. Thoreau registers this with characteristic ambivalence: he respects the anger even as it defeats him.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau does not condemn anger. He is interested in what anger reveals. The mosquito's wrath-wanderings are "cosmical" — they participate in the "everlasting vigor and fertility of the world" (002-021). The ant's wrath is the motive force of heroic action. The loon-god's anger is the natural order pushing back against human intrusion. In each case, anger is not pathology but vitality.

This is consistent with Thoreau's larger philosophy. A life of "quiet desperation" (001-001) is a life without even the energy for anger. To be angry is at least to be engaged. The question is not whether to feel anger but what to do with it: channel it into reform, into art, into the deliberate reconstruction of one's life, or let it curdle into bitterness. "Moral reform is the effort to throw off sleep" (002-021) — and anger, properly directed, is one of the forces that can accomplish that awakening.
