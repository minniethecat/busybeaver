# Reading

## Definition

Reading in *Walden* is not passive consumption but a demanding intellectual and spiritual discipline. Thoreau distinguishes sharply between "easy reading" — newspapers, popular fiction, and translated summaries — and the serious study of classical texts in their original languages.

For Thoreau, true reading is an act of aspiration: the reader must rise to meet the text, not expect the text to descend to the reader's level. This conception places reading at the center of his program for self-cultivation and intellectual independence.

## In Walden

Reading was woven into the daily fabric of Thoreau's life at the pond. He describes his routine as alternating between physical labor and quiet study:

> [008-001] After hoeing, or perhaps reading and writing, in the forenoon, I usually bathed again in the pond, swimming across one of its coves for a stint, and washed the dust of labor from my person, or smoothed out the last wrinkle which study had made, and for the afternoon was absolutely free.

Thoreau devotes an entire chapter, "Reading" (003), to articulating his philosophy of serious study. He opens by asserting that his life at Walden Pond was more conducive to serious reading than any university:

> [003-002] My residence was more favorable, not only to thought, but to serious reading, than a university; and though I was beyond the range of the ordinary circulating library, I had more than ever come within the influence of those books which circulate round the world, whose sentences were first written on stone and are not yet one jot decayed.

He laments that most people never truly read, having learned to decode words in childhood but never progressing beyond that mechanical skill:

> [003-007] The works of the great poets have never yet been read by mankind, for only great poets can read them. They have only been read as the multitude read the stars, at most astrologically, not astronomically. Most men have learned to read to serve a paltry convenience, as they have learned to cipher in order to keep accounts and not be cheated in trade; but of reading as a noble intellectual exercise they know little or nothing.

He extends this critique to the reading habits of his Concord neighbors:

> [003-008] I think that having learned our letters we should read the best that is in literature, and not be forever repeating our a b abs, and words of one syllable, in the fourth or fifth classes, sitting on the lowest and foremost form all our lives.

Thoreau insists that the classics — Homer, Virgil, the ancient philosophers — must be read in their original Greek and Latin, not in translation. The labor of learning an ancient language is precisely what makes reading worthwhile, transforming the reader in the process.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's view of reading is inseparable from his transcendentalist conviction that truth is eternal. Ancient classics survive because they speak to the permanent human condition; modern books are mostly ephemeral.

Reading, properly practiced, is participation in a conversation that spans millennia. His insistence on original languages may seem elitist, but his rationale is democratic: anyone willing to do the work can join the conversation. The obstacle is not access but effort. His philosophy of reading is, finally, a philosophy of self-reliance applied to the life of the mind.
