# Trade / Commerce

## Definition

Trade in *Walden* is the economic practice that Thoreau simultaneously engages in and condemns. He sells his beans, surveys land for payment, and keeps meticulous accounts of his income and expenses — yet he consistently treats commerce as a degradation of human possibility, a system that "makes your head, as well as your fingers, serve your belly" (001-017). This doubleness is not hypocrisy but philosophy: Thoreau participates in the market economy precisely to demonstrate how little of it one actually needs. Trade is unavoidable, but it is not central, and the art of living deliberately consists in keeping it in its proper place — peripheral to the true business of life, which is not exchange but experience.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most systematic treatment of trade appears in "Economy," where he documents, with ironic precision, the balance sheet of his Walden experiment. Every nail, every board, every pound of rye meal is accounted for, and the totals are presented with the gravity of a merchant's ledger. The parody is unmistakable: Thoreau is performing the language of commerce in order to expose its limitations. "If your trade is with the Celestial Empire," he writes, "some small settling of accounts may take place on this side of the water" (001-039) — the metaphor of celestial trade reframes commerce as a spiritual transaction, and the ledger of dollars and cents as a pale shadow of the true accounting.

The railroad is the physical embodiment of trade in Walden, and Thoreau's ambivalence toward it is instructive. The "iron horse" brings goods, news, and visitors to Concord; it is "the trader" whose enterprise and energy Thoreau cannot help but admire. Yet the railroad also represents the subordination of nature to commerce, the transformation of landscape into corridor. The men who build and run the railroad — "the mechanics and tradesmen who come in person to the pond" (013-014) — are both the agents of this transformation and its victims, their labor serving a system that profits others.

Thoreau's own trading practices are deliberately minimal. He grows beans and sells a portion of his crop, but the sale is not the point of the growing. The bean-field is "a connecting link between wild and cultivated fields" (007-001), not a commercial enterprise, and Thoreau reports his profit — $8.71½ — with ironic satisfaction. The small sum proves his point: one can live on very little, and the "trade" that occupies so much of most people's lives is largely unnecessary. "Trade curses everything it handles," he observes, "and though you trade in messages from heaven, the whole curse of trade attaches to the business" (002-019). Even spiritual commerce — the buying and selling of religious consolation — is contaminated by the medium through which it passes.

The ice trade receives particular attention in "The Pond in Winter." Thoreau describes the harvesting of Walden's ice with a mixture of scientific curiosity and moral critique. The ice, cut in blocks and shipped to the tropics, becomes a commodity — "Walden ice" for the tables of Calcutta and Bombay. Thoreau notes the irony: "Thus it appears that the sweltering inhabitants of Charleston and New Orleans, of Madras and Bombay and Calcutta, drink at my well" (016-010). The pond's water, dispersed across the globe by commerce, unites distant places in a single circulatory system — but the unity is commercial rather than spiritual, an accidental byproduct of trade rather than an intentional community.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's critique of trade is not a rejection of exchange as such but a diagnosis of what trade becomes when it expands beyond its proper sphere. Barter between neighbors, the direct exchange of goods and services, is an extension of the household economy that Thoreau practices at Walden. But the market — anonymous, distant, driven by profit rather than need — is a different thing. It abstracts value from experience, turning the bean into a price and the ice into a commodity, and in doing so, it estranges people from the material conditions of their own lives.

The solution, for Thoreau, is not to abolish trade but to subordinate it to deliberate living. One trades because one must eat, but one does not live in order to trade. "Let not to get a living be thy trade, but thy sport" (010-018) — the phrase encapsulates Thoreau's economic philosophy. The word "sport" here means delight, play, the activity done for its own sake rather than for the wage it yields. Trade is a necessity; living is an art. The two should not be confused.
