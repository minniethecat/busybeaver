# Snow / Great Snow

## Definition

In *Walden*, snow is not merely winter weather but a transformative presence that remakes the landscape, deepens solitude, and tests the self-sufficiency of Thoreau's experiment. The "Great Snow" — a particularly heavy snowfall referenced across multiple chapters — becomes a narrative landmark, marking the boundary between the outer world and the interior life at the pond.

## In Walden

Snow appears across virtually every chapter that touches on winter. Thoreau describes himself as a "self-appointed inspector of snow storms and rain storms" (001-034), signaling from the start that natural forces are not obstacles to be avoided but phenomena to be witnessed and studied.

The Great Snow is introduced in the chapter "Sounds," where Thoreau notes the train's adaptation to winter: "On this morning of the Great Snow, perchance, which is still raging and chilling men's blood, I hear the muffled tone of their engine bell" (004-014). The snow transforms the familiar soundscape, muffling the iron horse that ordinarily dominates the chapter's attention. In the same passage, the "snow-plough" becomes emblematic of human persistence against the elements.

> [004-015] ... yet it shall be as pure as a snowflake, and if it be put into a pot and boiled, will come out an excellent dumpling.

The image of purity recurs throughout Walden's snow passages. Thoreau repeatedly links snow to moral and spiritual cleanliness — the snowflake as nature's standard of whiteness.

In "The Village," Thoreau observes how snow alters human geography: "Often in a snow storm, even by day, one will come out upon a well-known road and be unable to tell which way leads to the village" (008-002). Snow disorients the civilized traveler, revealing how thin the veneer of habitation truly is.

The pond itself changes under snow cover. Thoreau notes that "both water and ice were almost as blue as the sky, but the landscape being covered with snow, both water and ice were almost as invisible" (009-005), and describes how after "a light snow has fallen," the pond appears "as a clear white surface" (009-009).

The deepest engagement with snow comes in "House-Warming" and "Former Inhabitants." Thoreau "weathered some merry snow storms, and spent some cheerful winter evenings by my fireside" (014-001). Snow becomes the condition that makes the hearth meaningful:

> [014-016] The Great Snow! How cheerful it is to hear of!

When "the snow lay deepest no wanderer ventured near my house for a week or fortnight" (014-016), the solitude Thoreau sought becomes absolute. His path from the highway to his house was "through deepest snows and most dismal tempests" (014-020), yet he describes returning through these as a form of heroism.

In "Winter Animals," snow reveals the hidden life of the forest: foxes "ranged over the snow crust, in moonlight nights" (015-004), and a squirrel deposited acorn scraps "on to the snow crust by my door" (015-005). The snow, far from deadening the landscape, transcribes upon it the movements of creatures otherwise invisible.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, snow is both a physical reality and a spiritual metaphor. Its whiteness embodies the purity he cultivates in his own life — a standard against which the compromises of village existence are measured. The snow-bound weeks of deepest winter, when no visitor comes, are for him not deprivation but an intensification of his chosen solitude.

He also treats snow with a naturalist's precision, noting its effects on animal behavior, pond ice, and the microclimate of his house. This double vision — snow as symbol and snow as observable fact — is characteristic of Thoreau's method throughout Walden.

## Historical Context

Nineteenth-century New England winters were severe, and heavy snowfalls were routine facts of rural life. The "Great Snow" that Thoreau references may refer to a specific historical storm, but in the economy of the book it functions as a recurring archetype. For Thoreau's contemporaries, snow meant isolation, blocked roads, and the necessity of stored provisions — conditions that tested the self-reliance Thoreau championed.
