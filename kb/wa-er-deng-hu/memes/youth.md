# Youth

## Definition

Youth in *Walden* is not a phase to be outgrown but a quality to be cultivated. Thoreau treats youth as the state of maximum receptivity — the condition in which the world appears fresh, surprising, and worthy of wonder. The tragedy of most lives, in his view, is not aging but the premature abandonment of youth's perceptual gifts. "The youth may build or plant or sail, only let him not be hindered from doing that which he tells me he would like to do" (001-039): the youthful impulse toward action is not to be suppressed but directed, and Thoreau's entire Walden experiment can be understood as an attempt to recover, in his late twenties, the perceptual immediacy that most people leave behind in childhood.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most explicit treatment of youth appears in "Higher Laws," where he describes "a period in the history of the individual, as of the race, when the hunters are the best men" (011-006). This is the youth of the individual and the youth of humanity — the phase of primal energy, physical competence, and unmediated engagement with the natural world. Thoreau does not condemn this phase as primitive, as a more fastidious moralist might; he honors it as a necessary foundation. The youth who hunts is closer to the truth of his own animal nature than the adult who has merely forgotten it.

But youth, for Thoreau, is not only about physical vigor. It is primarily about perception — the capacity to see the world as if for the first time. "The same water which my youthful eyes fell on" (009-025), he writes of Walden Pond, linking his present perception to his earliest sight of the pond. The water has not aged; neither, in the moment of true attention, has the perceiver. Thoreau's famous call to "live deliberately" is, among other things, a call to recover the deliberateness of youth — the time when every choice felt significant because it was new.

The most lyrical treatment of youth comes in "Spring," where Thoreau finds the "symbol of perpetual youth" in a grass blade pushing through thawing earth: "the grass-blade, like a long green ribbon, streams from the sod into the summer" (017-013). The grass is young every spring, and so, potentially, is the observer. Thoreau's "Conclusion" returns to this theme, describing the "perennial youth" (018-021) of the person who remains open to experience, who does not allow the accumulation of years to become the accumulation of defenses.

Thoreau also recognizes youth's vulnerabilities. The "youths who were bent on this pursuit" of hunting (011-006) may, he acknowledges, become trapped in it — mistaking the phase for the destination. Youth is a gift, but it is a gift that must be consciously developed rather than merely enjoyed. The youthful impulse toward nature must mature into a philosophical relationship with nature; the youthful energy must become the deliberate energy of the awakened adult.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's treatment of youth reflects his broader critique of the life of habit. Habit is the antagonist of youth — the force that replaces fresh perception with stale expectation, spontaneous action with automatic routine. "It is worth the expense of youthful days and costly hours," he writes, "if you learn but some words of an ancient language, which are raised out of the trivialness of the street" (003-003). Youth's "expense" — its willingness to spend time on things that do not yield immediate practical returns — is exactly what the adult world of commerce and calculation extinguishes. The deliberate life is the life that refuses to let that expense be foreclosed.

This philosophy places Thoreau in a tradition that includes Wordsworth's "the child is father of the man" and Emerson's "self-reliance." But Thoreau is more practical than Wordsworth and more embodied than Emerson. His youth is not a lost paradise to be mourned but a capacity to be exercised — like a muscle that atrophies without use but can be strengthened again with attention.
