# Flint's Pond

A large, shallow pond about a mile east of Walden Pond in Lincoln, Massachusetts, contrasted unfavorably by Thoreau with the purer waters of Walden.

## Location and Geography

Flint's Pond (also called Sandy Pond) lies about a mile east of Walden Pond in Lincoln. It is the largest lake in the area at approximately 197 acres, but is comparatively shallow. Thoreau describes walking through the woods to reach it, a journey that "was often my recreation."

## In Walden

Flint's Pond appears in "The Ponds" chapter as a foil to Walden. Thoreau uses it to contrast the pure, deep, spring-fed Walden with the larger but shallower and less pristine pond named after a farmer who, in Thoreau's view, did not appreciate its beauty.

> [009-038] Flint's, or Sandy Pond, in Lincoln, our greatest lake and inland sea, lies about a mile east of Walden. It is much larger, being said to contain one hundred and ninety-seven acres, and is more fertile in fish; but it is comparatively shallow, and not remarkably pure.

> [009-039] *Flint's Pond!* Such is the poverty of our nomenclature. What right had the unclean and stupid farmer, whose farm abutted on this sky water, whose shores he has ruthlessly laid bare, to give his name to it?

## Ecological Features

Flint's Pond, despite Thoreau's criticism of its purity, is more fertile in fish than Walden. Thoreau notes chestnut trees along its shores and describes finding curious spherical balls of grass on its sandy bottom. The pond's water level fluctuates in sympathy with Walden.

## Significance

Thoreau's extended polemic against the naming of Flint's Pond is one of the most passionate passages in Walden. It crystallizes his critique of an economic worldview that reduces natural beauty to property value. The farmer Flint, whose name the pond bears, is Thoreau's archetype of the "skin-flint" who "would carry the landscape, who would carry his God, to market."
