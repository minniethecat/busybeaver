# Beans / The Bean-Field

## Definition

The bean-field in *Walden* is Thoreau's central parable of work: a two-and-a-half-acre plot of beans that he cultivates not principally for the harvest but for the discipline and insight the labor yields. The beans are at once a crop, a curriculum, and a spiritual exercise. Thoreau's famous declaration — "It was no longer beans that I hoed, nor I that hoed beans" (007-006) — marks the moment when agricultural labor dissolves into meditation, and the distinction between cultivating the soil and cultivating the self collapses.

## In Walden

The entire seventh chapter of *Walden*, "The Bean-Field," is devoted to this experiment. Thoreau opens with a precise description: "Meanwhile my beans, the length of whose rows, added together, was seven miles already planted" (007-001). The quantitative precision — seven miles of rows — is typical of Thoreau's method: he measures his labor in both the farmer's and the philosopher's units, and finds the two scales converging.

The bean-field is situated on ground that "had previously been cultivated in beans by the Indians" (007-003), establishing a lineage of cultivation that stretches back before European settlement. The "influence of the sun" is "seen in these bean leaves, corn blades, and potato vines" (007-002), but Thoreau sees more than chlorophyll: the same sun "which ripens my beans illumines at once a system of earths like ours" (001-019). The local crop opens onto the cosmic.

The labor of hoeing becomes Thoreau's chief spiritual discipline during his first summer at Walden. He hoes "from five o'clock in the morning till noon" (007-010) and the work "yielded an instant and immeasurable crop" (007-006) — not of beans but of attention. The enemy is the "haughty weeds" (007-004) that compete with his crop, and Thoreau personifies them into a moral adversary: "throwing dust upon their heads" (007-004) becomes a ritual of discrimination between the wanted and the unwanted.

Thoreau's accounting of the bean-field is characteristically exhaustive: he records expenditures for seed, hire of a horse and boy to plow, and the harvest of "twelve bushels of beans" (007-025) that sold for $16.94 — yielding a modest profit of $8.71½. But the balance sheet is ironic. The real yield was "a connecting link between wild and cultivated fields" (007-009) — the beans made him "a sojourner in civilized life again" (007-009) while keeping one foot in the wild. His true crop was the chapter itself.

The bean-field also serves as Thoreau's text for a critique of industrial agriculture. He notes that "husbandry was once a sacred art; but it is now pursued with irreverent haste and heedlessness" (007-012). The farmer who "only knows the surface of this earth" (007-012) misses everything Thoreau finds valuable in the work. His beans taught him "that if one would live simply and eat only the crop which he raised," he might need to work "less than half the time" of the conventional farmer (007-012).

## Perspective

Thoreau's bean-field is a strategic deployment of the pastoral tradition. Like Virgil's *Georgics*, it uses agricultural labor as a vehicle for philosophical reflection. But Thoreau inverts the classical model: he is not celebrating the dignity of the yeoman farmer but demonstrating that even the most prosaic labor — hoeing beans in the July sun — can become a spiritual exercise if undertaken with the right attention. The bean-field is not an escape from society but a vantage point from which society's assumptions about work, property, and value become visible and criticizable.

## Historical Context

The bean was a staple crop of nineteenth-century New England, valued for its nitrogen-fixing properties in crop rotation as well as its food value. Thoreau's choice of beans rather than a more prestigious or profitable crop is significant: the bean is humble, democratic, and associated with Native American agriculture (the "three sisters" of corn, beans, and squash). His bean-field, planted on ground first cultivated by the region's indigenous people, implicitly honors that agricultural tradition while using it to critique the commercial farming that had displaced it.
