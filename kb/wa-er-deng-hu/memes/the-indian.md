# The Indian

## Connection to Walden

The Indian pervades *Walden* as a **vanished presence** — the original inhabitant whose tracks are still visible in the landscape, whose skills Thoreau both admires and approximates, and whose displacement haunts the text as an unspoken moral shadow. Thoreau references Indians more than twenty times across the book, drawing on them as a benchmark of authentic living against which civilization is measured.

The most personal encounter comes early in *Economy*, when an Indian basket-seller visits Concord:

> [001-037] Not long since, a strolling Indian went to sell baskets at the house of a well-known lawyer in my neighborhood. "Do you wish to buy any baskets?" he asked. "No, we do not want any," was the answer. "What!" exclaimed the Indian, as he went out the gate, "do you mean to starve us?"

Thoreau uses this vignette as a parable of economic disconnection: the Indian's baskets are beautiful and useful, but the market has no place for them. The lawyer has no *need* that the Indian can meet within the terms of commerce — a failure Thoreau attributes not to the Indian but to the impoverished imagination of the buyer.

Thoreau himself has seen Penobscot Indians in Concord, living with a material simplicity he envies:

> [001-050] I have seen Penobscot Indians, in this town, living in tents of thin cotton cloth, while the snow was nearly a foot deep around them, and I thought that they would be glad to have the snow deeper, to keep out the wind.

He contrasts this economy of means with the overbuilt houses of his townsmen:

> [001-051] …which would buy a village of Indian wigwams, but now helps to keep the poor house.

In *Sounds*, Thoreau notes the commercial exploitation of Indian lands:

> [004-010] All the Indian huckleberry hills are stripped, all the cranberry meadows are raked, and the fruits of the earth are transported to the city.

The image is elegiac: the Indian's landscape has been converted into the townsman's commodity, and the knowledge that once sustained a way of life on those hills is being erased along with the berries.

Thoreau also records the material traces of Indian presence around Walden Pond — arrowheads, burned stumps, the remains of a "pow-wow" ground:

> [009-012] …anciently the Indians were holding a pow-wow upon a hill on the shore, which rose to the height of the pond, and from which there was a view over the whole pond.

> [009-015] At first you wonder if the Indians could have formed them on the spot.

The arrowheads Thoreau finds and collects are for him relics of a more authentic mode of inhabitation — a people who lived with the land rather than merely on it.

## Biography

The Indians Thoreau encountered in 19th-century Concord were mostly Penobscot people from Maine, who traveled south seasonally to sell baskets, herbal remedies, and other traditional crafts. They were remnants of the Algonquian-speaking peoples who had inhabited New England for millennia before European contact — the Massachusett, Nipmuc, Wampanoag, and Pennacook among them. By Thoreau's time, disease, war, and displacement had reduced their numbers drastically, and those who remained lived on the economic margins.

Thoreau's knowledge of Indians was not merely observational. He read widely in the ethnographic literature of his day — Schoolcraft's histories, the Jesuit Relations — and kept notebooks filled with Indian place-names, plant uses, and lore. His interest was both scholarly and philosophical: the Indian represented an alternative epistemology, a way of knowing nature through sustained, embodied contact rather than through the abstractions of science or commerce.

## Significance

Thoreau's Indian serves four interconnected functions in Walden:

1. **The Indian as ecological model.** The Indian knew how to live in New England without depleting it — a skill Thoreau admires and seeks to recover in his own experiment. The Indian's subsistence economy, based on direct relationship with land rather than market mediation, is Thoreau's implicit standard for authentic living.

2. **The Indian as spiritual antecedent.** Thoreau often frames his own practice — the morning bath, the simple diet, the solitary vigil — as a recovery of Indian wisdom. "I lived like the Puri Indians," he writes in *Sounds*, collapsing the distance between Concord and the Amazon, between 1845 and an immemorial past.

3. **The Indian as historical ghost.** The arrowheads, the burned stumps, the place-names — these are the traces of a people whose displacement the text registers without quite confronting. Thoreau's Walden is built on Indian land, and the book is, among other things, an elegy for what was lost.

4. **The Indian as critique of civilization.** In *The Bean-Field*, Thoreau explicitly compares his own agricultural practice to the Indians', finding the latter superior: "I do not see how the Indians could have done better" (006-010). The judgment is devastating — two centuries of "progress" have produced a method of farming no better than what the "savages" practiced.
