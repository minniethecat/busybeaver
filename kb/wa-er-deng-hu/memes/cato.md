# Cato the Elder

Roman statesman and agricultural writer (234–149 BCE) admired by Thoreau for his practical wisdom, cited in Walden's Economy chapter on husbandry and simple living.

## Connection to Walden

Thoreau invokes Cato in the Economy chapter as an authority on agriculture and household management. Cato's *De Re Rustica* serves as a classical precedent for Thoreau's own practical experiments at Walden Pond.

> [001-047] Old Cato, whose "De Re Rusticâ" is my "Cultivator," says—and the only translation I have seen makes sheer nonsense of the passage—"When you think of getting a farm turn it thus in your mind, not to buy greedily; nor spare your pains to look at it, and do not think it enough to go round it once. The oftener you go there the more it will please you, if it is good."

> [001-050] I am far from jesting. Economy is a subject which admits of being treated with levity, but it can not be disposed of. Cato's advice on husbandry is not for those who would cultivate the earth merely, but for those who would cultivate themselves.

## Biography

Marcus Porcius Cato (234–149 BCE), known as Cato the Elder or Cato the Censor, was a Roman statesman, soldier, and writer. He was the first Latin prose author of importance and wrote *De Agri Cultura* (On Agriculture), the oldest surviving complete Latin prose work. Cato championed traditional Roman virtues of frugality, hard work, and agricultural self-sufficiency—values that resonated deeply with Thoreau's philosophy at Walden.

## Significance

Cato represents for Thoreau the classical ideal of the farmer-philosopher: one who derives wisdom from direct engagement with the land. Thoreau positions his own experiment at Walden within this ancient tradition of agricultural writing, using Cato's authority to legitimize his critique of modern economic life.
