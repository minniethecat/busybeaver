# Wildness

## Definition

Wildness, for Thoreau, is not merely the absence of civilization but a positive, generative force — the raw vitality that underlies all life and creativity. More encompassing than "wilderness" (a physical place), wildness is a quality that can be found in a swamp, a spider, a bean-field, or the human soul. Thoreau's famous declaration — "In Wildness is the preservation of the World" — captures his conviction that contact with the wild is not an escape from life but its essential renewal.

## In Walden

Thoreau's celebration of wildness pervades Walden, from his delight in untamed creatures to his reverence for swamps and "dreary" places that others dismiss as worthless. He finds wildness not only in the deep woods but at the margins of his bean-field and in the pond itself.

> [004-004] in flower, had attracted many wild bees, gradually assumed their character and habits.

The wild animals around Walden Pond — the loon, the wild mouse, the partridge, the ant armies — are not merely objects of natural history observation but embodiments of a freedom Thoreau envies and seeks to emulate:

> [005-004] which we are accustomed to call wild and dreary, and also that they are not so wild and dreary as they seem.

Thoreau is particularly drawn to places that resist cultivation: swamps, bogs, and "dreary" thickets. In "The Ponds," he argues that such places are reservoirs of creative energy:

> [007-004] ch nature yields in the still wilder fields unimproved by man? The bean-field itself, Thoreau suggests, is enriched by the wildness at its edges.

For Thoreau, the pond itself embodies wildness at its most sublime — self-contained, indifferent to human concerns, eternally self-renewing. His daily baths in Walden are a ritual immersion in wild vitality.

## Thoreau's Perspective

Thoreau's wildness is fundamentally a spiritual category. He believes that civilization, for all its comforts, drains life of intensity and meaning. The remedy is contact with the wild — not as a weekend excursion but as a daily practice of attention to what is untamed in nature and in oneself.

He rejects the Puritan view of wilderness as a howling, demon-haunted waste, replacing it with a vision of wild places as sacred reservoirs. The swamp, traditionally seen as a cursed place, becomes for Thoreau a "sacred well" — a source of health and inspiration precisely because it resists human improvement.

Thoreau extends this logic to human nature: every person has a wild core that society tries to tame, and spiritual health depends on preserving it. His famous image of the "wild apple" — the untended tree that produces smaller but more intensely flavored fruit — is a metaphor for the self that resists cultivation and is richer for it.

## Historical Context

Thoreau wrote at a moment when "wilderness" was being reconceived in American culture. The Romantic movement, particularly Wordsworth and Coleridge, had transformed wild landscapes from objects of fear into sources of the sublime. In America, painters of the Hudson River School (Thomas Cole, Asher Durand) were celebrating wild nature as the nation's distinctive gift. Thoreau's innovation was to bring wildness down from the mountain peaks and locate it in the local — the Concord swamp, the Walden woods, the common woodchuck. He democratized wildness, making it accessible to anyone who would pay attention.
