# Thunder

## Definition

In *Walden*, thunder is nature's most direct and commanding voice — a sound that overwhelms human speech, shakes the earth, and announces powers beyond human control or comprehension. Thoreau treats thunder not as a meteorological phenomenon to be explained but as a sublime event to be experienced: it is the voice of the wild that civilization cannot domesticate, the reminder that nature's forces dwarf human projects.

## In Walden

Thunder first enters *Walden* through an unexpected channel: the railroad. Thoreau describes the locomotive's passage with mythological grandeur, its "snort like thunder, shaking the earth with his feet" (004-011). By likening the engine to a thundering beast, Thoreau simultaneously acknowledges industrial power and naturalizes it — the train's thunder is impressive, but nature's original thunder is greater. The comparison cuts both ways: the locomotive borrows nature's sublimity, but the metaphor also reminds readers that real thunder surpasses any mechanical imitation.

The most sustained thunder meditation occurs in "Baker Farm," where Thoreau is caught in a storm while visiting the Irish laborer John Field. He describes "one of those afternoons which seem indefinitely long before one, in which many events may happen" (010-010), when a shower compels him to shelter under a pine. The storm builds until "the thunder began to rumble with such emphasis" that it transforms the ordinary afternoon into an occasion of existential weight. Thoreau's shelter is minimal — a handkerchief for a shed, piled boughs over his head — and this vulnerability before the storm is precisely the condition he values. The thunder reminds him that safety is an illusion and that exposure to elemental force is a form of honesty.

Inside Field's cabin, the thunder continues to sound while Thoreau attempts to persuade the Irishman to simplify his life. The storm outside and the poverty within create a charged scene: "I had sat there many times through the rain and the thunder" (010-015), Thoreau reflects, but this particular storm sharpens the contrast between Field's grinding labor and Thoreau's deliberate leisure. The thunder becomes a moral auditor, its rumbling a judgment on the economic system that keeps Field poor while he works incessantly.

Leaving Field's cabin after the rain, Thoreau experiences a moment of transcendence catalyzed by the storm's passage: "as I ran down the hill toward the reddening west, with the rainbow over my shoulder, and some faint tinkling sounds borne to my ear through the cleansed air" (010-018). The thunder has passed; what remains is a washed world and a "Good Genius" that seems to speak directly to Thoreau. The sequence — thunder, rain, rainbow, inner voice — is a complete spiritual drama in miniature.

In "Spring," Thoreau records a local phenomenon that links thunder to Walden's own geology: "The fishermen say that the 'thundering of the pond' scares the fish" (017-002). This "thundering" is the sound of ice cracking and shifting — a terrestrial thunder produced by the pond's own processes of freezing and thawing. Even the pond, Thoreau suggests, has its thunderous voice, speaking in a register that only the attentive can hear.

> [017-044] We need the tonic of wildness — to wade sometimes in marshes where the bittern and the meadow-hen lurk, and hear the booming of the snipe; to smell the whispering sedge where only some wilder and more solitary fowl builds her nest.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, thunder is a "tonic of wildness" — a force that shocks the system out of its civilized torpor. Where his neighbors hear thunder and hurry indoors, Thoreau hears it and feels awakened. The thunderstorm is nature's protest against human attempts to make the world safe, predictable, and comfortable. It is the wild's refusal to be ignored. Thoreau's willingness to be out in the storm — under a pine, under a handkerchief, under the open sky — is a declaration of allegiance to the wild over the domestic, to exposure over protection, to life over mere survival.

## Historical Context

In the mid-nineteenth century, thunder was increasingly being explained by the science of electricity (Benjamin Franklin's experiments were a living memory), yet it retained its ancient associations with divine speech and judgment. Thoreau draws on both registers: he knows the scientific explanation but chooses to write about thunder in the language of myth and experience. His thunder belongs more to the world of Zeus and Jehovah than to the world of Leyden jars and lightning rods, though he would have been familiar with both.
