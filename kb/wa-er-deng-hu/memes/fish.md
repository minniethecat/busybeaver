# Fish

## Definition

Fish in *Walden* are both literal inhabitants of the pond and carriers of philosophical meaning. Thoreau observes perch, pickerel, pouts, and shiners with the precision of a naturalist and the reverence of a poet. Fishing is never mere sport or subsistence; it is a form of meditation, a means of plumbing the pond's invisible depths, and a metaphor for the pursuit of truth itself. The fish's silent, suspended life beneath the surface mirrors the hidden dimensions of consciousness that deliberate living seeks to access.

## In Walden

Thoreau's most famous meditation on fish appears early in *Where I Lived, and What I Lived For*: "Time is but the stream I go a-fishing in. I drink at it; but while I drink I see the sandy bottom and detect how shallow it is. Its thin current slides away, but eternity remains. I would drink deeper; fish in the sky, whose bottom is pebbly with stars" (002-030). Fishing here is transposed from a literal activity to a metaphysical pursuit — the fisherman casts his line not into Walden Pond but into the stream of time itself, seeking depths that the ordinary drinker cannot reach. The image of "fishing in the sky" inverts the natural order, suggesting that the true quarry of philosophical inquiry is not below but above.

The pond's fish are described with empirical care. Thoreau notes their visibility through the clear water: "Paddling over it, you may see, many feet beneath the surface the schools of perch and shiners, perhaps only an inch long, yet the former easily distinguished by their transverse bars" (009-006). The transparency of Walden Pond makes its fish visible in a way that land-based observers rarely experience — the water, Thoreau's "earth's eye," reveals its living contents without distortion.

Fishing itself is practiced as a contemplative ritual. Thoreau joins companions who have been "fishing on the pond since morning, as silent and motionless as a duck or a floating leaf" (009-002), and in warm evenings he sits in the boat playing the flute, watching perch hover as if charmed by the music (009-003). Midnight fishing by moonlight becomes an occasion for "serenaded by owls and foxes" (009-004), the pursuit of fish merging with the experience of nocturnal wildness.

The pickerel of Walden receives special tribute. Thoreau's exclamation — "Ah, the pickerel of Walden!" (016-005) — conveys wonder at the fish's beauty, which he finds "so foreign to the streets, even to the woods, foreign as Arabia to our Concord life." The pickerel's "transcendent beauty" and "celestial form" make it a creature that seems to belong to another order of existence, a visitor from the pond's mysterious depths.

> [002-030] Time is but the stream I go a-fishing in. I drink at it; but while I drink I see the sandy bottom and detect how shallow it is. Its thin current slides away, but eternity remains. I would drink deeper; fish in the sky, whose bottom is pebbly with stars.

> [009-006] Paddling over it, you may see, many feet beneath the surface the schools of perch and shiners, perhaps only an inch long, yet the former easily distinguished by their transverse bars, and you think that they must be ascetic fish that find a subsistence there.

> [016-005] Ah, the pickerel of Walden! when I see them lying on the ice, or in the well which the fisherman cuts in the ice, making a little hole to admit the water, I am always surprised by their rare beauty, as if they were fabulous fishes, they are so foreign to the streets, even to the woods, foreign as Arabia to our Concord life.

## Thoreau's Perspective

For Thoreau, fishing occupies a morally ambiguous middle ground between hunting and pure contemplation. In *Higher Laws*, he confesses to feeling drawn to the "savage delight" of catching fish but also senses that a fully awakened spiritual life would leave fishing behind: "I have found repeatedly, of late years, that I cannot fish without falling a little in self-respect" (011-006). Yet he does not condemn the fisherman. Instead, he acknowledges fishing as a stage in moral development — a primal engagement with nature that the young or the partly awakened may need, but that the fully awakened person may outgrow. The progression from hunter to fisherman to vegetarian to spiritual contemplative maps a Thoreauvian hierarchy of relations with the natural world.

## Historical Context

In mid-nineteenth-century New England, fishing was both a common subsistence practice and a growing recreational pursuit. Concord's ponds and rivers — Walden, Flint's, the Concord River — supported populations of pickerel, perch, pouts, and shiners that attracted fishermen from the village. Thoreau's interest in fish was shared by naturalists of the period, but his treatment of fish as philosophical objects, rather than merely as quarry or scientific specimens, distinguishes his writing from contemporary angling literature.
