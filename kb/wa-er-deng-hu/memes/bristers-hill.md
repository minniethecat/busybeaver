# Brister's Hill

A wooded hill near Walden Pond named after Brister Freeman, a formerly enslaved man and Revolutionary War veteran who settled there with his apple orchard.

## Location and Geography

Brister's Hill rises on the right-hand side of the road leading toward Walden Pond. A brook—"Brister's Spring"—oozes from under the hill, and Thoreau notes that the hill's slope was once covered with an orchard that had long since been destroyed by the time of his arrival.

> [012-011] The path to the pond lay through a deepish hollow, where a brook, oozing from under Brister's Hill, half a mile from my field, had made a boggy mead.

## Historical Significance

Brister's Hill preserves the memory of Brister Freeman, who cultivated an apple orchard on its slopes after gaining his freedom from slavery. By Thoreau's time, the orchard was gone, killed by pine trees that had overgrown the hill. The hill thus embodies Thoreau's theme of layered histories: the land remembers its previous inhabitants even after they are forgotten.

> [014-004] Down the road, on the right hand, on Brister's Hill, lived Brister Freeman, "a handy Negro," slave of Squire Cummings once—there where grow still the apple trees which Brister planted and tended; large old trees now, but their fruit still wild and ciderish to my taste.

> [014-005] Brister Freeman was the owner of a considerable piece of land, which he had fenced and cultivated. He had been a soldier in the Revolution, and had fought at Bunker Hill.

## In Walden

Brister's Hill is part of Thoreau's daily landscape. It stands near his bean-field and marks the route from Walden toward town. The hill appears in both "Brute Neighbors" and "Former Inhabitants," connecting Thoreau's present-tense experience of the woods to the human histories embedded in the land.
