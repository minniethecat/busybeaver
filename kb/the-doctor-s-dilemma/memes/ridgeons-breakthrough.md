## Summary

Ridgeon's breakthrough — his discovery of the [[opsonin|opsonin index]] and the development of a treatment based on it — is the backstory event that makes the entire moral drama of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* possible. The breakthrough occurs before the play begins, at [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's Hospital]], where [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] conducts his bacteriological research. Its consequences — scientific reputation, a knighthood, a treatment that can save lives but only in limited numbers — provide the conditions for the [[ridgeons-dilemma|central dilemma]].

The breakthrough is reported rather than dramatized. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]] discuss the opsonin treatment in Act I, with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] playing the skeptical elder physician who must be educated in the new science. When [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] asks "Opsonin? What the devil is opsonin?" [001-106], [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] explains the concept in terms that oscillate between genuine scientific exposition and self-conscious performance.

The knighthood awarded for this research is announced early in Act I: [[emmy|Emmy]] tells a character "He's been made a knight" [001-019]. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] himself reflects on the honor with characteristic ambivalence: "So that's why they made me a knight! And that's the medical profession!" [001-273] — an exclamation that acknowledges the professional recognition while mocking the system that bestows it.

## The Opsonin Index

The opsonin index was a genuine area of medical research in the early twentieth century, developed by Almroth Wright at St. Mary's Hospital. The index measured the capacity of a patient's blood to prepare bacteria for ingestion by [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]] — the immune cells that destroy invading organisms. By "stimulating the [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]]" [001-233], [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s treatment promised to enhance the body's natural defenses against tuberculosis.

Shaw treats the opsonin treatment with characteristic ambivalence. On one hand, it represents genuine [[scientific-medicine|scientific advance]] — the application of laboratory science to clinical medicine. On the other, it functions as a satirical device: the characters speak of "stimulating the [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]]" [001-233] as if it were a magical formula, and [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] reduces it to "any kind" of antitoxin [001-238], revealing the gap between scientific precision and medical practice.

## Dramatic Significance

The breakthrough is dramatically necessary because it creates scarcity. If [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] could treat unlimited patients, there would be no dilemma — he would simply treat both [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] and [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]. The limited capacity of the opsonin treatment — "I can only take ten patients" [001-211] — forces a choice that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is morally incapable of making honestly.

The breakthrough thus functions as both a plot device and a thematic argument. It demonstrates that scientific progress — the very thing that distinguishes modern [[scientific-medicine|scientific medicine]] from traditional [[quackery|quackery]] — creates new moral problems rather than solving old ones. More knowledge does not produce better decisions; it produces harder choices.
