## Overview

*The Doctor's Dilemma* is a five-act play by George Bernard Shaw, first produced at the Royal Court Theatre, London, on 20 November 1906, and published by Constable and Company the same year. Subtitled "A Tragedy," the play is a satirical comedy about the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] in Edwardian England. It follows [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]], a physician newly knighted for developing a treatment for [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]], as his professional success forces him into a moral crisis: with only ten treatment slots available, he must choose which patient lives and which dies. The play is accompanied by a substantial polemical Preface, "[[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]]," in which Shaw develops his critique of the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] at length.

## Synopsis

**Act I** (001-001–001-375): In [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] on the morning of his knighthood, the newly honored physician receives a succession of medical colleagues — [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]], [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]], [[bloomfield-bonington|Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington]], [[leo-schutzmacher|Leo Schutzmacher]], and [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]] — each embo[[death-and-mortality|dying]] a different pathology of the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]. A visit from [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]], pleading for treatment for her [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] husband [[louis-dubedat|Louis]], sets [[ridgeons-dilemma|the dilemma]] in motion. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] explains he can only take ten patients (001-316).

**Act II** (002-003–002-220): The [[medical-profession|doctors]] dine at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter Hotel]], Richmond. After dinner, they discuss [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s case on the moonlit terrace. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma crystallizes: treat [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] (brilliant, dishonest, morally suspect) or [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] (worthy, poor, a fellow doctor). [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confesses: "I must choose" (002-205). [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] frames the choice: "it's a plain choice between a man and a lot of pictures" (002-208).

**Act III** (003-002–003-287): In [[dubedats-studio|Dubedat's studio]], the [[medical-profession|doctors]] confront [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s charm and amorality are on full display. The confrontation between medical authority and [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic freedom exposes both as incomplete — and equally compromised. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] famously retorts: "It's always the way with the in[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic professions: when they're beaten in argument they fall back on intimidation" (003-145).

**Act IV** (004-002–004-186): [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] dies in his studio beneath his painting of [[dubedats-death|Cardinal Death]]. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] assemble as witnesses to the consequence of their judgment. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] pronounces the final verdict: "A blackguard's a blackguard; an honest man's an honest man" (004-166).

**Act V** (005-002–005-111): At [[the-posthumous-exhibition|a gallery exhibition]] of [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s posthumous work, the [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] world celebrates the dead [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confronts the irreversibility of his choice. The play ends on an ambiguous note — neither vindicating [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] nor condemning [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]], but leaving the audience to judge [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] that judges lives.

## Themes

- **[[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|The Medical Profession]]**: The play's central satirical target — a profession that judges worth before it heals illness. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s admission "I daresay I am a [[quackery|quack]], a [[quackery|quack]] with a qualification" (001-324) crystallizes [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|Shaw's critique]].
- **[[ridgeons-dilemma|The Moral Dilemma]]**: Utilitarian choice as a mask for [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity and personal desire. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confesses: "I must choose" (002-205), while [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] frames it as "a plain choice between a man and a lot of pictures" (002-208).
- **[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|Art versus Medicine]]**: Two ways of knowing the world — aesthetic truth against empirical utility. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s studio confrontation pits [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s vision against the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' moral categories.
- **[[private-practice|Private Practice]]**: The economic structure that transforms healers into gatekeepers. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s limited treatment capacity (001-316) makes scarcity, not healing, the premise of medical encounter.
- **[[truth-and-deception|Truth and Deception]]**: [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] mirrors [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]'s self-deception. The play's ambiguity refuses to let either side claim moral victory.

## Production History

The play premiered at the Royal Court Theatre on 20 November 1906, with a cast including Harley Granville-Barker as [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] and Lillah McC[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]hy as [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]. The production was p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] of the Court Theatre's celebrated Vedrenne-Barker management (1904–1907), which established Shaw as a major dramatist. The play has been regularly revived, with notable productions at the Haymarket Theatre (1942), the Mermaid Theatre (1964), and the National Theatre (2012).

## Critical Reception

At its premiere, the play was recognized as one of Shaw's most provocative dramatic works, combining the sustained polemical argument of his Prefaces with the theatrical energy of his best comedy. Critics noted the boldness of placing a medical ethics debate at the center of a popular entertainment. Subsequent scholarship has emphasized the play's structural innovation — [[preface-on-doctors|the Preface]] as an integral component of the dramatic argument, not merely a printed supplement — and its enduring relevance to debates about healthcare rationing and professional ethics. The play's central dilemma has been cited in bioethics literature as an early dramatization of triage and resource allocation in medicine.

## Related Works

Shaw wrote several other works engaging with medicine and [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]s: *The Philanderer* (1893) satirizes the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] more lightly; *Misalliance* (1910) includes a doctor character; and *Too True to Be Good* (1931) returns to themes of illness and professional authority. The [[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]] (1906) remains one of Shaw's most sustained polemical essays, arguing for the nationalization of medical services.
