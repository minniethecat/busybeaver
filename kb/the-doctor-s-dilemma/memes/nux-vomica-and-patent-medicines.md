## Overview

Patent medicines — commercially branded, mass-advertised remedies of uncertain efficacy — were a defining feature of Edwardian medical culture and a primary target of Shaw's critique of the [[medical-profession|medical profession]] in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]. The play's indignation is directed not only at the quacks who sold these products but at the respectable doctors whose practice was barely distinguishable from the patent-medicine trade. Shaw condemns "the scandalous advertisements of patent medicines" as part of "a huge commercial system of quackery and poisoning" [001-003].

[[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] embodies the respectable face of this commerce. He prescribes bottled remedies with proprietary names and recommends branded products to patients with the same breezy confidence he brings to everything else. His medical practice is, at bottom, a retail operation — the sale of branded products under the authority of a medical degree. The difference between B.B. and an outright [[quackery|quack]] is one of social position rather than intellectual substance.

## In the Play

### Bloomfield Bonington and the Bottle

[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s relationship to patent medicines is established early in the play. He recommends a specific bottled preparation to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], suggesting "a tablespoonful to a twelve-ounce bottle of water: nothing better, no nonsense about it" [001-003]. The formulation is revealing: the appeal is to simplicity and common sense ("no nonsense"), but the product itself is proprietary and unexamined. B.B. does not know what is in the bottle, how it works, or whether it works at all — he knows only that he prescribes it and patients accept it.

The "twelve-ounce bottle" becomes a symbol of B.B.'s medical philosophy. His treatment of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] consists of prescribing "a tube of some really stiff [[antitoxin]]" [003-001] — again, a branded product whose contents he has not verified and whose effects he cannot predict. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s verdict that putting a tube of serum in B.B.'s hands "is murder: simple murder" [001-003] applies with equal force to the patent medicines he routinely dispenses.

### The Disease of Superstition

B.B.'s defense of patent medicines is also a confession of the profession's intellectual failure. In a moment of unusual candor, he admits that doctors have fostered a culture of medical [[credulity-and-superstition-in-medicine|credulity]]: "We set the example. We spread the superstition. We taught the people to believe in bottles of medicine" [001-003]. This is the play's most damning critique — not that quacks exist, but that the medical profession itself created the demand for their products by training the public to expect a bottle for every ailment.

The confession implicates the entire structure of [[private-practice|private medical practice]], in which the doctor's income depends on satisfying the patient's demand for visible, tangible treatment. A bottle of medicine satisfies this demand in a way that advice about diet, rest, and hygiene does not — and so the market rewards the doctor who dispenses bottles, regardless of their contents or necessity.

## Medical Context

The patent medicine industry was a major economic force in Edwardian England. Products like Beecham's Pills, Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound, and countless other remedies were sold through aggressive newspaper advertising that promised cures for everything from consumption to nervous exhaustion. Many contained alcohol, opium, or other habit-forming substances. The British Medical Association campaigned against the trade throughout the early twentieth century, but regulation remained weak until well after the period of the play. Shaw's attack on patent medicines aligns him with the reform wing of the profession while also implicating the reformers themselves in the culture they claimed to oppose.
