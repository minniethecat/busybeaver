## Definition

Art and beauty in Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* represent an alternative value system to the medical-scientific materialism of the [[medical-profession|doctors]]. [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], the [[death-and-mortality|dying]] [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] at the center of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral crisis, embodies an aesthetic philosophy that treats beauty as a transcendent good — one that justifies, or at least complicates, his otherwise indefensible behavior (003-001).

## Context in the Play

[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s studio, where Act III takes place, is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s aesthetic counter-space to the medical [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] of Act I. The setting is dominated by [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s unfinished painting of "[[dubedats-death|Cardinal Death]]," a work that literalizes [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s confrontation with [[death-and-mortality|mortality]] (003-001). Unlike the [[medical-profession|doctors]] who speak of death in clinical abstractions, [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] renders it visible, even beautiful.

The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' reactions to Dubedat's art reveal their limitations. They cannot separate their moral judgment of the man from their aesthetic judgment of his work. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], the most humane of the [[medical-profession|doctors]], comes closest to appreciating [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s gift, but even he is constrained by his professional perspective.

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]'s unwavering faith in her husband's [[genius-and-talent|genius]] — a faith that survives every revelation of his dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] — represents [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most radical claim: that artistic truth may be a higher order of truth than factual accuracy.

After [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s death, his widow commissions a posthumous exhibition that achieves the success he never enjoyed in life. The play's final act takes place in a Bond Street gallery (005-001), completing the movement from medical space to aesthetic space. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] who attend the exhibition are forced to confront what they destroyed, and Shaw leaves the audience to weigh the cost of their moral certainty.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's own relationship to [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] was both passionate and polemical. As a music and theater critic before becoming a playwright, he had spent decades arguing for the social importance of [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]].

In [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]], he created a character who is both a devastating critique of romantic notions of [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]ic temperament and a genuine vessel for Shaw's own beliefs about the Life Force — the [[nature-and-evolution|creative evolution]]ary energy that, in Shaw's philosophy, drives human progress.

The play does not resolve the tension between [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] and medicine. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] is allowed to die so that the more "deserving" [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]] may live — a choice that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] himself comes to doubt. The beauty of [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s last works, displayed in the final act, stands as a silent rebuke to the utilitarian calculus that killed him.

## Historical Background

The play was written during the peak of the Edwardian [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] market boom, when [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]s like John Singer Sargent and James McNeill Whistler commanded enormous fees and social prestige. The Bond Street gallery setting of Act V reflects the commercialisation of [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] in this period.

Shaw was also responding to the legacy of the Aesthetic Movement — the "[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] for [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]'s sake" doctrine associated with Walter Pater and Oscar Wilde — which had placed beauty at the center of moral philosophy.
