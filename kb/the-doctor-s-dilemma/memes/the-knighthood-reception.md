## Summary

Act I takes place on the morning of Ridgeon's knighthood. As his colleagues call to offer congratulations, Shaw stages a parade of medical types that functions as [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s satirical overture: [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] the obsessive surgeon, [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] the vacuous celebrity physician, [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] the gruff elder statesman, [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] the impoverished GP, [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] the commercially honest Jewish doctor. Each entrance is a miniature character study; collectively, they compose an anatomy of the Edwardian [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] at its most self-congratulatory — and most vulnerable to [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]].

## Dramatic Context

Ridgeon's knighthood is the occasion for professional celebration, but Shaw immediately undercuts the festivity. The honor — awarded for developing the [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] — has made [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] the most sought-after physician in London, which means he must now choose which patients to save and which to let die. Professional success, [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] argues, is the precondition for moral failure.

## Characters Involved

All nine named characters appear or are introduced during this event. The parade of [[medical-profession|doctors]] — each announced by Emmy — establishes [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al hierarchy that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] will systematically dismantle. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]'s late entrance (001-299) shifts the tone from [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] to drama, as her plea for Louis's treatment introduces the human stakes that the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' professional posturing has obscured.

## Key Dialogue

> (001-275) We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy.

> (001-276) All professions are conspiracies against the laity.

> (001-178) He is known in the medical world as B.B.; and the envy roused by his success in practice is softened by the conviction that he is, scientifically considered, a colossal humbug.

## Thematic Significance

The knighthood reception establishes [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s central thesis: that [[honor-and-reputation|professional honor]]s and professional ethics are inversely related. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is knighted for his scientific achievement, but the knighthood immediately embroils him in the social and economic obligations that will corrupt his medical judgment. The parade of [[medical-profession|doctors]] is not just comic relief — it is a structural [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] of a profession whose internal hierarchies and rivalries make ethical practice impossible.
