## Overview

The Secretary, addressed as Mr. Danby, is a minor character in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* who appears only in Act V at the [[bond-street-gallery|Bond Street Picture Gallery]]. He manages the practical arrangements for [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s posthumous exhibition, handling catalogues, proofs, and visitors. Though his role is small, his knowing aside to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] about knowing [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] "better than she did, Sir Colenso, in some ways, perhaps" (005-018) signals the gulf between [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s idealized memory of her husband and the reality known to those who dealt with him professionally.

## Role in the Play

The Secretary's primary dramatic function is to establish the setting of Act V — the posthumous exhibition of [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s work in the [[bond-street-gallery|Bond Street Gallery]] — and to facilitate the encounter between [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]. His brief exchange with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] early in the act introduces the book *The Story of a King of Men* (005-017), [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s hagiographic memoir of her husband, and his knowing remark about knowing [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] establishes the contrast between private knowledge and public myth. His return with catalogues at the act's end (005-109) provides the practical mechanism for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s dismissal of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]].

## Character Analysis

Shaw characterizes the Secretary with economical precision. He is "fashionably dressed" (005-001) and sits at a writing-table "correcting catalogue proofs" (005-001) — a figure of practical competence in a world of [[art-and-beauty|artistic]] pretension. His professional cynicism is revealed in his exchange with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]: "Oh, we knew him. Better than she did, Sir Colenso, in some ways, perhaps" (005-018). This single line, followed by a significant look shared with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], is the Act's first indication that [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s idealization of [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] is not universally shared. The Secretary represents the commercial-art world that knew [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] as a difficult client who took money in advance and failed to deliver — the world of dealers and gallery managers that [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s memoir will overwrite with myth.

His practical concerns also serve to ground the ethereal atmosphere of the [[bond-street-gallery|gallery]] in commercial reality. He worries about the late catalogues, manages the private view, and notes with satisfaction that the critics "won't come again: there's no lunch today" (005-010) — a sardonic observation on the transactional nature of art journalism.

## Relationships

The Secretary deals deferentially with [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]], addressing her concerns about the late catalogues and the negative press notices. With [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], he shares a moment of knowing complicity about [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s true character. He has no interaction with the other [[medical-profession|doctors]].
