## Overview

The [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] is the primary satirical target of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Shaw portrays it not as a collection of individual practitioners but as an institution — a self-regulating monopoly whose claim to scientific authority masks its economic self-interest. The play's most famous line, "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276), distills a structural critique that [[preface-on-doctors|the Preface]] on Doctors develops at length: the profession controls entry, sets prices, mystifies its knowledge, and polices its own members — all while claiming to serve the public good. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confesses the institutional truth first: "We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy" (001-275). [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] broadens it to a universal law: "And we can't all be [[genius-and-talent|genius]]es like you. Every fool can get ill; but every fool can't be a good doctor: there are not enough good ones to go round" (001-276).

## Role in the Play

Shaw assembles a representative cross-section of the Edwardian medical establishment: the research scientist ([[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]), the surgical specialist ([[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]), the fashionable physician ([[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]), the commercial general practitioner ([[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]), the impoverished idealist ([[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]), and the retired elder ([[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]). Each represents a distinct modality of professional corruption, and together they constitute a systemic indictment.

The play's dramatic structure exposes professional solidarity as the mechanism that protects incompetence and venality. When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-dilemma|dilemma]] forces a choice between patients, the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter Hotel]] is a closed professional conversation — the patient is excluded from the decision about his own life. As [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] tells [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]: "when you live in an age that runs to pictures and statues and plays and brass bands because its men and women are not good enough to comfort its poor aching soul, you should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession because its business is to heal and mend men and women" (002-210). [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s sardonic reply — "In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I'm to kill my patient" (002-210) — captures the irony at [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s center.

The play's final figure for the profession is [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s exhausted cry: "And that's the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]!" (001-316).

## Historical Context

Shaw wrote in the context of Edwardian medical reform debates. The General Medical Council (established 1858) had standardized medical education and registration, but critics argued it had created a professional c[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]el that restricted entry and inflated fees. The British Medical Association was consolidating political influence. Shaw's Fabian socialist perspective led him to advocate for the nationalization of medical services — a position developed in [[preface-on-doctors|the Preface]] on Doctors and p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ially realized decades later in the National Health Service (1948).

The play also engages with contemporaneous scientific controversies. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] is based on Almroth Wright's immunological research at St. Mary's Hospital — cutting-edge science that Shaw treats with characteristic ambivalence, admiring its rigor while questioning its claim to moral authority.

Within [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s world, professional rivalry between specialties is the norm. [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] dismisses [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|physicians]] as "an illiterate profession, with no qualifications and no public register" (004-050). [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] critiques the surgical fad: "chloroform has done a lot of mischief. It's enabled every fool to be a surgeon" (001-126). And [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] dismisses surgery as "only manual labor. Brain — brain remains master of the situation" (001-178).
