## Overview

In *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, medical treatment is as subject to fashion as dress, and professional reputations rise and fall like hemlines. Shaw portrays an Edwardian medical marketplace in which the difference between a respected treatment and a dismissed one is often not evidence but prestige — who advocates it, how confidently, and from what address. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s long memory provides the play's diagnostic perspective: he has watched medical fashions cycle through the profession for decades and has learned to greet each new discovery with affectionate skepticism.

The play's doctors are not merely individuals with different clinical views; they are representatives of competing therapeutic trends. [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] embodies the surgical fashion — every ailment requires excision. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] represents the antitoxin fashion — treat everything with the latest serum. [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]'s father represented the commercial fashion — sell hope in a shop window. And [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] represents the newest fashion of all: the laboratory-based [[scientific-medicine|scientific medicine]] that measures, calibrates, and indexes.

## In the Play

### Sir Patrick's Diagnosis

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s historian of medical fashion. He remembers when surgeons were "chloroform surgeons" who operated without antiseptic, when physicians prescribed elaborate drug regimens, when the profession's status was barely above that of tradesmen [001-003]. His response to each new therapeutic enthusiasm is the same: patient observation, skeptical inquiry, refusal to be impressed by novelty.

When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] announces his [[opsonin|opsonin index]] discovery, [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s first response is characteristic: "Opsonin? What the devil is opsonin?" [001-106]. This is not ignorance but methodological resistance — the refusal to accept a new term as knowledge until it has been explained, tested, and demonstrated. In a profession of fashion-followers, Sir Patrick is the deliberate skeptic who insists on evidence.

### The Surgical Fashion

[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] is the most extreme expression of medical fashion as monomania. His fixation on the "[[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]" and his conviction that surgery can cure any condition — from tuberculosis to melancholy — represent the surgical trend taken to its logical extreme [001-115]. He has built a [[private-practice|fashionable practice]] and a substantial reputation on the simple principle that cutting is always better than not cutting.

His popularity is part of Shaw's point. Patients want decisive doctors who will do something rather than nothing. Surgery satisfies that desire; it is visibly active where medicine is often invisible. Walpole's fashionable success is not despite his intellectual limitations but because of them — his certainty is marketable in a way that [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s skepticism never could be.

### Ridgeon's Fashion

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s method is the newest fashion of all, and Shaw takes care to show that even this most scientific of doctors is not immune to fashion's influence. His knighthood, announced in the morning papers [001-019], makes him fashionable in a social sense — the titled doctor who attracts titled patients. His laboratory is the new site of medical prestige, displacing the surgeon's theater and the physician's consulting room. But the fashion cycle will continue; today's laboratory pioneer is tomorrow's old guard. Ridgeon himself seems dimly aware of this when he self-diagnoses as "a quack with a qualification" [001-324].

## Shaw's Argument

Shaw's treatment of medical fashion is characteristically double-edged. He does not argue that all medical innovation is mere fashion — the [[opsonin]] index is genuinely scientific [001-106] — but he insists that the distinction between scientific advance and fashionable trend can only be made retrospectively, through the slow accumulation of evidence, and not through professional consensus or social prestige.

The structural problem is that the medical marketplace rewards fashion. A doctor who adopts the newest treatment, publishes the most confident case reports, and cultivates the most prestigious practice will prosper regardless of whether his treatments actually work. [[private-practice]] economics and professional prestige create a perpetual motion machine of therapeutic fashion in which yesterday's cure becomes today's embarrassment — and the patient pays the price.
