## Definition

The [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] as depicted in Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* is a social institution characterized by internal hierarchies, economic self-interest, and a tension between scientific aspiration and clinical reality. Shaw uses [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] as a vehicle for his broader critique of how professional guilds protect their members at the expense of the laity (001-275) (001-276).

## Context in the Play

The play's action unfolds almost entirely among doctors. The opening stage direction introduces the medical student [[redpenny|Redpenny]], who serves an informal apprenticeship under [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]], "in return for unspecified advantages involved by intimate intercourse with a leader of his profession" (001-001). The [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] on [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]] becomes the setting where professional rivalries, diagnoses, and moral decisions intersect.

[[the-knighthood-reception|Ridgeon's knighthood]], recently awarded for his [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] treatment, immediately raises questions about professional recognition. "So that's why they made me a knight! And that's the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]!" [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] exclaims bitterly to [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] (001-273). [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s response — "When you know as much as I know of the ignorance and superstition of the patients, you'll wonder that we're half as good as we are" (001-274) — frames [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]'s defensive posture.

The famous exchange crystallizes Shaw's thesis: "We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy," declares [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] (001-275). [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] broadens the indictment: "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276). Yet he immediately qualifies this: "Every fool can get ill; but every fool can't be a good doctor: there are not enough good ones to go round" (001-276).

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] is characteristically double-edged. Through [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], he acknowledges medicine's genuine aspiration: "you should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession because its business is to heal and mend men and women" (002-210). But this idealism is immediately undercut by [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s devastating reply: "In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I'm to kill my patient" (002-211) — referring to the moral calculus that leads [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] to let [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] die in order to treat the more "worthy" [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]].

The Preface, titled "[[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]]," extends this critique into polemical prose. Shaw contrasts the old guard (represented by [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s professional intuition and clinical experience) with the new scientific generation ([[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s [[scientific-medicine|germ theory]] evangelism (001-209)), finding both wanting in different ways. The [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] delivers the outsider's verdict when he accuses the doctors of belonging to "in[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic professions" that resort to intimidation when they lose an argument (003-145).

## Historical Background

In Edwardian England, the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] was undergoing rapid transformation. The Medical Act of 1858 had established the General Medical Council and a unified medical register, but [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] remained stratified between elite consultants (like [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]), general practitioners, and unregistered practitioners. The [[scientific-medicine|germ theory]] revolution, led by Pasteur and Koch in the preceding decades, had reshaped medical education and created generational tensions of the kind Shaw dramatizes between the older [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] and the younger [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] (001-209).

Shaw was personally engaged with these debates. He served on the St. Pancras Borough Council and was a vocal supporter of the public health movement. The play's central dilemma — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] must choose which patient to treat because he can only take ten — reflects real constraints in a system where access to advanced medical care depended on private wealth and professional patronage (001-316).
