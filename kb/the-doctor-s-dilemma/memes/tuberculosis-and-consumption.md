## Overview

Tuberculosis — commonly called "consumption" in the Edwardian era — was a leading cause of death in early twentieth-century England and the disease at the center of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]. The play's entire medical plot turns on whether [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]] will treat [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s tuberculosis with his new [[opsonin]]-based [[inoculation]] method, or whether he will give the scarce treatment slot to a worthier patient instead [001-003].

Shaw uses tuberculosis as both a plot device and a vehicle for social commentary. The disease was strongly associated with poverty, overcrowding, and poor [[sanitation-and-public-health|sanitation]] — conditions that expose the limits of individual medical treatment and point toward broader public health reforms. Ridgeon's declaration that "my line is tuberculosis and typhoid and plague" [001-003] establishes him as a specialist in the diseases of poverty, while [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s coughing punctuates the dramatic action with a reminder of the mortality at stake [002-044].

## In the Play

### Louis Dubedat's Illness

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] is introduced to the [[medical-profession|doctors]] as a patient suffering from advanced tuberculosis. His illness is made physically present through coughing — first appearing in the stage directions as "[[louis-dubedat|Louis]] coughs, first out of modesty, then from tuberculosis" [002-044]. Shaw's staging ensures that the disease cannot be abstracted away into clinical discussion; the audience hears and sees its reality throughout the play.

The diagnosis is accepted by all the doctors, but their proposed treatments differ dramatically. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] believes [[opsonin]] treatment can cure consumption — a claim met with immediate skepticism from [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], who challenges him: "You've found out how to cure consumption, eh?" [001-003]. This exchange encapsulates the play's tension between medical innovation and professional skepticism.

### Competing Diagnoses and the Dilemma

The clarity of the tuberculosis diagnosis is muddied by [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]], who insists that Dubedat's case is "bad [[blood-poisoning]]" [001-219], dismissing the general practitioner's diagnosis with characteristic contempt. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] offers yet another framework — his universal faith in stimulating the [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]]. The competing diagnoses create the very uncertainty that makes [[ridgeons-dilemma|Ridgeon's dilemma]] possible: if the diagnosis were universally agreed upon, the question of who to treat would have a clear medical answer.

Dubedat's death in Act IV is described as having gone "through three months galloping consumption in three days" [004-012] — a rapid decline that dramatizes both the virulence of the disease and the fatal consequences of the doctors' inability to agree on treatment.

## Medical Context

In 1906, when Shaw wrote [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], tuberculosis was still poorly understood by the general public and even by many physicians. Robert Koch had identified the tubercle bacillus in 1882, but effective treatments would not arrive until the development of streptomycin in the 1940s. The Edwardian medical profession's tools — rest cures, sanatorium treatment, and various [[inoculation]] therapies — were largely palliative. Shaw's depiction of competing medical theories around a single disease reflects the genuine uncertainty of the period, while also satirizing the tendency of specialists to fit every disease into their preferred explanatory framework.
