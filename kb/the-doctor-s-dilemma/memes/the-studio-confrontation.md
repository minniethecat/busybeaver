## Summary

In Act III (003-001 to 003-263), the [[medical-profession|doctors]] — armed with evidence of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s financial dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] and bigamous past — arrive at his studio to confront him. What they intend as a moral tribunal becomes instead a dramatic inversion: Louis refuses to accept their authority, mocks their professional pretensions, and exposes the hypocrisy of men who "threaten me with death" (003-145) when their moral accusations fail. The scene is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s dramatic climax — the moment when the conflict between [[art-and-beauty|artistic truth]] and professional morality becomes irreconcilable.

## Dramatic Context

The confrontation has been prepared by the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]], where the [[medical-profession|doctors]] agreed that Louis's character disqualified him from treatment (002-175). But in his own studio — surrounded by his paintings [003-001], with [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] as witness — Louis is not the supplicant patient of the [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]]. He is [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] in his domain, and the [[medical-profession|doctors]] are intruders whose moral authority crumbles on contact with his audacity.

## Characters Involved

- **[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]**: At his most magnificent — dishonest, defiant, and utterly unintimidated by professional authority. His speech at 003-145 is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most quoted passage.
- **[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]**: Silent witness; her presence — and her knowledge of Louis's dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] — makes the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' moral posturing seem cruel and redundant.
- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]**: The reluctant prosecutor, whose attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] makes him complicit in the very hypocrisy he is attempting to enforce.
- **[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]**: The most visibly outraged — his surgical certainty offers no resources for dealing with a patient who refuses moral deference.
- **[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]**: Withdraws — recognizing that the tribunal has failed and that the [[medical-profession|doctors]] have lost the moral argument.

## Key Dialogue

> (003-145) It's always the way with the inartistic professions: when they're beaten in argument they fall back on intimidation. I never knew a lawyer who didn't threaten to put me in prison sooner or later. I never knew a parson who didn't threaten me with damnation. And now you threaten me with death.

## Thematic Significance

The studio confrontation is Shaw's most radical dramatic moment: [[louis-dubedat|the artist]], not the doctor, controls the moral argument. Louis's defense — that [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic [[genius-and-talent|genius]] operates by different moral rules than professional respectability [003-085] — is neither endorsed nor rejected by [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]. Instead, Shaw forces the audience to recognize that the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' moral condemnation is inseparable from their [[professional-rivalry|professional jealousy]]. They are not judging [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]]; they are punishing his refusal to defer to their authority.
