## Overview

*The Philanderer* (1893) is one of George Bernard Shaw's earliest plays, written for the collection he titled *Plays Unpleasant*, which also included *[[widowers-houses|Widowers' Houses]]* (1892) and *Mrs. Warren's Profession* (1893–94). The play is a comedy of manners that grapples with the "New Woman" question, Ibsenism, and the institution of marriage — themes that would continue to occupy Shaw throughout his career and that resurface in the marital dynamics of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*.

Where *Widowers' Houses* attacks slum landlordism and *Mrs. Warren's Profession* confronts prostitution and the economic position of women, *The Philanderer* takes a lighter tone — a comedy built around a man, Charteris, who is intellectually committed to free love but emotionally entangled with two women. The play explores the gap between ideological commitment and personal behavior, a tension that Shaw would dramatize more acidly in the character of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], whose medical ethics prove no match for his personal desires.

## Themes

### Marriage and the "New Woman"

*The Philanderer* was written in the context of the Ibsen debates that swept London in the 1890s — the controversy over plays like *A Doll's House* and *Ghosts* that challenged conventional morality, particularly around marriage. Charteris, the play's protagonist, is a self-proclaimed Ibsenite who rejects traditional marriage on principle — but his principles collapse when confronted with the emotional realities of the women he claims to love.

This gap between intellectual conviction and emotional behavior anticipates the central dynamic of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, where [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s scientific principles are overwhelmed by his attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]. Both plays stage the failure of ideology to govern desire.

### Medical and Scientific Themes

*The Philanderer* is not explicitly a medical play, but it anticipates the institutional and professional concerns that Shaw would develop more fully in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* (1906) and in the [[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]]. The Ibsenite interest in heredity, disease, and the physical determinants of behavior — the "syphilis plays" tradition — informs Shaw's entire dramatic project.

The character of Dr. Paramore in *The Philanderer*, a physician whose scientific claims are revealed to be hollow, is a prototype for figures like [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] and [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] in *The Doctor's Dilemma* — medical professionals whose pretensions to scientific authority mask professional self-interest.

## Publication and Reception

*The Philanderer* was published in 1898 in the first volume of *Plays Pleasant and Unpleasant*, but it had to wait until 1905 — the year before *The Doctor's Dilemma* — for its first professional production, at the Court Theatre under the management of J.E. Vedrenne and Harley Granville-Barker, the same team that would produce *The Doctor's Dilemma* the following year.

The play has generally been received as a minor work in Shaw's canon — a transitional piece that shows the young playwright finding his voice for the sustained polemical drama that would follow. Its interest for readers of *The Doctor's Dilemma* lies in its anticipation of the concerns — marital, medical, institutional — that Shaw would treat more fully and more ferociously in the later play.
