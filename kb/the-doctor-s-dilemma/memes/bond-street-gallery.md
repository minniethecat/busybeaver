## Description

"One of the smaller Bond Street Picture Galleries" (005-001) — the setting for [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s final act. The entrance is from a picture shop. Nearly in the middle of the gallery is a writing-table where the Secretary corrects catalogue proofs. The walls are "covered with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s works" — his posthumous triumph. Two screens, also covered with drawings, stand near the corners. A small door marked "Private" leads to a back room from which [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] emerges, "beautifully dressed and apparently very happy and prosperous" (005-002).

## Dramatic Significance

The gallery is the scene of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s final moral assessment. [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], dead since Act IV, is vindicated here — his paintings sell, his [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] grows, and his widow prospers. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] who condemned him attend the exhibition as guests, their moral authority rendered irrelevant by [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s posthumous success. The gallery setting reverses the power dynamic of the [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]]: here, [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] judges medicine, and the verdict is not flattering. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s final speech about the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' "dreadful mistake" about Louis (005-042) (005-043) is delivered in the very space that proves her right.

## Scenes Set Here

- **Act V (005-001–005-136)**: The entire final act. The posthumous exhibition. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confronts [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]. The play's moral resolution.

## Real-World Counterpart

Bond Street in Mayfair was — and remains — the center of London's commercial [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] market. Shaw's choice of this location places [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s posthumous success within the real geography of [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]-world validation. A "smaller" Bond Street gallery suggests modest but genuine success — not the Royal Academy, but a respectable commercial venue.
