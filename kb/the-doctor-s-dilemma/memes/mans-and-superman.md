## Overview

*Man and Superman* (1903) is George Bernard Shaw's most sustained dramatic statement of his philosophical convictions — a "comedy and a philosophy" whose blend of intellectual debate and social comedy established the model for the major plays of his middle period, including *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* (1906). The play develops Shaw's theory of the Life Force — the evolutionary drive toward higher consciousness that operates through human beings, and particularly through women's pursuit of the fathers of their children.

The play's third act, "Don Juan in Hell," is a dream sequence in which the characters debate the nature of heaven, hell, love, and evolution — a philosophical interlude that has often been detached from the play and performed independently. The play also includes *The Revolutionist's Handbook*, a prose appendix attributed to the character John Tanner, which develops Shaw's ideas about marriage, property, and social organization.

## Relation to The Doctor's Dilemma

*Man and Superman* is the play that most directly establishes the philosophical context for [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]. Written three years earlier, it articulates the theory of the Life Force that underlies [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the later play]]'s treatment of [[genius-and-talent|genius]] — the idea that Nature is an evolutionary force working through exceptional individuals to advance the species. This theory provides the intellectual justification for valuing a man of genius over an ordinary man — the very calculus that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] uses to condemn [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]].

But [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]] complicates the Life Force theory in ways that *Man and Superman* does not anticipate. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] may be a genius — or he may be a fraud. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s devotion to him may be the Life Force operating through her — or it may be blind self-deception. And [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision to let [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] die, which he justifies in terms of the higher value of artistic genius, is transparently driven by personal desire. The philosophical certainties of *Man and Superman* dissolve, in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the later play]], into moral ambiguity.

### The Woman Question

Both plays are fundamentally concerned with the evolving relationship between men and women. *Man and Superman* argues that women are Nature's agents in the evolutionary process — they pursue men, select mates, and drive the species forward. This reversal of the conventional Victorian gender dynamic (the man as active pursuer, the woman as passive recipient) is one of Shaw's signature ideas.

In [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]], this idea is complicated by economic and professional structures. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is not the pursuing woman of Shavian theory but a wife who depends on male authority — first her husband's, then the doctor's. The Life Force, if it is operating through her, is constrained by the institutional structures ([[private-practice|private medicine]], [[marriage-and-gender|Victorian marriage]], [[poverty-and-class|economic dependence]]) that Shaw's plays anatomize.

## Themes

- **The Life Force**: The central concept of *Man and Superman* — the evolutionary impulse that drives human development — is the intellectual framework that both enables and is challenged by [[ridgeons-dilemma|Ridgeon's dilemma]].
- **Marriage**: The play's treatment of marriage as a mechanism of the Life Force is developed with greater psychological complexity in *The Doctor's Dilemma*, where marriage is also an economic and legal institution.
- **Genius**: The special value of exceptional individuals, the justification for sacrificing the ordinary to save the extraordinary, is a theme that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|both]] plays share.

## Publication and Reception

*Man and Superman* was published in 1903 and first performed (without Act III, the "Don Juan in Hell" interlude) in 1905 at the Court Theatre. The complete play was not staged until 1915. Its critical reception was mixed — the philosophy was admired, the dramatic structure questioned — but it established Shaw as a major intellectual force in the English theater, a position he consolidated with [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]] the following year.
