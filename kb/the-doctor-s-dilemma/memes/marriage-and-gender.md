## Definition

Marriage in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* is both a dramatic relationship and a social institution under examination. Shaw uses the [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]s' marriage — [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s unwavering devotion to her dishonest husband — to explore the tensions between love, loyalty, and moral judgment, while [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s overwhelmingly male medical world highlights the exclusion of women from professional authority (002-002).

## Context in the Play

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s sole significant female character, and her position is defined entirely through her relationship to her husband. She is "wrapped up for dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ure" (002-002) — always on the verge of leaving, yet never leaving. Her defense of Louis, delivered to the assembled [[medical-profession|doctors]], is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s emotional and moral centrepiece. She knows her husband [[truth-and-deception|lies]] and cheats, yet she believes in his [[genius-and-talent|genius]] and his love.

The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' reactions to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] reveal their own gender assumptions. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s growing attraction to her complicates his medical judgment, and the all-male dinner p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y where [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s fate is decided (002-001) literalizes the exclusion of women from medical decision-making. Emmy, the serving-woman, represents the working-[[poverty-and-class|class]] woman whose labor enables [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al world but who has no voice in it (001-002).

## Shaw's Position

Shaw was a vocal supporter of women's rights and gender equality, active in the suffrage movement and a member of the Fabian Women's Group. Yet his female characters, including [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]], often embody a self-sacrificing devotion that modern readers may find problematic. Shaw's feminism was complex: he advocated for women's economic independence while also celebrating the nurturing, life-affirming qualities he associated with the feminine.

## Historical Background

The Edwardian period saw intensifying debates about marriage, divorce, and women's roles. The Married Women's Property Act (1882) had given wives control over their own earnings, and the suffrage movement was gaining momentum. Medical schools remained overwhelmingly male; the first woman to qualify as a doctor in Britain, Elizabeth Garrett Anderson, had done so only forty years earlier.
