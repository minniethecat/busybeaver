## Overview

The doctor as businessman is perhaps the most uncomfortable theme in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* — uncomfortable because it is not a caricature but a recognition. Shaw shows that [[medical-profession|medical practice]] in Edwardian London was, at its economic core, a business transaction: patients paid fees, doctors collected income, and the successful physician was one whose commercial instincts were as sharp as his clinical ones. The tragedy of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma lies partly in this: a man who wishes to be a scientist and a healer is forced by the system to be a businessman as well.

The spectrum runs from [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]], who serves the poor and cannot afford his own treatment, to [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]], who ran a shop-window practice with "Cure Guaranteed" [001-054] and retired wealthy to Hertfordshire [001-081]. Between them stand [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], and [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] — respected professionals whose [[honor-and-reputation|reputations]] are also their most valuable business assets.

## In the Play

### Schutzmacher's Business Model

[[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most explicit embodiment of the doctor-as-businessman. His father's practice dispensed with professional pretense entirely: "It was no brass plate. It was a shop window: red, you know, with black letters" [001-003]. The model was retail: cheap medicines, confident guarantees, high volume. "Cure Guaranteed" [001-054] is a commercial slogan, not a medical promise — and it made money.

Schutzmacher's own innovation was branding. When asked whether "Cure Guaranteed" appeared on the brass plate, he explains that it was his inspiration — a marketing insight disguised as a diagnostic breakthrough [001-054]. His frankness about the commercial nature of his practice is almost endearing, and certainly more honest than [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s pretense that his treatments are purely scientific.

### The Harley Street Business

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]] practice is a more refined business model but a business nonetheless. His patient list is capped at ten — an arbitrary limit that functions simultaneously as a medical decision (he can only treat so many) and a marketing strategy (scarcity increases value). His knighthood is openly acknowledged as a business asset: "Just rolling in money! I wish you rich G.P.'s would teach me how to make money" [001-047].

The dinner at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] in Act II is a business meeting dressed as a social occasion. The doctors discuss patients, treatments, and reputations over dinner [002-001] — the social economy of medical London is inseparable from its commercial economy. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s invitation to the dinner is itself a business transaction: her presence, and Louis's, will cement the professional relationships that Ridgeon needs to maintain his practice [001-360].

### The Fee Structure

The play's references to money are frequent and precise. "Guineas" is the currency of elite medicine [001-003]; "double the fees" for women's cases represents market segmentation [001-003]; Schutzmacher's "sixpence" shop is the low end of the market [001-054]; and [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]'s inability to pay any fee at all represents the market's failure [001-003]. Every doctor in the play knows exactly what his time is worth — and exactly how to price it.

## Shaw's Critique

Shaw's argument is not that doctors should be poor — his Fabian socialism was materialist, not ascetic — but that the commercial structure of medicine corrupts its clinical practice. When income depends on the number of paying patients, prevention is disincentivized. When reputation is a financial asset, honest uncertainty becomes unaffordable. When the market allocates treatment, the poor go without.

The solution Shaw implies is structural: public funding, salaried doctors, healthcare as a right rather than a commodity. Until that transformation occurs, every doctor is, to some degree, a businessman — and every patient is, to some degree, a customer. The medical transaction cannot be purified of its commercial logic; it can only be reorganized within a different economic system.
