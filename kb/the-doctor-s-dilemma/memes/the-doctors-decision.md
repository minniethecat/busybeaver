## Summary

The Doctors' Decision is the central moral action of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*: the collective choice by [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and his colleagues to refuse the [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] to [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] and instead treat [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]], the impoverished general practitioner. The decision is made on the terrace of the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter Hotel]] at Richmond after dinner, in evening dress, over coffee and cigarettes — a life-and-death choice indistinguishable from a social occasion.

The decision process unfolds in stages. First, the [[medical-profession|doctors]] discuss [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] in his absence after he and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] depart from the dinner [002-002]. Each doctor offers a diagnosis according to his specialty — [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] finds a surgical solution, [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] a musical one — in a satirical demonstration of professional tunnel vision. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] presents the [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] as the one genuine possibility, but frames it within the zero-sum logic of limited capacity.

The critical moment comes when [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]] presses [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] on the real reason for his decision. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] asks: "Would you let your own son die to save a man of genius?" — and the question's relentless logic tightens around him. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] delivers the play's most famous moral indictment:

> (002-210) You should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession because its business is to heal and mend men and women.

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s response — "In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I'm to kill my patient" [002-211] — acknowledges the decision's moral reality even as he proceeds to make it.

## The Two Patients

| Patient | Case | Decision |
|---------|------|----------|
| [[louis-dubedat]] | Young artist, tubercular, morally objectionable | Refused treatment |
| [[dr-blenkinsop]] | Impoverished GP, honest, professionally useful | Receives treatment |

The choice between these two men crystallizes the play's critique of [[private-practice|private medicine]]. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] is objectionable on every ground — he lies, he cheats, he manipulates, his marriage is bigamous — but [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s stated reason for choosing against him is not moral but medical: his death will free [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] to marry again. The supposedly clinical decision is in fact driven by personal desire.

## Key Dialogue

> (002-200) I did it because I wanted to marry his widow.

## Thematic Significance

The Doctors' Decision is Shaw's dramatization of how professional culture corrupts moral judgment. The decision is made collectively rather than individually — a conspiracy rather than a choice — and the setting (a hotel terrace after dinner) makes visible the entanglement of medicine with [[poverty-and-class|class]] and leisure. These men do not decide in a clinic; they decide as gentlemen at ease.

The decision also exposes the instrumental logic that underlies professional ethics. [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] is chosen not because he is morally superior to [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] but because he is professionally useful — a doctor who treats the poor may save "fifty lives" for every one that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] saves by treating him. This utilitarian calculus, while superficially rational, is indistinguishable from the professional self-interest that the play satirizes.
