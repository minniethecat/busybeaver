## Overview

Conscience, as an internal moral arbiter, is a recurring concern in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Shaw explores how professional obligations, personal desires, and institutional pressures can suppress or distort the voice of conscience.

## In the Play

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s central dilemma is fundamentally a crisis of conscience. He must decide which patient to save — a decision that forces him to weigh artistic value against medical need, personal attraction against professional duty. His conscience is troubled not by the fact of choosing but by the suspicion that his choice was corrupted by his attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]].

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], by contrast, appears to lack conventional conscience entirely. He borrows money without repaying, deceives his wife, and feels no remorse. Yet Shaw complicates this picture: Dubedat's dedication to his art is presented as a form of conscience — an aesthetic rather than moral one — that commands a different kind of respect.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's treatment of conscience reflects his broader critique of conventional morality. He suggests that professional conscience — the doctor's duty to heal — can become a convenient mask for self-interest, while the absence of conventional conscience in the artist may coexist with a higher creative integrity.

The play does not resolve the question of which conscience — the doctor's or the artist's — is more genuine. Ridgeon's moral certainty is undercut by his self-deception; Dubedat's apparent amorality is complicated by the integrity of his art.
