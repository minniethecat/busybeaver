## Overview

The knighthood is a recurring motif in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Three of the play's central doctors bear the title "Sir" — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]], [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]], and [[sir-ralph-bloomfield-bonington|Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington]] — and the play begins with a dinner celebrating Ridgeon's recent elevation.

## In the Play

The opening scene of Act I takes place in [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] on the occasion of his knighthood celebration (001-001). The honor is the ostensible reason for the gathering of doctors, but Shaw uses the occasion to expose the professional rivalries, vanities, and insecurities that the honor system generates.

Sir Patrick, who has held his title longest, treats knighthoods with worldly skepticism. Ridgeon's new knighthood marks his arrival at the pinnacle of professional recognition — a position that makes his subsequent moral failure all the more pointed.

## Shaw's Critique

Shaw uses the knighthood system to critique the conflation of professional success with moral authority. A knighthood was the highest honor available to a British physician, but Shaw suggests it functioned more as a marketing device than a guarantee of merit. The title "Sir" conferred social prestige and attracted wealthy patients, reinforcing the economic incentives that Shaw saw as corrupting medical practice.

The fact that Ridgeon's knighthood celebration frames the play's opening — and that his moral failure unfolds against this backdrop of professional celebration — is characteristic of Shaw's structural irony.
