# Phagocytosis

## Definition

Phagocytosis is the biological process by which certain white blood cells — called phagocytes — engulf and destroy foreign p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]icles such as bacteria, dead cells, and cellular debris. In Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* (1906), the theory serves as the scientific foundation of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s medical discovery and as a satirical vehicle for exposing the competing medical fads of the Edwardian era.

## Context in the Play

Phagocytosis is introduced early in Act I when [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] explains his discovery to [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]. The older physician, unimpressed by new theories, recalls having heard of phagocytes "years ago: long before you came into fashion" [001-152]. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s innovation is not the discovery of phagocytes themselves but his method of enhancing their action through what he calls "opsonin" — a substance that "butters" the microbes so that phagocytes will consume them [001-149].

[[bloomfield-bonington|Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington]] ([[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]) later elevates phagocytosis to a universal slogan — "stimulate the phagocytes" [001-283] — repeating the phrase with oracular confidence while misunderstanding the underlying mechanism entirely. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] notes privately that [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] "ought to know the difference between a [[inoculation|vaccine]] and an antitoxin" [001-320], and that his indiscriminate use of serums is "murder: simple murder."

The motif reaches its tragicomic climax when [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], having agreed to treat the [[death-and-mortality|dying]] [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], frames the patient in purely mechanistic terms: "To me you are simply a field of battle in which an invading army of tubercle bacilli struggles with a patriotic force of phagocytes" [004-848]. When the treatment fails and [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s condition worsens, [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] wonders aloud whether they have "over-stimulated the phagocytes" — that the white cells might have "not only eaten up the bacilli, but attacked and destroyed the red corpuscles as well" [005-972].

[[cutler-walpole|Walpole]], the surgeon, dismisses the entire theory as irrelevant, declaring flatly that "phagocytosis is pure rot: the cases are all [[blood-poisoning]]; and the knife is the real remedy" [001-297].

## Shaw's Position

Shaw uses phagocytosis as a satirical device to lampoon the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]'s tendency to reduce complex human suffering to reductive mechanistic slogans. Each doctor in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] appropriates the theory to suit his own commercial interest: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s genuine scientific caution is drowned out by [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s fashionable enthusiasm and [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s surgical dogmatism. The phrase "stimulate the phagocytes" becomes, in Shaw's hands, the medical equivalent of a political catchphrase — resonant, empty, and lethally misapplied.
