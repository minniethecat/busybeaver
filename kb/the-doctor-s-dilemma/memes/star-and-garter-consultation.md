## Summary

Act II takes place on the terrace of the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter Hotel]] at Richmond, where the [[medical-profession|doctors]] have dined with Louis and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]. After dinner, Louis and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]], and the [[medical-profession|doctors]] remain to discuss his case over coffee. The [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] that follows is Shaw's most concentrated [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] of [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]al culture: a decision about life and death conducted in evening dress, on a moonlit terrace, governed more by social performance than clinical evidence. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma crystallizes here — he knows he must choose between [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] and [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]], and the dinner has given him every social reason to prefer the doctor over [[louis-dubedat|the artist]].

## Dramatic Context

The dinner p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y has already established Louis's charm and his refusal to perform the grateful-patient role. His dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ure — "wrapped up for dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ure" (002-002) — leaves the [[medical-profession|doctors]] free to discuss him as a case rather than a person. The [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] unfolds as a series of professional performances: [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s grandiloquent evasions, [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s surgical certainty, [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s gruff wisdom. Only [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]], fumbling with the telephone (002-088), seems outside [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al performance — and it is precisely his ordinariness that will save him.

## Characters Involved

- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]**: Presiding, weighing, already leaning toward condemning [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]].
- **[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]**: Delivers the "high and great profession" speech (002-210) — [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral indictment.
- **[[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]**: [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s musical vacuity fills the [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] with sound signifying nothing.
- **[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]**: Surgical certainty as moral simplification.
- **[[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]]**: The unwitting beneficiary of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision — fumbling with the telephone, unaware his life is being weighed against another's.

## Key Dialogue

> (002-210) You should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession because its business is to heal and mend men and women.

> (002-211) In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I'm to kill my patient.

## Thematic Significance

The [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] is Shaw's most direct dramatization of how professional culture corrupts medical judgment. The setting — a hotel terrace, after dinner, in evening dress — makes visible the [[poverty-and-class|class]] dimensions of private medicine. These [[medical-profession|doctors]] do not consult in a clinic; they consult as [[gentleman|gentlemen]] at leisure. The life-and-death decision they make is indistinguishable from a social occasion, and this is Shaw's point: in a system where [[medical-profession|doctors]] are paid by the patients they attract rather than the patients they cure, medicine is always a social performance.
