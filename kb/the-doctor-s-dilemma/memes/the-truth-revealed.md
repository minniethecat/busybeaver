## Summary

The Truth Revealed is the climactic event of Act V of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*: the moment when [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]] confesses to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] that he deliberately chose not to treat [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], and that his motive was personal — he wanted to marry her. The confession occurs at [[bond-street-gallery|the Bond Street gallery]] during the [[the-opening-reception|opening reception]] of [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s posthumous exhibition, and it destroys whatever emotional peace [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] has achieved since her husband's death.

The stage is set with cruel irony: [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] has remarried, apparently happily, and the gallery walls are covered with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s works [005-001]. She has moved on — or believes she has. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s confession shatters this equilibrium. The truth "has got me by the throat," he says: "the truth must come out" [005-041]. He tells her that he "used that method" — referring to the [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] — "to kill" her first husband [005-041].

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s response is devastating: "How could you think that I—his wife—could ever think of you—" [005-001]. The truth that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] has carried for the duration of the play, and which he believes will liberate him once spoken, proves instead to be the instrument of her final disillusionment.

## Dramatic Context

The revelation occurs in the gallery where [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s art is being exhibited — his posthumous triumph, the validation of the [[genius-and-talent|genius]] in which [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] so passionately believes. The setting gives the confession an almost ritual quality: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] comes to the temple of [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s art to confess his crime against the artist.

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] is present, as he has been throughout, providing the moral commentary that the other characters lack. His presence recalls his "high and great profession" speech from the [[star-and-garter-consultation|Star and Garter]] [002-210] — the warning that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] ignored.

## Key Dialogue

> (005-041) the truth must come out. I used that method to kill him.

> (005-001) How could you think that I—his wife—could ever think of you—

> (005-049) Have you forgotten that he died?

## Thematic Significance

The Truth Revealed is the play's final argument about the relationship between knowledge and action. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] believes that telling the truth will absolve him — that confession is the necessary prelude to a new beginning with [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]. He is wrong on both counts. The truth does not absolve; it destroys. And [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s response — her incredulous "How could you think that I—his wife—could ever think of you—" [005-001] — reveals that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s entire moral calculus was based on a misunderstanding of the woman he claimed to love.

The truth, in Shaw's handling, is not redemptive but corrosive. It does not set [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] free; it exposes the self-deception that has governed his actions from the beginning. The physician who prided himself on his scientific objectivity is revealed as a man governed entirely by desire — and the revelation comes too late to undo the harm he has done.
