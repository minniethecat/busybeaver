## Overview

The Waiter is the most minor of the named (or quasi-named) characters in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, appearing in a single brief exchange at the end of Act II at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter Hotel]]. He speaks only two words — "Yes, sir" (002-219) — yet his appearance provides the practical punctuation to the act's climactic conversation between [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]].

## Role in the Play

The Waiter's sole function is to respond to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s request for the bill (002-218) at the conclusion of the [[star-and-garter-consultation|dinner at the Star and Garter]]. His entrance coincides with the end of the most ethically charged conversation in the play — the exchange in which [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]] weigh the lives of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] and [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]] against each other. The mundane request for the bill following this moral deliberation is a characteristic Shavian touch: the profound and the trivial coexist without comment.

The Waiter's appearance follows immediately after [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s exit line — "I'm going for my hat" (002-216) — and [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s ringing of the bell. The brief exchange — "My bill, please" / "Yes, sir" / "He goes for it" (002-218-002-220) — closes the act with a gesture toward the commercial realities that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] and his colleagues inhabit even as they debate matters of life and death.

## Character Analysis

The Waiter has no distinguishing characteristics beyond his profession and his function. He is not described in the stage directions beyond the simple notation "A waiter comes" (002-217 for context). His presence is entirely utilitarian — a reminder that the play's medical-philosophical drama takes place within a world of ordinary commercial transactions, where dinners must be paid for and waiters must be summoned. This juxtaposition of the cosmic and the mundane is a recurring Shaw technique: the play's most serious ethical question is framed by the rituals of a professional dinner party, complete with coffee, cigarettes, and a bill to be settled.

## Key Scenes

- **Act II (002-217)-(002-220)**: Sole appearance — summoned by [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s bell, receives the request for the bill, and departs to fetch it.
