## Overview

Antitoxin therapy is a central medical concept in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, serving both as [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s genuine scientific discovery and as the vehicle for Shaw's [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] of medical pretension.

The term appears throughout [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] in debates between qualified [[medical-profession|doctors]] and the enthusiastic but misguided [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] ([[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]), whose misunderstanding of antitoxin function drives much of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s medical comedy.

## In the Play

### B.B.'s Theory of Antitoxins

[[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]], a general practitioner who has adopted [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s terminology without understanding it, confidently expounds his theory of antitoxin treatment to the assembled [[medical-profession|doctors]]:

> [001-240] "Nature has provided, in the white corpuscles as you call them — in the phagocytes as we call them — a natural means of devouring and destroying all disease germs. There is at bottom only one genuinely scientific treatment for all diseases, and that is to stimulate the phagocytes. Stimulate the phagocytes. Drugs are a delusion. Find the germ of the disease; prepare from it a suitable antitoxin; inject it three times a day quarter of an hour before meals; and what is the result? The phagocytes are stimulated; they devour the disease; and the patient recovers — unless, of course, he's too far gone." [001-240]

[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] goes on to describe how he accidentally cross-[[inoculation|inoculate]]d two patients — a typhoid case with tetanus serum and vice versa — and both recovered. From this he concluded that "so long as you [[phagocytosis|stimulate the phagocytes]], what does it matter which p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]icular sort of serum you use?" [001-248]

He proudly claims to have "used all sorts of antitoxins absolutely indiscriminately, with perfectly satisfactory results" and even "treated a scarlet fever case with a sample of hydrophobia serum from the Pasteur Institute." [001-248]

### Ridgeon's Scientific Critique

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], the actual discoverer, is horrified by [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s misunderstanding. He privately tells [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]:

> [001-277] "But he really ought to know the difference between a vaccine and an antitoxin. Stimulate the phagocytes! The vaccine doesn't affect the phagocytes at all. He's all wrong: hopelessly, dangerously wrong. To put a tube of serum into his hands is murder: simple murder." [001-277]

The gap between [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s scientific understanding and [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s confident ignorance illustrates Shaw's broader critique — that the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]'s [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] rests on public credulity rather than genuine competence.

### Antitoxin in Dubedat's Treatment

When [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] agrees to take on [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] as a patient, he casually requests antitoxin from [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] without any diagnostic specificity:

> [003-239] "On Tuesday morning send me down a tube of some really stiff antitoxin. Any kind will do. Don't forget. Goodbye, Colly." [003-239]

This moment crystallizes [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]]: a [[death-and-mortality|dying]] man's treatment determined by a doctor who believes any antitoxin works for any disease, supplied by the one doctor who knows the truth but withholds it as p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] of his moral calculus.

## Medical Context

In early 20th-century medicine, antitoxin therapy (serum therapy) was a genuine scientific advance — Emil von Behring won the 1901 Nobel Prize for diphtheria antitoxin. Shaw uses the real science of immunology (phagocytosis, opsonin, antitoxin) as scaffolding for his dramatic dilemma, lending credibility to the medical debate while exposing how easily scientific language can be co-opted by those who do not understand it.
