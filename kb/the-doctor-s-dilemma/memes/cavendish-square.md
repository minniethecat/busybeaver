## Description

Cavendish Square is a garden square in the Marylebone district of London's West End, referenced in the stage directions of Act I of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. The stage direction locates the action in a consulting room within "the doctors' quarter between Cavendish Square and the Marylebone Road" [001-002]. This geographic detail is not incidental — it places [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s practice at the epicenter of Edwardian medical professionalism, where the most eminent London physicians clustered.

The square was laid out in the early eighteenth century as part of the Cavendish-Harley estate development. By the Edwardian period, it had become the heart of private medical London — the address that signified a physician had arrived at the top of his profession. The proximity to [[harley-street|Harley Street]], just one block away, reinforced this concentration of medical prestige.

## Dramatic Significance

Shaw's choice to specify "the doctors' quarter between Cavendish Square and the Marylebone Road" [001-002] is thematically deliberate. The concentration of medical practice in a single neighborhood was not a coincidence of urban geography but a structural feature of [[private-practice|private medical practice]]. A physician needed an address in the "right" quarter to attract the fee-paying patients who sustained his income.

The consulting room at [[ridgeons-consulting-room|this address]] is the space where [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] receives patients, writes prescriptions, and dispenses professional judgment — but the locale also represents the [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|institutional structure]] that makes this private, fee-for-service medicine possible. The neighborhood itself is a marker of [[poverty-and-class|class]]: the poor do not consult physicians in Cavendish Square.

## Real-World Counterpart

The real Cavendish Square was, and remains, a significant address in London's medical geography. Harley Street, one block away, became the definitive address for private medical consulting in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The area between Cavendish Square and the Marylebone Road was thickly populated with the consulting rooms and private practices of London's most prominent physicians.

Shaw's naming of this district is unusually specific for his stage directions and reflects his documentary interest in the actual conditions of Edwardian medicine. The detail grounds [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s satirical critique in a recognizable real-world geography — these are not generic doctors in a generic consulting room, but physicians whose professional identity is inseparable from their address.
