# The Gentleman

## Definition

The "gentleman" in Edwardian England was a social ideal combining birth, manners, moral conduct, and professional standing. In *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, Shaw systematically exposes the term's instability: it is invoked by characters to justify prejudice, claim moral superiority, or enforce social hierarchy, and its meaning shifts depending on who is speaking and what they want.

## Context in the Play

The word "gentleman" appears at strategic moments across [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], each instance revealing more about the speaker than the person described. [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]], the Jewish moneylender, enters as "a middle-aged gentleman, well dressed" [001-080] — the stage direction immediately qualifying his appearance with racial markers ("the handsome gentlemanly Jew") that signal the period's ambivalent inclusion of assimilated Jews within the gentlemanly [[poverty-and-class|class]].

The term's exclusionary edge sharpens when [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] reports [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s antisemitic insult: "he made a very uncalled-for remark about a Jew not understanding the feelings of a gentleman" [002-540]. [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]'s dry response — "I must say you Gentiles are very hard to please. You say we are no gentlemen when we lend money; and when we refuse to lend it you say just the same" — exposes the double bind of a social category defined to exclude.

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] himself is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most knowing manipulator of gentlemanly rhetoric. Faced with the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' moral judgment of his bigamous [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]], he counter-attacks: "if you'd told me that [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] wasn't married, I'd have had the gentlemanly feeling and [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic instinct to say that she carried her [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] certificate in her face" [003-764]. He then instructs the [[medical-profession|doctors]] on proper conduct: "when [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] comes in, please remember that she's a lady, and that you are supposed to be gentlemen" [004-872]. The irony is multiple: the "immoral [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]" lectures the "moral men" on gentlemanly behavior, and the moral men — who have just discovered his bigamy and fraud — comply.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw uses the gentleman concept to perform a surgical dissection of Edwardian hypocrisy. The term's meaning is shown to be entirely situational: it can denote birth ([[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]'s Jewishness disqualifies him regardless of manners), professional standing (the titled [[medical-profession|doctors]] are gentlemen by rank), moral conduct ([[art-and-beauty|Dubedat's art]] and sacrifice are gentlemanly, his dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] is not), or mere rhetorical convenience. By [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s end, no character has a stable claim to the label, and the audience is left to question whether "gentleman" describes anything real at all — or simply who holds the power to define it.
