## Definition

Professional rivalry among [[medical-profession|doctors]] is both a source of comedy and a driver of tragedy in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Shaw depicts the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] not as a collegial community but as a competitive marketplace where [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]]s, patients, and fees are contested. The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' collective decision about [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s fate is shaped as much by their rivalries with each other — and with [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] who challenges their authority — as by medical or ethical considerations (001-275).

## Context in the Play

The four principal [[medical-profession|doctors]] represent competing approaches to medicine, and their interactions are marked by barely concealed competition. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s scientific evangelism (001-209) is p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ly a generational challenge to [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s clinical empiricism. [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]'s obsession with the "[[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]" (004-050) is a bid for surgical distinction in a profession dominated by [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|physicians]]. [[the-knighthood-reception|Ridgeon's knighthood]] provokes his colleagues' envy even as it secures his status.

[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s entry into this competitive ecosystem is explosive. As an [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]], he threatens the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' claim to cultural authority; as a charismatic personality, he challenges their social dominance; as a patient who refuses to be grateful, he undermines [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al compact. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] unite against him not because they agree about medicine but because they share an interest in defending their profession's prestige (003-145).

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s description of the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] as "a conspiracy" (001-275) captures the way professional solidarity masks competitive self-interest. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] conspire against the laity, but they also conspire against each other.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw saw professional rivalry as an inevitable consequence of market-based medicine. In a system where [[medical-profession|doctors]] compete for patients and fees, collegiality is always compromised by self-interest. His Fabian solution — socialized medicine with salaried [[medical-profession|doctors]] — was designed to eliminate this structural conflict.

## Historical Background

The Edwardian medical marketplace was fiercely competitive. The Medical Register listed all qualified practitioners, but there was no limit on numbers, and London in p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]icular was overcrowded with [[medical-profession|doctors]] competing for wealthy patients. The stakes of professional [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] were high: a [[private-practice|Harley Street]] address and a knighthood could mean the difference between prosperity and obscurity.
