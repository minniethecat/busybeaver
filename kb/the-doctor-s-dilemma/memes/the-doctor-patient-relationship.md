## Definition

The doctor-patient relationship in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* is characterized by profound asymmetry. Doctors possess knowledge, authority, and the power to decide who receives treatment; patients possess vulnerability, hope, and — in the case of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] — a competing claim to truth. Shaw exposes the paternalistic assumptions underlying Edwardian [[medical-profession|medical practice]] and questions whether genuine consent is possible under conditions of radical inequality (001-275) (001-276).

## Context in the Play

The play's opening scene establishes [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al distance between doctor and patient. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] is a space of controlled access, mediated by the serving-woman Emmy, who filters the anxious patients waiting outside (001-003). The "lady bothering me to see the doctor" (001-003) is not named — she is defined entirely by her supplicant relationship to medical authority.

When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] invites [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] to bring her husband to a [[medical-profession|doctors]]' dinner, he transforms the clinical relationship into a social one. The dinner p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y (002-001) becomes a de facto tribunal where [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s fate is decided not through medical examination but through social judgment. The patient never consents to this process because he never knows it is happening.

[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s confrontation with the [[medical-profession|doctors]] in Act III (003-145) inverts the expected power dynamic. The patient accuses the [[medical-profession|doctors]] of bad faith, and they have no effective response except to wield their ultimate power — the power to let him die.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw argued that the doctor-patient relationship could never be ethically sound under a fee-for-service system because the doctor's financial interest conflicted with the patient's welfare. His solution was state-funded healthcare that would remove the economic incentive to overtreat, undertreat, or select patients based on ability to pay. The play dramatizes this argument by showing what happens when [[medical-profession|doctors]] become judges of their patients' moral worth.

## Historical Background

The Edwardian doctor-patient relationship was governed by professional etiquette rather than formal ethical codes. Doctors were expected to maintain a paternalistic authority; patients were expected to defer. The concept of informed consent did not exist in anything like its modern form. [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|Shaw's critique]] was radical for its time and anticipated many of the patient-rights movements of the late twentieth century.
