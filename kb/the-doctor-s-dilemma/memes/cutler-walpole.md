## Overview

Cutler Walpole is Shaw's most concentrated satirical portrait in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* — a surgeon who has built his career on removing the entirely fictional "[[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]," a structure he claims is responsible for virtually all human illness. Shaw's stage direction presents him as "an energetic, unhesitating man of forty, with a cleanly modelled face," someone who "seems never at a loss, never in doubt: one feels that if he made a mistake he would make it thoroughly and firmly" (001-126). He is "sm[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ly dressed with a fancy waistcoat, a richly colored scarf secured by a handsome ring, ornaments on his watch chain, spats on his shoes, and a general air of the well-to-do sportsman about him" (001-126). Walpole represents the tendency of specialists to see every medical problem through the lens of their own favored procedure, and his comic obsessiveness masks the genuine danger of surgical overreach.

## Role in the Play

Walpole functions primarily as comic relief — his relentless promotion of the nuciform-sac operation is one of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s running jokes — but his role has a serious underside. He represents the authoritarian dimension of [[medical-profession|medical practice]]: the surgeon who knows what is wrong with you before you have described your symptoms. Upon meeting [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], he immediately diagnoses him: "I know what's the matter with you. I can see it in your complexion. I can feel it in the grip of your hand" (001-126). His prescription is always the same: "Blood-poisoning. Ninety-five percent of the human race suffer from [[blood-poisoning|chronic blood-poisoning]], and die of it. It's as simple as A.B.C. Your [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]] is full of decaying matter — undigested food and waste products — rank [[blood-poisoning|ptomaines]]. Now you take my advice, [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]. Let me cut it out for you. You'll be another man afterwards" (001-126). His confrontation with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] in Act III exposes the limitations of surgical certainty when faced with [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic intelligence.

## Character Analysis

Shaw uses Walpole to satirize the surgical culture of his era, when ambitious surgeons competed to identify new "syndromes" and "procedures" that would bear their names. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] provides the historical context: "The father used to snip off the ends of people's uvulas for fifty [[private-practice|guinea]]s, and paint throats with caustic every day for a year at two [[private-practice|guinea]]s a time. His brother-in-law extirpated tonsils for two hundred [[private-practice|guinea]]s until he took up women's cases at double the fees. Cutler himself worked hard at anatomy to find something fresh to operate on; and at last he got hold of something he calls the [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]], which he's made quite the fashion" (001-126). The [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]] — entirely imaginary — is Shaw's metaphor for the tendency of medical specialties to create the diseases they claim to cure. Walpole insists that "in an intelligently governed country people wouldn't be allowed to go about with [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]s, making themselves centres of infection. The operation ought to be compulsory: it's ten times more important than [[inoculation|vaccination]]" (001-126). Yet he himself admits he hasn't got one: "I'm as sound as a bell. [[About]] five percent of the population haven't got any; and I'm one of the five percent" (001-126). His dismissal of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s profession as "an illiterate profession, with no qualifications and no public register" (004-050) captures the inter-specialty rivalry that divided Edwardian medicine.

## Relationships

Walpole's relationships with the other [[medical-profession|doctors]] are marked by professional contempt barely concealed beneath collegiality. He dismisses [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] treatment with: "The opsonin is simple rot, as any capable surgeon can tell you; but we're all delighted to see your personal qualities officially recognized" (001-126). He dismisses [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s germ-theory evangelism as naive. His interactions with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] reveal a professional authoritarian who has never been challenged by a patient before — and who has no effective response when challenged. When [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] pawns his gold cigarette case, Walpole can only manage a defeated "It adds to your fascination" (003-145).

## Key Scenes

- **Act I (001-126)**: First appearance — bursts in to congratulate [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] on his knighthood, immediately diagnoses him with [[blood-poisoning]], and recommends removing his [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]].
- **Act I (001-126)**: [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s historical account of the Walpole surgical dynasty and the invention of the [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]].
- **Act III (003-145)**: Confrontation with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] in [[dubedats-studio|the studio]] — discovers his cigarette case has been pawned, and ends up lending [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] half-a-crown to pay the pawnbroker's fee.
- **Act IV (004-050)**: Writing names for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] in the death certificate book — "you'd better let me write the names down for you: you're sure to get them wrong. That comes of belonging to an illiterate profession, with no qualifications and no public register."
