## Overview

The Preface on Doctors is George Bernard Shaw's extended polemical essay published with the first edition of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* in 1906. At approximately 20,000 words, it is one of Shaw's most sustained pieces of non-fiction prose — a systematic critique of the Edwardian [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] that develops the arguments dramatized in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]. Shaw advocates for the nationalization of medical services, attacks the fee-for-service model of private practice, and argues that the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]'s claim to scientific authority masks a self-interested monopoly. The play itself dramatizes this thesis through characters like [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]], whose practice Shaw describes as treating "any kind" of disease with the same nostrum [001-238].

## Synopsis

The Preface proceeds through several interconnected arguments:

1. **The economic critique**: Private practice creates perverse incentives — [[medical-profession|doctors]] profit from illness, not health; the fee system rewards prolonged treatment rather than cure; and the poorest patients receive the worst care. This is dramatized in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] through [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]], a general practitioner whose "[[poverty-and-class|poverty]]" limits his ability to practice competently [001-010], and through [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s frank admission that private practice turns [[medical-profession|doctors]] into businessmen [001-311].

2. **The epistemological critique**: Medical knowledge is less scientific than [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] claims. Shaw attacks vivisection not on humanitarian grounds but on epistemological ones — animal experiments, he argues, produce misleading results because animal physiology differs from human. In [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] — despite its scientific apparatus — functions as a satirical device: the characters speak of "stimulating the [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]]" as if it were a magical formula rather than a verifiable medical procedure [001-233]. [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] even reduces it to "any kind" of antitoxin [001-238], revealing the gap between scientific pretension and practice.

3. **The professional critique**: The [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] is a "conspiracy against the laity" — a self-regulating monopoly that controls entry, sets prices, mystifies its knowledge, and polices its own members. The General Medical Council, far from protecting patients, protects [[medical-profession|doctors]] from competition. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] voices a version of this critique when he admits he is "a [[quackery|quack]] with a qualification" [001-324], acknowledging that professional credentials dignify practices indistinguishable from fraud.

4. **The biographical portrait**: Shaw includes a vivid character sketch of Almroth Wright, the bacteriologist at St. Mary's Hospital whose opsonin research inspired [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s treatment. The portrait is admiring and mocking in equal measure — Wright is a genuine scientific innovator whose personality embodies everything absurd about professional authority. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s combination of genuine scientific achievement [001-053] and moral blindness in choosing who lives and dies [004-189] mirrors the dual portrait Shaw paints of Wright.

## Characters Referenced

While the Preface is non-fiction, it names several real medical figures who correspond to [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s characters:

- **Almroth Wright** (1861–1947): Bacteriologist at St. Mary's Hospital, Paddington. Model for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]. Developed the [[opsonin|opsonin index]] — a method of measuring antibodies in the blood.
- **Sir William Osler** (1849–1919): Regius Professor of Medicine at Oxford. Referenced as an example of medical eminence resting on [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] rather than evidence.
- **Sir Frederick Treves** (1853–1923): Surgeon, famous for treating the "Elephant Man." Shaw criticizes his advocacy of vivisection.

## Themes

- **[[private-practice|Private Practice]]**: The Preface develops the economic critique that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] dramatizes — the fee system corrupts medicine by making [[medical-profession|doctors]] dependent on illness for income. [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]'s desperation for paying patients [001-010] and [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s confession about "selling cures" embody this argument [001-311].
- **[[scientific-medicine|Scientific Medicine]]**: Shaw's epistemological skepticism about medical science is more developed in the Preface than in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], where it is implied rather than argued. The opsonin running joke — "[[phagocytosis|stimulate the phagocytes]]" [001-233] — is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s comic stand-in for the Preface's serious argument about scientific overreach.
- **[[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|The Medical Profession]]**: The Preface provides the analytical framework for [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s dramatic [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]]. Shaw's notion of [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] as a "conspiracy against the laity" is embodied in the consulting-room scenes [001-001 to 001-340], where five [[medical-profession|doctors]] discuss patients as clinical cases rather than human beings.

## Critical Reception

The Preface was controversial on publication. Medical journals attacked Shaw as ignorant and irresponsible; lay reviewers praised his courage in confronting a powerful profession. The essay's proposal for a national health service was dismissed as utopian at the time but was p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ially realized with the creation of the National Health Service in 1948 — a development Shaw lived to see and celebrate. Modern scholarship generally acknowledges the Preface's prescience about healthcare economics while noting its scientific limitations ([[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|Shaw's critique]] of [[scientific-medicine|germ theory]], in p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]icular, has not aged well).
