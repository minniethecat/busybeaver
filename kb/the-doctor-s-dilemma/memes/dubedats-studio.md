## Description

[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s studio is the setting for both his moral trial (Act III) and his death (Act IV). In Act III, the studio is described as a working [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]'s space — "a large room" with canvases, drawings, and painting materials. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] poses for Louis on a model's throne. By Act IV, the studio has been transformed: "The easel is pushed back to the wall. [[dubedats-death|Cardinal Death]], holding his scythe and hourglass like a sceptre and globe, sits on the throne" (004-001). The hats of [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] and [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] hang on the hatstand — a domestic detail that underscores how the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] has colonized [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s space.

## Dramatic Significance

The studio functions as contested territory between the aesthetic and medical worlds. It is Louis's domain — the physical expression of his [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic identity — but it is progressively invaded by the [[medical-profession|doctors]] who will decide his fate. The transformation between Acts III and IV, with the easel pushed aside and the allegorical figure of Death installed on the model's throne, makes visible [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s central conflict: [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] versus medicine, individual [[genius-and-talent|genius]] versus professional authority. Louis's death in his own studio — the place where he created — gives the scene a tragic irony that no clinical setting could achieve.
