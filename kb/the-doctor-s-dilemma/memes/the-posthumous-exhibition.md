## Summary

The final act takes place at [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s posthumous exhibition in a Bond Street gallery. The walls are "covered with [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s works" (005-001) — [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s posthumous triumph. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]], "beautifully dressed and apparently very happy and prosperous" (005-002), presides over a success that Louis never achieved in life. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] — attend as guests, their professional authority rendered irrelevant by [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s belated vindication. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s attempt to justify himself to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is met with a devastating rejection that exposes the moral bankruptcy of his "dilemma."

## Dramatic Context

The exhibition reverses every power dynamic [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] has established. In Act I, the [[medical-profession|doctors]] held court in [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]]; in Act V, they are spectators in Louis's gallery. The [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] they condemned is now celebrated; the wife they pitied is now prosperous; and the "high and great profession" has been reduced to making social calls on a woman who no longer needs them.

## Characters Involved

- **[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]**: Transformed from supplicant to sovereign — she has curated Louis's legacy and built a successful life without the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' help.
- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]**: Comes to the exhibition seeking absolution and receives instead a moral condemnation more devastating than any medical [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]].
- **[[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]**: Still radiating self-satisfaction, but now irrelevant — Louis's posthumous success exposes [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s professional vacuity.
- **[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]**: Unable to process a world in which the patient he condemned has been vindicated by the [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] market.

## Key Dialogue

> (005-043) Well, did you find us so very cruel, after all? They tell me that though you have dropped me, you stay for weeks with the Bloomfield Boningtons and the Walpoles.

> (005-042) I hope you may never feel what I felt when I had to put him into the hands of men who defend the torture of animals because they are only brutes.

## Thematic Significance

The posthumous exhibition is Shaw's final judgment on the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]. Art survives; professional authority does not. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s quiet prosperity and Louis's posthumous fame are [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s answer to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s utilitarian calculus: [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] was worth saving, and the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' decision to let him die was not a rational choice but a moral failure disguised as professional judgment.
