## Overview

Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington — known universally as "B.B." — is Shaw's satirical portrait of the fashionable Edwardian physician whose social charisma far exceeds his scientific acumen. Described in the stage direction as having "a head like a tall and slender egg" and a "most musical voice," he radiates "an enormous self-satisfaction, cheering, reassuring, healing by the mere incompatibility of disease or anxiety with his welcome presence" (001-178). He is "known in the medical world as B.B.; and the envy roused by his success in practice is softened by the conviction that he is, scientifically considered, a colossal humbug" (001-178). This devastating judgment is tempered by Shaw's admission that "he knows just as much (and just as little) as his contemporaries," and "the qualifications that pass muster in common men reveal their weakness when hung on his egregious personality" (001-178).

## Role in the Play

Bonington represents the triumph of manner over matter in the medical marketplace. Where [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] agonizes over ethical choices and [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] obsesses over surgical procedure, B.B. simply radiates confidence and collects fees. He declares of himself, "I do the work of ten men. Am I giddy? No. No. If you're not well, you have a disease" (001-178). His appointment as [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s physician — after [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] withdraws — is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most pointed demonstration that medical outcomes are determined less by scientific competence than by social performance. When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] nominates him to take over treatment, the decision seals Louis's fate (003-243) (003-244).

## Character Analysis

Shaw uses B.B. to critique a medical culture in which [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] and [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|bedside manner]] substitute for clinical judgment. His comic obliviousness — forgetting he sent a singer to a surgeon for a vocal cord operation when the surgeon removed her [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]] instead — exemplifies [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al absentmindedness that his charm perpetually excuses. B.B.'s response: "You removed her [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]. Well, well! force of habit! force of habit! Never mind, ne-e-e-ver mind. She got back her voice after it, and thinks you the greatest surgeon alive; and so you are, so you are, so you are" (001-185). His scientific reasoning is circular: "What is a disease? The lodgment in the system of a pathogenic germ, and the multiplication of that germ. What is the remedy? A very simple one. Find the germ and kill it" (001-178). He champions [[scientific-medicine|germ theory]] with such surface confidence that his contradictions are invisible to him. After accidentally swapping typhoid and tetanus [[antitoxin|antitoxins]] and seeing both patients recover, he concludes triumphantly: "so long as you [[phagocytosis|stimulate the phagocytes]], what does it matter which p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]icular sort of serum you use for the purpose?" (001-178). He even admits privately that the [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]] "is utter nonsense: there's no such organ. It's a mere accidental kink in the membrane, occurring in perhaps two-and-a-half percent of the population" — but adds, "of course I'm glad for [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s sake that the operation is fashionable" (001-178).

## Relationships

B.B. maintains cordial relations with all the [[medical-profession|doctors]], largely because he lacks the self-awareness to recognize when he is being mocked. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] treats him with wry amusement — his pronouncement that "Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington is a very eminent physician" (002-214) drips with irony. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] uses him as a convenient alternative when he decides not to treat Louis. B.B.'s patient relationship with [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] — the man whose treatment he takes over from [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] — is never shown directly, but Louis's death under his care speaks to the limits of charm as a therapeutic agent. When Louis calls him "Poor old B.B." on his deathbed, B.B. misinterprets it entirely: "Poor fellow! Brain going" (004-001). After Louis's death, [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] stays "for weeks with the Bloomfield Boningtons and the [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]s" (005-043).

## Key Scenes

- **Act I (001-178)**: Grand entrance — "Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington wafts himself into the room" — followed by an extensive character-establishing stage direction. His first exchange reveals his attitude toward medical science and his relationship with his colleagues.
- **Act I (001-185)**: Exchange with [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] about the [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]] — "You removed her [[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]. Well, well! force of habit!"
- **Act II (002-002)**: Stage direction notes that "neither [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s years nor Bloomfield Bonington's majesty have the smallest apparent effect on [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]."
- **Act III (003-145)**: B.B. confronts Louis in his studio, eventually losing his temper: "I'll tell you what you are, sir. You're a scoundrel."
- **Act III (003-243–003-244)**: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] nominates B.B. to take over Louis's treatment.
- **Act V (005-043)**: [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] stays "for weeks with the Bloomfield Boningtons and the [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]s" after Louis's death.
