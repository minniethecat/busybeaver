## Overview

Money is the invisible force driving much of the action in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. The economic structure of Edwardian medicine — in which doctors competed for wealthy patients and the poor depended on charity — shapes every character's choices and constrains the play's moral possibilities.

## In the Play

The economic dimension appears most clearly in the contrast between [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]] and the fashionable physicians. Blenkinsop, who treats the poor, is himself impoverished and tubercular. The Harley Street consultants, by contrast, enjoy knighthoods, fine dinners, and social prestige.

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]'s cavalier relationship with money — borrowing without repaying, charming his way through debts — is presented as both a moral failing and, paradoxically, as somehow less corrupt than the doctors' respectable fee-collecting. Dubedat takes money openly; the doctors cloak their economic interests in professional dignity.

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma is partly economic: his limited treatment capacity means he must choose which patients to save, but the choice is inflected by the economic hierarchies of [[private-practice|private practice]].

## Shaw's Critique

Shaw, a lifelong socialist and Fabian, saw the fee-for-service model as fundamentally corrupting medicine. When a doctor's income depends on pleasing wealthy patients, clinical judgment is inevitably compromised. The play's portrait of the medical profession is inseparable from its economic critique: the doctors are not individually wicked but structurally corrupted by a system that rewards style over substance.
