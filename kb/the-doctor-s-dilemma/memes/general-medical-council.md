## Overview

The General Medical Council (GMC) is the statutory body responsible for regulating the medical profession in the United Kingdom, established by the Medical Act of 1858. In the polemical framework of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* and especially [[preface-on-doctors|its Preface]], the GMC represents the institutional mechanism through which [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|the medical profession]] regulates itself — and, in Shaw's analysis, protects its members from accountability.

The GMC maintains the Medical Register, the official list of qualified practitioners. Only those on the Register may legally practice medicine in the United Kingdom. The Council also has the power to strike doctors from the Register for professional misconduct — the ultimate professional sanction. Shaw's critique is that these powers, while nominally exercised in the public interest, function in practice to protect [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|the profession]] from competition and external scrutiny.

## Role in the Play

The GMC is never named in the dialogue of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], but its absence is part of Shaw's satirical strategy. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] in the play operate with complete professional impunity: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] can decide which patient lives and which dies [002-189]; [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] can propose unnecessary surgery; [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] can prescribe the same nostrum for "any kind" of disease [001-238]. No regulatory body intervenes, no professional sanction is threatened, no external oversight constrains their judgment. The GMC's invisibility is the measure of its failure.

The [[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]] develops the argument that the play dramatizes. Shaw characterizes the profession as a "conspiracy against the laity" — a self-regulating monopoly whose regulatory apparatus serves the monopolists. The GMC, far from being a check on professional abuses, is the institutional form of the conspiracy: a body of doctors regulating doctors, accountable only to themselves. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s confession that he is "a [[quackery|quack]] with a qualification" [001-324] cuts through the regulatory fiction: the GMC's qualifications dignify practices that are functionally indistinguishable from fraud.

## Historical Context

The Medical Act of 1858 created the General Council of Medical Education and Registration (later renamed the General Medical Council) in response to decades of professional agitation for statutory regulation. The Act was a compromise that gave [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|the profession]] significant control over its own regulation while establishing the principle that the state had an interest in who could practice medicine.

By Shaw's time, the GMC had established the Medical Register as the definitive list of qualified practitioners and had begun exercising its disciplinary functions — but the profession-dominated composition of the Council meant that disciplinary action was directed primarily at the most egregious cases. A physician like [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], whose misconduct was moral rather than technical, would never appear before the GMC.

## Comparative Context

The GMC and the [[royal-college-of-physicians|Royal College of Physicians]] represent different dimensions of professional regulation. The College is a voluntary professional body concerned with education, standards, and professional culture; the GMC is a statutory body with legal powers. Together, they constitute the dual architecture of medical self-regulation that Shaw attacks — the College defining professional excellence, the GMC defining professional legitimacy, and neither providing genuine accountability to patients.
