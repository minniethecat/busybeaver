## Overview

Vaccination — the deliberate introduction of weakened or killed disease agents to stimulate immunity — was, by 1906, the most prominent and controversial application of [[germ-theory|germ theory]] to public health. In [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]], vaccination serves as the benchmark against which [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] measures his own discovery. His declaration that the [[opsonin]] method is "ten times more important than vaccination" [001-003] is a calculated provocation, asserting that his laboratory-based, individualized approach represents the future of medicine.

Shaw places vaccination at the center of a complex web of medical and social questions: the relationship between prevention and cure, the tension between individual treatment and [[sanitation-and-public-health|public health]], and the conflict between scientific evidence and popular [[credulity-and-superstition-in-medicine|credulity]]. The play does not settle these questions but dramatizes the difficulties of making medical judgments in a climate of incomplete knowledge and commercial interest.

## In the Play

### Ridgeon's Claim

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] makes his claim about vaccination's relative importance during the Act I discussion with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]. The older doctor, struggling to understand the new discovery, provides the skeptical counterpoint. Ridgeon explains the principle connecting vaccination to his own work: "of course the principle of all vaccines is the same" [001-003]. Yet his superior claim rests on the precision of the [[opsonin]] index, which transforms vaccination from a crude population-level intervention into a targeted, individually calibrated treatment.

The distinction is crucial to Ridgeon's self-image as a [[scientific-medicine|scientific]] physician. Vaccination may prevent disease in populations, but the opsonin index can tell him, for any individual patient, whether [[inoculation]] will cure or kill. This marks the transition from the era of mass public health to the era of personalized laboratory medicine — a transition that Shaw both celebrates and interrogates.

### The Anti-Vaccinationist Context

Shaw also engages with the heated vaccination controversies of his time. In the Preface on Doctors, he discusses the anti-vaccination movement and the professional arrogance of doctors who dismiss lay skepticism. The play itself handles the issue through the character of [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], who notes the historical struggle to establish vaccination against smallpox and draws parallels to Ridgeon's own struggle for recognition.

The fact that Ridgeon once treated "a notorious and avowed anti-vaccinationist" [003-001] — with poor results, it is hinted — suggests that medical success may depend partly on the patient's belief and cooperation, a theme that connects vaccination to Shaw's broader interest in [[the-placebo-effect|the power of belief]] in healing.

## Medical Context

Edward Jenner's development of smallpox vaccination in 1796 had made vaccination the model of preventive medicine, and by 1906 smallpox vaccination was compulsory in England despite ongoing public resistance. Louis Pasteur's extension of the principle to rabies and anthrax had further elevated vaccination's scientific prestige. Shaw's treatment of the subject is characteristically double-edged: he acknowledges vaccination's importance while warning against the professional complacency that treats it as a solved problem and dismisses further innovation.
