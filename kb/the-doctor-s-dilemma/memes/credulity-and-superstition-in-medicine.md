## Overview

Credulity and superstition — the irrational beliefs that sustain faith in medical authority — form a central theme of Shaw's critique of the [[medical-profession|medical profession]] in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]. The play argues that the public's willingness to believe in doctors' pronouncements, combined with the profession's cultivation of that belief through ceremony, jargon, and commercial advertising, creates a medical marketplace in which neither patient nor physician is capable of distinguishing genuine knowledge from [[quackery|quackery]].

The play's most devastating confession comes from [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]], who admits that doctors themselves are responsible for the culture of medical credulity: "We set the example. We spread the superstition. We taught the people to believe in bottles of medicine" [001-003]. This is not a defense of the profession but an indictment — a recognition that the public's irrational faith in medicine was manufactured by the very practitioners who benefit from it.

## In the Play

### The Manufacture of Medical Faith

[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s admission about spreading superstition [001-003] is the play's key moment of professional self-awareness, and it is all the more damning for being delivered in his characteristically unreflective manner. B.B. is not troubled by the confession; he offers it as an observation, not a crisis of conscience. His indifference to the ethical implications of manufacturing credulity is, for Shaw, the deeper indictment — the medical profession has not only created a culture of irrational belief but has lost the capacity to recognize that this is a problem.

The mechanism of superstition is made visible throughout the play. The doctors' use of technical language — "opsonin," "phagocytosis," "nuciform sac," "ptomaines" — impresses the lay public without communicating actual information. [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] is awed by the consultation: "Wonderful thing it must be to be a doctor!" [002-002] — an awe that the doctors accept as their professional due rather than recognizing as the credulity that sustains their authority.

### The Complicity of Patients

Shaw is careful not to portray patients as innocent victims of medical manipulation. The public's credulity is presented as a willing collaboration, driven by the human desire for certainty in the face of illness and death. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s enthusiasm for the doctors' attention is matched by his wife [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s desperate faith in their power to save him — a faith that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] exploits when he withholds the truth about why he is not treating her husband.

The play thus describes a system in which credulity flows in both directions: patients believe in doctors because they need to, and doctors believe in their own authority because the market rewards it. The result is a closed loop of mutual reinforcement that resists correction by evidence or experience — the very definition of superstition.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's critique of medical credulity is part of his broader rationalist project. As a Fabian socialist and a devotee of [[scientific-medicine|scientific thinking]], he believed that social progress depended on replacing superstition with evidence, authority with inquiry, and faith with skepticism. The medical profession was, in his view, both a beneficiary of the public's credulity and an obstacle to its replacement by genuine knowledge. The paradox of the play is that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], the most genuinely scientific of the doctors, is also the one whose actions most clearly exploit the very credulity that science is meant to dispel.
