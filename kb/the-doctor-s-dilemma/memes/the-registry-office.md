## Description

The Registry Office is the civil office where [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]'s marriage was legally registered, referenced in Act III of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* during discussions about the legal standing of their union. The marriage certificate issued by the Registry Office is the document that gives legal force to their relationship — but the play raises the question of whether [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] was legally free to marry at all.

In [[dubedats-studio|Dubedat's studio]], [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] acknowledges the legal irregularity of his marriage to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]: "Of course it's bigamy" [003-117]. He further declares that "the criminal law on the subject of bigamy" is of no moral concern to him [003-117]. The Registry Office, as the civil institution that records marriages and therefore enforces marriage law, stands as the institutional counterpoint to [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s [[genius-and-talent|artistic]] disregard for legal convention.

## Dramatic Significance

The Registry Office never appears on stage; it is a purely referential space — an absent institution whose legal authority is invoked only to be dismissed. This is characteristic of Shaw's dramatic method: the institutions that ought to regulate conduct are notable by their absence from the scenes of action. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] decide who shall live and who shall die without reference to any hospital board or regulatory body; the Registry Office's marriage laws are brushed aside with a phrase.

The bigamy revelation is one of Shaw's devices for complicating audience sympathy. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] is charming, talented, and dying — a figure who might easily command uncomplicated pity. The insight that his marriage is legally void (and would have been void regardless of his death, since bigamy renders a marriage void ab initio) introduces moral complexity that resists easy judgment. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] does not recognize the Registry Office's authority — but the audience, living in a world where civil registration is the basis of legal identity, must reckon with what it means to dismiss that authority.

## Legal Context

Under English law in 1906, marriage was regulated by the Marriage Act of 1836 and subsequent legislation. Civil marriage at a registry office had been available since 1837, and bigamy — marrying while a previous spouse was still living — was a criminal offense. The Registry Office would have been the institution that both solemnized civil marriages and maintained the registers that made bigamy prosecutions possible.

[[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s casual acknowledgment of his bigamy is of a piece with his other transgressions — borrowing money he cannot repay, lying to the [[medical-profession|doctors]] about his financial resources, appropriating the artistic work of others. The Registry Office, like the [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|medical profession]] itself, represents the institutional order that [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] both depends on and defies.
