## Overview

St. Anne's Hospital is [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s institutional affiliation — the site of his opsonin research and the institutional context for his professional identity. While the hospital never appears on stage, it is referenced in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s exposition as the foundation of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s scientific authority. Unlike the [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] (private, commercial) and the [[star-and-garter-hotel|hotel]] (social, leisure), the hospital represents the public-facing, institutional dimension of [[medical-profession|medical practice]] — the site where science is supposed to serve the common good.

## Role in the Play

St. Anne's functions as an offstage institutional anchor for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s professional legitimacy. His knighthood, earned through hospital-based research [001-273], elevates him from practitioner to public figure. The hospital's absence from the stage is significant: [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] deliberately confines its action to the [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]], [[star-and-garter-hotel|the hotel]], [[dubedats-studio|the studio]], and [[bond-street-gallery|the gallery]] — spaces of private judgment and social performance rather than public service.

The hospital's invisibility supports [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|Shaw's critique]]. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] tells [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] he "can only take ten patients" [001-211] — a constraint imposed by the rationing logic of private practice, not by the hospital's public mission. If [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] showed St. Anne's functioning as a genuine public institution — treating the poor, advancing science without regard to fees — it would complicate the [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]]. By keeping the hospital offstage, Shaw ensures that the audience sees only the commercial and social dimensions of [[medical-profession|medical practice]].

## Historical Context

Teaching hospitals in Edwardian London (St. Mary's, St. B[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]holomew's, Guy's, St. Thomas's) were sites of genuine medical advance — bacteriology, immunology, antiseptic surgery — but also of rigid professional hierarchy and [[poverty-and-class|class]] exclusion. Honorary consulting positions at these hospitals were prestigious and unpaid; [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|physicians]] earned their livings through private practice, using hospital appointments to attract fee-paying patients. [[the-knighthood-reception|Ridgeon's knighthood]] for his hospital-based opsonin research [001-273] reflects the real-world career pattern of figures like Almroth Wright.

Shaw's model for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] was Almroth Wright, a bacteriologist at St. Mary's Hospital, Paddington, whose opsonin research was at the cutting edge of immunology. Wright combined genuine scientific achievement with a personality that Shaw found both admirable and absurd — the prototype for the brilliant professional whose moral judgment lags behind his technical competence.
