## Overview

Dr. Leo Schutzmacher is a general practitioner in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* who represents the commercial side of Edwardian medicine. His "Cure Guaranteed" practice — in which patients pay only if cured — is presented as both a business innovation and an ethical evasion.

## Character Profile

Schutzmacher is described as one of the doctors attending [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dinner in Act I (001-003). He studied at University College school alongside other members of the medical establishment. His most distinctive feature is his business model: he guarantees his cures, promising to refund fees if treatment fails.

## Role in the Play

### Acts I–II: Medical Consultation

Schutzmacher participates in the dinner and the examination of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]. His presence adds to the play's panoramic picture of the medical profession, representing the entrepreneurial general practitioner alongside the more prestigious consultants.

### The "Cure Guaranteed" System

Shaw uses Schutzmacher's "Cure Guaranteed" as a satirical device. The system appears generous — patients only pay if cured — but in practice it exploits the placebo effect and the fact that most illnesses resolve naturally. Patients who recover credit the doctor; those who don't are not charged, but this costs the doctor nothing.

## Shaw's Satirical Purpose

Schutzmacher embodies Shaw's critique of the economic structure of private medical practice. The "Cure Guaranteed" is a business model disguised as ethical innovation — it sounds generous while functioning as a marketing device. Shaw suggests that the fee-for-service model of [[private-practice|private practice]] inevitably corrupts medical judgment, and Schutzmacher's cheerful commercialism is one face of that corruption.
