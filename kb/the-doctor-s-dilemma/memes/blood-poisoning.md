# Blood Poisoning

## Definition

Blood poisoning (septicaemia) is a serious bloodstream infection caused by bacteria entering the blood from an infection elsewhere in the body. In *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, it becomes the signature [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] of [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]], a surgeon who sees the condition everywhere and treats it with a single surgical procedure — the removal of the "nuciform sac" — in a satirical portrait of medical monomania and the commercial incentives of fashionable surgery.

## Context in the Play

[[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] first deploys his [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] on [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] himself, interrupting a conversation about [[the-knighthood-reception|Ridgeon's knighthood]] with an unsolicited medical opinion: "Blood-poisoning. Ninety-five percent of the human race suffer from chronic blood-poisoning, and die of it. It's as simple as A.B.C. Your nuciform sac is full of decaying matter — undigested food and waste products — rank ptomaines" [001-207–209]. The proposed remedy is invariably surgical: "Let me cut it out for you. You'll be another man afterwards."

When [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] approaches the [[medical-profession|doctors]] to save her [[death-and-mortality|dying]] husband, [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] immediately overrides the presumed [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] of consumption: "the usual wrong [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]]: these damned general practitioners ought never to be allowed to touch a patient except under the orders of a consultant" [001-219]. His own verdict is instant: "the case is as plain as a pikestaff: bad blood-poisoning." He offers to operate for free, motivated by his self-professed love of "making people happy."

The [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] becomes [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s rhetorical reflex. When the [[medical-profession|doctors]] debate [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s moral character and whether he deserves treatment, [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] circles back: "I feel perfectly convinced that this is not a moral case at all: it's a physical one. There's something abnormal about his brain. That means, probably, some morbid condition affecting the spinal cord. And that means the circulation. In short, it's clear to me that he's suffering from an obscure form of blood-poisoning, which is almost certainly due to an accumulation of ptomaines in the nuciform sac. I'll remove the sac" [004-831].

[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s verdict on [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s theory is characteristically sweeping: "The nuciform sac is utter nonsense: there's no such organ. It's a mere accidental kink in the membrane, occurring in perhaps two-and-a-half percent of the population" [001-298]. Yet [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] declines to condemn the practice outright, noting that "the operation will do them no harm: indeed, I've known the nervous shakeup and the fortnight in bed do people a lot of good after a hard London season; but still it's a shocking fraud."

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] of [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] is at once specific and universal. Blood poisoning and the nuciform sac lampoon a real surgical fad of the Edwardian period (the "auto-intoxication" theory and the vogue for removing the allegedly toxic colon or appendix), but the deeper target is the structural incentive that turns every specialist into a monomaniac. [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] is not a conscious fraud — he genuinely believes — but his belief is shaped by what he can bill for. The surgeon who only has a knife sees every problem as surgical; the operating table, not [[ridgeons-laboratory|the laboratory]] or the bedside, becomes the only source of truth.
