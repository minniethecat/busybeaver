## Overview

Emmy is [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s elderly serving-woman in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, and one of the play's most memorable comic creations. Shaw devotes an entire stage-direction paragraph to her physical description and character (001-002), giving her a vividness that rivals the major characters. She functions simultaneously as gatekeeper, domestic tyrant, comic relief, and the perpetual survivor who has seen every [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|doctor]]'s pretensions come and go. Her relationship with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is that of an old family nurse to a child, and she exploits this maternal role to secure indulgences and exercise authority far beyond her station.

## Role in the Play

Emmy's primary dramatic function is to control access to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] — she announces the succession of [[medical-profession|doctors]] who arrive to congratulate him on his knighthood, while also serving as the persistent advocate for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]], whose repeated attempts to see [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] she supports against his explicit orders. She delivers the news of the knighthood to [[redpenny|Redpenny]] (001-019) and presides over the entire Act I parade of medical visitors, from [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] to [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]. In the play's final line of Act I, she secures [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s reluctant agreement to see [[jennifer-dubedat|Mrs. Dubedat]], setting the entire plot in motion.

## Character Analysis

Shaw's stage-direction portrait of Emmy (001-002) is one of his richest: she is an "old serving-woman who has never known the cares, the preoccupations, the responsibilities, jealousies, and anxieties of personal beauty" (001-002). Her physical ugliness — "the complexion of a never-washed gypsy, incurable by any detergent" and "a whole crop of small beards and moustaches, mostly springing from moles all over her face" (001-002) — is described with elaborate comic relish. Shaw notes that she "has used her ugliness to secure indulgences unattainable by Cleopatra or Fair Rosamund, and has the further great advantage over them that age increases her qualification instead of impairing it" (001-002). She is, he concludes, "a walking sermon on the vanity of feminine prettiness" (001-002). Like [[redpenny|Redpenny]], she has only one name — "she has no discovered surname, and is known throughout the doctors' quarter between Cavendish Square and the Marylebone Road simply as Emmy" (001-002).

Emmy is the play's embodiment of practical domestic power. She treats the newly knighted [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]] as a child, asking whether he has "finished your two eggs" (001-025) and whether he has "put on your clean vest" (001-027), and calling him "my ducky diamond" (001-029). She freely expresses her scepticism about his scientific discoveries — "I always thought his great discoveries was fudge (let alone the mess of them) with his drops of blood and tubes full of Maltese fever and the like" (001-021) — and worries that his knighthood will lead him to replace her with "a footman like all the rest" (001-023). Her self-assessment is confident: "he'll never have any comfort with anybody but me to answer the door. I know who to let in and who to keep out" (001-023).

Emmy is also a moral agent in the play. She persistently advocates for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]], the "poor lady" waiting downstairs. She bribes easily — [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] gives her half-a-crown — but her advocacy goes beyond mercenary interest: "She's just the kind that puts him in a good temper" (001-023). When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] tries to dismiss [[jennifer-dubedat|Mrs. Dubedat]] unseen, Emmy's coaxing — "come now: just see her for a minute to please me: there's a good boy" (001-288) — succeeds where direct orders fail. It is Emmy, not any of the great doctors, who ensures that [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] gets her consultation.

## Relationships

Emmy's relationship with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is the play's most intimate domestic bond — she knows his habits, his diet, his wardrobe, and his moods, and treats him with a maternal condescension that he tolerates and even seems to expect. With [[redpenny|Redpenny]], she engages in bickering that reveals the parallel subordination they both occupy within the consulting-room hierarchy, though Emmy clearly outranks him in practical authority. Her exchanges with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]] are marked by mutual insult — he calls her "an ugly old devil, and no mistake" (001-283), to which she retorts "You're no beauty yourself" (001-284). With the other [[medical-profession|doctors]], she is brisk and businesslike, hurrying [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] out when his motor frightens [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s horses (001-249) and scolding [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] for keeping his horses "standing in the draught" (001-278).
