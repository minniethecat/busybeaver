## Description

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s consulting room is [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al domain of a successful Edwardian physician. It has two windows looking on Queen Anne Street. Between the windows is a marble-topped console with haunched gilt legs ending in sphinx claws. A huge pier-glass surmounts it, "mostly disabled from reflection by elaborate painting on its surface of palms, ferns, li[[truth-and-deception|lies]], tulips" (001-003). Near the console is a couch with a claw-foot, castored stretcher. The writing-table at which [[redpenny|Redpenny]] works is untidy with papers. The room is "a regular menagerie" of medical appointments, reflecting both [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s professional success and the materialism of private [[medical-profession|medical practice]].

The room's furnishings date from the mid-nineteenth century and remain unaltered: green Venetian blinds, rep curtains, a gasalier converted to electric lighting, and green wallpaper and carpets (001-003). This frozen Victorian decor is itself a dramatic statement — the consulting room is a museum of professional respectability, its modernity (the electric conversion) merely cosmetic. On the writing-table sit a microscope, test tubes, and a spirit lamp — the tools of [[scientific-medicine|scientific medicine]] half-buried in commercial paperwork (001-003).

## Dramatic Significance

The consulting room represents the institutional power and economic privilege of Edwardian private medicine. It is here that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] dispenses treatment, receives colleagues, and makes [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al judgments that will determine who lives and who dies. The room's elaborate furnishings — the marble console, the painted pier-glass, the claw-footed couch — signal wealth and status, but also the hollowness of professional display. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s observation that "all professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276) is spoken in this very room, implicating its furnishings in the moral failure they witness.

The room also serves as a stage for Shaw's [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] of medical self-presentation. [[redpenny|Redpenny]] sits amid the untidy litter of papers, writing [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s correspondence — the unglamorous labor that sustains [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al facade. [[emmy|Emmy]], the elderly serving-woman who has "never known the cares, the preoccupations, the responsibilities, jealousies, and anxieties of personal beauty" (001-002), is the room's most permanent inhabitant and its sharpest social critic.

The parade of [[medical-profession|doctors]] through the consulting room on the morning of [[the-knighthood-reception|Ridgeon's knighthood]] constitutes a [[medical-ethics|medical morality]] play in miniature. Each visitor — [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] the surgical monomaniac, [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] the fashionable placebo-prescriber, [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] the commercial cynic, [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] the impoverished idealist — represents a different pathology of the [[medical-profession|profession]], and each is judged by the room itself, whose institutional gravity exposes their various inadequacies.

## Scenes Set Here

- **Act I (001-001–001-303)**: The entire first act. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] receives colleagues on the morning of his knighthood. The parade of [[medical-profession|doctors]] — [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]], [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]], [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] — and the arrival of [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] all take place within this room. The act concludes with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s fateful invitation to dinner at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]].

## Real-World Counterpart

Queen Anne Street in Marylebone was, in Shaw's era, a fashionable address for medical consultants. The street runs between [[private-practice|Harley Street]] and Chandos Street, in the he[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] of London's private medical district. Shaw's choice of this location anchors [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s medical [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|satire]] in a recognizable geography of Edwardian professional life. The area had been associated with elite medicine since the early nineteenth century, when [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|physicians]] began moving westward from the City into the newly developed Marylebone estate. By 1906, the [[private-practice|Harley Street]] district was synonymous with the highest (and most expensive) standards of private medical care.
