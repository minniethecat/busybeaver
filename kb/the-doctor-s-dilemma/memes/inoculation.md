# Inoculation

## Definition

Inoculation is the deliberate introduction of disease material — typically a weakened or killed pathogen — into a healthy body to provoke an immune response and confer protection against future infection. In *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, inoculation serves as the historical and scientific pivot around which Shaw stages a debate about medical progress, professional hubris, and the cyclical nature of scientific "discovery."

## Context in the Play

The play opens its medical debate with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]'s deflating observation about [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s celebrated discovery: "it's fully a hundred and fifty since yours was made last. That's something to be proud of. But your discovery's not new. It's only inoculation" [001-126]. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s father, he reveals, "practised inoculation until it was made criminal in eighteen-forty," and the prohibition "broke the poor old man's he[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]" [001-126].

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] does not dispute the lineage but insists his contribution is precision: "I found out that the inoculation that ought to cure sometimes kills" [001-145]. His "[[opsonin|opsonin index]]" quantifies when it is safe to inoculate — "If the figure is one, inoculate and cure: if it's under point eight, inoculate and kill" [001-157]. The ethical stakes are explicit: "To inject a vaccine into a patient without first testing his opsonin is as near murder as a respectable practitioner can get" [001-159].

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], who has "tried these modern inoculations a bit myself" and "killed people with them; and I've cured people with them," gave up because "I never could tell which I was going to do" [001-146]. His weary skepticism stands as [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most grounded medical perspective.

[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] later conflates inoculation with [[antitoxin|antitoxins]], serums, and his own indiscriminate "[[phagocytosis|stimulate the phagocytes]]" method, declaring that he has "used all sorts of [[antitoxin|antitoxins]] absolutely indiscriminately, with perfectly satisfactory results" [001-291]. He even claims that [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s father "anticipated a discovery of my own" — the idea that "inoculation cured all fevers" by stimulating the [[phagocytosis|phagocytes]] [001-291].

## Shaw's Position

Shaw uses inoculation to dramatize a thesis about scientific knowledge: that genuinely new discoveries are rarer than professional vanity admits, and that the true advancement is often in precision and safety rather than in the discovery itself. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s contribution is real — knowing *when* to inoculate is the difference between cure and murder — but [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]'s eagerness to adopt fashionable treatments without understanding the underlying mechanism renders the advance moot. [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], who cannot tell "the difference between a vaccine and an antitoxin" [001-320], becomes [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s emblem of lethal therapeutic enthusiasm.
