# Opsonin

## Definition

In Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, opsonin is the substance that "butters" disease germs so that the body's white blood cells ([[phagocytosis|phagocytes]]) will consume them. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s specific contribution is the "opsonin index" — a quantitative measurement of the patient's blood that determines whether inoculation will cure or kill. The concept anchors [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s medical plot, embo[[death-and-mortality|dying]] the tension between genuine scientific advance and its misuse by an undiscerning profession.

## Context in the Play

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] introduces the term in Act I during a conversation with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]. When the older physician, fumbling for his spectacles, picks up [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s pamphlet and asks "Opsonin? What the devil is opsonin?" [001-148], [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] offers a memorably vivid definition: "Opsonin is what you butter the disease germs with to make your [[phagocytosis|white blood corpuscles]] eat them" [001-149].

The discovery's practical significance emerges in [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s extended explanation of the positive and negative phases. The patient's body naturally manufactures opsonin, but "by ups and downs — Nature being always rhythmical" [001-155]. The opsonin index quantifies this rhythm: "Send a drop of the patient's blood to [[ridgeons-laboratory|the laboratory]] at [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's]]; and in fifteen minutes I'll give you his opsonin index in figures. If the figure is one, [[inoculation|inoculate]] and cure: if it's under point eight, [[inoculation|inoculate]] and kill" [001-157]. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] calls this "the most important [discovery] that has been made since Harvey discovered the circulation of the blood."

The profession at large, however, cannot be trusted with the tool. [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] cheerfully admits he "didn't need any explanations" and has "handled these [[antitoxin|antitoxins]] ever since they first came out" [001-235], completely missing the distinction [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] insists upon. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s private verdict is damning: "To put a tube of serum into his hands is murder: simple murder" [001-320].

[[cutler-walpole|Walpole]], representing surgical skepticism, dismisses the entire discovery: "The opsonin is simple rot, as any capable surgeon can tell you" [001-195]. By [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s end, [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is training only "a handful of men at [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's]]" [001-369] — the discovery too precise and too dangerous for general use.

## Shaw's Position

The opsonin plotline serves Shaw's broader critique of the gap between scientific knowledge and clinical practice. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s discovery is genuine — Shaw, a committed proponent of scientific medicine, does not satirize the science itself — but [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] demonstrates that a discovery is only as valuable as [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] that app[[truth-and-deception|lies]] it. The tragedy of [[ridgeons-dilemma|the dilemma]] (which patient to save) arises precisely because [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s method is real: he can actually predict outcomes, and the scarce resource is not the treatment but the competence to administer it safely.
