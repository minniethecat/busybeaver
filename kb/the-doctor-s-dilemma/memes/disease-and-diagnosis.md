## Definition

Disease in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* is never simply a biological fact. It is always mediated by professional judgment, economic calculation, and moral evaluation. The play's central medical question — does [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s tuberculosis treatment work? — remains deliberately unresolved, shifting attention from the disease itself to the social processes through which disease is defined, diagnosed, and treated (001-209).

## Context in the Play

Tuberculosis, the disease from which [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] suffers, was in 1906 both a specific clinical entity (the tubercle bacillus had been identified by Koch in 1882) and a cultural symbol (the "romantic" disease of [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]s and poets). Shaw exploits this doubleness: [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s tuberculosis is real, but [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s interest is less in the pathology than in what the diagnosis enables — the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' claim to authority over the patient's body and fate.

The play presents multiple diagnostic frameworks in competition. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s germ-theory certainty (001-209) is challenged by [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s clinical skepticism. [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]'s surgical obsession with the "[[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]" represents diagnosis as professional branding — every surgeon sees the disease that matches his specialty. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s treatment occupies an ambiguous position between genuine innovation and professional self-promotion.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw was deeply interested in the sociology of medical knowledge. He recognized that diagnosis is never purely objective — it is shaped by professional culture, economic incentives, and the doctor's personal interests. The play's refusal to resolve the question of whether [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s treatment works is not dramatic evasion but philosophical argument: medical certainty is always p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ly constructed.

## Historical Background

The period 1880–1910 saw the transformation of diagnosis from a clinical [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] based on patient history and physical examination to a laboratory science based on bacteriology and biochemistry. The tuberculin controversy of the 1890s — Koch's premature announcement of a tuberculosis cure — had demonstrated both the promise and the peril of [[scientific-medicine|laboratory medicine]]. Shaw, who followed these developments through his friendship with Almroth Wright, was well positioned to dramatize the epistemological uncertainties of the new science.
