## Definition

Nature in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* serves as both a descriptive category and a normative standard. When [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] declares that "all professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276), he is making a claim about human nature — that self-interest is natural, and that professional ethics are an [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ificial constraint that never fully succeeds. Shaw's philosophy of creative evolution, which he would develop more fully in later works, provides the metaphysical backdrop for [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s treatment of nature (001-276).

## Context in the Play

The play's [[medical-profession|doctors]] constantly appeal to nature to justify their decisions. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] invokes the natural certainty of science (001-209); [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] appeals to the natural malevolence of the "[[blood-poisoning|nuciform sac]]"; [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] claims the natural right of the physician to judge his patients. But each appeal to nature is exposed as a rationalization of self-interest.

[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]], by contrast, claims to represent nature through [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] — the spontaneous, vital, creative force that [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|professional medicine]] suppresses. His painting of [[dubedats-death|Cardinal Death]] (004-001) is "natural" in a sense that the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' clinical reports are not: it confronts death directly rather than managing it through euphemism and professional ritual.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's philosophy of creative evolution, influenced by Lamarck and Bergson, held that life is driven by a vital force that operates through human will and creativity. Nature is not static but progressive; the purpose of human society is to channel the Life Force toward higher forms of organization. The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' failure in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] is not that they violate nature, but that they misunderstand it — they treat life as a mechanical problem to be solved rather than a creative process to be served.

## Historical Background

The Edwardian period was marked by intense debate about evolution's implications for ethics and society. Darwin's theory of natural selection had challenged traditional religious morality, and various attempts were made to derive ethical norms from evolutionary biology — most influentially by Herbert Spencer, whose "social Darwinism" Shaw rejected. Shaw's alternative, creative evolution, retained the progressive thrust of Spencer's thought while replacing its competitive mechanism with a cooperative, creative one.
