## Overview

Louis Dubedat is the most complex figure in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* — an [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] of genuine [[genius-and-talent|talent]] whose personal dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] forces [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s [[medical-profession|doctors]] into an impossible moral choice. He is charming, manipulative, visionary, and [[death-and-mortality|dying]] of [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]]. Shaw uses him to test the limits of professional ethics: can a man who [[truth-and-deception|lies]] about money and [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] still deserve to live? And who has the right to decide? "He is a slim young man of 23, physically still a stripling, and pretty, though not effeminate. He has turquoise blue eyes, and a trick of looking you straight in the face with them, which, combined with a frank smile, is very engaging" (002-002). He "is as natural as a cat: he moves among men as most men move among things" (002-002).

## Role in the Play

Dubedat is both victim and provocateur. His illness makes him dependent on the very [[medical-profession|doctors]] whose authority he mocks, and his refusal to perform gratitude disrupts [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al compact that sustains their power. Neither "[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s years nor [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s majesty have the smallest apparent effect on him" (002-002). His death — decided by a dinner-p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y tribunal rather than a medical [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] — exposes [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s central irony: the [[medical-profession|doctors]] who condemn his dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] are themselves engaged in a professional conspiracy. As [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] declares, "We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy" (001-275), and [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] extends the indictment: "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276).

## Character Analysis

Dubedat embodies Shaw's ambivalent relationship to [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic [[genius-and-talent|genius]]. He is genuinely gifted — his posthumous exhibition succeeds — and genuinely dishonest. He borrows money without repaying it, pawns [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]'s gold cigarette case, and has a bigamous past. Yet his defense against the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' accusations is not to deny the facts but to reframe them. He tells [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]], "I used to think that our [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] was all an affectation, and that I'd break loose and run away some day. But now that I'm going to be broken loose whether I like it or not, I'm perfectly fond of you" (004-001). His deathbed speech transforms purely aesthetic values — beauty, immortal flame, the burning bush — into his moral testament: "I want you to look beautiful. I want people to see in your eyes that you were married to me" (004-001). The play neither condemns nor absolves him, instead asking whether [[art-and-beauty|artistic truth]] and [[truth-and-deception|moral truth]] can ever be reconciled.

## Relationships

Dubedat's [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s emotional center. She knows he [[truth-and-deception|lies]] and cheats, yet her faith in his [[genius-and-talent|genius]] is unshakeable. He patronizes her as a matter of course, calling her "Jinny-Gwinny" (002-002), yet his [[death-and-mortality|dying]] confession reveals a deeper attachment: "I'm perfectly fond of you, and perfectly satisfied because I'm going to live as p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] of you and not as my troublesome self" (004-001). His relationship with the [[medical-profession|doctors]] is adversarial from the st[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] — he sees through their professional pretensions and refuses the patient's expected role of grateful supplicant. His brief exchange with [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] is the closest he comes to mutual respect.

## Key Scenes

- **Act II (002-002)**: First appearance at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] dinner — Louis arrives, pulls on his gloves behind [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s chair, and calls his wife "Jinny-Gwinny."
- **Act III (003-145)**: Confrontation with the [[medical-profession|doctors]] in his studio — [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] demands the return of his pawned cigarette case, and Louis eventually declares his credo: "It's always the way with the in[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic professions: when they're beaten in argument they fall back on intimidation."
- **Act IV (004-001)**: Death scene — "[[dubedats-death|Cardinal Death]], holding his scythe and hourglass like a sceptre and globe, sits on the throne." Louis makes his wife promise not to be a widow, to keep beautiful, and to burn his body: "the last of me shall be the flame in the burning bush."
- **Act V (005-001)**: Posthumous exhibition at the Bond Street gallery, where [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] calls him "Clever brute!" and [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] responds, "to you living things have no souls."

## Quotations

> (003-145) It's always the way with the inartistic professions: when they're beaten in argument they fall back on intimidation. I never knew a lawyer who didn't threaten to put me in prison sooner or later. I never knew a parson who didn't threaten me with damnation. And now you threaten me with death.

> (004-001) I want you to look beautiful. I want people to see in your eyes that you were married to me. The people in Italy used to point at Dante and say "There goes the man who has been in hell." I want them to point at you and say "There goes a woman who has been in heaven."

> (004-001) I'm sorry to disappoint the poor little worms; but the last of me shall be the flame in the burning bush. Whenever you see the flame, Jennifer, that will be me.
