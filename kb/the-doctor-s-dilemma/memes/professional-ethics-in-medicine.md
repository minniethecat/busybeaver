## Overview

Professional ethics in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* constitutes the framework within which Shaw conducts his most sustained assault on [[medical-profession|the medical profession]]'s self-image. The [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|play]] systematically documents the distance between the profession's avowed ethical standards and the actual conduct of its practitioners, revealing professional ethics to be as much a tool of self-exoneration as a guide to right action. Each of Shaw's doctors represents a different strategy for reconciling ethical obligation with personal interest [003-001].

Shaw's preface to the play — almost as long as the dramatic text itself — develops a comprehensive critique of professional ethics that treats medical morality as a subset of the broader problem of professional power. The famous epigram that opens the preface — "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" — frames the ethical question not as one of individual virtue but of structural complicity. Doctors may individually be honorable, but collectively they form a guild whose ethical codes prioritize the protection of practitioners over the protection of patients [001-003].

## In the Play

### Ridgeon's Moral Calculus

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] represents the most sophisticated and self-deceptive version of professional ethics in the [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|play]]. Presented with a genuine ethical dilemma — which of two patients should receive the limited [[opsonin|inoculation]] treatment — he persuades himself that his decision can be made on "scientific" grounds rather than personal ones. The [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|play]]'s dramatic structure exposes this rationalization: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] treats [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]], the poor but "deserving" doctor, and allows [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], the artist he envies for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s devotion, to die [002-002].

The ethical corruption is procedural rather than substantive. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] follows all the outward forms of ethical deliberation — he consults colleagues, gathers opinions, weighs clinical evidence — but these forms serve to legitimate a decision he has already made on personal grounds. Shaw's point is not that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is uniquely corrupt, but that professional ethics provides exactly the kind of procedural cover that makes such corruption invisible even to the perpetrator [005-001].

### The Spectrum of Professional Conduct

The [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|play]]'s other doctors embody a range of ethical positions. [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]] operates with cheerful moral obliviousness, removing the "nuciform sac" from every patient he encounters and describing his surgical enthusiasm as professional dedication [001-003]. [[sir-ralph-bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] dispenses his [[antitoxin]] with avuncular confidence, untroubled by the lack of evidence for its efficacy. [[sir-ralph-bloomfield-bonington|Sir Ralph]]'s ethical failure is not malice but complacency — the assumption that good intentions guarantee good outcomes [001-003].

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], the oldest and most reflective of the doctors, occupies a more ambiguous ethical position. He is aware of his profession's limitations ("you should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession" [002-210]) and yet participates in [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s conspiracy to let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die. His complicity is the most damning because he knows better [002-211].

## Analysis

Shaw's anatomy of professional ethics draws on the specific historical context of Edwardian medical reform. The General Medical Council, established by the Medical Act of 1858, had by the early 1900s consolidated professional authority over licensing, discipline, and ethical standards. Shaw saw this consolidation not as a triumph of professionalism but as the entrenchment of guild power. The preface's comparison of doctors to tradesmen — "the doctor as a private tradesman" — is an ethical argument disguised as an economic one: professional ethics, in Shaw's view, functions primarily to protect the trade [001-003].

The [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|play]]'s ethical analysis extends beyond medicine to any profession that claims moral authority over its clients. By making [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] a newly-knighted doctor — the news of his [[knight|knighthood]] arrives in Act I — Shaw explicitly connects professional ethics to social status and class hierarchy [001-019].
