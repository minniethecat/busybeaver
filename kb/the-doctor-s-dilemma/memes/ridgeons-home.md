## Description

Ridgeon's home on Queen Anne Street serves as the setting for the entirety of Act I of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. The stage direction describes it as a consulting room with "two windows looking on Queen Anne Street" [001-003], establishing the domestic-professional hybrid space that characterizes [[private-practice|private medical practice]] in Edwardian London. The consulting room is not a separate office building but part of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s residence — his professional and personal lives occupy the same address.

The room itself is both workplace and social space, furnished as a consulting room but also functioning as the reception area for [[the-knighthood-reception|the knighthood reception]] that forms the opening action. [[emmy|Emmy]], Ridgeon's domestic servant, serves as both household staff and receptionist, greeting patients and announcing the arrival of medical colleagues with the same mixture of household and professional protocol [001-002].

The consulting room contains the telephone — a marker of modern professional communication — and the famous picture titled "Jennifer" that [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] notices on her first visit [001-333].

## Dramatic Significance

The fusion of domestic and professional space at [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] is thematically significant. Shaw uses the setting to collapse the distance between the doctor's private self and his professional role, exposing how personal feeling — in particular, [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] — contaminates medical judgment.

The consulting room is also a site of professional performance. The five [[medical-profession|doctors]] who appear in Act I — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]], [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]], [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]], and [[leo-schutzmacher|Leo Schutzmacher]] — each performs his professional identity in this domestic space, turning medical consultation into social theater. The room's dual identity as home and clinic mirrors the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' dual identity as scientists and businessmen.

The setting on Queen Anne Street places Ridgeon's home within "the doctors' quarter between [[cavendish-square|Cavendish Square]] and the Marylebone Road" [001-002], anchoring the drama in the actual geography of Edwardian medical London.
