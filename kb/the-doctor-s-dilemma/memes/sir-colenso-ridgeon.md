## Overview

Sir Colenso Ridgeon is the protagonist of Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* — a physician who has recently been knighted for developing a revolutionary [[opsonin]] treatment for [[tuberculosis]]. The play opens on the day of his knighthood and traces his moral descent from professional triumph to ethical crisis. Ridgeon's [[ridgeons-dilemma|dilemma]] — which patient to treat when he can only take ten — transforms him from healer to judge, and ultimately to something closer to executioner. He stands at the intersection of [[scientific-medicine|scientific medicine]], [[private-practice|private practice]], and the [[medical-profession|medical profession]]'s darkest self-deception.

## Role in the Play

Ridgeon is both [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s sympathetic center and its moral problem. As the doctor with the [[opsonin]] cure, he initially appears as a benevolent figure — hardworking, scientifically innovative, and genuinely concerned for his patients. The opening stage direction presents him in his [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]], surrounded by the instruments of his profession.

But as [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] unfolds, his decisions become increasingly compromised by [[professional-rivalry|professional rivalry]], personal attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]], and the structural injustices of [[private-practice|private practice]]. His famous line — "We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy" (001-275) — is both a confession and an accusation. The line encapsulates the central irony of his character: he can diagnose the corruption of his profession but cannot escape it.

## Character Analysis

Ridgeon embodies the contradictions of the Edwardian [[medical-profession|medical profession]]. He is genuinely dedicated to [[scientific-medicine|science]] and genuinely proud of his achievements, yet he cannot escape the economic logic of [[private-practice|private practice]]. His [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] is a genuine medical advance — and also a scarce commodity that forces him into the role of gatekeeper.

Shaw constructs Ridgeon as a case study in how professional power corrupts. The man who begins [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] accepting congratulations for his knighthood ends it having caused the death of a patient he could have saved. The play's radical claim is that Ridgeon is not evil — he is ordinary. His corruption is the corruption of a system that gives [[medical-profession|doctors]] the power to judge worth at the same time that they claim to heal without discrimination.

His decision to let [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] die so that the "worthier" [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]] may live is presented as a rational utilitarian choice, but Shaw exposes its hidden motives: Ridgeon's attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]], his [[professional-rivalry|professional jealousy]] of [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s [[art-and-beauty|artistic]] charisma, and his [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity with [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] later observes the inescapable logic: "A blackguard's a blackguard; an honest man's an honest man" — morality is not situation-dependent (004-166).

## Relationships

Ridgeon's relationships define [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral landscape:

- **[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]**: Their mentor-protege bond is affectionate but asymmetrical. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s worldly wisdom — "more than twenty years older, not yet quite at the end of his tether, but near it" (001-072) — both guides and judges the younger man. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] names the conspiracy; Ridgeon enacts it.
- **[[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]**: Competitive professional courtesy masking fundamental disagreements about medical philosophy. [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]'s faith in "stimulants" and "the will to live" stands in comic opposition to Ridgeon's scientific pretensions (001-209).
- **[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]]**: His attraction to her compromises every subsequent medical decision. The play makes clear that Ridgeon does not simply choose [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] over [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] on utilitarian grounds — he chooses to eliminate a rival for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s devotion. Ridgeon confesses: "if I let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die, I'll marry his widow" (002-201).
- **[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]**: Doctor to patient, rival to rival — their relationship is the engine of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s tragedy. [[art-and-beauty|Dubedat's art]]istic [[genius-and-talent|genius]] threatens Ridgeon's professional authority in ways the doctor cannot acknowledge.
- **[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]**: Professional colleague whose surgical enthusiasm — declaring "Blood-poisoning" as the universal [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] (001-164, 001-200) — represents everything Ridgeon's scientific medicine claims to have transcended.

## Critical Reception

Critics have interpreted Ridgeon as one of Shaw's most complex protagonists — a figure whose moral descent is rendered with clinical precision. Some readings emphasize Ridgeon as a tragic figure trapped by the contradictions of his profession; others see him as a satirical portrait of the liberal professional whose claimed benevolence masks self-interest. The character has been compared to Ibsen's Dr. Stockmann in *An Enemy of the People*, though Ridgeon's trajectory is morally inverted: he begins where Stockmann ends, and his subsequent choices dismantle his initial moral authority. The character's name — Colenso — evokes John William Colenso, the controversial Bishop of Natal whose theological rationalism made him a figure of modernist controversy, suggesting Shaw's interest in the collision between institutional authority and individual conscience.
