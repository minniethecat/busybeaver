## Overview

Sacrifice operates as a central moral theme in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, explored through multiple competing frameworks: [[louis-dubedat|the artist]]'s sacrifice for his work, the wife's sacrifice for her husband, and the doctor's decision to sacrifice one patient for another. Shaw uses each character's understanding of sacrifice to reveal their moral character and to interrogate the very concept of selflessness.

## In the Play

### Louis Dubedat's Sacrifice

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] frames his complicity in his wife's theft as a noble sacrifice. When confronted about [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s criminal act of pawning a borrowed cigarette case, he explains:

> [003-129] "Well, *I* risked imprisonment for her sake. I could have been had up for it just as much as she. But when a man makes a sacrifice of that sort for a woman, he doesn't go and brag about it to her; at least, not if he's a gentleman." [003-129]

The irony is sharp: [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] claims [[gentleman|gentlemanly]] discretion while simultaneously deploying the sacrifice narrative to deflect moral responsibility. His "sacrifice" is in fact complicity in a crime he orchestrated.

### Jennifer's Understanding

After [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s death, [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] reflects on her husband's nature with a clarity that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] finds both admirable and frustrating:

> [005-097] "I know quite well what you mean by his selfishness. He sacrificed everything for his art. In a certain sense he had even to sacrifice everybody —" [005-097]

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] interrupts her with the crucial qualification:

> [005-098] "Everybody except himself. By keeping that back he lost the right to sacrifice you, and gave me the right to sacrifice him. Which I did." [005-098]

This exchange crystallizes [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral calculus. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] claims the right to sacrifice [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] because [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] never truly sacrificed anything of his own. [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s response — "He was one of the men who know what women know: that self-sacrifice is vain and cowardly" [005-099] — offers a genuinely alternative moral framework in which sacrifice is not a virtue but a form of vanity.

### Ridgeon's Medical Sacrifice

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision to let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die so that [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] might live is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s titular dilemma rendered as sacrifice. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] sacrifices one patient — the morally dubious [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] — to save another — the honest but impoverished doctor. His self-justification rests on a utilitarian calculus that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] both presents and undermines.

## Thematic Significance

Shaw uses sacrifice to expose the gap between stated and actual moral reasoning. Each character who invokes sacrifice — [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s [[gentleman|gentlemanly]] forbearance, [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s medical triage, [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic devotion — does so in service of a pre-existing commitment. Sacrifice in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] is never purely altruistic; it is always entangled with self-interest, vanity, and the need to justify one's choices.
