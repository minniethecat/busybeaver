## Overview

Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington, universally referred to as "B.B.," is a fashionable and supremely self-confident physician in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Shaw uses B.B. as a vehicle for satirizing the medical culture in which reputation and bedside manner substitute for genuine clinical judgment.

## Character Profile

B.B. is described in the stage directions as possessing "majesty" and an imposing presence (002-002). He has adopted [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s medical terminology — particularly regarding opsonin and phagocytosis — but has thoroughly misunderstood its meaning.

His most notable characteristic is his unshakeable confidence in his own medical theories, most famously his belief that "there is at bottom only one genuinely scientific treatment for all diseases, and that is to stimulate the phagocytes" (001-240).

## Role in the Play

### Act I: Medical Consultation

B.B. first appears when [[emmy|Emmy]] announces him at [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] (001-003). He participates in the dinner celebrating Ridgeon's knighthood, where he confidently expounds his theory of universal antitoxin treatment.

### Act II: The Dubedat Case

B.B. is among the doctors who examine [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]. His diagnostic approach is characteristically bold and imprecise, reflecting his tendency toward fashionable medical theories rather than rigorous science.

### Acts III–V: Dubedat's Treatment

When B.B. agrees to take over Dubedat's treatment from Ridgeon, he casually requests "a tube of some really stiff antitoxin. Any kind will do" (003-239). This moment crystallizes Shaw's critique: a dying man's treatment is entrusted to a doctor who believes any antitoxin works for any disease.

## Shaw's Satirical Purpose

Shaw uses B.B. to critique a medical culture in which [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]] and [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|bedside manner]] substitute for clinical judgment. B.B.'s comic obliviousness — he never realizes that his confident pronouncements are dangerously wrong — makes him one of Shaw's most effective satirical creations.

B.B. represents the triumph of style over substance in [[medical-profession|medical practice]]. His patients adore him not because he cures them but because he looks and sounds like a great physician. The fact that he accidentally cross-inoculated two patients (typhoid serum for tetanus and vice versa) and both recovered only reinforces his belief in his own genius (001-248).

## Relationships

- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]**: B.B. admires Ridgeon and has adopted his terminology, but Ridgeon is privately horrified by B.B.'s misunderstandings, calling his use of antitoxin "murder: simple murder" (001-277).
- **[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]]**: B.B. treats Sir Patrick with deference due to his seniority, though Sir Patrick views him with amused skepticism.
- **[[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]]**: Fellow fashionable physician; they move in the same social-professional circles.
- **[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]**: B.B. becomes Dubedat's treating physician after Ridgeon withdraws, administered with his characteristic confident imprecision.

## Quotations

> [001-240] "Nature has provided, in the white corpuscles as you call them — in the phagocytes as we call them — a natural means of devouring and destroying all disease germs. There is at bottom only one genuinely scientific treatment for all diseases, and that is to stimulate the phagocytes."

> [003-239] "On Tuesday morning send me down a tube of some really stiff antitoxin. Any kind will do. Don't forget. Goodbye, Colly."
