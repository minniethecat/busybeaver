## Overview

The Newspaper Man is a minor character in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* who appears only in Act IV, arriving at [[dubedats-studio|Dubedat's studio]] to interview the dying [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]. Shaw uses him as a vehicle for satirizing the press — he is described as "disabled for ordinary business pursuits by a congenital erroneousness which renders him incapable of describing accurately anything he sees, or understanding or reporting accurately anything he hears" (004-038). His cheerful incompetence and ghoulish interest in [[dubedats-death|Dubedat's death]] provide dark comic relief during the play's most somber scene.

## Role in the Play

The Newspaper Man serves two functions. Dramatically, his presence gives [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] an audience for his [[dubedats-death|deathbed]] performance — [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] specifically asks for the journalist and insists on being wheeled into the studio to receive him. Thematically, the Newspaper Man represents the public appetite for sensationalism that will shape [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s posthumous reputation. His inability to understand or accurately report anything he witnesses mirrors the gap between private truth and public myth that defines the play's final act in the [[bond-street-gallery|Bond Street Gallery]].

## Character Analysis

Shaw's stage-direction portrait of the Newspaper Man is one of his most merciless satirical sketches. He is "disabled for ordinary business pursuits by a congenital erroneousness which renders him incapable of describing accurately anything he sees" (004-038). His only possible employment is journalism because "a newspaper, not having to act on its description and reports, but only to sell them to idly curious people, has nothing but honor to lose by inaccuracy and unveracity" (004-038). He struggles with his notebook because "he cannot write shorthand, and does not write with ease in any hand" (004-038), and he cannot spell basic medical terms — confusing "tubercle" with "cubical" (004-045).

Despite his cheerful, affable manner, the Newspaper Man's behavior reveals complete indifference to [[dubedats-death|Dubedat]]'s suffering and the grief of his wife. He is openly excited by the prospect of witnessing a death: "I say: I am in luck today" (004-053). He wants to photograph [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] holding a lancet (004-053), and he asks [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] whether [[jennifer-dubedat|Mrs. Dubedat]] would give him "a few words on How It Feels to be a Widow" (004-157), calling it "Rather a good title for an article" (004-157). After witnessing [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s moving death and his creed ("I believe in Michelangelo, Velasquez, and Rembrandt"), the Newspaper Man can only observe that "it showed a very nice feeling, his being so particular about his wife going into proper mourning for him and making her promise never to marry again" (004-152) — a complete misrepresentation of everything [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] actually said.

## Relationships

The Newspaper Man's primary interactions are with [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]], who treats him with open contempt — snatching his notebook to write the names correctly, threatening to throw him out if he talks to the patient, and refusing to be photographed. With [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]], his encounter ends with him being edged out of the door. His interaction with the dying [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] is limited to a glib offer of service (004-063) and his final presence at the deathbed (004-134). He never speaks directly to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]].

- **Act IV (004-045)-(004-046)**: Confuses "tubercle" with "cubical."
- **Act IV (004-049)-(004-050)**: Mishears "Ridgeon" as "Pigeon"; [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] snatches his notebook.
- **Act IV (004-053)**: Produces a camera, says "I am in luck today," wants to photograph [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]] with a lancet.
- **Act IV (004-057)**: Sits on the piano stool to await [[louis-dubedat|Louis]].
- **Act IV (004-063)**: Glib introduction to the dying [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]: "I represent the press."
- **Act IV (004-134)**: Excited response when [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] asks if he is present.
- **Act IV (004-152)**: Misinterprets [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s instructions about [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] not wearing black.
- **Act IV (004-157)**: Asks for "a few words on How It Feels to be a Widow."
- **Act IV (004-159)-(004-162)**: Edged out by [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]].
