## Description

The Star and Garter Hotel at Richmond, on the Thames west of London, is the setting for the entirety of Act II of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. The stage direction establishes the scene as a moonlit summer evening after dinner on the hotel terrace:

> (002-001) After dinner on the terrace at the Star and Garter, Richmond. Cloudless summer night; nothing disturbs the stillness except from time to time the long trajectory of a distant train and the measured clucking of oars coming up from the Thames in the valley below. The dinner is over; and three of the eight chairs are empty. Sir Patrick, with his back to the view, is at the head of the square table with Ridgeon. The two chairs opposite them are empty. On their right come, first, a vacant chair, and then one very fully occupied by B.B., who basks blissfully in the moonbeams. On their left, Schutzmacher and Walpole. The entrance to the hotel is on their right, behind B.B. The five men are silently enjoying their coffee and cigarettes, full of food, and not altogether void of wine.

The scene is suffused with a beauty that provides ironic counterpoint to the moral ugliness of the medical [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] that follows. [[jennifer-dubedat]]'s entrance — described in the stage direction as "wrapped up for dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ure" (002-002) — and her reaction to the evening immediately establishes the sensory richness of the setting against which the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' moral failures will be measured. The hotel terrace is a liminal space: between indoors and outdoors, between the city and the countryside, between professional duty and social pleasure.

## Dramatic Significance

The Star and Garter setting performs three critical dramatic functions:

**First**, it displaces the medical drama from the clinical to the social. The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' discussion of who shall live and who shall die takes place not in a surgery or a hospital ward but over after-dinner coffee — medicine as a social ritual conducted among professional peers.

**Second**, the beauty of the setting — the moonlit terrace, the summer air, the river below — creates [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most sustained passage of ironic counterpoint. The natural world operates by different laws than the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' professional conspiracy.

**Third**, the hotel's removal from central London — Richmond was a country destination, accessible by train but perceptibly outside the city — mirrors the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' removal from the consequences of their decisions. They can decide [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s fate over coffee because the hotel insulates them from the reality of what they are doing. The setting is a spatial metaphor for professional distance.

## Scenes Set Here

- **Act II (002-001–002-220)**: The entire second act. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] dine, the [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]s arrive, [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s case is discussed among the [[medical-profession|doctors]] in his absence, and [[ridgeons-dilemma|Ridgeon's dilemma]] crystallizes. [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s pivotal speeches — both the "high and great profession" speech (002-210) and the "conspiracy" speech — are delivered here, their moral force sharpened by the incongruously pleasant setting.

## Real-World Counterpart

The Star and Garter Hotel in Richmond was a real establishment, a grand hotel on Richmond Hill built in the eighteenth century and substantially expanded in the Victorian period. It was a popular destination for London professionals — a country outing within easy reach of the city, accessible by train from Waterloo. The dining room was celebrated, and the terrace overlooking the Thames was one of the most famous views in England.

The original hotel was demolished in the early twentieth century and replaced by the current Star and Garter building (now a care home operated by the Royal Star and Garter charity for disabled ex-service personnel). Shaw's choice of this location situates [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s medical elite within a recognizable geography of Edwardian leisure — the hotel was exactly the kind of place where a newly knighted physician like [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] would celebrate his elevation.
