## Overview

Selfishness and altruism are intertwined in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* to the point of indistinguishability. Every ostensibly altruistic act reveals a selfish motive; every selfish confession contains a seed of genuine generosity. [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] — "Not that I'm selfish" [004-105] — protests against a charge that the play simultaneously confirms and complicates. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision to save [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] is presented as altruistic sacrifice but functions as self-interested elimination of a rival for [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s affection.

Shaw's treatment of this theme is characteristically unsentimental. He does not celebrate altruism nor condemn selfishness but examines both as they actually operate — in the messy, compromised, self-deceiving medium of human motivation. The play's insight is that selfishness and altruism are not opposites but adjacent positions on a spectrum that no one navigates cleanly.

## In the Play

### Ridgeon's Mixed Motives

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most sustained study in mixed motivation. He genuinely wants to help his patients — his work at [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's]] is unpaid charity — but he also genuinely enjoys his knighthood, his income, and his professional [[honor-and-reputation|reputation]]. When he decides to let [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] die so that [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] can live, the decision is simultaneously: (1) a utilitarian calculation about the greater good, (2) an expression of professional solidarity with a fellow doctor, (3) the elimination of a romantic rival, and (4) the gratification of professional jealousy.

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] sees the tangled motives clearly. His "high and great profession" speech [002-210] is an appeal to pure altruism — "its business is to heal and mend men and women" — but it is immediately followed by the conspiracy to let Louis die. The moment captures the play's essential insight: altruism is invoked precisely when it is about to be betrayed.

### Louis Dubedat's Selfish Generosity

[[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] is the play's most frankly selfish character, and also, paradoxically, one of its most generous. He borrows money without repaying it, charms women without committing to them, and treats the world as a resource for his [[art-and-beauty|art]]. "Not that I'm selfish," he protests, "With all my faults I don't think I'm that" [004-105]. The protest is both true and false — he is selfish in ways he does not perceive, but he is also capable of genuine warmth, genuine gratitude, genuine delight in others.

His selfishness has a quality of innocence. He does not calculate; he simply assumes that the world will accommodate his needs because it always has. His "sensitivenesses, even little selfishnesses that are too trivial for" general observation [003-001] are the selfishnesses of a man who has never had to consider others because others have always considered him. This is not malice but privilege — and Shaw shows it to be as damaging as malice.

### Jennifer's Self-Sacrifice

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] embodies a form of altruism that the play neither celebrates nor condemns but examines with clinical precision. Her devotion to Louis is total — she protects him, provides for him, believes in him — but it is also, in a sense, self-serving. Her identity is defined by her devotion; without Louis to serve, who would she be? Her line "When you want money, you will always come to me" [003-001] is both generous and controlling: the generosity is real, but so is the power relationship it establishes.

The play's treatment of Jennifer's altruism is one of its subtlest achievements. Shaw does not reduce her to a victim or a saint but shows her as a woman whose self-sacrifice is both genuinely admirable and structurally problematic — the product of a social order that defines women through their service to men.

### Dr. Blenkinsop's Silent Altruism

[[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most consistently altruistic character — a doctor who has impoverished himself serving working-class patients who cannot pay. His altruism is undemonstrative, almost invisible within the play's dramatic economy. He does not speak of sacrifice; he simply lives it. And his reward is to become a charity case himself, dependent on the very system that his life's work has implicitly criticized.

The irony is structural: altruism, in the play's moral economy, is punished rather than rewarded. [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] serves the poor and becomes poor; [[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] sells quack cures and becomes wealthy. The play does not resolve this contradiction but insists that we confront it.

## Shaw's Argument

Shaw's Fabian socialism led him to be skeptical of individual altruism as a solution to structural problems. Kindness, generosity, and self-sacrifice are real virtues, but they cannot substitute for just institutions. A system that depends on individual altruism to provide healthcare is a system that will fail the poor. The solution is not more generous doctors but public healthcare — structural altruism rather than personal.

The play's final image — [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] refusing to forgive [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] — suggests that genuine altruism may require something harder than generosity: the willingness to judge, to hold accountable, to refuse to let comfortable resolutions substitute for genuine justice.
