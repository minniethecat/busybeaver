## Summary

Dubedat's marriage proposal to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is a backstory event that establishes the foundation of the Dubedats' relationship — a union that proves to be as morally complex as everything else about [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]. The proposal is referenced indirectly in Act II during the [[star-and-garter-consultation|Star and Garter consultation]], when [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] confesses his motive for letting [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] die: "I did it because I wanted to marry his widow" [002-200].

[[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s own account of their relationship, delivered in Act III in [[dubedats-studio|his studio]], reveals the terms on which he proposed: he expected [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] to believe in his [[genius-and-talent|genius]], to support him financially and emotionally, and to accept that "his artist's power of appealing to the imagination" [002-002] entitled him to disregard conventional morality. The marriage that results from this proposal is, from the beginning, an arrangement built on [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s terms.

## The Bigamy Revelation

The legal status of the marriage is called into question in Act III when [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] casually acknowledges that his marriage to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is bigamous. He had a previous wife who was still living at the time of the marriage: "Of course it's bigamy; but still he's very young" [003-117]. His dismissal of the "criminal law on the subject of bigamy" [003-117] as irrelevant to the "fundamental truth" of his relationship with [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] is characteristic — [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] treats all institutions, legal and medical alike, as obstacles to be charmed or brushed aside.

This revelation complicates the moral calculus of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]. If the marriage was legally void from the beginning, then [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] was never legally "married" to [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]. Her devotion to him, which is the emotional core of her character, rests on a relationship that the law does not recognize. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] proposes marriage while already married — a deception that mirrors his other deceptions: borrowing money under false pretenses, lying to the doctors about his resources.

## Thematic Significance

The marriage proposal and its bigamous foundation exemplify the play's concern with the gap between legal forms and human realities. [[louis-dubedat|Louis]]'s proposal creates a relationship that is real in emotional terms but void in legal ones. The [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|medical profession]] operates with a similar gap — real suffering treated through a system whose legal and economic structures corrupt the physician-patient relationship. [[the-registry-office|The Registry Office]], where the marriage was registered (or should have been), stands as an institutional marker of the boundary between the legally valid and the genuinely real.

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s response to the bigamy disclosure — or rather, her silence on the matter — is equally significant. Her devotion to [[louis-dubedat|Louis]] does not depend on legal forms. She does not question the validity of their marriage; she simply continues to love him. This commitment is both admirable and troubling, like so much in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] — a devotion that transcends legal forms but also enables exploitation.
