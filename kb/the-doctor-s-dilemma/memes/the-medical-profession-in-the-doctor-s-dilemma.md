## Definition

Shaw's portrayal of the medical profession in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* is one of the most sustained satirical treatments of medicine in English drama. The play assembles a representative cross-section of Edwardian practitioners — the scientific innovator ([[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]), the surgical monomaniac ([[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]), the fashionable placebo-prescriber ([[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]), the cynical general practitioner ([[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]), the impoverished idealist ([[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]), and the retired elder ([[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]) — and through them anatomizes a profession that Shaw regarded as fundamentally corrupt. The play's most famous line — "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276) — distills a structural critique: that professional expertise is a form of monopoly power, and that [[medical-profession|doctors]] use their authority to extract wealth from the sick rather than to heal them.

## Context in the Play

Shaw develops his medical critique through the interactions and conflicts among the six [[medical-profession|doctors]] who assemble in [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] in Act I. Each doctor represents a different corruption of the medical ideal, and their discussions — ranging from professional rivalries over treatment methods to the collective decision about [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s fate at the [[star-and-garter-hotel]] — expose the gap between medicine's pretensions to scientific objectivity and its actual operation as a commercial guild. The profession functions in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] not as a collection of individual healers but as a system whose structure determines outcomes: the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity, professional courtesy, and economic interests consistently override their duty to patients.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw was deeply engaged with medical reform debates of his era. His friendship with the bacteriologist Almroth Wright (a model for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]) gave him insider knowledge of medical research culture, and his Fabian socialism informed his critique of [[private-practice]] as incompatible with genuine public health. The play's [[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]], published with [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] text, functions as a political polemic arguing for the nationalization of medicine.

Shaw's position in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] is not anti-science but anti-professional-monopoly. He acknowledges [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s genuine scientific innovation but argues that the economic structure of private practice corrupts even the well-intentioned practitioner. The play also responds to the Ibsenite tradition of problem drama: where Ibsen's *An Enemy of the People* (1882) made a doctor the heroic truth-teller against a corrupt community, Shaw inverts the formula — the doctor is the corruption, and truth comes from [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] and the outsider ([[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s Irish perspective).

## Historical Background

Shaw wrote *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* in 1906, at the height of Edwardian medical reform debates. The British Medical Association was consolidating professional power; the General Medical Council was standardizing medical education. The play's Preface engages directly with these debates, citing contemporary medical controversies and naming specific practitioners of the period.

The play draws on Shaw's personal knowledge of the medical world. His close association with Almroth Wright, the pioneering immunologist and bacteriologist at St. Mary's Hospital, Paddington, provided the scientific basis for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]]. The play also engages with broader Victorian and Edwardian anxieties about the growing power of professional elites — anxieties that Shaw, as a Fabian socialist, channeled into a structural critique rather than individual blame.

## The Six Doctors

Each doctor in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* represents a distinct corruption of the medical ideal:

- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon]]**: The tragedy of the well-intentioned professional. His [[opsonin]] treatment is a genuine medical advance, but the scarcity it creates transforms him from healer to gatekeeper. His moral failure — choosing [[dr-blenkinsop]] over [[louis-dubedat]] — is the inevitable consequence of a system that makes [[medical-profession|doctors]] arbiters of worth.
- **[[sir-patrick-cullen]]**: The conscience who cannot act. He knows [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]] is corrupt but is too old and too comfortable to change it. His wisdom is genuine but sterile — it diagnoses without curing. He delivers [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most famous line: "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276).
- **[[cutler-walpole]]**: The reduction of medicine to technique. His surgical monomania — diagnosing every ailment as "[[blood-poisoning]]" (001-199, 001-200) and insisting "the knife is the real remedy" — represents [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]'s tendency to substitute procedure for judgment.
- **[[bloomfield-bonington]]**: The reduction of medicine to fashion. His faith in "stimulants" and indiscriminate application of treatments he does not understand (001-188–001-196) is epistemologically empty but socially effective — it pleases patients and generates fees.
- **[[leo-schutzmacher]]**: The reduction of medicine to commerce. His "Cure Guaranteed" (001-054) is cynicism dressed as confidence; he has abandoned even the pretense of scientific medicine.
- **[[dr-blenkinsop]]**: The exception that proves the rule. A genuinely dedicated physician serving the poor for nothing — but the system rewards him with [[poverty-and-class|poverty]] and [[tuberculosis]]. His virtue is [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]'s indictment.

## The Conspiracy Thesis

The play's central metaphor — profession as conspiracy — is developed through [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s speeches but enacted by every doctor on stage:

> (001-275) We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy.

> (001-276) All professions are conspiracies against the laity.

The "conspiracy" operates through exclusion (only [[medical-profession|doctors]] can judge [[medical-profession|doctors]]), mystification (medical jargon conceals commercial motives), and solidarity (professionals protect each other at the patient's expense). The [[ridgeons-dilemma|dilemma]] is the conspiracy made visible: a group of [[medical-profession|doctors]], over dinner at the [[star-and-garter-hotel]], decide which patient deserves to live. The formality of the medical [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] conceals a death sentence.
