## Act II

[002-001] After dinner on the terrace at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]], Richmond. Cloudless summer night; nothing disturbs the stillness except from time to time the long trajectory of a distant train and the measured clucking of oars coming up from the Thames in the valley below. The dinner is over; and three of the eight chairs are empty. **[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]**, with his back to the view, is at the head of the square table with **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]**. The two chairs opposite them are empty. On their right come, first, a vacant chair, and then one very fully occupied by **[[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]]**, who basks blissfully in the moonbeams. On their left, **[[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]** and **[[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]**. The entrance to the hotel is on their right, behind **B.B.** The five men are silently enjoying their coffee and cigarettes, full of food, and not altogether void of wine.

[002-002] **Mrs. [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]**, wrapped up for departure, comes in. They rise, except **Sir Patrick**; but she takes one of the vacant places at the foot of the table, next **B.B.**; and they sit down again.

| [002-003] Mrs. Dubedat | *As she enters.* Louis will be here presently. He is showing Dr. Blenkinsop how to work the telephone. *She sits.* Oh, I am so sorry we have to go. It seems such a shame, this beautiful night. And we have enjoyed ourselves so much. |

| [002-004] Ridgeon | I don’t believe another half-hour would do Mr. Dubedat a bit of harm. |

| [002-005] Sir Patrick | Come now, Colly, come! come! none of that. You take your man home, Mrs. Dubedat; and get him to bed before eleven. |

| [002-006] B.B. | Yes, yes. Bed before eleven. Quite right, quite right. Sorry to lose you, my dear lady; but Sir Patrick’s orders are the laws of—er—of Tyre and Sidon. |

| [002-007] Walpole | Let me take you home in my motor. |

| [002-008] Sir Patrick | No. You ought to be ashamed of yourself, Walpole. Your motor will take Mr. and Mrs. Dubedat to the station, and quite far enough too for an open carriage at night. |

| [002-009] Mrs. Dubedat | Oh, I am sure the train is best. |

| [002-010] Ridgeon | Well, Mrs. Dubedat, we have had a most enjoyable evening. |

| [002-011] Walpole | Most enjoyable. |

| [002-012] B.B. | Delightful. Charming. Unforgettable. |

| [002-013] Mrs. Dubedat | *With a touch of shy anxiety.* What did you think of Louis? Or am I wrong to ask? |

| [002-014] Ridgeon | Wrong! Why, we are all charmed with him. |

| [002-015] Walpole | Delighted. |

| [002-016] B.B. | Most happy to have met him. A privilege, a real privilege. |

| [002-017] Sir Patrick | *Grunts.* ! |

| [002-018] Mrs. Dubedat | *Quickly.* Sir Patrick: are *you* uneasy about him? |

| [002-019] Sir Patrick | *Discreetly.* I admire his drawings greatly, ma’am. |

| [002-020] Mrs. Dubedat | Yes; but I meant— |

| [002-021] Ridgeon | You shall go away quite happy. He’s worth saving. He must and shall be saved. |

| [002-022]  | ***Mrs. Dubedat** rises and gasps with delight, relief, and gratitude. They all rise except **Sir Patrick** and **Schutzmacher**, and come reassuringly to her.* |

| [002-023] B.B. | Certainly, *cer*-tainly. |

| [002-024] Walpole | There’s no real difficulty, if only you know what to do. |

| [002-025] Mrs. Dubedat | Oh, how can I ever thank you! From this night I can begin to be happy at last. You don’t know what I feel. |

| [002-026]  | *She sits down in tears. They crowd about her to console her.* |

| [002-027] B.B. | My dear lady: come come! come come! *Very persuasively.* come come! |

| [002-028] Walpole | Don’t mind us. Have a good cry. |

| [002-029] Ridgeon | No: don’t cry. Your husband had better not know that we’ve been talking about him. |

| [002-030] Mrs. Dubedat | *Quickly pulling herself together.* No, of course not. Please don’t mind me. What a glorious thing it must be to be a doctor! *They laugh.* Don’t laugh. You don’t know what you’ve done for me. I never knew until now how deadly afraid I was—how I had come to dread the worst. I never dared let myself know. But now the relief has come: now I know. |

| [002-031]  | ***Louis Dubedat** comes from the hotel, in his overcoat, his throat wrapped in a shawl. He is a slim young man of 23, physically still a stripling, and pretty, though not effeminate. He has turquoise blue eyes, and a trick of looking you straight in the face with them, which, combined with a frank smile, is very engaging. Although he is all nerves, and very observant and quick of apprehension, he is not in the least shy. He is younger than Jennifer; but he patronizes her as a matter of course. The doctors do not put him out in the least: neither **Sir Patrick’s** years nor **Bloomfield Bonington’s** majesty have the smallest apparent effect on him: he is as natural as a cat: he moves among men as most men move among things, though he is intentionally making himself agreeable to them on this occasion. Like all people who can be depended on to take care of themselves, he is welcome company; and his artist’s power of appealing to the imagination gains him credit for all sorts of qualities and powers, whether he possesses them or not.* |

| [002-032] Louis | *Pulling on his gloves behind **Ridgeon’s** chair.* Now, Jinny-Gwinny: the motor has come round. |

| [002-033] Ridgeon | Why do you let him spoil your beautiful name like that, Mrs. Dubedat? |

| [002-034] Mrs. Dubedat | Oh, on grand occasions I am Jennifer. |

| [002-035] B.B. | You are a bachelor: you do not understand these things, Ridgeon. Look at me *They look.* I also have two names. In moments of domestic worry, I am simple Ralph. When the sun shines in the home, I am Beedle-Deedle-Dumkins. Such is married life! Mr. Dubedat: may I ask you to do me a favor before you go. Will you sign your name to this menu card, under the sketch you have made of me? |

| [002-036] Walpole | Yes; and mine too, if you will be so good. |

| [002-037] Louis | Certainly. *He sits down and signs the cards.* |

| [002-038] Mrs. Dubedat | Won’t you sign Dr. Schutzmacher’s for him, Louis? |

| [002-039] Louis | I don’t think Dr. Schutzmacher is pleased with his portrait. I’ll tear it up. *He reaches across the table for **Schutzmacher’s** menu card, and is about to tear it. **Schutzmacher** makes no sign.* |

| [002-040] Ridgeon | No, no: if Loony doesn’t want it, I do. |

| [002-041] Louis | I’ll sign it for you with pleasure. *He signs and hands it to **Ridgeon**.* I’ve just been making a little note of the river tonight: it will work up into something good. *He shows a pocket sketchbook.* I think I’ll call it the Silver Danube. |

| [002-042] B.B. | Ah, charming, charming. |

| [002-043] Walpole | Very sweet. You’re a nailer at pastel. |

| [002-044]  | ***Louis** coughs, first out of modesty, then from tuberculosis.* |

| [002-045] Sir Patrick | Now then, Mr. Dubedat: you’ve had enough of the night air. Take him home, ma’am. |

| [002-046] Mrs. Dubedat | Yes. Come, Louis. |

| [002-047] Ridgeon | Never fear. Never mind. I’ll make that cough all right. |

| [002-048] B.B. | We will stimulate the phagocytes. *With tender effusion, shaking her hand.* Good night, Mrs. Dubedat. Good night. Good night. |

| [002-049] Walpole | If the phagocytes fail, come to me. I’ll put you right. |

| [002-050] Louis | Good night, Sir Patrick. Happy to have met you. |

| [002-051] Sir Patrick | ’Night. *Half a grunt.* |

| [002-052] Mrs. Dubedat | Good night, Sir Patrick. |

| [002-053] Sir Patrick | Cover yourself well up. Don’t think your lungs are made of iron because they’re better than his. Good night. |

| [002-054] Mrs. Dubedat | Thank you. Thank you. Nothing hurts me. Good night. |

| [002-055]  | ***Louis** goes out through the hotel without noticing **Schutzmacher**. **Mrs. Dubedat** hesitates, then bows to him. **Schutzmacher** rises and bows formally, German fashion. She goes out, attended by **Ridgeon**. The rest resume their seats, ruminating or smoking quietly.* |

| [002-056] B.B. | *Harmoniously.* Dee-lightful couple! Charming woman! Gifted lad! Remarkable talent! Graceful outlines! Perfect evening! Great success! Interesting case! Glorious night! Exquisite scenery! Capital dinner! Stimulating conversation! Restful outing! Good wine! Happy ending! Touching gratitude! Lucky Ridgeon— |

| [002-057] Ridgeon | *Returning.* What’s that? Calling me, B.B.? *He goes back to his seat next **Sir Patrick**.* |

| [002-058] B.B. | No, no. Only congratulating you on a most successful evening! Enchanting woman! Thorough breeding! Gentle nature! Refined— |

| [002-059]  | ***Blenkinsop** comes from the hotel and takes the empty chair next **Ridgeon**.* |

| [002-060] Blenkinsop | I’m so sorry to have left you like this, Ridgeon; but it was a telephone message from the police. They’ve found half a milkman at our level crossing with a prescription of mine in its pocket. Where’s Mr. Dubedat? |

| [002-061] Ridgeon | Gone. |

| [002-062] Blenkinsop | *Rising, very pale.* Gone! |

| [002-063] Ridgeon | Just this moment— |

| [002-064] Blenkinsop | Perhaps I could overtake him—*he rushes into the hotel.* |

| [002-065] Walpole | *Calling after him.* He’s in the motor, man, miles off. You can—*giving it up.* No use. |

| [002-066] Ridgeon | They’re really very nice people. I confess I was afraid the husband would turn out an appalling bounder. But he’s almost as charming in his way as she is in hers. And there’s no mistake about his being a genius. It’s something to have got a case really worth saving. Somebody else will have to go; but at all events it will be easy to find a worse man. |

| [002-067] Sir Patrick | How do you know? |

| [002-068] Ridgeon | Come now, Sir Paddy, no growling. Have something more to drink. |

| [002-069] Sir Patrick | No, thank you. |

| [002-070] Walpole | Do you see anything wrong with Dubedat, B.B.? |

| [002-071] B.B. | Oh, a charming young fellow. Besides, after all, what could be wrong with him? Look at him. What could be wrong with him? |

| [002-072] Sir Patrick | There are two things that can be wrong with any man. One of them is a cheque. The other is a woman. Until you know that a man’s sound on these two points, you know nothing about him. |

| [002-073] B.B. | Ah, cynic, cynic! |

| [002-074] Walpole | He’s all right as to the cheque, for a while at all events. He talked to me quite frankly before dinner as to the pressure of money difficulties on an artist. He says he has no vices and is very economical, but that there’s one extravagance he can’t afford and yet can’t resist; and that is dressing his wife prettily. So I said, bang plump out, “Let me lend you twenty pounds, and pay me when your ship comes home.” He was really very nice about it. He took it like a man; and it was a pleasure to see how happy it made him, poor chap. |

| [002-075] B.B. | *Who has listened to **Walpole** with growing perturbation.* But—but—but—when was this, may I ask? |

| [002-076] Walpole | When I joined you that time down by the river. |

| [002-077] B.B. | But, my dear Walpole, he had just borrowed ten pounds from me. |

| [002-078] Walpole | What! |

| [002-079] Sir Patrick | *Grunts.* ! |

| [002-080] B.B. | *Indulgently.* Well, well, it was really hardly borrowing; for he said heaven only knew when he could pay me. I couldn’t refuse. It appears that Mrs. Dubedat has taken a sort of fancy to me— |

| [002-081] Walpole | *Quickly.* No: it was to me. |

| [002-082] B.B. | Certainly not. Your name was never mentioned between us. He is so wrapped up in his work that he has to leave her a good deal alone; and the poor innocent young fellow—he has of course no idea of my position or how busy I am—actually wanted me to call occasionally and talk to her. |

| [002-083] Walpole | Exactly what he said to me! |

| [002-084] B.B. | Pooh! Pooh pooh! Really, I must say. *Much disturbed, he rises and goes up to the balustrade, contemplating the landscape vexedly.* |

| [002-085] Walpole | Look here, Ridgeon! this is beginning to look serious. |

| [002-086]  | ***Blenkinsop**, very anxious and wretched, but trying to look unconcerned, comes back.* |

| [002-087] Ridgeon | Well, did you catch him? |

| [002-088] Blenkinsop | No. Excuse my running away like that. *He sits down at the foot of the table, next **Bloomfeld Bonington’s** chair.* |

| [002-089] Walpole | Anything the matter? |

| [002-090] Blenkinsop | Oh no. A trifle—something ridiculous. It can’t be helped. Never mind. |

| [002-091] Ridgeon | Was it anything about Dubedat? |

| [002-092] Blenkinsop | *Almost breaking down.* I ought to keep it to myself, I know. I can’t tell you, Ridgeon, how ashamed I am of dragging my miserable poverty to your dinner after all your kindness. It’s not that you won’t ask me again; but it’s so humiliating. And I did so look forward to one evening in my dress clothes (*They’re* still presentable, you see) with all my troubles left behind, just like old times. |

| [002-093] Ridgeon | But what has happened? |

| [002-094] Blenkinsop | Oh, nothing. It’s too ridiculous. I had just scraped up four shillings for this little outing; and it cost me one-and-fourpence to get here. Well, Dubedat asked me to lend him half-a-crown to tip the chambermaid of the room his wife left her wraps in, and for the cloakroom. He said he only wanted it for five minutes, as she had his purse. So of course I lent it to him. And he’s forgotten to pay me. I’ve just tuppence to get back with. |

| [002-095] Ridgeon | Oh, never mind that— |

| [002-096] Blenkinsop | *Stopping him resolutely.* No: I know what you’re going to say; but I won’t take it. I’ve never borrowed a penny; and I never will. I’ve nothing left but my friends; and I won’t sell them. If none of you were to be able to meet me without being afraid that my civility was leading up to the loan of five shillings, there would be an end of everything for me. I’ll take your old clothes, Colly, sooner than disgrace you by talking to you in the street in my own; but I won’t borrow money. I’ll train it as far as the twopence will take me; and I’ll tramp the rest. |

| [002-097] Walpole | You’ll do the whole distance in my motor. *They are all greatly relieved; and **Walpole** hastens to get away from the painful subject by adding.* Did he get anything out of you, Mr. Schutzmacher? |

| [002-098] Schutzmacher | *Shakes his head in a most expressive negative.* |

| [002-099] Walpole | You didn’t appreciate his drawing, I think. |

| [002-100] Schutzmacher | Oh yes I did. I should have liked very much to have kept the sketch and got it autographed. |

| [002-101] B.B. | But why didn’t you? |

| [002-102] Schutzmacher | Well, the fact is, when I joined Dubedat after his conversation with Mr. Walpole, he said the Jews were the only people who knew anything about art, and that though he had to put up with your Philistine twaddle, as he called it, it was what I said about the drawings that really pleased him. He also said that his wife was greatly struck with my knowledge, and that she always admired Jews. Then he asked me to advance him fifty pounds on the security of the drawings. |

| [002-103]  | *All exclaiming together:* |

| [002-104] B.B. | No, no. Positively! Seriously! |

| [002-105] Walpole | What! Another fifty! |

| [002-106] Blenkinsop | Think of that! |

| [002-107] Sir Patrick | *Grunts.* ! |

| [002-108] Schutzmacher | Of course I couldn’t lend money to a stranger like that. |

| [002-109] B.B. | I envy you the power to say No, Mr. Schutzmacher. Of course, I knew I oughtn’t to lend money to a young fellow in that way; but I simply hadn’t the nerve to refuse. I couldn’t very well, you know, could I? |

| [002-110] Schutzmacher | I don’t understand that. I felt that I couldn’t very well lend it. |

| [002-111] Walpole | What did he say? |

| [002-112] Schutzmacher | Well, he made a very uncalled-for remark about a Jew not understanding the feelings of a gentleman. I must say you Gentiles are very hard to please. You say we are no gentlemen when we lend money; and when we refuse to lend it you say just the same. I didn’t mean to behave badly. As I told him, I might have lent it to him if he had been a Jew himself. |

| [002-113] Sir Patrick | *With a grunt.* And what did he say to that? |

| [002-114] Schutzmacher | Oh, he began trying to persuade me that he was one of the chosen people—that his artistic faculty showed it, and that his name was as foreign as my own. He said he didn’t really want fifty pounds; that he was only joking; that all he wanted was a couple of sovereigns. |

| [002-115] B.B. | No, no, Mr. Schutzmacher. You invented that last touch. Seriously, now? |

| [002-116] Schutzmacher | No. You can’t improve on Nature in telling stories about gentlemen like Mr. Dubedat. |

| [002-117] Blenkinsop | You certainly do stand by one another, you chosen people, Mr. Schutzmacher. |

| [002-118] Schutzmacher | Not at all. Personally, I like Englishmen better than Jews, and always associate with them. That’s only natural, because, as I am a Jew, there’s nothing interesting in a Jew to me, whereas there is always something interesting and foreign in an Englishman. But in money matters it’s quite different. You see, when an Englishman borrows, all he knows or cares is that he wants money; and he’ll sign anything to get it, without in the least understanding it, or intending to carry out the agreement if it turns out badly for him. In fact, he thinks you a cad if you ask him to carry it out under such circumstances. Just like the *Merchant of Venice*, you know. But if a Jew makes an agreement, he means to keep it and expects you to keep it. If he wants money for a time, he borrows it and knows he must pay it at the end of the time. If he knows he can’t pay, he begs it as a gift. |

| [002-119] Ridgeon | Come, Loony! do you mean to say that Jews are never rogues and thieves? |

| [002-120] Schutzmacher | Oh, not at all. But I was not talking of criminals. I was comparing honest Englishmen with honest Jews. |

| [002-121]  | *One of the hotel maids, a pretty, fair-haired woman of about 25, comes from the hotel, rather furtively. She accosts **Ridgeon**.* |

| [002-122] The Maid | I beg your pardon, sir— |

| [002-123] Ridgeon | Eh? |

| [002-124] The Maid | I beg pardon, sir. It’s not about the hotel. I’m not allowed to be on the terrace; and I should be discharged if I were seen speaking to you, unless you were kind enough to say you called me to ask whether the motor has come back from the station yet. |

| [002-125] Walpole | Has it? |

| [002-126] The Maid | Yes, sir. |

| [002-127] Ridgeon | Well, what do you want? |

| [002-128] The Maid | Would you mind, sir, giving me the address of the gentleman that was with you at dinner? |

| [002-129] Ridgeon | *Sharply.* Yes, of course I should mind very much. You have no right to ask. |

| [002-130] The Maid | Yes, sir, I know it looks like that. But what am I to do? |

| [002-131] Sir Patrick | What’s the matter with you? |

| [002-132] The Maid | Nothing, sir. I want the address: that’s all. |

| [002-133] B.B. | You mean the young gentleman? |

| [002-134] The Maid | Yes, sir: that went to catch the train with the woman he brought with him. |

| [002-135] Ridgeon | The woman! Do you mean the lady who dined here? the gentleman’s wife? |

| [002-136] The Maid | Don’t believe them, sir. She can’t be his wife. I’m his wife. |

| [002-137]  | *In amazed remonstrance:* |

| [002-138] B.B. | My good girl! |

| [002-139] Ridgeon | You his wife! |

| [002-140] Walpole | What! what’s that? Oh, this is getting perfectly fascinating, Ridgeon. |

| [002-141] The Maid | I could run upstairs and get you my marriage lines in a minute, sir, if you doubt my word. He’s Mr. Louis Dubedat, isn’t he? |

| [002-142] Ridgeon | Yes. |

| [002-143] The Maid | Well, sir, you may believe me or not; but I’m the lawful Mrs. Dubedat. |

| [002-144] Sir Patrick | And why aren’t you living with your husband? |

| [002-145] The Maid | We couldn’t afford it, sir. I had thirty pounds saved; and we spent it all on our honeymoon in three weeks, and a lot more that he borrowed. Then I had to go back into service, and he went to London to get work at his drawing; and he never wrote me a line or sent me an address. I never saw nor heard of him again until I caught sight of him from the window going off in the motor with that woman. |

| [002-146] Sir Patrick | Well, that’s two wives to start with. |

| [002-147] B.B. | Now upon my soul I don’t want to be uncharitable; but really I’m beginning to suspect that our young friend is rather careless. |

| [002-148] Sir Patrick | Beginning to think! How long will it take you, man, to find out that he’s a damned young blackguard? |

| [002-149] Blenkinsop | Oh, that’s severe, Sir Patrick, very severe. Of course it’s bigamy; but still he’s very young; and she’s very pretty. Mr. Walpole: may I sponge on you for another of those nice cigarettes of yours? *He changes his seat for the one next **Walpole**.* |

| [002-150] Walpole | Certainly. *He feels in his pockets.* Oh bother! Where—? *Suddenly remembering.* I say: I recollect now: I passed my cigarette case to Dubedat and he didn’t return it. It was a gold one. |

| [002-151] The Maid | He didn’t mean any harm: he never thinks about things like that, sir. I’ll get it back for you, sir, if you’ll tell me where to find him. |

| [002-152] Ridgeon | What am I to do? Shall I give her the address or not? |

| [002-153] Sir Patrick | Give her your own address; and then we’ll see. *To the **maid**.* You’ll have to be content with that for the present, my girl. ***Ridgeon** gives her his card.* What’s your name? |

| [002-154] The Maid | Minnie Tinwell, sir. |

| [002-155] Sir Patrick | Well, you write him a letter to care of this gentleman; and it will be sent on. Now be off with you. |

| [002-156] The Maid | Thank you, sir. I’m sure you wouldn’t see me wronged. Thank you all, gentlemen; and excuse the liberty. |

| [002-157]  | *She goes into the hotel. They watch her in silence.* |

| [002-158] Ridgeon | *When she is gone.* Do you realize, chaps, that we have promised Mrs. Dubedat to save this fellow’s life? |

| [002-159] Blenkinsop | What’s the matter with him? |

| [002-160] Ridgeon | Tuberculosis. |

| [002-161] Blenkinsop | *Interested.* And can you cure that? |

| [002-162] Ridgeon | I believe so. |

| [002-163] Blenkinsop | Then I wish you’d cure me. My right lung is touched, I’m sorry to say. |

| [002-164]  | *All together:* |

| [002-165] Ridgeon | What! Your lung is going? |

| [002-166] B.B. | My dear Blenkinsop, what do you tell me? *Full of concern for **Blenkinsop** he comes back from the balustrade.* |

| [002-167] Sir Patrick | Eh? Eh? What’s that? |

| [002-168] Walpole | Hullo, you mustn’t neglect this, you know. |

| [002-169] Blenkinsop | *Putting his fingers in his ears.* No, no: it’s no use. I know what you’re going to say: I’ve said it often to others. I can’t afford to take care of myself; and there’s an end of it. If a fortnight’s holiday would save my life, I’d have to die. I shall get on as others have to get on. We can’t all go to St. Moritz or to Egypt, you know, Sir Ralph. Don’t talk about it. |

| [002-170]  | *Embarrassed silence.* |

| [002-171] Sir Patrick | *Grunts and looks hard at **Ridgeon**.* ! |

| [002-172] Schutzmacher | *Looking at his watch and rising.* I must go. It’s been a very pleasant evening, Colly. You might let me have my portrait if you don’t mind. I’ll send Mr. Dubedat that couple of sovereigns for it. |

| [002-173] Ridgeon | *Giving him the menu card.* Oh don’t do that, Loony. I don’t think he’d like that. |

| [002-174] Schutzmacher | Well, of course I shan’t if you feel that way about it. But I don’t think you understand Dubedat. However, perhaps that’s because I’m a Jew. Good night, Dr. Blenkinsop *shaking hands.* |

| [002-175] Blenkinsop | Good night, sir—I mean—Good night. |

| [002-176] Schutzmacher | *Waving his hand to the rest.* Goodnight, everybody. |

| [002-177] Walpole, B.B., Sir Patrick, Ridgeon | Good night. |

| [002-178]  | **B.B.** repeats the salutation several times, in varied musical tones. **Schutzmacher** goes out. |

| [002-179] Sir Patrick | It’s time for us all to move. *He rises and comes between **Blenkinsop** and **Walpole**. **Ridgeon** also rises.* Mr. Walpole: take Blenkinsop home: he’s had enough of the open air cure for tonight. Have you a thick overcoat to wear in the motor, Dr. Blenkinsop? |

| [002-180] Blenkinsop | Oh, they’ll give me some brown paper in the hotel; and a few thicknesses of brown paper across the chest are better than any fur coat. |

| [002-181] Walpole | Well, come along. Good night, Colly. You’re coming with us, aren’t you, B.B.? |

| [002-182] B.B. | Yes: I’m coming. ***Walpole** and **Blenkinsop** go into the hotel.* Good night, my dear Ridgeon *shaking hands affectionately.* Don’t let us lose sight of your interesting patient and his very charming wife. We must not judge him too hastily, you know. *With unction.* Gooooooood-night, Paddy. Bless you, dear old chap. ***Sir Patrick** utters a formidable grunt. B.B. laughs and pats him indulgently on the shoulder.* Good night. Good night. Good night. Good night. *He good nights himself into the hotel.* |

| [002-183]  | *The others have meanwhile gone without ceremony. **Ridgeon** and **Sir Patrick** are left alone together. **Ridgeon**, deep in thought, comes down to **Sir Patrick**.* |

| [002-184] Sir Patrick | Well, Mr. Savior of Lives: which is it to be? that honest decent man Blenkinsop, or that rotten blackguard of an artist, eh? |

| [002-185] Ridgeon | It’s not an easy case to judge, is it? Blenkinsop’s an honest decent man; but is he any use? Dubedat’s a rotten blackguard; but he’s a genuine source of pretty and pleasant and good things. |

| [002-186] Sir Patrick | What will he be a source of for that poor innocent wife of his, when she finds him out? |

| [002-187] Ridgeon | That’s true. Her life will be a hell. |

| [002-188] Sir Patrick | And tell me this. Suppose you had this choice put before you: either to go through life and find all the pictures bad but all the men and women good, or to go through life and find all the pictures good and all the men and women rotten. Which would you choose? |

| [002-189] Ridgeon | That’s a devilishly difficult question, Paddy. The pictures are so agreeable, and the good people so infernally disagreeable and mischievous, that I really can’t undertake to say offhand which I should prefer to do without. |

| [002-190] Sir Patrick | Come come! none of your cleverness with me: I’m too old for it. Blenkinsop isn’t that sort of good man; and you know it. |

| [002-191] Ridgeon | It would be simpler if Blenkinsop could paint Dubedat’s pictures. |

| [002-192] Sir Patrick | It would be simpler still if Dubedat had some of Blenkinsop’s honesty. The world isn’t going to be made simple for you, my lad: you must take it as it is. You’ve to hold the scales between Blenkinsop and Dubedat. Hold them fairly. |

| [002-193] Ridgeon | Well, I’ll be as fair as I can. I’ll put into one scale all the pounds Dubedat has borrowed, and into the other all the half-crowns that Blenkinsop hasn’t borrowed. |

| [002-194] Sir Patrick | And you’ll take out of Dubedat’s scale all the faith he has destroyed and the honor he has lost, and you’ll put into Blenkinsop’s scale all the faith he has justified and the honor he has created. |

| [002-195] Ridgeon | Come come, Paddy! none of your claptrap with me: I’m too sceptical for it. I’m not at all convinced that the world wouldn’t be a better world if everybody behaved as Dubedat does than it is now that everybody behaves as Blenkinsop does. |

| [002-196] Sir Patrick | Then why don’t you behave as Dubedat does? |

| [002-197] Ridgeon | Ah, that beats me. That’s the experimental test. Still, it’s a dilemma. It’s a dilemma. You see there’s a complication we haven’t mentioned. |

| [002-198] Sir Patrick | What’s that? |

| [002-199] Ridgeon | Well, if I let Blenkinsop die, at least nobody can say I did it because I wanted to marry his widow. |

| [002-200] Sir Patrick | Eh? What’s that? |

| [002-201] Ridgeon | Now if I let Dubedat die, I’ll marry his widow. |

| [002-202] Sir Patrick | Perhaps she won’t have you, you know. |

| [002-203] Ridgeon | *With a self-assured shake of the head.* I’ve a pretty good flair for that sort of thing. I know when a woman is interested in me. She is. |

| [002-204] Sir Patrick | Well, sometimes a man knows best; and sometimes he knows worst. You’d much better cure them both. |

| [002-205] Ridgeon | I can’t. I’m at my limit. I can squeeze in one more case, but not two. I must choose. |

| [002-206] Sir Patrick | Well, you must choose as if she didn’t exist: that’s clear. |

| [002-207] Ridgeon | Is that clear to you? Mind: it’s not clear to me. She troubles my judgment. |

| [002-208] Sir Patrick | To me, it’s a plain choice between a man and a lot of pictures. |

| [002-209] Ridgeon | It’s easier to replace a dead man than a good picture. |

| [002-210] Sir Patrick | Colly: when you live in an age that runs to pictures and statues and plays and brass bands because its men and women are not good enough to comfort its poor aching soul, you should thank Providence that you belong to a profession which is a high and great profession because its business is to heal and mend men and women. |

| [002-211] Ridgeon | In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I’m to kill my patient. |

| [002-212] Sir Patrick | Don’t talk wicked nonsense. You can’t kill him. But you can leave him in other hands. |

| [002-213] Ridgeon | In B.B.’s, for instance: eh? *Looking at him significantly.* |

| [002-214] Sir Patrick | *Demurely facing his look.* Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington is a very eminent physician. |

| [002-215] Ridgeon | He is. |

| [002-216] Sir Patrick | I’m going for my hat. |

| [002-217]  | ***Ridgeon** strikes the bell as **Sir Patrick** makes for the hotel. A **waiter** comes.* |

| [002-218] Ridgeon | *To the **waiter**.* My bill, please. |

| [002-219] Waiter | Yes, sir. |

| [002-220]  | *He goes for it.* |
