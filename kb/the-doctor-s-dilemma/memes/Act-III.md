## Act III

[003-001] In **[[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]’s** studio. Viewed from the large window the outer door is in the wall on the left at the near end. The door leading to the inner rooms is in the opposite wall, at the far end. The facing wall has neither window nor door. The plaster on all the walls is uncovered and undecorated, except by scrawlings of charcoal sketches and memoranda. There is a studio throne (a chair on a dais) a little to the left, opposite the inner door, and an easel to the right, opposite the outer door, with a dilapidated chair at it. Near the easel and against the wall is a bare wooden table with bottles and jars of oil and medium, paint-smudged rags, tubes of color, brushes, charcoal, a small lay figure, a kettle and spirit-lamp, and other odds and ends. By the table is a sofa, littered with drawing blocks, sketchbooks, loose sheets of paper, newspapers, books, and more smudged rags. Next the outer door is an umbrella and hat stand, occupied partly by Louis’ hats and cloak and muffler, and partly by odds and ends of costumes. There is an old piano stool on the near side of this door. In the corner near the inner door is a little tea-table. A lay figure, in a cardinal’s robe and hat, with an hourglass in one hand and a scythe slung on its back, smiles with inane malice at **Louis**, who, in a milkman’s smock much smudged with colors, is painting a piece of brocade which he has draped about his wife. She is sitting on the throne, not interested in the painting, and appealing to him very anxiously about another matter.

| [003-002] Mrs. Dubedat | Promise. |

| [003-003] Louis | *Putting on a touch of paint with notable skill and care and answering quite perfunctorily.* I promise, my darling. |

| [003-004] Mrs. Dubedat | When you want money, you will always come to me. |

| [003-005] Louis | But it’s so sordid, dearest. I hate money. I can’t keep always bothering you for money, money, money. That’s what drives me sometimes to ask other people, though I hate doing it. |

| [003-006] Mrs. Dubedat | It is far better to ask me, dear. It gives people a wrong idea of you. |

| [003-007] Louis | But I want to spare your little fortune, and raise money on my own work. Don’t be unhappy, love: I can easily earn enough to pay it all back. I shall have a one-man-show next season; and then there will be no more money troubles. *Putting down his palette.* There! I mustn’t do any more on that until it’s bone-dry; so you may come down. |

| [003-008] Mrs. Dubedat | *Throwing off the drapery as she steps down, and revealing a plain frock of tussore silk.* But you have promised, remember, seriously and faithfully, never to borrow again until you have first asked me. |

| [003-009] Louis | Seriously and faithfully. *Embracing her.* Ah, my love, how right you are! how much it means to me to have you by me to guard me against living too much in the skies. On my solemn oath, from this moment forth I will never borrow another penny. |

| [003-010] Mrs. Dubedat | *Delighted.* Ah, that’s right. Does his wicked worrying wife torment him and drag him down from the clouds? *She kisses him.* And now, dear, won’t you finish those drawings for Maclean? |

| [003-011] Louis | Oh, they don’t matter. I’ve got nearly all the money from him in advance. |

| [003-012] Mrs. Dubedat | But, dearest, that is just the reason why you should finish them. He asked me the other day whether you really intended to finish them. |

| [003-013] Louis | Confound his impudence! What the devil does he take me for? Now that just destroys all my interest in the beastly job. I’ve a good mind to throw up the commission, and pay him back his money. |

| [003-014] Mrs. Dubedat | We can’t afford that, dear. You had better finish the drawings and have done with them. I think it is a mistake to accept money in advance. |

| [003-015] Louis | But how are we to live? |

| [003-016] Mrs. Dubedat | Well, Louis, it is getting hard enough as it is, now that they are all refusing to pay except on delivery. |

| [003-017] Louis | Damn those fellows! they think of nothing and care for nothing but their wretched money. |

| [003-018] Mrs. Dubedat | Still, if they pay us, they ought to have what they pay for. |

| [003-019] Louis | *Coaxing.* There now: that’s enough lecturing for today. I’ve promised to be good, haven’t I? |

| [003-020] Mrs. Dudebat | *Putting her arms round his neck.* You know that I hate lecturing, and that I don’t for a moment misunderstand you, dear, don’t you? |

| [003-021] Louis | *Fondly.* I know. I know. I’m a wretch; and you’re an angel. Oh, if only I were strong enough to work steadily, I’d make my darling’s house a temple, and her shrine a chapel more beautiful than was ever imagined. I can’t pass the shops without wrestling with the temptation to go in and order all the really good things they have for you. |

| [003-022] Mrs. Dubedat | I want nothing but you, dear. *She gives him a caress, to which he responds so passionately that she disengages herself.* There! be good now: remember that the doctors are coming this morning. Isn’t it extraordinarily kind of them, Louis, to insist on coming? all of them, to consult about you? |

| [003-023] Louis | *Coolly.* Oh, I daresay they think it will be a feather in their cap to cure a rising artist. They wouldn’t come if it didn’t amuse them, anyhow. *Someone knocks at the door.* I say: it’s not time yet, is it? |

| [003-024] Mrs. Dudebat | No, not quite yet. |

| [003-025] Louis | *Opening the door and finding **Ridgeon** there.* Hello, Ridgeon. Delighted to see you. Come in. |

| [003-026] Mrs. Dudebat | *Shaking hands.* It’s so good of you to come, doctor. |

| [003-027] Louis | Excuse this place, won’t you? It’s only a studio, you know: there’s no real convenience for living here. But we pig along somehow, thanks to Jennifer. |

| [003-028] Mrs. Dubedat | Now I’ll run away. Perhaps later on, when you’re finished with Louis, I may come in and hear the verdict. ***Ridgeon** bows rather constrainedly.* Would you rather I didn’t? |

| [003-029] Ridgeon | Not at all. Not at all. |

| [003-030]  | ***Mrs. Dubedat** looks at him, a little puzzled by his formal manner; then goes into the inner room.* |

| [003-031] Louis | *Flippantly.* I say: don’t look so grave. There’s nothing awful going to happen, is there? |

| [003-032] Ridgeon | No. |

| [003-033] Louis | That’s all right. Poor Jennifer has been looking forward to your visit more than you can imagine. She’s taken quite a fancy to you, Ridgeon. The poor girl has nobody to talk to: I’m always painting. *Taking up a sketch.* There’s a little sketch I made of her yesterday. |

| [003-034] Ridgeon | She showed it to me a fortnight ago when she first called on me. |

| [003-035] Louis | *Quite unabashed.* Oh! did she? Good Lord! how time does fly! I could have sworn I’d only just finished it. It’s hard for her here, seeing me piling up drawings and nothing coming in for them. Of course I shall sell them next year fast enough, after my one-man-show; but while the grass grows the steed starves. I hate to have her coming to me for money, and having none to give her. But what can I do? |

| [003-036] Ridgeon | I understood that Mrs. Dubedat had some property of her own. |

| [003-037] Louis | Oh yes, a little; but how could a man with any decency of feeling touch that? Suppose I did, what would she have to live on if I died? I’m not insured: can’t afford the premiums. *Picking out another drawing.* How do you like that? |

| [003-038] Ridgeon | *Putting it aside.* I have not come here today to look at your drawings. I have more serious and pressing business with you. |

| [003-039] Louis | You want to sound my wretched lung. *With impulsive candor.* My dear Ridgeon: I’ll be frank with you. What’s the matter in this house isn’t lungs but bills. It doesn’t matter about me; but Jennifer has actually to economize in the matter of food. You’ve made us feel that we can treat you as a friend. Will you lend us a hundred and fifty pounds? |

| [003-040] Ridgeon | No. |

| [003-041] Louis | *Surprised.* Why not? |

| [003-042] Ridgeon | I am not a rich man; and I want every penny I can spare and more for my researches. |

| [003-043] Louis | You mean you’d want the money back again. |

| [003-044] Ridgeon | I presume people sometimes have that in view when they lend money. |

| [003-045] Louis | *After a moment’s reflection.* Well, I can manage that for you. I’ll give you a cheque﻿—or see here: there’s no reason why you shouldn’t have your bit too: I’ll give you a cheque for two hundred. |

| [003-046] Ridgeon | Why not cash the cheque at once without troubling me? |

| [003-047] Louis | Bless you! they wouldn’t cash it: I’m overdrawn as it is. No: the way to work it is this. I’ll postdate the cheque next October. In October Jennifer’s dividends come in. Well, you present the cheque. It will be returned marked “refer to drawer” or some rubbish of that sort. Then you can take it to Jennifer, and hint that if the cheque isn’t taken up at once I shall be put in prison. She’ll pay you like a shot. You’ll clear fifty pounds; and you’ll do me a real service; for I do want the money very badly, old chap, I assure you. |

| [003-048] Ridgeon | *Staring at him.* You see no objection to the transaction; and you anticipate none from me! |

| [003-049] Louis | Well, what objection can there be? It’s quite safe. I can convince you about the dividends. |

| [003-050] Ridgeon | I mean on the score of its being﻿—shall I say dishonorable? |

| [003-051] Louis | Well, of course I shouldn’t suggest it if I didn’t want the money. |

| [003-052] Ridgeon | Indeed! Well, you will have to find some other means of getting it. |

| [003-053] Louis | Do you mean that you refuse? |

| [003-054] Ridgeon | Do I mean﻿—! *Letting his indignation loose.* Of course I refuse, man. What do you take me for? How dare you make such a proposal to me? |

| [003-055] Louis | Why not? |

| [003-056] Ridgeon | Faugh! You would not understand me if I tried to explain. Now, once for all, I will not lend you a farthing. I should be glad to help your wife; but lending you money is no service to her. |

| [003-057] Louis | Oh well, if you’re in earnest about helping her, I’ll tell you what you might do. You might get your patients to buy some of my things, or to give me a few portrait commissions. |

| [003-058] Ridgeon | My patients call me in as a physician, not as a commercial traveller. |

| [003-059]  | *A knock at the door. **Louis** goes unconcernedly to open it, pursuing the subject as he goes.* |

| [003-060] Louis | But you must have great influence with them. You must know such lots of things about them﻿—private things that they wouldn’t like to have known. They wouldn’t dare to refuse you. |

| [003-061] Ridgeon | *Exploding.* Well, upon my﻿— |

| [003-062]  | ***Louis** opens the door, and admits **Sir Patrick**, **Sir Ralph**, and **Walpole**.* |

| [003-063] Ridgeon | *Proceeding furiously.* Walpole: I’ve been here hardly ten minutes; and already he’s tried to borrow £150 from me. Then he proposed that I should get the money for him by blackmailing his wife; and you’ve just interrupted him in the act of suggesting that I should blackmail my patients into sitting to him for their portraits. |

| [003-064] Louis | Well, Ridgeon, if this is what you call being an honorable man! I spoke to you in confidence. |

| [003-065] Sir Patrick | We’re all going to speak to you in confidence, young man. |

| [003-066] Walpole | *Hanging his hat on the only peg left vacant on the hatstand.* We shall make ourselves at home for half an hour, Dubedat. Don’t be alarmed: you’re a most fascinating chap; and we love you. |

| [003-067] Louis | Oh, all right, all right. Sit down﻿—anywhere you can. Take this chair, Sir Patrick *indicating the one on the throne.* Up‑z‑z‑z! *Helping him up: **Sir Patrick** grunts and enthrones himself.* Here you are, B.B. ***Sir Ralph** glares at the familiarity; but Louis, quite undisturbed, puts a big book and a sofa cushion on the dais, on Sir Patrick’s right; and B.B. sits down, under protest.* Let me take your hat. *He takes **B.B.’s** hat unceremoniously, and substitutes it for the cardinal’s hat on the head of the lay figure, thereby ingeniously destroying the dignity of the conclave. He then draws the piano stool from the wall and offers it to **Walpole**.* You don’t mind this, Walpole, do you? ***Walpole** accepts the stool, and puts his hand into his pocket for his cigarette case. Missing it, he is reminded of his loss.* |

| [003-068] Walpole | By the way, I’ll trouble you for my cigarette case, if you don’t mind? |

| [003-069] Louis | What cigarette case? |

| [003-070] Walpole | The gold one I lent you at the Star and Garter. |

| [003-071] Louis | *Surprised.* Was that yours? |

| [003-072] Walpole | Yes. |

| [003-073] Louis | I’m awfully sorry, old chap. I wondered whose it was. I’m sorry to say this is all that’s left of it. *He hitches up his smock; produces a card from his waistcoat pocket; and hands it to **Walpole**.* |

| [003-074] Walpole | A pawn ticket! |

| [003-075] Louis | *Reassuringly.* It’s quite safe: he can’t sell it for a year, you know. I say, my dear Walpole, I am sorry. *He places his hand ingenuously on **Walpole’s** shoulder and looks frankly at him.* |

| [003-076] Walpole | *Sinking on the stool with a gasp.* Don’t mention it. It adds to your fascination. |

| [003-077] Ridgeon | *Who has been standing near the easel.* Before we go any further, you have a debt to pay, Mr. Dubedat. |

| [003-078] Louis | I have a precious lot of debts to pay, Ridgeon. I’ll fetch you a chair. *He makes for the inner door.* |

| [003-079] Ridgeon | *Stopping him.* You shall not leave the room until you pay it. It’s a small one; and pay it you must and shall. I don’t so much mind your borrowing ten pounds from one of my guests and twenty pounds from the other﻿— |

| [003-080] Walpole | I walked into it, you know. I offered it. |

| [003-081] Ridgeon | —they could afford it. But to clean poor Blenkinsop out of his last half-crown was damnable. I intend to give him that half-crown and to be in a position to pledge him my word that you paid it. I’ll have that out of you, at all events. |

| [003-082] B.B. | Quite right, Ridgeon. Quite right. Come, young man! down with the dust. Pay up. |

| [003-083] Louis | Oh, you needn’t make such a fuss about it. Of course I’ll pay it. I had no idea the poor fellow was hard up. I’m as shocked as any of you about it. *Putting his hand into his pocket.* Here you are. *Finding his pocket empty.* Oh, I say, I haven’t any money on me just at present. Walpole: would you mind lending me half-a-crown just to settle this. |

| [003-084] Walpole | Lend you half﻿—*his voice faints away.* |

| [003-085] Louis | Well, if you don’t, Blenkinsop won’t get it; for I haven’t a rap: you may search my pockets if you like. |

| [003-086] Walpole | That’s conclusive. *He produces half-a-crown.* |

| [003-087] Louis | *Passing it to **Ridgeon**.* There! I’m really glad that’s settled: it was the only thing that was on my conscience. Now I hope you’re all satisfied. |

| [003-088] Sir Patrick | Not quite, Mr. Dubedat. Do you happen to know a young woman named Minnie Tinwell? |

| [003-089] Louis | Minnie! I should think I do; and Minnie knows me too. She’s a really nice good girl, considering her station. What’s become of her? |

| [003-090] Walpole | It’s no use bluffing, Dubedat. We’ve seen Minnie’s marriage lines. |

| [003-091] Louis | *Coolly.* Indeed? Have you seen Jennifer’s? |

| [003-092] Ridgeon | *Rising in irrepressible rage.* Do you dare insinuate that Mrs. Dubedat is living with you without being married to you? |

| [003-093] Louis | Why not? |

| [003-094]  | *Echoing him in various tones of scandalized amazement:* |

| [003-095] B.B. | Why not! |

| [003-096] Sir Patrick | Why not! |

| [003-097] Ridgeon | Why not! |

| [003-098] Walpole | Why not! |

| [003-099] Louis | Yes, why not? Lots of people do it: just as good people as you. Why don’t you learn to think, instead of bleating and bashing like a lot of sheep when you come up against anything you’re not accustomed to? *Contemplating their amazed faces with a chuckle.* I say: I should like to draw the lot of you now: you do look jolly foolish. Especially you, Ridgeon. I had you that time, you know. |

| [003-100] Ridgeon | How, pray? |

| [003-101] Louis | Well, you set up to appreciate Jennifer, you know. And you despise me, don’t you? |

| [003-102] Ridgeon | *Curtly.* I loathe you. *He sits down again on the sofa.* |

| [003-103] Louis | Just so. And yet you believe that Jennifer is a bad lot because you think I told you so. |

| [003-104] Ridgeon | Were you lying? |

| [003-105] Louis | No; but you were smelling out a scandal instead of keeping your mind clean and wholesome. I can just play with people like you. I only asked you had you seen Jennifer’s marriage lines; and you concluded straight away that she hadn’t got any. You don’t know a lady when you see one. |

| [003-106] B.B. | *Majestically.* What do you mean by that, may I ask? |

| [003-107] Louis | Now, I’m only an immoral artist; but if *you’d* told me that Jennifer wasn’t married, I’d have had the gentlemanly feeling and artistic instinct to say that she carried her marriage certificate in her face and in her character. But you are all moral men; and Jennifer is only an artist’s wife﻿—probably a model; and morality consists in suspecting other people of not being legally married. Aren’t you ashamed of yourselves? Can one of you look me in the face after it? |

| [003-108] Walpole | It’s very hard to look you in the face, Dubedat; you have such a dazzling cheek. What about Minnie Tinwell, eh? |

| [003-109] Louis | Minnie Tinwell is a young woman who has had three weeks of glorious happiness in her poor little life, which is more than most girls in her position get, I can tell you. Ask her whether she’d take it back if she could. She’s got her name into history, that girl. My little sketches of her will be bought by collectors at Christie’s. She’ll have a page in my biography. Pretty good, that, for a still-room maid at a seaside hotel, I think. What have you fellows done for her to compare with that? |

| [003-110] Ridgeon | We haven’t trapped her into a mock marriage and deserted her. |

| [003-111] Louis | No: you wouldn’t have the pluck. But don’t fuss yourselves. I didn’t desert little Minnie. We spent all our money﻿— |

| [003-112] Walpole | All *her* money. Thirty pounds. |

| [003-113] Louis | I said all our money: hers and mine too. Her thirty pounds didn’t last three days. I had to borrow four times as much to spend on her. But I didn’t grudge it; and she didn’t grudge her few pounds either, the brave little lassie. When we were cleaned out, we’d had enough of it: you can hardly suppose that we were fit company for longer than that: I an artist, and she quite out of art and literature and refined living and everything else. There was no desertion, no misunderstanding, no police court or divorce court sensation for you moral chaps to lick your lips over at breakfast. We just said, Well, the money’s gone: we’ve had a good time that can never be taken from us; so kiss; part good friends; and she back to service, and I back to my studio and my Jennifer, both the better and happier for our holiday. |

| [003-114] Walpole | Quite a little poem, by George! |

| [003-115] B.B. | If you had been scientifically trained, Mr. Dubedat, you would know how very seldom an actual case bears out a principle. In medical practice a man may die when, scientifically speaking, he ought to have lived. I have actually known a man die of a disease from which he was, scientifically speaking, immune. But that does not affect the fundamental truth of science. In just the same way, in moral cases, a man’s behavior may be quite harmless and even beneficial, when he is morally behaving like a scoundrel. And he may do great harm when he is morally acting on the highest principles. But that does not affect the fundamental truth of morality. |

| [003-116] Sir Patrick | And it doesn’t affect the criminal law on the subject of bigamy. |

| [003-117] Louis | Oh bigamy! bigamy! bigamy! What a fascination anything connected with the police has for you all, you moralists! I’ve proved to you that you were utterly wrong on the moral point: now I’m going to show you that you’re utterly wrong on the legal point; and I hope it will be a lesson to you not to be so jolly cocksure next time. |

| [003-118] Walpole | Rot! You were married already when you married her; and that settles it. |

| [003-119] Louis | Does it! Why can’t you think? How do you know she wasn’t married already too? |

| [003-120]  | *All crying out together:* |

| [003-121] B.B. | Walpole! Ridgeon! |

| [003-122] Ridgeon | This is beyond everything! |

| [003-123] Walpole | Well, damn me! |

| [003-124] Sir Patrick | You young rascal. |

| [003-125] Louis | *Ignoring their outcry.* She was married to the steward of a liner. He cleared out and left her; and she thought, poor girl, that it was the law that if you hadn’t heard of your husband for three years you might marry again. So as she was a thoroughly respectable girl and refused to have anything to say to me unless we were married I went through the ceremony to please her and to preserve her self-respect. |

| [003-126] Ridgeon | Did you tell her you were already married? |

| [003-127] Louis | Of course not. Don’t you see that if she had known, she wouldn’t have considered herself my wife? You don’t seem to understand, somehow. |

| [003-128] Sir Patrick | You let her risk imprisonment in her ignorance of the law? |

| [003-129] Louis | Well, *I* risked imprisonment for her sake. I could have been had up for it just as much as she. But when a man makes a sacrifice of that sort for a woman, he doesn’t go and brag about it to her; at least, not if he’s a gentleman. |

| [003-130] Walpole | What are we to do with this daisy? |

| [003-131] Louis | *Impatiently.* Oh, go and do whatever the devil you please. Put Minnie in prison. Put me in prison. Kill Jennifer with the disgrace of it all. And then, when you’ve done all the mischief you can, go to church and feel good about it. *He sits down pettishly on the old chair at the easel, and takes up a sketching block, on which he begins to draw.* |

| [003-132] Walpole | He’s got us. |

| [003-133] Sir Patrick | *Grimly.* He has. |

| [003-134] B.B. | But is he to be allowed to defy the criminal law of the land? |

| [003-135] Sir Patrick | The criminal law is no use to decent people. It only helps blackguards to blackmail their families. What are we family doctors doing half our time but conspiring with the family solicitor to keep some rascal out of jail and some family out of disgrace? |

| [003-136] B.B. | But at least it will punish him. |

| [003-137] Sir Patrick | Oh, yes: It’ll punish him. It’ll punish not only him but everybody connected with him, innocent and guilty alike. It’ll throw his board and lodging on our rates and taxes for a couple of years, and then turn him loose on us a more dangerous blackguard than ever. It’ll put the girl in prison and ruin her: It’ll lay his wife’s life waste. You may put the criminal law out of your head once for all: it’s only fit for fools and savages. |

| [003-138] Louis | Would you mind turning your face a little more this way, Sir Patrick. ***Sir Patrick** turns indignantly and glares at him.* Oh, that’s too much. |

| [003-139] Sir Patrick | Put down your foolish pencil, man; and think of your position. You can defy the laws made by men; but there are other laws to reckon with. Do you know that you’re going to die? |

| [003-140] Louis | We’re all going to die, aren’t we? |

| [003-141] Walpole | We’re not all going to die in six months. |

| [003-142] Louis | How do you know? |

| [003-143]  | *This for **B.B.** is the last straw. He completely loses his temper and begins to walk excitedly about.* |

| [003-144] B.B. | Upon my soul, I will not stand this. It is in questionable taste under any circumstances or in any company to harp on the subject of death; but it is a dastardly advantage to take of a medical man. *Thundering at **Dubedat**.* I will not allow it, do you hear? |

| [003-145] Louis | Well, I didn’t begin it: you chaps did. It’s always the way with the inartistic professions: when they’re beaten in argument they fall back on intimidation. I never knew a lawyer who didn’t threaten to put me in prison sooner or later. I never knew a parson who didn’t threaten me with damnation. And now you threaten me with death. With all your talk you’ve only one real trump in your hand, and that’s Intimidation. Well, I’m not a coward; so it’s no use with me. |

| [003-146] B.B. | *Advancing upon him.* I’ll tell you what you are, sir. You’re a scoundrel. |

| [003-147] Louis | Oh, I don’t mind you calling me a scoundrel a bit. It’s only a word: a word that you don’t know the meaning of. What is a scoundrel? |

| [003-148] B.B. | You are a scoundrel, sir. |

| [003-149] Louis | Just so. What is a scoundrel? I am. What am I? A scoundrel. It’s just arguing in a circle. And you imagine you’re a man of science! |

| [003-150] B.B. | I﻿—I﻿—I﻿—I have a good mind to take you by the scruff of your neck, you infamous rascal, and give you a sound thrashing. |

| [003-151] Louis | I wish you would. You’d pay me something handsome to keep it out of court afterwards. ***B.B.**, baffled, flings away from him with a snort.* Have you any more civilities to address to me in my own house? I should like to get them over before my wife comes back. *He resumes his sketching.* |

| [003-152] Ridgeon | My mind’s made up. When the law breaks down, honest men must find a remedy for themselves. I will not lift a finger to save this reptile. |

| [003-153] B.B. | That is the word I was trying to remember. Reptile. |

| [003-154] Walpole | I can’t help rather liking you, Dubedat. But you certainly are a thoroughgoing specimen. |

| [003-155] Sir Patrick | You know our opinion of you now, at all events. |

| [003-156] Louis | *Patiently putting down his pencil.* Look here. All this is no good. You don’t understand. You imagine that I’m simply an ordinary criminal. |

| [003-157] Walpole | Not an ordinary one, Dubedat. Do yourself justice. |

| [003-158] Louis | Well you’re on the wrong tack altogether. I’m not a criminal. All your moralizings have no value for me. I don’t believe in morality. I’m a disciple of Bernard Shaw. |

| [003-159] Sir Patrick | *Puzzled.* Eh? |

| [003-160] B.B. | *Waving his hand as if the subject was now disposed of.* That’s enough, I wish to hear no more. |
