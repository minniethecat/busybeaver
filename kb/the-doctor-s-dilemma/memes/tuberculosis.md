## Overview

Tuberculosis is the disease at the center of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]] suffers from pulmonary tuberculosis, and his condition forces the play's central moral question: which patient deserves the limited medical resources available?

## In the Play

Tuberculosis was a death sentence in Edwardian England, with no effective cure available. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]]'s opsonin-based treatment represents the best available medical approach of the era, but its scarcity — Ridgeon can only treat a limited number of patients — creates the play's dramatic dilemma.

The disease also carries social and moral connotations. Tuberculosis was often romanticized in 19th-century literature as the "consumptive artist's disease," associated with heightened sensitivity and creative genius. Shaw engages with this tradition through Dubedat's character while complicating it with his characteristic satirical skepticism.

## Medical Context

In the early 20th century, tuberculosis was one of the leading causes of death in Britain. The sanatorium movement offered rest, fresh air, and nutrition as the primary treatment. Effective antibiotic treatment (streptomycin) would not arrive until the 1940s. Shaw's play accurately reflects the medical reality of the period: a tuberculosis diagnosis was effectively a death sentence.

[[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]], the impoverished general practitioner, also suffers from tuberculosis — a detail that sharpens the play's social critique. Blenkinsop's condition is attributed to overwork and poor nutrition (002-045), conditions common among the working poor.

## Dramatic Function

Tuberculosis serves multiple dramatic functions in the play:

- **Moral catalyst**: The scarcity of effective treatment forces Ridgeon's choice between Dubedat and Blenkinsop.
- **Social commentary**: The disease affects rich and poor differently, exposing class inequities in medical care.
- **Romantic irony**: Dubedat's artistic persona aligns with the romanticized consumptive archetype, even as Shaw undercuts this romanticism.
