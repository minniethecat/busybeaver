## Definition

Class and poverty form the unspoken substrate of Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. The play's medical crisis — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] must choose whom to treat — is ultimately a crisis of distribution: in a society where healthcare is a commodity, the poor receive less of it. [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]], the impoverished general practitioner who serves working-class patients, embodies the class contradictions of Edwardian medicine (001-275) (001-276).

## Context in the Play

The play's class structure is mapped onto its medical geography. [[private-practice|Harley Street]] and [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]] represent elite private practice, serving wealthy patients who pay in [[private-practice|guineas]]. The poor exist at the margins: Emmy the serving-woman (001-002), [[redpenny|Redpenny]] the unpaid apprentice (001-001), and the patients whom [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] treats for minimal fees in working-class districts.

[[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most ethically unambiguous figure — a doctor who serves the poor at great personal cost — yet he is also the least powerful. His poverty makes him dependent on [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s charity, and [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s utilitarian calculus ("the worthier patient should live") is uncomfortably entangled with class judgment. Would [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] be deemed more "worthy" if he were not also poor?

The structural irony is sharp: [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] is a doctor who cannot afford medical treatment. He has devoted his life to healing the poor and has become one of them in consequence. His [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] — the disease that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] is designed to cure — is itself a disease of poverty, spread by overcrowding and malnutrition. Shaw makes visible what the [[private-practice|Harley Street]] [[medical-profession|doctors]] prefer not to see: that illness has class.

[[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]] provides the inverse class trajectory. His "Cure Guaranteed" shop practice among the midlands working class has made him wealthy — "I have a place in Hertfordshire besides our flat in town" (001-081) — while [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]'s ethical service has left him impoverished. The play thus exposes the perverse incentives of a system that rewards commercial cynicism and punishes professional idealism.

The serving-woman [[emmy|Emmy]] is a crucial class witness. Her e[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]hy skepticism — "I never was so taken aback. I always thought his great discoveries was fudge" (001-012) — punctures [[the-medical-profession-as-institution|the profession]]al pretensions of the [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]]. She knows the [[medical-profession|doctors]] as men who eat two eggs for breakfast (001-009), not as scientific authorities. Her working-class perspective functions as [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s choric truth-telling.

## Shaw's Position

As a Fabian socialist, Shaw saw healthcare inequality as a symptom of capitalism's moral failure. He argued that the state should guarantee medical care as a right, not a commodity. The play makes this argument dramatically rather than rhetorically: we feel the injustice of [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]]'s situation not because Shaw tells us it is unjust, but because [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s structure forces us to confront the consequences of a system that rations life by wealth.

Shaw's socialism was specifically Fabian — gradualist, reformist, committed to working through existing institutions rather than overthrowing them. The play's class critique operates through character and situation rather than direct polemic, exemplifying Shaw's belief that the theater was a more effective instrument of social change than the pamphlet. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s privileged position — [[private-practice|Harley Street]] practice, knighthood, [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's]] hospital appointment — is not attacked directly but is shown to be incompatible with equitable medical care.

## Historical Background

The 1905 Royal Commission on the Poor Laws was deliberating as Shaw wrote [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], and the debate between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor shaped social policy. Working-class access to healthcare was through a patchwork of Friendly Societies, charity hospitals, and out-patient dispensaries — a system that the 1911 National Insurance Act would begin, but only begin, to reform.

The Edwardian period saw the first systematic investigations of the relationship between poverty and health. Charles Booth's surveys of London poverty (1889–1903) and Seebohm Rowntree's study of York (1901) had demonstrated statistically what novelists and playwrights had long portrayed dramatically: that poverty killed. The [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] that afflicts both [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] and [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] was the era's great class-marked disease — a "romantic" affliction when it struck [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]s, a social problem when it struck the poor.
