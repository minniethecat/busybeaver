## Overview

Dr. Blenkinsop is the most economically vulnerable doctor in *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* — a general practitioner serving poor patients who cannot afford his fees. His presence in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] is brief but structurally essential: he is the "worthy" patient whom [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] chooses to treat instead of [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], making visible the utilitarian calculus that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral drama revolves around.

## Role in the Play

Blenkinsop functions as the moral counterweight to [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]]. Where [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] is brilliant, dishonest, and [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ically productive, Blenkinsop is ordinary, honest, and socially useful — a doctor who "goes round to the of the people who can't afford it" (001-211). [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision to let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die so that Blenkinsop may live is presented as a rational utilitarian choice, but Shaw exposes the [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity underlying it: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] chooses the man who shares his profession and his social values over [[louis-dubedat|the artist]] who challenges them.

## Character Analysis

Shaw uses Blenkinsop to test [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s — and the audience's — commitment to utilitarian ethics. If one must choose which patient to save, and if the calculus is genuinely imp[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ial, then Blenkinsop is the correct choice: he serves the poor, his work is irreplaceable, and the net social benefit of his survival clearly exceeds that of an [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] of uncertain productivity. But Shaw undermines this logic by showing [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision is contaminated by personal attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] and [[professional-rivalry|professional jealousy]] of Louis. Blenkinsop never learns that he was chosen to live at another man's expense — a dramatic irony that shadows every subsequent reference to him.

## Relationships

Blenkinsop's relationship with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is that of a grateful colleague — he knows [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] has saved his life but does not know the cost. His interaction with the other [[medical-profession|doctors]] at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] dinner (002-088) shows him fumbling with the telephone, a small comic detail that underscores his distance from the fashionable medical world of [[bloomfield-bonington|B.B.]] and [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]. He is "an elderly man" in [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s dismissive description (003-249), invisible to the aesthetic world that [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] represents.
