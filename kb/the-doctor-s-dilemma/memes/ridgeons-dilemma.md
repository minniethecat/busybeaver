## Summary

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma is the central dramatic engine of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. Having developed a new [[opsonin]] treatment for [[tuberculosis]] that can only treat a limited number of patients, [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] must choose whom to save. The choice crystallizes when he learns of two prospective patients: [[louis-dubedat]], a brilliant but dishonest [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]], and [[dr-blenkinsop]], a poor general practitioner who serves those who cannot pay. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision — to let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die so that [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] may live — is presented as utilitarian rationality but exposed by Shaw as a tissue of [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity, [[professional-rivalry|professional jealousy]], and personal desire. The dilemma is not merely a plot device but a structural analysis of how [[private-practice]] transforms healers into judges, and judges into executioners.

## Dramatic Context

The dilemma emerges from [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s professional success: his knighthood and his [[opsonin]] treatment have made him the most sought-after physician in London. But success creates scarcity — [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] tells [[jennifer-dubedat|Mrs. Dubedat]]: "But I have at [[st-annes-hospital|the hospital]] ten tuberculous patients whose lives I believe I can save" (001-314) — and scarcity forces choice. The irony is precise: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s triumph as a medical researcher produces a situation in which he must practice not medicine but triage.

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s famous line — "All professions are conspiracies against the laity" (001-276) — provides the moral framework. The [[medical-profession|doctors]]' decision about who lives and who dies is not a scientific judgment but a professional conspiracy. The dilemma reveals that the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]]'s claim to treat all patients equally is a fiction maintained by the convenient fact that most [[medical-profession|doctors]] never hold the power of life and death so nakedly.

## Characters Involved

- **[[sir-colenso-ridgeon]]**: The decider, whose hidden motives — attraction to [[jennifer-dubedat]], [[professional-rivalry|professional jealousy]] of [[louis-dubedat|Louis]], [[poverty-and-class|class]] solidarity with [[dr-blenkinsop]] — contaminate his utilitarian calculus. His line "In short, as a member of a high and great profession, I'm to kill my patient" (002-211) is both a confession and the most honest thing any doctor says in [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]].
- **[[sir-patrick-cullen]]**: The moral conscience who names the conspiracy but cannot prevent it. His "high and great profession" speech (002-210) is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most sustained appeal to medical idealism — and its failure is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s darkest judgment.
- **[[dr-blenkinsop]]**: The "worthy" patient, chosen to live because he shares [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s profession and [[poverty-and-class|class]] values. His [[poverty-and-class|poverty]] and dedication make him an easy moral choice — which is exactly the problem: [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] suggests that easy moral choices hide harder structural corruptions.
- **[[louis-dubedat]]**: The "unworthy" patient, condemned to die because his [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ic [[genius-and-talent|genius]] threatens the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' authority. His dis[[truth-and-deception|honesty]] — borrowing money, charming women — is real but irrelevant to his medical need.
- **[[jennifer-dubedat]]**: The unwitting catalyst. Her plea for Louis's treatment at [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Ridgeon's consulting room]] triggers the doctor's fatal attraction, which then masquerades as utilitarian judgment.

## Thematic Significance

The dilemma embodies [[social-criticism-in-the-doctors-dilemma|Shaw's critique]] of medical utilitarianism. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s choice — save the useful doctor, sacrifice the dishonest [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] — appears rational but is compromised at every level: by his desire for [[jennifer-dubedat]], by professional solidarity with [[dr-blenkinsop]], by envy of [[art-and-beauty|Dubedat's art]]istic charisma. The play's radical claim is not that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] makes the wrong choice, but that the very act of choosing — of turning medicine into a tribunal of worth — corrupts the healer beyond redemption.

The dilemma also exposes the structural inequality of [[private-practice]]. If medicine were organized as a public service — as Shaw argued it should be — the question of which patient "deserves" treatment would never arise. The dilemma is not a tragic inevitability but a political symptom: it exists only because the [[the-medical-profession-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|medical profession]] is organized around profit rather than need.

## Critical Analysis

Shaw's presentation of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s dilemma engages with a long tradition of utilitarian thought-experiments in moral philosophy. The choice between two lives — one conventionally worthy, one [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]]ically valuable but personally objectionable — echoes Jeremy Bentham's hedonic calculus while exposing its inadequacy. Shaw's innovation is to locate the abstract philosophical dilemma within a specific institutional context: [[private-practice]] medicine. The dilemma is not the product of tragic circumstance but of a deliberately designed economic structure that makes [[medical-profession|doctors]] into gatekeepers.

The play's structure reinforces this critique. The dilemma is not resolved in Act I, where it is first [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]iculated, but extends through Act II (the social [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] at [[star-and-garter-hotel|the hotel]]), Act III ([[dubedats-studio|the studio]] confrontation), and only reaches its conclusion in Act IV ([[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]]'s death). This elongation prevents the audience from treating the dilemma as a momentary decision; instead, it becomes a sustained demonstration of how professional systems produce and prolong moral crisis.
