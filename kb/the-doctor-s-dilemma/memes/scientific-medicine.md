## Definition

Scientific medicine, as depicted in Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*, refers to the late nineteenth-century transformation of [[medical-profession|medical practice]] from clinical empiricism to laboratory-based [[disease-and-diagnosis|diagnosis]] and treatment. The germ theory of disease, associated with Pasteur and Koch, provided a new theoretical foundation that Shaw simultaneously respected and satirized (001-209).

## Context in the Play

The generational divide between old and new medicine is dramatized through the characters of [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]] and [[bloomfield-bonington|Sir Ralph Bloomfield Bonington]]. [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] represents the self-confident new generation, proudly contrasting his germ-theory training with the "ignorantly bleeding and cupping and purging" of [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]]'s era (001-209). He declares: "Science is always simple and always profound. It is only the half-truths that are dangerous" (001-209).

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] occupies an intermediate position. His new [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] treatment — which earned him his knighthood — is a product of scientific research. Yet he remains skeptical of [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]'s dogmatic scientism. The [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] on [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]] contains the apparatus of the new medicine: [[redpenny|Redpenny]] serves as "domestic laboratory assistant" to [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] (001-001), and the room is equipped for both clinical [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] and laboratory work.

[[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]], the old guard, offers a deflating counterpoint. His "plain, downright, sometimes rather arid common sense" (001-072) represents clinical wisdom that predates [[ridgeons-laboratory|the laboratory]] revolution. Yet Shaw does not simply side with tradition: [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] concedes the advances of the new generation even as he mocks their pretensions.

## Shaw's Position

Shaw's attitude toward scientific medicine was characteristically ambivalent. As a Fabian socialist and public health advocate, he supported evidence-based medicine and state-funded healthcare. But he was deeply suspicious of professional monopo[[truth-and-deception|lies]] on knowledge, and he used [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]] to satirize the way scientific language could become a new form of priestly authority.

The play's central scientific question — whether [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] treatment actually works — is left deliberately ambiguous. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] himself doubts: "We're not a profession: we're a conspiracy" (001-275). The implication is that scientific medicine, for all its genuine advances, remains embedded in a social and economic system that distorts its practice.

## Historical Background

The germ theory revolution had transformed medicine in the decades before [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s 1906 premiere. Robert Koch identified the tubercle bacillus in 1882, and Louis Pasteur's work on [[inoculation|vaccination]] and sterilisation created the intellectual framework for antiseptic surgery and public health measures. The General Medical Council, established by the Medical Act of 1858, had begun standardising medical education around scientific principles.

Shaw followed these developments closely. He was friends with Almroth Wright, the immunologist who pioneered typhoid [[inoculation|vaccination]] and whom some scholars identify as a p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ial model for [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]. The play's medical debates reflect real controversies of the period, including disputes over [[disease-and-diagnosis|tuberculosis]] treatment, the ethics of human experimentation, and the conflict between laboratory researchers and practicing clinicians.
