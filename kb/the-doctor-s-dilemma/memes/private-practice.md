## Definition

Private practice in Shaw's *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* refers to the fee-for-service economic model that structured Edwardian medical care. Doctors on Harley Street and [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]] treated wealthy patients for substantial fees, while the poor relied on charity hospitals or went without treatment. Shaw uses this economic structure as the hidden engine of [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral crisis: [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] can only treat ten patients at a time because his practice is structured around paying clients (001-356) (001-358).

## Context in the Play

The play's opening establishes the economic geography of medical London. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s [[ridgeons-consulting-room|consulting room]] is on [[ridgeons-consulting-room|Queen Anne Street]], a real location in the Harley Street medical district (001-003). His patient list is capped at ten — an arbitrary limit imposed not by medical necessity but by the economics of elite private practice:

> (001-356) I have at the hospital ten tuberculous patients whose lives I believe I can save.

> (001-358) Try to think of those ten patients as ten shipwrecked men on a raft — a raft that is barely large enough to save them — that will not support one more.

When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] receives his knighthood, his first reaction is cynical:

> (001-273) So that's why they made me a knight! And that's the medical profession!

The dinner p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y in Act II brings the economics into sharp focus. The [[medical-profession|doctors]] dine at the [[star-and-garter-hotel|Star and Garter]] in Richmond after a long day of [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]]s (002-001). The contrast between their professional affluence and [[dr-blenkinsop|Dr. Blenkinsop]]'s [[poverty-and-class|poverty]] — he works as a poorly paid general practitioner serving the working [[poverty-and-class|class]] — exposes the injustices of the system. [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] cannot afford the treatment that [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] offers to wealthy patrons.

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s invitation to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] crystallizes the economic logic:

> (001-360) it was to have been a bachelors' dinner. I'm a bachelor. Now if you will entertain for me, and bring your husband, he will meet me; and he will meet some of the most eminent men in my profession

The social economy of dinners and introductions is inseparable from the medical economy of fees and treatments.

[[leo-schutzmacher|Schutzmacher]]'s commercial success provides a counterpoint: his shop-window with "Cure Guaranteed" and "Advice and medicine sixpence" (001-054) represents the frank commercialization that Harley Street conceals beneath professional dignity.

## Shaw's Position

As a Fabian socialist, Shaw was a committed advocate for state-funded healthcare. The play's economic critique is explicit: a system in which [[medical-profession|doctors]]' incomes depend on the number of paying patients they treat creates perverse incentives. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s decision to let [[louis-dubedat|Dubedat]] die so that [[dr-blenkinsop|Blenkinsop]] may live is p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ly an attempt to correct for these market distortions — but Shaw shows that individual moral choices cannot fix a structurally broken system.

The Preface to [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]], titled "[[preface-on-doctors|Preface on Doctors]]," extends this argument into polemical prose. Shaw argues that [[medical-profession|doctors]], like all professionals, are trapped in an economic system that rewards them for treating illness rather than preventing it:

> (001-276) Every fool can get ill; but every fool can't be a good doctor: there are not enough good ones to go round.

The [[scientific-medicine|germ theory]], for all its scientific validity, has done nothing to change this fundamental misalignment of incentives. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s own treatment — the [[opsonin|opsonin index]] developed at [[st-annes-hospital|St. Anne's]] hospital — is itself rationed by the constraints of private practice economics (001-360) (001-368).

## Historical Background

In 1906, when [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]] premiered, the British healthcare system was a patchwork of private practice, charity hospitals, and Friendly Society schemes. The National Insurance Act of 1911, which Shaw supported, would provide limited health coverage for workers, but the National Health Service was still four decades away. Harley Street had already established itself as the address of choice for elite medical consultants, a status it retains to the present day.

Shaw served on the St. Pancras Borough Council from 1897 to 1903 and had direct experience with public health administration. His 1911 pamphlet "[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]" (expanded from [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s Preface) became an influential text in the movement for healthcare reform. The medical marketplace of Edwardian London, with its elaborate fee structures — the standard guinea for a Harley Street [[the-doctor-patient-relationship|consultation]] — made healthcare a luxury commodity that the working [[poverty-and-class|class]]es could scarcely afford.
