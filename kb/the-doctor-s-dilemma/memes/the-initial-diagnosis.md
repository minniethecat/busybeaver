## Summary

The initial diagnosis is the pivotal encounter in Act I of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]* when [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer Dubedat]] visits [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Sir Colenso Ridgeon]] at [[ridgeons-consulting-room|his consulting room]] on Queen Anne Street to ask him to treat her husband, [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], who is suffering from tuberculosis. The consultation unfolds within the context of [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s recently discovered [[opsonin|opsonin treatment]] — a therapy that can only treat a limited number of patients, forcing the physician to choose among the desperate [001-211].

[[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]]'s entrance into the consulting room is described with notable care in the stage directions: she is "beyond all demur an arrestingly good-looking young woman" [001-300]. Her beauty and her earnest devotion to her husband immediately engage [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s sympathy — and, the play suggests, something more than sympathy. Her presence in his consulting room transforms a clinical decision into a human entanglement.

## Dramatic Context

The consultation occurs against a backdrop of professional celebration. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] has just been knighted for his opsonin research [001-273], and his colleagues — [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick Cullen]], [[cutler-walpole|Cutler Walpole]], [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]], and [[leo-schutzmacher|Leo Schutzmacher]] — have gathered to congratulate him. The arrival of [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] disrupts this self-congratulatory atmosphere with a genuine human need.

[[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] explains to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] the constraints of his treatment: he can only take ten patients [001-211]. He uses a striking metaphor: "Try to think of those ten patients as ten shipwrecked men on a raft" [001-211] — an image that frames the medical decision as a lifeboat ethics scenario in which someone must be left to drown.

## Key Dialogue

> (001-211) I can only take ten patients. I've got my hands full.

> (001-134) Won't you sit down and tell me the truth: I can bear it.

> (001-333) My name is on a brass plate on a door that leads from Queen Anne Street into this room.

## Thematic Significance

The initial diagnosis introduces the play's central moral problem: the allocation of scarce medical resources. [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s discovery of the opsonin index gives him the power to cure — but only ten patients at a time. The encounter with [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] transforms this abstract allocation problem into a personal dilemma. Her beauty, her devotion, and her vulnerability all work on [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s judgment, making it impossible for him to decide purely on clinical grounds — if such a thing were ever possible.

The consultation also establishes [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s characteristic pattern of self-deception. He presents himself to [[jennifer-dubedat|Jennifer]] as a dispassionate scientist wrestling with technical limitations, but the audience can see — and [[sir-patrick-cullen|Sir Patrick]] will later articulate — that his judgment is already being shaped by desire.
