## Overview

Jennifer Dubedat is the moral he[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] of *[[the-doctors-dilemma-play|The Doctor's Dilemma]]*. As the wife of the [[death-and-mortality|dying]] [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|artist]] [[louis-dubedat|Louis Dubedat]], she occupies a position of radical vulnerability — dependent on [[medical-profession|doctors]] she does not fully trust, defending a husband she knows to be dishonest, and maintaining a faith in both love and [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] that [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s rational, professional world cannot accommodate. Shaw's stage direction describes her as "beyond all demur an arrestingly good-looking young woman" with "something of the grace and romance of a wild creature, with a good deal of the elegance and dignity of a fine lady" (001-299). She is "tall, slender, and strong" with "unexpectedly narrow, subtle, dark-fringed eyes that alter her expression disturbingly when she is excited" (001-299).

## Role in the Play

Jennifer is the only character who bridges [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s two worlds — the medical and the aesthetic — without being corrupted by either. She navigates [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s professional world to secure treatment for her husband while refusing to accept the [[medical-profession|doctors]]' judgment of his character. When she first meets [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]], she implores: "Doctor: you must save my husband. You must. When I explain to you, you will see that you must. It is not an ordinary case, not like any other case. He is not like anybody else in the world" (001-299). Her posthumous curation of Louis's exhibition — which achieves the success he never found in life — is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s most powerful rebuttal to medical utilitarianism. By Act V, she has published her memoir *The Story of a King of Men. By His Wife* and presides over the Bond Street gallery opening (005-001).

## Character Analysis

Shaw makes Jennifer [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s moral test case. She knows her husband [[truth-and-deception|lies]] about money and has a bigamous past, yet she loves him without illusion. Her loyalty is not blindness but a conscious choice — a commitment to the truth of [[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]] over the truth of facts. When [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] calls Louis a "Clever brute" in [[bond-street-gallery|the gallery]], she rep[[truth-and-deception|lies]]: "Don't you see that what is really dreadful is that to you living things have no souls" (005-001). Her final exchange with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is devastating: "You are so utterly, so wildly wrong; so incapable of appreciating Louis" (005-001). When he reveals that he deliberately let Louis die, she responds not with anger but with unnerving serenity: "I knew quite well that you did not like Louis; but it is not your fault: you don't understand: that is all" (005-001). [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s confession of medical murder has no power over her worldview.

## Relationships

Jennifer's relationship with Louis is [[the-doctors-dilemma-play|the play]]'s primary emotional bond — asymmetrical in moral terms (he deceives, she forgives) but equal in emotional commitment. He calls her "Jinny-Gwinny" (002-002) and on his deathbed tells her: "It has been heaven, darling, hasn't it — sometimes?" She answers: "Oh yes, yes. Always, always" (004-001). Her interaction with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is charged with unacknowledged attraction on his side and strategic calculation on hers. He is "extremely susceptible to the beauty of women" and "instinctively assumes the defensive" at the sight of her (001-299). She treats the other [[medical-profession|doctors]] with a wary courtesy that never quite conceals her awareness of their limitations. After Louis's death, she stays "for weeks with the [[bloomfield-bonington|Bloomfield Bonington]]s and the [[cutler-walpole|Walpole]]s" (005-043), though she drops [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] entirely.

## Key Scenes

- **Act I (001-299)**: First meeting with [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] to secure treatment for Louis. She brings his drawings as proof: "Doctor: look at these. You understand drawings." [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]] is captivated by a drawing titled "Jennifer" — her given name, the Cornish form of Guinevere.
- **Act II (002-002)**: The dinner p[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]y — "Mrs. Dubedat, wrapped up for dep[[art-and-the-artist-in-the-doctor-s-dilemma|art]]ure, comes in." After the [[medical-profession|doctors]] agree to take Louis's case, she gasps "with delight, relief, and gratitude" (002-002).
- **Act IV (004-001)**: Louis's death. She holds his hand, promises not to be a widow, and listens as he describes his vision of im[[death-and-mortality|mortality]] as "the flame in the burning bush."
- **Act V (005-001)**: The posthumous exhibition. She has published *The Story of a King of Men*, curated the show, and faces [[sir-colenso-ridgeon|Ridgeon]]'s truth about Louis's death with quiet, unshakable faith: "you don't understand: that is all."
