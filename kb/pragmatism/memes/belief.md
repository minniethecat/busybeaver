## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, **belief** is not a passive mental state reflecting self-evident truth but an **attitude of the will** toward an idea, adopted and sustained because it serves practical human purposes. Belief is the engine of inquiry: it is formulated when uncertainty breaks down immediate certitude, and it is validated or discarded by the consequences of acting upon it. As [[william-james|James]] put it, belief is "essentially an attitude of the will towards an idea" (003-006), and acquiring a belief requires merely that one "act as if the thing in question were [[reality|real]]" (003-008) until it proves its worth in experience.

## Origin and Context

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] conception of belief grows from [[william-james|James]]'s insight that volition and inclination play a role in accepting all beliefs, scientific as well as religious. In his _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_ (1890), [[william-james|James]] argued in a short passage on "The Relations of Belief and Will" that belief requires no [[rationality|rational]] proof prior to action — it is adopted first and tested afterward (003-005). This insight became the basis for his famous doctrine of "[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]," first presented in an 1896 paper to a theological audience, which asserted the legitimacy of [[religious-experience|faith]] preceding knowledge (001-146). [[william-james|James]]'s psychology departed from the [[david-hume|Hume]]an tradition, which treated belief as a faint copy of sensation; instead, [[william-james|James]] saw belief as active, purposive, and indispensable to all knowledge.

> "Seeing that inclination and volition indisputably play a part in the _acceptance_ of all beliefs, scientific and religious, what is the logical significance of this fact? This yields the problem 'The Will to Believe,' and more generally of 'the place of Will in cognition.'" (001-026--001-028)

## Key Arguments

**Belief as volitional act.** [[william-james|James]] argued that belief is not the passive endpoint of [[rationality|rational]] proof but a practical commitment. To acquire a belief, one does not first verify and then accept; one accepts first and verifies through action:

> "In order to acquire a belief 'we need only in cold blood act as if the thing in question were real, and keep acting as if it were real, and it will infallibly end by growing into such a connection with our life that it will become real.'" (003-007--003-010)

**Belief as instrument of inquiry.** In the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] account, belief arises when immediate certitude breaks down in the course of pursuing a human purpose. Ideas and beliefs are formulated "and acted on, to fill the gap where immediate certitude has broken down" (005-162). Every belief is therefore a [[truth|truth-claim]] that must be tested by its consequences, not accepted on the authority of its assertion (005-050).

**Testing beliefs by consequences.** [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] rejects the idea that belief can be validated by intuition or self-evidence alone. Instead, beliefs are tested pragmatically: "It should be clear, though it is often misunderstood, that there is nothing arbitrary or 'subjective' in this [[pragmatic-method|method of testing]] beliefs. It does not mean that we are free to assert the truth of every idea which seems to us pretty or pleasant" (005-188--005-189). A belief is validated if its consequences are satisfactory and promote the purpose in hand (005-169).

**The fallacy of self-evident belief.** [[william-james|James]] warns that the mere feeling of certainty is no guarantee of truth:

> People instinctively hold their beliefs to be "self-evidently or indisputably true" (004-004).

This naive dogmatism collapses under social scrutiny:

> "Social life plainly would become impossible if all rigidly insisted on the absolute rightness of their own beliefs and the absolute wrongness of all others." (004-016--004-017)

**Beliefs evolve through reconstruction.** Under [[darwinism|Darwinian]] influence, pragmatism treats beliefs as subject to continuous revision: "Since the world changes, his 'truths' must change to fit it. He is faced with the necessity of a continuous reconstruction of beliefs" (001-107). The function of thought is "to effect the necessary changes in beliefs as economically and usefully as possible" (001-111).

**[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]] generalized.** Though the Will to Believe was first illustrated in a theological context, [[william-james|James]]'s successors showed it was a universal principle of knowledge: "that the will to believe was capable of being generalized into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge was soon shown in [[fcs-schiller|Dr. Schiller]]'s _Axioms as Postulates_" (003-190--003-192). Critics who charged that the Will to Believe licensed arbitrary belief "simply failed to see that [[verification|verification by experience]] is just as integral a part of voluntaristic procedure as experimental postulation" (003-186--003-187).

## Variants and Interpretations

- **Religious belief (Will to Believe):** The right to adopt a religious hypothesis when the evidence is inconclusive but the decision is forced, living, and momentous — the original context of [[william-james|James]]'s doctrine.
- **Scientific belief (postulation):** The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] method of postulating laws and causes as working [[hypothesis|hypotheses]], verified by their predictive success: "The postulation of 'causes' is verified by its success" (003-115).
- **Common-sense belief:** The inherited stock of practical beliefs about physical objects, causation, and the self that pragmatism treats as useful instruments, not metaphysical truths (002-012, 002-036).
- **Belief vs. [[truth|truth-claim]]:** Every belief is initially a mere formal [[truth|truth-claim]]; validated beliefs are those whose consequences promote the purpose they were devised to serve (005-050, 005-169).

## Criticisms and Debates

The doctrine of the Will to Believe drew sharp criticism for allegedly licensing wishful thinking. Detractors claimed [[william-james|James]] "allowed everyone to believe whatever he pleased and to call it 'true'" (003-184--003-185). [[william-james|James]] and his defenders replied that [[verification|verification by experience]] is integral to the doctrine — a belief adopted by the will must still survive [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]]ing in the world of fact (003-186).

A broader criticism targets the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] conflation of belief with practical attitude: if belief is simply what one is willing to act on, does this not reduce the distinction between warranted and unwarranted conviction to mere expediency? The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] response is that there is no higher court of appeal than the test of consequences in human experience.
