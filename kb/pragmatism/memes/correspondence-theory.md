## Definition

The correspondence theory of truth holds that a statement or thought is true if and only if it corresponds to, or agrees with, [[reality|real]]ity. It is the oldest and most intuitive definition of truth in Western philosophy, tracing back to Aristotle's formulation that "to say of what is that it is not, or of what is not that it is, is false, while to say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true."

> The oldest and commonest definition of a 'truth' which is given is that it is 'the correspondence of a thought to reality.' (005-078)

## Origin & Context

The correspondence theory has been the default account of truth throughout most of Western philosophy, endorsed in various forms by Aristotle, Thomas Aquinas, the British [[empiricism|empiricist]]s, and early analytic philosophers including Bertrand Russell and G. E. Moore. In *[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]*, [[william-james|William James]] subjects this theory to sustained criticism as part of his broader argument for the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]] of evaluating philosophical ideas.

[[william-james|James]] situates the correspondence theory within the [[intellectualism|intellectualist]] tradition — the tendency to treat truth as a static, purely cognitive relation between thought and its object, divorced from practical consequences and human interests. He argues that while the correspondence theory appears straightforward, it collapses under scrutiny because it provides no actual *test* for distinguishing true from false beliefs.

> But Intellectualism never perceived the difficulties lurking in it. At first sight this seems a brave attempt to get outside the circle of thought in order to test its value and to control its vagaries. Unluckily, this theory can only assert, and neither explains nor proves, the connection between the thought and the reality it desiderates. [005-079--005-082]

## Key Arguments

### James's Criticisms of the Correspondence Theory

**1. The theory cannot test truth claims.** [[william-james|James]] argues that the correspondence theory merely redefines truth rather than providing a method for determining whether any particular assertion is true. To verify correspondence, one would need independent access to [[reality|real]]ity — but if [[reality|real]]ity were already directly known, there would be no need to assert ideas about it in the first place.

> The correspondence theory, then, does not _test_ the truth-claim of the assertion; it only gives a fresh definition of it. A 'true' thought, it says, is one which _claims to correspond_ with a 'reality.' _But so does a false,_ and hence the theory leaves us as we were, puzzled to distinguish them. [005-095--005-099]

**2. [[verification|Verification]] requires independent access to [[reality|real]]ity.** The theory demands that [[reality|real]]ity be knowable apart from our ideas about it, so that the two can be compared. But [[william-james|James]] points out that this is a circular requirement — we can only access [[reality|real]]ity through our ideas, making the comparison impossible.

> For, granting that it is the intent of every thought to correspond with reality, we must yet inquire how the alleged correspondence is to be made out. Made out it must be; for as the criterion is quite formal and holds of all assertions, the claim to 'correspond' may be false. [005-085--005-087]

**3. Practical limits of correspondence.** [[william-james|James]] acknowledges that the correspondence test works in a limited domain — namely, perceptual judgments that can be experimentally verified through action. However, this scope is narrow and does not cover the full range of human beliefs, including scientific theories, religious [[belief|conviction]]s, and ethical principles.

> The limits within which this correspondence works are very strait. It applies only to such judgments as are anticipations of perception, and will test a truth-claim only where there is willingness to act on it. It implies an experiment, and is not a wholly intellectual process. [005-106--005-109]

### Comparison with the Coherence Theory

[[william-james|James]] contrasts the correspondence theory with the coherence theory of truth (associated with idealist philosophers), which holds that an idea is true if it is consistent with the rest of our thoughts and can be integrated into a coherent system. While [[william-james|James]] finds the coherence theory also inadequate, he notes that the correspondence theory at least has the virtue of insisting on external verification rather than purely internal consistency.

> The superiority of the 'correspondence' theory over the belief in 'intuitions' lies in its insistence that thought is not to audit its own accounts. Its success or failure depends upon factors external to it, which establish the truth or falsehood of its claims. [005-110--005-113]

### The Absolute Version

[[william-james|James]] identifies a theological variant of the correspondence theory, according to which human truth is measured by its correspondence with the experience of an [[the-absolute|Absolute Mind]]. He dismisses this as the correspondence doctrine "in a fresh disguise," since it merely displaces the verification problem onto an inaccessible Absolute rather than solving it.

> But here we have the correspondence doctrine once again in a fresh disguise; our human systems are now 'true' if they correspond with the Absolute's. But as there is no way for us of sharing the Absolute [[experience|Experience]], our test is again illusory, and productive of a depressing scepticism. [005-134--005-138]

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Classical Correspondence (Aristotelian)**: [[truth|Truth]] as isomorphism between proposition and fact — the standard formulation inherited by medieval scholasticism and early modern philosophy.
- **Empiricist Correspondence (Lockean)**: Ideas in the mind correspond to qualities in external objects; truth is the agreement of our ideas with [[reality|the real]]ities of things.
- **[[logic|Logic]]al Atomism (Russell, early Wittgenstein)**: Elementary propositions are true when they correspond to atomic facts in the world; the truth of complex propositions is a function of their elementary constituents.
- **[[the-absolute|Absolute Idealism]]'s Version**: [[truth|Truth]] as correspondence with the experience of an [[the-absolute|Absolute Mind]] — the version [[william-james|James]] critiques as an evasion of the verification problem.

## Criticisms & Debates

[[william-james|James]]'s critique of the correspondence theory is part of his broader pragmatic redefinition of truth in terms of practical consequences and "what it is better for us to believe." Critics of [[william-james|James]]'s alternative (see [[truth]]) argue that the pragmatic theory conflates the *nature* of truth with the *criteria* for recognizing it, and that correspondence remains the most intuitive account of what truth *means*, even if it does not provide a practical test.

The correspondence theory has been revived in contemporary philosophy through Alfred Tarski's semantic theory of truth and the truthmaker theory in contemporary metaphysics, both of which attempt to formalize the intuition behind correspondence while avoiding the circularity problems [[william-james|James]] identified.
