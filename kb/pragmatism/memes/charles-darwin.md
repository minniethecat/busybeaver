## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was a British naturalist whose theory of [[darwinism|evolution]] by [[darwinism|natural selection]] — presented in _On the Origin of Species_ (1859) — transformed not only biology but the entire intellectual landscape of the late nineteenth century. In [[william-james|William James]]'s 1907 lectures on [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]], Darwin is explicitly named as a foundational figure whose work supplied the [[darwinism|evolution]]ary framework essential to pragmatic thought. [[william-james|James]] identifies [[darwinism|Darwinism]] as a decisive intellectual current, declaring in Lecture I:

> Another fountain-head of Pragmatic philosophy has been Darwinism. (001-099)

Darwin's importance to pragmatism is not merely historical but constitutive: the pragmatic [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]], truth, and mind is built on [[darwinism|evolution]]ary premises that could not have been formulated before Darwin. As [[william-james|James]] argues, "the Pragmatic is the only philosophizing which has completely assimilated Evolution" (001-100–001-101).

## Life

- **1809** — Born in Shrewsbury, England, to a wealthy family; his grandfather was Erasmus Darwin, an early [[darwinism|evolution]]ist thinker.
- **1825–1827** — Studied medicine at the University of Edinburgh but found surgical practice distasteful; began natural history studies.
- **1828–1831** — Attended Christ's College, Cambridge, intending to become a clergyman; studied under botanist John Stevens Henslow, who recommended him for the HMS Beagle expedition.
- **1831–1836** — Voyage of the HMS Beagle; Darwin's observations of South American geology, fossils, and the distinctive fauna of the Galapagos Islands laid the empirical foundations for his theory of [[darwinism|natural selection]].
- **1838** — Read Thomas Malthus's _Essay on the Principle of Population_, which crystallized the mechanism of [[darwinism|natural selection]] — the "struggle for existence" in which favorable variations are preserved.
- **1859** — Published _On the Origin of Species_, presenting the theory of [[darwinism|evolution]] by [[darwinism|natural selection]] and providing overwhelming evidence for the mutability of species.
- **1871** — Published _The Descent of Man_, extending [[darwinism|evolution]]ary theory to human beings.
- **1882** — Died at Down House, Kent; buried in Westminster Abbey.

## Key Ideas

1. **The Mutability of Species**: Darwin's central claim overturned the long-entrenched doctrine of fixed, immutable species created in their final form. His demonstration of the "[[reality|real]] fluidity of natural species" (001-101) was of direct philosophical significance to [[william-james|James]], who saw it as a vindication of process over fixity in every sphere of thought.

2. **Natural Selection as a Non-Teleological Mechanism**: The mechanism of [[darwinism|natural selection]] — the differential survival of fortuitous variations — offered a model of adaptive change that required no guiding intelligence and no appeal to final causes. [[william-james|James]] drew on this logic to account for the survival of mental "variations" in the history of belief. In his _[[psychology|Psychology]]_, he argued that the mind's organization "is essentially biological" and:

> It has evolved according to sound Darwinian principles, and in so doing the fittest of its 'variations' have survived. (002-227–002-228)

3. **Evolution as Universal Framework**: Darwin's theory provided not just a biological hypothesis but an organizing metaphor for all human knowledge. [[william-james|James]] argues that "the Pragmatic is the only philosophizing which has completely assimilated Evolution" (001-100–001-101), treating the growth of concepts, beliefs, and truths themselves as an [[darwinism|evolution]]ary process continuous with organic [[darwinism|evolution]].

4. **The Rejection of Static Essentialism**: By demonstrating that species are not fixed kinds with immutable essences, Darwin undermined the whole essentialist metaphysics that had governed Western philosophy since Aristotle. [[reality|Reality]] is a process, not a set of static forms — and pragmatic thinking is the only one adequate to this [[reality|real]]ity:

> The insight into the real fluidity of natural species ought long ago to have toned down the artificial rigidity of logical classifications. To know reality man can no longer rest in a 'timeless' contemplation of a static system; he must expand his thoughts so as to cope with a perpetually changing process. Since the world changes, his 'truths' must change to fit it. He is faced with the necessity of a continuous reconstruction of beliefs. (001-101–001-107)

5. **[[darwinism|Darwinian]] [[logic|Logic]] in Epistemology**: [[william-james|James]] applied [[darwinism|Darwinian]] [[rationality|reason]]ing to the problem of [[apriorism|a priori]] knowledge. Rather than treat the mind's axiomatic principles as transcendent necessities ([[immanuel-kant|Kant]]) or as passive copies of experience ([[david-hume|Hume]]), [[william-james|James]]:

> suggested that the use of these axiomatic principles might be construed in Darwinian style as a 'variation' surviving by its fitness, thus introducing into his account of mental process the important idea that thinking might be tested by its vital value. (003-020–003-023)

## Relation to Pragmatism

Charles Darwin is a **precursor and intellectual catalyst** for [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]. [[william-james|James]] does not treat him as a self-conscious [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] — Darwin was a working naturalist, not a philosopher — but as the originator of a set of ideas without which pragmatism could not have taken its distinctive form.

Darwin's influence runs through pragmatism along three interconnected lines. First, his theory provided the [[darwinism|evolution]]ary infrastructure for the pragmatic theory of truth: if species adapt to changing environments, so too must beliefs, concepts, and truths. Second, his non-teleological mechanism of variation-and-selection gave [[william-james|James]] a framework for understanding mental life as an adaptive instrument rather than a mirror of [[reality|real]]ity. Third, and most concretely, Darwin's ideas directly shaped the instrumentalist school of pragmatism:

> This influence of Darwin has inspired the logical theories of Professor Dewey and the 'Chicago School' of Pragmatists. Thought in their writings is essentially the instrument of this readjustment. Its function is to effect the necessary changes in beliefs as economically and usefully as possible. It is an evolving process which keeps pace with the evolution of reality and the changing situations of mortal life. (001-108–001-113)

[[william-james|James]]'s reading of Darwin is distinctive in emphasizing not the struggle for existence as a grim Malthusian fight, but rather the open-ended, forward-looking character of [[darwinism|evolution]]ary processes. The mind evolves not by blindly responding to past conditions but "purposively" — a question [[william-james|James]] raises explicitly:

> But were these variations quite fortuitous? May they not have been purposive responses to the stimulation of environment? Can logic have been invented like saws and ships for purposes of human service? (002-228–002-231)

This question — whether mental [[darwinism|evolution]] is merely blind variation or purposive adaptation — is central to the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] conception of thought as an active, interest-driven tool rather than a passive mirror of [[reality|real]]ity.

## Major Works

- _On the Origin of Species_ (1859) — The foundational text of [[darwinism|evolution]]ary biology; the work that established the mutability of species and the mechanism of [[darwinism|natural selection]].
- _The Descent of Man_ (1871) — Extended [[darwinism|evolution]]ary theory to human origins and psychology.
- _The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals_ (1872) — Applied [[darwinism|evolution]]ary [[rationality|reason]]ing to mental and emotional life, anticipating themes in [[william-james|James]]'s _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_.
- _The Autobiography of Charles Darwin_ (1887, posthumous) — Darwin's own account of his intellectual development.

[[william-james|James]] cites [[john-dewey|John Dewey]]'s _The Influence of [[darwinism|Darwinism]], and Other Essays_ (1910) in the bibliography of Lecture VIII (008-161), acknowledging the importance of Darwin's legacy for the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition.
