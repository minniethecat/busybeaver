## Overview

Alfred North Whitehead (1861–1947) was a British mathematician and philosopher. He began his career as a mathematician at Cambridge, co-authoring _Principia Mathematica_ (1910–1913) with [[bertrand-russell|Bertrand Russell]]. After moving to Harvard in 1924, he developed a distinctive metaphysical system known as process philosophy, which shares important affinities with [[pragmatism|American pragmatism]].

## Connection to Pragmatism

Though Whitehead was not a pragmatist per se, his later philosophy resonates deeply with pragmatist themes. His rejection of the "bifurcation of nature" (the Cartesian split between mind and matter) parallels [[william-james|James]]'s radical empiricism, which insists that relations are given in experience just as directly as terms. Whitehead's concept of "prehension" — the idea that every actual entity grasps or feels its world — echoes [[william-james|James]]'s emphasis on the continuity and intimacy of experience.

Whitehead's process metaphysics also shares with [[john-dewey|Dewey]]'s pragmatism a thoroughgoing rejection of static substance metaphysics in favor of a dynamic, event-based ontology. Both thinkers saw reality as fundamentally temporal and relational rather than composed of fixed, independent substances.

## Key Works

- _Principia Mathematica_ (1910–1913, with Bertrand Russell) — a monumental work in mathematical logic
- _Science and the Modern World_ (1925) — a critical examination of scientific materialism
- _Process and Reality_ (1929) — his magnum opus in metaphysics
