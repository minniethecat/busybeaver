## Definition

Relativism, in the context of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, is the epistemological view that [[truth|truth-claim]]s must be evaluated comparatively — by weighing opinions against one another — rather than by appeal to an external, absolute standard. On this view, we assess the relative merits of competing claims without presuming access to a [[truth|Truth]] that transcends all human perspectives.

> The former view is _relativism_, the latter is _absolutism_, in the matter of truth. As regards the first problem, the first question is whether we shall try to _test_ opinions and to arrive at a standard of value by which to measure them by comparing the opinions themselves with one another, or shall presume that there must be some absolute standard which alone is truly true, whether we are aware of it or not. (004-035)

## Origin & Context

Relativism as an epistemological stance has ancient roots — [[william-james|James]] traces it to Protagoras's dictum that "Man is the measure of all things" (001-112). However, it is in the context of the breakdown of [[absolutism|absolutist]] epistemology that relativism acquires its modern urgency. When the appeal to [[absolutism|absolute truth]] proves practically empty, relativism emerges not as a preferred option but as a description of how human inquiry actually operates.

[[william-james|James]] presents relativism and absolutism as the two fundamental alternatives in the theory of truth. [[absolutism|Absolutism]] is more intuitively appealing but leads to insoluble dilemmas; relativism is less comforting but more honest about the human epistemic condition.

## Key Arguments

**[[absolutism|Absolutism]] leads to scepticism.** If there is an [[absolutism|absolute truth]] that no one can access, then all human knowledge is, in practice, relative — exactly what the relativist claims:

> If it is admitted that no one can arrogate to himself its possession, what use is it to believe that it is an ideal? For if no one can assume that he has it, all _human_ truth is, in fact, such as the relativist asserted, and scepticism is just as inevitable as before. (004-041--004-043)

**Relativism does not entail scepticism.** Relativism denies [[absolutism|absolute truth]] but does not deny that some opinions are better supported than others. We can compare, test, and evaluate without an absolute yardstick:

> It makes no difference to the sceptical inference whether there is no absolute truth, or whether it is unattained by man, and humanly unattainable. (004-043)

**The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] way out.** [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism shows how to accept the relativist insight — that we have no access to [[absolutism|absolute truth]] — while avoiding the slide into scepticism. The [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] evaluates [[truth|truth-claim]]s by their consequences, not by their correspondence to an inaccessible absolute:

> To come to terms with relativism is to renounce the dogmatic attitude altogether, to recognize that 'absolute' truth is a chimera, and to be content with the sort of truth that is attainable by human beings. (004-103, paraphrase)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Epistemological relativism**: [[truth|Truth]]-claims can only be evaluated within a framework of other claims, never against an absolute, framework-independent standard.
- **Cultural relativism**: Different cultures or communities may have different standards of truth, and there is no neutral vantage point from which to adjudicate between them.
- **Pragmatic relativism**: [[william-james|James]]'s distinctive position — truth is relative to human purposes and practices, but this does not make it arbitrary; purposes and practices are themselves subject to testing and revision.

## Criticisms & Debates

The most persistent criticism of relativism is that it is self-refuting: if all truth is relative, then the claim "all truth is relative" must itself be only relatively true. [[william-james|James]] does not directly address this logical objection, but his pragmatism suggests a different approach: the question is not whether relativism is absolutely true (that would be self-contradictory), but whether it is more _useful_ than absolutism as a guide to inquiry.

A second criticism is that relativism removes the motivation for inquiry — if no view is ultimately truer than any other, why investigate? [[william-james|James]]'s answer is that relativism does not entail the equality of all opinions; it merely denies that equality must be measured against an absolute. Some opinions demonstrably work better than others, and that is enough to motivate inquiry.
