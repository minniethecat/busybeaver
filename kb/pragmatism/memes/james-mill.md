## Overview

James Mill (1773–1836) was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and political economist, best known as the father of [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]] and a leading figure in the British empiricist and utilitarian traditions. His _Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind_ (1829) was a landmark work of [[british-associationism|associationist]] psychology, applying the principles of association to explain all mental phenomena as compounds built from simple sensory elements.

## Role in the Pragmatist Narrative

In [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, James Mill represents the empiricist tradition that the pragmatists both inherit and transform. The associationist psychology that Mill championed — the view that all complex ideas are built from simple sensations through the laws of association — is precisely the picture of mind that [[william-james|James]] rejects in favor of a dynamic, functionalist account in which consciousness is a selective, purpose-driven stream rather than a passive compound of atomic sensations.

James Mill's empiricism was a rigorous, even austere, doctrine: it treated experience as a chain of discrete, atomistic sensations linked by contiguity. For [[william-james|James]], this "psychological chemistry," as Mill called it, missed the active, selective, forward-looking character of mental life.

## Key Works

- _Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind_ (1829) — a systematic exposition of associationist psychology
- _History of British India_ (1817) — a major historical work that shaped British understanding of India
