## Overview

_Studies in Humanism_ (1907) is [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]]'s major monograph developing the philosophical doctrine of [[humanism|Humanism]] — the position that all knowledge, truth, and [[reality|real]]ity are human artifacts shaped by human purposes, valuations, and selective interests. Schiller introduced the term "[[humanism|Humanism]]" into philosophic terminology as a label for his general philosophical position, distinct from [[william-james|William James]]'s narrower epistemological focus under the name "[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]" (008-030--008-033). The work stands alongside _[[humanism|Humanism]]: Philosophical Essays_ (1903) as Schiller's definitive statement of the Humanist outlook (008-152--008-153).

[[william-james|James]] devotes Lectures VII ("[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[humanism|Humanism]]") and VIII ("[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[religion|Religion]]") in large part to expounding Schiller's Humanist position as the natural culmination of the pragmatic outlook. As [[william-james|James]] presents it, the transition from [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] to [[humanism|Humanism]] moves "from the narrower logical question, 'What constitutes the "truth" of a statement?' to a wider outlook, from which we can survey the place of [[knowledge|knowing]] in human life at large" (008-026--008-029).

## Summary

_Studies in Humanism_ develops the argument that truth and knowledge are irreducibly human products. Schiller grounds this in a Protagorean starting point: the dictum that "Man is the measure of all things" foreshadows "the 'Pragmatic,' or, as he calls it, the 'Humanist,' solution" to the problem of distinguishing truth from error (001-120--001-122). [[william-james|James]] notes that Schiller demonstrated "already in the days of [[plato|Plato]] the distinction between 'truth' and 'error' was baffling philosophy" and that [[plato|Plato]]'s _Theaetetus_ failed to establish it (001-117--001-119).

The work extends beyond epistemology into a comprehensive philosophical vision. It treats truth-values as one species of valuation among many — alongside moral, aesthetic, and practical judgments — and argues that all such valuations derive their meaning from reference to specific human purposes (008-040--008-049). The _summum bonum_, or supreme good, is conceived as "the ideal of the harmonious satisfaction of all purposes" (008-048--008-049).

A central theme is the plasticity of [[reality|real]]ity. [[humanism|Humanism]] refuses to treat the world as "a given and completed whole" (008-100). What we call [[reality|the real]] world is always "a selection from a larger whole from which we have ventured to exclude great masses of irrelevance" (008-101--008-103). This selective character of [[reality|real]]ity applies even to philosophers: "the thinkers who declare they want to know all about '[[reality|real]]ity'" in fact wish to know what they "consider best worth [[knowledge|knowing]]" — an inescapably personal valuation (008-087--008-088).

## Key Contributions

1. **[[humanism|Humanism]] as a comprehensive philosophical position**: Schiller moved beyond pragmatism's methodological focus to a full metaphysical and epistemological doctrine. Where [[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] addresses "the original question of the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]," Schiller's [[humanism|Humanism]] describes "his general philosophical position" (008-030--008-033).

2. **[[truth|Truth]] as human product**: The humanist contention that "[[truth|Truth]] is human" rescues man "from the despondency in which his failure to grasp [[absolutism|absolute truth]] had left him" (007-052--007-053). [[truth|Truth]]-values function within human life by "indicating a relation to the cognitive end" — and this end is not self-sufficing but must "subserve our vital purposes" (008-050--008-055).

3. **Voluntarist epistemology**: Schiller generalized [[william-james|James]]'s "will to believe" into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge. [[william-james|James]] acknowledges that this generalization "was soon shown in [[fcs-schiller|Dr. Schiller]]'s _[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]_" (003-191--003-192), a related work whose arguments are developed in _Studies in Humanism_.

4. **Critique of [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]]**: Schiller joined forces with Alfred Sidgwick in "urging a radical criticism of the procedures of [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]]" and showing "the gulf between them and the processes of concrete thought" (001-132--001-133). His _[[formal-logic|Formal Logic]]_ (1912) continued this critique (008-154).

5. **[[meliorism|Meliorism]] and the possibility of progress**: [[humanism|Humanism]] is "neither an Optimism nor a Pessimism" but "concedes to man the right and duty to improve the world" (008-131--008-134). It provides "[[rationality|reason]]s for our [[religious-experience|faith]] in the possibility of Progress" by showing how we may act on our hopes and correct our errors (008-136--008-137).

## Notable Passages

> To the Humanist the best definition of life is one which displays it as throughout purposive, as a rational pursuit of ends. This raises the question of the validity of valuations. (008-034--008-036)

> What, then, from the standpoint of Humanism, is the function of 'truth-values' in our life? They indicate a relation to the cognitive end. What is this end? Surely not self-sufficing? A truth that is merely true in itself has no interest for human life, and no human mind has an interest in discovering and affirming it. Truth, therefore, cannot stand aloof from life. It must somehow subserve our vital purposes. (008-050--008-055)

> The pragmatic notion that _Truth is practical_ closes the artificial gulf between the theoretic and the practical side of life, and assigns to truth a biological function and vital value. The humanist contention that _Truth is human_ rescues man from the despondency in which his failure to grasp absolute truth had left him. (007-050--007-053)

> For 'reality,' the object of the mind's search, is always a selection, conceived after the likeness of the heart's desire, the product of a human purpose. (008-096--008-098)

> Our world is plastic, it is most 'really' what we can make of it, and the process of our making is not ended. Whether a decree of Fate has fixed any ultimate limits to our efforts we have no means of knowing, and no occasion to assume. Is not our wisest course, then, to persist in trying? (008-107--008-112)

> The superiority of Humanism, then, lies in this, that it does not discourage human enterprise by assuming that the real is completely rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort. In the drama that unrolls reality, every man, it teaches, has a duty and a power to play his humble but essential part. (008-127--008-131)

> Humanism is neither an Optimism nor a Pessimism — both of which must consistently, in their extreme form, deny that reality can be improved — but concedes to man the right and duty to improve the world. It impresses us with the necessity of acting, it vindicates the procedure of acting on our hopes, it shows us how we may correct our errors, and so gives reasons for our faith in the possibility of Progress. (008-131--008-137)

## Reception & Influence

_Studies in Humanism_ was recognized by [[william-james|James]] as a major contribution to the pragmatic movement. [[william-james|James]] included it alongside Schiller's _[[humanism|Humanism]]: Philosophical Essays_ (1903), _Riddles of the Sphinx_ (1891), and _[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]_ (1902) in the bibliography of _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (008-149--008-153).

Schiller's [[humanism|Humanism]] radicalized the human element in pragmatism, arguing that all truths are human artifacts shaped by human interests and purposes. This position was both influential and controversial: critics charged it with subjectivism, while proponents saw it as pragmatism's logical culmination. Schiller traced the humanist impulse back to Protagoras, claiming that "Man is the measure of all things" was the earliest expression of the pragmatic outlook (001-120--001-121).

Schiller also advanced a powerful critique of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], arguing that "[[immanuel-kant|Kant]] failed to answer [[david-hume|Hume]] because he accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology, and that no _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine" (001-124--001-126). This critique, developed across his works including _Studies in Humanism_, positioned [[humanism|Humanism]] as an alternative to both [[rationality|rational]]ist apriorism and [[empiricism|empiricist]] [[scepticism|skepticism]].
