## Definition

Pragmatism is a [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]] that began in the United States around 1870. Its core claim is that the meaning and truth of ideas should be evaluated by their practical consequences — what difference they make in human experience and action. As [[william-james|William James]] put it, pragmatism is "a new name for some old ways of thinking" (001-001), treating philosophy not as a set of fixed dogmas but as a method for resolving otherwise interminable metaphysical disputes.

## Origin & Context

The pragmatic movement emerged from the [[metaphysical-club|Metaphysical Club]] at Harvard in the 1870s, where Charles Sanders Peirce first formulated what he called the "[[pragmatic-method|pragmatic maxim]]." [[william-james|James]] later developed and popularized these ideas, describing pragmatism as a mediating philosophy between tough-minded empiricism and tender-minded [[rationality|rational]]ism (001-050).

> All these ideas are mistaken. Pragmatism is neither a revolt against reason nor a celebration of irrationality. It is, rather, an attempt to explain how reason actually works in human life. (001-012)

The historical context was the late 19th-century intellectual ferment following [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] — the same period that gave birth to pragmatism's sister movements in psychology and social science (001-115).

## Key Arguments

1. **The [[pragmatic-method|Pragmatic Method]]**: Interpret each notion by tracing its practical consequences. If two competing theories produce no practical difference, the dispute is meaningless — what [[william-james|James]] called "no difference that makes a difference" (001-050).

2. **[[truth|Truth]] as Instrumental**: [[truth|Truth]] is not a static property of propositions but something that "happens to an idea" — it becomes true through events, through its capacity to guide us successfully through experience (005-071).

3. **[[empiricism|Empiricism]] Radicalized**: Pragmatism extends empiricism beyond sensory data to include relations, values, and possibilities — everything that makes a concrete difference in experience (001-137).

> Pragmatism, therefore, by refuting 'useless' abstractions whose pretended objective truth was incapable of verification, does full justice also to the spiritual demands which the rationalist postulates of an absolute truth seemed to satisfy. (005-200)

4. **[[psychology|Psychology]] as Foundation**: The most sensational approach to pragmatism comes from psychology, particularly the stream of consciousness and the functional view of mind that [[william-james|James]] developed (001-150).

## Variants & Interpretations

Pragmatism is not a monolithic doctrine. Key variants include:

- **Peircean Pragmaticism** — emphasizing logical method and the community of inquiry
- **[[william-james|James]]ian Pragmatism** — emphasizing individual experience, [[religion|religious belief]], and the will to believe
- **[[john-dewey|Dewey]]an Instrumentalism** — emphasizing education, democracy, and social reform through inquiry

[[william-james|James]] describes his version as "the philosophy of action" that takes its generic name from the Greek πράγμα (pragma), meaning action or deed (001-155).

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics have charged pragmatism with reducing truth to mere utility, conflating what works with what is true. [[william-james|James]] responded that pragmatism does not deny objective [[reality|real]]ity but insists that our only access to truth is through its practical bearings in experience (007-070).

Bertrand Russell and other analytic philosophers criticized pragmatism's theory of truth as conflating the definition of truth with the criterion for recognizing it — a debate that continues in contemporary philosophy.
