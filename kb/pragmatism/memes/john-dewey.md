## Overview

John Dewey (1859--1952) was an American philosopher, psychologist, and educational reformer who ranks among the three central founders of [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] alongside Charles Sanders Peirce and [[william-james|William James]]. In [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907), Dewey appears as the leader of the "[[chicago-school|Chicago School]]" of Pragmatists, whose logical theories are explicitly credited to the influence of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]. [[william-james|James]] identifies Dewey's instrumentalist conception of thought -- the view that thinking is a tool for readjusting beliefs to a changing world -- as a cornerstone of the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]].

## Life

- **1859**: Born in Burlington, Vermont; the year of [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s _On the Origin of Species_.
- **1879**: Graduates from the University of Vermont.
- **1884**: Earns Ph.D. from Johns Hopkins University; studies under G. Stanley Hall and Charles Sanders Peirce.
- **1894**: Appointed chair of philosophy, psychology, and pedagogy at the newly founded University of Chicago; establishes the Laboratory School.
- **1904**: Leaves Chicago for Columbia University, where he remains for the rest of his career.
- **1920s--1930s**: Becomes a prominent public intellectual, writing on democracy, education, art, and social reform.
- **1952**: Dies in New York City at age 92.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Thought as Instrumental Readjustment

Dewey's most distinctive contribution is instrumentalism: the view that thought is not a passive mirror of [[reality|real]]ity but an active instrument for coping with a changing world. [[william-james|James]] places this idea at the core of pragmatism's second fountain-head:

> The insight into the real fluidity of natural species ought long ago to have toned down the artificial rigidity of logical classifications. To know reality man can no longer rest in a 'timeless' contemplation of a static system; he must expand his thoughts so as to cope with a perpetually changing process. Since the world changes, his 'truths' must change to fit it. He is faced with the necessity of a continuous reconstruction of beliefs.
> (001-107)

> This influence of Darwin has inspired the logical theories of Professor Dewey and the 'Chicago School' of Pragmatists. Thought in their writings is essentially the instrument of this readjustment. Its function is to effect the necessary changes in beliefs as economically and usefully as possible. It is an evolving process which keeps pace with the evolution of reality and the changing situations of mortal life.
> (001-108)

### 2. Thought Begins in Perplexity, Not Certainty

Dewey argued that genuine thinking does not begin from settled truths but from problematic situations that disrupt habitual belief. [[william-james|James]] endorses this position explicitly:

> In real life thought starts from perplexities, from situations in which, as Professor Dewey says, beliefs have to be 'reconstructed,' and it aims at setting doubts at rest. It is psychologically impossible for a rational mind to assert what it knows to be true, and supposes everyone else to admit the truth of.
> (005-035)

### 3. The Darwinian Turn

Dewey's instrumentalism is inseparable from the [[darwinism|evolution]]ary perspective. [[william-james|James]] identifies [[darwinism|Darwinism]] as a direct fountain-head of [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]] -- and Dewey as its clearest philosophical exponent:

> Another fountain-head of Pragmatic philosophy has been Darwinism. Indeed, the Pragmatic is the only philosophizing which has completely assimilated Evolution.
> (001-099)

### 4. The Conditional Nature of Truth

Building on the [[darwinism|evolution]]ary insight, Dewey held that all conceptual systems, including logic and mathematics, have only conditional truth. [[william-james|James]] echoes this:

> All conceptual systems of the human mind have a merely conditional truth, depending on the circumstances of their application.
> (001-097)

## Relation to Pragmatism

John Dewey was a **developer** of [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]. Peirce originated the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic maxim]], [[william-james|James]] popularized and humanized it, and Dewey transformed it into a comprehensive instrument for educational, social, and democratic reform. [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ treats Dewey as the intellectual leader of the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] -- the group that most fully assimilated [[darwinism|Darwinian]] [[darwinism|evolution]] into logic and epistemology. Where [[william-james|James]] emphasized the personal and religious dimensions of truth, Dewey emphasized its social and experimental dimensions, insisting that philosophy must address concrete human problems rather than abstract metaphysical puzzles.

[[william-james|James]] himself did not live to see Dewey's mature works -- _Democracy and Education_ (1916), _[[experience|Experience]] and Nature_ (1925), _The Quest for Certainty_ (1929) -- but his 1907 lectures clearly position Dewey as a central ally in the pragmatic movement, particularly in linking pragmatism to the [[darwinism|Darwinian]] r[[darwinism|evolution]] in science.
