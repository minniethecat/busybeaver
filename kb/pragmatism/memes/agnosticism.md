## Definition

Agnosticism is the epistemological position that the existence or non-existence of God is unknown and (some agnostics add) unknowable — that the evidence is insufficient to warrant belief either way. The term was coined by [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]]'s disciple Thomas Henry Huxley in 1869. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ and _[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]_, agnosticism is the primary target of his voluntarist epistemology: [[william-james|James]] argues that in cases where the evidence is genuinely inconclusive and the choice is living, forced, and momentous, suspending judgment is not a neutral act — it has practical consequences equivalent to choosing disbelief.

## Origin & Context

Agnosticism emerged in the Victorian period as a response to the conflict between religious tradition and scientific [[positivism|positivism]]. Huxley presented agnosticism as the intellectually honest position: one should not claim to know what one does not know. This position was championed by [[wk-clifford|W.K. Clifford]], whose essay "The Ethics of Belief" (1877) argued that "it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence."

[[william-james|James]]'s _[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]_ is a direct response to Clifford. [[william-james|James]] does not dispute Clifford's evidentialism in cases where evidence is available. His argument is that there are cases — the religious hypothesis being the paradigm — where the evidence is *necessarily* inconclusive (because the truth of the hypothesis can only be verified by living as if it were true) and where the choice is forced (not to decide is to decide against).

## Key Arguments

### The Pragmatic Critique of Agnosticism

[[william-james|James]]'s case against agnosticism is pragmatic:

1. **Agnosticism is not neutral**: In a forced option — believe in God or don't — suspending judgment is practically equivalent to not believing. Both result in the same conduct: living without reference to God.

2. **The risk asymmetry**: If theism is true, the agnostic loses the benefits of religious belief. If theism is false, the agnostic gains nothing (both the agnostic and the atheist are equally right — and equally dead). The agnostic thus risks a real loss for no potential gain.

3. **Some truths require commitment**: The truth of a personal relationship cannot be verified from the outside — one must commit to the relationship to discover whether it "works." [[william-james|James]] argues that the religious hypothesis is like this: it can only be tested by those who commit to it.

### Agnosticism vs. Theism vs. Atheism: The Pragmatic Difference

| Position | Practical Consequence |
|----------|---------------------|
| [[theism|Theism]] | Lives as if God exists — experiences religious comfort, moral motivation, and (potentially) verification through experience |
| Agnosticism | Lives as if God does not exist (since no action is guided by divine reference) — experiences no religious consequences |
| Atheism | Lives as if God does not exist — same practical result as agnosticism |

From a pragmatic standpoint, agnosticism and atheism are functionally identical.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Evidentialist agnosticism**: The view that the evidence is currently insufficient but belief could become warranted with better evidence. Huxley's position.
- **Principled agnosticism**: The view that the question is in principle unanswerable — we cannot know. Stronger than evidentialist agnosticism.
- **Pragmatic agnosticism**: The view that the question should be settled by practical consequences rather than theoretical evidence. This is James's position — but he concludes that the practical consequences favor belief.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics argue that [[william-james|James]]'s argument for belief over agnosticism proves too much — it would justify belief in any comforting illusion. [[william-james|James]]'s response is that the argument applies only to genuine, living hypotheses — options that are real possibilities for the believer — and that religious belief is subject to ongoing verification in experience.
