## Definition

Logic is the systematic study of the principles of valid inference and demonstration. In the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition, formal logic is critically examined for its tendency to abstract away from meaning and concrete experience, treating [[rationality|reason]]ing as a purely syntactic operation disconnected from practical consequences.

[[william-james|William James]], in his [[gifford-lectures|Gifford Lectures]] that became _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907), engaged with the critique of formal logic developed by his contemporaries — particularly [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]] and Alfred Sidgwick — to argue that [[truth]] cannot be determined by logical form alone, but must be tested by its consequences in lived [[experience]].

## Origin & Context

The classical tradition of logic, stretching from Aristotle through the medieval Scholastics to nineteenth-century textbooks, treated logic as the science of the laws of thought — a formal system of rules that guarantee valid inference regardless of content. This conception of logic as a purely formal discipline reached its apex in the algebraic logic of George Boole and the logical systems of the late nineteenth century.

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] challenge to formal logic emerged in the context of a broader revolt against [[intellectualism]] — the philosophical tendency to privilege abstract [[rationality|reason]]ing over concrete experience. [[william-james|James]] situated his discussion of logic within Lecture I ("The Present Dilemma in [[philosophy|Philosophy]]"), where he framed the problem as one of meaning:

> The weird verbalism of the traditional Logic suggests a problem which strikes deeper even than the question, "What _do_ you mean by truth?" viz.: "Do you mean anything?" and so the "problem of Meaning" is propounded by the failure of Formal Logic. Is Logic not concerned at all with _meaning_, is it only juggling with empty forms of words? (001-035--001-040)

[[william-james|James]] returned to this critique in Lecture VI, where he directly addressed the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] theory of truth and its conflict with formal logic:

> Intellectualism appeals to Logic, which it conceives as a purely formal science and its impregnable citadel. (006-004)

## Key Arguments

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique of formal logic, as developed by [[william-james|James]] and his allies, rests on several interconnected arguments:

1. **Logic abstracts from meaning.** Formal logic treats propositions as symbols whose truth depends solely on their logical form. But in actual [[rationality|reason]]ing, [[william-james|James]] argued, meaning is inseparable from the practical consequences of believing a proposition. As he noted in Lecture V: "Outside of Formal Logic and lunatic asylums no one wishes to assert that 'A is A.' All significant assertion takes the form 'A is B'" (003-147--003-148).

2. **Logical validity ≠ truth.** The fact that a conclusion follows validly from premises does not guarantee the truth of those premises or their applicability to concrete situations. [[william-james|James]] endorsed Sidgwick's demonstration "that the belief in formal truth renders Logic merely verbal, and that the actual _meaning_ of assertions completely escapes its net" (001-132--001-135).

3. **Logic needs pragmatic reform.** The failure of formal logic to account for meaning and practical consequence led [[william-james|James]] to call for "a new Theory of [[knowledge|Knowledge]] and a thorough reform of Logic" (001-130). This reform would reconnect [[reason|logical reason]]ing with its human context — the purposes, needs, and consequences that give propositions their meaning.

4. **Logic mistakes words for concepts.** [[william-james|James]] argued that formal logic treats words as stable tokens with fixed meanings, ignoring the fluid and context-dependent nature of actual thinking: "Formal Logic has, in fact, mistaken _words_, which are (within the same language) stable, for the concepts which they are supposed to represent" (005-245--005-251).

| Argument | Target | Pragmatist Alternative |
|----------|--------|----------------------|
| Abstraction from meaning | Traditional syllogistic logic | Logic grounded in practical consequences |
| Validity ≠ truth | Formal deductive systems | [[truth|Truth]] as verifiable through experience |
| Need for reform | Textbook logic of 19th c. | Schiller's _Formal Logic_ (1912) |
| Words ≠ concepts | Linguistic formalism | Context-sensitive meaning |

## Variants & Interpretations

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique of formal logic took different forms among its proponents:

- **[[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]]** developed the most systematic [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique in his _Formal Logic_ (1912), which [[william-james|James]] explicitly references in Lecture V (005-250). Schiller argued that all logical operations presuppose a human purpose and cannot be understood apart from the volitional context of inquiry.

- **Alfred Sidgwick** approached the problem from the angle of actual [[rationality|reason]]ing practice, showing through detailed examples "the gulf between [formal logic's procedures] and the processes of concrete thought" (001-132--001-133). His work emphasized the role of context, presumption, and fallibility in [[reality|real]]-world argumentation.

- **C.S. Peirce**, while sharing the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] emphasis on consequences, developed a more constructive approach to logic — seeing it not as a purely formal system but as the normative science of inquiry. His work on abduction, deduction, and induction offered an alternative to both formal logic and its wholesale rejection.

- **[[john-dewey|John Dewey]]** extended the critique in his _Essays in Experimental Logic_ (1916), arguing that logical forms arise from and are justified by their function in inquiry, not by [[apriorism|a priori]] formal properties.

## Criticisms & Debates

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique of formal logic faced several counterarguments:

Bertrand Russell and the early analytic philosophers argued that the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]]s conflated _logic_ (the normative study of valid inference) with _psychology_ (the descriptive study of how people actually think). The fact that [[reality|real]] [[rationality|reason]]ing is context-dependent and fallible, they contended, does not invalidate the logical norms that [[rationality|reason]]ing ought to respect.

The development of modern symbolic logic by Frege, Russell, and Whitehead demonstrated that formal systems could handle increasingly subtle distinctions of meaning — suggesting that the problem was not with formalism per se but with the impoverished formal systems of the nineteenth-century textbook tradition that [[william-james|James]] and Schiller were reacting against.

Nevertheless, the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] emphasis on the connection between logic and inquiry has proven influential, resurfacing in the works of later philosophers such as W.V.O. Quine (who argued that logic is not immune to empirical revision) and Hilary Putnam (whose "internal [[reality|real]]ism" echoed [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] themes about the entanglement of logic with practical [[rationality|reason]]).
