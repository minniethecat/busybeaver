## Overview

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) was a British philosopher, biologist, and sociologist whose synthetic philosophy — applying evolutionary principles to every domain of knowledge — was the dominant intellectual system of the English-speaking world in the late nineteenth century. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Spencer appears as both an influence and an antagonist: [[william-james|James]] admired Spencer's empiricist, evolutionary approach but rejected his deterministic, mechanistic conclusions. The pragmatist account of [[truth|truth]] as evolving and adaptive owes something to Spencer's evolutionary framework, even as [[william-james|James]] transformed it into something distinctively pragmatic.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1820** — Born in Derby, England, to a family of religious dissenters.
- **1848** — Became sub-editor of _The Economist_ in London.
- **1851** — Published _Social Statics_, advocating radical individualism.
- **1855** — Published _Principles of Psychology_, proposing an evolutionary account of mind; influenced [[william-james|James]]'s own approach.
- **1862** — Published _First Principles_, the foundation of his "Synthetic Philosophy."
- **1864–1896** — Published the multi-volume _System of Synthetic Philosophy_.
- **1903** — Died in Brighton, his reputation already in decline.

## Key Contributions

Spencer's "Synthetic Philosophy" aimed to unify all knowledge under the principle of evolution — from the formation of galaxies through the development of life to the evolution of societies and moral systems. He coined the phrase "survival of the fittest" (which [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] later adopted) and applied evolutionary thinking to psychology, sociology, and ethics decades before it became mainstream.

Spencer's evolutionary psychology was particularly significant for [[william-james|James]]: Spencer argued that mental faculties are products of adaptation, a view that [[william-james|James]] developed in _[[principles-of-psychology|The Principles of Psychology]]_ into a full-fledged functionalist psychology.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s relationship to Spencer was ambivalent. He respected Spencer's evolutionary naturalism and empiricist commitments but rejected Spencer's determinism and his tendency to treat evolution as an all-explaining metaphysical principle. Where Spencer saw evolution as a closed, necessary process, [[william-james|James]] saw it as open, contingent, and compatible with genuine novelty and [[free-will|free will]].
