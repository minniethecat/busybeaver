## Overview

_Axioms as Postulates_ (1902) is an essay by [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]] that [[william-james|William James]] credited with generalizing the "will to believe" into a comprehensive voluntarist [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]. First published in the collection _[[personal-idealism|Personal Idealism]]_ (edited by Henry Sturt), the essay argues that even the most fundamental logical principles — including the Law of Identity — are not self-evident truths but [[postulation|postulates]] adopted because they serve human purposes. [[william-james|James]] recognized Schiller's argument as a crucial step in the development of pragmatism from a theory of [[religion|religious belief]] into a general epistemology.

## Summary

Schiller's central argument is that logical axioms function as [[postulation|postulates]] rather than as necessary truths. We adopt principles like "A is A" not because they are logically compulsory, but because they are practically indispensable. The essay extends [[william-james|James]]'s earlier argument in "[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]" — that in certain religious and moral contexts, our passional nature must decide — into the domain of logic and epistemology itself.

[[william-james|James]] describes this generalization as a pivotal moment in the development of [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]]:

> The will to believe was capable of being generalized into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge was soon shown in Dr. Schiller's _Axioms as Postulates_. (003-191--003-192)

The essay appeared as a chapter in _[[personal-idealism|Personal Idealism]]_, a collection that [[william-james|James]] references in his survey of the Humanist literature:

> _Axioms as Postulates_ (in _Personal Idealism_, ed. Henry Sturt, 1902). (008-149--008-150)

## Key Contributions

1. **Generalization of the will to believe**: Schiller transformed [[william-james|James]]'s focused argument about [[religion|religious belief]] into a comprehensive epistemological position — all knowledge, including logical knowledge, involves an act of volitional commitment.

2. **[[logic|Logic]]al axioms as [[postulation|postulates]]**: By treating the Law of Identity and other fundamental principles as [[postulation|postulates]] rather than self-evident truths, Schiller undermined the [[rationality|rational]]ist claim that logic provides access to necessary, universal truths independent of human purposes.

3. **Bridge to [[humanism|Humanism]]**: _Axioms as Postulates_ was an early statement of the voluntarist epistemology that Schiller would later develop into the full philosophical doctrine of [[humanism|Humanism]] in works such as _[[humanism|Humanism]]: Philosophical Essays_ (1903) and _[[studies-in-humanism|Studies in Humanism]]_ (1907).

## Notable Passages

> The will to believe was capable of being generalized into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge was soon shown in Dr. Schiller's _Axioms as Postulates_. (003-191--003-192)

## Reception & Influence

_Axioms as Postulates_ influenced [[william-james|James]]'s own thinking about the role of volition in knowledge, and provided the epistemological foundation for Schiller's later development of [[humanism|Humanism]]. Its inclusion in _[[personal-idealism|Personal Idealism]]_ placed it within a broader movement of British philosophers challenging absolute idealism from a personalist and [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] direction.
