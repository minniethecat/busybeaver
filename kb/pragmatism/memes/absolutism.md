## Definition

Absolutism, in the context of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, is the epistemological doctrine that there exists a single, absolute standard of truth — a [[truth|Truth]] that is wholly independent of human perspectives, practices, and purposes. [[truth|Truth]], on this view, is not a matter of degree or context; a proposition is either absolutely true or absolutely false. Absolutism stands in direct opposition to relativism, which holds that [[truth|truth-claim]]s can only be compared and evaluated against one another, without appeal to an external absolute standard.

> The former view is _relativism_, the latter is _absolutism_, in the matter of truth. (004-035)

## Origin & Context

The absolutist conception of truth has deep roots in Western philosophy, from Plato's theory of Forms through medieval [[religion|theology]] to modern [[rationality|rational]]ism. [[william-james|James]] presents absolutism as the natural, pre-philosophical attitude: it is "more congenial to our natural prejudices" (004-036) and is typically the first approach people take when thinking about truth.

However, [[william-james|James]] argues that this intuitive appeal conceals deep problems. Absolutism "soon conducts dogmatism to an awkward series of dilemmas" (004-037) that render it practically useless as a guide to inquiry.

> Now, there can be no doubt that absolutism is more congenial to our natural prejudices. Accordingly it is the method tried first; but it soon conducts dogmatism to an awkward series of dilemmas. (004-036--004-037)

## Key Arguments

**The dilemma of possession.** If absolute truth exists, who possesses it? No individual or group can credibly claim absolute access:

> If there is absolute truth, who has it? and who can use the absolute criterion of opinions it is supposed to form? Not, surely, everyone who _thinks_ he has. It will never do to let every dogmatist vote for himself and condemn all others. That way war and madness lie. Until there is absolute agreement, there cannot be absolute truth. (004-038--004-039)

**The ideal is practically empty.** Even if absolute truth is treated as a regulative ideal rather than a possession, it does no practical work:

> But would it save us? If it is admitted that no one can arrogate to himself its possession, what use is it to believe that it is an ideal? For if no one can assume that he has it, all _human_ truth is, in fact, such as the relativist asserted, and scepticism is just as inevitable as before. (004-041--004-043)

**Certainty is not a reliable criterion.** The appeal to subjective certainty cannot distinguish genuine truth from mere [[belief|conviction]]:

> A critically-minded man will urge against it that _'certainty' is a subjective and psychological criterion_, and that no one has been able to devise a method for distinguishing the alleged logical from the undeniable psychological certainty. (004-048)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Metaphysical absolutism**: The claim that absolute truth exists as a mind-independent [[reality|real]]ity, even if inaccessible (e.g., certain forms of [[reality|real]]ism).
- **Methodological absolutism**: The practical stance that one should seek the one true answer — [[william-james|James]] sees this as leading inevitably to dogmatism.
- **Ideal absolutism**: The view, associated with British idealism and figures like F.H. Bradley, that truth is ultimately the property of the Absolute — a total, coherent system of [[reality|real]]ity.

## Criticisms & Debates

[[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique of absolutism is not that it is false, but that it is _useless_. An absolute standard that no one can access, apply, or verify does no intellectual work. It is, in pragmatic terms, a distinction that makes no difference:

> It makes no difference to the sceptical inference whether there is no absolute truth, or whether it is unattained by man, and humanly unattainable. (004-043)

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative — that truth is something that _happens_ to an idea through its verification in experience — replaces the static, absolute conception with a dynamic, processual one that actually guides inquiry.
