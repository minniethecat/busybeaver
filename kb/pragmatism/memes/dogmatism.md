## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, dogmatism is the natural human tendency to hold one's beliefs as self-evidently and indisputably true, without subjecting them to testing or social scrutiny. It is the instinctive starting point of all philosophical thinking, but also the obstacle that pragmatism seeks to overcome by replacing [[absolutism|absolute truth]]-claims with a method of practical verification and public testing.

## Origin and Context

Dogmatism is not a school or a doctrine but a temperamental disposition -- an instinct that [[william-james|James]] identifies as universal. He treats it as the default human condition from which philosophy must painfully grow, and the source of the "dilemmas" that traditional [[rationality|rational]]ism cannot resolve.

> Every man, probably, is by instinct a dogmatist. He feels perfectly sure that he knows some things, and is right about them against the world. Whatever he believes in he does not doubt, but holds to be self-evidently or indisputably true. (004-002--004-005)

The concept serves as a foil to the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]]. Where the dogmatist asserts finality, the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] insists on testing; where the dogmatist appeals to private intuition, the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] demands public verification.

## Key Arguments

**Dogmatism as the starting point of philosophy.** [[william-james|James]] presents dogmatism not as a rare intellectual error but as every person's default mode of belief. We all begin as dogmatists, convinced that our own [[belief|conviction]]s are obvious truths that any [[rationality|reason]]able person must share.

> His naive dogmatism, moreover, spontaneously assumes that his truth is universal and shared by all others. (004-005--004-007)

**Social life forces dogmatism into crisis.** Were a person to live entirely in isolation, dogmatism would be lifelong and incurable. The [[reality|real]]ity of social life -- the encounter with others who hold different beliefs with equal [[belief|conviction]] -- is what drives the dogmatist toward philosophy.

> But as he lives socially, he has in practice to outgrow it, and this lands him in a serious theoretical dilemma... Social life plainly would become impossible if all rigidly insisted on the absolute rightness of their own beliefs and the absolute wrongness of all others. (004-012--004-017)

From this social friction arise three problems that define the pragmatic inquiry: how to compare opinions, how to interpret the belief in [[absolutism|absolute truth]], and how to prevent the penitent dogmatist from collapsing into scepticism (004-024--004-029).

**The dilemmas of dogmatism.** [[william-james|James]] traces how the dogmatist's commitment to [[absolutism|absolute truth]] leads inexorably to a series of logical dilemmas. Appealing to [[absolutism|absolute truth]] is useless when no one can agree on who possesses it; treating it as an unattained ideal leaves scepticism intact; appealing to subjective "certainty" simply licenses every dogmatist equally (004-039--004-062).

> Absolutism is more congenial to our natural prejudices. Accordingly it is the method tried first; but it soon conducts dogmatism to an awkward series of dilemmas. (004-036--004-038)

**Dogmatism in science.** The dogmatic habit is not confined to philosophy. [[william-james|James]] observes that even science, once confident in the finality of its laws, has been forced to abandon dogmatism as its own methods proved self-correcting.

> What it concerns him to note is that dogmatic confidence in the finality of scientific laws has given place to a belief that our "laws" are only working formulae for scientific purposes, and that no science can truly boast of having read off the mind of the Deity. (001-078--001-081)

**The final dilemma.** Dogmatism, pursued to its logical conclusion, ends in the recognition that no belief -- no matter how firmly held -- has survived historical scrutiny untouched. The dogmatist must either embrace scepticism or renounce the dogmatic attitude entirely.

> Thus the last dilemma of dogmatism is reached... we must either admit scepticism to be the issue of the debate, or else, condemning our absolute view of truth, find some means of utilizing the relative truths which are all that humanity seems able to grasp. (004-097--004-102)

> To come to terms with relativism is to renounce the dogmatic attitude entirely, and to approach the problems of philosophy in a totally different spirit. (004-103--004-105)

## Variants and Interpretations

- **Naive dogmatism**: The pre-philosophical [[belief|conviction]] that one's own beliefs are obviously true. [[william-james|James]] describes this as the default human condition (004-002--004-005).
- **Absolute dogmatism vs. relativism**: [[william-james|James]] frames the entire epistemological debate as a choice between asserting [[absolutism|absolute truth]] (dogmatism) and accepting that all truth is humanly situated and revisable (relativism) (004-035--004-038).
- **Judgmental dogmatism**: In the context of [[truth|truth-claim]]s, [[william-james|James]] notes that the maker of a judgment may dogmatically insist on its universal [[belief|acceptance]] without testing -- but this is folly, and anyone except the dogmatist recognizes that judgments are proposals for testing rather than final decrees (005-043--005-045).

## Criticisms and Debates

**The scepticism trap.** The most serious criticism of the dogmatic position is that it contains no internal mechanism to stop the slide into [[scepticism|universal scepticism]]. Once the dogmatist concedes that others may be just as certain of their opposing beliefs, the appeal to subjective certainty becomes worthless. The history of philosophy -- "strewn with the remains of 'infallible' systems and 'absolute' certainties" (004-091--004-092) -- testifies that dogmatism cannot deliver on its promise of final knowledge.

**[[david-hume|Hume]]'s critique.** [[william-james|James]], following [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]], notes that [[david-hume|Hume]]'s criticism of dogmatism exposed the failure of [[apriorism|a priori]] philosophy: "[[immanuel-kant|Kant]] failed to answer [[david-hume|Hume]] because he accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology, and no [[apriorism|a priori]] philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine" (001-124--001-127). This historical failure is one of the primary motivations for pragmatism's rejection of the dogmatic starting point.

**The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative.** [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] replaces dogmatism not with scepticism but with a method. To renounce the dogmatic attitude is to accept that all [[truth|truth-claim]]s are provisional, testable, and revisable. [[william-james|James]]'s alternative is not the abandonment of truth but its reconstruction on practical, human foundations -- truth as something that "happens to an idea" rather than a static property it possesses inherently.
