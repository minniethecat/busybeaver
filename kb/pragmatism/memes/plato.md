## Overview

Plato (428–348 BCE) is the foundational figure of Western philosophy, whose theory of Forms and dialectical method set the terms for nearly every subsequent epistemological debate. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907), Plato appears not as a systematic thinker to be engaged directly, but as the origin point of a philosophical problem that pragmatism claims to dissolve: the distinction between truth and error, and the nature of knowledge itself.

[[william-james|James]], following [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]]'s scholarship, presents Plato as the first philosopher to wrestle seriously with the truth/error distinction — and the first to fail to establish it on firm grounds. The Platonic tradition, which treats the physical world as an inferior shadow of eternal Forms, stands in direct opposition to the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]], which insists that truth is made in the course of experience rather than discovered in a transcendent [[reality|real]]m.

## Life

- **428 BCE** — Born in Athens to an aristocratic family
- **c. 407 BCE** — Becomes a student of Socrates, whose dialectical method shapes Plato's philosophical approach
- **399 BCE** — Execution of Socrates; Plato leaves Athens and travels extensively
- **c. 387 BCE** — Founds the Academy in Athens, the first institution of higher learning in the Western world
- **c. 380–360 BCE** — Writes the _Republic_, _Phaedo_, _Symposium_, and other middle-period dialogues
- **c. 369 BCE** — Writes the _Theaetetus_, a dialogue devoted to the question "What is knowledge?"
- **348 BCE** — Dies in Athens at approximately age 80

## Key Ideas

### The Problem of Knowledge in the _Theaetetus_

Plato's _Theaetetus_ is the first sustained attempt in Western philosophy to define what knowledge is. The dialogue examines and rejects several definitions — knowledge as perception, knowledge as true judgment, knowledge as true judgment with an account — without arriving at a satisfactory conclusion. [[william-james|James]], drawing on Schiller's analysis, sees this failure as historically significant:

> Philosophy itself has been rent by internal convulsions. These have been emphasized in the work of Dr. F.C.S. Schiller, who has shown that already in the days of Plato the distinction between 'truth' and 'error' was baffling philosophy, that Plato's _Theaetetus_ has failed to establish it. (001-117--001-119)

The _Theaetetus_'s failure to define knowledge is not, in [[william-james|James]]'s view, a mere historical curiosity. It reveals a deep structural problem in the Platonic approach: the assumption that truth and error must be distinguished by appeal to a static, transcendent standard. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] proposes instead that the distinction emerges from the practical consequences of believing one thing rather than another.

### Protagoras and the Pragmatic Foreshadowing

The _Theaetetus_ examines and ultimately rejects Protagoras's dictum that "Man is the measure of all things." Yet [[william-james|James]], again following Schiller, sees in Protagoras a foreshadowing of the pragmatic theory of truth:

> The famous dictum of Protagoras, 'Man is the measure of all things,' distinctly foreshadows the 'Pragmatic,' or, as he calls it, the 'Humanist,' solution of the difficulty. (001-120--001-122)

For pragmatism, Protagoras's insight — that truth is measured by human purposes and experience — represents a more promising starting point than Plato's quest for eternal Forms. Where Plato saw relativism and sought to overcome it with a metaphysical [[reality|real]]m of Ideas, pragmatism embraces the human measure and develops it into a coherent theory of truth as that which works in the way of belief.

### The Platonic Rejection of Experience

The Platonic tradition treats the physical world — the world of sight and touch, of concrete experience — as a degraded copy of a higher, intelligible [[reality|real]]ity of Forms or Ideas. [[william-james|James]] presents this as the defining gesture of [[rationality|rational]]ist philosophy, one that pragmatism fundamentally rejects:

> The Platonist treats our physical world of sight and touch, which we think the most real of all, as a mere illusion compared to the 'Ideas' of his metaphysical world. (008-083--008-085)

For the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]], the world of concrete experience is not an illusion to be transcended but the only world there is. [[truth|Truth]] is not discovered by turning away from experience toward abstract Ideas, but by engaging with experience and tracing the practical differences that ideas make within it.

## Relation to Pragmatism

Plato stands at the **origin** of the [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]] that pragmatism seeks to overcome, but also inadvertently points toward pragmatism's own solution.

On one hand, Plato initiated the [[rationality|rational]]ist quest for absolute, transcendent truth — a quest that, in [[william-james|James]]'s view, culminated in the "vain complications" of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] and the "futility" of [[the-absolute|Absolute Idealism]]. The Platonic Forms are the archetype for every subsequent attempt to locate truth outside of concrete human experience.

On the other hand, Plato's own dialogue — the _Theaetetus_ — demonstrated that the problem of knowledge cannot be solved within a Platonic framework. By staging the failure of every essentialist definition of knowledge, Plato unwittingly made the case for a pragmatic approach: if truth cannot be defined by correspondence to a static [[reality|real]]ity, perhaps it must be defined by its practical consequences.

The Protagorean position that Plato rejected — "man is the measure" — turns out, in the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]]'s hands, to be not a surrender to relativism but the starting point for a more honest and workable account of how truth actually functions in human life.

### Plato's Enduring Legacy

Beyond the immediate context of the _Theaetetus_, Plato's influence permeates the entire tradition that pragmatism inherits and challenges. Whitehead's famous remark that "the safest general characterization of the European philosophical tradition is that it consists of a series of footnotes to Plato" captures the depth of this influence. For James, Plato represents not merely an ancient interlocutor but the living source of the intellectualist habit — the conviction that truth is something eternal, static, and discovered by reason alone — that pragmatism must unlearn.

## Major Works

- _Theaetetus_ — Plato's sustained inquiry into the nature of knowledge, discussed by [[william-james|James]] via Schiller
- _Republic_ — Plato's most comprehensive work, presenting the theory of Forms and the philosopher-king
- _Phaedo_ — The immortality of the soul and the theory of Forms
- _Symposium_ — The nature of love and the ascent to the Form of Beauty
- _Meno_ — [[knowledge|Knowledge]] as recollection; the paradox of inquiry
- _Timaeus_ — Plato's cosmology, influential on later Platonism
