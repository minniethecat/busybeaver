## CHAPTER III

[003-001] WILL IN COGNITION

[003-002] The [[psychology|new psychology]] of [[william-james|James]] was bound to produce a new theory of

[003-003] knowledge, and though it did not actually explore this problem, it

[003-004] contained several valuable suggestions upon the subject. For instance,

[003-005] in a brief passage discussing 'The Relations of [[belief|Belief]] and Will,' James

[003-006] pointed out that belief is essentially an attitude of the will towards

[003-007] an idea, adding that in order to acquire a belief 'we need only in cold

[003-008] blood act as if the thing in question were [[reality|real]], and keep acting as if

[003-009] it were real, and it will infallibly end by growing into such a

[003-010] connection with our life that it will become real' (ii., p. 321). This

[003-011] passage is an outline of the doctrine of '[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]],' which he

[003-012] was afterwards to develop so forcibly.

[003-013] Again, in his last chapter, James criticized the doctrine of Spencer

[003-014] that all the principles of thought, all its general truths and axioms,

[003-015] were derived from impressions of the external world. He argued, on the

[003-016] other hand, that such ways of looking at phenomena must originate in the

[003-017] mind, and be prior to the experience which confirms them. Without

[003-018] digging further into the character of this mental contribution to

[003-019] knowledge, James contented himself with the suggestion that the use of

[003-020] these axiomatic principles might be construed in [[darwinism|Darwinian]] style as a

[003-021] 'variation' surviving by its fitness, thus introducing into his account

[003-022] of mental process the important idea that thinking might be tested by

[003-023] its vital value.

[003-024] What if knowledge be neither a dull submission to dictation from without

[003-025] nor an unexplained necessity of thought? What if it be a bold adventure,

[003-026] an experimental sally of a Will to live, to know and to control reality?

[003-027] What if its principles were frankly _risky_, and their truth had to be

[003-028] _desired_ before it was tested and assured? In a word, what if first

[003-029] principles were to begin with _[[postulation|postulates]]?_ Thus the way is paved from

[003-030] the new psychology to a new [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]. A third alternative to

[003-031] the banal dilemma of 'empiricism' or 'apriorism' suggests itself.

[003-032] The old _[[empiricism|empiricist]]_ view, as typified by Mill, was that the mind had

[003-033] been impressed with all its principles, such as the truths of

[003-034] arithmetic, the axioms of geometry, and the law of causation, by an

[003-035] uncontradicted course of experience, until it generalized facts into

[003-036] 'laws,' and was enabled to predict a similar future with certainty. But

[003-037] this theory had really been exploded in advance by [[david-hume|Hume]]. Facts do not

[003-038] _appear_ as causally connected, nor, if they did, would this guarantee

[003-039] that they will continue to do so in the future. The continuum of

[003-040] experience, we may add, is not _given_ as a series of arithmetical units

[003-041] or geometrical equalities, unless we deliberately measure it out in

[003-042] accordance with mathematical principles. Empiricism thus gives no real

[003-043] account of the scientific [[rationality|rational order]] of the world.

[003-044] But does it follow from the failure of empiricism that apriorism is

[003-045] true? This has always been assumed, and held to dispense rationalist

[003-046] philosophers from giving any direct and positive proof that these

[003-047] principles are _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ truths. But manifestly their procedure is

[003-048] logically far from cogent. If a third explanation can be thought of, it

[003-049] will _not_ follow that apriorism is true. All that follows is that

[003-050] _something_ has to be assumed before experience proves it. What that

[003-051] something is, and whence it comes, remains an open question. Moreover,

[003-052] apriorism has _not_ escaped from the empirical doubt about the future.

[003-053] Even granted that facts now conform to the necessities of our thoughts,

[003-054] why should they so comport themselves for ever?

[003-055] Let us, therefore, try a compromise, which ignores neither that which we

[003-056] bring to experience (like empiricism), nor that which we gain from

[003-057] experience (like apriorism). This compromise is effected by the doctrine

[003-058] of postulation. For though a postulate proceeds from us, and is meant to

[003-059] guide thought in anticipating facts, it yet allows the facts to test and

[003-060] mould it; so that its working modifies, expands, or restricts its

[003-061] demands, and fits it to meet the exigencies of experience, and permits,

[003-062] also, a certain reinterpretation of the previous 'facts' in order to

[003-063] conform them to the postulate.

[003-064] A postulate thus fully meets the demands of apriorism. It is 'universal'

[003-065] in claim, because it is convenient and economical to make a rule carry

[003-066] as far as it will go; and it is 'necessary,' because all fresh facts are

[003-067] on principle subjected to it, in the hope that they will support and

[003-068] illustrate it. Yet a postulate can never be accused of being a mere

[003-069] sophistication, or a bar to the progress of knowledge, because it is

[003-070] always willing to submit to verification in the course of fresh

[003-071] experience, and can always be reconstructed or abandoned, should it

[003-072] cease to edify. A long and successful course of service raises a

[003-073] postulate to the dignity of an 'axiom'--_i.e._, a principle which it is

[003-074] incredible anyone should think worth disputing--whereas repeated failure

[003-075] in application degrades it to the position of a prejudice--_i.e._, an _a

[003-076] priori_ opinion which is always belied by its consequences.

[003-077] A 'postulate' thus differs essentially from the '_a priori_ truth' by

[003-078] its dependence upon the will, by its being the product of a free choice.

[003-079] We have always to select the assumptions upon which we mean to act in

[003-080] our commerce with reality. We select the rules upon which we go, and we

[003-081] select the 'facts' by which we claim to support our rules, stripping

[003-082] them of all the 'irrelevant' details involved by their position in the

[003-083] flux of happenings. Thus we emphasize that side of things which fits in

[003-084] with our expectations, until the facts are 'faked' sufficiently to

[003-085] figure as 'cases' of our 'law.' Postulation and the verifying of

[003-086] postulates is thus a process of reciprocal discrimination and selection.

[003-087] The postulate once formulated, we seek in the flux for confirmations of

[003-088] it, and thus construct a system of 'facts' which are relative to it;

[003-089] that is how the postulate reacts upon experience. If, on the other hand,

[003-090] this process of selection is unfruitful, and the confirmations of our

[003-091] rule turn out infinitesimal, we alter the rule; and thus the 'facts' in

[003-092] the case reject the postulate.

[003-093] This continuous process of selection and rejection of 'principles' and

[003-094] 'facts' has, as we have said, a thoroughly _biological_ tinge. The

[003-095] fitness of a postulate to survive is being continually tested. It

[003-096] springs in the first place from a human hope that events may be

[003-097] systematized in a certain way, and it endures so long as it enables men

[003-098] to deal with them in that way. If it fails, the formation of fresh

[003-099] ideals and fresh [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] is demanded; but that which causes one

[003-100] postulate to prevail over another is always the satisfaction which, if

[003-101] successful, it promises to some need or desire. Thus 'thought' is

[003-102] everywhere inspired by 'will.' It is an _instrument_, the most potent

[003-103] man has found, whereby he brings about a harmony with his environment.

[003-104] This harmony is always something of a compromise. We postulate

[003-105] conformity between Nature and one of our ideals. We usually desire more

[003-106] than we can get, but insist on all that Nature can concede.

[003-107] Causation serves as a good example. [[experience|Experience]] as it first comes to us

[003-108] is a mere flood of happenings, with no distinction between causal and

[003-109] casual sequences. Clearly our whole ability to control our life, or even

[003-110] to continue it, demands that we should _predict_ what happens, and guide

[003-111] our actions accordingly. We therefore postulate a right to _dissect_

[003-112] the flux, to fit together selected series without reference to the

[003-113] rest. Thus, a systematic network of natural 'laws' is slowly knit

[003-114] together, and chaos visibly transforms itself into scientific order. The

[003-115] postulation of 'causes' is verified by its success. Moreover, it is to

[003-116] be noted that to this postulate there is no alternative. A belief that

[003-117] all events are casual would be scientifically worthless. So is a

[003-118] doctrine (still popular among philosophers) that the only true 'cause'

[003-119] is the total universe at one moment, the only true 'effect,' the whole

[003-120] of reality at the next. For that is merely to reinstate the given chaos

[003-121] science tried to analyse, and to forbid us to make selections from it.

[003-122] It would make prediction wholly vain, and entangle truth in a totality

[003-123] of things which is unique at every instant, and never can recur.

[003-124] The principles of mathematics are as clearly postulates. In Euclidean

[003-125] geometry we assume definitions of 'points,' 'lines,' 'surfaces,' etc.,

[003-126] which are never found in nature, but form the most convenient

[003-127] abstractions for measuring things. Both 'space' and 'time,' as defined

[003-128] for mathematical purposes, are ideal constructions drawn from empirical

[003-129] 'space' (extension) and 'time' (succession) feelings, and purged of the

[003-130] subjective variations of these experiences. Nevertheless, geometry

[003-131] forms the handiest system for applying to experience and calculating

[003-132] shapes and motions. But, ideally, other systems might be used. The

[003-133] 'metageometries' have constructed other ideal 'spaces' out of postulates

[003-134] differing from Euclid's, though when applied to real space their greater

[003-135] complexity destroys their value. The postulatory character of the

[003-136] arithmetical unit is quite as clear; for, in application, we always have

[003-137] to _agree_ as to what is to count as 'one'; if we agree to count apples,

[003-138] and count the two halves of an apple as each equalling one, we are said

[003-139] to be 'wrong,' though, if we were dividing the apple among two

[003-140] applicants, it would be quite right to treat each half as 'one' share.

[003-141] Again, though one penny added to another makes two, one drop of water

[003-142] added to another makes one, or a dozen, according as it is dropped.

[003-143] Common sense, therefore, admits that we may reckon variously, and that

[003-144] arithmetic does not _apply_ to _all_ things.

[003-145] Again, it is impossible to concede any meaning even to the central 'law

[003-146] of thought' itself--the Law of Identity ('A is A')--except as a

[003-147] postulate. Outside of [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]] and lunatic asylums no one wishes to

[003-148] assert that 'A is A.' All significant assertion takes the form 'A is B.'

[003-149] But A and B are _different_, and, indeed, no two 'A's' are ever _quite_

[003-150] the same. Hence, when we assert either the 'identity' of 'A' in two

[003-151] contexts, or that of 'A' and 'B,' in 'A is B,' we are clearly _ignoring

[003-152] differences which really exist--i.e._, we postulate that in spite of

[003-153] these differences A and B will for our purposes behave as if they were

[003-154] one ('identical'). And we should realize that this postulate is of our

[003-155] making, and involves a risk. It may be that experience refuses to

[003-156] confirm it, and convicts us instead of a 'mistaken identity.' In short,

[003-157] _every identity we reason from is made by our postulating an irrelevance

[003-158] of differences_.

[003-159] There is thus, perhaps, no fundamental procedure of thought in which we

[003-160] cannot trace some deliberately adopted attitude. We distinguish between

[003-161] 'ourselves' and the 'external' world, perhaps because we have more

[003-162] control over our thoughts and limbs, and less, or none, over sticks and

[003-163] stones and mountains; fundamental as it is, it is a distinction _within_

[003-164] experience, and is not given ready-made, but elaborated in the course of

[003-165] our dealings with it. Similarly, in accordance with its varying degrees

[003-166] of vividness, continuity, and value, experience itself gets sorted into

[003-167] 'realities,' 'dreams,' and 'hallucinations.' In short, when the

[003-168] processes of discriminating between 'dreams' and 'reality' are

[003-169] considered, all these distinctions will ultimately be found to be

[003-170] judgments of value.

[003-171] Nor is it only in the realm of scientific knowing that postulation

[003-172] reveals itself as a practicable and successful method of anticipating

[003-173] experience and consolidating fact. The same method has always been

[003-174] employed by man in reaching out towards the final syntheses which (in

[003-175] imagination) complete his vision of reality. The 'truths' of all

[003-176] religions originate in postulates. 'Gods' and 'devils,' 'heavens' and

[003-177] 'hells,' are essentially demands for a moral order in experience which

[003-178] transcend the given. The value of the actual world is supplemented and

[003-179] enhanced by being conceived as projected and continued into a greater,

[003-180] and our postulates are verified by the salutary influence they exercise

[003-181] on our earthly life. Both postulation and verification, then, are

[003-182] applicable to the problems of [[religious-experience|religion]] as of science. This is the

[003-183] meaning of the Will to Believe. When James first defined and defended

[003-184] it, it provoked abundant protest, on the ground that it allowed everyone

[003-185] to believe whatever he pleased and to call it 'true.' The critics had

[003-186] simply failed to see that [[verification|verification by experience]] is just as integral

[003-187] a part of voluntaristic procedure as experimental postulation, and that

[003-188] James himself had from the first asserted this. Indeed, that he had

[003-189] first given a theological illustration of the function of volition in

[003-190] knowing was merely an accident. But that the will to believe was capable

[003-191] of being generalized into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge was soon

[003-192] shown in [[fcs-schiller|Dr. Schiller]]'s _[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]_.
