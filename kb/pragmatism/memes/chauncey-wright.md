## Overview

Chauncey Wright (1830–1875) was an American philosopher and mathematician, remembered as the "Socrates of Cambridge" for his conversational brilliance and his role in the [[metaphysical-club|Metaphysical Club]], the informal discussion group where [[pragmatism|pragmatism]] was born. A committed empiricist and Darwinian, Wright argued that metaphysical speculation should be disciplined by the methods of natural science.

## Role in Early Pragmatism

Wright's most significant contribution to pragmatism was his critique of [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]]'s evolutionary philosophy. Wright accepted [[charles-darwin|Darwinian]] evolution but rejected Spencer's attempt to turn it into a universal metaphysical principle. His careful distinction between scientific explanation and metaphysical speculation influenced [[charles-sanders-peirce|Peirce]]'s formulation of the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic maxim]] and [[william-james|James]]'s empiricist temperament.

Wright also defended the view that consciousness is a natural phenomenon subject to scientific investigation — a position that [[william-james|James]] developed at length in _[[principles-of-psychology|The Principles of Psychology]]_. His essay "The Evolution of Self-Consciousness" (1873) was a pioneering attempt to give a naturalistic account of the mind.
