## Definition

Humanism, in the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] sense introduced by [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]], is the philosophical doctrine that all knowledge and truth are human artifacts — products of human purposes, valuations, and selective interests rather than copies of an independently existing [[reality|real]]ity "in itself." It transitions from pragmatism's narrower epistemological question into "a wider outlook, from which we can survey the place of [[knowledge|knowing]] in human life at large" (008-029). [[william-james|James]] presents it as pragmatism's natural extension: "This may be called the transition from [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] to Humanism" (008-029).

## Origin & Context

The term "Humanism" was "introduced into philosophic terminology by [[fcs-schiller|Dr. Schiller]] in order to describe his general philosophical position as distinct from the original question of the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]], which had been treated by [[william-james|James]] under the name of [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]" (008-030--008-033). Schiller, whom [[william-james|James]] credits with showing that "already in the days of Plato the distinction between 'truth' and 'error' was baffling philosophy" (001-117), argued that Protagoras's dictum "Man is the measure of all things" foreshadows "the 'Pragmatic,' or, as he calls it, the 'Humanist,' solution of the difficulty" (001-121). Schiller also argued that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] failed to answer [[david-hume|Hume]] and that "no *[[apriorism|a priori]]* philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine" (001-126).

> To the Humanist the best definition of life is one which displays it as throughout purposive, as a rational pursuit of ends. (008-034--008-035)

The key works in which Schiller developed Humanism were *Humanism: Philosophical Essays* (1903) and *[[studies-in-humanism|Studies in Humanism]]* (1907) (008-152--008-153). [[william-james|James]] devoted Lecture VII, "[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and Humanism," and Lecture VIII, "[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[religion|Religion]]," in large part to expounding the Humanist position as the capstone of the pragmatic outlook.

## Key Arguments

1. **[[truth|Truth]] Is Human**: The humanist contention complements the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic maxim]]. While pragmatism holds that "[[truth|Truth]] is practical," Humanism adds that "[[truth|Truth]] is human" — it "rescues man from the despondency in which his failure to grasp [[absolutism|absolute truth]] had left him" (007-052--007-053). The Protagorean dictum that "Man is the measure of all things" assures human beings that their knowledge may become adequate to their [[reality|real]]ity (007-054--007-055).

> The pragmatic notion that _Truth is practical_ closes the artificial gulf between the theoretic and the practical side of life, and assigns to truth a biological function and vital value. The humanist contention that _Truth is human_ rescues man from the despondency in which his failure to grasp absolute truth had left him. (007-050--007-053)

2. **[[reality|Reality]] Is Always a Selection**: Humanism refuses to treat the world as "a given and completed whole" (008-100). What we call [[reality|the real]] world is always "a selection from a larger whole from which we have ventured to exclude great masses of irrelevance" (008-101--008-103). Every thinker chooses what counts as [[reality|real]] based on purposes and values — the deliberateness of this selection is masked by habit in ordinary life, but it becomes visible in philosophy.

> For good or ill, the world with which the Humanist contends is always a world that reveals itself to him. Reality, as it is assumed, presumed, or guessed to be 'in itself,' apart from our experience of it, is cancelled from his reckonings. (008-113--008-116)

> Human experience does not warrant such gratuitous demands. It confirms neither the rigid system of unchanging fact which realism postulates (seeing that the only facts that science speaks of are ever changing in its progress), nor finds its problems, conflicts, and errors credible as a reflexion of any Universal Mind, unless Idealism ultimately repudiates the sanity of its Absolute. (008-121--008-126)

3. **The World Is Plastic and Improvable**: Humanism's most distinctive practical consequence is its rejection of the assumption that [[reality|real]]ity is "completely rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort" (008-128). Instead, "our world is plastic, it is most '[[reality|real]]ly' what we can make of it, and the process of our making is not ended" (008-108--008-109). This is neither an Optimism nor a Pessimism — both of which, in their extreme forms, "deny that [[reality|real]]ity can be improved" — but "concedes to man the right and duty to improve the world" (008-133--008-134).

> The superiority of Humanism, then, lies in this, that it does not discourage human enterprise by assuming that the real is completely rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort. In the drama that unrolls reality, every man, it teaches, has a duty and a power to play his humble but essential part. (008-127--008-131)

> Humanism is neither an Optimism nor a Pessimism — both of which must consistently, in their extreme form, deny that reality can be improved — but concedes to man the right and duty to improve the world. It impresses us with the necessity of acting, it vindicates the procedure of acting on our hopes, it shows us how we may correct our errors, and so gives reasons for our faith in the possibility of Progress. (008-131--008-137)

4. **[[truth|Truth]] Is a Form of Valuation**: Humanism treats truth-values as one species of valuation among many. As [[william-james|James]] (via Schiller) explains, in conduct actions are judged "good" or "evil" and "right" or "wrong"; "in thinking, ideas are 'true' or 'false,' and 'relevant' or 'irrelevant'" (008-042--008-043). All these adjectives "either denotes value or censures lack of worth, and each gets its meaning by reference to the specific purpose, moral, aesthetic, or intellectual, it appeals to" (008-045--008-047). The *summum bonum* is then "the ideal of the harmonious satisfaction of all purposes" (008-048--008-049).

> What, then, from the standpoint of Humanism, is the function of 'truth-values' in our life? They indicate a relation to the cognitive end. What is this end? Surely not self-sufficing? A truth that is merely true in itself has no interest for human life, and no human mind has an interest in discovering and affirming it. Truth, therefore, cannot stand aloof from life. It must somehow subserve our vital purposes. (008-050--008-055)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Schiller's Original Humanism**: Schiller developed Humanism as a comprehensive philosophical position that goes beyond the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]. His works *Humanism: Philosophical Essays* (1903) and *[[studies-in-humanism|Studies in Humanism]]* (1907) present truth as a human product, logic as a social and scientific problem, and the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] as the universal [[pragmatic-method|method of testing]] all assertions (008-152--008-153). Schiller traced the humanist impulse back to Protagoras and argued that "Man is the measure of all things" was the earliest expression of the pragmatic outlook (001-120--001-121).

- **[[william-james|James]]'s Religious Humanism**: In [[william-james|James]]'s hands, Humanism becomes the philosophical foundation for the right to believe. By treating [[reality|real]]ity as plastic rather than fixed, Humanism opens the door to meliorism — the view that human effort can genuinely improve the world. [[william-james|James]] connects this to religious [[religious-experience|faith]]: if our world is "most '[[reality|real]]ly' what we can make of it" (008-108), then acting on religious hope becomes a [[rationality|rational]] wager rather than wishful thinking.

- **Humanism as Anti-[[absolutism|Absolutism]]**: Humanism functions as the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative to the Absolute. Where absolutism posits a completed, perfect [[reality|real]]ity beyond human experience, Humanism insists that "he cannot discover how he (or anyone) can get any 'knowledge' or 'intuition' which transcends all human faculties" (008-116--008-118). The metaphysician's claims about [[reality|real]]ity-in-itself are "personal [[postulation|postulates]] which, in so far as they cannot be subjected to the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]], must remain open questions" (008-119--008-120).

## Criticisms & Debates

A perennial objection to Humanism is that it collapses into subjectivism or relativism — if all truth is human truth, is there any check on wishful thinking? [[william-james|James]] and Schiller respond that the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]] remains objective: ideas must work in "a world of fact" and prove their value through consequences. Humanism does not mean "we are free to assert the truth of every idea which seems to us pretty or pleasant" (005-187--005-189). The test of practical working provides a genuine, if fallible, constraint.

Critics also charge that Humanism's refusal to acknowledge [[reality|real]]ity-in-itself leaves it unable to explain the stubbornness of the world — the way [[reality|real]]ity resists our purposes. The Humanist replies that this very resistance is part of "the world that reveals itself" to us (008-113--008-114) and that our knowledge of it is precisely the product of coping with that resistance.

A more specific criticism targets Humanism's theory of truth as valuation. If truth is one value among many, how do we adjudicate conflicts between truth and other values (e.g., between a comforting falsehood and a distressing truth)? The Humanist response is that the *summum bonum* is "the harmonious satisfaction of all purposes" (008-048--008-049), but this ideal of harmony remains aspi[[rationality|rational]] rather than systematically articulated.
