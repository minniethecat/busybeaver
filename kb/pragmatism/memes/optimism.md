## Definition

Optimism, in the philosophical sense, is the doctrine that [[reality|real]]ity is fundamentally good — that this world, despite its apparent evils, is the best possible world, or at least that good outweighs evil in the cosmic balance. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, optimism is the characteristic metaphysical stance of the tender-minded temperament: the conviction that the universe is friendly, that things ultimately work out for the best, and that evil is somehow reconciled in the larger whole. [[william-james|James]] treats optimism with sympathy but also with pragmatist skepticism: an optimism that has no practical consequences — that does not motivate concrete action — is pragmatically meaningless.

## Origin & Context

Philosophical optimism received its classic formulation in Leibniz's _Theodicy_ (1710), which argued that this is the "best of all possible worlds" — that God, being all-good and all-powerful, must have created the best world that was logically possible. Voltaire's _Candide_ (1759) satirized this position mercilessly, but optimism persisted in more subtle forms, particularly in the absolute idealism of Hegel and his followers.

In [[william-james|James]]'s intellectual context, optimism was the dominant tone of absolute idealism: the conviction that all partial evils are reconciled in the Absolute Whole was, in effect, a sophisticated philosophical optimism. [[william-james|James]]'s [[meliorism|meliorism]] — the view that the world is neither wholly good nor wholly bad, and that human action can make it better — is his pragmatic alternative to both abstract optimism and abstract [[pessimism|pessimism]].

## Key Arguments

### Optimism in the Temperament Typology

[[william-james|James]] places optimism at the tender-minded pole of the philosophical temperament spectrum:

| Temperament | View of the World | Practical Attitude |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Tender-minded (optimist) | The world is fundamentally good; evil is ultimately reconciled | Trust, contemplation, moral holidays |
| Tough-minded ([[pessimism|pessimist]]) | The world is fundamentally bad; good is fleeting | Resignation or struggle |
| Pragmatist ([[meliorism|meliorist]]) | The world is a mix of good and evil; human action can tip the balance | Active engagement, moral effort |

### The Pragmatic Test of Optimism

[[william-james|James]] applies the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]]: what practical difference does optimism make? If optimism encourages passivity — "everything will work out regardless of what I do" — it is pragmatically harmful. If optimism motivates constructive action — "things can be made better" — it shades into [[meliorism|meliorism]] and is pragmatically valuable. The content of the optimism, not the label, determines its pragmatic value.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Leibnizian optimism**: The metaphysical claim that this is the best of all possible worlds.
- **Panglossian optimism**: The naive refusal to acknowledge the reality of evil; the target of Voltaire's satire.
- **Melioristic optimism**: The belief that things can be improved through human effort. James's preferred position.
- **Pragmatic optimism**: The working assumption that effort is worthwhile — a postulate, not a metaphysical claim.
