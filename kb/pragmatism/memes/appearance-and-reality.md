## Overview

_Appearance and Reality: A Metaphysical Essay_ (1893) is the major work of British idealist philosopher [[fh-bradley|F. H. Bradley]]. It is the classic statement of Absolute Idealism — the view that ordinary experience is riddled with contradictions and that only the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], a unified, non-relational whole, is truly real.

## Central Arguments

Bradley's method is dialectical: he examines fundamental concepts — substance, quality, relation, space, time, causation, the self — and argues that each, when pressed, leads to contradiction. Ordinary things are "appearances" because they cannot be consistently thought. Yet Bradley does not conclude that nothing is real; rather, reality must be something that transcends the contradictions of ordinary thought — the Absolute, a harmonious, all-embracing experience in which all distinctions are reconciled.

## Relevance to Pragmatism

_Appearance and Reality_ was published just six years before [[william-james|William James]] delivered the lectures that became _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, and it represents the [[idealism|idealist]]-[[rationalism|rationalist]] position that [[william-james|James]] is most concerned to refute. For [[william-james|James]], Bradley's Absolute is the paradigmatic example of a philosophical concept that makes no practical difference: if all is already harmonized in the Absolute, then nothing we do matters.

The pragmatist alternative — that [[truth|truth]] is made, not found, and that [[reality|reality]] is not a completed whole but an ongoing, pluralistic process — is developed in direct opposition to Bradley's monistic absolutism. The debate between [[william-james|James]] and Bradley defined the central metaphysical dispute of the era.
