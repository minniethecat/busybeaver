## Definition

Mysticism, in [[william-james|William James]]'s psychological and pragmatic treatment, refers to direct, personal experiences of union with or consciousness of a divine or ultimate reality — experiences characterized by ineffability, noetic quality (a sense of knowledge), transiency, and passivity. In his Gifford Lectures, published as _[[the-varieties-of-religious-experience|The Varieties of Religious Experience]]_ (1902), [[william-james|James]] treats mystical experiences not as pathological episodes or as proofs of dogma but as empirical data — genuine experiences whose truth is to be judged pragmatically by their fruits in the lives of those who have them.

## Origin & Context

Mysticism has been a feature of every major religious tradition — from the Upanishads and Buddhist meditation to Sufi poetry, Christian contemplative practice, and Jewish Kabbalah. [[william-james|James]] drew particularly on Christian mystics (St. Teresa of Avila, St. John of the Cross) and on the emerging psychological literature on altered states of consciousness, which he studied with the empirical rigor of a scientist.

[[william-james|James]]'s treatment of mysticism is distinctive: he neither dismisses it as superstition nor endorses it as revelation. Instead, he applies the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]]. Mystical experiences are real experiences with real consequences, and their truth is measured by those consequences — are they life-enhancing? Do they produce moral improvement? Do they "work" in the ongoing course of experience?

## Key Arguments

### The Four Marks of Mystical Experience

[[william-james|James]] identified four characteristics that mark mystical states:

1. **Ineffability**: The experience defies expression — it cannot be adequately conveyed in words to those who have not had it.
2. **Noetic quality**: Mystical states are states of knowledge — they seem to those who have them to reveal truths inaccessible to the discursive intellect.
3. **Transiency**: Mystical states rarely last more than an hour or two and cannot be sustained at will.
4. **Passivity**: The mystic feels grasped by a superior power — the experience is something that happens *to* them, not something they produce.

### The Pragmatic Assessment

From a pragmatic standpoint, mystical experiences are validated not by their origins (which may be purely psychological) but by their consequences: do they make people better? Happier? More morally engaged? [[william-james|James]]'s answer is that they often do, and that this pragmatic validation gives mystical experience genuine cognitive authority, even if it does not establish any particular theological interpretation of the experience.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Theistic mysticism**: Experiencing union with a personal God. The dominant form in Western traditions.
- **Monistic mysticism**: Experiencing identity with an impersonal Absolute or cosmic consciousness. More common in Eastern traditions.
- **Nature mysticism**: Experiencing unity with the natural world.
- **Pragmatic mysticism**: James's approach — mysticism assessed by its practical fruits rather than its doctrinal claims.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics argue that the pragmatic criterion cannot distinguish between genuinely veridical mystical experiences and comforting delusions. [[william-james|James]] replies that this is true of *all* experience — we never have a guarantee of veridicality independent of ongoing verification. The mystic, like the scientist, tests their experiences by their coherence with the rest of experience over time.
