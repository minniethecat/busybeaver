## Overview

Francis Herbert Bradley (1846–1924) was the leading figure of British Idealism and author of _Appearance and Reality_ (1893), the most influential statement of absolute idealism in the English-speaking world. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Bradley is the implied antagonist — the philosopher whose absolute monism represents everything that pragmatism's [[pluralism|pluralistic]] empiricism opposes. Though [[william-james|James]] rarely names Bradley directly, the "Absolute" that [[william-james|James]] subjects to pragmatic critique is substantially Bradley's Absolute.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1846** — Born in Clapham, London, son of an evangelical preacher.
- **1865** — Entered University College, Oxford.
- **1870** — Elected to a fellowship at Merton College, Oxford, where he lived in scholarly seclusion for the rest of his life.
- **1876** — Published _Ethical Studies_, establishing his reputation.
- **1893** — Published _Appearance and Reality_, his metaphysical magnum opus.
- **1924** — Died in Oxford; awarded the Order of Merit, the first philosopher so honored.

## Key Contributions

Bradley's _Appearance and Reality_ argued that the world of ordinary experience — with its particular things, relations, space, time, and selves — is mere "appearance." True reality is a single, seamless, supra-relational Absolute in which all distinctions are reconciled. This was the purest and most rigorous statement of the metaphysical position that [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism was designed to overcome.

Bradley also developed a distinctive ethical philosophy (in _Ethical Studies_) centered on "My Station and Its Duties" — the view that moral obligations derive from one's social role — which anticipates some communitarian themes later developed by [[john-dewey|John Dewey]] and others.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

Bradley and [[william-james|James]] stand at opposite poles of late nineteenth-century philosophy. Where [[william-james|James]] found meaning in particular, concrete experiences, Bradley found it only in the Absolute Whole. Where [[william-james|James]] celebrated the plural, the unfinished, and the possible, Bradley insisted that only the complete, unified, and actual is real. Their opposition defines the central tension of the era.
