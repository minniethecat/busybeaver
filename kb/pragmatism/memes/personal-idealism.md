## Overview

_Personal Idealism_ (1902), edited by Henry Sturt, is a collection of philosophical essays that marked a significant turning point in British philosophy at the beginning of the twentieth century. The volume brought together thinkers who were dissatisfied with [[the-absolute|Absolute Idealism]] — then the dominant school in British philosophy — and sought to develop alternatives that emphasized the personal, the volitional, and the practical dimensions of human knowledge. Its most historically significant contribution was [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]]'s essay "[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]," which [[william-james|William James]] recognized as a foundational text of voluntarist epistemology.

[[william-james|James]] references _Personal Idealism_ in the final lecture of _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ as part of his survey of the Humanist literature that had grown up alongside the pragmatic movement:

> _Axioms as Postulates_ (in _Personal Idealism_, ed. Henry Sturt, 1902). (008-149--008-150)

A related volume appeared a few years later:

> _The Standpoint of Personal Idealism_, 1906. (008-157)

## Summary

The essays in _Personal Idealism_ collectively argued against the Absolute Idealist tendency to dissolve the individual person into an abstract universal consciousness. Against this, the contributors emphasized the irreducible [[reality|real]]ity of the personal self, the role of volition in knowledge, and the practical grounding of philosophical concepts in human experience. The volume provided an institutional and intellectual platform for what would become, in Schiller's hands, the doctrine of [[humanism|Humanism]] — and, in [[william-james|James]]'s, the philosophy of [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]].

Schiller's contribution, "[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]," was the collection's most influential essay. It argued that even the most basic logical principles — such as the Law of Identity — are not necessary truths discovered by pure [[rationality|reason]], but [[postulation|postulates]] adopted because of their practical utility. [[william-james|James]] saw this as the crucial generalization of his own "will to believe" argument into a full voluntarist [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] (003-191--003-192).

## Key Contributions

1. **Challenge to [[the-absolute|Absolute Idealism]]**: The volume provided a collective statement from younger British philosophers who rejected the monistic metaphysics of F.H. Bradley and Bernard Bosanquet, arguing instead for a pluralistic universe grounded in personal experience.

2. **Platform for voluntarist epistemology**: Schiller's "[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]" used the collection to launch the argument that all knowledge involves volition — a position that became central to both [[humanism|Humanism]] and [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]].

3. **Bridge between idealism and pragmatism**: _Personal Idealism_ represented a transitional moment in British philosophy, preserving the idealist emphasis on mind and experience while redirecting it toward the practical, personal, and purposive dimensions of human life that pragmatism would foreground.

## Reception & Influence

_Personal Idealism_ helped create the intellectual space within which pragmatism and [[humanism|Humanism]] could find a British audience. The volume's contributors, particularly Schiller, would go on to develop the personalist and [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] themes first articulated here into mature philosophical positions. [[william-james|James]]'s acknowledgment of the volume in Lecture VIII of _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ reflects his recognition of the British Humanist movement as a parallel and complementary development to American pragmatism.
