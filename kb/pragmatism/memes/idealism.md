## Definition

Idealism is the metaphysical doctrine that reality is fundamentally mental, spiritual, or constituted by ideas rather than material substances. In the context of [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, idealism — particularly in its "absolute" form — represents the tender-minded philosophical temperament taken to its logical extreme: a universe conceived as unified, rational, and governed by an all-encompassing [[the-absolute|Absolute]] Mind. [[william-james|James]] treats idealism not as false but as a one-sided abstraction that pragmatism must mediate against, balancing its spiritual insights with the tough-minded empiricist demand for concrete, verifiable consequences.

> The idealistic theories of the 'Absolute' reveal their practical meaning only when we translate them into the concrete differences they make in human experience. (001-127)

## Origin & Context

Idealism has deep roots in Western philosophy. [[plato|Plato]]'s theory of Forms established the archetypal idealist framework: true reality lies not in the changing world of sensory experience but in an eternal, intelligible realm of ideal archetypes. This thread was developed through medieval philosophy and received its modern formulation in the work of [[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]], who argued that the mind actively structures experience through [[apriorism|a priori]] categories rather than passively receiving sensory data.

In the late nineteenth century, when [[william-james|James]] was developing his pragmatism, absolute idealism — particularly the Hegelian variety championed by [[fh-bradley|F.H. Bradley]] and [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]] — dominated Anglo-American philosophy. [[william-james|James]] encountered idealism not as a historical curiosity but as the living philosophical establishment against which pragmatism had to define itself. His critique is respectful but decisive: idealism captures genuine human needs for unity and spiritual meaning, but it does so at the cost of disconnecting from concrete, particular experiences.

## Key Arguments

### The Pragmatic Critique of Idealism

[[william-james|James]] does not argue that idealism is logically false. His pragmatic method asks instead: what practical difference does it make to believe in an Absolute? His answer is nuanced:

1. **The holiday-taking function**: Belief in the Absolute provides psychological comfort — it allows one to take "moral holidays" by trusting that all particular evils are somehow reconciled in the larger whole. This is a real practical consequence and therefore, from a pragmatic standpoint, gives the Absolute genuine meaning.

2. **But no concrete guidance**: The Absolute provides no guidance for navigating specific situations. It "runs on all fours with the vulgarest superstition" in that both make claims that have no verifiable consequences for particular experiences.

3. **Idealism as one-sided abstraction**: [[william-james|James]]'s deepest criticism is that idealism abstracts one aspect of experience — its continuity, unity, and rational structure — and treats this abstraction as the whole of reality, neglecting the discontinuous, plural, and irrational aspects of lived experience.

### Idealism in James's Typology of Temperaments

[[william-james|James]] famously categorizes philosophical temperaments along a spectrum:

| Pole | Temperament | Key Traits | Philosophical Expression |
|------|------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Tender-minded | Rationalistic | Intellectualistic, idealistic, optimistic, religious, free-willist, monistic, dogmatical | Absolute idealism |
| Tough-minded | Empiricist | Sensationalistic, materialistic, pessimistic, irreligious, fatalistic, pluralistic, skeptical | Classical empiricism |

Idealism represents the tender-minded pole in its purest form. [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism is offered as a *mediating* philosophy that can honor the spiritual insights of idealism while remaining accountable to the empiricist demand for concrete verification.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Platonic Idealism**: The view that ultimate reality consists of eternal, immaterial Forms. [[plato|Plato]]'s theory provides the archetype for all subsequent idealisms: the conviction that the world of sensory appearance is less real than an intelligible order beyond it.

- **Kantian Transcendental Idealism**: [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s view that the mind imposes [[apriorism|a priori]] categories (space, time, causality) on experience, such that we can never know things as they are in themselves — only as they appear to us through these structuring forms. [[william-james|James]] treats Kant as a crucial precursor who recognized the active role of mind but who retained an intellectualist commitment to [[apriorism|a priori]] necessities.

- **Absolute Idealism (Hegelian)**: The view that all of reality is the self-manifestation of an Absolute Spirit or Mind. In its Anglo-American form ([[fh-bradley|F.H. Bradley]], [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]]), this was the dominant philosophical position that [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism directly challenged.

- **Personal Idealism**: A more moderate idealism, represented in the volume _[[personal-idealism|Personal Idealism]]_ (1902, ed. Henry Sturt), which insisted that reality is ultimately personal — constituted by individual minds — rather than absorbed into a single Absolute. This form of idealism was more congenial to [[william-james|James]]'s pluralistic sympathies.

## Criticisms & Debates

The pragmatist critique of idealism centers on the question of verification: if the Absolute has no concrete, verifiable consequences for particular experiences, then what does it mean to say it exists? [[william-james|James]]'s answer is characteristically diplomatic — the Absolute *does* have practical consequences (emotional comfort, moral holidays) and therefore *does* have meaning — but these consequences are insufficient to make it a *necessary* postulate of thought. The pragmatist can acknowledge the spiritual appeal of idealism while insisting on a more pluralistic, open-ended account of [[reality|real]]ity.

Critics of pragmatism, in turn, charged that [[william-james|James]]'s instrumental account of [[truth|truth]] collapsed into a form of subjective idealism — the very position he claimed to be mediating against. The debate between pragmatic and idealist accounts of truth and reality remains a living tension in contemporary philosophy.
