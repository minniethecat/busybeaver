## Overview

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831) was the culminating figure of German idealism, whose philosophy of Absolute Spirit — the view that all of reality is the self-manifestation of a single, rational, developing Mind — dominated nineteenth-century philosophy. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Hegel (and the Hegelian tradition represented by [[fh-bradley|F.H. Bradley]] and [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]]) is the primary philosophical adversary: pragmatism's [[pluralism|pluralistic]], empiricist, forward-looking orientation is defined in explicit opposition to Hegelian absolutism's monistic, rationalist, backward-looking system.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1770** — Born in Stuttgart, Germany.
- **1801** — Began teaching at the University of Jena alongside Schelling.
- **1807** — Published _Phenomenology of Spirit_, his most influential work.
- **1812–1816** — Published _Science of Logic_.
- **1818** — Appointed professor at the University of Berlin, where he achieved intellectual dominance.
- **1831** — Died of cholera in Berlin.

## Key Contributions

Hegel's system is built on the idea that reality is a single, rational process — Geist (Spirit or Mind) — that develops through a dialectical movement of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis toward ever-greater self-consciousness. Everything — nature, history, art, religion, philosophy — is a moment in this unfolding of Absolute Spirit.

In the English-speaking world, Hegel's influence was transmitted through the British Idealists — [[fh-bradley|F.H. Bradley]], [[th-green|T.H. Green]], Edward Caird — and through [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]] in America. It was this tradition that [[william-james|James]] encountered as the philosophical establishment against which pragmatism had to define itself.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s critique of Hegel is the pragmatic method in action: what practical difference does the Hegelian Absolute make? The answer — comfort, a sense of ultimate reconciliation, the ability to take "moral holidays" — is real but insufficient. Hegelianism purchases unity at the price of neglecting the genuinely unfinished, loose, and plural character of experience.
