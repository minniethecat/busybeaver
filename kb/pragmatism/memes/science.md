## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]], science is reconceived as a human activity whose "laws" are not transcriptions of an eternal divine order but working instruments — "working formulae for scientific purposes" (001-080) — that enable prediction and control of experience. Science is fundamentally "a policy and not a creed" (001-083), a method of inquiry that succeeds not because it mirrors an independent [[reality|real]]ity but because it yields "a convenient means of mastering the flux of events" (001-087). Its authority derives from practical consequences — the ability to anticipate and consolidate experience — not from any claim to absolute finality.

## Origin & Context

[[william-james|James]] developed his pragmatic conception of science against the backdrop of a r[[darwinism|evolution]]ary transformation in the late-nineteenth-century scientific worldview. The Newtonian paradigm, which for two centuries had seemed to present the immutable laws of nature, was being unsettled by new developments in physics, mathematics, and biology. [[william-james|James]] recognized that this upheaval carried profound philosophical implications: if even the most secure scientific laws could be revised, then the intellectualist model of science as the discovery of eternal verities was untenable.

> The immense growth of scientific knowledge during the last century was bound to react on human conceptions of scientific procedure. The enormous number of new facts brought to light by manipulating hypotheses could not but modify our view of scientific law. Laws no longer seem to scientists the immutable foundations of an eternal order, but are inevitably treated as man-made formulae for grouping and predicting the events which verify them. (001-066–001-072)

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] treatment of science thus emerges from a historical observation: the actual practice of working scientists had already abandoned the intellectualist ideal of fixed, final laws. Physicists like Mach, Duhem, and Ostwald (001-073) were exploring alternative formulations of even the best-established laws, and Newtonian physics was no longer final (001-074). [[william-james|James]] seized on this development as confirmation that the pragmatic account of knowledge — as provisional, instrumental, and validated by consequences — accorded with the actual conduct of the most successful human inquiry.

> Dogmatic confidence in the finality of scientific laws has given place to a belief that our "laws" are only working formulae for scientific purposes, and that no science can truly boast of having read off the mind of the Deity. (001-079–001-081)

## Key Arguments

1. **Scientific Laws as Human Instruments, Not Transcendent [[truth|Truth]]s**: [[william-james|James]] insists that scientific laws are "man-made formulae for grouping and predicting the events which verify them" (001-072). They are not discovered as pre-existing features of [[reality|real]]ity but constructed as tools for organizing the flux of experience. Even mathematics, long regarded as the paradigm of absolute knowledge, "has not escaped the stigma of relativity" (001-088), with the invention of non-Euclidean geometries demonstrating that its axioms are human posits, not metaphysical necessities.

> As Sir J.J. Thomson neatly puts it, a scientific theory, for the enlightened modern scientist, is a 'policy and not a creed.' Science has become content to be only 'a conceptual shorthand,' provided that its message be humanly intelligible. It no longer claims truth because abstractly and absolutely it 'corresponds with Nature,' but because it yields a convenient means of mastering the flux of events. (001-082–001-087)

2. **Science as the Selective Organization of [[experience|Experience]]**: Science does not passively record the world as it is. It actively selects, abstracts, and systematizes. [[william-james|James]] argues that the postulation of "causes" is verified by its success in knitting together a "systematic network of natural laws" out of the chaotic flux of experience (003-114). A belief that all events are merely casual "would be scientifically worthless" (003-117); science works precisely because it imposes selective patterns on the chaos.

> Thus, a systematic network of natural 'laws' is slowly knit together, and chaos visibly transforms itself into scientific order. The postulation of 'causes' is verified by its success. (003-114–003-115)

3. **The Unity of Scientific and Religious Method**: [[william-james|James]] draws a striking parallel between scientific and religious thinking. Both proceed by postulation and verification — the same method that reveals itself as "a practicable and successful method of anticipating experience and consolidating fact" in science (003-172–003-173) has also "always been employed by man in reaching out towards the final syntheses which (in imagination) complete his vision of [[reality|real]]ity" (003-174–003-175). This is not to reduce [[religious-experience|religion]] to science, but to recognize that both are modes of the same fundamental human enterprise of making experience meaningful through the testing of [[postulation|postulates]] by their vital consequences.

> Both postulation and verification, then, are applicable to the problems of religion as of science. This is the meaning of the Will to Believe. (003-182–003-183)

4. **Scientific [[truth|Truth]] as Continuously Corrected [[hypothesis|Hypothesis]]**: [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] appeals to the actual history of science to confirm its view that truth is not a static property but an evolving process. The history of science reveals "a continuous correction of 'truths,' which were re-valued as 'errors,' as better statements for them became available" (005-072–005-073). What we call scientific truth is simply the best-[[hypothesis|working hypothesis]] currently available, always subject to revision by further experience.

> For a confirmation of this doctrine Pragmatism appeals to the history of scientific truth, which has shown a continuous correction of 'truths,' which were re-valued as 'errors,' as better statements for them became available. (005-072–005-074)

5. **The Insufficiency of [[logic|Formal Logic]] for Scientific Thinking**: [[william-james|James]] launches a sharp critique of the intellectualist attempt to ground science in formal logic. When logic treats forms of thought "in abstraction from their matter" (006-007), it becomes "a game with verbal symbols which is entirely irrelevant to scientific thinking" (006-019). Genuine scientific [[rationality|reason]]ing is always contextual, always tied to particular consequences in experience, and cannot be reduced to formal validity alone.

> This 'science,' therefore, finally reduces to mere verbalism, distracted by inconsistent relapses into 'psychology.' But will this conception of Logic either work out consistently in itself or lead to a tenable theory of scientific thinking? Emphatically not. (006-041–006-044)

6. **Science as Purposive Activity**: Scientific inquiry, like all human thinking, is guided by purpose. Whether "abstracting, classifying, and predicting (as in science), it is always the fact that a particular kind of [[reality|real]]ity is needed for some serious or trivial purpose which guides the operations of the thinker" (008-063–008-064). Science is not disinterested contemplation but a tool-driven activity aimed at specific human ends. The facts that science speaks of "are ever changing in its progress" (008-123), confirming that scientific knowledge is dynamic and purposively driven rather than a transcription of static [[reality|real]]ity.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Intellectualist/Correspondence View**: Science as the discovery of eternally fixed laws that "correspond with Nature" (001-086). This is the pre-pragmatic view that [[william-james|James]] rejects as inconsistent with the actual history and practice of science.

- **Instrumentalist View (Thomson/[[william-james|James]])**: Science as "a policy and not a creed" (001-083), valued for its predictive and organizational power rather than its ontological truth. This is the core pragmatic interpretation.

- **Empiricist Positivism**: Science as the systematic accumulation of [[sensation|sense-data]] and their lawful regularities. [[william-james|James]] criticizes this because passive empiricism "gives no [[reality|real]] account of the scientific [[rationality|rational order]] of the world" (003-043) — it cannot explain the active, selective, purposive character of scientific theorizing.

- **Humanist Interpretation (Schiller)**: Science as an expression of human needs and values, continuous with other forms of human meaning-making including [[religious-experience|religion]]. The postulational method operates across both domains (003-182).

## Criticisms & Debates

A persistent criticism of the pragmatic view of science is that it conflates the _utility_ of scientific theories with their _truth_. If science is valued only for its predictive and instrumental success, then two incompatible theories that make identical predictions would be equally "true" — a consequence that seems to sever science from any claim to describe [[reality|real]]ity as it is. [[william-james|James]]'s response is implicit in his treatment of science as "conceptual shorthand" (001-084): the notion of describing [[reality|real]]ity "as it is" independently of any human conceptual scheme is itself incoherent. What we call [[reality|real]]ity is always [[reality|real]]ity-as-organized-by-some-purpose.

A second line of criticism concerns the scientific status of the pragmatic theory itself. If all knowledge claims, including philosophical ones, must be tested by their practical consequences, then pragmatism itself must be subject to the same criterion. [[william-james|James]] accepts this: pragmatism does not claim exemption from its own method. It stands or falls by its capacity to resolve philosophical problems that intellectualism cannot — a [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]] of pragmatism itself.

Finally, some critics argue that [[william-james|James]]'s alignment of science with [[religious-experience|religion]] under the umbrella of "postulation and verification" (003-182) erases important distinctions between empirical testing and existential commitment. The "verification" of a religious [[postulation|postulate]] operates through its "salutary influence on earthly life" (003-180), while the verification of a scientific [[postulation|postulate]] operates through controlled prediction of observable events. Whether the same word "verification" can cover both without equivocation remains a live debate in [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophy.
