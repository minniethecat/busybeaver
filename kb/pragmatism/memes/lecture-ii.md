## CHAPTER II

[002-001] THE NEW PSYCHOLOGY

[002-002] Until the year 1890, when [[william-james|James]]'s _Principles_ were published, the

[002-003] psychology of [[david-hume|Hume]] reigned absolutely in philosophy.[A] All empiricists

[002-004] accepted it enthusiastically, as the sum of philosophic wisdom; all

[002-005] apriorists submitted to it, even in supplementing and modifying it by

[002-006] 'transcendental' and metaphysical additions; in either case it remained

[002-007] uncontested _as psychology_, and, by propounding an utterly erroneous

[002-008] analysis of the mind and its experience, entangled philosophy in

[002-009] inextricable difficulties.

[002-010] Hume had, as philosophers commonly do, set out from the practically

[002-011] sufficient analysis of experience which all find ready-made in language.

[002-012] He accepted, therefore, from common sense the belief that physical

[002-013] reality is composed of a multitude of separate existences that act on

[002-014] one another, and tried to conceive mental life strictly on the same

[002-015] analogy. His theory of experience, therefore, closely parallels the

[002-016] atomistic theory of matter. Just as the physicist explains bodies as

[002-017] collections of discrete particles, so Hume reduced all the contents of

[002-018] the mind to a number of elementary sensations. Whether the mind was

[002-019] reflecting on its own internal ideas, or whether it was undergoing

[002-020] impressions which it supposed to come from an external source, all that

[002-021] was really happening was a succession of detached sensations. It seemed

[002-022] to Hume indisputable that every distinct perception (or 'impression')

[002-023] was a distinct existence, and that all 'ideas' were equally distinct,

[002-024] though fainter, copies of impressions. Beyond impressions and ideas it

[002-025] was unnecessary to look. Thus to look at a chessboard was to have a

[002-026] number of sensations of black and white arranged in a certain order, to

[002-027] listen to a piece of music was to experience a succession of loud and

[002-028] soft auditory sensations, to handle a stone was to receive a group of

[002-029] sensations of touch. To suppose that anything beyond these sensory units

[002-030] was ever really experienced was futile fiction. [[experience|Experience]] was a mosaic,

[002-031] of which the stones were the detached sensations, and their washed-out

[002-032] copies, the ideas.

[002-033] If this analysis of the mind were correct--and its correctness was not

[002-034] disputed for more than a hundred years, for were not the sensations

[002-035] admitted to be the ultimate analysis of all that was perceived?--the

[002-036] [[common-sense]] belief that knowledge revealed a world outside the thinker

[002-037] was, of course, erroneous. For common sense could hardly treat 'things'

[002-038] as merely 'sensations' artificially grouped together in space, each

[002-039] 'thing' being a complex of a number of sensations having relation to

[002-040] similar complexes. It held rather that the successive appearances of

[002-041] things were related in time, in such a way that they could be supposed

[002-042] to reveal a single object able to endure in spite of surface changes,

[002-043] and to manifest the identity of its sensory 'qualities.' Similarly, the

[002-044] succession of ideas within the mind was for it supported by the inward

[002-045] unity of the soul within which they arose. Moreover, Hume's analysis

[002-046] made havoc of all idea, of 'causation.' If every sensation was a

[002-047] separate being, how was it to be connected with any other in any regular

[002-048] or necessary connection? Two events related as 'cause' and 'effect' must

[002-049] be a myth.

[002-050] These subversive consequences of his theory Hume did not conceal, though

[002-051] he did not push his mental 'atomism' to its logical extreme. When he

[002-052] defined material objects as 'coloured points disposed in a certain

[002-053] order,' he was in fact admitting space as a relating factor; when he

[002-054] spoke of the succession of impressions and ideas in experience, he was

[002-055] tacitly assuming that what was apprehended was not a bare succession of

[002-056] sensations, but _also_ the fact that they were succeeding one another,

[002-057] and so allowing a sense of temporal relation. But further than this he

[002-058] refused to go. The idea of a continuous self was fantastic. There was

[002-059] nothing beneath the ideas to connect them. The notion of causal

[002-060] connection was equally chimerical. Each sensation was distinct and

[002-061] existed in its own right. It could therefore occur alone. There was

[002-062] nothing to link together the distinct impressions. Hence necessary

[002-063] connection in events could not be more than a fiction of the mind based

[002-064] on expectation of customary sequences; how the mind he had described as

[002-065] non-existent could form an expectation or observe a sequence was calmly

[002-066] left a mystery.

[002-067] Hume, then, seemed to leave to his successors in philosophy a task of

[002-068] synthesis. He had tumbled the soul off her high watch-tower, but how to

[002-069] combine her shattered fragments again into a working unity he declined

[002-070] to say. He saw the sceptical implications of his analysis, but professed

[002-071] himself unable to suggest a remedy.

[002-072] He had, however, made the embarrassments of the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]

[002-073] sufficiently clear for [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], his most important successor, to hit upon

[002-074] the most obvious palliative, and in the _Critique of [[reason|Pure Reason]]_ Kant

[002-075] set himself to patch up Hume's analysis. Experience as it came through

[002-076] the channels of sense, he admitted Hume had analysed correctly; it was

[002-077] 'a manifold,' a whirl of separate sensations. But these _per se_ could

[002-078] not yield knowledge. They must be made to cohere, and the way to do this

[002-079] he had found. The mind on to which they fell was equipped with a

[002-080] complicated apparatus of faculties which could organize the chaotic

[002-081] manifold of sense and turn it into the connected world which common

[002-082] sense and science recognize. First it views the data of sense in the

[002-083] light of its own 'pure [[intuition|intuitions]],' and, lo! they are seen to be in

[002-084] Space and Time; then it solidifies them with its own 'categories,' which

[002-085] turn them into 'substances' and 'causes' and endow them with all the

[002-086] attributes required to sustain that status; finally it refers them all

[002-087] to a Transcendental Ego, which is not, indeed, a soul, but sufficiently

[002-088] like one to provide something that can admire the creative synthesis of

[002-089] 'mind as such.'

[002-090] Had Hume lived to read Kant's _Critique_, he would probably have jeered

[002-091] at the vain complications of Kant's transcendental machinery, and made

[002-092] it clear that between the primary manifold of sensation and the first

[002-093] constructions of the intellect there still yawns a gulf which Kant's

[002-094] laboured explanations nowhere bridge.

[002-095] Why does the chaotic 'matter' of sensations submit itself so tamely to

[002-096] the forming of the mind? How can the _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ necessities of thought,

[002-097] which are the 'presuppositions' of the complexities Kant loved, operate

[002-098] upon so alien a stuff as the sensations are assumed to be? And, after

[002-099] all, was not Kant a bit premature in proclaiming the _finality_ of his

[002-100] analysis and of his refutation of empiricism for all time? The searching

[002-101] question, Why should the future resemble the past? had received no

[002-102] answer, and so might not the mind itself, with all its categories, be

[002-103] susceptible to change? Was it certain that the miracle whereby the data

[002-104] presented to our faculties conformed to them would be a standing one?

[002-105] Had not Kant himself as good as admitted that our faculties might

[002-106] distort reality instead of making it intelligible?

[002-107] The truth is that at this point Kant is open to a charge against which

[002-108] the assumptions he shared with Hume admit of no defence. Hume had been

[002-109] the first to discover that we are in the habit of trying to rationalize

[002-110] our [[sensation|sense-data]] by putting ideal constructions upon them, though he had

[002-111] abstained from sanctifying the practice by a hideous jargon of technical

[002-112] terminology. But this way of eking out the facts only seemed to him to

[002-113] _falsify_ them. [[truth|Truth]] in his view was to be reached by accepting with

[002-114] docility the sensations given from without. To set to work to 'imagine'

[002-115] connections between them, and to claim for them a higher truth, had

[002-116] seemed to him an outrage. What right, then, had Kant to legitimate the

[002-117] mind's impudence in tampering with sensations? Was not every _a priori_

[002-118] form an 'imagination,' and a vain one at that?

[002-119] To these objections the Kantian school have never found an answer. They

[002-120] have simply repeated Kant's phrases about the necessary

[002-121] 'presuppositions' which were to be added to Hume's data. The English

[002-122] psychologists (the Mills, Bain, etc.) exhibited a similar fidelity. They

[002-123] never accepted the _a priori_, but relied on 'the association of ideas'

[002-124] to build up a mind out of isolated sensations. But was this expedient

[002-125] really thinkable? For if all 'sensations' or qualities are separate

[002-126] entities, how can the addition of more 'distinct existences' of the same

[002-127] sort really bind them together? If in 'the cat is upon the wall,' 'upon'

[002-128] is a distinct entity which has to relate 'cat' and 'wall,' what is to

[002-129] connect 'cat' with 'upon' and 'upon' with 'wall'? The atomizing method

[002-130] carried to its logical extreme demands that not only 'sensations' but

[002-131] also 'thoughts' should be essentially disconnected, and then, of course,

[002-132] _no_ thinking can cohere.

[002-133] [[psychology|Psychology]], then, had worked itself to a breakdown by accepting the

[002-134] 'sensationalistic' analysis offered by Hume, and dragged philosophy with

[002-135] it. Yet the escape was as easy as the egg of Columbus to the insight of

[002-136] genius. William James had merely to invert the problem. Instead of

[002-137] assuming with Hume that because some experiences seemed to attest the

[002-138] presence of distinct objects, all connections were illusory and all

[002-139] experience must ultimately consist of psychical atoms, James had merely

[002-140] to maintain that this separation was secondary and artificial, and that

[002-141] experience was initially a continuum. Once this is pointed out, the fact

[002-142] is obvious. The [[stream-of-consciousness|stream of experience]] no doubt contains what it is

[002-143] afterwards possible to single out as 'sensations,' but it presents them

[002-144] also as connected by 'relations.' Moreover, the 'sensations' or

[002-145] 'qualities' and their 'relations' exhibit the immediate indiscerptible

[002-146] unity of a fluid rather than a succession of flashes. Temporal and

[002-147] spatial relations with all the connections they sustain are perceived

[002-148] just as directly as what we come to distinguish as the 'things' in them.

[002-149] 'Consciousness,' James insists, 'does not appear to itself chopped up in

[002-150] bits,' and 'we ought to say a feeling of _and_, a feeling of _if_, a

[002-151] feeling of _but_, and a feeling of _by_, quite as readily as we say a

[002-152] feeling of _blue_ or a feeling of _cold_. All things in experience

[002-153] naturally 'compenetrate,' to use a phrase of [[henri-bergson|Bergson]]'s; they are

[002-154] distinct and they are united at the same time.

[002-155] The great crux in Hume is thus seen to be illusory. Immediate experience

[002-156] does not require 'synthesis': it calls for 'analysis.' It is not a

[002-157] jigsaw puzzle, to be pieced together without glue: it is a confused

[002-158] whole which has to be divided and set in order for clear thinking.

[002-159] Hume's mistake was to have started from experience _as partly analysed_

[002-160] by common sense, and not from the flux _as given_. His 'sensations' were

[002-161] the qualities already analysed out of the flux; he took these selections

[002-162] for the whole and neglected the other less obvious features in it--viz.,

[002-163] the relations which floated them.

[002-164] Thus the puzzle 'How do "relations" relate?' received its solution in

[002-165] this new account of experience. Philosophers are puzzled by this

[002-166] question because they confuse percepts with concepts. Percepts are

[002-167] _given_ in relation; but concepts, being ideal dissections of the

[002-168] perceptual flux, are discontinuous terms which have to be related by an

[002-169] act of thought, because they were made for this very purpose of

[002-170] distinction. Thus the eye sees cats sitting upon walls, as parts of a

[002-171] rural landscape, and without the sharp distinctions which exist between

[002-172] the concepts 'cat,' 'upon,' 'wall.' These ideas were _meant_ to

[002-173] disconnect 'the cat' in thought from the site it sat upon. Thought,

[002-174] then, has _made_ the 'atomism' it professed to find. It has only to

[002-175] unmake it, and to allow the distinctions it held apart to merge again

[002-176] into the stream of change.

[002-177] All Hume's problems, therefore, are unreal, and those of his apriorist

[002-178] critics are doubly removed from reality. The whole conception of

[002-179] philosophy as aiming at uniting disjointed data in a higher synthesis

[002-180] runs counter to [[reality|the real]] movement, which aims at the analysis of a given

[002-181] whole. The real question about causation is not how events can be

[002-182] connected causally, but why are certain antecedents preferred and

[002-183] dissected out and entitled 'causes.' So the 'self' is not one

[002-184] (undiscoverable) item imagined to keep in order a host of other such

[002-185] items. Any given moment of a consciousness is just the mass of its

[002-186] 'sensations,' but these are consciously the heirs of its history and

[002-187] connected with a past which is remembered. No Transcendental Ego could

[002-188] do more to support the process of experience than is achieved by 'a

[002-189] stream of consciousness which carries its own past along.' Here, then,

[002-190] is the straight way James desiderated, a critical philosophy which goes,

[002-191] not 'through' the complexities of Kantism, but leaves them on one side

[002-192] as superfluous 'curios.'

[002-193] But there remains an even more important deduction from the new

[002-194] psychology. Hume had been convicted of error in selecting those elements

[002-195] of the flux which served his purpose and neglecting the rest. But this

[002-196] mistake might reveal the important fact that all analysis was a choice,

[002-197] and inspired by volitions. A mind that analyses cannot but be _active_

[002-198] in handling its experience. It manipulates it to serve its ends. It

[002-199] emphasizes only those portions of the flux which seem to it important.

[002-200] In a better and fairer analysis than Hume's these features will persist.

[002-201] It, too, would be a product of selection, of a selection depending on

[002-202] its maker's preferences. As James showed, the distinction between

[002-203] 'dreams' and 'realities,' between 'things' and 'illusions,' results only

[002-204] from the differential values we attach to the parts of the flux

[002-205] according as they seem important or interesting to us or not. The

[002-206] volitional contribution is all-pervasive in our thinking. And once this

[002-207] volitional interference with 'pure perception' is shown to be

[002-208] indispensable, it must be allowed to be legitimate. Nor can this

[002-209] approval of our interference be restricted to selections. It must be

[002-210] extended to _additions_. Just as we can select factors from 'the given'

[002-211] to construct 'reality,' we can add [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] to it to make it

[002-212] 'intelligible.' We can claim the right of causal analysis, and assume

[002-213] that our dissections have laid bare the inner springs of the connection

[002-214] of events. Moreover, to the 'real world which our choice has built out

[002-215] of the chaos of 'appearances' we may hypothetically add 'infernal' and

[002-216] 'heavenly' regions.[B] Both are transformations of 'the given' by the

[002-217] will, but, like the [[postulation|postulate]] of causal series, experience _may_ confirm

[002-218] them. Kant's _a priori_ activity of the mind may thus in a sense supply

[002-219] an answer to Hume--but only in a voluntaristic philosophy which would

[002-220] probably have seemed too bold both to him and to Hume.

[002-221] There can be no doubt that we do not approach the data of perception in

[002-222] an attitude of quiescent resignation. Our desires and needs equip us

[002-223] with assumptions and 'first principles,' which originate from within,

[002-224] not from without. But how precisely should this mental contribution to

[002-225] knowledge be conceived? In the last chapter of his _Psychology_ James

[002-226] suggested that the mind's organization is essentially biological. It has

[002-227] evolved according to sound [[darwinism|Darwinian]] principles, and in so doing the

[002-228] fittest of its 'variations' have survived. But were these variations

[002-229] quite fortuitous? May they not have been purposive responses to the

[002-230] stimulation of environment? Can logic have been invented like saws and

[002-231] ships for purposes of human service? These are some of the stimulating

[002-232] questions which James's work in _Psychology_ has suggested.
