## Overview

_A Treatise of Human Nature_ (1739–1740) is [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s first and most ambitious philosophical work. Though it "fell dead-born from the press" in Hume's own memorable phrase, it is now recognized as one of the most important works in the history of Western philosophy. The _Treatise_ aims to establish a complete "science of man" grounded in empirical observation rather than rationalist first principles.

## Central Arguments

The _Treatise_ is divided into three books: "Of the Understanding" (on epistemology and the nature of ideas), "Of the Passions" (on moral psychology), and "Of Morals" (on ethics). Hume's most influential arguments include:

- The distinction between "impressions" (vivid perceptions) and "ideas" (faint copies), which becomes the basis of his empiricist criterion of meaning
- The argument that causal necessity is not rationally demonstrable but is a product of custom and association
- The denial of any rational proof of the existence of an external world or a substantial self
- The claim that reason is and ought to be "the slave of the passions"

## Connection to Pragmatism

In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Hume's _Treatise_ is the founding document of the empiricist tradition that pragmatism both inherits and transforms. [[william-james|James]] praises Hume's empiricist method — his insistence on tracing ideas back to experience — but rejects Hume's atomistic psychology, in which experience is a chain of discrete, disconnected sensations. For [[william-james|James]], experience is continuous and relational, not atomic — a critique that underpins his [[radical-empiricism|radical empiricism]].

Hume's sceptical conclusions — that we cannot rationally justify our belief in causation, the external world, or the self — also set the stage for the pragmatist redefinition of [[truth|truth]]. Where Hume leaves these beliefs rationally unjustified, [[william-james|James]] argues that their practical indispensability is itself a form of validation.
