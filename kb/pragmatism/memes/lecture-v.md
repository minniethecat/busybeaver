## CHAPTER V

[005-001] THE PROBLEM OF TRUTH AND ERROR

[005-002] It has been shown in the last chapter how urgent has become the problem

[005-003] of discriminating between the true and false among relative 'truths.'

[005-004] For [[absolutism|absolute truth]] has become a chimera, [[intuition|self-evidence]] an illusion, and

[005-005] intuition untrustworthy. All three are psychologically very [[reality|real]] to

[005-006] those who believe in them, but logically they succumb to the assaults of

[005-007] a scepticism which infers from the fact that no 'truths' are absolute

[005-008] that all may reasonably be overthrown.

[005-009] The only obstacle to its triumph lies in the existence of 'relative'

[005-010] truths which are _not_ absolute, and do not claim to be, and in the

[005-011] unexamined possibility that in a [[relativism|relativist]] interpretation of all truth

[005-012] a meaning may be found for the distinction between 'true' and 'false.'

[005-013] Now, not even a sceptic could deny that the size of an object is better

[005-014] measured by a yard-measure than by the eye, even though it may be

[005-015] meaningless to ask what its size may be absolutely; or that it is

[005-016] probable that bread will be found more nourishing than stone, even

[005-017] though it may not be a perfect elixir of life. Even if he denied this,

[005-018] the sceptic's _acts_ would convict his _words_ of insincerity, and

[005-019] _practically_, at any rate, no one has been or can be a sceptic,

[005-020] whatever the extent of his _theoretic_ doubts.

[005-021] This fact is construed by the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] as a significant indication of

[005-022] the way out of the epistemological _impasse_. The 'relative' truths,

[005-023] which [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]] passed by with contempt, may differ in _practical

[005-024] value_ and lead to the conceptions of _practical truth_ and certainty

[005-025] which may be better adapted to the requirements of human life than the

[005-026] elusive and discredited ideals of absolute truth and certainty, and may

[005-027] enable us to justify the distinctions we make between the 'true' and the

[005-028] 'false. At any rate, this suggestion seems worth following up.

[005-029] To begin with, we must radically disabuse our minds of the idea that

[005-030] thinking _starts from certainty._ Even the self-evident and

[005-031] self-confident 'intuitions' that impress the uncritical so much with

[005-032] their claim to infallibility are really the results of antecedent doubts

[005-033] and ponderings, and would never be enunciated unless there were thought

[005-034] to be a dispute about them. In real life thought starts from

[005-035] perplexities, from situations in which, as [[john-dewey|Professor Dewey]] says, beliefs

[005-036] have to be 'reconstructed,' and it aims at setting doubts at rest. It is

[005-037] psychologically impossible for a [[rationality|rational]] mind to assert what it knows

[005-038] to be true, and supposes everyone else to admit the truth of. This is

[005-039] why even a philosopher's conversation does not consist of a rehearsal of

[005-040] all the unchallenged truisms that he can remember.

[005-041] Being thus conditioned by a doubt, every judgment is a challenge. It

[005-042] claims truth, and backs its claims by the authority of its maker; but it

[005-043] would be folly to imagine that it thereby becomes _ipso facto_ true, or

[005-044] is meant to be universally accepted without testing. Its maker must know

[005-045] this as well as anyone, unless his dogmatism has quite blotted out his

[005-046] common sense. Indeed, he may himself have given preference to the

[005-047] judgment he made over the alternatives that occurred to him only after

[005-048] much debate and hesitation, and may propound it only as a basis for

[005-049] further discussion and testing.

[005-050] Initially, then, every judgment is a _[[truth|truth-claim]]_, and this claim is

[005-051] merely _formal_. It does not _mean_ that the claim is absolutely true,

[005-052] and that it is impious to question it. On the contrary, it has still to

[005-053] be validated by others, and may work in such a way that its own maker

[005-054] withdraws it, and corrects it by a better. The intellectualist accounts

[005-055] of truth have all failed to make this vital distinction between

[005-056] 'truth-claim' and validated truth. They rest on a _confusion of formal

[005-057] with absolute truth_, and it is on this account that they cannot

[005-058] distinguish between 'truth' and error. For false judgments also formally

[005-059] claim 'truth,' No judgment alleges that it is false.[C]

[005-060] On the other hand, if the distinction between truth-claims and validated

[005-061] truths is made, there ceases to be any _theoretic_ difficulty about the

[005-062] conception and correction of errors, however difficult it may be to

[005-063] detect them in practice. 'Truths' will be 'claims' which have worked

[005-064] well and maintained themselves; 'errors,' such as have been superseded

[005-065] by better ones. All 'truths' must be _tested_ by something more

[005-066] objective than their own self-assertiveness, and this testing by their

[005-067] working and the consequences to which they lead may go on indefinitely.

[005-068] In other words, however much a 'truth' has been validated, it is always

[005-069] possible to test it further. _I.e.,_ it is never theoretically

[005-070] 'absolute,' however well it may practically be assured. For a

[005-071] confirmation of this doctrine Pragmatism appeals to the history of

[005-072] scientific truth, which has shown a continuous correction of 'truths,'

[005-073] which were re-valued as 'errors,' as better statements for them became

[005-074] available.

[005-075] It may also be confirmed negatively by the breakdown of the current

[005-076] definitions of truth, which all seem in the end to mean nothing.

[005-077] The oldest and commonest definition of a 'truth' which is given is that

[005-078] it is 'the correspondence of a thought to reality.' But Intellectualism

[005-079] never perceived the difficulties lurking in it. At first sight this

[005-080] seems a brave attempt to get outside the circle of thought in order to

[005-081] test its value and to control its vagaries. Unluckily, this theory can

[005-082] only assert, and neither explains nor proves, the connection between the

[005-083] thought and the reality it desiderates. For, granting that it is the

[005-084] intent of every thought to correspond with reality, we must yet inquire

[005-085] how the alleged correspondence is to be made out. Made out it must be;

[005-086] for as the criterion is quite formal and holds of all assertions, the

[005-087] claim to 'correspond' may be false. To prove the correspondence, then,

[005-088] the 'reality' would have somehow to be known apart from the truth-claim

[005-089] of the thought, in order that the two might be compared and found to

[005-090] agree. But if the reality were already known directly, what would be

[005-091] the need of asserting an idea of it and claiming 'truth' for this? How,

[005-092] moreover, could the claim be tested, if, as is admitted, the reality is

[005-093] not directly known? To assert the 'correspondence' must become a

[005-094] groundless [[postulation|postulate]] about something which is defined to transcend all

[005-095] knowledge. The [[correspondence-theory|correspondence theory]], then, does not _test_ the

[005-096] truth-claim of the assertion; it only gives a fresh definition of it. A

[005-097] 'true' thought, it says, is one which _claims to correspond_ with a

[005-098] 'reality.' _But so does a false,_ and hence the theory leaves us as we

[005-099] were, puzzled to distinguish them.[D]

[005-100] Yet the theory is not wholly wrong. Many of our thoughts do claim to

[005-101] correspond with reality in ways that can be verified. If the judgment

[005-102] 'There is a green carpet in my hall' is taken to mean 'If I enter my

[005-103] hall, I shall _see_ a green carpet,' perception tests whether the

[005-104] judgment 'corresponds' with the reality perceived, and so goes to

[005-105] validate or disprove the claim. But the limits within which this

[005-106] correspondence works are very strait. It applies only to such judgments

[005-107] as are anticipations of perception,[E] and will test a truth-claim only

[005-108] where there is willingness to act on it. It implies an experiment, and

[005-109] is not a wholly intellectual process.

[005-110] The superiority of the 'correspondence' theory over the belief in

[005-111] 'intuitions' lies in its insistence that thought is not to audit its own

[005-112] accounts. Its success or failure depends upon factors external to it,

[005-113] which establish the truth or falsehood of its claims. No such guarantee

[005-114] is offered by the next theory, which is known as the 'consistence' or

[005-115] 'coherence' theory. In order to avoid the difficulty which wrecked the

[005-116] 'correspondence' theory, that of making the truth of an assertion reside

[005-117] in an inexperienceable relation to an unattainable reality, this view

[005-118] maintains that an idea is true if it is consistent with the rest of our

[005-119] thoughts, and so can be fitted with them into a coherent system. No

[005-120] doubt a coherence among our ideas is a convenience and a part of their

[005-121] 'working,' but it is hardly a test of their objective truth. For a

[005-122] harmonious system of thoughts is conceivable which would either not

[005-123] apply to reality at all, or, if applied, would completely fail. On this

[005-124] theory systematic delusions, fictions, and dreams, might properly lay

[005-125] claim to truth. True, they might not be quite consistent: but neither

[005-126] are the systems of our sciences. If, then, this _absolute_ coherence be

[005-127] insisted on, this test condemns our whole knowledge; if not, it remains

[005-128] formal, and fails to recognize any distinctions of value in the claims

[005-129] which can be systematized.

[005-130] To avoid this _reductio ad absurdum_, it has been suggested that it is

[005-131] not the coherence of the idea in human, finite, minds which constitutes

[005-132] 'truth,' but the perfect consistency of the experience of an Absolute

[005-133] Mind. The test, then, of our limited coherency will lie in its relation

[005-134] to this [[the-absolute|Absolute System]]. But here we have the correspondence doctrine

[005-135] once again in a fresh disguise; our human systems are now 'true' if they

[005-136] correspond with the Absolute's, But as there is no way for us of sharing

[005-137] the Absolute [[experience|Experience]], our test is again illusory, and productive of a

[005-138] depressing scepticism; and, again, we have only asserted that truth is

[005-139] what _claims_ to be part of the Absolute System.

[005-140] A word may be devoted to the simple refusal of intuitionists to give an

[005-141] account of Truth on the ground that it is 'indefinable.' Truth is taken

[005-142] to be an ultimate unanalyzable quality of certain propositions,

[005-143] intuitively felt, and incapable of description. Error, by the same

[005-144] token, should be equally indefinable and as immediately apprehended.

[005-145] How, then, can there be differences of opinion, and mistakes as to what

[005-146] is true and what false? How is it that a proposition which is felt to be

[005-147] 'true' so often turns out to be erroneous? If all errors are felt to be

[005-148] true by those they deceive, is it not clear that immediate feeling is

[005-149] not a good enough test of a validated truth? Thus, once again, we find

[005-150] that an account of truth-claim is being foisted on us in place of a

[005-151] description of truth-testing.

[005-152] The intellectualist, then, being in every case unable to justify the

[005-153] vital distinction commonly made between the true and the false, we

[005-154] return to the pragmatist. He starts with no preconceptions as to what

[005-155] truth must mean, whether it exists or not; he is content to watch how

[005-156] _de facto_ claims to truth get themselves validated in experience. He

[005-157] observes that every question is intimately related to some scheme of

[005-158] human purposes. For it has to be _put_, in order to come into being.

[005-159] Hence every inquiry arises, and every question is asked, because of

[005-160] obstacles and problems which arise in the carrying out of human

[005-161] purposes. So soon as uncertainty arises in the course of fulfilling a

[005-162] purpose, an idea or belief is formulated _and acted on_, to fill the gap

[005-163] where immediate certitude has broken down. This engenders the

[005-164] truth-claim, which is necessarily a 'good' in its maker's eyes, because

[005-165] it has been selected by him and judged _preferable_ to any alternative

[005-166] that occurred to him.

[005-167] How, then, is it tested? Simply by the consequences which follow from

[005-168] adopting it and using it as an assumption upon which to work. If these

[005-169] consequences are satisfactory, if they promote the purpose in hand,

[005-170] instead of thwarting it, and thus have a valuable effect upon life, then

[005-171] the truth-claim maintains its 'truth,' and is so far validated. This is

[005-172] the universal [[pragmatic-method|method of testing]] assertions alike in the formation of

[005-173] mathematical laws, physical [[hypothesis|hypotheses]], religious beliefs, and ethical

[005-174] postulates. Hence such pragmatic aphorisms as 'truth is useful' or

[005-175] 'truth is a matter of practical consequences' mean essentially that all

[005-176] assertions must be _tested by being applied to a real problem of

[005-177] [[knowledge|knowing]]._ What is signified by such statements is that no 'truth' must

[005-178] be accepted merely on account of the insistence of its claim, but that

[005-179] every idea must be tested by the consequences of its working. Its truth

[005-180] will then depend upon those consequences being fruitful for life in

[005-181] general, and in particular for the purpose behind the particular inquiry

[005-182] in which it arose. Truth is a _value_ and a satisfaction; but

[005-183] 'intellectual satisfaction' is not a morbid delight in dialectical and

[005-184] verbal juggling: it is the satisfaction which rewards the hard labour of

[005-185] rationalizing experience and rendering it more conformable with human

[005-186] desires.

[005-187] It should be clear, though it is often misunderstood, that there is

[005-188] nothing arbitrary or 'subjective' in this method of testing beliefs. It

[005-189] does not mean that we are free to assert the truth of every idea which

[005-190] seems to us pretty or pleasant. The very term 'useful' was chosen by

[005-191] pragmatists as a protest against the common philosophic licence of

[005-192] alleging 'truths' which could never be applied or tested, and were

[005-193] supposed to be none the worse for being 'useless.' It is clear both that

[005-194] such 'truths' must be a monopoly of Intellectualism, and also that they

[005-195] do allow every man to believe whatever he wishes, provided only that he

[005-196] boldly claims 'self-evidence' for his idiosyncrasy. In this purely

[005-197] subjective sense, into which Intellectualism is driven, it is, however,

[005-198] clear that there can be no useless ideas. For any idea anyone decided to

[005-199] adopt, because it pleased or amused him, would be _ipso facto_ true.

[005-200] Pragmatism, therefore, by refuting 'useless' knowledge, shows that it

[005-201] does _not_ admit such merely subjective 'uses.' It insists that ideas

[005-202] must be more objectively useful--viz., by showing ability to cope with

[005-203] the situation they were devised to meet. If they fail to harmonize with

[005-204] the situation they are untrue, however attractive they may be. For ideas

[005-205] do not function in a void; they have to work in a world of fact, and to

[005-206] adapt themselves to all facts, though they may succeed in transforming

[005-207] them in the end.

[005-208] Nor has an idea to reckon only with facts: it has also to cohere with

[005-209] other ideas. It must be congruous with the mass of other beliefs held

[005-210] for good reasons by the thinker who accepts it. For no one can afford to

[005-211] have a stock of beliefs which conflict too violently with those of his

[005-212] fellows. If his 'intuitions' contrast too seriously with those of

[005-213] others, and he acts on them, he will be shut up as a lunatic. If, then,

[005-214] the 'useful' idea has to approve itself both to its maker and his

[005-215] fellows without developing limitations in its use, it is clear that a

[005-216] pragmatic truth is really far less arbitrary and subjective than the

[005-217] 'truths' accepted as absolute, on the bare ground that they seem

[005-218] 'self-evident' to a few intellectualists.

[005-219] If, however, it be urged that pragmatic truths never grow absolutely

[005-220] true at all, and that the most prolonged pragmatic tests do not exclude

[005-221] the possibility of an ultimate error in the idea, there is no difficulty

[005-222] about admitting this. The pragmatic test yields _practical_, and not

[005-223] 'absolute,' certainty. The existence of absolute certainty is denied,

[005-224] and the demand for it, in a world which contains only the practical

[005-225] sort, merely plays into the hands of scepticism. The uncertainty of all

[005-226] our verificatory processes, however, is not the creation of the

[005-227] pragmatist, nor is he a god to abolish it. Abstractly, there is always a

[005-228] doubt about what transcends our immediate experience, and this is why it

[005-229] is so healthy to have to repudiate so many theoretic doubts in every act

[005-230] we do. For beliefs have to be acted on, and the results of the action

[005-231] rightly react on the beliefs. The pragmatic test is practically

[005-232] adequate, and is the only one available. That it brings out the risk of

[005-233] action only brings out its superiority to a theory which cannot get

[005-234] started at all until it is supplied with absolute certainty, and

[005-235] meantime can only idly rail at all existing human truths.

[005-236] We have in all this consistently referred the truth of ideas to

[005-237] individual experiences for verification. This evidently makes all truths

[005-238] in some sense dependent upon the personality of those who assert and

[005-239] accept them. Intellectualist logic, on the other hand, has always

[005-240] proclaimed that mental processes, if true, are 'independent' of the

[005-241] idiosyncrasies of particular minds. Ideas have a _fixed_ meaning, and

[005-242] cohere in bodies of 'universal' truth, quite irrespective of whether any

[005-243] particular mind harbours them or not. This is not only a contention

[005-244] fatal to the pragmatic claims, but also bound up with other assumptions

[005-245] of [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]]. So it becomes necessary to inquire whether this [[logic|Logic]]

[005-246] is a success, and so can coherently abstract from the personality of the

[005-247] knower and the particular situations that incite him to know.
