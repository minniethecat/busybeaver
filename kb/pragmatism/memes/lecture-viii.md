## CHAPTER VIII

[008-001] THOUGHT AND LIFE

[008-002] The mission of [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] is to bring [[philosophy|Philosophy]] into relation to [[reality|real]]

[008-003] Life and Action. So far from regarding Thought as a self-centred,

[008-004] self-enclosed activity, Pragmatism insists upon replacing it in its

[008-005] context among the other functions of life, and in measuring its value by

[008-006] its effect upon them. So far, again, from regarding the abstract

[008-007] intellect as a vast Juggernaut machine which absorbs and crushes the

[008-008] individual thinker, it treats him individually as having his own

[008-009] constitution, _raison d'être,_ and intrinsic interest, and credits him

[008-010] with a power to make new truths and to enrich the resources of thought.

[008-011] Each thinker has before him an individual situation, a system of aims

[008-012] and values, a stock of knowledge and of means from which he must select

[008-013] what is relevant to his ends, and so cannot escape in any judgment from

[008-014] the responsibilities of a personal decision.

[008-015] Thus, for Pragmatism _every thought is an act_ with a person behind it,

[008-016] who is responsible for launching it into the world of fact. The result

[008-017] of this change of attitude is immediate. In the first place, as has been

[008-018] shown in Chapter V., by bringing thought face to face with the whole

[008-019] experience upon which it claims to work, we are enabled to find a

[008-020] tangible rule for evaluating its assertions and distinguishing truth

[008-021] from error. And, secondly, by recognizing that the mind is not an

[008-022] apparatus which functions in a vacuum, but is a constituent of an

[008-023] individual organism, we see that thinking always depends upon a purpose;

[008-024] for it is the purpose of an inquiry which gives reflection its cue, and

[008-025] determines its scope and (most essential of all) its meaning.

[008-026] We are thus led from the narrower logical question, 'What constitutes

[008-027] the "truth" of a statement?' to a wider outlook, from which we can

[008-028] survey the place of [[knowledge|knowing]] in human life at large. This may be called

[008-029] the transition from Pragmatism to [[humanism|Humanism]]. This last word was

[008-030] introduced into philosophic terminology by [[fcs-schiller|Dr. Schiller]] in order to

[008-031] describe his general philosophical position as distinct from the

[008-032] original question of the theory of knowledge, which had been treated by

[008-033] [[william-james|James]] under the name of Pragmatism.

[008-034] To the Humanist the best definition of life is one which displays it as

[008-035] throughout purposive, as a [[rationality|rational]] pursuit of ends. This raises the

[008-036] question of the validity of valuations. Valuation is a widespread human

[008-037] practice. In their most general aspect we classify all objects as 'good'

[008-038] and 'bad,' according as they are ends to be pursued or avoided, or means

[008-039] which further or frustrate the pursuit of ends. This general antithesis

[008-040] between the 'good' and the 'bad' has numerous specific forms, applicable

[008-041] to different departments of human activity. Thus, in conduct, actions

[008-042] are judged 'good' or 'evil' and 'right' or 'wrong'; in thinking, ideas

[008-043] are 'true' or 'false,' and 'relevant' or 'irrelevant'; for art, objects

[008-044] are 'beautiful' or 'ugly,' and so forth, for the modes of valuation in

[008-045] life are innumerable. Any one of these adjectives either denotes value

[008-046] or censures lack of worth, and each gets its meaning by reference to the

[008-047] specific purpose, moral, aesthetic, or intellectual, it appeals to. The

[008-048] _summum bonum_, or supreme good, will then be the ideal of the

[008-049] harmonious satisfaction of all purposes.

[008-050] What, then, from the standpoint of Humanism, is the function of

[008-051] 'truth-values' in our life? They indicate a relation to the cognitive

[008-052] end. What is this end? Surely not self-sufficing? A truth that is merely

[008-053] true in itself has no interest for human life, and no human mind has an

[008-054] interest in discovering and affirming it. [[truth|Truth]], therefore, cannot

[008-055] stand aloof from life. It must somehow subserve our vital purposes. But

[008-056] how shall it do this? Only by becoming applicable to the reality we have

[008-057] to live with, by becoming useful for the changes we desire to effect in

[008-058] it. Whoever will not admit this, and renders truth inapplicable, does in

[008-059] fact render it unmeaning.

[008-060] The fact that thought essentially refers to a 'reality' external to it

[008-061] in no way diminishes its purposive character. Whether the mind is

[008-062] idealizing an aspect of reality (as in mathematics) or abstracting,

[008-063] classifying, and predicting (as in science), it is always the fact that

[008-064] a particular kind of reality is needed for some serious or trivial

[008-065] purpose which guides the operations of the thinker. A mind which craved

[008-066] to embrace all or 'any' reality need not _think_; it would do better to

[008-067] float without discrimination upon the flux of change. This procedure

[008-068] would be so absolutely antithetical to human knowing that it seems a

[008-069] wanton paradox on that account to treat it as the final goal of

[008-070] knowledge.

[008-071] Actually, of course, the philosophers who claim to be devoted to pure

[008-072] theory follow no such course. They deliberately choose their ideal of

[008-073] what is worth knowing--_e.g._, 'God,' or 'the unity of all things,' or

[008-074] 'the laws of the universe'--and, disregarding all other existences,

[008-075] they pursue the kind of reality they desire because of its religious or

[008-076] moral or aesthetic value. For there could be no greater mistake than to

[008-077] suppose that the common antithesis between 'reality' and the 'un-real'

[008-078] usually means the same thing as the distinction between what 'exists'

[008-079] and what is absolutely non-existent. On the contrary, it is usually a

[008-080] judgment of value. We may say that the 'haunted' house is real and the

[008-081] 'ghost' is not; but as an hallucination the ghost is real enough. Utopia

[008-082] is unreal for the politician, but exists as an ideal for the theorist.

[008-083] The [[plato|Platonist]] treats our physical world of sight and touch, which we

[008-084] think the most real of all, as a mere illusion compared to the 'Ideas'

[008-085] of his metaphysical world. The thinker who declares he wants to know all

[008-086] about 'reality' does not mean that he wishes to investigate _everything_

[008-087] which in any sense exists, but that he wishes to know what _he_

[008-088] considers _best worth knowing_--and this, of course, implies a personal

[008-089] valuation, a purged and expurgated extract, which will not offend his

[008-090] taste. So all philosophies are, in fact, selective. Even the more

[008-091] conscientious rationalists show very little anxiety to include in their

[008-092] intellectual scheme a knowledge of their opponents' opinions--indeed,

[008-093] they seem to think that the existence of such facts may be made

[008-094] dependent wholly on their will to recognize them. An exposition of

[008-095] Pragmatism is for them a 'reality' which does not count: it is not worth

[008-096] knowing about. And this is only natural, after all. For 'reality,' the

[008-097] object of the mind's search, is always a selection, conceived after the

[008-098] likeness of the heart's desire, the product of a human purpose.

[008-099] To recognize this is to appreciate the wisdom of Humanism's refusal to

[008-100] treat the world, for good or bad, as a given and completed whole. For

[008-101] not only is what we call the real world always a selection from a larger

[008-102] whole from which we have ventured to exclude great masses of

[008-103] irrelevance, but every day brings fresh experience, and may bring fresh

[008-104] enlightenment. And since man has always an interest in improving his

[008-105] condition, is it not futile to forbid him to re-make his world as beat

[008-106] he can? Why prematurely claim to have reached finality, when unexpected

[008-107] novelties may shatter any system before it is even completed? Our world

[008-108] is plastic, it is most 'really' what we can make of it, and the process

[008-109] of our making is not ended. Whether a decree of Fate has fixed any

[008-110] ultimate limits to our efforts we have no means of knowing, and no

[008-111] occasion to assume. Is not our wisest course, then, to persist in

[008-112] trying? It is bad method ever to despair of knowing what we need.

[008-113] For good or ill, the world with which the Humanist contends is always a

[008-114] world that reveals itself to him. Reality, as it is assumed, presumed,

[008-115] or guessed to be 'in itself,' apart from our experience of it, is

[008-116] cancelled from his reckonings. For he cannot discover how he (or anyone)

[008-117] can get any 'knowledge' or 'intuition' which transcends all human

[008-118] faculties. The theories of metaphysicians on these lofty themes he

[008-119] regards as personal [[postulation|postulates]] which, in so far as they cannot be

[008-120] subjected to the pragmatic method, must remain open questions. Human

[008-121] experience does not warrant such gratuitous demands. It confirms neither

[008-122] the rigid system of unchanging fact which realism postulates (seeing

[008-123] that the only facts that science speaks of are ever changing in its

[008-124] progress), nor finds its problems, conflicts, and errors credible as a

[008-125] reflexion of any Universal Mind, unless Idealism ultimately repudiates

[008-126] the sanity of its Absolute.

[008-127] The superiority of Humanism, then, lies in this, that it does not

[008-128] discourage human enterprise by assuming that the real is completely

[008-129] rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort. In the

[008-130] drama that unrolls reality, every man, it teaches, has a duty and a

[008-131] power to play his humble but essential part. Humanism is neither an

[008-132] Optimism nor a Pessimism--both of which must consistently, in their

[008-133] extreme form, deny that reality can be improved--but concedes to man the

[008-134] right and duty to improve the world. It impresses us with the necessity

[008-135] of acting, it vindicates the procedure of acting on our hopes, it shows

[008-136] us how we may correct our errors, and so gives reasons for our [[religious-experience|faith]] in

[008-137] the possibility of Progress.
