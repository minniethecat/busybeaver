## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, "reality" is not an absolute, mind-independent given but a **purposive selection from experience**, shaped by human interests, values, and action. Reality is plastic -- "it is most 'really' what we can make of it, and the process of our making is not ended" (008-108). The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] rejects the notion of reality "in itself" apart from all human experience and instead treats reality as what reveals itself through practical engagement with the world.

## Origin and Context

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] conception of reality emerged from [[william-james|James]]'s critique of intellectualist [[philosophy|philosophy]], which treated reality as a fixed, completed system to be passively copied by the mind. [[william-james|James]] developed this view across the 1907 lectures, culminating in Lectures VII and VIII where he explicitly articulates the humanist doctrine that "reality, the object of the mind's search, is always a selection, conceived after the likeness of the heart's desire, the product of a human purpose" (008-097). Drawing on [[darwinism|Darwinian]] influence and the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]], [[william-james|James]] held that thought is an instrument of readjustment that "keeps pace with the [[darwinism|evolution]] of reality and the changing situations of mortal life" (001-113).

## Key Arguments

**Reality is selected, not merely found.** [[william-james|James]] argues that all philosophies are selective. The thinker who claims to want to know "all [[About|about]] reality" does not mean investigating everything that exists:
> "The thinker who declares he wants to know all about 'reality' does not mean that he wishes to investigate _everything_ which in any sense exists, but that he wishes to know what _he_ considers _best worth knowing_ -- and this, of course, implies a personal valuation, a purged and expurgated extract, which will not offend his taste." (008-086--008-089)

**The antithesis between "real" and "unreal" is a judgment of value.** What we call "real" depends on what matters to us:
> "For there could be no greater mistake than to suppose that the common antithesis between 'reality' and the 'un-real' usually means the same thing as the distinction between what 'exists' and what is absolutely non-existent. On the contrary, it is usually a judgment of value." (008-077--008-079)

**Reality is plastic and improvable.** [[humanism|Humanism]] encourages human enterprise by rejecting the assumption that the real is completely rigid:
> "The superiority of Humanism, then, lies in this, that it does not discourage human enterprise by assuming that the real is completely rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort. In the drama that unrolls reality, every man, it teaches, has a duty and a power to play his humble but essential part." (008-127--008-130)

**Reality "in itself" is canceled from pragmatic reckoning.** The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] refuses to speculate about a reality that transcends all human faculties:
> "Reality, as it is assumed, presumed, or guessed to be 'in itself,' apart from our experience of it, is cancelled from his reckonings. For he cannot discover how he (or anyone) can get any 'knowledge' or 'intuition' which transcends all human faculties." (008-114--008-117)

**The [[correspondence-theory|correspondence theory]] fails because reality must serve as a test.** The intellectualist theory that truth is correspondence to reality collapses because reality cannot simultaneously be that which is unknown and that which verifies:
> "To prove the correspondence, then, the 'reality' would have somehow to be known apart from the truth-claim of the thought, in order that the two might be compared and found to agree. But if the reality were already known directly, what would be the need of asserting an idea of it and claiming 'truth' for this?" (005-088--005-090)

**Thought refers to reality but is purposive, not passive.** The mind does not passively reflect reality; it selectively engages it for practical purposes:
> "Whether the mind is idealizing an aspect of reality (as in mathematics) or abstracting, classifying, and predicting (as in science), it is always the fact that a particular kind of reality is needed for some serious or trivial purpose which guides the operations of the thinker." (008-062--008-064)

## Variants and Interpretations

**The threefold structure of reality.** Across the lectures, [[william-james|James]] implicitly distinguishes three dimensions of reality: (1) the **physical reality** of sense experience, composed of "a multitude of separate existences that act on one another" (002-013); (2) **reality as value-selection**, where what is "real" depends on what we care about (008-077); and (3) **reality as postulation**, where human beings project ideals -- religious, moral, aesthetic -- that "complete his vision of reality" (003-175).

**Humanist vs. [[rationality|rational]]ist reality.** For the [[rationality|rational]]ist, reality is a timeless, [[the-absolute|absolute system]] awaiting discovery. For the humanist, reality is always a "world that reveals itself to him" (008-114), never the world "in itself." [[william-james|James]]'s humanism refuses any "inexperienceable relation to an unattainable reality" (005-117).

## Criticisms and Debates

Intellectualist critics charge that pragmatism reduces reality to whatever is useful, denying objective existence altogether. [[william-james|James]] responded that pragmatism does not deny an objective world but insists that "no truth and no reality are wholly 'objective,' in the sense of wholly indifferent to our action" (007-065--007-066). The human factor in all knowledge "does not preclude our apprehending and measuring an 'objective' world as real as, and more knowable than, any other theory can offer" (007-068--007-069).

Realists object that [[william-james|James]] conflates epistemology (how we know reality) with ontology (what reality is). [[william-james|James]]'s reply is pragmatic: a reality that cannot be experienced or verified is a reality "which does not count: it is not worth [[knowledge|knowing]] about" (008-095--008-096).
