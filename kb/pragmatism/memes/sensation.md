## Definition

In the [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]] critiqued by [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, "sensation" refers to the elementary, discrete unit of sensory experience -- the atomistic building block from which, according to classical empiricism, all mental content is constructed. [[william-james|James]]'s [[empiricism|radical empiricism]] overturned this model, demonstrating that sensations are not primordial givens but artificial abstractions carved out of an originally continuous [[experience|experiential flux]] by subsequent conceptual analysis.

## Origin & Context

The atomistic conception of sensation traces to [[david-hume|David Hume]], who reduced all mental content to "a number of elementary sensations" (002-018), treating the mind as a mosaic of discrete sensory units:

> Experience was a mosaic, of which the stones were the detached sensations, and their washed-out copies, the ideas. (002-030)

[[david-hume|Hume]]'s analysis, modeled on the atomistic physics of his day, held that "every distinct perception (or 'impression') was a distinct existence" (002-021) -- a view that went undisputed for over a century.

> If this analysis of the mind were correct--and its correctness was not disputed for more than a hundred years, for were not the sensations admitted to be the ultimate analysis of all that was perceived? (002-033)

[[immanuel-kant|Kant]] accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s premise that experience arrives as "a manifold, a whirl of separate sensations" (002-075), then attempted to rescue coherence by adding synthetic [[apriorism|a priori]] categories imposed by the mind. But this entire framing, [[william-james|James]] argued, was the root error.

## Key Arguments

1. **Sensations are secondary, not primary.** [[william-james|James]]'s crucial inversion: what [[david-hume|Hume]] took as the elementary given is actually the product of conceptual dissection. "This separation was secondary and artificial" (002-141). [[experience|Experience]] does not begin as atoms requiring synthesis; it begins as a continuum requiring analysis.

2. **Relations are as immediate as sensations.** Against the assumption that only sensory qualities are directly given, [[william-james|James]] maintained that connective relations are equally present in experience:

   > The stream of experience no doubt contains what it is afterwards possible to single out as 'sensations,' but it presents them also as connected by 'relations.' Moreover, the 'sensations' or 'qualities' and their 'relations' exhibit the immediate indiscerptible unity of a fluid rather than a succession of flashes. (002-142)

3. **[[david-hume|Hume]]'s "sensations" were already abstracted.** [[william-james|James]] exposed the methodological error at the heart of the sensation doctrine:

   > Hume's mistake was to have started from experience *as partly analysed* by common sense, and not from the flux *as given*. His 'sensations' were the qualities already analysed out of the flux; he took these selections for the whole and neglected the other less obvious features in it--viz., the relations which floated them. (002-159)

4. **The puzzle of synthesis is self-inflicted.** Once one accepts the atomistic premise, connecting discrete sensations becomes an insoluble problem. The atomizing method "carried to its logical extreme demands that not only 'sensations' but also 'thoughts' should be essentially disconnected, and then, of course, *no* thinking can cohere" (002-130). Psychological atomism logically terminates in incoherence.

5. **Immediate experience does not require synthesis -- it calls for analysis.** The classical problem of epistemology was posed upside-down:

   > The whole conception of philosophy as aiming at uniting disjointed data in a higher synthesis runs counter to the real movement, which aims at the analysis of a given whole. (002-179)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **[[david-hume|Hume]]an sensation**: The classical view -- sensations are the fundamental, irreducible atoms of experience, each a "distinct existence" (002-059), with no intrinsic relations between them. This atomism "made havoc of all idea, of 'causation'" (002-044) and rendered the self a fiction.
- **[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] sensation**: Accepts the [[david-hume|Hume]]an starting point of "a manifold, a whirl of separate sensations" (002-075) but adds a transcendental apparatus of pure intuitions (Space, Time) and categories (Substance, Cause) imposed by the mind to produce coherent experience. [[william-james|James]] saw this as a vain complication that never bridged the gap between sensation-matter and mental form (002-092).
- **[[william-james|James]]ian sensation**: Sensations are not elementary givens but products of retrospective analysis. "The [[experience|stream of experience]] no doubt contains what it is afterwards possible to single out as 'sensations'" (002-142) -- the key phrase being "afterwards possible to single out." In genuine [[experience|immediate experience]], qualities come fused with relations; sensation as a discrete unit is a conceptual artifact.
- **Associationist sensation** (Mill, Bain): The British associationist school attempted to "build up a mind out of isolated sensations" (002-124) through laws of association, without [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] [[apriorism|a priori]] machinery. [[william-james|James]] rejected this as equally futile: "if all 'sensations' or qualities are separate entities, how can the addition of more 'distinct existences' of the same sort [[reality|real]]ly bind them together?" (002-125).

## Criticisms & Debates

The sensation-doctrine's dominance in modern philosophy created what [[william-james|James]] diagnosed as a systematic distortion of the entire epistemological project. Three lines of criticism are central to the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] rebuttal:

First, **the genetic fallacy**: [[david-hume|Hume]] (and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] after him) mistook the refined products of conceptual analysis for the raw materials of experience. As [[william-james|James]] put it, [[david-hume|Hume]] "started from experience *as partly analysed* by common sense, and not from the flux *as given*" (002-159). The philosopher who thinks he is describing primitive experience is actually redescribing what he previously dissected.

Second, **the binding problem**: If sensations are truly separate existences, no amount of association or transcendental machinery can explain how they cohere into a unified world. "Why does the chaotic 'matter' of sensations submit itself so tamely to the forming of the mind?" (002-094). [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] never answered this; the [[david-hume|Hume]]an critique that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] merely multiplied entities without necessity (002-090) remains potent.

Third, **the practical consequence**: In Lecture VII, [[william-james|James]]'s summary judgment is blunt -- intellectualist psychology "comminuted [the mind's integrity] into a dust-cloud of sensations" (007-005). The atomistic theory of sensation yields a picture of mind and world so fragmented that it cannot account for the most ordinary facts of experience: that we perceive cats sitting on walls, not "cat" plus "upon" plus "wall" as separate sense-data (002-170).

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] resolution is not to refine the sensation concept but to abandon it as a primitive category. Concepts, including "sensation" itself, "being ideal dissections of the perceptual flux, are discontinuous terms which have to be related by an act of thought, because they were made for this very purpose of distinction" (002-167). Sensation is a tool of analysis, not a building block of [[reality|real]]ity.
