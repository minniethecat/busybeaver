## Overview

Common Sense Philosophy, also known as the Scottish School of Common Sense, is the [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]] founded by Thomas Reid (1710–1796) in reaction to the [[scepticism|skepticism]] he saw as the inevitable outcome of the "way of ideas" shared by [[rationalism|Rationalism]] and [[british-empiricism|British Empiricism]]. The school's central claim is that certain beliefs — in the existence of an external world, in the reliability of perception and memory, in [[reality|the real]]ity of other minds — are delivered by common sense and stand in no need of philosophical justification. To demand a proof of them is to misunderstand the nature of proof itself.

[[william-james|William James]] invoked common sense repeatedly in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ as a philosophical counterweight to both [[rationality|rational]]ist system-building and [[empiricism|empiricist]] [[scepticism|skepticism]]. Common sense, for [[william-james|James]], was not a philosophical school so much as a layer of belief — the inherited, unreflective stock of [[belief|conviction]]s that pragmatism must respect even as it subjects them to critical examination.

## Core Ideas

Common Sense Philosophy is defined by several interlocking commitments:

1. **The primacy of [[common-sense]] belief.** There are certain first principles — the existence of a material world, the reliability of the senses, [[reality|the real]]ity of causal power — that are justified by their very unavoidability. They are not conclusions of [[rationality|reason]]ing but presuppositions of it.

2. **Rejection of the "way of ideas."** Reid argued that Locke, Berkeley, and [[david-hume|Hume]] shared a fatal assumption: that the immediate objects of perception are ideas in the mind, not things in the world. Once this premise is granted, Reid held, [[scepticism|skepticism]] about the external world is inevitable — there is no valid inference from the existence of ideas to the existence of things.

3. **Direct [[reality|real]]ism about perception.** Against the representational theory of perception, Reid defended a form of direct [[reality|real]]ism: in perception we are directly aware of objects, not of mental intermediaries.

4. **[[philosophy|Philosophy]] as the systematization of common sense.** The task of philosophy is not to overturn common sense but to articulate and defend it. Where philosophy and common sense conflict, the philosopher has almost certainly gone wrong.

## Relation to Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s relationship to Common Sense Philosophy was complex. He admired its anti-skeptical spirit and shared its impatience with philosophical theories that contradict lived experience:

> He accepted, therefore, from common sense the belief that physical reality is composed of a multitude of separate existences that act on one another, and tried to conceive mental life strictly on the same model. (002-012--002-014)

But [[william-james|James]] was not a simple [[common-sense]] [[reality|real]]ist. He recognized that common sense itself is a historical product — a set of beliefs that have proven their practical utility over generations:

> Hume's mistake was to have started from experience _as partly analysed_ by common sense, and not from the flux _as given_. (002-160)

For [[william-james|James]], common sense was not a foundation but a tool — one among many ways of organizing experience:

> But common sense is undismayed by the pragmatist's discovery that if the Syllogism is to have any sense its premisses _must_ be taken as disputable. (006-165--006-167)

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]]'s distinctive contribution was to show that [[common-sense]] beliefs could be both indispensable (we cannot live or think without them) and revisable (we may find better ones). This is the pragmatic theory of truth applied to the most fundamental layer of belief.

## Key Figures

The Scottish School's principal representative, and the figure most relevant to [[william-james|James]]'s discussion, was Thomas Reid (1710–1796), professor of moral philosophy at Glasgow and [[david-hume|Hume]]'s most formidable contemporary critic. Reid's major works — _An Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of [[common-sense|Common Sense]]_ (1764), _Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man_ (1785), and _Essays on the Active Powers of Man_ (1788) — developed a comprehensive alternative to the [[empiricism|empiricist]] theory of ideas.

Reid's influence extended through Dugald Stewart (1753–1828), his successor at Edinburgh, and Sir William Hamilton (1788–1856), whose edition of Reid's works helped bring the Scottish School to the attention of nineteenth-century philosophers including [[william-james|James]] Mill and, indirectly, [[william-james|William James]] himself.

## Historical Context

Common Sense Philosophy emerged in the Scottish Enlightenment of the late eighteenth century, a period of intense philosophical activity centered on the universities of Glasgow, Edinburgh, and Aberdeen. Reid was himself a product of this milieu — he studied at Aberdeen and taught there before succeeding Adam Smith at Glasgow.

The school arose in direct response to [[david-hume|Hume]]'s _Treatise of Human Nature_ (1739). Reid initially admired [[david-hume|Hume]]'s logical rigor but came to believe that [[david-hume|Hume]] had demonstrated, not that [[common-sense]] beliefs are false, but that a philosophy built on the "way of ideas" cannot justify them. The proper response to [[david-hume|Hume]]an [[scepticism|skepticism]], Reid argued, was not to find better arguments but to abandon the premise that made [[scepticism|skepticism]] inevitable.

In the nineteenth century, the Scottish School became the dominant philosophy in American universities — a fact of considerable importance for understanding the intellectual context in which pragmatism emerged. [[william-james|James]] himself was educated in a curriculum shaped by Scottish [[reality|real]]ism, and his repeated appeals to common sense in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ reflect this inheritance.
