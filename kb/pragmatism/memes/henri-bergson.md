## Overview

Henri Bergson (1859–1941) was the most celebrated French philosopher of the early twentieth century, renowned for his original contributions to the philosophy of time, consciousness, and biological [[darwinism|evolution]]. [[william-james|William James]] regarded Bergson as a kindred spirit in the revolt against [[intellectualism]], praising his work for confirming and extending [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] insights from a European perspective.

Bergson's philosophy centers on the distinction between _la durée_ (lived duration) — the qualitative, flowing character of temporal experience — and the spatialized, quantitative time of science and common sense. This distinction underpinned his broader critique of the intellect as a practical instrument that distorts [[reality|real]]ity when it attempts to grasp the nature of consciousness, freedom, and life itself.

## Life

- **1859** — Born in Paris to a Polish Jewish father and an English mother.
- **1878–1881** — Studied at the École Normale Supérieure.
- **1889** — Published _Time and Free Will_ (_Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience_), his doctoral thesis.
- **1896** — Published _Matter and Memory_ (_Matière et mémoire_).
- **1900** — Appointed to the Chair of Greek [[philosophy|Philosophy]] at the Collège de France.
- **1907** — Published _Creative Evolution_ (_L'Évolution créatrice_), his most influential work.
- **1913** — Visited the United States; his lectures drew enormous crowds.
- **1927** — Awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature.
- **1941** — Died in occupied Paris.

## Key Ideas

### Duration (La Durée)

Bergson's most fundamental insight — that time as lived is qualitatively different from time as measured. Real duration is a continuous, indivisible flow in which past, present, and future interpenetrate, irreducible to a series of discrete instants arranged on a spatial timeline.

### The Intellect and Intuition

Bergson argued that the intellect, shaped by [[darwinism|evolution]]ary pressures for practical action, deals in static, spatialized concepts that cannot capture the fluidity of lived [[reality|real]]ity. Genuine knowledge of duration and consciousness requires _intuition_ — a direct, sympathetic apprehension of [[reality|real]]ity from within. This critique of [[intellectualism]] paralleled [[william-james|James]]'s own arguments: "[[intellectualism|Intellectualism]] appeals to [[logic|Logic]], which it conceives as a purely formal science" (006-004), while intuition attends to the concrete flow of [[experience]].

### Creative Evolution

Bergson's [[darwinism|evolution]]ary philosophy challenged both mechanistic [[darwinism|Darwinism]] and teleological finalism, proposing instead an _élan vital_ (vital impulse) — a creative, unpredictable force driving the diversification of life. This view resonated with [[william-james|James]]'s pluralistic vision of an "unfinished" universe open to genuine novelty.

## Relation to Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]] read Bergson's _Creative Evolution_ shortly after publishing _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ and recognized a powerful ally. In a letter to Bergson, [[william-james|James]] wrote that Bergson's work had "simply conquered" him. While Bergson did not identify as a [[pragmatism|pragmatist]], his emphasis on action, his critique of static intellectual categories, and his attention to concrete experience placed him in close alignment with [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] themes.

The two philosophers shared a [[belief|conviction]] that philosophy had been distorted by its preference for the fixed, the abstract, and the eternal over the flowing, the concrete, and the temporal. [[william-james|James]] saw in Bergson's duration a metaphysical foundation for the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] insistence that [[truth]] is not a static property but something that "happens to an idea."
