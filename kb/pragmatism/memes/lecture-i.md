## CHAPTER I

[001-001] THE GENESIS OF PRAGMATISM

[001-002] There is a curious impression to-day in the world of thought that

[001-003] [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] is the most audacious of philosophic novelties, the most

[001-004] anarchical transvaluation of all respectable traditions. Sometimes it is

[001-005] pictured as an insurgence of emotion against logic, sometimes as an

[001-006] assault of [[religion|theology]] upon the integrity of [[reason|Pure Reason]]. One day it is

[001-007] described as the reckless theorizing of dilettanti whose knowledge of

[001-008] philosophy is too superficial to require refutation, the next as a

[001-009] transatlantic importation of the debasing slang of the Wild West. Abroad

[001-010] it is frequently denounced as an outbreak of the sordid commercialism of

[001-011] the Anglo-Saxon mind.

[001-012] All these ideas are mistaken. Pragmatism is neither a revolt against

[001-013] philosophy nor a revolution in philosophy, except in so far as it is an

[001-014] important [[darwinism|evolution]] of philosophy. It is a collective name for the most

[001-015] modern solution of puzzles which have impeded philosophical progress

[001-016] from time immemorial, and it has arisen naturally in the course of

[001-017] philosophical reflection. It answers the big problems which are as

[001-018] familiar to the scientist and the theologian as to the metaphysician and

[001-019] epistemologist, and which are both intelligible and interesting to

[001-020] common sense.

[001-021] The following questions stand out: (1) Can the possibility of knowledge

[001-022] be maintained against [[david-hume|Hume]] and other sceptics? Certainly, if it can be

[001-023] shown that 'The New [[psychology|Psychology]]' has antiquated the analysis of mind

[001-024] which Hume assumed and '[[british-associationism|British Associationism]]' respectfully continued

[001-025] to uphold. (2) Seeing that inclination and volition indisputably play a

[001-026] part in the _[[belief|acceptance]]_ of all beliefs, scientific and religious, what

[001-027] is the logical significance of this fact? This yields the problem 'The

[001-028] Will to Believe,' and more generally of 'the place of Will in

[001-029] cognition.' (3) Is there no criterion by which the divergent claims of

[001-030] rival creeds and philosophies--to be possessed of unconditional

[001-031] truth--can be scientifically tested? The sceptic's sneer, that the

[001-032] shifting systems of philosophy illustrate only the changing fashions of

[001-033] a great illusion about man's capacity for truth, plunges dogmatism into

[001-034] a 'Dilemma,' from which it can emerge only by finding a way of

[001-035] discriminating a 'truth' from an 'error,' and so solving the 'problem of

[001-036] [[truth|Truth]] and Error.' The weird verbalism of the traditional [[logic|Logic]] suggests

[001-037] a problem which strikes deeper even than the question, 'What _do_ you

[001-038] mean by truth?' viz.: 'Do you mean anything?' and so the 'problem of

[001-039] Meaning' is propounded by the failure of [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]]. Is Logic not

[001-040] concerned at all with _meaning_, is it only juggling with empty forms of

[001-041] words? Lastly, if from all this there springs up a conviction of 'The

[001-042] Bankruptcy of [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]],' the question suggests itself whether the

[001-043] relation between abstract thinking and concrete experience, between

[001-044] 'Thought' and 'Life,' has been rightly grasped. Is life worth living

[001-045] only for the sake of philosophic contemplation, or is thinking only

[001-046] worth doing to aid us in the struggle for life? Are 'theory' and

[001-047] 'practice' two separate kingdoms with rigid frontiers, strictly guarded,

[001-048] or does it appear that theories which cannot be applied have, in the

[001-049] end, neither worth, nor truth, nor even meaning?

[001-050] It is plain from this catalogue of inquiries that Pragmatism makes no

[001-051] abrupt breach in tradition. It is not the _pétroleuse_ of philosophy. It

[001-052] does not wipe out the history of speculation in order to announce a

[001-053] millennium of new ideas; it claims, on the contrary, to be the

[001-054] culmination and _dénoûment_ of that history. It cannot rightly be

[001-055] represented as trying either to sell new lamps for old, or to

[001-056] jerry-build a new metaphysical system on the ruins of all previous

[001-057] achievements. Its [[reality|real]] task is singularly modest. It aims merely at

[001-058] instructing system-builders in the elementary laws which condition the

[001-059] stability of such structures and conduce to their conservation.

[001-060] It is therefore a grave mistake to regard it as a parochial

[001-061] eccentricity, as a specific Americanism. Nor is it the product of the

[001-062] misplaced ingenuity of individual paradox-mongers. It has come into

[001-063] being by the _convergence_ of distinct lines of thought pursued in

[001-064] different countries by different thinkers.

[001-065] 1. One of the most interesting of these has originated in the scientific

[001-066] world. The immense growth of [[science|scientific knowledge]] during the last

[001-067] century was bound to react on human conceptions of scientific procedure.

[001-068] The enormous number of new facts brought to light by manipulating

[001-069] [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] could not but modify our view of scientific law. Laws no

[001-070] longer seem to scientists the immutable foundations of an eternal order,

[001-071] but are inevitably treated as man-made formulae for grouping and

[001-072] predicting the events which verify them. The labours of physicists like

[001-073] Mach, Duhem, and Ostwald, point to alternative formulations of new

[001-074] hypotheses for the best established laws. The physics of Newton are no

[001-075] longer final, and the notion of 'energy' is a dangerous rival to the

[001-076] older conception of 'matter.' It is, of course, indifferent to the

[001-077] philosopher whether the new physics are successful in superseding the

[001-078] old or not. What it concerns him to note is that dogmatic confidence in

[001-079] the finality of scientific laws has given place to a belief that our

[001-080] "laws" are only working formulae for scientific purposes, and that no

[001-081] science can truly boast of having read off the mind of the Deity. As Sir

[001-082] J.J. Thomson neatly puts it, a scientific theory, for the enlightened

[001-083] modern scientist, is a 'policy and not a creed.' Science has become

[001-084] content to be only 'a conceptual shorthand,' provided that its message

[001-085] be humanly intelligible. It no longer claims truth because abstractly

[001-086] and absolutely it 'corresponds with Nature,' but because it yields a

[001-087] convenient means of mastering the flux of events.

[001-088] Even mathematics, long the pattern of absolute knowledge, has not

[001-089] escaped the stigma of relativity. 'Metageometries' have been invented by

[001-090] Riemann and Lobatschewski as rivals to the assumptions of Euclid, and

[001-091] the brilliant writings of Poincaré have explained the human devices on

[001-092] which mathematical concepts rest. Euclidean geometry is reduced to a

[001-093] useful interpretation of the data of experience; it is not theoretically

[001-094] the only one. Its superior validity is dependent upon its use when

[001-095] applied to the physical world. Even mathematics, therefore, lend

[001-096] themselves to the philosophic inference drawn by [[henri-bergson|Henri Bergson]] and

[001-097] others, that all conceptual systems of the human mind have a merely

[001-098] conditional truth, depending on the circumstances of their application.

[001-099] 2. Another fountain-head of Pragmatic philosophy has been [[darwinian-revolution|Darwinism]].

[001-100] Indeed, the Pragmatic is the only philosophizing which has completely

[001-101] assimilated Evolution. The insight into the real fluidity of natural

[001-102] species ought long ago to have toned down the artificial rigidity of

[001-103] logical classifications. To know reality man can no longer rest in a

[001-104] 'timeless' contemplation of a static system; he must expand his thoughts

[001-105] so as to cope with a perpetually changing process. Since the world

[001-106] changes, his 'truths' must change to fit it. He is faced with the

[001-107] necessity of a continuous reconstruction of beliefs. This influence of

[001-108] [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] has inspired the logical theories of [[john-dewey|Professor Dewey]] and the

[001-109] '[[chicago-school|Chicago School]]' of Pragmatists. Thought in their writings is

[001-110] essentially the instrument of this readjustment. Its function is to

[001-111] effect the necessary changes in beliefs as economically and usefully as

[001-112] possible. It is an evolving process which keeps pace with the evolution

[001-113] of reality and the changing situations of mortal life.

[001-114] 3. It is not, however, entirely the reaction of science upon philosophy

[001-115] which has given birth to Pragmatism. [[philosophy|Philosophy]] itself has been rent by

[001-116] internal convulsions. These have been emphasized in the work of Dr.

[001-117] [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]], who has shown that already in the days of [[plato|Plato]] the

[001-118] distinction between 'truth' and 'error' was baffling philosophy, that

[001-119] Plato's _Theaetetus_ has failed to establish it, and that the famous

[001-120] dictum of Protagoras, 'Man is the measure of all things,' distinctly

[001-121] foreshadows the 'Pragmatic,' or, as he calls it, the 'Humanist,'

[001-122] solution of the difficulty.

[001-123] Elsewhere Dr. Schiller has commented on the controversies raised by

[001-124] Hume's criticism of dogmatism. He has shown that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] failed to answer

[001-125] Hume because he accepted Hume's psychology, and that no _[[apriorism|a priori]]_

[001-126] philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable

[001-127] doctrine. The idealistic theories of the 'Absolute' reveal their

[001-128] futility by their want of application to the genuine problems of life,

[001-129] and by the theoretic agnosticism from which they cannot escape. Hence

[001-130] the need for a new Theory of [[knowledge|Knowledge]] and a thorough reform of Logic.

[001-131] 4. At this point he joins forces with Mr. Alfred Sidgwick, who has long

[001-132] been urging a radical criticism of the procedures of Formal Logic, and

[001-133] shown the gulf between them and the processes of concrete thought.

[001-134] Sidgwick has demonstrated that the belief in formal truth renders Logic

[001-135] merely verbal, and that the actual _meaning_ of assertions completely

[001-136] escapes it.

[001-137] 5. The most sensational approach to Pragmatism, however, is that from

[001-138] the side of [[religious-experience|religion]]. The Pragmatic method of deciding religious

[001-139] problems, which asserts the legitimacy of a 'Faith' that precedes

[001-140] knowledge, has always been, more or less consciously, practised by the

[001-141] religious. It is brilliantly advocated in the _Thoughts_ of Pascal, and

[001-142] clearly and forcibly defended in that most remarkable essay in

[001-143] unprofessional philosophy, Cardinal Newman's _Grammar of Assent_. This

[001-144] line of reasoning, however, is most familiarly associated with the name

[001-145] of [[william-james|William James]]; he first illustrated the [[pragmatic-method|Pragmatic Method]] by a famous

[001-146] paper (for a theological audience) on _[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]],_ and founded

[001-147] the psychological study of religious experience in his [[alfred-sidgwick|Gifford Lectures]]

[001-148] on _The Varieties of Religious [[experience|Experience]]_.

[001-149] 6. This brings us to the last, and historically the most fertile, of the

[001-150] sources of Pragmatism, Psychology. The publication in 1890 of James's

[001-151] great _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_ opened a new era in the history of that

[001-152] science. More than that, it was destined in the long run to work a

[001-153] transformation in philosophy as a whole, by introducing into it those

[001-154] biological and voluntaristic principles to which he afterwards applied

[001-155] the generic name of Pragmatism, or philosophy of action. We must pass,

[001-156] then, to consider the New Psychology of William James.
