## Definition

A hypothesis, in [[william-james|William James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]], is a provisional belief or [[postulation|postulate]] that is freely adopted for the purpose of testing its practical consequences. Rather than requiring antecedent justification from self-evident first principles, hypotheses earn their legitimacy through the [[verification]] process — their capacity to organize experience, generate successful predictions, and guide effective action. This conception of knowledge as hypothesis-driven and experimentally tested is central to [[william-james|James]]'s break with classical [[rationality|rationalist]] and [[empiricism|empiricist]] epistemology.

> So long as the logician regards his premisses not as hypotheses to be tested, but as established truths, he must condemn the Syllogism as a formal fallacy. It is inevitably a _petitio principii_. [006-143--006-145]

## Origin & Context

The elevation of the hypothesis from a mere rhetorical device to the fundamental unit of scientific [[rationality|reason]]ing was a hallmark of 19th-century philosophy of science. Figures like William Whewell, John Stuart Mill, and especially Charles Sanders Peirce (with his notion of "abduction") argued that scientific discovery proceeds not by deductive inference from established truths but by the creative formulation and empirical testing of hypotheses.

[[william-james|James]] extends this insight from [[science]] to philosophy and [[religious-experience|religion]]. The [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] treats all beliefs — scientific, philosophical, and [[religious-experience|religious]] — as hypotheses whose truth is measured by their consequences. This generalization of the hypothetico-experimental method across all domains of inquiry is one of the most distinctive features of [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism.

> The enormous number of new facts brought to light by manipulating hypotheses could not but modify our view of scientific law. Laws no longer seem to scientists the immutable foundations of an eternal order, but are inevitably treated as man-made formulae for grouping and predicting the events which verify them. [001-069--001-072]

## Key Arguments

### Hypotheses Replace Fixed Truths

[[william-james|James]] argues that the modern scientific worldview has fundamentally transformed the status of knowledge claims. What were once regarded as necessary truths or "laws of nature" are now understood as hypotheses — provisional formulations that are always subject to revision in light of new evidence. Even the physics of Newton, once considered the paradigm of certain knowledge, is now understood as one hypothesis among possible alternatives.

> The labours of physicists like Mach, Duhem, and Ostwald, point to alternative formulations of new hypotheses for the best established laws. The physics of Newton are no longer final, and the notion of 'energy' is a dangerous rival to the older conception of 'matter.' [001-074--001-075]

### The Universality of Hypothetical Thinking

[[william-james|James]] argues that all domains of human belief — not just science — operate through the postulation and testing of hypotheses. Mathematical laws, physical theories, [[religion|religious belief]]s, and ethical principles are all, from a pragmatic standpoint, hypotheses that are verified (or not) by their practical effects.

> Mathematical laws, physical hypotheses, religious beliefs, and ethical principles — all are hypotheses. (005-173)

### Fresh Hypotheses and Progress

For [[william-james|James]], intellectual progress depends on the willingness to formulate and test new hypotheses. The demand for absolute certainty is not only unfulfillable but actively harmful, because it discourages the creative experimentation that drives both scientific discovery and philosophical insight.

> Ideals and fresh hypotheses is demanded; but that which causes one hypothesis to prevail over another is always its superior practical value. (003-099)

### Reality Construction Through Hypotheses

[[william-james|James]] extends the hypothetical method even to our conception of [[reality|real]]ity itself. What we call "[[reality|real]]ity" is not a fixed, pre-given structure but something we actively construct by adding interpretive hypotheses to the raw data of sensation. This constructivist dimension of [[william-james|James]]'s thought is one of its most radical philosophical implications.

> To construct 'reality,' we can add hypotheses to it to make it more congruous with our desires and more responsive to our control. (002-211)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Scientific Hypothesis**: The classical model — a testable proposition about the natural world, subject to experimental confirmation or disconfirmation. In [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, the scientific hypothesis is the paradigm but not the exclusive form of hypothetical [[rationality|reason]]ing.
- **Religious Hypothesis**: [[william-james|James]] controversially extends the hypothetical model to [[religion|religious belief]], arguing that [[religious-experience|faith]] is a "working hypothesis" whose verification comes through the transformed quality of the believer's life.
- **Metaphysical Hypothesis**: Philosophical positions (materialism, theism, idealism) are treated as large-scale hypotheses about the ultimate nature of [[reality|real]]ity, to be evaluated by their practical consequences rather than by abstract argument alone.
- **Abduction (Peirce)**: Charles Sanders Peirce's term for the creative inference that generates new hypotheses — distinct from both deduction (drawing necessary conclusions) and induction (generalizing from observed cases).

## Criticisms & Debates

**The "anything goes" objection**: Critics argue that if all beliefs are hypotheses verified by their practical consequences, then any belief that "works" for someone counts as true. [[william-james|James]]'s response is that verification is a long-term, communal, and rigorous process — not merely subjective satisfaction. A hypothesis must work *in the long run* and *for the community of inquirers*, not merely provide temporary comfort.

**Hypothesis vs. [[truth|Truth]]**: A key tension in [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism is whether hypotheses that "work" are *thereby true* (the radical reading) or merely the best available candidates for truth (the moderate reading). This debate continues in contemporary [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] scholarship.

**The role of creativity**: [[william-james|James]]'s emphasis on the "free" postulation of hypotheses raises questions about the constraints on legitimate hypothesizing. If hypotheses are freely created, what prevents purely fantastical beliefs from qualifying? [[william-james|James]]'s answer lies in the verification process: a hypothesis that cannot be connected to concrete experience and tested by its consequences will be eliminated — but only if we are willing to subject it to the test.
