## Overview

John Locke (1632–1704) was the founding figure of British empiricism and one of the most influential philosophers of the Enlightenment. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Locke stands at the head of the empiricist tradition that [[william-james|James]] both honors and radicalizes — pragmatism is, in [[william-james|James]]'s self-understanding, empiricism carried to its logical conclusion. Locke's insistence that all knowledge originates in experience, his critique of innate ideas, and his account of the mind as initially a "white paper" (tabula rasa) established the framework within which [[william-james|James]] would later develop his more dynamic, functionalist account of mind.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1632** — Born in Wrington, Somerset, England.
- **1652** — Entered Christ Church, Oxford, where he studied medicine and natural philosophy.
- **1666** — Met Lord Ashley (later Earl of Shaftesbury), beginning a lifelong political and intellectual partnership.
- **1689** — Published _An Essay Concerning Human Understanding_ and _Two Treatises of Government_.
- **1690** — Published _Two Treatises_ (dated 1690, actually published 1689), establishing his political philosophy.
- **1704** — Died at Oates, Essex.

## Key Contributions

Locke's _Essay Concerning Human Understanding_ (1689) is the founding document of empiricism: the argument that all ideas derive from experience (sensation and reflection), that there are no innate ideas, and that knowledge is limited to what experience can warrant. This empiricist commitment is the bedrock on which [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism is built — [[william-james|James]] never repudiates Locke's experiential starting point, only his overly passive conception of the mind.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]] inherits Locke's empiricist commitment to grounding knowledge in experience but transforms it in two crucial ways:

1. **The mind is active, not passive**: Where Locke's mind receives simple ideas and combines them, [[william-james|James]]'s mind is always selecting, attending, and interpreting — consciousness is purposive from the start.

2. **Experience includes relations**: Where Locke's experience consists of discrete, atomistic ideas, [[william-james|James]]'s experience includes connections, transitions, and relations — this is the insight of [[radical-empiricism|radical empiricism]].
