## Overview

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist whose methodological doubt and quest for certainty established the rationalist tradition in modern philosophy. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Descartes appears as the archetypal intellectualist — the philosopher who demanded absolute, antecedent certainty as the foundation for knowledge and who treated the mind as a self-sufficient thinking substance distinct from the body. [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism is, in significant part, an extended critique of the Cartesian demand for foundations: the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]] replaces Descartes's quest for certainty with a forward-looking process of [[verification|verification]] through experience.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1596** — Born in La Haye, France.
- **1619** — Experienced a series of dreams that inspired his method of radical doubt.
- **1637** — Published _Discourse on the Method_, introducing "I think, therefore I am" (cogito ergo sum).
- **1641** — Published _Meditations on First Philosophy_, his most sustained philosophical work.
- **1649** — Invited to Stockholm by Queen Christina of Sweden.
- **1650** — Died in Stockholm of pneumonia.

## Key Contributions

Descartes's method of radical doubt — doubting everything that can be doubted until reaching an indubitable foundation — established the paradigm for modern epistemology. His dualism of mind and body (res cogitans and res extensa) set the terms for centuries of debate about the mind-body problem.

[[william-james|James]] engages Descartes on multiple fronts: he rejects the Cartesian demand for absolute certainty (replacing it with pragmatic verification), rejects Cartesian dualism (replacing it with a functionalist account in which mind and body are aspects of a unified [[experience|experiential]] process), and rejects the conception of mind as a self-sufficient thinking substance (replacing it with the [[stream-of-consciousness|stream of consciousness]]).

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]] treats Descartes as the founder of the intellectualist tradition that pragmatism must overcome:

| Cartesiansim | Pragmatist Alternative |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Quest for absolute certainty | Fallibilist [[verification|verification]] through experience |
| Mind as thinking substance | [[consciousness|Consciousness]] as a functional process |
| Mind-body dualism | Psychophysical continuity |
| Foundationalism | [[postulation|Postulation]] and testing |
