## Overview

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was a German philosopher best known for his metaphysics of the will and his philosophical [[pessimism|pessimism]]. In his major work, _The World as Will and Representation_ (1818), Schopenhauer argued that the underlying reality of the world is not rational, as [[hegel|Hegel]] claimed, but a blind, striving will that manifests itself in all of nature and, most painfully, in human desire and suffering.

## Relevance to Pragmatism

Schopenhauer appears in the pragmatist narrative primarily as a representative of philosophical [[pessimism|pessimism]] — the view that [[reality|reality]] is fundamentally irrational and that existence is characterized by suffering rather than progress. [[william-james|William James]] discusses pessimism at length in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (especially Lecture I), contrasting it with the melioristic optimism of the pragmatist outlook.

For [[william-james|James]], Schopenhauer's pessimism represents one pole of the philosophical temperament: where rationalism tends toward an optimistic monism (all is one, and the one is good), Schopenhauer's irrationalism tends toward a pessimistic monism (all is one, and the one is blind striving). The pragmatist alternative is a [[pluralism|pluralistic]] [[meliorism|meliorism]]: the world is neither guaranteed good nor inevitably bad, but improvable through human effort.

## Key Works

- _The World as Will and Representation_ (1818/1844) — his magnum opus
- _On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason_ (1813) — his doctoral dissertation
