## Overview

German Idealism was the philosophical movement that dominated German academic philosophy from the publication of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_ (1781) through Hegel's death (1831). It was characterized by the [[belief|conviction]] that [[reality|real]]ity is fundamentally mental or spiritual in nature, that systematic philosophy can grasp the absolute ground of all being, and that the structure of [[rationality|reason]] is identical with the structure of [[reality|real]]ity itself.

For [[william-james|William James]] and the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition, German Idealism — particularly in its Hegelian and neo-Hegelian forms — was the primary adversary. [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907) is, in large measure, an extended argument against the idealist claim that truth is a matter of logical coherence within a complete system, rather than a matter of practical consequences in lived [[experience]].

## Origins & Development

German Idealism emerged from the perceived incompleteness of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s critical philosophy. [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] had argued that knowledge is limited to the phenomenal [[reality|real]]m — things as they appear to us — while the noumenal [[reality|real]]m of "things in themselves" remains inaccessible. The post-[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] idealists found this duality unsatisfying and sought to overcome it by arguing that [[reality|real]]ity itself is a manifestation of mind or spirit (_Geist_).

- **[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]** (1724–1804) provided the starting point: the mind actively structures experience through categories, but cannot know things in themselves.
- **Fichte** (1762–1814) radicalized [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] by eliminating the thing-in-itself and grounding all [[reality|real]]ity in the activity of the absolute ego.
- **Schelling** (1775–1854) developed a philosophy of nature that treated the natural world as "visible spirit."
- **Hegel** (1770–1831) built the most ambitious system, tracing the dialectical self-development of Absolute Spirit through logic, nature, and history.

## Relation to Pragmatism

[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] defined itself in conscious opposition to the German Idealist tradition. [[william-james|James]]'s famous distinction between the "tender-minded" and "tough-minded" temperaments in philosophy maps closely onto the idealist/[[empiricism|empiricist]] divide — with German Idealism as the purest expression of the tender-minded temperament.

[[william-james|James]]'s specific objections included:

1. **The denial of novelty.** Idealist systems treat time and change as merely apparent, reducing genuine novelty to the unfolding of what was already implicit in the Absolute. For [[william-james|James]], this "block universe" view renders life meaningless and action pointless.

2. **The conflation of truth with coherence.** Idealism judges truth by logical consistency within a complete system of thought. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] judges truth by practical consequences in concrete experience. As [[william-james|James]] argued: "[[intellectualism|Intellectualism]] appeals to [[logic|Logic]], which it conceives as a purely formal science" (006-004) — but logic alone cannot determine what is [[reality|real]].

3. **The neglect of particular facts.** The idealist drive toward systematic completeness leads philosophers to "pass by with contempt" the messy, particular facts of experience that pragmatism treats as primary (005-023).

4. **Practical irrelevance.** For all its intellectual grandeur, German Idealism offered no method for resolving concrete disputes. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]'s "[[pragmatism|pragmatic method]]" was designed to make philosophy relevant to life by asking of every concept: what practical difference does it make?

## Legacy & Criticism

The influence of German Idealism extended far beyond Germany. In Britain, the "British Idealists" — T.H. Green, F.H. Bradley, Bernard Bosanquet — dominated academic philosophy in the late nineteenth century. It was against this Anglo-Hegelian establishment that Russell, Moore, and the early analytic philosophers rebelled — a revolt that paralleled [[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] critique in America.

In retrospect, pragmatism and German Idealism share more than [[william-james|James]] acknowledged. Both traditions insist that the mind plays an active, constitutive role in knowledge; both reject the passive empiricism that treats the mind as a blank slate. The difference lies in how they understand this activity: for the idealist, the mind's contribution is a fixed transcendental structure; for the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]], it is an evolving set of habits and tools shaped by experience and tested by consequences.
