## Definition

Naturalism is the philosophical view that [[reality|real]]ity is exhausted by nature — that there is no supernatural realm, and that all phenomena, including mind, morality, and [[religion|religious experience]], can be understood through natural causes and processes. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, naturalism is both a starting point and a position to be transformed: [[william-james|James]] is a naturalist in his commitment to empirical methods and evolutionary explanations, but he rejects the reductionist naturalism that denies the reality of [[consciousness|conscious]] experience, [[free-will|free will]], and religious meaning.

## Origin & Context

Naturalism has ancient roots (the pre-Socratic materialists, Epicurus, Lucretius) but gained philosophical dominance in the nineteenth century with the rise of scientific [[darwinism|Darwinism]], which seemed to show that all of life — including human life — could be explained by natural processes without appeal to divine design. [[william-james|James]] was deeply shaped by this scientific naturalism: his training in physiology and psychology was thoroughly naturalistic, and he never repudiated the commitment to empirical evidence that naturalism embodies.

However, [[william-james|James]] believed that naturalism, as usually formulated, was too narrow. The "nature" that includes only matter and motion cannot account for the qualitative reality of [[consciousness|conscious]] experience, the efficacy of conscious choice, or the transformative power of [[religious-experience|religious experience]].

## Key Arguments

### James's Pragmatic Naturalism

[[william-james|James]]'s contribution is to broaden the scope of naturalism without abandoning its empiricist rigor:

1. **Experience is part of nature**: [[consciousness|Consciousness]] and its qualities are natural phenomena — they are as real and as deserving of scientific study as any material process.

2. **Teleology is natural**: Purposes, goals, and values are not supernatural additions to nature but aspects of the natural functioning of conscious organisms.

3. **Religion is natural**: [[religious-experience|Religious experiences]] are natural psychological phenomena whose effects can be studied empirically. Whether they also point to a supernatural reality is a question to be settled pragmatically — by their fruits.

### Beyond Reductionist Naturalism

[[william-james|James]] resists the reductionist naturalism that says: "consciousness is nothing but brain activity." His reply is pragmatic: "nothing but" claims of this sort have no practical consequences (the experience of consciousness remains what it is regardless of the metaphysical claim). A properly pragmatic naturalism accepts all aspects of experience as data and seeks to understand their relationships without denying the reality of any of them.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Reductionist naturalism**: Everything is ultimately physical; mind is brain. Rejected by James.
- **Methodological naturalism**: Science proceeds as if only natural causes exist, without metaphysical commitment. A working principle James accepts.
- **Pragmatic naturalism**: James's position — all aspects of experience are real and natural, and their significance is measured by their practical consequences.
- **Religious naturalism**: The view that religious experience is a natural human capacity, whether or not it points to a supernatural reality. Compatible with James's position.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics charge that James's pragmatic naturalism is too permissive — by treating religious experience as empirical data, it opens the door to superstition. James's reply is the pragmatic one: test it. A religious belief, like a scientific hypothesis, is validated or invalidated by its consequences in ongoing experience.
