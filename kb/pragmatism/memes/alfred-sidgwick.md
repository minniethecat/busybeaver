## Overview

The Gifford Lectures are among the most prestigious lecture series in the English-speaking academic world, established by the will of Adam Lord Gifford (1820-1887), a Scottish judge and philosopher. Gifford's bequest mandated lectures on "Natural Theology" -- understood broadly as the knowledge of God through [[rationality|reason]] and experience rather than revelation -- at the four ancient Scottish universities: Edinburgh, Glasgow, St Andrews, and Aberdeen.

[[william-james|William James]] delivered two series of Gifford Lectures. The first, in 1901-1902 at Edinburgh, became _[[the-varieties-of-religious-experience|The Varieties of Religious Experience]]_ -- a work James himself references in the lectures as foundational to "the psychological study of religious experience" (001-147). The second, in 1906-1907, became _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking_ -- the work that serves as the primary corpus for this wiki.

## William James's Gifford Lectures

[[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] lectures were delivered at the University of Edinburgh in the winter of 1906-1907 and published later in 1907. The lectures marked a turning point in the public reception of pragmatism, transforming what had been a somewhat esoteric philosophical method developed by C.S. Peirce and [[william-james|James]] into a widely discussed intellectual movement.

The lectures are organized into eight parts, corresponding to chapters in the published volume:

| Lecture | Title | Key Theme |
|---------|-------|-----------|
| I | The Present Dilemma in [[philosophy|Philosophy]] | Tender-minded vs. tough-minded |
| II | What [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] Means | The [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] and theory of truth |
| III | Some Metaphysical Problems Pragmatically Considered | Substance, materialism, design |
| IV | The One and the Many | Monism vs. pluralism |
| V | [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[common-sense|Common Sense]] | The [[darwinism|evolution]] of cognitive categories |
| VI | [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]'s Conception of [[truth|Truth]] | [[truth|Truth]] as verification and satisfaction |
| VII | [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[humanism|Humanism]] | The human contribution to truth |
| VIII | [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[religion|Religion]] | Pluralism, meliorism, and the moral life |

## Significance

The lectures' bibliography cites both _The Will to Believe_ (008-141) and _The Varieties of Religious Experience_ (008-142) as foundational to the pragmatist treatment of religious life. The Gifford Lectures provided [[william-james|James]] with an ideal platform. The mandate for "natural [[religion|theology]]" -- broadly interpreted -- allowed him to address the full range of human concerns that pragmatism engaged: science, [[religious-experience|religion]], ethics, and metaphysics. The lectures' public character, delivered to a general educated audience rather than a specialist philosophical society, shaped [[william-james|James]]'s accessible, vivid, and non-technical style -- one of the reasons _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ remains one of the most widely read works of American philosophy.

The Gifford Lectureship has been held by many of the most important philosophers and scientists of the modern era, including [[henri-bergson|Henri Bergson]], Alfred North Whitehead, [[john-dewey|John Dewey]], Albert Schweitzer, Niels Bohr, Hannah Arendt, and many others. [[william-james|James]]'s lectures stand as one of the series' most enduring contributions.
