## CHAPTER VII

[007-001] THE BANKRUPTCY OF INTELLECTUALISM

[007-002] We have now struggled through the quagmires of intellectualist

[007-003] philosophy, and found that neither in its [[psychology|Psychology]], which divided the

[007-004] mind's integrity into a heap of faculties, and comminuted it into a

[007-005] dust-cloud of sensations; nor in its Epistemology, which ignored the

[007-006] will to know and the value of [[knowledge|knowing]]; nor in its [[logic|Logic]], which

[007-007] abstracted thought wholly from the thinking and the thinker, and so

[007-008] finally from, all meaning, could man find a practicable route of

[007-009] philosophic progress. But our struggles will not have been in vain if

[007-010] they have left us with a willingness to try the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative,

[007-011] and convinced us that it is not a wanton innovation, but the only path

[007-012] of salvation for the scientific spirit.

[007-013] But before we venture on it, it will be well to restore confidence in

[007-014] the solvency of human thought by analysing the causes of the bankruptcy

[007-015] of [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]] and exposing the extravagance of the assumptions

[007-016] which conducted to it.

[007-017] Was it not, after all, an unwarranted assumption that severed the

[007-018] intellect from its natural connection with human activity? No doubt it

[007-019] seemed to simplify the problem to suppose that the functioning of the

[007-020] intellect could be studied as a thing apart, and unrelated to the

[007-021] general context of the vital functions. Again, it was to simplify to

[007-022] assume that thought could be considered apart from the personality of

[007-023] the human thinker. But it should not have been forgotten that it is

[007-024] possible to pay too dearly for simplifications and abstractions, and

[007-025] that they all involve a risk, which the event may show should never have

[007-026] been taken. So it is in this case. Its rash assumptions confront

[007-027] Intellectualism with a host of problems it cannot attack. It can do

[007-028] nothing to assuage the conflict of opinions which all claim truth with

[007-029] equal confidence. It cannot understand the correction of error which is

[007-030] continually proceeding. Nor can it understand, either the existence of

[007-031] error or the meaning of truth, or the means of distinguishing between

[007-032] them. It has no means of testing and confuting even the wildest and

[007-033] maddest assertions. It cannot discriminate between the [[intuition|intuitions]] of the

[007-034] sage and of the lunatic. It is forced to view energy of will in knowing

[007-035] as a source merely of corruption, and when it finds that as a psychic

[007-036] fact willing is ineradicable, it must conclude that we are

[007-037] constitutionally incapable of that passive reflection of reality which

[007-038] it regards as the _sine qua non_ of truth. Hence, if disinterestedness

[007-039] is the condition of knowing, knowledge is impossible. And it is so

[007-040] entangled in its unintelligible theory of truth as a copying of reality

[007-041] that, rather than renounce it, when it finds that human knowing is _not_

[007-042] copying, it prefers a surrender to [[scepticism|Scepticism]].

[007-043] Yet is not its whole procedure a signal example of human arbitrariness

[007-044] and perversity? We professed to be impelled by logical necessity at

[007-045] every step, but were free to escape from all our perplexities by

[007-046] adopting the pragmatic inferences from them. The [[pragmatic-method|Pragmatic Method]] of

[007-047] observing the consequences readily suggests the means of discriminating

[007-048] between truth and error, of sifting values and of testing claims. And,

[007-049] though not infallible, it is adequate to all our needs. The pragmatic

[007-050] notion that _[[truth|Truth]] is practical_ closes the artificial gulf between the

[007-051] theoretic and the practical side of life, and assigns to truth a

[007-052] biological function and vital value. The humanist contention that _Truth

[007-053] is human_ rescues man from the despondency in which his failure to grasp

[007-054] [[absolutism|absolute truth]] had left him. The Protagorean dictum that _Man is the

[007-055] measure of all things_ assures him that _his_ knowledge may become

[007-056] adequate to _his_ reality, and that the value of truths and the

[007-057] differences between truth and error also are susceptible of estimation.

[007-058] True, this policy averts the bankruptcy of the intellect by scaling down

[007-059] the intolerable charges on it. True, practical knowledge is not

[007-060] absolute; but if it is enough to live by, is it not better to live by it

[007-061] than to be lured on to perish in the deserts of Scepticism by the

[007-062] _mirage_ of an absolute truth not humanly attainable? True, verification

[007-063] is not 'proof,' but as its conclusions are not incorrigible, its defects

[007-064] are not fatal, and its demands are not impracticable. True, no truth and

[007-065] no reality are wholly 'objective,' in the sense of wholly indifferent to

[007-066] our action; but to say that the human and 'subjective' factor in all

[007-067] knowledge must be taken into account does not preclude our apprehending

[007-068] and measuring an 'objective' world as [[reality|real]] as, and more knowable than,

[007-069] any other theory can offer.

[007-070] Thus the proposals of Pragmatism for reconstructing the business of the

[007-071] intellect, and rescuing it from the bankruptcy of Intellectualism, are

[007-072] not unreasonable. They open out to it a prospect of recovering its

[007-073] credit and its usefulness by returning to the service of Life.
