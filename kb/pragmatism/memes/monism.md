## Definition

Monism is the metaphysical doctrine that [[reality|real]]ity is ultimately one — that behind the apparent diversity of the world lies a single substance, principle, or system. In the context of [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, monism — especially in its "absolute" form — represents the tender-minded, rationalist temperament carried to its logical conclusion: a universe conceived as a seamlessly unified whole in which every particular is necessitated by the total system. [[william-james|James]] treats monism not as false but as one-sided: it captures the genuine unity and continuity of experience but neglects its equally genuine diversity and looseness.

## Origin & Context

Monism has ancient roots — Parmenides argued that all change is illusion and reality is a single, unchanging One — but its modern form is most powerfully expressed in the absolute idealism of Hegel and his Anglo-American followers. In [[william-james|James]]'s intellectual context, monism was the dominant philosophical position, championed by figures like [[fh-bradley|F.H. Bradley]] and [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]], who argued that all apparent diversity is ultimately reconciled in the Absolute.

[[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatic]] critique of monism is methodological: what practical difference does it make to believe that reality is one? His answer is characteristically nuanced — monism provides genuine psychological comfort (the "moral holiday" effect) — but it provides no guidance for navigating the particular, plural, and unfinished aspects of actual experience.

## Key Arguments

### James's Pragmatic Critique of Monism

[[william-james|James]] applies the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]] to monism: trace the practical consequences of believing in the One. His analysis reveals:

1. **The comfort of unity**: Believing that all evils are somehow reconciled in the Whole provides psychological relief — this is a real practical consequence.
2. **The cost of abstraction**: Monism purchases unity at the price of neglecting the genuinely loose, unfinished, and plural character of experience.
3. **The pragmatic alternative**: [[pluralism|Pluralism]] — the view that reality is partly unified and partly disconnected — is more adequate to experience and has better practical consequences.

### Monism in the Temperament Typology

In [[william-james|James]]'s famous classification:

| Temperament | Metaphysical Correlate | Practical Attitude |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Tender-minded (rationalist) | Monism | Passive trust in the Whole |
| Tough-minded (empiricist) | [[pluralism|Pluralism]] | Active engagement with particulars |

Pragmatism mediates: it recognizes the genuine pull toward unity (monism captures something real) while insisting on accountability to the plural, particular texture of experience.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Substance monism**: The view that there is only one kind of substance (e.g., only matter, or only mind). Spinoza's position.
- **Absolute monism**: The view that all of reality forms a single, logically interconnected system. Hegel's position; the target of James's critique.
- **Neutral monism**: The view that the fundamental stuff of reality is neither mental nor physical but neutral between the two. James's [[radical-empiricism|radical empiricism]] approaches this position.
- **Methodological monism**: The commitment to a single method of inquiry. Pragmatism explicitly rejects this in favor of methodological [[pluralism|pluralism]].

## Criticisms & Debates

The central debate between monism and [[pluralism|pluralism]] remains unresolved in philosophy: does the success of science in unifying apparently diverse phenomena support monism, or does the irreducible plurality of methods and perspectives support pluralism? [[william-james|James]]'s contribution is to shift the question from abstract metaphysics to pragmatic consequences — what difference does each view make to how we live?
