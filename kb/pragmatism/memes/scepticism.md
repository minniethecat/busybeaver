## Definition

Scepticism (or skepticism) is the philosophical view that certain or absolute knowledge is either unattainable or does not exist. In its strongest form, universal scepticism holds that no belief can be justified with certainty. In [[william-james|William James]]'s *[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]*, scepticism figures as the destructive consequence of holding truth to an impossible [[absolutism|absolutist]] standard — the inevitable endpoint of demanding that human knowledge meet criteria it can never satisfy.

## Origin & Context

Scepticism has ancient roots in Pyrrho of Elis (c. 360–270 BCE) and the Pyrrhonian tradition, which advocated suspension of judgment (*epoché*) as the path to tranquillity. In modern philosophy, Descartes's method of doubt and [[david-hume|Hume]]'s [[empiricism|empiricist]] scepticism set the stage for [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s critical response. [[william-james|James]] inherits this tradition but gives it a distinctively pragmatic treatment: rather than refuting scepticism through abstract argument, he argues that the demand for absolute certainty is itself the problem, and that a pragmatic redefinition of [[truth]] dissolves the sceptical challenge.

> At this point universal scepticism begins to raise its hydra head, and to grin at the dogmatist's discomfiture. For in point of fact the history of thought reveals, not a steady accumulation of indubitable truth, but a continuous strife of opinions, in which the most widely accepted beliefs daily succumb to fresh criticism and fall into disrepute as the 'errors of the past.' [004-083--004-088]

## Key Arguments

### Scepticism as the Nemesis of Absolutism

[[william-james|James]] portrays scepticism as the logical outcome of [[dogmatism|dogmatic]] philosophy. When truth is defined in [[absolutism|absolutist]] terms — as perfect correspondence with [[reality|real]]ity or as membership in an [[the-absolute|Absolute System]] — and it becomes clear that human beings can never attain such truth, the only consistent conclusion is sceptical despair. The demand for absolute certainty, [[william-james|James]] argues, is self-defeating.

> In view of the diversity of human beliefs and the discredit which has historically fallen on the most axiomatic articles of faith, we must either admit scepticism to be the issue of the debate, or else, condemning our absolute view of truth, find some means of utilizing the relative truths which are all that humanity seems able to grasp. [004-100--004-102]

### The Pragmatic Alternative

[[william-james|James]]'s solution to the sceptical challenge is not to refute scepticism on its own terms but to change the terms entirely. The pragmatic theory of truth replaces the demand for absolute, once-and-for-all certainty with a practical, ongoing process of [[verification]] through experience and action. By accepting that all human truths are provisional and subject to revision, pragmatism deprives scepticism of its destructive force.

> The pragmatic test yields _practical_, and not 'absolute,' certainty. The existence of absolute certainty is denied, and the demand for it, in a world which contains only the practical sort, merely plays into the hands of scepticism. (005-225)

### Relativism and Scepticism

[[william-james|James]] distinguishes between a debilitating scepticism and a pragmatic [[relativism]] that acknowledges the fallibility of all human knowledge without abandoning the distinction between better and worse beliefs. The key is not to deny uncertainty but to manage it through continuous testing and practical engagement.

> A scepticism which infers from the fact that no 'truths' are absolute that all may reasonably be overthrown. The only obstacle to its triumph lies in the existence of 'relative' truths which are _not_ absolute, and do not claim to be. [005-007--005-010]

### The Absolute and the Sceptical Trap

[[william-james|James]] shows how even sophisticated [[philosophy|philosophical systems]] that invoke an [[the-absolute|Absolute Mind]] fail to escape the sceptical problem. If truth is defined as correspondence with the Absolute's experience, the test remains illusory — for we have no access to the Absolute's perspective — and the result is again "depressing scepticism."

> But as there is no way for us of sharing the Absolute Experience, our test is again illusory, and productive of a depressing scepticism; and, again, we have only asserted that truth is what _claims_ to be part of the Absolute System. [005-137--005-139]

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Pyrrhonian Scepticism**: The classical Greek tradition of suspending judgment on all matters, seeking tranquillity through the acknowledgment that for every argument an equal counter-argument can be made.
- **Cartesian Scepticism**: Methodological doubt as a tool for finding indubitable foundations — Descartes's strategy of doubting everything that can possibly be doubted, leading to the *cogito*.
- **[[david-hume|Hume]]an Scepticism**: The [[empiricism|empiricist]] conclusion that induction, causation, and the self cannot be [[rationality|rational]]ly justified, leaving custom and habit as the only guides to belief.
- **Pragmatic Anti-Scepticism**: [[william-james|James]]'s position that scepticism is dissolved rather than refuted — by lowering the epistemic bar from absolute certainty to practical adequacy, the sceptic's challenge loses its grip.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics of the pragmatic response to scepticism argue that [[william-james|James]] simply changes the subject: the sceptic asks whether our beliefs are *true* in a correspondence sense, and [[william-james|James]] answers by redefining truth in terms of practical consequences. This may satisfy the demands of action but does not address the underlying epistemological challenge. Defenders of [[william-james|James]] reply that the sceptic's demand for absolute certainty is itself an un[[rationality|reason]]able standard that no [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] should have to meet.

The relationship between scepticism and [[science]] is also debated. [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism aligns with the scientific attitude of treating all theories as provisional and subject to revision — a form of "organized scepticism" that is productive rather than paralyzing.
