## Definition

In the Pragmatic tradition, psychology refers both to the empirical study of mental life and to a methodological approach in philosophy that explains ideas, beliefs, and knowledge by tracing their origins in concrete experience. [[william-james|James]] distinguishes between two phases: **[[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology**, which reigned in philosophy until 1890 and grounded all knowledge in sensory impressions (002-003); and the **new psychology** introduced by [[william-james|James]]'s own _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_ (1890), which transformed the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] by treating thought as an active, functional process rather than a passive reception of impressions (003-002, 003-030).

## Origin & Context

Before 1890, [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s psychology "reigned absolutely in philosophy" (002-003). [[david-hume|Hume]] had argued that all ideas derive from sensory impressions, reducing complex concepts to their simplest experiential origins. This approach was "uncontested _as psychology_" — it accurately described how the mind processes experience — but its philosophical implications created a deep difficulty: if all knowledge comes from atomistic impressions, how can we explain the coherence and unity of human understanding? (002-007)

> "Until the year 1890, when James's _Principles_ were published... the psychology of Hume reigned absolutely in philosophy. All empiricists followed him." (002-003)

The publication of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_ in 1890 marked a decisive break. The "new psychology of [[william-james|James]] was bound to produce a new [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]]" (003-030), because [[william-james|James]] reconceived the mind not as a collection of discrete impressions but as a continuous "stream of consciousness" actively selecting and organizing experience (002-189). This psychological reorientation directly enabled the Pragmatic theory of truth.

## Key Arguments

**1. Psychology as the foundation of epistemology.** [[william-james|James]] argues that the new psychology leads necessarily to a new [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] (003-030). The way we understand the mind's operations directly shapes what we count as knowledge and how we justify beliefs.

**2. The [[david-hume|Hume]]an inheritance and its limits.** [[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology was empirically accurate but philosophically inadequate. [[william-james|James]] notes that "[[david-hume|Hume]] had been convicted of error" by later developments (002-194) — [[david-hume|Hume]] wrongly assumed that because ideas originate in discrete impressions, knowledge itself must be built from discrete, independent units. The new psychology recognized that mental life is fluid and continuous.

**3. Psychology vs. logic.** [[william-james|James]] engages a persistent debate about the boundary between psychology (describing how people actually think) and logic (prescribing how they ought to think). The distinction between "logic" and "psychology" is one "which may be abandoned to psychology" (006-078) — suggesting that [[william-james|James]] sees the sharp separation as artificial, since logical norms themselves emerge from psychological processes (006-025, 006-074).

> "The new psychology of James was bound to produce a new theory of knowledge." (003-030)

**4. The inevitability of psychological explanation.** [[william-james|James]] insists that when we trace how ideas are actually formed and held, "this is plainly an affair of psychology" (006-066). The charge that [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] commits "inconsistent relapses into psychology" (006-042) misunderstands that all philosophical accounts of knowledge implicitly rely on some psychological model — the only question is whether that model is explicit and empirically grounded or hidden and dogmatic.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Humean psychology**: [[knowledge|Knowledge]] built from atomistic sensory impressions; all ideas traceable to experience. Empirically rigorous but philosophically limited (002-003, 002-007).
- **[[william-james|James]]ian "new psychology"**: The mind as an active, selective, continuous stream; thought serves practical ends, not merely passive recording (003-002, 003-030).
- **Psychology as method**: Using psychological analysis as a philosophical tool to dissolve seemingly intractable metaphysical disputes (006-066, 006-078).

## Criticisms & Debates

A persistent criticism, which [[william-james|James]] directly addresses, is that [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] conflates psychology with philosophy — that explaining _how_ we come to believe something is not the same as determining _whether_ it is true. The dilemma "either verbalism or psychology" (006-049) captures this tension: traditional philosophers accuse Pragmatists of reducing logical questions to psychological ones.

[[william-james|James]]'s response is twofold. First, he argues that the separation between logic and psychology is itself a philosophical decision that cannot be justified without circularity (006-078). Second, he maintains that the new psychology does not replace logical analysis but enriches it by revealing the actual processes by which human beings form, test, and revise beliefs (006-066).
