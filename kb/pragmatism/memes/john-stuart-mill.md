## Overview

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was the most influential British philosopher of the nineteenth century, whose work in logic, ethics, political philosophy, and epistemology shaped the intellectual world into which [[william-james|William James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatism]] was born. [[william-james|James]] dedicated _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ "to the memory of John Stuart Mill, from whom I first learned the pragmatic openness of mind," signaling Mill's importance as a precursor to the pragmatic approach.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1806** — Born in London; received an extraordinary education designed by his father, James Mill.
- **1823** — Began work at the East India Company.
- **1843** — Published _A System of Logic_, his major contribution to philosophy of science.
- **1859** — Published _On Liberty_, the classic defense of individual freedom.
- **1863** — Published _Utilitarianism_.
- **1873** — Died in Avignon, France.

## Key Contributions

Mill's empiricism was more sophisticated than his predecessors': he recognized the active role of mind in knowledge, the importance of hypothesis and experimentation in science, and the limitations of purely deductive reasoning. These commitments anticipate key pragmatist themes.

Mill's _On Liberty_ provided the liberal framework that pragmatists like [[john-dewey|John Dewey]] would later transform into a more communitarian, democratic vision.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

Mill influenced [[william-james|James]] in several ways: his empiricist commitment to grounding knowledge in experience; his recognition that even logic and mathematics have an empirical basis; his defense of individual liberty of thought; and above all, his "openness of mind" — the willingness to consider unconventional hypotheses and to judge ideas by their practical consequences.
