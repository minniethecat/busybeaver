## CHAPTER IV

[004-001] THE DILEMMAS OF DOGMATISM

[004-002] Every man, probably, is by instinct a [[dogmatism|dogmatist]]. He feels perfectly sure

[004-003] that he knows some things, and is right about them against the world.

[004-004] Whatever he believes in he does not doubt, but holds to be

[004-005] self-evidently or indisputably true. His naive dogmatism, moreover,

[004-006] spontaneously assumes that his truth is universal and shared by all

[004-007] others.

[004-008] If now he could live like a fakir, wholly wrapped in a cloud of his own

[004-009] imaginings, and nothing ever happened to disappoint his expectations, to

[004-010] jar upon his prejudices, and to convict him of error; if he never held

[004-011] converse with anyone who took a different view and controverted him, his

[004-012] dogmatism would be lifelong and incurable. But as he lives socially, he

[004-013] has in practice to outgrow it, and this lands him in a serious

[004-014] theoretical dilemma. He has to learn to live with others who differ from

[004-015] him in their dogmatizing. Social life plainly would become impossible

[004-016] if all rigidly insisted on the absolute rightness of their own beliefs

[004-017] and the absolute wrongness of all others.

[004-018] So compromises have to be made to get at a common 'truth.' It must be

[004-019] recognized that not everything which is believed to be 'knowledge' is

[004-020] knowledge. In fact, it is safer to assume that none have knowledge,

[004-021] though all think they have; to say fact, men only have 'opinions,' which

[004-022] may be nearer to or farther from 'the truth,' but are not of necessity

[004-023] as unquestionable as they seem to be. Out of this concession to the

[004-024] social life arise three problems. How are 'opinions' to be compared with

[004-025] each other, and how is the extent of their 'truth' or 'error' to be

[004-026] determined? How is the belief in [[absolutism|absolute truth]] to be interpreted and

[004-027] discounted? How is the penitent dogmatist, once he has allowed doubt to

[004-028] corrupt his self-confidence, to be stopped from doubting all things and

[004-029] turning sceptic?

[004-030] As regards the first problem, the first question is whether we shall try

[004-031] to _test_ opinions and to arrive at a standard of value by which to

[004-032] measure them by comparing the opinions themselves with one another, or

[004-033] shall presume that there must be some absolute standard which alone is

[004-034] truly true, whether we are aware of it or not. The former view is

[004-035] _relativism_, the latter is _absolutism_, in the matter of truth.

[004-036] Now, there can be no doubt that absolutism is more congenial to our

[004-037] natural prejudices. Accordingly it is the method tried first; but it

[004-038] soon conducts dogmatism to an awkward series of dilemmas.

[004-039] 1. If there is absolute truth, who has it? and who can use the absolute

[004-040] criterion of opinions it is supposed to form? Not, surely, everyone who

[004-041] _thinks_ he has. It will never do to let every dogmatist vote for

[004-042] himself and condemn all others. That way war and madness lie. Until

[004-043] there is absolute agreement, there cannot be absolute truth.

[004-044] 2. But absolute truth may still be reverenced as an ideal, to save us

[004-045] from the scepticism to which a complete [[relativism|relativity of truth]] would lead.

[004-046] But would it save us? If it is admitted that no one can arrogate to

[004-047] himself its possession, what use is it to believe that it is an ideal?

[004-048] For if no one can assume that he has it, all _human_ truth is, in fact,

[004-049] such as the relativist asserted, and scepticism is just as inevitable as

[004-050] before. It makes no difference to the sceptical inference whether there

[004-051] is no absolute truth, or whether it is unattained by man, and human

[004-052] unattainable.

[004-053] 3. It was a mistake, therefore, to admit that opinions cannot be

[004-054] compared together. Some are much more certain than others, and, indeed,

[004-055] 'self-evident' and 'intuitive.' Let us therefore take these to be

[004-056] 'truer.' If so, the thinker who feels most certain he is right is most

[004-057] likely to be right.

[004-058] 4. This suggestion will be welcomed by all dogmatists--until they

[004-059] discover that it does not help them to agree together, because they are

[004-060] all as certain as can be. But a critically-minded man will urge against

[004-061] it that _'certainty' is a subjective and psychological criterion_, and

[004-062] that no one has been able to devise a method for distinguishing the

[004-063] alleged logical from the undeniable psychological certainty. He will

[004-064] hesitate to say, therefore, that because a belief seems certain it is

[004-065] true, and to trust the formal claim to infallibility which is made in

[004-066] every judgment. And when '[[intuition|intuitions]]' are appealed to, he will ask how

[004-067] 'true' intuitions are to be discriminated from 'false,' sound from

[004-068] insane, and inquire to what he is committing himself in admitting the

[004-069] truth of intuitions. He will demand, therefore, the publication of a

[004-070] list of the intuitions which are absolutely true. But he will not get

[004-071] it, and if he did, it may be predicted that he would not find a single

[004-072] one which has not been disputed by some eminent philosopher.

[004-073] 5. Intuitions, therefore, are an embarrassment, rather than a help to

[004-074] [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]]. It has to maintain both that intuitions are the

[004-075] foundations of all truth and certitude, and also that not all are true.

[004-076] But our natural curiosity as to how these sorts are to be known apart is

[004-077] left unsatisfied. We must not ask which are true, and which not. No one

[004-078] can say in advance about what matters intuitive certainty is possible;

[004-079] what is, or is not, an intuition is revealed only to reflection after

[004-080] the event. Only if an intuition _has_ played us false, we may be sure it

[004-081] _was_ not infallible; it must either have been one of the fallible sort,

[004-082] or else no intuition at all.

[004-083] 6. At this point [[scepticism|universal scepticism]] begins to raise its hydra head,

[004-084] and to grin at the dogmatist's discomfiture. For in point of fact the

[004-085] history of thought reveals, not a steady accumulation of indubitable

[004-086] truth, but a continuous strife of opinions, in which the most widely

[004-087] accepted beliefs daily succumb to fresh criticism and fall into

[004-088] disrepute as the 'errors of the past.' Nothing, it seems, can guarantee

[004-089] a 'truth,' however firmly it may be believed for a time, from the

[004-090] corrosive force of new speculation and changed opinion; to survey the

[004-091] field of philosophic dispute, strewn with the remains of 'infallible'

[004-092] systems and 'absolute' certainties, is to be led irresistibly to a

[004-093] sceptical doubt as to the competence of human thought. If 'absolute

[004-094] truth' is our ideal and acquaintance with 'absolute reality' our aim,

[004-095] then, in view of the persistent illusions on both these points to which

[004-096] the human mind is liable, it seems necessary to recognize the

[004-097] hopelessness of our search. Thus the last dilemma of dogmatism is

[004-098] reached. In view of the diversity of human beliefs and the discredit

[004-099] which has historically fallen on the most axiomatic articles of [[religious-experience|faith]],

[004-100] we must either admit scepticism to be the issue of the debate, or else,

[004-101] condemning our absolute view of truth, find some means of utilizing the

[004-102] relative truths which are all that humanity seems able to grasp. But to

[004-103] come to terms with relativism is to renounce the dogmatic attitude

[004-104] entirely, and to approach the problems of philosophy in a totally

[004-105] different spirit.
