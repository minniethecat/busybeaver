## Overview

William James (1842–1910) was an American philosopher and psychologist, widely regarded as the father of American psychology and a founding figure of [[pragmatism]]. His 1907 [[gifford-lectures|Gifford Lectures]], published as _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking_, constitute the most accessible and influential statement of the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophy — and serve as the primary corpus for this wiki.

James's intellectual project was defined by a sustained attempt to reconcile scientific empiricism with the spiritual and religious dimensions of human experience. Rejecting both the dogmatic [[rationality|rational]]ism of [[the-absolute|Absolute Idealism]] and the reductive materialism of nineteenth-century [[science|science]], he developed a philosophy grounded in the "cash-value" of ideas — their practical consequences for human life. His work bridged [[psychology]], [[philosophy]], and [[religion]], and his literary style — vivid, direct, and free of academic jargon — made him one of the most widely read philosophers of his era.

## Life

- **1842** — Born in New York City to Henry James Sr., a Swedenborgian theologian, and Mary Robertson Walsh. Brother of novelist Henry James.
- **1861–1864** — Studied at Harvard (chemistry, then medicine), interrupted by periods of ill health and depression.
- **1865–1866** — Accompanied Louis Agassiz on the Thayer Expedition to Brazil, which shifted his interests toward biology and psychology.
- **1869** — Earned M.D. from Harvard Medical School; experienced a severe existential crisis that would inform his later philosophy.
- **1872** — Appointed instructor in physiology at Harvard; began teaching psychology in 1875 — one of the first psychology courses in America.
- **1878** — Married Alice Howe Gibbens; the same year, committed to the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] principle after reading Charles Renouvier on free will.
- **1880s** — Published landmark papers on psychology; became full professor at Harvard. Helped found the [[metaphysical-club]] with C.S. Peirce, Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., and others.
- **1890** — Published _[[principles-of-psychology|The Principles of Psychology]]_, a monumental two-volume work that established psychology as an experimental science.

> The publication in 1890 of James's _Principles of Psychology_ marked an epoch in the history of the subject, not only in America but in the world. (001-150--001-156)

- **1897** — Published _[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]] and Other Essays in Popular [[philosophy|Philosophy]]_.
- **1902** — Published _[[the-varieties-of-religious-experience|The Varieties of Religious Experience]]_, based on his [[gifford-lectures|Gifford Lectures]] at Edinburgh, exploring [[religion|religious experience]] from a psychological perspective.
- **1906–1907** — Delivered the [[gifford-lectures|Gifford Lectures]] on [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] at the University of Edinburgh (published as _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ in 1907).
- **1907** — Retired from Harvard; continued to write and lecture.
- **1909** — Published _The Meaning of [[truth|Truth]]_ and _A Pluralistic Universe_.
- **1910** — Died at his summer home in Chocorua, New Hampshire. His final, incomplete work _Some Problems of [[philosophy|Philosophy]]_ was published posthumously in 1911.

## Key Ideas

### The Pragmatic Method

James's core philosophical contribution was the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] — the principle that the meaning of any concept is found in its practical consequences. Rather than asking what a concept "[[reality|real]]ly is" in some metaphysical sense, pragmatism asks: "What difference would it practically make to anyone if this notion rather than that notion were true?" (002-136--002-139).

This method was first articulated by C.S. Peirce in the 1870s, but James transformed it into a broader philosophical approach — one that could be applied to questions in metaphysics, epistemology, and [[religious-experience|religion]].

### The Will to Believe

In his controversial essay "[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]" (1896), James argued that under certain conditions — when a choice is "live," "forced," and "momentous," and when the evidence is insufficient to decide the matter on purely intellectual grounds — it is [[rationality|rational]]ly permissible to believe on the basis of one's "passional nature." This doctrine became one of the most discussed and criticized aspects of James's philosophy.

### Radical Empiricism

Late in his career, James developed the doctrine of "[[empiricism|radical empiricism]]," which held that the relations between things — conjunctive as well as disjunctive — are given in experience just as directly as the things themselves. This challenged both the atomistic empiricism of [[david-hume|Hume]] and Mill and the [[rationality|rational]]ist monism of the Absolute Idealists.

> "Consciousness," James insists, "does not appear to itself chopped up in bits. It is nothing jointed; it flows. A 'river' or a 'stream' are the metaphors by which it is most naturally described." (002-149)

### Pragmatist Theory of Truth

James's theory of truth — that "true ideas are those that we can assimilate, validate, corroborate, and verify" — was the most controversial element of his philosophy. Critics charged him with reducing truth to utility or subjective satisfaction, but James insisted that truth, like all human [[concept-index|concepts]], must "work" in the world of experience to earn its status.

## Relation to Pragmatism

William James was the primary popularizer and one of the chief architects of pragmatism. While C.S. Peirce originated the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic maxim]], it was James who gave pragmatism its name, its public voice, and its broadest philosophical application. James extended pragmatism beyond Peirce's focus on scientific meaning into questions of [[religious-experience|religion]], ethics, and metaphysics.

James's relationship to pragmatism is both foundational and personal: "William James had merely to invert the problem" (002-136), transforming an abstract method of clarifying ideas into a living philosophy engaged with the full range of human concerns. His formulation of pragmatism as a method for settling metaphysical disputes — by tracing the practical consequences of competing claims — remains the most widely cited definition of the tradition.
