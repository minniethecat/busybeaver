## Overview

Thomas Reid (1710–1796) was a Scottish philosopher who founded the Scottish School of Common Sense. He was the principal critic of [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s skepticism, arguing that [[david-hume|Hume]]'s conclusions followed necessarily from the "way of ideas" — the empiricist assumption that we directly perceive only our own ideas rather than external objects. Reid's alternative, direct realism, held that perception puts us in immediate contact with the external world.

## Connection to Pragmatism

Reid's philosophy anticipates several pragmatist themes. His insistence that philosophical theories must answer to the deliverances of [[common-sense|common sense]] — that philosophizing cannot simply overturn beliefs that are practically necessary for living — prefigures the pragmatist view that theory must be tested by practice. [[charles-sanders-peirce|Charles Sanders Peirce]] explicitly acknowledged Reid's influence, and the pragmatist appeal to the communal, practical validation of belief echoes Reid's confidence in the shared convictions of ordinary life.

The [[common-sense-philosophy|Common Sense]] tradition Reid founded also influenced [[william-james|James]]'s own frequent appeals to the practical necessities of belief — for example, in "[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]," where [[william-james|James]] argues that certain beliefs cannot be suspended because they are practically required for action.

## Key Works

- _An Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense_ (1764) — his major critique of Hume and the "way of ideas"
- _Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man_ (1785)
- _Essays on the Active Powers of Man_ (1788)
