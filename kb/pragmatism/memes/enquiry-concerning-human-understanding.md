## Overview

_An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding_ (1748) is [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s most accessible and influential philosophical work. A recasting of material from Book I of the earlier _[[treatise-of-human-nature|Treatise of Human Nature]]_, the _Enquiry_ presents Hume's empiricist epistemology in a more polished and concise form. It contains his famous arguments concerning causation, miracles, and the limits of human reason.

## Central Arguments

The _Enquiry_'s most celebrated passages include:

- The analysis of causation: Hume argues that our idea of necessary connection between cause and effect is not derived from reason but from custom — the habitual association of constantly conjoined events
- The critique of miracles: Hume argues that it is never rational to believe in a miracle on the basis of testimony, since the probability of a violation of natural law must always be lower than the probability of mistaken or deceptive testimony
- The "fork" between relations of ideas and matters of fact: all meaningful propositions are either analytic (true by definition) or synthetic (verifiable by experience)

## Relevance to Pragmatism

The _Enquiry_'s argument that causal necessity is a matter of custom rather than rational insight is a direct ancestor of the pragmatist view that our most fundamental beliefs are habits of action rather than deliverances of pure reason. [[william-james|William James]], in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, builds on Hume's insight: if our belief in causation is a practical habit rather than a rational intuition, then all our beliefs should be evaluated by their practical consequences rather than their supposed rational foundations.

Hume's conclusion that "reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions" also resonates with the pragmatist view that [[truth|truth]] and [[knowledge|knowledge]] serve life — that the intellect is an instrument of practical existence rather than a mirror of nature.
