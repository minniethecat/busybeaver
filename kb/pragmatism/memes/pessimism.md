## Definition

Pessimism, in the philosophical sense, is the doctrine that [[reality|real]]ity is fundamentally indifferent or hostile to human welfare — that suffering predominates over happiness and that existence is, on balance, a burden rather than a gift. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, pessimism is the characteristic metaphysical stance of the tough-minded, empiricist temperament: the conviction that the universe is indifferent, that there is no cosmic guarantee of happy endings, and that moral effort takes place against a background of ultimate meaninglessness. [[william-james|James]] treats pessimism as a genuine and honest response to the facts of experience but argues that it, like [[optimism|optimism]], is a one-sided abstraction.

## Origin & Context

Philosophical pessimism was most powerfully articulated by Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860), who argued that the world is the expression of a blind, insatiable Will that produces only suffering. This position was developed by later thinkers like Eduard von Hartmann and influenced the existentialist tradition.

In [[william-james|James]]'s context, pessimism was the temperamental shadow of empiricism: the honest empiricist, looking at the evidence without tender-minded wishful thinking, cannot help but notice that the universe does not care about human welfare. [[william-james|James]] himself was deeply acquainted with this pessimistic vision — his early depression was rooted in the conviction that determinism and materialism made human life meaningless.

## Key Arguments

### Pessimism in the Temperament Typology

[[william-james|James]] places pessimism at the tough-minded pole:

| Temperament | View of the World | Practical Attitude |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Tender-minded ([[optimism|optimist]]) | Everything works out for the best | Passive trust |
| Tough-minded (pessimist) | Nothing ultimately matters | Resignation or heroic struggle |
| Pragmatist ([[meliorism|meliorist]]) | Things can be made better through effort | Active engagement |

### The Pragmatic Test of Pessimism

[[william-james|James]] applies the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]] to pessimism: what practical difference does it make? If pessimism leads to resignation — "nothing matters, so why try?" — it is pragmatically self-defeating. If pessimism leads to heroic engagement — "things are bad, therefore I must fight to make them better" — it transforms into [[meliorism|meliorism]]. The pragmatic value of pessimism depends entirely on what it motivates.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Schopenhauerian pessimism**: The metaphysical claim that the world is the expression of a blind, suffering-producing Will.
- **Empirical pessimism**: The inductive conclusion that suffering predominates — a factual claim, not a metaphysical one.
- **Heroic pessimism**: The stance that life is meaningless but must be lived with courage and integrity. An existentialist position.
- **Pragmatic meliorism**: James's alternative — the working hypothesis that effort can improve things, regardless of the ultimate cosmic balance.
