## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]ian pragmatism, truth is not an inert property of propositions but a dynamic process — "truth is a _value_ and a satisfaction" that "happens to an idea" when its practical consequences prove fruitful for human life and [[experience|experience]] (005-182). Truth is the validation of a truth-claim through its working in concrete situations, not a static correspondence to a transcendent [[reality|real]]ity.

## Origin & Context

The problem of truth is the central epistemological concern of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907). Lecture V, "[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[common-sense|Common Sense]]," opens with "THE PROBLEM OF TRUTH AND ERROR" (005-001), framing the issue as urgent because the traditional foundations of truth — [[absolutism|absolute truth]], [[intuition|self-evidence]], and intuition — have all collapsed under skeptical scrutiny. [[william-james|James]] develops his pragmatic theory of truth in explicit opposition to the intellectualist tradition, which (he argues) conflates the formal "truth-claim" every judgment makes with actual validated truth (005-055). This distinction is the cornerstone of his argument: every judgment initially claims truth, but only those whose consequences prove satisfactory in practice become validated truths (005-063).

> For absolute truth has become a chimera, self-evidence an illusion, and intuition untrustworthy. All three are psychologically very real to those who believe in them, but logically they succumb to the assaults of a scepticism which infers from the fact that no 'truths' are absolute that all may reasonably be overthrown. (005-004)

## Key Arguments

1. **Truth-Claim vs. Validated Truth**: [[william-james|James]] insists on a vital distinction [[intellectualism|intellectualism]] overlooks. Every judgment asserts a truth-claim — it is "merely _formal_" and does not mean the claim is absolutely true (005-051). Validated truths are those "which have worked well and maintained themselves," while errors are those "superseded by better ones" (005-063). This processual view makes truth subject to continuous testing and revision.

> Initially, then, every judgment is a _truth-claim_, and this claim is merely _formal_. It does not _mean_ that the claim is absolutely true, and that it is impious to question it. On the contrary, it has still to be validated by others, and may work in such a way that its own maker withdraws it, and corrects it by a better. (005-050)

2. **The [[correspondence-theory|Correspondence Theory]] Critique**: [[william-james|James]] engages directly with the oldest definition — "truth is the correspondence of a thought to [[reality|real]]ity" (005-077) — and finds it formally empty. For the correspondence to be verified, [[reality|real]]ity would need to be known independently of the truth-claim, which is impossible. The theory "does not _test_ the truth-claim of the assertion; it only gives a fresh definition of it" (005-096).

> A 'true' thought, it says, is one which _claims to correspond_ with a 'reality.' _But so does a false,_ and hence the theory leaves us as we were, puzzled to distinguish them. (005-097)

3. **The Coherence Theory Critique**: The coherence theory — truth as consistency within a system of ideas — fares no better. [[william-james|James]] grants that coherence is "a convenience and a part of their 'working,' but it is hardly a test of their objective truth" (005-121). A completely coherent system of delusions could satisfy the formal requirement. Appealing to coherence within an [[the-absolute|Absolute Mind]] merely reintroduces the correspondence problem in a new guise (005-132).

4. **Truth as Practical [[verification|Verification]]**: The pragmatic alternative is that truth-claims are tested "by the consequences which follow from adopting it and using it as an assumption upon which to work" (005-167). If these consequences "promote the purpose in hand, instead of thwarting it," the claim is validated (005-169). This is the universal method — it applies to "mathematical laws, physical [[hypothesis|hypotheses]], [[religion|religious belief]]s, and ethical [[postulation|postulates]]" alike (005-173).

> What is signified by such statements is that no 'truth' must be accepted merely on account of the insistence of its claim, but that every idea must be tested by the consequences of its working. Its truth will then depend upon those consequences being fruitful for life in general, and in particular for the purpose behind the particular inquiry in which it arose. (005-177)

5. **Truth as Human and Practical**: The pragmatic notion that "Truth is practical" closes the artificial gulf between theory and practice, assigning truth a biological function and vital value (007-050). The humanist complement — that "Truth is human" — rescues philosophical inquiry from the despondency of unattainable absolute standards (007-052). Truth is always truth _for_ someone, relative to a context of inquiry and action.

6. **Truth Is Not Arbitrary**: [[william-james|James]] emphatically denies that pragmatic truth is subjective. "It does not mean that we are free to assert the truth of every idea which seems to us pretty or pleasant" (005-189). The testing process is constrained by objective facts: ideas "have to work in a world of fact, and to adapt themselves to all facts" (005-205). A pragmatic truth is "far less arbitrary and subjective than the 'truths' accepted as absolute, on the bare ground that they seem 'self-evident' to a few intellectualists" (005-216).

7. **Truth and Absolute Certainty**: Pragmatic truth yields practical, not absolute, certainty. [[william-james|James]] concedes that "the most prolonged [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]]s do not exclude the possibility of an ultimate error" (005-220), but insists that demanding absolute certainty merely "plays into the hands of [[scepticism|scepticism]]" (005-224). The [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]] is "practically adequate, and is the only one available" (005-231).

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Peirce's Pragmatic Maxim**: Charles Sanders Peirce originally formulated the pragmatic approach to truth as a method for clarifying ideas — the meaning of a conception lies in its conceivable practical effects. [[william-james|James]] broadened this into a full theory of truth.

- **[[william-james|James]]'s Individualistic Truth**: [[william-james|James]] emphasizes that truth is always someone's truth — "this evidently makes all truths in some sense dependent upon the personality of those who assert and accept them" (005-237). This contrasts with intellectualist [[logic|logic]]'s claim that truths are independent of particular minds.

- **[[john-dewey|Dewey]]'s Instrumentalism**: [[john-dewey|John Dewey]] developed truth as "warranted assertibility" — a judgment becomes warranted through the process of inquiry, not through correspondence to a fixed antecedent [[reality|real]]ity.

- **Schiller's [[humanism|Humanism]]**: [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]] radicalized the human element, arguing that all truths are human artifacts shaped by human interests and purposes (007-052).

## Criticisms & Debates

Bertrand Russell and other early analytic philosophers charged that pragmatism conflated the _definition_ of truth with the _criterion_ for recognizing it — reducing truth to what is useful rather than what is accurate. [[william-james|James]] responded that pragmatism does not deny objective [[reality|real]]ity but merely insists that our only access to truth is through its practical bearings in experience (007-070).

G.E. Moore accused [[william-james|James]] of confusing the act of verification with the property of being true. The objection is that a proposition might be true whether or not anyone has verified it. Pragmatists reply that while this may be logically possible, it makes no practical difference and is thus a meaningless metaphysical abstraction.

Critics also argued that the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic test]] is circular: we verify beliefs by their consequences, but we evaluate which consequences are "satisfactory" using beliefs we already hold.
