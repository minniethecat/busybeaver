## Overview

The Darwinian Revolution refers to the profound transformation of biology, philosophy, and the broader intellectual culture following the publication of [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s _On the Origin of Species_ (1859). [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s theory of [[darwinism|evolution]] by [[darwinism|natural selection]] replaced the static, designed order of traditional natural [[religion|theology]] with a dynamic, historical, and radically contingent view of life -- a shift whose philosophical implications pragmatism was among the first to fully absorb.

For pragmatism, [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] was more than a biologist: he provided a new model for understanding [[truth]], [[knowledge]], and mind itself. If species evolve through variation and selection in response to environmental pressures, then human cognition -- a product of biological [[darwinism|evolution]] -- must be understood in the same terms. Ideas, like organisms, survive and spread not because they correspond to an eternal [[reality|real]]ity but because they _work_.

## Darwin's Influence on Pragmatism

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition is unintelligible without [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]. Key connections include:

1. **Anti-essentialism.** [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] showed that biological species are not fixed essences but historical populations with blurred boundaries. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] extended this anti-essentialism to concepts, truth, and meaning -- all are historically contingent products of human inquiry, not reflections of eternal Forms.

2. **The primacy of function.** Natural selection explains biological traits by their function -- what they do for the organism. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] explains ideas by their function -- what practical difference they make. [[pragmatic-method|The pragmatic method]] is [[darwinism|natural selection]] applied to concepts.

3. **Contingency and novelty.** [[charles-darwin|Darwin]] replaced the idea of a predetermined cosmic plan with genuinely open-ended [[darwinism|evolution]]ary history. [[william-james|James]]'s pluralism -- his insistence that the universe is "unfinished" and open to genuine novelty -- is [[darwinism|Darwinian]] metaphysics.

4. **Mind as adaptation.** [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s later work on the expression of emotion and the continuity between human and animal minds provided the foundation for [[william-james|James]]'s functional psychology. Mind is not a transcendental ego but an organ that evolved to help organisms cope with their environments -- its organization is, as James puts it, "essentially biological" and has "evolved according to sound Darwinian principles" (002-227).

[[william-james|James]] acknowledged [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s influence explicitly. In Lecture II of _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, he described the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] method as applying "the principle of [[darwinism|natural selection]]" to philosophical ideas, asking whether mental variations were "purposive responses to the stimulation of the environment" (002-228): "Theories thus become instruments, not answers to enigmas, in which we can rest."

## The Larger Revolution

Beyond biology, [[charles-darwin|Darwin]]'s work transformed:

- **[[philosophy|Philosophy]]:** undermined essentialism, teleology, and the argument from design; provided a new model of explanation (historical-narrative rather than deductive-nomological)
- **Theology:** challenged the argument from design and the special status of humanity; forced a rethinking of the relationship between science and [[religious-experience|religion]] that [[william-james|James]] addressed directly in _Varieties_ and _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_
- **Social thought:** inspired (and was misappropriated by) Spencer's social [[darwinism|Darwinism]], which [[william-james|James]] criticized as confusing [[darwinism|evolution]]ary description with ethical prescription
