## Definition

Apriorism is the epistemological position that certain fundamental principles — such as the laws of logic, the axioms of mathematics, and the categories of causation — are _a priori_ truths: necessary, universal, and known independently of sensory experience. In the context of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, apriorism is presented as the traditional [[rationality|rational]]ist alternative to empiricism, and as a doctrine that pragmatism seeks to transcend through the theory of postulation.

> But does it follow from the failure of empiricism that apriorism is true? This has always been assumed, and held to dispense rationalist philosophers from giving any direct and positive proof that these principles are _a priori_ truths. (003-044--003-047)

## Origin & Context

Apriorism has its classical roots in [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s critical philosophy, which held that the mind actively structures experience through innate categories and forms of intuition. In the intellectual landscape of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, apriorism — along with its [[empiricism|empiricist]] rival — represents one pole of the "banal dilemma" (003-031) that pragmatism aims to dissolve.

> The old _empiricist_ view, as typified by Mill, was that the mind had been impressed with all its principles, such as the truths of arithmetic, the axioms of geometry, and the law of causation, by an uncontradicted course of experience, until it generalized facts into 'laws.' ... But does it follow from the failure of empiricism that apriorism is true? (003-029--003-044)

[[william-james|James]] acknowledges that apriorism captures something genuine — the active contribution of the mind to knowledge — but argues that it goes too far in treating these contributions as fixed necessities rather than as revisable [[postulation|postulates]].

## Key Arguments

**Apriorism's logical weakness.** [[william-james|James]] argues that the failure of empiricism does not logically prove apriorism; there may be a third alternative:

> If a third explanation can be thought of, it will _not_ follow that apriorism is true. All that follows is that _something_ has to be assumed before experience proves it. What that something is, and whence it comes, remains an open question. (003-047)

**The problem of future conformity.** Even if principles are _a priori_, there is no guarantee that experience will continue to conform to them:

> Moreover, apriorism has _not_ escaped from the empirical doubt about the future. Even granted that facts now conform to the necessities of our thoughts, why should they so comport themselves for ever? (003-049--003-052)

**[[postulation|Postulation]] as a superior alternative.** The doctrine of postulation takes what is valuable in apriorism (the mind's active role) while correcting its chief defect (the claim of necessity):

> A postulate thus fully meets the demands of apriorism. It is 'universal' in claim, because it is convenient and economical to make a rule carry as far as it will go; and it is 'necessary,' because all fresh facts are on principle subjected to it, in the hope that they will support and illustrate it. Yet a postulate can never be accused of being a mere sophistication, or a bar to the progress of knowledge, because it is always willing to submit to verification. (003-064--003-068)

**The difference between [[postulation|postulate]] and _a priori_ truth.** The crucial distinction lies in the role of the will:

> A 'postulate' thus differs essentially from the '_a priori_ truth' by its dependence upon the will, by its being the product of a free choice. (003-077)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] apriorism**: The classical form — the mind imposes fixed categories (causality, substance, etc.) on the manifold of sensation (002-218).
- **Rationalist apriorism**: The stronger claim that _a priori_ principles yield substantive knowledge about [[reality|real]]ity without experiential input.
- **Psychological apriorism**: The view, attributed by [[william-james|James]] to the "apriorists" who accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology (002-005), that _a priori_ elements are simply mental additions to otherwise [[david-hume|Hume]]an experience.

## Criticisms & Debates

[[william-james|James]]'s criticism of apriorism is fundamentally pragmatic: it is an explanatory dead end. By treating principles as necessary rather than as instruments, apriorism cannot explain _why_ certain principles succeed while others fail. It also cannot account for conceptual change — if principles are _a priori_ necessities, how does knowledge evolve?

> A long and successful course of service raises a postulate to the dignity of an 'axiom' ... whereas repeated failure in application degrades it to the position of a prejudice — i.e., an _a priori_ opinion which is always belied by its consequences. (003-073)

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] reply is that what apriorism mistakes for necessity is [[reality|real]]ly just the accumulated success of long-verified [[postulation|postulates]] that have become so deeply entrenched that we can no longer imagine alternatives.
