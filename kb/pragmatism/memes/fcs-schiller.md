## Overview

F.C.S. Schiller (1864–1937) was a British philosopher and the leading representative of pragmatism in England. He developed a distinctive philosophical position he called "[[humanism|Humanism]]," which [[william-james|William James]] recognized as closely allied with pragmatism. Schiller argued that all knowledge is shaped by human purposes and volition, tracing this insight back to the pre-Socratic thinker Protagoras. His work on logic and epistemology provided philosophical depth to the pragmatic movement and [[william-james|James]] relied on Schiller's scholarship to bolster the historical case for pragmatism.

## Life

- **1864** — Born Ferdinand Canning Scott Schiller in Schleswig-Holstein (then part of Denmark).
- **1882–1886** — Studied at Balliol College, Oxford, where he was influenced by British idealism before turning toward pragmatism.
- **1893** — Began teaching at Corpus Christi College, Oxford.
- **1897** — Published an essay that [[william-james|James]] later described as the first to generalize the will to believe into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge (003-192).
- **1903** — Published _[[humanism|Humanism]]: Philosophical Essays_, articulating his mature philosophical position.
- **1912** — Published _[[logic|Formal Logic]]_, a critique of traditional formal logic from a pragmatic standpoint, referenced by [[william-james|James]] (005-250).
- **1926–1937** — Taught at the University of Southern California in the final decade of his life.
- **1937** — Died in Los Angeles.

## Key Ideas

### Humanism as a Philosophical Position

Schiller introduced the term "[[humanism|Humanism]]" into philosophical discourse to describe his broader position, which [[william-james|James]] distinguished from his own narrower focus on the [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] under the name [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]].

> "This last word was introduced into philosophic terminology by Dr. Schiller in order to describe his general philosophical position as distinct from the original question of the theory of knowledge, which had been treated by James under the name of Pragmatism." (008-030)

### The Protagorean Roots of Pragmatism

Schiller demonstrated that the pragmatic approach to truth had deep historical roots, tracing it back to [[plato|Plato]]'s _Theaetetus_ and the famous dictum of Protagoras. [[william-james|James]] credited Schiller with emphasizing the internal convulsions within philosophy that gave rise to the pragmatic movement.

> "These have been emphasized in the work of Dr. F.C.S. Schiller, who has shown that already in the days of Plato the distinction between 'truth' and 'error' was baffling philosophy, that Plato's _Theaetetus_ has failed to establish it, and that the famous dictum of Protagoras, 'Man is the measure of all things,' distinctly foreshadows the 'Pragmatic,' or, as he calls it, the 'Humanist,' solution of the difficulty." (001-117)

Schiller's scholarship thus provided the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] movement with a classical lineage, showing that the core problems of truth and knowledge had resisted solution since the founding of Western philosophy.

### Voluntarist Theory of Knowledge

Schiller generalized [[william-james|James]]'s concept of the will to believe into a comprehensive theory that all knowledge involves volition. His book _Axioms as Postulates_ showed how even the most fundamental logical axioms depend on human choice and purpose.

> "But that the will to believe was capable of being generalized into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge was soon shown in Dr. Schiller's _Axioms as Postulates_." (003-192)

### Critique of Dogmatism and A Priori Philosophy

Schiller argued that traditional [[rationality|rational]]ist and idealist systems failed to address genuine problems of life, and that the _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ tradition had not produced a consistent doctrine.

> "Elsewhere Dr. Schiller has commented on the controversies raised by Hume's criticism of dogmatism. He has shown that Kant failed to answer Hume because he accepted Hume's psychology, and that no _a priori_ philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine. The idealistic theories of the 'Absolute' reveal their futility by their want of application to the genuine problems of life." (001-123)

### Reform of Formal Logic

Schiller's work extended pragmatic principles into the domain of logic itself, arguing that formal logic must be reformed to account for the purposive nature of all thinking. His critique of traditional formal logic resonated with the pragmatic insistence that abstractions must be tested by their concrete application.

> "Not even 'I lie,' which is meaningless as it stands, _Cf._ Dr. Schiller's _Formal Logic_, p. 373." (005-250)

[[william-james|James]] invoked Schiller's treatment of logical paradoxes to illustrate how even the simplest self-referential statements require pragmatic context to be evaluated meaningfully.

## Relation to Pragmatism

Schiller was a key **ally** and independent co-developer of pragmatism. While [[william-james|James]] developed pragmatism as a method for settling metaphysical disputes by tracing their practical consequences, Schiller developed a parallel philosophical position he called [[humanism|Humanism]]. The two positions converge on the central insight that thought is always purposive and truth is made in the course of experience.

[[william-james|James]] acknowledged Schiller's priority in several important domains:
- Schiller first noted that the internal convulsions of philosophy itself had given birth to pragmatism (001-116).
- Schiller first identified the Protagorean foreshadowing of pragmatic ideas (001-120).
- Schiller introduced the term "[[humanism|Humanism]]" to designate the broader philosophical program (008-030).

For [[william-james|James]], the relationship between pragmatism and [[humanism|Humanism]] represented a natural expansion: pragmatism began as a [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] and method, while [[humanism|Humanism]] was the fuller philosophical vision of human beings as active makers of truth in a purposive world.

## Major Works

- **_Axioms as Postulates_** (1902) — Extended the will-to-believe into a voluntarist theory of all knowledge, demonstrating that logical axioms rest on human postulation rather than self-evidence. [[william-james|James]] cites this work as the key generalization of his own insight (003-192).
- **_[[humanism|Humanism]]: Philosophical Essays_** (1903) — A collection of essays articulating his mature [[humanism|Humanist philosophy]], arguing that all knowledge is relative to human purposes.
- **_[[studies-in-humanism|Studies in Humanism]]_** (1907) — Further development of Humanist epistemology, published in the same year as [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_.
- **_[[logic|Formal Logic]]_** (1912) — A critical treatment of traditional logic from a pragmatic perspective, cited by [[william-james|James]] in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (005-250).
- **_[[logic|Logic]] for Use_** (1929) — An accessible presentation of pragmatic logic.
