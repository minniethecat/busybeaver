## Overview

George Santayana (1863–1952) was a Spanish-American philosopher, poet, and cultural critic whose naturalism — materialist, ironic, and aesthetically oriented — provided both a complement and a contrast to [[william-james|William James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatism]]. As James's student and later colleague at Harvard, Santayana developed a distinctive philosophical voice that combined [[plato|Platonic]] idealism about essences with [[materialism|materialist]] naturalism about existence — a position he called "the realm of being." His critique of the "genteel tradition" in American culture resonated with pragmatist themes while his detached, contemplative sensibility contrasted sharply with James's engaged activism.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1863** — Born in Madrid, Spain; moved to Boston at age 8.
- **1886** — Graduated from Harvard College, where [[william-james|James]] was his teacher.
- **1889** — Received PhD from Harvard under [[william-james|James]]'s supervision.
- **1889–1912** — Taught at Harvard alongside [[william-james|James]] and [[josiah-royce|Josiah Royce]].
- **1912** — Left Harvard and moved permanently to Europe.
- **1952** — Died in Rome.

## Key Contributions

Santayana developed a comprehensive philosophical system in his multi-volume _The Life of Reason_ (1905–1906) and _Realms of Being_ (1927–1940). His naturalism affirmed that existence is material and governed by physical laws, but he also insisted that essences (the objects of thought and imagination) are eternal and ideal. This dualism of existence and essence — the material and the ideal — is quite different from James's pragmatism but shares its rejection of absolute idealism.

Santayana's most famous aphorism — "Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it" — reflects his conviction that wisdom consists in appreciating the ideal forms revealed in history and art while accepting the material conditions of existence.
