## Definition

Nominalism is the epistemological and metaphysical doctrine that universal concepts (such as "humanity," "redness," "justice") do not refer to independently existing abstract entities but are merely names (nomina) that we apply to groups of particular things that resemble one another. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, nominalism is the empiricist's natural philosophical tendency — the conviction that only particulars are real and that general terms are human constructions, not discoveries of pre-existing Forms. [[william-james|James]] aligns pragmatism with nominalism while also seeking to show that nominalism, properly understood, does not destroy the significance of general ideas but redirects it toward their practical function.

## Origin & Context

Nominalism emerged in medieval philosophy as a challenge to Platonic realism about universals. The debate — do universals exist independently of particulars (realism), only in particulars (moderate realism/Aristotelianism), or only as names (nominalism)? — was one of the central disputes of scholastic philosophy.

In the modern period, nominalism was revived by the British empiricists ([[john-locke|Locke]], [[david-hume|Hume]]), who argued that all knowledge derives from particular sensory experiences and that general ideas are abstractions from these particulars. [[william-james|James]] inherits this empiricist nominalism but transforms it: the pragmatist does not deny the reality of general ideas but insists that their reality consists in their practical function — their capacity to guide action and organize experience.

## Key Arguments

### Nominalism in James's Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism is nominalist in three respects:

1. **Methodological nominalism**: The [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]] treats general concepts not as eternal essences but as tools whose meaning is their practical consequences.

2. **Metaphysical nominalism**: [[william-james|James]]'s [[pluralism|pluralism]] — the view that reality is composed of many independent particulars — is the metaphysical expression of nominalism.

3. **Epistemological nominalism**: [[william-james|James]] insists that knowledge begins with particular experiences and that abstract categories are derivative, not foundational.

### Nominalism vs. Realism in the Temperament Typology

| Temperament | Philosophical Tendency | View of Universals |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Tender-minded (rationalist) | Conceptualism/Realism | Universals are real, independent of particulars |
| Tough-minded (empiricist) | Nominalism | Only particulars are real; universals are names |

Pragmatism mediates: universals are real *in their practical function* — not as Platonic Forms but as working tools for navigating experience.

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Extreme nominalism**: The view that only individual particulars exist and general terms have no meaning whatsoever. Not James's view.
- **Moderate nominalism**: The view that general terms are meaningful as classifications based on resemblance, without requiring independently existing universals. James's position.
- **Pragmatic nominalism**: The view that the reality of general ideas is their practical function — their "cash-value" in experience.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics charge that nominalism cannot account for the objectivity of scientific knowledge — if general concepts are mere names, why do they work so reliably? James's reply is that the reliability of general concepts is a pragmatic fact, not a metaphysical mystery: they work because they capture real patterns in experience, not because they refer to Platonic Forms.
