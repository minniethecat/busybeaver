## Definition

Darwinism, in the context of [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, refers not primarily to the biological theory of natural selection itself, but to the broader intellectual transformation it inspired: the shift from static, fixed, eternal categories to dynamic, evolving, adaptive processes. [[william-james|James]] identifies Darwinism as one of the principal "fountain-heads" of [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]], crediting it with fundamentally reshaping how philosophers think about truth, knowledge, and classification.

> Another fountain-head of Pragmatic philosophy has been Darwinism. Indeed, the Pragmatic is the only philosophizing which has completely assimilated Evolution. (001-099)

## Origin & Context

[[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]]'s _On the Origin of Species_ (1859) revolutionized not only biology but the entire intellectual climate of the late nineteenth century. Before [[charles-darwin|Darwin]], species were conceived as fixed, eternal kinds, each created according to a divine plan. After [[charles-darwin|Darwin]], species became fluid populations, continuously adapting to changing environments. [[william-james|James]] argues that philosophy was slow to absorb this insight.

> The insight into the real fluidity of natural species ought long ago to have toned down the artificial rigidity of logical classifications. (001-101)

The pragmatic movement, particularly through the work of [[john-dewey|John Dewey]] and the "[[chicago-school|Chicago School]]," was the first [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]] to fully integrate Darwinian thinking into its core methodology.

## Key Arguments

**[[truth|Truth]] must evolve with [[reality|real]]ity.** If the world is a perpetually changing process, then fixed, static truths cannot adequately describe it:

> To know reality man can no longer rest in a 'timeless' contemplation of a static system; he must expand his thoughts so as to cope with a perpetually changing process. Since the world changes, his 'truths' must change to fit it. He is faced with the necessity of a continuous reconstruction of beliefs. (001-101--001-108)

**Thought as an instrument of adaptation.** Darwinism transforms the function of thought from contemplation to action:

> This influence of Darwin has inspired the logical theories of Professor Dewey and the 'Chicago School' of Pragmatists. Thought in their writings is essentially the instrument of this readjustment. Its function is to effect the necessary changes in beliefs as economically and usefully as possible. (001-108)

**An evolving process.** [[knowledge|Knowledge]] itself is an evolutionary phenomenon:

> It is an evolving process which keeps pace with the evolution of reality and the changing situations of mortal life. (001-108--001-112)

**[[postulation|Postulation]] as Darwinian epistemology.** The doctrine of postulation — where principles are treated as [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] tested by survival rather than as eternal truths — directly mirrors Darwinian natural selection. Postulates that prove useful survive; those that fail are discarded (003-099--003-101).

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Biological Darwinism**: The scientific theory of evolution by natural selection, the empirical foundation.
- **Methodological Darwinism**: The application of evolutionary thinking to logic and epistemology — truth as that which survives testing ([[john-dewey|Dewey]], [[chicago-school|Chicago School]]).
- **Metaphysical Darwinism**: The broader worldview that treats all fixed categories (species, truths, principles) as contingent and historically conditioned.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics of Darwinism in philosophy have argued that reducing truth to adaptive success conflates what _works_ with what is _true_. [[william-james|James]]'s response is characteristically pragmatic: if truth is defined as that which enables us to cope with [[reality|real]]ity successfully, then there is no conflation — adaptive success _is_ the criterion of truth in a changing world.

A related challenge concerns whether Darwinism, applied to epistemology, leads to relativism. If truths evolve, does anything remain constant? [[william-james|James]] would reply that the constancy is not in the content of truths but in the _method_ — the continuous testing, revision, and reconstruction that characterizes all genuine inquiry.
