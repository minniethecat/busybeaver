## Overview

William Kingdon Clifford (1845–1879) was a British mathematician and philosopher whose essay "The Ethics of Belief" (1877) articulated the classic evidentialist principle: "It is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence." This principle was the direct target of [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[the-will-to-believe|The Will to Believe]]_ (1897). Clifford represented the scientific-rationalist conscience that [[william-james|James]] both respected and sought to limit: [[william-james|James]] agreed with Clifford that, where evidence is available, belief should be proportioned to evidence — but he insisted that there are vital cases (the religious hypothesis being the paradigm) where evidence is necessarily inconclusive and the choice is forced.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1845** — Born in Exeter, England.
- **1863** — Entered Trinity College, Cambridge; quickly established himself as a mathematical prodigy.
- **1871** — Appointed professor of applied mathematics at University College London.
- **1877** — Published "The Ethics of Belief" in the _Contemporary Review_.
- **1879** — Died of tuberculosis at age 33.

## Key Contributions

Clifford's "The Ethics of Belief" used the parable of a shipowner who suppresses his doubts about his vessel's seaworthiness and sends it to sea, where it sinks with all hands. The shipowner's sincere belief in his ship's safety does not excuse him, Clifford argued, because he had no right to believe on insufficient evidence. The principle generalizes: every belief we hold affects others, and we therefore have a moral obligation to proportion our beliefs to the evidence.

[[william-james|James]] accepted this principle for cases where evidence is available and the choice is not forced. His argument in _The Will to Believe_ was not that Clifford was wrong but that his principle did not apply to the religious hypothesis — a case where the evidence is necessarily inconclusive because the truth of the hypothesis can only be verified by those who commit to it.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

The Clifford-James debate defines the central tension in pragmatist epistemology: when is it legitimate to believe beyond the evidence? Clifford's answer — never — is the pure evidentialist position. James's answer — sometimes, when the option is living, forced, and momentous — is the pragmatist alternative. The debate remains unresolved in contemporary epistemology.
