## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, **common sense** is the inherited stock of practical beliefs and categories — about physical objects, causation, the self, and the external world — that human beings deploy to navigate experience. Common sense is not a repository of metaphysical truths but a set of **[[darwinism|evolution]]arily acquired conceptual instruments**: ways of carving up the flux of experience that have proven their practical utility over generations. As the title of Lecture V signals, pragmatism treats common sense as a functional achievement to be explained and critiqued, not as foundational knowledge to be taken at face value.

## Origin & Context

The concept of common sense enters the _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ lectures at two crucial junctures. First, [[william-james|James]] deploys it to diagnose **[[david-hume|Hume]]'s fundamental error**: [[david-hume|Hume]] uncritically adopted the deliverances of common sense as his starting point, when he should have gone behind them to the raw flux of experience itself.

> Hume's mistake was to have started from experience _as partly analysed_ by common sense, and not from the flux _as given_. His 'sensations' were the qualities already analysed out of the flux; he took these selections for the whole and neglected the other less obvious features in it--viz., the relations which floated them. (002-159--002-163)

Second, [[william-james|James]] frames the [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] project as an attempt to reconstruct — not question — the connected world that common sense presupposes:

> The mind on to which they fell was equipped with a complicated apparatus of faculties which could organize the chaotic manifold of sense and turn it into the connected world which common sense and science recognize. (002-079--002-082)

[[william-james|James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] treatment of common sense thus positions it as **both indispensable and misleading**: necessary for practical life, but dangerous when mistaken for primitive, uninterpreted datum.

## Key Arguments

**[[david-hume|Hume]]'s unexamined debt to common sense.** [[david-hume|Hume]] began his analysis of experience with the belief "that physical [[reality|real]]ity is composed of a multitude of separate existences that act on one another," a belief "accepted, therefore, from common sense" (002-012--002-013). This unexamined inheritance shaped his entire atomistic theory of mind, leading directly to the sceptical conclusions that followed.

> He accepted, therefore, from common sense the belief that physical reality is composed of a multitude of separate existences that act on one another, and tried to conceive mental life strictly on the same analogy. (002-012--002-014)

**Common sense as the target of scepticism.** The [[david-hume|Hume]]an analysis, once accepted for over a century as the definitive account of mind, had the consequence of making common sense itself appear erroneous:

> If this analysis of the mind were correct -- the common-sense belief that knowledge revealed a world outside the thinker was, of course, erroneous. For common sense could hardly treat 'things' as merely 'sensations' artificially grouped together in space. (002-036--002-038)

Thus common sense found itself attacked by the very [[empiricism|empiricist]] philosophy that had unwittingly borrowed its starting points.

**Common sense undismayed by formal logic.** [[william-james|James]] invokes common sense as a resilient counterpoint to formal logical analysis when the latter breaks down. Even when the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] shows that the syllogism cannot compel necessary assent, common sense remains undisturbed:

> But common sense is undismayed by the pragmatist's discovery that if the Syllogism is to have any sense its premisses _must_ be taken as disputable; for, unlike Formal Logic, it has perceived that men do not reason about what they think they know for certain, but about matters in dispute. (006-165--006-169)

**Common sense as practical check on dogmatism.** [[william-james|James]] deploys the notion of common sense as a corrective to philosophical dogmatism. When a thinker claims self-evidence for his judgments, [[william-james|James]] retorts that "his dogmatism has quite blotted out his common sense" (005-046). Common sense here functions as the pragmatic instinct that recognizes the difference between a formal [[truth|truth-claim]] and [[truth|validated truth]].

**Common sense admits flexibility in reckoning.** Common sense is not rigid or [[absolutism|absolutist]]; it acknowledges that different contexts call for different ways of counting and classifying:

> Common sense, therefore, admits that we may reckon variously, and that arithmetic does not _apply_ to _all_ things. (003-143--003-144)

This flexibility aligns common sense with the pragmatic temperament: it is practical and context-sensitive rather than dogmatic and [[absolutism|absolutist]].

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Common sense as [[darwinism|evolution]]ary inheritance:** The categories of common sense (thing, cause, self, space, time) are not _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ truths but conceptual tools discovered and refined through collective human experience. They have "evolved according to sound [[darwinism|Darwinian]] principles" (002-227).
- **Common sense as pre-philosophical starting point:** The unreflective stock of beliefs that both [[empiricism|empiricist]]s and [[rationality|rational]]ists uncritically adopt, which [[david-hume|Hume]] began from "as philosophers commonly do" (002-010), treating the pragmatic "analysis of experience which all find ready-made in language" as if it were primitive datum.
- **Common sense as practical wisdom:** The pragmatic instinct that recognizes when [[reason|formal reasoning]] has lost touch with the actual business of living — the attitude that remains "undismayed" (006-165) when abstract logic breaks down, and that knows dogmatism "has quite blotted out his common sense" (005-046).
- **Common sense as pragmatic temperament:** The disposition that admits flexibility in reckoning (003-143) and recognizes [[truth|truth-claim]]s for what they are — claims to be tested, not decrees to be obeyed — aligning with the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] method of resolving disputes by tracing practical consequences.

## Criticisms & Debates

[[william-james|James]]'s treatment of common sense cuts against both traditional philosophical poles. Against the [[empiricism|empiricist]]s, he charges that their allegedly radical starting points are simply common-sense selections that have been reified into primitive data. Against the [[rationality|rational]]ists, he insists that the categories they treat as _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ necessities are better understood as [[darwinism|evolution]]ary instruments whose authority derives from practical success, not transcendental deduction.

The implicit criticism of common sense in [[william-james|James]]'s account is subtle: common sense is not _false_ — it is _derivative_. The philosopher who mistakes it for primary experience commits the genetic fallacy, building systems on a foundation that was never inspected. Yet [[william-james|James]] does not advocate abandoning common sense, for doing so would make practical life impossible. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]]'s task is to **recognize common sense for what it is**: a set of useful instruments whose authority is practical, not metaphysical.

A deeper challenge arises from the tension between [[william-james|James]]'s [[darwinism|Darwinian]] account of common-sense categories as evolved instruments and his insistence that they are "selections" from the flux. If they are selections, selected by what? If by human purposes, then purpose must be prior to the act of selection — suggesting that common sense rests on something more fundamental than itself.
