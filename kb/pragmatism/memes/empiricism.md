## Definition

Empiricism is the epistemological doctrine that all knowledge originates in sensory experience and that the mind is, at birth, a blank slate upon which experience writes. In the context of [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism, empiricism designates the tradition running from Locke through [[david-hume|Hume]] to Mill — the view that the mind passively receives impressions from an external world, and that all principles of thought (mathematical truths, causal laws, geometrical axioms) are merely generalized regularities derived from an "uncontradicted course of experience" (003-035). [[william-james|James]] treats empiricism not as an outright falsehood but as an **incomplete account** of knowledge — one that, together with its mirror-image apriorism, constitutes the "banal dilemma" (003-031) that pragmatism aims to transcend.

## Origin & Context

Empiricism enters [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ lectures as the established philosophical orthodoxy of the late nineteenth century — an orthodoxy rooted in [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic psychology, which had reigned unchallenged for over a century. [[william-james|James]]'s treatment of empiricism is both historical and diagnostic: he traces its failures to an uncritical starting point in [[david-hume|Hume]]'s analysis of mind.

> Until the year 1890, when James's _Principles_ were published, the psychology of Hume reigned absolutely in philosophy. All empiricists accepted it enthusiastically, as the sum of philosophic wisdom; all apriorists submitted to it, even in supplementing and modifying it by 'transcendental' and metaphysical additions; in either case it remained uncontested _as psychology_, and, by propounding an utterly erroneous analysis of the mind and its experience, entangled philosophy in inextricable difficulties. (002-002--002-009)

The foundation of [[david-hume|Hume]]'s empiricism was a mental atomism that treated experience as composed of discrete, self-contained units. [[william-james|James]] exposes this as a product of common sense, not a description of raw experience:

> Hume had, as philosophers commonly do, set out from the practically sufficient analysis of experience which all find ready-made in language. He accepted, therefore, from common sense the belief that physical reality is composed of a multitude of separate existences that act on one another, and tried to conceive mental life strictly on the same analogy. His theory of experience, therefore, closely parallels the atomistic theory of matter. (002-010--002-015)

## Key Arguments

**Empiricism cannot account for [[rationality|rational order]].** [[william-james|James]] argues that empiricism fails to explain the organized, law-governed character of [[science|scientific knowledge]]. The mind's principles — arithmetic, geometry, causation — cannot be mere inductive generalizations from experience:

> The old _empiricist_ view, as typified by Mill, was that the mind had been impressed with all its principles, such as the truths of arithmetic, the axioms of geometry, and the law of causation, by an uncontradicted course of experience, until it generalized facts into 'laws,' and was enabled to predict a similar future with certainty. But this theory had really been exploded in advance by Hume. Facts do not _appear_ as causally connected, nor, if they did, would this guarantee that they will continue to do so in the future. (003-032--003-039)

**Empiricism gives no [[reality|real]] account of scientific order.** The continuum of experience is not intrinsically structured as a mathematical system — we impose that structure upon it. Empiricism, by treating experience as the source rather than the raw material of knowledge, cannot explain why mathematics works:

> The continuum of experience, we may add, is not _given_ as a series of arithmetical units or geometrical equalities, unless we deliberately measure it out in accordance with mathematical principles. Empiricism thus gives no real account of the scientific rational order of the world. (003-040--003-043)

**Empiricism reduces experience to a mosaic of sensations.** [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic approach produced a fundamentally disconnected picture of mental life — experience as nothing more than a succession of discrete sensations, with no [[reality|real]] continuity, no [[reality|real]] self, and no [[reality|real]] causation:

> To suppose that anything beyond these sensory units was ever really experienced was futile fiction. Experience was a mosaic, of which the stones were the detached sensations, and their washed-out copies, the ideas. (002-029--002-032)

**Empiricism cannot bridge from sensation to knowledge.** Neither the empiricists nor the [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] [[apriorism|apriorist]]s could coherently move from discrete sensory atoms to connected thought. The empiricist appeal to "the association of ideas" merely multiplied the problem:

> But was this expedient really thinkable? For if all 'sensations' or qualities are separate entities, how can the addition of more 'distinct existences' of the same sort really bind them together? (002-124--002-127)

**[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s refutation of empiricism was premature.** [[william-james|James]] highlights that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s attempt to overcome empiricism through transcendental machinery did not actually solve the problem. The [[david-hume|Hume]]an challenge remained unanswered:

> And, after all, was not Kant a bit premature in proclaiming the _finality_ of his analysis and of his refutation of empiricism for all time? The searching question, Why should the future resemble the past? had received no answer, and so might not the mind itself, with all its categories, be susceptible to change? (002-099--002-103)

**Empiricism and apriorism share a common flaw.** Both traditions share the same incorrect assumption — that the failure of one entails the truth of the other. [[william-james|James]] rejects this false dichotomy:

> But does it follow from the failure of empiricism that apriorism is true? This has always been assumed, and held to dispense rationalist philosophers from giving any direct and positive proof that these principles are _a priori_ truths. But manifestly their procedure is logically far from cogent. If a third explanation can be thought of, it will _not_ follow that apriorism is true. (003-044--003-049)

**[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] as the third way.** [[william-james|James]]'s solution is to transcend the empiricist-[[apriorism|apriorist]] dilemma entirely through the doctrine of postulation — a view that borrows from both while being identical with neither:

> Let us, therefore, try a compromise, which ignores neither that which we bring to experience (like empiricism), nor that which we gain from experience (like apriorism). This compromise is effected by the doctrine of postulation. For though a postulate proceeds from us, and is meant to guide thought in anticipating facts, it yet allows the facts to test and mould it; so that its working modifies, expands, or restricts its demands, and fits it to meet the exigencies of experience. (003-055--003-061)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **Classical empiricism (Locke, [[david-hume|Hume]]):** The view that the mind is a _tabula rasa_ — all ideas derive from sensory impressions. [[knowledge|Knowledge]] is built up through association of simple ideas into complex ones. This was the reigning psychology before [[william-james|James]]'s _Principles_ (002-002--002-009).
- **Mill's inductivist empiricism:** The nineteenth-century version that treats mathematical and logical principles as empirical generalizations from repeated experience, "enabled to predict a similar future with certainty" (003-035--003-036). [[william-james|James]] treats this as the view already "exploded in advance by [[david-hume|Hume]]" (003-037).
- **[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s qualified empiricism:** [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s analysis of sensory input as correct — experience "as it came through the channels of sense" was a "whirl of separate sensations" (002-076--002-077) — but supplemented it with _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ forms. [[william-james|James]] rejects this as a failed "palliative" (002-074) that merely postpones the problem.
- **Radical empiricism ([[william-james|James]]):** [[william-james|James]]'s own later doctrine (008-147), distinct from classical empiricism. It insists that relations between things are as [[reality|real]] and as directly experienced as the things themselves — rejecting the atomistic premise that only discrete sensations are given. "Radical empiricism" restores the connectedness that [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomism destroyed. This is not the empiricism criticized in the _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ lectures but [[william-james|James]]'s constructive alternative to it.

## Criticisms & Debates

[[william-james|James]]'s critique of empiricism is comprehensive and targets its core assumptions:

1. **The atomistic starting point is derivative, not primitive.** [[david-hume|Hume]] began his analysis "from experience _as partly analysed_" by common sense (002-159), not from the actual flux of experience as it is lived. His "sensations" were already the products of conceptual selection — the very thing empiricism claimed to avoid.

2. **Empiricism cannot account for the mind's active contribution.** [[knowledge|Knowledge]] requires principles (causation, mathematical truth, logical structure) that are brought _to_ experience rather than extracted _from_ it. [[william-james|James]]'s doctrine of postulation acknowledges this active element without falling into apriorism: we "bring to experience" (003-056) a set of [[postulation|postulates]] that experience then tests and refines.

3. **The empiricist argument from induction is circular.** The claim that the future will resemble the past cannot itself be justified by experience without begging the question. As [[william-james|James]] notes, "apriorism has _not_ escaped from the empirical doubt about the future" (003-052) — the problem of induction is symmetrical.

4. **Empiricism fails to recognize relations as experienced.** This is the point where [[william-james|James]]'s critique connects to his own "radical empiricism." Classical empiricism treats relations (causation, space, time, self-continuity) as fictions or mental additions. [[william-james|James]] insists they are as much part of experience as sensations, and that recognizing this dissolves the atomistic problem.

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] solution is not to abandon experience as the touchstone of knowledge — the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] remains empirical in a deeper sense — but to recognize that experience is richer, more connected, and more purposive than the atomistic tradition allowed. The "third alternative to the banal dilemma of 'empiricism' or 'apriorism'" (003-031) is a [[knowledge|theory of knowledge]] in which thinking is "tested by its vital value" (003-023) — an experimental, forward-looking enterprise rather than a backward-looking record of past sensations.
