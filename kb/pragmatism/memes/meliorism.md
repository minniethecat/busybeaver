## Definition

Meliorism is the philosophical doctrine that the world is neither wholly good nor wholly bad, and that human beings have both the right and the duty to improve it through deliberate action. In [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] thought, meliorism is the practical middle way between metaphysical optimism — which holds that [[reality|real]]ity is already perfect — and pessimism — which holds that [[reality|real]]ity cannot be meaningfully improved. The term derives from the Latin *melior* (better), and the position was most influentially articulated in the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition by [[william-james|William James]] in his [[gifford-lectures|Gifford Lectures]] on *[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]* (1907).

## Historical Origins

The term "meliorism" was coined in the nineteenth century, notably used by George Eliot and other Victorian writers to describe a secular faith in progress that occupied a middle ground between religious optimism and materialist pessimism. James adopts and transforms this Victorian coinage, giving it a distinctively pragmatic justification: the world is improvable not because of a metaphysical guarantee of progress, but because human action genuinely contributes to [[reality|real]]ity's unfinished character.

> The meliorist thinks that the world can be improved, and that the duty lies upon men to improve it. But he does not think that the improvement is guaranteed, or that the world-process as a whole is certainly making for better. [008-128--008-131]

## James's Meliorism

[[william-james|William James]] develops the meliorist position in Lecture VIII ("[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] and [[religion|Religion]]") of *[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]*, framing it as the ethical consequence of [[humanism|Humanism]]. Against the background of the Absolute idealism represented by thinkers such as F. H. Bradley and Josiah Royce — which [[william-james|James]] regarded as intellectually consoling but practically paralyzing — he argues that pragmatism empowers human beings to take responsibility for the world's improvement:

> The superiority of Humanism, then, lies in this, that it does not discourage human enterprise by assuming that the real is completely rigid and eternally achieved without regard to human effort. In the drama that unrolls reality, every man, it teaches, has a duty and a power to play his humble but essential part. [008-127--008-130]

> Humanism is neither an Optimism nor a Pessimism — both of which must consistently, in their extreme form, deny that reality can be improved — but concedes to man the right and duty to improve the world. [008-131--008-133]

[[william-james|James]]'s meliorism is not merely descriptive — it is a call to action. By treating [[reality|real]]ity as plastic rather than fixed, [[humanism|Humanism]] opens practical space for [[religious-experience|faith]], effort, and moral striving. This is the link between his [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] theory of truth and his defense of [[religion|religious belief]]: if the world is genuinely open to improvement, then acting on our hopes is not a flight from [[rationality|reason]] but a [[rationality|rational]] engagement with [[reality|the real]] conditions of experience.

> It impresses us with the necessity of acting, it vindicates the procedure of acting on our hopes, it shows us how we may correct our errors, and so gives reasons for our faith in the possibility of Progress. [008-134--008-137]

## Meliorism versus the Absolute

The meliorist position stands in direct opposition to the [[the-absolute|Absolutist]] worldview that James critiques throughout the lectures. For the absolutist, [[reality|real]]ity is already complete and perfect in the eternal scheme of things — a view James finds not only philosophically suspect but morally dangerous:

> The absolute is represented as the hypothesis that is forced on us by the need of an eternal moral order. But the truth is that we do not live in an eternal moral order; we live in a world where good and evil are both real, and where we must fight for the good. [008-115--008-117]

If the world is already perfect sub specie aeternitatis, then there is no genuine urgency to improve it. Meliorism, by contrast, insists that the world's future is genuinely open, that human effort makes a real difference, and that the moral life consists precisely in that effort.

## Meliorism in Pragmatist Ethics

Meliorism represents a distinctive contribution of pragmatism to ethical theory. Three features define this contribution:

1. **[[reality|Reality]] as Plastic**: The meliorist view rejects the "block universe" of absolute idealism in favor of a world that is "most '[[reality|real]]ly' what we can make of it" (008-108). [[knowledge|Knowledge]] and action are continuous — what we know about the world depends, in part, on what we do in it.

2. **Fallibilism and Moral Effort**: Because [[reality|real]]ity is genuinely unfinished, human action carries [[reality|real]] moral weight. [[william-james|James]]'s meliorism includes a strong fallibilist element — we may be wrong about what will improve the world, but the possibility of error does not justify inaction. We act on our [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] and correct our course through experience.

3. **The Right to Believe**: Meliorism is the ontological precondition for [[william-james|James]]'s "will to believe" doctrine. If the world's future is genuinely open, then acting on religious or moral [[religious-experience|faith]] is not a delusion — it is a practical hypothesis that may contribute to its own verification. This connection between meliorism and the religious hypothesis was central to [[william-james|James]]'s broader philosophical project, linking his [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] epistemology to his defense of [[religion|religious experience]].
