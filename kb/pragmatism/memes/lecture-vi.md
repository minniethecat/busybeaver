## CHAPTER VI

[006-001] THE FAILURE OF FORMAL LOGIC

[006-002] In order to escape the necessity of concerning itself with personality

[006-003] and particular circumstances in questions of truth and error,

[006-004] [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]] appeals to [[logic|Logic]], which it conceives as a purely formal

[006-005] science and its impregnable citadel. This appeal, however, rests on a

[006-006] number of questionable assumptions, and most of these are not avowed.

[006-007] 1. It assumes that forms of thought can be treated in abstraction from

[006-008] their matter--in other words, that the general types of thinking are

[006-009] never affected by the particular context in which they occur. Now, this

[006-010] means that the question of [[reality|real]] truth must not be raised; for, as we

[006-011] have seen (Chapter V.), real truth is always an affair of particular

[006-012] consequences. The result is, that as truth-claims are no longer tested,

[006-013] they _all pass as true_ for Logic, and are even raised to the rank of

[006-014] 'absolute truths,' or are mistaken for them. For the notion of a really

[006-015] ('materially') true judgment which someone has chosen, made, and tested,

[006-016] there is substituted that of a formally valid proposition, and in the

[006-017] end Logic gets so involved in the study of 'validity' that it puts aside

[006-018] altogether all real tests of truth, and becomes a game with verbal

[006-019] symbols which is entirely irrelevant to [[science|scientific thinking]].

[006-020] 2. [[formal-logic|Formal Logic]] assumes the right of abstracting from the whole process

[006-021] of making an assertion. It presumes that the assertion has already been

[006-022] made somehow. How, it does not inquire. Yet it is clear that in each

[006-023] case there were concrete reasons why just _that_ assertion was preferred

[006-024] to any other. These concrete reasons it makes bold to dismiss as

[006-025] 'psychological,' and between 'logic' and 'psychology'[F] it decrees an

[006-026] absolute divorce. Where, when, why, by and to whom, an assertion was

[006-027] made, is taken to be irrelevant, and put aside as 'extralogical.'

[006-028] 3. This convenient assumption, however, ultimately necessitates an

[006-029] abstraction from meaning, though Formal Logic does not avow this openly.

[006-030] Every assertion is meant to convey a certain meaning in a certain

[006-031] context, and therefore its verbal 'form' has to take on its own

[006-032] individual _nuance_ of meaning. What any particular form of words does

[006-033] in fact mean on any particular occasion always depends upon the use of

[006-034] the words in a particular context. Meaning, therefore, cannot be

[006-035] depersonalized; if meanings are depersonalized, they cease to be real,

[006-036] and become verbal.

[006-037] Formal Logic has, in fact, mistaken _words_, which are (within the same

[006-038] language) identical on all occasions, for the _thoughts_ they are

[006-039] intended to express, which are varied to suit each occasion. Words alone

[006-040] are tolerant of the abstract treatment Formal Logic demands. This

[006-041] 'science,' therefore, finally reduces to mere verbalism, distracted by

[006-042] inconsistent relapses into 'psychology.'

[006-043] But will this conception of Logic either work out consistently in itself

[006-044] or lead to a tenable theory of scientific thinking? Emphatically not.

[006-045] What is the use of a logic which (1) cannot effect the capital

[006-046] distinction of all thought, that between the true and the false? (2) is

[006-047] debarred by its own principles from considering the _meaning_ of any

[006-048] real assertion? and (3) is thus tossed helplessly from horn to horn of

[006-049] the dilemma 'either verbalism or psychology'?

[006-050] We may select a few examples of this fatal dilemma.

[006-051] 1. In dealing with what it calls 'the meaning' of terms, propositions,

[006-052] etc., Formal Logic has always to choose between the meaning of the

[006-053] _words_ and the meaning of the _man_. For it is clear that words which

[006-054] may be used ambiguously may on occasion leave no doubt as to their

[006-055] meaning, while conversely all may become 'ambiguous' in a context. If,

[006-056] therefore, the occasion is abstracted from, all forms must be treated

[006-057] verbally as ambiguous formulae, which may be used in different senses.

[006-058] If it is, nevertheless, attempted to deal with their actual meaning on

[006-059] any given occasion, what its maker meant the words to convey must be

[006-060] discovered, and the inquiry at once becomes 'psychological'--that is to

[006-061] say, 'extralogical.'

[006-062] 2. If judgments are not to be verbal ('propositions'), but real

[006-063] assertions which are actually meant, they must proceed from personal

[006-064] selections, and must have been chosen from among alternative

[006-065] formulations because of their superior value for their maker's purpose.

[006-066] But all this is plainly an affair of psychology. So inevitable is this

[006-067] that a truly formal Ideal of 'Logic' would exclude all judgment whatever

[006-068] from the complete system of 'eternal' [[truth|Truth]]. For from such a system no

[006-069] part could be rightly extracted to stand alone. Such a selection could

[006-070] be effected and justified only by the exigencies of a human thinker.

[006-071] The impotent verbalism of the formal treatment of judgment appears in

[006-072] another way when the question is raised _how_ a 'true' judgment is to be

[006-073] distinguished from a 'false.' For the logician, if his public will not

[006-074] accept either the relegation of this distinction to 'psychology' or the

[006-075] proper formal answer that _all_ judgments are (formally) 'true' and even

[006-076] 'infallible,' can think of nothing better to say than that if the

[006-077] 'judgment' is not true it was not a 'true judgment,' but a false

[006-078] 'opinion' which may be abandoned to 'psychology.'[G] Apparently he is

[006-079] not concerned to help men to discriminate between 'judgments' and

[006-080] 'opinions,' or even to show that true 'judgments' do in fact occur.

[006-081] 3. Inference involves Formal Logic in a host of difficulties.

[006-082] _(a)_ If it is not to be a verbal manipulation of phrases whose coming

[006-083] together is not inquired into, it must be a connected train of thought.

[006-084] But such a connection of thoughts cannot be conceived or understood

[006-085] without reference to the purpose of a reasoner, who _selects_ what he

[006-086] requires from the totality of 'truths.' If, then, 'Logic' has merely to

[006-087] contemplate this eternal and immutable system of truth in its integrity,

[006-088] and forbids all selection from it for a merely human purpose, how can it

[006-089] either justify, or even understand, the drawing of any inference

[006-090] whatever?

[006-091] (_b_) Formal Logic clearly will not quail before the charge of

[006-092] uselessness. But on its own principles it ought to be consistent. But by

[006-093] this test also, when it is rigorously judged by it, it fails completely.

[006-094] Its inconsistencies are many and incurable. It cannot even be consistent

[006-095] in its theory of the simplest fundamentals. It is found upon some

[006-096] occasions to define judgment as that which may be _either_ true _or_

[006-097] false; and upon others as that which is 'true' (formally)--_i.e._, it

[006-098] cannot decide whether or not to ignore the existence of error.

[006-099] (_c_) The Formal view of inference regards it as a 'paradox.' An

[006-100] inference is required on the one hand to supply fresh information, and

[006-101] on the other to follow rigorously from its premisses; it must, in a

[006-102] word, exhibit both _novelty_ and _necessity_. It would seem, however,

[006-103] that if our inference genuinely had imparted new knowledge, the event

[006-104] must be merely psychological; for how can any process or event perturb,

[006-105] or add to, the completed totality of truth in itself? On the other hand,

[006-106] if the 'necessity' of the operation be taken seriously, the 'inference'

[006-107] becomes illusory; for if the conclusion inferred is already contained

[006-108] in the premisses, what sense is there in the purely verbal process of

[006-109] drawing it out?

[006-110] (_d_) Most glaringly inadequate of all, however, is the Formal doctrine

[006-111] of 'Proof' contained in its theory of the Syllogism. A Formal or verbal

[006-112] syllogism depends essentially on the ability of its Middle Term to

[006-113] connect the terms in its conclusion. If, however, the Middle Term has

[006-114] not _the same_ meaning in the two premisses, the syllogism breaks in

[006-115] two, and no 'valid' conclusion can be reached. Now, whether in fact any

[006-116] particular Middle Term bears the same meaning in any actual reasoning

[006-117] Formal Logic has debarred itself from inquiring, by deciding that actual

[006-118] meaning was 'psychological.' It has to be content, therefore, with an

[006-119] identity _in the word_ employed for its Middle, But this evidence may

[006-120] always fail; for when two premisses which are (in general) 'true' are

[006-121] brought together for the purpose of drawing a particular conclusion, a

[006-122] glaring falsehood may result. _E.g._, it would in general be granted

[006-123] that 'iron sinks in water,' yet it does not follow that because 'this

[006-124] ship is iron' it will 'sink in water,' Hence syllogistic 'proof' seems

[006-125] quite devoid of the 'cogency' it claimed. After a conclusion has been

[006-126] 'demonstrated' _it has still to come true in fact_. This flaw in the

[006-127] Syllogism was first pointed out by Mr. Alfred Sidgwick.

[006-128] (_e_) The formal Syllogism, moreover, conceals another formal flaw. An

[006-129] infinite regress lurks in its bosom. For if its premisses are disputed,

[006-130] they must in turn be 'proved.' Four fresh premisses are needed, and if

[006-131] these again are challenged, the number of true premisses needed to prove

[006-132] the first conclusion goes on doubling at every step _ad infinitum_. The

[006-133] only way to stop the process that occurred to logicians was an appeal to

[006-134] the 'self-evident' truth of '[[intuition|intuitions]]'; but this has been shown to be

[006-135] argumentatively worthless. From this difficulty the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alone

[006-136] escapes, by assuming his premisses _provisionally_ and arguing

[006-137] _forwards_, in order to test them by their consequences. If the deduced

[006-138] conclusion can be verified in fact, the premisses grow more assured.

[006-139] Thus every real inference is an experiment, and 'proof' is an affair of

[006-140] continuous trial and verification--not an infinite falling back upon an

[006-141] elusive 'certainty,' but an infinite reaching forwards towards a fuller

[006-142] consummation.

[006-143] (_f_) So long as the logician regards his premisses not as [[hypothesis|hypotheses]] to

[006-144] be tested, but as established truths, he must condemn the Syllogism as a

[006-145] formal fallacy. It is inevitably a _petitio principii_. If the argument

[006-146] 'All men are mortal; Smith is a man, therefore Smith is mortal,' means

[006-147] that we know, before drawing our inference, that literally all men are

[006-148] mortal, we must already have discovered that Smith is mortal; if we did

[006-149] not know beforehand that Smith is mortal, we were not justified in

[006-150] stating that _all_ men are mortal. Nor is it an escape to interpret 'All

[006-151] men are mortal' to mean that immortals are excluded from 'man' by

[006-152] definition. For then the question is merely begged in the minor premiss.

[006-153] That 'Smith is a man' cannot be asserted without assuming that he is

[006-154] mortal. If, lastly, 'All men are mortal' be taken to state a law of

[006-155] nature conjoining inseparably mortality and humanity, the logician

[006-156] either already knows that Smith is rightly classed under the species

[006-157] 'man,' and so subject to its mortality, or else he _assumes_ this. But

[006-158] how does he know Smith is not like Elijah or Tithonus, a peculiar case,

[006-159] to which for some [[rationality|reason]] the law does not apply? Will he declare it to

[006-160] be 'intuitively certain' that whatever is called, or looks like, a case

[006-161] of a 'law' _ipso facto_ becomes one?

[006-162] The logician's analysis of reasoning, then, breaks down. In whichever

[006-163] way he interprets the Syllogism it is revealed as either a superfluity

[006-164] or a fallacy: it is never a 'formally valid inference' that can compel

[006-165] assent. But common sense is undismayed by the pragmatist's discovery

[006-166] that if the Syllogism is to have any sense its premisses _must_ be taken

[006-167] as disputable; for, unlike Formal Logic, it has perceived that men do

[006-168] not reason about what they think they know for certain, but about

[006-169] matters in dispute.

[006-170] 4. It is not necessary to dwell at length on the futility of the formal

[006-171] notion of Induction. Formal Induction presupposes that enough particular

[006-172] instances have been collected to establish a general rule; but in actual

[006-173] practice inductions always repose, not on indiscriminate observation,

[006-174] but on a _selection of relevant instances_, and never claim to be based

[006-175] upon an _exhaustive_ knowledge of particulars. Hence _in form_ the most

[006-176] satisfactory induction is always incomplete, and differs in no wise from

[006-177] a bad one. 'All bodies fall to the ground' is an induction which has

[006-178] worked. 'All swans are white' broke down when black swans were

[006-179] discovered in Australia. The validity of an induction, then, is not a

[006-180] question of form.

[006-181] The necessity for such selection no intellectualist theory of Induction

[006-182] has understood. All have aimed at exhaustiveness, and imagined that if

[006-183] it could be attained, inductive reasoning would be rendered sound, and

[006-184] not impossible. Their ideal 'cause' was the totality of reality,

[006-185] identified with its 'effect,' in a meaningless tautology. Nothing but

[006-186] voluntarism can enable logicians to see that our actual procedure in

[006-187] [[knowledge|knowing]] is the reverse of this, that causal explanation is the

[006-188] _analysis_ of a continuum, and that 'phenomena,' 'events,' 'effects,'

[006-189] and 'causes' are all creations of our selective attention; that in

[006-190] selecting them we run a risk of analyzing falsely, and that if we do,

[006-191] our 'inductions' will be worthless. But whether they are right or wrong,

[006-192] valuable or not, real reasoning from 'facts' can never be a 'formally

[006-193] valid' process.

[006-194] We are thus brought to see the hollowness of the contention that 'Pure

[006-195] [[reason|Reason]]' can ignore its psychological context and dehumanize itself. A

[006-196] thought, to be thought at all, must seem _worth_ thinking to someone, it

[006-197] must convey the meaning he intends, it must be true in his eyes and

[006-198] relevant to his purposes in the situation in which it arises--_i.e._, it

[006-199] must have a motive, a value, a meaning, a purpose, a context, and be

[006-200] selected from a greater whole for its relevance to these. None of these

[006-201] features does intellectualist logic deign to recognize. For if truth is

[006-202] absolute and not relative, it is all or nothing. Yet no actual thinking

[006-203] has such transcendent aims. It is content with selections relative to a

[006-204] concrete situation. If it were permissible to diversify a

[006-205] debate--_e.g._, about the authorship of the _Odyssey_--by an irruption

[006-206] of undisputed truths--_e.g._, a recitation of the multiplication

[006-207] table--how would it be possible to distinguish a philosopher from a

[006-208] lunatic?

[006-209] Formal Logic is either a perennial source of errors about real thinking,

[006-210] or at best an aimless dissection of a _caput mortuum--i.e._, of the

[006-211] verbal husks of dead thoughts, whose value Formal Logic could neither

[006-212] establish nor apprehend, A real Logic, therefore, would most anxiously

[006-213] avoid all the initial abstractions which have reduced Formal Logic to

[006-214] such impotence, and would abandon the insane attempt to eliminate the

[006-215] thinker from the theory of thought.
