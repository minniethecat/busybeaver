## Overview

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) is the central figure of modern philosophy, whose "Copernican R[[darwinism|evolution]]" in epistemology attempted to answer [[david-hume|Hume]]'s scepticism by positing that the mind actively structures experience through innate categories and forms of intuition. In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ (1907), Kant appears as the most important successor to [[david-hume|Hume]] and the chief representative of *[[apriorism|a priori]]* [[rationality|rational]]ism — a position [[william-james|James]] thoroughly critiques and ultimately dismisses. The [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]], as [[william-james|James]] presents it, goes not "through the complexities of Kantism, but leaves them on one side as superfluous 'curios'" (002-191).

[[william-james|James]]'s treatment of Kant is ambivalent but ultimately critical: he acknowledges Kant's genius in recognizing the insufficiency of [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic psychology, but argues that Kant's solution — a transcendental apparatus of categories, pure intuitions, and a Transcendental Ego — is an over-engineered palliative that obscures rather than solves the problem. For pragmatism, Kant represents the high-water mark of the [[rationality|rational]]ist temptation to multiply metaphysical machinery when a simpler, more empirical account of experience (one recognizing relations and continuities as directly given) would suffice.

## Life

- **1724** — Born in Konigsberg, East Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia)
- **1740–1746** — Studies at the University of Konigsberg, absorbing Wolffian [[rationality|rational]]ism and Newtonian physics
- **1755** — Obtains his doctorate and begins lecturing at the University of Konigsberg as a _Privatdozent_
- **1770** — Appointed Professor of [[logic|Logic]] and Metaphysics at Konigsberg; publishes the _Inaugural Dissertation_, marking his turn toward "critical" philosophy
- **1781** — Publishes the first edition of _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_ (_[[critique-of-pure-reason|Kritik der reinen Vernunft]]_), the foundational text of transcendental idealism
- **1783** — Publishes _Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics_ as a more accessible summary of the _Critique_
- **1785** — Publishes _Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals_, establishing the categorical imperative
- **1787** — Publishes the extensively revised second edition of the _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_
- **1788** — Publishes _Critique of Practical [[reason|Reason]]_, the second Critique
- **1790** — Publishes _Critique of Judgment_, completing the critical trilogy
- **1793** — Publishes _[[religion|Religion]] within the Bounds of Bare [[reason|Reason]]_; censured by the Prussian government
- **1804** — Dies in Konigsberg at age 79

## Key Ideas

### 1. The Synthetic A Priori and the Copernican Revolution

Kant's signature innovation was his claim that the mind does not passively receive experience but actively constitutes it. Rather than asking how knowledge conforms to objects, he reversed the question: objects must conform to our cognition. This yielded a class of "synthetic [[apriorism|a priori]]" judgments — necessary truths about experience (such as the principle of causation) that are not derived from experience but are the prior conditions that make experience possible.

[[william-james|James]], through his presentation in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, describes Kant's project as one of patching up [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic analysis:

> He had, however, made the embarrassments of the theory of knowledge sufficiently clear for Kant, his most important successor, to hit upon the most obvious palliative, and in the _Critique of Pure Reason_ Kant set himself to patch up Hume's analysis. Experience as it came through the channels of sense, he admitted Hume had analysed correctly; it was 'a manifold,' a whirl of separate sensations. But these _per se_ could not yield knowledge. They must be made to cohere. (002-073--002-078)

### 2. The Transcendental Apparatus

Kant posited an elaborate architecture of mental faculties: "pure intuitions" of Space and Time that give form to sensory data, twelve "categories" of the understanding (including substance and causality) that organize intuitions into objects of knowledge, and a "Transcendental Ego" that provides the unity of apperception — the "I think" that must accompany all representations. [[william-james|James]] finds this machinery gratuitous:

> The mind on to which they fell was equipped with a complicated apparatus of faculties which could organize the chaotic manifold of sense and turn it into the connected world which common sense and science recognize. First it views the data of sense in the light of its own 'pure intuitions,' and, lo! they are seen to be in Space and Time; then it solidifies them with its own 'categories,' which turn them into 'substances' and 'causes' and endow them with all the attributes required to sustain that status; finally it refers them all to a Transcendental Ego. (002-080--002-088)

### 3. The Unguarded Gulf

The central vulnerability in Kant's system is the unexplained gap between the sensory manifold and the categories that are supposed to organize it. Why should sensations submit so tamely to the mind's forming activity? [[william-james|James]] points out that [[david-hume|Hume]] would have seen through this:

> Had Hume lived to read Kant's _Critique_, he would probably have jeered at the vain complications of Kant's transcendental machinery, and made it clear that between the primary manifold of sensation and the first constructions of the intellect there still yawns a gulf which Kant's laboured explanations nowhere bridge. (002-090--002-094)

> Why does the chaotic 'matter' of sensations submit itself so tamely to the forming of the mind? How can the _a priori_ necessities of thought, which are the 'presuppositions' of the complexities Kant loved, operate upon so alien a stuff as the sensations are assumed to be? (002-095--002-098)

### 4. Premature Finality

[[william-james|James]] also charges Kant with overclaiming — treating his analysis as definitive and final rather than provisional:

> And, after all, was not Kant a bit premature in proclaiming the _finality_ of his analysis and of his refutation of empiricism for all time? The searching question, Why should the future resemble the past? had received no answer, and so might not the mind itself, with all its categories, be susceptible to change? (002-099--002-103)

> Had not Kant himself as good as admitted that our faculties might distort reality instead of making it intelligible? (002-105--002-106)

### 5. Legitimating the Mind's Impudence

[[william-james|James]] follows Schiller in arguing that by sanctifying the mind's constructive activity with a technical vocabulary, Kant effectively legitimized what [[david-hume|Hume]] had correctly identified as a falsification of sensory data:

> The truth is that at this point Kant is open to a charge against which the assumptions he shared with Hume admit of no defence. Hume had been the first to discover that we are in the habit of trying to rationalize our sense-data by putting ideal constructions upon them, though he had abstained from sanctifying the practice by a hideous jargon of technical terminology. But this way of eking out the facts only seemed to him to _falsify_ them. (002-107--002-113)

> What right, then, had Kant to legitimate the mind's impudence in tampering with sensations? Was not every _a priori_ form an 'imagination,' and a vain one at that? (002-116--002-118)

### 6. The Kantian Impasse

[[william-james|James]] notes that the Kantian school never resolved these objections and simply repeated their master's formulas:

> To these objections the Kantian school have never found an answer. They have simply repeated Kant's phrases about the necessary 'presuppositions' which were to be added to Hume's data. (002-119--002-121)

## Relation to Pragmatism

Kant stands in the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] narrative as the most significant **critic of empiricism** whose system is ultimately **rejected** rather than assimilated. His relation to pragmatism operates on several levels:

1. **Precursor in recognizing the active mind**: Kant was correct to see that the mind is not a passive recipient of sensations — a point pragmatism enthusiastically affirms. [[william-james|James]] writes that "Kant's _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ activity of the mind may thus in a sense supply an answer to [[david-hume|Hume]] — but only in a voluntaristic philosophy which would probably have seemed too bold both to him and to [[david-hume|Hume]]" (002-218--002-220). [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] accepts the activity of mind but naturalizes it as biological function rather than transcendental necessity.

2. **The wrong answer to the right question**: Kant correctly diagnosed the failure of [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic psychology but prescribed the wrong remedy. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] solution — [[william-james|James]]'s [[empiricism|radical empiricism]] — recognizes that relations are directly perceived in experience, making the entire transcendental apparatus unnecessary:

> Here, then, is the straight way James desiderated, a critical philosophy which goes, not 'through' the complexities of Kantism, but leaves them on one side as superfluous 'curios.' (002-190--002-192)

3. **Schiller's verdict**: [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]], the Oxford [[pragmatism|pragmatist]], argued that "Kant failed to answer [[david-hume|Hume]] because he accepted [[david-hume|Hume]]'s psychology, and that no _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine" (001-124--001-126). By accepting [[david-hume|Hume]]'s premise that sensations are discrete atoms, Kant was trapped into inventing elaborate machinery to reconnect them.

4. **From transcendental to voluntaristic**: The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative to Kant's [[rationality|rational]]ism is a voluntaristic epistemology in which the mind's organizing principles are [[postulation|postulates]] — risk-laden, experimentally tested, and biologically evolved — rather than necessary, timeless structures. As [[william-james|James]]'s presentation in Lecture III argues, "a third alternative to the banal dilemma of 'empiricism' or 'apriorism' suggests itself" (003-031).

## Major Works

- **_[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_** (_[[critique-of-pure-reason|Kritik der reinen Vernunft]]_, 1781/1787) — Kant's masterwork, referenced in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ as "the _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_" (002-074), where Kant "set himself to patch up [[david-hume|Hume]]'s analysis" (002-075)
- **_Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics_** (1783) — A more accessible restatement of the first Critique
- **_Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals_** (1785) — Foundation of Kantian deontological ethics and the categorical imperative
- **_Critique of Practical [[reason|Reason]]_** (1788) — The second Critique, on moral philosophy
- **_Critique of Judgment_** (1790) — The third Critique, on aesthetics and teleology
- **_[[religion|Religion]] within the Bounds of Bare [[reason|Reason]]_** (1793) — Kant's philosophy of [[religious-experience|religion]]
- **_Metaphysics of Morals_** (1797) — Systematic application of Kantian ethics to law and virtue
