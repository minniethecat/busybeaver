## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, "Reason" with a capital R denotes the intellectualist ideal of a pure, self-sufficient faculty capable of accessing truth independent of human psychology, purpose, or context. [[william-james|James]]'s sustained critique, running through Lectures I through VI, demonstrates that this conception of Reason is hollow: [[reality|real]] [[rationality|reason]]ing is always an activity of a concrete [[rationality|reason]]er, driven by specific purposes, selecting relevant materials from a totality, and validated not by formal structure but by practical consequences (006-195).

## Origin & Context

The target of [[william-james|James]]'s critique is the [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] legacy of "Pure Reason" — the notion that the mind possesses _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ structures which guarantee the validity of knowledge prior to and independently of experience. [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_ attempted to patch up [[david-hume|Hume]]'s skeptical analysis of experience by positing transcendental categories through which the chaotic manifold of sensation is organized into coherent knowledge (002-074). But [[william-james|James]] argues that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] opened an unbridgeable gulf between the mind's constructions and the sensations they are supposed to organize (002-093).

> In the _Critique of Pure Reason_ Kant set himself to patch up Hume's analysis. Experience as it came through the channels of sense, he admitted Hume had analysed correctly; it was 'a manifold,' a whirl of separate sensations. But these _per se_ could not yield knowledge. (002-074)

The historical framing of pragmatism itself was, in part, as an "assault of [[religion|theology]] upon the integrity of Pure Reason" (001-006) in the eyes of its critics. [[william-james|James]] explicitly distances pragmatism from this caricature while nevertheless affirming that the ideal of Pure Reason cannot survive pragmatic scrutiny.

> Sometimes it is pictured as an insurgence of emotion against logic, sometimes as an assault of theology upon the integrity of Pure Reason. One day it is described as the reckless theorizing of dilettanti whose knowledge of philosophy is too superficial to require refutation. (001-005)

## Key Arguments

1. **The Hollowness of "Pure Reason"**: [[william-james|James]] argues that "Pure Reason" is a fiction because thought is inseparable from the thinker. A thought, to be thought at all, "must seem _worth_ thinking to someone, it must convey the meaning he intends, it must be true in his eyes and relevant to his purposes in the situation in which it arises" (006-195). Formal logic's pretense of ignoring this psychological context is precisely what renders it incapable of explaining or justifying any actual [[rationality|reason]]ing.

> We are thus brought to see the hollowness of the contention that 'Pure Reason' can ignore its psychological context and dehumanize itself. A thought, to be thought at all, must seem _worth_ thinking to someone, it must convey the meaning he intends, it must be true in his eyes and relevant to his purposes in the situation in which it arises — _i.e._, it must have a motive, a value, a meaning, a purpose, a context, and be selected from a greater whole for its relevance to these. (006-194)

2. **Reasoning Is Always Purposive and Selective**: Every genuine act of [[rationality|reason]]ing involves a [[rationality|reason]]er who selects from an indefinite field of possible considerations. The abstract logician's model of inference as a mechanical derivation from given premises cannot capture actual [[rationality|reason]]ing because it omits the act of selection that makes inference possible in the first place. Inference "cannot be conceived or understood without reference to the purpose of a [[rationality|reason]]er, who _selects_ what he requires from the totality of 'truths'" (006-085). None of these essential features — motive, value, meaning, purpose, context — "does intellectualist logic deign to recognize" (006-201).

> A connection of thoughts cannot be conceived or understood without reference to the purpose of a reasoner, who _selects_ what he requires from the totality of 'truths.' If, then, 'Logic' has merely to contemplate this eternal and immutable system of truth in its integrity, and forbids all selection from it for a merely human purpose, how can it either justify, or even understand, the drawing of any inference whatever? (006-085)

3. **Formal Reasoning Cannot Be "Formally Valid"**: [[william-james|James]] demonstrates through detailed analysis of both the syllogism and induction that formal logic can never account for the actual validity of [[reality|real]] [[rationality|reason]]ing. The logician's analysis of the syllogism "breaks down" in every interpretation — it is "either a superfluity or a fallacy" and "never a 'formally valid inference' that can compel assent" (006-162). Similarly, the formal notion of induction presupposes exhaustive collection of instances, but actual induction always rests on a "selection of relevant instances" (006-173) whose relevance cannot be justified by formal criteria alone. Real [[rationality|reason]]ing from facts "can never be a 'formally valid' process" (006-192).

> The logician's analysis of reasoning, then, breaks down. In whichever way he interprets the Syllogism it is revealed as either a superfluity or a fallacy: it is never a 'formally valid inference' that can compel assent. But common sense is undismayed by the pragmatist's discovery that if the Syllogism is to have any sense its premisses _must_ be taken as disputable; for, unlike Formal Logic, it has perceived that men do not reason about what they think they know for certain, but about matters in dispute. (006-162)

4. **Reason Serves Life, Not Contemplation**: [[william-james|James]]'s pragmatism does not abolish [[rationality|reason]] but recontextualizes it. The intellect must be restored to "its natural connection with human activity" from which intellectualist philosophy had severed it (007-018). Reason is not a "vast Juggernaut machine which absorbs and crushes the individual thinker" (008-007); it is a living instrument wielded by persons for their purposes. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] reconstruction of the intellect rescues thought "from the bankruptcy of [[intellectualism|Intellectualism]]" (007-071) by scaling down the impossible demands placed on it and recognizing that [[knowledge|practical knowledge]], though not absolute, "is enough to live by" (007-060).

> Was it not, after all, an unwarranted assumption that severed the intellect from its natural connection with human activity? No doubt it seemed to simplify the problem to suppose that the functioning of the intellect could be studied as a thing apart, and unrelated to the general context of the vital functions. (007-017)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] Pure Reason** (_reine Vernunft_): The faculty by which the mind imposes _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ categories (substance, cause, unity) upon the sensory manifold, producing the ordered world of experience and science. [[william-james|James]] critiques this as leaving unexplained why sensations should submit to mental forming — "between the world of [[reality|real]] things and the ideal constructions of the intellect there still yawns a gulf which [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s laboured explanations nowhere bridge" (002-093).

- **Formal-[[logic|Logic]]al Reason**: The conception of [[rationality|reason]] as rule-governed manipulation of symbols according to formal schemata (syllogism, induction). [[william-james|James]]'s analysis in Lecture VI demonstrates that this conception cannot account for the validity of any actual inference, because it abstracts away the selective, purposive, and contextual dimensions essential to [[rationality|reason]]ing (006-162, 006-192).

- **Rationalist Reason**: The broader intellectualist tradition that treats [[rationality|reason]] as a transcendent faculty capable of accessing necessary truths independent of experience. [[william-james|James]] identifies this as the defining characteristic of the "tender-minded" philosophical temperament, which values principles over facts and system over consequences.

- **Pragmatic Reason**: The [[william-james|James]]ian alternative, in which [[rationality|reason]]ing is an activity performed by embodied, purposive agents who [[postulation|postulate]] principles, act on them, and revise them through their consequences in experience. Reason is not abolished but reconceived as a tool of life, validated by its fruits rather than its formal structure (003-058, 005-185).

## Criticisms & Debates

Intellectualist critics charge that pragmatism destroys the normative force of [[rationality|reason]]. If [[rationality|reason]]ing is always tied to particular purposes and contexts, what grounds the distinction between good [[rationality|reason]]ing and [[rationality|rational]]ization? [[william-james|James]] answers that the standard is pragmatic: [[postulation|postulates]] "have to work in a world of fact, and to adapt themselves to all facts" (005-205). The constraint is not formal but experiential — unsuccessful [[postulation|postulates]] are eliminated by their failure to guide action effectively.

A related objection is that pragmatism's critique of Pure Reason leads to relativism: if no [[rationality|reason]]ing is "formally valid," can any conclusion be [[rationality|rational]]ly compelled? [[william-james|James]]'s response is that the compulsion is practical rather than metaphysical. The success of a mode of [[rationality|reason]]ing in organizing experience and guiding action provides all the warrant that human beings can or need demand. "True, [[knowledge|practical knowledge]] is not absolute; but if it is enough to live by, is it not better to live by it than to be lured on to perish in the deserts of [[scepticism|Scepticism]] by the _mirage_ of an [[absolutism|absolute truth]] not humanly attainable?" (007-060).

Bertrand Russell and other early analytic philosophers charged that pragmatism conflates the _definition_ of truth with the _criterion_ for recognizing it, reducing truth to what is useful rather than what is accurate. [[william-james|James]]'s implicit response is that our only access to truth is through its practical bearings in experience, and that the demand for a purely formal criterion of [[rationality|rational]] validity is itself a legacy of the intellectualist ideal that pragmatism has shown to be empty.
