## Overview

Transcendentalism in its broadest sense is the philosophical movement inaugurated by [[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]]'s "transcendental" turn — the attempt to identify the [[apriorism|a priori]] conditions that make experience possible. Rather than asking how the mind can conform to objects (the [[empiricism|empiricist]] approach), [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] asked how objects must conform to the mind. The result was a complex "transcendental machinery" of categories, forms of intuition, and synthetic [[apriorism|a priori]] judgments.

[[william-james|William James]] discussed transcendentalism at two levels in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_: as a philosophical method ([[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s critical apparatus) and as a temperament (the "tender-minded" preference for elegant [[apriorism|a priori]] structures over messy empirical facts). At both levels, he was skeptical.

## Core Ideas

Transcendentalism in its [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] form is defined by several commitments:

1. **The Copernican r[[darwinism|evolution]] in philosophy.** [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] proposed that objects must conform to our cognition, rather than our cognition conforming to objects. The mind actively structures experience through its own forms and categories.

2. **The transcendental deduction.** [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] argued that certain concepts — causality, substance, unity — are necessary conditions of possible experience. Without them, experience would be a chaotic "manifold of sensation" with no coherence.

3. **The distinction between phenomena and noumena.** We can know things only as they appear to us (phenomena), never as they are in themselves (noumena). The transcendental machinery reveals the structure of appearance, not of [[reality|real]]ity.

4. **The synthetic [[apriorism|a priori]].** Certain judgments — such as those of mathematics and the fundamental principles of [[science|natural science]] — are both informative (synthetic) and independent of experience ([[apriorism|a priori]]).

## Relation to Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s critique of transcendentalism was pragmatic in form but radical in substance. He did not attempt to refute [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s arguments on their own terms; instead, he questioned whether the entire project was necessary:

> Had Hume lived to read Kant's _Critique_, he would probably have jeered at the vain complications of Kant's transcendental machinery, and made it clear that between the primary manifold of sensation and the first constructions of the intellect there still yawns a gulf which Kant's laboured explanations nowhere bridge. (002-090--002-094)

[[william-james|James]]'s point is that transcendental philosophy addresses a problem of its own making. If you start with the assumption that experience is a chaos of disconnected sensations (the [[empiricism|empiricist]] premise), then you need a transcendental apparatus to explain how order emerges. But if you recognize, with [[william-james|James]], that relations are given in experience itself — that the stream of consciousness comes already structured — then the transcendental machinery becomes superfluous.

[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s transcendental apparatus was, in [[william-james|James]]'s view, an attempt to supplement empiricism without abandoning its fundamental error:

> All apriorists submitted to it, even in supplementing and modifying it by "transcendental" and metaphysical additions; in either case it remained uncontested _as psychology_. (002-005--002-007)

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] alternative is to contest the psychology itself — to replace [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic picture of the mind with [[william-james|James]]'s dynamic, selective, relational one.

## Key Figures

| Philosopher | Role | Key Contribution |
|------------|------|-----------------|
| [[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]] (1724–1804) | Founder | _[[critique-of-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]_ (1781/1787); transcendental idealism |
| J. G. Fichte (1762–1814) | Successor | _Wissenschaftslehre_; radicalized [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s transcendental ego |
| Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882) | New England variant | _Nature_ (1836); literary-philosophical transcendentalism |
| Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862) | New England variant | _Walden_ (1854); practical transcendentalism |

The New England Transcendentalists — Emerson, Thoreau, Margaret Fuller — adapted [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] themes for American soil, emphasizing intuition, self-reliance, and the divinity of nature. [[william-james|James]] was ambivalent about this tradition: he shared its emphasis on individual experience and its resistance to dogma, but he mistrusted its tendency toward monistic idealism.
