## Overview

Ernst Mach (1838–1916) was an Austrian physicist and philosopher whose radical empiricism — the view that science should eliminate all unobservable entities and describe only the relations among sensations — was a major influence on [[william-james|William James]]'s [[pragmatism|pragmatic]] philosophy of [[science|science]]. [[william-james|James]] met Mach in Prague in 1882 and was deeply impressed by his philosophical approach, which treated scientific laws not as discoveries of transcendent truths but as economical descriptions of experiential regularities.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1838** — Born in Chirlitz, Moravia (now Czech Republic).
- **1864** — Appointed professor of mathematics at the University of Graz.
- **1867** — Appointed professor of physics at the University of Prague.
- **1882** — Met [[william-james|William James]] in Prague; began a lasting intellectual friendship.
- **1895** — Appointed professor of the history and theory of inductive sciences at the University of Vienna.
- **1916** — Died in Munich.

## Key Contributions

Mach's most significant contribution to philosophy was his critique of Newtonian absolute space and time, which paved the way for Einstein's relativity theory. More broadly, Mach argued that science should describe only what can be observed — relations among sensations — and should eliminate all metaphysical entities (atoms, forces, absolute space) that cannot be directly experienced.

This "phenomenalist" or "neutral monist" approach influenced [[william-james|James]]'s [[radical-empiricism|radical empiricism]], which similarly insisted that "pure experience" — the flux of sensations and relations — is the fundamental stuff of reality, with mind and matter being functional classifications within it rather than separate substances.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

Mach influenced [[william-james|James]] in several ways:

1. **The economic view of scientific laws**: Scientific laws are human instruments for organizing experience, not revelations of metaphysical truth — a view central to James's pragmatist account of science.
2. **The critique of metaphysical entities**: The pragmatic demand for "cash-value" in concrete experience echoes Mach's demand that science eliminate unobservables.
3. **The primacy of experience**: Both Mach and James start from experience as the given and derive mind and matter as functional distinctions within it.
