## Overview

George Berkeley (1685–1753) was an Irish philosopher and Anglican bishop whose radical empiricism led him to the startling conclusion that material substance does not exist — that to be is to be perceived (esse est percipi). In [[william-james|William James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, Berkeley appears as a pivotal figure: the empiricist who pushed Lockean principles to their logical conclusion and in doing so revealed both the power and the danger of empiricism. [[william-james|James]] admired Berkeley's willingness to follow an argument wherever it led, even when that meant denying the existence of matter, but he ultimately rejected Berkeley's conclusion as an overcorrection — pragmatism acknowledges the reality of the material world while insisting that our access to it is always mediated by experience.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1685** — Born in Kilkenny, Ireland.
- **1707** — Elected Fellow of Trinity College, Dublin.
- **1710** — Published _A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge_, his major philosophical work.
- **1713** — Published _Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous_.
- **1728** — Traveled to America, where he planned (unsuccessfully) to found a college in Bermuda.
- **1734** — Appointed Bishop of Cloyne.
- **1753** — Died in Oxford.

## Key Contributions

Berkeley's central argument is that the very concept of "matter" — a substance existing independently of perception — is incoherent. All we ever experience are ideas, and the claim that these ideas are caused by an unperceived material substance is both unverifiable and unnecessary. The regular order of our experiences is explained not by matter but by God, who directly produces our ideas.

This argument is a forerunner of the [[pragmatic-method|pragmatic method]]: Berkeley asks what practical difference the concept of "matter" makes, and finding none, declares it meaningless. [[william-james|James]] admired this move but applied it more carefully — where Berkeley eliminated matter, [[william-james|James]] insisted that the concept of matter *does* make a practical difference (it organizes our expectations about experience) and therefore *is* meaningful.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

Berkeley represents both a precursor to and a warning for pragmatism. His willingness to eliminate unverifiable concepts anticipates the pragmatic critique of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]], but his conclusion that matter does not exist shows what happens when the pragmatic impulse is not balanced by the tough-minded empiricist's respect for the recalcitrance of experience.
