## Definition

Intuition, in the philosophical context of James's lectures, refers to the claimed capacity of the mind to apprehend certain truths directly, without reliance on reasoning, sensory evidence, or practical consequences. It is positioned as the epistemological foundation of intellectualist and rationalist systems, which hold that some propositions are self-evidently true and known with immediate certainty.

## The Pragmatic Critique of Intuition

### The Discrimination Problem

The pragmatist critique of intuition centers on a practical problem: how are "true" intuitions to be distinguished from "false" ones? As the lectures argue, when intuitions are appealed to, the critic will ask "how 'true' intuitions are to be discriminated from 'false,' sound from insane." (004-066) This is not a theoretical question but a practical demand — the intellectualist who claims intuitive knowledge must provide a method for telling which intuitions are reliable.

The demand is sharpened by the observation that no such method exists. The pragmatist "will demand, therefore, the publication of a list of the intuitions which are absolutely true. But he will not get it, and if he did, it may be predicted that he would not find a single one which has not been disputed by some eminent philosopher." (004-070) The historical record of philosophy, in which every claimed intuition has been contested, undermines the claim that intuition provides a reliable foundation for knowledge.

### An Embarrassment to Intellectualism

Intuitions create a structural problem for intellectualist epistemology. Intellectualism "has to maintain both that intuitions are the foundations of all truth and certitude, and also that not all are true." (004-074) This dual commitment leaves intellectualism unable to answer the natural question of how true and false intuitions are to be told apart.

The lectures identify a further difficulty: "what is, or is not, an intuition is revealed only to reflection after the event. Only if an intuition _has_ played us false, we may be sure it _was_ not infallible." (004-079) This retrospective-only identification means intuition provides no forward guidance — one cannot know in advance whether a seeming intuition is genuine, and can only discover its falsity after it has already led one astray.

### Intuitions as Historical Products

Against the claim that intuitions are immediate deliverances of reason, the pragmatist account reinterprets them as historical products. "Even the self-evident and self-confident 'intuitions' that impress the uncritical so much with their claim to infallibility are really the results of antecedent doubts and ponderings, and would never be enunciated unless there were thought to be a dispute about them." (005-030) What appears as an a priori intuition is, on this view, actually a belief that has been shaped by prior inquiry and that is advanced precisely because it has been challenged.

This historical reinterpretation connects to the broader pragmatist thesis that "in real life thought starts from perplexities" (005-034) and aims at resolving doubts, rather than starting from certainties. Intuitions are not the starting points of thought but its end products, crystallized and misrecognized as original.

### The Refusal to Define

A further target of critique is the intuitionist's refusal to define truth on the grounds that it is "indefinable" — "an ultimate unanalyzable quality of certain propositions, intuitively felt, and incapable of description." (005-140) The lectures point out that if error is equally indefinable and immediately apprehended, the existence of persistent disagreement about what is true and what is false becomes inexplicable. The intuitionist position cannot account for the social and historical reality of cognitive dispute.

## The Pragmatic Alternative

In place of intuition, pragmatism proposes that claims to truth be tested by their practical consequences. The "correspondence" theory of truth "lies in its insistence that thought is not to audit its own accounts. Its success or failure depends upon factors external to it." (005-111) Rather than trusting the internal feeling of certainty that accompanies an intuition, the pragmatist looks to the external, verifiable consequences of believing a proposition. A belief is true not because it is intuitively apprehended as such, but because acting upon it leads to satisfactory outcomes.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Intuition is closely linked to **intellectualism** and **rationalism**, which treat intuitive self-evidence as a legitimate source of knowledge. It stands in opposition to the **pragmatic method**, which demands external verification. The critique of intuition also connects to the pragmatist rejection of **absolutism** in truth — the claim that some truths are known absolutely, without reference to context or consequence. Intuition functions in intellectualist systems as the mechanism by which absolute truths are supposedly accessed; undermining intuition thus undermines the absolutist position.
