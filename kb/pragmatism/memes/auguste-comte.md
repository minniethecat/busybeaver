## Overview

Auguste Comte (1798–1857) was a French philosopher who founded the doctrine of [[positivism|positivism]] and established sociology as a scientific discipline. His "law of three stages" held that human knowledge progresses from theological through metaphysical to positive (scientific) understanding — a developmental view of knowledge that influenced the pragmatist account of [[truth|truth]] as an evolving, progressive affair.

## Relevance to Pragmatism

Comte's positivism — the view that genuine knowledge is limited to what can be empirically verified — provides an important backdrop to the pragmatist theory of knowledge. [[william-james|William James]] engages with positivism in _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_ as a form of empiricism that, while admirably restrained in its truth-claims, risks becoming overly narrow by excluding religious and metaphysical questions that have practical significance for human life.

The pragmatist response to Comtean positivism, as articulated by [[william-james|James]], is that [[truth|truth]] cannot be limited to what is scientifically verifiable in the narrow sense. The religious hypothesis, for example, has practical consequences for how one lives, and these consequences are relevant to its truth — a view Comte would reject as a regression to theological thinking.
