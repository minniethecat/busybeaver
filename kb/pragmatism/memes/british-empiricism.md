## Overview

British Empiricism is the [[philosophy|philosophical tradition]], originating in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, that holds that all knowledge derives ultimately from sensory [[experience]]. Its three canonical figures — John Locke, George Berkeley, and [[david-hume|David Hume]] — developed increasingly radical versions of the [[empiricism|empiricist]] thesis, culminating in [[david-hume|Hume]]'s skeptical conclusion that neither causality, the self, nor the external world can be [[rationality|rational]]ly justified on purely [[empiricism|empiricist]] grounds.

[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] is often described as a radicalization of empiricism — "[[empiricism|radical empiricism]]," in [[william-james|James]]'s phrase. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]]s inherited the [[empiricism|empiricist]] commitment to grounding knowledge in experience while rejecting the atomistic psychology (experience as a succession of discrete sensations) that had led [[david-hume|Hume]] to [[scepticism|skepticism]].

## Core Doctrines

British Empiricism is defined by several interlocking commitments:

1. **The origin of ideas in experience.** All ideas are ultimately derived from sensory impressions ([[david-hume|Hume]]) or from reflection upon the operations of the mind on sensory material (Locke). There are no innate ideas.

2. **The primacy of experience over [[rationality|reason]].** [[reason|Reason]] alone, unaided by experience, cannot yield substantive knowledge about the world. Even mathematical and logical truths were understood by the more radical [[empiricism|empiricist]]s (Mill) as generalizations from experience.

3. **Anti-metaphysical orientation.** [[empiricism|Empiricism]] was skeptical of speculative metaphysical systems that claimed insight into [[reality|real]]ities beyond possible experience. The proper business of philosophy is the critical examination of the scope and limits of human understanding.

4. **Associationist psychology.** Complex ideas are built up from simple ones through the laws of association (contiguity, resemblance, cause and effect). This psychological framework, developed by Locke and refined by [[david-hume|Hume]] and later by [[william-james|James]] Mill and Alexander Bain, provided the background against which [[william-james|James]] developed his own psychology.

## Relation to Pragmatism

[[william-james|James]]'s relationship to British Empiricism was one of inheritance and transformation:

> William James had merely to invert the problem. Where Hume had dissolved the self into a "bundle of perceptions" and found no connecting principle, James insisted that the connections — the transitive relations between perceptions — are themselves given in experience. (002-136--002-149)

[[william-james|James]]'s "[[empiricism|radical empiricism]]" went beyond classical empiricism in three crucial respects:

1. **Relations are experienced.** Classical empiricism treated relations (causality, resemblance, spatial and temporal connection) as mental additions to sensory data. [[william-james|James]] argued that relations are directly experienced — "conjunctive relations" are as [[reality|real]] as the terms they connect.

2. **[[experience|Experience]] is a stream, not a sequence.** [[david-hume|Hume]]'s atomistic psychology treated experience as a succession of discrete impressions. [[william-james|James]]'s "stream of consciousness" reconceived experience as a continuous, flowing whole in which past, present, and future interpenetrate.

3. **Consequences are part of experience.** For the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]], the meaning and truth of an idea are not static properties but are constituted by the idea's practical consequences — and those consequences are themselves part of experience.

## Legacy

British Empiricism's most enduring legacy is its methodological commitment: philosophy should begin with experience, not with [[apriorism|a priori]] principles, and should be prepared to revise even its most fundamental assumptions in light of what experience reveals. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] took this commitment further than the classical [[empiricism|empiricist]]s dared — extending it to logic, mathematics, and the norms of [[rationality|rational]]ity themselves.
