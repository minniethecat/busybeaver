## Overview

_Formal Logic: A Scientific and Social Problem_ (1912) is [[fcs-schiller|F.C.S. Schiller]]'s sustained critique of traditional formal logic from the standpoint of [[pragmatism|pragmatic philosophy]]. The work argues that formal logic, by abstracting from meaning, purpose, and the concrete processes of human thought, has systematically misrepresented how thinking actually operates. Schiller contends that logic must be reformed to account for the purposive, personal, and context-dependent character of all [[rationality|reason]]ing — a position that extends the Humanist epistemology developed in his earlier works into the domain of logic.

[[william-james|William James]] references Schiller's _Formal Logic_ in a footnote to Lecture V of _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, citing it as an authority on the nature of logical paradoxes — specifically the difficulty posed by self-referential statements like the liar paradox:

> Not even "I lie," which is meaningless as it stands. _Cf._ Dr. Schiller's _Formal Logic_, p. 373. (005-249--005-250)

## Summary

Schiller's central argument in _Formal Logic_ is that traditional logic has systematically erred by treating words and propositions as if they had fixed meanings independent of the contexts and purposes in which they are used. Formal logic abstracts away from the psychological and social conditions of actual [[rationality|reason]]ing, producing a "juggling with empty forms of words" that bears little resemblance to the living processes of human thought (001-039--001-040).

The book develops a comprehensive critique of the assumptions underlying formal logic — its treatment of identity, its abstraction from meaning, its neglect of the personal and volitional dimensions of judgment — and proposes an alternative that makes "concrete thought" rather than abstract formalism the model for logical inquiry. This aligns with the broader pragmatic insistence that abstractions must be tested by their concrete applications.

Schiller joins forces with Alfred Sidgwick in this critique. As [[william-james|James]] notes, Sidgwick had "long been urging a radical criticism of the procedures of Formal Logic, and shown the gulf between them and the processes of concrete thought" (001-132--001-133). Schiller's _Formal Logic_ develops and systematizes this line of criticism.

## Key Contributions

1. **Pragmatic critique of formal logic**: Schiller demonstrates that formal logic's abstraction from meaning and purpose renders it incapable of modeling actual human [[rationality|reason]]ing. As [[william-james|James]] puts it, "Is [[logic|Logic]] not concerned at all with _meaning_, is it only juggling with empty forms of words?" (001-039--001-040)

2. **The personal dimension of [[rationality|reason]]ing**: All significant assertion takes the form "A is B" — where A and B are different — and this synthetic act of judgment involves an irreducibly personal and purposive element that formal logic cannot capture (003-147--003-149).

3. **Self-referential paradoxes as evidence**: Schiller used logical paradoxes, including the liar paradox ("I lie"), to demonstrate that even the simplest self-referential statements require pragmatic context to be evaluated meaningfully — formal treatment alone cannot determine their truth-value (005-249--005-250).

## Notable Passages

> Not even "I lie," which is meaningless as it stands. _Cf._ Dr. Schiller's _Formal Logic_, p. 373. (005-249--005-250)

> Sidgwick has demonstrated that the belief in formal truth renders Logic incapable of dealing with the processes of concrete thought. (001-132--001-133)

## Reception & Influence

_Formal Logic_ represents the application of pragmatic principles to the philosophy of logic, extending the Humanist epistemology Schiller developed in _[[axioms-as-postulates|Axioms as Postulates]]_ (1902) and _[[studies-in-humanism|Studies in Humanism]]_ (1907). The work's critique of formal logic's abstraction from meaning and purpose has been taken up by subsequent generations of philosophers working in the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition, though Schiller's specific proposals for logical reform were less influential than his critical arguments.
