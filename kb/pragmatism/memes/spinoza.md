## Overview

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) was a Dutch philosopher of Portuguese-Jewish descent whose rationalist metaphysics — identifying God with Nature (Deus sive Natura) and arguing that everything follows necessarily from the divine nature — was simultaneously the most radical and the most systematic philosophy of the seventeenth century. In [[william-james|William James]]'s thought, Spinoza appears as a complex figure: his [[pantheism|pantheistic]] identification of God with the universe anticipates the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] of the idealists that [[william-james|James]] critiques, but his naturalism and his treatment of the mind as part of nature rather than a separate substance also anticipate key pragmatist themes.

## Biographical Timeline

- **1632** — Born in Amsterdam to a family of Portuguese-Jewish immigrants.
- **1656** — Excommunicated from the Amsterdam Jewish community for "abominable heresies."
- **1663** — Published _Principles of Cartesian Philosophy_, his only work published under his own name during his lifetime.
- **1670** — Published _Theological-Political Treatise_ anonymously.
- **1677** — Died in The Hague of tuberculosis. His _Ethics_ was published posthumously.

## Key Contributions

Spinoza's _Ethics_ (1677) presents a complete philosophical system in geometric form, beginning with definitions and axioms and proceeding to demonstrate that there is only one substance (God/Nature), that everything follows necessarily from the divine nature, and that human blessedness consists in the intellectual love of God. This rigorous monism — everything is one, and everything is necessary — is both the supreme achievement of rationalist metaphysics and the clearest statement of the position that [[william-james|James]]'s [[pluralism|pluralism]] opposes.

## Relationship to James's Pragmatism

Spinoza influences [[william-james|James]] in two contrary ways. Negatively, Spinoza's necessitarianism is the ultimate expression of the "block universe" that [[william-james|James]]'s [[free-will|free will]] doctrine and [[meliorism|meliorism]] are designed to escape. Positively, Spinoza's naturalism — his treatment of mind as part of nature rather than a supernatural addition — and his focus on the practical, therapeutic goal of philosophy (achieving blessedness through understanding) resonate with pragmatist commitments.
