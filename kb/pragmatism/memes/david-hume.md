## Overview

David Hume is, in [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, the pivotal antagonist — the philosopher whose analysis of mind and experience created the impasse from which pragmatism offers the only genuine escape. His atomistic psychology, which treated all mental contents as discrete, self-contained sensations, "reigned absolutely in philosophy" until [[william-james|James]] published his _[[principles-of-psychology|Principles of Psychology]]_ in 1890 (002-002--002-003). [[william-james|James]] treats Hume not as a thinker to be refuted in detail but as the architect of a faulty starting point: a sensationalist framework that, by reducing experience to psychical atoms, made the unity of knowledge, the continuity of the self, and [[reality|the real]]ity of causation into insoluble problems. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] response is not to argue against Hume but to **invert the problem** — to recognize that experience is not a mosaic of disconnected sensations but an initially continuous flux (002-137--002-143). Hume thus serves in the lectures as both the founding figure of modern empiricism and the paradigmatic example of what goes wrong when philosophy starts from abstractions rather than from lived experience.

## Life

- **1711** — Born in Edinburgh, Scotland. Studied at the University of Edinburgh from age 12.
- **1734–1737** — Wrote _A Treatise of Human Nature_ during a period of intense private study in France.
- **1739–1740** — Published the _Treatise_ anonymously in London; it "fell dead-born from the press" by his own account.
- **1748** — Published _An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding_, a more accessible recasting of Book I of the _Treatise_.
- **1751** — Published _An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals_.
- **1754–1762** — Published the multi-volume _History of England_, which brought him fame and financial security.
- **1763–1766** — Served as private secretary to the British ambassador in Paris; became a celebrated figure in French intellectual circles.
- **1776** — Died in Edinburgh at age 65. His _Dialogues Concerning Natural [[religion|Religion]]_ were published posthumously in 1779.

## Key Ideas

**Mental atomism.** Hume treated the contents of the mind as discrete, self-contained units — impressions and their paler copies, ideas — modeled on the atomistic physics of his day:

> Just as the physicist explains bodies as collections of discrete particles, so Hume reduced all the contents of the mind to a number of elementary sensations. Whether the mind was reflecting on its own internal ideas, or whether it was undergoing impressions which it supposed to come from an external source, all that was really happening was a succession of detached sensations. (002-017--002-022)

**The denial of necessary connection.** Hume's atomism made causation unintelligible — if every sensation was a separate existence, no necessary bond could link them:

> Moreover, Hume's analysis made havoc of all idea, of 'causation.' If every sensation was a separate being, how was it to be connected with any other in any regular or necessary connection? Two events related as 'cause' and 'effect' must be a myth. (002-045--002-049)

**The dissolving self.** For Hume, the self was nothing more than a bundle of perceptions with no underlying substance — a conclusion he accepted with unflinching honesty:

> The idea of a continuous self was fantastic. There was nothing beneath the ideas to connect them. The notion of causal connection was equally chimerical. Each sensation was distinct and existed in its own right. It could therefore occur alone. (002-058--002-062)

**Sceptical consequences fully acknowledged.** Hume did not shrink from the destructive implications of his own analysis, though he offered no remedy:

> These subversive consequences of his theory Hume did not conceal, though he did not push his mental 'atomism' to its logical extreme. (002-050--002-052)

**The task left to posterity.** Hume's legacy was an unsolved problem — he had dismantled the soul but could not reassemble it:

> Hume, then, seemed to leave to his successors in philosophy a task of synthesis. He had tumbled the soul off her high watch-tower, but how to combine her shattered fragments again into a working unity he declined to say. He saw the sceptical implications of his analysis, but professed himself unable to suggest a remedy. (002-067--002-071)

**Hume's criticism of dogmatism.** In the opening lecture, [[william-james|James]] (via Schiller) recognizes Hume as the philosopher who first exposed the pretensions of dogmatic [[rationality|rational]]ism:

> Elsewhere Dr. Schiller has commented on the controversies raised by Hume's criticism of dogmatism. He has shown that Kant failed to answer Hume because he accepted Hume's psychology, and that no _a priori_ philosophers have since been able to devise any consistent and tenable doctrine. (001-124--001-128)

**The great crux is illusory.** [[william-james|James]]'s decisive diagnosis is that Hume's problems are not [[reality|real]] puzzles but artifacts of a mistaken starting point — experience is not broken and does not need to be pieced together:

> The great crux in Hume is thus seen to be illusory. Immediate experience does not require 'synthesis': it calls for 'analysis.' It is not a jigsaw puzzle, to be pieced together without glue: it is a confused whole which has to be divided and set in order for clear thinking. (002-155--002-159)

**Hume's mistake: starting from partially analyzed experience.** The root error was to treat the analyzed products of [[common-sense]] reflection — discrete "sensations" — as if they were the raw data of experience:

> Hume's mistake was to have started from experience _as partly analysed_ by common sense, and not from the flux _as given_. His 'sensations' were the qualities already analysed out of the flux; he took these selections for the whole and neglected the other less obvious features in it — viz., the relations which floated them. (002-159--002-164)

**Facts do not appear as causally connected.** Even Hume's [[empiricism|empiricist]] successors could not salvage the idea that experience directly delivers causal laws:

> But this theory had really been exploded in advance by Hume. Facts do not _appear_ as causally connected, nor, if they did, would this guarantee that they will continue to do so in the future. (003-037--003-039)

## Relation to Pragmatism

Hume stands in [[william-james|James]]'s lectures as the **unwitting catalyst for pragmatism**. His relationship to the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] movement is paradoxical: he is the thinker whose analysis created the very impasse that pragmatism resolves, yet his unflinching honesty in pursuing his assumptions to their sceptical conclusion makes him, in a sense, more useful to pragmatism than the [[apriorism|apriorist]]s who tried to paper over the cracks.

[[william-james|James]]'s treatment is critical but not dismissive. Hume "had been the first to discover that we are in the habit of trying to [[rationality|rational]]ize our [[sensation|sense-data]] by putting ideal constructions upon them" (002-108--002-110) — a discovery that pragmatism radicalizes by insisting that this constructive activity is not a distortion of truth but its essence. Where Hume saw the mind's tendency to add connections to sensations as an "outrage" (002-115) that falsified the data, pragmatism recognizes it as the normal operation of intelligence in the service of human purposes.

The escape from Hume's problems is not through the "vain complications of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s transcendental machinery" (002-090--002-091) but through [[william-james|James]]'s inversion: experience does not start as disconnected atoms requiring synthesis; it starts as a flowing continuum calling for analysis. "All Hume's problems," [[william-james|James]] concludes, "are un[[reality|real]], and those of his [[apriorism|apriorist]] critics are doubly removed from [[reality|real]]ity" (002-177--002-178).

The constructive lesson [[william-james|James]] draws from Hume's failure is that all analysis is selective and interest-driven. "Hume had been convicted of error in selecting those elements of the flux which served his purpose and neglecting the rest. But this mistake might reveal the important fact that all analysis was a choice, and inspired by volitions" (002-194--002-197). What Hume did unconsciously and restrictively, pragmatism does consciously and liberally — it embraces the volitional, selective character of all thinking and extends it from mere selection of data to the active postulation of [[hypothesis|hypotheses]].

In sum, Hume is for pragmatism the necessary precursor: the thinker who took empiricism to its logical breaking point and thereby demonstrated that philosophy needed an entirely new starting point — one that begins, not with atomic sensations, but with the continuous stream of lived experience and the practical interests that shape it.
