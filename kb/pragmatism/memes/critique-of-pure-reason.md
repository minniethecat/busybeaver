## Overview

The _Critique of Pure Reason_ (_Kritik der reinen Vernunft_, 1781; second edition 1787) is [[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]]'s epochal investigation into the nature and limits of human knowledge. [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] sought to resolve the impasse between [[rationality|rational]]ism (which claimed [[rationality|reason]] alone could grasp [[reality|real]]ity) and empiricism (which traced all knowledge to sensory experience) by arguing that the mind actively structures experience through innate categories — space, time, causality — that make knowledge possible but also limit it to [[reality|the real]]m of possible experience.

For the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] tradition, [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s _Critique_ is both ancestor and adversary. [[william-james|James]] acknowledged [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s transformative role in哲学 while rejecting his central claim that the structures of thought are fixed, universal, and knowable [[apriorism|a priori]]. [[pragmatism|Pragmatism]] inverts the [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] project: instead of deducing the necessary conditions of possible experience, it traces the practical consequences of ideas in actual experience.

## Summary

The _Critique_ is organized around [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s "Copernican R[[darwinism|evolution]]" in philosophy: rather than assuming that knowledge must conform to objects, [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] proposed that objects must conform to our mode of cognition. The mind imposes forms of intuition (space and time) and categories of understanding (causality, substance, etc.) on the raw data of sensation, producing the structured world of experience.

This synthesis yields [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s crucial distinction between **phenomena** (things as they appear to us, shaped by our cognitive apparatus) and **noumena** (things as they are in themselves, inaccessible to knowledge). All theoretical knowledge is limited to the phenomenal [[reality|real]]m; claims about God, freedom, and immortality — while [[rationality|rational]]ly defensible as "[[postulation|postulates]] of practical [[rationality|reason]]" — cannot be objects of theoretical knowledge.

## Key Contributions

The _Critique_ established the framework within which nineteenth-century philosophy — including the traditions pragmatism rebelled against — operated. Its key contributions include:

- The transcendental method: deducing the necessary conditions of possible experience
- The distinction between analytic and synthetic judgments, and the discovery of the synthetic [[apriorism|a priori]]
- The limitation of theoretical [[rationality|reason]] to make room for practical [[religious-experience|faith]]
- The systematic critique of traditional metaphysics (the paralogisms, antinomies, and ideal of pure [[rationality|reason]])

[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]'s relationship to [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] is complex. [[william-james|James]] praised [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] for "introducing the human mind as an active contributor to knowledge" but rejected [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s claim that the mind's contribution is fixed and universal: "The categories," [[william-james|James]] wrote in effect, "are habits of thought shaped by [[darwinism|evolution]] and experience — not [[apriorism|a priori]] furniture of the transcendental ego."

## Notable Passages

> The pragmatist critique of [[intellectualism]] has its deepest roots in Kant's demonstration that pure reason, operating without the constraint of possible experience, inevitably falls into contradiction. James built on this insight while replacing Kant's transcendental apparatus with an evolutionary and psychological account of the mind's active role in knowledge. (001-130--001-132)

## Reception & Influence

The _Critique of Pure Reason_ transformed Western philosophy. [[german-idealism|German Idealism]] (Fichte, Schelling, Hegel) developed in its wake; nineteenth-century neo-[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]]ism dominated German academic philosophy; and twentieth-century analytic philosophy took [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s questions about the conditions of meaningful discourse as its starting point.

For pragmatism, [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] served as a crucial point of departure. His insight that the mind actively structures experience was retained; his claim that the structure is fixed and knowable [[apriorism|a priori]] was rejected. [[john-dewey|Dewey]], in particular, developed a "Copernican R[[darwinism|evolution]]" of his own: the categories are not transcendental conditions imposed by a timeless ego but tools developed by organisms coping with their environment — and, like all tools, subject to revision when they fail to work.
