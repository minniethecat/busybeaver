## Definition

In [[william-james|James]]'s _[[pragmatism|Pragmatism]]_, rationality is reconceived as a practical, purposive activity rather than a passive reflection of an antecedent rational order. The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] view holds that the mind does not merely discover rational structure in the world — it _[[postulation|postulates]]_ it, acts upon those [[postulation|postulates]], and validates or revises them through their consequences in experience (003-055). Rationality is thus a tool of life, not a transcendent faculty: "thought is not a wholly intellectual process" (005-109).

## Origin & Context

The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] account of rationality emerges from [[william-james|James]]'s critique of both [[david-hume|Hume]]an empiricism and [[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] apriorism. [[david-hume|Hume]] had discovered that "we are in the habit of trying to rationalize our [[sensation|sense-data]] by putting ideal constructions upon them" (002-109), but he regarded this as a falsification of the given. [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] legitimated the mind's constructive activity through transcendental categories, but could not explain why the chaotic matter of sensation should submit to the mind's forming (002-095). [[william-james|James]]'s innovation was to reconceive this mental contribution in biological terms: the mind's organization is essentially [[darwinism|Darwinian]], evolving through the survival of fittest variations in response to environmental demands (002-226).

> The first to discover that we are in the habit of trying to rationalize our sense-data by putting ideal constructions upon them, though he had abstained from sanctifying the practice by a hideous jargon of technical terminology. But this way of eking out the facts only seemed to him to _falsify_ them. (002-109)

## Key Arguments

1. **Rationality as [[postulation|Postulation]], Not Discovery**: The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] view rejects both empiricism (which derives all principles from experience) and apriorism (which treats them as pre-existing necessities of thought). Instead, principles are _[[postulation|postulates]]_ — "proceeding from us, and meant to guide thought in anticipating facts," yet always "willing to submit to verification in the course of fresh experience" (003-058). Unlike _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ truths, [[postulation|postulates]] depend upon the will and are "the product of a free choice" (003-077). Their truth is not guaranteed in advance but earned through consequences.

> For though a postulate proceeds from us, and is meant to guide thought in anticipating facts, it yet allows the facts to test and mould it; so that its working modifies, expands, or restricts its demands, and fits it to meet the exigencies of experience. (003-058)

2. **Rationality Starts from Doubt, Not Certainty**: [[william-james|James]] insists that "thinking starts from perplexities" and not from self-evident foundations (005-030). It is "psychologically impossible for a rational mind to assert what it knows to be true, and supposes everyone else admits the truth of" (005-037). [[reason|Reason]]ing is always motivated by a specific problem or dispute — "men do not reason about what they think they know for certain, but about matters in dispute" (006-168). This situates rationality in the service of practical life rather than abstract contemplation.

> It is psychologically impossible for a rational mind to assert what it knows to be true, and supposes everyone else to admit the truth of. This is why even a philosopher's conversation does not consist of a rehearsal of all the unchallenged truisms that he can remember. (005-037)

3. **The Selectivity of All [[reason|Reason]]ing**: Every act of reasoning involves selection — "the purpose of a reasoner, who _selects_ what he requires from the totality of 'truths'" (006-085). Formal logic's attempt to abstract from this selective activity renders it unable to explain or justify any actual inference. Intellectualist logic "deigns to recognize" none of the features that make [[reality|real]] thought possible: motive, value, meaning, purpose, context, and relevance (006-201). Real reasoning from facts can never be a "formally valid process" (006-192).

> A connection of thoughts cannot be conceived or understood without reference to the purpose of a reasoner, who _selects_ what he requires from the totality of 'truths.' If, then, 'Logic' has merely to contemplate this eternal and immutable system of truth in its integrity, and forbids all selection from it for a merely human purpose, how can it either justify, or even understand, the drawing of any inference whatever? (006-085)

4. **Rationality as Intellectual Satisfaction**: The outcome of rational activity is not a morbid delight in dialectical cleverness but "the satisfaction which rewards the hard labour of rationalizing experience and rendering it more conformable with human desires" (005-185). This connects rationality to the broader pragmatic thesis that truth is a value: intellectual satisfaction is not an end in itself but a sign that thought has successfully organized experience for human purposes.

> 'Intellectual satisfaction' is not a morbid delight in dialectical and verbal juggling: it is the satisfaction which rewards the hard labour of rationalizing experience and rendering it more conformable with human desires. (005-183)

5. **Life as Rational Pursuit of Ends**: At the level of [[humanism|Humanism]] (Schiller), rationality characterizes life itself: "the best definition of life is one which displays it as throughout purposive, as a rational pursuit of ends" (008-035). Thought is not a self-enclosed activity but must be placed "in its context among the other functions of life" and its value measured "by its effect upon them" (008-003). Even the most conscientious rationalists cannot escape the selectivity inherent in all thinking — they "show very little anxiety to include in their intellectual scheme a knowledge of their opponents' opinions" (008-091).

> To the Humanist the best definition of life is one which displays it as throughout purposive, as a rational pursuit of ends. This raises the question of the validity of valuations. (008-035)

## Variants & Interpretations

- **[[immanuel-kant|Kantian]] Rationality**: The mind imposes _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ categories (substance, cause) upon the chaotic manifold of sense, producing the connected world of common sense and science (002-079). [[william-james|James]] critiques this for failing to explain _why_ sensations submit to mental forming — a gulf yawns "which [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s laboured explanations nowhere bridge" (002-093).

- **[[david-hume|Hume]]an Skeptical Rationality**: [[david-hume|Hume]] acknowledged that "we are in the habit of trying to rationalize our [[sensation|sense-data]]" (002-109) but regarded this as a falsification of experience — a charge [[william-james|James]] turns against [[david-hume|Hume]] by showing that the very "sensations" [[david-hume|Hume]] takes as given are already products of selective analysis (002-159).

- **Pragmatic/[[postulation|Postulation]]al Rationality**: The [[william-james|James]]ian alternative treats rational principles as [[postulation|postulates]] — freely chosen assumptions that guide inquiry but remain subject to verification and revision through their practical consequences (003-058). This renders rationality fallible, experimental, and intrinsically connected to human purposes.

- **Instrumentalist Rationality ([[john-dewey|Dewey]])**: [[john-dewey|John Dewey]] developed the view that reason is a tool for solving problems — reflective thought arises when habitual action is blocked, and its function is to reconstruct the situation so action can resume.

## Criticisms & Debates

Critics charge that pragmatism reduces rationality to mere utility, eliminating any distinction between what is rational and what is merely convenient. If the standard of reason is "what works," does this not license wishful thinking and self-deception? [[william-james|James]] anticipates this objection: "it does not mean that we are free to assert the truth of every idea which seems to us pretty or pleasant" (005-189). Postulates "have to work in a world of fact, and to adapt themselves to all facts" (005-205).

A deeper objection concerns the status of logical necessity. If rational principles are [[postulation|postulates]] rather than _[[apriorism|a priori]]_ truths, in what sense can reasoning compel assent? The [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] replies that [[postulation|postulates]] earn their necessity through their success — "a long and successful course of service raises a [[postulation|postulate]] to the dignity of an 'axiom'" (003-073). Compulsion is practical, not metaphysical: we are compelled because abandoning the [[postulation|postulate]] would cripple our ability to navigate the world.

Intellectualists object that pragmatism cannot account for the self-correcting character of reason — how we recognize our own errors. [[william-james|James]] replies that the [[pragmatism|pragmatic method]] "shows us how we may correct our errors, and so gives reasons for our [[religious-experience|faith]] in the possibility of Progress" (008-136). Error correction is built into the process of testing [[postulation|postulates]] against consequences, rather than requiring an appeal to an infallible standard.
