## Overview

Henry Ryecroft's death occurs on a summer evening, after "little more than a lustrum" of quiet retirement in Devon. He dies suddenly of heart disease, passing away on the sofa of his study in a manner that reflects the peace he had found in his final years.

## Details

Ryecroft had been suffering from a heart condition of which he was unaware. The disease had been progressing silently throughout his retirement. On the day of his death, he had taken "a long walk in very hot weather." Returning home, he lay down upon the sofa in his study and died in his sleep.

Gissing's account of the death is notably serene: Ryecroft "passed from slumber into the great silence," with his face described as calm. This peaceful death fulfills Ryecroft's own wish — "it had always been his wish to die suddenly; he dreaded the thought of illness, chiefly because of the trouble it gave to others."

The death occurred during summer, after approximately five years ("a lustrum") of retirement. Ryecroft was in his mid-fifties at the time of his death.

## Significance

Ryecroft's death is the event that brings the Private Papers into existence. Without his death, Gissing would never have examined his papers, never decided to print "this little volume." The death is also thematically significant: Ryecroft dies in a state of contentment, his face calm, suggesting that his final years had brought him the peace that a lifetime of struggle had denied him.

## Key Passages

> On a summer evening, after a long walk in very hot weather, he lay down upon the sofa in his study, and there — as his calm face declared — passed from slumber into the great silence.
