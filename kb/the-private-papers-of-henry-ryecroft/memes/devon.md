## Overview

Devon is the English county where Henry Ryecroft spent the final and happiest years of his life. After receiving an unexpected annuity of three hundred pounds, Ryecroft "quitted the London suburb where of late he had been living, and, turning to the part of England which he loved best, he presently established himself in a cottage near Exeter" (Preface). The Devon landscape — its green valleys, pine-clad ridges, and mild climate — provided not merely a backdrop but the essential condition for Ryecroft's late-flowering contentment and the composition of the private papers themselves.

## First Encounter: The Awakening

Ryecroft's first visit to Devon, before his permanent settlement, was a transformative experience. Escaping London on an "irresistible impulse" at the end of March, he found himself in a landscape that seemed supernatural after years of urban confinement:

> On an irresistible impulse, I suddenly made up my mind to go into Devon, a part of England I had never seen. At the end of March I escaped from my grim lodgings, and, before I had time to reflect on the details of my undertaking, I found myself sitting in sunshine at a spot very near to where I now dwell — before me the green valley of the broadening Exe and the pine-clad ridge of Haldon. That was one of the moments of my life when I have tasted exquisite joy. (Spring-IX)

The experience was an awakening of senses long dormant. Ryecroft, who had "walked in darkness, yet knew it not," suddenly became aware of plants and flowers, of sun and sky. He gathered specimens from the hedgerows, promising himself to identify them all — a botanical interest that persisted for the remainder of his life. "To me the flowers became symbolical of a great release, of a wonderful awakening" (Spring-IX).

## Climate and Seasons

Devon's mild climate was a recurring source of gratitude. Ryecroft contrasted it sharply with the "long deferment of the calendar's promise, that weeping gloom of March and April, that bitter blast outraging the honour of May" that characterised other parts of England:

> The early coming of spring in this happy Devon gladdens my heart. I think with chill discomfort of those parts of England where the primrose shivers beneath a sky of threat rather than of solace. (Spring-VIII)

The county's gentleness allowed Ryecroft — a self-described "sun-worshipper" — to live in closer harmony with the procession of the seasons, a theme that became central to the structure of the private papers themselves.

## Home as Sacred Space

Devon transformed Ryecroft's relationship to place. After decades of rented rooms and transient lodgings, ownership of a cottage in the Devon countryside awakened something profound:

> When one is at home, how one's affections grow about everything in the neighbourhood! I always thought with fondness of this corner of Devon, but what was that compared with the love which now strengthens in me day by day! Beginning with my house, every stick and stone of it is dear to me as my heart's blood; I find myself laying an affectionate hand on the doorpost, giving a pat, as I go by, to the garden gate. (Autumn-II)

This attachment extended to the surrounding villages — Heavitree, Brampford Speke, Newton St. Cyres — whose names became "delightful to his ear." Ryecroft read the local Exeter paper, learned every road and lane, and came to feel that "the very clouds that pass above my house are more interesting and beautiful than clouds elsewhere" (Autumn-II).

## The Landscape as Character

Ryecroft's descriptions of Devon are precise and affectionate. The valley of the Exe, the pine-clad ridge of Haldon, the "rich, warm valleys, by orchards bursting into bloom," the "pine-clad heights, gazing over moorland brown with last year's heather" — these are rendered with the care of a man for whom landscape had become a form of spiritual sustenance. On his daily rambles, he could walk thirty miles without fatigue, sustained by "the golden beams" of the sun and the "intoxicating fragrance" of the earth.
