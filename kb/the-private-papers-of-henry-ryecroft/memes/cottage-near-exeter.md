## Overview

The cottage near Exeter is Henry Ryecroft's retirement home in Devon, where he lived for the final five years of his life after receiving an unexpected annuity. It serves as the setting from which all of Ryecroft's journal reflections are written.

## Description

The cottage is described as a "plain little house amid its half-wild garden," situated with a "fine view across the valley of the Exe to Haldon." It contains a "cosy book-room" where Ryecroft does his reading and writing. The surrounding landscape features "lanes and meadows" suitable for rambling walks.

The location is in Devon, in the southwestern English countryside near Exeter — "the part of England which he loved best." Ryecroft moved there after leaving "the London suburb where of late he had been living."

## Ryecroft's Connection

This cottage represents the fulfillment of Ryecroft's long-deferred hopes. After decades of poverty and hackwork in London, the cottage provides the "tranquillity of mind and condition" he had never dared to expect. His friends who visited "will not forget" the hospitality there — "the host's cordial, gleeful hospitality, rambles with him in lanes and meadows, long talks amid the stillness of the rural night."

It is from this cottage that Ryecroft writes his seasonal journal entries, observing the changing landscape through the windows of his book-room and during his walks. The cottage is also where he dies, on a summer evening, lying down on his study sofa.
