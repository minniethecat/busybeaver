## Overview

Retirement from literary labour and the discovery of true leisure is the central theme of *The Private Papers of Henry Ryecroft*. After twenty years of hackwork in London, Ryecroft's unexpected annuity allows him to experience, for the first time, a life free from the compulsion to earn a living — and to reflect on what that freedom means.

## Ryecroft's View

Ryecroft's retirement is not earned but bestowed — a legacy from "an acquaintance, more his friend than he imagined." The sudden liberation from toil at age fifty gives him "a period of such tranquillity of mind and condition as he had never dared to hope." For Ryecroft, who had "never incurred debt" and feared dependence above all else, the annuity of three hundred pounds a year represents "something more than a competency."

Throughout his journal, Ryecroft reflects on the contrast between his past life of grinding literary labour and his present freedom to read, walk, and think without commercial pressure. He "bade farewell to authorship" upon leaving London, telling his friend Gissing that "he hoped never to write another line for publication." Yet the journal itself represents an intermediate form of writing — private, unprompted, "merely for his own satisfaction."

The five years of retirement ("little more than a lustrum") are cut short by heart disease. Ryecroft's contentment in his final years is framed as a hard-won peace: the reward not of ambition fulfilled but of ambition transcended.

## Key Passages

> At the age of fifty, just when his health had begun to fail and his energies to show abatement, Ryecroft had the rare good fortune to find himself suddenly released from toil, and to enter upon a period of such tranquillity of mind and condition as he had never dared to hope.

> When he left London, Ryecroft bade farewell to authorship. He told me that he hoped never to write another line for publication.
