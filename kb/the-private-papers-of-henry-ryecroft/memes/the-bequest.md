## Overview

The bequest of a life annuity of three hundred pounds is the pivotal event in Henry Ryecroft's life. Received at the age of fifty from a deceased acquaintance, it liberates Ryecroft from a lifetime of literary poverty and enables his retreat to rural Devon.

## Details

The bequest came from "an acquaintance, more his friend than he imagined," who died and left Ryecroft a life annuity. The annual sum was three hundred pounds. Ryecroft, a widower with a married daughter and no dependents, recognized this as "something more than a competency" — enough to live on without working.

The event occurred when Ryecroft "had reached his fortieth year" — actually at fifty, when "his health had begun to fail and his energies to show abatement." The bequest came as a complete surprise: Ryecroft "learnt with astonishment" of his good fortune.

Upon receiving the annuity, Ryecroft acted quickly: "In a few weeks he quitted the London suburb where of late he had been living" and moved to Devon.

## Significance

The bequest is the hinge on which Ryecroft's entire narrative turns. Without it, there would be no retirement, no cottage in Devon, and no Private Papers. The event transforms Ryecroft's material circumstances overnight, but it also precipitates a deeper transformation: the shift from a life defined by labour to one defined by reflection.

The fact that the bequest was unearned (not the fruit of Ryecroft's own literary success) is thematically important. It arrives as grace rather than reward, allowing Ryecroft to experience a contentment he had long ceased to hope for.

## Key Passages

> On the death of an acquaintance, more his friend than he imagined, the wayworn man of letters learnt with astonishment that there was bequeathed to him a life annuity of three hundred pounds.
