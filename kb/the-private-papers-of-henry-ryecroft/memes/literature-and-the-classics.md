## Overview

Literature — and classical literature in particular — was the central passion of Henry Ryecroft's intellectual life. From the Homer and Shakespeare that lay upon his filthy deal table in the Tottenham Court Road cellar to the Gibbon, Pausanias, and Dahn that arrived in brown-paper parcels at his Devon cottage, books were not merely possessions but companions, memory-triggers, and the primary medium through which Ryecroft understood his own existence.

## The Scent of Books

Ryecroft's relationship to literature was intensely physical. He "knew every book of mine by its *scent*," and the act of putting his nose between the pages could summon entire epochs of his past:

> My Gibbon, for example, my well-bound eight-volume Milman edition, which I have read and read and read again for more than thirty years — never do I open it but the scent of the noble page restores to me all the exultant happiness of that moment when I received it as a prize. (Summer-XXVII)

The inherited Cambridge Shakespeare carried the odour of his father's bookcase and childhood; the Globe Shakespeare, purchased as an extravagance during lean years, was "regarded with that peculiar affection which results from sacrifice" (Summer-XXVII). For Ryecroft, a library copy could never substitute for a book that was one's own.

## Sacrificial Purchases

The acquisition of books during Ryecroft's years of poverty often meant choosing between intellectual and bodily nourishment. His purchase of Heyne's *Tibullus* for sixpence — all the money he possessed in the world — became emblematic:

> Sixpence was all I had — yes, all I had in the world; it would purchase a plate of meat and vegetables. But I did not dare to hope that the Tibullus would wait until the morrow, when a certain small sum fell due to me. I paced the pavement, fingering the coppers in my pocket, eyeing the stall, two appetites at combat within me. The book was bought and I went home with it, and as I made a dinner of bread and butter I gloated over the pages. (Summer-XXVII)

Inside the volume, a pencilled inscription — "Perlegi, Oct. 4, 1792" — connected Ryecroft across a century to another unknown scholar who had presumably made a similar sacrifice. "I like to imagine some poor scholar, poor and eager as I myself, who bought the volume with drops of his blood" (Summer-XXVII).

## The Classical World

Ryecroft's reading ranged across Greek and Roman literature. He could "think that I can read Homer, and, assuredly, if any man enjoys him, it is I." Yet he was acutely conscious of the gulf that translation could never quite bridge:

> Can I for a moment dream that Homer yields me all his music, that his word is to me as to him who walked by the Hellenic shore when Hellas lived? I know that there reaches me across the vast of time no more than a faint and broken echo. (Summer-XXVII)

His classical reading extended to Appuleius and Lucian, Petronius and the Greek Anthology, Diogenes Laertius — pursued during the years when he "was literally starving in London" yet settled himself at the British Museum "reading as disinterestedly as if I had been without a care" (Autumn-XVII).

## The Scholar Manqué

Ryecroft believed he had "in me the making of a scholar" — a life that would have suited him far better than the hackwork by which he earned his bread. The sentence from the *Imitatio Christi* that he took most to heart captured this ideal: "*In omnibus requiem quaesivi, et nusquam inveni nisi in angulo cum libro*" — "In all things I have sought rest, and nowhere have I found it save in a corner with a book" (Autumn-XVII).

Michelet's confession — "*J'ai passé à côté du monde, et j'ai pris l'histoire pour la vie*" (I passed beside the world, and I took history for life) — Ryecroft recognised as his own true ideal. Through all his "battlings and miseries," he had "always lived more in the past than in the present."

## Reading for Its Own Sake

The liberation of retirement brought a fundamental reorientation in Ryecroft's relationship to books. No longer did he read with a notebook at hand, mining texts for "apt quotation in something I might write — one of the evil results of a literary life" (Summer-XXI). Now he could read "for your own pleasure, for your solace and strengthening." When he caught himself asking to what end he read and remembered, he recognised the question as "as foolish a question as ever man put to himself" (Summer-XXI).
