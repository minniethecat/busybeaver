## Overview

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) figures in Henry Ryecroft's private papers as the single most important literary presence in his life. Across four decades of poverty-stricken London garrets and tranquil Devon retirement, Shakespeare's works remained Ryecroft's constant companion — the inherited Cambridge edition from his father, the Globe volume purchased as an extravagance in lean years, and *The Tempest* read and re-read in the quiet of his cottage. Ryecroft's reflections on Shakespeare encompass not only literary judgment but deeply personal associations of scent, memory, and national identity.

## Ryecroft's Shakespeare: A Personal Possession

Ryecroft's most intimate connection to Shakespeare was forged through physical books — objects imbued with memory and scent. The great Cambridge Shakespeare, inherited from his father, carried an odour that transported him back to childhood:

> For these volumes belonged to my father, and before I was old enough to read them with understanding, it was often permitted me, as a treat, to take down one of them from the bookcase, and reverently to turn the leaves. The volumes smell exactly as they did in that old time, and what a strange tenderness comes upon me when I hold one of them in hand. (Summer-XXVII)

For daily reading, Ryecroft preferred his Globe edition — a volume whose purchase had once been a sacrifice, endowing it with "that peculiar affection which results from sacrifice" (Summer-XXVII). This material relationship to books — their scent, their provenance, their physical history — is central to Ryecroft's experience of literature.

## In the Garret: Homer and Shakespeare

During the years of deepest poverty, when Ryecroft inhabited a stone-floored front cellar off Tottenham Court Road, Shakespeare was among the few books that lay upon his filthy deal table:

> Here I lived; here I wrote. Yes, "literary work" was done at that filthy deal table, on which, by the by, lay my Homer, my Shakespeare, and the few other books I then possessed. (Spring-X)

That Shakespeare and Homer were the two books named — the two that survived every privation — speaks to their foundational importance. They represent the twin pillars of Ryecroft's intellectual life: classical antiquity and English literature.

## On Art and Genius

In a sustained meditation on the nature of artistic creation, Ryecroft deployed Shakespeare as the ultimate counterexample to the Victorian cult of artistic conscientiousness. Against the school of Flaubert — with its emphasis on deliberate craft and elaborate preparation — Ryecroft set the example of Shakespeare's apparent carelessness:

> Why, after all, has one not heard that a certain William Shakespeare turned out his so-called works of art with something like criminal carelessness? (Summer-XXI)

The point, for Ryecroft, is that supreme art flows not from method but from what he called "the zest of life" — an overflowing vitality that simply is, and cannot be manufactured by taking thought. Shakespeare, Cervantes, and Scott are invoked together as "sinners against Art" who "are none the less among the world's supreme artists" (Summer-XXI).

## *The Tempest* and the Final Vision

Ryecroft reserved his highest praise for *The Tempest*, which he read on a summer day in Devon and declared "perhaps the play that I love best." He believed it to be Shakespeare's last work, written in retirement at Stratford — a circumstance that must have resonated deeply with a man who had himself withdrawn from the literary world. His appreciation operates on multiple levels:

**Linguistic mastery**: "For a man whose life's business it has been to study the English tongue, what joy can equal that of marking the happy ease wherewith Shakespeare surpasses, in mere command of words, every achievement of those even who, apart from him, are great?" (Summer-XXVII)

**Creative sport**: "He seems to sport with language, to amuse himself with new discovery of its resources" (Summer-XXVII). This image of the aged poet at play, no longer striving but joyfully creating, mirrors Ryecroft's own ideal of a creative life lived for its own satisfaction.

**The Caliban passages**: Ryecroft was especially struck by the language Shakespeare invented for Caliban — words that "smack of the moist and spawning earth, of the life of creatures that cannot rise above the soil" (Summer-XXVII).

## Shakespeare and England

The essay culminates in an extraordinary passage that fuses literary appreciation with national sentiment. For Ryecroft, Shakespeare and England are inseparable:

> Let every land have joy of its poet; for the poet is the land itself, all its greatness and its sweetness, all that incommunicable heritage for which men live and die. As I close the book, love and reverence possess me. Whether does my full heart turn to the great Enchanter, or to the Island upon which he has laid his spell? I know not. I cannot think of them apart. In the love and reverence awakened by that voice of voices, Shakespeare and England are but one. (Summer-XXVII)

This is not jingoism but the natural culmination of a life spent with the English language. Ryecroft, who read Greek and Latin, who admired Goethe and the French, nevertheless acknowledged that reading Shakespeare in his mother tongue was an irreplaceable gift — a communion impossible through translation.

## Sunday Reading

A childhood habit, reinforced across a lifetime, established Sunday as the day for Shakespeare, Homer, Virgil, and Milton. In Ryecroft's Devon cottage, with no interruptions but the note of a linnet or the humming of a bee, these reading sessions acquired a quasi-religious character:

> I open the volume somewhat formally; is it not sacred, if the word have any meaning at all? (Summer-VI)
