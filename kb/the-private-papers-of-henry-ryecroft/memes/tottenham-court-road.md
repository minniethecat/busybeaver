## Overview

Tottenham Court Road and its surrounding alleys represent the geography of Henry Ryecroft's deepest poverty. Here, in a stone-floored front cellar costing four-and-sixpence a week, Ryecroft wrote his earliest literary work with Homer and Shakespeare upon a filthy deal table. Despite the squalor, his recollections of this district are marked not by bitterness but by a curious warmth — the memory of youthful endurance and fierce hope.

## The Front Cellar

Ryecroft's most vividly remembered lodging was an alley hidden on the west side of Tottenham Court Road, where financial necessity drove him from a back bedroom to an even cheaper front cellar:

> The front cellar was stone-floored; its furniture was a table, a chair, a washstand, and a bed; the window, which of course had never been cleaned since it was put in, received light through a flat grating in the alley above. Here I lived; here I wrote. Yes, "literary work" was done at that filthy deal table, on which, by the by, lay my Homer, my Shakespeare, and the few other books I then possessed. (Spring-X)

The sound of policemen's boots tramping across the grating above his window was a feature of nightly life — their heavy feet passing on the way to relieve guard. Yet Ryecroft's retrospect is devoid of self-pity. On the contrary, he found a certain romance in these conditions: "I have passed nights of dreamless repose on beds which it would now make my bones ache only to look at" (Spring-X).

## A Garreteer's Geography

Tottenham Court Road sat at the centre of Ryecroft's impoverished London. He recalls "the winding way by which I went from Oxford Street, at the foot of Tottenham Court Road, to Leicester Square," passing a shop with "puddings and pies kept hot by steam rising through perforated metal" — a place where he often stood "raging with hunger, unable to purchase even one pennyworth of food" (Spring-X).

The district was, in Ryecroft's laconic phrase, part of "the dear old horrors." Yet the horror was tempered by the pride of survival. Looking back from the comfort of Devon, he could say:

> Now, owning all the misery of it in comparison with what should have been, I find that part of life interesting and pleasant to look back upon — greatly more so than many subsequent times, when I lived amid decencies and had enough to eat. (Spring-X)

## The Literary *Vie de Bohème*

Ryecroft acknowledged the existence of a literary subculture in the Tottenham Court Road district — "garreteers in the Tottenham Court Road district, or in unredeemed Chelsea; they make their little *vie de Bohème*, and are consciously proud of it" (Spring-VIII). But he himself stood apart. Unlike the typical struggling writer who found companionship in shared hardship, Ryecroft was a determined solitary: "I never belonged to any cluster; I shrank from casual acquaintance, and, through the grim years, had but one friend with whom I held converse" (Spring-VIII).

## The Sixpence

A recurring motif in Ryecroft's memories of this period is the sixpence — a sum that could mean either a meal or a book, but not both. The difference between the back bedroom and the front cellar was "sixpence a week, and sixpence, in those days, was a very great consideration — why, it meant a couple of meals" (Spring-X). Once, finding a sixpence in the street produced "an exultation which is vivid in me at this moment" (Spring-X).
