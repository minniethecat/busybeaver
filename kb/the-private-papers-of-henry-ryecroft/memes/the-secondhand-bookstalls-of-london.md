## Overview

The secondhand bookstalls and bookshops of Victorian London were, for Henry Ryecroft, places of intense drama — sites where intellectual desire and bodily need fought pitched battles over the possession of sixpence. From the stall in Goodge Street where he purchased Heyne's *Tibullus* at the cost of his dinner, to the Holywell Street shop presided over by a former Catholic priest, these establishments were not merely commercial venues but the staging grounds of a life in which books were acquired "with drops of his blood."

## The Goodge Street Stall

The stall in Goodge Street — "a stall where now and then one found an excellent thing among quantities of rubbish" — was the scene of one of Ryecroft's most vivid recollections. The purchase of Heyne's *Tibullus* for sixpence became emblematic of the choices that defined his intellectual life:

> Sixpence was all I had — yes, all I had in the world; it would purchase a plate of meat and vegetables. But I did not dare to hope that the Tibullus would wait until the morrow, when a certain small sum fell due to me. I paced the pavement, fingering the coppers in my pocket, eyeing the stall, two appetites at combat within me. The book was bought and I went home with it, and as I made a dinner of bread and butter I gloated over the pages. (Summer-XXVII)

The material details are precise: the coppers in the pocket, the pavement paced, the stall eyed. Ryecroft presents the episode without melodrama, yet the phrase "two appetites at combat within me" captures the genuine anguish of the moment. A sixpence could not satisfy both hunger and intellectual desire; one had to yield.

## Holywell Street

The Holywell Street bookshop was presided over by "the little grey old man" who had once been a Catholic priest and "still had a certain priestly dignity about him." It was here that Ryecroft encountered Jung-Stilling's autobiography — a volume he resisted buying on two occasions before finally succumbing:

> It caught my eye in Holywell Street; the name was familiar to me in *Wahrheit und Dichtung*, and curiosity grew as I glanced over the pages. But that day I resisted; in truth, I could not afford the eighteen-pence, which means that just then I was poor indeed. (Summer-XXVII)

The bookseller's response when Ryecroft finally made the purchase is unforgettable. Taking the volume, opening it, musing for a moment, the old priest said "as if thinking aloud: 'Yes, I wish I had time to read it'" (Summer-XXVII). The remark, in its gentle melancholy, could have come from Ryecroft himself.

## A Reader's Geography

Ryecroft's London was mapped by its bookshops. The winding way from Oxford Street to Leicester Square passed a shop with steaming puddings in the window, but the important landmarks were the stalls and shops where volumes could be found. The geography of poverty and the geography of book-acquisition were one and the same: the distance between the coffee-shop where a sixpenny dinner could be had and the stall where a sixpenny Tibullus awaited was measured not in yards but in the internal calculus of sacrifice.

## The Physicality of Acquisition

For Ryecroft, buying books secondhand was an experience that engaged all the senses. The unpacking of volumes ordered from catalogues — "The first glimpse of bindings when the inmost protective wrapper has been folded back! The first scent of books! The first gleam of a gilded title!" — was "greater still" than the happiness of browsing stalls. But both experiences shared a common element: the material encounter with the book as object, not merely as text.

Lamb's "ragged veterans" was the phrase Ryecroft used to describe his own collection — volumes that had "passed from the secondhand stall" and bore the marks of their history. The pencilled inscription in his Tibullus — "Perlegi, Oct. 4, 1792" — was not a defacement but a connection across time to another reader who had, perhaps, made a similar sacrifice.
