## Overview

Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee of 1897 — the second Jubilee, following the Golden Jubilee of 1887 — prompted some of Henry Ryecroft's most searching reflections on English national character, the nature of progress, and the price of modernity. Writing from his Devon cottage, Ryecroft observed the distant bonfires on the hills and found himself caught between genuine appreciation of English common sense and a deepening melancholy at what the celebration signified.

## The Bonfires on the Hills

Ryecroft's account opens with a vivid image of the Jubilee beacons, simultaneously rooted in the present and resonant with antiquity:

> It is the second Jubilee. Bonfires blaze upon the hills, making one think of the watchman on Agamemnon's citadel. (It were more germane to the matter to think of Queen Elizabeth and the Armada.) (Summer-XV)

The parenthetical aside is characteristic — an immediate literary correction, substituting English for Greek, Elizabeth for Agamemnon. Yet the classical allusion is not withdrawn; it hovers behind the scene, lending the bonfires a dignity that spans millennia.

## The Triumph of the Average Man

Ryecroft saw in the Jubilee a legitimate celebration of English common sense — the same pragmatic genius that had produced the constitutional monarchy itself:

> English monarchy, as we know it, is a triumph of English common sense. Grant that men cannot do without an overlord; how to make that over-lordship consist with the largest practical measure of national and individual liberty? We, at all events, have for a time solved the question. (Summer-XV)

The qualification — "for a time only, of course" — is typical of Ryecroft's refusal of easy triumphalism. Yet he grants the Jubilee its due. Looking back over sixty years, he acknowledged "many improvements in the material life of the English people": greater cleanliness and sobriety, diminished brutality, extended education, abolished tyrannies, abated sufferings. These were "mere details," and "whether they indicate a solid advance in civilization cannot yet be determined." But they were real, and they were what the average Briton could understand and celebrate:

> So let cressets flare into the night from all the hills! It is no purchased exultation, no servile flattery. The People acclaims itself, yet not without genuine gratitude and affection towards the Representative of its glory and its power. (Summer-XV)

## The Elegiac Undertone

Yet the Jubilee also prompted a darker meditation. In a later passage, Ryecroft returned to the event with unrelieved melancholy:

> The roaring "Jubilee" of last summer was for me an occasion of sadness; it meant that so much was over and gone — so much of good and noble, the like of which the world will not see again, and that a new time of which only the perils are clearly visible, is rushing upon us. (Winter-V)

Ryecroft's pessimism about the coming age is striking. The "generous hopes and aspirations of forty years ago" — the faith in science as deliverer — had yielded to a darker vision. Science, once seen as liberator, had revealed itself as tyrant. "This is the course of things; we must accept it. But it is some comfort to me that I — poor little mortal — have had no part in bringing the tyrant to his throne" (Winter-V).

## A Victorian's Ambivalence

The two passages, taken together, capture the essential ambivalence of the late-Victorian intellectual. Ryecroft could genuinely rejoice in the material and political achievements of the age while simultaneously mourning the spiritual and cultural losses that accompanied them. The Jubilee, as the supreme symbolic moment of the era, concentrated this doubleness: it was at once "a triumph of the average man" and a funeral for "so much of good and noble."
