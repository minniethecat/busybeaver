## Overview

The British Museum Reading Room occupies a singular place in Henry Ryecroft's private papers — as sanctuary, university, and symbol. During the years when he was "literally starving in London," Ryecroft spent countless days at the great domed reading room, pursuing classical scholarship with a disinterested passion that defied his material circumstances. The Reading Room was where Ryecroft the hack writer became, in his own mind, Ryecroft the scholar manqué.

## A Sanctuary for the Starving Scholar

Ryecroft's account of his Reading Room years is one of the most moving passages in the private papers. Having breakfasted on dry bread and carrying another piece for dinner, he would settle at a desk with books that "by no possibility could be a source of immediate profit":

> It astounds me to remember that, having breakfasted on dry bread, and carrying in my pocket another piece of bread to serve for dinner, I settled myself at a desk in the great Reading-Room with books before me which by no possibility could be a source of immediate profit. At such a time, I worked through German tomes on Ancient Philosophy. At such a time, I read Appuleius and Lucian, Petronius and the Greek Anthology, Diogenes Laertius and — heaven knows what! My hunger was forgotten; the garret to which I must return to pass the night never perturbed my thoughts. (Autumn-XVII)

The list of authors is itself a manifesto: Greek and Latin classics, pursued not for publication but for their own sake. The Reading Room made possible a double life — breadline poverty outside its walls, intellectual riches within.

## Casual Ablutions

Among Ryecroft's most characteristic anecdotes is the notice he discovered in the Museum lavatory:

> "Readers are requested to bear in mind that these basins are to be used only for casual ablutions." (Spring-X)

The inscription was, for Ryecroft, darkly comic. He had himself "more than once, been glad to use this soap and water more largely than the sense of the authorities contemplated," and he knew of "poor fellows working under the great dome whose need, in this respect, was greater than mine." The notice exposed, with bureaucratic precision, the thin line between the Museum as temple of learning and the Museum as the last resort of the destitute scholar.

## The Ideal Life

Looking back from Devon, Ryecroft saw the Reading Room years as expressions of his true nature — the scholar's life he might have led under different circumstances. He quotes Michelet with evident self-recognition: "*J'ai passé à côté du monde, et j'ai pris l'histoire pour la vie*" — I passed beside the world, and I took history for life. "That, as I can see now, was my true ideal" (Autumn-XVII).

The tragedy — and Ryecroft did not regard it as a tragedy — was that the conditions which enabled this scholarship (enforced leisure, minimal material needs) were the same conditions that made a formal scholarly career impossible. He was, as he put it, a man who "had in me the making of a scholar" but for whom "scholarship in the high sense was denied me, and now it is too late."

## A Divided Verdict

Ryecroft's feeling toward the young man he had been — "that thin, white-faced youth" — was complex. On the one hand, he felt pride: "On the whole, it seems to me something to be rather proud of; I smile approvingly at that thin, white-faced youth." On the other, he insisted on the distance between past and present self: "Me? My very self? No, no! He has been dead these thirty years" (Autumn-XVII).

The Reading Room thus becomes a site of memory as much as learning — the place where a former self lived and died, where hunger was forgotten in the presence of Lucian and Petronius, where the scholar that Ryecroft never became was nonetheless fully alive.
