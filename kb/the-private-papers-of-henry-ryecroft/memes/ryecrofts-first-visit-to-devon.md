## Overview

Ryecroft's first visit to Devon — undertaken on an "irresistible impulse" at the end of March, at a time when he was still immersed in the grinding labour of London literary life — was a pivotal event in his personal history. The journey transformed his relationship to the natural world, awakened a passion for botany and landscape, and prefigured the permanent retreat that would define his final years. In retrospect, Ryecroft regarded it as a second birth: "I had stepped into a new life" (Spring-IX).

## The Irresistible Impulse

The decision to go to Devon was uncharacteristically spontaneous for a man who lived by rigid self-discipline. Ryecroft described it as an "irresistible impulse" — a sudden determination to see "a part of England I had never seen." At the end of March, "I escaped from my grim lodgings, and, before I had time to reflect on the details of my undertaking, I found myself sitting in sunshine at a spot very near to where I now dwell" (Spring-IX).

The brevity of the account — the journey itself is barely described — emphasises the instantaneous nature of the transformation. The destination, not the travel, was what mattered.

## The Awakening

The experience was overwhelming. Though Ryecroft had been familiar with the countryside as a boy, the years of London had effaced those memories:

> Those years of London had obscured all my earlier life; I was like a man town-born and bred, who scarce knows anything but street vistas. The light, the air, had for me something of the supernatural — affected me, indeed, only less than at a later time did the atmosphere of Italy. (Spring-IX)

The language of religious experience is deliberate. Ryecroft, who was not conventionally pious, describes himself as discovering that he was "a sun-worshipper" and declares: "Under that radiant firmament, I could have thrown myself upon my knees in adoration." He walked bareheaded, avoiding every strip of shadow, covering "some thirty miles" without fatigue.

## Botanical Conversion

One specific transformation marked this day: Ryecroft became a botanist. Before Devon, he had "cared very little about plants and flowers"; afterward, he "found myself eagerly interested in every blossom, in every growth of the wayside." He gathered specimens, promising to buy a guidebook and identify them all — a promise he kept.

> To me the flowers became symbolical of a great release, of a wonderful awakening. My eyes had all at once been opened; till then I had walked in darkness, yet knew it not. (Spring-IX)

The Pauline echo — "I had walked in darkness, yet knew it not" — is the closest Ryecroft comes to describing a conversion experience. What was converted was not his theology but his sensorium. He had been blind to the natural world; now he saw.

## A New Life

The days that followed were a sustained idyll. Lodging in an outer street of Exeter "which savour more of country than of town," Ryecroft set forth each morning to "make discoveries." He followed the windings of the Exe, now inland through "rich, warm valleys, by orchards bursting into bloom," now seaward to "pine-clad heights, gazing over moorland brown with last year's heather."

The psychological liberation was as profound as the sensory awakening. For a brief period, Ryecroft — "the egoist in grain" — forgot himself entirely:

> So intense was my delight in the beautiful world about me that I forgot even myself; I enjoyed without retrospect or forecast; I, the egoist in grain, forgot to scrutinize my own emotions, or to trouble my happiness by comparison with others' happier fortune. (Spring-IX)

## Legacy

The first visit to Devon did not immediately change Ryecroft's circumstances — he returned to London and to hackwork. But it planted a seed. When, years later, the annuity from his friend's bequest gave him the means to choose where to live, he knew exactly where to go. The cottage near Exeter was not merely convenient; it was the fulfilment of a vision first glimpsed on that spring day when "I had stepped into a new life."
