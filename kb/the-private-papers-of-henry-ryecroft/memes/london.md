## Overview

London is the city where Henry Ryecroft spent most of his adult life as a struggling writer. It represents the world of poverty, hackwork, and "grim necessity" that he escapes when he retires to Devon.

## Description

Ryecroft lived in "the London suburb where of late he had been living" before his retirement. The text does not specify which suburb, but it was clearly a modest neighbourhood appropriate to his straitened circumstances. When his annuity arrives, "in a few weeks he quitted the London suburb" — a swift departure suggesting little attachment to the place.

## Ryecroft's Connection

London was the site of Ryecroft's entire working life — "for twenty years he had lived by the pen" there, producing "a great deal of mere hackwork; he reviewed, he translated, he wrote articles." The city embodies the hardship and disappointed ambition that shaped his character. His departure from London marks the definitive break between his old life of toil and his new life of rural contentment.

The contrast between London and Devon recurs throughout the journal as Ryecroft reflects on the difference between the urban grind and the natural rhythms of the countryside.

## Key Passages

> In a few weeks he quitted the London suburb where of late he had been living, and, turning to the part of England which he loved best, he presently established himself in a cottage near Exeter.
