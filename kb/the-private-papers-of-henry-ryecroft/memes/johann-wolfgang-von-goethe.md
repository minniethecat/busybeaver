## Overview

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) appears in Henry Ryecroft's reflections as a touchstone in the debate over artistic method. While the late Victorian literary world elevated Flaubertian conscientiousness — elaborate preparation, painstaking revision, artistic "conscience" — Ryecroft countered with Goethe's example: a genius whose finest lyrics were "scribbled as fast as pen could go, thwartwise on the paper, because he could not stop to set it straight" (Summer-XXI). Goethe thus serves, alongside Shakespeare, as evidence for Ryecroft's conviction that art springs from vitality rather than deliberation.

## Faust and the Youthful Lyrics

Ryecroft's reading of Goethe was discriminating. He acknowledged that Goethe "took thought enough about his Faust" — the monumental drama that occupied decades of the poet's life. But the weight of his argument falls on the contrast between this mature deliberation and the unstudied brilliance of the early work:

> Goethe (the example so often urged by imitators unlike him in every feature of humanity) took thought enough about his Faust; but what of those youthtime lyrics, not the least precious of his achievements, which were scribbled as fast as pen could go, thwartwise on the paper, because he could not stop to set it straight? (Summer-XXI)

The parenthetical aside — "imitators unlike him in every feature of humanity" — is characteristic of Ryecroft's sharpness. Those who invoke Goethe to justify elaborate artistic method miss the point: Goethe's method was an expression of Goethe's nature, not a transferable technique.

## Art and the Zest of Life

Ryecroft's meditation on Goethe belongs to a larger argument about the nature of art. His proposed definition — "an expression, satisfying and abiding, of the zest of life" (Summer-XXI) — finds its confirmation in the spontaneous creation of the young Goethe. The artist in his creative moment is "moved and inspired by supreme enjoyment of some aspect of the world about him; an enjoyment in itself keener than that experienced by another man, and intensified, prolonged, by the power — which comes to him we know not how — of recording in visible or audible form that emotion of rare vitality" (Summer-XXI).

This vision of art as overflowing vitality rather than calculated craft places Goethe alongside Shakespeare and Cervantes in Ryecroft's pantheon of natural geniuses. The fact that Goethe was German — that Ryecroft, so deeply English in his literary patriotism, could acknowledge this — speaks to the universalism of his literary judgment.

## Ryecroft's Reading of German Literature

Ryecroft's familiarity with Goethe extended to the broader context of German letters. His well-bound eight-volume Milman edition of Gibbon was a prized possession, but his reading ranged widely across languages. During his years of poverty at the British Museum, "I worked through German tomes on Ancient Philosophy" (Spring-XVI). His purchase of Jung-Stilling's autobiography — prompted by encountering the name in Goethe's *Wahrheit und Dichtung* (Poetry and Truth) — shows how Goethe served as a portal to wider German literary culture.
