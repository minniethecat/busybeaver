The predatory animus is Veblen's term for the spiritual attitude or "habit of mind" that regards for[[christian-endeavor]]ful seizure, competitive mastery, and the assertion of personal prowess as the natural and honorable forms of human activity. It is the psychological foundation of the leisure-class institution (001-028).

## Definition

Veblen defines the predatory animus as the "habitual bellicose frame of mind — a prevalent habit of judging facts and events from the point of view of the fight" (001-026). It is not merely the practice of fighting but the spiritual attitude that makes fighting seem natural, honorable, and definitive of human worth. The predatory phase of culture is attained "only when the predatory attitude has become the habitual and accredited spiritual attitude for the members of the group; when the fight has become the dominant note in the current theory of life" (001-026).

Veblen situates the predatory animus between the instinct of workmanship and the canon of [[conspicuous-waste]] "in point of generality and of psychological content" (005-003), suggesting that it is a fundamental human propensity shaped by cultural conditions — less universal than the instinct of workmanship but broader than any specific canon of expenditure.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen introduces the predatory animus in Chapter I as the spiritual correlative of the transition from [[peaceable-savagery]] to the predatory stage of culture. Under the predatory animus, "aggression becomes the accredited form of action, and [[trophies]] serves as prima facie evidence of successful aggression" (001-022).

The concept reappears in Chapter V where Veblen identifies it as one of the "broad principles of human nature" that shape the scheme of life, alongside the instinct of workmanship and the canon of conspicuous waste (005-003). Here the predatory animus is presented as an enduring force that moderates and shapes the expression of the other two principles.

In Chapter IX, Veblen analyzes how modern institutions conserve and transmit the predatory animus. He argues that certain social structures "conduce to the conservation of the [[prowess]]s and the predatory animus" through both "education and tradition" (009-023). Even in industrial society, where cooperation and efficiency are the ostensible values, the predatory animus persists in transformed guises — in competitive business practices, in the culture of sports, and in the social valuation of aggressive personalities.

## Significance

The predatory animus is a key bridge concept in Veblen's theory, connecting his evolutionary anthropology to his critique of modern institutions. It explains why the leisure class retains its cultural authority even in societies that have formally abandoned the predatory mode of life: the spiritual attitude outlasts the material conditions that produced it.

The concept also illuminates Veblen's analysis of conservatism. The predatory animus is one of the "archaic traits" that the leisure class conserves and that the industrial system, in Veblen's view, should render obsolete. The persistence of this animus in modern business and political life is, for Veblen, evidence of the incomplete adaptation of institutions to the requirements of industrial efficiency.
