# Herbert Spen[[christian-endeavor]]r

The English philosopher and sociologist (1820–1903) whose evolutionary theory of society provides a significant intellectual backdrop for Veblen's own evolutionary approach to economic institutions. Spencer's concept of social evolution from militant to industrial societies parallels Veblen's transition from predatory to quasi-[[peaceable-savagery]]s.

## Background

Herbert Spencer was the most influential evolutionary thinker of the late nineteenth century, applying [[charles-darwin]]ian principles to social development in works such as *The Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896). His theory of social evolution posited a general progression from simple, homogeneous, militant societies to complex, heterogeneous, industrial societies — a framework that Veblen both inherits and transforms.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen engages with Spencer's evolutionary framework primarily in Chapter IX, where he discusses the conservation of archaic traits. Spencer's model of social evolution provides Veblen with a vocabulary and conceptual structure, but Veblen departs significantly in his emphasis on the persistence of predatory traits in modern industrial society — where Spencer saw linear progress, Veblen saw the tenacious survival of barbarian values in pecuniary guise.

Veblen's concept of the instinct of workmanship and its tension with the predatory instinct echoes Spencer's distinction between industrial and militant social types, but Veblen gives the conflict a psychological and institutional depth that Spencer's more mechanistic evolutionism lacked. The leisure class, in Veblen's analysis, represents precisely the kind of archaic survival that Spencer's progressive scheme could not adequately explain.

## Key Ideas

Spencer's key contributions that Veblen engages with include: the concept of social evolution as a process of increasing differentiation and complexity; the distinction between militant societies (organized for war, characterized by coercive cooperation) and industrial societies (organized for peaceful production, characterized by voluntary cooperation); and the idea that institutions evolve through a process of adaptation to material circumstances.
