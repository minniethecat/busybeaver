# Pea[[christian-endeavor]]able Savagery

**Peaceable Savagery** is Veblen's term for the earliest hypothesized stage of human cultural development — a condition of primitive communal life characterized by small, sedentary groups lacking defined class divisions, institutionalized ownership, or the invidious distinction between [[prowess]] (honorable) and [[exploit-vs-drudgery]] (dishonorable) occupations. It serves in *[[Title-Page]]* as the evolutionary baseline from which the predatory barbarian culture and its accompanying leisure class emerge.

## Veblen's Treatment

### The Character of Peaceable Communities

Veblen describes the communities of the peaceable savage stage as "commonly peaceable and sedentary; they are poor; and individual ownership is not a dominant feature of their economic system" (001-007). In these communities, the economic differentiation that produces a leisure class has not yet occurred. Labor is undifferentiated by sex or status in the way that characterizes later stages; there is no systematic assignment of productive work to a subordinate class and [[honorific]] exploit to a dominant one.

Three features define the peaceable savage stage in Veblen's account:

1. **Absence of a leisure class** — there is no group systematically exempted from productive labor. The entire community participates in the work of subsistence.

2. **Absence of institutionalized ownership** — while personal possessions may exist, there is no developed system of private property as a basis of social esteem. The "[[pecuniary-emulation]]" that drives competitive accumulation has not yet found expression in property relations.

3. **Absence of the exploit/drudgery distinction** — occupations are not ranked according to whether they involve an assertion of prowess. The differentiation of employments into honorable (warfare, hunting, governance) and dishonorable (productive labor) categories — the fundamental distinction on which [[leisure-class]] rests — has not yet emerged.

### The Transition to Predatory Culture

The passage from peaceable savagery to the predatory stage is, in Veblen's account, the critical evolutionary transition that produces the leisure class. This transition is driven, he suggests, by the development of a warlike habit of life — "the conversion of the community from a peaceable to a fighting habit" — that creates the conditions for the differentiation of occupations, the emergence of ownership, and the [[emergence-of-leisure-class]]s (001-008).

> "When the community passes from peaceable savagery to a predatory phase of life, the conditions of the struggle for existence change. The people acquire a fighting habit, and the conditions of success in the struggle for existence come to be different." [001-022]

This transition is not instantaneous. Veblen describes intermediate stages — the "quasi-peaceable" conditions of early pastoral and agricultural societies — in which formal peace prevails but predatory values have already begun to structure social relations. The passage through these intermediate stages is marked by the gradual extension of ownership, the consolidation of class distinctions, and the elaboration of the institutions ([[ceremonial-observances]], [[decorum]], [[conspicuous-consumption]]) through which class status is expressed and maintained.

### Evidentiary Status

Veblen is notably cautious [[About]] the evidentiary basis for the peaceable savage stage. He acknowledges that "there is no distinct evidence that there has been such an initial stage of peaceable life as is here assumed" (001-026). The evidence from contemporary "primitive" cultures is ambiguous: some groups (the [[todas]], the [[ainu]], certain [[bushman]] communities) approximate the peaceable type, but even these, Veblen notes, may represent cases of "cultural degeneration" from a higher state rather than survivals of an original peaceable condition.

Despite this empirical caution, Veblen insists on the logical necessity of the peaceable stage. "The substantial difference between the peaceable and the predatory phase of culture," he argues, "is a difference of a character so marked as to be a safe ground for a broad distinction between the two cultural stages" (001-027). The concept is a necessary postulate of Veblen's evolutionary scheme: without an initial peaceable condition, the emergence of predatory institutions cannot be explained as a historical development rather than a natural given.

### Relationship to Later Stages

The peaceable savage concept serves an important rhetorical and analytical function in Veblen's argument. By establishing that the leisure class is not a universal feature of human society but a product of specific historical conditions (the transition to predatory culture), Veblen undermines any claim that class distinctions are natural, inevitable, or ordained. The peaceable savage stage demonstrates that human beings have lived — and can live — without the institutions of class, ownership, and invidious distinction that structure modern society.

This is not to say that Veblen advocates a return to primitive communalism. The evolutionary scheme is, in his view, irreversible — once the predatory habit has been established, it cannot simply be undone. But the concept of peaceable savagery provides a critical perspective on modern institutions: they are not eternal but historical, not natural but contingent, and their apparent inevitability is a product of the very habits of thought they have produced.

## Significance

The concept of peaceable savagery is significant in Veblen's framework for three reasons:

1. **Establishes the historical contingency of the leisure class** — by positing an initial stage in which no leisure class existed, Veblen makes the leisure class an object of historical explanation rather than a taken-for-granted feature of social life.

2. **Provides the evolutionary baseline for subsequent analysis** — the peaceable stage is the zero point from which all subsequent institutional developments are measured. Without this baseline, Veblen's evolutionary argument would lack a starting point.

3. **Supports the critical dimension of the theory** — the existence of a pre-class stage of human culture implies that class divisions, however deeply entrenched, are not inevitable. The "naturalness" of class distinctions is exposed as an artifact of the predatory cultural tradition rather than a fact of human nature.
