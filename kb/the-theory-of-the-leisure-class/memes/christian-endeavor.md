# Christian Endeavor

The Young People's Society for Christian Endeavor was an interdenominational evangelical organization founded in 1881 in Portland, Maine, by Francis E. Clark. It rapidly became one of the largest Protestant youth movements of the late 19th century, organizing young people into local societies for prayer, Bible study, and practical Christian service. By the 1890s, when Veblen was writing, Christian Endeavor claimed millions of members across dozens of denominations in North America and beyond.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen cites Christian Endeavor — alongside the YMCA — as an example of the lay religious organizations that serve as outlets for the "sporting temperament" within the framework of devout observance. In his analysis, these organizations are not accidental expressions of religious enthusiasm but structurally significant: they channel the predatory and competitive impulses — the same dispositions that find expression in sports, warfare, and delinquency — into "practical religion," thereby making [[the-church]] "militant." This serves Veblen's broader argument that devoutness and the [[predatory-temperament]] are not opposites but complementary expressions of the same archaic habit of mind. (012-006)

Veblen returns to Christian Endeavor in a later discussion of non-invidious interests, where he groups it among the voluntary organizations — including neighborhood guilds, sewing-clubs, art clubs, and commercial clubs — that, "in some slight measure," direct social energy away from purely pecuniary or invidious ends. However, Veblen's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: even these ostensibly altruistic organizations remain, in his view, expressions of the same underlying temperament that animates [[leisure-class]] and its institutions. (013-009)

## Historical Context

Christian Endeavor flourished during the same period that produced the Social Gospel movement, the YMCA's rapid expansion, and the rise of intercollegiate athletics — all movements that Veblen would have observed firsthand in the American religious and educational landscape of the 1880s and 1890s. The organization's emphasis on "decision for Christ," public testimony, and active Christian citizenship reflected the broader evangelical strategy of mobilizing lay energy through structured, association-based forms — a pattern Veblen recognized as characteristic of the late barbarian cultural stage he analyzed throughout *[[Title-Page]]*.
