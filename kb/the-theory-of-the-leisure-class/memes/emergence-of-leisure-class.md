# Emergen[[christian-endeavor]] of the Leisure Class

The macro-historical process that Veblen traces in Chapter I, spanning the transition from primitive savage communities (no leisure class) through lower and higher stages of barbarism to the fully developed leisure class of feudal society — a process driven by the gradual institutionalization of [[pecuniary-emulation]] and the accumulation of surplus wealth.

## Historical Context

Veblen situates the emergence of the leisure class within an evolutionary framework spanning the entire course of human cultural development. In the earliest stage — primitive savagery — communities such as the [[andaman-islanders]] and the [[todas]] show "the absence of a leisure class and the absence, in great measure, of the animus or spiritual attitude on which the institution of a leisure class rests" (001-006). The transition begins with the development of a [[predatory-stage]] in which physical [[prowess]] becomes the basis for invidious distinction.

## Veblen's Analysis

The emergence proceeds through identifiable stages. In the lower [[barbarian-culture]] (exemplified by [[north-american-tribes]]), "the exemption of the superior class from work has not gone far enough to make the designation 'leisure class' altogether applicable," but the invidious distinction between men's work (exploit) and women's work ([[exploit-vs-drudgery]]) is already firmly established (001-003). In the early barbarian stage ([[polynesian-islanders]], [[iceland]]ic community), the leisure class is "found in a less differentiated form" with rigorous class distinctions but less internal differentiation. Finally, at the higher barbarian stage ([[feudal-europe]], [[feudal-japan]]), the leisure class achieves its fullest institutional development.

The driving force throughout is the combination of surplus production (making exemption from labor materially possible) and invidious comparison (making exemption from labor socially desirable). "The institution of a leisure class has emerged gradually during the transition from primitive savagery to barbarism; or more precisely, during the transition from a peaceable to a consistently warlike habit of life" (001-008).
