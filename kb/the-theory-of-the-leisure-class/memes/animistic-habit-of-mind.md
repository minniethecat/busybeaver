# Animistic Habit of Mind

The **Animistic Habit of Mind** is a fundamental con[[christian-endeavor]]pt in Veblen's evolutionary psychology — the characteristic cognitive orientation of pre-modern cultures in which natural phenomena are apprehended not as the outcome of impersonal, mechanical causation but as expressions of purpose, intention, and personal agency. It is, in Veblen's scheme, the direct cognitive counterpart and antagonist of the [[matter-of-fact]] habit of mind fostered by modern industrial employment.

## Veblen's Treatment

### Nature of Animistic Thinking

Veblen defines the animistic habit of mind as the tendency to interpret events in terms of "propensity" or "bent" — to see in natural phenomena the working of purposes and intentions analogous to human will. This mode of apprehension is, he argues, the "common-sense" of early cultures. The savage or barbarian does not merely believe in animistic forces; he perceives the world through an animistic frame that renders impersonal causation literally unthinkable.

> "In the apprehension of the animistic savage or barbarian, the events of the external world are in great part shaped by a preternatural, personal, quasi-personal, or at least anthropomorphic agency." [001-014]

The animistic habit of mind operates through personification: it treats natural objects, forces, and sequences as though they possess will, intention, and the capacity for response to human entreaty. This personification is not a theory but a habit — an ingrained mode of perception that shapes experience before it reaches the level of conscious belief.

### Development and Scope

Veblen traces the development of animistic thinking through successive phases, from the "incipient animistic belief" of the earliest cultural stages to the fully elaborated anthropomorphic theologies of later religious systems (011-003), (011-004). The animistic sense, he argues, is "pervading" — it colors every aspect of experience, not merely the specifically religious (011-014).

The scope of animistic thinking extends beyond religion to encompass the apprehension of material objects and natural processes. There is, Veblen contends, an "animistic sense of a propensity in material things" — a sense that objects possess inherent tendencies and dispositions that operate in quasi-personal ways (011-015). This sense is the cognitive foundation of magical practice, ritual, and the "occult sciences" more broadly.

### Relationship to Predatory Culture

The animistic habit of mind is, in Veblen's evolutionary scheme, characteristic of the predatory and pre-predatory stages of culture. In the predatory stage, the world is apprehended as a arena of competing personal agencies — gods, spirits, forces — whose favor must be secured through ritual, sacrifice, and propitiation. This mode of thinking is structurally analogous to the predatory social order itself: just as the barbarian man contends with other men for dominance, so he contends with supernatural agencies for favor and protection.

The animistic temper is "recruited and conserved" by the institutions of "devout life" — religious observances that give "exercise to the animistic sense and to the emulative proclivities" (012-007). This linkage between animism and emulation is important: the same competitive, invidious impulse that drives status-seeking in social life drives the propitiation of supernatural agencies, and both are sustained by the same cognitive habits.

### Persistence as Survival

Crucially, Veblen does not treat the animistic habit of mind as a historical relic. It persists in modern society as a "survival" — a habit of thought shaped by earlier cultural conditions that continues to operate even after those conditions have been superseded. The animistic temper is most strongly conserved among [[leisure-class]] and those insulated from the discipline of machine industry. The industrial worker, by contrast, is subjected to the "enforced cognizance of matter-of-fact sequence" that progressively erodes the animistic frame (012-041).

This differential distribution of animistic habits is central to Veblen's analysis of class divergence. The leisure class retains archaic modes of thought — including the animistic apprehension of the world — precisely because its [[industrial-exemption]] leaves it free from the disciplinary force that would dissolve them. The persistence of religion, of belief in luck (Chapter 11), and of the "sense of status" are all expressions of the same underlying animistic habit of mind.

### Animism and the Instinct of Workmanship

Veblen's account of animism is complicated by its relationship to the instinct of workmanship. The "animistic habit of mind," he suggests, "may occur in the early stages of culture as a concomitant of the instinct of workmanship" (011-010). The same impulse to understand and manipulate the environment that produces technology also produces magical thinking — the difference lies in the institutional context that channels this impulse toward either matter-of-fact verification or ceremonial adequacy.

This ambivalence — animism as both an expression of the same cognitive impulse that drives science and a barrier to scientific thinking — runs throughout Veblen's treatment. The animistic habit is not simply error; it is a natural, even inevitable mode of thought in cultural settings where the discipline of machine industry has not yet established the matter-of-fact frame as the standard of cognitive adequacy.

## Significance

The animistic habit of mind is significant in Veblen's theoretical framework for several reasons:

1. **Explains the persistence of religion** — Veblen provides a non-theological explanation for religious belief by tracing it to animistic habits of thought that are conserved by institutional arrangements (the leisure class, [[the-church]], the classical curriculum) and eroded by industrial employment, not by rational argument.

2. **Grounds the concept of survivals** — the animistic habit is a paradigmatic case of an institutional survival: a mode of thought shaped by archaic conditions that persists into modern society and continues to structure behavior (e.g., belief in luck, [[ceremonial-observances]]) long after its original material basis has disappeared.

3. **Links class to cognition** — by arguing that the animistic habit is differentially distributed across classes, Veblen makes cognitive orientation a dimension of class analysis. The leisure class thinks differently from the industrial class not because of innate differences but because of differential exposure to the discipline of machine industry.

4. **Provides the antithesis to matter-of-fact** — animism and matter-of-fact form a binary opposition that structures Veblen's entire analysis of modern culture. Every institution, every practice, every belief can be located on a continuum between these two cognitive poles, and the movement of history is toward the gradual displacement of animistic by matter-of-fact habits — a movement that Veblen presents as neither complete nor inevitable.
