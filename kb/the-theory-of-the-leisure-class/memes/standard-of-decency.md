The standard of de[[christian-endeavor]]ncy, in Veblen's analysis, is the socially enforced minimum of consumption and display that members of each social class must maintain to preserve their reputability. It is not a fixed floor but an upward-moving target set by the class immediately above (005-002).

## Definition

Veblen defines the standard of decency as the conventional level of expenditure and display that a person must meet to "live up to the conventional standard of decency in the amount and grade of goods consumed" . The standard operates through external pressure ("popular insistence on conformity") and internal habituation: people come to experience the standard as a matter of "common sense" and personal propriety.

Crucially, the standard is "indefinitely extensible" . As a person's pecuniary ability increases, the standard rises to absorb the increment. But retrenchment — reducing one's scale of expenditure — is much more difficult than expansion, because the standard becomes habitual. "It is much more difficult to recede from a scale of expenditure once adopted than it is to extend the accustomed scale in response to an accession of wealth" .

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the standard of decency most fully in Chapter V (The Pecuniary Standard of Living). He argues that the standard functions through emulation: "each class envies and emulates the class next above it in the social scale" (005-002). The standard is not set by the average expenditure of one's own class but by "an ideal of consumption that lies just beyond our reach" (005-002).

The wealthy leisure class serves as the ultimate reference point: "all canons of reputability and decency, and all standards of consumption, are traced back by insensible gradations to the usages and habits of thought of the highest social and pecuniary class" (005-002). This creates a cascading structure of emulation in which each class strives to meet the standard set by the class above it.

Veblen also analyzes the domestic consequences of the standard of decency. The requirement to maintain visible consumption at a respectable level means that "the domestic life of most classes is relatively shabby, as compared with the éclat of that overt portion of their life that is carried on before the eyes of observers" (005-008). People sacrifice private comfort to public display, and the standard of decency becomes a "Malthusian prudential check" on birth rates among the classes most subject to its demands (005-008).

The standard has a temporal dimension as well. During what Veblen calls the "quasi-peaceable" stage of cultural development, "the standard of decency in expenditure" becomes "incorporated in the accredited standard of decency" (004-024) — it shifts from a flexible social convention to a hardened requirement enforced by "the common sense of the community."

## Significance

The standard of decency is Veblen's answer to the question of why consumption perpetually expands beyond physical needs. It provides the mechanism by which class structure — the invidious comparison between social ranks — translates into concrete patterns of expenditure. The concept explains the paradox that even as industrial productivity increases, people do not work less but instead raise their consumption standards to match.

This concept is essential to understanding Veblen's broader argument that economic behavior cannot be reduced to utility maximization. The standard of decency shows that consumption is a social language — a system of signals [[About]] status and belonging — before it is a matter of individual satisfaction.
