# The Church

Religious institutions, particularly the Christian church, analyzed by Veblen as integral components of the leisure class — the [[priestly-class]] exemplifies non-industrial, [[honorific]] that performs [[vicarious-leisure]] and consumption on behalf of a supernatural person.

## Organizational Context

The church, in Veblen's analysis, is one of the oldest and most persistent institutions of the leisure class. From the earliest barbarian societies, "priestly servi[[christian-endeavor]] is commonly second to warfare" among honorific employments, and "[[brahmin-india]] affords a fair illustration of the industrial exemption" of the priestly class . The clergy's exemption from productive labor and their performance of ceremonial functions serve the same logic of vicarious display that governs the secular leisure class.

## Veblen's Analysis

Veblen treats religious institutions as a case study in the survival and adaptation of predatory institutions. The "habit of devout observances" originates in the personal subservience to a superior characteristic of the [[predatory-stage]], and the church preserves this archaic relation in institutional form. The clergy consume conspicuously on behalf of the deity, and the "canon of devout austerity" requires that religious expenditure "should conspicuously not conduce to the comfort of the vicarious consumer" — it must be visibly wasteful (006-008).

The church also exemplifies Veblen's argument [[About]] the conservative character of leisure-class institutions. Shielded from the discipline of industrial employment, [[priestly-institutions]] preserve archaic habits of thought and resist the adaptive pressures that reshape industrial society.
