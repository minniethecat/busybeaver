# Veblen and the Institutional Economics Tradition

## Thesis

Thorstein Veblen's *[[Title-Page]]* (1899) inaugurated the American institutional economics tradition, a heterodox school that rejected the abstract, utility-maximizing premises of neoclassical economics in favor of an evolutionary, historically grounded analysis of economic institutions. While Veblen himself remained aloof from institution-building, his con[[christian-endeavor]]ptual framework — the distinction between industry and business, the analysis of consumption as social display, the theory of institutional lag — was taken up and developed by three major figures: Wesley Clair Mitchell, John R. Commons, and John Kenneth Galbraith. This article traces the Veblenian lineage through these thinkers and assesses his legacy in contemporary economic thought.

## Veblen's Challenge to Neoclassical Economics

Veblen's relationship to mainstream economics was one of fundamental critique. In a series of methodological essays (1898–1909), he argued that neoclassical economics was pre-[[charles-darwin]]ian in its assumptions — it treated human nature as fixed, self-interested, and hedonistic rather than as a product of evolving institutions and habits of thought. The "hedonistic calculus" of marginal utility theory, Veblen argued, was a taxonomic exercise in classification rather than a genuinely evolutionary explanation of economic behavior .

*The Theory of the Leisure Class* was Veblen's constructive alternative — a demonstration of what an evolutionary economics might look like. Instead of assuming that consumers rationally maximize utility, Veblen showed how consumption is driven by emulation, status competition, and institutionalized standards of decency. Instead of treating [[ownership]] as a natural legal category, he traced its origin to the seizure of persons as [[trophies]] in [[predatory-culture]]. The book was an exercise in institutional genealogy — showing how present economic behavior carries the "survivals" of earlier cultural stages .

## Wesley Clair Mitchell: Business Cycles and Empirical Institutionalism

Wesley Clair Mitchell (1874–1948), Veblen's student at the University of Chicago, was the first major figure to operationalize Veblen's institutional approach through systematic empirical research. Mitchell's *Business Cycles* (1913) applied Veblen's distinction between "industry" (the production of useful goods) and "business" (the pursuit of pecuniary gain) to the study of economic fluctuations. Mitchell argued that business cycles were not external shocks to an otherwise stable system but endogenous features of a pecuniary economy organized around the pursuit of profit.

Mitchell founded the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) in 1920, institutionalizing the empirical study of economic phenomena in a way that was broadly Veblenian in spirit — focused on measurement, historical sequence, and institutional context rather than deductive theory. Though Mitchell's work lacked Veblen's satirical edge and theoretical radicalism, it gave institutional economics a program of empirical research that Veblen himself never attempted.

## John R. Commons: Legal Foundations of Capitalism

John R. Commons (1862–1945), a contemporary of Veblen at the University of Wisconsin, shared Veblen's institutional orientation but developed it in a distinct direction. Where Veblen emphasized the evolutionary and anthropological dimensions of institutions, Commons focused on their legal and transactional structure. His major work, *Legal Foundations of Capitalism* (1924), analyzed property rights, contract law, and the legal framework of economic transactions as the "working rules" that govern economic life.

Commons shared Veblen's view that economics must be an evolutionary science, but he was more optimistic [[About]] the possibility of institutional reform through collective action. Where Veblen's analysis often reads as a deterministic account of cultural forces operating beyond conscious control, Commons emphasized the role of legislatures, courts, and collective bargaining in deliberately shaping economic institutions. This difference in emphasis produced two distinct strands of institutional economics: the more critical, Veblenian strand and the more reformist, Commonsian strand.

## John Kenneth Galbraith: The Affluent Society

John Kenneth Galbraith (1908–2006) represents the most direct twentieth-century continuation of Veblen's critique of consumer capitalism. Galbraith's *The Affluent Society* (1958) updated Veblen's theory of conspicuous consumption for the postwar era of mass affluence. Galbraith argued that in a society of abundance, the traditional economic problem — scarcity — had been effectively solved, but economic institutions continued to operate as if scarcity were the fundamental constraint. The result was "private affluence and public squalor" — a systematic underinvestment in public goods accompanied by the artificial stimulation of private consumption through advertising.

Galbraith's concept of the "dependence effect" — the idea that consumer wants are created by the production process itself rather than being an autonomous expression of individual preference — is a direct descendant of Veblen's analysis of how the "[[pecuniary-standard-of-living]]" is institutionally determined rather than individually chosen. Galbraith acknowledged Veblen explicitly as his intellectual predecessor and shared Veblen's gift for ironic social commentary.

## Veblen's Legacy in Contemporary Economics

Veblen's direct influence on mainstream economics has been limited. The neoclassical framework he criticized remains dominant, and institutional economics, though it persists as a heterodox tradition, has never become the central paradigm. However, Veblenian themes have periodically resurfaced in unexpected places.

Behavioral economics — the study of systematic deviations from rational choice — engages, albeit implicitly, with the Veblenian insight that economic behavior is shaped by social context, cognitive habits, and institutional norms rather than by utility maximization. The concept of "positional goods" (Fred Hirsch, 1976), which are valued for their relative scarcity rather than their absolute utility, formalizes Veblen's analysis of invidious comparison. The growing literature on inequality and social status (Wilkinson and Pickett, *The Spirit Level*, 2009) echoes Veblen's concern with the social costs of invidious ranking.

More broadly, Veblen's method — the analysis of economic institutions as products of historical evolution rather than as expressions of universal rationality — has influenced the "varieties of capitalism" literature, evolutionary economics, and the recent "institutional turn" in development economics (Acemoglu and Robinson, *Why Nations Fail*, 2012). While these contemporary authors rarely cite Veblen directly, his conceptual vocabulary — the distinction between productive and extractive institutions, the emphasis on historical path-dependence, and the analysis of institutional persistence — remains foundational to heterodox economic thought.

## Conclusion

Veblen's institutional economics represents a road not taken by the mainstream of the discipline, but one whose themes continue to resurface whenever the limitations of the neoclassical framework become apparent. Through Mitchell's empirical institutionalism, Commons's legal analysis, and Galbraith's public critique, Veblen's influence persisted through the twentieth century. His deeper legacy, however, may be methodological rather than doctrinal: the insistence that economic behavior cannot be understood apart from the evolving institutions, habits of thought, and cultural valuations that give it meaning and direction.
