# Pecuniary Reputability

The form of social esteem that is acquired and measured by the visible possession of wealth; reputation denominated in dollars rather than in deeds.

## Definition

Pecuniary reputability is the specific kind of social standing that derives from wealth and its conspicuous display. Veblen uses this term to distinguish reputation based on economic suc[[christian-endeavor]]ss from reputation based on other forms of merit — moral character, skill, wisdom, or service. In the [[pecuniary-culture]], pecuniary reputability becomes the dominant form of social esteem, subordinating or displacing other standards of judgment. The concept is essential to Veblen's theory because it explains why the accumulation of wealth is pursued not merely for comfort or security but for the social recognition it commands.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen introduces pecuniary reputability early in the work, describing modern society as consumed by a "struggle for pecuniary reputability" (002-016). This struggle is not a peripheral feature of economic life but its organizing principle. The competitive drive for pecuniary reputability is intensified by the visibility of consumption: "the struggle for pecuniary reputability" is accentuated "by visiting with a sharper disapproval any shortcoming in the approved standard of consumption" (002-019).

The concept operates across multiple domains of life. In the sphere of domestic service and [[vicarious-consumption]], Veblen observes that servants serve "as a method of imputing pecuniary reputability to the master or to the household" (003-031). In the built environment, even ecclesiastical architecture is shaped by "the pecuniary reputability of conspicuously [[conspicuous-consumption]]" rather than by spiritual considerations (006-006).

Most significantly, Veblen argues that pecuniary reputability acts as a canon that systematically distorts aesthetic judgment. "The canons of pecuniary reputability do, directly or indirectly, materially affect our judgments of the beauty of goods" (006-012). The more expensive an object, the more beautiful it appears — not because cost correlates with beauty, but because the canon of pecuniary reputability substitutes for the aesthetic sense. This substitution operates even in domains where one would expect independent standards: "the requirements of pecuniary reputability and those of beauty in the naive sense do not coincide" (006-039), yet the former consistently overrides the latter.

In the realm of fashion, Veblen notes that the canon of pecuniary reputability governs the rate of change: "the more rapidly will fashion shift from one style to another, the more readily the canon of pecuniary reputability can be appealed to" (007-013). The impermanence of fashion is itself an expression of the need to continually re-establish pecuniary standing through new expenditure.

## Significance

Pecuniary reputability is the mechanism through which wealth translates into social status. It provides the motivational engine for Veblen's entire system: people do not consume merely to satisfy wants but to acquire and maintain reputability. This concept connects directly to pecuniary emulation (the process by which lower classes imitate the consumption of higher classes to acquire reputability), invidious comparison (the negative side of reputability — the diminishment of others' standing), and conspicuous waste (the most efficient means of demonstrating pecuniary reputability). By showing that reputability is pecuniary rather than moral, Veblen exposes the value system of modern capitalism as fundamentally circular: wealth confers reputability, and reputability justifies the pursuit of more wealth.
