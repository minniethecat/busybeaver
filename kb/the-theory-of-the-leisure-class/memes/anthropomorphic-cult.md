# Anthropomorphic Cult

The form of religious organization — dominant in the higher stages of barbarian culture — in which deities are personified as powerful beings requiring costly servi[[christian-endeavor]], and the priestly class functions as their vicarious consumer.

## Definition

Anthropomorphic cult, in Veblen's analysis, is the religious system characteristic of the predatory and [[quasi-peaceable-stage]]s of cultural evolution, in which superhuman agents are conceived in human-like terms and served through a professional [[priestly-class]]. The defining feature of the anthropomorphic cult is not its theology but its economic structure: the deity is a quasi-personal master, and the priesthood functions as a class of vicarious consumers who perform wasteful ceremonial service on the deity's behalf. This economic logic — [[conspicuous-waste]] disguised as piety — is what distinguishes the anthropomorphic cult from earlier, less institutionalized forms of religious observance.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the concept most fully in his discussion of priestly institutions and their relationship to [[leisure-class]]. He observes that "in the priestly life of all anthropomorphic cults the marks of a vicarious consumption" are clearly visible (006-009). The priestly class does not produce; it consumes on behalf of the deity, and the splendor of the cult's material apparatus — temples, vestments, rituals — serves as vicarious conspicuous waste that imputes honor to the divine personage.

The anthropomorphic cult, Veblen argues, rests on three pillars: an animistic habit of thought (the tendency to attribute agency to inanimate phenomena), a well-developed sense of status, and the predatory temperament. "An animistic habit, a well-formed anthropomorphic cult, and a lively sense of status" form an interdependent complex that characterizes the higher barbarian cultures (011-013). This is not merely a historical observation; Veblen argues that these three elements persist into modern society, though in attenuated form.

A crucial element of Veblen's analysis is that the anthropomorphic cult acts to conserve the predatory temperament. "Anthropomorphic cults act to conserve, if not to initiate, the predatory temperament and the sense of status" (011-014). The cult preserves habits of thought — dominance and submission, honor and servitude — that originated in the [[predatory-stage]] and that the rationalizing force of industrial society would otherwise tend to dissolve. In this sense, the anthropomorphic cult is a conservative institution: it retards the evolutionary adaptation of habits of thought to the requirements of industrial life.

Veblen identifies a connection between the anthropomorphic cult and the delinquent or criminal type: "Both the delinquent and the man of devout life" share the same "temperament which inclines to an anthropomorphic cult" (012-004). This provocative claim reflects Veblen's view that the anthropomorphic disposition — the tendency to personify authority and to relate to it through submission and propitiation — underlies both religious devotion and certain forms of antisocial behavior.

In the modern context, Veblen sees the anthropomorphic cult as competing with the scientific spirit. The habits of thought fostered by the cult — personal causation, status relations, [[ceremonial-observances]] — are fundamentally at odds with the impersonal, cause-and-effect reasoning required by modern science and industry. The anthropomorphic cult "rests on habits of thought" that are incompatible with "the [[matter-of-fact]] that comes from the workmanlike handling of materials" (012-019).

## Significance

The anthropomorphic cult concept is central to Veblen's institutional analysis of religion. It strips [[the-church]] of their theological self-understanding and reveals their economic function: the cult is a mechanism for vicarious consumption and the conservation of archaic status relations. This analysis connects religion to the broader leisure-class system — the priesthood is, in economic terms, a leisure-class occupation, exempt from industrial employment and engaged in [[honorific]], non-productive ceremonial service. The concept also illuminates Veblen's theory of cultural lag: the anthropomorphic cult preserves predatory-era habits of thought well into the industrial age, creating a tension between the requirements of modern economic life and the inherited dispositions that the cult reinforces.
