Honorific, in Veblen's usage, describes anything that confers social esteem by demonstrating [[prowess]] or exemption from productive labor. The term is [[christian-endeavor]]ntral to his theory of how economic behavior is shaped by social status considerations rather than utility (001-023).

## Definition

Veblen defines the honorific in terms of the archaic barbarian equation of honor with force. "Honorable" originally connotes "formidable"; "worthy" is "prepotent" (001-023). A honorific act, in its simplest form, is "a recognized successful act of aggression" (001-023). As civilization develops, this predatory sense is sublimated into subtler forms: any employment, expenditure, or display that demonstrates the agent's exemption from industrial usefulness becomes honorific.

The concept operates across multiple domains in Veblen's analysis: honorific employment (warfare, [[priestly-class]], governance), honorific leisure (the conspicuous abstention from labor), and honorific consumption (wasteful expenditure that displays wealth). In each case, the honorific quality derives not from utility but from the capacity to demonstrate status through what Veblen calls "conspicuous exemption from productive work" (003-010).

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen traces the honorific from its roots in the [[predatory-stage]] of culture through to modern industrial society. Among barbarian peoples, the chief honorific employments are warfare, government, [[devout-observances]], and sports — all activities that demonstrate prowess rather than productive diligence.

The critical transition in Veblen's account is the substitution of wealth for prowess as the basis of honorific status. As society moves from the predatory to the [[quasi-peaceable-stage]], "the possession of wealth gains in relative importance and effectiveness as a customary basis of repute and esteem" (002-011). Honorific booty — [[trophies]] taken in combat — gives way to honorific wealth — [[ownership]] acquired through pecuniary success. But the underlying logic remains the same: what is honorific is what demonstrates competitive superiority over others.

Veblen also analyzes how the honorific character of leisure — the "honorific leisure" of the upper classes — operates as a visible mark of status. The leisure class demonstrates its [[industrial-exemption]] through "a conspicuous abstention from labour" (003-010), and this abstention becomes honorific precisely because it would be economically impossible for the lower classes to imitate.

In the domain of consumption, Veblen argues that goods are valued not only for their utility but for their honorific character — their capacity to display the owner's wealth and taste. "The base classes," he notes, "by force of the requirements of the [[pecuniary-standard-of-living]], are compelled to consume in a manner that is honorific" (004-004), even when this consumption exceeds their physical needs.

## Significance

The honorific concept provides the psychological and anthropological foundation for Veblen's entire theory of [[conspicuous-consumption]] and conspicuous leisure. Without understanding the honorific — the way that certain activities and expenditures confer social esteem — one cannot understand why economic behavior systematically departs from rational utility-maximization.

The honorific also illuminates Veblen's methodology: he is not merely describing economic behavior but excavating the archaic cultural logic that persists beneath modern institutions. The honorific is a "survival" from the [[barbarian-culture]] of culture, but it remains the operative principle shaping consumption, education, [[dress-as-pecuniary-expression]], and even religious observance in modern industrial society.
