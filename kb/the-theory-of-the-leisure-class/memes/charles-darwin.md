# Charles Darwin

The English naturalist (1809–1882) whose theory of evolution by natural selection, articulated in *On the Origin of Species* (1859) and *The Des[[christian-endeavor]]nt of Man* (1871), provides the foundational scientific framework for Veblen's evolutionary approach to economics. Veblen's entire method — tracing the emergence, persistence, and transformation of institutions through selective adaptation — is fundamentally Darwinian.

## Background

Darwin's theory of evolution revolutionized nineteenth-century thought by providing a naturalistic explanation for the origin and diversity of species. His concept of natural selection — the differential survival and reproduction of variants in response to environmental pressures — was rapidly extended beyond biology to social theory by thinkers such as [[herbert-spencer]], and it is this extended evolutionary method that Veblen brings to bear on economic institutions.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen's relationship to Darwin is methodological rather than citational. Although Darwin is explicitly referenced only sparingly (in Chapters IX and XI), the entire structure of Veblen's argument is Darwinian: institutions are treated as evolving through a process of selective adaptation to material circumstances, with [[leisure-class]], [[conspicuous-consumption]], and the [[pecuniary-canons-of-taste]] all understood as institutional variants that have survived because they serve (or once served) a selective function in the struggle for status.

Veblen's concept of the "instinct of workmanship" as an evolved human propensity directly echoes Darwin's treatment of instincts in *The Descent of Man*. Similarly, Veblen's analysis of the [[predatory-stage]] as a stage in which certain traits (aggression, the drive for dominance) are selectively favored draws on Darwinian reasoning [[About]] the conditions under which particular behavioral dispositions confer survival advantages.

Where Veblen departs from Darwin is in his emphasis on the *relative* character of selective pressures. For Veblen, the crucial selective environment is not the natural world but the social world of [[pecuniary-emulation]] — it is status competition, not physical survival, that drives the evolution of modern economic institutions.

## Key Ideas

Darwin's key contributions that Veblen draws on include: the concept of evolution through natural selection; the idea that instincts and behavioral dispositions are products of evolutionary history; the method of explaining present traits by reference to past selective pressures; and the continuity between human and animal behavior that undermines claims of human exceptionalism.
