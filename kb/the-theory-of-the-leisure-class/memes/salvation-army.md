# Salvation Army

The Salvation Army is an evangelical Christian movement and charitable organization founded in London in 1865 by Methodist ministers William and Catherine Booth. Organized on a quasi-military model with ranks, uniforms, and marching bands, the movement expanded rapidly to the United States by 1880. By the 1890s, its distinctive combination of revivalist preaching, street evangelism, and social service work for the urban poor had made it one of the most visible and controversial religious organizations in the English-speaking world.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen's reference to the Salvation Army appears in his analysis of the relationship between devoutness and the [[predatory-temperament]]. He identifies the Salvation Army as an instance of a religious cult "of the character which the habits of mind of the athlete and the delinquent require" — an organization that channels the competitive, combative, and display-oriented dispositions of the "sporting" type into religious expression. Veblen further notes that the Salvation Army appears to recruit disproportionately from "lower-class delinquents" and to include among its officers "a larger proportion of men with a sporting record than the proportion of such men in the aggregate population." (012-004)

This analysis serves Veblen's broader thesis that the religious impulse and the predatory impulse share a common psychological root: the archaic "[[animistic-habit-of-mind]]" that attributes purpose and agency to events and that finds satisfaction in the dramatic, the spectacular, and the combative. The Salvation Army's military organization, its street-corner dramatics, and its appeal to the socially marginal all illustrate, for Veblen, the convergence of athletic, delinquent, and devout temperaments that the leisure-class institution preserves and transmits.

## Historical Context

The Salvation Army's militarized structure and street-level evangelism placed it at the intersection of several late-Victorian social currents: urban poverty and slum clearance movements, the professionalization of charity, and the broader evangelical awakening that also produced the [[ymca]], [[christian-endeavor]], and the Social Gospel. Veblen's characterization of its membership as drawn from "lower-class delinquents" reflects not only the Army's actual ministry to the urban poor but also the class prejudices of the period, in which revivalist enthusiasm and emotional religiosity were often associated with the lower social orders.
