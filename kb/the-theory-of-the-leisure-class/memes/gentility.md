# Gentility

The complex of manners, tastes, and deportment cultivated by [[leisure-class]] as eviden[[christian-endeavor]] of hereditary [[industrial-exemption]] — a key mechanism by which class boundaries are maintained and transmitted across generations.

## Definition

In Veblen's analysis, gentility is the embodied, behavioral evidence of a life spent in conspicuous leisure. It is not merely politeness or good breeding but a visible, habitual demonstration that one has never been compelled to engage in productive work. "Refined tastes, manners, habits of life are a useful evidence of gentility, because good breeding requires time, application, and expense" — resources unavailable to those who must labor for a living (003-018).

Gentility is distinguished from mere wealth by its hereditary character. It cannot be acquired overnight through expenditure alone; it requires the accumulation of leisure across generations. It is, in Veblen's framework, a form of cultural capital whose principal function is exclusion — marking off those born into the leisure class from those who have merely acquired money.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the concept of gentility primarily in relation to the vicarious leisure performed by women and servants. The wife and domestic servants demonstrate the master's wealth by consuming goods and performing ceremonial services, and in doing so, they also embody and transmit gentility. The wife, being "ordinarily of gentle blood," is herself a bearer of inherited status, and "the fact of her being so will hasten her exemption from vulgar employment" (003-026).

Gentility is transmissible — and this is crucial to its social function. Veblen argues that "with the inheritance of gentility goes the inheritance of obligatory leisure" (004-010). The transmission mechanism creates a structural dilemma: gentility can be inherited without the wealth needed to sustain a life of dignified leisure. The "genteel poor" — those with gentle blood but insufficient means — represent a persistent anomaly in the class system, one that Veblen treats with characteristic irony.

The concept also illuminates the function of the elaborate code of manners Veblen analyzes throughout Chapter III. The ceremonial usages, the "punctilios," the nuanced gradations of decorum — these are not arbitrary. They constitute a system of signs by which insiders recognize each other and detect outsiders. A departure from the code "is intrinsically odious" because it threatens the entire symbolic apparatus of class distinction (003-017).

## Significance

Gentility is one of Veblen's most subtle concepts, bridging his economic analysis of leisure with his sociological analysis of status. It shows how class inequality is inscribed not just in the distribution of wealth but in the very texture of daily behavior — in how people speak, eat, [[dress-as-pecuniary-expression]], and carry themselves. The concept anticipates later sociological work on cultural capital and distinction while remaining firmly rooted in Veblen's evolutionary framework.
