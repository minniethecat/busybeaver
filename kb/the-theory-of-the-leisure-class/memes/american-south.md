# American South

The American South — the southeastern region of the United States — was, in the late 19th [[christian-endeavor]]ntury when Veblen wrote, a predominantly agrarian society still shaped by the legacy of the plantation economy and the antebellum social hierarchy. Its economic structure, characterized by labor-intensive agriculture, limited industrialization, and a sharply stratified class system, distinguished it from the industrializing North.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen devotes sustained attention to the American South in Chapter 12, "Devout Observances," where he identifies it as the only region of the United States possessing anything resembling a hereditary leisure class. Unlike the industrial North, where class position was increasingly determined by pecuniary achievement in business, the Southern leisure class rested on inherited land, traditional status distinctions, and exemption from productive labor — features that Veblen traces to the region's pre-industrial, quasi-feudal plantation economy. (012-039)

Veblen correlates this distinctive class structure with what he observes as the greater devoutness of the Southern population. He argues that religious observance functions as a mark of reputability for the leisure class and that this pattern is especially pronounced where the leisure class retains an archaic, non-industrial character. The Southern leisure class, being "somewhat given to devout observances — more so than any class of corresponding pecuniary standing elsewhere in the American community," illustrates Veblen's broader thesis that devoutness and the leisure class institution reinforce one another in pre-modern social formations. (012-039)

Extending the analysis beyond the leisure class proper, Veblen notes that the peculiar economic circumstances of the South produce a wider cultural pattern: the "greater devoutness of the Southern population, both white and black," correlates with a scheme of life "which in many ways recalls the barbarian status" — an economic order marked by personal mastery and subservience rather than the impersonal relations of modern industry. (012-040) He further observes that the religious expression of lower-class Southern groups, particularly the African American population, shows a pronounced inclination toward "spectacular observances," emotional displays, and what he terms "reversion" to pre-institutional forms of worship — a pattern he ties to the region's broader cultural distance from the [[matter-of-fact]] habits of thought fostered by industrial employment. (006-012)
