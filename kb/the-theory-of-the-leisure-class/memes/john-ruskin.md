# John Ruskin

John Ruskin (1819–1900), the Victorian art critic, social thinker, and Slade Professor of Fine Art at Oxford, is cited by Veblen together with William Morris as an "eager spokesman" for what Veblen critically characterizes as the "exaltation of the defective" — the aesthetic doctrine that values visible imperfections in hand-wrought goods as marks of superiority over the impersonal perfection of machine manufacture. (006-053)

## Background

Ruskin was the preeminent English art critic of the nineteenth [[christian-endeavor]]ntury, whose influence extended from aesthetics into social reform. His major works — *Modern Painters* (1843–1860), *The Seven Lamps of Architecture* (1849), and *The Stones of Venice* (1851–1853) — articulated a philosophy in which the moral condition of a society was expressed in its art and architecture. Ruskin argued passionately for the superiority of Gothic craftsmanship over the products of industrial manufacture, maintaining that the visible marks of the artisan's hand and the organic irregularity of pre-industrial building were indices of human freedom and creativity. His ideas profoundly influenced the Arts and Crafts Movement, the Gothic Revival in architecture, and early British socialism. Ruskin's Oxford lectures and prolific writings made him a central intellectual figure of his era, and his advocacy for the dignity of manual labor and the unity of art and life resonated with reformers across the political spectrum.

Veblen's engagement with Ruskin came at a moment when the Arts and Crafts aesthetic had achieved wide cultural prestige. By 1899, when *[[Title-Page]]* was published, Ruskin's ideas [[About]] the [[honorific]] character of hand work had become part of the educated taste of the Anglo-American [[leisure-class]] — precisely the class whose aesthetic judgments Veblen sought to analyze.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen deploys Ruskin in Chapter Six ("[[pecuniary-canons-of-taste]]") as an instance of the broader cultural dynamic he calls the canon of [[conspicuous-waste]]. Veblen's argument proceeds from a sociological observation: machine-made goods are "cheap, and therefore indecorous" because their consumption "is therefore not honorific, since it does not serve the purpose of a favorable [[pecuniary-emulation]] with other consumers." Against this background, the hand-wrought article — precisely because it is more expensive, more labor-intensive, and less perfect in its execution — acquires honorific status. (006-052) (006-053)

In Veblen's reading, Ruskin's aesthetic preference for the hand-crafted is not a genuine aesthetic philosophy standing outside social relations. Rather, it is an expression of the leisure-class value system: what is perceived as beautiful is in fact what is expensive, wasteful, and inaccessible to the common consumer. The visible imperfections of hand-wrought goods, Veblen argues, "being honorific, are accounted marks of superiority in point of beauty, or serviceability, or both." Ruskin and Morris's advocacy for handicraft is thus reinterpreted as a symptom of [[pecuniary-culture]] rather than a critique of it. (006-053)

Veblen extends the analysis to suggest that Ruskin's position was historically contingent: "So much of the work and speculations of this group of men as fairly comes under the characterization here given would have been impossible at a time when the visibly more perfect goods were not the cheaper." The Arts and Crafts aesthetic, in this view, is an effect of the very industrial development it claims to oppose: hand work acquired its honorific value only when industrial production made it scarce and therefore expensive. (006-053)
