# Pecuniary Canons of Taste

The principle that aesthetic judgment — what a community finds beautiful, gra[[christian-endeavor]]ful, or fitting — is shaped not by universal standards of beauty but by the norm of conspicuous waste. Veblen argues that the canons of taste are "habits of thought" formed under the selective guidance of the [[pecuniary-reputability]].

## Definition

The pecuniary canons of taste are the criteria by which objects, styles, and modes of life are judged aesthetically acceptable, criteria that Veblen shows to be products of the same competitive dynamic that drives conspicuous consumption. Objects are perceived as beautiful "somewhat in proportion as they are expensive" — not because expense inherently produces beauty, but because the canon of conspicuous waste has trained the community's aesthetic sensibilities to find [[honorific]] pleasing (006-015). The canons of taste thus serve as a hidden mechanism that translates pecuniary norms into apparently natural aesthetic preferences.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the concept most fully in Chapter VI ("Pecuniary Canons of Taste"). He argues that the canon of waste is "present, consciously, in our canons of taste" as a "selective norm" that guides the formation of aesthetic habits (006-015). This is not a conscious preference for expensive things, but a deeply habituated sensibility: people genuinely experience the costly as beautiful and the inexpensive as aesthetically deficient.

The concept has broad application. Veblen analyzes how the canons of taste shape preferences in domestic animals (horses, dogs, cats prized for breeds that exhibit useless but costly traits), landscaping (the "pecuniary beauty" of a manicured lawn), dress (fabrics and styles that visibly demonstrate inability to do physical labor), and architecture (monumental features that serve no practical purpose). In each case, the aesthetic judgment tracks the degree of conspicuous waste rather than any independent standard of beauty.

Chapter VII extends the analysis specifically to dress, showing that "the canon of expensiveness" shapes clothing aesthetics: fabrics are prized for being delicate (easily soiled, demonstrating the wearer does no manual work), styles change rapidly (demonstrating ability to keep up with fashion's costly obsolescence), and impractical features like high heels or long skirts persist because they visibly impede productive labor (007-002).

## Significance

The pecuniary canons of taste represent Veblen's most radical challenge to the idea of autonomous aesthetic judgment. By showing that taste is socially constructed through the mechanism of conspicuous waste, he anticipates later sociological and anthropological critiques of aesthetics. The concept is foundational for the sociology of consumption, fashion theory, and the critique of "good taste" as a vehicle of class distinction.
