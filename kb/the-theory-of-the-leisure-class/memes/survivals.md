# Survivals

Veblen's deployment of the anthropological con[[christian-endeavor]]pt of "survivals" — traits, institutions, and habits of thought that originated under earlier cultural conditions but persist into the present, often in transformed or vestigial form — as an analytical tool for explaining the archaic character of modern economic behavior.

## Definition

The concept of survivals was developed by the anthropologist E.B. Tylor to describe cultural elements that continue to exist after the conditions that produced them have passed away. Veblen adopts this framework and applies it systematically to the institutions and habits of thought of modern industrial society. In his usage, survivals are not merely quaint relics but active forces that "cumber the ground and hinder the full exercise of economic efficiency" (012-015).

Veblen identifies survivals at multiple levels: in social institutions ([[leisure-class]] itself is a survival from the predatory stage), in habits of thought (animistic and anthropomorphic reasoning), in ritual and ceremony ([[devout-observances]], academic regalia), and in aesthetic standards (the preference for the "[[honorific]]" over the useful).

## Veblen's Treatment

The concept appears early in the treatise, where Veblen describes manners and ceremonial usages as "symbolical and conventionalised survivals representing former acts of dominance or of personal service" (003-016). What appears as refined politeness is, in this reading, a fossilized residue of feudal obeisance.

In the domain of consumption, Veblen argues that sumptuary restrictions — the prohibition of luxury goods for lower classes — leave behind survivals even after their formal abolition. "We may accordingly look for survivals of the tabu on luxuries" in the lingering sense that certain forms of consumption are presumptuous for those of lower station (004-005).

The concept reaches its fullest expression in Veblen's discussion of religion and devoutness. He identifies the "sentiment of personal fealty" and the "general habit of mind of which that sentiment is an expression" as "survivals which cumber the ground" in a modern industrial context (012-015). This is not a crude dismissal of religion but an application of his evolutionary framework: devotional attitudes that served integrative functions in earlier social orders become obstacles in a system organized around impersonal, [[matter-of-fact]] causation.

In his treatment of survivals, Veblen extends the analysis to the highest reaches of culture. The aesthetic ideals perpetuated by the leisure class derive their authority from "psychological survivals, in the way of persistent and archaic habits of thought" (009-011). Even the aesthetic appreciation of poetry and the fine arts carries forward "survivals from a more primitive type of culture" (010-024). This analysis reaches its institutional culmination in Chapter XIV, where Veblen reads the rituals and curricula of [[universities]] as "ritualistic survivals and reversions" that indicate "a prevalence of archaic habits of thought" in the learned class (014-009, 014-016).

## Significance

The survivals concept is methodologically central to Veblen's entire project. It provides the evolutionary logic that allows him to read modern institutions — from the family to the university — as palimpsests bearing traces of earlier cultural strata. Without this concept, his characteristic argumentative move — "this apparently modern institution is really a barbarian survival" — would lack its theoretical anchor. The concept demonstrates his intellectual debt to nineteenth-century evolutionary anthropology while showing how he transformed it into a tool of social criticism.
