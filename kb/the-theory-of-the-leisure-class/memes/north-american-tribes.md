# North American Hunting Tribes

A key ethnographic referen[[christian-endeavor]] group in Veblen's evolutionary scheme, used to illustrate the transitional stage between primitive savagery and fully developed barbarian culture — a stage in which a [[leisure-class]] has begun to differentiate but has not yet achieved full institutional form. Together with the [[polynesian-islanders]] and [[andaman-islanders]], these tribal societies form Veblen's comparative baseline for tracing the emergence of class distinctions (001-003).

## Background

Veblen references North American hunting tribes — likely drawing on the ethnographic literature of the nineteenth century concerning Plains tribes such as the Sioux, Cheyenne, and Comanche — as examples of nomadic hunting societies. These were societies organized around the pursuit of large game (primarily bison), with a warrior culture and a clear distinction between male and female economic roles.

The key structural feature of these societies, from Veblen's perspective, is that they represent the lower [[barbarian-culture]] — a phase where the transition from [[peaceable-savagery]] to warlike barbarism is underway but incomplete. In Veblen's typology, this is the stage immediately following the peaceable communities of the [[andaman-islanders]] and preceding the full institutional development of a leisure class found in [[feudal-europe]] and [[feudal-japan]].

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

The North American hunting tribes serve as Veblen's primary illustration of the lower barbarian stage, the phase immediately preceding the emergence of a fully developed leisure class. "Any one of the North American hunting tribes may be taken as a convenient illustration" of communities where "the exemption of the superior class from work has not gone far enough to make the designation 'leisure class' altogether applicable" (001-003). This is a crucial theoretical point: class distinction based on occupation exists before the leisure class itself is fully formed.

The crucial observation is the invidious character of the sexual division of labor: "the women are, by [[prescription]], held to those employments out of which the industrial occupations proper develop at the next advance. The men are exempt from these vulgar employments and are reserved for war, hunting, sports, and [[devout-observances]]" (001-003). This anticipates Veblen's later argument that the sexual division of labor is the template for all subsequent class distinctions between [[honorific]] and ignoble employments.

Veblen also notes that "in many hunting tribes the man must not bring home the game he has killed, but must send it by the hand of a fellow hunter" — an early form of the honorific principle that distances the individual from productive work (001-018). This taboo against appearing to labor productively is the germ of the later [[industrial-exemption]] that defines leisure-class status.

## Significance

The North American tribes case is methodologically important because it demonstrates Veblen's comparative-evolutionary method at work. By positioning these societies between the peaceable Andaman Islanders and the stratified feudal cultures of Europe and Japan, Veblen constructs a narrative of institutional development that is neither teleological nor narrowly economic. The emergence of class is shown to be contingent on specific cultural conditions — particularly the shift from peaceable to warlike habits — rather than an inevitable consequence of material progress.
