# Andaman Islanders

An ethnographic referen[[christian-endeavor]] group that Veblen uses in Chapter I to illustrate the primitive savage stage of culture — communities characterized by the absence of a leisure class and the absence of the invidious distinctions that define barbarian and modern societies.

## Background

The Andaman Islanders, inhabitants of the [[andaman-islands]] in the Bay of Bengal, were studied by nineteenth-century ethnographers as an example of a relatively isolated and technologically simple society. Veblen draws on this ethnographic literature — particularly the work of E.H. Man and A.R. Radcliffe-Brown — to illustrate the cultural stage that precedes the [[emergence-of-leisure-class]].

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

The Andaman Islanders serve a crucial theoretical function in Veblen's evolutionary scheme. They represent the baseline against which the emergence of the leisure class can be measured — a culture in which "there is no hierarchy of economic classes" and the [[predatory-animus]] that sustains the leisure class has not taken institutional form (001-006).

Veblen describes them as providing "as good an instance of this phase of culture as may be had," acknowledging the difficulty of finding genuinely primitive communities uncontaminated by regression from higher stages (001-006). He groups them with the [[todas]], the [[ainu]], and some [[bushman]] and Eskimo groups as examples of cultures lacking a leisure class.

The Andaman Islanders also appear later in Chapter I in the context of the sexual division of labor. Veblen notes that "the difference in mass, in physiological character, and in temperament may be slight among the members of the primitive group; it appears, in fact, to be relatively slight and inconsequential in some of the more archaic communities with which we are acquainted — as for instance the tribes of the Andamans" (001-017). This observation supports his argument that the extreme sexual division of labor found in barbarian and modern societies is a cultural development, not a biological given.
