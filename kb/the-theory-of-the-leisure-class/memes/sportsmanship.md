# Sportsmanship

The competitive athletic impulse that Veblen identifies as a modern survival of the predatory temperament — an institutionalized channel for the archaic drive toward [[prowess]], dominan[[christian-endeavor]], and emulative physical contest.

## Definition

Sportsmanship, in Veblen's usage, is not the modern ethical ideal of fair play but rather the underlying psychological disposition that inclines individuals toward athletic competition. He treats it as essentially continuous with the predatory temperament: "the instinct of sportsmanship, as it might well be called, is essentially a deep-seated spiritual trait of the predatory type" (010-026). The term designates the evolved form of the drive to physical exploit — a drive that, in the predatory stage, expressed itself in hunting and warfare, and that in the [[pecuniary-culture]] finds its outlet in organized sports and athletic contests.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen introduces the concept in his discussion of modern [[survivals]] of the predatory temperament, grouping sportsmanship with other "manifestations of the predatory temperament" that "are all to be classed under the head of exploit" (010-014). These manifestations "are partly simple and unreflected expressions of an attitude of emulative ferocity" — the sportsman does not consciously choose to express predatory impulses, but the structure of athletic competition channels those impulses into socially acceptable forms.

The term "sportsmanship proper" (010-015) distinguishes the core competitive impulse from the paraphernalia of organized sport — the rules, the equipment, the spectatorship — all of which are secondary elaborations. What is primary is the drive to contest, to measure oneself against an opponent, and to prevail — the same drive that animated the predatory warrior.

Veblen locates sportsmanship within the broader barbarian cultural complex: "The barbarian culture shows sportsmanship, status, and anthropomorphism" as three interrelated expressions of the predatory habit of mind (011-013). Sportsmanship is thus not an isolated phenomenon but part of a coherent cultural configuration that includes the sense of status (dominance hierarchy) and the [[anthropomorphic-cult]] (personalized authority). All three rest on the same foundation: the tendency to conceive of social relations in terms of personal dominance and submission.

The connection between sportsmanship and the sense of honor is particularly significant for Veblen. He describes "an expression of sportsmanship and a derivative of predatory honor" (012-039), suggesting that the modern concept of honor — the sensitivity to slights, the readiness to defend one's reputation — is itself a transmuted form of the sportsman's competitive drive. Even in "the [[higher-learning]]," Veblen finds "in substance an expression of sportsmanship — of the predatory and [[animistic-habit-of-mind]]" (014-032), indicating how deeply the competitive impulse penetrates into institutions ostensibly devoted to disinterested inquiry.

## Significance

Sportsmanship serves as Veblen's most concrete illustration of how the predatory temperament survives into modernity. Sports are not, in this analysis, a harmless leisure activity but the institutionalized perpetuation of an archaic psychological complex — a channel through which the predatory disposition is preserved and legitimated in a society that has otherwise renounced overt violence as a means of social competition. The concept illuminates the persistence of the [[predatory-stage]] within the pecuniary culture: the sports field is, in effect, a reservation where the predatory temperament can express itself without threatening the industrial order.
