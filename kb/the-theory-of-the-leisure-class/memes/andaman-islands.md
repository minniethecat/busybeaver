# Andaman Islands

The Andaman Islands are an archipelago in the Bay of Bengal, situated between India and Myanmar. Historically home to several indigenous tribal groups — including the Great Andamanese, Onge, Jarawa, and Sentinelese — these communities maintained small-scale hunting-and-gathering economies with minimal social stratification into the late 19th [[christian-endeavor]]ntury, when Veblen was writing.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen references the [[andaman-islanders]] in the opening chapter of *[[Title-Page]]* as one of the best available illustrations of the earliest stage of cultural development — the peaceable savage phase. In this stage, communities are small, sedentary, and lack a differentiated leisure class; economic distinctions are absent, and the "[[pecuniary-emulation]]" that drives [[conspicuous-consumption]] in later stages has not yet emerged. Veblen cites the Andaman tribes alongside the Todas of the [[nilgiri-hills]] as ethnographic evidence that the leisure class is not a universal or original feature of human society but an institution that arises only after a certain degree of economic development and [[predatory-stage]]. (001-006)

Veblen returns to the Andaman example when tracing the emergence of [[ownership]] and the [[invidious-comparison]] between men's and women's work. He notes that in the most archaic communities — the Andaman Islanders among them — the differentiation of function along sex lines, while present, is "relatively slight and inconsequential" compared to the pronounced separation that characterizes later predatory stages. This observation supports his broader argument that gender-based economic distinctions intensify with the development of the leisure class institution. (001-017)

## Related Concepts

- [[todas]] — the Nilgiri Hills tribe cited alongside the Andaman Islanders as exemplars of primitive savagery
