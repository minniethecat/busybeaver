# Elizabeth Cady Stanton (1815–1902)

American suffragist, abolitionist, and a principal organizer of the 1848 Seneca Falls Convention. A leading theorist and strategist of the early women's rights movement in the United States.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen refers to Stanton in Chapter 13, "[[survivals]] of the Non-Invidious Interests," where he examines the position of women under the [[pecuniary-culture]]. In discussing the common-sense view that women's participation in civic or industrial affairs threatens the social order, Veblen deploys an ironic rhetorical gesture — quoting Stanton's own language of indignation, but inversely, to characterize the traditionalist position that he himself is in the pro[[christian-endeavor]]ss of dissecting. (013-023)

The passage appears within Veblen's broader argument that the "good and beautiful scheme of life" — the inherited canons of [[standard-of-decency]] — assigns women a vicarious, ancillary sphere, and that any departure from this assigned role is felt as a "menace to that social order." Veblen is not endorsing this view; he is anatomizing it. The invocation of Stanton — even if "inversely" — signals his awareness of the women's rights discourse of his era and his recognition of it as a counter-force to the traditional scheme of status. (013-023)

## Key Ideas

Stanton's thought is not expounded directly by Veblen, but the rhetorical structure of his reference suggests that he understood her position — that women's subordination was not a natural fact but a social arrangement — to be the foil against which the pecuniary culture's "common sense" defined itself. Her name functions in the text as a metonym for the "New Woman" movement that Veblen discusses at length in this chapter. (013-025)

## References

> "All this fume and froth of 'emancipating woman from the slavery of man' and so on, is, to use the chaste and expressive language of Elizabeth Cady Stanton inversely, 'utter rot.' The social relations of the sexes are fixed by nature." [013-023]
