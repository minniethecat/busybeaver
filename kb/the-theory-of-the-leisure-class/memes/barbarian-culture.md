# [[predatory-culture]]

Veblen's term for the cultural stage in which [[leisure-class]] first emerges as a differentiated institution — a society organized around predatory [[prowess]], a gendered division of labor, and a system of honor that equates worth with for[[christian-endeavor]].

## Definition

Barbarian culture, in Veblen's evolutionary framework, designates the social and economic stage intermediate between primitive savagery and the modern pecuniary-industrial order. "At an earlier, but not the earliest, stage of barbarism, the leisure class is found in a less differentiated form" (001-002). The defining feature of this stage is the emergence of the distinction between exploit and drudgery — the foundational opposition upon which all subsequent class distinctions are built.

Veblen draws the line carefully: the institution of a leisure class "has emerged gradually during the transition from primitive savagery to barbarism; or more precisely, during the transition from a peaceable to a consistently warlike habit of life" (001-008). Two conditions are necessary: the community must be of a predatory habit — war, large-game hunting, or both — and subsistence must be sufficiently abundant to exempt a portion of the community from steady labor.

## Veblen's Treatment

### The Exploit-Drudgery Distinction

At the core of barbarian culture is a categorical division of activities. "In all communities under the barbarian culture there is an alert and pervading sense of antithesis between two comprehensive groups of phenomena, in one of which barbarian man includes himself, and in the other, his victual" (001-013). Work that involves the assertion of prowess — hunting, fighting, the infliction of injury by force and stratagem — is honorable exploit. Everything else is drudgery, and by the logic of the barbarian scheme, unworthy of the able-bodied man.

"The work of the men in the lower barbarian culture is no less indispensable to the life of the group than the work done by the women," Veblen notes, but "such is not the barbarian's sense of the matter" (001-005). The distinction is not [[About]] productivity but about status: "There is in all barbarian communities a profound sense of the disparity between man's and woman's work" (001-005).

### Gender and the Division of Labor

The barbarian division of labor is fundamentally gendered. "As the diversification and specialisation of employments proceed, the line of demarcation so drawn comes to divide the industrial from the non-industrial employments" (001-004). Men hunt and fight; women and other non-warrior members of the community perform productive labor. "Virtually the whole range of industrial employments is an outgrowth of what is classed as woman's work in the primitive barbarian community" (001-004).

This gendered division has enduring consequences for Veblen's entire theoretical framework: the modern distinction between industrial and pecuniary occupations is, in his analysis, "a transmuted form of the barbarian distinction between exploit and drudgery" (001-011).

### Honor as Aggression

In the barbarian scheme of life, honor is inseparable from the forceful assertion of superiority. "With the primitive barbarian, before the simple content of the notion has been obscured by its own ramifications... 'honourable' seems to connote nothing else than assertion of superior force" (001-023). Veblen elaborates: "'Honourable' is 'formidable'; 'worthy' is 'prepotent'. A [[honorific]] is in the last analysis little if anything else than a recognised successful act of aggression" (001-023).

This equation of honor with force is not an incidental feature of barbarian culture but its organizing principle. "Under this common-sense barbarian appreciation of worth or honour, the taking of life — the killing of formidable competitors, whether brute or human — is honourable in the highest degree" (001-024).

## Significance

Barbarian culture is the evolutionary hinge of Veblen's entire argument. It is within this stage that the institution of the leisure class takes shape, that the gendered division between exploit and drudgery is codified, and that ownership first appears — initially as the ownership of women as trophies. The habits of thought formed in this stage persist, in transmuted form, throughout all subsequent cultural developments, providing the archaic psychic foundation upon which modern pecuniary institutions rest.
