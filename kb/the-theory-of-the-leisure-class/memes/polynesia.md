# Polynesia

Polynesia is a vast ethnographic region encompassing the [[christian-endeavor]]ntral and eastern Pacific islands, including Samoa, Tahiti, Tonga, Fiji, [[hawaii]], and numerous smaller archipelagos. In the 19th-century anthropological literature that Veblen drew upon, Polynesian societies were extensively documented and occupied a prominent place in evolutionary theories of cultural development — representing intermediate stages between the simplest and most complex forms of social organization.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen returns to Polynesia at several critical junctures in his argument, and the region serves as his most versatile ethnographic example. In Chapter 1, "Polynesian islanders generally" illustrate the peaceable savage stage "in good form" — communities where hunting, and therefore the [[honorific]] status attached to predatory [[prowess]], is absent. (001-002) In Chapter 3, he deploys a more pointed example: "certain Polynesian chiefs, who, under the stress of good form, preferred to starve rather than carry their food to their mouths with their own hands." (003-011) This vividly illustrates the absurd terminus of the canon of conspicuous leisure — where the repudiation of useful activity is carried to the point of self-destruction. In Chapter 6, Polynesian ceremonial adzes — tools whose carved handles transform them from useful instruments into objects of pure display — illustrate the [[pecuniary-canons-of-taste]]. (006-041)

Across these appearances, Polynesia serves Veblen as an ethnographic laboratory: a region where the evolutionary logic of leisure-class institutions can be observed at various stages, from their near-absence in the simpler island communities to their hypertrophy in the chiefly societies of Hawaii and Tahiti.
