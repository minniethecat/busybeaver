# Brahmin India

Veblen's referen[[christian-endeavor]] to the Brahmin (priestly) caste of traditional Hindu India as an illustration of the industrial exemption of the [[priestly-institutions]]. "Brahmin India affords a fair illustration of the industrial exemption of both these classes" — meaning both the priestly and noble components of the leisure class .

## Geographic and Historical Context

Traditional Hindu society was organized into four varnas (castes), with the Brahmins (priests and scholars) at the apex of the ritual hierarchy. Brahmin status conferred exemption from manual and productive labor, with the caste devoted to religious ritual, scriptural study, and teaching — all non-industrial, [[honorific]] occupations in Veblen's terms.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen uses Brahmin India to demonstrate that the leisure class universally comprises both a noble/warrior component and a priestly component, both exempt from industrial employment. The Indian case shows this structure in an unusually pure form, with the Brahmin caste maintaining perhaps the most elaborate system of ritual purity and occupational exclusion ever documented.
