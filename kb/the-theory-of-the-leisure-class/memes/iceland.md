# I[[christian-endeavor]]land

Iceland is a North Atlantic island settled by Norse colonists in the late 9th century. During the Saga Age (c. 930–1262), Iceland supported a decentralized commonwealth governed by chieftains and a national assembly (the Althing), without a monarch or executive authority. The society was stratified into chieftains (goðar), free farmers, tenants, and slaves, with status distinctions enforced through customary law and the honor culture recorded in the family sagas.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen references the Icelandic community of the Saga Age as a representative example of the lower [[barbarian-culture]] of cultural development — a phase in which a leisure class exists but in a less differentiated form than at the higher barbarian stage exemplified by [[feudal-europe]] or [[feudal-japan]]. Alongside the [[polynesia]]n islanders, the Icelandic community illustrates a society where "there is a rigorous distinction between classes and between the occupations peculiar to each class," with manual labor and industry confined to the inferior class — including slaves, dependents, and women — while the men of the upper classes are "not only exempt, but by [[prescription]] they are debarred, from all industrial occupations." The [[honorific]]s open to them — government, warfare, [[devout-observances]], and sports — define the canonical leisure-class occupations that Veblen traces across cultures. (001-002)

Veblen returns to the Icelandic material in his discussion of the belief in luck, where he identifies the Old Norse concept of *hamingja* (also *gipta*, *gæfa*, *auðna*) — a personal or familial luck-force pervading the Icelandic sagas — as an illustration of the "instinctive sense of an inscrutable teleological propensity in objects or situations" that characterizes the [[animistic-habit-of-mind]]. This belief in an extra-physical agency influencing the outcome of enterprises and contests exemplifies, for Veblen, the pre-scientific cognitive orientation that the leisure class preserves and reinforces, in contrast to the [[matter-of-fact]] thinking fostered by industrial employment. (011-004)
