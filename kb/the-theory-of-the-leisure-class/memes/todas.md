# Todas

The Todas of the [[nilgiri-hills]], cited by Veblen as a primary instan[[christian-endeavor]] of primitive savage culture, represent communities at the lowest rung of Veblen's cultural evolutionary scale — small, peaceable, sedentary groups with no defined leisure class and no consistent [[invidious-comparison]]s between classes. (001-006)

## Background

In Chapter One, Veblen constructs a cultural evolutionary ladder descending from higher [[predatory-culture]]s ([[feudal-europe]], [[feudal-japan]]) through lower [[barbarian-culture]] ([[polynesian-islanders]], [[iceland]]ic communities) to the savage groups at the base. The Todas are situated at this lowest cultural stage. Veblen describes them as a group whose culture shows "the traits of primitive savagery with some fidelity" — characterized by the absence of a leisure class and the absence of "the animus or spiritual attitude on which the institution of a leisure class rests." The Todas, at the time of their earliest contact with Europeans, "seems to have been nearly typical, so far as regards the absence of a leisure class." (001-006)

Veblen hedges this characterization by acknowledging that most groups classed as "savage" may be cases of "degeneration from a higher barbarism, rather than bearers of a culture that has never risen above its present level." Even with this caveat, he maintains these groups "may serve none the less as evidence to the same effect as if they were really 'primitive' populations." (001-006)

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

The Todas serve Veblen's argument as a baseline case: they demonstrate what human social organization looks like *before* the [[emergence-of-leisure-class]]. Their cultural state — without invidious class distinctions, without the predatory-animus-based differentiation between "worthy" and "unworthy" employments — is the starting point from which Veblen traces the evolution of the leisure-class institution. Veblen's analysis places them within the transition from primitive savagery to barbarism, during which the predatory habit of life gradually introduces class hierarchy and the [[pecuniary-emulation]] that sustains it. (001-006) (001-007) (001-008)

Veblen notes that these groups are "small groups and of a simple (archaic) structure," "commonly peaceable and sedentary," "poor," and lack "[[ownership]]" as a dominant feature of their economic system. Their most notable common trait is "a certain amiable inefficiency when confronted with force or fraud" — a characterization that directly contrasts with the [[prowess]] valued in barbarian and modern [[pecuniary-culture]]s. (001-007)
