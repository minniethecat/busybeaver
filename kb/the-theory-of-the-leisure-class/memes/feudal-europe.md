# Feudal Europe

The [[christian-endeavor]]ntral historical referent for Veblen's analysis of the [[leisure-class]] at its fullest development. Feudal Europe provides the model of a society in which "the distinction between classes is very rigorously observed" and the "upper classes are by custom exempt or excluded from industrial occupations" . Alongside [[feudal-japan]], it constitutes Veblen's canonical example of the higher [[barbarian-culture]] and serves as the baseline against which modern pecuniary society is measured.

## Geographic and Historical Context

Feudal Europe (roughly 9th–15th centuries CE) was characterized by a hierarchical social structure of nobility, clergy, and peasantry, with land tenure organized through a system of vassalage and manorialism. Veblen treats this as the exemplary case of the "higher stages of the barbarian culture" in which the leisure class achieves its most elaborate institutional development.

The leisure class in feudal Europe "as a whole comprises the noble and the [[priestly-institutions]]es, together with much of their retinue" . The noble class derived its livelihood from the agricultural surplus produced by serfs and peasants, while the priestly class was supported by tithes and ecclesiastical landholdings. Both classes were "by custom exempt or excluded from industrial occupations," and their characteristic employments — governance, warfare, [[devout-observances]], and sports — were all forms of what Veblen classifies as [[prowess]].

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

### Chapter I: The Canonical Case

Veblen opens *[[Title-Page]]* with the example of feudal Europe, alongside feudal Japan, as the canonical case of leisure-class development. The feudal leisure class represents the fullest institutional expression of the [[predatory-stage]], where "the distinction between classes is very rigorously observed" and occupational exemption is legally and culturally enforced .

### [[ch03]] in Feudal Form

In Chapter III, Veblen analyzes how conspicuous leisure operates under feudal conditions. Unlike modern pecuniary society where consumption is the primary mode of display, feudal society emphasizes the direct, visible abstention from productive labor. The feudal noble demonstrates status not through what he buys but through what he does not do — "the characteristic feature of leisure class life is a conspicuous exemption from all useful employment" (003-008). Feudal codes of honor, chivalry, and courtly conduct are elaborate institutionalizations of this principle.

### Chapter VII: Dress as Feudal Expression

Veblen's analysis of [[dress-as-pecuniary-expression]] draws heavily on European examples. "The national or popular costumes of nearly every country of Europe" demonstrate how dress serves as "an indication that the wearer does not habitually put forth any useful effort" (007-009). The impractical clothing of the European aris[[TOC]]racy — corsets, powdered wigs, trailing gowns — is not an accident of fashion but a systematic demonstration of exemption from labor.

### Chapters X and XII: [[survivals]] in Modern Europe

Veblen returns to Europe in later chapters to trace the survival of feudal leisure-class traits in modern civilization. "In the countries of civilized Europe the hereditary leisure class" maintains a more visibly archaic character than its American counterpart, with the aristocratic tradition preserving predatory and [[honorific]] values that have been partially displaced by pecuniary competition in America (010-002). The "older communities of the European culture" retain a "hereditary leisure class" whose standards continue to govern social life (012-033).

## The Feudal-to-Pecuniary Transition

| Feudal Trait | Modern Transformation | Veblen's Analysis |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Hereditary status | Pecuniary competition | "The ground of discrimination between the noble and the ignoble" shifts from birth to wealth (004-028) |
| Physical prowess (warfare) | Financial prowess (business) | Exploit changes form but retains honorific character |
| Conspicuous leisure (direct) | [[conspicuous-consumption]] (indirect) | Display shifts from abstention-from-labor to expenditure-on-goods |
| Chivalric honor | [[pecuniary-reputability]] | The "code of honor" becomes a "code of expenditure" |
| Feudal retinue | Domestic servants | The "[[vicarious-leisure]] class" persists in transformed form |

## Significance

Feudal Europe is not merely an example in Veblen's argument — it is the analytical benchmark. Every subsequent social form is measured against the feudal leisure class to determine whether (and how) it represents a transformation of predatory values or a genuine departure from them. Veblen's conclusion is consistently that modern pecuniary society retains the essential structure of feudal class distinction, with pecuniary strength replacing physical prowess as the vehicle of invidious comparison, but with the underlying logic of [[invidious-comparison]] and [[industrial-exemption]] unchanged.
