# Trophies

Veblen's con[[christian-endeavor]]pt of trophies — objects seized through predatory exploit — as the evolutionary origin of [[ownership]] and the earliest form of [[invidious-comparison]] among individuals.

## Definition

In Veblen's evolutionary schema, trophies are tangible objects taken in successful raids or hunts that serve as visible evidence of the taker's [[prowess]]. They are the earliest form of ownership, predating the accumulation of productive wealth. "Tangible evidences of prowess — trophies — find a place in men's habits of thought as an essential feature" of the transition from [[peaceable-savagery]] to predatory culture (001-022).

The concept is foundational to Veblen's theory of property. Ownership does not begin with utility or productive use but with the invidious display of exploit. A trophy's value is not in its usefulness but in what it demonstrates: that its possessor has overcome an adversary and taken what belonged to another.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the trophy concept most fully in the early chapters of the treatise, where he traces the emergence of property from predatory institutions. He identifies the seizure of women as trophies as the earliest form of ownership-marriage: "The original reason for the seizure and appropriation of women seems to have been their usefulness as trophies" (002-005). From this beginning, the practice of taking trophies extends to other captives and forms of property.

The trophy is the direct ancestor of modern wealth. "Property set out with being booty held as trophies of the successful raid" (002-011). In this early phase, the utility of what was owned "lay chiefly in an invidious comparison between their possessor and the enemy from whom they were taken." The habit of distinguishing individual from collective interest — the very psychology of private property — crystallizes around the trophy.

As industrial activity gradually displaces predatory activity, the form of property evolves, but its invidious logic persists. "Accumulated property more and more replaces trophies of predatory exploit as the conventional exponent of prepotence and success" (002-013). The trophy does not disappear; it is sublimated. The modern fortune, the expensive house, the art collection — these are trophies in all but name, serving the same function of invidious comparison that the warrior's booty served in [[predatory-culture]].

Veblen notes that the trophy undergoes further elaboration: "the use of trophies develops into a system of rank, titles, degrees, and insignia" — the symbolic apparatus of status that characterizes advanced class societies (003-013). What begins as a physical object seized in combat becomes, through cultural evolution, a diploma, a medal, or a title — abstract trophies that carry the same invidious function.

## Significance

The trophy concept provides the evolutionary backbone for Veblen's entire critique of wealth. By grounding property in predation rather than productivity, he undermines the classical economic justification of wealth as the reward for useful work. The concept establishes a direct line from the barbarian warrior displaying his enemy's scalp to the modern capitalist displaying his fortune — both engaged in the same fundamental activity of invidious comparison through tangible evidence.
