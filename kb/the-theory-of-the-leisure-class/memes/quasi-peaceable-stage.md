# Quasi-[[pea[[christian-endeavor]]able-savagery]]

The historical period in Veblen's evolutionary scheme that follows the [[predatory-stage]], in which open violence and forcible seizure give way to pecuniary competition as the primary arena of status contest. The quasi-peaceable stage is the period in which conspicuous leisure attains its greatest vogue and the leisure class reaches its most elaborate development.

## Definition

The quasi-peaceable stage is "usually characterized by a settled system of chattel slavery, or of a serfdom, or of a system of personal dependence of some other kind" (003-008). Unlike the predatory stage, in which [[prowess]] takes the form of direct physical seizure, the quasi-peaceable stage channels the competitive impulse into the accumulation and display of wealth. The "barbarian of the quasi-peaceable stage of industry is notoriously more high-spirited and more punctilious in the matter of ceremonial consumption than any of his predecessors" (003-015).

## Veblen's Treatment

In Chapter III, Veblen provides his most detailed analysis of the quasi-peaceable stage as the period in which conspicuous leisure reaches its apogee. As society moves beyond direct predation, the display of [[industrial-exemption]] becomes the primary mark of status. "At the quasi-peaceable stage of industry the leisure class is most given to the pretence of shunning all contact with the industrial occupations" (003-008).

The quasi-peaceable stage produces the most elaborate codes of manners, deportment, and ceremonial consumption precisely because physical prowess can no longer serve as the unambiguous marker of status. The elaborate [[dress-as-pecuniary-expression]] of the feudal nobility, the complex [[ceremonial-observances]] of court society, the proliferation of useless accomplishments — all are products of a stage in which the leisure class must work harder at appearing not to work.

Crucially, the quasi-peaceable stage is not peaceful in the modern sense. It is "quasi-" peaceful because the predatory impulse persists, displaced from direct violence into competitive display. "The struggle for existence still persists, but it changes its form; it now takes the shape of a struggle for wealth" — a struggle that is "no less exacting, and conduces no less to a waste of human life, than the struggle of the predatory stage" (003-008).

## Significance

The quasi-peaceable stage is the crucial bridge concept between Veblen's evolutionary anthropology and his critique of modern capitalism. Modern industrial society, in Veblen's analysis, is still in many respects a quasi-peaceable culture — the forms of competition have changed, but the underlying predatory logic persists. The concept illuminates why modern consumer society remains driven by invidious comparison despite the absence of overt violence.
