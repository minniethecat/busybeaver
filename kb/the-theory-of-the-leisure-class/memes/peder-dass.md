# Peder Dass

Peder Dass (1647–1707), the Norwegian Baroque poet and [[martin-luther]]an parish priest, is cited by Veblen in Chapter Fourteen of *[[Title-Page]]* as an illustration of a curious ethnographic phenomenon: the belief among nineteenth-[[christian-endeavor]]ntury Norwegian peasants that learned men — particularly clergymen — were masters of the Black Art. Veblen lists Dass alongside Luther, [[philipp-melanchthon]], and [[grundtvig]] as figures whose erudition was so far beyond the comprehension of rural communities that it was assimilated into the category of magic. [0881]

## Background

Peder Dass served as the parish priest of Alstahaug in northern Norway, a remote coastal district where he ministered for nearly two decades. He is best remembered as the author of *Nordlands Trompet* (The Trumpet of Nordland), a topographical poem describing the landscape, people, and economy of northern Norway with vivid detail and warm humor. Written in the 1670s but not published until 1739, the poem became one of the most beloved works of Norwegian Baroque literature, celebrated for its plain-spoken piety, local color, and intimate knowledge of peasant life. Dass also wrote a substantial body of hymnody and religious verse, including versifications of the catechism and biblical narratives, which circulated widely in manuscript and in print.

The folk reputation of Peder Dass as a master of the Black Art — a belief Veblen reports as current among Norwegian peasants as late as the mid-nineteenth century — is a striking example of the transformation of clerical prestige into magical prestige. Dass's Latin learning, his books, his vestments, and his ritual authority placed him in a category of knowledge that was, to the unlettered, essentially indistinguishable from sorcery. The Norwegian folk tradition preserved numerous legends attributing supernatural powers to Dass, including the ability to control winds and sail through the air — beliefs that Veblen's analysis treats not as quaint folklore but as evidence of a persistent cultural logic.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen invokes Peder Dass within a sustained analysis of the origins of learning in priestly and magical practice. His argument, developed over several pages of Chapter Fourteen, is that the social authority of the [[higher-learning]]es is historically continuous with the magical authority of shamans and priests: "the standing of the savant in the mind of the altogether unlettered is in great measure rated in terms of intimacy with the occult forces." The Norwegian peasants' attribution of magical powers to Dass serves as Veblen's illustration of this principle at work in the modern European context — not in a remote primitive culture but in the rural periphery of a Christian nation. [0881]

The figure of Peder Dass is particularly significant for Veblen's argument because the attribution of magical power to a Lutheran clergyman — a representative of the most rationalized and least ritualistic form of Western Christianity — demonstrates the resilience of the [[animistic-habit-of-mind]]. Even where the official theology disenchants the world, the folk perception of learned authority re-enchants it. For Veblen, this is not an error to be corrected but a structural feature of the social organization of knowledge: the gap between the learned and the unlearned is always bridged by the category of the occult. [0881]
