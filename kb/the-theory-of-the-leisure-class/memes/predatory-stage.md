# Predatory Stage

The crucial transitional phase in Veblen's cultural evolution scheme — the shift from [[pea[[christian-endeavor]]able-savagery]] to a warlike, predatory habit of life. This transition establishes the institutional foundations of the [[leisure-class]], private property, and the fundamental distinction between [[honorific]] [[prowess]] and ignoble drudgery that structures all subsequent social organization. The predatory stage is Veblen's answer to the question of origins: where do class distinctions come from, and why does productive labor carry a stigma? (001-008)

## Definition

Veblen defines the predatory stage by a specific "habit of life" — "war or the hunt or both" as the organizing principle of male activity (001-008). For a community to enter the predatory stage, two conditions must be met: "(1) the community must be of a predatory habit of life (war or the hunt or both); that is to say, the men constitute an industrial community, must habitually inflict injury and destruction in carrying out their purpose" and "(2) the subsistence must be obtainable on sufficiently easy terms" to permit exemption of a significant portion of the population from routine labor (001-017).

The crucial distinction is between exploit and industry. In Veblen's terminology, exploit is "the conversion to one's own ends of energies previously directed to some other end by another agent" — it involves the assertion of will over resistant forces, whether human, animal, or natural. Industry, by contrast, is the direct shaping of materials for human use. The predatory stage is when exploit becomes not merely a means of survival but the organizing principle of social honor.

## Historical Emergence

Veblen traces the emergence of the predatory stage in Chapter I through a careful evolutionary argument. In the earliest human communities — what he terms the peaceable savage stage — the distinction between classes does not exist. Men and women perform different economic functions, but neither set of functions carries invidious distinction. The communities "are small and sedentary, they are poor, and [[ownership]] is not a dominant feature of their economic system" (001-006).

The transition occurs "on the transition from peaceable savagery to a predatory phase of life" when "the conditions of the fighting capacity come to receive a higher rating in the community's scheme of life" (001-022). This shift is driven by two linked developments: the adoption of a warlike habit of life (necessitated by competition with other groups) and the accumulation of sufficient surplus to support a class of specialist fighters.

"The predatory phase of culture is attained by a community only when the [[predatory-animus]] has become the habitual and accredited mental attitude of the group — when the fight has become the dominant note in the current theory of life" (001-026). This is not merely a change in economic organization but a transformation of values, habits of thought, and the criteria by which individuals are judged.

## Institutional Consequences

The predatory stage produces three enduring institutional consequences:

### Class Distinction

During the predatory stage, "labour comes to be associated in men's habits of thought with weakness and subjection to a master. It is, therefore, a mark of inferiority, and therefore comes to be accounted unworthy of man in his best estate" (003-002). The division between [[exploit-vs-drudgery]], first established as a distinction between men's and women's work during the transition from peaceable savagery, hardens into a class distinction between those who engage in honorific exploit and those who perform ignoble labor.

### Private Property

The predatory stage transforms ownership from a practical arrangement into an invidious institution. Under predatory conditions, "the accumulation of property at this stage is a matter of exploit — it is a trophy of successful aggression" (002-019). Ownership becomes not merely a means of subsistence but a form of honorific display — "an [[pecuniary-emulation]], a comparison as to relative prowess" (002-015). This is the origin of the link between wealth and social standing that characterizes all subsequent stages.

### The Leisure Class

The leisure class proper emerges during "the predatory stage proper," when "the distinction between the exploit and the drudgery class is very plain" (003-008). The occupations of the leisure class — warfare, governance, religious observance, sports — are all direct expressions of the predatory animus channeled into socially approved forms. "The characteristic feature of leisure class life is a conspicuous exemption from all useful employment," and this exemption acquires its honorific character during the predatory stage (003-008).

## The Predatory-to-Quasi-Peaceable Transition

| Aspect | Predatory Stage | [[quasi-peaceable-stage]] |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Primary activity | Warfare, hunting | Pecuniary competition, ownership |
| Form of exploit | Physical (violence) | Economic (accumulation) |
| Class structure | Warrior elite vs. producers | Pecuniary elite vs. industrial workers |
| Display mode | [[trophies]], captives | [[conspicuous-consumption]], [[conspicuous-leisure]] |
| Status basis | Prowess in combat | Pecuniary strength |
| Institutional form | Chieftainship, war band | Property rights, market exchange |

"The quasi-peaceable stage follows the predatory stage proper, the two being successive phases of [[barbarian-culture]]" (003-036). The key continuity is that the predatory animus — the impulse toward invidious comparison and dominance — persists through the transition, merely changing its vehicle from physical violence to pecuniary competition.

## Significance

The predatory stage is the pivot of Veblen's evolutionary theory. Without it, the link between class distinction and exemption from labor would be inexplicable — there would be no reason why productive work should carry a stigma. By grounding the leisure class in the predatory habit of life, Veblen locates the origins of modern social hierarchy not in efficiency or natural talent but in a specific historical transformation — the shift from peaceable cooperation to warlike competition. This historicization is the core of his critique: institutions that present themselves as natural, inevitable, or divinely ordained are in fact the contingent products of a particular evolutionary trajectory.
