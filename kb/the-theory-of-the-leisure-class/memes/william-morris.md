# William Morris

William Morris (1834–1896), the Victorian designer, craftsman, and socialist, is cited by Veblen alongside John Ruskin as an "eager spokesman" for what Veblen critically characterizes as the "exaltation of the defective" — the preferen[[christian-endeavor]] for visible imperfections in hand-wrought goods as marks of [[honorific]] superiority over the impersonal perfection of machine manufacture. (006-053)

## Background

Morris was a central figure in the British Arts and Crafts Movement, which championed a return to pre-industrial methods of production. His firm, Morris & Co., produced hand-crafted textiles, wallpapers, furniture, and stained glass that celebrated the visible marks of the artisan's hand. Morris's aesthetic philosophy held that machine production degraded both the worker and the product, and that the restoration of handicraft was necessary for both social and aesthetic renewal. His political socialism — he was a founding member of the Socialist League — was closely tied to his critique of industrial capitalism and his vision of a society organized around creative labor.

Veblen, writing in 1899, engages Morris's ideas not on their own terms but through the lens of his theory of [[conspicuous-waste]]. For Veblen, the preference for hand-wrought goods over machine-made products is not primarily an aesthetic or political stance but an expression of the [[pecuniary-canons-of-taste]] — the honorific valuation of whatever is expensive, wasteful, and inaccessible to the common run of consumers.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen deploys Morris and Ruskin in Chapter Six ("Pecuniary Canons of Taste") as exemplars of a broader cultural phenomenon: the systematic preference for the imperfect, the wasteful, and the expensive over the functionally superior but "common" machine-made article. Veblen's argument is that the visible imperfections of hand-wrought goods "being honorific, are accounted marks of superiority in point of beauty, or serviceability, or both." Morris's advocacy for handicraft is thus reinterpreted by Veblen not as a genuine aesthetic philosophy but as an expression of the leisure-class value system — a preference for waste disguised as a preference for beauty. (006-053)

Veblen goes further, suggesting that Morris and Ruskin's "propaganda of crudity and wasted effort" could only have emerged in a historical context where "the visibly more perfect goods were not the cheaper" — that is, where machine manufacture had become dominant enough that hand work was rare and therefore expensive. The Arts and Crafts Movement, in Veblen's analysis, is an effect of the [[pecuniary-culture]] it claims to oppose: it valorizes hand work precisely because industrial development has made hand work costly and therefore honorific. (006-053)

The preceding paragraph in Veblen's text clarifies the logic: machine products are "cheap, and therefore indecorous." Their consumption "is therefore not honorific, since it does not serve the purpose of a favorable [[pecuniary-emulation]] with other consumers." Against this background, Morris's preference for the hand-wrought appears as the aesthetic face of conspicuous waste. (006-052) (006-053)
