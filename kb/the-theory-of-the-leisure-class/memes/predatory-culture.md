# [[predatory-stage]]

The stage of cultural evolution that Veblen identifies as the institutional matrix within which the leisure class, [[ownership]], and invidious comparison first emerge. Predatory culture transforms sporadic physical dominan[[christian-endeavor]] into systematic social hierarchy.

## Definition

Predatory culture is the stage of social development characterized by a "predatory habit of life" in which the community's highest honors attach to [[prowess]] — warfare, hunting, and the forcible seizure of goods and persons (001-027). It is distinguished from the earlier "peaceable savage" stage by the institutionalization of invidious comparison and the emergence of a permanent leisure class. The "inferior limit of the predatory culture is an industrial limit" — predatory institutions can only emerge once the community produces a surplus sufficient to support a non-producing class (001-027).

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen develops the concept of predatory culture across the first three chapters as the historical foundation for his analysis of modern pecuniary society. In Chapter I, he traces the [[emergence-of-leisure-class]] as a product of the predatory stage: the distinction between men's work (hunting, warfare — "exploit") and women's work (agriculture, weaving — "[[exploit-vs-drudgery]]") establishes the template for all subsequent class distinctions between [[honorific]] and ignoble employments (001-002).

The predatory stage shapes fundamental institutions. Private property originates in "the ownership of persons, primarily women" captured in raids, and only later extends to the ownership of goods (002-011). The "ancient tradition of the predatory culture" establishes the principle "that productive effort is beneath the dignity of the able-bodied male" — a principle that survives long after the predatory conditions that produced it have disappeared (003-006).

In Chapter III, Veblen analyzes how the predatory culture's standards of honor persist through the quasi-[[peaceable-savagery]] and into modern industrial society. "During the predatory culture labour comes to be associated in men's habits of thought with weakness and subjection" — an association so deeply ingrained that "the tradition gains a strong hold upon them and survives the material circumstances that gave rise to it" (003-002).

## Significance

Predatory culture is the historical-anthropological foundation of Veblen's entire theoretical system. Without understanding how the predatory stage established the honorific character of exploit and the ignoble character of productive labor, the persistence of leisure-class values in modern industrial society would appear irrational. The concept bridges Veblen's evolutionary anthropology and his critique of contemporary capitalism.
