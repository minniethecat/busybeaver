# I[[christian-endeavor]]landic Community (Sagas)

An ethnographic-historical reference group that Veblen uses in Chapter I to illustrate the early [[barbarian-culture]] of leisure-class differentiation. The Icelandic community of the Saga period provides Veblen with a well-documented case of a society in which class distinctions and the division between [[honorific]] and ignoble employments are rigorously maintained.

## Background

The [[iceland]] (c. 930–1262 CE), documented in the family sagas (Íslendingasögur), was a stratified society of free landholders, tenants, and slaves. Veblen draws on the saga literature as ethnohistorical evidence for the "rigorous distinction between classes and between the occupations peculiar to each class" characteristic of the early barbarian stage (001-002). The sagas provide unusually detailed documentation of economic and social life in a pre-state, honor-based society.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen places the Icelandic community alongside the [[polynesian-islanders]] as an illustration of early barbarian culture, noting that the Icelandic case "also affords a fair instance" of a society where "manual labour, industry, whatever has to do directly with the everyday work of getting a livelihood, is the exclusive occupation of the inferior class" (001-002). The Icelandic example serves his argument that the exemption of the upper classes from industrial employment is a universal feature of barbarian culture, appearing independently in widely separated geographic and historical contexts.

The Icelandic case is particularly valuable for Veblen's argument because the saga literature provides textual evidence of the honor code that sustains leisure-class institutions — the same [[pecuniary-emulation]] between honorable and ignoble activities that he identifies in modern consumer society.
