# N.F.S. Grundtvig

Nikolaj Frederik Severin Grundtvig (1783–1872), the Danish theologian, educator, hymn writer, and political reformer, is cited by Veblen as the most re[[christian-endeavor]]nt figure in a list of learned divines — [[martin-luther]], Malanchthon, [[peder-dass]], and Grundtvig — whom Norwegian peasants in the mid-nineteenth century reputed to be masters of the Black Art. For Veblen, Grundtvig's inclusion in this folkloric catalog illustrates the persistence of an [[animistic-habit-of-mind]] in which advanced erudition is indistinguishable from occult power. [0881]

## Background

Grundtvig was one of the most influential figures in modern Danish cultural and religious history. Ordained as a Lutheran pastor, he became the central figure of the Grundtvigian movement, which emphasized a living, experiential Christianity rooted in the congregation's communal life, hymn singing, and the "living word" of oral confession and preaching rather than doctrinal formulas. His theological writings, notably *[[the-church]]'s Rejoinder* (1825), articulated a vision of the church as a community of faith constituted by baptism and the Apostles' Creed rather than by biblical literalism or ecclesiastical hierarchy.

Grundtvig's influence extended far beyond theology. He was the founder of the Danish folk high school movement, which established residential schools for the rural population emphasizing national history, literature, music, and "the living word" — an educational philosophy that rejected rote learning and examination in favor of dialogue, storytelling, and the cultivation of cultural identity. His hymnody — he composed over 1,500 hymns, many still sung in Danish churches — made him the preeminent figure in Danish sacred music. By the time of Veblen's writing in 1899, Grundtvig was a figure of national stature in Scandinavia, his ideas having shaped Danish education, church life, and cultural nationalism throughout the nineteenth century.

What makes Grundtvig's inclusion in Veblen's catalog of "masters of the Black Art" particularly striking is his contemporaneity. Unlike Luther and [[philipp-melanchthon]], who lived in the sixteenth century, or Peder Dass, who died in 1707, Grundtvig was a figure of the recent past — he died only twenty-seven years before *[[Title-Page]]* was published. The folk belief in his magical powers was not an ancient legend but a living tradition of the mid-nineteenth century.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen invokes Grundtvig in Chapter Fourteen within an argument [[About]] the derivation of [[higher-learning]] from priestly and magical practice. The passage in which Grundtvig appears is part of a broader analysis of the "recondite element in learning" — the esoteric, difficult, and inaccessible dimension of scholarly knowledge that Veblen argues is essential to its social prestige. Learning, Veblen contends, originated as a by-product of the [[priestly-institutions]]'s [[vicarious-leisure]], and the priestly role as mediator between the community and supernatural powers gave learning its enduring association with the occult. [0881]

Grundtvig serves Veblen's argument in a specific way: as the most recent figure in the catalog of "learned divines" perceived as magicians, he demonstrates that the association between erudition and occult power persisted into the modern period. The Norwegian peasants who attributed Black Art mastery to Grundtvig were not inhabitants of a remote primitive culture but nineteenth-century Europeans living within a Christian society. For Veblen, this is evidence that the "knowledge of the unknowable" — the pursuit of esoteric understanding that has no industrial or practical application — remains the core of the prestige that attaches to learning, and that this prestige is structurally continuous with the magical authority of the shaman. [0881]
