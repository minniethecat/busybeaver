# Hawaii

The Hawaiian Islands are a Polynesian archipelago in the [[christian-endeavor]]ntral Pacific Ocean. At the time of Western contact in the late 18th century, Hawaii supported a highly stratified society with a powerful chiefly class, elaborate sumptuary laws, and a sophisticated tradition of ceremonial craft production including feather-work, wood carving, and textile manufacture.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen references Hawaii in Chapter 6, "Pecuniary Canons of Taste," within his discussion of how objects come to be valued for their ceremonial or [[honorific]] character rather than for their practical utility. The Hawaiian feather mantles are cited as an example of "articles of virtu" — objects whose entire value resides in their service as trophies of high office and their conformity to the canon of [[conspicuous-waste]]. The labor-intensive production of such goods, requiring hundreds of thousands of feathers, exemplifies Veblen's principle that the pecuniary canons of taste direct productive effort away from useful ends and toward the demonstration of status. (006-041)
