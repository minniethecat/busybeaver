# Matter-of-Fact

The "matter-of-fact" habit of mind is, in Veblen's analysis, the distinctive cognitive orientation fostered by modern industrial occupations — a dispassionate, cause-and-effect mode of reasoning that stands in systematic opposition to the animistic, personified, and [[christian-endeavor]]remonial thinking of earlier cultural stages. It is the mental counterpart of the machine process: where the machine operates through impersonal mechanical sequence, the matter-of-fact temper apprehends the world through the same lens of causation without purpose.

## Veblen's Treatment

### Definition and Character

The matter-of-fact temper is, Veblen argues, a habit of mind that "recognizes the value of mechanical sequence and impersonal causation" (012-015). It is fundamentally non-animistic: it does not personify natural phenomena or attribute purpose to events, but instead apprehends them as links in chains of cause and effect. Veblen treats this habit of thought not as an innate human faculty but as a cultural product, slowly cultivated by the discipline of machine industry over generations.

The matter-of-fact frame of mind is characterized by skepticism toward received authority, resistance to ceremonial [[prescription]], and a preference for empirical verification over tradition. It is, in Veblen's terms, "iconoclastic" — it dissolves the aura of sacredness and naturalness that surrounds established institutions, exposing them as contingent products of historical circumstances rather than expressions of eternal truth.

### Origins in the Industrial Process

Veblen locates the origin of the matter-of-fact temper in the daily experience of industrial workers. The person "whose occupation approaches the mechanical industries" is subjected to an "enforced cognizance of matter-of-fact sequence" (012-041). The machine process disciplines the mind: it tolerates no appeals to authority, accepts no ceremonial exceptions to physical law, and rewards only the accurate prediction of cause-and-effect relationships.

> "The discipline of the machine process inculcates a habit of thinking in terms of causation, and this habit of thinking in terms of impersonal sequence has, in the course of modern industrial development... come to be the dominant factor in the guidance of the affairs of modern life." [012-036]

This discipline is cumulative and intergenerational. As the industrial process becomes more complex and pervasive, the "ready appreciation of matter-of-fact sequence gains proportionately" (011-009). Veblen argues that the growth of the matter-of-fact temper is not merely an individual psychological phenomenon but a broad cultural transformation — a shift in the "common-sense" apprehension of the world that affects even those not directly employed in mechanical industries.

### Contrast with Animistic and Anthropomorphic Thinking

Veblen develops the matter-of-fact concept largely through contrast with its opposite: the animistic habit of mind. Animistic thinking personifies natural phenomena, attributes purpose and intention to events, and interprets the world through the lens of personal agency rather than impersonal causation. This mode of thought, Veblen argues, is characteristic of earlier cultural stages (the predatory and pre-predatory) and persists in modern society among [[leisure-class]] and those insulated from the discipline of machine industry.

> "The habits of thought that rule in the modern industrial processes are, in their scope and method, impersonal and matter-of-fact; while the habits of thought that rule in the ceremonial and leisure-class employments are, in an eminent degree, personal, anthropomorphic, and conventional." [012-015]

The two habits of thought are not merely different but actively antagonistic. The matter-of-fact temper "lowers productive efficiency" if the worker clings to animistic alternatives, because animistic thinking introduces considerations of propriety, status, and ceremonial fitness that are irrelevant to mechanical causation (011-008). Conversely, the matter-of-fact temper erodes the sense of sacredness and propriety that sustains ceremonial institutions — it is, Veblen suggests, inherently corrosive to traditional religion, class deference, and the "sense of status" on which leisure-class dominance depends.

### Matter-of-Fact Knowledge and Higher Learning

The most sustained treatment appears in Chapter 14, where Veblen examines the conflict within [[universities]] between the classical-humanistic curriculum and the rising scientific disciplines. The traditional humanities, he argues, cultivate an archaic temperament — valuing anthropomorphism, classical precedent, and "clannish self-complacency" — while the newer learning represents the "matter-of-fact knowledge of things" (014-029), (014-031).

> "Those branches of knowledge which make for efficiency (ultimately productive efficiency) have gradually been gaining ground against those branches which make for a consumptive quasi-scholarly or quasi-artistic leisure." [014-029]

This conflict is not merely academic; it reflects a deeper tension between the leisure-class ideal of culture and the industrial requirement for productive efficiency. The classical curriculum, with its emphasis on dead languages, ancient precedent, and ceremonial form, is functionally adapted to the leisure class — it provides the [[occult-sciences]] that marks class membership without contributing to industrial productivity. The scientific curriculum, by contrast, embodies the matter-of-fact standpoint and threatens the leisure class's monopoly on prestige by making knowledge instrumental rather than [[honorific]].

Veblen notes the pejorative language used by defenders of classical education to dismiss scientific knowledge: the term "sub-human" applied to "matter-of-fact knowledge of mankind and of everyday life" (014-030) reveals, he argues, the invidious basis of the classical ideal — it is precisely because matter-of-fact knowledge is useful, and therefore associated with industrial employment, that it is deemed "lower" than the useless but honorific classical learning.

### The Matter-of-Fact Temper and Modern Institutions

Beyond education, Veblen traces the influence of the matter-of-fact temper across a range of modern institutions. In religion, the spread of matter-of-fact habits of thought produces a "lax allegiance" to doctrinal orthodoxy, particularly among the "town populations engaged in the mechanical industries" (012-041). The matter-of-fact temper makes it increasingly difficult to sustain the anthropomorphic conception of divinity and the elaborate ceremonial apparatus that Veblen identifies as leisure-class survivals within [[the-church]].

In politics and social life, the matter-of-fact standpoint tends to undermine the "sense of status" — the intuitive acceptance of rank and hierarchy as natural and legitimate. Veblen sees this as a slow, uneven process, constantly resisted by the "vested interests" of the leisure class and their institutional allies (the church, the university, the state). The matter-of-fact habit of mind does not produce revolution; it produces a gradual erosion of deference, a quiet skepticism toward authority that accumulates across generations.

### Limits and Ambivalence

Veblen does not present the matter-of-fact temper as an unqualified good. It is "iconoclastic" and "inimical to all tradition" (014-032) — qualities that may serve industrial efficiency but that also dissolve the social bonds maintained by ceremonial and ritual. Moreover, the matter-of-fact temper, taken to its extreme, produces a "colorless" and "unimaginative" outlook that Veblen associates with the narrow utilitarianism of the industrial classes.

There is also a tension within Veblen's own argument. The matter-of-fact temper is the cognitive product of the machine process, yet Veblen also identifies an "instinct of workmanship" — a natural human propensity for effective, goal-directed action — that predates the machine age. The relationship between the instinct of workmanship and the matter-of-fact habit of mind is not fully clarified: the former seems to be a native human endowment, the latter a culturally specific expression of it. This ambiguity reflects a broader tension in Veblen's thought between evolutionary determinism (habits of thought as products of institutional environment) and instinct psychology (habits of thought as expressions of innate human propensities).

## Significance

The matter-of-fact concept is central to Veblen's evolutionary account of modern society for several reasons:

1. **Cognitive foundation of social change** — the concept provides the mechanism by which economic institutions (the machine process) reshape mental habits, which in turn reshape social institutions. It is the cognitive link in Veblen's materialist chain of causation.

2. **Explanation of class divergence** — the uneven distribution of the matter-of-fact temper (concentrated among industrial workers, attenuated in the leisure class) explains why different classes think differently and why the leisure class remains attached to archaic habits of thought — they are insulated from the discipline that would erode them.

3. **Institutional analysis of education** — the conflict between classical and scientific curricula is, in Veblen's hands, not a debate [[About]] pedagogical method but an episode in the larger struggle between leisure-class and industrial values, each expressed in the cognitive habits appropriate to its economic function.

4. **Secularization thesis** — the matter-of-fact concept provides Veblen with a non-theological explanation for the decline of religious devotion: it is not that people become more rational, but that the daily experience of machine industry makes anthropomorphic thinking increasingly difficult to sustain.
