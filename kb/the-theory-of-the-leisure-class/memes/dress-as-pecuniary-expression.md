# Dress as Pecuniary Expression

Veblen's analysis of clothing as perhaps the most transparent and ubiquitous form of conspicuous consumption — a domain in which the canons of waste, expensiveness, and novelty operate with particular for[[christian-endeavor]] and visibility.

## Definition

Dress, in Veblen's framework, serves a dual purpose: it protects the body and it displays the wearer's pecuniary standing. The latter function, he argues, dominates the former. The "dress of women" in particular is "the most striking example of the working of the canon of conspicuous waste" because women's clothing has historically been designed to demonstrate that the wearer is exempt from productive labor — fabrics too delicate for work, styles too constraining for movement, and a pace of fashion change that makes last season's dress a visible mark of pecuniary inadequacy (007-002).

## Veblen's Treatment

Chapter VII ("Dress as an Expression of the [[pecuniary-culture]]") is devoted entirely to this concept. Veblen identifies three principles that govern dress: the canon of expensiveness (clothing must visibly cost money), the canon of novelty (clothing must be current, demonstrating that the wearer can afford constant replacement), and the canon of ineptitude (clothing must demonstrate the wearer's unfitness for productive labor).

The principle of expensiveness is the most fundamental. "Our dress, in order to serve its purpose as an evidence of our ability to pay, should be not merely expensive but should also make plain to all observers that the wearer is not engaged in any kind of productive labor" (007-002). This is why high heels, long skirts, delicate fabrics, and elaborate hairstyles persist in women's fashion — they visibly impede physical work.

The canon of novelty, which Veblen identifies as a derivative of the canon of expensiveness, drives the constant turnover of fashion. "The requirement of novelty is an outgrowth of the canon of expensiveness" — wearing last year's style signals that one cannot afford this year's, which is tantamount to advertising a decline in pecuniary standing. The fashion cycle thus functions as a mechanism for making visible the ability to continually expend.

The third principle, the canon of ineptitude, applies most strictly to women's dress. The "spiritual" function of women's clothing is "to demonstrate that the wearer's life is a life of leisure, or that she is, directly or indirectly, an exponent of [[vicarious-leisure]] or of vicarious consumption performed by the household" (007-002). The more a garment disables the wearer, the more effectively it performs its status-display function.

## Significance

Dress as pecuniary expression is Veblen's most concrete and accessible application of his theoretical framework, and it remains one of the most cited analyses in fashion theory and the sociology of consumption. The concept's explanatory power extends to contemporary phenomena: the luxury goods market, fast fashion's paradox (cheap copies of expensive styles), and the semiotics of professional dress codes.
