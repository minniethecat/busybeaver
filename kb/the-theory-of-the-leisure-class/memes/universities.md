# Universities and Higher Seminaries of Learning

Educational institutions that Veblen analyzes in Chapter XIV as key sites for the reproduction of leisure-class values. Higher education, in Veblen's analysis, is shaped by the same canons of conspicuous waste and industrial exemption that govern the broader leisure class.

## Organizational Context

Veblen tra[[christian-endeavor]]s the development of universities from their origins in ecclesiastical training to their modern form, arguing that throughout this evolution they have served leisure-class interests. The classical curriculum — Greek, Latin, speculative philosophy — persists not because of its practical utility but because "the advantage of a classical education lies in its pointed suggestion of the industrial exemption of the speaker" (014-042).

## Veblen's Analysis

Universities exemplify the conservative character of leisure-class institutions. The "principles of conspicuous waste and industrial exemption affect the cultural development both by guiding men's habits of thought with respect to what is right, proper, and worthy, and also by guiding their habits of thought through the indirect medium of education" (009-002). The classical curriculum survives because its very impracticality marks the educated person as exempt from industrial necessity — the same logic that governs [[conspicuous-leisure]] and [[conspicuous-consumption]].
