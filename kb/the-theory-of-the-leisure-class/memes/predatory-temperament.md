# Predatory Temperament

The enduring psychological disposition toward aggressive competition, [[prowess]], and dominan[[christian-endeavor]] — a vestige of humanity's predatory evolutionary past that Veblen argues persists into modern pecuniary society.

## Definition

Predatory temperament is Veblen's term for the cluster of aggressive, competitive, and dominance-seeking psychological traits that originated in the [[predatory-stage]] of human cultural evolution and that continue to shape behavior in modern industrial society. It stands in opposition to the instinct of workmanship — the peaceful, productive, and serviceable inclination. Where the instinct of workmanship seeks efficient outcomes, the predatory temperament seeks victory, mastery, and the subjugation of others. Veblen treats it not as a moral failing of individuals but as an anthropological inheritance: a psychological complex that was adaptive in an earlier cultural stage but has become maladaptive under modern industrial conditions.

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen identifies the predatory temperament as a "secondary expression" of the deeper predatory animus, manifesting as "veneration for the archaic occupations and the archaic scheme of life" (006-056). This temperament is not evenly distributed across society; it varies by occupation and social position. [[leisure-class]], being exempt from industrial employments that would discipline the instinct of workmanship, tends to preserve and accentuate the predatory temperament. Veblen observes that "the leisure class is in great measure sheltered from the stress of those economic exigencies which drive the working classes to a habitual recognition of the industrial attitude" (009-008).

The predatory temperament is sustained by specific institutions and occupations. Veblen identifies warfare, sports, government, and religious observance as domains that foster the predatory disposition. "The occurrence of non-predatory temperament with the class at that stage would mean a departure from the normal" (013-007); the predatory temperament is the norm for the leisure class, and its absence would be anomalous. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: the leisure class selects for and cultivates predatory traits, and these traits in turn justify the leisure class's privileged position.

The modern survival of the predatory temperament is most visible in sports and athletic pursuits. Veblen writes that "these manifestations of the predatory temperament are all to be classed under the head of exploit; they are partly simple and unreflected expressions of an attitude of emulative ferocity" (010-014). Sports, in this analysis, are not harmless recreation but the institutionalized expression of a predatory psychological inheritance. The "inclination to sports" is evidence "of the survival of the predatory temperament and habits" (010-027).

Veblen argues that the persistence of the predatory temperament represents a drag on industrial efficiency and social progress. The predatory disposition "does not lead itself to all the purposes of modern life" (009-009); it is fundamentally at odds with the requirements of a complex industrial society that depends on cooperation, technical precision, and impersonal efficiency.

## Significance

The concept of predatory temperament is central to Veblen's evolutionary theory of institutions. It provides the psychological mechanism through which archaic cultural patterns persist into modernity — the predatory habits of thought, embedded in temperament, resist the rationalizing force of industrial employment. This concept bridges Veblen's anthropology (the stages of cultural evolution) with his critique of modern institutions: the predatory temperament explains why the leisure class values [[honorific]] and exploit-based activities over productive work, and why institutions like sports, religion, and government retain their archaic, non-industrial character. The predatory temperament is the psychological substrate of the entire leisure-class value system.
