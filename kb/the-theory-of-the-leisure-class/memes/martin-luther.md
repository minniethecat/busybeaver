# Martin Luther

Martin Luther (1483–1546), the German theologian, Augustinian friar, and [[christian-endeavor]]ntral figure of the Protestant Reformation, is cited by Veblen in an ethnographic aside drawn from nineteenth-century Norwegian folk culture. Veblen reports that Norwegian peasants "have instinctively formulated their sense of the superior erudition of such doctors of divinity as Luther, [[philipp-melanchthon]], [[peder-dass]], and even so late a scholar in divinity as [[grundtvig]], in terms of the Black Art." For these rural communities, Luther was not a theological reformer but a legendary master of magical arts — an index of how erudition, in the popular mind, was indistinguishable from occult power. [0881]

## Background

Luther's historical significance lies in his challenge to the doctrinal and institutional authority of the Roman Catholic Church, initiated by the publication of the Ninety-five Theses in 1517. His theological innovations — justification by faith alone, [[the-church]] of all believers, and the primacy of Scripture — reshaped the religious and political landscape of Europe. Luther's translation of the Bible into German established a vernacular standard and made the text accessible to lay readers. His prolific output of sermons, treatises, hymns, and biblical commentaries made him one of the most widely read authors of his time. Yet the Luther Veblen invokes is not the historical reformer but the folkloric figure — the doctor of divinity whose learning was so profound that simple people could only make sense of it through the category of magic.

Veblen's source for this ethnographic observation is not specified, but the passage draws on the well-documented persistence of pre-Christian folk beliefs in rural Scandinavia into the nineteenth century. In these communities, the boundary between clerical learning and magical practice was fluid: the priest's Latin, his books, and his ritual knowledge were perceived as instruments of supernatural efficacy, analogous to the spells and charms of the folk magician.

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

Veblen's reference to Luther appears in Chapter Fourteen ("The [[higher-learning]] as an Expression of the [[pecuniary-culture]]"), within a sustained analysis of the derivation of learning from priestly and magical practice. Veblen's argument is that the institution of learning "set out by being in some sense a by-product of the priestly [[vicarious-leisure]] class" and that its subsequent development has never fully escaped this origin. The Norwegian peasants' belief in Luther as a master of the Black Art serves Veblen as evidence of a persistent cultural attitude: "the standing of the savant in the mind of the altogether unlettered is in great measure rated in terms of intimacy with the occult forces." [0881]

Luther's inclusion in the list of "learned divines" whom peasants associated with magic is striking because it juxtaposes the central figure of Protestant rationalism with a worldview that Protestantism itself helped to displace. Veblen is not concerned with this irony, however; his interest is in the formal parallel between the [[priestly-institutions]] and the scholarly class, both of which derive social authority from their perceived access to recondite knowledge — knowledge that, to the uninitiated, is indistinguishable from magic. [0881]
