# Prowess

Prowess, in Veblen's usage, is the complex of predatory aptitudes — physical for[[christian-endeavor]], strategic cunning, and the capacity for successful aggression — that forms the primary standard of honor and social esteem in barbarian (predatory) cultures. It stands in systematic opposition to "drudgery," the industrial and productive labor that the [[predatory-stage]] codes as unworthy and servile. The concept is fundamental to Veblen's evolutionary account of [[leisure-class]] because it provides the historical origin of both the invidious distinction between occupations and the institution of ownership.

## Veblen's Treatment

### Prowess as the Criterion of Honor in [[predatory-culture]]

Veblen develops the concept of prowess across the opening chapters of *[[Title-Page]]* as the anthropological pivot between the peaceable savage stage and the barbarian stage of cultural development. In the earliest human communities, he argues, there is no leisure class; economic activity is undifferentiated and no distinction is drawn between honorable and dishonorable occupations. The emergence of a leisure class coincides with the emergence of the distinction between "exploit" — activities involving the exercise of prowess, such as warfare, hunting, and governance — and "drudgery" — the everyday labor of producing subsistence, assigned to women and subordinate classes.

> "The distinction between exploit and drudgery is an invidious distinction between employments. Those employments which are to be classed as exploit are worthy, honorable, noble; other employments, which do not contain this element of exploit, and especially those which imply subservience or submission, are unworthy, debasing, ignoble." [001-008]

The barbarian scheme of life draws this distinction "with great rigour and assurance" (001-013). An activity's [[honorific]] character derives entirely from whether it involves an "assertion of prowess," not from any consideration of utility or diligence (001-015). The barbarian man's work, in its "best development," is distinguished by the fact that it involves "a considerable element of exploit" — it is primarily an exercise in "the capacity to take and hold by force" (001-018).

> "The care of the means of life, even the means of life of the able-bodied, falls to the women. The differentiation out of which the leisure class emerges is a differentiation of employments according as the work partakes more immediately of the nature of exploit or of the nature of drudgery." [001-004], [001-006]

### Trophies: Tangible Evidence of Prowess

In the barbarian stage, prowess is displayed through tangible trophies — seized goods, captives, and other "evidences of exploit." Veblen identifies these trophies as the earliest form of property and the evolutionary root of modern ownership. The transition from trophies to property is, for Veblen, a transition in the mode of demonstrating prowess: from the direct seizure of goods to the accumulation of wealth as evidence of competitive success.

> "Tangible evidences of prowess — trophies — find a place in men's habits of thought as an essential feature of the paraphernalia of life. Booty, trophies of the chase or of the raid, come to be prized as conventionally evidence of successful aggression." [001-022]

The display of trophies serves a double function: it is direct evidence of the owner's prowess and, more fundamentally, it satisfies the "[[pecuniary-emulation]]" that drives all status behavior. The trophies serve as "conventionally accepted evidence of successful exploit" (001-022), and the accumulation of such evidence becomes the chief means by which the barbarian man secures his social position. This linkage between prowess-evidence and wealth is one of Veblen's most important theoretical moves: it establishes that property originates not in utility but in the competitive demonstration of predatory success.

### Prowess and the Origins of Ownership

Veblen traces the institution of ownership directly to the exercise of prowess. The earliest form of ownership, he argues, is "the ownership of the women by the able-bodied men of the community" (002-003) — women being among the earliest trophies of successful predatory exploit. From this origin in the seizure of persons, ownership extends to the products of their labor and eventually to all forms of property.

The psychological mechanism at work is emulation: "the propensity for achievement — the [[instinct-of-workmanship]] — and the repugnance to futility" are bent to the service of competitive display (002-009). What drives the accumulation of property, Veblen argues, is not the desire for subsistence or comfort but the desire to demonstrate prowess through the possession of goods that serve as its conventional evidence.

> "So soon as the possession of property becomes the basis of popular esteem, therefore, it becomes also a requisite to that complacency which we call self-respect." [002-012]

### The Persistence of Prowess in Modern Society

Crucially, Veblen argues that the valuation of prowess does not disappear with the advent of modern industrial society. The "predatory aptitudes, habits, and traditions" accumulated through barbarian culture persist as a "cumulative growth" that shapes modern institutions and character types.

In later chapters, Veblen identifies the "martial spirit" — a contemporary expression of the predatory temperament — as particularly pronounced among the leisure class and the lower-class delinquent, and relatively weaker among the industrial classes whose daily work fosters matter-of-fact habits of thought. The modern leisure class, exempted from the discipline of industrial employment, retains in "more or less tempered form" the predatory aptitudes of the barbarian stage (010-002), (010-003).

> "The temperament which inclines men to sports and to devotion to athletic games is substantially the same as that which inclines them to warlike enterprise; and the law of natural selection which has brought this temperament into the position of dominance in the leisure class has also been operative in the propagation and conservation of the warlike type of man in the community." [010-023]

Modern competitive business, sports, and politics all provide channels for the expression of prowess within the framework of [[pecuniary-culture]]. The "predaceous temper" does not vanish; it is redirected. Sports in particular serve as a "conventional outlet" for the predatory impulse that the modern industrial system otherwise suppresses (010-026).

### Prowess and the Instinct of Workmanship

Veblen's analysis of prowess is complicated by its relationship to the "instinct of workmanship." The barbarian's pursuit of prowess is not merely destructive — it channels a genuine impulse toward effective action and achievement. "The distinction between exploit and drudgery," Veblen explains, "is of very ancient date" and is not purely convention but rooted in "the difference between man's sense of the character of the two classes of activity" (001-014). The barbarian admires prowess not merely because it is traditional but because it represents a form of effective, purposive action — albeit one directed toward competitive rather than productive ends.

This ambivalence is central to Veblen's broader argument [[About]] modern economic behavior. The "pecuniary employments" of the modern businessman, he argues, inherit the honorific character of exploit while often being no more productive (in the industrial sense) than the predatory activities of the barbarian. The instinct of workmanship is thus "contaminated" by the predatory tradition: it seeks expression through competitive achievement rather than through productive industry.

## Significance

The concept of prowess is foundational to Veblen's theory of the leisure class for four reasons:

1. **Origin of occupational distinction** — prowess explains the historical origin of the distinction between honorable and dishonorable occupations. This distinction, Veblen argues, continues to structure modern class relations, with "pecuniary" employments retaining the honorific character of exploit while "industrial" employments inherit the stigma of drudgery.

2. **Evolutionary link to property** — prowess provides the evolutionary link between the trophies of barbarian exploit and modern property. Ownership originates not in utility but in the demonstration of predatory success; this genealogical claim is foundational to Veblen's critique of the "natural" right of property.

3. **Persistence of archaic values** — the concept illuminates the psychological mechanism by which archaic values survive into industrial society. The "predatory aptitude" is not eliminated by modern economic conditions but redirected into competitive business, sports, and status display. This is a core instance of Veblen's broader concept of [[survivals]].

4. **Ambivalence of the instinct of workmanship** — prowess reveals the internal tension within Veblen's account of human motivation. The same impulse toward effective action can be channeled into either productive industry (the matter-of-fact habit of mind) or competitive display (the [[predatory-temperament]]). This ambivalence runs through the entire book and is never fully resolved.
