# Prescription

Veblen's use of the term "prescription" to denote the normative authority that customary arrangements acquire simply by virtue of their established duration — a con[[christian-endeavor]]pt central to his explanation of how class distinctions and the leisure-class scheme of life perpetuate themselves.

## Definition

In Veblen's usage, "prescription" refers to the social-psychological process by which existing customs, institutions, and class arrangements acquire the force of moral obligation through long habituation. A practice becomes prescriptive when it is no longer questioned but accepted as natural, inevitable, and right. As Veblen puts it, when a social arrangement is "ancient and unquestioned," it carries the force of prescription (003-002).

The term is borrowed from legal discourse — where prescription establishes rights through long usage — but Veblen applies it broadly to any social convention that has hardened into an apparently natural norm. "Prescription presently seizes upon this conventional arrangement," he writes, transforming a merely convenient custom into a morally obligatory standard (003-010).

## Veblen's Treatment

Veblen deploys the concept throughout the treatise to explain the remarkable durability of class-based norms. The pecuniary standard of living, for instance, derives its binding force not from rational calculation but from prescription: a given scale of expenditure becomes "a matter of propriety, under pain of disesteem and ostracism" once prescription has attached to it (005-003).

The mechanism operates selectively to preserve leisure-class interests. The positive discipline of the leisure-class scheme of life operates "by prescription and by selective elimination" to suppress aptitudes and habits of thought that run counter to its logic (013-004). This is not a conspiracy but an emergent [[ownership]] of the system — customs advantageous to the dominant class gradually acquire prescriptive authority, while alternatives are progressively delegitimized.

Veblen extends the concept even to the most violent social institutions. The feudal noble's "systematic slaughter" of rivals became "a prescription that can not be violated except by a breach of that license which the constituted ruling class feels it must at all hazard defend" (010-017). Here, the prescriptive force transforms aggression into privilege, demonstrating how thoroughly social power can naturalize itself through custom.

Within the domestic sphere, prescription operates to maintain gender hierarchy. The inherited scheme of life "by the divine right of prescription, places the woman in a position" ancillary to the man, making any departure from traditional roles feel unnatural and unwomanly (013-023).

## Significance

Prescription is a key mechanism in Veblen's account of institutional inertia. It explains why class structures persist even when their original economic rationale has disappeared. The concept bridges his theory of social emulation (which explains the spread of norms) with his analysis of conservatism (which explains their persistence). For Veblen, prescription is the psychological cement that holds the edifice of class society together, making historically contingent arrangements appear as facts of nature.
