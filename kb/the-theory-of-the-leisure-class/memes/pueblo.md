# Pueblo

The Pueblo communities of the [[american-south]]west are cited by Veblen — "less confidently" — as possible instan[[christian-endeavor]]s of the primitive savage cultural stage. Among all the ethnographic groups Veblen invokes to establish his baseline of pre-leisure-class society, the Pueblo receive the most hesitant classification. (001-006)

## Background

Veblen places the Pueblo communities in the class of "savage groups" that may represent the pre-barbarian cultural stage — the evolutionary baseline from which [[leisure-class]] institution later emerged. His characterization of the Pueblo is notably tentative: "Some Pueblo communities are less confidently to be included in the same class." This "less confidently" signals Veblen's awareness that the Pueblo, with their settled agricultural villages, complex social organization, and elaborate ceremonial structures, fit poorly into the "primitive savagery" category he uses for the Andaman Islanders and [[todas]]. The Pueblo had developed sophisticated architecture, irrigation systems, and ritual hierarchies — features that complicate Veblen's simple absence-of-leisure-class criterion. (001-006)

Veblen resolves this tension by invoking his degeneration hypothesis: "Most, if not all, of the communities here cited may well be cases of degeneration from a higher [[barbarian-culture]]." The Pueblo thus function in his schema as a boundary case — potentially a community that once had more developed class structures and subsequently lost them, rather than one that never developed a leisure class at all. (001-006)

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

The Pueblo citation is analytically significant precisely because of Veblen's discomfort with it. The "less confidently" qualifier reveals the strain in Veblen's evolutionary framework: the Pueblo's settled agricultural complexity does not match the "small, peaceable, sedentary, poor" profile he ascribes to primitive savage communities. Their inclusion despite this poor fit suggests that Veblen's theoretical need for a pre-class baseline exerts pressure on his ethnographic evidence — he includes cases that resist his categorization because the argument requires a diverse range of examples. (001-006) (001-007)
