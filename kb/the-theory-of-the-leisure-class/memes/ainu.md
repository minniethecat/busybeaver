# Ainu

The Ainu of Yezo (present-day [[yezo]]), cited by Veblen as a further instan[[christian-endeavor]] of primitive savage culture, occupy the same cultural evolutionary position as the [[todas]] and Andaman Islanders — communities at the lowest rung of Veblen's developmental ladder, where no leisure class has yet crystallized. (001-006)

## Background

Veblen places the Ainu in the class of "savage groups" that represent the pre-[[barbarian-culture]] of cultural development. His characterization is drawn from the ethnographic record available at the time of writing (1899), when the Ainu were widely studied as an example of an indigenous hunting-and-gathering society. Veblen groups them alongside the Todas, [[bushman]], and Eskimo as communities "classed as 'savage'" — though he qualifies the designation by noting that most such groups "show traces of regression from a more advanced cultural stage." The Ainu are cited "as a further instance" after the Andaman Islanders and Todas, implying they share the defining features: absence of a leisure class, small-group structure, and a peaceable-sedentary way of life. (001-006) (001-007)

## Role in Veblen's Analysis

The Ainu serve Veblen's argument as a comparative example reinforcing the anthropological baseline for the leisure-class institution. By citing multiple geographically dispersed groups ([[andaman-islands]] in the Indian Ocean, Todas in South India, Ainu in northern Japan, Bushman in southern Africa), Veblen constructs a cross-cultural argument that the absence of a leisure class is not a regional anomaly but a feature of a particular cultural stage. The Ainu contribute to Veblen's broader claim that "the institution of a leisure class has emerged gradually during the transition from primitive savagery to barbarism" — a transition marked by the shift from peaceable to warlike habits of life. (001-006) (001-008)

The Ainu's placement in Veblen's schema also implicitly supports his argument [[About]] the relationship between the predatory habit of life and [[leisure-class]]: groups without consistent warlike organization, like the Ainu in Veblen's characterization, are also those without developed class hierarchies based on [[pecuniary-emulation]]. (001-008)
