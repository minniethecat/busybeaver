# Broncho Bill
> Don't you hear the big spurs jingle?
> Don't you feel the red blood tingle?
> Be it smile or be it frown,
> Be it dance or be it fight,
> Broncho Bill has come to town
> To dance a dance tonight.
>
> (005-001)


[[bill|Broncho Bill]] is the doomed protagonist of [[william-maxwell|William Maxwell]]'s narrative poem "[[The-Dance-at-Silver-Valley|The Dance at Silver Valley]]" (also identified in the attribution simply as "[[bill|Broncho Bill]] and [[belle-mcclure|Belle McClure]]"). A cowboy from the ranch at Beaver Bend, [[bill|Bill]] comes to town for a dance, falls afoul of the gambler [[jim-kershaw|Jim Kershaw]] over the affections of [[belle-mcclure|Belle McClure]], and dies on the dance-hall floor (005-001).

## Character Background

[[bill|Bill]] is introduced as "the broncho buster, from the ranch at Beaver Bend," a man with "Ninety steers and but one life in his hands to spend" (005-001) — a ratio that identifies him as a working cowboy, not a gambler or townsman. The detail is significant: [[bill|Bill]]'s life is measured in cattle, not cards, and his tragedy is that he has entered a world (the dance hall, the love triangle, the gambler's domain) where his cowboy skills offer no protection.

|------|----------|------|
| "The Dance at Silver Valley" | (005-001) | protagonist / victim |

[[bill|Bill]]'s arc in the poem is compressed into a single evening: he arrives "shaved and slick, all his throat is bare" (005-001), dances with [[belle-mcclure|Belle McClure]], threatens [[denver-jim|Jim]] Kershaw ("it is you or me must travel down to hell" (005-001)), and is shot dead. His dying words — "You've finished me; you've gambler's luck; you've won the trick and Belle" (005-001) — acknowledge the fatal asymmetry: the gambler's weapon is the gun, the cowboy's is the [[lasso|rope]].

## Significance

[[bill|Broncho Bill]] embodies the cowboy-as-tragic-figure archetype that runs through Western balladry. Unlike the comic cowboys of the yarns — "[[texas-tom|Texas Tom]]," "[[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" — [[bill|Bill]] is neither ridiculous nor rough-hewn. He is competent (a bronco buster, a man who handles ninety steers), handsome (shaved and slick for the dance), and doomed. His [[death-imagery|death]] is not a cautionary tale but an inevitability in a world where cowboys and gamblers occupy incompatible moral universes. The poem's final image — [[bill|Bill]]'s body lying "white" on the billiard cloth while the dance continues upstairs (005-001) — captures the indifference of the [[the-west|frontier]] social world to individual tragedy.
