## Overview

Bill appears in 5 passages across the anthology as an everyman cowboy figure. In "Broncho vs. Bicycle," he is the bronco rider pitted against the newfangled bicycle in a comic contest of old West versus new technology: "Bill, the broncho buster, / That centaur of the plain" (005-001). Bill's encounters with modernity, [[love|romance]], and danger capture the cowboy's position at the hinge of historical change.

Bill's generic name is significant—unlike named poets ([[Charles-Badger-Clark]], [[James-Barton-Adams]]), Bill represents the anonymous cowboy whose experiences form the anthology's raw material. He is the figure the songs are about, even when he is not the figure who composed them. The name "Bill" itself—plain, unadorned, common to the point of anonymity—makes him a placeholder for the countless working cowboys whose names never made it into the historical record.

## Character Type

Bill represents the working cowboy as opposed to the poet-cowboy or rancher-cowboy. His concerns are immediate and practical: horses, equipment, survival, and the occasional comic mishap. His presence in the anthology grounds the more reflective poems in the daily texture of trail life.

As a character type, Bill belongs to the anthology's gallery of cowboy figures—each representing a different facet of [[the-west|Western identity]]. [[Texas-Tom]] is the storyteller, [[High-Chin-Bob]] the boastful hero, [[Snagtooth-Sal]] the lost beloved. Bill is none of these; he is the competent, unspectacular hand who does the work and sometimes finds himself in absurd situations.

## The Comic Everyman

Bill's most distinctive appearance is in "Broncho vs. Bicycle," where he serves as the vehicle for one of the anthology's most consciously funny poems. The contest between the bronco and the bicycle is a contest between two eras—the horse-powered West of Bill's world and the mechanical future that would soon displace it. Bill's victory in this comic arena is also the poem's way of claiming that cowboy values (strength, skill, authenticity) will outlast the technologies that threaten to replace them.

But the poem's humor cuts both ways: Bill is not merely the triumphant cowboy but also a figure of comedy, his dignity perpetually at risk. This doubleness—heroic and ridiculous at once—is characteristic of the anthology's treatment of cowboy masculinity. The cowboy can laugh at himself, and Bill is often the figure who permits that laughter.

> Don't you hear the big spurs jingle?
> Don't you feel the red blood tingle?
> Be it smile or be it frown,
> Be it dance or be it fight,
> Broncho Bill has come to town
> To dance a dance tonight.
>
> (005-001)

## Symbolic Significance

### The Anonymous Cowboy

Bill's importance to the anthology lies in his ordinariness. The named poets—Clark, Adams, [[Henry-Herbert-Knibbs]], [[Arthur-Chapman]]—are the anthology's voices; Bill is its subject. He represents the thousands of working cowboys who drove the cattle, slept in the bunkhouses, and sang the songs that Lomax sought to preserve. That the anthology gives him a name—even such a plain one—is itself a quiet act of memorial.

### The Cowboy at the Threshold

Bill's encounter with the bicycle in "Broncho vs. Bicycle" positions him at the threshold of modernity. The cowboy who could [[lasso|rope]] a steer and break a bronco now confronts a machine—and wins, but the victory is comic and precarious. By 1916, when the anthology was published, the bicycle (like the automobile that followed) was transforming the same Western landscape that Bill's world depended on. His comic triumph is also the anthology's elegy for a way of life.
