## Overview

E. A. Brinninstool (Earl Alonzo Brinninstool, 1870–1957) was an American cowboy poet, journalist, and historian of the American West who contributed two poems to "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]": "[[The-Ol-Cow-Hawse|The Ol' Cow Hawse]]" (ch048) and "[[The-Disappointed-Tenderfoot|The Disappointed Tenderfoot]]" (ch078). His work bridges the oral cowboy ballad tradition and the self-conscious historical preservation of Western folklore.

Two of his poems appear with his byline:

> o' glory on an Ol' Cow Hawse E. A. Brinninstool. (045-001)

> n tents sich things is done. E. A. Brinninstool. (075-001)

## Life & Context

Born in Warsaw, New York, Brinninstool moved West and worked as a cowboy and rancher before becoming a journalist and historian. He published extensively on Western Americana, including books on Indian wars, [[the-west|frontier]] outlaws, and cowboy life. His firsthand experience as a working cowboy gives his verse an authenticity that distinguishes it from Eastern literary treatments of Western themes.

## Key Appearances in the Anthology

| Poem | Chapter | Theme |
|------|---------|-------|
| "The Ol' Cow Hawse" | 048 | The aging cow horse — a tribute to loyalty and the working partnership between cowboy and mount |
| "The Disappointed Tenderfoot" | 078 | Comic portrait of an Eastern greenhorn's failed attempt at cowboy life |

## Poetic Style

Brinninstool's cowboy verse is grounded in specific, observed detail — the gait of an old horse, the equipment of the trail, the physical comedy of the tenderfoot's failures. His tone ranges from elegiac (in "The Ol' Cow Hawse" (045-001)) to humorous (in "The Disappointed Tenderfoot" (075-001)), reflecting the anthology's broader tonal range between sentiment and comedy.

## Significance

Brinninstool represents the working cowboy who became a writer — not the poet who visited the West as a literary tourist, but the hand who lived the work and then turned to verse (and later, history) to preserve it. His later career as a Western historian underscores the documentary impulse behind even the anthology's comic poems. His contributions (045-001) (075-001) exemplify the insider's perspective that [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] valued in selecting poems for the collection.
