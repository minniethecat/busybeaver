## Overview

"[[lasca|Lasca]]," by [[Frank-Desprez]], is the longest poem in the anthology and arguably its most ambitious single work. Running to over 40 stanzas of galloping anapestic meter, it tells the story of a cowboy's doomed love for a Mexican girl on the [[texas|Texas]] range, culminating in a [[stampede|cattle stampede]] and her heroic [[death-imagery|death]].

> I want free life and I want fresh air!
> And I sigh for the canter after the cattle (010-001)

The poem's central relationship — between the Anglo cowboy narrator and his beloved [[lasca|Lasca]] — explicitly crosses ethnic and cultural boundaries in a way rare for the anthology. [[lasca|Lasca]] is not a distant ideal but a vividly physical presence: she rides, she fights, she dies saving the narrator from a stampede.

> And Lasca! Lasca used to ride
> On a mouse-gray [[mustang]] close to my side (010-001)

## Structure and Meter

The poem's relentless anapestic gallop — three syllables per foot, two unstressed and one stressed — mimics the rhythm of a running horse. This metrical choice is not decoration but mimesis: the poem's sound is its meaning. The long lines and driving rhythm create an unstoppable momentum that matches the poem's themes of wildness, speed, and the inevitable approach of tragedy.

The poem moves through three clear movements: courtship on the range, domestic happiness, and the catastrophic stampede that claims [[lasca|Lasca]]'s life. Each movement increases in pace and tension until the final section, where the meter itself seems to break against the rocks of [[lasca|Lasca]]'s [[death-imagery|death]].

## Key Themes

### Love Across Boundaries

The narrator's love for [[lasca|Lasca]] crosses the line between Anglo and Mexican, cowboy and woman, free range and domesticity. Their courtship takes place entirely within the cowboy's world — on horseback, in the open — rather than in a conventional domestic setting.

### The Stampede as Fate

Like many cowboy poems, "[[lasca|Lasca]]" treats the stampede not as accident but as revelation. The cattle's panic reveals what was always latent in the range's beauty: the constant proximity of violent [[death-imagery|death]]. [[lasca|Lasca]]'s sacrifice — throwing herself between the narrator and the [[stampede|stampeding herd]] — is the poem's moral center, transforming romantic love into heroic self-sacrifice.

### Home and Exile

[[lasca|Lasca]] offers the narrator a vision of domestic happiness on the range: a home, a partner, a settled life. Her [[death-imagery|death]] makes him an eternal exile, condemned to wander the range that was once their shared home. The poem's final stanzas are among the bleakest in the anthology — a vision of the cowboy not as free wanderer but as dispossessed mourner.

## Literary Significance

"[[lasca|Lasca]]" was widely anthologized and memorized in the early twentieth century, becoming one of the most famous cowboy poems of its era. Its combination of galloping meter, tragic [[love|romance]], and vivid Western imagery made it a favorite for recitation and a touchstone for the genre of cowboy poetry. Within Lomax's anthology, it represents the full realization of what cowboy verse could achieve at its most ambitious — not just humorous anecdote or folk song, but sustained narrative of genuine emotional power.
