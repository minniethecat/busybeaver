## Overview

Colorado appears in the poems as both a geographic landmark and a place of origin for the cowboys themselves. "I've swum the Colorado where she runs close down to" (058-001) evokes the great river that gave the state its name—a formidable barrier that tested the cowboy's skill and nerve.

The state figures into the Mackenzie Trail, one of the historic cattle routes that "across the Brazos, Colorado" (066-001) carried herds northward from [[texas|Texas]]. The Colorado River crossing was a milestone on [[trail-driving|the long drive]], its waters a remembered challenge in the cowboy's mental map of the trail.

## The Colorado River

The Colorado River of [[texas|Texas]] (distinct from the larger Colorado River of the Grand [[canyon|Canyon]]) flows southeast across the state, intersecting the cattle trails that carried herds from South [[texas|Texas]] to the northern Plains. When a cowboy boasts "I've swum the Colorado where she runs close down to" (058-001), he invokes the river as a rite of passage—to swim a major river on horseback, perhaps in flood, was to prove one's competence as a cowhand.

The river appears alongside other [[texas|Texas]] waterways in the trail narrative: "Across the Brazos, Colorado, through Concho's broad, fair land" (066-001) maps the route by its river crossings, each name a waypoint on the journey north.

## In the Poems

A Colorado [[puncher|cowpuncher]] contributes directly to the collection: "As sent by Elwood Adams, a Colorado [[puncher|cowpuncher]]" (077-001) introduces one of the poems, grounding the anthology's songs in the lived experience of Colorado ranch hands. The state thus appears not only as a place on the map but as a community of working cowboys whose songs and stories enrich the collection.

Elwood Adams represents the oral tradition that Lomax sought to preserve—a working cowboy who took the time to write down and send in a song he knew from the range. His contribution demonstrates that Colorado's cowboy culture was not merely a destination for [[texas|Texas]] herds but a self-sustaining community with its own poetic voice.

> I've swum the Colorado where she runs close down to hell;
> I've braced the faro layouts in Cheyenne;
> I've fought for muddy water with a bunch of howlin' swine
> An' swallowed hot tamales and cayenne;
>
> (058-001)

## The Mackenzie Trail

The Mackenzie Trail, named for General Ranald S. Mackenzie, was one of the historic routes that connected [[texas|Texas]] ranches to northern markets. Crossing the Colorado and Brazos rivers, it passed through some of the most storied ranch country in the West. The trail's mention alongside the river crossings (066-001) anchors Colorado within the larger geography of the cattle trade—not a terminus like [[kansas|Kansas]] or [[montana|Montana]], but a vital corridor through which herds and men passed.
