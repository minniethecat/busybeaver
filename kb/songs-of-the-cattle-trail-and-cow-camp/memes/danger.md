## Overview

Danger is the constant companion of the cowboy — the ever-present threat of stampede, storm, and violent encounter that defines the cowboy's relationship with mortality on the cattle trail.

## In the Poems

The anthology presents danger not as occasional crisis but as the fundamental condition of trail life. "[[lasca|Lasca]]" distills it into a single line: "And dash and danger, and life and love" (010-001), where danger sits equal among the elemental forces that shape existence.

The cowboy's relationship with danger is complex — part professional pride, part fatalistic acceptance. "For his wild life of duty and danger" (053-001) frames peril as inseparable from the cowboy's calling, a cost willingly borne. The trail itself is described as a place where men "Braved danger, [[death-imagery|death]], and found a resting place" (066-001), the triad linking danger, mortality, and the peace that follows.

The vigilante poem "The Vigilantes" acknowledges danger as the justification for righteous violence: "Its horrors and dangers our hearts can abide, / That still we most solemnly swear" (064-001). Danger here justifies the code — the cowboy's willingness to face peril becomes the moral foundation for [[the-west|frontier]] justice.

> I want free life, and I want fresh air;
> And I sigh for the canter after the cattle,
> The crack of the whips like shots in battle,
> The medley of hoofs and horns and heads
> That wars and wrangles and scatters and spreads;
> The green beneath and the blue above,
> And dash and danger, and life and love—
> And Lasca!
>
> (010-001)

## Significance

Danger defines the stakes that give cowboy poetry its dramatic tension. Without the constant proximity of [[death-imagery|death]] and injury, the cowboy's courage would be mere posturing. The anthology does not sensationalize danger — it treats it as the baseline reality from which every trail story begins.
