## Overview

The cayuse—a horse bred from the stock of the Cayuse tribe of present-day [[oregon|Oregon]] and Washington—became a generic Western term for an Indian pony or any small, tough range horse. Prized for endurance over beauty, the cayuse was the working animal of the [[the-cattle-trail|cattle trail]], carrying its rider across the vast expanses of the open range and, when the land turned cruel, sometimes leaving nothing but a ribcage bleaching under the [[desert-imagery|desert]] sun.

In [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]]'s collection, the cayuse appears in three distinct moods. It is first a point of pride: "That 'ere cayuse she's a-forkin' / Sure's somethin' fine" (032-001), a [[cowboy-culture|cowboy]] admiring the horse beneath a skilled rider. Then it is a marker of catastrophe: "I counted the ribs of my dead cayuse and cursed at the desert sky" (061-001), a line that distills the desolation of a rider who has lost his mount in the wasteland. Finally, it is the object of a restless search: "He vainly sought for a bad cayuse" (075-001), suggesting a horse with spirit enough to challenge its rider—the kind of mount a cowboy respects.

> Thar she goes a-lopin', stranger,
> Khaki-gowned, with flyin' hair,
> Talk about your classy ridin'—
> That 'ere cayuse she's a-forkin'
> Sure's somethin' on the leap.
>
> (032-001)

## Significance

The cayuse embodies the cowboy's relationship with his horse: practical, unsentimental, and yet inseparable from his identity. Unlike the pedigreed thoroughbred, the cayuse was a working partner—tough, small, and unglamorous—and in that partnership the cowboy saw his own reflection: resilient, resourceful, and shaped by the land itself.
