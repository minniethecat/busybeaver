# Cowboy Culture

## Overview

The cowboy is the central figure of "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]," [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s 1916 collection of Western verse. As Lomax explains in his Introduction, the volume is "limited to attempts, more or less poetic, in translating scenes connected with the life of a cowboy" (P03-002). The cowboy emerges from these pages not merely as a laborer but as a cultural icon — a figure whose songs, code of honor, and way of life define the American [[the-west|frontier]] experience at its most authentic.

The anthology's three-part structure maps the cowboy's full cultural portrait: Part I ([[Part-I-Cowboy-Yarns|Cowboy Yarns]]), Part II ([[Part-II-The-Cowboy-Off-Guard|The Cowboy Off Guard]]), and Part III ([[Part-III-Cowboy-Types|Cowboy Types]]) move from action to feeling to taxonomy, capturing the cowboy at work, at rest, and in archetype.

## In Simple Terms: Who Was the Cowboy?

The cowboy of the Lomax anthology is not the gunslinging hero of Hollywood Westerns. He is a working man — a hired hand on horseback whose job was to move cattle across vast distances. Think of him as a nineteenth-century long-haul trucker who handled livestock instead of freight: spending weeks or months on the trail, sleeping under the stars, facing danger from weather and stampeding animals, and entertaining himself and his companions with songs and stories around the campfire at night.

What made the cowboy different from other laborers of his era was the unique combination of skills his work demanded — expert horsemanship, roping ability, knowledge of cattle behavior, survival skills on the open range — and the code of personal honor that emerged from the [[loneliness|isolation]] of the trail. When you're days from the nearest town or law officer, your word and your reputation are all you have.

The cowboy era was remarkably brief — roughly 1866 to 1890, from the end of the Civil War to the closing of the open range by barbed wire and railroads. That brevity is part of why figures like [[John-A-Lomax]] felt such urgency to preserve [[singing-and-songmaking|cowboy songs]]: the culture they documented was already vanishing even as they wrote it down.

## The Cowboy Code

Central to cowboy culture is an unwritten code of conduct — a set of values that govern life on the [[The-Cattle-Trail|trail]] and in the camp. The poems of the anthology repeatedly articulate these principles:

| Virtue | Expression in the Poetry |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Honor** | "I'll never turn him loose!" — a promise kept even at personal cost (012-001) |
| **Courage** | Facing [[stampede]], storm, and "the mad [[stampede]]" of cattle (010-001) |
| **Kindness to women** | "The cowboy, you'll find, to women is kind" (053-001) |
| **Loyalty** | The bond between cowboys — "Thank God! here comes a man" (067-001) |
| **Self-reliance** | The cowboy's pride in solving problems without appeal to outside authority |
| **Honesty** | "His word is his bond" — a recurring theme in trail negotiations |

> "the cowboy, you'll find, to women is kind, / Regardless of the time or weather" (053-001)

This code translates [[the-west|frontier]] necessity into moral principle. The [[loneliness|isolation]] of the trail — days from any town, any law, any backup — meant that a cowboy's character was his only currency.

## Daily Life & Labor

### On the Trail

The cattle drive is the defining activity of cowboy culture. Poems across the anthology depict [[trail-driving|the long drive]]s from [[Texas]] to northern railheads, along the [[The-Cattle-Trail|Chisholm and other trails]]. As Lomax describes it in the Introduction:

> "The long, long trail winding from Texas to Montana" (P03-002)

The drive combined grueling labor ([[riding-imagery|riding]], roping, night-watching) with the camaraderie of the trail crew and the constant presence of danger — [[stampede]]s, river crossings, storms, and the threat of hostile encounters.

### In the Bunkhouse

When the work of the day was done, the bunkhouse became the cultural heart of cowboy life. The poems depict evenings of [[singing-and-songmaking|music-making]] ("The Bunkhouse Orchestra," set to "Turkey in the Straw" (046-001)), storytelling, and the composition of songs. [[Charles-Badger-Clark]]'s poetry, drawn "From recitation, original" (006-001), captures this oral performance tradition.

### The Round-Up

The seasonal [[round-up]] (or "rodeo"), as celebrated in poems like "The Cattle [[round-up|Round-Up]]," gathered cowboys from multiple outfits to brand, sort, and drive cattle. These gatherings were both work and social event — the closest thing the cowboy had to a community festival.

## Emotional Landscape

The anthology's Part II ("The Cowboy Off Guard") reveals the cowboy's inner life, moving beyond the action-hero image of Part I. Recurring emotional themes include:

- **[[loneliness|Loneliness]]** — the solitary vigil on night watch, the "poor heart and a lonely stone" (017-001)
- **Longing for Home** — the tension between the freedom of the trail and the pull of domestic life
- **The Value of Companionship** — the deep bonds formed among men who face danger together
- **Nostalgia for Vanishing World** — poems like "The Old [[the-cattleman|Cowman]]" and "The Call of the Plains" mourn a [[the-west|frontier]] already closing

## Cowboy Types

Part III of the anthology catalogs the diversity within cowboy culture, distinguishing:

| Type | Characteristics | Example Poem |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| **The Ranger** | Lawman, upholder of [[the-west|frontier]] justice | "A Ranger" (057-001) |
| **The [[the-cattleman|Cowman]]** | Established rancher, employer | "The Old [[the-cattleman|Cowman]]" |
| **The Tenderfoot** | Inexperienced newcomer, object of humor | "The Disappointed Tenderfoot" |
| **The Singer** | Poet-musician who preserves cowboy song | [[Charles-Badger-Clark]] |
| **The [[outlaw]]** | Cowboy who lives outside written law | "The Sinners" (067-001) |
