## Overview

"The End of the Trail" is the anthology's closing poem — the final voice in a collection of voices, positioned to send the reader back into the world with the cowboy's last words ringing in their ears. The poem is a meditation on endings: the end of the trail drive, the end of a life, the end of an era.

> But now is the end of the trail in sight (035-001)

The poem's placement is significant. After 78 poems spanning humor, love, violence, and song, the anthology closes on a note of quiet acceptance. The cowboy has ridden his last ride; what remains is memory, and the poems themselves.

## Structure

The poem moves through three stages: recognition (the narrator sees the end approaching), retrospection (he looks back at what the trail has meant), and acceptance (he faces the end without fear). This tripartite structure echoes the anthology's own organization — three parts, each with its own emotional register — and gives the closing poem a formal rightness that rewards the reader who has journeyed through the entire collection.

## Thematic Weight

### Mortality

The poem treats [[death-imagery|death]] not as tragedy but as arrival. The trail has been long, the work hard, the losses real — and now the end is a release rather than a defeat. This is the cowboy Stoicism that runs throughout the anthology, here given its fullest and most explicit expression.

### Memory

What survives the end of the trail? The poem's answer is implicit: the songs survive. The anthology itself is the testament — the songs of the cattle trail and cow camp preserved against the day when the trails themselves are gone and the camps are silent.

### The Closing of the West

By 1916, when Lomax published the anthology, the open range was closing. Fences, railroads, and settlement were transforming the West the poems celebrated. "The End of the Trail" is thus not merely a personal meditation on mortality but a cultural elegy — a lament for a way of life that was already vanishing even as it was being sung into memory.
