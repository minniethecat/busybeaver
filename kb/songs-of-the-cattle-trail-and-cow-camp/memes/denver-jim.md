## Overview

Denver Jim is the [[outlaw]] protagonist of "Denver Jim" (ch066), a [[the-west|frontier]] ballad by Sherman D. Richardson. Cornered by vigilantes who intend to hang him, Jim is spared at the last moment when a letter from his mother — read aloud by his captor — reveals his humanity and his family's dependence on him.

> "Say, fellers, that ornery thief must be nigh us,"
> "For I jist saw him across this way to the right;"
> "Ah, there he is now right under that burr-oak"
> "As fearless and cool as if waitin' all night."
> "Out with my lasso, boys, if it ain't Denver Jim"
> "That's corralled here at last!"
>
> (063-001)

## Character Analysis

Jim is introduced through the eyes of his captors — a posse of vigilantes who have tracked him through the night. He is described as "fearless and cool as if waitin' all night" (066-001), accepting his fate with a stoicism that marks him as a man of the [[the-west|frontier]] rather than a common criminal.

The posse's leader takes a grim, almost ritualistic tone in pronouncing sentence: "In ten minutes more we'll be crossin' the [[prairie]], / An' you will be hangin' there right from that limb" (066-001). Jim is given the traditional last drink and a chance to speak. His only request is that his captor answer a letter — not for himself, but for his mother.

The letter, from "[[arkansas|Long Point]], [[arkansas|Arkansas]], the thirteenth of August," reveals Jim as a dutiful son who sent money home to a mother "sorely in need" (066-001). The vigilantes, moved by "the love of a mother" (066-001), reverse their verdict: they remove the noose, give him a horse, and tell him to "come eastward to stay with her always" (066-001).

## Significance

"Denver Jim" is notable for its treatment of [[the-west|frontier]] justice as personal rather than institutional. The poem does not question whether Jim is guilty — he is introduced as an "ornery thief" (066-001) — but argues that a man's relationship to his mother transcends his crimes. The tag line "For higher than law is the love of a mother" (066-001) encapsulates a moral code in which family loyalty ranks above legal obligation.

The poem is attributed to Sherman D. Richardson, a lesser-known Western poet whose work appears in the Lomax anthology alongside more famous contributors.
