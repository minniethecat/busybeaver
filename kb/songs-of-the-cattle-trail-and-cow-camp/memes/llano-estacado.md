## Overview

The Llano Estacado (Spanish for "Staked Plains") is an immense mesa stretching across eastern [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] and the [[texas|Texas]] Panhandle. In the era of the open range, it was one of the most formidable landscapes a cowboy could cross—featureless, water-scarce, and disorienting. Its very name carried weight among cattlemen: a place where stakes were driven into the ground to mark a route across a plain so flat that travelers had no other landmarks.

The anthology invokes the Llano in two very different registers. One poem traces the old Mackenzie Trail as it climbs onto the plateau and vanishes: "The Llano Estacado (beyond lie wastes / Of alkali and hunger gaunt and [[death-imagery|death]])— / And here is lost in shifting rifts of sand" (066-001). The trail is swallowed by the plateau, a geographical dead-end that mirrors the closing of the cowboy era itself. Another poem makes the Llano the southern anchor of the cowboy's enduring fame:

> To the memory of the cowboy whose fame must e'er endure
> From the Llano Estacado to Dakota's distant sands,
> Where were herded countless thousands in the days of fenceless lands. (073-001)

The geographic span from the Staked Plains to the northern [[dakota|Dakota]] prairies encompasses the entire range of the cowboy's world—and by implication, the world that was lost when the fences went up.

## In the Poems

### Denver Jim (Chapter 66)

The route of the old Mackenzie Trail is traced across the rivers and plains of [[texas|Texas]]: "Across the Brazos, [[colorado|Colorado]], through / Concho's broad, fair valley, sweeping on / By [[abilene|Abilene]] it climbs upon the plains, / The Llano Estacado" (066-001). The poem then opens a grim vista: "beyond lie wastes / Of alkali and hunger gaunt and [[death-imagery|death]]" (066-001). The trail, after crossing named rivers and passing settled towns, is "lost in shifting rifts of sand" (066-001) on the featureless plateau—a fitting metaphor for the vanishing of the open-range cowboy.

### [[A-Cowboy-Toast|A Cowboy Toast]] (Chapter 73)

In what appears to be an elegiac toast within "[[gila-river|The Gila]] Monster Route," the speaker raises a "bumper of water, clear and pure" (073-001) to the memory of the cowboy. The Llano Estacado serves as the southern boundary of the cowboy's domain, paired with "[[dakota|Dakota]]'s distant sands" (073-001) at the northern extreme. Between these two markers lay "the days of fenceless lands" (073-001)—an era when cattle were herded in "countless thousands" (073-001) across an unfenced continent.
