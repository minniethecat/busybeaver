## Overview

The rich animal imagery of the cattle trail poems—horses, cattle, coyotes, wolves, and the creatures that share the cowboy's vast landscape.

## Examples

Animal Imagery appears across all three parts of the anthology, woven into narratives of trail driving, night watching, and campfire gatherings. The poets—many of them working cowboys themselves—drew on direct sensory experience, giving their imagery an authenticity that distinguishes these verses from the romanticized Western poetry of Eastern writers.

## Poetic Function

Animal Imagery serves multiple functions in the anthology: it roots the poems in the cowboy's immediate physical world, it creates atmosphere and mood, and it transforms the mundane details of trail life into vehicles for emotional and philosophical reflection.

## In the Poems

### Coyote

The coyote is the cowboy's nocturnal companion — at once a threat and a familiar presence. Its howl punctuates the night and marks the boundary between civilization and wilderness:

> And the coyote howls 'round the cowboy's home
>
> (P03-001)

The soundscape of the trail is defined by these wild voices. The coyote appears "at the dim lit dusk of eve when the lone coyotes roam" (009-001) and as "the sly coyote trots here and there" (010-001), a creature that shares the landscape with the cowboy but remains forever untamed.

A "gaunt coyote" (038-001) lingers as a solitary witness to the cowboy alone with his thoughts, while the "coyotes sing their lullaby" (002-001) — the wild world singing the cowboy to sleep beneath the open sky.

### Cattle

The cattle are the center of the cowboy's working world. "The canter after the cattle, the crack of the whips" (010-001) defines the rhythm of trail life. In the early days, "the prairie was big and the cattle were as 'scary' as ever" (019-001) — wild, unpredictable, and demanding constant vigilance.
