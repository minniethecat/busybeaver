## Overview

Arkansas appears in "[[denver-jim]]" not as a cattle trail destination but as a point of origin and waiting—the home left behind when a young man ventures into the Western [[the-west|frontier]]. The poem's setting at "Long Point, Arkansas" (063-001) frames the emotional geography of [[the-cattle-trail]]'s human cost: the distance between a mother's settled life and her son's disappearance into the trail-and-mining world of [[the-west]].

The poem opens with a precise anchoring in time and place: "'Long Point, Arkansas, the thirteenth of August" (063-001), immediately casting the narrative as a letter or plea from a mother seeking news of her son [[denver-jim|James]], "somewhere out in [[the-west]]" (063-001). The specificity of the date and location makes Arkansas the fixed reference point against which the vast, unknowable West is measured—a technique that turns a personal geography into universal drama.

Unlike the trail towns of [[kansas]], [[colorado]], or [[texas]]—places of arrival, commerce, and cowboy revelry—the Arkansas of these poems is a place of stillness and waiting. It represents the domestic world the cowboy has left behind, the home that sends letters eastward and receives only silence in return.

## In the Poems

### The Mother's Letter (Chapter 63)

The emotional core of "[[denver-jim]]" turns on a letter from the mother in Long Point, Arkansas. When the posse has [[denver-jim|Jim]] cornered and a noose around his neck, [[denver-jim|Jim]]'s final request is for the posse leader to answer his mother's letter. The posse leader reads it aloud:

"'Long Point, Arkansas, the thirteenth of August, / My dearest son [[denver-jim|James]], somewhere out in [[the-west]], / For long, weary months I've been waiting for tidings / Since your last loving letter came eastward to bless'" (063-001).

The letter continues with gratitude for money [[denver-jim|Jim]] sent home: "God bless you, my son, for thus sending that money, / Remembering your mother when sorely in need" (063-001). The mother's voice, relayed through the rough posse leader's reading, transforms the scene. Her closing wish—"How I long so to see you come into the doorway, / As you used to, of old, when weary, to rest" (063-001)—invokes the domestic doorway of the Arkansas home as the antithesis of the [[prairie]] limb from which [[denver-jim|Jim]] is about to hang.

### Arkansas as Moral Turning Point

The letter from Arkansas reverses the poem's entire trajectory. After hearing it, the posse leader removes the noose: "An' fer fear I might want it I'll take off that [[lasso]]" (063-001). He tells [[denver-jim|Jim]] to return east: "when yer see yer old mother jist tell her / That a wee bit o' writin' kinder hastened the day / When her boy could come eastward to stay with her always" (063-001).

The poem closes with a moral judgment that elevates the Arkansas home above [[the-west|frontier]] justice: "For higher than law is the love of a mother— / It reversed the decision—the court was adjourned" (063-001). Arkansas, in this telling, is not merely a place on a map but the embodiment of a moral claim that overrides the rough code of the trail.

> 'Long Point, Arkansas, the thirteenth of August,
> My dearest son James, somewhere out in the West,
> The letter was finished, was folded and sealed,
> And the pen pushed back wearily into its rest.
>
> (063-001)
