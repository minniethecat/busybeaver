## Overview

New Mexico Territory was one of the vast regions traversed by cowboys during the cattle trail era. Its presence in this collection is personal rather than geographical: it was here that a cowboy's impromptu performance gave the collector direct evidence of how printed poems entered oral tradition and were transformed.

The Introduction records a formative episode: "one night in New Mexico a cowboy sang to me, in typical cowboy music, [[larry-chittenden|Larry Chittenden]]'s entire '[[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball'" (P03-002). This moment—a printed poem returning to oral performance in a remote camp—embodies the two-way flow between written verse and cowboy song that the anthology documents.

## A Crucible of Oral Tradition

The New Mexico episode illuminates a central theme of Lomax's collecting philosophy. The cowboy who sang Chittenden's poem had learned it not from a book but from hearing it sung by other cowboys around campfires and in bunkhouses. The printed poem had entered oral circulation so thoroughly that the singer no longer remembered—if he ever knew—that it originated in a published volume.

Lomax notes the consequence: "since that time the poem has often come to me in manuscript form as an original cowboy song" (P03-002). The [[loneliness|isolation]] of places like New Mexico—far from printing presses and literary culture—created conditions in which written verse and oral song became indistinguishable. A cowboy who heard a poem sung around a campfire had no way to verify its origin, and in the retelling, the poem acquired the variations and the musical setting that made it truly a cowboy song.

## The Collector's Journey

New Mexico Territory, still not a state when some of the early collecting was done (it achieved statehood in 1912), represented the [[the-west|frontier]] edge of Lomax's collecting range. Its remoteness made it both difficult to reach and rich in undistributed material—songs that had not yet been written down, still circulating only in the memories and voices of working cowboys.

The territory's role in the anthology is thus methodological rather than poetic: it taught Lomax that collecting [[singing-and-songmaking|cowboy songs]] meant listening as much as reading, that the richest sources were not in libraries but in cow camps, and that a printed poem's journey into oral tradition was as important as the journey of an oral song onto the printed page.

## In the Poems

New Mexico's significance in the collection lies not in poems set there but in the collecting process itself. The Introduction notes that after hearing the song in New Mexico, "since that time the poem has often come to me in manuscript form as an original cowboy song" (P03-002), illustrating how the region's [[loneliness|isolation]] became a crucible in which literary verse was reshaped by oral tradition into something the cowboys recognized as their own.
