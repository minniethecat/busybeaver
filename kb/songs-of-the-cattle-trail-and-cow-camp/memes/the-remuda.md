## Overview

The herd of spare horses that accompanied every cattle drive—each cowboy required multiple mounts, making the remuda essential trail infrastructure.

As depicted throughout *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, the remuda was an integral part of the cowboy's world—not merely a practical necessity but a source of identity, pride, and poetic inspiration.

## In the Anthology

The poems collected by [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] capture the remuda as lived experience rather than romanticized abstraction. The verses reflect the working cowboy's intimate knowledge of his tools, practices, and social roles—knowledge earned through long days in the saddle and long nights under the stars.

## Cultural Significance

The Remuda represents more than a practical facet of trail life; it embodies the values and self-image of the cowboy. The anthology preserves these details at the moment when the open-range era was fading, making it not only literature but a document of a vanished way of life.
