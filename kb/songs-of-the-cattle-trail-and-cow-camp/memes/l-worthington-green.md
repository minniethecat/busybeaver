## Overview

L. Worthington Green is a poet whose sole contribution to the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology is "The Bull Fight" (ch027), a narrative poem set in Mexico that depicts a *corrida de toros* from the perspective of the spectators — particularly a young woman named Pepita and her escort Juan. The poem uses a refrain structure to build the drama and concludes with the bull's [[death-imagery|death]].

## Literary Context

"The Bull Fight" is unusual within the Lomax anthology for its Mexican setting and subject matter. While most poems in the collection focus on the Anglo-American cowboy experience, Green's poem transports the reader south of the border to a *corrida de toros*, complete with Spanish-language refrains: *"Se corren los toros!"* ("The bulls are running!") and *"¡Sta muerto el toro!"* ("The bull is dead!") (027-001).

The poem is structured around the experience of Juan, who brings his *Pepita* into town for the festival, and the dramatic arc follows the bullfight through to its fatal conclusion. The refrain evolves through the poem — from the anticipatory "Se corren los toros!" that opens each stanza to the final, decisive "¡Sta muerto el toro!" — mirroring the progression from spectacle to sacrifice.

The poem's By-line attribution "L. Worthington Green" and its closing line "And Juan takes his Pepita back from the town" (027-001) give it the character of a complete narrative arc: arrival, spectacle, and return. The use of Spanish refrains suggests either first-hand knowledge of Mexican culture or a deliberate literary exoticism that was popular in early twentieth-century American regional writing.

> The couriers from Chihuahua go
> To distant Cusi and Santavo,
> Announce the feast of all the year the crown—
> Se corren los toros!
> And Juan brings his Pepita into town.
> The rancherias on the mountain side,
> The haciendas of the Llano wide,
> Are quickened by the matador's renown.
> Se corren los toros!
> And Juan brings his Pepita into town.
> ...
> The bull is harried till the governor's word
> Bids the Diestro give the agile sword;
> Then shower the bravos and the roses down!
> 'Sta muerto el toro!
> And Juan takes his Pepita back from the town.
>
> (027-001)
