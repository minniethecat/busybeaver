## Overview

Glenn Norton is the co-author, with [[l-f-post|L. F. Post]], of "The Gila Monster Route" (ch070), a narrative poem about a "ding-bat" (hobo) thrown off a freight train and left to survive in the [[desert-imagery|desert]]. Little is known about Norton beyond this single poem in the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology.

## Literary Context

"The Gila Monster Route" tells the story of a tramp stranded on a railroad tie after being forcibly ejected from a freight train: "The lingering sunset across the plain / Kissed the rear-end door of an east-bound train" (070-001). The poem's opening establishes a stark contrast between the beauty of the Western sunset and the brutal reality of the hobo's predicament.

Norton and Post's poem employs the specialized vocabulary of railroad tramps — "ding-bat," "con high-balled," "manifest freight," "on the stem" — creating a linguistic world as self-contained as the cowboy's. The poem documents an underclass of Western experience rarely depicted in the anthology: not the heroic cowboy but the desperate traveler, the man who rides the rails not by choice but by necessity.

The poem's attribution as a joint work ("[[l-f-post|L. F. Post]] and Glenn Norton") is one of the few collaborative attributions in the anthology, suggesting either that the poem was composed jointly or that it reflects a shared experience of railroad life. The equal billing implies equal contribution.

> The lingering sunset across the plain
> Kissed the rear-end door of an east-bound train,
> And shone on a passing track close by
> Where a ding-bat sat on a rotting tie.
> He was ditched by a shock and a cruel fate.
> The con high-balled, and the manifest freight
> Pulled out on the stem behind the mail,
> And she hit the ball on a sanded rail.
> As she pulled away in the falling light
> He could see the gleam of her red tail-light.
> Then the moon arose and the [[stars-imagery|stars]] came out—
> He was ditched on the Gila Monster Route.
>
> (070-001)
