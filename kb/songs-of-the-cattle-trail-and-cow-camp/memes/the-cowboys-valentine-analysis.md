## Overview

"I've ridden my bronco thru sunshine and rain" — the opening line of "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s Valentine," collected in [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp* (1916). This analysis examines the poem's place in the anthology's celebration of cowboy life.

## Summary

The poem captures a distinctive aspect of the cowboy's world—tender poem in which a cowboy sends his heart across miles to his distant beloved—love and longing on cattle trail.

## Themes

The poem engages with the theme of **love**—a recurring concern throughout the anthology. The cowboy's experience, as the poem depicts it, is neither romanticized nor diminished but presented with the directness and authenticity characteristic of the best Western verse.

## Style and Structure

The poem employs the vernacular voice and regular meter typical of cowboy verse, drawing on oral tradition while achieving the formal polish expected of published poetry. Its language bridges the campfire and the printed page.

## Place in the Anthology

Within *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, this poem contributes to the anthology's documentary ambition—preserving the full emotional range of cowboy experience from [[loneliness|solitude]] to celebration, from comedy to elegy.
