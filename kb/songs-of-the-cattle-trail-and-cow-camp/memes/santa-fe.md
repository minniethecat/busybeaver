## Overview

Santa Fe, the oldest capital city in the United States, sits at the southern terminus of the [[rockies|Rocky Mountains]] in present-day [[new-mexico|New Mexico]]. During the nineteenth century it was the [[the-west|west]]ern end of the Santa Fe Trail, a major commercial route connecting [[missouri|Missouri]] to the South[[the-west|west]]. By the time of the cattle trail era, Santa Fe was an established trading center whose name evoked the exotic and the remote to cowboys working ranges further south.

In this anthology, Santa Fe appears not as a town but as a trail—the historic route down which a peculiar figure rides into a [[pecos|Pecos River]] camp, setting up one of the collection's most elaborate comic poems. The Santa Fe Trail, as the conduit of commerce and strange travelers from the East, becomes the vehicle for a collision between the old West of the cowboy and the new technology of the bicycle.

## In the Poems

### Broncho Versus Bicycle: The Trail as Comic Threshold (Chapter 8)

"Broncho Versus Bicycle" is the collection's longest comic narrative, a tall-tale account of a ten-mile race between a cowboy's prized broncho and an Eastern traveler's bicycle. The poem opens at the moment of arrival:

"The first that we saw of the high-tone tramp / War over thar at our [[pecos|Pecos]] camp; / He war comin' down the Santa Fe trail / Astride of a wheel with a crooked tail, / A-skinnin' along with a merry song / An' a-ringin' a little warnin' gong" (008-001).

The Santa Fe Trail here functions as a threshold between worlds. The Easterner—later identified as [[texas-tom|Tom]] Stevens from "a town they call Bosting, in old Yankeedom" (008-001)—rides down a route that for decades had carried traders, settlers, and Army supply wagons. But his conveyance is not a wagon or a horse: it is a bicycle, "a wheel with a crooked tail." The trail, which once marked the [[the-west|frontier]] of American expansion, now carries the future into the cowboy's camp.

### Old Trail, New Technology

The historical resonance of the Santa Fe Trail deepens the poem's central comic contrast. The trail had been a route of [[the-west|west]]ward expansion since the 1820s; by the time of the cattle boom it was a well-established artery. The cowboy narrator, confident in his "lightnin' broncho," assumes the race is "the softest snap I had ever struck" (008-001). But the Easterner's machine—which he claims to have "whirled... round the world" (008-001)—proves faster than horseflesh.

The poem's comedy is grounded in real geography: the race is run "to the Navajo cross, ten miles away— / As handsome a track as you ever seed / Fer testin' a hosses prettiest speed" (008-001). The Santa Fe Trail provides the stage on which the old West meets the new, and the cowboy learns that even the fastest broncho cannot outrun technological change.

### The Honorable Defeat

Crucially, the cowboy loses with grace: "Now, pard, when a cowboy gits licked he don't swar / Nor kick, if the beatin' are done on the squar" (008-001). The poem ends with the cowboy handing over his broncho and receiving a letter from Boston explaining the horse was donated to "a feller named Pope, who stands at the head / O' the ranch where the cussed wheel hosses are bred" (008-001)—a reference to the Pope Manufacturing Company, the real-world bicycle manufacturer. The Santa Fe Trail, which brought the bicycle into the cowboy's world, thus becomes the route by which the cowboy's world acknowledges its own passing.
