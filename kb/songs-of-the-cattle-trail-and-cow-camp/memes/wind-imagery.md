## Overview

The wind is the most pervasive natural element in *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, appearing in 19 distinct passages across all three parts. More than mere weather, the wind functions as the prairie's voice—carrying the sounds of cattle, song, and danger across vast distances. It is both a companion to the lonely cowboy and a force of nature that "wars with the wind when the weather is rough" (010-001).

The wind's sensory richness distinguishes it from other imagery types: it is simultaneously visual (dust devils, waving grass), auditory (howling, whispering, "flaps our slickers" (006-001)), and tactile (the bite of a cold night wind, the relief of a warm breeze). This multi-sensory quality makes wind imagery a unifying thread across the anthology's diverse poems, linking disparate scenes through a shared atmospheric vocabulary.

## Examples

### Wind as the Prairie's Voice

Many poems treat the wind as an intentional speaker:

> "Ho! wind of the far, far prairies!
> Free as the waves of the sea!" (071-001)

The exclamatory address suggests personification: the wind is something to be spoken to, not merely endured. Its freedom mirrors the cowboy's own idealized self-image.

### Wind as Adversary

The wind's destructive side is equally present:

> "And wars with the wind when the weather is rough" (010-001)

Wind is an opponent to be battled, testing the cowboy's resilience. This adversarial wind strips away comfort and exposes the raw conditions of range life.

### Wind as Carrier

Wind carries sound across the open range, connecting distant points:

> "When the night wind flaps our slickers" (006-001)

The tactile immediacy of wind on raingear grounds the abstract concept in physical sensation.

### Wind in Company

The wind frequently accompanies the cowboy's [[singing-and-songmaking|music-making]] and social moments:

> "For the snow is on the mountains and the wind is on the plain" (046-001)

Wind here sets the scene for a musical gathering, its presence on the plain balancing the snow's presence on the peaks—a layered landscape.

### Wind and Nostalgia

Wind triggers memory and longing:

> "Your voice is sweet as in alien street
> I dream of the boundless plains" (071-001)

The wind's voice evokes the plains even when the speaker is far away, making it a conduit for homesickness.

## Poetic Function

Wind imagery serves three interconnected roles in the anthology:

1. **Spatial Anchor** — Wind defines the prairie's scale. Its unimpeded movement across vast distances makes the landscape's enormity palpable, reminding the reader that the cowboy inhabits a world without walls.

2. **Emotional Barometer** — The wind's character shifts with the poem's mood: gentle breezes accompany love songs, howling gales accompany danger and loss. Wind externalizes internal states.

3. **[[freedom|Freedom]] Emblem** — The wind's uncontainable nature makes it the anthology's most potent symbol of [[freedom-of-the-range|Western freedom]]. As the epigraph notes, "every wind careening / Seems here to breathe a son" [P02-001]—wind and song are inseparable in these poems.
