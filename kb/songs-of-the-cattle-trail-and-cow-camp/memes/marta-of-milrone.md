## Overview

Marta of Milrone is the cowgirl who rides at the narrator's side in the epic ballad "Marta of Milrone" (ch020), one of the longest and most distinctive poems in the anthology. She represents the rare figure of the cowgirl — a female participant in the cattle-driving world that was overwhelmingly male — and is presented as an equal partner in work, danger, and love.

> I shot him where the Rio flows;
> I shot him when the moon arose;
> And where he lies the vulture knows
> Along the Tinto River.
> In schools of eastern culture pale
> My cloistered flesh began to fail;
>
> (017-001)

## Character Analysis

The narrator tells his story in the first person, beginning with a confession of violence: "I shot him where the Rio flows; / I shot him when the moon arose" (017-001). Sent West for his health after a breakdown, he finds not just physical recovery but purpose on the range: "But strong I grew and tall I grew; / I drank the region's balm and dew" (017-001).

Marta enters the narrative as his constant [[riding-imagery|riding]] companion: "And oft it was my joy to ride / Over the sand-blown ocean wide / While, ever smiling at my side, / Rode Marta of Milrone" (017-001). The repetition of her full name — "Marta of Milrone" — gives her a ballad-like dignity, as though she belongs to a specific place and cannot be separated from it.

Unlike the passive love interests common in Eastern poetry of the period, Marta is an active participant in the trail life: she rides with him among "four thousand hoofs" (017-001), shares the desert landscape, and represents the freedom of the open range as fully as any male character in the anthology.

## Significance

Marta stands out in the anthology as one of the few named female characters who is not a distant love object, mother, or comic figure. She rides, works, and loves on equal terms with the male narrator. Her full name locates her in a specific geography — Milrone — implying a rootedness and identity that the itinerant cowboys often lack. In a collection dominated by male voices singing of male experience, Marta of Milrone offers a rare, if brief, vision of shared adventure between men and women on the [[the-west|frontier]].
