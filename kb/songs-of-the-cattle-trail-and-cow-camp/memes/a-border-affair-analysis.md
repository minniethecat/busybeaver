## Overview

"A Border Affair," by [[Charles-Badger-Clark]], is unique in the Lomax anthology: a love poem told in alternating English and Spanish, capturing the cultural hybridity of the American South[[the-west|west]]. The poem's narrator is an Anglo cowboy in love with a Mexican woman, and the poem's bilingual form enacts the cross-cultural encounter it describes.

> Adiós, mi corazón (024-001)

The poem's most famous line — its refrain — is entirely in Spanish. This is not decoration but argument: the poem insists that some things (love, farewell, heartbreak) can only be said in the lover's tongue. The English-speaking cowboy surrenders his native language at the moment of greatest emotional intensity.

## Bilingual Form

Clark's bilingualism is not merely ornamental. The Spanish lines carry the poem's emotional weight — the declarations of love, the farewells, the laments. The English lines provide context, narrative, and description. The effect is of a voice that moves between two worlds, belonging fully to neither but enriched by both.

This formal choice reflects a historical reality: the cattle trails of the South[[the-west|west]] crossed linguistic and cultural borders as often as they crossed rivers. [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]] routinely worked alongside Mexican vaqueros, from whom they borrowed equipment, techniques, and vocabulary. Clark's poem makes this borrowing visible, elevating it from practical necessity to poetic form.

## Romantic Border Crossing

The poem's romantic plot — an Anglo cowboy in love with a Mexican woman — mirrors the cultural border crossing enacted by its form. The love affair is itself a border affair: a relationship that crosses the boundaries of language, culture, and national identity.

The poem is notably respectful of its Mexican subject. She is not exoticized or stereotyped; she is allowed dignity, agency, and the last word. The poem's final "Adiós" is as much hers as his — a mutual farewell in a language they share.

## Legacy

"A Border Affair" has been widely anthologized and set to music, becoming one of the most enduring of all cowboy love songs. Its bilingual form was ahead of its time, anticipating twentieth-century American poetry's engagement with linguistic hybridity and border identity.
