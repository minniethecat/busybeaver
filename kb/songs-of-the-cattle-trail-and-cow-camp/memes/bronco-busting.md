## Overview

The art and danger of breaking wild horses—one of the cowboy's most iconic and perilous skills, celebrated in poems like 'Broncho Versus Bicycle' and '[[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Versus Broncho.'

As depicted throughout *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, bronco busting was an integral part of the cowboy's world—not merely a practical necessity but a source of identity, pride, and poetic inspiration.

## In the Poems

The anthology's verses are saturated with references to the broncho — the wild or half-wild horse that defined the cowboy's daily work and self-image. In "[[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Versus Broncho," the speaker declares himself "a broncho twistin' wonder on the fly" (006-001), asserting mastery over the untamed mount as a badge of professional pride.

"[[bill|Bill]], the broncho buster, from the ranch at Beaver's Dam" (005-001) introduces a named practitioner whose skill is central to the ranch economy. The seasoned hand declares "I've busted bronchos off and on / Since first I struck their trail" (044-001), treating bronco busting as a lifelong vocation whose accumulated wisdom marks the veteran cowboy.

In "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] at the Carnival," the arena spectacle is contrasted with real range work: "as a-toppin' of a broncho that has got it in fur you" (041-001). The poem "When You're on the Range" satirizes the advice-giving culture around bronco [[riding-imagery|riding]] — "They all offers you prescriptions / How a broncho should be rode" (042-001) — acknowledging both the danger and the bravado.

The physical toll appears in "I've rode a pitchin' broncho till the sky was underneath" (058-001), while the horse itself is a constant presence: "He's fond of his horse, it's a broncho, of course" (053-001).

> Don't you hear the big spurs jingle?
> Don't you feel the red blood tingle?
> Be it smile or be it frown,
> Be it dance or be it fight,
> Broncho Bill has come to town
> To dance a dance tonight.
>
> (005-001)

## Cultural Significance

Bronco Busting represents more than a practical facet of trail life; it embodies the values and self-image of the cowboy. The anthology preserves these details at the moment when the open-range era was fading, making it not only literature but a document of a vanished way of life.
