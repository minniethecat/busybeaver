## Overview

The Alamo, the storied mission-fortress in [[san-antonio|San Antonio]], [[texas|Texas]], appears in [[frank-desprez|Frank Desprez]]'s poem "[[lasca|Lasca]]" as the setting for a fateful romantic encounter. The narrator recalls meeting "a glorious girl in the Alamo" one Sunday—a meeting that turns violent when the heroine draws a dagger from her garter and stabs him in a flash of jealous passion.

The choice of the Alamo as the poem's setting carries symbolic weight. Long before it became a shrine to [[texas|Texas]] independence, the Alamo was a Spanish mission, and its weathered stone walls provide a dramatic backdrop for Desprez's tale of [[the-west|frontier]] passion. The juxtaposition of the sacred (a Sunday at the old mission) with the profane (a stabbing by a jealous lover) gives the poem its darkly comic charge.

## In the Poems

"[[lasca|Lasca]]" references the Alamo in a swift, memorable stanza that shifts from [[love|romance]] to violence in a single line:

> "One Sunday, in San Antonio,
> To a glorious girl in the Alamo,
> She drew from her garter a little dagger,
> And—sting of a wasp—it made me stagger!" (010-001)

The narrator survives ("An inch to the left, or an inch to the right, / And I shouldn't be maundering here tonight"), but the Alamo remains fixed in the poem as the geographic marker of a love that was as dangerous as it was thrilling. The landmark serves as a metonym for [[san-antonio|San Antonio]] itself—historic, romantic, and touched by violence.
