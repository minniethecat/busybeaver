## Overview

The practice of marking cattle with hot irons—a ritual of ownership, a symbol of ranch identity, and a recurring motif in cowboy verse.

As depicted throughout *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, branding was an integral part of the cowboy's world—not merely a practical necessity but a source of identity, pride, and poetic inspiration.

## In the Anthology

The poems collected by [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] capture branding as lived experience rather than romanticized abstraction. The anthology's verses show the branding iron as both a tool of commerce and a symbol of the cowboy's mastery over the wild:

> The last steer has been branded / And the last beef has been run
>
> (023-001)

This closing line from a cowboy song marks branding as the definitive act that completes the [[round-up]] — the moment when the wild herd becomes property.

## Branding as Identity

The brand carries personal meaning. As one cowboy declares:

> The one 'at wears my brand / Must be sproutin' wings to fly
>
> (022-001)

Here the brand is more than a mark of ownership — it's an extension of the cowboy's identity, something he takes pride in. The imagery of the branded steer "sproutin' wings" elevates the mundane act of marking cattle into something approaching the mythic.

The branding iron itself is "het in the fire" (021-001), glowing with the heat that transforms it from mere metal into a tool of transformation. And in the cowboy's vision of paradise, "we'll [[lasso|rope]] 'em and brand 'em fair" (009-001) — branding is not grudging labor but part of the idealized cowboy life in the hereafter.

## Cultural Significance

Branding represents more than a practical facet of trail life; it embodies the values and self-image of the cowboy. The anthology preserves these details at the moment when the open-range era was fading, making it not only literature but a document of a vanished way of life.
