## Overview

San Antonio, one of the oldest European settlements in [[texas]], appears in "[[lasca]]" as a site of romantic conflict. In [[frank-desprez]]'s poem, it is the city where the narrator's indiscreet flirtation triggers [[lasca]]'s passionate jealousy—a moment that nearly ends in tragedy well before the poem's fatal [[stampede]].

Founded in 1718 as a Spanish mission and presidio, San Antonio grew into the largest city in [[texas]] during [[the-cattle-trail]] era. Its position at the crossroads of the [[chisholm-trail]] and the Old Spanish Trail made it a natural gathering point for cattle drives heading north from the [[rio-grande]] plains. By the 1870s, San Antonio was the cultural and commercial capital of South [[texas]], where Mexican, Spanish, Anglo, and German influences converged.

The reference to "the [[alamo]]" within San Antonio (010-001) layers the city with historical resonance, connecting the poem's personal drama to [[texas]]'s defining symbol of heroic sacrifice. In "[[lasca]]," the city's blend of Old Spain and [[the-west|frontier]] [[texas]] provides the perfect backdrop for the narrator's dangerous passion.

## Geography

San Antonio lies at the headwaters of the San Antonio River on the Balcones Escarpment, the geological boundary between the Edwards Plateau to the north and the Gulf Coastal Plain to the south. The region's mild climate, abundant springs, and fertile river valleys made it a natural settlement site from pre-Columbian times through the Spanish colonial period and into [[the-cattle-trail]] era.

The city sits approximately 150 miles north of the [[rio-grande]] and 200 miles [[the-west|west]] of the Gulf of Mexico, placing it squarely on the route of south [[texas]] cattle drives moving toward the [[kansas]] railheads. The surrounding brush country—dense [[mesquite]] and cactus—gave the region its distinctive character and made the open streets of San Antonio an oasis of civilization on the long trail north.

## In the Poems

In "[[lasca]]," San Antonio is the setting for a pivotal scene: "One Sunday, in San Antonio / To a glorious girl in the [[alamo]]" (010-001), the narrator makes [[lasca]] jealous "for fun." Her response is immediate and violent—"She drew from her garter a little dagger / And—sting of a wasp—it made me stagger!" (010-001).

The geographical specificity is telling. San Antonio, with its Spanish colonial heritage and the [[alamo]]'s symbolic weight, provides a backdrop where the "vigorous vintage of Old Spain" (010-001) runs through [[lasca]]'s veins. The town represents the meeting point of Mexican, Spanish, and Anglo-Texan cultures that shaped the cattle-trail South—a place where passions run as hot as the [[texas]] sun and a lover's quarrel can turn deadly in an instant.

The irony of the scene is that this near-fatal jealousy in San Antonio is merely prologue. The poem's true tragedy awaits on the open range, where a [[stampede]]—not a lover's dagger—will claim both [[lasca]] and the narrator.
