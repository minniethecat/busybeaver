# Larry Chittenden
> For example, one night in New Mexico a cowboy sang to me,
> in typical cowboy music, Larry Chittenden's entire "Cowboys' Christmas Ball";
> since that time the poem has often come to me in manuscript form
> as an original cowboy song.
>
> (P03-002)


Larry Chittenden (William Lawrence Chittenden, 1862–1934) was an American cowboy poet whose "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball" became one of the most celebrated poems in the cowboy canon — a piece so thoroughly absorbed into oral tradition that [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]], in the Introduction to *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, cited it as his prime example of how printed verse could become folk song in the West.

## Life & Context

Chittenden was born in New Jersey in 1862, but like many who wrote the West's songs, he came to the range from the East. His 1885 collection *Ranch Verses* contained "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball," the poem that would become his lasting legacy. By the time Lomax was collecting songs in the early 1900s, Chittenden's Christmas poem had entered the oral repertoire so completely that it was sung back to Lomax as an anonymous folk song.

Lomax tells the story in the Introduction: "one night in [[new-mexico|New Mexico]] a cowboy sang to me, in typical cowboy music, Larry Chittenden's entire '[[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball'; since that time the poem has often come to me in manuscript form as an original cowboy song" (P03-002). The poem was, Lomax noted, changed in oral transmission — "usually, it must be confessed, resulting in bettering the verse" (P03-002) — a phenomenon that illustrates the porous boundary between literary composition and folk tradition in cowboy poetry.

The poem, running over 150 lines, recounts a Christmas dance in Anson, [[texas|Texas]], with comic verve and detailed observation of cowboy social life. Its refrain — "Huh! hit wuz gittin' happy, 'The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball'" (048-001) — captures the high-spirited energy of the occasion.

## Significance

Chittenden's case is significant for what it reveals about authorship in the cowboy song tradition. His poem "entered" the folk repertoire so seamlessly that cowboys sang it as their own — a testament both to the poem's authenticity of voice and to the porous boundary between "literary" and "folk" in Western verse. The changes introduced in oral transmission, which Lomax noted "bettered the verse," also suggest a distinctive feature of the cowboy song tradition: collective, anonymous improvement was part of how songs lived. Chittenden's authorial identity was never entirely erased (his name appears in the anthology attribution), but the song's life exceeded its author's — exactly the fusion of written and oral culture that Lomax's anthology sought to document.
