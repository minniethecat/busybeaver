# The Bunkhouse Orchestra

## Overview

"The Bunkhouse Orchestra" offers the anthology's most vivid portrait of cowboy domesticity — the bunkhouse at night, with the day's long ride behind them, as seven men transform from weary drovers into a makeshift band. An anonymous poem placed in [[Part-II-The-Cowboy-Off-Guard|Part II]], it captures the cowboy "off guard" — not [[riding-imagery|riding]], not roping, not facing danger, but making music in the glow of a dobe fireplace. The poem's joyful energy and sonic texture make it one of the anthology's most immediate and endearing pieces.

## Text & Structure

The full poem text is available on the chapter page [[The-Bunkhouse-Orchestra]]. The anonymous poet structures the poem around the tension between the harshness of the day's ride and the warmth of the evening's music. Each stanza alternates between a recollection of trail hardship ("Freezy was the day's ride, lengthy was the trail" (046-001)) and the present-tense pleasure of the bunkhouse performance, always returning to the refrain-like invocation of "Turkey in the Straw" — the fiddle tune that organizes the poem's sound-world.

## Musical Analysis

### Instruments of the Trail

The poem inventories the cowboy orchestra: mouth-harps, banjos, and "old guitarra" (046-001) — portable instruments that could survive the trail. This instrumentation reflects the historical reality: cowboys carried harmonicas, Jew's harps, and small stringed instruments, and the bunkhouse was their concert hall. The "guitarra" (Spanish loanword) points to the Mexican influence on cowboy material culture, echoing the cross-cultural dynamics explored in "[[Lasca]]".

### "Turkey in the Straw" as Leitmotif

The fiddle tune "Turkey in the Straw" recurs through every stanza like a musical refrain, anchoring the poem's sound-world in a real piece of American vernacular music. The tune was already a standard by 1916 — it had been a minstrel-show staple, a dance-hall favorite, and a fiddler's showpiece for decades. By making it the bunkhouse band's signature piece, the poet connects the cowboy to a broader tradition of American folk [[singing-and-songmaking|music-making]].

### Sound as Community

The poem's sonic texture — twanging instruments, "coyote with the croup" (046-001) (comically bad singing), stamping feet — creates a portrait of music as communal practice, not polished performance. Quality doesn't matter; participation does. This is consistent with the anthology's broader representation of cowboy song as "the West's most original contribution" [P03-002], as Lomax wrote in the Introduction.

## Themes

### The Cowboy Off Guard

Placed in Part II ("The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Off Guard"), the poem embodies the section's organizing concept: the cowboy not as heroic rider but as ordinary man, relaxing with his comrades. The contrast between the day's hardships and the evening's pleasures structures the poem emotionally, making the bunkhouse warmth feel earned.

### Domesticity on the Trail

The bunkhouse — a crude structure where cowboys slept between trail drives — becomes in this poem a site of genuine domestic warmth. "Shinin' dobe fire-place, shadows on the wall" (046-001) — the adobe fireplace casts domestic light, transforming a rough shelter into a home. This revaluation of the cowboy's material culture connects to [[Cowboy-Culture]]'s analysis of bunkhouse life.

### The Comic Body

Shorty — the band's dancer and comic center — embodies the poem's celebration of the physical. "He laughed till he was raw" (046-001) after getting thrown from his horse, but tonight "he's done forgot it prancin' 'Turkey in the Straw'" (046-001). The body that endures on the trail becomes the body that celebrates in the bunkhouse — the poem insists on the wholeness of cowboy experience, its blend of suffering and joy.

## Historical Context

The poem's anonymity is significant — it suggests a piece of genuine bunkhouse folklore rather than a self-consciously literary composition. Lomax's decision to include it alongside the work of named poets like [[Charles-Badger-Clark]] and [[James-Barton-Adams]] reflects his editorial commitment to preserving the full range of cowboy expression, from polished verse to folk song.
