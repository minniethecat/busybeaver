## Overview

[[the-round-up|The round-up]] was the great gathering—the seasonal ritual in which cowboys from neighboring ranches converged to comb the open range, collect scattered herds, and sort cattle by brand. It was at once labor and festival: days of hard [[riding-imagery|riding]], nights of campfire fellowship, and the proving ground where a hand's reputation was made. In the poems collected by [[john-a-lomax|John Lomax]], the round-up provides the backdrop for tales of skill, humor, and danger.

The round-up appears across the collection in varied tones. "At a round-up on [[gila-river|the Gila]] / One sweet morning" (006-001) opens a ballad of courtship on the range, while another poem remembers how "every boy on the round-up swore / He never seed sich a horse" (008-001)—the gathering as the arena for [[cowboy-humor|tall tales]]. The crew itself becomes a fellowship: "we'll save 'em prime for the round-up time, and we [[riders-imagery|riders]]'ll all be there" (009-001). Yet the round-up also strips the land bare—"to roam the prairies / That the round-up crew has stripped" (023-001)—and a cowboy's impatient question, "How long's the round-up goin' to run?" (033-001), captures the grind behind the glamour.

## Significance

The round-up was more than a practical necessity; it was the defining institution of the open-range cattle industry. Before fences carved up the West, the spring and fall round-ups were the only mechanism for asserting ownership over livestock that wandered freely across hundreds of square miles. In Lomax's collection, the round-up serves as both literal setting and narrative frame—a gathering that brings characters together, sets stories in motion, and embodies the tension between freedom and labor that runs through all cowboy verse.
