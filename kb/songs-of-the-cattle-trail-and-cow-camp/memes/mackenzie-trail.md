## Overview

The Mackenzie Trail was one of the historic cattle and wagon routes of the Texas frontier, memorialized in [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s poem "[[The-Old-Mackenzie-Trail|The Old Mackenzie Trail]]" (066-001). Named for the frontier military commander Ranald S. Mackenzie — "Brave old Mackenzie long has laid him down / To rest beyond the trail that bears his name" — the route carried cattle drives, wagon trains, and immigrants westward across the Texas plains.

By the time Lomax collected his anthology, the trail was already a relic of a vanishing era: "It winds—this old forgotten cattle trail— / Through valleys still and silent even now" (066-001). The poem transforms the abandoned route into an elegy for the frontier itself, tracing its path from the Brazos River to its disappearance in the shifting sands of the [[llano-estacado|Llano Estacado]].

## Route

Lomax's poem maps the trail's course across Texas (066-001):

- **Brazos River** — eastern origin, crossing the divide
- **Colorado River** — major crossing point
- **Concho River** — through Concho's "broad, fair valley"
- **Abilene** — "by [[abilene|Abilene]] it climbs upon the plains"
- **Llano Estacado** — the trail reaches the Staked Plains before vanishing "in shifting rifts of sand"

## Historical Layers

The poem layers multiple eras of the trail's history (066-001):

- **Spanish exploration**: "De Vaca gathered piñons by the way" — Álvar Núñez Cabeza de Vaca, the Spanish explorer who traversed Texas in the 1530s
- **French exploration**: "La Salle, the gentle Frenchman, crossed this course, / And went to death and to a nameless grave" — René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle, murdered in Texas in 1687
- **Native presence**: "Comanches and Apaches from the north / Came sweeping southward"
- **Westward migration**: "the immigrants, / Who bore the westward fever in their brain"
- **Cattle era**: "the mavericks, winding stream of horns / To [[kansas|Kansas]] bound"

## In the Anthology

"[[The-Old-Mackenzie-Trail|The Old Mackenzie Trail]]" is one of the longest and most historically dense poems in *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*. Unlike most poems in the collection — which focus on a single character, event, or comic anecdote — Lomax's poem is a panoramic meditation on the trail as a palimpsest of frontier history, from pre-Columbian times through the cattle-drive era to its ghostly present.

The poem closes with a supernatural image: "when dusky night shuts down... One sees dim horsemen skimming o'er the plain / Hard by Mackenzie's trail" (066-001) — the ghost riders of the vanished frontier, still traveling westward.
