## Overview

Unbranded cattle roaming the open range—the [[maverick]] represented both opportunity (free wealth for the taking) and the lawless spirit of the unfenced West.

As depicted throughout *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, the [[maverick]] was an integral part of the cowboy's world—not merely a practical necessity but a source of identity, pride, and poetic inspiration.

## In the Anthology

The poems collected by [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] capture the [[maverick]] as lived experience rather than romanticized abstraction. The anthology preserves the [[maverick]] in vivid, memorable phrases:

> No more the mavericks, winding stream of horns
>
> (066-001)

This elegiac line captures the end of the open-range era — the "winding stream of horns" evokes the great herds of unbranded cattle moving across the unfenced plains, a sight that was already vanishing when Lomax published the anthology.

The [[maverick]] also appears through the figure of the cowboy who hunts them. "Chin [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] of sinful pride and [[maverick]]-hungry [[lasso|rope]]" (013-001) personifies the [[lasso|rope]] itself as hungry for unbranded cattle — the tool shares its owner's ambition. The phrase "[[maverick]]-hungry" condenses the acquisitive drive of the open-range cowboy into a single evocative compound.

## The Maverick as Metaphor

The term extends beyond cattle. A cowboy accused of evading honest work is asked: "Won't you 'low to quit a-playin' [[maverick]]?" (028-001) — the [[maverick]] becomes a metaphor for anyone who drifts outside the bounds of proper conduct and settled society.

## Cultural Significance

The [[maverick]] represents more than a practical facet of trail life; it embodies the values and self-image of the cowboy. The anthology preserves these details at the moment when the open-range era was fading, making it not only literature but a document of a vanished way of life.
