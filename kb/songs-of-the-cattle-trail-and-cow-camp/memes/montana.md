## Overview

Montana appears in the anthology as the trail's destination—the northern range toward which the great cattle drives moved from [[texas|Texas]]. The Introduction references "the long, long trail winding from [[texas|Texas]] to Montana" (P03-002), framing Montana as both geographic end point and symbolic destination. For the trail-driving cowboy, Montana meant the journey's completion, the delivery of the herd, and (for some) the start of a new life in new country.

Montana's open ranges in the 1870s–1880s represented the last [[the-west|frontier]] of the cattle industry—a vast, sparsely settled territory where the open-range system persisted longer than anywhere else in the United States. When the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology was published in 1916, Montana still carried the aura of the [[the-west|frontier]], even as railroads and settlement were closing the open range.

## Geographic Context

Montana Territory (statehood 1889) was the northern reach of the Great Plains cattle kingdom. Trails from [[texas|Texas]] terminated at railheads and ranges across Montana, where cattle were fattened on native grasses before shipment east. Major trail termini included Miles City and Glendive on the Northern Pacific Railroad. The distance from the [[Rio-Grande]] crossings in [[texas|Texas]] to the Montana ranges exceeded 1,500 miles—a journey of two to three months on the trail.

The Montana landscape—rolling plains, badlands, and the distant [[rockies|Rockies]]—was notably different from the [[texas|Texas]] brush country where the drives began. This geographic transition, from Southwestern aridity to Northern prairie, shaped the trail experience that the anthology's poems chronicle.

## In the Poems

Montana functions in the anthology as the horizon toward which the cowboy rides—a place imagined more than described, its distance defining the scale of the trail itself. Unlike [[Texas]], which appears richly imagined in several poems as a place of origin and identity, Montana is the silent destination: named but not dwelled upon, a promise rather than a presence.

This asymmetry—[[texas|Texas]] as rich origin, Montana as abstract destination—maps onto the emotional geography of the cattle trail. The cowboy leaves a known world ([[texas|Texas]], home, the familiar) and rides toward an unknown one (Montana, the northern range, the future). The trail poems are thus narratives of departure more than arrival.

The Introduction's framing of "the long, long trail winding from [[texas|Texas]] to Montana" (P03-002) captures this: the trail is named by its endpoints, but its poetry happens in between, in the journey itself.

## Symbolic Significance

### Destination and Promise

Montana in the anthology represents the economic logic of the cattle industry—the trail existed because Montana ranges and railheads created markets for [[texas|Texas]] cattle. But it also carries symbolic weight as the cowboy's horizon: the place where the work ends, the pay is collected, and (perhaps) a new chapter begins.

### The Last Open Range

By 1916, when Lomax published the anthology, Montana's open-range era was ending. The catastrophic winter of 1886–1887 had already demonstrated the limits of open-range ranching, and barbed wire was closing the plains. Montana in the anthology is thus doubly nostalgic—a memory of a destination that, by the time the poems were collected, no longer represented the [[the-west|frontier]] it once had.
