## Overview

The prairie is the cowboy's true home—not the ranch house or the town but the open grassland that stretches beyond sight in every direction. In the poetry of [[the-cattle-trail|the cattle trail]], the prairie is both a physical reality and a spiritual condition: it is where the cattle graze, where the trail unwinds, and where the cowboy confronts the scale of his own existence against an unbroken horizon.

> Oh, the last steer has been branded
> And the last beef has been shipped,
> And I'm free to roam the prairies
> That the round-up crew has swept;
>
> (023-001)

## In the Anthology

The prairie carries memory. One poem reflects on "the past, when the prairie was big and the cattle / Were all over the country" (019-001)—a time before fences and settlement, when the open range seemed endless. The word "big" does double work here: it describes the physical expanse and also the largeness of spirit that the open country fostered in the men who rode it.

For the working cowboy, the prairie is where freedom lives. "I'm free to roam the prairies / That the [[round-up]] crew has swept" (023-001)—the declaration links physical movement to personal liberty. To roam the prairie is to exercise the cowboy's most fundamental right: the right to go where the work and the cattle lead, unconstrained by walls or fences.

The prairie is also a place of mystery. The [[wind-imagery|wind]] comes "a-sighing in the prairie grass" (034-001), carrying sounds that might be natural or might be something else—a voice calling from the beyond, a presence felt but not seen. The prairie's emptiness makes it a theater for the imagination, where every sound carries implication.

Even the prairie's smallest inhabitants register its vastness. "The prairie dogs are sneezin'" (048-001) in a landscape so empty that their small sounds carry. The prairie dog's sneeze is a comic detail, but it also underscores the silence that surrounds it—in a louder world, no one would notice.

## Cultural Significance

The prairie is the essential landscape of cowboy identity. Unlike [[mountain-imagery|the mountains]]—which block and channel—or the desert—which threatens—the prairie offers openness and possibility. It is the landscape of the trail drive, where the horizon is always ahead and the route is chosen by the cowboy's judgment rather than by geography. In the anthology's verse, to lose the prairie is to lose the conditions that made the cowboy possible.
