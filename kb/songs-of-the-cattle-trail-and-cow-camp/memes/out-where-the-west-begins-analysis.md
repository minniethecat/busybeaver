# Out Where the West Begins

## Overview

"Out Where the West Begins" opens [[Part-I-Cowboy-Yarns|Part I]] of "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" as a declaration of regional identity and moral geography. Written by [[Charles-Badger-Clark]] and placed as the first poem in the first section, it serves as the anthology's manifesto — defining the West not by longitude but by the quality of its human relationships. The poem's signature claim, "Out where the handclasp's a little stronger / Out where the smile dwells a little longer" (003-001), establishes the [[Cowboy-Culture|cowboy's world]] as a realm where character, not circumstance, defines place.

## Text & Structure

The full poem text is available on the chapter page [[Out-Where-the-West-Begins]]. Clark employs a three-stanza structure, each beginning with the anaphoric "Out where..." refrain and each closing with the poem's title line, creating a litany that builds emotional and rhetorical force through repetition.

| Stanza | Focus | Key Imagery | Refrain |
|--------|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Stanza 1** | Human bonds | Handclasp, smile, home | "That's where the West begins" |
| **Stanza 2** | Natural world | Brighter sun, whiter snows, wider spaces | "That's where the West begins" |
| **Stanza 3** | Moral character | Men defined by integrity, not wealth | "That's where the West begins" |

The poem's architecture moves deliberately from the interpersonal (stanza 1) through the environmental (stanza 2) to the ethical (stanza 3), constructing an argument that the West is ultimately a moral condition — not a place on a map but a quality of soul. As Lomax notes in the Introduction, the anthology sought to preserve "the West as the cowboy knew it" (P03-001), and Clark's poem gives that knowing its definitive form.

## Rhetorical Analysis

### Anaphora as Declaration

The repeated "Out where..." constructs a geography defined by absence and presence simultaneously: absence of the constraints of Eastern civilization, presence of authentic human connection. Each line begins with the same two words, and each builds toward the same closing — the effect is incantatory, almost liturgical, transforming a poem about place into a creed about character.

### Contrast with the Implied East

Clark never explicitly names the East, but the contrast is everywhere present. "A little stronger" and "a little longer" are comparative adjectives — stronger than what? longer than what? The answer is the implied Eastern norm against which the West measures itself as more authentic. This technique allows the poem to define the West without ever descending into polemic.

> "Out where the handclasp's a little stronger, / Out where the smile dwells a little longer" (003-001)

### Landscape as Moral Argument

The natural imagery of Stanza 2 — "brighter sunshine," "whiter snows," "wider spaces" — does double work. It describes the actual geography of the American West while also suggesting moral qualities: brightness (clarity, transparency), whiteness (purity), wideness (generosity, openness). The [[The-Cattle-Trail|Western landscape]] becomes a metaphor for the cowboy's character.

## Themes

### Regional Identity as Moral Geography

The poem's controlling idea — that the West is a quality of character, not a cartographic fact — echoes throughout the anthology. Other poets in the collection, including [[James-Barton-Adams]] and [[Henry-Herbert-Knibbs]], explore similar themes, but Clark's poem gives the idea its most concentrated expression.

### Home and Belonging

The poem's final stanza culminates in an image of home: "That's where the home is" (003-001). For the cowboy, who spent months on the trail far from any domestic space, "home" was a powerful and complex concept — sometimes the ranch, sometimes the open range itself, sometimes a person rather than a place.

## Historical Context

Clark wrote this poem in the early twentieth century, when the open-range cattle industry that had defined the cowboy era was giving way to fenced pastures, railroads, and settlement. The poem can be read as a defense of the cowboy's world against these encroachments — a claim that what mattered about the West was not its economic productivity but its moral character.

The decision to open the anthology with this poem was Lomax's, not Clark's, and it reflects Lomax's editorial vision: the collection was not merely a grab-bag of Western verse but an argument about the value of cowboy culture, which Lomax saw as "the West's most original contribution to American life" (P03-002).
