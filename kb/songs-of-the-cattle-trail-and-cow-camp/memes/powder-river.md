## Overview

The Powder River flows northward through Wyoming and [[montana|Montana]], draining the [[the-west|west]]ern slope of the Bighorn Mountains into the Yellowstone River. In the lexicon of cowboy poetry, the Powder River country represents the northernmost reach of the open-range cattle industry—a region of tough horses, tough men, and legendary [[the-round-up|roundup]]s.

The river gave its name to the Powder River Basin, a vast grassland that supported some of the largest cattle operations of the open-range era. In the 1880s and 1890s, the basin was the scene of the Johnson County War, a violent conflict between large cattle barons and small ranchers that became emblematic of the range wars that shaped the cowboy's world.

Two poems in the anthology invoke the Powder River as a landmark of the [[montana|northern range]]. In both, the river marks not a crossing point but a geographical zone where exceptional challenges arise—an unbreakable bronco in one, and a braggart cowboy's mythical ascension in the other.

## Geography

The Powder River rises in the Bighorn Mountains of north-central Wyoming and flows northward for approximately 375 miles through Wyoming and southeastern [[montana|Montana]] before joining the Yellowstone River near Terry, [[montana|Montana]]. Unlike many Western rivers, the Powder is often shallow and braided—its name derives not from gunpowder but from the fine, powdery sand along its banks.

The surrounding Powder River Basin, a structural geologic depression rich in coal and grassland, covers much of northeastern Wyoming. During the cattle trail era, the basin's open range and reliable water made it prime cattle country, drawing herds from [[texas|Texas]] along the northern trails.

## In the Poems

"The Legend of Boastful [[bill|Bill]]" uses the Powder River as the northern boundary of cowboy country. [[bill|Bill]] boasts: "I can ride the highest liver / 'Twixt the Gulf an' Powder River" (006-001), drawing a line from the Gulf of Mexico to the Powder as the full extent of the cattle kingdom. The river serves as the northern terminus of cowboy competence—beyond it lies territory where neither [[bill|Bill]] nor any cowboy of his acquaintance can claim mastery.

"The Bronc That Wouldn't Bust" opens with the narrator's hard-won humility: "I've had the knack of bustin' broncs since I was seventeen, / But I struck one on Powder River / And say, hands, he was the first / And only living broncho / That your servant couldn't burst" (044-001). The Powder River bronco—a "no-count buckskin" that "took natural to the air" and launched the cowboy skyward—represents the exceptional challenge that the [[montana|northern range]] reserves for even the most confident [[riders-imagery|riders]].

In both poems, the Powder River functions as more than geography. It is a mythic boundary where the ordinary rules of cowboy life bend, and where pride meets its reckoning.
