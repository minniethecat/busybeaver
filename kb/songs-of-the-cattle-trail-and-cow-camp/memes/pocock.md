## Overview

Pocock is a poet known for his dialect verse narrative *Curley*, which is quoted as an epigraph in four poems throughout the Lomax anthology: "The Chase" (ch030), "A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s Son" (ch036), "A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Song" (ch037), and "The End of the Trail" (ch078). His work is characterized by colloquial cowboy speech, sentimental reflections on motherhood and love, and a distinctive use of dialect spelling to capture the sound of [[the-west|frontier]] English.

## Literary Significance

Pocock's *Curley* serves as a touchstone of cowboy dialect verse within the Lomax anthology. Each poem that quotes him uses a fragment of *Curley* as an epigraph, suggesting that Lomax considered Pocock's work representative of an authentic cowboy voice.

The quoted fragments span a wide emotional range: from the playful flirtation of "Why, the darling! She's waiting to see!" (030-001) to the tender maternal anxiety of "Will y'u care for yo' mothah, little boy?" (036-001), from the romantic devotion of the *Lolita* stanza (037-001) to the elegiac finality of "We need you any mo'!" (078-001). This range suggests that *Curley* was a substantial narrative work covering the full arc of a cowboy's life and relationships.

The dialect conventions — *yo'* for "your," *mothah* for "mother," *heah* for "here," *befo'* for "before," *mo'* for "more" — place Pocock in the tradition of late nineteenth-century regionalist poetry that sought to capture spoken vernacular on the page.

> Here's a moccasin track in the drifts,
> It's no more than the length of my hand;
> An' her instep—just see how it lifts!
> If that ain't the best in the land!
> For the maid ran as free as the wind—
> Why, the darling! She's waiting to see!
>
> (030-001)
