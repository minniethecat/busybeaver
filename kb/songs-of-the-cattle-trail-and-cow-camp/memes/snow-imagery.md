## Overview

Snow appears in 9 passages in the anthology, almost always on mountains rather than on the ground where the cowboy rides. "The snow is on the mountains" (046-001) is the characteristic formulation: snow as distant decoration rather than immediate experience. This distance is significant—snow marks the boundary between the [[cowboy-culture|cowboy]]'s world (warm plains, working range) and the higher, colder, less habitable world of the peaks.

Snow's whiteness carries symbolic weight. In a landscape dominated by browns, grays, and greens, snow is the only pure white—associated with cleanliness, purity, and the sublime. It is the color of distance, of aspiration, of what is beautiful precisely because it is out of reach.

## Examples

### Snow as Seasonal Marker

Snow tracks winter's arrival:

> "For the snow is on the mountains and the wind is on the plain" (046-001)

The parallel clauses join mountain snow and plains wind as coordinated seasonal facts—winter expressed at two different altitudes simultaneously.

### Snow as Distance

Snow defines remoteness:

> "Where the mountains are covered with snow" [P03-001]

From the anthology's opening invocation, snow establishes the West's defining landscape—the peaks are white, and that whiteness signals both beauty and inaccessibility.

### Snow and Contrast

Snow's whiteness creates visual contrast:

The white peaks against [[sky-imagery|blue sky]], the white snow against gray rock—snow provides the anthology's starkest color contrasts, the visual equivalent of the desert's "lava rock and glaring sand" in a different palette.

## Poetic Function

Snow imagery serves three purposes:

1. **Seasonal Gauge** — Snow on mountains means winter; its presence or absence tracks the year.

2. **Distance Marker** — Snow is always on peaks, never underfoot; it defines the visual limit of the cowboy's world.

3. **Purity Symbol** — Snow's whiteness carries connotations of cleanliness and transcendence rare in the anthology's otherwise earthy, pragmatic sensibility.
