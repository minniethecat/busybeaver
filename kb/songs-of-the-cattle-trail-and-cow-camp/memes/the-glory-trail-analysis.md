# The Glory Trail

## Overview

"The Glory Trail" is [[Charles-Badger-Clark]]'s comic masterpiece in "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" — a mock-heroic narrative in which a cowboy named [[High-Chin-Bob]] roping a mountain lion becomes an epic quest for glory. Set "way high up the Mogollons" (012-001) in the [[arizona|Arizona]] mountains, the poem transforms a routine predator kill into a celebration of cowboy self-mythologizing: [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s triumphant refrain, "Oh, glory be to me" (012-001), is both absurd and oddly magnificent.

## Text & Structure

The full poem text is available on the chapter page [[The-Glory-Trail]]. Clark structures the poem as a three-act comedy: the lion's discovery and [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s arrival (Act I), the [[lasso|rope]]-and-toss struggle (Act II), and the triumphant return to camp with the "scalp" (Act III). Each act is punctuated by [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s refrain — "Oh, glory be to me" — whose repetition transforms self-praise into ritual.

| Act | Action | [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s State | Comic Device |
|-----|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Act I** | Lion found, [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] arrives | Confident | His magnificent self-regard |
| **Act II** | Roping and struggle | Mortal danger | Lion tosses him "like a bug" |
| **Act III** | Triumph & storytelling | Victorious | "Fame's unfadin' flowers" |

## Comic Technique

### The Mock-Heroic Register

Clark's central comic strategy is mock-heroic inflation: a cowboy roping a mountain lion is treated with the diction and formal weight of epic poetry. "Oh, glory be to me... / And fame's unfadin' flowers!" (012-001) — [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] speaks like Achilles, but his battleground is the Mogollon Rim and his enemy a predator harassing yearling cattle. The gap between rhetoric and reality is the poem's engine of humor.

### The Anti-Heroic Detail

Clark punctures [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s self-glorification with physical comedy. The lion "tossed him like a bug" (012-001); [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] ends up "scramblin' through the brush" (012-001); his triumphant return involves a lion pelt that smells. These details don't mock [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] so much as humanize him — his bravado is genuine, and his physical struggles make it earned.

> "'Oh, glory be to me,' says he, / 'And fame's unfadin' flowers! / All meddlin' hands are far away; / I ride my good top-hawse today / And I'm top-rope of the Lazy J — / Hi! kitty cat, you're ours!'" (012-001)

### The Solo Cowboy

Structurally, the poem makes much of [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s [[loneliness|solitude]] — "All meddlin' hands are far away" (012-001). No one witnesses his heroism except himself, the lion, and the mountain landscape. This [[loneliness|isolation]] is both the condition of his glory (no one to contradict his version) and its pathos (no one to celebrate with). The poem ends with [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] [[riding-imagery|riding]] into camp, ready to tell the tale — and the reader understands that the storytelling will be as much a performance as the hunt itself.

## Themes

### Glory and Self-Fashioning

The poem's central theme — that glory is as much a story you tell as a deed you do — resonates with the anthology's broader interest in cowboy self-representation. [[high-chin-bob|Bob]] doesn't just kill the lion; he narrates his killing of the lion, in elevated language, to an imagined audience. This self-mythologizing impulse connects to [[The-Cowboy-and-His-Song]]'s argument that cowboy song is the cowboy's way of writing himself into history.

### Man vs. Wild

The poem stages one of the anthology's most explicit confrontations between cowboy and wilderness. The lion — "a lion cleaned a yearlin's bones / And licked his thankful chops" (012-001) — is a genuine threat, a predator that kills cattle. [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s victory is not against a symbolic wildness but against a real animal that the cowboy economy needs removed. Yet Clark's treatment is never merely instrumental: the lion is magnificent, the landscape sublime, and [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s triumph is as much about mastering his own fear as about killing a predator.

### The High Country

Set in the Mogollons (the Mogollon Rim of [[arizona|Arizona]]), the poem's mountain setting distinguishes it from the plains-and-prairie poems that dominate the anthology. The "way high up" (012-001) geography literalizes [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s quest for elevation — he seeks glory, so he climbs — and the mountain landscape's verticality reinforces the poem's mock-heroic architecture.

## Historical Context

The Mogollon Rim country of [[arizona|Arizona]] was part of the last open-range cattle territory in the American West. By the time Clark wrote this poem (likely early 1900s), the mountain lion was already being systematically exterminated by ranchers and government hunters — a campaign that would continue through the twentieth century. Clark's comic treatment doesn't engage with this ecological dimension directly, but the poem's matter-of-fact violence toward the lion reflects the practical reality of the cowboy's relationship with predators.
