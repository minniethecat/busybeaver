# Snagtooth Sal

## Overview

"[[Snagtooth-Sal]]" is one of the anthology's most tonally distinctive poems — a comic ballad about lost love that oscillates between boisterous humor and genuine [[loss|grief]]. Written by Lowell O. Reese and first published in the *Saturday Evening Post* before its inclusion in "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]", the poem captures the cowboy's emotional register at its most colloquial: heartbreak expressed not through high diction but through vernacular swagger and self-deprecating wit.

## Text & Structure

The full poem text is available on the chapter page [[Snagtooth-Sal]]. Reese employs a refrain structure alternating between narrative stanzas (each beginning with the narrator's past-tense reminiscence) and a four-line refrain beginning "[[snagtooth-sal|Sal]], [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]], / My heart is broke today" (025-001). A concluding quatrain provides the epitaph: "Here lies a lovin' an' a busted heart, begod!" (025-001).

| Section | Purpose | Tone |
|---------|---------|------|
| **Narrative stanzas** | Past-tense reminiscence of walking with [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]] | Nostalgic, humorous |
| **Refrain** | Present-tense [[loss|grief]] ("My heart is broke today") | Mournful, raw |
| **Epitaph** | Self-written gravestone inscription | Comic-heroic |

The interplay between these registers — the comic past and the grieving present — creates the poem's distinctive emotional texture. The humor doesn't undercut the [[loss|grief]]; it makes it more bearable, more recognizably human.

## Comic Technique

### Vernacular Voice

The poem's diction is deliberately and aggressively colloquial: "Happy as a lizard in the wavin' chaparral" (025-001), "busted heart, begod!" (025-001). This vernacular register serves two functions. It authenticates the cowboy speaker's voice — this is how a cowboy on the [[Laramie]] streets would talk — and it creates a tonal armor over the poem's genuine emotional wound. The speaker can only express [[loss|grief]] by wrapping it in comedy.

### The Pathos of Humor

Reese's comic technique is classically elegiac: the poem's funniest moments are also its saddest. "I would give creation to be walkin' with my gal — / Walkin' down through [[laramie|Laramie]] with [[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" (025-001) — the hyperbole ("give creation") is comic in its extravagance and heartbreaking in its sincerity. This is the cowboy's stoicism expressed not as silence but as deflection-through-humor.

## Themes

### Love and Loss in Cowboy Culture

"[[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" shares thematic ground with "[[Lasca]]" — both are poems about a cowboy who loses his woman — but their treatments could hardly differ more. [[lasca|Lasca]]'s narrator is shattered into silence; [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]]'s narrator processes [[loss|grief]] by turning it into performance. Together they suggest that the cowboy's emotional range was wider than the stoic stereotype allows, encompassing both tragic silence and comic expression.

### The Walk as Metaphor

The poem's central image — "Walkin' down through [[laramie|Laramie]] with [[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" (025-001) — elevates an ordinary act (walking) to the poem's emotional lodestone. In the narrative stanzas, walking with [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]] is the image of happiness; in the refrain, [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]] walks no more; in the epitaph, the narrator imagines being buried where he can never walk again. The progression from movement to stasis maps onto the emotional arc from joy to [[loss|grief]].

### Epitaph as Self-Fashioning

The poem's closing quatrain — "Plant a little stone above the little mound of sod; / Write: 'Here lies a lovin' an' a busted heart, begod!'" (025-001) — is a self-written epitaph, the cowboy authoring his own memorial. This gesture of self-fashioning connects to the broader anthology theme of the cowboy creating his own mythology (see [[The-Cowboy-and-His-Song]]).

## Historical Context

Reese's publication in the *Saturday Evening Post* marks "[[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" as one of the anthology's more commercially successful poems — the *Post* was a mass-circulation magazine that brought Western verse to a national audience. The poem's blend of humor, vernacular, and sentiment suited popular taste while also giving voice to genuine cowboy experience.
