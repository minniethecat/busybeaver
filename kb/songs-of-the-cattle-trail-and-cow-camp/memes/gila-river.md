## Overview

The Gila River is a tributary of the [[colorado|Colorado]] that flows through the arid Southwest, carving a route through what is now southern [[arizona|Arizona]] and [[new-mexico|New Mexico]]. During the cattle trail era, its valleys provided water and grazing for herds being driven across the desert, and its banks served as natural campsites for [[round-up]]s.

In the poetry of the cowboy, the Gila carries a dual identity: by daylight it is the scene of everyday ranch work—a [[round-up]] ground where cowboys test their mettle against bucking horses; by night and in misfortune, it becomes part of the "[[The-Gila-Monster-Route|Gila Monster Route]]," a landscape of abandonment where a man might find himself "ditched" with "nothing in sight but sand and space" (070-001).

## In the Poems

### [[The-Legend-of-Boastful-Bill|The Legend of Boastful Bill]] (Chapter 6)

The Gila provides the setting for one of the collection's great comic tales of cowboy bravado. The poem opens with the [[round-up]] crew assembled on the riverbank:

> At a round-up on the Gila
> One sweet morning long ago,
> Ten of us was throwed quite freely
> By a hoss from Idaho. (006-001)

When none of the crew can stay on the Northern horse, Boastful [[bill|Bill]] steps forward with a declaration of his range-wide credentials: "I can ride the highest liver / 'Twixt the Gulf an' [[powder-river|Powder River]], / An' I'll break this thing as easy as I'd flirt" (006-001). The Gila [[round-up]] ground becomes the arena for this contest of cowboy honor, the river's name grounding the tall tale in a real Southwestern geography.

### [[The-Gila-Monster-Route|The Gila Monster Route]] (Chapter 70)

In stark contrast to the comic bravado of Boastful [[bill|Bill]], "The Gila Monster Route" paints a grim picture of the desert the river defines. The poem opens with a man abandoned beside the tracks:

> The lingering sunset across the plain
> Kissed the rear-end door of an east-bound train... (070-001)

The stranded man watches his only hope of rescue disappear: "As she pulled away in the falling light / He could see the gleam of her red tail-light" (070-001). Then the full weight of his [[loneliness|isolation]] descends:

> Then the moon arose and the stars came out—
> He was ditched on the Gila Monster Route. (070-001)

The poem catalogues the emptiness: "Nothing in sight but sand and space; / No chance for a gink to feed his face; / Not even a shack to [[The-Gila-Monster-Route|The Gila Monster Route]]01). The Gila Monster Route, named for the venomous lizard native to the region, represents the desert at its most unforgiving—a place where the railroad passes through but offers no refuge to those cast off along its path.
