## Overview

The Bitterroot Valley in [[the-west|west]]ern [[montana|Montana]] appears in the anthology as the promised land of the cattle trail—"[[The-Land-of-the-Bitterroot|The Land of the Bitterroot]]" (072-001), where grass grows tall and water runs clear. For cowboys who had driven herds across the arid plains of [[texas|Texas]] and Wyoming, the Bitterroot represented the reward at the end of the long trail north.

> I admire the artificial art of the East;
> But I love more the inimitable art of the West,
> Where nature's handiwork lies in virginal beauty.
> Amidst the hum of city life and the clang of factory bells,
> In the land of the Bitterroot—
> The Indian land—Land of the Kootenai,
> I can tread earth's unmanacled space, a king, and free.
>
> (072-001)

## In the Anthology

The valley is celebrated in a poem that contrasts the harsh conditions of the southern trail with the lush abundance of the [[montana|northern range]]s. The poem places the Bitterroot in its geographic and cultural context: "In the land of the Bitterroot—The Indian land" (072-001), acknowledging both the valley's Native American heritage and its significance as the northern terminus of the "long, long trail winding from [[texas|Texas]] to [[montana|Montana]]" (P03-002).

## Geographic Context

The Bitterroot Valley lies between the Bitterroot Range and the Sapphire Mountains in what is now [[the-west|west]]ern [[montana|Montana]]. During the open-range era, it was prime cattle country, attracting herds driven north from [[texas|Texas]] along the great trail systems (P03-002).

## Significance

The Bitterroot represents the northern terminus of the cattle trail—the place where [[trail-driving|the long drive]] ended and a new life began. Its presence in the anthology balances the poems of hardship and danger with a vision of pastoral contentment.
