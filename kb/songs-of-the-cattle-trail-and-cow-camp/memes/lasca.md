# Lasca
> I want free life, and I want fresh air;
> And I sigh for the canter after the cattle,
> The crack of the whips like shots in battle,
> The green beneath and the blue above,
> And dash and danger, and life and love—
> And Lasca!
>
> (010-001)


Lasca is the title character and heroine of one of the most dramatic narrative poems in the Lomax anthology — a tragic love story set on the [[texas|Texas]]-Mexico border, in which a wild-spirited Mexican vaquera rides, loves, and ultimately dies saving her Anglo cowboy lover from a [[stampede]] of cattle.

## Character Background

Lasca is introduced in the poem as the narrator's [[riding-imagery|riding]] companion and lover — a figure defined by her freedom, her physical grace, and her fierce devotion. The poem opens with her name and the narrator's desire for open range life: "And Lasca! Lasca used to ride / On a mouse-grey [[mustang]] close to my side" (010-001). Her characterization is brief but vivid — she is "bold as the billows that beat," "wild as the breezes that blow," and "swayed in her suppleness to and fro" (010-001) — a figure of untamed nature as much as of human love.

Unlike many literary depictions of Mexican women in early 20th-century American verse, Lasca is portrayed with dignity and genuine passion. She knows no books or creeds, only "an Ave Maria" (010-001), and her loyalty is absolute: "Little she cared save to be at my side, / To ride with me, and ever to ride" (010-001).

## Key Appearances

Lasca appears exclusively in the poem that bears her name:

| Poem | PN Range | Author |
|------|----------|--------|
| "Lasca" | (010-001)–(010-001) | [[frank-desprez|Frank Desprez]] |

The poem's narrative arc moves from idyllic companionship through the approach of a cattle herd — "the thunder of thousands of hoofs" (010-001) — to Lasca's climactic act of self-sacrifice: she throws herself between the narrator and the [[stampede]], taking the fatal impact that was meant for him.

## Significance

Lasca occupies a distinctive position in the anthology's character gallery. Unlike the comic figures of cowboy yarns (such as [[Snagtooth-Sal]] or "[[texas-tom|Texas Tom]]"), Lasca's story is tragic and romantic in the high literary sense — closer to the tradition of *Carmen* or other operatic borderland heroines. Her [[death-imagery|death]] in the [[stampede]] makes her one of the few characters in the collection whose fate is not humorous but genuinely mournful, adding a register of tragic seriousness to an anthology otherwise dominated by the comic, the sentimental, and the celebratory.
