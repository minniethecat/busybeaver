# Clark and Adams: Two Poetic Visions of the West

## Overview

"[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" includes poems by dozens of contributors, but two poets stand out for the frequency and quality of their contributions: [[Charles-Badger-Clark]] and [[James-Barton-Adams]]. Together they anchor the anthology's literary ambitions, but they represent fundamentally different poetic sensibilities. Clark is the moral geographer — the poet who defines the West as a quality of character, whose poems move from landscape to ethics. Adams is the social observer — the poet who captures the human texture of cowboy life, the bunkhouse conversation and the trail-side encounter. Read together, they give the anthology a stereoscopic depth that neither alone could provide.

## [[charles-badger-clark|Charles Badger Clark]]: The Moral Geographer

Clark's signature poem, "[[Out-Where-the-West-Begins]]," placed at the very opening of Part I, announces his project: to define the West not by longitude but by the quality of its human relationships.

> Out where the handclasp's a little stronger, That's where the West begins (003-001)

The comparative adjective implies an East that is just a little weaker in its handclasps, and the entire poem lives in this space of implied moral contrast. [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] himself acknowledged Clark's centrality, citing "[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]" as a prime example of the authentic cowboy poem (P03-002).

Clark's characteristic moves:
- **Anaphoric elevation**: The repeated "Out where..." structure transforms description into incantation
- **Moralized landscape**: Natural features (brighter sun, whiter snows, wider spaces) become metaphors for human virtues
- **The solo voice**: Clark's poems tend toward the declarative, the individual perspective, the single voice making a claim about the world

His comic poems — notably "[[The-Glory-Trail]]" — reveal another side: the ability to puncture his own elevated register with physical comedy and self-deprecating humor.

> Oh, glory be to me, says he, And fame's my happy lot (012-001)

[[High-Chin-Bob]] roping a mountain lion while declaring "Oh, glory be to me" is Clark's mock-heroic answer to his own earnestness (013-001). The glory runs through Clark's work — his horse declares "My glory sings in the toil they crave" (009-001) — as a persistent motif linking labor and transcendence.

## [[james-barton-adams|James Barton Adams]]: The Social Observer

Adams's poems operate in a different key. Where Clark makes declarations, Adams tells stories — often with multiple voices, shifting perspectives, and a keen attention to social dynamics. His byline appears on nine poems across the anthology (011-001) (021-001) (029-001) (041-001) (047-001) (050-001) (073-001) (076-001), each capturing a distinct social situation within cowboy life.

Adams's characteristic moves:
- **Social texture**: Poems populated with specific people in specific situations
- **Multiple registers**: The ability to shift between comic, tender, and elegiac tones within a single poem
- **The communal voice**: Adams's poems often suggest a community of speakers, not just a single voice

Where Clark places the cowboy against the landscape, Adams places him against other people — in the bunkhouse, on the trail, at the dance. Clark's cowboy is defined by his relationship to the West; Adams's cowboy is defined by his relationships to other cowboys.

> heigh-toned dance (047-001) — Adams's comic observation of cowboy social life

## Complementary Visions

The anthology's selection places Clark and Adams in implicit dialogue. Clark's elevated moral register needs Adams's social comedy to ground it; Adams's attention to social texture needs Clark's moral geography to give it depth. Together they model two ways of singing the cowboy — the declarative and the narrative, the solitary and the communal, the moral and the social.

This complementarity reflects a broader tension in the anthology between the cowboy as symbol (the West personified, the knight-errant Lomax described) and the cowboy as person (the working hand, the man at the dance, the friend in the bunkhouse). Both are true, and the anthology's greatness lies in its refusal to choose between them.

## Beyond the Pair

The anthology includes other poetic voices — [[Henry-Herbert-Knibbs]], [[Arthur-Chapman]], [[Frank-Desprez]] (author of "[[Lasca]]"), and many anonymous contributors — who enrich the Clark-Adams framework. But Clark and Adams remain its twin pillars, and their complementary visions define the anthology's range: from the solitary moral claim to the crowded social world, from the elevated to the everyday, from the West as idea to the West as life.
