# Texas and Kansas: Origin and Destination in the Cowboy's Emotional Geography

## Overview

In "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]," two place names recur with a frequency and symbolic weight far exceeding their geographic significance: [[Texas]] and [[Kansas]]. Together they form the emotional axis of the cattle trail — [[texas|Texas]] as origin, [[kansas|Kansas]] as destination — but the symmetry is deceptive. [[texas|Texas]] is richly imagined as a place of identity and belonging; [[kansas|Kansas]] is a waypoint, a place of transaction and departure. This asymmetry reveals something essential about the cowboy's emotional world: the trail is a journey away from home, and its poetry is poetry of departure, not arrival.

## The Two Endpoints

### Texas: The Rich Origin

[[texas|Texas]] appears throughout the anthology as the cowboy's place of origin — not merely a geographic fact but a source of identity. In "[[Out-Where-the-West-Begins]]" and other poems, [[texas|Texas]] is the "home" the cowboy leaves behind, the country whose values he carries with him. The Introduction frames [[texas|Texas]] as the trail's starting point: "the long, long trail winding from [[texas|Texas]] to [[montana|Montana]]" [P03-002]. But where [[montana|Montana]] recedes into abstraction, [[texas|Texas]] remains vividly present — a place of specific landscapes (the brush country, the [[pecos|Pecos]], the [[rio-grande|Rio Grande]]) and specific qualities (independence, authenticity, size).

The semantic field around [[texas|Texas]] in the poems includes words like "home," "range," "belonging," and "freedom" — the vocabulary of identity and rootedness. Even when the cowboy is hundreds of miles from [[texas|Texas]], its presence in the poems is the presence of an absent center: the place he measures all other places against.

### Kansas: The Functional Destination

[[Kansas]] appears in the anthology in a different register. It is the destination of the mid-trail drives — the railheads at [[Abilene]] and [[dodge-city|Dodge City]] where cattle were sold and shipped east — but it rarely acquires the emotional resonance that [[texas|Texas]] carries. [[kansas|Kansas]] in the poems is functional: a place to deliver cattle, collect pay, and (usually) leave. It is the site of transaction, not transformation.

This is not to say [[kansas|Kansas]] is absent from the poems — "[[kansas|Kansas]] bluff" appears in "[[Lasca]]" (010-001) as a landscape image — but its appearances are topographic rather than symbolic. [[kansas|Kansas]] is a place on the map; [[texas|Texas]] is a place in the soul.

## The Asymmetry and Its Meaning

The [[texas|Texas]]-[[kansas|Kansas]] asymmetry maps onto a deeper structure in the anthology: the privileging of origin over destination, departure over arrival. The cowboy's emotional world is organized around what he leaves, not what he reaches. [[kansas|Kansas]] as destination is necessary but not celebrated; [[texas|Texas]] as origin is celebrated but not always explicitly — it is the unspoken ground against which all other places are measured.

This asymmetry also reflects the historical reality of the cattle drives. [[texas|Texas]] was home — the ranches, the families, the communities that produced the cowboy culture. [[kansas|Kansas]] was a railhead — a temporary destination, a point of sale, a place the cowboy passed through rather than inhabited. The poetry accurately records this economic geography even as it transforms it into symbol.

## Beyond the Binary

The anthology does not entirely reduce to this binary. [[Montana]] functions as the "far destination" beyond [[kansas|Kansas]], and [[New-Mexico]] and [[Colorado]] appear as intermediate regions with their own characters. The trail itself — embodied in the concept page [[The-Cattle-Trail]] — is the space between origin and destination, and it is in this between-space that most of the anthology's poetry actually unfolds. [[texas|Texas]] and [[kansas|Kansas]] are the conceptual poles; the poetry happens in the movement from one to the other.
