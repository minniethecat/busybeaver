## Overview

Charles Pitt is a poet whose sole contribution to the Lomax anthology is "The Bandit's Grave" (ch065), a short elegy set in the desert landscape. The poem memorializes an unnamed border bandit buried "'Neath the desert's brassy skies" with only cactus and wild poppy as his monuments.

## Literary Context

"The Bandit's Grave" is a compact desert elegy that transforms a criminal's burial site into a scene of austere natural beauty. The poem opens with stark geographic imagery: "'Mid lava rock and glaring sand, / 'Neath the desert's brassy skies" (065-001). The bandit lies "Bound in the silent chains of [[death-imagery|death]]" — a phrase that echoes the legal chains he evaded in life, now replaced by the final, inescapable bondage of mortality.

Nature serves as both guardian and memorial. The poppy "waves her golden glow / Above the lowly mound" while the cactus "stands with lances drawn — / A martial guard around" (065-001). The martial imagery of the cactus — "lances drawn" and "martial guard" — ironically grants the [[outlaw]] a soldier's honor in [[death-imagery|death]] that he never earned in life. The poppy's "golden glow" offers a gentler benediction.

The poem's closing lines suggest that [[death-imagery|death]] has purified the bandit: "His dreams are free from guile or greed, / Or foray's wild alarms" (065-001). The restless violence of the [[outlaw]]'s life gives way to a dreamless peace beneath the desert sky.

> 'Mid lava rock and glaring sand,
> 'Neath the desert's brassy skies,
> Bound in the silent chains of death
> A border bandit lies.
> The poppy waves her golden glow
> Above the lowly mound;
> The cactus stands with lances drawn—
> A martial guard around.
>
> (065-001)

Pitt's contribution is brief — a single poem in a large anthology — but it captures the anthology's recurring fascination with the [[love|romance]] of the [[outlaw]] figure and the redemptive power of the Western landscape.
