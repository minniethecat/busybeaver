## Overview

The Rocky Mountains form the great continental spine of [[the-west|west]]ern North America, stretching over 3,000 miles from British Columbia to [[new-mexico|New Mexico]]. In the poetry of the cattle trail, the Rockies represent more than geography—they embody the West's "inimitable art" (072-001), a wilderness where "nature's handiwork lies in virginal beauty" and the cowboy can lose himself in landscapes untouched by Eastern civilization.

For the cattle trail era, the Rockies defined the [[the-west|west]]ern boundary of the great grazing plains. The eastern slope—the foothills and mountain parks—provided summer range for cattle outfits, while the high passes remained largely the domain of prospectors, trappers, and the grizzly bears that gave [[denver-jim|James]] Fox's poem its title.

[[denver-jim|James]] Fox's poem contrasts the "hum of city life" in the East with the mountain air of the Rockies, making the mountains a symbol of authentic living and spiritual homecoming.

## Geography

The Rocky Mountain system comprises multiple parallel ranges running north-south through [[the-west|west]]ern North America. In the cattle trail context, the most significant sections are the [[colorado|Colorado]] Rockies (including the Front Range visible from the Great Plains), the Wyoming ranges (the Bighorns, the Wind River Range, the Tetons), and the [[montana|Montana]] Rockies that merge into the [[bitterroot|Bitterroot]] Range.

The foothills zone—the transitional country between the plains and the high peaks—was the cowboy's Rockies. Here, at elevations between 6,000 and 9,000 feet, timbered slopes gave way to mountain meadows where cattle could graze in summer, and where the grizzly still roamed in numbers that made "Where the Grizzly Dwells" a literal rather than fanciful title.

## In the Poems

"Where the Grizzly Dwells" places the Rockies at the center of the cowboy's imaginative geography. The narrator explicitly contrasts East and West: "I admire the artificial art of the East / But I love more the inimitable art of the West" (072-001). He then describes wandering "away from human habitations / Up the rugged slopes / Through the timbered stretches" into the Rocky Mountain wilderness.

The foothills of the Rockies are where the narrator "loses himself" (072-001)—a deliberate act of immersion in wild nature. From this vantage, among "the frightful cry of wolves" and bears "sneaking up behind," the cowboy contemplates his mortality and spiritual destiny, wondering if he "could go to the Unknown Place / The Happy Hunting Ground" (072-001).

The Rockies thus become a threshold between the earthly and the eternal—a place where the cowboy, stripped of civilization's comforts, confronts the deepest questions of existence amid "the hazy purple of the peaks" (072-001).
