# Charles Badger Clark

## Overview

Charles Badger Clark, Jr. (1883–1957) was the most prolific single contributor to [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" (1916), and arguably the finest cowboy poet of the early twentieth century. Lomax himself identified Clark's work as the anthology's standout, writing in the Introduction:

> "Of one example, Charles Badger Clark's 'High Chin Bob,' I have printed elsewhere the original story" (P03-002).

Clark's poetry appears across all three parts of the collection — from the opening [[Out-Where-the-West-Begins-Analysis|declaration of Western pride]] to the closing tribute to cowboy verse itself ("[[Sun-and-Saddle-Leather|Sun and Saddle Leather]]" (077-001)) — establishing him as the anthology's anchor voice. His work bridges the anonymous [[Cowboy-Culture|oral ballad tradition]] of the trail and the self-consciously literary Western poetry that followed, combining technical polish with the authentic voice of a working cowboy.

> Of one example, Charles Badger Clark's "High Chin Bob," I have printed,
> following Mr. Clark's recitation, the version which he has kindly furnished me,
> and which I think superior to the forms that are current in the cattle country.
>
> (P03-002)

> At a round-up on the Gila
> One sweet morning long ago,
> Ten of us was throwed quite freely
> By a hoss from Idaho.
> An' we 'lowed he'd go a-beggin'
> For a man to break his pride
> Till, a-hitchin' up one leggin',
> Boastful Bill cut loose an' cried:
>
> (006-001)

## Life & Context

Clark was born in Iowa in 1883 and spent most of his adult life ranching in South [[dakota|Dakota]]. Unlike many contributors who encountered cowboy life from the outside, Clark lived the work — his poems carry the authority of firsthand experience with trail-driving, bronco-breaking, and bunkhouse life. His father, a Methodist minister, gave him a literary grounding unusual for a working cowboy, which Clark turned to the service of Western themes rather than the Eastern literary conventions of his time.

His poems are notable for their range: from the humor of "The Bunkhouse Orchestra" (set to the tune of "Turkey in the Straw") to the elegiac "A Ranger," and from the narrative drive of "[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]" to the philosophical reflection of "[[Sun-and-Saddle-Leather|Sun and Saddle Leather]]." As Lomax notes in attribution, Clark's verses came "From recitation, original" (006-001) — the oral performance tradition was as important to him as the printed page.

## Poetic Style & Themes

Clark's verse combines the formal elements of traditional English poetry — regular meter, end-rhyme, and stanza structure — with the vernacular vocabulary and experience of the American cowboy. His characteristic themes include:

- **[[the-cowboy-code|The Cowboy Code]]** — personal honor, loyalty, and the [[the-cowboy-code|unwritten law]]s of the trail. "I'll never turn him loose!" (012-001) becomes a declaration of cowboy integrity.
- **The Vanishing Frontier** — an awareness that fences and settlement were replacing the open range. Clark's cowboys often look back on a world already passing.
- **Nature as Character** — the landscape is not mere backdrop but an active presence: the wind, the [[Rio-Grande|rivers]], the [[The-Cattle-Trail|trails]] and stars.
- **Humor and Pathos** — the [[The-Bunkhouse-Orchestra|bunkhouse musicians]] play comic instruments, while the old cowman faces his own obsolescence with dignity.

## Perspectives: How Others Saw Clark

Clark's position in the anthology and in Western letters is illuminated by how contemporaries and later readers viewed his work. This multi-perspective view reveals a poet whose reputation was built not on a single poem but on the cumulative weight of his contribution.

- **[[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s assessment** — Lomax, the anthology's editor, singled Clark out for special mention in the Introduction, describing "[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]" as a work whose "original story" he had "printed elsewhere" (P03-002). This editorial spotlight — unique among the anthology's many contributors — signals Lomax's recognition of Clark as the collection's anchor poet, the one whose work most fully embodied what the anthology aimed to preserve.

- **Fellow contributors' view** — Clark's poems appeared alongside those of [[James-Barton-Adams]], [[Henry-Herbert-Knibbs]], and [[Arthur-Chapman]], all established Western writers. The placement of "[[out-where-the-west-begins-analysis|Out Where the West Begins]]" as the opening poem of Part I (after the title poem) indicates that Lomax and his fellow contributors saw Clark's work as the natural starting point — a declaration of [[the-west|Western identity]] that set the tone for everything that followed.

- **The cowboy audience's response** — Clark's verses were "From recitation, original" (006-001), meaning they circulated orally in bunkhouses and around campfires before appearing in print. This dual life — as both recited performance and printed poem — made Clark unusual: he was a literary figure who was also genuinely part of the cowboy world he wrote about. Unlike Eastern writers who romanticized the West from a distance, Clark was one of their own.

- **Later literary assessment** — Clark's collected volume "[[Sun-and-Saddle-Leather|Sun and Saddle Leather]]" became a touchstone of Western American poetry. Critics have noted that his best-known lines — "Out where the handclasp's a little stronger / Out where the smile dwells a little longer" — achieved the rare feat of entering the popular imagination while retaining their literary craft. He is now recognized as a bridge figure between the anonymous trail ballad and the self-consciously literary Western poem.

## Significance

Clark occupies a unique position in American literary history. He stands at the intersection of folk tradition (the anonymous trail songs collected by [[Cowboy-Culture|cowboy singers]]), literary composition (the self-consciously crafted poem for publication), and oral performance (pieces meant to be recited in bunkhouses and around campfires). Lomax recognized this triple role when he chose Clark's work as exemplar of what the anthology aimed to preserve.

His influence extends beyond the Lomax anthology. "[[Sun-and-Saddle-Leather|Sun and Saddle Leather]]," his collected volume, became a touchstone of Western American poetry, and his best-known lines — "Out where the handclasp's a little stronger / Out where the smile dwells a little longer" — remain among the most quoted verses in the Western canon.
