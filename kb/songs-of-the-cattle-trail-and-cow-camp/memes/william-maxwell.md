# William Maxwell

William Maxwell was an American cowboy poet whose only contribution to [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp* — "[[The-Dance-at-Silver-Valley|The Dance at Silver Valley]]" — is one of the anthology's most dramatic narrative poems. Set at a [[the-west|frontier]] dance hall, the poem tells the story of [[broncho-bill|Broncho Bill]], a cowboy who comes to town to dance with [[belle-mcclure|Belle McClure]], only to be shot dead by the gambler [[jim-kershaw|Jim Kershaw]] in a jealous confrontation (005-001).

## Life & Context

Maxwell's biographical details are not recorded in the Lomax anthology beyond the attribution line appended to his poem. The poem's intimate knowledge of cowboy dance culture — the jingling spurs, the chaps and sombreros, the barroom drinks, and the fatal tension between cowboys and gamblers — suggests a writer familiar with the social world of the Western [[the-west|frontier]]. The poem's blend of ballad narrative, dramatic dialogue, and refrain structure places it in the tradition of the cowboy murder ballad.

## Key Appearances

| Poem | PN Range | Type |
|------|----------|------|
| "The Dance at Silver Valley" | (005-001) | narrative/murder ballad |

The poem introduces three named characters who have since become subjects of their own entries in the anthology's person gallery: [[Broncho-Bill]], the doomed cowboy; [[Belle-McClure]], the woman at the center of the fatal triangle; and [[Jim-Kershaw]], the gambler who shoots [[bill|Bill]]. The poem's refrain — "[[bill|Broncho Bill]] has come to town / To dance a dance tonight" (005-001) — frames the tragedy with the relentless music of the fiddle and banjo, a dramatic irony that intensifies as the narrative moves toward its bloody conclusion.

> Don't you hear the big spurs jingle?
> Don't you feel the red blood tingle?
> Be it smile or be it frown,
> Be it dance or be it fight,
> Broncho Bill has come to town
> To dance a dance tonight.
> And Belle McClure, as you may know,
> Was belle of all the valley;
> She wore her silks to every show
> And danced them on the alley.
>
> (005-001)

## Significance

Maxwell's "The Dance at Silver Valley" exemplifies the cowboy murder ballad tradition — a narrative form in which dance, drink, jealousy, and sudden violence compress into a single evening. Unlike the elegiac tone of the trail poems or the comic register of the yarns, Maxwell's poem is stark and fatalistic: [[bill|Bill]] knows the risk, Belle knows the stakes, and the gambler's "white and slim" hands (005-001) telegraph the outcome from the first mention of [[jim-kershaw|Jim Kershaw]]. The poem's enduring power lies in its economy — a complete tragedy in under a hundred lines, set to the rhythm of a fiddle tune.
