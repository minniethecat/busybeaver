# The Cattleman

## Overview

The cattleman stands alongside — and sometimes above — the [[Cowboy-Culture|cowboy]] in the poetry of "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]." If the cowboy is the anthology's hero, the cattleman is its patriarch: the rancher who owns the herds, the trail boss who organizes the drives, and the elder whose passing marks the end of an era. The Dedication page sets the tone, celebrating "all the virtues of the old time cattleman and none of the vices of the new" (P01-002), framing the cattleman as an ideal type — a moral as well as economic figure.

> "Let cattle rub my tombstone down / And coyotes wail their grief" — the cattleman's desired epitaph (069-001)

## The Cattle Industry in the Trail Era

The open-range cattle industry that flourished between the end of the Civil War (1865) and the arrival of barbed wire and railroads (c. 1890) was one of the most distinctive economic systems in American history. Its essential features shape the world of the anthology's poetry:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Open range** | Unfenced public land in [[texas|Texas]] and the Great Plains, grazed by cattle owned by multiple outfits |
| **Long drives** | Seasonal movement of cattle from southern ranges to northern railheads via the [[The-Cattle-Trail|trail system]] |
| **Round-ups** | Semi-annual gatherings to brand, sort, and count cattle across outfit boundaries |
| **Trail bosses** | Experienced cattlemen who organized and led the drives, wielding near-absolute authority |
| **Seasonal rhythm** | Spring [[branding]], summer grazing, fall drive, winter maintenance — a calendar tied to cattle biology |

The poems capture a world where "the [[prairie]] was big and the cattle / Were as 'scary' as ever" (019-001) — a cattleman's domain measured not in acres fenced but in horizons crossed.

## The Cattleman in the Poems

### The Old Cowman

The anthology's most poignant portrait of the cattleman appears in "The Old Cowman," a poem that elegizes not just a man but an entire way of life. The old cowman represents the passing of the open-range era — the transition from a world where "the [[prairie]] was big" (019-001) to one of fences, railroads, and the "vices of the new" cattleman (P01-002).

The poem's [[homesickness|nostalgia]] is not sentimental but historical: it records a genuine transformation of the Western economy and the human type it produced.

### The Trail Boss

The trail boss, a recurring figure in Part I's narratives, represents the cattleman in action. Poems like "The Cattle [[round-up|Round-Up]]" depict the trail boss's authority — absolute on the drive, earned through experience rather than title:

> "the dust hangs thick upon the trail / And the horns and the hoofs are drumming" (035-001)

The trail boss managed men, horses, and cattle across hundreds of miles of open country. His word was law; his judgment meant the difference between a successful drive and disaster.

### The Cattleman and the Cowboy

A crucial distinction runs through the anthology: the cattleman was not the cowboy. The cattleman owned; the [[work|cowboy work]]ed. The cattleman stayed (the ranch, the home range); the cowboy moved (the trail, the seasonal [[round-up]] circuit). Yet the two figures were interdependent — the cattleman provided the economic structure that made cowboy life possible, and the cowboy provided the labor that made cattle ranching profitable.

This relationship is sometimes tense in the poems, but more often it is portrayed as a bond of mutual respect — the cattleman as elder, the cowboy as younger brother in the fraternity of the range.

## Economic and Cultural Dimensions

### The Cattle Empire

At its height in the 1870s–1880s, the cattle industry transformed the American West. [[texas|Texas]] longhorn cattle, worth $4 a head in [[texas|Texas]], could sell for $40 or more at northern railheads. The economics of [[trail-driving|the long drive]] — the price spread between southern supply and northern demand — created fortunes, fueled boom towns like [[abilene|Abilene]] and [[dodge-city|Dodge City]], and drove the expansion of the [[The-Cattle-Trail|trail system]].

### The End of the Open Range

The cattleman's world began to collapse in the late 1880s. Barbed wire — "the devil's [[lasso|rope]]" — enclosed the open range. Railroads extended into [[texas|Texas]], eliminating the need for long drives. The catastrophic winter of 1886–1887 killed hundreds of thousands of cattle and bankrupted many ranchers. By the time Lomax's anthology was published in 1916, the "old time cattleman" was already a figure of memory, preserved in verse that served as elegy as much as celebration.

### The Cattleman as Cultural Memory

The Dedication's framing of the cattleman's "virtues" — implicit contrast with the "vices of the new" (P01-002) — establishes the anthology's stance: it is a work of cultural memory, preserving not just poems but a moral archetype. The cattleman, like the [[Cowboy-Culture|cowboy]], is presented as a type of American character worth remembering, even as the economic conditions that produced him fade into history.
