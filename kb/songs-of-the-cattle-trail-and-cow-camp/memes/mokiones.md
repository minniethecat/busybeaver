## Overview

The Mokiones—a mountain range whose precise geographic identity blends into the mythic landscape of cowboy verse—provide the dramatic high-altitude setting for [[charles-badger-clark|Charles Badger Clark]]'s "[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]." The poem opens "'Way high up in the Mokiones, among the mountain tops" (013-001), immediately establishing a remote, elevated world where the ordinary rules of the lowlands do not apply.

In Clark's poem, the Mokiones serve as a stage for cowboy bravado at its most extreme. It is here, "among the mountain tops," that [[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]] attempts to [[lasso|rope]] a mountain lion—an act of audacity that defines his character. The remoteness of the Mokiones, far from the cattle towns and trail routes of the plains, places [[high-chin-bob|Bob]]'s exploit in a realm of pure legend.

## In the Poems

"[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]" begins with the Mokiones as both geography and atmosphere:

> "'Way high up in the Mokiones, among the mountain tops,
> A lion cleaned a yearling's bones and licks his thankful chops;
> And who upon the scene should ride, a-trippin' down the slope,
> But High Chin Bob of sinful pride and maverick-hungry rope." (013-001)

The refrain "'Way high up in the Mokiones" reappears later in the poem (013-001), reinforcing the sense of elevation and [[loneliness|isolation]]. The mountains are not merely a backdrop but an essential element of the story's logic: only in such a wild, high place could a cowboy credibly attempt to [[lasso|rope]] a lion and live to boast about it. The Mokiones thus function as a mythic landscape where cowboy skill reaches its ultimate test.
