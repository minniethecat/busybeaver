## Overview

The cowboy's [[homesickness|longing for home]] runs through the anthology as a counterpoint to the freedom of the trail. Home exists more vividly in memory than in geography — a distant fixed point that gives the wandering life its meaning.

> The past, when the prairie was big and the cattle
> Were as wild as the deuce and as tough and as fat,
> The longhorns the equal of any rough battle,
>
> (019-001)

## Key Passages

The night guard's [[loneliness|solitude]] makes homesickness acute: "I'm night guard all alone tonight, / Dead homesick, lonely, tired and blue" (033-001). The home ranch beckons at the end of the drive: "then it's ride for the old home ranch / Where a maid love's light gleams" (035-001).

The cowboy's domestic imagination surfaces in "A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s Hopeless [[love|Love]]": "I'll open the little home corral an' give her the level best" (021-001), while another poem imagines settling down: "And talk o'er our homestead plans" (023-001). The tension between trail life and domesticity runs through "Will y'u think of yo' home when the world rolls wide?" (036-001).

The ultimate home is the celestial ranch: "seeks peace and quiet on the home ranch of the blest, / Where no storms or stampedes bother" (045-001). Heaven itself is imagined as a ranch — the cowboy's deepest idea of belonging.

## Significance

Home and belonging form the emotional counterweight to the freedom of the trail. The cowboy's identity oscillates between the open range and the distant hearth, and this tension — between restlessness and rootedness — gives the anthology much of its emotional complexity.
