## Overview

The [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail|Chisholm Trail]] was the major artery of the great [[texas|Texas]] cattle drives of the 1870s and 1880s. Stretching from south [[texas|Texas]] through Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) to the [[kansas|Kansas]] railheads at [[abilene|Abilene]], [[dodge-city|Dodge City]], and [[wichita|Wichita]], it carried millions of longhorns north to market. For the cowboy, the Chisholm was not just a route—it was a way of life, a seasonal rhythm that structured the year, and a proving ground for skill and endurance.

The trail enters this anthology through a single poem, but one that captures the full [[love|romance]] of the trail-driving era as a memory of youth and freedom:

> The past when we headed each year for Dodge City
> And punched up the drags on the old Chisholm Trail;
> When the world was all bright and the girls were all pretty,
> And a feller could "mav'rick" and stay out of jail. (019-001)

The poem frames the trail not as a hardship endured but as a lost golden age—a time "when the prairie was big and the cattle / Were as 'scary' as ever the antelope grew" (019-001), when what mattered most was "to carry a gun, to make our spurs rattle, / And to ride a blue streak" (019-001). The [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail|Chisholm Trail]] here is a conduit not just for cattle but for youth, freedom, and a vanished world.

## In the Poems

"The Drunken Desperado" (Chapter 19) opens as a convivial toast among old trail hands, calling for friendships "the chances of fortune have riven" to "be renewed and the smiling past come back again" (019-001). The [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail|Chisholm Trail]] appears at the emotional center of the poem, paired with [[dodge-city|Dodge City]] as the inseparable route-and-destination of cowboy memory. The wistful refrain—"when the world was all bright and the girls were all pretty"—marks the distance between the remembered trail and the present moment, making the Chisholm a landscape of [[homesickness|nostalgia]] as much as geography.
