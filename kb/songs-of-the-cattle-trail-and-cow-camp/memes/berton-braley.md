## Overview

Berton Braley (1882–1966) was an American poet, journalist, and prolific writer of light verse who contributed two poems to [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s "[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" (1916): "Pardners" (ch046) and "The Habit" (ch059). Braley was better known for his syndicated newspaper verse, industrial poetry, and inspirational work than for Western themes, making his presence in the Lomax anthology an interesting cross-pollination between popular journalism and cowboy balladry.

Two of Braley's poems bear his distinctive signature at their close:

> You ugly ol' scoundrel, you! Berton Braley. (043-001)

> t, why, you can't keep still. Berton Braley. (056-001)

## Life & Context

Braley was born in Madison, Wisconsin, and worked as a journalist for newspapers including the *New York Evening Mail* and the *New York Sun*. He published numerous collections of light verse and became one of the most widely syndicated poets in American newspapers during the 1910s and 1920s. His poem "The Habit" — about the cowboy's inability to quit the trail — reflects Braley's ability to write convincingly about American types beyond his own direct experience.

## Poetic Style

Braley's verse is characterized by strong rhythm, regular rhyme, and an accessible, journalistic clarity. His cowboy poems adopt the vernacular without imitating it — the diction is more literary than the anonymous folk ballads in the anthology, but he captures the emotional register of cowboy experience. His contributions to the Lomax volume (043-001) (056-001) demonstrate the broad appeal of cowboy themes to American popular writers in the early twentieth century.
