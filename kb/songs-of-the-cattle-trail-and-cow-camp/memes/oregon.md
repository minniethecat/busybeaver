## Overview

Oregon in the poetry of the cattle trail era represents the far edge of the American [[the-west|frontier]]—wild, forested, and remote. In "Jack Dempsey's Grave," the state's "lonely mountain side" and "giant fir and cedar" (018-001) form the final resting place of a champion boxer, far from the roar of Eastern applause.

The Oregon Country, jointly occupied by Britain and the United States until 1846, drew settlers along the Oregon Trail for decades before the cattle trail era. By the time of the poems, Oregon was a state (admitted 1859) yet still retained the aura of the remote West—a place where a man's grave might go unmarked beneath "the solemn pines" (018-001).

The poem uses Oregon's wilderness as a metaphor for oblivion and forgetting. Where New York once thundered Dempsey's acclaim, only the towering evergreens of the Pacific North[[the-west|west]] now keep their silent watch.

## Geography

Oregon spans from the Pacific Coast across the Cascade Range to the high desert of the Columbia Plateau in the east. The poem's setting—"where Columbia's mighty waters / Roll down to the Ocean's tide" (018-001)—locates Dempsey's grave in the Columbia River watershed, the great waterway that drains the interior North[[the-west|west]].

The landscape described in the poem—giant fir, cedar, ferns, and lichens (018-001)—is characteristic of the temperate rainforests of [[the-west|west]]ern Oregon, where the maritime climate produces some of the most productive forestland on earth. This dense, dark woodland contrasts sharply with the open [[prairie-imagery|grasslands]] of the cattle trails further east.

## In the Poems

"Jack Dempsey's Grave" opens with a precise geographical setting: "Far out in the wilds of Oregon / On a lonely mountain side / Where Columbia's mighty waters / Roll down to the Ocean's tide" (018-001). The poet Robertus [[love|Love]] describes Oregon through its defining natural features—the Columbia River, the mountain slopes, the ancient forests—creating a landscape of sublime [[loneliness|isolation]].

The poem returns to Oregon three times as a refrain of remoteness (018-001). Dempsey is "now at rest far in the West / In the wilds of Oregon," forgotten by the "ten thousand throats that thundered his acclaim." The final stanza sharpens the lament: "Ah me! can it be that New York would forget / Her bravest of the brave / And leave him in the wilds of Oregon / Unmarked in a lonely grave?"

Oregon serves as both a real place and a poetic symbol—the ultimate distance from fame, where a champion's memory dissolves into wilderness.
