## Overview

The Cowboy Code — sometimes called the Code of [[the-west]] — was the unwritten ethical system that governed behavior on [[the-cattle-trail]]. Never formally codified, it was transmitted through example, story, and song. The poems in the Lomax anthology are among the best surviving records of this code in practice: they show what cowboys expected of each other, what they admired, what they condemned, and what they considered worth singing about.

> Out where the handclasp's a little stronger (003-001)

[[charles-badger-clark]]'s moral geography — defining [[the-west]] by the quality of human relationships — is itself an articulation of the Code. The handclasp matters more here; the smile is friendlier; the greeting is more sincere. These are not descriptions of landscape but of ethics: the Code as lived experience.

## Core Values

### Loyalty

The cowboy's loyalty to his outfit, his horse, and his pards was the foundation of the Code. Poems throughout the anthology celebrate the bond between cowboy and horse (048-001), between trail partners (046-001), and between the cowboy and [[the-cattleman]] he served (009-001). Disloyalty — the betrayal of a trust — was the unforgivable sin.

### Courage

Physical courage was expected and rarely remarked upon; moral courage — the willingness to do the right thing when no one was watching — was the higher form. The Code required a cowboy to face danger without complaint and to protect the weak without reward.

### Hospitality

The open range was a harsh environment, and the Code mandated that any traveler be offered food, water, and shelter.

> Once more let the handclasp of years past be given (019-001)

The handclasp reappears throughout the anthology as a ritual of recognition — one cowboy greeting another, affirming their shared membership in a community defined by the Code.

### Self-Reliance

The Code expected each cowboy to pull his own weight, to do his job without supervision, and to solve his own problems. The cowboy who couldn't ride, couldn't [[lasso|rope]], or couldn't keep up was a burden to the entire outfit.

## The Code in the Poems

The poems do not preach the Code — they embody it. When a cowboy shares his last water with a stranger, when he risks his life to stop a [[stampede]] (010-001), when he keeps his word at personal cost, the poems present these actions as natural — as what any decent cowboy would do. This is the Code's most powerful quality: it is assumed, not argued.
