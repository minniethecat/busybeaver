## Overview

Cheyenne, Wyoming, was one of the legendary towns of the cattle trail era—a railhead on the Union Pacific where cowboys, gamblers, and drifters converged after months on the trail. In "The Insult," Cheyenne represents the proving ground of a cowboy's toughness, its rough reputation serving as an unimpeachable credential.

The poem's speaker catalogs his hard-won experience with [[the-west|frontier]] vice in a single, breathless catalog: "I've braced the faro layouts in Cheyenne" (058-001). To have faced the gambling tables of Cheyenne—and survived—is the seventh item in a list of thirteen extreme experiences that together constitute the speaker's claim to [[the-west|frontier]] authenticity. The line places Cheyenne alongside swimming the [[colorado|Colorado]] "where she runs close down to hell," fighting "for muddy water," swallowing "hot tamales and cayenne," and "dallied with the quicksands of the Grande"—each reference building a cumulative portrait of a man who has endured the ra[[the-west|west]] the West could inflict.

## In the Poems

### The Insult: Cheyenne as Credential (Chapter 58)

"The Insult" is structured as a comic monologue in which the speaker's entire catalog of [[the-west|frontier]] suffering serves a single punchline. After listing thirteen ordeals—from [[riding-imagery|riding]] "a pitchin' broncho till the sky was underneath" to being "shot plum full of holes" and serving "three years' campaignin' with the fightin', bitin' Ninth"—the speaker reveals what finally broke his legendary composure:

"I've had the yeller fever and been shot plum full of holes; / I've grabbed an army mule plum by the tail; / But I've never been so snortin', really highfalutin' mad / As when you up and hands me ginger ale" (058-001).

In this structure, Cheyenne's faro layouts are not merely a biographical detail but a building block in the poem's comic architecture. Each line raises the stakes of the speaker's toughness, so that the final insult—being offered ginger ale instead of whiskey—lands with maximum absurdity. Cheyenne, the wildest of wild trail towns, is the standard against which all other hardships are measured.

### The Catalog Form

Cheyenne appears within a specifically Western poetic form—the catalog of credentials. The speaker does not merely claim toughness; he enumerates it by place name, each location carrying a known reputation: "the [[colorado|Colorado]]," "Cheyenne," "the Grande," "a half a dozen burgs." The poem assumes the listener already knows what "Cheyenne" means—that the name alone carries the weight of the town's faro houses, its whiskey, its violence. This is place-as-shorthand, a technique that recurs throughout the collection's trail-town poems.
