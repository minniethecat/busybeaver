## Overview

"Here's to the cowboy, boys, wherever he may be" — so begins "A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Toast," a poem collected in [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp* (1916). This analysis examines the poem's structure, themes, and place within the anthology's broader portrait of cowboy life.

> Here's to the passing cowboy, the plowman's pioneer;
> His home, the boundless mesa, he of any man the peer;
> All day he roamed the prairies, at night he, with the stars,
> Kept vigil o'er thousands held by neither posts nor bars;
>
> (073-001)

## Summary

The poem adams's celebratory toast to cowboy life—a ritual of camaraderie, gratitude, and the pleasures of the trail shared around the campfire.

## Themes

The poem engages with the theme of **friendship**—a recurring concern throughout the anthology. Like other poems in the collection, it treats cowboy experience not as romanticized adventure but as lived reality, with all the complexity, humor, and hardship that entails.

## Style and Structure

The poem employs the characteristic vernacular voice of cowboy verse—direct, unpretentious, and grounded in the rhythms of trail speech. The language is colloquial without being crude, poetic without being artificial, capturing the distinctive register of the working cowboy.

## Place in the Anthology

Within the three-part structure of *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, this poem contributes to the anthology's documentary ambition: to preserve not just the songs but the full range of cowboy experience—comic, tragic, reflective, and celebratory.
