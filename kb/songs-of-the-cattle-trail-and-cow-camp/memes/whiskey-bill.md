## Overview

Whiskey Bill is the subject of an anonymous poem in [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*. Unlike the anthology's real-world cowboy poets and historical figures, Whiskey Bill is a purely literary creation — a composite outlaw-cowboy whose brief, violent story is compressed into a single compact poem of four stanzas (062-001).

The poem presents Bill as a figure of terror and pathos: a gun-wielding cowboy whose "joyous, aching spree" sends townspeople fleeing for cover. The sheriff confronts him, and the poem closes with Bill's apparent death — "Bill's soul went shootin' down the slide" — followed by the haunting elegiac question: "How are things on the Great Divide, O Whiskey Bill?" (062-001).

## In the Poem

"[[Whiskey-Bill-A-Fragment|Whiskey Bill—A Fragment]]" (Chapter 62) is attributed to "Anonymous." Its four stanzas move from comic terror to elegy: the opening stanzas depict Bill as a menacing figure whose approach empties the streets, while the final stanza records his fatal encounter with the law. The poem's tonal shift — from "mad Whiskey Bill" to "Poor Whiskey Bill!" — captures the dual vision of the outlaw cowboy in Western verse: simultaneously dangerous and doomed.

> A-down the road and gun in hand
> Comes Whiskey Bill, mad Whiskey Bill;
> A-lookin' for some place to land
> Comes Whiskey Bill.
> An' everybody'd like to be
> Ten miles away behind a tree
> When on his joyous, aching spree
> Starts Whiskey Bill.
>
> (062-001)
