# The Cowboy and His Song

## Overview

"[[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp]]" is, before anything else, a collection of *songs*. [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s Introduction frames the volume as a "byproduct" of his earlier collection "[[Cowboy-Songs-and-Other-Frontier-Ballads|Cowboy Songs and Other Frontier Ballads]]," and the poems he selected regularly refer to themselves as songs — meant to be sung, recited, and passed from cowboy to cowboy across the [[The-Cattle-Trail|trails]] and bunkhouses of the West.

> "From recitation, original, by Charles Badger Clark, Jr." (006-001)

The word "song" in the anthology's title is not a metaphor. Many of the poems bear explicit musical directions — "set to 'Turkey in the Straw'" (046-001) — and the Introduction describes cowboys "singing in right good cheer" as "the work of the fall is ending" (035-001). The cowboy's song was a functional part of trail life: a way to calm cattle on night watch, to pass time on long rides, to court at dances, and to memorialize the dead.

## Origins of the Cowboy Song

### The Anonymous Tradition

Before the literary cowboy poets — [[Charles-Badger-Clark]], [[James-Barton-Adams]], [[henry-herbert-knibbs|Henry Herbert Knibbs]] — there was the anonymous tradition of trail ballads. These songs, composed by unknown cowboys and passed orally from outfit to outfit, formed the substrate on which the anthology's literary verse was built.

Lomax's Introduction describes the process:

> "One night in New Mexico a cowboy sang to me, in typical cowboy whine, a song which he said had been composed by a friend of his" (P03-002)

This oral chain — cowboy composes, cowboy sings, collector transcribes — is the hidden structure of the entire anthology. The most celebrated poems in the collection, including [[Charles-Badger-Clark|Clark]]'s "[[high-chin-bob|High Chin Bob]]," originated in oral performance before reaching the printed page.

### The Vaquero Connection

The cowboy song tradition also owes a debt to the Mexican *vaqueros* who preceded Anglo cowboys on the ranges of South [[texas|Texas]] and the [[Rio-Grande|Rio Grande]] watershed. The corrido tradition of narrative balladry, the *décima* form of improvised verse, and the practice of singing to cattle during night watch all have Mexican antecedents that blended with Anglo folk traditions in the borderlands.

### From Oral to Literary

By 1916, when Lomax's anthology appeared, the transition from oral to literary cowboy verse was already well advanced. [[Charles-Badger-Clark]] represents the culmination of this transition — a poet who was himself a working cowboy and who composed in the oral tradition ("From recitation, original" (006-001)) but whose work was polished for publication. "[[Sun-and-Saddle-Leather|Sun and Saddle Leather]]" (077-001), Clark's collected volume, represents the cowboy song's migration from campfire to bookshelf.

## Form and Function

### Musical Settings

Many poems in the anthology specify or imply musical settings. "[[The-Bunkhouse-Orchestra|The Bunkhouse Orchestra]]" is explicitly "set to 'Turkey in the Straw'" (046-001), transforming the familiar fiddle tune into a vehicle for bunkhouse comedy. Other poems reference the tunes they were meant to accompany: waltzes for the dance songs, cowboy laments for the trail elegies, and the distinctive "cowboy whine" that Lomax describes in the Introduction.

### The Song as Social Bond

[[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] songs served multiple social functions:

| Function | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Work accompaniment** | Calming cattle, setting pace on the trail | Night-herding songs |
| **Courtship** | Dance songs, serenades | "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s Dance Song" (041-001) |
| **Memorial** | Elegies for dead cowboys and lost eras | "[[The-Old-Cowman]]" |
| **[[identity|Identity]] construction** | Defining cowboy character in verse | "[[out-where-the-west-begins-analysis|Out Where the West Begins]]" (003-001) |
| **Comedy** | Humor to relieve hardship | "The Bunkhouse Orchestra" (046-001) |

### Themes in Cowboy Song

The songs and poems of the anthology cluster around recurring thematic territories. The [[Cowboy-Culture|cowboy's world]] is defined by tension between:

- **[[freedom|Freedom]] vs. [[loneliness|Loneliness]]** — the trail offers liberty at the cost of [[loneliness|isolation]]
- **Action vs. Reflection** — Part I's yarns vs. Part II's interior meditations
- **The Present vs. The Past** — [[homesickness|nostalgia]] for a vanishing [[the-west|frontier]] pervades the collection
- **Individual vs. Community** — the solitary cowboy and the bunkhouse collective

## Lomax as Preserver

[[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s role in the cowboy song tradition cannot be overstated. His 1910 collection "[[Cowboy-Songs-and-Other-Frontier-Ballads|Cowboy Songs and Other Frontier Ballads]]" was the first systematic effort to collect and preserve cowboy oral poetry. "Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp" (1916) was its literary sequel — an anthology not of collected folk songs but of composed poems, many by cowboys who had themselves absorbed and transformed the oral tradition.

Lomax's editorial framing positions the collection as salvage ethnography — an attempt to "preserve for future generations" the "songs created and sung by the cowboys" (P03-002) before the trail era faded entirely from living memory. The anthology is thus both a work of literature and an act of cultural preservation.

## The Transformation of the Essay Genre

In his Introduction, Lomax reflects on the anthology's origins:

> "The volume is in reality a byproduct of my earlier collection, 'Cowboy Songs and Other Frontier Ballads'" (P03-002)

This self-effacing framing — the anthology as "byproduct" — acknowledges that the cowboy song tradition was first and foremost an oral phenomenon, not a literary one. The printed page was always secondary to the sung performance. What the anthology preserves is a snapshot of a living tradition at the moment of its transition from campfire to codex.
