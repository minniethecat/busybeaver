## Overview

Abilene, [[kansas|Kansas]], was one of the first and most famous cattle towns of the trail era—a railhead where the [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail|Chisholm Trail]] met the [[kansas|Kansas]] Pacific Railway. In the poems, Abilene represents the point of arrival where the West meets commerce and civilization, appearing both as a supply hub and a geographic waypoint on the great trail routes.

## In the Poems

### The Cowboy Christmas Ball (Chapter 48)

In "The Ol' Cow Hawse," Abilene is the source of refinement for a [[the-west|frontier]] celebration. The dance at "The Morning Star Hotel" features music from "a fiddle and a lively tambourine" (048-001), with the showpiece being a "viol come imported, by stage from Abilene" (048-001). The room was "togged out gorgeous—with mistletoe and shawls, / And candles flickered frescoes around the airy walls" (048-001). Abilene's stagecoach connection brought the trappings of civilization to the remote cow camps, making it a portal between the [[the-west|frontier]] and the wider world.

### The Mackenzie Trail (Chapter 66)

"[[denver-jim|Denver Jim]]" traces the route of the historic Mackenzie Trail across [[texas|Texas]]: "Across the Brazos, [[colorado|Colorado]], through / Concho's broad, fair valley, sweeping on / By Abilene it climbs upon the plains, / The [[llano-estacado|Llano Estacado]]" (066-001). The poem then darkens the view beyond: "beyond lie wastes / Of alkali and hunger gaunt and [[death-imagery|death]]" (066-001). Abilene here is a threshold—the last named settlement before the trail ascends into the harsh, empty expanse of the [[llano-estacado|Staked Plains]].

> The music was a fiddle and a lively tambourine,
> And a "viol come imported," by stage from Abilene.
> The room was togged out gorgeous—with mistletoe and shawls,
> And candles flickered frescoes around the airy walls.
>
> (048-001)
