## Overview

Jack Lorell is one of the two central figures in "The Ballad of Billy Leamont," a tragic cowboy ballad fragment preserved in Chapter 74 of [[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp|Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp]]. He rides "at the shoulder" of [[billy-leamont|Billy Leamont]] as the two cowboys leave town and head across the prairie. His unexplained death in a dell is the turning point of the ballad, precipitating Leamont's suicide (074-001).

## In the Ballad

The ballad's opening stanza establishes Lorell as Leamont's close companion:

> "Billy Leamont rode out of the town—
>
> *Close at his shoulder rode Jack Lorell—*
>
> Over the leagues of the [[prairie|prairies]] brown,
>
> Into the hills where the sun goes down—
>
> *Billy Leamont and Jack Lorell!*"
>
> (074-001)

The ballad's surviving conclusion reveals Lorell's fate:

> "Billy Leamont looked down the dell—
>
> *Dead below him lay Jack Lorell—*
>
> With his gun at his forehead he fired and fell,
>
> Then rode they two through the streets of hell—
>
> *Billy Leamont and Jack Lorell!*"
>
> (074-001)

The circumstances of Lorell's death — how he died, who or what killed him — are lost with the missing stanzas of the ballad. He exists in the surviving fragment as both presence and absence: named in every stanza's refrain, but silent and dead by the ballad's end.

## Significance

Jack Lorell and [[billy-leamont|Billy Leamont]] represent a recurring archetype in Western balladry and [[cowboy-culture]]: the paired cowboys whose bond is sealed in death. The ballad's refrain — "Billy Leamont and Jack Lorell!" — links the two names inseparably, so that even in the afterlife they "rode they two through the streets of hell" together (074-001).
