## Overview

The stampede was [[the-cattle-trail|trail drive]]'s most terrifying event — the sudden, inexplicable mass panic of a cattle herd that could turn order into chaos in seconds. Stampedes were triggered by lightning, sudden noises, the scent of a predator, or sometimes nothing discernible at all. Once started, a stampede could run for miles, scattering cattle, breaking legs, and killing any creature caught in its path.

The poems in the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology treat the stampede with a mixture of dread and respect. It is the moment when the cowboy's mastery of his world is most severely tested — when skill, courage, and luck must combine to avert disaster.

## Causes and Mechanisms

### Triggers

The most common trigger was lightning — a sudden flash and crack that would send the herd into blind panic. But stampedes could also be caused by a careless noise, the scent of a wolf, a horse's stumble, or the herd's own collective anxiety. [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] wisdom held that some nights were simply "stampede weather" — still, close, electric — and that any herd on such a night was a bomb waiting to go off.

### The Run

Once triggered, a stampede fed on its own momentum. The sound of thousands of hooves, the dust, the panic — all amplified the terror. Cattle ran blindly, often for miles, trampling whatever lay in their path. [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]] had to ride to the head of the herd — not away from it — and turn the leaders into a circle, gradually calming the herd through exhaustion and controlled movement.

## In the Poems

### Lasca

The most famous stampede in the anthology is the climax of "[[Lasca]]" (010-001), where the title character sacrifices herself to save the narrator from a stampeding herd. [[frank-desprez|Frank Desprez]]'s treatment of the stampede is both realistic (the sudden terror, the impossibility of stopping the herd) and symbolic (the stampede as the ultimate test of love).

### Other Appearances

Stampedes appear throughout the anthology as moments of maximum drama — the event that separates the competent cowboy from the merely adequate. A cowboy who could handle a stampede earned permanent respect; one who couldn't was unlikely to survive long enough to be memorialized in verse.

## Symbolic Meaning

Beyond its literal danger, the stampede functions in the poems as a symbol of chaos — the force that exists beneath the ordered surface of cowboy life, always ready to erupt. The cowboy's world is a world of control (of horses, of cattle, of self), and the stampede is the moment when control fails. How the cowboy responds — with courage or panic — is the poem's moral core.
