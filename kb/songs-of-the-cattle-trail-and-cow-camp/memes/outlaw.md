## Overview

The word "outlaw" carries a double meaning on the cattle trail. It describes the human fugitive—the [[rustler]], the killer, the man who lives beyond the reach of "Law and Order" (004-001). But it also describes the unbroken horse, the outlaw bronc that no cowboy has yet mastered, and the outlaw steer that charges rather than submits. In both senses, the outlaw represents the limit of civilization's reach—the creature, human or animal, that will not be corralled.

## In the Anthology

The human outlaw appears in stark terms. One poem speaks of a man "paid by Law and Order; He was outlaw, [[rustler]], killer" (004-001)—the three identities collapsed into a single figure, each term reinforcing the others. The outlaw here is defined by what stands against him: Law and Order personified as the force that has placed a price on his head.

Yet the term also reaches toward the mythic. The "celestial outlaw" (006-001) appears in a metaphor for a bucking horse, its spirit described as something that belongs to the stars rather than to earth. The outlaw bronc is not merely difficult—it is touched by the sublime, its rebellion a cosmic quality rather than a behavioral flaw.

The practical cowboy encounters the outlaw not as myth but as daily danger. One speaker boasts, "I've got no fear of an outlaw steer" (060-001), the confidence of a man who has faced the untamed and learned to handle it. The outlaw steer, like the outlaw horse, represents the test that every cowboy must pass.

In a gentler register, the term can simply describe spirited independence. "On that outlaw bronc a-straddle / She is one that can't be beat" (032-001)—here the outlaw horse is not a menace but a challenge met with admiration, its rider proud to have mastered what others could not.

## Cultural Significance

The outlaw occupies a central place in cowboy mythology precisely because the cowboy himself stands between civilization and wilderness. The outlaw—whether man or beast—is what the cowboy might become if he chose the wild completely. To [[lasso|rope]] and brand the outlaw is to assert human order over nature's chaos, but the poets of the anthology never pretend that the victory is permanent. Every [[corral]] holds the possibility of an outlaw within it.
