## Overview

Burke Jenkins is a poet whose contribution to the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology is "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] to His Friend in Need" (ch039), a poem that celebrates the cowboy's intimate and life-saving relationship with his firearm — a .45 caliber Colt revolver addressed with genuine affection as "you blessed old son-of-a-gun."

## Literary Context

"The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] to His Friend in Need" exemplifies a recurring motif in cowboy poetry: the personification of the cowboy's tools, particularly his gun. Jenkins closes the poem with a direct, affectionate address to the weapon: "For I love you, you blessed old son-of-a-gun — / You forty-five caliber Colt!" (039-001).

The poem's title — "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] to His Friend in Need" — frames the revolver not as a mere tool but as a companion and protector. The "friend in need" is the gun itself, the one constant presence that never fails its owner on the [[the-west|frontier]]. This personification elevates an object to the status of a relationship, reflecting the [[loneliness|isolation]] of cowboy life in which a man's most reliable companion might indeed be his weapon.

The tone is intimate and colloquial — the language of affection applied to a killing instrument. The phrase "blessed old son-of-a-gun" contains both genuine endearment and the rough-hewn diction characteristic of cowboy speech. The closing exclamation mark after "Colt!" suggests a toast or a declaration, as if the poem itself is a salute to the weapon.

Jenkins's poem belongs to a tradition of cowboy verse that treats firearms not as symbols of violence but as essential tools of survival and identity — akin to the cowboy's horse or saddle, deserving of the same loyalty and affection.

> You're very well polished, I'm free to confess,
> Well balanced, well rounded, a power for right;
> But cool and collected—no steel could be less;
> You're primed for continual fight.
> Your voice is a bellicose bark of ill-will,
> On hatred and choler you seem to have fed;
> But when I control you, your temper is nil;
> In fact, you're most easily led.
> Though lead is your diet and fight is your fun,
> I simply can't give you the jolt;
> For I love you, you blessed old son-of-a-gun—
> You forty-five caliber Colt!
>
> (039-001)
