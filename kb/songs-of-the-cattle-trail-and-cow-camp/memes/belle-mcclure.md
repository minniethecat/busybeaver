# Belle McClure

Belle McClure is the female lead of [[william-maxwell|William Maxwell]]'s narrative poem "[[The-Dance-at-Silver-Valley|The Dance at Silver Valley]]" — the woman whose affections become the fatal pivot between [[broncho-bill|Broncho Bill]] and the gambler [[jim-kershaw|Jim Kershaw]] (005-001). She is one of the few named female characters in [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, and her dramatic arc — from dancing partner to the object of lethal competition — condenses the anthology's rare engagement with romantic tragedy.

## Character Background

Belle enters the poem as an image of [[the-west|frontier]] femininity: "dressed in blue, ribbon in her hair" (005-001), she is the center of attention at the Silver Valley dance. Her social world is defined by the dance-hall economy of the Western [[the-west|frontier]] — a space where women's presence is scarce enough to warrant fatal competition. The parenthetical aside "(Out of hell that woman came)" (005-001) is attributed to [[bill|Bill]]'s fevered consciousness, but it also captures the poem's ambivalence about Belle's role: is she a prize, a temptress, or simply a woman caught between two violent men?

| "The Dance at Silver Valley" | (005-001) | central female figure / love interest |

Belle's presence in the poem is defined through the male gaze: [[bill|Bill]] "whirls a dizzy spin" with her (005-001), Kershaw "put his arm around her waist, her graceful waist and slim" (005-001), and [[bill|Bill]]'s dying words are "I must ride the trail alone. Goodbye to Belle McClure" (005-001) — a farewell in which Belle herself has no voice.

## Significance

Belle McClure occupies a distinctive position in the Lomax anthology as one of the few women named in the poems who is not a comic type or an idealized love object. She is neither [[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]] (the comic female cowboy) nor the idealized waitress of "A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s [[love|Love]] Song." She is, instead, a figure of genuine narrative consequence — the woman whose choice between two men determines the poem's tragic outcome. Her silence at the poem's end (we never hear her reaction to [[bill|Bill]]'s [[death-imagery|death]]) is itself a comment on the limited agency afforded to women in the cowboy ballad tradition.

> Don't you hear the big spurs jingle?
