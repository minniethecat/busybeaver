## Overview

The saddle was the cowboy's most intimate tool. He spent more waking hours in it than in any chair; its quality determined his comfort, his safety, and his effectiveness at work. The poems in the [[John-A-Lomax|Lomax]] anthology treat the saddle not as mere equipment but as a symbol of the cowboy's identity — the seat from which he faced the world.

> We'll save 'em prime for the round-up time, and we riders'll all be there (009-001)

Though the saddle is rarely the explicit subject of a poem, it is everywhere present by implication. The cowboy rides; therefore the saddle is the unmentioned intermediary between man and [[horse-imagery|horse]], ground and sky.

## Cultural Significance

### Craftsmanship

A good saddle was a major investment — often the most expensive item a cowboy owned. Saddles were made by skilled craftsmen, fitted to both horse and rider, and maintained with care. A cowboy's saddle was as personal as his hat, and its quality reflected his status.

### The Saddle as Home

The saddle was the closest thing to home many cowboys had during the months of a [[trail-driving|trail drive]]. It was where he slept (using it as a pillow), where he worked, and where he retreated when the camp grew too crowded. The poems' frequent imagery of [[riding-imagery|riding]] — the constant motion of cowboy life — makes the saddle the cowboy's true residence.

### The Unsaddled Horse

When a poem describes a horse being unsaddled, it signals rest — the end of the day's work, the arrival at camp, the moment when the cowboy transitions from laborer to human being. Conversely, saddling up in the morning is the poem's signal that action is about to begin.

## In the Poems

> I've rounded up the cattle, I've ridden many a mile

The poems mention saddles in passing — a [[cinch]] tightened, a stirrup adjusted — but these small details carry weight. The saddle is the grammar of cowboy life: so fundamental to every action that it rarely needs to be named explicitly.
