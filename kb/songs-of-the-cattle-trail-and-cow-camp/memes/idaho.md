## Overview

Idaho, as a territorial region of the American West, enters the cowboy verse of "The Legend of Boastful [[bill|Bill]]" through its equine exports. The poem opens with [[round-up|a round-up]] scene on [[gila-river|the Gila River]] where "a hoss from Idaho" has thrown ten cowboys—establishing the horse's formidable bucking reputation before the boastful title character steps forward to tame it.

The reference to Idaho is brief but evocative, drawing on the region's association with the rugged mountain West and its livestock. In the economy of cowboy balladry, a horse "from Idaho" carries the implication of wildness, strength, and an ornery disposition—qualities that set the stage for Boastful [[bill|Bill]]'s crowning moment of cowboy bravado.

## In the Poems

"The Legend of Boastful [[bill|Bill]]" opens with the Idaho horse as the central challenge that the poem's hero must overcome:

> "At a round-up on the Gila
> One sweet morning long ago,
> Ten of us was throwed quite freely
> By a hoss from Idaho." (006-001)

The horse's Idaho origin is essential to the poem's setup: this is no ordinary mount but a creature from the wild [[montana|northern range]]s, and subduing it will prove Boastful [[bill|Bill]]'s claim to be "a ornery proposition for to hurt." The geographic distance—from Idaho to [[gila-river|the Gila]] River in [[arizona|Arizona]]—also hints at the wide-ranging movement of horses and cattle across the Western territories.
