# Arthur Chapman

Arthur Chapman was an American cowboy poet whose single contribution to [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]]'s *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp* — "[[out-where-the-west-begins-analysis|Out Where the West Begins]]" — became the most widely recognized poem in the collection and one of the most frequently quoted expressions of [[the-west|Western identity]] in American letters. The poem opens the body of the anthology proper, immediately following Part I: [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Yarns [005].

## Life & Context

Chapman's biographical details remain sparse in the context of the Lomax anthology. Unlike major contributors such as [[Charles-Badger-Clark|Charles Badger Clark]] and [[Henry-Herbert-Knibbs|Henry Herbert Knibbs]], who were specifically thanked by name in Lomax's Introduction (P03-002), Chapman is identified only through the attribution line appended to his poem. "[[out-where-the-west-begins-analysis|Out Where the West Begins]]" precedes even Knibbs's "The Shallows of the Ford" in the anthology's ordering, suggesting that Lomax regarded it as the collection's keynote — a poem that sets the emotional and geographical stage for everything that follows.

"[[out-where-the-west-begins-analysis|Out Where the West Begins]]" is structured as an anaphoric litany, each stanza beginning "Out where…" and closing with the refrain "That's where the West begins" (003-001). The poem defines the West not by longitude but by quality of character — stronger handclasps, longer smiles, brighter sun, whiter snow — a rhetorical strategy that transforms geography into moral landscape.

## Significance

Chapman holds a unique position in the anthology: his single poem serves as its unofficial overture. Unlike Knibbs or Clark, who contributed multiple poems across different sections, Chapman contributed only one — but that one became the collection's most enduring and quotable statement of [[the-west|Western identity]]. The poem's refrain, "That's where the West begins," entered the American idiom, appearing on postcards, in speeches, and in countless reprints throughout the twentieth century.

> Out where the handclasp's a little stronger,
> Out where the smile dwells a little longer,
> That's where the West begins;
> Out where the sun is a little brighter,
> Where the snows that fall are a trifle whiter,
> Where the bonds of home are a wee bit tighter,
> That's where the West begins.
> Out where the skies are a trifle bluer,
> Out where friendship's a little truer,
> That's where the West begins;
> Out where a fresher breeze is blowing,
> Where there's laughter in every streamlet flowing,
> Where there's more of reaping and less of sowing,
> That's where the West begins.
>
> (003-001)
