## Overview

Laramie, Wyoming, appears in "[[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" as the setting for a cowboy's happiest memories—walking through town with his beloved [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]]. The town serves as the stage for a pastoral [[love|romance]] that ends in tragedy, framing Laramie as a place of both joy and loss along the cattle [[the-west|frontier]].

Founded in 1868 as a tent city along the Union Pacific Railroad, Laramie sits on the Laramie Plains between the Snowy Range and the Laramie Range of the [[rockies|Rocky Mountains]]. During the cattle trail era, the town served as a supply point and shipping hub for ranches on the open range of southeastern Wyoming.

The poem's repeated refrain—"Walkin' down through Laramie with [[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]"—makes the town name a rhythmic anchor in the verse, evoking the image of a young couple strolling through a Western cattle town in its early days.

## Geography

Laramie lies at an elevation of 7,165 feet on the high Laramie Plains of southeastern Wyoming, bordered by the Medicine Bow Mountains to the [[the-west|west]] and the Laramie Range to the east. The Laramie River flows northward through the town, a tributary of the North Platte, linking the settlement to the greater Platte River system that guided emigrant trails [[the-west|west]]ward.

The high plains climate—with its short summers, long winters, and ceaseless wind—shaped the character of the town and the lives of the cowboys who passed through it. The surrounding [[prairie-imagery|grasslands]] supported the cattle ranches that defined the regional economy during the open-range era.

## In the Poems

"[[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" opens with the narrator recalling his youth in Laramie: "I was young and happy and my heart was light and gay / Singin', always singin' through the sunny summer day / Happy as a lizard in the wavin' chaparral / Walkin' down through Laramie with [[snagtooth-sal|Snagtooth Sal]]" (025-001). The town is the geographic frame for the narrator's entire love story—from the "sunny summer day" of courtship to the final wish to be buried where "the lily blossoms spring" so that [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]] might still yearn to see him walking through Laramie.

The refrain repeats Laramie four times across the poem's stanzas (025-001), each time binding the narrator's emotional state to the town. In life, Laramie is where he walks proudly with his gal; in [[death-imagery|death]], it becomes the remembered landscape of irretrievable happiness. The poem's final stanza offers the narrator's dying wish—to be laid to rest in a grave where the wild rose and lily bloom, so that [[snagtooth-sal|Sal]] "will want to see me walkin' down through Laramie once more."
