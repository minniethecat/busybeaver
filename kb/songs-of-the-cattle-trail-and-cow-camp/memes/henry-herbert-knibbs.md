# Henry Herbert Knibbs

Henry Herbert Knibbs (1874–1945) was an American cowboy poet and novelist, recognized by [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] in the Introduction to *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp* as one of the anthology's principal literary contributors. Lomax singled out Knibbs and [[Charles-Badger-Clark|Charles Badger Clark]], Jr., as the two authors to whom his "grateful thanks" were especially extended: "others have been lifted from collections of Western verse written by such men as [[charles-badger-clark|Charles Badger Clark]], Jr., and Herbert H. Knibbs" (P03-002).

## Life & Context

Knibbs was born in Niagara Falls, Ontario, in 1874 and immigrated to the United States, where he worked a series of Western jobs — ranch hand, [[puncher|cowpuncher]], and writer — before establishing himself as a published poet and novelist. His experience of the West was both lived and literary: he knew the trail from the saddle as well as from the page. By the time Lomax's anthology appeared in 1916, Knibbs had already published several volumes of verse, and his reputation as a chronicler of cowboy life was secure. He continued writing into the 1930s and 1940s, producing Western novels alongside poetry, and died in 1945.

## Key Appearances

Knibbs contributed multiple poems to the Lomax anthology, spanning Part I ([[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Yarns) and Part II (The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Off Guard):

| Poem | PN Range | Type |
|------|----------|------|
| "The Shallows of the Ford" | [004] | narrative ballad |
| "The Desert" | [011] | descriptive/meditative |
| "The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]]'s Ball" | [019] | social/dance poem |
| "Riders of the Stars" | [009] | night-[[riding-imagery|riding]] meditation |

His poems are distinguished by their narrative economy and careful attention to sensory detail — the heat of the desert, the rhythm of the horse, the vast silence of the night ride.

> Did you ever wait for daylight when the stars along the river
> Floated thick and white as snowflakes in the water deep and strange,
> Till a whisper through the aspens made the current break and shiver
> As the frosty edge of morning seemed to melt and spread and change?
> Once I waited, almost wishing that the dawn would never find me;
> Saw the sun roll up the ranges like the glory of the Lord;
>
> (004-001)

## Significance

Alongside [[charles-badger-clark|Charles Badger Clark]], Knibbs represents the transition from anonymous oral tradition to literary cowboy poetry. Where Clark's voice is rhetorical and anthemic, Knibbs's is quieter and more meditative, as seen in "Riders of the Stars" (009-001), where the night ride becomes a near-mystical experience. His inclusion in the anthology, at Lomax's explicit invitation, signals the collection's ambition to bridge folk song and formal verse — to preserve not just the cowboy's songs but the best of what cowboy poets had written.
