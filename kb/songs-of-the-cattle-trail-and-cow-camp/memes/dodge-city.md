## Overview

Dodge City was the archetypal [[kansas]] cowtown of the 1870s and 1880s, the railhead where the [[santa-fe]] line met the great cattle trails from [[texas]]. For the cowboy who had spent months pushing longhorns north across the plains, Dodge City meant the end of the drive—payday, saloons, dance halls, and a taste of civilization before the long ride home. Its name alone conjured the rough-and-tumble freedom of the trail's end.

The anthology's single reference to Dodge City comes embedded in a poem of reunion and [[homesickness|nostalgia]]: "The past when we headed each year for Dodge City / And punched up the drags on [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail]]" (019-001). The town is inseparable from the trail that fed it, and both are inseparable from memory itself—the poem frames the Dodge City of the past as a place that exists now only in the recollection of aging cowboys.

## In the Poems

### The Cattle Round-Up: Nostalgia and the Lost Trail (Chapter 19)

"The Cattle [[round-up|Round-Up]]" is not, despite its title, a work-day poem. It is a reunion poem—the "season of pleasure" where old trail hands gather "for the sake of old times" and "drink to the lees in the eyes of the fair" (019-001). Dodge City appears in the poem's central nostalgic turn, as the speaker catalogs what has been lost:

"The past, when the [[prairie]] was big and the cattle / Were as 'scary' as ever the antelope grew— / When to carry a gun, to make our [[spurs]] rattle, / And to ride a blue streak was the most that we knew; / The past when we headed each year for Dodge City / And punched up the drags on [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail]]; / When the world was all bright and the girls were all pretty, / And a feller could 'mav'rick' and stay out of jail" (019-001).

The couplet linking Dodge City with the [[chisholm-trail]] is structurally significant: the town and the trail form a single unit of memory, a destination and its approach that together define the rhythm of the cowboy's year. The phonetic echo of "City" and "pretty" binds the town to the golden haze of youth, while the final line—"a feller could 'mav'rick' and stay out of jail"—acknowledges, with a wink, the legal gray zone that made Dodge City both thrilling and dangerous.

### Dodge City as Memory

The poem's frame—a reunion dance, the "Cattlemen's Ball"—places Dodge City in double retrospect. The cowboys, now older, remember the town as it was when they were young; the poem, written after the trail era had ended, remembers the cowboys themselves. Dodge City thus functions as a memory within a memory, a place that exists in three tenses: the real town of the 1870s–80s, the remembered town of the aging cowboy, and the elegized town of the poem's reader.

> The past when we headed each year for Dodge City
> And punched up the drags on the old Chisholm Trail;
>
> (019-001)
