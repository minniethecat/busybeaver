## Overview

Wichita, Kansas, was one of the key cattle towns of the post–Civil War era, serving as a railhead destination for [[texas|Texas]] trail herds moving north along the [[The-Old-Chisholm-Trail|Chisholm Trail]]. In the poem "To Hear Him Tell It," an old-time cowman recalls trailing cattle to Wichita as part of his reminiscences of the early days of the cattle trade, placing the town squarely in the geography of the great trail drives.

The cowman's memory of driving herds "up to Wichita, / Cross the [[kansas|Kansas]] line" (014-001) situates the town as a milestone on the long journey from [[texas|Texas]] to the northern markets, marking the point where the trail crossed from Indian Territory into [[kansas|Kansas]] and the railroad awaited.

## In the Poems

"To Hear Him Tell It" features Wichita in the speech of a veteran cowman recounting his exploits to a younger listener at a bar. The old-timer boasts of his early achievements:

> "Yessir, trailed 'em up to Wichita,
> Cross the Kansas line,
> Made deliveries at Benton
> As early as fifty-nine." (014-001)

The reference to 1859 places the cowman's trail-driving career in the earliest days of the cattle trade, before the great boom of the 1870s and 1880s. Wichita here stands not just as a physical destination but as a symbol of the trail driver's experience—the endpoint of a long, dusty journey where cattle became money and cowboys became legends.
