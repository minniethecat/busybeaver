## Overview

Chaps appear in 7 passages in the anthology. "Chaps, [[the-sombrero|sombrero]], handkerchief" (005-001) places them first in the inventory of cowboy dress, suggesting their primacy. More than any other garment, chaps defined the cowboy's working body—the leather-clad legs that could push through [[mesquite]], resist [[lasso|rope]] burns during roping, and shield against cold and rain on the open range.

The word "chaps" derives from Spanish "chaparajos" (or "chapareras"), literally "leather breeches," reflecting the vaquero origins of cowboy equipment. In the poems, chaps are a synecdoche for the cowboy himself—"star-dust on his chaps" (006-001) suggests both the rider's celestial ambition and his grounded, dusty reality.

> Twenty abreast down the Golden Street ten thousand riders marched;
> Bow-legged boys in their swinging chaps, all clumsily keeping time;
> And the Angel Host to the lone, last ghost their delicate eyebrows arched
> As the swaggering sons of the open range drew up to the throne sublime.
> Gaunt and grizzled, a Texas man from out of the concourse strode,
> And doffed his hat with a rude, rough grace, then lifted his eagle head;
>
> (009-001)

## Key Aspects

### Protection

Chaps protected the cowboy's legs from brush, cactus, thorns, and [[lasso|rope]] abrasion. On the cattle trail, where the rider might push through dense vegetation for hours, chaps were not decoration but survival equipment.

### Types

Working cowboys used heavy leather "shotgun" or "batwing" chaps. The anthology's chaps are working gear, not the fringed show chaps of later rodeo culture.

### Symbolism

"Star-dust on his chaps and saddle" (006-001) transforms functional leather into cosmic fabric—the cowboy carries starlight on his working clothes. This elevation of the practical to the mythic is characteristic of the anthology's sensibility.
