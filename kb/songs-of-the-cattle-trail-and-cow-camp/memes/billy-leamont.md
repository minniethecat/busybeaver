## Overview

Billy Leamont is the subject of a tragic cowboy ballad preserved as a fragment in Chapter 74 of [[Songs-of-the-Cattle-Trail-and-Cow-Camp|Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp]]. The ballad is only partially quoted — six lines plus a two-line fragment — but its narrative arc is clear: Leamont and his companion [[jack-lorell|Jack Lorell]] ride out of town together, something goes terribly wrong, and both men die by violence (074-001).

## The Ballad

"The Ballad of Billy Leamont" opens with the two cowboys riding out from town across the prairie:

> "Billy Leamont rode out of the town—
>
> *Close at his shoulder rode Jack Lorell—*
>
> Over the leagues of the prairies brown,
>
> Into the hills where the sun goes down—
>
> *Billy Leamont and Jack Lorell!*"
>
> (074-001)

The surviving fragment of the ballad's conclusion is stark and fatal:

> "Billy Leamont looked down the dell—
>
> *Dead below him lay Jack Lorell—*
>
> With his gun at his forehead he fired and fell,
>
> Then rode they two through the streets of hell—
>
> *Billy Leamont and Jack Lorell!*"
>
> (074-001)

The ballad's circumstances — what happened between the ride out and the deaths — remain unknown, the missing stanzas lost with the oral tradition from which the fragment was collected. This incompleteness gives the ballad a haunting quality: the listener knows the ending but not the story that led to it.

## Context

The ballad fragment is quoted as an epigraph to "[[Ridin-Up-the-Rocky-Trail-from-Town]]" (Chapter 74), a comic poem about cowboys getting drunk and brawling in town. The juxtaposition is deliberate: the comic poem celebrates the cowboy's "wild and strange" nature, while the tragic ballad shows where that wildness can lead — from barroom brawl to death on the prairie.
