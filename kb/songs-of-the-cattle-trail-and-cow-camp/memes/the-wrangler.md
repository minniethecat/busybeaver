## Overview

The cowboy responsible for the remuda—often the youngest hand on the drive, the wrangler's skill with horses was essential to trail operations.

As depicted throughout *Songs of the Cattle Trail and Cow Camp*, the wrangler was an integral part of the cowboy's world—not merely a practical necessity but a source of identity, pride, and poetic inspiration.

## In the Anthology

The poems collected by [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] capture the wrangler as lived experience rather than romanticized abstraction. The verses reflect the working cowboy's intimate knowledge of his tools, practices, and social roles—knowledge earned through long days in the saddle and long nights under the stars.

## Cultural Significance

The Wrangler represents more than a practical facet of trail life; it embodies the values and self-image of the cowboy. The anthology preserves these details at the moment when the open-range era was fading, making it not only literature but a document of a vanished way of life.
