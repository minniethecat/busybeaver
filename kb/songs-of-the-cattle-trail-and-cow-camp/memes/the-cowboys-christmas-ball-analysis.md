## Overview

"The [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]]' Christmas Ball," by [[larry-chittenden|William Lawrence Chittenden]], is perhaps the most famous single poem in the anthology — a work that transcended its page to become a living tradition. The poem commemorates a real event: the Christmas dance held at M.G. Rhodes's Morning Star Hotel in Anson City, [[texas|Texas]], in 1885. [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] himself noted the poem's enduring popularity in his Introduction [P03-002].

> That I attended "The Cowboys' Christmas Ball." (048-001)

The poem has since inspired an annual reenactment in Anson, [[texas|Texas]], making it one of the rare instances of a cowboy poem that migrated from literary anthology to living folk tradition.

## Structure and Setting

The poem opens with geographic precision:

> Way out in Western Texas, where the Clear Fork's waters flow

Chittenden meticulously establishes the dance's setting — the Morning Star Hotel, the musical accompaniment, the mixed company of cowboys and local women — before launching into the rollicking description of the dance itself. The poem's festive energy builds through accumulation of detail: the "fiddle and a lively tambourine," the "viol come imported, by stage from [[abilene|Abilene]]," the room decorated "with mistletoe and shawls" (048-001).

## Key Themes

### Civilization on the Frontier

The poem is a study in contrasts: the rough cowboys in their finest clothes, the imported violin from distant [[abilene]], the mistletoe and candles transforming a [[the-west|frontier]] hotel into a ballroom. The Christmas Ball represents the moment when the [[the-west|frontier]] dresses itself in civilization — not to reject its roughness, but to prove that roughness can dance.

### The Dance as Equalizer

At the Christmas Ball, social hierarchies dissolve. [[cowboy-culture|Cowboys]] who spend their days in the saddle become dancers; women who live in [[loneliness|isolation]] on the [[the-west|frontier]] become belles. The dance is a temporary utopia where the rules of the trail are suspended and replaced by the rules of the ballroom, however imperfectly learned.

### Music as Transformation

The poem's treatment of music is central: the fiddle and tambourine are not mere accompaniment but agents of transformation. The music turns the Morning Star Hotel into a magical space, the cowboys into gentlemen, and the harsh [[texas|Texas]] winter night into a celebration.

## Legacy

The poem's enduring legacy — from Lomax's anthology to the annual Anson Christmas Ball — demonstrates the power of cowboy poetry to shape cultural memory. Chittenden's poem did not merely describe a dance; it created a tradition. The fact that a poem preserved in a 1916 anthology continues to be celebrated in twenty-first-century [[texas|Texas]] is a testament to the authenticity of its vision.
