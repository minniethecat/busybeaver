## Overview

Dakota Territory marked the far northern limit of the cattle trail system, the distant horizon toward which [[texas|Texas]] herds were driven across the fenceless plains. In "[[A-Cowboy-Toast|A Cowboy Toast]]," Dakota stands as the endpoint of the cowboy's geographic imagination—the farthest reach from the southern starting points of the great trail drives, compressing thousands of miles of open range into a single couplet.

The poem places Dakota at the culmination of an elegy for the passing cowboy: "From the [[llano-estacado|Llano Estacado]] to Dakota's distant sands, / Where were herded countless thousands in the days of fenceless lands" (073-001). This geographic pairing—the [[llano-estacado|Staked Plains]] of [[texas|Texas]] in the south, the Dakota [[prairie-imagery|grasslands]] in the north—maps the entire vertical span of the cattle trail, making the cowboy's domain coterminous with the American West itself.

##[[A-Cowboy-Toast|A Cowboy Toast]]
### A Cowboy Toast: Elegy for the Open Range (Chapter 73)

"A [[cowboy-culture|Cowboy]] Toast" is structured as an extended elegy. Its refrain—"But cattle, cattle, cattle, and sun and sage and sand" (073-001)—pulses through the poem like a heartbeat, the repetition evoking the monotony and immensity of the cowboy's world. Dakota appears only at the poem's close, when the elegiac mode shifts to a toast:

"Then quaff with me a bumper of water, clear and pure, / To the memory of the cowboy whose fame must e'er endure / From [[llano-estacado|the Llano]] Estacado to Dakota's distant sands" (073-001).

The choice of "water, clear and pure" rather than whiskey is significant—this is a sober memorial, not a rowdy celebration. The poem's closing vision of an "altar in the Temple of the Brave" woven from "[[texas|Texas]] grasses" and its final return to the elemental catalogue—"cattle, cattle, cattle, and sage and sand and sun" (073-001)—places Dakota within a sacred geography of the cowboy's vanished world.

### The Northern Boundary

The phrase "Dakota's distant sands" is striking: sand, not prairie grass or fertile soil. The word choice suggests the alien quality of the far north as perceived from the southern trail driver's perspective—a place more desert than pasture, more mirage than destination. Like [[llano-estacado|the Llano]] Estacado at the southern end, Dakota's "sands" represent the existential limits of the cattleman's range.

> To the memory of the cowboy whose fame must e'er endure
> From the Llano Estacado to Dakota's distant sands,
> Where were herded countless thousands in the days of fenceless lands.
>
> (073-001)
