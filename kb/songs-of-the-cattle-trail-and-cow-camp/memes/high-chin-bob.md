## Overview

High Chin Bob is the protagonist of [[charles-badger-clark|Charles Badger Clark]]'s ballad "High Chin Bob" (ch016), a cowboy of "sinful pride and [[maverick]]-hungry [[lasso|rope]]" (013-001) who embodies the [[the-west|frontier]] virtues of persistence and refusal to quit. His name reflects his defining trait — chin held high in defiance of circumstance — and his adventure with a mountain lion in the Mokione Mountains stands as one of the anthology's most vivid narrative poems.

## Character Analysis

Bob is introduced descending into the [[mokiones|Mokiones]] on horseback: "a-trippin' down the slope, / But High Chin Bob of sinful pride and [[maverick]]-hungry [[lasso|rope]]" (013-001). His defining trait is pride — he declares "Oh, glory be to me!" (013-001) and sees himself as a heroic figure out of the dime novels he has read.

The poem is a comic ballad of cowboy bravado pushed to absurdity. Bob successfully [[lasso|rope]]s a mountain lion, but finds he can neither kill nor release it. For three days he is dragged across the mountains, his pride preventing surrender: "But I'll keep this dally round the horn until the toot of judgment morn / Before I'll holler 'nough!" (013-001)

The poem's conclusion transforms Bob from comic figure to folk hero. His final declaration — "Oh, pardner, tell my friends below I took a ragin' dream in tow, / And if I didn't lay him low, I never turned him loose!" (013-001) — reframes stubbornness as integrity. In the cowboy code, to "turn loose" is the ultimate failure; Bob's refusal to quit, however absurd, earns a kind of grim respect.

> 'Way high up in the Mokiones, among the mountain tops,
> A lion cleaned a yearling's bones and licks his thankful chops;
> And who upon the scene should ride, a-trippin' down the slope,
> But High Chin Bob of sinful pride and maverick-hungry rope.
>
> (013-001)

## Significance

Clark himself identified "High Chin Bob" as one of his signature works. [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]] noted in the Introduction that the poem circulated widely in oral transmission among cowboys, and he printed a cowboy variant alongside Clark's original [P03-002]. The poem demonstrates how oral ballad tradition could reshape literary verse — the cowboy versions often improved the rhythm and added vernacular authenticity.
