## Overview

The sombrero appears in 5 passages in the anthology. "[[chaps|Chaps]], sombrero, handkerchief" (005-001) lists the hat as part of the cowboy's defining costume, equal with his [[chaps|leather leggings]] and neckerchief. The sombrero's practical function—shielding face and neck from the relentless Western sun—elevated it from fashion to necessity, while its distinctive silhouette made it the cowboy's most recognizable attribute.

The sombrero's wide brim and high crown, adapted from Mexican vaquero tradition, marked the cowboy as a figure of the borderlands—culturally hybrid, practically evolved for a specific environment. In the poems, the sombrero signals cowboy identity as reliably as the saddle or the horse.

> Here's to the passing cowboy, the plowman's pioneer;
> His home, the boundless mesa, he of any man the peer;
> Around his wide sombrero was stretched the rattler's hide,
> His bridle sporting conchos, his lasso at his side.
>
> (073-001)

## Key Aspects

### Practical Function

The sombrero protected against sun, rain, and dust. Its wide brim created a portable shade; its high crown insulated against heat. It could also serve as a water bucket, a fan for campfires, and a signal flag.

### Symbol of Identity

"[[chaps|Chaps]], sombrero, handkerchief" (005-001) catalogs the cowboy through his clothing. The sombrero's silhouette was what people saw first—the hat made the man recognizable before his face was visible.

### Cultural Heritage

The sombrero's Mexican origins reflect the cattle industry's roots in Spanish and Mexican ranching traditions. The cowboy's adoption of the sombrero is part of the broader cultural synthesis that created the American cowboy from diverse sources.
