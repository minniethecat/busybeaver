## Overview

Texas is the origin point of the great cattle drives that form the backbone of cowboy poetry in this collection. "The long, long trail winding from Texas to [[montana|Montana]]" (P03-002) establishes the geographic arc of the cowboy's world. The anthology's editor, [[john-a-lomax|John A. Lomax]], compiled these songs from the University of Texas at [[austin|Austin]], where the songs of the range found their way into academic preservation (P03-003).

Texas cowboys appear throughout the poems as archetypes of [[the-cattle-trail|the cattle trail]]. "He was a Texas cowboy—never heard of Rome at all" (007-001) contrasts the rough-hewn Texan with classical civilization, celebrating the cowboy's untutored authenticity. The state looms so large in cowboy identity that its name becomes a byword for the cattle trade itself: "Wild days in Texas by the [[rio-grande|Rio Grande]]" (066-001) condenses an entire way of life into a single geographic phrase.

## Cowboys from Texas

Named Texas cowboys populate the collection's narratives, their state identity inseparable from their character. "Apache Johnson and Texas Ned / Saddled up their hosses and started out with speed" (008-001) presents a pair whose Texas origin certifies them as authentic [[riders-imagery|riders]]. In a celestial vision, a "gaunt and grizzled, a Texas man from out of the concourse" (009-001) appears among the star-[[riding-imagery|riding]] cowboys of the afterlife, his weathered Texas features marking a life spent on the range.

"Me and [[texas-tom|Texas Tom]] was out / Takin' of a moonlight scout" (022-001) opens a comic narrative, the name "[[texas-tom|Texas Tom]]" itself a badge of origin. The old Texas cowman who recounts selling cattle—"Said the old Texas cowman, 'Now I'm tellin' you!'" (014-001)—speaks with the authority of one who has driven herds from the state to northern markets.

## In the Poems

Texas is mentioned or evoked across numerous poems in the collection. "A Texas man from out of the concourse" (009-001) rides in the stars alongside other cowboy figures, his origin marking him as a true rider of the range. "She was a Texas maiden, she came of low degree" (011-001) opens a ballad set firmly in Texas soil.

"Way out in Western Texas, where the Clear Fork's waters flow" (048-001) locates a poem in the specific geography of the state's ranchlands, while "Some Texas steer pursuing o'er the pathless plains" (073-001) follows the trail of a longhorn across the open range. Texas appears even in the casual idiom of cowboy toughness: "Scratches don't count / In Texas, down by the [[rio-grande|Rio Grande]]" (010-001) records the code of the range—minor injuries are beneath notice in a land that bred hard men.

The state also serves as the place a cowboy leaves—or is run out of: "I stayed in Texas till they runned me out" (016-001) captures the restless mobility of cowboy life, where even one's home state could become a place of departure.

## Texas as Symbol: Freedom and Identity

Beyond its geographic reality, Texas functions in the anthology as a symbol — shorthand for the cowboy's origin story and the values of the open range. The phrase "Texas cowboy" recurs as a compound identity, the state name doing more work than mere location — it signals authenticity, toughness, and a certain untamed quality that the anthology celebrates.

The contrast with [[Kansas]] is instructive. [[kansas|Kansas]] appears in the poems primarily as a destination — the railhead, the endpoint of the drive, the place where cattle become commerce. Texas, by contrast, is the place of origin: where the cattle are born, where the cowboys are formed, where the trail begins. This Texas-as-origin / [[kansas|Kansas]]-as-destination dyad structures the emotional geography of the entire collection. To be "from Texas" in these poems is to be from the source — authentic in a way that no other origin can confer.

The symbolic weight of Texas extends even into the afterlife. When "a Texas man from out of the concourse" (009-001) appears in a vision of celestial [[riders-imagery|riders]], the detail is not incidental. In the cowboy's imagined heaven, the most authentic [[riders-imagery|riders]] are still from Texas — the state has become a cosmic credential, persisting beyond mortality.

## Geography of the Trail

Texas anchored the southern end of the great cattle trail system. From the [[Rio-Grande]] borderlands to the Panhandle plains, the state's vast rangelands bred the longhorn cattle that would be driven north to railheads in [[Kansas]] and beyond. The trail itself began in the Texas brush country, crossed rivers like the Brazos and the [[colorado|Colorado]] (066-001), and stretched toward the distant northern markets.
