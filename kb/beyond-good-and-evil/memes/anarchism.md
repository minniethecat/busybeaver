# Anarchism

## Overview

Anarchism — the political philosophy that advocates the abolition of the state and all forms of coercive hierarchy — occupies a paradoxical position in relation to Nietzsche's thought. On the surface, Nietzsche's celebration of individual sovereignty and his contempt for the state might seem to align him with anarchism.

But Nietzsche's "individualism" is aristocratic, not democratic: he does not believe that all individuals are equal or that all are capable of self-rule. The anarchist vision of a society without hierarchy is, for Nietzsche, a fantasy — and a dangerous one, because it disguises the will to power that would inevitably reassert itself in such a society.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Anarchism is not directly named in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but it is the implicit endpoint of the "democratic enlightenment" that Nietzsche critiques throughout the text. If the logic of democracy is the progressive elimination of all authority, then anarchism is democracy's logical conclusion.

In aphorism 202, Nietzsche describes the "herd animal" of modern Europe as having been "raised to the dignity of the supreme value" (006-002). The anarchist is the herd animal who has internalized the demand for equality so thoroughly that he cannot recognize the will to power in his own demand for liberation. He believes he is abolishing power; in fact, he is merely redirecting it.

## Core Doctrines

1. **The abolition of the state**: The demand that all coercive political authority be eliminated — which Nietzsche would see as a fantasy, since authority is not a contingent feature of political life but an expression of the will to power.
2. **Voluntary association**: The ideal of freely chosen communities replacing compulsory institutions — which Nietzsche would admire as an aesthetic ideal but doubt as a practical possibility.
3. **The rejection of hierarchy**: The claim that all hierarchies are illegitimate — which contradicts Nietzsche's fundamental conviction that the "order of rank" is the natural condition of healthy societies.
4. **Direct action**: The anarchist principle that individuals should act without waiting for institutional authorization — which Nietzsche might recognize as a form of the sovereign individual's self-command.

## Key Figures

- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** — The first self-described anarchist; his *What is Property?* (1840) laid the foundations of anarchist theory.
- **Mikhail Bakunin** — The Russian revolutionary who developed collectivist anarchism and opposed Marx in the First International.
- **Max Stirner** — The author of *The Ego and Its Own* (1844), whose radical individualism has often been compared to Nietzsche's (though Nietzsche's relationship to Stirner is contested and indirect).

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of anarchism is that it mistakes the abolition of power for liberation. The anarchist who destroys the state does not thereby eliminate the will to power; he merely creates a vacuum that will be filled by new forms of domination — probably less civilized and more brutal than the ones he overthrew.

More fundamentally, anarchism shares with Christianity and socialism the fantasy of a world without suffering, domination, or inequality — a world from which the tragic character of existence has been eliminated. For Nietzsche, such a world is neither possible nor desirable. The point is not to eliminate domination but to ennoble it, not to abolish hierarchy but to create hierarchies worthy of the name.
