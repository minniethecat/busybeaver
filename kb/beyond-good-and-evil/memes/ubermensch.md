# Übermensch

## Overview

The Übermensch (often translated as "Overman," "Superman," or "over-human") is one of Nietzsche's most famous and most misunderstood concepts. Introduced most prominently in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883–1885), the Übermensch is Nietzsche's ideal of human self-overcoming: a being who has moved beyond the constraints of conventional morality, created their own values, and affirmed life in its totality — including its suffering and its eternal recurrence. Though the term does not appear in *Beyond Good and Evil*, the figure of the "philosopher of the future" and the "free spirit" that populate BGE are the Übermensch under different names.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

The word *Übermensch* does not appear in *Beyond Good and Evil*. But the *concept* is everywhere. Nietzsche's "philosophers of the future" — those "free, VERY free spirits" [002-021] who stand beyond the opposition of good and evil — are the Übermensch in philosophical form. They are described as:

> …these philosophers of the future might rightly, perhaps also wrongly, be designated as attempters… [002-019]

The philosopher of the future is an "attempter" (*Versucher*) — someone who experiments with new modes of life and thought rather than defending inherited certainties. This experimental attitude is the core of the Übermensch ideal: the Übermensch does not receive values from tradition but creates them.

The Übermensch also stands in contrast to the "last man" — Nietzsche's term (from *Zarathustra*) for the comfortable, risk-averse modern individual who wants only security and mild pleasures. BGE's critique of democratic leveling and utilitarian morality is, in effect, a diagnosis of the conditions that produce the last man and prevent the emergence of the Übermensch.

For Nietzsche, the Übermensch is not a biological category but a spiritual and cultural achievement. It represents what humanity can become when it frees itself from what Nietzsche sees as the two-thousand-year tyranny of Christian-Platonic morality.
