# Stoicism

## Overview

Stoicism was the dominant philosophical school of the Hellenistic and Roman periods, founded by Zeno of Citium around 300 BCE. Its central tenet — that virtue consists in living according to Nature and reason — made it the unofficial philosophy of the Roman elite.

For Nietzsche, Stoicism exemplified the self-deception inherent in all moral philosophies that claim to derive values from "Nature."

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most famous engagement with Stoicism appears in aphorism 9 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where he launches a blistering critique of the Stoic ideal of "living according to Nature":

> "According to Nature" — you desire to live "according to Nature"? Oh, you noble Stoics, what fraud of words! Imagine a being like Nature, prodigal beyond measure, indifferent beyond measure, without purposes and consideration, without mercy and justice, fertile and desolate and uncertain at the same time; imagine Indifference itself as a power — how COULD you live according to this indifference? (001-009)

The point is devastating: Nature, as the Stoics themselves understood it, is a realm of deterministic necessity and cosmic indifference. To claim to "live according to Nature" is either to project one's preferences onto Nature, or to demand that one become as indifferent as Nature itself — a demand no human can fulfill without self-deception.

In aphorism 198, Nietzsche returns to Stoic themes in his discussion of the "disciplining" of the emotions. He critiques "the indispensable notion that one must CURTAIL and DOMESTICATE the passion, which the Stoics advised and fostered" (005-013), contrasting this with his own Dionysian ideal of the soul's fullness.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Living according to Nature**: The rational order of the cosmos (*logos*) provides the standard for human conduct; virtue is alignment with this order.
2. **The dichotomy of control**: Distinguishing what is within one's power (judgment, desire) from what is not (health, wealth, reputation); freedom lies in restricting concern to the former.
3. **Apatheia**: The ideal of freedom from disturbing passions, achieved through rational self-discipline — precisely the "curtailment" of emotion that Nietzsche critiques.
4. **Cosmopolitanism**: The Stoic ideal of the world-citizen, transcending local attachments — which Nietzsche might have recognized as a precursor to the "good European."
5. **Determinism and freedom**: The Stoic reconciliation of cosmic determinism with individual moral responsibility, which Nietzsche would see as another "fraud of words."

## Key Figures

- **Zeno of Citium** — Founder of the Stoa; his *Republic* outlined the original Stoic vision.
- **Seneca** — Roman statesman and philosopher; his *Letters* are the most influential Stoic text.
- **Epictetus** — The former slave whose *Enchiridion* (Manual) distills Stoic ethics to its essence.
- **Marcus Aurelius** — Roman emperor whose *Meditations* are a monument of Stoic self-examination.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Stoicism goes beyond the famous aphorism 9. The deeper problem with Stoicism is that it represents a form of tyranny exercised by reason over the instincts — a "disciplining" of the passions that Nietzsche regards as a disguised expression of the will to power. The Stoic does not eliminate the passions but channels them into self-domination; the will that sought to rule others turns inward and rules itself. This is precisely the mechanism Nietzsche identifies as the origin of the ascetic ideal.

Moreover, Stoicism's claim to derive its ethics from Nature is dishonest in the same way Nietzsche finds all moral systems dishonest: it projects a human valuation onto the cosmos and then claims to have discovered it there. The Stoic's Nature is a moralized Nature — "stammering 'Wisdom' and 'Virtue' at every step," as Nietzsche says of Kant — rather than the indifferent, purposeless force that genuine naturalism would acknowledge.
