# Socrates

## Overview

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) is, after Plato, the most important ancient figure in Nietzsche's philosophical genealogy. For Nietzsche, Socrates represents a turning point in the history of Western thought: the moment when instinct gave way to dialectics, when the tragic wisdom of the pre-Socratics was displaced by the rationalist optimism that would culminate in modern science. Socrates is the "theoretical man" — the type who believes that thought, guided by logic, can penetrate to the bottom of existence and even *correct* it.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Socrates appears in aphorism 190 of *Beyond Good and Evil* in a revealing passage: "There is something in the morality of Plato which does not really belong to Plato ... namely, Socratism, for which he himself was too noble" (005-005). Nietzsche distinguishes between the aristocratic Plato and the plebeian Socrates — the ugly, low-born dialectician who used reason as a weapon against his social superiors. Socratism is the leveling impulse in philosophy, the demand that every proposition justify itself before the tribunal of reason regardless of who utters it.

Socrates is also the implicit target of Nietzsche's critique of "the will to truth" in aphorism 1. It was Socrates who first demanded that life justify itself before reason — who made the value of existence dependent on its rational defensibility. Nietzsche's question — "WHY NOT RATHER untruth?" (001-001) — is directed against the Socratic prejudice that truth is always preferable to illusion.

## Key Ideas

1. **Dialectics**: The Socratic method of question and answer, which Nietzsche sees as an expression of *ressentiment* — the ugly man's revenge on the beautiful.
2. **The equation of reason with virtue**: Socrates' claim that knowledge is virtue and that no one does wrong willingly — a rationalist optimism that Nietzsche regards as naive.
3. **Theoretical optimism**: The faith that thought can correct existence — the founding prejudice of the Western philosophical tradition.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Plato** — Socrates' most famous pupil; Nietzsche distinguishes Socrates the plebeian dialectician from Plato the aristocratic visionary.
- **Euripides** — Nietzsche pairs Socrates with Euripides as the twin destroyers of Greek tragedy.
- **Jesus** — Nietzsche contrasts Socrates (who died cheerfully, without resentment) with Jesus (whose death Nietzsche sees as a form of revenge against life).
