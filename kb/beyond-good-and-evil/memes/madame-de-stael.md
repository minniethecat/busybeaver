# Madame de Staël

## Overview

Anne Louise Germaine de Staël-Holstein (22 April 1766 – 14 July 1817), commonly known as Madame de Staël, was a French-Swiss woman of letters, political theorist, and one of the most influential intellectual figures of Napoleonic Europe. Her salon at Coppet on Lake Geneva was a center of European liberal thought, and her works — notably *De l'Allemagne* (1813), which introduced German Romantic philosophy and literature to a French audience — shaped the cultural exchange between France and Germany. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche references Madame de Staël only to dismiss her, but the dismissal itself reveals the intellectual and political currents Nietzsche was reacting against.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's treatment of Madame de Staël in *Beyond Good and Evil* is brief, cutting, and characteristic of his views on women and intellectual culture. In Part VII ("Our Virtues"), Nietzsche devotes a series of aphorisms to the "woman question," and it is here that de Staël appears:

> It betrays corruption of the instincts — apart from the fact that it betrays bad taste — when a woman refers to Madame Roland, or Madame de Stael, or Monsieur George Sand, as though something were proved thereby in favour of "woman as she is." Among men, these are the three comical women as they are — nothing more! — and just the best involuntary counter-arguments against feminine emancipation and autonomy. [007-026]

Nietzsche's dismissiveness is unmistakable: de Staël, along with Madame Roland and George Sand, is reduced to a "comical woman." The passage is part of Nietzsche's broader polemic against women's emancipation — a theme that has made these sections of BGE among the most controversial in his corpus.

Nietzsche's real target here may be less de Staël herself than the use of historical examples to argue for women's intellectual equality. The logic he attacks is: "If women like de Staël were great intellectuals, then women in general are capable of intellectual greatness." Nietzsche's counter-move is to dismiss the examples rather than engage with the argument — a rhetorical strategy that reveals as much about his own commitments as about de Staël's legacy.

Earlier in BGE, Nietzsche also alludes to de Staël's famous confrontation with Napoleon:

> …Napoleon gave the too eloquent Madame de Stael to understand: *mulier taceat in ecclesia* [let the woman be silent in the church]… [007-025]

That Nietzsche reports Napoleon's silencing of de Staël without comment is itself a comment: the philosopher who elsewhere champions the "free spirit" here seems to endorse the emperor's patriarchal command.

## Key Ideas

**Literature as Cultural Mediation** — De Staël's *De l'Allemagne* introduced German thought to a French audience at a time when the two cultures were largely ignorant of each other. Nietzsche, himself a "good European" who valued cultural cross-pollination, might have recognized her as a forerunner of his own project — but his misogyny prevented him from doing so.

**Liberal Cosmopolitanism** — De Staël's salon was a model of the free intellectual exchange Nietzsche elsewhere champions. Her vision of a cosmopolitan European culture anticipates Nietzsche's "good Europeanism" — a connection Nietzsche never acknowledges.

**The Woman Question** — De Staël's career as a public intellectual made her an unavoidable reference point in nineteenth-century debates about women's intellectual capacities. Nietzsche's dismissal of her is part of a tradition of male philosophical misogyny that extends from Schopenhauer through to the twentieth century.
