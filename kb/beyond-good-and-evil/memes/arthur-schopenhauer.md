# Arthur Schopenhauer

## Overview

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was the German philosopher whose masterpiece *The World as Will and Representation* (1818/1844) provided Nietzsche with both his philosophical starting point and his primary target of critique. Nietzsche's early work *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872) is deeply Schopenhauerian in its metaphysics; by the time of *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886), Nietzsche has broken with Schopenhauer decisively, though the break is never complete. Schopenhauer remains the figure against whom Nietzsche defines his own philosophical project — the pessimist whose diagnosis of suffering Nietzsche accepts but whose remedy (aesthetic contemplation, ascetic renunciation) he rejects.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's treatment of Schopenhauer in *Beyond Good and Evil* is both respectful and critical. He credits Schopenhauer with philosophical depth while condemning the "half-Christian, half-German narrowness and stupidity" into which Schopenhauer's pessimism finally degenerated (003-013). The problem, for Nietzsche, is that Schopenhauer's pessimism remains covertly moralistic: it judges the world against a standard of what it *ought* to be and finds it wanting. This is disguised Christianity, not genuine world-affirmation.

In aphorism 19, Nietzsche critiques Schopenhauer's theory of the will. Schopenhauer "has given us to understand that the will alone is really known to us, absolutely and completely known" — but this is a "superstition," a projection of the grammatical habit of positing a subject behind every predicate (001-023). Nietzsche's critique of the "superstition of free will" is directed as much at Schopenhauer as at the wider metaphysical tradition.

In aphorism 252, Nietzsche places Schopenhauer in a lineage of German resistance to English empiricism, alongside Hegel and Goethe: "In the struggle against the English mechanical stultification of the world, Hegel and Schopenhauer (along with Goethe) were of one accord" (008-013). This is one of Nietzsche's rare moments of grouping Schopenhauer with figures he otherwise admires.

## Key Ideas

Schopenhauer's central doctrine is that the world has two aspects: as *representation* (the world as it appears to consciousness, structured by space, time, and causality) and as *will* (the world as it is in itself, a blind, striving, suffering-producing force). The will is the inner essence of all things, and because it is never satisfied — every satisfaction generates a new desire — existence is fundamentally characterized by suffering. Schopenhauer's proposed remedies are aesthetic contemplation (which temporarily stills the will) and ascetic renunciation (which aims at the permanent negation of the will-to-live).

Nietzsche accepts much of the diagnostic framework — the world as will, the ubiquity of suffering — but rejects the therapeutic conclusion. Where Schopenhauer counsels the negation of the will, Nietzsche demands its *affirmation*. The task is not to escape suffering but to embrace it as the condition of greatness.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Kant** — Schopenhauer's philosophy is built on Kant's transcendental idealism, though Schopenhauer simplifies Kant's elaborate categories into the single form of the "principle of sufficient reason."
- **Goethe** — Schopenhauer and Goethe were contemporaries; Schopenhauer admired Goethe's anti-Newtonian theory of color, and both represented the ideal of German *Geist* that Nietzsche inherited and transformed.
- **Wagner** — Schopenhauer's influence on Wagner (particularly *Tristan und Isolde*) was a key factor in Nietzsche's early embrace of Wagner and his later disillusionment with both figures.
