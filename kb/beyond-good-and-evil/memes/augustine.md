## Overview

Augustine of Hippo (354–430 CE) was a Roman North African theologian and philosopher whose writings — most notably *Confessions* and *The City of God* — shaped the development of Western Christianity and philosophy. In Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*, Augustine appears as a representative case in Nietzsche's psychological typology of religious belief: he is analyzed as an exemplar of the slavish, un-noble character that Nietzsche associates with Christian piety and the "weak" type of man in ages of cultural mixing and dissolution.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche references Augustine twice in *Beyond Good and Evil*, both times in the context of psychological diagnosis rather than theological engagement.

In §50, Nietzsche catalogues various forms of "the passion for God," ranging from Luther's churlish Protestantism to feminine mysticism. Augustine is placed in the category of "Oriental exaltation" characteristic of an elevated slave:

> There is an Oriental exaltation of the mind in it, like that of an undeservedly favoured or elevated slave, as in the case of St. Augustine, for instance, who lacks in an offensive manner, all nobility in bearing and desires. [003-007]

The judgment is severe: Augustine's religious passion is interpreted not as authentic spiritual experience but as the psychological state of a slave who has been given unearned favor and who consequently lacks the natural nobility of the aristocratic spirit. Nietzsche associates this with the broader "Oriental" character of Christianity — its roots in a slave revolt in morals, as developed more fully in *On the Genealogy of Morals*.

In §200, Nietzsche deploys Augustine within a broader typology of human character in ages of "dissolution" — periods where racial and cultural mixing produce contradictory instincts within individuals. Augustine is cited as the archetypal "weak" man who seeks happiness as repose:

> ...it is above all things the happiness of repose, of undisturbedness, of repletion, of final unity — it is the "Sabbath of Sabbaths," to use the expression of the holy rhetorician, St. Augustine, who was himself such a man. [005-015]

Nietzsche borrows Augustine's phrase "Sabbath of Sabbaths" to characterize the ultimate desire of the weak type: not the strife and self-overcoming of the strong, but the cessation of inner conflict. The "holy rhetorician" epithet is double-edged — it acknowledges Augustine's eloquence while implying that his theology is ultimately a form of persuasive self-deception.

## Key Ideas

Augustine serves two diagnostic functions in Nietzsche's analysis:

- **The religious type as slave psychology**: Augustine's passionate devotion to God is unmasked as the exaltation of a servile nature — one who, having been elevated without deserving it, cannot bear the posture of nobility. This aligns with Nietzsche's broader critique of Christian morality as a *ressentiment*-driven inversion of aristocratic values.
- **The weak man in ages of dissolution**: In ages of cultural and racial mixing (which Nietzsche both diagnoses and embodies as a modern European), individuals inherit contradictory instincts. The weak type — Augustine being the classic case — seeks to end this internal warfare through religious consolation: the peace of God as the ultimate narcotic.

Nietzsche's Augustine is not the theologian ofgrace and free will, nor the author of *The City of God*, but a psychological specimen: a man whose religious genius is inseparable from a fundamental lack of nobility.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[martin-luther|Martin Luther]]**: In §50, Luther's "churlish, honest-hearted" Protestant passion is compared with Augustine's Oriental exaltation — both lack the southern *delicatezza* that Nietzsche associates with aristocratic religious sensibility.
- **[[epicurus|Epicurus]]** and Epicureanism: The Epicurean ideal of tranquility (*ataraxia*) is paralleled with the Christian-Augustinian longing for the "Sabbath of Sabbaths" — both are philosophies of repose for those who cannot bear the tension of contradictory instincts.
- **[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]]**: Augustine's Christianity is the theological elaboration of the slave revolt that Nietzsche traces back to Jesus and the early Christian community.
