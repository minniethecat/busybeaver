**Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778) is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], one of the defining figures of [[modernity]] — and one of its most dangerous. Where the Enlightenment celebrated reason, Rousseau celebrated **feeling**; where [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] saw a state of nature requiring sovereign mastery, Rousseau saw natural goodness corrupted by civilization. Nietzsche regards Rousseau's philosophy as a sophisticated expression of [[ressentiment]] — the cry of the weak against the strong, disguised as a doctrine of natural virtue.

## Rousseau in BGE

Nietzsche's single direct reference to Rousseau in *Beyond Good and Evil* places him within a kind of fever-dream of modern Europe:

> The whole of Europe... bathed when it dreamed with Rousseau, when it danced round the Tree of Liberty... (008-006)

This is not neutral description. Nietzsche associates Rousseau with the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], the [[democracy|democratic]] movement, and the worship of "Nature" as a moral authority — all of which Nietzsche regards as symptoms of the [[slave-morality|slave revolt in morality]] entering its political phase.

## The "Return to Nature"

Rousseau's call for a **return to nature** is, for Nietzsche, the opposite of what it claims to be. The genuine return to nature — which Nietzsche associates with [[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]] — is a return to power, mastery, and the *innocence* of the predator. Rousseau's version is a return to weakness, sentiment, and the [[herd]]:

| Rousseau's Nature | Nietzsche's Nature |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Innocent, peaceful, egalitarian | Amoral, expansive, hierarchical |
| Corrupted by civilization | The source of the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]] |
| A standard for moral critique | Beyond good and evil |
