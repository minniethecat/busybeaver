# The Prince

## Overview

Machiavelli's The Prince (written 1513, published 1532) is the Renaissance text that most clearly anticipates Nietzsche's critique of moralized politics. Machiavelli's willingness to describe power as it is — rather than as moralists wish it to be — his recognition that effective rule sometimes requires cruelty, deception, and the violation of conventional morality — makes him, in Nietzsche's eyes, a forerunner of the 'immoralist' perspective.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Machiavelli is referenced in aphorism 28 of Beyond Good and Evil, where Nietzsche praises the 'tempo of Machiavelli's *Principe*' and its 'dangerous thoughts' — the kind of philosophical writing that is not for everyone but for those strong enough to face uncomfortable truths. Machiavelli represents philosophy as the art of the dangerous truth.

## Summary

The Prince is a manual for rulers, advising them on how to acquire and maintain power. Its notorious doctrines — that it is better to be feared than loved, that a prince must learn to be both fox and lion, that cruelty can be well-used or abused — are presented not as deviations from morality but as the actual logic of political success. Machiavelli does not celebrate evil; he describes the conditions of effective rule without moral illusion.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's admiration for The Prince is genuine but qualified. Machiavelli's realism about power is a necessary corrective to Christian moralizing, but his conception of power is still merely political — the power to rule a state, not the power to create values. The Prince clears the ground of moral illusion; it does not, and cannot, create the new values that Nietzsche's 'philosophers of the future' will legislate.
