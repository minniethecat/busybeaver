# Ressentiment

## Overview

*Ressentiment* (from the French *ressentir*, "to feel again, to re-sent") is one of Nietzsche's most important psychological concepts. It names the mechanism by which the powerless, incapable of directly avenging themselves against their oppressors, develop a *spiritual* form of revenge: they reinterpret their own weakness as virtue (humility, patience, forgiveness) and the strength of their oppressors as vice (pride, cruelty, selfishness). This "transvaluation of values" is, in Nietzsche's account, the psychological engine behind the emergence of [[slave-morality]] and, ultimately, of Christianity itself.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most explicit discussions of ressentiment appear in *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887), but *Beyond Good and Evil* lays the psychological foundation. In Part IX ("What Is Noble?"), Nietzsche anatomizes the noble and slave modes of valuation, and the structural role of what he would later call ressentiment is already clear.

The slave soul, Nietzsche argues, is characterized by a fundamentally *reactive* orientation to the world. It cannot generate values from its own fullness — it can only negate the values of the master:

> The slave's eye is not favorable to the virtues of the powerful: he is skeptical and suspicious, he has a subtle distrust of everything "good" that is honored there — he would like to persuade himself that even happiness is not genuine there. [009-004, paraphrase]

This suspicious, subtly corrosive attitude is ressentiment in action. The slave cannot admit, even to himself, that the master's power is genuinely valuable — for that would be to admit his own inferiority. So he must *reinterpret* the master's power as something hateful, something that ought not to exist. The master's self-confidence becomes "arrogance"; his cruelty becomes "evil"; his conquest becomes "oppression."

The triumph of ressentiment in Western history is, for Nietzsche, the Judeo-Christian moral tradition. The Jewish prophets — and after them, the early Christians — performed the most successful act of spiritual revenge in history: they convinced even the masters to feel guilty about their own strength. Two thousand years of Christian moral training have naturalized slave values to such an extent that we no longer recognize them as the product of ressentiment at all.
