**Hippolyte Taine** (1828–1893) was a French critic, historian, and philosopher associated with the [[positivism|positivist]] tradition. In *[[beyond-good-and-evil|Beyond Good and Evil]]*, [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] singles him out for extraordinary praise, calling him "the FIRST of living historians" (008-017). This is a rare and significant endorsement — Nietzsche was not given to praising his contemporaries, and his admiration for Taine reveals important affinities between Nietzsche's own genealogical method and Taine's historical naturalism.

## Nietzsche's Praise

> The German slovenliness of the psychologist... who at present, in the form of Taine — the FIRST of living historians — carries on a kind of scientific vivisection of the virtuous and the criminal... (008-017)

Nietzsche admires Taine precisely for what he sees as Taine's **un-German** qualities: precision, psychological acuity, and the willingness to subject moral phenomena to "scientific vivisection." Taine represents, for Nietzsche, a model of intellectual [[rigour]] that German philosophy has lost — the historian as psychologist, dissecting virtue and vice with the cold eye of the naturalist.

## Taine and the Will to Power

Taine's historical method — analyzing civilizations as products of *race*, *milieu*, and *moment* — shares with Nietzsche's [[will-to-power|Will to Power]] genealogy a commitment to explaining cultural phenomena in terms of underlying drives and conditions. Both thinkers reject transcendental explanations in favor of **naturalistic** ones, though Nietzsche would ultimately find Taine's positivism too mechanistic and insufficiently radical.
