# An Essay Concerning Human Understanding

## Overview

John Locke's *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) is the foundational text of British empiricism. Its central claim — that all ideas derive from experience, that the mind at birth is a "blank slate" (*tabula rasa*), and that knowledge is limited to what can be derived from sensation and reflection — established the framework for English-language philosophy for the next two centuries.

Nietzsche's engagement with Locke is mediated through his broader critique of the English empirical tradition. Locke represents, for Nietzsche, the "flat-headed" English philosophical type: sober, practical, commonsensical, and fundamentally uncritical of its own epistemological presuppositions. The empiricist who claims to derive all knowledge from experience has failed to ask what experience itself presupposes.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Locke appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in aphorism 252, where Nietzsche groups him with Hobbes and Hume as representatives of the "abasement" of the philosophical spirit associated with the English empirical tradition (008-013). Nietzsche does not engage Locke's arguments in detail; rather, he treats Locke as a type — the "English philosopher" whose commitment to experience conceals an unexamined metaphysics of the given.

## Summary

The *Essay* is organized in four books. Book I argues against innate ideas, contending that all ideas are derived from experience. Book II develops Locke's positive account of the origin of ideas in sensation and reflection, distinguishing simple ideas (which the mind receives passively) from complex ideas (which the mind constructs through combination, comparison, and abstraction). Book III treats language, arguing that words are signs of ideas rather than of things. Book IV investigates the nature and limits of knowledge, concluding that genuine knowledge is limited to what can be demonstrated from intuited connections among ideas.

Locke's empiricism is moderate: he acknowledges the limits of human understanding (we cannot know the real essences of things) but maintains that we can know enough for practical purposes. This modesty — the refusal to push inquiry beyond what experience can warrant — is precisely what Nietzsche regards as philosophically unsatisfactory. The philosopher's task is not to accommodate himself to the limits of experience but to question whether those limits are genuine.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's most fundamental objection to Locke's empiricism is that it takes experience as given when experience is always already an interpretation. The "simple ideas" Locke treats as the atomic building blocks of knowledge are themselves products of a long history of physiological, psychological, and linguistic construction. There is no "given" in experience — only what the active, interpreting organism has already shaped into something it can use.

Locke's "blank slate" is thus a philosophical fiction: the mind is never blank, never merely receptive, never passively registering an independent reality. It is always already active, already evaluating, already selecting and organizing according to its interests and its history. This is the Nietzschean doctrine of perspectivism in its negative form: the critique of the empiricist myth of the given.
