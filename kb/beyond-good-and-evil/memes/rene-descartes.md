# René Descartes

## Overview

René Descartes (1596–1650) is for Nietzsche the philosopher who, more than any other modern thinker, exemplifies the danger of grammar: the seduction of supposing that because every verb requires a subject, there must be a substantial 'I' behind every act of thinking. Descartes's *cogito* is not a discovery but a grammatical prejudice elevated into a first principle.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Descartes appears in aphorism 54 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where Nietzsche notes that modern philosophy has made "an ATTENTAT on the part of all philosophers on the old conception of the soul" — an attack that began with Descartes but has not yet been carried to its conclusion (003-011). Descartes started the critique of the soul but stopped halfway, retaining the 'I' as an indubitable foundation.

## Key Ideas

1. **Cogito ergo sum**: The Cartesian certainty that Nietzsche exposes as a grammatical fiction.
2. **Mind-body dualism**: Descartes's bifurcation of substance, which Nietzsche rejects as a relic of the Platonic two-world metaphysics.
3. **Methodological doubt**: Descartes's skepticism was half-hearted — he doubted everything except the value of truth itself.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Spinoza** — Spinoza began as a Cartesian but moved beyond dualism.
- **Kant** — Kant's transcendental subject is Descartes's *cogito* dressed in critical terminology.
- **Plato** — Descartes is Plato's modern heir; the *cogito* is the last form of the 'true world.'
