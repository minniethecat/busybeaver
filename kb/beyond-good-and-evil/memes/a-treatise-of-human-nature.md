# A Treatise of Human Nature

## Overview

David Hume's *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40) is the masterpiece of British empiricism and, for Nietzsche, the most philosophically respectable expression of the "English" tradition. Hume's skeptical conclusions — that causation is merely constant conjunction, that the self is a bundle of perceptions, that reason is the slave of the passions — anticipate Nietzschean themes in ways that distinguish Hume from the shallower empiricism of Locke.

Nietzsche's respect for Hume, expressed in *Beyond Good and Evil* and elsewhere, reflects his recognition that skepticism — genuine, hard-won, uncompromising skepticism — is a necessary stage in the philosophical development that culminates in the "philosophers of the future." Hume went further than any other modern philosopher in undermining the dogmatic certainties of metaphysics; he lacked only the strength to affirm what remained after the destruction.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Hume appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in aphorism 252, where Nietzsche groups him with Hobbes and Locke as representatives of the English empirical tradition (008-013). But this grouping is qualified by Nietzsche's greater respect for Hume's philosophical achievement. In aphorism 252, Nietzsche notes that it was "against Hume" that "Kant uprose and raised himself" — a recognition that Hume's skepticism was the challenge to which German Idealism was the response (008-013).

## Summary

The *Treatise* is organized in three books: (1) Of the Understanding, developing Hume's empiricist epistemology and his famous critiques of causation, substance, and personal identity; (2) Of the Passions, developing a naturalistic account of the emotions that anticipates Nietzsche's own psychology of the drives; and (3) Of Morals, arguing that moral distinctions are derived from sentiment rather than reason — a position Nietzsche radicalizes into his own genealogical critique of morality.

Hume's most famous arguments — that causation is not a necessary connection but a habitual expectation, that the self is not a substance but a "bundle of perceptions," that reason cannot motivate action without the cooperation of the passions — are all positions Nietzsche develops in his own way. Hume's skepticism is the most powerful expression of the critical impulse in modern philosophy; its limitation is that it remains critical rather than creative.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to Hume's *Treatise* is one of qualified admiration. Hume's skepticism — his demonstration that the mind's most basic operations (causal inference, the positing of a substantial self, the belief in an external world) are not justified by reason but are products of habit, instinct, and custom — is a crucial precursor to Nietzsche's own critique of the "given" in experience.

But Nietzsche goes further than Hume in two respects. First, he historicizes what Hume naturalizes: the habits and instincts that structure experience are not universal features of human nature but products of a long history of social, cultural, and physiological development. Second, he affirms what Hume merely describes: the recognition that reason is a tool of the passions is not, for Nietzsche, a cause for skepticism but an invitation to creativity. If reason does not command the passions, then the task is not to subordinate the passions to reason (as the philosophical tradition has always insisted) but to organize the passions into a hierarchy that serves life.
