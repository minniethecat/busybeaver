# Order of Rank

## Overview

The "order of rank" (*Rangordnung*) is Nietzsche's term for the hierarchical organization of human types, values, and cultures — a principle he advances against what he sees as the leveling tendencies of democracy, Christianity, and modern morality. It is not merely a political concept but a metaphysical and aesthetic one: the order of rank determines which values are higher and which are lower, which individuals command and which obey, which cultures flourish and which decay. For Nietzsche, the recognition and affirmation of rank is a sign of nobility; the denial of rank is a symptom of democratic decadence.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche develops the concept of rank most extensively in Chapters 5 ("The Natural History of Morals") and 9 ("What is Noble?"). In aphorism 194, he distinguishes between two ways in which the order of rank manifests: first, in the differing "lists of desirable things" that different types of human beings possess, and second — more profoundly — "in what they regard as actually HAVING and POSSESSING a desirable thing" (005-009). The noble type possesses differently from the base type; his mode of possession itself expresses rank.

Aphorism 263 declares: "There is an INSTINCT FOR RANK, which more than anything else is already the sign of a HIGH rank; there is a DELIGHT in the NUANCES of reverence which leads one to infer noble origin and habits" (009-007). This instinct for rank is not a crude will to dominate but a refined sensitivity to gradations of excellence. The noble soul does not assert his superiority; he *perceives* distinctions that the base soul cannot see.

In aphorism 270, Nietzsche radicalizes the concept by connecting it to suffering: "The intellectual haughtiness and loathing of every man who has suffered deeply — it almost determines the order of rank HOW deeply men can suffer" (009-014). Rank is not merely social or political but existential: those who have suffered more deeply *know more*, and this knowledge creates an unbridgeable distance between them and the unafflicted.

Nietzsche explicitly connects rank to his critique of modern egalitarianism in aphorism 202, where he laments that the "herd" and "herd-instincts" have become the measure of all value (005-017). The democratic movement represents, for Nietzsche, a systematic inversion of the order of rank: what was once base is now celebrated, and what was once noble is now suppressed.

## Nietzsche's Position

Nietzsche's affirmation of rank is not a defense of any existing political hierarchy — he is contemptuous of the Prussian aristocracy and the German state — but a call for the *creation* of new hierarchies based on spiritual and intellectual excellence rather than birth, wealth, or political power. The "philosophers of the future" will be "legislators" of new values (006-008), and their legislation will establish new orders of rank that reverse the democratic leveling of modernity.

This position is inseparable from Nietzsche's broader critique of morality. The "slave morality" that he traces to Judaism and Christianity is defined precisely by its *revaluation* of the noble order of rank — its declaration that the last shall be first and the meek shall inherit the earth. Nietzsche's project, in one sense, is to *re-reverse* this revaluation: to restore the "pathos of distance" that characterizes the noble relation to inferiors.

## Related Concepts

- **Master Morality** — The order of rank is the structural expression of master morality; it is the framework within which the noble type exercises his spontaneous self-affirmation.
- **Slave Morality** — Slave morality is defined by its *resentment* of the order of rank and its attempt to invert it.
- **Herd** — The herd is the mass of humanity that requires an order of rank to give it direction and meaning.
- **Will to Power** — The order of rank is one manifestation of the will to power: the drive to distinguish, to arrange, to command.

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's concept of the order of rank draws on classical sources — particularly Plato's tripartite soul and Aristotle's distinction between natural masters and natural slaves — but transposes them from a metaphysical to a physiological and psychological register. It also engages with the nineteenth-century debate over aristocracy and democracy sparked by the French Revolution and its aftermath, particularly in the work of Tocqueville (whom Nietzsche read) and the conservative reaction against egalitarianism. Nietzsche's innovation is to divorce the concept of rank from any defense of existing aristocracies and to project it into the future as a task for new philosopher-legislators.
