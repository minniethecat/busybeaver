# Parerga and Paralipomena

## Overview

Schopenhauer's Parerga and Paralipomena (1851) — 'Appendices and Omissions' — is the collection of essays, aphorisms, and occasional pieces that finally brought Schopenhauer the public recognition that The World as Will and Representation had failed to achieve. For the young Nietzsche, this was the accessible Schopenhauer — the Schopenhauer of the 'Aphorisms on the Wisdom of Life,' the essay 'On Women,' the dialogues on religion — and its influence on his own aphoristic style is unmistakable.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Parerga and Paralipomena does not appear by name in Beyond Good and Evil, but its spirit — the aphoristic form, the psychological penetration, the willingness to think against the consensus of the age — pervades Nietzsche's book. Nietzsche's critique of Schopenhauerian pessimism is a critique of the philosophy expressed in Parerga as much as in the magnum opus.

## Summary

The collection includes essays on the history of philosophy, university philosophy, spirit-seeing, and the metaphysics of sexual love, along with the 'Aphorisms on the Wisdom of Life' — Schopenhauer's most widely read work. The aphorisms develop Schopenhauer's pessimistic ethics in a popular form: life is suffering, happiness is the absence of pain, and the wise man cultivates detachment from the world.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to Parerga is defined by the tension between affinity and opposition. He learned the aphoristic form from Schopenhauer, admired his psychological acuity, and shared his contempt for academic philosophy. But the content of Schopenhauer's wisdom — world-denial, quietism, the cultivation of detachment — is precisely what Nietzsche's philosophy of life-affirmation opposes. Parerga is the text of Schopenhauer that Nietzsche most loved and most needed to overcome.
