# Candide

## Overview

Voltaire's *Candide, or Optimism* (1759) is the most famous work of Enlightenment satire — a devastating attack on Leibnizian theodicy, the philosophical claim that this is "the best of all possible worlds." For Nietzsche, Voltaire represents the "good European" at his finest: lucid, ironic, merciless toward pretension, and fundamentally honest about the character of existence.

Nietzsche's admiration for Voltaire is unusual among German philosophers and reflects his own Francophile cultural orientation. Voltaire's Enlightenment — skeptical, elegant, un-German — is the counterweight to the "half-Christian, half-German narrowness" that Nietzsche diagnoses in Schopenhauer and the broader German philosophical tradition.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Voltaire appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* as an exemplar of the French intellectual tradition Nietzsche admires. In aphorism 25, Nietzsche describes Helvetius as "far profounder than Voltaire, and consequently also, a good deal quieter" (002-003), but this comparative judgment does not diminish his respect for Voltaire's achievements.

In aphorism 35, he invokes Voltaire with the exclamation: "O Voltaire! O humanity! O idiocy!" (002-012), a characteristically Nietzschean expression of admiration mixed with exasperation. And in aphorism 224, he praises "the great Frenchmen of the nineteenth century" and includes Voltaire among the "good Europeans" who represent the "European spirit" against nationalism and provincialism (007-011).

## Summary

*Candide* follows its naive hero through a series of increasingly absurd disasters — war, earthquake, shipwreck, the Inquisition, enslavement — all while his tutor Pangloss insists that "all is for the best in the best of all possible worlds." The satire targets Leibniz's theodicy, the philosophical optimism that claimed to reconcile the existence of evil with the goodness of God by arguing that this world, despite appearances, is the best world God could have created.

Voltaire's conclusion is famous and deliberately anticlimactic: after all the catastrophes, Candide settles on a small farm and declares that "we must cultivate our garden." This is not a grand philosophical resolution but a practical withdrawal — a modest, honest engagement with the world as it is, rather than the world as philosophers claim it must be.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Voltaire's *Candide* anticipates Nietzsche's critique of philosophical optimism in several respects. The satire of Pangloss's theodicy — the demonstration that philosophical optimism is not a conclusion drawn from evidence but a dogma maintained in defiance of evidence — is continuous with Nietzsche's critique of all metaphysical justifications of suffering.

But Voltaire's conclusion — "cultivate our garden" — is not Nietzsche's. Voltaire counsels withdrawal, modesty, the small-scale cultivation of practical wisdom. Nietzsche counsels embrace, transformation, the large-scale creation of new values. Both reject the metaphysical consolation that suffering serves a higher purpose, but they draw opposite practical conclusions: Voltaire is a skeptic who has made his peace with the limits of human reason; Nietzsche is a prophet who demands that those limits be overcome.
