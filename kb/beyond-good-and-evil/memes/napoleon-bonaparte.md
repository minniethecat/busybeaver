# Napoleon Bonaparte

## Overview

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) is for Nietzsche not merely a historical figure but a philosophical type: the embodiment of the will to power in the political sphere, the man who imposed his commanding personality upon the chaos of post-revolutionary Europe and forged an empire through sheer force of will. Nietzsche's Napoleon is a synthesis of human and predator (*Raubmensch*), a "return to nature" in the midst of an overcivilized age — not a regression to barbarism but an ascent to a higher, more integrated form of humanity that transcends the pieties of modern morality.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Napoleon appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* as a reference point for discussions of greatness, command, and the "higher man." In aphorism 199, Nietzsche observes that "in all ages, as long as mankind has existed, there have also been human herds ... and always a great number who obey in proportion to the small number who command" (005-014). Napoleon is the modern instance of the commanding type — the exception who proves, by his existence, that greatness is still possible even in a democratic age.

Aphorism 256 develops the Napoleon theme at length. Nietzsche describes "the appearance of Napoleon" as "the last great test" of European civilization. Napoleon represented a "synthesis of the INHUMAN and the SUPERHUMAN" — not a monster but a higher type of humanity that integrates the predatory instincts civilization has suppressed. His appearance demonstrated "that the man is still possible who knows how to turn to his own advantage the revolution of conditions, the dissolution of all established authorities and customs" (009-006).

In aphorism 245, Nietzsche compares Napoleon favorably to the "modern" type of human being produced by democracy and education. "What is wanted," he writes, "are men who are master of themselves and of the situation," and Napoleon remains the exemplar of such mastery (008-004). The fact that Napoleon was ultimately defeated does not diminish his significance for Nietzsche; greatness is measured by the *quality* of the attempt, not by its outcome.

## Key Ideas

1. **Synthesis of human and predator**: Napoleon represents the integration of primal instincts with the highest cultural sophistication — "nature" raised to a higher power rather than suppressed.
2. **The commanding type**: Napoleon exemplifies the rare human who commands rather than obeys, whose will is strong enough to organize the wills of millions.
3. **Amorality of greatness**: Napoleon operated beyond good and evil, indifferent to the moral judgments of his age. His actions cannot be evaluated by herd morality.
4. **The problem of the "last man"**: Napoleon's appearance showed that the democratic leveling of humanity is not inevitable — the higher type remains possible.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Caesar** — Napoleon is Caesar's modern counterpart; both represent the synthesis of political genius and world-historical ambition.
- **Goethe** — Goethe admired Napoleon and met him in person; Nietzsche sees in both figures the "Dionysian" capacity for total self-realization.
- **Bismarck** — A contemporary German parallel, though Nietzsche's attitude toward Bismarck was more ambivalent (Bismarck was too Prussian, too nationalist).
