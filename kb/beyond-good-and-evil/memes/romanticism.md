# Romanticism

## Overview

Romanticism occupies an especially personal place in Nietzsche's thought. His early intellectual life was shaped by Romantic influences — Schopenhauer's metaphysical pessimism, Wagner's music-dramas, the cult of genius and creative individuality. *Beyond Good and Evil* marks Nietzsche's mature break with Romanticism, which he now diagnoses as a disguised form of weakness.

Romanticism's celebration of passion, creativity, and the irrational appeals to Nietzsche's own values. But he comes to see it as a "pessimism of weakness" — a longing for annihilation disguised as artistic abandon — rather than the Dionysian affirmation he advocates.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche does not treat Romanticism as a named movement in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but his critique of Wagner and his analysis of the "religious neurosis" engage Romantic themes throughout. In aphorism 256, he discusses the psychological type of the artist — "the genius" — and the danger that artistic creativity conceals a fundamental weakness of the will.

In aphorism 245, he critiques the Romantic ideal of the artist as a special, privileged type of humanity, arguing instead that "a great man" must be many things, including a philosopher and a commander: "One dare not speak of a GIFT for music alone, but of a GIFT for the plastic arts, for poetry, for philosophy" (008-004). The Romantic cult of the genius isolates one faculty at the expense of the whole.

## Core Doctrines

1. **The primacy of feeling over reason**: Romanticism elevates emotion, intuition, and imagination above rational calculation — a move Nietzsche partially endorses and partially critiques.
2. **The cult of genius**: The Romantic artist as the privileged vehicle of truth, which Nietzsche regards as a theological remnant.
3. **Nature as spiritual presence**: The Romantic vision of nature as infused with spirit or meaning, which Nietzsche's naturalism rejects.
4. **The longing for the infinite**: Romantic *Sehnsucht* — the yearning for transcendence — which Nietzsche diagnoses as a disguised death-wish.

## Key Figures

- **Novalis (Friedrich von Hardenberg)** — The quintessential Romantic poet-philosopher of longing and night.
- **Friedrich Schlegel** — The theorist of Romantic irony and the fragment.
- **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** — Not strictly a Romantic, but his *Werther* inaugurated the Romantic sensibility; his later classicism represents the overcoming of Romanticism that Nietzsche admires.
- **Richard Wagner** — The culmination of musical Romanticism and the figure through whom Nietzsche lived and overcame his own Romantic phase.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Romanticism is essentially that it mistakes weakness for depth. The Romantic's "suffering" is not the productive suffering of the creative spirit but the reactive suffering of one who cannot master life. The Romantic cult of passion disguises a fundamental incapacity for genuine self-overcoming — the Romantic does not *transform* suffering into strength but *wallows* in suffering as a sign of profundity.

This critique is inseparable from Nietzsche's break with Wagner. *Beyond Good and Evil* represents the philosophical consolidation of a personal and artistic rupture: Nietzsche has moved from Wagnerian Romanticism to the "gay science" of the free spirit, from the longing for redemption to the affirmation of existence as it is.
