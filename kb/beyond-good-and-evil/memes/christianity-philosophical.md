# Christianity as Philosophical System

## Overview

Nietzsche treats Christianity not primarily as a religion but as a moral and metaphysical system that has shaped the entire value-system of Western civilization. Its central innovation is the transvaluation of noble values: what was good (power, pride, beauty) becomes evil; what was bad (humility, weakness, suffering) becomes good. This is the slave revolt in morality.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Christianity pervades Beyond Good and Evil as the primary target of Nietzsche's genealogical critique. Aphorism 46 distinguishes early Christian faith from the skeptical tolerance of the late Roman world; aphorism 62 attacks Christianity as the religion of pity. Nietzsche's analysis culminates in the master-slave morality distinction in Chapter IX.

## Core Doctrines

1. The equality of all souls before God. 2. The inversion of the noble value-schema — the last shall be first. 3. The doctrine of sin and redemption. 4. The ascetic ideal — the denial of the body and the instincts in favor of a transcendent true world.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique is that Christianity is a religion of ressentiment — the revenge of the weak against the strong through the invention of a moral world-order. Christianity has educated the spirit through a long discipline, but at the cost of poisoning life itself. Its triumph represents the greatest catastrophe in the history of Western values.
