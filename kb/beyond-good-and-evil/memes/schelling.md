## Overview

Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling (1775–1854) was a German idealist philosopher and a central figure in the development of post-Kantian philosophy. Alongside [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] and Fichte, he was one of the principal architects of German Idealism, though his thought underwent several major transformations — from the *Naturphilosophie* of his early period to the later philosophy of mythology and revelation. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche invokes Schelling twice: once to illustrate the exuberant overreach of post-Kantian German philosophy, and once to deploy his anti-English verdict as ammunition in Nietzsche's own critique of English empiricism.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's two references to Schelling serve different rhetorical purposes — one mocking, one approving.

In §11, Nietzsche dissects Kant's claim to have discovered the "synthetic judgment a priori" and the ensuing rush among German philosophers to discover ever more "faculties." Schelling appears as the apotheosis of this competitive faculty-hunting:

> Then came the honeymoon of German philosophy. All the young theologians of the Tubingen institution went immediately into the groves — all seeking for "faculties." And what did they not find — in that innocent, rich, and still youthful period of the German spirit... Above all a faculty for the "transcendental"; Schelling christened it, intellectual intuition, and thereby gratified the most earnest longings of the naturally pious-inclined Germans. [001-012]

The tone is ironic. Nietzsche characterizes Schelling's "intellectual intuition" (*intellektuelle Anschauung*) not as a genuine philosophical discovery but as a wish-fulfillment fantasy that satisfied the religious yearnings of Germans unwilling to abandon transcendence after Kant's critical philosophy had demolished traditional metaphysics. The "honeymoon" metaphor frames this as a period of youthful exuberance — creative but ultimately a dream from which philosophy would need to awaken.

In §252, by contrast, Nietzsche deploys Schelling as an ally in his polemic against English philosophy. Surveying the English philosophical tradition from Bacon to Carlyle, Nietzsche declares the English "not a philosophical race" and cites Schelling's verdict on Locke:

> ...it was Locke of whom Schelling RIGHTLY said, "JE MEPRISE LOCKE"; in the struggle against the English mechanical stultification of the world, Hegel and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] (along with Goethe) were of one accord... [008-013]

The French phrase "*Je méprise Locke*" ("I despise Locke") is deployed approvingly. For Nietzsche, Schelling's contempt for Locke represents the properly German rejection of English empiricism's flattening of experience into mechanical sensation — a flattening that, Nietzsche argues, renders genuine philosophical depth impossible.

## Key Ideas

Schelling serves two distinct functions in Nietzsche's argument:

- **The excesses of German philosophy**: Schelling's "intellectual intuition" is presented as a symptom of post-Kantian philosophy's tendency to invent faculties rather than confront problems. This is part of Nietzsche's broader critique of German philosophy's verbal mystification — what he elsewhere calls the "comical *niaiserie allemande*."
- **Anti-English polemic**: Schelling's verdict on Locke is enlisted in Nietzsche's effort to draw a sharp line between German philosophical depth and English philosophical superficiality — a rhetorical move that Nietzsche himself elsewhere complicates with his own critiques of German philosophy.

The ambivalence is characteristic: Nietzsche mocks Schelling's idealism while appropriating his anti-empiricist rhetoric.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[immanuel-kant|Immanuel Kant]]**: Schelling's philosophy is a direct response to Kant's critical philosophy, attempting to restore what Kant had demolished.
- **[[g-w-f-hegel|Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel]]**: Schelling's contemporary and rival; both emerged from the Tübingen seminary and the post-Kantian ferment.
- **Johann Gottlieb Fichte**: Along with Schelling and Hegel, one of the three principal German Idealists.
- **[[arthur-schopenhauer|Arthur Schopenhauer]]**: Cited alongside Schelling in §252 as part of the German philosophical resistance to English empiricism.
- **John Locke**: The target of Schelling's contempt — Nietzsche cites this approval but does not himself endorse Schelling's positive philosophy.
