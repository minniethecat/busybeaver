## Overview

Honoré de Balzac (1799–1850) was a French novelist and playwright, best known for his monumental series of interlinked novels and stories collectively titled *La Comédie Humaine*, which offers a panoramic portrait of French society in the first half of the nineteenth century. A prodigious worker who produced over ninety novels and stories, Balzac was renowned for his obsessive work habits and his ambition to capture the totality of human life in fiction. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche references Balzac twice — once to borrow a French phrase for his own polemical purpose, and once as a prime specimen of the nineteenth-century "higher man" driven by insatiable creative energy.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's references to Balzac are brief but strategically placed.

In §204, Nietzsche opens a polemic against the modern tendency of science to claim authority over philosophy — a democratic leveling he sees as an improper alteration of rank. He prefaces his protest with a self-conscious gesture borrowed from Balzac:

> At the risk that moralizing may also reveal itself here as that which it has always been — namely, resolutely MONTRER SES PLAIES, according to Balzac — I would venture to protest against an improper and injurious alteration of rank... [006-001]

The French phrase "*montrer ses plaies*" ("to show one's wounds") is used to frame moralizing as a form of self-exposure — the moralist lays bare society's wounds rather than concealing them. Nietzsche appropriates Balzac's realist ethos: the novelist who exposes the sores of French society provides the model for the philosopher who diagnoses the pathologies of modern culture.

In §256, Nietzsche offers a panoramic portrait of the great nineteenth-century "higher men" — figures who transcended national boundaries and pointed toward a unified European culture. Balzac appears among the most eminent:

> ...as men, Tantaluses of the will, plebeian parvenus, who knew themselves to be incapable of a noble TEMPO or of a LENTO in life and action — think of Balzac, for instance,—unrestrained workers, almost destroying themselves by work; antinomians and rebels in manners, ambitious and insatiable, without equilibrium and enjoyment... [008-019]

Nietzsche's portrait is vivid and ambivalent. Balzac is characterized as a "Tantalus of the will" — reaching endlessly for what he cannot grasp — and an "unrestrained worker, almost destroying himself by work." He lacks the noble *tempo* and *lento* that Nietzsche associates with aristocratic self-mastery, yet he belongs to the "boldly daring, splendidly overbearing, high-flying" class of men who first taught the nineteenth century the concept of the "higher man." Balzac is not an ideal for Nietzsche — he lacks the classical composure Nietzsche admires — but he exemplifies the volcanic creative energy that characterizes the modern European spirit at its most vital.

## Key Ideas

Balzac's significance in Nietzsche's thought is as a **cultural type**:

- **The artist as plebeian parvenu**: Balzac embodies the modern artist who rises through sheer productive energy rather than inherited cultivation — admirable in output, yet lacking the noble *tempo* of classical taste.
- **The European over the national**: Nietzsche groups Balzac with [[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]], [[johann-wolfgang-von-goethe|Goethe]], Beethoven, [[stendhal|Stendhal]], [[heinrich-heine|Heine]], and [[arthur-schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] as figures whose creative labor transcended national boundaries and prepared the way for a unified European culture.
- **Work as self-destruction**: Balzac's legendary work habits — writing through the night, fueled by coffee, producing at an unsustainable pace — exemplify a distinctively modern form of self-overcoming that is also self-annihilation.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[stendhal|Stendhal]]**: Balzac's contemporary and rival in French literature; Nietzsche groups them together in §256 as part of the European "higher men."
- **[[heinrich-heine|Heinrich Heine]]**: Another figure in the §256 constellation of European cultural synthesis.
- **[[richard-wagner|Richard Wagner]]**: The central figure of §256; Nietzsche's portrait of Balzac as an "unrestrained worker" parallels his characterization of Wagner.
- **[[napoleon-bonaparte|Napoleon]]**: The archetypal "higher man" for Nietzsche; Balzac is mentioned alongside him as a different manifestation of the same type in the artistic sphere.

## Further Reading
