# Jeremy Bentham

## Overview

Jeremy Bentham (15 February 1748 – 6 June 1832) was an English philosopher, jurist, and social reformer regarded as the founder of modern utilitarianism. His principle of utility — that the right action is that which produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number — became the foundation of the radical philosophical and political movement known as utilitarianism. Bentham's influence extended through his student and successor [[john-stuart-mill]] to shape nineteenth-century British moral philosophy, legal reform, and political economy. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche never names Bentham directly, but he subjects the entire utilitarian tradition — of which Bentham is the architect — to withering critique.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Although Bentham himself is never mentioned by name in *Beyond Good and Evil*, his philosophical shadow falls across the book. Nietzsche's critique of utilitarianism in Part V ("Natural History of Morals") and Part VIII ("Peoples and Fatherlands") is directed at the tradition Bentham founded. Nietzsche's most direct characterization of the English utilitarian school appears in a passage on English philosophy:

> …the indefatigable, inevitable English utilitarians: how ponderously and respectably they stalk along in the footsteps of Bentham… [007-015]

This image of utilitarians marching "ponderously and respectably" in Bentham's footsteps captures Nietzsche's core objection: utilitarianism mistakes a narrow, bourgeois, English sensibility for a universal moral law. Bentham's calculus of pleasures and pains — his attempt to render ethics into a quasi-scientific, quantitative discipline — is for Nietzsche the very model of philosophical naivety:

> As regards every system of utilitarianism, one may at once assume that it is a symptom of a certain coarseness of moral sensibility. [005-005]

And Nietzsche further charges that utilitarian thinking is fundamentally incapable of grasping the real nature of philosophical inquiry:

> …the colour-blindness of the utilitarian, who sees nothing in philosophy beyond a problem-solving machine. [006-001]

Bentham's greatest happiness principle represents, from Nietzsche's perspective, the triumph of herd morality dressed in philosophical clothing — an attempt to make the values of the many the standard for all.

## Key Ideas

**The Principle of Utility** — Bentham's foundational idea is that human action is governed by the pursuit of pleasure and avoidance of pain. Morality consists in maximizing net pleasure across all affected persons. Nietzsche's objection is not to the descriptive claim (he too sees humans as driven by impulses) but to the normative one: that aggregate happiness, rather than excellence or power, should be the measure of value.

**The Panopticon** — Bentham designed the panopticon prison, in which inmates could be constantly observed without knowing whether they were being watched. This architectural scheme encapsulates the utilitarian dream of rational social control — precisely the kind of "improvement" that Nietzsche sees as leveling and despiritualizing.

**Legal and Social Reform** — Bentham advocated for democratic reform, animal rights, the decriminalization of homosexuality, and the abolition of slavery. Nietzsche would have seen these causes not as moral progress but as symptoms of democratic leveling — the reduction of all values to the comfort and safety of the herd.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[john-stuart-mill]]** — Bentham's most famous successor, who refined and humanized utilitarianism while remaining fundamentally within the tradition Nietzsche critiques
- **[[friedrich-nietzsche]]** — Nietzsche never names Bentham, but his critique of utilitarianism targets the philosophical tradition Bentham established
- **[[thomas-hobbes]]** — earlier English materialist and egoist, whose psychological hedonism provides one source for utilitarian thinking

## Further Reading

- Mill, John Stuart. *Utilitarianism* (1863) — the canonical nineteenth-century statement and refinement of Bentham's doctrine
- Nietzsche, Friedrich. *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886), Part V — Nietzsche's critique of utilitarian moral philosophy
- Harrison, Ross. *Bentham* (1983) — a comprehensive study of Bentham's philosophy
