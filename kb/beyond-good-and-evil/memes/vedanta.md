The **Vedanta** (literally "the end of the Vedas") is the philosophical tradition based on the [[upanishads|Upanishads]], representing the culmination of ancient Indian thought. [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] refers to the Vedanta twice in *[[beyond-good-and-evil|Beyond Good and Evil]]*, in both cases placing it alongside [[platonism|Platonism]] as one of the great [[metaphysics|metaphysical]] systems of world history. For Nietzsche, the Vedanta represents a mode of thinking fundamentally different from — yet structurally similar to — the Western philosophical tradition.

## Comparison with Platonism

In the Preface, Nietzsche identifies the Vedanta and Platonism as parallel "errors" — both are metaphysical systems that posit a true world behind the apparent one:

> The most protracted error... for instance, the Vedanta doctrine in Asia, and Platonism in Europe. (P04-001)

This is a characteristic Nietzschean move: to identify the Vedanta and Platonism as instances of the same **ascetic** impulse — the will to deny the world of Becoming in favor of a world of Being. Both traditions, in Nietzsche's view, are expressions of a [[will-to-nothingness|will to nothingness]] that devalues the body, the senses, and the world of change.

## The Power of the Ascetic Ideal

In aphorism 61, Nietzsche returns to the Vedanta to illustrate the astonishing power that ascetic ideals have exercised over humanity:

> Let them think of the immense power... as the Vedanta philosophy. (003-011)

The Vedanta is, in this context, evidence of the prodigious **strength** of the ascetic impulse — the fact that human beings across cultures have been willing to renounce the world, the body, and desire in pursuit of a metaphysical absolute. Nietzsche does not admire this renunciation, but he takes its power seriously.

| Tradition | "True World" | "Apparent World" | Nietzsche's Diagnosis |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Vedanta | Brahman (the One) | Maya (illusion) | Ascetic ideal, will to nothingness |
| Platonism | Forms/Ideas | Sensible world | Same structure, Greek variant |
| Christianity | Heaven/God | Fallen world | Popularized Platonism |
