# Immanuel Kant

## Overview

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) represents for Nietzsche both the apex and the nadir of the German philosophical tradition: its greatest technical achievement and its deepest entanglement in the Platonic-Christian moral inheritance that Nietzsche seeks to overcome. Kant's "critical philosophy" claimed to have subjected reason to its own tribunal, but Nietzsche sees this as a sham — Kant never questioned the *value* of the moral law he claimed to have discovered, and his elaborate architectonic was a rationalization of conclusions he had reached on entirely different grounds.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most sustained engagement with Kant in *Beyond Good and Evil* appears in aphorism 11, where he targets Kant's pride in his Table of Categories. "Kant was first and foremost proud of his Table of Categories," Nietzsche writes; "with it in his hand he said: 'This is the most difficult thing that could ever be undertaken on behalf of metaphysics.' Let us only understand this 'could be'!" (001-012). Nietzsche's suspicion is that Kant's discovery of a "new faculty in man" — the synthetic a priori — was not a discovery at all but an invention, a projection of Kant's own moral needs onto the structure of reason.

In aphorism 5, Nietzsche groups Kant with the "Tartuffery" of philosophers who "raise a loud and virtuous outcry when the problem of truthfulness is even hinted at in the remotest manner" (001-005). Kant's moral philosophy, with its categorical imperative and its postulates of God, freedom, and immortality, is the prime example of a philosophy that presents itself as the servant of truth while secretly serving morality.

Aphorism 210 discusses the "philosophical workers" — figures like Kant and Hegel — who perform the necessary preparatory labor for genuine philosophy but are not themselves philosophers in Nietzsche's sense. They "fix and formulate" values they have inherited rather than creating new ones (006-008).

## Key Ideas

1. **Categorical imperative**: Kant's supreme moral principle, which Nietzsche ridicules as a disguised form of the Christian Golden Rule — morality commanded from nowhere, for no one, to no purpose.
2. **Thing-in-itself**: The noumenal realm posited by Kant as unknowable, which Nietzsche rejects as a conceptual ghost — the last refuge of the Platonic "true world."
3. **Synthetic a priori**: Kant's supposedly revolutionary discovery, which Nietzsche treats as a philosophical sleight-of-hand.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Plato** — Kant is Plato's modern heir; the noumenal/phenomenal distinction is the Platonic dualism in epistemological form.
- **Schopenhauer** — Schopenhauer built on Kant while simplifying his elaborate structure; Nietzsche's critique of both is related.
- **Hume** — "It was AGAINST Hume that Kant uprose and raised himself" (008-013); Kant's project was a reaction to Hume's skepticism.
