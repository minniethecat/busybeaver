# Jesus of Nazareth

## Overview

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BCE – c. 30 CE) was a first-century Jewish preacher and religious leader whose teachings form the foundation of Christianity. Crucified under Pontius Pilate, Jesus's life and message — particularly the Sermon on the Mount and the commandment to love one's enemies — inaugurated a moral revolution that Nietzsche regards as the decisive event in the history of Western morality. For Nietzsche, Jesus is not mentioned by name in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but the religion that bears his name — Christianity — is the book's central antagonist.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Jesus himself is never directly named in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but his historical significance is everywhere presupposed. Nietzsche's critique of Christianity throughout the book is, at root, a critique of the moral revolution Jesus inaugurated — what Nietzsche elsewhere calls "the slave revolt in morality." In BGE, Nietzsche's attention is focused less on the historical figure of Jesus than on the psychological and cultural legacy of his teaching.

Nietzsche's most concentrated discussion of Christianity in BGE appears in Part III ("The Religious Disposition"), where he traces the psychological conditions that made Christian faith possible and attractive in the ancient world:

> Faith, such as early Christianity desired, and not infrequently achieved in the midst of a skeptical and southernly free-spirited world… [003-002]

Nietzsche argues that Christianity flourished not because it was true but because it offered a solution to a specific psychological need — what he calls the "religious neurosis." The Christian inversion of values — making the poor, the meek, and the suffering into the "blessed" — represents for Nietzsche the paradigmatic case of slave-morality (though the term itself appears most famously in *On the Genealogy of Morals* rather than BGE).

Christianity, for Nietzsche, is "Platonism for the people" [P04-001]: it takes Plato's metaphysical dualism (true world vs. apparent world) and makes it accessible to the masses, with heaven replacing the world of Forms and sin replacing ignorance. Nietzsche's philosophical project in BGE is, in large part, an attempt to undo the psychological damage wrought by two millennia of Christian moral training:

> …the enormous influence which Christianity has exercised… [001-015, paraphrase]

## Key Ideas

**The Sermon on the Mount** — Jesus's most concentrated ethical teaching (Matthew 5–7), including the Beatitudes ("Blessed are the meek…") and the command to love one's enemies. Nietzsche reads this as the founding document of slave-morality: the transvaluation of aristocratic values (strength, pride, conquest) into vices, and of servile virtues (humility, meekness, pity) into ideals.

**Agape (Selfless Love)** — The Christian ideal of agape, unconditional love for all persons including enemies, is for Nietzsche the supreme expression of ressentiment: the weak, unable to take revenge on the strong, reinterpret their impotence as virtue and call it "love."

**The Crucifixion** — Nietzsche sees the crucifixion not as a divine sacrifice but as a political execution, and the Christian interpretation of it — God sacrificing his son to redeem humanity — as a masterpiece of psychological manipulation: the ultimate guilt-trip.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[friedrich-nietzsche]]** — Nietzsche's entire philosophical project can be understood as a response to the moral revolution Jesus inaugurated
- **[[plato]]** — Nietzsche sees Christianity as "Platonism for the people" [P04-001], with Jesus popularizing the metaphysical dualism Plato originated
- **[[socrates]]** — Like Jesus, Socrates represents for Nietzsche the triumph of dialectical reason (i.e., weakness made clever) over instinct and aristocratic self-confidence
- **[[zarathustra]]** — Nietzsche's Zarathustra is conceived as the anti-Jesus: where Jesus says "blessed are the poor in spirit," Zarathustra teaches self-overcoming and will to power

## Further Reading

- *The Gospels* (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) — the primary sources for the life and teaching of Jesus
- Nietzsche, Friedrich. *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887), Essay I — Nietzsche's most sustained analysis of Christianity as slave-morality
- Nietzsche, Friedrich. *The Antichrist* (1888/1895) — Nietzsche's most direct engagement with the figure of Jesus and the Christian tradition
- Schweitzer, Albert. *The Quest of the Historical Jesus* (1906) — classic study of the historical Jesus and his relationship to subsequent Christian theology
