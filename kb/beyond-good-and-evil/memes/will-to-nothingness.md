# Will to Nothingness

## Overview

The will to nothingness (*Wille zum Nichts*) is one of Nietzsche's most radical psychological concepts. It designates the drive, characteristic of the ascetic and life-denying traditions in Western thought, to will *against* life itself — to seek the extinction of desire, the denial of the self, and ultimately the negation of existence. Nietzsche's most famous formulation appears not in *Beyond Good and Evil* but in the conclusion of *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887): "Man would rather will *nothingness* than *not will*." The will to nothingness is thus a perverse but intelligible expression of the will to power: even in willing its own annihilation, the will demonstrates its irreducible character.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Though the exact phrase "will to nothingness" does not appear in *Beyond Good and Evil*, the concept is everywhere anticipated. In Part III ("The Religious Disposition"), Nietzsche diagnoses the ascetic impulse as a form of cruelty turned inward:

> …the ascetic might wish to be more accurately informed concerning the psychology of the religious neurosis… [003-008]

The ascetic, for Nietzsche, is someone who has turned the will to power against itself. Unable to discharge their aggressive instincts outward — whether because of social constraint or psychological disposition — the ascetic redirects them inward, torturing their own body and soul in the name of "purification." This is the will to nothingness in its paradigmatic form: a will that has made itself its own enemy.

Nietzsche sees this pattern at work not only in religious asceticism but in the entire tradition of Western philosophy since Plato. The philosophical preference for the "true world" over the "apparent world," for Being over Becoming, for the eternal over the temporal — all of this is, in Nietzsche's analysis, a sublimated expression of the will to nothingness. Philosophers, no less than priests, have been life-deniers:

> …they all pose as if their real opinions had been discovered and attained through the self-evolving of a cold, pure, divinely indifferent dialectic… whereas, in fact, a prejudiced proposition, idea, or "suggestion," which is generally their heart's desire abstracted and refined, is defended by them with arguments sought out after the event. [001-005]

The philosophers' "will to truth" — their claim to pursue knowledge for its own sake — is, Nietzsche suspects, yet another mask of the ascetic ideal: a will that would rather have a certain, dead, unchanging "truth" than the uncertain, living, changing world of appearance.
