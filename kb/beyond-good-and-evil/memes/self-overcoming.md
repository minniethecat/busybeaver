# Self-Overcoming

## Overview

Self-overcoming (*Selbstüberwindung*) is arguably the central virtue of Nietzsche's ethical vision. It names the process by which an individual disciplines, redirects, and sublimates their own drives — not by repressing them (the ascetic method) but by organizing them into a higher unity under a dominant drive. Self-overcoming is thus distinguished from both self-indulgence (giving free rein to every impulse) and self-denial (trying to eliminate the impulses altogether). It is the *art* of the self: the creative, arduous labor of shaping one's own character.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche introduces the concept of self-overcoming most directly in his discussion of the will to power. He argues that the fundamental drive in all living things is not mere self-preservation but an expansive, appropriative striving:

> …a living thing seeks above all to DISCHARGE its strength — life itself is WILL TO POWER; self-preservation is only one of the indirect and most frequent RESULTS thereof. [001-013, paraphrase]

Self-overcoming is the specifically human form this striving takes. Unlike plants or animals, human beings can turn the will to power *inward* — directing it against their own inherited instincts, habits, and values. This inward turn is the origin of what Nietzsche calls "bad conscience" (the internalization of aggression), but it is also the origin of all higher culture. The same psychological mechanism that produces guilt and self-loathing can, when guided by a healthy dominant drive, produce self-mastery and creative achievement.

The philosopher, as Nietzsche understands the type, is someone who has achieved a high degree of self-overcoming:

> …the philosopher, as WE free spirits understand him — as the man of the most comprehensive responsibility, who has the conscience for the general development of mankind… [003-018]

The philosopher's "comprehensive responsibility" is not a duty imposed from outside but a self-imposed discipline — the product of self-overcoming. This is what distinguishes the genuine philosopher from the mere scholar or specialist: the philosopher has *made* themselves into the kind of being capable of bearing such responsibility.
