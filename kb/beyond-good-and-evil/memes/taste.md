# Taste

## Overview

Taste (*Geschmack*) is for Nietzsche far more than a matter of aesthetic preference: it is a fundamental mode of philosophical judgment, an instinctive capacity to discriminate higher from lower, noble from base, profound from superficial. Where the philosophical tradition has privileged reason as the faculty of judgment, Nietzsche insists that all judgment, including logical and moral judgment, ultimately rests on taste — on an immediate, pre-rational sense of what is fitting, beautiful, and worthy. "Good taste" is not a refinement added to philosophy from outside but philosophy's innermost criterion.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche appeals to taste throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* as a criterion that trumps argument. In aphorism 31, he describes "the worst of all tastes, THE TASTE FOR THE UNCONDITIONAL" as the mark of philosophical immaturity (002-008). The young soul demands absolutes — absolute truth, absolute value, absolute commitment — because it lacks the "art of NUANCE, which is the best gain of life." Maturity brings the capacity to suspend judgment, to tolerate ambiguity, to find satisfaction in what is partial, perspectival, and conditioned.

In aphorism 105, Nietzsche distinguishes between the "pia fraus" (pious fraud) and the "impia fraus" (impious fraud), arguing that the former "is still more repugnant to the taste (the 'piety') of the free spirit" than the latter (004-045). This is not a rational argument but an appeal to taste: the free spirit finds pious self-deception more *distasteful* than overt dishonesty. The moral judgment here is driven by aesthetic revulsion.

Taste also serves as Nietzsche's criterion for cultural health. In aphorism 224, he laments that "the historical sense" — the modern capacity to appreciate all cultures, all styles, all values indifferently — has produced a "semi-barbarism" of taste (007-013). The democratization of taste, like the democratization of everything else, has destroyed the capacity for discriminating judgment. To appreciate everything is to value nothing.

In aphorism 186, Nietzsche contrasts the subtlety of European moral sentiment with the coarseness of the "Science of Morals" that purports to study it, and remarks that the expression "Science of Morals" is "far too presumptuous and counter to GOOD taste" (005-001). The appeal to taste here functions as a philosophical objection: the scientific pretension to neutrality offends against the proper relation between knower and known.

## Nietzsche's Position

For Nietzsche, taste is not subjective in the modern sense — a merely personal preference with no claim on others. Taste is *cultivated* through discipline, and the taste of the noble soul has *authority* — not because it can be rationally demonstrated to be correct, but because it proceeds from a higher, more integrated organization of the instincts. The question "What does your taste say?" replaces the Kantian question "What does your reason command?" as the fundamental philosophical interrogation.

This does not mean that philosophy reduces to aesthetics. Rather, it means that the aesthetic dimension — the dimension of discrimination, of proportion, of fittingness — is internal to all judgment, including theoretical and moral judgment. The philosopher who lacks taste may still produce valid arguments, but he will never produce *great* philosophy, because he cannot distinguish what is important from what is merely correct.

## Related Concepts

- **Nuance** — Taste is the capacity for nuance; it distinguishes gradations that the crude palate cannot perceive.
- **Free Spirit** — The free spirit is distinguished by his taste — his instinctive revulsion from the common and the unconditional.
- **Order of Rank** — Taste is the faculty that perceives the order of rank; it is the organ of spiritual discrimination.
- **Instinct** — Taste is an instinct refined by cultivation, not a rational faculty.

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's elevation of taste draws on eighteenth-century aesthetics, particularly Kant's *Critique of Judgment* (the "subjective universality" of aesthetic judgment) and the British tradition of moral sense theory (Hutcheson, Hume), which grounded moral judgment in sentiment rather than reason. Nietzsche radicalizes both traditions by extending the logic of taste beyond the aesthetic and moral domains to encompass all judgment — including philosophical and scientific judgment. This anticipates twentieth-century developments in the philosophy of science (Kuhn's "paradigms," Feyerabend's "aesthetic" criteria of theory choice) and in post-structuralist thought (the critique of the fact/value distinction).
