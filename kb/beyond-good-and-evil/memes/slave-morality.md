# Slave Morality

## Overview

Slave morality (*Sklavenmoral*) is, alongside [[master-morality]], one of the two fundamental types of moral valuation in Nietzsche's ethical psychology. Unlike master morality, which originates in the self-affirmation of the noble soul, slave morality is fundamentally *reactive*: it begins by saying "no" to an external "evil" (the powerful, the noble, the master) and then defines "good" as whatever is opposed to that evil. This inversion of values — what Nietzsche calls the "slave revolt in morality" — is, in his account, the founding event of the Judeo-Christian moral tradition.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

While Nietzsche's most famous treatment of slave morality appears in *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887), *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886) lays the psychological groundwork. In Part IX ("What Is Noble?"), Nietzsche describes the slave's mode of valuation:

> According to slave-morality, therefore, the "evil" man arouses fear; according to master-morality, it is precisely the "good" man who arouses fear and seeks to arouse it, while the bad man is regarded as the despicable being. [009-004]

The crucial structural difference is that slave morality requires a hostile external world to function: it defines itself against an enemy. The slave does not say "I am good" but rather "You are evil, therefore I (who am unlike you) am good." This reactive structure makes slave morality fundamentally dependent on what it opposes — it cannot exist without an object of ressentiment.

Nietzsche identifies the historical origin of slave morality in the Jewish priesthood — a class that, lacking worldly power, developed a spiritual form of revenge against their Roman masters. Christianity, as the universalization of this Jewish moral revolution, spread slave morality across the Roman world and eventually throughout Europe:

> …the oppression of millenniums of Christianity (FOR CHRISTIANITY IS PLATONISM FOR THE "PEOPLE")… [P04-001]

What makes slave morality so powerful — and so dangerous, in Nietzsche's view — is its psychological sophistication. The slave develops an inner life of extraordinary depth precisely because he cannot act directly on his impulses. This "internalization" of the instincts gives rise to the human soul as we know it: deep, complex, self-tormenting, and capable of what Nietzsche calls "bad conscience."
