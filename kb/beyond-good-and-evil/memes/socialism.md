# Socialism

## Overview

Socialism — broadly understood as the political and economic movement advocating collective ownership and the redistribution of wealth — is for Nietzsche a symptom of the same *ressentiment* that produced Christianity. The socialist who demands economic equality is the heir of the Christian who demanded spiritual equality.

Both movements invert the "order of rank" that Nietzsche regards as the natural condition of healthy societies. Both make the suffering of the weak the standard by which the strong are judged. And both promise a future redemption — the Kingdom of God, the classless society — that justifies present sacrifice and deferred gratification.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's engagement with socialism in *Beyond Good and Evil* is indirect but unmistakable. His critique of "democratic enlightenment," of the "leveling" tendencies of modern politics, and of the "herd animal morality" that has become the default ethical framework of modern Europe are all, in part, critiques of socialism.

In aphorism 202, Nietzsche diagnoses the modern demand for equality as a form of "moral hypocrisy of the commanders" who "do not venture to describe themselves as commanders" (006-002). The socialist who claims to speak for the oppressed is exercising the will to power no less than the aristocrat — but dishonestly, disguising the will to command as a demand for justice.

In aphorism 44, Nietzsche warns that "the democratic movement is not only a degenerate form of political organization, but a degenerate, that is to say a decrescent, form of man" (003-001). This is a critique of the egalitarian premises that socialism and democracy share.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Economic equality**: The demand for the abolition of class distinctions through the redistribution of wealth — which Nietzsche regards as a disguised form of envy.
2. **Collective ownership**: The doctrine that the means of production should be owned in common — which Nietzsche sees as the subordination of the exceptional individual to the herd.
3. **Historical materialism**: The Marxist claim that economic relations determine consciousness — which Nietzsche partially accepts (consciousness is shaped by material conditions) but reverses in valuation (the point is to create higher types, not equalize conditions).
4. **The classless society**: The eschatological vision of a future without domination — which Nietzsche regards as a secularized version of the Christian heaven.

## Key Figures

- **Karl Marx** — The most influential socialist theorist, though Nietzsche read little if any Marx directly.
- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** — The French anarchist and mutualist whose slogan "Property is theft" crystallized the socialist critique.
- **Ferdinand Lassalle** — The German socialist with whom Nietzsche had some acquaintance through his sister Elisabeth.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's fundamental objection to socialism is that it is Christian morality without Christianity — the "slave revolt in morality" extended from the spiritual to the economic sphere. The socialist's "justice" is *ressentiment* rationalized; his "equality" is the demand that the strong be brought down to the level of the weak.

But Nietzsche's most penetrating critique is psychological: the socialist leader who claims to speak for the oppressed is himself exercising the will to power. He commands the herd of the oppressed no less than the aristocrat commands his subordinates. The difference is that the aristocrat acknowledges his will to power, while the socialist disguises it as a demand for justice. This is what Nietzsche means by the "dishonesty" of modern moral discourse: the refusal to acknowledge that all morality is a form of command.
