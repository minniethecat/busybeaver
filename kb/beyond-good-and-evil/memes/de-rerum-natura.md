# De Rerum Natura

## Overview

Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura* (On the Nature of Things), composed in the first century BCE, is the most complete surviving exposition of Epicurean philosophy and, for Nietzsche, a model of how philosophical ideas can be rendered with poetic power. Its central argument — that the universe consists of atoms and void, that the soul is mortal, that the gods are indifferent, and that the fear of death is the source of human misery — articulates a naturalistic, anti-teleological worldview that Nietzsche regards as a precursor to his own.

Nietzsche's admiration for Lucretius is part of his broader sympathy for Epicureanism. Where Platonism projects value onto a transcendent realm, Lucretius, following Epicurus, roots value in the material world — in the senses, in pleasure, in the contemplation of a cosmos without providence or purpose.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Lucretius is not named in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but his presence is felt throughout Nietzsche's engagement with Epicurean themes. The "Epicurean god" Nietzsche invokes in aphorism 61 — the indifferent, self-sufficient deity who contemplates the world without intervening — is drawn from Lucretius's description of the gods in Book II of *De Rerum Natura*: beings who dwell in the *intermundia*, the spaces between worlds, "their peace undisturbed, their life serene" (003-019).

Nietzsche's materialism — his insistence that the soul is a complex of drives, that consciousness is a surface phenomenon, that there is no transcendent self — echoes Lucretius's arguments for the mortality of the soul and the material basis of mind.

## Summary

*De Rerum Natura* is a didactic poem in six books. Books I–II establish the physical principles of Epicurean atomism: nothing comes from nothing, nothing returns to nothing, the universe consists of atoms and void, and the atoms are in eternal motion, combining and separating according to natural laws without divine intervention. Books III–IV argue for the mortality of the soul and the reliability of sensation, attacking the fear of death as the source of religious superstition. Books V–VI apply the atomic theory to the history of the cosmos, the development of civilization, and the explanation of natural phenomena without recourse to divine causes.

The poem's central therapeutic purpose is to liberate the reader from the fear of death and the gods. Lucretius's Epicurus is a hero who "traversed the flaming walls of the world" and returned with the truth: the gods do not punish, death is nothing to us, and the only life is this one, here and now.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to *De Rerum Natura* is one of deep sympathy. Lucretius's materialism, his anti-teleologism, his critique of religion as born of fear, his insistence on the mortality of the soul, his vision of a cosmos without providence — all of these anticipate central Nietzschean themes.

Where Nietzsche departs from Lucretius is in his evaluation of what follows from these truths. Lucretius, like Epicurus, prescribes tranquility — the withdrawal from political life, the cultivation of modest pleasures, the contemplation of the eternal atomic dance. Nietzsche prescribes self-overcoming, creative striving, the embrace of suffering as a condition of growth. Both reject transcendent consolations, but they draw opposite practical conclusions: Lucretius counsels a garden, Nietzsche counsels a mountain.
