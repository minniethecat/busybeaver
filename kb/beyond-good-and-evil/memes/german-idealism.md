# German Idealism

## Overview

German Idealism is the philosophical movement that dominated German philosophy from Kant's critical philosophy through Hegel's system, roughly 1780–1830. For Nietzsche, German Idealism represents both the apex and the exhaustion of the Western metaphysical tradition.

Its great achievement was to recognize the active, constructive role of the subject in constituting experience. Its great failure was to use this insight to reconstruct — at a higher level of abstraction — the very theological content it had supposedly criticized. The "Absolute Spirit" of Hegel is merely the Christian God translated into the language of philosophy.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's engagement with German Idealism in *Beyond Good and Evil* is concentrated in his critiques of Kant and Hegel. In aphorism 11, he challenges Kant's "faculty of synthetic judgments a priori," asking: "How are synthetic judgments a priori POSSIBLE?" and answering: "By virtue of a faculty" — a circular explanation that Nietzsche dismisses as tautological (001-011).

In aphorism 210, Nietzsche characterizes the idealist tradition as having failed the task of genuine philosophy: "The real PHILOSOPHERS, however, are COMMANDERS and LAW-GIVERS" who "reach for the future with creative hand" (006-004). German Idealism, for all its conceptual sophistication, did not command and legislate — it systematized and justified what already existed.

In aphorism 252, Nietzsche traces "the stultification of the world" to the influence of Hegel and his systematization (008-013), associating German Idealism with the broader phenomenon of German cultural dominance that he regards as intellectually stultifying.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Transcendental idealism**: Kant's doctrine that the mind actively constitutes the objects of experience through innate categories and forms of intuition.
2. **Absolute Idealism**: Hegel's doctrine that reality is ultimately Mind or Spirit (*Geist*) realizing itself through history.
3. **The dialectic**: Hegel's method of thesis-antithesis-synthesis, which Nietzsche regards as a disguised form of Christian providential history.
4. **The primacy of the subject**: The idealist insistence on the active role of consciousness in constituting reality — a partial truth that Nietzsche radicalizes into perspectivism.

## Key Figures

- **Immanuel Kant** — The founder of German Idealism; his "Copernican revolution" made the mind the legislator of nature.
- **Johann Gottlieb Fichte** — Developed Kant's philosophy into a systematic idealism of the absolute ego.
- **Friedrich Schelling** — The philosopher of nature and identity whose organicism influenced the Romantics.
- **G.W.F. Hegel** — The systematizer of Absolute Idealism; Nietzsche's principal target among the German Idealists.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's fundamental objection to German Idealism is that it is theology in disguise. Kant's limitation of knowledge to "make room for faith" — the famous claim of the first Critique's Preface — betrays the apologetic function of the entire system. The noumenal realm, the categorical imperative, the postulates of practical reason: all are attempts to preserve Christian morality on non-Christian foundations.

Hegel's system is the culmination of this project. The dialectic, which presents itself as a method of rational inquiry, is actually a narrative of redemption: the fall into alienation, the long historical travail, and the final reconciliation in Absolute Knowledge. This is Christian eschatology rewritten as philosophy, and Nietzsche's contempt for it is inseparable from his contempt for the theological tradition it inherits.
