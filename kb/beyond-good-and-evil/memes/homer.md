## Overview

Homer is the legendary ancient Greek poet to whom the authorship of the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*, two foundational epics of Western literature, is attributed. Little is known with certainty about his life; he is traditionally depicted as a blind bard from Ionia. In Nietzsche's thought, Homer represents the artistic and cultural zenith of pre-Socratic Greece — a world governed by noble values, agonistic striving, and a life-affirming sensibility that Nietzsche contrasts with the Socratic rationalism and Christian morality that followed.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche invokes Homer four times in *Beyond Good and Evil*, each reference serving as a cultural and aesthetic benchmark.

Nietzsche uses "Homeric language" as a form of jesting reference when characterizing the Platonic Socrates. Describing how Plato transformed the historical Socrates into something unrecognizable, Nietzsche remarks:

> In jest, and in Homeric language as well, what is the Platonic Socrates, if not — [Greek words inserted here.] [005-005]

The most substantial reference occurs in §224, where Nietzsche celebrates the modern European "historical sense" — the capacity to appreciate cultures and styles across time — as an advantage over the narrow tastes of earlier ages. Homer serves as the prime example:

> For instance, we enjoy Homer once more: it is perhaps our happiest acquisition that we know how to appreciate Homer, whom men of distinguished culture (as the French of the seventeenth century, like Saint-Evremond, who reproached him for his *esprit vaste*, and even [[voltaire|Voltaire]], the last echo of the century) cannot and could not so easily appropriate — whom they scarcely permitted themselves to enjoy. [007-011]

Nietzsche draws on Homeric imagery again in §228, where he critiques the ponderous prose of English utilitarian moralists:

> Observe, for example, the indefatigable, inevitable English utilitarians: how ponderously and respectably they stalk on, stalk along (a Homeric metaphor expresses it better) in the footsteps of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]... [007-015]

Finally, in §238, Nietzsche traces the evolution of Greek attitudes toward women, using Homer's era as a historical reference point. He argues that the Greeks, as cultural heirs of Asia, became "gradually STRICTER towards woman" as their civilization matured:

> ...as the Greeks did formerly; those best heirs and scholars of Asia — who, as is well known, with their INCREASING culture and amplitude of power, from Homer to the time of Pericles, became gradually STRICTER towards woman, in short, more Oriental. [007-039]

## Key Ideas

Homer's significance in Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil* is not as a philosophical source but as a **cultural marker**. Nietzsche uses Homer to:

- **Distinguish epochs**: The Homeric age represents an early, more vital phase of Greek culture — before Socratic rationalism, before the "Oriental" turn toward stricter social forms.
- **Illustrate the historical sense**: The modern European capacity to appreciate Homer — something the aristocratic French classicists could not do — is presented as a paradoxical virtue of democratic cultural mixing.
- **Contrast with philosophical prose**: Homeric metaphor is invoked as a more vivid, more natural mode of expression than the "ponderous" moral-philosophical discourse of the English utilitarians.

For Nietzsche, Homer embodies the artistic spirit of a culture that had not yet been infected by the "will to truth" — a pre-philosophical world where poetry, not dialectics, was the highest form of human expression.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[plato|Plato]]**: Nietzsche contrasts the Homeric world with Platonic philosophy; where Homer represents noble art, Plato represents the beginning of the Socratic-rationalist "decline."
- **[[voltaire|Voltaire]]** and the French classicists: Cited as examples of "distinguished culture" that could not properly appreciate Homer due to their narrowness of taste.
- **[[jeremy-bentham|Jeremy Bentham]]** and the English utilitarians: Their prose is contrasted unfavorably with Homeric expression — the modern moralists "stalk" ponderously where Homer moved with natural vitality.
