# Germany

## Overview

Germany occupies a unique position in *Beyond Good and Evil* — it is Nietzsche's homeland and the primary object of his cultural criticism. Writing as a German addressing German readers, Nietzsche combines insider knowledge with outsider contempt, diagnosing what he sees as Germany's philosophical immaturity, musical decadence, and dangerous nationalist undercurrents.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

### Tempo and Language

Nietzsche opens his discussion of Germany with a striking observation about national character and philosophical style:

> "The TEMPO, and flight out of Germany. But how could the German language, even in the hands of a master like Schopenhauer, imitate the TEMPO of Machiavelli, who in his Principe makes us breathe the dry, fine air of Florence, and cannot help presenting the most serious affairs in a boisterous allegrissimo…" [002-005]

The German language itself, Nietzsche suggests, resists the quick, light touch he admires in Romance writers. This is not merely a stylistic complaint — it reflects a deeper concern about German intellectual culture's heaviness and lack of grace.

### Wagner and German Music

Richard Wagner serves as Nietzsche's case study in German cultural pathology. Nietzsche writes of "the last great event in the history of German music (perhaps also his last, as far as Germany is concerned), namely, Richard Wagner" [003-003], acknowledging Wagner's significance while registering his distance from German musical nationalism.

### Religious and Intellectual Life

On German religious culture, Nietzsche observes that apart from the Church, Germany's "free-thinkers" of diverse kinds lack the genuine independence of mind he associates with the free spirit [003-015]. He finds German freethinking shallow — a reaction against religion rather than a genuine philosophical achievement.

### Schopenhauer's Influence

Nietzsche invokes Schopenhauer as a uniquely honest German voice whose "unintelligent rage against Hegel" severed the connection between Germans and their philosophical tradition [006-001]. This is backhanded praise — Schopenhauer's destruction was necessary but incomplete.

### The Jewish Question

In Chapter 8, Nietzsche addresses the position of Jews in Germany, arguing that Germany "has amply SUFFICIENT Jews" and that the German "stomach" has difficulty "disposing of this quantity of 'Jew'" [008-012]. He frames this not as a plea for exclusion but as a call for integration into a stronger European type.

## Nietzsche's Connection

Nietzsche's relationship to Germany was deeply ambivalent. Born in Röcken (then Prussia), he became a professor at Basel at 24, renounced his Prussian citizenship, and spent his creative life moving between Switzerland, Italy, and France. His critique of Germany in *Beyond Good and Evil* reflects this insider-outsider perspective — he knows German culture intimately but refuses to be bound by it.

## Historical Context

The Germany Nietzsche critiques was the recently unified German Reich (1871), experiencing rapid industrialization, militarization, and cultural nationalism. Bismarck's *Kulturkampf*, Wagner's Bayreuth cult, and the rise of anti-Semitic political movements form the background. Nietzsche's refusal of German nationalism — and his insistence on a European identity — was a consciously counter-cultural position.
