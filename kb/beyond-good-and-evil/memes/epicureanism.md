# Epicureanism

## Overview

Epicureanism, the philosophical school founded by Epicurus in Athens around 307 BCE, represents for Nietzsche one of the noblest alternatives to the Platonic-Christian tradition. Where Platonism projects value onto a transcendent realm, Epicureanism roots the good life in bodily pleasure and friendship.

Nietzsche's sympathy for Epicureanism reflects his own project of recovering an affirmative philosophy of immanence — a way of living "beyond good and evil" that requires no transcendent consolations.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's engagement with Epicureanism in *Beyond Good and Evil* is indirect but pervasive. In aphorism 7, he praises Epicurus's irreverent critique of Plato, calling the joke Epicurus made at the Platonists' expense "stinging" (001-007). The Epicurean refusal to take Platonic metaphysics seriously is precisely the attitude Nietzsche recommends.

In aphorism 61, Nietzsche invokes the "Epicurean god" as an image of the philosophical stance he admires: "With the decisive and impartial eye of an Epicurean god, I should think one would have to look upon the whole tragi-comedy of European morality" (003-019).

The Epicurean gods dwell in the *intermundia* — indifferent to human affairs, neither rewarding the good nor punishing the wicked. This is Nietzsche's model for the free spirit: observing human folly without being drawn into it.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Atomic materialism**: The universe consists of atoms and void; all phenomena, including thought and sensation, are material processes — a naturalism Nietzsche endorses.
2. **The nonexistence of death**: "Death is nothing to us" — since all sensation ceases with the dissolution of the body, there is no subject to experience death, and therefore nothing to fear.
3. **Pleasure as the highest good**: Not vulgar hedonism but *ataraxia* — the tranquility that comes from the absence of bodily pain and mental disturbance.
4. **The indifference of the gods**: The gods exist but take no interest in human affairs; there is no providence, no judgment, no afterlife — a vision Nietzsche shares.
5. **The Garden**: The Epicurean community of friends living apart from political life, cultivating philosophy as a practice of shared tranquility.

## Key Figures

- **Epicurus** — The founder; his *Principal Doctrines* and *Letter to Menoeceus* articulate the core of Epicurean philosophy.
- **Lucretius** — The Roman poet whose *De Rerum Natura* is the greatest literary monument of Epicureanism.
- **Philodemus** — The Epicurean philosopher whose works, recovered from Herculaneum, show Epicureanism's engagement with poetry and the arts.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's sympathy for Epicureanism is genuine but not uncritical. The Epicurean cultivation of tranquility, admirable as an alternative to the restless ambition of the Platonic and Christian traditions, risks becoming a form of quietism — a retreat from the world that Nietzsche, with his emphasis on self-overcoming and the creative will, cannot fully endorse. The Epicurean sage who has achieved *ataraxia* has, in a sense, completed the task of living; the Nietzschean free spirit is never complete, always in the process of self-overcoming.

Moreover, Epicureanism's identification of the good with pleasure — however refined — remains, from a Nietzschean perspective, a reactive valuation. It measures the worth of life by the quantity and quality of pleasure it yields, rather than affirming life in its totality, including its suffering, cruelty, and apparent meaninglessness. Nietzsche's Dionysian pessimism goes beyond Epicurean tranquility to an active embrace of existence in all its dimensions.
