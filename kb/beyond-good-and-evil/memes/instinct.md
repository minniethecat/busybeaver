# Instinct

## Overview

Instinct (*Instinkt* or *Trieb*) is a foundational concept in Nietzsche's naturalistic psychology. Against the philosophical tradition's privileging of reason, consciousness, and free will, Nietzsche insists that human behavior — including the most "spiritual" activities like philosophy, art, and religion — is driven by instincts that operate beneath the threshold of conscious deliberation. The task of the philosopher is not to transcend instinct but to understand, discipline, and redirect it.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's naturalism is announced early in *Beyond Good and Evil*. In aphorism 3, he observes that "the greater part of the conscious thinking of a philosopher is secretly influenced by his instincts, and forced into definite channels" (001-003). What a philosopher presents as a chain of rational arguments is, in reality, a post hoc justification of instinctive valuations. Philosophy is not the victory of reason over instinct but the expression of instinct in the language of reason.

In aphorism 26, Nietzsche describes how the "select man" is guided by a "still stronger instinct" toward solitude and self-protection (002-003). This instinct for privacy and distance is not antisocial but aristocratic — it is the instinct that preserves the higher type from being leveled by the herd. The instinct for rank, discussed in aphorism 263, is similarly presented as an innate sensitivity to gradations of excellence rather than a learned prejudice (009-007).

Nietzsche extends the logic of instinct to morality itself. In the Preface, he suggests that "the greater part of conscious thinking must be counted among the instinctive functions" — a claim he calls "the essence of perspectivism" (P04-001). What appears as free moral choice is, in fact, the outcome of competing instincts vying for dominance. The "will" is not a unitary faculty but a "social structure of many souls" — a hierarchy of instincts in which some command and others obey.

In aphorism 218, Nietzsche praises the French psychologists who have exposed the "betise bourgeoise" — the instinctual stupidity of the respectable middle class — and laments that German philosophy lacks comparable psychological acumen (007-005). The passage illustrates Nietzsche's method: the analysis of apparently rational or moral positions as disguised expressions of instinctual life.

## Nietzsche's Position

Nietzsche is not a "reductionist" in the sense of explaining all human phenomena in terms of a single basic drive (like Freud's libido or Schopenhauer's will). Rather, he posits a *plurality* of instincts — including "intellectual" instincts like the will to truth — that compete, combine, and hierarchize themselves within the individual. The "self" is not a substance but a polity of drives.

The ethical implication is that the goal of human development is not the elimination of instinct (as in ascetic ideals) but the *organization* of instincts into a productive hierarchy. The free spirit does not suppress his instincts but masters them, turning them toward creative ends. This is Nietzsche's version of the classical ideal of self-mastery (*enkrateia*), naturalized and psychologized.

## Related Concepts

- **Will to Power** — The will to power is the fundamental instinct that organizes all other instincts; it is the drive toward mastery, expansion, and appropriation.
- **Free Spirit** — The free spirit is one who has achieved a productive hierarchical organization of his instincts rather than being ruled by any single drive.
- **Intellectual Conscience** — This is itself an instinct — the instinct for honesty — that can come into conflict with other instincts (comfort, social approval).
- **Prejudice** — Philosophical prejudices are instinctive valuations that have been rationalized but not examined.

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's concept of instinct draws on several sources: the Romantic reaction against Enlightenment rationalism (particularly in Herder and Goethe), Schopenhauer's metaphysics of the will, and the emerging biological sciences of the nineteenth century, including Darwinian evolution. However, Nietzsche is critical of Darwin's emphasis on the "struggle for existence" and survival, which he sees as projecting bourgeois economic categories onto nature. His alternative — a psychology of instincts as expressions of the will to power — influenced Freud's drive theory, though Freud's systematic ambitions are quite un-Nietzschean. The instinct concept also prefigures the embodied cognition and evolutionary psychology of the late twentieth century.
