# The World as Will and Representation

## Overview

Arthur Schopenhauer's *The World as Will and Representation* (1818, expanded second edition 1844) was the single most important philosophical influence on the young Nietzsche. Schopenhauer's central thesis — that the world has two aspects: as Representation (the world as it appears to a perceiving subject) and as Will (the blind, striving, insatiable force that is the inner nature of all things) — provided the framework within which Nietzsche's own thought developed.

Nietzsche's relationship to this book is a trajectory from discipleship to rebellion. *Beyond Good and Evil* represents the mature Nietzsche's break with Schopenhauer: the will-to-live has been transformed into the will-to-power, pessimism has been replaced by tragic affirmation, and the Schopenhauerian ethics of compassion has been rejected as a disguised form of the ascetic ideal.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche engages Schopenhauer's central work throughout *Beyond Good and Evil*. In aphorism 19, he challenges Schopenhauer's concept of the will: "Schopenhauer has given us to understand that the will alone is really known to us, absolutely known, without addition or subtraction" — a claim Nietzsche regards as a "superstition" that takes a complex of sensations, thoughts, and affects and simplifies it into a single, unified faculty (001-023).

In aphorism 56, Nietzsche describes his project as an attempt to "go to the bottom of the question of pessimism and free it from the half-Christian, half-German narrowness and stupidity in which it has finally presented itself to this century, namely, in the form of Schopenhauer's philosophy" (003-013).

In aphorism 204, Nietzsche notes the "after-effect of Schopenhauer on the most modern Germany" as an example of the "stultification" of European culture (006-001), while acknowledging elsewhere the genuine philosophical achievement Schopenhauer's work represents.

## Summary

*The World as Will and Representation* is structured in four books, with the first and third treating the world as Representation (epistemology and aesthetics) and the second and fourth treating the world as Will (metaphysics and ethics). Schopenhauer's central argument is that the Kantian "thing-in-itself" is accessible through introspection: when we look within, we encounter ourselves as *will* — a blind, ceaseless striving that is never satisfied.

This insight is then universalized: the inner nature of all reality is Will, a metaphysical force that objectifies itself in the multiplicity of natural forms and drives. The Will is the source of all suffering, since its nature is to strive without satisfaction. The only escape is aesthetic contemplation (which temporarily stills the will) and, ultimately, the denial of the will-to-live — the ascetic renunciation that Schopenhauer identifies with Buddhism and Christianity at their purest.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to *The World as Will and Representation* is one of transformative appropriation. He accepts Schopenhauer's critique of Kant — the identification of the thing-in-itself with the will — but he revalues the will from a principle of suffering to a principle of creativity. The will is not something to be denied but something to be affirmed, cultivated, and directed toward the creation of new values.

More fundamentally, Nietzsche accepts Schopenhauer's diagnosis of existence as suffering while rejecting his evaluation of that suffering. Suffering is not an argument against life but a condition of growth, creativity, and self-overcoming. The "Dionysian pessimism" Nietzsche advocates is Schopenhauerian pessimism transformed: the recognition that existence is characterized by suffering is the starting point, not the conclusion.
