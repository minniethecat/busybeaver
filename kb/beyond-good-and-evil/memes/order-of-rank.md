**Order of Rank** (*Rangordnung*) is the [[nietzschean]] principle that all values, human types, and moralities exist in a [[hierarchy]] — that the fundamental error of [[democracy|democratic]] modernity is the denial of natural rank. For [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the capacity to perceive and respect differences of rank is the defining characteristic of the [[noble]] soul, while the [[herd]] instinct demands equality. This concept is intimately connected to the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]]: rank is determined by the quantity and quality of power a being can command and organize.

## The Pathos of Distance

The phrase "pathos of distance" (*Pathos der Distanz*) appears throughout the text as Nietzsche's term for the affective experience of rank — the feeling of elevation, of looking down and across an unbridgeable gulf, that characterizes noble self-consciousness:

> Every elevation of the type "man"... has hitherto been the work of an aristocratic society — and so will it always be — a society believing in a long scale of gradations of rank and differences of worth among men, and requiring slavery in some form or other. (009-004)

This pathos is not pride in the conventional sense but an **ontological** awareness: the noble soul experiences its superiority not as an opinion about itself but as a fact about the world. The [[slave-morality|slave]], by contrast, cannot comprehend rank except as oppression.

## Rank Among Values

In aphorism 195, Nietzsche asserts that different moralities themselves stand in relations of rank:

> The Jews — a people "born for slavery," as Tacitus and the whole ancient world says... they have known how to create for themselves a feeling of power and of eternal charm out of the very activities which were once despised. (005-010)

| Morality Type | Principle | Rank |
|--------------|-----------|------|
| [[master-morality|Master Morality]] | Self-affirmation, pathos of distance | Higher |
| [[slave-morality|Slave Morality]] | *Ressentiment*, revaluation of noble values | Lower |
| [[herd|Herd Morality]] | Utility, safety, equality | Lowest |

## Rank and the Philosopher

The philosopher, for Nietzsche, occupies the highest rank — not as a "lover of wisdom" in the traditional sense but as a **creator of values**:

> THE REAL PHILOSOPHERS, HOWEVER, ARE COMMANDERS AND LAW-GIVERS. (007-014)

In aphorism 212, Nietzsche elaborates on the rank-ordering function of the philosopher, who must survey the entire hierarchy of human types and values from a position of comprehensive superiority. This is not arrogance but a **task**: the philosopher's rank carries the responsibility of value-creation.
