# Zarathustra

## Overview

Zarathustra (also known as Zoroaster) was an ancient Persian prophet and the founder of Zoroastrianism, one of the world's earliest monotheistic religions. He is believed to have lived sometime between 1500 and 1000 BCE in what is now Iran. Zoroastrianism's central doctrine posits a cosmic struggle between truth/order (Asha) and falsehood/chaos (Druj), making Zarathustra historically the first thinker to formulate a thoroughgoing moral dualism — the very framework Nietzsche sought to overcome. Nietzsche chose Zarathustra as the central figure and mouthpiece of his philosophical novel *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883–1885).

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Zarathustra makes only one explicit appearance in *Beyond Good and Evil*, at the very end of the book (Part IX, "From the Heights," the epilogue poem). In this closing passage, he arrives as a friend and guest:

> The Guest of Guests, friend Zarathustra, came! [010-090]

This single, carefully placed reference is significant. *Beyond Good and Evil* was published in 1886, after the completion of *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883–1885). By placing Zarathustra at the conclusion of the book — as the titular "guest of guests" — Nietzsche signals that the "philosophy of the future" advanced in *Beyond Good and Evil* is continuous with the teaching of Zarathustra from his earlier work.

The historical Zarathustra's importance for Nietzsche lies precisely in his status as the originator of moral dualism. As Nietzsche explains elsewhere (*Ecce Homo*, "Why I Am a Destiny"), Zarathustra was the first to posit good and evil as metaphysical principles — and therefore must also be the first to recognize this as an error and move *beyond* good and evil. This makes Zarathustra a uniquely fitting symbol for Nietzsche's project of overcoming the moral worldview that Zarathustra himself inaugurated.

The free spirits Nietzsche addresses throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* — those "philosophers of the future" who stand beyond the opposition of good and evil — are in a sense disciples of Zarathustra, continuing his work of transvaluation.

## Key Ideas

**Moral Dualism and Its Overcoming** — Zarathustra's historical significance is as the originator of the good-evil dichotomy that has dominated Western moral thought. Nietzsche's philosophical Zarathustra represents both the culmination and the overcoming of this tradition — the one who created the distinction between good and evil is also the one who can transcend it.

**The Übermensch** — In *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Zarathustra teaches the doctrine of the Übermensch: a human being who has overcome the limitations of conventional morality and created their own values. While the term does not appear in *Beyond Good and Evil*, the "philosopher of the future" and the "free spirit" are the same archetype under different names.

**Eternal Return** — Zarathustra is also the teacher of eternal return, the idea that one should live so as to will the infinite repetition of one's life. This doctrine is the ultimate test of life-affirmation.

**Zarathustra as Anti-Prophet** — Unlike traditional prophets who claim divine authority, Nietzsche's Zarathustra speaks from his own experience and invites his listeners to go beyond him: "One repays a teacher badly if one always remains only a pupil."

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[friedrich-nietzsche]]** — Nietzsche chose Zarathustra as his philosophical mouthpiece; the relationship between author and character is complex and deliberate
- **[[plato]]** — Like Plato, Nietzsche uses a central figure (Zarathustra/Socrates) to dramatize philosophical ideas, but Zarathustra is conceived as the anti-Socrates
- **[[socrates]]** — Nietzsche contrasts Zarathustra with Socrates: Socrates represents the triumph of dialectical reason and decadence, while Zarathustra represents creative, life-affirming wisdom
- **[[thus-spoke-zarathustra]]** — The work in which Zarathustra is the central character and teacher
