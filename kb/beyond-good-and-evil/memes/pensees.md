# Pensees

## Overview

Blaise Pascal's *Pensees* (Thoughts), published posthumously in 1670, is a collection of fragments intended as an apology for the Christian religion. It is one of the most influential works of Christian philosophy and, for Nietzsche, one of the most instructive: Pascal is the Christian intellect at its most honest, its most psychologically penetrating, and therefore its most revealing.

Nietzsche's engagement with Pascal is marked by a profound, almost admiring ambivalence. Pascal is not a naive believer but a philosophical intelligence of the first rank who has chosen to subordinate reason to faith — a "sacrifice of the intellect" (*sacrifizio dell'intelletto*) that Nietzsche regards as both admirable (as an expression of the will to power) and horrifying (as an act of self-mutilation).

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Pascal appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* as an exemplar of the Christian type in its most intellectually serious form. In aphorism 45, Nietzsche describes "the intellectual conscience of Pascal" as something the ordinary "free-thinker" cannot begin to comprehend (003-001).

In aphorism 46, he compares early Christian faith to "the faith of Pascal, which resembles in a terrible manner a continual suicide of reason" (003-002). The "suicide of reason" metaphor captures Nietzsche's double attitude: suicide is an act of extraordinary will, but it is directed against life.

In aphorism 229, Nietzsche associates Pascal with "the cruelty against oneself" that characterizes the Christian ascetic — "the intellectual cruelty, the horrible inclination toward self-torture which Pascal, for instance, represents" (007-022).

## Summary

The *Pensees* are fragmentary and incomplete — Pascal died before he could organize them into the systematic apology he intended. They are organized around several central themes: the misery of the human condition without God, the insufficiency of reason to provide foundations for belief, the necessity of the "wager" (Pascal's famous argument that it is rational to bet on God's existence), and the superiority of the "reasons of the heart" over the reasons of reason.

Pascal's anthropology is Augustinian: human beings are fallen creatures, caught between grandeur (the capacity for thought, the aspiration to the infinite) and misery (finitude, mortality, the corruption of the will). Only divine grace, mediated through Christ, can resolve this contradiction. The *Pensees* are designed to bring the reader to the point of this recognition — to make the wager inescapable.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to the *Pensees* is one of horrified admiration. He recognizes in Pascal a psychological acuity that rivals his own — "the most instructive victim of Christianity," he calls him elsewhere, describing Pascal's self-mortification as "murdered, slowly, first in body, then in psychology." Pascal understood human nature better than almost any philosopher, and he used this understanding to destroy himself in the service of a faith Nietzsche regards as life-denying.

The *Pensees* are thus, for Nietzsche, a cautionary tale. Pascal's intellect — so powerful, so honest, so penetrating — was turned against life itself. The task Nietzsche sets for the philosophers of the future is to cultivate Pascal's psychological acuity without his self-destructive faith: to understand human nature as deeply as Pascal did, but to use that understanding in the service of life rather than against it.
