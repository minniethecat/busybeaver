# Darwinism

## Overview

Darwinism — the theory of evolution by natural selection articulated by Charles Darwin in *On the Origin of Species* (1859) — was one of the dominant intellectual forces of Nietzsche's age. Nietzsche absorbed Darwin's fundamental insight — that human beings are continuous with the rest of nature, products of an evolutionary process without teleology or providence — but he rejected the mechanism of natural selection.

For Nietzsche, the "struggle for existence" misrepresents the fundamental character of life. Organisms do not merely struggle to survive; they struggle to discharge their strength, to impose their form, to expand. The will to power, not the will to survive, is the fundamental drive. Darwinism, in Nietzsche's view, projects the economic categories of competitive capitalism — scarcity, competition, survival — onto nature and then claims to have discovered them there.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Darwin appears explicitly in *Beyond Good and Evil* in aphorism 253, where Nietzsche groups him with "the mediocre Englishmen — Darwin, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer" whose influence he deplores (008-014). The "mediocrity" here is not a judgment on Darwin's scientific work but on the philosophical uses to which Darwinism was being put.

In aphorism 14, Nietzsche more sympathetically groups "the Darwinists and anti-teleologists" among those who have contributed to the destruction of metaphysical illusion (001-018). Darwinism's anti-teleological dimension — its elimination of final causes from biology — is an achievement Nietzsche acknowledges, even as he criticizes its philosophical interpretation.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Evolution by natural selection**: The differential survival and reproduction of variants in response to environmental pressures — the mechanism Nietzsche replaces with the will to power.
2. **Common descent**: The doctrine that all life shares a common ancestry, which Nietzsche accepts as consistent with his naturalism.
3. **The struggle for existence**: The Malthusian premise that resources are scarce and organisms compete for survival — the doctrine Nietzsche rejects.
4. **Anti-teleology**: The elimination of final causes from biology, which Nietzsche endorses as a crucial step toward intellectual honesty.

## Key Figures

- **Charles Darwin** — The originator of the theory of evolution by natural selection.
- **Herbert Spencer** — The philosopher who coined "survival of the fittest" and developed an evolutionary philosophy that Nietzsche regarded as a caricature of Darwinism.
- **Thomas Henry Huxley** — "Darwin's bulldog," the public defender of evolutionary theory.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Darwinism has two dimensions. First, he argues that the "struggle for existence" is overstated: nature is characterized by abundance, not scarcity, and organisms typically have more energy than they need for mere survival. The fundamental drive is not to preserve life but to expend it, to risk it, to transform it.

Second, Nietzsche argues that Darwinism is philosophically naive in its interpretation of the struggle it describes. The Darwinians observe that organisms compete and conclude that the goal of the competition is survival. Nietzsche observes the same competition and concludes that survival is merely the condition of the real goal: the discharge of strength, the imposition of form, the exercise of power. The "fittest" in the Darwinian sense are not those who survive but those who dominate — and domination requires more than mere survival.
