# Blaise Pascal

## Overview

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) holds a unique and tragic place in Nietzsche's thought: he is the supreme example of intellectual greatness brought low by religious faith. Nietzsche admired Pascal's psychological acuity — his capacity to see into the depths of the human soul — while regarding his Christianity as a catastrophe, a "slow suicide of reason." Pascal is the proof that even the most penetrating intellect can be seduced by the ascetic ideal.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Pascal appears in aphorism 229 of *Beyond Good and Evil* in one of Nietzsche's most striking passages: "the old religious self-butchery" practiced by "Pascal, the most instructive victim of Christianity — slowly murdered, first corporeally, then psychologically — the whole logic of this most horrible form of inhuman cruelty" (007-022). Nietzsche presents Pascal not as a willing ascetic but as a *victim* — a mind of the first rank whose brilliance was turned against itself by the logic of Christian self-denial.

Nietzsche returns to Pascal aphorism 45, where he describes the "born psychologist" who must say despairingly to himself: "A single individual! alas, only a single individual!" (003-001). The implicit model here is Pascal — the solitary genius whose depth isolated him from the consolations of collective life.

## Key Ideas

1. **Pascal's Wager**: The argument that belief in God is rationally justified by the infinite reward it promises — which Nietzsche treats as a symptom of the corruption of reason by the will to believe.
2. **The "reasons of the heart"**: Pascal's famous distinction between *esprit de géométrie* and *esprit de finesse*, which Nietzsche appropriates in his own privileging of instinct over reason.
3. **The misery of man without God**: Pascal's portrait of human existence as wretched without divine grace, which Nietzsche inverts into an affirmation of human finitude.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Montaigne** — Pascal engaged deeply with Montaigne's skepticism; Nietzsche admired both but distinguished Pascal's Christian resolution from Montaigne's pagan acceptance.
- **Schopenhauer** — Pascal's pessimism anticipates Schopenhauer's; both see suffering as the fundamental character of existence.
- **Kierkegaard** — A parallel figure (though Nietzsche didn't read him): the passionate Christian thinker whose faith is a scandal to reason.
