# Skepticism (School)

## Overview

Skepticism as a philosophical school originated with Pyrrho of Elis (c. 360–270 BCE) and was systematized in the writings of Sextus Empiricus. Its central claim — that knowledge is impossible and that the wise person suspends judgment (*epoche*) — made it a perennial challenge to dogmatic philosophy from antiquity to modernity. Nietzsche engages skepticism ambivalently: he admires its critical power but regards modern skepticism as a symptom of exhaustion rather than strength.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most sustained treatment of skepticism as a historical phenomenon appears in aphorisms 208–210 of Part Six. He distinguishes the ancient skeptical tradition from its modern, pathological form. The ancient skeptics, he suggests, emerged from a world of "the extraordinarily abrupt mixing of races and classes" (006-007) — a condition of cultural complexity that produced a vigorous and hard-won doubt. Modern skepticism, by contrast, is a "paralysis of the will" — the inability to commit, decide, or create.

In aphorism 46, Nietzsche contrasts the "skeptical and southernly free-spirited world" of late antiquity with the "sincere, austere slave-faith" of Christianity (003-002). Here skepticism is associated with intellectual sophistication, tolerance, and the capacity for ironic detachment — virtues Nietzsche values.

In aphorism 54, Nietzsche notes that modern philosophy, "as epistemological skepticism, is secretly or openly ANTI-Christian" (003-011) — a recognition that skepticism, even in its modern form, serves a critical function against dogmatic religion.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Suspension of judgment** (*epoche*): The skeptic neither affirms nor denies any proposition, achieving tranquility through the refusal of commitment.
2. **The equipollence of arguments** (*isostheneia*): For every argument in favor of a position, an equally strong argument against it can be found.
3. **Appearances as the criterion of action**: The skeptic lives by appearances and social convention without believing in their truth.
4. **Skepticism as a way of life**: Ancient skepticism was not merely a theoretical position but a practice of living that aimed at *ataraxia* — the tranquility that comes from abandoning the search for certainty.

## Key Figures

- **Pyrrho of Elis** — The founder of ancient skepticism; his life exemplified the skeptical ideal of imperturbable tranquility.
- **Sextus Empiricus** — The systematizer of Pyrrhonian skepticism; his *Outlines of Pyrrhonism* is the primary surviving text.
- **Montaigne** — The Renaissance essayist whose skeptical humanism Nietzsche admired.
- **David Hume** — The modern philosopher whose skeptical naturalism Nietzsche respected as a partial anticipation of his own position.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's primary objection to skepticism is that it cannot sustain itself. The skeptic who refuses all affirmations is still making an affirmation: the affirmation that suspension of judgment is superior to commitment. Skepticism is thus a performative contradiction — a will that wills its own paralysis.

The genuine philosopher, for Nietzsche, must pass *through* skepticism without being arrested by it. The "philosophers of the future" may be skeptics "in the last-mentioned sense" — but "something in them would only be designated thereby — and not they themselves." With equal right they might be called "critics" or "experimenters" (006-007). Skepticism is an instrument, not an identity — a tool for clearing away dogmatic rubble, after which the creative work of valuation can begin.
