**Thus Spoke Zarathustra** (*Also Sprach Zarathustra*, 1883–1885) is [[friedrich-nietzsche|Friedrich Nietzsche]]'s most personal and literarily ambitious work. Subtitled "A Book for All and None," it presents, through the voice of the Persian prophet [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]], the central doctrines of Nietzsche's mature philosophy: the [[ubermensch|Übermensch]], the [[eternal-return|eternal return]], and the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]]. While *[[beyond-good-and-evil|Beyond Good and Evil]]* (1886) provides the systematic exposition of these ideas, *Zarathustra* provides their poetic incarnation.

## Zarathustra in BGE

In the closing poem "From the Heights," Nietzsche invokes Zarathustra as a friend and guest:

> The Guest of Guests, friend Zarathustra, came! (010-090)

This single reference, placed at the very end of *Beyond Good and Evil*, serves as a bridge between the two works. *Beyond Good and Evil* is the **philosophical** counterpart to *Zarathustra*'s **poetic** proclamation. Where Zarathustra speaks in parables and songs, the Nietzsche of BGE speaks in aphorisms and arguments — but the doctrines are the same.

## The Doctrines

| Doctrine | First Appearance | BGE Development |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| [[will-to-power|Will to Power]] | *Zarathustra* II, "Of Self-Overcoming" | Aphorisms 13, 22, 36, 259 |
| [[eternal-return|Eternal Return]] | *Zarathustra* III, "Of the Vision and the Riddle" | Aphorism 56 (hinted) |
| [[ubermensch|Übermensch]] | *Zarathustra* Prologue | Aphorisms 203, 212 (as "philosophers of the future") |
| Death of God | *Zarathustra* Prologue | Aphorisms 53, 55 (the "religious neurosis" in decline) |
