# Herd

## Overview

The herd (*Herde*) is Nietzsche's metaphor for the conformist majority whose instincts, values, and institutions dominate modern democratic society. The herd animal is characterized not by malice but by mediocrity: it fears independence, resents excellence, and elevates its own timidity into a moral principle. Nietzsche's critique of the herd is not merely elitist disdain but a diagnosis of how modern morality — particularly the values of equality, compassion, and utility — functions as a mechanism for the suppression of higher human types.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most explicit statement on the herd comes in aphorism 202 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where he acknowledges the offensive character of his language while insisting on its necessity:

> We know well enough how offensive it sounds when any one plainly, and without metaphor, counts man among the animals, but it will be accounted to us almost a CRIME, that it is precisely in respect to men of "modern ideas" that we have constantly applied the terms "herd," "herd-instincts," and such like expressions. What avail is it? We cannot do otherwise. (005-017)

The "modern ideas" Nietzsche targets — democracy, equality, human rights, socialism — are, in his analysis, expressions of the herd instinct. They represent not the triumph of reason over prejudice but the political ascendancy of the most common and least distinguished human type. The herd animal "glorifies his qualities" — prudence, patience, industry, modesty, fellow-feeling — and calls them "virtues" (005-017), while condemning the qualities of the exceptional individual — independence, ambition, risk-taking, hardness — as vices.

In aphorism 199, Nietzsche provides a genealogical analysis: "Inasmuch as in all ages, as long as mankind has existed, there have also been human herds ... and always a great number who obey in proportion to the small number who command" (005-014). Obedience is not an aberration but the normal condition of the human animal. The herd instinct is the instinct to obey, to conform, to seek safety in numbers. The "commanding" type is the exception — rare, dangerous, and essential to the health of the species.

Nietzsche connects the herd to language and thought itself. "Everywhere equality before the law" (001-026) — the democratic slogan — is, he claims, a reflection of "the democratic instincts of the modern soul" that have corrupted even physics and philosophy. The herd thinks as it lives: in majorities, in commonplaces, in received opinions.

## Nietzsche's Position

Nietzsche does not advocate the elimination of the herd — he recognizes it as a permanent feature of human social existence. His concern is rather that the herd has become *the measure of all value*. In a healthy culture, the herd would be *used* by higher types as an instrument for the production of greatness — a "substructure" of obedient laborers supporting a "higher order of architects" on the model of the Egyptian pyramids or the medieval Church (005-017). Modern democracy, by contrast, makes the herd the master and the exceptional individual the servant.

This is not a political program — Nietzsche is not a theorist of the state — but a diagnosis and a provocation. The point is not to design institutions that enforce the proper order of rank but to cultivate individuals strong enough to resist the gravitational pull of herd values.

## Related Concepts

- **Order of Rank** — The herd is the lowest term in Nietzsche's order of rank; the problem of modernity is that the herd has inverted this hierarchy.
- **Slave Morality** — Herd morality is the contemporary expression of slave morality: the transvaluation of noble virtues into vices and herd timidity into virtue.
- **Free Spirit** — The free spirit is defined by his emancipation from herd values; he thinks against the consensus of his age.
- **Master Morality** — The noble type stands outside and above the herd; his values are self-generated, not derived from the approval of the majority.

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's concept of the herd draws on the nineteenth-century discourse of mass society and the "crowd" — from Tocqueville's analysis of democratic conformism to Le Bon's psychology of crowds. But Nietzsche gives this discourse a distinctive biological and physiological inflection: the herd is not merely a social phenomenon but a *type* of human being, characterized by a specific organization of instincts. The concept influenced the development of mass psychology (Freud's *Group Psychology and the Analysis of the Ego*), the Frankfurt School's critique of the "culture industry," and Ortega y Gasset's *The Revolt of the Masses*. However, these later appropriations often domesticate Nietzsche's more radically anti-democratic elements, which remain challenging to liberal sensibilities.
