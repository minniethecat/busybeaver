# Plato

## Overview

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) is the foundational antagonist of Nietzsche's philosophy — "the greatest philosophical enemy of the human race," as Nietzsche would later call him. It was Plato who established the metaphysical dualism between the "true world" of the Forms and the "apparent world" of sensory experience, who elevated reason over the body, who made philosophy the servant of moral ideals rather than their interrogator. *Beyond Good and Evil* is, in one of its dimensions, a sustained attack on the Platonic inheritance that Nietzsche sees as having shaped all subsequent philosophy — including, paradoxically, the anti-Platonic materialism and positivism of the modern age.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Plato is a recurring presence in *Beyond Good and Evil*, though Nietzsche's treatment is less polemical than in his later works. In the Preface, Nietzsche identifies "the worst, most durable, and most dangerous of all errors" as "a dogmatist error — namely, Plato's invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself" (P04-001). This error — the positing of a transcendent reality more real than the world of experience — is the founding gesture of the entire Western philosophical tradition.

In aphorism 2, Nietzsche implicitly targets Plato when he critiques the "fundamental faith of the metaphysicians" — "the belief in the antithesis of values" (001-002). The Platonic habit of opposing truth to error, good to evil, being to becoming, real to apparent is, for Nietzsche, a symptom of a crude and immature philosophical taste — "the worst of all tastes, THE TASTE FOR THE UNCONDITIONAL" (002-008).

Aphorism 190 offers a surprising qualification. "There is something in the morality of Plato which does not really belong to Plato," Nietzsche writes, "but which only appears in his philosophy, one might say, in spite of him: namely, Socratism, for which he himself was too noble" (005-005). Nietzsche distinguishes between Plato himself — a complex, aristocratic figure — and the Socratic rationalism that uses Plato as its vehicle. This is not a defense of Plato but a complication of the picture: Plato was greater than his doctrine.

## Key Ideas

1. **Theory of Forms**: The doctrine that true reality consists of eternal, unchanging, immaterial Forms, of which the physical world is a mere copy — the "original error" of Western metaphysics.
2. **The philosopher-king**: The ideal of the philosopher as ruler, which Nietzsche appropriates and transforms into his concept of the "philosopher of the future" as "legislator" of values.
3. **The noble lie**: Plato's concept of politically necessary falsehood, which anticipates Nietzsche's discussions of the mask and the necessity of illusion.
4. **Socratic irony**: Nietzsche sees Socratic dialectics as a form of *ressentiment* — the ugly, plebeian Socrates using reason to shame and dominate his aristocratic interlocutors.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Socrates** — Nietzsche distinguishes Plato from Socrates; Plato was "too noble" for Socratism, and Socratic rationalism represents a degeneration of the Platonic inheritance.
- **Kant** — Nietzsche sees Kant as Plato's modern heir: the "true world" becomes the noumenal realm, and the categorical imperative is a secularized Form of the Good.
- **Christianity** — Nietzsche famously calls Christianity "Platonism for 'the people'" — the Platonic two-world metaphysics democratized and moralized for mass consumption.
