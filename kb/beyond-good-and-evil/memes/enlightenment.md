# Enlightenment

## Overview

The Enlightenment (c. 1685–1815) was the intellectual movement that championed reason, individual liberty, and scientific progress against traditional authority. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche treats the Enlightenment not as a triumph of reason but as a psychological and moral phenomenon — a "revolt of the ruled" that reshaped European values by inverting the aristocratic order.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's analysis of the Enlightenment centers on its psychological origins. He frames it as fundamentally a movement of the weak against the strong:

> "'Enlightenment' causes revolt, for the slave desires to be master, and not to submit to authority… The insurrection of the ruled is that which began with the French Revolution." [003-002]

This is a radical reinterpretation. Where Enlightenment thinkers saw themselves as freeing humanity from superstition and tyranny through reason, Nietzsche sees a deeper motivational structure: *ressentiment* — the desire of the weak to overthrow the strong by redefining their weakness as virtue.

### The French Revolution as Enlightenment's Outcome

Nietzsche connects the Enlightenment directly to the French Revolution. In his discussion of how historical events are "interpreted" by later observers, he writes:

> "As happened finally in all the enlightenment of modern times with the French Revolution (that terrible farce, quite superfluous when judged close at hand, into which, however, the noble and visionary spectators of all Europe have interpreted from a distance their own indignation and enthusiasm so long and passionately, UNTIL THE TEXT HAS DISAPPEARED UNDER THE INTERPRETATION)…" [002-015]

The capital letters on "UNTIL THE TEXT HAS DISAPPEARED UNDER THE INTERPRETATION" are Nietzsche's own — a warning about how ideology overwrites reality.

### Women and Enlightenment

In a characteristically provocative passage, Nietzsche comments on the gendered dimension of the Enlightenment:

> "Enlightenment hitherto has fortunately been men's affair, men's gift — therewith we 'remain among ourselves.'" [007-025]

This remark, embedded in his broader discussion of women and philosophy, suggests that the Enlightenment's claims to universality mask its gendered partiality.

## Historical Background

The Enlightenment spanned roughly from the late 17th century through the early 19th century. Key figures included Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, Hume, and Kant — the last of whom Nietzsche engages with extensively in *Beyond Good and Evil*. The movement's central commitments included the authority of reason over tradition, individual rights, religious toleration, and scientific empiricism. Politically, it inspired both the American Revolution (1776) and the French Revolution (1789).

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

Nietzsche reads the Enlightenment through the lens of his master-slave morality thesis. The Enlightenment's professed values — equality, universal rights, faith in reason — are not neutral intellectual achievements but the psychological weapons of a rising class. "Truth" and "reason" become the new absolutes, replacing God and king, but serving the same function: a framework of authority within which the "slave" can judge and condemn the "master."

This is not a rejection of Enlightenment intellect — Nietzsche's own style owes much to the French *philosophes* he admired (Voltaire, in particular). Rather, it is a genealogical unmasking: the Enlightenment claims to transcend power relations, but Nietzsche insists it is itself an expression of power — specifically, the will to power of the ruled.
