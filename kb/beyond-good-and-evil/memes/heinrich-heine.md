# Heinrich Heine

## Overview

Christian Johann Heinrich Heine (13 December 1797 – 17 February 1856) was one of the most significant German poets and literary figures of the nineteenth century. Born in Düsseldorf to a Jewish family, Heine converted to Christianity and spent the latter part of his life in Paris, where he became a bridge figure between German and French literary cultures. His lyric poetry, collected in the *Buch der Lieder* (1827), was set to music by composers including Schubert and Schumann. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche mentions Heine twice — and both references place Heine among the highest company of European genius.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's admiration for Heine is unmistakable. In Part VIII ("Peoples and Fatherlands"), Nietzsche twice invokes Heine in lists of the greatest figures of European culture.

First, Nietzsche describes Heine as a German who became more fully himself by leaving Germany. In a passage on the cultural sterility of German nationalism, Nietzsche notes that some of Germany's greatest spirits had to flee their homeland to flourish:

> …not to speak of Heinrich Heine, who has long ago been re-incarnated in the more refined and fastidious lyrists of Paris… [008-017]

The verb "re-incarnated" is significant: Heine did not simply move to Paris — he was reborn there, transformed into a more refined version of himself. For Nietzsche, Heine represents the possibility of transcending narrow national identity to become a "good European."

The second and more remarkable mention comes a few paragraphs later. In a passage arguing that the greatest European figures belong to Europe rather than to any single nation, Nietzsche lists:

> I think of such men as Napoleon, Goethe, Beethoven, Stendhal, Heinrich Heine, Schopenhauer: it must not be taken amiss if I also count Richard Wagner among them… [008-019]

This is extraordinary company — and Nietzsche places Heine in it without hesitation. Heine, in Nietzsche's estimation, stands alongside Goethe (Germany's greatest poet), Beethoven (its greatest composer), Napoleon (the supreme man of action), and Schopenhauer (Nietzsche's early philosophical mentor). This ranking tells us a great deal about how highly Nietzsche valued Heine's art and intellect.

## Key Ideas

**Lyric Poetry and Irony** — Heine's poetry combined Romantic lyricism with sharp, self-deflating irony. This combination — deep feeling undercut by wit — anticipates Nietzsche's own philosophical style, which similarly refuses to let profundity become pomposity.

**The "Good European"** — Heine's self-imposed exile in Paris made him a model of the "good European" Nietzsche champions in BGE: someone who has overcome national prejudice and inhabits a broader cultural horizon.

**German-Jewish Identity** — Heine's complex relationship to his German and Jewish identities — he converted to Christianity but remained culturally Jewish — prefigures Nietzsche's own ambivalent relationship to German culture. Nietzsche, who famously renounced his German citizenship, saw in Heine a kindred spirit.
