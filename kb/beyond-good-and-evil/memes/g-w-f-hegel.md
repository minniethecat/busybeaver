# G. W. F. Hegel

## Overview

G. W. F. Hegel (1770–1831) is for Nietzsche a representative figure of the "philosophical worker" — the systematic thinker whose imposing conceptual architecture conceals a fundamental submission to the values of his age. Nietzsche acknowledges Hegel's historical significance while dismissing his philosophy as a disguised theodicy: the famous claim that "the real is rational" is not a discovery but a capitulation, a philosophical blessing bestowed on whatever happens to exist.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's treatment of Hegel in *Beyond Good and Evil* is brief but pointed. In aphorism 252, Hegel is grouped with Schopenhauer and Goethe in their shared opposition to "the English mechanical stultification of the world" (008-013). This alignment is significant: Nietzsche prefers Hegel's grand historical narratives to the piecemeal empiricism of Locke and Hume, even though he rejects Hegel's conclusions.

In aphorism 211, Nietzsche places Hegel among the "philosophical workers" who, "after the excellent pattern of Kant and Hegel, have to fix and formulate some large class of facts — that is, some PREVIOUSLY ESTABLISHED valuation — in formulas" (006-008). The philosophical worker systematizes inherited values; the genuine philosopher creates new ones. Hegel belongs to the former category, for all his apparent radicalism.

## Key Ideas

1. **Dialectic**: Hegel's method of thesis-antithesis-synthesis, which Nietzsche sees as a formalization of the philosophical instinct for simplification.
2. **World-historical individuals**: Hegel's concept of the "cunning of reason" — great individuals as unconscious instruments of the World-Spirit — anticipates Nietzsche's own typology of "higher men," though Nietzsche rejects Hegel's providential framework.
3. **The real is rational**: Nietzsche regards this as philosophy at its most servile — the legitimation of existing power structures dressed in conceptual language.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Kant** — Hegel completes the German idealist project that Kant began.
- **Schopenhauer** — Hegel and Schopenhauer were rivals; Schopenhauer's contempt for Hegel was notorious.
- **Goethe** — Hegel admired Goethe, and both shared a commitment to seeing nature in developmental, organic terms.
