# Pessimism (School)

## Overview

Philosophical pessimism — the conviction that existence is fundamentally characterized by suffering and that non-existence would be preferable to existence — is the position Nietzsche inherited from Arthur Schopenhauer and spent his entire career attempting to overcome. *Beyond Good and Evil* is, in one of its dimensions, the record of this overcoming.

Pessimism is not, for Nietzsche, an error to be refuted but a truth to be endured. The pessimist is right about the facts: existence is characterized by suffering, cruelty, and the apparent absence of cosmic meaning. But the pessimist's negative evaluation of these facts — the judgment that suffering makes existence *worse* than non-existence — is an evaluation, not a fact, and it is this evaluation that Nietzsche challenges.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most sustained engagement with pessimism in *Beyond Good and Evil* appears in aphorism 56, where he describes his effort to "go to the bottom of the question of pessimism and free it from the half-Christian, half-German narrowness and stupidity in which it has finally presented itself to this century, namely, in the form of Schopenhauer's philosophy" (003-013).

The problem with Schopenhauerian pessimism, Nietzsche argues, is that it remains covertly Christian: it judges the world against a standard of what it *ought* to be and finds it wanting. This is not genuine world-affirmation but disguised world-denial. The pessimist who laments that existence is suffering is still attached to the idea that it *ought* not to be — an idea that only makes sense within a theodicy that pessimism claims to have abandoned.

## Core Doctrines

1. **The predominance of suffering**: The empirical claim that pain outweighs pleasure in human existence — which Nietzsche does not dispute.
2. **The negative evaluation of existence**: The normative claim that the predominance of suffering makes existence worse than non-existence — which Nietzsche disputes.
3. **The negation of the will**: Schopenhauer's prescription that the only rational response to the truth of pessimism is the denial of the will-to-live — which Nietzsche replaces with the affirmation of the will to power.
4. **Compassion as the highest virtue**: Schopenhauer's ethics of Mitleid (compassion), which Nietzsche regards as a disguised form of the ascetic ideal.

## Key Figures

- **Arthur Schopenhauer** — The philosopher who gave pessimism its most powerful modern articulation in *The World as Will and Representation*.
- **Eduard von Hartmann** — The author of *Philosophy of the Unconscious* (1869), who attempted to reconcile Schopenhauer's pessimism with Hegel's historical optimism.
- **Giacomo Leopardi** — The Italian poet whose pessimistic vision Nietzsche admired, later declaring him one of the "four most peculiar men of the nineteenth century."

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's response to pessimism is not optimism — which he regards as equally naive — but what he calls "tragic affirmation" or "Dionysian pessimism." This is a pessimism that has passed through the recognition of suffering and meaninglessness and emerged, not broken, but capable of saying Yes to existence *as it is*.

This is not a theoretical resolution of the problem of suffering but an existential stance. One affirms life not because one has been persuaded that it is good, but because one has cultivated the strength to bear its tragic character without resentment or flight. "Beyond good and evil" is the standpoint from which the question "Is existence good?" is itself transcended — replaced by the affirmation: "Thus I willed it."
