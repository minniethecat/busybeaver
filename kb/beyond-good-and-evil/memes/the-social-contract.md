# The Social Contract

## Overview

Jean-Jacques Rousseau's *The Social Contract* (1762) is one of the most influential works of modern political philosophy. Its opening declaration — "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains" — established the framework for democratic and revolutionary politics in the centuries that followed.

For Nietzsche, Rousseau is the quintessential figure of modern *ressentiment*: the man of the people who resents the aristocracy and invents a philosophy to justify his resentment. The "general will" — Rousseau's principle of political legitimacy — is, for Nietzsche, the will of the herd elevated to the dignity of a philosophical concept. The Social Contract does not liberate humanity; it merely replaces the rule of the strong by the strong with the rule of the weak by the weak.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Rousseau appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in aphorism 245, where Nietzsche describes "the Revolution" as "the time when Europe bathed when it dreamed with Rousseau" and "danced round the Tree of Liberty" (008-006). The Rousseauian dream — that human beings are naturally good and that society corrupts them — is, for Nietzsche, a fantasy that has had catastrophic political consequences.

Nietzsche's critique of democratic "leveling" throughout *Beyond Good and Evil* is implicitly a critique of Rousseau, even where Rousseau is not named. The demand that all human beings be treated as equals, that the authority of the exceptional be subordinated to the will of the many — this is the political expression of the slave morality that Nietzsche traces from Christianity through the Enlightenment to modern democracy.

## Summary

*The Social Contract* argues that legitimate political authority can only be based on the consent of the governed. Rousseau's solution to the problem of reconciling individual freedom with collective authority is the "general will" — the will of the people as a whole, directed toward the common good. The sovereign is the people themselves, assembled as a collective body, and the laws they enact are expressions of the general will.

Rousseau distinguishes the general will from the "will of all" (the mere aggregate of private interests) and argues that the general will is always right — though the people may not always perceive it correctly. The legislator's task is to help the people discern the general will; the citizen's duty is to obey it, even when it conflicts with private interest.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's opposition to Rousseau's *Social Contract* is fundamental. Rousseau's anthropology — the doctrine of natural human goodness — inverts what Nietzsche takes to be the truth about human nature: that human beings are naturally hierarchical, naturally aggressive, and naturally inclined to domination. Rousseau's "state of nature" is a pastoral fantasy; Nietzsche's is a field of contending wills to power.

Rousseau's politics — the subordination of the exceptional to the general — is the political expression of the slave morality. The "general will" is the will of the herd, and its elevation to the principle of political legitimacy is the revenge of the many against the few. Rousseau does not liberate humanity from tyranny; he merely replaces the tyranny of the strong with the tyranny of the weak — a tyranny that, because it operates in the name of equality and justice, is more insidious and more absolute than the tyranny it replaces.
