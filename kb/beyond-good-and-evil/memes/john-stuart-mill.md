# John Stuart Mill

## Overview

John Stuart Mill (20 May 1806 – 8 May 1873) was an English philosopher, political economist, and civil servant. He is one of the most influential thinkers in the history of liberalism and the most significant nineteenth-century exponent of utilitarianism, the ethical theory developed by his father James Mill and Jeremy Bentham. Mill's major works include *On Liberty* (1859), *Utilitarianism* (1863), and *The Subjection of Women* (1869). In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche mentions Mill by name, but only to dismiss him as a representative of a shallow and mediocre English philosophical culture.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's treatment of Mill is brief but pointed. Mill appears in a passage of Part VIII ("Peoples and Fatherlands") where Nietzsche diagnoses the character of English philosophy. Nietzsche groups Mill with Darwin and Herbert Spencer as "respectable but mediocre Englishmen":

> …I may mention Darwin, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer — begins to decline in the estimation of the European intellect. [008-014]

This dismissal must be understood in the context of Nietzsche's broader critique of English philosophy. The English, Nietzsche claims, are "not a philosophical race" [008-013]. Their thinking is characterized by a plodding, utilitarian spirit that reduces all philosophical questions to practical morality. Nietzsche's hostility toward English utilitarianism pervades the book:

> As regards every system of utilitarianism, one may at once assume that it is a symptom of a certain coarseness of moral sensibility. [005-005]

And more colorfully, Nietzsche ridicules "the indefatigable, inevitable English utilitarians: how ponderously and respectably they stalk along in the footsteps of Bentham" [007-015].

Mill's specific offense, in Nietzsche's eyes, is his attempt to ground morality in the principle of utility — the greatest happiness for the greatest number — which Nietzsche sees as a leveling, democratic instinct masquerading as philosophy. For Nietzsche, this represents the triumph of herd values disguised as rational ethical theory.

## Key Ideas

**Utilitarianism** — Mill refined Bentham's utilitarianism, distinguishing between higher and lower pleasures and arguing that "it is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied." Nietzsche would have found this distinction unconvincing, seeing the entire utilitarian framework as a symptom of what he calls "the colour-blindness of the utilitarian, who sees nothing in philosophy beyond a problem-solving machine" [006-001].

**Liberty and Individualism** — Mill's *On Liberty* argues for maximum individual freedom consistent with not harming others — the "harm principle." Nietzsche would have recognized a superficial affinity with his own individualism but rejected Mill's democratic, egalitarian foundation.

**The Subjection of Women** — Mill's then-radical argument for women's equality represents the democratic progressivism Nietzsche explicitly rejects in BGE. Nietzsche's own views on women in BGE are notoriously essentialist and hierarchical; Mill stands as Mill's antithesis on this question.
