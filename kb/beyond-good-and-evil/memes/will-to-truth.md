# Will to Truth

## Overview

The Will to Truth (*Wille zur Wahrheit*) is the philosophical drive toward unconditional truthfulness that Nietzsche subjects to radical critique in the opening aphorisms of *Beyond Good and Evil*. Rather than accepting the value of truth as self-evident, Nietzsche asks a more fundamental question: *why* truth rather than untruth? What is the *value* of this will to truth, and what does it reveal about the philosopher who pursues it? This interrogation of truthfulness itself — rather than any particular truth claim — marks one of Nietzsche's most original philosophical contributions.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche opens *Beyond Good and Evil* by placing the Will to Truth under suspicion. The very first aphorism dramatizes the question:

> The Will to Truth, which is to tempt us to many a hazardous enterprise, the famous Truthfulness of which all philosophers have hitherto spoken with respect, what questions has this Will to Truth not laid before us! ... WHO is it really that puts questions to us here? WHAT really is this "Will to Truth" in us? (001-001)

The questioning escalates in aphorism 2, where Nietzsche frames the problem as one of origins: how could the Will to Truth arise from its opposite — the will to deception? The metaphysical tradition, he notes, has always assumed that "things of the highest value must have a different origin, an origin of THEIR own" (001-002). This assumption — that truth cannot emerge from error — is itself a metaphysical prejudice that Nietzsche aims to overturn.

In aphorism 10, Nietzsche cautions against hearing only a "Will to Truth" in the background of modern philosophy's epistemological debates. The "problem of 'the real and the apparent world'" (001-011) may be driven less by truth-seeking than by an unacknowledged metaphysical ambition. Later, in aphorism 211, Nietzsche distinguishes the philosopher from the mere "philosophical worker," suggesting that the genuine philosopher goes beyond the will to truth to become a creator of values (006-008).

## Nietzsche's Position

Nietzsche does not reject truth or truthfulness outright. Rather, he historicizes and psychologizes the will to truth, treating it not as a transcendental imperative but as a historically conditioned drive with its own genealogy. His famous question — "WHY NOT RATHER untruth?" (001-001) — is not an endorsement of falsehood but a demand that philosophers take responsibility for their own fundamental values.

The danger Nietzsche identifies is that the will to truth, pursued unconditionally, may turn against life itself. An unexamined truthfulness can become ascetic, world-denying — a "will to death" in disguise. The philosopher's task is not to abandon truth but to recognize that the value of truth must itself be *created*, not discovered. This is the move from epistemology to what Nietzsche calls the "philosophy of the future."

## Related Concepts

- **Free Spirit** — The free spirit is the type of thinker capable of interrogating the will to truth without falling into nihilism or despair.
- **Perspectivism** — If all knowledge is perspectival, then the will to truth can never achieve a "God's-eye view"; it must embrace the partial, situated character of all knowing.
- **Prejudice** — The will to truth, when unexamined, becomes the "prejudice of philosophers" par excellence — the assumption that truth is inherently valuable.
- **Ascetic Ideal** — Nietzsche's later genealogy (in *On the Genealogy of Morals*) traces the will to truth to the ascetic ideal — the priestly devaluation of this world in favor of a transcendent "true world."

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's critique of the will to truth emerges from his engagement with the Kantian and Schopenhauerian traditions. Kant had made the "critique of pure reason" the foundation of philosophy, but Nietzsche radicalizes this: what is needed is not merely a *critique* of reason's limits but a *genealogy* of the value we place on truth itself. Schopenhauer's pessimism provided one model for interrogating the value of existence; Nietzsche extends this interrogation to the value of truth. The concept also engages with the broader nineteenth-century development of historicism and evolutionary thinking, which made it possible to see the "will to truth" not as an eternal faculty but as something that emerged and could therefore be questioned.
