# Master Morality

## Overview

Master morality (*Herrenmoral*) is one of the two fundamental types of moral valuation identified by Nietzsche in *Beyond Good and Evil* and developed more fully in *On the Genealogy of Morals*. It is the original form of moral judgment, characteristic of aristocratic and warrior cultures, in which the ruling class creates values by affirming its own qualities — strength, courage, pride, and excellence — as "good," and by contrast designating the qualities of the ruled — weakness, timidity, humility — as "bad." Master morality is thus an affirmative, self-referential system: the noble soul says "I am good, therefore what is unlike me is bad."

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most sustained treatment of master morality in BGE appears in Part IX ("What Is Noble?"), where he anatomizes the psychology of the aristocratic soul. The master type experiences itself as the source of all value:

> The noble type of man regards HIMSELF as a determiner of values; he does not require to be approved of; he passes the judgment: "What is injurious to me is injurious in itself." [009-002, paraphrase]

For Nietzsche, master morality is characterized by a fundamental self-reverence. The noble soul does not seek external validation because it knows itself to be the source of value. This is what distinguishes it sharply from slave morality, which requires an external "evil other" against which to define itself.

The historical tragedy, in Nietzsche's account, is that master morality was overthrown by what he calls the "slave revolt in morality" — a transvaluation of values spearheaded by the Jewish priestly class and inherited by Christianity. The qualities the masters called "good" (power, pride, conquest) were relabeled "evil," and the qualities of the oppressed (humility, pity, obedience) became "good."

Nietzsche's aim is not simply to restore master morality — he is too much a psychologist for that — but to point toward a "philosophy of the future" that would synthesize the psychological depth of the slave (the capacity for introspection and self-criticism) with the life-affirming confidence of the master.
