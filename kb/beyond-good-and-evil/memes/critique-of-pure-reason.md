# Critique of Pure Reason

## Overview

Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781, second edition 1787) is arguably the most important work of modern philosophy. Its "Copernican revolution" — the claim that objects must conform to our cognition rather than cognition to objects — reshaped the entire landscape of Western thought. For Nietzsche, the *Critique* represents both the high point of the critical philosophical impulse and its betrayal: Kant used the critique of reason to preserve what reason had apparently destroyed, carving out a space for faith, freedom, and immortality that the Enlightenment had called into question.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche engages the *Critique of Pure Reason* primarily through his critique of Kant's concept of synthetic a priori judgments. In aphorism 11, he challenges the very possibility that Kant took as his starting point: "How are synthetic judgments a priori POSSIBLE?" Nietzsche answers with mockery: "By virtue of a faculty" — a tautology that explains nothing (001-011).

In aphorism 12, Nietzsche extends his critique to the "actual influence which Kant exercised on German philosophy," characterizing it as an evasion of the real problems Kant had raised. Kant "invented a new kind of dogmatism" — transcendental idealism — to replace the dogmatism he had criticized (001-012).

In aphorism 210, Nietzsche contrasts Kant with his ideal of the genuine philosopher: "the real PHILOSOPHERS, however, are COMMANDERS and LAW-GIVERS" who do not merely analyze the conditions of possible experience but create new possibilities of experience (006-004).

## Summary

The *Critique of Pure Reason* is structured around Kant's distinction between the phenomenal world (the world as it appears to us, shaped by the innate forms of intuition — space and time — and the categories of the understanding) and the noumenal world (the world as it is in itself, which we can never know). Kant's central argument is that synthetic a priori knowledge — knowledge that is both informative and necessary — is possible because the mind actively constitutes experience according to its own structures.

The *Critique* thus limits knowledge by showing that certain traditional objects of metaphysics — God, the soul, the world as a whole — are beyond the reach of theoretical reason, since they cannot be given in sensible intuition. But Kant then argues that these same objects are restored as "postulates of practical reason" — necessary presuppositions of moral action — in the *Critique of Practical Reason*. This is the move Nietzsche regards as a betrayal: having shown that we cannot *know* God, Kant concludes that we must *believe* in Him.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to the *Critique of Pure Reason* is fundamentally ambivalent. He accepts Kant's critique of traditional metaphysics — the destruction of the rational proofs for God's existence, the demonstration that the soul is not a substance, the exposure of the antinomies into which reason falls when it oversteps its limits. These are genuine philosophical achievements that Nietzsche's own critique of metaphysics presupposes.

But Nietzsche rejects Kant's reconstruction of what he has destroyed. The noumenal world, the categorical imperative, the postulates of practical reason — these are for Nietzsche survivals of the theological-metaphysical framework that Kant's own critical method should have eliminated. Kant is, in Nietzsche's phrase, a "cunning Christian" who used the tools of Enlightenment to preserve the substance of faith.
