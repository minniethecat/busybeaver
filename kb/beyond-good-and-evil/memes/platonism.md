# Platonism

## Overview

Platonism is, for Nietzsche, far more than the philosophy of Plato: it is the master-error of Western civilization. It is the original "inversion" of values that placed the "true world" above the apparent world, Being above Becoming, and Reason above the body.

From Plato's Forms to the Christian afterlife to Kant's noumenal realm, Nietzsche traces a single genealogy of world-denial. In the Preface he declares: "Christianity is Platonism for 'the people'" (P04-001).

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's engagement with Platonism pervades *Beyond Good and Evil*, but its most concentrated treatment appears in the Preface. There Nietzsche identifies "Plato's invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself" as the founding error of Western metaphysics. "It meant standing truth on her head and denying PERSPECTIVE, the basic condition of all life" (P04-001).

In aphorism 7, Nietzsche contrasts Plato unfavorably with Epicurus, relishing the latter's joke that the Platonists were mere "flatterers of Dionysius" (001-007). And in aphorism 28, he identifies the "charm of the Platonic mode of thought" as a "NOBLE mode of thought" that consisted in "resistance to the obvious sense-evidence" (001-018) — an aristocratic refusal of the obvious in favor of the esoteric that Nietzsche himself partly admires, even as he rejects its conclusions.

## Core Doctrines

1. **The Theory of Forms**: The doctrine that true reality consists of eternal, unchanging, non-sensible Ideas or Forms, of which the sensible world is a mere copy or shadow.
2. **The immortality of the soul**: The doctrine that the rational soul pre-exists the body and survives death — the metaphysical basis of Christian eschatology.
3. **The Good as the highest Form**: The identification of the Form of the Good with the ultimate principle of reality, making ethics and metaphysics inseparable.
4. **The philosopher-king**: The political doctrine that only those who have ascended to knowledge of the Forms are fit to rule — which inverts the Nietzschean priority of command over knowledge.
5. **Dialectic as the path to truth**: The method of rational inquiry that ascends from opinion (*doxa*) to knowledge (*episteme*), which Nietzsche regards as a disguised form of the will to power.

## Key Figures

- **Plato** — The founder; his *Republic*, *Phaedo*, and *Symposium* articulate the core Platonic doctrines.
- **Socrates** — Plato's teacher and the protagonist of the dialogues; Nietzsche sees Socrates as the originator of the dialectical impulse and the "theoretical optimism" that Platonism systematizes.
- **Plotinus** — The founder of Neoplatonism, who developed the mystical and emanationist dimensions of the Platonic tradition.
- **Augustine** — The Christian Platonist par excellence, who synthesized Platonic metaphysics with Pauline theology.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Platonism operates at multiple levels. At the most fundamental level, Platonism is an error of perspective: it mistakes a human projection — the idea of a "true world" — for a discovery, and then uses this projection to devalue the only world that actually exists, the world of Becoming, sensation, and the body.

At a psychological level, Platonism is an expression of *ressentiment*: the philosopher who cannot master the world of Becoming invents a world of Being where his weakness becomes superiority. The Platonic sage who "despises the body" does so not because the body is contemptible but because the body resists his will to power.

At a historical level, Platonism is the catastrophe from which Western civilization has never recovered. Christianity inherited and popularized the Platonic inversion, making the "true world" accessible to all through faith rather than reason alone. Modern philosophy, even in its anti-Platonic moments, remains trapped within Platonic categories: the Kantian noumenon, the Hegelian Absolute, even the positivist's "fact" are all descendants of the Platonic Form.
