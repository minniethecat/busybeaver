# Baruch Spinoza

## Overview

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) occupies an ambivalent position in Nietzsche's thought. On the one hand, Nietzsche recognizes Spinoza as a philosophical ally: Spinoza's rejection of teleology, his critique of free will, his naturalization of ethics, and his refusal of anthropomorphic conceptions of God all anticipate central Nietzschean themes. On the other hand, Spinoza's rationalism — his commitment to the *more geometrico* (geometrical method) and his vision of the intellectual love of God — represents precisely the kind of philosophical asceticism that Nietzsche's thought is directed against.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Spinoza appears in aphorism 5 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where Nietzsche discusses the "mask" that philosophers wear. Spinoza's geometrical method — his pretense of deriving ethical truths from axioms and definitions with mathematical necessity — is a mask: "How much personal timidity and vulnerability does this masquerade of a sick recluse betray!" (001-005). Spinoza's rationalist armor conceals a fragile, hypersensitive soul that could not bear to expose itself directly.

In aphorism 13, Nietzsche praises Spinoza's concept of *conatus* — the striving of each thing to persevere in its being — as a precursor to his own concept of the will to power. But he also notes that Spinoza's rationalism prevented him from seeing the full implications of his own insight: the *conatus* is not a striving for self-preservation but for self-*expansion*.

## Key Ideas

1. **Pantheism** (*Deus sive Natura*): Spinoza's identification of God with Nature, which Nietzsche partially endorses as a step toward naturalism.
2. **Critique of teleology**: Spinoza's rejection of final causes — a crucial precursor to Nietzsche's own anti-teleological thinking.
3. **Conatus**: The drive toward self-preservation, which Nietzsche reinterprets as the will to power.
4. **Geometrical method**: Spinoza's attempt to model philosophy on Euclidean geometry — the mask that Nietzsche sees through.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Descartes** — Spinoza began as a Cartesian but moved beyond Descartes' dualism; Nietzsche sees this progression as exemplary.
- **Goethe** — Goethe was a devoted Spinozist; his pantheistic naturalism derives in part from Spinoza.
