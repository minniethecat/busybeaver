**Utilitarianism** is, for [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]], the quintessential expression of modern [[democracy|democratic]] [[morality]] — the reduction of ethical thinking to a calculus of the "greatest happiness of the greatest number." In *[[beyond-good-and-evil|Beyond Good and Evil]]*, Nietzsche treats the English utilitarians with a mixture of contempt and anthropological curiosity: they are not so much philosophers as symptoms — documents of the [[herd]] instinct that has come to dominate modern Europe.

## The Critique of Happiness as Goal

The opening of aphorism 198 identifies utilitarianism's fundamental error:

> All the systems of morals which address themselves with a view to their "happiness," as it is called — what else are they but suggestions for behaviour adapted to the degree of DANGER from themselves in which the individuals live. (005-013)

| Aspect | Nietzsche's Critique |
|--------|---------------------|
| Happiness as goal | Happiness is a sign of *decline*, not a purpose — the strong seek [[danger]], not comfort |
| Universalism | Moralities that speak to "all" generalize where generalization is not authorized |
| Calculability | The reduction of value to quantifiable pleasure eliminates [[order-of-rank|rank]] |
| Englishness | Utilitarianism is a national pathology, not a universal philosophy |

## The English Type

In aphorism 228, Nietzsche characterizes the English utilitarians as ponderous, respectable, and fundamentally mediocre:

> The indomitable, inevitable English utilitarians: how ponderously and respectably they stalk on... (007-015)

This is not merely ad hominem. Nietzsche's point is that utilitarianism is the philosophy of a **commercial civilization** — a people who think of value in terms of exchange, who measure everything by its utility, and who cannot conceive of greatness that is not useful to the many. [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] and [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] are, in this sense, honest spokesmen for their culture.

## The Will to Power of the Weak

Nietzsche's deepest critique is genealogical. Utilitarianism is not a discovery about the nature of value but an **expression** of the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]] of a specific type — the democratic, leveling, modern European. The "greatest happiness" principle is a strategy by which the many impose their values on the few, binding the exceptional to the standard of the [[herd]]. This is, for Nietzsche, the modern continuation of the "[[slave-morality|slave revolt in morality]]" that began with [[judaism|Judaism]] and was universalized by [[christianity|Christianity]].
