# Richard Wagner

## Overview

Richard Wagner (1813–1883) was the single most important personal and intellectual relationship of Nietzsche's life. As a young professor in Basel, Nietzsche was a frequent guest at Wagner's villa in Tribschen and dedicated *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872) to him, hailing Wagner's music drama as the rebirth of Greek tragedy. The break came gradually — over Wagner's nationalism, his Christianity (the opera *Parsifal*), his anti-Semitism, and what Nietzsche came to see as his theatrical vulgarity — and was announced publicly in *The Case of Wagner* (1888). By the time of *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886), Wagner is no longer a friend but a symptom: the representative of modern "decadence," the artist who seduces his audience into a narcotic state rather than challenging them into wakefulness.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Wagner appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* primarily as an implicit presence rather than a named target. In aphorism 245, Nietzsche contrasts the genuine "master" type with the merely theatrical — "the actor, the mime, the dancer, the singer, the virtuoso" — and while Wagner is not named, the description fits his diagnosis of Wagnerian opera as a form of emotional manipulation rather than genuine art (008-004).

In aphorism 256, Nietzsche describes the "synthesis of the INHUMAN and the SUPERHUMAN" that characterizes the higher type (009-006). This is implicitly contrasted with Wagner, who for all his artistic power remained a figure of *ressentiment* — a revolutionary who envied the aristocracy he sought to overthrow, a Christian moralist in pagan costume.

Aphorism 240 describes the danger of "the actor" — the type who lacks a genuine self and merely performs selves — and while the immediate reference is to the democratic culture of modern Europe, the critique draws on Nietzsche's long reflection on Wagner as the supreme actor-artist (007-041). Wagner, for the late Nietzsche, was not a genuine creator but a seducer, a hypnotist, a man who used music to overwhelm the critical faculties of his listeners.

## Key Ideas

1. **Gesamtkunstwerk** (total work of art): Wagner's ideal of the fusion of music, poetry, and drama influenced Nietzsche's early conception of tragedy.
2. **Decadence**: Nietzsche's later diagnosis of Wagner as a "decadent" — an artist who represents the exhaustion and sickness of European culture rather than its renewal.
3. **The actor problem**: Wagner as the type of the "actor" who lacks a stable self and merely projects personae.
4. **Music and physiology**: Nietzsche came to judge music by its physiological effects; Wagner's music, he concluded, was unhealthy — it overstimulated and exhausted the listener.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Schopenhauer** — Wagner was a devoted Schopenhauerian; *Tristan und Isolde* is essentially Schopenhauer's metaphysics set to music. Nietzsche's break with Wagner is inseparable from his break with Schopenhauer.
- **Goethe** — Nietzsche contrasts Wagner unfavorably with Goethe: Goethe disciplined himself into wholeness; Wagner dissipated himself into effects.
- **Bizet** — In *The Case of Wagner*, Nietzsche opposes Bizet's *Carmen* to Wagner as health to sickness, Mediterranean lightness to Germanic heaviness.
