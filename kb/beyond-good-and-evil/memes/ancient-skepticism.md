# Ancient Skepticism

## Overview

Nietzsche distinguishes ancient skepticism — particularly Pyrrhonism — from the weary modern skepticism he critiques in aphorisms 208–210. Ancient skepticism was an active, vigorous discipline of suspending judgment, not a symptom of cultural exhaustion. The ancient skeptic was a man of experiments who lived according to appearances without dogmatic commitment.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

In aphorisms 208–210, Nietzsche discusses modern skepticism as cultural exhaustion, contrasting it implicitly with the robust skepticism of antiquity. The ancient skeptics cultivated epoche (suspension of judgment) as a path to tranquility, not as a symptom of the paralysis of the will that Nietzsche diagnoses in modern Europe.

## Core Doctrines

1. Epoche — the suspension of judgment about the nature of things. 2. Ataraxia — tranquility achieved through freedom from dogmatic belief. 3. The equipollence of arguments — for every argument, an equal counter-argument can be found. 4. Living by appearances without affirming their truth.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique is that skepticism, even in its ancient form, remains reactive rather than creative. The skeptic suspends judgment; the philosopher of the future creates values. Skepticism is a necessary stage — it liberates from dogma — but it must itself be overcome. The free spirit passes through skepticism without making it a permanent residence.
