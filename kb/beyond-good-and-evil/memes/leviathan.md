# Leviathan

## Overview

Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651) is the founding text of modern political philosophy. Its central argument — that human beings in their natural condition are in a "war of all against all," and that only the sovereign power of the state can secure peace — represents, for Nietzsche, a rare moment of honesty in the history of political thought.

Nietzsche's relationship to Hobbes is ambivalent. He admires Hobbes's recognition that power, not justice, is the fundamental reality of political life. But he deplores the conclusion Hobbes draws: that the fear of violent death justifies the surrender of individual sovereignty to the state. The Hobbesian contract is, for Nietzsche, a disguised form of the slave morality — the weak banding together to protect themselves from the strong.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Hobbes appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* in aphorism 252, where Nietzsche groups him with Hume and Locke as representatives of the English empirical tradition that he associates with the "abasement" and "depreciation" of the philosophical spirit (008-013). This is not a dismissal of Hobbes's philosophical achievement but a critique of what Nietzsche sees as the flattening effect of the English philosophical temperament.

## Summary

The *Leviathan* is structured in four parts: (1) Of Man, developing Hobbes's materialist psychology and epistemology; (2) Of Commonwealth, presenting the theory of the social contract and the justification of absolute sovereignty; (3) Of a Christian Commonwealth, critiquing the political claims of ecclesiastical authority; and (4) Of the Kingdom of Darkness, attacking the "spiritual darkness" of scholastic philosophy and priestcraft.

Hobbes's central argument is that human beings in the "state of nature" — without political authority — are in a condition of perpetual conflict. Life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this condition, individuals contract with one another to establish a sovereign — the "Leviathan" — whose authority is absolute and whose function is to secure peace by monopolizing the use of force. The sovereign is not party to the contract; his power is not conditional on his performance.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's most significant engagement with Hobbes concerns the concept of the "state of nature." Hobbes's war of all against all is, in one sense, a precursor to Nietzsche's will to power: both recognize that conflict, not harmony, is the natural condition of human beings. But where Hobbes sees this conflict as a problem to be solved by the establishment of sovereignty, Nietzsche sees it as a condition to be affirmed — and, for the highest types, to be mastered.

The "blond beast" who imposes his will on others without guilt or hesitation is Hobbes's natural man, but with a crucial difference: Nietzsche's noble type does not contract with others to limit his power but exercises it fully, spontaneously, and joyfully. The Hobbesian contract is an expression of the will to power of the weak — a strategy by which the many bind the strong. Nietzsche's aristocracy is a return to the state of nature, but at a higher level — not a regression to barbarism but an ascent to a form of life that has integrated the predatory instincts into a higher cultural synthesis.
