# Republic (Plato)

## Overview

Plato's *Republic* (c. 375 BCE) is arguably the most influential work of philosophy ever written. Its central arguments — the theory of Forms, the immortality of the soul, the analogy between the just soul and the just city, the allegory of the cave, and the ideal of the philosopher-king — established the framework for Western metaphysics, ethics, and political philosophy.

For Nietzsche, the *Republic* is the founding document of the error that has dominated Western civilization. By positing a "true world" of eternal Forms behind the apparent world of Becoming, Plato invented the metaphysical structure that Christianity would later democratize as "Platonism for the people." The *Republic* is thus not merely a philosophical text but the origin of a civilizational catastrophe.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche engages the *Republic* throughout *Beyond Good and Evil*, most notably in the Preface, where he identifies "Plato's invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself" as "the worst, the most tiresome, and the most dangerous of errors" (P04-001). The *Republic*'s central gesture — the positing of a transcendent reality that devalues the world of experience — is the founding act of philosophical world-denial.

In aphorism 7, Nietzsche contrasts Plato unfavorably with Epicurus, relishing the Epicurean joke that the Platonists were mere "flatterers of Dionysius" (001-007). The *Republic*'s political project — the attempt to educate a tyrant into a philosopher-king — is exposed as naive and self-serving.

## Summary

The *Republic* is a dialogue in ten books, narrated by Socrates, that investigates the nature of justice. Socrates argues that justice in the individual soul is analogous to justice in the city: a just soul, like a just city, is one in which each part performs its proper function under the rule of reason. The ideal city is governed by philosopher-kings who have ascended from the cave of sensory illusion to the sunlight of the Form of the Good.

The dialogue's most famous passages — the allegory of the cave, the divided line, the myth of Er — articulate the Platonic metaphysics that Nietzsche spends his entire career attempting to overturn. The sensible world is a shadow; true reality is supersensible; the philosopher is the one who has escaped the cave and seen the sun; the task of philosophy is to lead others out of darkness.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to the *Republic* is one of determined opposition. He rejects its central metaphysical claim (that the sensible world is a copy of a more real intelligible world), its political ideal (the rule of philosophers who have transcended desire), and its ethical framework (the identification of virtue with knowledge and justice with psychological harmony).

Yet Nietzsche's opposition to Plato is not a simple rejection. He recognizes in Plato a philosopher of immense power — a "commander and law-giver" of the first rank whose valuations have shaped the course of Western history. The task is not to refute Plato (which would be to remain within the Platonic framework of argument and refutation) but to *overcome* him — to create new values that do not depend on the positing of a "true world" and that affirm the world of Becoming that Plato despised.
