# Faust

## Overview

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe's *Faust* (Part I, 1808; Part II, 1832) is the greatest work of German literature and, for Nietzsche, the supreme artistic expression of the life-affirming spirit he associates with Goethe himself. Faust — the scholar who makes a pact with Mephistopheles, seeking not pleasure but experience, striving, and self-overcoming — is a figure of immense significance for Nietzsche's thought.

Nietzsche's admiration for *Faust* is part of his broader admiration for Goethe: both represent a "Dionysian" capacity for total self-realization, an embrace of life in all its dimensions — including suffering, evil, and destruction — as material for creation and self-overcoming.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche quotes *Faust* directly in *Beyond Good and Evil*. In aphorism 290, he concludes with "the mind exalted" — a line from *Faust*, Part II, Act V (009-030), where the "Mothers" are invoked as the primal figures of transformation and creation. The reference is apt: Goethe's "Mothers" are the mysterious, generative powers that dwell beneath the surface of experience, and Nietzsche's "philosophers of the future" are those who dare to descend to them.

In aphorism 244, Nietzsche compares Goethe to Dante in their treatment of woman: "one will oppose what Dante and Goethe believed about woman — the former when he sang 'ella guardava suso, ed io in lei,' the latter when he rendered it 'the eternal feminine draws us ALOFT'" (007-029). Goethe's vision of the "eternal feminine" at the end of *Faust* is, for Nietzsche, an expression of Goethe's affirmative relationship to life.

## Summary

*Faust* is a two-part dramatic poem based on the medieval legend of the scholar who sells his soul to the devil. Goethe's Faust is not a simple sinner but a figure of Promethean striving: his pact with Mephistopheles is not for pleasure but for experience, for the fullness of life, for the moment of such intensity that he would wish it to endure — the moment that would fulfill his wager with the devil.

Part I focuses on the "Gretchen tragedy" — Faust's seduction and abandonment of Margarete — and explores themes of desire, guilt, and redemption. Part II expands into a cosmic drama of political power, classical beauty (embodied in Helen of Troy), and the final redemption of Faust through his productive activity — his land reclamation project, his creation of a new world from the sea.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

*Faust* is important for Nietzsche not as a doctrine but as an attitude. Faust's striving — his refusal to rest satisfied, his willingness to risk damnation for the sake of fuller experience — is the antithesis of the ascetic ideal. Where the Christian saint denies desire, Faust pursues it to its limit. Where the Schopenhauerian pessimist renounces the will, Faust affirms it with a passion that transforms even evil into a condition of growth.

Nietzsche does not endorse every aspect of Goethe's *Faust* — the final redemption, with its Christian overtones, sits uneasily with Nietzsche's anti-Christian sensibility. But the fundamental gesture of the work — the embrace of striving, the refusal of static satisfaction, the affirmation of life in its totality — is profoundly Nietzschean. Goethe's *Faust* is, in a sense, a poetic anticipation of the Dionysian philosophy that *Beyond Good and Evil* articulates in philosophical prose.
