# Buddhism

## Overview

Buddhism occupies a privileged position in Nietzsche's comparative analysis of religions. Unlike Christianity, which Nietzsche regards as a religion of *ressentiment* — born of the slave's revenge against the master — Buddhism is, for Nietzsche, a religion for the "close of a civilization," a post-metaphysical spiritual practice that has outgrown the crude anthropomorphism of earlier faiths.

Nietzsche's admiration for Buddhism is genuine but limited. Buddhism is more honest than Christianity — it does not posit a personal God, it does not promise an afterlife, it does not make suffering the consequence of sin. But it remains a form of world-denial: its goal of Nirvana is an escape from the cycle of rebirth, a negation of the will-to-live that is, from Nietzsche's perspective, a sophisticated form of the ascetic ideal.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche compares Buddhism and Christianity in aphorism 56, where he describes Buddhism as "a hundred times more realistic than Christianity" (003-013). Buddhism, he argues, has "the heritage of a cool and objective posing of problems in its composition" — it arises from centuries of philosophical debate and has dispensed with the mythological apparatus that Christianity retains.

In aphorism 61, Nietzsche more critically groups Buddhism with Christianity as religions of world-denial, noting that "perhaps nothing is so admirable in Christianity and Buddhism as their art of teaching even the lowliest to place themselves through piety in an illusory higher order of things" (003-018). This "art" is admirable as a psychological technique but condemnable as an orientation toward illusion.

In aphorism 202, Nietzsche warns that modern European culture is "threatened with a new Buddhism" — not the historical religion but a secularized form of world-denial that takes the form of "European Buddhism" or nihilistic quietism (005-017).

## Core Doctrines

1. **The Four Noble Truths**: The diagnosis of existence as suffering (*dukkha*), the identification of desire as its cause, the possibility of cessation, and the Eightfold Path as the way.
2. **Nirvana**: The extinction of desire and the escape from the cycle of rebirth — which Nietzsche interprets as a sophisticated form of the will to nothingness.
3. **Anatta (no-self)**: The doctrine that there is no permanent, substantial self — a position that superficially resembles Nietzsche's critique of the "soul-atom" but serves opposite ends.
4. **The Middle Way**: The Buddhist rejection of both sensual indulgence and ascetic extremism — which Nietzsche might recognize as a form of self-mastery.

## Key Figures

- **The Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama)** — The historical founder of Buddhism; Nietzsche's knowledge of Buddhism came primarily through Schopenhauer and nineteenth-century Orientalist scholarship.
- **Arthur Schopenhauer** — Not a Buddhist, but the Western philosopher who did most to introduce Buddhist ideas into European philosophy and whose pessimism Nietzsche regarded as a form of "European Buddhism."

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Buddhism is that, for all its sophistication, it remains a form of world-denial. The Buddhist who seeks Nirvana seeks the extinction of desire, the cessation of the will — precisely the "will to nothingness" that Nietzsche diagnoses as the hidden logic of the ascetic ideal. Buddhism is more honest than Christianity, but it leads to the same destination: the negation of life.

Nietzsche's own position — the affirmation of life, the Yes-saying to existence in its totality, the embrace of suffering as a condition of growth — is, in a sense, an anti-Buddhism. Where Buddhism says "life is suffering, therefore escape," Nietzsche says "life is suffering, therefore affirm." This is the difference between the will to nothingness and the will to power: between Nirvana and the eternal return.
