# On the Origin of Species

## Overview

Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection* (1859) is one of the most consequential works in the history of science. Its central argument — that species evolve through natural selection, with differential survival and reproduction producing adaptive change over geological time — eliminated the need for supernatural explanations of biological complexity and established the continuity of human beings with the rest of the animal kingdom.

For Nietzsche, the *Origin* was an ambivalent achievement. He welcomed its destruction of teleology and its naturalistic account of human origins. But he rejected the "struggle for existence" as the fundamental principle of life, arguing that organisms seek not merely to survive but to expand, dominate, and overcome.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche references Darwinism rather than the *Origin* specifically in *Beyond Good and Evil*. In aphorism 14, he groups "the Darwinists and anti-teleologists among the physiological workers of today" among those who represent the modern philosophical tendency he both admires and criticizes (001-018).

In aphorism 253, Nietzsche places Darwin among the "mediocre Englishmen" — "Darwin, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer" — whose influence on European culture he deplores (008-014). This expresses a critique not of the *Origin* as science but of the philosophical uses to which it was being put.

## Summary

The *Origin of Species* argues that the diversity of life can be explained by two principles: (1) organisms vary, and some of these variations are heritable; (2) organisms produce more offspring than can survive, leading to a "struggle for existence" in which those with advantageous variations are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over geological time, this process of natural selection produces the adaptation and diversification of species that had previously been attributed to divine design.

Darwin's argument was revolutionary not only for its conclusions but for its method: it showed that a purely naturalistic mechanism — one requiring no intelligent designer, no teleological direction, no vital force — could explain the most complex phenomena in nature. This was the achievement Nietzsche recognized and valued, even as he challenged its philosophical interpretation.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's relationship to the *Origin* is one of critical appropriation. He accepts Darwin's anti-teleological naturalism — the elimination of final causes and divine design from the explanation of life — as a crucial step toward intellectual honesty. "We are all atheists now," Nietzsche might have said, "thanks in part to Darwin."

But Nietzsche rejects the mechanism Darwin proposes. The "struggle for existence" is, for Nietzsche, a projection of Malthusian economics onto nature: it assumes scarcity where there is abundance, competition where there is creative expenditure, and survival where the real drive is toward expansion and domination. The will to power is Nietzsche's alternative to natural selection: life does not merely adapt to its environment; it imposes itself on its environment, reshaping the world according to its own internal imperatives.
