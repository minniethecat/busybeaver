# Luther's Bible

## Overview

Martin Luther's translation of the Bible into German (New Testament 1522, complete Bible 1534) is one of the foundational works of German literature. Its linguistic influence on the development of modern German is comparable to the influence of the King James Bible on English, and Nietzsche — despite his contempt for Luther's theology — acknowledges this achievement without reservation.

This acknowledgment is characteristic of Nietzsche's complex relationship to the Christian tradition: he can recognize and admire the cultural achievements of Christianity even as he condemns its fundamental orientation. Luther's Bible is a masterpiece of German prose — and the vehicle by which the "Platonism for the people" was transmitted to the German-speaking world.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most striking acknowledgment of Luther's Bible appears in aphorism 247 of *Beyond Good and Evil*: "Compared with Luther's Bible, almost everything else is literature" (008-008). This is one of the most generous judgments Nietzsche ever passes on a work of Christian origin, and it reflects his recognition that linguistic power can coexist with philosophical error.

The context of this judgment is Nietzsche's discussion of the German language and German culture. Luther's Bible created modern German prose — the instrument that Goethe, Schopenhauer, and Nietzsche himself would later use to such effect. The paradox is that this instrument was forged in the service of a cause Nietzsche opposes.

## Summary

Luther's translation of the Bible was a revolutionary linguistic and cultural event. Before Luther, the Bible existed in German only in fragmentary and often unreliable translations based on the Latin Vulgate. Luther translated directly from the original Greek and Hebrew, producing a German text of unprecedented clarity, force, and naturalness. His principle was to write not "Germanized Latin" but genuine German — the language "as the mother in the house, the children in the street, and the common man in the marketplace speak."

The theological content of Luther's Bible is, of course, the Christian scriptures, and Luther's translation is inseparable from his theological commitments: his marginal glosses, his prefaces to the individual books, and his famous decision to translate *logos* in John 1:1 not as "Word" but as "the Word was God" — all reflect the Lutheran hermeneutic of *sola scriptura* and justification by faith alone.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's acknowledgment of Luther's Bible is important for understanding his critical method. Nietzsche is not a simple anti-Christian polemicist; he is capable of recognizing that Christianity has produced works of enduring value and that the cultural achievements of Christianity are not nullified by the falseness of its metaphysical claims.

Luther's Bible is, in Nietzschean terms, an expression of the will to power — a will that created the German language as a literary medium. That this will was directed toward the propagation of Christianity does not diminish its creative power. The task Nietzsche sets is not to deny this achievement but to redirect the creative will toward life-affirming ends — to produce works of comparable power in the service of a Dionysian rather than a Christian vision.
