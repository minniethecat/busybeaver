# Darwinism as Worldview

## Overview

Darwinism in Nietzsche's usage refers not to the biological theory of evolution by natural selection (which he largely accepts) but to its philosophical appropriation — the claim that the struggle for existence is the fundamental principle of all life and that adaptation to environment is the measure of success. Nietzsche rejects this as a projection of nineteenth-century English commercial values onto nature. The will to power is not the will to survive but the will to dominate and create.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Darwinism appears in aphorism 253 as the English Darwinism Nietzsche critiques as a 'philosophy of the plebs' (008-014). In aphorism 14, Nietzsche argues that the struggle for existence is the exception, not the rule — the will to power, the drive to expand and dominate, is more fundamental than the mere drive to survive.

## Core Doctrines

1. Natural selection as the mechanism of evolutionary change. 2. The struggle for existence as the fundamental biological reality. 3. Adaptation to environment as the criterion of fitness. 4. Gradualism — evolution proceeds by small, incremental changes.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique is not of Darwin's biology but of its philosophical interpretation. The 'struggle for existence' is a Malthusian projection — bourgeois economists see competition everywhere because competition is all they know. In reality, life seeks not merely to survive but to expand, to dominate, to overflow — this is the will to power. The fittest are not the best-adapted but the most powerful, the most creative, the most commanding.
