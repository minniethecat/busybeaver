# Dialogues

## Overview

Plato's Dialogues — the collected body of philosophical conversations featuring Socrates — constitute the founding documents of Western philosophy. For Nietzsche, they represent the moment when the pre-Socratic tragic wisdom was displaced by dialectical reason, when myth gave way to argument, and when philosophy committed itself to the project of justifying existence before the tribunal of reason. The Dialogues are the original texts of the error Nietzsche's philosophy seeks to correct.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Plato's Dialogues are the implicit target of much of Nietzsche's critique in Beyond Good and Evil. The Preface names Plato's invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself as the foundational dogmatist error; the critique of the 'will to truth' in §1 targets the Socratic demand that life justify itself before reason.

## Summary

The Platonic corpus includes some 30 dialogues ranging from the early 'Socratic' works (Apology, Crito, Euthyphro) through the middle-period masterpieces (Republic, Symposium, Phaedo) to the late critical works (Theaetetus, Sophist, Parmenides). The central doctrines — the theory of Forms, the immortality of the soul, the philosopher-king, the superiority of reason to appetite — are articulated and defended across the dialogues with an artistry unmatched in the philosophical tradition.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's critique targets not the individual doctrines but the fundamental gesture of Platonism: the invention of a 'true world' behind the apparent one and the consequent denigration of the sensory, the bodily, the temporal. The Dialogues are philosophy's original sin — but also philosophy's greatest artistry. Nietzsche's ambivalence toward Plato reflects this double character: the artist Nietzsche admires, the metaphysician he opposes.
