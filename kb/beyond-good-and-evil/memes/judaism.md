**Judaism** occupies a pivotal position in [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s genealogy of Western [[morality]]. It is the source of what Nietzsche calls the "[[slave-morality|slave revolt in morality]]" — the moment when the [[priestly]] type revalued the values of the noble [[aristocracy]], transforming powerlessness into virtue and suffering into a claim to justice. While Nietzsche's judgments on Judaism are complex and often ambivalent, he consistently identifies the Jewish tradition as the origin of the moral revolution that would, through [[christianity|Christianity]], come to dominate Europe.

## The Book of Divine Justice

In aphorism 52, Nietzsche offers a striking appreciation of the Old Testament:

> In the Jewish "Old Testament," the book of divine justice, there are men, things, and sayings on such an immense scale, that Greek and Indian literature have nothing to compare with it. (003-009)

This is one of the highest compliments Nietzsche pays to any text. He contrasts the grandeur of the Old Testament with what he sees as the sentimental diminution of the New Testament. The Old Testament is a world of **power**, of patriarchs and kings, of [[cruelty]] and [[greatness]] — not yet softened by the Christian ethics of [[compassion|pity]].

## The Slave Revolt

In aphorism 195, Nietzsche identifies the Jewish people as the initiators of the most consequential moral revaluation in history:

> The Jews — a people "born for slavery," as Tacitus and the whole ancient world says... have known how to create for themselves a feeling of power and of eternal charm out of the very activities which were once despised. (005-010)

| Historical Phase | Moral Configuration | Source of Power |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Pre-exilic Israel | Noble-warrior values | Military and political power |
| Prophetic Judaism | Priestly revaluation begins | Moral interpretation of suffering |
| Post-exilic Judaism | Full slave revolt in morality | Spiritualization of powerlessness |
| Christianity | Universalization of Jewish values | Democratization of *ressentiment* |

## Nietzsche's Ambivalence

Nietzsche's position on Judaism is notably different from his position on Christianity. Europe "owes the Jews" many things, as aphorism 250 acknowledges — including "the grand style in morality" (008-011). The [[jew|Jewish]] people preserved their identity through millennia of dispersion precisely through the strength of their moral will. Nietzsche admires this strength even as he opposes its direction. The [[will-to-power|Will to Power]] that created the slave revolt was itself a formidable expression of power.
