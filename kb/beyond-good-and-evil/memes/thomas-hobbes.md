# Thomas Hobbes

## Overview

Thomas Hobbes (5 April 1588 – 4 December 1679) was an English philosopher, best known for his political treatise *Leviathan* (1651) and for his materialist, mechanistic account of human nature. Hobbes argued that in the "state of nature" — the condition of humanity without government — life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this condition, individuals enter into a social contract, surrendering their natural rights to an absolute sovereign. Hobbes's psychological egoism — the view that all human action is motivated by self-interest — anticipates important dimensions of Nietzsche's own thinking about human motivation, even as Nietzsche subjects Hobbes and the English philosophical tradition to sweeping critique.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche mentions Hobbes by name exactly once in *Beyond Good and Evil* — and it is not a compliment. In Part VIII ("Peoples and Fatherlands"), Nietzsche delivers his verdict on the English philosophical tradition:

> They are not a philosophical race—the English: Bacon represents an ATTACK on the philosophical spirit generally, Hobbes, Hume, and Locke, an abasement, and a depreciation of the idea of a "philosopher" for more than a century. [008-013]

Nietzsche groups Hobbes with Hume and Locke as thinkers who diminished, rather than advanced, the philosophical enterprise. For Nietzsche, Hobbes's materialist psychology and mechanistic account of human motivation represent precisely the kind of "English mechanical stultification of the world" [008-013] that German philosophy — from Kant through Hegel and Schopenhauer — had to struggle against.

Yet Nietzsche's own doctrine of the will to power can be read as a radicalization of Hobbes's psychological egoism. Hobbes argues that human beings are driven by "a perpetual and restless desire of power after power that ceases only in death" (*Leviathan*, ch. 11). Nietzsche deepens this psychological observation into a comprehensive metaphysical hypothesis: the will to power is not merely one drive among others but the fundamental character of all life. The crucial difference is evaluative: Hobbes sees the competitive struggle for power as a horror to be escaped through sovereign authority; Nietzsche sees the struggle — properly sublimated — as the very condition of human greatness.

The English utilitarians Nietzsche ridicules — "how ponderously and respectably they stalk on… in the footsteps of Bentham" [007-015] — represent for him the degenerate descendants of Hobbes's psychological hedonism: the reduction of all human striving to the calculus of pleasure and pain.

## Key Ideas

**Psychological Egoism** — Hobbes argues that all human action is motivated by self-interest: what we call "good" is simply what we desire, and "evil" what we avoid. Nietzsche radicalizes this insight by arguing that the fundamental drive is not mere self-preservation but the will to power — an expansive, appropriative striving that cannot be reduced to Hobbesian appetite.

**The State of Nature** — Hobbes's famous thought experiment imagines human beings without government, in a condition of perpetual war. Nietzsche would not dispute the descriptive claim — he too sees humans as naturally competitive and hierarchical — but he rejects the normative conclusion that this condition is to be escaped rather than sublimated.

**The Social Contract** — Hobbes's solution to the state of nature is the social contract: individuals surrender their natural liberty to a sovereign in exchange for security. The notion that individuals freely consent to be governed is, for Nietzsche, a fiction that conceals the actual origins of political order in conquest and domination.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **[[friedrich-nietzsche]]** — Nietzsche names Hobbes once, grouping him with Hume and Locke as an "abasement" of philosophy; yet Nietzsche inherits and transforms Hobbes's psychological egoism into the doctrine of the will to power
- **[[jean-jacques-rousseau]]** — Rousseau's social contract theory represents an alternative (optimistic) development of the contract tradition Hobbes inaugurated
- **[[jeremy-bentham]]** and **[[john-stuart-mill]]** — Hobbes's psychological hedonism provides one source for the utilitarian calculus; Nietzsche critiques both traditions
- **[[leviathan]]** — Hobbes's masterpiece, a foundational text of modern political philosophy
