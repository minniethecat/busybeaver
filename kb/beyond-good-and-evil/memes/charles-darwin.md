# Charles Darwin

## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) is a significant interlocutor for Nietzsche's thought, though Nietzsche's relationship to Darwinism is more critical than affirmative. Nietzsche recognized in Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection a crucial blow to teleological and theological worldviews.

He also criticized what he saw as Darwinism's overestimation of the "struggle for existence" at the expense of what Nietzsche considered the deeper drive: the will to power, which seeks not mere survival but expansion, mastery, and self-overcoming.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Darwin appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* primarily as a representative of the English "flat-headed" empirical tradition that Nietzsche associates with mediocrity and lack of philosophical depth.

In aphorism 14, Nietzsche characterizes the philosophical climate of modern Europe: "The whole of modern European philosophy — the Darwinists and anti-teleologists among the physiological workers of today offer us" (001-018). Nietzsche groups Darwinism with the broader anti-teleological movement in modern thought — a movement he partly endorses but also regards as philosophically naive.

In aphorism 253, Nietzsche places Darwin among the "mediocre Englishmen" whose influence on European taste he deplores: "I may mention Darwin, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer" (008-014). This expresses a critique of the philosophical reduction of value to survival and the flattening of human aspiration to mere adaptation.

Nietzsche's deeper engagement with Darwinian themes appears throughout the text even where Darwin is not named. The will to power is in part a response to the Darwinian "struggle for existence."

Nietzsche argues that organisms do not merely struggle to survive but to discharge their strength, to impose their form, to expand. Life is not defensive but expansive; the fundamental drive is not self-preservation but self-overcoming.

## Key Ideas

1. **Anti-teleological naturalism**: Darwin's theory eliminates final causes from biology, a move Nietzsche endorses and extends to philosophy.
2. **Critique of "struggle for existence"**: Nietzsche argues that Darwin overestimates the pressure of scarcity; nature is characterized by abundance, not need, and the fundamental struggle is for power, not survival.
3. **Will to power vs. natural selection**: The will to power is Nietzsche's alternative to the Darwinian mechanism — life seeks not merely to preserve itself but to expand and dominate.
4. **Critique of evolutionary optimism**: Nietzsche rejects the progressive interpretation of evolution (associated with Herbert Spencer) as a disguised form of Christian providence.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Herbert Spencer** — Spencer's "survival of the fittest" and evolutionary optimism represent for Nietzsche precisely the wrong interpretation of Darwin; Spencer is more the target than Darwin himself.
- **John Stuart Mill** — Grouped with Darwin among the "mediocre Englishmen," Mill represents the utilitarian flattening of ethics that Nietzsche associates with the English empirical tradition.
- **Arthur Schopenhauer** — Schopenhauer's "will to live" is the precursor that Nietzsche transforms into "will to power"; Darwin's "struggle for existence" is an intermediate step in this conceptual evolution.
- **David Hume** — The English empirical tradition that Nietzsche associates with Darwinism begins with Hume, whose skeptical naturalism Nietzsche respects more than its later developments.
