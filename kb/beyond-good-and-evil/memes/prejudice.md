# Prejudice

## Overview

In Nietzsche's usage, "prejudice" (*Vorurteil*) is elevated from a term of everyday disapproval to a central category of philosophical critique. The prejudices Nietzsche targets are not mere biases or bigotries but the fundamental, largely unconscious presuppositions that have structured the entire Western philosophical tradition. The title of Part I of *Beyond Good and Evil* — "On the Prejudices of Philosophers" — signals that the exposure and dismantling of such prejudices is the necessary preliminary to any genuine philosophizing.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

The concept of prejudice pervades the opening chapter of *Beyond Good and Evil*. Nietzsche identifies a series of interconnected prejudices that have governed philosophy from Plato to his own day. One of the most fundamental is the "prejudice of opposites" — the metaphysical assumption that things of the highest value cannot arise from their opposites. "HOW COULD anything originate out of its opposite?" Nietzsche quotes the metaphysicians as asking, before turning the question against them: what if the will to truth itself originated in the will to deception? (001-002)

The moral prejudice is another primary target. In aphorism 23, Nietzsche declares that "all psychology hitherto has run aground on moral prejudices and timidities" — it has not dared to venture into the depths of the human soul without the life-preserver of moral judgment (001-027). Psychologists and philosophers alike have been constrained by an unspoken commitment to *defending* morality rather than *investigating* it.

Nietzsche also exposes the "prejudice of the unconditional" — the philosopher's predilection for absolutes, for truths that admit of no nuance and no exception. This, he argues, is a symptom not of intellectual rigor but of a crude and immature taste. "Everything is so arranged that the worst of all tastes, THE TASTE FOR THE UNCONDITIONAL, is cruelly befooled and abused" by the complexity of reality (002-008).

In aphorism 34, Nietzsche extends the concept of prejudice to the very structure of cognition: "the ERRONEOUSNESS of the world in which we think we live is the surest and most certain thing our eyes can seize upon" (002-011). What we take to be knowledge may itself be the most deeply entrenched prejudice of all — the prejudice that the world is knowable in the way philosophers have assumed.

## Nietzsche's Position

Nietzsche does not claim to stand outside prejudice himself. Rather, his method is to make prejudices *visible as prejudices* — to strip them of their self-evidence and expose them as historical, psychological, and linguistic constructions. This is the meaning of his famous declaration that philosophers must become "suspicious" — suspicious of their own fundamental assumptions, of the language in which they think, of the moral valuations that shape their apparently neutral inquiries.

The overcoming of prejudice is not a one-time act but a continuous discipline. Nietzsche describes the free spirit as one who has learned to "look at things differently" — that is, to adopt multiple perspectives on the same object, thereby loosening the grip of any single prejudice. This perspectivism is the positive counterpart to the negative work of prejudice-exposure.

## Related Concepts

- **Will to Truth** — The most fundamental philosophical prejudice is the assumption that truth is inherently valuable.
- **Free Spirit** — The free spirit is one who has recognized and begun to overcome his own philosophical prejudices.
- **Perspectivism** — The epistemological stance that follows from the recognition that all knowing is conditioned by perspective and therefore by prejudice.
- **Skepticism** — Skepticism is a tool for exposing prejudice, but it can become a prejudice of its own if pursued unconditionally.

## Historical Context

Nietzsche's critique of philosophical prejudice has roots in the Enlightenment tradition of *Vorurteilskritik* — the critique of prejudice — associated with thinkers like Bacon (the "idols of the mind") and Kant (the critique of reason's self-deceptions). But Nietzsche radicalizes this tradition by extending the critique of prejudice to reason itself, to truth, and to the moral values that underpin the Enlightenment project. His genealogical method, developed more fully in *On the Genealogy of Morals*, is essentially a method for tracing prejudices back to their origins in human psychology, social organization, and the will to power. This approach influenced later traditions of ideology critique, from the Frankfurt School to post-structuralism.
