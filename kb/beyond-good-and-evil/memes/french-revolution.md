# French Revolution

## Overview

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was the founding political event of modern democratic Europe — the overthrow of the French monarchy and aristocracy in the name of liberty, equality, and fraternity. In *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche treats the Revolution not as a triumph of justice but as a case study in how the weak overthrow the strong by moralizing their resentment.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most memorable characterization of the Revolution is blunt:

> "As happened finally in all the enlightenment of modern times with the French Revolution (that terrible farce, quite superfluous when judged close at hand, into which, however, the noble and visionary spectators of all Europe have interpreted from a distance their own indignation and enthusiasm so long and passionately, UNTIL THE TEXT HAS DISAPPEARED UNDER THE INTERPRETATION)…" [002-015]

The phrase "terrible farce" captures his core claim: the Revolution was not a noble liberation but a theatrical bloodbath whose real meaning was supplied after the fact by intellectuals projecting their own fantasies onto it. The capitalization of "UNTIL THE TEXT HAS DISAPPEARED UNDER THE INTERPRETATION" is Nietzsche's own emphasis — a methodological warning about how ideology overwrites historical reality.

### The Slave Revolt in Morality

Nietzsche embeds the Revolution within his master-slave dialectic. In Chapter 3, he writes that the "insurrection of the ruled is that which began with the French Revolution" [003-002], directly linking the political event to his psychological analysis of *ressentiment*. The Revolution is the political expression of a moral revolution that had already occurred — the inversion of aristocratic values by Christian slave morality.

### Women and the Revolution

In a passage that combines his critique of democracy with his views on gender, Nietzsche observes:

> "Since the French Revolution the influence of woman in Europe has DECLINED in proportion as her rights and claims have increased." [007-040]

This is a characteristically counter-intuitive claim: the Revolution that proclaimed universal rights actually diminished women's real influence, replacing aristocratic women's informal power with the formal exclusion of democratic institutions.

### Comparison to Other Events

Nietzsche distinguishes the Revolution from genuinely transformative events. In Chapter 8, he notes that the Revolution was not "the event on account of which the noble and the powerful of the earth have trembled" [008-005] — that distinction belongs, in his view, to the rise of the Jews as a moral force in European history.

## Historical Background

The French Revolution began in 1789 with the convening of the Estates-General and the storming of the Bastille. It passed through multiple phases: constitutional monarchy (1789–1792), the Terror under Robespierre (1793–1794), the Directory (1795–1799), and Napoleon's rise to power. The Revolution abolished feudal privileges, nationalized Church property, executed Louis XVI, and proclaimed the Rights of Man. Its ideals spread across Europe through the Napoleonic Wars.

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

Nietzsche's reading of the Revolution is genealogical: he asks not "Was it just?" but "What type of person wanted this? What psychological need did it satisfy?" His answer is that the Revolution satisfied the *ressentiment* of the masses — their desire to see the noble brought low, reframed as a demand for equality.

This interpretation is not conservative nostalgia for the *ancien régime*. Nietzsche has no interest in restoring the French monarchy. His critique is aimed at the Revolution's self-understanding: the claim that it was motivated by universal reason and justice rather than by the will to power of a rising class. The Revolution is "superfluous when judged close at hand" because the real transformation — the slave revolt in morality — had already been accomplished by Christianity and the Enlightenment. The Revolution was merely the political after-effect.
