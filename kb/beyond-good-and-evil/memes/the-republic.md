# Republic

## Overview

Plato's Republic is for Nietzsche the founding document of the error — the work in which the Platonic two-world metaphysics, the doctrine of the Forms, the allegory of the cave, and the ideal of the philosopher-king are first articulated in their canonical form. Everything Nietzsche opposes in the Western philosophical tradition — the denigration of the sensory world, the elevation of reason over instinct, the subordination of the individual to the moral ideal — finds its first and most influential expression in the Republic.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

The Republic is the implicit target of many of Nietzsche's critiques in Beyond Good and Evil, particularly the Preface's identification of Plato's 'invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself' as the foundational dogmatist error (P04-001). In aphorism 190, Nietzsche distinguishes Plato from the Socratism that appears 'in his philosophy, one might say, in spite of him' (005-005).

## Summary

The Republic is structured as a dialogue in which Socrates and his interlocutors attempt to define justice. The discussion leads to the construction of an ideal city (Kallipolis) governed by philosopher-kings, structured into three classes (rulers, auxiliaries, producers), and founded on the metaphysical distinction between the visible world of becoming and the intelligible world of being. The allegory of the cave, the simile of the divided line, and the theory of Forms are presented as the philosophical foundations of this political vision.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's critique of the Republic is fundamental: it is not merely mistaken in its conclusions but perverse in its method — the subordination of truth to morality, of the real to the ideal, of life to the concept. The philosopher-king is a contradiction in terms: the genuine philosopher does not rule but creates; the genuine ruler does not philosophize but commands. The Republic's vision of justice is, in reality, the revenge of the conceptual against the vital.
