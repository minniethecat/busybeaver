# On the Genealogy of Morals

## Overview

Nietzsche's On the Genealogy of Morals (1887) was written as a 'supplement and clarification' of Beyond Good and Evil, developing the historical and psychological analysis of slave morality that BGE had outlined. The three essays — on the origins of good and evil, guilt and bad conscience, and the ascetic ideal — constitute Nietzsche's most sustained exercise in the genealogical method.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

The Genealogy is the direct continuation of Beyond Good and Evil. The master-slave morality distinction sketched in BGE Chapter IX is given full historical treatment in Essay I. The critique of the ascetic ideal that pervades BGE is developed systematically in Essay III. The two books are best read as a single continuous argument: BGE provides the philosophical framework, the Genealogy provides the historical evidence.

## Summary

Essay I traces the transformation of the noble 'good/bad' distinction into the slave-moral 'good/evil' opposition through the 'slave revolt in morality' led by the Jews and institutionalized by Christianity. Essay II analyzes the internalization of cruelty as 'bad conscience' and the role of debt, punishment, and the creditor-debtor relationship in the formation of moral concepts. Essay III examines the meaning of the ascetic ideal — the will to nothingness that has dominated Western culture — and its paradoxical role in preserving the will itself when life has become unbearable.

## Connection to Nietzsche's Thought

The Genealogy represents Nietzsche's mature method at its most powerful. Where BGE is aphoristic and provocative, the Genealogy is sustained and systematic — yet without succumbing to the 'systematic will' that Nietzsche critiques in the philosophical tradition. Together, the two books constitute the most complete statement of Nietzsche's mature philosophy.
