# Epicurus

## Overview

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) is one of the few ancient philosophers whom Nietzsche treats with consistent admiration and a sense of kinship. The founder of the Garden school, Epicurus represents for Nietzsche an alternative to Platonism: a philosophy grounded in the body rather than the Forms, in pleasure rather than transcendence.

Nietzsche's Epicurus is not the vulgar hedonist of Christian caricature but a refined voluptuary of the spirit — a subtle psychologist whose thinking prefigures Nietzsche's own.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Epicurus appears in aphorism 7 of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where Nietzsche recounts a joke of Epicurus at the expense of Plato and the Platonists. "What? There is nothing more stinging than the joke Epicurus took the liberty of making on Plato and the Platonists; he called them Dionysiokolakes" (001-007).

The term, meaning "flatterers of Dionysius" (the tyrant of Syracuse whom Plato attempted to educate), is a characteristically Epicurean barb: it reduces Platonic philosophy to sycophancy. Nietzsche's pleasure in recounting this joke is palpable: he shares Epicurus's irreverence toward the Platonic tradition.

Beyond this direct reference, Epicurus's presence in *Beyond Good and Evil* is felt throughout Nietzsche's critique of metaphysics. The Epicurean rejection of teleology, divine providence, and the immortality of the soul are all positions Nietzsche endorses and radicalizes.

The "Epicurean god" — indifferent, self-sufficient, contemplating the world without intervening — appears in aphorism 62 as a model for the philosophical stance Nietzsche recommends: "With the decisive and impartial eye of an Epicurean god, I should think one would have to look upon the whole tragi-comedy of European morality" (003-019).

## Key Ideas

1. **Anti-Platonism**: Epicurus's rejection of Platonic Forms and his materialist physics align him with Nietzsche's own anti-metaphysical project.
2. **Refined hedonism**: Epicurus's conception of pleasure as *ataraxia* (tranquility) rather than sensory excess resonates with Nietzsche's ideal of philosophical self-mastery.
3. **Rejection of teleology**: Epicurus's atomism and rejection of final causes anticipate Nietzsche's own naturalism and critique of the "given" meaning of existence.
4. **The indifferent gods**: Epicurus's gods, who dwell in the *intermundia* and take no interest in human affairs, prefigure Nietzsche's own post-theistic vision — a cosmos without providence, without judgment, and without cosmic meaning.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Plato** — Epicurus is Nietzsche's ally against Platonism; where Plato projects reality into the supersensible, Epicurus roots it in the body and the senses.
- **Lucretius** — The Roman poet who transmitted Epicurean philosophy to the Latin world; Nietzsche admired *De Rerum Natura* for its poetic rendering of materialist philosophy.
- **Schopenhauer** — Though Schopenhauer was a pessimist and Epicurus an optimist about pleasure, both share a fundamental honesty about the nature of existence that Nietzsche values.
- **Stoics** — Nietzsche contrasts Epicurus favorably with the Stoics, whose "living according to Nature" he regards as a fraud of self-deception; Epicurus, by contrast, is honest about the bodily basis of his philosophy.
