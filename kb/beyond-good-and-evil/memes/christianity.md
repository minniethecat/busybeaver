# Christianity

## Overview

Christianity occupies the center of Nietzsche's critique of Western civilization. More than a religion, it is for Nietzsche a comprehensive system of valuation — a "slave morality" born of *ressentiment* that inverted the aristocratic values of antiquity and imposed them on Europe through a combination of psychological manipulation and institutional power. Nietzsche's relationship to Christianity is not that of a neutral observer but of a determined opponent who nevertheless acknowledges Christianity's achievement: it has "done more for the spiritualizing and deepening of the European soul than any other single force" (003-019).

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Nietzsche's most sustained engagement with Christianity in *Beyond Good and Evil* occurs in Part Three, "The Religious Mood." In aphorism 46, he analyzes the psychology of Christian faith, distinguishing early Christianity's "sincere, austere slave-faith" from the ironic skepticism of the late antique world (003-002). Faith, for Nietzsche, is not a form of knowledge but a form of will — "a continual suicide of reason" that the believer undertakes because it serves a psychological need.

In aphorism 49, Nietzsche offers an unexpected acknowledgment: "Perhaps nothing so admirable in Christianity and Buddhism as their art of teaching even the lowliest to place themselves through piety in an illusory higher order of things" (003-018). This is the ambivalence that characterizes Nietzsche's treatment of Christianity throughout: he recognizes its psychological power and its cultural achievements even as he condemns its fundamental orientation toward world-denial.

Aphorism 62 articulates Nietzsche's most concentrated critique of Christian morality: "The whole tragi-comedy of European morality" is that it has cultivated a subtle form of cruelty — "the cruelty against oneself" — that the Christian directs inward, transforming the will to power into self-torment (003-019). Christianity did not eliminate cruelty; it interiorized it, creating the bad conscience and the ascetic ideal.

## Core Doctrines

1. **Original sin and redemption**: The doctrine that humanity is fallen and requires divine grace for salvation — Nietzsche's paradigm of life-denial.
2. **The reversal of values**: Christianity inverted the aristocratic equation of "good = noble = powerful," making the weak, the poor, and the suffering the "blessed."
3. **The afterlife as true life**: The devaluation of earthly existence in favor of a transcendent hereafter — the metaphysical core of Christian Platonism.
4. **The sacrifice of the intellect**: Faith requires the believer to accept doctrines that contradict reason and the evidence of the senses.
5. **Universal love as disguised will to power**: Christian *agape* is, for Nietzsche, a subtle form of domination — the weak binding the strong through guilt.

## Key Figures

- **Jesus of Nazareth** — The "only true Christian" who died on the cross; Nietzsche's attitude toward Jesus is surprisingly respectful, distinct from his contempt for institutional Christianity.
- **Paul** — The "first Christian," the real founder of Christianity as a world-historical force; it was Paul who transformed Jesus's practice into a theology of sin and redemption.
- **Augustine** — The great synthesis of Platonism and Christianity; his *Confessions* exemplify the Christian interiorization of cruelty.
- **Blaise Pascal** — The modern Christian intellect whose "sacrifice of the intellect" Nietzsche finds both admirable and horrifying.
- **Martin Luther** — The reformer who revived Pauline Christianity at the expense of the Renaissance.

## Nietzsche's Critique

Nietzsche's critique of Christianity operates on multiple registers. Morally, Christianity is a "slave revolt in morality" — the successful attempt of the weak to impose their values on the strong by making strength itself a source of guilt. Psychologically, Christianity is a system for the management of *ressentiment*: it channels the aggressive instincts of the powerless inward, creating the "bad conscience" and the ascetic ideal. Metaphysically, Christianity is Platonism adapted for mass consumption — the invention of a "true world" that devalues the only world that exists.

Yet Nietzsche's critique is not a simple rejection. Christianity has "done more for the spiritualizing and deepening of the European soul than any other single force" — it has cultivated capacities of introspection, psychological nuance, and spiritual aspiration that would not otherwise exist. The task is not to simply abandon Christianity but to *overcome* it — to appropriate its spiritual achievements while discarding its life-denying core. This is what Nietzsche means by the "self-overcoming of Christianity" that he sees underway in modern atheism and the "death of God."
