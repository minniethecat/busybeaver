# Martin Luther

## Overview

Martin Luther (1483–1546) occupies a complex and largely negative position in Nietzsche's thought. As the instigator of the Protestant Reformation, Luther represents for Nietzsche a crucial turning point.

Luther's rebellion against the cultural authority of the Church paradoxically restored and intensified the very Christian morality it appeared to challenge. Nietzsche's Luther is a figure of immense psychological force — a man whose "slave-faith" proved capable of reshaping European civilization — but also of destructive simplicity.

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Luther appears in *Beyond Good and Evil* primarily in the context of Nietzsche's analysis of faith as a psychological phenomenon.

In aphorism 46, Nietzsche contrasts the "skeptical and southernly free-spirited world" of late antiquity with the "sincere, austere slave-faith by which perhaps a Luther or a Cromwell ... remained attached to his God" (003-002). Luther here represents the "northern" type — severe, sincere, incapable of ironic detachment.

In aphorism 48, Nietzsche deepens this characterization: "The different kinds of it, like that of Luther — the whole of Protestantism lacks the SOUTH" (003-007). Protestantism, with Luther as its archetype, lacks the grace and aesthetic sensibility of the Catholic south.

Luther's faith is powerful but crude; his revolt liberated the German spirit from ecclesiastical authority only to impose a more austere form of Christianity.

In aphorism 247, Nietzsche makes a striking comparative judgment: "Compared with Luther's Bible, almost everything else is literature" (008-008). This acknowledges Luther's translation as a literary achievement of the first rank. Luther's linguistic genius created the modern German language, even if the content of his message was, for Nietzsche, a disaster.

## Key Ideas

1. **The Reformation as incomplete revolt**: Luther broke from the Church but intensified Christianity; he replaced institutional authority with the authority of conscience, which for Nietzsche is an even more effective form of internalized control.
2. **Northern severity vs. Southern grace**: Luther embodies the "northern" spiritual type — sincere, austere, humorless — contrasted with the sophisticated skepticism of the Catholic south.
3. **The psychological power of faith**: Luther's faith was not a gentle piety but a form of "slave-faith" that proved capable of world-historical effects precisely because of its intensity.
4. **Language as compensation**: Luther's translation of the Bible was a monumental literary achievement, creating modern German prose — a reminder that even those Nietzsche opposes intellectually may produce works of enduring value.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Blaise Pascal** — Pascal represents for Nietzsche a more refined version of the Christian type that Luther inaugurated; both exemplify "sacrifice of the intellect" in the service of faith.
- **Auguste Comte** — Nietzsche compares Comte's sociology, unfavorably, to Jesuitism, which in turn he links to Luther's Protestant severity as another form of northern spiritual imperialism.
- **Richard Wagner** — Wagner's aesthetic appropriation of Protestant chorale and his later "Parsifal" Christianity continue, for Nietzsche, the Lutheran trajectory.
- **Arthur Schopenhauer** — Schopenhauer's atheistic pessimism retains, for Nietzsche, a "half-Christian" character that Luther's Reformation helped establish as the default German spiritual mode.
