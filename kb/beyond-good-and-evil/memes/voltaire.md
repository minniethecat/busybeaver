# Voltaire

## Overview

Voltaire (1694–1778) represents for Nietzsche the type of the aristocratic free spirit — a man of wit, elegance, and intellectual courage who waged war against the Church and superstition without succumbing to the democratic leveling instincts of the Revolution his work helped inspire. Nietzsche dedicated *Human, All Too Human* (1878) to Voltaire, and though his later works move beyond Voltairean rationalism, the figure of Voltaire remains an important precursor to Nietzsche's own conception of the "good European."

## In *Beyond Good and Evil*

Voltaire appears only briefly in *Beyond Good and Evil*, but his presence is felt throughout. Nietzsche's ideal of the philosopher as a "free, VERY free spirit" — skeptical but not paralyzed, critical but not resentful — is Voltairean in its outlines, though Nietzsche radicalizes it far beyond anything Voltaire would have recognized. Voltaire's *écrasez l'infâme* ("crush the infamous thing" — the Church) anticipates Nietzsche's own war against the Platonic-Christian moral inheritance.

In aphorism 35, Nietzsche cites Voltaire's famous remark that "if God did not exist it would be necessary to invent him" and turns it against Voltaire himself: "Voltaire himself believed in God, didn't he?" The real free spirit, Nietzsche implies, would not need the hypothesis.

## Key Ideas

1. **Enlightenment rationalism**: Voltaire's commitment to reason and toleration, which Nietzsche partly admires and partly critiques as insufficiently radical.
2. **Anti-clericalism**: The war against the Church, which Nietzsche extends into a war against all otherworldly metaphysics.
3. **Aristocratic taste**: Voltaire's elegance and wit, which Nietzsche contrasts with the solemnity of German philosophy.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- **Rousseau** — Voltaire's great antagonist; Nietzsche shares Voltaire's contempt for Rousseau's sentimental egalitarianism.
- **Frederick the Great** — Voltaire's patron and correspondent; the model of the philosopher-courtier.
- **Diderot** — Fellow Encyclopedist; Nietzsche groups them as representatives of Enlightenment *esprit*.
