# Mrs. Arbuthnot

## Overview

Harriet Arbuthnot (1793–1834) was an English society hostess and diarist. She was the wife of Charles Arbuthnot, a Tory politician and close associate of the Duke of Wellington. In Ellenborough's diary, she appears as a central figure in Wellington's intimate circle, providing the Duke with political intelligence and serving as a social conduit between leading Tories.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough first encounters the Arbuthnots at a dinner with Lady Sandwich, where he had "a long" conversation with them (P01-141). Charles Arbuthnot appears frequently at the India Board, coming "to speak about his friend" (007-030) and inquiring whether "Lord Rosslyn would be cordial with us" (007-046).

Mrs. Arbuthnot's proximity to Wellington is evident when Hardinge relayed that "the Duke told Mrs. Arbuthnot I spoke very well last night" (023-126) — showing she served as the Duke's informal sounding board after parliamentary debates.

Ellenborough also records walking "as far as Mrs. Arbuthnot's with the Duke," during which Wellington confided "his view of the" political situation (033-157). The Arbuthnots were at the centre of Tory political society, with Wortley reporting that "Arbuthnot told him the Duke had read the evidence" on key questions (028-040).

## Key Appearances

- **Social introduction**: Ellenborough dined with Lady Sandwich and met the Arbuthnots for an extended conversation (P01-141).
- **Wellington's confidante**: The Duke used Mrs. Arbuthnot as an informal channel, relaying his assessment of Ellenborough's parliamentary performance (023-126).
- **Political intermediary**: Charles Arbuthnot acted as intermediary between Wellington and other political figures, conveying views and intelligence (007-030, 028-040).
