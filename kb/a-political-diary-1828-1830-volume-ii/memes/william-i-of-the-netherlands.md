**William I** (1772–1843) was King of the Netherlands and Grand Duke of Luxembourg from 1815 until his abdication in 1840. The diary captures him during the [[Belgian Revolution]] of 1830, when the southern provinces of his kingdom rose in revolt and declared independence as Belgium.

== The Belgian Crisis ==

The diary records the British government's involvement in the Belgian crisis. Wellington's ministry wrestled with the question of how to advise the King of the Netherlands during the rebellion. The Duke of Wellington, as [[Lord Rosslyn]] noted, was "declining to advise the King of the Netherlands." (043-155) This reflected the British position of cautious neutrality, avoiding direct interference while the great powers conferred.

The French government's stance complicated matters further. The diary notes communication with "the French Government that the King of the Netherlands" was being pressed by all sides. (046-111) The prospect of Belgium submitting back to the King seemed increasingly remote: "submission of Belgium to the King of the Netherlands. It may be" impossible. (046-127)

== Diplomacy and Personal Appeal ==

William I made direct appeals to the British government. The diarist records: "I read the King of the Netherlands' letter. He asks distinctly for" support. (047-140) He also convened his domestic political institutions: "The King of the Netherlands has called his States at the" Hague. (048-129)

By late 1830, the King's position had deteriorated. He was "much annoyed at the desertion" of allies. (049-010) His public statements reflected his frustration: "Read in the newspaper the King of the Netherlands' speech. It is querulous" in tone. (049-056)

== Personal Assessment ==

The diarist offered a sympathetic personal judgment: "I pity the King of the Netherlands, who is a good man. To be hated" by those he sought to govern struck Ellenborough as unjust. (050-063) This rare note of personal sympathy in the diary underscores the human dimension of the diplomatic crisis.
