[004-001]There are disturbances at Manchester, which look rather serious.

[004-002]_May 7._

[004-003]Nothing in the House.

[004-004]The meeting respecting the statue to the Duke of Wellington seems to have

[004-005]finished in detestable taste. Hunt proposing a vote of thanks to Lord

[004-006]Anglesey and O'Connell, and _Lord Darnley!_ speaking for it. Both these

[004-007]said the Catholic Bill arose out of Lord Anglesey's Government. Lord

[004-008]Darnley repeated the same thing to me to-day in the House. I told him the

[004-009]contrary was the fact. That Lord Anglesey had placed the carrying of the

[004-010]question in peril--that without his recall it could hardly have been

[004-011]carried.

[004-012]There have been serious disturbances at Manchester. The bakers' shops have

[004-013]been broken open and robbed, and money extorted by fear. This arises out of

[004-014]real distress; but it seems, as might be expected, that notorious thieves

[004-015]lead on the mobs.

[004-016]_May 8._

[004-017]The disturbances at Manchester have more the character of robbery than of

[004-018]riot. Baker's shops have been broken open and pillaged, and money has been

[004-019]extorted.

[004-020]At Rochdale an attack was made on the military. They behaved with extreme

[004-021]forbearance; but at last fired, and killed and wounded many.

[004-022]_May 9._

[004-023]Dined at the Trinity House. Hardinge, whom I met there, told me Wood had

[004-024]been asked by Lord Mansfield to go to the Pitt dinner on the 28th. Wood

[004-025]said he did not know whether the Ministers would go or not. Lord Mansfield

[004-026]said, 'Why, you must know, it is understood that as soon as Parliament is

[004-027]up the Government will be changed. At this dinner we shall make such a

[004-028]display of Protestant force as will enable the King to take us as his

[004-029]Ministers.'

[004-030]It is surprising to me that any able man as Lord Mansfield is should be so

[004-031]deluded by the lies of the Duke of Cumberland. The country is not agitated,

[004-032]it is not dissatisfied. It would repudiate, as an act of the basest

[004-033]treachery, such conduct towards a Government which had been permitted to

[004-034]carry a great measure, and which was displaced solely on grounds of

[004-035]personal pique.

[004-036]Manchester and its neighbourhood more quiet.

[004-037]Had some conversation with Peel about the next member for the direction. He

[004-038]inclines to Marryatt. Hardinge reported a communication from E. Ellice, who

[004-039]canvasses for his brother, Russell Ellice. E. Ellice offers some votes in

[004-040]the House of Commons if we will support his brother.

[004-041]I believe E. Ellice would be a good man, but the brother is a nonentity. I

[004-042]said we must strike at the mass and not at individuals. We must gain the

[004-043]city by assisting a fit man on public grounds. Peel agreed in this

[004-044]sentiment. I am sure it is the only wise course for any Government to

[004-045]pursue.

[004-046]_Monday, May 11._

[004-047]The King has got the habit of taking large doses of laudanum. He sent for

[004-048]the Chancellor yesterday, as usual, at two o'clock. When he got to the

[004-049]palace the King had taken a large dose of laudanum and was asleep. The

[004-050]Chancellor was told he would not wake for two or three hours, and would

[004-051]then be in a state of excessive irritation, so that he might just as well

[004-052]not see him.

[004-053]_May 12._

[004-054]The East Retford question was last night deferred till next session, so we

[004-055]may, I think, finish all our business by about June 10; that is really

[004-056]allowing full time.

[004-057]O'Connell published yesterday an argument on his right to sit in the House

[004-058]of Commons in the shape of a letter to the members. At first Lord Grey

[004-059]thought it unanswerable (as founded on the provisions of the Relief Bill);

[004-060]but at night he told me he had looked into the Bill and found it certainly

[004-061]excluded him. A large portion of the letter is quite absurd, that in which

[004-062]he assumes a right to have his claim decided in a court of law. Parliament

[004-063]alone is by common law the court in which the privileges of its own members

[004-064]can be decided.

[004-065]_May 12._

[004-066]House. Lord Lansdowne put a pompously worded question as to our intentions

[004-067]with respect to the course of proceeding on Indian affairs.

[004-068]I answered simply that we were as sensible as he was of the extreme

[004-069]importance of the question. That for my own part my mind was never absent

[004-070]from it, and that I had not been many days in office before I took measures

[004-071]for procuring the most extensive information, which would be laid before

[004-072]the House at the proper time. That the Government was desirous of forming

[004-073]its own opinion on the fullest information and with the greatest

[004-074]consideration; and that we wished the House to have the same opportunities.

[004-075]That I was not then prepared to inform him in what precise form we should

[004-076]propose that the enquiry should be made.

[004-077]The Chancellor introduced the Bill for appointing a new Equity Judge, and

[004-078]separating the Equity Jurisdiction from the Court of Exchequer. The latter

[004-079]object, by-the-bye, is not to be accomplished immediately, but it is part

[004-080]of the plan opened. He soothed Lord Eldon by high compliments to his

[004-081]judicial administration and to the correctness of his judgments. The wonder

[004-082]of the day is that Lord Eldon should have lived to hear a Chancellor so

[004-083]expose the errors of the Court of Chancery as they were exposed by Lord

[004-084]Lyndhurst to-day.

[004-085]_May 13._

[004-086]Recorder's report. The King not well. He has a slight stricture, of which

[004-087]he makes a great deal, and a bad cold. He seemed somnolent; but I have seen

[004-088]him worse.

[004-089]Before the Council there was a chapter of the Garter. The Duke of Richmond

[004-090]was elected. The knights wore their ordinary dress under the robe, which

[004-091]was short, and had no hats. The procession was formed by Garter. The

[004-092]Chancellor and Prelate of the Order and the Dean were present. It looked

[004-093]rather like a splendid funeral. The Duke of Cumberland took a great deal

[004-094]upon him.

[004-095]Cabinet dinner at Vesey Fitzgerald's at Somerset House.

[004-096]Much talk about Indian matters. Both Peel and Fitzgerald seem to be for

[004-097]Free Trade, and _unreasonable_ towards the Company.

[004-098]_May 15._

[004-099]In the House of Commons yesterday the motion for a Committee on East Indian

[004-100]affairs was negatived without a division, but promised for _early_ next

[004-101]session, and papers promised immediately.

[004-102]_May 16._

[004-103]Chairs at 11. We spoke of the Charter. They rather dislike the notion of

[004-104]using the King's name, and I fear Mr. Elphinstone and all the Indians will

[004-105]give their evidence against the change. I may be outvoted, but I shall not

[004-106]be convinced. [Footnote: This change was effected in 1858.]

[004-107]_May 17._

[004-108]Nothing political, except a grand dinner at the Duke of Norfolk's, given to

[004-109]the Duke of Wellington, which was very fine and very dull.

[004-110]The Duke told me he had read the Persian papers. The Russians had brought

[004-111]it on themselves.

[004-112]_May 19._

[004-113]In the House of Commons last night O'Connell was heard at the bar. The

[004-114]debate seems to have been temperate. It was decided on a discussion, 190 to

[004-115]116, that he must take the Oath of Supremacy.

[004-116]At the office had some conversation with Mr. Leach as to the plan of

[004-117]governing India in the King's name--the Directors being made ex officio

[004-118]Commissioners for the affairs of India. He seems to have some prejudices

[004-119]against the plan, but he adduced no real objections. I have begged him to

[004-120]put on paper all the objections which occurred to him.

[004-121]Wrote a long letter to Lord W. Bentinck on all subjects connected with the

[004-122]renewal of the Charter, and the general government of India.

[004-123]Dined at the Freemasons' Hall with the Society for Promoting Christian

[004-124]Knowledge. There were present 200 persons. I thought they would be very

[004-125]hostile to a Minister. However, when my name was mentioned by the Bishop of

[004-126]Durham, as a steward, there was much cheering. The Bishop of London, who

[004-127]was in the chair, begged me to return thanks for the stewards, which I did.

[004-128]I spoke of course of the wish entertained by the Ministers that a Society

[004-129]might prosper the interests of which were so much connected with those of

[004-130]the Established Church--of their determination in their several departments

[004-131]to further its objects. It was the duty of us all as Christians, but more

[004-132]peculiarly that of the Ministers, to advance objects intimately connected

[004-133]with the individual happiness of the people and with the stability of the

[004-134]State. I said something too of the intrinsic strength of the Protestant

[004-135]Church--of its rising in proportion to the difficulties which might

[004-136]surround it, to the dangers--if dangers there were (the Primate had spoken

[004-137]of them)--of its security in the zeal and ability of its ministers, and in

[004-138]the purity of its doctrines.

[004-139]On the whole I did well. I was loudly cheered--indeed, so much interrupted

[004-140]as to be enabled to think what I should say next.

[004-141]Indian business in the morning--Coal Committee.

[004-142]_May 20._

[004-143]Dined at the London Tavern with the Directors, at what is called a family

[004-144]dinner, to meet Mr. Elphinstone, the late Governor of Bombay. He has been

[004-145]thirty-three years absent from England, having left it at fifteen. He is

[004-146]one of the most distinguished servants the Company has ever had. He seems

[004-147]to be a quiet, mild, temperate man. I had some conversation with him, and

[004-148]have fixed that he should come to the Indian Board on Tuesday. I wish to

[004-149]have his opinion as to the expediency of governing India in the King's

[004-150]name.

[004-151]The Duke told Lord Bathurst and me the King had been very angry with him

[004-152]for going to the Duke of Norfolk's dinner, and now openly expressed his

[004-153]wish to get rid of his Ministers. The Duke wrote to the King and told him

[004-154]it really was not a subject he thought it necessary to speak to him about,

[004-155]that he dined with everybody and asked everybody to dinner, that had he

[004-156]known beforehand who were to dine with the Duke of Norfolk, which he did

[004-157]not, he could not have objected to any one of them. That the King himself

[004-158]had dined with the Duke of Norfolk. That most of the persons invited were

[004-159]either in his Majesty's service, or had been.

[004-160]It seems the king desired it might be intimated to the Duke that he was

[004-161]much displeased at the dinner, and that he and Cumberland damned us all.

[004-162]I told the Duke and Lord Bathurst what occurred at the dinner yesterday,

[004-163]with which they were much gratified.

[004-164]_May 21._

[004-165]Went to the Cabinet room at 2. Read papers, by which it seems that the

[004-166]Russian army is very little stronger than at the commencement of the last

[004-167]campaign, and that its materials are not so good. It has as yet no medical

[004-168]staff. The resources of the principalities are exhausted; the cattle of the

[004-169]peasants have been put in requisition; the ordinary cultivation of the land

[004-170]has been neglected. The river is worse than last year. There are reports of

[004-171]the successes of the Turks near Varna, and of that place being in danger.

[004-172]The recruiting of the Turkish army goes on well.

[004-173]House of Lords. The Chancellor's Bill, which creates a new Chancery judge.

[004-174]Opposition from Lord Eldon, Lord Redesdale, and Lord Holland, all saying

[004-175]they wished to see the whole plan before they agree to a part. Lord

[004-176]Tenterden approved of the making of the new judge, but wished his functions

[004-177]had been better defined.

[004-178]The Duke of Cumberland said the Non-contents had it; but he said it too

[004-179]late, and his people did not wish to divide.

[004-180]Lord Londonderry would have voted against us. I fear he is half mad. The

[004-181]House seems to treat him so.

[004-182]The Chancellor told me the King did many things personally uncivil to the

[004-183]Duke. He did not ask him to dinner to meet the Duke of Orleans. He wishes

[004-184]to force the Duke to offer his resignation. This he is much too prudent to

[004-185]do upon a mere personal pique.
