# Henry Brougham

## Overview

Henry Brougham (1778–1868), later 1st Baron Brougham and Vaux, was a prominent Whig politician, barrister, and reformer. During the period covered by Ellenborough's diary (1828–1830), Brougham was one of the most active and controversial figures in the House of Commons, appearing frequently in debates and attracting the attention — and often exasperation — of the Tory diarist.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough's diary records Brougham as a persistent and frequently disruptive presence in parliamentary proceedings. Brougham was seen as a figure whose support could be courted — Ellenborough notes that "Brougham may be quieted" (023-119) — but whose behaviour was unpredictable. Peel reportedly "thinks Brougham really rather mad, and would not be surprised to hear of his being in a strait waistcoat" (037-044), a remark that captures the Tory view of Brougham's volatility.

Brougham's interventions spanned multiple domains. He attacked Lord Conyngham, the Lord Steward, "for not being in readiness to swear in the House of Commons" (034-167), and made "a violent speech" on the matter. On another occasion he was described as "half frantic" and made "rather an apologetic speech for his attack upon the Lord Steward" (035-050). At a debate on East India Company affairs, Brougham "ridiculed the Directors who sat there in a mass, nine of them" (033-095), challenging their right to vote on questions in which they held a direct interest.

Brougham's alliance with other opposition figures appears repeatedly. He spoke alongside "Mackintosh, and that ass, M. A. Taylor" in "reprobation" of the government's handling of a private letter (022-134). His electoral success caused Ellenborough to lament: "I lamented Brougham's success in Yorkshire, and viewed with some" concern its implications (040-146).

## Key Appearances

- **Opposition speechmaking**: Made a violent speech against the Lord Steward and frequently challenged government positions in the Commons (034-167, 035-050).
- **East India debate**: Ridiculed the Directors of the East India Company sitting in a mass, questioning their impartiality (033-095).
- **Under the throne**: At a major debate, "Peel and Brougham were under the throne" together, a notable juxtaposition of the two leading parliamentary figures (036-079).
- **Yorkshire election**: His electoral success in Yorkshire was viewed with alarm by Tories (040-146).
- **Alleged intoxication**: Ellenborough recorded that "it seems Brougham was almost drunk" during one parliamentary sitting (036-147).
