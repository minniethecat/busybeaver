[039-001]We then came to the consideration of the East Retford question. All the

[039-002]press were for giving up the Bill. I took some part in the discussion.

[039-003]However, Peel was so strongly for the Lords going as the Commons had done,

[039-004]and for preventing the appearance of disunion in the Cabinet, that his

[039-005]wishes were acceded to, and we support the Bill. The Duke _thinks_ it will

[039-006]be thrown out, and I _hope_ it will. It will be very difficult to make a

[039-007]speech in favour of the Bill which will not commit us to a bad precedent.

[039-008]However, I shall try. Peel was very obstinate and disagreeable. In fact the

[039-009]interfering with the existing franchise never was made a Cabinet question.

[039-010]The giving the franchise to Bassetlaw [Footnote: The Hundred of Bassetlaw,

[039-011]forming the existing borough of East Retford.] rather than to Birmingham

[039-012]was, and it was because after an agreement that we should all vote for

[039-013]Bassetlaw, Huskisson voted for Birmingham and then resigned, that the

[039-014]separation took place.

[039-015]These questions never were made Government questions before, and it is much

[039-016]better they should not be.

[039-017]Peel thinks he will not be able to oppose reform in general if we do not

[039-018]show a disposition to punish individual cases of corruption.

[039-019]I did not get away till seven, and then went to Hardinge's to bring him

[039-020]down to Wilderness. [Footnote: Seat of Lord Camden, near Sevenoaks.] He

[039-021]told me the Speaker had been with the Duke and did not resign just now.

[039-022]There had been a question whether he should not; but it was thought we

[039-023]might be damaged at the elections if we made any change now. The Duke asked

[039-024]Hardinge what he thought as to taking Huskisson and Palmerston back again?

[039-025]Hardinge declared against having Huskisson, but recommended Palmerston. I

[039-026]dare say as soon as the elections are over something will be done, and that

[039-027]Palmerston will be offered the Chancellorship of the Exchequer.

[039-028]Peel once wanted Edward Stanley, but it seems he has wavered a good deal.

[039-029]Unless his manner should change it would be impossible to go on with him as

[039-030]Minister; but I trust in God we shall never lose the Duke.

[039-031]_July 19._

[039-032]Received at nine a card from Lord Bathurst informing me that the Queen

[039-033]would be in Downing Street at ten. Went in plain clothes as I was desired.

[039-034]Found the Queen was to be there to see the Guards, whom the King was to

[039-035]inspect. The Ministers were invited and the connections of the Bathursts.

[039-036]We were presented to the Queen, and kissed her hand. After the parade,

[039-037]which the King attended on foot, he joined the party, and they had

[039-038]breakfast. However, before that I went away. At one again at St. James's.

[039-039]The two Universities came up with addresses to the King and Queen. Oxford

[039-040]first. They very properly put their doctors first. The address was read by

[039-041]the Vice-Chancellor, and then, after the Queen's reply, the doctors and

[039-042]proctors, and a few others who formed the deputation, kissed the King's

[039-043]hand. As the Queen has no separate apartment the King retired, the Queen

[039-044]entered with her household and ladies, and then the same ceremony was gone

[039-045]through, the Ministers remaining on the left behind the ladies. The Queen

[039-046]read pretty well. She was obliged to rise each time to give her hand to be

[039-047]kissed. Cambridge came afterwards with the Duke of Gloucester and all the

[039-048]Peers, who belonged to the University, in their gowns at the head. This

[039-049]destroyed the character of the collegiate body. However, those only were

[039-050]presented who were presented of the Oxford deputation. The King went beyond

[039-051]his written speech to the men of Cambridge, and put us in a fright.

[039-052]However, it was good-humoured, and of no great harm--a sort of joke.

[039-053]I came away as I had business. Afterwards there was a Council, and the

[039-054]Lords Lieutenant were admitted to take the oaths.

[039-055]House. East Retford. The Chancellor made a capital speech, and we had a

[039-056]better division than case, 29 to 7. Lord Durham spoke temperately and well.

[039-057]Lord Grey well too. We had Wynford with us. There is no explaining that

[039-058]man. The Duke of Cumberland voted against us, and Eldon spoke.

[039-059]At St. James's. Lord Westmoreland told me that yesterday at a great dinner

[039-060]the King gave his household he gave as a toast, 'The land we live in, and

[039-061]let those who don't like it leave it.'

[039-062]This and many other things show his feelings towards the Duke of

[039-063]Cumberland.

[039-064]The King reviews a regiment every morning this week. He has been on

[039-065]horseback within these six weeks, but he has a rupture, and is now rather

[039-066]afraid of riding. He is going to change the uniforms of the Lords

[039-067]Lieutenant.

[039-068]We expect to prorogue on Friday and dissolve on Saturday.

[039-069]_July 20._

[039-070]Then East Retford. Lord Wharncliffe moved a resolution with the view of

[039-071]giving the franchise to Birmingham instead of the Hundred. Dudley spoke for

[039-072]Birmingham and well. I spoke shortly. I guarded myself against being

[039-073]considered as pledged to any other measure, intending to decide all

[039-074]measures according to the special circumstances of the case.

[039-075]The Duke was not so cautious as I was, and spoke strongly against giving

[039-076]the franchise to great towns. [Footnote: No one expected it to occur in two

[039-077]years' time.] Lord Holland said to the Chancellor, 'He will live to see it

[039-078]done.' I think I may, and therefore was cautious.

[039-079]We had 39 to 16.

[039-080]So ends the business of this Session.

[039-081]_July 21._

[039-082]Went at ten to the Duke of Wellington's, where the King and Queen were to

[039-083]breakfast after an inspection of the 2nd Life Guards. The day was beautiful

[039-084]and the people in excellent humour. The King first went with the Queen to

[039-085]the Regent's Park barracks, and then to the Knightsbridge barracks. When

[039-086]they came to the Duke's the King went to the window and was well cheered.

[039-087]They then called for the Queen, who went to the window and was very well

[039-088]received indeed.

[039-089]Yesterday evening the King walked out alone into St. James's Street. He

[039-090]found Watson Taylor and took his arm. The mob pressed upon him so much that

[039-091]Watson Taylor's shoes were trodden down at heel. While the King was alone

[039-092]an Irish woman came out of an alley and kissed him. This and a lecture from

[039-093]the Duke have cured him of walking out alone. At least he has promised not

[039-094]to do so again.

[039-095]House at 2. Aberdeen says the King spoke very well to the foreign Ministers

[039-096]to-day. There was an extraordinary number of naval officers, and the

[039-097]fullest _levée_ I ever saw. The King recognised very cordially all his old

[039-098]friends. He was very gracious indeed to Elphinstone, whom he saw for the

[039-099]first time. He was imprudent enough to make a sort of speech to the West

[039-100]Indian deputation, and pledged himself warmly to support their interests.

[039-101]This I saw. After I was gone I hear Astell and Campbell came up with the

[039-102]address of the East India Company, and that he spoke in similar terms to

[039-103]them. This the foolish Astell will publish everywhere.

[039-104]The Duke says he goes away when the King begins to speak. I really covered

[039-105]my face when he began to speak about the Catholics to the deputation from

[039-106]Cambridge. What he said to them, which was no more than an indifferent

[039-107]joke, has been variously misrepresented and not at all understood. It must

[039-108]have been imperfectly heard.

[039-109]The King is angry with the Duke of Gloucester for slurring over a part of

[039-110]the address from Cambridge, which was very loyal, and for not kissing his

[039-111]hand. He has reason to complain of this. The Duke of Gloucester kissed the

[039-112]Queen's hand with marked devotion.

[039-113]The Duke of Sussex has been already infusing poison into the King's ear and

[039-114]talking of invasions of the property of the Church. This the King told

[039-115]Peel. Those who observed the Duke of Sussex at the levee thought he seemed

[039-116]very triumphant, and received his Whig friends with a smile which said, 'We

[039-117]shall do them yet.'

[039-118]He was invested with the Thistle to-day. The King asked all the knights

[039-119]presented to drink a bottle of claret with him in October.

[039-120]Blomberg was up with an address. The King said, 'You and I know each other

[039-121]of old. You need not be presented. By-the-bye, you may as well dine with me

[039-122]to-day.'

[039-123]The King made an extemporaneous reply to the address of the Canons of

[039-124]Windsor the day after the funeral. They begged to have a copy. He

[039-125]endeavoured to recollect it for them, and sent it to Peel. Peel found some

[039-126]curious historical inaccuracies.

[039-127]The Duke of Wellington thinks we shall gradually bring the King round, and

[039-128]induce him to move more quietly. To thwart him directly would have a bad

[039-129]effect; but he may be led. In the meantime he is very well in health.

[039-130]The King has promised to dine with Leopold, who has asked the Duke, but not

[039-131]Aberdeen. The Duke thinks the King should not dine with him now. The two

[039-132]other Powers having manifested the greatest dissatisfaction with Leopold's

[039-133]conduct, and we having intimated it in the House, it would be incongruous

[039-134]and injurious for the King to dine with him. Leopold has written one if not

[039-135]two letters complaining of the conduct of the Allied Powers.

[039-136]We went to the House for fear Lord Durham should play us a trick, and it is

[039-137]perhaps fortunate we did, for he was there and made a protesting speech,

[039-138]which was followed by one from Westmoreland on the East Retford Bill.

[039-139]However, we had a majority in the House, and there was no division.

[039-140]_July 22._

[039-141]Rode to town. Cabinet. Considered the King's Speech. Peel had introduced a

[039-142]plagiarism from the first speech of the old King, 'Born and educated in

[039-143]this country, I glory in the name of Briton.' However, the whole sentence

[039-144]would not do, and it was omitted. I assisted in working the sentences into

[039-145]form, and breaking them up into short ones. Went away to dress for the

[039-146]Council, thinking the whole settled. Council at three. First the deputies

[039-147]of the two Houses carried up the joint address respecting Sir Jonah

[039-148]Barrington. Then the King being alone, and saying he was ready for his

[039-149]Ministers--none being there but me--I went in, and first asked him to allow

[039-150]Clare to wear the uniform the late King gave him. This led to a long talk

[039-151]about uniforms for Indian Governors, and I had some little difficulty to

[039-152]carry my coat without having a general consideration of the whole question

[039-153]of Governor's uniforms. I then told the King of the approaching death of

[039-154]Sir J. Macdonald. He asked whom we proposed sending in his place? I told

[039-155]him it did not entirely depend upon the King's Ministers, but that I

[039-156]thought, if we recommended a very fit man, we should get the Chairs to name

[039-157]him.

[039-158]The King said, 'You heard what I said to the East India Company yesterday?'

[039-159]I had not, but I bowed, and he added, 'I told them they should not be

[039-160]unfairly dealt with. There is a run on them, and the notions of people are

[039-161]very much exaggerated with regard to the question.'

[039-162]I said the question would require and receive the most mature consideration

[039-163]from his Ministers before they ventured to offer any advice to his Majesty

[039-164]upon the course to be pursued.

[039-165]The King said in about ten or twelve days he should be able to give me a

[039-166]day or two for Indian matters.

[039-167]I thought I had given time to the others to arrive, and rose. I should

[039-168]mention that he spoke of Algiers, and said he suspected there was an

[039-169]understanding about it between the Russians and the French.
