[009-001]_July 23._

[009-002]Chairs at eleven. Told them of the danger in which they were, from the

[009-003]feeling of the mercantile districts and of the country; that we could not

[009-004]look Parliament in the face without having done all in our power to effect

[009-005]reductions in a deficit of 800,000£ a year; that without a commanding case

[009-006]no Government, however strong, could venture to propose a renewal of the

[009-007]monopoly.

[009-008]They were obliged to me for my information. I advised them to turn their

[009-009]attention immediately to all the great points.

[009-010]On the subject of the six regiments the Court differ from the view I took.

[009-011]Loch gave me a long statement of facts, which I must read attentively, and

[009-012]then communicate with the Duke.

[009-013]They are so enamoured of old habits that they hesitate about desiring their

[009-014]Indian Governments and the subordinate correspondents of these Governments

[009-015]to place upon the back of their voluminous letters a _précis_ of their

[009-016]substance!

[009-017]After the Chairs were gone I saw Bankes and Leach, and while they were with

[009-018]me Sir Archibald Campbell called. I saw him immediately. He is a fat,

[009-019]rather intelligent-looking man, well mannered, and sensible. I talked to

[009-020]him of the idea of exchanging Tenasserim. [Footnote: The furthest province

[009-021]of the British territory towards Siam, extending along the coast south of

[009-022]Pegu, and lately conquered from the Burmese Empire.] He did not like giving

[009-023]up his conquest. I gave him one secret letter, and he will make his

[009-024]observations upon it.

[009-025]He left Lord William at the mouth of the Hooghly. They had found out the

[009-026]removal of the Government was contrary to law. They had intended to be

[009-027]itinerant for a year or two.

[009-028]It is only in the Bengal army that the officers are old. There they rise by

[009-029]seniority. In the Madras army they are made from fitness.

[009-030]The Madras army, though most gallant, was quite unequal, from deficiency of

[009-031]physical strength, to face the Burmese. The Burmese soldiers brought

[009-032]fourteen days' provisions. All men are liable to be called upon. They never

[009-033]had more than 120,000 in the field.

[009-034]The English army took 2,000 cannon, and it was believed the Burmese had

[009-035]2,500 left.

[009-036]Sir A. Campbell says there have been 60,000 refugees from Ava--all now

[009-037]settled in Tenasserim. I had thought there had never been more than 10,000,

[009-038]and that some, about half, had returned.

[009-039]Upon the whole, he seems enamoured of his conquests, but he did not adduce

[009-040]any good reason against exchanging it.

[009-041]At the Cabinet room. Saw Lord Rosslyn there, as I used to be last year,

[009-042]_désoeuvré_ and bored, as all Privy Seals will be. He seemed dissatisfied

[009-043]with the state of affairs in Ireland and in England. At Manchester there is

[009-044]a fear of a turn-out of some more cotton-spinners. Every thing depends upon

[009-045]the harvest.

[009-046]The negotiations with the Turks came to nothing. The Grand Vizier's answer

[009-047]to Diebitch is excellent.

[009-048]The sickness amongst the Russian troops continues, and Diebitch has not

[009-049]more than 40,000 men, even with Roth's corps.

[009-050]The Ambassadors have been very well received at Constantinople. All are in

[009-051]good humour there, notwithstanding the losses near Shumla.

[009-052]The Emperor does not go to the army.

[009-053]Lord Heytesbury represents Russia as being the least formidable of the

[009-054]great Powers for the purpose of offensive operations, and seems to think

[009-055]she contains many elements of convulsion.

[009-056]Metternich is trying to cajole the Russians by pretended fears of

[009-057]revolutionary principles.

[009-058]They talk of a King in Columbia, and the French are intriguing to place a

[009-059]French prince on the throne, after Bolivar.

[009-060]_July 25, 1829._

[009-061]Cabinet room. The Ambassadors seem to have been received most cordially at

[009-062]Constantinople. We know no more of the Grand Vizier's losses. That he

[009-063]experienced a complete defeat there can be no doubt.

[009-064]In Columbia, the French seem rather inclined to place, after Bolivar, a

[009-065]Prince of the House of Orleans on the throne, and it does not seem unlikely

[009-066]that the Columbians may consider it their best arrangement.

[009-067]The Emperor of Russia seems to be desirous of Peru, and the King of Prussia

[009-068]has, at his request, sent the Baron von Müffling as his Minister to the

[009-069]Porte to mediate.

[009-070]The Irish accounts are very bad. Lord F. Leveson seems now to think very

[009-071]seriously of the state of things. Doherty is come back much alarmed from

[009-072]Barris, where he has been with Blackie on a special commission.

[009-073]_July 28._

[009-074]I recommended to the attention of the Chairs the establishment of steam

[009-075]communication with India by the Red Sea.

[009-076]_July 29._

[009-077]Read _précis_ relative to Kotah.

[009-078]These _précis_ will make me thoroughly acquainted with the history and

[009-079]circumstances of the Rajpoot States, which are by far more interesting than

[009-080]others.

[009-081]There is a looseness and a vulgarity in the East India House writing, the

[009-082]literature of clerks which is quite disgusting. Our clerks write better

[009-083]than theirs, but they do not write concisely and correctly.

[009-084]_July 30._

[009-085]Read Lord Heytesbury's letters. He is very Russian. They have certainly got

[009-086]the plague at Odessa, and in all the stations of the Russian army.

[009-087]Met Peel at the Cabinet room. He said Ireland was in rather a better state.

[009-088]He agreed with me in thinking the Brunswickers were the cause of all the

[009-089]mischief. He believed the King had begged the Duke of Cumberland to stay,

[009-090]and that the Duchess was certainly coming over. They wish to attack the

[009-091]Ministry through the side of Ireland--to make a civil war rather than not

[009-092]turn out a Government.

[009-093]He had written to the Duke suggesting that we ought to have a Cabinet

[009-094]respecting Ireland, and he thought the Duke would come to town on his

[009-095]letter.

[009-096]_August 1._

[009-097]Had from Sir G. Murray papers relative to the Canada question, upon which

[009-098]he wishes to have the opinion of the Cabinet to-morrow. The immediate

[009-099]question is whether a Bill passed by the Colonial Legislature for altering

[009-100]the state of the representation shall be confirmed by the Crown.

[009-101]The state of Canada is such that I am convinced we ought in prudence to

[009-102]place the revenue collected under the 14th Geo. II. at the disposal of the

[009-103]Chambers, retaining, as they are willing to retain, a fixed salary for the

[009-104]Government judges, independent of the annual vote.

[009-105]_Sunday, August 2._

[009-106]Cabinet at 4. Irish question. Lord F. Leveson seems to be much alarmed. He

[009-107]wants to use the Bill of this year for the suppression of an expected

[009-108]meeting at Derry, which meeting is to be unarmed, sing songs, drink toasts,

[009-109]make speeches, and petition for a change of Ministers.

[009-110]It was considered that the powers entrusted to Government by the Bill for

[009-111]the suppression of the Roman Catholic Association were never intended to be

[009-112]exercised for the putting down of such a meeting as that intended to be

[009-113]held at Derry. If the Brunswickers there come out of their houses and have

[009-114]a procession _causing fear_ and threatening the peace, the common law can

[009-115]put them down. Care will be taken to have troops enough at Derry.

[009-116]Lord F. Leveson likewise asks whether he shall proclaim martial law! Peel

[009-117]very properly asks him what martial law is. In fact it is the absence of

[009-118]all law--and can only be endured when a country is on the eve of rebellion

[009-119]or actually in rebellion. [Footnote: This was exactly the description given

[009-120]of it by Lord Beaconsfield with reference to Jamaica in 1866.]

[009-121]It seems to me that Lord Francis is unequal to his situation. I wish we had

[009-122]Hardinge there. He would never go wrong.

[009-123]Herries told me he thought, after reading the papers I had sent him, that

[009-124]there was more of care for the Company than he expected.

[009-125]Peel has written a very good letter to Lord F. Gower, telling him that the

[009-126]first thing they must do is to establish an _efficient police_, to be paid

[009-127]for by Ireland--and of which the officers must be appointed by Government.

[009-128]_August 3._

[009-129]Saw Hardinge. He has perfected a very excellent system in Ireland by which

[009-130]all the 30,000 pensioners are divided into districts, in each of which is a

[009-131]chief constable who pays them. If they move from one district to another

[009-132]they have a ticket, so that the residence and the movements of all are

[009-133]known. Of 30,000 about 10,000 are fit for duty. Blank orders are ready at

[009-134]the Castle, directing the march of these men upon five central points,

[009-135]where they would be incorporated with the regiments, so that in a few days

[009-136]the army could be reinforced by 10,000 men. There are others who are not

[009-137]very capable of doing anything but mischief if against us. These would be

[009-138]ordered to the garrisons.

[009-139]I wish Hardinge was in Ireland instead of Lord Francis.

[009-140]_August 6._

[009-141]Chairs at 11.

[009-142]Astell does not seem to like my letters relative to the delay in answering

[009-143]despatches from India and in communicating events in India; and respecting

[009-144]the amount of military stores sent to India, and the expediency of

[009-145]enquiring whether their amount could not be diminished. Loch did not say

[009-146]anything. It was an attempt at bullying on Astell's part, which I resisted,

[009-147]and successfully.

[009-148]_August 10._

[009-149]The Russians appear to have passed the defiles on the northern side of the

[009-150]Balkans, and almost without loss. There is, I conclude, a force near

[009-151]Bourgas, but all that is to be hoped is that the Turks will be wise enough

[009-152]not to fight. It was an unlucky appointment, that of the Grand Vizier. Old

[009-153]Hussein never would have committed his fault.

[009-154]R. Gordon has been magnificently received at Constantinople.

[009-155]Polignac has been made Prime Minister of France. De Rigny is made Minister

[009-156]of Marine. The Government is Tory, and I should think very favourable to

[009-157]English alliance, not Greek, and certainly not Russian. If it should be

[009-158]able to stand, it must be good for us. Received letters from Colonel

[009-159]Macdonald from Tabriz. He says the Russians at Tiflis talk as if they were

[009-160]going to war with us.

[009-161]_August 11._

[009-162]Received Persian despatches. The Persians will pay no more. They wanted to

[009-163]go to war. No one would go as Envoy to Petersburg but an _attaché_. They

[009-164]all thought they should be beheaded. Macdonald seems to have kept them

[009-165]quiet.

[009-166]Cabinet room. Met Lord Melville. Read Gordon's letters from Constantinople.

[009-167]The Turks have not above 20,000 men there. They are not disposed to yield

[009-168]at all. Gordon thinks if we declared we would fix in any manner the limits

[009-169]of Greece, and maintain them, the Porte would not quarrel with us, and

[009-170]would rather do anything than yield the point of honour by acknowledging

[009-171]the independence of the Greeks.

[009-172]The Russians mean to pass the Balkans with 60,000 men and march on

[009-173]Adrianople. They send a large force by sea to Sizeboli to turn Bourgas.

[009-174]Lord Francis Leveson holds out the apprehension of a long religious contest

[009-175]in Ireland. [Footnote: Unhappily, like other pessimists, he seems to have

[009-176]judged Ireland correctly.] I believe he looks only at the surface and

[009-177]judges from first appearances.

[009-178]_August 12._

[009-179]A victory gained by Paskewitz over the Seraskier, whom he has taken

[009-180]prisoner, with thirty-one pieces of cannon, &c., near Erzeroum--that is,

[009-181]three days after the battle, Paskewitz, still in pursuit, was within forty

[009-182]miles of Erzeroum.

[009-183]Wrote two letters to the Duke--one on the subject of Sir J. P. Grant, who

[009-184]has closed the Courts at Bombay because the Government would not execute an

[009-185]unlawful process, and the other respecting Persian affairs, giving the

[009-186]substance of the despatches which I enclosed.

[009-187]We have a Cabinet to-morrow at 12 on Turkish affairs. I would not allow the

[009-188]Russians to advance any further. I would send one from our own body,

[009-189]_incognito,_ to Paris to talk to Polignac and endeavour to get him to join

[009-190]us in an act of vigorous intervention which would give character to his

[009-191]Government and save Constantinople. I would pass the English and French

[009-192]fleets through the Dardanelles, and give Russia a leaf out of the Greek

[009-193]Treaty. But I do not expect that this will be Aberdeen's course.
