# Lord Mansfield

## Overview

Lord Mansfield (David William Murray, 3rd Earl of Mansfield) was a Tory peer known for his ultra-Protestant convictions and his involvement in organising opposition to the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government following the passage of Catholic emancipation in 1829. Ellenborough regarded him as able but politically deluded.

## Opposition to the Government

Mansfield was actively plotting against the Wellington ministry in the spring of 1829. Hardinge reported that Wood "had been asked by Lord Mansfield to go to the Pitt dinner on the 28th." (004-024) Mansfield believed the government would soon fall: "Why, you must know, it is understood that as soon as Parliament is up the Government will be changed." (004-025)

His strategy was explicitly confessional. At the Pitt dinner, Mansfield anticipated "such a display of Protestant force as will enable the King to take us as his Ministers." (004-025) This was a direct appeal to the King's Protestant sympathies as a basis for displacing Wellington's pro-emancipation administration.

## Ellenborough's Assessment

Ellenborough, himself a Tory but loyal to Wellington, viewed Mansfield's plotting with contempt. After hearing him speak in the Lords, he noted: "Lord Mansfield spoke, sleepily and ill-naturedly. I was exhausted, and could not have answered him, had he said anything worth answering." (P01-083)

He expressed genuine surprise at the credulity of an otherwise capable man: "It is surprising to me that any able man as Lord Mansfield is should be so deluded by the lies of the Duke of Cumberland. The country is not agitated, it is not dissatisfied." (004-030)

## Political Significance

Mansfield represents the Tory-ultra faction that the diary chronicles in its estrangement from Wellington. His activities were part of a broader movement among Protestant Tories who felt betrayed by the government's emancipation policy and sought to reverse it through a change of ministry.
