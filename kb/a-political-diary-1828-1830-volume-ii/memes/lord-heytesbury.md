# Lord Heytesbury

## Overview

Lord Heytesbury (William à Court, 1st Baron Heytesbury) served as British ambassador to the Russian Empire during a critical period of Near Eastern diplomacy. His despatches from St. Petersburg were a constant source of information — and frustration — for the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s [[british-cabinet|Cabinet]] during the Russo-Turkish War and its aftermath.

## Role in the Diary

The diary reveals Heytesbury as a figure of persistent dissatisfaction within the Cabinet. His despatches arrived regularly, with Ellenborough recording that "a letter has been received from Lord Heytesbury" on Eastern questions (002-160) and noting his communications on the blockade of Candia (002-163). The Cabinet read "Lord Heytesbury's and Mr. Gordon's despatches" together, comparing intelligence from Constantinople and St. Petersburg (010-131).

A strong critical thread runs through the Cabinet's assessment. The diarist bluntly records "With Lord Heytesbury we are all dissatisfied" (013-078). A particular concern was his perceived Russophilia — the diary states flatly that "Lord Heytesbury seems to be a mere Russian" (010-132) and that he "represents Russia as being thoroughly" aligned with British interests in a way the Cabinet found naive (009-053). His despatches were said to "show a degree of infatuation" (013-125).

The government exercised active direction over Heytesbury. Ellenborough drafted instructions "directing him, if he should" encounter certain circumstances (014-014), carried proposed letters to [[Lord Aberdeen]] for approval (014-020), and notes Aberdeen agreeing to a specific instruction "as to what he shall endeavour" to achieve (014-027). A "very strong letter" was written to Heytesbury on the Eastern question (012-030), and the diarist records Aberdeen's "recent instruction to Lord Heytesbury" (013-016), indicating sustained micromanagement from London.

## Key Appearances

- **Russo-Turkish diplomacy**: Primary channel for intelligence on Russian intentions during the war and the Peace of Adrianople negotiations (002-160, 010-131).
- **Cabinet dissatisfaction**: Repeatedly criticised by the Wellington Cabinet for his perceived bias towards Russia (010-132, 013-078, 013-125).
- **Persian affairs**: Involved in diplomatic instructions regarding Persia, about which the Chairs "were much pleased" (014-035).
- **Directives from London**: Subject of sustained Cabinet-level correspondence directing his diplomatic conduct (012-030, 014-014, 014-027).
