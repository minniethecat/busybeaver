**Charles Abbott, 1st Baron Tenterden** (1762–1832) was a British jurist who served as Lord Chief Justice of the King's Bench from 1818 until his death. He was raised to the peerage in 1827 and sat in the [[House of Lords]] as a Tory.

== Judicial Career ==

Tenterden was one of the most respected judges of his era. Before becoming Lord Chief Justice, he served as a justice of the Court of Common Pleas. His legal opinions were influential in shaping commercial law, and his treatise on merchant shipping was a standard reference work.

== In the House of Lords ==

During the 1828–1830 period, Tenterden participated actively in the judicial business of the House of Lords. The diary records his position on various legislative matters. On one bill, "Tenterden approved of the making of the new judge, but wished his functions" (004-176) to be more clearly defined.

[[Lord Ellenborough]] records Tenterden's involvement in a significant legal clause: "Tenterden. We struck out a clause by which Le Blanc would have been obliged" (038-118) to comply with certain provisions. This demonstrates Tenterden's hands-on role in legislative drafting.

== Political Alignments ==

Tenterden generally aligned with the Tory majority. On a key vote, the diary records that "Lords Wynford, Tenterden, and Eldon all against the" measure. (036-074) This placed him alongside the more conservative wing of the party.

The diary's preface assessment is mixed: [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that "Lord Tenterden was not powerful" (P01-092) as a speaker, though he acknowledges [[Lord Grey]] "spoke better than he has done" in the same context. Another private note simply says "Tenterden very silly" (P01-196), reflecting perhaps a moment of judicial or political frustration.

== Later Years ==

Tenterden continued as Lord Chief Justice until his death in 1832. He presided over the Court of King's Bench during a period of significant legal reform agitation, maintaining the court's authority through the turbulence that preceded the [[Reform Bill]].
