Lord Aberdeen was Foreign Secretary throughout the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government (1828–1830), a period that coincided with the final phase of the [[Greek War of Independence]], the [[Portuguese Civil War]], and the [[July Revolution 1830]] in France. His diplomatic correspondence, read aloud at Cabinet meetings and recorded in Ellenborough's diary, reveals a statesman navigating between the Congress of Vienna settlement, Russian ambitions in the East, and the rising tide of liberal constitutional movements across Europe.

## Early Life and Career

George Hamilton-Gordon succeeded to the Earldom of Aberdeen in 1801 at the age of seventeen. Before taking the Foreign Office in 1828, he had served as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster and had been a peer representative for Scotland. His classical education and extensive continental travels shaped his diplomatic outlook — he was known to speak French and Italian fluently, and maintained correspondence with diplomatic counterparts across Europe.

## Foreign Policy and Diplomatic Approach

Aberdeen's tenure at the Foreign Office was marked by a cautious, pragmatic approach. He preferred private correspondence over public declarations, a style that the diarist Lord Ellenborough noted with a mixture of approval and anxiety:

> [010-060] …en suggested by the Duke that Aberdeen could write a private letter.
>
> [014-128] pencil, and it seems Aberdeen sends all his proposed despat…

His instinct was to de-escalate rather than confront. On the Greek Question, Aberdeen favored a mediated settlement rather than unilateral British action:

> [015-151] Aberdeen is for settling Greece as a P…

The diary records that Aberdeen "has always had a little priva…" (010-074), suggesting a preference for behind-the-scenes diplomacy over public posturing.

## Key Relationships

### With the Duke of Wellington

Aberdeen worked closely with the Prime Minister, the [[Duke of Wellington]], who, alongside [[Earl Bathurst]] at the Council, relied on Aberdeen's diplomatic judgment. The diary records frequent consultations: "Aberdeen about the Duke of Wellington" (P01-180) and "We met upon foreign affairs. Aberdeen read his instructions to Gord…" (002-041). When Cabinet ruptures threatened, Aberdeen was dispatched to the King at Windsor:

> [020-081] Cabinet at 2. Aberdeen was gone to the King at Winds…

### With Foreign Counterparts

Aberdeen maintained channels with Prince Lieven, the Russian ambassador, and with French diplomatic circles. His reading of European powers was distinctly realist — he "thought France and Russia wou…" (010-025) and was constantly assessing the alignment of the Great Powers.

### With the Tory Cabinet

Within the Cabinet, Aberdeen was not always in step with colleagues. The diary records tensions:

> [006-072] alterations. Aberdeen's the worst part. The King is…
>
> [010-101] I said a year ago Aberdeen would ruin us--he would gradu…

Yet he hosted Cabinet dinners at his residence (020-106), serving as a social hub for the government.

## Legacy and Assessment

Aberdeen's Foreign Secretaryship left mixed impressions. The diarist — himself a seasoned diplomat and later Governor-General of India — alternated between respect for Aberdeen's diligence and concern about his cautious temperament. The criticism that "Aberdeen would ruin us" (010-101) reflected anxieties that his conciliatory approach conceded too much to rival powers. Yet his dispatches were substantive and his judgment on European affairs commanded attention at the Cabinet table. Aberdeen would later return as Foreign Secretary under Peel (1841–1846) and eventually serve as Prime Minister (1852–1855), making him one of the few statesmen to hold all three great offices of state.
