[029-001]As to the last point there is an anachronism, as the orders not to leave

[029-002]Calcutta _as a Government_ arrived after the minutes were recorded.

[029-003]The Duke told Lord Combermere that all the orders for reduction of

[029-004]expenditure having proved inefficacious, it was necessary for the

[029-005]Government here to take reduction into their hands, and it was very natural

[029-006]and obvious to enforce an order twice repeated and already obeyed at the

[029-007]other presidencies.

[029-008]When the army assumed the tone which appeared in the memorials, it was

[029-009]impossible for the Government to do otherwise than insist upon the

[029-010]enforcement of the order. They had expected from him that his whole

[029-011]influence would have been used to strengthen the Government and to prevent

[029-012]any ebullition of feeling on the part of the army. Lord Combermere left the

[029-013]Duke very angry. If the King had been well he would have joined Lord

[029-014]Anglesey. As it is, I expect he will oppose the Government. Lord Hill saw

[029-015]him for a few minutes, and had only some unimportant conversation with him.

[029-016]He told Lord Hill he had made thirteen or fourteen lacs. He made seven lacs

[029-017]by prize money at Bhurtpore.

[029-018]The French have not yet given a written explanation as to Algiers. Their

[029-019]army is said to be in very fine order.

[029-020]Leopold seems to have insinuated that our yielding on the subject of the

[029-021]loan was sudden and late, &c. Aberdeen understood him to allude to the

[029-022]King's illness, and to impute our concession to the wish to get him out of

[029-023]the way. He took no notice of it, and treated the thing as settled.

[029-024]Preparations have been made for the event of the King's death.

[029-025]Peel has been obliged to leave London, as his father is dying.

[029-026]_May 4._

[029-027]Committee. No witnesses. Walked with Lords Bathurst and Rosslyn to the

[029-028]Duke's. The bulletin is good. The King had some sleep and is better.

[029-029]Halford's account, too, is better. The King slept six hours, but the water

[029-030]was so much increased about the legs that they have made punctures to draw

[029-031]it off. Upon the whole the account leads one to suppose the thing will be

[029-032]protracted.

[029-033]In the House of Commons last night, Goulburn was obliged to withdraw the

[029-034]vote of 100,000£. for Windsor Castle and refer it to a Committee upstairs.

[029-035]The expectation of a dissolution is acting powerfully on votes, and he

[029-036]would have been beaten. The Duke approved entirely of his having withdrawn

[029-037]the motion.

[029-038]The continuance of the King in this state would be highly inconvenient

[029-039]indeed. There would be no possibility of carrying on the money business in

[029-040]the House of Commons.

[029-041]In the House of Lords we had a motion from Lord MountCashel for an address

[029-042]for a commission to enquire into the abuses of the English and Irish

[029-043]Church. No one thought it worth while to reply to him.

[029-044]_May 5._

[029-045]Read and altered a letter relative to the new arrangement of civil

[029-046]allowances.

[029-047]Elphinstone approved generally of what I proposed--which is.

[029-048]1\. To depose every chief who shall harbour banditti.

[029-049]2\. To oblige them to give up refugee criminals under the same penalty.

[029-050]3\. To engage as many as possible to abandon their heritable jurisdictions.

[029-051]4\. To remit the arrears.

[029-052]5\. To form a local corps in which the chiefs and their relations should be

[029-053]officers (with only two or three Europeans) to maintain order. This corps

[029-054]to be a sort of bodyguard to the Resident. The robbers to be admitted as

[029-055]privates.

[029-056]6\. Troops to be brought if necessary from Cutch.

[029-057]7\. Every measure to be adopted to encourage the growth of cotton.

[029-058]These things I shall throw into a letter, which, however, will not be sent

[029-059]till Clare goes out.

[029-060]We talked of native education. I read to E. my alterations of the letter of

[029-061]last July relative to his plans for education, with which he seemed

[029-062]satisfied.

[029-063]He seems generally to approve of my views upon that subject, particularly

[029-064]of uniting the English with the native classes at the several colleges, and

[029-065]teaching the natives useful knowledge.

[029-066]They should be examined in the regulations of the company.

[029-067]Office, but first saw Hardinge, who seems full of the Duke of Clarence,

[029-068]with whom he is high in favour, as having, urged by Wood, had several

[029-069]things done for the young FitzClarences.

[029-070]He said the Duke thought the King might live four months.

[029-071]Cabinet dinner at the Chancellor's. The Duke saw the King, who looked very

[029-072]well, and seemed cheerful and in good humour.

[029-073]He was very ill yesterday. Black in the face, and the ends of his fingers

[029-074]black. They think he will go off suddenly in one of these attacks.

[029-075]Little water came from one leg, and they will scarify it again.

[029-076]O'Reilly, who probably performed the operation of scarifying, and who must

[029-077]know the state of the King, whom he saw daily, declared positively

[029-078]yesterday to Lord Maryborough, and with a face of surprise, that there was

[029-079]no water.

[029-080]The Duke of C. saw the King on Sunday, and was at Windsor and probably saw

[029-081]him to-day.

[029-082]The Duke of Sussex has lent the King an easy chair, and affectionate

[029-083]messages have passed between them.

[029-084]The Bishop of Chichester is now at Windsor, the Lord and Groom and Equerry

[029-085]in waiting, two physicians, besides O'Reilly and Sir Wathen Waller and

[029-086]Knighton.

[029-087]When they told the King they must make a puncture in about four hours, he

[029-088]desired it might be made at once if it was necessary.

[029-089]The Duke told the King he had told Sir H. Halford he would always find him

[029-090]intrepid--with which the King was much pleased.

[029-091]He said when he saw a thing was necessary he always made up his mind to it.

[029-092]Wortley told me the Household betted the King would be at Ascot.

[029-093]By-the-bye, Wortley did very well last night in not allowing Wynne to lead

[029-094]him into a speech on the half-castes. He spoke very officially and

[029-095]properly. I complimented him upon it. In fact it is an act of forbearance

[029-096]in any man, but especially in a young man, to throw away a speech.

[029-097]Precedents have been looked into, and every necessary step is known, should

[029-098]the King die.

[029-099]The Duke will immediately go in uniform to the Duke of Clarence and advise

[029-100]him to come to his house in town.

[029-101]A sketch of the speech will be prepared, but kings like making the

[029-102]declaration to the Privy Council themselves, as it is the only thing they

[029-103]can do without advice.

[029-104]Peel's father died on the 3rd.

[029-105]_May 6._

[029-106]Left my card with Lord Combermere, who called yesterday.

[029-107]The bulletin states the King to have been better yesterday, but to have had

[029-108]a bad night.

[029-109]The private letter to the Duke says he passed the night wretchedly, and

[029-110]with much inquietude. They find it necessary to make further punctures, and

[029-111]have sent for Brodie.

[029-112]The King spoke to Halford for some time with much composure and piety as to

[029-113]his situation.

[029-114]Lord Bathurst looked into the precedents in Queen Anne's reign, and at the

[029-115]declarations of several kings on their first meeting their Privy Council.

[029-116]House. A good and useful speech from Lord Goderich on the funded and

[029-117]expended debt. He showed that the receipt from taxes was about the same as

[029-118]in 1816, although 9 millions had been taken off, and that the interest of

[029-119]the National Debt would, in 1831, be reduced 44 millions below its amount

[029-120]in 1816.

[029-121]Cabinet at half-past ten at Aberdeen's. A letter from Leopold, endeavouring

[029-122]to throw upon us the blame of delay for two months, and treating

[029-123]acquiescence in his terms of loan as a _sine quâ non_. Now the terms we

[029-124]propose are not _exactly_ the same, as we make a payment by annual

[029-125]instalments a part of it, and I expect he will break off at last; but he

[029-126]will wait till the King is actually dead.

[029-127]_May 7._

[029-128]A very good account of the King. He has passed twenty-four hours with

[029-129]mitigated symptoms.

[029-130]Dined with Sir J. Murray. I must next year have an Indian dinner.

[029-131]_May 9._

[029-132]Read as I went to town to Cabinet, and returned in the carriage Cabell's

[029-133]memorandum on the Hyderabad transactions.

[029-134]The Duke read the letter he had received from Sir H. Halford. It gave a bad

[029-135]account of the King. Yesterday was a day 'of embarrassment and distress,'

[029-136]and he is swollen notwithstanding the punctures made by Brodie. He is

[029-137]anxious about himself, and must know his danger, yet he talks of the

[029-138]necessity of having a new dining-room at the Cottage ready by Ascot.

[029-139]We had much conversation respecting the law asserting his power of

[029-140]disposing of his property by will.

[029-141]The Chancellor was not there. He went to Windsor.

[029-142]The other matters considered were merely the mode of dealing with several

[029-143]questions to be brought on next week. It seems to be clear that no

[029-144]dependence whatever can be placed in the House of Commons. Every man will

[029-145]vote for his constituents.

[029-146]No answer has been received from Prince Leopold.

[029-147]My apprehension is that the King cannot live ten days.

[029-148]Lord Londonderry went to Windsor yesterday and saw the physician. He had a

[029-149]dinner afterwards at his villa, and told every one, the Lièvens being

[029-150]there, that the King was much worse than he had ever been. This was untrue,

[029-151]for the Duke left Windsor after Lord L., and when he left the Castle the

[029-152]King certainly was not worse, but rather better. I have no doubt Lord L.

[029-153]managed to tell Wood, [Footnote: Lord Londonderry's brother-in-law, having

[029-154]married Lady Caroline Stewart, also sister-in-law of Lord Ellenborough.]

[029-155]and Wood would tell the Duke of Clarence, who would think he was ill-used

[029-156]and deceived.

[029-157]_May 10._

[029-158]The Duke will read the Hyderabad memorandum as he goes down to Windsor on

[029-159]Wednesday.

[029-160]I told him of the alteration in the treaty with Nagpore.

[029-161]The Chancellor was at Windsor yesterday. He did not see the King. The

[029-162]physicians seemed to think it could not last a week. He is greatly swollen,

[029-163]and generally.

[029-164]Lord Bathurst went to Windsor to-day. His account was a little better, but

[029-165]his expectation did not go beyond a fortnight. In the meantime the

[029-166]physicians are afraid of telling the King of his danger.

[029-167]Sir W. Knighton sat up with him last night, and was much alarmed by one of

[029-168]the attacks, not having seen one before. However, he did not call Sir H.

[029-169]Halford.

[029-170]The probability is that the new Parliament will meet in the last week in

[029-171]July.

[029-172]The Speaker says the House of Commons is like a school two days before the

[029-173]holidays. They do not know what mischief to be at.

[029-174]Lord Rosslyn seems to think all sorts of intrigues are going on, and has

[029-175]some little doubt as to the Duke of Clarence. I have none.

[029-176]House. E. Retford again. Wrote to Lord Holland when I came home to call his

[029-177]attention to the Hickson Nullity of Marriage Bill. I cannot take a part;

[029-178]but he must do so if he wishes to preserve his grandfather's clause.

[029-179]_May 11._

[029-180]Heard from Lord Holland, who is fully alive to the consequences of the

[029-181]Bill. He thinks I am right not to take a part.

[029-182]There was an indigo-planter before the Committee to-day. It seems, as I

[029-183]supposed, to be just as unnecessary for indigo-manufacturers to be indigo-

[029-184]growers as it is for maltsters to be great farmers. This man took out no

[029-185]capital and he had no licence; yet he was permitted to reside and take a

[029-186]lease, and the agency houses lent him money at 10 and 12 per cent.

[029-187]The judge, Sir T. Strange, was a sensible man. He deprecated the

[029-188]introduction of English law into the provinces.

[029-189]The King is getting weaker, which the physicians dread more than his

[029-190]spasms. It is thought he can hardly last a week.
