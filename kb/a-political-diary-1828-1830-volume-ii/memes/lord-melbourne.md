# Lord Melbourne

## Overview

Lord Melbourne (William Lamb, 2nd Viscount Melbourne) was a Whig politician who during the period of Ellenborough's diary (1828–1830) held a position at the intersection of the Canningite and Whig oppositions. He was already known as an able parliamentary speaker, though Ellenborough's diary records him as performing "poorly" on at least one occasion (023-179). His later career would see him become Home Secretary and ultimately Prime Minister, but during these years he was maneuvering for position against the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government.

## Parliamentary Activity

Melbourne was active in foreign affairs debates. Lord Holland gave notice of a motion about Greece, and "Lord Melbourne moves for some papers respecting Portugal" in the same session. (023-032) His motion for Portuguese papers was pressed despite government resistance: "Then Lord Melbourne's motion for Portuguese papers. He did not speak well — but very bitterly." (024-127) This suggests Melbourne adopted an adversarial stance on the government's Portuguese policy, particularly regarding the Terceira affair.

In another debate, Goderich and Clanricarde spoke alongside him, and "Melbourne spoke; Lord Melbourne poorly." (023-179) The diary's assessment contrasts the quality of his delivery with his established reputation.

## Political Positioning

Melbourne was viewed with suspicion by the Wellington government. At a Cabinet meeting, the King had "suggested Lord Dudley or Melbourne" for a position, but "this was referred to and rejected by such of the Cabinet as could be on a sudden collected at the Foreign Office." (005-040) Ellenborough himself noted: "I should certainly have rejected both," indicating Melbourne was considered beyond the pale for the Wellington ministry.

By September 1830, as the government's position weakened, Melbourne's stance hardened. Greville reported "that Palmerston had no objection to come in. Lord Melbourne had; but they required the sacrifice of Aberdeen, Bathurst, and Arbuthnot." (044-124) This demand for the removal of senior ministers showed Melbourne's refusal to join a government on terms he considered insufficient.

## Character and Influence

Though a man of ability, Melbourne was seen as difficult to accommodate within coalitions. His demand for ministerial sacrifices alongside [[lord-palmerston|Lord Palmerston]] suggested a coordinated Canningite-Whig strategy. Ellenborough recorded these negotiations with skepticism, noting "there must be some mistake about this condition." (044-124)
