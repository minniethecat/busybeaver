[030-001]Read the memorandum on Hyderabad a second time, and sent it with the

[030-002]proposed letter and alterations to the Duke.

[030-003]Prepared materials for Lord Stanhope's motion about shipping on Thursday.

[030-004]_May 12._

[030-005]Cabinet dinner at Lord Rosslyn's.

[030-006]The Duke saw the King to-day. He said there was a decided alteration since

[030-007]Wednesday last. He was now in appearance an invalid, but not a dying man.

[030-008]His body is very much swollen. They took several quarts of water from his

[030-009]feet yesterday. He is good-humoured and alive. His eyes as brilliant as

[030-010]ever. His voice a little affected. His colour dark and sodden.

[030-011]The Duke thinks he may die at any time; but may live a fortnight or ten

[030-012]days--Knighton thinks so too. The other physicians think worse of him.

[030-013]He called for the 'Racing Calendar' yesterday. They were afraid he would

[030-014]call for the newspaper.

[030-015]Knighton found he was not aware there were now any bulletins.

[030-016]Knighton proposed to him the taking the sacrament, as he did not take it at

[030-017]Easter. He said he would think about it, but to be better before he took

[030-018]it. His taking it now might lead to the publishing of more bulletins.

[030-019]He continues to take the greatest interest in the improvements at the

[030-020]lodge.

[030-021]After dinner we talked only of the things necessary to be done on a demise.

[030-022]Lord B. seemed to say we _could not_ have the Duchess of Clarence as

[030-023]Regent, because there was no precedent. I trust this will be got over.

[030-024]Leopold has written an unsatisfactory answer to the last letter about the

[030-025]loan. However, he goes.

[030-026]The Porte has acquiesced in the arrangements of the protocol, so Leopold is

[030-027]Prince Sovereign of Greece.

[030-028]The Duke read Cabell's memorandum to-day. He thinks Cabell proposes doing

[030-029]more than should be done. He has a strong feeling as to the scandalous

[030-030]nature of the whole transaction. Lieutenant-Colonel Arabin has been

[030-031]infesting the Chancellor upon the subject.

[030-032]_May 13._

[030-033]Dined at four. Rode to the office and back, and to the House.

[030-034]Prepared for Lord Stanhope's motion for returns on shipping, &c.

[030-035]The Duke had a great deal of information, and answered Lord Stanhope. I

[030-036]spoke, however, afterwards, as I had some new facts. Then E. Retford till

[030-037]nine.

[030-038]Read letters from Sir John Macdonald and a paper he enclosed from

[030-039]'Blackwood's Magazine' in 1827 on the invasion of India by the Russians.

[030-040]_May 14._

[030-041]Colonel Briggs called. He is a clever man. He will prepare for me a

[030-042]memorandum on the composition of the native army. He seems equally

[030-043]conversant with revenue, judicial, and military matters.

[030-044]House. E. Retford as usual. The King is much relieved by the draining of

[030-045]the water from the punctures; but the wounds gave him much annoyance last

[030-046]night. The fear is they may lead to mortification. Lord Rosslyn and I go

[030-047]down on Sunday to Windsor to enquire.

[030-048]_May 15._

[030-049]Astell has sent to Lord Combermere the letter lately despatched to India in

[030-050]which the conduct of the several members of Government is commented upon as

[030-051]regards the Batta question. Lord Combermere only asked, as far as I

[030-052]recollect, to know upon what grounds his conduct has been censured. I told

[030-053]Astell to tell him the censure rested entirely on official documents with

[030-054]which he must be acquainted. The Duke was very angry with Astell, when I

[030-055]told him of it after the Cabinet, and expects a question in the House of

[030-056]Lords.

[030-057]I told Astell the letter ought not to have been given. It reveals what has

[030-058]been done with regard to the Batta question, and the news may possibly

[030-059]reach India through the press before the Government obtain it.

[030-060]Cabinet at half-past four. Not only have the Turks acceded to the

[030-061]arrangement for Greece, but the Greeks have done so too. Leopold adheres to

[030-062]his memorandum of March, and wants the power of drawing as much as he

[030-063]pleases of the loan at any time.

[030-064]He will be invited to meet the Plenipotentiaries or to send a person to

[030-065]meet them to discuss this point. The people about him say he means to break

[030-066]off. If he should, Peel thinks we could not do it upon a better point, and

[030-067]he is right.

[030-068]The King is decidedly better. The Duke saw him to day. He was looking more

[030-069]healthy. He has had some refreshing sleep. He is more likely to live than

[030-070]to die. The only danger is from mortification in consequence of the

[030-071]punctures; but his constitution is so good that in all probability he will

[030-072]avoid this danger. This wonderful recovery quite changes our position. In

[030-073]all public business we must now calculate upon his living--at least till

[030-074]the end of the Session.

[030-075]Lord Morpeth is to make a motion for the repeal of the Banishment Clause in

[030-076]the last Libel Act. To the repeal of that clause, which is inoperative

[030-077]against the common libeller, we have no objection, and the Attorney-General

[030-078]is pledged to it; but the House of Lords would not like, and the King would

[030-079]not endure, the repeal of that provision without the substitution of some

[030-080]other security. That proposed by the Attorney-General is the requiring

[030-081]security to the amount of 500L. from two sureties that the editor shall pay

[030-082]_fines_ on account of libels. This is reasonable, and would to some extent

[030-083]prevent the putting up, as is now done, men or women of straw as editors,

[030-084]who have no means of paying fines. The other proposal of the Attorney-

[030-085]General, that the types should be seizable to whomever they may belong, is

[030-086]objectionable and would hardly be carried. Peel is very sorry the question

[030-087]is stirred at the present moment. The press is generally with us or

[030-088]quiescent, and the 'Morning Journal,' [Footnote: It had been obliged to pay

[030-089]heavy damages for a libel on the Duke of Wellington.] a paper instituted to

[030-090]oppose the Government, has within these few days been given up altogether

[030-091]from the want of support. Certainly this is not the moment at which it is

[030-092]desirable to appear to commence an attack upon the Press--and the Attorney-

[030-093]General can do nothing that will not be suspected by them.

[030-094]The Duke has written a memorandum on the Hyderabad affair.

[030-095]_May 16._

[030-096]Read the Duke's memorandum; he mistakes the law. However, I cannot write

[030-097]notes upon his memorandum without the Act of Parliament. The King had an

[030-098]indifferent night, but still feels better. I only met Lord Bathurst, who

[030-099]told me so. He had not seen the private letter.

[030-100]Had a long conversation with Lady C. Wood at Lord Camden's about the

[030-101]Clarences. It seems there has been a great deal of hope excited in the

[030-102]Spencers.

[030-103]They expect Lord Holland to be made Minister, and their son Bob or Lord

[030-104]Darnley to be first Lord of the Admiralty!--_Nous verrons_.

[030-105]It seems the Duchess of Clarence and the Duchess of Kent were and are great

[030-106]friends, and the Duchess of Clarence is very fond of the young Princess.

[030-107]_Monday, May 17._

[030-108]At eleven set off with Lord Rosslyn for Windsor. We drove to the visitor's

[030-109]entrance. After a time Sir A. Barnard came. Lord Rosslyn said we did not

[030-110]presume to ask to see the King, but we were anxious to know how His Majesty

[030-111]was, and to present our humble duty to him.

[030-112]Sir A. asked if we would see Knighton? Lord Rosslyn said it would be very

[030-113]satisfactory. However, no Knighton came, but a message through Sir A.

[030-114]Barnard that Sir Wm. Knighton had gone in to the King and had mentioned we

[030-115]were there, and His Majesty had expressed himself very sensible of our kind

[030-116]attention. This I conclude is Knighton's own message, and that the King

[030-117]will never hear we have been. Sir A. Barnard seemed in excellent spirits

[030-118]about the King. He had a good night, and is certainly much better. He talks

[030-119]of being able to go to Ascot and to stand up in the carriage, though he

[030-120]could not go up into the stand.

[030-121]We met the Bishop of Chichester going back to town. I suppose he thinks he

[030-122]shall not be wanted.

[030-123]Rode down to the House. East Retford.

[030-124]The Duke's private account of the King is excellent.

[030-125]_May 18._

[030-126]Committee. Examined Colonel Briggs, who gave very good evidence indeed.

[030-127]Ordered the attendance of six witnesses for Tuesday, whom we shall

[030-128]endeavour to despatch, and that will enable everybody to go to Epsom on

[030-129]Thursday and Friday.

[030-130]The King much better. All his symptoms alleviated.

[030-131]To-morrow the Duke will get from him his signature to the message for a

[030-132]_stamper_. There are to be three signatures of Ministers, that is, of Privy

[030-133]Councillors, to authorise the stamper, who is to be nominated by the King

[030-134]to affix the royal stamp to instruments in the King's presence.

[030-135]By the account from Marseilles, it appears that there are 11 sail of the

[030-136]line and 28 frigates in the French expedition, in all 97 sails--about 350

[030-137]transports, carrying 75,000 tons. There will be 30,500 infantry, besides a

[030-138]very complete equipment of artillery, &c., 75 battering guns, 4,000 horses.

[030-139]The Luke of Angoulême's (the Dauphin's) visit has delayed the expedition

[030-140]four days. They will probably be on the sea _to-day_.

[030-141]Rosslyn was talking yesterday of the _danger_ from this expedition, and the

[030-142]annexation of Algiers to France. I do not fear it--we can, if we manage

[030-143]well, make it very costly by bringing forward the people of Tunis and

[030-144]Morocco, not near the coast, but almost from the desert. We must take care

[030-145]to secure Tunis, and then the French will be no gainers by their move.

[030-146]Lord Londonderry made a very foolish speech about foreign policy in putting

[030-147]off his motion, which stood for the 25th. Aberdeen promised the Greek

[030-148]papers on _Monday next_.

[030-149]_May 19._

[030-150]The Duke saw the King to-day and found him looking better than he did at

[030-151]the last Council.

[030-152]The drain from the legs is now very small. He was annoyed last night by

[030-153]them and sent for Halford, who sent off for Brodie; but there was nothing

[030-154]of importance. They cannot yet say that he will not ultimately die of this

[030-155]complaint. Knighton thinks he will be an invalid all his life. Tierney says

[030-156]they cannot tell for a week whether there is any mischief remaining about

[030-157]the chest. The Duke wished to speak to him about the stamp; but he made an

[030-158]excuse about his legs requiring some dressing, and the Duke, seeing he did

[030-159]not choose to talk about business, went away.

[030-160]It seems clear that Leopold means to abdicate.

[030-161]The Attorney-General has made his libel preventive measure a poor weak

[030-162]inoperative thing, ridiculous, and unconciliating.

[030-163]The French Chambers are dissolved as a _coup de théâtre_ on the sailing of

[030-164]the expedition, and they are to meet on August 3, by which time they expect

[030-165]to hear of its success.

[030-166]A union of parties is expected on the Greek affair. I am not sorry for it.

[030-167]The Huskissonians and Whigs are drawing nearer together. The Tories, on the

[030-168]other hand, are rather approximating to us--so that by the beginning of

[030-169]next Session men will be at last in their right places.

[030-170]_May 21._

[030-171]The King had a bad night. The private letter gave a bad account. He has

[030-172]been _drinking again_, very irritable, _intolerably_ so. Halford says,

[030-173]would neither sit in a chair nor lie in a bed, &c. Halford at last held

[030-174]strong language, and I believe told him his life depended on his obeying

[030-175]his physician.

[030-176]I am very much disappointed indeed at this. I hoped he was really getting

[030-177]better and would live.

[030-178]Aberdeen is to allow the instalments of the loan guaranteed to Leopold to

[030-179]be paid in four instead of eight years if he can keep him to his

[030-180]principality by doing so.

[030-181]The French were off on the 18th. There is a partial change in their

[030-182]Ministry.
