The [[Portuguese Civil War]] was the second major diplomatic preoccupation of the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government alongside the [[Greek War of Independence]]. When Dom Pedro, Emperor of Brazil, abdicated the Portuguese throne in favor of his young daughter Maria da Glória, he appointed his brother Dom Miguel as regent on condition that Miguel marry Maria and accept a constitutional charter. Miguel instead seized the throne as an absolutist monarch in 1828, triggering a dynastic crisis that drew in Britain, France, Spain, and Austria. Ellenborough's diary records the Wellington Cabinet's delicate management of the Portuguese Question — balancing treaty obligations to Britain's oldest ally against the risk of a wider Peninsular war.

## Background

Portugal had been Britain's ally since the Treaty of Windsor (1386). The Napoleonic Wars had driven the Portuguese court to Brazil (1807), and the Portuguese Empire's center of gravity had shifted across the Atlantic. The Liberal Revolution of 1820 forced the return of King João VI to Lisbon, while his son Pedro declared Brazilian independence and became Emperor in 1822. When João VI died in 1826, the succession crisis began: Pedro was the legitimate heir but ruled Brazil; his brother Miguel, exiled after an earlier absolutist coup attempt, was the favorite of the Portuguese traditionalists.

## Key Developments (1828–1830)

### Miguel's Usurpation

In 1828, Dom Miguel returned to Portugal, dissolved the Cortes, and had himself proclaimed absolute king. The Wellington government, committed to the constitutional settlement by the Quadruple Alliance, faced a dilemma: recognition of Miguel would stabilize Portugal but legitimize absolutist usurpation; opposition risked war.

## Consequences

The Portuguese crisis of 1828–1830 had lasting repercussions:

1. **British non-intervention**: Wellington's government refused to intervene militarily, a decision that preserved peace but disappointed British liberals and strained the alliance with constitutional Portugal.
2. **Prolonged conflict**: The civil war continued until 1834, when Miguel was finally defeated and exiled.
3. **Precedent for Peninsular diplomacy**: The crisis established patterns of Anglo-Iberian diplomatic management that would recur through the Carlist Wars and beyond.
4. **Domestic political cost**: Palmerston's motion on Portugal (025-140) embarrassed the government in Parliament and highlighted the divergence between Tory pragmatism and Whig liberalism on foreign policy.
