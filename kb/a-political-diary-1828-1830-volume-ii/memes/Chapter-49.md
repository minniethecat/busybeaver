[049-001]He asked whether they were distinctly to understand that the Cabinet had

[049-002]decided upon the termination of the monopoly? I said that the question not

[049-003]having yet been before the Cabinet I could not give an answer officially;

[049-004]but when the First Lord of the Treasury and the President of the Board of

[049-005]Control desired to know what the course of the Court would be in the event

[049-006]of its being proposed that the Court should administer the Government

[049-007]without monopoly, I thought it was not difficult to draw an inference.

[049-008]_October 19._

[049-009]Sent to the Duke a memorandum on his letter. Read at the Cabinet room. The

[049-010]King of the Netherlands is much annoyed at the desertion, as he thinks it,

[049-011]of his allies. He now proposes a Congress of the Four Powers and _France_

[049-012]at Breda or Cleves. He admits France very unwillingly, and by no means

[049-013]acquiesces in the reasoning in favour of the advance we made.

[049-014]Sir Ch. Bagot seems to think the Prince of Orange will be losing the

[049-015]affection of the Dutch without gaining the Belgians.

[049-016]The German Confederation is arming in the neighbourhood of Hanau for the

[049-017]preservation of the peace. They have put 6,000 or 7,000 men in motion, and

[049-018]have a reserve of 15,000 or 18,000.

[049-019]The excitement against Polignac and Peyronnet increases, and the Ministers

[049-020]run the hazard of their places by attempting to save them. I fear that is

[049-021]hopeless. The Spanish Radicals seem to find it would be dangerous to pass

[049-022]the frontier.

[049-023]_October 20._

[049-024]Office. Cabinet room. The Prince of Orange has written a most offensive

[049-025]letter to the King of the French, almost insinuating that the troubles in

[049-026]Belgium are fomented by France, and saying that by a declaration against

[049-027]the Belgians France would show her good faith, and secure the recognition

[049-028]of Russia. The French Cabinet is much offended at the silence of the King

[049-029]of the Netherlands, and Count Molé is going to write to the Dutch Minister

[049-030]upon the subject.

[049-031]Nesselrode seems to see great difficulties in the intervention of France in

[049-032]the settlement of Belgium--the union of Belgium and Holland having been

[049-033]made _against_ France. The Russian Minister at the Hague has general

[049-034]directions to follow the course of England upon all points not provided for

[049-035]by his instructions.

[049-036]There is a great fall in the Funds to-day; partly, it is said, in

[049-037]consequence of those who desired to keep up the Funds being no longer able

[049-038]to do so; partly from the general aspect of affairs. My surprise is that

[049-039]the Funds have not fallen before, and much more.

[049-040]Cabinet dinner at the Duke's. Showed the protocol of our Conference with

[049-041]the Chairs. The heads of the speech were read. Aberdeen's will not do at

[049-042]all. To my surprise he intended to announce the recognition of Miguel, he

[049-043]having engaged to do a great act of justice; that is, to publish the

[049-044]amnesty. He will not do it till a British Minister arrives at Lisbon; that

[049-045]is, he makes us, whom he has once deceived, dependent upon his word. This

[049-046]would be a very incautious step on our part. We meet on Friday to consider

[049-047]the speech in detail.

[049-048]We had a good deal of conversation about the Duchess of Kent's allowance,

[049-049]which is to be much increased. It is proposed to give her 20,000£ a year.

[049-050]She has now 12,000£ for herself and the Princess, out of which she pays

[049-051]interest and insurance upon 12,000£ she borrowed on the Duke of Kent's

[049-052]death for her outfit.

[049-053]The King has about thirty people at dinner every day, belonging to the

[049-054]Household. His expense must be enormous in living.

[049-055]_October 21._

[049-056]Read in the newspaper the King of the Netherlands' speech. It is querulous

[049-057]and angry. I really thought the Proclamation _extraordinary_ of the Prince

[049-058]of Orange a forgery; but it is genuine, and he throws off all connection

[049-059]with Holland, declaring the independence of Belgium, and placing himself

[049-060]practically at the head of the Rebellion!

[049-061]On Monday night at a dance at the Lodge, Hardinge saw accidentally in an

[049-062]evening newspaper, shown to him for another purpose, the advertisement of

[049-063]the Anti-Union Association, and by seven o'clock the next morning the Lord

[049-064]Lieutenant's Proclamation prohibiting it was placarded in the streets. This

[049-065]is decision. There was no riot. Persons in general were satisfied the act

[049-066]was right. O'Connell is alarmed. The Duke of Leinster is ready to sign a

[049-067]declaration in favour of the Union. All is safe in Ireland with Hardinge's

[049-068]promptitude. I wish he could remain and not come over to Parliament.

[049-069]_October 22._

[049-070]Saw Campbell, who was very nervous and anxious, and I at night wrote a

[049-071]letter to Lord Hill in favour of his son--more, I admit, from a father's

[049-072]feelings than from a conviction of being right.

[049-073]It seems the Lord Lieutenant not having been near Dublin when the

[049-074]Proclamation was issued by Hardinge, he must have had a blank Proclamation

[049-075]in his pocket, and have issued it without the opinion of the law officers.

[049-076]He has good debatable Parliamentary grounds of defence; but he has trodden

[049-077]upon the margin of the law. Not the worse for that in these times, when it

[049-078]is most important that every one should see the Government are vigilant and

[049-079]determined.

[049-080]Valdez, who entered Spain with a few hundred men, has been smashed at once.

[049-081]At the Cabinet we had a long discussion respecting the Regency question.

[049-082]Aberdeen started the objection that the proposed measure was destructive of

[049-083]the principle that the King could not die. On the other hand it was

[049-084]contended that we maintained that principle. We made a Regent for _a King_.

[049-085]We acknowledged _a King_; but we deferred taking the oath of allegiance

[049-086]till we knew who he was. The difficulties attending the _unkinging_ of a

[049-087]Sovereign on the birth of a Prince nearer to the present King seem greater

[049-088]than any attending the measure proposed. It was ultimately determined that

[049-089]the Chancellor should consult the judges and the law officers.

[049-090]_October 23._

[049-091]Cabinet. Twenty-six magistrates at Canterbury sentenced to three days'

[049-092]imprisonment threshing machine breakers, who pleaded guilty! Such has been

[049-093]the terror struck into them! Sir E. Knatchbull was in the chair.

[049-094]We went through the speech--not deciding absolutely upon the words; but

[049-095]generally upon the substance.

[049-096]Then arose a conversation as to the Regency which, in this last hour, is

[049-097]thought a point of importance. The Chancellor seems alarmed and unwilling

[049-098]to move the suspension of the rights of the presumptive heir until the non-

[049-099]existence of an heir apparent be ascertained, without the opinion of the

[049-100]judges. It is admitted there is no written opinion to guide us. The analogy

[049-101]of property is in favour of the heir presumptive; that of peerage in favour

[049-102]of the heir apparent _in utero_.

[049-103]_October 24._

[049-104]Cabinet at 4. Read two letters from Hardinge. By his account all the men of

[049-105]property will support the Government and the Union.

[049-106]The press is coming round--_bought_. A Mr. Conway, an able writer, is

[049-107]furious against O'Connell, and, upon the whole, the Press is on our side.

[049-108]Hardinge dilates with delight upon his military preparations and plans of

[049-109]defence, and seemingly will be disappointed if he cannot put them into

[049-110]execution.

[049-111]The Belgian Ministers resigned after the Prince of Orange's Proclamation.

[049-112]He is left without advisers. He has endeavoured to get Sir Charles Bagot to

[049-113]join him, and Grasioff. He sends for Cartwright. He seems much embarrassed.

[049-114]In fact he is in heart a Belgian, and would sacrifice everything to be King

[049-115]of Belgium. He never knew the Dutch, and not unnaturally likes the Belgians

[049-116]better. They are indignant at his conduct in Holland, and with reason. He

[049-117]seems to intend to rule the Dutch by means of the Belgians. This he cannot

[049-118]do.

[049-119]The Duke of Wellington always thought him a silly fellow.

[049-120]The Provisional Government is going to send some mission here.

[049-121]We had a long talk about the Regency. Really it does us little credit to

[049-122]begin now, within ten days of the meeting of Parliament, to consider that

[049-123]question seriously.

[049-124]The Chief Justices will be asked whether, supposing the Queen to be

[049-125]pregnant at the death of the King, the next living heir would succeed? How

[049-126]in the event of the birth of a child the _de facto_ Sovereign is to be put

[049-127]aside? And what should be done if the Queen only may be with child? The

[049-128]difficulty consists in the oath of allegiance, which must be altered and

[049-129]made conditional. But what a curious position the Queen Victoria would be

[049-130]placed in, if a baby were to oust her after eight months of reign!

[049-131]I think the course adopted will be this--to make an oath of allegiance

[049-132]conditional, saving the rights of a child to be born; to appoint the Regent

[049-133]who would be named for the Princess Victoria, with the provision that on

[049-134]the birth of a child the child's mother shall be Regent.

[049-135]_October 25._

[049-136]Cabinet at 4. Peel read letters he had received from Mr. Foster, the

[049-137]magistrate of Manchester, Mr. Hulton, of Hulton, and a manufacturer whose

[049-138]name I forget. They all give an alarming account of the state of

[049-139]Manchester. The colliers have turned out in some districts, and where they

[049-140]have turned out the mills are necessarily stopped. This has thrown numbers

[049-141]out of employment. These colliers can earn 10_s_. a day; that is, as much

[049-142]as many clergymen. The spinners can earn 5_s_. a day. Yet they turn out.

[049-143]This seems to be a manoeuvre like that of Lafitte when he refused to

[049-144]discount bills. To stop the supply of coal is to throw all mills out of

[049-145]work, and every one out of employment. The question is, Shall the masters

[049-146]resist? If they do, there will be an early collision. If they do not, they

[049-147]may defer it, but not long. Concession was counselled six weeks ago, on the

[049-148]ground that, after the events in Belgium and in France, collision was

[049-149]dangerous; and this even by bold men. It seems there are 3,000 infantry, 3

[049-150]guns, and about 600 or 800 cavalry near Manchester. Perhaps some howitzers

[049-151]may be sent, but more force there is not. Peel at the Cabinet wrote a

[049-152]letter to Mr. Taylor, saying that under ordinary circumstances he should

[049-153]have counselled resistance or rather non-concession; but now it was a

[049-154]doubtful question whether a collision at Manchester would not lead to

[049-155]collision in many other places, and was our force sufficient? He was

[049-156]desired to see Mr. Hulton, Sir E. Bouverie, and others, and to consider

[049-157]what could be done, particularly whether Volunteer Corps could be formed.

[049-158]The delegates who went to Mr. Chappell seem to be amenable to the law and

[049-159]get-at-able. This will be done.

[049-160]The law officers came in and were asked as to the power of the Crown to

[049-161]permit the formation of Volunteer Corps. They were desired to consider the

[049-162]point. By the Act of 1794 there seems to be no doubt about it.

[049-163]Hardinge is arrived. He has been calling out O'Connell. I am sorry for it,

[049-164]for O'Connell had declared he would not fight. O'Connell had called him the

[049-165]Duke's aide-de-camp. So far it does good, that it lowers O'Connell still

[049-166]more, and destroys the value of anything he might say against Hardinge.

[049-167]_October 26._

[049-168]Called on Hardinge. He says the accounts from Manchester to-day are worse.

[049-169]In the House Lord Hill showed me a letter (from Sir E. Bouverie, I think),

[049-170]giving a very alarming account--30,000 out of work, and apprehension of

[049-171]early collision.

[049-172]Parliament opened. Took the oaths. Office. Lord Dalhousie was so ill on

[049-173]June 4 that I have no idea of his being now Commander-in-Chief in India.

[049-174]Received a summons to a Cabinet at four _precisely_, and went to the

[049-175]Foreign Office; but nobody came. I think it must have been summoned to meet

[049-176]at Peel's house. The times are so critical that I should be sorry to lose a

[049-177]Cabinet. I could not find out that any summonses had been sent from the

[049-178]Foreign Office. There was a crowd of people in Downing Street, who had, I

[049-179]dare say, followed the Duke from the House of Lords. There were a good many

[049-180]about the House. All quite quiet.
