[040-001]I said I did not entertain much fear of the French having Algiers. With a

[040-002]little money we could raise Morocco on one side and Tunis on the other, and

[040-003]harass them from the interior, and while we took care they had not Tunis,

[040-004]Algiers was comparatively unimportant. With Tunis, Malta, and Corfu we

[040-005]should hold our hands across the Mediterranean.

[040-006]I went out and found them come. The Duke went in. The King gives up dining

[040-007]with Leopold. He gave it up the moment the objections to it were mentioned

[040-008]to him.

[040-009]The speech was, I found, much improved after I went away. The King said he

[040-010]thought nothing could be better, and indeed it is a very good speech. He

[040-011]said he thought the reference to the Catholic question was unavoidable, as

[040-012]it was the great measure of the Parliament; and it was particularly proper

[040-013]that he should refer to it as he had voted for it, really thinking that the

[040-014]Church would be more secure by means of Catholic admission than by their

[040-015]exclusion.

[040-016]I thought the King seemed a little tired. Well he might be. He had been at

[040-017]an inspection of troops, the Grenadier Guards and the Lancers, from ten to

[040-018]one, and the day was very hot. He inspected the troops on foot.

[040-019]The Duke of Wellington passed the King at the head of his regiment, and

[040-020]Lord Rosslyn at the head of his. Lord Rosslyn is delighted with the

[040-021]opportunities of wearing his uniform, and playing the general officer

[040-022]again.

[040-023]_July 24._

[040-024]Council at 11. Parliament dissolved. The seals were delivered to the

[040-025]Secretaries and to Goulburn. Herries kissed hands.

[040-026]Sir G. Clark becomes Under-Secretary to the Home Department. W. Peel goes

[040-027]to the Treasury. Charles Ross comes into Clark's place. Macnaughten goes

[040-028]out.

[040-029]_July 26._

[040-030]Dined at St. James's. The King of Wurtemburg, the Ministers, Foreign

[040-031]Ministers, Household, and Knights of the Garter there, in all 80. After

[040-032]dinner the King made a speech which made his Ministers' hearts fail within

[040-033]them. However, we were _quitte pour la peur_. He only spoke of his love of

[040-034]peace. The only thing painful was that he should speak at all, and before

[040-035]his servants, like a chairman of a public meeting.

[040-036]At the Duke of Wellington's on Sunday he made a speech, praising very much

[040-037]the Duke, and declaring his entire confidence in him. This was before the

[040-038]Foreign Ministers. The speech was a little warlike, I believe. The Duke's

[040-039]reply very short indeed, and peaceful. The King should recollect that what

[040-040]he speaks is as important as what is written in a State Paper.

[040-041]_July 28._

[040-042]Levée. Before it a Council, _standing_, in the King's closet, for swearing

[040-043]in Privy Councillors. Sir R. Wilson was presented on his restoration to the

[040-044]army, and holding the King's hand in his expressed his gratitude.

[040-045]The King made an energetic reply, and then there was a short rejoinder from

[040-046]Sir R. Wilson. I could not hear what was said. We afterwards shook hands

[040-047]cordially with Sir R. Wilson, whose restoration pleases everybody.

[040-048]The French Government have dissolved the Chamber without allowing it to

[040-049]assemble; have placed the press under restriction, and altered the mode of

[040-050]electing deputies, so as, as far as I can understand, to give to _les plus

[040-051]imposis_ the power of electing a majority.

[040-052]No letter has been received by any Foreign Minister or by us. The whole was

[040-053]kept a profound secret. The report to the King respecting the press, which

[040-054]is made the foundation of the Ordonnance, is a long violent declamation,

[040-055]very weakly written indeed. [Footnote: These were the celebrated Ordinances

[040-056]which cost Charles X. his crown.]

[040-057]_July 28._

[040-058]Cabinet at half-past three. I was rather late, and found them considering

[040-059]what should be said by Lord Stuart at Paris, respecting the late violent

[040-060]measures of the French Government. They had decided that Lord Stuart, if

[040-061]Prince Polignac endeavoured to draw from him in conversation his opinion,

[040-062]should say he was directed to offer none. They seemed inclined to tell him,

[040-063]if Prince Polignac required his opinion by offering an explanation, to say

[040-064]we considered the measure adopted was in violation of the Charter. At my

[040-065]suggestion, if Polignac asked his opinion more formally and offered no

[040-066]explanation, he was directed to request the explanation might be in

[040-067]writing, and he would transmit it to his Court, or it might be made through

[040-068]the French Ambassador here. The French Ambassador, however, knowing nothing

[040-069]of what was doing, left England on Monday, and would meet the news on his

[040-070]road to Paris.

[040-071]At six o'clock on Tuesday evening a row was going on, and a Guardsman had

[040-072]been killed. This was resistance when the police broke the types, &c., of a

[040-073]press which would go on. The idea is, that the Chamber of Deputies will

[040-074]meet, considering the dissolution to be illegal.

[040-075]Matuschevitz openly inveighs against the measure. It is doubtful whether

[040-076]Metternich did not advise it. He sent a long paper from Johannisberg,

[040-077]giving his views on the present position of the French Government.

[040-078]The King of Wurtemburg had an interview of two hours with the Duke of

[040-079]Wellington yesterday. He is very anxious on the subject of France. He says

[040-080]the people of Wurtemburg will cry out that a similar measure is intended

[040-081]against them--that everywhere the two extreme parties will be placed in

[040-082]collision. Bulow thinks the same. The Duke advised the King of Wurtemburg

[040-083]to avoid Paris on his return; but the King has some _emplettes_ to make,

[040-084]and goes there. The Duke advised him then, if he must go for his

[040-085]_emplettes_, to stay only a day. He said he would not stay above five or

[040-086]six! Thus is every consideration of real importance sacrificed to motives

[040-087]of private fancy and convenience!

[040-088]Lea informed Aberdeen that a vessel was fitting out in the Thames with

[040-089]Spanish refugees and arms to endeavour to raise an insurrection in Spain.

[040-090]After some time they found the vessel, and to-day she was detained. She had

[040-091]sixty-nine men, and about 150 stand of arms on board. They sank the printed

[040-092]proclamations which were picked up. Torrijos and Valdes were to be the

[040-093]leaders. Torrijos was to join below the revenue vessels. Some of the men

[040-094]had 10£ each, given to them by the Spanish Committee, to aid their voyage

[040-095]to Rio. There is some doubt under what law they are to be indicted, and the

[040-096]Attorney and Solicitor-General are out of town.

[040-097]Received a letter from Lord Heytesbury, enclosing one he had received from

[040-098]Captain Campbell, announcing the death of Sir J. Macdonald.

[040-099]_July 29._

[040-100]The Duke told me he had seen Rothschild that morning, who had recent

[040-101]intelligence from Paris. The Guards were faithful, but the 53rd Regiment,

[040-102]which was at the Hôtel de Ville, had joined the people, and so had

[040-103]individual soldiers of other regiments. The people and the National Guards

[040-104]were arming. The Chambers had assembled. The King was not at Paris. He was

[040-105]nought to be at Compiègne.

[040-106]The Duke thought Henry had better not go to Paris, that one party or the

[040-107]other would soon attack the English.

[040-108]Called on Elphinstone. Offered him Persia. He was much obliged, but said

[040-109]nothing would induce him ever to go to Asia again.

[040-110]Spoke to him of Monteith. He knows him, and a little doubted his

[040-111]discretion. He promised to find him, and send him to the Duke if he was in

[040-112]town; but he thought he was at Algiers. Spoke to him of Jenkins and Briggs.

[040-113]He says Jenkins is the abler man.

[040-114]Saw Lord Essex and Lord Clinton. They had heard the Duke of Orleans was

[040-115]proclaimed Regent.

[040-116]_July 31._

[040-117]Went to town early. Called at the Duke's to hear the news. None had arrived

[040-118]since yesterday morning. The Duke said he considered the King dethroned,

[040-119]and we should soon have to consider whether we should acknowledge the new

[040-120]Government. I observed that our course must depend very much upon the

[040-121]manner in which the French effected their Revolution. The King had put

[040-122]himself decidedly in the wrong, and if they make their Revolution as we

[040-123]made ours in 1688, there was no reason why we should not acknowledge the

[040-124]new Government, be it what it might. The Duke said the foreigners were

[040-125]already coming to know what we thought and meant to do. We should have them

[040-126]all in our train, and provided we took a reasonable course on the question

[040-127]of Algiers, and others which might arise, we should do very well. The

[040-128]mischief was that this event would place the two parties in presence on

[040-129]every occasion, and every trifling difference would resolve itself into one

[040-130]of Liberal and anti-Liberal. I said I feared whatever party predominated,

[040-131]even if the King regained his power, France would be precipitated into a

[040-132]war, for no party would be able to maintain itself in quiet times. The Duke

[040-133]said the King's Government was becoming very dangerous. It had, as was

[040-134]shown in the case of Algiers, and their discussions with us, no more

[040-135]morality than that of Buonaparte, and it had the favour of European Powers

[040-136]as an ancient dynasty, while it was prepared to act upon the principles of

[040-137]a new one. Now, under a Government of Revolutionary origin, all their Acts

[040-138]would be viewed with disfavour and suspicion.

[040-139]The Duke spoke very strongly against Canning's policy, in admitting France

[040-140]to the Triple Alliance [Footnote: By the Treaty of London with reference to

[040-141]Greece.] and thus bringing her into a prominent station in Europe again.

[040-142]She would naturally have risen again in good time. The time should not have

[040-143]been anticipated by us.

[040-144]The Duke agreed with me in thinking the Government here would be

[040-145]strengthened by what was occurring in France.

[040-146]I lamented Brougham's success in Yorkshire, and viewed with some

[040-147]apprehension the increased power it would give him. He said Yorkshire was

[040-148]quite radicalised by having four members. No gentleman could bear the

[040-149]expense--the middle classes had it all to themselves.

[040-150]At a quarter to four I called at the Treasury and found Rothschild had

[040-151]received intelligence down to the 29th, at 4 P.M. Drummond showed me the

[040-152]Duke's letter to Peel which contained this account:--That there was

[040-153]fighting all Wednesday, the 28th, and Thursday, till 3 P.M. There had been

[040-154]a terrible massacre, but the troops got the worst of it. The people were

[040-155]led by the students of the Ecole de Droit, and of the Ecole Polytechnique.

[040-156]The 53rd Regiment, which it was said yesterday had joined the people, had

[040-157]in fact surrendered. The people had armed themselves at the Arsenal. On the

[040-158]night of the 28th the Guards retook the Hôtel de Ville, but were driven out

[040-159]again, and retired to the Louvre. The firing did not cease at the Tuileries

[040-160]till past 3 P.M. The people pillaged it when the troops retreated, and the

[040-161]tri-coloured flag was hoisted there, and on the column in the Place

[040-162]Vendôme. The Ministers escaped by subterraneous passages from the

[040-163]Tuileries, and were with the King, who had a great many troops about him at

[040-164]St. Cloud. La Fayette commanded the National Guard, and was a member of the

[040-165]Provisional Government. Generals Gerard, Lafitte, and Casimir Perrier were

[040-166]the others. C. Perrier was deputed to the King at St. Cloud.

[040-167]No couriers were allowed to leave Paris. These letters were sent by private

[040-168]servants to the first stage.

[040-169]I told all this to Henry, but he goes. So do many others. There were thirty

[040-170]people applying for passports when he went for his. On the other hand many

[040-171]English come away.

[040-172]_August 2._

[040-173]There is a great deal of information in the 'Times.' The result is, that

[040-174]the King's offer to change his Ministers and to recall the Ordonnances was

[040-175]not accepted, and the Duke of Orleans accepted the office of Lieutenant-

[040-176]General of the kingdom. His address is quite in the spirit of the

[040-177]Revolution.
