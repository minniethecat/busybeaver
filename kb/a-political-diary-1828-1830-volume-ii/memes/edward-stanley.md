**Edward Smith-Stanley** (1799–1869), known as **Mr. Stanley** during the period of the diary, was a rising political figure who would later become the 14th Earl of Derby and serve three times as Prime Minister. In 1828–1830, he was a Whig MP beginning his ascent in British politics.

== Early Political Career ==

At the time of the diary, Stanley was making a name for himself as an eloquent Whig parliamentarian. The Speaker of the [[House of Commons]] told [[Lord Ellenborough]] that he thought highly of "Mr. Stanley" (008-110) — a significant endorsement from a neutral figure.

Stanley was noted as "being much annoyed" (002-079) by the opposition's tactics, suggesting his growing engagement with parliamentary strategy. His reputation as a speaker was already forming.

== Cabinet Calculations ==

As the Wellington ministry weakened, the Whig opposition began to consider Stanley for government positions. The diary records within the Cabinet discussions: "He is for getting Mr. Stanley, and suggests (or [[Lord Rosslyn|Rosslyn]] did..." (035-127) The entry drive home the point: "But the person to be got is Mr. Stanley." (037-002)

This emphasis on Stanley's recruitment demonstrates that even at this early stage of his career, he was viewed as a significant political asset.

== Later Career ==

Stanley would go on to serve as Chief Secretary for Ireland (1830–1833) under [[Lord Grey]], where he earned the nickname "Scorpion Stanley" for his fierce opposition to Irish nationalism. He later crossed the floor from the [[Whig Party]] to the Conservatives and served as Prime Minister three times: 1852, 1858–1859, and 1866–1868.

He was elevated to the peerage as Baron Stanley in 1844 and succeeded to the earldom in 1851, thereafter being known as the Earl of Derby.
