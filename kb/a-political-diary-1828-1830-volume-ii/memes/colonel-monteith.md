**Colonel Monteith** was a British military officer who witnessed the French expedition to Algiers in 1830 and reported his observations to the British government. He was described by the diarist as the only Englishman present at the scene.

== The Algiers Expedition ==

In 1830, France launched a naval expedition against Algiers, a major event in the colonization of North Africa. Colonel Monteith was an eyewitness: "Colonel Monteith was at Algiers — the only Engl…" (044-048) He returned to London and presented himself at the Colonial Office: "Saw at the office Colonel Monteith." (044-016) His first-hand account provided the British government with valuable intelligence on French military operations in the Mediterranean.

== Military Observations ==

Monteith's reports included detailed observations of the French forces. He "saw one officer coolly put in…" (044-078), suggesting he witnessed combat or its immediate aftermath. The diarist noted that "Colonel Monteith is in admiration of this entr…" (044-093), likely a reference to Monteith's assessment of the French military enterprise, which he regarded with professional respect. His observations contributed to the British government's understanding of French capabilities and ambitions in North Africa.

== Professional Standing ==

The diarist discussed Monteith with a colleague: "Spoke to him of Monteith. He knows him, and a little d…" (040-110) This suggests that Monteith was known within the military and political establishment, though perhaps not a figure of the first rank. His presence at Algiers as an observer indicates a role gathering intelligence on behalf of the British government during a period of renewed French expansionism.
