**Edward Boscawen, 1st Earl of Falmouth** (1787–1841), was a British peer and politician. An Ultra-Tory of the most uncompromising variety, he was a leading figure in the opposition to [[Duke of Wellington|Wellington]]'s ministry over Catholic emancipation.

== Ultra-Tory Opposition ==

Falmouth was among the hardest-line Ultra-Tories in the [[House of Lords]]. During the Catholic emancipation crisis, [[Lord Ellenborough]] records the political alignments of the peerage and notes that while many "parties congratulate the Duke. Falmouth alone still" (001-125) held out in opposition — a testament to his unbending principles even as other Ultra-Tories began to reconcile with Wellington's government.

He is listed among the prominent lords of the Ultra-Tory faction: "The Duke of Cumberland, Falmouth," (001-037) alongside other die-hard opponents of relief. His name appears in the diary's survey of the political landscape alongside "Lords — Falmouth, Penrhyn, Boston, Grantley," (P01-164) — a roll-call of the Upper House's most conservative members.

== Political Alignments ==

Falmouth's political significance extended beyond the Catholic question. In the wider parliamentary battles of the period, he was counted among the government's most persistent critics from the right. The diary describes Ultra-Tory peers rallying around their "valiant champion, Falmouth" (022-124) in the context of opposition to government measures — suggesting he had become something of a standard-bearer for the most conservative faction in the Lords.

He maintained close political alliances with other Ultra-Tory figures. The diary records him alongside "The Duke of Newcastle, Lord Falmouth, Sir E. Knatchbull, Sir R. Vy" (054-129) — a network of conservative peers and MPs who coordinated their opposition to reform.
