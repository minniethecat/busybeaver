[027-001]_April 6._

[027-002]Wrote to the Duke to tell him I had not yet received the Duke of

[027-003]Devonshire's memorandum respecting Sir W. Rumbold, and that in the meantime

[027-004]I was getting into as small a compass as possible the information he

[027-005]desired.

[027-006]I added that the liberation of the Nizam changed our position with respect

[027-007]to Sir W. Rumbold, and I should be glad to speak to him about it.

[027-008]I reminded him of Lord Clare's appointment, not yet approved by the King.

[027-009]_April 8._

[027-010]Cabinet at 2. The Committee on the Bank Charter to be taken out of

[027-011]Huskisson's hands.

[027-012]The King was not well yesterday. The Duke recollected Clare's appointment,

[027-013]and thinks I shall have the warrant in a day or two.

[027-014]_April 9._

[027-015]Wrote to Wrangham, begging him to send me the Cabinet box I desired the

[027-016]Cabinet messenger to take to my house yesterday. I think it contained the

[027-017]papers relative to Russian projects against India.

[027-018]I have been so unwell the last two days I have been unable to do any public

[027-019]business.

[027-020]_April 12._

[027-021]Had some conversation with Hardinge. He thinks the Duke will not remain in

[027-022]office above a year more, and that Peel will then be Minister, and that

[027-023]Peel looks forward to that now. I said I feared he would be a very Radical

[027-024]Minister.

[027-025]Hardinge thinks Sir G. Murray would be very well satisfied to be Master-

[027-026]General, that he feels the Colonial Office is above him. I doubt, however,

[027-027]if he would like leaving it. If Peel was Minister he would have all the

[027-028]Ministers he could in the House of Commons.

[027-029]From what Hardinge heard from Croker I am inclined to think that foolish

[027-030]fellow and others imagine they could go on without Peel.

[027-031]I do not think it impossible we may have a dissolution of Parliament if

[027-032]there should be a good harvest.

[027-033]_April 12._

[027-034]Sent the letter and list of Russian papers about China to the Duke. Wrote

[027-035]to Aberdeen and told him so. Observed at the same time that I should be

[027-036]very glad to make some arrangement with the Portuguese for excluding opium

[027-037]from their Indian ports; but I feared the present state of our relations

[027-038]with Portugal was not favourable for our doing so.

[027-039]_April 13._

[027-040]Found in London the papers I had sent to the Duke. He says he is sorry he

[027-041]has read them. He had thought better of Sir Ch. Metcalfe. The only one of

[027-042]the four who writes _common sense_ is Elphinstone.

[027-043]_April 15._

[027-044]The King was apparently very ill indeed yesterday.

[027-045]Received a medal struck for the native troops engaged in the Burmese war

[027-046]from Loch, and another to be transmitted to the King.

[027-047]_April 16._

[027-048]Saw Hardinge, who called upon me at R.

[027-049]The King has really been very ill, but certainly not worse than the

[027-050]bulletin made him.

[027-051]Sir H. Halford does not go down to-day, nor will there be any more

[027-052]bulletins.

[027-053]Hardinge seems to be dissatisfied with Peel, who he says is cold and never

[027-054]encourages any one. All this is very true.

[027-055]I think Hardinge rather looks to the Colonial Office. He thinks Sir G.

[027-056]Murray does not do the business well, and that he would be perfectly

[027-057]satisfied with the Ordnance. Hardinge does not like Ireland, yet, I think,

[027-058]he will find he goes to Ireland. The Duke certainly wishes it.

[027-059]The Duke of Clarence is very fond of Hardinge, and tells him all he means

[027-060]to do when he is 'King William.' This seems much confined at present to

[027-061]changes in uniforms. He means to make the Blues _red_, and to have gold

[027-062]lace for all the Line, and silver lace for all the Militia.

[027-063]_April 17._

[027-064]Saw Sir A. Campbell at 1. He came about his claim upon the Company. I told

[027-065]him I transacted all business of that nature in writing. I gave him

[027-066]information as to the proposal of the Chairs, which is to give him staff

[027-067]allowances for a year, instead of Batta, by which he would gain about

[027-068]15,850 R., or about 1,580£. What he wants is about 25,000£, or the

[027-069]difference between that and the value of his pension of 1,000£ a year--that

[027-070]is, 15,000£.

[027-071]Went to the Foreign Office. No news there or at the Treasury of the King.

[027-072]The report is that he is better.

[027-073]Read there for an hour and a half.

[027-074]Polignac offers, if it were desired, to sign a Convention upon the

[027-075]principles laid down in Aberdeen's despatch as to Algiers.

[027-076]He seems out of humour altogether with Leopold; Villele seems to have no

[027-077]great disposition to come in, although his friends have. He says the

[027-078]Opposition will in any case have 180 votes in the new Chamber.

[027-079]Spain will withdraw her Minister and have only a Chargé d'Affaires at

[027-080]Lisbon if Don Miguel will not grant the amnesty.

[027-081]France does not remonstrate against the abolition of the Salic Law in

[027-082]Spain, as she precluded herself by treaty from the succession. The law was

[027-083]otherwise in the old Spanish monarchy. [Footnote: The Salic law was

[027-084]introduced by Philip V. of Spain, the first Bourbon king, whose own claim

[027-085]was through his mother, daughter of Louis XIV., who had renounced the

[027-086]succession.] The abrogation of the Salic law is directed against Don

[027-087]Carlos, &c., and the King naturally wishes his own child to succeed, be the

[027-088]child male or female.

[027-089]Saw Mr. Downie on the part of Mr. Chippendale, the man who was removed by

[027-090]the Sign Manual from the service of the India Company. The Court and the

[027-091]Bengal Government did not view his offence in the same light. The poor man

[027-092]is ruined, but the feelings of humanity must not interfere with the

[027-093]interests of the public service. His removal was a good hint to the whole

[027-094]body of civil servants, and did good.

[027-095]_April 18._

[027-096]Brought Lord Clare home after church, and showed him my letter to Mr.

[027-097]Elphinstone respecting the chiefs of Kattywar and the Guicowar. Talked over

[027-098]the policy to be pursued with regard to them.

[027-099]He is to leave England in September, and means to go to Marseilles.

[027-100]_April 19._

[027-101]Lord William seems to have been much gratified by my letters in May and

[027-102]June affording the pledges of my support and the assurances of my

[027-103]confidence. Afterwards, however, he received my letter of July, intimating

[027-104]censure for the relaxations of the rules restricting the residence of

[027-105]Europeans, and a difference of opinion as to the Government leaving

[027-106]Calcutta. His letters are in a very good tone and temper.

[027-107]I sent all the letters to the Duke.

[027-108]_April 20._

[027-109]Drove to the Foreign Office and saw Aberdeen. Went to enquire how the King

[027-110]really was, for the bulletin of yesterday says his difficulty of breathing

[027-111]continues. Aberdeen said the King really was not so ill as the bulletin

[027-112]represented him to be. There was no present danger. The Duke thinks he

[027-113]understands the King's case exactly, and says he has no water on the chest,

[027-114]as is reported, but is _rather fat_. It is said the seat of pain is the

[027-115]prostate gland. The people about him are seriously alarmed.

[027-116]Advised Hardinge, who dined with me, to come forward on the Terceira

[027-117]question, which he seems inclined to do. Peel will be much obliged to him.

[027-118]I told him I thought the strong position was this: 'We are at liberty to

[027-119]prevent that which, if we permitted, would be a cause of war.' I think I

[027-120]shall write a memorandum for him.

[027-121]_April 21._

[027-122]Wrote to Astell to ask if he would buy the Russian China papers. I told him

[027-123]at the same time that a Russian ship was going at the charge of the Russian

[027-124]Government to India, Swan River, and China as a commercial feeler.

[027-125]Cabinet at 2. The King is rather better, but in a precarious state. The

[027-126]embarrassment in his breathing comes on in spasms. His digestion is good,

[027-127]and they think there is no water. The Duke will urge him to have regular

[027-128]bulletins published. He goes down tomorrow. He has not seen him since this

[027-129]day week. The King is in excellent humour with everybody, and never was

[027-130]more kind to the Duke.

[027-131]There has been a short difference between the King and Peel. The King

[027-132]having sent a pardon to Ireland for a Mr. Comyn, who burnt his house to

[027-133]defraud his landlord, &c., Peel insisted, and the man will be hanged; the

[027-134]Lord Lieutenant having taken upon himself to give a reprieve only, and not

[027-135]to promulgate the pardon.

[027-136]The Duke described the King as a bold man, afraid of nothing if his

[027-137]Ministers would stand by him, and certainly neither afraid of pain or of

[027-138]death. I did not think this of the King. In general he has been supposed to

[027-139]be a coward.

[027-140]In Cabinet it was decided to authorise and advise the Lord Lieutenant to

[027-141]put into execution the law for suppressing the association against that

[027-142]which O'Connell is now endeavouring to organise, and at the same time to

[027-143]give silk gowns to Shiel and two or three other Roman Catholic barristers,

[027-144]omitting O'Connell. However, this last measure will be mentioned to the

[027-145]King, although a King's letter is not required.

[027-146]We had afterwards a talk, and a long one, about Algiers.

[027-147]Prince Polignac sent a despatch to the Duc de Laval, giving explanations

[027-148]satisfactory upon the whole, but mixed up with matter accusatory of us. Of

[027-149]this despatch the Duc de Laval was not authorised to give a copy. We want a

[027-150]written declaration of their views, none other being official. They are

[027-151]afraid of their Chambers, and of giving a pledge to England different from

[027-152]that which they have given to other Powers, and with which other Powers

[027-153]have been satisfied. Peel thinks they will promise to abstain from

[027-154]permanent occupation, and exact an amount of indemnity so large, with

[027-155]occupation as a security, as to make that occupation permanent. If they got

[027-156]possession of Algiers, I do not believe they will ever give it up--say

[027-157]what they may.

[027-158]Peel objected to me saying what declaration would satisfy us, as in the

[027-159]event of their deceiving us, or quibbling, it would then seem to be our

[027-160]folly which had led to it.

[027-161]All seem to view the comparative statement of the prices of teas in the

[027-162]same light that I do, as fatal to the monopoly.

[027-163]_April 23._

[027-164]Rode to the Treasury to enquire after the King; but there were so many

[027-165]waiting to see the Duke I did not wait. The King is rather better.

[027-166]_April 24._

[027-167]A letter from Lady Macdonald enclosing one from the Nain Muhan to herself,

[027-168]very complimentary and really pretty. She is to be at Tabriz in October.

[027-169]The King has had two good nights.

[027-170]Peel's letters to the Lord Lieutenant respecting the suppression of the new

[027-171]Association and the appointment of Catholic King's Counsel was circulated.

[027-172]_Sunday, April 25._

[027-173]Read Aberdeen's and the Duke's speeches on the Terceira question, and

[027-174]afterwards wrote a memorandum for Hardinge's use, bringing into a short

[027-175]compass all the strong points of the case.

[027-176]Mr. Sullivan called upon me after church, and told me his son remained in

[027-177]India. It is very extraordinary that he should be glad of this, as he must

[027-178]be without the hope of ever seeing him.

[027-179]_April 26._

[027-180]Cabinet at 3. The King has had another good night. He has, however, had

[027-181]another attack. His pulse is in a weak state. He seems oppressed by fat. He

[027-182]is become alarmed about himself, which much increases danger in such a

[027-183]complaint. Consequently all the _entourage_ is alarmed too.

[027-184]The drawing-room and levee are to be postponed _sine die_. Trade and

[027-185]agriculture are both flourishing. The only embarrassment arises out of the

[027-186]uncertainty as to the King's health.

[027-187]Leopold is to have a loan of sixty millions, guaranteed in equal portions

[027-188]by the three Powers. The loan to have a sinking fund of 3 per cent, to be

[027-189]paid in equal portions in eight years. The guarantee is to Leopold and his

[027-190]descendants, being sovereigns of Greece.
