[020-001]I read Lord Stuart de Rothesay's last despatches and Lord Heytesbury's.

[020-002]There seems to me to be great over-confidence in their strength on the part

[020-003]of the French Ministers. I cannot help thinking they will fall. Villele

[020-004]will have nothing to do with the Government under this House of deputies,

[020-005]which declared his administration _deplorable_. He seems to stipulate for

[020-006]their dissolution.

[020-007]Halil Pacha takes to Petersburg fine presents for the Emperor and Empress,

[020-008]and other presents he is to distribute 'selon son gré et en son nom' which

[020-009]are enough to bribe all the ladies in Europe. There is a list of them

[020-010]extending over seven pages.

[020-011]It seems to be doubtful whether the French have not been endeavouring to

[020-012]induce Mehemet Ali to revenge their quarrel with Algiers by marching along

[020-013]the whole coast of Africa. The French are much out of humour with their

[020-014]Algerine follies, and heartily tired of their expensive gasconade.

[020-015]Mehemet Ali does not seem much inclined to send _his_ fleet to

[020-016]Constantinople, although he has honour enough to send the Sultan's.

[020-017]The Russians have launched two large ships (120 and 74), and they have

[020-018]bought a double-banked frigate built in the United States.

[020-019]_Monday, January 11._

[020-020]At the Cabinet room, where I met Sir George Murray; read the letters

[020-021]relative to the alterations in the judicial system of Scotland.

[020-022]Read a letter from Loch, allowing me to show to the Cabinet Lord William's

[020-023]letters. He wished them to be read, not shown, or rather not circulated;

[020-024]but it is contrary to all rule, so I left them to-day on the Cabinet table.

[020-025]The Duke told me yesterday he felt no concession could now be made,

[020-026]although it was a mighty foolish thing to have had a quarrel about.

[020-027]Got home at 5, dressed, and was going to business, when I found a note from

[020-028]Drummond, desiring me to call on the Duke as soon as I could. I ordered the

[020-029]carriage and went. Found the Chancellor there.

[020-030]It seems there is a great hitch about Prince Leopold's nomination as Prince

[020-031]Sovereign of Greece. The French have now proposed it. We desire it. Russia

[020-032]acquiesces. We have always declared we did not care who was Prince

[020-033]Sovereign of Greece, but we were resolved never to acknowledge as such a

[020-034]man in whom we had not confidence. Some time ago the King of Prussia

[020-035]applied through the Grand Duke of Mecklenburgh to the King for his vote in

[020-036]favour of Prince Charles of Mecklenburgh, the brother of the late Queen of

[020-037]Prussia and of the Duchess of Cumberland. This application was made through

[020-038]the Duke of Cumberland to the King, and the King returned an answer through

[020-039]the Duke of Cumberland. What this answer was is not known; but the King

[020-040]having mentioned the circumstance to Aberdeen, and he to the Duke,

[020-041]Aberdeen, by the Duke's desire, wrote through Sir Brook Taylor to the King

[020-042]of Prussia, and civilly put him off. This letter of course the King saw,

[020-043]and approved. The Duchess of Cumberland complains the answer of Aberdeen

[020-044]was very different from that given through the Duke of Cumberland by the

[020-045]King, and says it is an _intrigue_.

[020-046]The King has been put up to this, and tells Aberdeen he knows his own

[020-047]ground--that the people of England will not bear that 50,000£ a year shall

[020-048]be paid by them to the Prince of Greece. He does not care whether Leopold

[020-049]goes or no, but he is determined he shall leave his annuity behind him.

[020-050]The articles in the 'Standard' and other papers, a few days ago, are

[020-051]supposed to have had reference to this then intended rupture. Aberdeen goes

[020-052]to the King to-morrow, and the Duke having seen all the Cabinet, Aberdeen

[020-053]will, if it should be necessary, declare their concurrent opinion. The Duke

[020-054]thinks the King will yield to Aberdeen; to avoid seeing him--if he is

[020-055]obliged to go down, he will declare distinctly to the King that his Majesty

[020-056]had better name whatever Minister he may wish to give his confidence to;

[020-057]but that to whatever Minister he may choose to have, he ought to give his

[020-058]confidence.

[020-059]Certainly nothing can have been more scandalous than the King's conduct to

[020-060]the Duke. He has never given his Government the fair support. Say what the

[020-061]Duke will, he of Cumberland is believed.

[020-062]The Duke had a note about the King the other day from Lady Conyngham,

[020-063]written only to tell him the Duke of Cumberland had been four hours with

[020-064]His Majesty.

[020-065]That Prince Leopold will make an efficient King of Greece I do not believe;

[020-066]but he is not likely to be hostile to England. Prince Charles of

[020-067]Mecklenburgh, named by Prussia, would be really Russian, and the tool of

[020-068]States not friendly to us.

[020-069]Prince Leopold hopes, if he goes to Greece, that Government will purchase

[020-070]the lands he has bought, for which he has given 40,000£ or 50,000£.

[020-071]Determined to have my letter respecting the acquisition of information in

[020-072]Central Asia and the navigation of the Indus sent to the Chairs _to-

[020-073]morrow_, that _it may_ be sent, and be on record as _mine_, in the event of

[020-074]His Majesty turning me out the next day, as he will very possibly do.

[020-075]_January 12._

[020-076]Henry [Footnote: The Honourable H. S, Law, Lord Ellenborough's brother.]

[020-077]copied for transmission the letter in the Secret Department, and I took

[020-078]care it should be sent to the India House in the course of the day, that if

[020-079]I should be out to-morrow, I may have the credit of having originated a

[020-080]measure which, if effected, will be of incalculable value.

[020-081]Cabinet at 2. Aberdeen was gone to the King at Windsor. It seemed to be

[020-082]expected he would do nothing, and that the Duke would be obliged to go down

[020-083]to-morrow--the Duke thinks he shall succeed--and no one seems to dread a

[020-084]_turn out_. I am not quite so sure. The mischief is that these _sécousses_

[020-085]make a weak Government.

[020-086]I found in the box of drafts the letter to Sir Brook Taylor respecting Duke

[020-087]Charles of Mecklenburgh, which the King says he never saw or sanctioned. It

[020-088]bears his initials and approval, which have been traced out in ink over his

[020-089]pencil.

[020-090]The Duke of Cumberland wants, if it be but for a week, a friendly

[020-091]administration that he may get out of the Exchequer 30,000£ set apart for

[020-092]the annuity for his son's education, but to which he is not legally

[020-093]entitled, his son having been educated abroad. It is out of revenge for a

[020-094]hostile cheer, and to get this money, to which Lord Eldon and Lord Wynford

[020-095]have told him he has no right, that he is endeavouring to overthrow the

[020-096]Government.

[020-097]_January 13._

[020-098]After I came home read the minutes of the Governor-General and Council on

[020-099]the college at Calcutta. There is nothing so important as to preserve young

[020-100]men, who are to govern an Empire, from idleness, dissipation, and debt.

[020-101]This must be done. The Governor-General's own superintendence may effect

[020-102]much. The suspension of the incompetent may do more; but while the habits

[020-103]of expense are given at Hayleybury, and continued by their residence

[020-104]without any control in the midst of a dissipated capital, nothing will

[020-105]reform the system.

[020-106]Cabinet dinner at Aberdeen's. He was an hour and a half with the King

[020-107]yesterday. The King was much agitated in dressing himself for the

[020-108]interview. The man who shaved thought he should have cut him twenty times.

[020-109]He had taken 100 drops of laudanum to prepare himself for the interview.

[020-110]Aberdeen says it is a _real_ quarrel-not a plot to get rid of us--the King

[020-111]thoroughly hates Prince Leopold, and he has been made to think the

[020-112]Ministers have slighted him in this matter. The Duke goes down to him to-

[020-113]morrow. He can show the King that Leopold was first mentioned by France--

[020-114]that he was made acquainted with the proposal or rather suggestion made by

[020-115]France to Leopold on November 9, that he was then told we could not hear of

[020-116]it till our candidates, Prince John of Saxony and Ferdinand of Orange, were

[020-117]disposed of. The subject was again mentioned on November 24.

[020-118]In point of fact the earliest day on which it could have been made known to

[020-119]the King that France had distinctly proposed Leopold was Monday, and he was

[020-120]told on the Tuesday.

[020-121]The King seems to have been violently agitated. He said sneeringly to

[020-122]Aberdeen, '_If I may be allowed to ask, is Prince Leopold to be married to

[020-123]a daughter of the Duke of Orleans?_' [Footnote: This marriage took place in

[020-124]August 1832, when Prince Leopold had become King of the Belgians, and the

[020-125]Duke of Orleans King of the French.] Aberdeen said he had seen it in the

[020-126]newspaper and knew nothing more of it. The King alluded to the possibility

[020-127]of Government going out, admitted the inconvenience just before the meeting

[020-128]of Parliament, but said he was immovable. Leopold might go to the devil,

[020-129]but he should not carry English money out of the country. In the morning,

[020-130]talking to the Duchess of Gloucester, he said, 'If they want a Prince of my

[020-131]family, they might have had the Duke of Gloucester,' upon which the Duchess

[020-132]burst out a-laughing.

[020-133]The King seems thoroughly out of humour. He says 'Things seem going on very

[020-134]ill in India. Do not you mean to recall Lord William?' He had been made

[020-135]very angry in the morning by the 'Times' calling upon him to pay his

[020-136]brother's debts, and this morning the 'Morning Journal' places in

[020-137]juxtaposition the paragraphs in the 'Times,' and those for which it was

[020-138]lately prosecuted.

[020-139]Lady Conyngham is bored to death, and talks and really thinks of removing.

[020-140]She was to make a grand attack on the King to-day. I suppose she finds the

[020-141]Duchess of Cumberland gaining influence. Her note to the Duke the other

[020-142]day, to tell him the Duke of Cumberland had been four hours with the King,

[020-143]was intended to put him upon his guard.

[020-144]The Duke does not mean to resign to-morrow, but to request, if he should

[020-145]not succeed (which Aberdeen thinks he will not do), that the King will

[020-146]allow the Cabinet to put their opinions in writing-which the King cannot

[020-147]refuse. We shall then meet on Friday and decide what we shall do.

[020-148]The Chancellor took me aside and said it would be a foolish thing to go out

[020-149]about Leopold. So it would; but if we allow ourselves to be beaten in this,

[020-150]we may be beaten round the whole circle of public questions.

[020-151]When the Duke has proved the proposition was not made by us, that it came

[020-152]from France, the King will say, 'Well, if you did not think it worth while

[020-153]to propose him, why should you not reject him? Why adhere to him?'

[020-154]I feel very indifferent about the result.

[020-155]Dr. Seymour, Fitzgerald's physician, represents him as very ill indeed, and

[020-156]in _danger_ if he does any business; but Peel, who saw him to-day, thinks

[020-157]that much exaggerated.

[020-158]_January 14._

[020-159]Chairs at 11. I asked them to find out when Rothschild sold out his Indian

[020-160]stock. It seems (by a note I received in the evening) that he began on

[020-161]October 15, and at different times sold out 42,000£ stock. I sent the

[020-162]Chairman's note to Goulburn.

[020-163]About ten received the promised circular from the Duke. He was an hour and

[020-164]a half with the King, when he was obliged to leave him in consequence of

[020-165]his being unwell--and the King afterwards sent to desire he would come

[020-166]again on Saturday.

[020-167]For the first hour the King was in a state of irritated and contemptuous

[020-168]indignation. However, the Duke thinks he brought him to feel he had nothing

[020-169]to complain of in the conduct of his Government. He finished by getting

[020-170]into better temper and a good tone; but the Duke thinks he should have

[020-171]brought away his assent if he had been with him another hour. The Duke

[020-172]wishes to hear the opinion of the Cabinet upon some points, and we meet at

[020-173]two to-morrow.
