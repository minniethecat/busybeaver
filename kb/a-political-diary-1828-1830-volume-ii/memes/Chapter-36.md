[036-001]We had East Retford again. Lord Londonderry, whom Lord Durham puts forward

[036-002]as his tool, moved an adjournment. The question was postponed till Friday.

[036-003]Afterwards the Duke of Buckingham, when most peers had gone away, moved the

[036-004]same thing, and then Lord Londonderry twice. We had majorities but gave it

[036-005]up at last. The Chancellor is heartily tired of the whole thing. The Duke

[036-006]went away while Lord Londonderry was explaining in answer to his speech, to

[036-007]the noble Lord's great annoyance.

[036-008]I rode home with the Duke, who spoke of Lord Londonderry as a madman. He

[036-009]said Peel had not taken a sufficiently high line. He did not like the

[036-010]position he stood in in the House of Commons. The Duke said no Government

[036-011]was ever beaten by its enemies, but many have been by their friends.

[036-012]The King was very amenable and good-natured to-day.

[036-013]_June 30._

[036-014]Occupied all the morning in looking at the precedents in the case of

[036-015]regency. There are two modern contradictory precedents, 24 Geo. II. and 5

[036-016]Geo. III., and no experience of either, nor has there been a minority since

[036-017]Edward VI. in 1547.

[036-018]It is clear the sovereign is sovereign whatever be his age, and the Act

[036-019]appointing a regent must have his assent. Whatever has at any time been

[036-020]done, has been done or sanctioned by Parliament. Parliament cannot

[036-021]supersede the Royal authority.

[036-022]It is remarkable that Parliament in 1811 made provision for the care of the

[036-023]King's person in case of his death; but none for the care of the kingdom in

[036-024]the event of the Regent's death, although the Princess Charlotte was but

[036-025]fifteen.

[036-026]House at 5. The Duke moved the Address in a very short speech, not

[036-027]adverting to the regency. Lord Grey followed and declared his opinion of

[036-028]the incapacity of Government as exhibited in their measures during the last

[036-029]five months. Goderich said 'nothing had been done,' and was for going on

[036-030]with the business. Lord Harrowby wished a short Regency Bill to be passed,

[036-031]giving the regency to the Queen for six weeks, to provide for the case of

[036-032]pregnancy. The Chancellor made a speech, not long, admitting the law to be

[036-033]as stated, that is, that the sovereign immediately on accession possessed

[036-034]all Royal power. Eldon spoke against us, and treated the question of a King

[036-035]_en venire sa mere_ with jocularity. I followed, and observed gravely upon

[036-036]his jocularity on such a subject; then stated my view of the question, and

[036-037]expressed my regret and surprise at Lord Grey's declaration, added I was

[036-038]happy to know at last where we were, who were our friends and who were our

[036-039]enemies.

[036-040]Then got up the Duke of Richmond, totally misrepresenting what I had said

[036-041]as to Lord Eldon and Lord Grey, and endeavouring to make them appear as

[036-042]personal attacks to which no gentleman could submit. Lord Londonderry

[036-043]followed in the same tone. (After the Duke of Richmond I explained that I

[036-044]had not attributed improper motives to Lord Grey, nor attacked Lord Eldon's

[036-045]character.) We had afterwards Lord Lansdowne, Lord Harewood giving his

[036-046]first vote for the Government after the Catholic Question, and _that_

[036-047]because it was the first measure of the new King. A foolish reason, but I

[036-048]dare say many voted on the same ground. Lord Wharncliffe spoke against us,

[036-049]Lords Bute and Wicklow and the Duke of Buckingham for us, Lord Radnor

[036-050]shortly against. The Duke replied. Then Lord Grey spoke, and observed, of

[036-051]course, upon what I had said, but not angrily, and I made an explanation

[036-052]which was satisfactory, and set us quite right again. He had imagined me to

[036-053]say he owed a debt of gratitude to the Government for the measure of last

[036-054]session. I said he had expressed gratitude, but we had not claimed it,

[036-055]because we only did our duty. In the lobby during the debate Lord Jersey

[036-056]told me he was afraid Lord Grey might have misunderstood the meaning of

[036-057]what I said about gratitude, and begged me to set him right immediately if

[036-058]it was so.

[036-059]We had 100 to 54. A very good division. We went, at ten, to Goulburn's to

[036-060]dinner, and expected soon to see the members of the House of Commons, and

[036-061]to hear of as good a division there as in the Lords, but after an hour we

[036-062]heard the division had only been 185 to 139. This made us a little flat,

[036-063]and Lord Bathurst drank no more champagne.

[036-064]I intentionally committed the Government thoroughly with the Whigs, for

[036-065]after Lord Grey's declaration it was idle to expect a vote from them, and

[036-066]our people were pleased, as I knew they would be. The Duke of Bedford and

[036-067]Lord Jersey voted with us. So did Dudley.

[036-068]I shall have work enough now, as they have ten or twelve speakers, and we

[036-069]but three.

[036-070]_July 1._

[036-071]Looked over the debates on the Forgery Bill this morning. Committee at one.

[036-072]Examined a manufacturer of camlets and bombazines from Norwich. House.

[036-073]Forgery Bill. The Chancellor made an admirable speech, Lord Lansdowne

[036-074]followed him, then Lords Wynford, Tenterden, and Eldon all against the

[036-075]bill. We divided 77 to 20. The Duke was delighted, he said, '_How very

[036-076]right we were._' So said the Chancellor. Peel would have given it up. Now,

[036-077]I think one large majority will set public opinion right again. The

[036-078]Chancellor said all that was contained in Peel's two speeches and much

[036-079]more. Peel and Brougham were under the throne.

[036-080]Lord Bathurst, with whom I walked home from the House at three, when we

[036-081]talked of Goulburn's becoming Speaker, suggested Hardinge as Chancellor of

[036-082]the Exchequer. He would be an excellent one.

[036-083]I met Goulburn in the Park this morning. He did not seem much pleased with

[036-084]the House last night. I see there were strong words indeed in the second

[036-085]debate, Brougham talking of the _parasites_ of the Duke of Wellington. Peel

[036-086]asked whether he presumed to call him a parasite? There was great

[036-087]confusion, and it ended by Peel's making an explanation for Brougham, in

[036-088]which Brougham acquiesced. Several members, amongst the rest, I hear,

[036-089]Castlereagh, were going to call Brougham out.

[036-090]In the House Lord Bathurst told me Wortley had stayed away from the

[036-091]division last night, and had sent in his resignation. Soon after I received

[036-092]a note from Wortley telling me so, expressing great regret that he could

[036-093]not vote for a course of measures which excluded a Regency Bill. His regret

[036-094]was increased by my kindness and encouragement. I have sent his letter to

[036-095]the Duke, having shown it to Lord Bathurst in the House. I wrote an answer

[036-096]to say I felt great regret at his not being able to adopt our line, and

[036-097]expressing my personal regret at losing him, and my acknowledgments for the

[036-098]assistance I had derived from him.

[036-099]His father and father-in-law both voted against us last night. He says in

[036-100]his note he has taken his line entirely on his own view.

[036-101]I had some talk with Dudley in the lobby of the House. I began by saying he

[036-102]had acted very handsomely by us. He said he was friendly to the Government,

[036-103]and above all things unfriendly to Lord Grey and the Duke of Newcastle. The

[036-104]motion of last night he called pure faction.

[036-105]Salisbury told me he stayed away to-night not liking to vote against us, on

[036-106]account of yesterday's declaration of war. The Duke of Gordon told me he

[036-107]was much pleased with me last night. I do not, however, think I spoke as

[036-108]well as usual.

[036-109]Bankes I had some talk with. He said the Duke of Cumberland was hostile to

[036-110]the Duchess of Kent and Leopold. He would prefer the Queen as Regent. He

[036-111]had been much with the King for the last six weeks, and there was a good

[036-112]understanding between them. Bankes asked if I had left my name with him. I

[036-113]told him I had, and I believed all the rest had. By some mistake of a

[036-114]servant the summons to the Privy Council did not reach the Duke of

[036-115]Cumberland till the day after the accession, and he was very angry. It had

[036-116]been sent to Kew. He is satisfied now. Goulburn has hit upon a _mezzo

[036-117]termine_ which answers for the present session. He has reduced the duty on

[036-118]West Indian sugar to 24,9., and on East Indian sugar to 32s. The duty on

[036-119]other sugar to be 63s. I did not fail to tell Dudley and Bankes in what

[036-120]strong terms the King had expressed his determination to support the

[036-121]Government. They were both 'colpiti.' Dudley had had no idea terms so

[036-122]strong had been used. He comes to the Council to be sworn in on Saturday.

[036-123]_July 2._

[036-124]Chairs at eleven. They have sent a representation on the subject of the

[036-125]Kattywar draft, impugning, as I understand, for I have not yet read it, the

[036-126]power of the Board to give orders in the Secret Department which do not

[036-127]require secresy.

[036-128]I told the Chairs distinctly that I intended to take upon the King's

[036-129]Government the whole responsibility of the foreign policy of India.

[036-130]I saw Wortley, who thanked me very much indeed for my note of yesterday

[036-131]evening. He was much distressed, and evidently regrets extremely that he

[036-132]has tendered his resignation. He adheres, however, to his opinion that the

[036-133]Regency question should have been settled at least provisionally before

[036-134]Parliament separated. He was going to see Peel and afterwards the Duke.

[036-135]He told me the Government could not be conducted in the House of Commons

[036-136]unless some more Ministers would speak-that there must be a change.

[036-137]I called at Hardinge's. He told me the same thing, and that he had talked

[036-138]about it to the Duke yesterday and made him promise to place the

[036-139]ministerial seats in the House of Commons at Peel's disposal. Hardinge is

[036-140]for having Edward Stanley. He spoke of Wilmot Horton, but he is not of

[036-141]Cabinet calibre. I think Hardinge is disposed to displace Murray rather

[036-142]than either of the others. He talked again of making Peel First Lord of the

[036-143]Treasury and Chancellor of the Exchequer, and the Duke Secretary of State

[036-144]for Foreign Affairs--Aberdeen going to Ireland. Aberdeen would not go

[036-145]there, I think. I told Hardinge Lord Bathurst had suggested him as

[036-146]Chancellor of the Exchequer. He would not hear of it.

[036-147]It seems Brougham was almost drunk the other night. Hardinge and several

[036-148]others were getting up to question him when Peel stopped them. He pulled

[036-149]Hardinge down by the coat. Hardinge says Peel managed admirably.

[036-150]In the House spoke to the Chancellor and Lord Bathurst, and told them I had

[036-151]heard we must have an addition of strength to the Treasury Bench. They both

[036-152]said they believed so too. Lord Bathurst again mentioned Hardinge.

[036-153]Spoke to the Duke about Wortley. He said he had written a kind note to him,

[036-154]and told him he had been too hasty. He should have spoken to some of the

[036-155]Ministers first. The Duke evidently intends the thing to blow over.

[036-156]Spoke to Lord Wharncliffe about the same thing. He said he would neither

[036-157]have voted nor have spoken against Government on Wednesday if he had had an

[036-158]idea of Wortley's resigning, because it gave the appearance of concert, and

[036-159]there really was none. He did not know of the letters till after they had

[036-160]been written. I said Lord Harrowby's taking the same line, both voting and

[036-161]speaking, gave the appearance of concert. He said he thought Wortley

[036-162]altogether wrong. That a young man, having joined a Government, had no

[036-163]right, for a difference on a single point, to resign. If he differed upon a

[036-164]system of policy it was another thing. I said I thought it would be allowed

[036-165]to blow over. He afterwards talked to the Duke, and I have no doubt Wortley

[036-166]will remain.
