[032-001]The Commons passed the King's Signature Bill without a word.

[032-002]I thought it necessary to determine at once who should be the new judge at

[032-003]Bombay, and upon full consideration thought Awdry the best man. The

[032-004]Chancellor had no objection, and I immediately wrote to Awdry to tell him I

[032-005]should advise the King to appoint him.

[032-006]_May 29._

[032-007]Before the Cabinet met Hardinge and walked some time up and down Downing

[032-008]Street with him. He told me the Duke had proposed an exchange between him

[032-009]and Lord F. Leveson. Hardinge declined; however, he was at last induced to

[032-010]acquiesce. There cannot be a better thing for him, for the Government, and

[032-011]for Ireland, than his going there. I have always told him so. We may now be

[032-012]satisfied things will go on well there. Lord F. Leveson is a mere boy, and

[032-013]quite unequal to the situation. Hardinge will do admirably and be very

[032-014]popular. So will she. They will like an Irishwoman.

[032-015]_June 1._

[032-016]The King had a quiet night. In other respects he is much the same.

[032-017]_June 2._

[032-018]Employed all the morning on the Greek papers. Cabinet dinner at Peel's. The

[032-019]King rather better. They have opened punctures above the knees. 400 papers

[032-020]were stamped. Lord Farnborough was the stamper. The King was perfectly

[032-021]alive to all that was going on.

[032-022]A steamboat has made the passage from Bombay to Suez in a month and two

[032-023]days, leaving Bombay on March 20 and reaching Suez on April 22. The letters

[032-024]arrived here on May 31. The steamboat was detained ten days for coals.

[032-025]There was no steam conveyance from Alexandria to Malta, so we may reckon

[032-026]upon gaining fourteen days at least upon this passage. Besides, the steam

[032-027]vessel was probably a bad one.

[032-028]_June 3._

[032-029]House. Aberdeen, in reply to a question of Lord Londonderry's, promised all

[032-030]the protocols of Paris! A most voluminous mass of dull twaddle. The House

[032-031]postponed Miss Hickson's divorce case to Lord Salisbury and East Retford.

[032-032]We had only 18 to 69! The Duke seemed very angry, and I heard him speaking

[032-033]to Lord Bathurst of some peer who went out without voting, whose conduct

[032-034]seemed to make him very indignant.

[032-035]_June 4._

[032-036]House. All seems quiet again. Nothing more said about Leopold. There was to

[032-037]be a meeting to-day at Lord Lansdowne's which the Duke of Newcastle was

[032-038]expected to attend. Palmerston was at the last. [Footnote: The conjunction

[032-039]of these names indicated an alliance of Whigs, Canningites, and Tories

[032-040]irritated by the Roman Catholic Bill.] Rosslyn does not know whether Lord

[032-041]Grey was.

[032-042]The King not going on well by the bulletin; worse by the private account,

[032-043]which, however, I did not see. He has lost his appetite and grows weaker.

[032-044]The Duke has not yet read my Nagpore letter; but he will to-morrow. He

[032-045]seems to agree with me in general views upon the subject of our policy

[032-046]towards the native States.

[032-047]_June 5._

[032-048]Chairs at 11. They are dissatisfied with Malcolm for sending a steam vessel

[032-049]into the Red Sea, because he had no important intelligence to communicate!

[032-050]I shall never make these people feel they are at the head of a _State!_

[032-051]The bulletin to-day is very alarming. The Duke had not returned at half-

[032-052]past 4; but soon after he was seen coming into town looking very

[032-053]melancholy. The Duchess of Gloucester arrived an hour later. I thought the

[032-054]Duke had stayed to be there at the King's death. Knighton sent up to

[032-055]Goulburn to desire a warrant might be sent down to be stamped conveying the

[032-056]King's fines, &c., belonging to the Privy Purse.

[032-057]Goulburn very properly refused to send the warrant till he had seen the

[032-058]Duke. This looks as if they did not expect 24 hours.

[032-059]He was as ill as possible when Aberdeen saw him yesterday for a few

[032-060]minutes.

[032-061]A Cabinet is summoned for half-past 3 to-morrow.

[032-062]All is still again in the House of Commons, as well as with us. They have

[032-063]found the Leopold line will not do.

[032-064]_June 6._

[032-065]Cabinet at half-past 3. They all say Scarlett did ill. He did not fight

[032-066]gallantly, and he fought without judgment.

[032-067]The Duke said he thought the King was _really_ suffering yesterday; but

[032-068]from several circumstances he thought he would live three or four weeks.

[032-069]The physicians said eight days. He was better than when Aberdeen saw him on

[032-070]Friday. No stamping was done. Peel went down to-day. It was hoped some

[032-071]papers would be stamped. Peel had not returned when the Cabinet separated

[032-072]at 5.

[032-073]Aberdeen brought forward the question of a Bill it is thought necessary to

[032-074]introduce in consequence of slave-dealing by Brazilian subjects having now

[032-075]become piracy.

[032-076]Goulburn seems to be unable to fix any time for the conclusion of the

[032-077]Session in the event of a demise. I fear it will be necessary to sit a long

[032-078]time to get the necessary votes. There are no less than fifty subjects

[032-079]unvoted.

[032-080]_June 7._

[032-081]House. In going down met Goulburn, who said the account of the King was

[032-082]very bad. Halford had suggested it would be better for the Duke to go down;

[032-083]which he did. Peel thought the King very much changed indeed in the week

[032-084]which had elapsed since he last saw him.

[032-085]_June 8._

[032-086]Cabinet at 3. The diplomatic expenses were carried only by 18, and the

[032-087]abolition of the punishment of death for forgery was carried by 13. This is

[032-088]a very serious state of things; with such a Parliament there is no

[032-089]depending upon the carrying of any measure, and Peel is quite disgusted. As

[032-090]to the Forgery Bill it will be difficult to find juries to convict when a

[032-091]majority has decided against the punishment of death. I am satisfied that

[032-092]the property of many will be exposed to much danger by the abolition of the

[032-093]punishment of death.

[032-094]One Ashe who has libelled the Duke of Cumberland, or written a threatening

[032-095]letter, will be prosecuted as if he had done the same thing against any

[032-096]private individual.

[032-097]The Fee Bill will be altered in the Committee (which out of delicacy is

[032-098]indefinitely postponed) and the commissioners continued by endorsement.

[032-099]This is a very ingenious device, saving all the difficulty of dealing with

[032-100]patent offices and of sharing the present fees.

[032-101]Lord Combermere has written a letter to the Duke explaining and defending

[032-102]his conduct. This is a trouble brought upon us by Astell. He has written

[032-103]rather an impertinent answer to my letter respecting the 600£ for the

[032-104]Russian papers, or rather some one has written it for him and he has only

[032-105]signed it.

[032-106]I find Mr. Archibald Campbell, who applied yesterday to me for an

[032-107]assistant-surgeoncy, is Campbell of Blytheswood, a good voter and a great

[032-108]friend of Lord Melville's, and others. I have given him the surgeoncy. I

[032-109]told Planta, who is much pleased.

[032-110]The Duke was sent for because the physicians intended to acquaint the King

[032-111]with his danger.

[032-112]He was restless yesterday. The bulletin says he passed a very distressing

[032-113]day. He walked across the room, however, and will probably last some days.

[032-114]In the House, East Retford till 8, when I came away.

[032-115]_June 9._

[032-116]A better bulletin. Office before 12. Settled with Wortley the 'reasons' for

[032-117]abolishing the College. [Footnote: Haileybury.]

[032-118]At 3 Sir P. Freeling came. Went with him and Wortley to Lord Melville's.

[032-119]There will be no difficulty in getting the steam vessel to Alexandria.

[032-120]Read Colonel Macdonald's Journal for January, February, and to March 10. It

[032-121]is not so interesting as the last portion, or rather not so entertaining.

[032-122]These make no doubt from the account of Khosroo Murza and of the others who

[032-123]went to Petersburg, that the conquest of India by the route of Khiva and

[032-124]Bokhara is the favourite object of the Russians, and the whole people seem

[032-125]animated by hatred of England.

[032-126]Cabinet dinner _chez moi_. The Duke did not see the King to-day; the Dukes

[032-127]of Clarence and Cumberland being there, whom he did not wish to see. The

[032-128]King is better. There is coagulated lymph in his legs, one thigh, Tierney

[032-129]thinks, is a little swelled. He has had no embarrassment of breathing for

[032-130]thirty-six hours, and slept yesterday as soundly as a child.

[032-131]The man who was with the Queen and the Duke of York when they died is with

[032-132]the King now. When the King was sleeping yesterday Knighton said to him,

[032-133]'This is not the sleep of death!' The other answered, 'Lord, sir! he will

[032-134]not die!' They think the King has never thought himself in danger, not even

[032-135]when they told him he was. He seemed flurried, however, or they thought so,

[032-136]for a moment, and then they endeavoured to unsay; but the King, who was

[032-137]quite firm, said, 'No, no! I understand what you think. Call in the Bishop

[032-138]and let him read prayers.'

[032-139]Last night he was talking a great deal to Knighton, and was as amusing as

[032-140]ever. In constitution and in mind he is certainly a wonderful man. I have

[032-141]no doubt that the feeling that he is always in representation makes him

[032-142]behave in the face of death as a man would on the field of battle.

[032-143]_June 10._

[032-144]The King passed a restless night. He is weaker than he has been yet.

[032-145]East Retford. Salisbury concluded his case.

[032-146]_June 11._

[032-147]House. I expected to get away immediately; but Lord Londonderry made a

[032-148]motion for papers, which led to a discussion of an hour and a half. He was

[032-149]put down entirely by Aberdeen, who really, with a bad manner, said very

[032-150]good things. At last Lord Londonderry chose to say the Contents had it and

[032-151]did not divide, so that the motion was negatived _nemine contradicente_.

[032-152]Most scandalously many went out, not voting against the motion after

[032-153]Aberdeen had declared it would be injurious to the public service to give

[032-154]the Papers.

[032-155]The King rather better, but weaker.

[032-156]_June 12._

[032-157]Chairs. They did not come till half-past 11. I began to think they had

[032-158]taken huff and did not mean to come at all, as I had taken no notice of

[032-159]Astell's letter. However, they came. They do not much like my Nagpore

[032-160]letter, which it seems is contrary to the line of policy laid down by the

[032-161]Court and approved of by Wynne. I told them I took the responsibility upon

[032-162]myself. They were ministerial only. My opinion was confirmed by that of

[032-163]Jenkins and of the Duke.

[032-164]Met at dinner, at Hardinge's, Arbuthnot, with whom I had some conversation

[032-165]about the Report he is writing on the China Evidence. He is to show it to

[032-166]me. The Duke saw the King, who is much better. The King said he would defer

[032-167]taking the sacrament till he was well; but he takes it to-morrow as a

[032-168]_convalescent_.

[032-169]_June 13._

[032-170]Cabinet at half-past 3. First considered the line to be adopted on the

[032-171]Forgery Bill, which seems to be to allow it to pass unaltered, throwing the

[032-172]whole responsibility on the House of Commons; but Peel is to see the

[032-173]bankers and merchants that he may ascertain what their opinions are now the

[032-174]Bill has passed the Commons abolishing the punishment of death for forgery.

[032-175]Peel's idea is that no conviction would be obtained.

[032-176]I believe the French and the Russians are so alarmed by the effect produced

[032-177]in France by the continued exhibition of democratic violence in Greece and

[032-178]successful rebellion, that they would be disposed to enter into our views

[032-179]with respect to the nomination of a prince rather than leave the question

[032-180]open; but that they will procrastinate if they find we will unite with them

[032-181]in giving money which may keep Greece in a state of tranquillity. As to

[032-182]Capo d'Istria, he first wished to prevent the nomination of any prince and

[032-183]to keep the government to himself. When he found that would not do, he

[032-184]endeavoured to frighten Leopold into subserviency; but if he finds he can

[032-185]get money without having a prince, he will frighten other princes and

[032-186]remain there himself.
