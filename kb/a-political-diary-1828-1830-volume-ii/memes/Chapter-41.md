[041-001]The Guards are disorganised and desert.

[041-002]The Swiss only are said to remain with the King, who it is thought is gone

[041-003]to Nantes.

[041-004]Lord Stuart says if the Royalists do not resist, the French will invade

[041-005]Belgium in three months. The Deputies, at first in very small numbers, not

[041-006]more than thirty, nor at any time much above sixty, seem to have been

[041-007]irresolute. They were decided by others, and indeed the whole seems to have

[041-008]been done by the people. There is no appearance of previous concert. If

[041-009]there were leaders, they were the boys of the Ecole de Droit and the Ecole

[041-010]Polytechnique. Polignac seems to have been firm after the beginning of the

[041-011]fight, and when Lafitte and others went to Marmont at the Tuileries, in the

[041-012]middle of the tumult, he declared concession impossible.

[041-013]The Guards at St. Cloud told the King they would protect him, but would not

[041-014]advance again to Paris. General [blank] seems to have had 6,000 men at

[041-015]Versailles, but the people would not admit him. At Rouen there was great

[041-016]ferment, and forty pieces of cannon were sent by the people to the

[041-017]assistance of Paris. The troops seem to have been ordered upon Paris from

[041-018]all quarters. The total loss of life is estimated at 5,000.

[041-019]The people were becoming impatient, and cried _Vive la République! Vive

[041-020]Napoleon II._! This, it is said, determined the Duke of Orleans to accept:

[041-021]and the Deputies offered, because they feared the establishment of a

[041-022]Republic would be the signal of general war.

[041-023]I do not hear of the pillage of private houses. The churches have been

[041-024]pillaged and the palaces ransacked. The priests thought fit to fire from

[041-025]the Archbishop's palace, which led to the death of many and to the pillage

[041-026]of the palace.

[041-027]The Duke said they had done everything in the most offensive way, re-

[041-028]establishing the tri-coloured flag, &c. They seem determined to force the

[041-029]Revolution down the throat of Europe. He spoke of the Duke of Orleans'

[041-030]address. I said I supposed he was obliged for his own safety to throw

[041-031]himself at once into the Revolution. The more natural thing would have been

[041-032]for the French to have sent for young Napoleon. The Duke said he heard

[041-033]young Napoleon was getting hold of French pamphlets, &c.

[041-034]The Duke of Orleans asked Lord Stuart's advice as to accepting the Crown.

[041-035]Lord Stuart reminded him of his oath, and told him the Powers of Europe

[041-036]which restored the Bourbons could never recognise him.

[041-037]On consideration I think we should endeavour to induce the Powers which

[041-038]signed the Treaty of Vienna to declare that they are determined to maintain

[041-039]the territorial arrangements made by that treaty; but that they will not

[041-040]interfere with the internal Government of France.

[041-041]I think this declaration, made at once, would perhaps prevent any attempt

[041-042]on the part of the French to make war for the frontier of the Rhine.

[041-043]The elections go well for us, except Canterbury, where Lord Fordwich has

[041-044]beat our man, Henry B. Baring, the husband of Lady Augusta.

[041-045]_August 3._

[041-046]The accounts from Paris state that the Due de Mortemar, who had been

[041-047]appointed Minister by Charles X. himself, saw it was too late, and that the

[041-048]only chance for the House of Bourbon was in the placing the Duke of Orleans

[041-049]in the office of Lieut.-General.

[041-050]This he proposed himself to the Duke of Orleans, who wrote to the King, and

[041-051]in accepting the office said his conduct would show with what views he did

[041-052]it. Then he issued a tri-colour proclamation! Lord Stuart says this was

[041-053]done at the last possible moment. The proclamation was received with cries

[041-054]of _Vive la Republique! Vive Napoleon II._! However, these cries ceased,

[041-055]and it was hoped things would go on quietly. Sebastiani and B. Constant

[041-056]expressed hopes that in a few months men's minds would be tranquillised,

[041-057]and things placed on a regular footing It seems that the King is at

[041-058]Trianon, with about 4,000 guards. He talked of resigning to the Dauphin, if

[041-059]he had not already done so. It will probably be too late, and the Dauphin

[041-060]is supposed, I believe very justly, to be implicated in all that has

[041-061]passed.

[041-062]Lord Stuart states the loss of the troops at 3,000. That of the people at

[041-063]6,000. Of course these calculations are very vague, and probably

[041-064]exaggerated. It would appear as if there had been more preparation on the

[041-065]part of the insurgents than was imagined. The decisive measure, that of the

[041-066]Bank refusing discounts, was of course suggested by Lafitte. The Royalists

[041-067]are much in want of money. They left forty-two millions in their caisses,

[041-068]and 150 millions at the Bank! Bourmont was to leave Algiers on the 25th.

[041-069]Probably he was called home to be present at the crisis.

[041-070]The King's troops still remaining in force at St. Cloud, the barricades are

[041-071]continued.

[041-072]Everybody seems to think the military force was as ill-managed as

[041-073]everything else. Marmont acted _mollement_.

[041-074]We have been beaten at Canterbury, and what is worse at Norwich, where a

[041-075]brother of Peel's has been driven out by Robert Grant, the most decided

[041-076]enemy of the Government. No one declares himself the opponent of

[041-077]Government, and as such asks support; but our candidates do not succeed at

[041-078]popular elections.

[041-079]_August 4._

[041-080]To London early. The King of France is supposed to be gone towards

[041-081]Cherbourg. We fear he will come here. The Duke said the King seemed

[041-082]disposed to receive him, and reminded the King that the Pretender had been

[041-083]three times ordered out of Paris on the representations of this country. I

[041-084]was glad to find a very general feeling that the King of France could not

[041-085]be permitted to remain if intrigues were allowed by him. That he could have

[041-086]no more than a refuge. Peel seemed to feel this most strongly. The Duke

[041-087]seemed to think there had been previous concert on the part of the

[041-088]_patriots_.

[041-089]The King is violent against the Duke of Orleans.

[041-090]Our Duke of Orleans, as I call him, the Duke of Sussex, sticks close to the

[041-091]King whenever he appears.

[041-092]The Duke of Cumberland has resigned the Blues in a huff because they are

[041-093]placed under the Commander-in-Chief. However, he wore the uniform to-day at

[041-094]the levée.

[041-095]We have a Cabinet to-morrow at 4, on Civil List and Regency. Indeed we know

[041-096]not how soon we may meet Parliament. Perhaps on September 15.

[041-097]The Queen received the address of the London clergy. She had her whole

[041-098]_état major_.

[041-099]_August 5._

[041-100]At four Cabinet. Talk about the Civil List. There are pensions to the

[041-101]amount of about 7,000£ a year which the present King will pay, and he will

[041-102]pay 6,000£ a year to Mrs. Fitzherbert, her charge on Brighton. She had

[041-103]10,000£ a year before. Many pensions are struck off, one of 500£ to Sir J.

[041-104]Lake, many others, to jockies, &c.

[041-105]It seems the late King borrowed 50,000£ for himself and as much for the

[041-106]Duke of York, on the revenues of Hanover, which sums have been paid off.

[041-107]The King of France abdicated, and so did the Dauphin, in favour of the Duc

[041-108]de Bordeaux, in a letter addressed by them to the Duke of Orleans, in which

[041-109]his Lieut.-Generalship was treated as emanating from the King. The Duke of

[041-110]Orleans in his speech to the Chambers announced the abdications, but did

[041-111]not say they were in favour of Henry V. Hence the people of Paris, hearing

[041-112]the King made difficulties, supposed he had receded from his original

[041-113]promise--whereas he only said his original promise was conditional, _and

[041-114]had not_ been fairly made known. Be this as it may, 35,000 men set off for

[041-115]Rambouillet to take him, 10,000 were sent afterwards by the Duke of Orleans

[041-116]to protect him, and he has 7,000 at Rambouillet, chiefly cavalry and

[041-117]artillery, for the same purpose. I think there must be a smash.

[041-118]Stuart and Pozzo went to the Duke of Orleans to represent the personal

[041-119]danger of the King, and to desire that measures might be taken to preserve

[041-120]his life. The Duke is represented as having been _très ému_, and as having

[041-121]said that his character depended upon the preservation of the King's life,

[041-122]and the measures I mentioned were immediately taken.

[041-123]Chateaubriand and Hyde de Neuville are for the Due de Bordeaux.

[041-124]Stuart has, I know not why, counselled the Duc de Bordeaux's friends to be

[041-125]quiet.

[041-126]The Duke of Wellington thinks there is Radicalism in everything-that the

[041-127]Lieut.-General will have no power.

[041-128]The King went in grand state through the City to the Tower. He had six

[041-129]carriages and six. At the Tower the Duke gave him a breakfast. He then went

[041-130]on to Greenwich by water, and returned to London by land. He was very well

[041-131]received.

[041-132]_August 6._

[041-133]At the dinner we had the Ministers, Household, and Trinity House. Chairman

[041-134]and deputy-Chairman of the East India Company, Governor and deputy-Governor

[041-135]of the Bank, Lord Mayor, and Ward and Thompson, members for the City. The

[041-136]King made speeches and gave toasts as if he was Duke of Clarence at the

[041-137]Trinity House. He alarmed and pained us, but he did less mischief than I

[041-138]should have expected; and as all the people present were real friends, he

[041-139]only let down the dignity of the Crown.

[041-140]He gave the healths of the Ministers, and afterwards of the Duke of

[041-141]Wellington. Some things he said very well. The Duke answered very well.

[041-142]There is so much good feeling about the King that his errors of taste are

[041-143]pardoned. He will improve, and wear his robes more gracefully.

[041-144]_August 7._

[041-145]Cabinet. Determined that the principle of the Regency Bill should be that

[041-146]the mother of the Sovereign should be Regent. The Regent to have unlimited

[041-147]power. If any limitation, it should only be placed upon the creation of

[041-148]Peers, and a Council of Regency should exist only for that purpose.

[041-149]We separated till the 23rd.

[041-150]_August 9._

[041-151]In coming down to Sandgate read James's and Cabell's memoranda on the

[041-152]progress of the British Government in India, and our foreign relations.

[041-153]As I was coming out of Maidstone met the candidates coming in. Sir E.

[041-154]Knatchbull in a cocked hat, attended by thirty or forty gentlemen in black,

[041-155]all covered with dust, preceded by about six blue flags, and followed by

[041-156]some carriages with ugly women. Then came T. Law Hodges (why _Law_ I do not

[041-157]know), with many light blue flags, and some low people--few gentlemen. The

[041-158]numbers, however, of the Hodges colours and people were greater than that

[041-159]of the Knatchbull squad. Not a cheer for either. The whole thing flat and

[041-160]ridiculous--worthy of Hogarth. There were some people collected in

[041-161]Maidstone, but not so many as on a market day--there were none on the

[041-162]roads.

[041-163]By the 'Times' I see the Chamber has modified the Charter, and has

[041-164]proclaimed the Duke of Orleans King of the French; at least has offered him

[041-165]the Crown on the condition of his acceptance of the modified Charter.

[041-166]The Chamber of Peers is put by. It is only advised to _eliminate_ the last

[041-167]seventy-six peers.

[041-168]_August 10._

[041-169]Briscoe comes in for Surrey, to the exclusion of Jolliffe, our friend.

[041-170]Certainly the popular elections have all been unfavourable to us. In fact

[041-171]the Tories have not yet recovered their good-humour, and the Government has

[041-172]some furious enemies, and no warm friends. I do not think we can go on

[041-173]without an accession of speaking strength. Our measures must be modified to

[041-174]meet the circumstances of the times, and so far I have no fear.

[041-175]_August 13._

[041-176]Cabinet room. Read Lord Stuart's despatches. There is little in them that

[041-177]is not in the newspapers. He says the Revolution has been brought about by

[041-178]small proprietors acting under the influence of bankers and lawyers. The

[041-179]troops have shown no great popular feeling. Many have taken the opportunity

[041-180]of going home.
