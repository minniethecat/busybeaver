# Lord Plunket

## Overview

William Plunket, 1st Baron Plunket, was an Irish barrister and Whig politician. He served as Lord Chancellor of Ireland (1830–1834, 1835–1841) and was a leading advocate for Catholic emancipation. In Ellenborough''s diary, he appears delivering a speech of exceptional eloquence in the House of Lords during the Catholic Relief Bill debates.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records the Lords debate on the Catholic Relief Bill, noting that "Lord Eldon, who, after an ineffectual attempt on the part of Lord Redesdale to speak, followed Lord Grey, made a very weak, inefficient, powerless speech" (P01-102–104). By contrast, Lord Plunket's intervention was commanding: "Lord Plunket drew, with great power, a picture of the state of society in Ireland as affected by the laws. The whole of his speech was powerful" (P01-105–106).

Ellenborough judged that Plunket's speech, together with [[Lord Grey]]'s, would have enduring influence: "Lord Grey's speech, but still more Lord Plunket's, will have a greater effect upon the public mind, than any which have yet been delivered" (P01-132–133). The Lords divided with a substantial majority of 105 in favour, and Ellenborough reflected on the historic nature of the moment (P01-112–119).

## Key Appearances

- **Catholic Relief Bill debate**: Plunket delivered a powerful speech describing Irish society under the existing laws, which Ellenborough considered the most impactful of the debate (P01-105–106, P01-132–133).
