Lord Bathurst was one of the most seasoned members of the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s Cabinet, serving as Lord President of the Council throughout the 1828–1830 government. A veteran of Liverpool's long administration, Bathurst brought decades of experience to the Cabinet table. Ellenborough's diary records him as a steady presence — consulted on everything from Irish policy to sheriffs' appointments — and as a frequent host of the Cabinet dinners that served as informal governing sessions.

## Early Life and Career

Henry Bathurst succeeded his father as 3rd Earl Bathurst in 1794. His ministerial career spanned four decades: he served as President of the Board of Trade, Foreign Secretary (briefly in 1809), and Secretary of State for War and the Colonies under Lord Liverpool (1812–1827). By the time he joined Wellington's Cabinet, Bathurst was one of the most experienced administrators in British politics, having overseen colonial policy during the Napoleonic Wars and their aftermath.

## Role in Wellington's Cabinet

As Lord President of the Council, Bathurst's role was both formal and informal. He presided over Privy Council meetings and provided the Prime Minister with seasoned counsel. The diary records the Duke regularly sharing sensitive information with Bathurst and the diarist together:

> [004-162] I told the Duke and Lord Bathurst what occurred at the dinner y…

Bathurst was often among the small group of senior ministers who remained after larger meetings to discuss the most sensitive matters:

## Political Judgment and Influence

Bathurst's opinions carried weight. On the Irish question, the diarist consulted him:

> [003-131] I mentioned this to Lord Bathurst. He thought not.

On the question of parliamentary reform that would soon convulse British politics, Bathurst was characteristically cautious:

## Cabinet Host and Social Hub

Bathurst's London residence served as a regular venue for Cabinet dinners — informal working sessions where strategy was debated over the table:

| Date | Event | Diary Reference |
|------|-------|----------------|
| 1828 | Cabinet dinner at Bathurst's | 005-189 |
| 1829 | Cabinet dinner at Bathurst's | 028-042 |
| 1830 | Cabinet dinner — Bathurst absent | 021-088 |

These dinners were integral to the government's functioning. The diarist "walked home from" one such gathering with Bathurst (036-080), discussing appointments in transit.

## Key Relationships

### With the Duke of Wellington

Bathurst was among Wellington's most trusted lieutenants. When political crisis threatened in 1830, Bathurst's name appeared alongside Aberdeen's on the list of ministers whose sacrifice was demanded:

> [044-125] …ed the sacrifice of Aberdeen, Bathurst, and Arbuthnot. There must be

This placed Bathurst at the center of the Tory old guard that reformist pressure sought to displace.

### With the Diarist

Ellenborough clearly regarded Bathurst as an ally and confidant. They walked home together after debates, exchanged views on appointments, and shared assessments of colleagues. Bathurst suggested Hardinge for a position (036-145), and the diarist followed up — a pattern of quiet patronage characteristic of the unreformed political system.

## Legacy and Assessment

Bathurst was the quintessential Tory elder statesman — experienced, cautious, and loyal. His presence in Wellington's Cabinet provided institutional memory stretching back to the Napoleonic era. When the Reform crisis broke, Bathurst stood with the resisters, and his political career ended with the fall of the Tory government in 1830. His colonial secretaryship, during which he oversaw the abolition of the slave trade's enforcement, represents a more complex legacy than the diary alone captures.
