[025-001]We divided well. There being but fifteen present for Lord Stanhope's

[025-002]motion, and ten proxies.

[025-003]_February 26._

[025-004]Chairs at 11. Went over with them the letter on Batta.

[025-005]Lord Wharncliffe intends on Tuesday to propose examining the Chairman of

[025-006]the East Indian Company.

[025-007]_February 27._

[025-008]Wrote a note to Loch to tell him of Lord Wharncliffe's intention. He does

[025-009]not like the idea at all, and wishes to see me before the Committee sits. I

[025-010]have named Monday at eleven. I told him my feeling was against his being

[025-011]examined, as I thought it unfair; besides, he was not the best witness. I

[025-012]told Lord Wharncliffe he should examine Lord Amherst.

[025-013]At the Cabinet room I attempted to read the papers respecting Irish

[025-014]education. My opinion is that it would be better to let the matter rest for

[025-015]the present; the agitation of it may revive animosities, and if any good be

[025-016]attainable, it may be attained at a more favourable period than the

[025-017]present. I rather doubt whether it might not be yet more safely left to the

[025-018]people themselves, as education in England and in Scotland.

[025-019]_March 1._

[025-020]Cabinet. We were to have talked about Irish education, but more important

[025-021]matters intervened. There is a motion on Friday of Mr. Davenport's for a

[025-022]Committee on the internal state of the country. Peel thinks there will be a

[025-023]union of parties in favour of it. He feels it must be opposed. Some of the

[025-024]friends of Government have said they must vote for it. He proposes that

[025-025]Goulburn should to-morrow give notice of his intention of explaining his

[025-026]views as to taxation on Monday week. Peel thinks that he can procure an

[025-027]adjournment of the debate till after Goulburn's exposé.

[025-028]Goulburn suggests taking off the whole of the beer tax, and remitting the

[025-029]hop duty for this year, as well as remodelling it. He likewise proposes

[025-030]lowering the duties on East and West India sugar, the former from 37_s_. to

[025-031]25_s_., and the latter from 27_s_. to 20_s_.

[025-032]As the revenue is decreasing, these reductions cannot be taken from it.

[025-033]There must be a commutation. This he proposes to be a modified property

[025-034]tax, to apply to landed property, all fixed property, and the funds as well

[025-035]as all offices, but not to the profits of trade.

[025-036]_March 2._

[025-037]There seems to have been some incivility last night on the part of Sir

[025-038]Charles Burrell and Sir E. Knatchbull against me, with reference to my

[025-039]opposition to the Duke of Richmond's motion on the wool question last year.

[025-040]_March 3._

[025-041]Peel's. Met Bankes, Graham, and Ashley. It was, after talk, agreed that the

[025-042]papers asked should be refused, unless in the course of the debate it

[025-043]should appear that the granting of Grant's petition and the report of the

[025-044]Privy Council would improve the division. I expect a regular attack upon

[025-045]myself from all quarters. I would give a year of the House of Lords to be

[025-046]there to throw grape-shot amongst the small lawyers.

[025-047]Cabinet room. Read despatches relating to the expedition to Algiers, which

[025-048]is certainly going.

[025-049]Cabinet dinner at Peel's. The affair of the vacated offices becomes

[025-050]serious, for it seems certain that it is necessary to take the declaration

[025-051]again upon any new patent, and the Board of Admiralty should have taken the

[025-052]declaration as well as Castlereagh--the Board of Control as well as me.

[025-053]The Chancellor continues to have no objection to reducing the salaries of

[025-054]the Supreme Court Judges.

[025-055]_March 5._

[025-056]Chairs at 11. I got rid of them as soon as I could, as I wished to go to

[025-057]the Committee.

[025-058]Loch showed me a letter from Lord William Bentinck, by which it appears

[025-059]that the officers of the Cawnpore division of the army wished to have a

[025-060]general meeting for the election of delegates to England. Sir J.

[025-061]Whittingham forwarded their request to Lord Combermere, highly disapproving

[025-062]of it. Lord Combermere directed the Adjutant-General to write a letter

[025-063]coinciding with Sir J. Whittingham's opinions, and adding that he would be

[025-064]the advocate of the army both in India and in England. Lord William

[025-065](Bentinck) is going up the country with the _Government_ and wishes to take

[025-066]Lord Dalhousie with him. He expects very uncivil treatment, and says the

[025-067]discontent is deep-seated. The same account is received from other

[025-068]quarters.

[025-069]The debate was adjourned last night. R. Grant made a speech in a moderate

[025-070]tone, but disingenuous. Lord Ashley spoke good stuff apparently, but Henry

[025-071]says he could not hear him. Lord Graham was unembarrassed and did well; but

[025-072]the 'Times' hardly gives him ten words.

[025-073]I sent a note to Peel to-day observing upon the disingenuousness of Grant's

[025-074]speech. He told me he had been reading the papers, and saw it was no

[025-075]question of judicial independence, but of judicial aggression, and he

[025-076]thought the tone of the Governor who was in the right much better than that

[025-077]of the Judge who was in the wrong. So I hope he will make a good speech.

[025-078]_March 6._

[025-079]Read letters from Sir J. Macdonald. They came by Constantinople. The only

[025-080]news they contain is that the Russians certainly have the intention of

[025-081]conquering Khiva and Bokhara. This comes from Chasanes Murza. I told the

[025-082]Duke, who seems disposed to make it an European question.

[025-083]I showed the Duke a most atrocious libel on royalty which has been

[025-084]published in the 'Calcutta Gazette.' If the King saw it he would recall

[025-085]Lord William by the Sign Manual. A letter must be written immediately in

[025-086]the press. It is in such a state that our Government cannot stand if it be

[025-087]permitted to go on uncontrolled.

[025-088]I asked the Duke as to taxation. He said he thought it could be done

[025-089]without income tax. To lay on income tax would be to weaken ourselves in

[025-090]the opinion of all foreign Powers. Besides, it would prevent our reducing

[025-091]the Four per Cents.

[025-092]He calculated the loss of the beer duty at 3,500,000£. and, marine

[025-093]insurance, cider, remission of hop duty, &c., would make the loss

[025-094]4,500,000£.

[025-110]There may have been more; but he spoke, and I write from memory.

[025-111]I told him I thought that with a diminished duty on beer and an increased

[025-112]duty on spirits he could not expect an increase of 500,000£ on spirits. He

[025-113]admitted that was the weak point. He said he was sure we could not carry an

[025-114]income tax while we had a million surplus. If we have a good harvest, I

[025-115]have no doubt the increase on malt will be great; but I apprehend there

[025-116]must be a repayment of beer duties, and if there should be, the loss will

[025-117]be enormous.

[025-118]_March 8._

[025-119]Sent Mr. Elphinstone a letter giving an account of the travels to the North

[025-120]of the Paropamisan range into Cabul.

[025-121]The Duke said we really must look out for a new Governor-General. I

[025-122]suggested Hardinge. He said Hardinge had not as yet station enough in the

[025-123]opinion of the public, in the army, or in Parliament. He wished him to be

[025-124]Secretary in Ireland. It would have been much better if he had gone there

[025-125]instead of Lord F. Gower, and Lord P. to the War Office. To be sure, then

[025-126]we should not have had the reductions Hardinge had effected. He had, as I

[025-127]knew, always wished Hardinge to go to Ireland.

[025-128]I observed that Hardinge was rising every day in public estimation, which

[025-129]the Duke acknowledged, and I added that I was sure none would do the duty

[025-130]better, for he had firmness and habits of business. The Duke seems to think

[025-131]of Elphinstone. He said he was a very clever man. I told him I had been an

[025-132]hour and a half with Elphinstone last night. I told the Duke all my notions

[025-133]respecting individual responsibility, members of Council, &c., and that I

[025-134]had begged Elphinstone to think of them. The Duke seemed generally to

[025-135]approve of them. It seems Lord Wellesley never would go to Council. I do

[025-136]not wonder at it; but the Duke used to tell him he was Governor-General _in

[025-137]Council_--that he ought always to go there.

[025-138]_March 10._

[025-139]Dined with the Duke. Cabinet dinner. Only the Peers there. The others

[025-140]detained by Lord Palmerston's motion on Portugal, on which there was a

[025-141]majority of two to one, 150 odd to 70 something. Huskisson made a very bad

[025-142]dull speech. We talked about a successor to the Speaker. They seem to think

[025-143]he will not resign now, as he would not get a good pension in the present

[025-144]temper of the House.

[025-145]The candidates are Sir J. Beckett, Littleton, G. Bankes, Wynn of course. I

[025-146]mentioned Frankland Lewis as a good man, which he would be. I dare say the

[025-147]Chairs will think he should be elected unanimously.

[025-148]It seems there must be a Bill of Indemnity for not taking the declaration,

[025-149]two Bishops, Chester and Oxford, not having taken it. The Duke finds he has

[025-150]at Dover, as Lord Warden.

[025-151]We had some little conversation about the income tax, which the Duke is

[025-152]very hostile to, and I am glad we shall not have it.

[025-153]_March 11._

[025-154]The Russians have at last sent their reply to our expostulatory note. I

[025-155]have not had time to read it. Lord Heytesbury calculates that the last war

[025-156]cost them 12,000,000£, but they endeavour to conceal the amount.

[025-157]Peel told me the House was quite excited against the Bombay judges, and

[025-158]that the division fairly represented its real opinion.

[025-159]_March 12._

[025-160]There was but one black ball in the election of Lord Clare, and the Chairs

[025-161]think that was put in by mistake; no one objected.

[025-162]_March 13._

[025-163]Read Sir H. Parnell's pamphlet on taxation.

[025-164]Cabinet room at two. I had only got half through the Russian answer when

[025-165]the Cabinet met for the subject of taxation.

[025-166]I rather expected to find that the Duke had had communications with

[025-167]Goulburn, and that the idea of a property tax was given up. However, that

[025-168]seemed not to be the case. It was determined the whole beer duty should be

[025-169]given up in any case.

[025-188]The conversion of stock into annuities is proceeding at the rate of

[025-189]1,000,000 pounds a month, and the increased annual charge already is

[025-190]250,000 pounds. Certainly to this extent the estimated three millions of

[025-191]surplus might be fairly reduced; but to reduce the surplus to 1,200,000

[025-192]pounds or 1,600,000 pounds would be an entire abandonment of the system

[025-193]adopted by the Finance Committee and the Government.

[025-194]It seemed to me that the members of the House of Commons were all in favour

[025-195]of the income tax; all the Peers against it. The Duke was strongly against

[025-196]it. He apprehended the reduction of establishments, and particularly the

[025-197]pressure of the tax on men of 1,200 pounds a year, and under.

[025-198]If I imposed the income tax, I would make it the means of a thorough

[025-199]reconciliation between the higher and lower classes. In this manner only

[025-200]would it be effectual and make a strong Government.

[025-201]I object greatly to Goulburn's deductions from the old income tax. He

[025-202]excepts _occupiers_; that is, as regards land occupiers, quite right; but

[025-203]he excepts manufacturing capital and capital engaged in commerce. Now, why

[025-204]should the man who has 100,000 pounds in a manufactory, and makes 10 per

[025-205]cent, on that sum, pay nothing, while the man who invests his 100,000

[025-206]pounds in the funds gets only 3 1/2 per cent, and pays 5 per cent, out of

[025-207]that reduced profit? The man who has a manufacturing or _commercial

[025-208]capital_ is a _saving man_. He can afford to pay something to the State,

[025-209]and why should he not? So the lawyer who may be making 10,000 pounds a

[025-210]year is to pay nothing. If he takes 5,500£. a year and becomes a judge, he

[025-211]pays 137 pounds 10 shillings. Yet his interest is still for life.
