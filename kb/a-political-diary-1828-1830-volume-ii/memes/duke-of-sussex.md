# Duke of Sussex

## Overview

Prince Augustus Frederick, Duke of Sussex, was the sixth son of [[george-iv|George III]] and an uncle to the heir presumptive, Princess Victoria. Unlike his elder brothers, the Duke of Sussex held liberal political views and was sympathetic to the Whig opposition. Ellenborough's diary records him as a figure of suspicion within Tory circles, particularly for his perceived influence over the King.

## Political Influence at Court

The Duke of Sussex maintained close proximity to the King, cultivating personal influence. Ellenborough noted that "the Duke of Sussex has lent the King an easy chair, and affectionate" attentions that kept him in royal favour. (029-082) This personal bond had political implications that troubled the [[Duke of Wellington|Wellington]] administration.

During the crisis over parliamentary reform, the Duke of Sussex was seen as a conduit for opposition views. "The Duke of Sussex has been already infusing poison into the King's ear and" spreading arguments against the government's position. (039-113) Observers at court functions were watchful: "Those who observed the Duke of Sussex at the levee thought he seemed" to be positioning himself advantageously. (039-115)

## Association with Opposition Figures

The Duke of Sussex maintained relationships with prominent Whigs and reform-minded figures. Ellenborough recorded meeting him at social and political gatherings: he "rode to the Duchess of Kent's and Duke of Sussex's" residence, noting the connection between the royal household and reform circles. (035-072) Ellenborough drew a pointed comparison between the Duke of Sussex and the French royal who had just seized a crown: "Our Duke of Orleans, as I call him, the Duke of Sussex, sticks close to the" centre of political power. (041-090)

The comparison to the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orleans]] was no accident — it reflected Tory anxiety that a liberal-minded royal could play a similarly transformative role in British politics, particularly given the Duke of Sussex's position in the line of succession and his influence with the King.
