# Prince Emilius of Hesse

## Overview

Prince Emilius of Hesse-Darmstadt was a German prince who emerged as a candidate during the diplomatic search for a sovereign for the newly independent [[Greece]]. His candidacy was complicated by his prior service to Napoleon Bonaparte.

## Role in the Diary

Prince Emilius appears in the diary exclusively in the context of the Greek succession question, which occupied European diplomacy in 1829–1830. The great powers sought a monarch acceptable to all parties. Ellenborough records the deliberations:

"We first named Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg, whom the French would not hear of. Then Leopold! They did not like him. Prince Emilius of Hesse Darmstadt was thought of" (016-050). The French instead suggested Prince John of Saxony.

In a later discussion, Prince Emilius appeared in a longer list of candidates: "Prince Paul of Wurtemburgh, Prince Leopold, Prince Emilius of Hesse Darmstadt, and Prince Charles of Mecklenburgh" (021-024). The diary notes that "the seven first either declined or were rejected" (021-026). Crucially, "Prince Emilius of Hesse Darmstadt was an aide-de-camp of Bonaparte, and the King would not have him" (021-027), his Napoleonic association proving an insuperable obstacle to British approval.

## Key Appearances

- **Initial consideration**: Prince Emilius was "thought of" after Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg and Prince Leopold were rejected or met with objections (016-050).
- **Candidate list**: He appeared among seven named candidates for the Greek crown (021-024).
- **Vetoed**: The King refused to accept him because of his service as Bonaparte's aide-de-camp (021-027).
