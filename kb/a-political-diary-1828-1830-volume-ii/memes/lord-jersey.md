# Lord Jersey

## Overview

Lord Jersey (George Child Villiers, 5th Earl of Jersey) was a Tory peer who served as Lord Chamberlain to [[George IV]]. During the 1828–1830 period of Ellenborough's diary, he appears as a reliable government supporter in the [[House of Lords]] and a participant in the King's funeral ceremonies.

## Role in the Diary

Lord Jersey appears in Ellenborough's diary as a consistent political ally. During a debate, Jersey "told me he was afraid Lord Grey might have misunderstood the meaning of" a statement, indicating his engagement in parliamentary diplomacy. (036-055) The diary confirms bluntly: "Lord Jersey voted with us. So did Dudley." (036-067)

His position within Tory ranks is further illustrated by his listing alongside other government peers: "Downshire, Belhaven, Meldrum, and Lord Jersey." (026-120) He held Lord Lauderdale's proxy in the Lords, which the diary notes was "consequent upon Lord Rosslyn's accession" to government. (026-089)

## Court Role

As Lord Chamberlain, Jersey played a ceremonial role at the funeral of George IV. The diary records that "Lord Jersey walked as Lord Chamberlain, Lord Conyngham as Steward. He broke his staff into the grave." (038-084) This ritual breaking of the white staff — the symbol of the Lord Chamberlain's office — marked the formal end of his service to the deceased monarch.

His willingness to serve is noted when an unspecified role was "offered to Lord Jersey, who accepted willingly." (038-021)

## Political Alignment

Sitting behind the government in the Lords ("Downshire, Lord Wilton; and Lord Jersey sits behind us" — 026-089), Jersey was a dependable member of Wellington's support base. His acceptance of Lauderdale's proxy further strengthened the government's position in the upper house at a time when Wellington's administration was navigating the contentious issue of Catholic emancipation.
