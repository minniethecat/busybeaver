# Lord Farnham

## Overview

Lord Farnham was an Irish representative peer who appears in Ellenborough's diary as an active and respected contributor to House of Lords debates. Ellenborough noted him as an effective speaker who consistently spoke well and weighed in on questions of war, peace, and the Union with Ireland.

## Role in the Diary

Lord Farnham's earliest appearance in the diary is during a division in the Lords: "After a few sentences from Lord Farnham we divided." (P01-108) The brevity of the record suggests he was a regular participant whose contributions, while brief, were part of the normal rhythm of parliamentary business.

Ellenborough's personal assessment of Farnham was favourable. He "thought Lord Farnham very gentlemanlike" (001-162), a Johnsonian compliment that in the diary's idiom carries weight — Ellenborough was sparing with personal praise of his colleagues.

## Speaking Reputation

Farnham's effectiveness as a speaker is directly attested: "Lord Farnham spoke, as he always does, well." (051-017) On this occasion, "he deprecated the dissolution of the Union" (051-018), revealing his commitment to the Anglo-Irish constitutional settlement. As an Irish peer, his defence of the Union carried particular significance.

On questions of foreign policy, Farnham took a proactive stance. "Lord Farnham having suggested the necessity of preparing for war" (051-038), [[Lord Grey]] countered that "the preparation should be by gaining the hearts of our own people" (051-039). This exchange captures the tension between the Tory instinct for military readiness and the Whig emphasis on domestic conciliation during the unsettled period of 1828–1830.

## Political Significance

Farnham's combination of gentlemanly manner, reliable speaking ability, and Irish credentials made him a valuable supporter of the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s administration in the [[House of Lords]]. His defence of the Union and support for military preparedness aligned him with the government's conservative wing.
