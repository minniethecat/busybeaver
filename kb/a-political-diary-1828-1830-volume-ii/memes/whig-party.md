The [[Whig Party]], challenger to the [[Tory Party]] for control of the [[House of Commons]], that Ellenborough's diary describes was a party in transition. After nearly a quarter-century in opposition (with the brief exception of the 1806–1807 Ministry of All the Talents), the Whigs were fragmented, uncertain of their direction, and led by an aging [[Lord Grey]] who had largely withdrawn from active political management. Yet the diary records, with the clarity of a political insider watching his opponents, the slow coalescence of a Whig-led coalition that would, by November 1830, form a government and launch the era of Reform.

## The Whigs in Opposition (1828–1830)

### Weakness and Division

In 1828, while Tory elders like [[Earl Bathurst]] anchored the Cabinet, the Whigs were not a credible alternative government. The diary notes their internal divisions and the contempt in which Wellington's circle held them:

> [P01-148] his back upon the Whigs after what has taken place, …

Yet even in weakness, the Whigs were a factor in the government's calculations. Wellington needed their votes on Catholic emancipation and feared driving them into alliance with the Canningites:

> [005-025] …ckers, and we should have the Whigs hostile. It would be saying …

### The Canningite Factor

The Canningites — followers of the late George Canning who had left Wellington's government in 1828 — occupied a position between the Tory ministry and the Whig opposition. Their eventual fusion with the Whigs was a decisive political development:

> [030-167] The Huskissonians and Whigs are drawing nearer together.

This realignment, tracked anxiously by the diarist, would prove fatal to the Tory monopoly on power:

> [032-039] …ames indicated an alliance of Whigs, Canningites, and Tories
>
> [033-127] …e was sprung and all parties, Whigs

## The Whig Social World

The diary reveals the Whigs as a social as well as political formation. They gathered at Holland House, maintained their own proxy networks in the Lords (026-119), and were the subject of Tory dinner-table mockery:

> [035-044] …lk, I think), said, _'He is a Whig.'_ Lord
>
> [035-047] …rong; only remember, _he is a Whig_.'

This exchange — dismissing a candidate for office solely on the basis of his Whig identity — captures the tribal dimension of Regency politics.

## The Road to Power

The diary records the accelerating Whig momentum through 1830:

1. **Death of George IV (June 1830)**: Required a general election; the Whigs gained seats (039-116: "triumphant, and received his Whig friends with a smile")
2. **July Revolution in France**: Electrified British reform sentiment; the Whigs positioned themselves as the party of moderate constitutional change
3. **Wellington's rejection of Reform (November 1830)**: The Prime Minister's declaration against any parliamentary reform shattered his government
4. **Whig government formed**: Grey became Prime Minister; the diary records the Tory anxiety:
   > [054-164] My fear is that the Whigs will not be able to form a G…

The Whigs did form a government — and the diarist's fear was realized, not that they would fail, but that they would succeed in their reformist programme.

## Legacy

The Whig Party that took office in 1830 would pass the Great Reform Act (1832), abolish slavery (1833), and reshape British governance. The party described in Ellenborough's diary — divided, uncertain, and held together more by social connection than ideological coherence — was, in retrospect, on the threshold of its greatest achievement. The diarist, a committed Tory, recorded this transformation with a mixture of disdain and apprehension that makes his diary an invaluable source for understanding one of the pivot points of British political history.
