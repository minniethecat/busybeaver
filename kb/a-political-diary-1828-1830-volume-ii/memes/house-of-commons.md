The [[House of Commons]] was the cockpit of British politics during the Wellington government. Ellenborough, sitting in the Lords as a peer, watched its proceedings with the anxious attention of a minister whose legislative programme depended on a fractious and increasingly unreliable Commons majority. The diary records 78 explicit references to "Commons" — debates, votes, procedural maneuvers, and personality clashes — that together paint a portrait of an institution in transition, where the unreformed electoral system was coming under pressure from [[Parliamentary Reform]] agitation and the rising power of [[Whig Party]] orators.

## Structure and Membership

Debates over the [[East India Company]] charter and [[Slavery Abolition]] petitions filled the unreformed House of Commons, which comprised 658 members elected from a patchwork of county and borough constituencies. The system was notoriously unequal: rotten boroughs with a handful of voters returned MPs, while growing industrial cities like Manchester had no direct representation. ## The Commons and Reform

The diary's later entries (1830) record the growing pressure for parliamentary reform. The death of George IV and the accession of William IV triggered a general election that returned a Commons less amenable to Tory management:

> [033-118] …ajority of 13 in the House of Commons. I am

A majority of 13 on a key vote was dangerously thin — and the diary registers the government's increasing difficulty in holding its coalition together.

## Legacy

The House of Commons described in Ellenborough's diary was on the cusp of transformation. Within two years, the Great Reform Act of 1832 would sweep away the rotten boroughs, redistribute seats to the industrial cities, and expand the franchise — changes that the diarist, a Tory traditionalist, viewed with deep misgiving. The "loose" and unmanageable Commons of 1828–1830 was, in retrospect, the old constitution's last parliament.
