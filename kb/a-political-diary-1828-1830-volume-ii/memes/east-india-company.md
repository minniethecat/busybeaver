The governance of British India, intertwined with [[Slavery Abolition]] debates over West Indian versus East Indian labor, was arguably the largest single policy question facing the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s Cabinet — and Ellenborough, who had served on the Board of Control and would later become Governor-General of India, was at its center. The diary records, in exceptional detail, the debate over whether the [[East India Company]] should continue to govern India or whether administration should be transferred to the Crown. With an army of over 200,000 men, a territory stretching from the Himalayas to Ceylon, and a monopoly over trade with the East, the Company was a state within a state — and its charter was due for renewal.

## Background

The East India Company had evolved from a trading corporation chartered by Elizabeth I (1600) into the de facto sovereign of large parts of the Indian subcontinent. After the Battle of Plassey (1757), the Company acquired territorial dominion over Bengal; by the early nineteenth century, through a combination of subsidiary alliances and military conquest, it controlled most of India either directly or through client princes. The Charter Act of 1813 had opened Indian trade to private merchants but left the Company's administrative monopoly intact — a compromise that the Wellington government was expected to revisit.

## The Governance Debate (1828–1830)

### Company vs. Crown

The central question was whether India should be governed by the Company or directly by the King. The diary records a Cabinet deeply divided:

### The Board of Control

As the Crown's supervisory body over the Company, the Board of Control (where Ellenborough served) was the institutional nexus of the debate:

> [004-148] …ed that he should come to the Indian Board on Tuesday. I wish to

The diary records a steady stream of Indian business conducted at the Board — from military stores (009-144) to loans (007-176) to the Red Sea route (009-075).

### Patronage

Indian patronage — the power to appoint civil and military officers — was a major source of political influence. Ellenborough noted the tension:

> [008-129] patronage of India should be separated from the …
>
> [008-136] patronage of India from the Government.

## The Native Army Question

The diary reveals deep anxieties about the Indian army during peacetime:

> [006-183] …l-being of the native army of India_. I spoke
>
> [007-078] …eems to dread a long peace in India. We hold everything

A large standing army of Indian sepoys under British officers was expensive and, some feared, potentially dangerous if insufficiently occupied.

## The Ellenborough Connection

The diarist's personal investment in Indian affairs was extraordinary. He had already been considered for Governor-Generalship, dined with the East India volunteers (008-101), and wrote extensively on the subject. His ambition to govern India would eventually be realized in 1842 — but during the Wellington government, he was a persistent voice in Cabinet for strengthening Crown oversight while preserving the Company's administrative machinery:

> [011-091] …rectors the real Ministers of India, and almost emancipate
>
> [011-095] India. I spoke to him of his papers…

## Consequences

The India debates of 1828–1830 set the stage for the Charter Act of 1833, which would end the Company's commercial functions while preserving its administrative role — a compromise that lasted until the Indian Rebellion of 1857 prompted the Company's final abolition and the transfer of India to direct Crown rule in 1858.
