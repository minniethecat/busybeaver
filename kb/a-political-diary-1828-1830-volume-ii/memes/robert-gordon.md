**Sir Robert Gordon** (1791–1847) was a British diplomat who served as ambassador to the Ottoman Empire from 1828 to 1831. The diary follows his mission to Constantinople closely, particularly his management of the Eastern Question during the [[Russo-Turkish War]].

== Mission to Constantinople ==

Gordon was dispatched to the Sublime Porte with instructions from [[Lord Aberdeen]], the Foreign Secretary. The diary records: "Aberdeen read his instructions to Gordon, who" then departed for his post. (002-041) He was well received upon arrival: "R. Gordon has been magnificently received" in the Ottoman capital. (009-154)

His despatches provided the British government with crucial intelligence. The diarist "met Lord Melville. Read Gordon's letters from Constantinople." (009-166) These letters shaped British understanding of Ottoman affairs, and Gordon's assessment was direct: "Gordon thinks if we declared we would" take certain actions, the diplomatic landscape would shift. (009-168)

== Managing the Eastern Crisis ==

Gordon's reporting on the Ottoman Empire provided the British cabinet with essential information during the Russo-Turkish conflict. "Mr. Gordon's despatches" were circulated to Lord Heytesbury. (010-132) His analysis was stark: "Mr. Gordon describes the Turkish Empire" in terms that underscored its fragility. (010-134)

Gordon demonstrated considerable diplomatic skill in Constantinople. The diary notes that "Mr. Gordon managed to get a paper into the" right hands. (010-160) His letters were carefully studied, though at one point "on Mr. Gordon's letter, which is as yet unanswered," the government deliberated. (010-167)

== Assessment ==

The diarist's assessment of Gordon's performance was positive. He observed that "R. Gordon has of his own authority ordered" actions that proved effective. (011-163) More broadly: "R. Gordon seems to have done very well." (011-174) When faced with difficult Russian demands, "Gordon managed very well" to protect British interests. (011-176)

The verdict was clear: "I must say R. Gordon has done ably and well." (012-028) When faced with duplicitous Ottoman behaviour, "Sir R. Gordon seems to have done all that could" be expected of a capable diplomat. (012-118)
