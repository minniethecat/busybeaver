[026-001]In all this there seems to me unfairness.

[026-002]If the tax be imposed as it is proposed, it will be very difficult to

[026-003]include afterwards the classes now exempted. It will be impossible to take

[026-004]off the tax, and whenever a tax is unpopular, those upon whom it presses

[026-005]will say, 'Take it off. It is only adding 1/4 or 1/2 per cent. to the

[026-006]income tax.'

[026-007]A real property tax is the fairest of all taxes--but an income tax is the

[026-008]most unfair even when it affects all income; but when it affects the income

[026-009]of some who have a life interest, and not the income of others in the same

[026-010]situation, it is most unfair indeed.

[026-011]It is quite erroneous to suppose that those who pay an income tax are the

[026-012]only persons who suffer from it. The reduction of establishments, the

[026-013]diminished consumption, the increased economy in every article of

[026-014]expenditure on the part of those affected by it have necessarily the effect

[026-015]of reducing the wages of labour. The labourer may buy some things cheaper,

[026-016]but he has less wherewith to buy.

[026-017]_Sunday, March 14._

[026-018]Saw Hardinge at two. Told him how we stood as to the question of taxation.

[026-019]He said he thought the income tax would be popular, but agreed with me in

[026-020]thinking it should be established on strictly just principles.

[026-021]Cabinet at three. Goulburn read a new statement showing the surplus this

[026-022]year, if we reduced beer and leather, and next year too. The surplus this

[026-023]year is about 2 millions. Next year about 1,500,000£.

[026-024]The income tax reaches the funds, and the Irish, and the parsimonious, and

[026-025]the rich--so far it is good, but it likewise reaches the man of 100£ a

[026-026]year. It tends to diminution of establishments, to diminished demand for

[026-027]labour. To create an alteration in demand generally.

[026-028]It was proposed to exempt professions and trades. This was unjust, and it

[026-029]would have led to an entire separation and hostility between the landed

[026-030]proprietors and the united body of labourers and manufacturers.

[026-031]These last would have joined on all occasions in urging a further and still

[026-032]a further increase of income tax, and would never have consented to a tax

[026-033]on consumption. The income tax would finally absorb all other taxes.

[026-034]Another great objection to the income tax now is that it would have the

[026-035]effect of perilling the reduction of the 3 per cents.

[026-036]The Duke, Rosslyn, and I were decidedly against income tax. Lord Bathurst

[026-037]and Lord Melville, as well as the Chancellor, less decidedly so, but still

[026-038]in favour of abiding by the reduction of the beer and leather tax. Aberdeen

[026-039]said nothing, neither did Sir G. Murray, so they were understood to go with

[026-040]the majority.

[026-041]Goulburn acknowledged the discussion had to a great extent changed his

[026-042]opinion, and that he was not then prepared to propose the tax.

[026-043]Herries seemed much in its favour; but more, as it seemed to me, because he

[026-044]wished to maintain a large surplus according to the decision of the Finance

[026-045]Committee than for any good reason. Peel was decidedly for a property tax.

[026-046]He wished to reach such men as Baring, his father, Rothschild, and others,

[026-047]as well as absentees and Ireland. He thought too it was expedient to

[026-048]reconcile the lower with the higher classes, and to diminish the burthen of

[026-049]taxation on the poor man. I accede to the principle; but I doubt whether

[026-050]taxes on consumption do really press more heavily on the poor man than an

[026-051]income tax. What he has to look to is not the actual price of the article

[026-052]he wants, but the proportion which his wages bear to that price. It matters

[026-053]little to him what the price of candles may be, if he has not money

[026-054]wherewith to purchase them. That system of taxation is best for the poor

[026-055]man which most tends to increase the funds for the employment of labour;

[026-056]and every disturbance in the system, every alteration of demand, does

[026-057]intrinsic mischief.

[026-058]After this matter was decided, Peel behaving most fairly, and declaring he

[026-059]would support the decision of the Cabinet whatever it might be, and that in

[026-060]this case the decision of the Treasury was to be principally looked to, we

[026-061]talked of Queen Donna Maria, in whose name Don Pedro has established a

[026-062]Regency in Terceira.

[026-063]I read Leopold's letter to Lord Aberdeen, in which he refers to his letter

[026-064]of February 11, for the statement of his views in taking the Greek coronet,

[026-065]saying that he only acceded from courtesy, and as a matter of form, to the

[026-066]protocol, and further urging some alteration in the frontier. He has made

[026-067]an application for a joint guarantee by the three Powers of a loan of

[026-068]60,000,000 paras, or 2,400,000£. Now we only agreed to guarantee 50,000£ a

[026-069]year, and that for troops. Nothing will be said upon this point till he has

[026-070]withdrawn his letter. He seems to be Aberdeen's pet. I do not think, had

[026-071]the Greeks searched Europe, they could have found a man whose character was

[026-072]more congenial to their own.

[026-073]_March 17._

[026-074]Leopold has withdrawn his obnoxious letter.

[026-075]_March 18._

[026-076]House at five. Debate on the Duke of Richmond's motion for a select

[026-077]Committee on the state of the labouring classes, and the effect of taxation

[026-078]upon the productive powers of industry.

[026-079]A most dull debate, till Lord Holland spoke. I answered him. Lord Lansdowne

[026-080]next, then the Duke. I spoke, showing the impracticability of the

[026-081]Committee. I however showed up Committees rather too much. This Lord

[026-082]Lansdowne took hold of, not very fairly, but he did it well.

[026-083]We had in the House 69; they 39.

[026-084]With proxies we had 140 to 61. My uncle voted in the minority, and so did

[026-085]Coplestone. Dudley, Lord Malmesbury, Lord Gower, voted with us.

[026-086]The Whigs, Brunswickers, and Canningites were in the minority. The Duke of

[026-087]Cumberland was there.

[026-088]I find we have some recruits--in proxies Lord Lauderdale, Duke of Bedford,

[026-089]Downshire, Lord Wilton; and Lord Jersey sits behind us. He has now Lord

[026-090]Lauderdale's proxy. All this is consequent upon Lord Rosslyn's accession.

[026-091]Lord Grey has now no one left. No one expressed a wish to turn out the

[026-092]Ministers.

[026-093]_March 19._

[026-094]It seems that in the House of Commons Huskisson made a friendly speech,

[026-095]finding he can do no harm, and Lord Althorp a very friendly one. In short,

[026-096]everybody seems to be of opinion that the worst thing that could be done

[026-097]would be to turn out the Government.

[026-098]Peel says, and so does Herries, that the House is in favour of an income

[026-099]tax. That what we have determined upon is the best for this year, but that

[026-100]next year there must be an income tax.

[026-101]Cabinet. Leopold wants more money. It was agreed he should have 70,000

[026-102]pounds a year loan guaranteed to him for seven years, instead of 50,000

[026-103]pounds.

[026-104]The holders of 4 per cents. are to have the option of 100 pounds stock 3

[026-105]1/2, or 70 pounds stock 5 per cents. Trustees may only convert into the 3

[026-106]1/2 per cents.

[026-107]_March 20._

[026-108]Chairs at 11. They have made some alterations in the letter to the Indian

[026-109]Government respecting their conduct, and have praised Lord William for his

[026-110]_perseverance_, &c. This is contrary to the Duke's view and to mine. I

[026-111]shall see whether I can allow their amendments.

[026-112]I find they have likewise altered much in the letter relative to Batta.

[026-113]_March 23._

[026-114]The Duke, Lord Bathurst, and Rosslyn went away at 2 to the Cabinet, where

[026-115]they decided against the Jew Relief Bill. The bishops have intimated that

[026-116]they must unanimously oppose it.

[026-117]Debate on Lord Clanricarde's motion on the eternal Terceira question. The

[026-118]Duke spoke very well. The House was flat. The division with proxies 126 to

[026-119]31, 4 to 1. We have now of Whig proxies Bedford, Lauderdale, Wilton,

[026-120]Downshire, Belhaven, Meldrum, and Lord Jersey.

[026-121]_March 24._

[026-122]Cabinet dinner at Sir J. Murray's. Considered what course should be adopted

[026-123]upon P. Thompson's motion for a committee to revise taxation. Peel still

[026-124]hankers after the property tax, and rather unwillingly opposes this motion.

[026-125]However, it will be done on the ground that the remission of such a

[026-126]question to a committee would derange, by existing apprehensions and hopes,

[026-127]the whole industry of the country. In fact it would likewise vest the

[026-128]Government in the committee. Peel, Planta, and Holmes all think the

[026-129]division will be close. I do not apprehend that, if the debate be well

[026-130]conducted.

[026-131]Had a long conversation with the Duke upon Indian matters. The

[026-132]recollections of his youth are strong upon him, and he still clings to the

[026-133]old forms.

[026-134]_March 25._

[026-135]Read some evidence before the Commons on the China trade.

[026-136]Committee. Examined Mr. Elphinstone. He gave very good evidence.

[026-137]House. A flat discussion on the Kentish petition.

[026-138]_March 26._

[026-139]We had two to one last night. The House not very full. It seemed by no

[026-140]means the wish of the House to have a property tax; quite the contrary.

[026-141]Mr. Elphinstone re-examined by Lord Lansdowne and others. He gave a very

[026-142]good evidence, and quite knocked up colonisation.

[026-143]_Monday, March 29, 1830._

[026-144]Office at 2. Looked over regulations, &c., relative to the half-castes and

[026-145]considered their question. Came to a decided opinion against their

[026-146]admissibility to offices which can be held by natives.

[026-147]When Lord Carlisle presented the petition I said very little, expressed

[026-148]compassion for their situation, and a wish to relieve it in any manner

[026-149]consistent with the conservation of our empire and the well-being of the

[026-150]great body of the native population. I said what they asked was not

[026-151]equality of rights, but privilege.

[026-152]Lord King's resolutions on the Corn Laws. A dull debate which lasted till

[026-153]nine--no division. The Duke did not speak well, and it was unnecessary for

[026-154]him to speak at all.

[026-155]_March 30._

[026-156]Committee. Examined Mr. Chaplin, who gave a very good evidence. He is

[026-157]decidedly against the employment of half-castes.

[026-158]I told the Duke at the Committee that I had written to the King immediately

[026-159]on Clare's appointment, and afterwards to Sir F. Watson, when I sent the

[026-160]warrant and had got no answer. The Duke said he would enquire about it. He

[026-161]thought he should have spoken to the King _before_. However, he would

[026-162]settle it.

[026-163]_March 31._

[026-164]Committee at 1. Examined Mr. Ricketts, the half-caste, when Lord Carlisle

[026-165]had examined him in chief. Mr. Ricketts did not seem to know much about the

[026-166]law. It was odd enough to observe him looking round to me after every

[026-167]answer.

[026-168]We had afterwards Mr. Baker, a strong contrast indeed with Mr. Ricketts. He

[026-169]gave very curious evidence relative to the trade of the Arabs of Malabar

[026-170]with Scinde, the Persian Gulf, and the Red Sea.

[026-171]_April 2._

[026-172]Cabinet. Question whether the French should be allowed to hire 9,000 tons

[026-173]of transports now in the river for the expedition against Algiers. The Duke

[026-174]was strongly against it. The French had behaved so ill to us, concealing

[026-175]their objects from us, and revealing them to other Courts, besides

[026-176]intriguing with the Pasha of Egypt.

[026-177]Aberdeen was for giving the permission. He thought the French would

[026-178]consider it quite a hostile measure if we refused permission. However,

[026-179]permission will not be given.

[026-180]Leopold is still negotiating about the money, and it seems doubtful whether

[026-181]he will not resign at last.

[026-182]_April 3._

[026-183]The Company have got into an awkward scrape. It seems they have not made

[026-184]out their account of the prime cost of their tea as merchants do, that they

[026-185]have charged all losses whether from fixed rate of exchange or other

[026-186]causes, whereas merchants in general state prime cost on a calculation of

[026-187]the price in the place where the article is purchased, the other

[026-188]calculations going in diminution of profit.

[026-189]I begin to think the maintenance of the monopoly will be impossible. I have

[026-190]long thought it very inexpedient. It would leave a sullen, settled feeling

[026-191]of discontent in the minds of the manufacturers and merchants of England.
