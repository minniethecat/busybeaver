# Prince Frederick of Orange

## Overview

Prince Frederick of the Netherlands (Willem Frederik Karel, Prince of Orange-Nassau) was the second son of King [[William I of the Netherlands]]. During the period of Ellenborough's diary, he figured in British diplomacy regarding the Greek succession and, more prominently, as a military commander during the [[Belgian Revolution]] of 1830.

## Role in the Diary

Prince Frederick first appears in the diary in connection with the search for a European throne. Ellenborough records diplomatic discussions about offering crowns "to Prince Frederick of Orange, and to [[Prince Leopold]], whom Russia would have had willingly." (015-147) The diary notes dryly: "I wonder Aberdeen did not laugh when he was proposed." (015-148)

A formal proposal was subsequently made. "The proposition was made by us to Prince Frederick of Orange on November 13, his final answer received on August 11" (021-010), though there was "a slight error in" the dates as reconstructed. (021-011)

## The Belgian Revolution

Prince Frederick's most significant role in the diary relates to the Belgian uprising against Dutch rule in 1830. As the commander of Dutch forces, he was positioned "at Vilvorde, and two bodies of 10,000 each are marching upon the same point." (043-106) The diary also considered the possibility of offering the Belgian "crown at once, without the intervention of France, to Prince Frederick of Orange" (044-002), with Prince Charles of Bavaria as an alternative should he refuse.

The military situation was precarious. The diary records that the "troops are said to have lost only 600 men. Prince Frederick is about two leagues from Brussels, on the road to Louvain, waiting for heavy guns." (046-058) A proclamation was issued "by Prince Frederick of Orange, noticing the excesses of the populace, and announcing that the troops would relieve the burgher guard." (045-077)

By late 1830, the situation had deteriorated. The diary notes that "nor Prince Frederick can return to Brussels; but the Prince of Orange may, and this will, I think, be finally settled." (047-128)

## Historical Context

Prince Frederick's military campaign to suppress the Belgian Revolution ultimately failed, and Belgium secured its independence in 1831. His appearance in the diary provides a contemporary British perspective on the crisis that reshaped the map of the Low Countries.
