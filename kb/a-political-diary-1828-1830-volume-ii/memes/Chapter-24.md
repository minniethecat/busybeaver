[024-001]On the East Retford [Footnote: It will be remembered that this question had

[024-002]led to the resignation of Huskinsson and his friends.] question last night

[024-003]we had a majority of twenty-seven in a House of 226 members--the high

[024-004]Tories voting with Government.

[024-005]Bankes has now the offer of a Lordship of the Admiralty till Croker can be

[024-006]got rid of; but he will not go. Castlereagh will have the Treasury

[024-007]Lordship--that is, 600£ a year more for having been careless.

[024-008]_February 13._

[024-009]After seeing the Chairs spoke to the Duke about the Bombay succession. He

[024-010]asked what I meant to do with Elphinstone? I considered he had left India

[024-011]altogether. The Duke thought he must return--that he would go to Bombay

[024-012]again with the expectation of afterwards going to Madras. I think the Duke

[024-013]has an idea of making him Governor-General. I mentioned Mr. Chaplin. The

[024-014]Duke mentioned Mr. Jenkins, of whom he thought highly. He had done well at

[024-015]Nagpore, and he had had some correspondence with him when in India which

[024-016]gave him a good opinion of him. The Duke spoke of Mr. Russell, but thought

[024-017]he had been mixed up with the Hyderabad transactions. I then mentioned

[024-018]Clare. The Duke thought him better than any of the others mentioned. That

[024-019]it was a great thing to have a man of rank; he must be well supported; he

[024-020]had not a very strong mind. However, on the whole he seemed better than the

[024-021]others, and I am to propose him.

[024-022]I am very glad to have Clare. I have a great respect and regard for him--

[024-023]but I have a little hesitation as to his fitness. He will, however, be a

[024-024]most zealous and honourable servant of the public, and his good manners

[024-025]will keep people in good humour and in order.

[024-026]Leopold has sent in his answer. I have not seen it yet. He accepts on

[024-027]conditions.

[024-028]The debate last night in the Commons is considered very favourable.

[024-029]Dawson's amendment was adopted--and Planta and Holmes say the temper of

[024-030]the country gentlemen is much improved. They are quite in spirits again.

[024-031]A hint of Peel's, but a hint that the Government did not fear an appeal to

[024-032]the country, seems to have had a good effect.

[024-033]_February 14._

[024-034]Cabinet. On Thursday Peel, in opening the Compensation Bill, will detail

[024-035]the various legal reforms.

[024-036]He is disposed to diminish gradually the number of crimes for which the

[024-037]punishment of death is awarded. The Duke seemed reluctant and so did

[024-038]others. However, the Chancellor did not object.

[024-039]My father considered that where a man could not protect his own property

[024-040]the law ought to protect it for him by higher penalties. However, now it

[024-041]seems a man must protect his own property, and punishments are to be

[024-042]proportioned more to the extent of the moral offence than to the necessity

[024-043]for preventing crime.

[024-044]Then we considered Leopold's answer. The man accepts provided--

[024-045]1\. There is a guarantee of the new State.

[024-046]2\. That the frontier is slightly altered.

[024-047]3\. That the three powers protect the present insurgents in Samos and

[024-048]Candia.

[024-049]4\. That a loan of 1,500,000£ is guaranteed.

[024-050]5\. That he may have troops furnished to him.

[024-051]6\. He stipulates that the Greeks should have the power of declining him,

[024-052]_le soussigné_, as their Prince.

[024-053]A guarantee there will probably be, and therefore the alteration of

[024-054]boundaries, which Leopold knew could not be listened to, is in fact

[024-055]unnecessary.

[024-056]Each power separately and individually may use its good offices with the

[024-057]Porte for the protection of the Greeks in Samos and Candia, and indeed,

[024-058]under the agreement as to an amnesty, each would be bound to do so; but no

[024-059]triple agreement will be entered into, the object being to get out of the

[024-060]Treaty of July 6.

[024-061]Aberdeen seemed disposed to allow 1,000 men of each of the three Powers to

[024-062]go to Greece. This would continue the triple action, and as these troops

[024-063]would go, not against any external enemy, but against Greeks, the measure

[024-064]would be somewhat in contradiction to the declaration the other night that

[024-065]the Greeks and their Prince might make what Government they pleased. After

[024-066]some conversation it seemed the general opinion that it would be better to

[024-067]pay the cost of the troops than to have our own there, and in fact the same

[024-068]money would enable Greece to have twice the number of Germans or Swiss that

[024-069]she could have of British. This I thought. But I suggested that Greece

[024-070]could not want a large sum down. A sum might be required for outfit, but

[024-071]then an annual sum. Peel proposed the whole loan guaranteed should be

[024-072]700,000£, of which 100,000£ to be paid down as outfit, and then 100,000£ a

[024-073]year for six years at 5 per cent; the three Powers guaranteeing each a

[024-074]third part of the interest. It is better to guarantee the loan, then to pay

[024-075]money down. The loan, they say, can be made at three. Aberdeen says the

[024-076]Greeks give a most flourishing exposé of their future finances, and he

[024-077]thinks they will become a rich State, and the Powers be exposed to no

[024-078]danger of being called upon for the payment of the interest. I think he

[024-079]begins to love his Greek progeny.

[024-080]The Duke only desired we would get out of the treaty. I suggested the

[024-081]inexpediency of our joining in the guarantee. A guarantee gave no right of

[024-082]intervention we should not otherwise possess, and it obliged us to

[024-083]interfere when we might not desire to do so. However, I fear there will be

[024-084]a guarantee.

[024-085]_February 16._

[024-086]Cabinet. There seems to be little doubt that the Emperor Pedro means to

[024-087]direct an expedition from Rio against Portugal, Terceira being the point of

[024-088]_rassemblement_. This is a practical answer to the question recently put by

[024-089]us conjointly with France and Austria as to the intentions of the Emperor,

[024-090]and therefore we are at liberty to act as if a specific answer had been

[024-091]received. It seems Austria will be very unwilling to recognise Don Miguel;

[024-092]France not.

[024-093]The object of recognising him is to prevent a revolutionary war in Portugal

[024-094]and the entrance of Spanish troops into Portugal to oppose it.

[024-095]Whenever Miguel is recognised, I think Lord Rosslyn will be made Master-

[024-096]General of the Ordnance, Lord Beresford going to Portugal as Minister, and

[024-097]then the Privy Seal will be disposable. I dare say the Duke, out of good

[024-098]nature, will offer it to Lord Westmoreland.

[024-099]Aberdeen read the remonstrance he proposed sending to Spain against the

[024-100]proposed expedition to Mexico.

[024-101]Leopold met the Plenipotentiaries, and Aberdeen thinks he would have

[024-102]acceded, but he evidently required the sanction of another person. The

[024-103]French Ambassador used very strong language, telling him his Court would be

[024-104]very much hurt indeed at finding him make these difficulties after all that

[024-105]had passed, &c.

[024-106]Peel told me he was disposed to grant the motion for any correspondence

[024-107]between the Board of Control or any member of it, &c., with a direct

[024-108]negative. To move the previous question was an admission of some error. I

[024-109]was telling him the circumstances when it was necessary to attend to

[024-110]Aberdeen's business. I must tell him to-morrow.

[024-111]_February 17._

[024-112]At the Cabinet dinner at Lord Melville's, talked to Peel and gave him a

[024-113]copy of the report of the Privy Council and of my letter to Sir J. Grant.

[024-114]He is disposed to take a high tone, and thinks men will follow him better

[024-115]when he does than when he temporises. I am sure they will.

[024-116]He says he would reduce everything so low as not to be beat upon

[024-117]establishments. If he is beat upon unimportant questions he does not care,

[024-118]and will not go out. They will not get a majority for stopping supplies,

[024-119]and if they can agree upon motions, he is prepared to play the game of '83

[024-120][Footnote: Alluding to Pitt's course at the beginning of his first

[024-121]Ministry. He retained office a whole Session in spite of the motions

[024-122]carried against him, and in the general election of 1784 obtained an

[024-123]overwhelming majority.] with them. I am sure he is right.

[024-124]_February 18._

[024-125]House. First a question from Lord Holland whether the orders to the Admiral

[024-126]respecting Greek slaves, &c., would, after the settlement of Greece, apply

[024-127]to Candiot Greeks. Then Lord Melbourne's motion for Portuguese papers. He

[024-128]did not speak well--but very bitterly. Goderich spoke pathetically against

[024-129]the Terceira affair--Lord Wharncliffe well with us--Lansdowne wide and

[024-130]loose--the Duke very excellent--Aberdeen worse than usual, and very

[024-131]imprudent, abusing Miguel and making awkward admissions.

[024-132]It was quite established that Canning had nothing to say to the Portuguese

[024-133]Constitution, and I think we shall hear no more of Terceira. Fifty-two to

[024-134]twenty-one--no proxies.

[024-135]_February 19._

[024-136]Cabinet. Leopold's answer. He wants troops and money. After long talk it

[024-137]was resolved the French troops might stay a year, till he could raise

[024-138]others, and money should be given.

[024-139]_February 20, 1830._

[024-140]In riding with Lord Rosslyn had a long conversation with him upon Indian

[024-141]matters. He had just been reading the despatches from Lord Stuart and Lord

[024-142]Heytesbury upon these subjects. I told him I had anticipated all Lord H.

[024-143]suggested and had done, I really thought, all that could be done. I am to

[024-144]send him the secret letter. He thinks, as I do, that Aberdeen is in a great

[024-145]hurry to get rid of the Greek question, and disposed to incur future

[024-146]embarrassments to avoid present inconvenience.

[024-147]Lord Rosslyn does not much like the division of last night, but I believe

[024-148]it was a good one.

[024-149]_February 21._

[024-150]This morning looked through the finance accounts of the three years, ending

[024-151]1819, and the three ending 1828, with a view to comparing the state of the

[024-152]country with what it was before Peel's Bill. The increased consumption is

[024-153]astonishing. The increase of British tonnage and in the number of seamen

[024-154]since 1819 is equal to the whole tonnage and to all the seamen in the

[024-155]foreign trade with Great Britain, although that is increased nearly in the

[024-156]same proportion with our own.

[024-157]The increased consumption of tea and coffee is 50 per cent. The number of

[024-158]pounds in 1819 being about 30,000,000 of pounds, and now 45,000,000 pounds.

[024-159]The import of foreign raw produce is much increased--of that produce which

[024-160]competes with the landed produce of England.

[024-161]Hardinge called. He thinks the Government quite safe now. Indeed, he never

[024-162]had much apprehension. He regrets Sir James Graham's divergence from the

[024-163]road which leads to office. He thinks he came up to London intending well;

[024-164]but that he thought under present circumstances he could be a more

[024-165]considerable man out of office than he would be in a subordinate situation.

[024-166]The Duke of Northumberland says the salary of the Lord-Lieutenant may well

[024-167]be reduced to 20,000£ a year.

[024-168]_February 24._

[024-169]Lord Rosslyn, who called upon me at the office, thinks I may go a little

[024-170]too far in my directions with regard to Russian spies, that is, in a public

[024-171]despatch. I had directed that if it appeared danger was likely to arise

[024-172]from their return to Europe or from their passage into any Asiatic country,

[024-173]their persons should be placed under restraint, and in all cases their

[024-174]papers and letters got possession of. He suggests that this might be

[024-175]mentioned in a private letter, or left to the discretion of the Local

[024-176]Governments.

[024-177]We had a long conversation on Lord Stanhope's motion for to-morrow, when

[024-178]Whigs and Tories are to combine to beat us.

[024-179]The division last night in the House of Commons on Lord J. Russell's motion

[024-180]for giving two members to Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds, was not

[024-181]satisfactory. There were 140 for it, and only 188 against it. The Tories

[024-182]stayed away.

[024-183]_February 25._

[024-184]House at 4 and until 2. Lord Stanhope's motion for a Committee of the whole

[024-185]House on the internal state of the country. He made a weak speech, because

[024-186]to get votes he abstained from stating the cause of distress, which in his

[024-187]opinion is currency, or any remedy. Goderich and Lansdowne made good

[024-188]speeches. Rosebery not a bad one, though as usual pompous. All suggesting

[024-189]some remedies--all for reducing taxation, but against a Committee of the

[024-190]House. Lord Radnor made a good vulgar speech. King spoke better than usual.

[024-191]He proposed, but afterwards withdrew, an amendment for a Committee

[024-192]upstairs. The Duke, who alone spoke on our side, did not speak well, and

[024-193]some of his statements were hazardous. Lords Darnley and Bute declared

[024-194]there was no distress near them.
