[018-001]There is a good paper of Aberdeen's to Sir R. Gordon, in which he considers

[018-002]the Turkish Empire as falling, and our interest as being to raise Greece,

[018-003]that that State may be the heir of the Ottoman Power. With this view he

[018-004]considers it to be of primary importance that the Government of new Greece

[018-005]should not be revolutionary, and the Prince a good one.

[018-006]There is another good paper defending England against an accusation of

[018-007]Metternich that we should have spoken in a firmer tone to Russia at an

[018-008]earlier period. The King seems much taken with these papers, and writes

[018-009]great encomiums upon them.

[018-010]By Lord Stuart's account it appears probable that Villele will come in. The

[018-011]Government mean to avoid all questions upon which it is possible to have a

[018-012]difference of opinion, and to bring forward only measures of clear and

[018-013]undeniable utility. They think that, if their opponents should endeavour to

[018-014]throw out these measures, the Chambers will support Government.

[018-015]France coincides with us entirely as to the Portuguese question; but

[018-016]wishes, and she is right, that questions more specific had been put to the

[018-017]Emperor Pedro. The intention seems to be to acknowledge Miguel on

[018-018]conditions, when Pedro admits he can do nothing.

[018-019]_December 16._

[018-020]Read Lord Ashley's memorandum on the judicial administration of India. I

[018-021]wrote a note on returning it in which I said he seemed to have taken great

[018-022]pains to collect the opinions which had been given by different persons

[018-023]upon the subject. Mine had been expressed by me in a letter to Sir J.

[018-024]Malcolm on August 7, in which I declared my general concurrence in the

[018-025]views entertained by him and intimated by him in his minute, giving an

[018-026]account of his tour in the southern Mahratta country. I had added that I

[018-027]was satisfied the more we could avail ourselves of the services of the

[018-028]natives in the fiscal and judicial administration the better, and that all

[018-029]good government must rest upon the village system. I told Sir J. Malcolm I

[018-030]had come to my office without any preconceived opinions, that I had kept

[018-031]out of the way of prejudiced men, and had allowed opinions to form

[018-032]themselves gradually in my own mind as I acquired more knowledge from pure

[018-033]sources. I could not, if I had written this passage on purpose, have had

[018-034]one more suited to my purpose. It showed Ashley I was not _prejudiced_,

[018-035]that my opinions were formed before I read his memorandum, and that I had

[018-036]formed them by abstaining from the course he has pursued--for he allows all

[018-037]sorts of persons to come and talk to him, and to inoculate him with their

[018-038]notions.

[018-039]I afterwards said that he would see by Sir Thomas Munro's memorandum of

[018-040]December 31, 1824, that he thought we had succeeded better in the judicial

[018-041]than in the fiscal administration of India, and in the criminal better than

[018-042]in the civil branch of the judicial government. This I said to show I had

[018-043]read Sir T. Munro's memorandum, which he did not give me credit for having

[018-044]done; and that it was not so much to the judicial as to the revenue branch

[018-045]that he should have directed his attention, with a view to improvements--

[018-046]the field being greater.

[018-047]I then said I did not doubt that there were capable natives to be found,

[018-048]but I did doubt that they would be selected, for that the European servants

[018-049]had disappointed me. The natives were better than I expected, &c., &c.

[018-050]Saw the Duke. Suggested to him Sir J. Malcolm's being made provisional

[018-051]successor to Lord W. Bentinck for the reasons I have mentioned. He thought

[018-052]well of the suggestion; but said we must consider it, and mention it in

[018-053]Cabinet, as Lord William was a great card, and we must not do anything to

[018-054]offend unnecessarily him and his connection. The objection occurred to him

[018-055]that had occurred to me, that Sir J. Malcolm would die if he went to

[018-056]Calcutta. I hope he would not go there, that he would remain in the upper

[018-057]provinces. But I look to the effect of the nomination upon the conduct of

[018-058]people in India, and that of Lord William himself, more than to his actual

[018-059]succession.

[018-060]The Duke then said we must look not to India only, but to all Asia, and

[018-061]asked me if I had read Evans's book. I told him I had; that in forty-eight

[018-062]hours after I read it I had sent a copy to Macdonald and another to

[018-063]Malcolm. I told him all the views I had with regard to the navigation of

[018-064]the Indus and the opening of a trade with Cabul and Bokhara. He said our

[018-065]minds appeared to have been travelling the same way. We must have good

[018-066]information of what the Russians might be doing there. I reminded him I had

[018-067]desired the Government a year ago to obtain information as to all the

[018-068]countries between the Caspian and the Indus, and I intended now to give a

[018-069]more particular direction. He said Macdonald should have his eye upon the

[018-070]Caspian, and information as to those countries would be best obtained

[018-071]through natives. I reminded him that that had been the suggestion in my

[018-072]letter of last year. The Duke's opinion is that it is a question of

[018-073]_expense only_. That if the Russians got 20,000 or 30,000 men into Cabul we

[018-074]could beat them; but that by hanging upon us there they could put us to an

[018-075]enormous expense in military preparation, and in quelling insurrections.

[018-076]They could not move in that direction without views hostile to us, and by

[018-077]threatening us there they would think to embarrass us in Europe. I proposed

[018-078]that in the event of the Russians moving in that direction we should permit

[018-079]the Government of India to act as an Asiatic Power. By money at least, he

[018-080]allowed, without further orders, not to move in advance without

[018-081]instructions. But the Duke is ready to take up the question here in Europe,

[018-082]if the Russians move towards India with views of evident hostility.

[018-083]He approves of a message going at once with orders to Macdonald.

[018-084]_December 18._

[018-085]Chairs. They will consider favourably Mrs. Hastings's case; but she must

[018-086]address her representation to them.

[018-087]I told them of my suggestion of making Malcolm provisional successor to

[018-088]Lord William, and the reasons for it. They seemed to like the idea; but the

[018-089]same objection occurred to them which had occurred to the Duke and to me--

[018-090]that if Malcolm went to Calcutta he would die. I said I did not want him to

[018-091]go. I did not look to his going. I looked to the moral effect of the

[018-092]appointment upon Lord William and upon all their servants in India. They

[018-093]want to get some political man of high rank and talents and determined

[018-094]character to go. They are heartily sick of Lord William. Whom they want to

[018-095]send I do not know.

[018-096]I told them of my conversation with the Duke and went over the same ground.

[018-097]They acquiesced in all I said. We shall have the missions to Scinde and to

[018-098]Lahore, and the commercial venture up the Indus, and the instruction to

[018-099]Macdonald. In short, all I want.

[018-100]Despatches are at hand from Lord William, dated May 1, in triplicate, and

[018-101]without the minutes which are referred to as containing the sentiments of

[018-102]the Government. These despatches merely refer the subject to the

[018-103]consideration of the Court.

[018-104]One Jones, it seems, has written almost all the memorials, and is

[018-105]considered a rebel more than a Radical.

[018-106]We had a little conversation respecting the future Government of India. I

[018-107]told them it must be a strong Government, and I doubted whether in its

[018-108]present form it could secure obedience in India. It required more of

[018-109]appearance. They seemed to feel that. Astell acknowledged there was nothing

[018-110]imposing in the name of 'the Company,' and that the present Government was

[018-111]fallen into contempt.

[018-112]I told them I was satisfied that the patronage and the appeals should

[018-113]always remain where they were. I paid them a high compliment, which they

[018-114]justly deserve, upon the fairness of their conduct in deciding upon the

[018-115]claims of their servants.

[018-116]They feel their Government is weak in its last year; but that the Ministers

[018-117]could not do otherwise than have a committee.

[018-118]_December 18._

[018-119]Wrote a letter to the Duke, which he may send to the King, stating the

[018-120]result of my communication to the Chairs respecting Mrs. Hastings.

[018-121]Requested information as to the trade of the Caspian, that carried on by

[018-122]the caravans to Bokhara, and the general condition of that country,

[018-123]desiring likewise that means might be taken to keep us constantly informed

[018-124]of any movements made by the Russians towards the Sea of Aral, and of any

[018-125]attempt to make establishments on the east coast of the Caspian.

[018-126]Wrote to the Duke to tell him what was done and how entirely the Chairs

[018-127]entered into his views.

[018-128]_December 19._

[018-129]Wrote to Loch to suggest that he should send Meyendorff's and Mouravief's

[018-130]books to Macdonald.

[018-131]Read a clever pamphlet on the China trade, and in coming down to Worthing

[018-132]all the papers Hardinge sent me relative to the new pension regulations.

[018-133]_December 20._

[018-134]Read Meyendorff's 'Tour in Bokhara.' It contains all the information I want

[018-135]as to the commerce between Bokhara and Russia. We can easily supply Bokhara

[018-136]with many things the Russians now furnish, and with all Indian goods

[018-137]cheaper by the Indus than the Ganges; but what the Bokharians are to send

[018-138]us in return I do not well see, except turquoises, lapis lazuli, and the

[018-139]ducats they receive from Russia. We may get shawls cheaper by navigating

[018-140]the Indus.

[018-141]_December 21._

[018-142]Read the memorandum the Chairs gave me respecting the application of steam

[018-143]navigation to the internal and external communications of India. It has

[018-144]been prepared carefully and ably, and is very interesting. It suggests the

[018-145]navigation of the Euphrates to Balis or Bir by steam, and thence the

[018-146]passage by Aleppo to Latakia or Scanderoon. It likewise suggests that it

[018-147]might be more expeditious to cross the desert from Suez to Lake Menzaleh,

[018-148]or direct to the sea.

[018-149]_December 22._

[018-150]Wrote to Lord Hill, telling him of Sir G. Walker's dangerous illness, and

[018-151]intimating the importance, under the present circumstances of Madras, of

[018-152]having not only a good soldier as Commander-in-Chief, but a man possessed

[018-153]of good civil qualities.

[018-154]Sent a copy of this letter to the Duke.

[018-155]_December 25._

[018-156]Read a memorandum of Jones on the last mission to Lahore, and a very long

[018-157]secret despatch in 1811 upon the subject of Runjeet Singh's attempt to

[018-158]establish himself on the left bank of the Sutlege, and his retreat in

[018-159]consequence of remonstrances and military demonstration on the part of the

[018-160]British Government.

[018-161]_December 26._

[018-162]Called by appointment on Lady Macdonald, who came here to speak to me about

[018-163]Sir J. Macdonald's salary and position at Tabriz. She says that after the

[018-164]letter he wrote, representing the inexpediency of Sir H. Willock's

[018-165]remaining as his first assistant and the non-existence of any necessity for

[018-166]two assistants, if the Bengal Government do not recall Willock Sir J.

[018-167]Macdonald cannot remain. She has likewise a good deal to say respecting the

[018-168]salary. I think 9,000£ a year a proper salary. The Ambassador at

[018-169]Constantinople has 8,000£ and a house; but Constantinople is on the sea,

[018-170]and the charge of bringing European goods to Tabriz through Russia is so

[018-171]considerable that 1,000£ a year ought to be added for the charge.

[018-172]_December 29._

[018-173]Received three letters from Lord W. Bentinck, of July 6 and 8 and August 2.

[018-174]In that of the 6th he speaks of my private letter to Sir J. Malcolm,

[018-175]published in the 'Calcutta Newspaper.' In that of the 8th he sends it to

[018-176]me, the names being altered, and all between brackets being interpolated,

[018-177]and in fact in the light of comment. In that of August 2 he speaks of the

[018-178]temper of the army, &c., and all public subjects. I have sent the three

[018-179]letters to the Duke.
