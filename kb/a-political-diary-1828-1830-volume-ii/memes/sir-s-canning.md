**Stratford Canning** (1786–1880), later 1st Viscount Stratford de Redcliffe, was a British diplomat and a cousin of Prime Minister George Canning. During the period of [[Lord Ellenborough]]'s diary, he was engaged in British diplomacy in the Near East and appears in connection with Greek affairs.

== Diplomatic Service ==

Canning served as British ambassador to the Ottoman Empire, where he was deeply involved in the Eastern Question — the diplomatic problem posed by the decline of Ottoman power and the emergence of new states in the Balkans. His expertise on Greek affairs made him a significant figure in the diplomatic negotiations chronicled by [[Lord Ellenborough]].

The diary records his presence at diplomatic gatherings: "Stratford Canning and Lord Strangford were at t" (008-147), placing him among the circle of diplomats and politicians engaged with Near Eastern questions. Lord Strangford, another diplomat with extensive experience in Constantinople and St. Petersburg, was a natural associate.

== The Greek Question ==

The diary captures a moment of decision regarding Canning's diplomatic posting. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records a discussion about "keeping Stratford Canning" (010-070) in his role — suggesting that his continuation in post was a matter of active deliberation within the government. This reflects the sensitivity of the Greek question and the importance attached to having the right diplomat in place.

Canning's cousin [[George Canning]] had been Prime Minister briefly in 1827, and his diplomatic network and political connections — the "Canningites" — remained a significant faction in British politics throughout the period of the diary.

== Later Career ==

Though the diary only captures glimpses of his activities, Canning went on to a long and distinguished diplomatic career, serving multiple terms as ambassador to Constantinople and earning the nickname "the Great Elchi" for his influence over Ottoman affairs. He was raised to the peerage as Viscount Stratford de Redcliffe in 1852.
