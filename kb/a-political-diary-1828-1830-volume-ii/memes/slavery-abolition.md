Alongside debates over the [[East India Company]]'s charter, the abolition of the slave trade (1807) had been one of Britain's great moral achievements, but by the late 1820s the unfinished business of slavery itself — and the persistence of illegal slave trading — continued to occupy Cabinet and Parliament. Ellenborough's diary records the Wellington government grappling with the diplomacy of slave-trade suppression, the legal complexities of slave evidence in colonial courts, and the gathering momentum of the abolitionist movement that would culminate in the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833.

## The Slave Trade after 1807

Though Britain had abolished its own slave trade in 1807, other nations continued the traffic. British diplomacy — and the Royal Navy — were actively engaged in suppressing it. The diary records specific Cabinet discussions on Brazilian slave trading:

> [005-142] …d altogether to the Brazilian slave trade from March
>
> [005-144] …seize ships fitted up for the slave trade, whether they had slave…

The Brazilian traffic was particularly vexing, as Britain sought to maintain good relations with newly independent Brazil while enforcing anti-slavery treaties:

> [032-074] introduce in consequence of slave-dealing by Brazilian subjects…

## The "Eternal Slave Question"

The diarist captured the Cabinet's weariness with the seemingly endless legal and diplomatic complexities of the slave issue:

> [037-171] … The business was the eternal slave question--what answer

This "eternal question" involved multiple dimensions:

### Naval Enforcement

The Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron patrolled the Atlantic to interdict slave ships. The diary records debates over the rules of engagement — could ships be seized before slaves were actually on board?

> [008-049] …future vessels fitted for the slave trade, even if they had no
>
> [008-050] slaves on board, should be seizable…

Aberdeen, as Foreign Secretary, was involved in these discussions:

> [008-038] …ulgently inclined towards the slave dealers--not so

### Slave Evidence in Colonial Courts

One of the most contentious legal issues was whether slave testimony should be admissible in colonial courts. The diary records a pivotal meeting:

> [037-174] admission of slave evidence and other points. A …
>
> [037-175] making slave evidence admissible, and it w…
>
> [037-184] …acquiesce in the admission of slave evidence, although

This debate touched on fundamental questions of legal personhood and colonial governance — issues that divided the Cabinet along ideological lines.

## Political Context

The slavery question was deeply entangled with the broader reform movement. The [[Whig Party]], in opposition during Wellington's government, championed abolition as part of its reformist platform. The [[House of Commons]] was the venue for increasingly insistent abolitionist petitions. The Tory Cabinet, dominated by men who saw property rights (including slave property) as fundamental, was cautious:

- **Wellington's position**: Pragmatic; opposed to precipitate action that might destabilize the West Indian colonies
- **Aberdeen's role**: Managed the diplomatic dimension — treaty enforcement and naval suppression
- **The evangelical lobby**: Wilberforce and the Clapham Sect maintained pressure through Parliament and public opinion

## Consequences

The diary captures a government on the defensive against growing abolitionist sentiment. Within three years of these entries, the Great Reform Act would sweep the Tories from power, and the Whig government of [[Lord Grey]] would pass the Slavery Abolition Act (1833), emancipating 800,000 enslaved people in British colonies and establishing a £20 million compensation scheme for slave-owners. The debates recorded in Ellenborough's diary represent the last substantive engagement of an unreformed Tory government with a moral question it could neither fully embrace nor entirely ignore.
