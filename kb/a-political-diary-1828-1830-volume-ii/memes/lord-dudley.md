# Lord Dudley

## Overview

John Ward, 1st Earl of Dudley (1781–1833), was a Tory politician who served as Foreign Secretary from 1827 to 1828 under four successive prime ministers. In Ellenborough's diary, Dudley appears as a significant political figure whose diplomatic letters, voting record, and conversations in the House of Commons lobby are frequently noted.

## Role in the Diary

### Foreign Secretary

Dudley's tenure at the Foreign Office is a recurring theme. Ellenborough observed that when Dudley was in office, his conduct was compared favourably with that of [[Lord Aberdeen]]: "Aberdeen did when Dudley was in office, and I believe all Foreign" ministers operated differently (006-189). The diary also records the detailed diplomatic correspondence that passed through Dudley's hands, noting that "Dudley's letters used to occupy us for days, and certainly they were" affairs of considerable weight (013-172).

### Parliamentary Votes

Dudley's voting record aligned broadly with the Tory government, though not always reliably. On one occasion, the diarist noted that "Dudley was in the House at one time, but he did not vote" (033-178). On another division, Ellenborough recorded with satisfaction that "Lord Jersey voted with us. So did Dudley" (036-067). These observations reflect the diarist's close attention to parliamentary arithmetic.

### Lobby Conversation

Ellenborough records a candid exchange with Dudley in the House of Commons lobby. He opened by telling Dudley about the government's sugar duties: "I had some talk with Dudley in the lobby of the House. I began by saying he" had been misinformed about the sugar question (036-101). The diary continues: "I did not fail to tell Dudley and Bankes in what" terms the duties stood at 63 shillings (036-119). "They were both 'colpiti.' Dudley had had no idea terms so" generous were under consideration (036-121). The Italian _colpiti_ — struck or stunned — conveys the impact of the revelation.

### Office Recommendations

Dudley's name arose in discussions of patronage and appointments. When one candidate met with "objection," the diarist records that someone "suggested Lord Dudley or Melbourne" as an alternative (005-040), indicating Dudley's standing as a safe pair of hands in the political establishment. On the question of franchise reform, "Dudley spoke for" giving the franchise to Birmingham instead of the Hundred (039-071).

## Key Appearances

- **Foreign Office conduct**: Compared to Aberdeen's subsequent management of foreign affairs (006-189).
- **Diplomatic correspondence**: His letters were known for their length and significance (013-172).
- **Parliamentary votes**: Ellenborough tracked Dudley's attendance and voting closely (033-178, 036-067).
- **Sugar duties**: Surprised in the lobby by the government's generous terms on sugar (036-101–121).
- **Birmingham franchise**: Spoke in favour of giving the franchise to Birmingham instead of the Hundred (039-071).
