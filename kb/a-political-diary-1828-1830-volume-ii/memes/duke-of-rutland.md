# Duke of Rutland

## Overview

John Henry Manners, 5th Duke of Rutland (1778–1857), was a British peer and Tory politician. A firm opponent of Catholic emancipation, he was a significant figure in the Lords during the debates on the Catholic Relief Bill of 1829. Ellenborough's diary captures his shifting vote and its impact on the government's calculations.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records the Duke of Rutland as a crucial vote during the Catholic emancipation crisis. During a key division, he notes that "The Duke of Rutland, who was not expected, and indeed every doubtful vote was with us" (P01-129). This unexpected support from an anti-emancipation peer was significant enough to warrant special mention — it helped the government secure its majority and left Ellenborough observing that "the Protestants are subdued" (P01-131).

However, Rutland's position was not consistent. In a subsequent reference, Ellenborough notes that "The Duke of Rutland against, having been with us before" (001-040). This is listed alongside other wavering peers including Winchelsea and Kenyon, who were "lost to the Government" (001-038). The diary thus captures the fluidity of aristocratic voting alignments during one of the most divisive parliamentary episodes of the era.

## Key Appearances

- **Unexpected support**: Rutland's vote with the government, contrary to expectations, helped secure the emancipation majority (P01-129–131).
- **Shifting allegiance**: He subsequently voted against the government, joining other Tory peers who had broken with Wellington over emancipation (001-040).
