# Duke of Clarence

## Overview

The Duke of Clarence (Prince William Henry) was the third son of George III and heir presumptive to the British throne during the Wellington ministry (1828–1830). Upon the death of his elder brother [[Lord Eldon|George IV]] in June 1830, he ascended as King William IV. Ellenborough's diary captures him in the final years of his royal dukedom — ill, politically engaged, and in close contact with the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government.

## Role in the Diary

During the period covered by the diary, the Duke of Clarence appears as a figure of both political and personal significance. The Duke of Wellington maintained regular communication with him, visiting "in uniform" to advise him on matters of state (029-099). Clarence, in turn, kept the Duke informed through letters on sensitive subjects, including the King's health (033-087).

The diary records the transition from George IV to William IV in a terse footnote: "The Duke of Clarence now became William IV" (034-015). Even after his accession, the diarist observed that the new King "made speeches and gave toasts as if he was Duke of Clarence at the" table — suggesting the informality of his earlier character persisted (041-136).

## Key Appearances

- **Illness and absence**: At the start of the diary period, Clarence "was absent, being ill. He had fourteen leeches on his" person, reflecting his precarious health (001-041).
- **Relationship with Hardinge**: The diary notes that "the Duke of Clarence is very fond of Hardinge, and tells him all he means to do" — suggesting a close and somewhat indiscreet confidant relationship with the military secretary (027-059).
- **The King's final illness**: During George IV's decline, Clarence "was in the room with him" and received frank warnings about the King's danger (031-131, 031-134).
- **The succession**: Wellington's government navigated the transition carefully, with "some little doubt as to the Duke of Clarence. I have none," the diarist records, suggesting confidence in the succession (029-175).

## Political Alignment

The Duke of Clarence was seen as broadly sympathetic to the Wellington administration, though the diary hints at tensions. Wellington believed that if certain communications reached Clarence, he "would think he was ill-used" (029-155). The careful management of Clarence reflects his pivotal constitutional position as the heir apparent during a period of intense political conflict over Catholic emancipation and parliamentary reform.
