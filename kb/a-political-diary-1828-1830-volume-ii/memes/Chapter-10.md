[010-001]Drummond, whom I saw, said the Duke was delighted with the account of the

[010-002]Jaghirdars of the Kistna. Granville is gone to Ireland.

[010-003]The Duke was gone to Windsor. It is the King's birthday.

[010-004]_August 13._

[010-005]When the Cabinet was assembled the Duke said we were not to consider the

[010-006]state of things at Constantinople, and what we should do. He thought the

[010-007]Russians would get to Constantinople, and into it. If they did he thought

[010-008]there was an end of the Ottoman Empire. He was doubtful whether, after the

[010-009]innovations introduced, the Turks would cordially support Mahmoud,

[010-010][Footnote: Sultan Mahmoud, as is well known, remodelled the whole internal

[010-011]organisation of the Turkish Empire. He was denounced as the Giaour Sultan

[010-012]by old-fashioned Turks.] and already there were insurrections of the

[010-013]Greeks. It was just what he predicted in his letter to La Ferronays, and

[010-014]what Lord Dudley afterwards said in a letter to Lièven; the success of the

[010-015]Russians was the dissolution of an Empire which could not be reconstituted.

[010-016]It was too late to interfere by force, even if we had been disposed to do

[010-017]so alone.

[010-018]He thought France, if we did nothing, would be quiet--if we did anything,

[010-019]she would take the other line. Polignac was a more able man than people

[010-020]supposed, and he would adhere to the course he adopted. We might endeavour,

[010-021]at any rate, to ascertain his feelings and intentions.

[010-022]As to the Greek question we must have a conference, and consider the

[010-023]suggestions of the Ambassadors, namely, that whatever we chose to make

[010-024]Greece, should be declared independent, and guaranteed. Both the Duke and

[010-025]Aberdeen thought France and Russia would both take the proposition into

[010-026]consideration. The former as to _limits_, the latter for delay. France had

[010-027]already told us that, provided we could agree upon the limits, she was

[010-028]inclined to adopt the suggestion of the Ambassadors.

[010-029]We asked whether the permanent occupation of Constantinople by Russia was

[010-030]to be submitted to? The answer was, _No_, to be opposed by war. It seemed

[010-031]to me and to Fitzgerald we had better endeavour to prevent, at a small

[010-032]expense, even if alone, a measure we could only retrieve if it took place

[010-033]at an enormous expense, if at all, and which would in all probability

[010-034]effect the ruin of the Turkish Empire. I did not think affairs quite so

[010-035]desperate. I thought the Russians might get to Adrianople, but not to

[010-036]Constantinople, and that they could not maintain themselves at Adrianople

[010-037]without the command of the sea. We had six ships at the mouth of the

[010-038]Dardanelles, and these with the Turkish Fleet would open the Black Sea.

[010-039]I was for passing our ships up to Constantinople and placing them at the

[010-040]disposal of the Ambassador, for from hence we cannot give orders adapted to

[010-041]circumstances. It was replied _that_ would be war. If war were to be

[010-042]declared we should do as much mischief as possible, and go to Cronstadt,

[010-043]not to the Black Sea. We should have our ships beyond the Bosphorus when

[010-044]Russia occupied the Dardanelles, and shut us in. This would make us

[010-045]ridiculous.

[010-046]As the object is not to do mischief to Russia, but to save the Turkish

[010-047]Empire, I should say that measure was to be effected at the Bosphorus, for

[010-048]Constantinople, once taken, and the Ottoman Power annihilated, it would be

[010-049]of no use to distress Russia.

[010-050]Fitzgerald seemed to be of my opinion that, however desperate the chance,

[010-051]we should do all we could to save Constantinople, and at any risk.

[010-052]It was determined that our fleet in the Mediterranean should be reinforced

[010-053]by three or four line-of-battle ships, on the principle that wherever any

[010-054]Power had a large force, we should have one--not a very wise principle, it

[010-055]seems to me, if we are never to use force. I interceded for a few powerful

[010-056]steamers, with 68 pound carronades, and I think Lord Melville seemed

[010-057]inclined to acquiesce.

[010-058]Questions are to be put to Polignac to ascertain what he would do in

[010-059]certain events. I said he never would open himself to Lord Stuart. It was

[010-060]then suggested by the Duke that Aberdeen could write a private letter. This

[010-061]will, I believe, be done. I said to Fitzgerald, who was next to me,

[010-062]'Neither letter nor Stuart will get anything out of Polignac. One of

[010-063]ourselves should go to Paris as an individual, see Polignac, and return

[010-064]before the Conference.'

[010-065]I suggested Rosslyn, as he had nothing to do. Fitzgerald said he could go

[010-066]and return in a week, and seemed to wish to do so. However, nothing was

[010-067]said openly; and with all the means of success in our hands, for, I think,

[010-068]Polignac _might_ be brought into our views, we shall lose all by not using

[010-069]proper instruments; just as we have lost the Greek question by persisting

[010-070]in keeping Stratford Canning.

[010-071]We had a good deal of conversation as to the limits of Greece. The Duke was

[010-072]for adhering to the Morea. It was _really_ the best line. It was what we

[010-073]had guaranteed. We had told the Turks we did not mean to go beyond it.

[010-074]Aberdeen has always had a little private hankering after Athens, though he

[010-075]ridicules it. He had no scruple about annexing Athens, although not yet

[010-076]taken. I said I thought Polignac would be disposed to hold our language to

[010-077]Russia, if we would make some concession on the subject of Greece, and

[010-078]enable him to settle that question with _éclat_. He would then be supported

[010-079]by France in any strong language he might hold, and would establish himself

[010-080]by the experiment of his first fortnight of office.

[010-081]However, the Cabinet seems disposed to look at accessories, not at

[010-082]principles, at the minor objects rather than at _the one great object_,

[010-083]which is inducing France to act with us to prevent the occupation of

[010-084]Constantinople or to force its evacuation. Instead of yielding upon points

[010-085]of minor importance, in order to carry the question, we are to insist now

[010-086]on the minor points-the evacuation of the Morea by the French, and then, I

[010-087]fear we shall weaken Polignac's Government, and lose our object.

[010-088]Our foreign policy has certainly been, most unsuccessful. We have succeeded

[010-089]in nothing.

[010-090]The communication to be made to Polignac is to be made to him

[010-091]confidentially, and he is to know it is not to be made to Austria. It is

[010-092]considered that in any case Austria would support France and England if

[010-093]they acted together, and any indication Austria might give of moving alone

[010-094]would bring down Prussia upon her. This line, I think, well considered and

[010-095]prudent.

[010-096]It seemed to be thought that, if the Turkish Empire should be _dissolved_,

[010-097]Austria might be inclined to share the spoils and be quiet; but if it were

[010-098]only _weakened_, she would feel she suffered.

[010-099]It seemed to be admitted by all that we ought to have taken a decided step

[010-100]long ago. That we were too late, and that we were inexcusable.

[010-101]I said a year ago Aberdeen would ruin us--he would gradually let us down,

[010-102]not by any flagrant error, but by being always under the mark. The Duke,

[010-103]occupied as he is as Prime Minister, wanted an efficient secretary for

[010-104]Foreign Affairs, and he could not have had a worse.

[010-105]Peel seems to think Ireland stands much better since the proclamation

[010-106]respecting the attack made by the Ribbonmen upon the Orangemen in

[010-107]Fermanagh. He seems to think the Irish Government ready enough when things

[010-108]are brought to their notice, but that they do not read or attend to the

[010-109]reports made to them.

[010-110]_August 19._

[010-111]I am inclined to think from what Colonel Hodgson says that leather might be

[010-112]made in India as well as here. They have the hide of the buffalo. They want

[010-113]the _tanning_, and some one must be sent from this country to teach them.

[010-114]He told me of a Mr. Cotton who was long at Tanjore, where the iron is, and

[010-115]I have written to him.

[010-116]_August 22._

[010-117]The Russians have taken Erzeroum, and have quite dispersed the Turkish army

[010-118]in Asia. Every success of theirs in that quarter makes my heart bleed. I

[010-119]consider it a victory gained over me, as Asia is _mine_.

[010-120]_August 28._

[010-121]The 'Courier' of last night throws doubts on the reported victory of

[010-122]Kirkhilissa. The Sultan is said to be now ready to treat. The plague is in

[010-123]the Russian army, and in the country before them. Had a long conversation

[010-124]with Hardinge on Indian affairs.

[010-125]_August 29._

[010-126]Read a letter from Mr. Cartwright, the Consul at Constantinople, dated the

[010-127]9th. The loss of Erzeroum is to be attributed to the Janizaries. In all

[010-128]Asia they seem to be rising. The Russians are not expected to advance till

[010-129]they are joined by 15,000 men, coming by sea. Thus our fleet would have

[010-130]saved Constantinople.

[010-131]Cabinet at half-past three. Before the Cabinet read Lord Heytesbury's and

[010-132]Mr. Gordon's despatches. Lord Heytesbury seems to be a mere Russian.

[010-133]_August 31._

[010-134]Mr. Gordon describes the Turkish Empire as falling to pieces. The national

[010-135]enthusiasm and religious feeling of the people seem to be gone. The Sultan

[010-136]is unpopular. The populace of Adrianople desires the advance of the

[010-137]Russians, so scandalous has been the conduct of the Asiatics. The Pacha of

[010-138]Egypt gives no assistance, and thinks the weakness of the Porte constitutes

[010-139]his strength. The people of Trebizond have invited Count Paskewitz.

[010-140]Erzeroum was lost by the treachery of the Janizaries.

[010-141]The Sultan has acceded to the Treaty of London. This accession is

[010-142]qualified, but not in such a manner as to preclude negotiation. He has

[010-143]consented to treat with Russia, to give freedom to the navigation of the

[010-144]Black Sea, and to observe the Treaty of Akerman--but he stipulates for the

[010-145]integrity of the Ottoman dominions in Europe and Asia. He has not, however,

[010-146]sent Plenipotentiaries.

[010-147]General Muffling, the Prussian, is arrived at Constantinople. He reports

[010-148]the moderate views of the Emperor Nicholas, and states them.

[010-149]The French Government, from the information it derived from its Minister at

[010-150]Berlin, has instructed Count Guilleminot to declare to the Turks the terms

[010-151]on which Russia will make peace. Russia requires the execution of the

[010-152]Treaty of Akerman--indemnity--(but moderate) for the expenses of the war

[010-153]and the losses sustained by her commerce, for which indemnity, as it seems,

[010-154]she is willing to take Anapa.

[010-155]She requires the free navigation of the Dardanelles for all nations. This

[010-156]cession to be secured by treaty, not by territorial occupation.

[010-157]The terms of the Turks are not very dissimilar; but as Count Diebitch has

[010-158]orders to advance till preliminaries are signed, a catastrophe may take

[010-159]place still.

[010-160]Mr. Gordon managed to get a paper into the Sultan's own hands, which may

[010-161]have led in some measure to this result. He naturally gave credit to the

[010-162]information contained in the Despatches of Count Guilleminot, but the

[010-163]French Government have no authority for their opinion as to the terms on

[010-164]which Russia will make peace. No communication to that effect has been made

[010-165]officially to them.

[010-166]The French and Russian Ministers at the Conference said they could not act

[010-167]on Mr. Gordon's letter, which is as yet uncorroborated by Count

[010-168]Guilleminot. They could not yet act as if Turkey had acceded to the Treaty

[010-169]of London.

[010-170]The Russians would now declare the independence of Greece within the Gulfs

[010-171]of Volo and Arta, and they wanted Aberdeen to take that instead of the

[010-172]treaty. He thought he could get them to declare the independence of Greece

[010-173]_within the Morea_--that they would be satisfied with that, and that, if

[010-174]they would, we had better secure that for the Turks now, than run the risk

[010-175]of the event of war and of the extension which might be given to the terms

[010-176]which might be forced upon them under the Treaty of London.
