# Mr. Fergusson

## Overview

Mr. Fergusson was a legal or official figure connected with East India Company (EIC) administration, who appears in Ellenborough's diary during 1828. His role appears to have involved advising on delicate legal questions concerning the Indian territories, and he participated in parliamentary proceedings relating to the EIC.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough's first reference to Fergusson comes in the context of legislative delicacy: "this was one of much delicacy. I should speak to Fergusson" (001-102). The matter, though not detailed in surviving text, was significant enough that a Cabinet had been summoned over it.

Shortly afterward, Ellenborough records a meeting with Fergusson concerning a petition from Hindu and Muslim residents of Calcutta: "Saw Mr. Fergusson respecting a petition from Hindoos and Mahometans at Calcutta, praying to be allowed to sit on grand juries" (001-129). Fergusson supported the petition's principle — noting that Indians were already permitted to serve on petty juries — but expressed reservations about allowing them to sit on trials of Christians (001-134).

At an EIC debate, Fergusson's choice of terminology drew mockery: "Fergusson spoke of 'the Court.' Brougham said he was not surprised he should make that mistake seeing such an array of directors" (033-096). The occasion saw Brougham challenge the Directors' right to vote on matters in which they held a direct personal interest.

## Key Appearances

- **Legislative advice**: Consulted on a matter described as "one of much delicacy," with implications serious enough to summon a Cabinet (001-102).
- **Calcutta petition**: Advised Ellenborough on the Hindu and Muslim petition concerning grand jury service, supporting eligibility in principle while noting concerns about trials of Christians (001-129, 001-134).
- **East India debate**: Spoke at a parliamentary proceeding concerning the EIC, where his reference to "the Court" of Directors was seized upon by Henry Brougham (033-096).
