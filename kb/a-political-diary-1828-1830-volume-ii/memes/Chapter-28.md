[028-001]Thus he has obtained almost all he asked, and what he most wanted, the

[028-002]money.

[028-003]Peel seems to think the King's death by no means improbable. If it should

[028-004]take place, Parliament would adjourn till after the funeral, and then be

[028-005]dissolved.

[028-006]In the House Lord Durham, in presenting a petition against the East Indian

[028-007]monopoly, said he gathered from what had fallen from His Majesty's

[028-008]Ministers that they were determined to maintain it.

[028-009]I said, 'I cannot admit that anything which has fallen from me, or, in my

[028-010]presence, from any of my noble colleagues, can justify the noble lord in

[028-011]assuming that His Majesty's Government have formed any determined opinion

[028-012]upon the subject.'

[028-013]_April 27._

[028-014]House. East Retford case. The Duke showed me a letter from Halford which

[028-015]gives a very alarming account of the King. He went on much the same till

[028-016]half-past three this morning, when Halford was sent for and remained till

[028-017]half-past eight. The embarrassment of breathing was considerable. The King

[028-018]was rather better at half-past ten, when the bulletin was dated. Halford

[028-019]says he can tell more than he can write. He does write that there is

[028-020]_water_, and it is evident the King is very much alarmed.

[028-021]From the letter I should say he could not live many weeks.

[028-022]In the House Lord Strangford told me that Sir W. Seymour [Footnote:

[028-023]Recently appointed a judge at Bombay.] was dead. He died in December--a

[028-024]short time after the birth of his son.

[028-025]Really the mortality amongst judges is awful.

[028-026]_April 28._

[028-027]Went to Guildhall to be present at the trial of Serjeant Kearney for the

[028-028]assault on Astell. I was not called as a witness. The man was very

[028-029]intemperate indeed, and abused Astell very much. He spoke of my kind

[028-030]interference, &c., but made a mistake in imagining that I had advocated

[028-031]with the Chairs the loan he asked of 250£. I came away as soon as the

[028-032]Recorder began to sum up. It was curious to see how justice was

[028-033]administered. The Recorder, an old twaddle, who talked half the time with

[028-034]the accused, and allowed him to make speeches instead of putting questions,

[028-035]and Sir C. Hunter, Sir J. Shaw, and another alderman!

[028-036]Went to the office at 3. Loch, with whom I had some conversation at

[028-037]Guildhall, told me he had heard the explanation Melville intended to give

[028-038]of the matter of prime cost, and he thought it satisfactory. Wortley said

[028-039]Arbuthnot by no means thought it satisfactory, but was to put the

[028-040]questions. Wortley said Arbuthnot told him the Duke had read the evidence

[028-041]and was himself satisfied the monopoly could not be maintained.

[028-042]Cabinet dinner at Lord Bathurst's. The Duke was at Windsor this morning. He

[028-043]did not see the King because the King refused to see the Duke of

[028-044]Cumberland, and begged the Duke would not see him unless it was very

[028-045]pressing, that the rebuff to the Duke of Cumberland might be less.

[028-046]Accordingly, the Duke sent in on paper what he had to say, and he got two

[028-047]signatures, although they were given very reluctantly. The King says it is

[028-048]_unkind_ in those about him to urge him to sign, as they know how

[028-049]distressing it is to him. In fact _yesterday_ it would have been death to

[028-050]move his arm. We are to meet on Friday to consider what shall be done. Some

[028-051]means must be devised of getting signatures, for his state may last some

[028-052]months. He was ill for four hours yesterday evening. Halford was with him

[028-053]all the time, and held his hand. Halford says he is sure the King would

[028-054]have died had he not been there. He was nearly dead as it was. However,

[028-055]after this attack, which began at half-past two, he had a solid dinner and

[028-056]slept well, and this morning he woke much relieved, but _with a dropsy_--

[028-057]that is, an external dropsy, the water being between the skin. Knighton

[028-058]thinks some must be upon the chest; but the two others are inclined to

[028-059]think not. He may live days, weeks, or even months; but I doubt his living

[028-060]weeks. On Sunday he saw the women, and on Monday too. He was then alarmed

[028-061]about himself. Now he mistakes water for gout, although his legs are

[028-062]swelled to double their usual size. The physicians do not undeceive him.

[028-063]However, the public will find it out. He has not read the newspapers for

[028-064]two days _He_ is much relieved by the effusion of water.

[028-065]It seems the medical men when they read the first bulletin said, 'It must

[028-066]end in water.'

[028-067]Lord Rosslyn has looked into the Acts, &c., and finds there is no

[028-068]difficulty at all about the money vote on the Bills. They all went on at

[028-069]the accession of the present King.

[028-070]The Duke was requested by the physicians and the people about the King not

[028-071]to mention Shiel's proposed appointment; to make it, if he thought it

[028-072]essential, but to spare the King all discussion. Of course, as it is

[028-073]thought the King would be agitated, the Duke has neither mentioned it nor

[028-074]done it.

[028-075]There was in circulation a letter from the Duke of Northumberland

[028-076]expressing his extreme satisfaction at the decision of the Government with

[028-077]respect to the putting down of the new associations, and likewise with

[028-078]respect to the making of the Catholic silk gowns.

[028-079]The bulletins are to be now shown at St. James's; a lord and groom-in-

[028-080]waiting will be there.

[028-081]Received a letter from Sir J. Dewar [Footnote: Chief Justice of Bombay and

[028-082]a colleague of Sir W. Seymour. They were the two judges referred to in the

[028-083]letter to Sir J. Malcolm.] to inform me of the death of Sir W. Seymour. He

[028-084]died more of the fear of dying than of fever. His apprehension for Lady

[028-085]Seymour affected him very much. She was confined the day he was taken ill.

[028-086]_April 29._

[028-087]Halford thinks worse of the King. There have been other attacks of

[028-088]embarrassment of breathing. I do not myself think he will live a fortnight.

[028-089]There was an excellent division on Terceira about 2-1/2 to 1. Hardinge was

[028-090]not wanted.

[028-091]_April 30._

[028-092]Cabinet. The King very ill yesterday. The least exertion brings on an

[028-093]attack. Halford thinks he has water in the abdomen and chest. He had some

[028-094]sleep, and was better in the morning when they issued the bulletin, which

[028-095]says his symptoms were alleviated. However, the bulletin so little

[028-096]corresponds with his real state that they think he saw it. It seems to be

[028-097]now more an affair of days than of weeks. It may happen at any moment.

[028-098]Peel suggested the possible case of both Kings dying before an Act

[028-099]appointing a regent, and we may be called upon to provide for it. The

[028-100]Duchess of Clarence would be Queen Regent.

[028-101]We talked about a Bill for enabling the King to give authority for the

[028-102]affixing of the Sign Manual.

[028-103]To avoid delay and the examination of physicians Rosslyn proposed that, if

[028-104]the King would sign it, there should be a message.

[028-105]It will be arranged that there shall be two Ministers present-one to

[028-106]countersign, the other to affix the stamp.

[028-107]The Attorney- and Solicitor-General were called in. They evidently thought

[028-108]the King's mind was gone as well as his head, for they proposed a

[028-109]delegation of the Royal authority.

[028-110]Planta called upon me to ask more particulars as to the office of Signer of

[028-111]the Writs. It seems it comes in lucky time to oblige Lord Chandos, who has

[028-112]long wanted something for a Mr. Wentworth, and nothing could have happened

[028-113]more conveniently for the Government.

[028-114]_May 1._

[028-115]Met Lord Rosslyn, who told me he and Lord Bathurst met every committee day

[028-116]Lord Londonderry and Lord Durham on the Coal Committee. Sometimes they

[028-117]could not get a fifth, and then they adjourned joyfully. Both Lord

[028-118]Londonderry and Lord Durham continued most wrong-headed upon the question.

[028-119]_May 2._

[028-120]I rode as fast as I could to town as soon as church was over (for the Duke

[028-121]had wished to see me before he went to church, thinking I was in town), and

[028-122]in Brompton met Lord Rosslyn, who told me there was no Cabinet, and that

[028-123]the Duke had found the King better than he expected.

[028-124]Rode at once to Apsley House. The Duke was gone out, having left word he

[028-125]should be back soon if I came. I waited an hour. When he returned he told

[028-126]me he had no idea I was out of town, or he would not have written. Lord

[028-127]Combermere had asked to see him, which he could not refuse.

[028-128]The Duke said that on Friday the King was much better. The miracle which

[028-129]the physicians had said could alone save him seemed accomplished. Great

[028-130]quantities of ether-quantities much greater than are usually given-had

[028-131]apparently restored him, and all were in good spirits, when, feeling

[028-132]himself much better, he drank a great deal and was actually sick! Thence

[028-133]the indifferent night of Friday. On Saturday he was better again, and when

[028-134]the Duke saw him, seemingly very well, quite alive--in very good humour

[028-135]with everybody, and quite without nervousness. However, he passed a bad

[028-136]night, as the bulletin says, probably in consequence of having drunk again.

[028-137]Sir H. Halford was quite in tears on Saturday, not more on account of the

[028-138]King's state than on account of his own professional disappointment. He had

[028-139]thought on the Friday that he had accomplished a miracle. They have treated

[028-140]the King as if he had been a hospital patient, and have _épuisé'd_ the

[028-141]resources of art boldly applied to his case.

[028-142]The King did not express the least apprehension to the Duke; but to the

[028-143]women he speaks of his danger, and as if he was a dying man. The Duke

[028-144]thinks he does this to try and vex Lady Conyngham.

[028-145]The thing most surprising to me is the Duke's opinion of the King's firm

[028-146]courage. He said he had seen him not only now, but before, when he was

[028-147]considered not to have twenty-four hours of life in him, yet he, knowing

[028-148]his situation, was perfectly firm.

[028-149]Before the Duke came I had some talk with Holmes, whom I met with Drummond.

[028-150]Holmes said they could finish the session by the end of July if they acted

[028-151]with that view. I fear it will last much longer if the King lives, and if

[028-152]he dies, that we shall have a six weeks' session in August and September.

[028-153]Holmes said he did not think the King's illness by any means diminished the

[028-154]strength of Government. He thought the friends of Government were rather

[028-155]more disposed to come down, and he could on any great question get 300.

[028-156]He had gone round on Wednesday to the reporters, and had told them they

[028-157]would never have a holiday if they reported speeches on a Wednesday, so

[028-158]they did not, and they will not. This will put an end to all speechifying

[028-159]on holidays.

[028-160]_May 3._

[028-161]Cabinet. Saw a letter from Halford to the Duke. The King was 'in a most

[028-162]distressing, not to say alarming, state' from eight to-day evening to half-

[028-163]past three. He cannot get sleep. Halford says it was 'a gigantic struggle.'

[028-164]The Duke saw Lord Combermere to-day, having received the letters I sent him

[028-165]before the interview. The Duke told him the Government were parties to the

[028-166]disapprobation expressed by the Court of Directors.

[028-167]Lord C. threw the whole blame upon Lord W. Bentinck. He had carried the

[028-168]order into execution without communication with him, 'and had told the army

[028-169]if they objected to it, they might memorialise.'

[028-170]This _I do not believe_.

[028-171]Lord C. said the army was not in a state approaching to mutiny, and never

[028-172]had been.

[028-173]He had not said it was in his minutes (but he did in a letter); as to the

[028-174]minutes of the other members of Council, he was not responsible for them.

[028-175]They were civilians. Besides, Lord W. wished to go up the country. He had

[028-176]received in July a letter telling him he was not to go except in a case of

[028-177]emergency, then the Government was not to move from Calcutta, and he

[028-178]endeavoured in his minute and the others in theirs to make an apparent case

[028-179]of emergency that they might move.
