# Duke of Richmond

## Overview

Charles Gordon-Lennox, 5th Duke of Richmond, was a significant figure in the [[tory-party|Tory]] party during the late 1820s. Though nominally of the same political camp, he emerged as a persistent parliamentary critic of the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s administration, using his position in the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] to challenge government measures on the franchise, the wool trade, and the sale of beer. Ellenborough's diary records these clashes with evident frustration.

## Role in the Diary

Richmond appears as a recurrent parliamentary adversary. The diarist records multiple instances of the Duke opposing government bills and introducing motions that Wellington's ministry found obstructive. The diary's tone suggests Richmond was seen as imprudent and prone to misrepresentation: "The Duke of Richmond has been very imprudent. Had he" exercised more judgment, the implication runs, he would have been a more constructive force (002-068).

In a telling episode during debate, the diarist records: "Then got up the Duke of Richmond, totally misrepresenting what" had been said, prompting an immediate corrective response (036-040). After which, the diarist "explained that I" had been misunderstood, and the debate continued in the same adversarial tone (036-043).

## Key Parliamentary Interventions

- **Franchise Bill opposition**: Richmond was listed among the opponents of the Franchise Bill, alongside [[lord-eldon|Lord Eldon]] and others (P01-155).
- **Wool trade motion**: He "moved for a select committee on the wool question," triggering a significant debate (025-039, 026-076).
- **Sale of Beer Bill**: The diarist "Answered the Duke of Richmond on the sale of Beer Bill," suggesting direct parliamentary confrontation (037-137).
- **Postal reform**: Richmond was considered for a government post, with speculation that he was "to be Postmaster-General" or even "First Lord of" the Admiralty (053-109, 054-023).

## Political Significance

Despite his oppositional stance, Richmond's prominence within the Tory party made him a figure the government could not ignore. The diary records that "the Duke of Richmond said they should vote for the" motion, indicating his influence over other peers (033-121). At one point, the diarist admitted he "had nearly forgotten" Richmond when listing political actors — yet Richmond's interventions consistently commanded attention in the chamber (051-017).
