# Lord Palmerston

## Overview

Lord Palmerston (Henry John Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston) was a leading Canningite politician during the period of Ellenborough's diary. Though in opposition to the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government for most of 1828–1830, he was repeatedly courted for office. His foreign policy expertise made him a formidable opposition figure.

## Parliamentary Opposition

Palmerston was a consistent critic of Wellington's foreign policy. On one occasion, a Cabinet dinner was disrupted because "the others [were] detained by Lord Palmerston's motion on Portugal, on which there was a majority of two to one, 150 odd to 70 something." (025-140)

Ellenborough noted Palmerston's rhetorical skill with some resentment. The government could not "reply without entering into an undignified discussion with Palmerston, who would be the real controversialist." (031-072) One dispatch was suspected: "Palmerston wrote it. He takes popular ground." (031-034)

## Courtship by the Government

As Wellington's ministry weakened, Palmerston became an attractive potential recruit. Ellenborough recorded: "He thinks Palmerston the best man to have in Goulburn's place, Goulburn going to the Speakership." (037-156)

The Duke himself asked "Hardinge what he thought as to taking Huskisson and Palmerston back again? Hardinge declared against having Huskisson, but recommended Palmerston." (039-024) Following this, Ellenborough predicted "Palmerston will be offered the Chancellorship of the Exchequer." (039-027)

Palmerston appeared receptive to overtures. At a political gathering, "Huskisson and Palmerston were there. Huskisson very sulky and sour. Palmerston very cordial, as if he thought he might come in, I should be glad if he did." (038-109)

## Relations with the Whigs

By late 1829, Palmerston was moving closer to the Whig opposition. A footnote in the diary records that "it appears from Lord Palmerston's published papers that this was an error. He had already determined to act with the Whigs." (047-059) This alignment was sealed when the Whigs took power in 1830.

His connection with the Whig leadership was noted: Palmerston "was to be made a great man of" in the projected Whig administration. (031-115) He was recorded at a political meeting at Lord Lansdowne's alongside the Duke of Newcastle — a grouping that "indicated an alliance of Whigs, Canningites, and Tories irritated by the Roman Catholic Bill." (032-038)
