[042-001]The new King's oath-taking was flatly received. As long as he can keep La

[042-002]Fayette with him he is master of Paris.

[042-003]Lord Stuart seems to have behaved prudently in merely acknowledging the

[042-004]receipt of the communication from Marshal Jourdan of his being appointed

[042-005]foreign secretary. The Neapolitan Ambassador wished to have a note

[042-006]generally agreed upon. All the Ambassadors say they are so sure England

[042-007]will judge rightly, that they will, without instructions, follow in our

[042-008]wake.

[042-009]La Fayette has originated the idea of a mission of deputies of the National

[042-010]Guard to London to thank the English people for their sympathy. Lord Stuart

[042-011]hopes the King will induce La Fayette to give up this mischievous and

[042-012]foolish scheme.

[042-013]_August 18._

[042-014]Lord J. Russell is not returned for Bedford. He lost it by one vote. He has

[042-015]published a good address, and is evidently very indignant.

[042-016]Brougham has had questions put to him by Martin Bree, which he has answered

[042-017]satisfactorily to the venereal doctor. It would have been good fun had they

[042-018]fought.

[042-019]The only merit of the French Revolution seems to be that it has not been

[042-020]vindictive. If they are wise they will not touch the lives of the

[042-021]Ministers. The new King calls his eldest son Duke of Orleans. All the

[042-022]daughters are to be Princesses of Orleans, distinguished by their Christian

[042-023]names.

[042-024]This is like Henry IV.'s policy in reserving the Duchy of Lancaster. He

[042-025]wishes to be able to make room for Henry V. He has given up his property to

[042-026]his eldest son's little children, and would probably, if he were displaced,

[042-027]emigrate quietly, as he has often done before, and leave his children in

[042-028]possession.

[042-029]When Brougham accused the Duke of Wellington of advising Polignac, the

[042-030]whole meeting of his own friends expressed dissent. It is incredible that

[042-031]he should be so foolish as to believe such a thing, or as to attempt to

[042-032]make others believe it.

[042-033]_August 19._

[042-034]I see by the 'Sun' that the ex-King of France is arrived at Portsmouth. I

[042-035]am very sorry for it, although he will not be received by the King, and

[042-036]will probably sail immediately. He may require refitting, for I dare say he

[042-037]brought off little from Rambouillet. His packets are accompanied by two

[042-038]French vessels of war, and all the French vessels at Spithead hoisted the

[042-039]tri-coloured flag when he was known to be there.

[042-040]_August 20._

[042-041]It seems the Royal party have landed at Cowes.

[042-042]_August 23._

[042-043]Went to the Cabinet room to read despatches. Lord Stuart represents the

[042-044]Government as by no means settled; anxious to remain at peace, and to

[042-045]prevent revolution, but not secure. Things which are essential the new King

[042-046]is obliged to ask humbly of La Fayette, who is now really Sovereign.

[042-047]La Fayette wanted to dissolve the Chamber. The King rightly thought that to

[042-048]do so now would be to make a Convention. [Footnote: I.e. as in 1792.]

[042-049]Some persons are gone off to bring Napoleon II., but the Austrians will

[042-050]stop them on the way.

[042-051]The Prussians on the first intelligence of the events at Paris sent orders

[042-052]to their Minister to come away, but he was directed not to do so without

[042-053]concert with his colleagues. They met, and agreed to recommend him to stay.

[042-054]The disposition of Metternich and Nesselrode, who met at Toplitz or

[042-055]Carlsbad, I forget which, was the same and reasonable--to leave France to

[042-056]settle her own affairs quietly, and only to interfere if she invaded the

[042-057]peace of other States.

[042-058]The Duke has left a memorandum on the Cabinet table showing clearly from

[042-059]treaties that this is not a case in which we are bound to interfere. We

[042-060]engaged to support a constitutional monarch against revolutionary

[042-061]movements, but the monarch having violated the constitution has broken the

[042-062]condition. France may still form a part of the Congress of Europe, in

[042-063]'Union or _Pacific Concert_,' with the four great Powers. The treaty of

[042-064]offensive alliance between those Powers is dormant, while France remains

[042-065]under a constitutional King.

[042-066]The Duke properly thinks that the sooner, after having taken a decent time

[042-067]for deliberation, we can recognise the Duke of Orleans, the better for him

[042-068]and for us.

[042-069]He expects at no distant period war, as the consequence of these events,

[042-070]and I fear he may be right. It will arise by the imitation of the Spaniards

[042-071]and Portuguese, and the ambitious sympathy of the French.

[042-072]It is evident that Russia means to indulge France with Algiers.

[042-073]_August 23._

[042-074]Received a letter from the Duke respecting Rajpootana. He thinks the

[042-075]cession of territory will only lead to new demands on our part, and advises

[042-076]that, unless it should be necessary to give some instruction, the letter

[042-077]should not be sent. He thinks, too, that as no brevet has been given to

[042-078]King's officers in Ava, none can be given to those of the Company. I am to

[042-079]see him tomorrow upon these points.

[042-080]Cabinet at 3. Showed Herries the answer I proposed sending, respecting the

[042-081]Interest Bills, of which he entirely approved.

[042-082]Peel was not at the Cabinet.

[042-083]Read the Duke of Orleans' letter to the King, which is proper. He says he

[042-084]laments and wishes he could have prevented the fall of the eldest branch of

[042-085]his family. He _devoted_ himself to prevent misfortunes which would have

[042-086]endangered the peace of Europe. He avows pacific intentions.

[042-087]The King is to receive General Baudrand, who brought the letter in the

[042-088]Levee, which will be before the Council on Wednesday.

[042-089]The King of the French will be acknowledged. A letter will be written to

[042-090]our Ministers with the great powers stating our reasons for doing so. This

[042-091]will be read to the Foreign Ministers here.

[042-092]I suggested that it might be as well to make the letter substantially the

[042-093]Duke's Memorandum, and particularly to remind France that the Quadruple

[042-094]Alliance still existed. We shall have the drafts of the letter tomorrow.

[042-095]Parliament to be prorogued to October 26.

[042-096]To-morrow the Brazilians will acknowledge Miguel as the Regent, if he will

[042-097]marry Maria da Gloria. Then came some absurd conditions. However, the thing

[042-098]is to be considered to-morrow. Aberdeen's idea is that there is no doing

[042-099]anything with Don Pedro, and that we must acknowledge Don Miguel as soon as

[042-100]he will grant an amnesty.

[042-101]We were to have a Council on Wednesday for the prorogation. The King will

[042-102]not much like this, as he wanted to go to Ascot, but he may have it as

[042-103]early as he likes, and he ought to receive General Baudrand soon. We may

[042-104]have the Council at 10, and he may be at Ascot in excellent time.

[042-105]_August 24._

[042-106]The Council is at 1. At 1 I went to the Duke. Told him of my recent letters

[042-107]to the Chairs. He said we must not make bankrupts of the Company, if we

[042-108]would use them hereafter. I said it was my duty to state the case of the

[042-109]public, as the Board were guardians of the territorial revenue.

[042-110]A letter from Count Moltke, requesting to see me. I have appointed to-

[042-111]morrow at 3.

[042-112]Cabinet at 3. Aberdeen read the proposed letter from the King to King Louis

[042-113]Philippe. With a few trifling alterations it was adopted.

[042-114]The Duke called on Marmont to-day, and received from him a military account

[042-115]of the affair at Paris. Marmont said he knew nothing of the Ordonnances,

[042-116]and disapproved of them. He was at the King's levée on the Tuesday, and was

[042-117]told there were _quelques inquiétudes_ at Paris, and to take the command of

[042-118]the troops. He found only 7,000 men. Polignac, forgetting any were _en

[042-119]congé_, thought there were 12,000. He occupied the Places de l'Hôtel de

[042-120]Ville, de la Bastille, de Victoire, and de Vendôme in sufficient force. His

[042-121]troops were not attacked. He withdrew them at night, and reoccupied the

[042-122]Posts in the morning. Then the attack began. The troops maintained

[042-123]themselves, but he found it necessary to withdraw them to the Louvre, the

[042-124]Tuileries, the Pont Neuf, and the Place de Vendôme. In the Louvre he had

[042-125]two battalions of Swiss; two battalions of the Line in the Place de

[042-126]Vendôme; the Guards in the Tuileries. He kept open his communication with

[042-127]the country by posts at all the avenues leading to the garden of the

[042-128]Tuileries and the Bois de Boulogne, Champs Elysées, &c. The battalion at

[042-129]the Place de la Bastille could not retreat by the straight road, and was

[042-130]obliged to march all round Paris, crossing the river at the bridge nearest

[042-131]Charenton, and coming to the Tuileries by the Faubourg.

[042-132]The two battalions in the Place de Vendôme went over to the people. He then

[042-133]sent one battalion from the Louvre to the grille of the Tuileries garden,

[042-134]opposite the Rue de Rivoli, and so protected his flank. On Thursday he had

[042-135]lost 1,800 men, killed and wounded; and 1,200 _égarés_--besides the two

[042-136]battalions; but he had received a reinforcement of 3,000 men. The troops

[042-137]were _extenués de fatigue_. When Lafitte and the others came to him he told

[042-138]him he could not order the fire to cease. He was attacked.

[042-139]If the fire of the people ceased, his troops would not fire. He fairly told

[042-140]the King it was not _une commotion_, nor even _une insurrection_, but _une

[042-141]Révolution_. There were not above thirty or forty people behind the

[042-142]barriers, but all the windows were occupied by armed men. He counselled

[042-143]concession, but Polignac would not hear of it. He said Polignac was

[042-144]_l'homme le plus présomptueux_ he had ever seen.

[042-145]When the Louvre was attacked the Swiss ran out towards the Tuileries and

[042-146]carried with them a battalion he had in the Place de Carrousel, as well as

[042-147]two guns he had with him. The rush was such he could hardly get upon his

[042-148]horse, and the men ran so fast that a person he sent after them on

[042-149]horseback found them almost at the extremity of the Tuileries garden.

[042-150]However, some returned to protect the retreat of about sixty men whom he

[042-151]had got together to defend the grille at the Arc de Triomphe in the Place

[042-152]de Carrousel. They were just enabled to retreat.

[042-153]Marmont is violent against the Swiss, who were, he says, retained in the

[042-154]French service by higher pay and privileges for _this very thing_, and yet

[042-155]they ran away in this shameful manner.

[042-156]Marmont means to go to Italy for a year. After that he hopes he can return

[042-157]to France. He has no wish to emigrate.

[042-158]If the account in Lord Stuart's report be correct, France is in a

[042-159]deplorable state. In many parts of the country no taxes are paid, and the

[042-160]Republican party has not lost hope.

[042-161]The conditions of what Don Pedro considers a conciliatory arrangement are

[042-162]entirely inadmissible. They are founded upon the marriage of Donna Maria da

[042-163]Gloria, and England, France, and Austria are to guarantee her against any

[042-164]_injure_ she may receive from her husband. Certainly we may safely say

[042-165]these terms are inadmissible, and so break off all negotiations with Don

[042-166]Pedro, who, since these terms were proposed by him, has recognised the

[042-167]independent Regency of Terceira. By-the-bye, one of his terms is the

[042-168]payment, by Portugal, of all the expenses incurred by himself for Donna

[042-169]Maria.

[042-170]It seems the draft of a decree of amnesty has been sent to Lisbon, and if

[042-171]Miguel will pass that decree we are to recognise him.

[042-172]The Chancellor and others seemed to think this was an awkward time, and we

[042-173]had better wait a little. I think so too. However, undoubtedly our early

[042-174]recognition of Miguel might lead to the prevention of a Portuguese

[042-175]Revolution.

[042-176]There was much conversation respecting the Bank Charter. It seemed to be

[042-177]the general opinion that Government should take it upon itself to arrange

[042-178]terms with the Bank, which terms will be prohibition to any other Bank to

[042-179]issue notes within twenty-five miles of London. This being granted, the

[042-180]Bank will do the public business for 100,000£ a year less. The whole

[042-181]question of country banking, whether it is to be with limited or unlimited

[042-182]responsibility, a limited or an unlimited number of partners, is to be left

[042-183]open to Parliament.
