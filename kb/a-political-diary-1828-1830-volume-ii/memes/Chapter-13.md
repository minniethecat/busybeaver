[013-001]We must recollect that Turkey had bound herself to acquiesce in the

[013-002]decision of the Conference upon the Greek Treaty--that is, to defer to our

[013-003]mediation. Could we, as mediators, propose to Turkey to cede Attica,

[013-004]Negropont, and other possessions she now holds? and would we willingly

[013-005]bring the frontiers of the Greek state into contact with our Ionian

[013-006]Islands?

[013-007]If Greece were to have a sovereign, Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg would be

[013-008]the best man for us--Austria would prefer him. France admitted that the

[013-009]wishes of Austria ought to be consulted.

[013-010]France, however, rather wished for Prince Charles of Bavaria. Russia for a

[013-011]Duke of Saxe-Weimar.

[013-012]Aberdeen seemed to think there would be no great difficulty in carrying our

[013-013]point, and having Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg.

[013-014]Peel said he thought we could not allow a treaty such as that signed by

[013-015]Turkey to pass without a remonstrance on our part. We referred to a letter

[013-016]of Dudley's, and to Aberdeen's recent instruction to Lord Heytesbury, and

[013-017]likewise to the various declarations of moderation put forth by the Emperor

[013-018]Nicholas. Several ways were started of expressing our opinion--a sort of

[013-019]circular to the Powers which signed the Treaty of the Congress--a

[013-020]declaration to Parliament.

[013-021]The Duke suggested a remonstrance to the Emperor Nicholas to be

[013-022]communicated in the first instance only to Russia.

[013-023]This seems likely to be adopted, but we are to have another Cabinet to-

[013-024]morrow.

[013-025]In whatever we do we must endeavour to keep Austria out of the scrape, for

[013-026]there is nothing the Russians would like so much as the opportunity of

[013-027]marching to Vienna.

[013-028]Not only it would be romantic for us alone to go to war to maintain the

[013-029]balance of power, but it would, in this case, be absurd indeed, for, if our

[013-030]armies had driven the Russians out of Turkey, we could not reconstitute the

[013-031]Turkish Empire. It is dissolved in its own weakness.

[013-032]Great dissatisfaction was expressed, and justly, at the conduct of Lord

[013-033]Heytesbury, who has been humbugged by the Russians all along.

[013-034]The King has run up a bill of 4,000£ for clothes in six months. All the

[013-035]offices of the Household, except the Chamberlain's, which has 1,900£ in

[013-036]hand, are falling into arrear, and if there should be an arrear upon the

[013-037]whole civil list, it must come before Parliament.

[013-038]Fitzgerald gives a very bad account of trade generally.

[013-039]The King does not like us better than he did, and the Duke of Cumberland

[013-040]means to keep his son in England, and educate him here, taking the 6,000£ a

[013-041]year. He wants to drive the Government to make him Viceroy of Hanover.

[013-042]The Cabinet dined with the Duke.

[013-043]_October 8._

[013-044]Cabinet at 3. A great deal of conversation of which the result was that a

[013-045]remonstrance should be made to Russia on the subject of the terms of the

[013-046]peace. This remonstrance will temperately but strongly, more by statement

[013-047]of facts than by observations, show that the peace is not such as the

[013-048]Emperor had given us reason to expect he would require, and that it in

[013-049]reality threatens the existence of the Turkish Empire; that the destruction

[013-050]of that Empire would seriously affect the peace of Europe by changing the

[013-051]relative position of the several States.

[013-052]Aberdeen wants a guarantee of the territorial possessions of Turkey, not of

[013-053]its Government. [Footnote: It is observable that this guarantee seems to

[013-054]have said nothing of the internal system of government, and so far to have

[013-055]been unconditional. It would therefore have gone considerably beyond the

[013-056]Anglo-Turkish Convention of 1878. It would also have applied to Europe as

[013-057]well as Asia. It is a commentary on the statement of Mr. Gladstone, in

[013-058]later days a colleague of Lord Aberdeen, that no statesman whom he had

[013-059]known in former times would ever have listened to the idea of such an

[013-060]engagement.] I think no one seems much inclined to agree with him. Such a

[013-061]guarantee would impose obligations without conferring rights upon us. It

[013-062]would be a guarantee which would give rise to infinite complications, and

[013-063]which would embarrass us very much.

[013-064]Without a guarantee we may succeed in bringing the great States to an

[013-065]understanding that the distribution of the Turkish territories, in the

[013-066]event of the falling to pieces of that State, must be a subject for the

[013-067]decision of a Congress.

[013-068]Austria has expressed herself very frankly. She is ready to do anything.

[013-069]She sees the danger and desires to know our view of it. The real view of

[013-070]France does not seem to be very different; but there is no dependence to be

[013-071]placed upon a Government trembling for its life. Prussia will be satisfied

[013-072]with the peace. Her sovereign is very weak, and the Prussians think their

[013-073]interest is served by the progress of Russia in a direction contrary to

[013-074]them, and in which she menaces Austria.

[013-075]The smuggling case is said to tell against Lord Stuart. He writes

[013-076]unintelligibly, and the French will not trust him--so I shall not be sorry

[013-077]if we can get rid of him.

[013-078]With Lord Heytesbury we are all dissatisfied, and have been from the

[013-079]beginning. There is a Council on Monday, and we have a Cabinet on Sunday at

[013-080]3, when we are to hear Aberdeen's letter, and may probably have the Treaty.

[013-081]There seems a determination to effect an armistice by force if the

[013-082]Conference will not order it in Greece.

[013-083]We have nine good ships there. The Russians seven bad ones, and the French

[013-084]two.

[013-085]Before the Conference can proceed the 10th Article of the Treaty of Peace

[013-086]must be declared _non avenu_--that which obliges the Porte to accept the

[013-087]Protocol of March 22--all negotiation upon that Protocol having been

[013-088]committed by Russia to the French and English Ambassadors, and it having

[013-089]been expressly reserved to the Porte by us, that her objections should be

[013-090]fairly weighed.

[013-091]The French have taken advantage of the peace to order their troops home

[013-092]from the Morea.

[013-093]_October 9._

[013-094]Read many of the Protocols of the early Conferences after the Russian,

[013-095]declaration of war. I shall to-morrow read these again carefully and sketch

[013-096]_my_ State paper.

[013-097]If I was in opposition I should describe the details relative to the

[013-098]Principalities, as showing the moderation of the thief who would stipulate

[013-099]that men should sleep with their doors open, till they have ransomed

[013-100]themselves by paying their uttermost farthing.

[013-101]_October 10._

[013-102]Received a letter from Sir J. Malcolm. He seems pleased with the secret

[013-103]dispatches relative to Persia and the Pacha of Bagdad. He seems upon the

[013-104]whole very much gratified, and very grateful.

[013-105]He strongly presses the appointment of an Indian as his successor, and

[013-106]mentions Sir Ch. Metcalfe and Jenkins. He likewise mentions a Mr. Chaplin,

[013-107]of whom I never heard. I take Jenkins to be a cleverer man than Sir Ch.

[013-108]Metcalfe, [Footnote: Afterwards Lord Metcalfe.] who rather disappoints me.

[013-109]Had three letters by Petersburg from Colonel McDonald, the last dated in

[013-110]August. The Persians, thoroughly alarmed, are doing all they can to satisfy

[013-111]the Emperor Nicholas by punishing the persons engaged in the massacre of

[013-112]the Russian mission; but they had an insurrection to quell on banishing the

[013-113]High Priest, who was at the head of all. As they conclude all the bad

[013-114]characters had a hand in it they mean to take the opportunity of punishing

[013-115]them. Paskewitz is said to have from 20,000 to 22,000 men--to have

[013-116]sustained no loss in the late engagements, but to suffer from the plague.

[013-117]At Erzeroum the Mahometans are not only satisfied, but well pleased. The

[013-118]Government of a Russian general is better than that of a Turkish Pasha.

[013-119]The Prince Abbas Mirza is at last doing something towards making an army.

[013-120]Major Hart, alone, however, keeps it together. The troops are as yet ill-

[013-121]armed, but they have their pay. McDonald thinks the King not likely to live

[013-122]long. He wants a cypher.

[013-123]_October 11, Sunday._

[013-124]Came up from Worthing to a Cabinet. Before we met read the last letters

[013-125]from Lord Heytesbury, which show a degree of infatuation respecting the

[013-126]Russians, which is quite wonderful.

[013-127]Before we began to talk Rothschild called out the Duke of Wellington, and

[013-128]offered at once all the money to pay the Russian Indemnity. He said he only

[013-129]wanted the guarantee of England!

[013-130]If the Russians remained in the Principalities there would be a general

[013-131]war.

[013-132]Irvine, an English loan jobber, saw the Duke yesterday with the same offer.

[013-133]The joke is that Rothschild is to pay the money for the Turks, and to be

[013-134]made King of Jerusalem.

[013-135]Aberdeen began by begging we would first settle the Greek question. He

[013-136]brought a paper the Russians were willing to deliver in containing a sort

[013-137]of apology for the 10th Article, and declaring that it by no means

[013-138]interfered with the powers of the Conference. We took a great deal of time

[013-139]in considering whether we should not suggest some alteration in this

[013-140]paper--some is to be proposed--not very essential.

[013-141]We had a long discussion as to the name of the new State. At last it seemed

[013-142]to be thought 'Sovereign Prince of Greece' was the best. Aberdeen thinks

[013-143]he shall have little difficulty about the Prince. The Russians agree to the

[013-144]description given; but I dare say they imagine we mean to describe a

[013-145]different man. I suspect they think we want to give them Leopold.

[013-146]Aberdeen read a letter he proposed sending to Lord Stuart, the purport of

[013-147]which was that we wanted to know what he meant to do towards redeeming

[013-148]France from the responsibility she had incurred and made us incur by giving

[013-149]instructions to Count Guilleminot, stating the terms of peace and the

[013-150]moderation of the Emperor--instructions which misled our Ambassador, and

[013-151]induced the two Ambassadors to give assurances to the Porte which events

[013-152]proved to be unfounded.

[013-153]The letter, I think, likewise desired him to enquire in what form our joint

[013-154]representations as to the amount of the indemnity were to be made. To these

[013-155]the Ambassadors have pledged the two Cabinets.

[013-156]There was a great deal more in the letter which is to be left out. It went

[013-157]into the details of the treaty, or rather of its effects.

[013-158]The offer is to be made to the Turks of an independent Greece, from the

[013-159]Gulf of Volo to Missolonghi, or of a Greece under Suzeraineté, with

[013-160]Negropont, and the line from Volo to the Gulf of Arta.

[013-161]I think we are all agreed that at the commencement of the war it was our

[013-162]interest to take as little as possible from Turkey--that now it is our

[013-163]interest to make Greece a substantive State, which may hereafter receive

[013-164]the _débris_ of the Ottoman Empire. [Footnote: This may explain the

[013-165]apparently illiberal views of many of the Cabinet as to the Greek

[013-166]boundaries. They saw the difficulty of any halting place outside the

[013-167]Isthmus of Corinth, short of a wider boundary even than that ultimately

[013-168]adopted.]

[013-169]As to the really important matter, the remonstrance to Russia, nothing was

[013-170]done. Nothing is, I conclude, written, and Aberdeen does not like Cabinet

[013-171]criticism, nor do I think the Cabinet at all agreed as to what should be

[013-172]said. Dudley's letters used to occupy us for days, and certainly they were

[013-173]the better for it--although we lost a good deal of time occasionally.

[013-174]Aberdeen said he would send it to me. I think I shall write an _esquisse_

[013-175]myself. We are to have no more Cabinets for some time. The Chancellor

[013-176]wishes to have the remaining fortnight of his holidays uninterrupted.

[013-177]_October 12._

[013-178]Went to town at quarter-past one. To the Foreign Office. The treaty arrived

[013-179]last night. Lord Aberdeen took it with him to Windsor. It differs

[013-180]materially from the _projet_. The Articles respecting indemnity are

[013-181]_relégués_ to a separate transaction. The payment of 100,000 ducats is to

[013-182]lead to the evacuation of Adrianople; 400,000 form the next payment, then

[013-183]500,000, and 500,000, making the sum originally demanded for individual

[013-184]losses; but, as I understand Mr. Backhouse, eighteen months must elapse

[013-185]before Turkey can be evacuated to the Danube. I had much conversation with

[013-186]him as to other points. On looking into the Act of the Congress I find the

[013-187]Powers adhering to it may be considered as binding themselves not to

[013-188]_disturb_ the territorial arrangements that Act establishes; but they are

[013-189]not bound to _maintain_ them. Thus if France appropriated to herself Spain,

[013-190]she would violate the treaty, but no Power signing the treaty would be

[013-191]obliged, by virtue of that Act, to make war upon France for doing so.
