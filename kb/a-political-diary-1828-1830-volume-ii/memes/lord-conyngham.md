**Henry Conyngham, 1st Marquess Conyngham** (1766–1832) was an Anglo-Irish peer and courtier. He served as Lord Chamberlain in the royal household of [[George IV]] and later [[William IV]], and his wife, Lady Conyngham, was the last mistress of George IV. The diary of [[Lord Ellenborough]] documents Conyngham's role at court and the political tensions surrounding his position.

== Court Position ==

The Conyngham family occupied a central place at court. Lady Conyngham's illness was a matter of significant concern, with the diary noting that there was "no idea of the Court" moving while she was unwell. (016-104) Conyngham himself was a figure of political controversy: [[Henry Brougham]] made a violent speech against him in Parliament for failing to attend to his duties. (034-167)

== Political Controversy ==

Conyngham's position was precarious. The diary records an expectation that he would resign, though the outcome was uncertain. (037-041) A foreign contact warned Conyngham "he must expect a storm," suggesting his court influence attracted hostility. (021-005) Upon the death of George IV, Conyngham's role as Lord Chamberlain ended symbolically when he broke his staff of office and placed it into the King's grave. (038-085)
