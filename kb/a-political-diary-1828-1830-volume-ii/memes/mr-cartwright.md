**Mr. Cartwright** was a British diplomat who served as consul at Constantinople and later played a role in the events surrounding the [[Belgian Revolution]] of 1830. His reports from the field provided the British government with crucial intelligence during a period of international upheaval.

== Diplomatic Service at Constantinople ==

Cartwright's earliest appearance in the diary is in 1828, when the diarist "Read a letter from Mr. Cartwright, the Consul at Constantinople." (010-126) This places him at the heart of the Eastern Question, reporting on Ottoman affairs during a critical period that included the Russo-Turkish War. His despatches continued to arrive at the Foreign Office: "Mr. Cartwright's reports are come. He describes…" (046-042)

== The Belgian Crisis ==

By 1830, Cartwright had been reassigned to Brussels, where he witnessed the Belgian Revolution at close quarters. The diary records his arrival under dramatic circumstances: "Mr. Cartwright arrived at the office; but a…" (045-186) This interrupted arrival hints at the disorder surrounding the Belgian uprising. The diarist later noted that "Cartwright came away, and is nearly dest…" (050-125), suggesting the consul had been forced to flee his post as the situation deteriorated.

== Official Communications ==

Cartwright maintained a regular correspondence with the British government. A letter is recorded as coming "from Mr. Cartwright. When Sir Ch. Bagot wrote last…" (045-128) His despatches are mentioned alongside those of other key diplomats. During the Belgian crisis, the diarist recorded that a foreign envoy "sends for Cartwright. He seems much embarrassed." (049-113) This suggests Cartwright was an important source of information during a confused and rapidly changing situation.
