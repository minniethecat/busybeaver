[015-001]I think it is clear that the invasion of India could not be attempted till

[015-002]the third year; but when should we begin to take precautions? A Government

[015-003]wholly Asiatic would not be still if the Russians took possession of China;

[015-004]but ours, chained by European politics, would hardly move if they entered

[015-005]Cabul.

[015-006]We ought to have full information as to Cabul, Bokhara, and China.

[015-007]My letter of last year directed the attaining of information; but I dare

[015-008]say nothing has been done.

[015-009]_October 30._

[015-010]Received a Memorial from Mr. Fullerton, asking some remuneration beyond his

[015-011]salary for past services. He has a claim _if we were rich_. I think he

[015-012]should have 10,000 dollars. I dare say he thinks 20,000. Thoughtless

[015-013]extravagance is the destruction of generosity and even of justice.

[015-014]Upon the subject of the invasion of India my idea is that the thing is not

[015-015]only practicable, but easy, unless we determine to act as an Asiatic Power.

[015-016]On the acquisition of Khiva by the Russians we should occupy Lahore and

[015-017]Cabul.[Footnote: It may be remembered that Lord Ellenborough strongly

[015-018]disapproved of any occupation of Afghanistan, or interference with its

[015-019]internal affairs, in 1840-42. At that time Russia had not advanced to

[015-020]Khiva. It is clear that he would not have held the same opinion as to our

[015-021]policy towards Afghanistan after the events of 1873-74.] It is not on the

[015-022]Indus that an enemy is to be met. If we do not meet him in Cabul, at the

[015-023]foot of the Hindoo Koosh, or in its passes, we had better remain in the

[015-024]Sutlege. If the Russians once occupy Cabul they may remain there with the

[015-025]Indus in their front, till they have organised insurrection in our rear,

[015-026]and completely equipped their army. I fear there are passes from Balkh upon

[015-027]Peshawur. If these could be closed and the enemy poured upon Cabul we

[015-028]should know where to meet him. Now we, being at Cabul, might be cut off

[015-029]from its resources by the descent of the enemy upon Peshawur.

[015-030]There is some road from Roondorg through Cashmere, but I do not fear that.

[015-031]The road an enemy would choose would be that by the Valley of the Cachgu.

[015-032]We know nothing of these passes, nothing of the country beyond them,

[015-033]nothing of the course of the Indus--but we should have full information so

[015-034]as to be able to crush an advancing enemy, by making the whole country

[015-035]hostile, which money would do.

[015-065]The out provisional battalions, local corps, &c., of 198,000, I do not

[015-066]think above 100,000 could possibly be disposable, and there would not be

[015-067]70,000 effectives. The Artillery must be very numerous. I omitted the

[015-068]Company's English Regiments, about 3,000 men.

[015-069]Of all nations the Russians are the least adapted for an enterprise of this

[015-070]nature. They have neither medical staff nor commissariat, and the men are

[015-071]without resource. A French army would be the best. I doubt the possibility

[015-072]of Russia bringing more than 20,000 men to Cabul, and these could not

[015-073]descend the mountains till the third year, if Cabul was occupied. What I

[015-074]fear is an occupation of Khiva unknown to us. No preparation on our part--

[015-075]no marching forward--so that in three or four months from leaving Khiva the

[015-076]enemy might be at Cabul. I am sure we can defeat the enterprise. We ought

[015-077]to defeat it before the enemy reaches the Indus. If 20,000 Russians should

[015-078]reach the Indus, it will be a sharp fight.

[015-079]_November 1, 1829._

[015-080]A letter from the Duke. He returned the papers I sent him. He has doubts as

[015-081]to the expediency of making the Commissary-General of Stores I proposed;

[015-082]but he seems to have supposed I wished to do away with the Military Board.

[015-083]I have explained what I meant.

[015-084]He approves of my suggestions as to correspondence, but thinks every paper

[015-085]must be sent home, and the collections formed here. I have explained that I

[015-086]always intended every paper should be sent home, and I have told him that I

[015-087]had the opinion of the clerks I consulted that the collections might be

[015-088]framed in India, with a saving of time, and without diminishing the check

[015-089]on the local Governments.

[015-090]_November 4._

[015-091]Received from Aberdeen his draft of a remonstrance to Russia, which, it

[015-092]seems, must be sent at last. He has already shown it to the Duke and Peel.

[015-093]There is no great substantive objection to it; but it is not very carefully

[015-094]written. I shall send it to him tomorrow with many proposed alterations. In

[015-095]the second box came Gaily [Footnote: H. Gaily Knight. Best known for his

[015-096]works on the Normans in Sicily, and Ecclesiastical Architecture in Italy.]

[015-097]Knight's letter to Aberdeen; which is a poor, flimsy production. A

[015-098]peacock's feather in the hilt of a Drawcansir's sword.

[015-099]_November 5._

[015-100]Altered, not only verbally, but substantially, Aberdeen's paper, and sent

[015-101]it to him.

[015-102]Cabinet room. Read a Memorandum by Lord Heytesbury, of a conversation he

[015-103]has had with the Emperor of Russia. The Emperor expects the early downfall

[015-104]of the Porte--and a Revolution in France. Asks if another march to Paris

[015-105]would be possible? Lord Heytesbury saw Nesselrode afterwards and told him

[015-106]what the Emperor had said. Nesselrode said the Emperor always saw things

[015-107]_en noir_. He had a different opinion. He did not think the Porte in

[015-108]immediate danger, nor did he expect a French Revolution.

[015-109]The other guarantees they talk of are further cessions in Asia,

[015-110]specifically Batoum, or the occupation of Varna, or Silistria, instead of

[015-111]the Principalities. The latter is worse, and the Turks will probably

[015-112]consent to neither. They do not value the Principalities, and they know

[015-113]Europe does.[Footnote: The Principalities, as commanding the lower course

[015-114]of the Danube, were all important to Austria especially. Thus, occupation

[015-115]by Russia, while it would have been felt as a menace to Central Europe,

[015-116]would have left Turkey a compact state beyond the Danube.]

[015-117]_November 6._

[015-118]Saw Aberdeen. He is always gloomy about _divisions_. He is afraid of an

[015-119]attack on Foreign Policy. He thinks the two parties will unite in that. He

[015-120]hears there has been some approximation between Lord Grey [Footnote: Lord

[015-121]Grey had been separated from the bulk of the Whig party since their

[015-122]junction with Canning in 1827.] and Lord Holland. At the same time it is

[015-123]said there is a notion of bringing in Lord Grey. I suspect this report to

[015-124]have been fabricated by the Ultra-Tories to annoy the King.

[015-125]He thinks the Duke is annoyed, more particularly at the King's not treating

[015-126]him well, and at his Government not being well supported.

[015-127]In fact, however, it is a Government which will not fall, for the King

[015-128]hates the Whigs; the people do not regard them. He may like the Tories, but

[015-129]he knows they cannot make a Government, and the Duke's Administration has

[015-130]four-fifths of the country.

[015-131]Received a letter from the Duke, telling me he had settled Colonel

[015-132]McDonald's knighthood, and asking me if I should be ready to talk about

[015-133]India on the 13th. I said about Batta certainly; about India I had rather

[015-134]talk first to Lord Melville and him.

[015-135]Wrote to the Duchess of Kent telling her a Bengal cavalry cadetship was at

[015-136]her disposal for the son of Colonel Harvey.

[015-137]There is a very interesting letter from an English officer at Adrianople

[015-138]with respect to the state of the Russian army. It has suffered and suffers

[015-139]most dreadfully.

[015-140]I told Aberdeen if I had seen the account of the conversation between Lord

[015-141]Heytesbury and the Emperor Nicholas before I read his proposed letter, I

[015-142]should have suggested that much stress should have been laid upon the

[015-143]effect the downfall of Turkey would have upon affairs in France.

[015-144]Polignac seems confident he can stand. He thinks he has the Chambers. The

[015-145]French behave ill in the settlement of the Greek business, and object

[015-146]altogether to our man, Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg. They equally object

[015-147]to Prince Frederick of Orange, and to Prince Leopold, whom Russia would

[015-148]have had willingly. I wonder Aberdeen did not laugh when he was proposed.

[015-149]They want to settle the thing without a Prince. I suppose they want a

[015-150]Frenchman.

[015-151]Aberdeen is for settling Greece as a Power into whose lap the broken parts

[015-152]of Turkey may fall. He gives up Euboea. That is, the surrender of Euboea is

[015-153]to be proposed to the Porte, with a frontier limited in other respects,

[015-154]instead of the protocol of March 22.

[015-155]The Turks who have left the Morea have no indemnity. The Turks who are in

[015-156]the other parts of the new Greece remain. It is altogether a wonderful

[015-157]business. These anti-revolutionary States combining to revolutionise a

[015-158]rebellious province of an unoffending ally!

[015-159]_November 11._

[015-160]It seems the French do not like the idea of giving to the Turks the option

[015-161]of an independent State with smaller limits, or of a State under

[015-162]Suzeraineté with extended limits, contrary to the treaty, and sending at

[015-163]the same time secret instructions to the Ambassadors to insist upon the

[015-164]_entire_ independence of the new Greek State. The French seem likewise to

[015-165]have been offended at the protocol having been settled between Russia and

[015-166]us, before they were called in to give their opinion. No wonder. Certainly

[015-167]our diplomacy has not succeeded. We have failed in all our objects.

[015-168]_November 13._

[015-169]Cabinet. I was first called upon to say my say upon the general Indian

[015-170]question. I observed that the present prospective deficiency was one

[015-171]million a year. That until we could ascertain whether that deficiency could

[015-172]be diminished or done away with we were really not ourselves prepared to

[015-173]come to a decision upon the future government of India; nor would

[015-174]Parliament endure that the China trade should be closed upon the country

[015-175]for twenty years more without first inquiring whether it was necessary. The

[015-176]first question was, 'Can we make such a reduction of expenditure, or effect

[015-177]such an increase in income as to enable the Government of India to go on

[015-178]without any assistance direct or indirect from England?' If it can, then we

[015-179]have the China trade in our hands. If it cannot, we have to decide whether

[015-180]the necessary assistance shall be found by means of a continuance of the

[015-181]monopoly or in some other manner.

[015-182]I stated the increase of two millions in six years in the civil charges of

[015-183]Bengal; that the Court had issued the strongest instructions, and the local

[015-184]Government seemed to have a real intention to curtail expenditure. That I

[015-185]had done something, and should do all I could, investigating every item.

[015-186]Peel suggested a commission. I said that had occurred to me last year. The

[015-187]Duke, however, objected to a commission as really superseding the Governor-

[015-188]General and being the Government. Another objection certainly is the delay.

[015-189]Difficulties would be thrown in its way, and we should at last be obliged

[015-190]to decide without its final report, having thrown away our time here in

[015-191]waiting for it.

[015-192]I mentioned that the character of the local Government was 'disrespect and

[015-193]disobedience.' That nothing but a long continuance of strict rule could

[015-194]bring India into real subjection. It was this disobedience which was the

[015-195]chief source of increased expenditure. It arose in a great measure from the

[015-196]unequal hand which had been held over them--the indulgence of the Court of

[015-197]Directors--and the great delays in the communication with India arising out

[015-198]of the system of correspondence. I had endeavoured to remedy that, and

[015-199]hoped to get an answer to letters within the year. It was now two years and

[015-200]a half. I had likewise endeavoured to make arrangements for steam

[015-201]communication by the Red Sea. I hoped to be able to send a letter to Bombay

[015-202]in sixty days.
