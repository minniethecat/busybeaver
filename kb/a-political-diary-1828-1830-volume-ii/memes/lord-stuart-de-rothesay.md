# Lord Stuart de Rothesay

## Overview

Charles Stuart, 1st Baron Stuart de Rothesay, served as British Ambassador to France from 1815 to 1830, making him one of the longest-serving British diplomats in Paris during the post-Napoleonic era. Throughout the period of Ellenborough's diary (1828–1830), Stuart was the principal channel of communication between the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government and the French court of Charles X, navigating the diplomatic complexities that culminated in the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]].

## Role in the Diary

Stuart's despatches from Paris were a regular feature of Cabinet business, read and discussed by Wellington and his colleagues. The diary records the diarist "reading the despatches from Lord Stuart and Lord" others, sifting intelligence from the French capital (024-141). Wellington relied on Stuart not only for formal diplomatic reporting but as a personal channel, regarding him as "a good one" for conveying sensitive messages (031-170).

The diary captures a nuanced portrait of a diplomat under strain. The diarist records that "great complaints are made of Lord Stuart, who gives little" satisfaction to his political masters — a criticism that may reflect the inherent tension between a long-serving ambassador and an activist Foreign Secretary (019-068). Wellington's approach to managing this tension was characteristically pragmatic: he sought "to put off Lord Stuart by" indirect means rather than confront him directly (012-138).

## Key Appearances

- **French political intelligence**: Stuart reported on French electoral prospects, "considering M. de la Bourdonnaye's" chances at the next elections and advising the British government on the shifting political landscape (011-120, 011-167).
- **The July Revolution context**: Cabinet discussions focused on "what should be said by Lord Stuart at Paris, respecting the late" events in France, calibrating Britain's diplomatic response to the overthrow of Charles X (040-059, 040-060).
- **Correspondence management**: Wellington "wrote to Lord Stuart to ascertain whether there is" a basis for negotiation, using the ambassador as his fact-finding instrument (021-054).
- **Personal standing**: The diary notes that a particular "case is said to tell against Lord Stuart," indicating the precarious position of even senior diplomats when political winds shifted in London (013-075).

## Diplomatic Significance

Stuart's tenure bridged the final years of the Bourbon Restoration and the revolution that brought Louis Philippe to power. His detailed despatches provided the Wellington Cabinet with essential intelligence during a period when Anglo-French relations were critical to the European balance of power. The diary confirms that Wellington personally directed Stuart's diplomatic messaging, ensuring that Britain's voice in Paris was calibrated to Cabinet decisions.
