# Duke of Wellington

## Overview

The Duke of Wellington served as Prime Minister during the period covered by Ellenborough's political diary (1828–1830). He led a Tory government that navigated the Catholic Emancipation crisis and faced mounting pressure for parliamentary reform. Ellenborough, as a member of Wellington's cabinet, recorded the Duke's decisions and political calculations in detail.

## Leadership of the Government

Wellington directed the government's strategy on multiple fronts. The Cabinet met regularly at Apsley House and elsewhere to coordinate responses. (001-006) When the King was urged to refuse receiving petitions except through Peel, the Duke wrote to the King advising accordingly. (001-010) His leadership style was pragmatic: "The Duke thinks the King will yield, and that the meeting will be a failure." (001-017)

## Catholic Emancipation

Despite his personal reservations, Wellington took up the measure of Catholic Relief. The second reading of the Catholic Relief Bill was a significant parliamentary event. The Duke was obliged to say something civil to the Whigs, but he did it "sparingly, and *contre coeur*." (001-032)

## Fall of the Ministry

Wellington's ministry fell in November 1830 after refusing to commit to parliamentary reform. Ellenborough's diary captures the growing pressure and internal divisions within the cabinet during the final months.
