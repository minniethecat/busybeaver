[012-001]Polignac seems excellently well disposed. He would act cordially with us if

[012-002]he dared. At present he is obliged to cover all he does under the

[012-003]instructions given to Guilleminot by his predecessor under a different

[012-004]state of things, before the great Russian successes. He talks of a Congress

[012-005]of the Powers interested, and of a joint declaration if Russia should not

[012-006]adhere to her promise.

[012-007]Russia may be kept to her promises by the fear of a revolutionary movement

[012-008]in France. The French Opposition desire the success of the Russians, the

[012-009]dissolution of the Turkish Empire, and the occupation of the Dardanelles by

[012-010]the Emperor Nicholas, because they know that such events would lead to a

[012-011]_sotto sopra_ in Europe, a general scramble in which they would get the

[012-012]Rhine as their boundary. Generally, I have no doubt, young France wishes

[012-013]for confusion.

[012-014]Austria is alarmed and would do nothing. The Prussians hold that the

[012-015]existence of the Ottoman Empire is not essential to the balance of power

[012-016](that is, some of them do), and they would be glad to see Austria and

[012-017]Russia divide Turkey, Prussia having her compensation in Germany. However,

[012-018]Muffling, going rather beyond his instructions, has been made to do good.

[012-019]I think all things tend to the preservation of peace if there should be no

[012-020]explosion at Constantinople or in France. The Ottoman Empire seems,

[012-021]however, to be falling to pieces. The Government has been so oppressive

[012-022]that the people will not fight for it. The Sultan has but 4,000 troops, and

[012-023]it is said the appearance of 10,000 Russians would lead to the capture of

[012-024]Constantinople.

[012-025]Diebitch seems to dread the catastrophe which might ensue, and the

[012-026]ambassadors have placed before him in strong terms the fatal consequences

[012-027]of an explosion at Constantinople.

[012-028]I must say R. Gordon has done ably and well.

[012-029]The rascally Russians have been intriguing with our Ionian subjects, and

[012-030]Aberdeen has written a very strong letter to Lord Heytesbury on the

[012-031]subject.

[012-032]Polignac, desirous as he is of withdrawing the French troops altogether

[012-033]from the Morea, is at present afraid of doing so.

[012-034]Aberdeen told me things were not going on well here. The King has quite

[012-035]lost the sight of one eye, and the sight of the other is indistinct. It

[012-036]gives him pain, too, and the fear of blindness makes him nervous. The Duke

[012-037]of Cumberland is always about him, as mischievous as ever, but pretending

[012-038]not to be hostile.

[012-039]The Duke of Wellington gives the King up as a bad job. He sees him very

[012-040]seldom. At first he liked seeing him and setting things to rights; but he

[012-041]says he found what he did one day was undone the next, and he is in

[012-042]despair. The King has no constancy. There is no depending upon him from one

[012-043]day to another.

[012-044]Aberdeen says the accession of Rosslyn has not produced the effect we

[012-045]anticipated--that Lord Grey is very hostile. What we shall do for a

[012-046]majority next session I know not, but I think we shall stand, [Footnote:

[012-047]This might have been but for the events on the Continent in the year

[012-048]following, which formed a new starting-point in the politics of a large

[012-049]part of Europe.] although we shall not, I fear, be a strong Government. The

[012-050]Catholic Relief Bill has destroyed our unity and the spirit of party. It

[012-051]has likewise destroyed that of the Opposition, who have no longer any

[012-052]rallying point. Thus the formation of a strong Government is difficult. The

[012-053]Brunswickers cannot form one, and the King cannot be persuaded to make one

[012-054]out of the Opposition. Indeed, that the Duke of Cumberland would never

[012-055]advise. The Brunswickers will endeavour to make terms with us as a body--to

[012-056]make martyrs of some of the old Protestants, particularly of the Duke and

[012-057]Peel, and placing themselves at the head to go on as well as they could

[012-058]with the rest of us. This will not do.

[012-059]_September 26._

[012-060]The Chairs, or rather the Court, somewhat impertinently object to the

[012-061]addition I made to a recent draft, recommending an enquiry by practical and

[012-062]scientific men as to the powers India may possess of producing many

[012-063]articles of stores now sent from England. They say this is liable to

[012-064]misconstruction, and then misconstrue it themselves. They suppose these

[012-065]practical men, not being servants of the Company, to sit in judgment upon

[012-066]the proceedings of the military Board. I have corrected their intentional

[012-067]misconstruction, and have acquiesced in the substitution of a draft they

[012-068]propose to send instead, which will, I hope, practically effect my object,

[012-069]and therefore I have said we are willing our object should be attained in

[012-070]the manner most agreeable to the Court of Directors.

[012-071]It is very lucky I had just sent them my letter about stores. It will

[012-072]appear to be written subsequently to theirs. They think to humbug and to

[012-073]bully me. They will find both difficult.

[012-074]_September 30._

[012-075]Read the collection respecting the health of the King's troops. It is

[012-076]incredible to me that so many things should remain to be done--nothing

[012-077]seems to have been done that ought to have been done. I fear our finances

[012-078]make the building of new barracks impossible at present. We could not build

[012-079]proper barracks for all the European troops in India much under a million.

[012-080]Still much may be done for their health.

[012-081]_October 5._

[012-082]Arrived in London at 3. To the Cabinet room, where I found Lord Bathurst,

[012-083]come up to town for Seymour Bathurst's [Footnote: Hon. Seymour Bathurst,

[012-084]fourth son of third Earl Bathurst, married October 6, 1829, Julia, daughter

[012-085]of John Peter Hankey, Esq.] marriage, and afterwards Fitzgerald came in.

[012-086]Fitzgerald was a fortnight in Ireland, and gives a bad account of it.

[012-087]A letter from Metternich says peace was actually signed. Sir E. Gordon's

[012-088]despatches give every reason to expect it soon would be. The peace cannot

[012-089]last. I am inclined to think it would have been better for the Russians to

[012-090]have occupied Constantinople, and for the Ottoman Empire to have been

[012-091]overthrown that we might have known at once where we were, than to have had

[012-092]such a peace as this. It is practically present occupation (for a year) of

[012-093]_more_ than they now hold, for they are to have the fortresses ceded to

[012-094]them. They exact 750,000£ for the pretended losses of their merchants, and

[012-095]five millions for themselves. The indemnity to the merchants to be paid by

[012-096]three instalments. On the payment of the first, Adrianople and a few places

[012-097]on the coast to be given up. On the payment of the second everything to the

[012-098]Balkan, and on the third Bulgaria. These payments occupy a year.

[012-099]The five millions are to be paid in ten years, or sooner if the Turks can

[012-100]manage it. The Principalities to be occupied till the payment. The Turks to

[012-101]confirm the Government established during the ten years, and not to impose

[012-102]any taxes for two years more.

[012-103]All the fortresses on the left bank to be destroyed. None of the islands to

[012-104]belong to Turkey. No Turk to enter the principalities. The princes to be

[012-105]for life. All payments _in kind_ from the Principalities to cease, and

[012-106]instead the Turks and the princes to _agree upon a compensation_! It is

[012-107]unnecessary to go through the other articles relative to the

[012-108]Principalities. The treaty contains a real cession of them to Russia.

[012-109]The terms as to the navigation of merchantmen, their not being searched in

[012-110]a Turkish port, the refusal of acquiescence in the demands of the Russian

[012-111]Minister where any injury is pretended to have been done to a Russian, to

[012-112]be _just ground for reprisal_, &c., are of a nature intolerable to an

[012-113]independent Power, and not to be carried into execution.

[012-114]On the side of Asia everything is ceded that can enable Russia to attack

[012-115]either Turkey or Persia with advantage.

[012-116]The terms imposed with regard to indemnities are extravagant and altogether

[012-117]contrary to all the Emperor's promises. He has not deceived us; but he has

[012-118]lied to us most foully. Sir R. Gordon seems to have done all that could be

[012-119]done. Perhaps he has saved Constantinople from conflagration, and the

[012-120]Empire from dissolution. He has managed to settle the Greek question,

[012-121]Turkey consenting to everything the allies may determine under the protocol

[012-122]of March 22. Sir R. Gordon has taken upon himself to order up the English

[012-123]ships, and Guilleminot has ordered up the French ships, but they were still

[012-124]at Smyrna when the dispatch came away. These ships, it is hoped, may be

[012-125]some check on the Russians, and ostensibly they only go up to

[012-126]Constantinople to save Christians. However, if the Russians advance they

[012-127]will probably lead the Turks to fight. Gordon and Guilleminot have very

[012-128]properly told the Sultan they will remain by him in any case.

[012-129]The Turks declare the terms are, as regards payment, such as they have

[012-130]really no means of complying with. The allies will make representations to

[012-131]Petersburg to obtain a relaxation of these conditions.

[012-132]In the meantime, while this was doing at Constantinople, Lord Heytesbury

[012-133]was asking Nesselrode what the terms he intended to propose were, and

[012-134]Nesselrode would not tell him. Lord Heytesbury's despatch and Gordon's are

[012-135]both dated on September 10. The 12th was to be the day of signature. Lord

[012-136]Stuart by Aberdeen's directions has been pressing Polignac very hard to

[012-137]withdraw the French troops from the Morea, and Polignac has been obliged to

[012-138]plead the weakness of his Government, and to put off Lord Stuart by

[012-139]referring it to the Conference. I should say from what the papers show of

[012-140]Polignac that he will not stand. I do not know what his antagonists may be,

[012-141]but he is evidently not a powerful man.

[012-142]A Liberal told Fitzgerald their object was now in France to make the King

[012-143]of the Netherlands King of France, and give Holland to Prussia, taking

[012-144]Belgium and everything to the Rhine to themselves.

[012-145]I should say things looked ill everywhere, and unless we can make the

[012-146]Emperor of Russia fear a convulsion in France, and determine to recede from

[012-147]some of his stipulations with Turkey to satisfy the rest of Europe, we

[012-148]shall have war, and war under the most unfavourable circumstances--that is,

[012-149]if Austria be not as pusillanimous as she may be weak, for she ought never

[012-150]to consent to the establishment of the Russians on the Danube.

[012-151]The only line for the Turks to pursue is to promise everything; to

[012-152]endeavour to perform everything, and to withdraw to Asia, leaving the rest

[012-153]of Europe to settle who shall have Constantinople. _Now_ they could not do

[012-154]that, as they are too weak; but six months hence they may.

[012-155]We dine with the Duke on Wednesday--and shall then, I suppose, determine

[012-156]what we are to do.

[012-157]_October 7._

[012-158]Cabinet at 3. All present except Lord Melville.

[012-159]Aberdeen read a paper he had written before the peace was known, the object

[012-160]of which was to show that the Ottoman Empire was dissolved, and that it

[012-161]could not be reconstituted; that our views with regard to Greece should now

[012-162]change with circumstances, and that we should endeavour to make it a

[012-163]substantive state. To Turkey it could no longer signify whether Greece had

[012-164]a more extended or more limited line of frontier, and our desire should be

[012-165]to place a fit man upon the throne. France is willing to propose in the

[012-166]Conference that to Turkey should be offered the alternative of a Greece

[012-167]with extended limits under Suzeraineté, &c., according to the Protocol of

[012-168]March 22, or a Greece with narrower limits, entirely independent.

[012-169]The Duke said we must first have satisfaction for the insertion of the

[012-170]Article in the treaty of peace which bound Turkey to the Protocol of March

[012-171]22; Russia, as a party to the Treaty of London, having no right to settle

[012-172]that treaty herself. Next, we should insist on an armistice between the

[012-173]Greeks and Turks.
