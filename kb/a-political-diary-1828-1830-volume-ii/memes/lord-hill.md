# Lord Hill

## Overview

General Rowland Hill, 1st Viscount Hill, served as Commander-in-Chief of the British Army from 1828 to 1842. A trusted subordinate of the [[Duke of Wellington]] during the Peninsular War, Hill brought military expertise and administrative judgment to the army's highest command during a period of post-war retrenchment and reform debates. Ellenborough's diary records his regular interaction with the government on matters of army patronage, discipline, and appointments.

## Role in the Diary

Hill appears primarily in his capacity as the officer responsible for managing the army's personnel and discipline. The diary records Wellington consulting him on sensitive patronage matters: the Duke "saw Lord Hill" regarding political opposition within the army and the delicate task of balancing merit against political connections (029-014, 029-016).

A recurring theme is the pressure exerted on Hill through informal channels. The diarist himself "Wrote to Lord Hill, telling him of Sir G. Walker's" situation, illustrating the constant personal lobbying faced by the Commander-in-Chief (018-150). Hill's resistance to improper influence is noted with approval: "Lord Hill feels the thing asked cannot be" granted, and the diarist concluded that "unless Lord Hill is allowed to exercise the" full authority of his office, the army's discipline would suffer (021-121, 021-122).

## Key Appearances

- **Patronage disputes**: Hill received letters from influential figures seeking preferment for their connections, which he showed to the diarist: "Lord Hill showed me a letter from Sir F." seeking a promotion for a "very young captain" — a request Hill felt unable to grant (021-114, 021-119).
- **Military command integrity**: Wellington told Hill that "he had made thirteen or fourteen" enemies through necessary decisions, reinforcing his support for the Commander-in-Chief's authority (029-016).
- **Social and ceremonial**: The diary records dining at "Lord Hill's. A party chiefly military," establishing Hill's role in the military-social nexus of Regency London (005-016).
- **The King's health**: During George IV's final illness, Hill "left Windsor" and reported on the King's condition, suggesting his proximity to the royal household (033-161).
