[007-001]_June 25._

[007-002]At the office found a letter with enclosures from Colonel Macdonald, dated

[007-003]Tabriz April 20. What he has been doing in Persia I do not know.

[007-004]I have written to him to call upon me on Saturday.

[007-005]Called on the Duke to tell him the substance--which is, that the Turks have

[007-006]already 30,000 men and sixty pieces of cannon at Erzeroum. That a

[007-007]dispossessed Pacha is in arms at Akiska. That the Russians have reinforced

[007-008]the garrisons of Natshiran and Abbasabad, and have withdrawn all their

[007-009]troops to the left bank of the Araxes, with the exception of those who

[007-010]garrison Bayazid. The plague seems rife at Erivan. The Russians about Count

[007-011]Paskewitz abuse the English very much.

[007-012]_June 27._

[007-013]The Chairs told me Lord W. Bentinck had extended to all persons the benefit

[007-014]of the regulation as to coffee planters, _omitting, however_, all the

[007-015]restrictive clauses. They think very seriously of this, and very justly.

[007-016]The Calcutta newspapers consider the principle of colonisation to be

[007-017]conceded.

[007-018]We must abrogate this 'Regulation' without loss of time. I went to the Duke

[007-019]to tell him of it. He said Lord W. Bentinck was not to be trusted, and we

[007-020]should be obliged to recall him. He is gone down in a steamboat to Penang.

[007-021]No news of much importance at the Cabinet room, except that Lord

[007-022]Heytesbury's despatches confirm the account of the sickness of the Russian

[007-023]army.

[007-024]The Turks seem to have given the Russians a great smash at Eski Arnaut.

[007-025]_June 30._

[007-026]A battle near Schumla between the Russians and Turks. The Turks were

[007-027]besieging Pravadi. Diebitsch marched from Silistria and moved upon their

[007-028]communications with Schumla. The Turks seem to have been surprised. They

[007-029]fought gallantly, however, and seem to have caused the Russians great loss.

[007-030]Saw Arbuthnot. He came to the India Board to speak about his friend,

[007-031]Russell Ellice, whom he wishes to make a Director. We afterwards talked of

[007-032]the House and the Government. I think all will turn out well. We have six

[007-033]months before us, but certainly at present we are weak in the House of

[007-034]Commons, though I believe gathering strength in the country, and already

[007-035]very strong there. If we play the great game, striking at the mass, we must

[007-036]succeed. It would never do to go picking up individuals. We must do our

[007-037]best for the country, and we shall have it with us. The worst of it is, the

[007-038]King is the most faithless of men, and Cumberland is at work.

[007-039]The Duke asked Hardinge the other day what he thought of the Government. He

[007-040]said he thought that by losing Canningites and Brunswickers it was fifty

[007-041]weaker than Lord Liverpool's, and these fifty go the other way, making a

[007-042]difference of one hundred on a division. Lord Camden thought if the

[007-043]Brunswickers would not come in we must get a few Whigs--Abercromby, Sir

[007-044]James Graham, the Althorpe people. Stanley would come for anything good,

[007-045]and Brougham too.

[007-046]Arbuthnot asked me if I thought Lord Rosslyn would be cordial with us. I

[007-047]said Yes. His letter of acceptance was most cordial, and with the Lords he

[007-048]was on excellent terms. The only danger would be if Peel and the Commoners

[007-049]were shy.

[007-050]Lord Grey, I said, I did not think in very good humour, but he would differ

[007-051]on foreign politics rather than on questions of a domestic nature. The Duke

[007-052]will not be coquetting with him, because he says very honestly he should be

[007-053]exciting expectations in Lord Grey which, while the King lives, he does not

[007-054]think he can gratify.

[007-055]Saw Mr. Elphinstone by appointment. I wished to have his opinion with

[007-056]regard to the new settlement of Indian Government, which may take place on

[007-057]the expiration of the present Charter. He seemed to think that the

[007-058]Administration of the Government in the King's name would be agreeable to

[007-059]the Civil and Military Services, and to people in England. He doubted

[007-060]whether, as regarded the princes of India, it would signify much, as they

[007-061]now pretty well understood us. He doubted whether the orders of Government

[007-062]here would be better obeyed. He thought there might be an advantage in

[007-063]keeping the King's authority in reserve, to be used only on grand

[007-064]occasions. He confessed, however, that 'having been educated, and having

[007-065]lived under the existing system, he was not best qualified to propose to

[007-066]another. He had his prejudices.' He thought the best mode of arriving at

[007-067]the truth would be by taking the opinions of practical Indians as to

[007-068]reforms and alterations suggested by theoretical men.

[007-069]I asked him to consider the expediency of dividing the territory as now

[007-070]into three unequal Presidencies, of giving to the Governor-General the

[007-071]labour of superintending the Administration in detail of the Bengal

[007-072]Presidency--of having Members of Council. I told him there were many minor

[007-073]points of detail discoverable only by those employed at home, which

[007-074]required and must receive amendment. Such, for instance, is the

[007-075]interpretation given to the Act of Parliament, by which a _regulation_ must

[007-076]be sanctioned or rejected _in extenso,_ there being no power to alter a

[007-077]word, or to reject part and take the rest.

[007-078]Mr. Elphinstone seems to dread a long peace in India. We hold everything

[007-079]together by the Native Army, and we cannot retain that unless we retain the

[007-080]affections of the European officers. In the present state of our finances

[007-081]this is difficult.

[007-082]_July 1._

[007-083]At half-past five received a letter from the Chairman, and the draft

[007-084]relative to the removal of the Governor from Calcutta. The Court wished to

[007-085]have it back to-day. That was impossible; but they have omitted words I

[007-086]inserted in the _précis,_ and must restore, declaring that had the removal

[007-087]been legal, still the Members of Council would have been ordered back. I

[007-088]have now been obliged to give reasons for this addition, and the reasons

[007-089]will be so much worse, as matters of record, that I have suggested to the

[007-090]Chairman he had better substitute a draft containing the words.

[007-091]I think we must detain the _Pallas_ that it may take out both letters--this

[007-092]and the one relating to the leases which is not yet prepared, or we must

[007-093]have an overland dispatch.

[007-094]Delay is one of the inconveniences attending the present system of Indian

[007-095]Government. I told the Chairman in my private note that if we allowed Lord

[007-096]W. Bentinck to emancipate himself in this manner we should really be

[007-097]abandoning all real control over the Government of India. I see clearly

[007-098]there is a Bentinck party in the Court.

[007-099]_July 2._

[007-100]Saw Hardinge. We had some conversation upon the subject of the Government.

[007-101]He seems more alarmed than I am. I trust to the King's fears and the Duke's

[007-102]fortune; besides, we have the country.

[007-103]Hardinge told me the King was very much out of humour. The admission of

[007-104]Lord Rosslyn had not answered. None followed. Lord Durham, Calthorpe, and

[007-105]others left Lord Lansdowne to coalesce with Lord Grey. Hardinge wished me

[007-106]to try Herries again, with the view of opening the Mint by making him

[007-107]Chancellor of the Exchequer in India; but I told him Herries said his

[007-108]domestic circumstances made it impossible, and the Duke did not seem to

[007-109]like it at all.

[007-110]Herries thinks Lord Durham would be glad to be Minister at Naples; for my

[007-111]part I am sure nothing will win Lord Grey but a place for Lord Grey

[007-112]himself, and _that_, in the present state of the King's mind, the Duke is

[007-113]not in a condition to offer.

[007-114]_July 4._

[007-115]Cabinet at half-past three.

[007-116]The Duke read a list of the several points to be considered before the next

[007-117]Session. I cannot recollect half of them. East India Charter; Bank Charter;

[007-118]Usury Laws; East Retford; Duties on Sugar; Duties on Tobacco; Canada; West

[007-119]Indies; Education in Ireland; Irish and English Churches; Poor in Ireland;

[007-120]Public Works; Commission on Ecclesiastical Courts; Reform of English

[007-121]Courts; Reform of Welsh Judicature; Reform of Courts of Equity; Scotch Law

[007-122]of Entail; Salaries of Scotch Judges--_increase_; Salaries of English

[007-123]Judges--_reduction_; Grand Juries, Ireland; Militia Laws; Stamp Duties,

[007-124]&c., &c.

[007-125]The only talk we had was about Irish Poor, and Public Works in Ireland. The

[007-126]feeling seemed against anything like Poor Laws, and against Public Works

[007-127]too. This is mine. The first productive of mischief, the second useless.

[007-128]Undoubtedly it is a great hardship that the English parish should have the

[007-129]burden of Irish poor, but on the other hand in many cases the payers of

[007-130]poor's rates in these parishes have derived advantage from Irish labour.

[007-131]Fitzgerald, Peel, and Goulburn are to look into this subject, and all

[007-132]connected with Ireland.

[007-133]Fitzgerald, Peel, Lord Rosslyn, and, I think, either Herries or Goulburn

[007-134]seemed to think the opposition to the continuance of the China monopoly

[007-135]would be much greater than we expected. Fitzgerald seemed desirous the

[007-136]question of commerce should be reserved, and that of Government decided. I

[007-137]told him the two were inseparably connected.

[007-138]_July 6._

[007-139]Wrote to Lord W. Bentinck telling him I much regretted the having been

[007-140]obliged to send the two letters, relative to the removal of the Government,

[007-141]and the leases--told him the Duke coincided in opinion with the Court.

[007-142]I then expressed my surprise that the Local Government did not obey better.

[007-143]Said they seemed to forget the orders of the Directors were the King's

[007-144]orders transmitted through the channel of the Court and the Board. I added

[007-145]I should endeavour to introduce into every branch of Indian Government the

[007-146]subordination and the improvements now established in the King's

[007-147]service--depended on his co-operation, &c. I sent the letter to the Duke

[007-148]to ask him if I should send it.

[007-149]_July 7._

[007-150]At quarter to six a messenger arrived from the Duke, to whom I sent

[007-151]yesterday my letter to Lord W. asking if I should send it? The Duke desires

[007-152]to see the despatches to which it refers. I have accordingly begged Jones

[007-153]to send them to him. I shall however be in town early myself to-morrow.

[007-154]I told the Duke in my note I should stay in town till late to-morrow to

[007-155]sign the letter as to the six regiments if they passed it. I am glad to

[007-156]have an excuse for not going to Windsor to the Recorder's Report.

[007-157]_July 8._

[007-158]Office at 2. Wilson absent, so I could not transact any military business.

[007-159]Carried the letters relative to the leases and the six regiments to the

[007-160]Duke. He said mine about the regiments was _very good indeed_.

[007-161]The Emperor of Russia seems to have laid himself out most ably at Berlin to

[007-162]captivate the King, and the army, and the people.

[007-163]Seymour's despatches are useful. He mentions _small_ things, which show the

[007-164]character of men.

[007-165]The Emperor does not disguise his desire of peace. He wants no _garanties

[007-166]matérielles_ at the Bosphorus for safe passage. He asks the principle of a

[007-167]pecuniary indemnity, but does not seem disposed to contest the details.

[007-168]Bernstorff observed truly, we could not get out of the Greek Treaty without

[007-169]the help of Russia, and Russia wanted us to get out of the way.

[007-170]The Sultan begins to affect European manners. Calls upon ladies and talks

[007-171]about education! Dines with a merchant! After all, considering his

[007-172]education and his _entourage_, Sultan Mahmoud is the most remarkable man in

[007-173]Europe.

[007-174]_July 9._

[007-175]Office at 2. Met Herries. Told him I should send him a statement of our

[007-176]Indian loans, and place Leach at his disposal. We could then talk them

[007-177]over, and see whether we could effect any financial operation. My idea is

[007-178]that by offering some little higher interest in. India we might induce the

[007-179]holders of the remittable loan to give up that privilege of receiving the

[007-180]interest in England if resident here.

[007-181]Saw Major Cunningham. He looks more than forty, well, certainly, but I

[007-182]should doubt his doing much hard work. He does not think himself a good

[007-183]person to command Irregulars. His Rohillas were almost in as good order as

[007-184]Regulars.
