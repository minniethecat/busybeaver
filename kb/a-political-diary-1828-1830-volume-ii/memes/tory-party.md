# Tory Party

## Overview

The Tory Party was the dominant political force in Britain during the period covered by Ellenborough's diary (1828–1830). Led by the Duke of Wellington in the Lords and Robert Peel in the Commons, the party confronted the Catholic Question, electoral franchise reform, and the rising demand for parliamentary reform that would ultimately split the party and end its long ascendancy.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough was a Tory peer and cabinet member, writing from inside the party's leadership. His diary records the tensions within the party over Catholic Emancipation, which he observed "destroyed our unity." The party maintained a working majority through 1829–1830 but was increasingly divided between Ultra-Tories opposed to Catholic relief and more pragmatic elements supporting Wellington.

## Key Divisions

The Catholic Relief Bill created a rift within the party. Ellenborough noted the "hostile cheer" from some quarters and the difficulty of maintaining discipline. (020-094) As the reform question grew, the party's electoral position weakened. The Duke was "obliged to say something civil to the Whigs" despite his reluctance. (001-032)
