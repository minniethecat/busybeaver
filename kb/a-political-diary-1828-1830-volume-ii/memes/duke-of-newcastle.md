**Henry Pelham-Clinton, 4th Duke of Newcastle** (1785–1851), styled Earl of Lincoln until 1795, was a British peer and politician. A staunch Tory, he was known for his opposition to Catholic emancipation and his influence in Nottinghamshire politics.

== Political Position ==

The Duke of Newcastle was among the peers listed in the diary as opposing the government. Ellenborough records that "these peers have been" against the ministry, listing the Duke of Newcastle among them. (001-004) This placed him within the [[Tory Party|Ultra-Tory]] faction that resisted Wellington's conciliatory policies.

Newcastle's opposition was strong enough to draw notice at [[Lord Lansdowne]]'s gatherings, where the Duke's presence was noted as significant. (032-037) He was described as being "friendly to [[Lord Grey]] and the Duke of Newcastle," suggesting a potential alignment with Whig figures on certain issues. (036-103)

== Electoral Influence ==

As a major landowner, Newcastle wielded significant electoral influence. He controlled several parliamentary seats in Nottinghamshire, commonly referred to as "Newcastle's boroughs." The diary notes his connections with [[Lord Falmouth]] and Sir E. Knatchbull, fellow Tory peers. (054-129)

His resistance to reform made him a target of the [[Reform Bill]] agitation that followed the Wellington ministry's fall.

== Personal Life ==

Newcastle was known for his rigid Tory principles and his sometimes difficult personality. His staunch opposition to Catholic emancipation in 1829 placed him at odds with Wellington's government, contributing to the fractures within the Tory party that eventually led to the ministry's collapse.
