[016-001]The Cabinet seemed generally to acquiesce in the expediency of only having

[016-002]a Committee this year.

[016-003]At first they all seemed to think the continuance of the government in the

[016-004]Company a matter of course. I told them that even with the China trade the

[016-005]Government could not now go on without great reductions of expenditure, and

[016-006]that I hoped the Cabinet would not come to a hasty decision upon a question

[016-007]involving so many important political and financial considerations. The

[016-008]present system was not one of great expense, but it was one involving great

[016-009]delay--and delay was expense, and not only expense but abandonment of

[016-010]authority. It was in this point of view that I hoped the Cabinet would look

[016-011]at the question when it came before them.

[016-012]I mean to go quietly to work; but I mean, if I can, to substitute the

[016-013]King's government for that of the Company. [Footnote: This was not carried

[016-014]out till 1858, after the great mutiny.] I am sure that in doing so I shall

[016-015]confer a great benefit upon India and effect the measure which is most

[016-016]likely to retain for England the possession of India.

[016-017]We afterwards spoke of the Batta question. I read Lord Wellesley's letter,

[016-018]and stated the opinions of Sir J. Malcolm, Sir Archibald Campbell, and Sir

[016-019]J. Nicholls.

[016-020]I stated that it seemed the feeling in the army was excited more by the

[016-021]apprehension of further reductions than by the establishment of the half-

[016-022]Batta stations; that if concessions were made to the Bengal army, the other

[016-023]armies would be discontented and further demands would be made.

[016-024]The Duke said, as a soldier, and having been in India at the time, he must

[016-025]say he thought the orders of 1828 [Footnote: Orders issued by Lord William

[016-026]Bentinck, abolishing full batta or the larger scale of allowances to the

[016-027]military at stations where half-batta only had been recognised, before the

[016-028]Act of the Bengal Government allowing full batta in consideration of

[016-029]officers providing themselves with quarters.--See Thornton's _British

[016-030]India_, pp. 221-25.] a breach of faith--but these having been issued, he

[016-031]thought we must stand to them. The general opinion was that as nothing

[016-032]could be said or done till the arrival of despatches, there could be no

[016-033]necessity for deciding.

[016-034]I mentioned my Supreme Court Bill, which will be ready immediately.

[016-035]I hope to save--ultimately 60,000 pounds a year in the Supreme Courts.

[016-044]Ireland is put off till Monday, that we may all read the papers. We dine

[016-045]with the Duke to-morrow.

[016-046]The French oppose all the people we name for the Greek coronet. They have

[016-047]named Prince Charles of Bavaria, and the second son of the King of Bavaria

[016-048]with a regency till he is of age! However, this folly they did not press.

[016-049]We first named Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg, whom the French would not

[016-050]hear of. Then Leopold! They did not like him. Prince Emilius of Hesse

[016-051]Darmstadt was thought of. The French have suggested Prince John of Saxony,

[016-052]second son of the King, a fine young man, about 28, but unknown. His elder

[016-053]brother too may soon succeed to the throne, and he has no children.

[016-054]Otherwise there is no objection to this Prince.

[016-055]It seems to me they are running after trifles. Russia adheres to us as to

[016-056]the Prince, or rather remains neutral, thinking I have no doubt that France

[016-057]and England will quarrel about the feather.

[016-058]The secret instruction which it was proposed to give to the Ambassadors is

[016-059]now abandoned, France having objected. They were to have been ordered to

[016-060]_insist_ upon Turkey taking one of two things of which she was to have

[016-061]ostensibly the pure option. Now they are only clearly to intimate their

[016-062]_wish_. However, it seems Russia will take a million of ducats less if

[016-063]Turkey will make Greece independent. That is, she will give up a claim to

[016-064]what she cannot get in order to effect that she has no right to ask.

[016-065]The French Government have, by giving new rates of pension, got 1,600 old

[016-066]officers out of the army, and filled important stations with friends of

[016-067]their own. They think they shall stand.

[016-068]I forgot to mention the Archduke Maximilian of Modena as one of the persons

[016-069]talked of for Greece. It seems uncertain whether any one of these Princes

[016-070]would take the coronet.

[016-071]_November 14, Saturday._

[016-072]Cabinet room. Rosslyn and afterwards Lord Bathurst there. Read the Irish

[016-073]papers, that is, Lord Francis Leveson's private letters to Peel and Peel's

[016-074]to him, with a letter from Peel to Leslie Foster, asking his opinion as to

[016-075]education and Maynooth, and Foster's reply. The latter is important. He

[016-076]thinks the political and religious hostility of the two parties is

[016-077]subsiding. The chiefs alone keep it up. The adherents are gradually falling

[016-078]off. To open the questions of education, &c., now, would be to open closing

[016-079]wounds, nor would anything be accomplished. The priests would resist

[016-080]everything proposed, and the Protestants would not be satisfied. The

[016-081]Kildare Street Society, however defective, does a great deal of good, more

[016-082]than could be expected from any new system we could carry at this moment.

[016-083]As to Maynooth, to withdraw the grant would not diminish the funds, while

[016-084]it would increase the bad feeling.

[016-085]The increased prevalence of outrage, arising more from a disorganised state

[016-086]of society than from politics or religion, and the _assassination_ plan,

[016-087]must be met by an extensive police, directed by stipendiary magistrates;

[016-088]and the expense of this police, and the indemnity to sufferers must be paid

[016-089]by the barony in which the outrage takes place.

[016-090]All Peel's letters are very sensible. Lord Francis Leveson's are in an odd

[016-091]style, rather affected occasionally, and his ideas are almost always such

[016-092]as require to be overruled. He is a forward boy; but I see nothing of the

[016-093]statesman in him. We ought to have had Hardinge there.

[016-094]Dined at the Duke's. A man of the name of Ashe is writing letters to the

[016-095]Duke of Cumberland threatening his life if he does not give up a book in

[016-096]MS.

[016-097]This book of Ashe's is a romance detailing all sorts of scandals of the

[016-098]Royal Family, and of horrors of the Duke of Cumberland. The book is

[016-099]actually in the possession of the Duke of Wellington.

[016-100]The King's violence, when there was an idea of Denman's [Footnote: The King

[016-101]always resented an offensive quotation of Denman's as counsel during the

[016-102]Queen's trial.] appearing for the Recorder, was greater, the Duke says,

[016-103]than what he showed during the Catholic question.

[016-104]Lady Conyngham has been and is very ill. There is no idea of the Court

[016-105]going to Brighton.

[016-106]_November 16._

[016-107]Cabinet. France, Austria, and England to ask Don Pedro distinctly what he

[016-108]means to do. We certainly cannot go on as we are with Portugal for ever.

[016-109]Aberdeen fears France may acknowledge Miguel first, and thus take our place

[016-110]with Portugal.

[016-111]The Duke says if we can keep Spain on good terms with Portugal, and with

[016-112]ourselves, the connection of France and Portugal does not signify, and we

[016-113]are much better off than with Portugal against Spain and France. This is

[016-114]true.

[016-115]A long talk about Ashe, who has written a libel on the Duke of Cumberland,

[016-116]which the Duke gave to the Duke of Wellington. Ashe wants it back, and

[016-117]threatens if he has it not returned to him; but in a letter, and in such

[016-118]terms that the Attorney-General does not think him liable to prosecution.

[016-119]He might be held to bail, perhaps, but that would bring out the case. It

[016-120]was decided to do nothing, but to take precautions against his doing

[016-121]mischief. The Duke of Cumberland has been cautioned.

[016-122]The Insurrection Act seems to be popular with Fitzgerald. Peel says it is

[016-123]bad in principle, and has the effect of placing the higher classes in

[016-124]hostility against the lower. The decision seemed to be to have a powerful

[016-125]police--stipendiary magistrates--frequent trials--constables appointed by

[016-126]Government--counties paying for additional police.

[016-127]Peel suggests the division of Ireland into smaller districts, and the

[016-128]acquiring a personal knowledge of individuals, and making the districts

[016-129]responsible.

[016-130]I believe the country is too populous, and the population too wicked, for

[016-131]this plan to succeed.

[016-132]The murderers will be brought in from a distance.

[016-133]The state of demoralisation in which the country is is dreadful. Murders

[016-134]are held to be of no account.

[016-135]_November 17._

[016-136]Read, as I came down to Worthing, Colonel McDonald's last despatches, and

[016-137]his private letter, which I received last night. Sent them to the Duke, and

[016-138]asked whether under the circumstances we should let Abbas Murza have some

[016-139]thousand stand of arms, Colonel McDonald doing his best to secure ultimate

[016-140]repayment.

[016-141]The Persian cavalry raised by the Russians in their newly conquered

[016-142]territories seem to have fought as well as any troops in their service.

[016-143]Colonel McDonald says it is from a disciplined Persian army alone,

[016-144]commanded by Russian officers, that he dreads the invasion of India. A

[016-145]European force would be wasted by the climate. The Pasha of Suleimania had

[016-146]too European a taste, and wanted to make regular soldiers without pay or

[016-147]clothing. So his soldiers turned him out, and made his brother Pacha.

[016-148]Colonel McDonald describes all that side of Turkey as going _au devant du

[016-149]conquérant_. Such has been the wretchedness of their government.

[016-150]_Worthing, November 18, 1829._

[016-151]At 11 P.M. received a letter from the Duke of Wellington by a messenger,

[016-152]telling me he regretted I had not met Lord Melville and him before the

[016-153]Cabinet, and proposing, as he and Lord Melville both wished to go out of

[016-154]town on Friday, that I should meet them either to-morrow, after 2, or on

[016-155]Friday morning.

[016-156]I wrote to say I would be with him at 3 to-morrow.

[016-157]_November 19._

[016-158]Met the Duke and Lord Melville.

[016-159]After conversation on topics connected with the subject we came to the

[016-160]point, which was that the Duke wished both to preserve the monopoly and the

[016-161]Company as administrators of Indian affairs.

[016-162]The Duke is much swayed by early recollections. He is besides very desirous

[016-163]of having the City of London in his hands.

[016-164]I admitted that the great and solid objection to placing the government of

[016-165]India directly in the hands of the Crown was the consequent increase of

[016-166]Parliamentary business, already too extensive to be well performed.

[016-167]As to the China trade, if the Government of India can be conducted without

[016-168]the assistance derived from it, I saw no reason for its continuance; but I

[016-169]had rather continue the monopoly than lose the Company as a trading Company

[016-170]to China, for I thought the trade might be greatly endangered were their

[016-171]commerce to cease. I said that the continuance of the system of carrying on

[016-172]the government through the instrumentality of the Company was not

[016-173]inconsistent with giving to it the efficiency, the vigour, and the celerity

[016-174]of the King's Government.

[016-175]Lord Melville admitted the cumbrousness of the present system.

[016-176]The Duke seemed to have no objection to alterations in details, provided

[016-177]the principle were adhered to.

[016-178]Both to-day and in the Cabinet on Friday last I was surprised by Lord

[016-179]Melville's inertness.

[016-180]The Duke wishes Leach's paper to be 'the case to be proved.' This may be

[016-181]done, and yet the necessary improvements introduced.

[016-182]Met Seymour, who had been with the Duke. He is just come from Berlin. He

[016-183]seemed to say that the great success of the war was wholly unexpected by

[016-184]the Emperor.

[016-185]_November 20._

[016-186]Wrote to Hylton Jolliffe to beg he would turn his attention to the subject

[016-187]of steam navigation to India by the Red Sea, as a private speculation.

[016-188]_November 21._

[016-189]Read a letter from Sir G. Murray. It seems the Duke, Lord Melville, and Sir

[016-190]George are to meet soon to consider whether some alteration should not be

[016-191]made in the rules of the Order of the Bath. I suggested that it might be an

[016-192]improvement to make civilians eligible to the lower grades of the Order. It

[016-193]might occasionally be very convenient to make a man a K.C.B. for civil

[016-194]service.
