[019-001]I was glad to have my letter. I can defend every word in it. It contains

[019-002]the simile of the elephants, which I am sorry for, as I fear those

[019-003]described _as tame_ may be foolish enough to endeavour to show they are not

[019-004]so by affecting a degree of vivacity beyond their nature; but still I can

[019-005]defend it.

[019-006]Lord William describes his position as not agreeable, having to effect the

[019-007]odious work of reduction. [Footnote: Besides the burning question of 'Half-

[019-008]Batta,' Lord W. Bentinck's administration was regarded as hostile in spirit

[019-009]to that of his predecessors, and so disliked by those who had served under

[019-010]them, especially by the military.] He says that in India no man thinks of

[019-011]anything but MONEY, that the local government has incurred great odium by

[019-012]carrying into effect the orders of the home authorities. He recommends Sir

[019-013]Charles Metcalfe as a man standing by Malcolm's side, and fit for the

[019-014]government of Bombay. I a little fear Sir Charles Metcalfe. He is rather

[019-015]too vehement. I doubt whether he would be a safe man. I am quite sure

[019-016]Courtney would be a very unfit man. The Governor of Bombay ought to be an

[019-017]Indian, but who is there?

[019-018]Lord William represents the Burmese Government as a barbarian Government.

[019-019]He says they have sacrificed all who assisted us, and that the difficulty

[019-020]in retroceding the Tenasserim provinces would be to know what to do with

[019-021]the 35,000 people who have sought our protection.

[019-022]This report makes the wisdom of our recent policy yet clearer than it

[019-023]appeared before.

[019-024]_December 31._

[019-025]Read twenty papers on the opium treaties and management in Central India.

[019-026]The Supreme Government have decided upon no longer limiting the extent of

[019-027]cultivation in Malwa, and upon permitting the free transit of the drug.

[019-028]This was expedient because undoubtedly our restrictions led to the most

[019-029]hostile feelings on the part both of princes and people, to the injury of

[019-030]the traders, to violent and offensive interference on our part in the

[019-031]internal policy of foreign States, and to smuggling protected by large

[019-032]bodies of armed men. The smugglers would soon have been Pindarries. This

[019-033]system began only in 1825. It was forced upon the small States, and not

[019-034]upon that of Gwalior, so that smuggling defeated the object.

[019-035]_January 2, 1830._

[019-036]Received from the Duke a note to say the publication of my private letter

[019-037]to Sir J. Malcolm did not signify one pin's head, and it _will have_ done

[019-038]good in India.

[019-039]Wrote a long letter to Lord William Bentinck. I pressed upon him the

[019-040]necessity of making the home and the local authorities draw together. I

[019-041]told him he was suffering not for his obedience but for the disobedience of

[019-042]his predecessors. Assured him of support, lamented the _ungentlemanlike_

[019-043]tone of society evidenced by the insult of the commanding officers to him,

[019-044]and by the publication of my private letter. I spoke in high terms of

[019-045]Lieut. W. Hislop's report on the opium arrangements (which on reflection I

[019-046]thought better than writing a letter to him), and I likewise spoke highly

[019-047]of Mr. Scott, the Commissioner in Assam. Acknowledged the Government could

[019-048]not have done otherwise than give up the opium treaties; but foretold a

[019-049]large falling off in the opium revenue from over-cultivation in Malwa.

[019-050]_January 3._

[019-051]A letter from Clare on East Indian matters which I answered at length. Sent

[019-052]Prendergast's pamphlet to Jones.

[019-053]Read reports on the Delhi and Firuz Shah's canal, by which it appears my

[019-054]plan of joining the Sutlege and Jumna is not visionary. It has been done.

[019-055]The canal can still be traced. Delhi seems in distant times to have been

[019-056]like Milan, in the midst of canals. The grand canal sent a branch through

[019-057]the palace. The water has been again turned in the same channel. When the

[019-058]water flowed into Delhi on the opening of the canal on May 30, 1820, the

[019-059]people went out to meet it and threw flowers into the stream. In those

[019-060]countries nothing can be done without water, and with water, and such a

[019-061]sun, anything.

[019-062]_January 4, 1830._

[019-063]Head Eraser's journey and finished it. It is very interesting, and shows

[019-064]how completely the Persian monarchy is falling to pieces.

[019-065]_January 5._

[019-066]Saw Wrangham. There is no news. The affairs of the Netherlands, he says,

[019-067]look rather better, and Polignac is very stout and says he is very strong.

[019-068]It seems great complaints are made of Lord Stuart, who gives little

[019-069]information, and what little he does give is incorrect.

[019-070]_January 6._

[019-071]Vesey Fitzgerald will certainly not be able to attend the House this year.

[019-072]His physicians say he would die in five minutes if he got up to speak. I

[019-073]heard G. Dawson tell the Duke to-day. I rather suspect G. Dawson would like

[019-074]Vesey's place.

[019-075]The Duke has been much occupied with the Greek question. I have not yet

[019-076]read any papers at the Foreign Office. He spoke to me of Bankes's going

[019-077]out, which he regretted.

[019-078]He had had some conversation last year at Belvoir with Lord Graham upon

[019-079]Indian affairs, and had been quite surprised to find how much he knew. He

[019-080]had thought he only knew how to comb his hair. The Duke thinks of Horace

[019-081]Twiss for secretary. He had thought of Mr. Wortley, Lord Wharncliffe's son,

[019-082]a very clever young man, but he wanted a _made_ man, not one to learn. I

[019-083]shall suggest Ashley's taking Horace Twiss's place, and Lord Graham being

[019-084]First Commissioner. This will force him to come forward. Then Wortley might

[019-085]be Second Commissioner. Horace Twiss is a clever man, but rather vulgar.

[019-086]However, he is a lawyer and a very good speaker, and will do very well.

[019-087]_January 7._

[019-088]I told the Chairs my views as to an alteration in the Supreme Court Bill.

[019-089]They seemed to approve if the thing could be done. I had afterwards some

[019-090]conversation with the Chancellor upon this subject. He admitted the force

[019-091]of my reasoning, but desired to have a memorandum about it, which indeed

[019-092]will be convenient to me as well as to him. It should state all the new

[019-093]circumstances since the establishment of the Supreme Court which render its

[019-094]existence less necessary than it was, and more inapplicable than ever to

[019-095]the condition of India.

[019-096]At the Duke's dinner I told the Duke and Rosslyn the substance of Lord

[019-097]William's letters. The Duke said the act [Footnote: In combining to oppose

[019-098]the Half-Batta orders. See Thornton's _British India_, vol. v.] of the

[019-099]officers was mutiny.

[019-100]The King is ill. He has lost a good deal of blood.

[019-101]_January 8._

[019-102]The King quite well again. In the morning began and nearly finished a

[019-103]memorandum on the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for the Chancellor.

[019-104]Cabinet at 2. Conversation respecting the abolition of the Welsh

[019-105]judgeships, and the addition of a judge to the Courts of King's Bench and

[019-106]Common Pleas, or Exchequer. The two new judges would be Circuit Judges of

[019-107]Wales. The Welsh gentlemen seem to be favourable to the change. The

[019-108]attornies, who are numerous and powerful, very hostile. The Chancellor

[019-109]introduces again his Bill of last Session. The Equity is to be separated

[019-110]from the Common Law Jurisdiction of the Court of Exchequer. The subject was

[019-111]only talked of, and decision deferred till Sunday next.

[019-112]We then talked of Ireland. The Grand Jury Presentment Bill is not yet

[019-113]prepared. The plan for a police is to place the nominations in the hands of

[019-114]the Lord-Lieutenant. To send stipendiary magistrates when and where they

[019-115]are wanted.

[019-116]Peel's suggestions went much further; but Lord F. Gower seems to me to be

[019-117]only a clever boy. He has as yet proposed _nothing_ worthy of adoption, and

[019-118]he has often been near the commission of errors from which he has been

[019-119]saved only by Peel's advice.

[019-120]He wished to establish stipendiary magistrates in every county, the effect

[019-121]of which would have been to disgust all the gentlemen magistrates, and to

[019-122]lead them to the abandonment of their duty. He wished too to unite in all

[019-123]cases the inspectorships of police with the office of stipendiary

[019-124]magistrate, to avoid collision; but the duties of inspector are of a mere

[019-125]ministerial and inferior character, and would not agree well with those of

[019-126]a magistrate.

[019-127]I must read to-morrow all the late protocols and despatches. The Russians

[019-128]and French have agreed to make Leopold Prince of Greece, but the King

[019-129]cannot endure the idea. Aberdeen thinks he has made a great conquest in

[019-130]carrying the point of Leopold's election. I confess I cannot understand the

[019-131]great advantage we derive from it. What an extraordinary scene! Those

[019-132]monarchical states, the most adverse to revolution, combine to assist the

[019-133]rebellion of a people against its sovereign, a rebellion commenced by

[019-134]murder and continued by treachery, stained with every crime that ever

[019-135]disgraced human nature! [Footnote: The massacres by the Greeks at

[019-136]Tripolitza and Athens, the latter in direct breach of a capitulation, had,

[019-137]according to a not unfavourable historian, cast a dark stain on the Greek

[019-138]cause and diminished the interest felt for it in foreign countries.

[019-139](Alison, _Hist. Europe_, 1815-52, iii. 150.)] They destroy the fleet of an

[019-140]unoffending Power in a time of profound peace in his own port. They thus

[019-141]facilitate the attack of an enemy, and in the extreme peril of the defeated

[019-142]sovereign they increase their demands in order to form a substantive State

[019-143]out of the ruins of his Empire. They then elect a Prince unknown to the

[019-144]people over whom he is to reign, and support him by equal assistance in

[019-145]ships and money! Those monarchical states set up a revolutionary government

[019-146]and maintain it in coparcenary! It was reserved for these times to witness

[019-147]such contradictions. I do not think any one is very well satisfied with

[019-148]them but Aberdeen. He is charmed.

[019-149]_Sunday, January 10._

[019-150]Cabinet. Conversation first as to an intended publication by Mr. Stapleton

[019-151]of a 'Life of Canning,' in which he means to insert the substance, if not

[019-152]the copies, of public papers relating to transactions not yet terminated.

[019-153]He has had it intimated to him that he will do so at his peril. He holds an

[019-154]office under the Government during pleasure. I said he had no right over

[019-155]private letters relating to public subjects which only came to the

[019-156]knowledge of the writer by his official situation. He should be told it was

[019-157]a high breach of public confidence, and he should be displaced if he was

[019-158]guilty of it. He will have a hint, but I fear not one sufficiently strong.

[019-159]It is Lady Canning who thinks she can injure the Duke of Wellington, and so

[019-160]publishes these papers. Stapleton is her editor. She demanded from Aberdeen

[019-161]official letters of Canning's, and actually threatened him with a suit in

[019-162]Chancery if he did not give them up. The Duke says he has copies of all

[019-163]Canning's letters, and he shall publish if they do. [Footnote: Augustus

[019-164]Granville Stapleton had been private secretary to Canning, and published

[019-165]about 1830-31 _The Political Life of George Canning_, and nearly thirty

[019-166]years later, _George Canning and his Times_. The latter work contains much

[019-167]correspondence the publication of which might have been objected to at the

[019-168]earlier date.]

[019-169]We had Scarlett and afterwards Bosanquet in upon the Welsh Judicature

[019-170]question. It was at last decided that the Equity Jurisdiction of the Courts

[019-171]of Great Session should be sent to the Court of Exchequer, that power

[019-172]should be taken to the King of directing the circuits to be held where he

[019-173]pleased, and that the two new judges of the English Courts should do the

[019-174]duty of the Welsh circuits. The proceedings to be assimilated to those of

[019-175]the English Courts.

[019-176]The saving by the reduction of the Welsh judges, after allowing for their

[019-177]pensions, will leave an ample fund for the compensation of the officers

[019-178]reduced.
