[045-001]_September 20, 1830._

[045-002]Wrote a long letter to Hardinge upon the political consequences of

[045-003]Huskisson's death, [Footnote: He was killed, as is well known, at the

[045-004]opening of the Liverpool and Manchester Railway.] urging the introduction

[045-005]of Palmerston and Stanley. The latter to Vent the junction of the Whig

[045-006]aristocracy with the Radicals.

[045-007]I am sure, if measures are not taken immediately, we shall have all the

[045-008]Huskissonians, Whigs and Ultra-Tories (the last are insane), united against

[045-009]us.

[045-010]Received from Sir J. Malcolm a letter with some enclosures about suttees.

[045-011]He has reluctantly and fearfully abolished suttee, making it culpable

[045-012]homicide to assist, and murder to force the victim. He has done it, I

[045-013]think, wisely by a repeal of a clause in one regulation and an amendment.

[045-014]Thus not putting it vainly forward as Lord William did in a pompous

[045-015]document.

[045-016]He has abolished the Military Board, I believe, very wisely; but there may

[045-017]be a difficulty with the Duke, if I cannot do it without talking to him

[045-018]about it. I believe Sir J. Malcolm is quite right, and that there would

[045-019]have been no hope of preserving a system of real economy had the Military

[045-020]Board been permitted to remain.

[045-021]I am curious to see his measure of checks on expenditure, that if it be

[045-022]good it may be adopted at the other Presidencies.

[045-023]Received some letters from Lord W. Bentinck. Lord Dalhousie has been very

[045-024]ill, and the command of the army would fall, Lord William says, into the

[045-025]weakest hands, if anything happened to him.

[045-026]The spirit of the army was becoming better, I gather from Lord William's

[045-027]letter, but it required much attention. I have been thinking all day of

[045-028]what measures may be adopted for improving it.

[045-029]_September 21._

[045-030]Office. Read to Cabell my memorandum on the alterations which might be

[045-031]introduced into the army, which I wrote hurriedly this morning. He was long

[045-032]in the military department, and can be of much use. Cabinet room. I think

[045-033]the result of Lord Stuart's dispatches is that the moderate party are

[045-034]gaining strength. I should say the facts we see in the newspapers lead to a

[045-035]different conclusion.

[045-036]The Ministers and the old leading members of the Chamber of Deputies act

[045-037]manfully against the crowd. Their declarations of intention are

[045-038]satisfactory. I really believe they mean to act honestly if they can.

[045-039]Austria seems to have hesitated about the acknowledgment of the King of the

[045-040]French after the receipt of a dispatch from Petersburg, and Metternich, who

[045-041]seems to be growing weak, wavered after he had received General Belliard

[045-042]very cordially.

[045-043]Prussia, that is _the King,_ hesitated about signing the letter to Louis

[045-044]Philippe when he heard of the doubts of Austria. The result, however, is

[045-045]that all _entrainés_ by us will acknowledge; the Emperor of Russia, who was

[045-046]the most reluctant, having determined to do so if the others did. I should

[045-047]say there is this satisfactory conclusion to be drawn from what we have

[045-048]seen, that if France showed a disposition to aggrandise herself all Europe

[045-049]would be against her.

[045-050]The object of the French Government is to place France exactly in the

[045-051]position in which she stood a fortnight before the ordonnances--that is,

[045-052]Talleyrand's wish, and he has _rédigé_ his own instructions.

[045-053]Read Aberdeen's letter, dated the 17th, stating the necessity of

[045-054]maintaining cordial intercourse with and between Spain and Portugal, and

[045-055]intimating that on the promulgation of an amnesty according to the terms

[045-056]recently communicated England will resume diplomatic relations with Miguel,

[045-057]but not otherwise.

[045-058]Spain seems to be sensible. There was a movement of folly about Royalist

[045-059]volunteers which was put down, and the Government seems by no means

[045-060]disposed to give way to Absolutists. If the Queen should have a son Spain

[045-061]will probably be tranquil.

[045-062]Talleyrand pretends the French will be reasonable about Algiers. I do not

[045-063]wish them to be so. I believe they could not have made a worse purchase.

[045-064]They will find the possession very expensive. Their troops will hate it,

[045-065]they will have nothing beyond their outposts, and it is no port.

[045-066]My first opinion is strengthened, that they could not be worse than if they

[045-067]were left as they are.

[045-068]_September 24._

[045-069]The populace and the burghers at Brussels have quarrelled, and fought a

[045-070]little. It seems the Liberals and the Catholics, [Footnote: They have

[045-071]formed the two opposing parties in the Belgian Chambers since the country

[045-072]became an independent State. They had temporarily united against Protestant

[045-073]Holland.] as the others are called, have been long diverging. The deputies

[045-074]and men of property, excepting M. de Stassart, have become alarmed. The

[045-075]Prince de Ligne and D'Aremberg and others have left Brussels. On the 21st,

[045-076]probably the 20th, in the evening a proclamation was published at Antwerp

[045-077]by Prince Frederick of Orange, noticing the excesses of the populace, and

[045-078]announcing that the troops would relieve the burgher guard. This must have

[045-079]been done in concert with the influential persons of the town who are

[045-080]alarmed for their property. The Liégeois are very violent. They will be

[045-081]expelled from Brussels. No more can get there, as the road is interrupted.

[045-082]The Dutch have but 20,000 men, of whom the Belgians are as three to five.

[045-083]The Belgians had begun to desert, but they did not join the Bruxellois in

[045-084]any numbers. The hanging of some of the Brussels mob would have an

[045-085]excellent effect.

[045-086]The Government of France seems to become weaker, and to permit things which

[045-087]discredit it.

[045-088]A night or two ago some _ouvriers_ insisted on going into the King's

[045-089]bedroom, after he was gone to sleep, woke him, and made him make a speech

[045-090]sitting up in his bed. Twelve departments have united against indirect

[045-091]taxes, and few pay those which are direct. Meanwhile, the Algerine treasure

[045-092]has been pillaged by the officers of the army, and ships clearing for

[045-093]Toulon go elsewhere to land it. They want a loan, while the fallen

[045-094]Government would have had a surplus. They will find the raising of a loan

[045-095]difficult. The French are displeased by the coldness of Austria and

[045-096]Prussia, and by the marching of Austrian and Prussian troops.

[045-097]The King of Saxony has resigned, or rather he has associated his nephew

[045-098]with himself as Co-Regent; the brother waiving his claim to the throne.

[045-099]The Landgrave of Hesse Cassel was met by a deputation requiring him to do a

[045-100]number of public acts, and amongst the rest the dismissal of all

[045-101]mistresses. It seems the Electoral Prince has one to whom he is going to be

[045-102]married.

[045-103]The Duke of Brunswick lately galloped off _lui Troisième_ while his palace

[045-104]was burning!

[045-105]These are odd times!

[045-106]However, here people seem to be inclined to be quiet. Even the Common

[045-107]Council have by a large majority decided against congratulating or noticing

[045-108]the French people.

[045-109]_September 26._

[045-110]Brandreth told me there was a report of the Belgian troops having entered

[045-111]Brussels, and of a great massacre. There will be news to-morrow as the wind

[045-112]is down.

[045-113]_September 27._

[045-114]No direct news from Brussels yet. There has been fighting for two days, and

[045-115]it was known at Antwerp that the first regiment that entered was nearly

[045-116]destroyed. It seems the invitation of one section was a ruse.

[045-117]There are to be no Cabinets for eight or ten days, the Civil List not being

[045-118]prepared. When we do meet we are not to separate.

[045-119]There seems to be every expectation of a new Ministry in Paris, and in the

[045-120]revolutionary sense.

[045-121]I saw Aberdeen. He rather expects it.

[045-122]Read the report of the Commission appointed to form the articles of

[045-123]accusation against the Ministers. It is a party speech, with little points

[045-124]and prettinesses, affecting moderation, and full of rancour. It is a nation

[045-125]which has no idea of justice.

[045-126]_September 28._

[045-127]Cabinet room. Dispatches of the 24th and 25th from Sir Ch. Bagot; but none

[045-128]from Mr. Cartwright. When Sir Ch. Bagot wrote last thirty hours had elapsed

[045-129]without official intelligence, although the distance is only thirteen

[045-130]hours. It was known there had been hard fighting, that it was necessary to

[045-131]take in succession every house in the Rue Neuve Royale, that the troops

[045-132]were in possession of the upper part of the town, and a proposition had

[045-133]been made by the lower town for a cessation of hostilities, after which

[045-134]they had recommenced.

[045-135]It is evident the resistance has been most serious. 20,000 French are in

[045-136]the town, and these probably direct the defence. All clubs, and councils of

[045-137]all sorts, had ceased to have power two days before the attack. There has

[045-138]been perfect anarchy. The troops behaved admirably. They were much

[045-139]exasperated. No assistance had been sent by the country.

[045-140]Aberdeen is confident the King's troops have been driven out, because no

[045-141]official accounts were sent. The Duke, and all the military men, say the

[045-142]non-arrival of dispatches proves nothing but that the affair was not over.

[045-143]During an engagement a general can think of nothing but victory. The

[045-144]importance of the result is incalculable.

[045-145]At Paris the National Guard have dispersed a meeting of lookers on, who

[045-146]were led by curiosity to crowd about a riding school in which the Society

[045-147]of Les Amis du Peuple met the day after they were denounced by Guizot in

[045-148]the Chamber as agitating France. Two officers of the National Guard entered

[045-149]the riding school, and warned the meeting of the danger they were bringing

[045-150]upon public tranquillity. On the representation of the second they

[045-151]adjourned.

[045-152]At dinner at Lord Rosslyn's the Duke said the French Government could not

[045-153]go on as it was. The chief of the National Guard necessarily commanded

[045-154]everything. The National Guard might become janissaries. I think the

[045-155]Government may go on as it is _in form,_ but it will vary _in substance_

[045-156]from day to day. Management, a little good fortune, and a few examples of

[045-157]determination may make it a fair Government; a single error may produce

[045-158]anarchy.

[045-159]The Duke gave an excellent account of the feeling at Liverpool, Manchester,

[045-160]and Birmingham. At Manchester it was better than at Birmingham, but there

[045-161]they received very coldly Tennyson's speech about giving them members, and

[045-162]at last put an end to it by striking their glasses with their knives, which

[045-163]made such a ringing that Tennyson was obliged to sit down. He deserved this

[045-164]for his bad taste.

[045-165]The Duke was astonished by the machinery. Those who have witnessed the

[045-166]improvements of late years expect progressive improvements so great that

[045-167]they say a man who laid out 100,000£ now in the best machinery would, if he

[045-168]refused to adopt the new improvements they anticipate, be without profit in

[045-169]five years and be ruined in ten.

[045-170]The rapidity of motion is so great in the steam carriages that even the

[045-171]Duke with his quick eyes could not see the figures on the posts which mark

[045-172]the distance at every quarter of a mile, and when two steam carriages

[045-173]crossed no face could be seen. [Footnote: This was on the Manchester and

[045-174]Liverpool Railway, then just opened, and describes the first impression

[045-175]made by railway travelling.] It was like the whizzing of a cannon ball. The

[045-176]cold is great, and they must have some defence against the wind, through

[045-177]which they pass so rapidly.

[045-178]A new canal without locks, which brings coals to Birmingham in two hours,

[045-179]which by the old canal required nine, is more magnificent even than the

[045-180]railroad, splendid as that is. The railroad cost a million. For several

[045-181]days after it was opened the proprietors made 250£ a day.

[045-182]The King has the gout. The Duke goes to Brighton to-morrow. We dine with

[045-183]him on Thursday. Cabinets will not begin till next week.

[045-184]_September 29._

[045-185]No news in the newspaper from Brussels. No dispatches from Sir Ch. Bagot or

[045-186]Mr. Cartwright arrived at the office; but a gentleman who left Brussels at

[045-187]five on Sunday reports that they were then fighting in the town, but the

[045-188]troops had the worst of it.
