[005-001]The King, our master, is the weakest man in England. He hates the Duke of

[005-002]Cumberland. He wishes his death. He is relieved when he is away; but he is

[005-003]afraid of him, and crouches to him.

[005-004]In reality the King never was better satisfied than with his present

[005-005]Ministers. He knows they will not flinch--that he is safe in their hands.

[005-006]_May 22._

[005-007]In the House Lord Melville presented the petition of the City of London

[005-008]praying, if the House persisted in ordering the production of their

[005-009]accounts of property other than of a public nature, to be heard at the bar

[005-010]by counsel. He moved that this petition should be considered on Tuesday. It

[005-011]being expected that on Monday these very accounts would be produced in the

[005-012]committee, and thus the order of the House rendered unnecessary. In this we

[005-013]were beaten too. Indeed, our management under Lord Melville as Admiral does

[005-014]not answer.

[005-015]We shall certainly lose the London Bridge Approaches Bill.

[005-016]Dined at Lord Hill's. A party chiefly military.

[005-017]_May 24._

[005-018]Cabinet at Peel's at 11 P.M.

[005-019]The arrangements determined upon. Lord E. Somerset to have Sir W. Clinton's

[005-020]office, and Trench Mr. Singleton's. Lord Rosslyn the Privy Seal. Lord

[005-021]Chandos was proposed, I should rather say suggested, but rejected

[005-022]immediately, as not of sufficient calibre for the Cabinet. Besides, his

[005-023]elevation for the purpose of holding the Privy Seal would offend the

[005-024]peerage, and be an insult to his father. It would not gain us the

[005-025]Brunswickers, and we should have the Whigs hostile. It would be saying to

[005-026]them, 'You shall never come in.'

[005-027]Rosslyn's appointment will be most useful. He will be of value in the

[005-028]Cabinet and invaluable in the House. His accession will break the Whigs, he

[005-029]is so popular with everybody.

[005-030]This is to be proposed to the King to-morrow. It is thought he will take no

[005-031]step without asking the Duke of Cumberland. He may refuse altogether. Then

[005-032]we go out. The legal arrangements cannot proceed, because Best [Footnote:

[005-033]Afterwards Lord Wynford.] communicated with the Duke of Cumberland and

[005-034]refused a peerage as the _condition_ of resignation. Alexander would go if

[005-035]he could have his peerage and a pension. Leach will not go unless he is to

[005-036]have a peerage and a pension of 7,000£ a year, a thing impossible.

[005-037]_May 25._

[005-038]Cabinet at 3. Waited a long time for the Duke. He came smiling and

[005-039]victorious. The King said he would manage Best. To Rosslyn he made some

[005-040]objection, and suggested Lord Dudley or Melbourne. This was referred to and

[005-041]rejected by such of the Cabinet as could be on a sudden collected at the

[005-042]Foreign Office. I was not there. I should certainly have rejected both,

[005-043]although very willing to have Dudley. The other would never have done. With

[005-044]Lord E. Somerset and Trench the King was well pleased. As the Duke left the

[005-045]room the King said, 'Come, you must acknowledge I have behaved well to

[005-046]you.' This he said frankly and good-humouredly. The Duke said, 'I assure

[005-047]your Majesty I am very sensible of it, and I feel very grateful to you.'

[005-048]Having thus established ourselves as a Government we were going to break

[005-049]our necks by attempting to pass the Chancellor's Bill, which the House of

[005-050]Commons does not like. However, after a talk, it was resolved to give it

[005-051]up.

[005-052]It seems the Tories have deserted us again. We are much in want of winter

[005-053]quarters.

[005-054]In the House we had the City of London petition. I took a more active part

[005-055]than usual in the conversation.

[005-056]Lord Rosslyn, having just lost his son, is gone to Tunbridge Wells, and the

[005-057]offer of the Privy Seal will be postponed till after to-morrow, when the

[005-058]King is to see Best at two, and it is hoped the Duke may be able to tell

[005-059]Rosslyn that Scarlett is to be Attorney-General.

[005-060]_May 26._

[005-061]The King sent Knighton for Chief Justice Best, and desired him not to tell

[005-062]the Duke of Cumberland; Best was sent for. So Best went, and accepted the

[005-063]terms offered. Thus we shall get Scarlett, and the King and the Duke be

[005-064]separated a little.

[005-065]Yesterday the Duke of Wellington did his business with the King while the

[005-066]Duke of Cumberland was hearing a clause in the House of Lords. The

[005-067]Chancellor, knowing how the Duke of Wellington was occupied, kept the Duke

[005-068]of Cumberland as long as he could.

[005-069]_May 27._

[005-070]Committee on London Bridge. Lord Londonderry, who came from the review in

[005-071]his uniform just covered by a frock coat, spoke against time on a

[005-072]collateral point for an hour and a half, and disgusted the Committee.

[005-073]_May 28._

[005-074]London Bridge Committee. Lord Londonderry a little better than before, but

[005-075]not much. He is running down his character altogether. He has now formed an

[005-076]alliance with the Duke of Cumberland, and through him made his peace with

[005-077]the King. The Duke of Cumberland wishes to be reconciled to the Duke of

[005-078]Wellington. In the House of Commons there is a small Ultra-Tory party, not

[005-079]fifty. In our House I doubt whether there are twenty.

[005-080]_May 30._

[005-081]Chairs. Lord W. Bentinck seems to be so ill as to make it doubtful whether

[005-082]he can remain in India should he recover. The letter is dated January 27.

[005-083]He was then in danger. The vessel did not leave Calcutta till the 30th. The

[005-084]news then was that he was better, and had sat up for six hours. It was a

[005-085]_coup de soleil_.

[005-086]London Bridge Committee.

[005-087]The Duke showed me a letter from Lord Rosslyn, accepting most cordially the

[005-088]Privy Seal.

[005-089]I suppose we shall have a Council on Monday, or on some early day next

[005-090]week, for me to give it up.

[005-091]_June 1._

[005-092]To the Cabinet room.

[005-093]There is a report that Varna [Footnote: Varna was in the hands of the

[005-094]Russians, having been taken in the previous campaign.] is _cernée_ by

[005-095]40,000 men, Bazardjik taken, the Russians running from Karasan, and from

[005-096]6,000 to 8,000 Russians, who had been thrown over the Danube at Hirsova,

[005-097]driven into it at Czernavoda by the garrison of Silistria. [Footnote: These

[005-098]reports seem to have been unfounded. Soon after this date the decisive

[005-099]battle of Kouleftcha opened to the Russians the road to Adrianople.]

[005-100]Clanwilliam wrote me he thought the Duke attached some credit to this last

[005-101]rumour.

[005-102]News from Calcutta of February 1 states that Lord William Bentinck was then

[005-103]out of danger. Lady William, who was going to set off to join him, had

[005-104]determined to expect him at Calcutta.

[005-105]Lord Rosslyn's appointment is in the newspapers to-day. The 'Times' highly

[005-106]delighted.

[005-107]_June 2._

[005-108]London Bridge Approaches Committee. Lord Londonderry very anxious to have

[005-109]an adjournment over the Derby; however, he must attend to 'the last

[005-110]concern.'

[005-111]House. Anatomy Bill put off till Friday. The Bishops, Lord Malmesbury, and

[005-112]many others very hostile to it.

[005-113]It seems certain that the Russians have recrossed the Danube. I am inclined

[005-114]to think they have been beaten.

[005-115]_June 3._

[005-116]The Bishop of Oxford is dead; a great Grecian is to succeed him.

[005-117]The King is in excellent humour. The Duke of Cumberland rather going down.

[005-118]We had some talk about the Anatomy Bill. The Duke is afraid of passing it.

[005-119]Indeed, it is not a Government measure. Probably it will be withdrawn for

[005-120]the year. The Bishops are very hostile to it.

[005-121]_June 4._

[005-122]London Bridge Committee from eleven till four. We made great progress in

[005-123]our evidence, and, indeed, nearly proved our case. From four to five we had

[005-124]a very painful discussion in consequence of some words which passed between

[005-125]Lord Durham and Lord Beresford. We succeeded at last in settling the

[005-126]difference.

[005-127]Lord Beresford, having no good word at his disposal, said he did not second

[005-128]the _evil deeds_ or _improprieties_ of noble lords. He really meant

[005-129]_irregularities_, and irregularities only as a member of the Committee.

[005-130]Lord Grey was present and much distressed. The Duke of Wellington's

[005-131]authority induced both to become amenable to the wish of the Committee.

[005-132]_June 5._

[005-133]Anatomy Bill. Some talk; but a general agreement suggested by the

[005-134]Archbishop of Canterbury, that the Bill should be read a second time, and

[005-135]not proceeded with this session. The Duke of Wellington expressed his

[005-136]general approbation of the principle, but thought postponement desirable.

[005-137]He pledged himself to _cooperate_ in bringing in a Bill on the same

[005-138]principle, and having the same objects, next year; but did not pledge

[005-139]himself to bring it in himself.

[005-140]_June 7._

[005-141]Cabinet at half-past three. First question: whether we should extend the

[005-142]time for putting an end altogether to the Brazilian slave trade from March

[005-143]13 to September 13, 1830, for the equivalent of obtaining for ever the

[005-144]right to seize ships fitted up for the slave trade, whether they had slaves

[005-145]on board or not. The Brazilians have been encouraged by their Government to

[005-146]interpret the treaty as permitting the return of any vessels quitting the

[005-147]Brazils on slave expeditions before March 13.

[005-148]Dr. Lushington, who was consulted by Aberdeen, seemed to think it was worth

[005-149]while to obtain the concession, but still seemed to think that by extending

[005-150]the time, we should permit the transportation of a very large number of

[005-151]slaves, of whom many might be destroyed by ill-treatment, and that it was

[005-152]hardly justifiable with a view to a distant advantage, to sacrifice

[005-153]immediately and certainly a great number of persons.

[005-154]This prevailed--the real fact being that Peel does not like awkward

[005-155]questions in the House of Commons.

[005-156]So the treaty remains as it is, and both parties will interpret it as they

[005-157]please. There will be many disputes, for the interpretation is very

[005-158]different.

[005-159]_June 8._

[005-160]Received a private letter from Colonel Macdonald at Tabriz, with copies of

[005-161]letters received by him from a gentleman he had sent to Teheran on hearing

[005-162]of the massacre of the Russian mission; and from another gentleman,

[005-163]travelling unofficially, who first heard the report between Tabriz and

[005-164]Kamsin.

[005-165]These accounts only confirm what we had already heard of the arrogance and

[005-166]violence of the Russians. They deserved their fate.

[005-167]Colonel Macdonald says that General Paskewitz cannot dispose of more than

[005-168]25,000, or, at most, 30,000 men, although he has a nominal force of 110,000

[005-169]men under his command.

[005-170]Colonel Macdonald says there has been no serious resistance on the part of

[005-171]the Turks, except at Akhalsik.

[005-172]He has done what he can to dissuade them from war with the Russians; but I

[005-173]think the universal feeling of the people will propel them.

[005-174]The insurrection at Teheran appears to have been instigated by the Mollahs

[005-175]and the women, but it was evidently national, or it must have failed.

[005-176]_June 10._

[005-177]Council. Lord Winford kissed hands. He walked in with great difficulty on

[005-178]two crutches, which he placed behind him and so leant back upon. The King

[005-179]had a chair brought for him, and had him wheeled out. The man who pushed

[005-180]his chair very nearly shipwrecked him at the door.

[005-181]The Attorney-General (Scarlett), [Footnote: Afterwards Lord Abinger.] the

[005-182]Chief Justice of the Common Pleas (Tindal), and the Solicitor-General

[005-183](Sugden), [Footnote: Afterwards Lord St. Leonards. Lord Chancellor 1862. ]

[005-184]all kissed hands. The Chief Justice of the Common Pleas was sworn in as

[005-185]Privy Councillor. Lord Rosslyn was sworn in as Privy Councillor and Privy

[005-186]Seal. The King did not address a word to me, who gave up the seal, or to

[005-187]Rosslyn, who received it.

[005-188]House. Nothing of moment.

[005-189]Dinner at Lord Bathurst's. Lord Rosslyn dined here.

[005-190]Aberdeen read a paper lately received from the Russians, in which they

[005-191]concede all we ask about blockades, &c., except as to the Gulf of Enos. The

[005-192]Duke says he shall bring Lièven to the point about this, and generally

[005-193]about their views. He feels the Government is stronger now than it was--

[005-194]that the country is stronger, and we may insist more. He says the question

[005-195]is, 'Shall we permit the ruin of the Turkish Empire?' I have long felt that

[005-196]to be the case, and to that I answer 'No.'
