**George Finch-Hatton, 10th Earl of Winchilsea** (1791–1858), styled Earl of Winchilsea and Nottingham, was a British peer and politician. An Ultra-Tory, he was one of the most outspoken opponents of Catholic emancipation in the [[House of Lords]].

== Ultra-Tory Opposition ==

Winchelsea was a leading figure among the Ultra-Tories who broke with the [[Duke of Wellington|Wellington]] ministry over Catholic emancipation in 1829. The diary lists him among those peers "lost to the Government" (001-038) as a result of the emancipation crisis.

His opposition was vehement. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that "Lord Winchelsea was very mad, wished to expel the bishops, to prevent" (P01-160) — indicating the intensity of his anti-Catholic feeling and his willingness to take extreme positions in parliamentary debate.

== Political Alignments ==

Winchelsea is listed alongside other prominent Tory peers: "Malmesbury, Winchelsea, and Clanricarde" (P01-156) in the diary's survey of the political landscape. He was counted among the peers voting against the government alongside "Rolle, Howe, O'Neil, Bexley, Winchelsea, Farnham, and six bishops." (001-005)

Despite his opposition to Wellington's policies, Winchelsea maintained connections across the political spectrum. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that the King "was delighted with Lord Winchelsea. He was" (001-160) — suggesting that even the monarch found Winchelsea's forceful personality engaging, if not his politics.

== The Kentish Connection ==

Winchelsea's political base was in Kent, where he was a major landowner. The diary notes that "Lord Winchelsea and [[Edward Knatchbull|Sir Edward Knatchbull]] have been here at every" (048-012) opportunity, indicating his regular attendance and engagement with county politics.

During the debate on the [[Reform Bill]], [[Lord Lansdowne]] made a speech on the second reading "and Lord Winchelsea" (033-120) was among those responding — a sign of his continued engagement with the great constitutional questions of the day.
