Sir George Murray (1772–1846) was a British Army officer and Tory politician who served as **Secretary of State for War and the Colonies** in the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s ministry from 1828 to 1830. He was a close colleague of the Duke, having served under him during the Peninsular War as Quartermaster-General.

== Role in the Wellington Ministry ==

Murray was present at Cabinet meetings during the Wellington administration. In the diary of [[Lord Ellenborough]], Murray is recorded as attending the Cabinet room in January 1829, where he read letters relative to alterations in the judicial system of Scotland. (020-020)

== Political Views ==

Murray shared Wellington's concerns about the political landscape. The diary records that after a Cabinet meeting, he "expressed his surprise the Duke should cling to the hope of reclaiming the ultra-Tories, whom he would not get." (023-024) This indicates Murray was pragmatic about the political realities facing the ministry, particularly regarding the [[Tory Party]]'s internal divisions over Catholic emancipation.

Murray was present at a dinner where he, alongside the [[Lord Lyndhurst|Chancellor]], congratulated the diarist on a speech. (023-127) This suggests he was actively engaged in the social-political life of Wellington's government.

== Military Career ==

Before his political career, Murray had a distinguished military record. He served under Wellington in the Peninsular War (1808–1814) as Quartermaster-General, a role that made him responsible for the army's logistics and movements. His military experience informed his work at the [[British Cabinet|Colonial Office]], where he oversaw army administration across the British Empire.

== Later Life ==

Murray continued in office until the fall of the Wellington ministry in November 1830. He later served as Master-General of the Ordnance in [[Robert Peel]]'s first government (1834–1835).
