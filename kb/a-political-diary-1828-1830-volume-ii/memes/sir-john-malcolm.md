**Sir John Malcolm** (1769–1833) was a British soldier, diplomat, and colonial administrator. He served as Governor of Bombay and was a prolific writer on Indian affairs. The diary records extensive correspondence between Malcolm and the diarist, [[Edward Law, 1st Earl of Ellenborough]], who was then President of the Board of Control.

== Indian Administration ==

Malcolm's letters to the diarist covered a wide range of Indian policy matters. He wrote two letters to Sir Pulteney Malcolm on naval and administrative coordination. (002-045) His correspondence with the diarist repeatedly addressed the governance of the Indian territories and the challenges of colonial administration.

== Resignation and Views ==

The diary records a significant moment: "they had just got a letter from Sir John Malcolm, resigning." (006-135) This resignation was part of the turbulent administrative politics of British India. Malcolm's views were described as distinctive — "different from Sir J. Malcolm's — the latter wishing to make..." (014-170) — suggesting he held independent opinions on colonial policy.

== Policy Debates ==

Malcolm was involved in discussions about the village system of land tenure in India. The diarist wrote to Sir J. Malcolm on the village system, (018-029) and Malcolm's own letters "deprecate the..." (008-117) certain policies under consideration. He also "speaks of an intended deputation" (017-152) concerning Indian affairs and "seems to think that Lord William..." (017-154) had particular views on the matter.

Malcolm's career was described as "the most romantic and th[orough]" (008-180), reflecting his reputation as one of the ablest administrators in British India. He was later suggested as "provisional successor to L[ord]" (017-160), indicating the high regard in which he was held. The diarist even "suggested to him Sir J. Malcolm's being made provisional" (018-050) to the Duke, though he also noted concern that "Sir J. Malcolm would die if he went to" (018-055) the demanding post.
