**George Canning** (1770–1827) was a British Tory statesman who served as Foreign Secretary and, briefly, as Prime Minister in 1827 before his death. Though he died before the diary's main period, his political legacy—the Canningite faction—remained a significant force in the parliamentary manoeuvring of 1828–1830.

== The Canningite Faction ==

After Canning's death, his followers formed a distinct political group that held the balance of power between the Ultra-Tories and the Whigs. The diary notes the composition of parliamentary forces: the government drew support from "the Ultra-Tories, the Canningites, and some Whigs." (003-057) This coalition was inherently unstable, and the Canningites were a pivotal swing group.

The Canningites' voting strength was demonstrated in key divisions. On one occasion, "The Whigs, Brunswickers, and Canningites were in the minority" against the government. (026-086) Their alignment was fluid: "the Whigs, Canningites, and Tories" could form shifting alliances depending on the issue. (032-039) In another division, "Richmond, Calthorpe, all the Canningites, of course" voted together. (033-177)

== Diplomatic Legacy ==

Canning's foreign policy legacy, particularly his support for liberal constitutionalism and the recognition of new states, continued to influence British diplomacy after his death. The diary reflects on his earlier political alignment, noting a "junction with Canning in 1827" as a reference point for political loyalties. (015-122)

Lord Ellenborough observed the persistence of what he called "the Canning venom" in political society, noting its presence at a gathering at Lady Jersey's. (001-070) This phrase captures the diarist's view of Canning's influence as a lingering, divisive force.

== Posthumous Reputation ==

Even years after his death, Canning's memory remained contested. The diary records that a biography was being prepared: "The Political Life of George Canning" was in progress, along with another work on "George Canning and his Times." (019-165) Lady Canning herself was active in defending her husband's reputation, and the diarist notes that "Lady Canning thinks she can injure the" government's standing. (019-159)
