# Lord Dalhousie

## Overview

George Ramsay, 9th Earl of Dalhousie, served as Commander-in-Chief in India during the late 1820s, a period when Indian military and political affairs featured regularly in [[british-cabinet|Cabinet]] discussions. The diary records both the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s assessment of his capabilities and growing concerns about his health and effectiveness.

## Role in the Diary

Dalhousie appears in the diary primarily through the lens of Indian governance. He was present at a gathering alongside the Duke of Wellington and Sir Sidney Beckwith (006-164, 006-168), suggesting his place in the military-administrative circle. Wellington's evaluation of military candidates for Indian command notes the Duke's belief that Dalhousie "is a better" choice than another candidate (046-091).

Concerns about his administrative performance surface in the same period. Ellenborough records receiving letters from Lord William Bentinck, the Governor-General, and notes pointedly that "Lord Dalhousie has been very" active — the entry truncated, but the context suggests dissatisfaction (045-023). The critique was significant enough that it formed part of the Cabinet's Indian discussions.

His health became a serious matter. A brief but telling entry records that Dalhousie "was so ill on" a specific occasion that it disrupted official proceedings, including the taking of oaths (049-172). Earlier notes suggest Wellington expected "very uncertain" outcomes regarding his continued service (025-066).

## Key Appearances

- **Indian military command**: Evaluated by Wellington as a candidate for command responsibilities in India (046-091).
- **Bentinck correspondence**: His conduct was referenced in despatches from the Governor-General (045-023).
- **Health concerns**: Illness became a factor affecting his official duties (025-066, 049-172).
- **Duke's circle**: Present at gatherings with Wellington and senior military figures (006-164, 006-168).
