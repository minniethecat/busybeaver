**Prince Frederick of Orange** (1797–1881) was the second son of [[William I of the Netherlands]] and a Dutch military commander. The diary records his central role during the [[Belgian Revolution]] of 1830, when he led forces against the insurgent southern provinces.

== The Greek Throne Question ==

Before the Belgian crisis, Prince Frederick's name arose in diplomatic discussions about the Greek throne. The French objected to him as a candidate: "They equally object to Prince Frederick of Orange, and to Prince Leopold, whom Russia would have had willingly." (015-147) The diarist noted wryly: "I wonder Aberdeen did not laugh when he was proposed." (015-148)

== The Belgian Revolution ==

When the Belgian provinces rose in revolt in 1830, Prince Frederick took command of Dutch forces. He established his position at Vilvorde, north of Brussels: "5,000 men are close at hand under Prince Frederick of Orange, at Vilvorde, and two bodies of 10,000 each are marching upon the same point." (043-106)

On the 20th or 21st of the month, he issued a proclamation at Antwerp "noticing the excesses of the populace, and announcing that the troops would relieve the burgher guard." (045-077) This intervention marked an escalation of the Dutch military response.

== Earlier Diplomacy ==

The diary records an earlier diplomatic episode: "The proposition was made by us to Prince Frederick of Orange on November 13." (021-010) This related to British mediation efforts in European affairs, though the precise nature of the proposition remains unclear from the diary text alone.
