**Lord William Cavendish-Bentinck** (1774–1839) was a British soldier and statesman who served as **Governor-General of India** from 1828 to 1835. His administration in India was a frequent subject of discussion in the Wellington Cabinet, as documented in [[Lord Ellenborough]]'s diary.

== Appointment and Early Administration ==

News of Bentinck's arrival and early actions in India reached London by February 1829. The diary records that "News from Calcutta of February 1 states that Lord William Bentinck was then..." beginning his administration. (005-102) As President of the [[East India Company|Board of Control]], Ellenborough was directly involved in overseeing Bentinck's actions.

Bentinck's administration was regarded by some in India as hostile to the interests of those who had served under his predecessors. The diary notes the military's particular discontent with his reforms. (017-049)

== Correspondence with Ellenborough ==

Ellenborough maintained direct correspondence with Bentinck. The diary records: "Wrote a long letter to Lord William Bentinck. I pressed upon him the..." importance of certain administrative priorities. (019-039) A letter from Bentinck was later shown to Ellenborough, demonstrating the reciprocal nature of their official correspondence. (025-058)

== Challenges in India ==

Bentinck faced significant challenges during his governorship. The diary reveals concerns about the temper of the Madras army and Bentinck's handling of the press in India. His administration's economy measures—particularly the reduction of military allowances known as "Half-Batta"—provoked strong opposition.

By 1830, the Wellington Cabinet was actively discussing Bentinck's potential recall. Ellenborough proposed making Lord William Bentinck "provisional successor" to the Commander-in-Chief in India, suggesting some ambivalence about his continued service. (046-094)

== Reforms ==

Despite the controversies, Bentinck's governorship is remembered for significant reforms, including the suppression of _thuggee_ (ritual murder), the abolition of _sati_ (widow burning), and the promotion of English education in India.
