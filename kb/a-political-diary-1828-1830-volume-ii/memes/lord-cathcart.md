# Lord Cathcart

## Overview

William Cathcart, 1st Earl Cathcart (1755–1843), was a senior British Army general and diplomat who had served with distinction in the Napoleonic Wars and later as ambassador to Russia. By 1828–1830, he was an elderly figure, holding the honorary post of Colonel of the Horse Guards but absent from his ceremonial duties.

## Role in the Diary

Lord Cathcart appears in Ellenborough's diary through his absence. Describing the Horse Guards colonels' duties, Ellenborough notes that one colonel "is the only colonel of the Horse Guards who ever does duty — Lord Cathcart being absent and Lord Harrington incapable" (003-078).

The context concerns the gold stick, a ceremonial office of the Household Cavalry. The active colonel, having "last got the gold stick from Lord Harrington," swore he "would never let it out of his hands" and used his authority to close the gates of the Horse Guards on Drawing-room day (003-078). This assertion of authority highlighted the vacuum left by Cathcart's absence.

## Key Appearances

- **Absent colonel**: Noted as the Colonel of the Horse Guards who "never does duty," leaving ceremonial responsibilities to others (003-078).
