# Lord Harrington

## Overview

Charles Stanhope, 3rd Earl of Harrington, was a British general and court official who held the ceremonial post of Gold Stick. In Ellenborough''s diary of 1828, he appears in connection with a dispute involving the Duke of Cumberland over access to the House of Lords and the management of royal ceremonies.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records an episode in which the [[Duke of Cumberland]] was angered by the admission of ladies to the House of Lords: "He is very angry at ladies being admitted to the House of Lords, and particularly at their going in such numbers the day the Duke of Norfolk took his seat" (003-073–075). The Duke had "sworn he will not leave England till he has turned out the present Ministers" (003-076–077).

In the same passage, Ellenborough notes that the Duke was "the only colonel of the Horse Guards who ever does duty — Lord Cathcart being absent and Lord Harrington incapable" (003-078). The Duke had "last got the gold stick from Lord Harrington he swore he would never let it out of his hands" (003-079). As Gold Stick, the Duke then "ordered the gates of the Horse Guards to be closed the day of the Drawing-room, and thus obliged all the Ministers who dressed in Downing Street to go all round" (003-080–082), causing disruption to government business.

## Key Appearances

- **Gold Stick transfer**: Lord Harrington was relieved of the Gold Stick by the Duke of Cumberland, who thereafter refused to surrender it and used the authority to inconvenience ministers (003-078–082).
