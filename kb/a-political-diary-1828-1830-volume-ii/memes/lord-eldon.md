# Lord Eldon

## Overview

Lord Eldon (John Scott, 1st Earl of Eldon) served as Lord Chancellor of Great Britain for most of the period between 1801 and 1827, making him one of the longest-serving holders of that office. A staunch [[tory-party|Tory]] and defender of the established order, he was a leading opponent of Catholic emancipation and legal reform in the [[british-cabinet|Cabinet]] and the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]].

## Role in the Diary

By the time of Ellenborough's diary (1828–1830), Lord Eldon was in opposition, having left office with Lord Liverpool's administration. He appears as a diminished but still vocal figure, frequently speaking in the House of Lords against the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government. The diary records him as "quite beaten" by political reversals (001-001), yet still delivering long speeches "containing no argument" (001-027).

Eldon's theological interests are noted: he and the Bishops "got into a theological discussion" at one gathering (P01-174), and he provoked "a strong attack" from the Lord Chancellor for speaking poorly in reply (P01-175).

## Key Appearances

- **Catholic Emancipation debate**: Eldon opened discussions afresh with others (P01-172) and mounted opposition alongside Lord Redesdale and Lord Holland (004-174).
- **Judicial role**: He was sent for by the King through Cumberland's influence to provide counsel during a political crisis (034-025).
- **Relationship with Wellington**: The Duke "soothed Lord Eldon by high compliments" when introducing his policy plan (004-080), though their relationship remained strained.
- **Final observations**: The diary remarks on the irony "that Lord Eldon should have lived to hear a Chancellor" propose reforms he long resisted (004-082).
