# Duke of Orleans

## Overview

Louis Philippe III, Duke of Orleans, became King of the French in August 1830 after the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution]] overthrew Charles X. His accession created a diplomatic challenge for the British government, as recorded extensively in Ellenborough's diary during the latter months of 1830. The [[Duke of Wellington]]'s cabinet debated whether to recognise the new French regime and how to manage relations with the Orleanist monarchy.

## Role in the Revolution of 1830

The Duke of Orleans was central to the political upheaval in France. Aberdeen noted that he had "seen it in the" newspapers that Orleans was proclaimed King. (020-125) The proclamation occurred rapidly: the Chamber of Deputies "proclaimed the Duke of Orleans King of the French; at least has offered him" the crown. (041-164)

Ellenborough recorded that "they had heard the Duke of Orleans was" active in political manoeuvring, and after Charles X's abdication, "the Duke of Orleans accepted the office of Lieutenant-General of the Kingdom." (040-175) Several factors pushed him toward the throne: "This, it is said, determined the Duke of Orleans to accept" the crown offered by the Chamber. (041-020)

## British Diplomatic Response

The British government closely followed Orleans' actions. The Duke of Wellington spoke of the Duke of Orleans' potential impact on European stability. (041-029) When Orleans "asked Lord Stuart's advice as to accepting the Crown," (041-034) it signalled his willingness to engage with British diplomacy. The Cabinet discussed whether to acknowledge the new King: "The King of the French will be acknowledged." (042-089) A formal letter was sent "to the King of the French, almost insinuating that the" British government expected certain assurances. (049-025)

Ellenborough himself drew a parallel between the French Duke of Orleans and a domestic royal: "Our Duke of Orleans, as I call him, the Duke of Sussex, sticks close to the" King. (041-090)
