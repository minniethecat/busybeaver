**George IV** (1762–1830) was King of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland from 1820 until his death in 1830. As the reigning monarch throughout the period covered by [[Lord Ellenborough]]'s diary, he is a constant if often indirect presence — the sovereign to whom ministers reported, whose consent was required for appointments, and whose political instincts shaped the course of government.

== The King and His Ministers ==

The diary provides a detailed portrait of the King's relationship with the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s ministry. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that "the Duke of Wellington wrote to the King to ask if he had any objection" (P01-018) to proposed measures, and that "the Duke has written to the King, acquainting him with the plan" (001-010) — the regular rhythm of ministerial communication with the sovereign.

The King could be a difficult master. The diary notes tension at Windsor, where "the King fancies he is in the" (P01-168) grip of mischief-makers, and records the delicate task of managing royal sensibilities: "to get the King to consent to give the" (P01-166) necessary approvals. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that "this will quiet Windsor" (P01-120) — a terse observation on the need to manage the King's court.

== Catholic Emancipation ==

The King's role in the Catholic emancipation crisis of 1829 was pivotal and personally painful. Having initially resisted emancipation as a violation of his coronation oath, George IV was ultimately persuaded by Wellington and [[Robert Peel]] to give his assent. The diary captures the uncertainty of this period: "If the King will not take Peel's advice" (001-016), the consequences could be severe. Yet [[Lord Ellenborough]] records that "the Duke thinks the King will yield" (001-017), and noted with relief that he "had _no doubt_ the King would take his advice" (001-022).

The political cost was considerable. The King's assent alienated the Ultra-Tory faction that had been his natural allies, and the diary records the ripples of this breach throughout the peerage.

== Court Life and Political Influence ==

George IV maintained an active court at Windsor, receiving ministers and foreign dignitaries. The diary notes "the King was to have received a number" (P01-120) of visitors, and records audiences where "His Majesty had expressed himself very" (030-115) forcefully. The King's political influence, though constitutionally limited, remained significant through his power to encourage, discourage, and withhold consent from his ministers.

[[Lord Ellenborough]] records a telling glimpse of the King's character: the King "was delighted with Lord Winchelsea. He was" (001-160) — even though Winchelsea was among the Ultra-Tory opponents of the King's own government. This suggests a monarch who could separate personal charm from political alignment.

== Death ==

The diary notes the King's passing with characteristic brevity: "And so King George IV is gone to" (038-100). His death in June 1830 brought his brother, the Duke of Clarence, to the throne as William IV, and precipitated a general election that would transform the political landscape.
