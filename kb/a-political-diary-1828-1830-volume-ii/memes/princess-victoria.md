# Princess Victoria

## Overview

Princess Alexandrina Victoria of Kent (1819–1901) was the only daughter of Prince Edward, Duke of Kent and Strathearn, and Princess Victoria of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld. At the time of Ellenborough's diary, she was the heir presumptive to the throne. She appears in the diary during 1830, when the government grappled with questions of her education and the terms of a potential regency during her minority.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records reading "all the papers relating to the education of the Princess Victoria" (048-098), reflecting the government's close attention to her upbringing as the future sovereign. The question of who should oversee her education was a matter of political significance.

The diary also captures the arrangements for a regency. A provision was discussed naming guardians "for the Princess Victoria, with the provision that on" her reaching the age of majority, she would assume full royal authority (049-133). In a subsequent entry, Ellenborough records the decision on the Regency Bill: "Decided that the Princess Victoria shall be considered Queen" (053-101), signalling the government's determination to avoid any ambiguity about her succession rights.

## Key Appearances

- **Education papers**: Ellenborough reviewed the full dossier of papers relating to the Princess's education (048-098).
- **Regency guardians**: The government deliberated on who should be named as guardians during any potential regency (049-133).
- **Succession secured**: The Regency Bill affirmed that the Princess would be considered Queen in her own right (053-101).
