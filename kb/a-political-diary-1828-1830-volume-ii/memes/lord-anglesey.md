# Lord Anglesey

## Overview

Lord Anglesey (Henry William Paget, 1st Marquess of Anglesey) was a distinguished cavalry commander who lost a leg at Waterloo before serving as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland (1828–1829). His tenure in Ireland became a major political controversy when his correspondence with the [[british-cabinet|Cabinet]] over Catholic emancipation policy led to a public rift with the [[Duke of Wellington]].

## Role in the Diary

The diary extensively documents the "Anglesey affair" — a chain of disputed letters and contradictory statements that consumed the government in early 1828. Ellenborough records reading "correspondence between the Duke and Lord Anglesey" (002-139), followed by many of Anglesey's "letters to Peel and of Peel's to him" (003-103).

The core dispute centred on whether Anglesey had the King's permission to read certain letters publicly. Anglesey "stated that he had the King's permission" (003-126), yet the Duke's account differed. When Curtis was examined, it emerged he had "told the Duke one thing and Lord Anglesey another" (003-137), creating an irreconcilable conflict.

Anglesey "brought forward his motion" (003-120) and "treated the contradiction as not proven" when the Duke replied (003-132). The affair exposed deep mistrust between the Dublin Castle administration and the London government.

## Key Appearances

- **Correspondence controversy (Chapter 3)**: The central figure in a multi-day parliamentary examination of letters between Dublin and London.
- **Catholic question**: His liberal sympathies towards Irish Catholics put him at odds with the more cautious Wellington-Peel approach.
- **Recall from Ireland**: The diary traces his deteriorating relationship with the government and eventual recall.
