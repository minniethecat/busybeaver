[050-001]_October 27._

[050-002]_Levée_ at two. Addresses from the Church of Scotland, and the Lord Mayor

[050-003]and Corporation of Dublin. Dr. Chalmers was with the Church of Scotland.

[050-004]The Recorder of Dublin, Mr. Shaw, who is member for Dublin, made a speech

[050-005]before he read the address--a thing quite unprecedented, and which might be

[050-006]very inconvenient. The speech itself was innocent. The _levée_ by no means

[050-007]full.

[050-008]Peel had an audience of the King, and in half an hour the King slept twenty

[050-009]minutes. He says he never knew any man so much altered in three months. His

[050-010]somnolency increases. He slept during an interview with Aberdeen yesterday.

[050-011]When the Duke saw him he was alive enough.

[050-012]Cabinet. Prince at the Chancellor's. Some conversation respecting the

[050-013]burnings in Kent. Peel thinks they were effected by a chemical process, by

[050-014]some substance deposited hours before, and igniting when the perpetrators

[050-015]are far off. The persons who met Lord Winchilsea expressed detestation of

[050-016]the burnings, and went away to break threshing machines, but a man who

[050-017]committed persons for breaking threshing machines had his ricks burnt;

[050-018]another suffered the same thing who defended his threshing machines. I

[050-019]believe the two offences to be committed by the same persons. The

[050-020]magistrates are supine and terror-struck; but they have no police, no

[050-021]military. Sir E. Knatchbull doubts whether they would arm as yeomen. Peel

[050-022]does not seem to me to view with sufficient alarm the effect these burnings

[050-023]will produce upon men's minds, and the example of impunity. Nothing was

[050-024]said about Manchester. All seemed to think less seriously of our dangers

[050-025]than they did some days back.

[050-026]The law officers mean to give in their report on the case put to them to-

[050-027]morrow. They will say it is not provided for. The Chancellor has the judges

[050-028]at dinner on Friday, and he will then obtain theirs.

[050-029]_October 28._

[050-030]Captain Harvey of the 4th Dragoons called by the King's desire to say the

[050-031]King of Persia told him when he was at Teheran that he was hurt at not

[050-032]receiving a letter from the King. I told Captain Harvey the King had

[050-033]announced his accession to the Shah of Persia as he had to other

[050-034]sovereigns. Captain Harvey was interpreter to his regiment. It seemed to me

[050-035]that he rather wished to command the Persian troops. He is brother to the

[050-036]tutor to Prince George of Cambridge. He is a very gentlemanlike man.

[050-037]The French insist on having the conferences respecting the settlement of

[050-038]Belgium at Paris, if there are to be any regular conferences. They cannot

[050-039]permit Talleyrand to act for them. The French would be jealous of him, &c.

[050-040]We had wished to have the conferences here for the very reason that we

[050-041]thought Talleyrand would do his utmost to have the credit of preserving

[050-042]peace. I see there will be no Congress. The French think that, if they

[050-043]stand still, the fruit will fall into their mouths. The folly of the Prince

[050-044]of Orange will ruin his party in Belgium. The ambition of the Belgians will

[050-045]induce them to attempt to form a separate State, which after much disorder

[050-046]will be found impracticable; and as they will not become Dutch, they _will_

[050-047]become French. Then we shall have a war, and present forbearance only

[050-048]postpones it. All the Volunteers who are acting in Belgium are French. All

[050-049]the forces in the field are commanded by Frenchmen. French money is

[050-050]employed. The French are really now carrying on the war covertly.

[050-051]Russia is paralysed by the devastating progress of the cholera morbus which

[050-052]has reached Moscow. The Emperor is gone to Moscow to establish order and

[050-053]obedience, for the civil and military authorities are quarrelling, and the

[050-054]troops are unwilling to form the cordon. All cordons I believe to be

[050-055]fruitless. It would be as wise to form a cordon against the wind. The

[050-056]disease advances, however, along the high roads and navigable rivers. It is

[050-057]the most extraordinary plague we have had.

[050-058]Prussia cannot act for fear of disorders at home, and Austria is literally

[050-059]the only power to which war is possible. The French dare not go to war for

[050-060]fear of a Republic.

[050-061]It seems the French Ministry will be partially changed, the Due de Broglie

[050-062]and Guizot going out. The Due de Broglie seems to be a pedantic coxcomb.

[050-063]I pity the King of the Netherlands, who is a good man. To be hated by two-

[050-064]thirds of his subjects, betrayed by his foolish son, and abandoned as he

[050-065]thinks by his allies, must be great trials to him; while, although the

[050-066]Dutch adore him and really love him, they will not give him money, and I

[050-067]have a little doubt whether they will fight much. Probably, however, the

[050-068]fear of pillage will make them do that for themselves.

[050-069]Read a very well-written pamphlet in reply to Brougham's two. I suspect the

[050-070]writer is Philpotts. It is too powerful for an ordinary man, and far beyond

[050-071]Croker. Neither is it in his style. Brougham has made Ridgway put forth a

[050-072]letter stating that he never communicated upon the subject of the pamphlet

[050-073]with Brougham--which is no denial that it is Brougham's.

[050-074]It is a good and useful pamphlet, and will teach the Whigs good manners by

[050-075]showing them they cannot commit aggression with impunity. There is no part

[050-076]much better done than that in which the falsehood and absurdity are shown

[050-077]of what was said in the Brougham pamphlets respecting me. To be sure my

[050-078]champion had a good case. What was said about me rather leads me to think

[050-079]Lord Durham or T. Moore had a hand in it.

[050-080]_October 29._

[050-081]The letters from Manchester recommend resistance on the part of the

[050-082]masters--that is, non-concession. This will put the colliers to the

[050-083]necessity of adopting _force_, and in the defence of property we should

[050-084]commence the contest, which can only be deferred, with great advantage. Mr.

[050-085]Foster thinks the views of the Union have been shaken by the increase of

[050-086]force near Manchester; and that, although there might be much disturbance,

[050-087]the event would not be doubtful. One committee of the Union has proposed

[050-088]acquiescence in the masters' terms.

[050-089]The accounts from Kent are bad. Peel has offered to send down a magistrate

[050-090]and police officers, and to go to any expense.

[050-091]He was to receive Mr. Hammond, Plumptree, Lord Camden, and others to-day.

[050-092]Poor Lord Camden, in the meantime, has the lumbago.

[050-093]_October 30._

[050-094]Cabinet. A very bad account of Manchester. No means of raising Volunteer

[050-095]corps. Little hope of uniting the masters. The operatives triumphant. No

[050-096]disposition, however, on their part to come to blows, and a confidence on

[050-097]the part of the magistrates that a fight would be in their favour; but then

[050-098]they must have _troops_, keep all they have, and get more if possible.

[050-099]Mr. Taylor recommends that constables should have the power of arresting

[050-100]_picketers_ without warrant.

[050-101]Went through the speech. It will do very well now.

[050-102]Spoke to the Duke about Indian finance, and told him the result. He wished

[050-103]to see all the papers, which were not yet quite ready. In the meantime

[050-104]nothing is to be done, and we are to appoint the Committee.

[050-105]The Attorney and Solicitor-General deprecate the prosecution of a libel

[050-106]transmitted for their opinion, and say they think it unadvisable to

[050-107]prosecute without the sanction of Parliament! What this means I do not

[050-108]know, unless it means that they are cowed.

[050-109]There is an infamous article in the _Times_ to-day, against the conduct of

[050-110]the farmers and country gentlemen, and there are worse in the _Morning

[050-111]Chronicle_.

[050-112]Had some conversation after dinner at St. James's with Frankland Lewis. He

[050-113]longs for the Grants. I told him it would not do, and what sort of a man

[050-114]Charles Grant was. Frankland Lewis does not seem to like his office, but he

[050-115]says he shall bring it into order if he remains there, and make it a Privy

[050-116]Councillor's office without drudgery. He and, indeed, all seem to wish they

[050-117]were better and more boldly led in the House of Commons. All we want is

[050-118]that.

[050-119]_October 31._

[050-120]Cabinet. On Monday the 25th the Prince of Orange left Antwerp. He embarked,

[050-121]and intended to go to see his father, and then to come to England! On the

[050-122]26th General Mellinot marched in and went on to Breda, with 5,000 men. On

[050-123]the 27th (there having been a parley on the 26th), the populace attempted

[050-124]to seize the arsenal. The citadel fired. The, town was on fire when Mr.

[050-125]Cartwright came away, and is nearly destroyed.

[050-126]At Maidstone two or three ringleaders were seized very gallantly by the

[050-127]magistrates, and carried off to the gaol by the cavalry at a canter.

[050-128]However, there are but thirty-four troopers there. So four troops have been

[050-129]sent from Windsor, a depot from some other place, and two guns from

[050-130]Woolwich. All this was rendered necessary by an intended meeting on

[050-131]Penenden Heath to-morrow. March, the Solicitor of the Treasury, is gone

[050-132]down.

[050-133]There was much conversation about the state of the Press, and a resolution

[050-134]taken to prosecute, notwithstanding the unwillingness of the law officers.

[050-135]Scarlet appears to be quite cowed by opposition and the Press.

[050-136]This Press may be bought, but we have no money. Five-sixths of the Foreign

[050-137]Secret Service money are preoccupied by permanent old charges--the Secret

[050-138]Service money of the Treasury is preoccupied in the same way.

[050-139]There is a small sum of droits which may be turned over to the Privy Purse,

[050-140]and then by the King to the Government, but it is not more than 3,000£. It

[050-141]is thought that perhaps some of the pensions on the Secret Service money of

[050-142]the Treasury may be turned over to the Foreign Office. The Treasury money

[050-143]is the only money applicable to the purchase of newspapers.

[050-144]We twaddled a great deal over the speech. It was proposed by Peel to insert

[050-145]a paragraph referring to the disturbed state of the country. He will write

[050-146]it, and we shall consider it in a Cabinet at St. James's to-morrow at one,

[050-147]before the Council.

[050-148]Lord Bathurst is more alarmed than any one; but Peel is a good deal alarmed

[050-149]too.

[050-150]There is _danger_, for there are many to attack and few ready to risk

[050-151]anything in defence. It was otherwise in 1793.

[050-152]The Duke thinks that with every disposition to do mischief there is no

[050-153]conspiracy, or we should have heard of it.

[050-154]_November 1._

[050-155]Cabinet at St. James's at one. The Lord-Lieutenant has prohibited, by

[050-156]Proclamation, the meeting of the Volunteer Society. Very properly and

[050-157]consistently. It was a much more dangerous society than the other. He is a

[050-158]firm man, not to be turned from the course he thinks right.

[050-159]O'Connell has not been spoken to in the clubs he has entered. At Brookes's

[050-160]they turned their backs upon him.

[050-161]There was no meeting at Maidstone. Probably they had intimation of the

[050-162]movements of troops. Lord Beresford told me there were 3,000 artillerymen

[050-163]at Woolwich, enough to serve guns for an army.

[050-164]Went through the speech again. Aberdeen is the most obstinate man I ever

[050-165]saw, about the mere _words_ of his part of the speech. We lost half an hour

[050-166]at least in talking about words to-day. Peel read his concluding sentence,

[050-167]which is very good. He laments the outrages, and the attempt to disturb the

[050-168]concord between portions of the empire whose union is essential to their

[050-169]mutual strength and happiness, declares the King's determination to exert

[050-170]the powers confided to him by the Law and the Constitution for the

[050-171]punishment of sedition, and ends by expressing a firm reliance on the

[050-172]loyalty of the great body of the people.

[050-173]As far as I could judge by the King's countenance when the speech was read,

[050-174]he acquiesced, and thought it right, but was pained at being obliged to

[050-175]hold such language.
