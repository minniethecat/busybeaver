# Duchess of Kent

## Overview

Princess Victoria of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld (1786–1861), known after her marriage as the Duchess of Kent, was the widow of [[Prince Edward, Duke of Kent]] and mother of the future [[Queen Victoria]]. During the period of Ellenborough's diary she was a central figure in Regency planning, as the government debated whether she should serve as Regent during the young [[Princess Victoria]]'s minority.

## Role in the Diary

The Duchess of Kent appears in the diary primarily in connection with the Regency question that preoccupied the [[British Cabinet]] during the late 1820s. As the mother of the heir presumptive, she was one of two candidates for the Regency — the other being [[Queen Adelaide]] (030-105). Ellenborough records the political calculation: "All we can do, then, is to give the King the option of choosing the Queen or the Duchess of Kent. He will name the Queen, and she will appoint the Duchess of Kent Regent" (034-123). The diarist notes that [[Leopold I of Belgium]] and the Duchess of Kent were aligned, and that the Duke of Wellington "would prefer the Queen as Regent" (036-110).

Financial matters also feature in the diary. Ellenborough records "a good deal of conversation about the Duchess of Kent's allowance" (049-048), noting that she had borrowed £12,000 on the Duke of Kent's estate (049-051). An earlier entry mentions that Ellenborough "Wrote to the Duchess of Kent telling her a Bengal cavalry cadetship was at her disposal" (015-135), reflecting her role in military patronage.

- **(036-110)** Wellington preferred the Queen as Regent over the Duchess of Kent
- **(049-048)** Discussions of her financial allowance and borrowed funds
