[035-001]As the King passed through the line we formed for him to go to the window

[035-002]he came up to me and said he must begin by chiding me for not coming to him

[035-003]yesterday. In fact he had forgot I was a Cabinet Minister, and he therefore

[035-004]would see me to-day. I said 'it was my first and I hoped it would be my

[035-005]last fault.' After the Proclamation he sent for the Duke of Wellington, and

[035-006]when the Duke left him, for me. He asked about China. I told him how we

[035-007]stood there. That there was an interruption which would probably prevent

[035-008]the arrival of any ships this year; that orders had been given for a double

[035-009]investment next year. I said the state of affairs generally was by no means

[035-010]satisfactory. The King said he was afraid Lord W. Bentinck had not been

[035-011]doing well. I said I feared he had let down the dignity of his office, and

[035-012]had when he first went there run after popularity too much, and allowed the

[035-013]press to get ahead. It would now be very difficult to check it. I added

[035-014]that he went to make great reductions and had made some. That that had

[035-015]rendered him unpopular. He was honest and well-meaning. The King said he

[035-016]should go down to Bushey soon, and as I was living near he would have me

[035-017]over at eleven o'clock some morning, and give me some hours to make him

[035-018]acquainted with the state of India. I told him of the secret letter to the

[035-019]Bengal Government about the Nagpore Treaty, and the principles laid down,

[035-020]of which he highly approved. He then expressed apprehension of Russia. I

[035-021]told him all that had been done upon that subject, and of the present to

[035-022]Runjeet Singh, and the navigation of the Indus, with all which he seemed

[035-023]much pleased. I said I would send him the secret letters, and get together

[035-024]information that would bring the whole state of India before him as

[035-025]concisely as possible. As I was led to mention Sir J. Macdonald, I asked a

[035-026]coat for him, and the King granted it, thinking it very proper.

[035-027]The Duke attends the opening of the King's will at 12.

[035-028]The late King died, as was thought, of fatness about the heart. The dropsy

[035-029]was gone.

[035-030]Cabinet. We had none at St. James's, but there was a council. The Duke of

[035-031]Norfolk attended to be sworn in as a Privy Councillor. We found, on

[035-032]reference to the Act of last session, that he must have taken the oath

[035-033]within three months before his receiving any office of trust or profit. So,

[035-034]on my proposal, the Petty Bag was sent for, and the Chancellor held a court

[035-035]of Chancery in the ball-room, where the Duke took the oath. He was

[035-036]afterwards sworn in, as were the Duke of Bedford, Sir S. Canning, Sir J.

[035-037]Mackintosh, Lord Bexley, and two or three others who were not in time

[035-038]yesterday. There were a good many orders in council, but of no moment.

[035-039]There was the usual proclamation against vice and immorality.

[035-040]The King did very well. He was very gracious to all who approached him, and

[035-041]had something to say to every one. He took little notice of Sir. J.

[035-042]Mackintosh.

[035-043]Lord Bathurst had to change a sheriff. The King, when he heard the name of

[035-044]the new one (sheriff of Suffolk, I think), said, _'He is a Whig.'_ Lord

[035-045]Bathurst said, 'He is a very good man, I believe, Sir, and is recommended

[035-046]by the Duke of Grafton.' 'Oh!' said the King, 'I do not mean to say it is

[035-047]wrong; only remember, _he is a Whig_.'

[035-048]After the council we went to Peel's, but we remained but a short time, the

[035-049]Duke going to the House and Peel too before 4. In our House not a word was

[035-050]said. In the Commons Brougham, who seems, as Frankland Lewis told me, half

[035-051]frantic, made rather an apologetic speech for his attack upon the Lord

[035-052]Steward, but again hinted at intentional disrespect towards the House of

[035-053]Commons, not on the part of Ministers in that House, but of persons

[035-054]elsewhere. He reminded Peel that whatever accession of strength Ministers

[035-055]might have recently obtained, they could not carry on the Government

[035-056]without the confidence of the House of Commons.

[035-057]His speech was very mysterious, and hardly any one understood it. Some

[035-058]thought he alluded to the accession of Lord Grey to the Government; that

[035-059]must have rested upon foolish rumour. He alluded, I conclude, to the King's

[035-060]support, now well known. What symptoms of disrespect for the House of

[035-061]Commons he may have discovered I know not. Probably he chooses to imagine

[035-062]them, to produce an effect.

[035-063]He is evidently mad with disappointment. He could not well be wooed in such

[035-064]a temper, even if he were to be wooed at all.

[035-065]After the House I rode to leave my name at the Princess Augusta's, and

[035-066]forgot the Duke of Cumberland, who lives close by; then I went to the Duke

[035-067]of Gloucester's, where I met F. Lewis, who told me of Brougham's speech and

[035-068]so on. I went with Wood to the Princess Sophia of Gloucester's. He told me

[035-069]all the King said of the late King's error in not frankly supporting his

[035-070]Government, and of his own determination to do so. He had been long in the

[035-071]habit of saying, 'the Queen is not with child.' There had been a report to

[035-072]that effect. Rode to the Duchess of Kent's and Duke of Sussex's. Met Lord

[035-073]Graham, Mr. and Mrs. Arbuthnot, and the Chancellor. Rode on with the

[035-074]Chancellor to Kensington. As we were coming away from the Palace we heard

[035-075]the trampling of horses behind us, and turning round, saw the King coming

[035-076]full tilt with his lancers; we had but just time to wheel round and salute

[035-077]His Majesty, who seemed much amused at seeing two of his Ministers amongst

[035-078]all the little children who were running by his carriage, and the

[035-079]Chancellor, so lately in all the gravity of his official robes, mounted on

[035-080]a little white New Forest pony of Lady Lyndhurst's. I rode on to

[035-081]Roehampton, dined there, and rode back.

[035-082]At 10 a Cabinet at Peel's. We framed the message. Peel was very flat. The

[035-083]measure of immediate dissolution is one he does not half approve. He wished

[035-084]to settle the Regency question. He has been put out of humour by having his

[035-085]opinions upon that point not at once acquiesced in. He sees all the

[035-086]difficulties of our position, and does not meet them with energy and

[035-087]_elan_. He certainly is not an agreeable person to transact business with,

[035-088]but he is a very able man.

[035-089]The accounts from Ireland are very bad. The potatoes are exhausted at

[035-090]Limerick, Tralee, and other places, and the new crop will not come in till

[035-091]August. At Limerick some stores have been forced, and the troops attacked

[035-092]with stones.

[035-093]At Tralee there was a subscription of 450£ for the purchase of potatoes;

[035-094]300£ was expended, and the Mayor of Tralee and other _gentlemen_ bought

[035-095]some of these potatoes, which were offered at a reduced price to the

[035-096]people, for _seed_! Can any country be tranquil in which resident gentlemen

[035-097]can do such things? A discretionary power has been given to the Lord

[035-098]Lieutenant to expend 3000£ in food, should it become necessary, without

[035-099]further reference.

[035-100]About 180 peers have taken the oaths. I fear we shall be beaten upon the

[035-101]Forgery Bill; we have a very narrow margin indeed, not above six or eight

[035-102]without bishops. It is supposed the bishops will stay away. I fear those

[035-103]will stay away who would, if present, vote with us, and all who are against

[035-104]will come. If this should be the case we must be defeated.

[035-105]The King was perfectly reasonable about Lord Combermere. The Duke showed

[035-106]His Majesty the letters which had passed, and the King said he should not

[035-107]think of it. He told Peel and Lord Melville he wished the Royal Academy to

[035-108]remain open till after the King's funeral, that he might see the

[035-109]exhibition, and said Peel should attend him when he went. This Peel thinks

[035-110]very foolish, and his disposition seems to be to turn the King into

[035-111]ridicule, and to throw the suspicion of insanity upon all his acts. This is

[035-112]the _tactique_ of the Whigs. The King takes the Sacrament on Sunday, and

[035-113]has desired the two English and one Irish archbishop to attend. This they

[035-114]call 'an indication.'

[035-115]_June 29._

[035-116]At half-past ten went to Lord Rosslyn's, to arrange with him the Lords'

[035-117]Address. Went with him to Peel's, to show it to him. He was reading when we

[035-118]went in, and hardly looked up. He heard the Address which I read, and

[035-119]approved of it; but he hardly took any notice of us or of it. He seemed

[035-120]really ill, and quite broken down.

[035-121]Called on Hardinge. We had some conversation respecting the state of the

[035-122]Government. His idea is that the strength of the Government in the House of

[035-123]Commons is much injured by Peel's being in a subordinate situation to the

[035-124]Duke. That if he was Chancellor of the Exchequer and First Lord of the

[035-125]Treasury, things would go on better, the Duke taking a secretaryship of

[035-126]State. This would do very well in the House of Commons, but very ill in the

[035-127]Cabinet. He is for getting Mr. Stanley, and suggests (or Rosslyn did, or

[035-128]both, for having talked to both on the same subject I may confound them)

[035-129]that Lord F. Leveson should be made a peer. I think that a good idea. He is

[035-130]of no use in the Commons, and his peerage would open a place which Mr.

[035-131]Stanley could fill.

[035-132]Rosslyn thinks Aberdeen's notions upon foreign politics have, together with

[035-133]his assumption of independence which is of recent date, made the Duke

[035-134]rather sore, and that he would not be sorry to have another Secretary of

[035-135]State for Foreign Affairs. Lord Rosslyn wants to have Lord Grey in, and

[035-136]says he would as soon be First Lord of the Admiralty as Foreign Secretary.

[035-137]Rosslyn would, I think, like to go to Ireland as Lord Lieutenant. He would

[035-138]willingly give up the Privy Seal to Aberdeen. He thinks Sir G. Murray would

[035-139]make an excellent Governor General. I fear he would be too indolent. He

[035-140]said he knew, if there was a vacancy, the Duke would be glad to make him

[035-141]Master General.

[035-142]I had said I believed Lord Beresford would go to Portugal as Minister, if

[035-143]Miguel would be on good terms with us. It seems Goulburn would be glad to

[035-144]be Speaker. That would open a proper office for Herries, and his offices

[035-145]might be divided, Lord Althorpe having the Board of Trade.

[035-146]I really think some arrangement must be made to give us strength in the

[035-147]House of Commons. Saw the Duke at two. He approved of the address. Rosslyn,

[035-148]was with him. I told him how ill Peel seemed. He said he would go to see

[035-149]him.

[035-150]House. The Duke moved the Address. He gave a character of the late King as

[035-151]one of the most accomplished, able, and remarkable men of the age. I saw

[035-152]Lord Grey smile a little, but the House generally was grave and formal.

[035-153]Lord Grey assented to the Address, but _laissait entrevoir_ that he should

[035-154]be hostile to the Address to-morrow, hinting at the Regency. The same thing

[035-155]was done in the Commons.

[035-156]The Duke told me the late King had three disorders which must have proved

[035-157]fatal, and he died of bursting a blood-vessel in the stomach. He had a

[035-158]concretion as large as an orange in his bladder, his liver was diseased,

[035-159]and his heart was ossified. Water there was not much, and all proceeding

[035-160]from the interruption of circulation about the heart. I read the report,

[035-161]signed by Halford, Tierney, Brodie, and A. Cooper.
