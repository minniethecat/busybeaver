[048-001]The farmers now leave their thrashing machines out in the fields to be

[048-002]broken.

[048-003]The rickyard of one gentleman was set on fire because he committed a man

[048-004]for machine-breaking. He lost 6,000£, nothing being insured.

[048-005]It seems suspicions are entertained that the machine-breakers are not all

[048-006]of the station they assume. They all wear smock frocks, but their language

[048-007]is better than their dress. When money was offered them, if they wanted it,

[048-008]by the Oxendens, they said they did not want money, they obeyed orders.

[048-009]It is reported, but this must be an exaggeration, that 500 assembled lately

[048-010]on a Down near Mr. Brockman's.

[048-011]The magistrates have no good evidence against any. Some Bow Street officers

[048-012]are here. Lord Winchelsea and Sir Edward Knatchbull have been here at every

[048-013]meeting of the magistrates, although they live eighteen miles off.

[048-014]The Provisional Government of Belgium have declared the independence of the

[048-015]country and the defeazance of the House of Orange. In the meantime the

[048-016]Prince of Orange is arrived at Antwerp, as Viceroy, with a Belgian Etat

[048-017]Major Civil.

[048-018]It seems probable the Chamber of Deputies will abolish the punishment of

[048-019]death for political offences, and so save Polignac.

[048-020]The levy of 108,000 men will hardly make the French army 240,000 effective,

[048-021]for it was not full before the Revolution, and numbers have deserted;

[048-022]besides the disbandment of the Guards, which was 25,000 men.

[048-023]_October 11._

[048-024]Cabinet. Aberdeen read Lord Stuart's account of his interview with Molé.

[048-025]Molé suggests an immediate conference, and thinks the Prince of Orange may

[048-026]be made Sovereign of La Belgique. No communication will be held by the

[048-027]French Government with the Provisional Government of Belgium. They will

[048-028]communicate through the King.

[048-029]It is proposed to have the Conference here. The Ministers of Austria,

[048-030]Prussia, and Russia have expressed their readiness to acquiesce in anything

[048-031]proposed by this country. They may inveigh against the diplomacy of

[048-032]England, but in moments of danger all rally under our wing.

[048-033]Molé distinctly admitted that the existence of the present Government of

[048-034]France depended on its remaining on good terms with England and Prussia,

[048-035]and the affairs of Belgium gave them an opportunity of showing _la droiture

[048-036]de leurs principes_, &c.--in short, of gaining a good character.

[048-037]It was decided against guaranteeing the sum of 500,000£ the Dutch wish to

[048-038]raise here. There would be no end to such loans if we once began to assist

[048-039]the credit of foreign States. Parliament would not approve of the measure.

[048-040]To the Dutch Government it is important that this Administration should

[048-041]remain, and likewise that their own credit should not be injured in all

[048-042]Europe by the confession of weakness which their recourse to us implies.

[048-043]To guarantee a loan is to give money, and to do that is to assist one of

[048-044]the parties to lose the mediatory character, and, in fact, put ourselves

[048-045]out of the Congress.

[048-046]Hardinge can reduce 57,000£ a year in net and on the Civil List, 30,000£ on

[048-047]the Pension List, and 27,000£ on officers of State.

[048-048]We had some talk about details, but Goulburn does not reduce as much as

[048-049]Hardinge.

[048-050]Had some talk with the Duke and Peel respecting the fires in Kent, and the

[048-051]breaking of frames. Five are in prison. The Duke thinks smugglers are at

[048-052]the bottom of it.

[048-053]There has been alarm at Carlisle. The officers in command of the castle

[048-054]apprehended an attempt to surprise it and seize the arms. Men had been seen

[048-055]measuring the wall. Sir J. Graham was alarmed about it. Orders will be

[048-056]given to provision for thirty days all the places where arms are kept, the

[048-057]town included, where there are 600,000 stand of arms. In the meantime all

[048-058]classes are more comfortable in this country than they ever were, and this

[048-059]alone keeps down insurrection. There are leaders but no troops.

[048-060]Hardinge reports that the spirit in Ireland is _disimproved_ since the

[048-061]events in Belgium.

[048-062]There is to be an Anti-Union Society, which, as soon as it meets, will be

[048-063]put down under the Act.

[048-064]_October 12._

[048-065]At nine went to Apsley House. Met the Chairs. We went in to the Duke. Our

[048-066]conversation lasted two hours. As they are to send in a _procès verbal_, it

[048-067]is unnecessary for me to state it. The substance was that, supposing the

[048-068]monopoly to be taken away, they would administer the Government of India as

[048-069]heretofore on one of two conditions; either closing their account with the

[048-070]public and receiving payment in full, or an equivalent annuity for all

[048-071]their property in India, in which case they would require no guarantee of

[048-072]the present dividend; or making over all their property, and taking a

[048-073]perpetual guarantee of the dividend.

[048-074]The public to make good in either case all deficiency of Indian revenue,

[048-075]and in either case the Company to be the agents for the territory,

[048-076]providing all necessary sums here and receiving repayment at a rate of

[048-077]exchange to be paid from time to time fairly.

[048-078]The Chairs were given to understand that the public being liable to the

[048-079]making good of Indian deficiency, we should require a strict control over

[048-080]the whole expenditure _here_, as well as in India.

[048-081]They show, especially Campbell, a disposition to leave off trading and

[048-082]become gentlemen. They were told by the Duke that if they did so we must be

[048-083]at liberty to revise our arrangement with them. We might as well go to the

[048-084]Bank as to them, if we were to treat with a body not commercial.

[048-085]The Duke seems much pleased with his foreign prospects.

[048-086]M. de Choiseul was waiting to see him. I suppose on the affair of Holyrood

[048-087]House.

[048-088]It seems probable that the French will abolish the punishment of death, and

[048-089]so save Polignac.

[048-090]_October 14._

[048-091]Found at the office several papers giving accounts of Radical meetings in

[048-092]Lancashire. All the old Radicals are reappearing on the scene. They do not

[048-093]as yet seem to be attended by any numerous assemblies, never above 200 or

[048-094]300.

[048-095]A letter from a clergyman at Wrotham speaks of burnings near that place,

[048-096]and of the bad conduct of the people who interfere with the working of the

[048-097]engines, and seem to rejoice in the destruction.

[048-098]Read all the papers relating to the education of the Princess Victoria, who

[048-099]seems to have been admirably brought up.

[048-100]At the Cabinet room read a long and excellent letter of Hardinge's

[048-101]respecting the state of Ireland.

[048-102]The 87th Regiment at Newry, when paraded for church, refused to march

[048-103]without music, to which it had been accustomed in the south. It had been

[048-104]discontinued in the north to avoid displeasing the Orangemen.

[048-105]The captain sent for the Lieutenant-Colonel Blair, who was at first

[048-106]disobeyed, but he placed a drum to have a drum-head court martial, and then

[048-107]they marched. The Duke says it is, and always has been, the worst regiment

[048-108]in the service. It ran away at Salamanca and exposed him to being taken

[048-109]prisoner. It has always been unmilitary, and from the same cause, a

[048-110]disposition to seek popularity on the part of its officers. Hardinge

[048-111]proposes embarking it at once for the West Indies. The Duke prefers

[048-112]bringing it to Dublin, where there are other regiments to keep it in order,

[048-113]and soon sending it to England, and by detachments at no distant period to

[048-114]Botany Bay. They do not expect there will be any further exhibition of

[048-115]mutinous spirit. The only mischief of this is the effect at this time.

[048-116]There have been apprehensions of an attempt to scale the Pigeon House, and

[048-117]a full garrison has been ordered into it, with directions to add to its

[048-118]defences on the seaside so as to protect it from escalade.

[048-119]Hardinge can bring twenty guns together in a very short time, at any point

[048-120]in Dublin. He talks of arming the students in Trinity College in the event

[048-121]of an explosion.

[048-122]They rather expect an explosion about the 18th or 19th, when probably there

[048-123]will be the first meeting of the new Association.

[048-124]This it will be the first object to put down by the Act of 1829. The

[048-125]meeting to petition for the repeal of the Union will be permitted.

[048-126]Hardinge is quite himself on horseback. The only fear is that he should be

[048-127]too lively. Peel seems to think he is; but it is a great comfort to have

[048-128]him there instead of Lord Francis Leveson, who was always wrong.

[048-129]The King of the Netherlands has called his States at the Hague, the

[048-130]Constitution requiring them to meet this year in Belgium. He takes

[048-131]advantage of the provision in the Constitution which permits him to call

[048-132]the States in Holland in case of war. They fear the loss of Antwerp. The

[048-133]Prince of Orange thinks things look better.

[048-134]The Netherland Ambassador is much annoyed at the refusal of pecuniary

[048-135]assistance; but, as was expected, the Dutch have got their money, only

[048-136]paying a little more for it.

[048-137]Our depots are only 160 strong. We have hardly a battalion. One or two at

[048-138]least of those which were going abroad will be retained for a time.

[048-139]The Duke of Brunswick does not much like abdicating. The Duke of Wellington

[048-140]thought he had brought him to make his brother Governor-General for his

[048-141]life, retaining the succession for his children. However, Aberdeen seems to

[048-142]have blundered him back again. He is to go to see the King on Saturday. The

[048-143]King desired he might come early, that he might not be obliged to have him

[048-144]to dinner, and he desired Aberdeen would remain in the room.

[048-145]Pozzo thinks the French Government is gaining strength; but they are very

[048-146]inefficient in preventing armed men from assembling on the frontiers of

[048-147]Spain.

[048-148]The French have exercised such coldness towards the Belgians that they are

[048-149]become unpopular. De Potter was French while he had hopes of becoming so.

[048-150]Now he is a Republican.

[048-151]The Austrians will send troops into the Sardinian dominions if there is any

[048-152]insurrection. [Footnote: They had similarly interfered to put down the

[048-153]Constitutional movement in Piedmont which followed on the Neapolitan

[048-154]revolution of 1821.] This by invitation.

[048-155]The Queen of Spain has, it is said, a son. [Footnote: It was a daughter,

[048-156]afterwards Queen Isabella II., born October 10, 1830. The alteration of the

[048-157]succession in favour of the female line led to a civil war on Ferdinand

[048-158]VII.'s death. A son might have secured peace, but probably without a

[048-159]Constitution.] This event would, it is thought, secure Spain against any

[048-160]revolutionary movement.

[048-161]_October 15._

[048-162]Called on the Duke. Settled with him the alterations necessary in the

[048-163]Chairs' memorandum of the conversation on the 12th. He thought we had gone

[048-164]too far in leading them to expect they should be repaid the money they had

[048-165]sunk in the territory while they held the Government.

[048-166]Received from him the opium letter. He thinks the principle good, but

[048-167]considers it is not fair to make the Scindians prevent the transit of

[048-168]opium. We cannot prevent them, for they are independent; but unless we

[048-169]endeavour to persuade them, and succeed in doing so, we shall lose our

[048-170]opium revenue.

[048-171]_October 16._

[048-172]Chairs at 11. Head over with them my alterations of their protocol. Astell

[048-173]did not seem to see the greatness of the variations. Campbell did, and

[048-174]particularly observed upon the words, 'value of the fixed property in India

[048-175]which might be adjudged to appertain to the Company in their commercial

[048-176]capacity.' He wanted an admission of the justice of the claims, leaving

[048-177]nothing for adjustment but their amount. I said we could not admit claims

[048-178]without examination, the nature of which we did not yet know. All we could

[048-179]admit was that the claims were such as should be submitted to examination,

[048-180]and their validity decided upon just principles.

[048-181]Astell wished to go back again and recommence the discussion. I said he

[048-182]knew I could decide nothing without the Cabinet, and he nothing without the

[048-183]Court; all he had to do now was to bring the subject before them.
