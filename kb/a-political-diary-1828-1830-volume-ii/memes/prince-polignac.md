# Prince Polignac

## Overview

Prince Jules de Polignac served as Prime Minister of France from August 1829 until his government fell during the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution]]. An ultra-royalist, his appointment alarmed the British government and triggered intensive diplomatic efforts to influence French policy. Ellenborough's diary records the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s cabinet debating how to engage with Polignac's ministry.

## Appointment as Prime Minister

The news of Polignac's elevation reached the British government with concern: "Polignac has been made Prime Minister of France. De Rigny is made Minister" of the Navy as part of the new administration. (009-155) The appointment signalled a rightward shift in French politics that London viewed as destabilising.

Lord Stuart, the British ambassador in Paris, was instructed to engage directly with the new premier, travelling "_incognito_, to Paris to talk to Polignac and endeavour to get him to join" British positions on European questions. (009-189) Some in the Cabinet acknowledged that "Polignac was a more able man than people" commonly assumed. (010-019)

## British Efforts to Influence Polignac

The Wellington cabinet developed a strategy of sustained diplomatic engagement with Polignac. "Questions are to be put to Polignac to ascertain what he would do in" various scenarios. (010-058) There was scepticism in some quarters: "Neither letter nor Stuart will get anything out of Polignac." (010-062) Some ministers argued that "ourselves should go to Paris as an individual, see Polignac, and return" with a direct assessment. (010-063)

Ellenborough took a pragmatic view, arguing that since "Polignac _might_ be brought into our views, we shall lose all by not using" the opportunity for engagement. (010-068) He believed that "Polignac would be disposed to hold our language to" other European powers. (010-076) British diplomats feared that aggressive pressure "shall weaken Polignac's Government, and lose our object." (010-087) The formal "communication to be made to Polignac is to be made to him" through carefully chosen channels. (010-090)

## Fall of the Ministry

Polignac's government collapsed in July 1830 when Charles X was overthrown. The revolution that swept him from power ushered in the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orleans]] as King of the French, transforming the diplomatic landscape that the British had spent months navigating.
