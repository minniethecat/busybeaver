**Mr. Chaplin** was a British colonial official and an authority on Indian affairs during the late 1820s. He was considered for a senior appointment in India and was examined as a witness before a Parliamentary committee, where his testimony was well received.

== Indian Affairs and Governorship ==

The diarist recorded discussions about filling the Governor-Generalship of India in early 1828. Mr. Chaplin was mentioned among the candidates: "I am disposed to think Mr. Chaplin the best Indian for the situation." (023-142) The diarist subsequently raised his name in official conversations: "I mentioned Mr. Chaplin." (024-013) These references suggest that Chaplin was regarded within government circles as a serious contender for high office in British India, and enjoyed the diarist's confidence.

== Committee Testimony ==

Mr. Chaplin appeared as a witness before a Parliamentary committee, where the diarist noted that he "gave a very good evidence." (026-156) The favourable assessment of his testimony indicates that his knowledge of Indian administration was valued by policymakers in [[House of Commons|the House of Commons]].

== Social and Professional Circle ==

Chaplin moved within the political and administrative network that included the diarist, [[Edward Law, 1st Earl of Ellenborough]]. A note records that a correspondent "likewise mentions a Mr. Chaplin" (013-106), placing him among the figures discussed in the diarist's regular correspondence on colonial affairs.
