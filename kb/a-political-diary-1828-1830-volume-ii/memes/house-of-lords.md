# House of Lords

## Overview

The House of Lords was the upper chamber of the British Parliament and the primary setting for Ellenborough's political diary. As a peer, Ellenborough participated in Lords debates, recorded speeches, and noted the outcomes of divisions. Major legislation of the period—including Catholic Relief and the Franchise Bill—passed through the Lords under Wellington's leadership.

## Role in the Diary

The Lords is the stage upon which much of the diary's political drama unfolds. Ellenborough records his shorthand "House." for days when the Lords sat. (001-024) He notes speeches, including "a long speech from Lord Eldon, containing no argument, and both flat and bad." (001-027) The chamber heard contributions from Lord Harrowby, Lord Lansdowne, and others on key questions. (001-029)

## Major Decisions

The Lords passed the Franchise Bill with "a majority of 104." (001-034) The Catholic Relief Bill's third reading was a critical moment. The chamber's composition and procedures shaped the government's legislative strategy throughout 1828–1830.
