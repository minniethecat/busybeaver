# Lord Cleveland

## Overview

Lord Cleveland (William Vane, 1st Duke of Cleveland) was a Whig peer who, despite his party affiliation, was described in Ellenborough's diary as "a faithful and powerful supporter of Government." (046-033) His appearances in the diary primarily concern ecclesiastical patronage and his role during the fall of the Wellington ministry in 1830.

## Ecclesiastical Patronage

Cleveland's main interaction with Ellenborough, who was Lord Privy Seal, involved church patronage. "A letter from Lord Cleveland, expressing a wish to have the Vicarage of Ilchester, and offering an equivalent living in Shropshire, or Cheshire" was received and forwarded to the Bishop of Bath and Wells. (046-030)

Ellenborough transmitted Cleveland's request "with a strong recommendation," noting Cleveland's political value to the government. He was realistic about the outcome, however: "I dare say he will refuse. Very likely he has given away the vicarage. I told Lord Cleveland I thought it probable." (046-035)

## Political Role in 1830

When the Wellington government fell in November 1830, Cleveland appeared among the crowds visiting the Duke. "The Duke said 300 people had called upon him to-day — amongst the rest Lord Cleveland, with whom Lord Grey was early this morning, and whom he in vain endeavoured to induce to go to Ireland." (054-157)

This encounter is significant: [[lord-grey|Lord Grey]], about to form a government, had met with Cleveland earlier that morning and tried to send him to Ireland — likely as Lord Lieutenant. Cleveland's visit to Wellington on the same day suggests divided loyalties or an attempt to maintain good relations with the outgoing administration.

## Political Character

Cleveland's identity as a Whig who remained "a faithful and powerful supporter" of Wellington's Tory government places him among the moderate Whigs who valued stable administration over strict party allegiance. His ecclesiastical patronage interests reflected the era's close entanglement of politics and church preferment.
