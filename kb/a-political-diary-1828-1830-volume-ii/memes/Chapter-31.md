[031-001]_May 23._

[031-002]Rode to the Cabinet at three from Roehampton. The bulletin is that the King

[031-003]had had embarrassments in his breathing.

[031-004]The Duke waited two and a half hours before he saw him yesterday. The King

[031-005]signed the two messages, and then said 'the Duke has just caught me in

[031-006]time!' and in an instant there was a gurgling in his throat. He seized

[031-007]Knighton's arm. The Duke ran for Halford, went out into the gallery where

[031-008]he did not find him, then into another room where he was. Halford

[031-009]immediately took a bottle from the table and gave the King something which

[031-010]seemed to relieve him.

[031-011]The Duke thinks the King was in pain three or four seconds; but it was a

[031-012]minute and a half before he was relieved. He then did not speak; but made a

[031-013]motion with his hand for the Duke to go.

[031-014]He had just before been talking of going to Ascot and then to Aix-la-

[031-015]Chapelle.

[031-016]The King was perfectly satisfied with the proposed arrangement for the

[031-017]stamp.

[031-018]He asked the news, was told Leopold was behaving very ill, and agreed.

[031-019]As to Algiers he was told the note of the French Minister was

[031-020]unsatisfactory, and that it was under consideration whether a note should

[031-021]not be presented. He thought it right.

[031-022]The Duke's opinion is that if the King should be seized with one of those

[031-023]attacks when no one was with him, he would die.

[031-024]The opinion of Halford and the others is that the disorder is mortal; but

[031-025]he may live six weeks or two months.

[031-026]The punctures are healed. They are afraid of opening them again for fear of

[031-027]mortification, and can only proceed by medicines.

[031-028]The King's state seems distressing. He can neither remain quiet in his

[031-029]chair or in his bed. He is in a state of constant restlessness.

[031-030]The Duke of Cumberland was there to-day, but the King had desired he might

[031-031]not see him.

[031-032]Leopold has declined. He sent a note to that effect on Friday night at

[031-033]twelve o'clock--very well written, not by himself. Aberdeen thinks

[031-034]Palmerston wrote it. He takes popular ground, and cannot impose himself

[031-035]upon _a reluctant people_. The fact is Friday's bulletin wrote his letter.

[031-036]The Duke thinks he will be shown up. The papers presented to-morrow will be

[031-037]no more than it was before intended to present; but Aberdeen will announce

[031-038]the _evasion_ of the sovereign, and say that that circumstance will render

[031-039]necessary the production of other papers which will be presented as soon as

[031-040]they can be printed. The whole discussion will turn upon Leopold's conduct.

[031-041]Aberdeen will be in the position of the manager of a country theatre who,

[031-042]just as the curtain is about to be drawn up, is obliged to come forward and

[031-043]announce that the amateur gentleman who had solicited the part of Macbeth,

[031-044]who had attended all the rehearsals, and whose only difficulty, which was

[031-045]about money, seemed to be in a fair way of adjustment, had unexpectedly

[031-046]intimated his intention to withdraw in a printed address to the galleries.

[031-047]Forsooth there should have been an appeal to the people of Greece on the

[031-048]subject of their Government! An appeal to the people of Newgate on the

[031-049]subject of the new police! [Footnote: This sentiment, however severe,

[031-050]represents the feeling about the Greeks of many Englishmen at that time,

[031-051]and especially of those who, as in the case of naval officers employed in

[031-052]Greek waters, had seen much of them during the war. Their struggle for

[031-053]independence was undoubtedly disgraced, not only by cruelty, but by a

[031-054]treachery and disregard of faith which, though perhaps attributable to past

[031-055]subjection and oppression, was peculiarly odious to English observers. Lord

[031-056]Ellenborough adopted this view.]

[031-057]By a letter of C. Capo d'Istria's, dated 25 M., April 6, written

[031-058]immediately after his receipt of one from Leopold (after his acceptance),

[031-059]it appears that Leopold had intimated his intention to change his religion.

[031-060]He must have had about forty-eight hours to consider the point.

[031-061]Lord Melville had heard that Leopold had consulted Lord Grey and Lord

[031-062]Lansdowne without acquainting one that he had seen the other.

[031-063]_May 24._

[031-064]Rode to the office at four to receive the manufacturers. Mr. Crawford was

[031-065]there, Finlay being ill. I told them of my plans as to the Indus. I

[031-066]directed their attention to the point of bringing out in evidence the

[031-067]effect the stoppage in China had upon the general trade of the East. I

[031-068]again desired them to show, if they could, why British manufactures did not

[031-069]go to China by the country trade.

[031-070]Met Aberdeen. Told him I thought, on consideration, that a reply to Leopold

[031-071]would lead to an answer from him, to which the Plenipotentiaries could not

[031-072]reply without entering into an undignified discussion with Palmerston, who

[031-073]would be the real controversialist.

[031-074]There should be an answer, but it should be addressed to the Residents, and

[031-075]what could not be addressed to them might be stated in Parliament, that is,

[031-076]all relating to letters, conversations, &c.

[031-077]I dare say Leopold will publish to-morrow. It is unlucky the French have

[031-078]troops in the Morea. If they had not, I should be disposed to leave the

[031-079]Greeks to settle their affairs as they pleased, giving them no money. They

[031-080]would soon become reasonable.

[031-081]The bulletin had 'The King had a sleepless night.'

[031-082]House at five. The message and address. The Opposition made no objection to

[031-083]the address, which was carried _nemine dissentiente_. Lord Grey seems to

[031-084]expect a delegation of the royal authority. I told Lord Holland I thought

[031-085]he would be satisfied.

[031-086]Then Aberdeen presented the Greek papers, and, having explained their

[031-087]contents, stated the change of circumstances since Friday night. He

[031-088]represented Leopold as having made preliminary objections on other points,

[031-089]but none on any but money since February 20, when he accepted. Within these

[031-090]few days other grounds have been taken, and the abdication is on these

[031-091]other grounds.

[031-092]There was much movement amongst the Opposition. Aberdeen was accused of

[031-093]unfairness. Lord Durham opened the fire, and I prevented Aberdeen from

[031-094]answering him. The others--Darnley, Lord Londonderry, and Lord Winchelsea,

[031-095]all for Leopold. In short there is a general union of all those who prefer

[031-096]the rising to the setting sun. We shall have a personal debate.

[031-097]We went into E. Retford. I sat by the Chancellor, and worked the Bill for

[031-098]the King's relief.

[031-099]In the House of Commons little was said upon these points. Aberdeen did

[031-100]well. He can make a biting speech as well as any one, and in a quiet way.

[031-101]_May 25._

[031-102]The King passed yesterday uncomfortably. He was a little relieved by

[031-103]medicines during the night. Water is forming again.

[031-104]House. The Chancellor explained very well the objects and details of the

[031-105]King's Relief [Footnote: Relieving him from the necessity of constant

[031-106]signatures.] Bill. The only objections made were to reading it to-morrow,

[031-107]and it was conceded that it should be read on Thursday--to its duration,

[031-108]and it was conceded that should last a month. Lord Grey, I hear, says it is

[031-109]too complicated, that it would have been better to appoint a Custos Regni.

[031-110]I hope he will say that on Thursday.

[031-111]There is but little hope of the King's living till the Bill is passed.

[031-112]_May 26._

[031-113]Hardinge, whom I met in the Park, told me Sir J. Graham informed him there

[031-114]was to be an opposition _à l'outrance_. That Lord Anglesey was to be

[031-115]Minister Lord Grey would serve with him. Palmerston was to be made a great

[031-116]man of. Huskisson to have nothing but revenge. The Duke of Richmond was to

[031-117]be had at all events. All this is childish.

[031-118]House. I expected nothing but the Chancellor's Bill, and went at half-past

[031-119]five, expecting to find Eldon in the midst of his speech; but I found Lord

[031-120]Durham talking about Greece, and soon engaged in the talk myself. Lord Grey

[031-121]was decidedly in opposition. I called the attention of the House to this,

[031-122]that our conduct was to be judged of by the papers on the table--the

[031-123]resignation of Leopold was not alleged to have taken place in consequence

[031-124]of any act of the Government. If noble Lords chose to put on one side the

[031-125]conduct of the Government, and to make this a mere personal question as to

[031-126]the conduct of Leopold we were prepared to enter into the discussion. In

[031-127]speaking of Leopold I said he 'was connected with this country by some of

[031-128]its dearest recollections.'

[031-129]Cabinet dinner. The King's digestion is affected now; but otherwise he is

[031-130]well. He has had many attacks of embarrassed breathing; but none serious.

[031-131]The Duke of Clarence was in the room with him (the Duke of W. being

[031-132]present) for a quarter of an hour today. The King talked of his own danger.

[031-133]He said, 'God's will be done. I have injured no man.' This he often

[031-134]repeated. He said, speaking of his own danger to the Duke of Clarence, 'it

[031-135]will all rest on you then.' He was in very good humour, very angry,

[031-136]however, with Leopold--his anger brought on a slight spasm.

[031-137]He afterwards talked of going to Ascot, and told the Duke to manage that he

[031-138]might be able to go to Aix-la-Chapelle.

[031-139]He is much pleased with the conduct of both Houses about his Signature

[031-140]Bill. After dinner Aberdeen read His proposed answer to Leopold to be

[031-141]addressed to the Residents with a copy of Leopold's letter. It was full of

[031-142]admissions, many of which Peel noticed. Aberdeen was going to meet Laval

[031-143]about it. I objected to sending a copy of the letter to Leopold, as that

[031-144]would as much lead to a reply as if they answered him directly. This the

[031-145]Cabinet seemed to feel; and if there is a letter to the Residents it will

[031-146]be printed with the other papers only, and not communicated.

[031-147]_May 27._

[031-148]Privy Council at one. The Archbishop of Canterbury ordered to frame a

[031-149]prayer for the King's recovery.

[031-150]Cabinet. King's Signature Bill amended. Then Aberdeen read a letter from

[031-151]the Residents in Greece giving an account of all that took place from the

[031-152]notification of the protocol to the Senate to their adhesion. Unfortunately

[031-153]this letter was not sent to Leopold as it ought to have been, when he on

[031-154]the 15th sent Capo d'Istria's letter to Aberdeen, and it is thought we

[031-155]cannot publish it. It shows that the adhesion was entire.

[031-156]No answer to his letter is to be published. We are to wait till we can have

[031-157]a protocol. Laval would not sign any joint letter to the Residents. Being

[031-158]so near he prefers waiting for the orders of his Court.

[031-159]House. King's Signature Bill passed, with some amendments. It is to last

[031-160]till the end of the Session.

[031-161]The King's command is to be signified by _word of mouth_, a very

[031-162]inconvenient mode to a sick man.

[031-163]East Retford for a House.

[031-164]All Columbia is at war again. The Mexicans are urging the Haytians to land

[031-165]5,000 men in Cuba. Peel fears war will begin there by the Americans taking

[031-166]Texas.

[031-167]Fitzgerald writes from Paris that he thinks the French will not retain

[031-168]Algiers. That an energetic demand on our part would have drawn from

[031-169]Polignac a distinct disavowal of the intention. That he does not think the

[031-170]channel (Lord Stuart) a good one.

[031-171]I think Fitzgerald would not at all dislike being made Ambassador at Paris.

[031-172]It seems there is a very sore feeling indeed excited by de Peyronnet's

[031-173]appointment. He thinks the only safety of the Government is in throwing

[031-174]themselves upon the ultra-Royalists.

[031-175]The King is a little better. His stomach begins to bear a little light food

[031-176]again.

[031-177]_May 28._ The account of the King not good.

[031-178]Cabinet. Found them talking about Scotch boroughs. Aberdeen presented the

[031-179]papers relative to Leopold in the House. Some conversation as to the

[031-180]correctness in point of form of presenting them printed. The rule is to

[031-181]present papers written by the King's command, and to have them printed for

[031-182]the immediate use of the House.
