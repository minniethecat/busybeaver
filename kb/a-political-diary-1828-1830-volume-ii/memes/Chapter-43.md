[043-001]I suggested that the most important question was the revision of taxation.

[043-002]My view now is that we must take off some of the taxes which press most on

[043-003]the poorer classes, and have an income tax. I dislike an income tax as much

[043-004]as any one. To me it is a very oppressive tax, but I believe it may become

[043-005]necessary.

[043-006]Walked to the corner of Hyde Park with Lord Rosslyn. Had some conversation

[043-007]with him respecting the changes necessary in the Government before we meet

[043-008]Parliament. He says Lord Althorpe will not come in without Lord Grey, and

[043-009]he is not sure Lord Grey would not stipulate for Lord Durham. The latter is

[043-010]out of the question on account of his temper. I do not think the Government

[043-011]could go on with the Duke and Lord Grey. Of the Huskissonians, Palmerston

[043-012]is the only one. To E. Stanley there is no objection.

[043-013]_August 26._

[043-014]At 3 Count Moltke came to the office. He had two Danish claims to speak

[043-015]about.

[043-016]Dinner at the Albion for Clare. There were present of the Ministers, Peel,

[043-017]Rosslyn, Goulburn, Herries; then Lord F. Leveson, Calcraft, the Solicitor-

[043-018]General, W. Peel, Lord G. Somerset, Planta, Gen. Macdonald, Col. Fitz-

[043-019]Clarence, Lord Tenterden. Of Clare's friends Glengall, Agar Ellis, Sneyd,

[043-020]Lord Templeton, besides H. Vyner, and Upton, who go with him.

[043-021]I spoke feebly, not being well; besides, I did not think it in good taste

[043-022]to make a great speech; but to leave Clare's the first speech of the day.

[043-023]Peel made a very good speech; but too much of it. Clare really spoke very

[043-024]feelingly and well. He spoke a little too much of his gratitude to the

[043-025]Court.

[043-026]I had some conversation with Loch. I was as well received as I expected,

[043-027]and better, considering the run that has been made at me. The Duke went off

[043-028]to Walmer Castle, very wisely, for he wants sea air; but Clare would have

[043-029]been more pleased had he been present, and the Directors too. The

[043-030]Ministers' healths were well received.

[043-031]_August 28._

[043-032]Received from Elphinstone his remarks upon the proposed letter to Bombay,

[043-033]respecting native education, of which he generally approves. He strongly

[043-034]urges the sending out of European professors, young men, acquainted with

[043-035]English literature, to learn the language there, and teach the natives. I

[043-036]have sent the extract from his letter to Astell, suggesting that the

[043-037]Universities of Oxford, Cambridge, Dublin, and Edinburgh should each name

[043-038]those from whom should be selected the necessary number. I have observed

[043-039]that the object of native education is of such importance that the state of

[043-040]the finances must not prevent its accomplishment.

[043-041]_August 30._

[043-042]Wrote a very long letter to Hardinge on the present position of the

[043-043]Government and our policy. I gave my opinion that any accession of men

[043-044]which destroyed the unity of the Duke's Government would do harm. That we

[043-045]must meet our difficulties by measures. That the first was a revision of

[043-046]taxation, that no men we could get would add moral strength to the

[043-047]Government, and the Whigs would not support unless they had half the

[043-048]Government. That the question of Reform could not be made an open question.

[043-049]It was best for the country that parties should be decidedly separated. It

[043-050]might then choose which it preferred, and men would be obliged to take a

[043-051]side. We had better be out with character than in with a detachment of the

[043-052]enemy, in possession of a gate. Still TALK we must have, and we want a

[043-053]financier. I said of myself that I cared little about office. I should

[043-054]without reluctance acquiesce in retirement if the Duke could fill my office

[043-055]more advantageously, and I believe Rosslyn would do. I thought Rosslyn

[043-056]would like Ireland or Paris.

[043-057]I do not think it improbable Hardinge may send this letter to the Duke.

[043-058]_August 31._

[043-059]An insurrection at Brussels, the houses of the Ministers burnt. The troops

[043-060]fired and killed many. They, not being 1,500, retired to the park, and

[043-061]formed before the palace. An evening paper I got at Ashford says the

[043-062]nobility had joined the people, and the troops had acceded on condition of

[043-063]keeping their arms, and guarding the palace. If this Revolution takes the

[043-064]line of union with France, war is almost inevitable. It may be only for a

[043-065]more popular form of Government, but what the people of the Netherlands

[043-066]desire is annexation to a great State. They are ashamed of being Dutch.

[043-067]Most fortunately all our manufacturers are in full employment, and the

[043-068]harvest is abundant. The peace and constitution of England have depended

[043-069]upon fine weather.

[043-070]Clare, from whom I heard to-day, tells me Lord Wellesley assures him there

[043-071]is to be a Revolution in Spain, and named the day. The nobles are supposed

[043-072]to be at the head of it. This may all be true, for our Ministers never find

[043-073]anything out; but my apprehension is that there will be a low, ill-

[043-074]supported revolutionary movement.

[043-075]Received a letter from Lady Londonderry. She first wishes me to obtain, if

[043-076]I can, Ward's exchange to a better climate. This I have told her I have

[043-077]already endeavoured to do; but that I have no expectation of Aberdeen's

[043-078]doing it.

[043-079]Lady L. says her brother was two hours with the Duke, and as long with Lord

[043-080]Grey. The latter would have acted a second part, but the Duke would not

[043-081]admit him. I have told her I think she must have misunderstood Lord

[043-082]Camden's account, and that she may be assured it is not the Duke's

[043-083]character to fear an equal.

[043-084]I sent her letter to Hardinge, and asked him if he knew anything of the

[043-085]affair. I cannot imagine when it can have taken place. Lord Camden was an

[043-086]odd person to employ. He knows so little of Lord Grey. Rosslyn would have

[043-087]been the natural envoy if it proceded from the Duke; but I think it must

[043-088]have been a volunteer of Lord Camden's.

[043-089]_September 2._

[043-090]Read the papers relative to the Danish claims. Canning seems to have

[043-091]decided one case, that of the Danish East India Company, hastily. However,

[043-092]we cannot undo a decision of a Secretary of State.

[043-093]The other case, that of the private individuals at Tranquebar, has been

[043-094]determined in their favour.

[043-095]_September 3._

[043-096]Had a long conversation with Herries, with whom I rode for a long time,

[043-097]respecting affairs, both here and abroad. He is rather downcast. However,

[043-098]he thinks this Belgian insurrection will be put down. Rothschild has

[043-099]exported 800,000£ in silver and 400,000£ in gold to meet his bills when

[043-100]they become due--diffident of having anything paid to himself.

[043-101]_September 5._

[043-102]Cabinet room. Found Lord Rosslyn there. He told me the substance of a

[043-103]report I did not see of Col. Jones, who was sent by the Duke to the

[043-104]Netherlands, and is returned. He says the Prince of Orange is with 1,600

[043-105]men in the park and palace at Brussels; 5,000 men are close at hand under

[043-106]Prince Frederick of Orange, at Vilvorde, and two bodies of 10,000 each are

[043-107]marching upon the same point. The troops at the palace have twelve guns.

[043-108]All the troops show a good disposition.

[043-109]The first deputation from Brussels was rather insolent. They were treated

[043-110]accordingly, and told to return without cockades, &c. They did so, and the

[043-111]Prince agreed to go into Brussels without troops. There was a great crowd,

[043-112]and for a moment he was separated from the staff and the Garde Bourgeoise,

[043-113]and alone in the midst of the people. He leapt his horse over a barrier and

[043-114]so got back. A Commission of very respectable men has been appointed to

[043-115]investigate grievances. So the thing will rest till the meeting of the

[043-116]States on September 13.

[043-117]There is a letter from Lord Heytesbury giving an account of his

[043-118]conversations with the Emperor of Russia. The Emperor is violent against

[043-119]the Bourbons; says very correctly that his treaties only oblige him to

[043-120]maintain a constitutional King. Still he may recognise, but shall always

[043-121]consider the Duke of Orleans as a usurper.

[043-122]Prussia seems very prudent; disposed to recognise, but to state the

[043-123]condition of peace--that the territorial possessions of 1815 shall be

[043-124]maintained. Austria seems to be less prudent. Metternich sent to Bernstorff

[043-125]the answer he intended to give, which required a declaration of not having

[043-126]any intention to interfere in the affairs of France, but required a pledge

[043-127]as to the observance of the Treaty of 1815 before recognition. Bernstorff

[043-128]very prudently advised Austria to recognise unconditionally.

[043-129]The Spaniards seem to have been in great consternation at first.

[043-130]The Minister (Addington) thinks the King and Queen are so popular, and the

[043-131]public interest is so much directed to the Queen's approaching

[043-132]accouchement, that no revolutionary movement of importance is likely to

[043-133]take place. He deprecates, however, the commencement of any such movement,

[043-134]because he thinks it would enable the Apostolical Party [Footnote: The name

[043-135]given in Spain and Portugal to the Absolutist and Clerical Party.] to

[043-136]induce the King to dismiss his present quiet Ministers, and have recourse

[043-137]to measures of rigour, which would infallibly ruin the dynasty. Spain, and

[043-138]indeed all the Powers, seem to look for instruction to England, and there

[043-139]can be no doubt that all will recognise and all be quiet. Salmon, when he

[043-140]communicated to the King the events in France, said, 'Your Majesty sees how

[043-141]dangerous over-zeal is in a Minister. No one could be more devoted to the

[043-142]Royal Family than Prince Polignac.'

[043-143]The King said, 'I see it.'

[043-144]However, notwithstanding this, they say he is so weak that he may adopt a

[043-145]violent course.

[043-146]Nothing can be more correct than the conduct of M. Molé, the French

[043-147]Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs. He is most anxious to preserve

[043-148]peace in Europe, the new King's Government in France, and himself in

[043-149]office. He is much alarmed by the events in Belgium, and wished our

[043-150]Minister to join the French Minister at Brussels in recommending some

[043-151]concession to the King of the Netherlands. The Duke has, as Rosslyn told

[043-152]me, written a memorandum to serve as the basis of Aberdeen's dispatch, very

[043-153]civil indeed to Molé, very much satisfied with the disposition evinced by

[043-154]the French Government, but, in our ignorance of the real state of things,

[043-155]declining to advise the King of the Netherlands.

[043-156]It is very amusing to see the French Government most _liberally_ permitting

[043-157]the Bonaparte family to return to France, and most _prudently_ sending

[043-158]circulars to all the Ministers of the Powers which signed the protocols of

[043-159]1815, urging them in the name of that treaty not to allow the members of

[043-160]the Bonaparte family to leave their present residences.

[043-161]It seems this is very necessary; for although their partisans can do little

[043-162]without their presence, they might do much with it.

[043-163]Martignac has got together sixty members of the Chamber of Deputies who

[043-164]will act _en masse_ for royalty.

[043-165]There is no military force to keep people in order, and the National Guard

[043-166]does not like doing so. In fact the Revolution is not over. Things may go

[043-167]on as they are, but we have as yet no security. The French seem heartily

[043-168]sick of Algiers. It costs a great deal of money. Tropical products will not

[043-169]grow there. The climate does not suit the French troops, who have besides a

[043-170]most extraordinary _maladie de pays._ They must send 15,000 men more there

[043-171]to maintain it, as now they have no more than the town. They are willing to

[043-172]give it up to the Sultan if he will renounce tribute, &c.

[043-173]I never considered the acquisition of importance to France. I always felt

[043-174]we might vex the French to death by the use of a little money which would

[043-175]at any time have brought forward all the Arabs from the desert. The port

[043-176]will only hold a few vessels.
