# Lord Graham

## Overview

Lord Graham (James Graham, styled Lord Graham as heir to the 3rd Duke of Montrose) was a young Tory politician who appears in Ellenborough's diary during the government's search for capable junior office-holders. The diary captures both appreciation of his unexpectedly deep knowledge of Indian affairs and later concerns about his political loyalty.

## Role in the Diary

Lord Graham first enters the diary through Wellington's assessment of his suitability for office. The Duke "had had some conversation last year at Belvoir with Lord Graham upon Indian affairs, and had been quite surprised to find how much he knew." (019-078) One observer "had thought he only knew how to comb his hair" — a dismissive view that Wellington's discovery overturned. (019-080)

This favourable impression led to a plan: Ellenborough records that he "shall suggest Ashley's taking Horace Twiss's place, and Lord Graham being First Commissioner." (019-083) The move was designed to "force him to come forward" (019-084) in parliamentary life, reflecting the administration's need to cultivate younger talent.

## Parliamentary Performance

Graham's maiden speech was received positively. The diary notes that "Lord Graham was unembarrassed and did well; but the 'Times' hardly gives him ten words." (025-071) This contrast between the favourable impression in the chamber and scant press coverage highlights the often private nature of parliamentary reputation-building.

## Political Strains

By the later period of the diary, Graham's relationship with the government appears to have frayed. [[Robert Peel]] "seems to think Lord Graham is dissatisfied and unfriendly. It seems he has been heard complaining of vacillation, &c., on the part of the" government. (037-035)

Later, Graham was encountered "in Downing Street by Lord Graham, who had been waiting to speak to me on the same subject. He seems to think our fall not so immediately necessary as" others feared. (051-140)

The trajectory from promising recruit to disillusioned backbencher captures the strains within Wellington's coalition as it confronted reform pressures.
