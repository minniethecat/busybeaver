[038-001]All these difficulties I foresaw when the Lords adopted the Commons'

[038-002]resolution; but I suppose Canning forced it upon Lord Bathurst and the

[038-003]Cabinet.

[038-004]House at 5. Debate on the Scotch Judicature Bill. Lord Wynford made a

[038-005]miserable speech, which proved he knew nothing about the subject. The

[038-006]Chancellor was very angry with him, and once interrupted him improperly.

[038-007]The debate was dull, and there was no division.

[038-008]_July 13._

[038-009]Went to St. James's at half-past one. A few Privy Councillors were there to

[038-010]be sworn in, amongst the rest the Duke of Hamilton.

[038-011]The Duke of Wellington had to talk over the King about giving a lodge in

[038-012]Bushey Park to one of the FitzClarences for his life, and about gazetting

[038-013]the Queen's household. He found the King very reasonable indeed.

[038-014]The King means to give his Ministers a grand dinner. He intends asking the

[038-015]Speaker and the two Gold Sticks, but _not_ the _third_, the Duke of

[038-016]Cumberland.

[038-017]The Duke of Buckingham is Lord Steward. A bad appointment. The office of

[038-018]Lord Chamberlain was offered by the Duke of Wellington to the Duke of

[038-019]Bedford, Lord Rosslyn going to make the offer. The Duke of Bedford was much

[038-020]gratified, but declined on the ground of his health. The office was then

[038-021]offered to Lord Jersey, who accepted willingly.

[038-022]House. The Chancellor made an excellent speech on the Welsh Judicature

[038-023]Bill, and it was read a second time without a further word. The Forgery

[038-024]Bill passed with a motion of Lord Holland's _pro formâ_ that he might

[038-025]protest.

[038-026]We had Sir Jonah Barrington for a short time. He is very roguish and sly.

[038-027]There are no particulars yet of the capture of Algiers, except that the

[038-028]fleet co-operated.

[038-029]The French seem to have been highly delighted.

[038-030]_July 15._

[038-031]Sir G. Murray, Goulburn, and Herries came down to Roehampton at four to

[038-032]dinner. At five we set off for Windsor. The day was beautiful, and all the

[038-033]world made it a holiday. Carriages of all sorts and hackney coaches were on

[038-034]the road all the morning to Richmond. I never saw so many persons there,

[038-035]and chiefly of the class of shopkeepers. London was quite empty, but the

[038-036]Park quite covered with the people. It seemed to be a day of general

[038-037]recreation.

[038-038]Arrived at Windsor at a quarter after seven. There were a few Lancers along

[038-039]the road from Frogmore, where the King and Queen were, but no crowd. Near

[038-040]the town there were a great many waggons. We turned to the right at the end

[038-041]of the Long Walk and drove through the park to the great gate of the

[038-042]Castle. Within the court were Horse and Foot Guards. We entered at the

[038-043]visitors' entrance, and went to St. George's Hall, where we all assembled.

[038-044]A great many were already come. They began forming the procession at half-

[038-045]past seven, and it was all formed so as to move before nine. I walked with

[038-046]Lord Hill. There were ten or twelve barons, a number of judges, six or

[038-047]eight bishops, and upon the whole a fair representation of the peerage and

[038-048]the Privy Council. There was a double line of Life Guardsmen within the

[038-049]castle, without Foot Guards, and the Blues in the chapel. We did not see

[038-050]the body as we passed. A screen of black concealed the room in which it lay

[038-051]in state. I imagine the King was in the room. As we returned it was open.

[038-052]It struck nine as we came to the Round Tower. A rocket was fired as soon as

[038-053]the body moved, to give notice to Linden for the firing of the minute guns.

[038-054]The bands of the several regiments played the Dead March in Saul, &c., as

[038-055]the procession passed. The Foot Guards stood close together with arms

[038-056]reversed, every fifth man having a flambeau. The platform was, in most

[038-057]places, open on both sides. There was a good deal of air, but the night was

[038-058]warm. Had there been rain, or had it been cold, some must have died. There

[038-059]were but few people on the right of the platform in the inner court, but in

[038-060]the outer court there was a dense mass of people, and all the roofs were

[038-061]covered. There was hardly a whisper. All the people seemed very decent in

[038-062]their dress, and their conduct was perfect. The procession entered at the

[038-063]great door of the chapel and turned to the left, went down to the end of

[038-064]the aisle and then turned, facing the door of the inner chapel. In the

[038-065]space we thus went round were the Eton boys. In the chapel there were some

[038-066]persons on the right of the altar. I could not well see who they were, as

[038-067]there was a sort of haze, but they were all in uniform. With this exception

[038-068]the chapel was empty. We were all placed as we entered in the seats and

[038-069]stalls. The body was drawn upon a carriage. It was too heavy to be carried.

[038-070]The King had a vast number of attendants, such as equerries, &c. Half of

[038-071]them captains in the navy. The attendants pressed rather too close upon

[038-072]him. He was in black with the collars of all the orders. He nodded

[038-073]occasionally as he recognised people; but when his countenance was still he

[038-074]looked very grave. He is become very like his father. The assistant

[038-075]mourners, who were Lords Goderich, Sidmouth, Granville, Grantham, Carlisle,

[038-076]and some others, had no seats and stood during the service. The last who

[038-077]entered were the Guard, the colours preceding. These came half way into the

[038-078]aisle, the colours depressed. The colonels of the battalions and the

[038-079]general, Sir H. Vivian, came in with their caps on and swords drawn, and

[038-080]stood to the right and left of the King, but not near him. The banners were

[038-081]depressed on the two sides of the grave. Over the grave was a black canopy,

[038-082]on the top of which was an enormous crown. The music was good. The service

[038-083]was very ill-read by the Dean Hobart, and the Garter could not make himself

[038-084]heard when he recited the King's titles. Lord Jersey walked as Lord

[038-085]Chamberlain, Lord Conyngham as Steward. He broke his staff into the grave.

[038-086]Lord Cholmondeley was there as Lord Great Chamberlain, and sat on the left

[038-087]of the aisle in a stall opposite the passage. On the other side was the

[038-088]Earl Marshal. When all was over the King went out by the small door on the

[038-089]left near the King's closet, and so by the cloister to the platform. As

[038-090]soon as he appeared the Guard received him with presented arms and God Save

[038-091]the King. We all returned by the way we came. There was tea in St. George's

[038-092]Hall but we went on, and finding Goulburn's servant, followed him to the

[038-093]carriage, which was on the other side of the entrance gate, and so got away

[038-094]even before the King. We were at Roehampton by half-past one. The whole

[038-095]procession lasted about two hours and a half or rather less--that is, from

[038-096]the first move to the end.

[038-097]It was very well arranged. Pohlman, our Deputy Black Rod, who is a Herald,

[038-098]was the acting person, and did his duty admirably. There was no

[038-099]interruption, no confusion, but everything managed as if we had been

[038-100]drilled and did the same thing every day. And so King George IV. is gone to

[038-101]his grave with all the pomp of royalty, and splendid the pageant was; but

[038-102]it was considered a mere pageant even by his household, who had lived so

[038-103]intimately with him for years. There was no regret. A coronation could

[038-104]hardly be gayer; but the procession was gravely done and decently.

[038-105]The magnificence of the castle aided the spectacle and made royalty appear

[038-106]almost as imposing in death as at the moment when the Crown was assumed in

[038-107]the Abbey.

[038-108]We had supper and they all went to London.

[038-109]Huskisson and Palmerston were there. Huskisson very sulky and sour.

[038-110]Palmerston very cordial, as if he thought he might come in, I should be

[038-111]glad if he did.

[038-112]It seems the Duke of Buckingham hints that he must have something more than

[038-113]the stewardship for his seven votes. No one likes his appointment, and we

[038-114]all feel as if an alliance with the Grenville party would bring us ill-

[038-115]luck.

[038-116]_July 16._

[038-117]House. Administration of Justice Bill. A great many amendments made by Lord

[038-118]Tenterden. We struck out a clause by which Le Blanc would have been obliged

[038-119]to sit to tax costs every day in the year. Lord Eldon said the Bill as it

[038-120]was originally drawn was more like a string of resolutions at the London

[038-121]Tavern than an Act of Parliament.

[038-122]The Attorney-General was very angry indeed at the alterations made in the

[038-123]Bill, and threatened to throw it over in the House of Commons.

[038-124]Nothing said about the Libel law; but Lord Holland is to say something on

[038-125]the third reading. Sir Jonah's case. W. Goady spoke. He spoke so slow, it

[038-126]was like a banker paying in sixpences to gain time. He was so dull I went

[038-127]away for fear of falling asleep. The Duke stayed and slept.

[038-128]The Duke remained at Windsor all night. I met him as he was coming down to

[038-129]the office to-day. He said he had remained to see the King and give up to

[038-130]him the late King's snuff-boxes, &c., which were all in a great box.

[038-131]Lord Wharncliffe told me he thought Duncombe, Bethel, Lord Morpeth, and

[038-132]Ramsden would come in for Yorkshire. Afterwards we heard Brougham was to

[038-133]stand. It will have a very bad effect if Hume and Brougham come in for

[038-134]great counties. Yet I dare say they will.

[038-135]Wortley goes down to stand for some Scotch boroughs, which will lead to the

[038-136]County of Forfar.

[038-137]Long Wellesley has been arrested by Gosling the Banker for 4,000£, on which

[038-138]it was found that he had but 3,000£ in the books in the Bank, so he

[038-139]remained in durance for the other 1,000£ till he found five people, each

[038-140]willing to be bound for 200£. This disposes of him for Essex. He had given

[038-141]out that he had 30,000£. An express has been sent off to a Mr. Lloyd, the

[038-142]son-in-law of the old Eliab Harvey, to stand for Essex. I know the man. He

[038-143]was at Ryde in 1813, and at Cowes in 1826. His daughters are rather pretty

[038-144]girls. I suggested Tower, who would have done very well for Essex.

[038-145]_July 17._

[038-146]St. James's at 2. The Lord Mayor and Aldermen first came up with their

[038-147]address, then the same with the Common Council. The King received the

[038-148]addresses, which were very loyal, on the throne. He read the answers very

[038-149]well. The Ministers stood on his left and the household on his right. About

[038-150]seven gentlemen pensioners were on each side from the door to the foot of

[038-151]the throne. The Lord Mayor, &c., were introduced by the Lord Chamberlain.

[038-152]It was well done, and is rather an imposing ceremony.

[038-153]Cabinet. First a question as to what should be done about Ashe, the man who

[038-154]wrote a libel on the Duke of Cumberland, which he sent to him and now

[038-155]reclaims. He has written many letters indicative of an intention to

[038-156]assassinate, and is now come up from Carlisle on foot, and has been walking

[038-157]opposite the Duke's house for three hours, having first written another

[038-158]letter of a threatening nature.

[038-159]Lord Wynford wrote to Peel on the Duke of Cumberland's part; but the Duke

[038-160]will not exhibit articles of the peace. Colonel Peter gave Ashe 5_s_. and

[038-161]he went away.

[038-162]The question was what could be done with him? I suggested that, as in the

[038-163]case of an expected duel, a magistrate on mere information that a breach of

[038-164]the peace was apprehended would take persons into custody and hold them to

[038-165]bail; so here the same thing might be done, one of the letters distinctly

[038-166]threatening a breach of the peace. This would secure the man till it could

[038-167]be discovered whether there was legal ground to indict him for the letters.

[038-168]This will be done.
