[044-001]The Emperor of Russia proposes to cut the Greek question short by proposing

[044-002]the crown at once, without the intervention of France, to Prince Frederick

[044-003]of Orange, and if he should refuse, then to Prince Charles of Bavaria, who

[044-004]we know will accept.

[044-005]I should say from all I have read to-day that if France should make an

[044-006]aggressive movement all Europe would be united against her as in 1813; but

[044-007]if she remain quiet within her own frontier no Power will wish to molest

[044-008]her.

[044-009]It is satisfactory to observe the increased prudence and reasonableness of

[044-010]the great States; their general union, and the deference which in the hour

[044-011]of danger they all show to the opinion of England.

[044-012]There are some apprehensions, I hear, of riots at Manchester. There is no

[044-013]cause for them. All men can get work. I would put them down with a strong

[044-014]hand.

[044-015]_September 6._

[044-016]Saw at the office Colonel Monteith.

[044-017]The King of Persia has about six millions sterling left in his treasury in

[044-018]gold and silver, besides jewels unsaleable on account of their high price,

[044-019]but which might be estimated at four millions more.

[044-020]There will be a civil war on the death of the Shah.

[044-021]Abbas Mirza might succeed if he had energy, but he is the weakest man on

[044-022]earth. Probably all the Rajahs will be put down and some new dynasty

[044-023]established.

[044-024]The chiefs are not likely to serve the Russians at any time. The Persians

[044-025]are fine men and make excellent soldiers, bearing heat and cold, but not

[044-026]wet and damp. Officers there are none.

[044-027]The Russians lose 10,000 men a year in Georgia and Caucasus, and it costs

[044-028]them about 500,000£ a year. They have never conquered the country.

[044-029]The cession lately obtained from Turkey has enabled the Russians to put

[044-030]down the robbers who lived in Abkasia; [Footnote: The country at the

[044-031]western end of the Caucasus.] but it is of no value for purposes of

[044-032]offensive war--of some for defence.

[044-033]It is cheaper in the proportion of 100 to 220 to send goods to Tabriz by

[044-034]Trebizond than by the Persian Gulf.

[044-035]The Imaum of Muscat carries on a large trade in opium between the Red Sea

[044-036]and China. He carries British manufactures to the Indus, and trades

[044-037]extensively with Cochin China, where sugar is half the price it is in

[044-038]India.

[044-039]The officers of the Crown Prince's army all speak Turkish. It is more

[044-040]important to have at the head of it a man of energy than one conversant

[044-041]with Persian.

[044-042]His rank should be increased, as now he is made to rank below the last

[044-043]member of the Mission.

[044-044]The disturbed state of Persia has driven much trade to the Indus which was

[044-045]carried on by the Euphrates.

[044-046]Persia may now be considered not as a monarchy, but a Federative State, all

[044-047]the King's sons being independent Princes.

[044-048]Colonel Monteith was at Algiers--the only Englishman in the army. There may

[044-049]have been twenty foreigners in all. He had letters of introduction and got

[044-050]there in a transport, taking his chance of being sent back. He was with the

[044-051]intendant of the army, and at the siege was attached to a division.

[044-052]Bourmont offered to receive him in his family. Bourmont was hated and

[044-053]despised. He seemed to take very little trouble about the army, and to

[044-054]leave everything to the generals of division. On the 19th, the day of the

[044-055]battle, he lost 600 men by not advancing sooner. The moment he advanced the

[044-056]enemy fled. The loss was 2,200 men in all, yet fifty were never to be seen

[044-057]dead and wounded together. The loss was by skirmishing at long shots along

[044-058]the whole of the line. This sometimes lasted all day, and the troops, being

[044-059]young, were too foolhardy. The Arabs are a miserable race, half naked.

[044-060]Everything beyond Algiers seems a desert. For eight miles round Algiers the

[044-061]cultivation is beautiful, and the villas more numerous than near any town

[044-062]he ever saw. A profusion of water. The town, miserable in the extreme,

[044-063]inhabited by Moors and the descendants of Turks, about 50,000. The port is

[044-064]formed by one pier which hardly protects two or three frigates. There is no

[044-065]safety in the bay.

[044-066]There were 3,000 Turkish soldiers in Algiers, and about 7,000 in the

[044-067]country. These kept order. Now they are sent away the French may colonise

[044-068]extensively, but they cannot keep the country with the present inhabitants.

[044-069]The Dey had ten millions sterling in gold and silver, a treasure which had

[044-070]been accumulating since the time of Barbarossa. [Footnote: A famous corsair

[044-071]of the sixteenth century.] He claimed 400,000£ as his own, and was allowed

[044-072]to carry it away. The French enquired about the jewels of the Regency. The

[044-073]Dey said there were no jewels but those which belonged to his wives, and

[044-074]_la galanterie Française_ would respect them as private property. So they

[044-075]did.

[044-076]There was a magazine containing 250,000£ of things in the trinket line.

[044-077]There were 150 ornamental daggers, all the presents of European princes,

[044-078]&c. Colonel Monteith saw one officer coolly put into his pocket a watch set

[044-079]in diamonds, which had evidently been given by a King of England, worth, he

[044-080]supposed, 2,000£.

[044-081]General Lavardo pillaged more openly than any one. He had thirty soldiers

[044-082]employed in carrying off his pillage.

[044-083]The affair at Belida was accidental. Bourmont went out with 1,600 men and

[044-084]invited the chiefs to meet him. They were coming peaceably; but some Arabs

[044-085]saw the French artillerymen taking their horses down to water without their

[044-086]guns, and they could not help attempting to steal. The artillerymen beat

[044-087]them off; but the firing having begun was soon converted into a battle.

[044-088]Bourmont beat them off, but thought it expedient to retreat.

[044-089]The beach was particularly favourable for landing. The weather fine, and

[044-090]there was plenty of time to prepare.

[044-091]The thing best done was by General Valagi, who in eighteen hours raised a

[044-092]continued work of a mile and a half. He had 1,600 sappers and miners.

[044-093]Colonel Monteith is in admiration of this entrenchment, which was

[044-094]beautifully finished, and was capable of resisting 30,000 regular troops.

[044-095]The Arabs are miserably mounted. The Dey's two best horses were not worth

[044-096]30l. each.

[044-097]Duperre he thought a man willing to do all, but quite overpowered by the

[044-098]management of 100 ships of war and 500 transports. His reports are all

[044-099]lies. Bourmont's are nearest the truth. The ships, with the exception of

[044-100]those which were in the Levant, were not in good order. There seemed to be

[044-101]no discipline.

[044-102]The army never wanted either water or provisions. Water was within three

[044-103]feet of the surface everywhere. In the gardens on the side of the hills

[044-104]towards Algiers the water was found at the depth of twenty feet.

[044-105]Nothing could be more perfect than the equipment of the army. They

[044-106]calculated the cost of the expedition at four millions.

[044-107]I see by the newspapers that the Prince of Orange yielded the point of the

[044-108]colours to the deputation from Brussels. He seems to have conceded a great

[044-109]deal, but to have acted with great personal courage and decision. It is

[044-110]expected that the Commission he appointed have asked for the separation of

[044-111]Holland from Belgium, and the establishment of a Federal union only; two

[044-112]countries under one King with distinct legislatures, armies, &c. The great

[044-113]towns are quiet. Holland ready to march upon Brussels.

[044-114]I shall not be satisfied unless some of the Bruxellois are hanged for

[044-115]pillage.

[044-116]The answers of the King seem to have been firm and judicious.

[044-117]It is impossible not to admire the constancy of the troops, who bivouacked

[044-118]for eight days in the park.

[044-119]The French Government seems too weak or too timid to prevent outrage in

[044-120]Paris. The printers' devils will have no machinery for printing! It is

[044-121]entertaining to see those who make all revolutions suffer by them.

[044-122]_September 7._

[044-123]Saw Greville at the Treasury. He told me he had got from Lord Chesterfield

[044-124]that Palmerston had no objection to come in. Lord Melbourne had; but they

[044-125]required the sacrifice of Aberdeen, Bathurst, and Arbuthnot. There must be

[044-126]some mistake about this condition. I told Greville if he could get a _fact_

[044-127]to communicate it to the Duke.

[044-128]It is feared the Prince of Orange is gone away to the Hague. He promised

[044-129]Colonel Jones he would be firm.

[044-130]_September 8._

[044-131]The Prince of Orange certainly went to the Hague. He was received there

[044-132]enthusiastically. The proposition he takes is for Federal union. I fear he

[044-133]must submit to some modification of that, or encounter real opposition and

[044-134]civil war.

[044-135]_September 9._

[044-136]Hardinge gives me rather an indifferent account of Ireland. Great animosity

[044-137]still existing between the Catholics and Protestants in the _lower_ ranks;

[044-138]in the higher, peace. A revolutionary disposition raised in the middle

[044-139]classes by the example of Prance. Great dissatisfaction in consequence of

[044-140]the proposed taxation of last session.

[044-141]He told the Duke, and so did Arbuthnot, that he might dispose of their

[044-142]offices if he wanted them. He seems to think Peel is tired and anxious to

[044-143]withdraw--annoyed at the idea of being unpopular, an idea the defeat of his

[044-144]brothers has given him. This makes him less energetic than he should be

[044-145]with respect to the measures necessary to strengthen himself in the House

[044-146]of Commons.

[044-147]_September 10._

[044-148]It seems the desire of separation is general in the Netherlands. It is the

[044-149]result of national prejudice and vanity. The Dutch seem just as violent the

[044-150]other way, and the deputies were rather in danger at Rotterdam. The

[044-151]separation will probably defeat the objects of the great Powers in 1814,

[044-152]for it is idle to expect such terms of Federal union as will enable the two

[044-153]States to act cordially together.

[044-154]_September 11._

[044-155]By withdrawing his troops from the palace, and going to the Hague, the

[044-156]Prince of Orange has ruined his cause. He has appeared to give it up.

[044-157]_September 13._

[044-158]Read on my way to London the intelligence obtained by Lord Heytesbury

[044-159]relative to the Russian trade with Tartary and on the Caspian. It is very

[044-160]full and satisfactory.

[044-161]The 'Times' has a sensible article on the state of France; the want of

[044-162]materials to form a constitutional monarchy, the growing dissatisfaction

[044-163]that _more_ is not done in a revolutionary sense, and the irresponsible

[044-164]power of a deliberative army of 800,000 men.

[044-165]Ghent and Antwerp seem to cling to the connection between Holland and

[044-166]Belgium, and I begin to hope that if France is tranquil the Bruxellois and

[044-167]Liègeois may grow tired and become reasonable. Men cannot play at

[044-168]barricades long when no one attacks them.

[044-169]_September 14._

[044-170]House of Lords. I had to wait half an hour for the seals, which were

[044-171]carelessly carried off by Lady Lyndhurst in her carriage.

[044-172]Talked to Rosslyn. He told me Aberdeen was led to expect another revolution

[044-173]in France. The paper they were going to prosecute was an _affiche_ calling

[044-174]upon the French people to overthrow _l'aristocratie bourgeoise_, which was

[044-175]as bad as the other, and to divide the lands.

[044-176]In the Netherlands the people and their leaders are divided, and if Antwerp

[044-177]and Ghent, &c., remain firm, it signifies little what Brussels does.

[044-178]Brussels will be brought into terms by distress.

[044-179]Rosslyn thinks some of the Whigs as well as of the Tories will be alarmed

[044-180]by events on the Continent and support Government.

[044-181]He hears of no negotiations for accessions.

[044-182]The people of Brunswick, very justly provoked, have turned the Duke

[044-183][Footnote: This was the eccentric Duke who died a few years ago at Geneva,

[044-184]bequeathing his whole property to the city, who have erected a monument to

[044-185]him.] out of the town and burnt his palace. He escaped with ten Hussars. He

[044-186]deserves his fate. I believe he is mad. He is a complete _vaurien._

[044-187]When Parliament is prorogued, as to-day, the peers are without their robes.

[044-188]The Chancellor was in his legal dress. The Commons appear without a summons

[044-189]by their clerks, and the Chancellor merely desires the proclamation to be

[044-190]read. However, as it is held, _improperly,_ to be the first day of the

[044-191]sitting of Parliament, the return of the Scotch peers is laid on the table.

[044-192]All this is sanctioned by precedent, but contrary to reason.
