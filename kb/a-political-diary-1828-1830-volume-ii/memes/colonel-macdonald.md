**Colonel Macdonald** was a British military officer and diplomat who served as the British envoy to Persia during the late 1820s. Stationed at Tabriz, he provided the British government with regular intelligence on the Russo-Persian War and the activities of Russian forces in the region.

== Mission to Persia ==

Colonel Macdonald's correspondence from Tabriz formed an important part of the diarist's understanding of Eastern affairs. In early 1828, the diarist "received a private letter from Colonel Macdonald at Tabriz, with copies of…" (005-160) His despatches arrived regularly: "a letter with enclosures from Colonel Macdonald, dated…" (007-002) These letters kept [[Edward Law, 1st Earl of Ellenborough|Lord Ellenborough]] informed of developments on Persia's northern frontier, where Russian expansion under General Paskewitz threatened British interests.

== Intelligence on Russian Operations ==

Macdonald's reports contained detailed observations of Russian military movements. He noted that "General Paskewitz c…" (005-167) — a reference to General Ivan Paskevich, the Russian commander who had recently defeated Persia in the war of 1826–1828. Macdonald "says there has been no seriou…" (005-170), downplaying the immediate threat while maintaining vigilance. His journal for early 1828 was read with close attention: "Read Colonel Macdonald's Journal for January, Februa…" (032-120) This journal was evidently circulated among senior British officials as a primary source on Persian affairs.

== Role in Anglo-Persian Relations ==

As envoy at a critical moment, Macdonald was the eyes and ears of the British government in a region where Russian influence was growing. His reports helped shape British policy towards Persia at a time when the balance of power in Central Asia was shifting in the aftermath of the Russo-Persian War.
