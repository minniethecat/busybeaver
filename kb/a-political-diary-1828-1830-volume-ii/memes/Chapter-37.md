[037-001]Lord Bathurst says W. Horton is a gentleman. I doubted it. He rather wishes

[037-002]to have Wilmot in office. But the person to be got is Mr. Stanley.

[037-003]We had a discussion on a motion of Lord Londonderry's whether we should

[037-004]proceed with East Retford or not. I followed Lord Grey and spoke very

[037-005]quietly but, I think, reasonably, for going on. I said if we were obliged

[037-006]to postpone any measure, the last we should postpone should be one deeply

[037-007]interesting to the House of Commons and affecting their privileges.

[037-008]I think we did all Peel could require. We had 45 to 13. I remained till

[037-009]eight, but I could stay no longer.

[037-010]Lord Londonderry attacked me again, and said instead of planting a dagger

[037-011]in the side of Lord Grey I should have applied a _healing plaster!_ His

[037-012]comparative civility to the Government to-day was to conciliate their

[037-013]support to Sir R. Gresley for Durham.

[037-014]The Duke told Hardinge yesterday I was always ready. I was a little too

[037-015]lively, but I was of great use.

[037-016]_July 3._

[037-017]The King has done two popular things. He has allowed a passage to be made

[037-018]from Waterloo Place into the Park, and he has dismissed all the late King's

[037-019]French cooks! He will have no foreigners about him.

[037-020]The foreign Ministers were all introduced to him to-day. He was very

[037-021]gracious, Aberdeen said, but he did not choose his topics quite so well as

[037-022]the late King, who had much tact and grace, neither does the King speak

[037-023]French well.

[037-024]Lord Combermere came and had an audience to present a picture of the King

[037-025]of Delhi, painted by an Indian artist. It seemed not ill done, and had the

[037-026]appearance of an ordinary picture, but when placed against the light was a

[037-027]transparency. Lord Combermere did not remain long with the King, and when

[037-028]he came out he seemed annoyed. He remained some time, and the Duke was

[037-029]afraid he remained to be sworn in, in consequence of some incautious

[037-030]promise of the King. It was arranged that Buller, who had a list of the

[037-031]Privy Councillors, should turn him out with the rest who were not so, when

[037-032]the Council began. However, he went away a little before.

[037-033]The Duke of Montrose has resigned, and the King has placed the office at

[037-034]the disposal of the Duke of Wellington.

[037-035]Peel seems to think Lord Graham is dissatisfied and unfriendly. It seems he

[037-036]has been heard complaining of vacillation, &c., on the part of the

[037-037]Government, and does not attend well.

[037-038]The Queen has named Lady Wellesley and Mrs. Berkeley Paget as two Ladies of

[037-039]the Bed-chamber. Valletort is to be in some high situation about the Queen.

[037-040]Lord Errol, her Master of the Horse.

[037-041]I conclude Lord Conyngham will resign, but it is not known.

[037-042]The Duke goes to Windsor to-morrow respecting the late King's papers, the

[037-043]Duke of Cumberland having meditated an _enlèvement_.

[037-044]Peel thinks Brougham really rather mad, and would not be surprised to hear

[037-045]he was confined. Last year he was melancholy, and his friends and _he

[037-046]himself_ feared he might commit suicide. Now he is in an excited state.

[037-047]Peel speaks of him as a most wonderful man in ability.

[037-048]It seems that last night the leaders came down to make an attack, but the

[037-049]followers, not having been consulted, would not support. R. Gordon came

[037-050]over to Herries and said he should vote with Government. Hume, who in the

[037-051]morning had sent to ask Planta whether Government intended to oppose him

[037-052]for Middlesex (a question Planta was desired not to answer till the end of

[037-053]next week), was very civil, and disposed to let business pass. It is not

[037-054]impossible that the House of Commons may have done their business by this

[037-055]day week.

[037-056]I am to look at the Beer Bill, and have already begun to read the Commons'

[037-057]debates upon it.

[037-058]_July 4._

[037-059]Read all the debates on the Beer Bill, made notes, and considered the

[037-060]subject. The Queen seems to have selected her maids of honour from the

[037-061]neighbourhood of Bushey. This is amiable and very right.

[037-062]_July 5._

[037-063]I proposed to Wortley, as Edward Stanley was an acquaintance of his, to

[037-064]give him a hint not to commit himself against the Government just now; but

[037-065]he says he does not know Stanley intimately enough.

[037-066]I asked him whether he did not find the Duke of Wellington very kind. He

[037-067]really had the kindest heart of any man I ever knew. When I looked up I saw

[037-068]the tears in his eyes.

[037-069]Clare told me he heard all the Whigs in our House, except Lord Lansdowne,

[037-070]were determined to push us _à l'outrance_; but Lord Lansdowne thought the

[037-071]Duke must endeavour to strengthen himself during the vacation. He could not

[037-072]do it now, as it would be a confession of weakness; but he thought he would

[037-073]do it before Parliament met. However, the others would not hear him.

[037-074]There was a meeting at Lord Althorpe's yesterday, but I have not heard the

[037-075]result.

[037-076]Talked to Clare about the affairs of Kattywar, and promised to give him

[037-077]precise instructions before he left England.

[037-078]He will remain at Bombay, I think. He tells his mother three years, but he

[037-079]will remain till he has made some money and done something worth going

[037-080]there to do. He has got Elphinstone to make a list of the civil servants

[037-081]_with their characters_.

[037-082]The King took the sacrament yesterday with the Royal Family, and afterwards

[037-083]received the bishops and the judges. He made long speeches to both. Thirty

[037-084]minutes to the first, and twenty to the second.

[037-085]Polignac seems quite firm, although certain he shall be in a minority of 1

[037-086]to 2 or 3. It is expected he will _evade_, and that Villele may be able to

[037-087]go on with the new Chamber.

[037-088]No news from Algiers. 15,000 men are assembled at Toulon as a reserve.

[037-089]_July 6._

[037-090]Cabinet. Peel said the Lord Advocate would resign if we did not pass the

[037-091]Scotch Judicature Bill, so we must struggle through with it. The Welsh

[037-092]Judicature Bill is to be passed too. This will keep us sitting some time.

[037-093]The Commons will have finished on Friday.

[037-094]House. We had the second reading of the Beer Bill. I said a few words to

[037-095]show the inaccuracy of one of Lord Malmesbury's conclusions; but I must

[037-096]speak in detail in the Committee.

[037-097]_July 7._

[037-098]Last night we had 247 to 93, a great division. The Tories in general voting

[037-099]with us.

[037-100]Looked over again all the papers relative to the Beer Bill.

[037-101]In my way back from Roehampton met Lord Ravensworth, who told me the King

[037-102]had the gout, and that he had given the Guelphic ribbon to his three sons-

[037-103]in-law. He likewise told me what I knew before, that the Duke of Montrose

[037-104]had resigned.

[037-105]I told him of the dismissal of the French cooks, which horrified him.

[037-106]Cabinet dinner at Herries's. All the House of Commons pleased with their

[037-107]division. They got three county members to speak for others. The Whigs did

[037-108]not like the motion, and were unwilling to divide. Robert Grant divided the

[037-109]House. The King was delighted with the division.

[037-110]He came to town to-day, almost for nothing, and received the Duke and

[037-111]others. He sent for Lord Rosslyn and told, him he had made his regiment the

[037-112]Queen's Own.

[037-113]He has changed the uniform of the navy, which is to be blue with red cuffs

[037-114]and facings. He wore the uniform so to-day.

[037-115]Aberdeen introduced Prince William of Prussia. The King desired him to

[037-116]stay, and said he should never receive foreigners except in his presence,

[037-117]and never but in his naval uniform. He should wear the military uniform now

[037-118]and then, but as little as possible.

[037-119]All the cavalry are to be in red.

[037-120]No news from Algiers. The Duke thinks they must be rather in want of

[037-121]provisions. The French are all in a state of sentiment, as Bourmont's

[037-122]second son has been dangerously wounded. Certainly the way in which it is

[037-123]mentioned in the dispatch is good, and indeed Bourmont, a very clever man,

[037-124]and first under fire with his four sons, will soon be popular with an army.

[037-125]Polignac seems to be insensible rather than bold. He thinks all will go on

[037-126]well still.

[037-127]The present intention is that we should all be in gala at the funeral, with

[037-128]black scarfs, &c.

[037-129]I have asked several to dine at Roehampton and go from thence.

[037-130]The Queen is to be present, I suppose, in the King's pew. The King is

[037-131]certainly to be chief mourner.

[037-132]We had a great deal of talk after dinner about elections. I fear they have

[037-133]not been attended to in time. It is hoped Seaford will be conquered from

[037-134]Lord Seaford, and that the two Grants will be thrown out. We have nobody

[037-135]for Surrey and nobody for Middlesex.

[037-136]_July 8._

[037-137]House. Answered the Duke of Richmond on the sale of Beer Bill. The Duke

[037-138]seemed very well satisfied, and the House was very attentive and cheered

[037-139]frequently. We had on a division 60 to 15.

[037-140]_July 9._

[037-141]Lord Radnor made some observations upon the continuing of the Irish Arms

[037-142]Bill without explaining the reason, the Bill having been introduced in

[037-143]troublesome times and expiring at the end of this Session. Lord Grey

[037-144]supported him. It is clear Lord Durham and Lord Radnor evidently intend to

[037-145]make us look about us and not do work in a slovenly manner. I cannot find

[037-146]fault with them.

[037-147]Lord Durham moved the printing of the Appropriation List, which was

[037-148]negatived without a division, as unusual; but I dare say he will ask

[037-149]questions as to some of the items.

[037-150]_July 10._

[037-151]As I was coming home from the office I called on Hardinge. He considers the

[037-152]division to have been invaluable to us here and even to France. Certainly

[037-153]the French funds rose when it was known the present King held the same

[037-154]course as his predecessor. Hardinge thinks many men are disposed to support

[037-155]the Duke's Government under the idea that all sorts of calamities would

[037-156]attend the weak Government which must succeed it. He thinks Palmerston the

[037-157]best man to have in Goulburn's place, Goulburn going to the Speakership. He

[037-158]thinks W. Horton would be better than Frankland Lewis as his successor at

[037-159]the War Office, it being necessary in either case to get Lord F. Leveson

[037-160]into the House of Lords. Fitzgerald has written to Hardinge, and seems

[037-161]eager about politics. I wish he was well and could come into office again.

[037-162]I do not know that the Duke or anybody would have any objection to

[037-163]Palmerston coming in by himself; but I doubt Huskisson's ever being in

[037-164]office again while the Duke lives. Neither will the Grants come in--indeed

[037-165]it is to be hoped they will both be turned out of their seats.

[037-166]_July 12._

[037-167]Office. Backhouse brought the account of Sir J. Macdonald's expected death;

[037-168]the date, May 12. Sir Henry Willock will take charge of the mission _ad

[037-169]interim_. He may be a sensible man, but the loss of Macdonald is severe. I

[037-170]do not know how we shall replace him.

[037-171]Cabinet at 2. The business was the eternal slave question--what answer

[037-172]should be given to Brougham to-morrow. He is expected to propose some

[037-173]pledge of proceeding _legislatively_ in the next session as to the

[037-174]admission of slave evidence and other points. A Bill has been prepared

[037-175]making slave evidence admissible, and it would probably have been

[037-176]introduced but for the early termination of the session. However, there

[037-177]seems to be great reluctance to embark in a contest with the Colonial

[037-178]legislatures. The foolish resolutions moved by Canning are deeply

[037-179]regretted. I was the only man who objected to them, when, two years after

[037-180]they had been found of no avail, it was proposed that the Lords should

[037-181]concur in them. Peel objects to any pledge on the part of Parliament, more

[037-182]particularly on the eve of a dissolution. It is thought that _by paying

[037-183]from our funds_ for an improved judicature in the West Indies we may induce

[037-184]the colonies to acquiesce in the admission of slave evidence, although

[037-185]imposed by the interposition of Parliament. I doubt it, and if we pass a

[037-186]law to which the colonies are adverse, which they will regard as being _no

[037-187]law_, how are we to execute it? We may make judges and pay them, but we

[037-188]must procure submission to those judges, and further, we must make

[037-189]_jurymen_.
