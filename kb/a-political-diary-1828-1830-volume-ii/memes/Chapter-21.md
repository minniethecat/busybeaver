[021-001]_January 15._

[021-002]The Duke gave the Cabinet an account of his interview with the King. The

[021-003]King was with Munster and the Duke of Cumberland when he went; but the Duke

[021-004]was admitted in about forty minutes, which time he passed with the Lady

[021-005]Conyngham, who told him he must expect a storm.

[021-006]The King was in bed, looking very ill. He said, 'Well, what is your

[021-007]business?' and seemed at first most indignant. The Duke, however, corrected

[021-008]his misapprehensions--showed him the dates, and proved that he had known

[021-009]from the first that it was probable Leopold would be proposed by France.

[021-010]The proposition was made by us to Prince Frederick of Orange on November

[021-011]13, his final answer received on August 11 (there may be a slight error in

[021-012]these dates, as I write from memory). In the meantime the King of France

[021-013]had about November 29, when Leopold took leave of him, told him he would

[021-014]propose him. This was known here immediately, and Leopold distinctly told

[021-015]he could not be heard of till our own candidate was disposed of. The

[021-016]regular proposal of Leopold did not arrive here till January 1, and was

[021-017]communicated to the King with the _projet_ of a protocol, for it was no

[021-018]more, on the 9th.

[021-019]It was still only a proposition, and the Government now come to advise the

[021-020]King to consent to it.

[021-021]The Duke showed the King that there had been ten candidates in all:--

[021-022]Prince Philip of Hesse Homburgh, Prince John of Saxony, Prince Frederick of

[021-023]Orange, Prince Charles of Bavaria, Prince Otho of Bavaria, the Archduke

[021-024]Maximilian, Prince Paul of Wurtemburgh, Prince Leopold, Prince Emilius of

[021-025]Hesse Darmstadt, and Prince Charles of Mecklenburgh.

[021-026]The seven first either declined or were rejected. Prince Emilius of Hesse

[021-027]Darmstadt was an aide-de-camp of Bonaparte, and the King would not have

[021-028]him, and with regard to the last, Prince Charles of Mecklenburgh, the Duke

[021-029]showed the King he was much more nearly connected with Prussia, and so with

[021-030]Russia, than with England. The King admitted this, and seemed to have been

[021-031]brought into good humour, when he became so ill that he was obliged to beg

[021-032]the Duke to leave him, and soon after sent him word he would see him in two

[021-033]days. The Duke says he was really unwell, and in fact was taking physic all

[021-034]the time he was with him.

[021-035]The Duke showed the King that _he alone_ had not the power of nomination.

[021-036]He had one voice out of three, and there were ten candidates.

[021-037]'At any rate,' said the King, 'Claremont reverts to the Crown.' The Duke,

[021-038]fearing he might wish to give it to the Duke of Cumberland, or somebody,

[021-039]asked the Chancellor to-day to look at the Act of Parliament and tell us

[021-040]what becomes of Claremont in the event of Leopold's being made King of

[021-041]Greece. The Chancellor looked and thought Claremont would certainly remain

[021-042]to Leopold, and if he died or gave it up go, not to the Crown, that is, not

[021-043]to the King, but, by specific enactment, become a portion of the revenue

[021-044]under the Woods and Forests. Of course Leopold will give up Claremont,

[021-045]which is in fact a source of expense. The Duke said Leopold would be at

[021-046]least innocuous, and he might be of use. The King asked how we could be

[021-047]such fools as to think he would be of any use.

[021-048]While the Duke was with the King the Duke of Cumberland was with Lady

[021-049]Conyngham, and told her, amongst other things, that the 'Times' was the

[021-050]Duke of Wellington's paper.

[021-051]The 'Morning Journal' is _his_ paper, and uses the expressions he puts into

[021-052]the King's mouth.

[021-053]Aberdeen says Leopold is quite aware of all he will have to go through.

[021-054]He has written to Lord Stuart to ascertain whether there is any truth in

[021-055]the report of his being engaged to the daughter of the Duke of Orleans.

[021-056]I cannot help thinking that is so, and that the French proposition

[021-057]originates in that.

[021-058]_January 16._

[021-059]Read last night a very interesting report by Captain Wade of his mission to

[021-060]Runjeet Singh in 1827.

[021-061]Received a box from the Duke with a circular note saying the King is not

[021-062]well enough to see him before Tuesday. He has seen no one since he saw the

[021-063]Duke, and the Duke hears he was not mistaken in his judgment of the effect

[021-064]he thought he had produced upon the King's mind; so I suppose this matter,

[021-065]which looked threatening at first, may be considered as settled, although

[021-066]not yet formally terminated.

[021-067]The King will, I dare say, make another plunge when he finds Claremont will

[021-068]not be at his personal disposal, as he seems to have imagined.

[021-069]_January 19._

[021-070]Read all day Sir Thomas Munro's Life, which contains a great deal of

[021-071]interesting and valuable information. He was a very great man.

[021-072]Talked to Hardinge of various matters. He was at Stowe when Lord Chandos in

[021-073]the middle of the night received a note from his father, communicating one

[021-074]from Sir. W. Fremantle, which informed him that the King was going to turn

[021-075]us all to the right about. Lord Chandos said to Hardinge he would never

[021-076]belong to a Government of which the Duke of Wellington was not a member.

[021-077]_January 19._

[021-078]Read the rest of the 'Life of Sir Thomas Munro,' a most valuable book. I

[021-079]believe there are no books so really useful as the lives of great and good

[021-080]men.

[021-081]On my arrival in town, found a note from Hardinge, who thinks the despatch

[021-082]as to watching the Russians and navigating the Indus quite perfect.

[021-083]The Duke went to-day to Windsor. About eight he sent round a box containing

[021-084]a note, saying that the King consented to Prince Leopold's being King of

[021-085]Greece. So for the present, at least, we are safe again. I never had much

[021-086]apprehension.

[021-087]_January 20._

[021-088]Cabinet dinner. Lord Bathurst not there. We had very little talk upon

[021-089]public matters. The Duke had a bad cold. The opinion seemed to be that the

[021-090]press of the session would be upon domestic matters, for the reduction of

[021-091]establishments and taxation.

[021-092]The King wrote to the Duke and _grumpily acceded_ to Leopold's appointment.

[021-093]Leopold is very _uppish_ upon the subject. He was at Cobham to-day and

[021-094]yesterday.

[021-095]I am to see Peel on Sunday at half-past one on Indian matters.

[021-096]_January 22._

[021-097]At one, Privy Council to consider the petition of the E. I. C. for the

[021-098]recall of Sir J. P. Grant. The Lord President, Lord Chief Baron, and Lord

[021-099]Chief Justice of Common Pleas present. The committee reported that they did

[021-100]not consider themselves warranted at present in advising Sir J. P. Grant's

[021-101]removal, but they thought it right he should be directed to proceed home

[021-102]that the several matters objected to him might be investigated.

[021-103]I took the opportunity of the presence of two judges to get a legal opinion

[021-104]as to Sir J. Malcolm's conduct in resisting the service of the Habeas

[021-105]Corpus _ad testificandum_.

[021-106]I took the opportunity likewise of laying before the two judges the change

[021-107]of circumstances since the institution of the Supreme Court, and the

[021-108]present reasons for making their jurisdiction without the limits of the

[021-109]Presidency the exception and not the rule.

[021-110]The judges seemed to enter into my view. The Lord Chief Baron suggested

[021-111]that there might be a previous enquiry before the Country Court, which

[021-112]might for that purpose be a sort of grand jury. [Footnote: _I.e._ when the

[021-113]case was to be transferred to the Supreme Court.]

[021-114]Lord Hill showed me a letter from Sir F. Watson addressed to Sir B. Taylor,

[021-115]as the King's first aide-de-camp, and directing him as such, by the King's

[021-116]command, to intimate to Lord Hill the pleasure it would give His Majesty to

[021-117]know that Lord Hill had given Captain Scarlett, the son of the Attorney-

[021-118]General, an opportunity of purchasing a majority. Captain Scarlett is a

[021-119]very young captain--and Lord Hill feels the thing asked cannot be done. He

[021-120]was going to see the Duke of Wellington about it. Not very long ago the

[021-121]King gave away a regiment without asking Lord Hill--however, that was

[021-122]settled; but it is clear that, unless Lord Hill is allowed to exercise the

[021-123]fair patronage of his office, he will resign.

[021-124]_January 26._

[021-125]Cabinet. It seems the French have acceded to the proposals of the Pasha of

[021-126]Egypt, and finding 50,000 men would be required to take Algiers, prefer his

[021-127]operating with 40,000 of his own. He pretends to have made arrangements

[021-128]which will secure an easy conquest, and promises to place Tunis, Tripoli,

[021-129]and Algiers under regular governments, nominally under the Sultan, whose

[021-130]consent he reckons upon, and capable of preserving the relations of peace

[021-131]with other Mediterranean Powers.

[021-132]The Pasha's army is commanded by French officers, and the annexation of

[021-133]these States to Egypt would be their practical annexation to France. When

[021-134]his army is disseminated along the coast of Africa, I might realise my

[021-135]dream of taking Egypt from India.

[021-136]We considered the proposed order in Council relative to the slave

[021-137]regulations of the King's own ceded colonies. The Duke was evidently not

[021-138]well, and he was rather out of humour. We were three hours and a half in

[021-139]Cabinet. He made various objections to the proposed regulations. He

[021-140]impressed upon us the danger of tampering with the rights of property. We

[021-141]were doing that with property of an _odious_ character, which we should not

[021-142]do in England. He pressed the effect in the West Indies and the example

[021-143]everywhere. He seemed to complain that the regulations were different from

[021-144]those agreed to in the summer. Sir G. Murray was very quiet. He is a very

[021-145]sensible man, but he is overawed by the Duke, having been under him so

[021-146]long.

[021-147]Poor old Tierney is dead, for which I am very sorry. He was a very good

[021-148]friend of mine.

[021-149]_January 27._

[021-150]Cabinet at four. There can be no Council to-morrow, as Greville has the

[021-151]gout and Buller is in Cornwall.

[021-152]There is to be an intimation sent to the Pasha to the effect that we

[021-153]_disapprove_ of the proposed attempt to conquer Tripoli, Tunis, and

[021-154]Algiers. France is to be told the same. I wished conditional orders to be

[021-155]given to the Fleet, and that the Pasha should be told orders had been

[021-156]given. It being doubtful whether French vessels might not convoy the

[021-157]Egyptian fleet and transports, I thought we had better now consider what we

[021-158]should do in that event; that we had better not threaten without

[021-159]determining to execute our threat, and that we should consider how we

[021-160]should deal with the French ships if we stopped the Egyptian--in short not

[021-161]take a first step which might make a second necessary, without knowing in

[021-162]our own minds what that second step should be. The Duke thinks the French

[021-163]will back out when they know our _disapprobation_, and that at any rate the

[021-164]Pasha would. I rather doubt this of either of them.

[021-165]The French say they have a sort of quarrel with Tripoli, but none with

[021-166]Tunis, and they enter into a scheme for conquering both as stepping-stones

[021-167]to Algiers. Tunis in their hands would be more dangerous than Algiers.

[021-168]Hardinge told me he had had a long conversation with Peel the other day on

[021-169]the state of the country. He thought Peel seemed to have apprehensions, and

[021-170]to think that if the King, through some intrigue of the Brunswickers, got

[021-171]rid of the Duke, things would go very ill indeed; that the authority of the

[021-172]Duke alone kept things quiet. England is in a bad state, because the

[021-173]country gentlemen have ill-paid rents; but Scotland and Ireland do very

[021-174]well, and the trade of the country is not depressed.

[021-175]Cabinet dinner at the Chancellor's. The Duke of Montrose there, as it was

[021-176]to have been a dinner for the sheriffs. I told the Duke of my notion of

[021-177]altering the law of succession to property in India, and enabling all

[021-178]existing proprietors to leave their estates as they please.
