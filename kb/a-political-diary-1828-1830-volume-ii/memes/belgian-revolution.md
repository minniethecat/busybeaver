# Belgian Revolution

## Overview

The Belgian Revolution, which began in August 1830, led to the separation of the southern provinces from the United Kingdom of the Netherlands and the establishment of an independent Belgium. This crisis unfolded alongside the July Revolution in France and placed additional strain on Wellington's government, which was already under domestic political pressure.

## The Crisis Unfolds

As the crisis developed, British officials watched with alarm. Ellenborough recorded that a colleague "is much alarmed by the events in Belgium." (043-149) The situation threatened the post-Napoleonic settlement of 1815, which had united the Low Countries under Dutch rule. The question of "Holland from Belgium" (044-111) became a pressing diplomatic issue for the British government.

## Diplomatic Ramifications

The Belgian crisis complicated Britain's European diplomacy at a crucial moment. The Wellington government, already weakened domestically, faced the challenge of maintaining stability while the Vienna settlement unravelled. The crisis ultimately led to the London Conference of 1830, which would recognize Belgian independence.
