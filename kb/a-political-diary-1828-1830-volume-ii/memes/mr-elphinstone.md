**Mountstuart Elphinstone** (1779–1859) was a British statesman and East India Company administrator who served as Governor of Bombay from 1819 to 1827. He appears frequently in [[Lord Ellenborough]]'s diary as an authority on Indian affairs, consulted on matters ranging from colonial administration to foreign policy.

== Governor of Bombay and Indian Authority ==

Elphinstone had returned from India by the time of the diary, but his expertise was still in high demand. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records dining with "Mr. Elphinstone, the late Governor of Bombay" (004-144), marking him as a figure of continuing influence in imperial circles. His knowledge of Indian governance was considered indispensable, and the diary notes that "Elphinstone approved generally of what I" (029-047) proposed regarding Indian policy.

== The India Committee ==

A significant portion of Elphinstone's diary appearances concerns his testimony before parliamentary committees. [[Lord Ellenborough]] records: "Committee. Examined Mr. Elphinstone. He gave very good evidence." (026-136) He was subsequently "re-examined by Lord Lansdowne" (026-141), indicating his evidence was considered weighty enough to warrant cross-examination by the opposition.

== The Nazarre and Kattawar Affairs ==

Elphinstone was involved in specific Indian territorial disputes. The diary notes a letter "from Mr. Elphinstone on Nazarre" (014-168), a disputed territory where he took the side of the local proprietor against competing claims. He also provided intelligence "respecting the chiefs of Kattawar" (027-097), a region in Gujarat where British paramountcy was being asserted over princely states.

== Persian Mission ==

In a notable episode, [[Lord Ellenborough]] "Called on Elphinstone. Offered him Persia." (040-108) — proposing a diplomatic mission to Persia. Elphinstone "was much pleased, but doubted whether his health would allow" (040-108) him to accept. This reflects both the high regard in which his diplomatic skills were held and his declining health in later years. The King himself "was very gracious indeed to Elphinstone, whom he saw for the" (039-098) first time around this period.

== Character and Reputation ==

The diary presents Elphinstone as a man of singular judgment. The Duke of Wellington remarked that "he was a very clever" (025-131) man after speaking with him for "an hour and a half with Elphinstone last night" (025-132). [[Lord Ellenborough]] himself observed that among those who write on Indian subjects, "who writes _common sense_ is Elphinstone" (027-042) — high praise from the diarist's exacting pen.
