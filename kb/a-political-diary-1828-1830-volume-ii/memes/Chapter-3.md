[003-001]I have always been for getting out of the treaty. We have been dragged

[003-002]along very unwillingly--we have been subjected to much humiliation. We seem

[003-003]to me to have gained nothing by all our compliances. We have been led on

[003-004]from the violation of one principle to that of another. Our position has

[003-005]discouraged Turkey. We have been made the tools of Russia, and have been

[003-006]duped with our eyes open. I think the sooner we get out of this false

[003-007]position the better, and there is no time so favourable for us to hold

[003-008]strong language as this, when by the settlement of the Catholic question we

[003-009]are really strengthened, and when all foreign Powers believe we are yet

[003-010]more strengthened than we are. The Duke is certainly for getting out. He

[003-011]has long wished it.

[003-012]A paper of Peel's was read suggesting the difficulties in which we should

[003-013]still be placed by our moral obligation towards the Greeks, and by our

[003-014]reasonable fear that on the principles of the Greek Treaty, to which we

[003-015]have unfortunately given our adhesion, Russia and France may combine and

[003-016]make a partition treaty. My expectation is that Russia and France would

[003-017]soon quarrel, and I think I could before now have made them jealous of each

[003-018]other, but we have done nothing.

[003-019]After much conversation, V. Fitzgerald agreeing with me and the others

[003-020]saying nothing, it was determined to insist upon the freedom of

[003-021]communication with Candia under the protocol, to insist upon the Greeks

[003-022]withdrawing from their advanced position near Prevesa _under the protocol_,

[003-023]and to insist likewise upon the withdrawing of the French troops from the

[003-024]Morea, according to the engagement.

[003-025]I am not satisfied with this. Every part of our diplomacy has been

[003-026]unfortunate. We have succeeded in nothing. I predicted if we became engaged

[003-027]in the war, it would be ultimately on a little point and not upon a great

[003-028]one. Our diplomacy cannot be defended. It is our weak point.

[003-029]House. All the Catholics there. Every good old name in England.

[003-030]The Duke of Norfolk is much pleased with the Duke of Wellington's answer to

[003-031]his enquiry as to the propriety of addressing the King. I am going to send

[003-032]him the Duke's original letter as a _record_.

[003-033]The King certainly received the Protestant peers, and particularly those

[003-034]who had been at Windsor, with great favour, and so the Bishop of Durham.

[003-035]The Duke of Cumberland stood at the King's left hand, and quizzed the

[003-036]people as they passed. He seemed _rayonnant_.

[003-037]After dinner I had some conversation with Loch, the Chairman, as to

[003-038]governing India in the King's name. He does not positively object. I think

[003-039]I shall be able to carry that point. I consider it to be of the most

[003-040]essential importance.

[003-041]_April 30._

[003-042]Cabinet at 12. Determined to fund eight millions of Exchequer Bills. No

[003-043]taxes to be taken off or imposed. We had some conversation as to the East

[003-044]Retford question. V. Fitzgerald communicated a proposal from Littleton to

[003-045]propose the adjournment of all discussion upon the subject till next year,

[003-046]as it is evident nothing can be done this year. Littleton proposed this

[003-047]because he wished to disappoint the mischievous designs of some people.

[003-048](Palmerston particularly.)

[003-049]It was determined to adhere to the line taken by the Government last year--

[003-050]namely, to that of throwing East Retford into the hundred. The Duke was

[003-051]decidedly of opinion that whatever we did we should do from ourselves, and

[003-052]certainly not act in concert with an enemy. The Tories look to our conduct

[003-053]upon this question as the touchstone.

[003-054]Drawing-room. The King, as yesterday, very civil to the Brunswickers and

[003-055]taking no notice of our friends. He took particular notice of the

[003-056]Brazilians. Madame de Lièven is endeavouring to form a Government with the

[003-057]Duke of Cumberland, the Ultra-Tories, the Canningites, and some Whigs.

[003-058]The King is very Russian. I believe all this will end in nothing. The

[003-059]Chancellor thinks they may try to make a change when Parliament is up, and

[003-060]so have six months before them. They may think of it; but the only object

[003-061]of such a Government would be _revenge._ They cannot repeal the Relief

[003-062]Bill, nor do they wish to pursue a different line of policy either at home

[003-063]or abroad.

[003-064]The foreigners think that having settled the Catholic question we are ready

[003-065]to draw the sword, and find a field of battle wherever we can. This the

[003-066]Russians are afraid of, and hence arises in some degree their wish to

[003-067]overthrow the Duke's Government; but the real foundation of all the Russian

[003-068]intrigues is Madame de Lièven's hatred for the Duke, and her rage at

[003-069]feeling she has overreached herself.

[003-070]_May 1._

[003-071]Sir Thomas Tyrwhitt was with the King for two hours to-day, the Duke of

[003-072]Cumberland being in the room and the King in bed. The King is very much out

[003-073]of humour, and abused everything and everybody. He is very angry at ladies

[003-074]being admitted to the House of Lords, and particularly at their going in

[003-075]such numbers the day the Duke of Norfolk took his seat. The Duke of

[003-076]Cumberland has sworn he will not leave England till he has turned out the

[003-077]present Ministers. He is the only colonel of the Horse Guards who ever does

[003-078]duty--Lord Cathcart being absent and Lord Harrington incapable. When he

[003-079]last got the gold stick from Lord Harrington he swore he would never let it

[003-080]out of his hands. As gold stick he ordered the gates of the Horse Guards to

[003-081]be closed the day of the Drawing-room, and thus obliged all the Ministers

[003-082]who dressed in Downing Street to go all round.

[003-083]He told Clanwilliam to-day with great satisfaction that the King never

[003-084]could again be on good terms with his Ministers.

[003-085]No arrangement is yet made with the Master of the Rolls. Everything waits

[003-086]for the legal promotions. The King will be delighted with Scarlett

[003-087][Footnote: Sir James Scarlett, afterwards Lord Abinger.] as Attorney-

[003-088]General, and the Chancellor tells me Bickersteth is to be Solicitor. I

[003-089]recollect hearing of him at Cambridge. He is a very clever man and a good

[003-090]speaker. Tindal is of course to be Master of the Rolls. I am most anxious

[003-091]to give up the Privy Seal to Rosslyn.

[003-092]_May 3._

[003-093]Cabinet at 2. Decided the Government was to take the same line exactly this

[003-094]year as to East Retford (that is, as to giving the two members to the

[003-095]Hundred) that it took last year. However, as it is impossible to get any

[003-096]Bill through the Lords this year, Peel will be very willing to accede to

[003-097]any proposition for postponing the whole question till next session.

[003-098]On the question of Irish Education and on that of the grant to Maynooth,

[003-099]the vote will be as before--it being said that the state of the session and

[003-100]the circumstances of the present period make it advisable that the question

[003-101]of any change should be deferred. Indeed, Ministers have not had time to

[003-102]consider it.

[003-103]Many of Lord Anglesey's letters to Peel and of Peel's answers were read. We

[003-104]have a very strong case against him on his letter to Dr. Curtis, which by a

[003-105]letter from Dr. Curtis to the Duke we know Lord Anglesey directed Dr.

[003-106]Murray to publish if it could be done with Curtis's consent, and which Dr.

[003-107]Murray did publish without obtaining such consent.

[003-108]Curtis's letter is dated January 2.

[003-109]Lord Anglesey wrote to Curtis for the Duke's letter and his answer, and had

[003-110]them two days before December 23, the date of his letter to Curtis.

[003-111]Peel thinks the East Indian Committee should not be refused. It is better

[003-112]for the East Indian Company that it should be granted than refused. I

[003-113]entirely coincide with him.

[003-114]_May 4._

[003-115]Coal Committee at 12. Met Lord Bathhurst, with whom I had some conversation

[003-116]as to the Duke's reading letters in answer to Lord Anglesey. He begged me

[003-117]to go to the Duke, and try to induce him not to do so. I found the Duke

[003-118]agreeing with me entirely as to the danger of the president, and disposed

[003-119]to read only what might be absolutely necessary.

[003-120]Lord Anglesey brought forward his motion for 'the letter of recall.'

[003-121]The Duke answered him, and so well that even Lord Holland could not say one

[003-122]word. So the thing ended.

[003-123]The Duke had been assured by the King, and within the last fortnight the

[003-124]King had given the same assurance to Aberdeen, that Lord Anglesey had not

[003-125]_permission_ to read confidential letters.

[003-126]Lord Anglesey stated that he had the King's permission.

[003-127]The Duke certainly seemed to contradict him.

[003-128]Lord Londonderry threw a note over to me suggesting that the contradiction

[003-129]was so direct there might be an awkward explanation out of doors unless the

[003-130]thing were softened down.

[003-131]I mentioned this to Lord Bathurst. He thought not.

[003-132]However, when he replied, Lord Anglesey treated the contradiction as

[003-133]absolute, and Lord Bathurst told the Duke he must give some explanation,

[003-134]which the Duke did, saying he did not mean to accuse Lord Anglesey of

[003-135]declaring he had the King's permission when he had not, but only that he

[003-136]had reason to think he had not. In fact, the King, as we always thought,

[003-137]told the Duke one thing and Lord Anglesey another; and the only result of

[003-138]the debate is that the King is proved to have told a lie.

[003-139]Lord Wharncliffe, who overtook me as I was riding home, considered Lord

[003-140]Anglesey to be blown out of water.

[003-141]At Lady Brownlow's ball I talked with Lord Farnborough, Longford, and

[003-142]Beresford. All thought the reading of the letters should have been stopped,

[003-143]and that the Duke did wrong to read anything. We could not stop the reading

[003-144]of the letters when the King's permission to read them was stated

[003-145]distinctly by Lord Anglesey. The misery is that we have a lying master.

[003-146]_May 5._

[003-147]I called at the Treasury and saw the Duke. On the subject of what took

[003-148]place yesterday he said, that having received the King's commands to

[003-149]declare Lord Anglesey had not his permission to read the letters, he could

[003-150]not do otherwise than make the observations he did. The gravamen of the

[003-151]charge against Lord Anglesey as arising out of those letters is that in the

[003-152]last he declares his intention of using them as public documents; and this

[003-153]being the ground upon which the King had acquiesced in his being relieved,

[003-154]for the King to have afterwards permitted the reading of those letters

[003-155]would have been a withdrawal of confidence from his Ministers.

[003-156]I met Lord Ravensworth and talked to him upon the subject. He seemed to be

[003-157]in a sort of alarm as to what took place yesterday. This is superfluous.

[003-158]The Duke's explanation that he did not mean to say Lord Anglesey had reason

[003-159]to think he was permitted to read those letters was quite sufficient. The

[003-160]Duke added that he had understood the contrary.

[003-161]Lord Ravensworth seemed to think his Royal master came the worst off--which

[003-162]is true.

[003-163]He told me the Duke of Cumberland had been abusing every one at Lady

[003-164]Brownlow's last night, and had declared, as he has before, that he would

[003-165]not go away till he had us out.

[003-166]Lord Anglesey is reported to be very ill to-day.

[003-167]_May 6._

[003-168]Cabinet dinner at Sir G. Murray's. The Duke saw the King to-day. He was in

[003-169]good humour, and said the Duke was quite right in declaring Lord Anglesey

[003-170]had not his permission to read the letters. It seems the King said the same

[003-171]thing in the Duke of Cumberland's presence on Monday at dinner, and this

[003-172]made the Duke so very angry that evening.

[003-173]We had a very good division last night on the Retford question. Almost all

[003-174]the Brunswickers voted with us--none against us.

[003-175]In fact the Government is very strong.
