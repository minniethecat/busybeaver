# Archbishop of Canterbury

## Overview

William Howley (1766–1848) served as Archbishop of Canterbury from 1828 until his death. A high churchman and conservative in ecclesiastical politics, he presided over the Church of England during the turbulent period of Catholic emancipation and parliamentary reform. In Ellenborough's diary, he appears in connection with the Catholic Relief Bill and Privy Council proceedings.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records the Archbishop's performance during the Catholic emancipation debates with characteristic bluntness: "Archbishop of Canterbury drivelled" (P01-061). This stands in contrast to the Primate of Ireland, who "made a strong" speech (P01-061). Despite the poor showing, the government carried the day decisively at 139 to 19.

The Archbishop later lent his support to the Catholic Relief Bill, being recorded among those agreeing "that the Bill should be read a second time" (005-134). After the bill's passage, his role shifted to administrative ecclesiastical duties. Ellenborough notes his presence at a Privy Council where the "Archbishop of Canterbury ordered to frame a" prayer or form of thanksgiving (031-148).

## Key Appearances

- **Catholic emancipation debate**: The Archbishop spoke poorly in the Lords — "drivelled" in Ellenborough's assessment — while the government carried the division 139 to 19 (P01-061).
- **Bill reading**: He concurred that the Catholic Relief Bill should proceed to a second reading (005-134).
- **Privy Council**: Attended the Privy Council following the King's death, tasked with framing ecclesiastical documents (031-148).
