[053-001]It was determined to endeavour to induce the mob to disperse as soon as the

[053-002]Rotunda was full, and then to read the Riot Act as soon as the law

[053-003]justified it, and to disperse them by police. There will be common

[053-004]constables there besides. Mr. Chambers will be there; and if he sends for

[053-005]assistance to the Horse Guards, two bodies of fifty each, each headed by a

[053-006]magistrate, will go over Westminster Bridge, one by Stamford Street, the

[053-007]other by the Blackfriars Road, to the Rotunda.

[053-008]There will be about 300 or 400 new police there. I suggested to Chambers

[053-009]the having a boat ready to take a note to the Horse Guards, as his

[053-010]messenger might be impeded in the streets. Persons are flocking in from

[053-011]Brixton and Deptford, and by the Kentish roads.

[053-012]Mr. Chambers represents the mob as very cowardly.

[053-013]There are two shorthand writers at the Rotunda. The speeches are not very

[053-014]seditious.

[053-015]The _Times_ is turning against us, and I hear the Press is worse than it

[053-016]was--none of the newspapers fighting our measure well.

[053-017]After the Duke was gone there was a little said about Reform. Many

[053-018]defections announced--the Staffords, young Hope, Lord Talbot, the Clives

[053-019]very unwilling to vote against it, thinking the public feeling so strong. I

[053-020]suggested that neither the Duke nor Peel had gone further than to say that

[053-021]no proposition had yet been made which seemed to them to be safe, and that

[053-022]we might perhaps agree to a Committee to inquire into the state of the

[053-023]Representation, and afterwards defeat the specific measures. Peel said he

[053-024]thought the terms of the motion did not signify. It was 'Reform, or no

[053-025]Reform!' He never would undertake the question of Reform. Lord Bathurst, of

[053-026]course, was against me, and generally they were; but they had, before my

[053-027]suggestion, said, 'Had we not better, then, consider what we shall do?'

[053-028]Afterwards they said nothing.

[053-029]Peel and the Duke both think the measure generally approved, and Peel is

[053-030]satisfied with the House of Commons. Goulburn, on the other hand, thinks

[053-031]the general feeling is against us.

[053-032]House. Nothing said. There was a crowd at the door, and much hooting. I had

[053-033]to drive my horse through it. While we were in the House the mob was

[053-034]removed by the police. Not knowing this, Clanwilliam and I came home in the

[053-035]Duke's carriage. There was no mob till we passed Bridge Street, where there

[053-036]were a good many people who recognised the carriage, and followed it

[053-037]hooting. They ran into Downing Street, and we passed on through the Horse

[053-038]Guards. I was glad to find a Grenadier at the Duke's. Clanwilliam said he

[053-039]had ten or twelve there.

[053-040]Altered the Bill respecting the fees of officers in the Superior Courts,

[053-041]and sent it with a letter to Lawford, appointing eleven on Thursday for

[053-042]seeing him at the office.

[053-043]_November 10._

[053-044]Office. Wrote a placard and showed it to Peel, who will have it printed.

[053-045]The tide is turning. Carlisle began to abuse the Duke last night, and found

[053-046]it would not do. Some cried out, 'He gained the Battle of Waterloo!' and

[053-047]Carlisle was obliged to begin to praise him. He then tried to abuse the new

[053-048]police, but that would not do, and he was obliged to praise them too.

[053-049]There was a good deal of rioting in different parts of the town. The City

[053-050]Police was inefficient, and at Temple Bar rascals were masters for some

[053-051]time. The new police, however, gave them a terrible licking opposite

[053-052]Southampton Street, and not far from Northumberland House. They got licked,

[053-053]too, in Piccadilly--and the whole was put down by the Civil Power.

[053-054]The military were so arranged that, had they been called for, they would

[053-055]have enveloped the rioters. The thing may be considered as nearly put down,

[053-056]and the Government strengthened by it.

[053-057]The Funds have risen to-day, and are as high as before the postponement of

[053-058]the King's visit--indeed higher. So much for Lord Clanricarde's speech.

[053-059]Cabinet dinner at the Duke's. The King is anxious about the duration of his

[053-060]Government. He would concede on the subject of Reform, although he is

[053-061]against it. Peel told him he thought that by opposing all Reform in the

[053-062]first instance the Government would be able to make better terms

[053-063]afterwards. The King said either course had its conveniences and

[053-064]inconveniences. He did not decide between them; but he evidently inclines

[053-065]to concession.

[053-066]It seems the Queen _now_ declares herself much disappointed at not going to

[053-067]Guildhall, and the Fitzclarence family are turning against the Government,

[053-068]wishing, as the Duke says, to be Dukes and Duchesses, which is impossible.

[053-069]On Tuesday night 4,000 troops could have been collected in St. James's Park

[053-070]in ten minutes. There were 2,000 police near Whitehall as a grand reserve.

[053-071]The Lord Mayor wrote to Peel acknowledging the total inefficiency of the

[053-072]City Police. The contrast between the City and Westminster was most

[053-073]striking.

[053-074]The Press is turning against us. Like cats, they are leaving the falling

[053-075]house.

[053-076]In the House of Commons this evening there was an almost unanimous shout

[053-077]when Peel admitted that the new Bishop of Exeter was to hold the living of

[053-078]Stanhope _in commendam_. It seems all unite upon that question, which is an

[053-079]unlucky one, although the interference of Parliament is quite irregular.

[053-080]There was much talk about the Regency question after dinner, and I left

[053-081]them talking still at half-past eleven.

[053-082]On Friday the Chancellor should open the question to the House, and we are

[053-083]not prepared, having called Parliament together for this specific purpose!

[053-084]We have neglected the Press too much. The Duke relies upon the support of

[053-085]'respectable people,' and despises the rabble; but the rabble read

[053-086]newspapers, and gradually carry along with them the 'respectable people'

[053-087]they outnumber.

[053-088]I do not think the being out of office for a Session would be of any

[053-089]ultimate disadvantage to me. I am sure I should enjoy better health, and I

[053-090]should have much more to do in the House. I should be enabled to regain my

[053-091]proper place.

[053-092]_November 11._

[053-093]Office. Saw Wortley. He says the spirits of our friends are improved, and

[053-094]those of our foes lowered, the few last days as to Reform. Cabinet at two.

[053-095]A fire at Melton-Constable. The country round Battle and Hawkhurst almost

[053-096]in insurrection. Troops sent there The accounts from France good. The

[053-097]French Government acknowledges the right of the Diet to drive the Belgians

[053-098]out of the Duchy of Luxembourg, which is a part of the German Empire. They

[053-099]have instructed Talleyrand to promote the interests of the Prince of

[053-100]Orange.

[053-101]Regency Bill. Decided that the Princess Victoria shall be considered Queen,

[053-102]and the oath of allegiance taken to her with the reservation of the rights

[053-103]of any child that might be born. If the child should be born, the Queen

[053-104]Dowager to be Regent. During the Princess's minority the Duchess of Kent.

[053-105]The Duke saw the King to-day, and found him very well satisfied with the

[053-106]postponement of the dinner, and tranquil.

[053-107]House. The Duke of Buckingham told me they had formed their Government, and

[053-108]expected to be in in a week. They think the Duke will resign after Tuesday.

[053-109]Lord Grey to be Foreign Secretary. The Duke of Richmond to be First Lord of

[053-110]the Treasury. Palmerston and Grant Secretaries of State. Lansdowne

[053-111]President. The Government to be as Tory as possible. The Chancellor to

[053-112]remain.

[053-113]Lothian told me all the best old friends of the Government were against

[053-114]Philpotts. I told him the reasons why Parliament should not interfere; with

[053-115]which he was satisfied, and was sorry he had not heard them before.

[053-116]Lord Camden spoke to me on the same subject. I wish we could get rid of

[053-117]Philpotts. He will damage us more than Reform.

[053-118]The Funds have risen to 84 3/4; that is, 7 1/2 per cent, in three days. I

[053-119]believe this is the consequence, not only of the broken heads, but of the

[053-120]idea that the Duke will be firm and not run away.

[053-121]We had a two hours' talk about agriculture; the Duke acquiescing in a

[053-122]motion of Salisbury's for a Committee on the Poor Laws.

[053-123]_November 12._

[053-124]Wrote a note to Hardinge, suggesting to him the expediency of calling upon

[053-125]Dr. Philpotts and placing before him the hopelessness of his keeping

[053-126]Stanhope, the damage to himself of a vote of Parliament, and to the Church

[053-127]from the example of Parliamentary interference, leading him to propose the

[053-128]exchange of Stanhope for a living near Exeter, and I mentioned Dr. Barnes.

[053-129]If this could be managed we should turn evil into good, and avoid the

[053-130]division we must lose. The Funds rose to 853/4, and then fell to 84 3/4,

[053-131]being still a rise. In the City they think the Government will stand.

[053-132]There have been threatening notices as near as Colnbrook. In Sussex and

[053-133]Kent things are very bad. I did not, however, see Peel to-day. There was

[053-134]nothing in the House.

[053-135]_November 13._

[053-136]It seems Peel and Scarlet licked Brougham well yesterday. The temper of the

[053-137]House is said to have been rather good. Hardinge told me Goulburn made an

[053-138]indifferent speech. Philpotts has so good a case that he looks confidently

[053-139]to the result of the debate. We agreed that there was no reason-why the

[053-140]_congé d'élire_ should not issue. Philpotts himself decides that it should,

[053-141]happen what may as to Stanhope.

[053-142]We had some talk as to the division on the Civil List. Peel is for refusing

[053-143]a Committee, and the separation of the diplomatic expenditure, and will not

[053-144]yield because he is weak. I think he is right. The better face we put upon

[053-145]it, the more votes we shall have.

[053-146]Hardinge suggested the placing of Doherty in Arbuthnot's office. Nothing

[053-147]could be better than that arrangement; but he thought, and I think, the

[053-148]Duke would not displace Arbuthnot. Arbuthnot knows more about my office

[053-149]than any one else. Where would they put me?

[053-150]We had some conversation respecting the Regency. It was determined to

[053-151]legislate as _little_ as we could.

[053-152]_November 14._

[053-153]Cabinet at four. Peel is of opinion that the fires are in many cases

[053-154]perpetrated for stock-jobbing purposes. They are certainly done by persons

[053-155]from London.

[053-156]He said he was satisfied that, whatever might be the division on Reform,

[053-157]the question was carried. Admiral Sotheron, Lindsay, he thought [blank],

[053-158]and I think he mentioned another, voted for it. If the county members did,

[053-159]and it was thrown out by the representatives of Scotch and English

[053-160]boroughs, it was impossible to stand much longer. He read a paper,

[053-161]circulated for signatures in the parish of St. Ann, in which the

[053-162]subscribers declare their readiness to be sworn in as special constables,

[053-163]and their determination to protect property. At the same time they declare

[053-164]their opinion that there ought to be a Reform, first in the House of

[053-165]Commons; but of Church and State. This he considers the commencement of a

[053-166]Burgher Guard. I cannot understand his reasoning; if he thinks Reform must

[053-167]be carried, surely it is better to vote a general resolution, and to fight

[053-168]the details. By objecting to the general resolution we shall probably be

[053-169]turned out, and have much less power to do good out of office than if we

[053-170]were in.

[053-171]It seems to me that obstinacy, and the fear of being again accused of

[053-172]ratting, lead to this determination to resist when resistance is, in his

[053-173]own opinion, fruitless.

[053-174]Clive, whom I saw to-day, is for a modified Reform; but he will vote for us

[053-175]in order to keep the Duke in.

[053-176]We had a long conversation about the Regency, and agreed upon the substance

[053-177]and form of the Bill. Aberdeen wanted again to open the whole question, on

[053-178]which he has no fixed opinion. He has come round entirely. First he thought

[053-179]the right was in the presumptive heir; now he thinks it must be in the

[053-180]child _in utero_.
