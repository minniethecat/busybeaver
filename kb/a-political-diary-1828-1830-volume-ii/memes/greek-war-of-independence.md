The [[Greek War of Independence]] was the most persistent diplomatic challenge facing the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government. The Treaty of London (1827), signed by Britain, France, and Russia, had committed the powers to mediate between the Ottoman Empire and the Greek insurgents. The accidental destruction of the Ottoman fleet at Navarino (October 1827) — an outcome no signatory had intended — transformed mediation into a de facto intervention. Ellenborough's diary from 1828 to 1830 records, in remarkable granular detail, the Cabinet's struggle to contain the consequences: Russian expansionism, French opportunism, and the challenge of constructing a viable Greek state.

## Background

The Greek insurrection against Ottoman rule began in 1821, running parallel to the [[Portuguese Civil War]] as the two great liberal-constitutional crises of the late 1820s. and rapidly attracted European philhellenic sentiment. By 1827, after years of brutal warfare and atrocities on both sides, the Great Powers intervened through the Treaty of London, demanding an armistice. The resulting naval demonstration at Navarino Bay produced an unintended battle that destroyed the Ottoman-Egyptian fleet, removing the Sultan's capacity to suppress the rebellion by force. Russia promptly declared war on the Ottoman Empire (1828), leaving Britain to manage a crisis that threatened the entire Eastern Mediterranean balance.

## Key Developments (1828–1830)

### The Protocols and the Morea

The diary records the Cabinet's prolonged negotiations over Greek boundaries. The initial proposal — an independent Greece limited to the Morea (Peloponnese) and the Cyclades — gave way to more expansive plans:

> [011-002] independence of Greece within the Morea (with Attica…

Aberdeen, as Foreign Secretary, was at the center of these discussions, with the diarist himself deeply involved:

### The Search for a Sovereign

Finding a ruler for the new Greek state proved contentious. Candidates including Prince Leopold of Saxe-Coburg (the future King of the Belgians) were considered:

> [019-128] …eed to make Leopold Prince of Greece, but the King
>
> [020-031] Sovereign of Greece. The French have now proposed…

Leopold initially accepted but later declined, partly due to the instability of the settlement (030-061). The diary captures the frustrations of the process.

### The Independence Protocol

In February 1830, the London Protocol finally declared Greece an independent sovereign state. The diarist noted the moment with characteristic skepticism:

> [031-086] Then Aberdeen presented the Greek papers, and, having explained…

The settlement was fragile. Russia continued to press its advantage; the Ottoman Empire had yet to fully accept the new reality; and the Greeks themselves were divided.

## Consequences

The Greek settlement had far-reaching consequences:

1. **Precedent for intervention**: The Treaty of London established that the Great Powers could collectively impose settlements on the Ottoman Empire — a precedent that would shape Eastern Question diplomacy for a century.
2. **Russian gains**: The Russo-Turkish War (1828–1829) ended with the Treaty of Adrianople, expanding Russian influence in the Balkans.
3. **Strain on the Tory Cabinet**: The prolonged Greek negotiations exposed divisions within Wellington's government, foreshadowing the ruptures of 1830.
4. **Independent Greece**: The Greek state established in 1830, though territorially limited, proved permanent — a rare successful national-liberation movement in the Metternich era.

## Diary Assessment

Ellenborough's diary reveals a government that never fully controlled the process it had set in motion. The diarist himself was skeptical of expansive Greek boundaries and worried about Russian manipulation:

> [009-157] English alliance, not Greek, and certainly not Russian. I…

Yet the outcome — an independent Greece aligned with Britain rather than Russia — was, by the standards of the time, a qualified diplomatic success.
