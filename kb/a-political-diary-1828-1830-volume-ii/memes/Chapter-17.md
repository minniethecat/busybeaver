[017-001]_Sunday, November 22._

[017-002]Told Bankes what the Duke wished respecting the Charter; but I likewise

[017-003]told him it had not yet been so determined in Cabinet, and that there was

[017-004]no objection to our making the Government more rapid and vigorous, and less

[017-005]like the Tullietudlem coach. I desired him to consider this _confidential_

[017-006]to himself and the Commissioners.

[017-007]_November 25._

[017-008]Received a note from Bankes announcing that the Duke had accepted his

[017-009]retirement from the office of secretary, and had consented to make him an

[017-010]extra commissioner.

[017-011]This has long been an idea of Bankes's, of which I never could see rational

[017-012]ground. Indeed, he seems to acknowledge it is not his own idea, but that of

[017-013]others, that on his return to the Government he should not have returned to

[017-014]the same office. In fact it is the influence of the Duke of Cumberland, and

[017-015]it is evident from the endeavour to detach Bankes from the Government now

[017-016]that the Brunswickers still have hopes. It is like giving notice to Lot and

[017-017]his family before the fall of fire and brimstone.

[017-018]Bankes's letter is full of kind and grateful expressions towards me.

[017-019]Indeed, we have always been on very friendly and confidential terms. I have

[017-020]expressed my regret at his resolution. I told him I think he acts upon

[017-021]mistaken views, and I assure him that in whatever position he may stand

[017-022]towards the Board, it will afford me much pleasure and advantage to remain

[017-023]on the same terms with him.

[017-024]The Duke will be angry, and I do not think Bankes will soon get an office

[017-025]again.

[017-026]_December 2._

[017-027]Read for an hour at the Cabinet room. There is a curious account of a

[017-028]conversation between De Rigny and an Austrian friend at Smyrna. De Rigny

[017-029]thinks very ill of the Government, and of the state of France. He too wants

[017-030]the Rhine! He judges truly enough of the results of the treaty. 'England,

[017-031]Austria, and France will talk, but nothing will be done.' He says Russia

[017-032]was very foolish not to go on. She might have dared anything. However, the

[017-033]army seems to have suffered severely. They acknowledge the loss of 130,000

[017-034]men in the two campaigns.

[017-035]Diebitch has partly evacuated Adrianople, leaving there, however, 6,000

[017-036]sick and a battalion. The plague spreads in the Principalities, and they do

[017-037]not know how to get the troops out of Turkey.

[017-038]Zuylen de Neyvelt and others give a very bad account of the state of

[017-039]Constantinople. They say the Turkish Empire _cannot_ hold together.

[017-040]I do not like Lord Stuart's account of the state of the French Ministry.

[017-041]They will bring in Villele, who is an able man, and he may save them; but

[017-042]theirs is a desperate game.

[017-043]The French seem to be disposed to go along with us in negotiating with the

[017-044]Emperor of Brazil [Footnote: _i.e._ with the Emperor Don Pedro, father of

[017-045]the ultimately successful candidate for the Portuguese throne, Donna Maria

[017-046]de Gloria.] for the recognition of Miguel. There would be a stipulation for

[017-047]amnesty, &c.

[017-048]_December 3._

[017-049]The Chairs talked of Lord William Bentinck. They are very much out of

[017-050]humour with him and heartily wish he was at home. He has neither written

[017-051]privately nor publicly, except upon trifling matters, for five months. He

[017-052]has declared his opinion in favour of colonisation. He is very unpopular.

[017-053]On the subject of Sir W. Rumbold he and Sir Ch. Metcalfe are very hostile,

[017-054]taking extreme views on the different sides. This hostility upon one

[017-055]subject will lead to difference upon others. The Government is not

[017-056]respected--and certainly there has been no moment when it was of more

[017-057]importance that the head of the Government should be respected than when it

[017-058]is necessary to effect a great economical reform. They describe the feeling

[017-059]at Madras as being still worse. There they did not think the governor an

[017-060]_honest man_.

[017-061]The Chairs expect a letter from Macdonald to the Secret Committee with

[017-062]copies of his last despatches which I have already received through

[017-063]Petersburg, so they are unwilling to accept a communication of them from

[017-064]me. The letter, permitting Abbas Murza to purchase 12,000 stand of arms and

[017-065]to pay for them by instalments, will therefore go without any reference to

[017-066]the last despatches received.

[017-067]Saw Aberdeen. He agrees with me in feeling much apprehension on the state

[017-068]of France as well as of Turkey. He seems, however, to think more of the

[017-069]state of parties here, and does not like the looks of the Duke of

[017-070]Cumberland (who was nearly dying last week) and of the King. It seems the

[017-071]King, although very well satisfied with measures of a public nature, is

[017-072]annoyed at not carrying some small jobs.

[017-073]There was a great party at Woburn lately, and the world of course say there

[017-074]is an approximation to the Grey party. Aberdeen thinks the Woburn party

[017-075]showed good wishes, and Lord Grey, it is said, does not mean to come up to

[017-076]town. However, he is said to think he has been slighted, whereas the Duke

[017-077]of Wellington _cannot_ do anything for him in the hostile state of the

[017-078]King's mind.

[017-079]I told Aberdeen confidentially of Bankes's going out, which is an

[017-080]indication, no doubt, of continued hostility on the part of the Duke of

[017-081]Cumberland.

[017-082]Saw Hardinge. Talked on various public subjects, and then told him of the

[017-083]probability that in three months Lord W. Bentinck would be recalled. I

[017-084]asked him whether he could be induced to go as Governor-General. He

[017-085]rejected the idea at first as unsuited to his rank in the army. I said we

[017-086]could make him Captain-General. He seemed to think it was a great field for

[017-087]a man who wished to obtain great fame, and if he was unmarried he would not

[017-088]be disinclined to go, but I should think domestic considerations would

[017-089]prevent him. I wish we had him as secretary in Ireland, but he is wanted

[017-090]_everywhere_. He is so useful. He would be _most useful_ in Ireland.

[017-091]Saw the Duke. I told him what the Chairs had said. He said he always

[017-092]thought Lord William would not succeed. Who could we get to replace him? He

[017-093]had always thought it did not signify as long as we had _one_ man in India;

[017-094]but we must have _one_. I told him that, seeing the difficulty of

[017-095]selection, I had thought it right to tell him what was likely to happen. I

[017-096]should not be much surprised if he thought of Lord Tweddale, whom he

[017-097]thought of for Ireland. I do not know him at all.

[017-098]_December 6._

[017-099]Read Sir W. Rumbold's letters, and the minutes in Council on the Hyderabad

[017-100]case. Sir W. is a cunning, clever man. Sir Ch. Metcalfe shows too much

[017-101]prejudice against Sir W. Rumbold; but he was at Hyderabad at the time, and

[017-102]he may be right. I suspect it was a disgraceful business.

[017-103]_December 9._

[017-104]Loch has got a cadetship for me. Colonel Baillie lends it. He postpones a

[017-105]nomination till next year in order to oblige me. I have thanked Loch, and

[017-106]begged him to thank Colonel Baillie.

[017-107]Wrote to Lady Belfast to tell her Mr. Verner had his cadetship. Begged her

[017-108]to make his family and friends understand thoroughly that this was a

[017-109]private favour I had led her to expect long before the discussion of the

[017-110]Catholic question.

[017-111]Wrote to Lord Hertford and enclosed an extract from my letter to Lady

[017-112]Belfast.

[017-113]Read a letter from Sir J. Malcolm, who is again troubled by Sir J. P.

[017-114]Grant. He enclosed a letter of his upon the subject to Lord W. Bentinck.

[017-115]The concluding paragraph of this letter refers to a letter from Lord

[017-116]William of June 18, at which time the spirit of the Bengal army continued

[017-117]bad.

[017-118]Read a letter from Jones, who will set himself to work about the navigation

[017-119]of the Indus. He says a Mr. Walter Hamilton speaks of the river being

[017-120]navigable for vessels of 200 tons to Lahore, and that from Lahore to the

[017-121]mouth of the river, 700 miles, is only a voyage of twelve days. And no

[017-122]British flag has ever floated upon the waters of this river! Please God it

[017-123]shall, and in triumph, to the source of all its tributary streams.

[017-124]_December 11._

[017-125]Read a letter from Lord Bathurst respecting the recall of Sir J. P. Grant.

[017-126]He had imagined I had said he had resigned. He seems surprised I should

[017-127]have supposed it possible a judge should be recalled without a formal

[017-128]meeting of the Privy Council. I reminded him of Sir T. Claridge's case, not

[017-129]half so strong as that of Sir J. P. Grant.

[017-130]_December 12._

[017-131]Read Fraser's travels.

[017-132]_December 13._

[017-133]A letter from Sir J. Malcolm, by which it seems that my letter to him of

[017-134]February 21 has been copied and become public: much to his annoyance.

[017-135][Footnote: This was the letter with the expression about a wild elephant

[017-136]between two tame ones which afterwards attracted so much criticism. It was

[017-137]intended as a private letter to Sir J. Malcolm, but by a mistake of one of

[017-138]his secretaries was copied as an official communication.]

[017-139]He sends me his letter to Lord W. Bentinck upon the subject. It seems by

[017-140]this letter, which adverts to other topics, that the spirit in Bengal is

[017-141]very bad--that Lord W. has hitherto done nothing to check it, and that with

[017-142]the press in his power he has allowed it to be more licentious than it ever

[017-143]was before.

[017-144]_December 14._

[017-145]Found at Roehampton a letter from the Duke enclosing one addressed by Mrs.

[017-146]Hastings to the King, applying for a pension. The King recommends it to the

[017-147]consideration of the Court of Directors. I doubt the Court venturing to

[017-148]propose any pension to the Court of Proprietors.

[017-149]I had another letter from the Duke enclosing a letter to him from Sir J.

[017-150]Malcolm and a copy of Sir J. Malcolm's letter to Lord W. Bentinck,

[017-151]respecting the unauthorised publication of my private letter--the same I

[017-152]received yesterday. Sir J. Malcolm speaks of an intended deputation from

[017-153]the Bengal army to England, which Lord William was determined not to allow;

[017-154]but Sir J. Malcolm seems to think that Lord William by his conduct at first

[017-155]brought on much of what has taken place. He has relaxed the reins of

[017-156]Government too much. I am satisfied that, without a change of form and

[017-157]name, it will be very difficult to regain the strength the Government has

[017-158]lost in India.

[017-159]I shall see the Duke if I can to-morrow and suggest the appointment of Sir

[017-160]J. Malcolm as provisional successor to Lord William. Sir J. Malcolm's

[017-161]sentiments are known, and his nomination would show the feeling of the

[017-162]Government here. It would be a hint to Lord William that we could replace

[017-163]him at once and make him do his duty. It would, in the event of anything

[017-164]happening to Lord William, guard against the mischiefs of an interregnum,

[017-165]which is always a time of weakness and of job.

[017-166]_December 15._

[017-167]The Duke gone to the Deepdene. Wrote to him to say I would not fail to

[017-168]bring the question of Mrs. Hastings's pension before the Chairs; but I

[017-169]enclosed a memorandum showing all that had been done for old Hastings, and

[017-170]reminded the Duke that the Court could not grant above 200£ a year without

[017-171]the sanction of two Courts of Proprietors.

[017-172]Cabinet room. Lord Heytesbury seems to have shown Nesselrode the protocol

[017-173]about November 25. The Count was greatly agitated, and put himself into a

[017-174]furious passion. Asked the use of it? Perhaps it would be difficult to say.

[017-175]Supposed it was intended for Parliament--which is very true. Said it would

[017-176]lead to a reply we should not like--create a paper war, prevent the two

[017-177]Courts from remaining upon the friendly terms he had hoped were

[017-178]re-established. The more angry he is, the more right I think we must

[017-179]feel we were to send it.
