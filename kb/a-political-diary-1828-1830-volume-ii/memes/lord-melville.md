# Lord Melville

## Overview

Lord Melville (Robert Saunders Dundas, 2nd Viscount Melville) served as First Lord of the Admiralty from 1812 to 1827 and again under the [[Duke of Wellington]] from 1828 to 1830. A steady [[tory-party|Tory]] administrator, he was deeply involved in naval and colonial affairs during the period covered by the diary.

## Role in the Diary

The diary portrays Melville as a central figure in the Wellington government's day-to-day operations. He appears regularly at Cabinet dinners — Ellenborough records a "Cabinet dinner at Lord Melville's" (006-032) and references to discussions "at the Cabinet dinner" at Melville's residence (024-112), indicating his role as a host and convener of government business.

Melville's Admiralty responsibilities feature prominently. He presented the petition of naval interests in the House (005-007), and Ellenborough reflects that "our management under Lord Melville as Admiral" had been effective (005-013). Melville is candid about institutional challenges, admitting "the cumbrousness" of certain administrative arrangements (016-175).

On foreign affairs, the diary shows Melville receiving intelligence: he "had heard that Leopold had communicated" sensitive information (031-061). Ellenborough records speaking to "Melville and Goulburn about" policy matters at Cabinet dinners (033-041), reflecting Melville's integration into the inner circle of the Wellington cabinet.

## Key Appearances

- **Cabinet gatherings**: Regular presence at Cabinet meetings and dinners, frequently hosting them at his own residence (006-032, 024-112).
- **Admiralty business**: Presented petitions (005-007), discussed naval ordnance with Ellenborough, and addressed administrative reform (005-013, 016-175).
- **House of Lords duties**: Gave parliamentary notices on behalf of the government (023-033).
- **Political counsel**: Part of the inner group alongside Peel and the Chancellor who supported key government positions (026-037).
