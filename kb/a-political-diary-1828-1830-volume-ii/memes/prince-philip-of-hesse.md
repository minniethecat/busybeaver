**Prince Philip of Hesse-Homburg** (1779–1846) was a German prince and Austrian general who emerged as a diplomatic candidate for a sovereign throne during the late 1820s. His name appears repeatedly in [[Edward Law, 1st Earl of Ellenborough|Lord Ellenborough]]'s diary as the British government considered suitable candidates for a European crown.

== A Candidate for Sovereignty ==

In early 1828, the creation of a new sovereign state prompted a search for an acceptable ruler. The diary records that the preference was "to have a sovereign, Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg would be…" (013-007) The diarist noted the importance of "having Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg" as the chosen candidate. (013-013) The British government and its allies settled on Philip as their man: they were "altogether to our man, Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg." (015-146) This language — "our man" — indicates that Philip was seen as reliably aligned with British interests.

== Diplomatic Negotiations ==

Philip's candidacy was not unopposed. The diary records that other powers "equally object…" (015-146) to the British-backed candidate. At a key meeting, the diarist recorded: "We first named Prince Philip of Hesse Homburg, whom the French woul…" (016-049) This suggests French resistance to Philip's candidacy, reflecting the broader Anglo-French rivalry in determining the political settlement of Europe after the Napoleonic Wars. Alternative candidates were considered alongside him, including "Prince John of Saxo…" (021-022)

== Significance ==

Prince Philip's candidacy illustrates the intricate dynastic diplomacy of the post-Napoleonic era, when the great powers negotiated over the occupancy of newly created or vacant European thrones. His repeated appearance in the diary across multiple entries in 1828 underscores the importance the British government attached to securing a friendly sovereign for strategic purposes.
