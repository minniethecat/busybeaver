[054-001]It appears certain that at Carlisle the 9th was looked to as the day of

[054-002]signal to them and to all England. It seems the plan was to attack the

[054-003]Guildhall and massacre all in it. There would have been a smash, but a most

[054-004]signal defeat, for there would have been 250 cavalry, and from 700 to 800

[054-005]Volunteers there (the East India Volunteers and the Artillery Company),

[054-006]besides a battalion within reach.

[054-007]Sir Claudius Hunter has published in the _Sunday Times_ a denial of the

[054-008]speeches attributed to him, and a statement of the City force. Their

[054-009]ordinary force is fifty-four men! With Volunteers, Artillery Company,

[054-010]Picket men, Firemen, Lumber Troop, &c., they would have had about 2,250.

[054-011]_November 15._

[054-012]House. A very temperate speech of Lord Durham, and a very good one of Lord

[054-013]Suffield, respecting the new police. Lord Bathurst observed to me they

[054-014]spoke as if they expected to come in. I mentioned Salisbury's motion for a

[054-015]Committee which is to be made on Monday next, and Lord Bathurst said 'Shall

[054-016]we be alive then?' He has a serious apprehension of being out.

[054-017]The Chancellor made a most excellent speech in moving the first reading of

[054-018]the Regency Bill, and was cheered on both sides of the House. It seems as

[054-019]if the measure would be unanimously approved. Lord Eldon seemed to say he

[054-020]should advise the Duke of Cumberland to acquiesce in it.

[054-021]The ultra Tories were to have a meeting to-day--thirty-eight of them--to

[054-022]decide what they should do about Reform. Yesterday the report was they

[054-023]joined us; but the Duke of Richmond will do all he can to make them go

[054-024]against us, and, if they do, I suppose we shall be obliged to make our

[054-025]bows.

[054-026]_November 16._

[054-027]Goulburn opposed the submitting the Civil List accounts to a Committee, and

[054-028]was defeated. We had 204 to 233. Majority against us, 29. Hobhouse asked

[054-029]Peel whether Ministers would resign, to which he got no answer. Brougham

[054-030]rose and said Ministers would have time for consideration.

[054-031]I suppose this division must be considered to be fatal to us. Henry is

[054-032]going off to take chambers. He means to apply himself to the Law. He is

[054-033]rather in a hurry. For my own part I am by no means sorry to be out of

[054-034]office. I think I shall be better able to regain my proper station in

[054-035]Opposition than I could have done in office, and the emoluments are of no

[054-036]value to me now.

[054-037]Office. Saw Wortley. He is glad that the division against us has been upon

[054-038]the Civil List, rather than upon Reform. He thinks we should resign to-day,

[054-039]and thus throw upon the Whigs the burden of bringing forward Reform as a

[054-040]Government measure. Probably Brougham would postpone his motion if we

[054-041]resigned.

[054-042]At about half-past three I received a note from Sir Robert Taylor desiring

[054-043]my immediate attendance at St. James's. I dressed and went, and in a few

[054-044]moments was admitted to the King. I met Lord Melville coming away. The King

[054-045]desired me to sit down, and asked me whether I had any expectation of the

[054-046]division of last night? I said no--I thought that upon any question

[054-047]connected with the Civil List we should have had a majority; that the

[054-048]question itself was one of little importance; but, as the Committee had not

[054-049]been granted before, Sir R. Peel thought it would be a confession of

[054-050]weakness not to oppose it now, and I thought he was right. The King said it

[054-051]was probably chosen as a question merely to try strength.

[054-052]The King asked me what had taken place between the Government and the

[054-053]Company. I told his Majesty, and added an outline of the plan I had for the

[054-054]new military arrangements, of which he seemed highly to approve. I then

[054-055]said I supposed I must take leave of his Majesty. He said in one sense his

[054-056]Ministers seemed to think they could not go on.

[054-057]I said I could not but express my sentiments, which were I was sure those

[054-058]of all my colleagues--the sentiments of deep gratitude to his Majesty for

[054-059]the constant kind and honourable confidence he had placed in us.

[054-060]His Majesty said he thought it his duty to give the full support of the

[054-061]Crown to his Ministers. He had confidence in those he found at his

[054-062]brother's demise; and since July 26, which was the commencement of our

[054-063]troubles, he had regarded with admiration that which was most important in

[054-064]their conduct, their Foreign Policy. He had a feeling of entire

[054-065]satisfaction with them.

[054-066]I said it must likewise be satisfactory to his Majesty to feel that his

[054-067]late Ministers, fully aware of the real difficulties of the country, would

[054-068]never be led by any personal or party feelings to do anything which could

[054-069]be _prejudicial_ to the country, and that whatever might be their

[054-070]differences in principle from his new Ministers they would ever support his

[054-071]Majesty's interests.

[054-072]The King was much affected, and had the tears in his eyes all the time I

[054-073]was speaking to him. I then rose and kissed his hand, and he shook hands

[054-074]with me, and wished me good-bye for the present. I asked for the _entrée_,

[054-075]which he gave me very good-naturedly. As I came away I met Rosslyn going

[054-076]in. The three Fitzclarences were in the lower room, seemingly enjoying our

[054-077]discomfiture.

[054-078]House at five. The Duke had already declared that the occurrence which had

[054-079]taken place elsewhere had induced him to think it his duty to tender his

[054-080]resignation to the King, and his Majesty had been graciously pleased to

[054-081]accept it.

[054-082]Lord Grosvenor asked a question as to the appointment of a successor to Mr.

[054-083]Buller, and Lord Bathurst said none had been made.

[054-084]It is a sad loss to Wm. Bathurst, who would have been Clerk of the Council

[054-085]if the Government had lasted three days longer.

[054-086]Nothing was said. Lord Grey has been sent for by the King.

[054-087]I went through all the protocols on the table, and have left hardly

[054-088]anything but two unanswered letters to my successor--one respecting the

[054-089]rate of Exchange between territory and commerce; the other respecting

[054-090]Hyderabad affairs.

[054-091]_November 19._

[054-092]Office. Saw Cabell, Jones, and Leach. They had all the tears in their eyes.

[054-093]Old Jones could hardly help bursting altogether into tears. Left directions

[054-094]with Leach for placing certain papers before my successor, showing the

[054-095]state of the finances and expenditure prospectively, and the position in

[054-096]which we were as to the renewal of the Charter.

[054-097]Cabell will place the Hyderabad papers before my successor, with my letter

[054-098]to Astell, and his reply.

[054-099]Called on Hardinge, who was not at home.

[054-100]I can only leave a memorandum in the office showing the nature and extent

[054-101]of the military alterations I projected.

[054-102]Called on the Duke. He told me Peel came to him in a very nervous state on

[054-103]Monday night. Arbuthnot and Goulburn were with him. It was clear that the

[054-104]majority would have been against us if there had been a House of 500. The

[054-105]Duke sent for the Chancellor, who said as soon as he heard of the division

[054-106]he thought the game was up--that we could not go on. The Duke went to the

[054-107]King in the morning, and told him it was better he should resign

[054-108]immediately, and so force the new Government to bring forward their measure

[054-109]of Reform. It was better for the country. The King asked the Duke's opinion

[054-110]of Lord Grey, and whether he had ever had any communication with him. The

[054-111]Duke said No. The King knew the personal objections the late King had to

[054-112]Lord Grey, and he could not, although often pressed by Lord Grey's friends,

[054-113]have any communication with him without either deceiving _him_ or deceiving

[054-114]the King; and he would not do either. The King asked what sort of a man

[054-115]Lord Grey was? The Duke said he really did not know. He had the reputation

[054-116]of being an ill-tempered, violent man; but he knew very little of him. He

[054-117]had never had any political conversation with him. The King was much

[054-118]agitated and distressed.

[054-119]I told the Duke what passed at my interview with his Majesty yesterday.

[054-120]Drummond, Greville, and Sir J. Shelley, whom I saw in the ante-room,

[054-121]congratulated me on being out, but condoled on Lord Durham's being removed

[054-122]out of my way. He goes Minister to Naples _vice_ Lord Burghersh,

[054-123]_dismissed_. It is understood Brougham will not _positively_ take my

[054-124]office.

[054-125]Levée. The Duke of Buckingham told me the King was much out of spirits. He

[054-126]expressed himself much pleased with his Ministers.

[054-127]The King desired Lord Camden to come and see him frequently--every three or

[054-128]four days.

[054-129]The Duke of Newcastle, Lord Falmouth, Sir E. Knatchbull, Sir R. Vyvyan,

[054-130]will not support the new Government. Having had their revenge they mean to

[054-131]put their knees in our backs and do all they can to get out the others.

[054-132]They are sorry for the work they have performed, and regret their vote.

[054-133]They had intended to stay away on the question of Reform--now they mean to

[054-134]vote against it.

[054-135]Lord Anglesey goes to Ireland; a very bad appointment. The Duke of

[054-136]Devonshire would have been a very unexceptionable one.

[054-137]None of the Whigs or Whig Radicals were at the levée, but a good many

[054-138]Tories. We were there as usual as Ministers, and those who had business

[054-139]with the King went in to him as usual.

[054-140]I proposed to Herries, Goulburn, Arbuthnot, and others, that we should in

[054-141]each department prepare a statement of what has been done since the Duke

[054-142]came into office. This we shall do to-morrow.

[054-143]I likewise proposed we should have a large sheet of paper with columns for

[054-144]the new Ministers, and in each column their pledges with the dates.

[054-145]Croker has promised to undertake a newspaper, probably the 'Star.'

[054-146]Arbuthnot told us before dinner that as yet no progress had been made by

[054-147]Lord Grey, except in getting Lord Althorp after much solicitation. Brougham

[054-148]has again in the House of Commons to-night declared he has nothing to do

[054-149]with the new Government, and will positively bring on his motion on the

[054-150]25th. The new Government wish to postpone the question till March, when

[054-151]they promise to bring in a Bill.

[054-152]Lord Lansdowne is said to be much dissatisfied, and the Palmerston party

[054-153]think they have not enough offered to them. It is evident that Brougham

[054-154]prefers power to temporary emolument and distinction, and he will be very

[054-155]dangerous acting at the head of the Whig Radicals.

[054-156]The Duke said 300 people had called upon him to-day--amongst the rest Lord

[054-157]Cleveland, with whom Lord Grey was early this morning, and whom he in vain

[054-158]endeavoured to induce to go to Ireland.

[054-159]William Bankes, whose father did us most mischief on Monday, and who did

[054-160]not vote with us, came to ask the Chancellor for a living to-day!

[054-161]Lord Grey was much agitated when he was with the King, and has expressed

[054-162]himself as very much struck by the strong terms in which the King declared

[054-163]his approbation of his late Ministers.

[054-164]My fear is that the Whigs will not be able to form a Government. It is of

[054-165]much importance to the country that their incompetence should be exhibited,

[054-166]and the fallacy of the grounds upon which they have been attempting to

[054-167]obtain popular favour. We shall never be strong until it is proved they

[054-168]cannot form a Government. Again I say my fear is they will be unable to

[054-169]take the first step. It was considered that we ought to transact all the

[054-170]ordinary business of our several departments.

[054-171]_November 18._

[054-172]Called on Hardinge. He is out of spirits. Yesterday at the meeting of the

[054-173]_employés_ Lord G. Somerset asked Peel if he would lead them--to which Peel

[054-174]gave a damping answer. Hardinge feels that he is capable of business, that

[054-175]his circumstances require he should exert himself and be in office; and, as

[054-176]he would not take office without the Duke's acquiescence, he thinks it

[054-177]rather hard he should be deprived of a Parliamentary leader, and thus of

[054-178]the means of coming in.
