[023-001]In the meantime it appears that Mr. Robertson, who is at Mexico,

[023-002]remonstrated strongly with M. de Bocaregna, respecting the objects of the

[023-003]embassy to Hayti, and he was told by Aberdeen that he did quite right, and

[023-004]that not only ourselves but other states might view with disapprobation an

[023-005]attempt to excite a warfare of an uncivilised character in Cuba.

[023-006]The French have assembled 35,000 men to attack Algiers. They promise not to

[023-007]keep it. [Footnote: This promise was repudiated by the Government of July.]

[023-008]They intimate their intention of assisting Mehemet Ali with a fleet; but in

[023-009]the meantime they are satisfied at Constantinople that Mehemet Ali will not

[023-010]move.

[023-011]Aberdeen told Laval that we had informed the Pasha of Egypt that we should

[023-012]view with disapprobation his attack upon Tunis and Tripoli without the

[023-013]consent of the Sultan. Laval begged this might be repeated to him three

[023-014]times.

[023-015]Much conversation as to the state of the House of Commons. The Tories are

[023-016]most radical. Sir R. Vyvyan told Holmes or Planta his object was to reduce

[023-017]the Government majorities as much as possible, and to make the Government

[023-018]as contemptible as possible. Sir E. Knatchbull leads about twenty-three. I

[023-019]think the probability is that, unless we make some coalition with the

[023-020]Whigs, we shall go to the ground between the two parties, [Footnote: This

[023-021]eventually occurred on the Civil List question after the accession of

[023-022]William IV.] both uniting against us upon some point (upon my letter to Sir

[023-023]J. Malcolm as likely as any other).

[023-024]I took home Sir George Murray. He expressed his surprise the Duke should

[023-025]cling to the hope of reclaiming the ultra-Tories, whom he would not get,

[023-026]and who were not worth having.

[023-027]I confess I think he carries it on too long, although I am not surprised he

[023-028]should have wished it at first.

[023-029]Prince Leopold has given no reply to Aberdeen's letter, or to the offer of

[023-030]the ambassadors.

[023-031]Lord Holland gives notice to-morrow of a motion about Greece, and Lord

[023-032]Melbourne moves for some papers respecting Portugal.

[023-033]Lord Melville gives notice for me of the committee on East Indian Affairs,

[023-034]and I am not to go down till Tuesday, that we may have out the letter to

[023-035]Malcolm and other Indian matters all at once.

[023-036]_February 8._

[023-037]Wrote a memorandum for Peel and Bankes to this effect: 'That I had neither

[023-038]copy nor recollection of the letter; but that I had no doubt the letter

[023-039]published as mine was substantially correct. It was a confidential

[023-040]exposition of the motives which induced me to recommend two judges to the

[023-041]King. [Footnote: It was suggested that with these colleagues Sir J. Grant

[023-042]would be like a wild elephant between two tame ones. Alluding to the method

[023-043]of taming captured elephants in India.] It was never intended to be

[023-044]published, nor did I expect it would be. The expressions, therefore, were

[023-045]unadvised, but the sentiments were and are mine, deliberately formed upon

[023-046]full consideration of the official documents before me.

[023-047]Cabinet. It appears on looking into papers of 1825 and 1826 that so far

[023-048]from our having prohibited Mexico and Columbia from making any attack upon

[023-049]Cuba, we uniformly abstained from doing anything of the kind. The Americans

[023-050]declared they could not see with indifference any state other than Spain in

[023-051]possession of Cuba, and further their disposition to interpose their power

[023-052]should war be conducted in Cuba in a _devastating_ manner, and with a view

[023-053]to the excitement of a servile war.

[023-054]We offered to guarantee Cuba to Spain in 1823 if she would negotiate with

[023-055]the colonies with a view to their recognition.

[023-056]Subsequently we were willing to enter into a tripartite guarantee of Cuba

[023-057]to Spain with the United States and France.

[023-058]The United States seemed not unwilling, but France held back.

[023-059]Peel is to say there was no record of any prohibition, but that the United

[023-060]States declared so, and it was possible Mr. Canning may have intimated a

[023-061]similar disposition on our part. This is to keep open to us the faculty of

[023-062]interfering if we please.

[023-063]The Duke thinks my letter does not signify one pin. The simile of the

[023-064]elephants evidently means no more than that an indiscreet judge was placed

[023-065]between two discreet ones.

[023-066]The Duke told me he had offered a Lordship of the Treasury to Ashley, who

[023-067]had declined it. He then told him to make himself master of the Batta

[023-068]question. Ashley said he had not seen the papers. He said, let him see the

[023-069]papers. I told him I had sent them the moment I got them to him, and he had

[023-070]desired me to send them to the Cabinet room, which I did. When they were

[023-071]taken from the Cabinet room they went to the India Board, and Ashley might

[023-072]have seen them. I had never kept any papers from him. We then talked about

[023-073]the speech to be made in moving the committee. The Duke seems inclined to

[023-074]have little said. Peel seems disposed to say little; but he knows little. I

[023-075]think they are wrong. I am sure it is necessary to correct the erroneous

[023-076]notions which have been propagated with respect to the trade. They will

[023-077]otherwise acquire so great a head it will be impossible to beat them back.

[023-078]However, this we are to talk over with Peel tomorrow.

[023-079]General King, who voted against the address on Thursday, is turned out by

[023-080]the King himself; the Duke having only mentioned the fact. I dare say the

[023-081]King may be alarmed by the spirit shown by the House of Commons.

[023-082]The suicide of . . . . on account of his wife's seduction by the Duke of

[023-083]Cumberland, will drive the Duke of Cumberland out of the field.

[023-084]_February 9._

[023-085]Called on the Duke. He advised a very narrowed statement in moving for the

[023-086]committee. I rather doubt his judgment upon this point. I fear the opinion

[023-087]of the country will become settled, and that when the strength of our case

[023-088]is brought forward it will be found unequal to the driving back of the

[023-089]stream. However, I made a speech as he desired. Lord Lansdowne said a few

[023-090]words.

[023-091]Lord Durham then questioned me as to the authenticity of my letter to Sir

[023-092]J. Malcolm. I acknowledged it was substantially correct, and declared I

[023-093]could not have entertained any other sentiments without a dereliction of

[023-094]duty. He expressed disapprobation, considering the letter as evincing a

[023-095]determination to control the independence of judges. The Duke replied--then

[023-096]Lord Melville--then Lord Holland--I last. I declared that, as my father's

[023-097]son, I was the last man capable of harbouring a thought against the

[023-098]independence of judges; but I would resist their usurpation, more

[023-099]especially when they usurped powers withheld from them by Parliament as

[023-100]dangerous to the peace of India and to the stability of the British power.

[023-101]I said India could not bear the collision of the Supreme Court and the

[023-102]Local Government. If we did not support the Government we should lose

[023-103]India.

[023-104]I was determined to maintain the integrity, the dignity, the authority, and

[023-105]the unapproachable power of the Local Government, and especially to support

[023-106]a man who, at that distance from England, acting in the faithful discharge

[023-107]of his public duty, incurred the highest responsibility and the greatest

[023-108]personal risk in defence of what he considered essential to the stability

[023-109]of the British power in India. I believe I did well. They all told me I

[023-110]should hear no more of it.

[023-111]_February 10._

[023-112]Saw Bankes. He says the House of Commons is loose indeed; but he thinks

[023-113]Ministers will have a majority on the East Retford business. The worst of

[023-114]it is that those who ought to be the friends of Government will not stay

[023-115]out a debate. Last night Peel and Goulburn were left with a decided

[023-116]minority, but the House was counted out.

[023-117]Saw Hardinge. He seems to think there is no great danger, and he thinks the

[023-118]House is in so loose a state that the accession of an individual or two

[023-119]would not draw others; that Brougham may be quieted, and that the others do

[023-120]not much signify.

[023-121]In the meantime Abercromby has been made Chief Baron of Scotland. Another

[023-122]Whig gone. A very valuable intimation to those who remain.

[023-123]Lord Lansdowne brings in Zachary Macaulay, son of the old saint. [Footnote:

[023-124]The late Lord Macaulay. He is erroneously described by his father's

[023-125]Christian name.] They say a very clever man indeed, at least as a writer.

[023-126]Hardinge told me the Duke told Mrs. Arbuthnot I spoke very well last night.

[023-127]At dinner the Chancellor and Sir George Murray congratulated me on what had

[023-128]taken place.

[023-129]After the Cabinet dinner, much talk and nothing settled. The motion of Sir

[023-130]J. Graham will, I think, be amended--and easily. There is a disposition,

[023-131]very properly, not to give Portuguese papers. As to the Lord Holland's

[023-132]motion on Friday no decision is come to.

[023-133]Gave the Duke the petition of the Bengal half-castes.

[023-134]Mr. Jenkins, who was for many years resident at Nagpore, called upon me and

[023-135]offered himself as successor to Sir J. Malcolm. He said the Chairs were

[023-136]disposed to him, if the Government had no objection. I said I was aware of

[023-137]the services he had rendered, but that there were many distinguished

[023-138]servants of the Company, and likewise persons of ability who had not been

[023-139]in India, whose several qualifications must be considered. It was further a

[023-140]point upon which I must of course communicate with the Duke of Wellington.

[023-141]The man is a person of dry cold manner, not prepossessing.

[023-142]I am disposed to think Mr. Chaplin the best Indian for the situation.

[023-143]_February 11._

[023-144]I think Polignac's Ministry must fall, and really, as regards himself, I

[023-145]cannot feel regret, as he is the greatest liar that has exercised

[023-146]diplomatist functions for a long time. I had thought better of him. If

[023-147]their expedition ever sails for Algiers they will find what it costs to

[023-148]send an expedition over sea. I think, however, they will succeed, and, if

[023-149]they do, they will keep Algiers.

[023-150]Sir R. Gordon entertains a very different opinion from that expressed by

[023-151]Aberdeen as to the future fate of the Ottoman Empire. He thinks the events

[023-152]of the late war prove little, and that the Sultan has learnt a lesson which

[023-153]will induce him to treat his rayas better--that the war once over, all men

[023-154]will return to their duty. However, he gives no good reasons for his

[023-155]opinion. He states very fairly the difficulty of his own position. He says

[023-156]he has hitherto believed it was the intention of his Government to support

[023-157]Turkey. He has therefore had influence, because where he has advised

[023-158]concession the Turks have understood we meant it should not be hurtful to

[023-159]them--but now, how can he advise the Turks to yield to what is asked, when

[023-160]he knows the Government think that the more is taken from Turkey, the more

[023-161]is saved from Russia? Sir R. Gordon says his colleagues are by no means of

[023-162]opinion that the Ottoman Empire is falling, and that France allows their

[023-163]officers to go in numbers to serve with the Turkish troops.

[023-164]Received a letter from Sir J. Macdonald in which he tells me the Turkish

[023-165]Asiatic provinces are falling away from the Sultan.

[023-166]He encloses a letter from a Mr. Sterling, giving a very interesting account

[023-167]of his journey by Meshed and near Balkh to Cabul. He took a new road to the

[023-168]north of the Paropamisan ridge. In Cabul he experienced no difficulty.

[023-169]_February 12._

[023-170]House. Lord Holland's motion of a resolution that the House would not be

[023-171]satisfied with any plan for the pacification and settlement of Greece,

[023-172]which did not secure to that state the means of independence by sea and

[023-173]land, and leave the Greeks free to have their own Constitution. His

[023-174]information was most inaccurate. Yet on this he founded his distrust of the

[023-175]Government. Notwithstanding this distrust he was neither with them nor

[023-176]against them, nor did he wish to turn them out. He made an indifferent

[023-177]speech. Aberdeen a fair one ill delivered. The Duke spoke admirably. The

[023-178]brains were beaten out of the motion. No division. Goderich and Clanricarde

[023-179]and Melbourne spoke; Lord Melbourne poorly.
