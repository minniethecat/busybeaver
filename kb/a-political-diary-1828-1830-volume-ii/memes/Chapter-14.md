[014-001]That the general treaty contains no guarantee is evident from the specific

[014-002]guarantee of the cessions made by Saxony to Prussia, which would have been

[014-003]unnecessary if the spirit of the treaty had been that of existent

[014-004]guarantee.

[014-005]_October 13._

[014-006]Cabinet room. Found Lord Rosslyn there. Read the treaty.

[014-007]The King was very well yesterday. The Recorder's Report was so long that

[014-008]half was deferred.

[014-009]The last dispatches from Persia, which arrived on Friday, were opened at

[014-010]the Foreign Office, and read by everybody. Aberdeen sent them to the Duke,

[014-011]who has probably taken them to Walmer in his carriage. The Chairs sent for

[014-012]them, and could not get them. I must put a stop to this. I have written to

[014-013]Lord Heytesbury to beg he will in future forward letters to their address.

[014-014]Wrote a 'proposed draft' to Lord Heytesbury, directing him, if he should

[014-015]have reason to think the Russians intend to exact further concession from

[014-016]Persia, to intimate that such an attempt will be considered by his Majesty

[014-017]as unfriendly to himself as an Asiatic Power. I doubt my getting the Duke

[014-018]to agree to the sending of this despatch; but I shall try.

[014-019]_October 14._

[014-020]Carried my proposed letter to Lord Heytesbury to Aberdeen, who agrees to

[014-021]send it with a trifling alteration, at least one not very important. Read

[014-022]to him my proposed letter to Lord Heytesbury on the Peace of Adrianople. He

[014-023]seemed to approve of great part of it. He has done nothing at his yet, and

[014-024]seems to think there is no hurry!

[014-025]We shall stand very ill in Parliament if we have nothing to show. I think

[014-026]mine is a good _cadre_ of a letter, but that specific instructions should

[014-027]be given to Lord Heytesbury as to what he shall endeavour to obtain in a

[014-028]separate despatch.

[014-029]Read my drafts to Lord Rosslyn after dinner. He seemed to think the view I

[014-030]took was the right, and that much of what I had written was very good, but

[014-031]that it might be shortened. So I think.

[014-032]_October 15._

[014-033]Henry copied the draft to Lord Heytesbury, for the Duke, to whom I sent it

[014-034]with a letter.

[014-035]Showed the Chairs the draft to Lord Heytesbury on Persia. They were much

[014-036]pleased with it. So was old Jones. Sent it to the Duke. In little doubt his

[014-037]approving it.

[014-038]Received from the Duke the Persian despatches which I gave to the Chairs.

[014-039]The Duke had not read them.

[014-040]Received from him a letter on the subject of half-Batta. He says as an

[014-041]officer he should have thought there was a compromise in 1801. That it

[014-042]should be looked into as a question of economy. That above all things in

[014-043]dealing with an army you must _be just_.

[014-044]The Duke thinks the publication of the letter of Lord Combermere's

[014-045]secretary indiscreet and _wicked_, and is very angry with Lord Combermere.

[014-046]A letter will be written to the Government on the subject, directing

[014-047]enquiry.

[014-048]_October 19, Sunday._

[014-049]Read McDonald's despatches from Persia, and sent them to the Duke, with a

[014-050]letter suggesting the heads of a letter to the Envoy.

[014-051]The Russians have given up one of the two crores due, and allow five years

[014-052]for paying the other. They mean, therefore, to rule Persia _by influence_.

[014-053]However, there is a good Mahometan and Anti-Russian feeling beyond the

[014-054]Euphrates, and if mischief happens, it is our fault.

[014-055]Received a letter from Hardinge respecting half-Batta. He is for standing

[014-056]firm and giving some general boon, as an addition to marching money, to the

[014-057]whole army. That is my idea. I am sure it is the safest course.

[014-058]Wrote to Loch, suggesting it, and at the same time advised him to answer

[014-059]the paragraphs respecting half-Batta, and not give misrepresentations too

[014-060]much head.

[014-061]_October 20._

[014-062]Two letters from the Duke, written very hastily. It is evident he did not

[014-063]like my making a sketch of a letter to Lord Heytesbury, and that he does

[014-064]not like any difference of opinion as to the Batta question.

[014-065]On the first point I still think I was right. He mentions some ideas of

[014-066]Russia ordering Diebitch across the Balkan, and even the Danube, of her

[014-067]giving up the Principalities, &c. In short he says all we know is that

[014-068]there is a peace--we do not know what it is--and it would be ridiculous to

[014-069]remonstrate against we know not what.

[014-070]My draft was written before these reports were spread; and I only, from

[014-071]anxiety to have the despatch well written and soon, sketched what I thought

[014-072]would do.

[014-073]As to the reports, I have told Aberdeen I cannot believe Russia has on a

[014-074]sudden ceased to be ambitious, or to use perfidy as a mode of accomplishing

[014-075]ambitious ends. She may give out she will make these changes--she may make

[014-076]some--but her object is to prevent all combination on the part of Austria,

[014-077]France, and England. If we do not remonstrate against what is signed, we

[014-078]shall lose all credit, if that which is executed should be comparatively

[014-079]favourable, and we shall incur great blame if no relaxation takes place. A

[014-080]remonstrance might be so worded as to do no harm to Turkey or to Europe,

[014-081]and to do good to us.

[014-082]The Duke's other letter was on the Batta question, upon which he does not

[014-083]like contradiction, yet I think his course would lead to continued demands

[014-084]on the part of all the armies. I have told him I shall be in town to see

[014-085]the Chairs on Saturday, and will try to see him on Friday, and, if he

[014-086]wishes, bring the Chairs to him on Saturday.

[014-087]_October 21._

[014-088]Received a long confused letter from Fitzgerald upon my project of a draft

[014-089]to Lord Heytesbury. He was at Sudborn, [Footnote: Seat of Lord Hertford, in

[014-090]Suffolk] where the Duke was. The Duke was not so much inclined to think the

[014-091]Russians would make any considerable concessions as Aberdeen, but he

[014-092]thought, and had made Fitzgerald think, it would be premature to

[014-093]remonstrate. I have written to Fitzgerald and told him my opinion more at

[014-094]length than I told Aberdeen yesterday.

[014-095]_October 23._

[014-096]Cabinet room. Read the despatches from Petersburg and Paris. All the hints

[014-097]of the Emperor of Russia's intention of not retaining his army in Turkey

[014-098]come through Paris, Nesselrode having on September 29 spoken thus

[014-099]specifically to the Duke de Mortemart, and merely talked about taking less

[014-100]money and making some change in the guarantees to Lord Heytesbury. I did

[014-101]not see Aberdeen, who was engaged with the Spanish Minister.

[014-102]I do not depart from my original idea that Russia does all this to gain

[014-103]time, and with as much perfidy as she has shown throughout.

[014-104]Polignac would take a loyal view if he durst.

[014-105]I cannot see the Duke till Monday, as he does not return to London till

[014-106]Sunday evening.

[014-107]I saw Hardinge and had a long talk with him about Batta, &c.

[014-108]_October 24._

[014-109]Chairs at 11.

[014-110]The Chairs say the Court have the matter entirely in their hands as to

[014-111]Batta. They wish to have the opinion of the Cabinet, and to be governed by

[014-112]that. I have written to the Duke to tell him so.

[014-113]I am glad there is to be a Cabinet, because I think a Cabinet will take a

[014-114]more popular view of the question than the Duke, and, as I think, a juster

[014-115]view. I am for standing firm.

[014-116]The Duke's letter on Persian affairs arrived while I was with the Chairs. I

[014-117]read it to them. The Duke suggests that McDonald should raise his escort in

[014-118]Persia--an excellent idea. He objects to Major Hart having an assignment of

[014-119]land. He thinks Willock may be recalled. The officers not; but if the

[014-120]prince will pay them, so much the better. I think the Duke may be right as

[014-121]to the assignments of land. Upon all the other points I entirely agree with

[014-122]him. Read last night a letter of Lushington's, or rather a minute, which

[014-123]shows he is determined to remain.

[014-124]Cabinet room. Cunningham came in and showed me a draft of Aberdeen's to our

[014-125]Minister in Spain on the recognition by Spain of Don Miguel--finding

[014-126]excuses for Spain, and saying we cannot do it. What I saw was the

[014-127]_brouillon_ which had been sent to the Duke. It had his observations in

[014-128]pencil, and it seems Aberdeen sends all his proposed despatches to him and

[014-129]alters them at his suggestion. Certainly Aberdeen, left to himself, would

[014-130]be a very incautious writer.

[014-131]_October 26._

[014-132]Office early. Saw Captain Hanchett on the subject of the navigation of the

[014-133]Red Sea. He was there two years and a half. He says in going in you should

[014-134]make Aden and wait there for a wind. Water can be had there. Avoid Mocha,

[014-135]where the anchorage is dangerous and the water bad, and go to the Island of

[014-136]Cameran, then straight up in mid channel. All the dangers are visible, and

[014-137]in the mid channel there are none. Cosseir a good little harbour, the

[014-138]danger is going up to Suez; but that easy for a steamer. He worked with

[014-139]topgallant sails against the north-west monsoon. There is a breeze along

[014-140]shore at all times. The danger has been occasioned by the timid sailing of

[014-141]the Arabs, who always hug the shore, and anchor at night.

[014-142]_October 27._

[014-143]I omitted yesterday to mention that at the Foreign Office I saw some

[014-144]despatches just received from Sir R. Gordon. I think the date of the first

[014-145]was October 2. He had the day before at last got the Turks to ratify the

[014-146]treaty, but it seems there was a hitch, and until the ratification the

[014-147]officers did not set off to stop hostilities in Asia. A Pasha had advanced

[014-148]on Philippopoli and General Geismar on Sophia. Diebitch threatened to

[014-149]advance on Constantinople. However, the day after he wrote his threatening

[014-150]letter he must have received the ratifications. The Sultan is very anxious

[014-151]to get the Egyptian fleet to Constantinople, probably as a pledge for the

[014-152]allegiance of the Pasha, and to show his greatest vassal obeys him. The

[014-153]Turks say it is the moral effect of the presence of the fleet on their own

[014-154]subjects that they want, that they have no idea of not acting faithfully.

[014-155]Sir R. Gordon assures me they mean to preserve the peace and must.

[014-156]He has written the representation the Turkish ambassador is to present to

[014-157]the Emperor. It would be a good remonstrance for us, but it is not a good

[014-158]one for the Turks. It is very well written, but it is quite European in its

[014-159]style, and the Russians will at once know, as I did, the author.

[014-160]The Turks intended to send a splendid embassy to Petersburg, and Halil

[014-161]Pasha, once the slave of the Seraskier, now the Sultan's son-in-law, was to

[014-162]have been the ambassador. He is their least officer. However, Diebitch

[014-163]tells them they must not send it till they have the Emperor's consent. The

[014-164]Turks have ready the first 100,000 ducats, to get the Russians out of

[014-165]Adrianople.

[014-166]I should say from these despatches that things do not look peaceful.

[014-167]_October 28._

[014-168]Had a letter yesterday from Mr. Elphinstone on Nazarre. It appears to be a

[014-169]fine on descents, &c., of Jaghire lands. I think his opinion will be

[014-170]different from Sir J. Malcolm's--the latter wishing to make the Jaghires

[014-171]hereditary, or rather to give a fee simple interest to the actual

[014-172]proprietor. Mr. Elphinstone, on the contrary, thinking they should be

[014-173]resumed on death without heirs.

[014-174]_October 29._

[014-175]Read a work just published by Colonel de Lacy Evans, on the practicability

[014-176]of a Russian invasion of India. The route would be first to China, across a

[014-177]desert from the shores of the Caspian--from China by water up the Oxus, to

[014-178]within 550 miles of Attock. The great difficulty is between the end of the

[014-179]river, and the southern side of the Hindoo Koosh. This difficulty, however,

[014-180]has been often surmounted, and the road is constantly travelled by

[014-181]caravans.
