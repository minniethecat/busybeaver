# Duke of Norfolk

## Overview

The Duke of Norfolk (Bernard Edward Howard, 12th Duke) was the premier duke and Earl Marshal of England — the most senior lay peer of the realm and the leading Roman Catholic aristocrat. His appearance in Ellenborough's diary coincides with the period of Catholic emancipation, in which he held a direct personal and political stake.

## Role in the Diary

The Duke of Norfolk appears in Ellenborough's diary as a prominent and socially active peer during the debates over Catholic emancipation. The diary records him attending the House of Lords "as happy as" could be (001-053), reflecting the buoyant mood of Catholic peers as emancipation advanced. He called on Ellenborough personally, though on one occasion did not find him at home (002-110).

Norfolk's relationship with the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government was complex. The diary notes he was "much pleased with the Duke of Wellington" at one point (003-030), but his social calendar became a political flashpoint — ministers who dined with him faced criticism, and some "openly expressed" their anxiety about attending his dinner (004-152). The diary records that most of those invited did ultimately dine with him (004-158).

## Key Appearances

- **Taking his seat**: The diary notes the attendance numbers on "the day the Duke of Norfolk took his seat" in the House of Lords, highlighting the public interest in his arrival (003-075).
- **The Norfolk dinner controversy**: A "grand dinner at the Duke of Norfolk's" (004-108) became a political event, with ministers anxious about being seen attending. Ellenborough records that some "were to dine" there while others openly worried about the political implications (004-152).
- **Earl Marshal at state occasions**: Norfolk attended the King's drawing-room as Earl Marshal "in black full dress" alongside Lord Camden and others (034-019), and was present when he needed to be sworn in as a Privy Councillor (034-062).
- **Historical precedent**: The diary notes that there had been no Duke of Norfolk as a Privy Councillor since the reign of James II, and that the previous one was a Protestant — marking the significance of Norfolk's position as a Catholic office-holder (034-065).
