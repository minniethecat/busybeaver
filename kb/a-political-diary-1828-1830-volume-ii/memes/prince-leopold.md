**Prince Leopold of Saxe-Coburg** (1790–1865) was a German prince who became the first King of the Belgians in 1831. During the period of the diary (1828–1830), he was at the centre of intense diplomatic negotiations over the throne of Greece, and his conduct became a major political issue for the [[Duke of Wellington|Wellington]] ministry.

== The Greek Throne Negotiations ==

In early 1830, Britain, France, and Russia agreed to offer Leopold the crown of Greece. The diary records [[Lord Ellenborough]]'s early scepticism: "That Prince Leopold will make an efficient King of Greece I do not believe." (020-065) 

Leopold's nomination was controversial from the start. The King, [[George IV]], "thoroughly hates Prince Leopold, and he has been made to think" the proposal originated with France. (020-111) [[Lord Aberdeen]] was tasked with managing the delicate diplomacy.

Leopold initially accepted the throne, but attached conditions that proved unacceptable. "Leopold has sent in his answer. He accepts provided—" (024-044) The diary notes that his demands for "troops and money" (024-136) complicated the settlement.

== The Abdication Crisis ==

By mid-1830, Leopold's position had become untenable. The diary observes: "It seems clear that Leopold means to abdicate." (030-160) His formal withdrawal came shortly after: "Leopold has declined. He sent a note to that effect on Friday night." (031-032)

The resignation triggered a political firestorm. [[Lord Ellenborough]] noted "a general union of all those who prefer" (031-095) attacking the government's handling of the affair. In the House of Lords, Ellenborough defended the ministry, speaking of Leopold's "connection with this country by some of" the closest ties — referring to his late wife, Princess Charlotte. (031-127)

== Connection to Britain ==

Leopold had married Princess Charlotte of Wales, the only legitimate child of [[George IV]], in 1816. Her death in childbirth in 1817 was a national tragedy. This connection gave Leopold a unique but complicated relationship with the British royal family and government.

The diary records the delicacy of this relationship: [[Lord Ellenborough]] objected to the King dining with Leopold, noting that "other Powers having manifested the greatest dissatisfaction with Leopold's" conduct. (039-132)

== Later: King of the Belgians ==

After withdrawing from the Greek candidacy, Leopold was soon proposed as King of Belgium following the [[Belgian Revolution]] of 1830. He accepted and reigned as Leopold I from 1831 until his death in 1865, proving a capable constitutional monarch.
