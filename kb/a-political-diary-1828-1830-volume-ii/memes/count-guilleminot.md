**Count Guilleminot** (Armand Charles Guilleminot, 1774–1840) was a French general and diplomat who served as ambassador to the Ottoman Empire. The diary records his involvement in the peace negotiations of the Russo-Turkish War of 1828–1829.

== The Russo-Turkish Peace ==

In 1829, the French government used its diplomatic channels to communicate Russian peace terms to the Ottoman Porte. The diary records: "The French Government, from the information it derived from its Minister at Berlin, has instructed Count Guilleminot to declare to the Turks the terms on which Russia will make peace." (010-150) Russia demanded the execution of prior treaty obligations.

The British Government received information through French diplomatic channels. [[Robert Gordon]], the British ambassador, "managed to get a paper into the Sultan's own hands, which may have led in some measure to this result." (010-160) Gordon "naturally gave credit to the information contained in the Despatches of Count Guilleminot." (010-162)

== Controversy Over Instructions ==

Guilleminot's role became a point of diplomatic friction. The British Government was concerned that France had misrepresented the situation. The diary records the British position: "We wanted to know what he meant to do towards redeeming France from the responsibility she had incurred and made us incur by giving instructions to Count Guilleminot, stating the terms of peace and the moderation of the Emperor--instructions which misled our Ambassador." (013-149)

This episode illustrates the complex triangular diplomacy between Britain, France, and Russia in the Eastern Question, with Guilleminot at the centre of communications between Paris and Constantinople.
