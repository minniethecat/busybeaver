# Catholic Emancipation

## Overview

The Catholic Emancipation was one of the dominant political issues in British politics during the late 1820s, culminating in the Roman Catholic Relief Act 1829. The Act removed the substantial legal restrictions that had been placed on Roman Catholics in the United Kingdom since the Reformation. Edward Law, 1st Earl of Ellenborough, recorded the progress of this measure extensively in his political diary.

## The Catholic Relief Bill

The Duke of Wellington, despite his personal reservations, took up the measure of Catholic Relief. The second reading of the Catholic Relief Bill was a significant parliamentary event. (P01-060) Lord Ellenborough noted the broad political implications: the Bill was seen as arising from Lord Anglesey's administration in Ireland. (004-007)

## Parliamentary Debate

The settlement of the Catholic question was viewed as enabling the Government to address other pressing issues. (003-064) When the question was settled, it was hoped that the Government would be ready to turn to other matters of state. (003-008)

## Impact on the Political Landscape

The Catholic Relief Bill had profound effects on party unity. As Ellenborough observed, the Bill "destroyed our unity" within the Tory party. (012-050) The Duke of Wellington himself later represented the Bill as having "produced none of the evils which were anticipated." (022-063)

The conduct of the Catholics themselves was noted as being "as excellent as that of any other class of His Majesty's subjects." (P01-126)
