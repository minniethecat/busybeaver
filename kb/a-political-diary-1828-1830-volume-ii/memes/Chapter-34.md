[034-001]The King much weaker.

[034-002]_June 26._

[034-003]At half-past eight this morning I received a Cabinet box containing the

[034-004]bulletin signed by Halford and Tierney of the King's death, and Halford's

[034-005]private letter to the Duke of Wellington. The letter stated that the King

[034-006]had slept for about two hours and woke a little before three. Soon

[034-007]afterwards, Sir W. Waller only being in the room, he suddenly put his hand

[034-008]to his breast, and said, 'Good God, what is the matter? This is death?' He

[034-009]then sent for Halford. He and the others came, and so soon afterwards as I

[034-010]have said, he expired without the least struggle or pain.

[034-011]Peel summoned a Cabinet at half-past ten. We met and talked of very little

[034-012]but in what dress we should go to the Council, which was to be at twelve.

[034-013]It was agreed we should go in black, shoes and stockings, but not full

[034-014]dress. However, after I left the room the Duke arrived, and said the King

[034-015][Footnote: The Duke of Clarence now became William IV] intended to appear

[034-016]in uniform, so the Duke, Lord Bathurst, Rosslyn, and Sir J. Murray, who

[034-017]were there, put on their uniforms. The group at the Council was most

[034-018]motley. Lords Grey, Lansdowne, Spencer, Tankerville, Sir J. Warrender, and

[034-019]some others being in black full dress. Lord Camden and some more in

[034-020]uniform, which several sent for after they arrived, as Salisbury and

[034-021]Hardinge. The mass, however, in plain black, some in colours. The Royal

[034-022]Dukes came in full dress.

[034-023]We waited a long time before the Council, almost two hours, a time occupied

[034-024]in audiences.

[034-025]The Duke of Cumberland got the King to send for Lord Eldon, who went in for

[034-026]a minute only. The Duke of Cumberland received his gold stick, and seemed

[034-027]very active. The Duke of Wellington, Lord Bathurst, Rosslyn, the

[034-028]Chancellor, and Sir R. Peel went in together, and personally acquainted the

[034-029]King with the late King's death. The King said he might not have an

[034-030]opportunity of seeing that day the rest of his late Majesty's confidential

[034-031]servants; but he told those present that all had his confidence, and that

[034-032]they would receive his _entire, cordial, and determined support_. He told

[034-033]the Chancellor in a private audience not only the same thing, but that if

[034-034]at any time he should hear reports of his ceasing to place confidence in

[034-035]his Government, they were not to be believed. If he had any fault to find

[034-036]he would at once tell them.

[034-037]When the Duke and the others came out from the King we all went to the

[034-038]ball-room, where we began to sign the proclamation, and a few, the Royal

[034-039]Dukes and others, had signed, when we were called to the Privy Council

[034-040]Room, where the King soon arrived, attended by the household of the late

[034-041]King. He took his seat, and read his declaration. He read it with much

[034-042]feeling, and it was well imagined, and will have a good effect. The Lord

[034-043]President entreated it might be printed.

[034-044]I should have mentioned that before the King came in the Council made the

[034-045]usual orders, with the addition of an order for defacing the late King's

[034-046]stamps, which was accordingly done by the clerk of the Council.

[034-047]When the declaration had been read the King took the Scotch oath in the

[034-048]usual form, the Lord-President reading it to him, and the King holding up

[034-049]his right hand.

[034-050]He then said it was a satisfaction to him to find such a Privy Council, and

[034-051]requested them all to take the oath.

[034-052]This the Royal Dukes did first, then the Speaker, that he might go to the

[034-053]House of Commons. Then the Archbishop and the Chancellor together, then the

[034-054]Dukes, with the Lord President and Privy Seal, then the Marquises, then

[034-055]others according to their rank. When all had taken the Privy Councillor's

[034-056]oath the Lord Chancellor took his, and the Clerk of the Council was sworn

[034-057]by the Lord President. The King then retired, and the Council ordered as

[034-058]usual respecting the disposal of the late King's body.

[034-059]After the swearing in we signed the Proclamation. Some remained to alter

[034-060]the Liturgy. Queen Adelaide is to be prayed for, and the rest of the Royal

[034-061]family.

[034-062]The Duke of Norfolk was there as Earl Marshal. He observed he was the only

[034-063]person there who was not a Privy Councillor, and expressed a wish to be

[034-064]one. The Duke mentioned it to the King, who readily assented. He observed

[034-065]there had been no Duke of Norfolk a member of the Privy Council since the

[034-066]time of James II., and that that Duke of Norfolk was a Protestant. The Duke

[034-067]of Norfolk, however, will consider the oath before he takes it. He would

[034-068]have taken the Earl Marshal's oath to-day, but it was not there.

[034-069]We met in Cabinet at 4.

[034-070]The only innovations I yet hear of are in the dress of regiments. The King

[034-071]intends, as he told Lord Farnborough, to live at Windsor. He intends to

[034-072]have a battalion of the Guards at Edinburgh, and a regiment of the Line at

[034-073]Windsor.

[034-074]I went in, by some misdirection, the wrong way, and found Wood and Sir Ch.

[034-075]Pole waiting for the King. Wood, whom I met near the Horse Guards, as I was

[034-076]riding down to the Cabinet, told me the King had rehearsed his declaration

[034-077]to him, Sir Ch. Pole, and Lord Errol, before he went into the Privy

[034-078]Council.

[034-079]There was no grief in the room in which we waited. It was like an ordinary

[034-080]_levée_.

[034-081]The Chancellor went down to the House between the Cabinet and the Council,

[034-082]and took the oaths.

[034-083]The Lord Steward was sent for by Peel, and only arrived a quarter before

[034-084]four at the House of Commons.

[034-085]Lord Holland, Grey, and others seemed to think the Proclamation ought to

[034-086]have been made to-day, and I think it might have been just as well.

[034-087]The Duke of Wellington was much cheered by the people. The Duke was called

[034-088]out of the Cabinet to see Halford, but we had a long conversation as to the

[034-089]course to be pursued with respect to the Parliament, and especially with

[034-090]respect to the Regency question.

[034-091]The House must sit next week, as the sugar duties expire on Saturday next,

[034-092]and Goulburn seems disposed to propose a Bill for the continuance of the

[034-093]present duties for a time; to take money on account for miscellaneous

[034-094]services; to throw over the judicial Bills and end the session at once.

[034-095]The stumbling block is the Regency question--whether it should be brought

[034-096]forward now, and if brought forward, who shall be Regent.

[034-097]Peel seems to think we can hardly avoid bringing it on; as the session

[034-098]would have lasted two months in the event of the late King's living, why

[034-099]should it not now, when the reason for Parliament sitting is so much

[034-100]greater? And what would be the situation of the country if the King should

[034-101]die, leaving a minor Queen?

[034-102]Peel suggested appointing the Queen Regent for a year. I said, depend upon

[034-103]it, when the King once has her as Regent he will never consent to change

[034-104]her, and if you appoint her for a year you appoint her for the whole time.

[034-105]He afterwards suggested her appointment for a year after the King's death

[034-106]on account of the probability of her pregnancy. To this I objected, the

[034-107]state of distraction in which the country would be placed during that year.

[034-108]It is impossible consistently with the constitution to have an Executive,

[034-109]of which the existence shall be dependent on the good pleasure of

[034-110]Parliament.

[034-111]Peel then suggested the giving to the King the power of naming either the

[034-112]Queen, the Duchess of Kent, or any member of the Royal family. The

[034-113]objection to this is that he ought to name one of the two first--that we

[034-114]got no security against a bad nomination, which we ought to do.

[034-115]The views we ought to have are these: to give all possible strength to the

[034-116]monarchy. This we do not, if we permit a frequent change of the Executive;

[034-117]if we diminish the power of the Crown while in the hands of a Regency. We

[034-118]want to give stability to the Government, and this can only be given by

[034-119]making the Queen Regent. If we do that we provide, as far as human wisdom

[034-120]can, for a stable Government of seven years.

[034-121]We can in no case _name_ any other person than the Queen, because she may

[034-122]become pregnant, and in that event it would be monstrous to make the

[034-123]Duchess of Kent Regent. All we can do, then, is to give the King the option

[034-124]of choosing the Queen or the Duchess of Kent. He will name the Queen, and

[034-125]she will be the best.

[034-126]It has been observed that all Kings of England die either on Saturdays or

[034-127]Sundays.

[034-128]_June 27._

[034-129]Came up to a Cabinet at half-past three. We had a great deal of

[034-130]conversation as to the course to be pursued. The Chancellor said that in

[034-131]the event of a minor succeeding to the throne, all the minor's acts would

[034-132]be valid, and under the responsibility of ministers the Great Seal might be

[034-133]put in the minor's name by the minor's sign manual to an Act creating a

[034-134]Regency.

[034-135]It was determined to take the opinion of the Attorney- and Solicitor-

[034-136]General upon this point.

[034-137]On the supposition that the law is as the Chancellor states, we considered

[034-138]what should be done. All turns upon our being able to get a temporary Act

[034-139]for the sugar duties, and if we cannot get that we are _really_ no longer a

[034-140]Government. It was determined to carry through the Beer Bill and Beer Duty

[034-141]Bill, to throw over Stamps in Ireland, and carry Spirits. To take a sum of

[034-142]800,000£ on account of miscellaneous estimates, and 250,000£ on account of

[034-143]the civil list.

[034-144]These last points were decided at a Cabinet at Sir R. Peel's, which

[034-145]assembled at eleven, and sat till near one; at which the Attorney- and

[034-146]Solicitor-General delivered their opinion, in conformity with that of the

[034-147]Chancellor as to the legal competency of a minor sovereign.

[034-148]The Attorney-General reminded us that if the King died before the new

[034-149]Parliament assembled, the old Parliament would revive.

[034-150]Peel talked a good deal of the Regency. He is much in favour of making the

[034-151]Queen Regent for a year after the King's death, to provide for the possible

[034-152]pregnancy. It seems the principle of all Regencies has been to make the

[034-153]guardian of the person Regent. It is curious that the case should never

[034-154]have been provided for of a Queen being left pregnant of an heir apparent,

[034-155]and that it should never have occurred. The difficulty would be infinite.

[034-156]I consider the death of the King to have been one of the fortunate events

[034-157]which have often saved the Duke of Wellington. I really do not know how we

[034-158]could have gone on, had he lived two months.

[034-159]The King wishes to make Lord Combermere a Privy Councillor, thinking all

[034-160]gold sticks have been so. We find he is misinformed, and the Duke means to

[034-161]show him the list of gold-sticks not Privy Councillors, and at the same

[034-162]time to tell him how Lord Combermere stands, having within these few months

[034-163]been censured by the Government. The Duke will show the King the

[034-164]correspondence which passed lately, and leave it to him to decide. There

[034-165]would be no objection to making him a Privy Councillor some months or a

[034-166]year hence.

[034-167]Brougham made a violent speech against Lord Conyngham for not being in

[034-168]readiness to swear in the House of Commons.

[034-169]_June 28._

[034-170]Went to St. James's at eleven. The Household, the Royal family, and the

[034-171]Ministers only were there. The King was dressed in plain black. He went to

[034-172]a large window looking into the courtyard, and stood forward. There were

[034-173]but few people there at first, the Horse Guards and the Heralds. The King's

[034-174]band played God Save the King, and those who were there cheered, upon which

[034-175]numbers of people came round from before the Palace and filled the

[034-176]courtyard. They then cheered well.
