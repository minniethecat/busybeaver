# Lord Combermere

## Overview

Lord Combermere (Stapleton Cotton, 1st Viscount Combermere) was a distinguished British cavalry officer who served with distinction in the Peninsular War and later as Commander-in-Chief in India (1825–1830). His correspondence with the [[Duke of Wellington]]'s government features prominently in Ellenborough's diary, particularly regarding army discipline and the contentious Batta question in India.

## Role in the Diary

Combermere appears in the diary primarily through his official correspondence from India. The Duke of Wellington took a close interest in Combermere's reports, seeing him personally on receiving communications (028-164). The diary records mutual visits and professional courtesies: "Left my card with Lord Combermere, who called yesterday" (029-106).

A significant thread concerns the publication of one of Combermere's letters, which became a matter of political controversy. The King was "very angry with Lord Combermere" over an "attacked" letter that had been made public (014-044, 014-045). Despite this, Wellington's respect for Combermere's military administration was evident: the Duke told him "that all the orders for reduction" were being implemented smoothly (029-003).

## Key Appearances

- **Army discipline**: Combermere "directed the Adjutant-General" on matters forwarded by the Duke, reflecting his administrative authority in India (025-062).
- **The Batta question**: Several diary entries reference Combermere's handling of the Batta (allowance) dispute, with Wellington receiving "the letter lately despatched" regarding the matter (030-049, 030-051).
- **Privy Council appointment**: After his return from India, "The King wishes to make Lord Combermere a Privy Councillor," recognizing his service (034-159).
- **Correspondence with Wellington**: The diary records multiple letters between Combermere and the Duke, including Wellington's replies and the King's approval of them (033-026, 033-069).
