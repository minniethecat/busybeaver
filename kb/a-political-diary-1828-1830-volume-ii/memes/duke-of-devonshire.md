# Duke of Devonshire

## Overview

William Cavendish, 6th Duke of Devonshire (1790–1858), was one of the wealthiest peers in Britain and a prominent Whig aristocrat. During the period of Ellenborough's diary, Devonshire appears in the context of diplomatic correspondence and appointments, reflecting his standing within the political establishment despite his Whig affiliation.

## Role in the Diary

Devonshire appears in Ellenborough's diary primarily in connection with Sir W. Rumbold's case. Ellenborough "wrote to the Duke to tell him I had not yet received the Duke of Devonshire's memorandum respecting Sir W. Rumbold" (027-003). The memorandum was part of Ellenborough's efforts to gather information and compress it "into as small a compass as possible" for presentation to Wellington.

Devonshire's name also arose in the context of an Irish appointment. When "Lord Anglesey goes to Ireland; a very bad appointment," Ellenborough noted that "the Duke of Devonshire would have been a very unexceptionable one" (054-136). This remark reflects both the Tory diarist's respect for Devonshire's fitness for office and the complex cross-party dynamics of the period — a Whig grandee was seen as preferable to an unreliable Tory appointee.

## Key Appearances

- **Rumbold memorandum**: Provided a memorandum concerning Sir W. Rumbold's case, which Ellenborough was preparing for the Duke of Wellington (027-003).
- **Irish appointment**: Named by Ellenborough as a far better candidate for the Irish appointment than Lord Anglesey, indicating the high regard in which Devonshire was held even by political opponents (054-136).
