# Bishop of Oxford

## Overview

Edward Legge (1767–1827) served as Bishop of Oxford from 1816 until his death. A classical scholar of note, he was the seventh son of William Legge, 2nd Earl of Dartmouth. In Ellenborough's diary, his death in early 1827 is recorded with a note on the character of his successor, reflecting the political interest attached to senior ecclesiastical appointments.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records two references to the Bishop of Oxford. The first appears at a Cabinet dinner at Sir Robert Peel's, where Peel remarked that "the Bishop of Oxford was ready" (P01-035) — likely referring to his willingness to support a government measure, perhaps related to Catholic emancipation or church reform.

The Bishop also appears during a parliamentary division, where Ellenborough notes "the Bishop of Oxford had" voted on one side of a contested question (P01-062). The diary notes that both he and another prelate had voted against the government position.

The Bishop's death is recorded tersely: "The Bishop of Oxford is dead; a great Grecian is to succeed him" (005-116). The successor — a classical scholar — was presumably chosen to continue the diocese's scholarly tradition.

## Key Appearances

- **Cabinet dinner**: Peel noted the Bishop's readiness at a Cabinet dinner, likely concerning a government bill (P01-035).
- **Parliamentary division**: The Bishop voted against the government alongside another prelate (P01-062).
- **Death and succession**: His death opened a major ecclesiastical appointment; Ellenborough noted his successor was "a great Grecian" (005-116).
