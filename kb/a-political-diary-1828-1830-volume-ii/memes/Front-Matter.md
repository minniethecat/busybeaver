[P01-016]DIARY

[P01-017]_April 1, 1829._

[P01-018]The Duke of Wellington wrote to the King to ask if he had any objection to

[P01-019]raising the galleries. He had none. So we sent for Sir T. Tyrwhit, and had

[P01-020]him at the Cabinet dinner to ask him whether he could fix the galleries by

[P01-021]four to-morrow. He said _No_. So we must do as we can.

[P01-022]Forty foreigners applied for seats to-day after four o'clock.

[P01-023]In the House I made the second reading of the Bills an order of the day at

[P01-024]the desire of Lord Malmesbury and Lord Grey. It is more formal so, but the

[P01-025]second reading might have been equally well moved without it.

[P01-026]Lord Grey said a few words on presenting a petition expressing a hope to be

[P01-027]convinced on the subject of the Franchise Bill, but laying ground for

[P01-028]voting against it. Lord Malmesbury likewise expressed himself against it.

[P01-029]We shall be hard pushed on this Bill. The Duke says we have 122 sure votes

[P01-030]and no more upon it.

[P01-031]The Bishop of Chester read prayers, his wife having died about ten days

[P01-032]ago. Really some one of the other Bishops might have relieved him.

[P01-033]Lord Shaftesbury, in the absence of the Chancellor, sat as Speaker. I moved

[P01-034]the bills _pro formâ_ for him.

[P01-035]At the Cabinet dinner at Peel's, Peel said the Bishop of Oxford was ready

[P01-036]to speak at any time, and wished to follow a violent bishop. He may easily

[P01-037]find one.

[P01-038]We had much talk about our approaching debates. Peel, after the Duke was

[P01-039]gone, regretted his having taken the line of expressing his anxiety to

[P01-040]relieve himself from the obloquy cast upon him, and his having put that

[P01-041]desire forward as his reason for pressing the second reading of the Bill on

[P01-042]Thursday. The Duke having said so, we could not back him out. We might

[P01-043]avoid taking the same ground, but we could not alter it.

[P01-044]Aberdeen mentioned the case of the Candian blockade. I am sorry to see he

[P01-045]does not communicate beforehand now with the Duke. He never looks forward

[P01-046]to the ultimate consequences of his measures. Now he talks of convoying

[P01-047]English ships to Candia, and telling them they may go there safely, and if

[P01-048]stopped shall be indemnified. But if the English ship finds a Russian off

[P01-049]Candia, and is warned off, yet persists, under the expectation of

[P01-050]indemnity, we should be obliged to pay the indemnity. The Russians, having

[P01-051]given warning, would be justified in taking the vessel.

[P01-052]So if we give convoy, and the convoy ship persists, we should come to

[P01-053]blows. All these things should be foreseen. Aberdeen thinks Lièven is

[P01-054]ignorant of Heyden's having had any orders. He excuses him as having acted

[P01-055]in the spirit of the treaty, to _avoid the effusion_ of blood!

[P01-056]One thing is clear; we cannot permit Russia, as a belligerent, to defeat

[P01-057]the objects of the Treaty of London, and yet act with her under that

[P01-058]treaty.

[P01-059]_April 2._

[P01-060]Second reading Catholic Relief Bill. The Duke made a very bad speech. The

[P01-061]Archbishop of Canterbury drivelled. The Primate of Ireland made a strong

[P01-062]speech, his manner admirable. Both these against. The Bishop of Oxford had

[P01-063]placed himself at our disposal to be used when wanted. We put him into the

[P01-064]debate here, wanting him very much. The first part of his speech was very

[P01-065]indifferent, the latter excellent. Lord Lansdowne spoke better than he has

[P01-066]done for some time, indeed for two years. The Bishop of London against us;

[P01-067]but he made a speech more useful than ten votes, in admirable taste,

[P01-068]looking to the measure as one to be certainly accomplished, &c. The Duke of

[P01-069]Richmond spoke very shortly, but better than he has ever done, in reply. We

[P01-070]adjourned at 1.

[P01-071]229 members in the House. Room for thirty more; the House not oppressively

[P01-072]hot; numbers of women. The tone of the debate temperate.

[P01-073]_April 3._

[P01-074]A speech from the Bishop of Durham, full of fallacies and extravagant, but

[P01-075]having its effect.

[P01-076]The Chancellor spoke admirably, endeavouring to bring up Eldon, but the old

[P01-077]man would not move. He wanted more time to consider his answer, by which he

[P01-078]will not improve it.

[P01-079]A speech from Goderich, very animated in his way, and very heavy. The House

[P01-080]did not cheer him once. He pressed himself upon it with bad taste. He spoke

[P01-081]upon all the collateral and unimportant points. He swung his arm about like

[P01-082]a boy throwing a stone from a sling.

[P01-083]Lord Mansfield spoke, sleepily and ill-naturedly. I was exhausted, and

[P01-084]could not have answered him, had he said anything worth answering.

[P01-085]We adjourned at two till one to-morrow.

[P01-086]_April 4._

[P01-087]House at 1. A long absurd speech from Lord Guildford, which must have given

[P01-088]much pain to Lady Ch. Lindsay, who sat under the throne, and who must have

[P01-089]been much annoyed at seeing to what her family had fallen. We had then Lord

[P01-090]Lilford, who rested too much on his notes, but who has a good manner. He

[P01-091]drew his points well, and spoke like a man, not like a boy.

[P01-092]Lord Tenterden was not powerful. Lord Grey spoke better than he has done

[P01-093]since 1827. He made a speech too long, and indeed the last half-hour was of

[P01-094]no use. He beat the brains out of the Coronation Oath, as an obstacle to

[P01-095]Catholic Concession, and read a curious letter of Lord Yestor to Lord

[P01-096]Tweddale, dated April, 1689, before William III. took the Coronation Oath,

[P01-097]in which Lord Tester mentions that it was understood that the king had in

[P01-098]council declared his understanding of the sense of the Coronation Oath--

[P01-099]that it bound him in his executive capacity, not in his legislative. Lord

[P01-100]Westmoreland made an odd, entertaining from its manner, and really very

[P01-101]good speech. He supported the Bill.

[P01-102]Lord Eldon, who, after an ineffectual attempt on the part of Lord Redesdale

[P01-103]to speak, followed Lord Grey, made a very weak, inefficient, powerless

[P01-104]speech. He seemed beaten, and in some respects his memory had failed him.

[P01-105]Lord Plunket drew, with great power, a picture of the state of society in

[P01-106]Ireland as affected by the laws. The whole of his speech was powerful.

[P01-107]His speech and Lord Grey's were excellent.

[P01-108]After a few sentences from Lord Farnham we divided.

[P01-119]Majority 105

[P01-120]This will quiet Windsor. The King was to have received a number of

[P01-121]petitions to be presented by peers to-day. The Primate of Ireland was to

[P01-122]have gone, and the Irish Bishops. The latter went. If they had not gone,

[P01-123]the King would have made some excuse for not receiving them.

[P01-124]The majority must put an end to all agitation in England, and tranquillise

[P01-125]Ireland. Indeed as regards this question Ireland is tranquil. The conduct

[P01-126]of the Catholics has been as excellent as that of the Protestants. Hitherto

[P01-127]the announcement of the measure has produced effects beyond what was

[P01-128]anticipated from its adoption.

[P01-129]The Duke of Rutland, who was not expected, and indeed every doubtful vote

[P01-130]was with us.

[P01-131]The Protestants are subdued.

[P01-132]Lord Grey's speech, but still more Lord Plunket's, will have a greater

[P01-133]effect upon the public mind, than any which have yet been delivered.

[P01-134]Really it seems like a dream! That I should, if I lived, live to see this I

[P01-135]did expect; but that I should see it so soon, and that I should happen to

[P01-136]be a member of the Government that carried it, I did not expect. I must say

[P01-137]with what delight I view the prospect of having Catholics in Parliament. I

[P01-138]am sure it will do more for the happiness of Ireland, and for the strength

[P01-139]of the Empire, than any measure that could have been adopted.

[P01-140]_April 5. _

[P01-141]Dined with Lady Sandwich and met the Arbuthnots, with whom I had a long

[P01-142]talk. She told me the Duke wanted to bring in Lord Chandos, by way of

[P01-143]conciliating the Tories. She thought Lord Rosslyn ought to have the Privy

[P01-144]Seal, and that, considering their late conduct, the Whigs should be

[P01-145]preferred to the Tories, whom we should have at any rate. That it was

[P01-146]enough not to punish them by depriving them of their offices.

[P01-147]In all this I agree. I think if the Duke should go to the Tories and turn

[P01-148]his back upon the Whigs after what has taken place, he will make Opposition

[P01-149]very acrimonious, and our debates very disagreeable.

[P01-150]I told her if the Privy Seal was to be a Tory, I thought the Duke of

[P01-151]Richmond the best. He is the most popular man in the House of Lords, and a

[P01-152]good debater. The Duke and Lord Bathurst say he is cunning; but as far as I

[P01-153]can judge he acts fairly.

[P01-154]_April 6._

[P01-155]House. Second reading Franchise Bill. Opposed by the Duke of Richmond, Lord

[P01-156]Malmesbury, Winchelsea, and Clanricarde. Lord Holland spoke in favour of

[P01-157]the Bill as connected with the Relief Bill. The Whigs voted with us. Dudley

[P01-158]spoke in favour, just to separate himself from the Canningites, for whom

[P01-159]Haddington spoke, more reluctant than the Whigs.

[P01-160]Lord Winchelsea was very mad, wished to expel the bishops, to prevent

[P01-161]translations, equalise their sees, &c. We had 139 to 19. The minority

[P01-162]were--Dukes: Cumberland, Gloucester, Brandon, Richmond, Newcastle;

[P01-163]Marquises--Salisbury, Clanrickarde; Earls--Winchelsea Malmesbury, O'Neil;

[P01-164]Lords--Falmouth, Penrhyn, Boston, Grantley, Glenlyon; Earl Digby, Earl

[P01-165]Romney.

[P01-166]The Duke goes to Windsor on Saturday to get the King to consent to give the

[P01-167]Royal assent on Thursday, the day before Good Friday. The Duke of

[P01-168]Cumberland has been mischievous at Windsor. The King fancies he is in the

[P01-169]situation of Louis XVI. That he shall run down by Liberalism. The Duke of

[P01-170]Cumberland swears he will turn us out, let who will be Ministers.

[P01-171]_April 7._

[P01-172]Lord Eldon and others opened afresh the question as to the principle of the

[P01-173]Bill on the first clause. We divided with more than 2 to 1.

[P01-174]The Bishops and Lord Eldon got into a theological discussion.

[P01-175]The Chancellor made a strong attack upon Lord Eldon, who really spoke very

[P01-176]childishly.

[P01-177]We had as many women as ever, but a new set, and some of the prettiest

[P01-178]girls in London--Miss Bagot, Miss Sheridan, and others.

[P01-179]At Windsor, last Sunday, the Duke of Cumberland spoke very warmly indeed to

[P01-180]Aberdeen about the Duke of Wellington. He said he had sat by us as our

[P01-181]friend, till the King's Ministers joined in the _hoot_ against him. (This

[P01-182]was particularly Lord Bathurst, who shook his head at him and cheered

[P01-183]offensively.) He seems in speaking of the Duke of Wellington to have used

[P01-184]terms hardly to be expected.

[P01-185]He told the Chancellor to-day that he should, before the Bill passed,

[P01-186]declare he never could again feel confidence in His Majesty's Ministers;

[P01-187]that the country was ruined; and that he should leave it and never return.

[P01-188]The Chancellor told him he advised him not to make the last promise. I hope

[P01-189]he will make it and keep it.

[P01-190]I observed him afterwards address the Chancellor very warmly, after he had

[P01-191]attacked Eldon.

[P01-192]A man of the name of Halcomb has advertised for a meeting on Friday, on the

[P01-193]road to Windsor, to carry petitions to the King.

[P01-194]April 8.

[P01-195]Committee on Relief Bill. No division. Several amendments. Those of Lord

[P01-196]Tenterden very silly.

[P01-197]I said a very few words twice.

[P01-198]The third reading is fixed for Friday. When the Duke of Cumberland heard

[P01-199]the third reading fixed he left the House like a disappointed fiend. He did

[P01-200]not take his hat off till he had got half-way down.
