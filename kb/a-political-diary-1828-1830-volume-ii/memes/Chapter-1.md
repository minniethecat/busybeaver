[001-001]Lord Eldon seems quite beaten.

[001-002]_April 9._

[001-003]Lord Eldon went to Windsor to-day with petitions. Yesterday Lord Howe and

[001-004]three others went. I believe these peers have been: Duke of Newcastle,

[001-005]Kenyon, Rolle, Howe, O'Neil, Bexley, Winchelsea, Farnham, and six bishops.

[001-006]Cabinet at 2. A meeting is advertised for to-morrow, to take place at

[001-007]Apsley House. Then to proceed to Slough or Salt Hill, or to Eton, to

[001-008]deliver there a petition to the Duke of Cumberland, who is then to present

[001-009]it to the King, and the people are to wait for an answer.

[001-010]The Duke has written to the King, acquainting him with the plan, and

[001-011]advising His Majesty to refuse to receive the petition except through the

[001-012]hands of Mr. Peel.

[001-013]Peel is going down to Windsor himself. The Duke writes to-night to tell the

[001-014]King he is going, and to repeat his advice of this morning as coming from

[001-015]the Cabinet.

[001-016]If the King will not take Peel's advice we go out.

[001-017]The Duke thinks the King will yield, and that the meeting will be a

[001-018]failure. So have I thought from the first. There is no agitation in London.

[001-019]No feeling, no excitement. The King will know Peel is coming in time to be

[001-020]able to inform the Duke of Cumberland, and prevent his setting out.

[001-021]In the House about nine the Duke received a letter from Sir W. Knighton,

[001-022]informing him that he had _no doubt_ the King would take his advice

[001-023]respecting the petitions. Eldon was there, and probably saw the letter.

[001-024]House. Got through the report of the Franchise Bill. Third reading fixed

[001-025]for to-morrow. I had to say a few words.

[001-026]_April 11, 1829._

[001-027]House. A long speech from Lord Eldon, containing no argument, and both flat

[001-028]and bad.

[001-029]Then a speech from Lord Harrowby, long and sensible; but heavily delivered

[001-030]and not wanted. A long speech from Lord Lansdowne, still less wanted, and

[001-031]very dull.

[001-032]The Duke was obliged to say something civil to the Whigs, but he did it

[001-033]sparingly, and _contre coeur_.

[001-034]We had a majority of 104. The Franchise Bill was likewise read a third

[001-035]time.

[001-036]The mutual congratulations were cordial. The House is in good humour again.

[001-037]All are glad to get rid of the question. The Duke of Cumberland, Falmouth,

[001-038]and Winchelsea, perhaps Kenyon, are lost to the Government, but no others.

[001-039]Lord Middleton voted with us, having been against on the second reading.

[001-040]The Duke of Rutland against, having been with us before.

[001-041]The Duke of Clarence was absent, being ill. He had fourteen leeches on his

[001-042]temples.

[001-043]The House was full of ladies. Mrs. Fox, Lady Jersey, Lady Pitt and her

[001-044]daughters, Lady A. Brudenell, Lady Harrowby, Lady G. Wortley, Lord Eldon's

[001-045]daughters, Lady Glengall, Mrs. and Miss Sheridan, the old Duchess of

[001-046]Richmond, Lady Manners, Lady Rolle, Lady Haddington, and many others.

[001-047]The intended row failed altogether. Only four carriages went down to

[001-048]Windsor. Halcomb and his two friends saw an equerry. They were told their

[001-049]petition must be presented through the Secretary of State, and went away

[001-050]quietly.

[001-051]The Duke of Cumberland said he must withdraw his support from the

[001-052]Government; but he was temperate. In fact he was beaten.

[001-053]The Duke of Norfolk was in the House, as happy as man could be.

[001-054]_April 11._

[001-055]Dr. Clarke and H. Fane both spoke of the Chancellor's speech in attack upon

[001-056]Eldon, as in bad taste and offensive. I shall endeavour to ascertain

[001-057]whether this is the general opinion. Not having heard Eldon, they cannot

[001-058]know how very mischievous and disingenuous he was.

[001-059]_April 12._

[001-060]Met the Lievens, Lyndhursts, Sir J. Murray, and others at dinner, at the

[001-061]Esterhazy's. The King has not yet sent back the commission to pass the

[001-062]Catholic Bill.

[001-063]The Lievens are more shy of me than ever.

[001-064]Lord Bathurst seemed to be much pleased with my idea of carrying on the

[001-065]Government of India in the King's name. He said it should be under a

[001-066]Secretary of State for India.

[001-067]The Chancellor approved highly of my notion of suggesting Herries for the

[001-068]Government of Bombay, if the directors will not have Courtney. He is

[001-069]useless to us, and a discredit. Besides, we want his place.

[001-070]Had some talk with Vernon at Lady Jersey's. He has the Canning venom about

[001-071]him still, and said we should still regret having lost Huskisson, &c.

[001-072]I said NEVER. He was an able man, but he would never do as a member of a

[001-073]Cabinet in which he was not chief. The Government would not have lived if

[001-074]he had continued in. I told him I had become satisfied from my short

[001-075]experience that a coalition Government could not conduct the affairs of the

[001-076]country with advantage--especially where the difference was [blank].

[001-077]The Duke of Cumberland is gone to Windsor. If the commission should not

[001-078]arrive to-night I dare say the Duke of Wellington will go to Windsor early

[001-079]to-morrow.

[001-080]Lady Jersey was very loud in her dispraise of the Duke of Richmond. Every

[001-081]one who knows him says he is very cunning. There is a mixture of good and

[001-082]bad taste about him. He is popular, and he would make a good man of

[001-083]business.

[001-084]_April 13, 1829, Monday._

[001-085]Chairs at 11. Informed them of Sir Sidney Beckwith's appointment to the

[001-086]command at Bombay.

[001-087]Told them my general idea was that it was necessary to fix a Lieutenant-

[001-088]Governor at Agra. I showed them it could be done without expense. Sir

[001-089]Charles Metcalfe should be the person appointed, with precise instructions

[001-090]obliging him to a system of non-interference in the internal concerns of

[001-091]the Malwa and Rajpoot States. Sir J. Malcolm would have interposed.

[001-092]The treaties with the Rajpoot States generally secure their internal

[001-093]independence. Those with the States of Malwa give us the right, and impose

[001-094]upon us the duty of supervision. It requires, therefore, a most delicate

[001-095]hand to bring the whole into one system animated by one spirit.

[001-096]I said incidentally to-day, 'I will not sit here to sacrifice India to

[001-097]England,' a sentiment which escaped me, but which I feel to be correct, not

[001-098]only socially but politically.

[001-099]Ashley came and bored me about a petition of some Hindoos and Mahometans in

[001-100]Calcutta, who wish to be grand jurors. I told him I could not proceed

[001-101]hastily in any matter of legislation, and that this was one of much

[001-102]delicacy. I should speak to Fergusson.

[001-103]A Cabinet had been fixed for 3. I concluded it was on account of a delay on

[001-104]the King's part in giving the Royal assent to the Relief Bill. The Cabinet

[001-105]was counter-ordered, the Commission having arrived at two.

[001-106]The Chancellor had sent a note to the King with the Bills, calling his

[001-107]attention to them. The King, on sending them back with the Commission

[001-108]signed, thanked the Chancellor for having called his attention to the

[001-109]Bills, and said he gave his assent reluctantly.

[001-110]The Chancellor had sent a note last night to Watson, the Equerry, desiring

[001-111]him to remind the King of the Commission.

[001-112]So at a few minutes before four to-day the Chancellor, Lord Bathurst, and I

[001-113]sat as Commissioners to give the Royal assent to the Relief Bill, and about

[001-114]thirty-nine others. So many had been kept back to force an early decision.

[001-115]The Indemnity Bill was one of the Bills, and the Militia Lists Bill

[001-116]another. There were thirteen peers in the House, and seven or eight more

[001-117]about. Lord Savoy, his son, young Lambton, Lady Petres, and her daughters,

[001-118]Mrs. Fox, and some other ladies were there--Lady Stanhope. The old Duchess

[001-119]of Richmond came too late.

[001-120]I observed that in passing each other very close the Duke of Wellington and

[001-121]the Duke of Cumberland took no notice of each other.

[001-122]Lord Durham said to me, 'Now the King will turn you all out in revenge as

[001-123]soon as he can,' to which I assented. He certainly will when he dares.

[001-124]The Duke of Norfolk and Mr. Petres were in the House, giving and receiving

[001-125]congratulations. All parties congratulate the Duke. Falmouth alone still

[001-126]looks sad and sombre. The Duke of Wellington has a bad cold. He was very

[001-127]hoarse, and wrapped himself in his cloak as soon as he had done speaking.

[001-128]_April 14._

[001-129]Saw Mr. Fergusson respecting a petition from Hindoos and Mahometans at

[001-130]Calcutta, praying to be allowed to sit on grand juries. He thinks they

[001-131]should--as they are allowed to sit on petty juries. If the matter had been

[001-132]well considered, the privilege they now ask should have been granted before

[001-133]that they have obtained.

[001-134]Mr. Fergusson is, however, rather afraid of allowing them to sit on the

[001-135]trial of Christians.

[001-136]By the newspapers I see that there has been a quarrel at Teheran, between

[001-137]some of the Russian Ambassador's suite and the populace, which led to an

[001-138]attack upon the Russian palace, and to the death of the Ambassador and all

[001-139]his people except two. This is an unfortunate event, as it will give the

[001-140]Russians a new claim to indemnity, which they will exercise inexorably.

[001-141]Probably they will insist on the junction of Persia in the attack on

[001-142]Turkey, as the only satisfaction they can accept.

[001-143]It is just possible that the example once given, and the people despairing

[001-144]of pardon, a rising against the Russians may take place, and something of a

[001-145]national feeling arise in Persia. But I fear this will not be the case. I

[001-146]suppose our Minister was at Tabriz.

[001-147]_April 15._

[001-148]The Duke was at Windsor to-day to ask the King's permission to restore the

[001-149]resigners. The King said he thought the Duke could not do better. He just

[001-150]mentioned Wetherell's name as if he thought he was to be excepted from the

[001-151]restoration, but desired to be _certior-factus_.

[001-152]The King was cold. The Duke had to wait twenty minutes, the Duke of

[001-153]Cumberland being with the King. However, I believe this delay may only have

[001-154]originated in a necessary change of dress on His Majesty's part, as he was

[001-155]sitting for his picture _in a Highland dress_. The Duke saw a large plaid

[001-156]bonnet in the room, and he believes the King had still on plaid stockings.

[001-157]The business of the restoration was finished in ten minutes, when the

[001-158]conversation flagged, and the Duke was rising to go away.

[001-159]However, something more was then said, and the interview in all lasted

[001-160]twenty minutes. The King said he was delighted with Lord Winchelsea. He was

[001-161]so gentlemanlike, and spoke _in so low a tone of voice!_ He likewise

[001-162]thought Lord Farnham very gentlemanlike, and Lord Rolle more violent than

[001-163]any.

[001-164]The Duke had to wait twenty minutes before he could see Lady Conyngham.

[001-165]They seemed to wish him not to see her. However, he did. She said all would

[001-166]have been quiet if the Duke of Cumberland had not come over, and all would

[001-167]be quiet when he went away. The King seemed relieved since the Bill was

[001-168]passed.

[001-169]On his return the Duke sent for George Bankes and offered him his place

[001-170]again. Bankes asked two or three days to consider. The Duke gave him till

[001-171]to-morrow.

[001-172]It seems he has now a notion that he owed his place not to the Duke but to

[001-173]some other influence. I think this has been insinuated to him since his

[001-174]resignation. The fact is otherwise. The King had mentioned Bankes for other

[001-175]situations, but not for the one he holds. On my return home I found Bankes

[001-176]had called upon me.

[001-177]After dinner we considered whether the prosecution of Lawless for his

[001-178]conduct at Ballybeg should be persevered in.

[001-179]Goulbourn, Peel, Lord Bathurst, Sir G. Murray, and I were for dropping it.

[001-180]I think the Chancellor inclined the same way. The Duke and the rest,

[001-181]Aberdeen being absent, were for going on.

[001-182]I thought no benefit would be derived from success. Even success would

[001-183]revive feelings and recollections which are dying away, and which we wish

[001-184]to be forgotten. If we decline proceeding we can say we did so from the

[001-185]fear of exciting dormant passions. If we proceed, we shall have no excuse

[001-186]should we revive the memory of bad times.
