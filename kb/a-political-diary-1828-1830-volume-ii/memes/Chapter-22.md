[022-001]_January 28._

[022-002]The 'Times' publishes my letter to Malcolm to-day, with comments.

[022-003]Upon the whole I am glad the letter has been published. I think no one can

[022-004]read it without seeing I am actuated only by public views, and that I am

[022-005]determined to do my duty.

[022-006]The editor of the 'Courier' called at the Indian Board and saw Bankes, and

[022-007]asked whether he should say anything. Bankes said he would see me before he

[022-008]gave an answer. I do not care about the publication, and the letter will

[022-009]defend itself.

[022-010]_January 29._

[022-011]Chairs have received very bad accounts of the temper of the Madras army,

[022-012]which has no cause of complaint. Lord W. Bentinck has been at last obliged

[022-013]to lay his hand upon the press, and, as might have been expected, is much

[022-014]more abused than if he had done so at first. The Radicals had begun to

[022-015]consider him one of themselves, and so think him a traitor when he refuses

[022-016]to go any further with them.

[022-017]I went to the Duke and told him what they said. He is, as usual, sanguine,

[022-018]and thinks it will blow over.

[022-019]I told the Duke I thought he had better look out for a Governor-General,

[022-020]for it might be necessary to recall Lord W. Bentinck. The objection to

[022-021]making Malcolm provisional successor is that he would stay till he died in

[022-022]order to be Governor-General one day. Otherwise his provisional appointment

[022-023]would strengthen the local Government very much.

[022-024]At the Cabinet they had all read my letter in the 'Times,' except the

[022-025]Chancellor. I told him to read it.

[022-026]Peel was indignant at the publication. Lord Rosslyn said Joseph Hume had

[022-027]had the letter some time in his possession, and must have sent it to the

[022-028]'Times.'

[022-029]Peel said it was a very good letter. I said I was not ashamed of it.

[022-030]They all laughed very much at the simile of the elephants.

[022-031]Cabinet. Much discussion as to the terms of the speech. Aberdeen's part was

[022-032]very ill done indeed. It underwent much alteration and was improved. That

[022-033]regarding distress and remedies was postponed. There is no remedy, and it

[022-034]is best to say so.

[022-035]In the meantime the export of almost all manufactures is increased largely

[022-036]in quantity, but the value is diminished. Still this proves continued and

[022-037]increased employment, although at low wages. This is a state of things in

[022-038]which we cannot try to make corn dearer or wool either. Nothing but the

[022-039]extreme cheapness of our manufactures makes their export possible.

[022-040]Aberdeen read his letter to Consul Barker respecting the. Pasha's designs.

[022-041]The last paragraph, which intimated that the Pasha's persistence 'would too

[022-042]probably lead to our decided opposition,' was omitted. It was thought that

[022-043]the recommendation, 'to weigh well the serious consequences of a measure

[022-044]highly objectionable to us, and to which other Powers could not but be

[022-045]unfavourable,' was thought sufficient to stop the Pasha.

[022-046]If the first words had stood, we must have used the same to France, and the

[022-047]threat might have led to collision. In any case the Pasha would have

[022-048]communicated the expressions to France.

[022-049]The Duke and the Chancellor were to see Leopold to-morrow.

[022-050]Another Cabinet to-morrow at four for going on with the Speech.

[022-051]_January 30._

[022-052]Hardinge called. He told me all was not settled as to Lord Chandos having

[022-053]the Mint. He referred to the Duke of Buckingham, [Footnote: He had, as

[022-054]appears from the Wellington correspondence, pressed for years his claims to

[022-055]a seat in the Cabinet, with an importunity to which the Duke of Wellington

[022-056]expressed his objection. His large parliamentary interest, which almost

[022-057]made him the chief of a party of his own, made him appear entitled to

[022-058]expect it.] who would rather have it himself, with a seat in the Cabinet.

[022-059]Lord MountCharles goes out to annoy his father, and force him to give him a

[022-060]larger allowance, unaccompanied by the condition of constant attendance in

[022-061]the House of Commons.

[022-062]Read the Duke of Northumberland's letter to Peel on the state of Ireland.

[022-063]The Duke represents the Catholic Relief Bill as having produced none of the

[022-064]evils anticipated by its opposers, if it has not produced all the benefits

[022-065]expected by its supporters--as having upon the whole worked better than

[022-066]could have been expected in so short a time and under such circumstances.

[022-067]The disturbances he thinks confined to the counties of Tipperary, Clare,

[022-068]and Roscommon; in the first produced by too high rents; in the second by

[022-069]late collision and the want of proper management on the part of the

[022-070]gentlemen; in the last by attempts to convert the Catholics, and the zeal

[022-071]of new converts. The Catholic Union is dissolved. The great body of the

[022-072]Catholics have abstained from the ostentation of triumph.

[022-073]_Monday, February 1._

[022-074]Bankes called this morning, but I did not see him. He saw Henry. He came to

[022-075]say he was out, and S. Wortley in his place. When he understood Lord

[022-076]Chandos did not take the Mint, he went to the Duke and offered to remain,

[022-077]thinking his going out, with Lord Chandos's declining to come in, might,

[022-078]taken together, embarrass the Government. However, the arrangement was

[022-079]already made.

[022-080]Read Lushington's minute on the Neilgherry hills. He wants to make an

[022-081]English colony there. If he had, every man would make some excuse, desert

[022-082]his duty in the hot months, and go to the Neilgherry hills.

[022-083]Read the first volume of Gamba's 'Travels in South Russia.' He was Consul

[022-084]of France, but writes like a Russian. He talks of restoring the commercial

[022-085]communication with Asia by the Phasis, Caspian, and Oxus. All this is

[022-086]absurd. Unless indeed the Russians, after occupying China, turn the Oxus

[022-087]into its old course, and thus enable themselves to carry goods by water

[022-088]carriage to the foot of the Himalaya, or rather within 250 miles of Cabul.

[022-089]_February 5._

[022-090]Received last night a note from the Duke asking me, if I could, to have a

[022-091]Cabinet to-day on Batta. If I could not, to send Peel the letters of

[022-092]Malcolm, &c.

[022-093]I determined to have the Cabinet. Peel had not read till the day before

[022-094]yesterday the Batta papers, and, although inclining to the opinion that the

[022-095]present orders must be maintained, he thinks it, as it is, a serious

[022-096]question for the Government to decide after the minutes of Lord William

[022-097]Bentinck and the members of council, with the apprehension of a mutiny as

[022-098]the possible result of our standing firm. I said if we gave way the other

[022-099]armies would bring forward their demands--that it was a question, not only

[022-100]between the Home Authorities and the army, but the Home Authorities and the

[022-101]Local Government which had for sixteen years resisted the orders sent to

[022-102]them.

[022-103]The Duke cautioned the Cabinet as to the character of the Indian army,

[022-104]which he said was a _mercenary army_, retained in obedience by nothing but

[022-105]the wish to return to England; but he thought after what had taken place we

[022-106]must resist, and adhere to our present orders. Peel wished all the members

[022-107]of the Cabinet to read the minutes before they decided, and there is to be

[022-108]a Cabinet on Sunday.

[022-109]It was determined that if a question should be asked to-night, Peel should

[022-110]say 'the orders had not been countermanded.' Peel observed very justly on

[022-111]the state of things which seemed to exist in India. An army sending such

[022-112]memorials to the Government, and the members of the Government writing

[022-113]pamphlets against each other. In point of fact, years will be required to

[022-114]restore a proper tone to the Government of India.

[022-115]I mentioned to the Duke the mission of two Russian Poles to India and

[022-116]Manilla, and that I suspected Russia of a wish to purchase Manilla. Neither

[022-117]the Duke nor Aberdeen seemed to think the Spaniards would or could sell the

[022-118]Philippines. However, Aberdeen will write to the man at Madrid to find out

[022-119]whether any proposal to that effect has been made by the Russian

[022-120]Government.

[022-121]The members of the House of Commons consider their majority last night

[022-122]fortunate. The House is very loose. In the majority and minority were the

[022-123]most opposite parties. O'Connell went out with Sadler. The Brunswickers are

[022-124]in high glee, and have sent for their valiant champion, Falmouth. In our

[022-125]House they made a poor show.

[022-126]Prince Leopold is not by any means disposed to take Greece without Candia,

[022-127]and it was thought, from Lord Lansdowne's speech, he and others had advised

[022-128]him to take this line. Aberdeen is very much embarrassed to find a

[022-129]substitute.

[022-130]_February 6._

[022-131]Spring-Rice asked Bankes in the House last night whether the letter to Sir

[022-132]J. Malcolm published as mine was mine. Bankes said that I had no copy of

[022-133]it, and therefore could not say it was correctly given. It was a private

[022-134]letter. Brougham, and Mackintosh, and that ass, M. A. Taylor, spoke in

[022-135]reprobation of it. Mackintosh most unfairly and disingenuously pretended to

[022-136]understand I endeavoured to get off by saying it was a private letter, and

[022-137]said it would be an extenuation of my offence if I would disavow the

[022-138]sentiments contained in it. What must he be himself to suppose I would

[022-139]disavow what I had written! Upon the whole, the tone taken by Peel and

[022-140]Bankes, but more especially by Peel, was too apologetical. I shall be

[022-141]obliged to go to the House on Monday to have a question put to me by Lord

[022-142]Lansdowne. I shall distinctly declare he may consider the letter as mine,

[022-143]and that I am ready to defend every line of it. Wrote to Lord Wellesley to

[022-144]offer to put his name upon the Committee on East India affairs if he would

[022-145]attend. He declines on account of ill-health.

[022-146]Received a note from Peel begging me to have the Chairs to meet him on the

[022-147]appointment of the committee. I sent to the Chairman, and he came and met

[022-148]Peel; but Astell was out of the way. We are to meet at half-past one to-

[022-149]morrow. Peel did not seem to have looked much into the subject, which the

[022-150]Chairman observed.

[022-151]Saw Bankes. He is not certain of succeeding now to the secretaryship of the

[022-152]Admiralty, but he expects it ultimately. He thinks the Duke of Buckingham

[022-153]had nothing to do with Lord Chandos's rejection of the Mint: but does not

[022-154]know how it went off. He thought that Lord Chandos had accepted, and the

[022-155]Duke seems to have thought so too.

[022-156]A very good account from Ireland. The country gradually and quietly coming

[022-157]round.

[022-158]_Sunday, February 7._

[022-159]Cabinet. First, Batta. The Duke gave his decided opinion in favour of

[022-160]adhering to the present order. After some conversation, but no opposition,

[022-161]the Cabinet acquiesced unanimously in that decision, which has been mine

[022-162]from the first.

[022-163]I had a moment's conversation with Peel about the letter to Sir J. Malcolm,

[022-164]and told him I would defend every word of it, elephants and all.

[022-165]Then we had a good deal of discussion respecting the policy to be pursued

[022-166]with regard to Cuba, against which the Mexicans are preparing to organise a

[022-167]slave insurrection, for which purpose they have sent a Minister to Hayti.

[022-168]It seems to be generally believed that Canning, about the year 1823, issued

[022-169]a sort of prohibition to the Mexican and Columbian States to attack Cuba,

[022-170]but no trace can be found in the Foreign Office of any such prohibition.

[022-171]Sir R. Wilson means to ask a question upon the subject to-morrow. He says,

[022-172]if you prohibit the Mexicans and Columbians from attacking Cuba, you should

[022-173]prohibit the Spaniards from attacking them--which is fair--in fact the

[022-174]expedition of Barradas was undertaken before we knew anything about it, and

[022-175]if we had wished we could not have interfered.

[022-176]The question as to what answer should be given to Sir R. Wilson, and what

[022-177]policy pursued, was deferred till to-morrow.
