[051-001]I had prepared a paragraph to be used in case it had been thought right to

[051-002]say anything about India. For my own part I thought it better not. We could

[051-003]not produce a measure this year, and it would hardly be fair by the Court

[051-004]to declare to Parliament that we thought the monopoly must be put an end to

[051-005]without having previously acquainted them with our determination. The Duke

[051-006]said he had seen nothing yet to satisfy him that the revenues of India

[051-007]could meet the expenditure without the China trade. I think his reluctance

[051-008]increases to put an end to the present system. My disposition to terminate

[051-009]the existence of the Company increases the more I see of them.

[051-010]_November 2_

[051-011]House at five. Lord Bute made a very long, heavy speech. Lord Monson a very

[051-012]little one, not bad. The stuff would do; but he has neither stature nor

[051-013]voice.

[051-014]We then had Lord Winchilsea, Lord Camden, Duke of Leinster, and Lord

[051-015]Farnham. Lord Winchilsea right in tone, but desiring inquiry into

[051-016]agricultural distress. This, too, was the burden of a mouthy speech made by

[051-017]the Duke of Richmond, whom I had nearly forgotten. Lord Farnham spoke, as

[051-018]he always does, well. He deprecated the dissolution of the Union, but

[051-019]desired relief for Ireland. This, too, was desired by the Duke of Leinster,

[051-020]who spoke very firmly, as all did, against agitators.

[051-021]Lord Grey said it was a moment of great _danger_ and _importance_.

[051-022]Fortitude, caution, and wisdom were required. He spoke strongly against the

[051-023]dissolution of the Union, and against the disturbers of the public peace

[051-024]everywhere. He used the words of the speech, _grief_ and _indignation_. He

[051-025]joined in the determination to put down sedition by law. Rejoiced no new

[051-026]laws were asked for. Approved of the prompt recognition of King Louis

[051-027]Philippe; lamented the _necessity_ of the French Revolution. Said 'all

[051-028]Revolutions were in themselves evils,' although they might produce eventual

[051-029]good. Expressed his hope, for the honour of France and for the interests of

[051-030]Liberty, that they would not sully a Revolution hitherto unstained by a

[051-031]single act of vengeance. This part of his speech was very well worded and

[051-032]spoken. He objected to the terms in which the passage respecting the

[051-033]Netherlands was worded, as seeming to cast all the blame upon the Belgians,

[051-034]and so to make our mediation less effectual. He likewise objected to the

[051-035]making the Portuguese Amnesty a seeming condition of the recognition of

[051-036]Miguel. Of the recognition itself he did not complain, as he had so long

[051-037]been King _de facto_. These objections were fair.

[051-038]Lord Farnham having suggested the necessity of preparing for war, Lord Grey

[051-039]said the preparation should be by gaining the hearts of our own people--and

[051-040]he advocated, but very temperately, Reform. He did not, however, allow that

[051-041]there was any abstract right to a particular mode of constituting a

[051-042]Legislature. The right of the people was to a _good Government_, and to

[051-043]whatever form of Legislative Assembly might seem best to secure that

[051-044]Government.

[051-045]His speech was good, and temperate, as well as firm. The Duke of Wellington

[051-046]followed him. He declared his intention to oppose Reform. He said we were

[051-047]not bound to interfere for the maintenance of the Amnesty further than by

[051-048]advice and remonstrance, not by war.

[051-049]I should mention that Lord Grey seemed pleased by the abandonment of the

[051-050]droits. He was not very well, and at times was almost unable to proceed.

[051-051]Upon the whole the tone of the debate was very good, and will do good.

[051-052]_November 3._

[051-053]Office at eleven to see Col. Houston.

[051-054]Upon the whole the debate in the Commons was satisfactory. Peel was very

[051-055]much cheered. O'Connell spoke well, and was heard in perfect silence.

[051-056]Brougham made an ordinary speech; theme a bad one, violent.

[051-057]There was much row in the streets yesterday; but all occasioned by attacks

[051-058]upon the police, and attempts to rescue pickpockets. The Guards were called

[051-059]out rather hastily. Colonel Rowan who commands the police has begged they

[051-060]may be left to themselves. They are quite strong enough.

[051-061]Cabinet dinner at Lord Rosslyn's. No House of Commons people there. The

[051-062]Prince of Orange is come. He has written to the King, and is to see him to-

[051-063]morrow. It seems there are 7,500 men in the citadel of Antwerp, which can

[051-064]only hold 2,000, and has provisions only for two months. The forts of Lillo

[051-065]and Liefkenshoek are ill-garrisoned; so is Breda, and so is Bergen-op-zoom.

[051-066]The Dutch have not 4,000 men in the field near Breda. The question is,

[051-067]whether the evacuation of the citadel of Antwerp would not be advisable for

[051-068]the purpose of getting out the 7,500 men. It seems that if Flushing be

[051-069]held, the Scheld is of no use. The Conference respecting Belgian affairs

[051-070]meets to-morrow, Talleyrand being sole representative of France. The first

[051-071]object will be to establish an armistice.

[051-072]After dinner we had some conversation respecting the debate in the Commons

[051-073]of last night. Peel is disgusted at not being supported by the three

[051-074]Cabinet Ministers present, who knew the whole subject which had been so

[051-075]often discussed in Cabinet--yet not one of them rose to answer Brougham.

[051-076]The Duke is very angry with them, and says he shall take an opportunity of

[051-077]advising Peel in their presence never to rise till Brougham has spoken, let

[051-078]others be abused as they may. If the three mutes will not speak, it is

[051-079]clear they will not remain in very long.

[051-080]I consider a debate to be a battle, in which the chief should be able to

[051-081]put every man into the fight, as he would every battalion, with a view to

[051-082]the ultimate object; he himself being the reserve.

[051-083]_November 4._

[051-084]It seems Sir G. Murray did speak last night, but he went further than he

[051-085]intended on Reform, and so rather damaged our position as a Government.

[051-086]Office. Saw Mr. Sullivan. He seems a sensible, liberal man. His evidence

[051-087]would be a death-blow to the government of the Company. He says the cotton

[051-088]of Coimbatore is carried to Tinnevelly and thence to Madras by country

[051-089]boats, where it is taken up by the China ships. It might be sent directly

[051-090]to the sea on the Malabar coast, the distance being 300 miles. There is no

[051-091]obstruction to the cultivation. The country is under a Ryotwar settlement.

[051-092]The unequal demand of the Company is very injurious. Their great demand at

[051-093]some periods encourages cultivation and raises prices exorbitantly--the

[051-094]next year there is no demand at all. They now purchase by contract. The

[051-095]contracts are too large for the native merchants, and fall, as jobs, into

[051-096]the hands of Europeans. Sufficient notice is not given of the contract. The

[051-097]native merchants have from one lac to one and a half.

[051-098]Great injury is sustained by the tobacco monopoly. The Company's officers

[051-099]sell it as retailers. The Government is, as I always thought, practically

[051-100]in the hands of the natives. They require European co-operation, but if

[051-101]they combine against their European superior he can do nothing. House at

[051-102]five. Lord Winchilsea made a violent tirade against the Administration,

[051-103]without any motion before the House. The Duke made a few observations on

[051-104]the point of order very quietly, and we rose.

[051-105]_November 5._

[051-106]St. James's at half-past one. The clergy of the Province of Canterbury were

[051-107]there, with their address on the accession. They were not expected, and

[051-108]there were no gentlemen pensioners. However, they delivered their address

[051-109]to the King on the throne, and a very good address it was. Peel had to

[051-110]write the answer in a hurry.

[051-111]Recorder's report. One man left for execution for a street robbery

[051-112]accompanied with violence.

[051-113]The Recorder gave but a bad account of the disposition of the City. The

[051-114]Chancellor seems a good deal alarmed, and so does Peel. Every precaution is

[051-115]taken, but I cannot help fearing there is a conspiracy of which we know

[051-116]nothing. Aberdeen suspects connection with France.

[051-117]We are to inquire into the circumstances of the fires in Normandy, which

[051-118]seem very much to resemble ours. We have had one near Godstone, and another

[051-119]at Fair-lawn, in Kent; the sufferers unoffending persons. The object seems

[051-120]to be to spread general terror. It is clear that they are effected by the

[051-121]discharge of some chemical preparation, which ignites after a time. No

[051-122]watching has any effect. Fires take place where no one has approached.

[051-123]Goulburn told me he thought Sir G. Murray had said much more than he

[051-124]intended, purely from want of habit of speaking; still he had done much

[051-125]injury.

[051-126]The new French Ministry is formed, and Lafitte is at its head. He pretends

[051-127]to have the same views as the late Ministry; but it is impossible to

[051-128]suppose the French can resist the offer of Belgium. We shall have no war if

[051-129]we can preserve internal peace and the integrity of the Constitution.

[051-130]_November 6._

[051-131]A letter from Hardinge, who seems to think we stand ill, not for want of

[051-132]numbers, but of speakers. Astell told me the Duke's declaration against

[051-133]Reform had injured him in the City.

[051-134]Saw Wortley, and had a long conversation with him respecting the state of

[051-135]the Government. He thinks we cannot go on. The Duke's declaration against

[051-136]Reform has made it impossible for any to join him, and upon the question of

[051-137]Reform it is doubtful if we should have numbers enough.

[051-138]We talked over possible Governments on the supposition that Lord Grey was

[051-139]at the head, and that Peel remained in. In walking away I was overtaken in

[051-140]Downing Street by Lord Graham, who had been waiting to speak to me on the

[051-141]same subject. He seems to think our fall not so immediately necessary as

[051-142]Wortley does. I then called on Hardinge, who had been with the Duke this

[051-143]morning. Hardinge had candidly told the Duke that if he had a minority on

[051-144]Reform, or a small majority, he would advise him to resign; and previously

[051-145]to tell the King in what a situation he stood. If he had a good majority he

[051-146]might perhaps get some to join; but if not, the position of the Government

[051-147]would be as bad in February, or worse, than it was now. The Duke said he

[051-148]thought things might do still. He had a number of young men who depended

[051-149]upon him. He would take care to give the King timely notice. The King had

[051-150]behaved very well to him. Indeed I know the Duke feels very strongly how

[051-151]admirably and how kindly the King has behaved.

[051-152]Lord Maryborough had been to Hardinge to express his fears for the Duke's

[051-153]life, and the Duke has received many letters informing him there is a

[051-154]conspiracy to assassinate him on Tuesday, as he goes to Guildhall.

[051-155]Hardinge said every precaution should be taken, but he begged Lord

[051-156]Maryborough not to tell the Duke his apprehensions. Hardinge, however, has

[051-157]the same; and fears there may be an attempt that day to make London a scene

[051-158]of barricades like Paris and Brussels. Troops will be disposed at intervals

[051-159]in bodies of half battalions, with provisions, and there will be 1,000

[051-160]cavalry. Two guns will be ready with the marines at the obelisk, and two in

[051-161]the park. Hardinge observed to the Duke that he knew he had bolts inside to

[051-162]the doors of the carnage, and added, 'I shall take pocket pistols!' The

[051-163]Duke said, 'Oh! I shall have pistols in the carriage.' Hardinge asked the

[051-164]Duke to take him, which he does. Arbuthnot goes with the Duke, too. I wish

[051-165]I could manage to follow him in my carriage. I shall buy a brace of double-

[051-166]barrelled pocket pistols on Monday. Hardinge showed me his.

[051-167]The Duke has made himself very obnoxious by declaring his resolution to

[051-168]oppose Reform, which in fact, however, he did not do in such terms as has

[051-169]been said.
