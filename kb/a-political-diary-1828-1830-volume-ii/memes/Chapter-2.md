[002-001]Reference is to be made to Ireland to ascertain the feeling about it there.

[002-002]Bankes came at twelve o'clock. He told me he had been with the Duke, and

[002-003]had received from him the offer of his old office. He had asked permission

[002-004]to consult one person, whose name he did not mention to the Duke,--it was

[002-005]the Duke of Cumberland. He had called at the Palace and found the Duke of

[002-006]Cumberland was at Windsor. He wanted to write to him to ask if he had any

[002-007]objection to his taking the office again.

[002-008]Bankes said he had attended none of the meetings at Lord Chandos's. He had

[002-009]avoided as much as he could all communication with the Duke of Cumberland.

[002-010]He had fully determined not to take a part with any new Government which

[002-011]might be formed, unless it should clearly appear the King had been unfairly

[002-012]dealt by, or unless there should be an attempt to make peers to carry the

[002-013]Bill. The Duke of Cumberland had always said that he made him his first

[002-014]object, and he had reason to think that he had mentioned him to the King,

[002-015]and had been instrumental in his appointment. The Duke of Cumberland had

[002-016]desired him to come to him (during the Bill), and had apparently intended

[002-017]to name some particular office for him, but seeing his coldness had only

[002-018]sounded him, and had received the answer I have mentioned above.

[002-019]The Duke of Cumberland had told him it was an understood thing that all

[002-020]were to be restored, and that he saw no reason why he should not take his

[002-021]office again.

[002-022]_This was ten days ago._

[002-023]I told him I advised, if he thought it necessary to write to the Duke of

[002-024]Cumberland at all, that he should merely state his intention to take his

[002-025]office back again, refer to his conversation with the Duke himself upon the

[002-026]point, and add _distinctly_ that, taking office, he could no longer have

[002-027]any communication on political matters with a person who had declared his

[002-028]hostility to the Government.

[002-029]I advised him to send off his own servant on a post-horse at six o'clock

[002-030]to-morrow morning, with a letter to the effect I have stated to the Duke of

[002-031]Cumberland, and whether he received an answer or not, to go to the Duke of

[002-032]Wellington and accept at 12.

[002-033]I advised him to tell the Duke the whole state of the case, and all he had

[002-034]done.

[002-035]The Duke of Wellington did not seem by any means well to-day. He was

[002-036]blooded yesterday.

[002-037]_April 16._

[002-038]Cabinet at 3. It seems Bankes called on the Duke this morning, but he was

[002-039]engaged. I told him all that passed between Bankes and me last night. If

[002-040]Bankes should go out the Duke means to offer his place to Sir J. Graham.

[002-041]We met upon foreign affairs. Aberdeen read his instructions to Gordon, who

[002-042]goes to Constantinople. They are unobjectionable.

[002-043]We then considered what was to be done in consequence of this second

[002-044]violation of their word on the part of the Russians in blockading Candia.

[002-045]Count Heyden has written two letters to Sir Pulteney Malcolm. In the first

[002-046]he justifies the blockade of Candia on the ground of its being necessary to

[002-047]protect the Morea from the Pacha of Egypt; in the second he rests it on the

[002-048]necessity of blockading the two extremities of Candia for the purpose of

[002-049]watching Constantinople.

[002-050]We cannot permit the Russians to make fools of us in this way--to promise

[002-051]one thing as parties to the Treaty of London, and to do another as

[002-052]belligerents.

[002-053]After the Cabinet I asked the Duke whether he still wished me to press

[002-054]Courtney upon the Directors. He said, Yes, he very much wanted his place. I

[002-055]said it had occurred to me that _Herries_ might take the Governorship of

[002-056]Bombay. It did not seem to have occurred to him. He said he thought Herries

[002-057]would not go; but he evidently thought it would be a very good thing if he

[002-058]would.

[002-059]The Duke said he wanted to have the places of Courtney and Sir G. Hill, and

[002-060]to bring in Lord Chandos and M. Fitzgerald. We mentioned Ashley. I

[002-061]suggested Ashley's going to the Treasury, and Sir J. Graham taking his

[002-062]place. This would, I dare say, be done, if we could get the place at the

[002-063]Treasury.

[002-064]I have not as yet heard a surmise as to the new Lord Privy Seal.

[002-065]Lord O'Neil has signed the Duke of Richmond's protest against the Franchise

[002-066]Bill. It is very hostile to the Government, and Lord O'Neil will probably

[002-067]be put out.

[002-068]The Duke of Richmond has been very imprudent. Had he taken a moderate line

[002-069]he probably might have been Privy Seal. His time is now gone by.

[002-070]_April 17._

[002-071]Went by appointment to see Lady Jersey. Found there Duncannon and Lord

[002-072]Sefton. Duncannon talked big about O'Connell's power, and in the same sense

[002-073]in which he talked to Fitzgerald, wishing to induce the Government to let

[002-074]him take his seat. I said we could not. It depended not on us, but upon the

[002-075]law.

[002-076]Lady Sefton came in afterwards for a few minutes, and Lord Rosslyn. Lady

[002-077]Jersey talked a great deal about the restoration, and feared the Whigs

[002-078]would imagine they were never to come in, and would form a violent

[002-079]opposition. She mentioned Mr. Stanley as being much annoyed, he having made

[002-080]a laudatory speech in favour of Peel.

[002-081]I told her it would have been very harsh to have eliminated those who had

[002-082]taken office under the idea that the Government was rather against than for

[002-083]the Catholics, certainly _neutral_, and that it was a little unreasonable

[002-084]to expect others to be turned out to make way for new friends.

[002-085]_April 18._

[002-086]The Duke thinks he could not offer the Privy Seal to Lord Grey, but he

[002-087]would be conciliated by having a friend--that is, Rosslyn--in. If we could

[002-088]get Lord Beresford out, Lord Rosslyn would go to the Ordnance.

[002-089]The Duke says the King would make it a point of honour to resist the

[002-090]introduction of Lord Grey, though in reality he was in communication with

[002-091]Lord Grey in 1820-21, after the Queen's trial, and then intended to bring

[002-092]him in and to turn out the then Ministers for the Milan Commission, he

[002-093]having been himself at the bottom of that Commission. The Duke, the only

[002-094]member of the Cabinet who was not mixed up with the Milan Commission,

[002-095]induced the King to give up his idea of making a change.

[002-096]Bankes received a letter from the Duke of Cumberland, very long, and

[002-097]against his acceptance of office; but he begged Bankes to go down to see

[002-098]him and talk it over. He did so. Bankes told him he would not accept if he

[002-099]on consideration objected, but he was determined not to join any other

[002-100]Government. The Duke of Cumberland spoke of himself as having been ill-used

[002-101]by the Duke of Wellington. This was explained. The conference ended by the

[002-102]Duke of Cumberland's acquiescing entirely in Bankes's acceptance of office.

[002-103]Bankes saw the Duke of Wellington and detailed the whole to him.

[002-104]_April 21._

[002-105]Called on Sir H. Hardinge at Richmond. He told me the Duke had at first

[002-106]great reluctance to have anything to do with the Whigs. By his account he

[002-107]must have principally contributed to lead the Duke to adopt that view which

[002-108]he has now of admitting Rosslyn, &c.

[002-109]_April 22._

[002-110]The Duke of Norfolk called, and, not finding me, left a note begging me to

[002-111]ascertain privately from the Duke of Wellington whether the King would be

[002-112]pleased if the English Catholics presented an address to him thanking him

[002-113]for the Relief Bill.

[002-114]Received a letter from the Duke of Wellington expressing a decided opinion

[002-115]against any address from the Roman Catholics. He says, 'Everything has been

[002-116]done that is possible to efface all distinctions between the King's

[002-117]subjects on the score of religion, and this with a view to the general

[002-118]benefit, and not to that of a particular body. I confess I shall think that

[002-119]this measure has failed in attaining its object if there should be any

[002-120]general act of a particular body.

[002-121]'In respect to the King himself I am certain that the most agreeable thing

[002-122]to him would be that all should remain quiet.

[002-123]'We must have no distinct body of Roman Catholics except in the churches

[002-124]and in affairs of religion. The less we act inconsistently with the

[002-125]principle the better.'

[002-126]I so entirely agree in opinion with the Duke of Wellington that, having for

[002-127]my own amusement written an address for the Roman Catholics in the event of

[002-128]their making any to the King, the first sentence I imagined was this: 'The

[002-129]Roman Catholics of England approach your Majesty for the last time as a

[002-130]body distinct from the rest of your Majesty's subjects.'

[002-131]_April 25._

[002-132]I had a good deal of conversation as to the next Director. There are three

[002-133]city men candidates, but none are good--Lyall, Ellice, and Douglas.

[002-134]Of Ellice no one knows anything. He is brother to the Ellice who married

[002-135]Lord Grey's sister. Lyall is, or was, Chairman of the Committee of

[002-136]Shipowners. Douglas is brother to Lord Queensbury. They say his is not a

[002-137]very good house.

[002-138]_April 28._

[002-139]Read the correspondence between the Duke and Lord Anglesey. Then read a

[002-140]memorandum of the Duke's in reply to one of Hardinge's on the subject of

[002-141]the discipline of the British army. Hardinge wished to introduce the

[002-142]Prussian [Footnote: Which did not include capital punishment. See

[002-143]_Wellington Correspondence_, vol. v. p. 932.] discipline into ours. The

[002-144]Duke shows that with our discipline we have more men fit for duty in

[002-145]proportion to our numbers than the Prussians in the proportion of two to

[002-146]one. That in Prussia the army is everything. There is no other profession.

[002-147]All are soldiers--the officer lives much with his men--they are always in

[002-148]masses, always in fertile countries.

[002-149]In our service the worst men in the community enter the army. The officers

[002-150]are gentlemen. They cannot mix with the men. Without discipline our army

[002-151]would be inferior to others. It is not even now the favourite profession.

[002-152]There is much jealousy of it. It is not popular with the common people. It

[002-153]is difficult to find recruits even in times of distress.

[002-154]I was in an army, the Duke concludes, which cannot be governed on the

[002-155]Prussian principle. You cannot treat the English soldier as a man of

[002-156]honour.

[002-157]The Duke had been with the King, who was in very good humour. He had not,

[002-158]however, got to close quarters with him as to the changes.

[002-159]_April 29._

[002-160]Cabinet at 12. A letter has been received from Lord Heytesbury, from which

[002-161]it is clear that Russia will very soon resume altogether the exercise of

[002-162]her belligerent rights in the Mediterranean.

[002-163]Nesselrode communicated to him the blockade of Candia. Lord Heytesbury only

[002-164]observed that 'it was a resumption of belligerent rights.' This Count

[002-165]Nesselrode did not deny, and he said they could not long remain in the

[002-166]false position in which they now were in the Mediterranean.

[002-167]Count Heyden at the end of January blockaded Candia on pretexts arising out

[002-168]of the state of Greece. In three weeks from that time he rested his

[002-169]interception of the Egyptian vessels near Candia on the necessary exercise

[002-170]of his rights as a belligerent. Lièven, when first spoken to, disavowed

[002-171]Heyden. He now changes his tone, and it is evident that Russia now for the

[002-172]second time breaks her word. The French do not behave much better. They

[002-173]have 6,000 men in the Morea, and mean to keep them there notwithstanding

[002-174]their engagement to withdraw their troops as soon as the Egyptians were

[002-175]embarked. To be sure, they say if we insist upon it they will withdraw

[002-176]them.
