[006-001]We had some conversation as to the charter. The Duke seems rather inclined

[006-002]to continue the _name_ of the Company. I am for the _name_ of the King.

[006-003]_June 11._

[006-004]The world has had imposed upon it a story of the Chancellor's _selling_ his

[006-005]Church preferment. The 'Age' is to bring forward its charges on Sunday

[006-006]next. This is an arrow from the Cumberland quiver.

[006-007]I mentioned Lord Clare's wish to look forward to the Government of Bombay

[006-008]or Madras to the Duke last night, and he did not by any means receive the

[006-009]proposition unfavourably. I told Clare so to-day.

[006-010]_June 13._

[006-011]Gaisford has refused the Bishopric of Oxford--wisely, for he was only a

[006-012]Grecian and had good preferment. He is a rough man too. I am glad he has

[006-013]refused it. I do not think mere Grecians good bishops.

[006-014]Lord Clare told me Glengall was to be the new Irish peer.

[006-015]_June 15._

[006-016]Committee as usual. Lord Londonderry more insane than ever. The Duke said

[006-017]he had never seen anything more painful.

[006-018]We made hardly any progress. The victory will belong to the _survivors_,

[006-019]and I do not think Lord Durham will be one of them.

[006-020]House. Lord Londonderry made a foolish speech, and the Duke an excellent

[006-021]one, very severe upon him, and defending the City. If we do not get the

[006-022]City by this Committee the City is impregnable.

[006-023]Hardinge told me Lord Grey seemed out of humour. I do not think he is in

[006-024]good humour.

[006-025]_June 16._

[006-026]At last some hope of a compromise respecting London Bridge.

[006-027]_June 17._

[006-028]The eternal Committee is, I trust, at an end. The agents have come to a

[006-029]compromise, and if the Common Council should confirm the terms, as I

[006-030]conclude they will, the thing will be at an end. We shall then have

[006-031]Parliament up by Monday or Tuesday next.

[006-032]Cabinet dinner at Lord Melville's. The Duke was astonished at Lord W.

[006-033]Bentinck's strong and sudden step of transferring the Supreme Government

[006-034]_pro tempore_ to Meerut. He said he always expected some wild measure from

[006-035]Lord W. Meerut was in too exposed a situation.

[006-036]Twenty thousand Afghan horse might ride in upon the seat of government if

[006-037]placed in the north-west provinces. It is astonishing how much the Duke is

[006-038]prejudiced by his old Indian feelings. Whatever _is_ he thinks best. Meerut

[006-039]is ill and absurdly chosen, but Calcutta is certainly the worst chosen seat

[006-040]of government.

[006-041]We are to have a Cabinet on Saturday for the King's speech. On Monday or

[006-042]Tuesday Parliament will be up. On Wednesday we dine at the India House, and

[006-043]on the Monday following, the 29th, will be the fish dinner.

[006-044]_June 18._

[006-045]Called to compliment the Duke on the anniversary of Waterloo. Left with him

[006-046]Lord W. Bentinck's minute and despatch on transferring the Supreme

[006-047]Government Departments and all _pro tempore_ to Meerut, and a proposed

[006-048]letter, censuring the Governor for having done this without previous

[006-049]sanction, and directing the members of Council and the Departments to

[006-050]return.

[006-051]The Duke objects to any removal of the seat of government to the upper

[006-052]provinces. It would there be exposed to the sudden inroads of cavalry. In

[006-053]India a cloud of cavalry rises like a squall in the Mediterranean. At

[006-054]Calcutta the Government, protected by the rivers, is safe, and always

[006-055]accessible from England.

[006-056]_June 19._

[006-057]Rode to town. Met Rosslyn. He told me Lord Clanrickarde [Footnote: Lord

[006-058]Clanrickarde was son-in-law of Mr. Canning.] intended to make some

[006-059]observations on foreign policy this evening.

[006-060]Had some conversation with the Duke. He doubted whether the Supreme

[006-061]Government _could_ leave Calcutta and preserve its powers. I told him of

[006-062]the newspaper report of to-day that leases for sixty years were to be given

[006-063]to indigo planters, and this without any authority from home. He seems to

[006-064]have suspected from the first that Lord W. would do some monstrous thing,

[006-065]and certainly he does seem to be emancipating himself.

[006-066]House. Lord Clanrickarde made his little speech. Aberdeen his. Then Lord

[006-067]Holland, and then the Duke. Afterwards Goderich. Lord Holland talked as

[006-068]usual very vaguely. No notice had been given, and few people knew there was

[006-069]anything to be done. So ends the House for this year.

[006-070]_June 20, 1829._

[006-071]Cabinet. King's speech. Some time occupied in wording it, but no material

[006-072]alterations. Aberdeen's the worst part. The King is made to _auspicate_ and

[006-073]to pray, but not to trust that the Franchise Bill and the Relief Bill will

[006-074]be productive of good.

[006-075]The Chancellor has prosecuted the 'Morning Journal' for a libel accusing

[006-076]him of having taken money for Sugden's appointment as Solicitor-General. I

[006-077]heard him tell Lord Bathurst, with reference to another calumny against

[006-078]him, that he had fortunately preserved through his secretary the grounds on

[006-079]which he had given every living he had disposed of.

[006-080]_June 21._

[006-081]Had a visit from Loch. He wishes the despatch to Lord William to be worded

[006-082]more gently, as he thinks Lord William _meant_ well. This shall be done.

[006-083]_June 22._

[006-084]Wrote draft paragraphs to the effect above stated to Lord W. Bentinck, and

[006-085]added a paragraph giving the Duke's reasoning against the removal of the

[006-086]Government from Calcutta to the north-west provinces.

[006-087]I had some conversation in the House with Lord Lauderdale on China trade,

[006-088]&c. He seems friendly to the Company and to the Government.

[006-089]Went to the House at 4. Found a good many peers there. By mere mistake a

[006-090]Bill, slightly and necessarily amended by the Lords, was not sent down to

[006-091]the Commons, although directions to that effect were given, and it by

[006-092]accident was placed amongst the Bills ready for the Royal assent. So it

[006-093]received the Royal assent. It became necessary to pass a Bill to make this

[006-094]Bill valid in law. Lord Shaftesbury thought our House ought to inform the

[006-095]Commons we had discovered the error; but the Speaker, [Footnote: C. Manners

[006-096]Sutton, afterwards Lord Canterbury.] to make a flourish, insisted on

[006-097]announcing it first to the House of Commons. All the steps to be taken were

[006-098]settled between the Speaker, Lord Shaftesbury, and Courtenay. When I went

[006-099]down I found it had not been settled that anything should be done first by

[006-100]us. I suggested that Lord Shaftesbury should acquaint the House with the

[006-101]circumstance, and that we should appoint a Committee to inquire before the

[006-102]message from the Commons came up. This was done.

[006-103]We ordered a message to be sent, but before our messengers left the House

[006-104]we heard the Commons would not receive a message, so I moved that the order

[006-105]we had just made should be rescinded, and we had a second conference. The

[006-106]Commons were well satisfied with our reply. The last sentence had been,

[006-107]'The Lords hope the Commons will be satisfied with this explanation.' As we

[006-108]in the first paragraph expressed our desire to preserve a good

[006-109]understanding between the two Houses, and in the second one regret that

[006-110]this mistake had taken place, I thought it was going too far to express _a

[006-111]hope_ only that our explanation would be satisfactory.

[006-112]We inserted 'the Lords _doubt not_,' instead of 'the Lords _hope_.'

[006-113]At night received a letter from the Duke of Wellington, saying he thought

[006-114]we might get Courtenay to resign at once and get in Lord Chandos. I am to

[006-115]see him at ten to-morrow on the subject.

[006-116]_June 23, 1829._

[006-117]Wrote early to the Chairs and begged them to come to me immediately. Sent

[006-118]Loch the Duke's note and told him why Lord Chandos's being brought in was

[006-119]of so much importance. Saw the Duke at 10. The King was very much out of

[006-120]humour yesterday. He wanted to make Nash a baronet. The Duke refused. The

[006-121]King then went upon his Speech, which he did not like and had altered. He

[006-122]left out the specific mention of the Relief and Franchise Bill, and there

[006-123]he was right, and he converted the prayer that the measure might

[006-124]tranquillise Ireland, &c., into a _hope_ that it would--thus making it a

[006-125]little stronger, but that he did not know.

[006-126]The Duke of Cumberland, on hearing of Castlereagh's appointment, said,

[006-127]'Whoever ratted he would not,' alluding to Lord Londonderry, who has been

[006-128]nibbling at the Cumberland faction. However, Lord Londonderry is much

[006-129]annoyed at Castlereagh's taking office. He neither likes the expense of an

[006-130]election for Downshire, nor losing a vote he thought he could dispose of.

[006-131]Hardinge will not sit again for Durham. Without Hardinge Lord Londonderry

[006-132]will have trouble enough there.

[006-133]The King was much out of humour during the Chapter of the Garter, and said

[006-134]everything was done wrong.

[006-135]Saw the Chairs. They had just got a letter from Sir John Malcolm, resigning

[006-136]from December 1, 1830. This would have been in any case a long time for

[006-137]Courtenay to wait out of office; but they said the idea of his being

[006-138]proposed had got wind, and several of the Directors were very adverse.

[006-139]Neither of the Chairs likes him, and if they supported him they would do it

[006-140]very reluctantly. As Loch goes out of office in April, and we cannot tell

[006-141]who will be deputy, and six new Directors come in, there really are not the

[006-142]means of saying to Courtenay, 'You are sure of your election,' and without

[006-143]this he could not be asked to resign.

[006-144]I took the Chairs to the Duke. He received them very cordially, told them I

[006-145]had stated the circumstances to him, and he gave up the point.

[006-146]We then talked of the legality of the removal of the Supreme Government

[006-147]from Calcutta. On looking into the acts it seems very doubtful whether any

[006-148]act done by the Governor-General in Council away from Calcutta would be

[006-149]valid unless it were one of the acts the Governor-General might do of his

[006-150]own authority. For instance, 'a regulation' issued by the Governor-General

[006-151]in Council at Meerut would not be valid, because the Governor-General alone

[006-152]could not issue one.

[006-153]The Duke said Lord William did everything with the best intentions; but he

[006-154]was a _wrong-headed man_, and if he went wrong he would continue in the

[006-155]wrong line. Other men might go wrong and find it out, and go back; but if

[006-156]he went wrong he would either not find it out, or, if he did, he would not

[006-157]go back.

[006-158]_June 24._

[006-159]Sat as Commissioner to prorogue Parliament. The King's alteration in the

[006-160]Speech certainly made it better and stronger. He now expresses his _sincere

[006-161]hope_ the measures of the session will produce tranquillity, &c. People

[006-162]thought the Speech rather short and jejune.

[006-163]Dined at the 'Albion' with the Directors. The dinner was given to Lord

[006-164]Dalhousie. There were there the Duke, the Chancellor, Peel, Sir J. Murray,

[006-165]Lord Rosslyn and Goulburn, the Speaker, the Attorney General, Courtenay,

[006-166]Ashley, and Bankes; Duke of Buccleuch, Lord Camden, Lord Montagu, Lord

[006-167]Hill, Sir Herbert Taylor, Sir Byam Martin, Sir A. Dickson, Colonel Houston,

[006-168]Lord Dalhousie, and Sir Sidney Beckwith, and their aides-de-camp; a great

[006-169]many Directors, and in all rather more than 100 people.

[006-170]The Duke, in returning thanks, spoke of the cordiality and good

[006-171]understanding existing between the Directors and the Government, _which was

[006-172]never more necessary to the Company than now_.

[006-173]I said the good understanding would always exist while such men as Loch

[006-174]were in the chair, and while I was at the Board of Control. I paid a high

[006-175]compliment to Loch, and then congratulated them on the appointments of the

[006-176]two Generals. Their mildness of manner, their benevolence of character, and

[006-177]the goodness of their natures would obtain for them the affectionate

[006-178]devotion of a grateful soldiery, and, educated in a school of continued

[006-179]victories, they were the fittest leaders of an army which had never met an

[006-180]enemy it had not subdued. I ended by saying I was sure they would devote

[006-181]themselves to the maintenance under all circumstances, not only of the

[006-182]efficiency, but of an object which they would pursue with equal interest--

[006-183]_of the happiness and well-being of the native army of India_. I spoke

[006-184]rather well, was attentively heard, and well received. I sat by the Duke of

[006-185]Buccleuch. We had a good deal of conversation. He seems a fine young man.

[006-186]Lord Rosslyn complained he could never see a draft till it was a month old,

[006-187]and that there had been no new despatches placed in the boxes since he came

[006-188]into office. I told him no one complained more of the same thing than

[006-189]Aberdeen did when Dudley was in office, and I believe all Foreign

[006-190]Secretaries had a shyness about showing their drafts till they were sent

[006-191]off and unalterable.
