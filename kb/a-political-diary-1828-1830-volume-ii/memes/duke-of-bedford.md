**John Russell, 6th Duke of Bedford** (1766–1839) was a British Whig politician and peer. He was the father of Lord John Russell, the future Prime Minister and architect of the [[Reform Bill]], and was himself a supporter of parliamentary reform.

== Political Involvement ==

The diary records the Duke of Bedford's involvement in key political moments. At the swearing-in of new peers, Bedford was noted among those present alongside [[Sir S. Canning]] and Sir J. Gregory. (035-036) This placed him within the Whig social-political network that was gaining influence as Wellington's ministry weakened.

Bedford's political instincts were noted as perceptive. The diary observes, "as I knew they would be, the Duke of Bedford and..." (036-066), suggesting the diarist respected Bedford's political judgment.

== Wellington Ministry and Reform ==

When the decision was made to approach certain figures for government service, "the Duke of Bedford was much..." involved in the discussions, indicating his centrality to Whig political calculations. (038-019) As the father of Lord John Russell, Bedford's household was a centre of Whig political thought and reform advocacy.

Bedford's proxies in the [[House of Lords]] included [[Lord Lauderdale]], another prominent Whig peer. (026-088) This proxy arrangement was part of the Whig strategy to maintain voting strength in the upper house.

== The Russell Connection ==

The Duke of Bedford's most significant political legacy was his son, Lord John Russell, who would become one of the leading figures in the [[Whig Party]] and twice serve as Prime Minister. The Russell family's commitment to parliamentary reform shaped British politics throughout the 1830s.
