[046-001]The Consul at Ostend reports that the King's troops evacuated Brussels on

[046-002]Sunday night; that reinforcements from the country were pouring into

[046-003]Brussels; that there had been an attempt at insurrection at Ostend, which

[046-004]was put down for the time by the Governor, who killed two and wounded six;

[046-005]that eleven or twelve men had marched in from Bruges, which was in

[046-006]possession of the Bourgeois; that Ghent was expected to rise, and in a few

[046-007]days all Belgium would be separated from the King.

[046-008]A son of Holmes of the Treasury arrived at the Foreign Office at four, and

[046-009]said he had left Ostend at three yesterday, when there was a report that

[046-010]the Dutch had made another attack and had recaptured the park.

[046-011]It seems they never had more than the park. They had to take, and did take,

[046-012]the Rue Royale. They were more thoroughly masters of the Place Royale. They

[046-013]planted guns against the town, which were answered by guns from the rebels.

[046-014]At five on Sunday the latter were gradually advancing, and picking off the

[046-015]troops in the park.

[046-016]The first day some rockets were fired and eighteen houses burnt; but Prince

[046-017]Frederick ordered the discontinuance of this, the only efficacious mode of

[046-018]attack.

[046-019]Lord Blantyre was killed. He was lame and on a sofa, but curiosity led him

[046-020]to crawl to the window and peep out, when a ball struck him in the

[046-021]forehead. Lady Blantyre and his children were with him. He was much

[046-022]esteemed. He was in the Peninsula, and a gallant officer.

[046-023]I think the employment of European officers in civil situations under

[046-024]native princes may be very useful to their subjects; and while we do not

[046-025]ourselves employ natives in high situations, to force all native princes to

[046-026]employ them is to make a striking contrast between their Government and

[046-027]ours, very injurious to ours.

[046-028]Jones seemed to hesitate and to think I committed myself. However, I feel

[046-029]sure of my ground.

[046-030]A letter from Lord Cleveland, expressing a wish to have the Vicarage of

[046-031]Ilchester, and offering an equivalent living in Shropshire, or Cheshire.

[046-032]I sent his letter to the Bishop of Bath and Wells, saying I should be much

[046-033]obliged to him if he could make the arrangement, Lord Cleveland being a

[046-034]faithful and powerful supporter of Government.

[046-035]Told Lord Cleveland I had transmitted his letter with a strong

[046-036]recommendation.

[046-037]I made my letter as agreeable to the Bishop as I could, but I dare say he

[046-038]will refuse. Very likely he has given away the vicarage. I told Lord

[046-039]Cleveland I thought it probable.

[046-040]_September 30._

[046-041]The Consul at Antwerp writes a long foolish letter in much alarm.

[046-042]Mr. Cartwright's reports are come. He describes a horrible carnage. The

[046-043]events much as we know them. Sir A. Bagot says his Russian colleague has,

[046-044]with the consent of the King and the Dutch Ministers, written home to say

[046-045]Belgium can only be preserved by foreign aid.

[046-046]At dinner at the Duke of Wellington's met Talleyrand and Vaudreuil. The

[046-047]others there were Aberdeen, Goulburn, Herries, Murray, Beresford, Lord F.

[046-048]Somerset, and Rosslyn.

[046-049]Talleyrand is not altered since 1815, except that he speaks thick. He has

[046-050]not even changed his hairdresser or his tailor.

[046-051]Lord Rosslyn showed me a letter from Lady Janet, who was in Brussels during

[046-052]the fight. She walked about frequently, and was treated with civility by

[046-053]the armed burghers. A few grape-shot fell into the courtyard, and she

[046-054]picked up one. She was at the Hotel de Brabant in the Rue Neuve. There was

[046-055]no pillage, nor any riot. The loss of the people was great. She left the

[046-056]town on Sunday (I think) with a passport from Count Hoogwoorst, and got

[046-057]round to Antwerp.

[046-058]The troops are said to have lost only 600 men. Prince Frederick is about

[046-059]two leagues from Brussels, on the road to Louvain, waiting for heavy guns.

[046-060]This is the report. I suspect he will retreat altogether.

[046-061]_October 1._

[046-062]On consideration thought it would be better to have a secret letter on the

[046-063]press, authorising the Government to allow their servants to be connected

[046-064]with the press. To this letter I thought it advisable to add an exhortation

[046-065]to redoubled zeal on the part of the Company's servants on account of the

[046-066]unsettled state in which the minds of men must be until it was decided

[046-067]under what form the future Government of India should be administered, and

[046-068]I directed the Government to make all thoroughly understand that no

[046-069]possible change could effect the public debt, or the rights of the natives

[046-070]or the just expectations of the European servants. My reason for thinking

[046-071]the officers of Government should be permitted to be concerned in the press

[046-072]is this, that if none but those who are unconnected with the Government,

[046-073]and who, according to the existing system, cannot be connected with it,

[046-074]manage the press, the probability is that everything will be said against

[046-075]the Government and nothing for it.

[046-076]I showed the proposed letter to the Duke. He thought it would be better to

[046-077]pay people for writing than to employ the Company's servants, and that the

[046-078]concluding paragraphs would lead the Government to suppose it was quite

[046-079]decided that the Company should be put an end to. It is wonderful the sort

[046-080]of prejudice he has in favour of the Company. He thinks that unless

[046-081]Directors selected writers and cadets we should have an inferior sort of

[046-082]people in India. I have no objection to the patronage being in a corporate

[046-083]body, but I am satisfied the present system leads to a degree of delay

[046-084]which is more mischievous than misdirection. He acknowledges, however, that

[046-085]the service is much changed. The exhibition made by Courtenay Smith has

[046-086]produced a strong impression upon his mind. He has done more injury to the

[046-087]Company in his mind than all the evidence. He still seems unwilling to make

[046-088]his opinion up against the continuance of the monopoly. It must fall,

[046-089]however.

[046-090]The King wishes to have Sir E. Barnes appointed provisional successor to

[046-091]Lord Dalhousie. The Duke thinks him a better man than Sir R. O'Callaghan,

[046-092]who was suggested by Lord F. Somerset. I suggested that it would be

[046-093]expedient to unite the influence of Governor-General with that of

[046-094]Commander-in-Chief, and make Lord William Bentinck provisional successor.

[046-095]The Duke seemed to think Lord William could not execute both duties, and

[046-096]that it was better to adhere to the general usage of separating the two

[046-097]offices. It seems that after Lord Hastings' return the Court intimated a

[046-098]disposition to separate the offices in future. I can do nothing against the

[046-099]King, the Duke, and the Horse Guards; but I am satisfied it would have been

[046-100]better to send Sir E. Barnes as second in command to the Governor-General.

[046-101]The King (Lord F. Somerset told me) was desirous of doing away with the

[046-102]Company's European regiments. He could not do a better thing. He has

[046-103]likewise some notion of bringing the army under himself. The Duke thinks it

[046-104]must be a _local army,_ and certainly it must. [Footnote: In accordance

[046-105]with this view Lord Ellenborough opposed the eventual amalgamation of the

[046-106]Queen's and the Indian army.] I believe it is better to make it an army of

[046-107]three Presidencies, not one army. My doubt is whether it would not be

[046-108]advisable to allow exchanges from the King's army to the Company's.

[046-109]Everything would be beneficial that raised the tone of the Indian army.

[046-110]The Duke showed me a draft letter he had written for Aberdeen to Lord

[046-111]Stuart, informing the French Government that the King of the Netherlands

[046-112]had required the assistance of his allies to re-establish his authority in

[046-113]Belgium. That it was as much the interest of France as of other Powers to

[046-114]put down a revolution not carried on by the higher or the middle, but by

[046-115]the lowest classes of the people. That we were desirous of concerting with

[046-116]France, as one of the contracting parties to the Treaty of Vienna, what

[046-117]course should be now adopted. It could not be supposed the Allies would

[046-118]forego the advantage of the union of Belgium and Holland for which they had

[046-119]sacrificed so much.

[046-120]This was the substance of the letter. It will not be sent without the

[046-121]concurrence of the Cabinet, which will be summoned the moment Peel comes to

[046-122]town, and he is hourly expected.

[046-123]I think this letter prudent, inasmuch as whatever may happen it will place

[046-124]us in the right; but I do not expect that France will do anything against

[046-125]the rebels, or sanction the doing of anything.

[046-126]The Duke considers, as indeed is clear enough, that it is idle to expect

[046-127]the future submission of Belgium to the King of the Netherlands. It may be

[046-128]possible to place it under a Prince of the House of Nassau. I do not think

[046-129]the Duke sees his way; but he expects war.

[046-130]_October 2._

[046-131]Cabinet. Aberdeen's letter to Lord Stuart. It is founded upon the Duke's

[046-132]memorandum, but much extended _à l'Indienne_. I think none approved of it

[046-133]but Lord Bathurst. I objected to the statement that the treaty of 1815

[046-134]imposed upon us _obligations_. It may give us _rights_, but it imposes no

[046-135]obligation. Then the principle of non-interference is advanced as just and

[046-136]wise, but there are peculiar circumstances attending the position of the

[046-137]Netherlands which make a difference.

[046-138]There is an assertion that the troubles in Belgium have been fomented by

[046-139]French agency, although not assisted by the Government, and a direct

[046-140]reference is made to the Barrier Treaties. France is requested to concert

[046-141]with us and the Allies to _suppress_ the anarchy which exists in the Low

[046-142]Countries. She is at the same time reminded that in no case can the Allies

[046-143]consent to renounce the security given to them by the Treaty of Paris in

[046-144]consequence of an insurrection amongst the lower orders at Brussels. Of

[046-145]this a great deal will be left out. Peel seemed to be rather averse to the

[046-146]whole tenor of the letter, which looks like an invitation to put down the

[046-147]insurrection by force. He sketched in a few words a letter which would be

[046-148]innocuous.

[046-149]The Duke's object is to make an effort to induce France to act with us to

[046-150]settle the Belgian affairs amicably. They cannot be settled _without_

[046-151]France, without a war. But is there any hope that the French Government

[046-152]will venture to give us her _appui_? If they be self-denying enough to

[046-153]renounce the hopes of annexing Belgium to France, their fears of the

[046-154]Jacobins will not allow them to do so. My expectation is that they will say

[046-155]they neither have interfered nor will interfere to dissolve the union

[046-156]between Holland and Belgium. That they will not interfere in the internal

[046-157]concerns of other States.

[046-158]Some think they will go farther and declare they will not allow other

[046-159]_Powers_ to do so. I do not expect this.

[046-160]Every word of this letter must be well weighed, for every discontented man

[046-161]in England and in France will criticise its words and its spirit. There is

[046-162]no writer more unsafe than Aberdeen.

[046-163]Rosslyn did not seem to like the letter at all, but he said little. I

[046-164]whispered to Peel that I wished he would bring a letter to-morrow. _Short_.

[046-165]It was at last agreed alterations should be made, and we are to meet at one

[046-166]to-morrow.

[046-167]Peel takes the letter home, and will, I trust, cut it down.

[046-168]The King Charles X. is in danger of being arrested, of which he naturally

[046-169]has a great horror, and he desires to be allowed to go to Holyrood House,

[046-170]where he would be safe. At Lulworth they are afraid of the Due de Bordeaux

[046-171]being kidnapped. The pretence is the getting masters from Edinburgh for the

[046-172]children.

[046-173]It may be feared that the placing him in a royal residence may look or be

[046-174]represented as looking like recognition. On the other hand his removal from

[046-175]the southern coast to Scotland is a renunciation of intrigues with France.
