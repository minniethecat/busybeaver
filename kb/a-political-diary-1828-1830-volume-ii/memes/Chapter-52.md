[052-001]Hardinge told me there was a proposal to Palmerston and others in the

[052-002]summer, and they at once started the difficulty of Reform, which put an end

[052-003]to the negotiation. If I thought Reform would tranquillise the country I

[052-004]should be quite satisfied with a change of Ministers which would produce

[052-005]internal contentment, but that I do not expect.

[052-006]I shall take care to have records in the office to show the line I was

[052-007]prepared to take on the East Indian Monopoly, and the steps already taken.

[052-008]I shall likewise leave a memorandum upon the alterations I propose in the

[052-009]army.

[052-010]_November 7, Sunday._

[052-011]All the morning occupied with a letter on the Salt question. At half-past

[052-012]two rode to the Cabinet robin. The Cabinet was to meet at three. We did

[052-013]not, however, all assemble till four, the Duke having been with Peel at the

[052-014]Home Office.

[052-015]Before the Duke came we had all been talking of the Lord Mayor's Day, and

[052-016]the manner in which we should go into the City and return, and the

[052-017]precautions taken against riot.

[052-018]The Duke and Peel came together, and it was evident from the first words

[052-019]the Duke spoke that he and Peel had made up their minds to put off the

[052-020]King's visit to the City. The Chancellor seemed almost to take fire at the

[052-021]idea of this, but the Duke very quietly begged him to hear the letters

[052-022]before he decided. The Duke then read various letters he had received, all

[052-023]warning him against going, as there was a plot to assassinate him, and

[052-024]raise a tumult. One of them was from Pearson, a Radical attorney. There was

[052-025]one from a coachmaker, saying he was satisfied, from what his men told him,

[052-026]there was such a design, and offering to come with eighteen of his people

[052-027]and guard the Duke. There was another offer, in a letter not read, to the

[052-028]same effect. There was an examination of a man who serves a Radical

[052-029]printer, and who formerly lived with Cobbett, which showed the intention to

[052-030]exist of attacking the Duke. The impression seemed to be general that the

[052-031]attempt would be made. There was a letter from the Lord Mayor elect

[052-032](Alderman Key) to the Duke, telling him there was an intention amongst

[052-033]disaffected persons to excite tumult and confusion, and to attack him; that

[052-034]he could not be in safety without a guard, and a strong one; and that if an

[052-035]attack was made _in one quarter_ the civil force would not be sufficient.

[052-036]The Duke said he would not go. Peel, who had received many letters

[052-037]informing him of the intention to assassinate him, said if he went he would

[052-038]go privately, and come away privately. He observed that if our force, the

[052-039]disposition of which was mentioned, and was admirable, succeeded in putting

[052-040]down a riot along the line of the procession, he could not answer for the

[052-041]security of life or property in other parts of the town. We had information

[052-042]that the Duke's house would be attacked while he was in the City, and it

[052-043]was to be feared that fires might take place to exercise terror and create

[052-044]a diversion.

[052-045]The feeling in the Duke's mind was that we should not be justified in

[052-046]giving an occasion for the shedding of blood, by means of a crowd of our

[052-047]own making. The consequences of the collision would be incalculable, and

[052-048]might affect all parts of England.

[052-049]The consequences of putting off the King's visit were not lost sight of;

[052-050]the effect it would produce on the Funds, and on public confidence--all

[052-051]that would be said against the Government as weighing down the King by its

[052-052]unpopularity.

[052-053]The letter it was proposed to send was written, and the Duke and Peel went

[052-054]with it to the King at a little before seven.

[052-055]While they were gone the feeling of the Cabinet underwent a change. Lord

[052-056]Bathurst first observed that it would put an end to the Government, and

[052-057]carry Reform. The Chancellor was most unwilling to postpone the King's

[052-058]visit. It would be said we did it for our sakes only, and sacrificed him.

[052-059]Lord Bathurst thought the King would take the advice, but be very angry,

[052-060]and get rid of us.

[052-061]There would be a violent storm in Parliament, and the mobs would come to

[052-062]our houses. All these feelings rested upon the supposition that the

[052-063]procession could return without a tumult, but the letter had been written

[052-064]on the supposition _that it could not_; which was the correct one. The Duke

[052-065]and Peel came back and told us the King had thought the advice quite right,

[052-066]and had behaved as well as possible. The tears were in his eyes while the

[052-067]Lord Mayor's letter was read. He said he had already determined in his own

[052-068]mind to bring the Duke and Peel back in his own carriage. The Duke thought

[052-069]the King had rather expected the advice, and that his mind was relieved by

[052-070]it.

[052-071]We knew the Queen was much alarmed; but it had been said that the King

[052-072]would not hear of there being any danger.

[052-073]The account of the King's manner of receiving the advice seemed to

[052-074]tranquillise those who had before been dissatisfied with the resolution

[052-075]which had been come to. We then went to the Home Office, where we found

[052-076]Alderman Thompson, Mr. Oldham (the Chairman of the Entertainment

[052-077]Committee), Lord Hill, Lord F. Somerset, Sir W. Gordon, General Macdonald,

[052-078]and Mr. Phillips. There were two City men I did not know.

[052-079]The Duke told them the course we had determined to adopt. Alderman Thompson

[052-080]said he anticipated the decision--that it could not be announced in more

[052-081]proper terms. There would be disappointment undoubtedly, but he thought

[052-082]people in general would be satisfied with the reasons. He was almost in

[052-083]tears, and indeed all were much affected--the _cause_ of the measure being

[052-084]the apprehended danger to the Duke.

[052-085]Just as the letter was going off Alderman Thompson observed that although

[052-086]he had no doubt the letter from the Lord Mayor elect was written by his

[052-087]authority, as it was in a handwriting in which a letter had been received

[052-088]from him by the Entertainment Committee, yet it was not in the Lord Mayor

[052-089]elect's handwriting, nor was it dated or signed by him as the other letter

[052-090]was. It was immediately determined that it must be ascertained whether the

[052-091]Lord Mayor elect had authorised the sending of the letter before Peel's

[052-092]letter to the Lord Mayor was delivered.

[052-093]Many began to think there was a hoax, and certainly the forgery of one

[052-094]letter would have thrown suspicion upon all the rest.

[052-095]We were to meet at half-past ten. In going down at half-past ten I called

[052-096]upon Hardinge, who was in his dressing-gown. His servant gone to bed. He

[052-097]did not seem at all surprised.

[052-098]Went on to the Cabinet room. Found every word of the letter was in the

[052-099]Lord Mayor elect's own handwriting.

[052-100]Mr. Phillips, Sir R. Binnie, and Col. Rowan came in, and Lord F. Somerset,

[052-101]and Sir W. Gordon. The artillerymen and marines, of whom there were to have

[052-102]been 500, with two guns, at the Obelisk, are not to be moved up. All the

[052-103]other troops are to remain, and every precaution to be taken, as an attempt

[052-104]to create disturbance may be expected on Tuesday.

[052-105]After we had disposed of this matter we spoke a little of Civil List and

[052-106]Regency. Notice is to be given to-morrow of the two bills, _as if we were

[052-107]still a Government_, but I now think nothing but general alarm can enable

[052-108]us to weather the question of Reform.

[052-109]_November 8._

[052-110]The letter to the Lord Mayor is in the _Times_, and the measure is

[052-111]temperately approved of.

[052-112]At the same time the result of the Conference on the affairs of Belgium is

[052-113]announced--namely, the declaration that there must be an armistice. This

[052-114]will, I trust, give more solid expectations of peace than men have

[052-115]entertained since the King's Speech. The opening of the West India ports to

[052-116]American ships is likewise announced. Both the measures are well-timed.

[052-117]Rode down to the Horse Guards. Overtaken by the Duke, who said he heard

[052-118]that people were delighted with the measure of postponing the King's visit

[052-119]to the City. However, whether they _would say so_ was another thing. He

[052-120]spoke with much feeling of the King's kindness. He said he had behaved as

[052-121]well as possible.

[052-122]Some boys hooted, but in general people took off their hats.

[052-123]Dodd, the coachmaker, told me the people in his neighbourhood were almost

[052-124]all well-disposed. There were very few Radicals. Colonel Jones had told him

[052-125]he could get very few people to attend his meetings, and none who were

[052-126]respectable.

[052-127]Met Hardinge. He considers it to be the end of the Government. We met Lord

[052-128]Hill, who lamented the measure, but concluded it was necessary. Went to the

[052-129]office, where I saw Wortley. He thought it a sad business, and fatal to the

[052-130]Government. He said London had been full of reports yesterday. Wynne was

[052-131]talked of for the India Board.

[052-132]Hardinge's idea (as well as the reports) was that Leach would be

[052-133]Chancellor, and Brougham Master of the Rolls.

[052-134]All the world was much amused by the Chancellor's giving a dinner to Lord

[052-135]Grey, Brougham, Lord Lansdowne, and others. They themselves must have been

[052-136]much amused, and the Chancellor's not getting to dinner till a quarter past

[052-137]eight, and going away at a quarter-past ten, must have satisfied them that

[052-138]something was in the wind.

[052-139]Desired Jones to make out the appointment of Leach's son to a clerkship

[052-140]immediately, and signed it in the course of the evening.

[052-141]House at five. It was very full. Every Whig who is above ground and some

[052-142]who are half under it were present. After an hour of talk about everything

[052-143]but the only thing men were thinking of, the Duke of Richmond outed with it

[052-144]in an offensive manner, and he is the last man who should have done so. The

[052-145]Duke made his explanation very well. Lord Grey afterwards spoke in a very

[052-146]bad temper, with personal civility, however, to the Duke. The Duke replied,

[052-147]which prevented my speaking at all. Lord Grey had spoken 'of measures

[052-148]tending to bring this country into the situation in which France was the

[052-149]time of the late Revolution;' words which should have been taken up, but

[052-150]the Duke's rising after him prevented it.

[052-151]Upon the whole I think the measure is considered right, and people are very

[052-152]glad; indeed, the danger is no longer hanging over their heads. I hear that

[052-153]in the Commons Peel did admirably, and that he was cheered by the whole

[052-154]House when a Colonel Davies _sneered_ at the letter from the Lord Mayor to

[052-155]the Duke. Brougham made as mischievous a speech as he could.

[052-156]The Chancellor gave notice of the Regency Bill for Friday.

[052-157]I do not think our friends see our danger, and they will never forgive us

[052-158]if we go out of office without absolute necessity.

[052-159]_November 9._

[052-160]Looked into the Salt question in the morning. Cabinet at two. There was

[052-161]last night a meeting at the Rotunda; about 2,000 people within, and 3,000

[052-162]or 4,000 without. About half-past ten they dispersed, and from 200 to 600

[052-163]ran down to Westminster, first going to the House, which was up, and then

[052-164]to Downing Street. The police licked them well, and sent them off. They

[052-165]came so quick that a man who headed them, and brought information to the

[052-166]Home Office, where Peel and the Duke were, could not, by hard running, get

[052-167]in advance above a minute, and they had passed the Horse Guards before the

[052-168]Duke, who went there by the back way from the Home Office, had got into the

[052-169]courtyard. He was going out at the door when the porter told him the mob

[052-170]was passing. One man was taken, in whose pocket was found his will, leaving

[052-171]his body to form a rampart against the troops, &c.
