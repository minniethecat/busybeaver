[008-001]He told me Lieutenant-Colonel Skinner was a man of large landed property.

[008-002]He had raised his corps very much from his own estate and neighbourhood,

[008-003]and was a sort of feudal chieftain. He has been educated like a native,

[008-004]though the son of a Colonel in the Company's service.

[008-005]Saw Sir Murray Maxwell. [Footnote: He had commanded the 'Alceste,' which

[008-006]took out Lord Amherst as Ambassador to China twelve years before.] It

[008-007]seemed to me Sir Murray wanted to be sent with a frigate to try to open a

[008-008]commercial communication with Pekin. He thinks even Japan might be induced

[008-009]to trade. The instant the Chinese found the ship was gone and Lord Amherst

[008-010]meant to return by land they would have nothing to say to him. They

[008-011]probably took him for a spy.

[008-012]Sir Murray thinks the Chinese might be led to give a port to the northward.

[008-013]He describes the Spanish population of Manilla as being very small--the

[008-014]native population large. It is but four days' sail, with a good breeze,

[008-015]from Manilla to Canton. Always a favourable wind. The harbour magnificent.

[008-016]I think the whole object of his visit was to get a ship, and a sort of half

[008-017]embassy.

[008-018]_July 10._

[008-019]Received a letter from Lord Clare, who saw the Duke yesterday. He says the

[008-020]Duke was very kind and told him he should get all the information he could

[008-021]before the Committee of next year. I shall most willingly assist him.

[008-022]_July 11._

[008-023]Cabinet. Talked of Ireland. The disposition to outrage seems increasing.

[008-024]The Duke said we were responsible for the success of the measure of this

[008-025]year, and we must put down the armed meetings. Warburton must be ordered to

[008-026]do so. The Duke said emphatically if we do not preserve the peace of

[008-027]Ireland we shall not be a Government. Peel is to write immediately. He

[008-028]thinks the first appearance of a determination to put down these meetings

[008-029]will have the effect of crushing them. We spoke of Poor Laws, Education,

[008-030]and Grand Juries. Lord F. Leveson _despairs_ as to the two first. Upon both

[008-031]the Government will form its opinion. I am glad to see that the more the

[008-032]question of Poor Laws is considered the more the introduction of them

[008-033]appears unadvisable, _or of any approach to them_. I have ever held this

[008-034]opinion.

[008-035]In Cabinet we again, having done so many weeks ago, considered whether any

[008-036]extension of time should be given to the Brazilians for the termination of

[008-037]their traffic in slaves.

[008-038]Aberdeen seemed very indulgently inclined towards the slave dealers--not so

[008-039]Peel and Fitzgerald. They seemed first of all to think it would be an

[008-040]awkward Parliamentary case, and Peel protested against our becoming

[008-041]responsible, as we should, for the horrible consequences which might attend

[008-042]the continuance of the trade for six months. The Chancellor thought a

[008-043]vessel leaving the coast of Africa, that is, engaging in the slave trade,

[008-044]at such a period as would afford a reasonable probability of her arriving

[008-045]on March 13, should be safe. I think February 13 was, after much desultory

[008-046]discussion, fixed as the day after which no vessel should leave the coast

[008-047]of Africa.

[008-048]The Brazilians had offered as an equivalent for six months an agreement

[008-049]that in future vessels fitted for the slave trade, even if they had no

[008-050]slaves on board, should be seizable. It seemed to be the opinion, a little

[008-051]exaggerated, I think, that no prospect of future prevention of slave-

[008-052]trading could justify us in permitting for an instant the immediate benefit

[008-053]we had within our grasp.

[008-054]_July 12._

[008-055]The great day in Ireland; but I hope its happening on Sunday may break its

[008-056]effect. The orders for vigorous interposition, determined upon on Saturday,

[008-057]will have been of no use in preventing collision to-day, or even to-morrow,

[008-058]should the anniversary be postponed.

[008-059]The Duke of Cumberland goes to Hanover, but he returns in October, and old

[008-060]Eldon meets him then in London. They had a regular Cabinet to decide

[008-061]whether he should go or not.

[008-062]Read the court-martial of Lieutenant Lewis, of the Bombay Artillery, who

[008-063]struck an officer in the presence of his wife. The Chairs wish to restore

[008-064]him. It is impossible. There is an end of all moral and gentlemanlike

[008-065]feeling if it be not understood that a man's person is sacred in the

[008-066]presence of his wife. We presume a wife to have feeling, and a man to

[008-067]respect it. _The blow_ alone would have been a good cause of dismissal.

[008-068]Had a letter from the Bishop of Calcutta, who, on offering to execute

[008-069]episcopal functions at the Cape, was told by Hay, of the Colonial Office,

[008-070]that the cape was not in his Patent, and he could no do so. This is a

[008-071]mistake. He can exercise episcopal functions, but not episcopal

[008-072]jurisdictions.

[008-073]Had a letter from Mr. Joliffe, of Merstham, [Footnote: The seat of the

[008-074]Joliffe family, near Reigate, in Surrey.] proposing steam-boat navigation

[008-075]to India. An application from Salisbury for a letter of recommendation to

[008-076]Lord W. Bentinck, in favour of Mr. Chester. Told him this was not a good

[008-077]time to ask a favour of Lord William, and it would be better to send the

[008-078]recommendation with the man, who does not sail till October.

[008-079]_July 15, 1829._

[008-080]Office. Found a letter from Loch, suggesting the irregularity of my sending

[008-081]for his officers, and communicating with them on the subject of despatches

[008-082]to be sent to the Indian Government, and expressing a hope that nothing

[008-083]would occur to interrupt the harmony which existed between us.

[008-084]I said in reply that I have expressed a wish to see Colonel Salmond, and

[008-085]afterwards to see Colonel Farant, merely from my desire to expedite

[008-086]business, and to do it well. That it was mentioned in conversation with

[008-087]Colonel Salmond and Mr. Wilson on Monday, that there was no irregularity in

[008-088]that course, and that I immediately determined to desist from it. That I

[008-089]believed I had so expressed myself at the time to Colonel Salmond.

[008-090]I added that I could assure him I would not willingly, by endeavouring to

[008-091]extend the limits assigned by Parliament to the power of the Board, or by

[008-092]my manner of exercising that power, interrupt the harmony which so happily

[008-093]existed between the court and me.

[008-094]Went to the Foreign Office. I fear the defeat of the Turks near Shumla was

[008-095]decisive; but still we have only Russian accounts, and they do _so lie_! It

[008-096]seems certain the Russians took the opportunity of opening a negotiation.

[008-097]The carelessness of the Turks in not keeping a good look-out towards

[008-098]Silistria seems unaccountable, and they dawdled sadly before Pravady. The

[008-099]new Vizier is very inferior to old Hussein Pacha, whose caution would have

[008-100]avoided this catastrophe.

[008-101]Dined with the East India volunteers. The officers of the regiment are all

[008-102]clerks in the Company's service. The non-commissioned officers and privates

[008-103]serve in their warehouses.

[008-104]There are now 600 men. During the war they had three regiments, each 800

[008-105]strong--all their own servants.

[008-106]When my health was drunk I spoke of the Duke of Wellington's natural

[008-107]fondness for India, of the high terms in which he always mentioned the

[008-108]gallantry of the Indian army, and the purity of the Civil Service. I said

[008-109]the Ministers were animated by his example, &c.

[008-110]The Speaker told me he thought Mr. Stanley [Footnote: A curious instance of

[008-111]the failure of political prophecies, even by men of judgment and

[008-112]experience. Seventeen years later he was leader of a party, and twenty-

[008-113]three years afterwards Prime Minister.] would never rise higher than he was

[008-114]now. It had been a curious Session--all men endeavouring to avoid

[008-115]committing themselves.

[008-116]_July 16._

[008-117]Loch showed me two letters of Sir J. Malcolm, in which he deprecates the

[008-118]sending of more writers, and says _numbers_ may be diminished, but not

[008-119]_salaries_, especially in the higher ranks; and if writers are sent they

[008-120]must be provided for. I believe he is right. I had already suggested the

[008-121]non-appointment of writers this year, and the Chairs seemed to acquiesce--

[008-122]indeed, to have thought of it themselves.

[008-123]Recorder's Report. Before the report Madame de Cayla, the Duchess D'Escars,

[008-124]&c., were presented to the King. I had some conversation with Rosslyn and

[008-125]Herries as to the Indian Question. Herries seemed to be afraid of the House

[008-126]of Commons. Rosslyn a little, too, of public opinion as to the opening of

[008-127]the China trade. They both seemed rather hostile to the continuance of the

[008-128]present system. I said I considered it to be a settled point that the

[008-129]patronage of India should be separated from the Government. The necessity

[008-130]of making that separation led to one great difficulty. The necessity of

[008-131]remitting home in goods 3,200,000£ led to another difficulty, and to making

[008-132]the Government of India, wherever it might be placed, _mercantile_. The

[008-133]East India Company would not, and could not, without the monopoly carry on

[008-134]the concern.

[008-135]Neither Herries nor Rosslyn seemed to admit the necessary separation of the

[008-136]patronage of India from the Government.

[008-137]I said that, if it might not be separated, it would be easy to make a

[008-138]better and a cheaper government. I can see that Peel, Fitzgerald, Herries,

[008-139]Rosslyn--perhaps Sir G. Murray--will be against the Company.

[008-140]The Duke said it was clear to him that the remittances must be made in

[008-141]goods, and could not be made by bills. He is for the monopoly.

[008-142]In a few days the papers will be printed. A copy will then be furnished to

[008-143]each member of the Government, and I shall receive their observations.

[008-144]The Recorder's Report was a very heavy one. All the cases bad, and seven

[008-145]ordered for execution.

[008-146]The King seemed very well.

[008-147]Stratford Canning and Lord Strangford were at the Court, to be presented on

[008-148]their return.

[008-149]Before the report we read the last Irish papers. The Duke of Northumberland

[008-150]and Lord F. Leveson seem to think rather favourably of the condition of

[008-151]Ireland. The belief of Peel and Goulburn, and, I believe, of the Duke, is

[008-152]that _one_ example would settle all.

[008-153]Lord F. Leveson says that the Brunswickers are encouraged _from St.

[008-154]James's_ to expect that the Relief Bill will be repealed. Many wish for an

[008-155]explosion, the Catholics less than the Protestants.

[008-156]_July 19._

[008-157]Hardinge and Wood dined with me. Hardinge says the Duke of Cumberland has

[008-158]determined not to leave England, but to send for the Duchess and his son.

[008-159]The Duchess of Gloucester did not before, and will not now, receive the

[008-160]Duchess of Cumberland. Old Eldon wants a guarantee that no more Whigs will

[008-161]be admitted. I believe he would be satisfied with none but his own

[008-162]admission.

[008-163]Hardinge seems to think we may not have a majority when Parliament meets. I

[008-164]think he is wrong. I trust to the Duke's fortune and to 'the being a

[008-165]Government,' which is much, and to the others not being able to form a

[008-166]Government, which is more.

[008-167]_July 22._

[008-168]Had a letter from Loch. He does not like the disbanding of the six

[008-169]regiments, but he says he brings it before the Court again on Monday,

[008-170]having promised every possible information.

[008-171]Read some of Colonel Tod's 'Rajastan.' I had rather see Rajastan or

[008-172]Rajpootana than any part of India. It would really be interesting. Colonel

[008-173]Tod seems to be an enthusiast about the country and the people. He was

[008-174]there apparently at least sixteen years. The story of the beautiful

[008-175]Princess of Oudeypore [Footnote: Krishna Komari. She was poisoned by her

[008-176]father to avoid the hostilities of the rival princes who demanded her hand.

[008-177]The father was still living when Colonel Tod wrote. The House of Oudeypore

[008-178]was the only native reigning family who disdained to intermarry even with

[008-179]the Emperors of Delhi. See Tod's _Rajasthan_, i. 066.] in Tod's book and

[008-180]Sir J. Malcolm's is the most romantic and the most interesting I know. That

[008-181]family of Oudeypore or Mewar seems to be the most ancient in the world. It

[008-182]far surpasses the Bourbons and the House of Hapsburg.
