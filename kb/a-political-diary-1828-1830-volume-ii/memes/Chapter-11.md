[011-001]However, even admitting that the Russians would be content with the

[011-002]independence of Greece within the Morea (with Attica, [Footnote: Attica was

[011-003]still held by the Turks, having been reconquered after its first occupation

[011-004]by the Greeks.] by-the-bye), it was the opinion of the Duke and of every

[011-005]one (but Aberdeen) that it would neither be generous nor honourable to

[011-006]force upon the Turks in their distress terms which _they_, attaching much

[011-007]value to the _suzeraineté_, might think less favourable than what they

[011-008]might obtain under the Treaty of London, and that we should be drawing

[011-009]ourselves into the embarrassment of what would be practically a new treaty

[011-010]at the moment that we were beginning to entertain hopes of getting out of

[011-011]that which had so long harassed us.

[011-012]Upon the whole, I think the aspect of Eastern affairs is better than it has

[011-013]been since we have been a Government.

[011-014]Diebitch is said to have 35,000 men, and a reserve of 40,000. I doubt the

[011-015]reserve being so strong. The 15,000 from Sebastopol have joined.

[011-016]Paskewitz is made Grand Cross of St. George.

[011-017]Diebitch will be so, of course.

[011-018]The King, Peel said, is very blind. He has lost the sight of one eye. The

[011-019]Duke said when he was at Windsor last, the King was particularly civil to

[011-020]him, and Peel and the Duke were both of opinion that the King would be most

[011-021]cordial with the Government if the Duke of Cumberland were away, and was

[011-022]now more so than could be expected under his influence.

[011-023]Aberdeen seems to have written the letter to Stuart, and Stuart to have

[011-024]communicated it to Prince Polignac. Stuart's idea is that Polignac has had

[011-025]too much to do in fixing himself to think much of foreign politics. He

[011-026]expressed himself, however, disposed to consult with England as to the

[011-027]measures which should be adopted if Russia should break her engagements.

[011-028]Several representations have been made to France for the withdrawing of the

[011-029]French troops from the Morea--but hitherto without effect. These troops

[011-030]keep the country quiet, and enable the whole force of the Greek State to

[011-031]act offensively. Thus, assisted by French and Russian money, the Greeks

[011-032]have acquired possession of everything within the Gulfs of Volo and Arta,

[011-033]except the Island of Negropont.

[011-034]_September 1, 1829._

[011-035]Read with attention a paper of Courtney's on Leach's observations. Wrote

[011-036]some memoranda upon it, which I shall send with it to the Duke, when I have

[011-037]got from Shepheard a statement of the benefit derived by the territory from

[011-038]the fixed rate of exchange. It is a valuable paper. I have written to thank

[011-039]him for it, and to ask him to give me the result of his considerations on

[011-040]the mode of transferring the Government of India from the Company to the

[011-041]King, without materially increasing the patronage of the Crown; and

[011-042]likewise the view he takes of the alterations it would be desirable to

[011-043]introduce, if the Company should continue to govern India, in the powers of

[011-044]the Board of Control and in its relations with the Court.

[011-045]_September 3._

[011-046]The Directors are much afraid of the Russians. So am I, and the Russians

[011-047]begin to threaten us. They hint that they have open to them the route to

[011-048]Bagdad, and they announce the presence in Petersburg of an Afghan Chief,

[011-049]and of Ambassadors from Runjeet Singh.

[011-050]I feel confident we shall have to fight the Russians on the Indus, and I

[011-051]have long had a presentiment that I should meet them there, and gain a

[011-052]great battle. All dreams, but I have had them a long time.

[011-053]I have some idea of a secret letter to Bombay, directing the Government to

[011-054]take possession of the Island of Karak, [Footnote: A small island in the

[011-055]Persian Gulf to the north-west of Bushire.] and of any other tenable point

[011-056]to seal the Euphrates, in the event of the Russians moving down.

[011-057]Loch wants to dethrone Runjeet Singh!

[011-058]_September 4, 1829._

[011-059]Saw Colonel Willoughby Cotton, who commanded _en second_ in Ava. He has

[011-060]lately visited, as Adjutant-General of King's troops, all the stations of

[011-061]the army in Bengal. He says no army can be in finer order. Lord Combermere

[011-062]has weeded all the old men. The regiments manoeuvre beautifully.

[011-063]Lord C. wishes to have two King's regiments cantoned under the Himalaya

[011-064]Mountains, where the climate is as good as in England.

[011-065]Runjeet Singh has conquered Cabul and Cashmere. He has French officers at

[011-066]the head of his infantry and cavalry, and about five others. His artillery

[011-067]he keeps under his own family. He has of regular troops 30,000 infantry,

[011-068]and 10,000 cavalry, about eighty guns. All these easily assembled near the

[011-069]capital.

[011-070]He is old, and when he dies his two sons are likely to quarrel and call us

[011-071]in.

[011-072]The two ex-Kings of Cabul are living at Ludeana on pensions. Zemaun Shah,

[011-073]the blind King, and his brother, who was King in Mr. Elphinstone's time.

[011-074]Colonel Cotton speaks most highly of the Madras troops. They are more

[011-075]disposable than the Bengal troops, more free from prejudice of caste.

[011-076]He regrets the reduction of the bodyguard which conducted itself nobly in

[011-077]Ava. I like a guard, and I would have an infantry as well as a cavalry

[011-078]guard, to be formed by picked men.

[011-079]Colonel Willoughby Cotton says Colonel Skinner is about 55. His son is a

[011-080]merchant, and goes every year into Cashmere for shawls. Skinner has still

[011-081]about 1,300 men, and is quartered not far from Delhi. His people fire the

[011-082]matchlock over the arm at full gallop, and with correct aim. They strike a

[011-083]tent-peg out of the ground with their lances.

[011-084]_September 5._

[011-085]Received an answer from the Duke. He thinks the question of the six

[011-086]regiments begins to be serious, as the Court throw upon the Government the

[011-087]responsibility of running the risk of a mutiny in the army--desires to see

[011-088]the paper, which I have sent him, and says it must go to the Cabinet.

[011-089]I feel satisfied I am right. If the Cabinet give in to the Court, they

[011-090]weaken my hands so much that I shall be unable to effect any great reform.

[011-091]They make the Directors the real Ministers of India, and almost emancipate

[011-092]the Indian Government. So I told the Duke in my letter.

[011-093]_September 7._

[011-094]Office. Saw Sir A. Campbell. He came to offer himself for a command in

[011-095]India. I spoke to him of his papers respecting war with the Burmese. He

[011-096]says large boats carrying 100 men could go up to Aeng, the troops need not

[011-097]land at Ramree. He was never an advocate for a diversion at Rangoon, and

[011-098]thinks they make too much fuss about the frontier of Munnipore.

[011-099]Saw a Mr. Cotton, for a long time collector of Tanjore. He is against

[011-100]introducing the Ryotwaree settlement into that country, and by his account

[011-101]it seems very ill adapted to it, for according to him the Murassidars are

[011-102]there really proprietors, and with them the settlement is now made for the

[011-103]village.

[011-104]I sent for him to tell me about the iron I had understood to be in the

[011-105]neighbourhood of Tanjore; but there is none, it is at Satara. He seems a

[011-106]sensible man, and I must see him again.

[011-107]The Turks seem to have endeavoured to back out of their accession to the

[011-108]Treaty of London, or rather to clog it with insuperable objections. But Mr.

[011-109]Gordon has brought them back again, and on August 12 all was right, but no

[011-110]Plenipotentiaries sent. The Russians were said to be moving on Adrianople.

[011-111]They had not above 35,000 men. There is a very bad account from Smyrna of

[011-112]the state of the population in Asia. In fact the Duke of Wellington's

[011-113]prediction is fulfilled. The Turkish Empire is breaking to pieces. By Lord

[011-114]Heytesbury's account the Russians are very desirous of peace, and very

[011-115]apprehensive that a popular tumult may put an end to the Sultan. It is

[011-116]impossible to see the end of the calamities which would occur, complicated

[011-117]as they would be, if such an event as the dissolution of the Turkish Empire

[011-118]took place.

[011-119]The new French Ministry is changing the municipalities. They hope to

[011-120]succeed at the next elections. Lord Stuart considers M. de la Bourdonnaye

[011-121]as the real head.

[011-122]Polignac very prudently rests on his oars as to Greece, and properly

[011-123]observes it is idle to make protocols here when the march of events may

[011-124]have altogether changed the state of things before the protocols arrive.

[011-125]_September 8._

[011-126]Office at 11. Went to the Duke. He read to me a long letter he had written

[011-127]on the question of the six regiments, in which he entered at length into

[011-128]the state of the Indian army such as he knows it to be, and concludes in

[011-129]favour of a revision of the line I had adopted with his approbation. He

[011-130]said the Government of India was wrong--every line of the proposed letter

[011-131]abstractedly right; but there was to be considered the expediency of

[011-132]writing it.

[011-133]I have written a letter to Lord W. Bentinck, stating confidentially the

[011-134]grounds of the change of opinion as to the disbanding of the six extra

[011-135]regiments. I added, 'However, such an event will not happen in your time,

[011-136]nor I hope in mine,' or something to that effect.

[011-137]_September 11._

[011-138]Chairs at 11. Read to them the Duke's letter on the six regiments. Told

[011-139]them I had written a private letter to Lord William to relieve his mind

[011-140]from the censure intended for former Governments (a very small portion of

[011-141]which is chargeable on him), and to caution him against similar errors.

[011-142]Gave them the alteration I had intended to make in the draft respecting

[011-143]pensions granted to King's soldiers enlisted into their army. They will

[011-144]consider it.

[011-145]_September 14._

[011-146]Read the papers containing the correspondence with the local Governments

[011-147]respecting the provision of stores in India. It is hardly credible, yet it

[011-148]is true, that till within these few years the Medical Board indented upon

[011-149]England for drugs which were produced in India! From Madras as late at 1827

[011-150]they indented for file handles and blacksmiths' tongs! From Bombay in 1826

[011-151]for wooden canteens and triangles! It is evident the local Governments have

[011-152]never displayed any energy.

[011-153]_September 16._

[011-154]Received from the Duke his ideas on the subject of a campaign against Ava.

[011-155]He would hold the great Dagon Temple at Rangoon, but only for the purpose

[011-156]of having vessels in the river to co-operate with the army.

[011-157]_September 17._

[011-158]To-day has been an idle day. I have done nothing; but I have taken

[011-159]exercise, and so acquired _health_, without which I cannot do business.

[011-160]_September 20._

[011-161]Met Mr. Conyngham of the Foreign Office. He told me the Turks were ready to

[011-162]make the required concessions. Of the disposition of the Russians nothing

[011-163]seems known. R. Gordon has of his own authority ordered up Sir Pulteney

[011-164]Malcolm from Vourla to the Dardanelles. I suppose to carry away Englishmen

[011-165]and their property in the event of an insurrection or of some terrible

[011-166]catastrophe at Constantinople.

[011-167]Lord Stuart, as I suspected, gives no opinion as to the probable result of

[011-168]the political contest in France.

[011-169]I had a letter from the Duke respecting half-Batta.

[011-170]_September 24._

[011-171]Cabinet room. Read all the letters from Petersburg, Paris, Berlin, and

[011-172]Constantinople during the last fortnight, and the despatches sent during

[011-173]the last month.

[011-174]R. Gordon seems to have done very well. He and Guilleminot have acted

[011-175]cordially together, and when they had induced the Porte to consent to make

[011-176]peace on the terms prescribed by the Russians, Gordon managed very

[011-177]prudently to get General Muffling to send his secretary to the Russian

[011-178]head-quarters with the Turkish Plenipotentiaries. Muffling would have gone

[011-179]with them to the Reis Effendi had he been well enough; as it was, he sent

[011-180]his secretary, who afterwards went to the Russian head-quarters and was

[011-181]thus enabled to state distinctly what had passed in the conference held

[011-182]with the Effendi. I think it very possible that without the intervention of

[011-183]the Prussian Minister, who was known to be acquainted with the feelings of

[011-184]the Emperor, General Diebitch would not have agreed to an armistice. The

[011-185]armistice seems to have been made on August 29. We know of it from Seymour

[011-186]at Berlin.
