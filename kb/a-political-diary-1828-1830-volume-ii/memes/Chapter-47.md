[047-001]It would be inconvenient if the King should wish to go to Edinburgh next

[047-002]year. Charles X. is to be told he cannot stay there after the spring.

[047-003]However, he will probably live there all his life.

[047-004]It would be a revolting sight to see a King imprisoned for debt, and all

[047-005]gentlemen, all men of feeling, would have cried out _shame_!

[047-006]We are right in feeling, but in policy I am not sure.

[047-007]Nieuport has fallen as well as Ostend. The Bruxellois are drilling, and

[047-008]threaten to attack Prince Frederick. Probably Van Holen drills them to keep

[047-009]them quiet.

[047-010]Many people have applied to Falck [Footnote: Dutch Minister.] for passports

[047-011]for Brussels, going in reality to join the rebels. Today two Irish

[047-012]labourers asked for passports! Brussels will become the sink of Europe, and

[047-013]every unquiet spirit will go there.

[047-014]The Duke thinks our attempt to make France act in concert with us the only

[047-015]chance of preserving peace.

[047-016]I fear its preservation is almost desperate. One thing is in favour of it,

[047-017]that all the European States desire it yet more than we do.

[047-018]I cautioned them to-day not to take any advanced position from which it

[047-019]would be difficult and discreditable to retreat. The people would not go in

[047-020]with us in a war to avert a distant danger, nor indeed for any object not

[047-021]commercially interesting.

[047-022]It came out accidentally in the course of conversation respecting the loan

[047-023]to the Netherlands that we had lent 20,000£ to the Greeks; the sum to be

[047-024]repaid by bills to be drawn by our Commissioner whenever the loan we are to

[047-025]guarantee may be made--that is, we are to be paid out of our own money.

[047-026]Of this loan I knew nothing, and my impression is that when it was

[047-027]earnestly pressed by Aberdeen such objections were stated on the ground of

[047-028]illegality that the decision was against it. Certainly nothing was decided

[047-029]in favour of it. I recollect having said I would rather advance a portion

[047-030]of the money myself than be a party to the transaction.

[047-031]_October 3._

[047-032]Cabinet. The Consul at Ostend announces that nothing remains to the King of

[047-033]the Netherlands but Antwerp. The troops have everywhere laid down their

[047-034]arms. On the 1st the Brussels papers announce that orders had been issued

[047-035]by the provisional Government for arresting all the Dutch officers.

[047-036]Peel read first the dispatch written by Aberdeen with the omissions agreed

[047-037]upon, and then his own substitutions. His is much the best. It speaks of

[047-038]'composing troubles' instead of 'suppressing anarchy,' avoids all mention

[047-039]of interference, and altogether is a more prudent paper, touching the

[047-040]Barrier very slightly. It was understood that Peel's was adopted.

[047-041]It is determined to allow the King, Charles X., to go to Holyrood House,

[047-042]but he will be told there is no furniture, or very little, and that he can

[047-043]only stay six months, and that no expense can be incurred on his account.

[047-044]He has admitted no one to an audience, but many have been to Lulworth to

[047-045]ask for places.

[047-046]Talleyrand says they have found an _ébauche_ of Polignac's, telling

[047-047]Bourmont that his proposal that the money taken at Algiers should be given

[047-048]to the Legion of Honour could not be complied with, as the King intended to

[047-049]distribute it amongst his most faithful friends. They pretend they do not

[047-050]intend to make use of this because there is no proof of its having been

[047-051]sent; in fact they do not use it because it reflects credit on Bourmont.

[047-052]Lord Rosslyn, with whom I walked as far as Pimlico Palace, showed me the

[047-053]Treasury list of the House of Commons. 311 decided friends and 189 enemies-

[047-054]that is 500; the remainder, consisting of moderate Tories, violent Tories,

[047-055]good and bad doubtfuls, as well as Huskissonians (the latter 13), are more

[047-056]likely to be against us than for us.

[047-057]Rosslyn still hankers after a coalition, but reform has made it impossible.

[047-058]We might have had this time last year Sir J. Graham. We might even now have

[047-059]Palmerston, [Footnote: It appears from Lord Palmerston's published papers

[047-060]that this was an error. He had already determined to act with the Whigs,

[047-061]and not to take office without Lord Grey and Lord Lansdowne. See Ashley's

[047-062]_Life of Lord Palmerston_, vol. i. p. 211.] but the Duke seems determined

[047-063]to go on as he is, Peel and all, even Bathurst, seem to have a correct view

[047-064]of the danger; but I see no flinching.

[047-065]_October 4._

[047-066]Saw the Duke. Suggested that we must soon consider what should be done with

[047-067]respect to the China trade. If we were to give up the monopoly we had

[047-068]better do so at once, on the first day of the Session, with a good grace,

[047-069]and not make ourselves appear to do it with reluctance. The Duke said we

[047-070]must certainly consider it. Had I talked with the Chairs about it? I said

[047-071]no. I had thought it best to wait till the Cabinet had come to a decision

[047-072]as to what should be done. I had privately advised them to turn over in

[047-073]their minds the plan of the Company going on with the Government slightly

[047-074]varied, but without monopoly.

[047-075]The Duke said he could not make up his mind without hearing first what the

[047-076]Chairs had to say. I observed that if they, that is Astell, thought the

[047-077]Government hesitated, they would certainly say they could not go on without

[047-078]monopoly. However, the Duke seemed to think it was impossible for the

[047-079]question to come before the Cabinet before we had talked with the Chairs.

[047-080]So I have asked him and Loch to meet at the Treasury at twelve on Monday.

[047-081]I should like to see Tucker and Stuart, but I must do it privately, as I

[047-082]have no principle to go upon in consulting with individual Directors.

[047-083]The Duke seems very reluctant to give up the monopoly, and to have very

[047-084]exaggerated ideas of the value of the Company's intervention.

[047-085]He showed me a letter he had received from Molé, in which he takes a very

[047-086]moderate view of the Belgian question. Expresses the most earnest desire

[047-087]for peace, as war would place everywhere the two extreme principles in

[047-088]conflict. France will not interfere, neither can she suffer others to

[047-089]interfere, in the internal affairs of the Netherlands. He hopes to be able

[047-090]to arrange everything amicably.

[047-091]A letter the Duke showed me from Rothschild's brother is still more

[047-092]satisfactory if the view taken in it be correct. He says France will, _with

[047-093]England and the Allies_, amicably settle the question; but she will not

[047-094]have to be excluded.

[047-095]He mentions Leopold as a probable King of Belgium.

[047-096]The Court of Turin [Footnote: The first French Republic had made a similar

[047-097]non-recognition a plea for seizing Savoy.] seems to be in a great fright

[047-098]because the French Government took huff at their not recognizing at once.

[047-099]They were afraid to do so till they heard what the great Powers did.

[047-100]M. de la Tour says they can bring 60,000 or 90,000 men into the field, if

[047-101]Genoa is guarded for them by a fleet; but Genoa would require 14,000 men.

[047-102]On that place they must retreat.

[047-103]The Spaniards seem to be going on well. They mean not to be _empressés_

[047-104]with their recognition, but are advised not to be the last.

[047-105]There have been insurrections at Hanau, Swerin, and I know not where else.

[047-106]The Diet intend to vary the law of the Empire and to allow any neighbour,

[047-107]whose assistance may be asked, to give it at once.

[047-108]The Emperor of Russia received General Athalia very graciously, but he

[047-109]keeps him waiting for his answer. Lieven professes himself well satisfied

[047-110]with our reasons for immediate recognition. So does Metternich. In fact

[047-111]they cannot do without us, and if we lead they must follow.

[047-112]_October 5._

[047-113]Cabinet. Goulburn's Civil List. He transfers to the Consolidated Fund all

[047-114]the salaries heretofore partly paid by the Civil List, and in diplomacy

[047-115]there is a reduction of 28,000£ a year.

[047-116]It is supposed there can be no reduction in the great departments in the

[047-117]article of tradesmen's bills, or in the Board of Works.

[047-118]The King gives up the Droits [Footnote: 'Droits of Admiralty.'] without any

[047-119]compensation. This is all a loss to the privy purse.

[047-120]It seems possible to reduce perspectively many officers in England and in

[047-121]Ireland who do not really contribute to the state of the crown. This,

[047-122]however, did not occur to Goulburn but to Peel.

[047-123]The account of Liege is very bad indeed. Things there seem going on in the

[047-124]style of the French Revolution.

[047-125]Nothing can be better than the account from France. They will be pleased by

[047-126]the letter read to them. All they feared was the attempt to exclude them

[047-127]from all concert in the settlement of Belgium. They think neither the King

[047-128]nor Prince Frederick can return to Brussels; but the Prince of Orange may,

[047-129]and this will, I think, be finally settled.

[047-130]_October 6._

[047-131]Council at 2. Talleyrand was presented. He backed to the window and read a

[047-132]speech in which there were several erasures. He declared the determination

[047-133]of France to pursue the course so wisely followed by England of non-

[047-134]interference. He spoke of himself as 'Ministre d'une Royaute votée à

[047-135]l'unanimité.'

[047-136]The King did not much like receiving him, and was a little nervous. To what

[047-137]Talleyrand said about noninterference the King answered it was a very good

[047-138]thing, especially when exercised _de bonne foi_. This he said by Aberdeen's

[047-139]advice.

[047-140]I read the King of the Netherlands' letter. He asks distinctly for

[047-141]_military assistance_.

[047-142]Cabinet dinner at the Duke's. The Prince of Orange is gone to Antwerp. This

[047-143]the Duke thinks the very worst step that could have been taken; the only

[047-144]mistake the King has made. In fact the King was unwilling, and ever since

[047-145]the affair of Brussels there has been a coolness between the King and the

[047-146]Prince. The Duke fears the consequences of the Prince's going, because he

[047-147]is a man devoted to popularity-vain. The Duke and Talleyrand were talking

[047-148]about popularity. The Duke said those who loved it never loved it with

[047-149]moderation. Talleyrand said, 'Il n'y a jamais de modération, où il n'y a

[047-150]pas de _goût_--et il n'y a pas de gout dans l'amour de la popularité!' The

[047-151]Duke asked Talleyrand what sort of a man the Duke of Orleans was. 'Un

[047-152]Prince de l'Ecole normale.' Of the Queen he said, 'Elle est bonne femme, et

[047-153]surtout grande dame--c'est ce qu'il nous faut.'

[047-154]Talleyrand said he had given the King a piece of advice, '_to go to

[047-155]Neuilly_'--that is, to rescue himself from the vagabond cortége.

[047-156]Talleyrand is very well pleased with the letter sent to Paris, and the

[047-157]foreign Ministers are satisfied.

[047-158]The King (our King) seemed to me to be very weary to-day. Aberdeen said he

[047-159]was a good deal distressed at the state of Europe, and rather anxious.

[047-160]Lord and Lady Holland and Rothschild appear to be the only people besides

[047-161]the Ministers who have called on Talleyrand. Lord Holland is very much with

[047-162]him. Lord Holland is doing all he can to save the lives of the French

[047-163]Ministers--for the interest of the French Government, not of the Ministers

[047-164]themselves. He has written to La Fayette and to the King.

[047-165]_October 7._

[047-166]I forgot on what authority it was mentioned yesterday, but it was mentioned

[047-167]as a fact that the Liberaux would not have done anything unless they had

[047-168]been certain of the Duke of Orleans. So afraid were they of a revolution

[047-169]that they would have submitted to the Ordonnances rather than run the risk

[047-170]of it.

[047-171]_October 9._

[047-172]At Canterbury heard more particulars of the machine-breaking now going on

[047-173]in the neighbourhood. Notice is given, and the frames are broken. One

[047-174]gentleman boasted at market they should not break his, as he had armed men

[047-175]to protect them. They on the same night set fire to his rickyard. Sir Henry

[047-176]Oxenden's sons went out to meet them, when they came according to notice to

[047-177]break Sir Henry's machines. One man spoke for the rest. He acknowledged Sir

[047-178]Henry seldom or ever used his machine, and that he was the landlord in Kent

[047-179]who gave most to the poor; but they must do as they were ordered; they

[047-180]would, however, do as little as they could, and they only sawed off a

[047-181]shaft.
