# Sir Archibald Campbell

## Overview

Sir Archibald Campbell was a British Army officer and colonial administrator, best known for commanding British forces in the First Anglo-Burmese War (1824–1826) and serving as the first Governor of British Burma. In Ellenborough's diary, he appears in connection with the future of the Burmese territories and the contentious "Batta question" affecting the Indian army.

## Role in the Diary

Ellenborough records a personal meeting with Sir Archibald Campbell at the India Board in April 1829. He describes Campbell as "a fat, rather intelligent-looking man, well mannered, and sensible" (009-018). The two discussed the prospect of exchanging Tenasserim, the southernmost British possession in Burma — an idea Campbell resisted, for "he did not like giving up his conquest" (009-020, 009-023). Ellenborough entrusted him with a secret letter for his observations (009-023).

Campbell's views carried weight in Indian military policy as well. During a discussion of the Batta question — a dispute over field allowances that had inflamed tensions in the Bengal Army — Ellenborough cited the opinions of "Sir J. Malcolm, Sir Archibald Campbell, and Sir J. Nicholls" (016-018), placing Campbell among the senior military authorities whose judgment he consulted.

## Key Appearances

- **Personal call at the India Board**: Campbell visited Ellenborough in April 1829; the diarist recorded his impressions of Campbell's appearance and manner (009-018).
- **Tenasserim exchange**: Ellenborough raised the idea of trading the Burmese province of Tenasserim; Campbell opposed relinquishing territory he had helped conquer (009-020–009-023).
- **Batta question**: Campbell's opinion was cited among those of senior Indian officers on the army allowance dispute (016-018).
