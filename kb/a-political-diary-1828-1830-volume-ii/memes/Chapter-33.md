[033-001]It is like paying money in consequence of a threatening letter. If it is

[033-002]done once there is no stopping.

[033-003]I said I believed the dissolution of the Acarnanian army, happen as it

[033-004]might, would be better than its maintenance, and that the state of anarchy

[033-005]into which it was pretended Greece would fall if it had not money, would be

[033-006]a better foundation of improvement than the state of military thraldom in

[033-007]which it is now held.

[033-008]Peel proposed that Dawkins should be instructed under circumstances of

[033-009]imminent danger to advance money not exceeding 20,000£, and this would be

[033-010]the best way of doing it. The Duke has great repugnance to giving anything,

[033-011]and objects to doing what might be considered an unconstitutional act. He

[033-012]hopes Aberdeen will be able to persuade the other Powers to give 30,000£

[033-013]each, leaving us out of the subscription.

[033-014]The thing was left undetermined. I suggested that it was by no means

[033-015]impossible a question might be asked by some 'friend of Greece' whether we

[033-016]intended to give or had given money in consequence of Capo d'Istria's

[033-017]representations, and then what we had done would come out. In fact if the

[033-018]King was well the matter would be brought before Parliament.

[033-019]His illness creates great embarrassment. It is doubtful whether the

[033-020]Government can command majorities on questions on which a defeat under

[033-021]ordinary circumstances would lead them to resign; but it is known that now

[033-022]they cannot resign and cannot dissolve, and the Opposition has no other

[033-023]effect than that of interfering with the conduct of public business.

[033-024]A powerful man would place this strongly before the country and bring the

[033-025]House to a sense of its duty.

[033-026]The Duke showed me the letter he had written to Lord Combermere in reply to

[033-027]his, upon my Memorandum. It is _excellent_.

[033-028]There is to be a great fight upon sugar. Charles Grant makes a proposition,

[033-029]and Goulburn proposes to modify his original proposition by suggesting the

[033-030]addition of 6_d_. a gallon to Scotch and Irish spirits and to rum, thus

[033-031]leaving the proportional burthen nearly the same. In addition to this he

[033-032]proposes lowering the duty on the inferior kinds of sugar.

[033-033]The French Expedition was in Palma Bay on May 31, awaiting the arrival of

[033-034]the last division, which was expected the next day.

[033-035]_June 15._

[033-036]The King much better. He has been in good spirits about himself, and has

[033-037]expectorated, which is thought a good sign.

[033-038]In the House of Commons Goulburn's altered plans seem to have succeeded

[033-039]with all parties as far as first impression goes.

[033-040]_June 16._

[033-041]At the Cabinet dinner spoke to Lord Melville and Goulburn about the

[033-042]embarrassments of the civil servants. Both are very much indisposed to

[033-043]grant the papers asked for by Hume on the subject. I shall write to

[033-044]Arbuthnot to do what he can to prevent their being given.

[033-045]The Duke got a number of papers stamped--indeed all the arrears, about 400.

[033-046]The King paid more attention to them than he ever did while he was well. He

[033-047]recollected everything.

[033-048]The Duke did not think him so well as when he last saw him. The physicians

[033-049]do not like this catarrh. The Duke thought his hand was hotter than usual,

[033-050]that he was larger, and that altogether he was not so well. His judgment

[033-051]has hitherto been so correct that I attach much importance to it.

[033-052]Peel spoke after dinner with much _ennui_ of his position in the House of

[033-053]Commons. He complained that it really was not worth a man's while to be

[033-054]there for so many hours every night. The sacrifice was too great. He said

[033-055]the Radicals had brought the House into such a state that no man could do

[033-056]business but themselves. He seemed not well, and thoroughly out of humour.

[033-057]We had some discussion about the Forgery Bill. We are to see the Governor

[033-058]and deputy-governor of the Bank, &c. The Duke is much indisposed to

[033-059]acquiesce in the Commons' amendment.

[033-060]Peel thinks that after the vote of the House of Commons no verdicts will be

[033-061]obtained; but may not a contrary vote of the House of Lords turn public

[033-062]opinion into its former course? I think it may.

[033-063]_June 17._

[033-064]In French newspaper a bad report of the French fleet, which is very much

[033-065]dispersed. One division was in sight of the shore on May 30 when it came on

[033-066]to blow, and they ran to Majorca. The other divisions will have gone to the

[033-067]rendezvous on the African shore, where they will have met no men-of-war and

[033-068]much bad weather. The star of Napoleon is set.

[033-069]Lord Combermere has written another letter to the Duke, in which he

[033-070]acknowledges his error as to the compact in 1796 and 1801, and says he was

[033-071]led into it by Col. Fagan. He restates all he before said on the other

[033-072]points, and still wishes his letter to go to the King.

[033-073]The King seems to have had a good night. I did not hear the private

[033-074]account.

[033-075]_June 18._

[033-076]Received last night from Astell a letter in which he speaks of an intended

[033-077]address of his respecting the Nagpore letter. I have told him he has

[033-078]already privately told me his opinion--that the Act of Parliament has made

[033-079]no provision for a representation on the part of the Secret Committee if

[033-080]they disagree with the Board, and I cannot receive any such representation

[033-081]officially. I have further told him that I think any more delay will be

[033-082]injurious to the public service.

[033-083]Wrote a letter to Runjeet Singh to go with the horses. Showed it to Lord

[033-084]Amherst, Clare, and Auckland. Lord Amherst and Clare were delighted with

[033-085]it. Showed it to the Duke, who approved. Saw the Duke.

[033-086]The King alarmed the princesses yesterday, but the Duke of Clarence did not

[033-087]think him so ill. I saw the Duke of Clarence's letter to the Duke of W.

[033-088]Halford thinks the expectoration is an additional evil.

[033-089]_June 19._

[033-090]At 11 Privy Council to hear the appeal of Elphinstone (that is, East India

[033-091]Company) against Ameerchund Bidruchund, a case of booty. Remained till

[033-092]half-past two, when I was obliged to come away, having a dinner at

[033-093]Roehampton. Indeed I do not think that upon a point affecting the revenues

[033-094]of India I ought to vote as a judge.

[033-095]Brougham ridiculed the Directors who sat there in a mass, nine of them.

[033-096]Fergusson spoke of "the Court." Brougham said he was not surprised he

[033-097]should make that mistake seeing such an array of directors. Brougham put it

[033-098]_ad verecundiam_ to the directors whether they would vote upon a question

[033-099]in which they were directly interested, and in which they had already

[033-100]appeared by Counsel.

[033-101]They were and will be very sulky. They will stay away and decline

[033-102]supporting Government.

[033-103]The bulletin is bad.

[033-104]Two most impertinent letters from Lord Arbuthnot and Mr. Arbuthnot asking

[033-105]for, or rather _demanding_, cadetships. They will find I am not to be

[033-106]bullied.

[033-107]_June 21._

[033-108]The King expectorated blood yesterday. He is failing in strength, and now

[033-109]certainly dying.

[033-110]Read a memorandum of Wilson's on a proposed remodelling of the army. It is

[033-111]founded on my idea of bringing it into the form it formerly had, with fewer

[033-112]European officers and more native officers, in higher ranks. He proposes

[033-113]having two more European Non-Commissioned officers, a Subadar Major, and

[033-114]another Subadar, and several minor things.

[033-115]_June 22._

[033-116]Cabinet. The Duke thought the character of the Government would be affected

[033-117]if we gave up the Forgery Bill in the Lords, not in consequence of any

[033-118]change of opinion, but of a majority of 13 in the House of Commons. I am

[033-119]satisfied the law, as it is, ought to be maintained. In the House Lord

[033-120]Lansdowne made a speech on moving the second reading, and Lord Winchelsea

[033-121]and the Duke of Richmond said they should vote for the Bill as it was--

[033-122]none, however, taking religious objections, Lord Lansdowne throwing out

[033-123]that he would consent to make the bill temporary. The Chancellor made a

[033-124]very good speech, expressing his general objections to the Bill as it

[033-125]stands, and reserving his reasons for the Committee.

[033-126]The King is rather worse and weaker.

[033-127]In the House of Commons last night a mine was sprung and all parties, Whigs

[033-128]and Tories, East and West Indians, united by a trick on the sugar duties.

[033-129]However, we had a majority.

[033-130]_June 23._

[033-131]It seems Peel and Herries and even Goulburn himself rather doubts whether

[033-132]the sugar arrangement will work, and Peel has some doubt as to his

[033-133]majority. Altogether he is very much out of humour, or rather _ennuyé_, and

[033-134]a very little would induce him to give up.

[033-135]Cabinet dinner. The Duke saw the King and some stamping took place. The

[033-136]King was much worse than on Saturday. The expectoration is matter from the

[033-137]lungs. Knighton says that if they can keep the bowels right he may live a

[033-138]month. Halford says if he was an ordinary man he should think he would not

[033-139]live three days. Tierney says his pulse almost failed while he was asleep

[033-140]this morning, and he thought he would have died. The Duke says he thinks

[033-141]more with Knighton than the others.

[033-142]The King was perfectly alive to all the business done. He talks of going to

[033-143]the Cottage still.

[033-144]Much talk at the Cabinet dinner as to what should be done as to

[033-145]dissolution; but all depends on the time of the King's death, and the state

[033-146]of public business then.

[033-147]Peel, Herries, and all seem to think the Low Party gains, and will gain

[033-148]strength. Hume, on Whitbread's retirement, is to come in for Middlesex.

[033-149]_June 24._

[033-150]House. Galway Franchise Bill read second time Counsel were to have been

[033-151]heard; but the petitioners declined having them. I fear we shall have a

[033-152]sharp debate about it to-morrow, and Lord Grey be directly opposed to the

[033-153]Duke, and the worst of it is I do not believe our case is very good.

[033-154]Hardinge and Wortley both say we are in a great scrape with these sugar

[033-155]duties, and Ireland, which was all with us, is hostile again on account of

[033-156]the spirit and stamp duties.

[033-157]Walked as far as Mrs. Arbuthnot's with the Duke. He told me his view of the

[033-158]Galway Franchise Bill, and is very certain of his case. He feels Goulburn

[033-159]has satisfied no one with his sugar duties.

[033-160]The King seems much worse by the bulletin; but the private account was not

[033-161]much so. He was said to be worse when Lord Hill left Windsor. I really

[033-162]believe that we are so bothered with sugar duties and other things that an

[033-163]immediate demise and immediate dissolution would be best for us, and for

[033-164]the country.

[033-165]_June 25._

[033-166]Went to the Duke about the Galway Bill before the House met. The Duke spoke

[033-167]very well and made a very good case. Lord Grey well, but the Chancellor

[033-168]demolished his speech, and placed the question on such good grounds that it

[033-169]was useless to speak afterwards; nor was there much subsequent debate. The

[033-170]Duke of Buckingham made a speech against us, in which he mistook every

[033-171]point, and gave me a great disposition to follow him; but I knew if I did I

[033-172]should have a whole hornet's nest upon me, and I wished to keep Durham and

[033-173]Radnor in check, or answer them. Had I spoken the debate would have lasted

[033-174]three hours more. As it was we got away by nine. On the division we had 62

[033-175]to 47. Not brilliant. Our case was excellent. I had feared it would be

[033-176]indifferent. The Chancellor had got it up admirably. Lord Londonderry, the

[033-177]Dukes of Newcastle and Richmond, Calthorpe, all the Canningites, of course

[033-178]voted against us. Dudley was in the House at one time, but he did not vote

[033-179]against us, nor has he once since he went out.
