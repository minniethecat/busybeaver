# Antoinette Nowak

Antoinette Nowak is a young Polish stenographer in [[Frank-Cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]]'s office and his second documented extramarital affair in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. Unlike the artistically-inclined [[Rita-Sohlberg|Rita Sohlberg]], Nowak is working-class — "a mere stenographer" in [[Aileen-Butler|Aileen Butler]]'s contemptuous phrase ([018-018]) — and her involvement with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] highlights the power imbalance inherent in his workplace romances.

## Biography

### Background and Origins

Dreiser introduces Antoinette Nowak as "a young Polish girl named Antoinette Nowak, reserved, seemingly astute" ([008-012]). Her Polish immigrant background and her position as a stenographer place her firmly in the working class — a significant contrast to the leisured, artistic women [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] also pursues. She serves as [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s amanuensis, taking dictation for his correspondence, including letters to his father: "he begs me to say, wrote Miss Antoinette Nowak, at his dictation, that he finds the papers of great interest" ([011-029]).

### Role in the Narrative

Nowak's relationship with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] develops gradually from professional to personal. She "had come to him fresh from a school of stenography" ([016-013]), and their "practical business relations took on a more intimate character" ([016-016]) over time. The affair attracts less narrative attention than [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s involvement with [[rita-sohlberg|Rita Sohlberg]] — Nowak is often mentioned in the same breath as Sohlberg, as one of "[[rita-sohlberg|Mrs. Sohlberg]] and then Antoinette Nowak as factors in the potpourri" ([017-008]).

Aileen's discovery of the affair provokes visceral class-based contempt. She rages at [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] for taking up "with a girl as common as Antoinette Nowak" ([018-009]) and dismisses her as "a mere stenographer" and "the wretched upstart" ([018-019]). Aileen's fury at Nowak is tinged with bewilderment — she cannot understand why [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] would risk his marriage for someone of such low social station, "wondering, wondering, wondering" ([018-009]).

When [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] ends the Sohlberg affair, he assures Aileen that "as for Antoinette Nowak, she would attend to her later" ([019-007]) — a coldly pragmatic dismissal that reveals the disposability of his working-class paramours.

### Relationships

Nowak's primary narrative relationship is with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], defined by the office hierarchy: employer to employee, older man to young immigrant woman. Her relationship to Aileen is entirely adversarial — Aileen sees her as both a personal threat and a social affront. [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s "interest in Antoinette Nowak" briefly provides him distraction from the collapse of the Sohlberg affair ([017-010]), casting Nowak as a secondary figure even within the category of mistress.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Dreiser describes Nowak as "reserved, seemingly astute" ([008-012]) — a young woman of practical intelligence rather than [[art|artistic sensibility]]. Her Polish background marks her as an immigrant outsider in [[chicago|Chicago]] society, someone for whom employment in [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s office represents a foothold in American life. The power differential between employer and stenographer colors every aspect of her portrayal; she has less agency than Sohlberg and is more easily dismissed.

### Social Position

As a Polish immigrant and working woman, Nowak occupies the lowest rung of the novel's [[society|social hierarchy]]. Aileen's incredulity at [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s interest in her — "a girl as common as Antoinette Nowak" ([018-009]) — underscores the class gulf. Nowak's social position makes her both vulnerable (she cannot fight back against Aileen's attacks) and pragmatically ambitious (her relationship with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] may represent social mobility).

### Motivations and Conflicts

Nowak's motivations remain largely implicit in the text. Her "reserved" nature and "seemingly astute" presentation suggest calculation rather than passion — she may view the affair as advancement, security, or simply the cost of proximity to a powerful man. Her conflict is systemic: as a working-class immigrant woman, she has few defenses when the affair collapses and Aileen's wrath descends.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

Antoinette Nowak appears only in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. She has no counterpart in *[[the-financier|The Financier]]* ([[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] affairs are of a different character) or *[[the-stoic|The Stoic]]*. Her structural role is as the working-class variant of [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s extramarital pattern — demonstrating that his romantic opportunism crosses class lines and that his office serves as a hunting ground as much as a place of business.
