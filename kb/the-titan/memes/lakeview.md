# Lakeview

**Lakeview** is among the first territories for which Cowperwood seeks franchise rights. The casual confidence of his request masks the strategic importance of the area in his consolidation plan.

## Strategic Value

Lakeview's value lies in its potential. Securing a [[franchise|franchise]] there gives Cowperwood control over future development, allowing him to dictate the terms on which the growing suburb connects to [[chicago|Chicago]]'s transit network.

## Suburban Autonomy

Lakeview was an independent township whose council could grant franchises without [[city-hall|City Hall]]'s involvement. "Suburbs such as Lakeview and Hyde Park, having town or village councils of their own, were permitted to grant franchises" — a jurisdictional loophole Cowperwood exploited brilliantly [007-018].

While [[peter-laughlin|Laughlin]] worried that the North Side would not "develop very rapidly for a number of years," Cowperwood saw that growth could be anticipated [007-013]. The key was securing rights before population arrived, then letting demographic expansion drive value.

## The Lakeview Gas and Fuel Company

Cowperwood told Laughlin: "I'll want you to get me a franchise in Lakeview shortly and build me a plant. I'll give you all the help you need. I'll arrange the money" [007-035]. The division of labor was characteristic — Laughlin for local connections, Cowperwood for [[capital|capital]].

The company's president, Mr. Sippens, became a key operative [008-011]. But Cowperwood warned that information flowed freely: "They're just as apt as not to run to this North Side Gas Company and tell them all about the whole thing" [008-008]. The intelligence war paralleled the financial one.

## Coordinated Campaign

The franchises "organized in Lakeview, [[hyde-park|Hyde Park]], and elsewhere outside the city limits" were building blocks [012-028]. Each township council was cultivated separately — local lawyers retained, councilmen befriended, objections neutralized.

The North Side suburbs had their own political ecology, distinct from the [[south-side|South Side]]'s Democratic machine. This independence was both opportunity and risk. Cowperwood's method was to cultivate multiple channels, ensuring no single point of failure could derail his plans. Lakeview exemplified his pattern: identify undervalued political assets, secure them quietly, hold until growth made them indispensable.
