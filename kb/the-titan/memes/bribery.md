# Bribery

**Bribery** is not an aberration in the world of *The Titan* — it is the operating system. The novel depicts a political economy in which public decisions are routinely purchased, and the distinction between legitimate influence and outright corruption is a matter of degree rather than kind.

## The Machinery of Corruption

Cowperwood's approach to bribery is systematic rather than opportunistic. He "bribed councilmen and legislators" (008-004) as a matter of course, treating political payments as a line item in his business calculations. The novel does not moralize about this — it describes it as how things are done. General Van Sickle, the Civil War veteran who fronts Cowperwood's franchise operations, is introduced with a "suggestive light on the whole situation" (008-004): his reputation, his connections, and his willingness to operate in the gray zone between influence and corruption make him an ideal instrument.

The newspaper investigation reveals "conspiracy and wholesale bribery of councilmen" (011-008), and the legal response includes "injunctions, and charges of bribery and conspiracy" (012-030). The accusations are not false — they are simply ineffective. The machinery of accountability — courts, newspapers, public opinion — is too weak to overcome the machinery of corruption.

## Bribery as Investment

Cowperwood regards bribery as an investment with a calculable return. The cost of buying a councilman's vote is weighed against the value of the franchise it secures. This calculus extends beyond cash payments to include jobs, contracts, stock subscriptions, and social favors — a diversified portfolio of influence. The narrator describes "the subtlest forms of political bribery" (043-001), recognizing that the most effective bribes are those that do not look like bribes at all.

## The Moral Economy

The novel's characters operate within a distinct moral economy. Bribery is wrong in principle but necessary in practice — a contradiction that produces elaborate rationalization. "Bribery and cajolery where ordinary bribery would not avail" (039-002) — the line captures the escalatory logic of corruption: what begins as a favor becomes a payment, and what begins as a payment becomes a system.

Cowperwood himself believes he is merely adapting to the rules of the game. His rivals — Schryhart, Arneel, Hand — employ the same methods but cloak them in respectability. The novel's critique is not of any individual's corruption but of a political system that makes corruption the price of participation.
