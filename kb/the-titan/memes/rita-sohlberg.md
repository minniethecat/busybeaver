# Rita Sohlberg

Rita Sohlberg is the wife of violinist [[Harold-Sohlberg|Harold Sohlberg]] and the first extramarital affair of [[Frank-Cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]] chronicled in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. Her involvement with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], while intense, proves fleeting — a pattern that repeats with [[Stephanie-Platow|Stephanie Platow]] and others. Through her, Dreiser explores the transactional nature of Gilded Age romantic entanglements and the cold calculus of attraction and social convenience.

## Biography

### Background and Origins

Rita Sohlberg enters the narrative as the wife of Harold Sohlberg, a violinist in [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s social orbit. Dreiser introduces her as "of the semi-phlegmatic type" — emotionally reserved yet physically compelling ([014-014]). Her marriage to Harold appears conventional but notably passionless, leaving her susceptible to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s advances.

### Role in the Narrative

The affair between [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] and Rita Sohlberg begins almost accidentally. Dreiser notes that their "relationship was fostered quite accidentally at first" ([015-001]), yet [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] quickly recognized her appeal: "Rita Sohlberg was by far the most pleasing woman who had come his way since Aileen" ([015-006]). The liaison intensifies rapidly, consuming [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s attention until his wife [[Aileen-Butler|Aileen Butler]] discovers it.

Aileen's reaction is volcanic. Upon learning of the affair, she confronts [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] with raw fury:

> She would die first! She would kill Rita Sohlberg and Antoinette Nowak and Cowperwood and herself first! ([018-019])

The "onslaught" of Aileen's discovery "took Rita Sohlberg back completely" ([018-027]), and the affair collapses under the weight of domestic confrontation.

By Chapter XIX, [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] has "broken up the liaison with Rita Sohlberg, anyway for the present" ([019-007]), though Aileen continues to taunt him about "your Rita Sohlbergs and your [[antoinette-nowak|Antoinette Nowak]]s" ([019-010]), recognizing that Rita is the first of a pattern rather than an isolated incident.

### Relationships

Rita's primary relationships are defined through [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s perspective. She serves as his introduction to extramarital adventure in [[chicago|Chicago]] — Dreiser later refers to "the spirit and personality of Rita Sohlberg" as a benchmark against which subsequent women are measured ([027-001]). After her departure, [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] becomes "aware of a new system of procedure" in romantic matters — suggesting that the Rita affair taught him how to conduct future liaisons with greater discretion ([021-010]).

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Dreiser characterizes Rita as "of the semi-phlegmatic type" ([014-014]) — outwardly calm but inwardly responsive to attention and flattery. She is "tactful" ([026-010]) and artistically-inclined, moving in the cultural circles that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] finds increasingly attractive as his wealth grows. Her personality serves as a template: [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] later compares [[Stephanie-Platow|Stephanie Platow]] to her, noting Platow had "much of the optimism of Rita Sohlberg" but was "endowed with a strange, sweet passivity" ([024-007]).

### Social Position

As the wife of a musician, Rita occupies the fringe of [[chicago|Chicago]]'s artistic society — a world [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is just beginning to explore after establishing his [[finance|financial]] empire. Her social position is modest; she lacks the independent wealth or status that would make her a strategic match for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], which may explain why the affair is ultimately disposable.

### Motivations and Conflicts

Rita's motivations remain somewhat opaque — Dreiser presents her primarily through [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s and Aileen's eyes. Her "seemingly urgent feeling for life" ([020-001]) suggests a woman dissatisfied with the narrow confines of her marriage, seeking vitality and excitement through illicit connection. Her conflict is external: caught between [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s desire and Aileen's rage, she is swept aside by forces stronger than her own will.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

Rita Sohlberg appears only in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]* (the second volume of the [[trilogy-of-desire|Trilogy of Desire]]). She has no presence in *[[the-financier|The Financier]]* ([[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] years) or *[[the-stoic|The Stoic]]* (his London period). Her role is structurally important as the *first* [[chicago|Chicago]] affair — the one that establishes the romantic pattern [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] will repeat, with variations, throughout the novel.
