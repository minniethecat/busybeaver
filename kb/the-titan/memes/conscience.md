# Conscience

**Conscience** in *The Titan* is notable primarily for its absence — or, more precisely, for the ways in which characters contrive to live without it. Cowperwood is repeatedly described as "conscienceless" (033-004, 062-001), and the novel treats this not as a defect but as a condition of his success. To have a conscience in the world of *The Titan* is to be at a competitive disadvantage.

## The Conscienceless Man

Cowperwood is "conscienceless" in the specific sense that he does not experience moral qualms as impediments to action. His Philadelphia critics call him a "conscienceless promoter" (033-004), and the novel's final chapters compare him to "ancient Roman emperors — ruthless, conscienceless, as desperate as himself" (062-001). The comparison is revealing: conscience, in this view, is a historical contingency, not a human universal. The Romans did without it; so can Cowperwood.

The narrator explains that Cowperwood has "no understanding of, and consequently no conscience in matters of large public import" (034-019). This is a careful formulation: it is not that Cowperwood is immoral in all things, but that his moral imagination does not extend beyond the personal. He can be generous to individuals while indifferent to the public consequences of his actions.

## Conscience and Its Costs

The novel's few conscientious characters do not fare well. Those who "crossed his conscience in some way" (023-007) — the phrase suggesting that Cowperwood does have a conscience, but one that is activated only by personal affront — find themselves his enemies. Having a conscience, in the novel's implicit accounting, makes you slower, more hesitant, and more vulnerable.

A character like Cecily Haguenin has "the devil's own time with her conscience" (015-002) — the struggle is presented as genuine but also as futile. Conscience causes suffering without producing better outcomes. The novel does not celebrate conscience's absence, but it documents its practical consequences with clinical detachment.

## The Novel's Moral Vision

*The Titan* does not argue that conscience is meaningless — only that it is powerless. The novel's moral vision is descriptive rather than prescriptive: it shows how the world works, and leaves judgment to the reader. Cowperwood's amorality is a fact; whether it is also a tragedy is a question the novel raises but does not answer.

"Life is to be learned from life itself" (062-022), the dying Cowperwood reflects — a philosophy that dispenses with external moral frameworks in favor of direct experience. Conscience, in this final accounting, is one of those things "the world is dosed with too much" — an impediment to clear seeing and effective action.
