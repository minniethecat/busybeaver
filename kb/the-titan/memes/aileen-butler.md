# Aileen Butler Cowperwood

Aileen Butler is the second Mrs. [[frank-cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]], and her story in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]* is a chronicle of [[ambition|aspiration]] and exclusion. Beautiful, passionate, and fiercely loyal, she follows [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] to [[chicago|Chicago]] with the expectation that her new life as a wealthy man's wife will grant her the [[reputation|social standing]] she craves. Instead, she finds that her past — as [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s mistress during his first marriage — closes more doors than his money can open.

## Biography

### The Journey West

Aileen "had come to the train to meet him" (003-005) when [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] first explored [[chicago|Chicago]], and her enthusiasm for the western city matches his own. "I think the West is the answer for us" (003-005), he tells her, and she believes it. "Immediately after their marriage [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] and Aileen journeyed to [[chicago|Chicago]] direct" (006-007), establishing themselves at the [[the-tremont|Tremont Hotel]] before moving to their mansion on [[north-shore-drive|North Shore Drive]].

### The Social Battle

Aileen's struggle for social acceptance is the novel's most sustained portrait of a woman's experience in Gilded Age America. She "wondered where her [[fate|chance]] for social [[stock-exchange|exchange]] would come in — her opportunity to shine" (003-018). She has "stepped smartly into [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] society" and expects [[chicago|Chicago]] to receive her similarly — but finds instead a wall of silent judgment.

### Love and Resentment

"He laughed, and so did she. She could not help it. She loved him so" (009-005). Aileen's love for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is genuine and passionate, but it is increasingly shadowed by his infidelities and her own growing bitterness. "We are not the only ones that have had marriage troubles, I'm sure" (009-048), she protests — but the knowledge that her situation is common does not make it easier to bear.

## Relationships

Aileen's world narrows as the novel progresses. Her relationship with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is the axis around which her life turns. Her attempts to form friendships with women like Mrs. [[anson-merrill|Anson Merrill]] and Mrs. [[judah-addison|Addison]] are rebuffed or remain superficial. She is isolated — too grand for the middle class, too tainted for the elite.

## Character Analysis

Aileen is defined by the gap between what she believes she deserves and what society is willing to grant her. Her [[art|beauty]], her wealth, and her passion should, by the logic of the Gilded Age, guarantee her success. That they do not — that "the so-called best society of a city interprets the phrase with certain material differences" (005-002) — is the novel's indictment of a system in which reputation matters more than substance.
