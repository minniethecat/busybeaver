# Politics

In *The Titan*, **politics** is inseparable from finance — Cowperwood cannot build his street-railway empire without controlling or influencing the political machinery of Chicago and, later, Illinois. As one character bluntly observes, "none of us are in politics or finance for our health" (012-034).

## The Political Landscape of Chicago

Chicago in the 1880s–1890s was governed by a complex web of aldermen, state legislators, and party bosses, all of whom could be bought, bullied, or bargained with. Cowperwood quickly learns that franchises and charters require political approval, and political approval requires strategic investment.

After consolidating his position, Cowperwood is described as navigating "the intricacies of politics, finance, and corporate control" (038-021), treating all three as complementary tools for accumulating and exercising power.

## Bribery and Influence

The novel is unflinching in its depiction of political corruption. Cowperwood distributes money strategically — to aldermen who control franchise votes, to state legislators who can pass enabling bills, to party bosses who can deliver blocs of support. This is presented not as moral failing but as the operational reality of Gilded Age capitalism.

## Political Opposition

Cowperwood's enemies use politics as a weapon against him, organizing "local elements in finance" to block his ambitions through legislative means (059-002). The political battles in the novel — over franchise renewals, over the fifty-year bill, over the governor's veto — are as dramatic as any financial maneuver.
