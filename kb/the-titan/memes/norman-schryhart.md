# Norman Schryhart

Norman Schryhart is one of [[chicago|Chicago]]'s dominant [[finance|financier]]s when [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] arrives — "a man of great physical and mental vigor, six feet tall, hale and strong" (011-015). As a leader of the city's [[finance|financial]] oligarchy, Schryhart sees [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] not as a colleague but as a threat to the established order he has helped construct. His opposition to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is both personal and systematic, [[ambition|drive]]n by territorial instinct as much as economic calculation.

## Biography

### The Dominating Type

Schryhart "did not believe in minor partnerships or investments" (011-027). He is a man who expects to [[power|control]] what he touches, a "dominating type of person" whose approach to business reflects his broader philosophy of life. When [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] rebuffs his initial overtures for partnership, Schryhart's response is swift and lasting: he becomes the central organizing force of the anti-[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] coalition.

### The Gas Franchise War

Schryhart's interest in gas consolidation brings him into direct conflict with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] early in the novel. He approaches [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] with a proposal for partnership, offering "thirty-five or forty percent of the whole" (011-043), but [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] recognizes the terms as disadvantageous. The rebuff stings Schryhart's pride and transforms what [[power|might]] have been an alliance into open war. "He who takes the sword may well perish by the sword" (012-001) — the narrative commentary following [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s dismissal of Schryhart foreshadows the long campaign of retaliation to come.

### The Political Campaign

Schryhart joins with [[harold-hand|Hand]] and [[arneel|Arneel]] to form the [[finance|financial]] backbone of the anti-[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] [[politics|political]] effort. He is "now bitterly opposed to him" (013-028) after [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s victories in the gas wars, and his social [[power|influence]] — "a force socially" — amplifies his [[finance|financial]] power. In the later chapters, when [[harold-hand|Hand]] declares "I must give in," Schryhart responds with characteristic vigor, laying "a fervently sympathetic hand on his shoulder" (050-003) and pledging continued resistance.

## Relationships

Schryhart's relationships are defined by alliance and enmity. His primary bond is with fellow [[finance|financier]]s [[arneel|Arneel]] and [[harold-hand|Hand]] — the triumvirate that coordinates opposition to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s expanding empire. His relationship with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is one of competitive hostility, a clash not merely of business strategies but of fundamentally different approaches to power.

## Character Analysis

Schryhart represents the established [[finance|financial]] order of [[chicago|Chicago]] — men who have risen through caution and alliance-building rather than the swashbuckling individualism that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] embodies. His antagonism is not hypocritical; he knows the system rewards aggression and [[power|influence]]-peddling. What he objects to is [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s refusal to play by the unwritten rules that govern how the game is played among gentlemen of property.
