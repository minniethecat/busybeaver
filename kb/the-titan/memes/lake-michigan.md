# Lake Michigan

**Lake Michigan** is the geographic feature that shapes Chicago — its shoreline, its climate, and its identity. [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s first view of the city arrives across the prairie, but the lake is the city's eastern boundary and its defining natural feature.

## The Waterfront

The lakefront is where Chicago's wealth displays itself — the mansions of [[north-shore-drive|North Shore Drive]] look out over the water, their windows framing a view that money has purchased. The lake is the city's aesthetic center, the natural beauty against which its industrial energy is measured.

## Dreiser's Apostrophe

Dreiser's invocation of Chicago opens with an image of the lake: "By its shimmering lake it lay, a king of shreds and patches, a maundering yokel with an epic in its mouth" [002-001]. The lake is what gives the raw, unfinished city its saving grace of beauty. Against the flat, featureless prairie that Cowperwood crossed by rail, the lake offered a boundary and a horizon.

## The North Shore and Recreation

"On one drive they skirted the lake on the North Shore," Dreiser writes, as [[aileen-butler|Aileen]] contemplates "the chalky, bluish-green waters" that stretched to the horizon [003-019]. The lake was a recreational amenity for the wealthy — a place for carriage drives, promenades, and the display of leisure. But it was also a commercial artery, connecting Chicago to the ports of the Great Lakes and, through the Erie Canal, to the [[new-york|New York]] markets.

The lake's shoreline was contested and valuable. The lakefront parks, the piers, the railroad rights-of-way that hugged the water's edge — all were objects of commercial and political competition. The lake was both natural wonder and economic resource, a duality that mirrored the [[chicago|Chicago]] Cowperwood sought to master.

## Drives and Dalliances

The lake provided setting for the novel's more romantic episodes. "Given the spring, the sunshine on the lake, white sails of ships, a few walks and talks on pensive afternoons" — the lakefront was where Cowperwood conducted his more delicate personal negotiations [014-012]. After dinner, he and his companions "drove along the lake-shore and out through an open bit of tree-blocked prairie land, the moon shining softly" [015-035]. The lake served as the novel's lyric counterpoint to the hard transactions of [[lasalle-street|LaSalle Street]] and [[city-hall|City Hall]].
