# Marriage

**Marriage** in *The Titan* is an institution under pressure. It serves simultaneously as a social credential, an economic arrangement, and an emotional framework — and for virtually every significant character in the novel, it fails to perform any of these functions adequately. Cowperwood's marriages, Aileen's marriage, and the marriages of the Chicago elite are all sites of negotiation, betrayal, and quiet desperation.

## Marriage as Social Contract

The novel treats marriage primarily as a social and economic arrangement rather than a romantic union. "Immediately after their marriage Cowperwood and Aileen journeyed to Chicago" (006-007) — the brevity of this transition underscores how marriage is, for them, a gateway to a new phase of life rather than a destination in itself. Aileen's status as Cowperwood's wife is a social position she must continually defend: "We are not the only ones that have had marriage troubles, I'm sure" (009-048), she protests, invoking the universality of marital difficulty as a shield against judgment.

## Desire and Its Limits

The novel is unusually candid about the relationship between desire and the institution of marriage. "Sex desire and its fruition being such an integral factor in the marriage and every other sex relation" (017-007), the narrator observes, in a passage that links the biological to the institutional. Marriage is supposed to contain and channel desire — but for Cowperwood, it never does. His affairs are not rejections of marriage so much as supplements to it; he sees no contradiction between being a husband and being a lover.

The novel also explores marriage from the outside: "Harold when he seemed to play divinely, and when life was all romance and art. Given the spring, a sudden Saturday afternoon marriage, a runaway day to Milwaukee" (014-012) — describing the impulsive wedding of a minor character that captures the romantic notion of marriage that the novel's central characters can no longer sustain.

## The Economics of Marriage

For the women of *The Titan*, marriage is often the only available economic strategy. Aileen's decision to remain with Cowperwood despite his infidelities is partly economic — "after her sudden marriage, she decided to continue" (014-013) — and partly a matter of identity. Divorce carried a social cost that most women of the period could not afford.

Cowperwood himself "had little faith in the ability of women aside from their value as objects of art" (014-041), and his marriages reflect this instrumental view. He marries for beauty, for social advantage, and for companionship — but never for the kind of partnership that the institution theoretically implies.
