# Art

For Frank Cowperwood, **art** is not a separate domain from business or politics — it is the lens through which he views all of life. His aesthetic sensibility is one of the novel's organizing principles: he judges people, cities, and financial maneuvers with the same eye for proportion, composition, and beauty that he brings to a painting.

## The Aesthetic Eye

Cowperwood perceives the world in artistic terms from his first arrival in Chicago. The city's raw energy "would compose itself into stirring artistic pictures" (001-014), and he studies "the streets as in the matter of art he would have studied a picture" (001-016). This is not dilettantism — it is a mode of cognition. The flat prairie, the converging rail lines, the wheat piled "giving it an artistic subtlety which touched his imagination" (001-012) — all of these become compositional elements in a living canvas.

## Art as Power

Cowperwood's art collecting is inseparable from his pursuit of power. Beautiful objects — paintings, sculptures, furnishings — are both the visible evidence of his success and instruments of social advancement. The gallery at 463 North Shore Drive is not merely a private pleasure; it announces to Chicago that Cowperwood has taste, cultivation, and the means to acquire what others cannot.

His relationship with women, too, is framed through aesthetic appreciation. He reflects on them "aside from their value as objects of art" (014-041), and his affairs are as much about possessing beauty as about romantic attachment.

## The Artist as Model

Cowperwood conceives of himself as a kind of artist — the financier as creator. His consolidations, his manipulations, his network-building are all acts of composition. Just as a painter arranges forms on a canvas, Cowperwood arranges capital, influence, and people into an integrated design. "Why, it fairly sang" (001-014), he thinks of the city — and of his own ambitions within it.
