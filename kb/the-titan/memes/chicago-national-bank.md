# Chicago National Bank

The **Chicago National Bank** (also referred to as the **Lake City National Bank**) was the largest financial institution in Chicago at the time of Frank Cowperwood's arrival in the city, holding deposits exceeding fourteen million dollars. Located at Dearborn and Monroe Streets, it was presided over by [[judah-addison|Judah Addison]], a shrewd and well-connected banker who became one of Cowperwood's earliest and most loyal allies in the West.

## Role in the Novel

### Cowperwood's Introduction to Chicago Finance

When Cowperwood first arrived in Chicago with letters of introduction to prominent citizens, he sought out Addison at the Lake City National Bank as his first point of contact. The bank served as Cowperwood's gateway into Chicago's financial and social elite. Addison, struck by Cowperwood's bearing and intelligence, immediately recognized him as an exceptional man.[^001-014]

[^001-014]: PN 001-014 — Cowperwood's first impressions of Chicago as a "seething city in the making" and his immediate attraction to its dynamic energy.

Their initial meeting established a pattern of mutual respect and financial collaboration that would persist throughout the novel. Addison, a "short, heavy, rubicund man with grayish-brown sideburns," was known for his keen judgment of character and his influential position in Chicago banking circles.[^002-008]

[^002-008]: PN 002-008 — Addison instinctively rises to greet Cowperwood, offering him an apple and demonstrating the immediate rapport between the two men.

### Financial Backing

The Chicago National Bank provided critical financial backing for Cowperwood's ventures in gas utilities and later street railways. Addison himself became one of a small group of Chicago financiers — including Alexander Rambaud, Millard Bailey, and Anton Videra — who watched Cowperwood's career with interest and invested alongside him.[^007-012]

[^007-012]: PN 007-012 — Cowperwood's backers include Judah Addison, Alexander Rambaud, Millard Bailey, and Anton Videra, a group who "watched him" with keen interest.

### Social Introduction

Through Addison, Cowperwood gained admission to the exclusive [[union-club-organization|Union League Club]] and the [[concept-pages|Calumet Club]], where he met the city's most powerful business and political figures. Addison arranged a pivotal noontime luncheon at the Union Club that brought Cowperwood into contact with "a varied company of men ranging in age from thirty-five to sixty-five," establishing his presence in Chicago's elite circles.[^002-022]

[^002-022]: PN 002-022 — The Union Club luncheon where Addison introduces Cowperwood to Chicago's leading men, including Arneel and directors of the stock exchange.

## Significance

The Chicago National Bank represents the established financial order of Gilded Age Chicago — conservative, well-capitalized, and deeply connected to the city's [[politics|political]] and [[society|social]] infrastructure. Addison's willingness to back Cowperwood despite his controversial past in Philadelphia demonstrates both the banker's independent judgment and the frontier openness that characterized Chicago finance in this era. The bank serves as a counterpoint to the more predatory financial tactics of Cowperwood's later rivals such as [[norman-schryhart|Norman Schryhart]] and [[harold-hand|Hosmer Hand]].
