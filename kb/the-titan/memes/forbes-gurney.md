# Forbes Gurney

Forbes Gurney is a young playwright and poet in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*, part of the bohemian-artistic set orbiting [[Stephanie-Platow|Stephanie Platow]]. Dreiser introduces him as "a playwright and poet by the name of Forbes Gurney — tall, fair, passionate" ([027-001]), and he serves as a romantic rival of sorts to [[Frank-Cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]] — representing the artistic, impractical alternative to the [[finance|financier]]'s wealth and power.

## Biography

### Background and Origins

Forbes Gurney is described as a "tall, melancholy youth, with brown hair and blue eyes, who looked like a portrait of Byron" ([027-010]). His Byronic appearance — tall, fair, melancholic, poetic — places him squarely in the romantic-artistic tradition that Dreiser uses to contrast with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s raw [[finance|financial]] masculinity.

### Role in the Narrative

Gurney enters the narrative through [[Gardner-Knowles|Gardner Knowles]]'s circle, "also by the latest arrival, Forbes Gurney" ([027-010]). He immediately attracts [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]]'s attention: "she hovered in the vicinity of Forbes Gurney. Why, she could scarcely say" ([027-016]). She describes him to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] with characteristic enthusiasm:

> There's a new man who comes up to the Players — Forbes Gurney. He's so charming; he's so pale and poetical! ([027-018])

[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s jealousy is tempered by dismissal. When [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]] speaks of "Forbes Gurney, he just drifts up there once in a while" ([027-022]), [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] contrasts himself favorably: "Compared to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], Forbes Gurney was a stripling beggar" ([027-025]). The [[finance|financial]] metaphor — "stripling beggar" — is characteristic of [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[philosophy|worldview]], where wealth is the ultimate measure of masculine worth.

The Gurney-[[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]] connection intensifies briefly, with "the atmosphere of poetry that surrounded Forbes Gurney" ([028-001]) drawing her in. Dreiser describes an ice-sailing scene: "[[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]] and Forbes Gurney sat forward of the mast looking into each other's eyes" ([028-002]). Yet this too fades as [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] solidifies his hold on [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]]. "So was her interest in Forbes Gurney" ([028-001]) — Dreiser frames her fascination as temporary and performative, the posture of a young woman experimenting with romantic identities.

### Relationships

Gurney's primary romantic connection is to [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie Platow]], though their involvement is brief and she eventually dismisses it as an affectation. His relationship to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is one of implicit rivalry — the poet versus the plutocrat, with the plutocrat winning handily. He is linked to the wider bohemian set through [[Gardner-Knowles|Gardner Knowles]], [[Lane-Cross|Lane Cross]], and the [[Garrick-Players|Garrick Players]].

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Dreiser sketches Gurney as a Byronic type: tall, fair, passionate, melancholic — the romantic poet as physical ideal. His "passion" ([027-001]) suggests intensity beneath the surface, while his "melancholy" ([027-010]) gives him an air of tragic sensitivity. [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]]'s description — "so pale and poetical" ([027-018]) — captures the aestheticized quality that attracts her: Gurney is less a person than a romantic ideal made flesh, someone who looks like he stepped out of a poem.

### Social Position

Gurney is a "stripling beggar" by [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s standards ([027-025]) — a young man of artistic ambition but no significant wealth. His connection to the Garrick Players gives him access to [[chicago|Chicago]]'s theatrical world, but his social position is precarious, dependent on talent and patronage rather than capital.

### Motivations and Conflicts

Gurney's motivations remain largely implicit. His interest in [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie]] appears genuine — the ice-sailing scene suggests real romantic chemistry — but he is outmatched by [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s resources and magnetism. His Byronic melancholy may be genuine artistic temperament or calculated persona; Dreiser leaves the question open. His conflict is the classic one of artist versus capitalist: Gurney offers poetry and passion; [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] offers power and security. In Dreiser's world, the outcome is predetermined.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

Forbes Gurney appears only in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]* as a minor figure in the [[chicago|Chicago]] bohemian set. He has no role in *[[the-financier|The Financier]]* or *[[the-stoic|The Stoic]]*. His function is archetypal: the Byronic poet as romantic alternative, representing the aesthetic and emotional dimensions that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[finance|financial]] world cannot accommodate — except as a temporary diversion for a young woman still exploring her romantic identity.
