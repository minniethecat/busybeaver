# Michael McKenty

Michael McKenty is the [[politics|political]] boss whose [[power|control]] of [[chicago|Chicago]]'s Democratic machine makes him the most powerful non-[[finance|financial]] figure in the novel. "As interesting and forceful an individual as one would care to meet" (012-021), McKenty operates at the intersection of votes, money, and [[power|influence]] — the man who can deliver or deny the franchise approvals on which [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s empire depends.

## Biography

### The Boss

McKenty embodies the urban [[politics|political]] machine of the Gilded Age. He [[power|control]]s councilmen, dispenses patronage, and negotiates the price of legislation with the same cold pragmatism that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] brings to [[finance|financial]] markets. When [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] needs city council approval for his franchises, it is McKenty he must approach — and McKenty he must pay.

### The Negotiation

The franchise negotiation reveals the machinery of Gilded Age politics in its most candid form. "Not so much. We may never need to lay a pipe or build a plant. They will offer to sell out, buy, or combine before that. We can fix the terms. Leave it to me" (012-015) — McKenty's language is that of a businessman who happens to deal in legislation rather than commodities.

### The Alliance

McKenty and [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] develop a pragmatic alliance based on mutual utility. McKenty provides the [[politics|political]] cover; [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] provides the capital. Their relationship is not friendship — it is business, conducted with professionalism and without sentiment.

## Relationships

McKenty's primary relationship in the novel is with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], for whom he serves as [[politics|political]] fixer and gatekeeper. He also maintains a web of relationships with councilmen, party operatives, and other [[politics|political]] figures — the "machine" that he commands.

## Character Analysis

McKenty represents the democratization of power in Gilded Age America — not the democracy of ideals but the democracy of the marketplace. His power derives from his ability to deliver votes, and his willingness to sell those votes to the highest bidder is treated by the novel not as [[bribery|corruption]] but as the normal operation of politics. "Now, Mr. McKenty, it was not very long after I started out to get franchises" (012-030) — [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s direct address captures the transactional nature of their relationship.
