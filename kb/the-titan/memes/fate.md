# Fate

**Fate** operates in *The Titan* as a counterforce to ambition. If Cowperwood's will drives the plot forward, fate is what pushes back — the unpredictable, the accidental, and the circumstantial that no amount of planning can eliminate. The novel is attentive to the role of chance in human affairs, and its most dramatic reversals are attributed, at least in part, to forces beyond any character's control.

## Fate as Narrative Force

The narrator frequently invokes fate as an explanatory device. "A gratuitous humor on the part of fate" (033-001), one passage begins, introducing a turn of events with the suggestion that the universe itself has a dark sense of comedy. When Cowperwood's rival Braxmar is suddenly removed from the scene, it is "by a strange stroke of fate" (054-001) — the phrase acknowledging that even the most carefully laid plans are subject to intervention from outside.

This is not fatalism in the classical sense. The novel does not suggest that characters are powerless — only that their power is bounded. Fate sets the stage; ambition acts upon it. The tension between these two forces is one of the novel's central dramatic engines.

## The Characters' Relationship to Fate

Different characters relate to fate differently. Cowperwood treats it as an adversary to be outmaneuvered — he acknowledges its existence but refuses to submit to it. Minor characters are more likely to be its victims. Harold Sohlberg "had been in tantrums over fate, life, his lack of success" (015-022), raging against forces he cannot control.

"The state and fate of Cecily Haguenin" (031-006) — the phrase links a character's condition to her destiny, suggesting that for some figures in the novel, fate is not an external force but an internal condition. Cecily's "fate" is inseparable from who she is.

## The Chicago Fire as Fate's Paradigm

The novel's backstory — Cowperwood's financial destruction in the Chicago fire and his subsequent recovery — establishes fate's role early. The fire is nothing Cowperwood could have predicted or prevented; it is the purest expression of fate in the novel. His response to it — rebuilding, relocating, refusing to be defeated — defines his character. Fate destroys; ambition rebuilds.

The panic that threatens Cowperwood's empire late in the novel is another visitation of fate: "him and his partner to their fates" (047-018), the narrator says, as economic forces beyond any individual's control sweep through Chicago. Men "could have no objection" to being ruined — because objection would be meaningless against forces of this magnitude.
