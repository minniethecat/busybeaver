# Cecily Haguenin

Cecily Haguenin is the daughter of Mr. Haguenin, publisher of the *Press*, and her seduction by [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] represents one of the most calculating and destructive episodes in the novel. Unlike [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie Platow]], who enters into the affair with a certain fatalistic self-awareness, Cecily is an unwitting pawn in a larger game — her relationship with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] becomes "pathetic anger" (034-001) for her betrayed father and ammunition for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s enemies.

## Biography

### The Publisher's Daughter

Cecily grows up in the household of a prominent [[chicago|Chicago]] newspaper publisher, a man who initially treats [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] as an ally. When [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] visits "the office of the Press in a blowing snowstorm" (023-009), Haguenin is "glad to see him" — a cordiality that the [[finance|financier]] will later exploit. The Haguenin household becomes a bridge that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] crosses with devastating consequences.

### The Seduction

[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s seduction of Cecily is part of a broader pattern: he sees women as extensions of the aesthetic world he collects and arranges. But in this case, the affair carries particular risks because of her father's position in the newspaper world. [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] needs "the assistance of the newspapers, or at least their friendly neutrality" (023-010) for his street-railway schemes, and the seduction of a publisher's daughter undermines precisely the goodwill he has cultivated.

### The Reckoning

When the affair is discovered, Haguenin's fury becomes part of the broader anti-[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] coalition. The "pathetic anger of Haguenin" (034-001), combined with the rage of [[harold-hand|Hand]] (whose wife [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] has also seduced), creates "an atmosphere highly charged with possibilities of destructive retaliation." The personal and the [[politics|political]] merge: [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s romantic conquests become strategic liabilities.

## Relationships

Cecily's defining relationship is with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] — a man who sees in her both [[art|beauty]] and utility, and who ultimately sacrifices her well-being to his appetites. Her relationship with her father, Haguenin, is transformed by the affair from filial affection into a shared experience of betrayal.

## Character Analysis

Cecily Haguenin is one of the novel's most tragic figures because she is the least agentic. Where [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie Platow]] at least approaches her affair with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] aware of what she is doing, Cecily seems to be acted upon — a young woman whose inexperience makes her vulnerable to a man for whom conquest is both habit and philosophy.
