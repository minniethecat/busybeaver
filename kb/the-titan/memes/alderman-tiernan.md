# Alderman Tiernan

Alderman Tiernan is a [[chicago|Chicago]] city councilman who appears during the extended [[franchise|street-railway franchise]] hearings in the later chapters of *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. He is the most vividly rendered of the aldermanic voices — blustering, defiant, ungrammatical, and yet possessed of a rough eloquence that makes him one of the few characters in the novel who speaks truth to power without calculation.

## Biography

### Background and Origins

Little is revealed about Tiernan's background beyond his position as an alderman. His speech patterns — blunt, colloquial, grammatically irregular — suggest working-class or immigrant origins, a man of the wards rather than the drawing rooms. He appears in the narrative fully formed, a voice from the council chamber floor.

### Role in the Narrative

Tiernan's role is concentrated in a single extended scene: the [[franchise|street-railway franchise]] hearings, where various interests compete for the city's approval. While most speakers are lawyers, lobbyists, and businessmen speaking in polished interest-group rhetoric, Tiernan's interventions are raw and personal. He rises repeatedly to challenge the proceedings:

> Alderman Tiernan (rising). "I say now, Mr. Mayor, I want to ask a question here." ([061-081])

His concerns are practical rather than ideological — compensation for displaced workers, fairness for small operators, resistance to the consolidation of power in a few hands. When challenged, he responds with defiance:

> Alderman Tiernan (bravely). "My mind ain't the kind that changes just because some high-and-mighty corporation tells it to." ([061-088])

His most characteristic declaration is bluntly self-interested but honest:

> Alderman Tiernan. "I'm talking for compensation for these men that's put their money and their labor into these lines, and I don't care who knows it." ([061-090])

He dismisses the competing interests with contempt: "I don't give a damn for the street-railways or the gas companies or any of 'em" ([061-092]). This indiscriminate hostility to all corporate power, while not sophisticated, gives Tiernan's voice an authenticity that the more polished speakers lack.

In an aside to a fellow alderman, Tiernan murmurs approval of [[Alderman-Kerrigan|Alderman Kerrigan]]'s speech: "Say, that's pretty good. That's the stuff" ([061-103]). His final appearances show him nervously assessing the shifting [[politics|political]] winds — "Say, has Ballenberg weakened?" ([061-114]) — and observing the dynamics of the chamber: "There goes Fogarty over to the other side" ([061-120]).

### Relationships

Tiernan's narrative relationships are collegial and adversarial: he is one voice among many in the aldermanic chamber, sometimes collaborating with like-minded colleagues, more often shouting into the wind of moneyed interests.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Tiernan is the novel's most vivid minor [[politics|political]] character — a man of "brave" self-assertion, unafraid to sound foolish or vulgar in the service of what he believes to be right. His ungrammatical speech ("My mind ain't the kind," "these men that's put their money") marks his class position but does not diminish his effectiveness. In Dreiser's rendering, Tiernan's rough authenticity contrasts favorably with the polished hypocrisy of the corporate lobbyists.

### Social Position

As an alderman, Tiernan occupies the lower tier of [[chicago|Chicago]]'s [[politics|political]] class — a ward [[politics|politician]] rather than a city-wide power broker. His constituents are presumably working-class and immigrant, the people who would lose jobs and investments if the [[franchise|street-railway franchise]]s are consolidated in the hands of [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] and his rivals.

### Motivations and Conflicts

Tiernan's stated motivation is compensation for small investors and workers — "these men that's put their money and their labor into these lines" ([061-090]). Whether this is genuine populism or [[politics|political]] positioning is unclear, but Dreiser presents him with enough sympathy that the former interpretation seems more likely. His conflict is structural: the individual alderman against the machinery of corporate power, a fight that the novel suggests is already lost before it begins.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

Alderman Tiernan appears only in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*, in the [[franchise|street-railway franchise]] hearings of the later chapters. He has no counterpart in *[[the-financier|The Financier]]* or *[[the-stoic|The Stoic]]*. He represents a Dreiser type that recurs throughout the Trilogy: the minor public official whose voice, however inarticulate, speaks for interests and constituencies that the grand narrative of [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s ambition would otherwise erase.
