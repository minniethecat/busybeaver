# Philadelphia

**Philadelphia** is a significant location in the geography of *The Titan* — a node in the network of cities, neighborhoods, and institutions through which Cowperwood moves and against which he measures his ambition.

## Significance

Philadelphia represents a dimension of the novel's spatial imagination — the places that characters come from, go to, and contend with in their pursuit of wealth and power.

## The Departure

"When Frank Algernon Cowperwood emerged from the Eastern District Penitentiary in Philadelphia he realized that the old life he had lived in that city since boyhood was ended" [001-001]. This opening sentence establishes Philadelphia as both origin and closed chapter. "Though now free and rich, he was still a scandal to the pretenders" [001-002]. The city's social world — with its "sweet refinement in sections, its pretensions to American social supremacy" — had shut its doors [001-010].

Philadelphia represented everything Cowperwood had outgrown. Its financial world was provincial compared to [[new-york|New York]]'s, its social codes more rigid than [[chicago|Chicago]]'s. "How different, for some reason, from Philadelphia!" Cowperwood reflects on approaching Chicago. "That was a stirring city, too. He had thought it wonderful at one time" [001-014]. But Philadelphia was now defined by judgment.

## The Eastern Connections

Cowperwood maintained Philadelphia ties through his lawyer, Harper Steger, who handled the divorce negotiations with his first wife. His Philadelphia and New York exchange seats funded his Chicago ventures. He told [[peter-laughlin|Laughlin]], "I have connections in Philadelphia," using eastern roots as currency [004-011].

The Philadelphia scandal was a permanent shadow. Cowperwood "had come to think that never again, perhaps, could he hope to become socially acceptable" [005-002]. Every Chicago rebuff echoed Philadelphia's earlier judgment, making [[aileen-butler|Aileen]]'s social campaign doubly fraught.

## Old World, New World

The contrast between Philadelphia and [[chicago|Chicago]] structures the novel's geography of ambition. Philadelphia is the old America — established, settled, judgmental. Chicago is the new — raw, open, forgiving of ambition's sins. Cowperwood's westward journey is a migration from one America to the other, from a city that condemned him to one where he could start again.
