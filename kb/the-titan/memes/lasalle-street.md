# LaSalle Street

LaSalle Street was to [[chicago|Chicago]] what Wall Street was to [[new-york|New York]] — the concentrated nerve center of the city's financial life. In the late 1870s, this narrow canyon of stone and brick housed the banks, brokerage offices, and commodity commission houses that fueled the explosive growth of the midwestern metropolis. For [[frank-cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]], LaSalle Street was the natural habitat of ambition.

When Cowperwood first arrived in Chicago, his "early peregrinations in La Salle Street" were deliberate reconnaissance. He "inquired after clever traders on 'change, and then gave them one small commission after another" — testing the waters, building connections, establishing his reputation one transaction at a time [004-002]. The street was his laboratory for understanding Chicago's particular commercial chemistry.

## The Partnership Offices

It was at the corner of LaSalle and Madison that Cowperwood and [[peter-laughlin|Peter Laughlin]] established the firm of Peter Laughlin & Co., grain and commission merchants. Their offices occupied "rooms on the ground floor of a corner at La Salle and Madison, in the heart of the Chicago financial district" [004-026]. The location was strategic: close to the [[chicago-board-of-trade|Board of Trade]], within walking distance of the banks on [[state-street|State Street]], and visible to the men who mattered.

The choice to put Laughlin's name first was characteristic of Cowperwood's Chicago strategy. Still shadowed by his Philadelphia scandal, he understood the value of a respected local front. Laughlin — an old-time Board of Trade operator with deep connections in the grain trade — provided the credibility Cowperwood's money alone could not buy [004-001]. Behind the modest signage, however, Cowperwood was the controlling intelligence.

## The Geography of Power

LaSalle Street's significance extended beyond commerce. The street was also home to the private offices from which Cowperwood managed his expanding empire. It was here that he received visitors, negotiated with politicians, and conducted the discreet meetings that advanced his [[franchise|franchise]] schemes. His "handsome private office in La Salle Street" became a destination for those seeking his favor or fearing his reach [016-013].

The street's tunnels under the Chicago River — at LaSalle and Washington Streets — were contested territory in the [[politics|political]] battles over transit infrastructure. When Cowperwood sought to consolidate his control over the city's tunnels, the question of "what he owns under the river at Washington and La Salle streets" became a matter of public debate [021-014]. LaSalle Street was not just a financial address; it was a node in the city's circulatory system of power.
