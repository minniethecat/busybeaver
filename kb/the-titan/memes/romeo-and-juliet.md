# Romeo and Juliet

_**Romeo and Juliet**_ (c. 1597) is William Shakespeare's tragedy of young lovers from feuding families in Verona. In Dreiser's _The Titan_, the play is performed by the **Garrick Players**, an amateur theatrical circle in Chicago central to [[frank-cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]]'s personal life.

## The Garrick Players

The Garrick Players were an amateur theatrical society including several figures from the novel's social world. By degrees, the group "developed no little skill and craftsmanship in presenting one form and another of classic and semi-classic play."

Their repertoire included "Romeo and Juliet, with few properties of any kind, The Learned Ladies of Moliere, Sheridan's The Rivals, and the Elektra of Sophocles."[^024-015]

## Connection to the Novel

### Stephanie Platow

The Garrick Players' productions are connected to Cowperwood's romantic entanglement with Stephanie Platow, a young actress in the company. "Cowperwood gained his first real impression of Stephanie at the Garrick Players, where he went with Aileen once to witness a performance of Elektra."[^025-001]

[^025-001]: PN 025-001 — Cowperwood's first impression of Stephanie Platow at the Garrick Players during a performance of Elektra.

Stephanie's theatrical ambitions provided the social context for her affair with Cowperwood, which became one of the novel's most developed romantic episodes and a source of public scandal. Mrs. Platow herself commented on how "Stephanie was gradually improving in her art."[^029-001]

[^029-001]: PN 029-001 — Mrs. Platow informs Aileen about Stephanie's progress with the Garrick Players, inadvertently revealing the connection.

### Burton Stimson

One of Cowperwood's lawyers, Burton Stimson, is described as "a pale, dark-haired Romeoish youth with burning eyes." Dreiser immediately clarifies: "Stimson was no mooning Romeo, however, but an able, keen, determined man."[^008-018]

[^008-018]: PN 008-018 — Burton Stimson characterized as a "Romeoish youth" but emphatically not a "mooning Romeo," emphasizing his professional competence and drive.

This allusion illustrates how Shakespeare's play functioned in the cultural vocabulary of Dreiser's era — as shorthand for passionate, impractical youth, a type against which Cowperwood's hardheaded professionals define themselves.

## Cultural Significance

Shakespeare's play operates in _The Titan_ as a marker of cultural aspiration. The Garrick Players' productions represent the effort of Chicago's rising elite to acquire cultural capital that Eastern counterparts had inherited.

Amateur theatricals, art collections, and European travel were the instruments by which Chicago's newly rich sought to transform economic power into social legitimacy. The choice of _Romeo and Juliet_ for the Garrick repertoire is thematically resonant: the novel's world of mature calculation and financial warfare contrasts with the impulsive passion of Shakespeare's young lovers.
