# Society

**Society** in *The Titan* is a gatekeeper, a battlefield, and a mirror. Chicago's social hierarchy — its clubs, its drawing rooms, its calendars of calls and receptions — operates as a parallel power structure alongside the political and financial arenas. For Frank and Aileen Cowperwood, conquering society proves as difficult as conquering the street-railway system.

## The Structure of Chicago Society

The social world the Cowperwoods enter is finely stratified. "Some of them were in society, some not, but all of them were powerful" (003-002) — Dreiser's narrator makes clear that wealth alone does not guarantee social standing. The old families, the newly rich, the merely comfortable, and the aspirants each occupy distinct tiers. "Smart daughters, society-bred sons, handsome wives" (003-022) populate the Cowperwood circle, but acceptance is never automatic.

Aileen in particular feels the weight of social judgment. Having "stepped smartly into Philadelphia society" (003-018), she expects Chicago to receive her similarly — but finds instead that her reputation as Cowperwood's former mistress closes as many doors as his money opens.

## Society as Battleground

For Cowperwood, "the so-called best society of a city interprets the phrase with certain material differences" (005-002). He recognizes that social acceptance is a function of power: "once he became sufficiently powerful financially he might then exact a social following" (005-002). His strategy is not to beg for admission but to become indispensable — to accumulate such wealth and influence that society must come to him.

The Chicago social elite — the Arneels, the Merrills, the Schryharts — use their drawing rooms as Cowperwood uses the stock exchange: to trade in reputation, to form alliances, and to exclude rivals. "Those who, having grown suddenly rich from dull poverty, could not so easily forget the village church and the village social standards" (009-026) — the narrator captures the anxiety of the newly wealthy, caught between their origins and their aspirations.

## Aileen's Social War

Aileen's struggle for social acceptance runs parallel to Cowperwood's financial campaigns. She "wondered where her chance for social exchange would come in — her opportunity to shine" (003-018), and the novel tracks her painful education in the unwritten rules of Chicago's elite. Her beauty and her money open doors at first, but her past — and her temperament — keep them from staying open.
