# The Tremont

The Tremont Hotel was one of Chicago's finest hostelries during the Gilded Age — a grand establishment that catered to visiting dignitaries, commercial travelers of means, and newly arrived aspirants to the city's fortunes. For [[frank-cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]] and [[aileen-butler|Aileen]], the Tremont served as their first Chicago address, a temporary perch from which to survey the city they meant to conquer.

After their hasty marriage in the obscure village of Dalston, near Pittsburg, the Cowperwoods "journeyed to Chicago direct, and took the best rooms that the Tremont provided, for the time being" [006-007]. The choice was characteristic of Cowperwood: always the best accommodations available, always the appearance of success, even when the future remained uncertain. The Tremont's suites afforded them the luxury required to entertain potential allies and the anonymity needed to avoid Philadelphia scandal-seekers.

## A Temporary Refuge

The Tremont also served a strategic purpose during the early, precarious phase of Cowperwood's Chicago venture. Before the [[north-shore-drive|Michigan Avenue]] mansion was secured — before the social campaign had even properly begun — the hotel provided a buffer zone between Cowperwood's past and his future. "For another short period he might have been found living at the Tremont in Chicago, avoiding for the time being, because of Aileen's company, anything more than a nodding acquaintance with his associates" [003-016]. The hotel allowed him to control the pace at which his new life was revealed.

It was at the Tremont that Aileen began her transformation into a Chicago hostess. She "sought and discovered a French maid" upon arrival, beginning the process of assembling the household staff that would later manage the Michigan Avenue mansion [006-010]. The hotel's public rooms provided a stage for her first, tentative appearances in Chicago society — appearances that were often met with the cool reserve that old Chicago extended to newcomers.

## A Hub of Commerce

The Tremont was more than a residence; it was a venue for the informal commerce that characterized Gilded Age business. Cowperwood received visitors here, conducted interviews, and assessed potential partners. "I met him over at the Tremont talkin' to Hvranek," one Chicago political operator reported, noting Cowperwood's cool handshake — "like a dead fish" — and his unnerving self-possession [039-031]. The hotel lobby and dining room were extensions of [[lasalle-street|LaSalle Street]]'s financial ecology, places where deals were conceived and reputations assayed over oysters and champagne.

As Cowperwood's fortunes rose and the Michigan Avenue house opened its doors, the Tremont faded from his daily life. But it remained significant as the threshold — the liminal space between Philadelphia failure and Chicago triumph.
