# Hosmer Hand

Hosmer Hand is one of the "great manipulators, like [[arneel|Arneel]], [[harold-hand|Hand]], [[norman-schryhart|Schryhart]] and others" (007-002) who dominate [[chicago|Chicago]] finance when [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] arrives. But [[harold-hand|Hand]]'s opposition to the [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] outsider acquires a dimension that makes him unique among [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s enemies: personal betrayal. [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s seduction of Mrs. Hosmer Hand transforms a business competitor into a cuckolded husband bent on revenge, making [[harold-hand|Hand]] the novel's most emotionally [[ambition|drive]]n antagonist.

## Biography

### The Establishment Man

[[harold-hand|Hand]] is "a solid, phlegmatic, heavy-thinking person who had some years before lost his first wife" (031-007). He is an established figure in [[chicago|Chicago]] finance — an "eminent investor" whose [[fate|fortune]] and position place him at the center of the city's conservative establishment. His opposition to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] begins as business calculation but is quickly overtaken by something more visceral.

### The Personal Betrayal

"Far more serious were his cogitations with regard to a liaison he had recently ventured to establish with Mrs. Hosmer Hand" (031-007) — the narrative, focalizing through [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]], treats [[harold-hand|Hand]]'s wife's seduction as a strategic problem. But for [[harold-hand|Hand]] himself, this is a wound that never heals. His "solemn rage" (034-001), combined with Haguenin's "pathetic anger," constitutes an "atmosphere highly charged with possibilities of destructive retaliation."

### The Campaign

[[harold-hand|Hand]]'s campaign against [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is relentless. "Hosmer Hand, feeling that he had the burden of a great duty upon him, was not slow in acting. He was always, when aroused, a determined and able fighter" (034-019). At the [[arneel|Arneel]] house conference, his declaration "I must give in!" (050-002) marks a temporary defeat, but [[harold-hand|Hand]] is not finished. [[norman-schryhart|Schryhart]] assures him, "Every dollar that I have is at your disposal" (050-003), and the coalition presses on. [[harold-hand|Hand]]'s persistence is fueled by something no [[finance|financial]] calculation can neutralize: the bitter knowledge that his enemy has stolen his wife's affections.

## Relationships

[[harold-hand|Hand]]'s marriage to Mrs. Hosmer Hand becomes a battlefield in his war with [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]. His alliances with [[norman-schryhart|Schryhart]] and [[arneel|Arneel]] form the [[finance|financial]] and [[politics|political]] backbone of the anti-[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] campaign. But what distinguishes [[harold-hand|Hand]] from his allies is the personal dimension — for him, this is not just business.

## Character Analysis

[[harold-hand|Hand]] embodies the vulnerability that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] exploits. A "phlegmatic, heavy-thinking" man who has rebuilt his life after losing his first wife, [[harold-hand|Hand]] is susceptible because he has something to protect beyond money. His rage is not the cool tactical anger of an [[arneel|Arneel]] but the hot fury of a man whose home has been invaded. In the novel's moral economy, this makes [[harold-hand|Hand]] sympathetic even as his methods — [[politics|political]] manipulation, [[finance|financial]] warfare — mirror [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s own.
