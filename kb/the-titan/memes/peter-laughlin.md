# Peter Laughlin

Peter Laughlin is an aging grain and commission merchant who becomes [[Frank-Cowperwood|Frank Cowperwood]]'s first [[chicago|Chicago]] business partner in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. The firm of **Peter Laughlin & Co.** serves as [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s vehicle for re-entering the [[finance|financial]] world after his [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] imprisonment, with Laughlin's established reputation and trading seat providing cover for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s operations during his parole period. Dreiser portrays Laughlin as a canny-but-simple old trader — "a character" whose native shrewdness lacks the sophistication of [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[finance|financial]] genius.

## Biography

### Background and Origins

When [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] encounters him, Peter Laughlin is "a [[chicago-board-of-trade|Board of Trade]] operator" and "wheat and corn trader" approximately sixty years old ([004-001]–[004-002]). He has spent decades in the grain pits, accumulating a modest [[fate|fortune]] and an honest reputation. The office described by Dreiser is "fair-sized but rather dusty" ([004-004]), suggesting a comfortable but unglamorous operation — a veteran trader content with steady returns rather than ambitious expansion.

### Role in the Narrative

[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] "stumbled one morning on old Peter Laughlin" ([004-002]) and immediately recognized the opportunity: a partnership with Laughlin would give [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] access to the [[chicago-board-of-trade|Board of Trade]] and a legitimate business front, circumventing the restrictions of his parole.

[[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s pitch to Laughlin is characteristically direct. "Well, Mr. Laughlin," he observes, "you must have been doing pretty well these last few years" ([004-009]). When Laughlin demurs about his modest success, [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] makes his proposal: "What I would like to do is establish a small grain and commission business of my own" ([004-011]), offering to supply the capital and strategy while Laughlin supplies the name and trading seat.

Laughlin, "who was now all of sixty years of age" ([004-007]), is both flattered and cautious. He agrees after deliberation, and "two weeks later the sign of Peter Laughlin & Co., grain and commission merchants, appeared on the door" ([004-026]). The choice of naming — Peter Laughlin & Co. rather than [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] & Co. — is deliberate: [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s name cannot appear while he is on parole.

Laughlin proves a loyal partner. Dreiser writes that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] "had annexed him as a surgeon selects a knife" ([005-002]) — the surgical metaphor emphasizing Laughlin's utility to [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s larger designs. Under [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s direction, the firm prospers. "Old Peter Laughlin, rejuvenated by [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s electric presence, bought and sold with a new vigor" ([007-001]). The refrain "So Laughlin bought, and Peter Laughlin & Co. won" ([007-010]) recurs through the early [[chicago|Chicago]] chapters.

Laughlin also serves as a connector, introducing [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] to figures like [[desoto-sippens|Henry De Soto Sippens]] ([007-022]) and vouching for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s business acumen to the [[chicago|Chicago]] commercial community.

### Relationships

Laughlin's primary relationship is professional: he is [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s front man, the respectable face of an operation [[ambition|drive]]n by [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s strategic genius. The relationship is symbiotic — Laughlin gets renewed vigor and profit, [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] gets legitimacy and market access. Laughlin is also connected to the broader [[chicago|Chicago]] grain-trading community through decades of [[chicago-board-of-trade|Board of Trade]] membership, and his endorsement opens doors for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] that would otherwise remain closed.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Dreiser sketches Laughlin with economy and some affection. He is "of a naivete so naive and strange" ([005-002]) — a man of simple habits and straightforward business ethics, entirely unprepared for [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s sophisticated [[finance|financial]] operations. His catchphrase, "Allers last," reflects a lifetime of cautious trading — buying at the bottom, waiting, selling on the rise. [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s energy "rejuvenates" him, but Laughlin never fully grasps the scale or nature of the operations he fronts.

### Social Position

Laughlin occupies the respectable middle tier of [[chicago|Chicago]]'s commercial world: a [[chicago-board-of-trade|Board of Trade]] member, an honest trader, a man of some substance but not great wealth. His dusty office and old-fashioned ways mark him as a pre-Gilded Age figure — a remnant of an earlier, simpler capitalism that [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s generation is rendering obsolete.

### Motivations and Conflicts

Laughlin's motivation is straightforward: profit and a comfortable old age. He is not ambitious in [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s mold; the partnership offers him excitement and increased returns without requiring him to change his fundamental nature. His conflict is latent: he is the public face of operations he does not fully understand, exposing him to risks he cannot assess. The novel does not dramatize this tension, but it is implicit in the [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]-Laughlin arrangement.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

Peter Laughlin appears only in *[[the-titan|The Titan]]*. He has no counterpart in *[[the-financier|The Financier]]* (where [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]] is already established in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]) or *[[the-stoic|The Stoic]]* (set in London). His role is structurally essential as the first step in [[frank-cowperwood|Cowperwood]]'s [[chicago|Chicago]] rebuild — the legitimate front that enables the ex-convict to re-enter the [[finance|financial]] world. The pattern of using established local figures as fronts recurs throughout the Trilogy.
