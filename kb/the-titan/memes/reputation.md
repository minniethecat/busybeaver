# Reputation

**Reputation** is a form of capital in *The Titan* — convertible into credit, influence, and access, but also fragile and vulnerable to attack. Every major character in the novel must manage their reputation, and the drama of the plot is often driven by the intersection of private action and public perception.

## The Currency of Standing

Cowperwood understands that reputation has measurable economic value. When Chicago's banking establishment turns against him, "men who know how precious is that reputation for probity, solidarity, and conservative investment" (034-002) use their own good names as weapons against his. The financial establishment's refusal to do business with him is framed as a moral judgment — but it is, in practice, a coordinated reputational attack.

Aileen, too, grasps the power of reputation even as she struggles to maintain her own. She is "struck by the glitter of a reputation" (014-008), recognizing that social standing can be more powerful than wealth in Chicago's drawing rooms.

## Reputation and Scandal

Cowperwood's Philadelphia scandal follows him to Chicago, shaping how he is perceived before he has had a chance to act. His "already nefarious reputation" (033-005) is cited by newspapers and rivals as evidence of his unfitness for public trust. The novel is attentive to how reputation, once damaged, becomes a lens through which all subsequent actions are interpreted — Cowperwood's legitimate business maneuvers are read as schemes because his reputation marks him as a schemer.

Mrs. Huddlestone, a minor character, is described as having "a reputation" — the word itself, unqualified, carrying the weight of social judgment. The novel shows how reputation for women in Gilded Age society is more fragile and more consequential than for men: a woman's reputation, once lost, cannot be rebuilt.

## The Reputation Economy

The novel depicts a reputation economy in which "they were taking their future reputations for sagacity and discrimination in hand" (010-014) — reputation is something you invest in, something that can appreciate or depreciate, and something that must be actively managed. Cowperwood's rivals — Schryhart, Arneel, Hand — have sterling reputations not because they are honest (they are not) but because they have mastered the art of reputation management.

Cowperwood "wanted fame and reputation, but he wanted money even more" (023-001). This ordering — reputation after money — reflects his philosophy: money is the foundation, and reputation the superstructure. He believes that wealth will eventually purchase the reputation it cannot earn.
