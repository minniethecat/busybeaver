# The Titan

_**The Titan**_ (1914) is the second novel in Theodore Dreiser's **Trilogy of Desire**, the centerpiece of a trilogy that chronicles the career of [[frank-cowperwood|Frank Algernon Cowperwood]], a fictionalized version of the Chicago street-railway magnate Charles Tyson Yerkes. It is the novel for which this wiki serves as a companion and reference.

## Plot Overview

The novel opens with Cowperwood's release from the Eastern District Penitentiary in Philadelphia and his immediate departure for Chicago, accompanied by his young mistress Aileen Butler. "When Frank Algernon Cowperwood emerged from the Eastern District Penitentiary in Philadelphia he realized that the old life he had lived in that city since boyhood was ended. His youth was gone, and with it had been lost the great business prospects of his earlier manhood. He must begin again."[^001-001]

[^001-001]: PN 001-001 — The novel's opening paragraph, establishing Cowperwood's situation at the beginning of his Chicago career.

Arriving in Chicago, Cowperwood finds "a seething city in the making" — raw, ambitious, and unformed, a perfect arena for his talents.[^001-014] He establishes himself through a partnership with grain trader Peter Laughlin, secures the backing of banker [[judah-addison|Judah Addison]], and begins his campaign to dominate the city's gas and street-railway utilities.

[^001-014]: PN 001-014 — Cowperwood's first impression of Chicago as a dynamic, growing city that appeals to his speculative instincts.

### The Gas Wars

Cowperwood's first major Chicago campaign targets the city's three gas monopolies. When his consolidation offers are rejected, he organizes rival suburban gas companies, using dummy presidents and political influence to secure franchises. The gas companies eventually capitulate, selling out on favorable terms.

### The Franchise War

With capital from the gas victory, Cowperwood expands into street railways, acquiring the dilapidated [[north-chicago-street-railway|North Chicago Street Railway]] and systematically consolidating the city's transit lines. His methods — bribery, political manipulation through [[michael-mckenty|Michael McKenty]]'s [[democratic-machine|Democratic machine]], and financial pressure — draw fierce opposition from Chicago's established business elite.

### Personal Life

Cowperwood's marriage to [[aileen-butler|Aileen Butler Cowperwood]] deteriorates as his affairs multiply: with Rita Sohlberg, the wife of a violinist; with [[stephanie-platow|Stephanie Platow]], a young actress in the Garrick Players; and eventually with [[berenice-fleming|Berenice Fleming]], the daughter of a Louisville boarding-house keeper who becomes the great romantic obsession of his later life.

The social warfare that accompanies the business war proves devastating to Aileen, whose attempts to establish herself in Chicago society are repeatedly thwarted by the wives of Cowperwood's enemies.

## Setting

The novel is set primarily in Chicago during the 1870s and 1880s, with episodes in Philadelphia, New York, Louisville, and London. Its Chicago is a city of raw energy and colossal ambition — "this singing flame of a city, this all America, this poet in chaps and buckskin, this rude, raw Titan, this Burns!"[^002-001]

[^002-001]: PN 002-001 — Chicago described as a "singing flame of a city," a "rude, raw Titan" — the passage from which the novel draws its title.

## Historical Basis

_The Titan_ is a roman a clef based closely on the career of Charles Tyson Yerkes (1837–1905), the financier who dominated Chicago's street-railway system in the 1880s and 1890s. Cowperwood's battles with the gas companies, his franchise manipulations, his political alliance with a McKenty-like boss, and his eventual move to London all parallel Yerkes's actual career.

## Literary Significance

_The Titan_ represents Dreiser at the height of his powers — a sprawling, densely detailed portrait of American capitalism in its most aggressive phase. The novel's refusal to judge its protagonist, its documentary thoroughness in depicting financial and political mechanics, and its frank treatment of sexuality all contributed to its reputation as a landmark of American naturalism.
