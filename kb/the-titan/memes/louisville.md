# Louisville

**Louisville** enters *The Titan* through [[berenice-fleming|Berenice Fleming]]'s family connections. It is a southern waypoint — the Kentucky city where Cowperwood's business travels and personal interests converge during the novel's later chapters.

## The Southern Connection

Louisville represents the geographic reach of Cowperwood's network — far from [[chicago|Chicago]] and [[new-york|New York]], but connected to both through the web of [[finance|finance]], family, and desire that the novel traces across the American landscape.

## Business and Discovery

Cowperwood's Louisville connection originated in commerce. "Among those with whom he did business" in Louisville, he "encountered a certain Col. Nathaniel Gillis, very wealthy, a horseman and breeder" [040-002]. The colonel represented the southern gentry — men of old wealth and established position, very different from the scrambling new-money operators of Chicago.

It was through this business connection that Cowperwood was introduced to Mrs. Carter and, through her, to Berenice. The discovery of Berenice's photograph "in a photographer's window in Louisville" — "a rather large affair which Mrs. Carter had had made" — crystallized his interest [040-020]. The city became, for Cowperwood, not just a business destination but a place freighted with personal significance.

## Mrs. Carter's World

Louisville housed a complex social world that Dreiser sketches with care. Mrs. Carter had arrived there after a wandering life, and "until, finally, in Louisville, she had become, not openly, but actually, the mistress of a house of ill repute" [040-018]. The city's ambiguous moral geography — part southern gentility, part commercial vice — mirrored the dual nature of Cowperwood's own interests there.

When Cowperwood "chanced to be in Louisville" again, he "discovered Mrs. Carter in a very troubled social condition" [040-021]. His intervention in her affairs, and through her in Berenice's life, marked the beginning of the relationship that would define the novel's second half. Louisville was the city where Cowperwood's worlds of business and desire first converged around the figure of Berenice.
