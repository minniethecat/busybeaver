# Chicago Board of Trade

The **Chicago Board of Trade** represents the agricultural foundation of Chicago's wealth. While the [[stock-exchange|stock exchange]] trades in abstractions — shares, bonds, futures — the Board of Trade connects [[finance|finance]] to the physical reality of grain, livestock, and the fertile prairie.

## The Grain Economy

Cowperwood "wanted to talk to some bankers and grain and commission men" upon his arrival in [[chicago|Chicago]], recognizing that the Board of Trade was as central to the city's economy as any stock exchange [001-015]. "Some great grain transactions had been made here" — the city's wealth was built on the produce of the surrounding farmland.

## Laughlin and the Partnership

Cowperwood's partnership with [[peter-laughlin|Peter Laughlin]], "an old-time Board of Trade operator," was the foundation of his Chicago fortune [004-001]. Laughlin was a self-made man of the old school — shrewd, rough-edged, and deeply connected in the grain trade. He held a Board of Trade seat, giving the partnership immediate access to the floor.

The sign of Peter Laughlin & Co., grain and commission merchants, appeared over a door "at La Salle and Madison, in the heart of the Chicago financial district" — proximity to the Board being essential [004-026].

## Grain vs. Securities

The Board of Trade differed from the [[new-york|New York]] Stock Exchange in one crucial respect: it dealt primarily in physical commodities. "Few, if any, local stocks were dealt in on the Board of Trade," Cowperwood learned [002-026]. New York stocks arrived "by way of leased wires." Chicago finance was rooted in wheat, corn, and pork — the tangible produce of the prairies.

Cowperwood adapted quickly, using Board of Trade connections to trade grain futures while simultaneously pursuing gas consolidation and street-railway [[franchise|franchises]]. "I have a seat on the New York Stock Exchange," he told Laughlin, explaining ambitions beyond the grain pit [004-021]. But the Board provided essential local credibility.

## A Gateway to the City

Other speculators "came primarily to gamble on the Board of Trade" but discovered broader opportunities [014-009]. Cowperwood was never a mere gambler. He used the Board as a platform for building the reputation that enabled his entry into gas, railways, and municipal [[politics|politics]]. The Board of Trade was where Chicago first took his measure.
