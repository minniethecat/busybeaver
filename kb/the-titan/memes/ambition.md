# Ambition

**Ambition** is the novel's engine. It drives Cowperwood from Philadelphia to Chicago, from street-railways to elevated lines, from Aileen to Berenice. It is the force that the narrator treats not as a character flaw but as a natural phenomenon — like gravity or evolution, inevitable and amoral.

## Cowperwood's Ambition

Cowperwood's ambition is distinguished by its scope and its refusal of limits. He is not content to be rich — he must be the richest. He is not content to control one line — he must control them all. The narrator observes that "ambition, becomes finally dominating" (050-007), describing a force that grows by feeding on its own successes. Each acquisition makes the next one necessary; each victory raises the bar for what counts as victory.

This is ambition as compulsion rather than choice. Cowperwood does not decide to be ambitious — he is ambitious, and every decision he makes is downstream of that fundamental orientation.

## Ambition's Varieties

The novel presents a taxonomy of ambition. Aileen's is social — "thereafter it was Aileen's ambition to associate with Mrs. Anson Merrill" (009-037) — a hunger for position and recognition. Berenice's is artistic and dramatic — she speaks of "her dramatic ambitions" (026-001) and "her plays and her ambitions" (027-016). Even minor characters like Mr. Rambaud are moved by "an ancient ambition" (006-031) that the narrative treats with respect rather than irony.

Cowperwood's father figure, Addison, is notable for his relative lack of ambition — he is "without too much personal ambition" (003-016), content to serve as adviser rather than principal. His role highlights by contrast the exceptional nature of Cowperwood's drive.

## The Social Cost of Ambition

Ambition in *The Titan* is not costless. Cowperwood's ambition strains his marriage, alienates potential allies, and makes him enemies. The narrator is attentive to the human wreckage that ambition leaves in its wake — ruined competitors, neglected wives, betrayed friends. Yet the novel never suggests that ambition should therefore be restrained. It is simply a fact, like the weather, to be navigated rather than judged.

"When combined with beauty and ambition in a girl" (025-019), the narrator notes, Cowperwood finds a combination irresistible — recognizing in Berenice the same relentless drive he sees in himself.
