# South Side

The **South Side** of Chicago is where Cowperwood launches his franchise campaign. The South Side was Chicago's most established division in the 1870s — home to the Union Stock Yards, the original settlement, and the industrial infrastructure that made Chicago the meatpacking capital of the world.

## The Political Landscape

The South Side is [[michael-mckenty|McKenty]]'s political base, its councilmen the foot soldiers of the Democratic machine. "Kent Barrows McKibben began silent, polite work on the South Side, coming into the confidence, by degrees, of the various councilmen" [008-017]. The district was the testing ground for the political machinery that would control the city.

## The South Side Gas Company

The South Side Gas Company, "organized in 1848 to do business on the South Side, was the most flourishing and important" of the three Chicago gas companies [007-013]. It served the oldest section of the city, including [[lasalle-street|LaSalle Street]]'s financial district and the elite residences along [[north-shore-drive|Michigan Avenue]].

Cowperwood's "old-time enemy, the South Side Gas Company" fought his consolidation through political channels [008-011]. But his financial resources proved decisive. Victory on the South Side laid the foundation for the eventual [[franchise|franchise]] consolidation of all three city gas companies.

## The Via Appia

Michigan Avenue's southern stretch was "the Via Appia of the South Side," where "all the urgent aspirants to notable fortunes" paraded during the fashionable hour [003-022]. The avenue was both social theater and a display of industrial wealth.

But the South Side was more than its mansions. It encompassed working-class neighborhoods supplying labor to the stockyards. "Silent, polite work" among the councilmen required "coming into the confidence" of men whose votes were commodities [008-019]. The district was a microcosm of Gilded Age [[chicago|Chicago]] — the elegant and the industrial, the political and the commercial, layered on top of one another like the city's own geology of power.
