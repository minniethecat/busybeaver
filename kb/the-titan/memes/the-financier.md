# The Financier

*The Financier* (1912) is the first novel in Theodore Dreiser's **Trilogy of Desire**, preceding [[the-titan]] and [[the-stoic]]. It chronicles the early career of [[frank-cowperwood]] in Philadelphia — his precocious entry into finance, his rapid ascent as a broker, his marriage to Lillian Semple, his affair with Aileen Butler, the collapse of his fortunes following the [[chicago-fire|Great Chicago Fire of 1871]], his imprisonment in the Eastern District Penitentiary, and his eventual release and rebuilding of a fortune during the [[panic-of-1873]]. Throughout *The Titan*, Cowperwood is repeatedly referred to as "the financier" — the identifier that links him to the earlier novel and to the archetype Dreiser invented, the title that signifies not merely an occupation but a complete human type.

> "Addison called him 'a born financier,' a 'manipulator' sure to make money."
> (010-027)

## Plot Summary

### The Philadelphia Years

The novel opens with Cowperwood's Philadelphia boyhood, tracing his precocious entry into finance — his first stock trade at thirteen, his apprenticeship in a brokerage house, his rapid mastery of the financial arts. His marriage to Lillian Semple, a wealthy widow, provides both capital and social standing but proves emotionally unsatisfying. The marriage is a transaction, and Cowperwood treats it as one, with the same cool calculation he applies to his business affairs.

### The Aileen Butler Affair

Cowperwood's affair with Aileen Butler, the daughter of his political patron Edward Butler, provides the novel's central romantic conflict. The affair is passionate and reckless — a violation of the bonds of patronage and loyalty that sustain Cowperwood's political world. When Butler discovers the relationship, his fury is absolute: he uses his political influence to ensure Cowperwood's prosecution for financial crimes that, in other circumstances, would have been settled quietly.

### Imprisonment and Rebuilding

Convicted of embezzlement, Cowperwood serves thirteen months in the Eastern District Penitentiary. The prison experience is transformative rather than reformative — it hardens his determination, sharpens his understanding of power, and teaches him that conventional morality is a weapon wielded by the strong against those who threaten their position. Upon release, he rebuilds his fortune during the Panic of 1873, shorting the market while others panic, and emerges stronger than before. The novel concludes with his departure for Chicago, setting the stage for *The Titan*.

## Connection to The Titan

*The Financier* provides essential backstory for [[the-titan]]. Cowperwood's Chicago career — his gas and railway ventures, his political machinations, his social ambitions, and his relationships with [[aileen-butler]], [[stephanie-platow]], and [[berenice-fleming]] — cannot be fully understood without his Philadelphia experience.

| Element | The Financier | The Titan |
|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Setting | Philadelphia | Chicago |
| Cowperwood's age | Youth to early middle age | Established financier |
| Central conflict | Personal (Butler's vengeance) | Institutional (franchise wars) |
| Romantic focus | Aileen Butler | Stephanie Platow → Berenice Fleming |
| Political arena | Ward politics, judicial corruption | City council, state legislature |
| Outcome | Imprisonment → recovery | Empire consolidation → panic |

The Philadelphia experience shapes Cowperwood's Chicago methods. His understanding that political protection can be withdrawn by a single betrayed patron leads him to diversify his political relationships in Chicago — never depending on [[michael-mckenty]] alone. His experience of imprisonment teaches him that legal vulnerability is the financier's greatest risk, driving his meticulous attention to the legal forms of his Chicago operations. And his survival of the Panic of 1873 gives him the confidence — some would say the hubris — to believe he can survive any financial crisis.

> "Even his enemies acknowledged it: Schryhart addressed him as 'the financier' during their confrontations over the gas franchises — the narrative voice itself adopting the archetypal designation."
> (011-058)

## Literary Significance

*The Financier* pioneered the financial novel as a serious literary form. Before Dreiser, American fiction had treated business as either backdrop or moral fable — the honest merchant rewarded, the greedy speculator punished. Dreiser's innovation was to treat finance as a subject worthy of the same serious attention that novelists had given to love, war, and religion, and to refuse the moral judgments that the genre seemed to demand.

The novel's naturalistic method — its insistence that character is shaped by environment, biology, and circumstance rather than free will — aligned Dreiser with European contemporaries like Zola and Hardy while establishing a distinctly American voice. The term "financier" itself, repeated throughout the trilogy, becomes a title signifying not merely occupation but a complete human type: the man who understands that money is not an end but an instrument, that value is not a property of things but a function of perception, and that the greatest profits lie not in making things but in rearranging the relationships between those who do.

## Publication History

Published by Harper & Brothers in 1912 after a difficult editorial process. Dreiser's original manuscript was substantially revised at the publisher's insistence — cuts and alterations that softened the novel's more explicit treatments of sex and its more radical challenges to conventional morality. A restored edition based on the original manuscript was published in 2010, restoring passages that had been suppressed for nearly a century.

## In the Trilogy of Desire

*The Financier* is the foundation on which the [[trilogy-of-desire]] is built. It establishes Cowperwood's character, his methods, and his philosophy; it introduces the central themes of ambition, amorality, and the mechanics of power; and it ends with the protagonist poised for the Chicago career that *The Titan* will chronicle. The trilogy's arc — Philadelphia, Chicago, New York/London — traces a geographical expansion that mirrors Cowperwood's expanding ambition, and each volume examines a different stage in the life cycle of power: its formation (*The Financier*), its consolidation (*The Titan*), and its legacy (*The Stoic*).
