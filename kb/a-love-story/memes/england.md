# England

## Overview

England is the home country of the Delmé family and the narrative's point of origin. Sir Henry Delmé leaves England "with very unenviable sensations" (006-026), and much of the novel's emotional weight derives from the distance between English characters and their homeland. The "proud flag of merry England" (007-008) serves as a symbol of national identity against which the Mediterranean settings are contrasted.

## Geographic Context

England in the novel is represented through its institutions — the country estate, the drawing room, the regiment — rather than detailed landscape description. The Delmé family's Norman ancestry (001-023) roots them in English history, while their present circumstances connect them to England's military and social networks.

## Role in Narrative

England functions primarily as the place characters leave and return to. Sir Henry embarks "when leaving England" (008-021), and news travels by "courier from England" (006-029). It is the administrative and emotional home base, the place where letters arrive and where families are reunited.

## Associated Characters

- **Sir Henry Delmé** — Leaves England at the novel's opening
- **Emily Delmé** — Returns to England and marries there
- **Mrs. Vernon** — Her drawing-room at Leamington represents English domestic society
