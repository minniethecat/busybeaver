# Section 33

[033-001] As being in harmony with the moustaches, beard, and bandit propensities--which true bürschen delight to cultivate--they received the strangers with an unfriendly stare, and continued to vociferate their chorus.

[033-002] [[sir-henry-delme|Sir Henry]], a little dismayed at the prospect before them, called for the landlord and his bill of fare; and had the pleasure of discovering, that the provisions had been consumed, and that two hours would elapse, before more could be procured.

[033-003] At this announcement, Delmé looked somewhat blank. One of the students, observing this, approached, and apologising, in English, for their voracity, commenced conversing with the landlord, as to the best course to be pursued towards obtaining supper.

[033-004] His comrades, seeing one of their number speaking with the travellers, threw off some part of their reserve, and made way for them at the table.

[033-005] George and Henry accepted the proffered seats, although they declined joining the drinking party.

[033-006] The students, however, did not appear at ease. As if to relieve their embarrassment, one of them addressed the young man, with whom Sir Henry had conversed.

[033-007] "Carl! it is your turn now! if you have not a song, we must have an original story."

[033-008] Carl at once complied, and related the following.

[033-009] The First Story.

[033-010] Perhaps some of you remember Fritz Hartmann and his friend Leichtberg.

[033-011] They were the founders of the last new liberty club, and were famous at _renowning_.

[033-012] These patriots became officers of the Imperial Guard, and at Vienna were soon known for their friendship and their gallantries.

[033-013] Fritz had much sentiment and imagination; but some how or other, this did not preserve him from inconstancy.

[033-014] If he was always kind and gentle, he was not always faithful.

[033-015] His old college chums had the privilege of joking him on these subjects; and we always did so without mercy. Fritz would sometimes combat our assertions, but they ordinarily made him laugh so much, that a stranger would have deemed he assented to their truth.

[033-016] One night after the opera, the friends supped together at Fritz's.

[033-017] I was of the party, and brought for my share a few bottles of Johannisberg, that had been sent me by my uncle from the last vintage.

[033-018] Over these we got more than usually merry, and sang all the songs and choruses of Mother Heidelberg, till the small hours arrived. The sitting room we were in, communicated on one side with the bedroom;--on the other, with a little closet, containing nothing but some old trunks.

[033-019] This last was closed, but there was a small aperture in the door, over which was a slight iron lattice work.

[033-020] The officer who had last tenanted Fritz's quarters, had kept pheasants there, and had had this made on purpose.

[033-021] After one of our songs, Leichtberg attacked Fritz on the old score.

[033-022] "Fritz! you very Werter of sentiment! I was amazed to see you with no loves to-night at the opera. Where is the widow with sandy hair? or the actress who gave your _kirschenwasser_ such a benefit? where our sallow-faced friend? or more than all, where may the fair Pole be who sells such charming fruit? Fritz! Fritz! your sudden attachment to grapes is too ominous."

[033-023] "Come, Leichtberg!" said Hartmann, laughing, "this is really not fair. Do you know I think myself very constant, and as to the Pole, I have thought of little else for these three months."

[033-024] "Not so fast! not so fast! Master Hartmann. Was it not on Wednesday week I met you arm in arm with the actress? Were you not waltzing with the widow at the Tivoli? have you not"-- "Come, come!" said Fritz, reddening, "let us say no more. I confess to having made a fool of myself with the actress, but she begged and prayed to see me once more, ere we parted for ever. With this exception----" "Yes, yes!" interrupted Leichtberg, "I know you, Master Fritz, and all your evil doings. Have you heard of our Polish affaire de coeur, Carl?", and he turned to me.

[033-025] "No!" replied I, "let me hear it."

[033-026] "Well, you must know that a certain friend of ours is very economical, and markets for himself. He bargains for fruit and flowers with the peasant girls, and the prettiest always get his orders, and bring up their baskets, and--we will say no more. Well! our friend meets a foreign face, dark eye--Greek contour--and figure indescribable. She brings him home her well arranged bouquets. He swears her lips are redder than her roses--her brow whiter than lilies--and her breath--which he stoops to inhale--far sweeter than her jasmines. To his amazement, the young flower girl sees no such great attractions in the Imperial Guardsman; leaves her nosegays,--throws his Napoleon, which he had asked her to change, in his face,--and makes her indignant exit. Our sentimental friend finds out her home, and half her history;--renews his flattering tales--piques her pride,--rouses her jealousy;--and makes her love him, bon gré--mal gré, better than either fruit or flowers.

[033-027] "Fritz swears eternal constancy, and keeps it, as I have already told you, with the actress and the sandy haired widow."

[033-028] Leichtberg told this story inimitably, and Fritz laughed as much as I did.

[033-029] At length we rose to wish him good night, and saw him turn to his bedroom door, followed by a Swiss dog, which always slept under his bed. The rest of the story we heard from his dying lips.

[033-030] It was as near as he could guess, between two and three in the morning, that he awoke with the impression that some one was near him. For a time he lay restless and ill at ease; with the vague helpless feeling, that often attacks one, after a good supper.

[033-031] Fritz had just made up his mind to ascribe to this cause, all his nervousness; when something seemed to drop in the adjoining room; and his dog, starting to its feet, commenced barking furiously.

[033-032] Again all was still.

[033-033] He got up for a moment, but fancying he heard a footstep on the stair, concluded that the noise proceeded from one of the inmates of the house, who was come home later than usual.

[033-034] But Fritz could not sleep; and his dog seemed to share his feelings; for he turned on his side restlessly, and occasionally gave a quick solitary bark.

[033-035] Suddenly a conviction flashed across Hartmann, that there was indeed some one in the chamber.

[033-036] His curtain stirred.

[033-037] He sprang from his bed, and reached his tinder box. As the steel struck sparks from the flint, these revealed the face of the intruder.

[033-038] It was the young Polish girl.

[033-039] A fur cloak was closely folded around her;--her face was deadly pale;--with one hand she drew back her long dark hair, while she silently uplifted the other.

[033-040] Our friend's last impression was his falling back, at the moment his dog made a spring at the girl.

[033-041] The inmates of the house were alarmed. His friends were all sent for.

[033-042] I arrived among the earliest. What a sight met me!

[033-043] The members of the household were so stupefied that they had done nothing.

[033-044] Fritz Hartmann lay on the floor insensible:--his night shirt steeped in blood, still flowing from a mortal wound in his breast.

[033-045] At his feet, moaning bitterly, its fangs and mouth filled with mingled fur and gore, lay the Swiss dog, with two or three deep gashes across the throat. In the adjoining room, thrown near the door, was the instrument of Fritz's death--one of the knives we had used the evening before.

[033-046] Beside it, lay a woman's cloak, the fur literally dripping with blood.

[033-047] Fritz lingered for five hours. Before death, he was sensible, and told us what I have stated:--and acknowledged that he had loved the girl, more than her station in life might seem to warrant.

[033-048] Of course, the young Pole had been concealed in the closet, and heard Leichtberg's sallies. [[love|Love]] and jealousy effected the rest.

[033-049] We never caught her, although we had all the Vienna police at our beck; and accurate descriptions of her person were forwarded to the frontiers.

[033-050] We were not quite certain as to her fate, but we rather suppose her to have escaped by a back garden; in which case she must have made a most dangerous leap; and then to have passed as a courier, riding as such into Livonia.

[033-051] Where she obtained the money or means to effect this, God knows. She must have been a heroine in her way, for this dog is not easily overpowered, and yet--look here! these scars were given him by that young girl.

[033-052] The student whistled to a dog at his feet, which came and licked his hand, while he showed the wounds in his throat.

[033-053] "I call him Hartmann," continued he, "after my old friend. His father sent him to me just after the funeral, and Leichtberg has got his meershaum."

[033-054] * * * * * The students listened attentively to the story, refilling their pipes during its progress, with becoming gravity. Carl turned towards his right hand neighbour. "Wilhelm! I call on you!"

[033-055] The student, whom he addressed, passed his hand through his long heard, and thus commenced.

[033-056] The Second Story.

[033-057] My father's brother married at Lausanne, in the Canton de Vaud, and resided there. He died early, and left one son; who, as you may suppose, was half a Frenchman. In spite of that, I thought Caspar von Hazenfeldt a very handsome fellow. His chestnut hair knotted in curls over his shoulders. His eyes, the veins of his temples, and I would almost say, his very teeth, had a blueish tint, that I have noticed in few men; and which must, I think, be the peculiar characteristic of his complexion. When engaged in pleasure parties, either pic-nicing at the signal, or promenading in the evening on Mont Benon, or sitting tête-à-tête at Languedoc, he had no eyes or ears but for Caroline de Werner.

[033-058] He waltzed with her--he talked with her--and he walked with her--until he had fairly talked, walked, and waltzed himself into love.

[033-059] She was the daughter of a rich old colonel of the Empire:--he was the poor son of a poorer widow. What could he do? Caspar von Hazenfeldt could gaze on the house of the old soldier; but the avenue of elms, the waving corn-fields, and the luxuriant gardens, told him that the heiress of Beau-Séjour could never be his.

[033-060] He was one evening sitting on a stone, in a little ruined chapel, near the house of his beloved; ruminating as usual on his ill fate, and considering which would be the better plan, to mend his fortunes by travel, or mar them by suicide;--when an elderly gentleman, dressed in a plain suit of black, appeared hat in hand before him.

[033-061] After the usual compliments, they entered into conversation, and at last, having walked for some distance, towards Hazenfeldt's house, agreed to meet again at the chapel on the next evening.

[033-062] Suffice it to say that they often met, and as often parted, on the margin of the little stream, that ran before the door of Caspar's mother's house:--that they became great friends;--and that the young man confided the tale of his love, hopes, and miseries, to the sympathising senior.

[033-063] At last _the old gentleman_, for such he really was, told Caspar that he would help him in a trice, through all his difficulties.

[033-064] "There is one condition, Caspar!" said he, "but that is a mere trifle. You are young, and would be quite happy, were it not for this love affair of yours:--you sleep soundly, you seek and quit your bed early, and you care not for night-roving. Henceforth, lend me your body from ten at night, until two in the morning, and I promise that Caroline de Werner shall be yours. Here she is!" continued he, as he opened his snuff box, and showed the lid to Caspar, "here she is!"

[033-065] And sure enough, there she was on the inside of the lid, apparently reading to the gouty old colonel, as he sat in his easy chair in the petit salon of Beau-Sejour.
