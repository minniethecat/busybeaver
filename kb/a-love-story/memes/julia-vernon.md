# Julia Vernon

## Overview

Julia Vernon is introduced as a young woman of notable character — "Julia Vernon was a very noble girl" (027-026). She is connected to the Delmé family through social networks, and her image recurs throughout the novel as a presence in Sir Henry Delmé's consciousness. When he thought of her, "there was a consequent interest" in his fate (006-026), and her memory serves as both comfort and "reproach" (006-034). Her role is that of an alternative romantic possibility — the woman who might have been, had fate directed otherwise.

## Biography

Julia Vernon appears first as a figure observed from a distance. "Julia Vernon saw much of this" (006-009) — the narrator positions her as witness to events that shape the Delmé family. Her own family background connects her to the wider social world of the novel: she is among the Vernons, a family introduced through the widowed Mrs. Vernon.

Her presence in Sir Henry's thoughts is persistent. "The image of Julia Vernon suddenly rose before him" (019-034) at unexpected moments, and his thoughts "anxiously centred in Julia Vernon" (038-049) as he recalled "her grace" and character. The novel's final reference to her — "Julia Vernon. ætate 22" (039-050) — has the gravity of an epitaph, marking her with the Latin formula for age at death.

## Relationships
