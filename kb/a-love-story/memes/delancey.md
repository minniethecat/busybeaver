# Delancey

## Overview

Delancey is introduced as Henry Delmé's "dear friend" (010-001), a soldier whose relationship with Acmé and subsequent duel form one of the novel's most dramatic subplots. He "had himself introduced Delancey at the house where Acmé resided" (008-022), making him a pivotal figure in the triangulation of feeling that complicates the Delmé family's emotional landscape.

## Biography

Delancey's story is told largely through others. George's narrative reveals that Delancey was "wounded" in a duel (008-023) — an event that echoes through subsequent chapters. Henry speaks of him with warmth: "I made mention of my dear friend Delancey. We were indeed dear friends" (010-001).

When Henry "sought my friend Delancey, and remonstrated with him" (010-005), the scene reveals the moral dimensions of friendship — a bond strong enough to support hard truths. Delancey's absence from the group with "influence over his brother" (012-032) is noted, marking his significance by omission.

## Key Scenes

- **Introduction at Acmé's residence** (008-022): The moment that sets the triangular dynamic in motion.
- **The duel** (008-023): His wounding, a dramatic turning point.
- **Remonstrance** (010-005): Henry's difficult conversation with his dear friend.
