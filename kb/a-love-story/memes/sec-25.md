# Section 25

[025-001] "Not exactly, your honour!" replied the domestic, with perfect gravity, "but there ought to be capital snipe shooting here."

[025-002] "Ah! che vero Inglese!" said the laughing [[acme|Acmé]].

[025-003] They retraced their steps to the inn, and were ushered into the supper room, which was neither more nor less than the kitchen, although formerly, perhaps, the show room of the mansion. Around the deep-set fireplace, watching the simmering of the cauldron, were grouped some peasants.

[025-004] The supper table was laid in one corner of the room; and although neither the accommodation nor the viands were very tempting, there was such a disposition to be happy, that the meal was as much enjoyed as if served up in a palace.

[025-005] The repast concluded, Acmé rose; and observing a countryman with his arm bound up, enquired if he had met with an accident; and patiently listened to the prosy narrative of age.

[025-006] An old bronzed husbandman, too, was smoking his short earthen pipe, near the window sill.

[025-007] "What a study for Lanfranc!" said the happy wife, as she took up a burnt stick, and sketched his dried visage to the life.

[025-008] The old man regarded his portrait on the wall, with intense satisfaction; and commenced dilating on what he had been in youth.

[025-009] How different, thought [[sir-henry-delme|Sir Henry]], is all this from the conduct of a well bred English girl! yet how natural and amiable does it appear in Acmé!

[025-010] With what an endearing manner--with what sweet frankness--does this young foreigner wile away--what would otherwise have been--a tedious evening in an uncomfortable inn!

[025-011] As the night advanced, George brought out the guitar; and Acmé warbled to its accompaniment like a fairy bird.

[025-012] It was a late hour, before Delmé ventured to remind the songstress, that they must prosecute their journey early on the following morning.

[025-013] "I will take your hint," said Acmé, as she shook his hand, and tripped out of the room; "buona sera! miei Signori."

[025-014] "She is a dear creature!" said Delmé, "She is indeed!" replied his brother, "and I am a fortunate man. Henry! I think I shall be jealous of you, one of these days. I do believe she loves you as well as she does me!"

[025-015] The brothers retired.

[025-016] Sir Henry's repose was unbroken, until morning dawned; when George entered his room in the greatest agitation, and with a face as pale as death, told him Acmé was ill.

[025-017] Delmé arose immediately; and at George's earnest solicitation, entered the room.

[025-018] Her left cheek, suffused with hectic, rested on one small hand. The other arm was thrown over the bed-clothes. Her eyes sparkled like diamonds. Her lips murmured indistinctly--the mind was evidently wandering.

[025-019] A man and horse were sent express to [[naples|Naples]]. The whole of that weary day, [[george-delme|George Delmé]] was by Acmé's side, preparing cooling drinks, and vainly endeavouring to be calm.

[025-020] As the delirium continued, she seemed to be transported to the scenes of her early youth, As night wore on, the fever, if it were such, gradually increased.

[025-021] George's state of mind bordered on distraction. Sir Henry became exceedingly alarmed, and anxious for the presence of the medical attendant.

[025-022] At about four o'clock the following morning, [[doctor-pormont|Doctor Pormont]] was announced, Cold and forbidding as was his aspect, George hailed him as his tutelary angel, and burst into tears, as he implored him to exert his skill to the uttermost.

[025-023] The physician approached the invalid, and in a moment saw that the case was a critical one.

[025-024] His patient was bled twice during the day, and strong opiates administered.

[025-025] Towards evening, she slept; and awoke with restored consciousness, but with feelings keenly alive to her own danger.

[025-026] The following night and day she lingered on, speaking but little.

[025-027] During the whole of that time, even, when she slept, George's hand remained locked in hers. On this, her tears would sometimes fall, but these she strove to restrain.

[025-028] To the others around her, she spoke gratefully, and with feminine softness; but her whole heart seemed to be with George.

[025-029] Doctor Pormont, to do him justice, was unremitting in his exertions, and hardly took rest.

[025-030] All his professional skill was called to her aid; but from the second day, he saw it was in vain.

[025-031] The strength of the invalid failed her more and more.

[025-032] Doctor Pormont at length called Sir Henry on one side, and informed him that he entertained no doubt of a fatal result; and recommended his at once procuring such religious consolation as might be in his power.

[025-033] No Protestant clergyman was near at hand, even had Delmé thought it adviseable to procure one.

[025-034] But he was well aware, that however Acme might have sympathised with George, her earlier religious impressions would now in all probability be revived.

[025-035] A Catholic priest was sent for, and arrived quickly. He was habited in the brown garb of his order, his waist girt with a knotted cord. He bore in his hand the sainted pyx, and commenced to shrive the dying girl.

[025-036] It was the soft hour of sunset, and the prospect in rear of the mansion, presented a wide sea of rich coloured splendour.

[025-037] Over the window, had been placed a sheet, in order to exclude the light from the invalid's chamber. The priest knelt by her bedside; and folding his hands together, began to pray.

[025-038] The rays of the setting sun, fitfully flickered on the sheet, over whose surface, light shadows swiftly played, ever and anon glancing on the shorn head of the kneeling friar.

[025-039] His intelligent face was expressive of firm belief.

[025-040] His eye turned reverentially to heaven, as in deep and sonorous accents, he implored forgiveness for the sufferer, for the sins committed during her mortal coil.

[025-041] Acmé sat up in her bed. On her countenance, calm devotion seemed to usurp the place of earthly affections, and earthly passions.

[025-042] The soul was preparing for its upward flight. Delmé led away the sorrowing husband, and the minister of Christ was left alone, to hear the contrite outpourings of a weak departing sinner.

[025-043] The priest left the chamber, but spoke not, either to the physician, or the expecting brothers. His impassioned glance belonged to another and a higher world.

[025-044] He made one low obeisance--his robes swept the passage quickly--and the Franciscan friar sought his lonely cell to reflect on death.

[025-045] The brothers re-entered. They found Acmé in the attitude in which they had left her--her features wearing an expression at once radiant and resigned.

[025-046] But--as her eye met George's--as she saw the havoc grief had already made--the feelings of the woman resumed the mastery.

[025-047] She extended her arms--she brought his lip to hers--as if she would have made _that_ its resting place for ever.

[025-048] Alas! an inward pang told her to be brief. She drew away her face, crimsoned with her passion's flush--tremblingly grasped his hand---and, with voice choked by emotion, gave her last farewell.

[025-049] "Giorgio, my dearest! my own! I shall soon join my parents. I feel this--and my mother's words, as she met me by the olive tree, ring in my ear.

[025-050] "She told me I should die thus; but she told me, too, that I should kill the one dearest to me on earth. Thank God! this cannot be--for I know my life to be ebbing fast.

[025-051] "Dearest I do not mourn for me too much. You may find another Acmé--as true. But, oh! sometimes--yes! even when your hearts cling fondly together, as ours were wont to do--think of your own Acmé--who loved you first--and only--and does it now! oh! how well! Giorgio! dear! dearest!

[025-052] adieu! My feet are _so, so_ cold--and ice seems"-- A change shadowed the face, as from some corporeal pang.

[025-053] She tried to raise an ebony cross hung round her neck.

[025-054] In the effort, her features became convulsed--and George heard a low gurgling in the throat, as from suffocation.

[025-055] Ah! that awful precursor of "the first dark hour of nothingness."

[025-056] George Delmé sprang to his feet, and was supporting her head, when the physician grasped his arm.

[025-057] "Stop! stop! you are preventing"---- The lower lip quivered--and drooped--slightly! very slightly!

[025-058] The head fell back.

[025-059] One long deep drawn sigh shook the exhausted frame.

[025-060] The face seemed to become fixed.

[025-061] Doctor Pormont extended his hand, and silently closed those dark fringed lids.

[025-062] The cold finger, with its harsh touch, once more brought consciousness.

[025-063] Once more the lid trembled! there was an upward glance that looked reproachful!

[025-064] Another short sigh! Another!

[025-065] Lustreless and glaring was that once bright eye!

[025-066] Again the physician extended his hand.

[025-067] "Assuredly, gentlemen! vitality hath departed!"

[025-068] A deep--solemn--awful silence--which not a breath disturbed--came over that chamber of death.

[025-069] It seemed as if the insects had ceased their hum--that twilight had suddenly turned to night--that an odour, as of clay, was floating around them, and impregnating the very atmosphere.

[025-070] George took the guitar, whose chords were never more to be woke to harmony by that loved hand, and dashed it to the ground.

[025-071] Ere Delmé could clasp him, he had staggered to the bedside--and fallen over Acmé's still form.

[025-072] And did her frame thrill with rapture? did she bound to his caress? did her lip falter from her grateful emotion?--did she bury his cheek in her raven tresses?

[025-073] No, no! still--still--still were all these! still as death!

[025-074] Chapter IV.

[025-075] [[rome|Rome]].

[025-076] "Woe unto us, not her; for she sleeps well."

[025-077] * * * * * "The Niobe of nations! there she stands, Childless and crownless, in her voiceless woe; An empty urn within her wither'd hands, Whose holy dust was scatter'd long ago.

[025-078] The Scipios' tomb contains no ashes now; The very sepulchres lie tenantless Of their heroic dwellers; dost thou flow, Old Tiber! through a marble wilderness?

[025-079] Rise, with thy yellow waves, and mantle her distress."

[025-080] Undertakers! not one word shall henceforth pass our lips in your dispraise!

[025-081] An useful and meritorious tribe are you!

[025-082] What! though sleek and rosy cheeked, you seem to have little in common with the wreck of our hopes?

[025-083] What! if our ears be shocked by profane jests on the weight of your burden, as you bear away from the accustomed mansion, what _was_ its light and its load star--but what _is_--pent up in your dark, narrow tenement, but-- "A heap, To make men tremble, that never weep."

[025-084] What! if our swimming eye--as we follow those dear--dear remains to their last lone resting place--glance on the heartless myrmidons, who salute the passer by with nods of recognition, and smiles of indifference?

[025-085] What! if, returning homewards--choked with bitter recollections, which rise fantastic, quick, and ill-defined--the very ghosts of departed scenes and years--what if we start as we then perceive you--lightsome of heart, and glib of speech--clustered and smirking, on that roof of nodding plumes--neath which, one short hour since--lay what was dearest to us on earth?

[025-086] Let us not heed these things! for--light as is the task to traders in death's dark trappings; painful and soul-subduing are those withering details to the grieving and heart-struck mourner!

[025-087] We left George lying half insensible by the side of his dead wife.

[025-088] Sir Henry and Thompson carried him to the apartment of the former, and while Thompson hung over his master, attempting to restore consciousness--Delmé had a short conference with Doctor Pormont as to their ulterior proceedings.

[025-089] Doctor Pormont--as might be expected--enjoined the greatest promptitude, and recommended that poor Acmé's remains, should be consigned to the burial place of the hamlet.

[025-090] George's objections to this, however, as soon as he was well enough to comprehend what was going forward, seemed quite insurmountable; and after Sir Henry had sought the place by moonlight, and found it wild and open, with goats browsing on the unpicturesque graves, and with nothing to mark the sanctity of the spot, save a glaring painted picture of the Virgin, his own prejudices became enlisted, and he consented to proceed to Rome.

[025-091] After this decision was made, he found it utterly impossible, to procure a separate conveyance for the corpse; and was equally unsuccessful in his attempt to procure that--which from being a common want, he had been disposed to consider of every day attainment--a coffin.
