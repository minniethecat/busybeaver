# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in *A Love Story* is both a narrative destination and a site of social tension. The novel treats marriage not as the simple happy ending of romance but as a complex negotiation between personal desire and social expectation. From the deferral of marriage due to circumstance (006-024) to the anxiety about whether a "proposed marriage would meet with the entire approval" of family (023-030), marriage functions as a lens through which the novel examines the intersection of private feeling and public obligation.

## Manifestations in Text

### Marriage Deferred and Anticipated

The novel's treatment of marriage is characterized by deferral. Clarendon and Emily's marriage "was deferred" (006-024) — a delay that generates narrative tension. Similarly, news of "George's intended marriage" (023-029) travels through letters, making marriage an event that is anticipated, discussed, and worried over long before it occurs. This deferral structure makes marriage a horizon rather than a destination.

### Marriage and Social Judgment

Marriage is inseparable from social evaluation. A character who "made a marriage so disgracefully low" (013-017) suffers enduring social consequences, while Sir Henry "regarded his sister's marriage as an event" of significance (038-019). The novel tracks how marriage choices reflect on families and how social approval — or its absence — shapes marital outcomes.

### Marriage as Life Marker

Alongside death, marriage serves as one of the novel's primary life markers. It appears alongside "news of a death — or a marriage" (004-031) as the two poles of human experience, the events that punctuate a life and give it narrative shape. The novel's arc moves toward "the marriage of his sister" and other resolutions (038-047), using marriage as a structuring device for its conclusion.

## Key Passages

> News of a death — or a marriage — the tender valentine. (004-031)

Marriage and death as the paired poles of life's significant events — the novel's emotional spectrum in a single line.

> The marriage of the latter was deferred. (006-024)

Deferral as narrative technique — marriage postponed creates the temporal space for the novel's complications.

> He regarded his sister's marriage as an event, at which it might be permitted to feel satisfaction. (038-019)

The cautious, qualified embrace of marriage — social propriety modulating personal feeling.

## Related Characters

- **Emily Delmé** — Her marriage to Clarendon is one of the novel's central marital plots
- **George Delmé** — His "intended marriage" (023-029) provides a secondary marital thread
- **Sir Henry Delmé** — His perspective on his sister's marriage reveals the novel's social logic
