# Section 28

[028-001] The driver was dogged and unmoved; and the travellers had neglected to draw up a written bargain, which is a precaution absolutely necessary in [[italy|Italy]].

[028-002] They soon found they had no alternative but to submit. It was with a very bad grace they did so, for Englishmen have a due abhorrence of imposition.

[028-003] They at length stepped from the vehicle--indulged in some vehement remonstrances--smiled at Thompson's voluble execrations, which they found were equally unavailing--and were finally obliged to give up the point.

[028-004] They were shown into a small room. The chief inmates were some Papal soldiers of ruffianly air, engaged in the clamorous game of moro. Unlike the close shorn Englishmen, their beards and mustachios, were allowed to grow to such length, as to hide the greater part of the face.

[028-005] Their animated gestures and savage countenances, would have accorded well with a bandit group by Salvator.

[028-006] The landlord, an obsequious little man, with face pregnant with mischievous cunning, was watching with interest, the turns of the game; and assisting his guests, to quaff his vino ordinario, which [[sir-henry-delme|Sir Henry]] afterwards found was ordinary enough.

[028-007] Delmé's equanimity of temper was already considerably disturbed.

[028-008] The scanty accommodation afforded them, by no means diminished his choler; which he began to expend on the obstinate driver, who had followed them into the room, and was busily placing chairs round one of the tables.

[028-009] "See what you can get for supper, you rascal!"

[028-010] "Signore! there are some excellent fowls, and the very best wine of Velletri."

[028-011] The wine was produced and proved vinegar.

[028-012] The host bustled away loud in its praise, and a few seconds afterwards, the dying shriek of a veteran tenant of the poultry yard, warned them that supper was preparing.

[028-013] "Thompson!" said George, rather languidly, "do, like a good fellow, see that they put no garlic with the fowl!"

[028-014] "I will, Sir," replied the domestic; "and the wine, Mr. George, seems none of the best. I have a flask of brandy in the rumble."

[028-015] "Just the thing!" said Sir Henry.

[028-016] To their surprise, the landlord proffered sugar and lemons.

[028-017] Sir Henry's countenance somewhat brightened, and he declared he would make punch.

[028-018] Punch! thou just type of matrimony! thy ingredients of sweets and bitters so artfully blended, that we know not which predominate,--so deceptive, too, that we imbibe long and potent draughts, nor awake to a consciousness of thy power, till awoke by headache.

[028-019] Hail to thee! all hail!

[028-020] Thy very name, eked out by thine appropriate receptacle, recals raptures past--bids us appreciate joys present--and enjoins us duly to reverence thee, if we hope for joys in futurity.

[028-021] A bowl of punch! each merry bacchanal rises at the call!

[028-022] Moderate bacchanals all! for where is the abandoned sot, who would not rather dole out his filthy lucre, on an increase of the mere alchohol--than expend it on those grateful adjuncts, which, throwing a graceful veil over that spirit's grossness, impart to it its chief and its best attraction.

[028-023] Up rises then each hearty bacchanal! thrice waving the clear tinkling crystal, ere he emits that joyful burst, fresh from the heart, which from his uncontrolled emotion, meets the ear husky and indistinct.

[028-024] Delmé squeezed the lemons into not a bad substitute for a bowl, viz. a red earthen vase of rough workmanship, but elegant shape, somewhat resembling a modern wine cooler.

[028-025] George stood at the inn door, wistfully looking upward; when he remarked an intelligent boy of fourteen, with dark piercing eyes, observing him somewhat earnestly.

[028-026] On finding he was noticed, he approached with an air of ingenuous embarrassment--pulled off his cap--and said in a tone of enquiry, "Un Signore Inglese?"

[028-027] "Yes! my fine fellow! Do you know anything of me or the English?"

[028-028] "Oh yes!" replied the boy with vivacity, replacing his cap, "I have travelled in [[england|England]], and like [[london|London]] very much."

[028-029] George conversed with him for some time; and found him to be one of that class, whose numbers make us unmindful of their wants or their loneliness; who eke out a miserable pittance, by carrying busts of plaster-of-[[paris|Paris]]--grinding on an organ--or displaying through Europe, the tricks of some poodle dog, or the eccentricities of a monkey disguised in scarlet.

[028-030] It is rare that these come from a part of Italy so far south; but it appeared in this instance, that Giuseppe's father being a carrier, had taken him with him to [[milan|Milan]]--had there met a friend, rich in an organ and porcupine--and had entrusted the boy to his care, in order that he might see the world, and make his fortune.

[028-031] Giuseppe gave a narrative of some little events, that had occurred to him during his wanderings, which greatly interested George; and he finally concluded, by saying that his father had now retired to his native place at Barberini, where many strangers came to see the "antichità." George, on referring to the guide book, found that this was indeed the case; and that Isola Barberini is marked as the site of ancient Veii, the rival of young [[rome|Rome]].

[028-032] "And when do you go there, youngster, and how far is it from this?"

[028-033] "I am going now, Signore, to be in time for supper. It is only a 'piccolo giro' across the fields; and looks as well by moonlight as at any other time."

[028-034] "Ah!" replied George, "I would be glad to accompany you. Henry," said he, as he entered the room of the inn, "I am away on a classic excursion to Veii. The night is lovely--I have an excellent guide--and shall be back before you have finished your punch making.

[028-035] "_Do_ let me go!" and he lowered his voice, and the tears swam in his eyes, "I cannot endure these rude sounds of merriment, and a moonlight walk will at least afford nothing that can _thus_ pain me."

[028-036] Sir Henry looked out. The night was perfectly fine. The young peasant, all willingness, had already shouldered his bundle, and was preparing to move forward.

[028-037] "You must not be late, George," said his brother, assenting to his proposal. "Do not stay too long about the ruins. Remember that you are still delicate, and that I shall wait supper for you."

[028-038] As the boy led on, George followed him in a foot path, which led through fields of meadow land, corn, and rye.

[028-039] The fire-flies--mimic meteors--were giddily winging their way from bush to bush,--illuming the atmosphere, and imparting to the scene a glittering beauty, which a summer night in a northern clime cannot boast.

[028-040] As they approached somewhat nearer to the hamlet, their course was over ground more rugged; and the disjointed fragments of rocks strewed, and at intervals obstructed, the path.

[028-041] The cottages were soon reached.

[028-042] The villagers were all in front of their dwellings, taking their last meal for the day, in the open air.

[028-043] The young guide stopped in front of a cottage, a little apart from the rest. The family party were seated round a rude table, on which were plates and napkins.

[028-044] Before the master of the house--a wrinkled old man, with long grey hair--was a smoking tureen of bread soup, over which he was in the act of sprinkling some grated Parmesan cheese.

[028-045] A plate of green figs, and a large water melon--the cocomero--made up the repast.

[028-046] "Giuseppe! you are late for supper," said the old patriarch, as the boy approached to whisper his introduction of the stranger.

[028-047] The old man waved his hand courteously--made a short apology for the humble viands--and pointed to a vacant seat.

[028-048] "Many thanks," said George, "but my supper already awaits me. I will not, however, interfere with my young guide. Show me the ruins, Giuseppe, and I will trouble you no further."

[028-049] The boy moved on towards what were indeed ruins, or rather the vestige of such.

[028-050] Here a misshapen stone--there a shattered column--decaying walls, overgrown with nettles--arches and caves, choked up with rank vegetation--bespoke remains unheeded, and but rarely visited.

[028-051] George threw the boy a piece of silver--heard his repeated cautions as to his way to Storta--and wished him good night, as he hurried back to the cottage.

[028-052] [[george-delme|George Delmé]] sat on the shaft of a broken pillar, his face almost buried in his hands, as he looked around him on a scene once so famous.

[028-053] But with him classic feelings were not upper-most. The widowed heart mourned its loneliness; and in that calm hour found the full relief of tears.

[028-054] The mourner rose, and turned his face homeward, slowly--sadly--but resignedly.

[028-055] The heavens had become more overcast--and clouds occasionally were hiding the moon.

[028-056] It was with some difficulty that George avoided the pieces of rock which obstructed the path.

[028-057] The road seemed longer, and wilder, than he had previously thought it.

[028-058] Suddenly the loud bay of dogs was borne to his ear; and almost, before he had time to turn from the path, two large hounds brushed past him, followed by a rider--his gun slung before his saddle--and his horse fearlessly clattering over the loose stones.

[028-059] The horseman seemed a young Roman farmer. He did not salute, and probably did not observe our traveller. As the sound from the horse receded, and the clamour of the dogs died away, a feeling almost akin to alarm crossed George's mind.

[028-060] George was one, however, who rarely gave way to vague fears.

[028-061] It so happened that he was armed.

[028-062] [[delancey|Delancey]] had made him a present of a brace of pocket pistols, during the days of their friendship; and, very much to Sir Henry's annoyance, George had been in the habit, since leaving [[malta|Malta]], of constantly carrying these about him.

[028-063] He strode on without adventure, until entering the field of rye.

[028-064] The pathway became very narrow--so that on either side him, he grazed against the bearded ears.

[028-065] Suddenly he heard a rustling sound. The moon at the moment broke from a dark cloud, and he fancied he discerned a figure near him half hid by the rye.

[028-066] Again the moon was shrouded.

[028-067] A rustling again ensued.

[028-068] George felt a ponderous blow, which, aimed at the left shoulder, struck his left arm.

[028-069] The collar of his coat was instantaneously grasped.

[028-070] For a moment, George Delmé felt irresolute--then drew a pistol from his pocket and fired.

[028-071] The hold was loosened--a man fell at his feet.

[028-072] The pistol's flash revealed another figure, which diving into the corn--fled precipitately.

[028-073] Let us turn to Sir Henry Delmé and to Thompson.

[028-074] For some time after George's departure, they were busily engaged in preparing supper.

[028-075] While they were thus occupied, they noticed that the Papal soldiers whispered much together--but this gave rise to no suspicion on their part.

[028-076] One by one the soldiers strolled out, and the landlord betook himself to the kitchen.

[028-077] The punch was duly made, and Sir Henry, leaving the room, paced thoughtfully in front of the inn.

[028-078] At length it struck him, that it was almost time for his brother to return.

[028-079] He was entering the inn, for the purpose of making some enquiries; when he saw one of the soldiers cross the road hurriedly, and go into the courtyard, where he was immediately joined by the vetturino.

[028-080] Delmé turned in to the house, and called for the landlord.

[028-081] Before the latter could appear, George rushed into the room.

[028-082] His hat was off--his eyes glared wildly--his long hair streamed back, wet with the dews of night. He dragged with him the body of one of the soldiers; and threw it with supernatural strength into the very centre of the room.

[028-083] "Supper!" said he, "ha, ha, ha! _I_ have brought you supper!"

[028-084] The man was quite dead.

[028-085] The bullet had pierced his neck and throat. The blood was yet flowing, and had dabbled the white vest. His beard and hair were clotted with gore.

[028-086] Shocked as Sir Henry was, the truth flashed on him. He lost not a moment in beckoning to Thompson, and rushing towards the stable. The driver was still there, conversing with the soldier.

[028-087] As Sir Henry approached, they evinced involuntary confusion; and the vetturino---at once unmanned--fell on his knees, and commenced a confession.
