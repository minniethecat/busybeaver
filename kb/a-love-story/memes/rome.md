# Rome

## Overview

Rome appears in the novel's later sections as a destination of cultural and emotional significance. Characters travel toward it: "the best mode of proceeding to Rome" (023-047) is discussed with medical authority, and travelers arrive "from Rome yesterday evening" (024-002) bearing news. The city represents the culmination of the Italian journey that structures much of the novel's second volume.

## Geographic Context

Rome is reached via the Pontine marshes, and "Rome and its environs were represented" (023-036) in the consciousness of travelers as a place of distinctive character. It sits at the end of a journey through Italy, a terminal point laden with historical and personal meaning.

## Role in Narrative

Rome functions as a horizon rather than a fully realized setting — it is the place characters are going toward, the destination discussed and anticipated. The journey "on their way to Rome" (024-033) carries narrative weight as a movement toward culmination.

## Associated Characters

- **Sir Henry Delmé** — His Italian travels bring him toward Rome
- **Doctor Pormont** — Advises on the "best mode of proceeding to Rome" (023-047)
