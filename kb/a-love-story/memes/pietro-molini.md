## Overview

Pietro Molini is a Milanese _vetturino_ — a hired driver — who transports Sir Henry Delmé's party through the Italian peninsula. Introduced in the second volume as the honest counterpart to the disreputable Vittore Santado (029-029–029-031), Pietro embodies rustic integrity in a narrative otherwise dominated by aristocratic concerns. The narrator devotes an extended, elegiac portrait to this "poor simple Pietro" (029-031–029-044), celebrating his modest aspirations, his care for his horses, and his treasured collection of passengers' notes. His friendship with [[thompson|Thompson]] across the barrier of language (029-052) offers one of the novel's most touching depictions of cross-class camaraderie.

## Biography

Pietro Molini enters the narrative at Storta, where Sir Henry — "very particular" in his choice — selects him as the new vetturino to replace the scheming Vittore Santado (029-028). The narrator immediately draws a sharp contrast between the two drivers: Vittore, a "consummate rascal," and Pietro, "the most honest of his tribe" (029-029–029-031).

Pietro is described in vivid, affectionate detail: elderly, gaunt, with a face "scored and furrowed like the surface of the hedge pippin" (029-031). His pleasures are simple — the jingling of his horses' bells, the safety of his passengers, the scraps of paper from grateful travellers that he treasures in his breeches pocket (029-032–029-035). His importance swells each evening as he tends his horses and arranges the next day's departure with his passengers (029-038).

The narrator interrupts Pietro's introduction with a proleptic passage anticipating the driver's death: crossing from Basle to Strasbourg in the depths of winter, Pietro sleeps as usual, trusting in his horses' sure-footedness — but a fragment of rock, loosened from its ancient bed, lies across the road (029-041–029-044). This elegiac foreknowledge colours every subsequent scene of the driver's cheerful service with a tinge of mortality.

During the journey, Pietro forms an unlikely bond with [[thompson|Thompson]], Sir Henry's English servant. Despite their lack of a common language, "he and Thompson soon became staunch allies; and the want of a common language seemed only to cement their union" (029-052). The two servants, seated side by side at the front of the carriage, become a recurring image of cross-cultural fellowship (030-030).

Pietro assists in caring for George during his episodes of illness, helping to carry him to the carriage and administering cordials (031-004). He continues with the party through the Italian leg of their journey, and his satisfaction is such that Sir Henry arranges for his continued service at Florence (030-030).

By the German leg of the journey, Pietro appears on the deck of the steamer at Frankfort-on-the-Maine, "assisting Thompson to arrange the portmanteaux" (036-065). His presence at this transitional moment, as the travelling party prepares to separate, underscores his role as a steady hand through the novel's most turbulent passages.

## Key Scenes

### The Contrast of the Two Vetturini (029-028–029-031)

> There certainly could not be a greater contrast, than between the two Vetturini. (029-029)

The narrator introduces Pietro by juxtaposing him against his predecessor Vittore — a Roman "inclined to corpulency, oily faced, plausible, and a most consummate rascal" — with Pietro, "elderly, with hardly an ounce of flesh on his body, with face scored and furrowed like the surface of the hedge pippin, rough in his manners, and the most honest of his tribe" (029-031). This passage establishes Pietro as a moral anchor in a world of schemers.

### The Extended Portrait (029-031–029-044)

The narrator devotes an unusually long passage to celebrating Pietro's character: his cheerful halloos to his horses, his treasured scraps of passengers' praise, his evening rituals of tending the animals, and the simple contentment that prompts the narrator to declare, "A King might have envied Pietro Molini" (029-039). This extended portrait — rare for a servant character — marks Pietro as a figure of genuine authorial affection.

### The Proleptic Death (029-041–029-044)

> Crossing from Basle to Strasbourg, in the depth of winter, and descending an undulated valley, Pietro slept as usual. Implicitly relying on the sure footedness of his horses, a fond dream of German beer, German tobacco, and German sauerkraut, soothed his slumbers. A fragment of rock had been loosened from its ancient bed, and lay across the road. (029-042–029-044)

The narrator interrupts Pietro's cheerful introduction to anticipate his death — a foreshadowing that lends poignancy to every subsequent moment of the driver's happiness. This technique, looking forward to tragedy amid present contentment, is characteristic of the novel's handling of mortality.

### Alliance with Thompson (029-052, 030-030)

> He and Thompson soon became staunch allies; and the want of a common language seemed only to cement their union. (029-052)

Pietro's friendship with the English servant Thompson, formed without the benefit of a shared tongue, represents one of the novel's most optimistic visions of human connection across barriers of nationality and class.

### Farewell at Frankfurt (036-065)

Pietro appears for the final time on the deck of the steamer, helping Thompson arrange luggage for the departure. His presence at this parting moment — when the travelling companions go their separate ways — provides continuity and a quiet note of faithful service completed.

## Character Analysis

Pietro Molini occupies a unique position in the novel's moral economy. Unlike the aristocratic characters whose virtues and failings are rendered through complex psychological portraiture, Pietro is presented as a type — the honest peasant — yet the narrator's treatment of him is anything but perfunctory. The extended portrait (029-031–029-044) exceeds what strict narrative economy would require, suggesting genuine authorial investment in this minor figure.

The narrator's choice to anticipate Pietro's death within the same passage that introduces him — the rock fragment on the road from Basle to Strasbourg — creates a distinctive tonal effect. Pietro's simple joys (his horses' bells, his passengers' praise, his dreams of German beer) are rendered all the more precious by the reader's knowledge that they are finite. This technique mirrors the novel's broader meditation on mortality and the fragility of happiness — themes more explicitly explored in the [[death|concept of death]] and [[grief]] that permeate the Delmé family's story.

Pietro's friendship with Thompson is particularly significant. In a novel structured around the bonds between brothers (Sir Henry and George), lovers (Sir Henry and Acmé, George and the various objects of his affection), and countrymen, the alliance of an English servant and an Italian driver — forging connection without language — offers a democratic counterpoint to the novel's hierarchical social world. Their silent camaraderie seated side by side on the driver's bench (030-030) is one of the quietest but most resonant images of [[friendship]] in the narrative.
