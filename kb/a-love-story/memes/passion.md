# Passion

## Overview

Passion in *A Love Story* is the intensifier of all other emotions — the force that transforms affection into obsession, sorrow into despair, and duty into fanaticism. The novel treats passion with ambivalence: it is the source of life's highest raptures but also its most dangerous excesses. Characters are measured by their capacity to feel passionately and their ability to govern that feeling.

## Manifestations in Text

### Passion and Love

The novel frequently intertwines passion with love, yet distinguishes them. Love elevated by passion becomes transcendent — a "rapture unalloyed by passion's grosser sense" (009-015). But this very elevation carries risk; passion unchecked becomes "a hopeless passion" (002-048) that consumes the one who harbors it. George Delmé's confession describes "vainly struggling against a passion" (010-001), framing passion as something to be resisted rather than embraced.

### Passion as Vital Force

The novel also celebrates passion as evidence of a fully lived life. The narrator describes the human countenance "struggling in its passion — enraptured in its madness" (011-010), treating passionate expression as the truest revelation of character. Those who feel nothing passionately are, in the novel's moral universe, only half alive.

### The Physicality of Passion

Passion manifests physically throughout the novel: "tones became more impassioned — they breathed of love" (005-009). The voice, the body, the very atmosphere become vehicles for passionate feeling, suggesting that passion cannot be contained within the purely mental.

## Key Passages

> Is not love half fed by thy attractions? pre-eminent passion dost thou not contribute? (002-044)

Passion as the animating force behind love — sensory experience feeds romantic feeling.

> A rapture unalloyed by passion's grosser sense. (009-015)

The ideal of passion purified — intense feeling elevated beyond mere physical desire.

> Whether struggling in its passion — enraptured in its madness. (011-010)

Passion and madness as adjacent states, separated by the thinnest of boundaries.
