# Clarendon Gage

## Overview

Clarendon Gage is introduced as "a neighbouring land proprietor" in the drawing room of Delmé (002-004). His character is explicitly contrasted with Sir Henry's: "Clarendon was very dissimilar" (002-007) to the baronet. Where Sir Henry is grave and duty-bound, Clarendon represents a lighter, more convivial temperament. His courtship of Emily Delmé provides one of the novel's romantic subplots.

## Biography

Clarendon appears first in the social setting of the Delmé drawing room, greeted warmly: "Glad to see you looking so well, Clarendon!" (002-005). He is part of the family's intimate circle, comfortable enough to be addressed by Emily — "Come! Clarendon," said Emily (002-008) — and to "read aloud his first contribution" (002-016) to the group's literary entertainment.

His relationship with Emily develops against the backdrop of family concern. Their marriage is "deferred" (006-024), a delay that creates narrative tension and positions Clarendon as a figure of patient attachment. His presence at the ball and his flight with Emily (005-020) marks him as both romantic lead and social participant.

## Key Scenes

- **Drawing room introduction** (002-004–002-008): Clarendon's character established through contrast and social grace.
- **Reading aloud** (002-016): A moment that reveals his role in the family's cultural life.
- **The ball** (005-020): His romantic flight with Emily.
