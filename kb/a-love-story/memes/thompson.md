## Overview

Thompson is the loyal servant of the [[sir-henry-delme|Delmé family]], serving both Sir Henry Delmé and his brother [[george-delme|George Delmé]] throughout the novel. From his first appearance in England (015-031) to the closing scenes in the Australian colonies (040-018), Thompson embodies the qualities of steadfast [[fidelity]] and quiet [[devotion]] — attributes that the narrator singles out as "dog fidelity" (029-018). Though a secondary character, Thompson's constant presence at pivotal moments — from [[the-funeral|George's funeral]] preparations to [[the-departure|the family's departure from England]] — makes him an indispensable witness to the Delmé family's joys and sorrows.

## Biography

Thompson enters the narrative as Sir Henry Delmé's servant in England, handling practical affairs with quiet competence. When confronted by an insolent creditor at Sir Henry's door, Thompson smoothly protects his master with a discreet signal about the unsuitability of the Arab horse (015-031, 015-033).

During George Delmé's illness, Thompson serves as a go-between, conveying messages from [[acme|Acmé]] about George's condition and receiving instructions from Sir Henry (016-002). He accompanies both brothers to the fateful vault scene, where he supports the staggering Sir Henry and physically seizes the Maltese antagonist (017-006, 017-008).

Thompson takes charge of the logistics for the family's departure voyage, working through the greater part of the night to ready everything (018-009). His care extends to the personal belongings of the household, including [[emily-delme|Emily Delmé's]] guitar case (024-049).

When George falls ill during the Continental journey, Thompson carries him to safety alongside Sir Henry and assists in his care (025-088). Throughout the travels through Italy and Germany, Thompson's devotion to the brothers earns him the narrator's explicit praise as displaying "dog fidelity" (029-018).

Thompson forms an unlikely friendship with [[pietro-molini|Pietro Molini]], the Milanese driver, despite their lack of a common language — a bond the narrator describes as making them "staunch allies" (029-052, 030-030).

In the novel's later chapters, Thompson accompanies Sir Henry through Germany to Frankfurt-on-the-Maine, where they part from their travelling companions (036-063). It is Thompson who gently warns Sir Henry when it is time to leave the vessel for the final time (036-069).

By the novel's conclusion, Thompson has followed Sir Henry to the Australian colonies, reappearing as a "well-appointed groom" who greets his master with respectful familiarity (040-018). He is present for the final homecoming, escorting Sir Henry through the last miles of his journey (041-017).

## Key Scenes

### Confronting the Creditor (015-031–015-033)

Thompson's first appearance showcases his protective discretion. When a creditor confronts Sir Henry, Thompson smoothly intervenes with a quiet sign, fabricating an excuse about the Arab horse being lame to defuse the situation.

### The Vault Scene (016–017)

Thompson accompanies the brothers to the subterranean vault, where he assists Sir Henry in restraining the Maltese antagonist and supporting George during the harrowing scene among the corpses. This is Thompson's most physically active and dramatic sequence.

### Preparing for Departure (018-009)

> The day of departure at length arrived. Thompson had been busy the greater part of the night in getting every thing ready for the voyage.

Thompson's practical devotion is shown through his tireless preparations for the family's departure from England — a scene that exemplifies his quiet, behind-the-scenes service (018-009).

### Caring for George in Italy (025-088, 028–029)

When George falls dangerously ill on the Continent, Thompson carries him alongside Sir Henry and helps restore consciousness (025-088). Throughout the Italian journey, he remains at George's side, earning the narrator's praise for his "dog fidelity" (029-018).

### Alliance with Pietro (029-052, 030-030)

> He and Thompson soon became staunch allies; and the want of a common language seemed only to cement their union. (029-052)

Thompson's friendship with the Milanese driver [[pietro-molini|Pietro Molini]] — two servants from different worlds who bond across the language barrier — offers a subplot of genuine warmth amid the novel's darker themes.

### The Final Homecoming (040-018, 041-017)

In the novel's closing chapters, Thompson reappears as Sir Henry's groom in the colonies, having followed his master across the world. His presence at the final homecoming — "touching his hat respectfully" (040-018) then leading Sir Henry through the last miles — provides a note of faithful continuity to the story's end.

## Character Analysis

Thompson represents the ideal of faithful service in the nineteenth-century novel. Unlike many servant characters of the period who serve merely as comic relief or plot devices, Thompson is given a substantive arc that parallels the Delmé brothers' own journey: he travels with them from England through the Mediterranean and Continental Europe to the Australian colonies, a geographical span that mirrors the novel's thematic breadth.

What distinguishes Thompson is the narrator's choice to name his devotion explicitly: "the sort of dog fidelity displayed by Thompson towards the brothers" (029-018). The phrase "dog fidelity" — at once admiring and condescending — captures the class-bound perspective from which servants were viewed in the period, while also elevating Thompson's loyalty to a quality worthy of narrative attention. His friendship with Pietro Molini across a language barrier (029-052) further humanises him, showing that bonds of loyalty and shared experience transcend the formal master-servant relationship.

Thompson's arc also serves a structural function in the novel. His reappearance in the colonial chapters (040-018) provides continuity between the English and Mediterranean sections of the narrative, anchoring the reader in a familiar presence as the setting shifts dramatically. In this sense, Thompson is not merely a servant but a narrative constant — a figure whose unwavering presence underscores the novel's themes of [[fidelity]] and [[devotion]] across time and distance.
