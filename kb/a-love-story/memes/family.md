# Family

## Overview

The Delmé family is the social and emotional center of *A Love Story*. "An old Norman one" (001-023) whose "family device" — "the eagle with extended wings" (001-022) — adorns their gatherings, the Delmés represent an ancient order of English gentry. At the period of the novel, the family "consisted but of three members" (001-024): Sir Henry, George, and Emily — a compact unit whose bonds of affection, obligation, and shared history drive the narrative.

## Manifestations in Text

### Family as Identity

The novel establishes family as the primary source of identity. The Delmé name carries weight: "the family estates became wholly unencumbered" (001-026) through prudent management, linking family honor to financial responsibility. The "ancient device of her family, reposing on its time worn pedestal" (001-035) connects the present generation to a lineage that shapes their sense of self.

### The Delmé Siblings

The three Delmé siblings — Henry, George, and Emily — form a tight triangle of affection and obligation. Despite their small number, their relationships encompass the full range of familial bonds: protective elder brother (Henry to Emily), fraternal friendship (Henry and George), and shared concern for each other's welfare. The "family party" (004-006) that travels together reinforces the novel's insistence that family is not merely background but the active context of all significant experience.

### Family Beyond Blood

The novel also gestures toward family-like bonds beyond blood. The Vernon family — "a widow, with a large family and small means" (004-015) — provides a counterpoint to the Delmés' security, suggesting that family obligation extends across class lines through the social networks of gentry life.

## Key Passages

> The Delmé family was an old Norman one, on whose antiquity the eulogists of the family dwelt with delight. (001-023)

The novel's foundational statement of familial identity — antiquity conferring legitimacy and weight.

> The family estates became wholly unencumbered. (001-026)

Financial prudence as familial virtue — the Delmé legacy maintained through careful stewardship of "the head of the family" (001-030).

## Related Characters

- **George Delmé** — Brother whose welfare is a constant family concern
- **Emily Delmé** — Sister whose marriage represents the family's continuation
