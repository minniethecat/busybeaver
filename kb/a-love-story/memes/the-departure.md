## Overview

The Departure is a recurring and structurally significant event in _A Love Story_, marking the transitions between the novel's major settings. The most fully rendered departure is the family's embarkation from England for the Mediterranean — a scene in which [[thompson|Thompson]] labours through the night to ready the luggage (018-009), [[acme|Acmé]] parts from her relations (018-011), and the party boards the vessel on "a lovely morning" with a propitious wind (018-010). But departure is also a narrative pattern: Sir Henry prepares to leave Leamington for London (004-036), George prepares to sail from England earlier in the story (006-023), and later the brothers' Continental journey is punctuated by a series of departures (026-013, 027-064) culminating in the final parting at Frankfurt-on-the-Maine (036-063). Across these episodes, departure functions as the novel's primary mechanism of narrative propulsion and emotional transition.

## Context

The departure motif in _A Love Story_ is rooted in the broader nineteenth-century experience of mobility — military service, colonial emigration, and the Grand Tour all required protracted leave-takings that carried the weight of possible permanent separation. For the Delmé family, each departure is shadowed by the possibility of death. Sir Henry's departure from Leamington is prompted by anxiety over George's unknown fate (004-036). George's own earlier departure for the Mediterranean is the event that severed his connection with his English friends and led to his fateful encounter with Acmé (006-021–006-023). The family's collective departure from England (Chapter 18) carries the characters away from everything familiar and into the Mediterranean world where the novel's central tragedies unfold.

The narrator's treatment of departure scenes is characteristically ambivalent: each leave-taking is rendered with attention to practical detail (Thompson's packing, Pietro's arrangements) while also evoking the emotional weight of separation. The departure from Valletta, watching "crowds, of whom not one feels an interest in _his_ fate" (006-030), crystallises the loneliness that underlies the novel's cosmopolitan surface.

## Narrative Significance

The Departure serves three interrelated narrative functions. First, it is a **plot mechanism**: each departure physically moves characters between the novel's settings (England → Mediterranean → Italy → Germany → colonies), enabling the geographical breadth that distinguishes the narrative. Without the repeated motif of departure, the novel would lack its characteristic sweep across Europe and the Mediterranean.

Second, departure functions as an **emotional register**. The narrator consistently uses departure scenes to reveal character: Sir Henry's brisk efficiency in arranging travel contrasts with George's passive acquiescence; Thompson's sleepless night of preparation (018-009) embodies his [[devotion]] to the family; Acmé's calm parting from her relations (018-011) demonstrates her [[fidelity]] to George above all other ties.

Third, departure carries **thematic weight** as a figure for mortality. The "loneliness, almost of despair" that infects "the landsman's mind, as he recedes from an unfamiliar port" and sees "crowds watching listlessly his vessel's departure — crowds, of whom not one feels an interest in _his_ fate" (006-030) prefigures the ultimate departure of death. The novel's many leave-takings — from Leamington, from England, from Valletta, from Rome, from Frankfurt — form a chain of separations that culminates in the final and irrevocable departures of [[death]].
