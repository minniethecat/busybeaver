# Section 26

(026-001) XXXI. When thou seest Satyro, think of Socraticus and Eutyches, or

(026-002) Hymen, and when Euphrates, think of Eutychio, and Sylvanus, when

(026-003) Alciphron, of Tropaeophorus, when Xenophon, of Crito, or Severus. And

(026-004) when thou doest look upon thyself, fancy unto thyself some one or other

(026-005) of the Cæsars; and so for every one, some one or other that hath been

(026-006) for estate and profession answerable unto him. Then let this come to thy

(026-007) mind at the same time; and where now are they all? Nowhere or anywhere?

(026-008) For so shalt thou at all time be able to perceive how all worldly

(026-009) things are but as the smoke, that vanisheth away: or, indeed, mere

(026-010) nothing. Especially when thou shalt call to mind this also, that

(026-011) whatsoever is once changed, shall never be again as long as the world

(026-012) endureth. And thou then, how long shalt thou endure? And why doth it not

(026-013) suffice thee, if virtuously, and as becometh thee, thou mayest pass that

(026-014) portion of time, how little soever it be, that is allotted unto thee?

(026-015) XXXII. What a subject, and what a course of life is it, that thou doest

(026-016) so much desire to be rid of. For all these things, what are they, but

(026-017) fit objects for an [[knowledge|understanding]], that beholdeth everything according to

(026-018) its true nature, to exercise itself upon? Be patient, therefore, until

(026-019) that (as a strong stomach that turns all things into his own nature; and

(026-020) as a great fire that turneth in flame and light, whatsoever thou doest

(026-021) cast into it) thou have made these things also familiar, and as it were

(026-022) natural unto thee.

(026-023) XXXIII. Let it not be in any man's power, to say truly of thee, that

(026-024) thou art not truly simple, or sincere and open, or not good. Let him be

(026-025) deceived whosoever he be that shall have any such opinion of thee. For

(026-026) all this doth depend of thee. For who is it that should hinder thee from

(026-027) being either truly simple or good? Do thou only resolve rather not to

(026-028) live, than not to be such. For indeed neither doth it stand with reason

(026-029) that he should live that is not such. What then is it that may upon this

(026-030) present occasion according to best reason and discretion, either be said

(026-031) or done? For whatsoever it be, it is in thy power either to do it, or

(026-032) to say it, and therefore seek not any pretences, as though thou wert

(026-033) hindered. Thou wilt never cease groaning and complaining, until such

(026-034) time as that, what pleasure is unto the voluptuous, be unto thee, to do

(026-035) in everything that presents itself, whatsoever may be done conformably

(026-036) and agreeably to the proper constitution of man, or, to man as he is a

(026-037) man. For thou must account that pleasure, whatsoever it be, that thou

(026-038) mayest do according to thine own nature. And to do this, every place

(026-039) will fit thee. Unto the _cylindrus_, or roller, it is not granted to

(026-040) move everywhere according to its own proper motion, as neither unto

(026-041) the water, nor unto the fire, nor unto any other thing, that either is

(026-042) merely natural, or natural and sensitive; but not rational for many

(026-043) things there be that can hinder their operations. But of the mind and

(026-044) understanding this is the proper privilege, that according to its own

(026-045) nature, and as it will itself, it can pass through every obstacle that

(026-046) it finds, and keep straight on forwards. Setting therefore before thine

(026-047) eyes this happiness and felicity of thy mind, whereby it is able to pass

(026-048) through all things, and is capable of all motions, whether as the fire,

(026-049) upwards; or as the stone downwards, or as the _cylindrus_ through that

(026-050) which is sloping: content thyself with it, and seek not after any other

(026-051) thing. For all other kind of hindrances that are not hindrances of thy

(026-052) mind either they are proper to the body, or merely proceed from the

(026-053) opinion, reason not making that resistance that it should, but basely,

(026-054) and cowardly [[pain|suffering]] itself to be foiled; and of themselves can

(026-055) neither wound, nor do any hurt at all. Else must he of necessity,

(026-056) whosoever he be that meets with any of them, become worse than he was

(026-057) before. For so is it in all other subjects, that that is thought hurtful

(026-058) unto them, whereby they are made worse. But here contrariwise, man (if

(026-059) he make that good use of them that he should) is rather the better

(026-060) and the more praiseworthy for any of those kind of hindrances, than

(026-061) otherwise. But generally remember that nothing can hurt a natural

(026-062) citizen, that is not hurtful unto the city itself, nor anything hurt

(026-063) the city, that is not hurtful unto the law itself. But none of these

(026-064) casualties, or external hindrances, do hurt the law itself; or, are

(026-065) contrary to that course of justice and equity, by which public societies

(026-066) are maintained: neither therefore do they hurt either city or citizen.

(026-067) XXXIV. As he that is bitten by a mad dog, is afraid of everything almost

(026-068) that he seeth: so unto him, whom the dogmata have once bitten, or in

(026-069) whom true knowledge hath made an impression, everything almost that

(026-070) he sees or reads be it never so short or ordinary, doth afford a good

(026-071) memento; to put him out of all grief and fear, as that of the poet, 'The

(026-072) winds blow upon the trees, and their leaves fall upon the ground. Then

(026-073) do the trees begin to bud again, and by the spring-time they put forth

(026-074) new branches. So is the generation of men; some come into the world, and

(026-075) others go out of it.' Of these leaves then thy children are. And they

(026-076) also that applaud thee so gravely, or, that applaud thy speeches, with

(026-077) that their usual acclamation, ἀξιοπίστως, O wisely spoken I and speak

(026-078) well of thee, as on the other side, they that stick not to curse thee,

(026-079) they that privately and secretly dispraise and deride thee, they also

(026-080) are but leaves. And they also that shall follow, in whose memories

(026-081) the names of men famous after death, is preserved, they are but leaves

(026-082) neither. For even so is it of all these worldly things. Their spring

(026-083) comes, and they are put forth. Then blows the wind, and they go down.

(026-084) And then in lieu of them grow others out of the wood or common matter

(026-085) of all things, like unto them. But, to endure but for a while, is common

(026-086) unto all. Why then shouldest thou so earnestly either seek after these

(026-087) things, or fly from them, as though they should endure for ever? Yet a

(026-088) little while, and thine eyes will be closed up, and for him that carries

(026-089) thee to thy grave shall another mourn within a while after.

(026-090) XXXV. A good eye must be good to see whatsoever is to be seen, and not

(026-091) green things only. For that is proper to sore eyes. So must a good

(026-092) ear, and a good smell be ready for whatsoever is either to be heard,

(026-093) or smelt: and a good stomach as indifferent to all kinds of food, as

(026-094) a millstone is, to whatsoever she was made for to grind. As ready

(026-095) therefore must a sound understanding be for whatsoever shall happen. But

(026-096) he that saith, O that my children might live! and, O that all men might

(026-097) commend me for whatsoever I do! is an eye that seeks after green things;

(026-098) or as teeth, after that which is tender.

(026-099) XXXVI. There is not any man that is so happy in his death, but that some

(026-100) of those that are by him when he dies, will be ready to rejoice at his

(026-101) supposed calamity. Is it one that was virtuous and wise indeed? will

(026-102) there not some one or other be found, who thus will say to himself;

(026-103) 'Well now at last shall I be at rest from this pedagogue. He did not

(026-104) indeed otherwise trouble us much: but I know well enough that in his

(026-105) heart, he did much condemn us.' Thus will they speak of the virtuous.

(026-106) But as for us, alas I how many things be there, for which there be many

(026-107) that glad would be to be rid of us. This therefore if thou shalt think

(026-108) of whensoever thou diest, thou shalt die the more willingly, when thou

(026-109) shalt think with thyself; I am now to depart from that world, wherein

(026-110) those that have been my nearest friends and acquaintances, they whom I

(026-111) have so much suffered for, so often prayed for, and for whom I have

(026-112) taken such care, even they would have me die, hoping that after my death

(026-113) they shall live happier, than they did before. What then should any man

(026-114) desire to continue here any longer? Nevertheless, whensoever thou diest,

(026-115) thou must not be less kind and loving unto them for it; but as before,

(026-116) see them, continue to be their friend, to wish them well, and meekly,

(026-117) and gently to carry thyself towards them, but yet so that on the other

(026-118) side, it make thee not the more unwilling to die. But as it fareth with

(026-119) them that die an easy quick death, whose soul is soon separated from

(026-120) their bodies, so must thy separation from them be. To these had nature

(026-121) joined and annexed me: now she parts us; I am ready to depart, as from

(026-122) friends and kinsmen, but yet without either reluctancy or compulsion.

(026-123) For this also is according to [[nature|Nature]].

(026-124) XXXVII. Use thyself; as often, as thou seest any man do anything,

(026-125) presently (if it be possible) to say unto thyself, What is this man's

(026-126) end in this his action? But begin this course with thyself first of all,

(026-127) and diligently examine thyself concerning whatsoever thou doest.

(026-128) XXXVIII. Remember, that that which sets a man at work, and hath power

(026-129) over the affections to draw them either one way, or the other way, is

(026-130) not any external thing properly, but that which is hidden within every

(026-131) man's dogmata, and opinions: That, that is rhetoric; that is life; that

(026-132) (to speak true) is man himself. As for thy body, which as a vessel, or

(026-133) a case, compasseth thee about, and the many and curious instruments

(026-134) that it hath annexed unto it, let them not trouble thy thoughts. For

(026-135) of themselves they are but as a carpenter's axe, but that they are born

(026-136) with us, and naturally sticking unto us. But otherwise, without the

(026-137) inward cause that hath power to move them, and to restrain them, those

(026-138) parts are of themselves of no more use unto us, than the shuttle is

(026-139) of itself to the weaver, or the pen to the writer, or the whip to the

(026-140) coachman.

(026-141) THE ELEVENTH BOOK

(026-142) I. The natural properties, and privileges of a reasonable soul are: That

(026-143) she seeth herself; that she can order, and compose herself: that

(026-144) she makes herself as she will herself: that she reaps her own fruits

(026-145) whatsoever, whereas plants, trees, unreasonable creatures, what fruit

(026-146) soever (be it either fruit properly, or analogically only) they bear,

(026-147) they bear them unto others, and not to themselves. Again; whensoever,

(026-148) and wheresoever, sooner or later, her life doth end, she hath her own

(026-149) end nevertheless. For it is not with her, as with dancers and players,

(026-150) who if they be interrupted in any part of their action, the whole action

(026-151) must needs be imperfect: but she in what part of time or action soever

(026-152) she be surprised, can make that which she hath in her hand whatsoever it

(026-153) be, complete and full, so that she may depart with that comfort, 'I have

(026-154) lived; neither want I anything of that which properly did belong unto

(026-155) me.' Again, she compasseth the whole world, and penetrateth into the

(026-156) vanity, and mere outside (wanting substance and solidity) of it, and

(026-157) stretcheth herself unto the infiniteness of eternity; and the revolution

(026-158) or restoration of all things after a certain period of time, to the same

(026-159) state and place as before, she fetcheth about, and doth comprehend in

(026-160) herself; and considers withal, and sees clearly this, that neither they

(026-161) that shall follow us, shall see any new thing, that we have not seen,

(026-162) nor they that went before, anything more than we: but that he that is

(026-163) once come to forty (if he have any wit at all) can in a manner (for

(026-164) that they are all of one kind) see all things, both past and future. As

(026-165) proper is it, and natural to the soul of man to love her neighbour, to

(026-166) be true and modest; and to regard nothing so much as herself: which is

(026-167) also the property of the law: whereby by the way it appears, that sound

(026-168) reason and justice comes all to one, and therefore that justice is the

(026-169) chief thing, that reasonable creatures ought to propose unto themselves

(026-170) as their end.
