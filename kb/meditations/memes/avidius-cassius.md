## Overview

Gaius Avidius Cassius was a successful Roman general who served under Marcus Aurelius in the Parthian War and later governed the eastern provinces. In 175 CE, following a false rumor of Marcus's death, Cassius declared himself emperor and raised what Marcus called "the unexpected rising in the east" [001-095]. The rebellion collapsed when his own troops abandoned him.

## The Rebellion

Cassius governed Syria and commanded substantial legions in the eastern empire. When he heard a report — spread by Marcus's wife Faustina, according to some accounts — that Marcus was dying, Cassius proclaimed himself emperor. Marcus responded with restraint: rather than ordering mass reprisals, he expressed a wish to pardon the usurper, saying he would have shown mercy had Cassius survived to face him [002-165].

### Significance in the Meditations

The Cassius episode tested Marcus's Stoic principles under acute political pressure. Faced with a personal betrayal by a trusted commander, Marcus responded not with rage but with philosophical composure — demonstrating the Stoic ideal of responding to injury with justice rather than vengeance.
