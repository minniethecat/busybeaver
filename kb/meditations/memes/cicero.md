## Overview

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was a Roman orator, statesman, and philosophical writer. His letters and rhetorical works formed part of the classical education that Marcus Aurelius received under the guidance of his tutor Marcus Cornelius Fronto.

## Life and Career

Cicero was a leading figure of the late Roman Republic — a skilled orator, consul (63 BCE), and prolific writer whose philosophical works introduced Greek thought to Roman readers. Though not a Stoic himself (he aligned with Academic skepticism), his ethical and political writings influenced later Stoic thought.

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius studied Cicero's works on the recommendation of his tutor Fronto. In their preserved correspondence, Fronto urges the young Marcus to study Cicero's letters, noting they "are all worth reading" and will improve his vocabulary and style. Marcus continued this literary interest even after becoming emperor, still requesting copies of Cicero's Letters from his adviser.

## Influence and Significance

Cicero represented the ideal of Roman eloquence and republican virtue. His fate — executed during the proscriptions of the Second Triumvirate — stood as a cautionary example of the fragility of political liberty, a theme that resonated with the Stoic emphasis on inner freedom regardless of external circumstances.
