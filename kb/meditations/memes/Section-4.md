# Section 4

(004-001) VI. Of Sextus, mildness and the pattern of a family governed with

(004-002) paternal affection; and a purpose to live according to nature: to be

(004-003) grave without affectation: to observe carefully the several dispositions

(004-004) of my friends, not to be offended with idiots, nor unseasonably to set

(004-005) upon those that are carried with the vulgar opinions, with the theorems,

(004-006) and tenets of philosophers: his conversation being an example how a man

(004-007) might accommodate himself to all men and companies; so that though his

(004-008) company were sweeter and more pleasing than any flatterer's cogging and

(004-009) fawning; yet was it at the same time most respected and reverenced: who

(004-010) also had a proper happiness and faculty, rationally and methodically to

(004-011) find out, and set in order all necessary determinations and instructions

(004-012) for a man's life. A man without ever the least appearance of anger, or

(004-013) any other passion; able at the same time most exactly to observe the

(004-014) Stoic _Apathia_, or unpassionateness, and yet to be most tender-hearted:

(004-015) ever of good credit; and yet almost without any noise, or rumour: very

(004-016) learned, and yet making little show.

(004-017) VII. From Alexander the Grammarian, to be un-reprovable myself, and not

(004-018) reproachfully to reprehend any man for a barbarism, or a solecism, or

(004-019) any false pronunciation, but dextrously by way of answer, or testimony,

(004-020) or confirmation of the same matter (taking no notice of the word) to

(004-021) utter it as it should have been spoken; or by some other such close and

(004-022) indirect admonition, handsomely and civilly to tell him of it.

(004-023) VIII. Of [[fronto|Fronto]], to how much envy and fraud and hypocrisy the state of a

(004-024) tyrannous king is subject unto, and how they who are commonly called

(004-025) εὐπατρίδαι, _i.e._ nobly born, are in some sort incapable, or void

(004-026) of natural affection.

(004-027) IX. Of Alexander the Platonic, not often nor without great necessity to

(004-028) say, or to write to any man in a letter, 'I am not at leisure'; nor in

(004-029) this manner still to put off those duties, which we owe to our friends

(004-030) and acquaintances (to every one in his kind) under pretence of urgent

(004-031) affairs.

(004-032) X. Of Catulus, not to contemn any friend's expostulation, though unjust,

(004-033) but to strive to reduce him to his former disposition: freely and

(004-034) heartily to speak well of all my masters upon any occasion, as it is

(004-035) reported of Domitius, and Athenodotus: and to love my children with true

(004-036) affection.

(004-037) XI. From my brother Severus, to be kind and loving to all them of my

(004-038) house and family; by whom also I came to the knowledge of Thrasea and

(004-039) Helvidius, and Cato, and Dio, and Brutus. He it was also that did put me

(004-040) in the first conceit and desire of an equal commonwealth, administered

(004-041) by justice and equality; and of a kingdom wherein should be regarded

(004-042) nothing more than the good and welfare of the subjects. Of him also,

(004-043) to observe a constant tenor, (not interrupted, with any other cares and

(004-044) distractions,) in the study and esteem of philosophy: to be bountiful

(004-045) and liberal in the largest measure; always to hope the best; and to

(004-046) be confident that my friends love me. In whom I moreover observed open

(004-047) dealing towards those whom he reproved at any time, and that his friends

(004-048) might without all doubt or much observation know what he would, or would

(004-049) not, so open and plain was he.

(004-050) XII. From Claudius Maximus, in all things to endeavour to have power

(004-051) of myself, and in nothing to be carried about; to be cheerful and

(004-052) courageous in all sudden chances and accidents, as in sicknesses: to

(004-053) love mildness, and [[temperance|moderation]], and gravity: and to do my business,

(004-054) whatsoever it be, thoroughly, and without querulousness. Whatsoever

(004-055) he said, all men believed him that as he spake, so he thought, and

(004-056) whatsoever he did, that he did it with a good intent. His manner was,

(004-057) never to wonder at anything; never to be in haste, and yet never

(004-058) slow: nor to be perplexed, or dejected, or at any time unseemly, or

(004-059) excessively to laugh: nor to be angry, or suspicious, but ever ready to

(004-060) do good, and to forgive, and to speak truth; and all this, as one that

(004-061) seemed rather of himself to have been straight and right, than ever to

(004-062) have been rectified or redressed; neither was there any man that ever

(004-063) thought himself undervalued by him, or that could find in his heart, to

(004-064) think himself a better man than he. He would also be very pleasant and

(004-065) gracious.

(004-066) XIII. In my father, I observed his meekness; his constancy without

(004-067) wavering in those things, which after a due examination and

(004-068) deliberation, he had determined. How free from all vanity he carried

(004-069) himself in matter of honour and dignity, (as they are esteemed:) his

(004-070) laboriousness and assiduity, his readiness to hear any man, that had

(004-071) aught to say tending to any common good: how generally and impartially

(004-072) he would give every man his due; his skill and knowledge, when rigour

(004-073) or extremity, or when remissness or moderation was in season; how he did

(004-074) abstain from all unchaste love of youths; his moderate condescending to

(004-075) other men's occasions as an ordinary man, neither absolutely requiring

(004-076) of his friends, that they should wait upon him at his ordinary meals,

(004-077) nor that they should of necessity accompany him in his journeys; and

(004-078) that whensoever any business upon some necessary occasions was to be put

(004-079) off and omitted before it could be ended, he was ever found when he

(004-080) went about it again, the same man that he was before. His accurate

(004-081) examination of things in consultations, and patient hearing of others.

(004-082) He would not hastily give over the search of the matter, as one easy to

(004-083) be satisfied with sudden notions and apprehensions. His care to preserve

(004-084) his friends; how neither at any time he would carry himself towards them

(004-085) with disdainful neglect, and grow weary of them; nor yet at any time

(004-086) be madly fond of them. His contented mind in all things, his cheerful

(004-087) countenance, his care to foresee things afar off, and to take order for

(004-088) the least, without any noise or clamour. Moreover how all acclamations

(004-089) and flattery were repressed by him: how carefully he observed all things

(004-090) necessary to the government, and kept an account of the common expenses,

(004-091) and how patiently he did abide that he was reprehended by some for this

(004-092) his strict and rigid kind of dealing. How he was neither a superstitious

(004-093) worshipper of the gods, nor an ambitious pleaser of men, or studious of

(004-094) popular applause; but sober in all things, and everywhere observant of

(004-095) that which was fitting; no affecter of novelties: in those things which

(004-096) conduced to his ease and convenience, (plenty whereof his fortune

(004-097) did afford him,) without pride and bragging, yet with all freedom and

(004-098) liberty: so that as he did freely enjoy them without any anxiety or

(004-099) affectation when they were present; so when absent, he found no want

(004-100) of them. Moreover, that he was never commended by any man, as either a

(004-101) learned acute man, or an obsequious officious man, or a fine orator; but

(004-102) as a ripe mature man, a perfect sound man; one that could not endure to

(004-103) be flattered; able to govern both himself and others. Moreover, how much

(004-104) he did honour all true philosophers, without upbraiding those that were

(004-105) not so; his sociableness, his gracious and delightful conversation, but

(004-106) never unto satiety; his care of his body within bounds and measure,

(004-107) not as one that desired to live long, or over-studious of neatness, and

(004-108) elegancy; and yet not as one that did not regard it: so that through his

(004-109) own care and providence, he seldom needed any inward physic, or outward

(004-110) applications: but especially how ingeniously he would yield to any that

(004-111) had obtained any peculiar faculty, as either eloquence, or the knowledge

(004-112) of the laws, or of ancient customs, or the like; and how he concurred

(004-113) with them, in his best care and endeavour that every one of them might

(004-114) in his kind, for that wherein he excelled, be regarded and esteemed: and

(004-115) although he did all things carefully after the ancient customs of his

(004-116) forefathers, yet even of this was he not desirous that men should take

(004-117) notice, that he did imitate ancient customs. Again, how he was not

(004-118) easily moved and tossed up and down, but loved to be constant, both in

(004-119) the same places and businesses; and how after his great fits of headache

(004-120) he would return fresh and vigorous to his wonted affairs. Again, that

(004-121) secrets he neither had many, nor often, and such only as concerned

(004-122) public matters: his discretion and moderation, in exhibiting of the

(004-123) public sights and shows for the pleasure and pastime of the people: in

(004-124) public buildings. congiaries, and the like. In all these things,

(004-125) having a respect unto men only as men, and to the equity of the things

(004-126) themselves, and not unto the [[fame|glory]] that might follow. Never wont to

(004-127) use the baths at unseasonable hours; no builder; never curious, or

(004-128) solicitous, either about his meat, or about the workmanship, or colour

(004-129) of his clothes, or about anything that belonged to external beauty.

(004-130) In all his conversation, far from all inhumanity, all boldness, and

(004-131) incivility, all greediness and impetuosity; never doing anything with

(004-132) such earnestness, and intention, that a man could say of him, that

(004-133) he did sweat about it: but contrariwise, all things distinctly, as at

(004-134) leisure; without trouble; orderly, soundly, and agreeably. A man might

(004-135) have applied that to him, which is recorded of Socrates, that he knew

(004-136) how to want, and to enjoy those things, in the want whereof, most men

(004-137) show themselves weak; and in the fruition, intemperate: but to hold out

(004-138) firm and constant, and to keep within the compass of true moderation and

(004-139) sobriety in either estate, is proper to a man, who hath a perfect and

(004-140) invincible soul; such as he showed himself in the sickness of Maximus.

(004-141) XIV. From the gods I received that I had good grandfathers, and parents,

(004-142) a good sister, good masters, good domestics, loving kinsmen, almost all

(004-143) that I have; and that I never through haste and rashness transgressed

(004-144) against any of them, notwithstanding that my disposition was such,

(004-145) as that such a thing (if occasion had been) might very well have been

(004-146) committed by me, but that It was the mercy of the gods, to prevent such

(004-147) a concurring of matters and occasions, as might make me to incur this

(004-148) blame. That I was not long brought up by the concubine of my father;

(004-149) that I preserved the flower of my youth. That I took not upon me to be

(004-150) a man before my time, but rather put it off longer than I needed. That

(004-151) I lived under the government of my lord and father, who would take

(004-152) away from me all pride and vainglory, and reduce me to that conceit and

(004-153) opinion that it was not impossible for a prince to live in the court

(004-154) without a troop of guards and followers, extraordinary apparel, such

(004-155) and such torches and statues, and other like particulars of state and

(004-156) magnificence; but that a man may reduce and contract himself almost to

(004-157) the state of a private man, and yet for all that not to become the more

(004-158) base and remiss in those public matters and affairs, wherein power and

(004-159) authority is requisite. That I have had such a brother, who by his own

(004-160) example might stir me up to think of myself; and by his respect and

(004-161) love, [[pleasure|delight]] and please me. That I have got ingenuous children, and

(004-162) that they were not born distorted, nor with any other natural deformity.

(004-163) That I was no great proficient in the study of rhetoric and poetry, and

(004-164) of other faculties, which perchance I might have dwelt upon, if I had

(004-165) found myself to go on in them with success. That I did by times prefer

(004-166) those, by whom I was brought up, to such places and dignities, which

(004-167) they seemed unto me most to desire; and that I did not put them off with

(004-168) hope and expectation, that (since that they were yet but young) I would

(004-169) do the same hereafter. That I ever knew Apollonius and [[rusticus|Rusticus]], and

(004-170) Maximus. That I have had occasion often and effectually to consider and

(004-171) meditate with myself, concerning that life which is according to nature,

(004-172) what the nature and manner of it is: so that as for the gods and such

(004-173) suggestions, helps and inspirations, as might be expected from them,

(004-174) nothing did hinder, but that I might have begun long before to live

(004-175) according to nature; or that even now that I was not yet partaker and

(004-176) in present possession of that life, that I myself (in that I did not

(004-177) observe those inward motions, and suggestions, yea and almost plain and

(004-178) apparent instructions and admonitions of the gods,) was the only cause

(004-179) of it. That my body in such a life, hath been able to hold out so long.

(004-180) That I never had to do with Benedicta and Theodotus, yea and afterwards

(004-181) when I fell into some fits of love, I was soon cured. That having been

(004-182) often displeased with Rusticus, I never did him anything for which

(004-183) afterwards I had occasion to repent. That it being so that my mother was

(004-184) to die young, yet she lived with me all her latter years. That as often

(004-185) as I had a purpose to help and succour any that either were poor, or

(004-186) fallen into some present necessity, I never was answered by my officers

(004-187) that there was not ready money enough to do it; and that I myself never

(004-188) had occasion to require the like succour from any other. That I have

(004-189) such a wife, so obedient, so loving, so ingenuous. That I had choice of

(004-190) fit and able men, to whom I might commit the bringing up of my children.

(004-191) That by dreams I have received help, as for other things, so in

(004-192) particular, how I might stay my casting of blood, and cure my dizziness,

(004-193) as that also that happened to thee in Cajeta, as unto Chryses when he

(004-194) prayed by the seashore. And when I did first apply myself to philosophy,

(004-195) that I did not fall into the hands of some sophists, or spent my time

(004-196) either in reading the manifold volumes of ordinary philosophers, nor in

(004-197) practising myself in the solution of arguments and fallacies, nor dwelt

(004-198) upon the studies of the meteors, and other natural curiosities. All

(004-199) these things without the assistance of the gods, and fortune, could not

(004-200) have been.
