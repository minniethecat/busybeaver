## Overview

Harmony (Greek συμπάθεια / sympatheia, "fellow-feeling, cosmic sympathy") in Stoic philosophy refers to the interconnected unity of all parts of the cosmos. Because the entire universe is pervaded by one divine reason (logos), every part is linked to every other part through a network of mutual influence and coordination. Marcus Aurelius drew on this concept to frame both his cosmology — all things are "as it were in an harmony" — and his ethics — human beings should live in concord with one another and with the whole. (012-098) (011-019)

## In Stoic Cosmology

The doctrine of cosmic sympathy was developed by the early Stoics, particularly [[Chrysippus]], as part of their physical theory. Since the cosmos is a single living organism animated by divine reason (πνεῦμα / pneuma), events in one part affect the whole — just as the condition of one organ affects the entire body. This "natural and unavoidable sympathy" means that rational beings, as parts of the cosmic whole, naturally share in its movements and are affected by its changes. (023-072) Marcus reflects that through the diffusion of the rational principle, all things are linked by "natural sympathy." (009-047) Even the soul, despite its commerce with the body, retains a sympathetic connection to the larger rational order. (016-168) (021-171)

## Harmony Among Human Beings

The cosmic harmony has a direct ethical implication: human beings, as rational parts of the same whole, are naturally made for concord. Marcus compares the parts of the universe to stones in a building — they "agree as it were in an harmony" — and to members of a body that function together as one. (011-016) (011-019) He observes that "harmony itself is but one," meaning that the apparent diversity of things conceals an underlying unity that the wise person should recognize and align with. The good life is one lived in harmony with this cosmic order, "wronging no man" and "living in harmony with friends and family." (033-032) (032-014)

## In the Meditations

For Marcus, the recognition of cosmic harmony is a source of tranquility and moral orientation. He encourages himself to see how all things "which are best" fit together "as it were in an harmony," and to use this perception to cultivate inner harmony: "thou shalt attain unto the harmony, if thou dost use thyself to this practice." (012-098) (013-050) The interconnectedness of all things means that nothing befalls any part that is not for the good of the whole — a Stoic theodicy that Marcus deploys to accept even painful events as consonant with the universal harmony. The practice he recommends is to "cleanse from thy mind whatsoever by sympathy might adhere unto it" — that is, to remain inwardly free from the disturbances that sympathetic connection with external things can introduce. (028-148)
