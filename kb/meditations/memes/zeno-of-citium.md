## Overview

Zeno of Citium was a Greek philosopher born in Cyprus at an uncertain date, who founded the Stoic school of philosophy in Athens around 300 BCE (001-173). His school took its name from the *Stoa Poikile* (Painted Porch) where he taught, and came to be one of the most influential philosophical systems of antiquity (001-182).

## Life and Career

Zeno established his school at the Stoa in Athens, where the movement acquired its name — the "School of the Porch" (001-182, 034-214). As the founding figure, Zeno articulated the core Stoic principle: to live consistently and in conformity with nature (*ὁμολογουμένως τῇ φύσει*) (002-003). His teachings laid the groundwork for the threefold division of Stoic philosophy into Physics, Ethics, and Logic.

The uncompromising rigidity characteristic of Zeno's original formulation was later softened by subsequent Stoic thinkers, most notably in Marcus Aurelius' Meditations, where the sternness of the early school gives way to a more personal and humane practice (002-084).

## Legacy and Influence

Although Zeno himself wrote no surviving works, his founding vision shaped the entire Stoic tradition. The school was systematized by Chrysippus — of whom it was said "But for Chrysippus, there had been no Porch" (001-183) — and later transmitted through Epictetus to Marcus Aurelius. Through the Meditations, Zeno's original insight that virtue consists in living according to nature reached across two millennia.
