# Section 11

(011-001) VII. The form of the Athenians' prayer did run thus: 'O rain, rain, good

(011-002) Jupiter, upon all the grounds and fields that belong to the Athenians.'

(011-003) Either we should not pray at all, or thus absolutely and freely; and not

(011-004) every one for himself in particular alone.

(011-005) VIII. As we say commonly, The physician hath prescribed unto this man,

(011-006) riding; unto another, cold baths; unto a third, to go barefoot: so it

(011-007) is alike to say, The nature of the universe hath prescribed unto this

(011-008) man sickness, or blindness, or some loss, or damage or some such thing.

(011-009) For as there, when we say of a physician, that he hath prescribed

(011-010) anything, our meaning is, that he hath appointed this for that, as

(011-011) subordinate and conducing to health: so here, whatsoever doth happen

(011-012) unto any, is ordained unto him as a thing subordinate unto the fates,

(011-013) and therefore do we say of such things, that they do συμβαίνειν, that

(011-014) is, happen, or fall together; as of square stones, when either in

(011-015) walls, or pyramids in a certain position they fit one another, and

(011-016) agree as it were in an harmony, the masons say, that they do

(011-017) συμβαίνειν; as if thou shouldest say, fall together: so that in the

(011-018) general, though the things be divers that make it, yet the consent or

(011-019) harmony itself is but one. And as the whole world is made up of all the

(011-020) particular bodies of the world, one perfect and complete body, of the

(011-021) same nature that particular bodies; so is the destiny of particular

(011-022) causes and events one general one, of the same nature that particular

(011-023) causes are. What I now say, even they that are mere idiots are not

(011-024) ignorant of: for they say commonly τοῦτο ἔφερεν ἀυτῷ, that is, This

(011-025) his destiny hath brought upon him. This therefore is by the fates

(011-026) properly and particularly brought upon this, as that unto this in

(011-027) particular is by the physician prescribed. These therefore let us

(011-028) accept of in like manner, as we do those that are prescribed unto us

(011-029) our physicians. For them also in themselves shall We find to contain

(011-030) many harsh things, but we nevertheless, in hope of health, and

(011-031) recovery, accept of them. Let the fulfilling and accomplishment of

(011-032) those things which the common nature hath determined, be unto thee as

(011-033) thy health. Accept then, and be pleased with whatsoever doth happen,

(011-034) though otherwise harsh and un-pleasing, as tending to that end, to the

(011-035) health and welfare of the universe, and to Jove's happiness and

(011-036) prosperity. For this whatsoever it be, should not have been produced,

(011-037) had it not conduced to the good of the universe. For neither doth any

(011-038) ordinary particular nature bring anything to pass, that is not to

(011-039) whatsoever is within the sphere of its own proper administration and

(011-040) government agreeable and subordinate. For these two considerations then

(011-041) thou must be well pleased with anything that doth happen unto thee.

(011-042) First, because that for thee properly it was brought to pass, and unto

(011-043) thee it was prescribed; and that from the very beginning by the series

(011-044) and connection of the first causes, it hath ever had a reference unto

(011-045) thee. And secondly, because the good success and perfect welfare, and

(011-046) indeed the very continuance of Him, that is the Administrator of the

(011-047) whole, doth in a manner depend on it. For the whole (because whole,

(011-048) therefore entire and perfect) is maimed, and mutilated, if thou shalt

(011-049) cut off anything at all, whereby the coherence, and contiguity as of

(011-050) parts, so of causes, is maintained and preserved. Of which certain it

(011-051) is, that thou doest (as much as lieth in thee) cut off, and in some

(011-052) sort violently take somewhat away, as often as thou art displeased with

(011-053) anything that happeneth.

(011-054) IX. Be not discontented, be not disheartened, be not out of hope, if

(011-055) often it succeed not so well with thee punctually and precisely to do

(011-056) all things according to the right dogmata, but being once cast off,

(011-057) return unto them again: and as for those many and more frequent

(011-058) occurrences, either of worldly distractions, or human infirmities, which

(011-059) as a man thou canst not but in some measure be subject unto, be not thou

(011-060) discontented with them; but however, love and affect that only which

(011-061) thou dust return unto: a philosopher's life, and proper occupation after

(011-062) the most exact manner. And when thou dust return to thy philosophy,

(011-063) return not unto it as the manner of some is, after play and liberty as

(011-064) it were, to their schoolmasters and pedagogues; but as they that have

(011-065) sore eyes to their sponge and egg: or as another to his cataplasm; or

(011-066) as others to their fomentations: so shalt not thou make it a matter of

(011-067) ostentation at all to obey reason but of ease and comfort. And

(011-068) remember that philosophy requireth nothing of thee, but what thy

(011-069) nature requireth, and wouldest thou thyself desire anything that is

(011-070) not according to nature? for which of these sayest thou; that which is

(011-071) according to nature or against it, is of itself more kind and pleasing?

(011-072) Is it not for that respect especially, that pleasure itself is to so

(011-073) many men's hurt and overthrow, most prevalent, because esteemed commonly

(011-074) most kind, and natural? But consider well whether magnanimity rather,

(011-075) and true liberty, and true simplicity, and equanimity, and holiness;

(011-076) whether these be not most kind and natural? And prudency itself, what

(011-077) more kind and amiable than it, when thou shalt truly consider with

(011-078) thyself, what it is through all the proper objects of thy rational

(011-079) intellectual faculty currently to go on without any fall or stumble?

(011-080) As for the things of the world, their true nature is in a manner so

(011-081) involved with obscurity, that unto many philosophers, and those no

(011-082) mean ones, they seemed altogether incomprehensible, and the Stoics

(011-083) themselves, though they judge them not altogether incomprehensible,

(011-084) yet scarce and not without much difficulty, comprehensible, so that

(011-085) all assent of ours is fallible, for who is he that is infallible in his

(011-086) conclusions? From the nature of things, pass now unto their subjects

(011-087) and matter: how temporary, how vile are they I such as may be in the

(011-088) power and possession of some abominable loose liver, of some common

(011-089) strumpet, of some notorious oppressor and extortioner. Pass from thence

(011-090) to the dispositions of them that thou doest ordinarily converse with,

(011-091) how hardly do we bear, even with the most loving and amiable! that I may

(011-092) not say, how hard it is for us to bear even with our own selves, in such

(011-093) obscurity, and impurity of things: in such and so continual a flux both

(011-094) of the substances and time; both of the motions themselves, and things

(011-095) moved; what it is that we can fasten upon; either to honour, and respect

(011-096) especially; or seriously, and studiously to seek after; I cannot so much

(011-097) as conceive For indeed they are things contrary.

(011-098) X. Thou must comfort thyself in the expectation of thy natural

(011-099) dissolution, and in the meantime not grieve at the delay; but rest

(011-100) contented in those two things. First, that nothing shall happen unto

(011-101) thee, which is not according to the nature of the universe. Secondly,

(011-102) that it is in thy power, to do nothing against thine own proper God, and

(011-103) inward spirit. For it is not in any man's power to constrain thee to

(011-104) transgress against him.

(011-105) XI. What is the use that now at this present I make of my soul? Thus

(011-106) from time to time and upon all occasions thou must put this question to

(011-107) thyself; what is now that part of mine which they call the rational

(011-108) mistress part, employed about? Whose soul do I now properly possess? a

(011-109) child's? or a youth's? a woman's? or a tyrant's? some brute, or some

(011-110) wild beast's soul?

(011-111) XII. What those things are in themselves, which by the greatest part are

(011-112) esteemed good, thou mayest gather even from this. For if a man shall

(011-113) hear things mentioned as good, which are really good indeed, such as are

(011-114) prudence, temperance, justice, fortitude, after so much heard and

(011-115) conceived, he cannot endure to hear of any more, for the word good is

(011-116) properly spoken of them. But as for those which by the vulgar are

(011-117) esteemed good, if he shall hear them mentioned as good, he doth hearken

(011-118) for more. He is well contented to hear, that what is spoken by the

(011-119) comedian, is but familiarly and popularly spoken, so that even the

(011-120) vulgar apprehend the difference. For why is it else, that this offends

(011-121) not and needs not to be excused, when virtues are styled good: but that

(011-122) which is spoken in commendation of wealth, pleasure, or honour, we

(011-123) entertain it only as merrily and pleasantly spoken? Proceed therefore,

(011-124) and inquire further, whether it may not be that those things also which

(011-125) being mentioned upon the stage were merrily, and with great applause of

(011-126) the multitude, scoffed at with this jest, that they that possessed them

(011-127) had not in all the world of their own, (such was their affluence and

(011-128) plenty) so much as a place where to avoid their excrements. Whether, I

(011-129) say, those ought not also in very deed to be much respected, and

(011-130) esteemed of, as the only things that are truly good.

(011-131) XIII. All that I consist of, is either form or matter. No corruption

(011-132) can reduce either of these unto nothing: for neither did I of nothing

(011-133) become a subsistent creature. Every part of mine then will by mutation

(011-134) be disposed into a certain part of the whole world, and that in time

(011-135) into another part; and so _in infinitum;_ by which kind of mutation, I

(011-136) also became what I am, and so did they that begot me, and they before

(011-137) them, and so upwards _in infinitum_. For so we may be allowed to speak,

(011-138) though the age and government of the world, be to some certain periods

(011-139) of time limited, and confined.

(011-140) XIV. [[reason|Reason]], and rational power, are faculties which content themselves

(011-141) with themselves, and their own proper operations. And as for their first

(011-142) inclination and motion, that they take from themselves. But their

(011-143) progress is right to the end and object, which is in their way, as it

(011-144) were, and lieth just before them: that is, which is feasible and

(011-145) possible, whether it be that which at the first they proposed to

(011-146) themselves, or no. For which reason also such actions are termed

(011-147) κατορθώσεις, to intimate the directness of the way, by which they are

(011-148) achieved. Nothing must be thought to belong to a man, which doth not

(011-149) belong unto him as he is a man. These, the event of purposes, are not

(011-150) things required in a man. The nature of man doth not profess any such

(011-151) things. The final ends and consummations of actions are nothing at all

(011-152) to a man's nature. The end therefore of a man, or the _summum bonum_

(011-153) whereby that end is fulfilled, cannot consist in the consummation of

(011-154) actions purposed and intended. Again, concerning these outward worldly

(011-155) things, were it so that any of them did properly belong unto man, then

(011-156) would it not belong unto man, to condemn them and to stand in opposition

(011-157) with them. Neither would he be praiseworthy that can live without them;

(011-158) or he good, (if these were good indeed) who of his own accord doth

(011-159) deprive himself of any of them. But we see contrariwise, that the more a

(011-160) man doth withdraw himself from these wherein external pomp and greatness

(011-161) doth consist, or any other like these; or the better he doth bear with

(011-162) the loss of these, the better he is accounted.

(011-163) XV. Such as thy thoughts and ordinary cogitations are, such will thy

(011-164) mind be in time. For the soul doth as it were receive its tincture from

(011-165) the fancies, and imaginations. Dye it therefore and thoroughly soak it

(011-166) with the assiduity of these cogitations. As for example. Wheresoever

(011-167) thou mayest live, there it is in thy power to live well and happy. But

(011-168) thou mayest live at the Court, there then also mayest thou live well and

(011-169) happy. Again, that which everything is made for, he is also made unto

(011-170) that, and cannot but naturally incline unto it. That which anything

(011-171) doth naturally incline unto, therein is his end. Wherein the end of

(011-172) everything doth consist, therein also doth his good and benefit consist.

(011-173) Society therefore is the proper good of a rational creature. For that we

(011-174) are made for society, it hath long since been demonstrated. Or can any

(011-175) man make any question of this, that whatsoever is naturally worse and

(011-176) inferior, is ordinarily subordinated to that which is better? and that

(011-177) those things that are best, are made one for another? And those things

(011-178) that have souls, are better than those that have none? and of those that

(011-179) have, those best that have rational souls?
