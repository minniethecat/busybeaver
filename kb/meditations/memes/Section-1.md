(001-020) INTRODUCTION

(001-021) MARCUS AURELIUS ANTONINUS was born on April 26, A.D. 121. His real name

(001-022) was M. Annius Verus, and he was sprung of a noble family which claimed

(001-023) descent from Numa, second King of [[rome|Rome]]. Thus the most religious of

(001-024) emperors came of the blood of the most pious of early kings. His father,

(001-025) Annius Verus, had held high office in Rome, and his grandfather, of

(001-026) the same name, had been thrice Consul. Both his parents died young, but

(001-027) Marcus held them in loving remembrance. On his father's death Marcus

(001-028) was adopted by his grandfather, the consular Annius Verus, and there was

(001-029) deep love between these two. On the very first page of his book Marcus

(001-030) gratefully declares how of his grandfather he had learned to be gentle

(001-031) and meek, and to refrain from all anger and passion. The Emperor [[hadrian|Hadrian]]

(001-032) divined the fine character of the lad, whom he used to call not Verus

(001-033) but Verissimus, more Truthful than his own name. He advanced Marcus to

(001-034) equestrian rank when six years of age, and at the age of eight made him

(001-035) a member of the ancient Salian priesthood. The boy's aunt, Annia Galeria

(001-036) [[faustina|Faustina]], was married to [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]], afterwards emperor. Hence it

(001-037) came about that Antoninus, having no son, adopted Marcus, changing his

(001-038) name to that which he is known by, and betrothed him to his daughter

(001-039) Faustina. His education was conducted with all care. The ablest teachers

(001-040) were engaged for him, and he was trained in the strict doctrine of the

(001-041) Stoic philosophy, which was his great [[pleasure|delight]]. He was taught to dress

(001-042) plainly and to live simply, to avoid all softness and luxury. His body

(001-043) was trained to hardihood by wrestling, hunting, and outdoor games; and

(001-044) though his constitution was weak, he showed great personal courage to

(001-045) encounter the fiercest boars. At the same time he was kept from the

(001-046) extravagancies of his day. The great excitement in Rome was the strife

(001-047) of the Factions, as they were called, in the circus. The racing drivers

(001-048) used to adopt one of four colours--red, blue, white, or green--and their

(001-049) partisans showed an eagerness in supporting them which nothing could

(001-050) surpass. Riot and corruption went in the train of the racing chariots;

(001-051) and from all these things Marcus held severely aloof.

(001-052) In 140 Marcus was raised to the consulship, and in 145 his betrothal

(001-053) was consummated by marriage. Two years later Faustina brought him a

(001-054) daughter; and soon after the tribunate and other imperial honours were

(001-055) conferred upon him.

(001-056) Antoninus Pius died in 161, and Marcus assumed the imperial state. He

(001-057) at once associated with himself L. Ceionius Commodus, whom Antoninus had

(001-058) adopted as a younger son at the same time with Marcus, giving him the

(001-059) name of [[lucius-verus|Lucius Aurelius Verus]]. Henceforth the two are colleagues in the

(001-060) empire, the junior being trained as it were to succeed. No sooner was

(001-061) Marcus settled upon the throne than wars broke out on all sides. In

(001-062) the east, Vologeses III. of Parthia began a long-meditated revolt by

(001-063) destroying a whole Roman Legion and invading Syria (162). Verus was sent

(001-064) off in hot haste to quell this rising; and he fulfilled his trust by

(001-065) plunging into drunkenness and debauchery, while the war was left to his

(001-066) officers. Soon after Marcus had to face a more serious danger at home in

(001-067) the coalition of several powerful tribes on the northern frontier. Chief

(001-068) among those were the Marcomanni or Marchmen, the Quadi (mentioned in

(001-069) this book), the Sarmatians, the Catti, the Jazyges. In Rome itself there

(001-070) was pestilence and starvation, the one brought from the east by Verus's

(001-071) legions, the other caused by floods which had destroyed vast quantities

(001-072) of grain. After all had been done possible to allay famine and to supply

(001-073) pressing needs--Marcus being forced even to sell the imperial jewels to

(001-074) find money--both emperors set forth to a struggle which was to continue

(001-075) more or less during the rest of Marcus's reign. During these wars, in

(001-076) 169, Verus died. We have no means of following the campaigns in detail;

(001-077) but thus much is certain, that in the end the Romans succeeded in

(001-078) crushing the barbarian tribes, and effecting a settlement which made the

(001-079) empire more secure. Marcus was himself commander-in-chief, and victory

(001-080) was due no less to his own ability than to his wisdom in choice of

(001-081) lieutenants, shown conspicuously in the case of Pertinax. There were

(001-082) several important battles fought in these campaigns; and one of them has

(001-083) become celebrated for the legend of the Thundering Legion. In a battle

(001-084) against the Quadi in 174, the day seemed to be going in favour of

(001-085) the foe, when on a sudden arose a great storm of thunder and rain the

(001-086) lightning struck the barbarians with terror, and they turned to rout.

(001-087) In later days this storm was said to have been sent in answer to the

(001-088) prayers of a legion which contained many Christians, and the name

(001-089) Thundering Legion should be given to it on this account. The title of

(001-090) Thundering Legion is known at an earlier date, so this part of the story

(001-091) at least cannot be true; but the aid of the storm is acknowledged by one

(001-092) of the scenes carved on Antonine's Column at Rome, which commemorates

(001-093) these wars.

(001-094) The settlement made after these troubles might have been more

(001-095) satisfactory but for an unexpected rising in the east. Avidius Cassius,

(001-096) an able captain who had won [[fame|renown]] in the Parthian wars, was at this

(001-097) time chief governor of the eastern provinces. By whatever means induced,

(001-098) he had conceived the project of proclaiming himself emperor as soon as

(001-099) Marcus, who was then in feeble health, should die; and a report having

(001-100) been conveyed to him that Marcus was dead, Cassius did as he had

(001-101) planned. Marcus, on hearing the news, immediately patched up a peace and

(001-102) returned home to meet this new peril. The emperors great grief was that

(001-103) he must needs engage in the horrors of civil strife. He praised the

(001-104) qualities of Cassius, and expressed a heartfelt wish that Cassius might

(001-105) not be driven to do himself a hurt before he should have the opportunity

(001-106) to grant a free pardon. But before he could come to the east news had

(001-107) come to Cassius that the emperor still lived; his followers fell away

(001-108) from him, and he was assassinated. Marcus now went to the east, and

(001-109) while there the murderers brought the head of Cassius to him; but the

(001-110) emperor indignantly refused their gift, nor would he admit the men to

(001-111) his presence.

(001-112) On this journey his wife, Faustina, died. At his return the emperor

(001-113) celebrated a triumph (176). Immediately afterwards he repaired to

(001-114) Germany, and took up once more the burden of war. His operations were

(001-115) followed by complete success; but the troubles of late years had been

(001-116) too much for his constitution, at no time robust, and on March 17, 180,

(001-117) he died in Pannonia.

(001-118) The good emperor was not spared domestic troubles. Faustina had borne

(001-119) him several children, of whom he was passionately fond. Their innocent

(001-120) faces may still be seen in many a sculpture gallery, recalling with odd

(001-121) effect the dreamy countenance of their father. But they died one by

(001-122) one, and when Marcus came to his own end only one of his sons still

(001-123) lived--the weak and worthless Commodus. On his father's death Commodus,

(001-124) who succeeded him, undid the work of many campaigns by a hasty and

(001-125) unwise peace; and his reign of twelve years proved him to be a ferocious

(001-126) and bloodthirsty tyrant. Scandal has made free with the name of Faustina

(001-127) herself, who is accused not only of unfaithfulness, but of intriguing

(001-128) with Cassius and egging him on to his fatal rebellion, it must be

(001-129) admitted that these charges rest on no sure evidence; and the emperor,

(001-130) at all events, loved her dearly, nor ever felt the slightest qualm of

(001-131) suspicion.

(001-132) As a soldier we have seen that Marcus was both capable and successful;

(001-133) as an administrator he was prudent and conscientious. Although steeped

(001-134) in the teachings of philosophy, he did not attempt to remodel the world

(001-135) on any preconceived plan. He trod the path beaten by his predecessors,

(001-136) seeking only to do his duty as well as he could, and to keep out

(001-137) corruption. He did some unwise things, it is true. To create a compeer

(001-138) in empire, as he did with Verus, was a dangerous innovation which could

(001-139) only succeed if one of the two effaced himself; and under Diocletian

(001-140) this very precedent caused the Roman Empire to split into halves. He

(001-141) erred in his civil administration by too much centralising. But the

(001-142) strong point of his reign was the administration of justice. Marcus

(001-143) sought by-laws to protect the weak, to make the lot of the slaves

(001-144) less hard, to stand in place of father to the fatherless. Charitable

(001-145) foundations were endowed for rearing and educating poor children. The

(001-146) provinces were protected against oppression, and public help was given

(001-147) to cities or districts which might be visited by calamity. The great

(001-148) blot on his name, and one hard indeed to explain, is his treatment

(001-149) of the Christians. In his reign Justin at Rome became a martyr to

(001-150) his faith, and Polycarp at Smyrna, and we know of many outbreaks of

(001-151) fanaticism in the provinces which caused the death of the faithful. It

(001-152) is no excuse to plead that he knew nothing about the atrocities done in

(001-153) his name: it was his duty to know, and if he did not he would have been

(001-154) the first to confess that he had failed in his duty. But from his own

(001-155) tone in speaking of the Christians it is clear he knew them only from

(001-156) calumny; and we hear of no measures taken even to secure that they

(001-157) should have a fair hearing. In this respect Trajan was better than he.

(001-158) To a thoughtful mind such a religion as that of Rome would give small

(001-159) satisfaction. Its legends were often childish or impossible; its

(001-160) teaching had little to do with morality. The Roman religion was in fact

(001-161) of the nature of a bargain: men paid certain sacrifices and rites, and

(001-162) the gods granted their favour, irrespective of right or wrong. In this

(001-163) case all devout souls were thrown back upon philosophy, as they had

(001-164) been, though to a less extent, in Greece. There were under the early

(001-165) empire two rival schools which practically divided the field between

(001-166) them, Stoicism and Epicureanism. The ideal set before each was

(001-167) nominally much the same. The Stoics aspired to ἁπάθεια, the repression

(001-168) of all emotion, and the Epicureans to ἀταραξία, freedom from all

(001-169) disturbance; yet in the upshot the one has become a synonym of stubborn

(001-170) endurance, the other for unbridled licence. With Epicureanism we have

(001-171) nothing to do now; but it will be worth while to sketch the history and

(001-172) tenets of the Stoic sect.

(001-173) Zeno, the founder of Stoicism, was born in Cyprus at some date unknown,

(001-174) but his life may be said roughly to be between the years 350 and 250

(001-175) B.C. Cyprus has been from time immemorial a meeting-place of the East

(001-176) and West, and although we cannot grant any importance to a possible

(001-177) strain of Phoenician blood in him (for the Phœnicians were no

(001-178) philosophers), yet it is quite likely that through Asia Minor he may

(001-179) have come in touch with the Far East. He studied under the cynic

(001-180) Crates, but he did not neglect other philosophical systems. After many

(001-181) years' study he opened his own school in a colonnade in [[athens|Athens]] called

(001-182) the Painted Porch, or Stoa, which gave the Stoics their name. Next to

(001-183) Zeno, the School of the Porch owes most to Chrysippus (280--207 b.c.),

(001-184) who organised Stoicism into a system. Of him it was said,
