# Section 27

(027-001) II. A pleasant song or dance; the Pancratiast's exercise, sports that

(027-002) thou art wont to be much taken with, thou shalt easily contemn; if

(027-003) the harmonious voice thou shalt divide into so many particular sounds

(027-004) whereof it doth consist, and of every one in particular shall ask

(027-005) thyself; whether this or that sound is it, that doth so conquer thee.

(027-006) For thou wilt be ashamed of it. And so for shame, if accordingly thou

(027-007) shalt consider it, every particular motion and posture by itself: and

(027-008) so for the wrestler's exercise too. Generally then, whatsoever it be,

(027-009) besides virtue, and those things that proceed from virtue that thou art

(027-010) subject to be much affected with, remember presently thus to divide

(027-011) it, and by this kind of division, in each particular to attain unto the

(027-012) contempt of the whole. This thou must transfer and apply to thy whole

(027-013) life also.

(027-014) III. That soul which is ever ready, even now presently (if need be) from

(027-015) the body, whether by way of extinction, or dispersion, or continuation

(027-016) in another place and estate to be separated, how blessed and happy is

(027-017) it! But this readiness of it, it must proceed, not from an obstinate and

(027-018) peremptory resolution of the mind, violently and passionately set upon

(027-019) Opposition, as Christians are wont; but from a peculiar judgment; with

(027-020) discretion and gravity, so that others may be persuaded also and drawn

(027-021) to the like example, but without any noise and passionate exclamations.

(027-022) IV. Have I done anything charitably? then am I benefited by it. See

(027-023) that this upon all occasions may present itself unto thy mind, and never

(027-024) cease to think of it. What is thy profession? to be good. And how should

(027-025) this be well brought to pass, but by certain theorems and doctrines;

(027-026) some Concerning the nature of the universe, and some Concerning the

(027-027) proper and particular constitution of man?

(027-028) V. Tragedies were at first brought in and instituted, to put men in mind

(027-029) of worldly chances and casualties: that these things in the ordinary

(027-030) course of nature did so happen: that men that were much pleased and

(027-031) delighted by such accidents upon this stage, would not by the same

(027-032) things in a greater stage be grieved and afflicted: for here you see

(027-033) what is the end of all such things; and that even they that cry out

(027-034) so mournfully to Cithaeron, must bear them for all their cries and

(027-035) exclamations, as well as others. And in very truth many good things are

(027-036) spoken by these poets; as that (for example) is an excellent passage:

(027-037) 'But if so be that I and my two children be neglected by the Gods, they

(027-038) have some reason even for that,' &c. And again, 'It will but little

(027-039) avail thee to storm and rage against the things themselves,' &c. Again,

(027-040) 'To reap one's life, as a ripe ear of corn;' and whatsoever else is

(027-041) to be found in them, that is of the same kind. After the tragedy, the

(027-042) ancient comedy was brought in, which had the liberty to inveigh against

(027-043) personal vices; being therefore through this her freedom and liberty

(027-044) of speech of very good use and effect, to restrain men from pride

(027-045) and arrogancy. To which end it was, that Diogenes took also the same

(027-046) liberty. After these, what were either the Middle, or New Comedy

(027-047) admitted for, but merely, (Or for the most part at least) for the

(027-048) [[pleasure|delight]] and pleasure of curious and excellent imitation? 'It will steal

(027-049) away; look to it,' &c. Why, no man denies, but that these also have some

(027-050) good things whereof that may be one: but the whole drift and foundation

(027-051) of that kind of dramatical poetry, what is it else, but as we have said?

(027-052) VI. How clearly doth it appear unto thee, that no other course of thy

(027-053) life could fit a true philosopher's practice better, than this very

(027-054) course, that thou art now already in?

(027-055) VII. A branch cut off from the continuity of that which was next unto

(027-056) it, must needs be cut off from the whole tree: so a man that is divided

(027-057) from another man, is divided from the whole society. A branch is cut off

(027-058) by another, but he that hates and is averse, cuts himself off from his

(027-059) neighbour, and knows not that at the same time he divides himself from

(027-060) the whole body, or corporation. But herein is the gift and mercy of God,

(027-061) the Author of this society, in that, once cut off we may grow together

(027-062) and become part of the whole again. But if this happen often the misery

(027-063) is that the further a man is run in this division, the harder he is to

(027-064) be reunited and restored again: and however the branch which, once cut

(027-065) of afterwards was graffed in, gardeners can tell you is not like that

(027-066) which sprouted together at first, and still continued in the unity of

(027-067) the body.

(027-068) VIII. To grow together like fellow branches in matter of good

(027-069) correspondence and affection; but not in matter of opinions. They that

(027-070) shall oppose thee in thy right courses, as it is not in their power to

(027-071) divert thee from thy good action, so neither let it be to divert thee

(027-072) from thy good affection towards them. But be it thy care to keep thyself

(027-073) constant in both; both in a right judgment and action, and in true

(027-074) meekness towards them, that either shall do their endeavour to hinder

(027-075) thee, or at least will be displeased with thee for what thou hast done.

(027-076) For to fail in either (either in the one to give over for fear, or in

(027-077) the other to forsake thy natural affection towards him, who by nature is

(027-078) both thy friend and thy kinsman) is equally base, and much savouring of

(027-079) the disposition of a cowardly fugitive soldier.

(027-080) IX. It is not possible that any nature should be inferior unto art,

(027-081) since that all arts imitate nature. If this be so; that the most perfect

(027-082) and general nature of all natures should in her operation come short of

(027-083) the skill of arts, is most improbable. Now common is it to all arts, to

(027-084) make that which is worse for the better's sake. Much more then doth the

(027-085) common nature do the same. Hence is the first ground of justice. From

(027-086) justice all other virtues have their existence. For justice cannot be

(027-087) preserved, if either we settle our minds and affections upon worldly

(027-088) things; or be apt to be deceived, or rash, and inconstant.

(027-089) X. The things themselves (which either to get or to avoid thou art put

(027-090) to so much trouble) come not unto thee themselves; but thou in a manner

(027-091) goest unto them. Let then thine own judgment and opinion concerning

(027-092) those things be at rest; and as for the things themselves, they stand

(027-093) still and quiet, without any noise or stir at all; and so shall all

(027-094) pursuing and flying cease.

(027-095) XI. Then is the soul as Empedocles doth liken it, like unto a sphere or

(027-096) globe, when she is all of one form and figure: when she neither greedily

(027-097) stretcheth out herself unto anything, nor basely contracts herself, or

(027-098) lies flat and dejected; but shineth all with light, whereby she does see

(027-099) and behold the true nature, both that of the universe, and her own in

(027-100) particular.

(027-101) XII. Will any contemn me? let him look to that, upon what grounds he

(027-102) does it: my care shall be that I may never be found either doing or

(027-103) speaking anything that doth truly deserve contempt. Will any hate me?

(027-104) let him look to that. I for my part will be kind and loving unto all,

(027-105) and even unto him that hates me, whom-soever he be, will I be ready to

(027-106) show his error, not by way of exprobation or ostentation of my patience,

(027-107) but ingenuously and meekly: such as was that famous Phocion, if so be

(027-108) that he did not dissemble. For it is inwardly that these things must be:

(027-109) that the Gods who look inwardly, and not upon the outward appearance,

(027-110) may behold a man truly free from all indignation and grief. For what

(027-111) hurt can it be unto thee whatsoever any man else doth, as long as thou

(027-112) mayest do that which is proper and suitable to thine own nature? Wilt

(027-113) not thou (a man wholly appointed to be both what, and as the common good

(027-114) shall require) accept of that which is now seasonable to the nature

(027-115) of the universe?

(027-116) XIII. They contemn one another, and yet they seek to please one another:

(027-117) and whilest they seek to surpass one another in worldly pomp and

(027-118) greatness, they most debase and prostitute themselves in their better

(027-119) part one to another.

(027-120) XIV. How rotten and insincere is he, that saith, I am resolved to carry

(027-121) myself hereafter towards you with all ingenuity and simplicity. O man,

(027-122) what doest thou mean! what needs this profession of thine? the thing

(027-123) itself will show it. It ought to be written upon thy forehead. No sooner

(027-124) thy voice is heard, than thy countenance must be able to show what is in

(027-125) thy mind: even as he that is loved knows presently by the looks of his

(027-126) sweetheart what is in her mind. Such must he be for all the world, that

(027-127) is truly simple and good, as he whose arm-holes are offensive, that

(027-128) whosoever stands by, as soon as ever he comes near him, may as it were

(027-129) smell him whether he will or no. But the affectation of simplicity

(027-130) is nowise laudable. There is nothing more shameful than perfidious

(027-131) friendship. Above all things, that must be avoided. However true

(027-132) goodness, simplicity, and kindness cannot so be hidden, but that as

(027-133) we have already said in the very eyes and countenance they will show

(027-134) themselves.

(027-135) XV. To live happily is an inward power of the soul, when she is affected

(027-136) with indifferency, towards those things that are by their nature

(027-137) indifferent. To be thus affected she must consider all worldly objects

(027-138) both divided and whole: remembering withal that no object can of itself

(027-139) beget any opinion in us, neither can come to us, but stands without

(027-140) still and quiet; but that we ourselves beget, and as it were print in

(027-141) ourselves opinions concerning them. Now it is in our power, not to print

(027-142) them; and if they creep in and lurk in some corner, it is in our

(027-143) power to wipe them off. Remembering moreover, that this care and

(027-144) circumspection of thine, is to continue but for a while, and then thy

(027-145) life will be at an end. And what should hinder, but that thou mayest do

(027-146) well with all these things? For if they be according to nature, rejoice

(027-147) in them, and let them be pleasing and acceptable unto thee. But if

(027-148) they be against nature, seek thou that which is according to thine own

(027-149) nature, and whether it be for thy credit or no, use all possible speed

(027-150) for the attainment of it: for no man ought to be blamed, for seeking his

(027-151) own good and happiness.

(027-152) XVI. Of everything thou must consider from whence it came, of what

(027-153) things it doth consist, and into what it will be changed: what will be

(027-154) the nature of it, or what it will be like unto when it is changed; and

(027-155) that it can suffer no hurt by this change. And as for other men's either

(027-156) foolishness or wickedness, that it may not trouble and grieve thee;

(027-157) first generally thus; What reference have I unto these? and that we are

(027-158) all born for one another's good: then more particularly after another

(027-159) consideration; as a ram is first in a flock of sheep, and a bull in a

(027-160) herd of cattle, so am I born to rule over them. Begin yet higher, even

(027-161) from this: if atoms be not the beginning of all things, than which to

(027-162) believe nothing can be more absurd, then must we needs grant that there

(027-163) is a nature, that doth govern the universe. If such a nature, then are

(027-164) all worse things made for the better's sake; and all better for one

(027-165) another's sake. Secondly, what manner of men they be, at board, and upon

(027-166) their beds, and so forth. But above all things, how they are forced by

(027-167) their opinions that they hold, to do what they do; and even those things

(027-168) that they do, with what pride and self-conceit they do them. Thirdly,

(027-169) that if they do these things rightly, thou hast no reason to be grieved.

(027-170) But if not rightly, it must needs be that they do them against their

(027-171) wills, and through mere ignorance. For as, according to Plato's opinion,

(027-172) no soul doth willingly err, so by consequent neither doth it anything

(027-173) otherwise than it ought, but against her will. Therefore are they

(027-174) grieved, whensoever they hear themselves charged, either of injustice,

(027-175) or unconscionableness, or covetousness, or in general, of any injurious

(027-176) kind of dealing towards their neighbours. Fourthly, that thou thyself

(027-177) doest transgress in many things, and art even such another as they are.

(027-178) And though perchance thou doest forbear the very act of some sins, yet

(027-179) hast thou in thyself an habitual disposition to them, but that either

(027-180) through fear, or vainglory, or some such other ambitious foolish

(027-181) respect, thou art restrained. Fifthly, that whether they have sinned or

(027-182) no, thou doest not understand perfectly. For many things are done by

(027-183) way of discreet policy; and generally a man must know many things

(027-184) first, before he be able truly and judiciously to judge of another

(027-185) man's action. Sixthly, that whensoever thou doest take on grievously, or

(027-186) makest great woe, little doest thou remember then that a man's life is

(027-187) but for a moment of time, and that within a while we shall all be in our

(027-188) graves. Seventhly, that it is not the sins and transgressions themselves

(027-189) that trouble us properly; for they have their existence in their

(027-190) minds and understandings only, that commit them; but our own opinions

(027-191) concerning those sins. Remove then, and be content to part with that

(027-192) conceit of thine, that it is a grievous thing, and thou hast removed

(027-193) thine anger. But how should I remove it? How? reasoning with thyself

(027-194) that it is not shameful. For if that which is shameful, be not the only

(027-195) true evil that is, thou also wilt be driven whilest thou doest follow

(027-196) the common instinct of nature, to avoid that which is evil, to commit

(027-197) many unjust things, and to become a thief, and anything, that will

(027-198) make to the attainment of thy intended worldly ends. Eighthly, how many

(027-199) things may and do oftentimes follow upon such fits of anger and grief;

(027-200) far more grievous in themselves, than those very things which we are so

(027-201) grieved or angry for. Ninthly, that meekness is a thing unconquerable,

(027-202) if it be true and natural, and not affected or hypocritical. For how

(027-203) shall even the most fierce and malicious that thou shalt conceive, be

(027-204) able to hold on against thee, if thou shalt still continue meek and

(027-205) loving unto him; and that even at that time, when he is about to do

(027-206) thee wrong, thou shalt be well disposed, and in good temper, with all

(027-207) meekness to teach him, and to instruct him better? As for example; My

(027-208) son, we were not born for this, to hurt and annoy one another; it will

(027-209) be thy hurt not mine, my son: and so to show him forcibly and fully,

(027-210) that it is so in very deed: and that neither bees do it one to another,

(027-211) nor any other creatures that are naturally sociable. But this thou must

(027-212) do, not scoffingly, not by way of exprobation, but tenderly without

(027-213) any harshness of words. Neither must thou do it by way of exercise, or

(027-214) ostentation, that they that are by and hear thee, may admire thee: but

(027-215) so always that nobody be privy to it, but himself alone: yea, though

(027-216) there be more present at the same time. These nine particular heads, as

(027-217) so many gifts from the Muses, see that thou remember well: and begin one

(027-218) day, whilest thou art yet alive, to be a man indeed. But on the other

(027-219) side thou must take heed, as much to flatter them, as to be angry with

(027-220) them: for both are equally uncharitable, and equally hurtful. And in thy

(027-221) passions, take it presently to thy consideration, that to be angry is

(027-222) not the part of a man, but that to be meek and gentle, as it savours of

(027-223) more humanity, so of more manhood. That in this, there is strength

(027-224) and nerves, or vigour and fortitude: whereof anger and indignation is

(027-225) altogether void. For the nearer everything is unto unpassionateness,

(027-226) the nearer it is unto power. And as grief doth proceed from weakness,

(027-227) so doth anger. For both, both he that is angry and that grieveth, have

(027-228) received a wound, and cowardly have as it were yielded themselves unto

(027-229) their affections. If thou wilt have a tenth also, receive this tenth

(027-230) gift from Hercules the guide and leader of the Muses: that is a mad

(027-231) man's part, to look that there should be no wicked men in the world,

(027-232) because it is impossible. Now for a man to brook well enough, that there

(027-233) should be wicked men in the world, but not to endure that any

(027-234) should transgress against himself, is against all equity, and indeed

(027-235) tyrannical.
