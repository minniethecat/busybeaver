# Section 24

(024-001) III. Whatsoever doth happen unto thee, thou art naturally by thy natural

(024-002) constitution either able, or not able to bear. If thou beest able, be

(024-003) not offended, but bear it according to thy natural constitution, or as

(024-004) nature hath enabled thee. If thou beest not able, be not offended. For

(024-005) it will soon make an end of thee, and itself, (whatsoever it be) at the

(024-006) same time end with thee. But remember, that whatsoever by the strength

(024-007) of opinion, grounded upon a certain apprehension of both true profit and

(024-008) duty, thou canst conceive tolerable; that thou art able to bear that by

(024-009) thy natural constitution.

(024-010) IV. Him that offends, to teach with love and meek ness, and to show him

(024-011) his error. But if thou canst not, then to blame thyself; or rather not

(024-012) thyself neither, if thy will and endeavours have not been wanting.

(024-013) V. Whatsoever it be that happens unto thee, it is that which from all

(024-014) time was appointed unto thee. For by the same coherence of causes, by

(024-015) which thy substance from all eternity was appointed to be, was also

(024-016) whatsoever should happen unto it, destinated and appointed.

(024-017) VI. Either with Epicurus, we must fondly imagine the atoms to be the

(024-018) cause of all things, or we must needs grant a nature. Let this then be

(024-019) thy first ground, that thou art part of that universe, which is governed

(024-020) by nature. Then secondly, that to those parts that are of the same kind

(024-021) and nature as thou art, thou hast relation of kindred. For of these,

(024-022) if I shall always be mindful, first as I am a part, I shall never be

(024-023) displeased with anything, that falls to my particular share of the

(024-024) common chances of the world. For nothing that is behoveful unto the

(024-025) whole, can be truly hurtful to that which is part of it. For this

(024-026) being the common privilege of all natures, that they contain nothing in

(024-027) themselves that is hurtful unto them; it cannot be that the nature of

(024-028) the universe (whose privilege beyond other particular natures, is,

(024-029) that she cannot against her will by any higher external cause be

(024-030) constrained,) should beget anything and cherish it in her bosom that

(024-031) should tend to her own hurt and prejudice. As then I bear in mind that

(024-032) I am a part of such an universe, I shall not be displeased with anything

(024-033) that happens. And as I have relation of kindred to those parts that

(024-034) are of the same kind and nature that I am, so I shall be careful to

(024-035) do nothing that is prejudicial to the community, but in all my

(024-036) deliberations shall they that are of my kind ever be; and the common

(024-037) good, that, which all my intentions and resolutions shall drive unto,

(024-038) as that which is contrary unto it, I shall by all means endeavour to

(024-039) prevent and avoid. These things once so fixed and concluded, as thou

(024-040) wouldst think him a happy citizen, whose constant study and practice

(024-041) were for the good and benefit of his fellow citizens, and the carriage

(024-042) of the city such towards him, that he were well pleased with it; so must

(024-043) it needs be with thee, that thou shalt live a happy life.

(024-044) VII. All parts of the world, (all things I mean that are contained

(024-045) within the whole world), must of necessity at some time or other come to

(024-046) corruption. Alteration I should say, to speak truly and properly; but

(024-047) that I may be the better understood, I am content at this time to use

(024-048) that more common word. Now say I, if so be that this be both hurtful

(024-049) unto them, and yet unavoidable, would not, thinkest thou, the whole

(024-050) itself be in a sweet case, all the parts of it being subject to

(024-051) alteration, yea and by their making itself fitted for corruption, as

(024-052) consisting of things different and contrary? And did nature then either

(024-053) of herself thus project and purpose the affliction and misery of her

(024-054) parts, and therefore of purpose so made them, not only that haply they

(024-055) might, but of necessity that they should fall into evil; or did not she

(024-056) know what she did, when she made them? For either of these two to say,

(024-057) is equally absurd. But to let pass nature in general, and to reason of

(024-058) things particular according to their own particular natures; how absurd

(024-059) and ridiculous is it, first to say that all parts of the whole are, by

(024-060) their proper natural constitution, subject to alteration; and then when

(024-061) any such thing doth happen, as when one doth fall sick and dieth, to

(024-062) take on and wonder as though some strange thing had happened? Though

(024-063) this besides might move not so grievously to take on when any such thing

(024-064) doth happen, that whatsoever is dissolved, it is dissolved into those

(024-065) things, whereof it was compounded. For every dissolution is either

(024-066) a mere dispersion, of the elements into those elements again whereof

(024-067) everything did consist, or a change, of that which is more solid into

(024-068) earth; and of that which is pure and subtile or spiritual, into air.

(024-069) So that by this means nothing is lost, but all resumed again into those

(024-070) rational generative seeds of the universe; and this universe, either

(024-071) after a certain period of time to lie consumed by fire, or by continual

(024-072) changes to be renewed, and so for ever to endure. Now that solid and

(024-073) spiritual that we speak of, thou must not conceive it to be that very

(024-074) same, which at first was, when thou wert born. For alas! all this that

(024-075) now thou art in either kind, either for matter of substance, or of life,

(024-076) hath but two or three days ago partly from meats eaten, and partly from

(024-077) air breathed in, received all its influx, being the same then in no

(024-078) other respect, than a running river, maintained by the perpetual influx

(024-079) and new supply of waters, is the same. That therefore which thou hast

(024-080) since received, not that which came from thy mother, is that which

(024-081) comes to change and corruption. But suppose that that for the general

(024-082) substance, and more solid part of it, should still cleave unto thee

(024-083) never so close, yet what is that to the proper qualities and affections

(024-084) of it, by which persons are distinguished, which certainly are quite

(024-085) different?

(024-086) VIII. Now that thou hast taken these names upon thee of good, modest,

(024-087) true; of ἔμφρων, σύμφρων, ὑπέρφρων; take heed lest at any times by

(024-088) doing anything that is contrary, thou be but improperly so called, and

(024-089) lose thy right to these appellations. Or if thou do, return unto them

(024-090) again with all possible speed. And remember, that the word ἔμφρων notes

(024-091) unto thee an intent and intelligent consideration of every object that

(024-092) presents itself unto thee, without distraction. And the word σύμφρων, a

(024-093) ready and contented acceptation of whatsoever by the appointment of the

(024-094) common nature, happens unto thee. And the word ὑπέρφρων, a

(024-095) super-extension, or a transcendent, and outreaching disposition of thy

(024-096) mind, whereby it passeth by all bodily pains and pleasures, honour and

(024-097) credit, death and whatsoever is of the same nature, as matters of

(024-098) absolute indifferency, and in no wise to be stood upon by a wise man.

(024-099) These then if inviolably thou shalt observe, and shalt not be ambitious

(024-100) to be so called by others, both thou thyself shalt become a new man,

(024-101) and thou shalt begin a new life. For to continue such as hitherto thou

(024-102) hast been, to undergo those distractions and distempers as thou must

(024-103) needs for such a life as hitherto thou hast lived, is the part of one

(024-104) that is very foolish, and is overfond of his life. Whom a man might

(024-105) compare to one of those half-eaten wretches, matched in the

(024-106) amphitheatre with wild beasts; who as full as they are all the body

(024-107) over with wounds and blood, desire for a great favour, that they may be

(024-108) reserved till the next day, then also, and in the same estate to be

(024-109) exposed to the same nails and teeth as before. Away therefore, ship

(024-110) thyself; and from the troubles and distractions of thy former life

(024-111) convey thyself as it were unto these few names; and if thou canst abide

(024-112) in them, or be constant in the practice and possession of them,

(024-113) continue there as glad and joyful as one that were translated unto some

(024-114) such place of bliss and happiness as that which by Hesiod and Plato is

(024-115) called the Islands of the Blessed, by others called the Elysian Fields.

(024-116) And whensoever thou findest thyself; that thou art in danger of a

(024-117) relapse, and that thou art not able to master and overcome those

(024-118) difficulties and temptations that present themselves in thy present

(024-119) station: get thee into any private corner, where thou mayst be better

(024-120) able. Or if that will not serve forsake even thy life rather. But so

(024-121) that it be not in passion but in a plain voluntary modest way: this

(024-122) being the only commendable action of thy whole life that thus thou art

(024-123) departed, or this having been the main work and business of thy whole

(024-124) life, that thou mightest thus depart. Now for the better remembrance of

(024-125) those names that we have spoken of, thou shalt find it a very good

(024-126) help, to remember the Gods as often as may be: and that, the thing

(024-127) which they require at our hands of as many of us, as are by nature

(024-128) reasonable creation is not that with fair words, and outward show of

(024-129) piety and devotion we should flatter them, but that we should become

(024-130) like unto them: and that as all other natural creatures, the fig tree

(024-131) for example; the dog the bee: both do, all of them, and apply

(024-132) themselves unto that which by their natural constitution, is proper

(024-133) unto them; so man likewise should do that, which by his nature, as he

(024-134) is a man, belongs unto him.

(024-135) IX. Toys and fooleries at home, wars abroad: sometimes terror, sometimes

(024-136) torpor, or stupid sloth: this is thy daily slavery. By little and

(024-137) little, if thou doest not better look to it, those sacred dogmata will

(024-138) be blotted out of thy mind. How many things be there, which when as

(024-139) a mere naturalist, thou hast barely considered of according to their

(024-140) nature, thou doest let pass without any further use? Whereas thou

(024-141) shouldst in all things so join action and contemplation, that thou

(024-142) mightest both at the same time attend all present occasions, to perform

(024-143) everything duly and carefully and yet so intend the contemplative part

(024-144) too, that no part of that [[pleasure|delight]] and pleasure, which the contemplative

(024-145) knowledge of everything according to its true nature doth of itself

(024-146) afford, might be lost. Or, that the true and contemnplative knowledge

(024-147) of everything according to its own nature, might of itself, (action

(024-148) being subject to many lets and impediments) afford unto thee sufficient

(024-149) pleasure and happiness. Not apparent indeed, but not concealed. And when

(024-150) shalt thou attain to the happiness of true simplicity, and unaffected

(024-151) gravity? When shalt thou rejoice in the certain knowledge of every

(024-152) particular object according to its true nature: as what the matter and

(024-153) substance of it is; what use it is for in the world: how long it can

(024-154) subsist: what things it doth consist of: who they be that are capable of

(024-155) it, and who they that can give it, and take it away?

(024-156) X. As the spider, when it hath caught the fly that it hunted after, is

(024-157) not little proud, nor meanly conceited of herself: as he likewise that

(024-158) hath caught an hare, or hath taken a fish with his net: as another for

(024-159) the taking of a boar, and another of a bear: so may they be proud,

(024-160) and applaud themselves for their valiant acts against the Sarmatai, or

(024-161) northern nations lately defeated. For these also, these famous soldiers

(024-162) and warlike men, if thou dost look into their minds and opinions, what

(024-163) do they for the most part but hunt after prey?
