## Overview

Nero was the fifth and last Julio-Claudian emperor, whose reign descended into tyranny, extravagance, and persecution. In the *Meditations*, Marcus Aurelius uses Nero as a benchmark of unrestrained passion and cruelty — the opposite of the Stoic ruler who governs by reason and justice (007-142).

## Life and Career

Nero came to power at age 16 and ruled for fourteen years. His reign began with promise but deteriorated into notorious excess, including the murder of his mother Agrippina, the Great Fire of Rome (64 CE), and the persecution of Christians. He was declared a public enemy by the Senate and committed suicide in 68 CE.

## Role in the Meditations

Marcus cites Nero in a passage on the mastery of passion. He observes that to be driven by "the lusts and desires of the soul" is the condition of "wild beasts and monsters, such as Phalaris and Nero were" (007-142). This judgment reflects the Stoic conviction that the tyrant is not powerful but enslaved — ruled by passions rather than ruling himself. Nero also appears by implication in Marcus's praise of his own adoptive family: the Antonine dynasty defined itself against the memory of Neronian tyranny. The philosopher Thrasea Paetus and Helvidius Priscus, whom Marcus admires for their integrity (004-026), were both executed under Nero (034-127, 034-223).

## Legacy and Influence

For the Stoics, and for Marcus in particular, Nero embodied the proof that absolute power without philosophical discipline leads to self-destruction. The contrast between Nero and the Antonine emperors — especially Antoninus Pius — is a recurring subtext of the *Meditations*.
