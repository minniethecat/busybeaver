## Overview

The Good (Greek τὸ ἀγαθόν / _to agathon_, Latin _summum bonum_) is the central ethical concept of Stoicism. The Stoics made the radical claim that virtue alone is good and vice alone is evil. Everything else — health, wealth, reputation, even life itself — belongs to the class of "indifferents" (ἀδιάφορα). (002-040) (002-044)

## The Highest Good

The Stoic ethical system identifies the highest good with the virtuous life. Virtue alone constitutes happiness; vice alone is unhappiness. This is not a speculative claim — it follows from the Stoic understanding of human nature as fundamentally rational. To live according to reason, which is to live according to nature, is the only genuine good. (002-040) (002-041)

The divine ruling principle directs all things toward the good of the whole, not the good of any individual part. The highest human good is to consciously cooperate with this divine purpose — to work with God for the common good. (002-024) (002-025) (002-026)

## The Doctrine of Indifferents

The Stoic doctrine of the good rests on a sharp distinction. Nothing is good but virtue; nothing is bad but vice. External conditions — health and sickness, wealth and poverty, pleasure and pain — are "indifferent" (ἀδιάφορα). They neither contribute to nor detract from the soul's true good, which is its rational alignment with nature. (002-044) (002-045) (002-046)

This doctrine does not counsel indifference to the world. The Wise Man engages fully with circumstances; he simply refuses to mistake externals for the genuine good. He can be happy "even when stretched upon the rack" because his happiness depends on virtue alone. (002-048) (002-049)

## The Good in the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius embodied the Stoic good throughout his life. He was "not spared domestic troubles" — his wife Faustina, his son Commodus, the burdens of empire — yet he maintained that these externals were not evil. The good emperor focused on what was in his power: his judgments, his choices, his character. (001-118)
