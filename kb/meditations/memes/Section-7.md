# Section 7

(007-001) V. Do nothing against thy will, nor contrary to the community, nor

(007-002) without due examination, nor with reluctancy. Affect not to set out thy

(007-003) thoughts with curious neat language. Be neither a great talker, nor a

(007-004) great undertaker. Moreover, let thy God that is in thee to rule over

(007-005) thee, find by thee, that he hath to do with a man; an aged man; a

(007-006) sociable man; a Roman; a prince; one that hath ordered his life, as

(007-007) one that expecteth, as it were, nothing but the sound of the trumpet,

(007-008) sounding a retreat to depart out of this life with all expedition. One

(007-009) who for his word or actions neither needs an oath, nor any man to be a

(007-010) witness.

(007-011) VI. To be cheerful, and to stand in no need, either of other men's help

(007-012) or attendance, or of that rest and tranquillity, which thou must be

(007-013) beholding to others for. Rather like one that is straight of himself, or

(007-014) hath ever been straight, than one that hath been rectified.

(007-015) VII. If thou shalt find anything in this mortal life better than

(007-016) [[justice|righteousness]], than truth, temperance, fortitude, and in general better

(007-017) than a mind contented both with those things which according to right

(007-018) and reason she doth, and in those, which without her will and knowledge

(007-019) happen unto thee by the providence; if I say, thou canst find out

(007-020) anything better than this, apply thyself unto it with thy whole heart,

(007-021) and that which is best wheresoever thou dost find it, enjoy freely. But

(007-022) if nothing thou shalt find worthy to be preferred to that spirit which

(007-023) is within thee; if nothing better than to subject unto thee thine own

(007-024) lusts and desires, and not to give way to any fancies or imaginations

(007-025) before thou hast duly considered of them, nothing better than to

(007-026) withdraw thyself (to use Socrates his words) from all sensuality, and

(007-027) submit thyself unto the gods, and to have care of all men in general: if

(007-028) thou shalt find that all other things in comparison of this, are but

(007-029) vile, and of little moment; then give not way to any other thing, which

(007-030) being once though but affected and inclined unto, it will no more be in

(007-031) thy power without all distraction as thou oughtest to prefer and to

(007-032) pursue after that good, which is thine own and thy proper good. For it

(007-033) is not lawful, that anything that is of another and inferior kind and

(007-034) nature, be it what it will, as either popular applause, or honour, or

(007-035) riches, or pleasures; should be suffered to confront and contest as it

(007-036) were, with that which is rational, and operatively good. For all these

(007-037) things, if once though but for a while, they begin to please, they

(007-038) presently prevail, and pervert a man's mind, or turn a man from the

(007-039) right way. Do thou therefore I say absolutely and freely make choice of

(007-040) that which is best, and stick unto it. Now, that they say is best, which

(007-041) is most profitable. If they mean profitable to man as he is a rational

(007-042) man, stand thou to it, and maintain it; but if they mean profitable, as

(007-043) he is a creature, only reject it; and from this thy tenet and conclusion

(007-044) keep off carefully all plausible shows and colours of external

(007-045) appearance, that thou mayest be able to discern things rightly.

(007-046) VIII. Never esteem of anything as profitable, which shall ever constrain

(007-047) thee either to break thy faith, or to lose thy modesty; to hate any man,

(007-048) to suspect, to curse, to dissemble, to lust after anything, that

(007-049) requireth the secret of walls or veils. But he that preferreth before

(007-050) all things his rational part and spirit, and the sacred mysteries of

(007-051) virtue which issueth from it, he shall never lament and exclaim, never

(007-052) sigh; he shall never want either solitude or company: and which is

(007-053) chiefest of all, he shall live without either desire or fear. And as for

(007-054) life, whether for a long or short time he shall enjoy his soul thus

(007-055) compassed about with a body, he is altogether indifferent. For if even

(007-056) now he were to depart, he is as ready for it, as for any other action,

(007-057) which may be performed with modesty and decency. For all his life long,

(007-058) this is his only care, that his mind may always be occupied in such

(007-059) intentions and objects, as are proper to a rational sociable creature.

(007-060) IX. In the mind that is once truly disciplined and purged, thou canst

(007-061) not find anything, either foul or impure, or as it were festered:

(007-062) nothing that is either servile, or affected: no partial tie; no

(007-063) malicious averseness; nothing obnoxious; nothing concealed. The life of

(007-064) such an one, death can never surprise as imperfect; as of an actor, that

(007-065) should die before he had ended, or the play itself were at an end, a man

(007-066) might speak.

(007-067) X. Use thine opinative faculty with all honour and respect, for in

(007-068) her indeed is all: that thy opinion do not beget in thy [[knowledge|understanding]]

(007-069) anything contrary to either nature, or the proper constitution of a

(007-070) rational creature. The end and object of a rational constitution is, to

(007-071) do nothing rashly, to be kindly affected towards men, and in all things

(007-072) willingly to submit unto the gods. Casting therefore all other things

(007-073) aside, keep thyself to these few, and remember withal that no man

(007-074) properly can be said to live more than that which is now present, which

(007-075) is but a moment of time. Whatsoever is besides either is already past,

(007-076) or uncertain. The time therefore that any man doth live, is but a

(007-077) little, and the place where he liveth, is but a very little corner of

(007-078) the earth, and the greatest fame that can remain of a man after his

(007-079) death, even that is but little, and that too, such as it is whilst it

(007-080) is, is by the succession of silly mortal men preserved, who likewise

(007-081) shall shortly die, and even whiles they live know not what in very deed

(007-082) they themselves are: and much less can know one, who long before is dead

(007-083) and gone.

(007-084) XI. To these ever-present helps and mementoes, let one more be added,

(007-085) ever to make a particular description and delineation as it were of

(007-086) every object that presents itself to thy mind, that thou mayest wholly

(007-087) and throughly contemplate it, in its own proper nature, bare and naked;

(007-088) wholly, and severally; divided into its several parts and quarters: and

(007-089) then by thyself in thy mind, to call both it, and those things of which

(007-090) it doth consist, and in which it shall be resolved, by their own proper

(007-091) true names, and appellations. For there is nothing so effectual to beget

(007-092) true magnanimity, as to be able truly and methodically to examine and

(007-093) consider all things that happen in this life, and so to penetrate

(007-094) into their natures, that at the same time, this also may concur in our

(007-095) apprehensions: what is the true use of it? and what is the true nature

(007-096) of this universe, to which it is useful? how much in regard of the

(007-097) universe may it be esteemed? how much in regard of man, a citizen of the

(007-098) supreme city, of which all other cities in the world are as it were but

(007-099) houses and families?

(007-100) XII. What is this, that now my fancy is set upon? of what things doth

(007-101) it consist? how long can it last? which of all the virtues is the proper

(007-102) virtue for this present use? as whether meekness, fortitude, truth,

(007-103) faith, sincerity, contentation, or any of the rest? Of everything

(007-104) therefore thou must use thyself to say, This immediately comes from God,

(007-105) this by that fatal connection, and concatenation of things, or (which

(007-106) almost comes to one) by some coincidental casualty. And as for this, it

(007-107) proceeds from my neighbour, my kinsman, my fellow: through his ignorance

(007-108) indeed, because he knows not what is truly natural unto him: but I know

(007-109) it, and therefore carry myself towards him according to the natural law

(007-110) of fellowship; that is kindly, and justly. As for those things that of

(007-111) themselves are altogether indifferent, as in my best judgment I conceive

(007-112) everything to deserve more or less, so I carry myself towards it.

(007-113) XIII. If thou shalt intend that which is present, following the rule of

(007-114) right and reason carefully, solidly, meekly, and shalt not intermix

(007-115) any other businesses, but shall study this only to preserve thy spirit

(007-116) unpolluted, and pure, and shall cleave unto him without either hope

(007-117) or fear of anything, in all things that thou shalt either do or speak,

(007-118) contenting thyself with heroical truth, thou shalt live happily; and

(007-119) from this, there is no man that can hinder thee.

(007-120) XIV. As physicians and chirurgeons have always their instruments ready

(007-121) at hand for all sudden cures; so have thou always thy dogmata in a

(007-122) readiness for the knowledge of things, both divine and human: and

(007-123) whatsoever thou dost, even in the smallest things that thou dost, thou

(007-124) must ever remember that mutual relation, and connection that is between

(007-125) these two things divine, and things human. For without relation unto

(007-126) God, thou shalt never speed in any worldly actions; nor on the other

(007-127) side in any divine, without some respect had to things human.

(007-128) XV. Be not deceived; for thou shalt never live to read thy moral

(007-129) commentaries, nor the acts of the famous Romans and Grecians; nor those

(007-130) excerpta from several books; all which thou hadst provided and laid

(007-131) up for thyself against thine old age. Hasten therefore to an end, and

(007-132) giving over all vain hopes, help thyself in time if thou carest for

(007-133) thyself, as thou oughtest to do.

(007-134) XVI. To steal, to sow, to buy, to be at rest, to see what is to be done

(007-135) (which is not seen by the eyes, but by another kind of sight:) what

(007-136) these words mean, and how many ways to be understood, they do not

(007-137) understand. The body, the soul, the understanding. As the senses

(007-138) naturally belong to the body, and the desires and affections to the

(007-139) soul, so do the dogmata to the understanding.

(007-140) XVII. To be capable of fancies and imaginations, is common to man and

(007-141) beast. To be violently drawn and moved by the lusts and desires of the

(007-142) soul, is proper to wild beasts and monsters, such as Phalaris and Nero

(007-143) were. To follow reason for ordinary duties and actions is common to them

(007-144) also, who believe not that there be any gods, and for their advantage

(007-145) would make no conscience to betray their own country; and who when once

(007-146) the doors be shut upon them, dare do anything. If therefore all things

(007-147) else be common to these likewise, it follows, that for a man to like and

(007-148) embrace all things that happen and are destinated unto him, and not to

(007-149) trouble and molest that spirit which is seated in the temple of his own

(007-150) breast, with a multitude of vain fancies and imaginations, but to keep

(007-151) him propitious and to obey him as a god, never either speaking anything

(007-152) contrary to truth, or doing anything contrary to justice, is the only

(007-153) true property of a good man. And such a one, though no man should

(007-154) believe that he liveth as he doth, either sincerely and conscionably,

(007-155) or cheerful and contentedly; yet is he neither with any man at all angry

(007-156) for it, nor diverted by it from the way that leadeth to the end of his

(007-157) life, through which a man must pass pure, ever ready to depart, and

(007-158) willing of himself without any compulsion to fit and accommodate himself

(007-159) to his proper lot and portion.

(007-160) THE FOURTH BOOK

(007-161) I. That inward mistress part of man if it be in its own true natural

(007-162) temper, is towards all worldly chances and events ever so disposed and

(007-163) affected, that it will easily turn and apply itself to that which may

(007-164) be, and is within its own power to compass, when that cannot be which at

(007-165) first it intended. For it never doth absolutely addict and apply itself

(007-166) to any one object, but whatsoever it is that it doth now intend and

(007-167) prosecute, it doth prosecute it with exception and reservation; so that

(007-168) whatsoever it is that falls out contrary to its first intentions, even

(007-169) that afterwards it makes its proper object. Even as the fire when it

(007-170) prevails upon those things that are in his way; by which things indeed a

(007-171) little fire would have been quenched, but a great fire doth soon turn to

(007-172) its own nature, and so consume whatsoever comes in his way: yea by those

(007-173) very things it is made greater and greater.
