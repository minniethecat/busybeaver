## Overview

Opinion (Greek ὑπόληψις / _hypolēpsis_ — supposition, judgment; also δόγμα / _dogma_ — tenet, belief, principle) is a foundational concept in Stoic epistemology and ethics. In the Meditations, opinion denotes the soul's act of assigning meaning, value, or disturbance to the bare impressions delivered by the senses. Marcus insists that external things have no power to disturb the mind; it is _opinion alone_ — the judgment formed within — that produces all agitation. (002-062) (008-054)

> "Desire and dislike, opinion and affection, are within the power of the will; whereas health, wealth, honour, and other such things are generally not so." (002-062)

## In Stoic Epistemology

In the Stoic theory of knowledge, the newborn soul is likened to a blank sheet of paper. Sense impressions (φαντασίαι / _phantasiai_) write upon this surface, and through accumulated experience the soul unconsciously forms general notions (κοιναὶ ἔννοιαι / _koinai ennoiai_) and anticipations (προλήψεις / _prolēpseis_). Opinion occupies the space between bare impression and certain knowledge (ἐπιστήμη / _epistēmē_): it is the act of assent by which the rational soul judges an appearance to be true or false, good or evil.

The defining Stoic insight is that opinion mediates _all_ human experience of value. As Marcus writes, things themselves "stand without still and quiet, and that it is from the opinion only which is within, that all the tumult and all the trouble doth proceed." (008-054) The same event — pain, loss, insult — is neutral until opinion pronounces upon it. This is why Marcus can say: "Remember that all is but opinion and conceit, for those things are plain and apparent, which were spoken unto Monimus the Cynic." (006-023)

The distinction between opinion and knowledge runs through the Stoic system. Knowledge is the "holding perception" (καταληπτικὴ φαντασία) — an impression so vivid and consistent that it cannot be denied. Opinion, by contrast, is the soul's fallible judgment, susceptible to error, passion, and correction. (002-035) (002-036)

## Core Characteristics

Opinion in the Meditations exhibits several defining properties:

| Property | Description | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal** | Opinion is within the sphere of moral purpose (ἐφ' ἡμῖν); external things are not | (002-062) (015-091) |
| **Causal** | Opinion is the sole efficient cause of emotional disturbance — not events, not other people | (008-054) (008-088) |
| **Revisable** | Opinions can and must be tested against reason, nature, and truth | (014-002) (014-078) |
| **Additive** | We "add" opinion to bare events, transforming neutral impressions into apparent goods or evils | (012-070) (014-152) |
| **Governed by Dogmata** | Our governing principles (δόγματα) determine the character of all our opinions and actions | (012-013) (012-141) |

**The Distinction from Knowledge.** Where knowledge (_epistēmē_) is unshakeable — the "holding perception" that grasps reality with certainty — opinion is the soul's mutable and fallible judgment. False opinion is the root of all vice: "do I think that error and false opinion is less powerful to make men transgress, than either choler, being immoderate and excessive, to cause the jaundice?" (015-111) The wise man cultivates right opinion, tested against reason and experience, and remains ever ready to correct it when shown to be in error. (002-035) (014-002)

**The Power to Exclude Opinion.** A central Stoic discipline is the deliberate refusal to add opinion to impression. Marcus asserts: "It is in thy power absolutely to exclude all manner of conceit and opinion, as concerning this matter; and by the same means, to exclude all grief and sorrow from thy soul." (015-091) Things themselves "have no such power, whereby to beget and force upon us any opinion at all." (015-094) The soul is not a passive recipient of impressions but an active judge — and it can refuse to assent.

This doctrine reaches its most dramatic expression in Book 16, where Marcus personifies opinion and commands it to depart: "What then dost thou do here, O opinion? By the Gods I adjure thee, that thou get thee gone, as thou earnest: for I need thee not." (016-065)

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius returns to the theme of opinion across every Book of the Meditations, treating it as a practical discipline rather than abstract theory. His engagement takes several forms:

**Opinion as the Gate of Emotion.** The emperor reminds himself that no external event — not the malice of others, not bodily pain, not the approach of death — can trouble him except through his own judgment. "Let opinion be taken away, and no man will think himself wronged. If no man shall think himself wronged, then is there no more any such thing as wrong." (008-088) The implication is radical: the moral quality of experience is determined entirely from within.

**Against Vulgar Opinion.** From his teacher Sextus of Chaeronea, Marcus learned the example of a man who refused to be "carried with the vulgar opinions, with the theorems, and tenets of philosophers" — one who lived by principle, not by the shifting judgments of the crowd. (004-005) Similarly, he warns against being "carried by conceit and common opinion," distinguishing social convention from rational judgment. (012-150)

**Opinion and Right Action.** Right action follows from right opinion. Marcus prays: "if in the course both of thine opinions and actions, thou wilt observe a true method." (012-141) The inner governance of opinion and the outward conduct of life are inseparable: "according as the dogmata and opinions are, which she doth vouchsafe herself; so are those things which, as accessories, have their existence." (012-013) The soul's governing principles shape its entire world.

**Willingness to Revise.** Marcus holds himself to the standard of openness he admired in Antoninus Pius, who "would bear with them that with all boldness and liberty opposed his opinions; and even rejoice if any man could better advise him." (014-078) The Stoic does not cling to opinion out of pride: "If anybody shall reprove me, and shall make it apparent unto me, that in any either opinion or action of mine I do err, I will most gladly retract. For it is the truth that I seek after." (014-002)

**The Discipline of Assent.** Throughout the later Books, Marcus sharpens his focus on the moment-by-moment practice of managing opinion. "Wipe off all opinion stay the force and violence of unreasonable lusts and affections: circumscribe the present time." (016-149) The Stoic examines each impression as it arises, strips it of the added judgment, and sees the thing as it is — bare, indifferent, powerless to harm. "Cast away from thee opinion, and thou art safe." (029-122)

This is not a counsel of passivity. It is the rigorous practice of a mind that refuses to be the victim of its own judgments, insisting instead on the sovereignty of reason over every impression that presents itself for assent.
