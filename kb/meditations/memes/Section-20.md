# Section 20

(020-001) XXXIII. As almost all her other faculties and properties the nature of

(020-002) the universe hath imparted unto every reasonable creature, so this in

(020-003) particular we have received from her, that as whatsoever doth oppose

(020-004) itself unto her, and doth withstand her in her purposes and intentions,

(020-005) she doth, though against its will and intention, bring it about to

(020-006) herself, to serve herself of it in the execution of her own destinated

(020-007) ends; and so by this though not intended co-operation of it with herself

(020-008) makes it part of herself whether it will or no. So may every reasonable

(020-009) creature, what crosses and impediments soever it meets with in the

(020-010) course of this mortal life, it may use them as fit and proper objects,

(020-011) to the furtherance of whatsoever it intended and absolutely proposed

(020-012) unto itself as its natural end and happiness.

(020-013) XXXIV. Let not the general representation unto thyself of the

(020-014) wretchedness of this our mortal life, trouble thee. Let not thy mind

(020-015) wander up and down, and heap together in her thoughts the many troubles

(020-016) and grievous calamities which thou art as subject unto as any other. But

(020-017) as everything in particular doth happen, put this question unto thyself,

(020-018) and say: What is it that in this present matter, seems unto thee so

(020-019) intolerable? For thou wilt be ashamed to confess it. Then upon this

(020-020) presently call to mind, that neither that which is future, nor that

(020-021) which is past can hurt thee; but that only which is present. (And that

(020-022) also is much lessened, if thou dost lightly circumscribe it:) and then

(020-023) check thy mind if for so little a while, (a mere instant), it cannot

(020-024) hold out with patience.

(020-025) XXXV. What? are either Panthea or Pergamus abiding to this day by their

(020-026) masters' tombs? or either Chabrias or Diotimus by that of Adrianus? O

(020-027) foolery! For what if they did, would their masters be sensible of It? or

(020-028) if sensible, would they be glad of it? or if glad, were these immortal?

(020-029) Was not it appointed unto them also (both men and women,) to become

(020-030) old in time, and then to die? And these once dead, what would become of

(020-031) these former? And when all is done, what is all this for, but for a mere

(020-032) bag of blood and corruption?

(020-033) XXXVI. If thou beest quick-sighted, be so in matter of judgment, and

(020-034) best discretion, saith he.

(020-035) XXXVII. In the whole constitution of man, I see not any virtue contrary

(020-036) to justice, whereby it may be resisted and opposed. But one whereby

(020-037) pleasure and voluptuousness may be resisted and opposed, I see:

(020-038) continence.

(020-039) XXXVIII. If thou canst but withdraw conceit and opinion concerning that

(020-040) which may seem hurtful and offensive, thou thyself art as safe, as safe

(020-041) may be. Thou thyself? and who is that? Thy reason. 'Yea, but I am not

(020-042) reason.' Well, be it so. However, let not thy reason or [[knowledge|understanding]]

(020-043) admit of grief, and if there be anything in thee that is grieved, let

(020-044) that, (whatsoever it be,) conceive its own grief, if it can.

(020-045) XXXIX. That which is a hindrance of the senses, is an evil to the

(020-046) sensitive nature. That which is a hindrance of the appetitive and

(020-047) prosecutive faculty, is an evil to the sensitive nature. As of the

(020-048) sensitive, so of the vegetative constitution, whatsoever is a hindrance

(020-049) unto it, is also in that respect an evil unto the same. And so likewise,

(020-050) whatsoever is a hindrance unto the mind and understanding, must needs

(020-051) be the proper evil of the reasonable nature. Now apply all those things

(020-052) unto thyself. Do either pain or pleasure seize on thee? Let the senses

(020-053) look to that. Hast thou met with Some obstacle or other in thy purpose

(020-054) and intention? If thou didst propose without due reservation and

(020-055) exception now hath thy reasonable part received a blow indeed But if in

(020-056) general thou didst propose unto thyself what soever might be, thou art

(020-057) not thereby either hurt, nor properly hindered. For in those things that

(020-058) properly belong unto the mind, she cannot be hindered by any man. It

(020-059) is not fire, nor iron; nor the power of a tyrant nor the power of a

(020-060) slandering tongue; nor anything else that can penetrate into her.

(020-061) XL. If once round and solid, there is no fear that ever it will change.

(020-062) XLI. Why should I grieve myself; who never did willingly grieve any

(020-063) other! One thing rejoices one and another thing another. As for me, this

(020-064) is my joy, if my understanding be right and sound, as neither averse

(020-065) from any man, nor refusing any of those things which as a man I am

(020-066) subject unto; if I can look upon all things in the world meekly and

(020-067) kindly; accept all things and carry myself towards everything according

(020-068) to to true worth of the thing itself.

(020-069) XLII. This time that is now present, bestow thou upon thyself. They that

(020-070) rather hunt for fame after death, do not consider, that those men that

(020-071) shall be hereafter, will be even such, as these whom now they can so

(020-072) hardly bear with. And besides they also will be mortal men. But to

(020-073) consider the thing in itself, if so many with so many voices, shall make

(020-074) such and such a sound, or shall have such and such an opinion concerning

(020-075) thee, what is it to thee?

(020-076) XLIII. Take me and throw me where thou wilt: I am indifferent. For there

(020-077) also I shall have that spirit which is within me propitious; that is

(020-078) well pleased and fully contented both in that constant disposition, and

(020-079) with those particular actions, which to its own proper constitution are

(020-080) suitable and agreeable.

(020-081) XLIV. Is this then a thing of that worth, that for it my soul should

(020-082) suffer, and become worse than it was? as either basely dejected, or

(020-083) disordinately affected, or confounded within itself, or terrified? What

(020-084) can there be, that thou shouldest so much esteem?

(020-085) XLV. Nothing can happen unto thee, which is not incidental unto thee, as

(020-086) thou art a man. As nothing can happen either to an ox, a vine, or to

(020-087) a stone, which is not incidental unto them; unto every one in his own

(020-088) kind. If therefore nothing can happen unto anything, which is not both

(020-089) usual and natural; why art thou displeased? Sure the common nature

(020-090) of all would not bring anything upon any, that were intolerable. If

(020-091) therefore it be a thing external that causes thy grief, know, that it is

(020-092) not that properly that doth cause it, but thine own conceit and opinion

(020-093) concerning the thing: which thou mayest rid thyself of, when thou wilt.

(020-094) But if it be somewhat that is amiss in thine own disposition, that doth

(020-095) grieve thee, mayest thou not rectify thy moral tenets and opinions. But

(020-096) if it grieve thee, that thou doest not perform that which seemeth unto

(020-097) thee right and just, why doest not thou choose rather to perform it than

(020-098) to grieve? But somewhat that is stronger than thyself doth hinder thee.

(020-099) Let it not grieve thee then, if it be not thy fault that the thing is

(020-100) not performed. 'Yea but it is a thing of that nature, as that thy life

(020-101) is not worth the while, except it may be performed.' If it be so, upon

(020-102) condition that thou be kindly and lovingly disposed towards all men,

(020-103) thou mayest be gone. For even then, as much as at any time, art thou in

(020-104) a very good estate of performance, when thou doest die in charity with

(020-105) those, that are an obstacle unto thy performance.

(020-106) XLVI. Remember that thy mind is of that nature as that it becometh

(020-107) altogether unconquerable, when once recollected in herself, she seeks no

(020-108) other content than this, that she cannot be forced: yea though it so

(020-109) fall out, that it be even against reason itself, that it cloth bandy.

(020-110) How much less when by the help of reason she is able to judge of things

(020-111) with discretion? And therefore let thy chief fort and place of defence

(020-112) be, a mind free from passions. A stronger place, (whereunto to make his

(020-113) refuge, and so to become impregnable) and better fortified than this,

(020-114) hath no man. He that seeth not this is unlearned. He that seeth it, and

(020-115) betaketh not himself to this place of refuge, is unhappy.

(020-116) XLVII. Keep thyself to the first bare and naked apprehensions of things,

(020-117) as they present themselves unto thee, and add not unto them. It is

(020-118) reported unto thee, that such a one speaketh ill of thee. Well; that he

(020-119) speaketh ill of thee, so much is reported. But that thou art hurt

(020-120) thereby, is not reported: that is the addition of opinion, which thou

(020-121) must exclude. I see that my child is sick. That he is sick, I see, but

(020-122) that he is in danger of his life also, I see it not. Thus thou must use

(020-123) to keep thyself to the first motions and apprehensions of things, as

(020-124) they present themselves outwardly; and add not unto them from within

(020-125) thyself through mere conceit and opinion. Or rather add unto them: hut

(020-126) as one that understandeth the true nature of all things that happen in

(020-127) the world.

(020-128) XLVIII. Is the cucumber bitter? set it away. Brambles are in the way?

(020-129) avoid them. Let this suffice. Add not presently speaking unto thyself,

(020-130) What serve these things for in the world? For, this, one that is

(020-131) acquainted with the mysteries of nature, will laugh at thee for it; as a

(020-132) carpenter would or a shoemaker, if meeting in either of their shops with

(020-133) some shavings, or small remnants of their work, thou shouldest blame

(020-134) them for it. And yet those men, it is not for want of a place where to

(020-135) throw them that they keep them in their shops for a while: but the

(020-136) nature of the universe hath no such out-place; but herein doth consist

(020-137) the wonder of her art and skill, that she having once circumscribed

(020-138) herself within some certain bounds and limits, whatsoever is within her

(020-139) that seems either corrupted, or old, or unprofitable, she can change it

(020-140) into herself, and of these very things can make new things; so that she

(020-141) needeth not to seek elsewhere out of herself either for a new supply of

(020-142) matter and substance, or for a place where to throw out whatsoever is

(020-143) irrecoverably putrid and corrupt. Thus she, as for place, so for matter

(020-144) and art, is herself sufficient unto herself.

(020-145) XLIX. Not to be slack and negligent; or loose, and wanton in thy

(020-146) actions; nor contentious, and troublesome in thy conversation; nor to

(020-147) rove and wander in thy fancies and imaginations. Not basely to contract

(020-148) thy soul; nor boisterously to sally out with it, or furiously to launch

(020-149) out as it were, nor ever to want employment.

(020-150) L. 'They kill me, they cut my flesh; they persecute my person with

(020-151) curses.' What then? May not thy mind for all this continue pure,

(020-152) prudent, temperate, just? As a fountain of sweet and clear water, though

(020-153) she be cursed by some stander by, yet do her springs nevertheless still

(020-154) run as sweet and clear as before; yea though either dirt or dung be

(020-155) thrown in, yet is it no sooner thrown, than dispersed, and she cleared.

(020-156) She cannot be dyed or infected by it. What then must I do, that I

(020-157) may have within myself an overflowing fountain, and not a well? Beget

(020-158) thyself by continual pains and endeavours to true liberty with charity,

(020-159) and true simplicity and modesty.

(020-160) LI. He that knoweth not what the world is, knoweth not where he himself

(020-161) is. And he that knoweth not what the world was made for, cannot possibly

(020-162) know either what are the qualities, or what is the nature of the world.

(020-163) Now he that in either of these is to seek, for what he himself was made

(020-164) is ignorant also. What then dost thou think of that man, who proposeth

(020-165) unto himself, as a matter of great moment, the noise and applause

(020-166) of men, who both where they are, and what they are themselves, are

(020-167) altogether ignorant? Dost thou desire to be commended of that man, who

(020-168) thrice in one hour perchance, doth himself curse himself? Dost thou

(020-169) desire to please him, who pleaseth not himself? or dost thou think that

(020-170) he pleaseth himself, who doth use to repent himself almost of everything

(020-171) that he doth?
