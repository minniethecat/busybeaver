## Overview

Prudence (Greek φρόνησις / phronēsis, "practical wisdom") is one of the four cardinal virtues of classical ethics, adopted by the Stoic school as essential to the philosophical life. It is the virtue of knowing what to do — the capacity to discern the right action in particular circumstances and to carry it out with sound judgment. Marcus Aurelius lists "prudence, temperance, justice, fortitude" together as the complete set of cardinal virtues, following the Stoic tradition. (011-114)

## In Stoic Ethics

Within the Stoic framework, prudence is the virtue of the rational faculty applied to action. It is not merely theoretical knowledge of what is good but the practical ability to apply that knowledge in the specific situations of life. The prudent person does not act on impulse or convention but on a clear-eyed assessment of what virtue requires in the present moment. Marcus asks pointedly whether a given course of action reflects "prudence" — implying that every action should be tested against the standard of practical wisdom. (012-010) The Stoics subsumed prudence under the general head of wisdom (σοφία), along with good judgment, discretion, and resourcefulness.

## Prudence in Daily Life

Prudence operates at the intersection of knowledge and action. It is not enough to know what is right in the abstract; one must know how to implement it in concrete circumstances, with "prudence and discretion, according to the present occasion." (025-025) This requires attention to context, sensitivity to particulars, and the flexibility to adapt principles to situations. Marcus's portrait of Antoninus Pius exemplifies prudence in governance — knowing "when remissness or moderation was in season" and exercising "discretion and moderation" in public affairs demonstrates practical wisdom in its highest form. Prudence is the virtue that prevents other virtues from becoming rigid or misapplied.

## In the Meditations

For Marcus, prudence is an active quality to be exercised in every deliberation. He treats it as a standard against which actions are measured: does this action reflect prudence, or is it driven by impulse, habit, or social pressure? (012-010) The prudent person acts "with prudence and discretion, according to the present occasion" — the phrase captures both the intellectual dimension (knowing what to do) and the practical dimension (doing it in the right way, at the right time). (025-025) Throughout the Meditations, Marcus models prudence in his method of self-examination: each reflection is an exercise in applying general Stoic principles to the specific circumstances of his life and rule.
