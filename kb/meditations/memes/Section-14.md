# Section 14

(014-001) XX. If anybody shall reprove me, and shall make it apparent unto me,

(014-002) that in any either opinion or action of mine I do err, I will most

(014-003) gladly retract. For it is the truth that I seek after, by which I am

(014-004) sure that never any man was hurt; and as sure, that he is hurt that

(014-005) continueth in any error, or ignorance whatsoever.

(014-006) XXI. I for my part will do what belongs unto me; as for other things,

(014-007) whether things unsensible or things irrational; or if rational, yet

(014-008) deceived and ignorant of the true way, they shall not trouble or

(014-009) distract me. For as for those creatures which are not endued with reason

(014-010) and all other things and-matters of the world whatsoever I freely, and

(014-011) generously, as one endued with reason, of things that have none, make

(014-012) use of them. And as for men, towards them as naturally partakers of the

(014-013) same reason, my care is to carry myself sociably. But whatsoever it is

(014-014) that thou art about, remember to call upon the Gods. And as for the time

(014-015) how long thou shalt live to do these things, let it be altogether

(014-016) indifferent unto thee, for even three such hours are sufficient.

(014-017) XXII. Alexander of Macedon, and he that dressed his mules, when once

(014-018) dead both came to one. For either they were both resumed into those

(014-019) original rational essences from whence all things in the world are

(014-020) propagated; or both after one fashion were scattered into atoms.

(014-021) XXIII Consider how many different things, whether they concern our

(014-022) bodies, or our souls, in a moment of time come to pass in every one of

(014-023) us, and so thou wilt not wonder if many more things or rather all things

(014-024) that are done, can at one time subsist, and coexist in that both one and

(014-025) general, which we call the world.

(014-026) XXIV. if any should put this question unto thee, how this word [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus]]

(014-027) is written, wouldst thou not presently fix thine intention upon it, and

(014-028) utter out in order every letter of it? And if any shall begin to gainsay

(014-029) thee, and quarrel with thee about it; wilt thou quarrel with him again,

(014-030) or rather go on meekly as thou hast begun, until thou hast numbered out

(014-031) every letter? Here then likewise remember, that every duty that belongs

(014-032) unto a man doth consist of some certain letters or numbers as it were,

(014-033) to which without any noise or tumult keeping thyself thou must orderly

(014-034) proceed to thy proposed end, forbearing to quarrel with him that would

(014-035) quarrel and fall out with thee.

(014-036) XXV. Is it not a cruel thing to forbid men to affect those things, which

(014-037) they conceive to agree best with their own natures, and to tend most

(014-038) to their own proper good and behoof? But thou after a sort deniest them

(014-039) this liberty, as often as thou art angry with them for their sins. For

(014-040) surely they are led unto those sins whatsoever they be, as to

(014-041) their proper good and commodity. But it is not so (thou wilt object

(014-042) perchance). Thou therefore teach them better, and make it appear unto

(014-043) them: but be not thou angry with them.

(014-044) XXVI. [[death|Death]] is a cessation from the impression of the senses, the

(014-045) tyranny of the passions, the errors of the mind, and the servitude of

(014-046) the body.

(014-047) XXVII. If in this kind of life thy body be able to hold out, it is a

(014-048) shame that thy soul should faint first, and give over, take heed, lest

(014-049) of a philosopher thou become a mere Cæsar in time, and receive a new

(014-050) tincture from the court. For it may happen if thou dost not take heed.

(014-051) Keep thyself therefore, truly simple, good, sincere, grave, free

(014-052) from all ostentation, a lover of that which is just, religious, kind,

(014-053) tender-hearted, strong and vigorous to undergo anything that becomes

(014-054) thee. Endeavour to continue such, as philosophy (hadst thou wholly and

(014-055) constantly applied thyself unto it) would have made, and secured thee.

(014-056) Worship the Gods, procure the welfare of men, this life is short.

(014-057) Charitable actions, and a holy disposition, is the only fruit of this

(014-058) earthly life.

(014-059) XXVIII. Do all things as becometh the disciple of Antoninus Pius.

(014-060) Remember his resolute constancy in things that were done by him

(014-061) according to reason, his equability in all things, his sanctity; the

(014-062) cheerfulness of his countenance, his sweetness, and how free he was from

(014-063) all vainglory; how careful to come to the true and exact knowledge of

(014-064) matters in hand, and how he would by no means give over till he did

(014-065) fully, and plainly understand the whole state of the business; and how

(014-066) patiently, and without any contestation he would bear with them, that

(014-067) did unjustly condemn him: how he would never be over-hasty in anything,

(014-068) nor give ear to slanders and false accusations, but examine and observe

(014-069) with best diligence the several actions and dispositions of men. Again,

(014-070) how he was no backbiter, nor easily frightened, nor suspicious, and in

(014-071) his language free from all affectation and curiosity: and how easily he

(014-072) would content himself with few things, as lodging, bedding, clothing,

(014-073) and ordinary nourishment, and attendance. How able to endure labour, how

(014-074) patient; able through his spare diet to continue from morning to evening

(014-075) without any necessity of withdrawing before his accustomed hours to

(014-076) the necessities of nature: his uniformity and constancy in matter of

(014-077) friendship. How he would bear with them that with all boldness and

(014-078) liberty opposed his opinions; and even rejoice if any man could better

(014-079) advise him: and lastly, how religious he was without superstition. All

(014-080) these things of him remember, that whensoever thy last hour shall

(014-081) come upon thee, it may find thee, as it did him, ready for it in the

(014-082) possession of a good conscience.

(014-083) XXIX. Stir up thy mind, and recall thy wits again from thy natural

(014-084) dreams, and visions, and when thou art perfectly awoken, and canst

(014-085) perceive that they were but dreams that troubled thee, as one newly

(014-086) awakened out of another kind of sleep look upon these worldly things

(014-087) with the same mind as thou didst upon those, that thou sawest in thy

(014-088) sleep.

(014-089) XXX. I consist of body and soul. Unto my body all things are

(014-090) indifferent, for of itself it cannot affect one thing more than another

(014-091) with apprehension of any difference; as for my mind, all things which

(014-092) are not within the verge of her own operation, are indifferent unto her,

(014-093) and for her own operations, those altogether depend of her; neither

(014-094) does she busy herself about any, but those that are present; for as

(014-095) for future and past operations, those also are now at this present

(014-096) indifferent unto her.

(014-097) XXXI. As long as the foot doth that which belongeth unto it to do, and

(014-098) the hand that which belongs unto it, their labour, whatsoever it be, is

(014-099) not unnatural. So a man as long as he doth that which is proper unto

(014-100) a man, his labour cannot be against nature; and if it be not against

(014-101) nature, then neither is it hurtful unto him. But if it were so that

(014-102) happiness did consist in pleasure: how came notorious robbers, impure

(014-103) abominable livers, parricides, and tyrants, in so large a measure to

(014-104) have their part of pleasures?

(014-105) XXXII. Dost thou not see, how even those that profess mechanic arts,

(014-106) though in some respect they be no better than mere idiots, yet they

(014-107) stick close to the course of their trade, neither can they find in

(014-108) their heart to decline from it: and is it not a grievous thing that

(014-109) an architect, or a physician shall respect the course and mysteries of

(014-110) their profession, more than a man the proper course and condition of his

(014-111) own nature, reason, which is common to him and to the Gods?

(014-112) XXXIII. Asia, Europe; what are they, but as corners of the whole world;

(014-113) of which the whole sea, is but as one drop; and the great Mount Athos,

(014-114) but as a clod, as all present time is but as one point of eternity. All,

(014-115) petty things; all things that are soon altered, soon perished. And all

(014-116) things come from one beginning; either all severally and particularly

(014-117) deliberated and resolved upon, by the general ruler and governor of all;

(014-118) or all by necessary consequence. So that the dreadful hiatus of a gaping

(014-119) lion, and all poison, and all hurtful things, are but (as the thorn and

(014-120) the mire) the necessary consequences of goodly fair things. Think not

(014-121) of these therefore, as things contrary to those which thou dost much

(014-122) honour, and respect; but consider in thy mind the true fountain of all.

(014-123) XXXIV He that seeth the things that are now, hath Seen all that either

(014-124) was ever, or ever shall be, for all things are of one kind; and all like

(014-125) one unto another. Meditate often upon the connection of all things in

(014-126) the world; and upon the mutual relation that they have one unto another.

(014-127) For all things are after a sort folded and involved one within another,

(014-128) and by these means all agree well together. For one thing is consequent

(014-129) unto another, by local motion, by natural conspiration and agreement,

(014-130) and by substantial union, or, reduction of all substances into one.

(014-131) XXXV. Fit and accommodate thyself to that estate and to those

(014-132) occurrences, which by the destinies have been annexed unto thee; and

(014-133) love those men whom thy [[providence|fate]] it is to live with; but love them truly. An

(014-134) instrument, a tool, an utensil, whatsoever it be, if it be fit for the

(014-135) purpose it was made for, it is as it should be though he perchance that

(014-136) made and fitted it, be out of sight and gone. But in things natural,

(014-137) that power which hath framed and fitted them, is and abideth within them

(014-138) still: for which reason she ought also the more to be respected, and we

(014-139) are the more obliged (if we may live and pass our time according to her

(014-140) purpose and intention) to think that all is well with us, and according

(014-141) to our own minds. After this manner also, and in this respect it is,

(014-142) that he that is all in all doth enjoy his happiness.

(014-143) XXXVI. What things soever are not within the proper power and

(014-144) jurisdiction of thine own will either to compass or avoid, if thou shalt

(014-145) propose unto thyself any of those things as either good, or evil; it

(014-146) must needs be that according as thou shalt either fall into that which

(014-147) thou dost think evil, or miss of that which thou dost think good, so

(014-148) wilt thou be ready both to complain of the Gods, and to hate those men,

(014-149) who either shall be so indeed, or shall by thee be suspected as the

(014-150) cause either of thy missing of the one, or falling into the other. And

(014-151) indeed we must needs commit many evils, if we incline to any of these

(014-152) things, more or less, with an opinion of any difference. But if we mind

(014-153) and fancy those things only, as good and bad, which wholly depend of our

(014-154) own wills, there is no more occasion why we should either murmur against

(014-155) the Gods, or be at enmity with any man.

(014-156) XXXVII. We all work to one effect, some willingly, and with a rational

(014-157) apprehension of what we do: others without any such knowledge. As I

(014-158) think Heraclitus in a place speaketh of them that sleep, that even they

(014-159) do work in their kind, and do confer to the general operations of the

(014-160) world. One man therefore doth co-operate after one sort, and another

(014-161) after another sort; but even he that doth murmur, and to his power doth

(014-162) resist and hinder; even he as much as any doth co-operate. For of such

(014-163) also did the world stand in need. Now do thou consider among which of

(014-164) these thou wilt rank thyself. For as for him who is the Administrator

(014-165) of all, he will make good use of thee whether thou wilt or no, and make

(014-166) thee (as a part and member of the whole) so to co-operate with him,

(014-167) that whatsoever thou doest, shall turn to the furtherance of his own

(014-168) counsels, and resolutions. But be not thou for shame such a part of the

(014-169) whole, as that vile and ridiculous verse (which Chrysippus in a place

(014-170) doth mention) is a part of the comedy. XXXVIII. Doth either the sun take

(014-171) upon him to do that which belongs to the rain? or his son Aesculapius

(014-172) that, which unto the earth doth properly belong? How is it with every

(014-173) one of the stars in particular? Though they all differ one from another,

(014-174) and have their several charges and functions by themselves, do they not

(014-175) all nevertheless concur and co-operate to one end?
