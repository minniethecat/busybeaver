## Overview

In Stoic thought, the **passions** (Greek: πάθη, *pathē*) are irrational and excessive emotions that arise from faulty judgments about what is good or bad. Unlike mere feelings, which are natural, the passions represent a disturbance of the soul's rational equilibrium — a failure to correctly assess the value of external things (002-095). Marcus Aurelius, following Epictetus, treats the mastery of passion as essential to the Stoic project of inner freedom.

> "The passions standeth very peace of heart."
> (002-095)

## Core Characteristics

The Stoics classified the primary passions into four categories: desire (ἐπιθυμία), fear (φόβος), pleasure (ἡδονή), and distress (λύπη), each arising from a mistaken belief that something external is either good or evil. Marcus emphasizes that passion stands opposed to reason: the domain of the untroubled mind is where passion cannot disturb (002-095), while the person ruled by passion loses the capacity for clear judgment. His own first book praises those who taught him "to refrain from all anger and passion" (001-031).

The Stoic ideal of *apatheia* — often mistranslated as "apathy" — does not mean the absence of all feeling. Rather, it describes a state in which the rational soul is no longer disturbed by passions that arise from false value judgments. Marcus aspires to this condition while remaining, by all accounts, a man of deep feeling: capable of observing the Stoic "unpassionateness, and yet to be most tender-hearted" (001-105). The balance between emotional discipline and genuine human warmth is one of the central tensions of the Meditations.

## In the Meditations

Throughout the *Meditations*, Marcus returns to the discipline of passion as a daily practice. He urges himself to purge "passion and evil affection" from the mind (002-152), recognizing that emotional disturbance arises not from events themselves but from the judgments we apply to them. The ideal is not passionlessness in the sense of numbness, but the tranquility (*ataraxia*) that comes when the rational soul is no longer tossed about by false evaluations of external goods (004-013).

Marcus is acutely aware of the physical dimension of passion. He speaks of "allaying the many distempers of the soul" (007-044), using medical language that reflects the Stoic view of passions as diseases of the mind requiring therapeutic treatment. The philosopher is, in this sense, a physician of the soul — and the Meditations is his daily regimen. The goal is not to extirpate emotion but to ensure that whatever arises in the soul does so with the consent of reason, never "passionately set upon opposition" (003-067) or driven by impulse unchecked.

> "Purge all passion and evil affection."
> (002-152)
