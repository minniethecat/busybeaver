# Section 25

(025-001) XI. To find out, and set to thyself some certain way and method of

(025-002) contemplation, whereby thou mayest clearly discern and represent unto

(025-003) thyself, the mutual change of all things, the one into the other. Bear

(025-004) it in thy mind evermore, and see that thou be throughly well exercised

(025-005) in this particular. For there is not anything more effectual to beget

(025-006) true magnanimity.

(025-007) XII. He hath got loose from the bonds of his body, and perceiving that

(025-008) within a very little while he must of necessity bid the world farewell,

(025-009) and leave all these things behind him, he wholly applied himself, as to

(025-010) [[justice|righteousness]] in all his actions, so to the common nature in all things

(025-011) that should happen unto him. And contenting himself with these two

(025-012) things, to do all things justly, and whatsoever God doth send to like

(025-013) well of it: what others shall either say or think of him, or shall do

(025-014) against him, he doth not so much as trouble his thoughts with it. To go

(025-015) on straight, whither right and reason directed him, and by so doing to

(025-016) follow God, was the only thing that he did mind, that, his only business

(025-017) and occupation.

(025-018) XIII. What use is there of suspicion at all? or, why should thoughts

(025-019) of mistrust, and suspicion concerning that which is future, trouble thy

(025-020) mind at all? What now is to be done, if thou mayest search and inquiry

(025-021) into that, what needs thou care for more? And if thou art well able to

(025-022) perceive it alone, let no man divert thee from it. But if alone thou

(025-023) doest not so well perceive it, suspend thine action, and take advice

(025-024) from the best. And if there be anything else that doth hinder thee, go

(025-025) on with prudence and discretion, according to the present occasion

(025-026) and opportunity, still proposing that unto thyself, which thou doest

(025-027) conceive most right and just. For to hit that aright, and to speed in

(025-028) the prosecution of it, must needs be happiness, since it is that only

(025-029) which we can truly and properly be said to miss of, or miscarry in.

(025-030) XIV. What is that that is slow, and yet quick? merry, and yet grave? He

(025-031) that in all things doth follow reason for his guide.

(025-032) XV. In the morning as soon as thou art awaked, when thy judgment, before

(025-033) either thy affections, or external objects have wrought upon it, is yet

(025-034) most free and impartial: put this question to thyself, whether if that

(025-035) which is right and just be done, the doing of it by thyself, or by

(025-036) others when thou art not able thyself; be a thing material or no. For

(025-037) sure it is not. And as for these that keep such a life, and stand so

(025-038) much upon the praises, or dispraises of other men, hast thou forgotten

(025-039) what manner of men they be? that such and such upon their beds, and such

(025-040) at their board: what their ordinary actions are: what they pursue after,

(025-041) and what they fly from: what thefts and rapines they commit, if not with

(025-042) their hands and feet, yet with that more precious part of theirs, their

(025-043) minds: which (would it but admit of them) might enjoy faith, modesty,

(025-044) truth, justice, a good spirit.

(025-045) XVI. Give what thou wilt, and take away what thou wilt, saith he that is

(025-046) well taught and truly modest, to Him that gives, and takes away. And it

(025-047) is not out of a stout and peremptory resolution, that he saith it, but

(025-048) in mere love, and humble submission.

(025-049) XVII. So live as indifferent to the world and all worldly objects, as

(025-050) one who liveth by himself alone upon some desert hill. For whether here,

(025-051) or there, if the whole world be but as one town, it matters not much for

(025-052) the place. Let them behold and see a man, that is a man indeed, living

(025-053) according to the true nature of man. If they cannot bear with me, let

(025-054) them kill me. For better were it to die, than so to live as they would

(025-055) have thee.

(025-056) XVIII. Make it not any longer a matter of dispute or discourse, what are

(025-057) the signs and proprieties of a good man, but really and actually to be

(025-058) such.

(025-059) XIX. Ever to represent unto thyself; and to set before thee, both the

(025-060) general age and time of the world, and the whole substance of it. And

(025-061) how all things particular in respect of these are for their substance,

(025-062) as one of the least seeds that is: and for their duration, as the

(025-063) turning of the pestle in the mortar once about. Then to fix thy mind

(025-064) upon every particular object of the world, and to conceive it, (as it

(025-065) is indeed,) as already being in the state of dissolution, and of change;

(025-066) tending to some kind of either putrefaction or dispersion; or whatsoever

(025-067) else it is, that is the death as it were of everything in his own kind.

(025-068) XX. Consider them through all actions and occupations, of their lives:

(025-069) as when they eat, and when they sleep: when they are in the act of

(025-070) necessary exoneration, and when in the act of lust. Again, when they

(025-071) either are in their greatest exultation; and in the middle of all

(025-072) their pomp and [[fame|glory]]; or being angry and displeased, in great state and

(025-073) majesty, as from an higher place, they chide and rebuke. How base and

(025-074) slavish, but a little while ago, they were fain to be, that they might

(025-075) come to this; and within a very little while what will be their estate,

(025-076) when death hath once seized upon them.

(025-077) XXI. That is best for every one, that the common nature of all doth send

(025-078) unto every one, and then is it best, when she doth send it.

(025-079) XXII. The earth, saith the poet, doth often long after the rain. So is

(025-080) the glorious sky often as desirous to fall upon the earth, which argues

(025-081) a mutual kind of love between them. And so (say I) doth the world bear

(025-082) a certain affection of love to whatsoever shall come to pass With thine

(025-083) affections shall mine concur, O world. The same (and no other) shall the

(025-084) object of my longing be which is of thine. Now that the world doth love

(025-085) it is true indeed so is it as commonly said, and acknowledged ledged,

(025-086) when, according to the Greek phrase, imitated by the Latins, of things

(025-087) that used to be, we say commonly, that they love to be.

(025-088) XXIII. Either thou dost Continue in this kind of life and that is it,

(025-089) which so long thou hast been used unto and therefore tolerable: or thou

(025-090) doest retire, or leave the world, and that of thine own accord, and then

(025-091) thou hast thy mind: or thy life is cut off; and then mayst thou

(025-092) rejoice that thou hast ended thy charge. One of these must needs be.

(025-093) Be therefore of good comfort.

(025-094) XXIV Let it always appear and be manifest unto thee that solitariness,

(025-095) and desert places, by many philosophers so much esteemed of and

(025-096) affected, are of themselves but thus and thus; and that all things are

(025-097) them to them that live in towns, and converse with others as they are

(025-098) the same nature everywhere to be seen and observed: to them that have

(025-099) retired themselves to the top of mountains, and to desert havens, or

(025-100) what other desert and inhabited places soever. For anywhere it thou wilt

(025-101) mayest thou quickly find and apply that to thyself; which Plato saith of

(025-102) his philosopher, in a place: as private and retired, saith he, as if he

(025-103) were shut up and enclosed about in some shepherd's lodge, on the top of

(025-104) a hill. There by thyself to put these questions to thyself or to enter

(025-105) in these considerations: What is my chief and principal part, which hath

(025-106) power over the rest? What is now the present estate of it, as I use it;

(025-107) and what is it, that I employ it about? Is it now void of reason ir no?

(025-108) Is it free, and separated; or so affixed, so congealed and grown

(025-109) together as it were with the flesh, that it is swayed by the motions and

(025-110) inclinations of it?

(025-111) XXV. He that runs away from his master is a fugitive. But the law is

(025-112) every man's master. He therefore that forsakes the law, is a fugitive.

(025-113) So is he, whosoever he be, that is either sorry, angry, or afraid, or

(025-114) for anything that either hath been, is, or shall be by his appointment,

(025-115) who is the Lord and Governor of the universe. For he truly and properly

(025-116) is Νόμος, or the law, as the only νέμων, or distributor and dispenser

(025-117) of all things that happen unto any one in his lifetime--Whatsoever then

(025-118) is either sorry, angry, or afraid, is a fugitive.

(025-119) XXVI. From man is the seed, that once cast into the womb man hath no

(025-120) more to do with it. Another cause succeedeth, and undertakes the

(025-121) work, and in time brings a child (that wonderful effect from such a

(025-122) beginning!) to perfection. Again, man lets food down through his

(025-123) throat; and that once down, he hath no more to do with it. Another

(025-124) cause succeedeth and distributeth this food into the senses, and the

(025-125) affections: into life, and into strength; and doth with it those other

(025-126) many and marvellous things, that belong unto man. These things therefore

(025-127) that are so secretly and invisibly wrought and brought to pass, thou

(025-128) must use to behold and contemplate; and not the things themselves only,

(025-129) but the power also by which they are effected; that thou mayst behold

(025-130) it, though not with the eyes of the body, yet as plainly and visibly as

(025-131) thou canst see and discern the outward efficient cause of the depression

(025-132) and elevation of anything.

(025-133) XXVII. Ever to mind and consider with thyself; how all things that now

(025-134) are, have been heretofore much after the same sort, and after the same

(025-135) fashion that now they are: and so to think of those things which shall

(025-136) be hereafter also. Moreover, whole dramata, and uniform scenes, or

(025-137) scenes that comprehend the lives and actions of men of one calling and

(025-138) profession, as many as either in thine own experience thou hast known,

(025-139) or by reading of ancient histories; (as the whole court of Adrianus,

(025-140) the whole court of [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]], the whole court of Philippus, that of

(025-141) Alexander, that of Crœsus): to set them all before thine eyes. For thou

(025-142) shalt find that they are all but after one sort and fashion: only that

(025-143) the actors were others.

(025-144) XXVIII. As a pig that cries and flings when his throat is cut, fancy to

(025-145) thyself every one to be, that grieves for any worldly thing and takes

(025-146) on. Such a one is he also, who upon his bed alone, doth bewail

(025-147) the miseries of this our mortal life. And remember this, that Unto

(025-148) reasonable creatures only it is granted that they may willingly and

(025-149) freely submit unto [[providence|Providence]]: but absolutely to submit, is a necessity

(025-150) imposed upon all creatures equally.

(025-151) XXIX. Whatsoever it is that thou goest about, consider of it by thyself,

(025-152) and ask thyself, What? because I shall do this no more when I am dead,

(025-153) should therefore death seem grievous unto me?

(025-154) XXX. When thou art offended with any man's transgression, presently

(025-155) reflect upon thyself; and consider what thou thyself art guilty of in

(025-156) the same kind. As that thou also perchance dost think it a happiness

(025-157) either to be rich, or to live in pleasure, or to be praised and

(025-158) commended, and so of the rest in particular. For this if thou shalt call

(025-159) to mind, thou shalt soon forget thine anger; especially when at the same

(025-160) time this also shall concur in thy thoughts, that he was constrained by

(025-161) his error and ignorance so to do: for how can he choose as long as he

(025-162) is of that opinion? Do thou therefore if thou canst, take away that from

(025-163) him, that forceth him to do as he doth.
