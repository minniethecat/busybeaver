## Overview

Monimus was a Cynic philosopher of the 4th–3rd century BCE, a student of Diogenes of Sinope. He is directly referenced by Marcus Aurelius in the Meditations as an authority on the distinction between reality and mere opinion.

## Life and Career

Monimus of Syracuse was a slave who became a follower of Diogenes the Cynic. According to tradition, he feigned madness to be dismissed from service and devote himself to philosophy. The Cynics, like the Stoics who inherited much from them, emphasized living according to nature and rejecting social conventions as empty opinion.

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius (006-013) invokes Monimus in a passage on the nature of opinion: "Remember that all is but opinion and conceit, for those things are plain and apparent, which were spoken unto Monimus the Cynic; and as plain and apparent is the use that may be made of those things, if that which is true and serious in them, be received as well as that which is sweet and pleasing."

This passage illustrates a central Stoic technique: recognizing that our distress comes not from events themselves but from our judgments about them. What Monimus taught — that things are "plain and apparent" — reinforces the Stoic discipline of assent.

## Philosophical Significance

Monimus represents the Cynic strand within the Stoic tradition. His teaching that things are as they appear, stripped of the opinions we project onto them, directly supports the Stoic practice of examining impressions (phantasiai) before assenting to them. Marcus uses Monimus's example to remind himself that truth is accessible — we need only to receive it seriously rather than be distracted by what is merely "sweet and pleasing."
