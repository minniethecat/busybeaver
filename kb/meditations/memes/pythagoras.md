## Overview

Pythagoras of Samos was a pre-Socratic philosopher, mathematician, and moral teacher of the 6th century BCE. Marcus Aurelius names him alongside Heraclitus and Socrates as one of the heroic philosophers whose lives and deaths demonstrate the transience of even the greatest human achievement. For Marcus, Pythagoras represents the philosophical tradition that preceded and influenced Stoic thought.

## The Pythagorean Tradition

> Pythagoras of Samos, a philosopher, scientist, and moralist of the 6th century B.C. (034-184)

Pythagoras founded a philosophical and religious movement that combined mathematical inquiry with ethical discipline and belief in the transmigration of souls. His school emphasized purification of the soul through philosophical contemplation, mathematical study, and a regulated way of life — practices that resonated with later Stoic disciplines of self-examination and rational living.

## In the Meditations

> philosophers; Heraclitus, Pythagoras, Socrates. Where so many heroes of the old times; and then so many brave captains of the latter times. (015-046)

Marcus groups Pythagoras with Heraclitus and Socrates as the three great pre-Stoic philosophers. All three are invoked in a passage meditating on mortality: just as these towering figures have been "long since swallowed up and devoured" by time, so too will every living person pass away. The mention is not doctrinal — it is existential. Marcus is not engaging with Pythagorean teachings but reflecting on the ultimate equality of all human lives before death, regardless of intellectual achievement.

## Pythagorean Influence on Stoicism

While the Meditations does not engage deeply with Pythagorean philosophy, several Stoic themes have Pythagorean antecedents: the disciplined examination of one's actions, the belief in a rationally ordered cosmos, and the practice of viewing human life from the perspective of cosmic scale. The Pythagorean emphasis on harmony and proportion also influenced Stoic physics, particularly in the concept of the universe as an ordered whole (kosmos).
