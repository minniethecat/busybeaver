## Overview

Virtue (from Latin _virtus_, translating Greek ἀρετή / aretē) is the foundational ethical concept in [[Stoicism]], defined as living in conformity with nature — that is, in accordance with the rational order of the universe. The Stoics held that virtue alone constitutes the supreme good, and that vice alone is evil. (002-044)

## Stoic Conception

The Stoic school, founded by Zeno of Citium, defined the end of life as "living consistently" (ὁμολογουμένος ζῆν), later refined as "living in conformity with nature" (ὁμολογουμένος τῇ φύσει ζῆν). This conformity to nature constitutes the Stoic understanding of virtue. (002-003) (002-005) (002-006)

In the individual soul, virtue plays the same governing role that Providence plays in the universe: as Providence rules the cosmos, so virtue in the soul must rule the human being. (002-028) (002-029)

## The Doctrine of Indifferents

A defining feature of Stoic ethics is the radical claim that there is no intermediate state between virtue and vice. External conditions — health and sickness, wealth and poverty, pleasure and pain — are classified as "indifferents" (ἀδιάφορα), neither good nor bad in themselves. These externals merely provide the arena in which virtue exercises itself. (002-043) (002-044) (002-047) (002-048)

The ideal Wise Man (σοφός) is self-sufficient (αὐταρκής) — knowing these truths, he remains content even under extreme hardship. (002-048)

## The Cardinal Virtues

Stoic ethics enumerates four cardinal virtues, inherited from the Platonic tradition. Moderation (σωφροσύνη / sōphrosynē) was especially esteemed in Greek thought as the virtue of self-control and due measure. (002-068) The virtues also included wisdom (φρόνησις), courage (ἀνδρεία), and justice (δικαιοσύνη).

Marcus Aurelius, in Book Seven of the Meditations, reflects on the practical application of the virtues. He asks which virtue is most appropriate to each circumstance — whether meekness, fortitude, truth, or faithfulness — and reminds himself to apply the fitting virtue to the present need. (007-101) (007-102)

## Virtue in the Meditations

For Marcus Aurelius, the cultivation of virtue is inseparable from the care of the rational soul. He writes that one who truly values the rational faculty and the sacred mysteries of virtue will live free from lamentation, desire, and fear, indifferent even to the length of his life. (007-051) (007-052) (007-054)

He further observes that virtue is not merely a theoretical notion but a practical force: it is a motion of the soul unlike ordinary impulses, and its exercise is visible in the daily conduct of those we encounter. (013-145) (015-079)
