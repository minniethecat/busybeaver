## Overview

Marcus Junius Brutus (85–42 BCE), traditionally known as one of the liberators of Rome who ended the monarchy and as one of the assassins of Julius Caesar, was a figure of profound symbolic importance in Roman political thought. For Marcus Aurelius, Brutus was an exemplar of republican virtue introduced through his philosophical education.

## Life and Career

The name Brutus carries dual significance: (1) Lucius Junius Brutus, who expelled the last king of Rome and founded the Republic, and (2) Marcus Junius Brutus, who participated in the assassination of Julius Caesar to preserve republican liberty. Both were regarded as symbols of resistance to tyranny in the Stoic-influenced Roman political tradition.

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius (004-011) acknowledges Brutus among the exemplars introduced to him by his brother Severus: "from my brother Severus... by whom also I came to the knowledge of Thrasea and Helvidius, and Cato, and Dio, and Brutus. He it was also that did put me in the first conceit and desire of an equal commonwealth, administered by justice and equality." This passage places Brutus in a select lineage of republican martyrs whose examples shaped Marcus's conception of just governance.

## Philosophical Significance

Brutus represented for the Stoic-influenced senatorial class the ideal of principled resistance to arbitrary power. His example informed Marcus's own struggle to rule as a philosopher-king — exercising absolute power while remaining answerable to reason, justice, and the common good. The tension between imperial authority and republican ideals was a defining feature of Marcus's reign.
