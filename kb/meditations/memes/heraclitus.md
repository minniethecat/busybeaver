## Overview

Heraclitus of Ephesus (c. 535–475 BCE) was a pre-Socratic philosopher whose doctrine of universal flux — that everything is in constant change, like a river into which one cannot step twice — became a foundational influence on Stoic physics. Marcus Aurelius explicitly cites Heraclitus and urges himself never to forget his teachings. (010-001) (034-128)

## Doctrine of Flux

Heraclitus taught that the fundamental nature of reality is change: all things are in perpetual transformation, governed by the logos (reason) that harmonizes opposites. Fire, the most changeable element, was for Heraclitus the primary substance. This doctrine of universal impermanence became central to Stoic cosmology, which held that the cosmos periodically dissolves into fire and is reborn. Marcus returns to the Heraclitean theme of flux throughout the Meditations, reminding himself that everything — empires, bodies, reputations — is in constant transformation.

## In the Meditations

Marcus treats Heraclitus with special reverence. He opens one book with the instruction: "Let that of Heraclitus never be out of thy mind" — the teaching that earth becomes water, water becomes air, and all things are in cyclical transformation. (010-001) He reflects that Heraclitus spoke of souls dissolving into the elements, and lists him among the great philosophers alongside Pythagoras and Socrates. (015-046) (014-158) Marcus also uses Heraclitus to contemplate mortality: "what are these to Diogenes, Heraclitus, and Socrates?" — all great minds, all long dead, yet their wisdom persists. (018-121) He records biographical details, noting that Heraclitus died of dropsy, a reminder that even the wisest are subject to the body's frailty. (006-117)

## Influence on Stoicism

Heraclitus's concept of the logos — the rational principle that orders the cosmos — was adopted and transformed by the Stoics into their central theological doctrine. The Stoic god is, in essence, the Heraclitean logos made personal and providential. The doctrine of cosmic cycles (ekpyrosis) and the unity of opposites also derive from Heraclitus. For Marcus, who was deeply influenced by Stoic physics, Heraclitus provided the cosmological framework within which ethical practice made sense: if everything is in flux, then attachment to any particular arrangement of things is irrational.
