## Overview

Lucilla was the daughter of Marcus Aurelius and Faustina the Younger, and wife of Lucius Verus, Marcus's co-emperor. She was a central figure in the Antonine dynasty, connecting the imperial household to the line of succession. After Verus's death, Marcus arranged her remarriage to Tiberius Claudius Pompeianus, a respected senator. Lucilla's life exemplifies the dynastic role of Roman imperial women.

## Family and Marriage

As one of Marcus Aurelius's daughters, Lucilla was born into the imperial family around 150 CE. Her marriage to Lucius Verus in 164 CE strengthened the political bond between the two co-emperors. The union produced several children, though none survived to hold significant power.

> He married Lucilla, daughter of M. A., and died 169 A.D. (034-230)

After Verus's death in 169 CE, Lucilla was remarried to Pompeianus, a loyal general of Marcus. Despite her reduced status, she remained a prominent figure at court. Her later involvement in a conspiracy against her brother Commodus led to her exile and death.

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius reflects on Lucilla in the context of impermanence and the transience of all human bonds. The mention of her burial of Verus — and her own subsequent burial by others — serves as a meditation on the universal cycle of mortality.

> XXIV. Lucilla buried Verus; then was Lucilla herself buried by others. So Secunda Maximus, then Secunda herself. So Epitynchanus, Diotimus. (019-092)

This passage appears in a sequence where Marcus contemplates how every person who buries another will themselves be buried, emphasizing the Stoic theme of universal mortality and the equality of all human lives before death.

## The Antonine Context

> Lucilla, daughter of M. Aurelius, and wife of Verus, whom she survived. (034-140)

Lucilla's position in the Antonine dynasty illustrates the broader Stoic principle that high birth and imperial status offer no protection against suffering and mortality. Her story — from emperor's daughter to exile — reflects the Stoic teaching that fortune is indifferent to rank.
