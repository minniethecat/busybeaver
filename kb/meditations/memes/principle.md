## Overview

Principles (Greek δόγματα / dogmata, "doctrines, resolutions") in Stoic philosophy are the core philosophical tenets that the practitioner internalizes and applies to daily life. Unlike abstract theories, the dogmata are practical rules of judgment — brief, memorable formulations of Stoic doctrine that can be called to mind in moments of decision. Marcus Aurelius structured his Meditations around the continual reinforcement of such principles, "seeking to call his principles to mind, and to strengthen them." (002-163) (002-166)

## In Stoic Practice

The Stoic school placed great emphasis on the internalization of correct principles. The "ruling principle" (ἡγεμονικόν) — the rational faculty that governs the soul — must be equipped with sound dogmata to judge impressions correctly. Just as the divine ruling principle "makes all things work together" in the cosmos, so the human ruling principle must order the inner life according to right doctrines. (002-024) (002-066)

The goal of Stoic education was not merely to understand these principles intellectually but to possess them so thoroughly that they arise spontaneously — to be "perfect in right principles, that when the time comes, the action may follow." (003-056) The ruling principle itself is one of the central dogmata: the divine element within, the faculty that distinguishes true from false, and that must be kept pure and unobstructed. (002-020)

The Stoic "art of living" (τέχνη περὶ βίον) consists precisely in this: the systematic acquisition and application of correct principles to every impression and impulse. Epictetus, whose teachings Marcus studied closely, made the examination of impressions against principles the center of his practical program. Before assenting to any impression, the Stoic asks: "Is this in my control? Does it concern what is truly good or bad?" The principles supply the answers.

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius's Meditations is itself an extended exercise in reinforcing principles. He writes that one who "faithfully carries out his own principle" lives the philosophical life regardless of external circumstances. (002-166) Throughout the work, he returns to core dogmata: that only virtue is good, that externals are indifferent, that all things are interconnected, that death is natural and not to be feared. Each book restates these principles in new formulations, as if Marcus is strengthening philosophical muscles through repetition.

The "retiring of thyself" that he recommends is the practice of withdrawing into the inner citadel where the principles are stored and refreshed. (008-046) The philosopher "doth put philosophy in practice" not by abstract argument but by living according to these internalized principles. (009-066) Marcus calls principles the "arms" of the philosopher — the defenses against error and passion that must be kept ready at all times. When he exhorts himself to "be upright, not kept upright," he points to the difference between merely knowing a principle and being shaped by it. (003-042) The principle must become character.

## Key Stoic Principles in the Meditations

Among the principles Marcus rehearses most frequently are: the distinction between what is in our power and what is not (the "dichotomy of control"); the doctrine that virtue is the sole good and vice the sole evil; the recognition that all things change and pass away; the identification of nature with divine reason; the duty to act for the common good; and the practice of examining every impression before assenting. These are not separate doctrines but facets of a single integrated outlook — the Stoic "art of living" — that Marcus applies to every situation the imperial life presents.
