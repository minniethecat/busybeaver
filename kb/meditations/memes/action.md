## Overview

Action (Greek: *katorthomata*, "right actions" or "proper functions") is the practical expression of Stoic virtue. In Stoic ethics, an action is not evaluated by its outcome — which lies beyond our control — but by the intention and judgment that animate it. Marcus Aurelius urges himself to "go about every action as thy last action" (005-064), treating each deed as a complete moral performance.

## In Stoic Ethics

The Stoic doctrine of proper functions (*kathekonta*) distinguishes between actions appropriate to one's nature and those that achieve the perfection of virtue. Zeno first articulated that "in a man of perfect wisdom, certain actions were proper" (002-057). These *katorthomata* represent the fullest realization of moral conduct — actions performed from right reason, with full understanding and virtuous intent.

Not all appropriate actions qualify as right actions. An appropriate action is one that a reasonable person would do in given circumstances; a right action requires the additional element of the agent's virtuous disposition. The Stoics held that the sage alone performs *katorthomata*, while ordinary people perform *kathekonta* — actions that are reasonable but may lack the perfect internal alignment of wisdom (002-057).

The scope of action is bounded by what lies within our power. External results — success, reputation, the reactions of others — are "indifferents" that do not determine an action's moral worth. What matters is whether the action proceeds from sound judgment and aims at the common good (010-112).

## Core Characteristics

**Deliberate intent.** Marcus insists that one should "order all thy actions by that knowledge" of nature and virtue (006-083). Every action must be preceded by reflection: "at every action a man must privately by way of admonition suggest unto himself, what relation it hath to the common good" (009-020). Rash or unexamined action is incompatible with Stoic discipline.

**Social orientation.** Right action is inherently sociable — it serves the community of rational beings. Marcus writes of "actions tending to the common good" (010-112) and calls for actions that are "charitable" and speech that is truthful (009-107). An action that harms the community cannot be right, regardless of its other qualities (011-146).

**Self-sufficiency.** The Stoic acts without attachment to outcomes. "I continue my course by actions according to nature, until I fall and sink into my rest" (010-127) — the action itself is the reward, not its consequences. This detachment from results is what frees the Stoic from anxiety and disappointment.

**Simplicity.** Marcus repeatedly warns against unnecessary actions. One should "confine himself to necessary actions; to such and so many only, as the reason of a man intended for society doth require" (009-013). Superfluous activity drains energy and distracts from what truly matters (009-022).

## In the Meditations

The theme of right action runs through the Meditations as a practical daily discipline. In Book 5, Marcus frames each action as a potential final act: "if thou shalt go about every action as thy last action, free from all vanity, from all passionate affection" (005-064). This *memento mori* approach transforms mundane deeds into occasions for virtue.

Book 9 develops the social dimension most fully. Marcus reflects on the "paucity of actions" that are truly necessary (009-016) and warns against multiplying unnecessary ones. The ideal is "to be just in all his actions" (009-129) while recognizing that "the final ends and consummations of actions are nothing at all to the soul" (011-151) — outcomes are transient; only the character expressed in action endures.

The Meditations closes Book 12 with the resolution to "continue my course by actions according to nature" (010-127), affirming that right action is not a destination but a continuous practice — a way of moving through the world aligned with reason and nature.
