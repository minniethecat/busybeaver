# Section 8

(008-001) II. Let nothing be done rashly, and at random, but all things according

(008-002) to the most exact and perfect rules of art.

(008-003) III. They seek for themselves private retiring

(008-004) places, as country villages, the sea-shore, mountains; yea thou thyself

(008-005) art wont to long much after such places. But all this thou must know

(008-006) proceeds from simplicity in the highest degree. At what time soever thou

(008-007) wilt, it is in thy power to retire into thyself, and to be at rest, and

(008-008) free from all businesses. A man cannot any whither retire better than

(008-009) to his own soul; he especially who is beforehand provided of such

(008-010) things within, which whensoever he doth withdraw himself to look in, may

(008-011) presently afford unto him perfect ease and tranquillity. By tranquillity

(008-012) I understand a decent orderly disposition and carriage, free from

(008-013) all confusion and tumultuousness. Afford then thyself this retiring

(008-014) continually, and thereby refresh and renew thyself. Let these precepts

(008-015) be brief and fundamental, which as soon as thou dost call them to mind,

(008-016) may suffice thee to purge thy soul throughly, and to send thee away well

(008-017) pleased with those things whatsoever they be, which now again after this

(008-018) short withdrawing of thy soul into herself thou dost return unto. For

(008-019) what is it that thou art offended at? Can it be at the wickedness of

(008-020) men, when thou dost call to mind this conclusion, that all reasonable

(008-021) creatures are made one for another? and that it is part of justice to

(008-022) bear with them? and that it is against their wills that they offend?

(008-023) and how many already, who once likewise prosecuted their enmities,

(008-024) suspected, hated, and fiercely contended, are now long ago stretched

(008-025) out, and reduced unto ashes? It is time for thee to make an end. As for

(008-026) those things which among the common chances of the world happen unto

(008-027) thee as thy particular lot and portion, canst thou be displeased with

(008-028) any of them, when thou dost call that our ordinary dilemma to mind,

(008-029) either a providence, or Democritus his atoms; and with it, whatsoever we

(008-030) brought to prove that the whole world is as it were one city? And as for

(008-031) thy body, what canst thou fear, if thou dost consider that thy mind and

(008-032) [[knowledge|understanding]], when once it hath recollected itself, and knows its own

(008-033) power, hath in this life and breath (whether it run smoothly and gently,

(008-034) or whether harshly and rudely), no interest at all, but is altogether

(008-035) indifferent: and whatsoever else thou hast heard and assented unto

(008-036) concerning either pain or pleasure? But the care of thine honour and

(008-037) [[fame|reputation]] will perchance distract thee? How can that be, if thou

(008-038) dost look back, and consider both how quickly all things that are, are

(008-039) forgotten, and what an immense chaos of eternity was before, and will

(008-040) follow after all things: and the vanity of praise, and the inconstancy

(008-041) and variableness of human judgments and opinions, and the narrowness of

(008-042) the place, wherein it is limited and circumscribed? For the whole earth

(008-043) is but as one point; and of it, this inhabited part of it, is but a very

(008-044) little part; and of this part, how many in number, and what manner of

(008-045) men are they, that will commend thee? What remains then, but that thou

(008-046) often put in practice this kind of retiring of thyself, to this little

(008-047) part of thyself; and above all things, keep thyself from distraction,

(008-048) and intend not anything vehemently, but be free and consider all things,

(008-049) as a man whose proper object is [[virtue|Virtue]], as a man whose true nature is

(008-050) to be kind and sociable, as a citizen, as a mortal creature. Among

(008-051) other things, which to consider, and look into thou must use to withdraw

(008-052) thyself, let those two be among the most obvious and at hand. One, that

(008-053) the things or objects themselves reach not unto the soul, but stand

(008-054) without still and quiet, and that it is from the opinion only which is

(008-055) within, that all the tumult and all the trouble doth proceed. The next,

(008-056) that all these things, which now thou seest, shall within a very little

(008-057) while be changed, and be no more: and ever call to mind, how many

(008-058) changes and alterations in the world thou thyself hast already been an

(008-059) eyewitness of in thy time. This world is mere change, and this life,

(008-060) opinion.

(008-061) IV. If to understand and to be reasonable be common unto all men, then

(008-062) is that reason, for which we are termed reasonable, common unto all. If

(008-063) reason is general, then is that reason also, which prescribeth what is

(008-064) to be done and what not, common unto all. If that, then law. If law,

(008-065) then are we fellow-citizens. If so, then are we partners in some one

(008-066) commonweal. If so, then the world is as it were a city. For which other

(008-067) commonweal is it, that all men can be said to be members of? From this

(008-068) common city it is, that understanding, reason, and law is derived unto

(008-069) us, for from whence else? For as that which in me is earthly I have from

(008-070) some common earth; and that which is moist from some other element is

(008-071) imparted; as my breath and life hath its proper fountain; and that

(008-072) likewise which is dry and fiery in me: (for there is nothing which doth

(008-073) not proceed from something; as also there is nothing that can be reduced

(008-074) unto mere nothing:) so also is there some common beginning from whence

(008-075) my understanding hath proceeded.

(008-076) V. As generation is, so also death, a secret of nature's wisdom: a

(008-077) mixture of elements, resolved into the same elements again, a thing

(008-078) surely which no man ought to be ashamed of: in a series of other fatal

(008-079) events and consequences, which a rational creature is subject unto,

(008-080) not improper or incongruous, nor contrary to the natural and proper

(008-081) constitution of man himself.

(008-082) VI. Such and such things, from such and such causes, must of necessity

(008-083) proceed. He that would not have such things to happen, is as he that

(008-084) would have the fig-tree grow without any sap or moisture. In sum,

(008-085) remember this, that within a very little while, both thou and he shall

(008-086) both be dead, and after a little while more, not so much as your names

(008-087) and memories shall be remaining.

(008-088) VII. Let opinion be taken away, and no man will think himself wronged.

(008-089) If no man shall think himself wronged, then is there no more any such

(008-090) thing as wrong. That which makes not man himself the worse, cannot

(008-091) make his life the worse, neither can it hurt him either inwardly

(008-092) or outwardly. It was expedient in nature that it should be so, and

(008-093) therefore necessary.

(008-094) VIII. Whatsoever doth happen in the world, doth happen justly, and so if

(008-095) thou dost well take heed, thou shalt find it. I say not only in right

(008-096) order by a series of inevitable consequences, but according to justice

(008-097) and as it were by way of equal distribution, according to the true worth

(008-098) of everything. Continue then to take notice of it, as thou hast begun,

(008-099) and whatsoever thou dost, do it not without this proviso, that it be a

(008-100) thing of that nature that a good man (as the word good is properly

(008-101) taken) may do it. This observe carefully in every action.

(008-102) IX. Conceit no such things, as he that wrongeth thee conceiveth,

(008-103) or would have thee to conceive, but look into the matter itself, and see

(008-104) what it is in very truth.

(008-105) X. These two rules, thou must have always in a readiness. First, do

(008-106) nothing at all, but what reason proceeding from that regal and supreme

(008-107) part, shall for the good and benefit of men, suggest unto thee. And

(008-108) secondly, if any man that is present shall be able to rectify thee or to

(008-109) turn thee from some erroneous persuasion, that thou be always ready to

(008-110) change thy mind, and this change to proceed, not from any respect of any

(008-111) pleasure or credit thereon depending, but always from some probable

(008-112) apparent ground of justice, or of some public good thereby to be

(008-113) furthered; or from some other such inducement.

(008-114) XI. Hast thou reason? I have. Why then makest thou not use of it? For if

(008-115) thy reason do her part, what more canst thou require?

(008-116) XII. As a part hitherto thou hast had a particular subsistence: and now

(008-117) shalt thou vanish away into the common substance of Him, who first begot

(008-118) thee, or rather thou shalt be resumed again into that original rational

(008-119) substance, out of which all others have issued, and are propagated.

(008-120) Many small pieces of frankincense are set upon the same altar, one drops

(008-121) first and is consumed, another after; and it comes all to one.

(008-122) XIII. Within ten days, if so happen, thou shalt be esteemed a god of

(008-123) them, who now if thou shalt return to the dogmata and to the honouring

(008-124) of reason, will esteem of thee no better than of a mere brute, and of an

(008-125) ape.

(008-126) XIV. Not as though thou hadst thousands of years to live. [[death|Death]] hangs

(008-127) over thee: whilst yet thou livest, whilst thou mayest, be good.

(008-128) XV. Now much time and leisure doth he gain, who is not curious to know

(008-129) what his neighbour hath said, or hath done, or hath attempted, but only

(008-130) what he doth himself, that it may be just and holy? or to express it in

(008-131) Agathos' words, Not to look about upon the evil conditions of others,

(008-132) but to run on straight in the line, without any loose and extravagant

(008-133) agitation.

(008-134) XVI. He who is greedy of credit and reputation after his death, doth

(008-135) not consider, that they themselves by whom he is remembered, shall soon

(008-136) after every one of them be dead; and they likewise that succeed those;

(008-137) until at last all memory, which hitherto by the succession of men

(008-138) admiring and soon after dying hath had its course, be quite extinct.

(008-139) But suppose that both they that shall remember thee, and thy memory

(008-140) with them should be immortal, what is that to thee? I will not say to

(008-141) thee after thou art dead; but even to thee living, what is thy praise?

(008-142) But only for a secret and politic consideration, which we call

(008-143) οἰκονομίαν, or dispensation. For as for that, that it is the gift of

(008-144) nature, whatsoever is commended in thee, what might be objected from

(008-145) thence, let that now that we are upon another consideration be omitted

(008-146) as unseasonable. That which is fair and goodly, whatsoever it be, and

(008-147) in what respect soever it be, that it is fair and goodly, it is so of

(008-148) itself, and terminates in itself, not admitting praise as a part or

(008-149) member: that therefore which is praised, is not thereby made either

(008-150) better or worse. This I understand even of those things, that are

(008-151) commonly called fair and good, as those which are commended either for

(008-152) the matter itself, or for curious workmanship. As for that which is

(008-153) truly good, what can it stand in need of more than either justice or

(008-154) truth; or more than either kindness and modesty? Which of all those,

(008-155) either becomes good or fair, because commended; or dispraised suffers

(008-156) any damage? Doth the emerald become worse in itself, or more vile if it

(008-157) be not commended? Doth gold, or ivory, or purple? Is there anything

(008-158) that doth though never so common, as a knife, a flower, or a tree?

(008-159) XVII. If so be that the souls remain after death (say they that will not

(008-160) believe it); how is the air from all eternity able to contain them? How

(008-161) is the earth (say I) ever from that time able to Contain the bodies

(008-162) of them that are buried? For as here the change and resolution of dead

(008-163) bodies into another kind of subsistence (whatsoever it be;) makes place

(008-164) for other dead bodies: so the souls after death transferred into the

(008-165) air, after they have conversed there a while, are either by way of

(008-166) transmutation, or transfusion, or conflagration, received again into

(008-167) that original rational substance, from which all others do proceed:

(008-168) and so give way to those souls, who before coupled and associated unto

(008-169) bodies, now begin to subsist single. This, upon a supposition that the

(008-170) souls after death do for a while subsist single, may be answered. And

(008-171) here, (besides the number of bodies, so buried and contained by the

(008-172) earth), we may further consider the number of several beasts, eaten

(008-173) by us men, and by other creatures. For notwithstanding that such a

(008-174) multitude of them is daily consumed, and as it were buried in the bodies

(008-175) of the eaters, yet is the same place and body able to contain them, by

(008-176) reason of their conversion, partly into blood, partly into air and fire.

(008-177) What in these things is the speculation of truth? to divide things into

(008-178) that which is passive and material; and that which is active and formal.
