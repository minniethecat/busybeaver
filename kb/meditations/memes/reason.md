## Overview

Reason (Greek λόγος / logos, Latin _ratio_) is the ruling principle (ἡγεμονικόν / _hēgemonikon_) in Stoic philosophy — the divine rational element immanent in both the cosmos and the human soul. For Marcus Aurelius, reason is the faculty by which one discerns truth, governs the passions, and lives in accordance with nature. (002-019) (005-019)

## In Stoic Cosmology

In Stoic physics, reason is one of the manifold forms through which the immanent spiritual force — identical with God — manifests itself in the material universe. Alongside fire, æther, spirit, and soul, reason is listed among the expressions of the divine ruling principle that pervades all things. (002-018) (002-019)

The universe itself is God, and the popular deities are understood as manifestations of this single rational principle. The divine ruling principle directs all things toward the good of the whole, and the soul of each human being is an emanation from this godhead. (002-021) (002-022) (002-023)

## Reason as Ruler of the Soul

Just as Providence rules the universe, so reason — or virtue — must rule the individual soul. The Stoics identified the rational faculty as "the mistress and overruling part of man." Marcus Aurelius repeatedly urges the suppression of unreasonable desires and passions, insisting that all action be guided by reason and discretion. (002-028) (002-029) (005-019) (005-029) (005-084)

The goal is a life free from vanity and from "passionate and wilful aberration from reason." When one acts from reason rather than impulse, one participates in the same divine nature that orders the cosmos. (005-010) (005-065)

## Practical Reason in the Meditations

Throughout the Meditations, Marcus Aurelius returns to the imperative of subjecting every impression and impulse to rational scrutiny. He asks himself to regard nothing — "though never so little" — except by the standard of right reason. The cultivation of reason is central to his daily spiritual exercises. (003-158) (005-010)

From his teachers and predecessors, Marcus learned the virtues of reasonableness — simplicity, gratitude, and a love of truth. These qualities, exemplified in figures like Antoninus Pius, represent reason embodied in conduct. (002-177)
