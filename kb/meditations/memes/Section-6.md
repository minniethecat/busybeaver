# Section 6

(006-001) XII. If thou shouldst live three thousand, or as many as ten thousands

(006-002) of years, yet remember this, that man can part with no life properly,

(006-003) save with that little part of life, which he now lives: and that which

(006-004) he lives, is no other, than that which at every instant he parts with.

(006-005) That then which is longest of duration, and that which is shortest, come

(006-006) both to one effect. For although in regard of that which is already past

(006-007) there may be some inequality, yet that time which is now present and

(006-008) in being, is equal unto all men. And that being it which we part with

(006-009) whensoever we die, it doth manifestly appear, that it can be but a

(006-010) moment of time, that we then part with. For as for that which is either

(006-011) past or to come, a man cannot be said properly to part with it. For

(006-012) how should a man part with that which he hath not? These two things

(006-013) therefore thou must remember. First, that all things in the world from

(006-014) all eternity, by a perpetual revolution of the same times and things

(006-015) ever continued and renewed, are of one kind and nature; so that whether

(006-016) for a hundred or two hundred years only, or for an infinite space of

(006-017) time, a man see those things which are still the same, it can be no

(006-018) matter of great moment. And secondly, that that life which any the

(006-019) longest liver, or the shortest liver parts with, is for length and

(006-020) duration the very same, for that only which is present, is that, which

(006-021) either of them can lose, as being that only which they have; for that

(006-022) which he hath not, no man can truly be said to lose.

(006-023) XIII. Remember that all is but opinion and conceit, for those things

(006-024) are plain and apparent, which were spoken unto Monimus the Cynic; and as

(006-025) plain and apparent is the use that may be made of those things, if that

(006-026) which is true and serious in them, be received as well as that which is

(006-027) sweet and pleasing.

(006-028) XIV. A man's soul doth wrong and disrespect itself first and especially,

(006-029) when as much as in itself lies it becomes an aposteme, and as it were an

(006-030) excrescency of the world, for to be grieved and displeased with anything

(006-031) that happens in the world, is direct apostacy from the nature of the

(006-032) universe; part of which, all particular natures of the world, are.

(006-033) Secondly, when she either is averse from any man, or led by contrary

(006-034) desires or affections, tending to his hurt and prejudice; such as are

(006-035) the souls of them that are angry. Thirdly, when she is overcome by any

(006-036) pleasure or pain. Fourthly, when she doth dissemble, and covertly and

(006-037) falsely either doth or saith anything. Fifthly, when she doth either

(006-038) affect or endeavour anything to no certain end, but rashly and without

(006-039) due ratiocination and consideration, how consequent or inconsequent it

(006-040) is to the common end. For even the least things ought not to be done,

(006-041) without relation unto the end; and the end of the reasonable creatures

(006-042) is, to follow and obey him, who is the reason as it were, and the law of

(006-043) this great city, and ancient commonwealth.

(006-044) XV. The time of a man's life is as a point; the substance of it ever

(006-045) flowing, the sense obscure; and the whole composition of the body

(006-046) tending to corruption. His soul is restless, fortune uncertain, and fame

(006-047) doubtful; to be brief, as a stream so are all things belonging to the

(006-048) body; as a dream, or as a smoke, so are all that belong unto the soul.

(006-049) Our life is a warfare, and a mere pilgrimage. [[fame|Fame]] after life is no

(006-050) better than oblivion. What is it then that will adhere and follow? Only

(006-051) one thing, philosophy. And philosophy doth consist in this, for a man to

(006-052) preserve that spirit which is within him, from all manner of contumelies

(006-053) and injuries, and above all pains or pleasures; never to do anything

(006-054) either rashly, or feignedly, or hypocritically: wholly to depend from

(006-055) himself and his own proper actions: all things that happen unto him to

(006-056) embrace contentedly, as coming from Him from whom he himself also came;

(006-057) and above all things, with all meekness and a calm cheerfulness, to

(006-058) expect death, as being nothing else but the resolution of those

(006-059) elements, of which every creature is composed. And if the elements

(006-060) themselves suffer nothing by this their perpetual conversion of one into

(006-061) another, that dissolution, and alteration, which is so common unto all,

(006-062) why should it be feared by any? Is not this according to nature? But

(006-063) nothing that is according to nature can be evil.

(006-064) _Whilst I was at Carnuntum._

(006-065) THE THIRD BOOK

(006-066) I. A man must not only consider how daily his life wasteth and

(006-067) decreaseth, but this also, that if he live long, he cannot be certain,

(006-068) whether his [[knowledge|understanding]] shall continue so able and sufficient,

(006-069) for either discreet consideration, in matter of businesses; or for

(006-070) contemplation: it being the thing, whereon true knowledge of things both

(006-071) divine and human, doth depend. For if once he shall begin to dote,

(006-072) his respiration, nutrition, his imaginative, and appetitive, and other

(006-073) natural faculties, may still continue the same: he shall find no want of

(006-074) them. But how to make that right use of himself that he should, how

(006-075) to observe exactly in all things that which is right and just, how to

(006-076) redress and rectify all wrong, or sudden apprehensions and imaginations,

(006-077) and even of this particular, whether he should live any longer or no, to

(006-078) consider duly; for all such things, wherein the best strength and vigour

(006-079) of the mind is most requisite; his power and ability will be past and

(006-080) gone. Thou must hasten therefore; not only because thou art every day

(006-081) nearer unto death than other, but also because that intellective faculty

(006-082) in thee, whereby thou art enabled to know the true nature of things, and

(006-083) to order all thy actions by that knowledge, doth daily waste and decay:

(006-084) or, may fail thee before thou die.

(006-085) II. This also thou must observe, that whatsoever it is that naturally

(006-086) doth happen to things natural, hath somewhat in itself that is pleasing

(006-087) and delightful: as a great loaf when it is baked, some parts of it

(006-088) cleave as it were, and part asunder, and make the crust of it rugged and

(006-089) unequal, and yet those parts of it, though in some sort it be against

(006-090) the art and intention of baking itself, that they are thus cleft and

(006-091) parted, which should have been and were first made all even and uniform,

(006-092) they become it well nevertheless, and have a certain peculiar property,

(006-093) to stir the appetite. So figs are accounted fairest and ripest then,

(006-094) when they begin to shrink, and wither as it were. So ripe olives, when

(006-095) they are next to putrefaction, then are they in their proper beauty. The

(006-096) hanging down of grapes--the brow of a lion, the froth of a foaming wild

(006-097) boar, and many other like things, though by themselves considered, they

(006-098) are far from any beauty, yet because they happen naturally, they both

(006-099) are comely, and delightful; so that if a man shall with a profound mind

(006-100) and apprehension, consider all things in the world, even among all those

(006-101) things which are but mere accessories and natural appendices as it were,

(006-102) there will scarce appear anything unto him, wherein he will not find

(006-103) matter of pleasure and [[pleasure|delight]]. So will he behold with as much pleasure

(006-104) the true _rictus_ of wild beasts, as those which by skilful painters and

(006-105) other artificers are imitated. So will he be able to perceive the proper

(006-106) ripeness and beauty of old age, whether in man or woman: and whatsoever

(006-107) else it is that is beautiful and alluring in whatsoever is, with chaste

(006-108) and continent eyes he will soon find out and discern. Those and many

(006-109) other things will he discern, not credible unto every one, but unto them

(006-110) only who are truly and familiarly acquainted, both with nature itself,

(006-111) and all natural things.

(006-112) III. Hippocrates having cured many sicknesses, fell sick himself and

(006-113) died. The Chaldeans and Astrologians having foretold the deaths of

(006-114) divers, were afterwards themselves surprised by the fates. Alexander and

(006-115) Pompeius, and Caius Cæsar, having destroyed so many towns, and cut

(006-116) off in the field so many thousands both of horse and foot, yet they

(006-117) themselves at last were fain to part with their own lives. Heraclitus

(006-118) having written so many natural tracts concerning the last and general

(006-119) conflagration of the world, died afterwards all filled with water

(006-120) within, and all bedaubed with dirt and dung without. Lice killed

(006-121) Democritus; and Socrates, another sort of vermin, wicked ungodly men.

(006-122) How then stands the case? Thou hast taken ship, thou hast sailed, thou

(006-123) art come to land, go out, if to another life, there also shalt thou find

(006-124) gods, who are everywhere. If all life and sense shall cease, then shalt

(006-125) thou cease also to be subject to either pains or pleasures; and to serve

(006-126) and tend this vile cottage; so much the viler, by how much that which

(006-127) ministers unto it doth excel; the one being a rational substance, and a

(006-128) spirit, the other nothing but earth and blood.

(006-129) IV. Spend not the remnant of thy days in thoughts and fancies concerning

(006-130) other men, when it is not in relation to some common good, when by it

(006-131) thou art hindered from some other better work. That is, spend not thy

(006-132) time in thinking, what such a man doth, and to what end: what he saith,

(006-133) and what he thinks, and what he is about, and such other things or

(006-134) curiosities, which make a man to rove and wander from the care and

(006-135) observation of that part of himself, which is rational, and overruling.

(006-136) See therefore in the whole series and connection of thy thoughts, that

(006-137) thou be careful to prevent whatsoever is idle and impertinent: but

(006-138) especially, whatsoever is curious and malicious: and thou must use

(006-139) thyself to think only of such things, of which if a man upon a sudden

(006-140) should ask thee, what it is that thou art now thinking, thou mayest

(006-141) answer This, and That, freely and boldly, that so by thy thoughts it may

(006-142) presently appear that in all thee is sincere, and peaceable; as becometh

(006-143) one that is made for society, and regards not pleasures, nor gives way

(006-144) to any voluptuous imaginations at all: free from all contentiousness,

(006-145) envy, and suspicion, and from whatsoever else thou wouldest blush to

(006-146) confess thy thoughts were set upon. He that is such, is he surely that

(006-147) doth not put off to lay hold on that which is best indeed, a very priest

(006-148) and minister of the gods, well acquainted and in good correspondence

(006-149) with him especially that is seated and placed within himself, as in

(006-150) a temple and sacrary: to whom also he keeps and preserves himself

(006-151) unspotted by pleasure, undaunted by pain; free from any manner of wrong,

(006-152) or contumely, by himself offered unto himself: not capable of any evil

(006-153) from others: a wrestler of the best sort, and for the highest prize,

(006-154) that he may not be cast down by any passion or affection of his own;

(006-155) deeply dyed and drenched in [[justice|righteousness]], embracing and accepting with

(006-156) his whole heart whatsoever either happeneth or is allotted unto him. One

(006-157) who not often, nor without some great necessity tending to some public

(006-158) good, mindeth what any other, either speaks, or doth, or purposeth: for

(006-159) those things only that are in his own power, or that are truly his own,

(006-160) are the objects of his employments, and his thoughts are ever taken

(006-161) up with those things, which of the whole universe are by the fates or

(006-162) [[providence|Providence]] destinated and appropriated unto himself. Those things that

(006-163) are his own, and in his own power, he himself takes order, for that they

(006-164) be good: and as for those that happen unto him, he believes them to be

(006-165) so. For that lot and portion which is assigned to every one, as it is

(006-166) unavoidable and necessary, so is it always profitable. He remembers

(006-167) besides that whatsoever partakes of reason, is akin unto him, and that

(006-168) to care for all men generally, is agreeing to the nature of a man: but

(006-169) as for honour and praise, that they ought not generally to be admitted

(006-170) and accepted of from all, but from such only, who live according to

(006-171) nature. As for them that do not, what manner of men they be at home,

(006-172) or abroad; day or night, how conditioned themselves with what manner of

(006-173) conditions, or with men of what conditions they moil and pass away

(006-174) the time together, he knoweth, and remembers right well, he therefore

(006-175) regards not such praise and approbation, as proceeding from them, who

(006-176) cannot like and approve themselves.
