# Section 18

(018-001) XXXVI. Take heed lest at any time thou stand so affected, though towards

(018-002) unnatural evil men, as ordinary men are commonly one towards another.

(018-003) XXXVII. How know we whether Socrates were so eminent indeed, and of so

(018-004) extraordinary a disposition? For that he died more gloriously, that he

(018-005) disputed with the Sophists more subtilty; that he watched in the frost

(018-006) more assiduously; that being commanded to fetch innocent Salaminius, he

(018-007) refused to do it more generously; all this will not serve. Nor that he

(018-008) walked in the streets, with much gravity and majesty, as was objected

(018-009) unto him by his adversaries: which nevertheless a man may well doubt of,

(018-010) whether it were so or no, or, which above all the rest, if so be that

(018-011) it were true, a man would well consider of, whether commendable, or

(018-012) dis-commendable. The thing therefore that we must inquire into, is this;

(018-013) what manner of soul Socrates had: whether his disposition was such; as

(018-014) that all that he stood upon, and sought after in this world, was barely

(018-015) this, that he might ever carry himself justly towards men, and holily

(018-016) towards the Gods. Neither vexing himself to no purpose at the wickedness

(018-017) of others, nor yet ever condescending to any man's evil fact, or evil

(018-018) intentions, through either fear, or engagement of friendship. Whether of

(018-019) those things that happened unto him by God's appointment, he neither did

(018-020) wonder at any when it did happen, or thought it intolerable in the trial

(018-021) of it. And lastly, whether he never did suffer his mind to sympathise

(018-022) with the senses, and affections of the body. For we must not think that

(018-023) [[nature|Nature]] hath so mixed and tempered it with the body, as that she hath not

(018-024) power to circumscribe herself, and by herself to intend her own ends and

(018-025) occasions.

(018-026) XXXVIII. For it is a thing very possible, that a man should be a very

(018-027) divine man, and yet be altogether unknown. This thou must ever be

(018-028) mindful of, as of this also, that a man's true happiness doth consist

(018-029) in very few things. And that although thou dost despair, that thou shalt

(018-030) ever be a good either logician, or naturalist, yet thou art never the

(018-031) further off by it from being either liberal, or modest, or charitable,

(018-032) or obedient unto God.

(018-033) XXXIX. Free from all compulsion in all cheerfulness and alacrity thou

(018-034) mayst run out thy time, though men should exclaim against thee never so

(018-035) much, and the wild beasts should pull in sunder the poor members of thy

(018-036) pampered mass of flesh. For what in either of these or the like cases

(018-037) should hinder the mind to retain her own rest and tranquillity,

(018-038) consisting both in the right judgment of those things that happen unto

(018-039) her, and in the ready use of all present matters and occasions? So that

(018-040) her judgment may say, to that which is befallen her by way of cross:

(018-041) this thou art in very deed, and according to thy true nature:

(018-042) notwithstanding that in the judgment of opinion thou dust appear

(018-043) otherwise: and her discretion to the present object; thou art that,

(018-044) which I sought for. For whatsoever it be, that is now present, shall

(018-045) ever be embraced by me as a fit and seasonable object, both for my

(018-046) reasonable faculty, and for my sociable, or charitable inclination to

(018-047) work upon. And that which is principal in this matter, is that it may be

(018-048) referred either unto the praise of God, or to the good of men. For

(018-049) either unto God or man, whatsoever it is that doth happen in the world

(018-050) hath in the ordinary course of nature its proper reference; neither is

(018-051) there anything, that in regard of nature is either new, or reluctant and

(018-052) intractable, but all things both usual and easy.

(018-053) XL. Then hath a man attained to the estate of perfection in his life and

(018-054) conversation, when he so spends every day, as if it were his last day:

(018-055) never hot and vehement in his affections, nor yet so cold and stupid as

(018-056) one that had no sense; and free from all manner of dissimulation.

(018-057) XLI. Can the Gods, who are immortal, for the continuance of so many ages

(018-058) bear without indignation with such and so many sinners, as have ever

(018-059) been, yea not only so, but also take such care for them, that they want

(018-060) nothing; and dust thou so grievously take on, as one that could bear

(018-061) with them no longer; thou that art but for a moment of time? yea thou

(018-062) that art one of those sinners thyself? A very ridiculous thing it is,

(018-063) that any man should dispense with [[evil|vice]] and wickedness in himself, which

(018-064) is in his power to restrain; and should go about to suppress it in

(018-065) others, which is altogether impossible.

(018-066) XLII. What object soever, our reasonable and sociable faculty doth meet

(018-067) with, that affords nothing either for the satisfaction of reason, or for

(018-068) the practice of charity, she worthily doth think unworthy of herself.

(018-069) XLIII. When thou hast done well, and another is benefited by thy action,

(018-070) must thou like a very fool look for a third thing besides, as that

(018-071) it may appear unto others also that thou hast done well, or that thou

(018-072) mayest in time, receive one good turn for another? No man useth to be

(018-073) weary of that which is beneficial unto him. But every action according

(018-074) to nature, is beneficial. Be not weary then of doing that which is

(018-075) beneficial unto thee, whilst it is so unto others.

(018-076) XLIV. The nature of the universe did once certainly before it was

(018-077) created, whatsoever it hath done since, deliberate and so resolve upon

(018-078) the creation of the world. Now since that time, whatsoever it is, that

(018-079) is and happens in the world, is either but a consequent of that one and

(018-080) first deliberation: or if so be that this ruling rational part of the

(018-081) world, takes any thought and care of things particular, they are surely

(018-082) his reasonable and principal creatures, that are the proper object of

(018-083) his particular care and providence. This often thought upon, will much

(018-084) conduce to thy tranquillity.

(018-085) THE EIGHTH BOOK

(018-086) I. This also, among other things, may serve to keep thee from vainglory;

(018-087) if thou shalt consider, that thou art now altogether incapable of the

(018-088) commendation of one, who all his life long, or from his youth at least,

(018-089) hath lived a philosopher's life. For both unto others, and to thyself

(018-090) especially, it is well known, that thou hast done many things contrary

(018-091) to that perfection of life. Thou hast therefore been confounded in thy

(018-092) course, and henceforth it will be hard for thee to recover the title and

(018-093) credit of a philosopher. And to it also is thy calling and profession

(018-094) repugnant. If therefore thou dost truly understand, what it is that is

(018-095) of moment indeed; as for thy fame and credit, take no thought or care

(018-096) for that: let it suffice thee if all the rest of thy life, be it more or

(018-097) less, thou shalt live as thy nature requireth, or according to the true

(018-098) and natural end of thy making. Take pains therefore to know what it is

(018-099) that thy nature requireth, and let nothing else distract thee. Thou

(018-100) hast already had sufficient experience, that of those many things that

(018-101) hitherto thou hast erred and wandered about, thou couldst not find

(018-102) happiness in any of them. Not in syllogisms, and logical subtilties, not

(018-103) in wealth, not in honour and [[fame|reputation]], not in pleasure. In none of all

(018-104) these. Wherein then is it to be found? In the practice of those things,

(018-105) which the nature of man, as he is a man, doth require. How then shall

(018-106) he do those things? if his dogmata, or moral tenets and opinions (from

(018-107) which all motions and actions do proceed), be right and true. Which be

(018-108) those dogmata? Those that concern that which is good or evil, as that

(018-109) there is nothing truly good and beneficial unto man, but that which

(018-110) makes him just, temperate, courageous, liberal; and that there is

(018-111) nothing truly evil and hurtful unto man, but that which causeth the

(018-112) contrary effects.

(018-113) II. Upon every action that thou art about, put this question to thyself;

(018-114) How will this when it is done agree with me? Shall I have no occasion

(018-115) to repent of it? Yet a very little while and I am dead and gone; and

(018-116) all things are at end. What then do I care for more than this, that my

(018-117) present action whatsoever it be, may be the proper action of one that

(018-118) is reasonable; whose end is, the common good; who in all things is ruled

(018-119) and governed by the same law of right and reason, by which God Himself

(018-120) is.

(018-121) III. Alexander, Caius, Pompeius; what are these to Diogenes, Heraclitus,

(018-122) and Socrates? These penetrated into the true nature of things; into all

(018-123) causes, and all subjects: and upon these did they exercise their power

(018-124) and authority. But as for those, as the extent of their error was, so

(018-125) far did their slavery extend.

(018-126) IV. What they have done, they will still do, although thou shouldst hang

(018-127) thyself. First; let it not trouble thee. For all things both good and

(018-128) evil: come to pass according to the nature and general condition of the

(018-129) universe, and within a very little while, all things will be at an

(018-130) end; no man will be remembered: as now of Africanus (for example) and

(018-131) Augustus it is already come to pass. Then secondly; fix thy mind upon

(018-132) the thing itself; look into it, and remembering thyself, that thou art

(018-133) bound nevertheless to be a good man, and what it is that thy nature

(018-134) requireth of thee as thou art a man, be not diverted from what thou art

(018-135) about, and speak that which seemeth unto thee most just: only speak it

(018-136) kindly, modestly, and without hypocrisy.

(018-137) V. That which the nature of the universe doth busy herself about, is;

(018-138) that which is here, to transfer it thither, to change it, and thence

(018-139) again to take it away, and to carry it to another place. So that thou

(018-140) needest not fear any new thing. For all things are usual and ordinary;

(018-141) and all things are disposed by equality.

(018-142) VI. Every particular nature hath content, when in its own proper course

(018-143) it speeds. A reasonable nature doth then speed, when first in matter of

(018-144) fancies and imaginations, it gives no consent to that which is either

(018-145) false uncertain. Secondly, when in all its motions and resolutions it

(018-146) takes its level at the common good only, and that it desireth nothing,

(018-147) and flieth from nothing, bet what is in its own power to compass or

(018-148) avoid. And lastly, when it willingly and gladly embraceth, whatsoever is

(018-149) dealt and appointed unto it by the common nature. For it is part of it;

(018-150) even as the nature of any one leaf, is part of the common nature of all

(018-151) plants and trees. But that the nature of a leaf, is part of a nature

(018-152) both unreasonable and unsensible, and which in its proper end may be

(018-153) hindered; or, which is servile and slavish: whereas the nature of man is

(018-154) part of a common nature which cannot be hindered, and which is both

(018-155) reasonable and just. From whence also it is, that according to the

(018-156) worth of everything, she doth make such equal distribution of all

(018-157) things, as of duration, substance form, operation, and of events and

(018-158) accidents. But herein consider not whether thou shalt find this equality

(018-159) in everything absolutely and by itself; but whether in all the

(018-160) particulars of some one thing taken together, and compared with all the

(018-161) particulars of some other thing, and them together likewise.

(018-162) VII. Thou hast no time nor opportunity to read. What then? Hast thou

(018-163) not time and opportunity to exercise thyself, not to wrong thyself; to

(018-164) strive against all carnal pleasures and pains, and to get the upper hand

(018-165) of them; to contemn honour and vainglory; and not only, not to be angry

(018-166) with them, whom towards thee thou doest find unsensible and unthankful;

(018-167) but also to have a care of them still, and of their welfare?
