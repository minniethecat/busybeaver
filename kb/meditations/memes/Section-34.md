# Section 34

(034-001) "The hardihood of Socrates was famous"; see Plato, Siymposium, p. 220.

(034-002) BOOK X XXII. The Greek means, "paltry breath bearing up corpses, so that

(034-003) the tale of Dead Man's Land is clearer."

(034-004) XXII. "The poet" (21): Euripides, frag. 898 (Nauck); compare Aeschylus,

(034-005) Danaides, frag. 44.

(034-006) XXIV. "Plato" (23): Theaetetus, p. 174 D.

(034-007) XXXIV. "The poet" (34): Homer, Iliad, vi. 147.

(034-008) XXXIV. "Wood": A translation of ulh, "matter."

(034-009) XXXVIII. "Rhetoric" (38): Rather "the gift of speech"; or perhaps the

(034-010) "decree" of the reasoning faculty.

(034-011) BOOK XI V. "Cithaeron" (6): Oedipus utters this cry after discovering

(034-012) that he has fulfilled his awful doom, he was exposed on Cithaeron as

(034-013) an infant to die, and the cry implies that he wishes he had died there.

(034-014) Sophocles, Oedipus Tyrannus, 1391.

(034-015) V. "New Comedy...," etc. C. has here strayed from the Greek rather

(034-016) widely. Translate: "and understand to what end the New Comedy was

(034-017) adopted, which by small degrees degenerated into a mere show of skill

(034-018) in mimicry." C. writes Comedia Vetus, Media, Nova. XII. "Phocion" (13):

(034-019) When about to be put to death he charged his son to bear no malice

(034-020) against the Athenians.

(034-021) XXVIII. "My heart," etc. (31): From Homer, Odyssey ix. 413. "They will"

(034-022) From Hesiod, Opera et Dies, 184.

(034-023) "[[epictetus|Epictetus]]" Arr. i. II, 37.

(034-024) XXX. "Cut down grapes" (35): Correct "ears of corn." "Epictetus"(36):

(034-025) Arr. 3, 22, 105.

(034-026) GLOSSARY

(034-027) This Glossary includes all proper names (excepting a few which are

(034-028) insignificant or unknown) and all obsolete or obscure words. ADRIANUS,

(034-029) or [[hadrian|Hadrian]] (76-138 A. D.), 14th Roman Emperor.

(034-030) Agrippa, M. Vipsanius (63-12 B.C.), a distinguished soldier under

(034-031) Augustus.

(034-032) Alexander the Great, King of Macedonia, and Conqueror of the East,

(034-033) 356-323 B.C.

(034-034) Antisthenes of [[athens|Athens]], founder of the sect of Cynic philosophers, and an

(034-035) opponent of Plato, 5th century B.C [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]], 15th Roman Emperor,

(034-036) 138-161 AD. one of the best princes that ever mounted a throne.

(034-037) Apathia: the Stoic ideal was calmness in all circumstance an

(034-038) insensibility to pain, and absence of all exaltation at, pleasure or

(034-039) good fortune.

(034-040) Apelles, a famous painter of antiquity.

(034-041) Apollonius of Alexandria, called Dyscolus, or the 'ill-tempered,'

(034-042) a great grammarian.

(034-043) Aposteme, tumour, excrescence.

(034-044) Archimedes of Syracuse 287-212 B.C., the most famous mathematician of

(034-045) antiquity.

(034-046) Athos, a mountain promontory at the N. of the Aegean Sea.

(034-047) Augustus, first Roman Emperor (ruled 31 B.C.-14 AD.).

(034-048) Avoid, void.

(034-049) BACCHIUS: there Were several persons of this name, and the one meant is

(034-050) perhaps the musician.

(034-051) Brutus (1) the liberator of the Roman people from their kings, and (2)

(034-052) the murderer of Cæsar.

(034-053) Both names were household words.

(034-054) Cæsar, Caius, Julius, the Dictator and Conqueror.

(034-055) Caieta, a town in Latium.

(034-056) Camillus, a famous dictator in the early days of the Roman Republic.

(034-057) Carnuntum, a town on the Danube in Upper Pannonia.

(034-058) Cato, called of Utica, a Stoic who died by his own hand after the battle

(034-059) of Thapsus, 46 B.C. His name was proverbial for virtue and courage.

(034-060) Cautelous, cautious.

(034-061) Cecrops, first legendary King of Athens.

(034-062) Charax, perhaps the priestly historian of that name, whose date is

(034-063) unknown, except that it must be later than Nero.

(034-064) Chirurgeon, surgeon.

(034-065) Chrysippus, 280-207 B.C., a Stoic philosopher, and the founder of

(034-066) Stoicism as a systematic philosophy.

(034-067) Circus, the Circus Maximus at [[rome|Rome]], where games were held.

(034-068) There were four companies who contracted to provide horses, drivers,

(034-069) etc. These were called Factiones, and each had its distinguishing

(034-070) colour: russata (red), albata (white), veneta (blue), prasina (green).

(034-071) There was high rivalry between them, and riots and bloodshed not

(034-072) infrequently.

(034-073) Cithaeron, a mountain range N. of Attica.

(034-074) Comedy, ancient; a term applied to the Attic comedy of Aristophanes and

(034-075) his time, which criticised persons and politics, like a modern comic

(034-076) journal, such as Punck. See New Comedy.

(034-077) Compendious, short.

(034-078) Conceit, opinion.

(034-079) Contentation, contentment.

(034-080) Crates, a Cynic philosopher of the 4th century B.C.

(034-081) Crœsus, King of Lydia, proverbial for wealth; he reigned 560-546 B.C.

(034-082) Cynics, a school of philosophers, founded by Antisthenes. Their texts

(034-083) were a kind of caricature of Socraticism. Nothing was good but virtue,

(034-084) nothing bad but [[evil|vice]]. The Cynics repudiated all civil and social claims,

(034-085) and attempted to return to what they called a state of nature. Many of

(034-086) them were very disgusting in their manners.

(034-087) DEMETRIUS of Phalerum, an Athenian orator, statesman, philosopher, and

(034-088) poet. Born 345 B.C.

(034-089) Democritus of Abdera (460-361 B.C.), celebrated as the 'laughing

(034-090) philosopher,' whose constant thought was 'What fools these mortals be.'

(034-091) He invented the Atomic Theory.

(034-092) Dio of Syracuse, a disciple of Plato, and afterwards tyrant of Syracuse.

(034-093) Murdered 353 B.C.

(034-094) Diogenes, the Cynic, born about 412 B.C., renowned for his rudeness and

(034-095) hardihood.

(034-096) Diognetus, a painter.

(034-097) Dispense with, put up with.

(034-098) Dogmata, pithy sayings, or philosophical rules of life.

(034-099) EMPEDOCLES of Agrigentum, fl.

(034-100) 5th century B.C., a philosopher, who first laid down that there were

(034-101) "four elements." He believed in the transmigration of souls, and the

(034-102) indestructibility of matter.

(034-103) Epictetus, a famous Stoic philosopher. He was of Phrygia, at first a

(034-104) slave, then freedman, lame, poor, and contented.

(034-105) The work called Encheiridion was compiled by a pupil from his

(034-106) discourses.

(034-107) Epicureans, a sect of philosophers founded by Epicurus, who "combined

(034-108) the physics of Democritus," i.e. the atomic theory, "with the ethics of

(034-109) Aristippus."

(034-110) They proposed to live for happiness, but the word did not bear that

(034-111) coarse and vulgar sense originally which it soon took.

(034-112) Epicurus of Samos, 342-270 B.C.

(034-113) Lived at Athens in his "gardens," an urbane and kindly, if somewhat

(034-114) useless, life. His character was simple and temperate, and had none of

(034-115) the vice or indulgence which was afterwards associated with the name of

(034-116) Epicurean.

(034-117) Eudoxus of Cnidus, a famous astronomer and physician of the 4th century

(034-118) B. C.

(034-119) FATAL, fated.

(034-120) Fortuit, chance (adj.).

(034-121) [[fronto|Fronto]], M. Cornelius, a rhetorician and pleader, made consul in 143 A.D.

(034-122) A number of his letters to M, Aur. and others are extant.

(034-123) GRANUA, a tributary of the Danube.

(034-124) HELICE, ancient capital city of Achaia, swallowed up by an earthquake,

(034-125) 373 B.C.

(034-126) Helvidius Priscus, son-in-law of Thrasea Paetus, a noble man and a lover

(034-127) of liberty. He was banished by Nero, and put to death by Vespasian.

(034-128) Heraclitus of Ephesus, who lived in the 6th century B.C. He wrote on

(034-129) philosophy and natural [[knowledge|science]].

(034-130) Herculaneum, near Mount Vesuvius, buried by the eruption of 79 AD.

(034-131) Hercules, p. 167, should be Apollo. See Muses.

(034-132) Hiatus, gap.

(034-133) Hipparchus of Bithynia, an astronomer of the 2nd century B.C., "The true

(034-134) father of astronomy."

(034-135) Hippocrates of Cos, about 460-357 B.C. One of the most famous physicians

(034-136) of antiquity.

(034-137) IDIOT, means merely the non-proficient in anything, the "layman," he who

(034-138) was not technically trained in any art, craft, or calling.

(034-139) LEONNATUS, a distinguished general under Alexander the Great.

(034-140) Lucilla, daughter of M. Aurelius, and wife of Verus, whom she survived.

(034-141) MÆCENAS, a trusted adviser of Augustus, and a munificent patron of wits

(034-142) and literary men.

(034-143) Maximus, Claudius, a Stoic philosopher.

(034-144) Menippus, a Cynic philosopher.

(034-145) Meteores, ta metewrologika, "high philosophy," used specially of

(034-146) astronomy and natural philosophy, which were bound up with other

(034-147) speculations.

(034-148) Middle Comedy, something midway between the Old and New Comedy. See

(034-149) Comedy, Ancient, and New Comedy.

(034-150) Middle things, Book 7, XXV. The Stoics divided all things into virtue,

(034-151) vice, and indifferent things; but as "indifferent" they regarded most of

(034-152) those things which the world regards as good or bad, such as wealth or

(034-153) poverty. Of these, some were "to be desired," some "to be rejected."

(034-154) Muses, the nine deities who presided over various kinds of poesy, music,

(034-155) etc. Their leader was Apollo, one of whose titles is Musegetes, the

(034-156) Leader of the Muses.

(034-157) NERVES, strings.

(034-158) New Comedy, the Attic Comedy of Menander and his school, which

(034-159) criticised not persons but manners, like a modern comic opera. See

(034-160) Comedy, Ancient.

(034-161) PALESTRA, wrestling school.

(034-162) Pancratiast, competitor in the pancratium, a combined contest which

(034-163) comprised boxing and wrestling.

(034-164) Parmularii, gladiators armed with a small round shield (parma).

(034-165) Pheidias, the most famous sculptor of antiquity.

(034-166) Philippus, founder of the Macedonian supremacy, and father of Alexander

(034-167) the Great.

(034-168) Phocion, an Athenian general and statesman, a noble and high-minded man,

(034-169) 4th century B.C.

(034-170) He was called by Demosthenes, "the pruner of my periods."

(034-171) He was put to death by the State in 317, on a false suspicion, and left

(034-172) a message for his son "to bear no grudge against the Athenians."

(034-173) Pine, torment.

(034-174) Plato of Athens, 429-347 B.C. He used the dialectic method invented by

(034-175) his master Socrates.

(034-176) He was, perhaps, as much poet as philosopher. He is generally identified

(034-177) with the Theory of Ideas, that things are what they are by participation

(034-178) with our eternal Idea. His "Commonwealth" was a kind of Utopia.

(034-179) Platonics, followers of Plato.

(034-180) Pompeii, near Mount Vesuvius, buried in the eruption of 79 A. D.

(034-181) Pompeius, C. Pompeius Magnus, a very successful general at the end of

(034-182) the Roman Republic (106-48 B.C.).

(034-183) Prestidigitator, juggler.

(034-184) Pythagoras of Samos, a philosopher, scientist, and moralist of the 6th

(034-185) century B.C.

(034-186) QUADI, a tribe of S. Germany.

(034-187) M. Aurelius carried on war against them, and part of this book was

(034-188) written in the field.

(034-189) RICTUS, gape, jaws.

(034-190) [[rusticus|Rusticus]], Q. Junius, or Stoic philosopher, twice made consul by M.

(034-191) Aurelius.

(034-192) SACRARY, shrine.

(034-193) Salaminius, Book 7, XXXVII. Leon of Sala-mis. Socrates was ordered by

(034-194) the Thirty Tyrants to fetch him before them, and Socrates, at his own

(034-195) peril, refused.

(034-196) Sarmatae, a tribe dwelling in Poland.

(034-197) Sceletum, skeleton.

(034-198) Sceptics, a school of philosophy founded by Pyrrho (4th century B.C.).

(034-199) He advocated "suspension of judgment," and taught the relativity of

(034-200) knowledge and impossibility of proof. The school is not unlike the

(034-201) Agnostic school.

(034-202) Scipio, the name of two great soldiers, P. Corn. Scipio Africanus,

(034-203) conqueror of Hannibal, and P.

(034-204) Corn. Sc. Afr. Minor, who came into the family by adoption, who

(034-205) destroyed Carthage.

(034-206) Secutoriani (a word coined by C.), the Sececutores, light-armed

(034-207) gladiators, who were pitted against others with net and trident.

(034-208) Sextus of Chaeronea, a Stoic philosopher, nephew of Plutarch.

(034-209) Silly, simple, common.

(034-210) Sinuessa, a town in Latium.

(034-211) Socrates, an Athenian philosopher (469-399 B.C.), founder of the

(034-212) dialectic method. Put to death on a trumped-up charge by his countrymen.

(034-213) Stint, limit (without implying niggardliness).

(034-214) Stoics, a philosophic system founded by Zeno (4th century B.C.), and

(034-215) systematised by Chrysippus (3rd century B.C.). Their physical theory

(034-216) was a pantheistic materialism, their summum bonum "to live according

(034-217) to nature." Their wise man needs nothing, he is sufficient to himself;

(034-218) virtue is good, vice bad, external things indifferent.

(034-219) THEOPHRASTUS, a philosopher, pupil of Aristotle, and his successor as

(034-220) president of the Lyceum. He wrote a large number of works on philosophy

(034-221) and natural history. Died 287 B.C.

(034-222) Thrasea, P. Thrasea Pactus, a senator and Stoic philosopher, a noble and

(034-223) courageous man. He was condemned to death by Nero.

(034-224) Tiberius, 2nd Roman Emperor (14-31 AD.). He spent the latter part of his

(034-225) life at Capreae (Capri), off Naples, in luxury or debauchery, neglecting

(034-226) his imperial duties.

(034-227) To-torn, torn to pieces.

(034-228) Trajan, 13th Roman Emperor, 52-117 A.D.

(034-229) VERUS, Lucius Aurelius, colleague of M. Aurelius in the Empire.

(034-230) He married Lucilla, daughter of M. A., and died 169 A.D.

(034-231) Vespasian, 9th Roman Emperor XENOCRATES of Chalcedon, 396-314 B.C., a

(034-232) philosopher, and president of the Academy.
