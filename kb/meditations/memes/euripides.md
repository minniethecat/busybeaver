## Overview

Euripides, the youngest of the three great Athenian tragedians, is the most quoted Greek dramatist in Marcus Aurelius's Meditations. Alongside Homer and Plato, Euripides was one of the classical authors Marcus kept close at hand throughout his philosophical life [031-118].

## Quotations in the Meditations

Throughout Book 11 and the later sections of the Meditations, Marcus draws repeatedly on Euripidean fragments. He cites the *Bellerophon* — "It will be as it will be" — as a lesson in acceptance [033-171]. From the *Hypsipyle* he quotes on the nature of mortal life [033-172]. The *Chrysippus* provides a reflection on the constituent parts of man [033-177], and the *Supplices* offers Stoic-adjacent counsel on diet and simplicity [033-179]. A fragment preserved in Marcus — "The poet says" — counsels equanimity [034-004].

## Philosophical Significance

Euripides occupies an unusual position in Stoic reception: his plays, especially the *Heracles* and *Trojan Women*, dramatize themes of suffering, fate, and human limitation that Stoic philosophers found congenial. For Marcus, Euripides was not merely a literary ornament but a genuine source of philosophical insight — the poet whose lines could reinforce the rational precepts Marcus rehearsed daily.
