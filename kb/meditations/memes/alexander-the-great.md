## Overview

Alexander the Great (356–323 BCE), king of Macedon and conqueror of the known world, appears in the Meditations not as a hero to be admired but as a cautionary example. Marcus Aurelius repeatedly invokes Alexander to illustrate the vanity of military glory, political power, and posthumous fame — all of which Alexander possessed in abundance, and none of which prevented his early death or the dissolution of his empire. (006-114) (014-017)

## Life and Conquests

Alexander inherited the Macedonian throne at twenty and, within twelve years, conquered the Persian Empire from the Mediterranean to the Indus River. He was tutored by Aristotle, carried Greek culture across Asia, and died in Babylon at thirty-two. His empire fragmented immediately after his death, divided among his generals. For the Stoics, Alexander's life was the perfect illustration of the doctrine of indifferents: he possessed every external advantage — power, wealth, fame, genius — yet none of it made him virtuous, and all of it vanished at his death.

## Marcus's Critique

Marcus uses Alexander as a rhetorical device to puncture the illusions of ambition. He pairs "Alexander of Macedon, and he that dressed his mules" — the conqueror and the mule-driver — observing that death equalizes them both. (014-017) He notes that "Alexander and Caesar and Pompeius" — the great conquerors of history — are all dead, and "what are these to Diogenes, Heraclitus, and Socrates?" — the philosophers who pursued wisdom rather than empire. (018-121) Alexander "and his father Philip" and the Macedonian dynasty are swept away like all things: none of their power prevented the dissolution of their achievements. (022-128) The conqueror who was "surprised by the fates" serves as Marcus's reminder that no amount of power can exempt one from mortality. (006-114)

## Stoic Significance

Alexander illustrates the Stoic teaching that externals — power, fame, conquest — are indifferents that cannot contribute to happiness. Marcus's repeated invocation of Alexander is not historical curiosity but moral instruction: if the most powerful man in history could not escape death, illness, or the vanity of his achievements, then neither can anyone else. The proper response is not to seek Alexander's power but to cultivate the wisdom of Socrates and the equanimity of the Stoic sage — inner goods that death cannot touch.
