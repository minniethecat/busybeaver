# Section 3

(003-001) If his was that honest and true heart which is the Christian ideal, this

(003-002) is the more wonderful in that he lacked the faith which makes Christians

(003-003) strong. He could say, it is true, 'either there is a God, and then all

(003-004) is well; or if all things go by chance and fortune, yet mayest thou use

(003-005) thine own providence in those things that concern thee properly; and

(003-006) then art thou well.' Or again, 'We must needs grant that there is a

(003-007) nature that doth govern the universe.' But his own part in the scheme

(003-008) of things is so small, that he does not hope for any personal happiness

(003-009) beyond what a serene soul may win in this mortal life. 'O my soul, the

(003-010) time I trust will be, when thou shalt be good, simple, more open and

(003-011) visible, than that body by which it is enclosed;' but this is said of

(003-012) the calm contentment with human lot which he hopes to attain, not of a

(003-013) time when the trammels of the body shall be cast off. For the rest, the

(003-014) world and its fame and wealth, 'all is vanity.' The gods may perhaps

(003-015) have a particular care for him, but their especial care is for the

(003-016) universe at large: thus much should suffice. His gods are better than

(003-017) the Stoic gods, who sit aloof from all human things, untroubled and

(003-018) uncaring, but his personal hope is hardly stronger. On this point he

(003-019) says little, though there are many allusions to death as the natural

(003-020) end; doubtless he expected his soul one day to be absorbed into the

(003-021) universal soul, since nothing comes out of nothing, and nothing can be

(003-022) annihilated. His mood is one of strenuous weariness; he does his duty as

(003-023) a good soldier, waiting for the sound of the trumpet which shall sound

(003-024) the retreat; he has not that cheerful confidence which led Socrates

(003-025) through a life no less noble, to a death which was to bring him into the

(003-026) company of gods he had worshipped and men whom he had revered.

(003-027) But although [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]] may have held intellectually that his soul

(003-028) was destined to be absorbed, and to lose consciousness of itself, there

(003-029) were times when he felt, as all who hold it must sometimes feel, how

(003-030) unsatisfying is such a creed. Then he gropes blindly after something

(003-031) less empty and vain. 'Thou hast taken ship,' he says, 'thou hast sailed,

(003-032) thou art come to land, go out, if to another life, there also shalt

(003-033) thou find gods, who are everywhere.' There is more in this than the

(003-034) assumption of a rival theory for argument's sake. If worldly things

(003-035) 'be but as a dream, the thought is not far off that there may be an

(003-036) awakening to what is real. When he speaks of death as a necessary

(003-037) change, and points out that nothing useful and profitable can be brought

(003-038) about without change, did he perhaps think of the change in a corn of

(003-039) wheat, which is not quickened except it die? [[nature|Nature]]'s marvellous power

(003-040) of recreating out of Corruption is surely not confined to bodily things.

(003-041) Many of his thoughts sound like far-off echoes of St. Paul; and it is

(003-042) strange indeed that this most Christian of emperors has nothing good

(003-043) to say of the Christians. To him they are only sectaries 'violently and

(003-044) passionately set upon opposition.

(003-045) Profound as philosophy these _Meditations_ certainly are not; but Marcus

(003-046) Aurelius was too sincere not to see the essence of such things as

(003-047) came within his experience. Ancient religions were for the most

(003-048) part concerned with outward things. Do the necessary rites, and you

(003-049) propitiate the gods; and these rites were often trivial, sometimes

(003-050) violated right feeling or even morality. Even when the gods stood on the

(003-051) side of [[justice|righteousness]], they were concerned with the act more than with

(003-052) the intent. But Marcus Aurelius knows that what the heart is full of,

(003-053) the man will do. 'Such as thy thoughts and ordinary cogitations are,' he

(003-054) says, 'such will thy mind be in time.' And every page of the book shows

(003-055) us that he knew thought was sure to issue in act. He drills his soul, as

(003-056) it were, in right principles, that when the time comes, it may be guided

(003-057) by them. To wait until the emergency is to be too late.

(003-058) He sees also the true essence of happiness. 'If happiness did consist

(003-059) in pleasure, how came notorious robbers, impure abominable livers,

(003-060) parricides, and tyrants, in so large a measure to have their part of

(003-061) pleasures?' He who had all the world's pleasures at command can write

(003-062) thus 'A happy lot and portion is, good inclinations of the soul, good

(003-063) desires, good actions.'

(003-064) By the irony of [[providence|fate]] this man, so gentle and good, so desirous of quiet

(003-065) joys and a mind free from care, was set at the head of the Roman Empire

(003-066) when great dangers threatened from east and west. For several years he

(003-067) himself commanded his armies in chief. In camp before the Quadi he dates

(003-068) the first book of his _Meditations_, and shows how he could retire within

(003-069) himself amid the coarse clangour of arms. The pomps and glories which

(003-070) he despised were all his; what to most men is an ambition or a dream, to

(003-071) him was a round of weary tasks which nothing but the stern sense of duty

(003-072) could carry him through. And he did his work well. His wars were slow

(003-073) and tedious, but successful. With a statesman's wisdom he foresaw the

(003-074) danger to [[rome|Rome]] of the barbarian hordes from the north, and took measures

(003-075) to meet it. As it was, his settlement gave two centuries of respite

(003-076) to the Roman Empire; had he fulfilled the plan of pushing the imperial

(003-077) frontiers to the Elbe, which seems to have been in his mind, much more

(003-078) might have been accomplished. But death cut short his designs.

(003-079) Truly a rare opportunity was given to Marcus Aurelius of showing what

(003-080) the mind can do in despite of circumstances. Most peaceful of warriors,

(003-081) a magnificent monarch whose ideal was quiet happiness in home life, bent

(003-082) to obscurity yet born to greatness, the loving father of children who

(003-083) died young or turned out hateful, his life was one paradox. That nothing

(003-084) might lack, it was in camp before the face of the enemy that he passed

(003-085) away and went to his own place.

(003-090) Renan’s “Marc-Aurèle”—in his “History of the Origins of Christianity,”

(003-091) which appeared in 1882—is the most vital and original book to be had

(003-092) relating to the time of Marcus Aurelius. Pater’s “Marius the Epicurean”

(003-093) forms another outside commentary, which is of service in the

(003-094) imaginative attempt to create again the period.

(003-095) MARCUS AURELIUS ANTONINUS THE ROMAN EMPEROR

(003-096) HIS FIRST BOOK

(003-097) concerning HIMSELF:

(003-098) Wherein [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus]] recordeth, What and of whom, whether Parents, Friends,

(003-099) or Masters; by their good examples, or good advice and counsel, he had

(003-100) learned:

(003-101) Divided into Numbers or Sections.

(003-102) ANTONINUS Book vi. Num. xlviii. Whensoever thou wilt rejoice thyself,

(003-103) think and meditate upon those good parts and especial gifts, which thou

(003-104) hast observed in any of them that live with thee:

(003-105) as industry in one, in another modesty, in another bountifulness, in

(003-106) another some other thing. For nothing can so much rejoice thee, as

(003-107) the resemblances and parallels of several virtues, eminent in the

(003-108) dispositions of them that live with thee, especially when all at once,

(003-109) as it were, they represent themselves unto thee. See therefore, that

(003-110) thou have them always in a readiness.

(003-111) THE FIRST BOOK

(003-112) I. Of my grandfather Verus I have learned to be gentle and meek, and to

(003-113) refrain from all anger and passion. From the fame and memory of him that

(003-114) begot me I have learned both shamefastness and manlike behaviour. Of my

(003-115) mother I have learned to be religious, and bountiful; and to forbear,

(003-116) not only to do, but to intend any evil; to content myself with a spare

(003-117) diet, and to fly all such excess as is incidental to great wealth. Of my

(003-118) great-grandfather, both to frequent public schools and auditories, and

(003-119) to get me good and able teachers at home; and that I ought not to think

(003-120) much, if upon such occasions, I were at excessive charges.

(003-121) II. Of him that brought me up, not to be fondly addicted to either of

(003-122) the two great factions of the coursers in the circus, called Prasini,

(003-123) and Veneti: nor in the amphitheatre partially to favour any of the

(003-124) gladiators, or fencers, as either the Parmularii, or the Secutores.

(003-125) Moreover, to endure labour; nor to need many things; when I have

(003-126) anything to do, to do it myself rather than by others; not to meddle

(003-127) with many businesses; and not easily to admit of any slander.

(003-128) III. Of Diognetus, not to busy myself about vain things, and not easily

(003-129) to believe those things, which are commonly spoken, by such as take upon

(003-130) them to work wonders, and by sorcerers, or prestidigitators, and

(003-131) impostors; concerning the power of charms, and their driving out of

(003-132) demons, or evil spirits; and the like. Not to keep quails for the game;

(003-133) nor to be mad after such things. Not to be offended with other men's

(003-134) liberty of speech, and to apply myself unto philosophy. Him also I must

(003-135) thank, that ever I heard first Bacchius, then Tandasis and Marcianus,

(003-136) and that I did write dialogues in my youth; and that I took liking to

(003-137) the philosophers' little couch and skins, and such other things, which

(003-138) by the Grecian discipline are proper to those who profess philosophy.

(003-139) IV. To [[rusticus|Rusticus]] I am beholding, that I first entered into the conceit

(003-140) that my life wanted some redress and cure. And then, that I did not

(003-141) fall into the ambition of ordinary sophists, either to write tracts

(003-142) concerning the common theorems, or to exhort men unto virtue and the

(003-143) study of philosophy by public orations; as also that I never by way of

(003-144) ostentation did affect to show myself an active able man, for any kind

(003-145) of bodily exercises. And that I gave over the study of rhetoric and

(003-146) poetry, and of elegant neat language. That I did not use to walk about

(003-147) the house in my long robe, nor to do any such things. Moreover I learned

(003-148) of him to write letters without any affectation, or curiosity; such as

(003-149) that was, which by him was written to my mother from Sinuessa: and to be

(003-150) easy and ready to be reconciled, and well pleased again with them that

(003-151) had offended me, as soon as any of them would be content to seek unto

(003-152) me again. To read with diligence; not to rest satisfied with a light and

(003-153) superficial knowledge, nor quickly to assent to things commonly spoken

(003-154) of: whom also I must thank that ever I lighted upon [[epictetus|Epictetus]] his

(003-155) _Hypomnemata_, or moral commentaries and common-factions: which also he

(003-156) gave me of his own.

(003-157) V. From Apollonius, true liberty, and unvariable steadfastness, and not

(003-158) to regard anything at all, though never so little, but right and reason:

(003-159) and always, whether in the sharpest pains, or after the loss of a child,

(003-160) or in long diseases, to be still the same man; who also was a present

(003-161) and visible example unto me, that it was possible for the same man to

(003-162) be both vehement and remiss: a man not subject to be vexed, and offended

(003-163) with the incapacity of his scholars and auditors in his lectures and

(003-164) expositions; and a true pattern of a man who of all his good gifts

(003-165) and faculties, least esteemed in himself, that his excellent skill and

(003-166) ability to teach and persuade others the common theorems and maxims of

(003-167) the Stoic philosophy. Of him also I learned how to receive favours and

(003-168) kindnesses (as commonly they are accounted:) from friends, so that I

(003-169) might not become obnoxious unto them, for them, nor more yielding upon

(003-170) occasion, than in right I ought; and yet so that I should not pass them

(003-171) neither, as an unsensible and unthankful man.
