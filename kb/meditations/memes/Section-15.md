# Section 15

(015-001) XXXIX. If so be that the Gods have deliberated in particular of those

(015-002) things that should happen unto me, I must stand to their deliberation,

(015-003) as discrete and wise. For that a God should be an imprudent God, is a

(015-004) thing hard even to conceive: and why should they resolve to do me hurt?

(015-005) for what profit either unto them or the universe (which they specially

(015-006) take care for) could arise from it? But if so be that they have not

(015-007) deliberated of me in particular, certainly they have of the whole in

(015-008) general, and those things which in consequence and coherence of this

(015-009) general deliberation happen unto me in particular, I am bound to embrace

(015-010) and accept of. But if so be that they have not deliberated at all (which

(015-011) indeed is very irreligious for any man to believe: for then let us

(015-012) neither sacrifice, nor pray, nor respect our oaths, neither let us any

(015-013) more use any of those things, which we persuaded of the presence and

(015-014) secret conversation of the Gods among us, daily use and practise:)

(015-015) but, I say, if so be that they have not indeed either in general, or

(015-016) particular deliberated of any of those things, that happen unto us

(015-017) in this world; yet God be thanked, that of those things that

(015-018) concern myself, it is lawful for me to deliberate myself, and all my

(015-019) deliberation is but concerning that which may be to me most profitable.

(015-020) Now that unto every one is most profitable, which is according to his

(015-021) own constitution and nature. And my nature is, to be rational in all my

(015-022) actions and as a good, and natural member of a city and commonwealth,

(015-023) towards my fellow members ever to be sociably and kindly disposed and

(015-024) affected. My city and country as I am [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus]], is [[rome|Rome]]; as a man, the

(015-025) whole world. Those things therefore that are expedient and profitable to

(015-026) those cities, are the only things that are good and expedient for me.

(015-027) XL. Whatsoever in any kind doth happen to any one, is expedient to the

(015-028) whole. And thus much to content us might suffice, that it is expedient

(015-029) for the whole in general. But yet this also shalt thou generally

(015-030) perceive, if thou dost diligently take heed, that whatsoever doth happen

(015-031) to any one man or men.... And now I am content that the word expedient,

(015-032) should more generally be understood of those things which we otherwise

(015-033) call middle things, or things indifferent; as health, wealth, and the

(015-034) like.

(015-035) XLI. As the ordinary shows of the theatre and of other such places,

(015-036) when thou art presented with them, affect thee; as the same things still

(015-037) seen, and in the same fashion, make the sight ingrateful and tedious;

(015-038) so must all the things that we see all our life long affect us. For all

(015-039) things, above and below, are still the same, and from the same causes.

(015-040) When then will there be an end?

(015-041) XLII. Let the several deaths of men of all sorts, and of all sorts of

(015-042) professions, and of all sort of nations, be a perpetual object of thy

(015-043) thoughts,... so that thou mayst even come down to Philistio, Phœbus,

(015-044) and Origanion. Pass now to other generations. Thither shall we after

(015-045) many changes, where so many brave orators are; where so many grave

(015-046) philosophers; Heraclitus, Pythagoras, Socrates. Where so many heroes of

(015-047) the old times; and then so many brave captains of the latter times; and

(015-048) so many kings. After all these, where Eudoxus, Hipparchus, Archimedes;

(015-049) where so many other sharp, generous, industrious, subtile, peremptory

(015-050) dispositions; and among others, even they, that have been the greatest

(015-051) scoffers and deriders of the frailty and brevity of this our human life;

(015-052) as Menippus, and others, as many as there have been such as he. Of all

(015-053) these consider, that they long since are all dead, and gone. And what do

(015-054) they suffer by it! Nay they that have not so much as a name remaining,

(015-055) what are they the worse for it? One thing there is, and that only, which

(015-056) is worth our while in this world, and ought by us much to be esteemed;

(015-057) and that is, according to truth and [[justice|righteousness]], meekly and lovingly

(015-058) to converse with false, and unrighteous men.

(015-059) XLIII. When thou wilt comfort and cheer thyself, call to mind the

(015-060) several gifts and virtues of them, whom thou dost daily converse with;

(015-061) as for example, the industry of the one; the modesty of another; the

(015-062) liberality of a third; of another some other thing. For nothing can so

(015-063) much rejoice thee, as the resemblances and parallels of several virtues,

(015-064) visible and eminent in the dispositions of those who live with thee;

(015-065) especially when, all at once, as near as may be, they represent

(015-066) themselves unto thee. And therefore thou must have them always in a

(015-067) readiness.

(015-068) XLIV. Dost thou grieve that thou dost weigh but so many pounds, and not

(015-069) three hundred rather? Just as much reason hast thou to grieve that

(015-070) thou must live but so many years, and not longer. For as for bulk and

(015-071) substance thou dost content thyself with that proportion of it that is

(015-072) allotted unto thee, so shouldst thou for time.

(015-073) XLV. Let us do our best endeavours to persuade them; but however, if

(015-074) reason and justice lead thee to it, do it, though they be never so much

(015-075) against it. But if any shall by force withstand thee, and hinder thee in

(015-076) it, convert thy virtuous inclination from one object unto another, from

(015-077) justice to contented equanimity, and cheerful patience: so that what in

(015-078) the one is thy hindrance, thou mayst make use of it for the exercise of

(015-079) another virtue: and remember that it was with due exception, and

(015-080) reservation, that thou didst at first incline and desire. For thou didst

(015-081) not set thy mind upon things impossible. Upon what then? that all thy

(015-082) desires might ever be moderated with this due kind of reservation. And

(015-083) this thou hast, and mayst always obtain, whether the thing desired be in

(015-084) thy power or no. And what do I care for more, if that for which I was

(015-085) born and brought forth into the world (to rule all my desires with

(015-086) reason and discretion) may be?

(015-087) XLVI. The ambitious supposeth another man's act, praise and applause, to

(015-088) be his own happiness; the voluptuous his own sense and feeling; but he

(015-089) that is wise, his own action.

(015-090) XLVII. It is in thy power absolutely to exclude all manner of conceit

(015-091) and opinion, as concerning this matter; and by the same means, to

(015-092) exclude all grief and sorrow from thy soul. For as for the things and

(015-093) objects themselves, they of themselves have no such power, whereby to

(015-094) beget and force upon us any opinion at all.

(015-095) XLVIII. Use thyself when any man speaks unto thee, so to hearken unto

(015-096) him, as that in the interim thou give not way to any other thoughts;

(015-097) that so thou mayst (as far as is possible) seem fixed and fastened to

(015-098) his very soul, whosoever he be that speaks unto thee.

(015-099) XLIX. That which is not good for the bee-hive, cannot be good for the

(015-100) bee.

(015-101) L. Will either passengers, or patients, find fault and complain, either

(015-102) the one if they be well carried, or the others if well cured? Do they

(015-103) take care for any more than this; the one, that their shipmaster may

(015-104) bring them safe to land, and the other, that their physician may effect

(015-105) their recovery?

(015-106) LI. How many of them who came into the world at the same time when I

(015-107) did, are already gone out of it?

(015-108) LII. To them that are sick of the jaundice, honey seems bitter; and to

(015-109) them that are bitten by a mad dog, the water terrible; and to children,

(015-110) a little ball seems a fine thing. And why then should I be angry? or

(015-111) do I think that error and false opinion is less powerful to make men

(015-112) transgress, than either choler, being immoderate and excessive, to cause

(015-113) the jaundice; or poison, to cause rage?

(015-114) LIII. No man can hinder thee to live as thy nature doth require. Nothing

(015-115) can happen unto thee, but what the common good of nature doth require.

(015-116) LIV. What manner of men they be whom they seek to please, and what to

(015-117) get, and by what actions: how soon time will cover and bury all things,

(015-118) and how many it hath already buried!

(015-119) THE SEVENTH BOOK

(015-120) I. What is wickedness? It is that which many time and often thou hast

(015-121) already seen and known in the world. And so oft as anything doth happen

(015-122) that might otherwise trouble thee, let this memento presently come to

(015-123) thy mind, that it is that which thou hast already often Seen and known.

(015-124) Generally, above and below, thou shalt find but the same things. The

(015-125) very same things whereof ancient stories, middle age stories, and fresh

(015-126) stories are full whereof towns are full, and houses full. There is

(015-127) nothing that is new. All things that are, are both usual and of little

(015-128) continuance.

(015-129) II. What fear is there that thy dogmata, or philosophical resolutions

(015-130) and conclusions, should become dead in thee, and lose their proper

(015-131) power and efficacy to make thee live happy, as long as those proper

(015-132) and correlative fancies, and representations of things on which they

(015-133) mutually depend (which continually to stir up and revive is in thy

(015-134) power,) are still kept fresh and alive? It is in my power concerning

(015-135) this thing that is happened, what soever it be, to conceit that which is

(015-136) right and true. If it be, why then am I troubled? Those things that are

(015-137) without my [[knowledge|understanding]], are nothing to it at all: and that is it only,

(015-138) which doth properly concern me. Be always in this mind, and thou wilt be

(015-139) right.

(015-140) III. That which most men would think themselves most happy for, and

(015-141) would prefer before all things, if the Gods would grant it unto them

(015-142) after their deaths, thou mayst whilst thou livest grant unto thyself; to

(015-143) live again. See the things of the world again, as thou hast already seen

(015-144) them. For what is it else to live again? Public shows and solemnities

(015-145) with much pomp and vanity, stage plays, flocks and herds; conflicts

(015-146) and contentions: a bone thrown to a company of hungry curs; a bait for

(015-147) greedy fishes; the painfulness, and continual burden-bearing of wretched

(015-148) ants, the running to and fro of terrified mice: little puppets drawn up

(015-149) and down with wires and nerves: these be the objects of the world among

(015-150) all these thou must stand steadfast, meekly affected, and free from all

(015-151) manner of indignation; with this right ratiocination and apprehension;

(015-152) that as the worth is of those things which a man doth affect, so is in

(015-153) very deed every man's worth more or less.

(015-154) IV. Word after word, every one by itself, must the things that are

(015-155) spoken be conceived and understood; and so the things that are done,

(015-156) purpose after purpose, every one by itself likewise. And as in matter of

(015-157) purposes and actions, we must presently see what is the proper use and

(015-158) relation of every one; so of words must we be as ready, to consider of

(015-159) every one what is the true meaning, and signification of it according to

(015-160) truth and nature, however it be taken in common use.

(015-161) V. Is my reason, and understanding sufficient for this, or no? If it be

(015-162) sufficient, without any private applause, or public ostentation as of an

(015-163) instrument, which by nature I am provided of, I will make use of it for

(015-164) the work in hand, as of an instrument, which by nature I am provided of.

(015-165) if it be not, and that otherwise it belong not unto me particularly as

(015-166) a private duty, I will either give it over, and leave it to some other

(015-167) that can better effect it: or I will endeavour it; but with the help

(015-168) of some other, who with the joint help of my reason, is able to bring

(015-169) somewhat to pass, that will now be seasonable and useful for the common

(015-170) good. For whatsoever I do either by myself, or with some other, the

(015-171) only thing that I must intend, is, that it be good and expedient for

(015-172) the public. For as for praise, consider how many who once were much

(015-173) commended, are now already quite forgotten, yea they that commended

(015-174) them, how even they themselves are long since dead and gone. Be not

(015-175) therefore ashamed, whensoever thou must use the help of others. For

(015-176) whatsoever it be that lieth upon thee to effect, thou must propose it

(015-177) unto thyself, as the scaling of walls is unto a soldier. And what if

(015-178) thou through either lameness or some other impediment art not able to

(015-179) reach unto the top of the battlements alone, which with the help of

(015-180) another thou mayst; wilt thou therefore give it over, or go about it

(015-181) with less courage and alacrity, because thou canst not effect it all

(015-182) alone?
