## Overview

Marcus Cornelius Fronto (c. 100–170 CE) was a Roman rhetorician, orator, and suffect consul, best known as the rhetoric teacher and lifelong friend of Marcus Aurelius. Their correspondence — lost for centuries and rediscovered in a palimpsest by Cardinal Mai in 1815 — reveals a relationship of extraordinary warmth and candor. (030-074) (030-076)

## Life and Career

Fronto was born in Cirta, Numidia (modern Algeria), and rose to become the most celebrated orator of his age. He held the suffect consulship in 143 CE and enjoyed unparalleled literary prestige among his contemporaries. His ambitious works — speeches, historical treatises — are lost; all that survives was recovered from a Milan palimpsest in 1815 and supplemented by later Vatican fragments. The extant corpus includes letters exchanged with Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius, Lucius Verus, and friends, together with rhetorical exercises and historical fragments. ## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

The correspondence begins when Marcus was about eighteen, Fronto being some thirty years older. By this stage Marcus's formal education was finished and Fronto acted more as adviser than tutor — urging simplicity in public speech, recommending the study of Cicero, and sending rhetorical themes for the prince to analyze from opposing sides. (031-075) (031-110)

The letters pulse with genuine affection. Marcus writes to his teacher in language unguarded and intimate:

> "nor does it allow me to make haste to my Fronto, my life and delight, to be near him at such a moment of ill-health in particular, to hold his hands, to chafe gently that identical foot, so far as may be done without discomfort, to attend him in the bath, to support his steps with my arm." (031-034)

The devotion was reciprocal. Fronto possessed what the Meditations' editor calls "a tongue which could speak the truth" — a rare gift in the flattery-soaked Roman court — and Marcus treasured it:

> "How happy I am that my friend Marcus Cornelius, so distinguished as an orator and so noble as a man, thinks me worth praising and blaming." (030-092)

When Marcus, still a young Caesar, offered candid advice about a legal speech Fronto was drafting, the older man received the criticism without resentment. Fronto "shows no loss of temper at the interference, nor shrinks from stating his case with frankness; and Marcus, with forbearance rare in a prince, asks him to withdraw some words." Marcus then wrote:

> "This one thing, my dearest Fronto, is enough to make me truly grateful to you, that so far from rejecting my counsel, you have even approved it." (030-158) (030-164)

In Book I of the Meditations, Marcus acknowledges a deeper lesson. Fronto taught him to recognize the moral corruption that attends absolute rule:

> "Of Fronto, to how much envy and fraud and hypocrisy the state of a tyrannous king is subject unto." (004-023)

Fronto was not solely a lofty stylist. He descended from the heights of eloquence to offer the prince practical counsel on managing his entourage:

> "do not let your friends envy." (032-016) (032-012)

He also dissuaded Marcus from the study of Greek — an irony given that the Meditations themselves were composed in Greek. (031-119)

## Legacy and Influence

The recovery of Fronto's letters transformed modern understanding of second-century literary culture and the formative years of the philosopher-emperor. The introductory essay to the Meditations delivers a split verdict on his literary merit: his prose strikes the modern reader as "vapid" and pedantic — "Never was a great literary reputation less deserved." Yet the essayist concedes a larger truth:

> "A sincere heart is better than literary taste; and if Fronto had not done his duty by the young prince, it is not easy to understand the friendship which remained between them up to the last." (030-104) (030-092)

Fronto's truest legacy is not his lost orations but the evidence his letters supply of a teacher who prized honesty above flattery, and a pupil who loved him for it.
