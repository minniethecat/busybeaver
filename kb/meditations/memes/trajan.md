## Overview

Trajan (Marcus Ulpius Traianus, 52–117 CE) was the thirteenth Roman emperor, ruling from 98 to 117 CE. Under his reign the empire reached its greatest territorial extent. Marcus Aurelius references Trajan in the *Meditations* as a standard of imperial justice — noting that even Trajan was superior to some in ensuring a fair hearing for the accused (004-020) — and invokes his age alongside other vanished epochs to illustrate the transience of all things (008-020).

## Life and Career

Born in Hispania, Trajan rose through the military ranks before being adopted by Emperor Nerva. As emperor, he was renowned for his military campaigns (conquering Dacia and Parthia), his extensive public building program in Rome (including Trajan's Forum and Column), and his reputation for fairness and accessibility. His correspondence with Pliny the Younger on the governance of provinces demonstrates the conscientious administration that made him a benchmark for later emperors.

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

Marcus invokes Trajan's example in a passage criticizing the Roman legal system's treatment of Christians: "we hear of no measures taken even to secure that they should have a fair hearing. In this respect Trajan was better than he" (004-020). This rare criticism of Marcus's own era by comparison with a past emperor shows Trajan functioning as a standard of just rule. Marcus also includes Trajan in his meditation on the passage of ages: "consider now the times of Trajan. There likewise thou seest the very self-same things, and that age also is now over and ended" (008-020). Trajan thus serves both as a moral exemplar and as evidence of universal impermanence.

## Legacy and Influence

Trajan's reputation as *optimus princeps* (best ruler) endured throughout the Antonine period. The traditional acclamation for a new emperor — "may he be more fortunate than Augustus and better than Trajan" (*felicior Augusto, melior Traiano*) — shows the standard he set. For Marcus, who ruled as part of the Antonine dynasty that claimed Trajan's legacy, the comparison with Trajan was both an inspiration and a challenge.
