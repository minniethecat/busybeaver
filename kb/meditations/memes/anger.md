## Overview

Anger (*orgē* in Greek, *ira* in Latin) is, in Stoic philosophy, the most dangerous of the passions. Unlike Aristotle, who allowed that anger could be virtuous when felt at the right time and in due measure, the Stoics held that anger is always irrational and always harmful. It arises from the false judgment that one has been wronged and that retaliation is appropriate. Marcus Aurelius, following Epictetus and Seneca, treats anger as a test of philosophical character — the mark of a person who has not yet mastered their ruling faculty [004-012, 016-118].

## In Stoic Ethics

Seneca's *De Ira* is the most comprehensive Stoic treatment of anger, arguing that it is a form of temporary insanity that clouds judgment and leads to actions one would never choose in a calm state. Marcus Aurelius, who had read Seneca and was steeped in this tradition, condenses the Stoic position into practical maxims for daily use. The key Stoic insight is that anger is not caused by external events but by the judgment one forms about those events. No one can make you angry without your consent — anger requires the ruling faculty's assent to the impression that an injury has occurred (012-084).

The Stoic antidote to anger is twofold. First, one must examine the initial impression before assenting: has a genuine wrong been committed, or is this merely a frustrated preference? Second, one must recall that all human beings act from what they believe to be good — the wrongdoer errs not from malice but from ignorance of the true good. This cognitive reframing dissolves the grounds for anger before the passion can take hold (017-014).

## Core Characteristics

Anger is distinguished from other passions by its social dimension. Fear may be solitary; desire may be private. But anger typically involves another person — someone who is perceived to have transgressed. This makes anger a particularly important passion for Marcus, whose daily life as emperor involved constant friction with difficult people [009-052, 009-053].

Marcus's personal discipline regarding anger was legendary. In Book 1, he records with gratitude that he learned from his grandfather Verus "to refrain from all anger and passion" — a lesson reinforced by the example of Antoninus Pius, who never showed even the appearance of anger [001-031, 004-012]. These early entries are not merely autobiographical; they establish anger-management as a multigenerational Stoic inheritance.

The Meditations approaches anger through a distinctive technique: re-description. When someone provokes anger, Marcus advises stepping back and re-describing the situation in terms of universal nature. The person who wrongs you is a kinsman, a fellow participant in reason, acting from ignorance rather than malice. Their action is part of the cosmic fabric, woven by the same nature that governs all things. From this elevated perspective, anger becomes not merely irrational but absurd — like being angry at a stone for falling or at fire for burning (017-025).

## In the Meditations

Anger appears across the Meditations not as an abstract topic but as a lived challenge. Marcus writes to himself about the dangers of being "beguiled" by anger, especially when one feels justified in it (013-076). He notes that anger against nature is particularly futile, since nature's operations are neither good nor evil from the Stoic perspective (017-014).

The passage in Book 22 is especially compressed: "Children's anger, mere babels; wretched souls" (022-075). This brief, almost dismissive treatment captures the Stoic view in miniature: anger is childish, beneath the dignity of a rational adult. It belongs to those who have not yet grown into philosophical maturity.

In Book 16, Marcus states the core Stoic position with clarity: "anger and passion is against reason" (016-118). This is not a softening of the earlier strict view but its reaffirmation. Anger is intrinsically irrational; there is no such thing as justified anger in the Stoic framework. The earlier praise of Antoninus, who showed "never the least appearance of anger" (004-012), sets the standard: not to control anger but to transcend it entirely.
