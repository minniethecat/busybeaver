## Overview

Temperance (Greek σωφροσύνη / _sōphrosynē_, Latin _temperantia_) is one of the four cardinal Stoic virtues and was "the favourite Greek virtue." It is the virtue of moderation — the rational governance of desire, the discipline that keeps the soul free from excess and intemperance. (002-068)

## The Greek Virtue of Moderation

Temperance (_sōphrosynē_) held a privileged place in Greek ethical thought long before the Stoics. It represented the ideal of the well-ordered soul — neither ascetic nor indulgent, but governed by reason and proportion. The Stoics adopted this virtue and integrated it into their system, placing it alongside wisdom, courage, and justice. (002-068)

## Temperance in the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius pairs temperance with truth and fortitude as the virtues whose proper application he must discern in each situation. "Which of all the virtues is the proper virtue for this present use? as whether meekness, fortitude, truth, fidelity." Temperance appears in this list as the virtue of measure and proportion — knowing how much is enough, when to act and when to refrain. (007-101) (007-102)
