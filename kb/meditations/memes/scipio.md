## Overview

The name "Scipio" refers primarily to two great figures of the Roman Republic: Publius Cornelius Scipio Africanus (236–183 BCE), who defeated Hannibal in the Second Punic War, and his adoptive grandson Publius Cornelius Scipio Africanus Minor (185–129 BCE), who destroyed Carthage in the Third Punic War. Both were renowned as exemplars of Roman military and civic virtue.

## Life and Career

Scipio Africanus earned his name by defeating Hannibal at the Battle of Zama (202 BCE), ending the Second Punic War. His grandson, Scipio Africanus Minor, completed Rome's victory over Carthage by razing the city in 146 BCE. Both men embodied the Roman ideal of the citizen-soldier who serves the republic with distinction.

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius mentions "Scipio" in a list of once-famous names now grown obscure: "Camillus, Caeso, Volesius, Leonnatus; not long after, Scipio, Cato, then Augustus, then Hadrian, then Antoninus Pius: all these in a short time will be out of date." This serves his meditation on the impermanence of fame and the vanity of posthumous reputation — a central theme of the Meditations.

## Significance

Scipio represents for Marcus the transience of even the greatest worldly renown. Despite his monumental achievements, his name, like all names, fades with time. This Stoic insight — that fame is indifferent and fleeting — is reinforced by Scipio's inclusion in Marcus's catalogue of the forgotten.
