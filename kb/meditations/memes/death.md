## Overview

Death (Greek θάνατος / _thanatos_) holds a prominent place in Stoic ethical reflection. For the Stoics, death is neither good nor evil — it is an "indifferent" (ἀδιάφορον), a natural process that cannot harm the virtuous soul. The meditation on mortality (_memento mori_) is a central spiritual exercise in the Meditations. (002-044)

## Death as Indifferent

The Stoic classification of death as indifferent follows from their fundamental ethical principle: nothing is good but virtue, and nothing is evil but vice. Death, like health or wealth, belongs to the class of externals that do not affect the soul's capacity for virtue. The Wise Man is prepared for death at any moment, not because he despises life, but because he does not mistake its length for its quality. (002-044) (002-045)

## Death in the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius returns to the theme of death throughout the Meditations with remarkable frequency. He reminds himself of the mortality of all things — emperors and slaves, the famous and the forgotten, entire cities and civilizations. This is not morbid rumination but a spiritual discipline: by confronting death honestly, one learns to value what truly matters and to release attachment to what does not. (002-131)

The brevity of life — "few and evil are the days of man's life" — is not a cause for despair but a call to urgency. Each moment must be lived as if it were the last, with full attention and full virtue. (002-131)
