# Section 12

(012-001) XVI. To desire things impossible is the part of a mad man. But it is a

(012-002) thing impossible, that wicked man should not commit some such things.

(012-003) Neither doth anything happen to any man, which in the ordinary course

(012-004) of nature as natural unto him doth not happen. Again, the same things

(012-005) happen unto others also. And truly, if either he that is ignorant that

(012-006) such a thing hath happened unto him, or he that is ambitious to be

(012-007) commended for his magnanimity, can be patient, and is not grieved: is it

(012-008) not a grievous thing, that either ignorance, or a vain desire to please

(012-009) and to be commended, should be more powerful and effectual than true

(012-010) prudence? As for the things themselves, they touch not the soul, neither

(012-011) can they have any access unto it: neither can they of themselves any

(012-012) ways either affect it, or move it. For she herself alone can affect and

(012-013) move herself, and according as the dogmata and opinions are, which she

(012-014) doth vouchsafe herself; so are those things which, as accessories, have

(012-015) any co-existence with her.

(012-016) XVII. After one consideration, man is nearest unto us; as we are bound

(012-017) to do them good, and to bear with them. But as he may oppose any of our

(012-018) true proper actions, so man is unto me but as a thing indifferent: even

(012-019) as the sun, or the wind, or some wild beast. By some of these it may be,

(012-020) that some operation or other of mine, may be hindered; however, of my

(012-021) mind and resolution itself, there can be no let or impediment, by reason

(012-022) of that ordinary constant both exception (or reservation wherewith it

(012-023) inclineth) and ready conversion of objects; from that which may not be,

(012-024) to that which may be, which in the prosecution of its inclinations, as

(012-025) occasion serves, it doth observe. For by these the mind doth turn and

(012-026) convert any impediment whatsoever, to be her aim and purpose. So that

(012-027) what before was the impediment, is now the principal object of her

(012-028) working; and that which before was in her way, is now her readiest way.

(012-029) XVIII. Honour that which is chiefest and most powerful in the world, and

(012-030) that is it, which makes use of all things, and governs all things. So

(012-031) also in thyself; honour that which is chiefest, and most powerful; and

(012-032) is of one kind and nature with that which we now spake of. For it is the

(012-033) very same, which being in thee, turneth all other things to its own use,

(012-034) and by whom also thy life is governed.

(012-035) XIX. That which doth not hurt the city itself; cannot hurt any citizen.

(012-036) This rule thou must remember to apply and make use of upon every conceit

(012-037) and apprehension of wrong. If the whole city be not hurt by this,

(012-038) neither am I certainly. And if the whole be not, why should I make it

(012-039) my private grievance? consider rather what it is wherein he is overseen

(012-040) that is thought to have done the wrong. Again, often meditate how

(012-041) swiftly all things that subsist, and all things that are done in the

(012-042) world, are carried away, and as it were conveyed out of sight: for both

(012-043) the substance themselves, we see as a flood, are in a continual flux;

(012-044) and all actions in a perpetual change; and the causes themselves,

(012-045) subject to a thousand alterations, neither is there anything almost,

(012-046) that may ever be said to be now settled and constant. Next unto this,

(012-047) and which follows upon it, consider both the infiniteness of the time

(012-048) already past, and the immense vastness of that which is to come, wherein

(012-049) all things are to be resolved and annihilated. Art not thou then a

(012-050) very fool, who for these things, art either puffed up with pride, or

(012-051) distracted with cares, or canst find in thy heart to make such moans as

(012-052) for a thing that would trouble thee for a very long time? Consider the

(012-053) whole universe whereof thou art but a very little part, and the whole

(012-054) age of the world together, whereof but a short and very momentary

(012-055) portion is allotted unto thee, and all the fates and destinies together,

(012-056) of which how much is it that comes to thy part and share! Again: another

(012-057) doth trespass against me. Let him look to that. He is master of his own

(012-058) disposition, and of his own operation. I for my part am in the meantime

(012-059) in possession of as much, as the common nature would have me to possess:

(012-060) and that which mine own nature would have me do, I do.

(012-061) XX. Let not that chief commanding part of thy soul be ever subject to

(012-062) any variation through any corporal either pain or pleasure, neither

(012-063) suffer it to be mixed with these, but let it both circumscribe itself,

(012-064) and confine those affections to their own proper parts and members.

(012-065) But if at any time they do reflect and rebound upon the mind and

(012-066) [[knowledge|understanding]] (as in an united and compacted body it must needs;) then

(012-067) must thou not go about to resist sense and feeling, it being natural.

(012-068) However let not thy understanding to this natural sense and feeling,

(012-069) which whether unto our flesh pleasant or painful, is unto us nothing

(012-070) properly, add an opinion of either good or bad and all is well.

(012-071) XXI. To live with the Gods. He liveth with the Gods, who at all times

(012-072) affords unto them the spectacle of a soul, both contented and well

(012-073) pleased with whatsoever is afforded, or allotted unto her; and

(012-074) performing whatsoever is pleasing to that Spirit, whom (being part of

(012-075) himself) Jove hath appointed to every man as his overseer and governor.

(012-076) XXII. Be not angry neither with him whose breath, neither with him whose

(012-077) arm holes, are offensive. What can he do? such is his breath naturally,

(012-078) and such are his arm holes; and from such, such an effect, and such

(012-079) a smell must of necessity proceed. 'O, but the man (sayest thou) hath

(012-080) understanding in him, and might of himself know, that he by standing

(012-081) near, cannot choose but offend.' And thou also (God bless thee!) hast

(012-082) understanding. Let thy reasonable faculty, work upon his reasonable

(012-083) faculty; show him his fault, admonish him. If he hearken unto thee, thou

(012-084) hast cured him, and there will be no more occasion of anger.

(012-085) XXIII. 'Where there shall neither roarer be, nor harlot.' Why so? As

(012-086) thou dost purpose to live, when thou hast retired thyself to some such

(012-087) place, where neither roarer nor harlot is: so mayest thou here. And if

(012-088) they will not suffer thee, then mayest thou leave thy life rather than

(012-089) thy calling, but so as one that doth not think himself anyways wronged.

(012-090) Only as one would say, Here is a smoke; I will out of it. And what a

(012-091) great matter is this! Now till some such thing force me out, I will

(012-092) continue free; neither shall any man hinder me to do what I will, and

(012-093) my will shall ever be by the proper nature of a reasonable and sociable

(012-094) creature, regulated and directed.

(012-095) XXIV. That rational essence by which the universe is governed, is for

(012-096) community and society; and therefore hath it both made the things that

(012-097) are worse, for the best, and hath allied and knit together those

(012-098) which are best, as it were in an harmony. Seest thou not how it hath

(012-099) sub-ordinated, and co-ordinated? and how it hath distributed unto

(012-100) everything according to its worth? and those which have the pre-eminency

(012-101) and superiority above all, hath it united together, into a mutual

(012-102) consent and agreement.

(012-103) XXV. How hast thou carried thyself hitherto towards the Gods? towards

(012-104) thy parents? towards thy brethren? towards thy wife? towards thy

(012-105) children? towards thy masters? thy foster-fathers? thy friends? thy

(012-106) domestics? thy servants? Is it so with thee, that hitherto thou hast

(012-107) neither by word or deed wronged any of them? Remember withal through how

(012-108) many things thou hast already passed, and how many thou hast been able

(012-109) to endure; so that now the legend of thy life is full, and thy charge is

(012-110) accomplished. Again, how many truly good things have certainly by thee

(012-111) been discerned? how many pleasures, how many pains hast thou passed over

(012-112) with contempt? how many things eternally glorious hast thou despised?

(012-113) towards how many perverse unreasonable men hast thou carried thyself

(012-114) kindly, and discreetly?

(012-115) XXVI. Why should imprudent unlearned souls trouble that which is

(012-116) both learned, and prudent? And which is that that is so? she that

(012-117) understandeth the beginning and the end, and hath the true knowledge of

(012-118) that rational essence, that passeth through all things subsisting, and

(012-119) through all ages being ever the same, disposing and dispensing as it

(012-120) were this universe by certain periods of time.

(012-121) XXVII. Within a very little while, thou wilt be either ashes, or a

(012-122) sceletum; and a name perchance; and perchance, not so much as a name.

(012-123) And what is that but an empty sound, and a rebounding echo? Those things

(012-124) which in this life are dearest unto us, and of most account, they are in

(012-125) themselves but vain, putrid, contemptible. The most weighty and serious,

(012-126) if rightly esteemed, but as puppies, biting one another: or untoward

(012-127) children, now laughing and then crying. As for faith, and modesty, and

(012-128) justice, and truth, they long since, as one of the poets hath it, have

(012-129) abandoned this spacious earth, and retired themselves unto heaven. What

(012-130) is it then that doth keep thee here, if things sensible be so mutable

(012-131) and unsettled? and the senses so obscure, and so fallible? and our souls

(012-132) nothing but an exhalation of blood? and to be in credit among such,

(012-133) be but vanity? What is it that thou dost stay for? an extinction, or a

(012-134) translation; either of them with a propitious and contented mind. But

(012-135) still that time come, what will content thee? what else, but to worship

(012-136) and praise the Gods; and to do good unto men. To bear with them, and

(012-137) to forbear to do them any wrong. And for all external things belonging

(012-138) either to this thy wretched body, or life, to remember that they are

(012-139) neither thine, nor in thy power.

(012-140) XXVIII. Thou mayest always speed, if thou wilt but make choice of the

(012-141) right way; if in the course both of thine opinions and actions, thou

(012-142) wilt observe a true method. These two things be common to the souls, as

(012-143) of God, so of men, and of every reasonable creature, first that in their

(012-144) own proper work they cannot be hindered by anything: and secondly, that

(012-145) their happiness doth consist in a disposition to, and in the practice of

(012-146) [[justice|righteousness]]; and that in these their desire is terminated.

(012-147) XXIX. If this neither be my wicked act, nor an act anyways depending

(012-148) from any wickedness of mine, and that by it the public is not hurt; what

(012-149) doth it concern me? And wherein can the public be hurt? For thou must

(012-150) not altogether be carried by conceit and common opinion: as for help

(012-151) thou must afford that unto them after thy best ability, and as occasion

(012-152) shall require, though they sustain damage, but in these middle or

(012-153) worldly things; but however do not thou conceive that they are truly

(012-154) hurt thereby: for that is not right. But as that old foster-father

(012-155) in the comedy, being now to take his leave doth with a great deal of

(012-156) ceremony, require his foster-child's rhombus, or rattle-top, remembering

(012-157) nevertheless that it is but a rhombus; so here also do thou likewise.

(012-158) For indeed what is all this pleading and public bawling for at the

(012-159) courts? O man, hast thou forgotten what those things are! yea but they

(012-160) are things that others much care for, and highly esteem of. Wilt thou

(012-161) therefore be a fool too? Once I was; let that suffice.

(012-162) XXX. Let death surprise me when it will, and where it will, I may be

(012-163) εὔμοιρος, or a happy man, nevertheless.
