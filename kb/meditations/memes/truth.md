## Overview

Truth (Greek ἀλήθεια / _alētheia_) is the goal of Stoic epistemology. The school developed a sophisticated theory of the "criterion of truth" (κριτήριον / _kritērion_) — a standard by which genuine knowledge could be distinguished from mere opinion or error. (002-031)

## The Criterion of Truth

The Stoics compared the newborn soul to a blank sheet of paper. Sense impressions (φαντασίαι) write upon this surface, and through accumulated experience the soul forms general notions. When an impression is so vivid and consistent that it cannot be denied, it is called a "holding perception" (καταληπτικὴ φαντασία) — one that proceeds from and grasps truth itself. (002-031) (002-032) (002-034) (002-036) (002-037)

Ideas and inferences produced artificially — through deduction or speculation — were tested against this standard of holding perception. Truth was not a matter of logical consistency alone but of correspondence with reality as directly apprehended. (002-037) (002-038)

## Truth and the Wise Man

The Stoic Wise Man, knowing these truths, achieves self-sufficiency (αὐταρκής). He is content even in extreme hardship because he possesses certain knowledge of the good. Truth, in this sense, is not merely cognitive — it is liberating. (002-049)

## Truth in the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius pairs truth with justice as the twin pillars of right conduct. To speak truth, to act without falsehood, to prefer truth over comfort or advantage — these are the marks of the philosophical life. In his self-examination, he asks which virtue the present moment demands: "whether meekness, fortitude, truth, fidelity." Truth is not abstract but practical — the virtue of seeing and saying what is real. (004-060) (007-016) (007-102)

To act contrary to truth is, for Marcus, the fundamental error — "the only thing that is truly hurtful." Living in truth means living in alignment with reality, without self-deception or evasion. (007-152)
