# Section 9

(009-001) XVIII. Not to wander out of the way, but upon every motion and desire,

(009-002) to perform that which is just: and ever to be careful to attain to the

(009-003) true natural apprehension of every fancy, that presents itself.

(009-004) XIX. Whatsoever is expedient unto thee, O World, is expedient unto me;

(009-005) nothing can either be 'unseasonable unto me, or out of date, which unto

(009-006) thee is seasonable. Whatsoever thy seasons bear, shall ever by me be

(009-007) esteemed as happy fruit, and increase. O [[nature|Nature]]! from thee are all

(009-008) things, in thee all things subsist, and to thee all tend. Could he say

(009-009) of [[athens|Athens]], Thou lovely city of Cecrops; and shalt not thou say of the

(009-010) world, Thou lovely city of God?

(009-011) XX. They will say commonly, Meddle not with many things, if thou wilt

(009-012) live cheerfully. Certainly there is nothing better, than for a man

(009-013) to confine himself to necessary actions; to such and so many only, as

(009-014) reason in a creature that knows itself born for society, will command

(009-015) and enjoin. This will not only procure that cheerfulness, which from the

(009-016) goodness, but that also, which from the paucity of actions doth usually

(009-017) proceed. For since it is so, that most of those things, which we either

(009-018) speak or do, are unnecessary; if a man shall cut them off, it must needs

(009-019) follow that he shall thereby gain much leisure, and save much trouble,

(009-020) and therefore at every action a man must privately by way of admonition

(009-021) suggest unto himself, What? may not this that now I go about, be of the

(009-022) number of unnecessary actions? Neither must he use himself to cut off

(009-023) actions only, but thoughts and imaginations also, that are unnecessary

(009-024) for so will unnecessary consequent actions the better be prevented and

(009-025) cut off.

(009-026) XXI. Try also how a good man's life; (of one, who is well pleased with

(009-027) those things whatsoever, which among the common changes and chances of

(009-028) this world fall to his own lot and share; and can live well contented

(009-029) and fully satisfied in the justice of his own proper present action,

(009-030) and in the goodness of his disposition for the future:) will agree with

(009-031) thee. Thou hast had experience of that other kind of life: make now

(009-032) trial of this also. Trouble not thyself any more henceforth, reduce

(009-033) thyself unto perfect simplicity. Doth any man offend? It is against

(009-034) himself that he doth offend: why should it trouble thee? Hath anything

(009-035) happened unto thee? It is well, whatsoever it be, it is that which

(009-036) of all the common chances of the world from the very beginning in the

(009-037) series of all other things that have, or shall happen, was destinated

(009-038) and appointed unto thee. To comprehend all in a few words, our life is

(009-039) short; we must endeavour to gain the present time with best discretion

(009-040) and justice. Use recreation with sobriety.

(009-041) XXII. Either this world is a κόσμος or comely piece, because all

(009-042) disposed and governed by certain order: or if it be a mixture, though

(009-043) confused, yet still it is a comely piece. For is it possible that in

(009-044) thee there should be any beauty at all, and that in the whole world

(009-045) there should be nothing but disorder and confusion? and all things in it

(009-046) too, by natural different properties one from another differenced and

(009-047) distinguished; and yet all through diffused, and by natural sympathy,

(009-048) one to another united, as they are?

(009-049) XXIII. A black or malign disposition, an effeminate disposition; an

(009-050) hard inexorable disposition, a wild inhuman disposition, a sheepish

(009-051) disposition, a childish disposition; a blockish, a false, a scurril, a

(009-052) fraudulent, a tyrannical: what then? If he be a stranger in the world,

(009-053) that knows not the things that are in it; why not be a stranger as well,

(009-054) that wonders at the things that are done in it?

(009-055) XXIV. He is a true fugitive, that flies from reason, by which men are

(009-056) sociable. He blind, who cannot see with the eyes of his [[knowledge|understanding]].

(009-057) He poor, that stands in need of another, and hath not in himself all

(009-058) things needful for this life. He an aposteme of the world, who by being

(009-059) discontented with those things that happen unto him in the world,

(009-060) doth as it were apostatise, and separate himself from common nature's

(009-061) rational administration. For the same nature it is that brings this

(009-062) unto thee, whatsoever it be, that first brought thee into the world. He

(009-063) raises sedition in the city, who by irrational actions withdraws his own

(009-064) soul from that one and common soul of all rational creatures.

(009-065) XXV. There is, who without so much as a coat; and there is, who without

(009-066) so much as a book, doth put philosophy in practice. I am half naked,

(009-067) neither have I bread to eat, and yet I depart not from reason, saith

(009-068) one. But I say; I want the food of good teaching, and instructions, and

(009-069) yet I depart not from reason.

(009-070) XXVI. What art and profession soever thou hast learned, endeavour to

(009-071) affect it, and comfort thyself in it; and pass the remainder of thy life

(009-072) as one who from his whole heart commits himself and whatsoever belongs

(009-073) unto him, unto the gods: and as for men, carry not thyself either

(009-074) tyrannically or servilely towards any.

(009-075) XXVII. Consider in my mind, for example's sake, the times of Vespasian:

(009-076) thou shalt see but the same things: some marrying, some bringing up

(009-077) children, some sick, some dying, some fighting, some feasting, some

(009-078) merchandising, some tilling, some flattering, some boasting, some

(009-079) suspecting, some undermining, some wishing to die, some fretting and

(009-080) murmuring at their present estate, some wooing, some hoarding, some

(009-081) seeking after magistracies, and some after kingdoms. And is not that

(009-082) their age quite over, and ended? Again, consider now the times of

(009-083) Trajan. There likewise thou seest the very self-same things, and that

(009-084) age also is now over and ended. In the like manner consider other

(009-085) periods, both of times and of whole nations, and see how many men, after

(009-086) they had with all their might and main intended and prosecuted some one

(009-087) worldly thing or other did soon after drop away, and were resolved into

(009-088) the elements. But especially thou must call to mind them, whom thou

(009-089) thyself in thy lifetime hast known much distracted about vain things,

(009-090) and in the meantime neglecting to do that, and closely and unseparably

(009-091) (as fully satisfied with it) to adhere unto it, which their own proper

(009-092) constitution did require. And here thou must remember, that thy carriage

(009-093) in every business must be according to the worth and due proportion of

(009-094) it, for so shalt thou not easily be tired out and vexed, if thou shalt

(009-095) not dwell upon small matters longer than is fitting.

(009-096) XXVIII. Those words which once were common and ordinary, are now become

(009-097) obscure and obsolete; and so the names of men once commonly known and

(009-098) famous, are now become in a manner obscure and obsolete names. Camillus,

(009-099) Cæso, Volesius, Leonnatus; not long after, Scipio, Cato, then Augustus,

(009-100) then Adrianus, then [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]]: all these in a short time will

(009-101) be out of date, and, as things of another world as it were, become

(009-102) fabulous. And this I say of them, who once shined as the wonders of

(009-103) their ages, for as for the rest, no sooner are they expired, than with

(009-104) them all their fame and memory. And what is it then that shall always be

(009-105) remembered? all is vanity. What is it that we must bestow our care and

(009-106) diligence upon? even upon this only: that our minds and wills be just;

(009-107) that our actions be charitable; that our speech be never deceitful, or

(009-108) that our understanding be not subject to error; that our inclination be

(009-109) always set to embrace whatsoever shall happen unto us, as necessary,

(009-110) as usual, as ordinary, as flowing from such a beginning, and such a

(009-111) fountain, from which both thou thyself and all things are.

(009-112) Willingly therefore, and wholly surrender up thyself unto that fatal

(009-113) concatenation, yielding up thyself unto the fates, to be disposed of at

(009-114) their pleasure.

(009-115) XXIX. Whatsoever is now present, and from day to day hath its existence;

(009-116) all objects of memories, and the minds and memories themselves,

(009-117) incessantly consider, all things that are, have their being by change

(009-118) and alteration. Use thyself therefore often to meditate upon this, that

(009-119) the nature of the universe delights in nothing more, than in altering

(009-120) those things that are, and in making others like unto them. So that we

(009-121) may say, that whatsoever is, is but as it were the seed of that which

(009-122) shall be. For if thou think that that only is seed, which either the

(009-123) earth or the womb receiveth, thou art very simple.

(009-124) XXX. Thou art now ready to die, and yet hast thou not attained to

(009-125) that perfect simplicity: thou art yet subject to many troubles and

(009-126) perturbations; not yet free from all fear and suspicion of external

(009-127) accidents; nor yet either so meekly disposed towards all men, as thou

(009-128) shouldest; or so affected as one, whose only study and only wisdom is,

(009-129) to be just in all his actions.

(009-130) XXXI. Behold and observe, what is the state of their rational part; and

(009-131) those that the world doth account wise, see what things they fly and are

(009-132) afraid of; and what things they hunt after.

(009-133) XXXII. In another man's mind and understanding thy evil Cannot subsist,

(009-134) nor in any proper temper or distemper of the natural constitution of thy

(009-135) body, which is but as it were the coat or cottage of thy soul. Wherein

(009-136) then, but in that part of thee, wherein the conceit, and apprehension

(009-137) of any misery can subsist? Let not that part therefore admit any such

(009-138) conceit, and then all is well. Though thy body which is so near it

(009-139) should either be cut or burnt, or suffer any corruption or putrefaction,

(009-140) yet let that part to which it belongs to judge of these, be still at

(009-141) rest; that is, let her judge this, that whatsoever it is, that equally

(009-142) may happen to a wicked man, and to a good man, is neither good nor evil.

(009-143) For that which happens equally to him that lives according to nature,

(009-144) and to him that doth not, is neither according to nature, nor against

(009-145) it; and by consequent, neither good nor bad.

(009-146) XXXIII. Ever consider and think upon the world as being but one living

(009-147) substance, and having but one soul, and how all things in the world, are

(009-148) terminated into one sensitive power; and are done by one general motion

(009-149) as it were, and deliberation of that one soul; and how all things that

(009-150) are, concur in the cause of one another's being, and by what manner of

(009-151) connection and concatenation all things happen.

(009-152) XXXIV. What art thou, that better and divine part excepted, but as

(009-153) [[epictetus|Epictetus]] said well, a wretched soul, appointed to carry a carcass up

(009-154) and down?

(009-155) XXXV. To suffer change can be no hurt; as no benefit it is, by change to

(009-156) attain to being. The age and time of the world is as it were a flood and

(009-157) swift current, consisting of the things that are brought to pass in

(009-158) the world. For as soon as anything hath appeared, and is passed away,

(009-159) another succeeds, and that also will presently out of sight.

(009-160) XXXVI. Whatsoever doth happen in the world, is, in the course of nature,

(009-161) as usual and ordinary as a rose in the spring, and fruit in summer. Of

(009-162) the same nature is sickness and death; slander, and lying in wait, and

(009-163) whatsoever else ordinarily doth unto fools use to be occasion either

(009-164) of joy or sorrow. That, whatsoever it is, that comes after, doth always

(009-165) very naturally, and as it were familiarly, follow upon that which was

(009-166) before. For thou must consider the things of the world, not as a loose

(009-167) independent number, consisting merely of necessary events; but as a

(009-168) discreet connection of things orderly and harmoniously disposed. There

(009-169) is then to be seen in the things of the world, not a bare succession,

(009-170) but an admirable correspondence and affinity.
