## Overview

Justice (Greek δικαιοσύνη / _dikaiosynē_, Latin _iustitia_) is one of the four cardinal virtues in Stoic ethics, alongside wisdom, courage, and temperance. It is the virtue of rendering to each what is due, grounded in the recognition that all rational beings share a common nature and are made for one another. (008-021)

## Justice in Stoic Ethics

The Stoic conception of justice flows from their cosmology. Since all human beings share in the divine rational principle, they constitute a single community — a cosmopolis. Justice is the virtue that governs relations within this universal city. It requires treating every person according to their nature as a rational being, without regard to external status or circumstance. (008-021)

The Stoics held that justice and equality were natural principles, not mere social conventions. Marcus Aurelius admired the ideal of a kingdom "wherein should be regarded" — a political order governed by justice and equality rather than power and privilege. (004-041)

## Justice and the Inner Life

For Marcus Aurelius, justice is not only a public virtue but an inward discipline. The "petty annoyances of injustice or unkindness" are referred inward to the soul, where they are to be managed by rational reflection rather than outward retaliation. The Stoic does not demand justice from the world; he practices it himself. (002-112)

In his private meditations, Marcus lists justice alongside truth, gravity, natural affection, and freedom as the qualities that define a life well lived. Justice is inseparable from the other virtues — it is truth in action, wisdom in relation to others. (005-062)

## Justice in the Meditations

The administration of justice was a central duty of the Roman emperor, and Marcus took this responsibility seriously. The Meditations record his constant vigilance against acting contrary to justice — for acting unjustly harms the agent more than the victim, corrupting the soul's alignment with reason.
