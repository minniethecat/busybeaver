## Overview

In Stoic ethics, **temper** or temperance (Greek: ἐγκράτεια, *enkrateia*) is the virtue of self-mastery — the capacity to govern one's desires, impulses, and emotions through reason. It is one of the four cardinal virtues, alongside wisdom, justice, and courage. Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations* portray temper not as mere restraint but as the positive condition of a soul that is harmoniously ordered (007-016).

> "Temperate? or wise? or circumspect?"
> (010-057)

## Core Characteristics

Temperance in the Stoic sense is broader than abstinence from pleasure: it is the proper ordering of all desires according to nature and reason. Marcus contrasts the temperate person with the "intemperate" who lacks self-governance (004-137), and describes the temperate character as one that maintains equilibrium in all circumstances. True temper is "towards all worldly chances and events ever so disposed" — not merely the suppression of appetite but a consistent disposition of moderation and equanimity (007-162).

The virtue operates at multiple levels: it governs bodily appetites, checks the impulse toward anger, moderates ambition, and ensures that even grief — the most human of emotions — does not become excessive. In this sense, temper is not one virtue among equals but the guardian of the soul's overall order.

## In the Meditations

Marcus holds up temperance as a daily aspiration. He asks himself: "temperate? or wise? or circumspect?" (010-057), treating self-mastery as part of the continual self-examination that defines the Stoic life. His first book celebrates those who modeled a "modest self-reliance" (002-101), and throughout the work he returns to the practice of checking impulses before they become passions. For Marcus, the temperate soul is not deprived but free — released from the tyranny of excessive desire.

## Temperance Among the Cardinal Virtues

Marcus inherited the fourfold classification of virtue from Plato through the Stoic tradition, but he gives temperance a distinctively practical inflection. Where wisdom discerns what is truly good, courage withstands what is fearful, and justice renders to each their due, temperance ensures that the soul itself remains rightly ordered — neither grasping at pleasures nor recoiling from pains beyond what reason prescribes.

The emperor frames temperance not as a single act but as a settled disposition of character. After observing "so much heard and read of prudence, temperance, justice, fortitude" (008-008), he insists that these are not subjects for academic study alone but must be embodied in daily conduct. The temperance he practices is not the austerity of the desert ascetic but the measured self-governance of a man living fully in the world — at court, in camp, among the duties of empire — while refusing to let external circumstances dictate his inner state.

> "So much heard and read of prudence, temperance, justice, fortitude."
> (008-008)
