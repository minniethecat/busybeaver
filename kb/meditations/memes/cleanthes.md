## Overview

Cleanthes of Assos (c. 330–230 BCE) was a Greek Stoic philosopher who succeeded Zeno of Citium as the second head (scholarch) of the Stoic school in Athens. He is best known for his *Hymn to Zeus*, one of the most complete surviving texts from the early Stoa, and for teaching Chrysippus, who would become the great systematizer of Stoicism. Marcus refers to Cleanthes and his place in the Stoic lineage when he notes that "but for Chrysippus, there had been no Porch" — a remark about the third scholarch that implicitly acknowledges Cleanthes' essential role in preserving the school between its founder and its greatest theorist (001-182–183).

## Life and Career

Cleanthes was born in Assos, in the Troad, and came to Athens as a young man to study under Zeno of Citium, the founder of the Stoic school. Unlike many philosophers of the period who came from wealthy families, Cleanthes was said to have worked as a water-carrier by night to support his studies — an image of disciplined endurance that made him an exemplar of Stoic self-sufficiency.

> I do not know how: but every man loves himself better than all the rest of men, and yet sets less value upon his own conceit and opinion of himself than upon the conceit and opinion of others. (Zeno's school, 001-173)

After Zeno's death, Cleanthes assumed leadership of the Stoa and held it for over thirty years. His tenure was marked not by dramatic doctrinal innovation but by faithful transmission: he preserved Zeno's teachings and passed them, intact, to Chrysippus, whose systematic genius would give Stoicism its definitive form. Marcus acknowledges Cleanthes' role as the indispensable middle link between the founder and the systematizer when he reflects on the succession from Zeno, through Cleanthes, to Chrysippus (001-182–183).

## Philosophical Contributions

Cleanthes' most enduring contribution is the *Hymn to Zeus*, a short poem of profound theological significance. The hymn articulates a vision of Zeus as the rational principle that governs the cosmos — not a capricious deity but the immanent logos that orders all things toward the good. This conception of a rational, providential universe became central to Stoic theology and deeply influenced Marcus Aurelius' own worldview.

> The virtue of a man makes his own commendation. (002-119)

The hymn's most famous lines — "Lead me, O Zeus, and thou too, Destiny, wherever you have appointed me" — express the core Stoic principle of willing acceptance of fate. This is the same disposition Marcus cultivates throughout the Meditations: the readiness to follow nature wherever it leads, without resistance or complaint. Marcus echoes this Cleanthean sentiment when he writes that the rational soul "can pass through every obstacle" that confronts it, adapting to circumstance without being diminished (008-035).

In addition to the hymn, Cleanthes contributed to Stoic physics and epistemology. He developed the doctrine of *tonos* — the tension or "vital heat" that pervades all matter and gives it coherence — a concept that would influence later Stoic physics. He also wrote on logic and ethics, though most of his works survive only in fragments preserved by later authors.

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

Marcus does not name Cleanthes directly in the Meditations. However, the theology expressed in the *Hymn to Zeus* — a cosmos governed by universal law, to which the wise person willingly conforms — aligns deeply with Marcus's own understanding of providence and nature (004-023). Cleanthes' poetic insistence that nothing happens apart from Zeus's design finds its philosophical counterpart in Marcus's repeated assertion that "nothing is evil which is according to nature" (002-127).

> All things whatsoever that are in the whole world, are contained in it as parts and members of one body, consisting of one substance and of one soul. (004-040)

Cleanthes also exemplifies a quality Marcus valued deeply: the willingness to labor in obscurity for the sake of the philosophical tradition. As a man who worked by night and studied by day, who preserved and transmitted without seeking personal fame, Cleanthes embodied the Stoic ideal of service to the logos. Marcus, who often reminded himself that fame is empty and that the only worthwhile ambition is to do one's work well, would have recognized in Cleanthes a kindred spirit.

## Legacy and Influence

As the bridge between Zeno's founding vision and Chrysippus' systematic elaboration, Cleanthes played an essential role in the survival of early Stoicism. His *Hymn to Zeus* remains a landmark text of philosophical theology, articulating the Stoic conception of a rational, providential cosmos in poetic form. The hymn influenced not only the later Stoic tradition but also early Christian theology, which found in Cleanthes' monotheistic language a natural point of contact with the Stoic worldview.

> Paul, in his speech to the Athenians on Mar's Hill, quoted Cleanthes: "For we are also his offspring" — a line from the *Hymn to Zeus* that entered the Christian canon through Acts 17:28.

Cleanthes' doctrine of *tonos* — the vital tension that sustains all things — also proved influential, providing a physical basis for the Stoic theory of the soul and its ruling faculty. Though largely overshadowed by Chrysippus in later doxography, Cleanthes was remembered by the Stoics themselves as a figure of immense integrity and dedication — the man who kept the Porch standing long enough for it to flourish.
