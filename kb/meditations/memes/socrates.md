## Overview

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was the Athenian philosopher whose life and death became the founding exemplar of the Western philosophical tradition. Though he wrote nothing, his method of questioning and his willingness to die for his principles made him the model of the philosophical life. Marcus Aurelius invokes Socrates repeatedly in the Meditations, treating him as the archetype of wisdom, integrity, and cheerful confidence in the face of death. (003-024) (015-046)

## Life and Death

Socrates lived in Athens during its golden age and its decline, serving as a hoplite in the Peloponnesian War before devoting himself to philosophy. His practice of questioning prominent Athenians about virtue, justice, and knowledge earned him both devoted followers and powerful enemies. In 399 BCE, he was tried and condemned to death on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth. He accepted the verdict with equanimity, refusing opportunities to escape, and drank the hemlock in the company of his friends — a death that became the defining image of philosophical integrity.

## Marcus's Socrates

For Marcus, Socrates represents the highest ideal of the philosophical life. He speaks of "Socrates his words" advising withdrawal from sensuality — a theme Marcus himself develops at length. (007-026) He places Socrates among the great philosophers whose example transcends their mortality: "Heraclitus, Pythagoras, Socrates — where so many heroes of philosophy?" (015-046) He contrasts Socrates with the "vermin" of false accusers, noting that even the wisest were persecuted. (006-121) The quality Marcus most admires in Socrates is the "cheerful confidence" with which he faced death — not grim resignation but active trust in reason and virtue. (003-024) Marcus also notes the Socratic practice recorded by his followers, reflecting that Socrates "knew how to abstain from, and how to enjoy, those things which many are too weak to abstain from and cannot enjoy without excess." (004-135)

## Legacy

Socrates's influence on Stoicism came primarily through his student Plato and through the Cynic tradition, which claimed Socrates as a precursor. The Stoics saw in Socrates the living proof that virtue is sufficient for happiness — that a person who possesses wisdom and integrity cannot be harmed by external circumstances, not even by death. Marcus names Socrates alongside Epictetus as someone whose example proves that the philosophical life is possible for mortal beings. (016-085)
