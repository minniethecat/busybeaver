# Section 22

(022-001) VIII. Man, God, the world, every one in their kind, bear some fruits.

(022-002) All things have their proper time to bear. Though by custom, the word

(022-003) itself is in a manner become proper unto the vine, and the like, yet is

(022-004) it so nevertheless, as we have said. As for reason, that beareth both

(022-005) common fruit for the use of others; and peculiar, which itself doth

(022-006) enjoy. [[reason|Reason]] is of a diffusive nature, what itself is in itself, it

(022-007) begets in others, and so doth multiply.

(022-008) IX. Either teach them better if it be in thy power; or if it be not,

(022-009) remember that for this use, to bear with them patiently, was mildness

(022-010) and goodness granted unto thee. The Gods themselves are good unto such;

(022-011) yea and in some things, (as in matter of health, of wealth, of honour,)

(022-012) are content often to further their endeavours: so good and gracious are

(022-013) they. And mightest thou not be so too? or, tell me, what doth hinder

(022-014) thee?

(022-015) X. Labour not as one to whom it is appointed to be wretched, nor as one

(022-016) that either would be pitied, or admired; but let this be thine only care

(022-017) and desire; so always and in all things to prosecute or to forbear, as

(022-018) the law of charity, or mutual society doth require.

(022-019) XI. This day I did come out of all my trouble. Nay I have cast out all

(022-020) my trouble; it should rather be for that which troubled thee, whatsoever

(022-021) it was, was not without anywhere that thou shouldest come out of it, but

(022-022) within in thine own opinions, from whence it must be cast out, before

(022-023) thou canst truly and constantly be at ease.

(022-024) XII. All those things, for matter of experience are usual and ordinary;

(022-025) for their continuance but for a day; and for their matter, most base and

(022-026) filthy. As they were in the days of those whom we have buried, so are

(022-027) they now also, and no otherwise.

(022-028) XIII. The things themselves that affect us, they stand without doors,

(022-029) neither knowing anything themselves nor able to utter anything unto

(022-030) others concerning themselves. What then is it, that passeth verdict on

(022-031) them? The [[knowledge|understanding]].

(022-032) XIV. As virtue and wickedness consist not in passion, but in action; so

(022-033) neither doth the true good or evil of a reasonable charitable man

(022-034) consist in passion, but in operation and action.

(022-035) XV. To the stone that is cast up, when it comes down it is no hurt unto

(022-036) it; as neither benefit, when it doth ascend.

(022-037) XVI. Sift their minds and understandings, and behold what men they be,

(022-038) whom thou dost stand in fear of what they shall judge of thee, what they

(022-039) themselves judge of themselves.

(022-040) XVII. All things that are in the world, are always in the estate

(022-041) of alteration. Thou also art in a perpetual change, yea and under

(022-042) corruption too, in some part: and so is the whole world.

(022-043) XVIII. it is not thine, but another man's sin. Why should it trouble

(022-044) thee? Let him look to it, whose sin it is.

(022-045) XIX. Of an operation and of a purpose there is an ending, or of an

(022-046) action and of a purpose we say commonly, that it is at an end: from

(022-047) opinion also there is an absolute cessation, which is as it were the

(022-048) death of it. In all this there is no hurt. Apply this now to a man's

(022-049) age, as first, a child; then a youth, then a young man, then an old man;

(022-050) every change from one age to another is a kind of death And all this

(022-051) while here no matter of grief yet. Pass now unto that life first, that

(022-052) which thou livedst under thy grandfather, then under thy mother, then

(022-053) under thy father. And thus when through the whole course of thy life

(022-054) hitherto thou hast found and observed many alterations, many changes,

(022-055) many kinds of endings and cessations, put this question to thyself What

(022-056) matter of grief or sorrow dost thou find in any of these? Or what doest

(022-057) thou suffer through any of these? If in none of these, then neither

(022-058) in the ending and consummation of thy whole life, which is also but a

(022-059) cessation and change.

(022-060) XX. As occasion shall require, either to thine own understanding, or to

(022-061) that of the universe, or to his, whom thou hast now to do with, let thy

(022-062) refuge be with all speed. To thine own, that it resolve upon nothing

(022-063) against justice. To that of the universe, that thou mayest remember,

(022-064) part of whom thou art. Of his, that thou mayest consider whether in the

(022-065) estate of ignorance, or of knowledge. And then also must thou call to

(022-066) mind, that he is thy kinsman.

(022-067) XXI. As thou thyself, whoever thou art, were made for the perfection and

(022-068) consummation, being a member of it, of a common society; so must every

(022-069) action of thine tend to the perfection and consummation of a life that

(022-070) is truly sociable. What action soever of thine therefore that either

(022-071) immediately or afar off, hath not reference to the common good, that is

(022-072) an exorbitant and disorderly action; yea it is seditious; as one among

(022-073) the people who from such and such a consent and unity, should factiously

(022-074) divide and separate himself.

(022-075) XXII. Children's anger, mere babels; wretched souls bearing up dead

(022-076) bodies, that they may not have their fall so soon: even as it is in that

(022-077) common dirge song.

(022-078) XXIII. Go to the quality of the cause from which the effect doth

(022-079) proceed. Behold it by itself bare and naked, separated from all that is

(022-080) material. Then consider the utmost bounds of time that that cause, thus

(022-081) and thus qualified, can subsist and abide.

(022-082) XXIV. Infinite are the troubles and miseries, that thou hast already

(022-083) been put to, by reason of this only, because that for all happiness

(022-084) it did not suffice thee, or, that thou didst not account it sufficient

(022-085) happiness, that thy understanding did operate according to its natural

(022-086) constitution.

(022-087) XXV. When any shall either impeach thee with false accusations, or

(022-088) hatefully reproach thee, or shall use any such carriage towards thee,

(022-089) get thee presently to their minds and understandings, and look in them,

(022-090) and behold what manner of men they be. Thou shalt see, that there is no

(022-091) such occasion why it should trouble thee, what such as they are think of

(022-092) thee. Yet must thou love them still, for by nature they are thy friends.

(022-093) And the Gods themselves, in those things that they seek from them as

(022-094) matters of great moment, are well content, all manner of ways, as by

(022-095) dreams and oracles, to help them as well as others.

(022-096) XXVI. Up and down, from one age to another, go the ordinary things of

(022-097) the world; being still the same. And either of everything in particular

(022-098) before it come to pass, the mind of the universe doth consider with

(022-099) itself and deliberate: and if so, then submit for shame unto the

(022-100) determination of such an excellent understanding: or once for all it did

(022-101) resolve upon all things in general; and since that whatsoever happens,

(022-102) happens by a necessary consequence, and all things indivisibly in a

(022-103) manner and inseparably hold one of another. In sum, either there is a

(022-104) God, and then all is well; or if all things go by chance and fortune,

(022-105) yet mayest thou use thine own providence in those things that concern

(022-106) thee properly; and then art thou well.

(022-107) XXVII. Within a while the earth shall cover us all, and then she herself

(022-108) shall have her change. And then the course will be, from one period of

(022-109) eternity unto another, and so a perpetual eternity. Now can any man

(022-110) that shall consider with himself in his mind the several rollings or

(022-111) successions of so many changes and alterations, and the swiftness of all

(022-112) these rulings; can he otherwise but contemn in his heart and despise

(022-113) all worldly things? The cause of the universe is as it were a strong

(022-114) torrent, it carrieth all away.

(022-115) XXVIII. And these your professed politicians, the only true practical

(022-116) philosophers of the world, (as they think of themselves) so full of

(022-117) affected gravity, or such professed lovers of virtue and honesty, what

(022-118) wretches be they in very deed; how vile and contemptible in themselves?

(022-119) O man! what ado doest thou keep? Do what thy nature doth now require.

(022-120) Resolve upon it, if thou mayest: and take no thought, whether anybody

(022-121) shall know it or no. Yea, but sayest thou, I must not expect a Plato's

(022-122) commonwealth. If they profit though never so little, I must be content;

(022-123) and think much even of that little progress. Doth then any of them

(022-124) forsake their former false opinions that I should think they profit? For

(022-125) without a change of opinions, alas! what is all that ostentation, but

(022-126) mere wretchedness of slavish minds, that groan privately, and yet would

(022-127) make a show of obedience to reason, and truth? Go too now and tell me

(022-128) of Alexander and Philippus, and Demetrius Phalereus. Whether they

(022-129) understood what the common nature requireth, and could rule themselves

(022-130) or no, they know best themselves. But if they kept a life, and

(022-131) swaggered; I (God be thanked) am not bound to imitate them. The effect

(022-132) of true philosophy is, unaffected simplicity and modesty. Persuade me

(022-133) not to ostentation and vainglory.

(022-134) XXIX. From some high place as it were to look down, and to behold

(022-135) here flocks, and there sacrifices, without number; and all kind of

(022-136) navigation; some in a rough and stormy sea, and some in a calm: the

(022-137) general differences, or different estates of things, some, that are now

(022-138) first upon being; the several and mutual relations of those things that

(022-139) are together; and some other things that are at their last. Their lives

(022-140) also, who were long ago, and theirs who shall be hereafter, and the

(022-141) present estate and life of those many nations of barbarians that are

(022-142) now in the world, thou must likewise consider in thy mind. And how many

(022-143) there be, who never so much as heard of thy name, how many that will

(022-144) soon forget it; how many who but even now did commend thee, within a

(022-145) very little while perchance will speak ill of thee. So that neither

(022-146) fame, nor honour, nor anything else that this world doth afford, is

(022-147) worth the while. The sum then of all; whatsoever doth happen unto thee,

(022-148) whereof God is the cause, to accept it contentedly: whatsoever thou

(022-149) doest, whereof thou thyself art the cause, to do it justly: which will

(022-150) be, if both in thy resolution and in thy action thou have no further

(022-151) end, than to do good unto others, as being that, which by thy natural

(022-152) constitution, as a man, thou art bound unto.

(022-153) XXX. Many of those things that trouble and straiten thee, it is in thy

(022-154) power to cut off, as wholly depending from mere conceit and opinion; and

(022-155) then thou shalt have room enough.

(022-156) XXXI. To comprehend the whole world together in thy mind, and the whole

(022-157) course of this present age to represent it unto thyself, and to fix thy

(022-158) thoughts upon the sudden change of every particular object. How short

(022-159) the time is from the generation of anything, unto the dissolution of

(022-160) the same; but how immense and infinite both that which was before the

(022-161) generation, and that which after the generation of it shall be. All

(022-162) things that thou seest, will soon be perished, and they that see their

(022-163) corruptions, will soon vanish away themselves. He that dieth a hundred

(022-164) years old, and he that dieth young, shall come all to one.

(022-165) XXXII. What are their minds and understandings; and what the things that

(022-166) they apply themselves unto: what do they love, and what do they hate

(022-167) for? Fancy to thyself the estate of their souls openly to be seen. When

(022-168) they think they hurt them shrewdly, whom they speak ill of; and when

(022-169) they think they do them a very good turn, whom they commend and extol: O

(022-170) how full are they then of conceit, and opinion!
