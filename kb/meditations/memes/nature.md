## Overview

Nature (Greek φύσις / _physis_) is the foundational concept of Stoic ethics and cosmology. The Stoic ethical imperative — to "live in conformity with nature" (ὁμολογουμένος τῇ φύσει ζῆν) — identifies the supreme good with harmony between individual conduct and the rational order of the cosmos. (002-004) (002-005)

## The Stoic Maxim

Zeno of Citium, the founder of Stoicism, first formulated the end of life as "living consistently" (ὁμολογουμένος ζῆν). His successors refined this to "living in conformity with nature," clarifying that consistency must be grounded in knowledge of the cosmic order, not mere personal habit. (002-003) (002-004)

This maxim might be misinterpreted as advocating surrender to every natural impulse. The Stoics emphatically rejected this reading: living in accord with nature requires first understanding what nature is. To this end, the Stoics developed a threefold division of philosophy into Physics, Logic, and Ethics. (002-006) (002-007) (002-008) (002-009)

## Nature as Divine Order

The Stoic conception of nature is inseparable from their theology. The divine ruling principle makes all things work together for the good of the whole. The highest human good lies in consciously cooperating with this divine purpose — working with God for the common good. (002-024) (002-025) (002-026)

In the individual, it is virtue alone that enables a person to live in accord with nature. As Providence governs the universe, so virtue must govern the soul. (002-027) (002-028)

## Nature as Moral Standard

The Stoics judged the worth of actions not by their social consequences but by their conformity or nonconformity to nature. An act is good because it accords with the rational order; it is evil because it deviates from it. External goods — wealth, health, reputation — are indifferent (ἀδιάφορα) because they do not affect one's capacity to live according to nature. (001-161) (002-085) (002-180)
