## Overview

Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was a Stoic philosopher born a slave in Hierapolis, Phrygia. Freed by his master Epaphroditus, he studied under the great Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus and later founded his own school in Nicopolis after being banished from Rome by Domitian. Epictetus wrote nothing himself, but his student Arrian preserved his teachings in the *Discourses* and *Enchiridion*. Marcus Aurelius records his gratitude "that ever I lighted upon Epictetus his writings" (001-004), and the *Meditations* are saturated with Epictetan themes and direct quotations.

## Life and Career

Born into slavery, Epictetus studied Stoic philosophy under Musonius Rufus, who was renowned for teaching even to slaves. After gaining freedom, he taught in Rome until Domitian expelled all philosophers from the city around 93 CE. Epictetus relocated to Nicopolis on the Greek coast, founding a school that attracted students from across the empire. His lectures, preserved by Arrian, articulate the core Stoic doctrines: the dichotomy of control (some things are up to us, others not), the discipline of assent, and the proper use of impressions. His influence on Marcus came primarily through these transcribed teachings.

## Influence on Marcus Aurelius and Stoicism

Marcus repeatedly invokes Epictetus as an authority. In Book 5, he writes: "Epictetus said well, a wretched soul, appointed to carry a carcass up and down" (005-019), echoing the Stoic theme of the body as an indifferent vessel carrying the ruling faculty. In Book 11, Marcus quotes Epictetus directly: "'Of the free will there is no thief or robber'" (019-031), affirming the Stoic doctrine that moral choice (*prohairesis*) is inviolable. Marcus's entire moral psychology — the distinction between impressions and judgments, the practice of saying "this is an impression, not the thing itself" — derives from Epictetan teaching. The three disciplines of the Meditations (desire, action, assent) map directly onto Epictetus's ethical framework.

## Legacy and Influence

Epictetus is the most significant single influence on the *Meditations*. Where Marcus learned Stoic physics from the school tradition and public duty from Antoninus Pius, the inner discipline and psychological techniques that define the work come directly from Epictetus. Through Marcus, Epictetus's teachings shaped later Stoic revival movements from the Renaissance to modern cognitive behavioral therapy. His insistence that moral freedom cannot be taken from us became one of Stoicism's most enduring contributions to Western thought.
