## Overview

Fame (Greek δόξα / _doxa_, Latin _gloria_) is a recurring target of Stoic critique in the Meditations. Marcus Aurelius repeatedly reminds himself that posthumous reputation is worthless — the dead are soon forgotten, and the opinion of future generations, who are no wiser than the present, is no standard by which to live. (002-107)

## The Emptiness of Fame

The Stoic argument against the pursuit of fame is both practical and philosophical. Practically, fame is fleeting: all who are famous now will be forgotten, just as all who were famous before are forgotten now. Philosophically, fame depends on the opinions of others, and the opinions of others are externals — beyond one's control and irrelevant to virtue. As emperor, Marcus was the most famous man in the Roman world, yet he repeatedly told himself that this fame meant nothing. The crowds who cheer today will forget tomorrow. Only virtue endures. (002-107)

## Fame and the Inner Life

The pursuit of fame is harmful not only because it is futile but because it distracts from the inner work of philosophy. Fame-seeking is a form of slavery — making one's happiness dependent on the judgment of strangers. Marcus distinguishes the life of genuine duty from the life oriented outward, toward the gaze of others, rather than inward, toward the governance of the soul. (002-107)
