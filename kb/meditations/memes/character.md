## Overview

Character (Greek ἦθος / ēthos, "custom, disposition, moral nature") in Stoic philosophy is the settled moral quality of a person, formed by the accumulation of choices, judgments, and habits over a lifetime. Unlike momentary impulses or passing emotions, character is durable — it is what a person reliably is, not what they occasionally do. Marcus Aurelius devoted careful attention to the character of those around him, treating the observation of fine character as both a source of moral instruction and a reminder of what the philosophical life should produce. (001-032) (034-114)

## In Stoic Ethics

The Stoics held that character is entirely within our power, because it is constituted by our judgments and choices — the only things that truly depend on us. External circumstances do not shape character; rather, character shapes how we respond to circumstances. This is why the same adversity can crush one person and ennoble another. The formation of character is the central project of Stoic ethics: through philosophical study, self-examination, and the repeated practice of right judgment, one gradually transforms one's ēthos from the raw material of natural temperament into the finished product of virtue. The ideal is the Stoic sage (σοφός), whose character is so perfectly formed that they act virtuously in every circumstance without internal conflict.

## Exemplars of Character

Marcus Aurelius opens the Meditations with an extended reflection on the character of those who shaped him — a practice that reveals how central the observation of character was to his moral method. He recalls how his teacher "divined the fine character of the lad" and nurtured it. (001-032) He offers a sustained portrait of Antoninus Pius, whose character was "simple and temperate, and had none of the vulgar love of show" — a model of imperial virtue. (034-114) He even reflects on noble character outside his immediate circle, noting approvingly that "character and princely generosity" are recognizable across cultures and stations. (030-114) These exemplars serve not as objects of uncritical hero-worship but as living proofs that virtue is attainable — each demonstrates a particular facet of character that Marcus seeks to cultivate in himself.

## In the Meditations

Beyond the portraits of exemplars, Marcus treats character as an ongoing personal project. He asks himself whether he is becoming the person he intends to be — whether his character is aligning with his principles. The question is not "Have I performed virtuous actions today?" but "Am I becoming more virtuous in my settled dispositions?" He also reflects on how even those of great talent may lack nobility of character, citing the example of Telauges: not all who are gifted are "nobler in character than Sophocles." (033-183) This recognition — that talent and character are independent dimensions — sharpens the Stoic emphasis on the priority of character over all other human attributes.
