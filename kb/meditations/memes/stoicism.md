## Overview

Stoicism is the philosophical school that provides the intellectual foundation for the Meditations (001-041). Founded by Zeno of Citium in Athens around 300 BCE (001-173), the school takes its name from the *Stoa Poikile* (Painted Porch) where Zeno taught (001-182). The Stoic system was organized into a comprehensive body of doctrine by Chrysippus (280–207 BCE), of whom it was said: "But for Chrysippus, there had been no Porch" (002-001).

## In Stoic Ethics

The central Stoic ethical principle is to live in conformity with nature (*ὁμολογουμένως τῇ φύσει*), a formula first articulated by Zeno (002-003, 002-005). To achieve this, the Stoics held that one must first understand what nature is, which required a threefold division of philosophy into Physics, Ethics, and Logic (002-008, 002-009).

The Stoics aspired to *ἁπάθεια* (apatheia), the repression of the emotions — though in practice the ideal differed from the Epicurean *ἀταραξία* only in name (001-167). A central ethical doctrine held that virtue is the sole good and vice the sole evil; all external things — health, wealth, pleasure, pain — were classified as "indifferents" (ἁδιάφορα) (034-150). The Stoic was called upon to control desire and aversion, pursuing nothing that is not within the sphere of moral purpose (002-064).

## Core Doctrines

**Physics.** The Stoic system of physics was materialistic — only bodies and their phenomena alone really exist (002-014, 002-016). The Stoics held that the universe is governed by divine reason (*logos*), and the Stoic gods sit aloof from all human affairs (003-017).

**The Sage.** The Stoic sage became in Marcus Aurelius' hands almost a religious ideal — a figure of perfect wisdom who controls their desires, lives in accord with nature, and cannot be harmed by external events (002-087). The uncompromising stiffness of Zeno or Chrysippus is softened and transformed in the Meditations into a more personal and introspective practice (002-084).

**Theory of Knowledge.** In Logic, the Stoic system is noteworthy for their theory of knowledge and their contributions to grammar and propositional logic (002-030). The Stoics regarded speculation as a means to an end — knowledge was to serve right conduct, not idle curiosity (002-002).

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius was not a head of a school writing a treatise on Stoicism; he was an emperor writing to himself (002-080). His engagement with Stoicism came primarily through the lectures of Junius Rusticus, who introduced him to the Discourses of Epictetus (003-167, 034-103). The Stoic ideal of *apatheia* — calmness in all circumstances — pervades the work (034-037, 004-014).

The emperor's Stoicism is practical rather than theoretical. He repeatedly returns to the theme that the Stoic was called upon to control his desires, to distinguish between what is in his power and what is not, and to accept whatever happens as the will of a providentially ordered universe (002-064).
