## Overview

In Stoic philosophy, discipline (_askesis_, ἄσκησις) is not punishment or deprivation but systematic training in virtue — the daily practice by which the soul is strengthened against error and passion. Marcus Aurelius adopts the language of the philosophical schools when he speaks of "the Grecian discipline" (003-138), referring to the tradition of spiritual exercises by which a person shapes their character. Discipline is the bridge between knowing what is right and actually doing it: philosophy without practice is mere cleverness; with practice, it becomes "a strict man of the old discipline" (032-154).

## The Three Disciplines

Pierre Hadot's influential reading of the Meditations identifies three fundamental disciplines that structure Marcus's practice. The **discipline of judgment** (_hupolepsis_) concerns the correct use of impressions — refusing to assent to false value judgments about externals. The **discipline of desire** (_orexis_) aligns what we want with cosmic nature, desiring only what fate actually delivers. The **discipline of action** (_horme_) governs our impulses toward others, ensuring that every act is just, kind, and sociable.

Marcus does not systematize these three disciplines explicitly, but they pervade every Book of the Meditations. His morning self-exhortation — "Betimes in the morning" (005-001) — is itself a discipline: the regular practice of reminding himself what kind of day awaits and what virtues it demands. The entire work is a written record of spiritual exercise, the "Grecian discipline" made daily habit.

## Philosophical Exercise and Self-Training

For Marcus, philosophy is inherently practical. He describes his own turn to philosophy not as an intellectual conversion but as a decision "to apply myself unto philosophy" as a way of life (003-134). This involved specific exercises: premeditation on adversity (_praemeditatio malorum_), examination of conscience at day's end, and the constant testing of impressions against reason.

The Stoic tradition provided a rich repertoire of such exercises. Marcus mentions "bodily exercises" (003-145) but subordinates them to the training of the soul. The true athlete of virtue "must so exercise thyself" that neither success nor failure disturbs the ruling faculty (010-152). Every occasion — even annoyance, even pain — becomes "the exercise of" virtue (015-078), material for practice.

## Discipline and the Ruling Faculty

The mind that is "once truly disciplined and purged" is proof against disturbance (007-060). Marcus's metaphor is of purification: discipline removes the rust and corruption of false opinion, revealing the natural brilliance of reason beneath. This disciplined mind "exercise justice" (016-146) not as a strain but as its natural function — discipline has made virtue second nature.

Crucially, discipline is not solitary. Marcus's models are figures like "Africanus" and "Metellus" (032-165) — Romans of "the old discipline" whose lives of rigor and duty exemplify the Stoic ideal (032-154). Discipline connects the individual practitioner to a tradition, a lineage of those who have trained themselves in the same way.

## In the Meditations

Discipline appears throughout the Meditations as both subject and method:

- **Book 3**: Marcus recounts his turn to philosophy and mentions the "Grecian discipline" as the proper training for one who professes philosophy (003-138) (003-134) (003-145).
- **Book 7**: "The mind that is once truly disciplined and purged" — the fullest statement of discipline's effect on the ruling faculty (007-060).
- **Book 10**: "So exercise thyself" — the imperative form of philosophical self-training (010-152).
- **Book 15**: Every encounter is "for the exercise of" virtue — discipline as the reframing of all experience (015-078).
- **Book 16**: Discipline culminates in justice — "if it exercise justice" the ruling faculty achieves its purpose (016-146). The mind "still retain its own tranquillity" through disciplined detachment (016-168).
- **Book 18**: The disciplined life seizes every "time and opportunity to exercise thyself" (018-163).
- **Book 25**: The imperative to be "thoroughly well exercised" in philosophical practice (025-004).
- **Book 32**: Marcus invokes the ancestral Roman models — "a strict man of the old discipline" and "the discipline of Africanus" — anchoring his Greek philosophy in Roman virtue (032-154) (032-165).
