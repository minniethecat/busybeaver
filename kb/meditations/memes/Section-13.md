# Section 13

(013-001) For he is a happy man, who in his lifetime dealeth unto himself a happy

(013-002) lot and portion. A happy lot and portion is, good inclinations of the

(013-003) soul, good desires, good actions.

(013-004) THE SIXTH BOOK

(013-005) I. The matter itself, of which the universe doth consist, is of itself

(013-006) very tractable and pliable. That rational essence that doth govern it,

(013-007) hath in itself no cause to do evil. It hath no evil in itself; neither

(013-008) can it do anything that is evil: neither can anything be hurt by it. And

(013-009) all things are done and determined according to its will and prescript.

(013-010) II. Be it all one unto thee, whether half frozen or well warm; whether

(013-011) only slumbering, or after a full sleep; whether discommended or

(013-012) commended thou do thy duty: or whether dying or doing somewhat else; for

(013-013) that also 'to die,' must among the rest be reckoned as one of the duties

(013-014) and actions of our lives.

(013-015) III. Look in, let not either the proper quality, or the true worth of

(013-016) anything pass thee, before thou hast fully apprehended it.

(013-017) IV. All substances come soon to their change, and either they shall

(013-018) be resolved by way of exhalation (if so be that all things shall be

(013-019) reunited into one substance), or as others maintain, they shall be

(013-020) scattered and dispersed. As for that Rational Essence by which all

(013-021) things are governed, as it best understandeth itself, both its own

(013-022) disposition, and what it doth, and what matter it hath to do with and

(013-023) accordingly doth all things; so we that do not, no wonder, if we wonder

(013-024) at many things, the reasons whereof we cannot comprehend.

(013-025) V. The best kind of revenge is, not to become like unto them.

(013-026) VI. Let this be thy only joy, and thy only comfort, from one sociable

(013-027) kind action without intermission to pass unto another, God being ever in

(013-028) thy mind.

(013-029) VII. The rational commanding part, as it alone can stir up and turn

(013-030) itself; so it maketh both itself to be, and everything that happeneth,

(013-031) to appear unto itself, as it will itself.

(013-032) VIII. According to the nature of the universe all things particular are

(013-033) determined, not according to any other nature, either about compassing

(013-034) and containing; or within, dispersed and contained; or without,

(013-035) depending. Either this universe is a mere confused mass, and an

(013-036) intricate context of things, which shall in time be scattered and

(013-037) dispersed again: or it is an union consisting of order, and administered

(013-038) by [[providence|Providence]]. If the first, why should I desire to continue any longer

(013-039) in this fortuit confusion and commixtion? or why should I take care for

(013-040) anything else, but that as soon as may be I may be earth again? And

(013-041) why should I trouble myself any more whilst I seek to please the Gods?

(013-042) Whatsoever I do, dispersion is my end, and will come upon me whether I

(013-043) will or no. But if the latter be, then am not I religious in vain;

(013-044) then will I be quiet and patient, and put my trust in Him, who is the

(013-045) Governor of all.

(013-046) IX. Whensoever by some present hard occurrences thou art constrained to

(013-047) be in some sort troubled and vexed, return unto thyself as soon as may

(013-048) be, and be not out of tune longer than thou must needs. For so shalt

(013-049) thou be the better able to keep thy part another time, and to maintain

(013-050) the harmony, if thou dost use thyself to this continually; once out,

(013-051) presently to have recourse unto it, and to begin again.

(013-052) X. If it were that thou hadst at one time both a stepmother, and

(013-053) a natural mother living, thou wouldst honour and respect her also;

(013-054) nevertheless to thine own natural mother would thy refuge, and recourse

(013-055) be continually. So let the court and thy philosophy be unto thee. Have

(013-056) recourse unto it often, and comfort thyself in her, by whom it is that

(013-057) those other things are made tolerable unto thee, and thou also in those

(013-058) things not intolerable unto others.

(013-059) XI. How marvellous useful it is for a man to represent unto himself

(013-060) meats, and all such things that are for the mouth, under a right

(013-061) apprehension and imagination! as for example: This is the carcass of a

(013-062) fish; this of a bird; and this of a hog. And again more generally; This

(013-063) phalernum, this excellent highly commended wine, is but the bare juice

(013-064) of an ordinary grape. This purple robe, but sheep's hairs, dyed with

(013-065) the blood of a shellfish. So for coitus, it is but the attrition of an

(013-066) ordinary base entrail, and the excretion of a little vile snivel, with

(013-067) a certain kind of convulsion: according to Hippocrates his opinion. How

(013-068) excellent useful are these lively fancies and representations of things,

(013-069) thus penetrating and passing through the objects, to make their true

(013-070) nature known and apparent! This must thou use all thy life long, and

(013-071) upon all occasions: and then especially, when matters are apprehended

(013-072) as of great worth and respect, thy art and care must be to uncover

(013-073) them, and to behold their vileness, and to take away from them all those

(013-074) serious circumstances and expressions, under which they made so grave

(013-075) a show. For outward pomp and appearance is a great juggler; and then

(013-076) especially art thou most in danger to be beguiled by it, when (to

(013-077) a man's thinking) thou most seemest to be employed about matters of

(013-078) moment.

(013-079) XII. See what Crates pronounceth concerning Xenocrates himself.

(013-080) XIII. Those things which the common sort of people do admire, are most

(013-081) of them such things as are very general, and may be comprehended under

(013-082) things merely natural, or naturally affected and qualified: as stones,

(013-083) wood, figs, vines, olives. Those that be admired by them that are more

(013-084) moderate and restrained, are comprehended under things animated: as

(013-085) flocks and herds. Those that are yet more gentle and curious, their

(013-086) admiration is commonly confined to reasonable creatures only; not in

(013-087) general as they are reasonable, but as they are capable of art, or of

(013-088) some craft and subtile invention: or perchance barely to reasonable

(013-089) creatures; as they that [[pleasure|delight]] in the possession of many slaves. But

(013-090) he that honours a reasonable soul in general, as it is reasonable and

(013-091) naturally sociable, doth little regard anything else: and above all

(013-092) things is careful to preserve his own, in the continual habit and

(013-093) exercise both of reason and sociableness: and thereby doth co-operate

(013-094) with him, of whose nature he doth also participate; God.

(013-095) XIV. Some things hasten to be, and others to be no more. And even

(013-096) whatsoever now is, some part thereof hath already perished. Perpetual

(013-097) fluxes and alterations renew the world, as the perpetual course of time

(013-098) doth make the age of the world (of itself infinite) to appear always

(013-099) fresh and new. In such a flux and course of all things, what of these

(013-100) things that hasten so fast away should any man regard, since among all

(013-101) there is not any that a man may fasten and fix upon? as if a man would

(013-102) settle his affection upon some ordinary sparrow living by him, who is no

(013-103) sooner seen, than out of sight. For we must not think otherwise of our

(013-104) lives, than as a mere exhalation of blood, or of an ordinary respiration

(013-105) of air. For what in our common apprehension is, to breathe in the air

(013-106) and to breathe it out again, which we do daily: so much is it and no

(013-107) more, at once to breathe out all thy respirative faculty into that

(013-108) common air from whence but lately (as being but from yesterday, and

(013-109) to-day), thou didst first breathe it in, and with it, life.

(013-110) XV. Not vegetative spiration, it is not surely (which plants have) that

(013-111) in this life should be so dear unto us; nor sensitive respiration, the

(013-112) proper life of beasts, both tame and wild; nor this our imaginative

(013-113) faculty; nor that we are subject to be led and carried up and down by

(013-114) the strength of our sensual appetites; or that we can gather, and live

(013-115) together; or that we can feed: for that in effect is no better, than

(013-116) that we can void the excrements of our food. What is it then that should

(013-117) be dear unto us? to hear a clattering noise? if not that, then neither

(013-118) to be applauded by the tongues of men. For the praises of many tongues,

(013-119) is in effect no better than the clattering of so many tongues. If then

(013-120) neither applause, what is there remaining that should be dear unto thee?

(013-121) This I think: that in all thy motions and actions thou be moved,

(013-122) and restrained according to thine own true natural constitution and

(013-123) Construction only. And to this even ordinary arts and professions do

(013-124) lead us. For it is that which every art doth aim at, that whatsoever it

(013-125) is, that is by art effected and prepared, may be fit for that work that

(013-126) it is prepared for. This is the end that he that dresseth the vine, and

(013-127) he that takes upon him either to tame colts, or to train up dogs,

(013-128) doth aim at. What else doth the education of children, and all learned

(013-129) professions tend unto? Certainly then it is that, which should be dear

(013-130) unto us also. If in this particular it go well with thee, care not for

(013-131) the obtaining of other things. But is it so, that thou canst not but

(013-132) respect other things also? Then canst not thou truly be free? then canst

(013-133) thou not have self-content: then wilt thou ever be subject to passions.

(013-134) For it is not possible, but that thou must be envious, and jealous, and

(013-135) suspicious of them whom thou knowest can bereave thee of such things;

(013-136) and again, a secret underminer of them, whom thou seest in present

(013-137) possession of that which is dear unto thee. To be short, he must of

(013-138) necessity be full of confusion within himself, and often accuse the

(013-139) Gods, whosoever stands in need of these things. But if thou shalt

(013-140) honour and respect thy mind only, that will make thee acceptable

(013-141) towards thyself, towards thy friends very tractable; and conformable

(013-142) and concordant with the Gods; that is, accepting with praises whatsoever

(013-143) they shall think good to appoint and allot unto thee.

(013-144) XVI. Under, above, and about, are the motions of the elements; but

(013-145) the motion of virtue, is none of those motions, but is somewhat more

(013-146) excellent and divine. Whose way (to speed and prosper in it) must be

(013-147) through a way, that is not easily comprehended.

(013-148) XVII. Who can choose but wonder at them? They will not speak well of

(013-149) them that are at the same time with them, and live with them; yet they

(013-150) themselves are very ambitious, that they that shall follow, whom they

(013-151) have never seen, nor shall ever see, should speak well of them. As if

(013-152) a man should grieve that he hath not been commended by them, that lived

(013-153) before him.

(013-154) XVIII. Do not ever conceive anything impossible to man, which by thee

(013-155) cannot, or not without much difficulty be effected; but whatsoever in

(013-156) general thou canst Conceive possible and proper unto any man, think that

(013-157) very possible unto thee also.

(013-158) XIX. Suppose that at the palestra somebody hath all to-torn thee with

(013-159) his nails, and hath broken thy head. Well, thou art wounded. Yet thou

(013-160) dost not exclaim; thou art not offended with him. Thou dost not suspect

(013-161) him for it afterwards, as one that watcheth to do thee a mischief. Yea

(013-162) even then, though thou dost thy best to save thyself from him, yet not

(013-163) from him as an enemy. It is not by way of any suspicious indignation,

(013-164) but by way of gentle and friendly declination. Keep the same mind and

(013-165) disposition in other parts of thy life also. For many things there be,

(013-166) which we must conceit and apprehend, as though we had had to do with an

(013-167) antagonist at the palestra. For as I said, it is very possible for us to

(013-168) avoid and decline, though we neither suspect, nor hate.
