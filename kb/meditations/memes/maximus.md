## Overview

Claudius Maximus was a Stoic philosopher and Roman senator who served as one of Marcus Aurelius's teachers. In the opening book of the *Meditations*, Marcus records his debt to Maximus for lessons in self-command, cheerfulness under sickness, and the conduct of public business without complaint (001-012). His example of an "invincible soul" during illness is singled out for special admiration (001-014).

## Life and Career

Claudius Maximus held senatorial rank in Rome and was among the tutors engaged to instruct the young Marcus in Stoic philosophy and practical governance. He appears in the long catalogue of Book 1 where Marcus, following the ancient tradition of gratitude, names each person from whom he learned something of value. Maximus's lessons were practical rather than theoretical — how to maintain equanimity in office, how to bear sickness, and how to treat others with openness and plain dealing.

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

From Maximus, Marcus reports that he learned "in all things to endeavour to have power of myself, and in nothing to be carried about; to be cheerful and courageous in all sudden chances and accidents, as in sicknesses" (001-012). He further notes Maximus's example of moderation, gravity, and thoroughness in all business undertaken. Marcus also recalls that the gods granted him the fortune "that I ever knew Apollonius and Rusticus, and Maximus" (001-014), placing him among the three named teachers whose influence was most formative.

## Legacy and Influence

Maximus represents the Stoic ideal of the philosopher-statesman who brings philosophy to bear on practical life. His emphasis on self-mastery and cheerful endurance of illness directly shaped Marcus's own practice. The Meditations' recurring theme of maintaining inner calm regardless of external circumstances owes much to Maximus's teaching.
