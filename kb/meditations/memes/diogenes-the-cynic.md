## Overview

Diogenes the Cynic (c. 412–323 BCE) was a Greek philosopher who practiced and preached an extreme form of ascetic simplicity, rejecting all social conventions, material possessions, and political entanglements. The Cynic school he founded profoundly influenced Stoicism, particularly through its emphasis on inner freedom, self-sufficiency, and indifference to externals. Marcus Aurelius cites Diogenes as an exemplar of the philosophical life and notes his fearless confrontation with power. (027-045) (018-121)

## Life and Practice

Diogenes was exiled from his native Sinope and settled in Athens, where he famously lived in a large ceramic jar (pithos) and owned nothing but a cloak, a staff, and a bowl — which he discarded upon seeing a boy drink from his hands. His philosophy was lived, not written: he demonstrated his principles through action, often in provocative and theatrical ways. When Alexander the Great visited him and offered to grant any wish, Diogenes reportedly replied, "Stand out of my sunlight." This encounter — the most powerful man in the world and the man who owned nothing — became the emblematic image of Cynic freedom.

## Diogenes and Stoicism

The Stoics traced their philosophical lineage through the Cynics back to Socrates. Zeno of Citium, the founder of Stoicism, studied under the Cynic Crates and absorbed the Cynic emphasis on self-discipline, simplicity, and indifference to social convention. While the Stoics moderated Cynic extremism — rejecting public indecency and anti-political withdrawal — they preserved the core Cynic insight: that virtue alone is sufficient for happiness, and that external goods are not merely unnecessary but potentially corrupting.

## In the Meditations

Marcus invokes Diogenes in a characteristic gesture: listing the great men of history — "Alexander, Caius, Pompeius; what are these to Diogenes, Heraclitus, and Socrates?" (018-121) The conquerors are measured against the philosophers and found wanting. He also notes the freedom that Diogenes exemplified: "to which end it was, that Diogenes took also the same course" — the course of inner liberty through detachment from externals. (027-045) A fragment preserves the biographical note that "Diogenes, the Cynic, [was] born about 412 B.C." — evidence that Marcus, or his sources, took care with historical details even for figures used as moral exemplars. (034-094)
