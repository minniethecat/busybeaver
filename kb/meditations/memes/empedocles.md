## Overview

Empedocles (c. 494–434 BCE) was a pre-Socratic philosopher from Agrigentum in Sicily, known for his doctrine of the four elements (earth, air, fire, water) and the two cosmic forces of Love and Strife. Marcus Aurelius quotes Empedocles's image of the soul as a perfectly round sphere twice in the *Meditations*, using it as an allegory for the soul's ideal state of undisturbed tranquility (021-011, 024-011).

## Life and Career

Empedocles was a philosopher, poet, and physician who wrote in hexameter verse. His cosmology held that all matter is composed of four eternal elements combined and separated by Love and Strife in an endless cosmic cycle. He was also a democratic political leader in Agrigentum and, according to legend, died by throwing himself into Mount Etna. His surviving fragments include the influential poems *On Nature* and *Purifications*.

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

Marcus invokes Empedocles's poetic image of the soul twice in the *Meditations*, both times to describe the ideal state of inner calm. In Book 11, he writes: "Then is the soul as Empedocles doth liken it, like unto a sphere or globe, when she is all of one form and figure: when she neither greedily covets anything, nor basely flies from anything, but is all of one form, all resplendent with light and truth" (021-011). The same image recurs in Book 12: "like unto Empedocles his allegorical sphere, 'all round and circular'" (024-011). For Marcus, the spherical soul represents perfect self-sufficiency and freedom from the perturbations of desire and aversion.

## Legacy and Influence

Empedocles influenced Plato, Aristotle, and the Stoics through his theory of elements and his conception of cosmic cycles. The Stoic theory of *ekpyrosis* (periodic cosmic conflagration) has roots in Empedoclean cosmology. Marcus's use of the Empedoclean sphere image shows how pre-Socratic poetry could be repurposed for Stoic spiritual exercise — the perfectly round, self-contained sphere becoming a metaphor for the sage whose happiness depends on nothing external.
