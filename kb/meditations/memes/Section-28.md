# Section 28

(028-001) XVII. Four several dispositions or inclinations there be of the mind and

(028-002) [[knowledge|understanding]], which to be aware of, thou must carefully observe: and

(028-003) whensoever thou doest discover them, thou must rectify them, saying to

(028-004) thyself concerning every one of them, This imagination is not necessary;

(028-005) this is uncharitable: this thou shalt speak as another man's slave, or

(028-006) instrument; than which nothing can be more senseless and absurd: for

(028-007) the fourth, thou shalt sharply check and upbraid thyself; for that

(028-008) thou doest suffer that more divine part in thee, to become subject and

(028-009) obnoxious to that more ignoble part of thy body, and the gross lusts

(028-010) and concupiscences thereof.

(028-011) XVIII. What portion soever, either of air or fire there be in thee,

(028-012) although by nature it tend upwards, submitting nevertheless to the

(028-013) ordinance of the universe, it abides here below in this mixed body. So

(028-014) whatsoever is in thee, either earthy, or humid, although by nature it

(028-015) tend downwards, yet is it against its nature both raised upwards, and

(028-016) standing, or consistent. So obedient are even the elements themselves to

(028-017) the universe, abiding patiently wheresoever (though against their

(028-018) nature) they are placed, until the sound as it were of their retreat,

(028-019) and separation. Is it not a grievous thing then, that thy reasonable

(028-020) part only should be disobedient, and should not endure to keep its

(028-021) place: yea though it be nothing enjoined that is contrary unto it, but

(028-022) that only which is according to its nature? For we cannot say of it when

(028-023) it is disobedient, as we say of the fire, or air, that it tends upwards

(028-024) towards its proper element, for then goes it the quite contrary way. For

(028-025) the motion of the mind to any injustice, or incontinency, or to sorrow,

(028-026) or to fear, is nothing else but a separation from nature. Also when the

(028-027) mind is grieved for anything that is happened by the divine providence,

(028-028) then doth it likewise forsake its own place. For it was ordained unto

(028-029) holiness and godliness, which specially consist in an humble submission

(028-030) to God and His providence in all things; as well as unto justice: these

(028-031) also being part of those duties, which as naturally sociable, we are

(028-032) bound unto; and without which we cannot happily converse one with

(028-033) another: yea and the very ground and fountain indeed of all just

(028-034) actions.

(028-035) XIX. He that hath not one and the self-same general end always as long

(028-036) as he liveth, cannot possibly be one and the self-same man always. But

(028-037) this will not suffice except thou add also what ought to be this general

(028-038) end. For as the general conceit and apprehension of all those things

(028-039) which upon no certain ground are by the greater part of men deemed good,

(028-040) cannot be uniform and agreeable, but that only which is limited and

(028-041) restrained by some certain proprieties and conditions, as of community:

(028-042) that nothing be conceived good, which is not commonly and publicly

(028-043) good: so must the end also that we propose unto ourselves, be common

(028-044) and sociable. For he that doth direct all his own private motions and

(028-045) purposes to that end, all his actions will be agreeable and uniform; and

(028-046) by that means will be still the same man.

(028-047) XX. Remember the fable of the country mouse and the city mouse, and the

(028-048) great fright and terror that this was put into.

(028-049) XXI. Socrates was wont to call the common conceits and opinions of men,

(028-050) the common bugbears of the world: the proper terror of silly children.

(028-051) XXII. The Lacedæmonians at their public spectacles were wont to appoint

(028-052) seats and forms for their strangers in the shadow, they themselves were

(028-053) content to sit anywhere.

(028-054) XXIII. What Socrates answered unto Perdiccas, why he did not come unto

(028-055) him, Lest of all deaths I should die the worst kind of death, said he:

(028-056) that is, not able to requite the good that hath been done unto me.

(028-057) XXIV. In the ancient mystical letters of the Ephesians, there was an

(028-058) item, that a man should always have in his mind some one or other of the

(028-059) ancient worthies.

(028-060) XXV. The Pythagoreans were wont betimes in the morning the first thing

(028-061) they did, to look up unto the heavens, to put themselves in mind of them

(028-062) who constantly and invariably did perform their task: as also to put

(028-063) themselves in mind of orderliness, or good order, and of purity, and of

(028-064) naked simplicity. For no star or planet hath any cover before it.

(028-065) XXVI. How Socrates looked, when he was fain to gird himself with a

(028-066) skin, Xanthippe his wife having taken away his clothes, and carried them

(028-067) abroad with her, and what he said to his fellows and friends, who were

(028-068) ashamed; and out of respect to him, did retire themselves when they saw

(028-069) him thus decked.

(028-070) XXVII. In matter of writing or reading thou must needs be taught before

(028-071) thou can do either: much more in matter of life. 'For thou art born a

(028-072) mere slave, to thy senses and brutish affections;' destitute without

(028-073) teaching of all true knowledge and sound reason.

(028-074) XXVIII. 'My heart smiled within me.' 'They will accuse even virtue

(028-075) herself; with heinous and opprobrious words.'

(028-076) XXIX. As they that long after figs in winter when they cannot be had; so

(028-077) are they that long after children, before they be granted them.

(028-078) XXX. 'As often as a father kisseth his child, he should say secretly

(028-079) with himself' (said [[epictetus|Epictetus]],) 'tomorrow perchance shall he die.' But

(028-080) these words be ominous. No words ominous (said he) that signify anything

(028-081) that is natural: in very truth and deed not more ominous than this, 'to

(028-082) cut down grapes when they are ripe.' Green grapes, ripe grapes, dried

(028-083) grapes, or raisins: so many changes and mutations of one thing, not into

(028-084) that which was not absolutely, but rather so many several changes and

(028-085) mutations, not into that which hath no being at all, but into that which

(028-086) is not yet in being.

(028-087) XXXI. 'Of the free will there is no thief or robber:' out of Epictetus;

(028-088) Whose is this also: that we should find a certain art and method of

(028-089) assenting; and that we should always observe with great care and heed

(028-090) the inclinations of our minds, that they may always be with their due

(028-091) restraint and reservation, always charitable, and according to the

(028-092) true worth of every present object. And as for earnest longing, that we

(028-093) should altogether avoid it: and to use averseness in those things only,

(028-094) that wholly depend of our own wills. It is not about ordinary petty

(028-095) matters, believe it, that all our strife and contention is, but whether,

(028-096) with the vulgar, we should be mad, or by the help of philosophy wise and

(028-097) sober, said he. XXXII. Socrates said, 'What will you have? the souls of

(028-098) reasonable, or unreasonable creatures? Of reasonable. But what? Of those

(028-099) whose reason is sound and perfect? or of those whose reason is vitiated

(028-100) and corrupted? Of those whose reason is sound and perfect. Why then

(028-101) labour ye not for such? Because we have them already. What then do ye so

(028-102) strive and contend between you?'

(028-103) THE TWELFTH BOOK

(028-104) I. Whatsoever thou doest hereafter aspire unto, thou mayest even now

(028-105) enjoy and possess, if thou doest not envy thyself thine own happiness.

(028-106) And that will be, if thou shalt forget all that is past, and for the

(028-107) future, refer thyself wholly to the Divine [[providence|Providence]], and shalt bend

(028-108) and apply all thy present thoughts and intentions to holiness and

(028-109) [[justice|righteousness]]. To holiness, in accepting willingly whatsoever is sent

(028-110) by the Divine Providence, as being that which the nature of the universe

(028-111) hath appointed unto thee, which also hath appointed thee for that,

(028-112) whatsoever it be. To righteousness, in speaking the truth freely, and

(028-113) without ambiguity; and in doing all things justly and discreetly. Now in

(028-114) this good course, let not other men's either wickedness, or opinion, or

(028-115) voice hinder thee: no, nor the sense of this thy pampered mass of flesh:

(028-116) for let that which suffers, look to itself. If therefore whensoever the

(028-117) time of thy departing shall come, thou shalt readily leave all things,

(028-118) and shalt respect thy mind only, and that divine part of thine, and this

(028-119) shall be thine only fear, not that some time or other thou shalt cease

(028-120) to live, but thou shalt never begin to live according to nature: then

(028-121) shalt thou be a man indeed, worthy of that world, from which thou hadst

(028-122) thy beginning; then shalt thou cease to be a stranger in thy country,

(028-123) and to wonder at those things that happen daily, as things strange and

(028-124) unexpected, and anxiously to depend of divers things that are not in thy

(028-125) power.

(028-126) II. God beholds our minds and understandings, bare and naked from these

(028-127) material vessels, and outsides, and all earthly dross. For with His

(028-128) simple and pure understanding, He pierceth into our inmost and purest

(028-129) parts, which from His, as it were by a water pipe and channel, first

(028-130) flowed and issued. This if thou also shalt use to do, thou shalt

(028-131) rid thyself of that manifold luggage, wherewith thou art round about

(028-132) encumbered. For he that does regard neither his body, nor his clothing,

(028-133) nor his dwelling, nor any such external furniture, must needs gain unto

(028-134) himself great rest and ease. Three things there be in all, which thou

(028-135) doest consist of; thy body, thy life, and thy mind. Of these the two

(028-136) former, are so far forth thine, as that thou art bound to take care for

(028-137) them. But the third alone is that which is properly thine. If then thou

(028-138) shalt separate from thyself, that is from thy mind, whatsoever other men

(028-139) either do or say, or whatsoever thou thyself hast heretofore either

(028-140) done or said; and all troublesome thoughts concerning the future, and

(028-141) whatsoever, (as either belonging to thy body or life:) is without the

(028-142) jurisdiction of thine own will, and whatsoever in the ordinary course

(028-143) of human chances and accidents doth happen unto thee; so that thy

(028-144) mind (keeping herself loose and free from all outward coincidental

(028-145) entanglements; always in a readiness to depart:) shall live by herself,

(028-146) and to herself, doing that which is just, accepting whatsoever doth

(028-147) happen, and speaking the truth always; if, I say, thou shalt separate

(028-148) from thy mind, whatsoever by sympathy might adhere unto it, and all time

(028-149) both past and future, and shalt make thyself in all points and respects,

(028-150) like unto Empedocles his allegorical sphere, 'all round and circular,'

(028-151) &c., and shalt think of no longer life than that which is now present:

(028-152) then shalt thou be truly able to pass the remainder of thy days without

(028-153) troubles and distractions; nobly and generously disposed, and in good

(028-154) favour and correspondency, with that spirit which is within thee.

(028-155) III. I have often wondered how it should come to pass, that every man

(028-156) loving himself best, should more regard other men's opinions concerning

(028-157) himself than his own. For if any God or grave master standing by,

(028-158) should command any of us to think nothing by himself but what he should

(028-159) presently speak out; no man were able to endure it, though but for one

(028-160) day. Thus do we fear more what our neighbours will think of us, than

(028-161) what we ourselves.

(028-162) IV. how come it to pass that the Gods having ordered all other things

(028-163) so well and so lovingly, should be overseen in this one only thing, that

(028-164) whereas then hath been some very good men that have made many covenants

(028-165) as it were with God and by many holy actions and outward services

(028-166) contracted a kind of familiarity with Him; that these men when once they

(028-167) are dead, should never be restored to life, but be extinct for ever. But

(028-168) this thou mayest be sure of, that this (if it be so indeed) would

(028-169) never have been so ordered by the Gods, had it been fit otherwise. For

(028-170) certainly it was possible, had it been more just so and had it been

(028-171) according to nature, the nature of the universe would easily have borne

(028-172) it. But now because it is not so, (if so be that it be not so indeed) be

(028-173) therefore confident that it was not fit it should be so for thou seest

(028-174) thyself, that now seeking after this matter, how freely thou doest argue

(028-175) and contest with God. But were not the Gods both just and good in the

(028-176) highest degree, thou durst not thus reason with them. Now if just and

(028-177) good, it could not be that in the creation of the world, they should

(028-178) either unjustly or unreasonably oversee anything.
