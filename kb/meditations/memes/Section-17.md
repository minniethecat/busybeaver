# Section 17

(017-001) XXIII. Out of Plato. 'He then whose mind is endowed with true

(017-002) magnanimity, who hath accustomed himself to the contemplation both of

(017-003) all times, and of all things in general; can this mortal life (thinkest

(017-004) thou) seem any great matter unto him? It is not possible, answered he.

(017-005) Then neither will such a one account death a grievous thing? By no

(017-006) means.'

(017-007) XXIV. Out of Antisthenes. 'It is a princely thing to do well, and to be

(017-008) ill-spoken of. It is a shameful thing that the face should be subject

(017-009) unto the mind, to be put into what shape it will, and to be dressed by

(017-010) it as it will; and that the mind should not bestow so much care upon

(017-011) herself, as to fashion herself, and to dress herself as best becometh

(017-012) her.'

(017-013) XXV. Out of several poets and comics. 'It will but little avail thee,

(017-014) to turn thine anger and indignation upon the things themselves that have

(017-015) fallen across unto thee. For as for them, they are not sensible of it,

(017-016) &c. Thou shalt but make thyself a laughing-stock; both unto the Gods and

(017-017) men, &c. Our life is reaped like a ripe ear of corn; one is yet

(017-018) standing and another is down, &c. But if so be that I and my children be

(017-019) neglected by the gods, there is some reason even for that, &c. As long

(017-020) as right and equity is of my side, &c. Not to lament with them, not to

(017-021) tremble, &c.'

(017-022) XXVI. Out of Plato. 'My answer, full of justice and equity, should be

(017-023) this: Thy speech is not right, O man! if thou supposest that he that is

(017-024) of any worth at all, should apprehend either life or death, as a matter

(017-025) of great hazard and danger; and should not make this rather his only

(017-026) care, to examine his own actions, whether just or unjust: whether

(017-027) actions of a good, or of a wicked man, &c. For thus in very truth stands

(017-028) the case, O ye men of [[athens|Athens]]. What place or station soever a man either

(017-029) hath chosen to himself, judging it best for himself; or is by lawful

(017-030) authority put and settled in, therein do I think (all appearance of

(017-031) danger notwithstanding) that he should continue, as one who feareth

(017-032) neither death, nor anything else, so much as he feareth to commit

(017-033) anything that is vicious and shameful, &c. But, O noble sir, consider

(017-034) I pray, whether true generosity and true happiness, do not consist in

(017-035) somewhat else rather, than in the preservation either of our, or other

(017-036) men's lives. For it is not the part of a man that is a man indeed, to

(017-037) desire to live long or to make much of his life whilst he liveth: but

(017-038) rather (he that is such) will in these things wholly refer himself unto

(017-039) the Gods, and believing that which every woman can tell him, that no man

(017-040) can escape death; the only thing that he takes thought and care for is

(017-041) this, that what time he liveth, he may live as well and as virtuously

(017-042) as he can possibly, &c. To look about, and with the eyes to follow the

(017-043) course of the stars and planets as though thou wouldst run with them;

(017-044) and to mind perpetually the several changes of the elements one into

(017-045) another. For such fancies and imaginations, help much to purge away

(017-046) the dross and filth of this our earthly life,' &c. That also is a fine

(017-047) passage of Plato's, where he speaketh of worldly things in these words:

(017-048) 'Thou must also as from some higher place look down, as it were, upon

(017-049) the things of this world, as flocks, armies, husbandmen's labours,

(017-050) marriages, divorces, generations, deaths: the tumults of courts and

(017-051) places of judicatures; desert places; the several nations of barbarians,

(017-052) public festivals, mournings, fairs, markets.' How all things upon earth

(017-053) are pell-mell; and how miraculously things contrary one to another,

(017-054) concur to the beauty and perfection of this universe.

(017-055) XXVII. To look back upon things of former ages, as upon the manifold

(017-056) changes and conversions of several monarchies and commonwealths. We

(017-057) may also foresee things future, for they shall all be of the same kind;

(017-058) neither is it possible that they should leave the tune, or break the

(017-059) concert that is now begun, as it were, by these things that are now done

(017-060) and brought to pass in the world. It comes all to one therefore, whether

(017-061) a man be a spectator of the things of this life but forty years, or

(017-062) whether he see them ten thousand years together: for what shall he

(017-063) see more? 'And as for those parts that came from the earth, they shall

(017-064) return unto the earth again; and those that came from heaven, they

(017-065) also shall return unto those heavenly places.' Whether it be a mere

(017-066) dissolution and unbinding of the manifold intricacies and entanglements

(017-067) of the confused atoms; or some such dispersion of the simple and

(017-068) incorruptible elements... 'With meats and drinks and divers charms, they

(017-069) seek to divert the channel, that they might not die. Yet must we needs

(017-070) endure that blast of wind that cometh from above, though we toil and

(017-071) labour never so much.'

(017-072) XXVIII. He hath a stronger body, and is a better wrestler than I. What

(017-073) then? Is he more bountiful? is he more modest? Doth he bear all adverse

(017-074) chances with more equanimity: or with his neighbour's offences with more

(017-075) meekness and gentleness than I?

(017-076) XXIX. Where the matter may be effected agreeably to that reason, which

(017-077) both unto the Gods and men is common, there can be no just cause of

(017-078) grief or sorrow. For where the fruit and benefit of an action well begun

(017-079) and prosecuted according to the proper constitution of man may be reaped

(017-080) and obtained, or is sure and certain, it is against reason that any

(017-081) damage should there be suspected. In all places, and at all times, it is

(017-082) in thy power religiously to embrace whatsoever by God's appointment is

(017-083) happened unto thee, and justly to converse with those men, whom thou

(017-084) hast to do with, and accurately to examine every fancy that presents

(017-085) itself, that nothing may slip and steal in, before thou hast rightly

(017-086) apprehended the true nature of it.

(017-087) XXX. Look not about upon other men's minds and understandings; but look

(017-088) right on forwards whither nature, both that of the universe, in those

(017-089) things that happen unto thee; and thine in particular, in those things

(017-090) that are done by thee: doth lead, and direct thee. Now every one is

(017-091) bound to do that, which is consequent and agreeable to that end which

(017-092) by his true natural constitution he was ordained unto. As for all other

(017-093) things, they are ordained for the use of reasonable creatures: as in all

(017-094) things we see that that which is worse and inferior, is made for

(017-095) that which is better. Reasonable creatures, they are ordained one for

(017-096) another. That therefore which is chief in every man's constitution, is,

(017-097) that he intend the common good. The second is, that he yield not to any

(017-098) lusts and motions of the flesh. For it is the part and privilege of the

(017-099) reasonable and intellective faculty, that she can so bound herself,

(017-100) as that neither the sensitive, nor the appetitive faculties, may not

(017-101) anyways prevail upon her. For both these are brutish. And therefore over

(017-102) both she challengeth mastery, and cannot anyways endure, if in her right

(017-103) temper, to be subject unto either. And this indeed most justly. For

(017-104) by nature she was ordained to command all in the body. The third

(017-105) thing proper to man by his constitution, is, to avoid all rashness and

(017-106) precipitancy; and not to be subject to error. To these things then, let

(017-107) the mind apply herself and go straight on, without any distraction about

(017-108) other things, and she hath her end, and by consequent her happiness.

(017-109) XXXI. As one who had lived, and were now to die by right, whatsoever is

(017-110) yet remaining, bestow that wholly as a gracious overplus upon a virtuous

(017-111) life. Love and affect that only, whatsoever it be that happeneth, and is

(017-112) by the fates appointed unto thee. For what can be more reasonable? And

(017-113) as anything doth happen unto thee by way of cross, or calamity, call

(017-114) to mind presently and set before thine eyes, the examples of some other

(017-115) men, to whom the self-same thing did once happen likewise. Well, what

(017-116) did they? They grieved; they wondered; they complained. And where are

(017-117) they now? All dead and gone. Wilt thou also be like one of them?

(017-118) Or rather leaving to men of the world (whose life both in regard of

(017-119) themselves, and them that they converse with, is nothing but mere

(017-120) mutability; or men of as fickle minds, as fickle bodies; ever changing

(017-121) and soon changed themselves) let it be thine only care and study, how to

(017-122) make a right use of all such accidents. For there is good use to be made

(017-123) of them, and they will prove fit matter for thee to work upon, if it

(017-124) shall be both thy care and thy desire, that whatsoever thou doest, thou

(017-125) thyself mayst like and approve thyself for it. And both these, see,

(017-126) that thou remember well, according as the diversity of the matter of

(017-127) the action that thou art about shall require. Look within; within is the

(017-128) fountain of all good. Such a fountain, where springing waters can never

(017-129) fail, so thou dig still deeper and deeper.

(017-130) XXXII. Thou must use thyself also to keep thy body fixed and steady;

(017-131) free from all loose fluctuant either motion, or posture. And as upon thy

(017-132) face and looks, thy mind hath easily power over them to keep them to

(017-133) that which is grave and decent; so let it challenge the same power over

(017-134) the whole body also. But so observe all things in this kind, as that it

(017-135) be without any manner of affectation.

(017-136) XXXIII. The art of true living in this world is more like a wrestler's,

(017-137) than a dancer's practice. For in this they both agree, to teach a man

(017-138) whatsoever falls upon him, that he may be ready for it, and that nothing

(017-139) may cast him down.

(017-140) XXXIV. Thou must continually ponder and consider with thyself, what

(017-141) manner of men they be, and for their minds and understandings what is

(017-142) their present estate, whose good word and testimony thou dost desire.

(017-143) For then neither wilt thou see cause to complain of them that offend

(017-144) against their wills; or find any want of their applause, if once

(017-145) thou dost but penetrate into the true force and ground both of their

(017-146) opinions, and of their desires. 'No soul (saith he) is willingly bereft

(017-147) of the truth,' and by consequent, neither of justice, or temperance, or

(017-148) kindness, and mildness; nor of anything that is of the same kind. It is

(017-149) most needful that thou shouldst always remember this. For so shalt thou

(017-150) be far more gentle and moderate towards all men.

(017-151) XXXV. What pain soever thou art in, let this presently come to thy mind,

(017-152) that it is not a thing whereof thou needest to be ashamed, neither is it

(017-153) a thing whereby thy [[knowledge|understanding]], that hath the government of all,

(017-154) can be made worse. For neither in regard of the substance of it, nor

(017-155) in regard of the end of it (which is, to intend the common good) can

(017-156) it alter and corrupt it. This also of Epicurus mayst thou in most pains

(017-157) find some help of, that it is 'neither intolerable, nor eternal;' so

(017-158) thou keep thyself to the true bounds and limits of reason and give not

(017-159) way to opinion. This also thou must consider, that many things there be,

(017-160) which oftentimes unsensibly trouble and vex thee, as not armed against

(017-161) them with patience, because they go not ordinarily under the name of

(017-162) pains, which in very deed are of the same nature as pain; as to slumber

(017-163) unquietly, to suffer heat, to want appetite: when therefore any of these

(017-164) things make thee discontented, check thyself with these words: Now hath

(017-165) pain given thee the foil; thy courage hath failed thee.
