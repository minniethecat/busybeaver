# Section 10

(010-001) XXXVII. Let that of Heraclitus never be out of thy mind, that the death

(010-002) of earth, is water, and the death of water, is air; and the death of

(010-003) air, is fire; and so on the contrary. Remember him also who was

(010-004) ignorant whither the way did lead, and how that reason being the thing

(010-005) by which all things in the world are administered, and which men are

(010-006) continually and most inwardly conversant with: yet is the thing, which

(010-007) ordinarily they are most in opposition with, and how those things which

(010-008) daily happen among them, cease not daily to be strange unto them, and

(010-009) that we should not either speak, or do anything as men in their sleep,

(010-010) by opinion and bare imagination: for then we think we speak and do, and

(010-011) that we must not be as children, who follow their father's example; for

(010-012) best reason alleging their bare καθότι παρειλήφαμεν; or, as by

(010-013) successive tradition from our forefathers we have received it.

(010-014) XXXVIII. Even as if any of the gods should tell thee, Thou shalt

(010-015) certainly die to-morrow, or next day, thou wouldst not, except thou wert

(010-016) extremely base and pusillanimous, take it for a great benefit, rather

(010-017) to die the next day after, than to-morrow; (for alas, what is the

(010-018) difference!) so, for the same reason, think it no great matter to die

(010-019) rather many years after, than the very next day.

(010-020) XXXIX. Let it be thy perpetual meditation, how many physicians who

(010-021) once looked so grim, and so theatrically shrunk their brows upon their

(010-022) patients, are dead and gone themselves. How many astrologers, after that

(010-023) in great ostentation they had foretold the death of some others, how

(010-024) many philosophers after so many elaborate tracts and volumes concerning

(010-025) either [[death|mortality]] or immortality; how many brave captains and commanders,

(010-026) after the death and slaughter of so many; how many kings and tyrants,

(010-027) after they had with such horror and insolency abused their power upon

(010-028) men's lives, as though themselves had been immortal; how many, that

(010-029) I may so speak, whole cities both men and towns: Helice, Pompeii,

(010-030) Herculaneum, and others innumerable are dead and gone. Run them over

(010-031) also, whom thou thyself, one after another, hast known in thy time

(010-032) to drop away. Such and such a one took care of such and such a one's

(010-033) burial, and soon after was buried himself. So one, so another: and all

(010-034) things in a short time. For herein lieth all indeed, ever to look upon

(010-035) all worldly things, as things for their continuance, that are but for a

(010-036) day: and for their worth, most vile, and contemptible, as for example,

(010-037) What is man? That which but the other day when he was conceived was vile

(010-038) snivel; and within few days shall be either an embalmed carcass, or mere

(010-039) ashes. Thus must thou according to truth and nature, throughly consider

(010-040) how man's life is but for a very moment of time, and so depart meek and

(010-041) contented: even as if a ripe olive falling should praise the ground that

(010-042) bare her, and give thanks to the tree that begat her.

(010-043) XL. Thou must be like a promontory of the sea, against which though

(010-044) the waves beat continually, yet it both itself stands, and about it are

(010-045) those swelling waves stilled and quieted.

(010-046) XLI. Oh, wretched I, to whom this mischance is happened! nay, happy I,

(010-047) to whom this thing being happened, I can continue without grief; neither

(010-048) wounded by that which is present, nor in fear of that which is to come.

(010-049) For as for this, it might have happened unto any man, but any man having

(010-050) such a thing befallen him, could not have continued without grief. Why

(010-051) then should that rather be an unhappiness, than this a happiness? But

(010-052) however, canst thou, O man! term that unhappiness, which is no mischance

(010-053) to the nature of man I Canst thou think that a mischance to the nature

(010-054) of man, which is not contrary to the end and will of his nature? What

(010-055) then hast thou learned is the will of man's nature? Doth that then which

(010-056) hath happened unto thee, hinder thee from being just? or magnanimous? or

(010-057) temperate? or wise? or circumspect? or true? or modest? or free? or from

(010-058) anything else of all those things in the present enjoying and possession

(010-059) whereof the nature of man, (as then enjoying all that is proper unto

(010-060) her,) is fully satisfied? Now to conclude; upon all occasion of sorrow

(010-061) remember henceforth to make use of this dogma, that whatsoever it is

(010-062) that hath happened unto thee, is in very deed no such thing of itself,

(010-063) as a misfortune; but that to bear it generously, is certainly great

(010-064) happiness.

(010-065) XLII. It is but an ordinary coarse one, yet it is a good effectual

(010-066) remedy against the fear of death, for a man to consider in his mind the

(010-067) examples of such, who greedily and covetously (as it were) did for a

(010-068) long time enjoy their lives. What have they got more, than they whose

(010-069) deaths have been untimely? Are not they themselves dead at the last?

(010-070) as Cadiciant's, Fabius, Julianus Lepidus, or any other who in their

(010-071) lifetime having buried many, were at the last buried themselves. The

(010-072) whole space of any man's life, is but little; and as little as it is,

(010-073) with what troubles, with what manner of dispositions, and in the society

(010-074) of how wretched a body must it be passed! Let it be therefore unto thee

(010-075) altogether as a matter of indifferency. For if thou shalt look backward;

(010-076) behold, what an infinite chaos of time doth present itself unto thee;

(010-077) and as infinite a chaos, if thou shalt look forward. In that which is

(010-078) so infinite, what difference can there be between that which liveth but

(010-079) three days, and that which liveth three ages?

(010-080) XLIII. Let thy course ever be the most compendious way. The most

(010-081) compendious, is that which is according to nature: that is, in all both

(010-082) words and deeds, ever to follow that which is most sound and perfect.

(010-083) For such a resolution will free a man from all trouble, strife,

(010-084) dissembling, and ostentation.

(010-085) THE FIFTH BOOK

(010-086) I. In the morning when thou findest thyself unwilling to rise, consider

(010-087) with thyself presently, it is to go about a man's work that I am stirred

(010-088) up. Am I then yet unwilling to go about that, for which I myself was

(010-089) born and brought forth into this world? Or was I made for this, to

(010-090) lay me down, and make much of myself in a warm bed? 'O but this is

(010-091) pleasing.' And was it then for this that thou wert born, that thou

(010-092) mightest enjoy pleasure? Was it not in very truth for this, that thou

(010-093) mightest always be busy and in action? Seest thou not how all things

(010-094) in the world besides, how every tree md plant, how sparrows and ants,

(010-095) spiders and bees: how all in their kind are intent as it were orderly to

(010-096) perform whatsoever (towards the preservation of this orderly universe)

(010-097) naturally doth become and belong unto thin? And wilt not thou do that,

(010-098) which belongs unto a man to do? Wilt not thou run to do that, which thy

(010-099) nature doth require? 'But thou must have some rest.' Yes, thou must.

(010-100) [[nature|Nature]] hath of that also, as well as of eating and drinking, allowed

(010-101) thee a certain stint. But thou guest beyond thy stint, and beyond that

(010-102) which would suffice, and in matter of action, there thou comest short of

(010-103) that which thou mayest. It must needs be therefore, that thou dost not

(010-104) love thyself, for if thou didst, thou wouldst also love thy nature, and

(010-105) that which thy nature doth propose unto herself as her end. Others,

(010-106) as many as take pleasure in their trade and profession, can even pine

(010-107) themselves at their works, and neglect their bodies and their food for

(010-108) it; and doest thou less honour thy nature, than an ordinary mechanic

(010-109) his trade; or a good dancer his art? than a covetous man his silver, and

(010-110) vainglorious man applause? These to whatsoever they take an affection,

(010-111) can be content to want their meat and sleep, to further that every one

(010-112) which he affects: and shall actions tending to the common good of

(010-113) human society, seem more vile unto thee, or worthy of less respect and

(010-114) intention?

(010-115) II. How easy a thing is it for a man to put off from him all turbulent

(010-116) adventitious imaginations, and presently to be in perfect rest and

(010-117) tranquillity!

(010-118) III. Think thyself fit and worthy to speak, or to do anything that is

(010-119) according to nature, and let not the reproach, or report of some that

(010-120) may ensue upon it, ever deter thee. If it be right and honest to be

(010-121) spoken or done, undervalue not thyself so much, as to be discouraged

(010-122) from it. As for them, they have their own rational over-ruling part, and

(010-123) their own proper inclination: which thou must not stand and look

(010-124) about to take notice of, but go on straight, whither both thine own

(010-125) particular, and the common nature do lead thee; and the way of both

(010-126) these, is but one.

(010-127) IV. I continue my course by actions according to nature, until I

(010-128) fall and cease, breathing out my last breath into that air, by which

(010-129) continually breathed in I did live; and falling upon that earth, out of

(010-130) whose gifts and fruits my father gathered his seed, my mother her

(010-131) blood, and my nurse her milk, out of which for so many years I have

(010-132) been provided, both of meat and drink. And lastly, which beareth me that

(010-133) tread upon it, and beareth with me that so many ways do abuse it, or

(010-134) so freely make use of it, so many ways to so many ends.

(010-135) V. No man can admire thee for thy sharp acute language, such is thy

(010-136) natural disability that way. Be it so: yet there be many other good

(010-137) things, for the want of which thou canst not plead the want or natural

(010-138) ability. Let them be seen in thee, which depend wholly from thee;

(010-139) sincerity, gravity, laboriousness, contempt of pleasures; be not

(010-140) querulous, be Content with little, be kind, be free; avoid all

(010-141) superfluity, all vain prattling; be magnanimous. Doest not thou

(010-142) perceive, how many things there be, which notwithstanding any pretence

(010-143) of natural indisposition and unfitness, thou mightest have performed and

(010-144) exhibited, and yet still thou doest voluntarily continue drooping

(010-145) downwards? Or wilt thou say that it is through defect of thy natural

(010-146) constitution, that thou art constrained to murmur, to be base and

(010-147) wretched to flatter; now to accuse, and now to please, and pacify thy

(010-148) body: to be vainglorious, to be so giddy-headed., and unsettled in thy

(010-149) thoughts? nay (witnesses be the Gods) of all these thou mightest have

(010-150) been rid long ago: only, this thou must have been contented with, to

(010-151) have borne the blame of one that is somewhat slow and dull, wherein thou

(010-152) must so exercise thyself, as one who neither doth much take to heart

(010-153) this his natural defect, nor yet pleaseth himself in it.

(010-154) VI. Such there be, who when they have done a good turn to any, are ready

(010-155) to set them on the score for it, and to require retaliation. Others

(010-156) there be, who though they stand not upon retaliation, to require any,

(010-157) yet they think with themselves nevertheless, that such a one is their

(010-158) debtor, and they know as their word is what they have done. Others again

(010-159) there be, who when they have done any such thing, do not so much as

(010-160) know what they have done; but are like unto the vine, which beareth her

(010-161) grapes, and when once she hath borne her own proper fruit, is contented

(010-162) and seeks for no further recompense. As a horse after a race, and a

(010-163) hunting dog when he hath hunted, and a bee when she hath made her honey,

(010-164) look not for applause and commendation; so neither doth that man that

(010-165) rightly doth understand his own nature when he hath done a good turn:

(010-166) but from one doth proceed to do another, even as the vine after she hath

(010-167) once borne fruit in her own proper season, is ready for another time.

(010-168) Thou therefore must be one of them, who what they do, barely do it

(010-169) without any further thought, and are in a manner insensible of what they

(010-170) do. 'Nay but,' will some reply perchance, 'this very thing a rational

(010-171) man is bound unto, to understand what it is, that he doeth.' For it

(010-172) is the property, say they, of one that is naturally sociable, to be

(010-173) sensible, that he doth operate sociably: nay, and to desire, that the

(010-174) party him self that is sociably dealt with, should be sensible of it

(010-175) too. I answer, That which thou sayest is true indeed, but the true

(010-176) meaning of that which is said, thou dost not understand. And therefore

(010-177) art thou one of those first, whom I mentioned. For they also are led by

(010-178) a probable appearance of reason. But if thou dost desire to understand

(010-179) truly what it is that is said, fear not that thou shalt therefore give

(010-180) over any sociable action.
