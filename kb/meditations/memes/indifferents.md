## Overview

Indifferents (Greek ἀδιάφορα / adiaphora, "things not differentiated") are, in Stoic philosophy, all things that are neither morally good nor morally evil. Health, wealth, reputation, pleasure, pain, poverty, sickness, and even life and death — all fall into this middle category. The Stoics held that only virtue is good and only vice is evil; everything else is indifferent with respect to the moral life. (002-047) (002-054)

## In Stoic Ethics

The doctrine of indifferents is foundational to Stoic ethics, which draws a sharp line between what depends on us (our judgments, choices, and character) and what does not (external circumstances). The Stoic founder [[Zeno of Citium]] and later systematizer [[Chrysippus]] taught that externals, though indifferent with respect to goodness, could still be "preferred" (προηγμένα) or "dispreferred" (ἀποπροηγμένα) — health is naturally preferred over sickness, but neither affects one's moral worth. Marcus Aurelius follows this tradition when he calls health, wealth, and the like "middle things, or things indifferent." (015-033)

## Core Distinctions

The Stoics subdivided indifferents into three classes: those according to nature (preferred, like health), those contrary to nature (dispreferred, like illness), and those strictly neutral (like whether one has an odd or even number of hairs). The key ethical point is that indifferents cannot touch what is truly one's own — the ruling faculty (ἡγεμονικόν). Marcus echoes this: whatever is indifferent "cannot affect" the inner self. (014-090) (002-054) (002-058)

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius returns to the theme of indifferents repeatedly as a practical discipline. He reminds himself that even a fellow human being "is unto me but as a thing indifferent" — not in the sense of callousness, but as an exercise in not being disturbed by others' actions, which lie outside one's control. (012-018) In Book Seven, he writes that when one's ruling faculty is strong, one becomes "altogether indifferent" to bodily conditions, neither craving pleasure nor fearing pain. (007-055) (007-111) He extends the principle to cosmic scale: even the dissolution of the elements themselves is "indifferent" to Nature, whose operations proceed regardless. (014-092) (014-096) The practice is to regard all externals — even three successive misfortunes — as indifferent, since they cannot harm the moral purpose. (014-016) (008-035)
