## Overview

Epicurus was a Greek philosopher of the Hellenistic period who founded one of the two great schools — alongside Stoicism — that dominated Roman intellectual life. Marcus Aurelius engages with Epicurean ideas throughout the *Meditations*, sometimes borrowing useful insights, more often drawing sharp contrasts with Stoic teaching (017-156).

## Life and Career

Epicurus was born on the island of Samos and established his school in Athens around 307 BCE. He taught in the "Garden," a community that admitted women and slaves, and lived what contemporaries described as a simple and temperate life — contrary to later caricatures of Epicureanism as indulgence. The Epicurean system combined Democritus's atomic theory with an ethics that sought ataraxia (tranquility) through the rational management of desire.

## Role in the Meditations

Marcus engages Epicurus on multiple fronts. He adopts one practical insight: that pain is "neither intolerable, nor eternal" (017-156) — a Stoic-compatible consolation in physical suffering. But on fundamentals, Marcus draws a firm line. He presents the choice between two worldviews: either all things happen by Providence through reason, or "according to Epicurus, atoms be the cause of all things" — and the latter, for Marcus, reduces life to an "accidentary confusion" (023-035). In a third passage, Marcus records Epicurus's conduct during his own final illness: "my discourses were not concerning the nature of my disease" but instead focused on philosophy (023-067) — a model of philosophical composure Marcus implicitly approves. In Book 9, Marcus offers the stark disjunction: "Either with Epicurus, we must fondly imagine the atoms to be the cause of all things, or we must needs grant a nature" (024-017).

## Legacy and Influence

The Epicurean-Stoic rivalry shaped Roman intellectual culture. While Marcus ultimately rejects Epicurean physics, his engagement is serious and respectful — he quotes Epicurus directly, adopts what is useful, and treats the school as a worthy opponent rather than a caricature.
