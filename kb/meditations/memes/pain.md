## Overview

Pain (Greek πόνος / _ponos_, Latin _dolor_) is the Stoic test case for the doctrine of indifferents. If virtue alone is good, then even extreme physical pain cannot constitute genuine evil. The Stoic Wise Man, knowing this truth, remains content "even when stretched upon the rack." (002-048) (002-049)

## Pain as Indifferent

The Stoic classification of pain as indifferent is perhaps the most counterintuitive element of their ethics. Pain feels bad — undeniably so. The Stoics do not deny this. What they deny is that the feeling of pain constitutes moral harm. The body suffers; the soul, if it maintains its rational judgment, does not. This is not a denial of the reality of pain but a reclassification of its significance: pain is a bodily event; good and evil are states of the soul. (002-046) (002-048)

## Pain and Virtue

The virtue of suffering in itself — an idea foreign to much of Greek thought — finds expression in Stoicism. Pain, endured rightly, can become an occasion for the exercise of virtue: courage, patience, fortitude. It is not the pain itself that is good but the soul's response to it. (002-122) Marcus Aurelius's own life was marked by chronic illness and physical frailty. His meditation on pain is not abstract — it is the record of a man who knew suffering intimately.
