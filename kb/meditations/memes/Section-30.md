# Section 30

(030-001) XXIII. There is but one light of the sun, though it be intercepted by

(030-002) walls and mountains, and other thousand objects. There is but one common

(030-003) substance of the whole world, though it be concluded and restrained into

(030-004) several different bodies, in number infinite. There is but one common

(030-005) soul, though divided into innumerable particular essences and natures.

(030-006) So is there but one common intellectual soul, though it seem to be

(030-007) divided. And as for all other parts of those generals which we have

(030-008) mentioned, as either sensitive souls or subjects, these of themselves

(030-009) (as naturally irrational) have no common mutual reference one unto

(030-010) another, though many of them contain a mind, or reasonable faculty in

(030-011) them, whereby they are ruled and governed. But of every reasonable mind,

(030-012) this the particular nature, that it hath reference to whatsoever is

(030-013) of her own kind, and desireth to be united: neither can this common

(030-014) affection, or mutual unity and correspondency, be here intercepted or

(030-015) divided, or confined to particulars as those other common things are.

(030-016) XXIV. What doest thou desire? To live long. What? To enjoy the

(030-017) operations of a sensitive soul; or of the appetitive faculty? or wouldst

(030-018) thou grow, and then decrease again? Wouldst thou long be able to talk,

(030-019) to think and reason with thyself? Which of all these seems unto thee a

(030-020) worthy object of thy desire? Now if of all these thou doest find that

(030-021) they be but little worth in themselves, proceed on unto the last, which

(030-022) is, in all things to follow God and reason. But for a man to grieve that

(030-023) by death he shall be deprived of any of these things, is both against

(030-024) God and reason.

(030-025) XXV. What a small portion of vast and infinite eternity it is, that is

(030-026) allowed unto every one of us, and how soon it vanisheth into the general

(030-027) age of the world: of the common substance, and of the common soul also

(030-028) what a small portion is allotted unto us: and in what a little clod of

(030-029) the whole earth (as it were) it is that thou doest crawl. After thou

(030-030) shalt rightly have considered these things with thyself; fancy not

(030-031) anything else in the world any more to be of any weight and moment

(030-032) but this, to do that only which thine own nature doth require; and to

(030-033) conform thyself to that which the common nature doth afford.

(030-034) XXVI. What is the present estate of my [[knowledge|understanding]]? For herein lieth

(030-035) all indeed. As for all other things, they are without the compass of

(030-036) mine own will: and if without the compass of my will, then are they as

(030-037) dead things unto me, and as it were mere smoke.

(030-038) XXVII. To stir up a man to the contempt of death this among other

(030-039) things, is of good power and efficacy, that even they who esteemed

(030-040) pleasure to be happiness, and pain misery, did nevertheless many of them

(030-041) contemn death as much as any. And can death be terrible to him, to

(030-042) whom that only seems good, which in the ordinary course of nature is

(030-043) seasonable? to him, to whom, whether his actions be many or few, so they

(030-044) be all good, is all one; and who whether he behold the things of the

(030-045) world being always the same either for many years, or for few years

(030-046) only, is altogether indifferent? O man! as a citizen thou hast lived,

(030-047) and conversed in this great city the world. Whether just for so many

(030-048) years, or no, what is it unto thee? Thou hast lived (thou mayest be

(030-049) sure) as long as the laws and orders of the city required; which may be

(030-050) the common comfort of all. Why then should it be grievous unto thee, if

(030-051) (not a tyrant, nor an unjust judge, but) the same nature that brought

(030-052) thee in, doth now send thee out of the world? As if the praetor should

(030-053) fairly dismiss him from the stage, whom he had taken in to act a while.

(030-054) Oh, but the play is not yet at an end, there are but three acts yet

(030-055) acted of it? Thou hast well said: for in matter of life, three acts is

(030-056) the whole play. Now to set a certain time to every man's acting, belongs

(030-057) unto him only, who as first he was of thy composition, so is now the

(030-058) cause of thy dissolution. As for thyself; thou hast to do with

(030-059) neither. Go thy ways then well pleased and contented: for so is He that

(030-060) dismisseth thee.

(030-061) APPENDIX

(030-062) CORRESPONDENCE OF M. AURELIUS ANTONINUS AND M. CORNELIUS FRONTO[1]

(030-063) M. CORNELIUS FRONTO was a Roman by descent, but of provincial birth,

(030-064) being native to Cirta, in Numidia. Thence he migrated to [[rome|Rome]] in the

(030-065) reign of [[hadrian|Hadrian]], and became the most famous rhetorician of his day.

(030-066) As a pleader and orator he was counted by his contemporaries hardly

(030-067) inferior to Tully himself, and as a teacher his aid was sought for the

(030-068) noblest youths of Rome. To him was entrusted the education of M.

(030-069) Aurelius and of his colleague L. Verus in their boyhood; and he was

(030-070) rewarded for his efforts by a seat in the Senate and the consular rank

(030-071) (A.D. 143). By the exercise of his profession he became wealthy; and if

(030-072) he speaks of his means as not great,[2] he must be comparing his wealth

(030-073) with the grandees of Rome, not with the ordinary citizen.

(030-074) Before the present century nothing was known of the works of [[fronto|Fronto]],

(030-075) except a grammatical treatise; but in 1815 Cardinal Mai published a

(030-076) number of letters and some short essays of Fronto, which he had

(030-077) discovered in a palimpsest at Milan. Other parts of the same MS. he

(030-078) found later in the Vatican, the whole being collected

(030-079) [1] References are made to the edition of Naber, Leipzig (Trübner),

(030-080) 1867.

(030-081) [2] Ad Verum imp. Aur. Caes., ii, 7. and edited in the year 1823.

(030-082) We now possess parts of his correspondence with [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]], with M.

(030-083) Aurelius, with L. Verus, and with certain of his friends, and also

(030-084) several rhetorical and historical fragments. Though none of the more

(030-085) ambitious works of Fronto have survived, there are enough to give proof

(030-086) of his powers. Never was a great literary [[fame|reputation]] less deserved. It

(030-087) would be hard to conceive of anything more vapid than the style and

(030-088) conception of these letters; clearly the man was a pedant without

(030-089) imagination or taste. Such indeed was the age he lived in, and it is no

(030-090) marvel that he was like to his age. But there must have been more in him

(030-091) than mere pedantry; there was indeed a heart in the man, which Marcus

(030-092) found, and he found also a tongue which could speak the truth. Fronto's

(030-093) letters are by no means free from exaggeration and laudation, but they

(030-094) do not show that loathsome flattery which filled the Roman court. He

(030-095) really admires what he praises, and his way of saying so is not unlike

(030-096) what often passes for criticism at the present day. He is not afraid to

(030-097) reprove what he thinks amiss; and the astonishment of Marcus at this

(030-098) will prove, if proof were needed, that he was not used to plain dealing.

(030-099) "How happy I am," he writes, "that my friend Marcus Cornelius, so

(030-100) distinguished as an orator and so noble as a man, thinks me worth

(030-101) praising and blaming."[3] In another place he deems himself blest

(030-102) because Pronto had taught him to speak the truth[4] although the context

(030-103) shows him to be speaking of expression, it is still a point in favour of

(030-104) Pronto. A sincere heart is better than literary taste; and if Fronto had

(030-105) not done his duty by the young prince, it is not easy to understand the

(030-106) friendship which remained between them up to the last.

(030-107) [3] Ad M. Caes iii. 17

(030-108) [4] Ad M. Caes iii. 12

(030-109) An example of the frankness which was between them is given by a

(030-110) difference they had over the case of Herodes Atticus. Herodes was a

(030-111) Greek rhetorician who had a school at Rome, and [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]] was

(030-112) among his pupils. Both Marcus and the Emperor Antoninus had a high

(030-113) opinion of Herodes; and all we know goes to prove he was a man of high

(030-114) character and princely generosity. When quite young he was made

(030-115) administrator of the free cities in Asia, nor is it surprising to find

(030-116) that he made bitter enemies there; indeed, a just ruler was sure to make

(030-117) enemies. The end of it was that an Athenian deputation, headed by the

(030-118) orators Theodotus and Demostratus, made serious accusations against his

(030-119) honour. There is no need to discuss the merits of the case here; suffice

(030-120) it to say, Herodes succeeded in defending himself to the satisfaction of

(030-121) the emperor. Pronto appears to have taken the delegates' part, and to

(030-122) have accepted a brief for the prosecution, urged to some extent by

(030-123) personal considerations; and in this cause Marcus Aurelius writes to

(030-124) Fronto as follows:—

(030-125) 'AURELIUS CÆSAR to his friend FRONTO, greeting.[5]

(030-126) 'I know you have often told me you were anxious to find how you might

(030-127) best please me. Now is the time; now you can increase my love towards

(030-128) you, if it can be increased. A trial is at hand, in which people seem

(030-129) likely not only to hear your speech with pleasure, but to see your

(030-130) indignation with impatience. I see no one who dares give you a hint in

(030-131) the matter; for those who are less friendly, prefer to see you act with

(030-132) some inconsistency; and those who are more friendly, fear to seem too

(030-133) friendly to your opponent if they should dissuade you from your

(030-134) accusation; then again, in case you have prepared something neat for

(030-135) the occasion, they cannot endure to rob you of your harangue by

(030-136) silencing you. Therefore, whether you think me a rash counsellor, or a

(030-137) bold boy, or too kind to your opponent, not because I think it better,

(030-138) I will offer my counsel with some caution. But why have I said, offer

(030-139) my counsel? No, I demand it from you; I demand it boldly, and if I

(030-140) succeed, I promise to remain under your [[duty|obligation]]. What? you will say

(030-141) if I am attackt, shall I not pay tit for tat? Ah, but you will get

(030-142) greater glory, if even when attackt you answer nothing. Indeed, if he

(030-143) begins it, answer as you will and you will have fair excuse; but I have

(030-144) demanded of him that he shall not begin, and I think I have succeeded.

(030-145) I love each of you according to your merits and I know that lie was

(030-146) educated in the house of P. Calvisius, my grandfather, and that I was

(030-147) educated by you; therefore I am full of anxiety that this most

(030-148) disagreeable business shall be managed as honourably as possible. I

(030-149) trust you may approve my advice, for my intention you will approve. At

(030-150) least I prefer to write unwisely rather than to be silent unkindly.'

(030-151) [5] Ad M. Caes ii., 2.

(030-152) Fronto replied, thanking the prince for his advice, and promising that

(030-153) he will confine himself to the facts of the case. But he points out that

(030-154) the charges brought against Herodes were such, that they can hardly be

(030-155) made agreeable; amongst them being spoliation, violence, and murder.

(030-156) However, he is willing even to let some of these drop if it be the

(030-157) prince's pleasure. To this Marcus returned the following answer:--[6]

(030-158) 'This one thing, my dearest Fronto, is enough to make me truly grateful

(030-159) to you, that so far from rejecting my counsel, you have even approved

(030-160) it. As to the question you raise in your kind letter, my opinion is

(030-161) this: all that concerns the case which you are supporting must be

(030-162) clearly brought forward; what concerns your own feelings, though you may

(030-163) have had just provocation, should be left unsaid.' The story does credit

(030-164) to both. Fronto shows no loss of temper at the interference, nor shrinks

(030-165) from stating his case with frankness; and Marcus, with forbearance

(030-166) remarkable in a prince, does not command that his friend be left

(030-167) unmolested, but merely stipulates for a fair trial on the merits of the

(030-168) case.
