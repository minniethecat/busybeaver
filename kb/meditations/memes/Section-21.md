# Section 21

(021-001) LII. Not only now henceforth to have a common breath, or to hold

(021-002) correspondency of breath, with that air, that compasseth us about; but

(021-003) to have a common mind, or to hold correspondency of mind also with that

(021-004) rational substance, which compasseth all things. For, that also is of

(021-005) itself, and of its own nature (if a man can but draw it in as he should)

(021-006) everywhere diffused; and passeth through all things, no less than the

(021-007) air doth, if a man can but suck it in.

(021-008) LIII. Wickedness in general doth not hurt the world. Particular

(021-009) wickedness doth not hurt any other: only unto him it is hurtful,

(021-010) whosoever he be that offends, unto whom in great favour and mercy it is

(021-011) granted, that whensoever he himself shall but first desire it, he may be

(021-012) presently delivered of it. Unto my free-will my neighbour's free-will,

(021-013) whoever he be, (as his life, or his bode), is altogether indifferent.

(021-014) For though we are all made one for another, yet have our minds and

(021-015) understandings each of them their own proper and limited jurisdiction.

(021-016) For else another man's wickedness might be my evil which God would not

(021-017) have, that it might not be in another man's power to make me unhappy:

(021-018) which nothing now can do but mine own wickedness.

(021-019) LIV. The sun seemeth to be shed abroad. And indeed it is diffused but

(021-020) not effused. For that diffusion of it is a τάσις or an extension. For

(021-021) therefore are the beams of it called ἀκτῖνες from the word ἐκτείνεσθαι

(021-022) to be stretched out and extended. Now what a sunbeam is, thou mayest

(021-023) know if thou observe the light of the sun, when through some narrow

(021-024) hole it pierceth into some room that is dark. For it is always in a

(021-025) direct line. And as by any solid body, that it meets with in the way

(021-026) that is not penetrable by air, it is divided and abrupted, and yet

(021-027) neither slides off, or falls down, but stayeth there nevertheless: such

(021-028) must the diffusion in the mind be; not an effusion, but an extension.

(021-029) What obstacles and impediments soever she meeteth within her way, she

(021-030) must not violently, and by way of an impetuous onset light upon them;

(021-031) neither must she fall down; but she must stand, and give light unto

(021-032) that which doth admit of it. For as for that which doth not, it is its

(021-033) own fault and loss, if it bereave itself of her light.

(021-034) LV. He that feareth death, either feareth that he shall have no sense at

(021-035) all, or that his senses will not be the same. Whereas, he should rather

(021-036) comfort himself, that either no sense at all, and so no sense of evil;

(021-037) or if any sense, then another life, and so no death properly.

(021-038) LVI. All men are made one for another: either then teach them better, or

(021-039) bear with them.

(021-040) LVII. The motion of the mind is not as the motion of a dart. For

(021-041) the mind when it is wary and cautelous, and by way of diligent

(021-042) circumspection turneth herself many ways, may then as well be said to

(021-043) go straight on to the object, as when it useth no such circumspection.

(021-044) LVIII. To pierce and penetrate into the estate of every one's

(021-045) [[knowledge|understanding]] that thou hast to do with: as also to make the estate of

(021-046) thine own open, and penetrable to any other.

(021-047) THE NINTH BOOK

(021-048) I. He that is unjust, is also impious. For the nature of the universe,

(021-049) having made all reasonable creatures one for another, to the end that

(021-050) they should do one another good; more or less according to the several

(021-051) persons and occasions but in nowise hurt one another: it is manifest

(021-052) that he that doth transgress against this her will, is guilty of impiety

(021-053) towards the most ancient and venerable of all the deities. For the

(021-054) nature of the universe, is the nature the common parent of all, and

(021-055) therefore piously to be observed of all things that are, and that which

(021-056) now is, to whatsoever first was, and gave it its being, hath relation

(021-057) of blood and kindred. She is also called truth and is the first cause

(021-058) of all truths. He therefore that willingly and wittingly doth lie, is

(021-059) impious in that he doth receive, and so commit injustice: but he that

(021-060) against his will, in that he disagreeth from the nature of the universe,

(021-061) and in that striving with the nature of the world he doth in his

(021-062) particular, violate the general order of the world. For he doth no

(021-063) better than strive and war against it, who contrary to his own nature

(021-064) applieth himself to that which is contrary to truth. For nature had

(021-065) before furnished him with instincts and opportunities sufficient for the

(021-066) attainment of it; which he having hitherto neglected, is not now able

(021-067) to discern that which is false from that which is true. He also that

(021-068) pursues after pleasures, as that which is truly good and flies from

(021-069) pains, as that which is truly evil: is impious. For such a one must of

(021-070) necessity oftentimes accuse that common nature, as distributing many

(021-071) things both unto the evil, and unto the good, not according to the

(021-072) deserts of either: as unto the bad oftentimes pleasures, and the causes

(021-073) of pleasures; so unto the good, pains, and the occasions of pains.

(021-074) Again, he that feareth pains and crosses in this world, feareth some of

(021-075) those things which some time or other must needs happen in the world.

(021-076) And that we have already showed to be impious. And he that pursueth

(021-077) after pleasures, will not spare, to compass his desires, to do that

(021-078) which is unjust, and that is manifestly impious. Now those things which

(021-079) unto nature are equally indifferent (for she had not created both, both

(021-080) pain and pleasure, if both had not been unto her equally indifferent):

(021-081) they that will live according to nature, must in those things (as being

(021-082) of the same mind and disposition that she is) be as equally indifferent.

(021-083) Whosoever therefore in either matter of pleasure and pain; death and

(021-084) life; honour and dishonour, (which things nature in the administration

(021-085) of the world, indifferently doth make use of), is not as indifferent,

(021-086) it is apparent that he is impious. When I say that common nature

(021-087) doth indifferently make use of them, my meaning is, that they happen

(021-088) indifferently in the ordinary course of things, which by a necessary

(021-089) consequence, whether as principal or accessory, come to pass in the

(021-090) world, according to that first and ancient deliberation of [[providence|Providence]],

(021-091) by which she from some certain beginning, did resolve upon the creation

(021-092) of such a world, conceiving then in her womb as it were some certain

(021-093) rational generative seeds and faculties of things future, whether

(021-094) subjects, changes, successions; both such and such, and just so many.

(021-095) II. It were indeed more happy and comfortable, for a man to depart out

(021-096) of this world, having lived all his life long clear from all falsehood,

(021-097) dissimulation, voluptuousness, and pride. But if this cannot be, yet it

(021-098) is some comfort for a man joyfully to depart as weary, and out of love

(021-099) with those; rather than to desire to live, and to continue long in those

(021-100) wicked courses. Hath not yet experience taught thee to fly from the

(021-101) plague? For a far greater plague is the corruption of the mind, than any

(021-102) certain change and distemper of the common air can be. This is a plague

(021-103) of creatures, as they are living creatures; but that of men as they are

(021-104) men or reasonable.

(021-105) III. Thou must not in matter of death carry thyself scornfully, but as

(021-106) one that is well pleased with it, as being one of those things that

(021-107) nature hath appointed. For what thou dost conceive of these, of a boy to

(021-108) become a young man, to wax old, to grow, to ripen, to get teeth, or a

(021-109) beard, or grey hairs to beget, to bear, or to be delivered; or what

(021-110) other action soever it be, that is natural unto man according to the

(021-111) several seasons of his life; such a thing is it also to be dissolved. It

(021-112) is therefore the part of a wise man, in matter of death, not in any wise

(021-113) to carry himself either violently, or proudly but patiently to wait for

(021-114) it, as one of nature's operations: that with the same mind as now thou

(021-115) dost expect when that which yet is but an embryo in thy wife's belly

(021-116) shall come forth, thou mayst expect also when thy soul shall fall off

(021-117) from that outward coat or skin: wherein as a child in the belly it lieth

(021-118) involved and shut up. But thou desirest a more popular, and though not

(021-119) so direct and philosophical, yet a very powerful and penetrative recipe

(021-120) against the fear of death, nothing can make they more willing to part

(021-121) with thy life, than if thou shalt consider, both what the subjects

(021-122) themselves are that thou shalt part with, and what manner of disposition

(021-123) thou shalt no more have to do with. True it is, that, offended with them

(021-124) thou must not be by no means, but take care of them, and meekly bear

(021-125) with them However, this thou mayst remember, that whensoever it happens

(021-126) that thou depart, it shall not be from men that held the same opinions

(021-127) that thou dost. For that indeed, (if it were so) is the only thing that

(021-128) might make thee averse from death, and willing to continue here, if it

(021-129) were thy hap to live with men that had obtained the same belief that

(021-130) thou hast. But now, what a toil it is for thee to live with men of

(021-131) different opinions, thou seest: so that thou hast rather occasion to

(021-132) say, Hasten, I thee pray, O [[death|Death]]; lest I also in time forget myself.

(021-133) IV. He that sinneth, sinneth unto himself. He that is unjust, hurts

(021-134) himself, in that he makes himself worse than he was before. Not he only

(021-135) that committeth, but he also that omitteth something, is oftentimes

(021-136) unjust.

(021-137) V. If my present apprehension of the object be right, and my present

(021-138) action charitable, and this, towards whatsoever doth proceed from God,

(021-139) be my present disposition, to be well pleased with it, it sufficeth.

(021-140) VI. To wipe away fancy, to use deliberation, to quench concupiscence, to

(021-141) keep the mind free to herself.

(021-142) VII. Of all unreasonable creatures, there is but one unreasonable soul;

(021-143) and of all that are reasonable, but one reasonable soul, divided betwixt

(021-144) them all. As of all earthly things there is but one earth, and but one

(021-145) light that we see by; and but one air that we breathe in, as many as

(021-146) either breathe or see. Now whatsoever partakes of some common thing,

(021-147) naturally affects and inclines unto that whereof it is part, being of

(021-148) one kind and nature with it. Whatsoever is earthly, presseth downwards

(021-149) to the common earth. Whatsoever is liquid, would flow together. And

(021-150) whatsoever is airy, would be together likewise. So that without some

(021-151) obstacle, and some kind of violence, they cannot well be kept asunder.

(021-152) Whatsoever is fiery, doth not only by reason of the elementary fire tend

(021-153) upwards; but here also is so ready to join, and to burn together, that

(021-154) whatsoever doth want sufficient moisture to make resistance, is easily

(021-155) set on fire. Whatsoever therefore is partaker of that reasonable common

(021-156) nature, naturally doth as much and more long after his own kind. For by

(021-157) how much in its own nature it excels all other things, by so much more

(021-158) is it desirous to be joined and united unto that, which is of its own

(021-159) nature. As for unreasonable creatures then, they had not long been, but

(021-160) presently begun among them swarms, and flocks, and broods of young ones,

(021-161) and a kind of mutual love and affection. For though but unreasonable,

(021-162) yet a kind of soul these had, and therefore was that natural desire of

(021-163) union more strong and intense in them, as in creatures of a more

(021-164) excellent nature, than either in plants, or stones, or trees. But among

(021-165) reasonable creatures, begun commonwealths, friendships, families, public

(021-166) meetings, and even in their wars, conventions, and truces. Now among

(021-167) them that were yet of a more excellent nature, as the stars and planets,

(021-168) though by their nature far distant one from another, yet even among them

(021-169) began some mutual correspondency and unity. So proper is it to

(021-170) excellency in a high degree to affect unity, as that even in things so

(021-171) far distant, it could operate unto a mutual sympathy. But now behold,

(021-172) what is now come to pass. Those creatures that are reasonable, are now

(021-173) the only creatures that have forgotten their natural affection and

(021-174) inclination of one towards another. Among them alone of all other things

(021-175) that are of one kind, there is not to be found a general disposition to

(021-176) flow together. But though they fly from nature, yet are they stopt in

(021-177) their course, and apprehended. Do they what they can, nature doth

(021-178) prevail. And so shalt thou confess, if thou dost observe it. For sooner

(021-179) mayst thou find a thing earthly, where no earthly thing is, than find a

(021-180) man that naturally can live by himself alone.
