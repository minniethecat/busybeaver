# Section 16

(016-001) VI. Let not things future trouble thee. For if necessity so require that

(016-002) they come to pass, thou shalt (whensoever that is) be provided for them

(016-003) with the same reason, by which whatsoever is now present, is made both

(016-004) tolerable and acceptable unto thee. All things are linked and knitted

(016-005) together, and the knot is sacred, neither is there anything in the

(016-006) world, that is not kind and natural in regard of any other thing, or,

(016-007) that hath not some kind of reference and natural correspondence with

(016-008) whatsoever is in the world besides. For all things are ranked together,

(016-009) and by that decency of its due place and order that each particular

(016-010) doth observe, they all concur together to the making of one and the same

(016-011) κόσμος or world: as if you said, a comely piece, or an orderly

(016-012) composition. For all things throughout, there is but one and the same

(016-013) order; and through all things, one and the same God, the same substance

(016-014) and the same law. There is one common reason, and one common truth, that

(016-015) belongs unto all reasonable creatures, for neither is there save one

(016-016) perfection of all creatures that are of the same kind, and partakers of

(016-017) the same reason.

(016-018) VII. Whatsoever is material, doth soon vanish away into the common

(016-019) substance of the whole; and whatsoever is formal, or, whatsoever doth

(016-020) animate that which is material, is soon resumed into the common reason

(016-021) of the whole; and the fame and memory of anything, is soon swallowed up

(016-022) by the general age and duration of the whole.

(016-023) VIII. To a reasonable creature, the same action is both according

(016-024) to nature, and according to reason.

(016-025) IX. Straight of itself, not made straight.

(016-026) X. As several members in one body united, so are reasonable creatures

(016-027) in a body divided and dispersed, all made and prepared for one common

(016-028) operation. And this thou shalt apprehend the better, if thou shalt use

(016-029) thyself often to say to thyself, I am μέλος, or a member of the mass and

(016-030) body of reasonable substances. But if thou shalt say I am μέρος, or

(016-031) a part, thou dost not yet love men from thy heart. The joy that thou

(016-032) takest in the exercise of bounty, is not yet grounded upon a due

(016-033) ratiocination and right apprehension of the nature of things. Thou dost

(016-034) exercise it as yet upon this ground barely, as a thing convenient and

(016-035) fitting; not, as doing good to thyself, when thou dost good unto others.

(016-036) XI. Of things that are external, happen what will to that which can

(016-037) suffer by external accidents. Those things that suffer let them complain

(016-038) themselves, if they will; as for me, as long as I conceive no such

(016-039) thing, that that which is happened is evil, I have no hurt; and it is in

(016-040) my power not to conceive any such thing.

(016-041) XII. Whatsoever any man either doth or saith, thou must be good; not for

(016-042) any man's sake, but for thine own nature's sake; as if either gold, or

(016-043) the emerald, or purple, should ever be saying to themselves, Whatsoever

(016-044) any man either doth or saith, I must still be an emerald, and I must

(016-045) keep my colour.

(016-046) XIII. This may ever be my comfort and security: my [[knowledge|understanding]], that

(016-047) ruleth over all, will not of itself bring trouble and vexation upon

(016-048) itself. This I say; it will not put itself in any fear, it will not lead

(016-049) itself into any concupiscence. If it be in the power of any other to

(016-050) compel it to fear, or to grieve, it is free for him to use his power.

(016-051) But sure if itself do not of itself, through some false opinion or

(016-052) supposition incline itself to any such disposition; there is no fear.

(016-053) For as for the body, why should I make the grief of my body, to be the

(016-054) grief of my mind? If that itself can either fear or complain, let it.

(016-055) But as for the soul, which indeed, can only be truly sensible of either

(016-056) fear or grief; to which only it belongs according to its different

(016-057) imaginations and opinions, to admit of either of these, or of their

(016-058) contraries; thou mayst look to that thyself, that it suffer nothing.

(016-059) Induce her not to any such opinion or persuasion. The understanding

(016-060) is of itself sufficient unto itself, and needs not (if itself doth not

(016-061) bring itself to need) any other thing besides itself, and by consequent

(016-062) as it needs nothing, so neither can it be troubled or hindered by

(016-063) anything, if itself doth not trouble and hinder itself.

(016-064) XIV. What is εὐδαιμονία, or happiness: but ἀγαθὸς δαίμων, or, a good

(016-065) dæmon, or spirit? What then dost thou do here, O opinion? By the Gods

(016-066) I adjure thee, that thou get thee gone, as thou earnest: for I need thee

(016-067) not. Thou earnest indeed unto me according to thy ancient wonted manner.

(016-068) It is that, that all men have ever been subject unto. That thou camest

(016-069) therefore I am not angry with thee, only begone, now that I have found

(016-070) thee what thou art.

(016-071) XV. Is any man so foolish as to fear change, to which all things that

(016-072) once were not owe their being? And what is it, that is more pleasing and

(016-073) more familiar to the nature of the universe? How couldst thou thyself

(016-074) use thy ordinary hot baths, should not the wood that heateth them first

(016-075) be changed? How couldst thou receive any nourishment from those things

(016-076) that thou hast eaten, if they should not be changed? Can anything

(016-077) else almost (that is useful and profitable) be brought to pass without

(016-078) change? How then dost not thou perceive, that for thee also, by death,

(016-079) to come to change, is a thing of the very same nature, and as necessary

(016-080) for the nature of the universe?

(016-081) XVI. Through the substance of the universe, as through a torrent pass

(016-082) all particular bodies, being all of the same nature, and all joint

(016-083) workers with the universe itself as in one of our bodies so many

(016-084) members among themselves. How many such as Chrysippus, how many such

(016-085) as Socrates, how many such as [[epictetus|Epictetus]], hath the age of the world

(016-086) long since swallowed up and devoured? Let this, be it either men or

(016-087) businesses, that thou hast occasion to think of, to the end that thy

(016-088) thoughts be not distracted and thy mind too earnestly set upon anything,

(016-089) upon every such occasion presently come to thy mind. Of all my thoughts

(016-090) and cares, one only thing shall be the object, that I myself do nothing

(016-091) which to the proper constitution of man, (either in regard of the

(016-092) thing itself, or in regard of the manner, or of the time of doing,)

(016-093) is contrary. The time when thou shalt have forgotten all things, is

(016-094) at hand. And that time also is at hand, when thou thyself shalt be

(016-095) forgotten by all. Whilst thou art, apply thyself to that especially

(016-096) which unto man as he is a mart, is most proper and agreeable, and that

(016-097) is, for a man even to love them that transgress against him. This shall

(016-098) be, if at the same time that any such thing doth happen, thou call

(016-099) to mind, that they are thy kinsmen; that it is through ignorance and

(016-100) against their wills that they sin; and that within a very short while

(016-101) after, both thou and he shall be no more. But above all things, that he

(016-102) hath not done thee any hurt; for that by him thy mind and understanding

(016-103) is not made worse or more vile than it was before.

(016-104) XVII. The nature of the universe, of the common substance of all things

(016-105) as it were of so much wax hath now perchance formed a horse; and then,

(016-106) destroying that figure, hath new tempered and fashioned the matter of it

(016-107) into the form and substance of a tree: then that again into the form and

(016-108) substance of a man: and then that again into some other. Now every one

(016-109) of these doth subsist but for a very little while. As for dissolution,

(016-110) if it be no grievous thing to the chest or trunk, to be joined together;

(016-111) why should it be more grievous to be put asunder?

(016-112) XVIII. An angry countenance is much against nature, and it is oftentimes

(016-113) the proper countenance of them that are at the point of death. But were

(016-114) it so, that all anger and passion were so thoroughly quenched in thee,

(016-115) that it were altogether impossible to kindle it any more, yet herein

(016-116) must not thou rest satisfied, but further endeavour by good consequence

(016-117) of true ratiocination, perfectly to conceive and understand, that all

(016-118) anger and passion is against reason. For if thou shalt not be sensible

(016-119) of thine innocence; if that also shall be gone from thee, the comfort of

(016-120) a good conscience, that thou doest all things according to reason: what

(016-121) shouldest thou live any longer for? All things that now thou seest,

(016-122) are but for a moment. That nature, by which all things in the world are

(016-123) administered, will soon bring change and alteration upon them, and then

(016-124) of their substances make other things like unto them: and then soon

(016-125) after others again of the matter and substance of these: that so by

(016-126) these means, the world may still appear fresh and new.

(016-127) XIX. Whensoever any man doth trespass against other, presently consider

(016-128) with thyself what it was that he did suppose to be good, what to be

(016-129) evil, when he did trespass. For this when thou knowest, thou wilt pity

(016-130) him thou wilt have no occasion either to wonder, or to be angry. For

(016-131) either thou thyself dust yet live in that error and ignorance, as that

(016-132) thou dust suppose either that very thing that he doth, or some other

(016-133) like worldly thing, to be good; and so thou art bound to pardon him if

(016-134) he have done that which thou in the like case wouldst have done thyself.

(016-135) Or if so be that thou dost not any more suppose the same things to be

(016-136) good or evil, that he doth; how canst thou but be gentle unto him that

(016-137) is in an error?

(016-138) XX. Fancy not to thyself things future, as though they were present

(016-139) but of those that are present, take some aside, that thou takest most

(016-140) benefit of, and consider of them particularly, how wonderfully thou

(016-141) wouldst want them, if they were not present. But take heed withal, lest

(016-142) that whilst thou dust settle thy contentment in things present, thou

(016-143) grow in time so to overprize them, as that the want of them (whensoever

(016-144) it shall so fall out) should be a trouble and a vexation unto thee. Wind

(016-145) up thyself into thyself. Such is the nature of thy reasonable

(016-146) commanding part, as that if it exercise justice, and have by that means

(016-147) tranquillity within itself, it doth rest fully satisfied with itself

(016-148) without any other thing.

(016-149) XXI. Wipe off all opinion stay the force and violence of unreasonable

(016-150) lusts and affections: circumscribe the present time examine whatsoever

(016-151) it be that is happened, either to thyself or to another: divide all

(016-152) present objects, either in that which is formal or material think of the

(016-153) last hour. That which thy neighbour hath committed, where the guilt of

(016-154) it lieth, there let it rest. Examine in order whatsoever is spoken. Let

(016-155) thy mind penetrate both into the effects, and into the causes. Rejoice

(016-156) thyself with true simplicity, and modesty; and that all middle things

(016-157) between virtue and [[evil|vice]] are indifferent unto thee. Finally, love

(016-158) mankind; obey God.

(016-159) XXII. All things (saith he) are by certain order and appointment. And

(016-160) what if the elements only.

(016-161) It will suffice to remember, that all things in general are by certain

(016-162) order and appointment: or if it be but few. And as concerning death,

(016-163) that either dispersion, or the atoms, or annihilation, or extinction,

(016-164) or translation will ensue. And as concerning pain, that that which is

(016-165) intolerable is soon ended by death; and that which holds long must needs

(016-166) be tolerable; and that the mind in the meantime (which is all in all)

(016-167) may by way of interclusion, or interception, by stopping all manner of

(016-168) commerce and sympathy with the body, still retain its own tranquillity.

(016-169) Thy understanding is not made worse by it. As for those parts that

(016-170) suffer, let them, if they can, declare their grief themselves. As for

(016-171) praise and commendation, view their mind and understanding, what estate

(016-172) they are in; what kind of things they fly, and what things they seek

(016-173) after: and that as in the seaside, whatsoever was before to be seen,

(016-174) is by the continual succession of new heaps of sand cast up one upon

(016-175) another, soon hid and covered; so in this life, all former things by

(016-176) those which immediately succeed.
