## Overview

In Stoic philosophy, the **will** (Greek: προαίρεσις, *prohairesis*) is the faculty of moral choice — the mind's capacity to assent to or reject impressions, and to direct action according to reason. For Marcus Aurelius, the will is the sole domain of genuine human freedom: while external events lie beyond our control, the will's disposition toward them remains entirely within our power (002-063).

## Core Characteristics

The Stoic will is identified with the "ruling principle" (ἡγεμονικόν, *hēgemonikon*) — the directing mind that governs thought, judgment, and action. Unlike desire or emotion, which arise from impressions, the will is the active power that assents to or withholds assent from those impressions. Marcus describes it as "the power of the will" that distinguishes what is truly good from what is merely preferred (002-063), and notes that all else — "health, wealth, honour" — stands outside its domain (002-066).

## Will and the Discipline of Assent

The will operates most clearly in what Stoic teachers called the "discipline of assent" — the practice of examining every impression before accepting it as true or acting upon it. An impression presents itself ("this person has wronged me"); the will examines it ("is this truly an injury, or merely my judgment?"); and then either assents or withholds assent. This moment of suspension — the gap between impression and response — is where freedom resides. Marcus practices this discipline throughout the Meditations, constantly interrogating his first impressions and reminding himself that "things do not touch the soul" but stand motionless outside, while disturbance comes only from the judgments within. (004-003) (005-019)

## Training the Will

Like any faculty, the will requires training. Marcus describes a progressive discipline: first, refuse assent to the grosser passions (anger, lust, greed); then, gradually extend this vigilance to subtler disturbances (anxiety about reputation, irritation at small inconveniences); finally, cultivate the positive disposition of willing only what is just, benevolent, and in accordance with nature. This is not achieved through a single resolution but through daily, hourly practice — a constant "return to the ruling faculty" to check its state and correct its errors. Each moment of successful self-governance strengthens the will; each failure provides material for renewed effort. (005-010) (006-033)

## Will and Social Nature

The Stoic will is not a solitary faculty — it is inherently social. To will rightly is to will the good of the human community, for human nature is rational and social by design. Marcus frames this as a test of the will's health: does my will incline toward justice? Does it resist the impulse to retaliate? Does it seek to understand rather than to condemn? A properly functioning will spontaneously moves toward cooperation and beneficence, just as a healthy eye spontaneously moves toward light. When the will turns inward in self-absorption or outward in aggression, it is diseased and requires correction. (002-001) (009-038)

## In the Meditations

Marcus returns repeatedly to the will's central role in the inner life. He observes that a person's character follows the direction of their thoughts: "such as thy thoughts and thy habitual cogitations are, such will thy mind be in time" (003-053). The will, properly trained, maintains equanimity regardless of external fortune — it is the seat of the Stoic discipline of assent, through which one judges events correctly and refuses to be disturbed by what lies beyond one's power.
