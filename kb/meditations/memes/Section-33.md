# Section 33

(033-001) [40] Ad Verum. Imp. Aur. Caes., i. 3.

(033-002) 'To MY MASTER.

(033-003) 'I have a serious grievance against you, my dear master, yet indeed my

(033-004) grief is more than my grievance, because after so long a time I neither

(033-005) embraced you nor spoke to you, though you visited the palace, and the

(033-006) moment after I had left the prince my brother. I reproached my brother

(033-007) severely for not recalling me; nor durst he deny the fault.' [[fronto|Fronto]]

(033-008) again writes on one occasion: 'I have seen your daughter. It was like

(033-009) seeing you and [[faustina|Faustina]] in infancy, so much that is charming her face

(033-010) has taken from each of yours.' Or again, at a later date:[41] I have seen

(033-011) your chicks, most delightful sight that ever I saw in my life, so like

(033-012) you that nothing is more like than the likeness.... By the mercy of

(033-013) Heaven they have a healthy colour and strong lungs. One held a piece of

(033-014) white bread, like a little prince, the other a common piece, like a true

(033-015) philosophers son.'

(033-016) [41] Ad Ant. Imp i., 3.

(033-017) Marcus, we know, was devoted to his children. They were delicate in

(033-018) health, in spite of Fronto's assurance, and only one son survived the

(033-019) father. We find echoes of this affection now and again in the letters.

(033-020) 'We have summer heat here still,' writes Marcus, 'but since my little

(033-021) girls are pretty well, if I may say so, it is like the bracing climate

(033-022) of spring to us.'[42] When little Faustina came back from the valley of

(033-023) the shadow of death, her father at once writes to inform Fronto.[43]

(033-024) The sympathy he asks he also gives, and as old age brings more and more

(033-025) infirmity, Marcus becomes even more solicitous for his beloved teacher.

(033-026) The poor old man suffered a heavy blow in the death of his grandson, on

(033-027) which Marcus writes:[44] 'I have just heard of your misfortune. Feeling

(033-028) grieved as I do when one of your joints gives you pain, what do you

(033-029) think I feel, dear master, when you have pain of mind?' The old man's

(033-030) reply, in spite of a certain self-consciousness, is full of pathos. He

(033-031) recounts with pride the events of a long and upright life, in which he

(033-032) has wronged no man, and lived in harmony with his friends and family.

(033-033) His affectations fall away from him, as the cry of pain is forced from

(033-034) his heart:--

(033-035) [42] Ad M. Caes., v. 19

(033-036) [43] iv. 11

(033-037) [44] De Nepote Amissa

(033-038) [45]'Many such sorrows has fortune visited me with all my life long. To

(033-039) pass by my other afflictions, I have lost five children under the most

(033-040) pitiful conditions possible: for the five I lost one by one when each

(033-041) was my only child, [[pain|suffering]] these blows of bereavement in such a manner

(033-042) that each child was born to one already bereaved. Thus I ever lost my

(033-043) children without solace, and got them amidst fresh grief.....'

(033-044) [45] De Nepote Amissa 2

(033-045) The letter continues with reflections on the nature of death, 'more to

(033-046) be rejoiced at than bewailed, the younger one dies,' and an arraignment

(033-047) of [[providence|Providence]] not without dignity, wrung from him as it were by this

(033-048) last culminating misfortune. It concludes with a summing-up of his life

(033-049) in protest against the blow which has fallen on his grey head.

(033-050) 'Through my long life I have committed nothing which might bring

(033-051) dishonour, or disgrace, or shame: no deed of avarice or treachery have

(033-052) I done in all my day's: nay, but much generosity, much kindness, much

(033-053) truth and faithfulness have I shown, often at the risk of my own life.

(033-054) I have lived in amity with my good brother, whom I rejoice to see in

(033-055) possession of the highest office by your father's goodness, and by your

(033-056) friendship at peace and perfect rest. The offices which I have myself

(033-057) obtained I never strove for by any underhand means. I have cultivated

(033-058) my mind rather than my body; the pursuit of learning I have preferred to

(033-059) increasing my wealth. I preferred to be poor rather than bound by any'

(033-060) man's [[duty|obligation]], even to want rather than to beg. I have never been

(033-061) extravagant in spending money, I have earned it sometimes because I

(033-062) must. I have scrupulously spoken the truth, and have been glad to hear

(033-063) it spoken to me. I have thought it better to be neglected than to fawn,

(033-064) to be dumb than to feign, to be seldom a friend than to be often a

(033-065) flatterer. I have sought little, deserved not little. So far as I could,

(033-066) I have assisted each according to my means. I have given help readily

(033-067) to the deserving, fearlessly to the undeserving. No one by proving to be

(033-068) ungrateful has made me more slow to bestow promptly all benefits I could

(033-069) give, nor have I ever been harsh to ingratitude. (A fragmentary passage

(033-070) follows, in which he appears to speak of his desire for a peaceful

(033-071) end, and the desolation of his house.) I have suffered long and painful

(033-072) sickness, my beloved Marcus. Then I was visited by pitiful misfortunes:

(033-073) my wife I have lost, my grandson I have lost in Germany:[46] woe is me!

(033-074) I have lost my Decimanus. If I were made of iron, at this tine I could

(033-075) write no more.'

(033-076) [46] In the war against the Catti.

(033-077) It is noteworthy that in his _Meditations_ [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]] mentions

(033-078) Fronto only once.[47] All his literary studies, his oratory and

(033-079) criticism (such as it was) is forgotten; and, says he, 'Fronto taught

(033-080) me not to expect natural affection from the highly-born.' Fronto really

(033-081) said more than this: that 'affection' is not a Roman quality, nor has

(033-082) it a Latin name.[48] Roman or not Roman, Marcus found affection in

(033-083) Fronto; and if he outgrew his master's intellectual training, he never

(033-084) lost touch with the true heart of the man it is that which Fronto's

(033-085) name brings up to his remembrance, not dissertations on compound verbs

(033-086) or fatuous criticisms of style.

(033-087) [47] Book I., 8.

(033-089) NOTES

(033-090) This being neither a critical edition of the text nor an emended edition

(033-091) of Casaubon's translation, it has not been thought necessary to add full

(033-092) notes. Casaubon's own notes have been omitted, because for the most part

(033-093) they are discursive, and not necessary to an [[knowledge|understanding]] of what is

(033-094) written. In those which here follow, certain emendations of his

(033-095) are mentioned, which he proposes in his notes, and follows in the

(033-096) translation. In addition, one or two corrections are made where he has

(033-097) mistaken the Greek, and the translation might be misleading. Those which

(033-098) do not come under these two heads will explain themselves.

(033-099) The text itself has been prepared by a comparison of the editions of

(033-100) 1634 and 1635. It should be borne in mind that Casaubon's is often

(033-101) rather a paraphrase than a close translation; and it did not seem worth

(033-102) while to notice every variation or amplification of the original. In

(033-103) the original editions all that Casaubon conceives as understood, but

(033-104) not expressed, is enclosed in square brackets. These brackets are here

(033-105) omitted, as they interfere with the comfort of the reader; and so have

(033-106) some of the alternative renderings suggested by the translator. In a few

(033-107) cases, Latin words in the text have been replaced by English.

(033-108) Numbers in brackets refer to the Teubner text of Stich, but the

(033-109) divisions of the text are left unaltered. For some of the references

(033-110) identified I am indebted to Mr. G. H. Rendall's _Marcus Aurelius_.

(033-111) BOOK II "Both to frequent" (4). Gr. τὸ μή, C. conjectures τὸ μὲ. The

(033-112) text is probably right: "I did not frequent public lectures, and I was

(033-113) taught at home."

(033-114) VI Idiots.... philosophers (9). The reading is doubtful, but the meaning

(033-115) seems to be: "simple and unlearned men"

(033-116) XII "Claudius Maximus" (15). The reading of the Palatine MS. (now lost)

(033-117) was paraklhsiz Maximon, which C. supposes to conceal the letters kl as

(033-118) an abbreviation of Claudius.

(033-119) XIII "Patient hearing... He would not" (16). C. translates his

(033-120) conjectural reading epimonon ollan. on proapsth Stich suggests a reading

(033-121) with much the same sense: .....epimonon all antoi "Strict and rigid

(033-122) dealing" (16). C. translates tonvn (Pal. MS.) as though from tonoz,

(033-123) in the sense of "strain." "rigour." The reading of other MSS. tonvn is

(033-124) preferable.

(033-125) XIII "Congiaries" (13). dianomais, "doles."

(033-126) XIV "Cajeta" (17). The passage is certainly corrupt. C. spies a

(033-127) reference to Chryses praying by the sea-shore in the Illiad, and

(033-128) supposes M. Aurelius to have done the like. None of the emendations

(033-129) suggested is satisfactory. At § XV. Book II. is usually reckoned to

(033-130) begin. BOOK II III. "Do, soul" (6). If the received reading be right,

(033-131) it must be sarcastic; but there are several variants which show how

(033-132) unsatisfactory it is. C. translates "en gar o bioz ekasty so par eanty",

(033-133) which I do not understand. The sense required is: "Do not violence to

(033-134) thyself, for thou hast not long to use self-respect. Life is not (v. 1.

(033-135) so long for each, and this life for thee is all but done."

(033-136) X. "honour and credit do proceed" (12). The verb has dropt out of the

(033-137) text, but C. has supplied one of the required meaning.

(033-138) XI. "Consider," etc. (52). This verb is not in the Greek, which means:

(033-139) "(And reason also shows) how man, etc."

(033-140) BOOK IV XV. "Agathos" (18): This is probably not a proper name, but the

(033-141) text seems to be unsound. The meaning may be "the good man ought"

(033-142) XVI. oikonomian (16) is a "practical benefit," a secondary end. XXXIX.

(033-143) "For herein lieth all...." (~3). C. translates his conjecture olan for

(033-144) ola.

(033-145) BOOK V XIV. katorqwseiz (15): Acts of "rightness" or "straightness."

(033-146) XXIII. "Roarer" (28): Gr. "tragedian." Ed. 1 has whoremonger,' ed.

(033-147) 2 corrects to "harlot," but omits to alter' the word at its second

(033-148) occurrence.

(033-149) XXV. "Thou hast... them" (33): A quotation from Homer, Odyssey, iv. 690.

(033-150) XXVII. "One of the poets" (33): Hesiod, Op. et Dies, 197.

(033-151) XXIX and XXX. (36). The Greek appears to contain quotations from sources

(033-152) not known, and the translation is a paraphrase. (One or two alterations

(033-153) are here made on the authority of the second edition.) BOOK VI XIII.

(033-154) "Affected and qualified" (i4): exis, the power of cohesion shown in

(033-155) things inanimate; fusiz, power of growth seen in plants and the like.

(033-156) XVII. "Wonder at them" (18): i.e. mankind.

(033-157) XXXVII. "Chrysippus" (42): C. refers to a passage of Plutarch De

(033-158) Communibus Notitiis (c. xiv.), where Chrysippus is represented as saying

(033-159) that a coarse phrase may be vile in itself, yet have due place in a

(033-160) comedy as contributing to a certain effect.

(033-161) XL. "Man or men..." There is no hiatus in the Greek, which means:

(033-162) "Whatever (is beneficial) for a man is so for other men also."

(033-163) XLII. There is no hiatus in the Greek.
