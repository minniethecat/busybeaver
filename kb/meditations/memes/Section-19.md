# Section 19

(019-001) VIII. Forbear henceforth to complain of the trouble of a courtly life,

(019-002) either in public before others, or in private by thyself.

(019-003) IX. Repentance is an inward and self-reprehension for the neglect or

(019-004) omission of somewhat that was profitable. Now whatsoever is good, is

(019-005) also profitable, and it is the part of an honest virtuous man to set by

(019-006) it, and to make reckoning of it accordingly. But never did any honest

(019-007) virtuous man repent of the neglect or omission of any carnal pleasure:

(019-008) no carnal pleasure then is either good or profitable.

(019-009) X. This, what is it in itself, and by itself, according to its proper

(019-010) constitution? What is the substance of it? What is the matter, or proper

(019-011) use? What is the form or efficient cause? What is it for in this world,

(019-012) and how long will it abide? Thus must thou examine all things, that

(019-013) present themselves unto thee.

(019-014) XI. When thou art hard to be stirred up and awaked out of thy sleep,

(019-015) admonish thyself and call to mind, that, to perform actions tending to

(019-016) the common good is that which thine own proper constitution, and

(019-017) that which the nature of man do require. But to sleep, is common to

(019-018) unreasonable creatures also. And what more proper and natural, yea what

(019-019) more kind and pleasing, than that which is according to nature?

(019-020) XII. As every fancy and imagination presents itself unto thee, consider

(019-021) (if it be possible) the true nature, and the proper qualities of it, and

(019-022) reason with thyself about it.

(019-023) XIII. At thy first encounter with any one, say presently to thyself:

(019-024) This man, what are his opinions concerning that which is good or evil?

(019-025) as concerning pain, pleasure, and the causes of both; concerning honour,

(019-026) and dishonour, concerning life and death? thus and thus. Now if it be

(019-027) no wonder that a man should have such and such opinions, how can it be

(019-028) a wonder that he should do such and such things? I will remember then,

(019-029) that he cannot but do as he doth, holding those opinions that he doth.

(019-030) Remember, that as it is a shame for any man to wonder that a fig tree

(019-031) should bear figs, so also to wonder that the world should bear anything,

(019-032) whatsoever it is which in the ordinary course of nature it may bear.

(019-033) To a physician also and to a pilot it is a shame either for the one to

(019-034) wonder, that such and such a one should have an ague; or for the other,

(019-035) that the winds should prove Contrary.

(019-036) XIV. Remember, that to change thy mind upon occasion, and to follow him

(019-037) that is able to rectify thee, is equally ingenuous, as to find out at

(019-038) the first, what is right and just, without help. For of thee nothing is

(019-039) required, ti, is beyond the extent of thine own deliberation and jun.

(019-040) merit, and of thine own [[knowledge|understanding]].

(019-041) XV. If it were thine act and in thine own power, wouldest thou do

(019-042) it? If it were not, whom dost tin accuse? the atoms, or the Gods? For to

(019-043) do either, the part of a mad man. Thou must therefore blame nobody, but

(019-044) if it be in thy power, redress what is amiss; if it be not, to what end

(019-045) is it to complain? For nothing should be done but to some certain end.

(019-046) XVI. Whatsoever dieth and falleth, however and wheresoever it die

(019-047) and fall, it cannot fall out of the world, here it have its abode

(019-048) and change, here also shall it have its dissolution into its proper

(019-049) elements. The same are the world's elements, and the elements of which

(019-050) thou dost consist. And they when they are changed, they murmur not; why

(019-051) shouldest thou?

(019-052) XVII. Whatsoever is, was made for something: as a horse, a vine. Why

(019-053) wonderest thou? The sun itself will say of itself, I was made for

(019-054) something; and so hath every god its proper function. What then were

(019-055) then made for? to disport and [[pleasure|delight]] thyself? See how even common sense

(019-056) and reason cannot brook it.

(019-057) XVIII. [[nature|Nature]] hath its end as well in the end and final consummation of

(019-058) anything that is, as in the begin-nine and continuation of it.

(019-059) XIX. As one that tosseth up a ball. And what is a ball the better, if

(019-060) the motion of it be upwards; or the worse if it be downwards; or if it

(019-061) chance to fall upon the ground? So for the bubble; if it continue, what

(019-062) it the better? and if it dissolve, what is it the worse And so is it of

(019-063) a candle too. And so must thou reason with thyself, both in matter of

(019-064) fame, and in matter of death. For as for the body itself, (the subject

(019-065) of death) wouldest thou know the vileness of it? Turn it about that

(019-066) thou mayest behold it the worst sides upwards as well, as in its more

(019-067) ordinary pleasant shape; how doth it look, when it is old and withered?

(019-068) when sick and pained? when in the act of lust, and fornication? And

(019-069) as for fame. This life is short. Both he that praiseth, and he that is

(019-070) praised; he that remembers, and he that is remembered, will soon be dust

(019-071) and ashes. Besides, it is but in one corner of this part of the world

(019-072) that thou art praised; and yet in this corner, thou hast not the joint

(019-073) praises of all men; no nor scarce of any one constantly. And yet the

(019-074) whole earth itself, what is it but as one point, in regard of the whole

(019-075) world?

(019-076) XX. That which must be the subject of thy consideration, is either the

(019-077) matter itself, or the dogma, or the operation, or the true sense and

(019-078) signification.

(019-079) XXI. Most justly have these things happened unto thee: why dost not

(019-080) thou amend? O but thou hadst rather become good to-morrow, than to be

(019-081) so to-day.

(019-082) XXII. Shall I do it? I will; so the end of my action be to do good unto

(019-083) men. Doth anything by way of cross or adversity happen unto me? I accept

(019-084) it, with reference unto the Gods, and their providence; the fountain of

(019-085) all things, from which whatsoever comes to pass, doth hang and depend.

(019-086) XXIII. By one action judge of the rest: this bathing which usually takes

(019-087) up so much of our time, what is it? Oil, sweat, filth; or the sordes of

(019-088) the body: an excrementitious viscosity, the excrements of oil and other

(019-089) ointments used about the body, and mixed with the sordes of the body:

(019-090) all base and loathsome. And such almost is every part of our life;

(019-091) and every worldly object.

(019-092) XXIV. Lucilla buried Verus; then was Lucilla herself buried by others.

(019-093) So Secunda Maximus, then Secunda herself. So Epitynchanus, Diotimus;

(019-094) then Epitynchanus himself. So [[antoninus-pius|Antoninus Pius]], [[faustina|Faustina]] his wife; then

(019-095) Antoninus himself. This is the course of the world. First Celer,

(019-096) Adrianus; then Adrianus himself. And those austere ones; those that

(019-097) foretold other men's deaths; those that were so proud and stately, where

(019-098) are they now? Those austere ones I mean, such as were Charax, and

(019-099) Demetrius the Platonic, and Eudaemon, and others like unto those. They

(019-100) were all but for one day; all dead and gone long since. Some of them no

(019-101) sooner dead, than forgotten. Others soon turned into fables. Of others,

(019-102) even that which was fabulous, is now long since forgotten. This

(019-103) thereafter thou must remember, that whatsoever thou art compounded of,

(019-104) shall soon be dispersed, and that thy life and breath, or thy soul,

(019-105) shall either be no more or shall ranslated (sp.), and appointed to some

(019-106) certain place and station.

(019-107) XXV. The true joy of a man, is to do that which properly belongs unto a

(019-108) man. That which is most proper unto a man, is, first, to be kindly

(019-109) affected towards them that are of the same kind and nature as he is

(019-110) himself to contemn all sensual motions and appetites, to discern rightly

(019-111) all plausible fancies and imaginations, to contemplate the nature of the

(019-112) universe; both it, and things that are done in it. In which kind of

(019-113) contemplation three several relations are to be observed The first, to

(019-114) the apparent secondary cause. The Second to the first original cause,

(019-115) God, from whom originally proceeds whatsoever doth happen in the world.

(019-116) The third and last, to them that we live and converse with: what use may

(019-117) be made of it, to their use and benefit.

(019-118) XXVI. If pain be an evil, either it is in regard of the body; (and that

(019-119) cannot be, because the body of itself is altogether insensible:) or in

(019-120) regard of the soul But it is in the power of the soul, to preserve her

(019-121) own peace and tranquillity, and not to suppose that pain is evil. For

(019-122) all judgment and deliberation; all prosecution, or aversation is from

(019-123) within, whither the sense of evil (except it be let in by opinion)

(019-124) cannot penetrate.

(019-125) XXVII. Wipe off all idle fancies, and say unto thyself incessantly; Now

(019-126) if I will, it is in my power to keep out of this my soul all wickedness,

(019-127) all lust, and concupiscences, all trouble and confusion. But on the

(019-128) contrary to behold and consider all things according to their true

(019-129) nature, and to carry myself towards everything according to its true

(019-130) worth. Remember then this thy power that nature hath given thee.

(019-131) XXVIII. Whether thou speak in the Senate or whether thou speak to any

(019-132) particular, let thy speech In always grave and modest. But thou must

(019-133) not openly and vulgarly observe that sound and exact form of speaking,

(019-134) concerning that which is truly good and truly civil; the vanity of

(019-135) the world, and of worldly men: which otherwise truth and reason doth

(019-136) prescribe.

(019-137) XXIX. Augustus his court; his wife, his daughter, his nephews, his

(019-138) sons-in-law his sister, Agrippa, his kinsmen, his domestics, his

(019-139) friends; Areus, Mæcenas, his slayers of beasts for sacrifice and

(019-140) divination: there thou hast the death of a whole court together. Proceed

(019-141) now on to the rest that have been since that of Augustus. Hath death

(019-142) dwelt with them otherwise, though so many and so stately whilst they

(019-143) lived, than it doth use to deal with any one particular man? Consider

(019-144) now the death of a whole kindred and family, as of that of the Pompeys,

(019-145) as that also that useth to be written upon some monuments, HE WAS THE

(019-146) LAST OF HIS OWN KINDRED. O what care did his predecessors take, that

(019-147) they might leave a successor, yet behold at last one or other must of

(019-148) necessity be THE LAST. Here again therefore consider the death of a

(019-149) whole kindred.

(019-150) XXX. Contract thy whole life to the measure and proportion of one single

(019-151) action. And if in every particular action thou dost perform what is

(019-152) fitting to the utmost of thy power, let it suffice thee. And who can

(019-153) hinder thee, but that thou mayest perform what is fitting? But there may

(019-154) be some outward let and impediment. Not any, that can hinder thee, but

(019-155) that whatsoever thou dost, thou may do it, justly, temperately, and

(019-156) with the praise of God. Yea, but there may be somewhat, whereby some

(019-157) operation or other of thine may be hindered. And then, with that very

(019-158) thing that doth hinder, thou mayest he well pleased, and so by this

(019-159) gentle and equanimious conversion of thy mind unto that which may be,

(019-160) instead of that which at first thou didst intend, in the room of that

(019-161) former action there succeedeth another, which agrees as well with this

(019-162) contraction of thy life, that we now speak of.

(019-163) XXXI. Receive temporal blessings without ostentation, when they are sent

(019-164) and thou shalt be able to part with them with all readiness and facility

(019-165) when they are taken from thee again.

(019-166) XXXII. If ever thou sawest either a hand, or a foot, or a head lying by

(019-167) itself, in some place or other, as cut off from the rest of the body,

(019-168) such must thou conceive him to make himself, as much as in him lieth,

(019-169) that either is offended with anything that is happened, (whatsoever it

(019-170) be) and as it were divides himself from it: or that commits anything

(019-171) against the natural law of mutual correspondence, and society among men:

(019-172) or, he that, commits any act of uncharitableness. Whosoever thou art,

(019-173) thou art such, thou art cast forth I know not whither out of the general

(019-174) unity, which is according to nature. Thou went born indeed a part, but

(019-175) now thou hast cut thyself off. However, herein is matter of joy and

(019-176) exultation, that thou mayst be united again. God hath not granted

(019-177) it unto any other part, that once separated and cut off, it might be

(019-178) reunited, and come together again. But, behold, that GOODNESS how great

(019-179) and immense it is! which hath so much esteemed MAN. As at first he

(019-180) was so made, that he needed not, except he would himself, have divided

(019-181) himself from the whole; so once divided and cut off, IT hath so provided

(019-182) and ordered it, that if he would himself, he might return, and grow

(019-183) together again, and be admitted into its former rank and place of a

(019-184) part, as he was before.
