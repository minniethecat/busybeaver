# Section 31

(031-001) [6] Ad. M. Caes., iii. 5.

(031-002) Another example may be given from a letter of [[fronto|Fronto]]'s[7] Here is

(031-003) something else quarrelsome and querulous. I have sometimes found fault

(031-004) with you in your absence somewhat seriously in the company of a few

(031-005) of my most intimate friends: at times, for example, when you mixt in

(031-006) society with a more solemn look than was fitting, or would read books

(031-007) in the theatre or in a banquet; nor did I absent myself from theatre

(031-008) or banquet when you did.[8] Then I used to call you a hard man, no good

(031-009) company, even disagreeable, sometimes, when anger got the better of me.

(031-010) But did any one else in the same banquet speak against you, I could

(031-011) not endure to hear it with equanimity. Thus it was easier for me to say

(031-012) something to your disadvantage myself, than to hear others do it; just

(031-013) as I could more easily bear to chastise my daughter Gratia, than to see

(031-014) her chastised by another.'

(031-015) [7] Ad. M. Caes., iv. 12.

(031-016) [8] The text is obscure

(031-017) The affection between them is clear from every page of the

(031-018) correspondence. A few instances are now given, which were written at

(031-019) different periods

(031-020) To MY MASTER.[9]

(031-021) 'This is how I have past the last few days. My sister was suddenly

(031-022) seized with an internal pain, so violent that I was horrified at her

(031-023) looks; my mother in her trepidation on that account accidentally

(031-024) bruised her side on a corner of the wall; she and we were greatly

(031-025) troubled about that blow. For myself; on going to rest I found a

(031-026) scorpion in my bed; but I did not lie down upon him, I killed him

(031-027) first. If you are getting on better, that is a consolation. My mother

(031-028) is easier now, thanks be to God. Good-bye, best and sweetest master. My

(031-029) lady sends you greeting.'

(031-030) [9] Ad M. Caes., v. 8.

(031-031) [10]'What words can I find to fit my had luck, or how shall I upbraid as

(031-032) it deserves the hard constraint which is laid upon me? It ties me fast

(031-033) here, troubled my heart is, and beset by such anxiety; nor does it allow

(031-034) me to make haste to my Fronto, my life and [[pleasure|delight]], to be near him at

(031-035) such a moment of ill-health in particular, to hold his hands, to chafe

(031-036) gently that identical foot, so far as may be done without discomfort, to

(031-037) attend him in the bath, to support his steps with my arm.'

(031-038) [10] Ad M. Caes., i. 2.

(031-039) [11]'This morning I did not write to you, because I heard you were

(031-040) better, and because I was myself engaged in other business, and I

(031-041) cannot ever endure to write anything to you unless with mind at ease and

(031-042) untroubled and free. So if we are all right, let me know: what I desire,

(031-043) you know, and how properly I desire it, I know. Farewell, my master,

(031-044) always in every chance first in my mind, as you deserve to be. My

(031-045) master, see I am not asleep, and I compel myself to sleep, that you may

(031-046) not be angry with me. You gather I am writing this late at night.'

(031-047) [11] iii. 21.

(031-048) [12]'What spirit do you suppose is in me, when I remember how long it

(031-049) is since I have seen you, and why I have not seen you! and it may be

(031-050) I shall not see you for a few days yet, while you are strengthening

(031-051) yourself; as you must. So while you lie on the sick-bed, my spirit also

(031-052) will lie low anti, whenas,[13] by God's mercy you shall stand upright,

(031-053) my spirit too will stand firm, which is now burning with the strongest

(031-054) desire for you. Farewell, soul of your prince, your pupil.'

(031-055) [14]O my dear Fronto, most distinguished Consul! I yield, you have

(031-056) conquered: all who have ever loved before, you have conquered out and

(031-057) out in love's contest. Receive the victor's wreath; and the herald

(031-058) shall proclaim your victory aloud before your own tribunal: "M.

(031-059) Cornelius Fronto, Consul, wins, and is crowned victor in the Open

(031-060) International Love-race."[15] But beaten though I may be, I shall

(031-061) neither slacken nor relax my own zeal. Well, you shall love me more

(031-062) than any man loves any other man; but I, who possess a faculty of

(031-063) loving less strong, shall love you more than any one else loves you;

(031-064) more indeed than you love yourself. Gratia and I will have to fight for

(031-065) it; I doubt I shall not get the better of her. For, as Plautus says,

(031-066) her love is like rain, whose big drops not only penetrate the dress,

(031-067) but drench to the very marrow.'

(031-068) [12] Ad M. Caes., iii. 19.

(031-069) [13] The writer sometimes uses archaisms such as _quom_, which I render

(031-070) 'whenas'.

(031-071) [14] Ad M. Caes., ii. 2.

(031-072) [15] The writer parodies the proclamation at the Greek games; the words

(031-073) also are Greek.

(031-074) [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]] seems to have been about eighteen years of age when

(031-075) the correspondence begins, Fronto being some thirty years older.[16] The

(031-076) systematic education of the young prince seems to have been finisht, and

(031-077) Pronto now acts more as his adviser than his tutor. He recommends

(031-078) the prince to use simplicity in his public speeches, and to avoid

(031-079) affectation.[17] Marcus devotes his attention to the old authors who then

(031-080) had a great vogue at [[rome|Rome]]: Ennius, Plautus, Nævius, and such orators

(031-081) as Cato and Gracchus.[18] Pronto urges on him the study of Cicero, whose

(031-082) letters, he says, are all worth reading.

(031-083) [16] From internal evidence: the letters are not arranged in order of

(031-084) time. See Naher's _Prolegomena_, p. xx. foll.

(031-085) [17] Ad M. Caes., iii. x.

(031-086) [18] Ad M. Caes ii. 10,; iii. 18,; ii. 4.

(031-087) When he wishes to compliment Marcus he declares one or other of his

(031-088) letters has the true Tullian ring. Marcus gives his nights to reading

(031-089) when he ought to be sleeping. He exercises himself in verse composition

(031-090) and on rhetorical themes.

(031-091) 'It is very nice of you,' he writes to Fronto,[19] 'to ask for my

(031-092) hexameters; I would have sent them at once if I had them by me. The fact

(031-093) is my secretary, Anicetus-you know who I mean-did not pack up any of my

(031-094) compositions for me to take away with me. He knows my weakness; he was

(031-095) afraid that if I got hold of them I might, as usual, make smoke of them.

(031-096) However, there was no fear for the hexameters. I must confess the truth

(031-097) to my master: I love them. I study at night, since the day is taken up

(031-098) with the theatre. I am weary of an evening, and sleepy in the daylight,

(031-099) and so I don't do much. Yet I have made extracts from sixty books, five

(031-100) volumes of them, in these latter days. But when you read remember

(031-101) that the "sixty" includes plays of Novius, and farces, and some little

(031-102) speeches of Scipio; don't be too much startled at the number. You

(031-103) remember your Polemon; but I pray you do not remember Horace, who has

(031-104) died with Pollio as far as I am concerned.[20] Farewell, my dearest

(031-105) and most affectionate friend, most distinguished consul and my beloved

(031-106) master, whom I have not seen these two years. Those who say two months,

(031-107) count the days. Shall I ever see you again?'

(031-108) [19] Ad M. Caes., ii. 10.

(031-109) [20] He implies, as in i. 6, that he has ceased to study Horace.

(031-110) Sometimes Fronto sends him a theme to work up, as thus: 'M. Lucilius

(031-111) tribune of the people violently throws into prison a free Roman citizen,

(031-112) against the opinion of his colleagues who demand his release. For this

(031-113) act he is branded by the censor. Analyse the case, and then take both

(031-114) sides in turn, attacking and defending.'[21] Or again: 'A Roman consul,

(031-115) doffing his state robe, dons the gauntlet and kills a lion amongst

(031-116) the young men at the Quinquatrus in full view of the people of Rome.

(031-117) Denunciation before the censors.'[22] The prince has a fair knowledge of

(031-118) Greek, and quotes from Homer, Plato, Euripides, but for some reason

(031-119) Fronto dissuaded him from this study.[23] His _Meditations_ are written in

(031-120) Greek. He continued his literary studies throughout his life, and after

(031-121) he became emperor we still find him asking his adviser for copies of

(031-122) Cicero's Letters, by which he hopes to improve his vocabulary.[24] Pronto

(031-123) helps him with a supply of similes, which, it seems, he did not think of

(031-124) readily. It is to be feared that the fount of Marcus's eloquence was

(031-125) pumped up by artificial means.

(031-126) [21] Pollio was a grammarian, who taught Marcus.

(031-127) [22] Ad M. Caes., v. 27,; V. 22.

(031-128) [23] Ep. Gracae, 6.

(031-129) [24] Ad Anton. Imp., II. 4.

(031-130) Some idea of his literary style may be gathered from the letter which

(031-131) follows:[25]

(031-132) 'I heard Polemo declaim the other day, to say something of things

(031-133) sublunary. If you ask what I thought of him, listen. He seems to me an

(031-134) industrious farmer, endowed with the greatest skill, who has cultivated

(031-135) a large estate for corn and vines only, and indeed with a rich return

(031-136) of fine crops. But yet in that land of his there is no Pompeian fig or

(031-137) Arician vegetable, no Tarentine rose, or pleasing coppice, or thick

(031-138) grove, or shady plane tree; all is for use rather than for pleasure,

(031-139) such as one ought rather to commend, but cares not to love.

(031-140) [25] Ad M. Caes, ii. 5.

(031-141) A pretty bold idea, is it not, and rash judgment, to pass censure on a

(031-142) man of such [[fame|reputation]]? But whenas I remember that I am writing to you,

(031-143) I think I am less bold than you would have me.

(031-144) 'In that point I am wholly undecided.

(031-145) 'There's an unpremeditated hendecasyllable for you. So before I begin to

(031-146) poetize, I'll take an easy with you. Farewell, my heart's desire, your

(031-147) Verus's best beloved, most distinguisht consul, master most sweet.

(031-148) Farewell I ever pray, sweetest soul.

(031-149) What a letter do you think you have written me I could make bold to

(031-150) say, that never did she who bore me and nurst me, write anything SO

(031-151) delightful, so honey-sweet. And this does not come of your fine style

(031-152) and eloquence: otherwise not my mother only, but all who breathe.'

(031-153) To the pupil, never was anything on earth so fine as his master's

(031-154) eloquence; on this theme Marcus fairly bubbles over with enthusiasm.

(031-155) [26]'Well, if the ancient Greeks ever wrote anything like this, let

(031-156) those who know decide it: for me, if I dare say so, I never read any

(031-157) invective of Cato's so fine as your encomtum. O if my Lord[27] could be

(031-158) sufficiently praised, sufficiently praised he would have been

(031-159) undoubtedly by you! This kind of thing is not done nowadays.[28] It

(031-160) were easier to match Pheidias, easier to match Apelles, easier in a

(031-161) word to match Demosthenes himself, or Cato himself; than to match this

(031-162) finisht and perfect work. Never have I read anything more refined,

(031-163) anything more after the ancient type, anything more delicious, anything

(031-164) more Latin. O happy you, to be endowed with eloquence so great! O happy

(031-165) I, to be tinder the charge of such a master! O arguments,[29] O

(031-166) arrangement, O elegance, O wit, O beauty, O words, O brilliancy, O

(031-167) subtilty, O grace, O treatment, O everything! Mischief take me, if you

(031-168) ought not to have a rod put in your hand one day, a diadem on your

(031-169) brow, a tribunal raised for you; then the herald would summon us

(031-170) all-why do I say "us"? Would summnon all, those scholars and orators:

(031-171) one by one you would beckon them forward with your rod and admonish

(031-172) them. Hitherto I have had no fear of this admonition; many things help

(031-173) me to enter within your school. I write this in the utmost haste; for

(031-174) whenas I am sending you so kindly a letter from my Lord, what needs a

(031-175) longer letter of mine? Farewell then, glory of Roman eloquence, boast

(031-176) of your friends, magnifico, most delightful man, most distinguished

(031-177) consul, master most sweet.
