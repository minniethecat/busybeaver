# Section 29

(029-001) V. Use thyself even unto those things that thou doest at first despair

(029-002) of. For the left hand we see, which for the most part lieth idle because

(029-003) not used; yet doth it hold the bridle with more strength than the right,

(029-004) because it hath been used unto it.

(029-005) VI. Let these be the objects of thy ordinary meditation: to consider,

(029-006) what manner of men both for soul and body we ought to be, whensoever

(029-007) death shall surprise us: the shortness of this our mortal life: the

(029-008) immense vastness of the time that hath been before, and will he after

(029-009) us: the frailty of every worldly material object: all these things to

(029-010) consider, and behold clearly in themselves, all disguisement of external

(029-011) outside being removed and taken away. Again, to consider the efficient

(029-012) causes of all things: the proper ends and references of all actions:

(029-013) what pain is in itself; what pleasure, what death: what fame or

(029-014) honour, how every man is the true and proper ground of his own rest and

(029-015) tranquillity, and that no man can truly be hindered by any other: that

(029-016) all is but conceit and opinion. As for the use of thy dogmata, thou must

(029-017) carry thyself in the practice of them, rather like unto a pancratiastes,

(029-018) or one that at the same time both fights and wrestles with hands and

(029-019) feet, than a gladiator. For this, if he lose his sword that he fights

(029-020) with, he is gone: whereas the other hath still his hand free, which he

(029-021) may easily turn and manage at his will.

(029-022) VII. All worldly things thou must behold and consider, dividing them

(029-023) into matter, form, and reference, or their proper end.

(029-024) VIII. How happy is man in this his power that hath been granted unto

(029-025) him: that he needs not do anything but what God shall approve, and

(029-026) that he may embrace contentedly, whatsoever God doth send unto him?

(029-027) IX. Whatsoever doth happen in the ordinary course and consequence of

(029-028) natural events, neither the Gods, (for it is not possible, that they

(029-029) either wittingly or unwittingly should do anything amiss) nor men, (for

(029-030) it is through ignorance, and therefore against their wills that they do

(029-031) anything amiss) must be accused. None then must be accused.

(029-032) X. How ridiculous and strange is he, that wonders at anything that

(029-033) happens in this life in the ordinary course of nature!

(029-034) XI. Either [[providence|fate]], (and that either an absolute necessity, and unavoidable

(029-035) decree; or a placable and flexible Providence) or all is a mere

(029-036) casual confusion, void of all order and government. If an absolute and

(029-037) unavoidable necessity, why doest thou resist? If a placable and exorable

(029-038) Providence, make thyself worthy of the divine help and assistance. If

(029-039) all be a mere confusion without any moderator, or governor, then hast

(029-040) thou reason to congratulate thyself; that in such a general flood of

(029-041) confusion thou thyself hast obtained a reasonable faculty, whereby thou

(029-042) mayest govern thine own life and actions. But if thou beest carried

(029-043) away with the flood, it must be thy body perchance, or thy life, or some

(029-044) other thing that belongs unto them that is carried away: thy mind and

(029-045) [[knowledge|understanding]] cannot. Or should it be so, that the light of a candle

(029-046) indeed is still bright and lightsome until it be put out: and should

(029-047) truth, and [[justice|righteousness]], and temperance cease to shine in thee whilest

(029-048) thou thyself hast any being?

(029-049) XII. At the conceit and apprehension that such and such a one hath

(029-050) sinned, thus reason with thyself; What do I know whether this be a sin

(029-051) indeed, as it seems to be? But if it be, what do I know but that he

(029-052) himself hath already condemned himself for it? And that is all one as

(029-053) if a man should scratch and tear his own face, an object of compassion

(029-054) rather than of anger. Again, that he that would not have a vicious man

(029-055) to sin, is like unto him that would not have moisture in the fig, nor

(029-056) children to welp nor a horse to neigh, nor anything else that in the

(029-057) course of nature is necessary. For what shall he do that hath such an

(029-058) habit? If thou therefore beest powerful and eloquent, remedy it if thou

(029-059) canst.

(029-060) XIII. If it be not fitting, do it not. If it be not true, speak it not.

(029-061) Ever maintain thine own purpose and resolution free from all compulsion

(029-062) and necessity.

(029-063) XIV. Of everything that presents itself unto thee, to consider what the

(029-064) true nature of it is, and to unfold it, as it were, by dividing it into

(029-065) that which is formal: that which is material: the true use or end of it,

(029-066) and the just time that it is appointed to last.

(029-067) XV. It is high time for thee, to understand that there is somewhat in

(029-068) thee, better and more divine than either thy passions, or thy sensual

(029-069) appetites and affections. What is now the object of my mind, is it fear,

(029-070) or suspicion, or lust, or any such thing? To do nothing rashly without

(029-071) some certain end; let that be thy first care. The next, to have no other

(029-072) end than the common good. For, alas! yet a little while, and thou art no

(029-073) more: no more will any, either of those things that now thou seest, or

(029-074) of those men that now are living, be any more. For all things are by

(029-075) nature appointed soon to be changed, turned, and corrupted, that other

(029-076) things might succeed in their room.

(029-077) XVI. Remember that all is but opinion, and all opinion depends of the

(029-078) mind. Take thine opinion away, and then as a ship that hath stricken

(029-079) in within the arms and mouth of the harbour, a present calm; all things

(029-080) safe and steady: a bay, not capable of any storms and tempests: as the

(029-081) poet hath it.

(029-082) XVII. No operation whatsoever it he, ceasing for a while, can be truly

(029-083) said to suffer any evil, because it is at an end. Neither can he that

(029-084) is the author of that operation; for this very respect, because his

(029-085) operation is at an end, be said to suffer any evil. Likewise then,

(029-086) neither can the whole body of all our actions (which is our life) if in

(029-087) time it cease, be said to suffer any evil for this very reason, because

(029-088) it is at an end; nor he truly be said to have been ill affected, that

(029-089) did put a period to this series of actions. Now this time or certain

(029-090) period, depends of the determination of nature: sometimes of particular

(029-091) nature, as when a man dieth old; but of nature in general, however; the

(029-092) parts whereof thus changing one after another, the whole world still

(029-093) continues fresh and new. Now that is ever best and most seasonable,

(029-094) which is for the good of the whole. Thus it appears that death of

(029-095) itself can neither be hurtful to any in particular, because it is not a

(029-096) shameful thing (for neither is it a thing that depends of our own will,

(029-097) nor of itself contrary to the common good) and generally, as it is both

(029-098) expedient and seasonable to the whole, that in that respect it must

(029-099) needs be good. It is that also, which is brought unto us by the order

(029-100) and appointment of the Divine Providence; so that he whose will and

(029-101) mind in these things runs along with the Divine ordinance, and by this

(029-102) concurrence of his will and mind with the Divine Providence, is led

(029-103) and driven along, as it were by God Himself; may truly be termed and

(029-104) esteemed the θεοφόρητος, or divinely led and inspired.

(029-105) XVIII. These three things thou must have always in a readiness: first

(029-106) concerning thine own actions, whether thou doest nothing either idly,

(029-107) or otherwise, than justice and equity do require: and concerning those

(029-108) things that happen unto thee externally, that either they happen unto

(029-109) thee by chance, or by providence; of which two to accuse either, is

(029-110) equally against reason. Secondly, what like unto our bodies are

(029-111) whilest yet rude and imperfect, until they be animated: and from their

(029-112) animation, until their expiration: of what things they are compounded,

(029-113) and into what things they shall be dissolved. Thirdly, how vain all

(029-114) things will appear unto thee when, from on high as it were, looking

(029-115) down thou shalt contemplate all things upon earth, and the wonderful

(029-116) mutability, that they are subject unto: considering withal, the infinite

(029-117) both greatness and variety of things aerial and things celestial that

(029-118) are round about it. And that as often as thou shalt behold them, thou

(029-119) shalt still see the same: as the same things, so the same shortness of

(029-120) continuance of all those things. And, behold, these be the things that

(029-121) we are so proud and puffed up for.

(029-122) XIX. Cast away from thee opinion, and thou art safe. And what is it that

(029-123) hinders thee from casting of it away? When thou art grieved at anything,

(029-124) hast thou forgotten that all things happen according to the nature

(029-125) of the universe; and that him only it concerns, who is in fault; and

(029-126) moreover, that what is now done, is that which from ever hath been done

(029-127) in the world, and will ever be done, and is now done everywhere: how

(029-128) nearly all men are allied one to another by a kindred not of blood, nor

(029-129) of seed, but of the same mind. Thou hast also forgotten that every man's

(029-130) mind partakes of the Deity, and issueth from thence; and that no man can

(029-131) properly call anything his own, no not his son, nor his body, nor his

(029-132) life; for that they all proceed from that One who is the giver of all

(029-133) things: that all things are but opinion; that no man lives properly, but

(029-134) that very instant of time which is now present. And therefore that no

(029-135) man whensoever he dieth can properly be said to lose any more, than an

(029-136) instant of time.

(029-137) XX. Let thy thoughts ever run upon them, who once for some one thing or

(029-138) other, were moved with extraordinary indignation; who were once in

(029-139) the highest pitch of either honour, or calamity; or mutual hatred and

(029-140) enmity; or of any other fortune or condition whatsoever. Then consider

(029-141) what's now become of all those things. All is turned to smoke; all to

(029-142) ashes, and a mere fable; and perchance not so much as a fable. As also

(029-143) whatsoever is of this nature, as Fabius Catulinus in the field; Lucius

(029-144) Lupus, and Stertinius, at Baiæ Tiberius at Capreæ and Velius Rufus,

(029-145) and all such examples of vehement prosecution in worldly matters; let

(029-146) these also run in thy mind at the same time; and how vile every object

(029-147) of such earnest and vehement prosecution is; and how much more agreeable

(029-148) to true philosophy it is, for a man to carry himself in every matter

(029-149) that offers itself; justly, and moderately, as one that followeth the

(029-150) Gods with all simplicity. For, for a man to be proud and high conceited,

(029-151) that he is not proud and high conceited, is of all kind of pride and

(029-152) presumption, the most intolerable.

(029-153) XXI. To them that ask thee, Where hast thou seen the Gods, or how

(029-154) knowest thou certainly that there be Gods, that thou art so devout in

(029-155) their worship? I answer first of all, that even to the very eye, they

(029-156) are in some manner visible and apparent. Secondly, neither have I ever

(029-157) seen mine own soul, and yet I respect and honour it. So then for the

(029-158) Gods, by the daily experience that I have of their power and providence

(029-159) towards myself and others, I know certainly that they are, and therefore

(029-160) worship them.

(029-161) XXII. Herein doth consist happiness of life, for a man to know

(029-162) thoroughly the true nature of everything; what is the matter, and what

(029-163) is the form of it: with all his heart and soul, ever to do that which is

(029-164) just, and to speak the truth. What then remaineth but to enjoy thy life

(029-165) in a course and coherence of good actions, one upon another immediately

(029-166) succeeding, and never interrupted, though for never so little a while?
