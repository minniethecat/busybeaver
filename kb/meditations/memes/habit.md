## Overview

Habit (Greek ἄσκησις / askēsis, "exercise, training") in Stoic philosophy is the process by which philosophical principles are transformed from intellectual knowledge into settled dispositions of the soul. The Stoics held that virtue is not merely understood but practiced — and that practice, repeated over time, forms the habits that constitute character. Marcus Aurelius emphasized the "continual habit and exercise both of reason and sociableness" as the daily work of the philosopher. (013-092) (013-093) Like bodily exercises that strengthen the physique, philosophical exercises strengthen the soul. (003-145)

## In Stoic Training

The Stoic conception of habit draws on the broader Greek philosophical tradition of askēsis — disciplined training aimed at self-improvement. Unlike the extreme asceticism of some Cynics, Stoic habituation focused on the rational faculties: training oneself to judge correctly, to respond to impressions with equanimity, and to act with justice. Marcus notes that one should "so exercise thyself, as one who neither dreadeth death nor desireth it" — the middle path of rational habituation. (010-152) The Stoic practitioner develops an "habitual disposition" toward virtue, such that right action becomes increasingly natural rather than effortful. (027-179)

## Daily Practice

The cultivation of habit requires daily attention. Marcus recommends the practice of "retiring of thyself" — a form of mental withdrawal and self-examination that renews the mind's philosophical habits. (008-046) The philosopher who "doth put philosophy in practice" does so not through extraordinary acts but through the consistent habituation of ordinary responses — responding to insult with patience, to pleasure with moderation, to misfortune with equanimity. (009-066) The practice of right judgment extends to all situations: like a dancer who practices until movement becomes effortless, the philosopher practices right judgment until virtue becomes habitual. (017-137) Even the practice of charity is formed through repeated habituation rather than sporadic impulse. (018-068)

## In the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius's own practice, as revealed in the Meditations, is a model of Stoic habituation. The very act of writing — rising early to set down philosophical reflections — was itself a habit of self-cultivation. He asks himself whether he has the power to change a given habit, and answers that if one is truly free, one can reshape any disposition through repeated effort. (029-058) The habit of "cheerful patience" — meeting difficulties not with grim resignation but with active goodwill — was one he particularly cultivated. (015-077) (017-161) Even in the face of provocation, Marcus trained himself to respond without "indignation with impatience," recognizing that anger is a habit that can be broken through repeated refusal. (030-130) (020-024)
