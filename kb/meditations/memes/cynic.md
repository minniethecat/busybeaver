## Overview

Cynicism (Greek: Κυνισμός) was a philosophical school founded by Antisthenes of Athens in the 5th century BCE, reaching its most famous expression in Diogenes of Sinope. The Cynics practiced radical rejection of social convention, wealth, and comfort, holding that nothing was good but virtue and nothing bad but vice. Marcus Aurelius references the Cynic tradition in the *Meditations*, particularly the teaching of Monimus the Cynic that "all is but opinion" (002-019).

## In Stoic Ethics

Cynicism is the direct ancestor of Stoicism. Zeno of Citium, the founder of Stoicism, studied under the Cynic Crates before developing his own school. The Stoics adopted the Cynic emphasis on virtue as the sole good but moderated its extreme asceticism, permitting engagement with society and acceptance of wealth when properly used. The Cynic repudiation of all civil and social claims represented a rigorous form of the Stoic discipline of desire — stripping away attachment to externals. Marcus draws on this tradition when he reminds himself that reputation, luxury, and even life itself are "indifferent" things.

The relationship between Cynicism and Stoicism can be understood as a matter of degree rather than kind. Both schools agree that virtue is sufficient for happiness and that externals are indifferent. The Cynic, however, enacts this conviction through a visible rejection of social norms — living without home, property, or reputation — while the Stoic typically lives within society, accepting and using externals without attachment. Zeno's move from Cynicism to founding the Stoa was, in effect, the domestication of Cynic radicalism into a philosophy compatible with ordinary life. (007-093)

## Core Characteristics

The Cynic school was characterized by radical self-sufficiency (*autarkeia*), shamelessness in the face of social convention (*anaideia*), and public provocation as a method of moral instruction. Cynics lived as beggars, owning nothing, and used sharp wit to expose the hypocrisy of conventional morality. Monimus the Cynic, whom Marcus cites, taught that everything is opinion — a doctrine that strongly parallels the Stoic teaching that our judgments, not events, determine our peace of mind (002-019). The Cynic Crates renounced his wealth and taught Zeno the value of simple living.

The Cynic's deliberate poverty was not asceticism for its own sake but a philosophical demonstration: by living with nothing, the Cynic proved that nothing external is necessary for happiness. Diogenes, seeing a boy drink from his hands, threw away his cup — the act of a philosopher removing even the last vestige of unnecessary attachment. Marcus, though emperor, admired this spirit and sought to cultivate an inner Cynicism: the ability to be content with little, to see through the glitter of position and possession to the bare necessities of a rational life. (010-055)

## In the Meditations

Marcus invokes the Cynic legacy to reinforce the Stoic teaching that external goods are indifferent. He quotes Monimus the Cynic: "all is but opinion and conceit, for those things are plain and apparent, which were spoken unto Monimus the Cynic; and as plain and apparent is the use that may be made of those things, if that which is true and serious in them is received as well as that which is sweet and pleasant" (002-019). The Cynic reduction of everything to natural simplicity serves Marcus's practice of seeing through the glitter of imperial life to the bare reality beneath.

The Cynic influence appears throughout the Meditations in Marcus's repeated reminders that the emperor's purple is merely wool dyed with shellfish blood, that luxurious dishes are merely dead animals, that sexual pleasure is merely friction. This "Cynic reduction" — seeing things as they physically are, stripped of the value-judgments convention attaches to them — is one of Marcus's most characteristic mental exercises. It is Cynicism transformed from public performance into private spiritual practice, suitable for a man who cannot renounce the throne but can renounce the throne's hold on his soul. (006-033) (008-035)
