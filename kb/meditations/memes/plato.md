## Overview

Plato was an Athenian philosopher of the 5th–4th century BCE, student of Socrates and founder of the Academy. Marcus Aurelius quotes Plato directly in the *Meditations* and engages with Platonic ideas throughout the work, particularly the contemplation of all times and all existence from a higher perspective (017-001).

## Life and Career

Plato founded the Academy in Athens and authored over thirty dialogues that defined Western philosophy. His Theory of Ideas held that eternal Forms alone truly exist — a view the Stoics rejected in favor of materialism, though they retained Plato's emphasis on reason, order, and the soul's higher calling (002-043).

## Influence on Marcus Aurelius and Stoicism

Marcus invokes Plato repeatedly as an authority on magnanimity and cosmic perspective. In Book 7 of the Meditations, he quotes Plato's *Republic*: "He then whose mind is endowed with true magnanimity, who hath accustomed himself to the contemplation both of all times, and of all things in general" (017-001). A second passage from Plato's *Gorgias* teaches that one must not suppose a person of worth fears death more than anything shameful (017-022). Marcus also admires Plato's metaphor of looking down from a height upon worldly affairs, a device for achieving detachment (017-047). In Book 11, Marcus declares that even a "little progress" toward the ideal is acceptable, for one must not expect "a Plato's commonwealth" — acknowledging the gap between philosophical ideals and political reality (022-061).

## Legacy and Influence

Plato's influence runs through the entire Stoic tradition. While Stoic physics rejected Platonic idealism, the ethical framework of the virtues (wisdom, courage, justice, temperance) was adapted from Plato through the Hellenistic schools. Marcus's repeated quotation of Plato shows that the *Meditations* were in dialogue with the Platonism of the day as much as with Stoicism.
