## Overview

Pleasure (Greek ἡδονή / _hēdonē_) occupies a carefully circumscribed place in Stoic ethics. Against the Epicureans, who identified pleasure as the highest good, the Stoics classified it as an "indifferent" (ἀδιάφορον) — neither good nor evil, and certainly not the proper goal of human life. (002-044) (002-046)

## Pleasure and the Good

The Stoic argument against pleasure as the supreme good is both metaphysical and psychological. Pleasure is a bodily state, and the body is subordinate to the rational soul. The pursuit of pleasure as an end distorts the soul's judgment, leading it to mistake external satisfactions for genuine fulfillment. (002-046) The highest good is virtue — the soul's rational alignment with nature. Pleasure may accompany virtuous action as a by-product, but it must never be pursued as the primary aim. (002-040)

## Pleasure in the Meditations

Marcus Aurelius maintains a consistent wariness toward pleasure. He does not condemn all enjoyment — he appreciates the simple pleasures of life — but he vigilantly monitors his own relation to pleasure, ensuring it does not become a master rather than a servant. The discipline of desire, one of Epictetus's three disciplines, requires constant vigilance over the soul's tendency to attach itself to pleasant sensations.
