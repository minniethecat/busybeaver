# Section 23

(023-001) XXXIII. Loss and corruption, is in very deed nothing else but change and

(023-002) alteration; and that is it, which the nature of the universe doth most

(023-003) [[pleasure|delight]] in, by which, and according to which, whatsoever is done, is

(023-004) well done. For that was the estate of worldly things from the beginning,

(023-005) and so shall it ever be. Or wouldest thou rather say, that all things

(023-006) in the world have gone ill from the beginning for so many ages, and

(023-007) shall ever go ill? And then among so many deities, could no divine power

(023-008) be found all this while, that could rectify the things of the world? Or

(023-009) is the world, to incessant woes and miseries, for ever condemned?

(023-010) XXXIV. How base and putrid, every common matter is! Water, dust, and

(023-011) from the mixture of these bones, and all that loathsome stuff that our

(023-012) bodies do consist of: so subject to be infected, and corrupted. And

(023-013) again those other things that are so much prized and admired, as marble

(023-014) stones, what are they, but as it were the kernels of the earth? gold and

(023-015) silver, what are they, but as the more gross faeces of the earth? Thy

(023-016) most royal apparel, for matter, it is but as it were the hair of a silly

(023-017) sheep, and for colour, the very blood of a shell-fish; of this nature

(023-018) are all other things. Thy life itself, is some such thing too; a mere

(023-019) exhalation of blood: and it also, apt to be changed into some other

(023-020) common thing.

(023-021) XXXV. Will this querulousness, this murmuring, this complaining and

(023-022) dissembling never be at an end? What then is it, that troubleth thee?

(023-023) Doth any new thing happen unto thee? What doest thou so wonder at? At

(023-024) the cause, or the matter? Behold either by itself, is either of that

(023-025) weight and moment indeed? And besides these, there is not anything. But

(023-026) thy duty towards the Gods also, it is time thou shouldst acquit thyself

(023-027) of it with more goodness and simplicity.

(023-028) XXXVI. It is all one to see these things for a hundred of years together

(023-029) or but for three years.

(023-030) XXXVII. If he have sinned, his is the harm, not mine. But perchance he

(023-031) hath not.

(023-032) XXXVIII. Either all things by the providence of reason happen unto every

(023-033) particular, as a part of one general body; and then it is against reason

(023-034) that a part should complain of anything that happens for the good of the

(023-035) whole; or if, according to Epicurus, atoms be the cause of all things

(023-036) and that life be nothing else but an accidentary confusion of things,

(023-037) and death nothing else, but a mere dispersion and so of all other

(023-038) things: what doest thou trouble thyself for?

(023-039) XXXIX. Sayest thou unto that rational part, Thou art dead; corruption

(023-040) hath taken hold on thee? Doth it then also void excrements? Doth it like

(023-041) either oxen, or sheep, graze or feed; that it also should be mortal, as

(023-042) well as the body?

(023-043) XL. Either the Gods can do nothing for us at all, or they can still and

(023-044) allay all the distractions and distempers of thy mind. If they can do

(023-045) nothing, why doest thou pray? If they can, why wouldst not thou rather

(023-046) pray, that they will grant unto thee, that thou mayst neither fear, nor

(023-047) lust after any of those worldly things which cause these distractions

(023-048) and distempers of it? Why not rather, that thou mayst not at either

(023-049) their absence or presence, be grieved and discontented: than either that

(023-050) thou mayst obtain them, or that thou mayst avoid them? For certainly

(023-051) it must needs be, that if the Gods can help us in anything, they may in

(023-052) this kind also. But thou wilt say perchance, 'In those things the Gods

(023-053) have given me my liberty: and it is in mine own power to do what I

(023-054) will.' But if thou mayst use this liberty, rather to set thy mind at

(023-055) true liberty, than wilfully with baseness and servility of mind to

(023-056) affect those things, which either to compass or to avoid is not in thy

(023-057) power, wert not thou better? And as for the Gods, who hath told thee,

(023-058) that they may not help us up even in those things that they have put in

(023-059) our own power? whether it be so or no, thou shalt soon perceive, if

(023-060) thou wilt but try thyself and pray. One prayeth that he may compass his

(023-061) desire, to lie with such or such a one, pray thou that thou mayst not

(023-062) lust to lie with her. Another how he may be rid of such a one; pray thou

(023-063) that thou mayst so patiently bear with him, as that thou have no such

(023-064) need to be rid of him. Another, that he may not lose his child. Pray

(023-065) thou that thou mayst not fear to lose him. To this end and purpose, let

(023-066) all thy prayer be, and see what will be the event.

(023-067) XLI. 'In my sickness' (saith Epicurus of himself:) 'my discourses were

(023-068) not concerning the nature of my disease, neither was that, to them that

(023-069) came to visit me, the subject of my talk; but in the consideration and

(023-070) contemplation of that, which was of especial weight and moment, was all

(023-071) my time bestowed and spent, and among others in this very thing, how my

(023-072) mind, by a natural and unavoidable sympathy partaking in some sort with

(023-073) the present indisposition of my body, might nevertheless keep herself

(023-074) free from trouble, and in present possession of her own proper

(023-075) happiness. Neither did I leave the ordering of my body to the physicians

(023-076) altogether to do with me what they would, as though I expected any

(023-077) great matter from them, or as though I thought it a matter of such great

(023-078) consequence, by their means to recover my health: for my present estate,

(023-079) methought, liked me very well, and gave me good content.' Whether

(023-080) therefore in sickness (if thou chance to sicken) or in what other kind

(023-081) of extremity soever, endeavour thou also to be in thy mind so affected,

(023-082) as he doth report of himself: not to depart from thy philosophy for

(023-083) anything that can befall thee, nor to give ear to the discourses of

(023-084) silly people, and mere naturalists.

(023-085) XLII. It is common to all trades and professions to mind and intend that

(023-086) only, which now they are about, and the instrument whereby they work.

(023-087) XLIII. When at any time thou art offended with any one's impudency, put

(023-088) presently this question to thyself: 'What? Is it then possible, that

(023-089) there should not be any impudent men in the world! Certainly it is not

(023-090) possible.' Desire not then that which is impossible. For this one, (thou

(023-091) must think) whosoever he be, is one of those impudent ones, that

(023-092) the world cannot be without. So of the subtile and crafty, so of the

(023-093) perfidious, so of every one that offendeth, must thou ever be ready to

(023-094) reason with thyself. For whilst in general thou dost thus reason with

(023-095) thyself, that the kind of them must needs be in the world, thou wilt be

(023-096) the better able to use meekness towards every particular. This also

(023-097) thou shalt find of very good use, upon every such occasion, presently

(023-098) to consider with thyself, what proper virtue nature hath furnished man

(023-099) with, against such a [[evil|vice]], or to encounter with a disposition vicious

(023-100) in this kind. As for example, against the unthankful, it hath given

(023-101) goodness and meekness, as an antidote, and so against another vicious

(023-102) in another kind some other peculiar faculty. And generally, is it not

(023-103) in thy power to instruct him better, that is in an error? For whosoever

(023-104) sinneth, doth in that decline from his purposed end, and is certainly

(023-105) deceived, And again, what art thou the worse for his sin? For thou shalt

(023-106) not find that any one of these, against whom thou art incensed, hath in

(023-107) very deed done anything whereby thy mind (the only true subject of

(023-108) thy hurt and evil) can be made worse than it was. And what a matter of

(023-109) either grief or wonder is this, if he that is unlearned, do the deeds of

(023-110) one that is unlearned? Should not thou rather blame thyself, who, when

(023-111) upon very good grounds of reason, thou mightst have thought it very

(023-112) probable, that such a thing would by such a one be committed, didst not

(023-113) only not foresee it, but moreover dost wonder at it, that such a thing

(023-114) should be. But then especially, when thou dost find fault with either an

(023-115) unthankful, or a false man, must thou reflect upon thyself. For without

(023-116) all question, thou thyself art much in fault, if either of one that were

(023-117) of such a disposition, thou didst expect that he should be true unto

(023-118) thee: or when unto any thou didst a good turn, thou didst not there

(023-119) bound thy thoughts, as one that had obtained his end; nor didst not

(023-120) think that from the action itself thou hadst received a full reward of

(023-121) the good that thou hadst done. For what wouldst thou have more? Unto him

(023-122) that is a man, thou hast done a good turn: doth not that suffice thee?

(023-123) What thy nature required, that hast thou done. Must thou be rewarded for

(023-124) it? As if either the eye for that it seeth, or the feet that they go,

(023-125) should require satisfaction. For as these being by nature appointed for

(023-126) such an use, can challenge no more, than that they may work according

(023-127) to their natural constitution: so man being born to do good unto others

(023-128) whensoever he doth a real good unto any by helping them out of error; or

(023-129) though but in middle things, as in matter of wealth, life, preferment,

(023-130) and the like, doth help to further their desires he doth that for which

(023-131) he was made, and therefore can require no more.

(023-132) THE TENTH BOOK

(023-133) I. O my soul, the time I trust will be, when thou shalt be good, simple,

(023-134) single, more open and visible, than that body by which it is enclosed.

(023-135) Thou wilt one day be sensible of their happiness, whose end is love, and

(023-136) their affections dead to all worldly things. Thou shalt one day be full,

(023-137) and in want of no external thing: not seeking pleasure from anything,

(023-138) either living or insensible, that this world can afford; neither wanting

(023-139) time for the continuation of thy pleasure, nor place and opportunity,

(023-140) nor the favour either of the weather or of men. When thou shalt have

(023-141) content in thy present estate, and all things present shall add to thy

(023-142) content: when thou shalt persuade thyself, that thou hast all things;

(023-143) all for thy good, and all by the providence of the Gods: and of things

(023-144) future also shalt be as confident, that all will do well, as tending to

(023-145) the maintenance and preservation in some sort, of his perfect welfare

(023-146) and happiness, who is perfection of life, of goodness, and beauty; who

(023-147) begets all things, and containeth all things in himself, and in himself

(023-148) doth recollect all things from all places that are dissolved, that of

(023-149) them he may beget others again like unto them. Such one day shall be thy

(023-150) disposition, that thou shalt be able, both in regard of the Gods, and

(023-151) in regard of men, so to fit and order thy conversation, as neither

(023-152) to complain of them at any time, for anything that they do; nor to do

(023-153) anything thyself, for which thou mayest justly be condemned.

(023-154) II. As one who is altogether governed by nature, let it be thy care to

(023-155) observe what it is that thy nature in general doth require. That

(023-156) done, if thou find not that thy nature, as thou art a living sensible

(023-157) creature, will be the worse for it, thou mayest proceed. Next then thou

(023-158) must examine, what thy nature as thou art a living sensible creature,

(023-159) doth require. And that, whatsoever it be, thou mayest admit of and do

(023-160) it, if thy nature as thou art a reasonable living creature, will not be

(023-161) the worse for it. Now whatsoever is reasonable, is also sociable, Keep

(023-162) thyself to these rules, and trouble not thyself about idle things.
