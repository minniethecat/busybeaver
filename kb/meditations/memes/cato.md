## Overview

Marcus Porcius Cato, known as Cato the Younger or Cato of Utica, was a Roman senator and dedicated Stoic who became the symbol of principled opposition to tyranny. His suicide after Julius Caesar's victory at Thapsus (46 BCE) made his name "proverbial for virtue and courage" (034-047).

## Life and Career

Cato served as quaestor, tribune, and praetor, earning a reputation for incorruptibility rare in late Republican Rome. He opposed Caesar's rise and sided with Pompey in the civil war. After the Republican defeat at Thapsus, he took his own life rather than accept Caesar's pardon — an act of Stoic self-determination that Seneca and later writers celebrated.

## Role in the Meditations

Marcus learned of Cato through his philosophical education. His brother Severus introduced him to "the knowledge of Thrasea and Helvidius, and Cato, and Dio, and Brutus" — the canon of Republican martyrs who inspired "the first conceit and desire of an equal commonwealth, administered by justice and equality" (004-026). In Book 4, Marcus lists Cato among the names of the great that time will eventually obscure (009-064). Marcus also read Cato directly: his correspondence with Fronto records reading Cato's speech *On the Property of Pulchra* (032-038) and Cato's *Agriculture* (032-059), and he praises Cato's oratory alongside Demosthenes (031-157, 031-161).

### Legacy and Influence

Cato became the Stoic martyr par excellence — the man who chose death over submission to a tyrant. For Marcus, raised in the Antonine tradition of just rule, Cato represented the virtue of the governed who holds power accountable, complementing the virtue of the ruler who governs wisely.
