# Section 2

(002-001) 'But for Chrysippus, there had been no Porch.'

(002-002) The Stoics regarded speculation as a means to an end and that end was,

(002-003) as Zeno put it, to live consistently (ὁμολογουμένος ζῆν), or as it was

(002-004) later explained, to live in conformity with nature (ὁμολογουμένος τῇ

(002-005) φύσει ζῆν). This conforming of the life to nature was the Stoic idea of

(002-006) [[virtue|Virtue]]. This dictum might easily be taken to mean that virtue consists

(002-007) in yielding to each natural impulse; but that was very far from the

(002-008) Stoic meaning. In order to live in accord with nature, it is necessary

(002-009) to know what nature is; and to this end a threefold division of

(002-010) philosophy is made—into _Physics_, dealing with the universe and its

(002-011) laws, the problems of divine government and teleology; _Logic_, which

(002-012) trains the mind to discern true from false; and _Ethics_, which applies

(002-013) the knowledge thus gained and tested to practical life.

(002-014) The Stoic system of physics was materialism with an infusion of

(002-015) pantheism. In contradiction to Plato's view that the Ideas, or

(002-016) Prototypes, of phenomena alone really exist, the Stoics held that

(002-017) material objects alone existed; but immanent in the material universe

(002-018) was a spiritual force which acted through them, manifesting itself

(002-019) under many forms, as fire, æther, spirit, soul, reason, the ruling

(002-020) principle.

(002-021) The universe, then, is God, of whom the popular gods are manifestations;

(002-022) while legends and myths are allegorical. The soul of man is thus an

(002-023) emanation from the godhead, into whom it will eventually be re-absorbed.

(002-024) The divine ruling principle makes all things work together for good,

(002-025) but for the good of the whole. The highest good of man is consciously

(002-026) to work with God for the common good, and this is the sense in which

(002-027) the Stoic tried to live in accord with nature. In the individual it

(002-028) is virtue alone which enables him to do this; as [[providence|Providence]] rules the

(002-029) universe, so virtue in the soul must rule man.

(002-030) In Logic, the Stoic system is noteworthy for their theory as to the

(002-031) test of truth, the _Criterion_. They compared the new-born soul to a

(002-032) sheet of paper ready for writing. Upon this the senses write their

(002-033) impressions (φαντασίαι), and by experience of a number of these the

(002-034) soul unconsciously conceives general notions (κοιναὶ ἔννοιαι) or

(002-035) anticipations (προλήψεις). When the impression was such as to be

(002-036) irresistible it was called (καταληπτικὴ φαντασία) one that holds fast,

(002-037) or as they explained it, one proceeding from truth. Ideas and

(002-038) inferences artificially produced by deduction or the like were tested

(002-039) by this 'holding perception.' Of the Ethical application I have already

(002-040) spoken. The highest good was the virtuous life. Virtue alone is

(002-041) happiness, and [[evil|vice]] is unhappiness. Carrying this theory to its

(002-042) extreme, the Stoic said that there could be no gradations between

(002-043) virtue and vice, though of course each has its special manifestations.

(002-044) Moreover, nothing is good but virtue, and nothing but vice is bad.

(002-045) Those outside things which are commonly called good or bad, such as

(002-046) health and sickness, wealth and poverty, pleasure and pain, are to him

(002-047) indifferent (ἀδιάφορα). All these things are merely the sphere in which

(002-048) virtue may act. The ideal Wise Man is sufficient unto himself in all

(002-049) things (αὐταρκής); and knowing these truths, he will be happy even when

(002-050) stretched upon the rack. It is probable that no Stoic claimed for

(002-051) himself that he was this Wise Man, but that each strove after it as an

(002-052) ideal much as the Christian strives after a likeness to Christ. The

(002-053) exaggeration in this statement was, however, so obvious, that the later

(002-054) Stoics were driven to make a further subdivision of things indifferent

(002-055) into what is preferable (προηγμένα) and what is undesirable

(002-056) (ἀποπροηγμένα). They also held that for him who had not attained to the

(002-057) perfect wisdom, certain actions were proper. (καθήκοντα) These were

(002-058) neither virtuous nor vicious, but, like the indifferent things, held a

(002-059) middle place.

(002-060) Two points in the Stoic system deserve special mention. One is a

(002-061) careful distinction between things which are in our power and things

(002-062) which are not. Desire and dislike, opinion and affection, are within

(002-063) the power of the will; whereas health, wealth, honour, and other such

(002-064) are generally not so. The Stoic was called upon to control his desires

(002-065) and affections, and to guide his opinion; to bring his whole being

(002-066) under the sway of the will or leading principle, just as the universe

(002-067) is guided and governed by divine Providence. This is a special

(002-068) application of the favourite Greek virtue of [[temperance|moderation]], (σωφροσύνη)

(002-069) and has also its parallel in Christian ethics. The second point is a

(002-070) strong insistence on the unity of the universe, and on man's duty as

(002-071) part of a great whole. Public spirit was the most splendid political

(002-072) virtue of the ancient world, and it is here made cosmopolitan. It is

(002-073) again instructive to note that Christian sages insisted on the same

(002-074) thing. Christians are taught that they are members of a worldwide

(002-075) brotherhood, where is neither Greek nor Hebrew, bond nor free and that

(002-076) they live their lives as fellow-workers with God.

(002-077) Such is the system which underlies the Meditations of [[marcus-aurelius|Marcus Aurelius]].

(002-078) Some knowledge of it is necessary to the right [[knowledge|understanding]] of the

(002-079) book, but for us the chief interest lies elsewhere. We do not come to

(002-080) Marcus Aurelius for a treatise on Stoicism. He is no head of a school to

(002-081) lay down a body of doctrine for students; he does not even contemplate

(002-082) that others should read what he writes. His philosophy is not an eager

(002-083) intellectual inquiry, but more what we should call religious feeling.

(002-084) The uncompromising stiffness of Zeno or Chrysippus is softened and

(002-085) transformed by passing through a nature reverent and tolerant, gentle

(002-086) and free from guile; the grim resignation which made life possible to

(002-087) the Stoic sage becomes in him almost a mood of aspiration. His book

(002-088) records the innermost thoughts of his heart, set down to ease it, with

(002-089) such moral maxims and reflections as may help him to bear the burden of

(002-090) duty and the countless annoyances of a busy life.

(002-091) It is instructive to compare the _Meditations_ with another famous book,

(002-092) the _Imitation of Christ_. There is the same ideal of self-control in

(002-093) both. It should be a man's task, says the _Imitation_, 'to overcome

(002-094) himself, and every day to be stronger than himself.' 'In withstanding of

(002-095) the passions standeth very peace of heart.' 'Let us set the axe to the

(002-096) root, that we being purged of our passions may have a peaceable mind.'

(002-097) To this end there must be continual self-examination. 'If thou may not

(002-098) continually gather thyself together, namely sometimes do it, at least

(002-099) once a day, the morning or the evening. In the morning purpose, in the

(002-100) evening discuss the manner, what thou hast been this day, in word, work,

(002-101) and thought.' But while the Roman's temper is a modest self-reliance,

(002-102) the Christian aims at a more passive mood, humbleness and meekness,

(002-103) and reliance on the presence and personal friendship of God. The Roman

(002-104) scrutinises his faults with severity, but without the self-contempt

(002-105) which makes the Christian 'vile in his own sight.' The Christian, like

(002-106) the Roman, bids 'study to withdraw thine heart from the love of things

(002-107) visible'; but it is not the busy life of duty he has in mind so much as

(002-108) the contempt of all worldly things, and the 'cutting away of all

(002-109) lower delectations.' Both rate men's praise or blame at their real

(002-110) worthlessness; 'Let not thy peace,' says the Christian, 'be in the

(002-111) mouths of men.' But it is to God's censure the Christian appeals, the

(002-112) Roman to his own soul. The petty annoyances of injustice or unkindness

(002-113) are looked on by each with the same magnanimity. 'Why doth a little

(002-114) thing said or done against thee make thee sorry? It is no new thing; it

(002-115) is not the first, nor shall it be the last, if thou live long. At best

(002-116) suffer patiently, if thou canst not suffer joyously.' The Christian

(002-117) should sorrow more for other men's malice than for our own wrongs; but

(002-118) the Roman is inclined to wash his hands of the offender. 'Study to be

(002-119) patient in [[pain|suffering]] and bearing other men's defaults and all manner

(002-120) infirmities,' says the Christian; but the Roman would never have thought

(002-121) to add, 'If all men were perfect, what had we then to suffer of other

(002-122) men for God?' The virtue of suffering in itself is an idea which does

(002-123) not meet us in the _Meditations_. Both alike realise that man is one of a

(002-124) great community. 'No man is sufficient to himself,' says the Christian;

(002-125) 'we must bear together, help together, comfort together.' But while

(002-126) he sees a chief importance in zeal, in exalted emotion that is, and

(002-127) avoidance of lukewarmness, the Roman thought mainly of the duty to be

(002-128) done as well as might be, and less of the feeling which should go with

(002-129) the doing of it. To the saint as to the emperor, the world is a poor

(002-130) thing at best. 'Verily it is a misery to live upon the earth,' says the

(002-131) Christian; few and evil are the days of man's life, which passeth away

(002-132) suddenly as a shadow.

(002-133) But there is one great difference between the two books we are

(002-134) considering. The _Imitation_ is addressed to others, the _Meditations_

(002-135) by the writer to himself. We learn nothing from the _Imitation_ of

(002-136) the author's own life, except in so far as he may be assumed to have

(002-137) practised his own preachings; the _Meditations_ reflect mood by mood the

(002-138) mind of him who wrote them. In their intimacy and frankness lies their

(002-139) great charm. These notes are not sermons; they are not even confessions.

(002-140) There is always an air of self-consciousness in confessions; in such

(002-141) revelations there is always a danger of unctuousness or of vulgarity for

(002-142) the best of men. St. Augus-tine is not always clear of offence, and John

(002-143) Bunyan himself exaggerates venial peccadilloes into heinous sins. But

(002-144) Marcus Aurelius is neither vulgar nor unctuous; he extenuates nothing,

(002-145) but nothing sets down in malice. He never poses before an audience; he

(002-146) may not be profound, he is always sincere. And it is a lofty and serene

(002-147) soul which is here disclosed before us. Vulgar vices seem to have no

(002-148) temptation for him; this is not one tied and bound with chains which

(002-149) he strives to break. The faults he detects in himself are often such as

(002-150) most men would have no eyes to see. To serve the divine spirit which

(002-151) is implanted within him, a man must 'keep himself pure from all violent

(002-152) passion and evil affection, from all rashness and vanity, and from all

(002-153) manner of discontent, either in regard of the gods or men': or, as he

(002-154) says elsewhere, 'unspotted by pleasure, undaunted by pain.' Unwavering

(002-155) courtesy and consideration are his aims. 'Whatsoever any man either

(002-156) doth or saith, thou must be good;' 'doth any man offend? It is against

(002-157) himself that he doth offend: why should it trouble thee?' The offender

(002-158) needs pity, not wrath; those who must needs be corrected, should be

(002-159) treated with tact and gentleness; and one must be always ready to learn

(002-160) better. 'The best kind of revenge is, not to become like unto them.'

(002-161) There are so many hints of offence forgiven, that we may believe the

(002-162) notes followed sharp on the facts. Perhaps he has fallen short of his

(002-163) aim, and thus seeks to call his principles to mind, and to strengthen

(002-164) himself for the future. That these sayings are not mere talk is plain

(002-165) from the story of Avidius Cassius, who would have usurped his imperial

(002-166) throne. Thus the emperor faithfully carries out his own principle, that

(002-167) evil must be overcome with good. For each fault in others, [[nature|Nature]] (says

(002-168) he) has given us a counteracting virtue; 'as, for example, against the

(002-169) unthankful, it hath given goodness and meekness, as an antidote.'

(002-170) One so gentle towards a foe was sure to be a good friend; and indeed his

(002-171) pages are full of generous gratitude to those who had served him. In his

(002-172) First Book he sets down to account all the debts due to his kinsfolk

(002-173) and teachers. To his grandfather he owed his own gentle spirit, to

(002-174) his father shamefastness and courage; he learnt of his mother to be

(002-175) religious and bountiful and single-minded. [[rusticus|Rusticus]] did not work in

(002-176) vain, if he showed his pupil that his life needed amending. Apollonius

(002-177) taught him simplicity, reasonableness, gratitude, a love of true

(002-178) liberty. So the list runs on; every one he had dealings with seems

(002-179) to have given him something good, a sure proof of the goodness of his

(002-180) nature, which thought no evil.
