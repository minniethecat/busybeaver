## Overview

Annia Galeria Faustina Minor (c. 130--175 CE), known as Faustina the Younger, was Roman empress as the wife of [[Marcus Aurelius]] and the daughter of [[Antoninus Pius]]. She bore Marcus at least thirteen children, accompanied him on the northern campaigns as _Mater Castrorum_ ("Mother of the Camp"), and died at Halala in Cappadocia in 175 CE. Her reputation suffered from Roman gossip, though Marcus praised her unreservedly in the Meditations. (001-112) (001-118) (004-189)

## Life

Faustina was the daughter of Antoninus Pius and Faustina the Elder (Annia Galeria Faustina), who was also Marcus Aurelius's paternal aunt. When Hadrian arranged the succession, Antoninus adopted Marcus and betrothed him to his daughter Faustina. (001-036) (001-039)

The marriage was consummated in 145 CE. Two years later Faustina brought him a daughter -- the first of many children. (001-053)

## Relationship with Marcus Aurelius

The marriage was a dynastic arrangement, but evidence points to genuine affection. Marcus's letters to [[Fronto]] reveal tender concern for their children: when their daughter Faustina recovered from grave illness, Marcus wrote immediately to share the news. (033-022)

In the Meditations, Marcus thanks the gods "that I have such a wife, so obedient, so loving, so ingenuous" -- a rare personal confession in a work devoted to spiritual self-discipline. (004-189)

Faustina accompanied Marcus during the prolonged Marcomannic Wars, an unusual role for an empress. Her presence with the legions earned her the title _Mater Castrorum_. She died while traveling with Marcus in the east, at the village of Halala near Tyana. (001-112)

## Legacy and Reputation

Faustina's memory stands at a crossroads of official honor and vicious rumor. The Senate deified her and founded the _Puellae Faustinianae_, a charity for orphaned girls. Halala was renamed Faustinopolis. (001-112)

Yet "scandal has made free with the name of Faustina." Roman sources accused her of infidelity and of conspiring with the rebel Avidius Cassius. The introductory essay notes that "these charges rest on no sure evidence; and the emperor, at all events, loved her dearly, nor ever felt the slightest qualm of suspicion." (001-126) Marcus's private tribute -- "so obedient, so loving, so ingenuous" -- remains the most authoritative testimony, from a man who practiced rigorous self-honesty. (004-189)
